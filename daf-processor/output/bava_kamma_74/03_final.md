# Bava Kamma 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hachasha v. Hazama

### Dispute Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: עֵדִים שֶׁהוּכְחֲשׁוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף הוּזַּמּוּ – נֶהֱרָגִין. דְּהַכְחָשָׁה תְּחִילַּת הֲזַמָּה הִיא, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Witnesses** to a capital crime **who were** first **contradicted** by two other witnesses, and **ultimately they,** the first set of witnesses, **were rendered conspiring witnesses, are killed,** in accordance with the punishment for conspiring witnesses involved in a capital case, despite the fact that their testimony was already disqualified prior to the discovery of their conspiracy. The reason is that the **contradiction** of testimony **is the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony, **only** the process **has not** yet **been completed** at the time of the contradiction.

The sugya opens in the final lines of 73b with the dispute between Abaye and Rava over whether *hachasha techilat hazama* — whether contradiction (*hachasha*) constitutes the beginning of the process of *hazama* (the invalidation of witnesses as *eidim zomemim*). The practical stakes are significant. If witness group A testifies that Reuven committed a crime on Monday, and group B contradicts them by saying Reuven was with us on Monday — that is *hachasha*, which neutralizes group A's testimony. But can group B then go further and say "and you, group A, were also with us on Monday" — completing the process of *hazama*? Rava holds that *hachasha hi techilat hazama*: contradiction is merely the opening stage of a process that can lawfully culminate in *hazama*. Abaye disagrees: once testimony has been neutralized by *hachasha*, it is no longer live, and *hazama* cannot follow.

### Rava's Braita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מְעִידַנִי בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁסִּימֵּא אֶת עֵין עַבְדּוֹ וְהִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ״ – שֶׁהֲרֵי הָרַב אוֹמֵר כֵּן; וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין – מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי עַיִן לָעֶבֶד. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כִּדְקָתָנֵי, דְּלֵיכָּא כַּת אַחֲרִינָא; מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי עַיִן לָעֶבֶד?! בָּתַר דְּמַפְּקִי לֵיהּ לְחֵירוּת – דְּמֵי עֵינוֹ קָבָעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי? וְעוֹד, דְּמֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ עֶבֶד לְרַב בָּעֵי לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי! וְעוֹד, ״שֶׁהֲרֵי הָרַב אוֹמֵר כֵּן״ – הָרַב מִי נִיחָא לֵיהּ? אֶלָּא לָאו כְּגוֹן דַּאֲתוֹ בֵּי תְרֵי, וְאָמְרִי: ״הִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ, סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ״ – דְּבָעֵי מֵיתִיב לֵיהּ הָרַב דְּמֵי עֵינוֹ; וַאֲתוֹ בֵּי תְרֵי מְצִיעָאֵי, וְאָמְרִי: ״עֵינוֹ, וַהֲדַר שִׁינּוֹ״ – דְּלָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ אֶלָּא דְּמֵי שִׁינּוֹ, דְּקָא מַכְחֲשִׁי לֵיהּ קַמָּאֵי לִמְצִיעָאֵי. וְקָתָנֵי: ״וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין״ – מְצִיעָאֵי, ״מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי עַיִן לָעֶבֶד״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַכְחָשָׁה – תְּחִילַּת הֲזַמָּה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** that this is so? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Makkot* 1:3) that if witnesses say: **We testify with regard to so-and-so that he blinded his** Canaanite **slave's eye and** afterward **knocked out his tooth,** and therefore the master is obligated to pay him compensation for the value of his tooth, **as that is what the master says,** i.e., this testimony is advantageous to the master, **and** subsequently **they were found** to be **conspiring witnesses, they pay** the **value of an eye to the slave.** 
>
> Rava explains that the *baraita* as written is problematic: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** it is exactly **as it is taught** in the *baraita*, i.e., **that there is no other set** of witnesses other than those whose statement is quoted in the *baraita* and the opposing set of witnesses who establish them as conspiring witnesses, several problems arise: First, why should they **pay** the **value of an eye to the slave? After emancipating him** by testifying that his master has blinded him, should **they be required to pay** him the **value of his eye?** **And furthermore, they should be required to pay** the **value of the entire slave to the master,** as they intended to cause him to lose ownership of his slave through their false testimony. **And furthermore,** why does the *baraita* say: **As that is what the master says,** i.e., this testimony is advantageous to the master. **Is it** really **satisfactory to the master** that his slave be emancipated as a result of this testimony?
>
> **Rather,** it must be that this case involves another, competing, testimony that is not mentioned in the *baraita*. **Is it not** correct to say that the *baraita* is dealing with a case **where** prior to the testimonies mentioned in the *baraita* **two** other witnesses **came and said:** The master first **knocked out** the slave's **tooth** and then **blinded his eye, in** which case **the master is required** to emancipate the slave and also **to give him** the **value of his eye; and** then **an intermediate** set of **two** witnesses **came and said** the testimony quoted in the *baraita*, that first the master blinded the slave's **eye and then** knocked out **his tooth?** According to this account the master would still be required to emancipate the slave, but **he would be required to give him only** the **value of his tooth,** which is much less than the value of an eye. **In** this case the testimony of **the first** set of witnesses **contradicts** the testimony of **the intermediate ones.** **And this is** the meaning of the clause: **As that is what the master says,** i.e., this testimony is advantageous to the master. It means **that what** the intermediate witnesses **say,** i.e., that he owes the value of a tooth, **is satisfactory to him,** as according to the claim of the first set, he owes the value of an eye. Since there are now contradictory testimonies, the testimony stating the claim of a loss of lesser value is accepted, and in that case the master would have to pay the slave only for the value of his tooth, in addition to emancipating him. This judgment corresponds exactly to the testimony of the intermediate set of witnesses. **And** the *baraita* subsequently **teaches: And** then **the intermediate** set of witnesses **were found to be conspiring witnesses,** as a third set of witnesses testified that the intermediate set was not at the scene of the incident at all. Therefore, the intermediate set of witnesses **pays** the **value of an eye to the slave** less the value of a tooth, as this is the amount of monetary damage the slave stood to incur as a result of their testimony. Rava concludes his proof: **Learn from** the *baraita* that the **contradiction** of testimony **is the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony. Although the testimony of the intermediate set of witnesses had already been contradicted before it was shown to be conspiring testimony, nevertheless, they can still be established as conspiring witnesses at that later stage, which means that they must pay an amount equivalent to the loss they were attempting to cause through their testimony.

Rava attempts to establish his position from a *braita* dealing with witnesses who testify about injuries a master inflicted on his slave — specifically, whether the eye was knocked out first or the tooth. The order matters enormously: since knocking out either the eye or the tooth obligates the master to free the slave, the second injury would carry no such consequence, and only a monetary payment would apply. A master who has already lost his slave due to the tooth would naturally prefer testimony that the eye came second, so that he owes only the monetary value of the tooth. The *braita* describes a case where witnesses testify in the order the master prefers — eye then tooth — and are subsequently found to be *zomemim*. They must then pay the master the value of the eye, since their false testimony was aimed at depriving him of that amount. Rava argues that this *braita* presupposes the existence of prior contradicting testimony about the reverse order, and that the *zomemim* testimony followed and overrode it — demonstrating that *hazama* can succeed *hachasha*.

### Abaye's Two-Group Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא, דְּאַפְכִינְהוּ וְאַזְּמִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: No.** There is another possible interpretation for the *baraita*, which answers the questions raised above and yet does not serve as proof for Rava's opinion. According to this interpretation, the contradictory testimony was given after, not before, the testimony mentioned in the *baraita*. It is possible that the *baraita* is discussing a case **where** a second set of witnesses both **reversed** the order of events, by testifying that the tooth was knocked out before the blinding of the eye, **and** at the same time **rendered** the first set witnesses **as conspiring witnesses,** by testifying that those original witnesses were not at the scene of the supposed events they claimed to have witnessed. It can be argued, albeit in a far-fetched manner, that the testimony of the first set of witnesses is considered to be to the master's advantage even at this stage, because he knows what really happened, as reflected in the testimony of the later witnesses. Accordingly, Rava maintains that there are a total of three sets of witnesses in the case of the *baraita*, while Abaye says that there are only two sets.

Abaye rejects this reading entirely. He argues that there were never three groups of witnesses — only two. The *eidim* who carried out the *hazama* were themselves the ones who provided the alternative account of events. The Gemara introduces this with the phrase *lo d'hafchinun v'hazminun*: the very witnesses who reversed the story are also the ones who performed the *hazama*. How does Abaye know that the *seifa* of the *braita* involves only two groups? He infers it from the *seifa* itself. In the *seifa*, the case is that witnesses testify in the order favorable to the slave — tooth then eye — and the *eved* is happy with this. But the *eved* would be happy with this testimony even without any prior contradicting testimony, simply because it promises him his freedom and a monetary payment. There is therefore no textual signal in the *seifa* that a separate prior group testified to the reverse. Since the *seifa* is definitively a two-group case, Abaye argues, so too is the *reisha*.

### Eye/Tooth Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּסֵיפָא בְּמֵיפַךְ וַהֲזַמָּה, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי בְּמֵיפַךְ וַהֲזַמָּה. דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״מֵעִידַנוּ אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהִפִּיל אֶת שֵׁן עַבְדּוֹ, וְסִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ״ – שֶׁהֲרֵי הָעֶבֶד אוֹמֵר כֵּן; וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין – מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי עַיִן לָרַב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא קָא מוֹדוּ לְהוּ בָּתְרָאֵי בְּחַבְלָא כְּלָל, דְּמֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ עֶבֶד לְרַב בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא – דְּקָא מוֹדוּ כּוּלְּהוּ בְּחַבְלָא, (וּדְקָא אָפְכִינַן) [וּדְאַפְכִינְהוּ] וְאַזְּמִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the fact that **the latter clause** of the *baraita* states a ruling **with regard to** a case in which the second set of witnesses both **reverses** the order of events **and renders** the first pair **as conspiring witnesses,** it may be inferred that **the first case as well,** in parallel fashion, states a ruling **with regard to** a case in which the second set of witnesses both **reverses** the order of events **and renders** the first pair **as conspiring witnesses.**
>
> This is **as it teaches** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita*, that if witnesses say: **We testify** with regard to **so-and-so that he knocked out his** Canaanite **slave's tooth and** afterward **blinded his eye,** which requires the master to emancipate him and compensate him for the value of his eye, **as that is what the slave says,** i.e., this testimony is advantageous to the slave, **and** subsequently **they were found** to be **conspiring witnesses, they pay the value of an eye to the master.**
>
> Abaye analyzes this clause of the *baraita*: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** it is speaking of a situation **where the latter** set of witnesses, who attested that the first set of witnesses were conspiring witnesses, **do not concede that** there was any **injury at all** inflicted on the slave by his master, then there is no testimony to confirm that those injuries were inflicted other than that given by the witnesses who were shown to be conspiring witnesses. Consequently, **they should be required to pay the entire value of the slave to the master,** as this is the amount of monetary damage the master stood to incur as a result of their false testimony.
>
> **Rather, it is obvious that all of them,** i.e., both the first set of witnesses and the latter set, **concede with regard to the injury** inflicted on the slave by the master. They both agree that the master both blinded the slave's eye and knocked out his tooth and therefore is obligated to emancipate him. They disagree concerning only the order of events. The first set of witnesses said that the tooth injury occurred first and the eye injury occurred afterward. Consequently, they sought to require the master to emancipate the slave and compensate him for the eye injury. **And** the second set of witnesses **reversed** the order of events in their testimony, saying that the eye injury took place first, **and** in the same testimony, **they rendered** the first pair **conspiring witnesses,** by testifying that they were not present when the events occurred. Since the latter clause of the *baraita* must be interpreted in this manner, it stands to reason that the first case should be explained similarly.

The Gemara then works through the mechanics of how such testimony operates. The key constraint, noted by Rashi, is that in the *seifa* — where the *zomemim* are testifying that the eye came last — their testimony could never have stood unopposed if a prior *kat* had already testified that the eye came first. Testimony demanding greater payment (the eye) would not have been accepted had a contradicting account already been on record. Since such testimony was apparently accepted and acted upon, there was no prior *kat*, and the reversal must have come from the *mazimim* themselves.

The *reisha* operates differently. There, the *zomemim* testified that the tooth came last — a lesser obligation. Had a prior *kat* testified to the tooth and then a second *kat* testified to the eye, the court would follow the lesser of the two competing claims (since that represents the overlap), meaning the first *kat*'s testimony about the tooth would still have been operative rather than neutralized. This is consistent with three *kitot*: the first *kat* testifying tooth-then-eye, the middle *kat* testifying eye-then-tooth, and the *mazimim* then invalidating the middle *kat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקָא מְאַחֲרִי אַחוֹרֵיהֶם הָנֵי בָּתְרָאֵי – אַכַּתִּי דְּמֵי עֶבֶד לְרַב בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי; דְּכִי מְחַיְּיבִי לֵיהּ לְגַבְרָא – אַכַּתִּי גַּבְרָא לָאו בַּר חִיּוּבָא הוּא! אֶלָּא דְּקָא מְקַדְּמִי קַדּוֹמֵי הָנֵי בָּתְרָאֵי. וְאִי דְּלָא עָמַד בַּדִּין – אַכַּתִּי דְּמֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ עֶבֶד לְרַב בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ, דְּאַכַּתִּי גַּבְרָא לָא מִיחַיַּיב! אֶלָּא דְּעָמַד בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Abaye's interpretation of the *baraita*: **And what are the circumstances** of the case? The second set of witnesses, who are deemed credible, have established that the injuries did not take place on the date reported by the first set of witnesses. According to their testimony, when did the injuries take place? **If the latter** witnesses **postdated** the events, by testifying that the injuries were actually inflicted at a later stage than that mentioned by the first set of witnesses, the first set of witnesses should **still be required to pay the** full **value of a slave to the master.**
>
> The reason the first set of witnesses should be liable is **that when they** sought to **impose liability upon the man,** i.e., the master, this **man was not yet** burdened with any **liability.** When the first set of witnesses testified about the injuries, the master was not yet obligated to emancipate his slave or pay damages. Therefore, these conspiring witnesses should be required to pay these sums, which they had sought to impose on the master. **Rather,** it must be **that the latter** set of witnesses **predated** the time of the injuries, by testifying that they were inflicted at an earlier date than that attested to by the first set of witnesses.
>
> The Gemara continues to analyze the case: **And if** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the master **had not** yet **stood trial** for having injured his slave when the first set of witnesses submitted their testimony, those witnesses **should still be required to pay the entire value of the slave to the master, as** at the time of their testimony **the man,** i.e., the master, was **not yet liable.** The first set of witnesses sought to render the master liable at a time when he was exempt from liability, and therefore they should be obligated to pay him the full value of the slave.
>
> **Rather,** it must be **that** the master **had** already **stood trial** for having inflicted these injuries, and had been sentenced by the court to emancipate the slave for the first injury and pay him for the second injury. Then, the master evaded payment, and was subsequently brought before another court, where witnesses testified that the first court had established liability for knocking out the tooth first and then blinding the slave's eye. This testimony was subsequently rendered conspiring testimony by a second set of witnesses, who testified both that the first set of witnesses was not at the scene of the first trial and that the results of the trial were actually reversed, i.e., the master's liability was for blinding the eye first and then for knocking out the tooth. Since the first set of witnesses, who were rendered conspiring witnesses, sought to increase the master's liability payment from that of the value of a tooth to that of an eye, they must pay the value of an eye to the master.

The Gemara then addresses a subtle question posed by Rav Zeira: perhaps when a slave sustains both injuries, the Torah only provides for his freedom — once for the eye and once for the tooth — with no monetary payment at all? Abaye responds from the verse: *tachas eino*, "in exchange for his eye" — but not in exchange for his eye *and* his tooth. The Torah grants freedom for one injury; the other must be compensated differently, i.e., monetarily.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא, אֵימָא: סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ – נִיפּוֹק בְּעֵינוֹ; הִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ – נִיפּוֹק בְּשִׁינּוֹ; סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ וְהִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ – נִיפּוֹק בְּעֵינוֹ וְשִׁינּוֹ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, עָלֶיךָ אָמַר קְרָא: ״תַּחַת עֵינוֹ״ – וְלֹא תַּחַת עֵינוֹ וְשִׁינּוֹ, ״תַּחַת שִׁינּוֹ״ – וְלֹא תַּחַת שִׁינּוֹ וְעֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to** the basic assumption of the *baraita* that when a master inflicts two injuries upon his Canaanite slave he must indemnify the slave for the second injury. One can **say** instead that if the master **blinded his** slave's **eye,** the slave **should go** free **on account of his eye,** and if he **knocked out his tooth** he **should go** free **on account of his tooth,** and if the master both **blinded his eye and knocked out his tooth,** the slave **should go** free **on account of** both **his eye and his tooth,** without the master having to pay damages for the loss of his tooth.
>
> **Abaye said** to him in response: **With regard to your** claim, **the verse states:** "And if a man smites the eye of his slave or the eye of his maidservant and destroys it, he shall let him go free for his eye's sake" (Exodus 21:26). The phrase **"for his eye's sake"** indicates: **But not for the sake of** both **his eye and his tooth.** The same can be inferred from the subsequent verse: "And if he smites out his bondman's tooth or his bondwoman's tooth, he shall let him go free for his tooth's sake" (Exodus 21:27). The phrase **"for his tooth's sake"** indicates: **But not for the sake of** both **his eye and his tooth.**

### Hamarah B'Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: דּוּקְיָא דְרָבָא מֵהֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא מֵרֵישָׁא, רֵישָׁא מִי קָא מִתַּכְחֲשִׁי מְצִיעָאֵי? כֵּיוָן דְּאִי לָא מִתַּזְּמִי – סָהֲדוּתָא כְּווֹתַיְיהוּ קָא קָיְימָא, דְּדִינָא כְּווֹתַיְיהוּ פָּסְקִינַן, דְּיֵשׁ בִּכְלַל מָאתַיִם מָנֶה; הִלְכָּךְ קַמָּאֵי הוּא דְּקָא מִתַּכְחֲשִׁי, מְצִיעָאֵי לָא מִתַּכְחֲשִׁי מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** Rava sought to infer from this *baraita* to his opinion that contradiction of testimony is the start of determining that testimony is conspiring testimony. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said to Rav Ashi: From which** case in the *baraita* **is the inference of Rava? If we say** that his inference is **from the first clause,** this is difficult. In the case of **the first clause,** if it involves three sets of witnesses, as claimed by Rava, **is the intermediate** set of witnesses **contradicted** and rejected before they are established as conspiring witnesses? The first set of witnesses testified that the eye injury took place after the tooth injury, whereas the intermediate set of witnesses reversed the order. If so, the intermediate set of witnesses sought to lower the master's payment from the value of an eye to that of a tooth.
>
> Rav Acha continues: **Since if** the second set of witnesses **would not have been rendered conspiring witnesses, the testimony would** have been **established in accordance with** their statement, **as the judgment would have been decided in accordance with** their testimony. The reason their testimony would have determined the ruling is **that one hundred dinars** is subsumed **within two hundred,** i.e., testimony concerning a large sum includes testimony concerning a smaller sum.
>
> Rav Acha continues: **Therefore, it is the first** set of witnesses, who testified that the eye injury occurred last, **whose** testimony would be considered **contradicted** and rejected, whereas the testimony of **the intermediate** set of witnesses would **not** be considered **contradicted at all,** as it is accepted in full. If so, this is not a case of witnesses who were rejected due to contradiction and then subsequently rendered conspiring witnesses.

A further complication is introduced: even granting the structure of the case, the *braita* cannot be describing a situation in which the contradicting testimony refers to events that occurred *after* the first group's testimony was given. If the *batra'i* say the real events occurred on Monday (whereas the first group claimed Sunday), then when the first group testified about Sunday, the master had not yet been obligated to free the slave — so the false testimony was attempting to impose a full monetary liability for the slave's value, not merely to alter the order of injuries. That cannot be the *braita*'s intent.

Similarly, it is not enough that the master committed the act on an earlier date unless he had already appeared in *beis din* — since freeing a slave on account of an injury is a *knas*, and a *knas* is not a self-executing obligation. It is imposed by *beis din*. Consequently, the scenario must be that: (a) the second group's contradicting testimony refers to events that preceded Sunday, and (b) there had already been *hamarah b'din* — a court ruling — before the date the first group claimed the injury occurred. Only then can the first group be characterized as falsely shifting the monetary consequence, rather than falsely creating an obligation wholesale. This requirement for *hamarah b'din* applies equally to both the *reisha* and the *seifa* of the *braita*, and stands independently of the Abaye-Rava dispute.

### Rav Acha's Challenge

Rav Acha b'rei d'Rav Ika challenges Rava's inferences directly, focusing first on the *reisha*. He asks: what is actually the source of Rava's proof that the *reisha* involves three *kitot*? The *reisha* says the master is pleased with the testimony. While this does suggest a prior adverse account, Rav Acha points out that the middle *kat* — the one ultimately found to be *zomemim* — is not actually *neutralized* by the first *kat*. Because their testimony asserts the lesser obligation (the tooth rather than the eye), the principle of *d'is biklal meah masayim* — that the lesser is included in the greater — means the court will actually follow the second *kat*'s ruling. Their testimony prevails; it is the first *kat* whose position is set aside. So where is the *hachasha*? If the testimony of the *zomemim* was never truly contradicted, then the *reisha* does not demonstrate that *hazama* can follow *hachasha*.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, רָבָא סָבַר: מִדְּרֵישָׁא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת – סֵיפָא נָמֵי בְּשָׁלֹשׁ; וְדָיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא – כְּגוֹן דְּאָתוּ בֵּי תְרֵי, וְאָמְרִי: ״הִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ וְסִימֵּא עֵינוֹ״, וּפַסְקִינֵיהּ לְדִינָא אַפּוּמַּיְיהוּ; וְאָתוּ בֵּי תְרֵי אַחֲרִינֵי, וְאָמְרִי: ״סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ וְהִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ״, דְּקָא מַכְחֲשִׁי לְהוּ לְהָנֵי קַמָּאֵי; וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין קַמָּאֵי – מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי עַיִן לְרַב. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךְ הַכְחָשָׁה לָאו תְּחִילַּת הֲזָמָה הִיא, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלְּמִי? הָא אִתַּכְחַשׁוּ לְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הַכְחָשָׁה – תְּחִילַּת הֲזָמָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Rav Aḥa in response: **Rava maintains** that **from** the fact **that** the ruling of **the first clause** of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to** a case involving **three sets** of witnesses, it is logical to assume that the ruling of **the latter clause is also** stated **with regard to** a case involving **three** sets of witnesses, **and** Rava **infers** his ruling **from the latter clause.**
>
> Rava explains that the latter clause is referring to a case **where two** witnesses, i.e., the ones mentioned in the *baraita*, **come and say:** The master **knocked out** the slave's **tooth and** afterward **blinded his eye, and** the court **ruled** its **judgment in accordance with their statement,** obligating the master to emancipate his slave and compensate him for the value of his eye.
>
> **And** subsequently **two other** witnesses, who were not mentioned in the *baraita*, **come and say** that the events were reversed: First the master **blinded his** slave's **eye and** then **he knocked out his tooth,** so **that they contradict** the testimony of **those first** witnesses. At this point the master is obligated to emancipate his slave and pay him only the value of his tooth, as this much was required of him according to both sets of witnesses. **And** then the first pair **were found to be conspiring witnesses.** Therefore, as the *baraita* states, **the first** set of witnesses **pays** the **value of an eye to the master,** less the value of a tooth.
>
> Rava reaches his conclusion based on the following reasoning: **And if it enters your mind that** the **contradiction** of testimony **is not the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony, and witnesses who were first contradicted and then rendered conspiring witnesses are not punished as conspiring witnesses, **why must** these witnesses **pay? They were** already **contradicted from the outset,** before they were shown to be conspiring witnesses. **Rather, conclude from** the latter clause of the *baraita* that the **contradiction** of testimony **is the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony.

Rav Ashi, responding on behalf of Rava, explains the source of Rava's inference differently: Rava draws his proof not from the *reisha* alone but from the interplay between *reisha* and *seifa* considered together. Since the *reisha* clearly involves three *kitot* — as signaled by the master's satisfaction with the testimony — Rava infers that the *seifa* must also involve three *kitot*. And in the *seifa*, the *zomemim* are the group that claimed the eye came last. Their testimony *was* genuinely neutralized — the court followed the other group's testimony that the tooth came last, since that was the lesser obligation. Nevertheless, those neutralized witnesses can be found *zomemim* and made liable. This, Rava argues, proves *hachasha techilat hazama*: the fact that testimony was contradicted and set aside does not prevent it from subsequently being subject to *hazama*.

### Abaye's Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבַּיֵּי אָמַר לָךְ: בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא – לָא סַגִּי דְּלָא שָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת, שֶׁהֲרֵי קָתָנֵי ״הָרַב אוֹמֵר כֵּן״; אֶלָּא סֵיפָא – לְמָה לִי שָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת? ״שֶׁהֲרֵי הָעֶבֶד אוֹמֵר כֵּן״?! עֶבֶד – כֹּל דְּהוּ מֵימָר אָמַר, דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיפּוֹק לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how could **Abaye,** who disagrees with Rava, refute this proof? He could **say to you: Granted, it is impossible** to interpret **the first clause without** the supposition **that** there are **three sets** of witnesses involved. The reason is **that** it **is taught** with regard to that first clause: **As that is what the master says,** i.e., this testimony is satisfactory to the master. The only testimony recorded in the *baraita* is that the master blinded the slave's eye and then knocked out his tooth. Why would this be considered satisfactory to the master? It must therefore be assumed that this testimony was preceded by another one that was even less advantageous to the master. That testimony confirmed the injuries but placed the eye injury after the tooth injury, which involves greater liability for the master. Finally, a third set of witnesses must have arrived and rendered the intermediate set as conspiring witnesses, so that there are a total of three sets of witnesses.
>
> **But** with regard to **the latter clause, why do I** need to explain it as referring to **three sets** of witnesses? If you say that it is because the *baraita* states concerning this case: **As that is what the slave says,** i.e., this testimony is satisfactory to the slave, this does not prove that there is another testimony, not mentioned in the *baraita*, that is less favorable to the slave than this one.
>
> The reason is **that a slave would say anything,** i.e., any form of testimony would be favorable to him, **as it is satisfactory for him that he should be emancipated.** The court's acceptance of testimony confirming either order of events would result in his emancipation from slavery, and therefore both testimonies are satisfactory to him. Although there is a compelling reason to posit the existence of three sets of witnesses in the first clause, there is no justification to do so in the second clause, and therefore it should be explained as did Abaye, as discussing only two sets of witnesses.

Abaye accepts the argument regarding the *reisha*. When the *braita* states that the master is pleased with the testimony, there must indeed have been a prior account on record — a prior *kat* — for that pleasure to make sense. Abaye concedes: *bishlama reisha lo sagi delo shalosh kitos* — admittedly, the first case requires three *kitot*. But he disputes the inference to the *seifa*. The *seifa* states that the *eved* is pleased with the testimony, and Abaye argues this is uninformative. A slave would naturally prefer any testimony that promises his freedom, regardless of what came before. The *eved*'s pleasure does not signal the existence of a prior adverse *kat*. Therefore, the *seifa* is consistent with only two groups, and the *mazimim* themselves provided the reversal. Since the *seifa* — the case where genuine neutralization would be most pertinent — does not require three *kitot*, it offers no proof that *hazama* can follow *hachasha*.

## Theft/Slaughter Case

### Rav Idi's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: גָּנַב עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר עַל פִּיהֶם, וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין – מְשַׁלְּמִין לוֹ אֶת הַכֹּל. מַאי, לָאו דְּהֵעִידוּ עַל הַגְּנֵיבָה וְחָזְרוּ וְהֵעִידוּ עַל הַטְּבִיחָה, וְהוּזַּמּוּ עַל הַגְּנֵיבָה וְחָזְרוּ וְהוּזַּמּוּ עַל הַטְּבִיחָה? וְהָא כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ עַל הַגְּנֵיבָה – לְגַבֵּי טְבִיחָה הָווּ לְהוּ מוּכְחָשִׁין, וְקָתָנֵי: מְשַׁלְּמִין לוֹ אֶת הַכֹּל. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הַכְחָשָׁה לָאו תְּחִילַּת הֲזָמָה הִיא, אַטְּבִיחָה אַמַּאי מְשַׁלְּמִין? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַכְחָשָׁה תְּחִילַּת הֲזָמָה הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Rava's opinion that the contradiction of testimony is the start of determining that testimony is conspiring testimony. **Rav Idi bar Avin said: We too** can **learn** from the mishna that Rava's claim is correct, as it teaches (72b): If one **stole** an ox or a sheep, as established **based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses, **and he** subsequently **slaughtered** the animal **or sold** it, also **based on** the testimony of the same witnesses, **and** these witnesses **were found** to be **conspiring witnesses,** these conspiring witnesses **must pay everything,** i.e., not only the principal amount but also the fourfold or fivefold payment.
>
> **What, is it not** referring to a case **where they testified concerning the theft and then testified concerning the slaughter, and they were** subsequently **rendered conspiring witnesses with regard to the theft and then were rendered conspiring witnesses with regard to the slaughter?**
>
> Rav Idi bar Avin states his inference: **But once they were rendered conspiring witnesses with regard to the theft,** their testimony **with regard to the slaughter** is in effect **contradicted,** i.e., nullified, as testimony concerning the slaughter of an animal without prior testimony establishing that the animal was stolen is meaningless. **And** yet the mishna **teaches** that the witnesses **must pay everything. And if it enters your mind** that the **contradiction** of testimony **is not the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony, **why should they pay** a fine **for** lying about the **slaughter?** Their testimony concerning the slaughter was rendered irrelevant before it was established as conspiring testimony. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** this mishna that the **contradiction** of testimony **is the start of determining that** testimony is **conspiring** testimony?

Rav Idi bar Avin introduces a *tanna* teaching that appears to bear on the *hachasha-hazama* debate. The teaching addresses a case where two witnesses testified that someone stole an animal, and those same witnesses also testified that he subsequently *shechted* or sold it — and then both testimonies were found to be *hazama*. The ruling is *meshalmin lo es hakol*: the false witnesses must pay the accused the full four-or-five-times amount (five for an ox, four for a sheep). This is because their false testimony attempted to saddle Reuven with the penalty for theft plus the additional penalty of *tashlumei arba v'chamishah*.

### Application to Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ עַל הַטְּבִיחָה תְּחִילָּה,
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in refutation of this inference: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the witnesses were **rendered conspiring witnesses with regard to the slaughter first,** before they were rendered conspiring witnesses in their testimony concerning the theft itself. In this case, the testimony concerning the slaughter was not rendered irrelevant before it was rendered conspiring testimony.

The natural reading of this *baraita* is that the theft was attested to have occurred on one day and the *shechita* on a second day. Witnesses came and invalidated the theft testimony as *hazama* — "you were with us on Sunday, you could not have seen the theft" — and then separately invalidated the *shechita* testimony. The question is: once the theft is invalidated as *hazama*, does the *shechita* testimony not become effectively neutralized — since if he didn't steal it, he couldn't have *shechted* it? And yet the *baraita* says the full four-or-five must be paid. This seems to prove that even after *hachasha* (the neutralization of the *shechita* testimony), *hazama* can still follow. However, the Gemara deflects this proof: the *mazimim* could have invalidated the *shechita* testimony *first* — "you couldn't have seen the *shechita* on Monday, you were with us" — leaving the theft testimony intact at that point, and only then invalidating the theft testimony as well. In that sequence, there is no *hachasha* preceding *hazama*, and the case is fully resolved without implicating the Abaye-Rava dispute.

### Rabbi Elazar's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא: עֵדִים שֶׁהוּכְחֲשׁוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף הוּזַּמּוּ – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: נֶהֱרָגִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין. תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הוּא דְּאָמַר אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֵדִים שֶׁהוּכְחֲשׁוּ בְּנֶפֶשׁ – לוֹקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is a matter **in the dispute** between other *amora'im*, before Rava and Abaye discussed it. As it was stated: With regard to **witnesses** to a capital crime **who were** first **contradicted** by two other witnesses **and ultimately were rendered conspiring witnesses,** this is a matter of dispute between **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar. One said:** These witnesses **are killed,** like all conspiring witnesses in a capital case, as the contradiction of testimony is the start of determining that testimony is conspiring testimony, **and one said: They are not killed.**
>
> The Gemara suggests: **It may be concluded** that **it is Rabbi Elazar who says they are not killed. As Rabbi Elazar says: Witnesses who were contradicted in** a case involving the taking of **a life,** i.e., a capital case, **are flogged** for violating the prohibition: "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:13).

The Gemara now identifies the underlying Tannaitic dispute behind the Abaye-Rava disagreement. The debate is between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar regarding witnesses who were first contradicted (*huchashu*) and subsequently found to be *zomemim* in a capital case. One holds they are executed; the other holds they are not. The Gemara concludes that it is Rabbi Elazar who holds they are *not* executed — i.e., *hachasha* is *not* the beginning of *hazama*, consistent with Abaye's position.

### Malkus Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךְ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הוּא דְּאָמַר נֶהֱרָגִין – אַמַּאי לוֹקִין? הָוֵה לֵיהּ לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִּין, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִּין – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הוּא דְּאָמַר אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין? תִּסְתַּיַּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that **it is Rabbi Elazar who says** that witnesses to a capital crime who were first contradicted and ultimately found to be conspiring witnesses **are killed, why are they flogged** in this case? There is always the possibility that they might be rendered conspiring witnesses, which would render them liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment. Consequently, this is a case of **a prohibition given as warning** of liability **for court**-imposed **capital punishment, and** there is a principle that **one is not flogged for** violating **any prohibition given as warning** of liability **for court**-imposed **capital punishment.**
>
> **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** this statement that **it is Rabbi Elazar who says** that witnesses to a capital crime who were first contradicted and ultimately were found to be conspiring witnesses **are not killed?** The Gemara affirms: **It may be concluded** that this is so.

The proof for this attribution is striking. Rabbi Elazar is recorded as holding that witnesses who testify falsely in a capital case and are subsequently contradicted (*huchashu b'nefesh*) receive *malkus* — lashes — for transgressing the *lav* of *eidus sheker*. The Gemara reasons: if Rabbi Elazar held that *hachasha* is the beginning of *hazama*, then such witnesses could potentially face execution if *mazimim* were subsequently to appear. In that case, the *lav* of *eidus sheker* would be a *lav she'nitan l'azharas misas beis din* — a prohibition whose violation is subject to the death penalty — and by the well-established rule, one does not receive *malkus* for such a transgression. The very possibility that the testimony could ultimately lead to their execution would preclude *malkus*. The fact that Rabbi Elazar does award *malkus* in this scenario must therefore mean he holds that once witnesses have been contradicted (*huchash*), the process *cannot* culminate in *hazama*. Accordingly, the *lav* of *eidus sheker* retains its ordinary *malkus* consequence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקִין?! תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ, מַאי חָזֵית דְּסָמְכַתְּ אַהָנֵי? סְמוֹךְ אַהָנֵי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּבָא הָרוּג בְּרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to Rabbi Elazar's opinion that witnesses who were contradicted in a capital case are flogged. Why **are** they **flogged** simply because their testimony was contradicted? **They are two** witnesses, **and** they are contradicted by **two** other witnesses. **What did you see** in the testimony of the second set of witnesses **that** leads **you** to **rely on those** witnesses to the extent that the first set of witnesses is flogged? You could just as well **rely on these** witnesses, i.e., the first witnesses. How can the court ever determine with certainty that a given testimony is false based merely on contradictory testimony?
>
> **Abaye said:** The contradiction to which Rabbi Elazar is referring involves a case **where** the witnesses testified that they saw a certain person murdered, and subsequently the individual who was allegedly **killed came** to the court **on his feet,** conclusively disproving their testimony.

One might ask: how can the witnesses be proven to have lied if they have merely been contradicted by an equal number of witnesses? The Gemara answers by invoking the case of *ba harug b'raglav* — the "murdered" victim walks into *beis din* on his own two feet. In that scenario, there is irrefutable proof that the testimony was false, yet no *hazama* is involved. Rabbi Elazar — who holds that *hachasha* cannot be the beginning of *hazama* — can therefore posit a case of unambiguous *eidus sheker* that carries *malkus* without any risk of the proceeding eventually escalating to a capital punishment of the witnesses themselves.

Tosafos raises a pointed question about the application of *lav she'nitan l'azharas misas beis din* here. Ordinarily, this principle requires not merely that the act sometimes leads to capital liability, but that this *specific lav* serves as the warning for an offense punishable by death. Why, then, should the mere possibility that a subsequent *hazama* might follow suffice to categorize *eidus sheker* as such a *lav* in this case? Tosafos answers that since we know from an explicit *pasuk* that *eidus sheker* independently carries *malkus*, the general capacity of false capital testimony to lead to execution is not itself sufficient to displace the *malkus* — but if this *specific* testimony could terminate in the witnesses' execution, then the *lav* as applied to this testimony carries the character of *azharas misas beis din*, precluding *malkus* entirely.

## Mishna Payment

### No Four/Five Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גָּנַב עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. גָּנַב וְטָבַח בַּשַּׁבָּת; גָּנַב וְטָבַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה; גָּנַב מִשֶּׁל אָבִיו, וּמֵת אָבִיו, וְאַחַר כָּךְ טָבַח וּמָכַר; גָּנַב וְהִקְדִּישׁ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **stole** an ox or a sheep, as established **based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses, **and he** subsequently **slaughtered or sold** the stolen animal, as established **based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on his own** admission, i.e., he himself admitted that he performed these acts, without there being any witness testimony, **he pays** the **double payment, but he does not pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** If one **stole** an animal **and slaughtered** it **on Shabbat,** which is a capital offense, or if **he stole** an animal **and slaughtered** it **for the** purpose of **idol worship,** or if **he stole his father's** animal **and** subsequently **his father died, and afterward he slaughtered or sold** it, or if **he stole** an animal **and** subsequently **he consecrated** it as an offering **and afterward he slaughtered or sold** it, in all these cases the thief **pays** the **double payment, but he does not pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.**

The Mishnah enumerates circumstances in which a thief pays the double payment (*tashlumei khefel*) but not the four-or-five-times penalty (*tashlumei arba v'chamishah*). The general principle at work is that *tashlumei arba v'chamishah* is a *knas*, and various conditions that undermine the *knas* liability — either because the testimony is insufficient or because the act was performed under circumstances that affect the legal categories — will reduce the obligation to *khefel* alone.

### Special Cases

The first category involves theft established by two witnesses, but *shechita* or sale attested by only a single witness or by the thief's own admission: the double is paid but not four-or-five, since a *knas* requires two witnesses and cannot be self-imposed through admission. A second category is where someone steals and then *shechts* on *Shabbat*, or steals and *shechts* for *avodah zarah*. In both instances, the act of *shechita* renders the actor *chayav misa*, and the principle of *kim lei b'deraba minei* — that the greater penalty subsumes the lesser monetary obligation — exempts the thief from the payment. A third case is one who stole from his father, and his father died before the *shechita* or sale occurred: by the time the *shechita* took place, the animal had already passed into the thief's inheritance, and one does not pay four-or-five on one's own property. Finally, one who stole and then consecrated (*hikdish*) the animal before *shechting* or selling it: once the animal is *hekdesh*, it no longer belongs to the original owner in the conventional sense, and the four-or-five liability does not attach.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: קֳדָשִׁים – שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָם, מְשַׁלֵּם. תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָם, פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of **sacrificial** animals **for which** the owner **bears** financial **responsibility** to replace with another animal if one of the original animals that one stole is lost or dies, the thief is obligated to **pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** if he slaughters one of the animals. If it is a sacrificial animal **for which** the owner **bears no** financial **responsibility,** the thief **is exempt** from the fourfold or fivefold payment.

Rabbi Shimon introduces a further distinction within the *hekdesh* category. He holds that *kodashim she'chayav b'achrayusam* — consecrated animals for which the donor retains a replacement obligation — are still considered sufficiently "his" to generate the four-or-five liability. But *kodashim she'ein chayav b'achrayusam* — consecrated animals for which the donor bears no replacement obligation — are considered fully removed from his domain, and he is therefore exempt. The Gemara will elaborate on these distinctions.

## One Witness Analysis

### One Witness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד – פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמְרִי: הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – דּוּמְיָא דְּעַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד; מָה עַל פִּי [עֵד] אֶחָד – כִּי אָתֵי עֵד אֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף בַּהֲדֵיהּ, מִיחַיַּיב; עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ נָמֵי – כִּי אָתוּ עֵדִים, מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if the fact that the thief slaughtered or sold the animal was established **based on** the testimony of **one witness,** he is exempt from the fourfold or fivefold payment. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?**
>
> The Sages **say** in response: By stating this case, the mishna **teaches us this** *halakha*: The case of the thief slaughtering the animal established **based on** the testimony of **one witness is similar to** a case where it is established **based on his own** admission. **Just as** in the case where the slaughter is established **based on** the testimony of **one witness, if one** other **witness comes** and corroborates the testimony of the first, he **joins together with** the first witness and together their testimony is rendered a valid testimony of two, and the thief **becomes obligated** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, **so too,** where the slaughter is established **based on his own** admission, **if witnesses come** after his admission he **becomes obligated** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

The Gemara begins its analysis of the Mishnah by noting that the case of a single witness is obvious — since *tashlumei arba v'chamishah* is a *knas*, it requires two witnesses and a single *eid* is plainly insufficient. The real *chiddush* of the Mishnah, the Gemara explains, is the case of the thief's own admission. The Mishnah teaches that self-admission (*al pi atzmo*) is analogous to the testimony of a single witness. Just as a single witness's testimony, while insufficient to impose liability, does not prevent subsequent witnesses from doing so, so too a thief's admission: it does not impose liability, but neither does it preemptively exempt him if two witnesses subsequently appear.

### Rav Huna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב – דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This interpretation of the mishna serves **to exclude** that **which Rav Huna** says that **Rav says. As Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** One who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt** from payment, even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability.

This reading stands in direct tension with the position of Rav Huna citing Rav: *modeh b'knas v'achar kach ba'u eidim — patur*. Rav Huna holds that if someone admits to a *knas* and witnesses subsequently come, he is exempt from payment. The question is whether an admission to a *knas* functions like a form of *teshuvah* that permanently shields the confessor, or whether it is merely insufficient on its own to generate liability. The Mishnah's implication, as noted by the Gemara, cuts against Rav Huna's position — but the Gemara will now probe this dispute through a famous narrative.

## Rabban Gamliel Story

### The Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הוּנָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁסִּימֵּא אֶת עֵין טָבִי עַבְדּוֹ, וְהָיָה שָׂמֵחַ שִׂמְחָה גְּדוֹלָה. מְצָאוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר לוֹ: ״אִי אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁטָּבִי עַבְדִּי יָצָא לְחֵירוּת?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״לָמָּה?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״שֶׁסִּמִּיתִי אֶת עֵינוֹ״. אָמַר לוֹ: ״אֵין בִּדְבָרֶיךָ כְּלוּם, שֶׁכְּבָר אֵין לוֹ עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Since Rav Huna's *halakha* was mentioned, the Gemara discusses in detail **the** matter **itself. Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** One who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt** from payment, even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability. **Rav Ḥisda raised an objection to Rav Huna** from a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving Rabban Gamliel, who blinded the eye of his** Canaanite **slave Tavi, and he experienced great joy** as a result. Rabban Gamliel had long wanted to emancipate Tavi, but it is generally prohibited to emancipate a Canaanite slave. The injury provided a fortuitous opportunity for Rabban Gamliel to emancipate his slave, as blinding the eye of one's slave results in his emancipation (see Exodus 21:27).
>
> Rabban Gamliel **encountered Rabbi Yehoshua** and **said to him: Do you know that my slave Tavi was emancipated?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: Why?** What circumstances enabled you to emancipate him? Rabban Gamliel **said to him:** I was able to do so, **as I blinded his eye.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabban Gamliel: **Your statement is nothing,** and is not grounds for his emancipation, **as he has no witnesses** who can testify that you did this to him. The principle is that one does not pay a fine based on his own admission. One pays a fine only based on the testimony of witnesses. The requirement to emancipate one's slave after injuring his eye is a type of fine.

The Gemara records the celebrated incident of Rabban Gamliel and his slave Tevi. Rabban Gamliel blinded Tevi's eye — under what circumstances the text does not specify — and was overjoyed, because this meant Tevi would finally be able to go free. He encountered Rabbi Yehoshua and told him the news. Rabbi Yehoshua, however, informed him that nothing would come of it, because there were no witnesses.

### Rav Chisda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ עֵדִים – חַיָּיב, וְשָׁמְעִינַן מִינַּהּ: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from the *baraita*: **But** if Tavi **had witnesses** who could testify with regard to the injury, Rabban Gamliel would be **obligated** to emancipate him, even if they testify after Rabban Gamliel's admission. **And we** can **learn from** this that one who **admits** that he is liable **to** pay **a fine is liable,** even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify with regard to his liability.

Rav Chisda raises this narrative as a challenge to Rav Huna. The story implies that *had* there been witnesses, Tevi would have gone free — despite the fact that Rabban Gamliel had already admitted the act. This suggests that the master's admission does *not* exempt him from the *knas* of freeing the slave, and that if witnesses subsequently appear, the slave is indeed freed. This reading directly contradicts Rav Huna's position that admission to a *knas* followed by witnesses yields exemption.

### Rav Huna Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּלָא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין אוֹדִי. וְהָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אַב בֵּית דִּין הֲוָה! שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין הֲוָה קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said to** Rav Ḥisda: This case involving **Rabban Gamliel is different, as he admitted** to his actions when he was **not in the presence of a court.** An admission not in the presence of a court does not exempt the perpetrator from paying the relevant fine. The Gemara challenges: **But Rabbi Yehoshua was the president of the court,** so it is likely that the encounter took place in court. The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, Rabbi Yehoshua **was not** present **in court** when Rabban Gamliel encountered him.

Rav Huna responds that Rabban Gamliel's case is categorically different: *shani Rabban Gamliel delo bifnei beis din hoda'ei* — Rabban Gamliel's admission was not made before *beis din*. He disclosed the information to Rabbi Yehoshua in an informal encounter, not in the context of a judicial proceeding. An admission of a *knas* is only legally operative — whether to exempt or to simply remain non-obligating — when made before *beis din*. An offhand statement to a colleague, even a distinguished one, does not qualify.

The Gemara anticipates the objection that Rabbi Yehoshua served as *av beis din*, and responds by clarifying — at the opening of 75a — that Rabbi Yehoshua was simply not *sitting in beis din* at the time. Institutional standing does not convert a casual conversation into a judicial admission.

### Two Baraita Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין בִּדְבָרֶיךָ כְּלוּם, שֶׁכְּבָר הוֹדִיתָ, מַאי, לָאו תַּנָּאֵי הִיא – הַאי תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁכְּבָר אֵין לְךָ עֵדִים״, סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – חַיָּיב; וְהַאי תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁכְּבָר הוֹדִיתָ״, סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר? לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר; וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – הַאי תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁכְּבָר אֵין לְךָ עֵדִים״, סָבַר: חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין הֲוָה. וְהָךְ תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁכְּבָר הוֹדִיתָ״, סָבַר: בְּבֵית דִּין הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question from a different source: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabban Gamliel: **Your statement is nothing, as you have already admitted** to inflicting the injury yourself? This indicates that even if witnesses would subsequently testify about the injury, Rabban Gamliel would not emancipate Tavi.
>
> The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** the case that the difference between these two *baraitot* **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? This *tanna*** of the first *baraita*, **who says** that Rabbi Yehoshua's statement was: **As you have no witnesses, holds** that one who **admits** that he is liable **to** pay **a fine is liable** to pay the fine if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability. **And that *tanna*** of the second *baraita*, **who says** that Rabbi Yehoshua's statement was: **As you have already admitted, holds** that one who **admits** that he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt** from payment, even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability.
>
> The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No;** it is possible to understand the *baraitot* differently. **Everyone agrees** that one who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt,** even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability. **And they disagree with regard to** the following: **This *tanna*, who says** that Rabbi Yehoshua said: **As you have no witnesses, holds that** Rabbi Yehoshua **was outside the court** when Rabban Gamliel encountered him, and therefore his admission is disregarded. **And that *tanna*, who says** that Rabbi Yehoshua said: **As you have already admitted, holds** that Rabbi Yehoshua **was in the court** when Rabban Gamliel met him, so Rabban Gamliel's admission is a valid admission.

The Gemara then notes that two different versions of this same incident appear in *braitot*. The first version records Rabbi Yehoshua's response as: "there are no witnesses" — implying that had there been witnesses, the slave would have gone free even after the admission. This version appears to contradict Rav Huna's position, consistent with Rav Chisda's challenge. The second version records Rabbi Yehoshua's response as: "you have already admitted" — implying that the admission itself is the barrier to the slave's freedom, because admission to a *knas* exempts the admitter even if witnesses subsequently appear. This version strongly supports Rav Huna.

### Dispute Resolution

The Gemara considers whether these two *braitot* reflect an underlying *tannaic* dispute about the substantive law of *modeh b'knas*. However, it ultimately resolves the contradiction differently. Both *tanna'im* may in fact agree that admission to a *knas* before *beis din* exempts the admitter even if witnesses later appear. The dispute between the two versions of the story is not about the underlying law but about the facts of the case: one *tanna* understood that Rabban Gamliel's admission occurred *outside beis din* and was therefore not legally operative — hence Rabbi Yehoshua's response focused on the absence of witnesses. The other *tanna* understood the admission to have taken place *before beis din* — and therefore Rabbi Yehoshua pointed to the admission itself as the operative factor preventing Tevi's freedom.

On this reading, the *braita* that records *shekvar hodaita* — "you have already admitted" — clearly supports the position of Rav Huna amar Rav that *modeh b'knas v'achar kach ba'u eidim patur*. The *braita* recording *ein lecha eidim* is ambiguous: it can be read either as implying that witnesses would obligate him (against Rav Huna) or as reflecting a case where the admission was outside *beis din* and thus non-operative (consistent with Rav Huna). The Mishnah's *diyuk* against Rav Huna's position remains on the table, but the *braita* of *shekvar hodaita* stands as a clear tannaic endorsement of his ruling.
