# Bava Metzia 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Intro: Shevach Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֶה לַחֲבֵירוֹ וְנִמְצֵאת שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ. רַב אָמַר: יֵשׁ לוֹ מָעוֹת וְיֵשׁ לוֹ שֶׁבַח. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מָעוֹת יֵשׁ לוֹ, שֶׁבַח אֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that with regard to a case of **one who sells a field to another and it is found** subsequently **that it did not belong to** the seller, and the rightful owner repossesses the field from the buyer and the buyer then demands reimbursement from the seller, **Rav says** that the buyer **has** the right to be repaid the **money** that he paid for the field, **and** he also **has** the right to compensation for the **enhancement** of the value of the field while it was in his possession. **And Shmuel says** that **he has** the right to the **money, but he does not have** the right to compensation for the **enhancement.**

We pick up at the bottom middle of 14b, in the midst of a dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning a case where someone sells land that turns out not to have been his to sell. This is the scenario of *nimtza she'eino shelo* — the seller lacked title — and the question is what the purchaser can recover.

### Nimtza She'eino Shelo

When land is sold and the transaction is subsequently voided because the seller never owned the property, the sale is a *mekach ta'ut* — a mistaken transaction — and the purchaser is entitled to recover his purchase price. The further question is whether he may also recover the *shevach*, the increased value of the property that accrued during his ownership.

### Rav's Position

Rav holds that the purchaser can collect from the seller both the principal — the original purchase price — and the *shevach*, the appreciation in value. The reasoning is that we assume there is an implicit insurance, an *achrayut*, built into the sale, covering not just the principal but also the improvements the buyer made.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: פֵּירֵשׁ לוֹ אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח מַהוּ? טַעְמָא דִשְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא פָּירֵשׁ שְׁבָחָא, וְהָכָא הָא פָּירֵשׁ לֵהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא טַעְמֵיהּ דִשְׁמוּאֵל: כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ קַרְקַע, מִחֲזֵי כְּרִיבִּית. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין וְלָאו, וְרַפְיָא בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The students **raised a dilemma before Rav Huna: What is** the *halakha* if the seller **specified** that the buyer would receive payment for any **enhancement** in the value of the field in the event that the field is taken by the rightful owners? Is **the reason for** the opinion **of Shmuel because** the seller **did not specify** that the buyer would receive **the enhancement** when he sold him the field, **but here,** in this case, **he did specify it? Or perhaps Shmuel's reason is** that **since** the buyer is reimbursed but the seller **does not have** the right to the **land,** i.e., he is not given back the land, the transaction appears to have been a loan, and therefore payment for enhancement of the field **appears to be interest.** Rav Huna **said to them: Yes and no, and** the matter was **unclear to him.**

Shmuel disagrees. He holds that the purchaser cannot collect the *shevach* from the seller. The Gemara presses this position further: does Shmuel's ruling hold even if the parties explicitly wrote the insurance into the document? The answer may be yes, because since the property was never the seller's to convey, there was in reality never a sale at all — there was only a transfer of money. The seller received money, and now he must return it. If he returns more than he received, this has the appearance of *ribbit*, of interest on a loan. The purchaser can recover his expenses from the *nigzal* — the original owner — as Rashi and Tosafot explain, but the actual increase in value is lost. That is Shmuel's position.

The broader context of the sugya is the Gemara's attempt to determine whether, in a case of *gazlan* — theft — the purchaser from the thief can be reimbursed for the *shevach*. Various *braittot* have been cited that speak of such reimbursement, but Rav has been arguing that all those cases concern a *ba'al chov* — a creditor — not a *nigzal*. If a creditor seizes property that I sold to a third party, that third party may be reimbursed for the *shevach* because the original transaction was a genuine sale, and there is no concern of *ribbit*. The question remains whether the same applies in a *gazlan* case.

## Shevach Karka'ot

### Tanya Hachi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא תָּנֵי הָכִי: לְשֶׁבַח קַרְקָעוֹת כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וַהֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹבֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, וְשֶׁבַח – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But it is not taught that way** in a *baraita* that elaborates on the mishna, stating: **What** is the case in which one appropriates property **for enhancement of land?** It is a case where **one robbed another of a field and it is appropriated** by the court **from his possession. When he collects** payment, **he collects the principal,** i.e., the value of the field itself, **from liened property, and he collects** the **enhancement from unsold property.**

The Gemara now introduces a *braitta* with the formula *veha lo tani hachi* — "but did we not teach otherwise?" — signaling that this teaching cannot be limited to a *ba'al chov* case. The *braitta* opens: *ushevach karka'ot keitzad?* — "What is the case of collecting for the increased value of land?" The *Mishnah* had established a rule that one cannot collect for *shevach karka'ot* from encumbered property — i.e., from land the debtor has already sold — because purchasers need protection from uncertain claims. So the case in the *braitta* must be one where someone is coming to collect for *shevach* directly, not from third-party purchasers.

The *braitta* states: *harei shegazal sadeh mechaveiro* — I stole a field from someone — *veharei yotzei mitachat yado* — and it is being taken from me. *Keshehu goveh* — when he comes to collect — *goveh et hakeren minechasim meshubadim* — he collects the principal from encumbered property — *veshevach goveh minechasim benei chorin* — but the *shevach* he collects only from unencumbered property. This sounds, on its face, as though the *gazlan* himself is coming to collect reimbursement, which makes no sense.

### Heichi Dami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כִּדְקָתָנֵי, גַּזְלָן מִמַּאן גָּבֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו: כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּמְכָרָהּ לְאַחֵר וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the case? **If we say** that the case is **as it is taught** in the *baraita*, which indicates that it is the robber who collects, **from whom does** the **robber collect?** Who owes him money? **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where** one **robbed another of a field and sold it to another** person, i.e., to a third party, **and** that third party invested in the field and **enhanced it?** Accordingly, when the court appropriates the land from the purchaser, he collects the value of the enhancement from the unsold property of the robber who sold it to him. This interpretation poses a difficulty to Shmuel's opinion.

The Gemara therefore asks *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If it is literally as it sounds — the *gazlan* seeking reimbursement — who would the *gazlan* be collecting from? The *gazlan* had to return the stolen land; he has no claim. So the *braitta* must be elliptical and describing a more complex scenario.

### Gazilah Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: לָאו תָּרוֹצֵי קָא מְתָרְצַתְּ? תָּרֵיץ נָמֵי בְּבַעַל חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Shmuel could have **said to you: Did you not explain** the *baraita* by adding information, i.e., that the robber sold the field to a third party? If so, you could **also explain** that rather than referring to a robber, it is referring **to a creditor.** This interpretation would accord with the opinion of Shmuel.

The Gemara concludes that we are in fact dealing with a case of *gezeilah*, but the subject of the reimbursement is not the *gazlan*. The missing details are that after stealing the field, the *gazlan* sold it to a third party — call him Michael — who improved it. The original owner then reclaims the field. Michael, the purchaser, now wants to be reimbursed.

### Sale After Theft

With that reconstruction, the *braitta* reads as follows: I stole the field, sold it to Michael, Michael improved it, the original owner came and retrieved it. Michael can collect his principal from encumbered property — because that is a fixed, known amount — but the *shevach* he can only collect directly from the *gazlan*, not from the *gazlan*'s other sold properties, because the amount of *shevach* was not predetermined. This appears to be explicit evidence that even in a *gazlan* case, the purchaser is entitled to collect *shevach* from the seller.

The Gemara responds that we had to edit the *braitta* anyway to make it coherent — since it clearly cannot mean what it literally says. Given that we must depart from the plain reading in any case, one might simply substitute *ba'al chov* for *gazlan*, though this is admittedly a much larger editorial intervention. Still, the evidence seems to mount that in a *gazlan* case the purchaser is entitled to the *shevach*.

## Achilat Peirot

### Ba'al Chov & Peirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לַאֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וַהֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹבֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, וּפֵירוֹת גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another *baraita* that elaborates on the mishna and poses a difficulty to the opinion of Shmuel: **What** is the case in which one appropriates property **for consuming produce?** It is the case of **one who robbed another of a field, and it is appropriated from his possession. When he collects** payment, **he collects the principal from liened property and he collects** the **produce from unsold property.**

The Gemara continues: *ta shema* — let us try again. *Achilat peirot keitzad?* — "What is the case involving collection for fruit?" Just as one cannot collect *shevach karka'ot* from encumbered property, one cannot collect for *achilat peirot* — reimbursement for fruit eaten or seized from stolen land — from property the debtor has already sold, because the amount is indeterminate. The *braitta* again presents what sounds like a *gazlan* scenario: I stole a field, it is being taken from me, and when he comes to collect, he collects the principal from encumbered property but the *peirot* only from free property.

Again, this is grammatically strange — it sounds like the *gazlan* is collecting — and the Gemara supplies the same reconstruction: I stole the field, sold it to Michael, Michael improved it and produced fruit, and now the original owner reclaims it. Michael wants reimbursement not only for the *shevach* but for the *peirot* — the fruit he grew and was set to eat — which were taken along with the field.

This raises the crucial point: if this were a *ba'al chov* case rather than a *gazlan* case, the creditor could never have taken the *peirot* from Michael. A *ba'al chov* holds a lien only on the land itself, not on the fruit. If the fruit is ready to be harvested, it is considered legally detached from the field — *ke-talushi dami* — and the creditor has no claim to it. Therefore, if Michael is seeking reimbursement for lost *peirot*, it cannot be a *ba'al chov* who took them. Only the original owner — the *nigzal* — reclaiming what is his, could take both the land and its fruit. So this *braitta* cannot be reinterpreted as a *ba'al chov* case. It appears to be a strong challenge to Shmuel.

### Rava's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, מְלֵאָה פֵּירוֹת, וְאָכַל אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת, וְחָפַר בָּהּ בּוֹרוֹת, שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת. בָּא נִגְזָל לִגְבּוֹת קֶרֶן – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, בָּא נִגְזָל לִגְבּוֹת פֵּירוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where** one **stole a field full of produce from another, and** he **consumed the produce and dug pits, ditches, and caves in** the field, damaging it. When the **robbery victim comes to collect** the **principal** value of the field for the damage I did to it — **he collects** it **from** the robber's **liened property. But when the robbery victim comes to collect** the value of the **produce** he consumed — **he collects** it **from unsold property.**

Rava intervenes with a creative re-reading of the *braitta*. The *braitta*, he argues, need not involve a sale to Michael at all. The words *umachara l'acher* — "and sold it to another" — which the Gemara earlier inserted into the text to make sense of it, need not be read in. Instead, Rava proposes a different scenario entirely: I stole a field belonging to Joe, and the field was full of fruit. I ate the fruit and I also dug up the land, damaging it significantly. Joe, when he retrieves the field, is owed two things: compensation for the fruit I consumed and compensation for the depreciation of the field itself.

Now the *braitta* makes sense without any purchaser in the picture. *Ba nigzal ligbos keren* — when Joe comes to collect the principal value of the field for the damage I did to it — *goveh minechasim meshubadim*, he can collect from encumbered property, because the principal value of the field is a known, fixed amount. *Ba nigzal ligbos peiros* — but when Joe comes to collect for the fruit I ate — *goveh minechasim benei chorin*, he can only collect from my free property, not from property I sold, because the amount of fruit consumed is indeterminate. How much fruit might I have eaten over how many years? There is no way to fix that amount in advance, and purchasers from me could not have known to protect themselves against that claim.

Crucially, under this reading, there is no *lokei'ach* — no purchaser — in the picture at all. The case is about the *nigzal* being reimbursed by the *gazlan* directly. It is therefore no proof against Shmuel, who was speaking about whether the *lokei'ach* — the person who purchased from the *gazlan* — is entitled to reimbursement.

### Nitzal vs. Lokei'ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנְּטָלוּהָ מַסִּיקִין, בָּא נִגְזָל לִגְבּוֹת קֶרֶן – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, בָּא נִגְזָל לִגְבּוֹת פֵּירוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** It is a case **where** gentile **thugs took** the field from the robber by force due to previous dealings between them. In that case, when the **robbery victim comes to collect** the **principal, he collects** it **from liened property,** and when the **robbery victim comes to collect** payment for the **produce,** he can **collect** only **from unsold property.**

Rabba bar Rav Huna offers a similar answer with a different factual scenario. Rather than the *gazlan* having dug up and damaged the land, Rabba bar Rav Huna says the case is *ke-gon shenteluhah masikin* — that bandits (*masikin*) came and seized the stolen field from the *gazlan*. In this situation, the *gazlan* cannot return the field to Joe in its original state — it is currently in the hands of bandits — and as is established in Bava Kamma, the *gazlan* cannot invoke *harei shelcha lefanecha* ("here is your property before you") when the property is being held by others. He must therefore reimburse Joe in cash. Joe can collect the principal — the value of the land — from encumbered property I sold, because that amount is fixed. But Joe's claim for the *peirot* the bandits are eating year after year is indeterminate, and he can only collect that from my free property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: ״הֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ״ – בְּדִינָא מַשְׁמַע. וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא לָא אָמַר כְּרָבָא: ״הֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ״ – בְּעֵינָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rava did not state** his explanation of the *baraita* **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabba bar Rav Huna,** because the phrase: **It is appropriated from his possession, indicates** that the field was taken from him **legally** and not by thugs. **And Rabba bar Rav Huna did not state** his explanation of the *baraita* **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rava,** because the phrase: **It is appropriated from his possession, indicates** that the field is appropriated **in** its **unadulterated form,** and was not damaged.

The Gemara then explains why Rava did not use Rabba bar Rav Huna's scenario and vice versa. Rava read *harei hi yotzei mitachat yado* — "it is leaving his possession" — as *badina mashma*, meaning it is leaving through a court proceeding and being returned to the rightful owner. That implies legal restoration, not bandit seizure. Rabba bar Rav Huna, by contrast, read the phrase as *be'eina mashma* — "it is intact" — meaning the land is being taken away in its existing state, which is consistent with bandits seizing it. However, he could not read the phrase as referring to Rava's scenario of the land having been dug up and damaged, since land that has been torn apart is no longer going out in its original state.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ מְלֵאָה פֵּירוֹת וְאָכַל אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת וּמָכַר אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה, בָּא לוֹקֵחַ לִגְבּוֹת קֶרֶן – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. בָּא נִגְזָל לִגְבּוֹת פֵּירוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi stated** that the *baraita* **teaches** its rulings **disjunctively.** According to Rav Ashi, the *baraita* is referring to a case **where one robbed another of a field** while it was **full of produce, and he consumed the produce and sold the field.** When, after the true owner recovers the field from purchaser, the **purchaser comes to collect** the **principal** from the robber, i.e., the amount that he paid for the field, **he collects** it **from** the robber's **liened property.** When the **robbery victim comes to collect** payment for the **produce, he collects** only **from unsold property.**

Rav Ashi then offers a third approach, reading the two parts of the *braitta* — reimbursement for *peirot* and reimbursement for *keren* — as referring to two entirely different sub-cases. In the first case: I stole the field and ate the fruit. Joe wants reimbursement for the fruit; he collects from my free property only, since the fruit amount is indeterminate. In the second case: I stole the field, ate the fruit, and then sold the field to Michael. Joe comes and retrieves it from Michael. Now Michael — the *lokei'ach* — wants to be reimbursed for his purchase price. *Ba lokei'ach ligvos keren, goveh minechasim meshubadim* — the *lokei'ach* collecting his principal can go after encumbered property. Meanwhile, *ba nigzal ligvos peiros, goveh minechasim benei chorin* — Joe collecting for the fruit can only go after free property. Two plaintiffs, two separate claims, handled differently.

The bottom line of all these answers is the same: the cases involving reimbursement for *peirot* are cases of the *nigzal* being reimbursed by the *gazlan*, not cases of the *lokei'ach* being reimbursed. They therefore provide no proof against Shmuel's position that a *lokei'ach* from a *gazlan* is not entitled to collect *shevach*.

## Liens & Judgment

### Milveh Al Peh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין לְרָבָא בֵּין לְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה הוּא, וּמִלְוָה עַל פֶּה אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **According to both Rava and Rabba bar Rav Huna,** the money that the robber owes the robbery victim **has** the status of **a loan by oral** agreement, as it is not accompanied by documentation, **and** one who is owed **a loan by oral** agreement **cannot collect from liened property.**

Having described scenarios in which Joe — the *nigzal* — seeks reimbursement from the *gazlan* for damage to land and consumed fruit, and having stated that he may collect the principal from encumbered property, the Gemara raises a sharp question. This is a debt without documentation. It is not a formal loan; it is a tort or theft obligation. *Milveh al peh eino goveh minechasim meshubadim* — an oral loan, one without a written document, does not carry liens on the debtor's sold property. So where do Joe's liens on the *gazlan*'s sold property come from?

### K'she-Amad B'Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין וַהֲדַר זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the robber **stood trial** for his robbery and was found guilty, **and he subsequently sold** the land. Since he sold it after his liability was well known, the debt is equivalent to one that is written in a promissory note, and can be collected from liened property.

The Gemara answers: *hacha b'mai askinan — k'she-amad b'din*. The case is where Joe has already taken the *gazlan* to court and obtained a judgment. Once a court judgment has been issued against someone, the obligation becomes a matter of public record — analogous in its enforceability to a written document — and it creates liens from that point forward. Purchasers who acquired property from the *gazlan* after the judgment was issued are on constructive notice of it.

### Principal vs. Peirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, פֵּירוֹת נָמוּ! כְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין עַל הַקֶּרֶן, וְלֹא עָמַד בַּדִּין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת. וּמַאי פַּסְקָא? סְתָמָא דְמִילְּתָא כִּי תָּבַע אִינִישׁ – קַרְנָא תָּבַע בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the owner should collect payment for the **produce as well** from liened property. The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **when** the robber **stood trial for the principal, but did not** yet **stand trial for the produce.** The Gemara asks: **And why** was it stated **without qualification?** According to this explanation, the distinction is not between the principal and the produce but rather between debts for which the robber stood trial and those for which he did not stand trial. The Gemara answers: **The normal** way **of things** is that **when a person files a claim, he first claims the principal** and only afterward does he file claims with regard to other property, such as produce.

The Gemara immediately presses the implication: if liens arise from the judgment, and the judgment covers both the principal value of the land and the *peirot* eaten — since both were adjudicated — why does Joe have liens only for the principal and not for the *peirot*? Once the court has issued a ruling on a specific amount, it is no longer indeterminate. The reason we had said the *peirot* claim cannot attach to sold property was that the amount was unknown. But after *ha'amadah badin* — standing before the court — the amount is fixed. So why the asymmetry?

The Gemara offers what is acknowledged to be a weak answer: *stama demilta ki tava inish karna tava bereisha* — ordinarily, when a person presses his claim, he first pursues the principal. So we can assume that if a judgment has been issued on only one of the two claims, it is most likely the principal that was adjudicated first. The *peirot* claim may not yet have been brought before the court.

This answer is conceded to be fundamentally unsatisfying. The real operative distinction has nothing to do with whether the claim is for *keren* or for *peirot*. It depends entirely on whether there has been a judgment — *ha'amadah badin*. Whatever has been adjudicated creates liens; whatever has not, does not. The *keren*/*peirot* distinction in the *braitta* is purely incidental — a product of how people typically litigate, not a substantive legal distinction between the two types of claims.

This is also an appropriate moment to note a point that connects back to the larger sugya. When we discussed the *nimtza she'eino shelo* case — where I sold Michael a piece of land that turned out to be stolen — the question arose where Michael's liens against me for his purchase price come from. Tosafot explain that the bill of sale, the written record that Michael gave me money for the land, effectively functions as a loan document. Since it is written, it creates liens. But in the case where I merely stole Joe's land and damaged it, there is no document. Liens there arise, if at all, only from a court judgment.

## Shmuel & Insurance

### Shmuel's Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שִׁילַת: אִמְּלֵיךְ, וּכְתוֹב ״שׁוּפְרָא שְׁבָחָא וּפֵירֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Shmuel say to Rav Ḥinnana bar Sheilat,** who was a scribe: When you write a deed of sale, **consult** with the parties, **and** if they agree, **write** that the seller commits to compensate the buyer, in the event that the land is appropriated from him, with **superior-quality** land, and for the value of the **enhancement** of the land **and** the **produce** as well? That was the standard formula for deeds of sale.

The Gemara now returns to Shmuel's position. The question is posed sharply: does Shmuel really maintain that a *lokei'ach migazlan* — a purchaser from a thief — is entitled to no *shevach*? The Gemara cites a report that Shmuel instructed Rav Chinana bar Sheilat, who worked as a scribe, that when drawing up sale documents he should consult the seller and, if the seller agrees, write in the following protections for the purchaser: *shufra, shvacha, u-firi* — that the seller guarantees the purchaser against loss, including reimbursement from the seller's best quality property (*shufra*), reimbursement for improvements the purchaser made (*shevach*), and reimbursement for fruit (*piri*).

### Proof Shevach & Peirot

This advice from Shmuel himself seems to demonstrate that a *lokei'ach* can indeed be reimbursed for *shevach* and *peirot*, and that Shmuel endorses writing such protections into documents. This appears to contradict his ruling.

### Peirot Proves Gazlan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִי בְּבַעַל חוֹב – מִי אִית לֵיהּ פֵּירֵי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח, שֶׁבַח – אִין, אֲבָל פֵּירוֹת – לָא. אֶלָּא לָאו, בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִגַּזְלָן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **To what** case is this statement referring? **If** it is a case **where** the seller's **creditor** repossesses the land, **does** a creditor **have** rights to the **produce? But doesn't Shmuel say** that **a creditor collects** the value of **the enhancement** of the field, indicating that he **does** collect the value of the **enhancement, but** he does **not** collect the **produce? Rather,** is it **not** referring **to** a case of one who **buys** land **from a robber,** and the owner subsequently repossesses it? This contradicts Shmuel's earlier statement that one who buys land from a robber does not have the right to the value of the enhancement.

The Gemara tightens the argument: the inclusion of *peirot* in that insurance clause proves that we are speaking of a *gazlan* case, not a *ba'al chov* case. If it were a *ba'al chov* who seizes the property, the *ba'al chov* cannot take *peirot* — the lien extends only to the land, not to the fruit. A *ba'al chov* would never take Michael's *peirot*, so Michael would never need insurance against losing *peirot* to a *ba'al chov*. The only scenario in which Michael's *peirot* could be taken along with the land is if the original owner — the *nigzal* — reclaims what is his. So Shmuel's insurance clause covering *peirot* is clearly written for a *gazlan* scenario. This seems to prove that Shmuel agrees a *lokei'ach* can be reimbursed even when the property turned out to be stolen — at least when such insurance is explicitly provided.

## Ribbit Problem

### Mekach Ta'ut Ribbit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the robber **owns land,** which he can return to the buyer instead of paying him money. In that case, the transaction appears to be a sale and not payment of interest for a loan.

The Gemara now addresses the apparent contradiction within Shmuel's own position. Shmuel said you cannot collect *shevach* because it looks like *ribbit*. Yet Shmuel himself tells scribes to write in protection for *shevach* and *peirot* in a *gazlan* case. How can both be true?

The core problem is this: when land turns out not to have been the seller's, the transaction retroactively becomes a *mekach ta'ut*. There was never a genuine sale. What occurred was functionally a transfer of money — the purchaser gave the seller cash, and now the seller must return cash, plus more. Money in, more money out: that is *ribbit*.

### Reimburse with Land

Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction: *hacha b'mai askinan — kegon she'yesh lo karka*. The case Shmuel is permitting is one where the *gazlan* has land with which to reimburse the purchaser. Rather than handing back more cash than he received, the seller gives back a plot of land. The purchaser gave a thousand dollars, the field he received was later taken back, and in its place the seller conveys a different parcel. Happened to be a parcel worth fifteen hundred because the original field had been improved — but the form of the transaction is land for land, not money for more money. That looks far less like *ribbit*.

### Se'ah B'Se'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי מוּתָּר לִלְוֹת סְאָה בִּסְאָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But is it permitted** for one **to borrow a *se'a*** of grain **for** return of **a *se'a*** in a case **where he owns land?** The Sages render prohibited executing a loan of produce for return of the same amount of produce, lest the price rise in the interim, causing the debtor to return a higher value than he borrowed, which appears to be interest. This is the *halakha* even in a case where the borrower owns land. Similarly, in the case where the robber owns land, the payment of the value of the enhancement resembles the payment of interest.

Abaye challenges this answer. He asks: *ve'chi mutar ilvos se'ah b'se'ah bemakom she'yesh lo karka?* — is one permitted to borrow a *se'ah* of grain and repay a *se'ah* of grain merely because the borrower owns land? The rabbinic prohibition of *se'ah b'se'ah* — borrowing a commodity and returning the same quantity when the price may have shifted, thereby effectively paying back more in monetary terms — is not dissolved by the fact that the debtor has real property. Having land does not transform a fundamentally *ribbit*-looking transaction into a permissible one.

### Loan vs. Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָתָם הַלְוָאָה, הָכָא זְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him:** The distinction between the two cases is that **there,** with regard to borrowing a *se'a* and returning a *se'a*, the case in question involves **a loan,** whereas **here,** it is a case involving **a sale.** Since the field was bought from the robber, the additional value that the robber pays does not appear to be interest.

The response is a crucial conceptual distinction: *hasam halva'ah, hacha zevini* — there, we are dealing with a loan; here, we began with a sale. When a transaction is structured from the outset as a loan, we apply the strictest scrutiny. Any possible increment, however attenuated, is suspect. The rules of *ribbit* for loans are applied with hypersensitivity: context does not rescue a loan-based transaction. But this transaction began as a sale. It retroactively became a *mekach ta'ut*, but it was not conceived as a loan. Because the form was that of a commercial transaction, we afford more latitude. If the reimbursement does not starkly look like money in and more money out — if it is structured as land for land — we permit it.

### Kinyan Sudar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that this is what **Rav Yosef said: Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the buyer **performed an act of acquisition** at the time he purchased the land **from** the robber's **possession,** thereby formalizing a condition that should the field be appropriated from him, he will be reimbursed for any enhancement in its value. Since he acquired this right at the time he purchased the land, it does not appear as though he is receiving interest.

A second version of Rav Yosef's answer is recorded: *ikka d'amri — kegon she'kanu miyado*. In this formulation, the permitted case is not one where reimbursement is made in land, but one where at the time of the original sale the parties performed a *kinyan sudar* — a formal act of acquisition — by which the seller obligated himself to protect the purchaser, including against *shevach* and *peirot*.

### Kinyan Day One

The relevance of the *kinyan sudar* is this: the classic character of *ribbit* is a debt that accrues over time — money sits with the borrower, and the longer it sits, the more interest accumulates. Here, because of the *kinyan*, the seller obligated himself to the full reimbursement on day one. The obligation did not grow over time as a function of how long the purchaser held the money; it was fixed from the moment of the sale. This makes it structurally unlike a loan with interest and more like a commercial guarantee. Because it is not time-based accrual of obligation, it looks less like *ribbit*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי מוּתָּר לִלְוֹת סְאָה בִּסְאָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But is it permitted** for one **to borrow a *se'a* for a *se'a*** in a case **where he performed an act of acquisition** formalizing such a condition at the time he purchased the land **from** the lender's **possession?** Isn't it still considered to be interest and therefore prohibited?

Abaye challenges this answer in the same way: *ve'chi mutar ilvos se'ah b'se'ah bemakom she'kanu miyado?* Does performing a *kinyan* permit something that is otherwise rabbinically problematic as *ribbit*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם הַלְוָאָה, וְהָכָא זְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: There,** with regard to borrowing a *se'a* and returning a *se'a*, it is a case involving **a loan,** whereas **here** it is a case involving **a sale.** Buying at a low price and selling for a higher price is not considered to be interest.

The response is identical: *hasam halva'ah, hacha zevini*. Loans are held to an unforgiving standard. Sales that retroactively collapse into a reimbursement scenario are treated more leniently when the form of the original transaction was genuinely commercial.

The practical upshot is that Shmuel's prohibition on collecting *shevach* in a *gazlan* case applies only in the starkest form of the transaction. Shmuel agrees that the purchaser can be protected and reimbursed — but only where the reimbursement is structured in a way that avoids the appearance of *ribbit*: either by paying back in land rather than cash, or by formalizing the obligation through a *kinyan* at the time of the sale.

## Ba'al Chov & Shevach

### Sale Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח. אָמַר רָבָא: תֵּדַע, שֶׁכָּךְ כּוֹתֵב לוֹ מוֹכֵר לַלּוֹקֵחַ: ״אֲנָא אֵיקוּם וַאֲשַׁפֵּי וַאֲדַכֵּי וַאֲמָרֵיק זְבִינֵי אִילֵּין, אִינּוּן וְעַמְלֵיהוֹן וּשְׁבָחֵיהוֹן, וְאֵיקוּם קֳדָמָךְ, וּצְבִי זָבֹינָא דְּנַן וְקַבֵּיל עֲלוֹהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss Shmuel's statement **itself** that was mentioned above. **Shmuel says: A creditor collects** the value of **the enhancement** of the land. **Rava says: Know** that this is true, **as this** is the standard formulation that **the seller writes to the buyer** in a deed of sale: **I will stand and silence and purify and cleanse this sale,** i.e., I accept responsibility if the land is repossessed by my creditor. The text of the document continues: This applies to this property **itself, and the labor** invested **in it, and its enhancement; and I will present** its value **before you.** The witnesses then sign the document and attest: **And this seller consented and accepted upon himself** all of the commitments enumerated in the document. Evidently, a creditor can collect the value of the enhancement.

The Gemara now cites Rava: *teda* — "know this" — and explains why a *ba'al chov* is in fact entitled to seize property that has been improved by the purchaser. The reason is that the sale document itself provides the justification. When I sell property to Michael, the document contains language protecting Michael against loss if the property is ever seized.

### Guarantee Text

The text of the guarantee runs as follows: *ana eikum ve-ashpi ve-adki ve-amrik zevini ilin inun dei ve-amlehon ve-shvacheihen ve-eikum kodamach* — I will arise and establish and purify and cleanse this sale, and all the labor invested in it, and all its improvements, and I will stand before you and make you whole. The seller, through this language, accepts upon himself — *ve-kabil alohi* — the full responsibility for any loss, including loss of *shevach*. This is parallel to the classic *ketubbah* formulation of *ve-tzvi mar ploni ve-kabil alohi*.

The significance is clear: Eli the creditor can seize the improved field from Michael because Michael is not left without recourse. Michael has a written guarantee from the seller to reimburse him for whatever he loses — including the *shevach*. Because Michael is protected, the seizure is not unjust to him.

### Gift vs. Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה מַתָּנָה דְּלָא כָּתֵיב לֵיהּ הָכִי, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא טָרֵיף שְׁבָחָא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said to Rava: If that is so,** in the case of **a gift, where** the owner **does not write this** formulation **to** the recipient in the deed of gift, would you **indeed** say **that** the creditor **does not repossess** the value of **the enhancement** of the land from the recipient of the gift? Rava **said to him: Indeed.**

This raises the question: if I give property as a gift to Michael rather than selling it, I am typically not writing a guarantee. Does Eli the creditor have the same right to seize the *shevach* from a gift recipient? Rav Chiya bar Avin poses this to Rava: *matana de-lo kasav lei hachi* — in a gift where no such guarantee was written — *hachi nami de-lo taraf shvacha?* Would we also say in that case that the creditor cannot seize the *shevach*?

### Gift Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי יָפֶה כֹּחַ מַתָּנָה מִמֶּכֶר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין יָפֶה וְיָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said to Rava: **But is the** legal **power of a gift stronger than** that of **a sale,** as in the case of a sale the buyer loses the value of the enhancement if the land is repossessed? Rava **said to him: Yes, it is indeed stronger.** Since in a case of repossession, the recipient of the gift does not receive the value of the enhancement back from the one who gave him the gift, he is under no obligation to relinquish this value to the creditor.

Rava answers: yes, that is correct. In a gift case without a guarantee, the creditor can seize the principal — which the gift recipient always knew was subject to preexisting liens — but cannot seize the *shevach*, since the recipient of the gift was not protected against losing it.

This leads to the striking observation: *vechi yafeh koach matana mikoach mecher?* — is the recipient of a gift more protected than someone who paid cash? Intuitively, the purchaser who laid out money deserves more protection. Yet Rava confirms the paradox: yes, the gift recipient is better protected with respect to *shevach*. The explanation is simple: protection derives not from having paid money but from having received a guarantee. The purchaser has a guarantee written in his sale document, which is precisely why the creditor can take the *shevach* — and precisely why the purchaser can recover it from the seller. The gift recipient, lacking a guarantee, cannot recover anything from the donor, which is why the creditor cannot take more than what is covered by that implicit protection.

## Contradicting Braitta

### Braitta Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֶה לַחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב וּטְרָפָהּ, כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹבֶה, אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יוֹתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה – נוֹטֵל אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח מִבַּעַל הַקַּרְקַע, וְהַיְּצִיאָה מִבַּעַל חוֹב. וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא הוֹצָאָה שִׁיעוּר שֶׁבַח מִבַּעַל חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: In a case of **one who sells a field to another,** and the buyer **enhances it, and** then **a creditor comes and repossesses** the field, in this case **when** the buyer **collects** compensation, the *halakha* is as follows: **If** the value of **the enhancement** of the field **is greater than the** buyer's **expenses** in generating that enhancement, **he takes** the difference in value between **the enhancement** and the expenses **from the owner of the land,** i.e., the seller, **and** he is compensated for **the expenses by the creditor. And if the expenses were greater than the enhancement** of the field, **he** receives compensation for his **expenses, only** up **to the value of** the **enhancement, from the creditor.**

The Gemara cites another *braitta*: *ha-mocher sadeh la-chaveiro ve-hishvicha* — I sold a field to Michael and Michael improved it — *uva ba'al chov u-trafa* — and the creditor came and seized it. *Keshehu goveh im ha-shevach* — when the creditor collects, taking the improved value with the land — *notel et ha-shevach miba'al ha-karka ve-hotzaa miba'al chov* — Michael collects the *shevach* from the seller, while Eli reimburses Michael only for his expenses. This is a clear statement that the creditor seizes the land and its appreciation, but owes Michael reimbursement for expenses — and that the *shevach* itself flows back to me, the seller.

### The Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שְׁמוּאֵל בְּמַאי מוֹקֵים לַהּ? אִי בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִגַּזְלָן – קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לוֹקֵחַ מִגַּזְלָן לֵית לֵיהּ שְׁבָחָא. אִי בְּבַעַל חוֹב – קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how does Shmuel interpret** the *baraita*? **If** it is referring **to** one who **buys** a field **from a robber, the first clause** in the *baraita* poses **a difficulty** to Shmuel's opinion, **as Shmuel says** that one who **buys** a field **from a robber does not have** the right to compensation for **the enhancement** of the field, and the *baraita* states that the buyer is entitled to compensation for the enhancement. **If** it is referring **to a creditor,** then both **the first clause and the latter clause** in the *baraita* pose **a difficulty** to Shmuel's opinion, **as Shmuel says** that **a creditor collects the enhancement** of the field and needs to pay nothing.

This *braitta* seems to contradict Shmuel directly. Shmuel said *ba'al chov goveh et ha-shevach* — the creditor collects the *shevach*. Yet this *braitta* portrays the creditor as reimbursing Michael for his expenses, not simply taking everything. If the creditor is fully entitled to the *shevach*, why must he pay Michael anything? The *shevach* should simply be part of what the creditor takes in satisfaction of his debt.

### Gazlan Answer

The Gemara poses the dilemma: if this is a *lokei'ach migazlan* case — the field turned out to be stolen — then *kashya reisha*, we have a problem with the beginning of the analysis, since Shmuel says a *lokei'ach migazlan* is not entitled to *shevach*. If it is a *ba'al chov* case — the standard creditor scenario — then *kashya reisha ve-seifa*, the whole *braitta* contradicts Shmuel's ruling that the creditor is entitled to *shevach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִגַּזְלָן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע. אִי נָמֵי בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests two answers: **If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is referring **to** one who **buys** a field **from a robber,** in a case **where** the robber **owns land** with which he can compensate the buyer instead of paying him money. In that case, the compensation does not appear to be interest. **Alternatively,** it is referring **to** a case **where** the buyer **performed an act of acquisition** at the time he purchased the land **from** the robber's **possession,** thereby formalizing a condition that should the field be appropriated from him, he will be reimbursed for any enhancement in its value. Since the buyer acquired the enhancement at the time he paid for the field, it does not appear as if he is receiving interest.

The first answer the Gemara offers is to read it as a *gazlan* case — with Shmuel's permission applying in one of the two permitted forms. Recall that Shmuel does allow the *lokei'ach* to collect *shevach* from a *gazlan* either when the seller has land with which to reimburse, or when a *kinyan sudar* was performed at the time of sale. So the *braitta* may be describing exactly such a case: it turned out to be stolen, but reimbursement is permitted because one of those conditions is met — *kegon she'yesh lo karka*, or *she'kanu miyado*. In that framework, the original owner takes the land with its fruit, but Michael is entitled to collect *shevach* from the *gazlan*, while Joe reimburses Michael for his expenses in having improved what turned out to be Joe's land.

### Partial Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּבַעַל חוֹב. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּשֶׁבַח הַמַּגִּיעַ לַכְּתֵפַיִם, כָּאן בְּשֶׁבַח שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַגִּיעַ לַכְּתֵפַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that it is referring **to a creditor, but** nevertheless **it** is **not difficult** according to the opinion of Shmuel. **Here,** in the *baraita*, the reference is **to enhancement** of the field **that reaches** one's **shoulders,** i.e., the produce that grew due to the improvements made by the purchaser is almost fully grown and ripened, and it can soon be harvested and carried upon one's shoulders. At that point, the produce is considered independent of the land and is therefore not collected by the creditor unless he pays for the expenses. And **there,** in Shmuel's statement that the creditor collects the enhancement without paying compensation, the reference is **to enhancement that does not reach** one's **shoulders,** i.e., whose growth is not almost complete. At that point, the produce is considered to be part of the land.

The second answer treats it as a *ba'al chov* case and resolves the contradiction by noting that the debt may not equal the full value of the land. If the improved land is worth fifteen hundred and the debt is only a thousand, the creditor is not entitled to keep the full fifteen hundred. He takes the land in payment of his debt but must reimburse Michael for the five hundred by which the current value exceeds the debt. This is not a case where the creditor is fundamentally denied the *shevach*; it is a case where the creditor is not entitled to take more than the debt. The Gemara confirms this reading: *ha demasik bei ke-shiur ar'a ve-shevacha, ha de-lo masik bei ela ke-shiur ar'a* — in the case where the debt equals the full value of land plus improvement, the creditor takes it all; in the case where the debt equals only the original value of the land, the creditor takes only that and pays Michael the difference.

## Whose Discretion

### 1500 vs. 1000

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְלוֹקֵחַ לָא מָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כִּי אִית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְלוֹקֵחַ מָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב, נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ הֲוָה לִי זוּזֵי, הֲוָה מְסַלְּקִינָּךְ מִכּוּלַּהּ אַרְעָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּלֵית לִי זוּזֵי, הַב לִי גְּרִבָא דְאַרְעָא בְּהָא אַרְעָא שִׁיעוּר שְׁבָחַאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that even **if** the **purchaser has money, he is unable to dismiss** the **creditor** from his claim to the land by paying its value. **But according to the one who says** that **when** the **purchaser has money he can dismiss** the **creditor** by paying the value of the land, **let** the purchaser **say to him: If I had money, I would have dismissed you from the entire** plot of **land. Now that I do not have** enough **money** to pay off the entire debt, **give me** at least **a *se'a* of land from the land** that you wish to repossess, which is **the amount of my enhancement.**

The Gemara now explores the mechanics of the second answer. If Eli's debt is a thousand dollars, and the land is now worth fifteen hundred following Michael's improvements, Eli is entitled to take the land but must pay Michael the five hundred dollar difference. This much is clear. The more interesting question is how the transaction plays out.

### Buyoff Question

The Gemara asks: *ha nicha le-man d'amar ei it lei zuzay le-lokei'ach lo matzi misaleik lei le-ba'al chov* — according to the view that the *lokei'ach* cannot buy out the creditor with cash, Eli has a property-right in the land itself. He seizes the entire parcel, it is worth fifteen hundred, he owes only a thousand, so he pays Michael the five hundred in cash. That makes sense.

But consider the opposing view: that Michael *can* buy out the creditor. If Michael had a thousand dollars on hand, he could tender that sum to Eli and keep the land entirely. In that case, Eli has no absolute right to the land — only to payment of the debt. If that is so, why, when Michael lacks cash, must he cede the entire land to Eli and accept five hundred in cash back? Why can Michael not say: you are entitled to a thousand dollars' worth of this land, so take two-thirds of the parcel and leave me one-third? The value of land may appreciate further; Michael may reasonably prefer to keep a piece rather than accept cash. If his ability to buy out the creditor reflects a genuine power over the land, that power should extend to choosing how the land is divided.

### Apotiki Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי. דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא יְהֵא לְךָ פֵּרָעוֹן אֶלָּא מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the debtor, who sold this land to the purchaser, **set aside** his land as **designated repayment [*apoteiki*]** for the debt, **as he said to** the creditor: **You will be repaid only from this** piece of land. Consequently, the buyer cannot dismiss the creditor from any part of the land, even though he enhanced its value.

The Gemara resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan — ke-gon she'asa'ah apotiki*. The case is where Eli holds not a general lien against all of the seller's property, but a specific mortgage on this particular parcel — an *apotiki*, where the creditor was explicitly told this field and no other is the source of repayment. In such a case, Eli has a quasi-ownership stake in this specific land. Michael cannot buy him off with cash, because Eli's right runs specifically to this parcel. Since Michael cannot buy him off, Michael has no power to dictate terms; Eli has an absolute property-claim. He takes the whole parcel, and since the whole parcel exceeds his debt, he pays the difference in cash.

### Final Rule

The concluding principle that emerges is as follows. Where the debt equals the full value of the land including improvement, the creditor takes the entire parcel and owes Michael nothing — this is enabled precisely because Michael holds a guarantee from the seller and can recover from him. Where the debt equals only the original value of the land and the improvement represents additional value, the creditor must pay Michael the difference: either in cash or in land, depending on whether it is an *apotiki* arrangement or not. If it is an *apotiki* and Michael cannot buy the creditor off, the creditor has absolute discretion over the property and pays the excess in cash. If the creditor holds only a general lien and Michael has the theoretical power to tender payment and redeem the property, then Michael retains the discretion to demand that the creditor take only a proportionate share of the land corresponding to the debt, leaving Michael with the remainder.
