# Hullin 122 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jew Shechita

### Shechita Defects

The daf opens by continuing a discussion that had distinguished between two fundamentally different types of problems in *shechita*. On one hand, there is a case where the act of *shechita* was performed perfectly in a technical sense, but the problem is extrinsic — namely, that the one who performed it was a non-Jew. On the other hand, there is a case where the act itself was defective: there was a pause, there was pressure applied, or the knife was not properly checked. These two categories carry different legal weight, and the *gemara* treats them distinctly before moving forward.

### Non-Jew Physical Act

The *gemara* then cites a ruling of Rav Shmuel Yitzchak. The relevant principle is that *shechita* must be physically performed by a Jew. If a non-Jew is present and instructs or supervises, but a Jew performs the act, the *shechita* is valid. Conversely, if the non-Jew physically performs the cut — even with a Jew observing — it is not valid. The physical execution of the act by the Jew is the operative requirement.

### Food Not Yet Dead

The *gemara* records a question posed by Rav Zeira to Rav Sheshes: *mahu she-tatzil balu'a she-betochah?* — what is the law regarding an item swallowed inside this animal after it has been *shecht*ed?

The background principle is that something which is *balu'a* — swallowed inside a living creature — is not susceptible to *tumah*. If, for example, a person swallowed a gold ring and then entered a house containing a corpse, the ring would not become *tamei*, because it is absorbed within the living body. Once the animal dies and becomes a *neveilah*, however, it is simply meat, and any item inside it is no longer protected by the concept of *balu'a* — it can become *tamei* like any object in contact with a *neveilah*.

The question Rav Zeira raises concerns an intermediate case: an animal that has been *shecht*ed but has not yet died, and is still thrashing. This could be a non-kosher animal *shecht*ed by a Jew, or a kosher animal *shecht*ed by a non-Jew. Such an animal has been ruled to have the status of food — it is already considered *ochel*, a foodstuff — yet it is not yet technically dead and has not yet become a *neveilah* in the full sense. Does it still retain the power of *balu'a* to protect what is swallowed within it, since it is still alive? Or, since it is now treated as food, has the animal been sufficiently objectified such that items within it are no longer shielded?

The *gemara* raises the tension sharply: if you say this animal causes *tumas ochlin* — the *tumah* of foodstuffs — and yet still protects what is swallowed, you are claiming contradictory things. Once you treat the meat as food that can become *tamei*, it seems incoherent to say it simultaneously still functions as a living body that shelters its contents.

### Swallowed Objects

Abaye rules definitively: *eina matzeles ela balu'a she-betochah de-metamei tumas ochlin* — it does not protect the swallowed item. Since this animal after *shechita* already carries the status of food and can become *tamei* as *ochel*, it no longer functions to protect what is absorbed within it. The status of *balu'a* depends on the creature being genuinely alive in the operative sense; once it has been reclassified as a foodstuff, even while still physically alive, it no longer provides that protection.

### Bestiality Case

The *gemara* buttresses this intermediate status with a striking case. It notes: *ve-arova chayav de-eino metamei tumas neveilah* — one who commits an act of *arayot* with such an animal after *shechita* would still be liable, because the animal is not yet a *neveilah*. It has not yet died fully; it is still alive in a relevant legal sense, even while it is simultaneously already classified as food.

This is a genuinely peculiar middle state. The animal is food — objectified, susceptible to *tumas ochlin*, no longer protecting its swallowed contents — and yet it is still alive, still subject to the prohibition of bestiality. These two statuses coexist without contradiction: because it is food, it no longer shelters what is absorbed within it; because it is still alive, it has not yet crossed into the full status of *neveilah*, and the prohibition of bestiality still applies. The case of bestiality thus serves as the proof that the animal genuinely remains alive in a halakhically meaningful sense, while the ruling of Abaye establishes that being alive in this sense does not prevent the loss of the *balu'a* protection once food status has been conferred.

## Meat on Hide

### Rabbi Yehuda Alal

The *gemara* now transitions to the end of the *mishna*, returning to the theme of items that combine with food to constitute a minimum measure, even when they are not themselves considered food independently. The *mishna* cites Rabbi Yehuda: *ha-alal ha-mekunas* — the *alal*, the scraps of meat left on the hide when an animal is flayed, if deliberately gathered together such that there is a full *kezayit* in one place, is considered food, and indeed Rabbi Yehuda says it carries the status of *neveilah* and causes *tumas neveilah*.

### Rav Huna Condition

Rav Huna qualifies this ruling: *v'hu she-kinso* — only if the person actively gathered the meat himself. This is not merely a procedural observation. Rashi explains the significance: the status of something as *ochel* can shift with one's subjective relationship to it, and one can go in and out of treating something as food. But regarding *neveilah* — once something ceases to have the status of *neveilah*, it cannot reacquire that status. Rashi therefore explains that meat left on the hide after flaying is normally assumed to have been mentally discarded, abandoned. If someone else came along and scraped it together, it would not matter — the meat had already lost its *neveilah* status. But if the same person who did the flaying then went and scraped the meat together, that act reveals retroactively that he had never truly abandoned it. He always intended to collect it. That continuous intention means it never lost the *neveilah* status in the first place, and Rabbi Yehuda therefore rules that it is *neveilah*.

Rav Huna adds a second ruling: *shnei chatzei zeisim she-yeishan agabi ha-or, ha-or mivatlam* — if you have two half-*zeisim* of meat sitting on a piece of hide, the hide negates them. They lose their status. This second statement brings the discussion into close contact with the famous dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva that the *gemara* will address in a *mishna* coming up shortly.

### Yishmael/Akiva

The *gemara* notes the structural tension: the *mishna* we are studying is coming up for debate in that later *mishna*, where Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva argue about exactly this case — a hide flayed from an animal that is *neveilah*, on which there are two half-*zeisim* of meat. Everyone agrees that touching a half-*kezayit* here and a half-*kezayit* there separately does not render one *tamei*, since *tumah* requires contact with a full *kezayit* at once. But the question is: what if you carried this piece of leather on your back, thereby simultaneously carrying both half-*zeisim* at once? You have in effect carried a full *kezayit* of *neveilah*.

Rabbi Yishmael holds that you are *tamei*. The skin does not negate the two pieces of meat; they retain their status as *neveilah*, and together they constitute a full *kezayit* being carried. Rabbi Akiva holds that you are *tahor* — not because the carrying was somehow defective, but because the hide objectively nullifies each piece of meat that is less than a full *kezayit*. Each half-*zayit*, attached to the skin, has no independent significance; they are as if not present.

The *gemara* therefore asks: according to whom is Rav Huna's ruling formulated? *Aliba d'Rabbi Yishmael* — if it is according to Rabbi Yishmael, who says the skin does not negate the pieces of meat, then saying that the skin negates them seems to contradict him directly. *Ve-i aliba d'Rabbi Akiva* — if it is according to Rabbi Akiva, who says the skin does negate them, then Rav Huna's statement is obvious, *peshita*. What novel information is he providing?

### Yishmael Subjectivity

The *gemara* resolves: *le-olam aliba d'Rebbe Yishmael* — in fact we are proceeding according to Rabbi Yishmael, and a distinction within his position accounts for Rav Huna's ruling. When does Rabbi Yishmael say the skin does not negate the half-pieces of meat? *Hani mili she-platato chayah* — when the meat was torn off by a wild animal. *Aval platato sakin* — but if it came off in the course of your own flaying with a knife, *batil* — it is negated.

Rashi explains the logic: when you are actively flaying an animal, and meat comes off incidentally with the skin, you mentally discard it on the spot. It is like the piece of cucumber that goes off with the peel — you do not bother fishing it back out of the garbage. That implicit mental dismissal means you have subjectively negated the meat, and it loses its *neveilah* status. But if an animal tore the hide away and meat came with it, you had no role in that; you have not made any implicit decision to discard anything. The meat therefore retains its status.

The *gemara* then asks whether Rav Huna's ruling is consistent even under this reading of Rabbi Yishmael. The concern is as follows: Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishna* says that if you scrape together the scattered *alal*, it retains the status of *neveilah* — meaning that your subsequent act of gathering it reveals you never truly discarded it. This fits naturally with a reading of Rabbi Yishmael under which even knife-flayed pieces are not negated, and what matters is entirely your subjective relationship to the meat. If the skin never objectively negates anything, then gathering the scraps afterward simply shows you always cared about them, and they remain *neveilah*.

But if one reads Rabbi Yishmael as saying that knife-flaying does cause the skin to negate the pieces, then there is a difficulty: if the negation is based on the skin's objective relationship to the scraps, then Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that gathering them afterward preserves their status becomes incoherent — once objectively negated, subjective intent cannot restore them.

The *gemara* therefore rejects the reading that knife-flaying causes negation even according to Rabbi Yishmael. Rather, *le-olam* — truly and finally — according to Rabbi Yishmael, even knife-flayed pieces are *not* negated by the skin, *lo batil*. Everything under Rabbi Yishmael operates subjectively. When you flay and the meat falls away and you dismiss it, you have subjectively negated it. When you scrape it back together — as Rabbi Yehuda says — you have demonstrated that you never truly discarded it, and it retains its *neveilah* status. Both rulings of Rav Huna, and Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *mishna*, are all coherent within the framework of Rabbi Yishmael: nothing is objectively negated by the skin; it is entirely a matter of your subjective relationship to the scraps.

### Akiva Objectivity

With Rabbi Yishmael's position clarified, the *gemara* turns to Rav Huna's second statement — that two half-*zeisim* on a hide lose their status — and asks whether this simply restates Rabbi Akiva's position. Rav Huna is indeed ruling like Rabbi Akiva, and the *gemara* acknowledges the apparent redundancy: *peshita* — it is obvious, since Rabbi Akiva already says the skin negates them. What is Rav Huna adding?

One might have thought that Rabbi Akiva's principle applies specifically when the pieces were *platato sakin* — when human action, your deliberate flaying, produced the result, with the implicit act of discarding. One might therefore have thought that if an animal tore the pieces off and left them on the hide, even Rabbi Akiva would not say the skin negates them, since there was no human act of dismissal involved.

Rav Huna therefore teaches: *ta'ama d'Rabbi Akiva mipnei she-or mivatalan* — the reason Rabbi Akiva rules the skin negates the pieces is not about the psychological act of discarding at all. It is an objective reality. A piece of leather is a substantial object; pieces of meat amounting to less than a *kezayit*, attached to it, are simply insignificant relative to the leather. They are objectively nullified by it, regardless of how they got there. *Lo shna platata chayah, lo shna platata d'sakin* — it makes no difference whether it was torn off by a wild animal or flayed off by a knife. The objective principle is the same: the leather negates the significance of anything less than a full *kezayit* of meat attached to it.

The *seyfa* of the *mishna* on Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael — which the *gemara* notes is yet to be seen — states explicitly: *mipnei ma Rabbi Akiva metaher ba-or, mipnei she-or mivatalan* — Rabbi Akiva declares it *tahor* because the hide objectively negates them. This confirms that the operative principle for Rabbi Akiva is not subjective intent but the objective reality of the hide's dominance over insignificant scraps.

To summarize the dispute: Rabbi Yishmael rejects the idea that the leather can ever objectively negate the meat attached to it. For him, status is always determined by one's subjective relationship to the food. Flaying produces an implicit act of discarding; gathering it afterward demonstrates the absence of that intention. Rabbi Akiva holds that the objective relationship between the leather and small pieces of meat less than a *kezayit* determines their status — the leather renders them insignificant, irrespective of how they came to be there or what anyone intended. Rav Huna's statement clarifies that this objective principle of Rabbi Akiva applies equally whether the pieces arrived through human or natural means.

## Skins Like Meat

### Mishna Overview

The *gemara* now moves to the next *mishna*, which continues within the same broad thematic framework but introduces a distinct category of discussion. The preceding analysis concerned the hide of a large animal, which is not food and is not itself *neveilah* — only the meat matters. But there are certain animals, and certain parts of animals, where the skin is so thin, soft, and edible that it is genuinely like the meat itself. The *mishna* enumerates these cases, with primary attention to the implications for *tumah* and the status of *neveilah*:

*Elu she-oroteihen ke-vesaran* — the following things, whose skin is like their flesh:

*Or ha-adam* — the skin of a human being. In the context of *tumas meit*, a human corpse causes *tumah* and a minimum of a *kezayit* of flesh is required. If one has only a *kezayit* together — flesh combined with skin — the skin counts like the flesh for this purpose.

*Ve-or chazir she-yishuv* — the skin of a domesticated pig. Wild pigs presumably have much tougher hides, which would not be considered edible or meat-like. Rabbi Yehuda, however, says *af or chazir ha-bar* — even the wild pig's skin has the same status.

*Ve-or chatoteret shel gamal ha-rakah* — the skin of the hump of a young, soft camel. *Chatoteret* is the hump. Since the hump of a young camel is still soft, it is edible — at least for those who eat camel — and therefore has the status of meat for *neveilah* purposes.

*Ve-or harosh shel egel ha-rach* — the skin of the head of a young calf, the definition of which the *gemara* will discuss.

*Ve-or ha-parsos* — the skin of the area of the hooves.

*Ve-or beit ha-boshet* — the skin of the genital area of a female animal; even in a cow, the genital region is more exposed and flesh-like rather than tough leather.

*Ve-or ha-shilya* — the skin of a fetus. Even if the mother is a cow, an aborted fetus would have skin still soft enough to be meat-like.

*Ve-or shel tachat ha-alyah* — the skin beneath the fat tail of a sheep. The skin of a sheep generally would not have the status of meat, but directly beneath the fat tail it is soft, unworked, and effectively meat.

The *mishna* then adds certain lizard-like creatures from the *shemonah sheratzim*: *ve-or ha-anakah veha-koach veha-leta'ah veha-chomet* — these four from the second verse in the Torah listing the eight *sheratzim*. The *gemara* will explain how these lizard-like creatures are identified and why specifically this second group is listed.

The *mishna* concludes: *ve-chulan she-ibdan o she-hilach bahen kedei avodah, tehorin* — if one worked any of these skins, treating them as leather — by tanning them or by walking on them to flatten and harden them for a sufficient period — they lose their status as meat and become *tahor*. The exception is human skin: *chutz me-or ha-adam* — human skin, even after being worked and tanned, never loses its *tumah* status, for reasons the *gemara* will explain.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri offers a dissenting view: *shemonah sheratzim yesh lahen orot* — all eight *sheratzim* mentioned in the Torah have skin that is distinct from their flesh, and their skin does not cause *tumah*; only their flesh does.

The *mishna* raises the question of what exactly the line is between *sheratzim* and the larger animals — *chayot u-vehemot* — which cause *tumas neveilah* in their entirety when they die. All large animals that die without *shechita* cause *neveilah tumah*, but among *sheratzim* it is only the specific eight listed by the Torah in Parshat Shemini that cause *tumah* when dead. The practical differentiation appears to follow the criterion of proximity to the ground — crawling or ground-level creatures — though the exact biological boundary is not resolved in the *gemara* here.

### Human Skin

Ulla opens the *gemara*'s analysis with a striking statement: *devar Torah, or adam tahor* — biblically speaking, human skin is actually *tahor*. This requires explanation. Human bones cause *tumah*, unlike the bones of a *neveilah*; this is because with human *tumah* the concern is with the *nefesh adam*, the human soul, and the basis is not specifically the meat but a comparison to flesh in that it does not regenerate. Bones do not grow back and therefore carry *tumah*. The skin, however, is more ambiguous: when part of the skin is lost it may scar and partially heal. Moreover, since *tumas meit* does not require meat specifically the way *tumas neveilah* does, the skin's status is uncertain. Ulla therefore says that strictly from the Torah's perspective, human skin is *tahor*.

*Umah ta'am amru tamei?* — why then did the Rabbis declare it *tamei*? The answer is a *gezeirah*: *shema ya'aseh adam orot aviv ve-imo shetichin* — lest someone take the skin of his father or mother and fashion it into some type of leather mat. The image is profoundly disturbing, and Tosafot himself pauses to question why the Rabbis even entertained this concern. He notes that one is forbidden to derive *hana'ah* — material benefit — from a corpse in any case, so this would already be prohibited. Tosafot answers that because this particular act was so uniquely disturbing, the Rabbis felt it required an additional *gezeirah* beyond the general prohibition, to ensure that people maintain an especially marked distance from human remains in this regard. It may also be, as is suggested in the *gemara*, that there was something in the surrounding culture — perhaps Roman practices — that made this image a live concern for *Chazal* rather than a merely theoretical one.

There is a parallel image that appears even earlier than Ulla, in the *mishna* in Tractate Yadayim, in the context of the dispute between the Tzedukim and the Perushim over whether *kitvei ha-kodesh* cause *tumah*. There the Rabbis explain why the bones of a human cause *tumah* — lest a person take the bones of his father and mother and shape them into spoons or utensils. The concern about using the body of a parent for household implements runs through both sources, and the Dead Sea Scroll known as MMT — *Mikzat Ma'asei Torah* — shows that this very debate about whether bones of dead animal carcasses cause *tumah* was one of the explicit points of sectarian disagreement between the Qumran community and the halakhic authorities in Jerusalem.

The *gemara* then notes a variant formulation. Some transmit Ulla's statement in connection with the beginning of the *mishna*'s rule — that human skin, in its natural state, is only rabbinically *tamei*, not biblically. Others transmit it in connection with the *mishna*'s later clause, about skin that has been worked and tanned. Under this second version, Ulla is saying that once human skin has actually been processed into leather, it is biblically *tahor* — it is no longer flesh or skin in any meaningful sense. The *gezeirah* then applies specifically to prevent someone who actually converts human skin into leather from thereby rendering it *tahor*. The *gemara* notes the logical relationship between the two versions: if Ulla's statement is made about the beginning clause, then *a fortiori* it applies to the end clause, where the skin has been worked. If Ulla's statement is made about the end clause — worked skin is only rabbinically *tamei* — then the beginning clause, unworked skin, might carry biblical *tumah*, and the rabbinic *gezeirah* would apply only to the worked case where someone actually used it.

### Pig Skin

The *gemara* briefly resolves the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages regarding pig skin: *mar savar hai ashun vehai rachich* — one holds that the wild pig's skin is tough and the domesticated pig's skin is soft and meat-like; *umar savar hai nami rachich* — the other holds that even the wild pig's skin is soft enough to be considered like meat. This is a straightforward factual dispute about the physical properties of the skin, without a deeper structural principle at stake.

### Young Camel

The *gemara* turns to the definition of *gamal ha-rakah* — a young or soft camel. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling is cited: *kol zman shelo ta'ana* — as long as the camel has never carried a load, it is still considered young and its hump-skin is soft like meat.

Rav Yirmiya poses one question: what if the camel has reached the age at which one would normally begin loading it, but it has not yet actually been loaded? Is the operative criterion the physical act of carrying a load, which toughens the hump, or is it age — once a camel reaches the natural age for bearing burdens, does the skin become classified differently regardless of whether it has actually been used? Abaye poses the reverse question: what if the camel has not yet reached that standard age, but was put to work early and already carried a load? Would that premature use toughen the skin and change the *halakha*? Both questions are left as *teiku* — unresolved.

The *gemara* then recounts two parallel anecdotes about the same question being posed to *amoraim* in two different settings. Reish Lakish asked the question; Rav Yishmael bar Abba reported in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi the ruling *kol zman shelo ta'ana*. Reish Lakish responded warmly: *tiv lekabli* — come sit next to me. He was pleased with the explanation and honored the one who provided it.

Rabbi Zeira posed the same question; Ravin bar Chinena reported the same ruling in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi via Ulla. Ravin bar Chinena apparently began to repeat the answer, and Rabbi Zeira cut him off: *chada hava lach amartah* — you had one idea, you said it, move on.

The *gemara* appends an editorial observation: *tach chazi ma bein takifei de-Eretz Yisrael le-chasidei de-Bavel* — come and see the difference between the powerhouses of Eretz Yisrael and the *chasidim* of Bavel. Reish Lakish — a towering figure in his learning and his stature — responded to a straightforward but correct explanation with genuine warmth and honor. Rabbi Zeira — who was known throughout the tradition as a *chasid* of Bavel — nonetheless was dismissive and showed little respect to the one who answered him. The *gemara* notes this as a point of self-criticism: piety and scholarship do not automatically produce proper treatment of others, and Reish Lakish's conduct was more exemplary in this exchange.

### Young Calf

Regarding the *egel ha-rach*, the young calf whose head-skin is meat-like, Ulla says: *ben shnato* — one year old. Rabbi Yochanan says: *kol zman she-yonek* — as long as it is nursing.

The *gemara* asks how to read these two opinions in relation to one another. One possibility: Ulla means a year old *and* nursing — nursing is an implicit additional condition. Rabbi Yochanan then says only nursing matters, being more lenient and not requiring the year. The other possibility: Ulla means one year, period, whether nursing or not. Rabbi Yochanan then adds nursing as a further condition, being more stringent: the calf must be both within its first year *and* still nursing.

The *gemara* proves from the language that Rabbi Yochanan cannot be adding a stringency. He says *kol zman she-yonek* — as long as it is nursing. If he were adding a condition to Ulla's ruling, he would have said *vehu she-yonek*, "and it must be nursing" — adding to the prior standard. Since he frames it as an independent and complete criterion ("as long as it nurses"), he must be presenting nursing as *sufficient* on its own, making him more lenient than Ulla and setting aside the age criterion entirely.

### Yochanan Ruling

Reish Lakish then asks Rabbi Yochanan directly: does the skin of the head of a young calf cause *tumah* as *neveilah*? This question is, of course, answered straightforwardly by the *mishna* we are studying. Remarkably, Rabbi Yochanan answers *eino metamei* — it does not cause *tumah*. He rules against the *mishna*.

Reish Lakish presses him: *limadtanu rabbeinu* — but our teacher, you taught us the *mishna*, which explicitly lists the head-skin of the young calf among the things whose skin is like their flesh. How can you rule against it?

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *al taknitani* — do not press me on this point. *Bi-leshon yachid ani shoneh otah* — I teach that *mishna* as a minority opinion. It is not the consensus view. Although Rabbi Yochanan's general practice is to rule in accordance with the *stam mishna* — an unattributed *mishna* — here he believes the *stam* reflects only a single dissenting authority, and he does not rule accordingly.

### Baraita Pigul

The *gemara* identifies the source for Rabbi Yochanan's position in a *baraita* that discusses *pigul* — the disqualification of a sacrifice through an improper intention to consume it outside the proper time or place. *Pigul* applies only when one plans to eat something that is normally eaten; if a part of the sacrifice is not normally consumed, an intention regarding it does not create *pigul*. Whether a given skin counts as "normally eaten" therefore determines whether it falls within the *pigul* framework.

The *baraita* opens by discussing someone who *shochets* an *olah* with the intention to burn — *le-haktir* — a *kezayit* from the skin under the fat tail *chutz li-mkono* or *chutz li-zmano*. The wrong place renders it *pasul* without *karet*; the wrong time makes it *pigul* and one who eats it incurs *karet*. This recognizes the skin beneath the fat tail as *meat-like*.

Elazar ben Yehuda ish Avlom, reporting in the name of Rabbi Yaakov, and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda ish Kfar Ikum, reporting in the name of Rabbi Shimon, extend the principle: not only the skin under the fat tail, but also the skin of the head of the young calf, the skin of the hoof area, *ve-chol she-manu chachamim gabei tumah she-oroteihen ke-vesaran* — and anything in the list that the Sages enumerated for *tumah* purposes as having skin like meat — all of these fall under the *pigul* framework as well. The *baraita* adds *le-havi or shel beit ha-boshet* — to include the skin of the female genital area, which was not mentioned in the context of the *olah* only because an *olah* is male.

The critical implication: this enumeration — which matches our *mishna* — appears as the *second* opinion in the *baraita*. The first opinion in the *baraita* recognizes only the skin under the fat tail, and by implication regards the skin of the calf's head, the hooves, and all the other parts listed in the *mishna* as having the status of ordinary leather, not meat. It is on the basis of this first opinion that Rabbi Yochanan rules: our *mishna* is a *da'at yachid*, a minority position. For the skin of a large animal, Rabbi Yochanan would recognize only the skin beneath the fat tail of a sheep as having the status of meat.

### Animal Parts

The *gemara* clarifies the two specific anatomical terms in the *mishna*. Regarding *beit ha-parsos* — the hoof area — Rav says it means the hooves literally. Rabbi Chanina says it refers to *rekuvnin* — the lower leg segment sold together with the head when an animal is marketed. Animals have a lower joint below which is a bone section extending to the actual hoof; that segment from the lower joint downward is conventionally sold with the head. The skin wrapping that lower joint and the lower leg area, Rabbi Chanina says, is what the *mishna* refers to, and it is soft and flesh-like enough to carry the status of meat.

## Sheratzim Skin

### Eight Sheratzim

The Torah mentions eight *sheratzim* in two separate verses in Parshat Shemini. The first verse lists the *choled*, *achbar*, and *tzav* with the phrase *le-mineihu* — according to their kinds — concluding the first group. The second verse lists *koach*, *leta'ah*, *chomet*, and *tinshames* and concludes with the verse *eileh ha-teme'im lachem* — these are the *tamei* ones for you. These latter four are all understood to be lizard-like creatures. Identifying them precisely is difficult; one appears to be a monitor lizard, another may be a skink, and at least one has been associated by commentators on the fauna of the Torah with something resembling a gecko or even, in Rashi's reading on the Torah, a frog.

The *mishna* states that the skin of *ha-anakah*, *ha-koach*, *ha-leta'ah*, and *ha-chomet* is like their flesh. A *baraita* is cited that grounds this in the verse: *eileh ha-teme'im* — the word *eileh* is read as expansive, teaching *le-rabbot oroteihen ke-vesaram*, to include their skins as like their flesh for *tumah* purposes. The question then arises: perhaps this inclusive language applies to the entire list of eight *sheratzim*, including the first group?

### Rav Verse

Rav answers: *l'mineihu hifsik ha-inyan* — the phrase *le-mineihu* at the end of the first verse creates a separation between the two groups. The verse reads: *ha-choled ve-ha-achbar ve-ha-tzav le-mineihu* — and then a new listing begins. The concluding phrase *eileh ha-teme'im lachem* therefore refers only to the second group, the lizards of the second verse. Rav accordingly uses the biblical verse to determine which *sheratzim* have skin that is like their flesh: it is the second group alone, because the verse refers only to them.

### Tinshames

The *gemara* immediately notes a problem with Rav's verse-based approach: if Rav is going strictly by the verse, the verse includes *tinshames* — the last item in the second verse — yet our *mishna* does not list *tinshames*. How can Rav reconcile this omission?

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle that *Rav tanna hu* — Rav has the status of a *tanna* and may transmit or teach a version of the *mishna* that differs from ours. The statement *dekani tinshames* — Rav does include *tinshames* in his version of the list. According to Rav, all four items of the second verse, including *tinshames*, have skin like their flesh, consistently following the verse.

### Yehuda Feel

But our *mishna* — which is the *mishna* of the standard *tanna didan* — does not include *tinshames*. It cannot therefore be operating from the same verse-based logic as Rav. The *gemara* cites Rav Sheshes the son of Rav Idi: *tanna didan savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda d'azil basar gishta* — our *tanna* follows the approach of Rabbi Yehuda, who goes after *gishta*, physical feel.

Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishna* had said that even the skin of *ha-leta'ah* does not have the status of its flesh — meaning Rabbi Yehuda does not rely on the verse but on the physical reality of whether the skin is practically distinguishable from and harder than the flesh. *Gishta* — feel, tactile physical reality — is his criterion. Our *tanna* similarly proceeds not from the verse but from the empirical question of which creatures have skin soft enough and indistinguishable enough from their flesh to warrant the same *tumah* status.

This produces two divergent approaches to the question of which *sheratzim* have skin like their flesh. Rav proceeds from the biblical verse: the second group, as delimited by *le-mineihu*, has the inclusive language *eileh ha-teme'im* applied to it, and therefore all four creatures of that group — including *tinshames* — have skin like their flesh. Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna* of our *mishna* proceed from physical reality: the question is whether the skin is actually soft and flesh-like, and one can debate this empirically for specific creatures, just as one debated whether the wild pig's skin resembles its flesh. Under this approach, *tinshames* may be excluded because its skin is physically distinct enough from its flesh not to qualify, regardless of which verse it appears in.

The *mishna*'s final position — that Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds all eight *sheratzim* have skin distinct from their flesh — is also explicable on either framework: one could read the verse as not applying *eileh ha-teme'im* to the skin question at all, or one could make a physical determination that in all cases the skin of *sheratzim* is different enough from the flesh to be treated separately.
