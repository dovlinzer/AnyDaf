# Hullin 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bei Ilai Creatures

### Keresh Overview

The daf begins, picking up from 59b at the dividing point in the middle of the page, with a ruling about a creature called the *keresh*. The *gemara* had previously been engaged in distinguishing between *chaya* and *behema* based on features of the hooves and the horn, and had noted in passing that there exists an animal called the *keresh* — variously pronounced — which possesses only a single horn and whose identity no one can precisely determine, yet which is nonetheless *kosher*. The ruling at the two dots reads: *keresh af al pi she-ein lo ela keren achat mutar* — the *keresh*, even though it has only one horn, is permissible.

### Tagris Lion

Rav Yehuda identifies the *keresh* as *tabya d'Bei Ilai* — a deer from a place called *bei ilai*, which Rashi identifies as a certain forest. The *gemara* then introduces another creature associated with this same place: the *tagris*. Rashi acknowledges that he does not know the original context in which the *tagris* had been mentioned, but proceeds to define it as *arye d'Bei Ilai* — a lion from *bei ilai*.

### Lion Dimensions

Rav Kahana states: *tesha amot havei bein una le-una de-arye d'Bei Ilai* — there are nine *amot* between one ear and the next of the lion of *bei ilai*, a distance of roughly fifteen feet. It is clear from this description that the lion of *bei ilai* is no ordinary creature but something of a mystical nature. This raises an interesting question: if the lion of *bei ilai* is plainly mystical, why do the commentators not similarly treat the *keresh* as a mystical creature rather than laboring to identify it as an actual *kosher* species? The answer lies in the fact that the *keresh* appears in a *halakhic* context, and so identifying it with a real animal becomes obligatory for those who wish the ruling to carry practical force.

Rav Yosef adds that *shit-sar amot havei mashchei d'tabya d'Bei Ilai* — sixteen *amot*, approximately twenty-four feet, is the length of the skin or the body of the deer of *bei ilai*, i.e., the *keresh* with its single horn.

### Giraffe Theory

This dimension of twenty-four feet brings to the fore the suggestion found in the *sichat chullin* that the *keresh* may in fact be a giraffe. The animal is called a *tabya*, a deer, and indeed if one were to describe a giraffe without access to that term — noting its deer-like body structure, its relatively thin and horse-like frame — one would likely say it resembles a deer with a remarkably long neck. Giraffes stand up to nineteen feet and weigh between two thousand four hundred and four thousand pounds, making the identification with a sixteen-*amah* animal at least plausible. The spotted skin might also suggest comparison to a leopard, though the body structure points more toward a deer or horse. The suggestion is, in any case, a fascinating one, even if the identification cannot be established with certainty. As for the lion of *bei ilai* — no comparable naturalistic identification is offered, and Rashi himself confesses he does not know what it is.

## Caesar Dialogues

### Lion Comparison

The *gemara* now transitions to a series of exchanges between a Roman Caesar and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya was a second-generation *tanna*, student of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, whose colleagues included Rabban Gamliel of Yavneh and whose students included Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael. He thus lived in the period immediately following the *churban*, a historical detail that becomes significant when we consider the nature of these exchanges.

The underlying theme of all these encounters is that the Roman Caesar, operating within a pagan framework of multiple and anthropomorphic gods, consistently attempts to fit the Jewish God into a pagan conceptual framework. He takes seriously and literally language about God that the Jewish tradition understands metaphorically.

The Caesar opens the first exchange: *eloheichem k'aryeh masil* — your God is compared to a lion, as the verse states, *aryeh sha'ag mi lo yira, Hashem Elokim diber mi lo yinavei* — a lion has roared, who shall not fear? The Lord God has spoken, who shall not prophesy? The verse compares the irresistibility of God's speech to the roar of a lion. The Caesar's response is dismissive: *parasha katil aryeh* — a horseman can kill a lion. What is so impressive about your God if He is merely like a lion?

Rabbi Yehoshua responds: *lo k'hai aryeh masil, k'aryeh d'Bei Ilai masil* — He was not compared to just any lion but to the lion of *bei ilai*, which is of a wholly different order. The Caesar declares: *be'ina d'machzis li nihalei* — I want you to show me this lion. Rabbi Yehoshua replies: *lo matzi chazis lei* — you cannot see it. The Caesar insists — *ivra chazina lei* — in truth, I will see it.

### Lion's Roar

Rabbi Yehoshua prayed, and the lion was uprooted from its place. When the lion was four hundred *parsei* from Rome, it let out a single roar: every pregnant woman in hearing distance miscarried, and the walls of Rome fell. This is the meaning of *hashem elokim diber mi lo yinavei* — who shall not be compelled when God speaks. When it had advanced another three hundred *parsei* closer, it let out a second roar: the molars and incisors of all men fell from their mouths, and the Caesar himself fell from his throne to the ground. The Caesar immediately implored Rabbi Yehoshua: *b'matusa minach, bei rachamei alei d'lihadar l'duchsei* — by your entreaty, please pray that the lion return to its place. Rabbi Yehoshua prayed, and the lion returned.

The exchange makes vivid what was meant by comparing God to the lion of *bei ilai* rather than to an ordinary lion. There may also be a pointed subtext here: the Romans were famous for parading exotic animals, including lions, through Rome after military victories in distant lands. To the Roman Caesar, a lion was a trophy, something subdued and displayed. Rabbi Yehoshua's response — that there exist creatures before whom even Rome is helpless — is thus a response on the Caesar's own terms.

### Invisibility Proof

In the next exchange, the Caesar says: *be'ina d'ichzei lei l'elokeichu* — I want to see your God. Rabbi Yehoshua responds with the verse *lo yirani ha'adam vachai* — no man can see Me and live. The Caesar again insists: *ivra chazina lei*. Rabbi Yehoshua then led the Caesar outside and stood him before the sun at the height of summer, the *tekufat Tammuz*. He instructed him: look directly at the sun. The Caesar replied: *lo matzina* — I cannot; the light is too intense.

Rabbi Yehoshua replied: *yoma d'chad mishamshei d'ka'i kamei Kudsha Berich Hu, amart lo matzina l'istakulei bei, Shechina lo kol shekhen* — the sun, which is merely one of the servants that stands before God, you cannot look at directly; how much more so the *shechina* itself.

The Maharsha develops this argument further, noting that the point is not simply that the sun is bright. Rather, light is the least physical of all physical things — it is in a sense the boundary of the material world. If even that which is barely physical cannot be looked upon directly, how much more so that which transcends physicality entirely. This gives the argument its real philosophical force: it is not merely an argument from scale but an argument from the nature of the non-physical.

One might also ask about the use of the term *shechina* here, since this term does not appear in the *torah* or *tanach* as a divine name — only the verbal form, that God *shochein*, dwells. The answer may be that *shechina* specifically denotes God's presence as it is accessible within the world; it is thus precisely the right term to use in this context, since if anything could in principle be perceived, it would be the *shechina*. That even the *shechina* cannot be seen is the ultimate conclusion of the argument.

### Banquet Attempt

The Caesar now raises a third challenge: *be'ina d'a'avid lei nahama l'eloheichem* — I want to prepare a banquet for your God. One commentator, likely the Maharal, observes that the consistent logic of these three challenges is to place the Jewish God within a pagan framework. The first challenge took the lion metaphor literally; the second assumed God was the sort of entity that could be seen; and now the Caesar assumes that God, like the pagan gods, can be fed. This assumption is not entirely without basis even in Jewish thought: the *korban* is described in the *torah* as *lechem isheh*, a fire-offering, a *reiach nichoach*, a pleasing aroma. The difference is that Jewish tradition takes this language metaphorically while the Caesar takes it literally.

The parallel to the challenge about seeing God is instructive. The verse says *lo yirani ha'adam vachai*, and the *rambam* reads this as expressing God's utter non-physicality; but the simple meaning of the verse does not say God cannot be seen in principle, only that seeing Him results in death. Similarly, the *korban* language could, on its plain reading, support the pagan reading. The consistent theme of these exchanges is thus that the Caesar is reading the *pesukim* in their more literal, physical sense, and Rabbi Yehoshua is correcting him by applying the deeper theological understanding.

Rabbi Yehoshua's response to the banquet proposal is: *nefishi chiluvasei* — His troops are so numerous that one could not possibly feed them, let alone God Himself. He is again responding within the Caesar's own conceptual framework rather than directly dismantling it. He then instructs the Caesar to go to the banks of the river Revisa — a wide, open space — and prepare the feast there. The Caesar labored for six months of summer to prepare. Then *ata zika* — a wind came, Rashi explains, as part of Rabbi Yehoshua's plan all along, and *kanshenhu l'yama* — swept it all into the sea. The Caesar spent the next six months of winter preparing again, and then *ata mitra* — the rain came and *tavyei b'yama* — washed it all away.

When the Caesar demanded an explanation, Rabbi Yehoshua said: *hani knushai v'zulchai d'asu kamei* — these are the sweepers and dusters that go before God's troops, as a king's processional retinue would sweep the road and sprinkle water to settle the dust before the king passes. The wind and the rain are merely the sweepers; the main retinue of God has not even arrived. The Caesar acknowledged: *lo matzina* — I certainly cannot prepare a banquet for God if even His sweepers consume a full year's worth of preparation.

The argument here is somewhat indirect; it does not frontally assert God's non-physicality, but it gestures toward the total inadequacy of any physical offering to encompass the divine. The deeper argument — that feeding God is simply a category error — remains implicit, perhaps because Rabbi Yehoshua was meeting the Caesar on his own terms at each stage rather than asking him to abandon his entire theological framework at once.

## Daughter's Challenge

### Carpenter Argument

Having concluded the exchanges with the Caesar, the *gemara* now records a separate exchange between Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya and the Caesar's daughter. She said to him: *eloheichem nagra hu* — your God is a carpenter, as the verse says, *hamakareh bamayim aliyotav* — He who lays the beams of His upper chambers upon the waters, building His heavens. If He built His heavens, He is a craftsman, a carpenter.

There is an implicit subtext here, noted in *bereishit rabbah*: the argument that God did not create the world *yesh me'ayin*, from nothing, but rather worked with pre-existing materials — the *tohu*, the *vohu*, the *choshech*, the *mayim* — none of which are explicitly stated to have been created by God. The *torah* says *shamayim va'aretz* but does not specifically record the creation of *tohu va-vohu* or *choshech*. So the daughter's challenge is not merely mockery but carries a theological argument: your God, like any craftsman, had materials to work with.

### Leprosy Curse

She concluded by saying: tell your God *d'na'avid li chada mistorisa* — let Him make me a spool, a small item, just to see what He can actually do. Rabbi Yehoshua replied: *amar lachai* — let it be so. This is already a shift in tone from the Caesar dialogues, where Rabbi Yehoshua resisted being tested. Here he acquiesced, perhaps because her request was less a genuine theological inquiry and more a mockery.

He prayed — *bei rachamei aleh* — and she was struck with leprosy. The *gemara* clarifies what happened next: she was placed in the marketplace of Rome with a spool, because the practice in Rome was that anyone afflicted with leprosy — the medical condition, not the *tzara'at* of the *torah* — would sit in the marketplace with a spool, wrapping and unraveling thread, so that people could see the condition and pray for them. She had asked for a spool; she received one, in the most pointed way imaginable.

### Spool Gift

One day Rabbi Yehoshua was passing through the marketplace and saw her sitting there with her spool. He said to her: *shapirta mistorisa d'yahav lach Elohayich* — is it a beautiful spool that your God has given you? She responded: *eima leh l'Elahach lishkol mai d'yahav li* — tell your God to take back what He gave me. He replied: *elaha didan mehav yahiv mishkal lo shakil* — our God gives but does not take back.

This story is the most cryptic of the three. The Caesar dialogues were clearly organized around the theme of the pagan conceptualization of God. Here the point is harder to locate. One reading is that her challenge was not a sincere theological confrontation but a taunt — she was, so to speak, testing God — and the response therefore took a different form than the careful philosophical engagement of the earlier exchanges. The lesson, if there is one, may have to do with the irreversibility of divine action: unlike the lion that was sent and then recalled, what God gives in the course of providential events is not simply undone on request.

## Ox vs Donkey

### Ox Traits

Moving away from the *aggadic* material about *bei ilai* and the Caesar, the *gemara* records a series of practical descriptions attributed to Rav Yehuda. He states: *shor karsetan* — an ox has a wide body, *ufarsatan* — wide hooves, *rav reishia* — a large head, and *rav genovtei* — a large tail.

### Donkey Traits

In contrast, *v'chilufa b'chamra* — the donkey is the opposite in all of these respects: a smaller body, narrower hooves, a smaller head, and a smaller tail.

### Market Knowledge

The *gemara* asks: *lemai nafka mina* — why does this matter? The answer is *lemekach u-mimkar* — for commercial purposes. Rashi explains that this is less about challenging a completed transaction and more about practical buyer's guidance: if you are in the marketplace looking to purchase an ox or a donkey, you should know the characteristic features of each animal, so that if an animal lacks the expected features you know something is amiss. One might also suggest a narrower application: if animal parts are being sold separately — say, an ox tail for cooking — a knowledgeable buyer can distinguish ox parts from donkey parts by their size and shape. In any case, the Talmud's standard approach when physical descriptions do not yield an obvious *halakhic* application is to assign them commercial relevance: knowing what the world looks like is valuable in the realm of commerce and transactions.

## Adam's Offering

### Single Horn

The same Rav Yehuda now returns to a theme introduced earlier — the one-horned *keresh* — and connects it to a *midrashic* tradition about Adam HaRishon. Rav Yehuda states: *shor she-hikriv Adam HaRishon keren achat hayeta lo b'mitzcho* — the ox that the first man offered to God had a single horn in the middle of its forehead. This offering is not mentioned in the *pesukim* but is drawn from *midrash*, understood to have occurred after Adam confronted his sin before God. The prooftext is *va-teitav l'Hashem mi-shor par makrin mafris* — it shall be more pleasing to God than an ox, a bull, *makrin mafris*, with horns and hooves. The *gemara* asks: *mai makrin* — the word *makrin* with a *yud* implies two horns. But Rav Nachman responds: *mikeren ketiv* — the word is written without a *yud*, implying a single horn, and read in the context of Adam's offering, this is the animal with one horn.

### Horn Precedence

Rav Yehuda adds a further detail: *karnav kadmu lefarsotav* — the horn preceded the hooves. This is striking because normally a newborn animal is born with its hooves but grows its horns only later in life. This animal, however, came into being with its horn first. The same verse supports this: *makrin mafris* — horns first, then hooves.

### Full Stature Creation

This detail, the *gemara* notes, supports the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *kol ma'aseh bereishit bekomatan nivreu, l'da'atan nivreu, b'tzivyonam nivreu* — all of the acts of creation were formed in their full stature, with their knowledge or desire, and in their full appearance or beauty.

"Full stature" means that Adam was not created as an infant or fetus but as a fully formed adult, and similarly, the ox of Adam was not born and then grown — it emerged fully formed. If an animal rose up fully formed from the ground in an upright position, its horn, being at the top of its head, would emerge first — hence the horn preceded the hooves.

*L'da'atan* — Rashi explains this either as meaning the creatures were created already possessing developed intellect and instinct, rather than with an infant's undeveloped mind, or that in some sense God consulted the creatures about their form of creation. For animals, the former reading is more natural: the question of where animals' instincts come from is answered by saying they were created with those instincts already present.

*B'tzivyonam* is harder to distinguish from *l'da'atan*. Rashi glosses it as *le-tzivyonam be-dmutan she-bacharu lahem* — in the appearance they desired for themselves. The Maharshal and others note a triple play on the root *tzvi*: *tzvi* as glory (*tzvi hu l'chol ha-aratzot*), as beauty (*u-ltzvi u-letifaret*), and *tzave* in Aramaic meaning desire. The prooftext is *va-yechulu ha-shamayim ve-ha-aretz ve-chol tzeva'am* — read not as *tzeva'am*, their hosts, but as *tzivyonam*, their desire.

## Vegetation Logic

### Kal VaCheomer

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa expounds the verse *yehi chevod Hashem le-olam, yismach Hashem b'ma'asav* — God's glory will be forever, God will rejoice in His works — and identifies it as the verse said by the *sar ha-olam*, the officer of the world. Several commentators identify this figure with the angel *metatron*, understood in *chazal* as the angel charged with overseeing the affairs of this world — God's appointed agent over worldly matters. This concept of an angel with such a degree of authority is one that many find theologically uncomfortable from a strong monotheistic standpoint, but it does appear periodically in the *gemara*.

When did the *sar ha-olam* say this verse? *be-sha'ah she-amar haKadosh baruch hu l-mineihu ba-ilanot* — at the moment when God commanded the trees to come forth each according to its own kind. At that moment, *nas'u desha'im kal va-chomer b'atzman* — the grasses and vegetation made a *kal va-chomer* argument among themselves:

*Retzono shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu be-irvuvya* — if God wanted things mixed together, *lama amar l-mineihu ba-ilanot* — why did He demand that trees come forth each according to their kind? *ve-od, kal va-chomer* — and moreover, we can make an inference: *u-mah ilanot she-ein darchan latzet be-irvuvya* — trees, which by their nature tend to stand apart from one another with space between them, and yet God insisted *le-mineihu* — each according to its own kind; *anu al achat kamma ve-chamma* — we, grasses and vegetation, which naturally grow commingled and close together, how much more so should we come forth each according to its own kind. And so *miyad kol echad ve-echad yatza le-mino* — immediately each one came out according to its own species.

This *midrash* extends the earlier theme that creatures participated willingly — and even actively — in the process of creation. Here the vegetation does not merely acquiesce; it reasons from first principles, constructs a *kal va-chomer*, and acts on that inference.

### Officer's Praise

At this moment, *patach sar ha-olam ve-amar* — the officer of the world opened and declared: *yehi chevod Hashem le-olam, yismach Hashem b'ma'asav* — God's glory will be forever, God rejoices in His works. The occasion for this declaration was the spectacle of God's creatures figuring out on their own what God's will was, and acting accordingly.

### Grafting Kilayim

Ravina then poses a question building directly on this *midrash*: *hirkiv shenei desha'im zeh al gav zeh* — if someone grafts two different types of grass or vegetation together, what is the law? Tosafot notes that *kilayim*, the *torah*'s prohibition of forbidden mixtures, applies when one plants two different species of seeds together — wheat and barley, or different vegetable species. But the question here is about grafting vegetation as one grafts trees, and whether that would also constitute *kilayim*.

Rav Chanina bar Pappa's formulation poses two possibilities. On the one hand, *keivan d'lo ketiv behu le-mineihu, lo mechayev* — since the *torah* did not command vegetation to be *le-mineihu*, since that requirement was stated only regarding trees, perhaps there is no prohibition. On the other hand, *keivan d'hiskim yadeihu, ke-man d'chtiv behu le-mineihu dami* — since God expressed approval of what the vegetation did, it is as if it had been commanded, and therefore grafting it together might constitute *kilayim*. The *gemara* concludes *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

This is a striking passage. Not only does it deploy a *midrashic* narrative as the basis for a *halakhic* question, but it invokes the narrative of *ma'aseh bereishit* — the story of creation itself — to determine the scope of *halakhic* categories. The *kilayim* prohibition is explicitly linked in the *torah* to the concept of *le-mineihu*, and here the *gemara* is asking whether the *midrashic* account of how the world was created in its original separateness can expand the application of that prohibition beyond its explicit textual scope.

Tosafot raises an even more interesting possibility: perhaps the question is not about grafting vegetation per se, but about whether this prohibition applies as one of the *sheva mitzvot b'nei Noach*, the seven Noahide laws. There is an opinion that *kilayim* belongs among the Noahide prohibitions, derived from the verse *et chukotai tishmoru* — keep My statutes — understood as referring to statutes already established before Sinai, meaning the original order of creation in which each species was to remain according to its kind. In that reading, the question from *ma'aseh bereishit* becomes even more natural, since Noahide law inherently draws on the order established at creation. This makes the use of the *midrashic* narrative to determine a *halakhic* category considerably more elegant.

## Moon-Sun Story

### Co-Kings Problem

Rav Shimon Pazi raises a textual contradiction. One verse says *vaya'as Elohim et shenei ha-me'orot ha-gedolim* — God made the two great luminaries. Yet immediately the same passage reads *et ha-ma'or ha-gadol le-memshelet ha-yom ve-et ha-ma'or ha-katan le-memshelet ha-laila* — the great luminary to rule the day and the small luminary to rule the night. First both are called great, then one is great and one is small. The famous *midrash* resolves this:

*Amra yareiach lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — the moon said to God: *ribono shel olam, efshar lishnei melachim she-yishtamshu b'keser echad* — Master of the universe, is it possible for two kings to share one crown? The kings here are the sun and the moon themselves, both designated by the *torah* with dominion — *le-memshol*, to rule — and thus both kings. The partnership is inherently unstable because sovereignty is by nature singular. The moon's complaint is that this arrangement cannot stand.

### Dominion Division

God responded: *lechi u-ma'ati et atzmech* — go and make yourself smaller; you will be the subsidiary king. The moon objected: *hoil va-amarti lefanecha davar hagun, amait et atzmi* — because I raised a valid point, I should be the one to suffer? God then offered a consolation: *lechi u-mishli ba-yom u-va-laila* — you will have dominion in both the day and the night, since the moon can sometimes be seen during daylight hours. The moon replied: *deshraga b'tihara mai ahani* — what good is a candle in the daytime? To be present but invisible is no consolation.

### Calendar Role

God then offered a second consolation: *zilu limnu bach Yisrael yamim ve-shanim* — the Jewish people will count their days and years by you. The Jewish calendar operates primarily according to the lunar cycle, and months are reckoned by the moon. The moon, however, was still not fully satisfied, pointing out that the sun too plays an irreplaceable role in the calendar — the seasons are solar, and the calendar is periodically adjusted to reconcile the lunar months with the solar year. The verse itself concedes this: *va-yiheyu l'otot u-le-moadim u-le-yamim ve-shanim* — the *moadim*, the seasons, correspond to the sun, and even there the *moadim* is mentioned first.

The Maharsha notes, and it is worth following his observation, that a subtext runs throughout this *midrash*: the moon represents the Jewish people and the sun the nations of the world. The other nations count their calendar by the sun; the Jewish people by the moon. The moon waxes and wanes, is most prominent at night, and only occasionally visible in daylight — a metaphor for the rhythm of *galut* and the Jewish people's place in the world. The Jewish people's calendar, like the moon, has its periods of fullness and its periods of diminishment.

### Righteous Names

God offered a third consolation: *zil likru tzadikei bishmech* — the righteous will be named after you. *yaakov ha-katan, shmuel ha-katan, david ha-katan* — very often the greatest *tzadikim* bear the epithet "the small one," the humble one. Humility, the quality of making oneself small, is the mark of genuine greatness. There is also an implicit message here for the moon — and, in the metaphorical reading, for the Jewish people — that smallness in this world is not a deficiency but a distinguishing mark of the righteous.

### Divine Atonement

The *gemara* then records: *chazyei d'lo ka mitvatva da'atah* — God saw that the moon was still not appeased. And so God declared: *heviu kapara alay she-mi'ateti et ha-yareiach* — bring an atonement for Me, because I diminished the moon. This is the teaching of Reish Lakish: *ma nishtana se'ir shel Rosh Chodesh she-ne'emar bo la-Shem* — what is different about the goat offering of *rosh chodesh* that the verse calls it a *chatat la-Shem*, a sin offering for God? God says: *se'ir zeh yehei kapara al she-mi'ateti et ha-yareiach* — this goat shall be an atonement for the fact that I diminished the moon.

This is a striking *midrash*. Read with full literal force, it says that God sinned. Even softening the language — understanding it as God acknowledging that something necessary in the ordering of the world nonetheless caused harm, and that a form of reparation is therefore owed — the statement remains remarkable. The *gemara* deploys it to explain the unusual phrasing of *chatat la-Shem* at *rosh chodesh*: the atonement of *rosh chodesh* is not only Israel's atonement but, in some sense, God's own.

In the larger metaphorical reading, this passage suggests that even when the diminishment of the moon — the suffering of the Jewish people — is in some sense necessary or correct, it is not without moral weight. A reckoning is owed. The *rosh chodesh* offering becomes the vehicle through which that reckoning is expressed, month after month, with the moon's renewal.

## Prayer Creation

### Timeline Contradiction

Rav Ashi raises a further textual contradiction in the narrative of creation. The third day of creation records *va-totzei ha-aretz desha* — the earth brought forth grass and vegetation. Yet the second chapter of *bereishit* states *ve-chol siach ha-sadeh terem yihyeh ba-aretz* — all the vegetation of the field had not yet come forth, because there was as yet no man to work the ground and no rain had fallen. The vegetation seems to have come forth on the third day, but the second chapter implies it had not yet emerged even before Adam was created on the sixth day.

### Adam's Prayer

The *gemara* resolves: *melamed she-yatzu desha'im ve-amdu al petach karka* — the grass and vegetation came forth from the ground but stood at the very lip of the earth, *ad she-ba Adam HaRishon u-vikesh aleihem rachamim va-yardu geshamim va-tzmchu* — until Adam came and prayed for them, and rain fell, and they grew. The vegetation emerged on the third day but did not complete its growth until Adam prayed.

*Lelamedcha she-HaKadosh Baruch Hu mit'aveh li-tfilatam shel tzadikim* — this teaches that God desires the prayer of the righteous. God created the world in such a way that certain processes remained incomplete, awaiting the prayer of a righteous person to complete them. Adam was created precisely so that he would recognize the need for rain and pray for it, and only through that prayer would the creation be brought to its fullness.

### Nachman's Garden

This principle is illustrated by a practical example: *rav nachman bar papa havi lei ha'i ginasa* — Rav Nachman bar Papa had a garden, *shadi bei bizruni* — he sowed seeds in it, *v'lo tzmach* — and they did not sprout. *ba'a rachamei* — he prayed, *ata mitra* — rain came, *v'tzmach* — and they grew. The parallel to Adam's situation is explicit: seeds sown, no growth, prayer, rain, growth. The story of creation is not merely a cosmological narrative but a pattern that recurs in the lives of the righteous.

### Prayer Mechanism

The Maharsha connects this passage to a deeper *midrashic* tradition. God initially intended to create the world governed entirely by *middat ha-din*, strict justice — the first chapter of *bereishit* uses only the name *elokim*. But God saw that the world could not survive on justice alone, and so the second chapter introduces *hashem elokim*, the divine name that combines justice with compassion. The same phrase, *hashem elokim*, appears in the passage about the vegetation waiting for man to work and pray.

The theological point is that *tefillah* — prayer — is the mechanism through which God introduced compassion into a world structured by justice. The world runs according to its laws; those laws belong to *middat ha-din*. But God built into creation the possibility of prayer, so that through prayer *middat ha-rachamim* can override the strict operation of those laws. Rain is the paradigmatic expression of this: it comes from above, it gives life, and it is not mechanically determined but responsive to the prayer of one who recognizes the need and calls out.

God did not simply want man to exist in the world. God wanted man to look at the world, perceive its incompleteness, and pray. Creation reaches its fullness not through any automatic process but through the encounter between human prayer and divine response. This is, in the end, both the lesson of Adam's first act and the lesson of every garden that waits for rain.
