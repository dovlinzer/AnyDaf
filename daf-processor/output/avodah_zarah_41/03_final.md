# Avodah Zarah 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Statues & Iconography

### Statues of Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּאִנְדְּרָטִי שֶׁל מְלָכִים שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: We learned** that this dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is only **with regard to the statues [*be'andartei*] of kings.**

The Gemara picks up from the bottom of 41b, continuing the exploration of the *mishnah*'s three-way *machloket* regarding which statues carry the presumption of being objects of *avodah zarah* — that is, actually worshipped rather than merely erected for decoration. The central question is how one determines, from the statue's appearance alone, whether it is an idol or simply a commemorative or ornamental figure.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel opens by clarifying the *mishnah*'s subject matter: *be'andarti shel melakhim shaninu* — the *mishnah* is speaking about statues fashioned in the image of kings. Rashi explains the practice: when a king died, his likeness was placed at the entrance of the city as a memorial. The question of whether such a statue is prohibited as *avodah zarah* thus turns on specific visual indicators — what the statue is holding, and the interpretive weight those objects carry.

### Villages vs. Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּבְעוֹמְדִין עַל פֶּתַח מְדִינָה שָׁנִינוּ. אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁל כְּפָרִים, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁל כְּרַכִּים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּרִין, מַאי טַעְמָא? לְנוֹי עָבְדִי לְהוּ. וְדִכְפָרִים, מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְנוֹי קָעָבְדִי לְהוּ? דִּכְפָרִים וַדַּאי לְמִיפְלְחִינְהוּ עָבְדִי לְהוּ! אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר, הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁל כְּרַכִּים, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁל כְּפָרִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** furthermore, **we learned** that the dispute is only **with regard to** statues that **stand at the entrance to the region.** **Rabba says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is only **with regard to** statues that are erected **in villages. But with regard to** those that are erected **in cities, all agree** that they **are permitted,** i.e., that it is permitted to derive benefit from them. **What is the reason?** It is because they were **fashioned for ornamental** purposes and not for worship. The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to those erected **in villages, is there anyone who says** that **they are fashioned for ornamental** purposes? Those **in villages were certainly fashioned for** idol **worship.** How, then, can the Rabbis maintain that such statues are permitted? The Gemara answers: **Rather, if** such a distinction **was stated, this** is what **was stated: Rabba says** that the **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is only **with regard to** statues that are erected **in cities,** where they may have been fashioned simply for ornamental purposes. **But with regard to** those erected **in villages, all agree** that they are used for idol worship and are therefore **forbidden.**

Rabba bar Bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan then offers a geographic clarification: *be'omdim al petach medinah shaninu* — the *mishnah* is specifically addressing statues placed at the entrance of a city. Rava then reframes the dispute entirely. He proposes that the *machloket tanna'im* concerns statues in small towns (*kefarim*) specifically, not cities. The reasoning is as follows: in large cities (*kerakhim*), statues are understood by everyone to have been erected *lenoi* — for beautification, as part of a kind of municipal aesthetic project. In small villages, however, there are fewer resources and less incentive for purely decorative sculpture, so the presumption shifts toward worship.

Rava's formulation is then revised: the *machloket* in fact concerns cities, while in villages everyone agrees that statues are prohibited. The reasoning is symmetrical but inverse — in villages, the assumption is *vadai demefalchin hu avdei lehu*, that they are built definitively for the purpose of worship. It is in cities that the tanna'im disagree, because urban contexts introduce genuine ambiguity.

### Staff, Bird, Globe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָן אֲסוּרִין אַלָּא כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ מַקֵּל אוֹ צִפּוֹר אוֹ כַדּוּר. מַקֵּל — שֶׁרוֹדֶה אֶת עַצְמוֹ תַּחַת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ כְּמַקֵּל, צִפּוֹר — שֶׁתּוֹפֵשׂ אֶת עַצְמוֹ תַּחַת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ כְּצִפּוֹר, כַּדּוּר — שֶׁתּוֹפֵשׂ אֶת עַצְמוֹ תַּחַת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ כְּכַדּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** The **only** statues that are **forbidden** are: Any statue that has in its hand a staff, or a bird, or an orb, as these are indications that this statue is designated for idolatry. The Gemara explains that each of these items symbolizes the statue's supposed divinity, indicating its dominion over the world: **A staff** symbolizes dominion **as** the idol **rules itself under the entire world,** i.e., it rules the entire world, **like** one rules over an animal with **a staff. A bird** symbolizes dominion **as** the idol **grasps itself under the entire world,** i.e., it grasps the entire world, **as** one grasps **a bird** in his hand. **An orb** symbolizes dominion **as** the idol **grasps itself under the entire world,** i.e., it grasps the entire world, **as** one grasps **a ball** in his hand.

Having clarified the geographic parameters of the dispute, the Gemara turns to the position of the Rabbis — the middle position in the *mishnah* — which holds that a statue is presumed to be *avodah zarah* only when it is depicted holding one of three objects: a *makkel* (staff or scepter), a *tzippor* (bird), or a *kaddur* (globe or ball). The task is now to explain the symbolic significance of each.

### Symbolic Meanings

The staff (*makkel*): *she'rodeh et atzmo tachat kol ha'olam kulo kemakkel* — it presents the figure as ruling over the entire world with a scepter. A note of caution on the language: the Gemara employs inverted euphemistic phrasing throughout this passage, using *tachat* (under/beneath) where one would expect *al* (over), as a way of mocking the idol — implying it is being suppressed beneath the world rather than ruling over it. Readers must bear this in mind throughout.

The bird (*tzippor*): *shetofes et atzmo tachat kol ha'olam kulo ketzippor* — it presents the figure as controlling the entire world as one controls a trained bird of prey. Many pagan depictions showed the deity holding a falcon, eagle, or hawk, signifying mastery over the earth. The Roman statue of Jupiter, for example, holds precisely a staff, an eagle, and a globe — a first-century object now located in Russia that perfectly illustrates all three indicators simultaneously.

The globe (*kaddur*): *shetofes et atzmo tachat ha'olam kulo kekaddur* — it presents the figure as capable of holding and supporting the entire world like a ball. This implies awareness that the world is a sphere, a concept that goes back to Greek mathematical astronomy and was certainly current in the rabbinic era.

### Sword, Crown, Ring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא, הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן: סַיִיף, עֲטָרָה, וְטַבַּעַת. סַיִיף — מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר לִסְטִים בְּעָלְמָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף סְבוּר שֶׁהוֹרֵג אֶת עַצְמוֹ תַּחַת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. עֲטָרָה — מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר גָּדֵיל כְּלִילֵי בְּעָלְמָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף סְבוּר כַּעֲטָרָה לַכֶּלֶב. טַבַּעַת — מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר אִישְׁתְּיָימָא בְּעָלְמָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף סְבוּר שֶׁחוֹתֵם אֶת עַצְמוֹ תַּחַת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** in the *Tosefta* (6:1) that **they added** the following **to** the list of items that, when added to a statue, indicate that it is worshipped as an idol: **A sword** in its hand, **a crown** on its head, **and a ring** on its finger. With regard to **a sword,** the Sages **initially thought** that this **merely** indicates that it is a statue of **a bandit. But in the end they reasoned that** it symbolizes the notion that the idol has the power to **kill itself under the whole world,** i.e., to kill the entire world. With regard to **a crown,** the Sages **initially thought** that it is **merely a woven wreath. But in the end they reasoned** that it is **like the crown of a king.** With regard to **a ring,** the Sages **initially thought** that this **merely** symbolizes **the bearer of a signet ring [*ishtayema*]. But in the end they reasoned that** it is symbolic of the idol's supposed ability to **seal its** fate **under the whole world,** i.e., to seal the fate of the entire world, **for death.**

A *baraita* then adds three further indicators beyond those in the *mishnah*: a *sayif* (sword), an *atara* (crown), and a *taba'at* (ring). These too are iconic objects of military power and royal authority, and their presence on a statue shifts the presumption toward worship rather than mere commemoration. The *baraita* frames each of these as undergoing an interpretive evolution — an original reading and a revised, more theologically charged reading.

### Symbols & Divinity

The evolution in interpretation is striking. For the sword (*sayif*): *me'ikara savur listim be'alma* — originally, they thought a statue holding a sword was simply depicting a bandit or military commander. *U'levasof savar she'horeg et atzmo tachat kol ha'olam kulo* — but the later interpretation is that the idol presents itself as capable of killing the entire world, asserting a kind of cosmic violent dominion. Again, the *tachat* formulation inverts the meaning.

For the crown (*atara*): initially they interpreted it as a craftsman's ornamental addition — *uman sam lo atara*, a craftsman placed a crown upon it. Later, it was understood as *k'atara lamelech*, the crown of a king, symbolizing supreme rule and mastery.

For the ring (*taba'at*): the initial reading was *ishtima be'alma* — it is simply a sign of delegated authority, like when Achashverosh gives Haman or Mordechai his signet ring, empowering the recipient to sign documents in the king's name. The idol was thus originally seen as merely serving as an agent of some higher power. But the later interpretation — *she'chotem et atzmo tachat kol ha'olam kulo l'mita* — reads the ring as a seal of death over the entire world, asserting life-and-death dominion over all creation. This imagery will be immediately recognizable: it is precisely the language of Yom Kippur, where God presses the royal stamp sealing human fate. The rabbinic tradition likewise teaches that *chotamo shel hakadosh baruch hu emet* — the seal of the Holy One is truth.

What these three additions share with the original three is the same conceptual arc: a shift from symbols of local or regional royal authority to symbols of cosmic, divine power. The iconography moves from "this depicts a king" to "this depicts a deity claiming dominion over all existence." That shift is the operative halachic distinction. Whether the object held by the statue merely demonstrates royal rule or asserts divine, universal power determines the presumption that attaches to the statue.

It is worth noting that these *chazakot* are presumptions calibrated to their historical and cultural context. The symbolic grammar of staff, eagle, and globe carried specific theological freight in the ancient world. Whether those same presumptions apply unchanged to modern sculpture — where the overwhelming majority of representational statues are created purely as civic or artistic objects — is a genuine question. The principle underlying the *mishnah* is not mechanical but contextual: one must assess what the iconography of a given time and place actually communicates to its audience.

## Rav Ashi Challenge

### Rabban Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ כׇּל דָּבָר. תָּנָא: אֲפִילּוּ צְרוֹר, אֲפִילּוּ קֵיסָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** says: It is prohibited to derive benefit **even** from **any** statue **that has any item** whatsoever **in its hand.** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: It is prohibited to derive benefit from a statue **even** if it is merely holding **a stone,** or **even a twig.**

The Gemara now turns to the stringent position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. According to this view, the indicator of *avodah zarah* is not limited to any specific object of symbolic weight. A *tanna* teaches: *afilu tzror afilu keysam* — even if the statue is holding a pebble, or even a splinter of wood, that suffices to establish the presumption that it is an idol. Even the most trivial or seemingly degrading object in the statue's hand renders it prohibited according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Waste Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: תָּפַשׂ בְּיָדוֹ צוֹאָה, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא זִילוּ בְּאַפֵּיהּ כִּי צוֹאָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא הוּא מִיהוּ דְּזִיל בְּאַפֵּי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּצוֹאָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If the idol **is grasping excrement in its hand, what is** the *halakha*? Is this meant to honor the statue, indicating that it is an object of idol worship? **Do we say** that the statue is forbidden, as this indicates that **the entire world is inferior to it like excrement, or perhaps** does this indicate that the idol itself **is inferior to the entire world like excrement?** The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi then pushes this position to its logical extreme: *tafas beyado tzoa mahu* — what is the law if the statue is holding human waste in its hand? This is not simply a provocative hypothetical. The question gains traction from the rabbinic treatment of *Baal Peor*, the constructed *avodah zarah* whose mode of worship involved defecating before the idol. The Rabbis used that case to establish that even the most degrading act, if it constitutes the recognized form of worship for a particular idol, remains halachically significant. Similarly, *Markulis* — which corresponds to the Roman god Mercury — was worshipped, according to rabbinic depiction, by throwing stones at it, a destructive and seemingly contemptuous act that nonetheless counted as its proper worship.

Rav Ashi's question thus has real analytical bite: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said *any* object in the hand triggers the prohibition. Does that extend even to waste?

### Waste Symbolism

The Gemara entertains two possible readings of what waste in the hand of a statue might signify. The first: *kuli alma zil be'apei katzoa* — that this god treats all of creation as waste before him, that the entire world is contemptible and lowly in comparison to his magnitude. Under this reading, waste in the hand actually reinforces the divine pretension of the idol, and the presumption of *avodah zarah* would stand. The second reading inverts it: *de'azil be'apei kuli alma katzoa* — the statue degrades itself before the world, presenting itself as waste. Under this reading, no rational worshipper would erect such a statue as an object of veneration, and the presumption would not apply.

The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. The question was posed to probe the outer limits of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position and was left unanswered. It is worth noting that since the *halacha* does not in any case follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the practical stakes of the *teiku* are limited. Yet the fact that Rav Ashi asked the question at all may itself be telling — it is possible that Rav Ashi was inclined to rule in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view, and was therefore working out its precise parameters.

## Broken Statues

### Hands & Feet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצֵא שִׁבְרֵי צְלָמִים, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִין. מָצָא תַּבְנִית יָד אוֹ תַּבְנִית רֶגֶל, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן נֶעֱבָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who finds** unidentifiable **fragments of statues, these are permitted,** i.e., one may derive benefit from them. If one **found** an object in **the figure of a hand or** in **the figure of a foot, these are forbidden, as** objects **similar to those are worshipped.**

The Gemara now transitions to a new *mishnah*: *hamotzi shivrei tzelamim* — one who finds broken statues. The ruling is *harei elu mutarim* — they are permitted. The fact of breakage is sufficient to remove the presumption of *avodah zarah* status. However, the *mishnah* adds an important qualification: *matza tavnit yad o tavnit regel, harei elu asurim* — if one finds a fragment in the shape of a hand or the shape of a foot, those remain prohibited. The Steinsaltz edition helpfully reproduces a Roman coin depicting the foot of the god Serapis — a genuine artifact confirming that the Rabbis were not speaking theoretically. Certain cults did in fact worship isolated depictions of a deity's hand or foot, and the *mishnah* reflects awareness of this practice. The reason given: *mipnei shekayotzei bahen ne'evad* — because objects of this kind are worshipped.

### Shmuel's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲפִילּוּ שִׁבְרֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: שִׁבְרֵי צְלָמִים! הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ שִׁבְרֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי שִׁבְרֵי צְלָמִים — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא: מָצָא תַּבְנִית יָד תַּבְנִית רֶגֶל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן נֶעֱבָד. תְּנַן: מָצָא תַּבְנִית יָד תַּבְנִית רֶגֶל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ נֶעֱבָד. אַמַּאי? וְהָא שְׁבָרִים נִינְהוּ! תַּרְגְּמַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּעוֹמְדִין עַל בְּסִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says:** It is permitted to derive benefit **even** from **fragments of** objects that have been seen used in **idol worship.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **fragments of** nondescript **statues** are permitted? This indicates that it is prohibited to derive benefit from fragments of idols that were known to be worshipped. The Gemara answers: The mishna means that fragments of statues are permitted, and **the same is true even** of **fragments** of objects **of idol worship. And that which is taught** in the mishna: **Fragments of statues,** is not meant to exclude fragments of idols. Rather, this expression is used **because** the mishna **sought to teach** in **the last clause:** If one **found** an object in **the figure of a hand** or in **the figure of a foot, these are forbidden,** even if they are not known to be objects of idol worship, **as** objects **similar to those are worshipped.** **We learned** in the mishna: If one **found** an object in **the figure of a hand** or in **the figure of a foot, these are forbidden, as** objects **similar to those are worshipped.** The Gemara asks: **Why?** **But aren't they fragments** of idols, which are permitted according to Shmuel? The Gemara answers that **Shmuel interpreted** the mishna as follows: It is referring to a case **where** these objects that are in the figure of a hand or a foot **are standing on their pedestals,** which shows that they were designed this way initially and are not merely fragments.

Shmuel then makes a bold inferential move. He reads the *mishnah* as applying not only to *shivrei tzelamim* — broken pieces of ambiguous statues of unknown origin — but *afilu shivrei avodat kochavim*, even to broken pieces of objects that were known with certainty to have been worshipped as idols.

This reading is initially surprising. The *mishnah* says *shivrei tzelamim*, which is broader than confirmed idols — a *tzelem* is just a statue, not necessarily an object of worship. Why, then, does Shmuel extend the ruling to confirmed idols?

Shmuel's answer: *mishum de-ka-ba'ei le-matni seifa* — the reason the *mishnah* uses the broader term *tzelamim* rather than *avodat kochavim* is to set up the second clause of the *mishnah*, the ruling about hands and feet. The *mishnah* opens with the more general case in order to juxtapose it with the more specific prohibition that follows. Very often *mishnayot* are structured this way, with the first clause functioning as a foil or setup for the more pointed ruling in the *seifa*.

The practical implication of Shmuel's reading is significant: even pieces of confirmed idols, once broken, lose their prohibited status — with the exception of hands and feet, which retain their *avodah zarah* status regardless. Shmuel further qualifies his ruling on feet specifically: *be'omdim al besisan* — the prohibition applies specifically when the fragment is still attached to its base. A foot still mounted on a pedestal retains the character of an intact cultic object, while a detached fragment does not.

### Natural Breakage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, דְּהָא לָא בָּטְלָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת, מִסְּתָמָא בַּטּוֹלֵי מְבַטֵּיל לַהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: אִיהִי נַפְשַׁהּ לָא אַצְּלָה, לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא מַצְּלָה לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated:** With regard to objects of **idol worship that broke by themselves, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **prohibited** to derive benefit from them. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** It is **permitted.** The Gemara explains the sides of the dispute. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is **prohibited, as** its owner **did not revoke** its status as an object of idol worship. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that it is **permitted,** as the owner **presumably revoked** its status as an object of idol worship, **having said** to himself: If the idol **could not save** even **itself** from harm, **can it save that man,** i.e., myself?

The Gemara now raises a distinct but related question: *avodat kochavim she-nishbera me-eileha* — what is the law regarding an idol that breaks on its own, not through any human intervention? A dispute is recorded between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish.

Rabbi Yochanan says: *asura* — it remains prohibited. Reish Lakish says: *muteret* — it is permitted.

The stakes of this dispute concern the mechanism by which breakage affects the idol's status. Is it the mere fact of breakage that matters, or must there be an act of human *bitul* — an intentional nullification by a non-Jew — to remove the prohibition? Rabbi Yochanan holds that *deha lo batla* — because no person has come along to nullify it, the object retains its *avodah zarah* status. The breakage alone, without accompanying human intention, accomplishes nothing. Reish Lakish holds that *mistama bituli mevatla* — one may presume that the worshippers have nullified it in their minds, because *meimar amar i nafshei lo atzlah le-hahu gavra atzlah* — they will reason: if the idol could not even save itself from being broken, how will it save anyone else?

This reasoning consciously echoes the argument attributed to Avraham Avinu in the *midrash*. When Terach returned to find all the idols smashed and the large idol holding a staff, Avraham explained that the large idol had destroyed the others. Terach protested that this was absurd — idols cannot act on their own. And Avraham replied: *v'lo yishm'u oznecha mah she'motzi mipicha* — let your ears hear what your own mouth is saying. The same logic drives Reish Lakish's presumption: rational worshippers will draw the obvious conclusion.

That said, one should be careful not to take this rhetoric too far. The problem of *avodah zarah* is not that its adherents naively believe a stone statue possesses independent powers. The prophets — Hoshea, Yeshayahu, and others — employ this sharp polemic, but it does not fully capture the actual theology of ancient idol worship, in which the statue served as a vehicle or representation for a divine being. Reish Lakish borrows the prophetic rhetoric, but the psychological reality of the worshippers was more sophisticated than the argument implies.

### Dagon Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״וְרֹאשׁ דָּגוֹן וּשְׁתֵּי כַּפּוֹת יָדָיו כְּרֻתוֹת וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״עַל כֵּן לֹא יִדְרְכוּ כֹהֲנֵי דָגוֹן וְגוֹ׳״! אָמַר לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? הָתָם שֶׁמַּנִּיחִין אֶת הַדָּגוֹן, וְעוֹבְדִין אֶת הַמִּפְתָּן, דְּאָמְרוּ הָכִי: שַׁבְקֵיהּ אִיסָרֵיהּ לְדָגוֹן, וַאֲתָא אִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ עַל הַמִּפְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** from the passage in the book of Samuel recounting the downfall of the Philistine god Dagon: "And when they arose early the next morning, behold, Dagon was fallen on his face to the ground before the Ark of the Lord; **and the head of Dagon and both the palms of his hands lay cut off** upon the threshold; only the trunk of Dagon was left to him" (I Samuel 5:4). **And** from the next verse it seems that Dagon's worshippers accorded it honor despite its destruction, as **it is written: "Therefore, the priests of Dagon,** and anyone who comes into Dagon's house, **do not tread** on the threshold of Dagon in Ashdod until this day" (I Samuel 5:5). Evidently, when an idol breaks, its worshippers do not stop worshipping it. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **said to him:** Is there **proof from there? There,** the reason people did not tread on the threshold of Dagon is **that they had abandoned** their worship of **the Dagon and** would instead **worship the threshold** upon which Dagon was found, **as they stated this** reasoning: **The spirit of Dagon has left** the Dagon idol **and** instead **it came** and **rested upon the threshold.**

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish's position with a proof from the story of Dagon in Sefer Shemuel. The Plishtim captured the *aron* and brought it into the temple of their god. Each day, the statue of Dagon fell — and ultimately its head and the palms of both hands were severed. Yet despite this catastrophic destruction of the idol: *al kein lo yidrechu kohanei Dagon* — the priests of Dagon continued to be called the priests of Dagon, their devotion entirely unshaken. This is a remarkable proof: even in the face of the total physical destruction of their idol — manifestly broken by a superior power — the worshippers maintained their faith and their cult. Reish Lakish's presumption that worshippers will rationally conclude "the idol cannot even save itself" is flatly contradicted by the historical record.

Reish Lakish responds: *mishum re'aya?* — you bring a proof from there? *Hatam she-manichin et ha-Dagon v'ovdim et ha-miftan* — in that case, the worshippers did not simply continue with Dagon; rather, they stopped worshipping Dagon and transferred their devotion to the threshold of the temple, the *miftan*, against which the idol had broken. The reasoning attributed to them: *shavkei isurei l'Dagon* — they abandoned their devotion to Dagon and redirected it to what had apparently broken the statue. They assumed, in other words, that the threshold possessed a power greater than Dagon's.

This is precisely the mindset Reish Lakish is describing, just redirected: the worshippers did indeed conclude that Dagon had failed them. They drew the inference — just as Avraham in the *midrash* successively considered whether to worship fire, then water, then the sun — that something stronger had overcome their idol, and they transferred their worship accordingly. They did not abandon *avodah zarah*; they recalibrated it. But Reish Lakish's point stands: the breaking of the idol did effect a *bitul* of its status, even if new idolatry took its place.

## Safeik Vadai

### Principle Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וַדַּאי עֲבָדוּהָ, מִי יֵימַר דְּבַטְּלֻהָ? הָוֵי סָפֵק וּוַדַּאי, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** This is not comparable to an object of **idol worship,** which people **certainly worshipped,** and **who is to say that** its owner certainly **revoked it? It is** a conflict between **an uncertainty** as to whether or not it was revoked, **and a certainty** that it was worshipped, **and** the principle is that **an uncertainty does not override a certainty.**

The Gemara now introduces a new general principle emerging from the previous discussion. In the exchange about broken idols, the Gemara had posed the following challenge: a confirmed idol (*avodah kochavim vadai avaduha*) was definitely worshipped — it has unambiguous *avodah zarah* status. Even if you argue it is now broken, how do you know it was ever nullified? That remains a *safeik*. And *havei safeik vadai* — you have a *safeik* set against a *vadai*. The operative rule is stated: *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* — a doubtful status cannot extract an object from a status of certainty. The *safeik* of possible *bitul* cannot override the *vadai* of confirmed idol status.

Having articulated this principle, the Gemara immediately subjects it to scrutiny.

### Chaver Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי? וְהָתַנְיָא: חָבֵר שֶׁמֵּת, וְהִנִּיחַ מְגוּרָה מְלֵאָה פֵּירוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ הֵן בְּנֵי יוֹמָן — הֲרֵי הֵן בְּחֶזְקַת מְתוּקָּנִין. וְהָא הָכָא דְּוַדַּאי טְבִילִי, סָפֵק עַשְּׂרִינְהוּ, סָפֵק לָא עַשְּׂרִינְהוּ, וְקָאָתֵי סָפֵק וּמוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does an uncertainty not override a certainty? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a *ḥaver* who died and left a storehouse filled with produce, even** if the produce was there only **that day,** it has the **presumptive status of** produce that was ritually **prepared,** i.e., properly tithed. This is due to the presumption that the *ḥaver* tithed the produce himself or instructed others to do so. The Gemara infers: **And here,** in this case, the produce was **certainly untithed** at the outset, and there is **uncertainty** whether the *ḥaver* **tithed it,** and there is **uncertainty** whether **he did not tithe it, and** despite this conflict, **the uncertainty** whether it was tithed **comes and overrides the certainty** that it was untithed produce.

The first challenge comes from a *baraita*: *chaver shemet* — a *chaver*, a rabbinic scholar reliably known to be scrupulous in *terumah* and *ma'aser* obligations, died and left behind a storage basket full of produce (*megurah mlei'ah peirot*). Even if the fruit was freshly harvested that same day (*afilu hen bnei yoman*), the ruling is *harei hen b'chezkat metukanim* — we presume the *terumah* and *ma'aser* have already been separated.

This seems to violate the principle. The produce was *vadai tevel* — it was definitively untithed when it came off the tree. Whether this *chaver* actually separated *terumah* and *ma'aser* before he died is at best a *safeik*. Yet the ruling treats that *safeik* as sufficient to remove the produce from its *vadai tevel* status — precisely what *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* would seem to prohibit.

### Chazaka as Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם וַדַּאי וּוַדַּאי הוּא, דְּוַדַּאי עַשְּׂרִינְהוּ, כִּדְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא חוֹזָאָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא חוֹזָאָה: חֲזָקָה עַל חָבֵר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְתוּקָּן מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: **There,** in that case, conflict **is** between **certainty and certainty, as** the *ḥaver* **certainly tithed** the produce. This presumption is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Ḥanina Ḥoza'a; as Rabbi Ḥanina Ḥoza'a says:** There is **a presumption with regard to a *ḥaver* that he does not release an item from his possession that is not** ritually **prepared.**

The Gemara's first resolution is conceptually important: *hatam vadai vadai hu d'vadai asrinhu* — in the case of the *chaver*, it is not truly a *safeik* against a *vadai*. It is *vadai* against *vadai*. The *chazakah* of a *chaver* is so robust that we treat it as *vadai* — we are as certain that he separated *terumah* and *ma'aser* as we are that the fruit was *tevel* when it came from the tree.

This is underscored by the ruling of Rabbi Chanina Choza'ah: *chazakah al chaver she'eino motzi davar she'eino metukan mitachat yado* — the presumption about a *chaver* is not merely that he probably separated *terumah* and *ma'aser*, but that he absolutely never allows anything to leave his possession without it being fully *metukan*. The *chazakah* carries the legal weight of a certainty. This broader principle is worth noting: *chazakot* in Jewish law are treated with extraordinary legal force — sufficient, in other contexts, even to support capital punishment. The rabbis were willing to execute based on *chazakot*, and the principle *ein onshin min hadin* — that one may not execute based on *kal v'chomer* — marks the outer limit, not the inner limit, of *chazakah*'s reach.

### Safeik vs. Safeik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: מֵעִיקָּרָא לָא טְבִילִי, סָפֵק וְסָפֵק הוּא. אֶפְשָׁר דַּעֲבַד כִּדְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲרִים אָדָם עַל תְּבוּאָתוֹ וּמַכְנִיסָהּ בַּמּוֹץ שֶׁלָּהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא בְּהֶמְתּוֹ אוֹכֶלֶת וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that perhaps the produce did **not initially** have the status of **untithed** produce, and therefore the conflict **is** between **uncertainty and uncertainty.** This is because **it is possible that** there was never an obligation to tithe the produce, as the *ḥaver* may have **acted in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Oshaya, who says** that **a person can employ artifice** to circumvent obligations incumbent upon him in dealing **with his grain, and bring it into** his courtyard **in its chaff so that his animal may eat** from it. **And** this grain **is exempt from tithes.** Although the obligation to tithe produce that has been fully processed applies even to animal fodder, it is permitted to feed one's animal untithed produce that has not been fully processed. In light of this *halakha* it is possible that the produce in the storehouse of the *ḥaver* never needed to be tithed. Consequently, this case is a conflict between two uncertain factors, as it is uncertain whether or not the owner was obligated to tithe the produce in the first place, and even if he was required to do so, it is uncertain whether or not he tithed it.

The Gemara offers a second resolution: *v'iba'it eima, safeik v'safeik hu* — alternatively, the case of the *chaver* is actually a *safeik* against a *safeik*, not a *safeik* against a *vadai*. While the produce was on the tree, it was *vadai tevel* if it had reached the stage of *nigmar melachto* — completion of processing that triggers the obligation to tithe. But the Gemara introduces the possibility, based on a ruling of Rabbi Oshaya, that the produce might never have reached that obligatory stage at all.

Rabbi Oshaya rules: *maarim adam al tevu'ato u'machnisah b'motz shelah* — a person may employ a legal subterfuge (*ha'arama*) by harvesting grain together with its chaff and husks (*motz*), bringing it in that unprocessed state, *k'dei she'tehei behemto ochelet u'ptura min hama'aser* — so that his animals can eat it without the obligation of *ma'aser* attaching. Since the produce was never fully processed, it never became obligated. Under this approach, the produce may never have been *vadai tevel* at all — it may have been *safek tevel* from the start, because the possibility of Rabbi Oshaya's *ha'arama* always existed.

This *ha'arama* is technically permissible according to Rabbi Oshaya, though it is a matter of some debate whether rabbinic law restricts it, and it carries obvious religious-ethical weight: one who exploits such subterfuges to avoid tithing is circumventing the spirit, if not the letter, of the *mitzvot hateluyot ba'aretz*. Nonetheless, the Gemara employs it to resolve the challenge: since the produce could theoretically have been brought in unprocessed, the *vadai tevel* status was never fully established, and the case is therefore *safeik* against *safeik*. The principle *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* thus remains intact.

## Corpse Impurity

### Miscarriage Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּשִׁפְחָתוֹ שֶׁל מֵצִיק אֶחָד בְּרִימּוֹן, שֶׁהֵטִילָה נֵפֶל לְבוֹר, וּבָא כֹּהֵן וְהֵצִיץ לֵידַע אִם זָכָר אִם נְקֵבָה, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְטִיהֲרוּהוּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחוּלְדָּה וּבַרְדְּלָס מְצוּיִן שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: **And** is it so that **an uncertainty does not override a certainty? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident involving the maidservant** **of one violent person [*metzik*] in** the city of **Rimon, who cast a non-viable newborn into a pit, and a priest came and looked** into the pit **to ascertain whether** the baby was **male** or **whether** it was **female,** as the length of time of a woman's ritual impurity after childbirth, even if she gave birth to a non-viable newborn, depends on whether the child was male or female (see Leviticus, chapter 12). **And the incident came before the Sages** to rule whether or not the priest had contracted ritual impurity while standing over the corpse, **and they deemed him ritually pure.** The basis for this ruling was **due to** the fact **that** as **martens and polecats [*bardelas*] are common there,** it is likely that the body was dragged away before the priest arrived at the pit.

A second and more complex challenge to *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* is now introduced. A *baraita* teaches: *ma'ase b'shifchatah shel metzik echad b'Rimon she'hetila nefel l'bor* — there was an incident involving a slave woman belonging to a certain wealthy person in Rimon, in the north of Israel, who had a miscarriage and threw the fetus into a water cistern. A *kohen* came to examine it — *leida im zachar im nekeivah* — to determine whether it was male or female.

The case came before the Sages, and they declared the *kohen tahor* — despite his having peered into the airspace of the cistern where the corpse lay, he did not contract *tum'at met*. The reason: *mipnei she'chuldah u'var metzuyim sham* — because weasels and other scavenging animals (the precise identification of the second creature remains uncertain, with some suggesting a polecat or similar animal) were commonly found in that area and could have removed the body before the *kohen* examined the cistern.

The challenge to *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* is sharp: she *vadai* threw a fetus into the cistern — that is a *vadai*. Whether the animals actually removed it is a *safeik*. Yet the Sages ruled leniently based on that *safeik*, apparently allowing it to override the *vadai* of corpse presence.

### Fetal Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּוַדַּאי הֵטִילָה נֵפֶל, סָפֵק גֵּרְרוּהוּ, סָפֵק לֹא גֵּרְרוּהוּ, וְקָאָתֵי סָפֵק וּמוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי! לָא תֵּימָא הֵטִילָה נֵפֶל לְבוֹר, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הֵטִילָה כְּמִין נֵפֶל לְבוֹר. וְהָא ״לֵידַע אִם זָכָר אִם נְקֵבָה הוּא״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֵידַע אִם רוּחַ הִפִּילָה, אִם נֵפֶל הֵטִילָה, וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר נֵפֶל הֵטִילָה — לֵידַע אִם זָכָר אִם נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its objection: **And here,** in this case, **where** it is **certain** that the woman **cast** the **non-viable newborn** into the pit, and it is **uncertain** whether an animal **dragged it** away and it is **uncertain** whether **no** animal **dragged it** away, the Sages nevertheless ruled that **an uncertainty comes and overrides a certainty.** The Gemara rejects this interpretation of the *baraita*: **Do not say that** the woman certainly **cast a non-viable newborn into a pit; rather, say** that **she cast** an object **similar to a non-viable newborn into a pit.** Perhaps it was not the body of an infant; it might have merely been congealed blood, which does not impart impurity. Therefore, it is a conflict between uncertainty and uncertainty; it is unclear whether the item that was cast into the pit could have rendered the priest ritually impure, and even if it could have, it might have already been dragged away. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the *baraita*: **To ascertain whether** it was **male** or **whether** it was **female,** indicating that the only uncertainty was with regard to its sex, as it was certainly a non-viable newborn? The Gemara answers that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** The priest attempted to examine two aspects of the miscarried entity. He sought **to ascertain whether** the woman **miscarried,** bearing **an amorphous mass,** or **whether she cast a non-viable newborn** into the pit; **and if you say** that **she cast a non-viable newborn,** he sought **to ascertain whether** it was **male** or **whether** it was **female.**

The Gemara's first resolution: *lo teima hitila nefel la-bor ela eima hitila ke-min nefel la-bor* — do not read the *baraita* as saying she threw a *nefel* (fetus), but rather that she threw something resembling a *nefel*. The laws of fetal development, discussed extensively in Masechtot Ohalot and Niddah, establish that at various stages, what emerges from a miscarriage may not yet have the status of a human body for purposes of *tum'at met*. If the object was not yet a fully formed *nefel*, then there was no *vadai* of corpse impurity in the cistern to begin with — the entire case was a *safeik* from the outset.

The Gemara then addresses the apparent difficulty with this reading: the *baraita* says the *kohen* came *leida im zachar im nekeivah* — to determine male or female — implying the form was human enough to carry that distinction. The Gemara reinterprets: *hachi ka'amar leida im ruach hepila im nefel hitila* — the examination was not to determine sex but to determine whether what she had expelled was a *ruach* (a wind or formless emission, implying nothing of human status) or an actual *nefel* (a recognizable fetus with *tum'ah*-bearing status). The use of *hepila* versus *hitila* as distinct verbal forms supports this reading. And even granting that it was an actual *nefel*, the examination to determine male or female may have been for the woman's own *tum'ah* calculations — a female fetus generates a longer period of *tum'ah* for the mother than a male.

### Animal Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁחוּלְדָּה וּבַרְדְּלָס מְצוּיִן שָׁם — וַדַּאי גֵּרְרוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that there is a different answer: This case is not a conflict between certainty and uncertainty; rather it is a conflict between certainty and certainty. **Since martens and polecats are common there, they certainly dragged** the body away. Consequently, the ruling in this case does not contradict the principle that an uncertainty does not override a certainty.

The Gemara's second resolution circumvents the problem altogether: *v'iba'it eima, keivan she-chuldah u'vardelas metzuyim sham vadai giraruhu* — since scavenging animals of this kind were prevalent in that location, we may in fact treat their removal of the body not as a *safeik* but as a *vadai*. Animals of this type, given any opportunity to access meat, will invariably take it. Therefore the ruling of the Sages was not an instance of *safeik* overcoming *vadai* — it was *vadai* against *vadai*: the *vadai* of the fetus in the cistern is countered by the *vadai* that such animals would have removed it.

The principle *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* therefore remains intact. The discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
