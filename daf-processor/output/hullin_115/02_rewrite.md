# Hullin 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Meat & Milk

### Opening Remarks

The present daf continues a discussion that began on 114b, and which will occupy us for roughly the next daf and a half: how do we derive from the verses forbidding the *bishul* — the cooking — of meat and milk together that one is also prohibited from eating the mixture and even from deriving any benefit from it? This is a question that exercised the *tannaim* considerably, producing a remarkable variety of derivations. Contrary to the impression one might get from reading Rashi on the *parasha* — where he simply notes that the verse was repeated three times, once for cooking, once for eating, and once for benefit — the *tannaim* in fact offer close to a dozen independent approaches to these same questions, and only one of them corresponds to Rashi's familiar explanation.

## Intro: Meat & Milk…

### Problem Statement

The central problem is straightforward: the verse says *lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. The explicit prohibition is of *bishul*, cooking. How do we know that the cooked mixture is also forbidden to eat? And how do we know one may not derive any monetary or physical benefit from it? These are the questions that animate the entire discussion from here through the end of the daf.

## Rav Ashi: Kol To'evah

### Product of Aveirah

The Gemara opens with Rav Ashi's teaching: *minayin lebasar bechalav she'asur ba'achilah* — from where do we know that *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating? The verse states: *lo tochal kol to'evah* — do not eat any abomination (Devarim 14:3). The *derasha* is as follows: *kol she'ti'avti lecha harei hu beval tochal* — anything that I have rendered an abomination to you, you may not eat. If the Torah has rendered the act of cooking meat and milk together an abomination, then the product of that forbidden act must itself be considered an abomination, and therefore falls within the prohibition of *lo tochal kol to'evah*.

### Abbahu's Principle

The Gemara then continues: *ve'ein li ela ba'achilah* — this verse tells me only that the mixture is forbidden in eating. *Be'hana'ah minayin* — from where do I know that one may not derive benefit from it at all? The answer is *k'de'Rabbi Abbahu*: like the principle articulated by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Elazar, which is developed at length in Pesachim.

Rabbi Abbahu's principle is that whenever the Torah employs the language of *lo ye'achel*, *lo tochal*, or *lo tochelu* — in any of its conjugations — the prohibition encompasses not only eating but all forms of benefit, whether physical or monetary. This holds *ad she'paret lecha hakatuv*, unless the verse explicitly carves out a permission to derive benefit. And the proof that this is the standard meaning of such language is precisely from the one place where the Torah did make such an exception explicit — namely, the case of *neveilah*.

### Neveilah Exception

The verse concerning *neveilah* — an animal that was not properly slaughtered — says: *lo tochlu kol neveilah lager asher bisharecha titnenah va'achalah o machor lenochri* — do not eat any *neveilah*; give it to the *ger* in your gates or sell it to the foreigner. The Torah thus explicitly permits giving or selling the *neveilah*, i.e., deriving benefit from it. The fact that the Torah needed to spell out this permission teaches us that without such explicit permission, *lo tochal* would have prohibited benefit as well.

A *baraita* then elaborates the scope of this permission. The verse seems to indicate only that one gives it to the *ger* and sells it to the non-Jew. From where do I know I may sell it to the *ger*? The verse says *lager titnenah o machor* — give it to the *ger* or sell it — connecting the verb of selling to the *ger* as well. From where do I know I may give it to the non-Jew? The same repositioning of the *o* — "or" — connects the giving to the non-Jew as well: *titnena o machor lenochri*. The conclusion is: *nimtza echad ger ve'echad oved kochavim bein bimchira bein binetina* — whether the *ger* or the non-Jew, one may give or sell to either.

It should be noted that the *ger* in this context is understood by the Sages as a *ger toshav* — a resident alien who accepts the seven Noahide laws and dwells among Israel, but who does not observe the full corpus of Jewish law, as evidenced by the fact that the Torah permits him to eat *neveilah*. Indeed, this individual is sometimes referred to in rabbinic literature as a *ger ochel neveilah*, precisely on the basis of this verse.

### Meir vs. Yehudah

This is the position of Rabbi Meir, who rules that one may give or sell the *neveilah* to either the *ger* or the non-Jew interchangeably. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees and holds *dvarim kichtavam* — the words must be taken as written, without the play of repositioning. According to Rabbi Yehudah, one gives it to the *ger* and sells it to the non-Jew, and the categories may not be crossed.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama deRabbi Yehudah* — what is Rabbi Yehudah's reasoning? His argument is that if Rabbi Meir were correct that both options apply to both recipients, the Torah should have written the verse more explicitly, permitting all four combinations without ambiguity. The presence of the *o* — "or" — is therefore meant to separate the two clauses, not to combine their verbs. Give to the *ger*, sell to the non-Jew, and do not reverse the arrangement.

Rabbi Meir, for his part, reads the *o* not as a separator but as establishing a priority: one should give priority to bestowing the *neveilah* as a gift to the *ger* rather than selling it to the non-Jew, because there is a positive obligation to sustain the *ger toshav* in one's midst. Permission exists to do all of the above, but priority must be given to the welfare of the sojourner.

Rabbi Yehudah responds that no verse is needed to establish such a priority — it follows from *sevara*, plain logic: *ze ata metzuva lehachayoto veze i ata metzuva lehachayoto* — you are obligated to sustain the life of the resident sojourner, but you bear no equivalent obligation toward the foreigner. Priority needs no scriptural source. Therefore, the verse's only function must be to prohibit crossing the categories, which is precisely Rabbi Yehudah's position.

The upshot of this entire exchange, for purposes of the law of *basar bechalav*, is that all opinions agree the Torah had to go out of its way to permit benefit from *neveilah*, and that without such explicit permission, *lo tochal* would have encompassed *issur hana'ah* as well. The *halacha* follows the view that *lo tochal* means no benefit unless the Torah specifies otherwise, and by *basar bechalav*, no such exception appears.

It should be noted that there exists a lengthy *sugya* in Pesachim that catalogs the many instances where the Torah does permit benefit even after forbidding eating, demonstrating that in practice there are more exceptions to Rabbi Abbahu's rule than there are applications of it. Nevertheless, the principle stands, and *basar bechalav* is not among the exceptions.

### Tosafot: Trivial Benefit

Tosafot raises an important question on the *baraita*: the ruling that one must give the *neveilah* as a gift to the *ger* before selling it to the non-Jew seems to require a person to forgo profit in favor of charity. But a person is entitled to earn a livelihood — one has a *mitzvah* to sustain one's fellow Jew as well, and yet we do not require that all food be donated before it may be sold. Why should *neveilah* be different?

Tosafot's answer is that the entire discussion is predicated on a realistic assessment of what *neveilah* is: an animal that died on its own or was not properly slaughtered, more akin to a carcass than to market meat. What one could realistically hope to receive by selling such an item is a relatively trivial sum. Given that the monetary gain would be minimal, it is better to give it as a gift to the *ger toshav* and thereby fulfill the genuine obligation to sustain him, rather than to pocket a few coins in an open sale.

Tosafot makes explicit — in the lines immediately before the word *kederech* — that if a person actually wishes to sell something for its full value, he may certainly sell it to whoever wishes to buy, whether non-Jew or otherwise, without first being obligated to donate it. The priority of giving to the *ger* applies specifically in the case of *neveilah*, where the realizable monetary value is too trivial to outweigh the *mitzvah* of sustaining the sojourner.

## Challenges to Model

### Rashi's Defense

Having established Rav Ashi's model — that *basar bechalav* is forbidden as a *to'evah* because it is the product and embodiment of a forbidden act — the Gemara immediately raises a series of challenges. If the principle is that the product of a forbidden act is itself forbidden, why do we not apply this broadly? Why are the products of many other prohibitions entirely permissible?

Rashi's answer is this: *basar bechalav* is uniquely and directly the embodiment of the forbidden act itself. The prohibition of cooking meat and milk is constituted precisely by the combining of the two. The product — cooked *basar bechalav* — cannot exist without the commission of the *aveirah*; it *is* the *aveirah* in physical form. This stands in contrast to, say, cooking on *Shabbos*: the prohibition of *chilul Shabbos* is violated by the act of cooking, but the cooked meat is not in any sense an embodiment of *chilul Shabbos*. One could have cooked the same meat on Tuesday. The meat does not bear within it the stamp of the transgression. By *basar bechalav*, however, the cooked mixture of meat and milk is inseparable from the violation; there is no way to arrive at cooked *basar bechalav* without having done precisely what the Torah forbade.

This point connects to a broader conceptual question: is the prohibition of *basar bechalav* fundamentally about the act of *bishul* — with eating as merely a derivative consequence — or is it fundamentally about the eating, with *bishul* as a form of fence or precaution? The discussion here, particularly Rav Ashi's approach, seems to argue for the former: it is the *bishul* that is the core prohibition, and if one has already cooked them together, what one has produced is the very embodiment of what the Torah abhors. From that standpoint, the natural implication is that the product itself takes on the character of a *to'evah*.

One can also frame this in terms of what the *aveirah* of *basar bechalav* actually consists of. If the prohibition is understood as being about the commingling of tastes through cooking — the production of a hybrid flavor — then the cooked mixture is not incidental to the *aveirah* but is its very content. The Torah's concern, on that reading, is the existence of this hybrid taste-entity, and so of course the product that embodies it is itself forbidden.

### Kilayim Exception

The Gemara then asks: *kilay zerayim lissru* — the mixing of different seeds, which is also a form of forbidden mixture — why is its product not forbidden? The Gemara answers: *degali rachmana gabei kilay hakerem* — the Torah revealed, specifically in the case of *kilay hakerem* (mixed planting in a vineyard with grapes), that the product is forbidden. The verse *pen tikdash* is read as *pen tukad esh* — lest it be burnt in fire, meaning the mixture is *assur behana'ah*, forbidden in benefit. From the fact that the Torah singles out *kilay hakerem* for this stringency, one infers that ordinary *kilay zerayim* are permissible.

The Gemara then asks: but at a minimum, should *kilay zerayim* not be forbidden in eating even if they are permitted in benefit, given that they too are an embodiment of a forbidden mixture? The answer is that we know from a *heikesh* — an analogical link — to *kilay behema* (crossbreeding animals) that the products of such mixtures are permitted. And how do we know the products of crossbred animals are permitted? *Mi-de'asar rachmana kilayim legavoha* — since the Torah forbids bringing a crossbred animal as a *korban*, one can infer that for ordinary, non-consecrated purposes it is permitted. This inference then extends by analogy to mixed seeds: just as the product of crossbred animals is permitted to the layperson, so too mixed grains.

The Gemara thus works out a coherent picture: some products of forbidden mixtures are indeed forbidden — *kilay hakerem* and *basar bechalav* — while others, through explicit Torah permission derived by inference, are not. The exceptions are not arbitrary; they are derived through recognized hermeneutical tools.

### Mixed Animals

Similarly, the Gemara addresses the case of threshing with an ox and a donkey together — a violation of *kilay behema* — or muzzling a cow while it threshes. In both cases, a clear *aveirah* is committed, yet the grain produced is entirely permissible in eating and benefit. How can these cases be reconciled with the model that the product of a forbidden act is itself abominable?

The Gemara's answer is consistent with Rashi's framework: in these cases, the forbidden act is truly incidental to the product. The grain was not produced *by* the prohibition; the ox and donkey did not combine to create a hybrid product. The prohibition was in the use of those animals together, but the crushed grain bears no trace of the *aveirah*. It is not an embodiment of the prohibition in any meaningful sense.

### Mother & Child

The Gemara raises the case of *oto ve'et beno* — the prohibition against slaughtering a mother animal and its offspring on the same day. If one violated this prohibition, why is the meat of the second animal permissible? Should it not be considered the product of a forbidden act?

The Gemara answers: *mi-de'asar rachmana mechusar zeman legavoha* — since the Torah establishes the concept of *mechusar zeman* (an animal not yet eligible to be offered because of its age) as a prohibition specifically in the context of *korbanot*, one can infer that outside the context of consecrated offerings, slaughtering in violation of *oto ve'et beno* does not render the meat forbidden to the layperson.

Tosafot notes, however, that the Gemara could have given the same answer it gave elsewhere: the slaughtered calf is simply not an embodiment of the *aveirah* of *oto ve'et beno*. The calf could have been slaughtered the next day; nothing about a slaughtered calf intrinsically reflects the prohibition. The product here is entirely incidental to the transgression, quite unlike *basar bechalav*, where the cooked mixture cannot exist apart from the *aveirah*. Tosafot explains that the Gemara chose the answer it did because it was more readily available, not because the conceptual answer was unavailable.

### Shiluach Ha-Ken

The final challenge is the case of *shiluach ha-ken* — the obligation to send away the mother bird before taking eggs or chicks from a nest. If one violated this and took the mother bird, why is she not forbidden? Tosafot formulates this question slightly differently from the others: here it is not even clearly a case where one did an *aveirah* in the conventional sense. Rather, the Torah indicates that taking the mother bird is off-limits — *lo tikach ha'em al habanim* — and if the Torah has signaled that the mother bird is *ti'avti lecha*, something I have made off-limits to you, then should she not remain forbidden even after being caught and slaughtered?

The Gemara's answer is: *lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah* — the Torah would not have commanded sending the mother bird away in a manner that creates a *takala*, a stumbling block. If the sent-away bird were rendered permanently forbidden, then anyone who subsequently caught and ate her would be consuming forbidden food without any fault of their own. The Torah's command to send the bird away was plainly not intended to generate such a consequence. From this we learn that even where the Torah signals disapproval, the sent-away bird does not become permanently forbidden.

This case is particularly instructive for the conceptual framework being developed. The discussion across all these challenges illuminates a nuanced understanding of the relationship between *issur bishul* and *issur achilah* in the law of *basar bechalav*. The weight of Rav Ashi's approach is that it is the *bishul* that stands at the center — the core of what the Torah opposes — and the eating prohibition flows from the fact that the product of that *bishul* is itself a *to'evah*. Most products of forbidden acts do not carry this character. But *kilay hakerem* and *basar bechalav* do, because the forbidden act is not merely incidental to their existence — it is constitutive of it.

## Reish Lakish: Pesach

### Double Language

The Gemara now presents a second approach, that of Reish Lakish: *minayin lebasar bechalav she'asar* — from where do we know that *basar bechalav* is forbidden? The source Reish Lakish cites is the verse concerning the *Korban Pesach*: *al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal* — do not eat the Passover offering raw or cooked in water. The double language of *bishul* — *uvashel mevushal*, cooked, cooked — is superfluous. Why does the verse not simply say *bashel*? The extra word *mevushal* comes to teach: *yesh lecha bishul acher she'hu kazeh* — there is another type of *bishul* that the Torah similarly prohibits eating. What is that? *Basar bechalav*. Thus the prohibition against eating the cooked mixture is derived from the double language of cooking in the *Pesach* verse.

### Yochanan Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan responds sharply to Reish Lakish: *ke'urah zu she'shana Rabbi* — is what Rabbi taught something disgusting to you? Meaning: why are you inventing a novel *derasha* from the *Korban Pesach* when the *Tanna* known as *Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — already had a well-established *derasha* on the same question? Why not follow that?

### Rabbi: Psulei Mukdashin

Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi's *derasha* is based on the passage concerning *pesulei hamukdashin* — animals consecrated as offerings that became invalid and were subsequently redeemed. The Torah in that context says: *bish'arecha tochlenu hatamei vehatahir yachdav katzvi ucheayal* — you may eat it within your gates, the ritually impure and pure alike, as you would eat the deer and the hart. Then it says: *lo tochlenu* — do not eat it — referring to the blood. But the verse's final repetition of *lo tochlenu* is seemingly redundant. Rabbi derives: *bebasar bechalav hakatuv medaber* — the verse is here speaking about meat and milk.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know the repeated *lo tochlenu* refers to *basar bechalav* and not to some other prohibition in the Torah? The answer is *davar halamed me'inyano* — context. The verse is dealing with something that involves *shnei minin* — two kinds. According to Rashi, the animal in question has a dual character: it was once a *korban* and retains vestiges of its sacred status, yet has now been redeemed and is treated as *chullin*. It is a hybrid of two statuses. By analogy, *basar bechalav* is similarly a hybrid of two substances, and the *lo tochlenu* warns against consuming such a hybrid.

Rabbi Yochanan then challenges Reish Lakish: if you learned from the *Korban Pesach* rather than from *pesulei hamukdashin*, it is because from *pesulei hamukdashin* alone you could not derive *issur hana'ah*. Blood — the main subject of that verse — is only forbidden in eating, not in benefit. If the *derasha* for *basar bechalav* is learned from the same verse as blood, one might conclude that *basar bechalav* too is only forbidden in eating, not in benefit. The *Korban Pesach*, by contrast, is forbidden not only in eating but in all benefit if the *korban* becomes invalid. That is why Reish Lakish derived from the *Korban Pesach* context — to include *issur hana'ah* as well.

*Ve'Rabbi behan'ah mna lei* — and where does Rabbi himself derive the prohibition of *hana'ah* from? *Nafka lei mehacha* — he derives it from the verse *ki am kadosh atah*, which appears in connection with *basar bechalav* in Devarim 14. This verse says that Israel is a holy people to Hashem. By *gezeirah shavah* — specifically a shared use of the word *kadosh* — the Torah's statement *velo yihyeh kadesh bivnei Yisrael* (there shall be no male prostitute among Israel) is invoked: just as there the *kedasha* context involves a prohibition of pleasure that goes beyond eating, so too here *kedushah* in connection with *basar bechalav* extends to all forms of benefit.

Tosafot notes that the verse concerning *achilah* was also needed independently — so that we should not think the *kedushah* verse addresses only *hana'ah* that is not *achilah*. Rather, there are two separate prohibitions: *issur achilah* derived from one source and *issur hana'ah* derived from another.

## More Derivations

### Eliezer: Neveilah

The school of Rabbi Eliezer (*Dvei Rabbi Eliezer*) offers yet another approach, derived from the proximity of *neveilah* and *basar bechalav* within the same verse. The verse reads: *lo tochlu kol neveilah lager asher bisharecha titnenah va'achalah o machor lenochri ki am kadosh atah laHashem Elokecha lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo*. The Torah, in commanding one what to do with the *neveilah*, says give it or sell it — and then immediately concludes with the prohibition of cooking *basar bechalav*. The *derasha* is: the Torah is teaching, when you sell or give away the *neveilah*, do not first cook it as *basar bechalav* and then sell it — because if you cook it as *basar bechalav*, it will have become *assur behana'ah* and you will no longer be permitted to sell it or give it away. Thus the prohibition of deriving benefit from *basar bechalav* is embedded in the juxtaposition of the two verses.

### Yishmael: Three-fold

Finally, the school of Rabbi Yishmael offers the approach familiar from Rashi's commentary on the *parasha*: *lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo* is written three times in the Torah (Shemot 23:19, Shemot 34:26, Devarim 14:21). Each repetition carries its own prohibition: one for *issur bishul* (cooking), one for *issur achilah* (eating), and one for *issur hana'ah* (benefit).

This prompts reflection on the structure of the three prohibitions and their interrelationships. Is the *issur achilah* an independent prohibition, or merely a corollary of the *issur bishul*? Is the *issur hana'ah* an extension of the *issur achilah*, as Rabbi Abbahu's principle would suggest, or is it a conceptually independent category derived from an entirely different source, as the *derasha* from *kadesh bifnei Yisrael* implies? These questions carry practical *halachic* implications — for example, whether the *issur hana'ah* applies in a case where the *issur achilah* of *basar bechalav* did not take effect, such as when the meat itself was already *neveilah* when cooked with milk.

Rabbi Yishmael's *derasha* of three repetitions can be read either way — it could indicate three fully independent prohibitions, or three dimensions of a single interlocking prohibition. In any case, as the Gemara makes clear, this is the simplest and most elegant derivation, which likely explains why Rashi selected it for his commentary on the *parasha*: it simultaneously explains why the verse was repeated and answers the question of how we know *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating and benefit.

It is also evident, as one steps back from all these *drashot*, that in many cases the *tannaim* began with the received tradition — the *mesorah* — that *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating and in benefit, and then worked backwards to identify the scriptural basis. This is a well-established phenomenon in rabbinic literature. The Ramban, for example, explains that the rule requiring a *gezeirah shavah* to be received from one's teacher means only that one has a tradition regarding the *halacha* and that it may be derived via *gezeirah shavah*; the *tanna* then constructs the specific *gezeirah shavah* accordingly. The diversity of *drashot* here strongly suggests that the bottom line — that one may neither eat nor derive benefit from *basar bechalav* — was the starting point, and the verse-based derivations were the varied scholarly attempts to ground that tradition in the text of the Torah.

### Issi: Am Kadosh

Issi ben Yehudah offers a further *derasha*: the verse *ki am kadosh atah laHashem Elokecha* appears both by *basar bechalav* and in connection with *tereifah* — *ve'anshei kodesh tihyun li uvasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu*. The concept of *kedushah* as expressed in the Torah is intimately associated with restraining from forbidden foods. Since the Torah employs the term *am kadosh* in connection with *basar bechalav*, just as it does in connection with *tereifah* where the prohibition is clearly one of eating, so too the *kedushah* of *basar bechalav* carries with it a prohibition of eating, not merely a prohibition of cooking.

## Kal V'Chomer Logic

### Setup

With the *issur achilah* established via the *derasha* of Issi ben Yehudah from *am kadosh*, the Gemara then turns to deriving the *issur hana'ah* through a *kal vachomer*: *umah orlah she'lo ne'evda bah aveirah assurah behana'ah, basar bechalav shene'evda bo aveirah eino din she'assur behana'ah* — if *orlah*, regarding which no transgression was committed, is forbidden in benefit, then *basar bechalav*, regarding which a transgression was committed, should certainly be forbidden in benefit.

### Objection

But the *kal vachomer* is immediately challenged: there are many things regarding which transgressions were committed whose products are nonetheless fully permitted — grain threshed with an ox and donkey together, produce of a cow that was muzzled while threshing. These are clear cases where a *sin* was done and the product remains permissible. How then can we use the fact that an *aveirah* was committed as a basis for the *kal vachomer*?

### Chometz B'Pesach

The Gemara addresses a further potential objection to the *kal vachomer*: one might say that *orlah* is different because it was never permitted — it was forbidden from the moment of planting, while *basar bechalav* was permissible before it was cooked together. Something that was once permitted perhaps cannot become forbidden in benefit. The Gemara responds: *chometz bePesach yochiach* — *chometz* on Pesach proves otherwise. Leavened bread is entirely permissible before Pesach, yet on Pesach it is *assur behana'ah*. Thus the fact that something was once permitted does not prevent it from subsequently becoming forbidden in benefit.

The Gemara then challenges this: *ma lachometz bePesach she-ken anush kares* — perhaps *chometz* is different because its prohibition is punishable by *kares*. This does not apply to *basar bechalav*.

### Three-Case Rule

To counter this deflection, *kilay hakerem* is introduced as a third case. *Kilay hakerem* is not punishable by *kares*, was presumably permissible before the seeds were planted, and nevertheless is forbidden in benefit. Once these three cases are assembled — *orlah*, *chometz bePesach*, and *kilay hakerem* — neither the argument that it was once permissible nor the argument that it lacks the weight of *kares* can be used to distinguish *basar bechalav*, since *kilay hakerem* shares neither of those characteristics and is still *assur behana'ah*.

### Two vs. Three Cases

The Gemara then asks why all three cases were necessary. Why not simply derive the rule from the *mah hatzad* — the common denominator — of *orlah* and *chometz bePesach* alone? The answer Rav Ashi gives, in the name of Reish Lakish, is that with only two cases, the *mah hatzad* argument can be dismissed by pointing out an obvious exception — namely, *neveilah*, which is *assur ba'achilah* yet *mutar behana'ah*. If *basar bechalav* is only known to be forbidden in eating, one cannot simply assert that everything forbidden in eating is forbidden in benefit, since *neveilah* disproves that.

This leads to the broader logical principle articulated here. When deriving a rule from the common denominator of two cases, even a trivial internal challenge suffices to dismiss the rule's application. When one argues from the model of a single paradigmatic case (*binyan av*), a good internal reason is needed to dismiss its application to a new case. And when one derives from a *mah hatzad* of three cases, the analysis becomes more nuanced. Rav Mordechai transmits in the name of Reish Lakish: *kol mah hatzad migufo parchinan, me'alma lo parchinan* — when arguing from a common denominator, one may only challenge internally, not by pointing to external counterexamples.

When drawing from two cases, even a trivial internal distinction suffices to deflect the rule. When drawing from a *mah hatzad* of three cases, the situation depends on how the argument is structured. If the conclusion still rests on the common denominator of all three, a trivial question still suffices to dismiss it. But if, as in our case, one of the three cases — *kilay hakerem* — itself serves as a direct example that shares none of the supposedly distinguishing characteristics, then only a *kula ve'chumra* argument, a genuine reason of leniency or stringency, can deflect the rule; a trivial dismissal no longer suffices.

The upshot is this: *basar bechalav* is known to be *assur ba'achilah* by virtue of the principle of *am kadosh*. From that foundation, the *kal vachomer* establishes *issur hana'ah* by comparing *basar bechalav* to *orlah*, *chometz bePesach*, and *kilay hakerem* — all of which are *assur ba'achilah* and *assur behana'ah*, and from whose common denominator, properly reinforced by three cases, we apply the rule to *basar bechalav* as well.

Throughout this discussion, the centrality of the concept of *kedushah* — holiness — as a value instantiated through the discipline of eating is brought into sharp relief. The verse *ki am kadosh atah* does not merely add a prohibition of cooking; it signals that Israel's sanctity is expressed, in significant part, through restraint from forbidden foods. When that verse is affixed to the law of *basar bechalav*, it communicates not merely that cooking is prohibited but that eating — and indeed all benefit — is incompatible with the holiness the Torah demands.
