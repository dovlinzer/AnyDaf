# Hullin 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Meat & Milk

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִנַּיִן לְבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁאָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא תֹאכַל כׇּל תּוֹעֵבָה״ – כֹּל שֶׁתִּעַבְתִּי לָךְ הֲרֵי הוּא בְּבַל תֹּאכַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: From where** is it derived **that meat** cooked **in milk is prohibited for consumption,** even though the verse explicitly prohibits only cooking? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** elsewhere: **"You shall not eat any abominable thing"** (Deuteronomy 14:3). This verse teaches that with regard to **any** practice **that I have made abominable,** i.e., forbidden, **to you,** the product **is prohibited for consumption.**

The present daf continues a discussion that began on 114b, and which will occupy us for roughly the next daf and a half: how do we derive from the verses forbidding the *bishul* — the cooking — of meat and milk together that one is also prohibited from eating the mixture and even from deriving any benefit from it? This is a question that exercised the *tannaim* considerably, producing a remarkable variety of derivations. Contrary to the impression one might get from reading Rashi on the *parasha* — where he simply notes that the verse was repeated three times, once for cooking, once for eating, and once for benefit — the *tannaim* in fact offer close to a dozen independent approaches to these same questions, and only one of them corresponds to Rashi's familiar explanation.

## Intro: Meat & Milk (II)

### Problem Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה, בַּהֲנָאָה מִנַּיִן? כִּדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא יֹאכַל״, ״לֹא תֹאכַל״, ״לֹא תֹּאכְלוּ״ – אֶחָד אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה וְאֶחָד אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה בַּמַּשְׁמָע, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁפָּרַט לְךָ בִּנְבֵלָה לַגֵּר בִּנְתִינָה וְלַגּוֹי בִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived **only** that meat cooked in milk is prohibited **with regard to consumption; from where** do I derive that it is also prohibited **with regard to benefit?** It is derived **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Abbahu, as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: Wherever it is stated, "He shall not eat,"** or **"you,"** in the singular, **"shall not eat,"** or **"you,"** in the plural, **"shall not eat," both a prohibition** against **eating and a prohibition** against deriving **benefit** are indicated. This is so **unless the verse specifies for you** that one may derive benefit, **in the manner that it specified for you with regard to an animal carcass,** from which the verse explicitly permits one to derive benefit, as it states: "You may sell it to a foreigner" (Deuteronomy 14:21). Accordingly, one may provide such meat **to** a gentile **resident alien** in Eretz Yisrael **by giving** it to him as a gift, **and to** any other **gentile by sale.**

The central problem is straightforward: the verse says *lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. The explicit prohibition is of *bishul*, cooking. How do we know that the cooked mixture is also forbidden to eat? And how do we know one may not derive any monetary or physical benefit from it? These are the questions that animate the entire discussion from here through the end of the daf.

## Rav Ashi: Kol To'evah

### Product of Aveirah

The Gemara opens with Rav Ashi's teaching: *minayin lebasar bechalav she'asur ba'achilah* — from where do we know that *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating? The verse states: *lo tochal kol to'evah* — do not eat any abomination (Devarim 14:3). The *derasha* is as follows: *kol she'ti'avti lecha harei hu beval tochal* — anything that I have rendered an abomination to you, you may not eat. If the Torah has rendered the act of cooking meat and milk together an abomination, then the product of that forbidden act must itself be considered an abomination, and therefore falls within the prohibition of *lo tochal kol to'evah*.

### Abbahu's Principle

This verse tells me only that the mixture is forbidden in eating. *Be'hana'ah minayin* — from where do I know that one may not derive benefit from it at all? The answer is *k'de'Rabbi Abbahu*: like the principle articulated by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Elazar, which is developed at length in Pesachim.

Rabbi Abbahu's principle is that whenever the Torah employs the language of *lo ye'achel*, *lo tochal*, or *lo tochelu* — in any of its conjugations — the prohibition encompasses not only eating but all forms of benefit, whether physical or monetary. This holds *ad she'paret lecha hakatuv*, unless the verse explicitly carves out a permission to derive benefit. And the proof that this is the standard meaning of such language is precisely from the one place where the Torah did make such an exception explicit — namely, the case of *neveilah*.

### Neveilah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇּל נְבֵלָה לַגֵּר אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תִּתְּנֶנָּה וַאֲכָלָהּ אוֹ מָכוֹר לְנׇכְרִי״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְגֵר בִּנְתִינָה, וּלְגוֹי בִּמְכִירָה; לְגֵר בִּמְכִירָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לַגֵּר תִּתְּנֶנָּה אוֹ מָכוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall not eat of any unslaughtered animal carcass; you may give it to the resident alien who is within your gates, that he may eat it; or you may sell it to a foreigner;** for you are a sacred people to the Lord your God." From this verse **I have** derived **only** that it is permitted to provide such meat **to a resident alien through giving and to a gentile through selling. From where** is it derived that it is permitted to transfer an unslaughtered animal **to a resident alien through selling** as well? **The verse states: "You may give it to the stranger…or you may sell it,"** indicating that one has the option to do either of these.

The verse concerning *neveilah* — an animal that was not properly slaughtered — says: *lo tochlu kol neveilah lager asher bisharcha titnenah va'achalah o machor lenochri* — do not eat any *neveilah*; give it to the *ger* in your gates or sell it to the foreigner. The Torah thus explicitly permits giving or selling the *neveilah*, i.e., deriving benefit from it. The fact that the Torah needed to spell out this permission teaches us that without such explicit permission, *lo tochal* would have prohibited benefit as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגוֹי בִּנְתִינָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּתְּנֶנָּה… אוֹ מָכוֹר לְנׇכְרִי״. נִמְצָא אֶחָד גֵּר וְאֶחָד גּוֹי, בֵּין בִּמְכִירָה בֵּין בִּנְתִינָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן, לְגֵר בִּנְתִינָה וְלַגּוֹי בִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that it is permitted **to a gentile through giving** as well? **The verse states: "You may give it**…**or you may sell it to a foreigner."** Consequently, **one finds** that he may transfer an animal carcass to **both a resident alien and a gentile, both through selling or through giving.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** These **matters are** to be understood **as they are written;** one may transfer an unslaughtered animal carcass **to a resident alien** only **through giving, and to a gentile** only **through selling.**

A *baraita* then elaborates the scope of this permission. The verse seems to indicate only that one gives it to the *ger* and sells it to the non-Jew. From where do I know I may sell it to the *ger*? The verse says *lager titnenah o machor* — give it to the *ger* or sell it — connecting the verb of selling to the *ger* as well. From where do I know I may give it to the non-Jew? The same repositioning of the *o* — "or" — connects the giving to the non-Jew as well: *titnena o machor lenochri*. The conclusion is: *nimtza echad ger ve'echad oved kochavim bein bimchira bein binetina* — whether the *ger* or the non-Jew, one may give or sell to either.

It should be noted that the *ger* in this context is understood by the Sages as a *ger toshav* — a resident alien who accepts the seven Noahide laws and dwells among Israel, but who does not observe the full corpus of Jewish law, as evidenced by the fact that the Torah permits him to eat *neveilah*. Indeed, this individual is sometimes referred to in rabbinic literature as a *ger ochel neveilah*, precisely on the basis of this verse.

### Meir vs. Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇל נְבֵלָה לַגֵּר אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תִּתְּנֶנָּה וַאֲכָלָהּ וּמָכוֹר״ – ״אוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִדְבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן הוּא דַּאֲתָא: לְגֵר בִּנְתִינָה, וְלַגּוֹי בִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Yehuda holds that **if it enters your mind** to understand the verse **in accordance** with that which **Rabbi Meir says,** then **let the Merciful One write: You shall not eat of any animal carcass, you may give it to the resident alien who is within your gates that he may eat it and** also **you may sell it** to a foreigner. **Why do I** need the word **"or"** between these two options? **Learn from it that it comes to** teach that the **matters are** to be understood **as they are written: To a resident alien through giving** alone, **and to** any other **gentile through selling.**

This is the position of Rabbi Meir, who rules that one may give or sell the *neveilah* to either the *ger* or the non-Jew interchangeably. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees and holds *dvarim kichtavam* — the words must be taken as written, without the play of repositioning. According to Rabbi Yehudah, one gives it to the *ger* and sells it to the non-Jew, and the categories may not be crossed.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama deRabbi Yehudah* — what is Rabbi Yehudah's reasoning? His argument is that if Rabbi Meir were correct that both options apply to both recipients, the Torah should have written the verse more explicitly, permitting all four combinations without ambiguity. The presence of the *o* — "or" — is therefore meant to separate the two clauses, not to combine their verbs. Give to the *ger*, sell to the non-Jew, and do not reverse the arrangement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אָמַר לָךְ: הַאי ״אוֹ״ לְהַקְדִּים נְתִינָה דְּגֵר לִמְכִירָה דְּגוֹי. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְהַקְדִּים נְתִינָה דְגֵר לִמְכִירָה דְּגוֹי לָא צְרִיךְ קְרָא, סְבָרָא הוּא: זֶה אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ, וְזֶה אִי אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה לְהַחֲיוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Meir** explain the wording of the verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Meir could **say to you** that **this** word **"or"** teaches one **to prioritize to giving to a resident alien over selling to a gentile. And Rabbi Yehudah** holds that the **priority of giving to a resident alien over selling to a gentile does not require a verse,** as **it is** based on **logical reasoning: You are commanded** by the Torah **to sustain this** resident alien, **but you are not commanded to sustain that** gentile.

Rabbi Meir, for his part, reads the *o* not as a separator but as establishing a priority: one should give priority to bestowing the *neveilah* as a gift to the *ger* rather than selling it to the non-Jew, because there is a positive obligation to sustain the *ger toshav* in one's midst. Permission exists to do all of the above, but priority must be given to the welfare of the sojourner.

Rabbi Yehudah responds that no verse is needed to establish such a priority — it follows from *sevara*, plain logic: *ze ata metzuva lehachayoto veze i ata metzuva lehachayoto* — you are obligated to sustain the life of the resident sojourner, but you bear no equivalent obligation toward the foreigner. Priority needs no scriptural source. Therefore, the verse's only function must be to prohibit crossing the categories, which is precisely Rabbi Yehudah's position.

The upshot of this entire exchange, for purposes of the law of *basar bechalav*, is that all opinions agree the Torah had to go out of its way to permit benefit from *neveilah*, and that without such explicit permission, *lo tochal* would have encompassed *issur hana'ah* as well. The *halacha* follows the view that *lo tochal* means no benefit unless the Torah specifies otherwise, and by *basar bechalav*, no such exception appears.

It should be noted that there exists a lengthy *sugya* in Pesachim that catalogs the many instances where the Torah does permit benefit even after forbidding eating, demonstrating that in practice there are more exceptions to Rabbi Abbahu's rule than there are applications of it. Nevertheless, the principle stands, and *basar bechalav* is not among the exceptions.

### Tosafot: Trivial Benefit

Tosafot raises an important question on the *baraita*: the ruling that one must give the *neveilah* as a gift to the *ger* before selling it to the non-Jew seems to require a person to forgo profit in favor of charity. But a person is entitled to earn a livelihood — one has a *mitzvah* to sustain one's fellow Jew as well, and yet we do not require that all food be donated before it may be sold. Why should *neveilah* be different?

Tosafot's answer is that the entire discussion is predicated on a realistic assessment of what *neveilah* is: an animal that died on its own or was not properly slaughtered, more akin to a carcass than to market meat. What one could realistically hope to receive by selling such an item is a relatively trivial sum. Given that the monetary gain would be minimal, it is better to give it as a gift to the *ger toshav* and thereby fulfill the genuine obligation to sustain him, rather than to pocket a few coins in an open sale.

Tosafot makes explicit — in the lines immediately before the word *kederech* — that if a person actually wishes to sell something for its full value, he may certainly sell it to whoever wishes to buy, whether non-Jew or otherwise, without first being obligated to donate it. The priority of giving to the *ger* applies specifically in the case of *neveilah*, where the realizable monetary value is too trivial to outweigh the *mitzvah* of sustaining the sojourner.

## Challenges to Model

### Rashi's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, מַעֲשֵׂה שַׁבָּת לִיתַּסְרוּ, דְּהָא ״תִּיעַבְתִּי לְךָ״ הוּא. אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הִיא לָכֶם״ – הִיא קֹדֶשׁ, וְאֵין מַעֲשֶׂיהָ קֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi stated above that the product of any practice described in the Torah as abominable is prohibited for consumption. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then **let** the product of **an action** that desecrates **Shabbat,** e.g., food cooked on Shabbat, **be prohibited** for consumption, **as** desecration of Shabbat **is** a practice of which God states: **I have made** it **abominable to you,** in that it is prohibited to cook on Shabbat. How can this food be prohibited only to the one who cooked it as a penalty by rabbinic law, but be permitted to others (see 15a)? The Gemara answers: A product of the desecration of Shabbat is an exception to the rule, as **the verse states** with regard to Shabbat: **"For it is sacred to you"** (Exodus 31:14). One may infer: **It,** Shabbat itself, **is sacred, but** the products of **actions** that desecrate **it are not sacred,** i.e., not prohibited.

Having established Rav Ashi's model — that *basar bechalav* is forbidden as a *to'evah* because it is the product and embodiment of a forbidden act — the Gemara immediately raises a series of challenges. If the principle is that the product of a forbidden act is itself forbidden, why do we not apply this broadly? Why are the products of many other prohibitions entirely permissible?

Rashi's answer is this: *basar bechalav* is uniquely and directly the embodiment of the forbidden act itself. The prohibition of cooking meat and milk is constituted precisely by the combining of the two. The product — cooked *basar bechalav* — cannot exist without the commission of the *aveirah*; it *is* the *aveirah* in physical form. This stands in contrast to, say, cooking on *Shabbos*: the prohibition of *chilul Shabbos* is violated by the act of cooking, but the cooked meat is not in any sense an embodiment of *chilul Shabbos*. One could have cooked the same meat on Tuesday. The meat does not bear within it the stamp of the transgression. By *basar bechalav*, however, the cooked mixture of meat and milk is inseparable from the violation; there is no way to arrive at cooked *basar bechalav* without having done precisely what the Torah forbade.

This point connects to a broader conceptual question: is the prohibition of *basar bechalav* fundamentally about the act of *bishul* — with eating as merely a derivative consequence — or is it fundamentally about the eating, with *bishul* as a form of fence or precaution? The discussion here, particularly Rav Ashi's approach, seems to argue for the former: it is the *bishul* that is the core prohibition, and if one has already cooked them together, what one has produced is the very embodiment of what the Torah abhors. From that standpoint, the natural implication is that the product itself takes on the character of a *to'evah*.

One can also frame this in terms of what the *aveirah* of *basar bechalav* actually consists of. If the prohibition is understood as being about the commingling of tastes through cooking — the production of a hybrid flavor — then the cooked mixture is not incidental to the *aveirah* but is its very content. The Torah's concern, on that reading, is the existence of this hybrid taste-entity, and so of course the product that embodies it is itself forbidden.

### Kilayim Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּלְאֵי זְרָעִים לִיתַּסְרוּ, דְּהָא ״תִּיעַבְתִּי לְךָ״ הוּא! מִדְּגַלִּי רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם ״פֶּן תִּקְדַּשׁ״ – ״פֶּן תּוּקַד אֵשׁ״, מִכְּלָל דְּכִלְאֵי זְרָעִים שְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Diverse kinds of seeds** sown together **should be prohibited** for consumption, **as** this **is** a matter whose practice **I have made abominable to you.** The Gemara explains: One learns otherwise **from** the fact **that the Merciful One revealed with regard to diverse kinds in a vineyard:** "You shall not sow your vineyard with two kinds of seed; **lest** the growth of the seed that you will sow **be forfeited [*pen tikdash*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:9). The Sages read the phrase "be forfeited [*pen tikdash*]" as though it states: **Lest it be burned [*pen tukad esh*],** indicating that diverse kinds in a vineyard must be destroyed so that no benefit is derived from them. Since this is stated specifically with regard to a vineyard, one can conclude **by inference that** other **diverse kinds of seeds are permitted.**

The Gemara then asks: *kilay zerayim lissru* — the mixing of different seeds, which is also a form of forbidden mixture — why is its product not forbidden? The Gemara answers: *degali rachmana gabei kilay hakerem* — the Torah revealed, specifically in the case of *kilay hakerem* (mixed planting in a vineyard with grapes), that the product is forbidden. The verse *pen tikdash* is read as *pen tukad esh* — lest it be burnt in fire, meaning the mixture is *assur behana'ah*, forbidden in benefit. From the fact that the Torah singles out *kilay hakerem* for this stringency, one infers that ordinary *kilay zerayim* are permissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כִּלְאֵי כֶרֶם אֲסוּרִין בֵּין בַּאֲכִילָה בֵּין בַּהֲנָאָה, כִּלְאֵי זְרָעִים בַּאֲכִילָה אֲסִירִי, בַּהֲנָאָה שְׁרוּ. אִיתַּקּוּשׁ לְכִלְאֵי בְהֵמָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּהֶמְתְּךָ לֹא תַרְבִּיעַ כִּלְאַיִם שָׂדְךָ לֹא תִזְרַע כִּלְאָיִם״, מָה בְּהֶמְתְּךָ – הַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנָּה מוּתֶּרֶת, אַף שָׂדְךָ – הַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But say** instead that the uniqueness of **diverse kinds in a vineyard** is that they **are prohibited both for consumption and for benefit,** whereas **diverse kinds of seeds are prohibited for consumption** but **are permitted for benefit.** The Gemara responds: One cannot say that diverse kinds of seeds are prohibited for consumption, as **they are juxtaposed** in the Torah **to diverse kinds of animals,** whose offspring are permitted. **As it is written: "You shall not let your cattle mate with a diverse kind; you shall not sow your field with two kinds of seed"** (Leviticus 19:19). The juxtaposition teaches that **just as** with regard to **your cattle,** the animal **that comes from** diverse species **is permitted, so too,** with regard to **your field, that** produce **that comes from** diverse seeds **is permitted.**

The Gemara then asks: but at a minimum, should *kilay zerayim* not be forbidden in eating even if they are permitted in benefit, given that they too are an embodiment of a forbidden mixture? The answer is that we know from a *heikesh* — an analogical link — to *kilay behema* (crossbreeding animals) that the products of such mixtures are permitted. And how do we know the products of crossbred animals are permitted? *Mi-de'asar rachmana kilayim legavoha* — since the Torah forbids bringing a crossbred animal as a *korban*, one can infer that for ordinary, non-consecrated purposes it is permitted. This inference then extends by analogy to mixed seeds: just as the product of crossbred animals is permitted to the layperson, so too mixed grains.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִלְאֵי בְהֵמָה גּוּפַיְיהוּ מְנָא לַן? מִדַּאֲסַר רַחֲמָנָא כִּלְאַיִם לַגָּבוֹהַּ, מִכְּלָל דִּלְהֶדְיוֹט שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **diverse kinds of animals themselves, from where do we** derive that their offspring is permitted? The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that the Merciful One prohibits** the offspring of **diverse kinds** of animals for sacrifice **to the Most High** upon the altar (see 38b), learn **by inference that they are permitted to ordinary** people for consumption.

The Gemara thus works out a coherent picture: some products of forbidden mixtures are indeed forbidden — *kilay hakerem* and *basar bechalav* — while others, through explicit Torah permission derived by inference, are not. The exceptions are not arbitrary; they are derived through recognized hermeneutical tools.

### Mixed Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹרֵשׁ בְּשׁוֹר וּבַחֲמוֹר, וְחוֹסֵם פִּי פָרָה וְדָשׁ בָּהּ, לִיתַּסְרוּ, דְּהָא ״תִּיעַבְתִּי לְךָ״ הוּא! הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה שַׁבָּת דַּחֲמִירָא, מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוּתָּרִים; הָנֵי, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: If **one plows with an ox and with a donkey** together, thereby violating the prohibition in Deuteronomy 22:10, **or** if **one muzzles the mouth of a cow and threshes with it,** thereby violating the prohibition in Deuteronomy 25:4, the seeds or threshed grain **should be prohibited** for consumption according to the above principle, **as** these are both practices **I have made abominable to you.** The Gemara responds: **Now** that it has been established that products of desecration of Shabbat are permitted, one can derive the following using an *a fortiori* inference: **Just as** with regard to **Shabbat, which is** treated more **stringently,** the products of prohibited **actions are permitted;** is it **not all the more so** with regard to **these** prohibitions of plowing with an ox and a donkey and threshing with a muzzled animal, which are treated less stringently, that the products of prohibited actions should be permitted?

Similarly, the Gemara addresses the case of threshing with an ox and a donkey together — a violation of *kilay behema* — or muzzling a cow while it threshes. In both cases, a clear *aveirah* is committed, yet the grain produced is entirely permissible in eating and benefit. How can these cases be reconciled with the model that the product of a forbidden act is itself abominable?

The Gemara's answer is consistent with Rashi's framework: in these cases, the forbidden act is truly incidental to the product. The grain was not produced *by* the prohibition; the ox and donkey did not combine to create a hybrid product. The prohibition was in the use of those animals together, but the crushed grain bears no trace of the *aveirah*. It is not an embodiment of the prohibition in any meaningful sense.

### Mother & Child

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ לִיתְּסַר! מִדַּאֲסַר רַחֲמָנָא מְחוּסַּר זְמַן לַגָּבוֹהַּ, מִכְּלָל דִּלְהֶדְיוֹט שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **A mother and its offspring** slaughtered on the same day **should be prohibited** for consumption, as this too is a practice made abominable. The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that the Merciful One prohibits** an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived, i.e., that is less than eight days old, as an offering **to the Most High** (Leviticus 22:27), learn **by inference that** such animals are **permitted to ordinary** people for consumption. The offspring of a mother slaughtered that day is also considered an animal whose time has not yet arrived, since the prohibition against slaughtering it is limited to that day.

The Gemara raises the case of *oto ve'et beno* — the prohibition against slaughtering a mother animal and its offspring on the same day. If one violated this prohibition, why is the meat of the second animal permissible? Should it not be considered the product of a forbidden act?

The Gemara answers: *mi-de'asar rachmana mechusar zeman legavoha* — since the Torah establishes the concept of *mechusar zeman* (an animal not yet eligible to be offered because of its age) as a prohibition specifically in the context of *korbanot*, one can infer that outside the context of consecrated offerings, slaughtering in violation of *oto ve'et beno* does not render the meat forbidden to the layperson.

Tosafot notes, however, that the Gemara could have given the same answer it gave elsewhere: the slaughtered calf is simply not an embodiment of the *aveirah* of *oto ve'et beno*. The calf could have been slaughtered the next day; nothing about a slaughtered calf intrinsically reflects the prohibition. The product here is entirely incidental to the transgression, quite unlike *basar bechalav*, where the cooked mixture cannot exist apart from the *aveirah*. Tosafot explains that the Gemara chose the answer it did because it was more readily available, not because the conceptual answer was unavailable.

### Shiluach Ha-Ken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילּוּחַ הַקֵּן לִיתְּסַר! לֹא אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״שַׁלַּח״ לְתַקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a final difficulty: If one violated the mitzva that mandates **sending** away the mother bird from **the nest,** as it is stated: "You shall not take the mother with the young. You shall in any way let the mother go" (Deuteronomy 22:6–7), and the court instructed him to set the mother bird free, that mother bird **should be prohibited** to all, as this practice is also abominable. The Gemara responds: **The Torah did not say: Send** it away, if doing so could lead **to a mishap.** If the bird were prohibited, the Torah would not have commanded one to send it away, as others might eat it unwittingly.

The final challenge is the case of *shiluach ha-ken* — the obligation to send away the mother bird before taking eggs or chicks from a nest. If one violated this and took the mother bird, why is she not forbidden? Tosafot formulates this question slightly differently from the others: here it is not even clearly a case where one did an *aveirah* in the conventional sense. Rather, the Torah indicates that taking the mother bird is off-limits — *lo tikach ha'em al habanim* — and if the Torah has signaled that the mother bird is *ti'avti lecha*, something I have made off-limits to you, then should she not remain forbidden even after being caught and slaughtered?

The Gemara's answer is: *lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah* — the Torah would not have commanded sending the mother bird away in a manner that creates a *takala*, a stumbling block. If the sent-away bird were rendered permanently forbidden, then anyone who subsequently caught and ate her would be consuming forbidden food without any fault of their own. The Torah's command to send the bird away was plainly not intended to generate such a consequence. From this we learn that even where the Torah signals disapproval, the sent-away bird does not become permanently forbidden.

This case is particularly instructive for the conceptual framework being developed. The discussion across all these challenges illuminates a nuanced understanding of the relationship between *issur bishul* and *issur achilah* in the law of *basar bechalav*. The weight of Rav Ashi's approach is that it is the *bishul* that stands at the center — the core of what the Torah opposes — and the eating prohibition flows from the fact that the product of that *bishul* is itself a *to'evah*. Most products of forbidden acts do not carry this character. But *kilay hakerem* and *basar bechalav* do, because the forbidden act is not merely incidental to their existence — it is constitutive of it.

## Reish Lakish: Pesach

### Double Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִנַּיִן לְבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁאָסוּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אַל תֹּאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ נָא וּבָשֵׁל מְבֻשָּׁל״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מְבֻשָּׁל״. מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מְבֻשָּׁל״? לוֹמַר לְךָ יֵשׁ לְךָ בִּשּׁוּל אַחֵר שֶׁהוּא כָּזֶה, וְאֵי זֶה – זֶה בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish says: From where** is it derived **that meat** cooked **in milk is prohibited** for consumption? **The verse states** with regard to the Paschal offering: **"You shall not eat it partially roasted, nor boiled in any way"** (Exodus 12:9). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state:** "Boiled **in any way,"** since it could simply have stated: "Boiled." **What** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "Boiled **in any way?"** It is included **to tell you** that **there is another** manner of **cooking,** the product of **which is** prohibited **like this one. And which** cooking **is this? This is meat** cooked **in milk.**

The Gemara now presents a second approach, that of Reish Lakish: *minayin lebasar bechalav she'asar* — from where do we know that *basar bechalav* is forbidden? The source Reish Lakish cites is the verse concerning the *Korban Pesach*: *al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal* — do not eat the Passover offering raw or cooked in water. The double language of *bishul* — *uvashel mevushal*, cooked, cooked — is superfluous. Why does the verse not simply say *bashel*? The extra word *mevushal* comes to teach: *yesh lecha bishul acher she'hu kazeh* — there is another type of *bishul* that the Torah similarly prohibits eating. What is that? *Basar bechalav*. Thus the prohibition against eating the cooked mixture is derived from the double language of cooking in the *Pesach* verse.

### Yochanan Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּעוּרָה זוֹ שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי? ״לֹא תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ״ – בְּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Reish Lakish: **Is that** derivation **that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught ugly,** that you derive a new one? The verse states with regard to an animal's blood: "You shall not eat it; you shall pour it upon the earth as water" (Deuteronomy 12:24), and the next verse adds: **"You shall not eat it;** that it may go well with you, and with your children after you." Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi teaches that the redundant second verse is not referring to the prohibition of blood. Rather, **the verse is speaking of** the prohibition of **meat** cooked **in milk,** teaching that it is prohibited for consumption.

Rabbi Yochanan responds sharply to Reish Lakish: *ke'urah zu she'shana Rabbi* — is what Rabbi taught something disgusting to you? Meaning: why are you inventing a novel *derasha* from the *Korban Pesach* when the *Tanna* known as *Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — already had a well-established *derasha* on the same question? Why not follow that?

### Rabbi: Psulei Mukdashin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד מִכׇּל הָאִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה? אָמַרְתָּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִדְרֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן, דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָנוֹ. בַּמֶּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בִּשְׁנֵי מִינִין, אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁנֵי מִינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Do you say** that **the verse is speaking of** the prohibition of **meat** cooked **in milk? Or** perhaps **it is only** referring **to one of the** other **prohibitions in the Torah? You say: Go out and learn from the thirteen hermeneutical principles,** one of which is: **A matter that is derived from its context. To what are the** adjacent **verses referring?** They are referring **to** consecrated animals that were redeemed, which are a combination of **two types:** They are non-sacred in that they may be eaten, but they are prohibited for shearing and work, like consecrated animals. **Here too,** one may conclude that the verse is referring **to** a prohibition that involves **two types** of food, i.e., the prohibition of meat cooked in milk.

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's *derasha* is based on the passage concerning *pesulei hamukdashin* — animals consecrated as offerings that became invalid and were subsequently redeemed. The Torah in that context says: *bish'arecha tochlenu hatamei vehatahir yachdav katzvi ucheayal* — you may eat it within your gates, the ritually impure and pure alike, as you would eat the deer and the hart. Then it says: *lo tochlenu* — do not eat it — referring to the blood. But the verse's final repetition of *lo tochlenu* is seemingly redundant. Rabbi derives: *bebasar bechalav hakatuv medaber* — the verse is here speaking about meat and milk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהַהִיא – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בַּאֲכִילָה, אֲבָל בַּהֲנָאָה – לָא; קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish replied to Rabbi Yoḥanan: My exposition is still necessary, since **if** the source for the prohibition of eating meat cooked in milk were **from that** verse cited by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi alone, **I would say** that **this statement** applies only **to** the prohibition of **eating** meat cooked in milk, **but not to** that of deriving **benefit** from it. The verse cited with regard to the Paschal offering **teaches us** that even deriving benefit is prohibited, just as it is prohibited to derive benefit from a disqualified Paschal offering.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know the repeated *lo tochlenu* refers to *basar bechalav* and not to some other prohibition in the Torah? The answer is *davar halamed me'inyano* — context. The verse is dealing with something that involves *shnei minin* — two kinds. According to Rashi, the animal in question has a dual character: it was once a *korban* and retains vestiges of its sacred status, yet has now been redeemed and is treated as *chullin*. It is a hybrid of two statuses. By analogy, *basar bechalav* is similarly a hybrid of two substances, and the *lo tochlenu* warns against consuming such a hybrid.

Rabbi Yochanan then challenges Reish Lakish: if you learned from the *Korban Pesach* rather than from *pesulei hamukdashin*, it is because from *pesulei hamukdashin* alone you could not derive *issur hana'ah*. Blood — the main subject of that verse — is only forbidden in eating, not in benefit. If the *derasha* for *basar bechalav* is learned from the same verse as blood, one might conclude that *basar bechalav* too is only forbidden in eating, not in benefit. The *Korban Pesach*, by contrast, is forbidden not only in eating but in all benefit if the *korban* becomes invalid. That is why Reish Lakish derived from the *Korban Pesach* context — to include *issur hana'ah* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי, בַּהֲנָאָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָכָא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וְלֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ בִּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, מָה לְהַלָּן בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף כָּאן בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **from where** does he derive that meat cooked in milk is prohibited **for benefit?** The Gemara responds: **He derives it from here,** as **it is stated here: "For you are a sacred [*kadosh*] people unto the Lord** your God. You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk" (Deuteronomy 14:21). **And it is stated below: "Neither shall there be a sodomite [*kadesh*] of the sons of Israel"** (Deuteronomy 23:18). The similar expressions teach that **just as below,** the prohibition of sodomy is a prohibition that involves enjoyment, i.e., deriving **benefit,** not eating, **so too here,** meat cooked in milk is prohibited **with regard to** deriving **benefit.**

*Ve'Rabbi behan'ah mna lei* — and where does Rabbi himself derive the prohibition of *hana'ah* from? *Nafka lei mehacha* — he derives it from the verse *ki am kadosh atah*, which appears in connection with *basar bechalav* in Devarim 14. This verse says that Israel is a holy people to Hashem. By *gezeirah shavah* — specifically a shared use of the word *kadosh* — the Torah's statement *velo yihyeh kadesh bivnei Yisrael* (there shall be no male prostitute among Israel) is invoked: just as there the *kedasha* context involves a prohibition of pleasure that goes beyond eating, so too here *kedushah* in connection with *basar bechalav* extends to all forms of benefit.

Tosafot notes that the verse concerning *achilah* was also needed independently — so that we should not think the *kedushah* verse addresses only *hana'ah* that is not *achilah*. Rather, there are two separate prohibitions: *issur achilah* derived from one source and *issur hana'ah* derived from another.

## More Derivations

### Eliezer: Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר תָּנָא: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇל נְבֵלָה [וְגוֹ׳]״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: כְּשֶׁתִּמְכְּרֶנָּה – לֹא תְּבַשְּׁלֶנָּה וְתִמְכְּרֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Eliezer taught:** The verse states: **"You shall not eat of any animal carcass;** you may give it to the stranger who is within your gates, that he may eat it; or you may sell it to a foreigner; for you are a sacred people to the Lord your God; you shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk" (Deuteronomy 14:21). **The Torah stated** the prohibition of meat cooked in milk after the *halakha* of an animal carcass to teach that **when you sell** a carcass to a gentile, **you shall not cook it** in milk **and** then **sell it,** i.e., meat cooked in milk is prohibited for benefit and may not be sold.

The school of Rabbi Eliezer (*Dvei Rabbi Eliezer*) offers yet another approach, derived from the proximity of *neveilah* and *basar bechalav* within the same verse. The verse reads: *lo tochlu kol neveilah lager asher bisharcha titnenah va'achalah o machor lenochri ki am kadosh atah laHashem Elokecha lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo*. The Torah, in commanding one what to do with the *neveilah*, says give it or sell it — and then immediately concludes with the prohibition of cooking *basar bechalav*. The *derasha* is: the Torah is teaching, when you sell or give away the *neveilah*, do not first cook it as *basar bechalav* and then sell it — because if you cook it as *basar bechalav*, it will have become *assur behana'ah* and you will no longer be permitted to sell it or give it away. Thus the prohibition of deriving benefit from *basar bechalav* is embedded in the juxtaposition of the two verses.

### Yishmael: Three-fold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים, אֶחָד לְאִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה, וְאֶחָד לְאִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, וְאֶחָד לְאִיסּוּר בִּשּׁוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The Torah states **three times: "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk"** (Exodus 23:19, 34:26; Deuteronomy 14:21). **One** verse serves **to** teach **the prohibition** against **eating** meat cooked in milk, **and one** serves **to** teach **the prohibition** against deriving **benefit** from it, **and one** serves **to** teach **the prohibition** against **cooking** meat in milk.

Finally, the school of Rabbi Yishmael offers the approach familiar from Rashi's commentary on the *parasha*: *lo tevashel gdi bachalav imo* is written three times in the Torah (Shemot 23:19, Shemot 34:26, Devarim 14:21). Each repetition carries its own prohibition: one for *issur bishul* (cooking), one for *issur achilah* (eating), and one for *issur hana'ah* (benefit).

This prompts reflection on the structure of the three prohibitions and their interrelationships. Is the *issur achilah* an independent prohibition, or merely a corollary of the *issur bishul*? Is the *issur hana'ah* an extension of the *issur achilah*, as Rabbi Abbahu's principle would suggest, or is it a conceptually independent category derived from an entirely different source, as the *derasha* from *kadesh bifnei Yisrael* implies? These questions carry practical *halachic* implications — for example, whether the *issur hana'ah* applies in a case where the *issur achilah* of *basar bechalav* did not take effect, such as when the meat itself was already *neveilah* when cooked with milk.

Rabbi Yishmael's *derasha* of three repetitions can be read either way — it could indicate three fully independent prohibitions, or three dimensions of a single interlocking prohibition. In any case, as the Gemara makes clear, this is the simplest and most elegant derivation, which likely explains why Rashi selected it for his commentary on the *parasha*: it simultaneously explains why the verse was repeated and answers the question of how we know *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating and benefit.

It is also evident, as one steps back from all these *drashot*, that in many cases the *tannaim* began with the received tradition — the *mesorah* — that *basar bechalav* is forbidden in eating and in benefit, and then worked backwards to identify the scriptural basis. This is a well-established phenomenon in rabbinic literature. The Ramban, for example, explains that the rule requiring a *gezeirah shavah* to be received from one's teacher means only that one has a tradition regarding the *halacha* and that it may be derived via *gezeirah shavah*; the *tanna* then constructs the specific *gezeirah shavah* accordingly. The diversity of *drashot* here strongly suggests that the bottom line — that one may neither eat nor derive benefit from *basar bechalav* — was the starting point, and the verse-based derivations were the varied scholarly attempts to ground that tradition in the text of the Torah.

### Issi: Am Kadosh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁאָסוּר? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וְאַנְשֵׁי קֹדֶשׁ תִּהְיוּן לִי וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, מָה לְהַלָּן אָסוּר, אַף כָּאן אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Isi ben Yehuda says: From where** is it derived **that it is prohibited** to eat **meat** cooked **in milk? It is stated here: "For you are a sacred people** unto the Lord your God. You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk" (Deuteronomy 14:21). **And it is stated below: "And you shall be sacred men unto Me; therefore you shall not eat any flesh that is torn of animals in the field [*tereifa*];** you shall cast it to the dogs" (Exodus 22:30). **Just as below it is prohibited** to eat a *tereifa*, **so too here it is prohibited** to eat meat cooked in milk.

Issi ben Yehudah offers a further *derasha*: the verse *ki am kadosh atah laHashem Elokecha* appears both by *basar bechalav* and in connection with *tereifah* — *ve'anshei kodesh tihyun li uvasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu*. The concept of *kedushah* as expressed in the Torah is intimately associated with restraining from forbidden foods. Since the Torah employs the term *am kadosh* in connection with *basar bechalav*, just as it does in connection with *tereifah* where the prohibition is clearly one of eating, so too the *kedushah* of *basar bechalav* carries with it a prohibition of eating, not merely a prohibition of cooking.

## Kal V'Chomer Logic

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה, בַּהֲנָאָה מִנַּיִן? אָמַרְתָּ קַל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה עׇרְלָה שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶבְדָה בָּהּ עֲבֵירָה – אֲסוּרָה בַּהֲנָאָה, בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בּוֹ עֲבֵירָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** from this **I have** derived **only** that it is prohibited **for consumption. From where** do I derive that it is prohibited **for benefit** as well? **You** can **say** it can be derived by **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** with regard to the prohibition against eating **the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*],** which is treated less stringently **as no sin has been committed in** the planting of the tree and its production of fruit, **it is prohibited for benefit, is it not right that meat** cooked **in milk, with regard to which a sin has been committed, should be prohibited for benefit?**

With the *issur achilah* established via the *derasha* of Issi ben Yehudah from *am kadosh*, the Gemara then turns to deriving the *issur hana'ah* through a *kal vachomer*: *umah orlah she'lo ne'evda bah aveirah assurah behana'ah, basar bechalav shene'evda bo aveirah eino din she'assur behana'ah* — if *orlah*, regarding which no transgression was committed, is forbidden in benefit, then *basar bechalav*, regarding which a transgression was committed, should certainly be forbidden in benefit.

### Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְעׇרְלָה, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this, as *orla* exhibits a unique stringency: **What** is unique **about *orla*?** It is unique **in that it never had a time when it was fit.** The same cannot be said about meat and milk, which were each permitted on their own before being cooked together.

But the *kal vachomer* is immediately challenged: there are many things regarding which transgressions were committed whose products are nonetheless fully permitted — grain threshed with an ox and donkey together, produce of a cow that was muzzled while threshing. These are clear cases where a *sin* was done and the product remains permissible. How then can we use the fact that an *aveirah* was committed as a basis for the *kal vachomer*?

### Chometz B'Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, וְאָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. מָה לְחָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, שֶׁכֵּן עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let leavened bread on Passover prove** the point, **as it had a time when it was fit,** before Passover, **and** yet **it is prohibited for benefit.** The Gemara rejects this inference as well: **What** is unique **about** consumption of **leavened bread on Passover?** It is unique **in that it is punishable by *karet*,** unlike the prohibition of meat cooked in milk.

The Gemara addresses a further potential objection to the *kal vachomer*: one might say that *orlah* is different because it was never permitted — it was forbidden from the moment of planting, while *basar bechalav* was permissible before it was cooked together. Something that was once permitted perhaps cannot become forbidden in benefit. The Gemara responds: *chometz bePesach yochiach* — *chometz* on Pesach proves otherwise. Leavened bread is entirely permissible before Pesach, yet on Pesach it is *assur behana'ah*. Thus the fact that something was once permitted does not prevent it from subsequently becoming forbidden in benefit.

The Gemara then challenges this: *ma lachometz bePesach she-ken anush kares* — perhaps *chometz* is different because its prohibition is punishable by *kares*. This does not apply to *basar bechalav*.

### Three-Case Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם יוֹכִיחוּ, שֶׁאֵין עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Let** the prohibition of **diverse kinds in a vineyard prove** the point, **as** it **is not punishable by *karet*, and** yet **it is prohibited to** derive **benefit** from the product.

To counter this deflection, *kilay hakerem* is introduced as a third case. *Kilay hakerem* is not punishable by *kares*, was presumably permissible before the seeds were planted, and nevertheless is forbidden in benefit. Once these three cases are assembled — *orlah*, *chometz bePesach*, and *kilay hakerem* — neither the argument that it was once permissible nor the argument that it lacks the weight of *kares* can be used to distinguish *basar bechalav*, since *kilay hakerem* shares neither of those characteristics and is still *assur behana'ah*.

### Two vs. Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה? לַיְיתֵי כּוּלַּהּ בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעׇרְלָה: וּמָה עׇרְלָה, שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶבְדָה בָּהּ עֲבֵירָה, אֲסוּרָה בֵּין בַּאֲכִילָה בֵּין בַּהֲנָאָה; בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב, שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בּוֹ עֲבֵירָה, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאָסוּר בֵּין בַּאֲכִילָה בֵּין בַּהֲנָאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need both Isi ben Yehuda's **verbal analogy** to teach the prohibition of consumption and the *a fortiori* inference to teach the prohibition of benefit? **Let all of it,** both the prohibition of consumption and the prohibition against deriving benefit, **be derived** by an ***a fortiori*** inference **from *orla*: Just as *orla*,** which is treated less stringently, **as no sin has been committed in** the tree's planting, is nevertheless **prohibited both for consumption and for benefit; is it not right that meat** cooked **in milk, with regard to which a sin has been committed,** should be **prohibited both for consumption and for benefit?**

The Gemara then asks why all three cases were necessary. Why not simply derive the rule from the *mah hatzad* — the common denominator — of *orlah* and *chometz bePesach* alone? The answer Rav Ashi gives, in the name of Reish Lakish, is that with only two cases, the *mah hatzad* argument can be dismissed by pointing out an obvious exception — namely, *neveilah*, which is *assur ba'achilah* yet *mutar behana'ah*. If *basar bechalav* is only known to be forbidden in eating, one cannot simply assert that everything forbidden in eating is forbidden in benefit, since *neveilah* disproves that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: חוֹרֵשׁ בְּשׁוֹר וּבַחֲמוֹר, וְחוֹסֵם פִּי פָרָה וְדָשׁ בָּהּ יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בָּהֶם עֲבֵירָה וּשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** That inference would be invalid **because one could say:** The cases of one **who plows with an ox and with a donkey, and** one **who muzzles the mouth of a cow and threshes with it prove** it invalid. **As** in those cases, **a sin has been committed through them, and** yet benefit from the products of these actions is **permitted.** One cannot refute a verbal analogy with logical reasoning.

This leads to the broader logical principle articulated here. When deriving a rule from the common denominator of two cases, even a trivial internal challenge suffices to dismiss the rule's application. When one argues from the model of a single paradigmatic case (*binyan av*), a good internal reason is needed to dismiss its application to a new case. And when one derives from a *mah hatzad* of three cases, the analysis becomes more nuanced. Rav Mordechai transmits in the name of Reish Lakish: *kol mah hatzad migufo parchinan, me'alma lo parchinan* — when arguing from a common denominator, one may only challenge internally, not by pointing to external counterexamples.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, נְבֵלָה תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתֶּרֶת בַּהֲנָאָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל ״מָה הַצַּד״ – מִגּוּפוֹ פָּרְכִינַן, מֵעָלְמָא לָא פָּרְכִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** Such a derivation cannot be suggested, **because one can say** that the case of an unslaughtered **animal carcass proves** it invalid, **as** an animal carcass **is prohibited for consumption and** yet **it is permitted for benefit.** **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: We say in the name of Reish Lakish as follows:** For **any** analogy derived from **the common element** of two sources, **one can refute** the analogy only **by** invoking conflicting details of the two sources **themselves,** e.g., here, some stringency shared by *orla* and leavened bread, but not by meat cooked in milk. **One cannot refute** the analogy **from an external** case, as you do.

When drawing from two cases, even a trivial internal distinction suffices to deflect the rule. When drawing from a *mah hatzad* of three cases, the situation depends on how the argument is structured. If the conclusion still rests on the common denominator of all three, a trivial question still suffices to dismiss it. But if, as in our case, one of the three cases — *kilay hakerem* — itself serves as a direct example that shares none of the supposedly distinguishing characteristics, then only a *kula ve'chumra* argument, a genuine reason of leniency or stringency, can deflect the rule; a trivial dismissal no longer suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תֵּיתֵי בְּמָה הַצַּד? מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁכֵּן גִּדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, שֶׁכֵּן גִּדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then the question remains: Why not **let** the *halakha* **be derived by** analogy from **the common element** of *orla* and leavened bread on Passover? The Gemara responds: **Because** the analogy **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique **about the common element of** *orla* and leavened bread? It is unique **in that** they both involve produce **that grows from the ground,** whereas meat and milk do not grow from the ground. The Gemara counters: **If so,** then **even now** that the Gemara has invoked diverse kinds in a vineyard to prove the point, the derivation **can be refuted** in the same manner: **What** is unique **about** the prohibition of **diverse kinds in a vineyard?** It is unique **in that** it involves produce **that grows from the ground.**

The upshot is this: *basar bechalav* is known to be *assur ba'achilah* by virtue of the principle of *am kadosh*. From that foundation, the *kal vachomer* establishes *issur hana'ah* by comparing *basar bechalav* to *orlah*, *chometz bePesach*, and *kilay hakerem* — all of which are *assur ba'achilah* and *assur behana'ah*, and from whose common denominator, properly reinforced by three cases, we apply the rule to *basar bechalav* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל מָה הַצַּד פָּרְכִינַן כֹּל דְּהוּ, לֹא אִם אָמַרְתָּ חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא – קַל וָחוֹמֶר פָּרְכִינַן, כֹּל דְּהוּ – לָא פָּרְכִינַן. וְלִיפְרוֹךְ לְכוּלְּהוּ, מָה לְכוּלְּהוּ שֶׁכֵּן גִּדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע! אֶלָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: We say in the name of Reish Lakish as follows:** For **any** analogy derived from **the common element** of two sources, **one can refute** the analogy by invoking **any** factor shared by the two source cases but not by the target case. But this is insufficient to refute an analogy to **a single** source **from a single** source using the standard formulation: **No, if you say** the *halakha* applies to the source case, which possesses a unique factor X, must you say the same of the target case, which does not? **One can refute** such analogies using this formulation only by way of **an *a fortiori*** inference, i.e., if the unique factor X is a stringency. But **one cannot refute** it by simply mentioning **any** unique factor, e.g., that diverse kinds in a vineyard grow from the ground. The Gemara counters: **But** one need not invoke the case of diverse kinds in a vineyard by itself, since it is invoked in addition to the cases of *orla* and leavened bread. Consequently, this is effectively a derivation from the common element of all three sources, not from one single case to another. If so, **let us refute** the derivation from **all of them: What** is unique **about all of** the cases? They are unique **in that** they involve produce **that grows from the ground.** **Rather, Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: We say in the name of Reish Lakish as follows:**

Throughout this discussion, the centrality of the concept of *kedushah* — holiness — as a value instantiated through the discipline of eating is brought into sharp relief. The verse *ki am kadosh atah* does not merely add a prohibition of cooking; it signals that Israel's sanctity is expressed, in significant part, through restraint from forbidden foods. When that verse is affixed to the law of *basar bechalav*, it communicates not merely that cooking is prohibited but that eating — and indeed all benefit — is incompatible with the holiness the Torah demands.
