# Hullin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fetus Shechita

### Minalan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִנַּיִן שֶׁעוּבָּר מֻתָּר בִּשְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִן הַקְּרָא: ״וְכׇל בְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר הִיא מַפְרֶסֶת פַּרְסָה וְשׁוֹסַעַת שְׁתֵּי פְרָסוֹת בַּבְּהֵמָה אוֹתָהּ תֹּאכֵלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where** is it derived **that a fetus is permitted** for consumption through **the slaughter of its mother? Rav Huna said: From the verse: "And any animal that splits the hoof and has two split hooves, among those animals, that one you may eat"** (Leviticus 11:3).

The *gemara* opens with a fundamental question: *minalan* — from where do we know that the *shechita* of the mother renders the fetus permitted for consumption? The *mishna* had established this principle as settled law, but the *gemara* now seeks its scriptural basis. The discussion begins from the *pasuk*: *v'chol behemah mafreset parsa* — "and any animal that has a split hoof..." — and concludes with the phrase *babeheimah otah tochelu*, "amongst those animals that one you may eat."

### Behemah Be-Behemah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמְנַיַן "בְּהֵמָה" זוֹ וּ״בְּהֵמָה״ זוֹ – בְּהֵמָה בַּבְּהֵמָה, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַוָּלָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse counts the word "animal" here and the word "animal" there** — teaching the principle: **An animal within an animal,** implying **to include the fetus.**

The initial *drasha* proceeds as follows. The word *behemah* appears at both the beginning and the end of the *pasuk*. Reading these two appearances together yields the formulation *behemah ba-behemah* — an animal within an animal. If there is an animal inside another animal, then slaughtering the mother renders the inner entity permitted *l'rabos ha-vlad*, including the fetus.

### Temurah Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יָמִירוּ בּוֹ! הֲרֵי כָּתוּב: ״לְהַמִּיר בְּהֵמָה בִּבְהֵמָה״, וַהֲרֵי הוּא בְּהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But if that is so, one should be able to perform substitution with it!** After all, **it is written: "To substitute an animal for an animal,"** and here **it is** an **animal!**

The *gemara* immediately presses this *drasha*: *ela me'ata yamiru bo* — if the fetus is called a *behemah*, an actual animal, why then cannot it serve as the object of *temurah*? The laws of *temurah* — the forbidden exchange of one consecrated animal for another — operate on the phrase *lehamir behemah b'vehemah*, one *behemah* for another *behemah*. If the fetus qualifies as a *behemah*, the act of *temurah* performed with a fetus should be effective.

Yet the *mishna* states explicitly: *ein memirin lo evarim b'ubarim* — one cannot perform *temurah* using limbs against fetuses, nor fetuses against limbs, nor limbs or fetuses against whole animals, nor whole animals against limbs or fetuses. If any of these combinations are attempted, the act of *temurah* is not legally effective: no transgression has occurred and no sanctity is conferred on the second animal. How can this be, if the fetus is already being called a *behemah*?

### Vechol Behemah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר: בִּמְנַיַן "וְכׇל בְּהֵמָה" – "וְכׇל" בִּבְהֵמָה, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַוָּלָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, he said:** By counting the word **"and any animal,"** the word "any" serves **to include anything within an animal,** thereby **including the fetus.**

The *gemara* concedes the point and abandons the *behemah ba-behemah* reading. A new *drasha* is proposed. The *pasuk* begins *v'chol behemah* — "any animal" — and the word *v'chol*, "anything," is read expansively: it is almost as though the verse had said *v'chol b'vehemah*, anything within an animal. Under this reading, we are not calling the fetus a *behemah* in its own right. Rather, the verse permits anything located within the animal *l'rabos ha-vlad*, to include the fetus.

### Shleimah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, חַתַּךְ מִן הַטְּחוֹל וּמִן הַכְּלִיָּה בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבְּהֵמָה חַיָּה – אִתְּסַר! לֵימָא לִי מַאן טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי כְּתִיב "אוֹתָהּ" – שְׁלֵימָה וְלֹא חֲסֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If that is so,** then if one **cut away from the spleen or from the kidney while the animal was alive,** it **should be permitted** even though it is not in a complete form. The Gemara asks: **Let me** be informed: **What is the reason?** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: As it is written "that one,"** implying **a complete** entity, **not** something **deficient.**

This inclusive reading immediately generates a problem: if *anything* within the animal is permitted through the *shechita* of the mother, then why does the *mishna* rule that one who cuts away the spleen or the kidney while the animal is still alive — leaving them detached but still inside the body cavity — finds those organs forbidden even after the mother is subsequently slaughtered? According to the expansive reading, they too are "anything in the animal" and should be permitted.

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the word *otah* — "that one" — as a restriction: *shleimah v'lo chaseirah*, only something that constitutes a complete entity in itself, not a part of something larger. The fetus qualifies as a self-contained whole. The spleen and kidney, by contrast, are parts of the mother and have been severed from their context; they are not permitted by the *shechita* of the mother. Something that is a fragment of a larger entity does not fall under the permit.

### Dmut Yonah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ דְּמוּת יוֹנָה – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: If one slaughters an animal and finds inside it something that resembles a dove,** it is **forbidden for consumption.**

The *gemara* then tests this position with a further case. *rabbi yochanan* rules: *hashochet et habeheimah u-matza bah dmut yonah assur ba'achilah* — if one slaughters a *behemah* and finds inside it something that resembles a dove, it is forbidden, because the *shechita* of the mother does not work for it. This makes intuitive sense: the *shechita* of the mother works for something that is a natural extension of the mother's reproductive process, not for something aberrant. Whether the *gemara* means this literally — that a genetically alien creature is growing inside the animal — or whether it refers to a fetal stage at which the phenotype does not yet resemble a cow, the ruling stands: only something that presents as an animal-like entity is covered.

But how does this fit the *drasha*? If the verse permits "anything in the animal," there is no basis to exclude something that happens to look like a bird. It is a complete entity (*shleimah*), it is inside the animal — why is it forbidden?

### Hoofs Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵינָא פְּרָסוֹת, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In order for the fetus to be permitted, **I require** that it have **hooves,** and a dove-shaped fetus **does not have** hooves.

The *gemara* answers by invoking another word in the *pasuk*. The verse reads in full: *v'chol behemah asher hi mafreset parsah v'shosa'at shesa shtei frasot babeheimah otah tochelu* — "any animal that splits the hoof and has two split hooves, among those animals that one you may eat." The *drasha* "anything in an animal" still draws on *v'chol b'vehemah*, but the word *parsot* — hooves — limits what qualifies. Only something with hooves, something that resembles a *behemah* in that respect, is covered. This excludes the dove-like entity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלָא מֵעַתָּה, קָלוּט בִּמְעֵי פָּרָה לִיתְּסַר? הָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: ״פַּרְסָה בַּבְּהֵמָה תֹּאכֵלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** that the fetus is permitted only if it fulfills the conditions mentioned in that verse, then a fetus with **non-cloven** hooves found **inside a cow's womb should be forbidden,** whereas the *baraita* cited on 68b states that it is permitted. The Gemara answers: **This** is as **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** Each phrase in the verse is taken as a sufficient condition. Furthermore, the term "hoof" can be understood independently of the phrase "cloven into two hooves," and is referring to a non-cloven hoof. Accordingly, the verse can be interpreted as teaching: An animal that has **a hoof, in the animal,** it **you may eat,** and teaches that even a fetus with a non-cloven hoof is permitted.

The *gemara* then asks: according to this reasoning, *kalut b'mei parah leiaser* — should a fetus with an unsplit hoof, resembling a camel's hoof rather than a split cow's hoof, also be forbidden? The *gemara* answers: *ha tani d'vei Rabbi Yishmael k'Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai*. The school of *rabbi yishmael* taught in accordance with *rabbi shimon ben yochai*, whose *drasha* uses both the singular *parsah* and the plural *parsot* present in the verse. The singular *parsah* permits a fetus with an unsplit hoof — inside the mother — while the plural *parsot* permits one with split hooves. Note carefully that *rabbi yishmael*'s school treats the fetus with an unsplit hoof as permitted only while in the mother; once born, such an animal would be forbidden.

At this point, the *gemara* has navigated to the following position: the fetus is permitted through the *shechita* of the mother not because it is called a *behemah* in its own right, but because it is a complete entity (*shleimah*) inside the mother that possesses either a split or unsplit hoof — close enough in form to a *behemah* — and is therefore covered by the inclusive term *v'chol*, qualified by *otah* and *parsot/parsah*.

### Rabbi Ashimi Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ ״אֵין מְמִירִין״, הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּמַקֵּישׁ תְּמוּרָה לְמַעֲשֵׂר: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בָּאֵבָרִים וְעוּבָּרִים, אַף תְּמוּרָה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת בָּאֵבָרִים וְעוּבָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: Actually,** the *halakha* that the fetus and its severed pieces are permitted **should be** derived **as you** previously **said,** i.e., from the phrase: An animal in the animal. **And** with regard to **that which posed a difficulty for you,** i.e., the mishna in *Temura* that states **one cannot substitute** a non-sacred animal for the fetus of a pregnant offering, that difficulty can be resolved by saying: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who compares substitution to** the animal **tithe: Just as the** animal **tithe does not apply to limbs and fetuses** but only to live animals that can walk, as it is stated: "Whatever passes under the rod" (Leviticus 27:32), **so too, substitution does not apply to limbs and fetuses.**

*Rav Ashimi* now proposes returning to the original *drasha*. He says: *le'olam kede'kamart me'ikara* — in truth, let us restore the original formulation of *behemah ba-behemah*. This is a far more elegant *drasha* and naturally accounts for the requirement that the fetus resemble an animal: it must be a *behemah* inside the *behemah*. The one outstanding objection — why *temurah* does not work — he undertakes to answer directly.

### Temurah & Ma'aser

*Rav Ashimi* responds: *ha mani Rabbi Shimon hi de-makish temura le-ma'aser* — the *mishna* that says *temurah* cannot be performed with fetuses reflects the position of *rabbi shimon*, who derives the laws of *temurah* from *ma'aser behemah*, the tithing of animals. The verse governing *temurah* in the context of *ma'aser* reads: *ha'asiri yiheyeh kodesh la-Shem, lo yachalifenu v'lo yamir oto betov bera'* — the tenth shall be sacred to God, do not exchange or substitute it. The Torah repeats the prohibition of *temurah* in the context of *ma'aser*, and *rabbi shimon* draws legal conclusions from that juxtaposition.

One such conclusion: the *ma'aser* verse says *asher ta'avor tachat ha-shavet* — "that passes under the staff" — meaning the animal must walk on its own accord. A fetus cannot walk under a staff. Therefore, just as *ma'aser* does not apply to fetuses, *temurah* — which is compared to *ma'aser* by *rabbi shimon* — likewise does not apply to fetuses. In the *gemara*'s formulation: *ma ma'aser eino noheg be'evarim uve'ubarim, af temura einah noheget be'evarim uve'ubarim*. The same exclusion applies to limbs, for the same reason.

So the position of the *mishna* is entirely coherent: the fetus is called a *behemah*, justifying the *drasha* of *behemah ba-behemah*, but *temurah* still does not apply because *rabbi shimon* derives the inapplicability of *temurah* to fetuses from the *ma'aser* comparison. The *drasha* of *behemah ba-behemah* is thus restored.

### Rabbi Yosi Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא, דִּתְנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וַהֲלֹא בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁים, הָאוֹמֵר: ״רַגְלָהּ שֶׁל זוֹ עוֹלָה״ – כּוּלָּהּ עוֹלָה, אַף כְּשֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״רֶגֶל שֶׁל זוֹ תַּחַת זוֹ״ – תְּהֵא כּוּלָּהּ תְּמוּרָה תַּחְתֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say** that this mishna expresses the opinion of Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara explains: **As we learned** in the same mishna (*Temura* 10a) that **Rabbi Yosei says: But with regard to consecrated** offerings, if **one says the leg of this** animal **is a burnt offering,** then **all of** the animal **is** consecrated as **a burnt offering.** Accordingly, **even** with regard to substitution, if one says: **The leg of this** non-sacred animal should be substituted **instead of that** offering, **the entire** animal **should be a substitute in its stead.**

The *gemara* now proves that the *mishna* excluding *temurah* for fetuses reflects *rabbi shimon* by pointing to the dissent recorded at the end of that same *mishna*. *rabbi yosi* says: *v'lo be-mukdashim* — and is the law not otherwise even by consecrated animals? He argues that one can sanctify a limb, and by doing so the sanctity spreads to the entire animal (*kulah olah*). If sanctifying a limb can transform the whole animal, then an act of *temurah* performed on a limb — *raglah shel zu tehei zu tachat zu* — should likewise be effective, making the whole animal a *temura* in exchange for the consecrated animal. *rabbi yosi* holds that *temurah* can indeed be performed on limbs, and presumably on fetuses as well.

### Proving Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִי אִית לְהוּ הַאי סְבָרָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל הָאוֹמֵר ״רַגְלָהּ שֶׁל זוֹ עוֹלָה״ תְּהֵא כּוּלָּהּ עוֹלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן מִמֶּנּוּ לַה׳ יִהְיֶה קֹּדֶשׁ״ – מִמֶּנּוּ קֹדֶשׁ, וְלֹא כּוּלּוֹ קֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **To whom is** Rabbi Yosei **responding? If we say** he is responding **to Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, do they accept this reasoning? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that in the case of **one who says: The leg of this** animal **is a burnt offering, all of it will be a burnt offering.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if it is an animal of those that they bring as an offering to the Lord, **anything of it that one gives to the Lord, it shall be sacred"** (Leviticus 27:9). The verse indicates that the part **of it** that one gives will be **sacred, but all of it** will **not** be **sacred.**

The *gemara* now asks: to whom is *rabbi yosi* directing his argument? *leman ka-mahadar lei?* His proof rests on the premise that sanctifying a limb transforms the entire animal. But *rabbi meir* and *rabbi yehuda* do not accept that premise. For them, sanctifying a limb results in only the limb bearing sanctity, not the whole animal. Therefore *rabbi yosi* cannot be arguing with *rabbi meir* or *rabbi yehuda*. He must be addressing someone who shares his premise — and that person is *rabbi shimon*. This proves that the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* who holds that *temurah* cannot be performed on fetuses or limbs is *rabbi shimon*, which is precisely what *rav ashimi* asserted.

### Limb Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל תֵּצֵא לְחוּלִּין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יִהְיֶה״ – בַּהֲוָיָיתָהּ תְּהֵא. הָא כֵּיצַד? תִּמָּכֵר לְצׇרְכֵי עוֹלוֹת, וְדָמֶיהָ חוּלִּין, חוּץ מִדְּמֵי אֵבֶר שֶׁבָּהּ – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: מִנַּיִן לָאוֹמֵר ״רַגְלָהּ שֶׁל זוֹ עוֹלָה״ – תְּהֵא כּוּלָּהּ עוֹלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יִהְיֶה״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** A non-sacred animal with a consecrated limb may not be sacrificed. Accordingly, one **might** have thought the consecrated limb may be redeemed and thereby **transferred to non-sacred** status. Therefore, **the verse states: "It shall be** sacred," indicating that **it shall** remain **as it is. How** is **this** possible? The animal **should be sold for the needs of burnt offerings,** i.e., to an individual who will sacrifice the entire animal as a burnt offering, **and the payment** received **for** the animal will be **non-sacred, except for the payment** received in exchange **for** that one **limb** that is consecrated. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say: From where** is it derived **that** in the case of **one who says: The leg of this** animal **is a burnt offering, all of it becomes a burnt offering? The verse states: "It shall be** sacred." This serves **to include all of** the animal, indicating that it all becomes sacred.

A *baraita* makes the positions explicit. *yachol omer raglo shel zo ola tehei kulo ola* — one might think that if one declares "the leg of this animal is an *ola*," the entire animal becomes an *ola*. The verse says: *kol asher yiten mimenu la-Shem yiheyeh kodesh* — whatever one gives from it to God shall be sanctified. The word *mimenu* — from it — implies that only part becomes sanctified, *v'lo kulo kodesh*: not the whole animal becomes holy. But then can the leg simply be redeemed? No, because the verse adds *yiheyeh* — "shall be" — meaning *be-havayato tehei*, it retains its status. Since it cannot be redeemed without a *mum* and cannot be brought as a *korban* on its own, the practical resolution is *mocher le-tzorchei olot*: one sells the whole animal to someone who needs to bring an *ola*, at a discount excluding the value of the already-consecrated limb, and that person brings the whole animal. This, the *baraita* states, is the position of *rabbi meir* and *rabbi yehuda*.

*Rabbi Yosi* and *rabbi shimon*, however, derive from the word *yiheyeh* the opposite conclusion: *lirabos kulo*, the whole animal becomes an *ola*. If one sanctifies a limb, the entire animal is transformed. This is the shared premise between *rabbi yosi* and *rabbi shimon* that makes *rabbi yosi*'s argument regarding *temurah* legible only as addressed to *rabbi shimon*. The identification of the *tanna kamma* as *rabbi shimon* is thus confirmed.

### Rabbi Yosi's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי טַעְמָא דְנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is possible that Rabbi Yosei is not responding directly to any other opinion; rather, **he stated his own reasoning,** without reference to that of another Sage.

The *gemara* qualifies the proof. The final conclusion is *lo* — not quite. *rabbi yosi ta'ama de-nafshei ka'amar*: *rabbi yosi* is not necessarily addressing *rabbi shimon* at all. He is simply articulating his own reasoning. His position is: I hold that *temurah* can be performed on a limb, for the same reason I hold that sanctifying a limb transforms the whole animal. These two views are logically connected in *rabbi yosi*'s own system. He is not attempting to persuade *rabbi meir* and *rabbi yehuda*, who reject his first premise; he is simply explaining the inner coherence of his own position. From this reading, the identity of the *tanna kamma* remains somewhat open, though the *drasha* of *behemah ba-behemah* remains the most naturally elegant and most frequently cited basis for permitting the fetus through the mother's *shechita*.

### Spleen & Kidney


Before proceeding, it is worth pausing on a point that the *gemara* never explicitly addresses. Why exactly are the spleen and kidney forbidden after the mother's *shechita*? One might have assumed the reason is *eiver min ha-chai* — that by cutting them away from the living animal, one created a limb severed from a living creature, which remains forbidden regardless of subsequent *shechita*. But strikingly, this *gemara* never once invokes the concept of *eiver min ha-chai* in that discussion.

The *gemara*'s reasoning is rather that the *shechita* of the mother simply does not extend its permit to them. They are fragments of the mother, not complete entities, and the *drasha* — whether *behemah ba-behemah* or *v'chol b'vehemah* qualified by *otah* — covers only what qualifies as a whole thing. Once severed, the spleen and kidney are pieces of flesh inside the body cavity, no longer part of the mother and not covered by any *drasha* to include them. Their status would then be that of *neveila* — meat that did not undergo *shechita* — rather than *eiver min ha-chai*. The practical difference is significant: a non-Jew could consume them, and they would not generate *tumah*. This reading emerges organically from the *gemara*'s own logic, even if never stated outright.

## Bechor & Childbirth

### Mivakeret Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְבַכֶּרֶת הַמְקַשָּׁה לֵילֵד – מְחַתֵּךְ אֵבֶר אֵבֶר וּמַשְׁלִיךְ לִכְלָבִים. יָצָא רוּבּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה יִקָּבֵר, וְנִפְטְרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Upon its birth, the firstborn male offspring of a domesticated animal is automatically consecrated with firstborn status, and it is prohibited to derive benefit from it. Furthermore, if it dies, it may not be discarded, but must be buried. If **an animal that was giving birth to a firstborn male was encountering difficulty giving birth,** and in order to alleviate the difficulty one wishes to terminate the birth, **he may cut up** the fetus **limb by limb and cast it to the dogs.** Since the fetus was not born, it is non-sacred and does not require burial. If **a majority of** the fetus **had** already **emerged,** it is considered to have been born and is therefore consecrated; consequently, if one cut it up **it must be buried, and** the mother animal is **exempted from** having **firstborn** status conferred on any future offspring.

The next *mishna* shifts from the laws of *shechita* to the laws of *bechor*. The case is *hamivakeret ha-maksheh liyaled* — an animal giving birth for the first time (*mivakeret*, in the *hif'il*, meaning the one causing a firstborn to be born) that is experiencing difficulty in childbirth (*maksheh*). In such a situation one may cut the fetus limb by limb to save the mother's life. The *mishna* rules: *mechatich eiver eiver u-mashlich la-klavim* — one may cut it limb by limb and throw the pieces to the dogs.

The reasoning is that *bechor* sanctity attaches only at birth, and birth has not yet occurred. Each limb, as it emerges, is severed before the fetus is considered born, and therefore carries no *bechor* sanctity. Since the pieces have no sanctity, they may be discarded.

### Bechor Emergence


When precisely does the fetus become a *bechor*? The *mishna* implies two triggers: emergence of the head, or emergence of the majority (*rubo*) of the body. If the birth is headfirst, the head's emergence confers *bechor* status. If the birth is not headfirst — which is precisely the dangerous scenario motivating the intervention in our *mishna* — then the *rov*, the majority, must emerge for the *bechor* sanctity to attach. This is why, with each limb emerging, the act of cutting prevents the *bechor* from ever being "born" in the legally operative sense.

### Majority & Exemption


The *mishna* continues: *yatza rubo, harei zeh yikaver* — once the majority has emerged, the animal now bears *bechor* sanctity. Even if pieces continue to be cut at that point, what is being cut carries *bechor* sanctity and must be buried rather than discarded; it is a *korban* that has died and may not be used. Furthermore, *u-niftar min ha-bechora* — this birth exempts subsequent offspring from *bechor* status. Even a birth accomplished through the piecemeal collection of limbs until a majority has emerged constitutes a birth for *halachic* purposes, such that the next animal born to this mother will not be a *bechor*. *tosafot* draws an explicit parallel: a human *mishna* similarly holds that once a majority of the fetus emerges — even piecemeal — the mother is considered a *yoledet* and acquires the relevant *tumah*, a ruling that confirms that majority need not emerge at once.

### Piecemeal Status


The *mishna*'s language on this point is somewhat ambiguous — does *yatza rubo* refer to a majority that emerged all at once, or one that accumulated through the piecemeal dismemberment? *tosafot* clarifies that the logic applies to the accumulated majority as well, consistent with the parallel *mishna* concerning human birth and *tumat leidah*. The practical halachic question of how to measure "majority" — whether by volume, by number of limbs (*rov minyan u-vinyan*), or by some other standard — is left for further discussion, though the presumption is that it tracks the volume majority of the fetus.

## Selling to Non-Jew

### Third Sold Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: יָצָא שְׁלִישׁ וּמְכָרוֹ לְגוֹי, וְחָזַר וְיָצָא שְׁלִישׁ אַחֵר – רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ, רַבָּה אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: An amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to a case in which **one-third** of a firstborn fetus **emerged** from the womb **and** then **one sold it to a gentile, and then another one-third** of the fetus **emerged.** Once a majority of the fetus emerges it is considered born. The *halakha* is that a fetus partly owned by a gentile is not consecrated as a firstborn. In this case, the firstborn was sold to a gentile only after it had already partially emerged from the womb. **Rav Huna says** it is **consecrated,** while **Rabba says** it is **not consecrated.**

The *gemara* now presents a more complex scenario: *yatza shalish u-machro le'oved kochavim* — a third of the fetus emerged and was sold to a non-Jew. Since a *bechor* must be entirely owned by a Jew to bear *bechor* sanctity, selling even a portion to a non-Jew, in principle, exempts the animal from *bechor* status. The sale here was not of the fetus while still entirely in the womb (a known device for avoiding *bechor*), but of that portion that had already emerged. Then *vachazar v'yatza shalish acher* — the rest came out, bringing the total to a majority. What is the status? Has the sale to the non-Jew successfully prevented *bechor* sanctity from attaching?

### Rav Huna View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר קָדוֹשׁ: קָסָבַר לְמַפְרֵעַ קָדוֹשׁ, וְכֵיוָן דִּנְפַק לֵיהּ רוּבֵּיהּ – אִיגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא לְמַפְרֵעַ דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲוָה קָדוֹשׁ, וּמַאי דְּזַבֵּין – לֹא כְּלוּם זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Huna says** it is **consecrated,** as **he maintains** a firstborn is **consecrated retroactively** from the moment the first part of its body emerges from the womb. **And** therefore in this case, **once most of it had emerged,** it **became clarified retroactively that it had** already **been consecrated from the outset, and** so with regard to **that which he had sold** to a gentile, it arises that **he did not** actually **sell anything** at all. Since it had already been consecrated, he did not have full ownership of it to be able to sell it.

*Rav Huna amar kadosh* — it is sanctified as a *bechor*. His reasoning: *kasvar lemafrei'a kadosh* — once the majority emerges, the *bechor* sanctity takes effect retroactively. *de-keivan de-nafik lei rubei, iglai milta lemafrei'a de-me'ikara hava kadosh* — since the majority has now come out, the matter is revealed retroactively, and we understand that the sanctity in fact began from the moment the birth process commenced. *u-mai de-zavin lav klum zavin* — and since it was already sanctified from that earlier moment (viewed retroactively), the sale of a *bechor* is invalid, and the non-Jew acquired nothing. This is the *lemafrei'a* principle: halachic retroactivity that reaches back to the beginning of the process once the legally operative moment — emergence of the majority — is achieved.

### Rava View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ: קָסָבַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא קָדוֹשׁ, וּמַאי דְּזַבֵּין – שַׁפִּיר זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says** it is **not consecrated,** as he **maintains** a firstborn is consecrated **from that point forward,** i.e., only at the moment the majority of it emerges. **And** so with regard to **that which he had sold** to a gentile, it arises that **he had sold** it **properly,** i.e., the sale was valid because at the time of the sale it had not been consecrated. Accordingly, by the time the majority of its body emerged it was already partly owned by a gentile, and that prevented it from being consecrated.

*Rava amar eino kadosh* — it is not sanctified. His reasoning: *kasvar mikan u-lehaba kadosh* — the sanctity of *bechor* takes effect only from the moment of majority emergence going forward, not retroactively. *u-mah de-zavin shapir zavin* — the sale made before the majority emerged was therefore a valid sale. Once the majority does emerge, *bechor* sanctity would ordinarily attach, but now part of the fetus is already owned by a non-Jew. A *bechor* that is partly non-Jewish owned does not become a *bechor*, and therefore the sanctity does not apply.

### Sale Validity


The practical divergence is sharp. Under *rav huna*, the retroactive view, the sale of the third to the non-Jew is nullified by the *bechor* sanctity that is deemed to have existed from the beginning; the non-Jew acquired nothing, and the animal is a full *bechor*. Under *rava*, the forward-looking view, the sale was valid at the time it was made, the third remains in non-Jewish ownership, and the *bechor* sanctity never fully attaches.

## Cesarean & Natural

### Mixed Emergence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִתְּמַר: יָצָא שְׁלִישׁ דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן, וּשְׁנֵי שְׁלִישֵׁי דֶּרֶךְ רֶחֶם – רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, רַבָּה אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: Rav Huna and Rabba **follow their** lines of **reasoning, as** another dispute between them **was stated** with regard to a case in which **one-third** of a firstborn fetus **emerged through** the **wall** of the womb, i.e., via caesarean section, **and** then the other **two-thirds** emerged **through** the opening of the **womb.** The *halakha* is that a fetus is consecrated only if it emerged through the opening of the womb. In this case a majority did emerge through the opening, but the first majority to emerge was not through the opening. **Rav Huna says** it is **not consecrated** and **Rabba says** it is **consecrated.**

The *gemara* now presents a second scenario that tests the same underlying debate. *yatza shlish derech dofen u-shenei shlishim derech rechem* — a third of the fetus emerged through the wall of the womb (i.e., via Cesarean section), and then two-thirds emerged through the normal birth canal. Birth — in the halachically operative sense, *peter rechem* — requires emergence through the natural opening, not through a surgical incision in the uterine wall. So a third came out in a non-birth manner, and then the remaining two-thirds came out naturally, at which point the majority had been reached. Is the animal a *bechor*?

### Rav Huna: No Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ. רַב הוּנָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: לְמַפְרֵעַ קָדוֹשׁ, וְרוּבָּא קַמָּא לֵיתֵיהּ בְּרֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: When **Rav Huna says** it is **not consecrated Rav Huna** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** a firstborn is **consecrated retroactively,** since the birth consecrates it. **And** in this case, **the first majority** that emerged, which defined its birth, was **not through** the opening of **the womb.** Therefore, it was not consecrated.

*Rav Huna amar eino kadosh* — it is not sanctified. True to his *lemafrei'a* approach, once the majority emerges, he looks retroactively to the beginning of the process. The beginning of the process here was a Cesarean extraction — a non-birth. Since the process began through the wall of the womb and not through the natural opening, the entire birth process is tainted. *u-rubba kama leisa be-rechem* — and that initial portion was not from the normal birth canal. The retroactive view requires that the beginning of the process qualify as genuine birth, and here it does not.

### Rava: Sacred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ. רַבָּה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא קָדוֹשׁ, וְרוּבָּא דֶּרֶךְ רֶחֶם נָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, when **Rabba says** it is **consecrated Rabba** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** a firstborn is **consecrated from that point forward,** i.e., only once a majority of the fetus has emerged from the opening of the womb. This is because the emergence of a majority of the fetus through the opening of the womb consecrates it, even if that is not the first majority, **and** in this case **the majority** of the animal **emerged through** the opening of the **womb.**

*Rava amar kadosh* — *rava* says it is sanctified. *rava le-tameih de-amar mikan u-lehaba kadosh* — consistent with his forward-looking position, *rava* does not retroactively examine the process. He asks only: what is in front of us now? *u-rubba derech rechem nafka* — the majority emerged through the natural birth canal. That is the operative fact. The Cesarean third is simply ignored; it does not corrupt the analysis. Since the majority came out through the natural opening, *rava* considers the animal born and the *bechor* sanctity fully operative.

### Need Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא: בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִשּׁוּם דִּלְקוּלָּא, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ דִּלְחוּמְרָא – אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבָּה. וְאִי אִתְּמַר בְּהָא: בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבָּה, אֲבָל בְּהָא – אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** to convey Rav Huna and Rabba's dispute in both of these cases. **As, had** the Sages **taught us** only their dispute **with regard to this** case, where after one-third emerged it was sold to a gentile, one might have thought that it is only **in that** case that **Rav Huna says** the animal is consecrated retroactively. This is **because** to rule it is consecrated from that point forward **would be a leniency,** as then the fetus would be exempt from being subject to firstborn status. **But with regard to that** case, when one-third emerged through the wall of the womb, **where** Rabba's opinion would lead to **a stringency,** one might **say** that Rav Huna **concedes to Rabba.** Therefore, it was **necessary** for the dispute to be stated in both cases. **And** if their dispute **was stated** only **with regard to that** case, when one-third emerged through the wall of the womb, one might have thought it is only **in that** case that **Rabba says** the animal is consecrated from that point forward, as that results in a stringency, i.e., the fetus is subject to firstborn status. **But in this** case, when after one-third emerged it was sold to a gentile, **where** Rav Huna's opinion would lead to a stringency, one might **say** that Rabba **concedes to Rav Huna.** Therefore, it was **necessary** for the dispute to be stated in both cases.

This pairing of positions produces an intriguing reversal of the practical outcome. In the first scenario (selling to a non-Jew), *rav huna*'s retroactive view leads to the *stringent* conclusion (the animal is a *bechor*, the sale failed), while *rava*'s forward-looking view leads to the *lenient* conclusion (no *bechor*). In the second scenario (Cesarean plus natural birth), *rav huna*'s retroactive view leads to the *lenient* conclusion (not a *bechor*), while *rava*'s forward-looking view leads to the *stringent* conclusion (is a *bechor*). Neither *amora* is simply playing it strict or lenient; each is applying a consistent analytical principle.

This observation is itself the subject of the *gemara*'s closing discussion. *utzricha* — it is necessary to state both cases. Had the *gemara* only reported the first case, one might have supposed that *rav huna* adopts the retroactive view only because doing so there produces a *stringent* result (*le-chumra*), and perhaps in the second case, where retroactivity produces a *lenient* result, he would concede to *rava*. Conversely, had the *gemara* only reported the second case, one might have supposed that *rava* adopts the forward-looking view only because it is strict in that scenario, and in the first case perhaps he would agree with *rav huna*. By recording both cases, the *gemara* establishes that each *amora* holds his position consistently regardless of whether it leads to stringency or leniency in the particular case at hand. Their dispute is principled and systematic, not outcome-driven.
