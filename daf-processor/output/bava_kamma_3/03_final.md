# Bava Kamma 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avos & Toldos Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אָבוֹת נֶאֱמְרוּ בְּשׁוֹר – הַקֶּרֶן, וְהַשֵּׁן, וְהָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that delineates the primary categories of damage: **Three primary categories** of damage **were stated** in the Torah **with regard to an ox.** An ox causes damage in three ways, and each is classified as a distinct primary category of damage represented by a part of the body of the ox: There is **the** category of **Goring [*keren*],** literally, horn. This is referring to an ox goring a person or an animal and causing damage. **And** there is **the** category of **Eating [*shen*],** literally, tooth. This is referring to one's ox causing damage by consuming another person's produce. **And** there is **the** category of **Trampling [*regel*],** literally, foot. This is referring to an ox trampling another person's belongings and causing damage. These are classified as primary categories because they are mentioned explicitly in the Torah.

The *gemara* on daf 3 of *Bava Kamma* operates under the organizing framework of asking whether the *toldos* — the subcategories or derivatives — of the four major *avos* of *nezikin* mentioned in the *mishnah* share the same legal consequences as their parent categories, or whether they differ. The technical formulation is: *toldoseihen k'yotzei bahen o lav k'yotzei bahen* — do their derivatives follow the same rule or not? The *gemara* proceeds by working through each *av*, first defining the conceptual parameters of the category, then examining whether the things that fall under it carry identical *halachos*.

### Shor Subdivisions

The *mishnah* lists *shor* as one of the four primary categories. A *beraisa*, however, subdivides *shor* into three separate *avos*: *keren*, *shen*, and *regel*. These are not labeled *toldos* within the *mishnah* — they are treated as independent *avos* in their own right. This distinction will become consequential as the discussion unfolds.

---

## Keren Category

### Keren Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֶרֶן מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יִגַּח״ – אֵין נְגִיחָה אֶלָּא קֶרֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ לוֹ צִדְקִיָּה בֶן כְּנַעֲנָה קַרְנֵי בַרְזֶל, וַיֹּאמֶר: כֹּה אָמַר ה׳, בְּאֵלֶּה תְּנַגַּח אֶת אֲרָם וְגוֹ׳״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹרוֹ הָדָר לוֹ, וְקַרְנֵי רְאֵם קַרְנָיו, בָּהֶם עַמִּים יְנַגַּח״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **From where do we** derive the primary category of **Goring?** The source is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And **if** an ox **gores** a man or a woman" (Exodus 21:28); and **goring is** performed **only** with **a horn, as it is stated: "And Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah, made himself horns of iron, and said: So says the Lord: With these shall you gore the Arameans,** until they are consumed" (I Kings 22:11). **And** the verse also **states: "His firstborn bull, majesty is his, and his horns are the horns of the wild ox; with them he shall gore the nations"** (Deuteronomy 33:17).

The *gemara* describes *keren* as the *parshah* in the *Torah* dealing with goring, for which one pays half-damages for the first three occurrences and full damages thereafter. Crucially, however, what defines the *keren* category is not the specific body part — the horn — but rather the nature of the act. The defining characteristic of *keren* is *kavanaso l'hazik*: the animal's intention is to cause damage.

## Avos & Toldos Framework (continued)

### Rav Pappa Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: יֵשׁ מֵהֶן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן לָאו כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** After determining that there are instances where the legal status of subcategories is like that of primary categories, e.g., Shabbat, and there are instances where the legal status of subcategories is dissimilar to that of primary categories, e.g., ritual impurity, the Gemara asks: **Here,** with regard to the laws of damages, **what** is the relationship between the primary categories and their subcategories? **Rav Pappa said: There are, among** the primary categories of damage, some whose subcategories **are similar to them, and there are, among them,** some whose subcategories **are dissimilar to them.**

*Rav Pappa* makes the statement that some *toldos* share the same *halachos* as their *av* and some do not. The default presumption is that they share the same laws; the challenge is to identify the exceptional case where they diverge. The *gemara* therefore works through the list methodically, asking of each category: is this where *Rav Pappa*'s exception applies?

## Keren Category (continued)

### Keren Toldos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְקֶרֶן מַאי הִיא? נְגִיפָה, נְשִׁיכָה, רְבִיצָה, וּבְעִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its citation of the *baraita*: **What is a subcategory of Goring?** It includes any action that an ox performs with its body with the objective of inflicting damage: **Pushing [*negifa*], biting, crouching** upon items with the objective of inflicting damage, **and kicking.**

If *Rav Pappa*'s exception concerns *keren*, the *gemara* asks: what would a *toldah* of *keren* look like? The answer is that *keren* applies not only to goring with the horn but to any act in which the animal intends to cause damage — pushing, kicking, biting. These are all manifestations of *kavanaso l'hazik* through different body parts.

### Keren Toldos Same

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא קֶרֶן – דְּכַוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – כַּוּוֹנָתָן לְהַזִּיק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ! אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְקֶרֶן כְּקֶרֶן, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַשֵּׁן וָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **what is different** about **Goring** that defines it as a unique primary category? What is different is **that** the **objective** of the ox's action **is to cause damage, and** the ox is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you,** its owner. In **these** subcategories of Goring, i.e., pushing [*negifa*], biting, crouching, and kicking, **as well,** the **objective** of the oxen's actions **is to cause damage, and** the oxen are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent your oxen from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you. Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of Goring is like** that of the primary category of **Goring, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he was referring **to Eating and Trampling.**

The *gemara* then asks: how are these *toldos* different from *keren* itself? The answer is that they are not. *Mai shana keren d'kavanaso l'hazik u'mamoncha v'shmirasan alecha* — what distinguishes *keren* is that it involves intent to damage, and it is your property for which you bear responsibility. These *toldos* share precisely that characteristic: they are *kavanaso l'hazik*, they are your property, and you are therefore obligated to watch them. The consequence — *shmirasan alecha*, the duty of supervision — is the same, and you are *chayav* to pay.

It is worth pausing here, because the *gemara* states the conceptual identity of the *toldos* with the *av* without explicitly pronouncing that the *halacha* is the same. *Rashi*, later in the *gemara*, supplies the implied conclusion that you are *chayav* to pay. Alternatively, it may be that the phrase *u'mamoncha v'shmirasan alecha* is itself the operative legal consequence — not merely a descriptor — and the first consequence of falling into this category is the duty of guardianship, from which *chiyuv* to pay naturally follows.

The conclusion is: *toldos d'keren k'keren* — the *toldos* of *keren* follow the law of *keren*. That cannot be where *Rav Pappa*'s exception lies. *V'chi ka'amar Rav Pappa ashein v'regel* — perhaps *Rav Pappa* is speaking of *shen* and *regel*.

## Shen & Regel Exegesis

### Torah Text Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל הֵיכָא כְּתִיבִי? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשִׁלַּח״ – זֶה הָרֶגֶל, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where are Eating and Trampling written** in the Torah that led them to be classified as primary categories? The Gemara answers: The source is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "If a man causes a field or vineyard to be eaten, and he sends forth his animal, and it consumed in the field of another" (Exodus 22:4). The two parts of the verse are referring to different categories: **"And he sends forth," this is** a reference to **the** primary category of **Trampling,** as sending forth results in the animal trampling another's produce and damaging it, **and likewise it states:** "Happy are you that sow beside all waters **that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey"** (Isaiah 32:20). Clearly the term "send forth" is a reference to trampling by the feet of the animal.

Before defining these categories, the *gemara* first asks: *shen v'regel heicha ktivi* — where are *shen* and *regel* actually written in the *Torah*? The *beraisa* derives them from a *pasuk* in *Mishpatim* that, on a plain reading, makes no mention of an animal eating or trampling at all.

The *pesukim* in question read: *ki yaveer ish sadeh o kherem, veshilach et beiro uvi'er bisdeh acher, meitav sadehu umeitav karmo yeshaleim* — if a man burns a field or vineyard, and sends it out and it burns in another's field, he pays from the best of his fields. The next *pasuk* reads: *ki tetze esh umatz'ah kotzim venachal gadish o hakamah o hasadeh, shalaim yeshaleim hamaveer et habe'irah* — if a fire gets out and burns a haystack or standing grain, the one who lit the fire must pay.

A straightforward reading of these two *pesukim* treats both as describing fire. The first describes intentional arson — *veshilach* suggesting deliberate release, *ki yaveer* evoking burning — with the consequence of paying *meitav*, from the finest of one's property, as befits an intentional tortfeasor. The second describes an unintentional fire that gets out of control, with standard compensation. The difference in *halacha* between them — *meitav* for intentional, straight compensation for accidental — maps neatly onto this reading.

The *gemara*, however, does not read them this way. The second *pasuk* is about *eish*; it is the first that contains *shen* and *regel*.

### Gemara's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבִעֵר״ – זוֹ הַשֵּׁן, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יְבַעֵר הַגָּלָל עַד תֻּמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And it consumed," this is** a reference to **the** primary category of **Eating, and likewise it states:** "And I will utterly sweep away the house of Jeroboam, **as one consumes with the tooth until it be all gone"** (I Kings 14:10).

The *gemara* asks: how do you read *keren*, *shen*, and *regel* into *veshilach et beiro uvi'er bisdeh acher* when those words appear nowhere? A *beraisa* answers: *veshilach* — this is *regel*, trampling. And it cites a supporting *pasuk*: *meshalchei regel hashor vehachamor* — those who send forth the leg of the ox and the donkey. *U'vi'er* — and it consumes — *zeh hashen*, that is the tooth.

### Be'er & Juxtaposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״וְשִׁלַּח״ זוֹ הָרֶגֶל, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״. טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״, הָא לָאו הָכִי – בְּמַאי מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* just cited: **"And he sends forth," this** is a reference to **the** category of **Trampling, and likewise it says:** "Happy are you that sow beside all waters **that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey"** (Isaiah 32:20). The Gemara infers: **The reason** that the phrase: "And he sends forth," is interpreted as a reference to the category of Trampling is **that the Merciful One writes: "That send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey."** The Gemara asks: Were **it not for this** verse, **with regard to what would you have interpreted** that phrase?

The key that unlocks this reading is the word *be'irah*. As the *gemara* notes, *be'er* — to consume, to destroy — can refer to animals, to consuming. And *veshilach* has a parallel in the language of sending animals. The *gemara* thus reads the first *pasuk* as containing both *shen* and *regel*. The word *be'er* allows the reading of "consume" rather than "burn," and *veshilach* is attested in the context of animal legs elsewhere in *Tanach*.

### Veshilach & U'vi'er

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי קֶרֶן – כְּתִיב! אִי שֵׁן – כְּתִיב! אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי אַשֵּׁן, וְהָא דְּמִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא, הָא דְּלָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **If** you say that it could have been interpreted as referring to **Goring,** that cannot be, as Goring **is written** in a different verse. **If** you say that it could have been interpreted as referring to **Eating,** that too cannot be, as Eating **is written** in a different verse. Perforce the reference is to Trampling, and the *baraita* had no need to prove this from the phrase: "That send forth the feet." The Gemara answers that it **is necessary** for the *baraita* to cite the verse: "That send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey," as it **could enter your mind to say** that both **this** phrase: "And he sends forth," **and that** phrase: "And it consumed," are referring **to** the primary category of **Eating, and this** phrase: "And it consumed," is referring to a case **where the object** damaged **is completely destroyed,** and **this** phrase: "And he sends forth," is referring to a case **where the object** damaged **is not completely destroyed.** Therefore, the verse "that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey" **teaches us** that the phrase "and he sends forth," is referring to Trampling.

Once this interpretive move is made, the *gemara* assigns the two verbs as follows: *veshilach et beiro* is *regel* — trampling; *uvi'er bisdeh acher* is *shen* — eating. The *beraisa* supports *veshilach* as *regel* through the *pasuk meshalchei regel hashor vehachamor*, and supports *uvi'er* as *shen* through the *pasuk ka'asher yeva'er hagalal ad tumo* — as the *galal* consumes until it is finished. *Rashi* explains *galal* as the tooth that eats until done; alternatively, *galal* can connote excrement, reinforcing the association with consuming and expelling.

But the *gemara* immediately questions whether both verbs are necessary. By process of elimination, since *keren* has its own explicit *pasuk* and *regel* is now established through *veshilach*, the remaining verb *uvi'er* must be *shen*. Why then does the *Torah* need to write it at all?

### Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא אַרֶגֶל, שֵׁן דְּלָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא – מְנָלַן? דּוּמְיָא דְרֶגֶל, מָה רֶגֶל – לָא שְׁנָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא, וְלָא שְׁנָא לָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא; אַף שֵׁן – לָא שְׁנָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא, וְלָא שְׁנָא לָא מִכַּלְיָא קַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that we have interpreted** that the phrase "and he sends forth" is referring **to Trampling, from where do we** derive that one is liable with regard to acts categorized as **Eating** in a case **where the object** damaged **is not completely destroyed?** The primary category of Eating is derived from the phrase "and it consumed." The connotation of that phrase is damage in which the object is completely destroyed. The Gemara answers: It is derived because the category of Eating is juxtaposed to and therefore **similar to** the category **of Trampling: Just as** with regard to liability for the category of **Trampling, it is no different** if **the object** damaged **is completely destroyed and it is no different** if **the object** damaged **is not completely destroyed; so too,** with regard to liability for the category of **Eating, it is no different** whether **the object** damaged **is completely destroyed and it is no different** whether **the object** damaged **is not completely destroyed.**

The *gemara* answers: *itztarich* — it is necessary. Without the second verb, one might have read *veshilach et beiro* as covering two distinct scenarios of *regel*: one where the animal walks on its own (*azal mimmeila*) and one where the owner intentionally sends it (*shilach shiluchei*). The word *veshilach* sounds distinctly intentional — as though the owner deliberately dispatched the animal — and one might have thought only that case is covered. *U'vi'er* establishes that the second half of the *pasuk* is about *shen*, which in turn confirms that *veshilach* means *regel* — trampling — even when the animal goes on its own without the owner's direction.

Now the *gemara* raises the inverse problem. Since *veshilach* has been established as *regel*, and *uvi'er* as *shen*, we know you are liable when you intentionally send the animal. But how do we know you are liable for *regel* when the animal simply wanders on its own? *Dumya d'shen* — by juxtaposition to *shen*. Just as *shen* uses the verb *uvi'er*, which carries no connotation of deliberate sending, and you are therefore *chayav* whether the owner sent the animal or not, so too *regel*: *mah shen lo shana shelach shiluchei lo shana azla mimmeila, af regel lo shana shelach shiluchei lo shana azla mimmeila*.

### Juxtaposition Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאוֹקֵי אַשֵּׁן, רֶגֶל דְּאָזְלָה מִמֵּילָא – מְנָלַן? דּוּמְיָא דְשֵׁן, מָה שֵׁן – לָא שְׁנָא שַׁלְּחַהּ שַׁלּוֹחֵי, לָא שְׁנָא דְּאָזְלִי מִמֵּילָא; אַף רֶגֶל – לָא שְׁנָא שַׁלְּחַהּ שַׁלּוֹחֵי, לָא שְׁנָא אָזְלָה מִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that we have interpreted** that the phrase "and it consumed" is referring **to Eating, from where do we** derive that one is liable with regard to actions categorized as **Trampling** in a case **where** the animal **went** and caused damage **on its own?** The primary category of Trampling is derived from the phrase "and he sends forth." The connotation of that phrase is a case where the owner sent the animal to cause damage. The Gemara answers: It is derived because the category of Trampling is juxtaposed to and therefore **similar to** the category **of Eating: Just as** with regard to liability for the category of **Eating, it is no different** if the owner **sent** the animal to cause damage and **it is no different** if the animal **went** and caused damage **on its own, so too,** with regard to liability for the category of **Trampling, it is no different** if the owner **sent** the animal to cause damage and **it is no different** if the animal **went** and caused damage **on its own.**

A parallel point governs the other direction. The word *uvi'er* carries a sense of complete destruction — *machlya karnah*, destroying the principal entirely. One might therefore have thought *shen* liability applies only when the animal eats something to the point of complete destruction. How do we know *shen* applies even when the destruction is incomplete — *lo machlya karnah*?

Again by juxtaposition. *Regel* uses *veshilach et beiro*, which contains no language suggestive of complete destruction. By *regel*, it does not matter whether the principal is destroyed or not. Since *shen* and *regel* appear together in the same *pasuk* and share identical *halachos*, one learns *shen* from *regel*: *mah regel lo shana machlya karnah v'lo shana lo machlya karnah, af shen lo shana machlya karnah v'lo shana lo machlya karnah*.

This raises the question, raised in the *shiur*, of what exactly *lo machlya karnah* — not destroying the principal — means in practice. *Rashi* explains it as a case where an animal grazes on stalks or straw but the grain itself will grow back; the farmer will still get his full yield. The straw is the incidental byproduct and not the farmer's primary produce, so the grain-as-principal is not destroyed. *Tosafot*, however, challenges this: even in such a case the straw itself has value — it could be fed to animals or sold — and its destruction is *machlya karnah* of the straw.

*Tosafot*'s understanding of *lo machlya karnah* is therefore different: it refers to a case where the object itself is not physically destroyed but is rendered less marketable, as when an animal defiles fruit by contact with it. The fruit can still be sold, but at a lower price. This is not *machlya karnah* of the fruit itself, since the fruit remains intact; yet there is nonetheless a loss in value for which one is *chayav*. There is no physical damage to the object, only a reduction in its market value.

The *gemara* also raises the question of why both verbs were necessary if *veshilach* alone could have encompassed both *shen* and *regel*. The *pasuk meshallach regel hashor vehachamor* would have supplied *regel*, and *veshhen behemot ashallach bam* — with its explicit mention of *shen* — would have supplied *shen*. The *gemara* responds that without *uvi'er*, one might have read *veshilach* as covering only intentional cases — *heicha deshallach shiluchei* — and not cases where the animal went on its own — *aval azlu mimmeila lo*. The word *uvi'er* is therefore needed to establish that even autonomous action is covered.

The *gemara* also asks why the *Torah* could not simply have written *veshilach* alone, since that word carries resonances of both *regel* — through *meshalchei regel hashor vehachamor* — and *shen* — through *veshein behemot ashalach bam*. The answer: *o ha o ha* — without *uvi'er*, I would have known one or the other was included but could not have known both were of equal standing. The two verbs together teach that both *shen* and *regel* are fully covered, and they are of equal legal weight, even though the conceptual basis for liability in each case is distinct.

What emerges from this exegetical exercise is a precise articulation of what makes each category. *Shen* is characterized as *yesh hana'ah lehezekah* — the animal benefits through the act of damage; it is eating your produce and being nourished by it. *Regel* is characterized as *heizika matzui* — the damage is prevalent and common, occurring in the ordinary course of the animal going about its business.

These two conceptual bases are structurally interesting. If one frames *nezikin* liability in terms of the owner's negligence, *regel* represents a very high level of negligence: damage through *regel* is essentially unavoidable wherever animals walk, and the owner should have been far more vigilant. If one frames liability in terms of the animal's act — I am responsible for what my animal does the way I am responsible for what my employees or children do — then *shen* is the stronger case: the animal is knowingly and intentionally eating your food, which is a genuine act of the animal in a way that *regel* is not. *Regel* is closer to *mit'asek*, accidental and barely an act at all. *Keren*, interestingly, falls into both frameworks simultaneously — it is a genuine intentional act, though *keren tam* involves relatively low owner negligence given that the animal was not yet established as a gorer.

The point about intentionality deserves emphasis. Even when the owner deliberately releases the animal knowing it will trample or eat, this does not reclassify the act as *keren*. What matters is the animal's intentionality, not the owner's. An animal that eats your grain is doing what animals do; an animal that gores is acting with intent to harm. When I intentionally release my animals into your field knowing they will trample your fruit, the animals are still just walking — they have no intent to damage. That is why *shen* and *regel* are the same category: both involve an animal doing what it naturally does, without *kavanaso l'hazik*.

## Shen Category

### Shen Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְשֵׁן מַאי הִיא? נִתְחַכְּכָה בְּכוֹתֶל לַהֲנָאָתָהּ, וְטִינְּפָה פֵּירוֹת לַהֲנָאָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Having clarified the sources for the primary categories of Eating and Trampling, the Gemara proceeds to identify their subcategories and to determine whether it was with regard to these primary categories of damage that Rav Pappa said: There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them. **What is a subcategory of Eating?** It is a case where, for example, an animal **rubbed against a wall for its pleasure** and damaged the wall, **or** where **it sullied produce** by rolling on it **for its pleasure.**

Having established *shen* exegetically, the *gemara* now describes its conceptual identity: *hana'ah l'hezeika* — the animal derives benefit through the act of damage.

### Shen Examples

The *gemara* identifies *toldos* of *shen*: *nitchakecha bekosel l'hana'asa* — the animal rubbed against a wall for its own pleasure and the wall collapsed; *tinfa peiros l'hana'asa* — it soiled fruit for its own benefit.

### Shen Toldos Debate

The latter example generates a significant interpretive dispute. *Rashi* argues that the soiling cannot refer to the animal defecating on the fruit, because excrement that damages an object is not the animal's body doing the damage — it is a substance ejected from the body, closer in nature to a projectile. This is precisely the logic the *gemara* will use later regarding *tzrorot* — pebbles kicked by an animal's foot — where liability is reduced because it is the animal's *koach*, not its *guf*, that causes damage. Defecation, on *Rashi*'s analysis, is analogous: the excrement is *kocho*, not *gufo*.

*Rashi* therefore understands *tinfa peiros* as the animal rolling in the grain for physical pleasure — as elephants roll in mud — thereby damaging it with its body. This is a genuine *hana'ah l'hezeika*: the animal derives pleasure directly through its physical interaction with the produce, and the damage is caused by the body of the animal.

*Tosafot* challenges this reading on different grounds. *Tosafot*'s earlier discussion of *lo machlya karnah* had suggested that defiling fruit — rendering it less marketable without physically destroying it — is a valid case of *shen* liability. By implication, *Tosafot* may allow for defecation on fruit as a form of *shen*, even if the mechanism of damage is slightly more attenuated. The underlying question is whether *hana'ah l'hezeika* requires that the benefit and the damage be directly and necessarily linked, or whether it suffices that the animal acted for its own pleasure and incidentally caused damage.

### Shen Toldos Same

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא שֵׁן – דְּיֵשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – יֵשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקָן, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ! אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְשֵׁן כְּשֵׁן, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַתּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **what is different** about **Eating** that it is defined as a unique primary category? What is different is **that there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, and** the ox is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you,** its owner. In **these** subcategories of **Eating, as well, there is pleasure** for the animals **in** the course of the **damage** that **they** cause **and** the oxen are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent your oxen from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** Therefore, the subcategories of Eating are not dissimilar to the primary category. **Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a sub-category of Eating is like** that of the primary category of **Eating, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he is referring **to a subcategory of Trampling.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai shana shen de'ika hana'ah l'hezeika?* What distinguishes *shen* is the presence of *hana'ah l'hezeika* — benefit through damage — combined with *u'mamoncha v'shmirasan alecha*. The *toldos* of *shen* share precisely these characteristics. Therefore, *toldah d'shein k'shein* — the *toldah* of *shen* is like *shen*. This cannot be *Rav Pappa*'s exception.

## Regel Category

### Regel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל מַאי הִיא? הִזִּיקָה בְּגוּפָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִילּוּכָהּ, בִּשְׂעָרָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִילּוּכָהּ, בִּשְׁלִיף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, בִּפְרוּמְבְּיָא שֶׁבְּפִיהָ, בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is a subcategory of Trampling?** The classification is applied in a case where, for example, an animal **caused damage with its body,** not its legs, in the **course of its walking;** or caused damage **with its hair** that became entangled with an object in the **course of its walking;** or caused damage **with a rope that is upon it;** or caused damage **with a bit [*bifrumbiya*] that is in its mouth;** or caused damage **with a bell that is around its neck.**

The *gemara* now turns to *regel*. *Regel* is characterized as *heizika matzui* — damage that is prevalent and common, arising from the animal's ordinary activity.

### Regel Examples

The *toldos* of *regel* are: *heizika b'gufa derech hilucha* — damage caused by the animal's body in the course of its movement. Specifically: *b'sa'ara derech hilucha* — its tail (*Rashi* comments that the *gemara* may literally mean hair, though in context a tail seems the more natural reading given the parallel examples), swinging as the animal walks and knocking something over; *b'shlif halev u'b'pumbyah she'b'piyah* — the saddle or the bit in its mouth bumping into something; *b'zug she'b'tzavara* — the bell around its neck striking an object. Note that these include items attached to the animal but not the animal's body itself; a projectile, however, would be a different matter.

### Regel Incidental

*Rashi*'s formulation of *regel* is not merely *heizika matzui* but *heizika matzui derech hilucha* — prevalent damage in the course of the animal's movement — borrowing the phrase the *gemara* uses in defining the *toldos*. However, it would be a mistake to read *derech hilucha* too literally as requiring that the animal be walking. The more essential conceptual point is that the damage is incidental. The animal is going about its ordinary business — whatever that business is — and damage occurs not as the goal of the act but as a byproduct of normal activity. This is what generates the classification *heizika matzui*: damage of this kind is common precisely because animals do not navigate their environment with attention to avoiding harm to others' property. Whether the animal is walking, standing and swinging its tail, or otherwise engaged in ordinary behavior, the conceptual core is the same: the damage is unintentional and incidental to normal activity.

### Regel Toldos Same

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֶגֶל – דְּהֶזֵּיקוֹ מָצוּי, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – הֶזֵּיקָן מָצוּי, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ! אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל כְּרֶגֶל, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַתּוֹלָדָה דְבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **what is different** about **Trampling** that it is defined as a unique category? What is different is **that its damage is commonplace, and** the animal is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you,** its owner. In **these** subcategories of Trampling, **as well, their damage is commonplace, and** the oxen are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** Therefore, the subcategories of Trampling are not dissimilar to the primary category. **Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of Trampling is like** that of the primary category of **Trampling, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he is referring **to a subcategory of Pit.**

*Mai shana regel d'heizika matzui?* The *toldos* share this characteristic — *heizika matzui u'mamoncha u'shmirasan alecha* — and therefore carry the same law. *Toldah d'regel k'regel*. This also cannot be *Rav Pappa*'s exception.

## Bor Category

### Bor Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְבוֹר מַאי נִיהוּ? אִילֵּימָא אָב – עֲשָׂרָה, וְתוֹלָדָה – תִּשְׁעָה; לָא תִּשְׁעָה כְּתִיבִי, וְלָא עֲשָׂרָה כְּתִיבִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines that assertion: **What is a subcategory of Pit? If we say** that the **primary category** of Pit applies when, in the public domain, one leaves an uncovered pit that is **ten** handbreadths deep, and that is the pit mentioned in the Torah, **and a subcategory** of Pit applies when one leaves an uncovered pit that is **nine** handbreadths deep, there is no basis for that distinction, as **neither nine** handbreadths **are written** in the Torah, **nor are ten** handbreadths **written** in the Torah.

Moving down the *mishnah*'s list — *shor, bor, ma'aveh, hev'er* — the *gemara* reaches *bor*. Unlike the previous categories, the *gemara* does not stop to quote and expound the relevant *pasuk*, because the case of *bor* is entirely explicit: *ki yichreh ish bor o ki yiftach ish bor v'lo yichasenu v'nafal shama shor o chamor* — if a man digs a pit or opens a pit and does not cover it and an ox or donkey falls in. No exegetical acrobatics are needed. The *gemara* proceeds directly to the question of *toldos*.

### Depth Distinction

The *gemara* proposes: perhaps the *av* is a pit of 10 handbreadths depth and the *toldah* is one of 9 handbreadths. The *halachic* significance of this distinction is that *Chazal* determined a pit of 10 handbreadths has the natural propensity to cause an animal's death, while a pit of less than 10 handbreadths may cause injury but not death. For an animal that falls into a pit shallower than 10 handbreadths and dies, the owner of the pit is exempt — such a death is considered a freak accident, outside the range of expected consequences. For injury, however, even a shallower pit creates liability. If the *toldah* of *bor* — the 9-handbreadth pit — carries a different *halacha* (no liability for death), this would indeed be a case of *lav k'yotzei bahen*.

### Parallel Avos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא; ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְקִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן, עֲשָׂרָה – עָבְדָן מִיתָה, תִּשְׁעָה – נְזִיקִין עָבְדִי, מִיתָה לָא עָבְדִי. סוֹף סוֹף, זֶה אָב לְמִיתָה, וְזֶה אָב לִנְזָקִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This** is **not difficult,** as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And the carcass shall be for him"** (Exodus 21:34), **and the Sages have** an accepted **tradition** that a pit **ten** handbreadths deep **causes** the **death** of an animal that falls into it, but a pit **nine** handbreadths deep **causes damage** to an animal that falls into it but **does not cause death.** Accordingly, the pit written in the Torah (Exodus 21:33), which mentions the death of the animal that fell therein, is ten handbreadths deep. This is classified as the primary category of Pit, and a pit nine handbreadths deep is its subcategory. The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, this** pit that is ten handbreadths deep **is a primary category** of damage with regard **to death, and that** pit that is nine handbreadths deep **is a primary category** with regard **to damage.**

The *gemara* rejects this proposal. *Lo tes ktiv v'lo yud ktiv* — neither nine nor ten is written in the *pasuk*; you cannot call one the *av* and the other the *toldah* on that basis alone. The *gemara* then offers a resolution: *v'hamet yihiyeh lo amar Rachmana* — the end of the *pasuk* specifies *v'hamet yihiyeh lo*, indicating that the animal died, which establishes that the primary case in the *Torah* involves death. This would make the death-*bor* the *av* and the injury-*bor* the *toldah*, which would be *lav k'yotzei bahen*. *Chazal* assessed that 10 handbreadths could cause death while 9 could cause only injury.

However, the *gemara* ultimately declines to accept this framework. *Sof sof*, it insists: *zeh av l'mitah v'zeh av l'nezikin* — one is an *av* for death and the other is an independent *av* for injury. They are parallel *avos*, not an *av* and a *toldah*. This parallels a similar structural question found elsewhere — as by *Shabbat*, where the question arises whether *afiyah* and *bishul* are *av* and *toldah* or two parallel *avos*. Since the *pasuk* is sufficiently general and neither depth is explicitly specified, we decline to designate one as *toldah* of the other, and we therefore do not have a case of *lav k'yotzei bahen* here. *Rashi* explains that since the *pesukim* themselves speak broadly, we cannot impose an *av/toldah* hierarchy between the two depths.

### Bor Toldah Hazard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַאַבְנוֹ, סַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחָן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִזִּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Pappa's statement: There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, was stated in reference **to** these subcategories of Pit: **His stone, his knife, or his load,** any of **which he placed in a public domain** as obstacles, **and they caused damage** when people stumbled upon them.

What, then, is the genuine *toldah* of *bor*? *Avno sakino umaso shehinichan b'reshut harabim* — a stone, knife, or burden left in a public thoroughfare. If a person or animal comes along and is injured by tripping over them, this is a *toldah* of *bor*: a hazard placed in a public space that does not go out to damage but that people and animals will inevitably encounter. The *toldah* of *bor* is precisely this structure — a static hazard in a public place — as opposed to *bor* itself, which is a pit dug in the ground.

### Rav Shmuel Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ – בֵּין לְרַב וּבֵין לִשְׁמוּאֵל, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר. וְאִי דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ – אִי לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר; וְאִי לְרַב, דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִשּׁוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ, הַיְינוּ שׁוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where one is liable for damage caused by these obstacles? **If** it is a case **where one declared** these items **ownerless, both according to** the opinion of **Rav and according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** cited later in the Gemara, **these** cases **are** a subcategory of **Pit,** as both Rav and Shmuel agree that any obstacle declared ownerless by its owner that causes damage in the public domain is a subcategory of Pit. **And if** it is a case **where one did not declare them ownerless, there is still a difficulty. If** Rav Pappa's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, who says: We learned** that one is liable for damage caused by **all** obstacles that he places in a public domain, whether or not he declares them ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's pit, these** cases **are** a subcategory of **Pit. And if** Rav Pappa's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav, who says: We learned** that one is liable for damage caused by **all** obstacles that he places in a public domain that he did not declare ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's ox** that gored another animal, **these** cases **are** a subcategory of **Ox.** With regard to the subcategories of Ox, the Gemara already established that their legal status is like that of the primary category of Ox.

*Heichi dami* — the *gemara* now asks: in what circumstances? If the owner was *mafkir* — renounced ownership of the hazardous object — then according to both *Rav* and *Shmuel* this is a classic *bor* and the *toldah* is *k'yotzei bahen*.

But what if the owner did not renounce ownership? Here *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree. *Shmuel* holds *kulam mibor lamadnu* — all such cases are derived from *bor*, and even an owned hazard left in a public space falls under *bor*. *Rav*, however, holds *kulam mishor lamadnu* — all such cases derive from *shor*: since the object is the owner's property, that ownership is the primary defining characteristic and places it in the *shor* category.

The intuitive pull is toward *bor*: an object left in a public thoroughfare that people trip over feels far more like a pit than like an ox. But the *gemara* records the debate, and the point is that either way — *bein l'Rav bein l'Shmuel* — the *toldah* shares the same *halachos* as its *av*, whether that *av* is *bor* or *shor*. It is a *toldah k'yotzei bahen*.

### Bor Toldos Same

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא בּוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן תְּחִלַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – תְּחִלַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתָן לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ! אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְבוֹר כְּבוֹר, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַתּוֹלָדָה דְמַבְעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks concerning an obstacle that is a subcategory of Pit: **What is different** about **Pit** that it is defined it as a unique category? It is **that its initial formation,** i.e., the digging of the pit, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** it is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage to others, is incumbent **upon you.** In **these** subcategories of Pit, **too, their initial formation,** i.e., the placement of the obstacle in the public domain, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** they are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you. Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of Pit is like** that of the primary category of **Pit, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he is referring **to a subcategory of *Maveh*.**

The conceptual definition the *gemara* uses to unify *bor* and its *toldah* is: *techilat asiyaso l'nezek* — from its very inception, it is a hazard. This is what distinguishes *bor* from the other *avos*. An ox does not enter the world as a hazard — its identity as a *mazik* develops contingently. A fire immediately presents the danger of spread. But a pit, or a stone left in a public place, has the identity of a hazard from the moment it comes into being. That immediate and inherent dangerousness, combined with *mamoncha v'shmiraso alecha*, defines the category. The *toldah* shares this characteristic, and therefore *toldah d'bor k'bor*.

## Ma'aveh Dispute

### Ma'aveh Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּבְעֶה וְהַהֶבְעֵר כּוּ׳. מַאי ״מַבְעֶה״? רַב אָמַר: מַבְעֶה זֶה אָדָם, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַבְעֶה זֶה הַשֵּׁן.
>
> **Translation:** § One of the primary categories of damage enumerated in the mishna is **the** category of ***Maveh* and** another is **the** category of **Fire.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***Maveh*? Rav says: *Maveh*, that** is the category of **Man,** which includes a person who causes damage, but not the property or animal of a person that causes damage. **And Shmuel says: *Maveh*, this** is **the** category of **Eating.**

The *gemara* proceeds to *ma'aveh*, the third item in the *mishnah*'s list. The word itself is ambiguous. *Shmuel* says *ma'aveh zo shen* — *ma'aveh* is *shen*. Under this reading, the *mishnah*'s *shor* refers to *keren*, and *ma'aveh* covers *shen* and *regel*, which share identical *halachos* and thus go together. *Rav*, however, says *ma'aveh zeh adam* — *ma'aveh* refers to *adam hamazik*, the damaging person. This is a striking position: it places the liability of a human being who causes direct damage within the same taxonomic framework as the liability for one's animal or property.

### Ma'aveh as Shen

If *ma'aveh* is *shen*, the *gemara* notes that the question of *toldos* has already been answered: *okimna toldah d'shen k'shen* — we have already established that the *toldah* of *shen* is like *shen*. Nothing new needs to be said.

### Adam HaMazik Intro

If *ma'aveh* is *adam hamazik* per *Rav*'s view, then the *gemara* must now ask: what are the *avos* and *toldos* of intentional human damage?

## Adam HaMazik

### Sleep Toldah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: אָב – נֵיעוֹר, תּוֹלָדָה – יָשֵׁן; וְהָתְנַן: אָדָם מוּעָד לְעוֹלָם, בֵּין עֵר בֵּין יָשֵׁן!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that there is a distinction: The **primary category** is a case where one causes damage while **awake,** and **a subcategory** is a case where one causes damage while **asleep; but didn't we learn** in a mishna (26a): The legal status of **a person** is **always** that of one **forewarned,** and he is liable for any damage that he causes, **both** when **he is awake and** when **he is asleep?** Accordingly, that is not the distinction between a primary category and a subcategory.

The *gemara* proposes: perhaps the *av* is damage done while awake and the *toldah* is damage done while asleep. There is logic to this: normally a person acts as a conscious agent, and damage done in sleep is at some remove from ordinary responsible action — one's subjectivity, agency, and consciousness are diminished or absent.

### Always Liable

The *gemara* rejects this immediately. *V'hatnan: adam mu'ad l'olam, bein eir bein yashen* — we have already learned in a *mishnah* that a person is always a *mu'ad*, fully liable, whether awake or asleep. The *toldos* are therefore *k'yotzei bahen*. This cannot be *Rav Pappa*'s exception.

### Kocho Toldah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַכִּיחוֹ וְנִיעוֹ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי בַּהֲדֵי דְּאָזְלִי קָמַזְּקִי – כֹּחוֹ הָוֵה! אִי בָּתַר דְּנָיַיח – בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps Rav Pappa was referring **to** cases where one causes damage with **his phlegm or his spittle.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where one would be liable for damage in that case? **If** it is a case **where** his phlegm and spittle **cause damage as they were moving** through the air after the person expectorated, that **is** damage caused by **his** direct **action** and there is no room to distinguish between the damage that it caused and any other damage caused by one's direct action. **If** it is a case where his phlegm or spittle causes damage **after it comes to rest** on the ground, e.g., if one slipped in the spittle, fell, and was injured, **both according to Rav and according to Shmuel this is** a subcategory of **Pit,** as the spittle is an ownerless obstacle in the public domain.

The *gemara* proposes another candidate: *keicho v'ni'o* — spitting or projecting mucus. If one spits and the projectile damages an object — *Rashi* gives the example of spit landing on fine silk and causing water damage — that is *kocho*, one's projected force. The principle is that even if it is not one's body that directly causes the damage, something propelled by one's energy and still carrying that energy is treated as equivalent to a direct bodily act. That would make spitting a *toldah* of *adam hamazik*. But the *halacha* would be the same — *chayav* — so this is still *k'yotzei bahen*.

Alternatively: if the spit falls to the ground and an animal later slips on it, that is a different case entirely. *Bein l'Rav bein l'Shmuel hainu bor* — under both *Rav*'s and *Shmuel*'s views, that is *bor*, a hazard placed in a public space, and we have already established *toldas bor k'bor*. Conceptually, it is interesting to note the spectrum of cases involving human action: damage while awake, damage while asleep, damage through *kocho* while the force is still active, and damage through something that has come to rest and become a static hazard. Each is one degree further removed from the direct act of the person, yet the *halachos* remain the same in all cases.

### Adam Toldos Same

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְמַבְעֶה כְּמַבְעֶה. וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אַתּוֹלָדָה דְאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of *Maveh* is like** that of the primary category of ***Maveh*, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them; he was referring **to a subcategory of Fire.**

*Toldah d'mav'eh k'mav'eh* — the *toldah* of *ma'aveh* (*adam hamazik*) is like *ma'aveh*. All *toldos* of *adam hamazik* are *k'yotzei bahen*. Still not the answer to *Rav Pappa*'s exception.

## Esh Category

### Roof Hazard Toldah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְאֵשׁ מַאי נִיהוּ? אִילֵּימָא אַבְנוֹ, סַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחָן בְּרֹאשׁ גַּגּוֹ, וְנָפְלוּ בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה וְהִזִּיקוּ; הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בַּהֲדֵי דְּאָזְלוּ קָא מַזְּקִי – הַיְינוּ אֵשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is a subcategory of Fire? If we say** that it is a case of **his stone, his knife, or his load, where he placed them on top of his roof, and they fell** off the roof **by means of a common wind, and they caused damage, what are the circumstances** in which one would be liable for the damage that was caused? **If** it is a case **where** those items **caused damage as they were moving** through the air propelled by the wind, **that is** a case of **Fire.**

Proceeding to *eish* — fire — the *gemara* proposes a *toldah*: *avno v'sakino u'masa'o she'henichan b'rosh gago* — a stone, knife, or burden left on the roof of one's house, which is then blown off by the wind and causes damage. This parallels the case of the stone left in a public thoroughfare — the same objects appear — but the mechanism is different. Instead of being a static hazard that people walk into, these objects are set in motion by an external force. The connection to *eish* is that, like fire, the initial placement is by the owner, but a subsequent external force — the wind — causes the damage. In both cases, *koach acher me'urav* — another force mixes in to produce the harmful result.

### Foreseen Wind

An important qualifier: *venafelu beruach metzuya* — the objects must have fallen due to a *commonly expected* wind. If the wind was extraordinary — something one could not have foreseen — there is no liability. Foreseeability is built into the category.

### Esh Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא אֵשׁ – דְּכֹחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בָּהֶן, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – כֹּחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בָּהֶן, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ! אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְאֵשׁ כְּאֵשׁ, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַתּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **what is different** about **Fire** that defines it as a unique category? What is different is **that another force,** the wind, **is involved with** the fire in causing damage, as the wind causes the fire to spread, **and** the fire is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** In **these** subcategories of Fire **too, another force is involved with** the items placed on the roof and causes damage, as the wind causes them to fall, **and** they are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you. Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of Fire is like** that of the primary category of **Fire, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he was referring **to a subcategory of Trampling.**

The *gemara* formulates the conceptual identity of *eish* as: *koach acher me'urav bahen* — another force mixes into it and drives it. For fire, this is typically the wind that spreads the flame beyond where it was lit, or alternatively the fuel that sustains and propagates it. The owner lit the fire, and another force caused the spread. The roof-hazard case follows the same structure: the owner placed the object, and wind — an external force — caused it to move and cause damage. It is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, a *chidush* to classify a blown-off stone as *eish*, but the structural parallel is real.

### Esh Toldos Same

The *toldah* shares the characteristic of *koach acher me'urav bahen* and the owner's responsibility to guard against foreseeable harm. *Toldah d'eish k'eish* — *k'yotzei bahen*. Still not the exception.

## Tzrorot Exception

### Regel Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֶגֶל?! הָא אוֹקֵימְנָא תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל כְּרֶגֶל! בַּחֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת, דְּהִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: A subcategory of **Trampling? But didn't we** already **establish that** the status of **a subcategory of Trampling is similar to** that of the primary category of **Trampling?** The Gemara explains: Rav Pappa was referring **to** one's liability to pay **half the** cost of the **damage** caused by **pebbles** in a case where they were inadvertently propelled by the foot of a walking animal, **which is a *halakha* learned through tradition** and not explicitly written in the Torah.

Having worked through the entire list of *avos* and found every *toldah* to be *k'yotzei bahen*, the *gemara* finally reveals what *Rav Pappa* was pointing to — a case the discussion had been holding in reserve. *V'chi ka'amar Rav Pappa a'toldah d'regel* — when *Rav Pappa* said there is a *toldah* that is *lav k'yotzei bahen*, he meant the *toldah* of *regel*. The *gemara* notes that it had already declared *toldah d'regel k'regel*, and there the matter seemed settled. What had not yet been disclosed is the exceptional case.

### Pebbles Kicked

The exception is *tzrorot d'regel*: when an animal's foot, in the course of walking, dislodges pebbles and those pebbles fly off and cause damage. This is the *toldah* of *regel* that is *lav k'yotzei bahen*.

### Reduced Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל? לְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if it is a unique halakhic category and the payment is different, **why is it characterized** as **a subcategory of Trampling?** The Gemara explains: It is due to the fact that it shares a common aspect with Trampling. One whose animal propels pebbles while it walks and thereby causes damage is liable **to pay** half of the damages **from** his **superior-quality** property, and he does not pay only from the body of his animal, as is the *halakha* concerning half the cost of the damage paid by the owner of an innocuous ox that gored a person or an animal.

The reason for diminished liability is conceptually layered. *Regel* itself is characterized as *heizika matzui* — common, incidental damage through the animal's ordinary activity. *Regel* is already, conceptually speaking, close to *mit'asek*: the animal is not acting with intent, and the damage is incidental to normal walking. When the foot kicks a pebble and that pebble causes damage, we are one further degree removed. The damage is caused not by the animal's *guf* — its actual body — but by its *koach*, the force it imparted to the pebble. And this *koach* arises from an act that was itself incidental. The difference between an animal directly stepping on something (*regel*) and an animal kicking a pebble that then hits something (*tzrorot*) is precisely the difference between *gufo* and *kocho* — a conceptually significant distinction the *gemara* applies elsewhere with consequences for liability.

### Half Damages Tradition

The *halacha* of *tzrorot d'regel* is *chatzi nezek* — half damages — and this is transmitted as a received tradition: *hilcheta gemiri lah*. It is not derived analytically from first principles in the standard way but is known through *halachic* tradition.

### Conceptual Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִבַּעְיָא בָּעֵי רָבָא – דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: חֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, אוֹ מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה מְשַׁלֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rava raise a dilemma** with regard to that matter? **As Rava raised a dilemma:** With regard to one's liability to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** caused by **pebbles** propelled by the foot of his animal, does **one pay** damages exclusively **from** the **body** of his ox that caused the damage, **or** does **one pay** damages **from** his **superior-quality** property?

The final question is: why is *tzrorot d'regel* classified as a *toldah* of *regel* at all? If it carries a different *halacha*, one might think it should be its own category — or be classified under *keren*, which also involves half-damages. The answer is that its conceptual home is *regel*: the pebbles are dislodged in the course of the animal's ordinary walking, not through an intentional act of the animal. Its structural kinship is with *regel*, not with *keren*, even though the *halachic* consequence differs. It is a *toldah* of *regel* — conceptually derived from and related to *regel* — but with a special *halacha* that departs from the *av*: *lav k'yotzei bahen*, and therefore the answer to *Rav Pappa*'s challenge.
