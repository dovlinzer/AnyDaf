# Makkot 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Overview

### Opening & Context

*Masechet Makkot* opens *Seder Nezikin*'s final masechet, though its connection to what precedes it is not simply sequential. *Makkot* does not pick up from the last chapter of *Sanhedrin* — the famous *Perek Chelek* with its rich *aggadot* — but rather continues directly from the chapter that preceded it, the discussion of *nechnakim*, those subject to strangulation. The central topic there was *edim zomemim*, conspiring witnesses, and *Makkot* takes up precisely where that discussion left off.

### Three Chapters

The masechet contains three chapters. The first deals with *edim zomemim* and the penalties that attend them. The second deals with *galut*, the exile imposed upon one who kills inadvertently. The third deals with *makkot*, lashes. One might reasonably ask why a tractate named *Makkot* reserves its namesake subject for the final chapter — but as will become clear, lashes appear already in the very first chapter, as the operative punishment imposed upon *edim zomemim* in a range of cases.

### Connection to Sanhedrin

The organic continuity between *Sanhedrin* and *Makkot* is thus both thematic and structural. The legal framework of *edim zomemim* was introduced there, and *Makkot* develops its contours, its limits, and its consequences in systematic detail.

---

## Edim Zomemim Defined

### Testimony Required

To understand *edim zomemim*, one must first appreciate the basic architecture of Jewish criminal procedure. Any accusation against a defendant requires *edut* — formal testimony. Two witnesses must appear and testify to a specific act at a specific time and place: "On Monday at eight o'clock in the morning, Reuven stole one hundred dollars from so-and-so." The entire system of adjudication rests upon this testimony.

### Hachashah vs. Hazamah

Once witnesses have testified, a second pair of witnesses may respond — but there are two fundamentally different ways they can do so, and the distinction between them is critical.

The first is *hachashah*: the second set of witnesses contradicts the content of the testimony itself. They engage with the deed. "He didn't take one hundred dollars — he took two hundred dollars." Or: "No, you're wrong about what you saw." Both sets of witnesses are arguing about what happened, what was taken, what was done. This is a clash over the facts of the case, and it may result in the court being unable to adjudicate definitively — the conflicting testimonies cancel each other out, and the accused may go free due to insufficient evidence. That is *hachashah*: they deny (*lehachachish*) one another.

The second is *hazamah*: the second set of witnesses does not engage with the deed at all. They do not say whether the accused is guilty or innocent. What they challenge is not the act but the witnesses' capacity to have witnessed it. "You could not have seen anything, because you were not there." The accused may well be guilty — that question is left entirely open — but the original witnesses are disqualified because they were physically elsewhere.

### Imanu Hayitem

The operative formula for *hazamah* is *imanu hayitem* — "you were with us." The second set of witnesses declares: on that very day, at that very hour, you were with us in a different place entirely. You were not in Jerusalem at eight in the morning; you were with us in Eilat. Therefore you could not have witnessed anything. The phrase *imanu hayitem* crystallizes the entire enterprise of *edim zomemim*: it is not a challenge to what was seen but to who could have seen it.

This distinction carries enormous moral weight. *Hachashah* implies a possible error of perception or memory. *Hazamah* implies total fabrication. The witnesses were not even present. They constructed testimony from nothing and deployed the legal system as a weapon — not to pursue justice but to destroy another person.

### Pronoun Clarification

The pronoun question in *hazamah* deserves careful attention. When the second set of witnesses says *imanu hayitem*, "you were with us," the "you" refers to the original witnesses — not to the accused. The challenge is: you, the testifying witnesses, were somewhere else; therefore your testimony is invalid. This is categorically different from a claim that the accused himself was elsewhere. If the second group wishes to argue that the accused was in another location and therefore innocent, that is a challenge to the deed — and falls squarely under *hachashah*, not *hazamah*. The two categories are mutually exclusive in their focus: *hazamah* concerns only the witnesses' whereabouts; it says nothing about the accused.

### Focused Scope

*Edim zomemim* law operates in a tightly circumscribed domain. It is not concerned with the broader question of the case or the defendant's guilt or innocence. It concerns only one thing: whether the witnesses who testified were qualified — that is, physically present — to testify. The law is, in that sense, highly pixelated. The court is not reopening the case; it is examining the witnesses themselves.

## Process & Timing

### After Judgment

The moment at which *hazamah* can operate is also precisely defined. The *din* — the judgment — must already be complete. The court has heard all testimony, weighed it, and issued a sentence. The accused stands convicted. At that point — and only at that point — does the challenge of the second witnesses carry the weight needed to constitute *hazamah* and render the original witnesses *edim zomemim*.

If the challenge is raised during the trial, before sentencing, it functions differently: it is simply another set of witnesses whose testimony the court must weigh against the first set. No one is yet *edim zomemim*; the process of adjudication is still open. As the court proceedings unfold, parties may present whatever witnesses they have, and the court determines the truth of the matter as best it can.

### Sentence-Execution Window

*Edim zomemim* can only be established in the window between sentence and execution. The sentence has been declared, the accused is to receive a specific punishment — but that punishment has not yet been carried out. The second set of witnesses steps forward during this interval and says: on that day, you were with us elsewhere. At this moment the original witnesses become *edim zomemim*.

Once the sentence is executed, the law of *hazamah* no longer applies. Even if it later becomes clear that the original witnesses were *zomemim*, the court does not compound one death with another. The act is done; we do not go back. The executed cannot be revived, and we do not pursue further punishment on their account after the fact.

### Zamam & Intent

The word *zomemim* is itself revealing. The root *z-m-m* connotes deliberate plotting. In *Eshet Chayil* (Proverbs 31), the verse reads *zamemah sadeh vatikachehu* — she considers a field and acquires it; she schemes, she plans purposefully. *Zomemim*, therefore, implies not mere error but intentional conspiracy. These witnesses did not stumble into false testimony; they devised a plan to destroy someone through the machinery of the law.

### Measure for Measure

The Torah's punishment for *edim zomemim* flows directly from this: *va'asita lo ka'asher zamam la'asot* — "you shall do to him as he conspired to do." Whatever fate the witnesses plotted for the accused, that fate befalls the witnesses themselves. If they testified that he stole one hundred dinars, they pay one hundred dinars — to the accused, not to any third party. If their testimony would have resulted in his execution, they are executed. The principle is one of precise, symmetrical measure for measure.

## First Mishnah Cases

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד הָעֵדִים נַעֲשִׂים זוֹמְמִין? ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה״, אֵין אוֹמְרִים: יֵעָשֶׂה זֶה בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה תַּחְתָּיו, אֶלָּא לוֹקֶה אַרְבָּעִים.
״מְעִידִין אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לִגְלוֹת״, אֵין אוֹמְרִים: יִגְלֶה זֶה תַּחְתָּיו, אֶלָּא לוֹקֶה אַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How are witnesses rendered conspiring** witnesses? This applies in a case where two witnesses came before the court and said: **We testify with regard to so-and-so,** who is a priest, **that he is the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*,** a *yevama* who performed the rite of *ḥalitza* to free herself from the levirate bond. Those testimonies render him a *ḥalal* (see Leviticus 21:6–7), one disqualified from the priesthood due to flawed lineage. If a second set of witnesses testifies in court and renders the first set conspiring witnesses, **one does not say** with regard to each of the conspiring witnesses: **This** witness **shall be rendered the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* in his stead. Rather, he receives forty lashes** as punishment for his false testimony.
Likewise, in a case where two witnesses came before the court and said: **We testify with regard to so-and-so that he is liable to be exiled** to a city of refuge for unwittingly killing another (see Numbers 35:11), and a second set of witnesses testifies in court and renders the first set conspiring witnesses, **one does not say** with regard to each of the conspiring witnesses: **This** witness **shall be exiled in his stead. Rather, he receives forty lashes.**

The first *mishnah* of *Makkot* opens with the question *keitzad ha'edim na'asim zomemim* — "how do witnesses become *zomemim*?" The *Gemara* will immediately flag that this is, at a deeper level, the wrong question for this *mishnah*. The cases the *mishnah* presents are not cases that illustrate how *edim zomemim* come to be; they are cases in which the normal punishment of *edim zomemim* — receiving what they conspired to inflict — cannot be applied. The real question the *mishnah* addresses is: when do *edim zomemim* fail to receive the expected punishment?

The *mishnah* gives two examples and introduces a third outcome: in both cases, the witnesses receive forty lashes.

### Ben Gerusha Case

The first case: witnesses testify that a certain man is a *ben gerushah* or a *ben chalutzah* — the son of a woman who was divorced or the son of a *chalutzah*. As *Rashi* notes, this status is only consequential if the man's father is a *kohen*. A *kohen* is forbidden to marry a divorced woman; if he does and they have a son, that son bears the name *kohen* but is a *chalal* — stripped of functioning *kehunah* status. He cannot serve in the Temple, and the disqualification passes to his descendants. If witnesses come and testify that this man is a *ben gerushah* — attacking his priestly status — and a second set of witnesses then establishes that the first set were *edim zomemim*, the question becomes: what do we give the *zomemim*? The *mishnah* answers: not the same status. They do not become *bnei gerushah*. Instead they receive forty lashes.

### Galut Case

The second case: witnesses testify that a man is liable to *galut* — exile. This applies to one who killed another person *bishgaga*, inadvertently. The punishment for such a person is exile to an *ir miklat*, a city of refuge. If the witnesses who testified to the inadvertent killing are established as *edim zomemim*, one would expect them to receive the punishment they sought to impose — namely, exile. But the *mishnah* rules otherwise: *ein omrim yigleh zeh tachtav ela lokeh arba'im* — we do not say "let this one go into exile in his place"; instead, he receives forty lashes.

## Gemara's Reframing

### Question Reversed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא ״כֵּיצַד אֵין הָעֵדִים נַעֲשִׂים זוֹמְמִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְעוֹד, מִדְּקָתָנֵי לְקַמַּן: אֲבָל אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: ״הֵיאַךְ אַתֶּם מְעִידִין, הֲרֵי בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם אַתֶּם הֱיִיתֶם עִמָּנוּ בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ זוֹמְמִין, מִכְּלָל דְּאֵלּוּ אֵין זוֹמְמִין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara analyzes the opening question of the mishna: **But** based on the cases discussed in the mishna, the *tanna* **should have** asked: **How are witnesses not rendered conspiring** witnesses? The standard punishment for conspiring witnesses is the punishment that they conspired to have inflicted upon the subject of their testimony. The mishna cites anomalous cases where their punishment does not correspond to the punishment they sought to have inflicted. The Gemara asks: **And furthermore, from** the fact **that** the *tanna* **teaches** in a mishna cited **later** (5a): **But** if the second set of witnesses attempting to render the first set conspiring witnesses **said to them: How** can **you testify** to that incident when **on that day you were with us in such and such place, these** first witnesses **are conspiring** witnesses. One learns **by inference** from the final phrase in the cited passage: These are conspiring witnesses, that **those** enumerated in the mishna here **are not conspiring** witnesses.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the *mishnah*'s opening formulation. *Keitzad ein ha'edim na'asim zomemim miba'ei lei* — the *mishnah* should have asked, "In what cases are witnesses *not* treated as *zomemim*?" or more precisely, "In what cases do they fail to receive the normal punishment?" The cases presented are not illustrations of how *edim zomemim* come to exist — their status as *edim zomemim* is not in question — but illustrations of why the expected punishment cannot be applied. It may be that the proper formulation is: in what situations can we apply the punishment of *edim zomemim*, and in what situations can we not? The *mishnah* focuses on the cases in which the standard punishment is unavailable and a substitute must be found.

### Bat Kohen Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא הָתָם קָאֵי: כׇּל הַזּוֹמְמִין מַקְדִּימִין לְאוֹתָהּ מִיתָה, חוּץ מִזּוֹמְמֵי בַּת כֹּהֵן וּבוֹעֲלָהּ, שֶׁאֵין מַקְדִּימִין לְאוֹתָהּ מִיתָה אֶלָּא לְמִיתָה אַחֶרֶת.
וְיֵשׁ עֵדִים זוֹמְמִים אֲחֵרִים, שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן דִּין הֲזָמָה כׇּל עִיקָּר, אֶלָּא מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים, כֵּיצַד? ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה״, אֵין אוֹמְרִים: יֵעָשֶׂה זֶה בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה תַּחְתָּיו, אֶלָּא לוֹקֶה אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers both questions: **The *tanna* is standing there** in his studies, at the end of tractate *Sanhedrin*, which immediately precedes *Makkot*, and *Makkot* is often appended to the end of *Sanhedrin*. The mishna there teaches (89a): **All** those **who are** rendered **conspiring witnesses are led to** be executed with **the same** mode of **execution** with which they conspired to have their victim executed, **except for conspiring** witnesses who testified that **the daughter of a priest and her paramour** committed adultery, where the daughter of the priest would be executed by burning (see Leviticus 21:9) and her paramour would be executed by strangulation. In **that** case, they are **not taken directly to** be executed with **the same** mode of **execution** that they sought to have inflicted on the woman; **rather,** they are executed with **a different** mode of **execution,** the one they sought to have inflicted on the paramour.

Therefore, the *tanna* continues in this first mishna in *Makkot*: And there are other conspiring witnesses with regard to whom the court does not apply the *halakhot* governing the punishment in standard cases of conspiring testimony at all, and they do not receive the punishment they sought to have inflicted. Rather, they receive forty lashes.

The *Gemara* then draws the link to the final relevant *mishnah* of *Sanhedrin*, just before *Perek Chelek* — the case of the *Bat Kohen*. The passage there established: *kol hazomemim makdimim le'ota mita chutz mizomemei Bat Kohen uvo'ala* — all *zomemim* receive the very death they conspired to impose, except for *zomemim* who testified against a *Bat Kohen* and her paramour, who receive a different death.

The *Bat Kohen* who commits adultery is punished by fire — *srefa* — as the Torah states, *Bat Kohen ki techel liznot et aviha hi mechalelet ba'esh tisaref*. Her paramour, however, does not receive *srefa*; he receives *chenek*, strangulation. Because the two parties receive different punishments — *srefa* unique to her, *chenek* for him — the witnesses who conspired against them cannot simply receive "the same punishment." The *srefa* is specific to the *Bat Kohen*; other women in analogous situations receive *chenek*, not *srefa*. The very particularity of her punishment renders it impossible to impose on the witnesses by the measure-for-measure principle.

This case, which concluded the relevant section of *Sanhedrin*, thus serves as the structural bridge into *Makkot*.

### Lashes Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם״, ״לוֹ״ – וְלֹא לְזַרְעוֹ. וְלִיפְסְלוּהוּ לְדִידֵיהּ וְלָא לִיפְסְלוּ לְזַרְעֵיהּ! בָּעִינַן ״כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת״, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived that the court does not punish the witnesses with the punishment they sought to have inflicted and disqualify them from the priesthood? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And you shall do to him as he conspired"** (Deuteronomy 19:19), from which the Gemara infers: It is done **"to him," but not to his offspring.** Rendering the witness a *ḥalal* would disqualify his offspring as well. The Gemara challenges: **Let** the court **invalidate** the witness **and not invalidate his offspring.** The Gemara explains: That too would not accord with the directive in the verse, as based on the verse **we require** that the punishment be **"as he conspired to do"** (Deuteronomy 19:19), **and** that is **not** the case here, as the witness conspired to disqualify the subject of his testimony and his offspring.

The *Gemara* then cites the *tanna'im*: there are *edim zomemim* for whom the court does not apply the full law of *hazamah* — that is, imposing the exact punishment they conspired to inflict — but rather imposes forty lashes instead. The remainder of the *Gemara*'s discussion is dedicated to explaining why, in each of these cases, the standard punishment is unavailable, and lashes serve as the operative substitute.

## Ben Gerusha Deep Dive

### Definition

A *ben gerushah* is the son of a *kohen* and a divorced woman — a union the Torah prohibits for a *kohen*. A *ben chalutzah* is the son of a *kohen* and a *chalutzah* — a woman who performed *chalitzah* (the ceremony releasing her from levirate marriage) and who is thereafter disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. In either case, the issue arises only when the father is a *kohen*; for an ordinary Israelite, the mother's status as a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* carries no such consequence.

### Kohen Status

A *kohen*'s son born of a forbidden union bears the surname and lineage of his father but cannot function in the *kehunah*. He may not serve in the Temple. His priestly identity exists in name but not in practice. And critically, the disqualification is not limited to him alone — it extends to his offspring.

### Chalal Concept

The technical term for this disabled priestly status is *chalal*. The word is cognate with *chalil* (a flute — a hollow reed) and with *chalal* in the sense of a void or an empty space; in modern Hebrew it is used for a casualty of war, one who has been emptied of life. A *chalal* is a *kohen* who has been hollowed out of his function — present in form, absent in capacity. He cannot perform priestly service, and he is considered *mitchalel*, desecrated, in terms of his *kehunah*.

Whether a *chalal* retains certain priestly privileges — such as eating *terumah* — and what other aspects of *kehunah* remain available to him are subjects of broader halachic discussion. What is clear is that his status is fundamentally altered.

### Lo V'Lo L'Zaro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר פְּדָא אוֹמֵר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה הַמְחַלֵּל אֵינוֹ מִתְחַלֵּל, הַבָּא לְחַלֵּל וְלֹא חִילֵּל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִתְחַלֵּל? מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: אִם כֵּן בִּטַּלְתָּ תּוֹרַת עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Padda says** that this alternative form of punishment is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If one who** actually **disqualifies** another from the priesthood, i.e., a priest who fathers a son with a divorcée disqualifies their son from the priesthood, **is not** himself **disqualified** from the priesthood, so too with regard to this witness **who came to disqualify** another from the priesthood but was unsuccessful **and did not disqualify** him because he was rendered a conspiring witness, **isn't it logical that he should not be disqualified? Ravina objects to this** reasoning: **If so,** that the failure of the conspiring witnesses to achieve their objective is the consideration at the basis of the *a fortiori* inference, **you have** thereby **rendered the *halakha* of conspiring witnesses obsolete,** as one could claim:

The core *halakhic* problem is now apparent. The Torah commands regarding *edim zomemim*: *va'asitem lo ka'asher zamam* — do to them what they conspired to do. But the status of *ben gerushah* or *chalal* is not a punishment that can be imposed on a person independently of his lineage. It is an inherited condition: one is a *chalal* because of who one's parents are. You cannot create a *chalal* by judicial decree.

Moreover — and this is the decisive point — the *Gemara* derives from the verse: *lo v'lo l'zaro*. The verse says *lo*, "to him" — do to this witness what he conspired to do. But the status of *chalal* necessarily entails effects upon future generations: the witness's own children would also be disqualified. The Torah's formulation *lo* — singular, directed at the witness himself — excludes his offspring. The punishment must be confined to the witness; it cannot radiate forward into his descendants. Since making someone a *chalal* is inseparable from affecting his *zera*, his descendants, it is a punishment that structurally cannot be applied. *Ba'inan ka'asher zamam la'asot v'leika* — we require what he conspired to do, and here there is no available way to do it.

## Bar Pada's Logic

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הַסּוֹקֵל אֵינוֹ נִסְקָל, הַבָּא לִסְקוֹל וְלֹא סָקַל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִסָּקֵל? אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If one who stones** another, i.e., who testified that another is liable to be executed via the death penalty of stoning and was rendered a conspiring witness after that person was executed, **is not stoned,** as the *halakha* is that conspiring witnesses receive the punishment that they conspired to have inflicted and not the punishment that they actually had inflicted, then with regard to a conspiring witness **who came to stone** another **and** was unsuccessful **and did not stone** him, as he was rendered a conspiring witness before that person was executed, **isn't it logical that he should not be stoned?** The Gemara concludes: **Rather, it is clear as we answered initially:** "And you shall do to him as he conspired"; this should be done to him, but not to his offspring.

*Bar Pada* offers an independent argument via *kal vachomer*. The formulation, as it appears in the *Gemara*, is dense with wordplay on the root *ch-l-l*: *uma hamichalel eino mitchalel, haba lechalel velo chilel, eino din shelo yitchalel*. Unpacked: the one who is actively *mechalel* — who causes another to become a *chalal* — himself undergoes no change in status. Consider a *kohen* who enters into a forbidden relationship with a divorced woman. Their son is a *chalal*. The *kohen* father caused the *challalah*. And yet nothing happens to his own priestly standing — he is the *mechalel*, the one who desecrated, but he is not *mitchalel*, he himself is not desecrated. He may, of course, be obligated to end the relationship and cannot serve while in it, but his *kehunah* as such is not revoked.

Now, *Bar Pada* reasons: if a person who actually commits the forbidden act and actively produces a *chalal* himself suffers no change in priestly status — how could witnesses who merely conspired to produce this status, but did not actually do so (*haba lechalel velo chilel*), possibly be subjected to it? The one who did the deed loses nothing. The ones who conspired to do the deed lose... even less? The *kal vachomer* is: if the one who causes *challalah* is exempt from *mitchalel*, surely the one who only attempted and failed to cause it cannot be made *mitchalel* either.

### Challenge

*Ravina* challenges *Bar Pada* sharply: *im ken bitalta torat edim zomemim* — if this logic holds, you have dismantled the entire framework of *edim zomemim*. One could always construct a parallel *kal vachomer* for any punishment: the person who actually commits the act receives a certain punishment, while the one who merely conspired and was stopped receives... less? Surely less than the actual offender. So no *edim zomemim* could ever be punished. The argument proves too much.

### Sokel Parallel

The *Gemara* illustrates *Ravina*'s critique with a parallel case. *U'mah hasokel eino niskal, haba liskol v'lo sakal, eino din she'lo yissakel?* — "Just as the one who kills another by *skeila* (stoning) does not himself receive *skeila* (he receives *sayif*, execution by sword, as a murderer) — so too one who came to cause *skeila* but did not succeed need not receive *skeila* either?" Clearly this cannot be the law. The fact that the perpetrator of the original act receives a different punishment from what he caused does not automatically grant the same exemption to *edim zomemim*. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ela mechvarta k'deshaninan me'ikara* — the correct resolution is the original one already established. The constraint on punishing *edim zomemim* with the status of *chalal* derives from the verse *lo v'lo l'zaro* — the punishment must be applicable to the witness alone, and since *challalah* necessarily extends to his descendants, it cannot be imposed at all.

## Galut Case Analysis

### Galut Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעִידִין אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב גָּלוּת כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא יָנוּס אֶל אַחַת הֶעָרִים״ – הוּא, וְלָא זוֹמְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in a case where two witnesses came before the court and said: **We testify with regard to so-and-so that he is liable to** be punished with **exile,** one does not say that these witnesses shall be exiled in his stead; rather, they receive forty lashes. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Reish Lakish says:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states** with regard to an unwitting killer: **"And he shall flee to one of the cities"** (Deuteronomy 19:5), and the Gemara infers: **He** shall flee, **but conspiring** witnesses shall **not.**

The second case in the *mishnah* involves someone sentenced to *galut* — exile to an *ir miklat*. This punishment applies to one who kills another person *bishgaga*, inadvertently. The *ir miklat* is not a prison; it is a city of refuge, populated by *levi'im* and equipped with teachers. The killer must remain there until the death of the *Kohen Gadol*, after which he may return. The purpose is not punitive in the ordinary sense but rehabilitative and protective: to remove the unintentional killer from society and provide a space for reflection and return.

### Modern Example

The principle of *bishgaga* maps, at least conceptually, onto modern situations. A person driving carefully — within speed limits, sober, attentive — who is struck by circumstances outside his control and kills another, is in the position of one who acted *be'ones*, under compulsion. A person driving recklessly — drunk, speeding, negligent — who kills someone has acted in a manner closer to *shgaga bemeizid*: the act was unintentional, but the conditions that produced it were chosen. The *Gemara* recognizes these distinctions even in ancient categories.

### Torah Source

The *Gemara* identifies the verse that excludes *zomemim* from receiving this punishment: *hu yanus el achat he'arim* (Deuteronomy) — "he shall flee to one of the cities." The word *hu*, "he," is read restrictively: *hu v'lo zomemim* — he goes into exile, and not the conspiring witnesses. The verse's use of the singular personal pronoun creates a textual exclusion of any substitute.

### Rabbi Yochanan Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה הוּא, שֶׁעָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה – בְּמֵזִיד אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה, הֵן, שֶׁלֹּא עָשׂוּ מַעֲשֶׂה – בְּמֵזִיד אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִגְלוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** the killer, **who performed an action** that, had he done so **intentionally, he would not be exiled** for it even if he were not sentenced to death, e.g., because there was no forewarning, then in the case of the conspiring witnesses, **who did not perform an action,** as their conspiracy was exposed and their testimony rejected, even if they testified **intentionally, isn't it logical that they should not be exiled?**

*Rabbi Yochanan* reinforces this with a *kal vachomer*. If someone kills *bemeizid* — intentionally — and there is insufficient testimony or warning to convict him, he goes entirely free. Nothing happens to him. He either dies or walks away. Now, these *zomemim* did not kill anyone at all; they merely testified falsely about an inadvertent killing. Is it reasonable to impose *galut* upon them — when even the intentional killer may escape punishment entirely? The argument is not that the *zomemim* deserve lenience, but that the category of *galut* as a response doesn't fit their situation.

### Kaparah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא נוֹתֶנֶת, (וַהֲלֹא דִין הוּא): הוּא, שֶׁעָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה – בְּמֵזִיד לָא לִיגְלֵי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תֶּיהְוֵי לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה. הֵן, שֶׁלֹּא עָשׂוּ מַעֲשֶׂה – בְּמֵזִיד נָמֵי לִיגְלוֹ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֶיהְוֵי לְהוּ כַּפָּרָה! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But that** distinction **provides** support to the contrary. **Could this not** be derived through **logical inference?** With regard to **one who performed an action,** i.e., killed a person, if he did so **intentionally, let** him **not be exiled so that he will not have atonement** for his action, and he will instead receive harsh punishment at the hand of Heaven. With regard to the conspiring witnesses, **who did not perform an action,** even if they testified **intentionally, let them also be exiled so that they will have atonement** for their misdeed. The Gemara concludes: **Rather, it is clear in accordance with** the explanation **of Reish Lakish:** "And he shall flee to one of the cities"; he, but not conspiring witnesses.

There is a deeper reason why *galut* cannot be given to *edim zomemim*: *galut* is a form of *kaparah*, atonement. The person who kills *bishgaga* undergoes exile as a process of personal expiation — it addresses a soul that caused death, even unwillingly. The *zomemim*, by contrast, did not kill anyone. They conspired; they testified falsely; but no act of killing was committed through their hands. There is no act of unintentional killing for which they require atonement. Exile without an act to atone for is not *kaparah* — it is simply displacement. And since the function of *galut* is inextricably bound to the *kaparah* it provides for a specific kind of act, it cannot meaningfully be applied to those who committed no such act.

## Lashes Source

### Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: רֶמֶז לְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? רֶמֶז לְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין?! וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם״! אֶלָּא: רֶמֶז לְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין שֶׁלּוֹקִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה, מִנַּיִן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִצְדִּיקוּ אֶת הַצַּדִּיק וְהִרְשִׁיעוּ אֶת הָרָשָׁע. וְהָיָה אִם בִּן הַכּוֹת הָרָשָׁע״. מִשּׁוּם וְהִצְדִּיקוּ אֶת הַצַּדִּיק – וְהִרְשִׁיעוּ אֶת הָרָשָׁע, וְהָיָה אִם בִּן הַכּוֹת הָרָשָׁע?
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says: From where** is **an allusion in the Torah to conspiring witnesses** derived? The Gemara asks: Is **an allusion to conspiring witnesses** required? **But isn't it written** explicitly: **"And you shall do to him as he conspired"** (Deuteronomy 19:19)? **Rather,** Ulla's question is: **From where** is **an allusion in the Torah to** the *halakha* **that conspiring witnesses are flogged** in certain cases derived? It is derived from that **which is written:** "If there is a quarrel between people and they come to judgment, and the judges judge them, **and they vindicated the righteous and condemned the wicked, and it shall be if the wicked is deserving of lashes"** (Deuteronomy 25:1–2). Ostensibly, this verse is difficult: Is it **due to** the fact that **"they vindicated the righteous"** that they **"condemned the wicked, and it shall be if the wicked is deserving of lashes"?** In most disputes, the fact that one party is vindicated does not necessarily lead to lashes for the other party.

After establishing that *edim zomemim* cannot receive the status-transfer of *ben gerushah* or the exile of *galut*, the *Gemara* turns to what they *do* receive: lashes. *Ulla* asks: *remez l'edim zomemim shelokin min haTorah m'nayin* — where in the Torah is there a hint that *edim zomemim* receive lashes? This question might itself seem puzzling — surely the Torah addresses *edim zomemim* explicitly and there are clear verses about them — but *Ulla* is asking specifically for the source establishing lashes as their punishment in these cases.

### Pasuk Cited

*Ulla* identifies the verse as *v'hitzdiku et hatzadik v'hirshiu et harasha v'haya im bin hakot harasha* (Deuteronomy 25:1) — "and they shall justify the righteous and condemn the wicked, and if the wicked one is liable to lashes..." At first glance this verse is puzzling: what is the connection between justifying the righteous and condemning the wicked, and then beating the wicked? What scenario links these elements coherently?

### Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: עֵדִים שֶׁהִרְשִׁיעוּ אֶת הַצַּדִּיק, וַאֲתוֹ עֵדִים אַחֲרִינֵי וְהִצְדִּיקוּ אֶת הַצַּדִּיק דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא, וְשַׁוִּינְהוּ לְהָנֵי רְשָׁעִים, ״וְהָיָה אִם בִּן הַכּוֹת הָרָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse is addressing the case of **witnesses who,** through their testimony, **condemned the righteous, and other witnesses came and vindicated the original righteous** person **and rendered these** first set of witnesses **wicked** conspiring witnesses. In that case, the verse states: **"And it shall be if the wicked is deserving of lashes,"** indicating that lashes are an appropriate punishment for conspiring witnesses.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty by reading the verse as describing the *edim zomemim* scenario. Witnesses came and *hirshiu et hatzadik* — they falsely condemned the righteous man, the innocent accused. Then other witnesses came and *hitzdiku et hatzadik d'meikara* — they vindicated him, proving that the first witnesses were *zomemim*. These false witnesses are then the *reshaim*, the wicked, who are now to be beaten. The verse, read this way, describes precisely the sequence of *hazamah*: false accusation, vindication, and punishment of the accusers. And the punishment the verse specifies is lashes.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִ״לֹּא תַעֲנֶה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why not **derive** the *halakha* that conspiring witnesses are liable to receive lashes **from** the prohibition: **"You shall not bear** false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:13)? The Gemara answers: One is not flogged for violating that prohibition, **due to** the fact **that it is a prohibition that does not involve an action,** as one violates it through speech, not action, **and** the principle is: For **every prohibition that does not involve an action, one is not flogged for its** violation. Therefore, it was necessary to derive the *halakha* from the verse: "And they vindicated the righteous."

The *Gemara* challenges this derivation: *ve-tipok lei mi-lo ta'aneh* — why not simply derive the lashes from the prohibition of *lo ta'aneh be-rei'acha ed shaker*, "do not bear false witness against your neighbor"? That is an explicit prohibition. The answer is a point of general *halachic* principle.

### Lav Classification

*Lo ta'aneh* is a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — a prohibition that involves no physical action. Bearing false witness is accomplished entirely through speech; no deed is performed. The general rule is that a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* does not ordinarily carry lashes, because lashes are the penalty for transgressing a prohibition that involves a physical act. *Ulla* therefore needed a different source — one that positively establishes lashes for *edim zomemim* — rather than simply invoking the prohibition of false testimony, which by its nature as a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* would not automatically generate a punishment of lashes.

## Four Things Said

### Four Things Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין: אֵין נַעֲשִׂין בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה, וְאֵין גּוֹלִין לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט, וְאֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין אֶת הַכּוֹפֶר, וְאֵין נִמְכָּרִין בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמְרוּ: אַף אֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין עַל פִּי עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Four matters were stated with regard to conspiring witnesses,** i.e., there are four cases in which their punishment deviates from the norm. **They are not rendered the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*; they are not exiled to a city of refuge; they do not pay the ransom** if they testified that the forewarned ox of someone killed another; **and they are not sold as a Hebrew slave** in a case where they testified that one stole property and he would be sold into slavery if he lacked the means to repay the owner. The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Akiva: They also do not pay based on their own** admission. If they were rendered conspiring witnesses in one court, and before that court managed to collect the payment that they owed, they appeared in a different court and admitted that they had been rendered conspiring witnesses, they are exempt from payment.

A *Tanna'itic* teaching enumerates four things said regarding *edim zomemim*: they do not become a *ben gerushah* or *ben chalutzah*; they are not sent into exile; they do not pay *kofer*; and they are not sold as an *eved Ivri*.

### Fifth Thing

*Rabbi Akiva* adds a fifth: *af ein meshalmim al pi atzmam* — they cannot unilaterally redeem themselves by appearing before a different court and paying money to discharge their obligation. Even if they were to volunteer their guilt and offer payment, this self-initiated resolution is unavailable to them.

### First Two Explained

The first two items — the prohibition of imposing *ben gerushah* status and the unavailability of *galut* — have been analyzed at length in the preceding discussion.

### Kofer & Kaparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נַעֲשִׂין בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה – כְּדַאֲמַרַן. וְאֵין גּוֹלִין לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט – כְּדַאֲמַרַן. וְאֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין אֶת הַכּוֹפֶר – קָסָבְרִי: כּוּפְרָא – כַּפָּרָה, וְהָנֵי לָאו בְּנֵי כַּפָּרָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **They are not rendered the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*, as we stated** and explained earlier. **And they are not exiled to a city of refuge, as we stated** and explained earlier. **And they do not pay the ransom,** as these Sages **hold** that **the ransom** paid by one whose ox killed another is **atonement** for him, as he is liable for the actions of his animal, and it is not payment of damages. **And these** conspiring witnesses **are not subject to** a need for **atonement,** as their ox did not kill anyone.

The third item, *kofer*, requires explanation. *Kofer* is a monetary payment made in atonement — specifically, the ransom paid by the owner of an ox that gored a person to death, when the ox was known to be dangerous (*shor hamu'ad*). The payment functions as *kaparah*, expiation for an act. The *Gemara* states the principle: *kofra kaparah* — *kofer* is atonement. And *edim zomemim* are not *bnei kaparah* for this purpose — they are not parties for whom this form of atonement is applicable. They committed no physical act of killing. There is nothing for them to atone for through *kofer*, because *kofer* is calibrated to the act of a killer, and they did not kill.

### Kofer Discussion Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תָּנָא כּוּפְרָא כַּפָּרָה? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַן פִּדְיֹן נַפְשׁוֹ״ – דְּמֵי נִיזָּק. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: דְּמֵי מַזִּיק. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? דְּמָר סָבַר: כּוּפְרָא מָמוֹנָא, וּמָר סָבַר: כּוּפְרָא כַּפָּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **the ransom** is **atonement? Rav Ḥisda says: It is Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse concerning a case where the ox of one person gored another: "If ransom is imposed upon him, **and he shall give the redemption of his soul"** (Exodus 21:30); the term "his soul" means **the value of the victim,** i.e., the owner of the ox must pay the heirs of the deceased his value as it would be assessed were he sold as a slave in the market. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** The term "his soul" means **the value of the one liable for the damage. What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this** principle: **That** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **the ransom** is **monetary restitution** for the damage that his ox caused the victim; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that **the ransom** is **atonement,** as he thereby redeems his own soul from death at the hand of Heaven?

The *Gemara* then opens a further discussion: *man tanna kofra kaparah* — who is the *tanna* who holds that *kofer* functions as *kaparah*? *Rav Chisda* identifies the position as that of *Rabbi Yishmael bno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*, based on a *beraita* citing the verse *v'natan pidyon nafsho* from *Shemot*, *Parshat Mishpatim*. The underlying question is what the *kofer* payment represents: does one pay *dmei nizak* — the assessed value of the victim — or *dmei mazik* — the assessed value of the perpetrator? *Rabbi Yishmael bno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* holds *dmei mazik*. This disagreement has implications for how the *kaparah* function of *kofer* is understood, and the *Gemara*'s treatment of it opens a broader analysis that continues into the subsequent *daf*.
