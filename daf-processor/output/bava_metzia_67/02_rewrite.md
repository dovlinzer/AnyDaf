# Bava Metzia 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Asmachta Mechanics

### Asmachta Conditional

The sugya opens with a statement of Rav Papa on daf 66b, beginning approximately ten lines down: *hai asmachta zimnin kanyia ve-zimnin lo kanyia* — an *asmachta* sometimes effects an acquisition and sometimes does not. By way of background, an *asmachta* refers to a conditional transaction of a futures nature, classically where a borrower stakes a piece of land against a loan, with the lender acquiring the land outright if the loan is not repaid by a fixed date. The fundamental rabbinic concern is that the borrower never fully resolves in his mind to transfer the property, since he fully expects to repay in time, and therefore the conditional sale lacks the definitive mental commitment that a valid *kinyan* requires.

### Bar Drinker Case

Rav Papa illustrates the distinction with a behavioral test case: *ashkechei de-ka shati shakhra, kani* — if one finds the borrower at the tavern drinking, the *asmachta* takes effect. But *de-ka mehapekh a-zuzi lo kani* — if he is out searching for money to repay the loan, the *asmachta* does not take effect. The underlying logic is that a borrower idling at a tavern demonstrates that he has effectively resolved to let the deadline pass, and the conditional sale therefore acquires force. A borrower scrambling to find the funds, by contrast, has not abandoned his intention to repay, and the condition never fully crystallizes into a binding transfer. This reasoning has structural parallels to the *sugyot* in Ketubot dealing with *ones be-gittin*, where a husband who set a deadline for a *get* but was delayed en route faces analogous questions about intent and timing.

Rav Acha mi-Difti challenges Rav Papa's test by raising two alternative readings of the tavern scenario. First, *dilma lefakhukhei pakhdei ka shati* — perhaps the borrower is drinking precisely to dull his anxiety, as Rashi explains, drowning his sorrows rather than acquiescing to the loss. Second, *ei nami inash achrina asmakhyei a-zuzi* — perhaps another person has already promised to lend him the money. Either possibility undermines the inference that sitting in the tavern signals genuine abandonment of the right of redemption, leaving Rav Papa's behavioral test inconclusive even in the paradigm case.

### Precision Doctrine

Ravina proposes an alternative criterion: *ei kappid bidmei, vadai kani* — if the borrower was precise about the monetary amount, the conditional sale definitively takes effect. Rav Acha mi-Difti pushes back here as well, suggesting that the borrower's monetary precision may simply reflect a desire not to let the field depreciate in value — in other words, his precision is about self-interest in the transaction, not a wholehearted commitment to the conditional sale. In response, Rav Papa reformulates: *ei kappid be-ara, vadai kani* — if the borrower is precise and insistent about the land itself, then the *asmachta* takes effect. The underlying conceptual point in both alternatives is that the determination of whether an *asmachta* is valid turns on assessing the seller's subjective mental state — whether his mind was fully settled on the conditional transfer — and that different contextual facts bear on that assessment in different ways.

### Asmachta→Apothiki

Rav Papa then introduces a further principle: *af al gav de-amru rabanan asmachta lo kanyia, apotiki havei le-migba minei* — even though the Sages ruled that an *asmachta* does not effect a sale, the failed *asmachta* can nonetheless function as an *apothiki*. An *apothiki* is the designation of a specific property as the source of repayment for a loan. The lender does not acquire ownership of the field, but the field is earmarked as the asset from which the debt will be collected. Thus, Rav Papa argues, even where the conditional sale is void, the transaction retains legal force as a lien designation.

It is worth pausing here to note the broader taxonomy. *Asmachta*, *mashkon*, and *apothiki* are all distinct forms of collateralized obligation, each with its own *halachic* rules, even though colloquially we might group them together. *Shi'abud* in its general sense is a blanket lien on property, without any particular designation. *Apothiki* is the specific designation of a field as the source of repayment — but as the *gemara* will clarify, there are two gradations even within *apothiki*: the field designated as the primary source, or the field designated as the exclusive source.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan challenges Rav Papa: *mi ka'amar lei kani l'migvaya minei* — did the borrower ever actually say that the field was being designated for collection purposes? The language of the *asmachta* was a conditional sale, not a declaration that this field was to be the exclusive means of repayment. The attempted *apothiki* reading therefore lacks explicit textual grounding.

### Mar Zutra Challenge

Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, presses the challenge to Ravina: if the borrower explicitly said "acquire this field so that the loan may be collected from it," that would indeed sound like *apothiki* — but *sof sof Asmachta hi* — in the end, that is still an *asmachta*, since the sale remains conditional on non-repayment. The distinction Rav Papa draws between *asmachta* and *apothiki* thus threatens to collapse.

The *gemara* therefore clarifies what Rav Papa's *apothiki* case must actually look like: *lo yehei lach pir'on ela mizo* — the borrower declares that the lender shall have no recourse for repayment other than from this particular field. This is not a primary designation with fallback alternatives; it is an exclusive designation. The exclusivity is precisely what distinguishes the *apothiki* from the *asmachta*. In an *asmachta*, the borrower retains the option to repay in cash and thereby redeem the property — which is why his mind never fully settles on the transfer. In an exclusive *apothiki*, there is no alternative, and therefore the nature of the transaction is categorically different.

## Field Designation

### Flood & Fields

The *gemara* presents a case involving a field sold with a quality guarantee (*achrayut*). The seller guaranteed that if a creditor with a prior lien seized the field from the buyer, the seller would compensate from his own best land. The buyer asked: may I collect from your *idei idit* — your finest of the finest field? The seller replied that he wished to retain that field for himself, and offered instead to pay from his next-best field, the *idit*. The parties proceeded on that basis. Subsequently, the creditor did indeed seize the guaranteed field, and the buyer came to collect from the seller's *idit* field. Before he could do so, however, a flood washed away the seller's *idei idit* — the superior field that had been excluded from the guarantee.

### Quality Ranking

Rav Papa initially inclined to rule that the seller must still compensate from the *idit* field, since it physically exists and the guarantee was made against that specific field. Rav Acha mi-Difti challenges this position: *ki amri lach ana magvina lach d'hava idei idit kamai* — the reason the seller agreed to offer the *idit* field was precisely because he still had his *idei idit* field as his personal reserve. Now that the *idei idit* has been destroyed by the flood, the *idit* has effectively moved up in rank to fill that role. *Hashta kaima lei idit bi-mkom idei idit* — at this point the *idit* stands in the place of the *idei idit*, and the seller should therefore only be required to provide from the medium-quality field, since that is now what corresponds to the relative tier that was originally guaranteed. The conclusion the *gemara* leans toward is that the designation of field quality is relative rather than absolute. If the top-tier field is lost, the rankings shift downward by one, and the guarantee adjusts accordingly.

## Wine Collateral

### Wine=Currency

The *gemara* presents a case: Rav Bar Bar Shva owed Rav Kahana money, and declared that if he did not repay by a certain date, Rav Kahana could take his wine. This is structurally parallel to the earlier cases involving fields and conditional transfers. Rav Papa sought to argue that the laws of *asmachta* do not apply here: *hani mili be-ara, de-lav le-zavuni kaima* — the Sages' ruling that *asmachta lo kanyia* refers specifically to land, which is not normally in a state of market readiness. *Aval chamra keivan de-le-zavuni kai ke-zuzei dami* — wine, however, since it is essentially a commodity ready for sale, is comparable to currency itself. On this view, pledging wine against a loan is no different from pledging cash, and no *asmachta* problem arises.

### Futures Doctrine

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua counters by citing a tradition in the name of Rabbah: *kol de-ee lo kani* — any transaction structured as a conditional future does not effect an acquisition. The *asmachta* rule applies equally to movables, including wine, and the distinction between land and commodities is insufficient to eliminate the *asmachta* concern. This definitively closes the argument Rav Papa had sought to open.

## Asmachta Void

### Land & Produce

Rav Nachman now states: *hashta de-amri rabanan asmachta lo kanyia, hadar ara ve-hadar peiri* — now that the Sages have ruled that an *asmachta* does not effect an acquisition, both the land and all produce harvested during the period of the ostensible acquisition must be returned. The logic is straightforward: if the sale was invalid from the outset, the lender held the field without legal title throughout, and all fruits of the field collected in the interim must be disgorged.

### Mistaken Forgiveness

From this ruling, the *gemara* infers that Rav Nachman must hold *mechilah be-ta'us lo havei mechilah* — a waiver made under mistaken assumptions does not constitute a valid waiver. The reason this inference is necessary is as follows: the borrower and lender had both agreed to the *asmachta* arrangement, and both presumably understood that the lender would enjoy the field's produce during the loan period. One might argue that the borrower's acquiescence amounts to *mechilah* — a voluntary relinquishment of his rights to that produce. Rav Nachman's insistence on returning the produce demonstrates that he rejects this possibility. Since the borrower agreed under the mistaken belief that the *asmachta* sale was legally effective, his apparent *mechilah* was premised on a legal error, and *mechilah be-ta'us* does not stand.

### Date Futures Test

A *baraita* is adduced as a challenge to Rav Nachman's position. The *baraita* discusses the case of one who sells another person the future produce of his date palm: *ad she-lo bau la-olam yachol lachzor bo* — until the dates have actually come into existence, the seller may retract. Rav Nachman is cited as ruling *af misheba'u la-olam yachol lachzor bo* — even after the dates have come into existence, he may retract. This appears to follow from the *asmachta* principle: such a sale of futures is inherently defective, and either party retains the right to withdraw. But if Rav Nachman allows retraction even after the dates exist, then presumably the produce gathered during the period of the agreement cannot all be reclaimed. Does this not contradict his ruling that produce must be returned when *asmachta* fails?

### Consumed Fruits

Rav Nachman defends himself: *modina de-ishmit ve-akhil lo mafkinan minei* — he concedes that if the fruits were actually consumed, they cannot be extracted from the holder. The requirement to return produce applies only where the produce remains identifiable and extractable. If it has been entirely consumed, two compounding considerations converge. First, the *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis: even if the waiver was defective, practically speaking there is nothing to recover. Second, and more technically, there is the principle of *davar shelo ba la-olam* — the sale of something not yet in existence. The fruits of the date palm did not exist at the time of the original transaction, and the sale of such futures is generally unenforceable. These two considerations intersect in the *asmachta* context: the question of when the dates are considered to have "come into the world" — *ba la-olam* — determines the point at which retraction is no longer possible, while the *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine governs what must be returned when the transaction is unwound. The *gemara* notes that a further distinction applies: in a case of sale (*zevinei*) as opposed to a loan (*halva'ah*), the *asmachta* concern operates differently. In the loan context, the transaction sits much closer to the domain of *ribis*, and this proximity introduces additional stringency, as Rashi notes: *mekhzei ke-ribit she-mitkhila be-halva'ah ba leh, ve-ke-ribit ketzutzah dami* — it resembles interest that arose out of a loan from the outset, and appears similar to explicit prohibited interest.

## Ona'ah & Ilonit

### Rava's Challenge

The *gemara* relates that Rava was once sitting before Rav Nachman and wished to challenge him from the laws of *ona'ah*, though he was forestalled when Rav Nachman perceived the question and pre-empted with the answer of *ilonit*. The reconstructed challenge runs as follows: every case of *ona'ah* is by definition a case of *mechilah be-ta'us*. A buyer overcharged in a transaction presumably does not complain if the overcharge is less than a sixth (*shtus*) of the purchase price — and yet that acquiescence operates precisely because the buyer believes he is paying fair market value. The buyer is *mochel* under mistaken assumptions about price. If so, one might argue that *mechilah be-ta'us* here *is* in fact valid *mechilah* — which would contradict Rav Nachman's principle. This presents a potential refutation (*tiyuvta*) of Rav Nachman's position.

### Ilonit Exception

Rav Nachman is understood to have anticipated the *ona'ah* challenge and gestured toward the case of *ilonit* as his response. The *mishnah* rules that an *ilonit* — a woman who does not develop normal secondary sexual characteristics and is typically unable to bear children — forfeits her *ketubbah*, *peiros*, *mezonos*, and *bla'os* in the event of divorce. The situation is one of *mekach ta'us*, a transaction entered under mistaken terms: the husband married on the presumption of normal circumstances, and the marriage is effectively re-evaluated once the wife's condition becomes known. Nonetheless, despite the apparent *mechilah be-ta'us* on the wife's part — since she presumably would have assented to reduced financial protections in order to secure marriage (*denicha lah de-tipuk aleih shma de-ishus*, as Rashi explains, she benefits from acquiring the social standing of a married woman) — the Sages structured the ruling to facilitate her marriage. The *ilonit* exception thus reflects a broader principle: public policy considerations can override the ordinary logic of *mechilah be-ta'us*, allowing the law to treat an apparent waiver as effective even where a strict *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis might not support it.

### Ona'ah Rebuttal

A later *amora* steps in to observe that neither the *ona'ah* challenge nor the *ilonit* answer is actually persuasive. *Lo ona'ah tyuvta velo ilonit mesay'a.* The *ona'ah* case is not a valid challenge because the overcharged buyer was never engaged in a conscious *mechilah* at all. *De-lo yadei de-isye ona'ah de-machil gabeih* — the buyer did not know he was being overcharged and therefore had nothing to waive. He thought he was paying market price. This is categorically different from the *asmachta* case, where both parties entered the arrangement with full awareness that a conditional transfer was being made. In *asmachta*, the borrower actively agreed to a deviant transaction; the only question is whether his *mechilah* of those rights was vitiated by his mistaken legal assumption. In *ona'ah*, there was no conscious *mechilah* of any kind, because the discrepancy in price was entirely unknown to the buyer. The two cases are therefore not analogous, and *ona'ah* fails to serve as a counterexample to Rav Nachman's *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine.

## Redemption Case

### Repurchase Agreement

The *gemara* presents a case: a woman asked an agent to purchase a field on her behalf from one of her relatives. The agent did so. Afterward, the seller asked the agent: *ei havu li zuzei, mahadrat lah nihali?* — if I later obtain money, will she sell it back to me? The agent responded with the expression *at ve-navla achai* — roughly, "you and she are like siblings," implying of course she will accommodate you. There is a dispute in the commentaries over whether *navla* is the woman's name or simply an Aramaic term for sibling; either way, the agent is guaranteeing the seller an informal right of repurchase.

### Buyer's Intent

The *gemara* notes that because of this assurance, *samcha da'tei ve-lo gamar u-makni ara* — the seller's mind was put at ease and he never fully resolved to transfer the field irrevocably. Does this mean the original sale was incomplete? And if the buyer later conveys the field back to him, must she also return the produce (*hadar peiri*) she collected in the interim? These are the animating questions. If the seller's mind was never fully settled on the sale, one might treat the arrangement as an *asmachta*-type transaction, triggering the *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis — and by Rav Nachman's principle, produce would need to be returned along with the land.

### Abaye's Questions

Abaye presses several questions to Raba: how do we categorize the nature of this arrangement? Is the underlying agreement an *asmachta*? Does it raise concerns of *ribis ketzutzah* — explicit prohibited interest that can be remedied in court — or only *avak ribis* — the more attenuated "dust of interest" that is halachically problematic but cannot be extracted by a *beit din*? The *nafka mina* between these two categories is precisely whether a court case lies. Rabbah bar Rav Huna offers his assessment: *mistabra ke-avak ribis havei, ve-ein yotzin be-dayanim* — it appears that this arrangement is *avak ribis*, not actionable in court. Rava is then cited in agreement with that framing.

The broader question Abaye raises about *mashkon* parallels this case: just as the woman's informal repurchase agreement was problematic because *delo katz* — the value was imprecisely defined — similarly a *mashkon* arrangement in which the lender harvests produce of indeterminate value over an indefinite period also suffers from the same lack of precision. Whether it is then categorized as a sale or a loan is a further distinction: in the loan context, the *ribis* concern is even more acute, since *avak ribis* touching a loan more closely resembles the prohibited forms of interest.

### Ravina's Ruling

Rav Papi reports that Ravina actually adjudicated this type of case in practice: *ve-chashiv ve-afik piri* — he calculated the value of the produce and required it to be returned. This was contrary to the ruling of Rabbah bar Rav Huna, who had held that *avak ribis* is not recoverable in court. Ravina's practice thus treated the produce as extractable, presumably by applying Rav Nachman's *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine to require full restoration of the fruits.

## Mashkon Overview

### Mashkanta Sura

Mar son of Rav Yosef, citing Rava, introduces a critical practical distinction based on local custom. In a place where the practice is *de-misalkei* — where the court evicts the lender from the *mashkon* field once sufficient produce has been consumed to equal the value of the loan — *achal shiur zuzei, misalkinan lei*: once the lender has eaten produce worth the full amount of the loan, the court removes him. This is the *mashkanta* in its characteristic form in those communities: the lender's right to the produce is self-liquidating, with the field returning to the borrower once the debt has been discharged through produce.

### Eviction Payment

The immediate question is what happens if the lender has eaten exactly the value of the loan. The ruling is that the court may evict him at that point — the debt is discharged, and the collateral arrangement has run its course. The borrower has effectively repaid through the produce of the field, and no further cash payment is required to reclaim the land.

### Overeating Risk

However, *achal tefei lo mafkinan minei* — if the lender consumed more produce than the value of the loan, the court cannot extract the excess from him. Furthermore, *lo mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — the excess cannot be applied as a credit toward a different outstanding loan between the same parties. The reason is that the excess consumption takes on the character of *ribis*, and since *avak ribis* cannot be extracted by a court, there is no judicial mechanism for recovery. Rashi makes the point explicit: this is a case in a loan context where the *avak ribis* concern is heightened because the transaction arose out of a loan from the outset.

In a different jurisdiction — understood to be the practice of *ditmei* — the rule operates more strictly in the borrower's favor: *achal tefei mafkinan minei* — if the lender consumed more than the loan value, the court does extract the excess. And *mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — the excess can be applied across loans. The tighter enforcement reflects an understanding that the agreement was truly precise, limiting the lender's benefit to the exact loan amount.

### Rav Ashi Dilemma

Rav Ashi derives a striking consequence from the *first* model — the community practice where excess cannot be extracted. He argues: *hashta de-amrat achal tefei lo mafkinan minei, achal shiur zuzei nami lo mesalkinan lei belo zuzei* — if we accept that excess consumption cannot be extracted, then by parity of reasoning, even when the lender has consumed exactly the loan value, we cannot evict him without requiring the borrower to pay cash. The argument is subtle. The act of attempting to evict the lender without any payment — *salukei belo zuzei* — would retroactively recharacterize the lender's consumption of the produce as something other than repayment of the loan: *lav be-torat pira'on achlinhu* — he did not eat it in the capacity of collecting the debt. If consumption is reframed as not constituting repayment, it becomes *avak ribis* from the moment of eating. And since *avak ribis eino yotzei be-dayanin* — *avak ribis* cannot be adjudicated by a court — the entire remedial structure collapses. The upshot is that to evict the lender legitimately, the borrower must first pay back the full loan in cash, *ad she-yiten lo kol shiur ma'ot halvato*. Until that point, the lender's occupation of the field remains legally protected.

This dilemma leads to a further ruling: *avad Rav Ashi uvda be-yetomim ketanim ke-gedolim* — Rav Ashi handled a case involving minor orphans as he would a case involving adults. The context is that when a borrower dies leaving minor heirs, the law tends to be more protective of their financial interests precisely because they cannot themselves consent to waive the *ribis*. The instinct might be to treat the orphans' case as the stricter version, where excess produce is recoverable. But Rav Ashi applied the standard rule — *lo mafkinan minei, velo mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — treating the orphans' case on identical footing with the ordinary adult case, and holding this to be the foundational *ikar hadin*.

## Meshkanta Sura

### Sura Model

The *gemara* continues with a statement of Mar Zutra quoting Rav Papa. In a community where the court does evict the *mashkon* holder, the eviction applies even to date clusters that have already been placed into baskets for transport — *afilu mitamrei de-avudei*. Even if harvest is actively underway when the court intervenes, the cutoff is immediate. There is no backward calculation to determine precisely when each cluster was picked; the court simply evicts at the moment it arrives, and whatever has already been placed into baskets has been acquired.

Shifting to the broader structural question, the discussion addresses the *meshkanta de-Sura* — the Sura-style mortgage document. In Sura, where the community of *talmidei chachamim* was concentrated, they wrote in the *mashkanta* agreement the following formula: *be-mishlam shanya ilein tipuk ara da belo kesef* — when these fixed years have been completed, this land returns without any payment. This is the most stripped-down and unambiguous formulation: a fixed term, no post-facto assessment, no *nechyata*, and the land simply reverts at term. The preceding discussion about *nechyata* and *kituta* led to this point.

*Nechyata* refers to a fixed annual deduction applied against the loan balance — a kind of predetermined rate at which produce consumption credits the debt. Rav Papa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, in a joint ruling, address the broader implications of the two *mashkanta* models — the evictable and the non-evictable. For a *mashkanta* in a place where the court evicts: *ein ba'al chov goveh heimenu* — a creditor of the *mashkon* holder cannot seize that field, since the holder does not truly own it; *ein ha-bechor notel bah pi shnayim* — if the holder dies, his firstborn son does not inherit a double portion from that field; and *shevi'is meshamtatah* — the Sabbatical year cancels the underlying loan. The rationale for the last point is that this variety of *mashkanta* is not a genuine proprietary transfer of the field, and thus the loan retains its character as a loan subject to *shemitat kesafim*.

### Two Mashkanta Models

By contrast, in a place where the court does not evict — where the *mashkon* holder remains on the field until the borrower repays in cash: *ba'al chov goveh heimenu* — other creditors of the holder can seize the field, because the holder effectively owns it; *ha-bechor notel bo pi shnayim* — the firstborn inherits a double portion; *ve-ein shevi'is meshamtatah* — the Sabbatical year does not cancel the loan, because the arrangement functions as a genuine transfer of real property, and such a *mashkanta* falls within the category for which the *mishnah* at the end of *Shevi'is* holds that the loan is not cancelled. The structural distinction between the two models thus has cascading consequences across multiple domains of *halacha* — liens, inheritance, and *shemitat kesafim* — all pivoting on whether the court treats the collateral arrangement as a true proprietary transfer or merely a produce-based repayment mechanism.

## Harvest Ownership

### Basket Harvests

Mar Zutra, citing Rav Papa, addresses one further refinement: in a community where courts do evict the *mashkon* holder, they evict him even from dates already placed into baskets — *afilu mitamrei de-avudei*. The eviction is not deferred until after the harvest season concludes; it takes effect at the moment the court acts. One might think this would cause significant loss to the holder in mid-harvest, but the rule is uncompromising in protecting the borrower's ownership rights at that point.

### Nutkin Doctrine

The *gemara* then qualifies: *ve-i agvinhu be-sisnei kaninhu* — but if the holder already placed the dates into baskets, he has acquired them. This is the *nitkin* principle: acquisition of produce occurs at the point when it has been gathered into a vessel. The precise moment of eviction thus determines what the holder retains. The *gemara* then notes a further wrinkle based on a dispute about the status of the *kila* — the seller's domain or buyer's domain principle. According to the opinion that *kila she-lokeach bi-reshut mocher* — the purchased property remains in the legal domain of the seller — then *koneh lokeach afilu dela agvinhu be-sisnei* — the *mashkon* holder may acquire the dates even without having placed them into baskets, simply by virtue of the field's legal relationship to his ownership at that stage of the process. The *sugya* thus ends at the intersection of the *nitkin* doctrine and the broader question of when produce harvested from a *mashkon* field is treated as legally acquired by the holder.
