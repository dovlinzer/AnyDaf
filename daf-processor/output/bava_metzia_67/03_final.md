# Bava Metzia 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Asmachta Mechanics

### Asmachta Conditional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי אַסְמַכְתָּא, זִימְנִין קָנְיָא וְזִימְנִין לָא קָנְיָא. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּקָא שָׁתֵי שִׁכְרָא – קָנֵי. דְּקָא מְהַפֵּךְ אַזּוּזֵי – לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** With regard to **this *asmakhta*, sometimes** it **effects acquisition and sometimes** it **does not effect acquisition.** How so? If the lender **encountered** the borrower **while** the borrower **was drinking beer,** it **effects acquisition,** as, if the borrower agreed to transfer ownership of his field when he did not appear to be in financial stress, such as when he was enjoying a drink, he assumedly did so with full acceptance of the consequences, and therefore the transaction is valid. By contrast, if the lender encountered the borrower when he **was searching for money** with which to repay the loan, his agreement does **not effect acquisition,** as it is clear the borrower made the statement under duress.

The sugya opens with a statement of Rav Papa on daf 66b, beginning approximately ten lines down: *hai asmachta zimnin kanyia ve-zimnin lo kanyia* — an *asmachta* sometimes effects an acquisition and sometimes does not. By way of background, an *asmachta* refers to a conditional transaction of a futures nature, classically where a borrower stakes a piece of land against a loan, with the lender acquiring the land outright if the loan is not repaid by a fixed date. The fundamental rabbinic concern is that the borrower never fully resolves in his mind to transfer the property, since he fully expects to repay in time, and therefore the conditional sale lacks the definitive mental commitment that a valid *kinyan* requires.

### Bar Drinker Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: דִּלְמָא לְפַכּוֹחֵי פַּחְדֵּיהּ קָא שָׁתֵי, אִי נָמֵי: אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא אַסְמְכֵיהּ אַזּוּזֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא: אִי קָפֵיד בִּדְמֵי – וַדַּאי קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina:** In the case where the lender encountered the borrower while he was drinking beer, **perhaps he was drinking to neutralize his fear,** and he really was under financial pressure. **Or,** perhaps **someone else promised him money,** and therefore he remains in a precarious position even though he can repay the debt, which would again mean that his promise is merely an *asmakhta*. **Rather, Ravina said** that there is a different distinction: **If** the borrower **is particular about** the **money,** i.e., if he is unwilling to sell his property at less than its market value, the lender **has certainly acquired** the field, as the borrower is clearly not in such a precarious financial position, and he is therefore assumed to have willingly stated his agreement to give the field to the lender.

Rav Acha mi-Difti challenges Rav Papa's test by raising two alternative readings of the tavern scenario. First, *dilma lefakhukhei pakhdei ka shati* — perhaps the borrower is drinking precisely to dull his anxiety, as Rashi explains, drowning his sorrows rather than acquiescing to the loss. Second, *ei nami inash achrina asmakhyei a-zuzi* — perhaps another person has already promised to lend him the money. Either possibility undermines the inference that sitting in the tavern signals genuine abandonment of the right of redemption, leaving Rav Papa's behavioral test inconclusive even in the paradigm case.

### Precision Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: דִּלְמָא סָבַר כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּיתְּזִיל אַרְעֵיהּ, אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי קָפֵיד בְּאַרְעָא, וַדַּאי קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina:** Even in that case, **perhaps** the borrower **thinks** that he ought to present himself as particular about the price of his property **in order that the** price **of his** other **lands not be lowered.** If people know that he is in need of money they will pressure to him to lower the price, and therefore he acts as though he is particular about the price, but in reality he did not wish to sell the property at all. **Rather, Rav Pappa said** that there is a different distinction: **If he is particular about** any other **land** he owns and does not want to sell it even at its market rate, he is clearly not suffering from financial problems, and in that case the creditor **certainly acquires** the field.

Ravina proposes an alternative criterion: *ei kappid bidmei, vadai kani* — if the borrower was precise about the monetary amount, the conditional sale definitively takes effect. Rav Acha mi-Difti pushes back here as well, suggesting that the borrower's monetary precision may simply reflect a desire not to let the field depreciate in value — in other words, his precision is about self-interest in the transaction, not a wholehearted commitment to the conditional sale. In response, Rav Papa reformulates: *ei kappid be-ara, vadai kani* — if the borrower is precise and insistent about the land itself, then the *asmachta* takes effect. The underlying conceptual point in both alternatives is that the determination of whether an *asmachta* is valid turns on assessing the seller's subjective mental state — whether his mind was fully settled on the conditional transfer — and that different contextual facts bear on that assessment in different ways.

### Asmachta→Apothiki

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא, אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי הָוְיָא לְמִיגְבֵּא מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa said: Even though the Sages said** that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition,** and therefore even if he stipulated that the lender can collect the debt by seizing land, the lender does not acquire that land, nevertheless, the land **is** considered to be set aside as **designated repayment [*apoteiki*] for** the lender **to collect** repayment **from it** up to the amount of money owed.

Rav Papa then introduces a further principle: *af al gav de-amru rabanan asmachta lo kanyia, apotiki havei le-migba minei* — even though the Sages ruled that an *asmachta* does not effect a sale, the failed *asmachta* can nonetheless function as an *apothiki*. An *apothiki* is the designation of a specific property as the source of repayment for a loan. The lender does not acquire ownership of the field, but the field is earmarked as the asset from which the debt will be collected. Thus, Rav Papa argues, even where the conditional sale is void, the transaction retains legal force as a lien designation.

It is worth pausing here to note the broader taxonomy. *Asmachta*, *mashkon*, and *apothiki* are all distinct forms of collateralized obligation, each with its own *halachic* rules, even though colloquially we might group them together. *Shi'abud* in its general sense is a blanket lien on property, without any particular designation. *Apothiki* is the specific designation of a field as the source of repayment — but as the *gemara* will clarify, there are two gradations even within *apothiki*: the field designated as the primary source, or the field designated as the exclusive source.

### Rav Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ קְנִי לְגוּבְיָינָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: Did** the borrower **say to** the lender: **Acquire** it **for** the sake of debt **collection?** Since the borrower did not specify that transferring ownership of the field was in place of the payment of the debt, his statement is considered an *asmakhta*, and the field is not set aside as designated repayment.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan challenges Rav Papa: *mi ka'amar lei kani l'migvaya minei* — did the borrower ever actually say that the field was being designated for collection purposes? The language of the *asmachta* was a conditional sale, not a declaration that this field was to be the exclusive means of repayment. The attempted *apothiki* reading therefore lacks explicit textual grounding.

### Mar Zutra Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: וְאִי אָמַר קְנִי לְמִיגְבֵּא מִינֵּיהּ – קָנֵי? סוֹף סוֹף אַסְמַכְתָּא הִיא, וְאַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: And if he** had **said to him: Acquire** it **for** the sake of debt **collection,** would the lender then **acquire** it? **Ultimately, it is an *asmakhta*,** and the *halakha* is that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition.**

Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, presses the challenge to Ravina: if the borrower explicitly said "acquire this field so that the loan may be collected from it," that would indeed sound like *apothiki* — but *sof sof Asmachta hi* — in the end, that is still an *asmachta*, since the sale remains conditional on non-repayment. The distinction Rav Papa draws between *asmachta* and *apothiki* thus threatens to collapse.

### Apothiki Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, מַאי הִיא: דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לֹא יְהֵא לְךָ פֵּרָעוֹן אֶלָּא מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **But** in that case, **what is** the **designated repayment spoken** of by **Rav Pappa?** According to Mar Zutra's analysis, it makes no difference whether or not he said it was set aside as designated repayment of the loan. The Gemara explains: Rav Pappa is referring to a case, for example, **where** the borrower **said to** the lender: Not only will I give you the land if I fail to repay the loan, but even if I do pay **you will receive payment only from this** land alone, as I will pay off my debt to you by giving you land from this field. In that case the *asmakhta* is negated, while the promise of repayment from the land remains intact.

The *gemara* therefore clarifies what Rav Papa's *apothiki* case must actually look like: *lo yehei lach pir'on ela mizo* — the borrower declares that the lender shall have no recourse for repayment other than from this particular field. This is not a primary designation with fallback alternatives; it is an exclusive designation. The exclusivity is precisely what distinguishes the *apothiki* from the *asmachta*. In an *asmachta*, the borrower retains the option to repay in cash and thereby redeem the property — which is why his mind never fully settles on the transfer. In an exclusive *apothiki*, there is no alternative, and therefore the nature of the transaction is categorically different.

## Field Designation

### Flood & Fields

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ אַרְעָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ בְּאַחְרָיוּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי טָרְפוּ לַיהּ מִנַּאי מַגְבֵּית לִי מֵעִידֵּי עִידִּית דְּאִית לָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵעִידֵּי עִידִּית לָא מַגְבֵּינָא לָךְ, דְּבָעֵינָא לְמֵיקַם קַמַּאי, אֶלָּא מַגְבֵּינָא לָךְ מֵעִידִּית אַחֲרִיתִ[י] דְּאִית לִי. לְסוֹף טַרְפוּהָ מִינֵּיהּ, אֲתָא בִּדְקָא שְׁקֵיל לְעִידֵּי עִידִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who sold land to another with** a property **guarantee,** meaning that in the event that land is seized by the seller's creditors, the seller will reimburse the buyer his purchase money. The buyer **said to him: If** your creditors **seize** the land **from me,** will **you** allow **me to collect** that which is owed to me **from** the most **superior quality of** the **superior-quality** land **that you have?** The seller **said to him: I will not** allow **you to collect from** the most **superior quality of** my **superior-quality** land, **as I want to keep** those **before me. But I will** allow **you to collect from other superior-quality** land **I have. Ultimately,** creditors **seized** the field **from** the buyer, and in the meantime **a flood came and flooded** the seller's most **superior quality of** his **superior-quality** land.

The *gemara* presents a case involving a field sold with a quality guarantee (*achrayut*). The seller guaranteed that if a creditor with a prior lien seized the field from the buyer, the seller would compensate from his own best land. The buyer asked: may I collect from your *idei idit* — your finest of the finest field? The seller replied that he wished to retain that field for himself, and offered instead to pay from his next-best field, the *idit*. The parties proceeded on that basis. Subsequently, the creditor did indeed seize the guaranteed field, and the buyer came to collect from the seller's *idit* field. Before he could do so, however, a flood washed away the seller's *idei idit* — the superior field that had been excluded from the guarantee.

### Quality Ranking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר רַב פָּפָּא לְמֵימַר: מֵעִידִּית אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָא קָיְימָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ, כִּי אֲמַרִי לָךְ אֲנָא מַגְבֵּינָא לָךְ – דַּהֲוָה עִידֵּי עִידִּית קָיְימָא, הַשְׁתָּה קָיְימָא לֵיהּ עִידִּית בִּמְקוֹם עִידֵּי עִידִּית.
>
> **Translation:** As his remaining superior-quality land was now the best he had, the seller was unwilling to give them to the buyer and sought to repay him with intermediate-quality land, in accordance with the standard *halakha* concerning reimbursement to a buyer. **Rav Pappa thought to say:** Since the seller **said to** the buyer that he could collect **from** his **superior-quality** land, **and** this superior-quality land is **intact,** the seller must give it to him, in accordance with his promise. **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: But let** the seller **say to** the buyer: **When I said to you** that **I** would allow **you to collect** from my superior-quality land, that was **when the** most **superior quality** of my superior-quality land was **intact.** But **now** my **superior-quality** land **stands** for me **in place of the** most **superior quality of** the **superior-quality** land I had before, and I am not obligated to give you the very best of my property.

Rav Papa initially inclined to rule that the seller must still compensate from the *idit* field, since it physically exists and the guarantee was made against that specific field. Rav Acha mi-Difti challenges this position: *ki amri lach ana magvina lach d'hava idei idit kamai* — the reason the seller agreed to offer the *idit* field was precisely because he still had his *idei idit* field as his personal reserve. Now that the *idei idit* has been destroyed by the flood, the *idit* has effectively moved up in rank to fill that role. *Hashta kaima lei idit bi-mkom idei idit* — at this point the *idit* stands in the place of the *idei idit*, and the seller should therefore only be required to provide from the medium-quality field, since that is now what corresponds to the relative tier that was originally guaranteed. The conclusion the *gemara* leans toward is that the designation of field quality is relative rather than absolute. If the top-tier field is lost, the rankings shift downward by one, and the guarantee adjusts accordingly.

## Wine Collateral

### Wine=Currency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב בַּר שְׁבָא הֲוָה מַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא זוּזֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָא פָּרַעְנָא לָךְ לְיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי – גְּבִי מֵהַאי חַמְרָא. סְבַר רַב פָּפָּא לְמֵימַר: כִּי אָמְרִינַן אַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּאַרְעָא, דְּלָאו לְזַבּוֹנֵי קָיְימָא. אֲבָל חַמְרָא כֵּיוָן דִּלְזַבּוֹנֵי קָאֵי – כְּזוּזֵי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav bar Shabba owed money to Rav Kahana.** Rav bar Shabba **said to him: If I do not repay you by such and such a date,** you can **collect** the debt **from this wine. Rav Pappa thought to say: When we said** that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition, this matter** applies only **to land, which does not stand to be sold,** i.e., which is not typically bought and sold on the market, and therefore the promise is invalid, as he did not genuinely intend to give it to him. **But** in the case of **wine, since it does stand to be sold, it is** considered **like money,** and therefore he can claim the debt from wine, as he can claim the debt from any other item with monetary worth.

The *gemara* presents a case: Rav Bar Bar Shva owed Rav Kahana money, and declared that if he did not repay by a certain date, Rav Kahana could take his wine. This is structurally parallel to the earlier cases involving fields and conditional transfers. Rav Papa sought to argue that the laws of *asmachta* do not apply here: *hani mili be-ara, de-lav le-zavuni kaima* — the Sages' ruling that *asmachta lo kanyia* refers specifically to land, which is not normally in a state of market readiness. *Aval chamra keivan de-le-zavuni kai ke-zuzei dami* — wine, however, since it is essentially a commodity ready for sale, is comparable to currency itself. On this view, pledging wine against a loan is no different from pledging cash, and no *asmachta* problem arises.

### Futures Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה: כֹּל דְּאִי – לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: This** is what **we say in the name of Rabba: Any** statement including a condition introduced with the word: **If,** does **not effect acquistion.** Any agreement that does not involve a definitive commitment but does obligate one in the event of a particular outcome has the status of an *asmakhta* and does not effect acquisition, as the one setting the condition did not really intend to fulfill the commitment.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua counters by citing a tradition in the name of Rabbah: *kol de-ee lo kani* — any transaction structured as a conditional future does not effect an acquisition. The *asmachta* rule applies equally to movables, including wine, and the distinction between land and commodities is insufficient to eliminate the *asmachta* concern. This definitively closes the argument Rav Papa had sought to open.

## Asmachta Void

### Land & Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַשְׁתָּא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא, הָדְרָא אַרְעָא וְהָדְרִי פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman said: Now that the Sages have said** that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition,** in a case such as that in the mishna, when one promised another land for the repayment of a debt, if the lender in fact took it, **the land must be returned and** the value of any **produce** the creditor consumed from this land **must** also **be returned.**

Rav Nachman now states: *hashta de-amri rabanan asmachta lo kanyia, hadar ara ve-hadar peiri* — now that the Sages have ruled that an *asmachta* does not effect an acquisition, both the land and all produce harvested during the period of the ostensible acquisition must be returned. The logic is straightforward: if the sale was invalid from the outset, the lender held the field without legal title throughout, and all fruits of the field collected in the interim must be disgorged.

### Mistaken Forgiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן מְחִילָה בְּטָעוּת לָא הָוְיָא מְחִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: Is this **to say that Rav Naḥman maintains that mistaken forgiveness** of payment **is not** valid **forgiveness,** meaning that if one forgoes repayment of a loan of a certain amount of money in error, he can change his mind? In this case, the borrower had thought that the lender had acquired the land, and he therefore allowed him to consume the produce.

From this ruling, the *gemara* infers that Rav Nachman must hold *mechilah be-ta'us lo havei mechilah* — a waiver made under mistaken assumptions does not constitute a valid waiver. The reason this inference is necessary is as follows: the borrower and lender had both agreed to the *asmachta* arrangement, and both presumably understood that the lender would enjoy the field's produce during the loan period. One might argue that the borrower's acquiescence amounts to *mechilah* — a voluntary relinquishment of his rights to that produce. Rav Nachman's insistence on returning the produce demonstrates that he rejects this possibility. Since the borrower agreed under the mistaken belief that the *asmachta* sale was legally effective, his apparent *mechilah* was premised on a legal error, and *mechilah be-ta'us* does not stand.

### Date Futures Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת דֶּקֶל לַחֲבֵירוֹ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם – יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם – אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אַף מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **But it was stated** that *amora'im* disputed this very issue: **If one sells the fruit of a palm tree to another** before the fruit ripens, **Rav Huna says: He can retract** and cancel the sale **until** the fruits **have come into the world,** as the fruit is not yet in existence. But **once they have come into the world,** even if they are still unripe, **he cannot retract,** as once the fruits that are being acquired exist, the sale has gone into effect. **And Rav Naḥman says: He can retract even once they have come into the world,** as one cannot transfer ownership of an entity that has not yet come into the world, and the actual transaction was performed before the fruits existed.

A *baraita* is adduced as a challenge to Rav Nachman's position. The *baraita* discusses the case of one who sells another person the future produce of his date palm: *ad she-lo bau la-olam yachol lachzor bo* — until the dates have actually come into existence, the seller may retract. Rav Nachman is cited as ruling *af misheba'u la-olam yachol lachzor bo* — even after the dates have come into existence, he may retract. This appears to follow from the *asmachta* principle: such a sale of futures is inherently defective, and either party retains the right to withdraw. But if Rav Nachman allows retraction even after the dates exist, then presumably the produce gathered during the period of the agreement cannot all be reclaimed. Does this not contradict his ruling that produce must be returned when *asmachta* fails?

### Consumed Fruits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מוֹדֵינָא דְּאִי שָׁמֵיט וְאָכֵיל – לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ! הָתָם זְבִינֵי, הָכָא הַלְוָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said: I concede that if** the buyer **seized** the fruit **and consumed** it, **we do not take** its value **from him.** The reason is that since the seller initially accepted the transaction, although it involved a legal error, it can be assumed that he decided to waive his rights to the fruit and allowed the other to take it. Consequently, it can be demonstrated that Rav Naḥman holds that mistaken forgiveness is forgiveness. The Gemara rejects this proof: **There,** the discussion involves **a sale,** with regard to which it can be said that the seller waived his rights to the fruit. **Here,** it is referring to **a loan,** and not requiring the lender to reimburse the borrower for the produce consumed is considered a form of interest.

Rav Nachman defends himself: *modina de-ishmit ve-akhil lo mafkinan minei* — he concedes that if the fruits were actually consumed, they cannot be extracted from the holder. The requirement to return produce applies only where the produce remains identifiable and extractable. If it has been entirely consumed, two compounding considerations converge. First, the *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis: even if the waiver was defective, practically speaking there is nothing to recover. Second, and more technically, there is the principle of *davar shelo ba la-olam* — the sale of something not yet in existence. The fruits of the date palm did not exist at the time of the original transaction, and the sale of such futures is generally unenforceable. These two considerations intersect in the *asmachta* context: the question of when the dates are considered to have "come into the world" — *ba la-olam* — determines the point at which retraction is no longer possible, while the *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine governs what must be returned when the transaction is unwound. The *gemara* notes that a further distinction applies: in a case of sale (*zevinei*) as opposed to a loan (*halva'ah*), the *asmachta* concern operates differently. In the loan context, the transaction sits much closer to the domain of *ribis*, and this proximity introduces additional stringency, as Rashi notes: *mekhzei ke-ribit she-mitkhila be-halva'ah ba leh, ve-ke-ribit ketzutzah dami* — it resembles interest that arose out of a loan from the outset, and appears similar to explicit prohibited interest.

## Ona'ah & Ilonit

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, וּבְעַי לְאוֹתֹבֵיהּ אוֹנָאָה, וְאוֹדֵיק, חֲזִיתַן אַיְילוֹנִית. וַהֲרֵי אוֹנָאָה דִּמְחִילָה בְּטָעוּת הִיא, וְלָא הָוְיָא מְחִילָה! וְאוֹדֵיק, חֲזִיתַן אַיְילוֹנִית: הֲרֵי אַיְילוֹנִית דִּמְחִילָה בְּטָעוּת הִיא, וְהָוְיָא מְחִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I was sitting before Rav Naḥman** when he said that mistaken forgiveness is valid, **and I wanted to raise an objection to him** from the *halakha* of **exploitation, and he observed** me, anticipated my objection, **and showed me** that he was correct based on the *halakha* of **a sexually underdeveloped woman** who is incapable of bearing children **[*ailonit*].** The Gemara explains: Rava wanted to ask: **But** there is the case of **exploitation,** where the price paid in a sale was in excess of the market value (see 50b), **which is** a case of **mistaken forgiveness** at the time of the sale, **and** yet **it is not** considered **forgiveness.** The seller must return the excess money paid. **And he observed** me and **showed me** the *halakha* of **an *ailonit*. Doesn't** the case of **an *ailonit*** involve **mistaken forgiveness, and** yet **it is** valid **forgiveness?**

The *gemara* relates that Rava was once sitting before Rav Nachman and wished to challenge him from the laws of *ona'ah*, though he was forestalled when Rav Nachman perceived the question and pre-empted with the answer of *ilonit*. The reconstructed challenge runs as follows: every case of *ona'ah* is by definition a case of *mechilah be-ta'us*. A buyer overcharged in a transaction presumably does not complain if the overcharge is less than a sixth (*shtus*) of the purchase price — and yet that acquiescence operates precisely because the buyer believes he is paying fair market value. The buyer is *mochel* under mistaken assumptions about price. If so, one might argue that *mechilah be-ta'us* here *is* in fact valid *mechilah* — which would contradict Rav Nachman's principle. This presents a potential refutation (*tiyuvta*) of Rav Nachman's position.

### Ilonit Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַמְמָאֶנֶת וְהַשְּׁנִיָּיה וְהָאַיְילוֹנִית, אֵין לָהֶן לֹא כְּתוּבָּה וְלֹא פֵּירוֹת וְלֹא מְזוֹנוֹת וְלֹא בְּלָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **As we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 100b): In the case of a minor girl married off by her mother or brother **who refuses** to continue living with her husband, and in the case of a woman who is **a secondary** forbidden **relative, and** in the case of **an *ailonit*,** each of these women **is not entitled to** payment of **a marriage contract, and** they are **not** entitled to remuneration for the **produce** that the husband consumed from her property while they were together, **and** they are **not** entitled to **sustenance, and** they are **not** entitled to their **worn clothes** that were brought into the marriage as part of their dowry and became worn out during the marriage. The *ailonit* granted the rights to the profits the husband earned from her property while under the mistaken premise that she was married. Yet after the marriage was determined to have been contracted in error, the husband does not have to return these profits.

Rav Nachman is understood to have anticipated the *ona'ah* challenge and gestured toward the case of *ilonit* as his response. The *mishnah* rules that an *ilonit* — a woman who does not develop normal secondary sexual characteristics and is typically unable to bear children — forfeits her *ketubbah*, *peiros*, *mezonos*, and *bla'os* in the event of divorce. The situation is one of *mekach ta'us*, a transaction entered under mistaken terms: the husband married on the presumption of normal circumstances, and the marriage is effectively re-evaluated once the wife's condition becomes known. Nonetheless, despite the apparent *mechilah be-ta'us* on the wife's part — since she presumably would have assented to reduced financial protections in order to secure marriage (*denicha lah de-tipuk aleih shma de-ishus*, as Rashi explains, she benefits from acquiring the social standing of a married woman) — the Sages structured the ruling to facilitate her marriage. The *ilonit* exception thus reflects a broader principle: public policy considerations can override the ordinary logic of *mechilah be-ta'us*, allowing the law to treat an apparent waiver as effective even where a strict *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis might not support it.

### Ona'ah Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא: לָא אוֹנָאָה הָוְיָא תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ, וְלָא אַיְילוֹנִית מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ. לָא אוֹנָאָה תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ: דְּלָא יָדַע דְּאִיתֵיהּ אוֹנָאָה דְּמָחֵיל גַּבֵּיהּ. וְלָא אַיְילוֹנִית מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, דְּנִיחָא לַהּ (דְּתִיפּוֹק) [דְּנִיפּוֹק] עֲלַהּ שְׁמָא דְאִישׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But** that is **not so;** the *halakha* of **exploitation is not a refutation of** Rav Naḥman's opinion, **nor** does the case of **a sexually underdeveloped woman support his** opinion, as there are differences between the cases. **Exploitation is not a refutation of his** opinion, **as** the buyer **does not know that there is exploitation** at the time of the sale **that** would enable him to **forgive him** the amount he was overcharged, and therefore there is no forgiveness in this case at all. **Nor** does the case of **a sexually underdeveloped woman support his** opinion, **as it is amenable to her** for her husband to receive the profits in order **that she receive the name of a married** woman. She wants to be known as a woman who was once married, and therefore she willingly relinquishes her rights to the profits from her property during her marriage even if it will be found to have been contracted in error. There is no mistaken forgiveness in that case.

A later *amora* steps in to observe that neither the *ona'ah* challenge nor the *ilonit* answer is actually persuasive. *Lo ona'ah tyuvta velo ilonit mesay'a.* The *ona'ah* case is not a valid challenge because the overcharged buyer was never engaged in a conscious *mechilah* at all. *De-lo yadei de-isye ona'ah de-machil gabeih* — the buyer did not know he was being overcharged and therefore had nothing to waive. He thought he was paying market price. This is categorically different from the *asmachta* case, where both parties entered the arrangement with full awareness that a conditional transfer was being made. In *asmachta*, the borrower actively agreed to a deviant transaction; the only question is whether his *mechilah* of those rights was vitiated by his mistaken legal assumption. In *ona'ah*, there was no conscious *mechilah* of any kind, because the discrepancy in price was entirely unknown to the buyer. The two cases are therefore not analogous, and *ona'ah* fails to serve as a counterexample to Rav Nachman's *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine.

## Redemption Case

### Repurchase Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא: זִיל זְבֵין לִי אַרְעָא מִקָּרִיבַיי. אֲזַל זְבַן לַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָווּ לִי זוּזֵי מַהְדְּרַתְּ לַהּ נִיהֲלִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ וְנַוְולָא אַחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who said to a certain man: Go purchase** land **for me from my relatives. He went** and **purchased** land **for her.** The relative who sold her the property **said to** the man acting as her agent: **If I will have money** in the future, **will you give** the field **back to me?** The agent **said to him: You and she [*venavla*] are relatives,** and I assume that you will be able to come to an arrangement between the two of you.

The *gemara* presents a case: a woman asked an agent to purchase a field on her behalf from one of her relatives. The agent did so. Afterward, the seller asked the agent: *ei havu li zuzei, mahadrat lah nihali?* — if I later obtain money, will she sell it back to me? The agent responded with the expression *at ve-navla achai* — roughly, "you and she are like siblings," implying of course she will accommodate you. There is a dispute in the commentaries over whether *navla* is the woman's name or simply an Aramaic term for sibling; either way, the agent is guaranteeing the seller an informal right of repurchase.

### Buyer's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: כֹּל ״אַתְּ וְנַוְולָא אַחֵי״ (אָמַר) סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ, וְלָא גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the halakhic significance of this response. **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** In the case of **any** expression such as: **You and she are relatives,** that the agent **says,** the seller **relies** on the assumption that he will be able to come to an agreement with his relative, **and** therefore **he does not conclusively** resolve to **enable** the other to **acquire** the field.

The *gemara* notes that because of this assurance, *samcha da'tei ve-lo gamar u-makni ara* — the seller's mind was put at ease and he never fully resolved to transfer the field irrevocably. Does this mean the original sale was incomplete? And if the buyer later conveys the field back to him, must she also return the produce (*hadar peiri*) she collected in the interim? These are the animating questions. If the seller's mind was never fully settled on the sale, one might treat the arrangement as an *asmachta*-type transaction, triggering the *mechilah be-ta'us* analysis — and by Rav Nachman's principle, produce would need to be returned along with the land.

### Abaye's Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְעָא הָדְרָה, פֵּירֵי מַאי? רִבִּית קְצוּצָה הָווּ וְיוֹצְאִין בְּדַיָּינִין, אוֹ דִילְמָא כִּי אֲבַק רִבִּית הָווּ וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: In that case, **the land** itself **must be returned** to its owner, but **what** is the *halakha* with regard to the **produce** consumed by the buyer in the interim? Is **it** deemed **fixed interest,** prohibited by Torah law, **and can** it **be removed** from the buyer **by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges? Or perhaps** it is considered **like a hint of interest,** prohibited by rabbinic law, **and** therefore it **cannot be removed** from the buyer by means of legal proceedings adjudicated by judges?

Abaye presses several questions to Raba: how do we categorize the nature of this arrangement? Is the underlying agreement an *asmachta*? Does it raise concerns of *ribis ketzutzah* — explicit prohibited interest that can be remedied in court — or only *avak ribis* — the more attenuated "dust of interest" that is halachically problematic but cannot be extracted by a *beit din*? The *nafka mina* between these two categories is precisely whether a court case lies. Rabbah bar Rav Huna offers his assessment: *mistabra ke-avak ribis havei, ve-ein yotzin be-dayanim* — it appears that this arrangement is *avak ribis*, not actionable in court. Rava is then cited in agreement with that framing.

### Ravina's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כִּי אֲבַק רִבִּית הָווּ, וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין בְּדַיָּינִין. וְכֵן אָמַר רָבָא: כִּי אֲבַק רִבִּית הָווּ, וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין בְּדַיָּינִין. אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי: עֲבַד רָבִינָא עוֹבָדָא, וְחַשֵּׁיב וְאַפֵּיק פֵּירֵי, דְּלָא כְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: It stands to reason** that **it is like a hint of interest, and** it **cannot be removed** from the buyer **by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges. And Rava similarly said: It is like a hint of interest, and** it **cannot be removed** from the buyer **by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges. Rav Pappi said: Ravina performed an action** with regard to a case like this, **and he calculated and removed** from the lender the value of the **produce** he had consumed in the interim. This decision **was not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba bar Rav Huna.**

Rav Papi reports that Ravina actually adjudicated this type of case in practice: *ve-chashiv ve-afik piri* — he calculated the value of the produce and required it to be returned. This was contrary to the ruling of Rabbah bar Rav Huna, who had held that *avak ribis* is not recoverable in court. Ravina's practice thus treated the produce as extractable, presumably by applying Rav Nachman's *mechilah be-ta'us* doctrine to require full restoration of the fruits.

## Mashkon Overview

### Mashkanta Sura

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָא מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְסַלְּקִי, אֲכַל שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי – מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. אֲכַל טְפֵי – לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, וְלָא מְחַשְּׁבִינַן מִשְּׁטָרָא לִשְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Rav Yosef, said in the name of Rava:** With regard to **this** following type of **mortgage,** in which the lender holds part of the borrower's land and may consume its produce, the *halakha* depends on the local custom. **In a place where** the custom is that the borrower can repay the loan at any time and the court **removes** the lender from the land once the loan is repaid, then once the lender **consumed a measure** of produce equivalent to the amount of **money** that he lent, **we remove him** from the land at that point, as the produce consumed is considered repayment of the loan. If the lender **consumed** a measure of produce worth **more** than the value of the loan, **we do not remove** the value of the excess produce **from him,** as that which he consumed is already gone. **And** similarly, **we do not calculate** any extra produce as payment **from one document to another.** If the same borrower owed the lender an additional sum from another debt recorded in a different document, we do not consider the additional produce he consumed as part of the payment for the second document; rather, each loan is treated on its own terms.

Mar son of Rav Yosef, citing Rava, introduces a critical practical distinction based on local custom. In a place where the practice is *de-misalkei* — where the court evicts the lender from the *mashkon* field once sufficient produce has been consumed to equal the value of the loan — *achal shiur zuzei, misalkinan lei*: once the lender has eaten produce worth the full amount of the loan, the court removes him. This is the *mashkanta* in its characteristic form in those communities: the lender's right to the produce is self-liquidating, with the field returning to the borrower once the debt has been discharged through produce.

### Eviction Payment

The immediate question is what happens if the lender has eaten exactly the value of the loan. The ruling is that the court may evict him at that point — the debt is discharged, and the collateral arrangement has run its course. The borrower has effectively repaid through the produce of the field, and no further cash payment is required to reclaim the land.

### Overeating Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִדְיַתְמֵי אֲכַל שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי, מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. אֲכַל טְפֵי – מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, וּמְחַשְּׁבִינַן מִשְּׁטָרָא לִשְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if the field belonged **to orphans,** once the lender **consumed a measure** of produce equivalent to the amount of **money** he was owed, **we remove him** from the field. And if **he consumed** a measure of produce worth **more** than the value of the loan, **we take** the additional amount **from him; and we** do **calculate** extra produce as payment **from one document to another.** The reason is that the owner of the field generally forgives payment for the extra produce the lender consumed, but minor orphans are too young to forgive a debt, and the case is therefore judged according to the letter of the law.

However, *achal tefei lo mafkinan minei* — if the lender consumed more produce than the value of the loan, the court cannot extract the excess from him. Furthermore, *lo mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — the excess cannot be applied as a credit toward a different outstanding loan between the same parties. The reason is that the excess consumption takes on the character of *ribis*, and since *avak ribis* cannot be extracted by a court, there is no judicial mechanism for recovery. Rashi makes the point explicit: this is a case in a loan context where the *avak ribis* concern is heightened because the transaction arose out of a loan from the outset.

In a different jurisdiction — understood to be the practice of *ditmei* — the rule operates more strictly in the borrower's favor: *achal tefei mafkinan minei* — if the lender consumed more than the loan value, the court does extract the excess. And *mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — the excess can be applied across loans. The tighter enforcement reflects an understanding that the agreement was truly precise, limiting the lender's benefit to the exact loan amount.

### Rav Ashi Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אֲכַל טְפֵי – לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ. אֲכַל שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי נָמֵי – לָא מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ בְּלָא זוּזֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא: סַלּוֹקֵי בְּלָא זוּזֵי – אַפּוֹקֵי מִינֵּיהּ הוּא. הָוֵי אֲבַק רִבִּית, וַאֲבַק רִבִּית אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Now that you have said** that if **he consumed more** than the value of the loan **we do not take** it **from him,** as it is considered merely a hint of interest, it follows that if **he consumed a measure** of produce equivalent to the amount of the **money** owed him, **we also do not remove him** from the land **without** the borrower's paying him **money. What is the reason** for this? **To remove him** from the land **without** the borrower's paying him **money is like taking** money **from him,** and **this** consumption of the produce **is** only **a hint of interest,** and the *halakha* is that **a hint of interest cannot be removed by** legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges.**

Rav Ashi derives a striking consequence from the *first* model — the community practice where excess cannot be extracted. He argues: *hashta de-amrat achal tefei lo mafkinan minei, achal shiur zuzei nami lo mesalkinan lei belo zuzei* — if we accept that excess consumption cannot be extracted, then by parity of reasoning, even when the lender has consumed exactly the loan value, we cannot evict him without requiring the borrower to pay cash. The argument is subtle. The act of attempting to evict the lender without any payment — *salukei belo zuzei* — would retroactively recharacterize the lender's consumption of the produce as something other than repayment of the loan: *lav be-torat pira'on achlinhu* — he did not eat it in the capacity of collecting the debt. If consumption is reframed as not constituting repayment, it becomes *avak ribis* from the moment of eating. And since *avak ribis eino yotzei be-dayanin* — *avak ribis* cannot be adjudicated by a court — the entire remedial structure collapses. The upshot is that to evict the lender legitimately, the borrower must first pay back the full loan in cash, *ad she-yiten lo kol shiur ma'ot halvato*. Until that point, the lender's occupation of the field remains legally protected.

### Minor Orphans Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד רַב אָשֵׁי עוֹבָדָא בִּיתוֹמִים קְטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Ashi performed an action** in a case of this kind even **involving minor orphans,** **as** though they were **adults.** Even if the lender consumed produce equivalent to the amount of the debt, Rav Ashi would not collect the value of the produce from him, as this is merely a hint of interest and therefore it cannot be claimed in court.

This dilemma leads to a further ruling: *avad Rav Ashi uvda be-yetomim ketanim ke-gedolim* — Rav Ashi handled a case involving minor orphans as he would a case involving adults. The context is that when a borrower dies leaving minor heirs, the law tends to be more protective of their financial interests precisely because they cannot themselves consent to waive the *ribis*. The instinct might be to treat the orphans' case as the stricter version, where excess produce is recoverable. But Rav Ashi applied the standard rule — *lo mafkinan minei, velo mechashvinan mish'tar lish'tar* — treating the orphans' case on identical footing with the ordinary adult case, and holding this to be the foundational *ikar hadin*.

## Meshkanta Sura

### Sura Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הַאי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא, בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְסַלְּקִי – לָא נֵיכוֹל אֶלָּא בְּנַכְיְיתָא. וְצוּרְבָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן – אֲפִילּוּ בְּנַכְיְיתָא לָא נֵיכוֹל. אֶלָּא בְּמַאי נֵיכוֹל? בְּקִיצּוּתָא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קִיצּוּתָא שַׁרְיָא, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קִיצּוּתָא אֲסִירָא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִתְּמַר: קִיצּוּתָא, פְּלִיגָא בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא. חַד אָמַר: קִיצּוּתָא שַׁרְיָא, וְחַד אָמַר: קִיצּוּתָא אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava, son of Rav Yosef, says in the name of Rava:** With regard to **this** following type of **mortgage,** in which the lender holds part of the borrower's land and may consume its produce, the *halakha* depends on the local custom. **In a place where** the custom is that the borrower can repay the loan at any time and the court **removes** the lender from the land, the lender **may consume** the produce of the land **only with a deduction** in the amount of the loan granted to the borrower, equivalent in value to that of the produce consumed by the lender. **And a Torah scholar [*tzurva miderabbanan*],** who must be especially careful with regard to his conduct, **may not consume** the produce **even with a deduction** in the amount of the loan. The Gemara poses a question: **But** if so, **in what** manner **may he consume the** produce? The Gemara answers: **By a fixed** payment, the details of which the Gemara will soon explain. The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **a fixed** payment **is permitted, but according to the one who says** that **a fixed** payment **is forbidden, what is there to say? As it was stated: With regard to a fixed** payment, **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagreed: One said** that **a fixed** payment **is permitted, and one said a fixed** payment **is forbidden.**

Mar Zutra, citing Rav Papa, addresses one further refinement: in a community where courts do evict the *mashkon* holder, they evict him even from dates already placed into baskets — *afilu mitamrei de-avudei*. The eviction is not deferred until after the harvest season concludes; it takes effect at the moment the court acts. One might think this would cause significant loss to the holder in mid-harvest, but the rule is uncompromising in protecting the borrower's ownership rights at that point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי קִיצּוּתָא? דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: עַד חֲמֵשׁ שְׁנִין אָכֵילְנָא לַהּ בְּלָא נַכְיְיתָא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ שָׁיֵימְנָא לָךְ כּוּלְּהוּ פֵּירֵי. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כֹּל בְּלָא נַכְיְיתָא – אָסוּר, אֲבָל הֵיכִי דָּמֵי קִיצּוּתָא – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: עַד חֲמֵשׁ שְׁנִין אָכֵילְנָא בְּנַכְיְיתָא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ שָׁיֵימְנָא לָךְ כּוּלְּהוּ פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this **fixed** payment? The Gemara explains: One case of a fixed payment is **where** the lender **says to** the borrower: For a period of **up to five years, I will consume** the produce of the field **without a deduction** in the amount of the loan; **from that** point **forward I will appraise for you** the value of **all** the **produce** I consume and subtract this sum from the debt. **There are** those **who say** a different version of this discussion: **Any** consumption of produce by the lender **without a deduction** in the amount of the loan is **prohibited** as interest. **And what are the circumstances** of a permitted **fixed** payment? **When** the lender **says to** the borrower: For a period of **up to five years I will consume** the produce **with** a fixed **deduction** in the amount of the loan; **from this** point **forward I will appraise for you** the value of **all** the **produce** I consume and deduct the sum from the loan.

### Meshkanta Sura Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָסַר בְּקַמַּיְיתָא שָׁרֵי בְּבָתְרַיְיתָא, מַאן דְּאָסַר בְּבָתְרַיְיתָא, הֵיכִי שְׁרֵי לְמֵיכַל? שְׁרֵי כִּי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא דְסוּרָא, דְּכָתְבִי בַּהּ הָכִי: בְּמִשְׁלָם שְׁנַיָּא אִילֵּין תִּיפּוֹק אַרְעָא דָּא בְּלָא כְּסַף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The one who prohibits** the practice according **to the first** version **permits** the practice described **in the second.** The Gemara asks: But according to **the one who** also **prohibits** the practice described in **the second** version, **how is it permitted to consume** the produce of a mortgaged field? The Gemara replies: **It is permitted** in a case **like** that of **a mortgage** according to the custom in **Sura,** a city in Babylonia, **in which this is written** in the loan document: **Upon the completion of these years,** during which the lender may consume the produce of the field, **this land shall leave** his possession **without money** and return to the owner, as the entire amount of the loan will have been repaid by means of the consumption of the produce.

### Mashkanta Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הַאי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא, בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְסַלְּקִי – אֵין בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנָּה, וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּהּ פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וּשְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתָּהּ. וּבְאַתְרָא דְּלָא מְסַלְּקִי – בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנָּה וּבְכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּהּ פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, both say:** With regard to **this** type of **mortgage,** the *halakha* depends on the local custom. **In a place where** the custom is that the borrower can repay the loan at any time and the court **removes** the lender from the land, the land is not considered his property at all, and therefore another **creditor cannot collect** a debt owed by the lender **by** repossessing **it; and** if the lender dies, his **firstborn** son **does not receive a double portion of it** as part of his inheritance, as it did not belong to his father; **and the Sabbatical** Year **cancels** a debt of this kind, because it is not viewed as having already been collected. **But in a place where they do not remove** him from the land before the appointed time even if the debt is paid, then the land has been temporarily transferred to him, and therefore **a creditor can collect** a debt owed by the lender **by** repossessing **it, and** his **firstborn receives a double portion of it, and the Sabbatical** Year **does not cancel** the debt.

By contrast, in a place where the court does not evict — where the *mashkon* holder remains on the field until the borrower repays in cash: *ba'al chov goveh heimenu* — other creditors of the holder can seize the field, because the holder effectively owns it; *ha-bechor notel bo pi shnayim* — the firstborn inherits a double portion; *ve-ein shevi'is meshamtatah* — the Sabbatical year does not cancel the loan, because the arrangement functions as a genuine transfer of real property, and such a *mashkanta* falls within the category for which the *mishnah* at the end of *Shevi'is* holds that the loan is not cancelled. The structural distinction between the two models thus has cascading consequences across multiple domains of *halacha* — liens, inheritance, and *shemitat kesafim* — all pivoting on whether the court treats the collateral arrangement as a true proprietary transfer or merely a produce-based repayment mechanism.

## Harvest Ownership

### Basket Harvests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא, בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְסַלְּקִי – מְסַלְּקִי לֵיהּ וַאֲפִילּוּ מִתַּמְרֵי דְּאַבּוּדְיָא. וְאִי אַגְבְּהִנְהוּ בְּסִיסָנֵי, קְנָנְהוּ. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל לוֹקֵחַ בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ – אֲפִילּוּ דְּלָא אַגְבְּהִנְהוּ בְּסִיסָנֵי, קְנָנְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Mar Zutra said in the name of Rav Pappa:** With regard to **this** type of **mortgage,** the *halakha* depends on the local custom. **In a place where** the custom is that the borrower can repay the loan at any time and the court **removes** the lender from the land, **they remove him even from dates** he has already harvested and spread out on **the mats. But if he has lifted them** and placed them **in the baskets [*besisanei*], he has** thereby **acquired them,** and they are his. **And according to the one who says** that when an act of acquisition is performed by means of placing items in the **buyer's vessels** that are located **in the seller's domain the buyer has acquired** the items, **even if he did not lift them** while they were **in the baskets, he acquired them** when they were placed on the mats.

Mar Zutra, citing Rav Papa, addresses one further refinement: in a community where courts do evict the *mashkon* holder, they evict him even from dates already placed into baskets — *afilu mitamrei de-avudei*. The eviction is not deferred until after the harvest season concludes; it takes effect at the moment the court acts. One might think this would cause significant loss to the holder in mid-harvest, but the rule is uncompromising in protecting the borrower's ownership rights at that point.

### Nutkin Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְסַלְּקִי וְאָמַר ״לָא מִסְתַּלַּקְנָא״ – הָא קָאָמַר דְּלָא מִסְתַּלַּקְנָא. אֶלָּא בְּאַתְרָא דְּלָא מְסַלְּקִי, וְאָמַר ״מִסְתַּלַּקְנָא״ – מַאי: צָרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses other *halakhot* of mortgages. It is **obvious** that if the loan was given **in a place where they** generally **remove** the lender from the field upon the repayment of the debt, **but** upon lending the money, the lender **said: I will not be removed,** his stipulation is respected, **as he said that** he **will not be removed,** and he gave him the money only subject to this condition. **But** if the loan took place **in a place where they** generally **do not remove** him, **and he said: I will be removed** when the money is returned, **what** is the *halakha*? Is it **necessary to perform** a formal act of **acquisition with him** to formalize this commitment, **or** is it **not** necessary, as his statement alone is binding?

The *gemara* then qualifies: *ve-i agvinhu be-sisnei kaninhu* — but if the holder already placed the dates into baskets, he has acquired them. This is the *nitkin* principle: acquisition of produce occurs at the point when it has been gathered into a vessel. The precise moment of eviction thus determines what the holder retains. The *gemara* then notes a further wrinkle based on a dispute about the status of the *kila* — the seller's domain or buyer's domain principle. According to the opinion that *kila she-lokeach bi-reshut mocher* — the purchased property remains in the legal domain of the seller — then *koneh lokeach afilu dela agvinhu be-sisnei* — the *mashkon* holder may acquire the dates even without having placed them into baskets, simply by virtue of the field's legal relationship to his ownership at that stage of the process. The *sugya* thus ends at the intersection of the *nitkin* doctrine and the broader question of when produce harvested from a *mashkon* field is treated as legally acquired by the holder.
