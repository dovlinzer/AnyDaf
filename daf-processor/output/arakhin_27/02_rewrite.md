# Arakhin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Son Purchases Father's F…

### Scenario Setup

The mishnah presents the following scenario: *halokeach sadeh me-aviv u-meis aviv* — someone purchased a field from his father and then his father died, after which he sanctified the field. The question is how to classify that field's status for purposes of redemption. Is it a *sdei miknah*, a purchased field, because it was originally acquired through purchase? Or is it a *sdei achuza*, an inherited field, because had the son never purchased it, he would have inherited it regardless? The mishnah rules *harei hi k'sdei achuza* — it is treated as an inherited field — and the son therefore enjoys the full rights of an owner to redeem it and retain it when the *yovel* arrives.

Before turning to the more complex case in the mishnah, it is worth noting a fascinating *Rambam* and *Ra'avad* bearing on the earlier portion of the chapter. The mishnah had established that if the original owner did not personally redeem his field from *hekdesh* but instead bought it from a third party who had redeemed it, he loses the field when *yovel* arrives, since he was not the first to redeem it from the Temple treasury. The *Rambam*, however, rules the opposite: if someone else redeemed the field and the original owner subsequently purchased it from that person, the owner retains it when *yovel* comes. The *Ra'avad* objects sharply — this contradicts an explicit mishnah. He then adds, however, that upon consulting various manuscripts, he actually found a version of the mishnah that reads as the *Rambam* does. This exchange illustrates how much of this area of *halacha* remains underdeveloped in the *gemara*, given its practical irrelevance in later generations.

### Father Dies After Sancti…

The mishnah then turns to a harder case: *hikdisha ve-achar kach meis aviv* — he purchased the field, sanctified it, and only afterward did his father die. At the moment of sanctification, the son's relationship to the field was that of a purchaser alone; his father was still alive and the son had not yet inherited anything. Later, when *yovel* arrives, the field would naturally fall into the category of an inherited field since his father has since died. The question is: which moment determines the field's classification — the moment of sanctification or the moment of *yovel*?

### Rabbi Meir's Opinion

*Rabbi Meir* rules *harei hi k'sdei miknah* — it is treated as a purchased field. At the moment the son sanctified it, he had not yet inherited it, and therefore the determinative classification is that of a purchased field.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Shimon

*Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* rule *harei hi k'sdei achuza* — it is treated as an inherited field. They derive this from the verse: *im misdei miknato asher lo misdei achuzato* — "if from a purchased field that is not from his inherited field." The phrase is apparently redundant: of course a purchased field is not an inherited field. The Torah must therefore be pointing to a case where a field could be both purchased *and* inherited. The exclusion — *sdei she-einah re'uya lihyot sdei achuza*, a purchased field that is not in line to become inherited — carves out the scenario before us. Since this son was in line to inherit the field, it is treated as inherited.

A natural question arises: would *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* take this reasoning to its logical extreme and rule that even if the *yovel* arrived while the father was still alive, the field would revert to the son as a *sdei achuza*? That cannot be correct. If the son purchased the field from his father, sanctified it, and then *yovel* hit while the father was still living, logic demands the field revert to the father — it is his ancestral portion. To rule otherwise would allow a son to purchase a field from his father, sanctify it, and then reclaim it as his own *sdei achuza* even though he only ever held a lease-like interest for the remaining years until *yovel*. The ruling of *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* must therefore be limited to cases where the father has in fact died before *yovel* arrives. The verse's exclusion applies only to that case: the son was in line to inherit and has now actually inherited; we therefore treat the field as *sdei achuza*.

It is also worth considering the practical difference between the two opinions. If the father has died by the time *yovel* arrives, and the field is classified as a *sdei miknah* per *Rabbi Meir*, then it reverts to the original owner — the father — and passes to all of the father's heirs. If the son has brothers, the field will be divided among them all. By contrast, according to *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon*, since the son is treated as the field's owner by virtue of *sdei achuza*, he retains it entirely at *yovel*. This underscores the real *chiddush* of their position: it is not necessary that the son would have inherited the entire field himself. Even if he was merely one of several heirs, his status as an heir-in-line is sufficient to designate him as the owner for purposes of *sdei achuza*.

### Kinyan Peiros Debate

The *gemara* proposes that the underlying dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* may reflect the broader legal debate over *kinyan peiros k'kinyan haguf*: whether ownership of produce alone is legally equivalent to full ownership of the corpus. The relevance here is as follows. When the son purchases the field from his father in a period when *yovel* is operative, the purchase is effectively a lease for the remaining years until *yovel* — the son does not acquire outright title, only the right to work the land and benefit from its produce. This is *kinyan peiros*. Now, is that functionally equivalent to owning the land itself?

If *Rabbi Meir* holds *kinyan peiros k'kinyan haguf dami* — that the produce-lease is treated as full ownership — then even before the father dies, the son already has a status equivalent to full title. His first relationship to the land was therefore as a full owner, and when he sanctifies it, you might classify it as a *sdei miknah* based on that full-title-equivalent acquisition. A verse is then needed even for the simpler case — father dies, then son sanctifies — because without the verse one might have said that the prior purchase-as-title precludes *sdei achuza* status.

*Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon*, by contrast, would hold *kinyan peiros lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — the lease-purchase is not equivalent to full title. The son never had real title until his father died; at that point he first acquires genuine ownership, and any subsequent sanctification is obviously that of an inherited field. No verse is needed for the simple case. The verse's work is reserved for the harder case: where the son sanctified the field while still holding only his lease — while the father still lived — and the father died afterward. Only in that case does one need a scriptural basis to say we treat it as *sdei achuza*.

### Hermeneutic Analysis

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* rejects this framework. In truth, he says, *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* may agree that *kinyan peiros k'kinyan haguf dami* in other contexts. The difference here is purely hermeneutic. The verse could have been written simply: *im mi-sdei miknato asher lo sdei achuza* — "if from a purchased field that is not an inherited field." That formulation alone would have excluded the case of father-dies-then-sanctifies, since in that scenario the field is also an inherited field. What is the additional mem in the phrase *mi-sdei achuzato* — "from his inherited field" — teaching? It is teaching *sdei she-einah re'uya lihyot sdei achuza* — not merely a field that is not currently inherited, but a field that is not even in line to become inherited. The additional mem pushes us to the further case: purchased, then sanctified, then father dies. *Maidei mi-sdei achuzato* — what is the purpose of the word *mi-*? To exclude a field that, though purchased at the time of sanctification, was in line to become an inherited field. And that is precisely this case.

The *gemara* concludes, then, that the debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* is driven by hermeneutics — by which case each sage requires the verse to address — rather than by a direct consequence of the *kinyan peiros* debate.

The mishnah closes this section with a statement about purchased fields generally: *sdei miknah einah yotzah la-kohanim ba-Yovel* — a sanctified purchased field does not go out to the *kohanim* at *yovel*, but returns to its original owner. The reason given is *she-ein adam makdish davar she-eino shelo* — a person cannot sanctify something that is not fully his. Since a purchase in the *yovel* era is really only a lease for the years remaining until *yovel*, the buyer never owned the land outright. He cannot therefore cause it to leave the possession of the original owner. At *yovel*, it reverts.

The mishnah then states: *ha-kohanim ve-ha-Levi'im makdishim le-olam ve-go'alim le-olam* — the *kohanim* and *Levi'im* may sanctify and redeem at all times, both before and after *yovel*. This is derived from the verse in *Behar* concerning the cities of the *Levi'im*: *ge'ulat olam tihyeh la-Levi'im* — permanent redemption rights belong to the *Levi'im*. The *gemara* will explore what this means in the context of fields. The *gemara* notes: *bishlama go'alin itztarich* — the ruling that they may redeem is clearly necessary, *le-afukei mi-Yisrael de-lo parkei*, to exclude an ordinary Israelite who does not have that ongoing redemption right. The precise contours of what *go'alin le'olam* entails — whether it refers to redeeming from *hekdesh* directly, or whether it also allows the *kohen* or *Levi* to force a repurchase from a third-party buyer, and whether the *kohen* who sanctified retains priority over other *kohanim* — are left as open questions by the *gemara*.

## Redemption Without Jubil…

### No Jubilee Effects

When *yovel* is not operative, the fixed-rate valuation chart of the Torah — fifty *shekel* for a given acreage for the full cycle — no longer applies, since that rate is calibrated to the number of years remaining until *yovel*. With no *yovel*, the framework collapses and market value takes its place. The question then becomes how to determine that market value.

When *yovel* is operative, two powerful forces shape the redemption of a sanctified field: the fixed rate locks in the price, and *yovel* itself functions as a deadline — if the owner does not redeem by *yovel*, the field transfers to the *kohanim*. When *yovel* is not operative, neither force applies. The owner retains an indefinite right to redeem the field as long as it remains in *hekdesh*'s possession. If *hekdesh* sells it to a third party, the owner must negotiate with that buyer — there is no *yovel* to force a reversion. This is, as the *gemara* puts it, essentially how we treat property today: if one sanctifies one's field, one may attempt to buy it back before anyone else does, and if *hekdesh* has already sold it to another, one must deal with that buyer directly. The one remaining distinctively Torah-law feature is that if the owner wishes to repurchase from *hekdesh*, he must add a *chomesh* — a fifth. That obligation persists even when *yovel* is not in force.

One might ask: if there is no *yovel*, does the category of *sdei achuza* even apply? The answer is no — without *yovel*, there is no *sdei achuza* framework. Sanctifying a field when *yovel* is not operative is effectively treated as a standard dedication of movable property, except for the *chomesh* obligation.

The *Tosafot* make an important point about the historical setting of this mishnah. If there is no *Beit HaMikdash* at all, then while one can technically sanctify property — since sanctity is a metaphysical status and the Torah's categories do not disappear merely because there is no institution to collect — *Tosafot* notes that *hekdesh* in that case has no practical financial interest, and the entire mechanism of redemption reduces to a purely symbolic act. One would simply redeem on a *perutah* to remove the *kedushah*. There would be no bidding war. The mishnah's scenario — with its competitive auction and financial stakes — therefore must describe a period when there is a *Beit HaMikdash* but no *yovel*. That is precisely the period of the Second Temple, during which *yovel* did not apply because the majority of the Jewish people were not settled on the Land in accordance with their tribal portions.

### Owner's First Bid Right

The mishnah establishes: *omer lo petach atah rishon* — we say to the owner, make the first offer. The rationale is that since the owner must add a *chomesh* to whatever he bids, he will ultimately contribute more to the Temple treasury than any other bidder at the same base price. By inviting him to enter the bidding first, *hekdesh* maximizes the likelihood of receiving his augmented contribution.

In practice, a savvy owner will discount his bid to account for the eventual *chomesh* — bidding less than he would otherwise be willing to pay, knowing the *chomesh* will bring it up to his true ceiling. This exposes him to being outbid. The dynamics are thus genuinely those of an auction, not a simple assessment. The *gemara* identifies two reasons to want the owner involved in the bidding. *Chada de-aydi de-chaviva lei tfei hu parik lei* — first, because the field is personally dear to him, he may be willing to pay more than a disinterested third party. *Ve-od mitzvat geulah ba-adon* — and further, the Torah itself establishes that the owner has the first right and obligation to redeem what he has sanctified; the bidding process gives that right procedural expression. Beyond both of these, there is the concrete financial consideration: *she-ha-be'alim notnim chomesh ve-chol adam einam notnim chomesh* — the owner adds a fifth while everyone else does not.

### Poor Quality Field Case

A striking episode is recorded in the mishnah. Someone sanctified a field *mipnei ra'atah* — on account of its poor quality. The field was so unproductive that it was not worth the expense of working it. By sanctifying it, the person made a gesture of piety at essentially no personal cost. When told *petach atah rishon* — make the first offer — he offered a trivial amount. *Rabbi Yose* says that the amount he offered was the value of an egg — *harei sheli be-beitzah* — on the basis that *hekdesh nifdeh be-kesef u-ve-shaveh kesef*, that *hekdesh* may be redeemed with monetary equivalents, not only with coin. The court accepted his offer: *higatanu* — deal. And the consequence: *nimtza mafsid et sadehu lefanav* — he wound up paying for his trivial field and being stuck with it anyway.

The point of the episode is dual. First, it confirms that redemption is a bidding process and not a unilateral assessment by *hekdesh*. Second, and more significantly, it establishes that the court does not merely invite the owner to bid — it pressures him to bid. *Hekdesh* must not become a dumping ground for worthless property.

### Fifth Addition Requireme…

The *gemara* explores the precise mechanism of the owner's obligation to add a *chomesh*. The *tanna kamma* of the case holds that the owner must bid at least an *issar*, because an *issar* contains at least four or more *perutot*, meaning that when the *chomesh* — which is technically one quarter of the principal, such that the principal becomes four-fifths of the total — is calculated on an *issar*, the result is at least a *perutah*. One cannot add a *chomesh* of less than a *perutah*, since that is the minimum denomination for a valid halachic transaction. *Rabbi Yose*, however, holds that even if the bid is only a *beitzah* — an egg — whose fifth would fall below a *perutah*, it is still valid: *hekdesh nifdeh be-kesef u-ve-shaveh kesef*, even a non-monetary equivalent of minimal value suffices to redeem *hekdesh*. The *gemara* confirms that the dispute cannot concern whether equivalents are acceptable in principle — that is agreed upon by all. The debate is specifically whether one may redeem *hekdesh* when the *chomesh* that results would be less than a *perutah*.

The case therefore works as follows: *stama de-rabbanan de-amar ke-issar* — the anonymous rabbinic position requires at least an *issar* to ensure a valid *chomesh*. *Rabbi Yose* permits even a *beitzah*. In either case, if the owner makes even that minimal offer and the court accepts it, *nimtza mafsid* — he is left having paid something trivial and having retrieved his worthless field. The *gemara* confirms that the court does not merely suggest: *kofin* — they compel him. Or more precisely, per one reading: first they suggest, *omer lo petach atah rishon*, and if he declines, *kofin* — they coerce. The policy is clear: *hekdesh* refuses to absorb property it cannot use, and the obligation falls back on the owner.

## Auction Mechanics

### Bids Set Price

The mishnah now describes the full auction. One bidder offers ten *sela*, another twenty, another thirty, another forty, another fifty. The field is awarded to the highest bidder. But what happens if the winning bidder backs out? The *gemara*'s insight, and that of the mishnah itself, is that each bid functions not merely as an offer but as a *designation of value*. When the fifty-*shekel* bidder makes his offer, he has, in some objective sense, established the value of the field at fifty. If he then backs out, *hekdesh* can only recover forty from the next bidder. The fifty-*shekel* bidder has caused a loss of ten *shekel* and must make up that difference. *Memashkenin minichasav ad eser* — the court seizes assets to collect the ten-*shekel* gap.

### Backing Out Penalties

The principle applies uniformly at every level. If the forty-*shekel* bidder backs out, he pays ten — the difference between his bid and the thirty-*shekel* bid below him. If the thirty-*shekel* bidder backs out, he pays ten. If the twenty-*shekel* bidder backs out, he pays ten. Each backing-out causes a loss of ten, and each is responsible for exactly that gap. This structure serves as a powerful deterrent against frivolous or strategic bidding — entering the auction solely to drive up the price for others. Every bid is binding, and the bidder remains liable for the gap his withdrawal creates even if others above him have already withdrawn and been assessed their own penalties.

### Initial Bidder Backs Out

The case of the initial ten-*shekel* bidder who backs out is handled differently. Since there is no lower bid to serve as the floor, the court sells the field at market value — *mochrin oto be-shavyo* — and the ten-*shekel* bidder pays the difference between market value and ten. *Ve-nifra'in mi-shel eser et ha-motar* — the surplus above ten is collected from him.

## Owner vs. Other Bidders

### Owner's Fifth Advantage

The mishnah turns to competition between the owner and other bidders. When the owner and another bidder both offer twenty, *ha-be'alim kodmim mipnei she-hem mosifim chomesh* — the owner takes precedence because he will add a fifth. His effective payment is twenty-five, whereas the other bidder's payment is twenty. *Hekdesh* receives more from the owner.

### Non-Owner Bids Higher

Things become more complex when a non-owner bids higher than the owner. Say the owner bids twenty and another bidder says twenty-one. The non-owner's higher bid designates the value at twenty-one, yet the owner still wins the auction since his twenty plus his *chomesh* of five yields twenty-five, which is more than twenty-one. But because the non-owner's bid has set the price at twenty-one, the owner does not pay based on his own initial offer of twenty. He pays twenty-one — the price set by the competing bid — and then adds his *chomesh* calculated on his original bid of twenty, yielding a total of twenty-six. *Ha-be'alim notnim esrim ve-shesh.*

This is a striking consequence. The owner entered the auction prepared to pay twenty plus the *chomesh* — twenty-five total. Because another bidder said twenty-one, the owner is now committed to twenty-six. He wins, but at a higher cost than he anticipated. The rule is clear before one enters the auction: all bids are binding, bids set prices, and the owner's *chomesh* is calculated on his own bid regardless of what price has been set by another bidder's offer.

### Equivalence Point

The mishnah traces through successive increments. At twenty-two, twenty-three, and twenty-four offered by the non-owner, the owner still wins — paying twenty-seven, twenty-eight, and twenty-nine respectively — since his twenty plus five still outpaces the non-owner's offer. When the non-owner reaches twenty-five, the owner's twenty plus five equals exactly twenty-five — a tie — and the owner still receives the field since the tie goes to him, with his total payment now thirty. At this point the mishnah notes: *she-ein mosifin chomesh al iluyo shel zeh* — the *chomesh* is calculated only on the owner's own bid, not on the non-owner's higher offer.

### Owner's Final Offer Opti…

When another bidder reaches twenty-six, the owner's twenty plus five is now only twenty-five — strictly less than twenty-six — and the owner can no longer claim the field on the strength of his original bid alone. At this point the mishnah affords the owner an option: *im ratzu ha-be'alim notnim shloshim ve-echad ve-dinar* — if the owner wishes, he may submit a final offer of thirty-one and a fraction, which is equivalent to revising his base bid to approximately twenty-six, yielding a total that exceeds twenty-six. If he elects to do so, *ha-be'alim kodmim* — he takes the field. If not, *omer lo harei hi shelcha* — the field goes to the twenty-six bidder.

The mishnah's structure implies that outside of this final option, the auction is essentially a sealed-bid, single-round process — what one might call a silent auction. The owner is the one party who retains the right to a final counteroffer after the other bids are revealed.

## Auction Mechanics…

### Sequential vs. Simultane…

*Rav Chisda* introduces a critical distinction. The mishnah states that when the fifty-*shekel* bidder backs out, he pays ten; when the forty-*shekel* bidder backs out, he pays ten; and so on down to the twenty-*shekel* bidder, each paying ten. *Rav Chisda* asks: why does the forty-*shekel* bidder pay the full ten between his bid and the thirty-*shekel* bid below him? If the fifty-*shekel* bidder had also backed out, should he not share responsibility for that gap?

*Rav Chisda*'s answer is *lo kashya — kan be-vat achat kan be-zeh achar zeh*: there is no contradiction, because the two scenarios are different. If the fifty-*shekel* bidder backed out first — sequentially — and was assessed his ten-*shekel* penalty and departed, the auction then proceeds as if he were never there. The forty-*shekel* bidder now stands as the high bidder and bears full responsibility for the gap between his bid and the next. His backing out therefore costs him the full ten between forty and thirty. But if both the fifty and forty-*shekel* bidders back out *simultaneously*, their responsibilities overlap. Both bear responsibility for the gap between forty and thirty, since either one of them, had he remained, would have covered that gap. In the zone of overlap, liability is divided equally — *mishalshin beineihem*. The fifty-*shekel* bidder thus pays ten for the exclusive gap between fifty and forty, and then five for his half of the shared gap between forty and thirty — fifteen in total. The forty-*shekel* bidder pays five for his half of the gap between forty and thirty.

The *gemara* initially attempts to read the mishnah as describing separate auctions, each with only one bidder at a given level, in order to avoid the question of shared liability. But this reading is forced and ultimately abandoned. The text *nifra'in mi-shel asarah* — "collected from the ten-*shekel* bidder" — implies there are others in the same auction, not that he is the sole participant. *Rav Ashi* confirms that the resolution is *Rav Chisda*'s: sequential withdrawal and simultaneous withdrawal are governed by different rules, and a *baraita* that states *mishalshin beineihem* is describing the simultaneous case while the mishnah's straight ten-*shekel* penalty describes sequential cases.

## Owner vs. Other Bidder…

### Ma'aser Sheni Comparison

The *gemara* raises a challenge from the laws of *ma'aser sheni*. In our mishnah, *hekdesh* gives the field to the owner when the owner and another bidder are tied at the same base amount — precisely because the owner's *chomesh* will result in a higher total payment to the treasury. The fifth, in other words, is treated as a decisive factor. The *gemara* then cites a *baraita* about redeeming *ma'aser sheni*: if the owner offers one *sela* and another person offers one *sela* and an *issar*, the other person's bid wins — *mipnei she-mosif al ha-keren* — because he has added more to the principal. The owner's *chomesh* is not taken into account as a tiebreaker; the higher principal wins outright.

The *gemara* resolves this by pointing to a fundamental structural difference. *Hacha de-chumsha rivcha de-hekdesh hu, chumash adif* — in our case of a sanctified field, the *chomesh* that the owner adds goes to *hekdesh*. *Hekdesh* has a direct financial interest in receiving the *chomesh*, and so naturally it factors the fifth into its calculation of who offers more. By contrast, with *ma'aser sheni*, *karna tefei parik shapir, chumsha lo ichpat lan* — the fifth that the owner adds to redeem his *ma'aser sheni* accrues only to himself; he takes both the redeemed value and the money he spent to Jerusalem to eat. The institution receiving the redemption money has no stake in whether the owner adds more or less. Therefore, in the *ma'aser sheni* context, only the principal matters, and a higher principal wins regardless of what *chomesh* the owner might add.
