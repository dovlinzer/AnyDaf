# Shabbat 155 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bundles & Twigs

### Intro: Pikei & Kiffin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַתִּירִין פְּקִיעֵי עָמִיר לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, וּמְפַסְפְּסִין אֶת הַכִּיפִין, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַזִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may untie *peki'in* of grain before an animal** on Shabbat, **and one may spread the *kifin* but not the *zirin*.** These terms will be explained in the Gemara.

The Mishnah we turn to now belongs to a cluster of rulings concerning a person's farm animals and the question of *tircha* — unnecessary effort on Shabbat — even in contexts where no formal *melacha* is being performed. The Mishnah states: *matirin pikei amir lifnei behemah* — one may open bundles of straw in front of an animal; that is not considered undue effort, since one is simply providing the animal its food. The Mishnah further permits *mefaspasin et hakiffin* — one may spread or scatter the contents, rubbing and loosening the material so it is no longer tightly bundled. The question of precisely what *kiffin* means will be debated in the Gemara. One reading, to be developed below, is that *pikei* are bundles of straw bound in two places — one at the top and one at the bottom — while *kiffin* are bundles bound in three places.

### Why Spread Straw

Why would one spread out the straw rather than simply leave it in a loosened bundle? Rashi explains: *shekhen derekh shemefazrim labehemah ha'asavim, veyafeh la le'ochlan* — it is the normal practice to scatter the grass before the animal, and it is better for the animal to eat it that way. When the hay is compressed, *shekesheim dechukim mitchamemim* — it heats up, *ve'einah merichah reicha kol kach* — the smell does not waft as readily, *vekatzah bahen* — and the animal is less interested in it. So spreading the hay makes it more attractive and appetizing to the animals, and this purpose justifies the additional effort.

### Zirin Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַזִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** but not the *zirin*.

The Mishnah then states: *aval lo et hazirin* — but one may not undo bundles of *zirin*. According to the first approach to be presented in the Gemara, *zirin* are bundles not of straw but of twigs — the kind that might occasionally serve as animal food when moist, but are not generally regarded as such. The prohibition on undoing these bundles is thus categorically different from the permission to undo straw bundles, and the Gemara will explain the underlying rationale.

### Vetch & Carobs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְרַסְּקִין לֹא אֶת הַשַּׁחַת וְלֹא אֶת הֶחָרוּבִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, בֵּין דַּקָּה וּבֵין גַּסָּה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בֶּחָרוּבִין לַדַּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not crush hay or carobs before an animal** on Shabbat in order to facilitate its eating. He may do so **neither** for a **small** animal **[*daka*] nor** for a **large** one. **Rabbi Yehuda permits** to do so **with carobs for a small** animal, because it can swallow the hard carobs only with difficulty.

The Mishnah continues: *ein meraskin lo et hashachat velo et hacharuvin lifnei behemah* — one may not chop up *shachat* (vetch) or *charuvin* (carobs) before an animal, *bein dakah bein gasah* — whether a small animal such as a sheep or goat, or a large animal such as a cow, horse, or camel. Rabbi Yehuda dissents in one respect: *matir becharuvin ledakah* — he permits chopping carobs for a small animal, because carobs are hard and require pre-cutting to be edible for smaller animals.

The overarching theme of this Mishnah is the limit on *tircha* in the service of one's animals. On one hand, there is a duty to feed one's animals — the famous *derasha* on the verses of *Shema* establishes that one must feed one's animals before eating oneself, and *tzaar baalei chayim* is a recognized concern. On the other hand, the Rabbis drew a line against effort that is unnecessary. Farm animals occupy an ambiguous position: one might argue for greater leniency given the owner's responsibility toward them, or for stricter limits given that agricultural labor is quintessentially the domain of weekday work. The Gemara does not resolve this tension explicitly, but whatever threshold applies here is specific to the context of animals and cannot be mechanically applied elsewhere.

## Rav Huna's View

### Pikei=Kiffin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֵן הֵן פְּקִיעִין, הֵן הֵן כִּיפִין. פְּקִיעִין תְּרֵי, כִּיפִין תְּלָתָא, זִירִין — דְּאַרְזֵי. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מַתִּירִין פְּקִיעֵי עָמִיר לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, וּמְפַסְפְסִין. וְהוּא הַדִּין לְכִיפִין, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַזִּירִין — לֹא לְפַסְפֵס וְלֹא לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna said: They are** called ***peki'in*** and **they are** also called ***kifin*.** The difference between them is that ***peki'in*** are tied with **two** knots, whereas ***kifin*** are tied with **three. *Zirin*,** which may not be moved on Shabbat, are bundles **of cedar** branches eaten by animals when the branches are small and moist. **And this is what** the mishna **is saying: One may untie *peki'in* of grain before an animal and spread** them, **and the same is true for *kifin*, but not** for ***zirin*,** which **one** may **neither spread nor untie.**

Rav Huna offers the following reading of the Mishnah: *hen hen peki'in hen hen kiffin* — the *pikei* and the *kiffin* are the same thing; both refer to bundles of straw. The *pikei* are bound in two places and the *kiffin* in three, but the distinction is incidental. Both may be undone and spread out before the animal. The *zirin*, by contrast, are twigs of cedar — *zirin de'arzei* — which can be used as animal fodder when moist but are not generally so used. According to Rav Huna, the reading of the Mishnah is: one may both undo and spread out straw bundles, whether bound twice or three times, and this same license applies to *kiffin*. What one may not do at all — neither spread nor undo — is the *zirin*, the twigs.

### Non-Food Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא קָא סָבַר לְמִטְרַח בְּאוּכְלָא — טָרְחִינַן, לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי אוּכְלָא לָא מְשַׁוֵּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reason** for the opinion of **Rav Huna? He holds** that with regard **to exerting** oneself **with food** on Shabbat, **one may exert** himself; however, with regard **to rendering food** edible, **one** may **not render** it so. Bundles of crops which are fit for animal consumption in their present state may be further prepared on Shabbat. Cedar branches cannot be eaten when bound together; therefore, one may not exert himself to untie them and render them edible on Shabbat.

Why this stark asymmetry? Rav Chisda articulates the rationale behind Rav Huna's position.

### Chisda's Formula

*Kasavar: lemitrach be'ochla tarchinan; leshavei ochla lo mashvinan.* If something is already defined as food, one may exert additional effort to make it more suitable or appealing for the animals. What one may not do is transform non-food into food. The concern with *zirin* is not the physical exertion involved in undoing the bundle — indeed, undoing a bundle is minimal effort — but rather the conceptual act of redefining something. A bundle of twigs is presumed to be firewood. When one unties it and places it before an animal, one is in effect converting it from non-food into food. This is not *melacha* in the formal sense, but it resembles the creation of something, a kind of quasi-*melacha* in the domain of category: one is taking an object understood as firewood and rendering it food purely by context, by the act of placing it before an animal. That act, Rav Huna maintains, is what is forbidden.

## R. Yehuda's View

### R. Yehuda's Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: הֵן הֵן פְּקִיעִין, הֵן הֵן זִירִין. פְּקִיעִין תְּרֵי, זִירִין תְּלָתָא, כִּיפִין — דְּאַרְזֵי. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מַתִּירִין פְּקִיעֵי עָמִיר לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, אֲבָל פַּסְפּוֹסֵי — לָא, וְכִיפִין — פַּסְפּוֹסֵי נָמֵי מְפַסְפְּסִינַן, אֲבָל לֹא הַזִּירִין, לְפַסְפֵּס אֶלָּא לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda** understood the mishna differently and **said: They are** called ***peki'in* and they are** also called ***zirin*.** The difference between them is that ***peki'in*** are tied with **two** knots, whereas ***zirin*** are tied with **three. *Kifin*** are bundles **of cedar** branches. **And this is what** the mishna **is saying: One may untie *peki'in* of grain before an animal; however,** with regard to **spreading them, no,** he may not spread them. **And** with regard to ***kifin*, one** may **also spread them. However,** that is **not** the case with regard to ***zirin*,** as it is prohibited to **spread** them, and it is **only** permitted **to untie** them.

Rabbi Yehuda reads the Mishnah entirely differently. According to him, *hen hen peki'in hen hen zirin* — the *pikei* permitted at the opening of the Mishnah and the *zirin* forbidden at its close both refer to straw bundles; the difference is merely that *pikei* are bound in two places and *zirin* in three. The *kiffin* in the middle of the Mishnah, which says one may spread them out, are the twigs — *kiffin de'arzei*. Thus Rabbi Yehuda inverts Rav Huna's assignment of the second and third terms.

### Don't Scatter Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה — קָסָבַר: שַׁוּוֹיֵי אוּכְלָא — מְשַׁוֵּינַן, מִטְרָח בְּאוּכְלָא — לָא טָרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** for **Rav Yehuda's** opinion? **He holds** the opposite of Rav Huna's opinion. He holds that with regard to **rendering food** edible, **one** may **render** it so; however, with regard to **exerting** oneself on Shabbat **with food** that is already in an edible state, **one** may **not exert** himself.

The operative logic according to Rabbi Yehuda is precisely the reverse of Rav Huna's. Rabbi Yehuda has no problem with the conceptual transformation of non-food into food; if the animal will eat something once it is prepared, one may do what is necessary to enable that. What Rabbi Yehuda forbids is additional effort once the item is already edible. Thus the Mishnah, read through Rabbi Yehuda's lens, is saying: one may undo the straw bundles — whether bound twice or three times — but once the bundle is undone and the straw is accessible, the animal can eat it. At that point, spreading and scattering is unnecessary effort, and is therefore forbidden. The animal eats better from scattered straw, as Rashi notes, but that preference does not justify the additional exertion once the food is already consumable.

### Twigs Need Work

By contrast, the twigs — which Rabbi Yehuda identifies as the *kiffin* — may be scattered. Why? Because the animal will not eat them unless they are spread out and loosened. For these items, the extra work is not superfluous; it is what makes them edible in the first place. Since the goal is to make the item food for the animal, one may do whatever is necessary to achieve that goal. The effort is not gratuitous; it is the very mechanism of transformation from inedible to edible.

### Rava's Formula

Rava crystallizes Rabbi Yehuda's position: *kasavar: shavuyei ochla mashvinan; aval mitrach be'ochla — lo tarchinan*. To turn something into food — that is permitted. Once it is already considered food, once the animal can eat it without further preparation — then we do not permit any additional effort. The two positions thus represent diametrically opposed understandings of what is problematic on Shabbat in this domain: for Rav Huna, the problem is creating something, the quasi-*melacha* of converting non-food into food; for Rabbi Yehuda, the problem is purely one of unnecessary exertion once the food is already there.

## Testing Positions

### Vetch Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אֵין מְרַסְּקִין אֶת הַשַּׁחַת וְאֶת הֶחָרוּבִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, בֵּין דַּקָּה וּבֵין גַּסָּה. מַאי לָאו, חָרוּבִין דּוּמְיָא דְשַׁחַת: מָה שַׁחַת דְּרַכִּיכָא, אַף חָרוּבִין דְּרַכִּיכֵי. אַלְמָא לָא טָרְחִינַן בְּאוּכְלָא — וּתְיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **One may not crush hay or carobs before an animal** on Shabbat in order to facilitate its eating. He may do so **neither** for a **small** animal **nor** for a **large** one. **Is this not** referring to **carobs** that are **similar to hay? Just as** it is referring to **hay that is soft, so too,** it is referring to **carobs that are soft. Apparently, we do not exert** ourselves **with food.** Since the carobs are suitable for animal consumption without being crushed, it is prohibited to exert oneself and crush them. **And** this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Rav Huna.**

The Gemara now attempts to determine which position is supported by the sources. It begins with the latter portion of the Mishnah itself. *Ein meraskin et hashachat ve'et hacharuvin lifnei behemah, bein dakah bein gasah* — one may not chop up vetch or carobs before animals, large or small. The Gemara argues: presumably the carobs are analogous to the vetch — *charuvin dumya deshachat*. Just as vetch is soft and readily eaten — *shachat de'orcha*, suitable as is — so too the carobs are soft and suitable. If that is the case, then what the Mishnah is forbidding is additional effort on food that is already edible, which is exactly Rabbi Yehuda's principle. *Alma lo tarchinan be'ochla* — once it is food, we do not permit the extra effort. This appears to challenge Rav Huna.

### Huna Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב הוּנָא: לָא, שַׁחַת דּוּמְיָא דְּחָרוּבִין: מָה חָרוּבִין דַּאֲקוֹשֵׁי, אַף שַׁחַת דַּאֲקוֹשֵׁי. הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? בְּעִילֵי זוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Huna** could have **said to you: No,** the mishna is referring to **hay** that is **similar to carobs. Just as** it is referring to **carobs that are hard, so too,** it is referring to **hay that is hard** and crushing it renders it edible. The Gemara asks: **How is it** possible **to find** hay that is so hard that an animal cannot eat it? The Gemara answers: It is referring **to young donkeys,** that can only eat hay that is crushed well.

Rav Huna responds: do not read the analogy in that direction. *Shachat dumya decharuvin* — the vetch is like the carobs, not the other way around. Just as standard carobs are hard and not easily consumed without chopping — *charuvin de'akusha* — so too the vetch in this case is hard. And where does one find vetch that is too hard for an animal to eat? *Be'eilei zutrei* — with small foals, young donkeys, for whom even ordinary vetch is difficult to consume. The chopping is thus necessary to make the food edible for those particular animals, and the prohibition, according to Rav Huna, is about creating food from non-food, not about unnecessary effort on existing food.

This point is worth pausing over: whether something is already food or not yet food depends not only on the item itself but on the animal being fed. The same vetch that is edible for a full-grown horse may be effectively non-food for a newborn foal. This nuance surfaces again in the discussion of Rabbi Yehuda's exception for carobs fed to small animals.

### Gourds Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מְחַתְּכִין אֶת הַדִּלּוּעִין לִפְנֵי הַבְּהֵמָה וְאֶת הַנְּבֵלָה לִפְנֵי הַכְּלָבִים. מַאי לָאו, דִּלּוּעִין דּוּמְיָא דִנְבֵלָה: מָה נְבֵלָה — דְּרַכִּיכָא, אַף דִּלּוּעִין — דְּרַכִּיכֵי, אַלְמָא טָרְחִינַן בְּאוּכְלָא, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְרַב יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from that which we learned in another mishna: **One may chop pumpkins before an animal and an** animal **carcass before dogs. Is this not** referring to **pumpkins** that are **similar to an** animal **carcass? Just as an** animal **carcass is soft, so too,** the **pumpkins** referred to here **are soft. Apparently, one** may **exert** himself **with food, and** this is a **conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda.**

The Gemara then turns to a separate Mishnah: *mechatchin et hadlu'in lifnei behemah, ve'et haneveila lifnei hakalavim* — one may chop up gourds before animals, and a *neveila* (carcass) before dogs. The Gemara asks: presumably the gourds are analogous to the carcass — *dlu'in dumya deneveila*. A carcass is soft and readily eaten by meat-eating animals; if the gourds are similarly soft and edible without chopping, then the permission to chop them represents a ruling that extra effort on already-edible food is permitted. This would support Rav Huna. *Alma tarchinan be'ochla* — once it is food, we may exert additional effort.

### Huna Counter II

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַב יְהוּדָה: לָא, נְבֵלָה דּוּמְיָא דְּדִלּוּעִין: מָה דִלּוּעִין דְּאַשּׁוּנֵי, אַף נְבֵלָה — דְּאַשּׁוּנָא. וְהֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? בִּבְשַׂר פִּילֵי. אִי נָמֵי, בְּגוּרְיָיאתָא זוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yehuda** could have **said to you: No,** the mishna is referring to an animal **carcass** that is **similar to pumpkins. Just as** the reference is to **pumpkins that are hard, so too,** the reference is to an animal **carcass that is hard,** and chopping it renders it edible. The Gemara asks: **And how is it** possible **to find** an animal carcass that is so hard that another animal cannot eat it? The Gemara answers: The mishna is referring to **elephant flesh. Alternatively,** it can be explained that the mishna is referring to more common animal meat placed **before puppies** that can eat only chopped meat.

Rabbi Yehuda responds in kind: read the analogy the other way. The carcass is like the gourds — *neveila dumya dedlu'in*. The gourds are hard; so too the carcass is hard. And what kind of carcass is hard? *Devasar pili* — elephant meat, which is difficult to eat without chopping; or *beguryata zutrei* — for small puppies or whelps, for whom even ordinary meat must be specially prepared. Thus the chopping here too is about making something edible, not about improving already-edible food.

### Chanan Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַב חָנָן מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: מְפָרְכִין תֶּבֶן וְאַסְפַּסְתָּא וּמְעָרְבִין. אַלְמָא טָרְחִינַן בְּאוּכְלָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from **that which Rav Ḥanan of Neharde'a taught: One** may **crumble straw and alfalfa** on Shabbat **and mix** the two together, and the animal then eats the straw because it is mixed with the alfalfa. **Apparently, one** may **exert** himself **with food** on Shabbat.

The Gemara makes another attempt. Rav Chanan from Neharde'a taught: *mefarchin teven ve'aspasta ume'arvin* — one may chop up straw and alfalfa (or fodder) and mix them together. Straw and fodder are among the most obviously and readily edible items for animals; if anything is food in its given form, it is these. Yet the Mishnah permits chopping and mixing them, which seems to indicate that once something is food, one may still do extra effort to improve it. *Alma tarchinan be'ochla* — a seemingly strong proof for Rav Huna's position.

### Preserving Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֶּבֶן בְּתִיבְנָא סַרְיָא, אַסְפַּסְתָּא — בְּעִילֵי זוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** No, the **straw** mentioned here is **rotten straw** that requires special preparation to render it suitable for animal consumption, and **alfalfa** is taught with regard to **young donkeys** who can only eat it in small pieces.

The Gemara declines to accept this as a definitive refutation of Rabbi Yehuda's framework. Perhaps the straw in question is *betivna sarya* — slightly spoiled straw, which the animal would not eat in its current state; the chopping rehabilitates it. And perhaps the fodder is for *aylei zutrei* — young foals who require it to be more finely prepared. Thus even here, the chopping might be classified as making something into food rather than improving already-edible food.

The Gemara does not cleanly resolve the dispute. Both positions find textual support, and each can deflect the challenges directed at it by positing special circumstances. The overall thrust of the sugya, however, seems to favor the principle that the problem is making non-food into food, rather than exerting effort on food already fit for consumption — though the opposing reading is well-supported and cannot be dismissed.

## Force-Feeding Rules

### Camel Feeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין אוֹבְסִין אֶת הַגָּמָל וְלֹא דּוֹרְסִין, אֲבָל מַלְעִיטִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not forcibly overfeed a camel** on Shabbat **and one may not force-feed** it, even if in doing so he does not overfeed the camel. **However, one may place food into its mouth.**

The next Mishnah shifts from what is done to the food to how food is delivered to the animal. *Ein ovsin et hagamal* — one may not *oveis* a camel, which means one may not force enormous quantities of food into it — essentially filling its stomach like a trough, as the Gemara will explain. *Velo dorsin* — nor may one use the related technique of *dosin*, pushing food into the animal's throat, though less deeply than *ovsin*. These are two distinct modes of forced feeding, one pushing more deeply than the other. *Aval mal'atin* — but one may *mal'it*, which is to assist the animal in eating, placing food in its mouth without forcing it beyond where the animal could retrieve it.

### Calf Feeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מַאֲמִירִין אֶת הָעֲגָלִים, אֲבָל מַלְעִיטִין. וּמְהַלְקְטִין לַתַּרְנְגוֹלִין, וְנוֹתְנִין מַיִם לַמּוּרְסָן, אֲבָל לֹא גּוֹבְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the mishna makes a distinction, which will be explained in the Gemara, between two manners of placing food in the mouths of cattle. **One may not** place food in **the** mouths of **calves** on Shabbat in the manner of ***hamra'a*, but one may** do so in the manner of ***halata*. And one may force-feed chickens. And one may add water to bran** used as animal feed, **but one may not knead** the mixture.

The Mishnah continues: *ve'ein ma'amirin et ha'agalim* — one similarly may not *ma'amir* the calves; *aval mal'atin* — but one may *mal'it* them. The same distinction between impermissible force-feeding and permissible assistance applies to calves. For chickens, the Mishnah uses the term *mehal'kitin* — one may be *mehal'ket* for chickens, a different verb for what appears to be a related form of assistance. The precise meaning of *mechalkin* will be disputed in the Gemara.

### Water & Bran

The Mishnah then permits: *ve'notnim mayim lemursan* — one may add water to bran, which is how animal feed is typically prepared. This is noteworthy because adding water to grain or flour raises the concern of *lisha* — kneading, a prohibited Shabbat labor. The Mishnah permits adding the water but adds: *aval lo govlin* — one may not knead it. Adding water is permitted; mixing it into a dough-like consistency is not.

### Water for Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נוֹתְנִים מַיִם לִפְנֵי דְּבוֹרִים וְלִפְנֵי יוֹנִים שֶׁבַּשּׁוֹבָךְ, אֲבָל נוֹתְנִים לִפְנֵי אֲווֹזִים וְתַרְנְגוֹלִים, וְלִפְנֵי יוֹנֵי הַרְדִּיסָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not place water before bees or before doves in a dove-cote,** because they are capable of finding their own food; **however, one may place** water **before geese and chickens and before hardisian [*hardeisiyyot*] doves.**

Finally, the Mishnah distinguishes between categories of animals with respect to water. *Ve'ein notnim mayim lifnei devorim ulifnei yonim shebashovach* — one may not put out water for bees or for doves in the coop. *Aval notnim lifnei avazim velifnei tarnegolim velifnei yonei hardesi'ot* — but one may put out water for ducks, chickens, and Herodian doves (a domesticated species, possibly from the island of Rhodes, associated with Herod's time). The basis of this distinction — whether it concerns ownership, responsibility, or the animals' access to water in the wild — is the subject of the Gemara's discussion below.

## Force-Feed Debate

### Ovsin=Fullness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אֵין אוֹבְסִין״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֵין עוֹשִׂין לָהּ אֵבוּס בְּתוֹךְ מֵעֶיהָ. מִי אִיכָּא כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? אִין, וְכִדְאָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא דְּאוֹכְלַהּ כּוֹרָא, וְאַטְעֵינַהּ כּוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that one may not forcibly overfeed a camel on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **What is** meaning of: **One may not forcibly overfeed? Rav Yehuda said: One may not** feed a camel to the point that **it creates a trough inside of its stomach.** The Gemara asks: **Is there** the possibility of feeding a camel **in that manner?** The Gemara answers: **Yes; and as Rav Yirmeya of Difti said: I saw** an **Arab who fed** his camel **a *kor*** of food **and loaded it** with another ***kor*** on its back.

What does *ovsin* mean? Rav Yehuda explains: *ein ovsin la evus betoch me'eha* — one does not make a trough inside its stomach; one does not force-feed the animal to such a degree that its stomach becomes like a trough, packed completely full. The Gemara asks whether such a thing is even possible. The answer is yes — Rav Yirmeyah of Difti testified: *ledidi chazi li hahu taya de'akhla kora ve'atneia kora* — I once saw an Arab nomad who fed his camel an entire *kor* of grain and then loaded a *kor* onto its back. Camels are evidently capable of consuming and carrying extraordinary quantities. The prohibition of *ovsin* addresses precisely this extreme.

### Hamra vs. Malatin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מַאֲמִירִין. אֵיזוֹ הִיא הַמְרָאָה וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא הַלְעָטָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַמְרָאָה — לִמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַחֲזִיר, הַלְעָטָה — לִמְקוֹם שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְהַחֲזִיר. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לִמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַחֲזִיר, וְהַמְרָאָה בִּכְלִי, הַלְעָטָה בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One may not** place food in the mouths of calves on Shabbat in the manner of ***hamra'a*,** but one may do so in the manner of *halata*. The Gemara asks: **Which is *hamra'a* and which is *halata*? Rav Yehuda said: *Hamra'a*** is positioning food **into a place** in the animal's throat **from which it cannot return** and expel the food. ***Halata*** is positioning food **into a place** in the animal's mouth **from which it can return** and expel the food. **Rav Ḥisda said: Both this and that** refer to positioning food **into a place from which** the animal **cannot return** and expel the food; **however,** the difference between them is that in ***hamra'a*** the food is placed **with a vessel,** whereas **in *halata*** the food is placed **by hand.**

Beyond *ovsin*, there is the more ordinary prohibition of *hamra*, force-feeding, and the permitted *malatin*. Rav Yehuda defines the distinction as follows. *Hamra* — the forbidden force-feeding — pushes food *le-makom she-eino yakhol lehachzir*, to a place from which the animal cannot retrieve it; the animal is compelled to swallow. *Halatah* — the permitted assistance — places food only *le-makom she-yakhol lehachzir*, to a place from which the animal could still expel it if it chose. The animal retains the ability to spit it out; it is participating in the act of eating rather than being subjected to it.

### Chisda: Kli Issue

Rav Chisda disagrees with this framework. In his view, *idei ve-idei le-makom she-eino yakhol lehachzir* — both *hamra* and *halatah* involve feeding to the point of no return, where the animal cannot retrieve the food. The distinction is not anatomical but instrumental: *hamra bi-chli, halatah be-yad* — the forbidden act uses a specialized vessel, the permitted act uses the hand. On Shabbat, taking out specialized farm implements to force-feed one's animals transforms the activity from care-giving into a form of *ovdin de-chol*, weekday agricultural labor. Using one's bare hands, by contrast, retains the character of simple feeding and is therefore permissible. Interestingly, using a vessel actually reduces the physical effort relative to doing it by hand — yet it is the vessel-use that is forbidden. The concern here is not tircha but the character of the act.

### Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: מְהַלְקְטִין לַתַּרְנְגוֹלִין, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שֶׁמַּלְקִיטִין. וְאֵין מַלְקִיטִין לְיוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וּלְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּיה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין מְהַלְקְטִין. מַאי ״מְהַלְקְטִין״ וּמַאי ״מַלְקִיטִין״? אִילֵּימָא מְהַלְקְטִין — דְּסָפֵי לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ, מַלְקִיטִין — דְּשָׁדֵי לֵיהּ קַמַּיְיהוּ, מִכְּלָל דְּיוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּיה מִישְׁדֵּא קַמַּיְיהוּ נָמֵי לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection** from that which was taught in the *Tosefta*: **One may force-feed [*mehalketin*] chickens, and needless to say, one may *malkitin*. And one may not *malkitin* doves in a dove-cote or doves in an attic, and needless to say, one may not force-feed.** The Gemara asks: **What is *mehalketin* and what is *malkitin*? If you say** that ***mehalketin*** means **that one feeds** the bird **by hand** and ***malkitin*** means **that one throws** the food **before them, by inference, throwing food before doves in a dove-cote or** before **doves in an attic is also not** permitted. But why would that be prohibited?

Rav Yosef challenges both positions by citing the Mishnah's rulings about chickens and coop doves. The Mishnah states that one may perform *mechalkin* for chickens — and certainly *mal'atin* — but one may not perform *mal'atin* for coop doves, let alone *mechalkin*. If *mechalkin* means placing food in the hand before the animal and *mal'atin* means simply placing it in front, then by doves one could not even toss food in front of them — which seems extreme. If, on the other hand, *mal'atin* involves placing food in the mouth (and is forbidden for doves), then for chickens both mouth-feeding and force-feeding are permitted, implying that force-feeding per se is not the problem — only using a vessel is.

### Yehuda Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב יְהוּדָה: לְעוֹלָם ״מְהַלְקְטִין״ דְּסָפֵי לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ, ״מַלְקִיטִין״ דְּשָׁדֵי לֵיהּ קַמַּיְיהוּ, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ יוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּיה לְמִישְׁדֵּא קַמַּיְיהוּ נָמֵי לָא? הָנֵי מְזוֹנוֹתָן עָלֶיךָ, וְהָנֵי אֵין מְזוֹנוֹתָן עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda** could have **said to you: Actually, *mehalketin*** means **that one feeds** the bird **by hand,** and ***malkitin*** mean **that one throws** the food **before them. And that which was difficult for you: Is throwing** food **before doves in a dove-cote or doves in an attic also not** permitted on Shabbat? This is not difficult because with regard to **these** chickens and geese that were mentioned, sole responsibility for **their sustenance** is incumbent **upon you** as they are incapable of providing for themselves. **However,** in the case of **these** doves, responsibility for **their sustenance** is **not** incumbent **upon you,** and therefore, it is prohibited to place food before them.

Rav Yehuda defends his position: *mechalkin* means placing food in the mouth, *mal'atin* means placing it in front of the animal. Both of these are permitted for chickens, and both involve no force-feeding whatsoever. With respect to doves, the reason even *mal'atin* — the trivial act of placing food in front of them — is forbidden is not because of the nature of the act, but because of the nature of the animal: the doves are not the owner's responsibility, and anything done for an animal not in one's care is impermissible. Force-feeding, by contrast, remains forbidden for all animals regardless.

### Chisda Defense

Rav Chisda maintains his alternative: *mechalkin* for chickens means force-feeding using a vessel, while *mal'atin* means force-feeding by hand. The Mishnah thus permits both for chickens — confirming that force-feeding by hand is allowed — while prohibiting both for coop doves because they are not the owner's animals. The only thing universally forbidden is force-feeding with a vessel.

## Ownership Test

### Dog & Pig

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: נוֹתְנִין מְזוֹנוֹת לִפְנֵי כֶלֶב, וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין מְזוֹנוֹת לִפְנֵי חֲזִיר. וּמָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? זֶה מְזוֹנוֹתָיו עָלֶיךָ, וְזֶה אֵין מְזוֹנוֹתָיו עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may place sustenance before a dog** on Shabbat, **but one may not place sustenance before a pig. And what** is the **difference between this and that?** In **this** case of the dog, responsibility **for its sustenance is** incumbent **upon you, and** in **that** case of the pig, responsibility **for its sustenance is not** incumbent **upon you,** as no Jew raises pigs.

The Gemara grounds the distinction between permitted and forbidden animal-feeding in the concept of *mezonotan alecha* — "their sustenance is upon you," meaning you bear responsibility for their feeding. A *baraita* illustrates this with a stark and memorable contrast: *notnin mezonot lifnei kelev, ve'ein notnin mezonot lifnei chazir* — one may place food before a dog but not before a pig. The explanation: *zeh mezonotav alecha ve'zeh ein mezonotav alecha* — for the dog, you bear responsibility (presumably because it is your dog), while for the pig, you do not. A Jewish household does not keep a pig; any pig encountered is unowned from that household's perspective, and one bears no responsibility for it. The practical halacha thus tracks ownership and responsibility rather than the intrinsic character of the act.

### Water Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, אֵין נוֹתְנִים מַיִם לִפְנֵי דְּבוֹרִים וְלִפְנֵי יוֹנִים שֶׁבַּשּׁוֹבָךְ, אֲבָל נוֹתְנִים לִפְנֵי אֲווֹזִין וְלִפְנֵי תַרְנְגוֹלִין וְלִפְנֵי יוֹנֵי הַרְדִּיסָיוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָנֵי מְזוֹנוֹתָן עָלֶיךָ, וְהָנֵי אֵין מְזוֹנוֹתָן עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The language of **the mishna is also precise** in support of this explanation, as we learn: **And one may not place water before bees or before doves in a dove-cote** because they are capable of finding their own food; **however, one may place** water **before geese and chickens and before hardisian doves. What is the reason** for this distinction? **Is it not because** for **these,** geese and chickens, responsibility for **their sustenance** is incumbent **upon you, and** for **those,** bees and doves, responsibility for **their sustenance** is **not** incumbent **upon you?**

Rav Ashi draws the same conclusion from our Mishnah directly. The Mishnah prohibits putting water before bees and coop doves but permits it before ducks, chickens, and Herodian doves. The most natural explanation is: *hani mezonotan alecha ve'hani ein mezonotan alecha* — the former group are not your animals, the latter are. This confirms that even something as trivial as placing water before an animal is forbidden when it is not your responsibility.

### Responsibility Test

The force of Rav Ashi's point is that ownership or responsibility, not the degree of effort, governs this category of rulings. Even the most minimal act — setting out water — is prohibited when directed at animals outside one's care.

### Water Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, מַאי אִירְיָא מַיָּא? אֲפִילּוּ חִיטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא, שָׁאנֵי מַיָּא דִּשְׁכִיחִי בְּאַגְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And according to your reasoning, why** did the mishna cite a case **specifically** involving **water? Even wheat and barley** should **also not** be permitted. **Rather,** the reason for the distinction between the *halakhot* is that **water is different because it is found in a lake** or in other reservoirs, and therefore one need not exert himself to provide water for bees and doves. That is not the case with the rest of their food.

The Gemara, however, presses back: if the rule is purely about *mezonotan alecha*, why does the Mishnah specify water? One should presumably be forbidden from putting out any food — grain, bread — before such animals. Why does the Mishnah single out water as the forbidden item?

### Necessity Standard

*Shani mayim deshchichi be'agma* — water is different because it is readily available in the swamps and natural surroundings. The prohibition is not simply that the animals are not yours; it is that there is no necessity to what you are doing. When water is plentiful in the environment, placing water before the animals adds nothing — they do not need you to provide it, and therefore doing so constitutes unjustified effort. Food, by contrast, may not be as readily available, and providing it may well be necessary even for animals one does not own. The practical upshot of this reframing is nuanced: the question is not only whether the animal is yours, but whether what you are providing is genuinely necessary given what the animal can access on its own. On rainy days when water is everywhere, even one's own animals would not need water put out; on drought days, the calculus shifts. This provides a practical standard that must be evaluated against actual circumstances.

## Dog's Poverty

### Derasha: Dog

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַשׁ רַבִּי יוֹנָה אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״יוֹדֵעַ צַדִּיק דִּין דַּלִּים״ — יוֹדֵעַ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בַּכֶּלֶב שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתָיו מוּעָטִין, לְפִיכָךְ שׁוֹהָה אֲכִילָתוֹ בְּמֵעָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to the discussion of feeding dogs, the Gemara cites additional statements on the topic. **Rabbi Yona taught at the entrance to the house of the *Nasi*: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "The righteous man takes knowledge of the cause of the poor"** (Proverbs 29:7)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, knows that for a dog, its sustenance is scarce** and they are not fed sufficiently. **Therefore, its food remains in its intestines for three days** so that the dog will be sustained by that food.

Rabbi Yona delivered the following *derasha* at the entrance of the house of the *nasi*. The verse states: *yodeia tzaddik din dalim* — the righteous one knows the judgment of the poor. The *tzaddik* here is the Holy One Blessed be He, and the *dal*, the poor one, is the dog. *Yodeia Hakadosh Baruch Hu b'kelev shemezonotav mu'atin* — God knows that the dog is a poor creature, that food is scarce for it.

### Digestion Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִתְנַן: כַּמָּה תִּשְׁהֶה אֲכִילָתוֹ בְּמֵעָיו וִיהֵא טָמֵא — בַּכֶּלֶב שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים מֵעֵת לְעֵת, וּבָעוֹפוֹת וּבַדָּגִים כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּפּוֹל לָאוּר וְתִשָּׂרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **as we learned** in a mishna dealing with the *halakhot* of ritual impurity: After an animal eats flesh from a corpse, **how long does its food remain in its intestines** undigested **and** therefore **ritually impure? In** the case of **a dog** it is for **three twenty-four hour periods, and for fowl and fish,** who digest their food quickly, it is **the equivalent** of **the** time it takes **for** the flesh **to fall into the fire and be consumed** by the fire.

In consequence of this poverty, God arranged that *shoheh achilato be-meiav gimel yamim* — the food remains in the dog's stomach for three days, allowing it to sustain itself over longer intervals between meals, much as a predator that eats infrequently must extract maximum sustenance from each meal. This has a concrete halachic implication cited from the Mishnah: if a dog swallows flesh from a human corpse and then dies, how long after ingestion does the flesh remain *metamei* — capable of transmitting corpse-impurity — while still inside the dog? *Becheli gimel yamim, me'et le'et* — for a dog, seventy-two hours. For birds and fish, by contrast, the food is consumed almost instantaneously — *kedei shetipol le'or vetisaref* — as quickly as if it had fallen into a fire.

### Proper Conduct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמִשְׁדֵּא אוּמְצָא לְכַלְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna said: Learn from it:** It is **the way of the world,** i.e., proper conduct, **to throw a piece of meat before a dog,** as even the Holy One, Blessed be He, concerns Himself with the dog's sustenance.

Rav Huna draws a practical moral from the *derasha*: *orach ar'a*, proper conduct, demands that one throw a small piece of meat to a dog when one has it, because the dog is a *dal*, a creature of genuine poverty with little access to food. One should have compassion on it and provide what one can.

### Mari's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: מְשַׁח אוּדְנֵיהּ, וְחוּטְרָא אַבָּתְרֵיהּ. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּדַבְרָא, אֲבָל בְּמָתָא לָא, דְּאָתֵי לְמִסְרַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much food** should one give to a dog? **Rav Mari said:** Give it the equivalent of **the measure of its ear and** strike it immediately **thereafter with a staff** so that the dog will not grow attached to the one who fed it. **This applies** specifically when one is **in the field, but in the city,** one should **not** give anything to a dog because the dog **will be drawn** to follow him and remain with him.

Rav Mari qualifies this: *mashach ozneih* — give it a piece about the length of an ear, something small, *ve'chutra batreih* — and then the stick should follow. Like the children's book about the mouse and the cookie: if you show the dog too much kindness without establishing a boundary, it will not leave you alone. One must give a little and then drive it away, so it does not become a persistent nuisance.

### Field vs. City

This advice applies, however, only *be-dabrah* — in the field or open countryside, where animals roam widely and a dog encountered once may not return. *Aval be-mata* — in the city, do not even do this, *de'ati le-misrach* — because the dog will latch onto you. In the city, dogs have smaller territories and less food, and any generosity will result in the dog following one persistently.

### Pappa's Saying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֵית דְּעָנִי מִכַּלְבָּא, וְלֵית דְּעַתִּיר מֵחֲזִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: There is no** creature **poorer than a dog, and no** creature **richer than a pig,** as pigs will eat anything, and people provide them with plentiful amounts of food.

Rav Pappa offers a final aphorism that encapsulates the contrast: *leit de'anyi mi-chalba* — there is none poorer than a dog; *ve-leit de'atir me-chazira* — and there is none richer than a pig. The pig forages and eats virtually anything; it needs nothing from anyone. The dog, dependent on scraps and leftovers, epitomizes poverty among animals.

## Hamra vs. Halata

### Hamra Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: אֵיזוֹ הִיא הַמְרָאָה, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא הַלְעָטָה? הַמְרָאָה — מַרְבִּיצָהּ וּפוֹקֵס אֶת פִּיהָ וּמַאֲכִילָהּ כַּרְשִׁינִין וּמַיִם בְּבַת אַחַת. הַלְעָטָה — מַאֲכִילָהּ מְעוּמָּד וּמַשְׁקָהּ מְעוּמָּד, וְנוֹתְנִין כַּרְשִׁינִין בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן וּמַיִם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the halakhic ruling, a *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda: What is *hamra'a* and what is *halata*? *Hamra'a*** is when a person forcibly **lays** the animal on the ground **and forces its mouth open and feeds it vetch and water simultaneously** so that the animal will be unable to expel it. ***Halata*** is when **one feeds** the animal while it is **standing and gives it to drink** while it is **standing,** i.e., gives it food and drink in the usual manner, **and one gives it vetch separately and water separately,** to augment what the animal eats on its own.

The *sugya* concludes with a *baraita* that provides a precise account of the two force-feeding methods. *Hamra* — the forbidden form — proceeds as follows: one makes the animal kneel (*marbitza*), forces open its mouth (*ufokeis et piha*), and feeds it *karshinim* (vetch) together with water simultaneously, all at once.

### Halata Method

*Halata* — the permitted form — is entirely different in character: one feeds the animal while it is standing, gives it vetch separately, and gives it water separately. The feeding is sequential and non-coercive.

### Proof Structure

What does this *baraita* establish? The *hamra* method involves no vessel — it is done entirely by hand, with the animal kneeled and its mouth held open — yet it is forbidden. This directly refutes Rav Chisda's position that the only forbidden element is using a *kli*, a vessel. The *baraita* demonstrates that force-feeding without any vessel can itself be forbidden, and that the distinction between *hamra* and *halata* turns not on the use of a vessel but on the manner and degree of compulsion: simultaneous delivery of food and water into a kneeling animal with a pried-open mouth is categorically different from placing food and water before a standing animal in separate stages. The *baraita* thus provides the strongest support for Rav Yehuda's position that force-feeding per se — irrespective of whether a vessel is used — is what the Mishnah prohibits.
