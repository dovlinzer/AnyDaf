# Berakhot 15b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hearing requirement

### Mishna debate intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת שְׁמַע וְלֹא הִשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ — יָצָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who recites *Shema* and did not** recite in a manner **audible to his own ear,** either because he read inaudibly or because he is deaf, **fulfilled** his obligation. **Rabbi Yosei says: He did not fulfill** his obligation.

The *mishna* on 15b opens with a dispute between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Yehuda regarding the requirement of *mushma'a l'ozno* — whether one must be able to hear what one is saying when reciting the *Shema*. Rabbi Yehuda rules that if one read the *Shema* without hearing what was said, one has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation (*yatza*); Rabbi Yose rules that one has not. The *Gemara*'s overarching question is not simply who is lenient and who is stringent, but rather what precisely Rabbi Yehuda's position entails: does he permit it even *l'chatchila*, or does he merely rule that *b'di'avad* — after the fact — it suffices?

### Deaf person reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ — לֹא יִתְרוֹם. וְאִם תָּרַם — תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned there** in a mishna regarding the laws of separating tithes: **A deaf person who can speak but cannot hear may not separate *teruma*** *ab initio*, because he must recite a blessing over the separation of *teruma* and he is unable to hear the blessing. **But** after the fact, **if he did separate it, his *teruma* is** valid ***teruma*.**

The *Gemara* introduces a *mishna* from *Megillah*: everyone is fit to read the *Megillah* except a *cheresh* (deaf-mute), a *shoteh* (one who is not of sound mind), and a *katan* (minor) — with Rabbi Yehuda adding that a *katan* is in fact *kasher*. The *Gemara* reads *cheresh* here not in its usual sense of "deaf-mute" but as referring to someone who is deaf alone, such that the disqualification rests entirely on the inability to hear what one is saying. On this reading, who is the *tanna* behind this ruling?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** this ***tanna*** who holds that if **a deaf person who can speak but cannot hear** separates *teruma*, **it is** considered *teruma* **after the fact,** but ***ab initio*** he may **not** do so?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת ״שְׁמַע״ וְלֹא הִשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ — יָצָא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in our mishna: **One who recites *Shema* and did not** recite it so it was **audible to his own ear, he fulfilled** his obligation. **This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: He did not fulfill** his obligation.

*Rav Matna* answers: it must be Rabbi Yose. The reasoning is direct: our *mishna* already records that Rabbi Yose says one does not fulfill the obligation of *Shema* if one cannot hear what one is saying. If the disqualifying factor in the *Megillah mishna* is deafness — i.e., the inability to hear one's own words — then the ruling coheres with Rabbi Yose's position.

### Rabbi Yehuda position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא וְדִיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא, דִּילְמָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּלְכַתְּחִלָּה הוּא דְּלָא, הָא דִיעֲבַד — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי, חֵרֵשׁ דּוּמְיָא דְּשׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן. מָה שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא — אַף חֵרֵשׁ דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** do you conclude that the mishna cited from tractate *Megilla* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and** that **after the fact** his reading is **also not** valid? **Perhaps it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** although ***ab initio*** a deaf-mute may **not** read, **after the fact** his reading **is valid?** The Gemara responds: **This could not enter your mind, as it was taught:** A deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, in one phrase in our mishna; to teach that **a deaf-mute** is **similar** to **an imbecile and a minor. Just as** in the case of **an imbecile and a minor, even after the fact,** their reading is **not** valid, **so too** the reading of **a deaf-mute, even after the fact,** is **not** valid.

The *Gemara* resists this conclusion. Perhaps, it suggests, the *mishna* in *Megillah* represents Rabbi Yehuda's view and is speaking only *l'chatchila*: the deaf person *should not* read the *Megillah* at the outset, but if he did so *b'di'avad*, he would be *yotze*. The *Gemara* rejects this reading: the *mishna* groups *cheresh* together with *shoteh* and *katan*, and by *shoteh* and *katan* we certainly mean that even *b'di'avad* the obligation is not fulfilled — the structure of the list demands that *cheresh* be read the same way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא. וּמִי מָצֵית לְאוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר בְּקָטָן, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא?! וְדִילְמָא כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא וּתְרֵי גַוְונֵי קָטָן, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לִקְרוֹת אֶת הַמְּגִילָּה, חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּקָטָן שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְחִנּוּךְ, אֲבָל קָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְחִנּוּךְ — אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה כָּשֵׁר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שֶׁרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר בְּקָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this assertion based on their appearance in a common list: **And perhaps this** case is **as it is and that** case is **as it is;** although listed together, the circumstances of each case may be different, and no definite proof can be drawn from their juxtaposition. The Gemara objects from a different perspective: **And can you** really **establish** that the first clause in the mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? But from what was taught in the latter clause** of the mishna: **And Rabbi Yehuda deems a minor fit** to read the Megilla, this proves **by inference that the beginning** of the mishna **is not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara rejects this challenge as well: **And perhaps the entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and there are **two types of minors, and the mishna is incomplete and it teaches as follows: All are fit to read the Megilla except a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor. In what** case **is this said?** With regard to **a minor who has not** yet **reached** the age of **training** to fulfill the mitzvot. **However** in the case of **a minor who has reached** the age of **training, even *ab initio* he is fit** to read the Megilla. This is the **statement of Rabbi Yehuda, as Rabbi Yehuda deems a minor fit,** and his statement here comes to elucidate and not to dispute.

The *Gemara* then attempts a more elaborate accommodation. Perhaps the *mishna* is indeed Rabbi Yehuda's, and perhaps there are two categories of *katan*, requiring a *chisora mechasra* — a reading that the *mishna* is missing words. On this reconstruction: a *katan* who has not yet reached the age of *chinuch* cannot read the *Megillah* even as a *tanna* would require, while a *katan* who has reached the age of *chinuch* is *kasher* according to Rabbi Yehuda even *l'chatchila*. This preserves the possibility that the *mishna* is Rabbi Yehuda's and that its prohibition on a *cheresh* reading is merely *l'chatchila*.

### Terumah source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקִימְתָּא, כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — וְדִיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא? אֶלָּא הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי, חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ — תּוֹרֵם לְכַתְּחִלָּה,
>
> **Translation:** In accordance **with whose** opinion **did you establish** the mishna? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that **after the fact, yes,** his reading is valid, but ***ab initio*, no,** he may not read? **However, that** *baraita* **which was taught by Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi: A deaf** person **who speaks but does not hear may, *ab initio*, separate *teruma*.**

The *Gemara* now confronts this reading directly by introducing a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi rules that one who can speak but cannot hear may separate *terumah l'chatchila* — meaning there is no concern whatsoever that one cannot hear the *beracha* one recites. If this *baraita* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view, then Rabbi Yehuda clearly permits it even *l'chatchila*, and the attempt to read the *Megillah mishna* as Rabbi Yehuda allowing it only *b'di'avad* collapses: it simply does not align with Rabbi Yehuda's leniency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּיעֲבַד אִין לְכַתְּחִילָּה לָא. אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **Neither** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. As, if** you say that it is **Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion, didn't he say that **after the fact, yes,** he fulfilled his obligation, but ***ab initio*, no,** he may not fulfill his obligation in that manner? And **if** you say that it is **Rabbi Yosei's** opinion, didn't he say that **even after the fact he** did **not** fulfill his obligation?

But the *Gemara* presses from the other direction as well. A different *baraita* states: *lo yivarech adam birkat hamazon b'libo* — one should not recite *Birkat Hamazon* in one's heart; yet if one did, one is *yotze*. The *Gemara* interprets this *baraita* as addressing a case where one uttered the words but could not hear them. This *baraita* implies that *l'chatchila* one must hear, but *b'di'avad* it is sufficient. The problem: this *baraita* cannot be Rabbi Yehuda (who on the previous reading permits it even *l'chatchila*), nor can it be Rabbi Yose (who disqualifies it even *b'di'avad*). The *Gemara* thus finds itself with three positions and only two named disputants.

## L'chatchila positions

### B'di'avad braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי? אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יְבָרֵךְ אָדָם בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן בְּלִבּוֹ, וְאִם בֵּירַךְ — יָצָא, מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** This Gemara rejects this objection: **But rather, what,** will you explain the *baraita* in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** a deaf-mute is **also** fit to fulfill his obligation **even *ab initio*? However, that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not recite the Grace after Meals in his heart,** inaudibly, **and if he recited** the blessing in that manner, **he fulfilled** his obligation, in accordance with **whose** opinion is that *baraita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא אָמַר אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי, וְאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא אָמַר אֲפִילּוּ דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** It is in accordance **neither** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** As **if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't he say** that even ***ab initio* he may fulfill** his obligation in that manner? And **if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, didn't he say** that **even after the fact he** did **not** fulfill his obligation?

To summarize the impasse: one *baraita* (the *terumah* case) implies that even *l'chatchila* hearing is not required; a second *baraita* (*Birkat Hamazon*) implies that *l'chatchila* it is required, though *b'di'avad* it is sufficient; and our *mishna* records Rabbi Yose's position that even *b'di'avad* it is not sufficient. Three positions exist, yet the *mishna* presents only Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose. The *Gemara* must find a way to assign all three positions to specific *tannaim*.

### Two solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא הָא — דִידֵיהּ, הָא — דְרַבֵּיהּ. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת שְׁמַע צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר אָנֹכִי מְצַוְּךָ הַיּוֹם עַל לְבָבֶךָ״, אַחַר כַּוָּנַת הַלֵּב הֵן הֵן הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Actually,** we can explain that the *baraita*: A deaf person may, *ab initio*, separate *teruma*, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** according to whom a deaf-mute is **also** permitted to do so **even *ab initio*.** With regard to the opinion expressed that he is prohibited to recite *Shema* and Grace after Meals *ab initio*, **there is no difficulty, as this is his** own opinion **and that is his teacher's** opinion. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: One who recites *Shema* must make it audible to his ears, as it is stated: "Hear, Israel,** the Lord is our God, the Lord is One." The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Meir said to him: But it says: "Which I command you this day, upon your heart";** meaning that **the** significance of the **words follows the intention of the heart** and even *ab initio* one need not recite *Shema* audibly. The opinion that after the fact, a deaf person fulfilled his obligation to recite *Shema* is the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.

The *Gemara* offers two possible resolutions. The first: *le'olam Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the most lenient ruling (*l'chatchila* hearing is not required) is indeed Rabbi Yehuda's. The *Birkat Hamazon* baraita, which requires hearing *l'chatchila* but permits it *b'di'avad*, represents not Rabbi Yehuda but his *Rebbe*. A separate *baraita* confirms this: *amar Rabbi Yehuda mishum Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* — Rabbi Yehuda transmitted in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah that one who recites *Shema* should ensure it is audible (*tzarich she-yashmia l'ozno*), the word *tzarich* being understood as a *l'chatchila* requirement that does not invalidate the *b'di'avad* case. Rabbi Meir, cited in that same *baraita*, responds that the verse *v'hayu ha-devarim ha-eileh asher anochi metzavecha hayom al levavecha* — "on your heart" — teaches that the focus is on the *kavana*, the intention of the heart, not on audibility. Accordingly, Rabbi Meir requires no hearing even *l'chatchila*.

### Final resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כְּרַבֵּיהּ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא — רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this** point and the entire *baraita* has been cited, **even** if **you say** that **Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with** the opinion of **his teacher, it is,** nevertheless, **not difficult.** As **this** *baraita* that holds that he may not recite Grace after Meals *ab initio* but after the fact he fulfilled his obligation is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** while **this** *baraita* permitting a deaf person to separate *teruma ab initio* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

The *Gemara* then offers a second resolution: *hashta de-atit l'hachi* — now that this *baraita* has introduced us to Rabbi Meir's position, one may say that Rabbi Yehuda himself holds like his *Rebbe*, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah — requiring hearing *l'chatchila* but ruling one *yotze b'di'avad* — while it is Rabbi Meir who represents the fully lenient position of permitting it even *l'chatchila*.

The upshot is that we now have three clearly demarcated positions: (1) Rabbi Meir, who does not require hearing even *l'chatchila*; (2) Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah (and perhaps Rabbi Yehuda), who require it *l'chatchila* but rule one *yotze b'di'avad*; and (3) Rabbi Yose, who requires it even to the point that *b'di'avad* one has not fulfilled the obligation. The *Gemara* at this stage treats these positions as applying uniformly across *Shema*, *Megillah*, and the *berachot* of *terumah* — as though the same *halacha* governs all of them. As will be seen, this assumption will shortly be challenged.

## Halachic ruling scope

### Rav Chisda ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁילָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא צָרִיךְ וְאֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה. קָמַשְׁמַע לַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rav Sheila said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda who said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** as cited in our mishna. The Gemara explains: **And** although that may seem redundant, both statements **are necessary, as had** Rav Ḥisda only **taught us** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, I would have said,** as was suggested in the Gemara above, that Rabbi Yehuda permits to do so **even *ab initio*.** Therefore, **he teaches us** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda who said** it **in the name of** his teacher, **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya;** *ab initio* one must recite *Shema* in a manner audible to his own ears. **And had** Rav Ḥisda only **taught us** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, I would have said** that the phrase: Must make it audible, means not only **must** he recite it that way *ab initio*, but **also** after the fact **he has no remedy** if he failed to do so. Therefore, **he teaches us** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda;** *ab initio* one must recite *Shema* in a manner audible to his own ears, but if he failed to do so, after the fact his recitation is valid.

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Shila*: the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda as transmitted in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah. This ruling encodes two distinct points, and the *Gemara* notes that both are necessary. Had Rav Chisda stated only that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda of our *mishna*, one might have concluded that hearing is not required even *l'chatchila* — reading Rabbi Yehuda at his most lenient. By specifying that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda *in the name of* Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, Rav Chisda signals that *l'chatchila* one must hear. Conversely, had Rav Chisda stated only that we rule like Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's formulation that one *should* hear (*tzarich she-yashmia l'ozno*), one might have thought that *tzarich* here means an absolute requirement, leaving no remedy *b'di'avad*. By anchoring the ruling also in Rabbi Yehuda's *mishna* — where one who did not hear is still *yotze* — Rav Chisda makes clear that *b'di'avad* suffices without hearing. Both components of the ruling are thus indispensable.

### Rav Yosef limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא יָצָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַסְכֵּת וּשְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** The **dispute** as to whether or not a deaf person fulfills his obligation **is** only **in** the case of **the recitation of *Shema*, but with regard to the rest of the mitzvot, everyone agrees that he does not fulfill** his obligation if he does not hear his recitation, **as it is written: "Pay attention, and hear, Israel"** (Deuteronomy 27:9); meaning that one is required to listen and hear.

Rav Yosef introduces a critical qualification: *machlokes b'krias Shema* — the entire debate is specific to the recitation of *Shema*. When it comes to all other *mitzvot*, *divrei ha-kol*, everyone agrees that one who did not hear what one said has not fulfilled the obligation. Rav Yosef derives this from the verse *hasket u-shma Yisrael* — "be attentive and hear, O Israel" — which he reads as a universal requirement of audibility across all speech-based obligations. The particular *sugya* of *Shema*, by contrast, revolves around the phrase *al levavecha* — "on your heart" — which opens the possibility that intention and inner orientation are paramount, thereby loosening the requirement of audibility.

### Challenge & Shema unique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לֹא יְבָרֵךְ אָדָם בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן בְּלִבּוֹ, וְאִם בֵּירֵךְ — יָצָא. אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר — הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, דִּכְתִיב ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יָצָא. וְהָכְתִיב ״הַסְכֵּת וּשְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״! הַהוּא בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **objects** based on what was taught in a *baraita*: **One may not recite the Grace after Meals in his heart,** inaudibly, **and if he recited** the blessing in that manner, **he fulfilled** his obligation. In this example of the rest of the mitzvot, the obligation to hear the recitation of the blessing is only *ab initio*. **Rather,** Rav Yosef's statement must be emended. **If this was said, it was said as follows; Rav Yosef said:** The **dispute** as to whether or not a deaf person fulfills his obligation **is** only **in** the case of **the recitation of *Shema*, as it is written: "Hear, Israel." But regarding the rest of the mitzvot, all agree that** a deaf-mute **fulfills** his obligation. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it written: "Pay attention, and hear, Israel"?** The Gemara responds: **That** verse **is written with regard to matters of Torah;** one must pay close attention to what is written in the Torah.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges Rav Yosef from the *baraita* cited earlier: *lo yivarech adam Birkat Hamazon b'libo v'im berach yatza* — one should not recite *Birkat Hamazon* in one's heart, yet if one did, one is *yotze*. This demonstrates that even by *Birkat Hamazon*, a *mitzvah* other than *Shema*, there is a *b'di'avad* dispensation when one has not fully articulated the words audibly. This seems to contradict Rav Yosef's claim that by all other *mitzvot* everyone agrees one is not *yotze*.

The *Gemara* resolves this by correcting the formulation of Rav Yosef's ruling. By *Shema*, the debate is whether hearing is required even *l'chatchila* — whether the absence of audibility is a formal deficiency that the *tanna'im* dispute. By all other *mitzvot*, the ruling is that one is *yotze* — meaning audibility is generally not required and one who mutters without hearing has fulfilled the obligation. The verse *hasket u-shma Yisrael*, the *Gemara* concludes, is not a formal requirement for *mitzvot* at all; rather, it concerns the study and teaching of *Torah*. The distinctive feature of *Shema* is that its own text contains the word *shema* — "hear" — which is what generates the *machloket* about whether elevated audibility is demanded. All other speech-based obligations require only articulation, not hearing, and the debate is confined to *Shema* alone.

## Precise articulation

### Imprecise reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא וְלֹא דִּקְדֵּק בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יָצָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **One who recited** *Shema* and **was not** sufficiently **precise in** his enunciation of **its letters, Rabbi Yosei says: He fulfilled** his obligation. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He did not fulfill** his obligation.

The *mishna* records an additional rule: *kara v'lo dikdek b'osiyoseha* — if one read *Shema* without being precise in the articulation of its letters, the ruling is that one has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation after the fact.

### Leniency ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טָבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tavi said** that **Rabbi Yoshiya said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** **the statements in both** disputes that rule **leniently**.

Rabbi Tevi in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya rules: *halacha k'divrei shneihem l'hakel* — we rule leniently in accordance with both positions in the *mishna*. That is, both leniencies are operative: if one did not hear what one said, one is nonetheless *yotze*; and if one was imprecise in the articulation of the letters, one is also *yotze*. These two leniencies cohere around a common theme — both move away from a strictly formalistic understanding of the act of speech in *Shema* and toward a conception in which *kavana* and *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* are more central to the fulfillment of the obligation.

## Resurrection digression

### Womb-Sheol parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי טָבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״שָׁלוֹשׁ הֵנָּה לֹא תִשְׂבַּעְנָה. שְׁאוֹל וְעֹצֶר רָחַם״. וְכִי מָה עִנְיַן שְׁאוֹל אֵצֶל רֶחֶם? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה רֶחֶם מַכְנִיס וּמוֹצִיא, אַף שְׁאוֹל מַכְנִיס וּמוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Incidental to citing one statement by this combination of Sages, the Gemara cites another statement in their name: **And Rabbi Tavi said** that **Rabbi Yoshiya said: What is** meant by that which is **written: "There are three that are never satisfied…the grave and the barren womb"** (Proverbs 30:15–16)? We have to ask: **What does a grave have to do with a womb? Rather,** this juxtaposition comes **to tell you: Just as a womb takes in and gives forth, so too a grave takes in and gives forth** with the resurrection of the dead.

Rabbi Tevi, continuing in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya, turns to a verse in Proverbs: *shalosh heinah lo tishba'nah* — three things are never sated. Among the three listed is *Sheol*, the underworld, and *otzer rechem*, the barren womb. The *Gemara* asks: what is the conceptual connection between the underworld and the womb? *Ella lomar lach* — it is to teach you: just as the womb receives something in private and produces something outwardly, so too the underworld receives the dead and will one day produce them — a reference to *techiyat ha-meitim*, the resurrection of the dead.

### Kal vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה רֶחֶם שֶׁמַּכְנִיסִין בּוֹ בַּחֲשַׁאי, מוֹצִיאִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְּקוֹלֵי קוֹלוֹת. שְׁאוֹל שֶׁמַּכְנִיסִין בּוֹ בְּקוֹלֵי קוֹלוֹת, אֵינוֹ דִין שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְּקוֹלֵי קוֹלוֹת? מִכָּאן תְּשׁוּבָה לָאוֹמְרִים אֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And is this not an *a fortiori* inference: Just as** the fetus **is placed into the womb in private,** and the baby **is removed from it with loud cries** at childbirth; **the grave into which** the deceased **is placed with loud cries** of mourning at burial, **is it not right that** the body should **be removed with loud cries? From this** verse there is **a refutation to those who say that there is no Torah** source for the **resurrection of the dead.**

Rabbi Yoshiya develops this into a *kal va-chomer*: the womb receives in private — when a couple conceives, it is done in private — yet what it produces emerges publicly, with great tumult. All the more so, *Sheol*, which receives the dead publicly — burial is a major communal event — should certainly produce its contents publicly and with great fanfare at the time of resurrection. *Mikan teshuvah l'omrim ein techiyat ha-meitim min ha-Torah* — here is a response to those who claim that *techiyat ha-meitim* has no basis in the *Torah*. It should be noted that this is not, by any formal standard, the most rigorous of proofs — it does not begin from an explicit verse about resurrection, and the reading of the analogy is quite particular. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yoshiya presents this juxtaposition as a *remez*, a hint toward *techiyat ha-meitim*. The passage is introduced here because it was said by the same *amora* — Rabbi Tevi in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya — who had just ruled on the *halacha* of imprecise articulation.

## Writing & teaching

### Mezuza content

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ — הַכֹּל בִּכְתָב, אֲפִילּוּ צַוָּאוֹת. אָמַר לֵיהּ: דַּאֲמַר לָךְ מַנִּי — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר גַּבֵּי סוֹטָה — אָלוֹת כּוֹתֵב, צַוָּאוֹת אֵינוֹ כּוֹתֵב. וְהָתָם הוּא דִכְתִיב: ״וְכָתַב אֶת הָאָלוֹת הָאֵלֶּה״, אֲבָל הָכָא דִּכְתִיב: ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ — אֲפִילּוּ צַוָּאוֹת נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya taught** the following *baraita* **before Rava:** The verse, **"And you shall write them [*ukhtavtam*]** on the door posts of your house and your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9), should be understood as though it were written: "And you shall write them completely [*ukhtav tam*]," i.e., in their entirety. **Everything must be in writing** in the *mezuza* or phylacteries, **even the commands** to write *mezuzot* and phylacteries. Rava **said to him: Who said** this *baraita* **to you? It was Rabbi Yehuda,** who **said with regard to** the scroll of the ***sota*** that **one writes curses,** but **one does not write commands** and instructions how to administer the potion to the *sota*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, in the case of *mezuzot* and phylacteries the Torah taught that even the commands must be written in order to distinguish it from *sota*. **There, in** the case of the *sota*, **it is written: "And** the priest **shall write [*vekhatav*] these curses** in a scroll" (Numbers 5:23); only the curses are recorded in the scroll, and nothing more. **But here** in the case of *mezuza*, **where it is written: "And you shall write them** [***ukhtavtam***]," **even the commands** must **also** be written.

The *Gemara* now enters a brief but illuminating excursus on the content of the *mezuza*, prompted by the *parsha* of *Shema* that is under discussion. *Tani Rav Oshia kamei d'Rava* — Rav Oshia taught the following before Rava: *u'chtavtam*, the verse commanding the writing of the words on the doorposts, requires that one write *hakol bichtav* — everything in writing — *afilu tzava'ot*, even the command itself. That is, the verse *u'chtavtam al mezuzot beitecha* does not permit one to stop before writing *u'chtavtam* on the grounds that the word is the command to write rather than part of what is to be written. On the contrary, the entire *parsha*, including the *pasuk* that instructs the writing, must be included in the *mezuza*.

### Rava explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״וְכָתַב״? טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״אָלוֹת״. אָלוֹת אִין, צַוָּאוֹת — לָא. אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא נֵילַף ״כְּתִיבָה״ ״כְּתִיבָה״ מֵהָתָם: מָה הָתָם — אָלוֹת אִין, צַוָּאוֹת לָא. אַף הָכָא נָמֵי — צַוָּאוֹת לָא. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ — אֲפִילּוּ צַוָּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **Is that to say Rabbi Yehuda's reason** for writing only the curses in the *sota* scroll **is because** the verse in the portion of *sota* **says: "And** the priest **shall write [*vekhatav*],"** as opposed to: "And you shall write them [*ukhtavtam*]"? **Rabbi Yehuda's reason is because it is written** in the portion of the *sota*: These **curses,** meaning **curses, yes,** they must be recorded; **commands, no.** If that is Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning, there is no reason to derive the requirement to write the commands from the phrase: And you shall write them, in the case of the mitzva of *mezuza*. Derive it simply from the fact that the Torah did not distinguish between the various components of the portion. "And you shall write them" **was necessary,** as otherwise **it might have entered your mind to say: Let us derive** it by means of a verbal analogy, **writing** mentioned here **from writing** mentioned **there,** that **just as there curses, yes, commands, no; so too here, commands, no.** Therefore, **the Torah wrote: And you shall write them** in their entirety, **even the commands.**

Rava responds: the opinion you are teaching must be that of Rabbi Yehuda. The basis for Rava's remark is Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the context of *Sotah*: when the *kohen* writes the *parsha* of *Sotah* — the curses that are then dissolved into the water — Rabbi Yehuda says *alot kotev tzava'ot eino kotev*: one writes the curses themselves but not the verse that commands their writing. There the *pasuk* reads *v'chatav et ha'alot ha'eileh* — "he shall write these curses" — and Rabbi Yehuda understands this as a specific designation of what to write, excluding the command itself.

Why then, Rava asks rhetorically, is a *derashah* needed to include *u'chtavtam* in the *mezuza*? He explains: one might have thought to apply a *gezeira shava* — an analogy based on the shared concept of *ketiva*, writing — from *Sotah* to *mezuza*, concluding that just as by *Sotah* one writes only what precedes the command and not the command itself, so too by *mezuza* one need not write *u'chtavtam*. Therefore the *Torah* wrote *u'chtavtam* in the more general and inclusive form — "and you shall write them" — to indicate that *afilu tzava'ot nami*, the command to write is itself included among what must be written. The *Gemara* then clarifies that Rabbi Yehuda's distinction by *Sotah* is not merely because the verb there is in a simpler form, but because the *Torah* is explicit: *alot* — write the *curses*, which by specificity excludes the command. By *mezuza*, where no such specific limitation appears, the general formulation includes everything.

## Writing & teaching…

### V'limadetem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב עוֹבַדְיָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״וְלִמַּדְתֶּם״ שֶׁיְּהֵא לִמּוּדְךָ תָּם — שֶׁיִּתֵּן רֶיוַח בֵּין הַדְּבָקִים. עָנֵי רָבָא בָּתְרֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן ״עַל לְבָבֶךָ״, ״עַל לְבַבְכֶם״, ״בְּכָל לְבָבְךָ״, ״בְּכָל לְבַבְכֶם״, ״עֵשֶׂב בְּשָׂדְךָ״, ״וַאֲבַדְתֶּם מְהֵרָה״, ״הַכָּנָף פְּתִיל״, ״אֶתְכֶם מֵאֶרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note: **Rav Ovadya taught** a *baraita* **before Rava:** That which was stated: **"And you shall teach them [*velimadtem*]** to your children" (Deuteronomy 11:19) teaches that **your teaching must be complete [*tam*] that one** should **leave space between adjacent** words, where the last letter of the first and the first letter of the second are identical. One must distinguish between the words and enunciate each one clearly. **Rava responded after him** by way of explanation: **For example,** one must enunciate ***al levavekha*** and not read them as one word. So too, ***al levavkhem*, *bekhol levavekha*, *bekhol levavkhem*, *esev besadkha*, *va'avadtem mehera*, *hakanaf petil*,** and ***etkhem me'eretz*.** Because the last letter of the first word and the first letter of the second are identical, the words are liable to be enunciated together and the correct meaning is liable to be obscured.

*Tani Rabbi Ovadia kamei d'Rava* — Rabbi Ovadia taught the following before Rava, this time drawing from the next significant word in the *parsha*: *v'limadetem* — "and you shall teach them." The *derashah* is: *she-yehei limudchem tam* — your teaching should be *tam*, complete. And what does "complete" mean in practice? *She-yiten revach bein ha-dveikim* — you must give a space between the words that would naturally run together.

### Practical application

Rava responds by elaborating the practical application of this requirement in the recitation of *Shema*. When a word ends with the same letter that begins the next word, a careless reader might merge the two into a single unit. Rava provides the examples: *al levavecha* — the *lamed* ending *al* and the *lamed* beginning *levavecha* must be separated; similarly *b'chol levavecha*, *b'chol levavechem*, *eisev be-sadecha*, *ha-kanaf pasil*, and *eschem me-aretz*. In each case, the point is to articulate each word distinctly so that the *deveikut* — the running-together — between adjacent words is avoided.

## Precise articulation…

### Space between words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כָּל הַקּוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמְדַקְדֵּק בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתֶיהָ מְצַנְּנִין לוֹ גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּפָרֵשׂ שַׁדַּי מְלָכִים בָּהּ תַּשְׁלֵג בְּצַלְמוֹן״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּפָרֵשׂ״ אֶלָּא ״בְּפָרֵשׁ״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּצַלְמוֹן״ אֶלָּא ״בְּצַלְמָוֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** On this same topic, **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: Anyone who recites *Shema* and is punctilious in** enunciating **its letters, Gehenna is cooled for him, as it is stated: "When the Almighty scatters [*befares*] kings over it, it will snow in Tzalmon"** (Psalms 68:15). **Do not read *befares*,** When He scatters, **but *befaresh*,** When he enunciates. When one enunciates the name of God with precision, God will fulfill the verse: It will snow in Tzalmon, on his behalf. **Do not read *beTzalmon*,** in Tzalmon, **but *betzalmavet*,** in the shadow of death, a reference to Gehenna. As reward for enunciating God's name precisely, God will cool Gehenna for him.

The requirement of *revach bein ha-dveikim* articulated through the *derashah* of *v'limadetem* thus has direct and concrete consequences for how one reads *Shema*. The same letters at the boundary of adjacent words — whether the doubling of *lamed*, *beit*, *pei*, or *mem* followed by *alef* — must be clearly separated in pronunciation so that the boundary between words is audible and the meaning of each word is preserved.

### Reward for care

The *Gemara* adds an encouragement from Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina: *kol ha-korei Krias Shema u-medakdek b'osiyoseha* — whoever recites *Krias Shema* with care for its letters — *metzaninim lo Gehennom*, they cool down *Gehennom* for him. The verse cited is *bifros Shad-dai melachim bah tashleg be-Tzalmon*. The *derashah* reads: *al tikrei bifros* — do not read it as "when He spread out," *ela befareish* — rather read it as "when one articulates explicitly." *Al tikrei be-Tzalmon* — do not read it as the place Zalmon, *ela be-Tzalmaves* — rather, in the shadow of death, i.e., in *Gehennom*. So if one articulates the words of God — Rashi understands *melachim* here as referring to the *Torah*, likened to a royal crown — *b'Tzalmaves*, in the place of death, it will be cooled. The imagery is striking if not entirely transparent: the person is apparently still destined for *Gehennom*, but the careful recitation of *Shema* effects a cooling of that judgment. The precise correspondence between the act and the image is difficult to fully unpack, but the message of the *Gemara* is clear — there is reward for the care one takes in saying *Shema*.

### Tosafos expansion

*Tosafos* cites a significant addition from the *Rif*, who extends the category of *revach bein ha-dveikim* beyond cases of identical letters. The *Rif* adds the example of *d'chara af* — where the sound is similar even if the letters differ, creating a risk that *v'chara af* could be heard as *v'charaf*, meaning to blaspheme. He further adds *ure'isem oso*, *uzchartem es*, and *va'asisem es* — cases where the *mem* at the end of one word flows into the silent *alef* beginning the next, risking the merger of two words into one. Additionally, *Tosafos* notes the requirement to articulate the *zayin* of *tizkeru* clearly enough that it not be heard as *shin*, which would transform the word into *tishkeru* — "you shall be rewarded" — a reading that would imply the *mitzvot* are performed for reward. Much of the care that has become customary in the recitation of *Shema* flows from this *Gemara* and these further elaborations by the *Rif* and *Tosafos*.

## Reading errors

### Order requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקּוֹרֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ — לֹא יָצָא. קָרָא וְטָעָה — יַחְזוֹר לִמְקוֹם שֶׁטָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who recited** *Shema* **out of order,** meaning he did not read the verses sequentially, **he did not fulfill** his obligation. **One who recited and erred, should return to the place** in *Shema* **that he erred.**

The *mishna* rules: *ha-korei l'mafrea lo yatza* — one who reads *Shema* out of order has not fulfilled the obligation. The *parshiyot* of *Shema* must be recited in their proper sequence, and deviation from that sequence invalidates the reading entirely.

### Rabbi Elazar story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי הֲווֹ קָא קָטְרִין לֵיהּ גְּנָנָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אֵיזִיל וְאֶשְׁמַע מִלְּתָא דְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, וְאֵיתֵי וְאֵימָא לְכוּ. אֲזַל אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְתַנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָרָא וְטָעָה וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן טָעָה, בְּאֶמְצַע הַפֶּרֶק — יַחְזוֹר לָרֹאשׁ. בֵּין פֶּרֶק לְפֶרֶק — יַחְזוֹר לְפֶרֶק רִאשׁוֹן. בֵּין כְּתִיבָה לִכְתִיבָה — יַחְזוֹר לִכְתִיבָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an error in the recitation of *Shema*, the Gemara recounts: **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi were once tying a wedding canopy** in preparation **for** the wedding of **Rabbi Elazar. He said to them: In the meantime,** until you finish, **I will go and hear something in the study hall, and I will come and say it to you. He went and found the *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* in the study hall, **who was reciting** this *Tosefta* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** **One who recited** *Shema* **and erred, and does not know where** exactly **he erred;** if he was **in the middle of a paragraph** when he realized his error, **he must return to the beginning** of the paragraph; **if** he was **between one paragraph and another** when he realized his error but does not remember between which paragraphs, **he must return to the first** break between **paragraphs.** Similarly, **if one erred between writing and writing,** i.e., between the verse: "And you shall write them on the door posts of your house and on your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9) in the first paragraph and the identical verse (Deuteronomy 11:20) in the second paragraph, **he must return to the first writing.**

The *Gemara* frames the subsequent teaching within a charming narrative. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi were tying together an awning — Rashi identifies it as a *chuppah* being prepared for Rabbi Elazar, who was about to be married. Rabbi Elazar, finding himself at leisure while they worked, said to them: while you are doing this and I am standing idle, I will go to the *Beis Medrash*, hear what is being taught, and return to share it with you — so that the making of the *chuppah* and the learning of *Torah* might proceed together.

### Error procedures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא פָּתַח בִּ״לְמַעַן יִרְבּוּ יְמֵיכֶם״, אֲבָל פָּתַח בִּ״לְמַעַן יִרְבּוּ יְמֵיכֶם״ — סִרְכֵיהּ נָקֵט וְאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: They only taught** this *halakha* in a case **where one did not** yet **begin: "In order to lengthen your days"** (Deuteronomy 11:21) which follows that verse at the end of the second paragraph. **However, if he** already **began** to recite: **In order to lengthen your days,** he can assume that **he assumed his routine and continued** and completed the second paragraph.

Rabbi Elazar went and found a *Tanna* teaching before Rabbi Yochanan. The teaching addressed the case of one who recited *Shema*, made a mistake, and does not know where the mistake occurred. The *halacha* is as follows. If one erred in the middle of a *perek* — one knows which *parasha* one is in but does not know where within it the mistake was made — one returns to the beginning of that *perek*. If one erred between *perakim* — one is at a break and does not know which *perek* is next — one returns to the first *perek*, i.e., to the break between the first and second sections, and resumes from there. The same logic applies to the word *u'chtavtam*, which appears in both the first and second *parshiyot*: if one has lost track of which *u'chtavtam* one is in, one returns to the first.

### Sirchei nakat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא וַאֲמַר לְהוּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִלּוּ לֹא בָּאנוּ אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוֹעַ דָּבָר זֶה — דַּיֵּינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar **came and told** Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi what he heard. **They said to him: Had we come only to hear this, it would have been sufficient.**

To all of this, Rabbi Yochanan added a crucial qualification: *lo shanu ela she-lo pasach b'l'maan yirbu yomeichem* — this entire procedure applies only if one has not yet reached and begun the *pasuk* of *l'maan yirbu yomeichem*. But *aval pasach b'l'maan yirbu yomeichem*, if one has already opened with *l'maan yirbu yomeichem*, then *sirchei nakat v'azil* — one simply proceeds by habit. From that point onward, the *parshiyot* diverge sufficiently from one another that a person's habitual recitation can be relied upon. One may therefore assume that from *l'maan yirbu yomeichem* through the end of *Emes v'Yatziv*, the recitation proceeded correctly, and there is no need to return to any earlier point.

### Affirmation

Rabbi Elazar returned and reported this teaching to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi. They responded: *ilu lo banu ela lishmoa davar zeh dayeinu* — had we come only to hear this one thing, it would have been enough. The entire effort of making the *chuppah* was worthwhile for this teaching alone.

It is worth noting that the principle of *sirchei nakat* — the assumption that people act according to ingrained habit — carries significance well beyond this particular *halacha* of *Shema*. When a person cannot recall with certainty whether they performed a specific act, and the act in question is one they habitually perform, we may in certain halachic contexts assume that they did what they always do. The precise limits of this principle are subject to discussion, but it stands as an important tool in *halachic* reasoning across a range of situations.
