# Middot 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chambers & Access

### 38 Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁמֹנָה תָאִים הָיוּ שָׁם, חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּצָּפוֹן, חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּדָּרוֹם, וּשְׁמֹנָה בַּמַּעֲרָב. שֶׁבַּצָּפוֹן וְשֶׁבַּדָּרוֹם, חֲמִשָּׁה עַל גַּבֵּי חֲמִשָּׁה, וַחֲמִשָּׁה עַל גַּבֵּיהֶם. וְשֶׁבַּמַּעֲרָב, שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל גַּבֵּי שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּשְׁנַיִם עַל גַּבֵּיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** There were thirty-eight cells there, fifteen on the north, fifteen on the south, and eight on the west. On the north and on the south there were five over five and five again over these; On the west there were three over three and two over these.

The present sugya opens at the top of 37a, the third mishnah of the fourth perek, picking up where the previous discussion left off — namely, the doors leading from the *ulam* into the *heikhal*. The wall of the *heikhal* was six *amot* thick, and within that depth there were two sets of doors: two on the outer face and two on the inner face, functioning as a kind of double-door system.

The mishnah then introduces the *ta'im* — the chambers built into the walls of the *heikhal*. The text states: *ushloshim ushmonah ta'im hayu sham* — there were thirty-eight of these chambers in total. Fifteen ran along the north side, fifteen along the south, and eight along the west. At first glance, the arithmetic seems puzzling: if one looks at a cross-sectional diagram, one sees only five rooms along the north and south, not fifteen. The resolution is that these rooms were stacked in three tiers. Along the north and south there were five chambers per tier, three tiers high — yielding fifteen on each side. Along the west there were three, then three, then two — yielding eight. Why two rather than three on the uppermost tier of the western wall is not stated; the Mishnah simply reflects the number actually built.

What exactly were these chambers used for? The text is silent on this point. An Artscroll footnote, citing a source called *Tavnit Heikhal*, speculates that these cells served as storage for the gold, silver, and other valuables belonging to the Temple — functioning, in effect, as the *beit hamikdash*'s treasury. The logic is that the most protected location within the entire complex is the *heikhal* itself, and placing valuables there ensured they were maximally secured. The *Bartenura* offers a different suggestion, that at least some of these chambers were used for weaving the *paroches*. Another chamber in the complex was known as *Beit HaParoches*, so it is possible that the textile work associated with the curtains was distributed across various spaces. Ultimately, the Mishnah does not specify, and all identifications remain speculative.

### Inter-chamber Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁלֹשָׁה פְתָחִים הָיוּ לְכָל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, אֶחָד לַתָּא מִן הַיָּמִין, וְאֶחָד לַתָּא מִן הַשְּׂמֹאל, וְאֶחָד לַתָּא שֶׁעַל גַּבָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** Each had three openings, one to the cell on the right and one to the cell on the left and one to the cell above.

Having established the existence and layout of the thirty-eight *ta'im*, the mishnah turns to their interconnections. Each chamber had three doors: one leading to the adjacent chamber on the right (*echad min hata m'yamin*), one to the adjacent chamber on the left (*echad min hata m'smol*), and one leading to the chamber directly above (*echad l'ta she'gabav*). The side doors make immediate sense: if entry into the entire system of *ta'im* was through a single access point, one would need lateral passageways to move between chambers on the same level. The door above presumably corresponded to some form of internal ladder or staircase. The *Tiferes Yisrael* suggests there was a spiral staircase.

### Northeast Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְקֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית הָיוּ חֲמִשָּׁה פְתָחִים, אֶחָד לַתָּא מִן הַיָּמִין, וְאֶחָד לַתָּא שֶׁעַל גַּבָּיו, וְאֶחָד לַמְּסִבָּה, וְאֶחָד לַפִּשְׁפָּשׁ, וְאֶחָד לַהֵיכָל.
>
> **Translation:** In the [one at the] northeastern corner there were five openings, one to the cell on the right, one to the cell above, one to the mesibbah, one to the door, and one to the Hekhal.

The mishnah singles out the chamber at the northeast corner as a special case. Being at the far corner, it had no chamber to one side, but to compensate it had five doors in total rather than three. *U'v'keren mizrachis tzfonia hayu chamisha pesachim* — in the northeast chamber there were five openings. These were: one to the adjacent chamber, one to the chamber above, one to the *heikhal* itself, one to the *pishpash* (the tunnel or crawl space running through the six-*amah* wall), and one to the *mesiba* (the walk-around passage leading to the roof).

The door leading directly into the *heikhal* is significant beyond what it first appears. The fact that this door is enumerated as a special feature of the northeast chamber implies that none of the other *ta'im* had direct access into the *heikhal*. This explains precisely why the lateral passageways between chambers were necessary: since the only doorway opening into the *heikhal* was through this one northeast chamber, anyone needing to enter or exit the storage system had to pass through this single point and then move sideways — and upward — to reach any of the other thirty-seven chambers. From a purely logistical standpoint, this would have made storing and retrieving items cumbersome, requiring one to navigate through a chain of adjoining chambers rather than entering each independently.

The *pishpash* mentioned among the five doors connects to the procedure described elsewhere: when the *heikhal* was opened each morning, the officiating *kohen* would enter through this crawl space or tunnel within the wall, make his way into the northeast chamber, and from there pass through a second tunnel into the *heikhal* proper to open the doors from the inside. The northeast chamber was thus both a storage unit and an operational node in the daily functioning of the Temple.

### Wall Thickness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, חָמֵשׁ, וְרֹבֶד שֵׁשׁ. וְהָאֶמְצָעִית, שֵׁשׁ, וְרֹבֶד שֶׁבַע. וְהָעֶלְיוֹנָה, שֶׁבַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (מלכים א ו), הַיָּצִיעַ הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה חָמֵשׁ בָּאַמָּה רָחְבָּהּ וְהַתִּיכֹנָה שֵׁשׁ בָּאַמָּה רָחְבָּהּ וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית שֶׁבַע בָּאַמָּה רָחְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The [chamber] of the lowest [story] was five cubits wide and at the ceiling six cubits. The [chamber] of the middle [story] was six cubits wide and at the ceiling of seven. The [chamber] of the top [story] was seven cubits wide, as it says, "The lowest story was five cubits wide, the middle one 6 cubits wide and the third 7 cubits wide" (I Kings 6:6).

The mishnah then explains how the progressive stacking of the chambers was structurally achieved. Rather than simply carving the wall into solid compartments and using the stone itself as floors and ceilings, the builders used wooden beams to divide one level from the next. To lay these beams, they needed a ledge to rest them on. This was accomplished by leaving the outer wall of the *heikhal* slightly thicker at the lower level — the beams for the first tier rested on this ledge. When building the second tier, the wall was set back slightly further inward, providing a ledge for the next set of beams, and so on for the third tier. The practical consequence is that the chambers grew progressively wider as one ascended: the bottom chamber (*hatachtonah*) was five *amot* wide, with a beam-layer (*rovad*) of six *amot* above it; the middle chamber (*ha'emtza'it*) was six *amot* wide, with a beam-layer of seven; and the top chamber (*ha'elyonah*) was seven *amot* wide. This is supported by the verse from *Melachim* describing the *yatzia* of the first Temple — *hayatzia hatachtonah chamesh ba'amah rochav vehatichonah shesh ba'amah rochav vehashlishit sheva ba'amah rochav* — and the Mishnah applies this structural principle to the second *Beit HaMikdash* as well.

## Mesiba & Roof Access

### Mesiba Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְסִבָּה הָיְתָה עוֹלָה מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית לְקֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, שֶׁבָּהּ הָיוּ עוֹלִים לְגַגּוֹת הַתָּאִים. הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּמְּסִבָּה וּפָנָיו לַמַּעֲרָב. הָלַךְ עַל כָּל פְּנֵי הַצָּפוֹן, עַד שֶׁהוּא מַגִּיעַ לַמַּעֲרָב. הִגִּיעַ לַמַּעֲרָב, וְהָפַךְ פָּנָיו לַדָּרוֹם. הָלַךְ כָּל פְּנֵי מַעֲרָב עַד שֶׁהוּא מַגִּיעַ לַדָּרוֹם. הִגִּיעַ לַדָּרוֹם, וְהָפַךְ פָּנָיו לַמִּזְרָח. הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדָּרוֹם, עַד שֶׁהוּא מַגִּיעַ לְפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל עֲלִיָּה, שֶׁפִּתְחָהּ שֶׁל עֲלִיָּה פָּתוּחַ לַדָּרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The mesibbah (a winding walkway) went up from the north-east corner to the north-west corner by which they used to go up to the roofs of the cells. One would ascend the messibah facing the west, traversing the whole of the northern side till he reached the west. When he reached the west he turned to face south and then traversed whole of the west side till he reached the south. When he reached the south he turned to face eastwards and then traversed the south side till he reached the door of the upper chamber, since the door of the upper chamber opened to the south.

The *mesiba* — the inclined walk-around passage alluded to among the five doors of the northeast chamber — is now described in detail. *Vehamesibah hayetah olah* — the *mesiba* was ascending; the text itself makes clear that this was not a level corridor but an inclined passage that rose continuously as it wound around the upper reaches of the *heikhal*. It began at the northeast corner (*miken mizrachit tsfonit*), ran westward along the top of the northern wall to the northwest corner (*l'keren tzfonit maaravit*), from which one could gain access to the tops of the *ta'im* on the north side. The passage then turned south, traversing the entire western wall (*halach kol pney hamaarav*) until reaching the southwest. There one turned east and proceeded along the southern wall until arriving at the opening to the roof (*ad shehu megia l'pitchah shel aliyah*). The opening faced south — one had traversed three sides of the building before finally arriving at the means of ascending to the roof.

### Roof Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל עֲלִיָּה הָיוּ שְׁנֵי כְלוֹנָסוֹת שֶׁל אֶרֶז, שֶׁבָּהֶן הָיוּ עוֹלִין לְגַגָּהּ שֶׁל עֲלִיָּה. וְרָאשֵׁי פִסְפָּסִין מַבְדִּילִים בָּעֲלִיָּה בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ לְבֵין קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** In the doorway of the upper chamber were two columns of cedar by which they used to climb up to the roof of the upper chamber, and at the top of them was a row of stones showing the division in the upper chamber between the holy part and the Holy of Holies.

The roof opening (*pitchah shel aliyah*) faced southward, consistent with having arrived at the southwest and then proceeding to the southeastern point of the walk. At the opening stood two cedar poles (*shetei klunsaot shel erez*), through which one would climb to the surface of the roof (*shebahen hayu olim l'gagah shel aliyah*). This was essentially a ladder of cedarwood.

Once on the roof, the boundary between the space above the *kodesh* and the space above the *kodesh hakodashim* was marked by *paspasin* — either protruding wooden bolts or, according to Albeck, a mosaic line set into the surface — so that those working on the roof would know precisely where the sanctity of the inner sanctum began below their feet.

### Craftsmen Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוּלִין הָיוּ פְתוּחִין בָּעֲלִיָּה לְבֵית קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים, שֶׁבָּהֶן הָיוּ מְשַׁלְשְׁלִין אֶת הָאֻמָּנִים בְּתֵבוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָזוּנוּ עֵינֵיהֶן מִבֵּית קָדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** There were trap doors in the upper chamber opening into the Holy of Holies by which the workmen were let down in baskets so that they should not feast their eyes on the Holy of Holies.

From the roof, the Mishnah introduces what is perhaps its most striking structural detail. *Vehalulin hayu petuchin ba'aliyah l'veit kodshei hakodashim* — there were openings (*lulin*) in the roof directly above the *kodesh hakodashim*, and through these openings craftsmen who needed to do repair work inside the *kodesh hakodashim* were lowered in boxes on some kind of pulley system. *Shebahen hayu meshalshelin et ha'umanin be'teivot* — they would lower the artisans down in *teivot*, enclosed boxes. The reason given is *kedei shelo yazinu eineihem mi'veit kodshei hakodashim* — so that the craftsmen's eyes should not take in, or feast upon, the interior of the *kodesh hakodashim*. The boxes thus served both as a practical conveyance and as a kind of visual containment, keeping the craftsmen focused on the specific repair they were sent to perform without exposing them unnecessarily to the full interior of the most sacred space.

## Heichal Dimensions

### First Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַהֵיכָל מֵאָה עַל מֵאָה, עַל רוּם מֵאָה. הָאֹטֶם שֵׁשׁ אַמּוֹת, וְגָבְהוֹ אַרְבָּעִים אַמָּה, אַמָּה כִּיּוּר, וְאַמָּתַיִם בֵּית דִּלְפָה, וְאַמָּה תִּקְרָה, וְאַמָּה מַעֲזִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Hekhal was a hundred cubits by a hundred with a height of a hundred. The foundation was six cubits, then it rose forty, then a cubit for the ornamentation, two cubits for the guttering, a cubit for the ceiling and a cubit for the plastering.

The mishnah now turns to the overall dimensions of the *heikhal*. *VeHaHeichal meah al meah al rom meah* — the entire structure was one hundred *amot* by one hundred *amot* by one hundred *amot* high. The Mishnah proceeds to account for how the hundred-*amah* height is reached. First, *ha'otem shesh amot* — the foundation base was six *amot*. Then *vegovah arbaim amah* — the height of the first story was forty *amot*, not the twenty *amot* previously assumed. This is a significant point: the inner space of the *heikhal* at its first level rose to forty *amot* — roughly sixty feet. With three tiers of *ta'im* built into each side, even the topmost chambers would have been substantial spaces.

Above the forty *amot* of the first story came several additional layers before the roof of that level was complete. *Ve'amat kiyur* — one *amah* of crown molding or decorative engraving at the top of the wall. *Ve'amataiyim beit dilfah* — two *amot* of cross-beams, the structural girders laid before the roof planks. *Ve'amah tikrah* — one *amah* for the actual roof planking. *Ve'amah ma'azivah* — one *amah* for the plaster laid over the roof. Totaling the foundation (6), the first-story height (40), and the roofing layers (1 + 2 + 1 + 1 = 5), one arrives at fifty-one *amot*.

### Second Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגֹבַהּ שֶׁל עֲלִיָּה אַרְבָּעִים אַמָּה, וְאַמָּה כִּיּוּר, וְאַמָּתַיִם בֵּית דִּלְפָה, וְאַמָּה תִּקְרָה, וְאַמָּה מַעֲזִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The height of the upper chamber was forty cubits, there was a cubit for its ornamentation, two cubits for the guttering, a cubit for the ceiling, a cubit for the plastering.

Built atop this first level was an entire second story. *Vegovah shel aliyah mem amah* — the second story rose another forty *amot*. Above it the same roofing sequence was applied: *amat kiyur* (1), *amatayim beis tzelofa* (2), *amat tikra* (1), *amat maziva* (1) — another five *amot*. Adding forty plus five to the fifty-one already accumulated yields ninety-six *amot*.

## Heichal Dimensions (II)

### Upper Finish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת מַעֲקֶה, וְאַמָּה כָּלֵה עוֹרֵב, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, לֹא הָיָה כָּלֵה עוֹרֵב עוֹלֶה מִן הַמִּדָּה, אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת הָיָה מַעֲקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** three cubits for the parapet and a cubit for the spikes. Rabbi Judah says the spikes were not included in the measurement, but the parapet was four cubits.

The remaining four *amot* are accounted for by the finishing elements at the very top. *V'gimel amos ma'akeh* — three *amot* for the parapet, the safety railing running around the roof perimeter. *V'ama kala oreiv* — and one *amah* for the raven spikes, the iron or metal points installed to prevent ravens from roosting on the roof, which was an obvious attraction given the constant smoke and smell of the sacrificial offerings. This brings the total to one hundred *amot*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the final count: *lo haya kala oreiv oleh min hamiddah* — the raven spikes should not be counted as part of the structural measurement. In his view the parapet was actually four *amot* high, and together with the raven spikes one simply does not include everything in the formal tally of one hundred.

## E-W Measurements

### Walls & Ulam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהַמִּזְרָח לַמַּעֲרָב מֵאָה אַמָּה, כֹּתֶל הָאוּלָם חָמֵשׁ, וְהָאוּלָם אַחַד עָשָׂר, כֹּתֶל הַהֵיכָל שֵׁשׁ, וְתוֹכוֹ אַרְבָּעִים אַמָּה, אַמָּה טְרַקְסִין, וְעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה בֵּית קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים, כֹּתֶל הַהֵיכָל שֵׁשׁ, וְהַתָּא שֵׁשׁ, וְכֹתֶל הַתָּא חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** From east to west it was a hundred cubits: The wall of the porch five cubits, the porch itself eleven, the wall of the Hekhal six cubits and its interior forty, a cubit for the space between, and twenty cubits for the Holy of Holies, the wall of the Hekhal six cubits, the cell six cubits and the wall of the cell five.

The Mishnah now accounts for the full one hundred *amot* running east to west. Starting from the front: *kotel ha-Ulam chamesh* — the wall of the *ulam* was five *amot* thick. *Ve-ha-Ulam achas esreh* — the interior width of the *ulam* was eleven *amot*. This puts us at sixteen. *Kotel ha-Heichal shesh* — the wall of the *heikhal* itself was six *amot* thick, bringing us to twenty-two. It was through this six-*amah* wall that the *pishpash* tunnel ran, allowing access to the northeast chamber and from there to the interior of the *heikhal* to open the doors each morning.

### Interior Sanctums

*Besojo arbaim* — the interior of the *kodesh* was forty *amot*, bringing us to sixty-two. *Amah traksin* — then one *amah* for the *traksin*, the transitional space between the *kodesh* and the *kodesh hakodashim*. In the second *Beit HaMikdash*, since there was no wall of stone separating the two sancta (the *aron* being absent and the wall configuration different), two curtains were hung with a one-*amah* gap between them — one curtain offset from each side — so that the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* could pass between them without exposing the interior of the *kodesh hakodashim* until the last moment. This space of one *amah* is the *traksin*. We are now at sixty-three. *Ve-esrim amah Beis Kodesh Ha-kodashim* — the *kodesh hakodashim* itself was twenty *amot*, bringing us to eighty-three.

### Back Wall Rooms

*Kotel Heichal shesh* — six *amot* for the back wall of the *heikhal*, reaching eighty-nine. *Ve-ha-ta shesh* — six *amot* for the average-sized chamber behind the back wall. This is eighty-five, bringing us to ninety-five. *Ve-hakotel hata chamesh* — five *amot* for the rear outer wall of the *ta*, and one arrives at exactly one hundred.

## N-S Measurements

### North Section

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַצָּפוֹן לַדָּרוֹם שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה, כֹּתֶל הַמְּסִבָּה חָמֵשׁ, וְהַמְּסִבָּה שָׁלֹשׁ, כֹּתֶל הַתָּא חָמֵשׁ, וְהַתָּא שֵׁשׁ, כֹּתֶל הַהֵיכָל שֵׁשׁ, וְתוֹכוֹ עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, כֹּתֶל הַהֵיכָל שֵׁשׁ, וְהַתָּא שֵׁשׁ, וְכֹתֶל הַתָּא חָמֵשׁ, וּבֵית הוֹרָדַת הַמַּיִם שָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת, וְהַכֹּתֶל חָמֵשׁ אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** From north to south was seventy cubits: The wall of the mesibbah five cubits, the mesibbah itself three, the wall of the cell five and the cell itself six, the wall of the Hekhal six cubits and its interior twenty, then the wall of the Hekhal again six and the cell six and its wall five, then the place of the water descent three cubits and its wall five cubits.

From north to south the measurement was seventy *amot* — not counting the *ulam*, which will be addressed separately. Beginning at the northern exterior: *kotel hamesibah chamesh* — five *amot* for the outer wall through which the *mesiba* passed. *Ve-hamesibah shalosh* — three *amot* for the walk-space of the *mesiba* itself, reaching eight. *Kotel hata chamesh* — five *amot* for the wall before the *ta'im*, reaching thirteen. *Ve-ha-ta shesh* — six *amot* for the average chamber, reaching nineteen. *Kotel Heichal shesh* — six *amot* for the wall of the *heikhal*, reaching twenty-five.

### Center & South

*Besojo esrim amah* — the interior width of the *heikhal* was twenty *amot*, reaching forty-five. The southern side was then built as a mirror image of the northern: *kotel Heichal shesh* (six), *ve-ha-ta shesh* (six), *ve-kotel hata chamesh* (five) — another twenty-five *amot* — yielding seventy in total.

### Water Drainage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוּלָם עוֹדֵף עָלָיו חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה מִן הַצָּפוֹן, וַחֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה מִן הַדָּרוֹם, וְהוּא הָיָה נִקְרָא בֵּית הַחֲלִיפוֹת, שֶׁשָּׁם גּוֹנְזִים אֶת הַסַּכִּינִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Porch extended beyond this fifteen cubits on the north and fifteen cubits on the south, and this space was called the House of the slaughter-knives where they used to store the knives.

In the space on the south side corresponding to the *mesiba* on the north, the Mishnah places a different structure at the lower level: the *beis horadas hamayim* — the place for draining water. This was a three-*amah* space (*shalosh amos*), with five *amot* of outer wall (*ve-kotel chamesh amos*), completing the seventy. The *ulam*, which the Mishnah notes extended fifteen *amot* beyond the seventy on the north (*ha-ulam odef alav chamesh esreh amah min hatzafon*) and fifteen *amot* beyond on the south (*vechamesh esreh ama min hadarom*), accounts for the additional thirty *amot* needed to reach one hundred. The spaces where the *ulam* projected beyond the main body of the *heikhal* were called *Beit HaChalifot* — *shebahen gonzin et hasakin* — for it was there that the knives were stored, or according to *Rashi* in *Yoma*, where the members of the *mishmarot* kept their *shechita* knives in recesses within the walls. Why such a utilitarian and indeed sharp-edged item would be stored in a space within the *ulam* is a matter of some puzzlement; one suggestion is that *gonzim* here connotes the formal *genizah* of knives that had become unusable as Temple implements, since broken *klei hamikdash* require proper disposal.

## Shape & Symbolism

### Lion Shape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַהֵיכָל צַר מֵאַחֲרָיו, וְרָחָב מִלְּפָנָיו, וְדוֹמֶה לַאֲרִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (ישעיה כט), הוֹי אֲרִיאֵל אֲרִיאֵל קִרְיַת חָנָה דָוִד, מָה הָאֲרִי צַר מֵאַחֲרָיו וְרָחָב מִלְּפָנָיו, אַף הַהֵיכָל צַר מֵאַחֲרָיו וְרָחָב מִלְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Hekhal was narrow behind and broad in front, resembling a lion, as it says, "Ah, Ariel, Ariel, the city where David encamped" (Isaiah 29:1): Just as a lion is narrow behind and broad in front, so the Hekhal was narrow behind and broad in front.

*VeHaHeichal tzar me'achorav verachav milfanav* — the *heikhal* was narrow at the rear and wide at the front. This is structurally obvious from the fact that the *ulam* projected well beyond the footprint of the main building on both the north and south sides. The Mishnah draws an explicit comparison: *vedomeh le'ari* — it resembled a lion. The proof-text is the verse in *Yeshayahu*: *hoy Ariel kiryat chanah David* — "Ariel" meaning the lion of God. Just as a lion is narrow at its hindquarters and broad at its chest and mane, so too the *heikhal* was narrow at the back and broad at the front.

### Azarah Layout

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הָעֲזָרָה הָיְתָה אֹרֶךְ מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים וָשֶׁבַע עַל רֹחַב מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ. מִן הַמִּזְרָח לַמַּעֲרָב מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים וָשֶׁבַע, מְקוֹם דְּרִיסַת יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, מְקוֹם דְּרִיסַת הַכֹּהֲנִים אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, הַמִּזְבֵּחַ שְׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם, בֵּין הָאוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם אַמָּה, הַהֵיכָל מֵאָה אַמָּה, וְאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה לַאֲחוֹרֵי בֵית הַכַּפֹּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The whole of the courtyard was a hundred and eighty-seven cubits long by a hundred and thirty-five broad. From east to west it was a hundred and eighty-seven. The space in which the Israelites could go was eleven cubits. The space in which the priests could go was eleven cubits. The altar took up thirty-two. Between the Porch and the altar was twenty-two cubits. The Hekhal took up a hundred cubits, and there were eleven cubits behind the kapporet.

With this the fourth perek concludes, and the fifth and final perek begins. *Kol ha'azarah hayetah orech me'ah ushmonim vesheva al rochav me'ah ushloshim vechamesh* — the entire *azarah* was one hundred and eighty-seven *amot* long (east-west) by one hundred and thirty-five *amot* wide (north-south).

The east-to-west breakdown accounts for the 187 as follows: eleven *amot* for the space where *Yisrael* stood (*makom drisat raglei Yisrael*); eleven for the space of the *kohanim* before the *mizbeach*; thirty-two for the *mizbeach* itself; twenty-two for the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbeach*; one hundred for the *heikhal* structure (as already measured); and eleven more *amot* behind the *heikhal* to the outer rear wall — totaling one hundred and eighty-seven.

The north-to-south breakdown of 135 *amot* begins not from the outer wall but from the *kevesh* (the ramp of the *mizbeach*): sixty-two *amot* from the *kevesh* to the *mizbeach*. A note on the arithmetic: the *mizbeach* base was thirty *amot* and the *kevesh* ramp was thirty-two *amot* in length, yet the Mishnah gives sixty-two rather than sixty-four. The *Rambam* explains that while the diagonal length of the ramp is thirty-two *amot*, its horizontal base projection is only thirty *amot*, and together with the thirty-two *amot* for the *mizbeach* one arrives at sixty-two. From the *mizbeach* to the *taba'ot* (the rings used for slaughtering *korbanot*) was eight *amot*, reaching seventy. The space of the *taba'ot* was twenty-four *amot*, reaching ninety-four. From the *taba'ot* to the *shulchanot* (the stone tables used in the preparation of *korbanot*) was four *amot*, reaching ninety-eight. From the *shulchanot* to the *nanasim* (low platforms or stands) was four more, reaching one hundred and two. The *shulchanot* themselves are interestingly not given their own measurement in the Mishnah's tally; one suggestion is that the tables were portable and moved as needed. The remaining difference between what has been counted and 135 was accounted for by the gap between the outer wall and the *kevesh*, and the space occupied by the *nanasim* themselves.

## Six Chambers

### North Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁשׁ לְשָׁכוֹת הָיוּ בָעֲזָרָה, שָׁלֹשׁ בַּצָּפוֹן וְשָׁלֹשׁ בַּדָּרוֹם. שֶׁבַּצָּפוֹן, לִשְׁכַּת הַמֶּלַח, לִשְׁכַּת הַפַּרְוָה, לִשְׁכַּת הַמְדִיחִים. לִשְׁכַּת הַמֶּלַח, שָׁם הָיוּ נוֹתְנִים מֶלַח לַקָּרְבָּן. לִשְׁכַּת הַפַּרְוָה, שָׁם הָיוּ מוֹלְחִין עוֹרוֹת קָדָשִׁים, וְעַל גַּגָּהּ הָיָה בֵית הַטְּבִילָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** There were six chambers in the courtyard, three on the north and three on the south. On the north were the salt chamber, the parvah chamber and the washer's chamber. In the salt chamber they used to keep the salt for the offerings. In the parvah chamber they used to salt the skins of the animal-offerings. On its roof was the bath used by the high priest on Yom Kippur.

Having presented the overall dimensions of the *azarah*, the Mishnah turns to the six *lishkot* — chambers — built within it. These are distinct from all the *lishkot* mentioned in previous tractates (which were associated with gates or situated between the *ulam* and the *mizbeach* or beyond); the six described here were located in the area of *Ezrat Yisrael* and *Ezrat Kohanim*. Three stood on the north side and three on the south. There are variant readings (*girsaot*) among the commentators as to which chambers are north and which south, and the diagrams in some editions reflect a different arrangement than the Mishnah text.

The first northern chamber was *Lishkat HaMelach* — the salt chamber. Salt was applied to every *korban* before it was offered, and this was the room where the salt supply was maintained. The second was *Lishkat HaParvah*. The name *Parvah* is explained differently by different authorities: the *Bartenura* records a tradition that a sorcerer named Parvah built this chamber through witchcraft; the *Rambam* connects the name either to the word *parim* (bulls) or to the account of a *Magushi* (Persian sorcerer) who bored through the wall of the *azarah* in this chamber in order to observe the *avodah* and was killed for it, causing the chamber to be named after him. In any case, *Lishkat HaParvah* was the room where the skins (*orot*) of the *kodashim* were salted to preserve them for the *kohanim*, who received the hides as part of their portion. On its roof was the *mikva* (*beit tevilah*) used by the *kohen gadol* for his immersions on *Yom Kippur*.

### Madichim Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִשְׁכַּת הַמְדִיחִין, שֶׁשָּׁם הָיוּ מְדִיחִין קִרְבֵי הַקָּדָשִׁים. וּמִשָּׁם מְסִבָּה עוֹלָה לְגַג בֵּית הַפַּרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the washers' chamber they used to wash the entrails of the sacrificial animals, and from it a winding way went up to the roof of the parvah chamber.

The third northern chamber was *Lishkat HaMadichim* — the rinsing chamber, where the innards (*kirvei kadshei kodashim*) of the *korbanot* were washed out. Rinsing the viscera was done indoors in a designated chamber rather than in the open court precisely because it was a particularly unpleasant task. From *Lishkat HaMadichim* there was also a *mesiba* — a stairway or inclined passage — providing access to the roof of *Beit HaParvah*, which is where the *kohen gadol*'s *mikva* was located. It is striking that the route to the *kohen gadol*'s place of immersion ran through the room used for rinsing animal innards.

### South Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּדָּרוֹם, לִשְׁכַּת הָעֵץ, לִשְׁכַּת הַגּוֹלָה, לִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית. לִשְׁכַּת הָעֵץ, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, שָׁכַחְתִּי מֶה הָיְתָה מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר, לִשְׁכַּת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְהִיא הָיְתָה אֲחוֹרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן, וְגַג שְׁלָשְׁתָּן שָׁוֶה. לִשְׁכַּת הַגּוֹלָה, שָׁם הָיָה בוֹר קָבוּעַ, וְהַגַּלְגַּל נָתוּן עָלָיו, וּמִשָּׁם מַסְפִּיקִים מַיִם לְכָל הָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the south were the wood chamber, the chamber of the exile and the chamber of hewn stones. The wood chamber: Rabbi Eliezer ben Jacob says: I forget what it was used for. Abba Shaul says: It was the chamber of the high priest, and it was behind the two of them, and one roof covered all three. In the chamber of the exile there was a fixed cistern, with a wheel over it, and from there water was provided for all of the courtyard.

The three southern chambers were *Lishkat HaEtz*, *Lishkat HaGolah*, and *Lishkat HaGazit*. Regarding *Lishkat HaEtz* — the wood chamber — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov himself says *shakachti mah hayta meshameshet* — "I have forgotten what it was used for." This is a notable admission, and the repeated appearances of this phrase throughout the tractate are understood to signal that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov was the primary source of the Mishnah's architectural information; his acknowledged lapses of memory are therefore particularly significant. Abba Shaul, rather than answering the question of function, offers an alternative name: he calls it *Lishkat Kohen Gadol*, the chamber of the *kohen gadol*. The *Rosh* clarifies that Abba Shaul is not disagreeing about its function but simply providing a different name — no one, in fact, specifies what this chamber was used for. According to the text, all three southern chambers shared a common roof (*begag sheloshtan shaveh*).

*Lishkat HaGolah* contained a fixed well (*bor kavua*) fitted with a wheel or pulley (*galgal*), from which water was drawn to supply the entire *azarah*. The enormous quantities of water required — for rinsing the floor, for the *kohanim*, for various aspects of the *avodah* — were met through this well. The name *HaGolah* is explained either as a reference to the *bnei hagolah*, the returning exiles who dug it, or as a word related to *gal*, a wheel — the defining feature of the well's apparatus.

### Sanhedrin Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית, שָׁם הָיְתָה סַנְהֶדְרִי גְדוֹלָה שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹשֶׁבֶת וְדָנָה אֶת הַכְּהֻנָּה, וְכֹהֵן שֶׁנִּמְצָא בוֹ פְסוּל, לוֹבֵשׁ שְׁחוֹרִים וּמִתְעַטֵּף שְׁחוֹרִים, וְיוֹצֵא וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ. וְשֶׁלֹּא נִמְצָא בוֹ פְסוּל, לוֹבֵשׁ לְבָנִים וּמִתְעַטֵּף לְבָנִים, נִכְנָס וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the chamber of hewn stone the great Sanhedrin of Israel used to sit and judge the priesthood. A priest in whom was found a disqualification used to put on black garments and wrap himself in black and go away. One in whom no disqualification was found used to put on white garments and wrap himself in white and go in and serve along with his brother priests.

*Lishkat HaGazit* — the chamber of hewn stone — was the seat of the *Sanhedrin HaGedolah*, the supreme court of Israel. Since the *halacha* prohibits sitting in the *azarah* (except for kings of the Davidic dynasty), the *Sanhedrin* sat in the portion of *Lishkat HaGazit* that opened outward, toward the non-sacred side, while the inner portion of the chamber retained the *kedushah* of the *azarah*. One of the central functions of the *Sanhedrin* in this setting was *vedana et hakehunah* — adjudicating questions of priestly lineage and status.

## Kohanim Certification

### Disqualified Kohen

When a *kohen* was investigated and found to have a *psul* — a disqualifying flaw in his lineage, rendering him a *chalal* (the offspring of a forbidden priestly union) — the consequence was immediate and visible. *Lovesh shachorim umit'atef shachorim* — he would dress in black garments and wrap himself in a black shawl. *Yotze veholech lo* — and he would depart, his service in the *Beit HaMikdash* at an end.

### Qualified Kohen

By contrast, a *kohen* who underwent investigation and was found to have no *psul* in his lineage received the opposite treatment. *Lovesh levanim umit'atef levanim* — he dressed in white garments and wrapped himself in a white shawl. *Nichnas umeshamesh im echav hakohanim* — he then entered and served alongside his brother *kohanim*, his status confirmed and his right to *avodah* affirmed.

### Aaron's Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹם טוֹב הָיוּ עוֹשִׂים, שֶׁלֹּא נִמְצָא פְסוּל בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן, וְכָךְ הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים, בָּרוּךְ הַמָּקוֹם בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁלֹּא נִמְצָא פְסוּל בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן. וּבָרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁבָּחַר בְּאַהֲרֹן וּבְבָנָיו לַעֲמֹד לְשָׁרֵת לִפְנֵי ה' בְּבֵית קָדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** They used to make a feast because no blemish had been found in the seed of Aaron the priest, and they used to say: Blessed is the Omnipresent, blessed is He, for no blemish has been found in the seed of Aaron. Blessed is He who chose Aaron and his sons to stand to minister before the Lord in the Holy of Holies.

When a period had passed without any *psul* being discovered among those examined, a celebration was made. *Veyom tov hayu osim shelo nimtza psul bezaro shel Aharon hakohen* — they would make a festival day rejoicing that no blemish had been found in the lineage of Aaron the *kohen*. And this is the blessing with which the tractate concludes: *Baruch hamakom baruch hu shelo nimtza psul bezaro shel Aharon, uvaruch hu shebachar be'Aharon uvezaro la'amod ulesharit lifnei Hashem beveit kodesh hakodashim* — Blessed is the Omnipresent, blessed is He, that no blemish was found in the seed of Aaron; and blessed is He who chose Aaron and his descendants to stand and serve before God in the *beit kodesh hakodashim*.

With this the tractate of *Middot* is concluded, and with it the entire order of *Kodshim*.
