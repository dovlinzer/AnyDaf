# Bava Kamma 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bor: Yishmael vs. Akiva

### Pit Domain Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד אַחֵר – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who digs** part of **a pit on private property and opens its** entrance **in the public domain, or** digs a pit **in the public domain and opens its** entrance **on private property,** or digs a pit **on private property and opens its** entrance **on another** person's **private property,** is **liable** for damage caused by the pit in each case.

The bulk of this *daf* — indeed, the remainder of this entire *perek* — focuses our attention squarely on *bor*, the category of the pit, which until now has not received sustained analysis in terms of its biblical sources. The *mishna* had established that a *bor* is defined by where its opening lies, and that whether its opening is in *reshut harabim* (the public domain) or in *reshut hayachid* (the private domain), liability — *chiyuv* — applies. Our natural intuition, of course, is that a *bor b'reshut harabim* represents a far more serious public hazard than one in *reshut hayachid*, and that liability in the public domain should be far more obvious and severe. The *Gemara* now reveals, however, that there was a significant tannaitic dispute on exactly this question.

### Yishmael: Public Domain…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – חַיָּיב, וְזֶהוּ בּוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who digs a pit on private property and opens its** entrance **in the public domain, or** who digs a pit **in the public domain and opens its** entrance **on private property** is **liable, and this is** the case of **Pit that is stated in the Torah;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The *Gemara* opens with a *braita*: one who digs a pit in the private domain and opens it into the public domain — or, according to an alternative reading, digs in the public domain and opens it into the private domain — is *chayav*. This, declares Rabbi Yishmael, is the *bor* spoken of in the Torah. His position, on its face, seems to center the liability on the pit's relationship to *reshut harabim*.

### Akiva: Including Private…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הִפְקִיר רְשׁוּתוֹ וְלֹא הִפְקִיר בּוֹרוֹ – זֶהוּ בּוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** With regard to one who **renounced ownership of his property but did not renounce ownership of his pit** located in the property, **this is** the case of **Pit that is stated in the Torah.**

Rabbi Akiva offers a different formulation entirely: the *bor* referenced in the Torah is the case where one *hifkir reshuto v'lo hifkir boro* — one who renounces ownership over the surrounding domain while retaining ownership of the pit itself. That is, if a person digs a well in his private domain and then declares the surrounding land *hefker*, granting the public access to that space, while still holding onto ownership of the well — that is the *bor* the Torah is addressing. Rabbi Akiva is thus pointing to a *bor b'reshut hayachid* that has been rendered a public hazard through the owner's deliberate act of opening up access. Each *tanna*, then, appears to be championing a different type of *bor* as the paradigmatic Torah case.

### Rabba's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּבוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמִיחַיַּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִפְתַּח״ וְ״כִי יִכְרֶה״ – אִם עַל פְּתִיחָה חַיָּיב, עַל כְּרִיָּיה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! אֶלָּא שֶׁעַל עִסְקֵי פְּתִיחָה וְעַל עִסְקֵי כְּרִיָּיה בָּאָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In explanation of this dispute, **Rabba says: With regard to a pit** that a person digs or opens **into the public domain, everyone agrees that he is liable** for damage that occurs as a result. **What is the reason? The verse states:** "And **if** a man **shall open** a pit," **and** it also states: **"If** a man **shall dig** a pit" (Exodus 21:33), which raises the question: **If** one is **liable for opening** a pit by removing the cover of a pit that has already been dug, then is it **not all the more so** obvious that he should be liable **for digging** a new pit? What, then, does the latter phrase add? **Rather,** the interpretation of the verse must be that the responsibility for the pit **comes to him by engaging in opening** the pit **and by engaging in digging** the pit. Although he does not own the area itself, he is liable for creating a public hazard.

Rabba steps in to clarify that the debate is not quite as binary as it appears. He argues that regarding a *bor b'reshut harabim*, there is no dispute whatsoever — *kuli alma lo peligi d'chayav*. No authority would claim that one who digs a pit in the middle of a public thoroughfare is exempt. Rabba's proof is from the language of the *pesukim* themselves.

### Torah's Dual Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּבוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ נָמֵי חַיָּיב, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּעַל הַבּוֹר״ – בְּבוֹר דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּעָלִים קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא,
>
> **Translation:** in the case **where** one dug **the pit on his** own **property** and then renounced ownership of the surrounding area. Consequently, the entire area except for the pit is now open to public passage. In this case, **Rabbi Akiva maintains** that one who digs **a pit on his** own **property** is **also liable,** not only when the pit is located in the public domain, **as it is written: "The owner of the pit** shall pay" (Exodus 21:34). Clearly, **the Merciful One is referring to a pit that has an owner.** Therefore, the owner of the pit pays even if it is located on private property.

The Torah says: *ki yiftach ish bor o ki yichreh ish bor* — "if a man opens a pit, or if a man digs a pit." Rabba asks: if one is already liable for merely *opening* an existing pit, is it not all the more obvious that one is liable for actually *digging* one? Why, then, does the Torah need to state both *ki yiftach* and *ki yichreh*? The answer is that the dual formulation teaches that the conceptual basis of liability is the act of creation — digging and opening the hazard — not the fact of ownership. The emphasis falls on *al askei pticha v'al askei kriya ba'almah*: the liability attaches to the acts of opening and digging themselves. On this foundational principle, Rabba contends, all opinions concur; the debate *lo nechlaku ela b'bor b'reshuto* — the dispute is exclusively about whether one is also *chayav* for a pit situated within one's own domain.

Rabbi Yishmael holds that the phrase *ba'al habor* — "the owner of the pit" — does not establish ownership as an independent basis for liability. It simply means the one responsible for creating the hazard. If a person merely owns a pit on his property and it is not a genuine public hazard, he bears no liability. Why would he be *patur*? Rashi explains that when someone digs a pit in his yard and then makes the surrounding area *hefker*, there is an implicit stipulation: you are granting access to the area, but not in a manner that creates liability toward those who enter. You gave over the land, but only with the implicit understanding that falling into your well remains your own risk as the visitor. Tosafot, however, finds this explanation too narrow, since one might also imagine a case where the pit is dug right at the property line, adjacent to *reshut harabim*, without any formal declaration of *hefker*. In that scenario, someone walking in the public domain could easily slip into the pit. Tosafot therefore explains Rabbi Yishmael's exemption differently: even in such a case, one can say to the person who fell, this is my property — it is your responsibility to keep your distance. Whether the pit is surrounded by land I formally made *hefker*, or simply sits at my property line, the well belongs to me, and a person bears the responsibility not to encroach upon another's property. It is conceptually similar to the argument *shor b'reshusi mah ba'ei* — what business did you have in my pit?

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that *ba'al habor yeshallem* — the owner of the pit shall pay — indicates that the Torah explicitly contemplates a case of ownership, and that the owner remains *chayav* even in this scenario. The pit is still a hazard when people can walk right up to its edge, and Rabbi Akiva holds him liable.

### Akiva's Textual Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: בַּעַל הַתַּקָּלָה. אֶלָּא מַאי ״זֶהוּ בּוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה״ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? זֶהוּ בּוֹר שֶׁפָּתַח בּוֹ הַכָּתוּב תְּחִלָּה לְתַשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that "the owner of the pit" means that **the one responsible for the hazard** is liable even if he dug the pit in the public domain and then renounced ownership of it. But one who digs a pit on his own property is not liable. The Gemara asks: **Rather,** according to Rabba's explanation, **what** is meant by the clause: **This is** the case of **Pit that is stated in the Torah,** in reference to digging a pit on one's property, **that Rabbi Akiva states?** According to him, one is liable for damage in all cases, including digging in the public domain. The Gemara answers: It means that **this is the pit** with **which the verse initially introduces** the subject **of compensation** for damage caused. In that context, the Torah states: "The owner of the pit shall pay" (Exodus 21:34), indicating that even a pit owned on one's private property renders one liable.

The *Gemara* then addresses a pointed question: when Rabbi Akiva says *zehu bor ha'amur batorah* — "this is the pit spoken of in the Torah" — using the emphatic and seemingly exclusive term *zehu*, does he mean to reject Rabbi Yishmael's case entirely? The answer is no. *Zehu bor shepattach bo hakatuv techilla l'tashlumim* — Rabbi Akiva is saying that the pit one owns is the one the Torah opens with when it speaks of payment and liability, invoking *ba'al habor*. He is not excluding Rabbi Yishmael's case; he is simply asserting that the Torah's language about payment necessarily encompasses the case of ownership. The two positions are not mutually exclusive: Rabbi Yishmael's framework excludes nothing of Rabbi Akiva's, and Rabbi Akiva's *zehu* is not intended to negate Rabbi Yishmael's case either. Each *tanna* is identifying a case that the Torah addresses, not denying the validity of the other's case.

### Rav Yosef: Ownership Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּבוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמִחַיַּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? ״בַּעַל הַבּוֹר״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – בְּבוֹר דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּעָלִים עָסְקִינַן. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּבוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים נָמֵי חַיָּיב, דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִפְתַּח״ וְ״כִי יִכְרֶה״ – אִם עַל פְּתִיחָה חַיָּיב, עַל כְּרִיָּיה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֶלָּא שֶׁעַל עִסְקֵי פְתִיחָה וְעַל עִסְקֵי כְרִיָּיה בָּאָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef said** that one can explain the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva differently: **With regard to** one who dug **a pit on** his **private property** and then renounced ownership of the property aside from the pit, **everyone agrees that he is liable. What is the reason?** Since **the Merciful One states: "The owner of the pit,"** it indicates that **we are dealing with a pit that has an owner.** **When they disagree,** it is in a case **where the pit** was dug **in the public domain. Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that one is **also liable** for **a pit** dug **in the public domain, as it is written:** "And **if** a man **shall open** a pit," and: **"If** a man **shall dig** a pit" (Exodus 21:33), which raises the question: **If** he is **liable for opening** a pit by removing the cover of a pit that has already been dug, then is it **not all the more so** obvious that he should be liable **for digging** a new pit? **Rather,** the verse means that the responsibility for the pit **comes to him by engaging in opening** the pit **and by engaging in digging** the pit. Although he does not own the area itself, he is liable for creating a public hazard.

Rav Yosef takes the opposite approach from Rabba. Whereas Rabba holds that the conceptual foundation is the creation of a hazard — which maps more readily onto a model of *adam hamazzik*, causation by a person — Rav Yosef holds that fundamentally it is a question of ownership, a model of *mamon hamazzik*, one's property causing damage. Given that starting point, the paradigmatic *bor* in the Torah is, counterintuitively, the owned pit. *Bor b'reshut hayachid kuli alma lo peligi d'michayiv*: everyone agrees, says Rav Yosef, that as long as the pit is situated in a place where people can actually reach it, liability is established by ownership. His proof: *ba'al habor amar rachmana* — the Torah says "the owner of the pit," and so *b'bor d'it lei ba'alim askinan*, we are dealing with a pit that has an owner.

On this reading, the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is over whether one is also *chayav* when the pit is dug in *reshut harabim* — where, by definition, one has no ownership. Rabbi Yishmael says *bor b'reshut harabim nami chayav*, because of the verse *ki yiftach o ki yichreh* — if mere opening creates liability, certainly digging does, and this teaches that there is a basis for liability independent of ownership. Rabbi Akiva responds: the Torah needed both *ki yiftach* and *ki yichreh*, not to inform us about liability without ownership, but for a different reason altogether. Had the Torah only written *ki yiftach*, one might have thought that for an opened pit, covering it is sufficient to discharge one's obligation — and that is all one ever needs to do. And had the Torah only written *ki yichreh*, one might have thought the opposite: that for a dug pit, covering it is not sufficient, and one must actually refill it. The Torah therefore writes both: to tell us that even the digger is discharged by covering — *lo sagi lei b'chisuy ad d'tamyei*, it is not required to refill — and that even the opener bears full liability despite not having created the pit from scratch. The dual language is not teaching liability without ownership; it is teaching the parameters of *chisuy* as a discharge of obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – הָנְהוּ מִיצְרָךְ צְרִיכִי; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כִּי יִפְתַּח״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: פּוֹתֵחַ הוּא דְּסַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּכִסּוּי, כּוֹרֶה לָא סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּכִסּוּי עַד דְּטָאֵים לֵיהּ. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כִּי יִכְרֶה״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כְּרִיָּיה הוּא דְּבָעֵי כִּסּוּי, מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד מַעֲשֶׂה; אֲבָל פּוֹתֵחַ – דְּלָא עֲבַד מַעֲשֶׂה, אֵימָא כִּסּוּי נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva,** by contrast, holds that one is liable only in the case of a pit located on private property, where he renounced ownership of the property aside from the pit, and one cannot derive otherwise from any extraneous phrases. This is because **these** phrases **are necessary, for if the Merciful One wrote** only: **"If** a man **shall open** a pit" (Exodus 21:33), **I would say** that **it is** specifically when **one opens** the covering of a pit that has already been dug **that it is sufficient for him to** place **a covering** on it to absolve himself of responsibility for damage it causes. This is the case where the Torah states: "And not cover it" (Exodus 21:33). But if one actually **digs** a pit, it **would not be sufficient** to place **a covering** on it, and one would not be exempt from damages **unless he** completely **packs it.** **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** only: **"If** a man **shall dig," I would say** that **it is** specifically in a case of **digging that a covering is required** to exempt one from liability, **because he performed an action** of creating the pit. **But** for merely **opening** it, **where one has not performed an action, say** that he **does not require a covering** to absolve him of liability. Therefore, the Torah **teaches us** that he is liable for damage caused by a pit in both cases, and one should interpret the verse that states: "The owner of the pit" (Exodus 21:34), in its plain sense, i.e., that he is the owner of the pit because it is located on his private property. Accordingly, one would not be liable for a pit dug in the public domain.

The result is startling: according to Rabbi Akiva as understood by Rav Yosef, one who digs a pit in the middle of *reshut harabim* — creating an obvious and severe public danger — is nonetheless *patur*, because one has no ownership connection to that pit. This is admittedly counterintuitive, and the *Gemara* itself acknowledges the formalism at work. As was discussed earlier in the tractate in the context of *hefker nezikav*, negligence or fault alone is not sufficient; one requires either a direct act or a recognized formal category to establish liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי ״זֶהוּ בּוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה״ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? זֶהוּ בּוֹר שֶׁפָּתַח בּוֹ הַכָּתוּב תְּחִלָּה לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But rather,** according to Rav Yosef's explanation, **what** is meant by the clause: **This is** the case of **Pit that is stated in the Torah,** in reference to digging a pit in the public domain, **that Rabbi Yishmael states?** Isn't one liable for any pit, including one dug on his own property? The Gemara answers: It means that **this is the pit** with **which the verse initially introduces** the subject **of damage** caused by Pit, as explained by Rabbi Yishmael above. Only afterward does it state: "The owner of the pit shall pay," referring to a pit dug on one's own property.

This also raises a deeper structural question that is worth pausing over. According to Rav Yosef's framework, when Rabbi Yishmael says one is *chayav* for a *bor b'reshut harabim*, is he working with two distinct conceptual models — ownership for the private domain case, causation for the public domain case — or is he extending a single model of ownership to encompass the public domain case as well? One will recall a principle cited earlier in the tractate: *shnei devarim einam birshuso shel adam v'asaan hakatuv k'ilu hen birshuso* — two things a person does not actually own, and yet the Torah treats them as if he does: *chametz b'Pesach* and *bor b'reshut harabim*. On that reading, Rabbi Yishmael's liability for a *bor b'reshut harabim* is itself grounded in a constructive ownership the Torah imposes, and there is only one conceptual model — ownership — that operates throughout.

## Braita: Domain Variations

### Braita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – פָּטוּר; וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂים חָלָל תַּחַת רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹת, שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וּפְתָחוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חַיָּיב. וְהַחוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד הַסְּמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ הַחוֹפְרִים לְאוּשִּׁין – פָּטוּר. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה מְחִיצָה עֲשָׂרָה, אוֹ עַד שֶׁיַּרְחִיק מִמְּקוֹם דְּרִיסַת רַגְלֵי אָדָם וּמִמְּקוֹם דְּרִיסַת רַגְלֵי בְהֵמָה – אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who digs a pit** whose hollow space is **in the public domain and** then **opens its** entrance **into private property** is **exempt, and** this is the *halakha* **even though he does not have permission to do so.** The reason he does not have permission to do so is **that one may not create a hollow** space **under the public domain.** **One who digs pits, ditches, and caves on private property and** then **opens their** entrances **into the public domain** is **liable.** In addition, one **who digs pits on private property that is adjacent to the public domain** but not actually located in the public domain itself, **such as these** people **who dig foundations [*ushin*]** for their houses or walls, is **exempt. But Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems** one **liable** even for damage caused by pits made to lay foundations, **unless he builds a partition** that is **ten** handbreadths high in front of the pits, **or unless he distances** them by **four handbreadths from the area where pedestrians walk and from the area where animals walk.**

The *Gemara* now turns to test these positions against a *braita*. The *braita* presents several cases. First: one who digs a pit in *reshut harabim* and opens it into *reshut hayachid* — this person is *patur*, and explicitly so, even though he acted unlawfully, since *ein osim chalal tachat reshut harabim* — one has no right to hollow out a cavity beneath public ground. Nonetheless, since the pit's opening is in *reshut hayachid*, there is no liability for injuries that occur. Second: one who digs wells, canals, and other pit-types in *reshut hayachid* and opens them into *reshut harabim* — this person is *chayav*. The *braita* thus appears to ground liability squarely in *reshut harabim*.

### Edge Case: Foundation

The *braita* then introduces a more complex case: one who digs a pit in *reshut hayachid* situated immediately adjacent to *reshut harabim* — such as those who dig foundations (*ushin*) for a building. The *tanna kamma* holds such a person *patur*. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda, however, holds him *chayav* unless he erects a barrier ten *tefachim* high, or keeps the pit set back from the place where people actually walk by at least four *tefachim* — or, as some versions of the text have it, four *amot*, which arguably makes more practical sense. On Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda's view, the proximity to *reshut harabim* renders the excavation a genuine public hazard regardless of its technical location within one's private property, and the construction purpose does not excuse the owner from guarding against damage.

This creates an internal tension in the *braita*: the first part seems to exempt a pit opening in *reshut hayachid*, while the final portion seems to hold a *reshut hayachid* pit — the foundation case — liable.

### Rabba's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי? בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבָּה – רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **Granted, according to Rabba, the first clause,** where one is seemingly exempt for opening a pit onto private property even if he subsequently renounced ownership of the area, can be established in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, and the last clause** can be established in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who maintains that one is liable for a pit dug on his own property if it is open to public traffic.

Rabba resolves this tension by reading the *braita* as representing two different tannaitic positions. The opening of the *braita*, which exempts the *reshut hayachid* pit, reflects Rabbi Yishmael's position — consistent with Rabba's framework that Rabbi Yishmael holds one *chayav* only in *reshut harabim*. The concluding portion, which renders the edge-of-domain foundation case *chayav*, reflects Rabbi Akiva's position. The *braita* is thus split between two *tannaim*. This is, as the *Gemara* itself acknowledges, not an ideal resolution — one generally prefers not to split a single *braita* between two conflicting opinions — but it at least accounts for the text.

### Rav Yosef Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב יוֹסֵף, בִּשְׁלָמָא סֵיפָא – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל; אֶלָּא רֵישָׁא מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְלָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב יוֹסֵף: כּוּלַּהּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא, וְרֵישָׁא – שֶׁלֹּא הִפְקִיר לֹא רְשׁוּתוֹ וְלֹא בּוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rav Yosef's** explanation, **granted,** with regard to **the last clause, all agree** that this is the *halakha*. **But** with regard to **the first clause,** which exempts one who digs a pit on his own property, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this?** It appears to be **neither** that of **Rabbi Yishmael nor** that of **Rabbi Akiva,** since Rav Yosef explained that both of them maintain that one is liable for a pit on his own property, even if he subsequently renounces ownership of the area. The Gemara answers that **Rav Yosef** could have **said to you: Everyone agrees** with the **entire** passage. As for **the first clause,** which exempts one who digs a pit under the public domain and opens it onto his own property, it is referring to a case **where one did not renounce ownership of either his property or his pit.** This certainly is not included in the category of Pit with regard to damages, and the owner is therefore exempt.

Rav Yosef's reading is more elegant but faces its own challenge. Rav Yosef holds that everyone agrees one is *chayav* for *bor b'reshut hayachid*, which means the beginning of the *braita* — explicitly exempting a pit opened into *reshut hayachid* — seems to flatly contradict his position. *Lo Rabbi Yishmael v'lo Rabbi Akiva* — the opening of this *braita*, as he reads it, does not conform to any known position. Rav Yosef's answer: *kulah divrei hakol*, the entire *braita* can be read as consistent with all opinions. The beginning of the *braita*, he says, is not speaking of a pit at the edge of the domain — it is speaking of a pit opened in the middle of one's yard, surrounded by private property to which no one has access. In that situation, *patur* is obvious, because there is simply no liability when the public cannot reach the pit. It is not an exemption based on the fact that it is *reshut hayachid* as such, but rather an exemption because no hazard was created in the first place. The person did not make the surrounding area *hefker*, the pit is inaccessible, and the public has no claim upon it. Rashi understands the *chidush* of this case to be that even though the digging was done illegally — since one dug a cavity beneath *reshut harabim* — that illegality does not translate into tort liability; the two questions are separate.

### Rav Assi's Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאוֹקֵימְתַּהּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל, לְרַבָּה נָמֵי לָא תּוֹקְמַהּ כְּתַנָּאֵי – מִדְּרֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל; וְטַעְמָא דִּלְאוּשִּׁין, הָא לָאו לְאוּשִּׁין חַיָּיב – כְּגוֹן דְּאַרְוַוח אַרְווֹחֵי לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Now that you have interpreted Rav Yosef's** explanation of the *baraita* as being **in accordance with all** opinions, then **also according to Rabba,** you are **not** required to **establish it as** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. Rather, one can say that** **since the first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** who exempts one who renounces ownership of his property but not the pit, **the last clause** can **also** be explained in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael. And** it was previously inferred that **the reason** for the exemption when digging on one's private property adjacent to the public domain is that one digs **to** lay **foundations. But** if it is **not to** lay **foundations,** one is **liable. For instance,** in a case **where one widened** the pit unnecessarily, extending it **into the public domain,** he has effectively dug a pit in the public domain itself.

Rav Assi then observes that if Rav Yosef's reading of the *braita* — *divrei hakol* — is accepted, there is no longer any need for Rabba to read the *braita* as split between two *tannaim* either. The beginning of the *braita*, which Rabba read as Rabbi Yishmael's position, can now be read according to all opinions: *patur* because the pit is inaccessible, sitting in the middle of an enclosed private yard. And the foundation case at the end, which holds the person *chayav*, is likewise comprehensible to all, because — on Rabba's reading — the person crossed the property line and the excavation encroached into *reshut harabim*.

### Ushin Exemption

The *braita*'s exemption for the foundation case (*ushin*) requires some explanation, since fundamentally a pit adjacent to *reshut harabim* would appear to be a hazard. The *Gemara* suggests that the exemption is special and narrow: it represents a degree of latitude given to builders who need to excavate foundations near their property line, recognizing the practical necessity of construction. This is admittedly not the plain reading of the *braita*, which does not foreground the foundation purpose as a condition of exemption. As the *Gemara* frankly acknowledges, reading this as a unique exemption for building foundations is somewhat forced, and the *braita* taken at face value simply says one is *patur* for a pit adjacent to *reshut harabim*. A second *braita* raises the same issue from a different angle, explicitly holding *patur* for a pit in *reshut hayachid* even when it is right at the boundary with *reshut harabim* — which creates further pressure on Rav Yosef's position. Rav Yosef again answers that the foundation exemption applies, and the apparent leniency is a special dispensation. That this is not straightforward *pshat* in the *braita* is acknowledged; the *Gemara* has nonetheless found a workable resolution.

## Nechunya & Donation

### Donation Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָפַר וּפָתַח וּמָסַר לָרַבִּים – פָּטוּר. חָפַר וּפָתַח וְלֹא מָסַר לָרַבִּים – חַיָּיב, וְכֵן מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל נְחוּנְיָא חוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹת שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת, שֶׁהָיָה חוֹפֵר וּפוֹתֵחַ וּמוֹסֵר לָרַבִּים. וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: קִיֵּים זֶה הֲלָכָה זוֹ. הֲלָכָה זוֹ וְתוּ לָא?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אַף הֲלָכָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If someone **dug or opened** a well **and transferred** it **to the public** for their use, he is **exempt** from damage caused by the well. If **he dug or opened** a well **and did not transfer** it **to the public,** he is **liable. And this** was **the practice of Neḥunya the digger of pits, ditches, and caves, who would dig, open, and transfer** them **to the public** so that there would be wells of water for public use. **When the Sages heard about the matter, they said: This** individual **has fulfilled this *halakha*.** The Gemara asks: Only **this *halakha* and no more?** Isn't it well known that Neḥunya was a great man? **Rather, say:** He fulfilled **even this *halakha*** and did not forget to transfer the wells to the public.

A new *braita* is introduced: *chafar u'patach u'masar l'rabim* — one who digs a pit, opens it, and then donates it to the community, is *patur*. Once the pit is formally transferred to the public, the original owner's liability dissolves, because the pit is no longer his; it now belongs to the *rabim* and is their responsibility. If, however, he dug and opened but did not transfer ownership — *chayav*. This case assumes that the pit is situated in a place where it constitutes a genuine hazard to the public, and the distinction turns entirely on whether the owner retained or relinquished his connection to the pit.

Rashi explains the act of donation: the person says to the members of his community that this well which he dug is available for their use — for collecting rainwater, for watering animals — and he formally makes it over to them. There are genuine questions about the mechanism of this transfer: is there a formal *kinyan* required, or is the verbal declaration sufficient? And what if the community never sought or accepted the gift? These questions are not resolved in the *Gemara* here, though they are worth noting.

### Nechunya Practice

The *braita* continues: *v'chen minhago shel Nechunya chofer shichin u-me'arot* — this was the regular practice of Nechunya the digger of wells and canals. He would dig, open, and donate every pit to the public, and thus fulfill the requirements of this *halakha*. When the Sages heard of this, they said *kiyem zeh halakha zu* — this person fulfilled this *halakha*. The *Gemara* immediately questions the phrasing: *halakha zu v'tu lo*? Is fulfilling this one *halakha* his only achievement? The answer is: *af halakha zu* — the point is that he fulfilled even this *halakha*, in addition to all his other merits. His entire life was organized around the *mitzvah* of providing water to the community, and even in the technical legal domain he managed to do so flawlessly.

### Daughter's Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבִתּוֹ שֶׁל נְחוּנְיָא חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין שֶׁנָּפְלָה לְבוֹר גָּדוֹל, בָּאוּ וְהוֹדִיעוּ אֶת רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא. שָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁלוֹם. שְׁנִיָּה אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁלוֹם. שְׁלִישִׁית אָמַר לָהֶם: עָלְתָה. אָמְרוּ לָהּ: מִי הֶעֱלִךְ? אָמְרָה לָהֶם: זָכָר שֶׁל רְחֵלִים נִזְדַּמֵּן לִי, וְזָקֵן אֶחָד מַנְהִיגוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נָבִיא אַתָּה? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״לֹא נָבִיא אָנֹכִי, וְלֹא בֶּן נָבִיא אָנֹכִי״, אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמַרְתִּי: דָּבָר שֶׁאוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק מִצְטַעֵר בּוֹ – יִכָּשֵׁל בּוֹ זַרְעוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the deeds of Neḥunya, the Gemara relates that **the Sages taught: An incident** occurred **involving the daughter of Neḥunya the ditchdigger, where she fell into a large cistern** and no one could extricate her from it. **They came and informed Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa** so that he would pray on her behalf. When **the first hour** had passed from the time of her fall, **he said to them:** She is at **peace** and unharmed. After the **second** hour, **he said to them:** She is at **peace.** After the **third** hour, **he said to them: She has ascended** from the well, and indeed this was the case. **They said to her: Who brought you up** out of the well? **She said to them: A male sheep,** i.e., a ram, **happened** to come **to me, and a certain old man,** i.e., Abraham, **was leading it,** and he pulled me out. **They said to** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa: **Are you a prophet?** How did you know she had ascended? Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa **said to them: "I am no prophet, neither am I a prophet's son"** (Amos 7:14), **but this** is what **I said** to myself: **Shall the offspring** of Neḥunya **stumble by means of** the very **matter which distressed that righteous man?**

The *Gemara* then relates the famous story: *ma'aseh b'vito shel Nechunya chofer shichin shenafelah l'vor gadol* — it happened that the daughter of Nechunya the well-digger fell into a large pit. The news was brought to Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa. In the first hour, he said: *shalom* — all is well. In the second hour, he again said: *shalom*. In the third hour, he declared: *altah* — she has come out. Indeed, when they checked, they found she had emerged safely. They asked her how she escaped. She told them: *zechar shel racheilim nizdamen li v'zakein echad manhigo* — a ram appeared to her, led by a certain elderly man. Rashi identifies the ram as the ram of the *akeidah* and the elderly guide as Avraham Avinu. The *akeidah* connection is multilayered: there is the image of the lamb as substitute and salvation, the saving of a child at the last moment, and the parallel that just as Avraham and Yitzchak were the original diggers of wells — Yitzchak's life was characterized by re-digging his father's wells — so too Nechunya's merit in digging wells for the community brought about this miraculous rescue.

### Theological Irony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא: אַף עַל פִּי כֵן, מֵת בְּנוֹ בַּצָּמָא. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּסְבִיבָיו נִשְׂעֲרָה מְאֹד״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְדַקְדֵּק עִם סְבִיבָיו אֲפִילּוּ כְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה. רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״אֵל נַעֲרָץ בְּסוֹד קְדֹשִׁים רַבָּה, וְנוֹרָא עַל כׇּל סְבִיבָיו״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וַתְּרָן הוּא, יִוָּתְרוּ חַיָּיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַצּוּר תָּמִים פׇּעֳלוֹ, כִּי כׇל דְּרָכָיו מִשְׁפָּט״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa says:** Although Neḥunya ensured that others would have water, **even so, his son died of thirst,** fulfilling that which **is stated: "And around Him it storms [*nisara*] mightily"** (Psalms 50:3). This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, is scrupulous with those around Him,** i.e., the righteous, **even** to the extent **of a hairsbreadth [*hasa'ara*],** so that even minor transgressions elicit a severe punishment. **Rabbi Neḥunya says:** The same idea may be learned **from here,** in the following verse: **"A God dreaded in the great council of the holy ones, and feared by all those that surround Him"** (Psalms 89:8), indicating that God is most careful and exacting with those that surround Him, i.e., the righteous. **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Anyone who states** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, is forgiving [*vateran*]** of transgressions, **his life will be relinquished [*yivatru*], as it is stated: "The Rock, His work is perfect, for all His ways are justice"** (Deuteronomy 32:4). In other words, God does not waive heavenly justice.

When the people asked Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa how he could be so confident of her rescue, he replied: *lo navi anochi v'lo ben navi anochi* — I am neither a prophet nor the son of a prophet. He offered a simple theological argument: *davar she'asak bo tzaddik mitzta'er bo, yikasheil bo zar'o?* Could it be that the very thing this righteous person devoted his life to, the very cause that he anguished over and worked tirelessly to provide — could that be the instrument of his child's death? It is inconceivable. By the third hour, Rabbi Chanina reasoned, had she not already been rescued, she would certainly have been dead by then; since the principle holds, she must have been saved. Rashi notes this reasoning explicitly. The *Gemara* could have concluded here on that satisfying and morally orderly note. But it does not.

*Amar Rabbi Acha: af al pi chen, met bno batzama* — Rabbi Acha immediately introduces a devastating counterpoint: despite all of this, Nechunya's son died of thirst. The very irony is total and unmistakable. The man who spent his life ensuring that the community would have water to drink — his son died without water. The verse is invoked: *u-svivav nis'arah* — *melamed she-hakadosh baruch hu medakdek im svivav afilu k'chut ha-se'arah* — God is exacting with those who are closest to him, even to a hair's breadth. The closer one is to God, the more precisely divine justice is applied.

### Divine Justice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״ – וְלָא כְּתִיב ״אֶרֶךְ אַף״? אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם לַצַּדִּיקִים וְלָרְשָׁעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥana says, and some say** that **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** says: **What** is the meaning of that **which is written** in the verse that recounts the thirteen attributes of mercy: **"Long-suffering [*erekh appayim*]"** (Exodus 34:6), using the plural form, **and it is not written** as ***erekh af*,** in the singular? In order to teach that He is **long-suffering for** both **the righteous and for the wicked** and does not punish them immediately for their transgressions.

The theological tension in this *Gemara* is striking, and the *Gemara* appears deliberately to juxtapose two apparently contradictory principles. On one side: *davar she'tzaddik nitzta'er bo* — that which a righteous person devoted himself to will not become his stumbling block. On the other: God is exacting with those nearest to him, and his son died precisely of thirst. Tosafot works to reconcile this tension by arguing that the principle about the *tzaddik* does not cover every misfortune — it is only the specific instrument of one's devotion that will not be the cause of one's destruction. The daughter would not die by falling into a well; but the son dying of thirst is a different form of misfortune, not the same instrument. It is a fine distinction — a hair's breadth, as it were — but Tosafot requires it to maintain the principle.

Yet what is perhaps most refreshing about this *Gemara* is its refusal to remain comfortable with a tidy theological narrative. Even if Tosafot's reconciliation is technically valid, the *Gemara* inserts the son's death precisely to puncture the easy consolation of *hashgacha pratit* operating in perfectly neat ways. Tragedy and irony are woven into the world, and the *Gemara* insists we sit with that.

The *Gemara* then cites Rabbi Chanina himself — the same Rabbi Chanina who predicted the daughter's rescue — asserting that God is *erech apayim l'tzadikim v'la-rashaim* — long-tempered with both the righteous and the wicked. And Rabbi Chanina further warns: *kol omer hakadosh baruch hu vatran yivatru chayav* — whoever says that God is easy-going and overlooks things will have his own life treated leniently in the same manner, meaning without careful protection. *Ha-tzur tamim po'alo ki kol derachav mishpat* — the Rock is perfect in his work, for all his ways are justice. Nothing is forgotten; everything is accounted for. The *Mesilas Yesharim* grapples with the apparent tension between this exacting divine justice and the Torah's characterization of God as *chanun v'rachum*, compassionate and gracious. His resolution: the compassion expresses itself precisely in the provision of *teshuvah*; where *teshuvah* has been done, God forgives fully. Where it has not, the justice is exact. The two attributes do not contradict — they operate in different registers.

## Stones/Justice Narrative

### Stone Throwing Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יְסַקֵּל אָדָם מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: A person should not throw stones from his property into the public domain.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a related ruling: *lo yisakel adam meirshuto l'reshut harabim* — one should not clear stones from his private property by throwing them into the public domain. The principle on its face seems self-evident; throwing debris into a public thoroughfare creates an obvious hazard. The *Gemara* illustrates this with a narrative.

### Man & Chasid Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְסַקֵּל מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּמְצָאוֹ חָסִיד אֶחָד. אָמַר לוֹ: רֵיקָה, מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה מְסַקֵּל מֵרְשׁוּת שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלָּךְ לִרְשׁוּת שֶׁלָּךְ? לִגְלֵג עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** An incident** occurred **involving a certain individual who was throwing stones from his property into the public domain, and a certain pious man found him.** The latter **said to him: Lowlife [*reika*], for what** reason **are you throwing stones from property that is not yours into your property?** The man **mocked him,** as he did not understand what he meant, as the property from which he was throwing stones was his.

A certain person was clearing stones from his property and tossing them into *reshut harabim*. A *chasid* — and here, as elsewhere in Bava Kamma, the word *chasid* carries the specific connotation of one who is especially careful about causing harm to others; recall the *chasidim harishonim* discussed earlier in the tractate in connection with the disposal of broken glass — encountered him and rebuked him. *Reika*, he said — empty one, low life — *mipnei ma ata mesakeil meirshut she'eino shelcha l'reshut shelcha?* Why are you removing stones from a domain that is not yours and placing them in the domain that is yours? The man laughed him off. What do I care about the public domain? I am cleaning out my own yard. The *chasid*'s remark seemed absurd to him — the street was not *his*.

### Karmic Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְיָמִים נִצְרַךְ לִמְכּוֹר שָׂדֵהוּ, וְהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בְּאוֹתוֹ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְנִכְשָׁל בְּאוֹתָן אֲבָנִים; אָמַר: יָפֶה אָמַר לִי אוֹתוֹ חָסִיד – מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה מְסַקֵּל מֵרְשׁוּת שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלָּךְ לִרְשׁוּת שֶׁלָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Some days** later, **he was forced to sell his field** from which he had thrown the stones. **And he was walking in the same public domain** into which he had thrown them, **and he stumbled on those same stones. He said: That pious man said** it **well to me** when he said: **For what** reason **are you throwing stones from property that is not yours into your own property,** since that property no longer belongs to me, and only the public domain remains mine to use.

The *Gemara* then describes what happened next. *Liyamim* — in time — the man was reduced to poverty and was forced to sell his property. Now he walked the streets himself. *Venichshal be'otan avanim* — and he stumbled over those very stones he had thrown there. At that moment he said: *yafeh amar li oto chasid* — how right that righteous person was. *Mipnei ma ata mesakeil meirshut she'eino shelcha l'reshut shelcha* — why do you remove from what is not yours and put it into what is yours? What had been the anonymous public street had become, through his reversal of fortune, his domain — the space in which he now lived and walked. And what he had blithely discarded there as someone else's problem became his own hazard.

The story captures something important about how self-interest distorts our perception of communal obligation. It is easy to be meticulous about one's own property and dismissive about harms to others in shared space. The *chasid* of Bava Kamma is precisely the person who resists this distortion — who treats *reshut harabim* with the same care as *reshut hayachid*, recognizing that the public domain is, in a deep sense, everyone's domain including one's own. The story also echoes the preceding discussion about Nechunya: there, the mechanism of divine justice was that the *tzaddik*'s merit in a particular domain protected those he loved in that domain; here, the man's negligence in that same domain ultimately returns to injure himself in kind.

## Rav vs. Shmuel: Pit Damage

### Ten-Tefach Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר – חַיָּיב. אֶחָד הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר, שִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה, חֲרִיצִין וּנְעִיצִין – חַיָּיב. אִם כֵּן לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״בּוֹר״? מָה בּוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים; אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. הָיוּ פְּחוּתִין מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וּמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִם הוּזַּק בּוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who digs a pit in the public domain and an ox or a donkey fell into it,** he is **liable.** The *halakha* is the **same** for **one who digs** either **a pit; a ditch,** which is narrow and long; **or a cave,** which is rectangular and roofed; **trenches and** water **channels.** In all these cases he is **liable. If so, why is** the verse **stated** as referring to **a pit,** as it states: "And if a man shall open a pit" (Exodus 21:33)? To teach that **just as a pit that has sufficient** depth **to cause death** when falling into it is at least **ten handbreadths** deep, **so too, any** other excavations **that have sufficient** depth **to cause death** may be no less than **ten handbreadths.** If any of the types of excavations **were less than ten handbreadths** deep, **and an ox or a donkey fell into** one of **them and died,** the digger of the excavation is **exempt. But if it was injured in it,** not killed, he is **liable** to pay damages.

The *Gemara* returns to the laws of *bor* proper. The *mishna* had established that all types of excavations — *bor*, *shiach*, *me'ara*, *charitzin*, and *ne'itzin* — carry liability. The word *bor* in the Torah is not meant to exclude other forms of excavation; rather, it establishes the parameters of the classic case: just as a *bor* must be ten *tefachim* deep in order for one to bear liability in the event an animal is killed, so too any other pit-like excavation must be ten *tefachim* deep to carry that level of liability. If the pit is less than ten *tefachim* deep, one is *patur* in the event of a death, though still *chayav* if the animal was merely injured, since even a shallow pit can cause damage.

### Rav: Hevel Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: בּוֹר שֶׁחִיְּיבָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה – לְהֶבְלוֹ, וְלֹא לַחֲבָטוֹ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: חֲבָטָה – קַרְקַע עוֹלָם הוּא דְּמַזְּקָא לֵיהּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְהֶבְלוֹ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן לַחֲבָטוֹ. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: לַחֲבָטוֹ אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה, וְלֹא לְהֶבְלוֹ – הַתּוֹרָה הֵעִידָה עַל הַבּוֹר, וַאֲפִילּוּ מָלֵא סְפוֹגִין שֶׁל צֶמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says:** Damage by **Pit for which the Torah obligates one** to pay is referring specifically **to** damage caused by the pit's lethal **fumes,** i.e., suffocation, **but not to** damage caused by **the impact** of hitting the ground, for which the digger of the pit is exempt from paying compensation. The Gemara continues to explain: **Apparently,** it can be inferred that Rav **maintains** that with regard to the **impact** of hitting the bottom of the pit, **it is merely the ground that injures him.** The digger of the pit does not own the ground, so it is not a case where his property caused damage. Therefore, he does not bear responsibility for the damage. **And Shmuel says:** The Torah renders one liable for damage caused **by its** lethal **fumes, and all the more so for** damage resulting from **the impact.** Shmuel adds: **And if you say** that the **Torah spoke** only **about** liability for **its impact and not for its** lethal **fumes,** one could respond that **the Torah testifies about a pit** without specifying for which type of pit one is liable, **and** this includes **even** a pit **full of woolen sponges [*sefogin*],** which would completely absorb the impact.

Rav now introduces one of the most striking theoretical positions in this *sugya*: *bor shechiiva alav Torah l'hevlo v'lo l'chavato* — the *bor* for which the Torah made one liable is the *bor* of its *hevel*, its vapor or noxious air, and not its *chavatah*, the physical impact of hitting the ground. The liability of *bor*, according to Rav, is grounded not in the fact that one exposed ground and an animal struck that ground, but in the fact that one created a space in which a heavy or toxic air accumulates — an *hevel* — which itself causes injury or contributes to death.

This position seems initially bizarre. When a donkey falls into a pit and breaks its leg, the intuitive cause of injury is the physical impact with the ground, not the air quality in the pit. But Rav's position makes more sense when examined against the conceptual background of the *sugya*. The problem Rav is solving is this: when one digs a pit in *reshut harabim*, the ground at the bottom of that pit is not one's ground — it is public ground, ground that existed before one's digging, ground that one does not own. The Torah says *ba'al habor*, the owner of the pit is liable. If an animal's injury is caused by impact with ground that belonged to the public all along — ground that one merely exposed — then one's connection to that injury is attenuated. Rav resolves this by locating the liability not in the ground itself, but in the *hevel* — the heavy air — which is something the digger genuinely created, something that did not exist before the excavation and that is properly the digger's creation. It is the *hevel* that connects the owner to the damage, even when the physical impact is with *karka olam* — the eternally existing ground.

### Shmuel: Both Elements

Shmuel disagrees. For Shmuel, one is *chayav* for both the *hevel* and the *chavatah*. The ground impact is itself a consequence of the pit the person created, and there is no reason to exempt the digger from the consequences of the impact simply because the ground itself pre-existed.

### Mound Hypothetical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דַּעֲבַד גּוֹבַהּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; לְרַב – אַגּוֹבַהּ לָא מִיחַיַּיב, לִשְׁמוּאֵל – אַגּוֹבַהּ נָמֵי מִיחַיַּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָפַל״ – עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל דֶּרֶךְ נְפִילָה. וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, ״וְנָפַל״ – כֹּל דְּהוּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** the opinions of Rav and Shmuel, given that falling into any pit involves injury due to both the lethal fumes and the impact? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is in the case **where** one **fashioned a mound** with a height of ten handbreadths **in the public domain** without digging, and an animal fell from this raised platform and died. **According to Rav,** the one who fashioned the mound **is not liable for** damage by Pit in the case of **a mound,** since there are no fumes, as the animal fell to the level ground. By contrast, **according to Shmuel, he is also liable for** damage by Pit in the case of **a mound,** since there is nevertheless an impact when hitting the ground. The Gemara asks: **What is** the source for **the reasoning of Rav,** that one is not liable in that case? The Gemara answers: **Since the verse states: "And** an ox or a donkey **fall** therein" (Exodus 21:33), indicating that there is no liability for damage by Pit **unless** the animal **falls** in the **normal manner of falling,** but not where it first climbed onto an elevated surface and then fell from there to the level ground. **And according to Shmuel,** the term: **"And** an ox or a donkey **fall," indicates any manner** of falling, regardless of whether the animal fell into a hole or fell to the ground from an elevated surface.

The practical difference between Rav and Shmuel emerges clearly in the following case: one who creates a mound (*govah*) in *reshut harabim*, and a person trips over the mound and strikes the flat ground — not the mound itself. For Rav, one is *patur* in this case. There is no *hevel*; the person did not create a pit with accumulated heavy air; and the injury from striking the flat ground is *karka olam heizikato* — the eternally existing ground caused the injury, not the mound-maker. For Shmuel, one is nonetheless *chayav*, since the mound caused the fall and the injury is a direct consequence of one's creation. The mound-maker cannot disclaim responsibility simply because the final impact was with ground that preceded his action.

Rav's position on the mound should be distinguished from the liability for stones and knives left in *reshut harabim* that was discussed earlier in the tractate. If a person trips over a mound and strikes the mound itself, Rav would presumably hold the person *chayav*, since the injury is then from the thing one created. The exemption is specifically when the fall results in impact only with the flat ground — the *karka olam* — and there is no *hevel* establishing the digger's connection to that ground.

### Wool Cushion Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אִם כֵּן לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״בּוֹר״? מָה בּוֹר שֶׁהוּא כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל – ״אַף כֹּל״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי גּוֹבַהּ. אֶלָּא לְרַב – ״אַף כֹּל״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי חֲרִיצִין וּנְעִיצִין. חֲרִיצִין וּנְעִיצִין בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ! תָּנֵי, וַהֲדַר מְפָרֵשׁ. וְהָנֵי כּוּלְּהוּ דְּקָתָנֵי – לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכָא; דְּאִי תְּנָא בּוֹר, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בּוֹר עֲשָׂרָה – הוּא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקַטִּין וּכְרִיכָא; אֲבָל שִׁיחַ – דַּאֲרִיךְ, אֵימָא: בַּעֲשָׂרָה לֵית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא. וְאִי תְּנָא שִׁיחַ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שִׁיחַ עֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקַטִּין; אֲבָל מְעָרָה – דִּמְרַבְּעָא, אֵימָא: בַּעֲשָׂרָה לֵית בַּהּ הַבְלָא. וְאִי תָּנֵי מְעָרָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מְעָרָה בַּעֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּאִית בַּהּ הַבְלָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּמְטַלְּלָא; אֲבָל חֲרִיצִין – דְּלָא מְטַלְּלִי, אֵימָא: בַּעֲשָׂרָה לֵית בְּהוּ הַבְלָא. וְאִי תְּנָא חֲרִיצִין, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: חֲרִיצִין עֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּאִית בְּהוּ הַבְלָא, מִשּׁוּום דְּלֵית בְּהוּ רְוִיחָא מִלְּעֵיל טְפֵי מִתַּתַּאי; אֲבָל נְעִיצִין – דִּרְוִיחִי מִלְּעֵיל טְפֵי מִתַּתַּאי, אֵימָא בַּעֲשָׂרָה לֵית בְּהוּ הַבְלָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges Rav's opinion: **We learned** in the mishna: **If so, why is** the verse **stated** as referring to **a pit,** as it states: "And if a man shall open a pit" (Exodus 21:33)? To teach that **just as a pit that has sufficient** depth **to cause death** when falling into it is at least **ten handbreadths** deep, **so too, any** other excavations **that have sufficient** depth **to cause death** may be no less than **ten handbreadths.** Now, **granted** that according **to Shmuel,** the term: **So too, any** other, serves **to include** the case where the animal fell from **a height** of ten handbreadths, in which case the one who fashioned the mound would also be liable. **But according to Rav,** who exempts him in that case, **what** does the term: **So too, any** other, **add?** According to Rav, it serves **to include trenches and** water **channels.** The Gemara asks: But **trenches and** water **channels are explicitly taught** in the mishna. Why does the mishna then allude to them again? The Gemara answers: It first **teaches** the *halakha* about them **and then explains** its source in the Torah. Having mentioned these details, the Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **all these** cases **that are taught** by the mishna? The Gemara answers: They are **necessary, for had** it **taught** only the case of **a pit, I would say** that it **is** specifically **a pit** of **ten** handbreadths **that contains** lethal **fumes, because it is constricted and round.** Therefore, this measurement suffices to cause death. **But** concerning **a ditch, which is long, say** that **in** a case where it is **ten** handbreadths deep, **there are no** lethal **fumes** and there is no liability. Therefore, the mishna teaches both the case of a pit and a ditch. **And** furthermore, **had** the mishna **taught** the case of **a ditch** in addition to the pit, **I would say** that it **is** specifically **a ditch** of **ten** handbreadths **that contains** the necessary lethal **fumes, because it is narrow. But** concerning **a cave, which is rectangular** and not narrow, **say** that **in** a case where it is **ten** handbreadths deep, **there are no** lethal **fumes** and there is no liability. Therefore, the mishna teaches the case of a cave as well. **And** furthermore, **had** the mishna **taught** the case of **a cave** in addition to the previous two, **I would say** that it **is** specifically **a cave of ten** handbreadths **that contains** lethal **fumes, because it is covered. But** concerning **trenches, which are not covered, say** that at a depth **of ten** handbreadths, **there are no** lethal **fumes** and there is no liability. Therefore, the mishna also teaches the case of trenches. **And** finally, **had** the mishna **taught** the case of **trenches** in addition to the previous three, **I would say** that it **is** specifically **trenches** of **ten** handbreadths **that contain** lethal **fumes, since they are no wider at the top than at the bottom. But with regard to channels, which are wider at the top than at the bottom, say** that at a depth **of ten** handbreadths **there are no** lethal **fumes** and there is no liability. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** the case of channels as well, and each subsequent case listed contains a novel aspect.

The *Gemara* adduces proof that the Torah's liability for *bor* is indeed grounded in *hevel* rather than, or at least in addition to, *chavatah*: *ha-Torah heidah al ha-bor ve-afilu malei spurgin shel tzemer* — the Torah declares one liable for a *bor* even if it is filled with cushions of wool. If the basis of liability were solely the physical impact with the ground, then a pit stuffed with soft wool cushions would produce no injurious impact, and there should be no liability. Yet the Torah makes one liable regardless. This demonstrates that *hevel* — the noxious air that can build up in an enclosed space and cause death or harm — is itself a recognized source of liability, entirely independent of physical impact. This also means, as pointed out to the class, that *hevel* cannot be reduced to the mere conceptual act of "exposing the ground" — it is something distinct, something the creation of the pit brings into being.

The proof cuts against the earlier suggestion that *hevel* could be understood simply as a metaphor for the exposed ground now constituting a danger. If that were all *hevel* meant, there would be no liability in the wool-cushion case, since the exposed ground is entirely buffered. The Torah's liability in that case proves that *hevel* is a real, independent factor.

### Pit Types & Hevel

The *Gemara* then asks why the *mishna* needed to enumerate all the different types of pits and excavations if a single general term would have sufficed. The answer is that each type of excavation has different physical characteristics, and one might have thought the *hevel* requirement is only met by some of them. A classic round *bor* of ten *tefachim* — *katin v'karich*, narrow and deep — clearly generates *hevel* through the compression of air in a confined vertical space. A *shiach*, a long trench, is not as narrow and might be thought insufficient to trap *hevel* at the same depth. A *me'ara* — a cave-like excavation, square and covered — is wide in both horizontal dimensions, and one might have thought that its greater width prevents the accumulation of *hevel*; yet its covered top compensates, trapping the air. *Charitzin* are not covered and are wide in both directions; one might have thought their openness dissipates any *hevel* entirely. *Ne'itzin* are funnel-shaped — wider at the top than at the bottom — which might also seem to mitigate the accumulation of heavy air in their depths. The *Gemara* asserts that all of these, when ten *tefachim* deep, do in fact generate *hevel* sufficient to create liability for a killed animal, and the enumeration of each type is necessary to establish this across all the variations in shape, width, and coverage.

It must be emphasized that no one — not Rav, not the *Gemara* — actually imagines that *hevel* alone breaks an animal's leg or snaps a human bone. Rather, as Rashi makes clear, the *hevel* functions as the conceptual link between the pit-owner and the injury caused by the ground impact. Without *hevel*, the impact with the ground is simply *karka olam heizikato* — the eternal ground did it — and the pit-maker bears no responsibility. With *hevel*, the pit-maker has created something new in that space that ties him to whatever injury occurs within it. The real-world dispute between Rav and Shmuel, then, is whether that conceptual link — *hevel* — is necessary to establish liability for ground-impact injuries, or whether one is *chayav* for such injuries regardless, simply by virtue of having created the excavation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיוּ פְּחוּתִין מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִם הוּזַּק בּוֹ – חַיָּיב. נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וּמֵת – פָּטוּר; מַאי טַעְמָא – לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חַבְטָה? לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא. אִי הָכִי, אִם הוּזַּק בּוֹ חַיָּיב? הָא לֵית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא! אָמְרִי: אֵין הַבְלָא לְמִיתָה, וְיֵשׁ הַבְלָא לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further challenge from that which **we learned** in the mishna: If any of the types of excavations **were less than ten handbreadths** deep, **and an ox or a donkey fell into** one of **them and died,** the digger of the excavation is **exempt. But if it was injured,** not killed, he is **liable** to pay damages. **What is the reason** for the ruling of: If **an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **exempt? Is it not because** at this height **there is not** sufficient **impact,** although there are lethal fumes? The Gemara responds: **No,** he is exempt **because there are no** lethal **fumes.** The Gemara challenges this: **If that is so,** that a pit less than ten handbreadths deep lacks sufficient lethal fumes, why is the one who dug it **liable if** the animal **is injured; but there are no** lethal **fumes?** The Gemara **answers: There are not** sufficient **fumes to** cause **death, but there are** sufficient **fumes to** cause **damage.**
