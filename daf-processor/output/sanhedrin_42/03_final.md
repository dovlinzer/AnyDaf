# Sanhedrin 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Calendar Discrepancy

### Investigation Methods

The fifth chapter of Masechet Sanhedrin, *hayu bodkim*, concerns the examination of witnesses. The tradition presents three distinct frameworks for this examination. *Chakirot* are the formal investigations into the circumstances of the alleged crime: in which week, in which year, on which day. *Drishot* are inquiries into the defendant himself — who is he, what does he do? *Bdikot* are the more general questions put to witnesses. The precise boundary between these three categories was itself a matter of debate among the Rabbis, and the uncertainty about where one ends and the next begins is itself instructive.

### Mishnah Discrepancy Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל חוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **one** witness **says** the event occurred on the second of the month and one witness says that the event occurred on the third of the month, their testimony stands, since it is possible to say that one witness knows of the addition of a day to the previous month, while the other witness does not know of it. Their testimony is considered congruent. The Gemara asks: **Until how many** days into the month does the court assume that one of the witnesses does not know when the month began? **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says** that **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Until the majority of the month** has passed.

The *mishnah* underlying our *gemara* (from the previous *amud*) addresses discrepancies in testimony concerning the day of the month. If one witness says the event took place on the second of the month and another says it was the third, *edutan kayemet* — their testimony stands. The *mishnah* explains: *shezeh yode'a be'iburo shel chodesh vezeh eino yode'a be'iburo shel chodesh* — one witness knows precisely when the new moon was declared, and the other does not, which accounts for the one-day discrepancy. However, if one witness says the third of the month and the other says the fifth, *edutan beteilah* — their testimony is void.

### Rabbi Yochanan

The *gemara* asks: *ad kama?* — how far into the month does this leniency apply? Rabbi Acha bar Chanina said in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ad rubo shel chodesh* — up to the majority of the month, roughly the middle. Beyond that point, a person ought to know which day it is, and a discrepancy of even a day or two can no longer be excused by ignorance of the new moon's declaration.

### Rava's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בַּחֲמִשָּׁה״ – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא שֶׁזֶּה יוֹדֵעַ בִּשְׁנֵי עִיבּוּרִין וְזֶה אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בִּשְׁנֵי עִיבּוּרִין? אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם דִּבְרוּבָּה יָדַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: We learn** this in the mishna **as well,** as it teaches: If **one** witness **says** the event occurred **on the third** of the month **and one** witness **says** the event occurred **on the fifth** of the month, **their testimony is void. But why** is it void? **Let us say** that it not a contradiction, **as this** witness **knows of two additions,** i.e., that a day was added to the two previous months, **and that** witness **does not know of the two additions. Rather,** is it **not** void **because once the majority** of the month passed a witness **knows** what day it is, so that an error of two days cannot occur?

Rava observes that this principle is already implicit in the *mishnah* itself: *af anan nami tanina* — we also teach this. He presses the point: why should a discrepancy of two days — saying the third versus the fifth — invalidate testimony? Could we not say that one witness knows about two consecutive intercalations and the other knows only one? The *gemara* responds: *le'olam eima lach beruba nami lo yadat* — it is entirely possible for a person not to know which day of the month it is throughout most of the month. The crucial factor is the *shipura*, the *shofar* blast announcing the new moon. If there was only one day of *Rosh Chodesh* with one *shofar* blast, it is reasonable that someone missed it. But two blasts — *betrei shipura lo amar detei* — it is no longer plausible that a witness failed to register two separate announcements.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: בְּרוּבָּה נָמֵי לָא יָדַע, וּבְשִׁיפּוּרָא יָדַע. בְּחַד שִׁיפּוּרָא אָמַר דְּטָעֵי, בִּתְרֵי שִׁיפּוּרֵי לָא אָמַר דְּטָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Actually, I** could **say to you** that a witness may **not know** the day **even once the majority** of the month has passed. The reason for the ruling of the mishna is that he **knows about the *shofar* [*ubeshipura*],** which the court would blow at the New Moon. It is possible that **with regard to one** blast of the ***shofar*** it could be **said that he erred** and was not aware of it. **With regard to two** blasts of the ***shofar*** it **cannot** be **said that he erred.** By contrast, in the case of the mishna here, perhaps as long as the witnesses are testifying about two consecutive days within one month, the error of one of them persisted throughout the month.

Tosafot addresses a difficulty with this entire framework. The purpose of witness examination is to protect the defendant, yet we are being asked to invalidate testimony over calendar discrepancies that might be perfectly innocent. Tosafot resolves the matter by focusing on what the two witnesses actually agree upon: if both are certain the event occurred on, say, a Monday, and they simply disagree about whether that Monday was the 27th or the 28th of the month, the testimony is accepted — they are speaking of the same day of the week, however uncertain about its numerical date. But if the discrepancy is such that there is genuine uncertainty about which day of the week the event occurred, the testimony is disqualified as insufficiently reliable.

## Birkat Levana

### Days for Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד כַּמָּה מְבָרְכִין עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ? עַד שֶׁתִּתְמַלֵּא פְּגִימָתָהּ. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: עַד שִׁבְעָה. נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: עַד שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § Having cited a statement of Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina, citing Rabbi Asi, citing Rabbi Yoḥanan, the Gemara cites another statement in his name: **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says** that **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Until how many** days into a new month may **one recite a blessing on the month,** i.e., *Birkat HaLevana*? **Until the flaw of the** moon **is filled,** when it no longer appears deficient. **And** until **how many** days is that? **Rav Ya'akov bar Idi says** that **Rav Yehuda says: Until seven** days of the month have passed. The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Until sixteen** days of the month have passed.

Transitioning naturally from the discussion of the month and the calendar, the *gemara* addresses *birkat levana* — the blessing recited upon the renewal of the moon. The same chain of tradition — Rabbi Acha bar Chanina in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — addresses how many days after *Rosh Chodesh* one may still recite this blessing: *ad she-titmale pegimata* — until the defect of the moon is filled. The moon at the beginning of the month is incomplete — *pagum* — and the question is when the *miluy*, the filling of that defect, is considered complete.

### Moon's Shape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לְהוּ: הָא לְמִיהְוֵי כִּי יִתְרָא, הָא לְמִיהְוֵי כִּי נָפְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And they both hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** that one can recite the blessing until the flaw of the moon is filled. The dispute is that **this** one, i.e., Rav Ya'akov bar Idi, who holds one can recite the blessing until seven days have passed, understands Rabbi Yoḥanan to be referring **to** the day when the moon **will be like the string** of a bow. Before that point the moon appeared merely as a bow, and after seven days it appears like a half-circle, like a bow that has a string. **That** one, i.e., the Sages of Neharde'a, who holds one can recite the blessing until sixteen days have passed, understands Rabbi Yoḥanan to be referring **to** the day when the moon **will be like a sieve,** i.e., a full circle.

Rav Yaakov bar Iddi said in the name of Rav Yehuda: *ad shiva* — until the seventh day of the month. The sages of Neharde'a say: until the sixteenth, the middle of the month. Both opinions are understood to be consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's formulation, differing only in their definition of when the moon is considered "filled." One opinion holds that the moon must achieve the shape of a *yeter* — the string of a bow, a slight arc — while the other requires the shape of a *nafya* — a full round sieve, meaning the complete full moon.

### HaTov VeHaMeitiv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְלִיבָרֵיךְ ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ כִּי חָסַר מִי מְבָרְכִינַן ״דַּיַּין הָאֱמֶת״, דִּלְבָרֵיךְ ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״? וְלִיבָרְכִינְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ, לָא מְבָרְכִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: And they should bless** the blessing of: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, **Who is good and Who does good,** for the benefit that people derive from the light of moon. Ravina **said to him: Is that to say** that **when** the moon is **shrinking we bless,** as we do for other disasters: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, **the true Judge,** so **that we should** conversely **bless:** Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, **Who is good and Who does good,** when the moon is growing? Rav Aḥa of Difti said to him: You are correct, **and we should say them both:** The blessing of the true Judge, when the moon is waning, and the blessing of Who is good and Who does good, when the moon is waxing. Ravina answered him: **Since this is its nature, we do not bless** the moon. The waxing and waning of the moon is not an unexpected occurrence that requires these blessings.

Rav Acha of Difti posed a question to Ravina: since we recite *mechadesh chodashim* only for perhaps seven days — the period of the moon's filling — should we not also recite *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, the blessing reserved for especially beneficial news, in response to the moon's growth? And conversely, when the moon wanes, should we not say *Dayan HaEmet*, the blessing recited upon receiving bad news?

The *gemara* responds that both blessings could in principle be said — *levarechin'hu le-travaihu*. But then it adds the decisive qualification: *keivan de-hainu orchei lo mevar'chinan* — since this is simply the way of the world, we do not recite them. Rashi explains the distinction precisely: the blessing for the moon is an act of acknowledging *Hashem*'s creation — a recognition that the world operates according to its established order. It is categorically different from *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, which is recited when something personally beneficial has occurred to a person. When we recite *birkat levana*, we are not recipients of a personal gift; we are observers of the ongoing wonder of creation. The moon's renewal is not something done *for* us — it is something we witness and attest to. *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, by contrast — such as when a second bottle of wine is brought to one's table — is a response to a personal act of kindness. The two categories do not mix.

### Shechinah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַמְבָרֵךְ עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ כְּאִילּוּ מְקַבֵּל פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא ״הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ״. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אִילְמָלֵא לֹא זָכוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶלָּא לְהַקְבִּיל פְּנֵי אֲבִיהֶן שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם כָּל חֹדֶשׁ וָחֹדֶשׁ, דַּיָּים. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִלְכָּךְ נֵימְרִינְהוּ מְעוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says** that **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **anyone who blesses the** new **month in its** proper **time,** it is **as if he greets the Face of the Divine Presence.** Alluding to this, it **is written here** concerning the sanctification of the new month: **"This month** shall be for you the beginning of months" (Exodus 12:2), **and** it **is written there,** where the Jewish people encountered the Divine Presence at the splitting of the sea: **"This is my God and I will glorify Him"** (Exodus 15:2). The term "this" is employed in both verses. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: If the Jewish people merited to greet the Face of their Father in Heaven only** one time **each and every month,** it would **suffice for them,** since in the blessing of the moon there is an aspect of greeting the Divine Presence. **Abaye said: Therefore, we will say** the blessing **while standing,** in honor of the Divine Presence.

Rabbi Acha bar Chanina said in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hamevarech al hachodesh bizmano ke'ilu mekabel pnei Shechinah* — whoever blesses the new moon in its proper time, it is as though he receives the face of the *Shechinah*. This is a striking statement. Why should *birkat levana* carry such spiritual weight? Several possibilities suggest themselves. *Kiddush hachodesh* — the sanctification of the new month — was the very first *mitzvah* given to the Jewish people as a nation. There is something profound about a *mitzvah* that requires presence: not merely physical compliance, but awareness of time, awareness of the moment. So many hours pass in which we are not truly present; here we are called to *mamash* be present.

The *gemara* supplies a textual proof by *gezeirah shavah*. In the passage concerning the new month, the Torah uses the word *hazeh* — "this." In another context, we encounter *zeh Eli ve'anvehu* — "this is my God and I will glorify Him" — a verse from *Shirat HaYam*. What was the greatness of *Shirat HaYam*? The tradition teaches that *ra'ata shifcha mah shelo ra'ah Yechezkel ben Buzi* — even the maidservant at the Sea witnessed what the prophet Ezekiel, in all his sublime visions, did not see. At the Sea, the *Shechinah* was accessible to all. By the same *gezeirah shavah*, when one greets the new moon with its blessing at the right moment, one taps into something of that same direct encounter with the divine presence.

### Tana d'vei R. Yishmael

A teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael makes this point with characteristic force: *ilmalei zachu Yisrael ela lehakebil pnei avihem shebashamayim kol chodesh vechodesh dayam* — if Israel had merited nothing other than to welcome their heavenly Father each and every month, that alone would be sufficient. The language is striking — *lehakebil pnei* — to receive the face, as one would welcome a distinguished guest. If the entirety of Jewish achievement had been only this monthly encounter, it would have been enough to justify our creation.

### Standing Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָרִימָר וּמָר זוּטְרָא מְכַתְּפִי אַהֲדָדֵי וּמְבָרְכִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: בְּמַעְרְבָא מְבָרְכִי ״בָּרוּךְ מְחַדֵּשׁ חֳדָשִׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Mareimar and Mar Zutra would lean on one another's shoulders and recite the blessing. Rav Aḥa said to Rav Ashi: In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they recite** the following **blessing** on the moon: **Blessed is He Who renews the months.** Rav Ashi **said to him: Our women also recite that blessing,** meaning that this is an abridged version for the unlettered.

Abaye derives a practical ruling from this: *hilkach nimrinhu me'umad* — therefore, we recite *birkat levana* standing, not sitting, in recognition of the *Shechinah*-like quality of the moment. Merimar and Mar Zutra, who were elderly, would lean upon support — some say upon one another, some say upon their servants — specifically in order to fulfill this requirement of standing. The custom thus reflects the halakhic ruling directly.

Regarding the question of whether women are obligated in *kiddush levana*: the *gemara* raises it — *hani neshei didan nami mevarchi*, implying that women too should recite the blessing — but then sets the discussion aside without resolution, much as happens in a live *shiur* when a question opens onto a thicket of connected issues. The moon's cycle, the timing of women's marriages (traditionally on Wednesday, the fourth day of the week, the day the *me'orot* were created and the moon was diminished), and the question of *mitzvat asei shehazman grama* (time-bound positive commandments from which women are generally exempt) are all interlocked. The custom has become for women not to recite *kiddush levana*, but the full analysis of the sources behind that custom goes beyond what the *gemara* resolves at this point.

### Blessing Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״בָּרוּךְ [וְכוּ׳] אֲשֶׁר בְּמַאֲמָרוֹ בָּרָא שְׁחָקֵים, וּבְרוּחַ פִּיו כׇּל צְבָאָם. חֹק וּזְמַן נָתַן לָהֶם שֶׁלֹּא יְשַׁנּוּ אֶת תַּפְקִידָם, שָׂשִׂים וּשְׂמֵחִים לַעֲשׂוֹת רְצוֹן קוֹנָם. פּוֹעֲלֵי אֱמֶת שֶׁפְּעוּלָּתָם אֱמֶת. וְלַלְּבָנָה אָמַר שֶׁתִּתְחַדֵּשׁ עֲטֶרֶת תִּפְאֶרֶת לַעֲמוּסֵי בָטֶן, שֶׁהֵן עֲתִידִין לְהִתְחַדֵּשׁ כְּמוֹתָהּ וּלְפָאֵר לְיוֹצְרָם עַל שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ מְחַדֵּשׁ חֳדָשִׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the full version of the blessing **is** the version **of Rav Yehuda. As Rav Yehuda says: Blessed** are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, **Who by His word created the heavens, and by the breath of his mouth all their hosts. He set for them a law and a time, that they should not deviate from their task. And they are joyous and glad to perform the will of their Owner; they are workers of truth whose work is truth. And to the moon He said that it should renew** itself as **a crown of beauty for those He carried from the womb, as they are destined to be renewed like it, and to praise their Creator for the name of His glorious kingdom. Blessed are You the Lord, Who renews the months.**

Rav Acha said to Rav Ashi: in the west — that is, in Eretz Yisrael — they conclude the blessing with *baruch mechadesh chodashim*, which is in fact the *chatima*, the signature formula, of our own *birkat levana*. The full text of the blessing as it appears here is deeply moving:

> *Asher bema'amaro bara shechakim, uveruach piv kol tzeva'am, chok uzman natan lahem shelo yeshanu et tafkidam, sasim vesamechim la'asot retzon konam, po'alei emet shepe'ulatam emet. Velalevana amar shetitchadesh ateret tiferet le'amusei vaten, shehen atidim lehitkhadesh kemotah, ulefa'er leyotzram al shem kevod malchuto. Baruch Atah Hashem mechadesh chodashim.*

In His utterance He created the heavens; by the breath of His mouth, all their hosts. He gave them law and time so that they would not deviate from their appointed task. They are joyous and glad to do the will of their Creator — truthful workers whose deeds are truth. And to the moon He said: be renewed as a crown of glory for those laden from the womb, for they are destined to be renewed like her, and to glorify their Creator for the glory of His kingship. Blessed are You, *Hashem*, who renews the months.

The connection between the moon's renewal and the renewal of the Jewish people — *shehen atidim lehitkhadesh kemotah* — gives the blessing its emotional resonance. We are not merely observers of an astronomical phenomenon; we see in the waxing moon a reflection of our own destiny.

## Torah's Wars

### Mishnah Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי בְתַחְבֻּלוֹת תַּעֲשֶׂה לְּךָ מִלְחָמָה. אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּמִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא מִלְחַמְתָּהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה? בְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ חֲבִילוֹת שֶׁל מִשְׁנָה. קָרֵי רַב יוֹסֵף אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ: ״וְרׇב תְּבוּאוֹת בְּכֹחַ שׁוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another statement, citing Rabbi Aḥa, citing Rabbi Asi, citing Rabbi Yoḥanan. The verse states: **"For by wise advice you shall make your war"** (Proverbs 24:6). **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says** that **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: In whom do you find the war,** i.e., the ability to engage in disputes, **of Torah? In one who has in his possession bundles,** i.e., vast knowledge, **of Mishna.** One must first learn the primary sources before engaging in disputes of Torah. **Rav Yosef would read concerning himself** the verse: **"And much produce comes by the strength of the ox"** (Proverbs 14:4), i.e., one with great strength can bring a large yield. Rav Yosef was known to be particularly well-versed in tannaitic statements.

The *gemara* asks: *bemi atah motzei milchamtah shel Torah?* — in whom do you find someone capable of fighting the Torah's battles? The answer: *bemi sheyesh beyado chavilot shel Mishnah* — in the one who possesses bundles of *Mishnah*. Why *Mishnah* specifically, rather than *Torah* itself? Because the *Torah* contains apparent contradictions and repetitions, and it is the *Mishnah* that provides the tools to reconcile those tensions and harmonize what might otherwise appear inconsistent. One cannot fight for the *Torah* without the *Mishnah* as the instrument of reconciliation.

The *gemara* applies this to Rav Yosef himself — *k'Rav Yosef anafshei* — who said this of himself, citing the verse *verav tevuot be'choach shor*: *tevuah*, grain, is a reference to *Torah*, and *shor*, the ox, is the symbol of the tribe of Yosef. Rav Yosef thus understood himself as one who brought forth great *Torah* like the strength of an ox.

## Hourly Discrepancy

### Sunrise Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר בִּשְׁתֵּי שָׁעוֹת כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שָׁעוֹת, אֲבָל אֶחָד אוֹמֵר קוֹדֶם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר לְאַחַר הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **one** witness **says** that the event occurred **at two hours,** i.e., the second hour of the day from sunrise, and one witness says that the event occurred at three hours, their testimony stands. **Rav Shimi bar Ashi says:** This was **taught only** when there was a difference in the **hours, but** if **one** witness **says** that the event occurred **before the sunrise, and one says** that the event occurred **after the sunrise, their testimony is void.** Although this may be a smaller discrepancy in terms of time, the difference between before and after sunrise cannot be ascribed to an error.

The *mishnah* now addresses discrepancies not in the day of the month but in the hour of the day. If one witness says the event occurred at the second hour and another says the third, the testimony is generally accepted. Minor variations in time estimation do not disqualify witnesses. The critical exception, however, is when the discrepancy straddles *neitz hachamah* — sunrise. If one witness says the event occurred before sunrise and another says after sunrise, *edutan beteilah* — their testimony is void. The *gemara* extends this: even if one says it occurred right before sunrise and another says during sunrise itself, the testimony is still disqualified.

### Location Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״קוֹדֶם הָנֵץ״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּתוֹךְ הָנֵץ״. הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָא בְּגִילּוּיָא קָאֵי, וְזַהֲרוּרֵי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּחֲזָא – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** There is a clear difference between darkness and light. **Rather,** Rav Shimi bar Ashi said as follows: If **one** witness **says** that the event occurred **before the sunrise, and one says** that the event occurred **during the sunrise,** their testimony is void. The Gemara asks: Isn't **this also obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this** one who says: During the sunrise, **was standing in an exposed** place **and saw a mere shine** and thought he saw the sunrise, Rav Shimi bar Ashi **teaches us** the court does not assume this occurred, and deems the testimony incongruent.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi offers a penetrating observation. One might think the issue is simply the hour discrepancy between "before" and "after" sunrise. But the underlying problem is geographic. To have seen the sun rise while another person, present at the same event, had not yet seen it, the two witnesses must have been standing in different locations — perhaps one on higher ground, or one in a place with an unobstructed eastern horizon and the other not. That difference in location is itself what invalidates the testimony. The witnesses must have been in the same place observing the same event; if their reports of sunrise diverge, their physical positions diverge, and the testimony collapses.

## Court Procedure

### That Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַכְנִיסִין כּוּ׳. אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם וְתוּ לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אִם יֵשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – לֹא הָיָה יוֹרֵד מִשָּׁם לְעוֹלָם, וְאִם אֵין מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – אֵין יוֹרֵד כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא עֲלִיָּיתוֹ יְרִידָה לוֹ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּרְגּוּמַהּ אַאִם אֵין מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And afterward they bring in** the second witness and examine him. Later, the mishna states: But if one of the students said: I can teach a reason to acquit him, they raise him to the seat of the court and seat him among them, and he would not descend from there all day. The Gemara asks: **That day and no more? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:3): **If** the **statement of** that student **has substance he would never descend from there,** as his statement demonstrates that he is capable of deliberating with the other judges. **But if** the **statement of** that student **does not have substance, he would not descend from there the entire day, in order that his ascent should not be a descent for him,** i.e., to avoid humiliating him. **Abaye said: Interpret** the mishna to be **with regard to** a case when the **statement of** that student **does not have substance.**

The *mishnah* states that testimony is heard *oto hayom* — on that day, the day the defendant is present in court. The *gemara* immediately challenges this formulation: *oto hayom v'tu lo?* — only on that day and nothing more? The *mishnah* elsewhere teaches: if someone — whether a student or anyone present — says *yesh li lelamed zechut*, "I have something to say in his merit," he is brought up and seated among the judges, and *lo hayah yored misham kol hayom kulo* — he does not leave from there the entire day. Furthermore, a *beraita* teaches: *im yesh mamash bidvarav lo hayah yored misham le'olam* — if his words have genuine substance, he does not leave from there ever, meaning the court continues as long as he has something meaningful to contribute.

These are not the prosecution witnesses but character witnesses — not witnesses to the event itself, but those who wish to speak on behalf of the defendant's merit. The *talmidim* who are permitted to speak in favor of the defendant (*lelamed zechut*) may not speak to condemn him (*lelamed alav chovah*).

### Abaye Reconciles

Abaye reconciles the apparent tension between the sources. The key is the phrase *mamash bidvarav* — whether there is genuine substance to what the character witness is saying. If a person rises to speak and his remarks are substantively relevant to the defendant's case, he remains as long as necessary. If his remarks amount to nothing material — if, for instance, he can say only that the defendant bakes cookies — we would not keep him there, but we also do not simply dismiss him: *kedei shelo tehei aliyaso yeridah lo* — so that his having been called up should not become a humiliation for him. Abaye's reconciliation is thus that *oto hayom* describes the outer limit of the day, while the substantive standard of *mamash bidvarav* governs how long a character witness remains.

### Wine Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת כּוּ׳. יַיִן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״וּלְרוֹזְנִים אֵי שֵׁכָר״ – הָעוֹסְקִין בְּרָזוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם אַל יִשְׁתַּכְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the court **found** it fit to **acquit** the defendant during the deliberations, as all or a majority of them agree to acquit him, they excuse him. The mishna further teaches that the judges would not drink wine all day. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** they did **not** drink **wine? Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says** that it is because **the verse states:** "It is not for kings to drink wine, **nor for princes [*rozenim*] to say: Where is strong drink"** (Proverbs 31:4). Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina explains: This is a directive to **those who deal with the secret of the world [*berazo shel olam*],** i.e., such stringent matters: **Do not become drunk.**

The *mishnah* notes that neither the witnesses nor the character witnesses may drink wine throughout the day of proceedings. The *gemara* asks: why is wine specifically mentioned? Rabbi Acha bar Chanina cites a verse from Mishlei: *vela-roznim ei shachar* — do not give strong drink to rulers. The word *roznim* carries the Hebrew root for *raz*, secret. The principle invoked is the well-known *nichnas yayin yatza sod* — when wine enters, secrets exit. Those who are dealing with matters of such gravity — the secrets of life and justice — must not compromise their discretion with drink.

## Hung Courts

### Dismiss or Add

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת כּוּ׳. לֹא רָאוּ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the court **found** it fit to **acquit him** during the deliberations, as all or a majority of them agree to acquit him, they release him. It was further taught in the mishna that when the court cannot arrive at a verdict they add judges in pairs of two and deliberate until there is a clear verdict. If they added the maximum number of judges and still cannot reach a clear verdict, they discuss the matter until one of those who deems him liable sees the validity of the statements of those who acquit, and changes his position. The Gemara asks: If the judges do not change their position, as they **do not see** the validity of the position of those who acquit him, **what** is done?

When the court finds no merit in the defense and the vote stands without the required majority for conviction, what is the procedure? The *mishnah* discusses a split of 35 to 36 in a court of 71 — a genuinely hung court. And separately, what if the court of 23 stands at 12 to 11? There is not yet sufficient majority, but one could either add judges (the court may expand from 23 up to 71) or simply dismiss the defendant. Rav Pappa raises the question to Abaye: should we not simply dismiss him immediately?

### Rav Pappa/Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא: פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְלִיפְטְרֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֵצְאוּ מִבֵּית דִּין מְעוּרְבָּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa says: They release him,** as he was not found liable. **And likewise Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They release him. Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But** if they ultimately release him if the court is deadlocked, why do they attempt to convince each other at all when **they should release him from the outset?** Abaye **said to him: This** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The reason is **so that they not leave the court confounded,** without reaching some definite conclusion, as this would tarnish the reputation of the court.

The *gemara* presents two versions of the answer. In one version, Rav Pappa conveys in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *potrim oto* — dismiss him. The reason: *kedei shelo yetzu mibeis din me'urvavin* — so that the *beis din* should not emerge in a state of confusion without a clear decision. If no majority can be reached, the defendant goes free.

In the alternative version, Rav Pappa asks: but the *mishnah* speaks of adding judges — why add? Rav Yosef responds: *tei kavatach* — both approaches are valid. As a *beraita* establishes from Rabbi Yosi: just as the *beis din* of 71 cannot be expanded further when it reaches a hung vote of 35-36 — the defendant is dismissed — so too the *beis din* of 23 need not necessarily expand. If extra judges happen to be available, they may be added; if not, or if the deliberation is already sufficiently extended, the defendant may be dismissed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, וּלְמָה לִי יוֹסִיפוּ? לִיפְטְרֵיהּ מִבֵּי דִּינָא קַמָּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָאֵי כְּוָותָךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד, כָּךְ אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that this is what **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But why do I** need **them to add** judges at all when **they should release him from the first court?** Once the first court did not find him liable, they should release him. Why add judges? Abaye **said to him: Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with your** opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: Just as** the court **does not add** judges **to a court of seventy-one, so too,** the court **does not add** judges **to a court of twenty-three.** If the court of twenty-three cannot arrive at a verdict, they release him.

## Aged Judgment

### Difficult Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹמֵר בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת ״נִזְדַּקֵּן הַדִּין״, וְאֵין אוֹמֵר בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת ״נִזְדַּקֵּן הַדִּין״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: In** cases of **monetary law** one **says: The judgment has grown aged,** i.e., this matter is very difficult and requires scrutiny, **but in** cases of **capital law** one **does not say: The judgment has grown aged.**

The *gemara* discusses the phrase *nizdaken hadin* — the judgment has "aged." A *beraita* states that in *dinei mamonot*, civil cases, one may say the judgment has become aged or difficult, but in *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, one may not say this. The *gemara* immediately objects: *ipcha mibei lei* — should it not be the opposite? A capital case is far weightier than a civil case; should the threshold not be higher, not lower? The *gemara* considers that *nizdaken* might mean *chacham dina* — the judgment has become wise, ripened through deliberation — rather than simply "grown difficult."

### Chief Judge's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״נִזְדַּקֵּן הַדִּין״? אִילֵּימָא קַשׁ דִּינָא, אִיפְּכָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: אֵיפוֹךְ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וּמַאי ״נִזְדַּקֵּן הַדִּין״ – חֲכַם דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **The judgment has grown aged? If we say** that the intention is: **The judgment** has **aged,** i.e., taken an extended amount of time but requires additional deliberations, if so, **he should** say **the reverse,** as it is more critical to extend deliberations in cases of capital law than in cases of monetary law. **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ said in the name of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika: Reverse** the statement, and say that in cases of monetary law one does not say: The judgment has grown aged, but in cases of capital law one does say it. **Rav Ashi said: Actually, do not reverse** it. **And what** is the meaning of: **The judgment has grown aged?** It means that **the judgment** has grown **wise,** like an elder who has acquired wisdom. In other words, the deliberations have been exhausted, and it is time to vote on a verdict.

The *mishnah* specifies that it is the *gadol she-ba-dayanim*, the senior judge of the *beis din*, who pronounces this formula. A challenge is raised: if the formula means *chacham dina* — the judgment is wise — then it is fitting for the *gadol* to declare it, as a statement of confidence in the deliberation. But if the formula means *kash dina* — the judgment has been difficult — is it appropriate for the *gadol* to say so? Would that not be embarrassing?

### Appearance and Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: גָּדוֹל שֶׁבַּדַּיָּינִים אוֹמֵר ״נִזְדַּקֵּן הַדִּין״. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חֲכַם דִּינָא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר גָּדוֹל. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ קַשׁ דִּינָא, לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָא אָמַר גָּדוֹל? כַּסּוֹפֵי הוּא דְּקָא מְיכַסֵּיף נַפְשֵׁיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the first explanation from a *baraita*: In a situation where they did not reach a decision, **the greatest among the judges says: The judgment has grown aged.** The Gemara explains the objection: **Granted, if you say** this means: **The judgment** has grown **wise, this** would be why the *baraita* states **that the greatest** judge **says** this statement, as arriving at a verdict is an honor for the court, and consequently the greatest of the court should be the one to announce it. **But if you say** this means: **The judgment** has **aged, is it not enough that the greatest** among the judges in particular **not say** so? **Must he humiliate himself** by stating that they cannot reach a verdict?

The *gemara* turns the question around. *Eino domeh mitbayesh me-atzmo le-mitbayesh me-acherim* — it is not the same to be embarrassed by oneself as to be embarrassed by others. If the *gadol* himself says the judgment was difficult, he has chosen his own embarrassment, which is more manageable than having others expose it. Conversely, if *nizdaken* means *chacham dina*, the *gadol* might appear to be praising himself — and we have the principle *yehalelcha zar ve-lo picha*, a stranger should praise you, not your own mouth.

### Self-Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין. אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה מִתְבַּיֵּישׁ מֵעַצְמוֹ, לְמִתְבַּיֵּישׁ מֵאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** the greatest of the judges must also announce that the court cannot reach a verdict. **Being shamed by oneself is not comparable to being shamed by others.** Therefore, it is preferable that the greatest of the judges state this conclusion, rather than having one of the more junior judges state it.

The verse *yehalelcha zar ve-lo picha* — let a stranger praise you, not your own mouth — would seem to prohibit the *gadol* from declaring the judgment wise. A playful modern reading of the verse changes the vowel on the *vav*, yielding *ve-ilo picha* — "and if there is no stranger, then your mouth" — but this is of course a *derashah* for rhetorical effect, not a ruling.

### Gadol's Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי מִילְּתָא דְּבֵי דִינָא, דְּאַגָּדוֹל רַמְיָא. כְּדִתְנַן: גָּמְרוּ אֶת הַדָּבָר, הָיוּ מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתָן. גָּדוֹל שֶׁבַּדַּיָּינִים אוֹמֵר: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה זַכַּאי״, ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה חַיָּיב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **A matter of the court is different, as** its administration is **incumbent upon the greatest** judge. **As we learned** in a mishna (29a): **When they finished** deliberating **the matter, they would bring in** the litigants. **The greatest of the judges** would **say: So-and-so, you are exempt** from paying; or: **So-and-so, you are liable** to pay.

The *gemara* resolves the tension with the principle *shani milta de-bei dina* — the matter of the *beis din* is categorically different. The *gadol* is not speaking about himself; he is serving as a conduit — *de-gadol ramya*, the responsibility rests upon him — and the pronouncement of the judgment flows through him regardless of how it reflects on his personal standing. This is established from the *mishnah* itself: *gamru et hadavar hayu machnisim otam* — when the deliberation concluded, they brought in the parties, and *gadol she-ba-dayanim omer*, the senior judge pronounces: *ish ploni ata zakai*, so-and-so you are acquitted; *ish ploni ata chayav*, so-and-so you are liable. The *gadol* must make this pronouncement whether it redounds to his credit or his embarrassment, because the role transcends the individual.

## Stoning Location

### Stoning House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִגְמַר הַדִּין, מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְסוֹקְלוֹ. בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When **the trial has ended** in a guilty verdict and the condemned man has been sentenced to be stoned, **he is taken out to be stoned. The place of stoning was outside the court** and a little beyond it, **as it is stated** with regard to a blasphemer: **"Take out him who has cursed** to outside the camp, and let all that heard him lay their hands upon his head, and let all the congregation stone him" (Leviticus 24:14).

The *mishnah* at the top of 42b opens the sixth chapter: *nigmar hadin* — after the verdict has been rendered, the condemned man is taken out. *Beit hasekila haya chutz l'beit hadin* — the stoning house was outside the *beis din*. The source text is from Leviticus, the end of *Parshat Emor*, in the incident of the *mekalel*, the one who blasphemed: *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the curser outside the camp. *V'samchu kol hashomim et yadeihem v'ragmu oto kol ha'edah* — all who heard lay their hands upon him, and the entire congregation stones him.

### Signal System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד עוֹמֵד עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית דִּין, וְהַסּוּדָרִין בְּיָדוֹ, וְסוּס רָחוֹק מִמֶּנּוּ כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא רוֹאֵהוּ. אוֹמֵר אֶחָד: ״יֵשׁ לְלַמֵּד עָלָיו זְכוּת״, הַלָּה מְנִיף בְּסוּדָרִין, וְהַסּוּס רָץ וּמַעֲמִידָן. וַאֲפִילּוּ הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עַל עַצְמִי זְכוּת״, מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ פְּעָמִים, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **One** man **stands at the entrance to the court, with cloths [*vehasudarin*] in his hand, and** another man sits on **a horse at a distance from him** but **where he can** still **see him.** If **one** of the judges **says:** I **can teach** a reason to **acquit him, the other,** i.e., the man with the cloths, **waves the cloths** as a signal to the man on the horse, **and the horse races** off after the court agents who are leading the condemned man to his execution, **and** he **stops them,** and they wait until the court determines whether or not the argument has substance. **And even** if **he,** the condemned man himself, **says: I can teach** a reason to **acquit myself, he is returned** to the courthouse, **even four or five times, provided that there is substance to his words.**

The procedure described in the *mishnah* is elaborate. Once the condemned man is being led away, one person remains stationed at the entrance of the *beis din* with a *sudarin* — a cloth or scarf — in his hand. Rashi notes some ambiguity about the precise distance: *chutz l'beit hadin rachok m'beit hadin kzat* — outside the *beis din*, somewhat distant. A horse is stationed at a sufficient distance from this person so that the man at the entrance can be seen by the rider. If at any moment someone comes forward with new testimony in the defendant's favor, the man at the entrance waves the cloth, and *hasus ratz u'ma'amidan* — the horse races and the procession halts.

### Last Chance

Even the condemned man himself may speak: *v'afilu hu omer yesh li lilamed al atzmi zechut* — even if he himself says he has something to say in his own favor. *Machzirin oto afilu arba v'chamesh pe'amim* — they bring him back as many as four or five times, *u'vilvad she'yesh mamash bidvarav* — provided there is genuine substance to what he says. The court is not obligated to entertain every last-minute claim, but the principle is clear: we return him as many times as necessary to hear anything that might save his life.

### Karoz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת – פְּטָרוּהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל. וְכָרוֹז יוֹצֵא לְפָנָיו: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי בֶּן פְּלוֹנִי יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל עַל שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֵדָיו. כׇּל מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹ זְכוּת יָבֹא וִילַמֵּד עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, after the condemned man is returned to the courthouse, the judges **find** a reason to **acquit him, they** acquit him and **release him** immediately. **But if** they do **not** find a reason to acquit him, **he goes out to be stoned. And a crier goes out before him** and publicly proclaims: **So-and-so, son of so-and-so, is going out to be stoned because he committed such and such a transgression. And so-and-so and so-and-so are his witnesses. Anyone who knows** of a reason to **acquit him should come** forward **and teach** it **on his behalf.**

The condemned man does not walk to the execution site in silence. A *karoz* — a herald, an announcer — walks before him and proclaims publicly: this man, son of this man, is being executed for this offense, committed at this hour, in this place; anyone who has testimony in his behalf should come forward. The public nature of the announcement, combined with the signal system at the *beis din* entrance and the capacity to return the condemned man multiple times, reflects the halakhic system's deep reluctance to bring a person to execution. The community is enlisted as a last court of appeal.

### Outside Camp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּבֵית הַסְּקִילָה חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין הֲוָה, וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And was the place of stoning** just **outside the court and nothing more?** Does it suffice that the place of execution is only a short distance from the court and no further? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The place of stoning was outside** the distance that is equivalent to the length of the **three camps** in the wilderness: The camp of the Divine Presence, the camp of the Levites, and the camp of the Israelites. In Jerusalem there were three corresponding camps: The Temple with its courtyard, the Temple Mount, and the rest of the city. The distance in the wilderness outside of the three camps corresponds to a place outside the city walls and limits.

The *gemara* now confronts a contradiction. The *mishnah* says *beit hasekila haya chutz l'beit hadin* — outside the *beis din*. A *beraita* says *beit hasekila haya chutz l'shalosh machanot* — outside three camps. Which is it? And what do these "three camps" correspond to?

In the context of the desert, the layout was: the *Mishkan* at the center, corresponding to the *Beit Hamikdash* — this is the innermost camp. Surrounding it was *Machaneh Leviyah*, the camp of the Levites. Outside that was *Machaneh Yisrael*, the camp of Israel generally. The question is whether the verse's phrase *michutz lamachaneh* — outside the camp — means merely outside the innermost precinct, or outside all three concentric camps. If the *beis din* itself is situated somewhere outside these camps, does the condemned man still need to be taken further?

### Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ. וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי הָכִי, נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ דְּאִי נָפֵיק בֵּי דִינָא וְיָתֵיב חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, עָבְדִינַן בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא מִיתְחֲזֵי בֵּית דִּין רוֹצְחִין. אִי נָמֵי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִיהְוֵי לֵיהּ הַצָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** it is **as you said,** that the place of stoning was outside the three camps. **And** the practical **difference** from the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** the *halakha* in **this** manner is **that if** it happened that **the court went out and convened outside** the **three camps,** even then **the place of stoning is set up** at a certain distance **from the court,** and not immediately adjacent to it, **so that** the court **should not appear to be a court of killers. Alternatively,** the reason the place of stoning must be distanced from the court is **so that** the condemned man **might have** a chance to be **saved,** i.e., so that during the time it takes for him to be taken from the court to the place of stoning someone will devise a claim in his favor.

The *gemara* offers two reasons for requiring the execution site to be distant from the *beis din* itself. The first: *ki heichi dela mitchell'azi beis din rotzchin* — so that the *beis din* should not appear to be a house of murderers. Rashi states it plainly: *ki heichi dela litchazi beis din rotzchin, de-genai hadavar* — it is a disgrace for the *beis din* to appear as an execution ground. The *mishnah* in Makkot already teaches that a *Sanhedrin* that executes even once in seven years is called *chavlanit* — violent. How much more so if executions appear to take place at the *beis din*'s front door? The *beis din* must remain a house of justice, not a place of *ibud nefashot*, destruction of souls.

The second reason: *inami ki heichi detihavei lei hatzala* — so that there will be an opportunity for *hatzala*, for saving the condemned man. The greater the physical distance between the *beis din* and the execution site, the more time the *karoz*'s announcement has to reach potential witnesses who might come forward with exculpatory testimony. Distance is not punitive; it is protective. It is one more structural provision aimed at preventing an irreversible outcome.

## Biblical Passages

### Curser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּפָרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִין ״חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה״. מָה לְהַלָּן – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אַף כָּאן – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived that the stoning is carried out outside the three camps? **As the Sages taught** a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"Take out him who has cursed to outside the camp"** (Leviticus 24:14). This means: **Outside** the **three camps,** i.e., even outside the camp of the Israelites. Do **you say** that he is taken **outside** the **three camps, or** is he perhaps taken **outside only one camp?** Proof is brought that he must be taken outside the three camps: **It is stated here** that the condemned man is taken **"outside the camp" and it is stated with regard to the bulls** brought as sin-offerings **that are burned** that they must be burned **"outside the camp"** (Leviticus 4:12). **Just as there,** the bulls brought as sin-offerings are burned when **outside** the **three camps, so too here,** the condemned man is taken **outside** the **three camps.**

With both reasons established, the *gemara* now turns to the textual analysis. The *beraita* teaches: *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the curser outside the camp. The *gemara* asks: when the Torah says *michutz lamachaneh* about the *mekalel*, does it mean outside one camp, or outside all three camps? Could we establish that it means three from the analogy to *parim nisrafim*, the communal sin-offering bulls that are burned outside? We know *parim nisrafim* are taken outside all three camps — so *mah lehallan chutz l'shalosh machanot, af kan chutz l'shalosh machanot*: just as there outside three camps, so too here. But the analogy immediately runs into a problem.

### Burned Bulls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהוֹצִיא אֶת כׇּל הַפָּר אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to the bulls brought as sin-offerings that are burned, **from where do we** derive that they must be burned when outside all three camps? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated about the bull brought as a sin-offering of the High Priest: **"Even the whole bull shall he carry outside the camp** unto a clean place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it" (Leviticus 4:12), meaning that he should take it **outside** the **three camps. Do you say** that he takes it **outside** the **three camps, or** is he required to take it **outside only one camp?**

How do we know that the *parim nisrafim* themselves must be taken outside three camps? The Torah says about them, too, only *michutz lamachaneh* — in the singular. So we cannot use *parim nisrafim* to prove three camps for the *mekalel* if we have not yet established that the *parim nisrafim* themselves require three camps. The *gemara* must first derive the three-camp requirement for *parim nisrafim* on independent grounds.

### Incremental Camp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ בְּפַר הָעֵדָה, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְשָׂרַף אֹתוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׂרַף אֵת הַפָּר הָרִאשׁוֹן״ – לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **When** the verse **states with regard to the bull** brought as **a communal** sin-offering: "He shall carry the bull **outside the camp,** and burn it as he burned the first bull" (Leviticus 4:21), it requires explanation, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** "outside the camp," **since it is already stated** at the end of that same verse: **"And burn it as he burned the first bull,"** which indicates that all the *halakhot* of the bull brought as a sin-offering of a High Priest apply to the bull brought as a communal sin-offering. What then does the verse mean when it states "outside the camp"? **To give it a second camp,** i.e., it indicates that it must be removed not only from the camp of the Divine Presence, corresponding to the Temple, but also from the Levite camp, corresponding to the Temple Mount.

A second *beraita* addresses the *parim nisrafim* directly: *ve-hotzi et kol ha-par el michutz lamachaneh* — and take the entire bull outside the camp. Again: *atah omer chutz l'shalosh machanot o eino ela chutz l'machaneh achat?* — does this mean three camps or one? The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the Torah uses the phrase *michutz lamachaneh* in three separate sacrificial contexts, each one incrementally adding a camp. First: in the context of *parim nisrafim* — that gives us the first camp. Second: regarding *par ha-edah*, the communal atonement bull, the Torah says *el michutz lamachaneh* again — but we already know from the earlier verse that one applies to the other (*saraf oto ka-asher saraf et ha-par ha-rishon*), so this second mention must come to add a second camp. Third: in *Parshat Tzav*, when the Torah instructs the *kohen* to remove the *deshen*, the ashes of the altar, *al michutz lamachaneh* — again, we already know the ashes are removed outside the camp; this third mention must add the third camp. Through this technique of incremental inclusion — each additional mention of *michutz lamachaneh* extending the requirement by one more camp — the *gemara* establishes that *parim nisrafim* must indeed be taken outside all three camps.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אַל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ בְּדֶשֶׁן, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״עַל שֶׁפֶךְ הַדֶּשֶׁן יִשָּׂרֵף״ – לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁלִישִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** **And when** another verse **states with regard to** the removal of **the ash:** "And he shall put off his garments, and put on other garments, and carry forth the ashes **outside the camp** to a clean place" (Leviticus 6:4), that verse also requires an explanation, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this, **since it is already stated** with regard to the bull brought as a sin-offering of a High Priest: "Even the whole bull shall he carry outside the camp to a clean place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it on wood with fire; **where the ashes are poured out** shall it be burned" (Leviticus 4:12). The repetition of "outside the camp" indicates that he is required **to give it a third camp,** i.e., teaching that it is burned when outside the Israelite camp, corresponding to the land outside Jerusalem's walls.

### Wrong Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵילַף מִשְּׁחוּטֵי חוּץ? מָה לְהַלָּן חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת, אַף כָּאן חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this derivation of the *halakha* governing one who is sentenced to be stoned from the *halakha* governing the burning of bulls brought as sin-offerings. Perhaps the place of stoning **should be learned from** the *halakha* governing **offerings slaughtered outside** the Temple courtyard, about which the verse also uses the expression "outside the camp" (see Leviticus 17:3). **Just as there,** with regard to offerings slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, these words mean: **Outside** only **one camp,** the camp of the Divine Presence, corresponding to the Temple, as the next verse states: "And he did not bring it to the door of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:4), i.e., the camp of the Divine Presence, **so too here,** the condemned man should be stoned **outside one camp.**

One might object: there is another case in the Torah where the phrase *michutz lamachaneh* appears — *shochutei chutz*, the case of sacrifices slaughtered outside the designated sacred precinct. In that case too the words *michutz lamachaneh* appear, and there it refers to a single camp. Should we not learn the rule for the *mekalel* from *shochutei chutz*, which would give us only one camp? The *gemara* frames the question directly: there are (at least) four features the *mekalel* shares with *parim nisrafim* — the verb *hotze* (taking out), the phrase *michutz lamachaneh*, the character of *machshir* (enabling or atoning for something), and the making-right of a transgression. There are also features the *mekalel* shares with *shochutei chutz*: a human actor (*adam*), the element of sin (*chotei*), the involvement of a *neshamah*, and a quality of *pigul* — abomination or fundamental wrongness.

### Machshir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִפָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִין הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן ״הוֹצֵא אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: For three reasons it is **reasonable to derive** the *halakha* governing one who is sentenced to be stoned **from** the *halakha* governing **bulls** brought as sin-offerings **that are burned.** The Gemara explains: **As** with regard to the man to be stoned for blasphemy it states: **"Take out [*hotze*]** him who has cursed **to outside the camp,"** which is similar to what is stated about the sin bull-offerings: "Even the whole bull shall he carry [*vehotzi*] outside the camp," whereas with regard to offerings slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard it merely says: "Outside the camp." Additionally, both in the case of stoning and in the case of the bulls brought as sin-offerings, going outside the camp **renders** the act **fit,** as the blasphemer may not be stoned and the bulls may not be burned inside the camp, in contrast to the case of the offerings slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, where slaughtering them outside the camp renders the act forbidden. **And** additionally, both in the case of stoning and in the case of the bulls brought as sin-offerings, being outside the camp achieves atonement, as both capital punishment and sin-offerings **atone** for sin.

The *gemara*'s resolution is elegant: *machshir mi'machshir adif* — it is preferable to learn from something that atones or validates (*machshir*) rather than from something defined by its transgression. The *parim nisrafim* are taken outside as part of a holy atonement process; *shochutei chutz* represents a wrongful act — a sacrifice offered where it should not have been. We do not derive the proper place of execution from the case of a transgression. We derive it from the case of sacred atonement.

### Moshe's Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מֹשֶׁה הֵיכָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב? בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. ״וַיּוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The matter of the location of the place of stoning can be derived from the verses themselves: **Where was Moses sitting** when the matter of the blasphemer was brought before him? **In the Levite camp,** as he was a Levite, and it was there that the blasphemer was brought for trial. **And the Merciful One said to** Moses: **"Take out him who has cursed to outside the camp,"** indicating that he should be taken **outside the Levite camp** into the Israelite camp. And later it says: "And Moses spoke to the children of Israel, **and they brought him that had cursed out of the camp,** and they stoned him with a stone. And the children of Israel did as the Lord commanded Moses" (Leviticus 24:23), teaching that he was taken **outside the Israelite camp** as well.

Rav Pappa offers a further proof that the execution site must be outside all three camps, and it operates on entirely different grounds. He asks: *Moshe heicha havah yasiv?* — where was Moshe sitting when *Hashem* commanded him to take out the *mekalel*? Moshe sat in *Machaneh Leviyah*, the second camp. When *Hashem* said *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh*, that command was issued from within *Machaneh Leviyah* — so taking the man outside the camp means, first, outside *Machaneh Leviyah*: one camp. Then the Torah continues: *vayotziu et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — and they took the *mekalel* outside the camp. But he was already outside *Machaneh Leviyah*. The fact that the Torah records a second act of taking him out means they took him yet further — outside *Machaneh Yisrael*, the third camp. The *gemara* anticipates the objection that this second verse is simply describing the execution of the command rather than adding a second movement, but responds: the verse *u'vnei Yisrael asu ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe* — the children of Israel did as *Hashem* commanded Moshe — already covers the execution of the command. The second *vayotziu* must therefore be teaching something additional: namely, that the *mekalel* was taken past *Machaneh Leviyah* and past *Machaneh Yisrael*, so that the execution site lay beyond all inhabited camps. Even if the *beis din* itself happens to be located outside any of those camps, the stoning house must be further still. With that, the *gemara* sets the stage for the opening of 43a.
