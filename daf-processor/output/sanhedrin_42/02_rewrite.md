# Sanhedrin 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Calendar Discrepancy

### Investigation Methods

The fifth chapter of Masechet Sanhedrin, *hayu bodkim*, concerns the examination of witnesses. The tradition presents three distinct frameworks for this examination. *Chakirot* are the formal investigations into the circumstances of the alleged crime: in which week, in which year, on which day. *Drishot* are inquiries into the defendant himself — who is he, what does he do? *Bdikot* are the more general questions put to witnesses. The precise boundary between these three categories was itself a matter of debate among the Rabbis, and the uncertainty about where one ends and the next begins is itself instructive.

### Mishnah Discrepancy Rule

The *mishnah* underlying our *gemara* (from the previous *amud*) addresses discrepancies in testimony concerning the day of the month. If one witness says the event took place on the second of the month and another says it was the third, *edutan kayemet* — their testimony stands. The *mishnah* explains: *shezeh yode'a be'iburo shel chodesh vezeh eino yode'a be'iburo shel chodesh* — one witness knows precisely when the new moon was declared, and the other does not, which accounts for the one-day discrepancy. However, if one witness says the third of the month and the other says the fifth, *edutan beteilah* — their testimony is void.

### Rabbi Yochanan

The *gemara* asks: *ad kama?* — how far into the month does this leniency apply? Rabbi Acha bar Chanina said in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ad rubo shel chodesh* — up to the majority of the month, roughly the middle. Beyond that point, a person ought to know which day it is, and a discrepancy of even a day or two can no longer be excused by ignorance of the new moon's declaration.

### Rava's Analysis

Rava observes that this principle is already implicit in the *mishnah* itself: *af anan nami tanina* — we also teach this. He presses the point: why should a discrepancy of two days — saying the third versus the fifth — invalidate testimony? Could we not say that one witness knows about two consecutive intercalations and the other knows only one? The *gemara* responds: *le'olam eima lach beruba nami lo yadat* — it is entirely possible for a person not to know which day of the month it is throughout most of the month. The crucial factor is the *shipura*, the *shofar* blast announcing the new moon. If there was only one day of *Rosh Chodesh* with one *shofar* blast, it is reasonable that someone missed it. But two blasts — *betrei shipura lo amar detei* — it is no longer plausible that a witness failed to register two separate announcements.

Tosafot addresses a difficulty with this entire framework. The purpose of witness examination is to protect the defendant, yet we are being asked to invalidate testimony over calendar discrepancies that might be perfectly innocent. Tosafot resolves the matter by focusing on what the two witnesses actually agree upon: if both are certain the event occurred on, say, a Monday, and they simply disagree about whether that Monday was the 27th or the 28th of the month, the testimony is accepted — they are speaking of the same day of the week, however uncertain about its numerical date. But if the discrepancy is such that there is genuine uncertainty about which day of the week the event occurred, the testimony is disqualified as insufficiently reliable.

## Birkat Levana

### Days for Blessing

Transitioning naturally from the discussion of the month and the calendar, the *gemara* addresses *birkat levana* — the blessing recited upon the renewal of the moon. The same chain of tradition — Rabbi Acha bar Chanina in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — addresses how many days after *Rosh Chodesh* one may still recite this blessing: *ad she-titmale pegimata* — until the defect of the moon is filled. The moon at the beginning of the month is incomplete — *pagum* — and the question is when the *miluy*, the filling of that defect, is considered complete.

### Moon's Shape

Rav Yaakov bar Iddi said in the name of Rav Yehuda: *ad shiva* — until the seventh day of the month. The sages of Neharde'a say: until the sixteenth, the middle of the month. Both opinions are understood to be consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's formulation, differing only in their definition of when the moon is considered "filled." One opinion holds that the moon must achieve the shape of a *yeter* — the string of a bow, a slight arc — while the other requires the shape of a *nafya* — a full round sieve, meaning the complete full moon.

### HaTov VeHaMeitiv

Rav Acha of Difti posed a question to Ravina: since we recite *mechadesh chodashim* only for perhaps seven days — the period of the moon's filling — should we not also recite *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, the blessing reserved for especially beneficial news, in response to the moon's growth? And conversely, when the moon wanes, should we not say *Dayan HaEmet*, the blessing recited upon receiving bad news?

The *gemara* responds that both blessings could in principle be said — *levarechin'hu le-travaihu*. But then it adds the decisive qualification: *keivan de-hainu orchei lo mevar'chinan* — since this is simply the way of the world, we do not recite them. Rashi explains the distinction precisely: the blessing for the moon is an act of acknowledging *Hashem*'s creation — a recognition that the world operates according to its established order. It is categorically different from *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, which is recited when something personally beneficial has occurred to a person. When we recite *birkat levana*, we are not recipients of a personal gift; we are observers of the ongoing wonder of creation. The moon's renewal is not something done *for* us — it is something we witness and attest to. *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, by contrast — such as when a second bottle of wine is brought to one's table — is a response to a personal act of kindness. The two categories do not mix.

### Shechinah

Rabbi Acha bar Chanina said in the name of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hamevarech al hachodesh bizmano ke'ilu mekabel pnei Shechinah* — whoever blesses the new moon in its proper time, it is as though he receives the face of the *Shechinah*. This is a striking statement. Why should *birkat levana* carry such spiritual weight? Several possibilities suggest themselves. *Kiddush hachodesh* — the sanctification of the new month — was the very first *mitzvah* given to the Jewish people as a nation. There is something profound about a *mitzvah* that requires presence: not merely physical compliance, but awareness of time, awareness of the moment. So many hours pass in which we are not truly present; here we are called to *mamash* be present.

The *gemara* supplies a textual proof by *gezeirah shavah*. In the passage concerning the new month, the Torah uses the word *hazeh* — "this." In another context, we encounter *zeh Eli ve'anvehu* — "this is my God and I will glorify Him" — a verse from *Shirat HaYam*. What was the greatness of *Shirat HaYam*? The tradition teaches that *ra'ata shifcha mah shelo ra'ah Yechezkel ben Buzi* — even the maidservant at the Sea witnessed what the prophet Ezekiel, in all his sublime visions, did not see. At the Sea, the *Shechinah* was accessible to all. By the same *gezeirah shavah*, when one greets the new moon with its blessing at the right moment, one taps into something of that same direct encounter with the divine presence.

### Tana d'vei R. Yishmael

A teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael makes this point with characteristic force: *ilmalei zachu Yisrael ela lehakebil pnei avihem shebashamayim kol chodesh vechodesh dayam* — if Israel had merited nothing other than to welcome their heavenly Father each and every month, that alone would be sufficient. The language is striking — *lehakebil pnei* — to receive the face, as one would welcome a distinguished guest. If the entirety of Jewish achievement had been only this monthly encounter, it would have been enough to justify our creation.

### Standing Requirement

Abaye derives a practical ruling from this: *hilkach nimrinhu me'umad* — therefore, we recite *birkat levana* standing, not sitting, in recognition of the *Shechinah*-like quality of the moment. Merimar and Mar Zutra, who were elderly, would lean upon support — some say upon one another, some say upon their servants — specifically in order to fulfill this requirement of standing. The custom thus reflects the halakhic ruling directly.

Regarding the question of whether women are obligated in *kiddush levana*: the *gemara* raises it — *hani neshei didan nami mevarchi*, implying that women too should recite the blessing — but then sets the discussion aside without resolution, much as happens in a live *shiur* when a question opens onto a thicket of connected issues. The moon's cycle, the timing of women's marriages (traditionally on Wednesday, the fourth day of the week, the day the *me'orot* were created and the moon was diminished), and the question of *mitzvat asei shehazman grama* (time-bound positive commandments from which women are generally exempt) are all interlocked. The custom has become for women not to recite *kiddush levana*, but the full analysis of the sources behind that custom goes beyond what the *gemara* resolves at this point.

### Blessing Text

Rav Acha said to Rav Ashi: in the west — that is, in Eretz Yisrael — they conclude the blessing with *baruch mechadesh chodashim*, which is in fact the *chatima*, the signature formula, of our own *birkat levana*. The full text of the blessing as it appears here is deeply moving:

> *Asher bema'amaro bara shechakim, uveruach piv kol tzeva'am, chok uzman natan lahem shelo yeshanu et tafkidam, sasim vesamechim la'asot retzon konam, po'alei emet shepe'ulatam emet. Velalevana amar shetitchadesh ateret tiferet le'amusei vaten, shehen atidim lehitkhadesh kemotah, ulefa'er leyotzram al shem kevod malchuto. Baruch Atah Hashem mechadesh chodashim.*

In His utterance He created the heavens; by the breath of His mouth, all their hosts. He gave them law and time so that they would not deviate from their appointed task. They are joyous and glad to do the will of their Creator — truthful workers whose deeds are truth. And to the moon He said: be renewed as a crown of glory for those laden from the womb, for they are destined to be renewed like her, and to glorify their Creator for the glory of His kingship. Blessed are You, *Hashem*, who renews the months.

The connection between the moon's renewal and the renewal of the Jewish people — *shehen atidim lehitkhadesh kemotah* — gives the blessing its emotional resonance. We are not merely observers of an astronomical phenomenon; we see in the waxing moon a reflection of our own destiny.

## Torah's Wars

### Mishnah Bundles

The *gemara* asks: *bemi atah motzei milchamtah shel Torah?* — in whom do you find someone capable of fighting the Torah's battles? The answer: *bemi sheyesh beyado chavilot shel Mishnah* — in the one who possesses bundles of *Mishnah*. Why *Mishnah* specifically, rather than *Torah* itself? Because the *Torah* contains apparent contradictions and repetitions, and it is the *Mishnah* that provides the tools to reconcile those tensions and harmonize what might otherwise appear inconsistent. One cannot fight for the *Torah* without the *Mishnah* as the instrument of reconciliation.

The *gemara* applies this to Rav Yosef himself — *k'Rav Yosef anafshei* — who said this of himself, citing the verse *verav tevuot be'choach shor*: *tevuah*, grain, is a reference to *Torah*, and *shor*, the ox, is the symbol of the tribe of Yosef. Rav Yosef thus understood himself as one who brought forth great *Torah* like the strength of an ox.

## Hourly Discrepancy

### Sunrise Boundary

The *mishnah* now addresses discrepancies not in the day of the month but in the hour of the day. If one witness says the event occurred at the second hour and another says the third, the testimony is generally accepted. Minor variations in time estimation do not disqualify witnesses. The critical exception, however, is when the discrepancy straddles *neitz hachamah* — sunrise. If one witness says the event occurred before sunrise and another says after sunrise, *edutan beteilah* — their testimony is void. The *gemara* extends this: even if one says it occurred right before sunrise and another says during sunrise itself, the testimony is still disqualified.

### Location Factor

Rav Shimi bar Ashi offers a penetrating observation. One might think the issue is simply the hour discrepancy between "before" and "after" sunrise. But the underlying problem is geographic. To have seen the sun rise while another person, present at the same event, had not yet seen it, the two witnesses must have been standing in different locations — perhaps one on higher ground, or one in a place with an unobstructed eastern horizon and the other not. That difference in location is itself what invalidates the testimony. The witnesses must have been in the same place observing the same event; if their reports of sunrise diverge, their physical positions diverge, and the testimony collapses.

## Court Procedure

### That Day Rule

The *mishnah* states that testimony is heard *oto hayom* — on that day, the day the defendant is present in court. The *gemara* immediately challenges this formulation: *oto hayom v'tu lo?* — only on that day and nothing more? The *mishnah* elsewhere teaches: if someone — whether a student or anyone present — says *yesh li lelamed zechut*, "I have something to say in his merit," he is brought up and seated among the judges, and *lo hayah yored misham kol hayom kulo* — he does not leave from there the entire day. Furthermore, a *beraita* teaches: *im yesh mamash bidvarav lo hayah yored misham le'olam* — if his words have genuine substance, he does not leave from there ever, meaning the court continues as long as he has something meaningful to contribute.

These are not the prosecution witnesses but character witnesses — not witnesses to the event itself, but those who wish to speak on behalf of the defendant's merit. The *talmidim* who are permitted to speak in favor of the defendant (*lelamed zechut*) may not speak to condemn him (*lelamed alav chovah*).

### Abaye Reconciles

Abaye reconciles the apparent tension between the sources. The key is the phrase *mamash bidvarav* — whether there is genuine substance to what the character witness is saying. If a person rises to speak and his remarks are substantively relevant to the defendant's case, he remains as long as necessary. If his remarks amount to nothing material — if, for instance, he can say only that the defendant bakes cookies — we would not keep him there, but we also do not simply dismiss him: *kedei shelo tehei aliyaso yeridah lo* — so that his having been called up should not become a humiliation for him. Abaye's reconciliation is thus that *oto hayom* describes the outer limit of the day, while the substantive standard of *mamash bidvarav* governs how long a character witness remains.

### Wine Prohibition

The *mishnah* notes that neither the witnesses nor the character witnesses may drink wine throughout the day of proceedings. The *gemara* asks: why is wine specifically mentioned? Rabbi Acha bar Chanina cites a verse from Mishlei: *vela-roznim ei shachar* — do not give strong drink to rulers. The word *roznim* carries the Hebrew root for *raz*, secret. The principle invoked is the well-known *nichnas yayin yatza sod* — when wine enters, secrets exit. Those who are dealing with matters of such gravity — the secrets of life and justice — must not compromise their discretion with drink.

## Hung Courts

### Dismiss or Add

When the court finds no merit in the defense and the vote stands without the required majority for conviction, what is the procedure? The *mishnah* discusses a split of 35 to 36 in a court of 71 — a genuinely hung court. And separately, what if the court of 23 stands at 12 to 11? There is not yet sufficient majority, but one could either add judges (the court may expand from 23 up to 71) or simply dismiss the defendant. Rav Pappa raises the question to Abaye: should we not simply dismiss him immediately?

### Rav Pappa/Yosef

The *gemara* presents two versions of the answer. In one version, Rav Pappa conveys in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *potrim oto* — dismiss him. The reason: *kedei shelo yetzu mibeis din me'urvavin* — so that the *beis din* should not emerge in a state of confusion without a clear decision. If no majority can be reached, the defendant goes free.

In the alternative version, Rav Pappa asks: but the *mishnah* speaks of adding judges — why add? Rav Yosef responds: *tei kavatach* — both approaches are valid. As a *beraita* establishes from Rabbi Yosi: just as the *beis din* of 71 cannot be expanded further when it reaches a hung vote of 35-36 — the defendant is dismissed — so too the *beis din* of 23 need not necessarily expand. If extra judges happen to be available, they may be added; if not, or if the deliberation is already sufficiently extended, the defendant may be dismissed.

## Aged Judgment

### Difficult Judgment

The *gemara* discusses the phrase *nizdaken hadin* — the judgment has "aged." A *beraita* states that in *dinei mamonot*, civil cases, one may say the judgment has become aged or difficult, but in *dinei nefashot*, capital cases, one may not say this. The *gemara* immediately objects: *ipcha mibei lei* — should it not be the opposite? A capital case is far weightier than a civil case; should the threshold not be higher, not lower? The *gemara* considers that *nizdaken* might mean *chacham dina* — the judgment has become wise, ripened through deliberation — rather than simply "grown difficult."

### Chief Judge's Role

The *mishnah* specifies that it is the *gadol she-ba-dayanim*, the senior judge of the *beis din*, who pronounces this formula. A challenge is raised: if the formula means *chacham dina* — the judgment is wise — then it is fitting for the *gadol* to declare it, as a statement of confidence in the deliberation. But if the formula means *kash dina* — the judgment has been difficult — is it appropriate for the *gadol* to say so? Would that not be embarrassing?

The *gemara* turns the question around. *Eino domeh mitbayesh me-atzmo le-mitbayesh me-acherim* — it is not the same to be embarrassed by oneself as to be embarrassed by others. If the *gadol* himself says the judgment was difficult, he has chosen his own embarrassment, which is more manageable than having others expose it. Conversely, if *nizdaken* means *chacham dina*, the *gadol* might appear to be praising himself — and we have the principle *yehalelcha zar ve-lo picha*, a stranger should praise you, not your own mouth.

### Self-Praise

The verse *yehalelcha zar ve-lo picha* — let a stranger praise you, not your own mouth — would seem to prohibit the *gadol* from declaring the judgment wise. A playful modern reading of the verse changes the vowel on the *vav*, yielding *ve-ilo picha* — "and if there is no stranger, then your mouth" — but this is of course a *derashah* for rhetorical effect, not a ruling.

The *gemara* resolves the tension with the principle *shani milta de-bei dina* — the matter of the *beis din* is categorically different. The *gadol* is not speaking about himself; he is serving as a conduit — *de-gadol ramya*, the responsibility rests upon him — and the pronouncement of the judgment flows through him regardless of how it reflects on his personal standing. This is established from the *mishnah* itself: *gamru et hadavar hayu machnisim otam* — when the deliberation concluded, they brought in the parties, and *gadol she-ba-dayanim omer*, the senior judge pronounces: *ish ploni ata zakai*, so-and-so you are acquitted; *ish ploni ata chayav*, so-and-so you are liable. The *gadol* must make this pronouncement whether it redounds to his credit or his embarrassment, because the role transcends the individual.

## Trial Conclusion

### Hadran

*Hadran alach hayu bodkim* — we complete the fifth chapter of Masechet Sanhedrin, the chapter of witness examination. We now proceed to the sixth chapter, *perek shishi*, which opens with the words *nigmar hadin* — the judgment has been concluded — and takes up the question of what happens after conviction: where and how is the condemned person executed?

## Stoning Location

### Stoning House

The *mishnah* at the top of 42b opens the sixth chapter: *nigmar hadin* — after the verdict has been rendered, the condemned man is taken out. *Beit hasekila haya chutz l'beit hadin* — the stoning house was outside the *beis din*. The source text is from Leviticus, the end of *Parshat Emor*, in the incident of the *mekalel*, the one who blasphemed: *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the curser outside the camp. *V'samchu kol hashomim et yadeihem v'ragmu oto kol ha'edah* — all who heard lay their hands upon him, and the entire congregation stones him.

### Signal System

The procedure described in the *mishnah* is elaborate. Once the condemned man is being led away, one person remains stationed at the entrance of the *beis din* with a *sudarin* — a cloth or scarf — in his hand. Rashi notes some ambiguity about the precise distance: *chutz l'beit hadin rachok m'beit hadin kzat* — outside the *beis din*, somewhat distant. A horse is stationed at a sufficient distance from this person so that the man at the entrance can be seen by the rider. If at any moment someone comes forward with new testimony in the defendant's favor, the man at the entrance waves the cloth, and *hasus ratz u'ma'amidan* — the horse races and the procession halts.

### Last Chance

Even the condemned man himself may speak: *v'afilu hu omer yesh li lilamed al atzmi zechut* — even if he himself says he has something to say in his own favor. *Machzirin oto afilu arba v'chamesh pe'amim* — they bring him back as many as four or five times, *u'vilvad she'yesh mamash bidvarav* — provided there is genuine substance to what he says. The court is not obligated to entertain every last-minute claim, but the principle is clear: we return him as many times as necessary to hear anything that might save his life.

### Karoz

The condemned man does not walk to the execution site in silence. A *karoz* — a herald, an announcer — walks before him and proclaims publicly: this man, son of this man, is being executed for this offense, committed at this hour, in this place; anyone who has testimony in his behalf should come forward. The public nature of the announcement, combined with the signal system at the *beis din* entrance and the capacity to return the condemned man multiple times, reflects the halakhic system's deep reluctance to bring a person to execution. The community is enlisted as a last court of appeal.

### Outside Camp

The *gemara* now confronts a contradiction. The *mishnah* says *beit hasekila haya chutz l'beit hadin* — outside the *beis din*. A *beraita* says *beit hasekila haya chutz l'shalosh machanot* — outside three camps. Which is it? And what do these "three camps" correspond to?

In the context of the desert, the layout was: the *Mishkan* at the center, corresponding to the *Beit Hamikdash* — this is the innermost camp. Surrounding it was *Machaneh Leviyah*, the camp of the Levites. Outside that was *Machaneh Yisrael*, the camp of Israel generally. The question is whether the verse's phrase *michutz lamachaneh* — outside the camp — means merely outside the innermost precinct, or outside all three concentric camps. If the *beis din* itself is situated somewhere outside these camps, does the condemned man still need to be taken further?

### Appearance

The *gemara* offers two reasons for requiring the execution site to be distant from the *beis din* itself. The first: *ki heichi dela mitchazi beis din rotzchin* — so that the *beis din* should not appear to be a house of murderers. Rashi states it plainly: *ki heichi dela litchazi beis din rotzchin, de-genai hadavar* — it is a disgrace for the *beis din* to appear as an execution ground. The *mishnah* in Makkot already teaches that a *Sanhedrin* that executes even once in seven years is called *chavlanit* — violent. How much more so if executions appear to take place at the *beis din*'s front door? The *beis din* must remain a house of justice, not a place of *ibud nefashot*, destruction of souls.

### Opportunity

The second reason: *inami ki heichi detihavei lei hatzala* — so that there will be an opportunity for *hatzala*, for saving the condemned man. The greater the physical distance between the *beis din* and the execution site, the more time the *karoz*'s announcement has to reach potential witnesses who might come forward with exculpatory testimony. Distance is not punitive; it is protective. It is one more structural provision aimed at preventing an irreversible outcome.

## Biblical Passages

### Curser

With both reasons established, the *gemara* now turns to the textual analysis. The *beraita* teaches: *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the curser outside the camp. The *gemara* asks: when the Torah says *michutz lamachaneh* about the *mekalel*, does it mean outside one camp, or outside all three camps? Could we establish that it means three from the analogy to *parim nisrafim*, the communal sin-offering bulls that are burned outside? We know *parim nisrafim* are taken outside all three camps — so *mah lehallan chutz l'shalosh machanot, af kan chutz l'shalosh machanot*: just as there outside three camps, so too here. But the analogy immediately runs into a problem.

### Burned Bulls

How do we know that the *parim nisrafim* themselves must be taken outside three camps? The Torah says about them, too, only *michutz lamachaneh* — in the singular. So we cannot use *parim nisrafim* to prove three camps for the *mekalel* if we have not yet established that the *parim nisrafim* themselves require three camps. The *gemara* must first derive the three-camp requirement for *parim nisrafim* on independent grounds.

### Incremental Camp

A second *beraita* addresses the *parim nisrafim* directly: *ve-hotzi et kol ha-par el michutz lamachaneh* — and take the entire bull outside the camp. Again: *atah omer chutz l'shalosh machanot o eino ela chutz l'machaneh achat?* — does this mean three camps or one? The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the Torah uses the phrase *michutz lamachaneh* in three separate sacrificial contexts, each one incrementally adding a camp. First: in the context of *parim nisrafim* — that gives us the first camp. Second: regarding *par ha-edah*, the communal atonement bull, the Torah says *el michutz lamachaneh* again — but we already know from the earlier verse that one applies to the other (*saraf oto ka-asher saraf et ha-par ha-rishon*), so this second mention must come to add a second camp. Third: in *Parshat Tzav*, when the Torah instructs the *kohen* to remove the *deshen*, the ashes of the altar, *al michutz lamachaneh* — again, we already know the ashes are removed outside the camp; this third mention must add the third camp. Through this technique of incremental inclusion — each additional mention of *michutz lamachaneh* extending the requirement by one more camp — the *gemara* establishes that *parim nisrafim* must indeed be taken outside all three camps.

### Wrong Slaughter

One might object: there is another case in the Torah where the phrase *michutz lamachaneh* appears — *shochutei chutz*, the case of sacrifices slaughtered outside the designated sacred precinct. In that case too the words *michutz lamachaneh* appear, and there it refers to a single camp. Should we not learn the rule for the *mekalel* from *shochutei chutz*, which would give us only one camp? The *gemara* frames the question directly: there are (at least) four features the *mekalel* shares with *parim nisrafim* — the verb *hotze* (taking out), the phrase *michutz lamachaneh*, the character of *machshir* (enabling or atoning for something), and the making-right of a transgression. There are also features the *mekalel* shares with *shochutei chutz*: a human actor (*adam*), the element of sin (*chotei*), the involvement of a *neshamah*, and a quality of *pigul* — abomination or fundamental wrongness.

### Machshir

The *gemara*'s resolution is elegant: *machshir mi'machshir adif* — it is preferable to learn from something that atones or validates (*machshir*) rather than from something defined by its transgression. The *parim nisrafim* are taken outside as part of a holy atonement process; *shochutei chutz* represents a wrongful act — a sacrifice offered where it should not have been. We do not derive the proper place of execution from the case of a transgression. We derive it from the case of sacred atonement.

### Moshe's Location

Rav Pappa offers a further proof that the execution site must be outside all three camps, and it operates on entirely different grounds. He asks: *Moshe heicha havah yasiv?* — where was Moshe sitting when *Hashem* commanded him to take out the *mekalel*? Moshe sat in *Machaneh Leviyah*, the second camp. When *Hashem* said *hotze et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh*, that command was issued from within *Machaneh Leviyah* — so taking the man outside the camp means, first, outside *Machaneh Leviyah*: one camp. Then the Torah continues: *vayotziu et hamekalel el michutz lamachaneh* — and they took the *mekalel* outside the camp. But he was already outside *Machaneh Leviyah*. The fact that the Torah records a second act of taking him out means they took him yet further — outside *Machaneh Yisrael*, the third camp. The *gemara* anticipates the objection that this second verse is simply describing the execution of the command rather than adding a second movement, but responds: the verse *u'vnei Yisrael asu ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe* — the children of Israel did as *Hashem* commanded Moshe — already covers the execution of the command. The second *vayotziu* must therefore be teaching something additional: namely, that the *mekalel* was taken past *Machaneh Leviyah* and past *Machaneh Yisrael*, so that the execution site lay beyond all inhabited camps. Even if the *beis din* itself happens to be located outside any of those camps, the stoning house must be further still. With that, the *gemara* sets the stage for the opening of 43a.
