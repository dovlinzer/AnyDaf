# Bava Kamma 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview & Structure

### Five Muadim

Daf 27 spans the conclusion of the second *perek* and the opening of the third, and while the tractate as a whole is admirably structured—opening with *klalim* in the first *perek* before moving to individual *avot nezikin*—the division of material between individual chapters is somewhat less tidy. The second *perek* was dominated by discussions of *shen* and *regel*, but by its end we were already dealing with questions of *shor muad* versus *shor tam* and the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva concerning *keren b'reshut hanizak*—whether goring in a private domain incurs half or full damages. These discussions were themselves elaborations of a passage near the end of the first *perek* that enumerated five categories of *muadim*: *shen*, *regel*, *keren b'reshut hanizak*, *adam*, and *shor hamuad*.

### 2nd Perek Recap

The second *perek* addressed several of those five categories but left others only partially developed. The discussions of *shor muad*, the threshold of three gorings, and the reversibility of *muad* status were introduced but not fully resolved. Similarly, the chapter opened the discussion of *adam muad l'olam* without bringing it to completion.

### Adam Muad L'olam Start

We now find ourselves in the middle of that *adam muad l'olam* discussion, which continues past the formal chapter break into the third *perek*. The organizing question that carries us forward is: what precisely does it mean that a person is always *muad*, and what are the consequences of that principle for the full range of payments in cases of personal injury?

---

## Adam Muad & Arba'ah

### Nezek vs. Personal Injury

A foundational point was established earlier in the *sugya*: the principle of *adam muad l'olam*—that a person always pays full damages regardless of negligence or intent—applies specifically to *nezek*, to the payment for damage itself. However, the additional payments that accompany personal injury claims—*shevet*, *ripuy*, *boshet*, and *tza'ar*—do not apply at all times. The precise threshold under which those four payments are triggered was left unspecified by the *Gemara*, and that is the question we now turn to clarify.

### Rashi: Meizid Threshold

Rashi, commenting at the bottom of 26a, writes: *d'lo mechayev arba'ah devarim ela u'meizid o karov l'meizid, dumiya d'chabuna d'anashim, d'hasam ksiv ripuy v'shevet*. The *pasuk* from which *ripuy* and *shevet* are derived describes a scenario of people fighting—*rak shivto yiten v'rapo yerape*—an inherently intentional context. Rashi therefore reasons that to be liable for the *arba'ah devarim*, one need not be fully *mitkavein* to cause harm, but neither can one be entirely without culpability. His formulation is *meizid o karov l'meizid*: intentional or close to intentional. The question, then, is precisely where that threshold falls.

### Negligence Examples

Yesterday's discussion surveyed several scenarios that intuitively seem like significant negligence but which the *Gemara* nevertheless ruled do not reach the threshold for *arba'ah devarim*. The most striking is the case of someone who shoots an arrow intending it to travel a certain distance and it travels farther, injuring someone. What could be more negligent than firing an arrow in a direction where someone might be? And yet this falls short. Equally, the case of someone sunbathing on a rooftop who knew they had a stone resting on them, forgot about it, then stood up—also deemed insufficiently negligent. Both of these cases are exempt from the additional payments. Having established the lower end of the spectrum, we now examine what is required to cross the threshold.

---

## Arba'ah Devarim Cases

### Vessel Throwing

The *Gemara* opens with a series of rulings attributed to *Rava* involving objects thrown from rooftops. If one throws a vessel from a roof that was going to land on a cushion—breaking the fall—and someone removed the cushion, both parties are exempt: the thrower because it was going to land safely, and the remover of the cushion because the act was indirect. Similarly, if a vessel was thrown from a roof and was going to hit the ground, and someone broke it mid-fall, that second person is exempt because the vessel was already effectively destroyed. In each of these scenarios, the analysis of liability turns on precisely who performed the operative act of damage and whether the damage was already in motion before their intervention.

### Baby & Ox: Kofer

A more arresting case follows. If someone threw an infant from a rooftop and an ox gored the infant in mid-air, the question becomes whether the ox's owner owes *kofer*—the atonement payment for an ox that kills a person. This is a *plugta* between Rabbi Yishmael the son of Yochanan ben Beroka and the Rabbis, derived from the *pasuk*: *ve-natan pidyon nafsho*, "he shall give the redemption of his soul."

### Demei Dispute

A *brayta* records the dispute: the Rabbis hold that *kofer* is assessed as *demei nizak*—the market value of the victim, assessed as one would assess a slave's worth. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Yochanan ben Beroka holds it is *demei mazik*—the value of the owner of the ox.

The conceptual difference is significant and bears explanation. The *chiddush* of the Torah's law of *kofer* in contrast to ancient Near Eastern practice is that the ox's owner does not forfeit his own life, nor the lives of his children—the *pasuk* *o ven yigach o vat yigach ke-mishpat ha-zeh ye'aseh lo* is interpreted not as license to execute the owner's family but as establishing the principle that the obligation falls to a monetary substitute. How one understands that substitute depends on which life one is monetizing: the life taken, or the life that in justice deserved to be taken.

If *kofer* is the redemption of the victim's life, then one assigns a monetary figure to the person who was killed—the slave-market valuation. If *kofer* is a substitute for the life of the owner, then one assigns a monetary figure to what the owner would be worth.

The relevance to our case is direct: an infant who has been thrown from a rooftop has no slave-market value—who would pay for a child about to strike the ground? Under *demei nizak*, no *kofer* is owed at all. Under *demei mazik*, the ox's owner pays based on his own value. The question of whether *kofer* is owed in this scenario, and how much, thus turns entirely on which opinion one follows.

## Falling Person

### Erection Case

*Rava* now raises the case that, in pedagogical settings, is difficult to forget. A person falls from a rooftop and, in the course of the fall, happens to have an erection and winds up penetrating a woman below. *Chayav be-arba'ah devarim*—he is liable for four of the five payments: *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripuy*, and *shevet*. But, the *Gemara* adds, *u-ve-viah lo kanah*—the act of penetration does not constitute a halachically valid act of intercourse, and therefore if the woman were his *yevamah*, a brother's widow requiring *yibum*, this incident would not fulfill that obligation.

The *Gemara* thus presents a double result. On the one hand, the man is *chayav* for *arba'ah devarim*. On the other, the act is not legally cognizable as an act of intercourse. The *Gemara* is explicit that *boshet* is not included—*eino chayav al ha-boshet ad she-mitkavein*—because *boshet* requires actual intention, and as we are about to see, even the degree of negligence sufficient for the other four payments does not suffice for *boshet*.

But there is a double difficulty here. First, why is this case more negligent than the arrow-shooting scenario, which was already held insufficient for *arba'ah devarim*? Second, why does the erection not render the act legally valid for *yibum*?

### Kishuy & Yibum

The second difficulty raises the principle of *ein kishuy ella loda'at*—the assumption that an erection occurs only when the person desires it. This principle has practical *halachic* consequences: if a man is threatened with death unless he commits adultery, he is held liable for the forbidden act even though he acted under duress. The general rule is that one who acts under compulsion is exempt—*ones rachmanah patrei*—as in the case of killing under duress, where the actor, while obligated to give up his life rather than kill, is not himself considered a murderer. But sexual acts are treated differently precisely because of *ein kishuy ella loda'at*: since the erection is taken as evidence that desire was present, the act is deemed internally motivated, not externally coerced.

One might therefore reason that since the falling man has an erection, the act is treated as internally motivated and intentional, and this is why he is *chayav* for *arba'ah devarim*. But if that were the logic, the act should also be valid for *yibum*, since the *mishna* in *yevamos* teaches that *yibum* is effectuated whether the act was intentional or not—*bein b'shogeg bein b'mezid kanah*. This line of reasoning does not hold.

### Ruach Metzuyah

Rashi resolves both difficulties with a single explanation. He writes: *she-nafal b'ruach metzuya de-havei leh karov l'meizid*—the case is one where the man fell due to a common, predictable wind, *ruach metzuyah*. He was standing at the edge of the roof in conditions where a fall was highly foreseeable. That foreseeability constitutes the requisite degree of negligence—*karov l'meizid*—that triggers the *arba'ah devarim*.

Why, then, does the act not constitute *yibum*? Rashi explains: the *mishna* that says *yibum* is effectuated even unintentionally means the man intended some act of intercourse, even if with a different woman—*nitkaven l'biat ishah acheret*. He was performing an intentional act of sex and simply mistook the identity of his partner. Here, by contrast, *lo nitkaven l'shum biah*—he had no intention whatsoever of performing an act of intercourse. The act was entirely accidental. Therefore it does not effectuate *yibum*, and the erection is simply irrelevant to the analysis.

The explanation thus rests entirely on *ruach metzuyah*—the predictability of the wind. This now establishes the critical threshold: one is *chayav* for *arba'ah devarim* when the negligence rises to the level associated with a *ruach metzuyah*, the kind of foreseeable risk for which a *shomer chinam* would bear liability. The previous cases—the arrow, the forgotten stone—must therefore be understood as falling below this threshold.

## Boshet Payments

### Boshet Intent

Having established the threshold for the four payments of *shevet*, *ripuy*, *tza'ar*, and *nezek*, the *Gemara* now turns to the distinct standard applicable to *boshet*. The *mishna* states explicitly: *eino chayav al ha-boshet ad she-mitkavein*—one is not liable for the embarrassment payment unless one actually intends it. Negligence, even gross negligence, is not sufficient.

### Unexpected Wind

*Rava* presents a series of cases that map the boundaries precisely. If a person falls from a roof due to an unexpected wind—*ruach she'eina metzuyah*—and in falling damages and embarrasses someone below, he is *chayav* only for *nezek*, by virtue of *adam muad l'olam*, and exempt from the *arba'ah devarim* entirely, because a *ruach she'eina metzuyah* does not rise to *peshia*.

### Expected Wind

If the wind was expected—*ruach metzuyah*—and he damaged and embarrassed someone, he is *chayav* for *arba'ah devarim* but remains exempt from *boshet*, because even this degree of gross negligence does not constitute the intention that *boshet* requires. This passage makes explicit precisely where Rashi derived his reading of *ruach metzuyah* as the operative standard.

### Flipping Midair

The case then grows more interesting: *nithapeich chayav af al ha-boshet*—if the falling person deliberately flipped in midair, he is liable even for *boshet*. Rashi explains that the man turned himself so as to land on the person below rather than on the hard ground, preferring to break his fall at the other person's expense.

This is a remarkable *chiddush* on two levels. First, the man has no ill will toward the person below—he is acting entirely out of self-preservation, not malice. Second, he is not even intending to cause embarrassment; he is intending to cushion his fall. Yet this is sufficient for *boshet*. The reason becomes clear from the supporting *brayta*.

### Woman Intervening

The *Gemara* cites the *pasuk* concerning a woman who intervenes in a fight between her husband and another man and grabs the other man improperly—for which the Torah prescribes that her hand be cut off, understood by the Rabbis as an obligation to pay *boshet*. The *brayta* derives from the doubled language of the verse—*v'shalcha yada v'hechezika*—the principle that *keivan she-nitkavein le-hazik, af al pi she-lo nitkavein le-vayeish*: once a person intends to cause injury, even without any specific intent to embarrass, liability for *boshet* follows.

The levels can now be stated precisely. *Nezek* applies even in cases of complete *ones*. *Shevet*, *ripuy*, and *tza'ar* require *peshia*—the gross negligence associated with *ruach metzuyah*. *Boshet* requires *kavana le-hazik*—intent to cause harm—but not *kavana le-vayeish*, intent to cause embarrassment specifically. And the *chiddush* of the midair-flipping case is that deliberately performing an act one knows will likely cause injury to another constitutes *kavana le-hazik*, even when the motivation is entirely self-interested and even when one would prefer not to injure the other person at all.

## Coal on Heart

### Heart vs. Garment

*Rava* presents a pair of cases that establish a fundamental distinction. If someone places a burning coal on another person's heart and the person dies, the one who placed it is exempt—from both murder liability and possibly from the *galus* obligation. If someone places a burning coal on another person's garment and the garment burns, the one who placed it is liable.

### Self-Rescue

The logic of the first case rests on a foundational assumption: a person will always act to save their own life. It follows that if the person on whose heart the coal was placed did nothing to remove it, they effectively brought the death upon themselves. The *mazik* cannot be held responsible for a consequence the victim could and should have prevented.

*Rava* observes that both rulings are derivable from *mishnayot* already known. The case of the coal on the heart is established by the *mishna*: *kavsho le-toch ha'or o le-toch ha-mayim ve-eino yachol la'alot misham u-met chayav*—if one held a person's head underwater and the person could not escape, it is murder. But *dechapo le-toch ha'or le-toch ha-mayim... yachol la'alot misham u-met patur*—if one pushed a person into fire or water and the person could have gotten out but did not, one is exempt. The *chiddush* runs in both directions: holding someone's head underwater is murder even without pushing; pushing someone into water is not murder if they could have escaped. The organizing principle is that a person is assumed to do whatever is necessary to save their own life, and if they failed to, the responsibility reverts to them.

### Garment Tear Mishna

The case of the coal on the garment is established by a different *mishna*: *kera k'suti shaber kadi chayav*—if someone says "tear my garment" or "break my pitcher," and you do so, you must still pay, because permission to destroy does not automatically include permission to destroy without reimbursement. Only if the owner explicitly adds *al menat liftor*—on condition that you be exempt—are you free from payment.

This *mishna* teaches that when it comes to a person's property rather than their own body, we do not interpret the failure to remove a threat as acceptance of the loss without compensation. A person may allow their garment to burn—with the intention to collect compensation afterward. Unlike the case of one's own body, where a person is presumed to take all possible action to preserve their life, a person's behavior regarding their property may rationally include allowing damage with a view to recovery. Therefore the absence of self-help does not exempt the party who caused the damage.

The distinction drawn here is illuminating: when it is one's own body, the assumption of self-preservation is absolute. When it is one's property, the calculation is different—a person might reason, "you destroy my things, you will replace them." The victim's inaction cannot therefore be construed as releasing the tortfeasor from liability.

A question worth noting: the *mishna*'s ruling on the coal-on-the-heart case rests on the assumption that the victim was able to remove it. But since the person died, one might ask how we determine that removal was possible—perhaps they were paralyzed by fear, or otherwise unable. The *Gemara* appears to work with the presumption that the person was able to act, leaving the edge cases without explicit resolution.

### Slave Coal

*Rava* then poses a more difficult question: *hiniach gachelet al lev avdo mahu?* If someone places a coal on the heart of a bound slave—and Tosafot understands the slave to be bound, since otherwise the question is trivial—what is the rule? Is the slave treated *ke-gufo dami*, like the owner's own body, such that the owner is presumed to remove it and his failure to do so is self-inflicted loss? Or is the slave treated *ke-mamono dami*, like property, such that one might allow the slave to be destroyed with the expectation of reimbursement?

This question probes the deep ambiguity of slavery as a legal category. A slave is the owner's property in a formal sense, yet is also a human being whose life the owner is expected to value. Does the owner treat the slave as an extension of himself, or purely as a fungible asset?

### Ox Coal

The *Gemara* then extends the question: if one places a coal on a bound ox, what is the rule? Even if the slave is treated like the owner's own body—since the slave is a human being—does that reasoning extend to an animal? The owner might more readily accept the destruction of the ox and seek reimbursement.

*Rava* ultimately resolves both questions: *avdo ke-gufo, shoro ke-mamono*—one's slave is treated like one's own body, and one's ox like one's property. In the case of the slave, the owner's presumed recognition of the slave's humanity means we expect the owner to remove the coal; if he does not, the loss is his. In the case of the ox, the owner may calculate that reimbursement is adequate, and therefore the person who placed the coal cannot hide behind that expectation—they remain the proximate cause of the damage. This resolution captures a nuanced hierarchy: inanimate property, living animals, and human beings who are legally owned occupy distinct rungs, and the law's expectations of owner behavior track that hierarchy.

## Vessel in Street

### Public Domain Case

With the conclusion of that discussion, the *Gemara* moves to the next *perek*. The opening *mishna* presents the following scenario: *ha-meniach et ha-kad bi-reshut ha-rabbim*—someone leaves a vessel in the public domain. Another person comes along, trips on it, and breaks it—*patur*, exempt. If, however, the person who tripped suffered injury from the fall, *ba'al ha-chavis chayav be-nizko*—the owner of the vessel must pay for the injury, because by leaving the vessel in *reshut ha-rabbim* the owner created a *bor*.

The structure of the ruling is thus: the one who placed the vessel is liable when someone is injured, but the one who broke it is not. On the first point—liability for injury—the owner's negligence is clear; he created a public hazard. But the second point is puzzling.

### Adam Muad Puzzle

We just established that *adam muad l'olam*. If a person is always liable for the damage they cause, regardless of intent or negligence, why is the person who tripped and broke the vessel exempt? The *Gemara*'s initial response—*ibay lei l'iyunei u-mezal*, he should have watched where he was going—only deepens the puzzle, because it implies that liability turns on whether one was negligent, which is precisely what *adam muad l'olam* was supposed to eliminate. We will return to this.

---

## Kad vs. Chavis

### Term Interchangeability

Before addressing the substantive puzzle, the *Gemara* pauses to note a terminological inconsistency in the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* opens by describing a *kad*—a pitcher—being placed in *reshut ha-rabbim*, but concludes by referring to the owner as *ba'al ha-chavis*—the owner of the barrel. Similarly, the *Gemara* cites two additional *mishnayot* that oscillate between the two terms. In one *mishna*, two people are walking through *reshut ha-rabbim*, one carrying a barrel and one carrying a beam, and the pitcher of one breaks on the beam of the other; the text shifts from *chavis* to *kad* mid-discussion. In another, one man is carrying a barrel of wine and another a pitcher of honey, and when the barrel of honey cracks and the wine is poured to save the honey, the terms again shift.

Rav Pappa concludes: *hainu kad hainu chavis*—for purposes of the *mishna*'s language, *kad* and *chavis* refer to the same thing. But this raises a practical question: *le-mai nafqa minah?* In what context does it matter that these terms are interchangeable?

### Burden of Proof

The answer is: *le-mikach u-mimkar*—commercial transactions. If a buyer and seller agree on a transaction using the word *chavis*, and the seller delivers what he calls a *kad*, a dispute may arise over whether they meant the same thing. The *Gemara* defines the operative case: not a place where the terms are clearly distinct, nor a place where everyone uses them interchangeably, but rather a place where most people use each term in its standard sense—a *kad* is a pitcher, a *chavis* is a barrel—yet a minority use them interchangeably. One might think that we follow the majority usage to adjudicate the dispute. The *Gemara* rejects this: *ein holchin be-mamon achar ha-rov*—in monetary matters we do not follow the majority.

Instead, the governing principle is *hamotzi mi-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah*—whoever seeks to extract money from the other bears the burden of proof. If the seller has the money in hand and the buyer claims he wanted a barrel while the seller claims he delivered what was agreed upon (a pitcher), the buyer must prove his claim. Statistical likelihood, even if the majority of people in that locale use the term to mean a barrel, is insufficient to compel payment.

One might ask: could the price paid not serve as evidence? Tosafot addresses this directly. In some cases, the amount paid could indeed serve as proof—and there is another *Gemara* that says as much. But Tosafot notes that there are scenarios where the price offers no probative guidance: if no price was agreed upon in advance, or if the dispute is about quantity rather than type, or if one party agreed to buy a fixed monetary amount of a product, and it was delivered in the wrong vessel size. In those cases, the amount paid resolves nothing, and we are left with *hamotzi mi-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah* as the controlling principle. The point is that *rov* is not a mechanism for extracting money; only clear proof suffices for that.

## Three Views Debate

### Rav's View

Returning now to the substantive question of why the person who broke the vessel in *reshut ha-rabbim* is exempt, the *Gemara* presents three *amora'im* offering three different limiting constructions of the *mishna*.

The school of Rav explains: *be-mila reshut ha-rabbim kulloh chaviyos*—the street was completely filled with barrels, leaving no path for passage. In that case, the person who tripped had no choice; they could not avoid the obstacle. Since the barrel-placer has illegitimately occupied the entire public thoroughfare, the pedestrian's act of going through, even at the cost of breaking the vessel, is not blameworthy.

### Shmuel: Nighttime

Shmuel says: *ba'afilah shanu*—the *mishna* is speaking of nighttime. When it is dark, a person cannot see obstacles on the ground, and therefore their failure to avoid the barrel is not negligence. The exemption follows from the impossibility of watchfulness under those conditions.

### R. Yochanan: Corner

Rabbi Yochanan says: *be-keren zavis*—the vessel was placed around a corner. Even a person walking carefully and attentively could not see around a corner to anticipate what lay ahead. The exemption again rests on the impossibility, or at least the unreasonableness, of expecting avoidance under those physical conditions.

---

## Rav Pappa's View

### Intent to Break

Rav Pappa observes that the *mishna* as written cannot be reconciled with Rav's explanation. The *mishna* specifies the case of one who *niskal*—who tripped—and says that person is exempt. But if the barrel-placer has filled the entire street, Rav Pappa argues, then even someone who *intentionally* broke the barrel to get through should be exempt, not merely one who tripped. Why would the *mishna* need to specify the tripping case if the entire street was blocked?

Rav Zevid answers in the name of Rava: one can indeed read the *mishna* according to Rav's explanation—the street was entirely blocked—and in that case, yes, intentional breaking would also yield exemption from payment. The reason the *mishna* specifies the *niskal* case is because of what follows in the *mishna*: the ruling that the vessel-placer owes compensation to the person who tripped and was injured.

### Self-Damage

That clause applies specifically to the one who tripped—*davka niskal*. If the pedestrian intentionally broke the barrel to get through, and was injured in the process, they cannot then seek compensation from the barrel-placer. *Mai ta'ama? Hu d'azik anafshei.* He knowingly entered into a situation of deliberate physical contact with the obstacle; the risk of self-injury is his to bear. But the one who tripped accidentally, not knowing the barrel was there, retains the right to compensation for their injuries.

Two significant issues are flagged by this passage. First, the apparent right to intentionally break barrels that block a public thoroughfare without incurring liability—a form of self-help that raises the question of *avid inish dina l'nafshei*, taking the law into one's own hands, which will be addressed later. Second, the more immediate puzzle about *adam muad l'olam*: the entire discussion assumes that the pedestrian's liability turns on whether they were negligent, yet we established that negligence is irrelevant to *adam muad l'olam*. The *Gemara* does not raise this directly but proceeds as though negligence matters—and the *rishonim* step in to resolve it.

## E.Y. Teaching

### Rav Ulla

A tradition from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rav Ulla (some say Rabbi Ilai) advances a more sweeping claim: *lefi she-ein darkan shel bnei adam le-hitbonen bi-drachim*—people simply do not watch where they are going. This is a behavioral generalization: pedestrians are, as a rule, inattentive. Therefore the *mishna* need not invoke special circumstances like darkness or corners; in general, one is exempt for breaking a vessel left in *reshut ha-rabbim* because it is not the normal practice of people to be scanning the ground as they walk.

### Nahardea Case

A case then arose in Nahardea, where someone broke a barrel in *reshut ha-rabbim* under ordinary daytime conditions—not nighttime, not around a corner. Shmuel ruled the breaker liable. This is consistent with Shmuel's own explanation of the *mishna*: exemption applies only at night, whereas in the daytime, one should watch where one is going.

### Olive Press Season

A case in Pumbedita raised a similar question, and Rava ruled the breaker liable. The *Gemara* asks: does this mean Rava agrees with Shmuel that daytime liability is the default? Rav Pappa explains that the Pumbedita case was a particular situation—*karna d'atzra hava*, it was the olive-pressing season and the incident occurred near the olive press. Rashi notes that during the olive-pressing season, it is well known and entirely expected that pitchers and containers will be left in the street in front of the press. *D'keivan d'bireshut ka avdi*—since those engaged in the olive trade have implicit permission to use the street in that way, and since anyone passing through that area at that time of year is on notice of what to expect—*ibay lei l'iyunei u-meizal*, one is obligated to watch where one is going under those circumstances. Rava's ruling does not therefore contradict the Eretz Yisrael principle that people generally need not watch where they are going; it represents a case where a reasonable person would know to be careful.

The upshot is a spectrum of positions. Shmuel holds that in the daytime, one is generally expected to watch where one is going, and one is *chayav* for breaking an obstacle unless a special circumstance—night, a corner—intervenes. The Eretz Yisrael tradition holds that in general people are not expected to watch where they are going, but there are circumstances—such as the olive-press season in a location where containers are known to be present—where the expectation of attentiveness is reasonable.

## Tosafot vs. Ramban

### Tosafot Position

The persistent puzzle—how does any of this fit with *adam muad l'olam*?—is addressed by two *rishonim* who offer fundamentally different answers.

Tosafot's approach is to limit the scope of *adam muad l'olam*. When the *mishnah* and *Gemara* say that a person is always *muad*, they do not mean literally without qualification. Rather, Tosafot understands *adam muad l'olam* to mean that a person is liable across a wider range of negligence than we might otherwise require—in particular, that liability does not require full *peshia* but extends down to intermediate degrees of negligence, including something analogous to the level of loss or theft for which a *shomer sachar* would be *chayav*, or even lesser degrees. But at the very low end of the spectrum—where the conduct is so minimally negligent, or so close to an *ones*, that it would be unreasonable to hold a person accountable—the person is indeed exempt, and *adam muad l'olam* does not apply.

On this reading, the cases of walking in *reshut ha-rabbim* without watching where one is going represent a category of such minimal negligence that the principle of *muad l'olam* simply does not reach them. Tosafot is driven to this conclusion by the *sugya* before us: the *Gemara* asks *amai patur?*—why is the person exempt?—and proceeds to examine whether negligence was present. That entire analysis presupposes that negligence is a relevant variable for liability, which would not be the case if *adam muad l'olam* were truly absolute.

### Ramban Position

The Ramban offers a more striking solution. He accepts that *adam muad l'olam* is genuinely absolute. Even if a whirlwind lifted a person off the ground and deposited them on someone else's vessel, shattering it, the person would be *chayav*—complete *ones* is no defense. The principle is unqualified.

What, then, explains the exemption in *reshut ha-rabbim*? The Ramban's answer is: contributory negligence on the part of the *nizkak*, the victim—the person who placed the vessel. The *Gemara*'s discussion of whether pedestrians are expected to watch where they are going is not, for the Ramban, a discussion of whether the *mazik* was negligent. It is a discussion of whether the *nizkak* was negligent in placing the vessel there.

### Nizkak Negligence

If people generally do not watch where they are going—as the Eretz Yisrael tradition teaches—and you know this, then placing your vessel in the public thoroughfare is itself an act of self-induced risk. You know that people will not see it; you placed it there anyway. When the pedestrian trips and breaks it, you have contributed to, indeed invited, the damage. It is this contributory negligence of the *nizkak* that generates the exemption for the *mazik*—not any deficiency in the *mazik*'s own negligence.

Conversely, when the *Gemara* says *ibay lei l'iyunei u-meizal*—the pedestrian should have been watching—the Ramban reads this not as a claim that the pedestrian's inattentiveness constitutes negligence sufficient for liability. Rather, it is a claim that if people normally do watch where they are going, then the vessel-placer was not negligent in leaving the vessel there—a prudent person in that environment could have expected that it would be seen and avoided. Under those conditions, the *nizkak*'s placement is not negligent, and so the contributory negligence defense evaporates, and the *mazik*'s absolute liability under *adam muad l'olam* is restored.

The Ramban's framework thus preserves *adam muad l'olam* in its full force and relocates the entire analysis of negligence to the *nizkak*'s conduct. The question "should one watch where one is going?" is not about the *mazik*'s blameworthiness but about whether the *nizkak* assumed a foreseeable risk by placing the vessel in circumstances where damage was likely. This distinction—between negligence of the *mazik* and negligence of the *nizkak*—is the Ramban's central contribution to the analysis of this *sugya*.

## Vigilante Justice

### Well Partnership

The *Gemara*'s final passage for the day introduces a new theme that will be developed further. Rav Chisda wrote to Rav Nachman with the following scenario: two partners share a well, alternating days for drawing water. On his partner's day, one of them came and began drawing from the well. The other person, whose day it was, told him to stop. The partner ignored him. The aggrieved partner then took the handle of a hoe and struck the interloper. Rav Chisda wanted to know the appropriate fine for the assault.

The backdrop involves the standard *boshet* fines: using one's leg to push someone carries a payment of three *zuz*, using a kick carries five, and striking someone with a saddle-like implement carries thirteen. Rav Chisda wished to know what the payment would be for striking with the handle or blade of a hoe.

### Irreversible Loss

Rav Nachman's response is memorable. He wrote back: *Chisda, Chisda—knasa ka-magbit be-Vavel?* Are you collecting *knas* payments in Bavel? *Boshet* partakes of the character of a *knas*, and collecting such fines requires *semicha* that traces back to *Moshe Rabbeinu*, a form of ordination that no longer exists in Babylonia. The question of the fine is therefore moot.

But Rav Nachman presses further: *eima li gufa d'uvda heichi hava*—tell me precisely what happened. Once he understood the facts, Rav Nachman declared: let the man who was struck be hit a hundred times with the same hoe handle, and the one who struck him owes nothing. *Afilu gavna d'hai*—even given the manner in which he struck. The person who was drawing from the well out of turn was acting improperly, and the partner who struck him had a right to do so.

### Bmkom Pseida

The underlying principle, applicable even according to the opinion that generally prohibits self-help, is: *bi-mkom pseida avid inish dina le-nafshei*—in a case of irreversible loss, a person is permitted to take the law into their own hands.

The reasoning is that normally one must seek redress through the courts. But the possibility of later reimbursement presupposes that the damage is reversible—that money paid in court will actually make one whole. When the loss is irreversible, that presupposition fails. Rashi points out the kinds of irreversible losses at stake: plants that need water today may die; a well may run dry by the time a lawsuit concludes. If the loss that will result from delay cannot be remedied by any subsequent court award, then waiting for the court to act is not a viable option. In that case, immediate self-help—*avid inish dina le-nafshei*—is permitted.

This ruling is brought here because it provides the jurisprudential foundation for the earlier discussion: when a person intentionally breaks barrels filling the public thoroughfare to clear a path, is that a form of illegitimate self-help? The principle of *bi-mkom pseida* will govern that question, and its contours—what constitutes irreversible loss, when self-help is warranted—will be the subject of the next discussion.
