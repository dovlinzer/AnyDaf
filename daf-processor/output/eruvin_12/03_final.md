# Eruvin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzurat HaPetach Debate

### Tzurat HaPetach Proof

Before turning to today's primary material, a correction to yesterday's presentation is in order. In discussing whether the side-posts of a *tzurat hapetach* must physically reach the crossbeam, the *Gemara* had adduced a proof from the case of an arch. The proof offered yesterday was attributed to Rebbi Meir's distinctive principle of *gud asik* — imagining the curved portion carved straight — which is in fact a weak basis for a general rule, since that reasoning may be unique to his position. The stronger and more accurate proof is that the *Gemara* establishes that *everyone* agrees: if an arch rises to a height of ten *tefachim* and spans four *amot* in width, the fact that it curves above the posts is legally irrelevant. The top of the arch counts, because the actual physical top of the structure is what matters, even if the upright posts terminate lower down. This is the genuine principle, and its significance extends well beyond the technical dispute about arches: it is one of the foundations permitting the construction of contemporary *eruvin* using a *tzurat hapetach*, which is valid even when most of the fourth side is open and even when the opening exceeds ten *amot* in width — the ruling we follow, in contradistinction to the position of the Rambam.

### Beit Shammai vs Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶכְשֵׁר מָבוֹי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לְחָיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a basic dispute with regard to the method of **rendering an alleyway fit** for one to carry within it on Shabbat. **Beit Shammai say:** Both **a side post and a** cross **beam** are required. **Beit Hillel say: Either a side post or a** cross **beam. Rabbi Eliezer says:** Two **side posts** are required, one on each side of the alleyway.

With that correction in place, the *Gemara* now resumes the discussion of the *Mishnah*'s debate regarding what is required to render a *mavoi* — a dead-end alleyway — permissible for carrying on *Shabbat*. Beit Shammai holds that one requires both a *lechi* (a side-post) and a *kora* (a crossbeam). Beit Hillel holds that either a *lechi* or a *kora* alone suffices. The *Gemara* had been probing whether this dispute reflects a disagreement about function: does the requirement serve as an actual *mechitza* (wall), or merely as a *heker* (a recognizable marker that reminds one of the boundary)?

### Wall vs Heker Theme

These two questions — function and biblical status — constitute the two central themes that will recur throughout this *daf*. The first question is whether the *lechi* or *kora* functions as a genuine wall or merely as a symbolic marker. The second question concerns the relationship between biblical and rabbinic law: does a standard three-walled dead-end *mavoi* already constitute a biblical *reshut hayachid*, with the *lechi* or *kora* required only rabbinically to permit carrying? The straightforward reading is indeed that three walls suffice biblically, and that whatever is required for the fourth side is a rabbinic overlay enabling use of the space. These questions will be examined in depth as the *Gemara* works through multiple opinions.

## Rebbi Eliezer Position

### Two Lechayim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לְחָיַיִן. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְחָיַיִן וְקוֹרָה קָאָמַר, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְחָיַיִן בְּלֹא קוֹרָה קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Two **side posts** are required. A **dilemma was raised** before the Sages: Did **Rabbi Eliezer** intend to **say** that two **side posts and a** cross **beam** are required, adding a stringency to Beit Shammai's opinion, that in addition to the cross beam not one, but two side posts are required? **Or perhaps he** intended to **say** that two **side posts without a** cross **beam** are required.

The *Gemara* now picks up from three lines before the end of 11b. Rebbi Eliezer is quoted: *Rebbi Eliezer omer lechayayim* — Rebbi Eliezer says one requires two *lechayim*. This is a position distinct from all others discussed thus far: not Beit Shammai's requirement of both a *lechi* and a *kora*, and not Beit Hillel's requirement of either one. Rebbi Eliezer says two *lechayim*.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: when Rebbi Eliezer says two *lechayim*, does he mean two *lechayim* together with a *kora* — which would effectively constitute a *tzurat hapetach* and would clearly suggest that he is trying to approximate a genuine wall — or does he mean a novel position of two *lechayim* without any *kora* at all?

### Braita of Uvlin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁהָלַךְ אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן פְּרִידָא תַּלְמִידוֹ לְאוֹבָלִין וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב בְּמָבוֹי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא לֶחִי אֶחָד. אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, עֲשֵׂה לֶחִי אַחֵר. אָמַר לוֹ: וְכִי לְסוֹתְמוֹ אֲנִי צָרִיךְ? אָמַר לוֹ יִסָּתֵם, וּמָה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from that which was related in the *Tosefta*. There was **an incident involving Rabbi Eliezer, who went to Rabbi Yosei ben Perida, his disciple, at** the town of **Ovelin, and found him dwelling in an alleyway that had only one side post. He said to him: My son, set up another side post.** Rabbi Yosei **said to him: Am I required to close it up?** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Let it be closed up; what does it matter?**

To resolve this question, the *Gemara* cites a *braita* relating a story about Rebbi Eliezer himself. He once visited Rebbi Yosi ben Perida, his student, in a place called Uvlin. He found his student residing in a *mavoi* that had only one *lechi*, contrary to Rebbi Eliezer's own ruling that two are required. Rebbi Eliezer told him: *beni*, my son, add another *lechi* to make it permissible. The student pushed back: *vechi lesotmo ani tzarich* — do I need to seal it off entirely? Is requiring two *lechayim* not tantamount to demanding that I wall off the entire opening? Rebbi Eliezer replied: *s'tom uvcha* — yes, seal it off; that is what is required.

Now the *braita* continues, and at its end Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is quoted: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not debate the case of a *mavoi* that is less than four *amot* wide — there, everyone agrees no requirement at all applies. Their debate was limited to widths between four and ten *amot*, where Beit Shammai requires a *lechi* and a *kora* together, and Beit Hillel requires either a *lechi* or a *kora*. This position differs from the *Mishnah*, which states that for a *mavoi* less than four *amot*, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that either a *lechi* or a *kora* suffices — not that nothing is needed at all.

### Lechayim Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ כְּלוּם, עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ, עַל רָחָב מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְעַד עֶשֶׂר. שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in that same *Tosefta*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about an alleyway** whose width **is less than four cubits,** as they both agree **that** this alleyway **does not require anything** to render it permitted for one to carry within it. **About what did they disagree? About** an alleyway that is **wider than four cubits, and up to ten** cubits; **as Beit Shammai say:** It is permitted to carry within it only if there is both **a side post and a** cross **beam, and Beit Hillel say:** It requires **either a side post or a** cross **beam.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת ״וְכִי לְסוֹתְמוֹ אֲנִי צָרִיךְ״, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְחָיַיִן וְקוֹרָה — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אָמַר ״וְכִי לְסוֹתְמוֹ אֲנִי צָרִיךְ״. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְחָיַיִן בְּלֹא קוֹרָה — מַאי לְסוֹתְמוֹ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכִי לְסוֹתְמוֹ בִּלְחָיַיִן אֲנִי צָרִיךְ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof from this *Tosefta*. **In any case, it teaches:** Rabbi Yosei ben Perida said to Rabbi Eliezer: **Am I required to seal it? Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Eliezer requires two **side posts and a** cross **beam, for that** reason the disciple **said: Am I required to seal it? However, if you say** that he requires **side posts without a** cross **beam, what** is the meaning of **to seal it?** The entrance to the alleyway remains open from above. The Gemara rejects this argument: No absolute proof can be cited from here, as perhaps **this is what he is saying: Am I required to seal it with side posts?**

The *Gemara* returns to the question of Rebbi Eliezer. *Tani me'hat* — the *braita* does teach in any case that his student said *vechi lesotmo ani tzarich*, "must I seal it off entirely?" The *Gemara* reasons: if Rebbi Eliezer required two *lechayim* and a *kora*, that would make sense of the student's complaint — because two *lechayim* with a crossbeam spanning between them is a *tzurat hapetach*, and the student would understandably feel that is like constructing a complete wall. But if all Rebbi Eliezer required was two *lechayim* without any *kora*, why would that be called "sealing it off"? It is not such a dramatic requirement.

The *Gemara* answers: *hachi ka'amar* — the student's complaint should be read as: "must I seal it off with *lechayim*?" That is, the student considered the requirement of two *lechayim* an excessive demand, even without a *kora*, and his complaint was rhetorical rather than technical. The upshot is that this exchange does not definitively prove whether Rebbi Eliezer requires two *lechayim* with or without a *kora*. The simpler reading, given that no *kora* is explicitly mentioned in his ruling, is that Rebbi Eliezer means precisely two *lechayim* alone.

## Shimon ben Gamliel

### Less Than Four Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל מָבוֹי שֶׁפָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ כְּלוּם. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁהוּא נִיתָּר אוֹ בְּלֶחִי אוֹ בְּקוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *Tosefta*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** that **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel do not disagree about an alleyway** whose width **is less than four cubits,** as they both agree **that it does not require anything** to render it permitted to carry within it. **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **A certain disciple said before Rabbi Akiva in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about an alleyway** whose width **is less than four cubits,** as they both agree **that** carrying in an alleyway of that sort **is permitted by either a side post or a** cross **beam.** How could Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel have said that according to Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel even that minimal action is unnecessary?

### Rav Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְלֹא לְחָיַיִן כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֶלָּא: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה, כְּבֵית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: This is** what Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **is saying. It neither requires** both **a side post and a** cross **beam, in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, nor** does it require two **side posts, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer; rather,** it requires **either a side post or a** cross **beam, in accordance with** the statement of **Beit Hillel** with regard to a large alleyway. When it said that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel does not require anything, it meant anything more than that required by Beit Hillel.

Rav Ashi resolves this apparent conflict. When Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that less than four *amot* requires *kelum* — nothing — he does not mean literally nothing. He means *eino tzarich lechi vekora keBeit Shammai velo lechayim keRebbi Eliezer* — one does not need the more demanding requirements: not both a *lechi* and a *kora* as Beit Shammai rules, and not two *lechayim* as Rebbi Eliezer rules. All opinions agree that for a *mavoi* under four *amot*, one does not need anything excessive. What *is* needed — *ela o lechi o kora keBeit Hillel* — is still either a *lechi* or a *kora*, in accordance with Beit Hillel. This resolution is admittedly strained, since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel plainly seems to introduce a third position. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* endeavors to harmonize the sources and treats "nothing" as meaning "nothing beyond what Beit Hillel requires."

### Narrow Mavoi Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב אַחְלַי: וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יְחִיאֵל: עַד אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how** narrow must an alleyway be so that it would not require even a side post, according to all opinions? **Rav Aḥlei said, and some say** it was **Rav Yeḥiel** who said: **Up to** a width of **four** handbreadths, the alleyway requires nothing in order to render it permitted for one to carry within it.

Having established the rule for a *mavoi* under four *amot*, the *Gemara* now asks: how narrow can the *mavoi* become before no requirement whatsoever applies — before even a *lechi* or *kora* is unnecessary? Rav Achli, and some say Rav Yechiel, rules: *ad arba'ah tefachim* — until four *tefachim*. Once the opening of the *mavoi* is four *tefachim* or less, everyone agrees that nothing is required at all.

This is not a principle of *lavud* — the rule that a gap of less than three *tefachim* is legally treated as closed. Rather, the reasoning is that an opening of four *tefachim* or less is so narrow that it practically defines itself as a small doorway, and the *mavoi* is considered effectively closed even without any added structure. It is a striking ruling: even though this end of the alleyway is entirely open in a physical sense, if it is narrow enough everyone would agree that no fourth-wall requirement applies whatsoever. One might suggest that since the underlying purpose of the *lechi* or *kora* is to function as a *heker* — a reminder — when the opening is sufficiently narrow, that function is naturally fulfilled by the narrowness itself, and therefore no added marker is needed.

### Psei Chatzer Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּפַסֵּי חָצֵר. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּפַסֵּי חָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** that **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** that **Rav said: The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer with regard to the** upright **boards of a courtyard.** That is to say, the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer only about the number of side posts needed to permit carrying within an alleyway. However, they agree that if a courtyard was breached into the public domain, it can be considered closed only if upright boards of wall, similar to side posts, remain on both sides of the breach. **But Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer with regard to the** upright **boards of** wall that are required in **a courtyard.**

Rav Sheshet, quoting Rav Yirmeyah bar Abba quoting Rav, now notes that the Sages agree with Rebbi Eliezer's more demanding position in one specific context: *bepsei chatzer* — by the planks used to enclose a *chatzer* (courtyard), rather than a *mavoi*. The role of the *psei chatzer* has already been introduced: unlike a *mavoi*, a *chatzer* requires something more substantial than a simple *lechi*, and this more demanding requirement is called a *pas* (plank). On this point, Rav Nachman adds: *halacha keRebbi Eliezer bepasei chatzer* — the practical ruling follows Rebbi Eliezer with respect to the *psei chatzer*, meaning two planks are required. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then probes who exactly "agrees" to Rebbi Eliezer here: since the *Gemara* previously recorded the debate between Rebbi and the Sages over whether a *chatzer* requires a plank on both sides or only one, when we say that "everyone agrees" to Rebbi Eliezer's two-plank requirement by a *chatzer*, it is specifically Rebbi's position that aligns with Rebbi Eliezer. The alternative formulation — that we "rule like Rebbi Eliezer" by a *chatzer* — implies that even here there is a debate, just that the *halacha* follows the more demanding position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאן מוֹדִים? — רַבִּי. הֲלָכָה מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי, מַאן פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ — רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: חָצֵר נִיתֶּרֶת בְּפַס אֶחָד. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁנֵי פַסִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Who** are the Rabbis to whom Rav referred when he stated that they **concede** to Rabbi Eliezer? He was referring to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. Furthermore, as Rav Naḥman said that the ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, one can learn **by inference that** the Sages **dispute** this issue as well. **Who** are the ones who **disagree with** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? It is **the Rabbis, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: In **a courtyard** that was breached into the public domain, with the width of the breach not exceeding ten cubits, it **is permitted** to carry if **one** upright **board** remains on one side of the breach. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is permitted only if there remain **two** upright **boards,** one on each side of the breach.

## Mavoi vs Chatzer

### Different Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חָצֵר צְרִיכָה שְׁנֵי פַסִּין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פַּסֵּי חָצֵר צְרִיכִין שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A courtyard** that was breached **requires two** upright **boards** of wall on either side of the breach, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Asi: Did Rabbi Yoḥanan** really **say that? But weren't you the one who said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: The** upright **boards in a courtyard must be four** handbreadths wide? This indicates that only one board is necessary.

The *Gemara* now transitions to a fuller discussion of why a *chatzer* requires more than a *mavoi*. A *braita* establishes that a *chatzer* permits carrying with a *pas* (plank), while Rebbi maintains two *psim* are necessary. This invites a deeper examination of the structural distinction between the two domains.

Why should a *chatzer* demand more? Several possibilities suggest themselves. One is that a *chatzer* is used by all the residents of the houses that open into it, and therefore carries a much higher volume of foot traffic, requiring a more prominent delineation. Another, cited by *Tosafot*, is something of a counterintuitive logic: a *mavoi* opens directly onto a *reshut harabim*, which might seem to call for a stricter requirement for the *mavoi*. But in fact the stark contrast between the *mavoi* and the *reshut harabim* may actually reduce the need for a formal marker there, since the transition is already obvious.

What becomes clear — and this will be reinforced toward the end of the *daf* — is that the distinction between *chatzer* and *mavoi* is not primarily functional but structural. The *halacha* defines these domains not by how they are used or who owns them, but by their physical configuration: a space that is longer than it is wide is treated as a *mavoi* (an alleyway), while a space that is square or wider than it is long is treated as a *chatzer* (a courtyard). The more demanding requirement of the *chatzer* follows directly from this structural characterization.

It is also worth noting at this point the terminological distinction: what is called a *lechi* in the context of a *mavoi* is called a *pas* in the context of a *chatzer*. The difference in name may reflect a difference in required dimensions. As will emerge, a *lechi* may be a mere *mashehu* — any width at all — whereas a *pas* must meet a minimum width threshold. Some positions require the *pas* to be at least three *tefachim* wide; others require at least four.

### Chatzer Pas Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַב אַדָּא בַּר אֲבִימִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פַּפִּי: קְטַנָּה בְּעֶשֶׂר, וּגְדוֹלָה בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה!
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rav Adda bar Avimi teach** the following *baraita* **before Rabbi Ḥanina, and some say** it was **before Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi,** with regard to the *halakha* governing a small courtyard that was breached along its entire length into a large courtyard. The *baraita* teaches that the residents of the large courtyard may use their courtyard even if the **small** courtyard has a width of **ten** cubits, **and the large** one has a width of **eleven** cubits. In this case, the difference between the length of the smaller courtyard and that of the larger courtyard is only one cubit, i.e., six handbreadths. Therefore, there cannot be upright boards of four handbreadths on each side, as together they would amount to more than a cubit.

A *braita* spells out the dispute explicitly: *chatzer niteret bepas echad* — a *chatzer* is rendered permissible with one *pas*, while Rebbi says it requires two *psim*. This corresponds to the disagreement we have seen between Rebbi and the Sages about whether the *chatzer* must be enclosed on both sides or only one. The *Gemara* frames this debate in the context of the ruling recorded yesterday — that two *psim* flanking a smaller *chatzer* opening into a larger one explains why the larger one must be an *amah* wider (i.e., three *tefachim* on each side). This makes it clear that certain authorities require both a minimum width for each *pas* and coverage on both sides.

### Pas Dimensions Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סָלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא מִיַּמֵּי פָּרְשַׁהּ: בָּרוּחַ אַחַת בְּאַרְבָּעָה, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת — מַשֶּׁהוּ לְכָאן וּמַשֶּׁהוּ לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this difficulty: **When Rabbi Zeira ascended from** his **sea** travels, **he explained** the contradiction between the statements of Rav Yoḥanan in the following manner: If there is a upright board **in** only **one direction,** it must be **four** handbreadths, however, if there are upright boards **from two directions,** it suffices if there is **any amount here,** on one side, **and any amount there,** on the other side.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָדְתָנֵי אַדָּא בַּר אֲבִימִי — רַבִּי הִיא, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And that which Adda bar Avimi taught** with regard to the difference in size between the two courtyards is not universally accepted, as according to Rabbi Zeira it is sufficient if one courtyard is four handbreadths larger than the other. Rather, **it is** in accordance with the view of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who requires two upright boards of wall in a breached courtyard. And furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who says that a side post must be at least three handbreadths wide. Consequently, the two upright boards together must be at least six handbreadths, which is why the minimum difference between the smaller and the larger courtyards is a cubit.

Rav Asi quotes Rav Yochanan as ruling that a *chatzer* requires two *psim*. Rav Zeira challenges him: did Rav Yochanan not himself teach that the *psim* must each be four *tefachim* wide? If so, how can he require two of them — that would be a very substantial total enclosure, and certainly difficult to reconcile with the *braita* about a *chatzer* of ten and eleven *amot*. Moreover, one cannot say both — four *tefachim* each and two of them — since the arithmetic would not work with the case of a ten- and eleven-*amah* *chatzer*, where each *pas* is only three *tefachim*.

When Rav Zeira later resolved the matter, he explained: *meruach achat bearba'ah, mishtei ruchot mashehu mikan umashehu mikan* — if there is a *pas* on only one side, it must be four *tefachim* wide; if there are *psim* on both sides, even a *mashehu* — a minimal amount — on each side is sufficient. This elegantly reconciles Rav Yochanan's two statements. However, it does not explain the case of the ten- and eleven-*amah chatzerot*, where the *pas* is three *tefachim* on each side. The *Gemara* resolves that as well: that *braita* reflects the opinion of Rebbi, who requires two *psim*, and furthermore holds like Rebbi Yosi, who requires three *tefachim* on each side.

### Ten Eleven Amot

The *braita* taught by Rebbi Ada bar Avimi, before Rebbi Chanina — or some say before Rebbi Chanina bar Pappa — states: the smaller of two connected *chatzerot* is ten *amot* wide, the larger eleven *amot* wide. The reason for this one-*amah* differential is precisely the *psim* on each side: if the larger *chatzer* is one *amah* wider than the smaller, and that *amah* is divided as three *tefachim* on each side, then the opening between them is contained by a *pas* of three *tefachim* on each side. This is consistent with requiring both two *psim* and a minimum width of three *tefachim* each — the position of Rebbi following Rebbi Yosi. It is incompatible with simultaneously requiring four *tefachim* per *pas* and two *psim*, since three *tefachim* twice does not equal four *tefachim*.

### Two Yochanan Statements

To summarize the synthesis: Rav Yochanan's two statements are not contradictory. His ruling that four *tefachim* are required refers to the case of a single *pas* on one side only. His rulings that are compatible with *mashehu* on each side refer to the case where two *psim* are present. The *braita* of Rebbi Ada bar Avimi with its three-*tefach* *psim* reflects a third view: the combined opinion of Rebbi (who requires two *psim*) and Rebbi Yosi (who specifies three *tefachim* as the requisite width).

What emerges clearly from all of this is that by a *chatzer*, the requirement is substantially more demanding than by a *mavoi*. A *mavoi* requires only a *lechi* of any minimal width — a *mashehu*. A *chatzer* requires either one *pas* of four *tefachim*, or two *psim* of any minimal width, or according to Rebbi and Rebbi Yosi, two *psim* of three *tefachim* each.

## Shmuel Chatzer Ruling

### One Pas Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָצֵר נִיתֶּרֶת בְּפַס אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said that Rav Yehuda said that Shmuel said:** A breached **courtyard is permitted** if **one** upright **board** of wall remains on one side of the breach.

Rav Yosef cites Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *chatzer niteret bepas echad* — a *chatzer* is rendered permissible with one *pas*. No specification of four *tefachim* or two *psim* is given; the statement is presented simply.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חֲנַנְיָה בַּר שֵׁילָא: אַתְּ לָא תַּעֲבֵיד עוֹבָדָא אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּרוֹב דּוֹפֶן אוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי פַסִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Did Shmuel** really **say this? But didn't Shmuel say to Rav Ḥananya bar Sheila: You must not perform an action,** i.e., issue a ruling to permit carrying in a breached courtyard, **unless** there remains standing **either the majority of the wall or two** upright **boards** on either side of the breach.

Abaye immediately challenges Rav Yosef: did Shmuel really say one *pas* suffices? Elsewhere, Shmuel told Rav Chananya bar Shila: *lo ta'avid uvda ella o berov dofen o bishnei pasin* — do not issue a practical ruling unless the *chatzer* has either a majority of its fourth wall closed, or two *psim*. This is a considerably stricter requirement. How can Shmuel simultaneously say one *pas* is enough?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וַאֲנָא לָא יָדַעְנָא, דְּעוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בְּדוּרָה דִרְעוּתָא, לְשׁוֹן יָם הַנִּכְנָס לְחָצֵר הֲוָה. וַאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְלָא אַצְרְכֵיהּ אֶלָּא פַּס אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **I do not know** how to resolve this contradiction. All I know is that **there was an incident in a shepherds' village** with regard to **a narrow inlet of the sea that penetrated a courtyard,** breaching one of its walls in its entirety, **and** the matter **came before Rav Yehuda, and he required only one** upright **board** of wall to remain in order to permit it.

Rav Yosef responds: do I not know about the case in Dura de-Ra'usa, the village of the shepherds? There, a *leshon yam* — a tongue of water from a lake — entered a *chatzer*, and the case came before Rav Yehuda (Shmuel's student), and he ruled one *pas* sufficient. So Shmuel's position of one *pas* is well attested.

### Water Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְשׁוֹן יָם קָאָמְרַתְּ, קַל הוּא שֶׁהֵקֵלּוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמַיִם. כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי טַבְלָא מֵרַב: מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּתִּיר בְּחוּרְבָּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה מַתֶּרֶת אֶלָּא בְּמַיִם, קַל הוּא שֶׁהֵקֵלּוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Yosef: **You speak of a narrow inlet of the sea,** but an inlet is different and nothing can be derived from that case, for we know that **this is a leniency in which the Sages lessened** the requirements **in** cases involving **water.** In these cases, the Sages did not require properly constructed partitions, but were satisfied with inferior ones. The Gemara supports the assertion that the Sages were more lax with regard to water from the following **dilemma** that **Rabbi Tavla raised before Rav: Does a suspended partition permit** carrying **in a ruin?** Do we say that the remnants of the walls that are suspended in the air are considered as if they descend to the ground, closing off the area so that it is regarded as a private domain? Rav **said to him: A suspended partition** of this kind **permits** carrying **only** in the case of **water; this is a leniency in which the Sages lessened** the requirements **in** cases involving **water.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **In any case, it is difficult.** The contradiction between the conflicting statements of Shmuel remains unresolved.

Abaye, however, is unimpressed by this response: *leshon yam ka'amart*? You are proving from a case involving water? *Kalu she-heikilu chachamim bemayim* — the Sages applied special leniencies in cases involving water. One may not draw general *halachic* conclusions from rulings that pertain to the permission to draw water, since the rabbis deliberately loosened their standards in those cases to enable people to draw water on *Shabbat* — a critical practical need when there was no other water supply.

The principle is illustrated by the case of a *mechitza teluyah* — a suspended partition. Rav Tavla asked Rav: does a suspended partition (one that hangs above the ground without reaching it) permit carrying in a dry, ruined space below? Rav replied: *ein mechitzah teluyah materet ella bemayim* — a suspended partition is only effective for water. The partition is imagined as extending downward into the water, creating a *reshut hayachid* within the water below, enabling one to draw water from that space. This would not work to define a dry space. And the reason is: *kalu she-heikilu chachamim bemayim* — it is a special leniency for water. Therefore, one cannot derive general rules about *chatzer* enclosures from cases involving *leshon yam*.

So the contradiction stands: Shmuel says one *pas* in one place, and two *psim* or the majority of the wall in another. The *Gemara* acknowledges: *mikal makom kashya* — in any case the difficulty remains.

### Pappa Huna Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתוֹ רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִבֵּי רַב פֵּירְשׁוּהָ: מֵרוּחַ אַחַת — בְּאַרְבָּעָה, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת — מַשֶּׁהוּ לְכָאן וּמַשֶּׁהוּ לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the difficulty: **When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, came from the house of their teacher, they explained** the contradiction in the following manner: If there is an upright board **from** only **one direction,** it must be **of four** handbreadths; but if there are upright boards **from two directions,** i.e., both sides of the breach, it suffices if there is **a bit here,** on one side, **and bit here,** on the other side.

When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua came from the study hall, they explained: *meruach achat bearba'ah, mishtei ruchot mashehu lekan umashehu lekan* — when Shmuel says one *pas*, he means a *pas* of four *tefachim* on one side; when he says two *psim*, he means a *mashehu* on each side. The two statements refer to two different configurations, both of which Shmuel accepts as valid. This is the same resolution Rav Zeira had offered for Rav Yochanan's statements, and it now applies equally to Shmuel's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי — הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חֲנַנְיָה בַּר שֵׁילָא: אַתְּ לָא תַּעֲבֵיד עוֹבָדָא אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּרוֹב דּוֹפֶן אוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי פַסִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: If** this issue **is difficult for me** to understand, **this is my difficulty: For Shmuel said to Rav Ḥananya bar Sheila: You must not perform an action,** i.e., issue a ruling to permit carrying in a breached courtyard, **unless** there remains standing **either most of the wall or two** upright **boards** on either side of the breach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי רוֹב דּוֹפֶן? בְּפַס אַרְבָּעָה סַגִּי! וְכִי תֵּימָא מַאי בְּרוֹב דּוֹפֶן בְּדוֹפֶן שִׁבְעָה, דִּבְאַרְבְּעָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ רוֹב דּוֹפֶן, לְמָה לִי אַרְבָּעָה? בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ סַגִּי! דְּהָא אָמַר רַב אַחְלַי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יְחִיאֵל: עַד אַרְבָּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need most of the wall?** An upright **board of four** handbreadths **should suffice.** The Gemara further explains the difficulty: **And if you say, what is** the meaning of **most of the wall** mentioned here? It is referring to the special case where the **wall** is **seven** handbreadths wide, so that **four** handbreadths **constitutes most of the wall,** this too is difficult. Even if the wall is seven handbreadths wide, **why do I** require an upright board of **four** handbreadths to seal? **Three** handbreadths **and any amount should suffice, as Rav Aḥlei, and some say** it was **Rav Yeḥiel who said:** A narrow alleyway **up to four** handbreadths wide requires nothing at all. Here too, after sealing up slightly more than three handbreadths, the remaining gap that remains is less than four handbreadths, so nothing further should be required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כָּאן — בְּחָצֵר, כָּאן — בְּמָבוֹי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּרַב אַחְלַי גּוּפֵיהּ תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that **here,** the statement of Shmuel is referring **to a courtyard,** where even a breach of less than four handbreadths requires action. **There,** the statement of Rav Aḥlei, is referring **to an alleyway. And if you wish, say** that the statement of **Rav Aḥlei is itself** subject to a dispute between the ***tanna'im.**

The *Gemara* then turns to Shmuel's third formulation — *rov dofen*, the majority of the wall. Why would Shmuel mention the majority of the wall as a distinct category? Rav Pappa probes: if a *chatzer* has a wall of seven *tefachim*, and one closes off four *tefachim* of it, that four-*tefach* *pas* would constitute the majority of the wall. But then why speak in terms of *rov dofen* at all — why not simply say four *tefachim*? And why should four *tefachim* be the threshold — once you close off more than half of a seven-*tefach* wall, the remaining gap is less than four *tefachim*, and we have established that an opening less than four *tefachim* requires nothing at all. So the entire *rov dofen* language could be shorthand for "enough to reduce the gap to under four *tefachim*."

Two resolutions are offered. First: *kan bachatzer kan bamavoi* — the rule that an opening under four *tefachim* requires nothing may apply to a *mavoi* but not to a *chatzer*, where the higher standards mean that reducing the gap to under four *tefachim* is not on its own sufficient. Second: the rule itself that under four *tefachim* suffices may be disputed — *deRav Achlei gufa tannai hi* — and not universally accepted. Either way, the *Gemara* has shown that by a *chatzer*, the requirements are more stringent than by a *mavoi* at every level.

## Leshon Yam Scenario

### Water Into Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְשׁוֹן יָם הַנִּכְנָס לְחָצֵר — אֵין מְמַלְּאִין הֵימֶנּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ מְחִיצָה גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁפִּירְצָתוֹ בְּיוֹתֵר מֵעֲשָׂרָה, אֲבָל עֲשָׂרָה — אֵין צָרִיךְ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: With regard to **a narrow inlet of the sea that enters into a courtyard,** partially breaching one of its walls, **one may not fill** water **from it on Shabbat.** The inlet is a *karmelit*, from which it is prohibited to carry into a private domain, e.g. a courtyard. This is the *halakha* **unless there is a partition ten handbreadths high** at one side of the wall's breach, which would incorporate the inlet as part of the courtyard. **In what** case **is this statement said? Where the breach** through which the water enters **is more than ten** cubits wide; **but** if it is only **ten** cubits wide, **nothing is required."**

The *Gemara* now presents a *braita* treating a *leshon yam* — a tongue of water from a lake or sea — that enters a *chatzer*. The *braita* rules: *ein memalin heimena beShabbat* — one may not draw water from it on *Shabbat* — *ela im kein yesh lo mechitza gevohah asarah tefachim* — unless there is a partition of ten *tefachim* height on each side, which would presumably mean the required structure on both sides of the water's entry point, based on the rules we have discussed.

When does this requirement apply? When the opening through which the water enters is more than ten *amot* wide. If the water's opening is less than ten *amot*, no such additional partition is needed to permit drawing water.

The *braita* also appears to assume, incidentally, that carrying within the *chatzer* is never the issue — even when the opening is larger than ten *amot*, one may carry within the *chatzer* without constructing the additional partition. The requirement of the additional wall is tied only to the act of drawing water.

### Carrying vs Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמְלָא הוּא דְּלָא מְמַלְּאִינַן, הָא טַלְטוֹלֵי מְטַלְטְלִינַן. וְהָא נִפְרְצָה חָצֵר בִּמְלוֹאָהּ לְמָקוֹם הָאָסוּר לָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The *baraita* indicates that **one may not fill** water from the inlet because that would involve carrying from a *karmelit* into a private domain, **but** in the courtyard itself **one may** indeed **carry. But isn't the courtyard breached along its entirety,** i.e., more than ten cubits, **into a place that is prohibited to it?** Since it is prohibited to carry to or from the inlet, it should also be prohibited to carry within the courtyard itself.

This asymmetry prompts the *Gemara*'s question: if the *chatzer* has an opening into the lake or sea of more than ten *amot*, the *chatzer* has effectively been breached on one entire side into a space where carrying is forbidden. The rule is that a *chatzer* that is breached along its full width into a forbidden domain is itself forbidden for carrying. So why does the *braita* treat carrying in the *chatzer* as unproblematic and only restrict the drawing of water?

### Giduda Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דְּאִית לֵיהּ גִּידּוּדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here** in this *baraita*? It is a case **where** the wall has not been fully breached, but rather **remnants of the wall** remain on each side (Rabbeinu Ḥananel; Rif).

The *Gemara* answers, near the top of 12b: *hacha bemai askinan de'it leih giduda* — the case here is one where there is a *giduda*, a remnant of a wall. According to Rashi, the scenario is that the *chatzer* had a wall along that side, and the wall partially collapsed or was submerged. The water has now entered the *chatzer* through the breach, but the remnant of the original wall still exists beneath the water's surface — it is simply invisible. Because the wall remains physically present, even if underwater, the *chatzer* retains its enclosed status and carrying within it is permitted.

The permission to carry thus rests on the existence of this submerged wall. However, to draw water from the water itself, the existing structure is not sufficient if the opening exceeds ten *amot*, and an additional visible partition must be constructed.

*Tosafot* offers several explanations for this distinction. One suggestion is that when drawing water one carries it back through the yard toward the house, and at that point, standing near the house, the submerged wall on the far side is not visible, undermining its function as a *heker*. A second and perhaps simpler explanation is that when drawing water — lowering a bucket into the water — there is a realistic concern that the bucket will pass over the submerged wall into the prohibited space beyond; therefore a visible additional partition is required to prevent this. Both explanations preserve the principle that the submerged *giduda* suffices to permit general carrying in the *chatzer* but does not adequately safeguard against the specific risks associated with drawing water when the opening is large.

## Lechi Kora Functions

### Lechi Wall Kora Heker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ, הִכְשִׁירוֹ בְּלֶחִי, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ — חַיָּיב, הִכְשִׁירוֹ בְּקוֹרָה — הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** If several courtyards open onto a common alleyway, the residents of the houses in the courtyards are prohibited to carry in the alleyway, unless the alleyway is rendered fit for one to carry within it by placing a side post or a cross beam at its entrance, and by the inhabitants of each courtyard placing food in a common area for the duration of Shabbat, symbolically converting the entire alleyway into a single household. It is prohibited to carry in **an alleyway** that the residents **did not merge.** Nevertheless, if the alleyway **was rendered fit by** means of **a side post** placed at its entrance, **one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain **is liable;** the side post functions as a partition, and the alleyway is deemed a full-fledged private domain. If, however, the alleyway **was rendered fit by** means of a cross **beam, one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain **is exempt;** the cross beam functions only as a conspicuous marker. It is not considered a partition that renders the alleyway a private domain.

The *Gemara* now directly engages the fundamental question that has been hovering over the entire discussion. Rav Yehuda states: *mavoi shelo nishtatfu bo* — if the residents of a *mavoi* did not join together in the bread (*shituf*), and one erected a *lechi* — *hezorker letocho chayav* — then one who throws an object into it from a *reshut harabim* is liable. This means the *lechi* has created a biblical *reshut hayachid*. But if instead of a *lechi* one erected a *kora* — *hazorek letocho patur* — one is exempt, because the *kora* does not create a biblical *reshut hayachid*.

The implication is: a *lechi* functions as a genuine wall, sufficient to complete a biblical *reshut hayachid*; a *kora* functions only as a *heker* and does not constitute a wall at all.

There is something arresting about this formulation. It implies that the rabbis permit carrying in a *mavoi* that is not a biblical *reshut hayachid* — a *mavoi* with three walls and a *kora* is not a *reshut hayachid* according to this position, yet carrying there is rabbinically permitted. This is a significant departure from the usual assumption that rabbinic permission to carry requires at minimum a space that has already qualified as a *reshut hayachid* on a biblical level.

### Shituf Impact Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: טַעְמָא דְּלֹא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ, הָא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ הִכְשִׁירוֹ בְּקוֹרָה נָמֵי חַיָּיב?! וְכִי כִּכָּר זוֹ עֹשָׂה אוֹתוֹ רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד אוֹ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet strongly objects to this** due to the following: **The reason** that one is exempt in the latter case is due to the fact the residents of the alleyway **did not merge.** By inference, **if they did** in fact **merge, one would be liable even if** the alleyway **was rendered fit by** way of a cross **beam.** This, however, is difficult. One can ask: **Does this loaf,** through which the residents joined together to form a single household, **render** the alleyway **a private domain or a public domain?**

Rav Sheshet challenges this formulation directly: you qualified the ruling with *shelo nishtatfu bo* — that they did not perform a *shituf*. Does that mean that if they *did* perform a *shituf*, then even a *mavoi* with only a *kora* would become a biblical *reshut hayachid*? In other words, does the joint ownership created by the *shituf* — the communal participation in a shared loaf — transform the domain's biblical status? *Vechi kikar zu aseto reshut hayachid vereshut harabim* — does this loaf of bread determine whether a space is a *reshut hayachid* or a *reshut harabim*?

### Shituf Not Defining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: חֲצֵירוֹת שֶׁל רַבִּים וּמְבוֹאוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן מְפוּלָּשִׁין, בֵּין עֵירְבוּ וּבֵין לֹא עֵירְבוּ הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכָן חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Courtyards** shared **by many and alleyways that are not open** on two opposite sides, **whether** the residents **established an *eiruv* or did not establish an *eiruv*, one who throws** an object **into them** from the public domain **is liable.** This seems to contrary to Rav Yehuda's statement.

Intuitively, there is a kernel of logic to this idea. After all, many people assume that *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim* are at least partly defined by private versus public ownership. If so, one might think that the *shituf* — which creates a form of collective private ownership — could contribute to creating a *reshut hayachid*. But the *Gemara* emphatically rejects this. A *braita* is cited: *chatzerot shel rabbim umevoot she-einam mefulashim, bein eirvu uvein lo eirvu, hazorek letocho chayav* — public courtyards and dead-end alleyways, whether an *eruv* was made or not, constitute a *reshut hayachid* and one who throws into them is liable. Biblical domain status is determined entirely by walls, not by ownership or communal arrangements. The *shituf* or *eruv* has no bearing whatsoever on whether a space qualifies as a *reshut hayachid*.

### Mefulash Mavoi Lechi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מָבוֹי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְשִׁיתּוּף, הִכְשִׁירוֹ בְּלֶחִי — הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ חַיָּיב, הִכְשִׁירוֹ בְּקוֹרָה — הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated as follows. Rav Yehuda said:** In the case of **an alleyway that is not fit for merging,** i.e., an alleyway that is open on two opposite sides, if the alleyway **was rendered fit** for one to carry within it **by** means of **a side post, one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain **is liable.** In that case, the side post is considered a third partition, and since the alleyway is closed on three sides it is deemed a private domain. If, however, the alleyway **was rendered fit** for one to carry within in **by** means of a cross **beam, one who throws** an object **into it is exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: לֶחִי מִשּׁוּם מְחִיצָה וְקוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה: לֶחִי מִשּׁוּם מְחִיצָה וְקוֹרָה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה מִשּׁוּם הֶיכֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently,** Rav Yehuda **holds** that **a side post** functions **as a partition, whereas a** cross **beam** functions **as a conspicuous marker** but is not considered a partition. **And, so too, Rabba said: A side post** functions **as a partition, whereas** a cross **beam** functions **as a conspicuous marker. But Rava said: Both this,** the side post, **and that,** the cross beam, function **as a conspicuous marker.**

Since the *shituf* cannot affect biblical status, Rav Yehuda's original formulation must be reread. *Amar Rav Yehuda* — rather than conditioning the ruling on whether a *shituf* was or was not performed, what he actually means is this: *mavoi she-eino raui leshituf* — a *mavoi* for which *shituf* is not even possible. What *mavoi* is impossible to make a *shituf* for? A *mefulash* — one that is open at both ends, passing through from one *reshut harabim* to another. Such a *mavoi* has only two walls (the long sides), not three. Now the ruling makes sense. If one erects a *lechi* — creating a third wall — *hazorek letocho chayav*: biblically, it now has three walls and qualifies as a *reshut hayachid*. If one erects only a *kora*, the third side has no genuine wall; a *kora* is a *heker* only, not a wall. *Hazorek letocho patur* — it remains without three walls and is not a *reshut hayachid*.

*Alma kasavar lechi mishum mechitza, vekora mishum heker* — this establishes clearly that a *lechi* functions as a wall and a *kora* as a *heker*. And this position is shared by Rabbah as well: both agree that *lechi mishum mechitza, kora mishum heker*.

The implications are significant. For a standard *mavoi* with three existing walls, the *lechi* or *kora* for the fourth side is a rabbinic requirement only — the space is already a biblical *reshut hayachid* on the basis of its three walls. But for a *mefulash* *mavoi* that is open at both ends and has only two walls, the *lechi* elevates it to a biblical *reshut hayachid*, while a *kora* does not. The analogy to the laws of *sukkah* is instructive: *shtayim kehilchasam veshlishit afilu tefach* — two complete walls and a third wall of even minimal width constitutes a valid *sukkah*. Analogously, the *lechi* in this context functions as that minimal third wall.

### Rava Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבָא: הַזּוֹרֵק לְמָבוֹי, יֵשׁ לוֹ לֶחִי — חַיָּיב, אֵין לוֹ לֶחִי — פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Abba raised an objection to Rava** from the following *baraita*: **One who throws** an object from the public domain **into an alleyway, if** the alleyway **has a side post, he is liable; if it does not have a side post he is exempt.** This shows that a side post is considered a proper partition.

Rava disagrees. He holds: *echad zeh ve'echad zeh mishum heker* — both the *lechi* and the *kora* function only as a *heker*, as a recognizable marker. On this view, neither structure creates a wall in any meaningful biblical sense. Even a *lechi* does not transform a two-walled *mefulash mavoi* into a biblical *reshut hayachid*.

### Required Walls Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ אֶלָּא לֶחִי, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ — חַיָּיב, לֶחִי וְדָבָר אַחֵר — הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rava replied: **This is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** If the alleyway is closed on one side such that **it requires only a side post** in order to permit carrying within in, **one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain **is liable** because the alleyway already has three partitions and is therefore a proper private domain according to Torah law. However, if the alleyway requires **a side post and something else** in order to permit carrying within it, **one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain **is exempt** because the alleyway has only two partitions and is therefore not considered a private domain.

The *Gemara* responds that Rav Zeira has momentarily forgotten the ruling cited earlier by Rav Ami in the name of Rav Yochanan: *pasei chatzer tzrichin she'yehe bin arba* — the *psim* of a *chatzer* must be at least four *tefachim* wide. This is the essential difference between a *lechi* and a *pas*: a *lechi* may be any width whatsoever, a *mashehu*; a *pas* for a *chatzer* must be at least four *tefachim* wide (if only one *pas* is used). A *lechi*, by definition, does not meet this threshold and therefore cannot function as a *pas* for a *chatzer*.

## Rebbi Yehuda Two Houses

### Two Houses Lechi Kora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ, יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים בִּשְׁנֵי צִידֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, עוֹשֶׂה לֶחִי מִכָּאן וְלֶחִי מִכָּאן, אוֹ קוֹרָה מִכָּאן וְקוֹרָה מִכָּאן, וְנוֹשֵׂא וְנוֹתֵן בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **He raised an** additional **objection** to Rava from the following *baraita*. **Furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda said:** The *halakha* is as follows with regard to **one who has two houses** opposite each other **on two sides of the public domain,** if he chooses, **he may** create a private domain for himself in the area of the public domain. He may **place a** ten-handbreadth high **side post from here,** perpendicular to the public domain. This creates a symbolic wall which, in the *halakhot* of alleyways, has the legal status of a wall. **And,** he may place **an** additional **post from here,** on the other side, and that has the same legal status as if he closed the public domain on all of its sides. **Or,** he can implement a different solution appropriate for alleyways by placing **a beam** extending **from here,** from one end of one house, to the end of the house opposite it. This creates a symbolic partition across the width of the street. **And,** he may place **a beam** extending **from here,** from the other side of the house. According to Rabbi Yehuda, in that way, one is permitted to **carry** objects **and place** them **in** the area **between** the symbolic partitions, as he would in a private domain.

The *Gemara* now revisits the famous ruling of Rebbi Yehuda discussed previously. Rebbi Yehuda says: if there are two houses facing each other on two sides of a *reshut harabim*, one may place a *lechi* on each side, or a *kora* on each side, and carry in the street between them. The *reshut harabim* between the two houses is rendered permissible by these minimal structures on each end.

### Rabbis Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְעָרְבִין רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בְּכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: One may not place an *eiruv* in the public domain in that** way. One who seeks to transform a public domain into a private domain must place actual partitions. Apparently, according to Rabbi Yehuda, the side posts function as partitions in the public domain, creating a private domain between the two houses. It follows from this that a side post is in fact deemed a proper partition, contrary to Rava's statement.

The Sages reject this: *ein me'arvin reshut harabim bekach* — one cannot make an *eruv* for a *reshut harabim* with such minimal structures. Clearly the Sages hold that a *lechi* and a *kora* are insufficient to transform a *reshut harabim* into a space where carrying is permitted.

### Rava Position Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שְׁתֵּי מְחִיצּוֹת דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is not the reason behind Rabbi Yehuda's statement. Rather, **there Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **by Torah law two partitions** suffice to constitute a private domain, and he requires side posts only as a conspicuous marker. Therefore, Rava's position cannot be disproved from this source either.

What is the basis of Rebbi Yehuda's position? The *Gemara* explains: *hatam kasavar Rabbi Yehuda shtei mechitzot d'Oraita* — Rebbi Yehuda holds that biblically, only two walls are required to constitute a *reshut hayachid*. If two walls already create a biblical *reshut hayachid*, then the *lechi* or *kora* on each end serves as a rabbinic *heker* sufficient to permit carrying. But his position tells us nothing about whether a *lechi* or *kora* constitutes a wall for purposes of the third wall, since he does not require three walls to begin with.

This is a striking *Gemara*. Rebbi Yehuda's extraordinary leniency — permitting one to carry in an open street between two buildings merely by placing a post or a beam on each end — is not based on any theory that the *lechi* or *kora* builds a third or fourth wall. It is based entirely on his view that two walls alone already create a *reshut hayachid* on a biblical level. The rabbis who reject his position presumably require three walls for a biblical *reshut hayachid* and do not accept that two buildings facing each other provide even two adequate walls for a *reshut harabim*.

## Mavoi Chatzer Structure

### Square Becomes Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁאׇרְכּוֹ כְּרׇחְבּוֹ אֵינוֹ נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי מַשֶּׁהוּ. אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁאׇרְכּוֹ כְּרׇחְבּוֹ אֵינוֹ נִיתָּר בְּקוֹרָה טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said:** Unlike other alleyways, carrying within **an alleyway whose length is equal to its width is not permitted by** means of **a side post of minimal** width. Like a courtyard, carrying within it is permitted only by means of an upright board four handbreadths wide. **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav:** Carrying within **an alleyway whose length is equal to its width is not permitted by** a cross **beam** with the width **of a handbreadth.**

Rav Yehuda states in the name of Rav: *mavoi she'archo kerachbo* — a *mavoi* whose length equals its width — *eino niter belechi mashehu* — cannot be rendered permissible with a mere *lechi* of minimal width. Once the *mavoi* is as wide as it is long — in other words, square — it takes on the legal status of a *chatzer* and must be treated accordingly, requiring a *pas* of four *tefachim* or two *psim* rather than a simple *lechi*.

### Chiya Confirmation

Rav Chiya bar Ashi, also in the name of Rav, makes the same point from the opposite direction: *mavoi she'archo kerachbo eino niter bekora tefach* — once the length equals the width, even a *kora* of one *tefach* — the standard requirement for a *kora* in a *mavoi* — is no longer sufficient. This confirms from another angle that the transformation from *mavoi* to *chatzer* is triggered by the shape itself, regardless of function.

### Zeira Appreciation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כַּמָּה מְכַוְּונָן שְׁמַעְתָּא דְסָבֵי. כֵּיוָן דְּאׇרְכּוֹ כְּרׇחְבּוֹ — הָוֵה לֵיהּ חָצֵר, וְחָצֵר אֵינָהּ נִיתֶּרֶת בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, אֶלָּא בְּפַס אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: How precise are the traditions of the Elders.** He explains: **Since the length** of the alleyway **is equal to its width, it is** regarded like **a courtyard, and** carrying within **a courtyard is not permitted by** means of **a side post or** a cross **beam, but** only **by** means of **an** upright **board of four** handbreadths.

Rav Zeira expresses appreciation for this teaching: *kama nechuvanin shmaita desavi* — how precisely on target is this ruling of the elders. Once the length equals the width — *hava lei chatzer*, it is now a *chatzer* — and *chatzer eina niteret belechi vekora* — a *chatzer* cannot be rendered permissible with a *lechi* or a *kora*. Instead, *ella bepas arba* — one needs a *pas* of at least four *tefachim* (or two *psim* of minimal width).

This reflects an important conceptual theme: domain status is determined by objective physical structure, not by use or ownership. One might have thought that the difference between a *mavoi* and a *chatzer* is how the space is actually used — a *mavoi* is used as an alleyway, a *chatzer* as a courtyard. But the *halacha* determines identity purely on geometric grounds: if a space is longer than it is wide, it is a *mavoi*; if it is not longer than it is wide, it is a *chatzer*. This parallels the earlier principle that *reshut hayachid* status is determined by walls alone, not by ownership or the performance of an *eruv*.

### Zeira Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי, הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי: לֶיהֱוֵי הַאי לֶחִי כְּפַס מַשֶּׁהוּ, וְנִשְׁתְּרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** Nonetheless, **if** this issue **is difficult for me** to understand, **this is my difficulty: Let this side post be** considered **like** an upright **board of minimal** width **and permit** carrying within the alleyway, just as an upright board permits carrying in a breached courtyard.

Rav Zeira, however, raises a difficulty. Given that a *lechi* does not suffice for a *chatzer* and one needs a *pas* of four *tefachim*, why can a *lechi* not simply count as a *pas mashehu* — a minimal-width *pas*? After all, both a *lechi* and a *pas* are vertical structures placed alongside the opening; why should the mere width of the structure determine whether it satisfies the *chatzer* requirement?

### Nachman Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פַּסֵּי חָצֵר צְרִיכִין שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this is incorrect, as **that which Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said escaped** Rabbi Zeira's **attention: The** upright **boards of a courtyard must be four** handbreadths wide, whereas a side post may be of minimal size.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: נָקְטִינַן, אֵיזֶהוּ מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה — כֹּל שֶׁאׇרְכּוֹ יָתֵר עַל רׇחְבּוֹ וּבָתִּים וַחֲצֵרוֹת פְּתוּחִים לְתוֹכוֹ, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא חָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִיתֶּרֶת בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה אֶלָּא בְּפַס אַרְבָּעָה — כֹּל שֶׁמְּרוּבַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: We have a tradition** that states: **What is the** type of **alleyway** in **which** carrying **is permitted by** means of **a side post or** a cross **beam? Any** alleyway **whose length is greater than its width and has houses and courtyards opening into it. And what is the** type of **courtyard** in **which** carrying **is not permitted by** means of **a side post or** a cross **beam, but by** an upright **board of four** handbreadths? **Any** courtyard **that is square.**

The *Gemara* responds that Rav Zeira has momentarily forgotten the ruling cited earlier by Rav Ami in the name of Rav Yochanan: *pasei chatzer tzrichin she'yehe bin arba* — the *psim* of a *chatzer* must be at least four *tefachim* wide. This is the essential difference between a *lechi* and a *pas*: a *lechi* may be any width whatsoever, a *mashehu*; a *pas* for a *chatzer* must be at least four *tefachim* wide (if only one *pas* is used). A *lechi*, by definition, does not meet this threshold and therefore cannot function as a *pas* for a *chatzer*.

Rav Nachman then states a principle: *naktinan* — we take the following as established. *Eizu mavoi sheniter belechi vekora* — which *mavoi* is rendered permissible with a *lechi* or a *kora*, the less demanding requirement? *Kol she'archo yater al rachbo* — any space whose length exceeds its width. *Uvatim vechatzerot petuchin letokho* — and furthermore, houses open into yards and yards open into it. So to fully qualify as a *mavoi* entitled to the lenient rules, two conditions must be met: it must be longer than it is wide, and it must have the functional structure of houses feeding into yards that feed into the alleyway.

*Ve'eizu hi chatzer she'eina niteret belechi vekora ela bepas arba* — and which *chatzer* requires the more demanding *pas* of four *tefachim* rather than a *lechi* or *kora*? *Kol she-meruba'at* — anything that is square.

### Shape Irrelevant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְרוּבַּעַת אִין, עֲגוּלָּה לָא?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִי אׇרְכָּהּ יָתֵר עַל רׇחְבָּהּ — הָוֵה לֵיהּ מָבוֹי, וּמָבוֹי בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה סַגִּיא, וְאִי לָא — הָוֵה לַהּ חָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: If it is **square,** then **yes,** is it considered a courtyard? If it is **round, no,** is it not considered a courtyard? The Gemara makes a correction: **This is** what **it is saying: If its length is greater than its width, it is** considered **an alleyway, and** for **an alleyway a side post or a** cross **beam suffices; but if** its length is **not** greater than its width, i.e., it is square, **it is** considered **a courtyard.**

The *Gemara* immediately clarifies: does *meruba'at* mean specifically square, to the exclusion of circular or other shapes? *Meruba'at in, agulah lo*? No. *Hachi ka'amar* — this is what the rule means: *ee orchah yater al rachbah* — if its length exceeds its width, it is a *mavoi* and a *lechi* or *kora* suffices; *ve'ee la, havlei chatzer* — if it is not, it is a *chatzer*. Shape is entirely irrelevant. The space could be circular, triangular, trapezoidal, or any other form. The only criterion is whether the dimension aligned with the flow of entry and exit — what we might call the "length" — exceeds the perpendicular dimension. If it does, it is a *mavoi*; if not, it is a *chatzer*.

### Length Requirement Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? סָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל לְמֵימַר: עַד דְּאִיכָּא פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּרׇחְבָּהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב, הָכִי אָמַר חֲבִיבִי: אֲפִילּוּ מַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** by **how much** must its length exceed its width so that it can be considered an alleyway? **Shmuel thought** at first **to say:** It is not considered an alleyway **unless** its length **is double its width,** until **Rav said to him: My uncle** [***ḥavivi***], Rav Ḥiyya, **said this: Even** if its length is greater than its width by only **a minimal amount,** the *halakhot* of an alleyway apply to it.

Finally, the *Gemara* asks: how much longer does a space need to be than it is wide to qualify as a *mavoi*? *Vekamah* — how much? Shmuel initially held: *ad pi shenayim berachba* — it must be twice as long as it is wide. By this standard, a street that is ten *amot* wide would need to be at least twenty *amot* long to qualify as a *mavoi*. This is a demanding threshold. Rav, however, citing Rebbi Chiya, rules: *afilu mashehu* — even the smallest excess suffices. As long as the length exceeds the width by any amount at all, even a *mashehu*, the space is classified as a *mavoi* and may be rendered permissible with a *lechi* or a *kora*. The moment it is square or wider than it is long, it becomes a *chatzer* and the more demanding requirements apply.
