# Kiddushin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gilgul Shvu'ah

### Intro & Sotah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹקְקִים אֶת הַנְּכָסִים וְכוּ׳. אָמַר עוּלָּא: מִנַּיִן לְגִלְגּוּל שְׁבוּעָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָמְרָה הָאִשָּׁה אָמֵן אָמֵן״, וּתְנַן: עַל מָה הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת ״אָמֵן אָמֵן״? ״אָמֵן״ עַל הָאָלָה, ״אָמֵן״ עַל ״הַשְּׁבוּעָה״. ״אָמֵן״ אִם מֵאִישׁ זֶה, ״אָמֵן״ אִם מֵאִישׁ אַחֵר. ״אָמֵן״ שֶׁלֹּא סָטִיתִי אֲרוּסָה וּנְשׂוּאָה וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם וּכְנוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** in a legal dispute involving both land and movable property, if the defendant makes a partial admission of the claim with regard to the movable property, thereby rendering himself obligated to take an oath denying any responsibility for the remaining property, the movable property **binds the property** that serves as a guarantee, i.e., the land, so that he is forced to take an oath concerning the land as well, despite the fact that one is generally not obligated to take an oath for a claim involving land. **Ulla says: From where** is it derived **from the Torah that** one can impose **the extension of an oath,** i.e., if one is required to take an oath for one claim, the other party can obligate him to take an oath with regard to other claims which on their own would not lead to the imposition of an oath? **As it is stated** with regard to a *sota*: **"And the woman shall say: Amen, amen"** (Numbers 5:22), **and we learned** in a mishna (*Sota* 18a): **Concerning what does she say** the double expression of: **Amen, amen?** She says **amen on the curse,** as she accepts the curse upon herself if she is guilty, and **amen on the oath,** as she declares that she is not defiled. She states: **Amen** if I committed adultery **with this man** about whom I was warned, **amen** if I committed adultery **with another man. Amen that I did not stray** when I was **betrothed nor** after I was **married, nor as a widow waiting for my *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage, since a woman at that stage is prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse with any men, **nor** when **married** through levirate marriage to the *yavam*.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה לְהִתָּבַע בְּעֵד אֶחָד – מְגַלְגְּלִין, מָמוֹן, שֶׁנִּיתָּן לְהִתָּבַע בְּעֵד אֶחָד – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְגַלְגְּלִין?
>
> **Translation:** **where** an oath **cannot be imposed by one witness,** as two witnesses must testify that the wife secluded herself with the man concerning whom she was warned in order for her to be obligated to take the oath of a *sota*, and yet **one can extend** her oath, **is it not logical** that with regard to a claim involving **money, where** an oath **can be imposed by** the testimony of **one witness,** that **one can extend** the oath?

### Vadai vs. Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּוַדַּאי, סָפֵק מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the extension of an oath **in** the case of **a definite** claim, i.e., when the plaintiff is certain of his claim. **From where do we** derive that this *halakha* of the extension of an oath applies also to **uncertain** claims, when the plaintiff is not sure the defendant owes him money but merely suspects this to be the case?

### R. Shimon bar Yochai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֶמְרָה שְׁבוּעָה בַּחוּץ, וְנֶאֶמְרָה שְׁבוּעָה בִּפְנִים. מָה שְׁבוּעָה הָאֲמוּרָה בִּפְנִים – עָשָׂה בָּהּ סָפֵק כְּוַדַּאי, אַף שְׁבוּעָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּחוּץ – עָשָׂה בָּהּ סָפֵק כְּוַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: The Torah states an external oath,** i.e., an oath administered outside of the Temple, **and it states an internal oath,** an oath administered inside the Temple courtyard, i.e., the oath of a *sota*. **Just as with regard to an oath stated** in the Torah that is taken **inside** the Temple, the Torah **rendered uncertainty like certainty,** as in the case of a *sota* the husband's claim is based on suspicion and yet he can extend that oath; **so too, with regard to an oath stated** in the Torah that is taken **outside** the Temple, the Torah **rendered uncertainty** to be **like certainty,** i.e., all oaths can be extended to include even uncertain claims.

### Mammon & Suspicion

The *gemara* then affirms that this extension applies fully to monetary suspicions. Once the conditions for *gilgul shvu'ah* are met — a legitimate initial oath in the domain of *mamon* — the sweep of the rolling oath is broad: it can encompass *karka* (categorically excluded from ordinary monetary oaths), and it can encompass *safek* claims, where the claimant's basis is suspicion rather than certainty.

## Limits of Gilgul

### Extent of Gilgul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד הֵיכָן גִּלְגּוּל שְׁבוּעָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הִישָּׁבַע לִי שֶׁאֵין עַבְדִּי אַתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Until where** does the **extension of an oath** reach? It has been established that a plaintiff can attach other claims to the oath that the defendant is required to take, even if they do not relate to the current claim submitted in court. To what extent can the plaintiff impose additional oaths? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The *halakha* is **that** a plaintiff **can** even **say to** a defendant: **Take an oath to me that you are not my** Canaanite **slave.** If the defendant is required to take an oath, e.g., concerning denial of a debt, he can be forced to take an oath about this matter as well.

### Eved Ivri Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא שַׁמּוֹתֵי מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ! דְּתַנְיָא: הַקּוֹרֵא לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״עֶבֶד״ – יְהֵא בְּנִידּוּי. ״מַמְזֵר״ – סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. ״רָשָׁע״ – יוֹרֵד עִמּוֹ לְחַיָּיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But the court **ostracizes one** who says this to another, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who calls another a slave shall be ostracized.** One who calls another **a *mamzer* incurs the** punishment of **forty** lashes. If one calls another **a wicked person** then the insulted person may **harass him** in all aspects of **his life.** In light of this *halakha*, it is clear that the court will not force the accused to respond to this insult by taking an oath.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הִישָּׁבַע לִי שֶׁלֹּא נִמְכַּרְתָּ לִי בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. הַאי טַעַנְתָּא מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא, מָמוֹנָא אִית לֵיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ! רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי גּוּפוֹ קָנוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that the plaintiff can extend an oath by stating: **Take an oath to me that you were not sold to me as a Hebrew slave.** In this case the plaintiff is not questioning the man's lineage, as he is simply claiming that he was sold to him as a slave and must work for him. The Gemara asks: But there is nothing novel about this *halakha*, as this **is a proper claim** that **there is money** owed **to him** by the accused. The sale and service of a Hebrew slave can be assessed in monetary terms, and is analogous to all claims of debt, which can be imposed by extension of an oath. The Gemara answers: **Rava** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rava says: The Hebrew slave himself is acquired** by his master. Consequently, this claim involves not just money but ownership over his person as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ קַרְקַע! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: קַרְקַע הוּא דְּעָבְדִי אִינָשֵׁי דִּמְזַבְּנִי בְּצִינְעָא, אִם אִיתָא דְּזַבֵּין – לֵית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** similar to a claim concerning ownership of **land,** and the mishna already taught that an oath can be extended to include a claim concerning land. The Gemara answers: This ruling is necessary **lest you say: It is land that people are likely to sell privately,** and **if it is so that** the plaintiff had **sold** it to him, the sale would **not** have generated **publicity,** and the public would not know about it. Therefore, the plaintiff's claim that the defendant sold land to him is reasonable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי, אִם אִיתָא דְּזַבֵּין – קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, in **this** case, where the plaintiff claims that he purchased the defendant as a Hebrew slave, **if it is so that he purchased** him as a slave, the sale would have generated **publicity.** Since this supposed sale is not common knowledge, one might have thought that the defendant cannot be forced to take an oath to deny this claim. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that despite the absence of public knowledge, one can extend an oath to this claim as well.

### Tosafot Limits Gilgul

Precisely because the principle of *gilgul shvu'ah* seems so sweeping — *safek*, *karka*, far-fetched claims — Tosafot moves to impose some limit. Without any constraint, *gilgul shvu'ah* could theoretically allow one to compel a person to swear about virtually anything under the sun: "swear that you never stole anything from me in your life," "swear that you never borrowed anything from me," across every conceivable domain, on the basis of pure speculation.

Tosafot, citing *rebbi*, states explicitly: *shelo kol safek megalgelin* — not every speculative claim can be the subject of *gilgul*. If you are owed an oath by someone, you cannot simply roll it onto every suspicion you have ever entertained. Rather, *davka be-safek she-yesh lanu lachush k-tzat* — the claim appended through *gilgul* must have at least some minimal basis for concern. Tosafot offers the example: if we were partners — *shutaf o aris* — and we handled each other's money, then upon dissolution of the partnership there is a reasonable basis to suspect possible misappropriation, and *gilgul shvu'ah* can properly extend to that suspicion.

One might ask how this squares with the *gemara*'s own case of the *eved Ivri*, where the claim seemed quite far-fetched with no particular basis. The tension is real, and Tosafot is consciously pulling back from the most expansive reading of the *gemara*. The *ka mashma lan* of the *sugya* is that we do compel the oath in that case — but Tosafot argues that to take the principle to its fullest logical extreme, permitting any baseless speculation to trigger *gilgul shvu'ah*, would be absurd. There must be at least some minimal grounding.

## Chalifin Overview

### Archaic Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַנַּעֲשֶׂה דָּמִים בְּאַחֵר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה – נִתְחַיֵּיב זֶה בַּחֲלִיפָיו. כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה – נִתְחַיֵּיב זֶה בַּחֲלִיפָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna discusses a transaction involving the barter of two items. With regard to **all** items **used as monetary** value **for another** item, i.e., instead of a buyer paying money to the seller, they exchange items of value with each other, **once one** party in the transaction **acquires** the item he is receiving, **this** party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged for it.** Therefore, if it is destroyed or lost, he incurs the loss. **How so?** If **one exchanges an ox for a cow, or a donkey for an ox, once this** party **acquires** the animal that he is receiving, this party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged for it.**

### Zachah & Nitchayev

The *mishnah* then provides an illustration: *keitzad? hechlif shor be-farah o chamor be-shor* — for example, an ox exchanged for a cow, or a donkey for an ox. *Keivan she-zachah zeh, nitchayev zeh be-chalifav* — once this one takes possession, the other is obligated in its exchange.

There is an interpretive question about this second clause. Tosafot records a variant *girsa* that lacks the second *zeh*, reading simply *keivan she-zachah zeh, nitchayev be-chalifav* — once you have taken possession, you are obligated to give the exchange. On that reading, the clause means straightforwardly: once party A takes the donkey, he is obligated to give the cow.

The received *girsa* — with the second *zeh* — yields a different meaning. The introduction of a second *zeh* suggests we are pointing to the *other* party. The meaning becomes: once Shimon takes the donkey, Reuven — who has not yet retrieved the cow still sitting in Shimon's backyard — is now the one who bears responsibility for any loss that befalls the cow. The cow belongs to Reuven from the moment Shimon effects *kinyan* on his side of the transaction, even if Reuven has not yet come to collect it. *Nitchayev zeh be-chalifav* means not "he must deliver the exchange" but rather "he has become the owner of the exchange object and bears all associated liability for it."

Concretely: Reuven and Shimon agree to exchange a donkey for a cow. Shimon takes Reuven's donkey. The cow remains in Shimon's backyard. Reuven is now liable for any mishap that occurs to the cow — if it injures itself before Reuven comes to collect it, that is Reuven's loss and not Shimon's.

### Barter vs. Sudar

Before turning to the *gemara*'s analysis of the opening clause of the *mishnah*, it is important to appreciate that the topic of *kinyan chalifin* actually encompasses two conceptually distinct phenomena that the *gemara* tends to treat under unified rules, even though they are in fact quite different in character.

The first is pure barter: I give you one object, you give me another, and each party's *kinyan* on the received object is completed through the exchange itself. This is what the end of the *mishnah* — the case of the ox and the donkey — is clearly describing.

The second is *kinyan sudar*, the handkerchief acquisition, which is modeled on the *pasuk* in Rut: *va-shalaf ish na'alo ve-natan le-rei'eihu, ve-zot ha-te'udah be-Yisrael* — and a man drew off his shoe and gave it to his neighbor, and this was the manner of attestation in Israel. In *kinyan sudar*, one party hands a garment or object to the other, and this act of transfer constitutes a formal expression of *gemirat da'at* — complete and binding resolve — that can effect the transfer of entirely separate property, finalize contractual obligations, and so on. The handkerchief is not being exchanged *for* the property; it is rather a symbolic act that signifies commitment.

The difference between these two forms of *chalifin* is sharp. In *kinyan sudar*, the *gemara* debates whether the object (*kli*) must belong to the *koneh* (buyer) or the *makneh* (seller). If it must belong to the *makneh*, the logic — from the perspective of barter — becomes nearly unintelligible: in what sense is the seller giving the buyer his own object and receiving the sold property in return? As a symbolic signifier of *gemirat da'at*, this is coherent: I hand you my handkerchief as an expression of my decision to transfer my car to you. As barter, it makes no sense at all.

Tosafot takes this tension to its logical conclusion. If the rules of *kinyan sudar* — including the possibility that the *kli* belongs to the *makneh* — are applied mechanically to the barter case of the cow and the donkey, one arrives at the strange result that the *mishnah* would be describing not a swap (I give you my cow and receive your donkey) but a case where one party gives both objects. That is plainly not *chalifin* in any recognizable sense.

Yet the *gemara* does proceed to apply the categories of *kinyan sudar* — including the debate about *peirot* and *matbei'a* — to the barter case of the *mishnah*. One should bear this conceptual tension in mind as the discussion unfolds.

### Damim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ חֲלִיפִין מַאי נִיהוּ? – מַטְבֵּעַ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל הַנִּישּׁוֹם דָּמִים בְּאַחֵר,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the item given in **exchange** mentioned in the mishna? If it is referring to **a coin,** for which property is usually exchanged, can one **learn from** the mishna that **a coin can effect exchange,** i.e., it is possible to perform the act of acquisition of exchange, either a standard exchange or a symbolic exchange, using coins? This is problematic, as the *halakha* is that coins cannot be used for this act of acquisition. **Rav Yehuda said:** The phrase: All items used as monetary value for another item, is not referring to a coin. Rather, **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** With regard to **all** items **that** can be **appraised** when used **as monetary** value **for another** item, i.e., that their value can be appraised relative to the value of another item, excluding a coin, whose value is apparent,

## Three Categories

### Money as Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה נִתְחַיֵּיב בַּחֲלִיפִין. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **once one** party in the transaction **acquires** the item he is receiving, **this** party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged for it.** The novelty of the mishna is that all items, not only vessels, can be used to perform the act of acquisition of exchange. Therefore, one should not infer that the same is the *halakha* with regard to coins. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** afterward: **How so?** If **one exchanges an ox for a cow, or a donkey for an ox,** once this party acquires the animal that he is receiving, this party is obligated with regard to the item being exchanged for it. This clause apparently explains the previous clause, and employs the example of animals, not coins. The Gemara summarizes: **Learn from** this clause that the mishna is referring to acquisition through the exchange of items, not money.

To appreciate the *chiddush*, recall that *kinyan kesef* — acquisition through payment of money — does not work for *metaltelin* under rabbinic ordinance. If Reuven wishes to purchase Shimon's donkey, paying cash does not complete the sale; *meshicha* — taking physical possession — is required. Ordinarily, then, money functions as abstract currency, not as an object, and as such it cannot serve as the basis for *kinyan chalifin*. But if we reconceptualize the coins not as a payment for the donkey's price but as *an object being swapped for the donkey* — as if the coins were simply another physical item in a barter arrangement — then, at least theoretically, they might work.

A useful analogy: imagine a world in which currency had not yet been fully abstracted from physical objects, and all transactions were pure barter. In that world, a pile of coins would simply be another pile of items, no different in principle from a pile of grain or a collection of vessels. The coins would be appraised for their metallic value, just as the donkey is appraised for its utility, and the two parties would swap. In that frame, coins can serve as *chalifin*. The *chiddush* is that even within a monetized economy, one can conceptually reframe coins as objects rather than as currency and thereby perform *kinyan chalifin* with them.

The difficulty — and the reason this is ultimately rejected — is that coins are by nature abstract value. Treating them as mere physical objects is somewhat fictive. Indeed, the *gemara* eventually rules that *matbei'a lo na'aseh chalifin* — a coin cannot serve as the basis for *chalifin*. But the *hava amina* is that it could.

### Valuation Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּמַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין, מַאי כֵּיצַד? הָכִי קָאָמַר: פֵּירוֹת נָמֵי עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין. כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף בְּשַׂר שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה נִתְחַיֵּיב בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard **to what entered our minds initially, that a coin effects** symbolic **exchange, what** is the meaning of the clause: **How so,** if one exchanged an ox for a cow, once this party acquires the animal that he is receiving, this party is obligated with regard to the item being exchanged for it. This example does not involve a coin. The Gemara explains that it was assumed that **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** Not only can a coin be used in the act of acquisition of exchange, but **produce can also effect exchange. How so?** If **one exchanged meat of an ox for a cow, or** the **meat of a donkey for an ox, once this** party **acquires** the item that he is receiving, this party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged** for it.

On this reading, the clause is making the opposite point from the first interpretation: cash cannot be *chalifin*, but any non-cash object can. Cash is money; it does not need to be "converted" or assessed in monetary terms because it *is* the monetary term. Everything else — animals, produce, vessels — has a value that must be determined relative to money. It is precisely those things that are eligible for *chalifin*.

The grammar bears this out: *nishom* is a *nifal* of *shuma* — appraisal, assessment. Anything that must be appraised in monetary terms (because it is not itself monetary) is eligible for *chalifin*. *Deika nami*, the *gemara* notes — this reading is also supported by the *mishnah*'s own illustration: *keitzad? hechlif shor be-farah o chamor be-shor*, an ox for a cow and a donkey for an ox. The *keitzad* functions as an illustration, and an illustration involving livestock and animals confirms that the *mishnah* is talking about non-cash objects — precisely the category that Rav Yehuda identifies.

### Matbea Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דְּאָמַר: פֵּירוֹת עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין. אֶלָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן דְּאָמַר: פֵּירוֹת לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet, who says: Produce effects exchange,** i.e., the mode of acquisition of exchange applies not only to vessels but also to produce and animals. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who says: Produce does not effect exchange, what can be said?**

The *gemara* at this point raises an apparent implication from the first reading of the *mishnah*: *shema mina matbei'a na'aseh chalifin* — should we infer from the first reading that a coin can indeed serve as *chalifin*? The *gemara* answers that, to the contrary, the second reading is correct and we reject the first. Under the second reading, it is the very *absence* of monetary character — the need for appraisal — that qualifies an object for *chalifin*. Cash itself is disqualified.

This conclusion reflects the fundamental distinction between *kinyan kesef* and *kinyan chalifin*. *Kinyan kesef* uses money in its role as currency to pay for an object's price; the Sages annulled *kinyan kesef* for *metaltelin* out of concern for consumer protection — if the buyer pays and the seller has not yet delivered, the seller has no incentive to protect the goods. The Rabbis therefore replaced *kinyan kesef* with *kinyan meshicha*. *Kinyan chalifin*, by contrast, is a conceptually distinct mode of acquisition premised on the exchange of objects, not the payment of value. Money, as pure value rather than object, does not fit within *kinyan chalifin*.

### Peiros Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: יֵשׁ דָּמִים שֶׁהֵן כַּחֲלִיפִין, כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף דְּמֵי שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ דְּמֵי חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: According to this opinion, the mishna is dealing with money alone, and **this is what** the mishna **is saying: There is** a transaction involving **money that is like an exchange. How so?** If **one exchanged the monetary** value **of an ox for a cow, or the monetary** value **of a donkey for an ox,** the transaction is effective. In this case, one sold his ox to another for an agreed sum of money, and after the buyer acquired the ox by pulling it, he then offered to give the seller his cow in exchange for the money that he owes him. In this case the cow is acquired without the seller having to pull it. Although this acquisition initially was to be an exchange, it is ultimately a purchase for money, as the second animal is acquired as a result of the forgiving of the monetary debt.

Between a coin (*matbei'a*) at one end and a formal vessel or instrument (*kli*) at the other, there is the broad category of produce and ordinary physical goods — consumables, animals, and other non-fungible objects that are neither currency nor manufactured instruments.

The *mishnah*'s illustration — an ox for a cow — is the crux of the matter. Whether a *shor* or *chamor* belongs in the *peirot* category or the *kli* category is itself a question. One could argue that animals, as functional creatures that serve practical purposes, are analogous to instruments (*kli*); on the other hand, they are not manufactured objects, and their "functionality" is not the same as that of a vessel. Rashi and Tosafot both assume, for the purposes of reading this *gemara*, that the *shor* and *chamor* of the *mishnah* fall within the *peirot* category.

If that is so, the *mishnah*'s illustration only works according to Rav Sheshet, who permits *peirot* for *chalifin*. According to Rav Nachman, the *mishnah*'s case of the ox and cow cannot be an example of *chalifin* at all.

### Money via Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת. וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר לוֹ: ״נִשְׂרְפוּ חִיטֶּיךָ בָּעֲלִיָּיה״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this ruling in light of the *halakha* that one cannot acquire movable property by means of money alone? The Gemara explains that Rav Naḥman **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said: By Torah law money effects acquisition,** i.e., when one pays money he acquires the item, even if he has not yet performed another act of acquisition. **And what is** the **reason** that the Sages **said** that **pulling acquires** an item and money does not? This is a rabbinic **decree lest** the seller **say to** the buyer after receiving the money: **Your wheat was burned in the loft.** If a fire breaks out or some other mishap occurs after a seller receives the money, he will not bother to save the goods in his house because they no longer belong to him, and the buyer may incur a loss.

Rav Nachman's position creates a serious exegetical problem: if *peirot* cannot serve as *chalifin* and the only eligible object is a *kli*, there is no *kli* anywhere in the *mishnah*. How then does Rav Nachman read the *mishnah*?

The *gemara* proposes that Rav Nachman interprets the *mishnah* entirely differently. On his reading, the *mishnah* is not discussing *kinyan chalifin* at all. Rather: *yesh damim she-hen ke-chalifin* — there are cases where money functions analogously to *chalifin* in effecting acquisition. The illustration would then be: if you exchange the *money owed for* an ox with an actual cow — that is, if the outstanding debt from one transaction is offset against a new one — this can work to effect acquisition.

Concretely: Reuven sells his cow to Shimon, who now owes Reuven a hundred dollars. Before Shimon pays, Reuven tells Shimon: I will take your donkey in exchange for the hundred dollars you owe me. You keep the hundred, I get the donkey. On this reading, the "exchange" is not of physical objects but of a pre-existing monetary obligation. *Dmei shor be-farah* — the price-value of an ox against a cow — is being exchanged not in a physical barter but via the reallocation of a debt.

The underlying legal mechanism, the *gemara* explains, is that Rav Nachman holds like Rabbi Yochanan, who maintained that *mi-de-oraita ma'ot konot* — biblically speaking, *kinyan kesef* works for *metaltelin*. The reason the Sages replaced *kinyan kesef* with *kinyan meshicha* was a practical concern: if the buyer pays and the seller has no incentive to safeguard the goods, and then claims they were lost or destroyed, the buyer is left unprotected. But the Sages enacted this protective measure only for common scenarios (*milta de-shchicha*); for uncommon scenarios (*milta de-lo shchicha*), they did not extend the enactment, and the biblical rule of *kinyan kesef* remains operative.

This is a striking result. The offset of a prior debt — where nothing changes hands at all and the entire transaction is abstract — is identified as the uncommon scenario in which the Sages' enactment does not apply, so that *kinyan kesef* works there by biblical right. The *gemara* is effectively saying that the scenario where the most tenuous possible "payment" occurs is the one in which *kinyan kessef* is most firmly operative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דִשְׁכִיחָ[א] גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן, מִילְּתָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָ[א] לָא גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages therefore decreed that acquisition takes effect only when a buyer pulls the item. The reason that the mishna allows a transaction that indicates that one can effect acquisition using only money is because that case of the mishna as explained by Rav Naḥman is an uncommon occurrence. It is rare for one who has sold his animal in exchange for money to change his mind and request an animal from the purchaser instead. With regard to **a common occurrence, the Sages issued a decree,** whereas in the case of **an uncommon occurrence, the Sages did not issue a decree.** Consequently, the Sages did not apply their decree to this situation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמַר: מְשִׁיכָה מְפוֹרֶשֶׁת מִן הַתּוֹרָה, הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת דְּאָמַר פֵּירוֹת עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, מְתָרֵץ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how is the mishna explained **according to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, who** disagrees with Rabbi Yoḥanan and **says** that **pulling** is **explicitly** stated **in the Torah?** Reish Lakish maintains that the acquisition of movable property cannot be performed with money by Torah law, and therefore there can be no distinction between common and uncommon cases. **This works out well if** Reish Lakish **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet, who says** that **produce effects exchange.** If so, he can **explain** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב נַחְמָן דְּאָמַר פֵּירוֹת לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, וּמַטְבֵּעַ לָא קָנֵי, בְּמַאי מוֹקֵי לַהּ? עַל כֻּרְחָךְ, כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who says** that **produce does not effect exchange and a coin does not effect acquisition** by Torah law or by rabbinic law, **in what** manner **does he establish** the mishna? The Gemara answers: **Perforce** Reish Lakish **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet.**

Rashi already notes that logically the *shor* and *chamor* of the *mishnah* could just as well be placed in the *kli* category rather than *peirot*, which would dissolve the entire problem for Rav Nachman. But the deeper issue is conceptual: the *gemara* is applying to the plain barter case of the *mishnah* a set of categorical distinctions — *matbei'a*, *peirot*, *kli* — that properly belong to the *sugya* of *kinyan sudar*, where the *kinyan* is not a simple exchange of goods but a formal act of symbolic commitment. The conflation of barter and *kinyan sudar* under a single framework produces the interpretive contortions the *gemara* undertakes here.

The *gemara* concludes the passage regarding Reish Lakish, who holds that *meshicha meforeshet min ha-Torah* — the requirement of *meshicha* for *metaltelin* is explicitly derived from the Torah, and he does not share Rabbi Yochanan's view that *kinyan kesef* retains biblical force. For Reish Lakish, the Rav Nachman reading of the *mishnah* is simply impossible — there is no *kinyan kesef* to invoke. Reish Lakish must therefore read the *mishnah* as a case of *chalifin* with *peirot*, and is therefore compelled to hold like Rav Sheshet that *peirot* do indeed work for *chalifin*.

## Reshus Gavoa vs. Hedyot

### Kesef vs. Chazaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רְשׁוּת הַגָּבוֹהַּ בְּכֶסֶף, וּרְשׁוּת הַהֶדְיוֹט בַּחֲזָקָה. אֲמִירָתוֹ לְגָבוֹהַּ – כִּמְסִירָתוֹ לְהֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The authority of the Temple** treasury effects acquisition **by** means of **money** to the seller. **And the authority,** i.e., the mode of acquisition, **of a commoner** [***hedyot***] **is by possession.** Furthermore, **one's declaration to the Most High,** i.e., when one consecrates an item through speech, **is equivalent to transferring** an item **to a common person,** and the item is acquired by the Temple treasury through his mere speech.

### Amirah Principle

The *mishnah* uses the older terminology of *reshus gavoah* — the domain of the Most High — for what is elsewhere called *hekdesh*, and *reshus hedyot* — the domain of the ordinary person — for what is called *chulin*, non-consecrated property. *Hekdesh* acquires through money (*kesef*); a layperson acquires through *chazakah* — meaning physical possession, which in practice means *meshicha* for *metaltelin* and *chazakah* for *karka*.

The *mishnah* adds a further statement: *amiraso le-gavoah ki-mesiraso le-hedyot* — one's verbal declaration to the Most High is equivalent to physical delivery to an ordinary person. If I declare an ox to be a *korban olah*, or declare that my house is *hekdesh*, those words immediately transfer the status and ownership of the object to the *Beit HaMikdash*, without any physical act of *kinyan* being required. Contrast this with a transfer to another person, which requires either *meshicha* (for *metaltelin*) or *chazakah* (for *karka*).

### Gizbar Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד רְשׁוּת הַגָּבוֹהַּ בְּכֶסֶף? גִּיזְבָּר שֶׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת בִּבְהֵמָה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם – קָנָה. וּבַהֶדְיוֹט – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּמְשׁוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:6): **How does the authority of the Temple** treasury effect acquisition **by** means of **money?** With regard to the Temple **treasurer who gives coins for an animal, even** if the **animal is at the** other **end of the world, he acquires it** immediately. **And with regard to a commoner, he does not acquire** the animal **until he pulls** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד אֲמִירָתוֹ לַגָּבוֹהַּ כִּמְסִירָתוֹ לַהֶדְיוֹט? הָאוֹמֵר ״שׁוֹר זֶה עוֹלָה״, ״בַּיִת זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם – קָנָה. בַּהֶדְיוֹט – לֹא קָנָה
>
> **Translation:** **How is one's declaration to the Most High equivalent to transferring** an item **to a common person?** With regard to **one who says: This ox is a burnt-offering,** or: **This house is consecrated** property, the Temple treasury **acquires** these **even** if they are **at the** other **end of the world.** There is no need for a further act of acquisition, as that statement alone is sufficient. Whereas **with regard to a commoner, he does not acquire** property in this manner

### Kedusha & Ownership

One might initially ask whether this rule serves *hekdesh*'s interests. After all, we noted earlier that the reason the Sages replaced *kinyan kesef* with *kinyan meshicha* for ordinary transactions was to protect the buyer: if the buyer has already paid but not yet taken possession, and the seller has no incentive to safeguard the goods, the buyer bears the loss when something goes wrong. Does the same concern not apply to *hekdesh*?

This question sets up the discussion that follows. The *mishnah*'s statement that *amiraso le-gavoah ki-mesiraso le-hedyot* reflects a broader point about the nature of *hekdesh* property. When an animal or house is designated as *hekdesh*, two distinct legal dimensions are implicated: the *kedusha* — the sacred status — and the ownership. By a *korban*, such as an animal declared an *olah*, the primary dimension is the sacred status: it is not fundamentally a transaction of ownership that drives the designation, but the imposition of a new intrinsic status on the object. One who misuses *hekdesh* property violates the prohibition of *meilah* — misappropriation of sacred property — which is about the violation of the sacred status, not merely a property offense.

But when someone declares his house *hekdesh*, the primary intent is typically to make a donation to the *Beit HaMikdash*. There the ownership dimension is more central. Yet even there, the Torah derives from the verse *ish ki yakdish et beito kodesh la-Shem* — a man who declares his house holy to God — that the verbal designation alone effects both the sacred status and the transfer of ownership simultaneously. This is the power of *amirah le-gavoah*: words that create *kedusha* are also words that transfer ownership, and no separate physical act is required.

## Price Changes

### Mashchu Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיִּמְשׁוֹךְ וְיַחְזִיק. מְשָׁכוֹ בְּמָנֶה וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִפְדּוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁעָמַד בְּמָאתַיִם – נוֹתֵן מָאתַיִם. מַאי טַעְמָא – ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **until he pulls or takes possession** of it. Additionally, the Temple treasury has the following advantage: If a commoner **pulled** a consecrated item for the purpose of acquiring it **with one hundred dinars, and he did not manage to redeem it** by paying the one hundred dinars to the Temple treasurer **before** the item's price **stood at two hundred** dinars, he **gives two hundred** dinars. **What is the reason** for this? It states: **He will give the money and it will be assured to him** (see Leviticus 27:19). This indicates that one can acquire an item from the Temple treasury only by the actual transfer of money.

The *gemara* now explores a set of cases involving fluctuating market prices in the context of *hekdesh* transactions, and these cases reveal the precise way in which *hekdesh*'s acquisition rules are structured in its favor.

The first case: *mashchu be-maneh, ve-lo hispik lifdoto ad she-amad be-masayim* — a buyer did *meshicha* on an item priced at a hundred from *hekdesh*, but before he paid the money (*pidyon* — the redemption payment that removes the *kedusha* from the object), the market price rose to two hundred. Who owns the object?

Since *hekdesh* acquires through *kesef*, and no money has yet been paid, *hekdesh* still owns the object despite the buyer having performed *meshicha*. The *meshicha* accomplishes nothing: it does not finalize the transfer from *hekdesh* because *hekdesh* does not go by *meshicha*. Consequently, *hekdesh* is entitled to demand *masayim* — two hundred — before releasing the object. The buyer cannot claim the object at the original agreed price. The source is *ve-nasan ha-kesef ve-kam lo* — the object is acquired when the money is given.

### Padahu Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשָׁכוֹ בְּמָאתַיִם, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִפְדּוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁעָמַד בְּמָנֶה – נוֹתֵן מָאתַיִם. מַאי טַעְמָא – לֹא יְהֵא כֹּחַ הֶדְיוֹט חָמוּר מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, in the reverse case, when the commoner **pulled** an item when it was worth **two hundred** dinars, **and he did not manage to redeem it before** the value of the item **stood at one hundred dinars,** he **gives two hundred** dinars. **What is the reason** for this? **The power of a commoner should not be greater than** that of the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property. Since a commoner completes his acquisition by pulling, the item is acquired by him at that price, and the Temple treasury should not suffer a loss in a case where the commoner could not have reneged on his commitment had he been dealing with another commoner.

The second case is the inverse: *mashchu be-masayim, ve-lo hispik lifdoto ad she-amad be-maneh* — the buyer did *meshicha* when the item was worth two hundred, and before paying, the price dropped to a hundred. Now the buyer might reason: the sale was never final (no *kesef* has been paid), so I will only pay a hundred. The *halacha* is that he must pay two hundred. *Mah ta'ama? Lo yehei koach hedyot chamur me-hekdesh* — a layperson should not have greater legal force than *hekdesh*. If in an ordinary transaction the buyer could insist on a lower price after price fluctuation when the sale was not final, that would give the ordinary counterparty rights that *hekdesh* lacks. The rules are structured so that *hekdesh* is never placed at a disadvantage relative to a layperson.

The principle that emerges from the first two cases is that the *kinyanim* for *hekdesh* are stacked in *hekdesh*'s favor. *Hekdesh* uses whichever mode of acquisition — *kesef* or *meshicha* — works to its benefit in the specific situation.

### Mihu She-Para

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּדָאוֹ בְּמָאתַיִם, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לְמוֹשְׁכוֹ עַד שֶׁעָמַד בְּמָנֶה – נוֹתֵן מָאתַיִם, מַאי טַעְמָא – ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״. פְּדָאוֹ בְּמָנֶה וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לְמוֹשְׁכוֹ עַד שֶׁעָמַד בְּמָאתַיִם – מַה שֶּׁפָּדָה פָּדוּי, וְאֵין נוֹתֵן אֶלָּא מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if the commoner **redeemed it** at **two hundred** dinars, **but he did not manage to pull it before** its value **stood at one hundred dinars, he gives two hundred** dinars. **What is the reason?** It states: **He will give the money and it will be assured to him,** which indicates that the redemption of consecrated property is effected with money. By contrast, if **he redeemed it at one hundred dinars and did not manage to pull it before it stood at two hundred** dinars, **what he redeemed is redeemed, and he gives only one hundred dinars.**

The third case reverses the starting point: *padahu be-masayim, ve-lo hispik limshacho ad she-amad be-maneh* — the buyer paid two hundred in cash but had not yet performed *meshicha* when the price dropped to a hundred. The obvious implication of *hekdesh*'s ownership rules: *kinyan kesef* was effected by payment, and so the buyer owns the object and cannot demand a refund. He paid two hundred, the sale is complete, and the lower price is irrelevant.

But the next case is surprising. *Padahu be-maneh, ve-lo hispik limshacho ad she-amad be-masayim* — the buyer paid a hundred in cash, and before performing *meshicha*, the price rose to two hundred. One might expect *hekdesh* to invoke *meshicha*: since *meshicha* was never performed, and *hekdesh* can use whichever *kinyan* is most advantageous, perhaps *hekdesh* can now declare the sale incomplete on the basis that no *meshicha* was performed, and demand two hundred. The *gemara* rejects this.

### Limits on Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? הָכִי נָמֵי נֵימָא: לֹא יְהֵא כֹּחַ הֶדְיוֹט חָמוּר מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** is this the *halakha*? **So too, let us say: The power of a commoner should not be greater than** that of the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property, and if this occurred in a case where he was dealing with another commoner he would need to pay the full two hundred dinars, which was the value of the item when he pulled it. Shouldn't the Temple treasury have as much power as a common person?

*Mah she-pada paduy* — what has been redeemed is redeemed. The payment of a hundred was effective, and *hekdesh* cannot retroactively demand more. The *gemara*'s reasoning: *atu hedyot lav be-mi she-para kai?* — even in a transaction between ordinary people, if the buyer has paid money and the seller now wishes to back out before *meshicha* is performed, the seller does not escape consequence-free. The seller who reneges after receiving cash is subject to the *mi she-para* curse: may the One who exacted punishment upon the generation of the Flood and upon all those who violated their commitments exact punishment from one who does not stand by his word.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ הֶדְיוֹט לָאו בְּמִי שֶׁפָּרַע קָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is an additional impediment to reneging on an agreement with another commoner. **Is that to say** that **a commoner** who gave a seller money and changed his mind before acquiring the item is **not** forced to accept upon himself the curse **of: He Who exacted payment** from the generation of the flood (see Genesis, chapter 7) and the generation of the dispersion (see Genesis, chapter 11) will punish one who does not keep his word? Although a commoner can legally renege on his agreement at this stage, the Sages disapproved of such dishonest conduct, and one who did so would be cursed in this manner. Consequently, the power of a commoner is not greater than that of the Temple treasury, as it is not a simple matter to renege on an agreement with another commoner.

*Hekdesh*, like an ordinary person, is bound by the moral commitment implicit in having received cash. Even though *hekdesh* retains the technical right to insist on *meshicha* as the completing act, it cannot exercise that right in circumstances where doing so would be seen as reneging on a commitment already evidenced by the exchange of money. As it was put: *yado shel hekdesh al ha-elyona* — the advantage is always with *hekdesh* — but that principle cannot be used to permit the *Beit HaMikdash* to act as a scoundrel. *Hekdesh* must honor its commitments, and once cash has been accepted, backing out on the basis of subsequent price increases falls within the conduct that even ordinary commercial morality forbids.
