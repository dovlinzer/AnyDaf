# Bava Metzia 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah & Torah Transmission

### Zav & Tumat Midras

Before turning to the new *mishnah*, it is worth clarifying a point from the previous session concerning the transfer of *tumah* when a tree bends under the weight of people climbing it. The relevant figure is not any impure person in general, but *davka* a *zav* — a man or woman who has an abnormal genital discharge and thereby contracts a very high degree of *tumah*. There is a special rule applying to *zavim* and *zavot*: they generate *tumat midras*, the *tumah* produced when they stand or sit upon an object.

Ordinarily, when *tumah* transfers from an *av hatumah* — the primary source of impurity — to a second object, it descends a level: from *av* to *rishon*. The striking feature of *tumat midras* is that the transfer does not descend. The object upon which the *zav* sits or stands itself becomes an *av hatumah*. The question naturally arises: how can *tumah* ever transfer horizontally? *Tumah* should always diminish in potency as it passes from object to object.

The answer, associated with the Brisker analysis, is that no horizontal transfer of *tumah* is occurring at all. The object a *zav* sits upon acquires its own independent identity — it becomes, by definition, "the thing the *zav* sat upon," a halakhic category carrying *av hatumah* status as a matter of biblical law. Rather than the *zav*'s *tumah* somehow flowing into the chair at full strength, the chair is itself constituted as a new *av hatumah* because of the *zav*'s weight bearing down upon it. The weight-bearing object becomes, in a sense, an extension of the *zav* himself. From that new *av hatumah*, subsequent contact produces a *rishon l'tumah*, which is itself still a level of *tumah* of considerable stringency, though the third remove — *shlishi l'tumah* — is likely rabbinic.

### Sussman Theory

A second clarification concerns the discussion of *tirnanim* cited in the previous session. In speaking of those citations as "literary" creations, one must be careful: these traditions were not written down. They were transmitted orally. This is precisely the point argued at length by Yaakov Sussman in his monograph *Torah Shebal Peh Peshuto K'Mashmao: Kocha Shel Kotzo Shel Yod*, in which he demonstrates from evidence throughout both the *Yerushalmi* and *Bavli* that there is no trace of a written *mishnah* in the Amoraic period, and that any contrary impression reflects a misreading of the sources.

The difficulty with Sussman's broader thesis is that he extends this claim to deny the existence of any officially written *Torah Shebal Peh* during the Amoraic period — a position complicated by the fact that the *Bavli* itself repeatedly refers to *Sifra Debei Rav*, meaning either the book associated with Rav, the first-generation Babylonian *Amora*, or the book of the school-house of Rav. This clearly seems to denote some form of written *midrash halakhah* akin to the *Mekhilta*, *Sifra*, and *Sifrei* that we possess today. When confronted with the word "book," Sussman argues that it refers not to a physical written text but to the regimented, formalized interpretation transmitted within a school.

### Literary Transmission

This interpretive move opens onto a broader point about the nature of oral transmission. Even the small-scale exegetical units visible in the *Bavli* — where a *mishnah* is followed by "the school of Rabbi Yannai says that it means such-and-such" — can be understood as part of the intellectual *morasha* of a given school. A school develops characteristic readings of individual passages and transmits them generation after generation, even without committing them to writing. The word "literary," in this context, must be taken in a broad sense that includes any structured, transmissible formulation of meaning — not merely something written down or elaborately composed as a text.

This is not so foreign even in the modern *yeshiva* context. The *Torah* of Rav Soloveitchik, for instance, was transmitted largely orally: students heard his interpretations, repeated them to others, and preserved them in that way. The "literary construction" of a teaching, in this broader sense, is simply its being shaped into a form capable of transmission.

Returning to the *tannan hatam* citations discussed in the previous session: what they share is that each involves a *mishnah* containing a *shi'ur* — a measurement — and each is followed by an explanation from the school of Rabbi Yannai specifying what that *shi'ur* means. They are grouped together on that basis. Moreover, the presence of these citations constitutes evidence that there was indeed a developed *Talmud* — commentary on *mishnayot* — around *Seder Tahorot*, even though that *seder* has no tractates of its own in the *Bavli*. These three *tannan hatam* passages (one of which appears as a *tano rabbanan* but is in fact a *mishnah*) preserve what was effectively Babylonian *Talmud* on *Seder Tahorot*, a body of material that otherwise does not exist in the *Bavli* the way *Seder Nezikin* does.

## Makat Medina

### Hamekabel Sadeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וַאֲכָלָה חָגָב, אוֹ נִשְׁדְּפָה. אִם מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא – מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן חֲכוֹרוֹ. אִם אֵינָהּ מַכַּת מְדִינָה – אֵין מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן חֲכוֹרוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם קִיבְּלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בְּמָעוֹת, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אֵינוֹ מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מֵחֲכוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who receives a field from another** to cultivate in return **for** the payment of **ten *kor* of wheat per year, and** its produce **was blighted** by a crop disease or the like, the cultivator **gives** the owner the ten *kor* of wheat **from it** but does not have to provide him with high quality wheat. If the wheat stalks produced by the field **were** particularly **good** stalks of **wheat,** the cultivator **may not say to** the owner: **I will buy** regular wheat **from the market; rather,** he **gives him from inside** the field itself.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 105b reads:

If someone leases a field from another person — and this is a *chakhirut* lease, meaning the lessee pays a fixed amount rather than a share of the produce — and the entire crop is devoured by locusts or destroyed by blight, then if this disaster constitutes a *makat medina*, a widespread regional catastrophe, the lessor reduces the amount owed under the lease. If it is not a *makat medina*, the lessee must pay the full contracted sum regardless.

### Definition & Rabbi Yehuda

The Tanna *Rabbi Yehuda* adds a further qualification: if the lessee contracted to pay in coin rather than produce, then in either case — *makat medina* or not — there is no reduction.

### Blight on Plain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָמֵי מַכַּת מְדִינָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּגוֹן דְּאִישְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא. עוּלָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּדְּפוּ אַרְבַּע שָׂדוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **a regional disaster? Rav Yehuda said:** If, **for example, most of** that **valley** in which the field was located **was wind blasted,** it is considered a regional disaster. **Ulla said:** If, **for example, four fields were wind blasted on its four sides,** it is considered a regional disaster.

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly constitutes a *makat medina*? *Rav Yehuda* defines it as a case where most of the *baga* — the plain, the large contiguous agricultural tract — has been blighted. The disaster is thus still geographically contained but covers a substantial and identifiable agricultural area, of which the leased field is part.

*Ulla* offers a somewhat different formulation: a *makat medina* is established when four surrounding fields, one in each direction, have been blighted. This appears to set a more localized threshold than *Rav Yehuda*'s majority-of-the-*baga* standard, though the precise magnitude of a *baga* in the Babylonian context is uncertain. In the land of Israel, where the terrain is broken by hillsides and arable tracts are relatively small, the practical difference between these two definitions may be less dramatic than it initially appears. A contemporary analogy might be the situation of fires in Haifa: a substantial portion of the city is affected, a communal authority declares it a targeted attack, and questions arise about who bears the losses — government or private insurance. The *makat medina* concept is structurally similar.

### Ulla's Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא, בָּעוּ בְּמַעְרְבָא: נִשְׁדַּף תֶּלֶם אֶחָד עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלָּהּ, מַאי? נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר תֶּלֶם אֶחָד עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלָּהּ, מַהוּ? אִפְּסִיקא בְּיָרָא, מַאי? אַסְפַּסְתָּא,
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla** also **said: They raise** the following **dilemma in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: If **one furrow was wind blasted along its entire length,** adjacent to other fields that were wind blasted, **what** is the *halakha*? Is this considered to be part of the regional disaster? Conversely, if **one furrow remained** undamaged **along its entire length, what is** the *halakha*? Does the remaining furrow mean that the entire field is not considered to be part of the regional disaster? If **a fallow** field **divided** between the cultivated fields and the fields that were wind blasted, **what** is the *halakha*? Alternatively, if there was a field of **fodder** between this field and the others that were wind blasted,

*Ulla* further reports that in the West — *Eretz Yisrael* — a series of unresolved questions (*ba'u b'ma'arva*) were posed about the edges of this rule:

If only a single row of plants was damaged across the length of the field — what is the rule? If all but a single row was destroyed — what then? If pits separate the leased field from its neighbors — does that geographic interruption matter? If the surrounding fields are planted with *aspasta* (animal fodder) rather than food crops — is that comparable? If the surrounding fields are planted with a different grain variety — is that treated as a different crop? And specifically: if wheat and barley are involved on different sides, are these treated as *zera acher*, different plantings? Finally, what if the blight that struck the entire world was of one type — *shidafon* — while the blight on this field was of another type, *yerakon*? Or vice versa?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? זֶרַע אַחֵר, מַאי? חִיטֵּי לְגַבֵּי (שְׂעוֹרִים) [שְׂעָרֵי] כְּזֶרַע אַחֵר דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא? כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ בְּשִׁדָּפוֹן, וְשֶׁלּוֹ בְּיֵרָקוֹן, אִי נָמֵי: כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ בְּיֵרָקוֹן, וְשֶׁלּוֹ בְּשִׁדָּפוֹן, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **what** is the *halakha*? If the surrounding fields were planted with **a different** type of **seed, what** is the *halakha*? Likewise, is **wheat, in relation to barley,** considered **like a different** type of **seed or not?** Furthermore, if **the entire world,** i.e., all the surrounding fields, were blighted **by windblasts and his** was affected **by mildew;** or **alternatively,** if the fields of **the entire world** were struck **by mildew, and his** were blighted **with windblasts, what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara responds: No resolution is found to any of these dilemmas, and the dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The *Gemara* records a single verdict: *teiku* — these questions remain unresolved. *Ulla* transmits a chain of questions from *Eretz Yisrael* to which neither that tradition nor the Babylonian *Gemara* supplies an answer. A check of the *Yerushalmi* likewise reveals no parallel to these questions there, which suggests this is a tradition preserved in *Bavel* but not recorded in the Palestinian *Talmud*.

## Mazal & Divine Providence

### Wrong Crop Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִרְעַהּ חִיטֵּי, וַאֲזַל הוּא וְזַרְעַהּ שְׂעָרֵי, וְאִשְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא, וְאִשְׁתְּדוּף נָמֵי הָנָךְ שְׂעָרֵי דִּילֵיהּ, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ זְרַעְתַּהּ חִיטֵּי הֲוָה נָמֵי מִשְׁתַּדְפָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ זְרַעְתַּהּ חִיטֵּי הֲוָה מִקַּיַּים בִּי ״וְתִגְזַר אֹמֶר וְיָקׇם לָךְ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another question: If the owner **said to** the tenant farmer: **Plant** the field with **wheat, and he went and planted it** with **barley, and most of the valley was wind blasted, and these** fields with **barley of his were also wind blasted, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say that** the tenant farmer can **say to him:** Even **if I had planted it** with **wheat** it **would likewise have been wind blasted,** as all the surrounding fields suffered the same fate, **or perhaps** the owner **can say to him: Had you planted it** with **wheat,** the following verse **would have been fulfilled for me: "And you shall decree a matter and it will be established for you,** and the light shall shine upon your ways" (Job 22:28), since you might have merited greater success by following my wishes.

The *Gemara* now introduces a more intricate set of cases in which the operative question is not simply whether a *makat medina* occurred, but how personal fortune — *mazal* — interacts with contractual obligation and divine providence.

The first case: the *choker* (lessee) contracted to plant wheat but instead planted barley. A blight then struck most of the *baga*, and his barley was destroyed along with it. The *Gemara* poses the question: does the owner argue that had the lessee planted wheat as contracted, the wheat would have been blighted too — and therefore the *makat medina* rule applies and the rent is reduced? Or can the owner argue: had you planted wheat as we agreed, God would have upheld for me the verse *v'tigzar omer v'yakum lach* — that God fulfills the decrees of the righteous — and my crop would have been spared by divine providence?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי זְרַעְתַּהּ חִיטֵּי הֲוָה מִקַּיַּים בִּי ״וְתִגְזַר אֹמֶר וְיָקׇם לָךְ וְעַל דְּרָכֶיךָ נָגַהּ אוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It **stands to reason that** the owner can **say to him: Had you planted it** with **wheat** it **would have fulfilled for me: "And you shall decree a matter and it will be established for you, and the light shall shine upon your ways."**

This is a striking *sugya*. The entire discussion turns on whether metaphysical claims — specifically, claims about how divine providence operates — can be invoked to determine legal obligations. The *Gemara*'s answer (*mistavra*) favors the owner: it is more reasonable to say that had the lessee planted wheat as contracted, God would have protected that crop through the owner's prayers and merit. The owner prayed specifically for wheat, and the lessee disrupted that by planting barley. One might object that the owner should have prayed more generally for a successful harvest rather than specifically for wheat — but the owner's position is that he contracted for wheat, and it was reasonable for him to pray accordingly.

It is important to note that this *sugya* is not simply entertaining metaphysical speculation for its own sake. The metaphysics establish the underlying reality upon which the legal rule is constructed. If divine providence operates in a demonstrable way — protecting certain people's crops under certain conditions — then the legal consequences flow from that factual determination.

### Owner's Fields Destroyed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּדְפוּ כׇּל שְׂדוֹתָיו שֶׁל מַחְכִּיר, וְאִשְׁתְּדוּף נָמֵי הָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, וְלָא אִשְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אִשְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא – לָא מְנַכֵּי לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִשְׁתְּדוּף כּוּלְּהוּ אַרְעָתֵיהּ, מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי מִשּׁוּם לַתָּךְ דִּידָךְ הוּא, דְּהָא (מִשְׁתַּדְפוּ) [נִשְׁתַּדְפוּ] כָּל שְׂדוֹתֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another question: If **all the fields of the owner of the land were wind blasted and this** one **was also wind blasted with them, but the majority of the valley was not wind blasted, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since the majority of the valley was not wind blasted** the tenant farmer does **not subtract for the owner** the amount owed for his tenancy, as this is not a regional disaster, **or perhaps** could one claim that **since all the lands** of the owner **were wind blasted** the tenant **can say to** the owner: **This** happened **due to your** bad **fortune, as all your fields were wind blasted?**

The second case reverses the direction of the *mazal* argument. All of the landowner's fields throughout the country were blighted. This particular leased field, located in a plain where no other field was blighted, was also destroyed — but the rest of that plain was untouched.

Since most of the *baga* was not blighted, the strict *makat medina* rule does not formally apply. Can the owner therefore demand full payment? Or can the lessee argue: look at your fields — they were all destroyed throughout the country; that is your bad *mazal*, and this field too is a casualty of your bad luck rather than any localized natural disaster?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מִשּׁוּם לַתַּאי דִּידִי – הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּיַּיר לִי פּוּרְתָּא, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״כִּי נִשְׁאַרְנוּ מְעַט מֵהַרְבֵּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It **stands to reason that** the owner can **say to** the tenant: **If** it was **due to my** bad **fortune, a little would have been left for me, as it is written: "For we are left but a few from many"** (Jeremiah 42:2), which indicates that even one suffering from misfortune does not lose all he has.

The *Gemara* resolves this (*mistavra*) in favor of the landowner. The owner can respond to the lessee: had it been solely my bad luck causing this particular field to be blighted, divine providence would have left me something — as the verse says, *ki nisharnu me'at me'harbeh*, "for we have remained a small remnant." Bad luck does not wipe one out entirely; it leaves a small portion. Since the entire field was destroyed with nothing left, the destruction of this field cannot be attributed entirely to the owner's *mazal*. Thus the owner is not responsible, and the lessee still owes the full amount.

### All Fields Blighted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּדְּפוּ כׇּל שְׂדוֹתָיו שֶׁל חוֹכֵר וְאִשְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא, וְאִשְׁתְּדוּף נָמֵי הָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּאִשְׁתְּדוּף רוּבָּא דְבָאגָא מְנַכֵּי לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִשְׁתְּדוּף כּוּלְּהוּ אַרְעָתֵיהּ, מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם לַתָּךְ דִּידָךְ הוּא, דְּהָא (מִשְׁתַּדְּפוּ) [נִשְׁתַּדְּפוּ] כָּל שְׂדוֹתֶיךָ. מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם לַתָּאךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a similar case: If **all the fields of the tenant farmer were wind blasted and most of the valley was wind blasted and this** field **was also wind blasted with them, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since most of the valley was wind blasted** the tenant farmer **subtracts for the owner** the amount owed for his tenancy and does not pay, **or perhaps, since all** the tenant's **lands were wind blasted,** the owner **can say to the** tenant: The damage is **due to your** bad **fortune, as all your fields were wind blasted.** The Gemara responds: **It stands to reason that** the owner **can say to him:** It occurred **due to your** bad **fortune.**

The third case presents the inverse situation. The lessee himself owns many fields in addition to the one he leases. All of his own fields were destroyed by blight. The plain in which the leased field sits was also mostly blighted, and the leased field was destroyed as part of that general catastrophe.

On its face, this is a standard *makat medina* case, and the lessee would expect a reduction. But the owner argues: your fields all over the country were destroyed — that is your bad *mazal*, and that bad luck explains why this field too was blighted. Therefore you owe me the full amount.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? הָכָא נָמֵי נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי מִשּׁוּם לַתַּאי דִּידִי הוּא – הֲוָה מִשְׁ[תַּ]יַּיר לִי פּוּרְתָּא, דַּהֲוָה מִקַּיַּים בִּי ״כִּי נִשְׁאַרְנוּ מְעַט מֵהַרְבֵּה״. מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוָה חֲזֵית לְאִישְׁתַּיּוֹרֵי לָךְ מִידֵּי – הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּיַּיר לָךְ מִדְּנַפְשָׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** should this be so? **Here too, let us say to** the owner: **If** it was **due to my** bad **fortune, a little would have been left for me, as** the following verse **would have been fulfilled for me: "For we are left but a few from many."** The Gemara answers: This is not a valid claim **because** the owner can **say to** the tenant: **Had you been worthy of something being left for you,** it **would have been left from your own** private land, not the field you paid to cultivate.

The *Gemara* applies the same reasoning in reverse. The lessee can reply: had it been my bad luck causing this particular field's destruction, God would have left me something on it. Since nothing at all remained, the blight on this field cannot be attributed solely to my *mazal*. The broader *makat medina* was the cause, and accordingly the rent is reduced.

This trilogy of cases is analytically elegant: each invokes the principle that bad luck leaves a remnant, and the party whose personal *mazal* cannot account for the total destruction of the field is exonerated from full liability.

## Arachin Excursus

### Yovel & Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיְתָה שְׁנַת שִׁדָּפוֹן וְיֵרָקוֹן, אוֹ שְׁבִיעִית, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁנִים כִּשְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ – אֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna to the ruling that if there is a regional disaster the cultivator subtracts from the produce he owes as part of his tenancy. The *halakha* is that if one sells his field in Eretz Yisrael in a time when the *halakhot* of the Jubilee Year are in effect, he does not have the right to purchase it from the buyer until two years have passed. The mishna teaches (*Arakhin* 29b): If it **was a year of wind-blasted** crops **or mildew or** it was the **Sabbatical** Year, **or** if those **years were like the years of Elijah** in which no rain fell (see I Kings 17:1, 18:1–2), they **do not count** as part **of his tally** of years before he may repurchase his land.

At this point a challenge is raised from a *mishnah* in *Arachin* (Perek 7), concerning the redemption of land sold during the era when *Yovel* — the Jubilee — is operative. When a person sells ancestral land during such a period, the buyer must hold it for at least two years before the seller may redeem it. The verse states *u've-mispar shenei tevuot yimkor lach* — it is sold to you according to the number of years of produce. The buyer purchases a right to so many years of crops, and the price of redemption is adjusted proportionally based on the years remaining until *Yovel*.

The *mishnah* in *Arachin* teaches that years of *shidafon* or *yerakon* (blight), as well as a *shemitah* year or years like the years of Elijah when no rain fell anywhere, are not counted toward this minimum. Such years are excluded from the reckoning because they were not genuine years of agricultural produce.

### Blight Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: שִׁדָּפוֹן וְיֵרָקוֹן דּוּמְיָא דְּשָׁנִים כִּשְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ, מָה שְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ – דְּלָא הֲוַי תְּבוּאָה כְּלָל, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי – דְּלָא הֲוַי תְּבוּאָה כְּלָל. אֲבָל דְּאִיכָּא תְּבוּאָה – סָלְקָא לֵיהּ, וְלָא קָאָמְרִינַן מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the mishna: This *tanna* **teaches** that the cases of **windblasts and mildew** are **similar to** the case where **the years** were **like the years of Elijah. Just as the years of Elijah** is referring to a time **when there was no produce at all, so too, here,** windblasts and mildew are referring to cases **when there was no produce at all. But** by inference, one can learn that if **there was** some **produce,** it **counts** toward **his** tally of years before he may repurchase his land, **and we do not say that it is a regional disaster.**

The question arises: the *mishnah* in *Arachin* appears to treat *shidafon* and *yerakon* on analogy with the years of Elijah — that is, years when there were no crops anywhere in the world. How then can our *mishnah* in *Bava Metzia* speak of a localized regional blight as sufficient to trigger the *makat medina* rule? Is it possible that only a worldwide or nationwide agricultural collapse qualifies?

The *Gemara* answers: the cases are not comparable. The rule in *Arachin* is governed by a specific verse — *shenei tevuot*, years of crops. In that context, the only years excluded are years in which there are no crops anywhere in the world. The *makat medina* rule in *Bava Metzia*, by contrast, is not governed by that verse and operates on general principles. A localized regional blight can qualify as a *makat medina* for purposes of lease law without being a global catastrophe.

### Verse Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״בְּמִסְפַּר שְׁנֵי תְבוּאֹת יִמְכׇּר לָךְ״, שָׁנִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן תְּבוּאָה בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: There,** the case with regard to the sold field **is different, as the verse states** with regard to the sale and leasing of fields: **"According to the numbers of years of the crops he shall sell to you"** (Leviticus 25:15), which is referring to **years in which there is produce** harvested **in the world.**

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* makes this explicit: the rule in *Arachin* is different because of the verse *u've-mispar shenei tevuot yimkor lach*, which teaches that only years in which there is *tevuah* in the world count. Years of *shidafon* and *yerakon* qualify for exclusion only when the blight is total — when there is no *tevuah* anywhere, analogous to the years of Elijah when rain ceased everywhere.

### Shevi'it Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה שְׁבִיעִית תַּעֲלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן, דְּהָא אִיכָּא תְּבוּאָה בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁבִיעִית אַפְקַעְתָּא דְמַלְכָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: If that is so,** the **Sabbatical** Year **should count for him** as part **of his tally** of years, **as** at least **there is produce outside of Eretz** Yisrael. Rav Kahana **said to him:** The **Sabbatical** Year **is an abrogation of the King,** i.e., God. Therefore, it should not be included in the number of the years before land is repurchased.

*Rav Ashi* then poses a sharp question to *Rav Kahana*: if the *Arachin* rule excludes only years in which there is no *tevuah* in the world, why does *Shevi'it* — the Sabbatical Year — also get excluded? In *Eretz Yisrael* no planting occurs during *Shevi'it*, but the rest of the world plants and harvests normally. Crops grow in *chutz la'aretz* during a *Shevi'it* year. It is therefore not a year without *tevuah* in the world.

The answer: *Shevi'it afkata diMalka hi* — the Sabbatical Year is a divine decree of the King. God has declared that *Shevi'it* simply does not exist as an agricultural year in the context of *Eretz Yisrael*. It is categorically excluded from the universe of agriculture as far as the land of Israel is concerned, regardless of what grows elsewhere. This is not a factual judgment about the availability of produce worldwide, but a normative divine pronouncement.

### Hekdesh Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, שְׁבִיעִית לֹא תַּעֲלֶה לוֹ מִן הַגֵּירוּעַ! אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: נוֹתֵן סֶלַע וּפוּנְדְּיוֹן לְשָׁנָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּחַזְיָא לְמִישְׁטַחא בַּהּ פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: If that is so,** that the Sabbatical Year is entirely disregarded, then in the case of one who consecrates his field and wants to redeem it, the **Sabbatical** Year **should not count for him for the deduction** of the price of the field when it is redeemed. **Why did we learn** in a mishna (*Arakhin* 25a) that the one who consecrated his field **gives a *sela* and a *pundeyon*** coin, which is worth 16 *perutot*, to the Temple treasury **for** each **year** remaining until the Jubilee Year, including the Sabbatical Year, in accordance with the payment prescribed by the Torah (see Leviticus 27:16–19)? The amount to be paid per year, which is fifty shekels divided by the years remaining until the Jubilee Year, should not take the upcoming Sabbatical Years into account. Ravina **said to him: There it is different, as it is suitable for laying out produce on it.** Although one may not plant the field during the Sabbatical Year, one may use it for other purposes.

*Mar Zutra*, son of *Rav Mari*, raises a further difficulty to *Ravina*. If *Shevi'it* is excluded from the count of years when redeeming sold land — because it is *afkata diMalka* — why does it count when one is redeeming land consecrated as *hekdesh* (dedicated to the Temple)?

The mechanism of redeeming *hekdesh* land works as follows: when a person consecrates ancestral land to the Temple, the redemption price is assessed at a fixed rate — a *sela* and a *pundyon* per year remaining until *Yovel*. The *mishnah* in *Arachin* teaches this calculation includes all years including *Shevi'it*, yielding a forty-ninth of the total value per year (fifty years in a *Yovel* cycle, minus one for the *Yovel* year itself). If *Shevi'it* is truly excluded as a non-agricultural year, should one not subtract the *Shevi'it* years from the count and pay a forty-second (rather than a forty-ninth) of the total?

### Agricultural Work

*shani hatam de-chazya le-mishtach bah peirei* — that case is different because the land can still be used during *Shevi'it* for spreading out fruit to dry. Even though planting does not occur, a form of agricultural use — the spreading and processing of produce — does take place on the land. Therefore, *Shevi'it* counts as a year of agricultural activity for purposes of the *hekdesh* redemption calculation, even though it does not count for purposes of the *Arachin* land-sale redemption calculation, where the verse requires genuine years of *tevuah*.

## Planting Requirements

### Shmuel's Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁזְּרָעָהּ וְצִמְּחָה וַאֲכָלָהּ חָגָב. אֲבָל לֹא זְרָעָהּ כְּלָל – לָא, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ זְרַעְתַּהּ הֲוָה מִקַּיַּים בִּי ״לֹא יֵבֹשׁוּ בְּעֵת רָעָה וּבִימֵי רְעָבוֹן יִשְׂבָּעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: They taught** the *halakha* that if there is a regional disaster the cultivator subtracts from the produce he owes as part of his tenancy **only if** the tenant **planted** the field **and** it **sprouted and** then **grasshoppers consumed it,** or if he planted it with a different seed, **but** if **he did not plant it at all,** the tenant is **not** entitled to subtract from the amount he owes even if there was a regional disaster. This is **because** the owner can **say to him: Had you planted it,** perhaps my merit would have prevented the field from being affected by the epidemic, and the following verse **would have been fulfilled for me: "They will not be shamed in the time of evil, and in the days of famine they shall be satisfied"** (Psalms 37:19).

*Shmuel* now introduces a critical limitation on the *mishnah*'s *makat medina* rule. He says *lo shanu* — the *mishnah* only addressed the case where the lessee actually planted the land and the crop grew, and then the locusts or blight destroyed what had grown. But if the lessee did not plant at all, there is no reduction in what he owes. He must pay the full lease amount regardless.

The rationale the landowner can invoke: *ilu zar'at hava mekayem bi lo yevoshu beet ra'ah u've-yemei ra'avon yisba'u* — had you planted, God would have fulfilled for me the verse "they will not be ashamed in a time of evil, and in days of famine they shall be satisfied." Since you did not plant, you cannot claim that a *makat medina* struck and you were helpless. You prevented the divine protection from operating in the first place.

### Divine Verse Argument

*Rav Sheshet* challenges this position. How can such a metaphysical claim be entertained? There is a precedent: a shepherd who left his flock and went into town. While he was away, a wolf came and dragged away a sheep, and a lion came and killed another. The rule is that we do not say: had the shepherd been there, he would have saved the sheep. Rather, we assess the shepherd's capabilities: if he is the kind of person who could have driven off wolves and lions, he is liable; if not, he is exempt. The point is that we do not simply assume that presence guarantees rescue.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: רוֹעֶה שֶׁהָיָה רוֹעֶה וְהִנִּיחַ עֶדְרוֹ וּבָא לָעִיר, וּבָא זְאֵב וְטָרַף, וּבָא אֲרִי וְדָרַס – אֵין אוֹמְרִים: אִילּוּ הָיָה שָׁם הָיָה מַצִּיל, אֶלָּא אוֹמְדִים אוֹתוֹ אִם יָכוֹל לְהַצִּיל – חַיָּיב, וְאִם לָאו – פָּטוּר. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוֵית הָתָם הֲוָה מִקַּיַּים בִּי ״גַּם אֶת הָאֲרִי גַּם הַדּוֹב הִכָּה עַבְדֶּךָ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet raises an objection** from a *baraita*: In the case of **a shepherd who was herding** the animals of others, **and he left his flock and came to the town, and** in the meantime **a wolf came and tore** an animal to pieces, **or a lion came and trampled** one of the flock, **we do not say** definitively that **had he been there he would have rescued** them and therefore he is liable due to his absence. **Rather,** the court **estimates** with regard to **him: If he could** have **rescued** his animal by chasing a beast of this kind away, he is **liable,** as his departure from the scene was certainly a contributing factor to the damage. **If not,** he is **exempt** from liability. According to Shmuel's opinion, **why** is the shepherd exempt from liability? **Let** the owner **say to him: Had you been there,** the following verse **would have been fulfilled for me: "Your servant smote both the lion and the bear"** (I Samuel 17:36).

By the same logic, *Rav Sheshet* asks: why not argue about the shepherd, that had he been there, God would have fulfilled for him the verse that *David* invoked — "your servant has killed the lion and the bear"? Why is that argument not made on behalf of the shepherd's flock-owner?

### Shepherd & Wolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוֵית חֲזֵית לְאִיתְרְחוֹשֵׁי לָךְ נִיסָּא, הֲוָה (אִיתְרְחִישׁ) [מִיתְרְחִישׁ] לָךְ נִיסָּא כְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא דְּמֵתְיָין עִיזֵּי דּוּבֵּי בְּקַרְנַיְיהוּ. וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ: נְהִי דִּלְנִיסָּא רַבָּה לָא הֲוָה חֲזֵינָא – לְנִיסָּא זוּטָא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **because** the shepherd could **say to** the owner: **If you were worthy of a miracle occurring to you, a miracle would have** indeed **occurred to you as** it did to **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa, when** his **goats brought bears** impaled **on their horns** without any assistance on the part of a shepherd (see *Ta'anit* 25a). The Gemara asks: **And let** the owner **say to him: Granted that I was not worthy of a great miracle,** but **of a small miracle**

The *Gemara* answers: because that would require a *nisa raba* — a great miracle, like the miracle that occurred with *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa*, whose goat apparently brought a bear on its horns. We do not expect such dramatic miracles. But the counter-argument is pressed: even if we do not expect a *nisa raba*, perhaps we should expect a *nisa zuta* — a minor miracle? The *Gemara* finds this genuinely difficult: *kashya*. The argument is that if God wished to save the sheep, He could have done so whether the shepherd was present or not, and we should not hold the shepherd liable on the basis of what a small miracle might have accomplished. The tension between the metaphysical framework invoked in the earlier cases and the rejection of metaphysical reasoning here is left unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲזֵינָא? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **I was worthy.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this is **difficult.**

One also notices that the *Gemara* slips between *ze'ev* (wolf) and *dov* (bear) in narrating this case. This is not accidental: scholars have noted, following Ginzberg's commentary on the *Yerushalmi* in the final portion of Perek 1 of *Berachot*, that in Babylonian sources there is a pattern of interchange between the two, likely because *dov* in Eastern Aramaic phonology is closer in sound to *ze'ev* than one might expect, given that the consonants *dalet* and *zayin* are in fact linguistically proximate.

### Chazakah Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה זוֹרְעָהּ, וּשְׁלִישִׁית אֵינוֹ זוֹרְעָהּ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: שְׁלִישִׁית זוֹרְעָהּ, רְבִיעִית אֵינוֹ זוֹרְעָהּ. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא כְּרַבִּי, הָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in **one** *baraita*: With regard to one who cultivates a field, **the first and second time he plants** the field again if the crops were destroyed by some mishap, **but the third** time **he is not** required to **plant it** again. **And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that on the **third** occasion, he must **plant it** the field again, but after the **fourth** time the crops are destroyed, **he is not** required to **plant it** again. These two *baraitot* appear to contradict one another. The Gemara explains: That is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, whereas **that** *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

A further dispute emerges from two conflicting *beraitot* on the question of repeated planting failures. One *beraita* says that if the lessee plants once and fails, plants a second time and fails, he does not have to plant a third time — the lease obligation is already reduced. A second *beraita* says that the lessee must plant a third time before he is exempt, and only if a fourth planting would be necessary is he released from the obligation.

### Rabbi vs. R.S.b.G.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כְּרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר: בִּתְרֵי זִימְנֵי הָוְיָ[א] חֲזָקָה. הָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּאָמַר: בִּתְלָת זִימְנֵי הָוְיָ[א] חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This** *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **who says** that **presumptive status is** established **by two occasions,** while **that** *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says** that **presumptive status is** established **by three occasions.**

The resolution: *lo kashya* — there is no genuine contradiction. These two *beraitot* reflect the well-known *machloket* between *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* regarding the threshold for establishing a *chazakah*. *Rabbi* holds that a *chazakah* is established after two occurrences — *be-trei zimnei havya chazakah*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that three occurrences are required. The first *beraita*, which exempts after two failures, follows *Rabbi*. The second, which requires three before granting exemption, follows *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*.

The underlying conceptual difference between these two positions is worth noting. It is not merely a numerical disagreement. For *Rabbi*, what matters is the occurrence itself — once something has happened twice, it is established as a pattern. For *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, what must be established is the *re-occurrence*; the first instance establishes the thing, and the *chazakah* requires the re-occurrence to have happened twice. On this reading, both agree that two repetitions are needed to establish the presumption, but they disagree about whether the first instance counts as one of those repetitions or merely as the baseline. This *machloket* recurs across many tractates — *Yevamot*, *Eruvin*, *Ketubot*, *Sanhedrin*, and *Niddah* — and applies to contexts as varied as hemophilia and the laws of circumcision.

## Planting Calendar

### Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁזְּרָעָהּ וְצִמְּחָה וַאֲכָלָהּ חָגָב. אֲבָל זְרָעָהּ וְלֹא צִמְּחָה – מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּעַל הַקַּרְקַע: כׇּל יְמֵי זֶרַע זְרַעא לַהּ וֶאֱזִיל. וְעַד אֵימַת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עַד דְּאָתוּ אֲרִיסֵי מִדַּבְרָא וְקָיְימָא כִּימָה אַרֵישַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said: They taught** that a cultivator plants a limited number of times **only if he planted** the field **and it sprouted and locusts consumed** the crops, **but** if **he planted it and** the crops **did not sprout** at all, **the landowner can say to him:** You should **continue planting it** on **all the days** that are fit for **planting.** The Gemara asks: **And until when** does the period of planting last? The Gemara answers: **Rav Pappa said: Until** such time **that the sharecroppers come** in **from the field and** the stars of **Pleiades** are **stationed over their heads,** which occurs roughly during the month of *Shevat*.

*Resh Lakish* introduces yet another qualification on the *mishnah*. He says *lo shanu ela she-zara* — the *mishnah* speaks only where the lessee planted and the crop *grew*, and then a disaster struck. But if the lessee planted and the crop simply failed to grow — not from a regional blight but from a failed seeding — the owner can instruct him: *kol yemei zera zara ve-azel*, continue to plant for as long as the planting season lasts. The obligation to plant persists through the entire *yemei hazera*.

### End of Planting Season

This immediately raises the question: when does the planting season end? *Rav Pappa* answers: *ad de-atu areisei midabra be-kima* — until the workers come in from the field when the *kima* — the Pleiades star cluster — is directly overhead. *Rashi* works through the entire zodiac to establish when the Pleiades would be visible in this position. There is a *machloket* between *Rashi* and *Tosafot*: according to *Rashi*, we are speaking of sometime in the afternoon, which would place the relevant date in *Adar*; according to *Tosafot*, we are speaking of *tzet ha-kokhavim* — nightfall — when the workers return home, which would place the date earlier, in *Shevat*.

### Seasonal Divisions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר כִּדְבָרָיו: חֲצִי תִשְׁרֵי, מְרַחְשְׁוָן, וַחֲצִי כִסְלֵיו – זֶרַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita* that discusses the verse: "While the earth remains, planting and harvest, and cold and heat, and summer and winter, and day and night shall not cease" (Genesis 8:22). The *baraita* interprets this verse as referring to six seasons of the year: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Meir, and similarly, Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya would say in accordance with his statement:** The second **half of Tishrei,** all of **Marḥeshvan, and** the first **half of Kislev** are the days of **planting.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִי כִסְלֵיו, טֵבֵת, וַחֲצִי שְׁבָט – חוֹרֶף. חֲצִי שְׁבָט, אֲדָר, וַחֲצִי נִיסָן – קוֹר. חֲצִי נִיסָן, אִיָּיר, וַחֲצִי סִיוָן – קָצִיר. חֲצִי סִיוָן, תַּמּוּז, וַחֲצִי אָב – קַיִץ. חֲצִי אָב, אֱלוּל, וַחֲצִי תִּשְׁרֵי – חוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The second **half of Kislev,** all of **Tevet, and** the first **half of Shevat** are **the winter** days. The second **half of Shevat,** all of **Adar, and** the first **half of** Nisan are the period of **cold;** the second **half of Nisan,** all of **Iyar, and** the first **half of Sivan** are the **harvest** period. The second **half of Sivan,** all of **Tammuz, and half of Av** are the **summer** season, while the second **half of Av,** all of **Elul, and** the first **half of Tishrei** are the season of **heat.**

A *beraita* in the name of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* citing *Rabbi Meir*, and affirmed also by *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya*, divides the agricultural year into six two-month periods:

The second half of *Tishrei*, all of *Marcheshvan*, and the first half of *Kislev* constitute the planting season. The second half of *Kislev*, all of *Tevet*, and the first half of *Shevat* are *choref* — the cold period. The second half of *Shevat*, all of *Adar*, and the first half of *Nisan* are *kor* — the bitter cold. The second half of *Nisan*, all of *Iyar*, and the first half of *Sivan* are *katzir* — the harvest. The second half of *Sivan*, all of *Tammuz*, and the first half of *Av* are *kayitz* — summer. And the second half of *Av*, all of *Elul*, and the first half of *Tishrei* are the hot season.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹנֶה מִתִּשְׁרִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹנֶה מִמְּרַחְשְׁוָן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds: **Rabbi Yehuda** also would divide the year into these six seasons, but he **counts from** the beginning of **Tishrei** rather than from the middle. **Rabbi Shimon counts from Marḥeshvan,** so that Marḥeshvan and Kislev constitute the season of planting, and so on.

*Rabbi Yehuda* counts from the beginning of *Tishrei*; *Rabbi Shimon* counts from *Marcheshvan*. The question of who allows the most time for planting: *Rabbi Shimon* is most expansive. Yet *Rav Pappa*'s answer — extending planting into *Shevat* or even *Adar* — goes further than even *Rabbi Shimon*'s outer limit, presenting an apparent conflict.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן מֵיקֵל בְּכוּלְּהוּ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְכוּלֵּי הַאי לָא קָאָמַר! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּקַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ בְּחָרְפֵי, הָא דְּקַבְּלֵהּ מִינֵּיהּ בְּאַפְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its objection: **Who** is the most **lenient of all of them** in that the period of planting occurs at the latest time of the year? It is **Rabbi Shimon, and** even he **did not say** that the planting season extends **that far** to the time when Pleiades is above their heads. The Gemara responds: That is **not difficult,** as **this** is referring to a cultivator **who accepted from** the owner the planting **of early** crops, while **that** case involves a cultivator **who accepted from** the owner the planting **of late** crops, performed at a much later date.

The resolution: *lo kashya ha de-kabel minei de-charfei ha de-kabel minei de-aflei* — there is no contradiction because they are discussing different types of crops. *Rav Pappa* refers to *aflei*, late-planted crops, which have a longer planting window extending into *Shevat* or *Adar*. The *beraita*'s earlier dates refer to *charfei*, early-planted crops. Different agricultural calendars apply to different crops.

## Quality & Contract

### Poor Quality Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בַּעֲשָׂרָה כּוֹר חִטִּים לְשָׁנָה וְלָקְתָה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ מִתּוֹכָהּ. הָיוּ חִטֶּיהָ יָפוֹת, לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵינִי לוֹקֵחַ מִן הַשּׁוּק, אֶלָּא נוֹתֵן לוֹ מִתּוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who receives a field from another** to cultivate in return **for** the payment of **ten *kor* of wheat per year, and** its produce **was blighted** by a crop disease or the like, the cultivator **gives** the owner the ten *kor* of wheat **from it** but does not have to provide him with high quality wheat. If the wheat stalks produced by the field **were** particularly **good** stalks of **wheat,** the cultivator **may not say to** the owner: **I will buy** regular wheat **from the market; rather,** he **gives him from inside** the field itself.

The *Gemara* then addresses a *guf* from the *mishnah* (*Matnitin*): if someone leases a field with an obligation to pay ten *kor* of wheat per year, and the wheat produced is of low quality, he may pay the owner from that low-quality wheat — he need not purchase standard-quality wheat from the market. However, if the wheat produced was especially fine, he may not say "I will buy wheat from the market and pay you standard quality," pocketing the profit from the superior grain. He must pay the owner from the actual produce of the field.

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s position from the *mishnah* is revisited here: if the lessee contracted to pay in coin, no reduction applies regardless of circumstances. *Rava* had ruled in a practical case — involving a field on the banks of *Nahar Malka* that flooded — that the flood constituted a *makat medina* and the lessee's payment should be reduced, even though the payment was in coin (*bezuzi*). The *Rabbanan* challenged *Rava*: the *mishnah* records *Rabbi Yehuda* as saying that a coin-payment lease is never reduced. *Rava* dismissed this: *leit de-chash la le-d'Rabbi Yehuda* — nobody follows *Rabbi Yehuda* on this point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַבֵּיל אַרְעָא לְמִיזְרַעה בַּהּו תּוּמֵי אַגּוּדָּא דִּנְהַר מַלְכָּא סָבָא בְּזוּזֵי. אִיסְתְּכַר נְהַר מַלְכָּא סָבָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְהַר מַלְכָּא סָבָא לָא עֲבִיד דְּמִיסְתְּכַר, מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא, זִיל נַכֵּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda says: If** the cultivator **received it from** the owner **for** a fixed sum of **money,** whether a regional disaster occurred or not, he does not subtract the produce he owes as part of his tenancy. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who received land to plant garlic on it on the bank of the river Malka Sava** in exchange **for** a specified sum of **money.** The bank of **the river Malka Sava became dammed up.** The case **came before Rava,** who **said to** the cultivator: **The river Malka Sava does not usually dam up.** Therefore, **it is** classified as **a regional disaster; go subtract** this loss from the payment you owe **to** the owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: הָא אֲנַן תְּנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם קִבְּלָה הֵימֶנּוּ בְּמָעוֹת – בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אֵינוֹ מְנַכָּה לוֹ מִן חֲכוֹרוֹ! אֲמַר לְהוּ: לֵית דְּחָשׁ לַהּ לִדְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the story: **The Rabbis said to Rava: Didn't we learn** in the mishna here: **If** the cultivator **received it from** the owner **for** a fixed sum of **money, whether this** way or **whether that** way, i.e., whether a regional disaster occurred or not, **he does not subtract** the produce he owes as part of **his tenancy.** Rava **said to them: There is no** one **who is concerned for** the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda** since it is a minority opinion that is rejected.

### Fodder to Barley

A practical case: a man leased land to plant *aspasta* (animal fodder), with the lease payment denominated in barley. He harvested the fodder, plowed again, and planted barley. His barley crop was then damaged. He claimed that since his payment obligation was in barley, and the barley was harmed, he should be able to pay in low-quality barley under the *mishnah*'s rule.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַבֵּל אַרְעָא לְאַסְפַּסְתָּא בְּכוֹרֵי דִשְׂעָרֵי, עֲבַדָא אַסְפַּסְתָּא, וְחַרְשַׁהּ וְזַרְעַהּ שְׂעָרֵי, וּלְקוֹ הָנֵי שְׂעָרֵי. שַׁלְחַהּ רַב חֲבִיבָא מִסּוּרָא דִפְרָת לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, כִּי הַאי גַּוְנָא מַאי: כִּי ״לָקְתָה נוֹתֵן לוֹ מִתּוֹכָהּ״ דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who received land to** grow **hay** in exchange **for** paying the owner several ***kor* of barley.** After the field **produced hay,** the recipient **plowed and sowed it** with **barley, and that barley was blighted.** The worker sought to pay the owner from the damaged barley he had cultivated. **Rav Ḥaviva from Sura in the Euphrates sent** the following question **before Ravina: What** is the *halakha* with regard to **a case of this kind?** Is it **considered similar to** an instance of: If it **was blighted, he gives him from inside** the field, **or not?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא עֲבַדָא אַרְעָא שְׁלִיחוּתָא דְּמָרַהּ, הָכָא עֲבַדָא אַרְעָא שְׁלִיחוּתָא דְּמָרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: Is it comparable? There,** in the mishna, **the land did not perform its owner's mission,** and the cultivator also received blighted produce, whereas **here, the land did perform its owner's mission,** as the cultivator took the land for the purpose of growing hay, which it produced. His additional crop of barley was not part of their agreement and therefore he cannot pay his debt with blighted barley.

*Rav Chaviva* of *Sura* on the Euphrates sent an inquiry to *Ravina* on this question. *Ravina*'s answer: this case is not comparable to the *mishnah*. In the *mishnah*'s case, *lo avda ara shelichuta d'marei* — the land did not do what its master directed. The land failed in its assigned task and so the lessee need only pay from what it produced. Here, however, *avda ara shelichuta d'marei* — the land did precisely what it was instructed to do. It grew the *aspasta* as directed. The lessee then chose to plow and plant barley on his own initiative. Since the land fulfilled its mandate, the lessee must purchase standard barley from the market to satisfy his payment obligation.

### Spoiled Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַבֵּל פַּרְדֵּס מֵחַבְרֵיהּ בַּעֲשַׂר דַּנֵּי חַמְרָא, תְּקֵיף הָהוּא חַמְרָא. סְבַר רַב כָּהֲנָא לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ מַתְנִיתִין – לָקְתָה נוֹתֵן לוֹ מִתּוֹכָהּ. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא עֲבַדָא אַרְעָא שְׁלִיחוּתָא, הָכָא עֲבַדָא אַרְעָא שְׁלִיחוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who received an orchard from another** to cultivate in exchange **for** paying the owner **ten barrels of wine,** but **that wine** produced from the orchard's grapes **turned sour. Rav Kahana thought to say** that **this is** an example of the ruling of **the mishna** that if it **was blighted he** may **give him from inside** the field. **Rav Ashi said to him: Is it comparable? There,** in the mishna, **the land did not perform** its owner's **mission,** as the crop was blighted, whereas **here, the land** did **perform** its owner's **mission,** as there was nothing wrong with the grapes themselves, and the wine turned sour in the cultivator's possession.

A parallel case: a man leased a vineyard (*pardesa d'kerem*) with an obligation to pay ten jugs of wine. The wine that the vineyard produced spoiled. *Rav Kahana* thought this was analogous to the *mishnah*: since the produce was of poor quality, the lessee could pay in that poor-quality wine. *Rav Ashi* disagreed: *mi damei? hatam lo avda ara shelichuta* — in the *mishnah*, the land failed to do what was expected. Here the vineyard did exactly what it was supposed to do: it grew grapes and produced wine. That the wine subsequently spoiled is not a failure of the land's performance. Since the land fulfilled its role — *avda ara shelichuta* — the lessee must go to the market and purchase replacement wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַב אָשֵׁי בְּעִינְּבֵי דִּכְדוּם וּבְשָׂדֶה שֶׁלָּקְתָה בָּעוֹמָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rav Ashi concedes with regard to grapes that shrunk** over the course of their growth **and with regard to a field whose sheaves were blighted** that since the damage occurred to the crop itself, the cultivator can pay his debt from the produce of the field.

*Rav Ashi* himself concedes that his rule does not apply universally. He agrees that the *mishnah*'s principle would govern cases involving wormy grapes (*invei de-kadumi*) or a field where the produce was blighted after being stood up for drying. In those cases, the land can fairly be said to have not fully performed its role, and the lessee pays from what was produced.

## Crop Change Rules

### Barley/Wheat Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְזׇרְעָהּ שְׂעוֹרִים – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה חִטִּים. חִטִּים – יִזְרָעֶנָּה שְׂעוֹרִים, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹסֵר. תְּבוּאָה – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה קִטְנִית. קִטְנִית – יִזְרָעֶנָּה תְּבוּאָה, וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who receives a field from another** in order **to plant it** with **barley,** he may **not plant it** with **wheat,** as wheat weakens the field more than barley does. But if he receives it in order to plant **wheat,** he may **plant it** with **barley** if he wishes, but **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids** it. Similarly, if he receives it to plant **it with grain** he may **not plant it** with **legumes,** as they weaken the field more than grains do, but if he receives it in order to plant **legumes** he may **plant it** with **grain, but Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids** it.

The next *mishnah* addresses what crops a lessee may plant when the lease specifies a particular grain. If the lease specifies that the lessee is to plant barley, he may not plant wheat instead. But if the lease specifies wheat, the lessee may plant barley, because wheat is harder on the soil — it depletes the land more than barley does. Planting barley when wheat was contracted therefore causes the landowner no harm and may even benefit the land. The lessee is permitted to perform an act that is less damaging than what he contracted to perform. This principle illuminates the earlier case in which a lessee contracted to plant wheat but planted barley: the substitution was technically permissible, but as the *Gemara* established, if a *makat medina* then struck, the lessee could not invoke the owner's contractual expectation of wheat to claim protection — his deviation from the contract, even if allowed, voided his ability to make certain claims about divine providence.

### R.S.b.G. Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁאֵרִית יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יַעֲשׂוּ עַוְלָה וְלֹא יְדַבְּרוּ כָזָב וְלֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּפִיהֶם לְשׁוֹן תַּרְמִית״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reason of** the ruling of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel?** The landowner has apparently suffered no loss from the cultivator's actions. His reasoning is **as it is written: "The remnant of Israel shall not do iniquity, nor speak lies, neither shall a deceitful tongue be found in their mouth"** (Zephaniah 3:13). In other words, one may not retract from an obligation accepted upon oneself, even if no one suffers as a result.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* takes a stricter position. He rules that if the lessee contracted to plant *tevuah* — cereal grain — he may not switch to *kitnit* — legumes — even though legumes are generally considered less taxing on the soil than cereals. But if the lease specified legumes, the lessee may switch to cereals. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* prohibits the cereal-to-legume substitution despite the apparent benefit to the soil because the categories of *tevuah* and *kitnit* are sufficiently distinct that a switch between them represents a meaningful departure from the contracted arrangement, not merely a permissible downgrade within the same category.
