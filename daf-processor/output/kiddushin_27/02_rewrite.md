# Kiddushin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyan Agav Mechanics

### Agav Basics

The *daf* opens in the middle of a discussion carried over from 26b concerning *kinyan agav* — the mechanism by which one who acquires land simultaneously acquires movable property (*metaltelin*) through that act of acquisition. The animating question is whether the *metaltelin* must be physically present on the land itself (*tziburin betocha*) or whether the conceptual bundling of the two objects suffices even when they are elsewhere.

The logic behind requiring physical proximity is that the land serves as the *ikar* — the primary, weightier element — and the *metaltelin* are subordinate to it. Acquiring the land represents acquiring the whole package. The idea that *metaltelin* located off the property can be drawn along requires more explanation, since the physical unity that might seem to ground the mechanism is absent.

One might ask: if the *metaltelin* are physically on the land, why is *kinyan agav* even needed? The answer is that *kinyan chatzer* — acquiring objects by virtue of their presence within one's property — does not operate in all circumstances. In particular, a courtyard that is not *mishtameres* (guarded or protected) cannot effect *kinyan chatzer*. The *kinyan agav* mechanism thus provides an alternative route. With that background established, the *Gemara* proceeds to test the *tziburin betocha* requirement through a series of cases.

### Rabban Gamliel Case

The *Gemara* (bottom of 26b) introduces a famous episode involving Rabban Gamliel and the elders who were traveling together by ship. Rabban Gamliel said to the elders: the *maaser* which I will eventually separate from my grain — currently in my granary — is hereby given to Yehoshua (Rabbi Yehoshua, who was a *Levi*), and its place is rented to him (*umkomo muskar lo*). In this way, by renting the space to Yehoshua, Rabban Gamliel transferred ownership of the *maaser* through a rental — that is, through *sechirut* — of the physical location where the grain sat.

The second clause of the episode is equally striking: another *maaser* that I will eventually separate — this one from *maaser ani*, the poor tithe distributed in the third year — is given to Rabbi Akiva, so that he (*kedei sheyizkeh bo l'aniyim*) may take possession of it on behalf of the poor, for Rabbi Akiva served as a *gabbai l'aniyim*, a communal charity trustee. And again: *umkomo muskar lo*, its place is rented to him.

The *Gemara* presupposes that both transfers operate through *kinyan agav* rather than *kinyan chatzer*, even though the *Gemara* does not spell out why *kinyan chatzer* is excluded. The significant word in both clauses is *mekomo* — "its place" — which seems to imply that the *metaltelin* must be precisely on the rented property for the *kinyan* to take effect. The *Gemara* therefore raises: *shema mina deba'inan tziburin bah* — this appears to prove that physical presence on the land is required.

The *Gemara* deflects this inference: *shani hatam ki heichi delo letarchinhu* — the reason Rabban Gamliel specified the very location where the grain already sat was not technical necessity but practical consideration. By renting them that specific space, the recipients would have the right to leave the grain there indefinitely and would feel no pressure to remove it. Had he rented them a different parcel, they might feel compelled to relocate the grain. So specifying *mekomo* was a courtesy, not a legal requirement, and the case does not constitute proof that *tziburin betocha* is required.

A striking feature of this passage, however, is what the *Gemara's* answer implicitly concedes: that *kinyan agav* functions even through *sechirut* — through rental. This is itself a major *chiddush*. One might have argued that *kinyan agav* requires full ownership of the land, since it is through the acquisition of land that the *metaltelin* are swept along. A rental is not full ownership. The *Rambam* describes *sechirut* in one place as *kinyan peiros l'guf l'zman* — I own the property for its use for a limited period — but this is a qualified form of ownership at best. To say that such a limited acquisition of land is sufficient to effect full *kinyan agav* on other property, on *metaltelin*, is conceptually bold. The more natural answer would have been: this case involves *kinyan chatzer*, not *kinyan agav*, and *kinyan chatzer* naturally requires the objects to be on the rented premises. The *Gemara* declines that path entirely — and by doing so, commits to the remarkable position that *sechirut* suffices for *kinyan agav*.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Rashi* explains the background of the episode: Rabban Gamliel was a *chaver*, and *chaverim* were scrupulous about not leaving grain in an untithed (*tevel*) state, lest family members eat from it. The pressure to act, therefore, arose from this concern.

*Tosafot* raises several sharp objections to this reading. First: how did Rabban Gamliel find himself in this situation to begin with? A *chaver*, by definition, does not leave his produce untithed. If the grain was *tevel*, he should not have departed without first separating *terumot uma'asrot*. As the saying goes, a wise man avoids situations from which a smart man must extricate himself.

Second: Rabban Gamliel never says he is separating *terumot uma'asrot* at this moment. He says, "the *maaser* that I will eventually take (*she'ani atid lamod*)." The act of *hafrashat terumot uma'asrot* requires a declaration designating a specific portion — ten percent — as *maaser*, and must identify its location within the granary. None of that is present here. No tithing has actually occurred.

Third: if he had already separated the *terumot uma'asrot*, what urgency is there in transferring it to Yehoshua or Akiva? He could simply give it to them upon his return.

*Tosafot* therefore reconstructs the case differently: Rabban Gamliel had informed his household that the grain was *tevel* and they should not touch it. There was accordingly no concern about accidental consumption. The reason for the urgency was not *terumot uma'asrot* per se but *biur* — the obligation that obtains at the end of the third year of the *shemittah* cycle requiring one to remove and distribute *ma'asrot* that have not yet been transferred to their recipients. The point of renting the space and transferring it was therefore to fulfill his *biur* obligation by getting the *maaser ani* out of his possession and into the domain of the appropriate parties.

*Tosafot* acknowledges that this explanation is not entirely without difficulty — for how can there be an obligation of *biur* with respect to grain that is still technically *tevel*? And there are further questions about *terumah* and whether *terumah* is subject to *biur* at all. Nevertheless, *Tosafot*'s central insight is that the episode is about satisfying *biur*, not about the *hafrashah* itself.

### Shetar Categories

The *Gemara* pivots to introduce a teaching of Rav Abba bar Yitzchak citing Rav: there are two categories of documents (*shenei shtarot hen*) associated with land transactions, distinguished by whether the *shtar* is integral to effecting the sale or subordinate to it.

The first scenario: I instruct witnesses — *zekhu bisedeh zo l'Ploni vekhitvu lo et hashetar* — take possession of this field for Michael and write him out a bill of sale. Here two instructions are given: effect the *kinyan* (presumably through *kinyan sudar*) and then write the document. Since the *kinyan* is immediate and the writing of the *shtar* takes time, I may retract my authorization to write the *shtar* even after the *kinyan sudar* has been executed — *chozer bashetar ve'eino chozer bisedeh* — the sale itself stands but I may revoke the document. *Tosafot* asks what legitimate reason one might have for revoking the *shtar* after the fact. The answer offered is that publicizing the sale of one's property signals financial distress and can invite exploitation from creditors and business partners. This is a plausible consideration, even if the underlying motive is obvious.

The second scenario: I instruct them *al menat shetikhtevu lo et hashetar* — on the condition that you write the *shtar*. In this case, revoking the authorization for the *shtar* unravels the *kinyan* itself — *chozer bein bashetar bein bisedeh* — because the *kinyan sudar* was conditioned from the outset on the writing of the document.

### Rav Huna Ruling

Rav Huna extends the taxonomy to three categories (*shalosh shtarot hen*). The third case: the seller goes to witnesses in advance and says, write me a bill of sale for my field — *kotvin shtar lamocher af al pi she'ein loke'ach imo* — the witnesses may write such a document even without the buyer present, since the seller alone authorizes it. This pre-written *shtar* is then held by the seller, who will hand it to the buyer upon payment.

The operative ruling: *keivan shehechzik imo bekarka niknah shtar bechol makom shehu* — once the buyer performs *chazakah* on the land, he simultaneously acquires the pre-written *shtar* wherever that document may physically be located, even if it is nowhere near the property.

The *Gemara* notes: *shema mina lo ba'inan tziburin bah* — surely this proves that *tziburin betocha* is not required, since the *shtar* is acquired through *agav* even when not physically present on the land. The *Gemara* responds: *shani shtar de'asitra de'ara hu* — a bill of sale is categorically different because it is, conceptually, a handle (*asitra*) of the land itself. The deed of sale is so inherently bound up with the property it conveys that it constitutes an extension of that land. It is as if it is always "on" the land in a conceptual sense, and so this case does not require physical presence because the *shtar* is never truly separate from its land.

Moreover, when a *braita* quotes this ruling as an illustration of the general principle — *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut niknim im nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut* — it becomes clear that this is being treated not as a sui generis case but as a standard instance of *kinyan agav*. From this the *Gemara* draws its final conclusion: *shema mina lo ba'inan tziburin bah* — you do not need the objects to be piled upon the land for *kinyan agav* to operate.

## Agav Word Requirement

### Bill of Sale Proof

The preceding ruling about the pre-written *shtar* was identified by Rav Chiya bar Avin as an application of *kinyan agav*. Once the *Gemara* establishes that the *braita* treating it as *agav* is what Rav Chiya bar Avin himself invoked, this becomes the source of the *shema mina* conclusion — that *agav* does not require *tziburin betocha*.

### Agav Terminology

With the physical proximity question resolved, the *Gemara* turns to a new *iba'ya*: is the word *agav* itself technically required? When effecting a *kinyan agav*, must the seller explicitly say "you are acquiring the *metaltelin agav* — by means of / on the back of — this land," or is some other formulation sufficient?

The etymological aside is worth noting: *agav* derives from *gav*, meaning "back." The word thus conjures the image of the *metaltelin* riding on the back of the land — a conceptual picture of them being virtually present on the property even when physically absent. The question then is whether this very word, which performs that bundling function, must be used, or whether an equivalent expression will do.

### Immo vs Agav

The *Gemara* brings a proof: the cases discussed earlier — in which a *tefach al tefach* of land is transferred together with a hundred sheep — used the formulation *ve-imo me'ah tzon*, "and with it, a hundred sheep," rather than the word *agav*. Does this not prove that the word *agav* is unnecessary?

The *Gemara* deflects: *ve-li-te'amecha kni mi katani* — by the same reasoning, those formulations also do not use the word *kni* (take possession), yet obviously some verb of acquisition is required. The absence of *kni* in the example does not mean *kni* is unnecessary; it means the example is paraphrasing rather than reproducing technical language. The same applies to *agav*: the formulation used in the example was a paraphrase, and both *agav* and *kni* are in fact technically required.

### Technical Language

The *halakha* is therefore stated: *tziburin lo ba'inan, agav u'kni ba'inan* — physical presence of the *metaltelin* on the land is not required, but the technical terms *agav* and *kni* are required.

Looking at *Rashi*, he illustrates the *kni* requirement with the formulation *zecheh b'tefach al tefach l'Ploni* — "take possession of this *tefach* for Ploni." Note that *Rashi* uses the verb *zecheh* rather than the word *kni* itself, which suggests that what is required is a verb of acquisition, not specifically the word *kni*. The defect in the examples was the absence of any verb — *l'Ploni* ("this is for Ploni") lacks the required authorization. Any verb of taking possession, whether *kni* or *zecheh*, presumably satisfies the requirement.

The status of the word *agav* is more rigidly technical. The *Gemara* explicitly contrasts *agav* with *immo* and treats them as non-equivalent. The word *immo* ("with it") does not perform the bundling function that *agav* does. Whether *agav* is required even when the *metaltelin* are physically present on the land — where the physical connection itself might substitute for the verbal bundling — is a *machloket Rishonim*, though conceptually the requirement is most pressing when the objects are not on the property and the word alone must bridge the gap.

## Separate Acquisitions

### Mixed Transfers

The *Gemara* raises a further *iba'ya*: *sadeh b'mecher u'mitaltelin b'matanah* — may one sell the land and simultaneously give the *metaltelin* as a gift, effecting both in a single act? This question touches on how to conceptualize the mechanism of *agav*. If *agav* fuses the two objects into a single unit — land and *metaltelin* as one package — then it would be incoherent to treat them differently: how can one part of a single object be sold and another part given as a gift? But if *agav* is understood as a single *kinyan* that operates separately on two distinct objects, then it becomes entirely plausible that the two objects can be transferred through different legal modalities simultaneously.

### Maaser Proof

The *Gemara* brings a *ta shma* from the Rabban Gamliel case. In that episode, Rabban Gamliel rented the space — a form of *mecher*, a limited sale — while giving the *ma'aser* itself as something closer to a *matanah*. Since the *Gemara* has already established that the episode operates through *agav*, this constitutes proof that the land-acquisition (*sechirut* as a form of *mecher*) and the *metaltelin* transfer (the *ma'aser* as *matanah*) can be of different legal characters within a single *agav* transaction. This is consistent with conceptualizing *agav* as one *kinyan* effecting two discrete transfers, rather than transforming the two objects into one fused unit.

### Third-Party Transfer

A sharper question follows: *sadeh l'echad u'mitaltelin l'achar mahu* — may one effect a *kinyan agav* in which the land goes to one person and the *metaltelin* go to a different person entirely? This is conceptually more vexing than the mixed-transfer case. Even if *agav* involves two distinct transfers, they ordinarily both run in the same direction — from seller to buyer. Here, the *kinyan* on the land runs to person A, yet the transfer of the *metaltelin* is meant to benefit person B. Can a single act of *kinyan* simultaneously take something into my possession and take something else into someone else's possession?

### Akiva's Agency

The *Gemara* brings another *ta shma* from the Rabban Gamliel episode: Rabbi Akiva rented the space (for himself, as the party effecting the *kinyan*) while the *ma'aser ani* was given to the *aniyim* — *kedei sheyizkeh bo l'aniyim*. Does this not show that the *kinyan* of the land ran to Akiva while the *metaltelin* ran to the poor?

The *Gemara* responds with two answers. First: *mushkar l'ma'aser* — the rental itself was entirely for the sake of the *maaser*, meaning that Akiva was taking possession of the space on behalf of the *aniyim* as well, so both the space and the *metaltelin* were being acquired for the same beneficiaries. Second: *shani Rabbi Akiva d'yad aniyim hava* — Rabbi Akiva was effectively the "hand" of the poor, their authorized representative, so anything he acquired was intrinsically for them.

Both answers converge on the same point: this is not a true case of splitting the *agav* between two different parties. In one formulation, both elements were being transferred to the same beneficiary (the *aniyim*); in the other, Akiva and the *aniyim* were functionally one and the same agent-and-beneficiary. The underlying question — whether one can truly bifurcate a *kinyan agav* between two distinct parties — is left unresolved by the *Gemara*, along with the deeper theoretical question of what *zechiyah* is: does taking possession on behalf of another operate by the acquirer momentarily taking it in and then transferring it outward, or does it operate by the acquirer directly representing the beneficiary from the outset? The latter model makes it much harder to understand how one act could run to two different parties.

## Payment for Agav

### Rava's Payment Rule

Rava states: *lo shanu ela shenatan d'mei kulhan* — the rule that *agav* transfers *metaltelin* applies only if the buyer has already paid for all of the *metaltelin* in question. If he has paid for only some of them, *lo kanah ella kineged ma'otav* — he acquires only those he has paid for.

This ruling may initially seem self-evident: of course one only owns what one has paid for. But in fact it is not trivial. *Metaltelin* can normally be acquired through *meshichah* — physically drawing the object toward oneself — without any prior payment. If *agav* is simply a valid mode of *kinyan* on *metaltelin*, why should payment be a prerequisite here but not elsewhere?

The answer lies in understanding *kinyan agav* as an inherently weak mechanism. When one acquires *metaltelin*, normally one performs a *kinyan* directly on the *metaltelin* themselves — one touches them, pulls them, or takes them into one's domain. *Agav* performs no direct act whatsoever on the *metaltelin*. Everything is done to the land, and the *metaltelin* are swept along conceptually. This conceptual sweep is potent enough to complete a transaction only when nothing remains outstanding. If the money has already been paid, the transfer is fully prepared and only needs this final act of formalization; the *agav* can supply that finalization. But if the money has not been paid, there is an unresolved gap in the transaction, and the weak mechanism of *agav* cannot bridge it.

### Kinyan Strengths

A *braita* is cited in support of Rava: *yafeh koach kesef mikoach shtar vekoach shtar mikoach kesef* — *kesef* is stronger than *shtar* in certain respects, and *shtar* is stronger than *kesef* in others.

*Kesef* is stronger than *shtar*: money can be used to redeem *hekdeshot* and *maaser sheni*, transferring their *kedushah* — objects dedicated to the *Beis HaMikdash* or *ma'aser sheni* can have their sanctity transferred onto coins. A *shtar* cannot do this, because a *shtar* lacks *gufo mamon* — intrinsic monetary value.

*Shtar* is stronger than *kesef*: a *get* — a bill of divorce — can sever the marital bond, *she'shtar motzi b'vanot Yisrael*. Money cannot serve as a medium of divorce.

Both *kesef* and *shtar* are stronger than *chazakah*: *shenihem konim b'eved Ivri* — money and a document can effect the acquisition of a *eved Ivri*, a Hebrew bondsman. *Chazakah* cannot, because *chazakah* most fully constitutes the *kinyan* that makes something "one's own" in the physical and existential sense — and this character sits uneasily with the acquisition of a person.

*Chazakah* is stronger than *kesef* and *shtar* in the respect now relevant to our discussion, addressed in the next subsection.

### Scope of Payment

The *braita* concludes: *she'hachazakah machar lo eser sados b'eser medinos, keivan she'hechezik b'achat mehen kanah kulam* — if one sells ten fields in ten different countries, once the buyer performs *chazakah* on one of them, he acquires all of them.

The *braita* then adds: *bameh devarim amurim? She'nosan lo d'mei kulan* — when does this hold? When he has paid for all of them. *Aval lo nosan lo d'mei kulan, lo kanah ella kineged ma'otav* — if not, he acquires only those for which he has paid. This confirms Rava's ruling and the underlying logic: *kinyan agav* — or in this case, a parallel mechanism by which one *chazakah* effects *kinyan* on multiple parcels — functions only where payment has already been completed, because the mechanism is insufficiently direct to overcome outstanding obligations.

## Land to Land Agav

### Shmuel's Ruling

The *braita* itself supports a statement of Shmuel: *machar lo eser sados b'eser medinos, keivan she'hechezik b'achat mehen kanah kulam* — if one sells ten fields in ten different countries, a single act of *chazakah* on one acquires all of them. This is itself a major *chiddush*, distinct from anything previously established about *agav*.

The principle of *kinyan agav* as discussed until now involved land as the *ikar* and *metaltelin* as the secondary element swept along. The mechanism was grounded in the primacy of land: *metaltelin* are subordinate and can be conceptually placed "on" the land even when physically elsewhere. But the case of ten fields in ten countries involves equal-weight objects — land and land. There is no *ikar* and *tafel* relationship. How can a *chazakah* on one plot of land draw distant plots of land along with it?

### Braita Support

The *braita* cited in support of Rava confirms Shmuel's ruling while appending the payment requirement. That the *braita* frames this as the source of the payment condition also implies it treats the ten-field case as an established rule. The *chiddush* is therefore: not only can *agav* (or an analogous mechanism) transfer *metaltelin* along with land, but one can acquire multiple distant parcels of land through a single act of *chazakah* on one of them.

### Rav Acha Analogy

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika attempts to provide an independent proof that such a mechanism should work. He reasons: if I have ten animals tied on a single rope and I perform *meshichah* on one — taking it in my direction — do I not acquire all of them? Surely yes. So too, by analogy, performing *chazakah* on one plot should draw the others along.

### Connection Analysis

The *Gemara* immediately rejects this analogy in its initial formulation: *hatam aguda beyado* — in the animal case, the rope is in one's hand. One is physically holding a cord that is directly and continuously connected to all ten animals. Taking possession of the rope is in some meaningful sense taking possession of all that is attached to it. By contrast, ten fields in ten countries are not connected by any rope. There is no physical chain running from one to the other. *Hacha ein aguda beyado* — here, there is no rope in one's hand.

The analogy is then inverted — *ika de-amrei*, a second version of the exchange is reported, in which Rav Acha uses the same animal case as a proof against Shmuel. If I have ten animals on one rope but specify only the last animal — *zu kni* — do you acquire the others? Of course not; you specified only one. So too, specifying *chazakah* on one field should not confer the others. The *Gemara* responds to this version as well.

### Land as Sadna

The *Gemara's* response to the second version distinguishes land from animals in the opposite direction: *hatam gufim mechulakin* — animals are separate physical bodies, and specifying one excludes the others. *Hacha sadna de-ara chad hu* — here, the "anvil" of the land is one. *Rashi* identifies *sadna* as the anvil on which a blacksmith strikes — the broad, flat, unified surface. Land, whatever its legal subdivisions, is physically continuous. All land is connected through the expanse of the earth itself. Therefore, doing *chazakah* on one plot reaches through the physical continuity of the earth to the other plots as well. The seller has designated all of them for transfer; the physical unity of land provides the conceptual chain that the rope provided for the animals.

One might ask: if all land on earth is physically connected, why would only the seller's other fields be included and not everyone else's fields? The answer, implicit in the discussion, is that the *kinyan* reaches only those parcels that have been designated — but the mechanism by which a single *chazakah* can reach distant designated parcels is the physical unity of the earth's surface.

### Chiddushim Summary

The discussion of *kinyan agav* yields a remarkable collection of *chiddushim*, each one deserving attention in its own right.

The first *chiddush* is that *sechirut* — rental — suffices for *kinyan agav*. Acquiring a space through rental, a limited and qualified form of ownership, is sufficient to draw other property along as *agav*. The *Gemara* did not take the simpler path of classifying Rabban Gamliel's case as *kinyan chatzer* and instead endorsed the broader principle that even a *kinyan* through rental can be the vehicle for *agav*.

The second *chiddush* is that *tziburin betocha* is not required — the *metaltelin* need not be physically on the land — but the technical formulation of *agav* and *kni* must be used. Whether this verbal requirement applies only when the objects are not on the land or even when they are is debated among the *Rishonim*.

The third *chiddush* is that the two components of an *agav* transaction may be of different legal character: one may sell the land and give the *metaltelin* as a gift, demonstrating that the two objects are not fused into one unit but remain conceptually distinct, even as a single *kinyan* operates on both.

The fourth *chiddush* is Rava's ruling that *agav* requires prior payment for the *metaltelin* in question, reflecting the inherent weakness of a *kinyan* that touches the transferred object only indirectly.

The fifth and perhaps most conceptually surprising *chiddush* is Shmuel's ruling that a single *chazakah* on one parcel can transfer multiple parcels of land, grounded in the principle that *sadna de-ara chad hu* — all land is one unified expanse, and *chazakah* on one point of that expanse reaches the whole.

## Gilgul Shevua

### Gilgul Principle

Having concluded the extended analysis of *kinyan agav* — by which land draws *metaltelin* along with it in an acquisition — the *Gemara* introduces the converse phenomenon: *metaltelin* drawing along a *shevuah* obligation on *karka*. The formal rule is that one does not make a *shevuah* on *karka* — land is categorically excluded from the *shevuah* mechanism in monetary disputes. Yet if a *shevuah* is properly triggered regarding *metaltelin*, it can be "rolled" to include *karka* as well. This is *gilgul shevuah* — the rolling of an oath. The word *gilgul* (cognate with the word for reincarnation, in which a soul rolls and settles into a new vessel) here means the extension of an oath originally anchored on one matter to encompass additional matters that could not independently have triggered a *shevuah*.

### Sotah Source

Ulla asks: *minayin le-gilgul shevuah min ha-Torah* — where is the Scriptural basis for *gilgul shevuah*? His answer: from the *sotah* passage. The woman accused of *sotah* responds *Amein Amein* — a double affirmation. The *Gemara* (citing the Mishnah) unpacks the double *amein*: *Amein al ha-alah, Amein al ha-shevuah* — one *amein* accepts the curse (*alah*) and one accepts the oath (*shevuah*). This double acceptance is itself already an expansion: she accepts consequences on multiple levels from a single proceeding.

### Oath Expansion

But the expansion goes further. The double *amein* also addresses *amein im me-ish zeh ve-Amein im me-ish acher* — one oath regarding the specific man about whom the warning (*kinuy*) and seclusion (*stiiah*) occurred, and another regarding any other man. The *sotah* procedure is triggered by a particular warning and particular seclusion; the oath naturally covers that specific case. The fact that it can be extended to cover other men she may have been suspected with is an application of *gilgul shevuah*: once the *shevuah* is in motion, it rolls to include additional matters.

The *Gemara* continues: *amein she-lo satisi arusah u-nesuah ve-shomeres yavam u-kenuah* — the oath can also be extended to cover conduct during different stages of the woman's marital history: as an *arusah* (betrothed but not yet fully married), as a *nesuah* (fully married), as a *shomeres yavam* (a widow awaiting *yibum*), and as a *kenuah* (after *yibum* has been performed).

### Arusah Case

The *Gemara* homes in on the *arusah* case as the critical proof of *gilgul shevuah's* reach. The question is posed: *hai arusah heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario in which the woman must swear about what she did as an *arusah*?

If the *kinuy* (warning) occurred while she was an *arusah* and the *stirah* (seclusion) occurred while she was an *arusah*, this is a case entirely during *erusin*. But the Mishnah rules explicitly — *arusah ve-shomeret yavam lo shotot ve-lo notlot kesubah* — that an *arusah* and a *shomeret yavam* do not drink the *sotah* waters. They are categorically excluded.

### Tachad Ishech

The reason for this exclusion is derived from the verse: *tachas ishech* — "under your husband." The *sotah* laws apply only to a woman who has fully strayed from under her husband — that is, a woman in full *nisu'in*. An *arusah*, though betrothed, has not yet entered the husband's home in the full sense of *tachas ish*, and therefore the *sotah* procedure cannot apply to her conduct as an *arusah*.

The *Gemara* then considers a modified scenario: the *kinuy* occurred while she was an *arusah*, the seclusion (*stirah*) occurred while she was an *arusah*, but the husband proceeds with the marriage anyway — and now, as a *nesuah*, wishes to administer the *sotah* waters retroactively for the conduct that occurred during *erusin*. Now *tachas ishech* is satisfied in the sense that she is currently a *nesuah*. Can the *sotah* process proceed?

The *Gemara* rejects this as well, citing the verse *ve-nikah ha-ish me-avon* — the husband must be clean of sin. If she was suspected during *erusin* and he nonetheless slept with her prior to the drinking — which he would have done by proceeding through *chuppah* and *nisu'in* — then he has himself acted improperly (since a man suspecting his wife is not supposed to cohabit with her), and the waters cannot test her. *Bizman she-ish menukeh me-avon mayim bodkin es ishto, ein ish menukeh me-avon ein mayim bodkin es ishto.* There is therefore no configuration in which the *arusah* scenario can directly trigger the *sotah* process.

### Gilgul Despite Exclusion

The conclusion: *ella al yedei gilgul* — the only way she can be required to swear about her conduct as an *arusah* is through *gilgul shevuah*. Because she is already taking a *shevuah* about a qualifying case — one that directly meets all the requirements — the oath can be rolled to include the *arusah* scenario as well, even though that scenario is categorically excluded from independently generating a *sotah* obligation.

This is precisely the point the *Gemara* is making: *gilgul shevuah* is not merely an extension to adjacent or similar cases. It can reach matters that are categorically excluded — that could never, under any circumstances, have been the direct subject of the *shevuah*. In the monetary context, *karka* is categorically excluded from *shevuah*, just as the *arusah* scenario is categorically excluded from *sotah*. The *sotah* passage proves that such categorical exclusions do not block *gilgul*.

### Money Extension

The *Gemara* asks: *ashkan sotah de-isura* — we have established *gilgul shevuah* from *sotah*, a case of *issur* (religious prohibition). *Mamona minalan* — how do we know *gilgul* applies to monetary disputes? One might argue that *issur* is more stringent and therefore entitled to a more expansive *shevuah* regime. Or conversely, that the two domains operate by different rules entirely.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael (*tana devei Rabbi Yishmael*) provides a *kal vachomer*: *uma sotah shelo nitenah lehitava be-ed echad megalgelin* — by *sotah*, the *gilgul* is operative even though the *sotah* proceeding itself cannot be triggered by a single witness; it requires two witnesses to the seclusion. Yet even with this high threshold, *gilgul* functions. *Mamon shenitnah lehitava be-ed echad* — in monetary disputes, a single witness is sufficient to trigger a *shevuah*. The threshold is lower, and if *gilgul* functions despite the more demanding requirement in *sotah*, all the more so does *gilgul* function in monetary disputes where the triggering threshold is easier to meet. Accordingly, one who is obligated to take a *shevuah* in a monetary dispute can be required, through *gilgul shevuah*, to include *karka* in that oath — even though *karka* is categorically excluded from independently generating a *shevuah* obligation.

The *Gemara* continues with *gilgul shevuah* on the following *daf*.
