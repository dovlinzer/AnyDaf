# Bekhorot 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava: All in Pen

### All Animals in Pen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָיוּ לוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר טְלָאִים, לֹא יֹאמַר: אֶבְרוֹר עֲשָׂרָה וְאַכְנִיסֵם לַדִּיר וְאֶטּוֹל מֵהֶם אֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁאָר פְּטוּרִים, אֶלָּא כּוֹנְסָן לַדִּיר, וּמוֹצִיא עֲשָׂרָה, וְנוֹטֵל מֵהֶם אֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁאָר מִצְטָרְפִין לְגוֹרֶן אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** If one **had fifteen lambs, he may not say: I will separate ten and bring them into the pen, and I will take one of them** as animal tithe, **and the rest** will be **exempt. Rather,** he must **bring** them all **into the pen and remove ten** of them by causing them to emerge from the opening, **and take one of them,** the tenth one, as animal tithe. **And the remainder combine** with animals born later, to be tithed **in another** designated time of **gathering.**

We open on the penultimate daf of *Bekhorot* — 60a — with Rava's teaching on how to properly perform *maaser behemah*. If one has fifteen animals, Rava's first ruling is that one may not simply select ten and tithe from them alone; all fifteen must be placed into the pen. Rashi specifies that the prohibition applies in particular to selecting the worst ten, though the underlying principle appears broader: the *maaser* process must encompass one's entire flock without any deliberate selection. The animals must all enter the pen together and emerge in a randomized, undirected sequence.

### Leftovers Combine

Rava's second principle addresses what happens to the five animals that remain after the ten have exited and the tithe has been taken. One might have assumed that these five are now entirely exempt — perhaps a kind of remainder that falls outside any further obligation. Rava rules otherwise: if *Rosh Hashanah* has not yet arrived, those five do not simply disappear from the *maaser* calculus. They must combine with whatever animals are born thereafter, and the owner takes *maaser* from the combined group in the following year.

### Braita on Fifteen Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הָיוּ לוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר טְלָאִים, לֹא יֹאמַר: אֶבְרוֹר עֲשָׂרָה וְאֶטּוֹל מֵהֶן אֶחָד וְהַשְּׁאָר פְּטוּרִים, אֶלָּא כּוֹנְסָן לַדִּיר, וּמוֹצִיא עֲשָׂרָה וְנוֹטֵל מֵהֶן אֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁאָר מִצְטָרְפִין לְגוֹרֶן אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: If one **had fifteen lambs,** **he may not say: I will separate ten** and bring them into the pen, **and I will take one of them** as animal tithe **and the rest** will be **exempt; rather,** he must **bring** them all **into the pen and remove ten** of them by causing them to emerge from the opening, **and take one of them,** the tenth one, **and the remainder combine** with animals born later, to be tithed **in another** designated time of **gathering.**

A *braita* at the bottom of 59b confirms both of these points: *tanya nami hachi* — we have been taught similarly. If one had fifteen sheep, one may not say, "I will select ten, take the *maaser* from them, and let the remaining five go free." Rather, he brings all of them into the pen (*ela konasom ladir*), ten exit (*umotzi asarah*), one is taken (*venoteil mehen echad*), and the remaining five must combine with the next *goren* (*vehashar mitztaref legoren acher*).

### Rashi on Goren Restriction

Rashi raises a difficulty: is this *chiddush* truly new? We already learned in an earlier *mishnah* that if one has five animals from one *goren* and five from another, they combine. We already knew that a remainder under ten cannot simply be ignored — it must await further combinations. What then is the novelty here?

Rashi introduces a far-reaching *chiddush* of his own — one that is not stated explicitly anywhere in the *Gemara*. When those fifteen animals were all gathered together and the *goren* period arrived, and then the *maaser* was taken and five remained, those five are not merely obligated to combine with the next *goren*; they are also subject to the *goren* restriction immediately. That is, since they were present at the *goren*, one may not *shecht* them, sell them, or derive any benefit from them until they are combined with the next *goren* and *maaser* is properly taken. Even though all fifteen were in the pen together and the *maaser* process was performed correctly, the leftover five are frozen in a state of *goren* restriction.

This is a substantial *chiddush*, and Rashi acknowledges that it does not appear in the *Gemara* text itself. His motivation seems to be that the *braita* must be telling us something beyond what we already knew, and so he imports this additional dimension. Whether Rava himself intended this reading remains uncertain, but it is what Rashi reads into the *chiddush*.

It is worth noting that this *perek* is unusual in its physical layout: the pages are smaller because they contain a second edition of Rashi, labeled *Rashi Ksav Yad*. The standard Rashi printed here is noticeably more verbose than is typical, which raises the possibility that it may represent an earlier draft, or that questions of authorship are involved. This is a topic worth investigating further in works that survey the scholarship on such textual questions — such as Strack and Stemberger's introduction to the Talmud, which surveys the academic discussion on these issues in a rigorous and accessible way.

## Nineteen Sheep Problem

### Braita: 19 Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: תִּשְׁעָה עָשָׂר טְלָאִים, לֹא יֹאמַר: אֶבְרוֹר עֲשָׂרָה וְאֶטּוֹל מֵהֶן אֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁאָר פְּטוּרִין, אֶלָּא כּוֹנְסָן לַדִּיר, וּמוֹצִיא עֲשָׂרָה וְנוֹטֵל מֵהֶן אֶחָד, וְהַשְּׁאָר פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one has **nineteen lambs he may not say: I will separate ten** and bring them into the pen, **and I will take one of them** as animal tithe **and the rest** will be **exempt; rather,** he must **bring** them all **into the pen and remove ten** of them by causing them to emerge from the opening, **and take one of them,** the tenth one, **and the remainder are exempt?** According to this *baraita*, the remainder are not combined with animals born later.

The *Gemara* now cites a *braita* that presents a new case. If one has nineteen sheep, one may not say: "I will put ten into the pen, take one, and the remaining nine are exempt." Rather, all nineteen must be placed in the pen (*ela konesan ladir*), ten exit (*umotzi asarah*), one is taken (*venoteil mehen echad*) — and then the *braita* states: *vehashar peturin*, the remaining nine are exempt.

### Exempt vs. Combine

This creates an apparent contradiction. On one hand, we just established from Rava that leftover animals are not exempt but must combine with the next *goren*. On the other hand, this *braita* explicitly says the nine that remain are exempt. How are these two positions reconciled?

### Two-Door Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר סְחוֹרָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא בְּרִיגְלָא: בְּדִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי פְתָחִים עָסְקִינַן, וְיָצְאוּ תִּשְׁעָה בְּפֶתַח זֶה וְתִשְׁעָה בְּפֶתַח זֶה, דְּהַאיְךְ חַד חָזֵי לְהָכָא וּלְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Seḥora interpreted** the *baraita* **before Rava at** the time of the **Festival** discourse: **We are dealing with a pen that has two openings. And nine** of the lambs **emerged** through **this opening and nine** of them went out through **that opening, and this** last **one** remaining in the pen **is fit to** come out **here or to** come out **there.** Therefore, all the lambs emerged as part of a tally fit to reach ten.

The resolution was offered by Rav Huna bar Sechorah in the presence of Rava himself — apparently at a public *drasha* held during the period before the *regel*, when the *halachot* of *maaser behemah* were expounded since that was precisely when the tithing obligation arose. (One tradition among the *Geonim* identifies *Shabbat derigla* not as the Shabbat before the *regel* but as the Shabbat observing the *yahrtzeit* of a great figure who had passed that year — making the setting of this exchange potentially more intimate.)

Rav Huna bar Sechorah's answer: *bedir she'yesh lo shenei petachim askinan* — we are dealing with a pen that has two doors. Nine animals exited through one door, and nine animals exited through the other (*ve'yatze'u tishah b'petach zeh ve'tishah b'petach zeh*). One animal remains inside. That last animal — *dehach chad dechazi le'hacha u'le'hacha* — could have joined either group, and therefore each group of nine qualifies as a *minyan harauy*: a group that was capable of being a valid count of ten. Accordingly, when the *braita* says the remaining nine are exempt, it does not mean the nine still in the pen; it means the nine that exited the other door. Each group of nine is exempt by virtue of *minyan harauy* — not because they are truly leftover, but because each set could have been part of a complete set of ten.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁמָּנָה תִּשְׁעָה, וְכִי מְטָא עֲשָׂרָה קָרֵי ״חַד״ מֵרֵישָׁא! קָסָבַר: עֲשִׂירִי מֵאֵלָיו קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let him teach** instead that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he counted nine, and when the tenth** lamb **arrived** to be counted **he called** it number **one** and began counting again **from the start.** In such a case he designated only the nineteenth lamb that emerged as number ten, and therefore all the other lambs are exempt. The Gemara explains that Rav Huna bar Seḥora **holds** that the **tenth** animal to come out of the pen **is sanctified of its own** accord, even if it was designated as number one.

A related question arises: when there is one animal left in the pen, and Rava holds that the tenth animal is defined by its objective reality (*asiri me'elav kadosh*), what happens if you know this is the tenth but cannot determine which group's tenth it belongs to? Rashi resolves this by citing Rava's principle: since we know this animal is objectively the tenth, it automatically becomes *maaser* — even if we do not know to which group's counting it belongs.

### Pairs Counting Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ בְּגוֹרֶן, וּמְנָאָן זוּגוֹת זוּגוֹת! קָסָבַר: עֲשִׂירִי לְמִנְיָן בְּהֵמוֹת הוּא קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let him teach** instead that the *baraita* is referring **to** a case of a designated time for **gathering** the animals, and that the pen has only one opening, **but he counted them pair by pair,** i.e., he called the first pair number one, the second pair number two, and so on. In this manner, the nineteenth lamb would be designated as number ten and thereby exempt the others. The Gemara explains that Rav Huna bar Seḥora **holds** that the **tenth according to the number of animals is sanctified,** regardless of how he counts them. Accordingly, one of the fifth pair would be the tithed animal, and the remaining nine would not be considered part of a tally fit to reach ten.

The *Gemara* then poses an alternative: why not resolve the contradiction differently? Why not say that the person counted nine animals, and when the tenth arrived, accidentally called it "one" and began counting again — so the nineteenth animal was the one called "ten" in the second sequence, while the first nine were exempt due to *minyan harauy*? Why was the two-door scenario necessary?

### Rav Nachman's Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: זְכַאי אִימֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בַּר סְחוֹרָה, דְּשַׁנִּי לֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּרִיגְלָא כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** with regard to this incident: **The mother of Rav Huna bar Seḥora merited** to give birth to such a son, **who explained the *halakha*** before Rava **at** the time of **the Festival** discourse **in accordance with** Rava's **own *halakha*.**

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar asiri me'elav kadosh* — this would be consistent with Rava's own position that the tenth animal is automatically *kadosh* regardless of what it is called. If that is so, then calling the tenth animal "one" would not strip it of its sanctity; it would be *kadosh* regardless, leaving *maaser* and *chullin* mixed up with no resolution. Similarly, a suggestion that the animals were counted in pairs (*zugot zugot*) is entertained but ultimately also runs into Rava's principle — the *Gemara* will return to this — and so the two-door scenario remains the only clean answer.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then offers a ringing endorsement: *zakai imei d'Rav Huna bar Schora* — may the merit of Rav Huna bar Sechorah's mother be great. Not only did he publicly defend Rava's ruling before a large audience during the *regel*, but he defended Rava using Rava's own *svara*. His answer turned on *minyan harauy* — one of Rava's core concepts — and implicitly relied on Rava's principle that objective reality defines the *maaser*, not the counting, which is precisely why other proposed answers had to be rejected. His defense was internally consistent with Rava's entire framework.

## Tenth's Reality (III)

### Reality vs. Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם כְּאַחַת — מוֹנֶה אוֹתָם שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם, מְנָאָן אֶחָד — תְּשִׁיעִי וַעֲשִׂירִי מְקוּלְקָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **two** animals **emerged as one, one counts them as twos,** i.e., as though they came out one after the other. If he mistakenly **counted** two of the animals at the beginning or in the middle of the ten as **one,** and then continued his count, the **ninth and** the **tenth are flawed,** as he called the tenth: Ninth, and he called the eleventh: Tenth.

We arrive at the central conceptual question that will occupy the *Gemara* through the end of the *masechet*: when two animals emerge simultaneously, there is no objectively determinable tenth — the two exited together and we cannot know which crossed the threshold first. This raises the possibility that precisely where the physical reality does not clearly designate one animal as the tenth, the automatic *kedushah* of Rava's principle may not apply. It is possible to argue that even under *ei efshar letzamtzem*, one of the two was indeed a fraction of a moment ahead — but we simply do not know which.

This deeper tension — between what the owner designates through counting and what is determined by objective physical reality — will now be explored through the next *mishnah*. As a preliminary orientation: the *mishnah* we already learned makes clear that calling nine "ten" or eleven "ten" has real *halachic* consequences, and so what one says cannot be entirely irrelevant. At the same time, Rava teaches that the tenth is *kadosh* without any counting at all. The interplay between these two principles — the power of designation and the power of physical reality — is precisely what the *Gemara* is now working through.

## Ninth/Tenth/Eleventh

### Miscounting All Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״תְּשִׁיעִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — שְׁלָשְׁתָּן מְקוּדָּשִׁין: הַתְּשִׁיעִי נֶאֱכָל בְּמוּמוֹ, וְהָעֲשִׂירִי מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאַחַד עָשָׂר קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים וְעוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** If he mistakenly **called** the **ninth: Tenth, and** the **tenth: Ninth, and the eleventh: Tenth,** the **three of them are sacred,** although each has a different halakhic status. **The ninth is eaten in its blemished** state; **and the tenth is** animal **tithe,** which is sacrificed in the Temple and eaten by its owner; **and** the **eleventh is sacrificed as a peace offering,** from which the breast and the thigh are given to the priest. **And** the eleventh **renders** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for the peace offering consecrated as **a substitute** and he sacrifices it as a peace offering; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *mishnah* sets out the following case: one called the ninth animal "ten," called the tenth "nine," and called the eleventh "ten." The *mishnah* rules: *sheloshtan mekudashim* — all three are *kadosh*.

The ninth becomes *kadosh* by virtue of having been called "ten." However, it cannot be brought as a *korban*: the designation was made before a genuine tenth existed, so it lacks the full power of *maaser*. It must wait until it develops a blemish (*mum*), at which point it may be eaten in its blemished state.

The tenth is *maaser* in the fullest sense — objectively, by virtue of its position in the sequence, regardless of what it was called.

The eleventh becomes *kadosh* from having been designated "ten," and since it follows the genuine tenth and carries *kedushah* directly — though not the *kedushah* of *maaser* itself — it is brought as a *shelamim*. The *korban* most analogous to *maaser behemah* in its rules is *shelamim*: eaten by the owners for two days and the intervening night, classified as *kodshim kalim*.

A clarification is necessary here: there is no such thing as *temuras maaser*. Ordinarily, one can stand a *shelamim* before another animal, declare *temura*, and thereby transfer the *kedushah*. With *maaser*, this is unavailable — it is derived from a *pasuk* and will be discussed further in *Maseches Temurah*. The eleventh is therefore not a *temura* of *maaser*; it has its own direct *kedushah*, which resembles *maaser* sufficiently that it is treated as *shelamim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי יֵשׁ תְּמוּרָה עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה? אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִילּוּ הָיָה תְּמוּרָה — לֹא הָיָה קָרֵב. קָרָא לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר מְקוּדָּשׁ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם עֲשִׂירִי מִמֶּנּוּ, אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר מְקוּדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said:** The eleventh animal itself, which he called tenth, is a substitute for animal tithe, **and does a substitute render** another animal **a substitute?** Everyone agrees that a substitute is created only in exchange for an originally consecrated animal. The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** The eleventh animal is not considered a substitute for the animal tithe, since **if it were a substitute it would not be sacrificed,** as the substitute for an animal tithe is not sacrificed. If **one called the ninth** animal: **Tenth, and the tenth: Tenth, and the eleventh: Tenth, the eleventh is not consecrated. This is the principle:** In **any** situation **where the name** of the **tenth was not removed from** the tenth animal, the **eleventh** that was called the tenth **is not consecrated.**

The *mishnah* further states: *ve'osef temura* — if the owner then took yet another animal and declared a transfer of *kedushah* from the eleventh onto it, he has transgressed the prohibition of *temura*, and the new animal also becomes *kadosh*.

This last ruling is the subject of a dispute. Rabbi Meir holds that the eleventh can produce a *temura* — the new animal becomes *kadosh*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *v'chi yesh temura osah temura?* Can a *temura* produce another *temura*? In his view, the eleventh itself is conceptually a *temura* of the *maaser* — modeled on the mechanism by which *kedushah* is transferred from one animal to another by means of mislabeling. And since *temura* cannot produce another *temura*, the eleventh cannot extend its *kedushah* to a fourth animal.

Rabbi Meir's rejoinder — stated in his name by others — is that if the eleventh were truly a *temura*, it could not be brought as a *korban* at all, since *temuras maaser* cannot be brought as a *korban*. The fact that it is brought as a *shelamim* proves it is not a *temura* but a direct, autonomous *kedushah*. Therefore, it can produce a *temura*.

The underlying conceptual debate is significant: by what mechanism does the eleventh become *kadosh*? In Rabbi Yehuda's view, it is because the owner attempted to remove the *kedushah* from ten and transfer it to eleven — a process modeled on *temura*, even if not identical to it. In Rabbi Meir's view, calling eleven "ten" constitutes an independent act of *kedushah* — not a transfer but a direct sanctification, made possible by the fact that ten had already been mislabeled as nine, removing the competition that would otherwise render the designation of eleven meaningless.

### Uprooting the Tenth

This leads to the principle stated at the end of the *mishnah*: if one called nine "ten," ten "ten," and eleven "ten" — meaning ten was never mislabeled — then *ein achad asar mikudash*: the eleventh does not become *kadosh*. The governing rule is: *kol shelo ne'ekar shem asiri mimenu, ein achad asar kadosh* — if you do not uproot the name of ten, eleven does not become holy.

This principle is intuitive: the act of calling eleven "ten" is meaningful only if the claim that ten is "ten" has been contested. If ten has already been acknowledged as *maaser*, then calling eleven "ten" is simply an empty statement that conflicts with what everyone knows to be true. The designation carries no weight. Only when the owner has actively mislabeled ten — denied it its status — does the subsequent designation of eleven as "ten" have the power to confer *kedushah*.

## Tenth's Reality (III)

### Rava: Tenth Auto-Kadosh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְנָאָן זוּגוֹת זוּגוֹת, קִינְטָרִין קִינְטָרִין — עֲשִׂירִי לְמִנְיָנוֹ הוּא קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **counted** the animals **pair by pair,** i.e., he called the first pair: One, and the second pair: Two, and so on, or if one counted them **hundred [*kinteran*]** by **hundred,** the **tenth** according **to his number is sanctified.**

Rava now states his position explicitly. The tenth animal is *kadosh* automatically — *asiri me'elav kadosh* — regardless of how it is labeled. If one counted ten animals and called the tenth "one," it is still *kadosh*. This is Rava's consistent view: not only is one's counting unnecessary to confer *kedushah* on the tenth, but one's counting cannot subvert it either. Whether one calls it "nine," "eleven," "one," or any other designation, if it is objectively the tenth animal to exit, it is *kadosh*.

## Grouping Debate

### Counting by Groupings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְמִנְיָנוֹ״ מַאי? רַב מָארִי אָמַר: לַמִּנְיָן שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא קָדוֹשׁ, רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: לְמִנְיַן בְּהֵמוֹת הוּא קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When Rabbi Yoḥanan says that the tenth is sanctified according **to his number, what** does he mean? **Rav Mari says: It is sanctified according to his counting,** i.e., all the animals in whichever pair or group he calls: Ten, are sanctified, e.g., the tenth pair, which consists of the nineteenth and twentieth animals. **Rav Kahana says: It is sanctified according to the number of animals,** regardless of how he designates them.

Rabbi Yochanan's teaching: if one counted the animals in pairs (*zugot zugot*) or by hundreds (*kintrin kintrin*, from the Latin *centenarius*), the tenth of one's counting is *kadosh* (*asiri leminyano hu kadosh*). The precise meaning of this statement, however, is disputed.

Rav Mari understands Rabbi Yochanan to mean that one's chosen grouping defines the counting. If one counted by pairs, the tenth pair consists of two animals simultaneously exiting, and both become *kadosh* — two *maasrot* in a single moment. The owner's decision to regroup the animals as pairs is treated as a legitimate redefinition, and the tenth of that grouping, however many animals it contains, is the *maaser*. Rav Kahana, by contrast, understands Rabbi Yochanan to mean that one's designation is entirely irrelevant: *leminyan behemah hu kadosh* — the tenth animal to exit is *kadosh*, period. No regrouping alters this.

### Regrouping Ignored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם כְּאַחַת — מוֹנֶה אוֹתָן שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם. מְנָאָן אֶחָד — תְּשִׁיעִי וַעֲשִׂירִי מְקוּלְקָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites support for Rav Mari's opinion. **We learned** in the mishna: If **two** animals **emerged as one, one counts them as twos.** If he mistakenly **counted** a pair of animals in the middle as **one** and continued his count, the **ninth and** the **tenth are flawed,** as he called the tenth: Ninth, and the eleventh: Tenth.

The *mishnah*'s own ruling creates pressure on Rav Kahana's position: if one designates two animals as a single count when they exit together and calls them "five" instead of "five and six," the *mishnah* says that nine and ten become *mekulkalim* — confused. This implies that what one says has some consequence. If Rav Kahana were entirely correct that designation is ignored, it should not matter at all what one calls any animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: לַמִּנְיָן שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא קָדוֹשׁ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי הָווּ תְּשִׁיעִי וַעֲשִׂירִי מְקוּלְקָלִין, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״תְּשִׁיעִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: לְמִנְיַן בְּהֵמוֹת הוּא קָדוֹשׁ, לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״תְּשִׁיעִי״ קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״ קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says: It is sanctified according to his counting,** i.e., Rav Mari, it is **due to that** reason that **the ninth and** the **tenth are flawed,** because the number by which one designates them is significant, **and he called the tenth** animal the **ninth, and the eleventh** he designated as **tenth. But according to the one who says** that the animal **is sanctified according to the number of animals** regardless of how the owner designates each one, i.e., Rav Kahana, why are they flawed? It should be considered as though **he called the ninth** animal the **ninth, and the tenth** animal **he called: Tenth.**

The *Gemara* resolves this tension by limiting Rav Kahana's position carefully. According to his reading of Rabbi Yochanan: when one is deliberately attempting to regroup the animals — *heicha de'ichaven le'apukei zuga zuga* — counting intentionally by twos or hundreds, that entire framework is disregarded. Only sequential one-by-one counting is recognized; any deliberate restructuring of the counting unit is ignored entirely. However, when the confusion arises accidentally — two animals exit at once without intention, and one miscalls them — the error is not entirely without consequence. Because one is operating within the framework of sequential counting and made a mistake, one's mislabeling does have some impact, precisely what the *mishnah* reflects. This is admittedly a narrow and counterintuitive position: intentional regrouping is ignored, while an accidental mislabeling matters. The *Gemara* maintains this distinction because the *mishnah* requires it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי אָמֵינָא אֲנָא — הֵיכִי דְּאִיכַּוֵּין לְאַפּוֹקֵי זוּגוֹת זוּגוֹת, הֵיכָא דִּנְפַק מִמֵּילָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that according to Rav Kahana, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you: When I said** the designation goes according to the animal I was referring only to a case **where he intended to take out** the animals **pair by pair,** and he did not err. In such a case his designation is nullified and the animals are sanctified in accordance with the order they left the pen. But with regard to a case **where** the animal **emerged by itself** and the owner miscounted I did **not** say the designation goes according to the number of the animal, as the Torah explicitly includes a case where the sanctification follows a mistaken designation (see 60b).

## Tenth's Reality (III)…

### Backward Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מְנָאָן לְמַפְרֵעַ — עֲשִׂירִי שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן הוּא קָדוֹשׁ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמִנְיַן בְּהֵמוֹת הוּא קָדוֹשׁ — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לַמִּנְיָן שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא קָדוֹשׁ, עֲשִׂירִי ״חַד״ קָרֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from a *baraita*: If one **counted them backward,** i.e., ten, nine, eight, and so on, the **tenth** animal **in the numbering,** which he designated as number one, **is sanctified. Granted, according to the one who says** that the tithe **is sanctified according to the** number of **animals** regardless of how he designates each animal, i.e., Rav Kahana, this works out **well,** as the tenth animal is sanctified. **But according to the one who says it is sanctified** according **to his counting,** i.e., Rav Mari, why is the tenth animal sanctified? After all, **he called** the **tenth** animal number **one.**

A *braita* is cited: if one counted the animals backward — ten, nine, eight, seven, down to one — the tenth of that counting is *kadosh*, i.e., the animal one called "one." The *Gemara* notes that this poses no difficulty for Rav Kahana's position: he ignores one's designation entirely and says the tenth animal to exit is *kadosh* regardless of what it was called. But for Rav Mari — who says we follow the owner's counting — why should calling something "one" make it *kadosh*? One called it "one," not "ten."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִיתֵיהּ בְּמִנְיָנָא פָּרְסָאָה, דְּקָרוּ לְעַשְׂרָה ״חַד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The tenth animal is sanctified according to Rav Mari **because it** has been counted as number ten **in Persian counting, as they call the tenth** one in their language by the term **one,** i.e., they count only in units of ten, and call these units: One. Therefore, in this case there is no contradiction between the designation and the number of the animal.

Rava offers a creative resolution: *achva de'ita biminyana parsa'ah* — this is consistent with Persian usage, where the word for ten is cognate with or expressed as "one" (*dekaru le'asara chad*). In Persian idiom, the owner's statement of "one" at the tenth position could be understood as meaning ten. The *Gemara* does not press further, though it is evident that this answer is somewhat forced. A more principled response might have been that even counting backward preserves an identifiable tenth position in the sequence, which is distinct from a case of deliberate regrouping into pairs or hundreds. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* concludes with Rava's answer.

Throughout this discussion, the core tension is held in view: the *mishnah* makes clear that miscounting — calling nine "ten" or eleven "ten" — has real *halachic* consequences. Meanwhile, Rava insists that the tenth is *kadosh* independently of any designation. The open question, which continues to drive the *Gemara*'s analysis, is how these two forces — designation and objective reality — interact, and where each one takes precedence.

## Simultaneous Exit

### 9 & 10 Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בַּתְּשִׁיעִי, קְרָאָן ״תְּשִׁיעִי״ — עֲשִׂירִי וְחוּלִּין מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה, עֲשִׂירִי מֵאֵלָיו קָדוֹשׁ, לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״תְּשִׁיעִי״ קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** If **two** animals **emerged** from the pen together **as the ninth,** and **he called them** both the **ninth,** then the **tenth,** i.e., the tithe, **and a non-sacred** animal **are** considered **intermingled with each other.** Rava elaborates: The **tenth is sanctified of its own** accord, despite the fact that he called it the ninth. And the animal that came out **ninth** is non-sacred, as it came out ninth and **he called it the ninth.** Since there is no way to determine which animal is which, neither of them can be brought as an offering, and they must both be left to graze until they develop a blemish.

Rava presents a series of cases involving simultaneous exit. First: nine and ten exit together, and one calls them both "nine." The result is *asiri v'tishi'i me'uravin zeh b'zeh* — the *maaser* animal and the *chullin* animal are mixed up, since ten is objectively *kadosh* (*asiri me'elav kadosh*) regardless of what it was called, and nine is *chullin*. Not knowing which is which, one must wait for a *mum* to develop on both and then treat them accordingly.

### Both Called 10

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָאָן ״עֲשִׂירִי״, עֲשִׂירִי וּתְשִׁיעִי מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״עֲשִׂירִי״ קָא קָרֵי לְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If two animals emerged from the pen together as the ninth, and **he called them** both the **tenth,** then the **tenth,** i.e., the tithe, **and** the **ninth** animal, which he designated as the tenth, **are intermingled with each other. What is the reason? He called both of them** the **tenth.** The tenth has the sanctity of the tithe while the ninth was designated as the tenth, and therefore may not be eaten until it develops a blemish. Since there is no way to determine which animal is which, neither can be sacrificed, but they must both be left to graze until they develop a blemish.

Second: nine and ten exit together, and one calls them both "ten." Now the mix is different. Ten is *kadosh* as *maaser*, and nine — having been called "ten" — acquires *kedushah* of a kind that cannot be brought as a *korban* (since it was designated before a genuine tenth existed). The two animals — *maaser* and this non-*korban kedushah* — are mixed together. Once again, one waits for a *mum*.

### 10 & 11 Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בָּעֲשִׂירִי וּקְרָאָן ״עֲשִׂירִי״, עֲשִׂירִי וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה. קְרָאָן ״אַחַד עָשָׂר״, עֲשִׂירִי וְחוּלִּין מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** If **two** animals **emerged** from the pen together **as the tenth, and he called them** both the **tenth,** then the **tenth, and** the **eleventh** animal, which he designated as the tenth, **are intermingled with each other.** If **he called them** both the **eleventh, the tenth and a non-sacred** animal **are intermingled with each other.**

Third: ten and eleven exit together, and one calls them both "ten." Here an important principle emerges, prompted precisely by the question raised earlier: the *mishnah* states that eleven becomes *kadosh* only when the name of ten has been uprooted. But in this case, ten was not mislabeled — it was called "ten," which it genuinely is. How then does eleven acquire *kedushah*?

### No Competition Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַיְינוּ הָךְ! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּכֹל בַּת אַחַת תַּרְוַויְיהוּ קָא קָדְשִׁי, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם ״עֲשִׂירִי״ הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this additional** case? **This** reasoning for the *halakha* in the case where the animals emerged together as the tenth is **identical** to **that** of the first case, where the animals emerged together as the ninth. The Gemara answers: **This** latter clause **teaches us that** in **every** case where two animals emerge **as one they are both sanctified, and** this is the *halakha* **even though the name** of the **tenth was not removed from** the tenth animal. This situation is not included in the principle of the mishna: In any situation where the name of the tenth was not removed from the tenth animal, the eleventh that was called tenth is not consecrated.

The answer is that the *ne'ikar shem asiri* requirement — the need to uproot ten's designation — is not about *temura*-style transfer but about removing the competition that the real tenth presents to the eleventh's claim to *kedushah*. When ten and eleven exit sequentially, calling eleven "ten" is meaningless because ten has already secured its designation as *maaser*, and eleven cannot compete against it. The *ne'ikar* requirement exists to neutralize that competition: by mislabeling ten, the owner removes its established status, and calling eleven "ten" then carries weight.

But when ten and eleven exit simultaneously, there is no competition to begin with. Neither has been designated before the other; the owner is calling both "ten" at exactly the same moment. In this case, *tarvaihu kadshi* — both are *kadosh*. Ten is *maaser*, and eleven acquires *kedushah* to be brought as a *shelamim*, without any need to have mislabeled ten.

### Sequential vs. Simultaneous…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם ״עֲשִׂירִי״ הֵימֶנּוּ, וּתְנַן זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם ״עֲשִׂירִי״ הֵימֶנּוּ — אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר מְקוּדָּשׁ! הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, אֲבָל בְּבַת אַחַת — תַּרְוַיְיהוּ קָא קָדְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Kahana sat and recited this *halakha*. Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: But the name** of the **tenth has not been removed from it. And didn't we learn** in the mishna that **this is the principle:** In **any** situation **where the name** of the **tenth was not removed from** the tenth animal, the **eleventh** that was called tenth **is not consecrated?** Rav Kahana replied to Rav Ashi: **This statement,** that the designation of tenth must be removed, applies only when the animals leave the pen **one after the other. But** if two animals emerge from the pen **at the same time,** and he simultaneously designated the tenth and eleventh as tithe, **both of them are sanctified.**

The *Gemara* confirms this by noting that the *mishnah*'s general principle — *kol shelo ne'ekar shem asiri mimenu, ein achad asar kadosh* — was stated as a *klal* after giving a specific sequential case. When one already gave the specific case of sequential miscounting (ten-ten-ten), the *klal* was added to extend the rule further. What does it include?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה אַחַר זֶה — בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ: קָרָא לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר מְקוּדָּשׁ. ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לָאו לְאֵיתוֹיֵי בְּבַת אַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: How can the mishna be explained as referring only to a case where the animals left the pen **one after the other?** After all, the mishna **teaches explicitly:** If **one called the ninth** animal: **Tenth, and the tenth: Tenth, and the eleventh: Tenth, the eleventh is not consecrated.** This is the principle: In any situation where the name of the tenth was not removed from the tenth animal, the eleventh that was called tenth is not consecrated. **What** does the phrase: **This is the principle,** serve **to include?** Does it not serve **to include** a case where the tenth and eleventh animals leave the pen **at the same time?** And if so, it is teaching that in such a case the eleventh animal is not sanctified.

The *Gemara* concludes with Rav Kahana's answer:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי יָצָא עֲשִׂירִי וְלֹא דִּבֵּר, דְּהָא לֹא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם ״עֲשִׂירִי״ הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana would answer: **No;** it serves not to include that case, but **to include** a situation where **the tenth** animal **emerged** from the pen **and** the owner **did not speak** at all, and when the eleventh animal came out he designated it as the tenth. Therefore, the eleventh animal is not sanctified, **as he did not remove the name** of the **tenth from** the tenth animal. But if the tenth and eleventh animals came out at the same time, and he designated them both as the tenth, they are both sanctified.

Even silence about ten is insufficient to count as uprooting its name; one must actively mislabel it. If ten walks out and one remains silent, ten is still objectively *kadosh*, and eleven's subsequent designation carries no weight. To confer *kedushah* on eleven through the sequential process, one must affirmatively assign the wrong label to ten.

## Mislabeling Definition

### Silence on Tenth

The question now posed is: what exactly counts as mislabeling ten? If the tenth animal exits and one says nothing at all — neither calling it ten nor assigning it any other number — and then when the eleventh exits one calls it "ten," has one been *oker shem asiri*? Intuitively one might think so: ten was not acknowledged, and eleven was called ten. Is that sufficient?

### Active Mislabeling Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בָּעֲשִׂירִי, וְלֹא קָדַם אֶחָד מֵהֶן אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וּקְרָאָן ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — עֲשִׂירִי וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה, וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם עֲשִׂירִי הֵימֶנּוּ! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: כֹּל בְּבַת אַחַת תַּרְוַיְיהוּ קָא קָדְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that this must be the case, **because if you do not say this** explanation of the mishna there is a problem with **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: If **two** animals **emerged** from the pen together **as the tenth, and one** did **not** come out **before the other, and he called them** both the **tenth,** the **tenth and eleventh** animals **are intermingled with each other.** One is sacred with the sanctity of the animal tithe while the other is a peace offering, but there is no way to determine which animal is which. The Gemara explains the problem here: How can this *baraita* be reconciled with the mishna? **He did not remove the name** of the **tenth from** the tenth animal. **Rather, is it not due to** the fact **that we say:** In **any** case where two animals come out of the pen **at the same time both of them are sanctified?**

The *Gemara* answers: no. To be *oker shem asiri*, one must actively assign a wrong label to ten. Mere silence does not suffice. The rationale is that ten, as the genuine *maaser*, retains its objective status whether or not one speaks. Simply declining to acknowledge it leaves it fully intact as *kadosh*, and the subsequent designation of eleven as "ten" cannot override the standing of an animal that has already — silently but really — become *maaser*. Active mislabeling is required: one must call ten something it is not, thereby making a positive (if false) claim about its status that opens space for eleven's designation to be meaningful.

## Calling 11 as 10

### Rabbi Limits Mislabel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי? דְּתַנְיָא: קָרָא לַעֲשִׂירִי ״אַחַד עָשָׂר״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר קָדוֹשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַחַד עָשָׂר קָדוֹשׁ. כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֱקַר שֵׁם עֲשִׂירִי הֵימֶנּוּ — אֵין אַחַד עָשָׂר קָדוֹשׁ. אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּהֵמוֹת טוּבָא, דְּאָמְרִינַן: חַד עִישּׂוּרָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** To **what** statement of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi is the Gemara referring? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **called the tenth** animal the **eleventh and the eleventh** animal the **tenth,** the **eleventh** animal is **not sanctified;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The **eleventh** animal **is sanctified. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said a principle: As long as the name** of the **tenth has not been removed from** the tenth animal, the **eleventh** animal **is not sanctified.** To clarify why the eleventh animal is not sanctified according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi even though the name of the tenth was removed from the tenth animal when it was called the eleventh, **Rava said: Here we are dealing with** a case **where he has many animals,** and the reason is **that we say** that when he said: *Aḥad asar*, his intention was to **say: *Ḥad issura*.**

A *braita* presents a dispute. Rabbi (Rebbi) holds that if one calls ten "eleven" and then calls eleven "ten," that does not count as a proper *ne'ikar* — a sufficient mislabeling of ten. Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda disagrees and says this does count as a valid mislabeling, and eleven therefore becomes *kadosh*.

Rabbi's reasoning is that calling ten "eleven" — labeling it with a number that hasn't been reached yet — is a meaningless act. One cannot really be said to be denying ten its status by assigning it a label that belongs to the future. Calling ten "nine," by contrast, is a meaningful mislabeling: nine has already been reached, and asserting that ten is nine is an intelligible, if wrong, claim about the animal's place in the sequence. Calling it "eleven" before eleven has been encountered is too premature to constitute a genuine uprooting.

### Rava on Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי, כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי הֵיכָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּהֵמוֹת טוּבָא, דְּאָמְרִינַן: חַד עִישּׂוּרָא קָאָמַר, הָכָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ בְּהֵמוֹת טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, as **when Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said** his statement he was referring only to a case **where** the owner **has many animals** to tithe. The reason is **that we say** that when he said: *Aḥad asar*, eleventh, his intention was to **say: *Ḥad issura*,** one group of ten, i.e., that this tenth animal completes one set of ten animals, not that it is the eleventh. **Here** it is referring to a situation **where he does not have many animals** to tithe, and therefore he could not have been speaking of more than one set of ten, in which case Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees that calling it the eleventh removes its status as the tenth.

Rava, however, limits the scope of Rabbi's ruling. Rabbi's position — that calling ten "eleven" is not a valid mislabeling — applies specifically *heicha de'ika beheimos tuva*, when one has many animals. Why? Because when one has a large flock and is counting through multiple groups of ten, the phrase *achad asar* in Hebrew is genuinely ambiguous: it can mean "eleven" or it can mean "one ten" — that is, one of my groups of ten. In a large counting with many animals, calling ten *achad asar* might simply mean "this is one of my tens," and therefore the statement is not sufficiently explicit to count as a mislabeling of the genuine *maaser*.

### Few Animals Case

But if one has few animals — fewer than twenty, such that there can be no second group of ten — then *achad asar* is unambiguous and could not possibly mean anything other than eleven. In that case, calling ten "eleven" would constitute a proper mislabeling, and Rabbi would concede that eleven becomes *kadosh*. Rabbi's ruling, properly understood, is not a categorical statement about the word "eleven"; it is a context-sensitive determination about whether the designation is sufficiently clear.

## Mixed Offering Problem

### Ritual Differences

The final topic for this daf concerns the practical consequences that arise when *maaser* and *shelamim* become mixed up — specifically, in the case where ten and eleven both exit together, are both called "ten," and one therefore has a *maaser* animal and a *shelamim* animal without knowing which is which. The ritual requirements for the two *korbanos* differ in critical ways. *Shelamim* requires two blood-throws (*matanos*), while *maaser* requires only one. For *shelamim*, the breast and thigh (*chazeh v'hashok*) are given to the *kohanim*; for *maaser behemah*, they belong to the owner. These distinctions create a genuine dilemma when one cannot identify which animal is which.

### Tenth & 11th Mixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בָּעֲשִׂירִי וּקְרָאָן ״עֲשִׂירִי״, עֲשִׂירִי וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** If **two** animals **emerged** from the pen together **as the tenth, and he called them** both the **tenth,** then the **tenth, and** the **eleventh** animal, which he designated as the tenth, **are intermingled with each other.**

When ten and eleven both exit simultaneously and are both called "ten," the result is *asiri v'achad asar me'uravin zeh b'zeh* — the *maaser* and the *shelamim* are mixed together. In principle, one would like to bring both as *korbanos*, which is permissible since both are *kadosh* and both can be offered. But the uncertainty about which is the *maaser* and which is the *shelamim* creates the problem.

### Graze Until Blemished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בָּעֲשִׂירִי, תָּנֵי חֲדָא: יִרְעוּ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: יִקְרְיבוּ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: יָמוּתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the earlier discussion. The *baraita* teaches: If **two** animals **emerged** from the pen together **as the tenth,** and one did not come out before the other, and he called them both the tenth, the tenth and eleventh animals are intermingled with each other. The Gemara notes that there is apparently a dispute between *tanna'im* as to how these animals must be treated. **One *tanna*** taught that both animals must be left to **graze** until they develop a blemish, and then they may be eaten by the owner. **And one *tanna*** taught that both animals must **be sacrificed. And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that both animals **must die.**

A *braita* cites three positions. One view (*Rabbanan*) holds that both animals must be left to graze (*yeiru*) until they develop a blemish, after which they are redeemed and eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash* as ordinary meat. The reason is the principle *ein mevi'in kodshim l'veis hap'sul* — one may not bring *korbanos* in circumstances that risk a forbidden outcome.

### Priestly Eating Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּתַנְיָא יִרְעוּ — רַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: אֵין מְבִיאִין קָדָשִׁים לְבֵית הַפְּסוּל״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction. It is **not difficult: This** ruling, of the *tanna* **who taught** that both animals must be left to **graze** until they develop a blemish, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say: One may not bring sacrificial** animals **to a situation** where the time in which they may be eaten is decreased, thereby increasing **the** likelihood of **disqualification.** If both animals were to be sacrificed they would have to be eaten in accordance with the stringencies of both the animal tithe offering and the peace offering. They would have to be eaten within two days, like a peace offering, while the breast and the thigh would be given to the priests, as in the case of an animal tithe offering. Since only priests may eat those portions it is possible that they would be unable to eat them within the permitted time, and the remaining meat would become disqualified.

The specific risk here is the following. For *shelamim*, the breast and thigh go to the *kohanim* and may be eaten only by male *kohanim*. For *maaser*, those portions belong to the owner, who can invite family and friends to share the meal and ensure it is consumed within the required time. Since one does not know which animal is the *shelamim*, one must, out of caution, give the breast and thigh of both to the *kohanim*. But the *kohanim* — particularly a large priestly family (*beis av*) receiving a steady stream of *korbanos* — may have a glut of meat and may not manage to eat all of it within the permitted time. This would result in *notar*, meat left unconsumed beyond its permitted period, a serious *issur*. To avoid risking this outcome, the *Rabbanan* say one should not bring them at all, but let them graze until blemished.

### Both as Korbanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתָנֵי יִקְרְיבוּ — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מְבִיאִין קָדָשִׁים לְבֵית הַפְּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the ruling of **this** *tanna*, **who teaches:** Both animals must **be sacrificed, is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **one may bring sacrificial** animals **to a situation** where **the** likelihood of **disqualification** is increased.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He holds that one does bring both animals as *korbanos* despite the risk of portions not being consumed. His position is that the principle of *ein mevi'in kodshim l'veis hap'sul* does not override the obligation to bring *korbanos* that are available, and one relies on the *kohanim* to eat what is given to them in time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתָנֵי יָמוּתוּ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: טָעוּת מַעֲשֵׂר, תְּמוּרָה הָוְיָא, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: תְּמוּרַת מַעֲשֵׂר מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the ruling of **this** *tanna*, **who teaches:** Both animals **must die, is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says:** When the owner makes **a mistake** in designating animal **tithe,** e.g., if one designates the ninth animal as the tenth, the designated animal has the status of **a substitute** animal. And **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **a substitute for** an animal **tithe must** be left to **die.** Since it is uncertain which animal is the eleventh, both are left to die.

A third position in the *braita* — that both animals should be left to die (*yamusu*) — will be explained at the opening of the next daf.
