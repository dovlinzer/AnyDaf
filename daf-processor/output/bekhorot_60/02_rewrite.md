# Bekhorot 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava: All in Pen

### All Animals in Pen

We open on the penultimate daf of *Bekhorot* — 60a — with Rava's teaching on how to properly perform *maaser behemah*. If one has fifteen animals, Rava's first ruling is that one may not simply select ten and tithe from them alone; all fifteen must be placed into the pen. Rashi specifies that the prohibition applies in particular to selecting the worst ten, though the underlying principle appears broader: the *maaser* process must encompass one's entire flock without any deliberate selection. The animals must all enter the pen together and emerge in a randomized, undirected sequence.

### Leftovers Combine

Rava's second principle addresses what happens to the five animals that remain after the ten have exited and the tithe has been taken. One might have assumed that these five are now entirely exempt — perhaps a kind of remainder that falls outside any further obligation. Rava rules otherwise: if *Rosh Hashanah* has not yet arrived, those five do not simply disappear from the *maaser* calculus. They must combine with whatever animals are born thereafter, and the owner takes *maaser* from the combined group in the following year.

### Braita on Fifteen Sheep

A *braita* at the bottom of 59b confirms both of these points: *tanya nami hachi* — we have been taught similarly. If one had fifteen sheep, one may not say, "I will select ten, take the *maaser* from them, and let the remaining five go free." Rather, he brings all of them into the pen (*ela konasom ladir*), ten exit (*umotzi asarah*), one is taken (*venoteil mehen echad*), and the remaining five must combine with the next *goren* (*vehashar mitztaref legoren acher*).

### Rashi on Goren Restricti…

Rashi raises a difficulty: is this *chiddush* truly new? We already learned in an earlier *mishnah* that if one has five animals from one *goren* and five from another, they combine. We already knew that a remainder under ten cannot simply be ignored — it must await further combinations. What then is the novelty here?

Rashi introduces a far-reaching *chiddush* of his own — one that is not stated explicitly anywhere in the *Gemara*. When those fifteen animals were all gathered together and the *goren* period arrived, and then the *maaser* was taken and five remained, those five are not merely obligated to combine with the next *goren*; they are also subject to the *goren* restriction immediately. That is, since they were present at the *goren*, one may not *shecht* them, sell them, or derive any benefit from them until they are combined with the next *goren* and *maaser* is properly taken. Even though all fifteen were in the pen together and the *maaser* process was performed correctly, the leftover five are frozen in a state of *goren* restriction.

This is a substantial *chiddush*, and Rashi acknowledges that it does not appear in the *Gemara* text itself. His motivation seems to be that the *braita* must be telling us something beyond what we already knew, and so he imports this additional dimension. Whether Rava himself intended this reading remains uncertain, but it is what Rashi reads into the *chiddush*.

It is worth noting that this *perek* is unusual in its physical layout: the pages are smaller because they contain a second edition of Rashi, labeled *Rashi Ksav Yad*. The standard Rashi printed here is noticeably more verbose than is typical, which raises the possibility that it may represent an earlier draft, or that questions of authorship are involved. This is a topic worth investigating further in works that survey the scholarship on such textual questions — such as Strack and Stemberger's introduction to the Talmud, which surveys the academic discussion on these issues in a rigorous and accessible way.

## Nineteen Sheep Problem

### Braita: 19 Sheep

The *Gemara* now cites a *braita* that presents a new case. If one has nineteen sheep, one may not say: "I will put ten into the pen, take one, and the remaining nine are exempt." Rather, all nineteen must be placed in the pen (*ela konesan ladir*), ten exit (*umotzi asarah*), one is taken (*venoteil mehen echad*) — and then the *braita* states: *vehashar peturin*, the remaining nine are exempt.

### Exempt vs. Combine

This creates an apparent contradiction. On one hand, we just established from Rava that leftover animals are not exempt but must combine with the next *goren*. On the other hand, this *braita* explicitly says the nine that remain are exempt. How are these two positions reconciled?

### Two-Door Resolution

The resolution was offered by Rav Huna bar Sechorah in the presence of Rava himself — apparently at a public *drasha* held during the period before the *regel*, when the *halachot* of *maaser behemah* were expounded since that was precisely when the tithing obligation arose. (One tradition among the *Geonim* identifies *Shabbat derigla* not as the Shabbat before the *regel* but as the Shabbat observing the *yahrtzeit* of a great figure who had passed that year — making the setting of this exchange potentially more intimate.)

Rav Huna bar Sechorah's answer: *bedir she'yesh lo shenei petachim askinan* — we are dealing with a pen that has two doors. Nine animals exited through one door, and nine animals exited through the other (*ve'yatze'u tishah b'petach zeh ve'tishah b'petach zeh*). One animal remains inside. That last animal — *dehach chad dechazi le'hacha u'le'hacha* — could have joined either group, and therefore each group of nine qualifies as a *minyan harauy*: a group that was capable of being a valid count of ten. Accordingly, when the *braita* says the remaining nine are exempt, it does not mean the nine still in the pen; it means the nine that exited the other door. Each group of nine is exempt by virtue of *minyan harauy* — not because they are truly leftover, but because each set could have been part of a complete set of ten.

A related question arises: when there is one animal left in the pen, and Rava holds that the tenth animal is defined by its objective reality (*asiri me'elav kadosh*), what happens if you know this is the tenth but cannot determine which group's tenth it belongs to? Rashi resolves this by citing Rava's principle: since we know this animal is objectively the tenth, it automatically becomes *maaser* — even if we do not know to which group's counting it belongs.

### Pairs Counting Option

The *Gemara* then poses an alternative: why not resolve the contradiction differently? Why not say that the person counted nine animals, and when the tenth arrived, accidentally called it "one" and began counting again — so the nineteenth animal was the one called "ten" in the second sequence, while the first nine were exempt due to *minyan harauy*? Why was the two-door scenario necessary?

### Rav Nachman's Praise

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar asiri me'elav kadosh* — this would be consistent with Rava's own position that the tenth animal is automatically *kadosh* regardless of what it is called. If that is so, then calling the tenth animal "one" would not strip it of its sanctity; it would be *kadosh* regardless, leaving *maaser* and *chullin* mixed up with no resolution. Similarly, a suggestion that the animals were counted in pairs (*zugot zugot*) is entertained but ultimately also runs into Rava's principle — the *Gemara* will return to this — and so the two-door scenario remains the only clean answer.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then offers a ringing endorsement: *zakai imei d'Rav Huna bar Schora* — may the merit of Rav Huna bar Sechorah's mother be great. Not only did he publicly defend Rava's ruling before a large audience during the *regel*, but he defended Rava using Rava's own *svara*. His answer turned on *minyan harauy* — one of Rava's core concepts — and implicitly relied on Rava's principle that objective reality defines the *maaser*, not the counting, which is precisely why other proposed answers had to be rejected. His defense was internally consistent with Rava's entire framework.

## Tenth's Reality

### Reality vs. Designation

We arrive at the central conceptual question that will occupy the *Gemara* through the end of the *masechet*: when two animals emerge simultaneously, there is no objectively determinable tenth — the two exited together and we cannot know which crossed the threshold first. This raises the possibility that precisely where the physical reality does not clearly designate one animal as the tenth, the automatic *kedushah* of Rava's principle may not apply. It is possible to argue that even under *ei efshar letzamtzem*, one of the two was indeed a fraction of a moment ahead — but we simply do not know which.

This deeper tension — between what the owner designates through counting and what is determined by objective physical reality — will now be explored through the next *mishnah*. As a preliminary orientation: the *mishnah* we already learned makes clear that calling nine "ten" or eleven "ten" has real *halachic* consequences, and so what one says cannot be entirely irrelevant. At the same time, Rava teaches that the tenth is *kadosh* without any counting at all. The interplay between these two principles — the power of designation and the power of physical reality — is precisely what the *Gemara* is now working through.

## Ninth/Tenth/Eleventh

### Miscounting All Three

The *mishnah* sets out the following case: one called the ninth animal "ten," called the tenth "nine," and called the eleventh "ten." The *mishnah* rules: *sheloshtan mekudashim* — all three are *kadosh*.

### Ninth: Kadosh, No Korban

The ninth becomes *kadosh* by virtue of having been called "ten." However, it cannot be brought as a *korban*: the designation was made before a genuine tenth existed, so it lacks the full power of *maaser*. It must wait until it develops a blemish (*mum*), at which point it may be eaten in its blemished state.

### Tenth: Full Maaser

The tenth is *maaser* in the fullest sense — objectively, by virtue of its position in the sequence, regardless of what it was called.

### Eleventh: Shelamim

The eleventh becomes *kadosh* from having been designated "ten," and since it follows the genuine tenth and carries *kedushah* directly — though not the *kedushah* of *maaser* itself — it is brought as a *shelamim*. The *korban* most analogous to *maaser behemah* in its rules is *shelamim*: eaten by the owners for two days and the intervening night, classified as *kodshim kalim*.

A clarification is necessary here: there is no such thing as *temuras maaser*. Ordinarily, one can stand a *shelamim* before another animal, declare *temura*, and thereby transfer the *kedushah*. With *maaser*, this is unavailable — it is derived from a *pasuk* and will be discussed further in *Maseches Temurah*. The eleventh is therefore not a *temura* of *maaser*; it has its own direct *kedushah*, which resembles *maaser* sufficiently that it is treated as *shelamim*.

The *mishnah* further states: *ve'oseh temura* — if the owner then took yet another animal and declared a transfer of *kedushah* from the eleventh onto it, he has transgressed the prohibition of *temura*, and the new animal also becomes *kadosh*.

### Temurah Debate

This last ruling is the subject of a dispute. Rabbi Meir holds that the eleventh can produce a *temura* — the new animal becomes *kadosh*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *v'chi yesh temura osah temura?* Can a *temura* produce another *temura*? In his view, the eleventh itself is conceptually a *temura* of the *maaser* — modeled on the mechanism by which *kedushah* is transferred from one animal to another by means of mislabeling. And since *temura* cannot produce another *temura*, the eleventh cannot extend its *kedushah* to a fourth animal.

Rabbi Meir's rejoinder — stated in his name by others — is that if the eleventh were truly a *temura*, it could not be brought as a *korban* at all, since *temuras maaser* cannot be brought as a *korban*. The fact that it is brought as a *shelamim* proves it is not a *temura* but a direct, autonomous *kedushah*. Therefore, it can produce a *temura*.

The underlying conceptual debate is significant: by what mechanism does the eleventh become *kadosh*? In Rabbi Yehuda's view, it is because the owner attempted to remove the *kedushah* from ten and transfer it to eleven — a process modeled on *temura*, even if not identical to it. In Rabbi Meir's view, calling eleven "ten" constitutes an independent act of *kedushah* — not a transfer but a direct sanctification, made possible by the fact that ten had already been mislabeled as nine, removing the competition that would otherwise render the designation of eleven meaningless.

### Uprooting the Tenth

This leads to the principle stated at the end of the *mishnah*: if one called nine "ten," ten "ten," and eleven "ten" — meaning ten was never mislabeled — then *ein achad asar mikudash*: the eleventh does not become *kadosh*. The governing rule is: *kol shelo ne'ekar shem asiri mimenu, ein achad asar kadosh* — if you do not uproot the name of ten, eleven does not become holy.

This principle is intuitive: the act of calling eleven "ten" is meaningful only if the claim that ten is "ten" has been contested. If ten has already been acknowledged as *maaser*, then calling eleven "ten" is simply an empty statement that conflicts with what everyone knows to be true. The designation carries no weight. Only when the owner has actively mislabeled ten — denied it its status — does the subsequent designation of eleven as "ten" have the power to confer *kedushah*.

## Tenth's Reality…

### Rava: Tenth Auto-Kadosh

Rava now states his position explicitly. The tenth animal is *kadosh* automatically — *asiri me'elav kadosh* — regardless of how it is labeled. If one counted ten animals and called the tenth "one," it is still *kadosh*. This is Rava's consistent view: not only is one's counting unnecessary to confer *kedushah* on the tenth, but one's counting cannot subvert it either. Whether one calls it "nine," "eleven," "one," or any other designation, if it is objectively the tenth animal to exit, it is *kadosh*.

## Grouping Debate

### Counting by Groupings

Rabbi Yochanan's teaching: if one counted the animals in pairs (*zugot zugot*) or by hundreds (*kintrin kintrin*, from the Latin *centenarius*), the tenth of one's counting is *kadosh* (*asiri leminyano hu kadosh*). The precise meaning of this statement, however, is disputed.

### Grouping vs. Sequential

Rav Mari understands Rabbi Yochanan to mean that one's chosen grouping defines the counting. If one counted by pairs, the tenth pair consists of two animals simultaneously exiting, and both become *kadosh* — two *maasrot* in a single moment. The owner's decision to regroup the animals as pairs is treated as a legitimate redefinition, and the tenth of that grouping, however many animals it contains, is the *maaser*. Rav Kahana, by contrast, understands Rabbi Yochanan to mean that one's designation is entirely irrelevant: *leminyan behemah hu kadosh* — the tenth animal to exit is *kadosh*, period. No regrouping alters this.

The *mishnah*'s own ruling creates pressure on Rav Kahana's position: if one designates two animals as a single count when they exit together and calls them "five" instead of "five and six," the *mishnah* says that nine and ten become *mekulkalim* — confused. This implies that what one says has some consequence. If Rav Kahana were entirely correct that designation is ignored, it should not matter at all what one calls any animal.

### Regrouping Ignored

The *Gemara* resolves this tension by limiting Rav Kahana's position carefully. According to his reading of Rabbi Yochanan: when one is deliberately attempting to regroup the animals — *heicha de'ichaven le'apukei zuga zuga* — counting intentionally by twos or hundreds, that entire framework is disregarded. Only sequential one-by-one counting is recognized; any deliberate restructuring of the counting unit is ignored entirely. However, when the confusion arises accidentally — two animals exit at once without intention, and one miscalls them — the error is not entirely without consequence. Because one is operating within the framework of sequential counting and made a mistake, one's mislabeling does have some impact, precisely what the *mishnah* reflects. This is admittedly a narrow and counterintuitive position: intentional regrouping is ignored, while an accidental mislabeling matters. The *Gemara* maintains this distinction because the *mishnah* requires it.

## Tenth's Reality……

### Backward Counting

A *braita* is cited: if one counted the animals backward — ten, nine, eight, seven, down to one — the tenth of that counting is *kadosh*, i.e., the animal one called "one." The *Gemara* notes that this poses no difficulty for Rav Kahana's position: he ignores one's designation entirely and says the tenth animal to exit is *kadosh* regardless of what it was called. But for Rav Mari — who says we follow the owner's counting — why should calling something "one" make it *kadosh*? One called it "one," not "ten."

Rava offers a creative resolution: *achva de'ita biminyana parsa'ah* — this is consistent with Persian usage, where the word for ten is cognate with or expressed as "one" (*dekaru le'asara chad*). In Persian idiom, the owner's statement of "one" at the tenth position could be understood as meaning ten. The *Gemara* does not press further, though it is evident that this answer is somewhat forced. A more principled response might have been that even counting backward preserves an identifiable tenth position in the sequence, which is distinct from a case of deliberate regrouping into pairs or hundreds. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* concludes with Rava's answer.

Throughout this discussion, the core tension is held in view: the *mishnah* makes clear that miscounting — calling nine "ten" or eleven "ten" — has real *halachic* consequences. Meanwhile, Rava insists that the tenth is *kadosh* independently of any designation. The open question, which continues to drive the *Gemara*'s analysis, is how these two forces — designation and objective reality — interact, and where each one takes precedence.

## Simultaneous Exit

### 9 & 10 Together

Rava presents a series of cases involving simultaneous exit. First: nine and ten exit together, and one calls them both "nine." The result is *asiri v'tishi'i me'uravin zeh b'zeh* — the *maaser* animal and the *chullin* animal are mixed up, since ten is objectively *kadosh* (*asiri me'elav kadosh*) regardless of what it was called, and nine is *chullin*. Not knowing which is which, one must wait for a *mum* to develop on both and then treat them accordingly.

### Both Called 10

Second: nine and ten exit together, and one calls them both "ten." Now the mix is different. Ten is *kadosh* as *maaser*, and nine — having been called "ten" — acquires *kedushah* of a kind that cannot be brought as a *korban* (since it was designated before a genuine tenth existed). The two animals — *maaser* and this non-*korban kedushah* — are mixed together. Once again, one waits for a *mum*.

### 10 & 11 Together

Third: ten and eleven exit together, and one calls them both "ten." Here an important principle emerges, prompted precisely by the question raised earlier: the *mishnah* states that eleven becomes *kadosh* only when the name of ten has been uprooted. But in this case, ten was not mislabeled — it was called "ten," which it genuinely is. How then does eleven acquire *kedushah*?

### No Competition Principle

The answer is that the *ne'ikar shem asiri* requirement — the need to uproot ten's designation — is not about *temura*-style transfer but about removing the competition that the real tenth presents to the eleventh's claim to *kedushah*. When ten and eleven exit sequentially, calling eleven "ten" is meaningless because ten has already secured its designation as *maaser*, and eleven cannot compete against it. The *ne'ikar* requirement exists to neutralize that competition: by mislabeling ten, the owner removes its established status, and calling eleven "ten" then carries weight.

But when ten and eleven exit simultaneously, there is no competition to begin with. Neither has been designated before the other; the owner is calling both "ten" at exactly the same moment. In this case, *tarvaihu kadshi* — both are *kadosh*. Ten is *maaser*, and eleven acquires *kedushah* to be brought as a *shelamim*, without any need to have mislabeled ten.

### Sequential vs. Simultane…

The *Gemara* confirms this by noting that the *mishnah*'s general principle — *kol shelo ne'ekar shem asiri mimenu, ein achad asar kadosh* — was stated as a *klal* after giving a specific sequential case. When one already gave the specific case of sequential miscounting (ten-ten-ten), the *klal* was added to extend the rule further. What does it include? The *Gemara* concludes: it comes to include the case where ten exits and one says nothing (*lo diber*), and then calls eleven "ten." Even silence about ten is insufficient to count as uprooting its name; one must actively mislabel it. If ten walks out and one remains silent, ten is still objectively *kadosh*, and eleven's subsequent designation carries no weight. To confer *kedushah* on eleven through the sequential process, one must affirmatively assign the wrong label to ten.

## Mislabeling Definition

### Silence on Tenth

The question now posed is: what exactly counts as mislabeling ten? If the tenth animal exits and one says nothing at all — neither calling it ten nor assigning it any other number — and then when the eleventh exits one calls it "ten," has one been *oker shem asiri*? Intuitively one might think so: ten was not acknowledged, and eleven was called ten. Is that sufficient?

### Active Mislabeling Requi…

The *Gemara* answers: no. To be *oker shem asiri*, one must actively assign a wrong label to ten. Mere silence does not suffice. The rationale is that ten, as the genuine *maaser*, retains its objective status whether or not one speaks. Simply declining to acknowledge it leaves it fully intact as *kadosh*, and the subsequent designation of eleven as "ten" cannot override the standing of an animal that has already — silently but really — become *maaser*. Active mislabeling is required: one must call ten something it is not, thereby making a positive (if false) claim about its status that opens space for eleven's designation to be meaningful.

## Calling 11 as 10

### Rabbi Limits Mislabel

A *braita* presents a dispute. Rabbi (Rebbi) holds that if one calls ten "eleven" and then calls eleven "ten," that does not count as a proper *ne'ikar* — a sufficient mislabeling of ten. Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda disagrees and says this does count as a valid mislabeling, and eleven therefore becomes *kadosh*.

Rabbi's reasoning is that calling ten "eleven" — labeling it with a number that hasn't been reached yet — is a meaningless act. One cannot really be said to be denying ten its status by assigning it a label that belongs to the future. Calling ten "nine," by contrast, is a meaningful mislabeling: nine has already been reached, and asserting that ten is nine is an intelligible, if wrong, claim about the animal's place in the sequence. Calling it "eleven" before eleven has been encountered is too premature to constitute a genuine uprooting.

### Rava on Rabbi

Rava, however, limits the scope of Rabbi's ruling. Rabbi's position — that calling ten "eleven" is not a valid mislabeling — applies specifically *heicha de'ika beheimos tuva*, when one has many animals. Why? Because when one has a large flock and is counting through multiple groups of ten, the phrase *achad asar* in Hebrew is genuinely ambiguous: it can mean "eleven" or it can mean "one ten" — that is, one of my groups of ten. In a large counting with many animals, calling ten *achad asar* might simply mean "this is one of my tens," and therefore the statement is not sufficiently explicit to count as a mislabeling of the genuine *maaser*.

### Few Animals Case

But if one has few animals — fewer than twenty, such that there can be no second group of ten — then *achad asar* is unambiguous and could not possibly mean anything other than eleven. In that case, calling ten "eleven" would constitute a proper mislabeling, and Rabbi would concede that eleven becomes *kadosh*. Rabbi's ruling, properly understood, is not a categorical statement about the word "eleven"; it is a context-sensitive determination about whether the designation is sufficiently clear.

## Mixed Offering Problem

### Ritual Differences

The final topic for this daf concerns the practical consequences that arise when *maaser* and *shelamim* become mixed up — specifically, in the case where ten and eleven both exit together, are both called "ten," and one therefore has a *maaser* animal and a *shelamim* animal without knowing which is which. The ritual requirements for the two *korbanos* differ in critical ways. *Shelamim* requires two blood-throws (*matanos*), while *maaser* requires only one. For *shelamim*, the breast and thigh (*chazeh v'hashok*) are given to the *kohanim*; for *maaser behemah*, they belong to the owner. These distinctions create a genuine dilemma when one cannot identify which animal is which.

### Tenth & 11th Mixed

When ten and eleven both exit simultaneously and are both called "ten," the result is *asiri v'achad asar me'uravin zeh b'zeh* — the *maaser* and the *shelamim* are mixed together. In principle, one would like to bring both as *korbanos*, which is permissible since both are *kadosh* and both can be offered. But the uncertainty about which is the *maaser* and which is the *shelamim* creates the problem.

### Graze Until Blemished

A *braita* cites three positions. One view (*Rabbanan*) holds that both animals must be left to graze (*yeiru*) until they develop a blemish, after which they are redeemed and eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash* as ordinary meat. The reason is the principle *ein mevi'in kodshim l'veis hap'sul* — one may not bring *korbanos* in circumstances that risk a forbidden outcome.

### Priestly Eating Risk

The specific risk here is the following. For *shelamim*, the breast and thigh go to the *kohanim* and may be eaten only by male *kohanim*. For *maaser*, those portions belong to the owner, who can invite family and friends to share the meal and ensure it is consumed within the required time. Since one does not know which animal is the *shelamim*, one must, out of caution, give the breast and thigh of both to the *kohanim*. But the *kohanim* — particularly a large priestly family (*beis av*) receiving a steady stream of *korbanos* — may have a glut of meat and may not manage to eat all of it within the permitted time. This would result in *notar*, meat left unconsumed beyond its permitted period, a serious *issur*. To avoid risking this outcome, the *Rabbanan* say one should not bring them at all, but let them graze until blemished.

### Both as Korbanos

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He holds that one does bring both animals as *korbanos* despite the risk of portions not being consumed. His position is that the principle of *ein mevi'in kodshim l'veis hap'sul* does not override the obligation to bring *korbanos* that are available, and one relies on the *kohanim* to eat what is given to them in time.

A third position in the *braita* — that both animals should be left to die (*yamusu*) — will be explained at the opening of the next daf.
