# Hullin 44b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Talmid Chacham & Honesty

### Honest Ruling Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵיזֶהוּ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם? זֶה הָרוֹאֶה טְרֵפָה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Who is a Torah scholar? This is one who sees his own *tereifa*.** In other words, when the status of his own animal is uncertain, he deems it prohibited without concern for his own monetary loss.

The sugya opens with a statement of Rav Chisda: *eizehu talmid chacham? zeh haroeh treifah le'atzmo* — who is considered a genuine *talmid chacham*? One who rules honestly on his own animal's *treifot*. The placement of this teaching is initially jarring: it appears amid five lines of *aggadata* before the Gemara returns to the technical laws of *treifot*, and the transition feels abrupt. Yet the statement itself repays careful attention.

### Intellectual vs. Honesty

Rashi explains that a true *talmid chacham* is someone so honest and truthful that he is comfortable ruling on questions of *kashrut* affecting his own animal, because he knows that if the animal is *treif*, he will say so without flinching. The implicit corollary is striking: the standard being celebrated here is not intellectual prowess at all, but moral integrity. This raises an obvious and somewhat unsettling question. Is the definition of a *talmid chacham* really so low a bar? Should we not take honesty entirely for granted in a person of learning?

The discomfort runs deeper still. In our world we tend to measure a *talmid chacham* by how much he knows, not by whether he is honest. That sad observation may itself be the Gemara's point. The Gemara is deliberately pushing our buttons, forcing us to confront the relationship between intellectual mastery and moral character — and implying that the two are not nearly as conjoined as we might assume. Historically, the stakes were real: meat was expensive and rarely eaten; the one time a family *shechted* an animal and discovered a potential *treifah*, the temptation to rule leniently — particularly on one's own animal — must have been enormous. The Gemara also recalls a parallel passage in *zevachim* where the Sages found that adding *tumah* as a consequence of *notar* and *pigul* was more effective as a deterrent than the much graver consequence of *karet* — suggesting that different forms of concern operated differently on people, and that financial or quasi-superstitious consequences could be more practically motivating than purely religious ones.

### Tosafot's Solution

Tosafot are similarly bothered by the conflation of intellectual standing with personal honesty. They resolve it by pointing to the Gemara in *bava metzia*: ordinarily a lost object is returned only upon the presentation of a *siman*, yet the Gemara there allows a *talmid chacham* to recover a lost object on the basis of *tvi'at ayin* alone — recognition by sight. The premise underlying that leniency is precisely that a *talmid chacham* known to be religiously honest can be trusted in financial matters as well. The two domains are thus connected: reliability in *issur ve-heter* is the foundation for reliability in *mamon*. This is how Tosafot explain why the Gemara defines *talmid chacham* in terms of honesty rather than erudition.

### Soneh Matanos Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵיזֶהוּ ״שׂוֹנֵא מַתָּנֹת יִחְיֶה״? זֶה הָרוֹאֶה טְרֵפָה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says: Who is** referred to by the verse: **"He that hates gifts shall live"** (Proverbs 15:27)? **This is one who sees his own *tereifa*.** He is careful to avoid deriving benefit from that which is not his own, and even from items that are his concerning which it is questionable whether or not they are permitted.

The Gemara then records a second teaching of Rav Chisda: *eizehu soneh matanos yichyeh* — who truly fulfills the verse in *mishlei* that "one who hates gifts will live"? Again the answer is *zeh haroeh treifah le'atzmo*. The logic of the connection is as follows: a person who is scrupulously honest even when honesty costs him money — declaring his own animal *treif* at personal financial loss — is precisely the kind of person who is not driven by material gain. Such a person would naturally refuse gifts, and would fulfill *soneh matanos yichyeh* at the highest level. The Gemara is thus drawing a portrait of integrated character: honesty about *treifot* is not an isolated virtue but flows from, and expresses, a broader orientation toward financial disinterest.

## Learning Framework

### Three Learning Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל מִי שֶׁקּוֹרֵא וְשׁוֹנֶה, וְרוֹאֶה טְרֵפָה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְשִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים – עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״יְגִיעַ כַּפֶּיךָ כִּי תֹאכֵל אַשְׁרֶיךָ וְטוֹב לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra taught in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Anyone who reads** the Torah **and studies** the Mishna, **and sees his own *tereifa*, and has served Torah scholars** to learn the ways of halakhic judgment, **about him the verse states: "When you eat the labor of your hands, happy shall you be, and it shall be well with you"** (Psalms 128:2).

The Gemara continues with a teaching of Mar Zutra in the name of Rav Chisda: anyone who *koreh*, *shoneh*, and *meshamesh talmidei chachamim*, and who rules honestly on *treifot* affecting his own animals — of such a person the verse says, *yegia kapecha ki tochal, ashrecha ve'tov lach*. The three-part formula — *koreh*, *shoneh*, *meshamesh talmidei chachamim* — invites interpretation. The most natural reading maps it onto the three levels of Torah study: *koreh* is *mikra*, *shoneh* is *mishnah*, and *meshamesh talmidei chachamim* is *talmud* — meaning, in the plain sense, attendance upon Torah scholars to observe and absorb their way of life.

Rashi, however, takes a characteristically consistent if surprising position. Throughout *shas*, wherever the phrase *shimush talmidei chachamim* appears, Rashi interprets it not as literally serving or accompanying scholars in their daily lives, but as learning *gemara* — that is, listening to *talmidei chachamim* debate halacha. For Rashi, the three stages are therefore *chumash* and *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *gemara*. Most *rishonim* take the literal reading: being around *talmidei chachamim* and learning from how they conduct themselves. Rashi dissents, maintaining the parallel with the other two stages: all three are forms of textual study. The question of which form of learning yields more — sitting with a great scholar and observing how he lives, or laboring over the *gemara* itself — is one that the passage raises without quite answering.

### Reward Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: זוֹכֶה וְנוֹחֵל שְׁנֵי עוֹלָמוֹת, הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְהָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, ״אַשְׁרֶיךָ״ – בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וְטוֹב לָךְ״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid says:** Such a person **merits inheriting two worlds, this world and the World-to-Come.** When the verse states: **"Happy shall you be,"** it means **in this world,** and when it states: **"And it shall be well with you,"** it is referring **to the World-to-Come.**

The verse cited as the reward for this threefold path — *yegia kapecha ki tochal, ashrecha ve'tov lach* — is one that *chazal* return to repeatedly, and the very fact that they must reinterpret it is itself noteworthy. On its face, *yegia kapecha ki tochal* extols self-sufficiency and the dignity of physical labor: eat from the fruit of your own hands. *chazal* were not entirely comfortable leaving it at that. A verse that praises manual labor without mentioning Torah or virtue seemed to them incomplete as a statement of Jewish values, and so they consistently read *yegia kapecha* metaphorically: the "labor of your hands" refers to the intellectual and spiritual toil of learning *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *gemara*, and the capacity that labor produces — the ability to *pasken* on *treifot* with both competence and integrity.

The closing phrase *ashrecha ve'tov lach* then receives its standard *chazalic* reading: *ashrecha ba'olam hazeh, ve'tov lach la'olam haba* — happiness in this world and goodness in the world to come. This formulation appears again in the Gemara's citation of Razvid, who renders it: one who follows this path inherits both worlds.

### Ascetic Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, כִּי הֲווֹ מְשַׁדְּרִי לֵיהּ מִבֵּי נְשִׂיאָה מִידֵּי, לָא שָׁקֵיל, וְכִי הֲווֹ מְזַמְּנִי לֵיהּ, לָא אָזֵיל. אָמַר: לָא קָא בָּעֵי מָר דְּאֵיחֵי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשׂוֹנֵא מַתָּנוֹת יִחְיֶה״. רַבִּי זֵירָא, כִּי מְשַׁדְּרִי לֵיהּ, לָא שָׁקֵיל, כִּי הֲווֹ מַזְמְנִין לֵיהּ, אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: "He that hates gifts shall live," the Gemara relates that **when they would send Rabbi Elazar some** gift **from the house of the *Nasi*, he would not take** it. **And when they would invite him, he would not go** there. When declining these offers, **he said** to them: **Does Master not desire that I live? As it is written: "He that hates gifts shall live."** By contrast, **when they would send** a gift **to Rabbi Zeira, he would not take** it, but **when they would invite him he would go.**

To illustrate the ideal of *soneh matanos yichyeh*, the Gemara presents two figures. Rabbi Elazar would refuse any gift sent from the *nasi*'s household, and would likewise decline any invitation to dine there — maintaining total financial independence from communal patronage. Rav Pinchas ben Yair was similarly cited in this vein, as an exemplar of extreme self-sufficiency grounded in the rejection of gifts.

### Social Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְיַיקּוֹרֵי הוּא דְּמִתְיַיקְּרוּ בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **It is an honor for them to honor me.** My attendance is not for my benefit but for theirs.

Rav Zeira adopted a more moderate position. When gifts were sent to him, he too would refuse them — consistent with *soneh matanos yichyeh* — but when he was invited to a meal, he would accept. His reason, however, was not primarily social. He explained: *ityakura hu de-mityakru bi* — they are honored by my presence. The invitation creates honor for the host rather than a benefit for the guest, and therefore accepting it does not compromise the principle of rejecting gifts. One might note the irony: the strictest reading of *soneh matanos yichyeh* taken literally forces one back to *yegia kapecha ki tochal* in its most literal sense — live entirely from what you earn — which is precisely the interpretation *chazal* were trying to move beyond. The two teachings circle back upon each other.

## Trachea Defects

### Two Treifah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת. תָּנָא: כַּמָּה פְּסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת? בְּרוּבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Or** an animal with **a cut windpipe,** cut across its width, is a *tereifa*. With regard to this the Sages **taught: How much** must **the windpipe** be **cut** to render the animal a *tereifa*? **In its majority.**

The Gemara returns to the *gargeret* — the trachea — and the conditions that render it *treif*. The *mishnah* establishes two categories. The first is *nifsakah ha-gargeret*: if the trachea has suffered a substantial split along its length, it is *treif*. The second is a hole: if the trachea is pierced with a hole of the size of an *issar* — a particular coin — that too renders it *treif*. These are two distinct standards, one measured by the length of the trachea (majority), and one by the circumference of a circular hole.

### Combining Small Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: נִיקְּבָה כְּנָפָה – מִצְטָרְפִים לְרוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If the windpipe **was perforated** with a series of small holes around its circumference **like a sieve,** the small holes **join together to** constitute **a majority** of the circumference. Therefore, if their collective size is a majority of the circumference, the windpipe is considered cut.

The Gemara raises a question that recurs throughout discussions of *treifot*: what happens when, instead of one large hole or one long split, the trachea is punctured with many small holes, each individually too small to constitute a *treifah*? Do they combine? And if so, toward which standard — toward the length of a *rov* of the trachea, or toward the circumference of a *k'issar*?

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules: *nikva k'napa* — if the trachea is perforated like a sieve, *mitzstarfin le-rovah* — the holes combine toward the majority of the trachea's length. This is the initial ruling. But the Gemara immediately notes a parallel case that seems to point in the opposite direction.

### Skull Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יִרְמְיָה: וּבְגוּלְגּוֹלֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ נֶקֶב אֶחָד אָרוֹךְ, אֲפִילּוּ יֵשׁ בָּהּ נְקָבִים הַרְבֵּה – מִצְטָרְפִים לִמְלֹא מַקְדֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya raises an objection:** The mishna teaches (*Oholot* 2:3) that if a skull of a corpse has a round hole at least the size of a drill bit used for surgery, then the skull does not impart ritual impurity in a tent. With regard to this, a *baraita* states: **And in a skull that contains one long hole,** or **even** if **it has many** small **holes,** the areas of the holes **join together to** constitute the **size of a drill hole.**

The parallel case involves a skull that contains multiple small holes. The halacha is that if a skull is perforated with holes equivalent in total to the size of a *k'mlo makdech* — the width of a doctor's drill bit — the skull has begun to disintegrate and no longer conveys *tumah*. Crucially, even multiple small holes may combine to reach that circular measurement. This appears to be the converse of what the Gemara just ruled regarding the trachea. There, small holes combine toward the standard of *rov* — the length of a split. Here, small holes combine toward the standard of *k'mlo makdech* — a circular hole.

### Yirmiya's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּשִׁיעוּרַהּ מְלֹא מַקְדֵּחַ – לִמְלֹא מַקְדֵּחַ מִצְטָרְפִין. הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּשִׁיעוּרֵיהּ כְּאִיסָּר – לִכְאִיסָּר מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Evidently, since the** requisite **measure is** the **size of a drill hole,** the small holes **join together to** constitute the **size of a drill hole,** and not another measure. If so, **here, too,** in the case of the windpipe, **since the** requisite **measure** of a hole to render the animal a *tereifa* is the size of **an *issar***, the small holes should **join together to** constitute the size of **an *issar*.** Why must they constitute the majority of the circumference?

Rav Yirmiya poses the challenge explicitly. In the case of the skull, even multiple small holes combine toward the circular measurement — *k'mlo makdech*. The reasoning seems to be: since the standard for a skull is a hole of that circular size, all holes combine toward that standard. By the same logic, since the standard for the trachea being *treif* via a hole is *k'issar*, should not small holes in the trachea combine toward *k'issar* rather than toward *rov*? Why does the Gemara apply one standard for combination in one place and the opposite standard in another?

### Chisaron Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: נְקָבִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן חֶסְרוֹן מִצְטָרְפִין לִכְאִיסָּר, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן חֶסְרוֹן מִצְטָרְפִין לְרוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It escaped him,** i.e., Rav Yirmeya, **that which Rabbi Ḥelbo says** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya says** that **Rav says: Perforations that are a deficiency,** i.e., holes of significant area, **join together to** constitute the size of **an *issar*, and** perforations **that are not a deficiency,** but are as small as the holes of a sieve, must **join together to** constitute **a majority** of the circumference, which is the measure of a cut windpipe. Since the holes are not substantial in area, the windpipe cannot be said to be missing a piece, but it may be considered cut.

The Gemara answers by citing a ruling in the name of Rav Chelbo quoting Rav Chama bar Guria quoting Rav: it depends on the nature of the holes. *nekavim she-yesh bahen chisaron* — holes in which there is actual *chisaron*, meaning tissue has been removed from the wall of the trachea — *mitzstarfin k'issar*: they combine toward the circular measurement of the *issar*. *she-ein bahen chisaron* — holes in which there is no *chisaron*, meaning the wall was pierced but no flesh was actually removed (the tissue was merely pushed aside) — *mitzstarfin le-rov*: they combine toward the majority standard of a split.

The logic, as best as anatomical terminology allows, seems to be that a hole accompanied by *chisaron* is structurally more similar to a cut-out hole, and so combines toward the *k'issar* measurement. A hole without *chisaron* behaves more like an elongated crack or split, and so its accumulation is measured against the *rov* standard. Whether this distinction reflects a coherent physiological principle or partakes of *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — since the line between the two hole-types may seem arbitrary to an untrained eye — is not resolved. The Gemara simply presents the distinction as normative.

This yields three operative rules for the trachea: a split along the majority of its length renders it *treif*; a single hole the size of an *issar* renders it *treif*; and multiple small holes combine either toward the *rov* standard or toward the *k'issar* standard depending on whether they involve *chisaron* or not.

### Repetitive Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוּ מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי: נִיקְּבָה כְּנָפָה מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: נְקָבִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן חִסָּרוֹן – מִצְטָרְפִין לִכְאִיסָּר, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן חִסָּרוֹן – מִצְטָרְפִין לְרוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani asked Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi:** If the windpipe **was perforated like a sieve, what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said to him:** The Sages **said** with regard to this case: **Perforations that are a deficiency join together to** constitute the size of **an *issar*, and** perforations **that are not a deficiency join together to** constitute **a majority** of the circumference.

Rav Yitzchak bar Nachmani subsequently poses the question *nekava k'nafa mai hi* — what is the law if the trachea is perforated like a sieve? — before the Gemara, which has just answered precisely this question. The response, *harei amru*, is delivered with unmistakable impatience: "They have already said!" The Gemara repeats the ruling — holes with *chisaron* combine toward *k'issar*, holes without *chisaron* combine toward *rov* — as though the earlier discussion had settled the matter entirely. The tone of the exchange is notable: the Gemara's *harei amru* reads less like a polite citation and more like a rebuke for inattention.

### Coin Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְקַפְּלוֹ וּמַנִּיחוֹ עַל פִּי הַקָּנֶה, אִם חוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב הַקָּנֶה – טְרֵפָה, וְאִם לָאו – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani said: It was explained to me** personally **by Rabbi Elazar:** One severs the perforated tissue, **folds and lays it over the opening of the windpipe. If** it **covers the majority of the windpipe,** the animal is **a *tereifa*; and if not,** the animal is **kosher.**

The Gemara proceeds to ask: *u-vemah meshaarin* — how does one actually measure a hole in the trachea of a bird? Rav Yitzchak bar Nachmani reports a practice he received from Rabbi Elazar directly: one folds the excised piece of flesh and places it — *manicho al pi ha-kana* — over the opening of the trachea. If it covers the majority of the circumference of the tracheal opening, the animal is *treif*; if not, it is *keshera*. Rav Pappa adds a mnemonic: *nafya* — a sieve placed over a frame — to illustrate the visual image of a piece of tissue draped over the circular opening of the trachea. The measurement is thus not abstract but concretely geometric: does the hole, when laid flat over the opening, cover its majority?

For a hole that lacks *chisaron* — where the flesh remains but is merely slit open — the standard becomes slightly different. Since the flesh is still present and provides some structural protection, the hole must be large enough that an *issar* coin can pass through it comfortably, meaning the opening must be slightly larger than the coin's exact circumference. Rashi understands the coin to be measured standing upright — its width fitting through the slit — while Tosafot understand it as lying flat, the coin's face passing through the opening. These produce different practical thresholds, one more *chamur* and one more *kula* than the other.

### Hole Coverage Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְחֲתָה כְּדֶלֶת, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס אִיסָּר לְרׇחְבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If the windpipe **was missing** a piece so that its appearance was **like a door,** where the missing flesh was partially attached as though on a hinge, **Rav Naḥman said:** If the missing piece is **so** large **that an *issar* can enter** the hole **widthwise,** i.e., it is wider and taller than an *issar*, the animal is a *tereifa*.

The Gemara introduces the case of *niftachah ke-deles* — the hole is open like a door, meaning the flesh is splayed open rather than removed. Rav Nachman rules on the applicable *shiur* in this configuration. The principle governing all of these cases is that different physical configurations of the same wound carry different thresholds: complete removal of tissue, mere perforation without removal, and a flap-like opening each engage their respective standards.

### Tracheal Cracks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִסְדְּקָה, אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר בָּהּ אֶלָּא חוּלְיָא אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְחוּלְיָא אַחַת לְמַטָּה – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the windpipe **was cracked** along its length, **Rav said: Even** if **only one** undamaged **segment remains in** the windpipe **above** the crack **and one segment below** it, the animal is **kosher.**

The Gemara then turns to *nisdeka* — a crack in the trachea, as opposed to a hole or a split. A crack runs differently from a split: where a split runs along the length of the trachea and is through-and-through, a crack runs across the bands of the trachea and is not necessarily through-and-through. Since a lengthwise crack is more likely to heal on its own — the animal's neck movements do not force it further open the way a widthwise split would worsen — the Gemara treats it differently.

### Minimal Intact Ends

Rav initially states that even if the trachea is cracked along nearly its entire length, as long as one full *chulya* — one intact ring or band of the tracheal structure — remains at each end, the animal is *keshera*. The *chulyot* are the small ring-like segments that make up the trachea's length.

When this ruling was repeated before Rabbi Yochanan, he rejected it with impatience: *ma chulya uma chulya de-ka'amar Rav* — what is all this talk of *chulyot*? Rabbi Yochanan rules that no intact band is required. Rather, *mashu le-mala u-mashu le-mata* — a mere *mashu*, a minimal intact piece at each end of the crack, suffices to preserve the trachea's *kashrut*. The crack may extend along almost the full length of the structure, so long as something — however minimal — remains at each pole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר: מָה חוּלְיָא וּמָה חוּלְיָא דְּקָאָמַר רַב? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר בָּהּ אֶלָּא מַשֶּׁהוּ לְמַעְלָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ לְמַטָּה – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** this statement **before Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who **said: What** is this **segment and what** is that **segment that Rav says?** Segments are immaterial to the matter. **Rather, say: Even if any amount remained** intact **in** the windpipe **above** the crack, **and any amount below,** the animal is **kosher.**

When the same ruling was reported before Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yonatan — that only a *mashu* is required — Rabbi Yochanan expressed admiration: *yadein chavrein Bavla'ah le-parsha ke-hai ta'ama* — our Babylonian colleagues know how to explain this properly. The compliment is backhanded in the way characteristic of the ongoing Eretz Yisrael–Bavel tension, but it is genuine: Rabbi Yochanan affirmed that Rabbi Yonatan had arrived at the correct and well-reasoned ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הָכִי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: יָדְעִין חַבְרִין בַּבְלָאֵי לְפָרוֹשֵׁי כִּי הַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said this** statement in Eretz Yisrael **before Rabbi Yoḥanan in the name of Rabbi Yonatan** the Babylonian, i.e., that any amount of undamaged tissue above and below the crack renders the animal kosher. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to them,** excitedly: Do **our Babylonian friends know how to interpret in accordance with this explanation?** He was happy that Rabbi Yonatan interpreted it the same way he did.

## Neck-Lung Boundary

### Shechitah Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַצַּוָּאר כּוּלּוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁחִיטָה, מִטַּבַּעַת הַגְּדוֹלָה עַד כַּנְפֵי רֵיאָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef taught before Rabbi Yoḥanan: The entire neck is fit for slaughter, from the large** uppermost **ring** of the windpipe downward **until the lower edges of the lung.**

The Gemara now addresses the anatomical question of where the neck ends and the lung begins — a question relevant for *shechitah* and for the laws of *treifot*, and as the Gemara will note, also for *chazeh ve-shok*. Rav Chiyya bar Yosef taught before Rabbi Yochanan: *kol ha-tzavar kulo kasher li-shechitah* — the entire neck is valid for *shechitah*, from the *tabaat ha-gedolah* — the large ring at the top of the trachea — *ad kanfei reiah ha-tachtonah*, until the lower wings of the lung.

### Anatomical Orientation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תַּחְתּוֹנָה שֶׁהִיא עֶלְיוֹנָה, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר כֹּל שֶׁפּוֹשֶׁטֶת צַוָּארָהּ וְרוֹעָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תֵּאָנֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that the phrase: **Lower** edges of the lung, is referring to an animal hung by the feet; **that is,** it actually denotes the **upper** edges nearest the head. **As I say** that the area fit for slaughter is **all** the length of **the neck that** an animal **extends** in order to **graze, provided that it is not forced** to extend its neck further than it wishes.

Rava immediately clarifies what "lower" means, since the orientation of the animal changes depending on whether it is standing or hanging: *tachtonah she-hi elyonah* — the "lower" wings of the lung are those that are lower when the animal is hung upside down after *shechitah* for skinning, which are in fact the frontal wings when the animal is walking normally. The trachea is thus considered neck until it reaches the point parallel to those wings. The Gemara's diagram of the lung, with its multiple *unot* of different sizes arranged around the central structure, helps clarify which wings are in question.

### Stretched Neck Issue


A complication arises from the fact that the neck can be stretched. When a shochet slaughters, the demarcation line is invisible — the animal is intact, and the lung is not exposed. Moreover, an animal can extend its neck considerably beyond its resting position. Does a shechitah performed on the stretched portion of the neck remain valid?

### Grazing Stretch Test

Rav Ashi establishes the governing principle: *kol she-posheitet tzavarah ve-ro'eh* — any portion of the neck that the animal naturally extends when grazing counts as neck for the purposes of *shechitah*. This is a functional definition: wherever the animal's own neck reaches in the normal course of grazing is halakhically considered neck. The limiting condition is: *u-vilvad she-lo te'anes* — so long as the stretch was not forced. If the shochet or another person pulled the animal's neck to extend it artificially, the stretched portion does not qualify.

### Teiku on Self-Stretch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב חֲנִינָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב חֲנַנְיָה: אָנְסָה עַצְמָהּ מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanina, and some say Rav Ḥananya, raises a dilemma:** If the animal **forced itself** and extended its neck, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the additional area? The Gemara responds: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Chanina raises a further question that is left unresolved: *onsa atzmah mai hu* — if the animal itself stretched its own neck, even without external compulsion, does that stretched portion count as valid neck? The Gemara leaves this as *teiku*. The question is subtle: Rav Ashi's rule already stipulates that the animal's natural stretching in grazing counts as neck. The residual question apparently concerns a stretch that is self-initiated but not part of the animal's ordinary grazing movement — some intermediate case of the animal extending its neck for reasons of its own, not while actually grazing. Whether such a stretch is sufficient to extend the zone of valid *shechitah* remains undecided.

### Below-Chest Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיקַּב הַקָּנֶה לְמַטָּה מִן הֶחָזֶה – נִידּוֹן כְּרֵיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** And if **the windpipe was perforated below the breast, it is considered** to be **like** a perforated **lung,** which renders the animal a *tereifa* no matter the size of the perforation.

The Gemara applies the demarcation principle to *treifot*: once the trachea crosses the threshold into the zone treated as lung, defects in that portion of the trachea are judged by the standards of the lung, not the trachea. *nikva ha-kaneh le-veit challalo* — if the tracheal tube is punctured below the chest — *nidon ke-rei'ah*: it is treated with the same *treifah* standards as the lung.

### Priestly Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ חָזֶה? זֶה הָרוֹאֶה אֶת הַקַּרְקַע, לְמַטָּה – עַד הַצַּוָּאר, לְמַעְלָה – עַד הַכָּרֵס. חוֹתֵךְ שְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת מִשְּׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ, וְזֶהוּ חָזֶה הַנִּיתָּן לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Which is the breast** that must be given to the priests as a gift from every peace offering (see Leviticus 7:31)? **This** is the section **that faces the ground,** not the ribbed area on the sides. And lengthwise it extends **below,** when the animal is hung from the legs, **until the neck,** and **above until the rumen.** One **cuts the two ribs** nearest the head **from the two sides** of the animal **from both directions, and this is the breast that is given to the priests.**

The question of where neck ends and chest begins has a second halakhic application: the priestly portions of a *korban shelamim*. When bringing a *shelamim*, one must give the *kohen* the *chazeh* and the *shok*. The Gemara asks: *eizehu chazeh* — what counts as the breast? The answer is: *ze ha-ro'eh et ha-karka* — the part of the animal that faces the ground, from the forward end up to the neck and from the top end back to the stomach. The shochet cuts two ribs and two panels on each side — and that constitutes the *chazeh* given to the *kohanim*. The demarcation of neck from chest thus governs both *shechitah* validity and the boundaries of the priestly gift.

## Brain Anatomy

### Two Membranes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיקַּב קְרוּם שֶׁל מוֹחַ. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: קְרָמָא עִילָּאָה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אִינְּקִיב תַּתָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: If **the membrane of the brain was perforated,** the animal is a *tereifa*. The Gemara cites **Rav and Shmuel, who both say:** The brain is covered by two membranes, a thick outer membrane adjacent to the skull and a thin inner membrane adjacent to the brain. The animal is a *tereifa* if **the outer membrane** was perforated, **even if the inner** membrane **was not perforated.**

The Gemara turns to the brain — *mo'ach* — and the law of *nikav krum shel mo'ach*: a perforation of the brain's membrane. Two membranes surround the brain according to rabbinic anatomy, an outer and an inner. Rav and Shmuel both hold — *d'amri tarveihu* — that perforation of the outer membrane alone, even if the inner membrane remains intact, renders the animal *treif*. A second opinion holds that the animal is not *treif* unless the inner (*tata'ah*) membrane is also perforated — *ad de-inkiv tata'ah*.

The underlying question is whether a perforation in the outer membrane necessarily signals a disease process that will progress to the inner membrane, or whether the enclosing protection of the skull makes healing more likely and the two membranes must therefore be evaluated independently. Since the brain, unlike the lung or trachea, is enclosed within bone, there is a stronger argument that a wound in the outer membrane will not inevitably spread inward. This mitigating factor is what generates the dispute.

### Brain Sac Siman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: וְסִימָנָיךְ חַיְיתָא דְּמִתְּנַח בֵּיהּ מוֹחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: And** this is **your mnemonic** to remember the *halakha*: The **bag in which the brain rests,** i.e., the inner membrane.

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a *siman* to help identify these two membranes anatomically: *chayata de-manach beih mocha* — the sac in which the brain rests. The sac itself, as a bounded vessel, helps conceptualize how two distinct membranes can surround a single organ.

### Testicle Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בַּבֵּיצִים נִיכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** Even though the inner membrane of the brain is very thin and not easily visible, **its equivalent in the testicles,** which are also enclosed in a thin membrane, **is conspicuous,** as it has an appearance different from the testicles themselves.

Rav Chana bar Papa in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds a practical teaching: *kenegdo be-veitzim nikar* — the two membranes can be seen more clearly by analogy with the testicles. *chazal* were aware that the two layers of membrane are not easily distinguished when examining the brain directly. The testicles, however, display two clearly distinct encasing membranes visible to the naked eye. One who wants to train his perception of what two separate membranes look like should study the structure of the testicles first. (The Gemara uses *beveitzim* as the standard rabbinic euphemism, a word that has since shed any euphemistic quality through long familiarity.)

### Skull-Cord Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: מוֹחַ – כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבַּקְּדֵירָה נִדּוֹן כְּמוֹחַ, הִתְחִיל לִימָּשֵׁךְ נִדּוֹן כְּחוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says in the name of bar Kappara:** With regard to the **brain, everything inside the skull is considered** part of **the brain.** From the point where it **begins to extend** like a cord out of the base of the skull, it **is considered the spinal cord.**

Rav Shimon ben Pazi in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi in the name of Bar Kappara establishes the boundary between brain and spinal cord. *mo'ach kol mah she-be-kedeira* — everything within the skull, which the Gemara calls *kedeira* (lit. "pot") as a somewhat colorful anatomical metaphor — is treated as brain and governed by the halachot of brain. *hitchil limshoch* — once the matter begins to extend outward and thin into a cord — *nidon ke-chut ha-shidrah*: it is treated as spinal cord and its halachot change accordingly. The two — brain and spinal cord — have different *shiurim* for holes and different halachic consequences for defects, and so a clear anatomical boundary is legally necessary.

### Polin Markers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי: לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי – כְּמִין שְׁנֵי פּוֹלִין יֵשׁ מוּנָּחִין עַל פִּי הַקְּדֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani said: It was explained to me** personally **by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** himself: Protrusions **similar** to **two** [*polin*], two gland-like structures, **sitting at the** opening of the skull.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani reports directly from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi the specific anatomical marker: *ke-min shnei polin yesh munachin al pi ha-kedeira* — at the very opening of the skull there are two *polin*, two gland-like structures, sitting at the junction between skull and spinal canal. *min ha-polin u-lifnim* — inward from the *polin*, on the skull side — is treated as brain. *min ha-polin ve-lachutz* — outward from the *polin*, on the spinal side — is treated as spinal cord.

### Polin Classification


The Gemara then raises the natural residual question: *ee polin atzmah* — what is the halachic status of the *polin* themselves? Are they classified as brain or as spinal cord? The Gemara answers: *ani yodeia* — I do not know. It then adds: *u-mistavra ke-lifnim* — it is more logical that they should be classified as brain, on the inner side. Why this is the more logical conclusion, given that this is a question of anatomy rather than pure legal reasoning, is not fully explained. When the Gemara says *mistavra* in the context of a scientific question, it is invoking something like functional logic — perhaps that the *polin* serve as a threshold structure still within the containing framework of the skull, and therefore align structurally with the brain rather than with the elongated spinal cord. The question remains, in the strict sense, without a definitive resolution.

### Bird Brain Verification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּדַּק בַּאוֹף וְאַשְׁכַּח כְּמִין שְׁנֵי פּוֹלִין מוּנָּחִין עַל פִּי הַקְּדֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmiya investigated whether the same anatomical structure applies to birds. *Rabbi Yirmiya badak ba-of* — he examined the brain of a bird — *ve-ashkach ke-min shnei polin munachin al pi ha-kedeira*: he found the same two gland-like structures at the opening of the skull in birds as well.**

Rabbi Yirmiya investigated whether the same anatomical structure applies to birds. *rabbi yirmiya badak ba-of* — he examined the brain of a bird — *ve-ashkach ke-min shnei polin munachin al pi ha-kedeira*: he found the same two gland-like structures at the opening of the skull in birds as well. The same boundary therefore applies to birds as to animals, and the halachic framework of brain versus spinal cord operates identically in both.

## Heart & Vessels

### Heart Chamber Hole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיכַּב הַלֵּב לִבְתֵּי חַלָּלוֹ – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** [If] the heart's chamber was perforated, the animal is a *tereifa*.

The Gemara turns to the heart. *nitkav ha-lev le-veit challalo* — if a hole in the heart reaches the heart's chamber — the animal is *treif*. This is the basic principle.

### Chamber Size Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב זֵירָא: לִבְתֵּי חַלַּל קָטָן אוֹ לִבְתֵּי חַלַּל גָּדוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zeira immediately asks: does this mean that even a hole reaching only one of the minor chambers of the heart renders the animal a *tereifa*, or only if the hole penetrates the main central chamber?**

Rav Zeira immediately asks: *le-veit chalal katan o le-veit chalal gadol* — does this mean that even a hole reaching only one of the minor chambers of the heart renders the animal *treif*, or only if the hole penetrates the main central chamber?

### Lung-Heart Comparison


Abaye challenges the question itself. He points to the halacha of the lung: there, the ruling is that the animal is *treif* only when the hole reaches *le-veit ha-simponot* — the primary chamber housing the main bronchi. Presumably, he argues, the heart should follow the same principle: only the main chamber should count. The question therefore appears to answer itself.

### Language Distinction


The Gemara rejects Abaye's argument and defends Rav Zeira's question by identifying a critical difference in formulation. Regarding the lung, the language is *le-veit ha-simfonot* — "to the house of the bronchi," an expression pointing emphatically to the central, primary source. This is reinforced by Rabbah bar Tachlifa citing Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba citing Rav: *ad she-tinakeiv le-simpon gadol* — only when the hole reaches the main bronchus. The word *beit* before *simponot* indicates the ultimate source, the convergence point of all the bronchi.

Regarding the heart, by contrast, the language is *le-veit challalo* — "to its chamber." *beit chalal* is a general term: a chamber is a chamber, large or small. *mah li chalal gadol, mah li chalal katan* — what difference does it make whether the chamber is large or small? The formulation itself includes minor chambers. The two laws are therefore not analogous, and Rav Zeira's question stands as a genuine one: the language of the Mishnah regarding the heart encompasses any chamber, not only the primary one.

### Aorta Hole Standard


The Gemara next considers the *kaneh ha-lev* — the pipe of the heart, understood to be the aorta — as distinct from the heart itself. If the aorta is perforated, what is the *shiur*? Rav says *be-mashehu* — any perforation at all renders the animal *treif*. Shmuel says *be-rubo* — only if the majority of the aorta is perforated. The practical question of how an animal could survive a majority perforation of the aorta is acknowledged as pressing, though the Gemara does not resolve it on anatomical grounds.

### Aorta Location


The Gemara asks: *hai nihu kaneh ha-lev* — where exactly is the *kaneh ha-lev* located? Rav bar Yitzchak says in the name of Rav: it is embedded within the fat on the wall of the animal — *cheilev she-al gabei d'fanot*. The Gemara objects: *d'fanot salka da'atach* — you mean the stomach walls? That makes no anatomical sense when discussing the heart. The answer is corrected: it is embedded in the wall of the lung — *she-al gabei dophnei rei'ah*. The *kaneh ha-lev* is thus located within the lung's surrounding structure rather than the heart itself.

### Three Tracheal Pipes


The Gemara now introduces a teaching attributed to Amimar in the name of Rav Nachman that is among the most anatomically challenging passages of these *sugyot*. *tlati kani havei* — there are three pipes extending from the trachea. *chad parash le-liba* — one extends to the heart. *ve-chad parash le-rei'ah* — one to the lung. *ve-chad parash le-kavda* — one to the liver. The literal reading of the Gemara is that these three organs each receive a pipe directly from the trachea. This conflicts substantially with modern anatomical understanding, and commentators such as the *sichas chullin* invest considerable effort in demonstrating that *chazal* did not mean this literally — that the language refers to proximity or parallelism rather than direct anatomical connection. The literal Gemara text, however, states the three-pipe structure plainly.

### Pipe Standards


The halakhic consequence flows directly from the anatomical claim: each pipe takes on the *treifah* standards of the organ it serves. *d'rei'ah k'rei'ah* — the pipe going to the lung is judged by the laws of the lung. *d'kavda k'kavda* — the pipe to the liver by the laws of the liver. *d'liba* — the pipe to the heart was already the subject of the dispute between Rav and Shmuel regarding *be-mashehu* versus *be-rov*.

### Reversed Standards


Mar bar Chiya transmits the opposite ruling. The pipe leading to the lung is governed by the standards of the *liver*, and the pipe leading to the liver is governed by the standards of the *lung*. The logic of this reversal is not explained in the Gemara, and the inversion is startling. In any other *sugya* one might say that a ruling so counterintuitive must contain a layer of meaning one is simply not grasping. In the domain of *treifot*, however, where the rules do not always yield to pure logical deduction and where *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* often underlies the distinctions, the absence of stated reasoning is less surprising, even if the content remains puzzling.

### Rav's Credibility


The discussion closes with a scene that crystallizes the difficulty of *treifot* as a field of study. Rav Chiyya bar Yosef reported Rav's position — that even a *mashehu* perforation of the *kaneh ha-lev* renders the animal *treif* — before Shmuel. Shmuel's response was withering: *ei hachi amar Abba* — if Abba (Rav's familiar name) really said that — *lo yada be-treifot*: he has no understanding of *treifot*. Shmuel's position, that the standard is *be-rov*, is what he takes to be self-evidently correct, and Rav's *be-mashehu* standard strikes him as simply wrong.

The exchange is a fitting conclusion to these sugyas. *treifot* is a domain where the normal tools of Talmudic reasoning — *kol va-chomer*, analogy, logical inference — often fail to generate determinate answers, where the standards seem to resist systematic derivation, and where even great *amoraim* find themselves in sharp disagreement. Shmuel's dismissal of Rav is not merely polemical; it reflects a genuine frustration with the subject matter itself — a frustration the Gemara invites every serious student to share.
