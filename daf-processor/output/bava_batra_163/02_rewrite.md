# Bava Batra 163 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Body-Witness Gap

### Two-line rule

The *gemara* on 163a continues its discussion of the white space between the body of a *shtar* and the signatures of the *eidim*. The central concern is straightforward: if too much blank space is left between the end of the document's text and the witnesses' signatures, a forger could insert an additional line of content — one line that would be incorporated into the *shtar* itself, followed by a second line of boilerplate — thereby altering the meaning of the document. For this reason, two full lines of gap are prohibited.

### One-line gap permitted

A single line of space, however, is permissible. The *eidim* need some room to sign; they cannot always be expected to inscribe their names immediately flush against the last line of text. Even if that one-line space were filled in, the principle that we do not derive legal force from the final line of a document means we would not act on whatever is inserted there.

### Dots prevent abuse

The *gemara* turns to the space between the witnesses' signatures and the *beit din*'s *kiyum* — its formal validation of those signatures. Why are we not afraid that someone will fill in the gap between the *eidim* and the *kiyum*, insert forged witnesses, and then exploit the *kiyum* as if it validates the inserted material? The answer is that the blank area is rendered unusable by filling it with dots and splotches, preventing that space from being written upon in any meaningful way.

## Erasure concerns

### Rav: erasure kasher

At the bottom of 163a, the *gemara* raises a deeper question. The dots and splotches prevent someone from simply writing in the blank space, but what about a more sophisticated attack: cutting away the top portion of the *shtar*, erasing the smudgy area, and then rewriting whatever one wishes? The obvious defense is that anything written on an erased background is suspect — but the *gemara* immediately complicates this by citing Rav's ruling: *shtar ha-ba hu v'eidav al ha-machak kasher* — a *shtar* in which both the body and the witnesses' signatures appear on an erased background is valid.

To appreciate why this ruling is relevant, one must understand that the *gemara* will establish later — though it is worth anticipating here — that a single erasure looks visibly different from a double erasure. With that principle in mind, consider what concerns might attach to a *shtar* written entirely on an erased background. One might worry that the document originally had different content written on a clean background, which was then erased and replaced. But if both the body and the *eidim* are on the erased background, there is nothing particularly alarming: even if the entire document was composed on a previously erased surface, the witnesses' genuine signatures are there, and the *shtar* functions normally. The forger cannot gain anything simply by using erased parchment.

### Full erasure risk

The more serious concern is this: since the background is already erased, a forger could erase the top portion a second time and rewrite it with different content, leaving the witnesses' signatures intact on their once-erased section. He would then present the document with a *kiyum* — and the *kiyum*, having been issued on the original signatures, would appear to validate his fraudulent version. This is not, however, a concern unique to the specific scenario involving smudgy space below the *kiyum*. It is a general challenge to Rav's ruling: whenever there is a *kiyum* on a *shtar*, what prevents someone from erasing the entire top, rewriting it, and reusing the *kiyum*?

The *gemara* addresses the internal tension. It notes that according to Rav Kahana, who transmits the ruling that *shtar chuv eidav al ha-machak kasher* in the name of Shmuel, there is no contradiction — because Rav himself may have held that a *shtar* with both body and witnesses on erased background is invalid. Under Rav's own view, then, such a forged *shtar* — with the rewritten top and the witnesses both on an erased background — would be disqualified, and the *kiyum* would be of no use. But according to Rav Tavyumi, who transmits this ruling in the name of Rav himself, the difficulty remains: if Rav considers such a *shtar* valid, how does he protect against the reuse of a *kiyum*?

### Validation voided

The *gemara* resolves this with a direct ruling: *kol ki hai gavna ein mekaymin oso min ha-asharta shebo ela min ha-eidim shebo* — in precisely this case, where the entire *shtar* is written on an erased background, one does not rely on the embedded *kiyum* at all. The *kiyum* is effectively voided. Instead, the witnesses must be validated directly before the court. The fear that someone erased the entire top and reused the existing *kiyum* is neutralized precisely because we will never rely on a *kiyum* when the full document appears on a *machak* background — we insist on direct testimony about the witnesses' authenticity.

## Witness-Validation Gap

### One-line doc valid

Rabbi Yochanan now introduces a set of distinctions about the permissible gap between the witnesses' signatures and the *kiyum*. He begins with a related rule: *lo shanu ela bein eidim le-chtav* — the rule permitting one line of space (and prohibiting two) applies between the body of the document and the witnesses. However, between the witnesses and the *ashrata* — the validation mark — *afilu shita achas pasul*: even a single blank line is invalid.

### Separate lines invalid

The concern with a one-line gap between the witnesses and the *kiyum* becomes clear with the *gemara*'s next question. Since Rabbi Yochanan holds that a one-line *shtar* — that is, a document whose entire content occupies a single line — with the witnesses signing on that same line is valid, even a one-line gap before the *kiyum* is dangerous. A forger could cut away the body of the document, write his own one-line *shtar*, place the *eidim* on the same line, and then exploit the *kiyum* that follows.

### Small doc concern

The *gemara* articulates this precisely: *dilma megayez lei le-ila'i ve-chasav hu ve-edav be-shita achas* — perhaps the forger will cut away the top and write a one-line document together with the witnesses all on a single line, thereby producing a valid *shtar* backed by an existing *kiyum*. Since one-line documents with witnesses on the same line are in principle valid, no gap at all may be left between the witnesses and the *kiyum*.

Tosafot raises the question of why the forger would not be exposed by the *machak* — if he cuts away and rewrites, the erased area would be visible. Tosafot answers that Rabbi Yochanan is addressing a different scenario than Rav. Rav's ruling concerns a case where the space before the *kiyum* has already been rendered smudgy, and any erasure would be detectable. Rabbi Yochanan is speaking of a case where the background is clean — no smudgy section separates the *eidim* from the *kiyum*. In that case, there is no *machak* evidence to expose the forgery, and even one clean blank line cannot be tolerated.

The Rashbam explains the underlying logic: since the Rabbis did not wish to define a minimum size for a *shtar*, a one-line document remains valid. But because a one-line *shtar* is possible, the single-line gap rule that applies between the body and the witnesses cannot be carried over to the gap between the witnesses and the *kiyum*. For the body-witness gap, we permit one line because witnesses need some space to sign. But permitting even one line between the witnesses and the *kiyum* would enable the precise fraud Rabbi Yochanan describes.

### Witness notation

The *gemara* then asks: since a one-line *shtar* with witnesses on the same line is valid, why isn't there a risk even when we allow one line between the *eidim* and the body? Could someone not cut that line off, leaving the witnesses' signatures intact, insert their own one-line document, and then present the valid witnesses alongside a *kiyum*? The *gemara* addresses this: *ve-kasavar shtar ha-ba hu ve-edav be-shita achas kasher* — yes, such a document is valid. But *shtar ha-ba hu be-shita achas ve-edav be-shita acheres pasul* — a one-line *shtar* with the witnesses signing on a *different* line is invalid.

The Rashbam explains: any full-sized *shtar* must allow one line of space for the witnesses to sign, because they cannot always be expected to crowd right up against the last line of text. Since one line must be permitted, we are in effect vulnerable to the attack of cutting off that line and using the witnesses elsewhere. Therefore, a one-line *shtar* with witnesses on a separate line is ruled invalid — exactly because this is the scenario the forger would construct. A one-line *shtar* with witnesses on the *same* line is safe, because then the *eidim* cannot be detached and reused: the forger would have to forge them himself, which exposes him in a different way.

### Extra witness risk

The *gemara* raises one further challenge. Given that a one-line *shtar* with witnesses on the same line is valid, could a forger not write his own one-line *shtar* with forged *eidim* on the same line, and then additionally present the genuine *eidim* whose signatures appear below, arguing he added extra witnesses? He would say: *ana l'rabot be-eidin hu d'avidna* — I was merely adding supplementary witnesses. He would then ask that the *kiyum* be applied to those additional witnesses, since they can be validated directly.

The *gemara* dismisses this: *katav be-che hai gavna ein mekaymin lei ela min eidim she-l'malah* — precisely because of this risk, we insist on relying only on the witnesses who appear on the same line as the one-line document, and we do not validate the supplementary witnesses below. The *kiyum* follows the witnesses on the shared line, and any witnesses appended below are disregarded.

## Erasure detection

### Visual difference

Having resolved the questions about white space, the *gemara* now turns formally to the topic introduced earlier — the principle that a single erasure looks different from a double erasure. The *gemara* states: *eino domeh nimchak pa'am achat le-nimchak shtei pe'amim* — a parchment erased once does not look the same as one erased twice. This principle is what permits Rav's ruling that a *shtar* with body and witnesses both on an erased background is valid, for if the top were re-erased and rewritten, the discrepancy would be visible.

### Double erase risk

The *gemara* challenges even this: *v'lichush dilma shadi deyuta amkom eidim me'ikara u'machik lei* — perhaps a forger will deliberately spill ink near the area where the witnesses will sign, erase it in their presence, and thereby arrange for them to sign on a background that has already been erased twice. Then when he erases the top and rewrites it, both sections will appear to have been erased twice, and no discrepancy will be visible.

### Witnesses witness erasure

Abaye resolves this by invoking a further principle attributed to Rav: *ein eidim chotmim al ha-machak ela im ken nimchak bifneihem* — witnesses may sign on an erased background only if the erasure was performed in their presence. The Rashbam explains: the witnesses will examine the full background before signing. If they observe any inconsistency — if the area where they are asked to sign looks different from the rest of the document — they will refuse to sign. Having seen the full document erased in their presence, they will know whether the background is uniform. A deliberate ink-spill and re-erasure, done immediately before signing, would produce a visible discrepancy that the witnesses themselves would notice and reject.

### Clean body valid

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita*: *hu al ha-niyar ve-adav al ha-machak kasher* — if the body of the document is on a clean, unerased background and the witnesses have signed on an erased background, the *shtar* is valid. What is there to fear? One might worry that there were previously different witnesses, whose signatures were erased and replaced with new ones — but this would be an absurd and harmful substitution from the forger's perspective, since it would only weaken rather than strengthen his document. So there is no obvious concern.

Yet the *gemara* probes further: this scenario seems to invite abuse. What prevents someone from writing a *shtar* on clean parchment, having witnesses sign on the erased bottom portion, and then immediately erasing the top and rewriting whatever he wishes? Once the top is erased, the entire *shtar* is on a *machak* background, and Rav's ruling validates it. The *gemara* responds that such a *shtar*, in the form described, requires a special notation: the witnesses write between their own signatures — *bein sahada le-sahada* — that the body of the document was written on a clean background while they signed on an erased one. This embedded notation protects the document: cutting it off would sever one witness's signature, and erasing it would produce a double erasure detectable by comparison to the once-erased section directly adjacent.

### Erased body invalid

The opposite configuration — *hu al ha-machak ve-eidav al ha-niyar pasul* — where the body is on an erased background and the witnesses sign on a clean background, is invalid. The reason is that this configuration shouts its own fraudulence: the most natural explanation for the top being erased while the bottom is clean is that the document originally had a legitimate text on a clean background, which was erased and replaced with a forgery.

### Notation solution

The *gemara* asks: why not apply the same solution here — have the witnesses insert a notation between their signatures stating that the body was on an erased background? This would explain the anomaly innocently. The *gemara* pushes back: if we allow this, we must address the concern that the top will be re-erased and rewritten — and the response, as before, is that a double erasure is visible. But then the *gemara* asks why the *pasul* ruling stands at all. Cannot the witnesses simply note between their signatures that the body was on a *machak*, and then we are protected against re-erasure by the double-erasure principle?

### Comparison needed

The *gemara* answers with a critical limitation: *hani milei heicha de-ichas sahadei a-machaka* — the double-erasure is detectable only when there is a basis of comparison within the same document. In the case where the witnesses sign on a *machak* background, the top can be compared to the bottom: if the top is re-erased, it will look different from the once-erased bottom. But where the witnesses are on clean parchment and the top alone is on a *machak*, there is no internal reference point for comparison. One cannot tell whether the top was erased once or twice when the only available comparison is clean parchment, not a once-erased section.

The *gemara* considers whether one could bring a separate scroll, erase a portion of it, and compare it to the top of the suspect document. This is rejected: *lo dami machaka de-hai megilta le-machaka de-hai megilta* — no two scrolls erase identically. The texture, age, and composition of each piece of parchment differ, so a once-erased patch on one scroll cannot serve as a reliable reference for detecting a twice-erased patch on another.

The *gemara* then asks whether one might validate the witnesses' signatures before the court, then erase those very signatures and use the resulting once-erased area on the same document as a comparison. If the top looks similar to this freshly erased area, the top was erased only once; if it looks different, the top was erased twice.

### Age of erasure

Rav Hoshaya rejects this: *ein domeh nimchak bein yomo le-nimchak bein shenei yamim* — a freshly erased area does not look the same as an area that was erased some time ago. The original erasure on the top of the document may have been performed months or years before the signatures were affixed. A fresh comparison erasure will therefore not produce a reliable match, yielding false positives and false negatives. One might propose waiting a period of time before making the comparison, allowing the fresh erasure to age to match the original — but this merely pushes the problem back, since we do not know how long ago the original erasure was made, and in any case an aged erasure on one scroll still cannot perfectly replicate the aging of another.

### Beit Din error risk

Rabbi Yirmiyah closes the discussion with a broader principle: *chiyshinan le-vei dina to'in* — once the analysis reaches this level of complexity, we are concerned that the *beit din* will err in making the required distinctions. A system of document validation that depends on comparing the visual texture of erasures, with all the variables of parchment type, erasure age, and comparison methodology, is simply too prone to judicial mistake. Accordingly, the invalid scenario remains invalid, and no notation between the witnesses' signatures can rescue it.

To summarize the full picture: a *shtar* with body and witnesses both on a *machak* background is valid, provided the *kiyum* is disregarded and the witnesses are validated directly. A *shtar* with a clean body and witnesses on a *machak* is valid, provided the witnesses insert a notation between their signatures. A *shtar* with the body on a *machak* and the witnesses on a clean background is invalid, and no notation can repair it.

## Dating systems

### Mekushar form

The *gemara* transitions to a new topic, returning to the *mekushar* — the tied or folded document. By way of reminder: a *get mekushar* is structured so that the text appears on both sides of the fold, with the witnesses signing on the outside. According to the standard ruling, witnesses on the inside of a *mekushar* disqualify it.

### Inside witnesses valid

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel dissents: he holds that if the witnesses signed on the inside, the document remains valid, because one can simply undo the knots, open the document, and it will function as a standard *pashut* document. Tosafot notes that Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's position implies that the writing in a *mekushar* appeared on both sides of the fold — otherwise, when opened, the document would display large blank spaces and not resemble a normal *shtar*.

### Dating difference

Rabbi responds to Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's position at the top of 164b: *she-lo eino domeh zemano shel zeh le-zemano shel zeh* — the date in one cannot be converted to match the date of the other, because the dating systems for a *pashut* and a *mekushar* differ. In a *pashut*, the standard reckoning applies: if a king has reigned for one year, the document says "year one"; if two years, "year two." In a *mekushar*, however, the system adds one: a king who has reigned for one year is recorded as "year two," and a king of two years is recorded as "year three." The Rashbam explains this increment as a form of *kavod* — a gesture of honor implying the king had already been ruling when the document was drawn. One might also suggest that the difference reflects competing conventions about whether "year one" means the king has completed one year or is currently in his first year — analogous to the ambiguity discussed in tractate *Rosh Hashanah*.

Whatever the precise origin of the discrepancy, the Rabbis deliberately maintained different systems for the two document types, as part of a broader policy of making *mekushar* and *pashut* documents as formally distinct as possible, to prevent confusion between them.

### Postdating risk

The practical danger of converting a *mekushar* to a *pashut* — as Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel would permit — is illustrated as follows. A borrower takes a loan from a lender during year one of a king's reign. A *mekushar* is drafted, and since *mekushar* dating adds one, the document reads "year two." The borrower repays the loan within the year and asks for his *shtar* back. The lender claims it was lost — *irchas li be-chutla iteyehiv* — and instead provides a receipt dated "year one" using standard *pashut* dating.

Now the lender waits. Once year two actually arrives, he opens the *mekushar* — since, according to Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, opened witnesses can convert it to a *pashut* — and presents it as a *pashut shtar* purporting to establish a debt from "year two." The receipt the borrower holds says "year one," so it appears to predate the debt. The lender can now claim the debt was never repaid, seize property from purchasers who acquired land during year two, and harm innocent *lakuchot* who had no knowledge of a loan that never actually existed at that point.

The crucial asymmetry is that neither party necessarily acted in bad faith. The borrower accepted his receipt in good faith, not realizing that the dating systems would diverge when the *mekushar* was converted. A standard postdated *shtar* would have been flagged and rejected; this situation arises precisely because the postdating is structural and invisible.

### No receipts

The *gemara* resolves this by attributing to Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel a consistent secondary position: *kasavar ein kotvin shovar* — he holds that receipts should not be written at all. The correct procedure when a loan is repaid is to demand the return of the original *shtar*; no receipt should substitute for it. If receipts are prohibited, the entire scenario collapses — the borrower's only protection is retrieving his *shtar*, and if the *shtar* is genuinely lost, the lender cannot open and repurpose it. The system remains internally coherent.

### Rabbi's learning

The *gemara* raises an intriguing question: did Rabbi actually know the details of *mekushar* dating well enough to make the objection he raised? The evidence suggests he did not, at least not initially. A *shtar mekushar* was once brought before Rabbi, and he declared it a *shtar me'uchar* — a postdated document — failing to recognize that what appeared to be a discrepancy was simply the standard *mekushar* dating convention. It was Zonim — the name of a person, perhaps related to the Baisos ben Zonim known elsewhere in the *gemara* — who informed him: *kach minhaga shel uma zu* — this is the practice of this nation; a king of one year is recorded as two, a king of two as three.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction simply: *basar de-shamei mi-Zonim savra* — after Rabbi learned this from Zonim, he incorporated it into his reasoning. His later objection to Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's ruling was informed by what he had learned from Zonim. The *gemara* treats this straightforwardly — Rabbi learned something during his life and applied it thereafter.

## Roman archon titles

### Archon dating

A *shtar* is presented to the court in which the date is recorded as "in the year of so-and-so the *arkan*." Tosafot notes that *arkan* does not literally mean king; it derives from the Greek *archon*, referring to an appointed official or governor — in the Roman context, roughly analogous to a consul. The document does not specify which year of this official's tenure; it simply names him and uses his title.

### Archon=year 1

Rabbi Chanina rules: examine when this official began his term in office — *eimasai amad arkan be-arkanutei* — and assume the document refers to the first year of that tenure. Since the title appears without qualification, it defaults to year one.

### Multiple terms

The *gemara* challenges this: *ve-dilma de-arich malchusei* — perhaps the official served for multiple years, and the document could refer to any one of them. How can we assume year one?

Rav Hoshaya resolves this by explaining the Roman convention: *kach minhaga shel uma zu* — this is the practice of this nation. During his first year in office, an official is called *arkan*. During his second year, he is called *digon* — from the Greek prefix *di-*, meaning two. Since the *shtar* uses the title *arkan* without qualification, it unambiguously refers to the first year.

### Term designation

The *gemara* raises one remaining difficulty: perhaps the official's original term ended, someone else served, and then he was reelected. In that case he would again be in his "first year" of a new tenure, yet the situation is not the same as his original first year. Rabbi Yirmiyah resolves this: *hahu arkan digon karu lei* — if an official returns to office after an interruption, he is not called simply *arkan* but *arkan digon*, signifying that this is his second term of office. The unmodified title *arkan* therefore reliably identifies the first year of his first continuous tenure.

## Greek geometry terms

### Shape-number terms

The *gemara* concludes with a brief but elegant discussion of Greek geometric terminology and its application in two distinct areas of *halacha*. The terms in question are: *acta* (one), *digon* (two), *trigon* (three), *tetragon* (four), and *pentagon* (five). These are shape names — polygon, triangle, square, pentagon — repurposed in Talmudic literature as numerical designations.

### Nazir multiplicity

Sumchus applies this vocabulary to *nazir* vows. If someone declares *hareini nazir acta*, he accepts one period of *nezirut* (the standard being thirty days). *Digon* means two periods; *trigon*, three; *tetragon*, four; *pentagon*, five. The Greek shape-names function as numerical shorthand within the context of vow-acceptance.

### Four corners needed

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita* applying the same geometric vocabulary to the laws of house impurity from *nega'im* — leprosy. A round house (*bayit agul*), a *digon* house (two-cornered), a *trigon* house (triangular), or a *pentagon* house (five-cornered) — *eino metamei be-nega'im*: none of these structures can become *tamei* through the leprosy impurity that afflicts houses. Only a *tetragon* — a four-cornered house — *metamei be-nega'im*.

### Four walls source

The *gemara* asks: what is the scriptural basis for requiring four walls and four corners? The *baraita* derives it from the verses in *Vayikra* governing house-leprosy: one verse refers to *kir* (a wall) and another to *kirot* (walls, plural). Each use of the plural implies at least two walls, and the combination of two such verses yields four walls — and with four walls comes the requirement of four corners. Hence the leprosy impurity applies only to a *tetragon* structure, completing the *gemara*'s excursion into Greek geometric terminology as applied to *halacha*.
