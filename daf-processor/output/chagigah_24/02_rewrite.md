# Chagigah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hand Contamination Mecha…

### Terumah vs. Kodesh Hands

The daf opens approximately eight to ten lines from the bottom of amud alef, at the phrase *u-vi-trumah im nitmeis*, presenting another distinction between *kodesh* and *terumah*: if one hand becomes *tamei*, the other hand does not become *tamei* in the case of *terumah*, whereas in the case of *kodesh*, the contamination of one hand does render the other *tamei* as well. Before examining how the Gemara develops this rule, the Rishonim disagree about the very mechanism the Mishnah describes.

### Rashi: Direct Touch

Rashi understands the case as follows. A person has washed both hands and is careful to keep them *tahor*. Then one hand inadvertently touches something that carries a rabbinic *tumah*, rendering that hand a *sheni*. The question is how the other hand — which was carefully guarded and remains *tahor* — can then become *tamei* for *kodesh*. According to Rashi, the *chiddush* of the Mishnah is that if the *tamei* hand touches the *tahor* hand, which in turn is touching *kodesh*, the *tumah* is transferred through the *tahor* hand to the *kodesh*. In other words, the contamination travels in the direction of transfer: the *tamei* hand contaminates the *tahor* hand, and the *tahor* hand passes it along to the *kodesh* it is touching. This is the operative mechanism in Rashi's reading — the *tumah* moves through an extended chain of contact at the moment of touching.

### Tosfos: Extended Contact

Tosfos frames the case differently. According to Tosfos, the *chiddush* concerns how the hand *receives* the *tumah*, not how it transmits it. In Tosfos's reading, one hand is simultaneously touching the object that causes *tumah* — the Gemara's ironic example being *sifrei kodesh* — while the other hand is touching the first hand. Because both hands are in contact at that same moment, the *tumah* passes electrically, as it were, from the source through the first hand to the second. This is the meaning of *chiburin* — connection. The second hand is not directly touching the *tamei* object, yet because it is part of one continuous, extended act of contact, it too becomes a *sheni*.

The practical difference is directional: in Rashi's model the *tamei* hand is on the "input" side and the *tahor* hand mediates the transfer to *kodesh*; in Tosfos's model the *tamei* object is the source and both hands are simultaneously receiving through their physical connection. Either way, the Gemara will explain, some form of *chiburin* — indirect, conducted contact — is what drives the rule.

## Chiburin Debate Challenge

### Abaye's Dry Hand

The Gemara introduces the principle of Rav Shizbi: *be-chiburin shanu* — the rule that one hand renders the other *tamei* for *kodesh* applies only when they are in connection, but not otherwise (*she-lo be-chiburin lo*). Before the implications of this limitation are drawn out, Abaye poses a challenge from a *braisa*: even a *yad neguvah* — a dry hand — renders its companion hand *tamei* for *kodesh* but not for *terumah*. The significance of specifying *dry* is that when something is wet, *tumah* is far more easily transferred, and a *sheni* touching a wet substance can readily render it a *rishon*. The *chiddush* of the *braisa* is precisely that even when the hand is dry, this inter-hand contamination operates for *kodesh*.

### Rabbi Yose: Psul not Tam…

Alongside this *braisa*, Rabbi Yose bei Rabbi Yehuda registers a qualification: the second hand does not make *kodesh* fully *tamei* — it only renders it *pasul*. To appreciate this distinction: if a hand that is a *sheni* touches *kodesh*, it would normally make the *kodesh* into a *shlishi*, which is called *metamei* kodesh, because a *shlishi* of *kodesh* can still pass on its *tumah* one further degree to a *revii*. Rabbi Yose bei Rabbi Yehuda says that this secondary hand does not have the power to create a *shlishi*; it can only produce a *revii*, which is *posel kodesh* but no longer *metamei* it. The second hand is thus treated as a derivative — diminished in power compared to the original.

### Logic of Chiburin

The Gemara now raises a sharp analytical point against the *chiburin* limitation proposed by Rav Shizbi. If we restrict the entire rule of one hand contaminating the other to the scenario of simultaneous, electrically connected contact, then there is no conceptual *chiddush* in specifying that even a dry hand accomplishes this. Here is why: when we say the hands must be in *chiburin*, the operative logic is not that hand A became *tamei* and then independently transferred its *tumah* to hand B. Rather, the two hands together form one extended act of contact with the original source — hand A is, in effect, simply a longer arm reaching the *tumah*. If the mechanism is this kind of extended touching, then the question of whether hand B is wet or dry is irrelevant, because the hand is not acquiring *tumah* through the normal mechanism of a *sheni* making contact; it is quasi-directly touching the original source. The *chiddush* of dryness only makes sense if we are speaking of a genuinely independent transfer — hand A acquires *tumah*, becomes a *sheni*, and then *on its own* makes hand B into a *sheni* through touch, even though normally a *sheni* cannot do this. In that scenario, the specification that even a dry hand accomplishes this is a meaningful *chiddush*. But if we limit the case to *chiburin*, the dryness is no *chiddush* at all, because we are not relying on hand-to-hand transmission in the ordinary sense. This is the Gemara's challenge to the *chiburin* restriction.

## Other Person's Hand

### Reish Lakish Initial

The Gemara then introduces a related teaching. Reish Lakish states: *lo shanu ela yado ve-yado* — the rule that one hand renders another *tamei* applies only when both hands belong to the same person. If my *tamei* hand touches another person's *tahor* hand, Reish Lakish holds that no inter-hand contamination occurs for *kodesh*.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He holds: *echad yado ve-echad yad chaveiro* — the original *tamei* hand can render *tamei* not only one's own other hand but even another person's hand. However, Rabbi Yochanan adds a crucial asymmetry: *u-ve-oto ha-yad lifsol aval lo le-tamei*. The power to contaminate another hand is invested only in the original *tamei* hand. The second hand — whichever hand was rendered *tamei* as a consequence of the first — does not carry forward the same power. Even though that second hand is now a *sheni*, it cannot in turn contaminate a third hand, whether belonging to oneself or another. When it encounters *kodesh*, it is only *posel* it — rendering it a *revii* — rather than *metamei* it by creating a *shlishi*.

To clarify the structure: the original hand is *tamei*; because of the *ma'alah be-kodesh*, it is permitted to render another hand *tamei* — and Rabbi Yochanan says this works even across persons. But the derivative hand, precisely because it is derivative, is weaker, and cannot propagate the contamination further in the same manner.

The Gemara grounds Rabbi Yochanan's position in the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah appears to repeat the statement that one hand makes the other *tamei* for *kodesh* but not *terumah*. The question *ha tu lamah li* — why does the Mishnah need to say this twice? — drives the answer: the repetition comes to include *yad chaveiro*, to teach that even another person's hand can be rendered *tamei* by the original hand.

One might note the suggestive correlation between this debate and the earlier *chiburin* question. If the mechanism of inter-hand contamination is an extended act of simultaneous contact — one hand touching the *tamei* object while the other touches the first — then it is most natural to think of two hands belonging to the same person, forming a single agent. It is much harder to imagine one person's hand and another person's hand as constituting one "extended act." Conversely, if the contamination works through genuinely sequential steps — hand A becomes *tamei*, then later touches hand B, which thereby becomes *tamei* — then there is no particular reason it could not cross from one person to another, just as any ordinary *tumah* transfer can. The Gemara does not explicitly connect these two debates, but the correlation is apparent.

### Reish Lakish Retraction

The Gemara records that Reish Lakish ultimately retracted and came around to Rabbi Yochanan's position. The retraction is transmitted in a chain: Rabbi Yonah said in the name of Rabbi Ami said in the name of Reish Lakish — *echad yado ve-echad yad chaveiro*, one's own hand or another person's hand, the original *tamei* hand can render either *tamei*. But the formulation also preserves the asymmetry: *u-ve-oto ha-yad lifsol aval lo le-tamei* — only the original hand carries the full power; the derivative hand is diminished to *posel* but not *metamei*.

Significantly, when Reish Lakish comes around, he affirms both points simultaneously: that it extends to another person's hand, and that the second hand is weaker. These two concessions are logically linked. By accepting that the mechanism operates across separate persons — ruling out the "extended simultaneous act" model — Reish Lakish is implicitly conceding that the first hand and second hand are genuinely distinct steps in the chain. And once they are genuinely distinct steps, it follows that the second hand is a derivative and is correspondingly weaker.

## Secondary Hand Power

### Tannaim Debate

The Gemara notes that the very question of whether the second hand has the power of a *sheni* or is reduced to a *shlishi* is itself the subject of a Tannaitic dispute. The Mishnah states: *kol ha-posel bi-terumah metamei yadayim lihyot sheniyot* — anything that invalidates *terumah* (i.e., is itself a *sheni*) also renders hands into a *sheni*. Rabbi Yehoshua then says that one hand renders its companion hand *tamei*, implying that the second hand becomes a *sheni* with the same force as the first. The *chachamim*, by contrast, say: *yadayim sheniyot hen, ve-ein sheni oseh sheni* — hands are *sheniyot*, and a *sheni* cannot make another thing into a *sheni*. At first this seems to be an outright rejection of the entire rule of inter-hand contamination. But the Gemara does not want to read the *chachamim* as contradicting the foundational principle of our Mishnah.

### Shlishi Resolution

The Gemara therefore resolves that the *chachamim* are not saying the second hand acquires no *tumah* at all. Rather, they hold that one hand makes the other into a *shlishi*, not a *sheni*. A *shlishi* of *kodesh* is *posel* — it renders *kodesh* invalid by producing a *revii* — but it does not *metamei* in the stronger sense of creating a *shlishi* in the *kodesh* itself. This is exactly the position of Rabbi Yose bei Rabbi Yehuda and of Reish Lakish after his retraction. The Gemara then offers the possibility that this is the same debate as between Rabbi Yose (who uses the language *letamei bakodesh*, implying the second hand is a *sheni* and makes *kodesh* a *shlishi*) and Rabbi Yose bei Rabbi Yehuda (who says the second hand is only *lifsol*, making *kodesh* a *revii*). These three distinct questions — whether *chiburin* is required, whether another person's hand is included, and whether the second hand is a *sheni* or *shlishi* — all turn on a single underlying conceptual axis: is the second hand's *tumah* the product of an extended act of contact, or of a genuinely independent transmission? If the former, the second hand is as powerful as the first; if the latter, it is derivative and weaker.

## Eating & Unwashed Hands

### Kodesh Eating Scenario

The Gemara turns to a case that at first seems puzzling. A person is eating *kodesh* — say, meat from his *shelamim* — and his hands are a *sheni* and unwashed. The *kodesh* meat enters his mouth without his hands touching it: either another person places it in his mouth, or he uses a toothpick (a *kli etz* without a *beit kibul*, which is entirely *tahor* and transfers no *tumah*). Now he wishes to add some *chullin* food to his mouth — chopped onion or radish — and the food in question has never come into contact with liquid since it was separated from the ground or the animal. Such dry *chullin* is not susceptible to *tumah* at all; it has never been *huchshar*, rendered susceptible, through contact with liquid. So there seems to be no concern: the *kodesh* meat is already in his mouth, his hands are not touching it, and the *chullin* he wants to add is *Teflon* — immune to *tumah* in any case.

Yet the Mishnah distinguishes: for *kodesh*, the Rabbis decreed that one may not eat dry *chullin* in this situation; for *terumah*, no such decree was issued, and one may place dry *chullin* in one's mouth even while *terumah* is there. The *braisa* cited attributes this to Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, who raises a related question: is there even a concept of *negiva* — the relevance of dryness — when it comes to *kodesh*? After all, *chibat ha-kodesh machshartan*: the very sanctity of *kodesh* renders it susceptible to *tumah* without the need for prior contact with liquid. By *kodesh*, everything is already susceptible, wet or dry.

### Indirect Hand Risk

The Gemara resolves the framing. The *chiddush* is not about whether *kodesh* needs to be wet to be susceptible — of course it does not. The Gemara reframes the case as one of indirect risk: the concern is that when a person feeds himself *chullin* with his *tamei* hands while *kodesh* is in his mouth, he may inadvertently bring his hands close to his mouth and touch the *kodesh* that is there. For *kodesh*, the Rabbis decreed against this risk — do not feed yourself *chullin* in this situation. For *terumah* chewed in the mouth, the Rabbis were not concerned that the person's hands would make contact with the *terumah* inside.

This asymmetry again reflects the pattern of the entire chapter: *kodesh* demands a heightened degree of vigilance, and rabbinic decrees extend further to protect it, even in cases where the pathway to *tumah* is indirect.

## Onen & Mechusar

### Immersion Requirement

The Mishnah presents two additional cases where the stringency applied to *kodesh* but not *terumah* involves immersion. An *onen* — a person in the acute mourning period before burial of a close relative — may eat *terumah*, but once the burial has taken place and his *onen* status ends, he must immerse in a *mikveh* before he may eat *kodesh*. Similarly, a *mechusar kipurim* — a person who has completed his purification process but has not yet brought the final sacrifice that would permit him to eat *korbanot* — upon bringing that sacrifice, must immerse before eating *kodesh*, even though no such immersion is required before resuming *terumah*.

### Status Change Reason

The Gemara explains the reasoning: *keivan de-ha-idna hava asiri* — since until this moment these individuals were in a state of prohibition with respect to *kodesh*, the Rabbis required a formal act to mark their transition to a new status. Crucially, in both cases the change of status occurred through an event external to the person's body: the *onen*'s status changes because the deceased was buried; the *mechusar kipurim*'s status changes because a *korban* was brought in the *Beis HaMikdash*. The internal personal consequence — that one is now permitted to eat *kodesh* — results from something that happened outside oneself. To formally mark that internal status change, a physical act performed upon one's own body is required: immersion in the *mikveh*. This gives halachic concreteness to what was otherwise a passive change caused by external circumstances.

One observation worth noting: there is a striking phenomenon built into this entire system. A non-*kohen* can never eat *terumah*, yet he may eat *kodesh* — specifically, his own *shelamim* in Jerusalem. The level that is in certain respects more stringent, more exalted, is the one to which the ordinary Israelite has access; the level that in another sense sits below it on the hierarchy is the one from which he is permanently excluded. This is not a contradiction in the system but an intriguing structural feature of the different categories, and it surfaces organically in several of the distinctions we have seen throughout the chapter.

## Chomer B'Terumah

### Year-Round Belief

With the completion of the ten *ma'alot* of *kodesh* over *terumah*, the Mishnah transitions to a new subject: the conditions under which an *am ha'aretz* — a Jew not scrupulous in the laws of *tumah va-taharah* — is believed regarding the purity of what he produces or brings. The heading *chomer bi-terumah* ("a stringency of *terumah*") introduces what turns out to be a comparative leniency: the cases where *terumah* is treated *more* stringently than *kodesh* in terms of belief. Rabbi Yehuda rules that in Judea, the *am ha'aretz* is believed regarding the purity of wine and oil for *kodesh* throughout the entire year. This is consistent with what we have seen: because the Rabbis did not wish to disenfranchise the *amei ha'aretz* entirely from participation in the *Beis HaMikdash*, they extended belief to their *kodesh* claims year-round.

### Pressing Season Only

For *terumah*, however, no such year-round belief is extended. The *am ha'aretz* is believed about the *tahara* of his wine and oil for *terumah* only *bi-sha'at ha-gattot ve-ha-badim* — during the season of pressing grapes and pressing olives. The *gat* is the winepress, the *bad* is the olive press. It is specifically during this annual season of production and harvest that even for *terumah*, the *am ha'aretz* is given credence. The title *chomer bi-terumah* thus refers to the fact that *terumah* cannot be believed in year-round as *kodesh* can — a stringency relative to *kodesh*, even though *terumah* is generally considered a domain of greater stringency toward the *am ha'aretz*.

### Vessel Purification

Why is the pressing season different? Rashi explains: *ba-sha'at ha-gattot ve-ha-badim, ha-kol metaharin et keleihem* — at the time of the olive and grape presses, the entire community purifies its vessels. Just as before *Pesach* there is a communal atmosphere of preparing and *kashering* utensils, the arrival of pressing season prompts a community-wide practice of immersing all relevant vessels in the *mikveh* before production begins. Given this communal practice, there is a factual basis to assume that the *terumah* produced immediately after this purification is indeed *tahor*.

Rashi also notes — several lines later in his commentary — an additional policy consideration. The *amei ha'aretz* constituted a far larger proportion of the agricultural population than the *chaverim*, and were responsible for producing the overwhelming majority of *terumah*. To deny credibility to the *amei ha'aretz* regarding their *terumah* at precisely the moment when most of it is produced would effectively exclude the *kohanim chaverim* from the bulk of their *terumah*. Both the factual grounding — community-wide vessel purification — and the practical policy — not excluding *kohanim* from most of their *terumah* — together justify believing the *am ha'aretz* during this season. This is analogous to the policy considerations that justify believing them about *kodesh* year-round.

A clarification about the *amei ha'aretz* is in order here. They were not simply ignorant or non-observant. In many respects they were observant Jews — scrupulous about *Shabbat*, *kashrut*, and other areas of *halacha*. The concerns about them were specific: either insufficient knowledge of the details of *tumah va-taharah*, or insufficient scrupulousness in that particular domain, or both. Similarly, there were concerns about a minority of them regarding separation of *ma'aser*, though this likely reflected the significant financial burden of surrendering twenty percent of annual agricultural income. Characterizing them as generally non-observant would be an overextension of the Gemara's actual claims.

### Post-Season Barrel

Once the pressing season has passed, credibility lapses. If an *am ha'aretz* comes to a *kohen* with a barrel of wine and presents it as *terumah* after the season has ended, the *kohen* cannot accept it — *lo yekabelenah mimenu*. The ruling is not based on when the wine was produced but on when the claim is being made. Even if the wine was pressed during the legitimate season three weeks earlier, the time of presentation matters. If the *am ha'aretz* arrives after the season, he is not believed now. The *kohen* tells him to hold onto the barrel until the following pressing season — *manichah la-gat ha-ba'ah* — at which point the same barrel can be accepted. The same physical wine, ten months later, becomes acceptable because the relevant time is when the *am ha'aretz* makes his representation, not when he made the wine.

### Kodesh Separation

There is one exception to the post-season rule. If the *am ha'aretz* tells the *kohen* that from this very barrel he already separated a small amount of wine and sanctified it for *kodesh* — for *nesachim*, for instance — then the *kohen* can accept the remainder as *terumah*. The logic is that since the *kohen* believes him about the *kodesh* separated from that barrel year-round, and having believed him about the *kodesh*, it follows that the entire barrel must have been *tahor* at the time of separation. The credibility established for *kodesh* effectively extends retroactively to certify the purity of the entire barrel, and the *terumah* from it can then be accepted. The Gemara notes a *ne'eman* — he is believed in this case.

### Mixed Products

A further ruling addresses *kadei yayin ve-kadei shemen ha-meduma'ot* — barrels of wine and oil that are *meduma'at*, meaning mixed, in which *terumah* and *chullin* have not yet been separated or have become intermingled. For these the *am ha'aretz* is believed *bi-sha'at ha-gattot ve-ha-badim*, during the pressing season, consistent with the general rule for *terumah*. The Mishnah adds that he is also believed *kodem la-gattot shiv'im yom* — up to seventy days prior to the pressing season itself. The Gemara will examine precisely what this seventy-day extension modifies and whether it applies to the earlier rulings as well.

## Galilee Limitation

### Samaritan Barrier

The Mishnah specifies that the leniency of year-round belief for *kodesh* applies in Judea but not in the Galilee: *bi-Yehuda hein u-va-Galil lo*. Reish Lakish explains the geographic rationale: *mipnei she-retzu'a shel kutim mafseket beineihem* — because a strip of Samaritan territory separates the Galilee from Judea. This strip carries the halachic status of *eretz ha-amim* — non-Jewish territory — even though it falls within the geographic boundaries of *Eretz Yisrael*. Travel through *eretz ha-amim* imparts *tumah*, which the Rabbis likened to *tumat ha-meit*, based on the assumption that non-Jewish lands contain unattended buried corpses.

### Beis HaMikdash Logic

The entire justification for believing the *am ha'aretz* about *kodesh* is grounded in the practical need to allow them to bring their produce to the *Beis HaMikdash*. The Rabbis extended credibility so that the *amei ha'aretz* would not be entirely shut out of the Temple service. But if someone in the Galilee sanctifies wine or oil as *kodesh*, there is no viable path to bring it to the *Beis HaMikdash* without passing through the Samaritan strip — and traversing *eretz ha-amim* would render the *kodesh tamei*. Since it cannot reach the *Beis HaMikdash* in any case, the underlying policy justification for believing them disappears entirely. Without the impetus of Temple access, there is no reason to extend year-round credibility, and therefore the Galilee does not share the leniency of Judea.

### Transport Protection

The Gemara raises the obvious question: could one not protect the *kodesh* during transport through the Samaritan strip? One possibility is carrying it in a *shidah, teivah u-migdal* — a chest, box, or cabinet — suspended off the ground. If the container keeps the *kodesh* elevated above the ground throughout the journey, perhaps the *tumat ha-aretz* would not penetrate it.

The Gemara answers that this case follows the opinion of Rebbe, who rules *ohel zaruk lav shemei ohel* — a "thrown *ohel*," meaning a structure not rooted in the ground, does not have the legal status of an *ohel*. The relevance: *tumat meit* (and *tumat eretz ha-amim* operates similarly) spreads upward and outward through an *ohel*, and the roof of a proper *ohel* both contains the *tumah* within and prevents it from penetrating from outside. A box carried through *eretz ha-amim* would function as a protective covering only if it were a proper *ohel*. But since it is not connected to the ground — it is suspended, moving through the air, a "flying carpet" as it were — it lacks *ohel* status. The *tumah* from below passes through it and contaminates whatever is inside. The same analysis applies to a person who is himself carried through *eretz ha-amim* in a sealed box: Rebbe holds he becomes *tamei*, while Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehudah holds he is *tahor*. For practical purposes, since the stricter opinion is followed, this route of transport offers no protection.

A bridge, by contrast, does have *ohel* status, because its base is ultimately connected to the ground on both sides, even if there is air beneath it. The structural connection to the ground is what distinguishes a proper *ohel* from an *ohel zaruk*. An umbrella anchored to the ground would similarly constitute a valid *ohel*; one merely carried aloft would not.

### Sealed Vessel Limit

The Gemara then considers a second possibility: why not transport the *kodesh* in a pottery vessel sealed with a *tzamid patil* — a tight, airtight seal? Pottery vessels acquire *tumah* only through their internal airspace; if the opening is tightly sealed, *tumah* cannot penetrate from outside, and the contents would remain protected. This is a well-established principle for other types of *tumah*: a sealed pottery vessel provides protection.

Rabbi Elazar answers decisively: *ein kodesh nitzol bi-tzamid patil* — sanctified objects cannot be protected by the *tzamid patil*. This is an independent stringency applying to *kodesh*. The Gemara then notes a potential difficulty from a Mishnah that states *ein hatat nitzelet bi-tzamid patil* — the water of the *Parah Adumah* cannot be protected by the *tzamid patil*. One might infer from this that *kodesh* can be so protected; only the *mei hatat* at its highest level of sanctity cannot. The Gemara resolves: the Mishnah means to say that water that has not yet had the ashes of the *Parah Adumah* mixed in — water that is not yet fully sanctified — can be protected. Once the ashes are mixed in, that water also cannot be protected. But the Mishnah's rule is not a leniency for *kodesh* generally; rather, *kodesh* itself likewise cannot be protected by the *tzamid patil*. Both at the level of *kodesh* and at the level of *mei hatat*, the *tzamid patil* affords no protection.

The Gemara closes with a brief observation from Ulla: *haveraya medakkin be-Galila* — the *chaverim* in the Galilee do take care to maintain purity for their *kodesh*. The Gemara responds with a degree of gentle irony: given that Ulla was active some two centuries after the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*, why are they sanctifying anything at all? The Gemara's answer is: *manichin la-hen she-yavo Eliyahu ve-yetaharena* — they set it aside, preserving it, trusting that when Eliyahu HaNavi comes he will show the way to bring it from the Galilee to the *Beis HaMikdash* without its becoming *tamei* en route.

To summarize the concluding section: two distinct *chiddushim* emerge. First, *kodesh* is believed year-round from the *am ha'aretz* — more leniently than *terumah* in this respect — but only in Judea, where Temple access provides the policy rationale. Second, *terumah* is believed from the *am ha'aretz* during the pressing season, and this presumably applies regardless of geography, since *terumah* does not require transport to the *Beis HaMikdash*. The structure of the chapter thus concludes by illuminating how the very features that make *kodesh* more exalted — its connection to the *Beis HaMikdash*, its heightened sensitivity to *tumah* — simultaneously shape the contours of who is believed about it and under what circumstances.
