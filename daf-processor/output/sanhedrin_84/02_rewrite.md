# Sanhedrin 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Context

### Pinchas & Kaneim

Daf 84 is the final daf of the *Nisrafim* chapter, and we pick up from the bottom of 83b. The preceding *sugya* was devoted to the fascinating topic of *kana'im pogim bo* and the story of Pinchas. In this connection, it is worth noting a striking passage in the *Yerushalmi* — which has not been examined here in detail — where it states explicitly that what Pinchas did was *she-lo birtzon chachamim*, against what the rabbis would have sanctioned. The *Yerushalmi* goes further: at the moment Pinchas acted, *Bnei Yisrael* were prepared to place him in *cherem*, and only a divine voice declaring *hinneni noten lo et briti shalom* forestalled that outcome. This reinforces a tension already present in the *Bavli*: while *kana'im pogim bo* is a recognized halakhic category, it operates entirely outside the framework of law — consistent with the *Bavli*'s own position that Zimri could have turned around and killed Pinchas as a *rodef*. The *Yerushalmi* is a fitting complement to that understanding.

### Mitat Bidi Shamayim

The previous daf was largely devoted to cataloguing cases in which one incurs *chiyuv misa bidei shamayim* — death at the hands of Heaven. This may have begun as a digression, but it connects organically to the broader theme of the chapter, which is concerned with court-imposed executions. Running alongside that discussion, the *Gemara* naturally surveys those transgressions that are punishable not by a human court but by Heaven. The vast majority of these cases involve either *avoda* in the *Beit HaMikdash* or matters of *kodshim*.

### Arel, Onen, Yoshev

Before moving into the specific analyses, the *Gemara* draws a general distinction: while a *zar* who performs the *avoda*, a *kohen* who is *tamei*, or a *kohen* not wearing the *bigdei kehuna* are all subject to *misa bidei shamayim*, there is a separate category of disqualified *kohanim* whose service is invalidated but who are not subject to *misa bidei shamayim*. These are: the *arel* (an uncircumcised *kohen*), the *onen* (a *kohen* who has lost an immediate relative on the day of death), and the *yoshev* (a *kohen* who performs the service while sitting rather than standing). All three invalidate the *avoda* but incur only a *lav* — a negative prohibition — not death at the hands of Heaven.

---

## Arel

### Yechezkel Source

Where does the prohibition against an *arel* performing the *avoda* come from? Rav Chisda states that this is not derived from the Torah of Moshe Rabbeinu at all. The source is Yechezkel ben Buzi: *kol ben nechar arel lev ve-arel basar lo yavo el mikdashi* — no foreigner who is either of uncircumcised heart (i.e., an apostate) or of uncircumcised flesh may enter God's sanctuary to serve. The fact that this is only known from Yechezkel and not from the written Torah is itself noteworthy, and indeed, the concern about including Yechezkel in the canon precisely because of such passages — where he supplements or appears to stand in tension with the Pentateuch — is well known. The presumption here is that the prohibition always existed and Yechezkel's prophecy revealed it; but the *Gemara*'s language — "we did not learn this from Moshe's Torah" — does not make that entirely clear.

### Arel as Lav

Since the prohibition against an *arel* performing the *avoda* is derived solely from Yechezkel, there is plainly no basis to say that it carries *misa bidei shamayim*. The *Gemara* characterizes this prohibition as a *lav* — a negative biblical prohibition — noting that the language of Yechezkel is indeed formulated as a prohibition, even though it is not a prohibition from the Pentateuch. Precisely because there is no Pentateuchal source, there is certainly no ground to assert a death penalty.

---

## Onen

### Onen Source

The case of the *onen* is derived from the verse concerning the *kohen gadol*: *u-min ha-mikdash lo yetzei ve-lo yechalel et mikdash elohav* — he does not leave the Temple and does not desecrate the sanctuary of his God. The verse addresses the *kohen gadol* specifically: because he is required to remain in the *Beit HaMikdash* even after the death of an immediate relative, his service there is not rendered a *chilul*. The implicit inference — *ha-acher*, the ordinary *kohen* who does not leave when he becomes an *onen* — would desecrate the service. While this does not state a negative prohibition explicitly, it clearly indicates that performance of the *avoda* in such a state is forbidden and renders the *avoda* invalid.

### Chilul from Teruma

Rav Adda bar Ahava poses a question to Rava: since the verse uses the language of *chilul* — desecration — should we not learn from a *gezeira shava* of *chilul-chilul* that the *onen* who performs the service is subject to *misa bidei shamayim*, just as a *tamei kohen* who eats *teruma* is subject to *misa bidei shamayim*? The verse in the context of *teruma* states *ve-lo yisu alav cheit u-mesu bo ki yichaleluhu* — the theme of *chilul* accompanied by death was indeed the basis established on the previous daf for deriving *misa bidei shamayim* in other cases. Should the same logic not apply here?

### Chilul Limitation

The answer is that no *gezeira shava* can be drawn here. The *chilul* by the ordinary *kohen* who is an *onen* is not explicitly stated in the verse — it is only inferred implicitly from the verse concerning the *kohen gadol*. The *Gemara* states: *ha miklal ka-ati*, it comes only from implication; *havi davar haba min haklal*, it is something derived by inference; *ve-chol davar haba min haklal ein danin oso bi-gzeira shava* — one does not apply a *gezeira shava* to something that itself was derived only by implication. Tosafot note that there are cases where a *gezeira shava* is applied even to an implicit derivation, but the principle stated here is that such an implicit inference does not provide a sufficiently firm basis to extend the penalty to *misa bidei shamayim*. The implicit derivation is strong enough to establish the *lav* — the verse's statement *ve-lo yechalel et mikdash elohav* perhaps serving as the biblical prohibition for any act that constitutes *chilul* — but not strong enough to import the death penalty from *teruma*.

## Yoshev

### Standing Requirement

The case of the *yoshev* — a *kohen* who performs the *avoda* while seated — is derived from the verse: *ki bo bachar Hashem elohecha mikol shvatecha la'amod l'sharets* — for God has chosen him from among all your tribes to stand and to serve. Rava, citing Rav Nachman, derives from this that the *sheirus* in the Temple is mandated specifically in a posture of *amida* — standing — and not *yeshiva*. This gives rise to a *lav*, even if only implicit in the word *la'amod*.

### Yoshev as Lav

As with the *arel* and *onen*, the *Gemara* makes clear that the *yoshev* who performs the *avoda* renders the service invalid — he is *mechallel ha-avoda* — but that this does not rise to the level of *misa bidei shamayim*. The basis is not sufficiently strong. The *Gemara* notes that the *braisa* used the language of *lav*, though the *lav* here may be understood as meaning simply that it is prohibited, whether or not it rises to the level of a formal biblical *lav*. Regardless, all agree there is no basis for a death penalty.

---

## Baal Mum

### Rebbi's View

With *baal mum* — a *kohen* with a physical blemish who performs the *avoda* — we return to a dispute that connects directly to the subject of our *mishna*. It is interesting to observe that while the *Gemara* introduced this whole discussion as something of a digression, the issue of the *baal mum* circles back to the precise structural context in which we find ourselves: the debate over what punishment attaches to various forms of unauthorized *avoda*. Just as our *mishna* records a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* regarding a *zar* who performs the *avoda*, so too there is a parallel dispute here regarding the *baal mum*.

Rebbi holds that a *baal mum* who performs the *avoda* is subject to *misa bidei shamayim*. The *chachamim* hold it is a *lav*, a negative prohibition, but not *misa bidei shamayim*.

---

## Structure

### Final Chapters

Having concluded the chapters on *sekila*, *sreifa*, and *hereg* — with all their attendant digressions into *ben sorer u-moreh*, *rotzeach*, *rodef*, *kana'im pogim bo*, and *misa bidei shamayim* — what remains is *chenek*, the final mode of execution. The chapter *Elu Hen Ha-Nechnakin* covers all cases punishable by strangulation. Following that comes the final chapter, *Chelek*, which opens with the principle that all Israel has a *chelek l'olam haba* — with the exception of those enumerated cases who do not. The progression is theologically coherent: the tractate moves through all four modes of court-imposed death, and then turns to the question of what happens afterward — affirming that even those executed by the court ultimately retain their portion in the World to Come, with only a narrow category of individuals excluded.

### Bavli vs Yerushalmi

Notably, the *Yerushalmi* reverses the order: it places *Chelek* before *Elu Hen Ha-Nechnakin*. This is genuinely difficult to understand. Why, after treating *nisrafin* and *hereg* and all those digressions, would one move immediately into an aggadic discussion of who has a *chelek l'olam haba* and only then return to *nechnakin*? Furthermore, many tractates conclude with an aggadic passage that serves as a fitting capstone, and the end of *Chelek* — which is largely *aggadic* in character — does provide such a conclusion for the tractate as a whole, whereas *Elu Hen Ha-Nechnakin* ends with a purely technical halakhic discussion of *chenek*. (One might note in this connection that *Makot* was originally part of *Sanhedrin*, and *Makot* does end with an *aggadic* passage, which would address the structural concern about a proper ending — but the point about the logical ordering of the chapters remains.) The *Bavli*'s sequence is far more coherent.

### Chanuk Default

*Chenek* is the default category: any capital offense that carries an unspecified death penalty in the Torah receives *chenek*. One might therefore expect *chenek* to be a very large category. In practice, it is not as expansive as one might think, since the Torah frequently specifies a mode of execution, and those specifications have all been assigned to their respective chapters.

## Chanuk Cases

### Chanuk List

The *mishna* lists those who are executed by strangulation: the one who strikes his father or mother (*hamakeh aviv v'imo*); the kidnapper (*gonev nefesh mi-Yisrael*); the rebellious elder (*zaken mamre al pi beit din*); the false prophet (*navi hasheker*); one who prophesies in the name of *avoda zara*; a man who commits adultery (*haba al eishet ish*).

The *zaken mamre* and *navi hasheker* categories will receive extended treatment, as they do not appear elsewhere in *Shas* in this concentrated form. For the case of *navi hasheker* in particular, the *Gemara* is going to wrestle with the question of what mode of execution applies — a dispute that connects directly back to the *zar sheshimesh* debate we are about to examine.

### Bas Kohen

The *mishna* also addresses the case of *zommei bat kohen u-vo'alah* — conspiring witnesses who testify against a *bat kohen*, along with the man involved. We know that a *bat kohen* who commits adultery receives *sreifa* — the verse states *et aviha hi mechalelet ba-eish tisaref*. Yet the verse continues *hi ba-eish tisaref* — she is burned, but her partner is not. He receives whatever he would have received had she not been a *bat kohen*, which is *chenek*. The conspiring witnesses who testified against her likewise receive *chenek* — not *sreifa* — even though what they intended to do to her was to bring about her burning. This is derived both from the principle *hi ba-eish tisaref* — that the unique punishment is hers alone — and from the verse *ka-asher zamam la'asot le-achiv, le-achiv ve-lo le-achoto*: the *midah keneged midah* principle applies to "his brother," not "his sister," meaning that when the intended punishment is one uniquely assigned to a woman and to no one else, the conspiring witnesses do not receive that same punishment but rather receive *chenek*.

## Baal Mum…

### Blemish Source

Returning now to *baal mum*: what is the source for Rebbi's position that a blemished *kohen* who performs the *avoda* is subject to *misa bidei shamayim*? The verse states: *el ha-paroches lo yavo ve-el ha-mizbeach lo yigash ki mum bo — ve-lo yechalel et mikdashai* — he shall not come to the *paroches* and shall not approach the *mizbeach*, for he has a blemish, and he shall not desecrate My sanctuary. Since the verse explicitly uses the language of *chilul* — *ve-lo yechalel* — Rebbi draws a *gezeira shava* of *chilul-chilul* from *teruma*: just as *mita bidei shamayim* attaches to *chilul* in the context of *teruma*, so too it attaches here. *Mah halan b'misa af kan b'misa*.

---

## Baal Mum……

### Psul HaGuf

The *Gemara* now asks which source is the more apt basis for this analogy: should we learn *baal mum* performing the *avoda* from *tamei kohen* eating *teruma*, or from *nosar*? It makes more sense to learn from *teruma*: *shekein psul ha-guf* — both cases involve a personal disqualification residing in the person himself. The *tamei kohen* has a personal impurity; the *baal mum* has a personal blemish. *Nosar*, by contrast, is a defect in the object, not in the person. *Psul ha-guf mi-psul ha-guf*.

On the other hand, one might argue the reverse: learn from *nosar* rather than *teruma*, because *nosar* involves the *kodesh* context — it is *pnim*, within the *Beit HaMikdash*, the realm in which *pigul* and *nosar* are relevant concepts — whereas *teruma* is outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *baal mum* performing the *avoda* is an act entirely situated within the *Beit HaMikdash*, and therefore *nosar* is perhaps the better comparand.

The *Gemara* resolves this by noting that there is actually a still better comparand than either: a *kohen tamei* who performs the *avoda*. The case of *tamei kohen* serving shares both *psul ha-guf* — a personal disqualification — and the *mikdash pnim* context, since it involves the *Beit HaMikdash* where *pigul* and *nosar* are operative concepts. Learning *baal mum* from *tamei kohen* serving thus captures all the relevant features simultaneously, making it the most natural analogy.

### Chachamim View

What do the *rabanan* say in response to Rebbi? The verse in the context of *teruma* states *ve-meitu bo ki yichaleluhu* — and they shall die *bo*, "in it." The word *bo* functions as a limiting term, and the *chachamim* read it as *bo ve-lo be-vaal mum* — "in it," i.e., in that specific case of *teruma*, but not in the case of *baal mum*. Of course, the limiting word *bo* could in principle have been used to exclude any number of cases; why specifically exclude *baal mum*? Tosafot engage this question at length and discuss why it is *baal mum* rather than *tamei* that is excluded. The *chachamim*'s reasoning appears to be that the *baal mum* is less fully excluded from the Temple's sacrificial realm than the *tamei* kohen: the Torah explicitly states that a *baal mum* shares in the sacrifices that are brought — *chelek ke-chelek yochalu* — making it intelligible that his performing the *avoda* is not quite as severe as that of the *tamei kohen*, and thus does not carry *misa bidei shamayim*.

---

## Me'ilah

### Rebbi on Me'ilah

The next case is *me'ila* — the misappropriation of or deriving benefit from sanctified property. This connects back to our *mishna*, since one of the cases cited there under *kana'im pogim bo* was *gonev et ha-kisva*, stealing a Temple vessel (*kisva* being identified by the *Gemara* as a *kli shares*). *Me'ila* need not involve outright theft; any intentional derivation of benefit from sanctified property suffices. Here too, Rebbi holds that the violation of *me'ila bi-mezid* is punishable by *misa bidei shamayim*, while the *chachamim* hold it is merely a *lav*.

---

## Me'ilah…

### Cheit Comparison

What is Rebbi's basis? Rav Abba states: the verse in the context of *me'ila* uses the word *cheit* — *ve-chata bi-shgaga mi-kodshei Hashem* — and the verse in the context of *teruma* also uses *cheit* — *ve-lo yisu alav cheit u-mesu bo ki yichaleluhu*. Rebbi applies a *gezeira shava* of *cheit-cheit* from *teruma*: just as *teruma* carries *misa bidei shamayim*, so too does *me'ila*. *Mah halan b'misa af kan b'misa*.

### Chachamim Exclude

The *chachamim* again invoke the limiting word *bo* from the *teruma* verse — *ve-meitu bo ki yichaleluhu* — reading it as *bo ve-lo bi-me'ila*: the death penalty is confined to *teruma* and does not extend to *me'ila*. The same difficulty applies here as with *baal mum*: why does *bo* specifically exclude *me'ila* rather than some other case? The *Gemara* does not resolve this fully, and it remains an open question which case *bo* excludes and on what principled basis.

---

## Zar Sheshimesh

### Yishmael vs Akiva

The final entry in this list brings us back to our *mishna* directly: *zar sheshimesh be-mikdash* — a non-*kohen* who performs the Temple service. The *mishna* had recorded a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim*: Rabbi Akiva said *chenek*, and the *chachamim* said *misa bidei shamayim*. Note the structure: all the preceding debates were between *misa bidei shamayim* and a mere *lav*; here the dispute is elevated — *misa bidei shamayim* versus *chenek*, actual court-imposed execution. It is therefore necessary to understand why this particular transgression is treated differently. A *braisa* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael addresses this directly.

### Adas Korach

Rabbi Yishmael notes that the verse states *ve-ha-zar ha-karev yumat* — the stranger who draws near shall be put to death. The question is whether this means death by Heaven or death by the court. Rabbi Yishmael draws a comparison to the verse concerning *Adas Korach*: *kol ha-karev ha-karev el mishkan Hashem yamut* — all who draw near to the Tabernacle of God shall die. In that case, God Himself struck down those who drew near improperly. *Mah l'halan bidei shamayim af kan bidei shamayim*: just as there death came from Heaven, so too by the *zar* who serves, death is at the hands of Heaven. The conceptual parallel is precise: both cases involve someone unauthorized drawing near to a sacred space and being killed as a result.

### False Prophet

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He too reads *ve-ha-zar ha-karev yumat* as specifying a mode of death, but he connects it by *gezeira shava* to a different verse — *ve-ha-navi ha-hu o cholem ha-chalom ha-hu yumat*, the verse concerning the false prophet. Both use the identical word *yumat*. *Mah l'halan bidei beit din af kan bidei beit din*: just as the false prophet is executed by the court, so too the *zar* who serves is executed by the court.

### Skila vs Chenek

If the *zar* is executed by the court, as Rabbi Akiva maintains, the question becomes: by which mode? Rabbi Akiva's position, as stated in the *braisa*, is that we learn from *navi sheker* — and *navi sheker*, according to the *rabanan*, is executed by *skila*. Thus Rabbi Akiva would say *biskila af kan biskila*, execution by stoning. Rabbi Yonatan ben Nuri, however, holds that *navi sheker* is executed by *chenek*, and therefore the *zar* too would receive *chenek*.

### Three Opinions

This produces a three-way dispute: Rabbi Yishmael holds *misa bidei shamayim*; Rabbi Akiva (as reported in the *braisa*) holds *skila*; Rabbi Yonatan ben Nuri holds *chenek*. But our *mishna* records Rabbi Akiva as saying *chenek* — which is Rabbi Yonatan ben Nuri's position in the *braisa*. The *Gemara* resolves this with the formula *trei tanna'ei aliba d'Rabbi Akiva*: there is a dispute among Rabbi Akiva's students about what Rabbi Akiva actually held. The *mishna* represents Rabbi Shimon's transmission of Rabbi Akiva's view — Rabbi Shimon said *chenek* in the name of his teacher Rabbi Akiva. The *braisa* represents other students who understood Rabbi Akiva to have said *skila*.

The underlying dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is also clarified. Rabbi Akiva holds *donin yumat mi-yumat* — both the *zar* and the false prophet use the identical term *yumat*, and therefore the *zar* is executed by the court just as the false prophet is. *Ve-ein donin yumat mi-yamut* — one does not draw a *gezeira shava* from *yamut*, the different form used in the verse about *Adas Korach*. Rabbi Yishmael holds the opposite: *donin hedyot mi-hedyot ve-ein donin hedyot mi-navi* — we compare a common person who performs unauthorized service to another case involving common people acting outside their authorized role, namely *Adas Korach*, and we do not compare the common person to a *navi*, even a false one. Rabbi Akiva counters: *keivan she-hidiach ein lecha hedyot gadol mi-zeh* — since the false prophet leads people astray, he is in a category of the most ordinary sort of sinner rather than being a genuine prophet, so the comparison is valid.

What is particularly striking is the radical nature of Rabbi Akiva's position: virtually every other *mikdash*-related violation catalogued in this *sugya* is punishable by *misa bidei shamayim* at most, or simply a *lav*. Even a *tamei kohen* who enters the *Beit HaMikdash* incurs *karet*, not court-imposed death. The idea that a *zar sheshimesh* — which could be seen as a comparably serious transgression — would be punishable by the court strikes many as radical. And yet, this is precisely the position our *mishna* adopts, with the *chachamim* saying *misa bidei shamayim* and Rabbi Akiva saying *chenek*.

With this, *Elu Hen Ha-Nisrafin* concludes: *Hadran alach Elu Hen Ha-Nisrafin*.

## Makeh Aviv

### Source

The opening *mishna* of *Elu Hen Ha-Nechnakin* establishes *chenek* as the default mode of execution: *makat aviv ve-imo* — striking one's father or mother — carries the death penalty. The source is the verse *makeh aviv ve-imo mot yumat*, and the *Gemara* notes that any death penalty stated in the Torah without further specification is *chenek*.

### Makeh Not Killing

The *Gemara* immediately asks: perhaps *hamakeh* means to kill the parent — in which case this verse would be telling us that killing one's parent is punishable by *chenek*, whereas killing a stranger is punishable by the sword (*sayif*). Should we not be concerned that this would actually make the punishment *lighter* for the more severe act? The *Gemara* notes that this objection works well according to those who hold that *chenek* is a lighter death than *sayif*. But what of the opinion that *chenek* is the *weightier* form of death? According to that view, killing one's parent and receiving *chenek* would be a harsher punishment than killing a stranger and receiving the sword — which would be entirely coherent. The *Gemara* resolves the question from the verse *makeh ish ve-meit mot yumat* — when the Torah means to speak of killing, it says so explicitly: *makeh ... ve-meit*. And further: *o be-eiva hikahu be-yado va-yamut* — he struck him with anger and he died. The inference is: *shema mina kol heikha de-chtiv hikah stam, lav mita hi* — wherever the Torah says "striking" without adding "and he died," the reference is to striking alone, not to killing. *Makeh aviv ve-imo* therefore refers to striking a parent without killing him.

### Animal Comparison

The *mishna* on 84b states: *hamakeh aviv ve-imo eino chayav ad she-ya'aseh bahen chabura* — one is not liable until one makes a wound. The *Gemara* derives this from the verse that directly compares the smiting of an animal to the smiting of a person: *makeh nefesh behema yeshalmena nefesh tachat makeh adam yumat*. The comparison is precise: just as for a *behema*, the word *nefesh* indicates that some blood must have come out — since *nefesh* in this context connotes blood (*ki ha-dam hu ba-nefesh yechaper*) — so too for striking a parent, it is only when blood comes out, i.e., a *chabura* is made, that the prohibition is violated.

One might object: we use this same verse (*makeh nefesh behema... makeh adam yumat*) to derive the principle of *kim lei bi-drabbah minei*, and does it really mean that you are only required to pay damages for wounding an animal if blood comes out? The *Gemara* presses this: what if one throws stones at an animal and breaks its bones without causing any visible wound or bleeding — is one truly exempt from payment? Clearly not. So the *nefesh behema* cannot be establishing a *chabura* requirement for monetary liability in animal damage. The *Gemara* responds: since *nefesh* is in fact not needed to teach us anything about animal damages — one is obviously liable even without drawing blood — *im eino inyan le-nefesh behema, tenahu inyan le-nefesh adam*: redirect it to the case of *nefesh adam*, i.e., striking a parent. The *nefesh* comes specifically to teach that the offense against a parent requires a *chabura*.

### Refuah Exception

If *makeh* does not mean killing, and if the *chabura* requirement is the threshold, one might think any deliberate wounding of a parent transgresses the prohibition. The *Gemara* asks about the analogy further: *mah makeh behema li-refuah patur, af makeh adam li-refuah patur* — just as wounding an animal for the purpose of healing it exempts one from monetary liability, so too wounding a person in the course of medical treatment is exempt from the prohibition of *makeh avi ve-imo*. The comparison thus teaches a critical *halacha*: the prohibition does not apply when the wounding is for therapeutic purposes.

### Bloodletting

This immediately raises the question: *ben mahu she-yakiz dam le-aviv* — may a son perform bloodletting on his father? Bloodletting (*hakzat dam*) was a standard medical procedure in the *Gemara*'s world, involving deliberately opening a vein or cutting to release blood — an act that by its very nature produces a *chabura*. Two sources are offered. Rav Matna says: *ve-ahavta le-re'acha kamocha* — the general principle of love for one's fellow. This is a striking application of so broad a verse to override what would otherwise be a capital prohibition; yet here the *Gemara* deploys the principle to show that wounding a parent for therapeutic purposes is not what the Torah meant to prohibit. Rav Dimi bar Chinena offers the derivation from the *behema* comparison itself: *mah makeh behema li-refuah patur, af makeh adam li-refuah patur*.

One might argue — and a student raises a version of this point — that perhaps *makeh* itself, understood properly, would not apply to a therapeutic procedure: the word *makeh* implies an act of aggression or violation, not a surgical intervention performed with consent and for benefit. This is a conceptually powerful observation. The *Gemara* does not explicitly adopt this framing here, choosing instead to derive the exemption from the *behema* comparison and from *ve-ahavta le-re'acha kamocha*, but the intuition behind the question is sound.

### Accidental Wounding

The *Gemara* concludes with a case that is genuinely surprising. The *mishna* in the context of *Shabbos* permits using a sewing needle to remove a splinter on *Shabbos*, presumably because it is a permitted use of an implement. The *Gemara* asks: should we not be concerned that in the process of removing the splinter one might accidentally cause some bleeding — which would render one liable for *chilul Shabbos*? The answer given is *hatam me-kalkeil* — there, causing blood to come out is a *me-kalkeil* act, a destructive act, which is exempt from *Shabbos* liability. But the *Gemara* then raises a further question about removing a splinter from someone else in a non-*Shabbos* context: if one accidentally causes a *chabura* while performing what is otherwise a beneficial act — extracting a splinter from a friend — is that a *shiggas lav* of *makeh* the person? The *Gemara*'s conclusion is that it is indeed treated as a *shiggas lav*, even though the intent was entirely beneficial and the wound was unintended.

This conclusion is genuinely puzzling. The language of *eino miskaven* — unintentional action — would normally suggest exemption, and the therapeutic purpose would seem to fall under the *li-refuah patur* rule. Why this case constitutes a *shiggas lav* — even an accidental one — when the intent was beneficent and the blood came out as an unwanted byproduct, is not resolved at this point in the *sugya*. The *Gemara*'s treatment seems to rest on the fact that the bleeding, while unintended, was not incidental to the purpose of the act in the same way that bloodletting is the *point* of the therapeutic procedure. Because the splinter removal is aimed at something other than the blood — the blood is a possible but not necessary or desired byproduct — it may not fall under the rubric of *makeh li-refuah*. Whether this is fully coherent will be reviewed when the *sugya* continues.
