# Pesachim 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed-Up Pesachim

### Five Pesachim w/ Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ עוֹרוֹת פִּסְחֵיהֶן זֶה בָּזֶה, וְנִמְצְאוּת יַבֶּלֶת בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן — כּוּלָּן יוֹצְאִין לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה. אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאִיזְּרִיק דָּם — הֲוָה חֲזֵי לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל נִתְעָרְבוּ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה — חַיָּיבִין לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** **Five** people had the **hides of their Paschal lambs mixed up together, and a wart was found on one of them. All of them go out to the place for burning.** **Abaye said: They taught** that they are all exempt from the second *Pesaḥ* in a case **where** the hides **were mixed up together only after the sprinkling** of the blood on the altar, **for at the time the blood** of each of the other four animals **was sprinkled,** each one of the four unblemished lambs **was, at any rate, fit to be eaten,** and therefore the owners are considered to have fulfilled their obligation to slaughter a fit Paschal lamb. As such, they are exempt from the second *Pesaḥ*. **But if they were mixed up together before the sprinkling,** each of the five lambs could possibly be the blemished one. Because of the doubt that exists with regard to all of them, they are all disqualified from being offered. Therefore, none of the five people fulfill their obligation, and **they are** all **obligated to observe** the **second *Pesaḥ*.**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita* describing a case in which five different groups each brought a separate *korban Pesach*, and afterward the skins became mixed up. One of the animals was found to bear a *mum* (blemish), but it is unknown whose it was. The *gemara* modifies the scenario: the discovery occurred after the blood had already been thrown, meaning that at the time of *zerika* all five were presumptively valid. Since no one can identify which animal was blemished, all five carcasses must be burned — and, critically, none of the five groups knows whether they are the one obligated in a *Pesach Sheni*, so the *gemara* initially suggests that no one brings a *Pesach Sheni*. The *gemara* immediately objects: clearly one of the five *is* obligated. The problem is identifying who.

### Individual Pesach Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַיְתֵי כׇּל חַד וְחַד פֶּסַח, וְנַיְתְנֵי וְנֵימָא: אִי דִּידִי בַּעַל מוּם — הַאי דְּאַיְיתַי הַשְׁתָּא נִיהְוֵי פֶּסַח, וְאִי דִּידִי תָּם — הַאי דְּאַיְיתַי הַשְׁתָּא נִיהְוֵי שְׁלָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Let each one bring his Paschal lamb and stipulate and say: If mine was the blemished** lamb, **this** lamb **that I am bringing now shall be a Paschal lamb, and if mine was unblemished, this** lamb **that I am bringing now shall be a peace-offering!**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא אֶפְשָׁר, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק, דְּכֹהֲנִים הוּא דְּאָכְלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is not possible** to do this, **because there are** the **breast and** the **hind leg, which the priests eat** only if the offering is a peace-offering, but not if it is a Paschal lamb. Due to the distinction between the two types of offerings, the proposed stipulation does not provide a solution. It would remain unclear who should eat these portions of the offering.

The *gemara's* first proposed solution is to have each of the five bring his own *Pesach Sheni* with a *tenai* (stipulation): if my original offering was the blemished one, let this be my *Pesach Sheni*; if not, let it be a *shelamim*. The *gemara* objects: if it is a *shelamim*, the *kohen* must receive the *chazeh v'shok* (the breast and the side). So, one might suggest, simply give those portions to the *kohen* as a precaution — perhaps it is a *Pesach* and perhaps it is a *shelamim*, so cover both possibilities. But this fails: if the animal is in fact a *Pesach*, then the *kohen* who eats from it must be a registered member of that group — and eating the *chazeh v'shok* of a *Pesach* one is not enrolled in is forbidden. There is no clean way to handle the *chazeh v'shok*, and so this approach collapses.

### Priest Covers All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי כׇּל חַד וְחַד כֹּהֵן בַּהֲדֵיהּ. הַאי כֹּהֵן הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּעָבֵיד פֶּסַח — דִּילְמָא הַאי פֶּסַח הוּא, וְנִמְצָא פֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל שֶׁלֹּא לִמְנוּיָו. וְאִי דְּלָא עָבֵיד פֶּסַח — דִּילְמָא שְׁלָמִים הוּא, וְלָא עָבֵיד פֶּסַח!
>
> **Translation:** **Let each one** of these five people bringing a new Paschal lamb **bring a priest** to be registered **with him** for their Paschal lamb. Then the priest will eat the breasts and hind legs. Since the priest eats these portions of the offering in any event, there is no longer any practical distinction between the two types of offerings. The stipulation should therefore provide a solution.
>
> **What are the circumstances** of **this priest? If he had** already **performed** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb** during the first *Pesaḥ*, then, as the Gemara explained previously, he is unable to be registered to partake of a new Paschal lamb. If so, there is concern that **perhaps this** offering **is a Paschal lamb** and not a peace-offering, **and it will turn out** that a **Paschal lamb is eaten by those who did not register for it. And if,** on the other hand, the priest **had not performed** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb** on the first *Pesaḥ*, there is concern that **perhaps** this offering **is a peace-offering,** and it would turn out that this priest **did not perform** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb** at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי כֹּל חַמְשָׁה חַד כֹּהֵן דְּלָא עֲבַד פֶּסַח, וְנִימְּנֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ הָנֵי חֲמִשָּׁה פְּסָחִים, דְּמִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ אִיכָּא חַד דְּלָא עָבֵיד פֶּסַח, וְקָא נָפְקִי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Let all five** together **bring one priest, who has not** yet **performed** the ritual of **the Paschal lamb. And let** the priest **register for** all **these five Paschal lambs.** This should provide a solution. **Whichever way you look at it, there is one** person **who has not** yet **performed** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb, and** the priest will now **fulfill** his obligation together **with him.** If the priest then eats the breasts and hind legs of all the offerings, the stipulation should provide a viable solution.

The *gemara* advances a second solution: *veneyti kol chad vechad kohen bahadeih* — let each of the five bring along a *kohen* to be a member of his group. Then the *kohen* would be entitled to the *chazeh v'shok* should it turn out to be a *shelamim*. But the *gemara* asks: what kind of *kohen* are we talking about? If he already brought his own *Pesach Rishon*, then he is not obligated in a *Pesach Sheni*, and if the animal being brought now is in fact a *Pesach Sheni*, then this *kohen* is eating from a *Pesach* to which he does not belong — *venimtza Pesach ne'echal shelo limnuyav*. If, on the other hand, this *kohen* did *not* bring a *Pesach Rishon* and is himself obligated in a *Pesach Sheni*, then the reverse problem arises: if the animal turns out to be a *shelamim* and not a *Pesach Sheni*, the *kohen* has failed to be *yotzei* his own *Pesach Sheni* obligation.

The *gemara* then proposes a refinement: *veneyti kol chamisha chad kohen* — let all five groups together bring in a single *kohen* who did not bring a *Pesach Rishon* and is thus obligated in a *Pesach Sheni*. This *kohen* conditionally registers with each of the five groups separately: if you are the one obligated in *Pesach Sheni*, I am part of your group; and likewise for each of the others. *Demia nafshach* — either way it works out. Exactly one of the five animals is a genuine *Pesach Sheni*, and from that one this *kohen* fulfills his own obligation. The remaining four are *shelamim*, and from those he receives the *chazeh v'shok* of a *shelamim*, to which he is fully entitled. He does not need to know which is which; the halakhic reality sorts itself out.

### Sanctify as Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מְמַעֵיט בַּאֲכִילַת שְׁלָמִים, דְּאִילּוּ פֶּסַח לְיוֹם וְלַיְלָה, וְאִילּוּ שְׁלָמִים לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reason it is not possible to use the stipulation is **because it would reduce the** amount of time available for **eating the peace-offering, for a Paschal lamb** may be eaten only **for a day and a night, whereas a peace-offering** may be eaten **for two days and one night.** Were the stipulation to be made, all the offerings would have an uncertain status of either being a peace-offering or a Paschal lamb. They would therefore all be treated with the strictures of a Paschal lamb, and any meat remaining on the morning after the first night would have to be burnt. However, in the event that the offering was actually a peace-offering, this would be premature, since it may still be eaten for another day.

The *gemara* then raises a new difficulty: *ella mishum de'ka mema'et be'achilat shelamim* — even with the *kohen* solution in place, each owner is still bringing an animal designated either as a *Pesach Sheni* or, conditionally, as a *shelamim*. A *Pesach* must be eaten by daybreak of the fifteenth of Nissan, whereas a *shelamim* may be eaten through two days and one night. Since the owner must treat the animal as if it might be a *Pesach*, he is constrained to finish it by daybreak — thereby squandering the additional eating time that would have been available had it been a *shelamim*. *D'ilu Pesach l'yom valailah, v'ilu shelamim lishnei yamim valailah echad*: the time-frames are incompatible, and by treating it as a *Pesach* one potentially causes the meat of what is really a *shelamim* to become *notar*.

### Motar Pesach Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי מוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח. וְנֵימָא: אִי דִּידִי בַּעַל מוּם — הַאי דְּאַיְיתַי הַשְׁתָּא נִיהְוֵי פֶּסַח, אִי דִּידִי תָּם הוּא — נִיהְוֵי הַאי דְּאַיְיתַי הַשְׁתָּא שְׁלָמִים, דְּמוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל לְיוֹם וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Let** each one of **them** specify that if they have already fulfilled their obligations to bring a Paschal lamb, their intention is to consecrate and **bring** the current offering as **a surplus Paschal lamb.** The status of a surplus Paschal lamb is usually achieved when an animal is originally consecrated as a Paschal lamb but for some reason is not offered. Such an animal is offered as a type of peace-offering.
>
> The Gemara assumes here that it is also possible to consecrate an animal directly with this status, and therefore the following stipulation could be used. **And** when bringing their lambs **they should** each **say: If mine was the blemished** lamb, **this** lamb **that I am bringing now shall be a Paschal lamb;** and **if mine was unblemished, this** lamb **that I am bringing now shall be a** surplus Paschal lamb, which is a type of **peace-offering** and may therefore be offered even if one has already fulfilled his obligation to bring a Paschal lamb. Although **a surplus Paschal lamb** has the sanctity of a peace-offering, it **may be eaten for** only one **day and one night,** similar to a Paschal lamb. Since there is no distinction between the time allotted to eat the two types of offerings, this stipulation should provide a solution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַפְרִישִׁין תְּחִלָּה לַמּוֹתָרוֹת?! וְנִטְרְחוּ וְנַיְיתֵי מוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח!
>
> **Translation:** **But can we designate** animals **initially** as **surplus** Paschal lambs? This cannot be done; the sanctity of a surplus Paschal lamb is achieved only with an animal that was initially designated for a Paschal lamb but remained unused. If so, how is the suggested stipulation tenable? The Gemara explains: **Let them trouble themselves** to find **and bring** an unused leftover animal already invested with the sanctity of **a surplus Paschal lamb,** and let them make the suggested stipulation upon it. In this way, the stipulation should work and provide a solution.

The *gemara* proposes a solution: designate the animal not as an ordinary *shelamim* but as a *shelamim* arising from *motar Pesach* — leftover sanctified *Pesach* funds or animals. A *motar Pesach* brought as a *shelamim* is eaten only for a day and a night (not the full two days), matching the constraint already imposed by the *Pesach* possibility. Each person could declare: if my original offering was blemished, this is my *Pesach Sheni*; if not, let this be a *shelamim* that derives from *motar Pesach*.

According to Rashi, the *gemara* rejects this on the grounds that one cannot *initially* sanctify something as *motar* — leftovers, by definition, can only arise organically, not by stipulation. Tosafot, however, reads the *gemara* differently. No one would have thought to sanctify something explicitly as *motar*; rather, Tosafot understands the *gemara* to be proposing that one simply sanctify the animal as a *Pesach Sheni* outright, without any backup designation. If it then turns out that the person was already *yotzei* in Nissan and does not need a *Pesach Sheni*, the animal automatically acquires the status of *motar Pesach* — no stipulation required; that is simply the halakhic reality.

The *gemara*'s response to Tosafot's reading is that while this would technically work *bedi'avad*, it is problematic *lechatchila*: one should not sanctify an animal knowing it may end up as an unwanted *motar*. It would be preferable to stipulate the backup designation explicitly as a *shelamim*, even though that reintroduces the time-limitation problem.

### Semicha by Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם סְמִיכָה. דְּאִילּוּ פֶּסַח לָא בָּעֵי סְמִיכָה, וְאִילּוּ מוֹתָר בָּעֵי סְמִיכָה. הָא תִּינַח קׇרְבַּן אֲנָשִׁים. קׇרְבַּן נָשִׁים, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is not possible to use the stipulation **because** of the requirement to perform the act of **placing** one's hands on the head of the offering, **for a Paschal lamb does not need** the act of **placing** one's hands, **whereas a surplus** Paschal lamb, since it is a type of peace-offering, **does need** the act of **placing** one's hands. Due to this distinction, the proposed stipulation cannot provide a solution. It would remain unclear whether the offering requires one to perform the act of placing one's hands on its head, an act that one is prohibited to do on an offering that does not require it.
>
> **This** distinction **works out well** to explain why the stipulation can't be used for an **offering of men,** since a man's offering requires the act of placing one's hands. However, if the case involved an **offering of women,** for which there is no such requirement, **what is there to say?** In such a case, there is no distinction between a Paschal lamb and a surplus Paschal lamb, and therefore the stipulation should provide a solution.

Leaving the time problem aside, the *gemara* identifies yet another obstacle: *ella mishum semikhah*. A *shelamim* requires *semicha* — the owner rests his hands on the offering — whereas a *Pesach* does not. If the animal is a *Pesach* and one performs *semicha*, he is pressing his weight on a *korban* without cause, which is a rabbinic concern. If the animal is a *shelamim* and one omits *semicha*, the obligation is unfulfilled.

The *gemara* notes that this problem is solvable — *hatanach anashim* — if the five individuals are women: women are not required to perform *semicha* on their *shelamim*. If the animal is a *Pesach*, no *semicha* is needed. If it is a *shelamim* of a woman, *semicha* is also not required. So for women's offerings this particular obstacle falls away.

### Dam Shfika Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם מַתָּנוֹת. דְּאִילּוּ פֶּסַח — מַתָּנָה אַחַת, וְאִילּוּ שְׁלָמִים — שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע. מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? וְהָא תְּנַן: כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתַּן אַחַת — כִּפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is not possible to use the stipulation **because** of the requirement to apply **presentations** of sacrificial blood upon the altar. **For whereas** the blood of **a Paschal lamb** is applied to the altar in **one presentation,** the blood of a peace-offering is applied to the altar with **two that constitute four,** i.e., the blood is applied on two opposite corners so that it runs down all four sides of the altar.
>
> **What difference does** the distinction in the number of presentations **make? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **all** offerings whose blood must be **presented upon the outer altar,** once the blood has been **presented with one presentation,** the offering **has atoned,** even if more presentations are ideally required? If so, it should be possible to use the stipulation and then make a single presentation of blood upon the altar, which would be valid for whichever type of offering it turned out to be.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִילּוּ פֶּסַח בִּשְׁפִיכָה, וְאִילּוּ שְׁלָמִים בִּזְרִיקָה. מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין בִּזְרִיקָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן בִּשְׁפִיכָה יָצָא. אֵימַר דְּקָא אָמְרִינַן דְּאִי עֲבַד, לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proposal is not possible **because** of the following: **Whereas the** blood of the **Paschal lamb** must be applied to the altar **by pouring** it on the base of the altar, the blood of **a peace-offering is** applied **by sprinkling** it upon the altar.
>
> **What difference does** the manner of applying the blood **make? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **all** offerings whose blood must be applied to the altar **by sprinkling,** if **one applied** their blood **by pouring, one has** nonetheless **fulfilled** one's obligation? If so, it should be possible to use the stipulation and then pour the blood upon the base of the altar. This pouring would be valid, whichever type of offering it turned out to be. The Gemara answers: **Say** in response **that we say** that one fulfilled his obligation only **if he** already **did** it this way; but would he be allowed to do so ***ab initio* as well?** Certainly not. Since the offering cannot be offered in a manner that is permissible *ab initio*, the distinction in the manner of applying the blood will preclude the use of the stipulation.

Even with women's offerings, the *gemara* raises a further complication concerning the blood: *ella mishum matanot*. A *Pesach* requires only a single application of blood (*matanah achat*) on the *mizbeach*, whereas a *shelamim* requires two applications on diagonal corners, which together touch all four sides (*shtayim she'hein arba*). The *gemara* asks whether this matters: after all, the *Mishnah* teaches that *bedi'avad*, if any offering whose blood requires multiple applications had only one application made, it is still valid. So perform a single application and be covered in either case.

The *gemara* then raises a more fundamental distinction: *ella mishum de'ilu Pesach bishfika* — for a *Pesach* the blood is *poured* against the wall (*shefikh*), while for a *shelamim* it is *thrown* (*zerika*). Again the *gemara* notes that a *baraita* rules *bedi'avad* that anything meant to be thrown but instead poured is valid. But here the *gemara* draws the line: *aymur de'amrinan* — all of these *bedi'avad* leniencies apply only after the fact, *ee avad*, if one has already acted in such a manner. They cannot justify acting improperly *lechatchila*.

### Tosafot Objection

This brings us to the objection raised by Tosafot, which cuts to the heart of the entire discussion. Tosafot asks the obvious question: each of the concerns raised — limiting the time for *shelamim*, the *semicha* issue, the improper blood application — is relatively minor. Against these stands the possibility that one of the five individuals will fail to bring a *Pesach Sheni* that he is genuinely obligated in, risking *safek karet*. How can the *gemara* refuse all proposed solutions on *lechatchila* grounds when the alternative is *safek karet*?

Tosafot answers: *tov sheyidache safek karet beshev ve'al ta'aseh mimah she'ya'asu ruban shelo kehogen* — it is preferable to allow the possibility of *safek karet* through passive inaction than to require four out of five people to perform their *korbanot* improperly in order to protect the one. Invoking the principle of *omrim le'adam chet kedei she'yizkeh chaveirecha* — we do not tell one person to sin so that another may be acquitted — the *gemara* concludes that since the person in this predicament is in any case an *oneis*, we trust that he will be forgiven. At the very least, Tosafot is right to flag this as the obvious and pressing question underlying the entire passage.

## Father's Stipulation

### Who Gets There First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְבָנָיו: הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל מִי שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה מִכֶּם רִאשׁוֹן לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִכְנִיס הָרִאשׁוֹן רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ — זָכָה בְּחֶלְקוֹ וּמְזַכֶּה אֶת אֶחָיו עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to his children: I am slaughtering the Paschal lamb on behalf of whomever of you goes up first to Jerusalem, as soon as the first** of the children **has entered his head and the majority of his** body into Jerusalem, **he has acquired his portion and acquires** on behalf of **his brothers** their portions together **with him.**

The *Mishnah* presents a case in which a father declares: *hareini shocheit et hapesach al mi she'ya'aleh mikem rishon l'Yerushalayim* — "I am slaughtering the *Pesach* for whichever of you arrives in Jerusalem first." He slaughters it, and the definition of "arriving first" is *rosho ve'rubo* — the majority of the body having crossed the threshold, analogous to the principle of *rosho ve'rubo basukka*. Whoever first achieved *rosho ve'rubo* inside Jerusalem retroactively satisfies the condition and is considered the one enrolled in the *korban*. Moreover, the *Mishnah* adds, *zachah be'chelko u'mezakeh et echav imo* — the first son who "wins" can then bring his brothers to join in with his portion.

### Incentive, Not Breira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כְּדֵי לְזָרְזָן בְּמִצְווֹת קָאָמַר. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי: וּמְזַכֶּה אֶחָיו עִמּוֹ, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאַמְנִינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּלָא אַמְנִינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, לְבָתַר דְּשָׁחֵיט מִי קָא מִתְמַנּוּ? וְהָא תְּנַן: נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין יְדֵיהֶן מִמֶּנּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **You can learn from** the case in the mishna that **there is retroactive clarification.** Although when the father slaughters the Paschal lamb it cannot be known which child will ultimately enter Jerusalem first, once one of the children does enter, the children are considered to have been registered for the father's Paschal lamb from the outset. This is problematic, as no halakhic conclusion has been reached in the matter of retroactive clarification.
>
> **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The mishna is not based on retroactive clarification. Rather, the father included all his children in his Paschal lamb from the outset. He created this competition only **in order to enthuse them,** so that they would be expeditious **in** their fulfillment of **mitzvot;** but in fact his statement had no halakhic implications.
>
> According to the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan, the language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: And he acquires** on behalf of **his brothers** their portions together **with him. Granted, if you say that** the father **registered them from the beginning,** before slaughtering the Paschal lamb, the case **is well** understood. **But if you say** that **he did not register them from the beginning,** but only **after he slaughtered** the Paschal lamb, **can they** then **be registered? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: People can **be registered and withdraw themselves from** being registered for a Paschal lamb **until it is slaughtered,** but not after? Clearly, then, the children must have already been registered from the outset, and we may **learn from this** precise reading of the mishna that Rabbi Yoḥanan's explanation is correct.

The *gemara* immediately notes that this *Mishnah* appears to rely on *breira* — the principle of retroactive determination — which has been contested throughout these chapters. *Rabbi Yochanan* resolves this: *kedei lezarezan be'mitzvot kamar* — the father did not genuinely mean to create a legal stipulation. His declaration was purely motivational, intended to make his children eager and enthusiastic in the performance of *mitzvot*. In reality, the father had all his children in mind from the moment of *shechita*, so there is no actual *breira* involved.

The *gemara* finds support for this reading from within the *Mishnah* itself: *dika nami dektani u'mezakeh et echav imo*. If the father truly intended only the first-arriving son to be enrolled, there would be no mechanism by which that son could afterward bring his brothers into the group. As the very next *Mishnah* teaches, one can join or withdraw only until the moment of *shechita* — and once it is slaughtered, no new enrollments are possible. The fact that the brothers can join afterward proves that they were always implicitly included, and the father's statement was never a real condition.

The *Mishnah* is thus teaching us how to interpret this type of parental declaration as a matter of *halakha*: given the assumption that family members are considered included in the *Pesach* unless they actively object, the father's words are read as encouragement rather than exclusion.

### Daughters vs. Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מַעֲשֶׂה וְקָדְמוּ בָּנוֹת לְבָנִים, וְנִמְצָא בָּנוֹת זְרִיזוֹת וּבָנִים שְׁפָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **That** the competition was meant only to enthuse his children to be expeditious in their fulfillment of mitzvot **was also** implied by that which was **taught** in the conclusion of a *baraita* that also records this competition: There was **an incident** such as this, **and the daughters preceded the sons. And it turned out that the daughters** demonstrated that they **were enthusiastic,** whereas **the sons** demonstrated that they **were lazy.**

A *baraita* is cited: *maase ve'kadmu banot le'vanim* — there was an incident in which daughters arrived in Jerusalem before the sons, so that *nimtzu banot zerizot u'vanim shefalim* — the daughters turned out to be the zealous ones and the sons the sluggards. Tellingly, the *baraita* does not say *nimtzu banot ochlot u'vanim einam ochlim* — it does not say the daughters ate and the sons did not. The only consequence noted is a matter of honor and zeal, which confirms that the father's stipulation was never meant as a binding legal condition determining who eats. It was entirely a device to spur eagerness in the performance of the *mitzvah*.

## Membership Changes

### Kezayit Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לְעוֹלָם נִמְנִין עָלָיו עַד שֶׁיִּהְיֶה בּוֹ כְּזַיִת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Additional people **can always be registered for** a Paschal lamb, **as long as there will be** at least **an olive-bulk** of the lamb's meat **for each and every** person registered.

The *Mishnah* teaches that one may continue adding members to a *chavura* for a *Pesach* as long as each member retains at least a *kezayit*-sized portion. There is no upper limit on the number of members, only this lower bound: *leolam nimnin alav ad sheyesh bo kezayit le'chol echad ve'echad*.

### Rav Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶן מִמֶּנּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּזָּרֵק אֶת הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** People **can be registered and withdraw themselves from** being registered for a Paschal lamb **until it is slaughtered. Rabbi Shimon says:** Even **until** the priest **sprinkles the blood.**

The *Mishnah* further states that members may join or withdraw until the moment of *shechita*. Rabbi Shimon goes further: *ad sheyizarek hadam* — withdrawal is permitted even after *shechita*, up until the blood is thrown on the *mizbeach*. This is a surprising position, as we will see qualified by the *gemara*.

### Replace Whole Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִימְּנִי עָלָיו חֲבוּרָה זוֹ, חוֹזֶרֶת וְנִמְנִין עָלָיו חֲבוּרָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the first clause of the mishna **teaching us?** It is obvious that additional people can be registered. **This comes to teach us that although this group was registered for it,** the entire group **can withdraw and a different group can register for it,** despite the fact that no one from the original group remains.

The *gemara* asks what the *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* is. The answer: *de'af al gav de'mimnu alav chavura zu, chozer ve'nimnin alav chavura acheret* — even if one complete *chavura* had already registered for this *Pesach*, the entire group may be replaced by a new *chavura* before *shechita*. There is an opinion that at least one original member must remain, and it is this opinion that the *Mishnah* comes to exclude: complete replacement is permitted prior to *shechita*.

### Join/Withdraw Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחֲלוֹקֶת לִימָּשֵׁךְ, דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״מִהְיוֹת מִשֶּׂה״ — מֵחַיּוּתֵיהּ דְּשֶׂה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: מֵהֲוָיָיתֵיהּ דְּשֶׂה. אֲבָל לִימָּנוֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״בְּמִכְסַת נְפָשֹׁת״, וַהֲדַר: ״תָּכֹסּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: The dispute** is only with regard to when one is able **to withdraw** from being registered. Both sides derive their opinions from the following verse: "And if the household be too little for a lamb [*miheyot miseh*], then shall he and his neighbor next to his house take one according to the number of the souls; according to every man's eating you shall make your count for the lamb" (Exodus 12:4). The phrase *miheyot miseh* literally means: From being from a lamb. It is exegetically interpreted to refer to withdrawing from being registered for a lamb.
>
> **The Rabbis hold** that **"from being [*miheyot*] from a lamb"** indicates that one can withdraw only **during the life [*meiḥayutei*] of the lamb,** i.e., while the lamb is still alive. **And Rabbi Shimon holds** that the phrase indicates that one can withdraw **during the happenings [*meihavayatei*] of the lamb,** i.e., as long as the sacrificial service is still being performed, which culminates with the application of the blood upon the altar. **But** with regard **to being registered** for the Paschal lamb, **all agree that** one can be registered for a lamb only **until it is slaughtered, as the** previously cited **verse** concerning registration **states,** in its conclusion: **"According to the number [*bemikhsat*] of the people," and then "you shall make your count [*takhosu*]."** The word *takhosu* is taken to mean: You shall slaughter, in accordance with its meaning in Aramaic. The order of the verse therefore indicates that people can be registered only prior to the animal's slaughter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶן מִמֶּנּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: נִמְנִין עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט, וּמוֹשְׁכִין עַד שֶׁיִּזָּרֵק הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: People **can be registered and withdraw themselves from** being registered for a Paschal lamb **until it is slaughtered. Rabbi Shimon says: They can be registered until it is slaughtered and withdraw themselves** from being registered **until** the priest **sprinkles the blood.**

Abaye clarifies the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon: the disagreement concerns *withdrawal* only. The Rabbis derive from the verse *ve'im yim'at ha'bayit mehiyot miseh* that the *bayit* (i.e., *chavura*) may be reduced *michiyotei de'seh* — while the animal is still alive, i.e., before *shechita*. Rabbi Shimon reads *mehivaya de'seh* — until the animal has completed its process of being sacrificed, i.e., until the blood is thrown.

On joining, however, all agree: *nimnin ad shechita* — enrollment must occur before *shechita*. Rabbi Shimon does not extend his leniency to new enrollments after *shechita*, because the verse *bemichsas nefashot tachosu* — "according to the number of souls, slaughter" — is read as requiring that the count of members be established before the act of *shechita*. A member must be enrolled from the beginning of the sacrificial process; withdrawing, says Rabbi Shimon, remains possible until *zerika* since the *korban* is not yet complete until the blood has been thrown. This is confirmed by a *baraita* that states explicitly: *nimnin ad sheyishachat, u'moshchin ad sheyizarek hadam*.

## Uninvited Guest

### Solo Invitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְמַנֶּה עִמּוֹ אַחֵר בְּחֶלְקוֹ — רַשָּׁאִין בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה לִיתֵּן לוֹ אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְהֵן אוֹכְלִין מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one who is registered for a Paschal lamb unilaterally **registers another** person **with him in his portion** of the Paschal lamb, the other **members of** his **group are permitted to give him,** i.e., the one who included the additional person, only **his** portion, which was originally allotted to him. **And he,** the additional person, **eats from his** portion, i.e., the portion of he who added him; **and they,** the other members of the group, **eat from theirs.** This is because they did not agree to the inclusion of the additional person.

The *Mishnah* presents the case of *ha'memaneh imo acher be'chelko*: a member of a *chavura* goes ahead and invites someone to join the group without consulting his fellow members.

The *Mishnah* rules: *reshayim bnei chavura litein lo et shelo ve'hu ochel et shelo ve'hein ochlim et shelahem* — the other members of the *chavura* may give the inviting member his portion and have him eat it with his guest separately, while they eat their shares among themselves. As Rashi emphasizes, the language of *ve'hu ochel et shelo* implies a genuine physical separation into two distinct *chaburot*, not merely a portioning out while remaining at the same table. The one who issued the unauthorized invitation takes his share and the two of them form their own group, while the original members eat as their own group.

### Glutton Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה שֶׁהָיוּ יָדָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפוֹת, מַהוּ שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ: טוֹל חֶלְקְךָ וָצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is among the **members of a group one of them who has fine hands,** a euphemism for one who always hastens to take a large quantity of food, **what is** the *halakha* concerning whether **they can say to him: Take your** allotted **portion** to eat **and leave;** and don't take any more from the other's members portions?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן, מָצֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָא קַבֵּילְתּוּן. אוֹ דִילְמָא, מָצוּ לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: כִּי קַבֵּלְינַן — לְתַיקּוֹנֵי זְבִיחָה, אַדַּעְתָּא דְּאָכְלַתְּ טְפֵי מִינַּן — לָא קַבֵּלְינָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Do we say** that **he can say to them: You accepted me** in the group without preconditions, and you therefore have no right to limit how much I can take now? **Or perhaps they can say to him: When we accepted you, it was** only **for the preparation of the offering,** to ensure that enough people would be registered to guarantee that the entire offering would be eaten with none left over. However, **we did not accept you** with **the understanding that you would eat** considerably **more than us.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְמַנֶּה אֲחֵרִים עִמּוֹ עַל חֶלְקוֹ — רַשָּׁאִין [בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה] לִיתֵּן לוֹ אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהֵן אוֹכְלִין אֶת שֶׁלָּהֶן. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי לֵיהּ כְּיָדַיִם שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפוֹת. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יָדָיו יָפוֹת מָצֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ קַבֵּילְתּוּן, נִיהְוֵי הַאי כְּיָדָיו יָפוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from what we learned in the mishna: If one who registered for a Paschal lamb **registers other** people **with him in his portion,** the other **members of the group are permitted to give him** only **his** portion. **And** then **he** and the additional people **eat from his** portion, **and they,** the other members of the group, **eat from theirs. What is the reason** for this? **Is it not because it is comparable to** a case where **one of them has fine hands,** because in the case of one who adds a person, he and the additional person together eat more than one portion? **And if it could enter your mind** to say that one with **fine hands can say to them: You accepted me** without preconditions, then **this** person, who registered an additional person to join him, **should be** considered **as though he had fine hands,** and he should be able to take a double portion. Therefore, the mishna rules that the group can limit the quantity of food he takes. It is apparent that the claim of one with fine hands is not accepted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא, דֵּעוֹת שָׁאנֵי, דְּאִי נָמֵי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ כְּחַד מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה הוּא דְּאָכְלִי, מָצֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: דְּלָא נִיחָא לַן אִינָשׁ נוּכְרָא גַּבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **They say: No,** a comparison should not be drawn. Including additional people of individual **minds is different. Even if both of them** together would **eat** a quantity **equivalent to a single member of the group,** the other members of the group **can** nonetheless **say to him: It is not pleasant for us** to have **a strange person among us.** Therefore, no proof can be offered from the mishna.

The *gemara* then raises a question: *bnei chavura she'hayu yedeihem shel echad mehen yafot* — what if after joining together, one member turns out to eat disproportionately more than the others? The idiom is that his "hands are nice" — a polite way of saying he grabs and eats at an aggressive pace. May the group tell him *tol chelkecha u'tzei* — take your portion and leave?

Two positions are considered. One: the glutton may argue *ki kibbaltuhu* — you accepted me as a member of this group and the communal arrangement stands, you cannot now expel me. Two: the group may counter *ki kablinan le'tikkunei zevach* — we accepted you for the purpose of the *korban*, meaning to ensure that the meat would be consumed appropriately; we did not accept you on the assumption that you would eat disproportionately.

The *gemara* attempts to prove from the earlier case of the unauthorized invitation that the group can expel a disproportionate eater, reasoning that the case of the invited outsider is analogous to a person with a huge appetite. The *gemara* deflects this comparison: *deshani* — two people (the inviting member and his uninvited guest) are different from one overeater. Even if the two combined eat no more than a single normal portion, the group's right to expel them has nothing to do with quantity. *Matzi amri lei de'lo nicha lan inish de'garuch gaban* — they can simply say we do not want a stranger at our table; *kol de'chazina leh u'merachamana leh yativ ve'yeichol* — only someone we know and like is welcome to sit and eat with us. The case of the uninvited guest thus rests entirely on the social intimacy of the *chavura*, not on the financial concern of disproportionate eating. Whether the purely financial argument — the glutton eating more than his share — is itself grounds for expulsion remains the open question that the *gemara* proceeds to resolve.

## Shamash by Oven

### Shamash Eats First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַשַּׁמָּשׁ שֶׁאָכַל כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּצַד הַתַּנּוּר, אִם הָיָה פִּקֵּחַ — מְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ. אִם רָצוּ בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה לַעֲשׂוֹת טוֹבָה עִמּוֹ — בָּאִין וְיוֹשְׁבִין בְּצִדּוֹ וְאוֹכְלִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רָצוּ — אִין, לֹא רָצוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the question from another mishna: In the case of **an attendant who ate an olive-bulk of meat** of the Paschal lamb **next to the oven** in which it is being roasted, **if he were judicious, he should** continue eating there to **fill his stomach with it.** By eating **an olive-bulk,** he has established his current location as his place for eating his Paschal lamb, and he may not eat any more in an additional location. **If the members of the group wanted to do him a favor** so that he may continue eating, **they can come and sit at his side and eat** there; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara makes the following inference: **If they wanted** to do him a favor, **yes;** but **if they did not want** to do so, **no.**

The *gemara* introduces the case of the *shamash* (attendant) who was roasting the *Pesach* by the oven and inadvertently — or by taking a small taste — ate a *kezayit* of meat right there, *betzad tanur*. Having now eaten in that location, he cannot join the group in the other room to eat the *Pesach*, since the *Pesach* may not be eaten in two locations. *Im haya pikeach, memalei kreiso mimenu* — if he is smart, he should fill his stomach right there by the oven, since he cannot move to join the group in any case.

Rabbi Yehuda teaches: *ve'im ratzu bnei chavurah la'asos tovah imo, bo'in ve'yoshvin betzido ve'ochlin* — if the members of the group wish to be kind to the *shamash*, they may come and sit beside him at the oven and eat there. *Lo ratzu, lo* — if they do not wish to do so, they are under no obligation; they are entitled to remain in the other room.

### Can't Force Shamash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא לְהוּ: הָא קַבֵּילְתּוּן. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: כִּי קַבֵּלְינָךְ — אַדַּעְתָּא דְּנַטְרְחָךְ קַמַּן. לְמִטְרַח לַן לְדִידָךְ — לָא קַבֵּלְינָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **But why** should this be left to their discretion? **Let him say to them: You accepted me** unconditionally into the group, and therefore you have no right to prevent me from continuing to eat. The latter claim is not accepted, as is apparent by the mishna's ruling that it is left to the group's discretion.
>
> **It is different there,** in the case of an attendant, **for they** can **say to him: When we accepted you, it was with the understanding that you would exert yourself before us** and provide our needs by serving as our waiter; but **for us to exert ourselves** by moving to a place that is convenient **for you, we did not accept you.** Therefore, no proof can be derived from the mishna.

The *gemara* asks: why can the *shamash* not demand that the group come to him? After all, the principle established earlier was that a member who accepted others into his group cannot then claim they are stuck with him — yet symmetrically, shouldn't the group be stuck with the *shamash* since they accepted him? The *gemara* answers: *shani hasam de'amri lei* — it is different here, because the group can say to the *shamash*: under what terms did we accept you? *Adaata de'nishdrach kaman* — we accepted you with the understanding that you would come to serve us, that you would attend upon us. *Limtrach lan u'ledidach lo kablinach* — we did not accept you on the understanding that we would have to be dragged after you. The *shamash*, by wandering off and eating by himself, broke the implicit terms of his membership; the group cannot be compelled to follow him.

## Communal Eating

### Baraita: Glutton Kicked…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה שֶׁהָיוּ יָדָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפוֹת, רַשָּׁאִין לוֹמַר לוֹ: טוֹל חֶלְקְךָ וָצֵא. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חֲמִשָּׁה וְעָשׂוּ סִיבּוֹלֶת, רַשָּׁאִין לוֹמַר לוֹ: טוֹל חֶלְקְךָ וָצֵא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an explicit resolution to this question from the *Tosefta*: **Members of a group in which one of** the group **had fine hands are permitted to say** to him: **Take your** allotted **portion** to eat **and leave;** don't take any more from the other members' portions. **And not only** is this true of a group sharing a Paschal lamb, **but even five** people **who made a shared meal of friends [*sibolet*]** throughout the year **are permitted to say** to one who has fine hands: **Take your portion and leave.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that that is the ruling.

A *baraita* now addresses both the *Pesach* case and an analogous case of communal eating. *Bnei chavurah she'yadav shel echad mehen yafot, reshayim lomar lo: tol chelkecha bekatzei* — the members of the group may tell a disproportionate eater to take his portion and leave.

Beyond the *Pesach* case, the *baraita* continues: *afilu chamisha she'asu sibolot* — even five people who organized a communal meal arrangement (like a rotating food cooperative, in which each member takes a turn providing the meal for all), and one of them turns out to eat far more than his share, *reshayim lomar lo: tol chelkecha u'tzei* — they may give him his portion and remove him from the arrangement. This seems to suggest that a disproportionate eater can be expelled from any communal eating arrangement, not only a *korban Pesach*.

### Real Question: Divide?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְלֹא עוֹד״? לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא פֶּסַח, דְּמָצֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: כִּי קַבֵּלְינָךְ לְתַקּוֹנֵי זְבִיחָה, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ סִיבּוֹלֶת נָמֵי, דְּצַוְותָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, רַשָּׁאִים לוֹמַר לוֹ: טוֹל חֶלְקְךָ וָצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **What** new ruling is introduced by the expression: **And not only?** The *Tosefta* **is speaking** employing the style of: **There is no need.** The Gemara explains: **There is no need to state** this rule in the case of a group sharing a Paschal lamb, since they have a strong claim, **as they can say to him: When we accepted you, it was** only **for the preparation of the offering,** to ensure that there would be enough people to guarantee that the entire offering would be eaten. It is therefore immediately understood that the group retains a right to limit the size of the portion he takes. **Rather,** the *Tosefta* emphasizes that **even** with regard to **a shared friendly meal, which is for** the sake of **companionship only, they are permitted to say to him: Take your portion and leave.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הָא לָא אִיבַּעְיָא לַן, אֶלָּא הָכִי הוּא דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לַן: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה רַשָּׁאִין לְחַלֵּק, אוֹ אֵינָן רַשָּׁאִין לְחַלֵּק?
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **this** question concerning what a group may say to a member who has fine hands **is not our dilemma. Rather, this is our dilemma:** Are **members of a group permitted to divide up** into smaller separate groups, **or are they not allowed to divide up** if there is no special reason to do so?

The *gemara* reframes the underlying question. According to one version of the discussion, it was never unclear whether a group could expel a disproportionate eater — that was always obvious. The real question is: *bnei chavurah reshayim lachalok o einam reshayim lachalok?* — may the members of a *chavura* simply *choose* to divide up their portions and eat separately, even absent any complaint of disproportionate eating? In other words, may they abandon the communal eating arrangement arbitrarily, without cause?

This question has a social and a financial dimension. The social dimension is whether the communal experience itself is obligatory. The financial dimension — as Rashi and later codes understand it — is whether members may reorganize into separate groups, since communal eating means sharing from a common source while separate eating means dividing the animal and potentially forming distinct *chaburot*. If one member eats more slowly than others, the communal arrangement disadvantages him; but if each gets a fixed portion, that risk is eliminated. The question is whether such a division is permitted.

### Proof: Only If Unequal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה שֶׁהָיוּ יָדָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפוֹת — רַשָּׁאִין לוֹמַר לוֹ: טוֹל חֶלְקְךָ וָצֵא. יָדָיו יָפוֹת — אִין, אֵין יָדָיו יָפוֹת — לָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution: **Members of a group** in which **one of them had fine hands are permitted to say to him: Take your portion and leave,** which is equivalent to instructing him to form his own group, albeit of only one person. This indicates that only if **his hands are fine, yes,** they may divide into separate groups, but if **his hands are not fine, no,** they may not. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that they are not allowed to divide up into separate groups for no reason.

The *gemara* resolves this from the *baraita* itself: *yadav yafot — ein, ein yadav yafot — lo*. The *baraita* specifies that one may only remove a member — or, by extension, break up the communal arrangement — when there is disproportionate eating. Without such justification, the members may not unilaterally divide. *Shma mina* — this is the ruling. The default expectation is communal eating, and a member cannot demand separation without cause. This principle is codified in the *Rambam* and *Shulchan Aruch* for both *Pesach* and other communal eating contexts.

## Papa & Huna Story

### Papa Gobbles Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עָרִיבוּ רִיפְתָּא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. אַדְּאָכֵיל רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חֲדָא, אָכֵיל רַב פָּפָּא אַרְבַּע. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלַג לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַבֵּילְתּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, mixed** their **bread together** in order to share it between them. **By the time Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, ate one** slice, **Rav Pappa ate four** slices. Rav Huna **said to him:** Let us stop sharing. Instead, **divide** the meal **with me** so that I can eat my portion. **He said to him: You accepted** my companionship, and it is improper to now retract.

The *gemara* brings a vivid illustrative story. Rav Papa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua *arivu rifta bahadi hadadei* — they pooled together for a meal, joining their food or contributions equally. While Rav Huna ate one portion, Rav Papa ate four.

### Huna Wants Out

Rav Huna demanded *palgi* — give me my separate portion; he no longer wished to eat communally. He proceeded to cite multiple proofs that a member is entitled to his portion and may eat separately when the arrangement becomes inequitable.

### Papa Refuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ כׇּל הָנֵי תְּיוּבָתָא, וְשַׁנִּי כִּדְשַׁנִּינַן. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ כִּי קַבֵּלְינָךְ — לְתַיקּוֹנֵי זְבִיחָה. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ סִיבּוֹלֶת, פְּלַג לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **raised all these objections** mentioned previously, **and** Rav Pappa **answered** him **as we answered** that those cases dealt with exceptional circumstances. Rav Huna then **raised an objection** from the mishna with regard to the **members of a group** sharing a Paschal lamb. Rav Pappa **said to him: There,** they are permitted to limit his portion only because **they** can **say to him: When we accepted you it was for the preparation of the sacrifice,** but when friends share a meal it is with the understanding that each one will fully participate regardless of how much he eats. Rav Huna finally **raised an objection** from the ruling of the *Tosefta* concerning **a shared meal.** Rav Pappa accepted the proof and **divided** the meal **with him.**

Rav Papa responded *kavietun* — you accepted me as part of this group, invoking the same language used in the *Mishnah* and *baraita*. For each proof Rav Huna cited, Rav Papa gave a distinguishing answer: the case of the *shamash* is different because he withdrew from the group himself; the case of the *Pesach* is different because there the acceptance was *le'tikkunei zevach* — for the purposes of the offering — not a fundamentally social arrangement. When Rav Huna finally cited the *baraita* about *sibolot* — communal eating arrangements — which explicitly allows removal of a disproportionate eater from a social meal, Rav Papa conceded, and they divided their portions.

### Ravina Even Worse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל עָרֵיב בַּהֲדֵי רָבִינָא. אַדְּאָכֵיל רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חֲדָא, אָכֵיל רָבִינָא תְּמָנְיָא. אֲמַר: מְאָה פָּפֵּי וְלָא חֲדָא רָבִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** On another occasion Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, **went and mixed** his bread **together with Ravina. By the time Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, ate one** slice, **Ravina ate eight.** Rav Huna **said** jokingly: It is better to eat together with **one hundred Pappas and not** with **one Ravina,** because Ravina eats significantly more.

Rav Huna then took his portion and *arav behadi Ravina* — joined together with Ravina for a meal. This time, while Rav Huna ate one portion, Ravina ate eight. Rav Huna exclaimed: *me'ah Papei ve'lo chada Ravina* — I would rather have a hundred Rav Papas than one Ravina.

## Pesach vs. Other Offerin…

### Pesach: Join Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְמַנֶּה אֲחֵרִים עִמּוֹ עַל פִּסְחוֹ וְעַל חֲגִיגָתוֹ — מָעוֹת שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ חוּלִּין. וְהַמּוֹכֵר עוֹלָתוֹ וּשְׁלָמָיו — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם, וּמָעוֹת כׇּל שֶׁהֵן יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who registers others with him for his Paschal lamb** or **for his Festival peace-offering** for the fourteenth of Nisan and takes money from them for their share, **the money in his hand** that he took for them **is non-sacred. And one who sells his burnt-offering** or **peace-offerings** to another person **has not done anything;** the sale is completely invalid, **and any money** he receives for the transaction **should go** to the Temple fund to be used **for** the purchase of public **free-will offerings** which will be sacrificed as burnt**-**offerings.

A *baraita* juxtaposes the rules governing new membership in a *Pesach* against those governing other *korbanot*. *Ha'memaneh acherim imo al Pesacho* — if one invites others to join in his *Pesach*, even after the animal has been sanctified, *ma'ot she'beyado chulin* — the money he receives from the new members becomes *chulin*. The new member gains a portion in the sanctified *korban*, and the money changes hands in a way that somehow transitions out of *kedushah*. The *gemara* will explain the mechanics of this further, but the practical result is that joining a *Pesach* even post-sanctification is possible and the financial arrangement is workable.

### Olah: No Transfer

By contrast, *ha'mocher olato ve'shalmav* — one who attempts to sell or transfer ownership in a sanctified *olah* or *shelamim* — *lo asah ve'lo klum*: the transaction is entirely void. Ownership of a *korban* that has already been sanctified cannot be transferred. Furthermore, the money given by the would-be purchaser — rather than becoming *chulin* as in the *Pesach* case — *yiplu le'nedavah*: it must go toward a communal donation for the *Beit HaMikdash*, since it was given in the context of *kedushah* and cannot simply revert to ordinary status. The *baraita* thus establishes a sharp contrast: the *Pesach* uniquely permits post-sanctification enrollment with the financial consideration becoming *chulin*, whereas *olah* and *shelamim* permit no transfer at all and any money involved must go toward a *nedavah*. The *gemara* will continue to explain the conceptual underpinning of this distinction.
