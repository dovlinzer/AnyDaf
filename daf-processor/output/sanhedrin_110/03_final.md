# Sanhedrin 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Adat Korach

### Mishnah: No Olam Haba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲדַת קֹרַח אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּכַס עֲלֵיהֶם הָאָרֶץ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיֹּאבְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הֵן כַּאֲבֵידָה הַמִּתְבַּקֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תָּעִיתִי כְּשֶׂה אֹבֵד בַּקֵּשׁ עַבְדֶּךָ כִּי מִצְוֹתֶיךָ לֹא שָׁכָחְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The members of **the assembly of Korah have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the earth closed upon them"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in this world,** and also: **"And they perished from among the assembly"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** Although it says that they perished, **they are like a lost item that is sought,** ultimately found, and rehabilitated, **as it is stated: "I have gone astray like a lost sheep; seek out your servant, for I do not forget your mitzvot"** (Psalms 119:176).

The *Gemara* on this *daf* opens by citing a *Baraita*: *adat Korach ein lahem chelek le'olam haba* — the congregation of Korach has no share in the World to Come. The proof text is the verse describing their end: *vatechas aleihem ha'aretz*, the earth covered over them, *vayovdu mitoch hakahal*, and they were lost from the congregation.

### Vatechas vs. Vayovdu

The *Gemara* reads the verse in two parts. The first clause, *vatechas aleihem ha'aretz*, refers to *olam hazeh* — their physical destruction in this world. The second clause, *vayovdu mitoch hakahal*, refers to *olam haba* — they were severed from the congregation of Israel in the World to Come as well. This is *divrei Rabbi Akiva*.

### Aveidah Mitbakeshet

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira offers a strikingly different characterization: *harei hen ke'aveidah hamitbakeshet* — they are like lost property that is being sought. The souls of Korach's congregation are not annihilated; they are lost, but the implication is that God, as it were, desires to find them again. Their fate is not final — they remain, in some sense, retrievable.

### Bakesh Avdecha

The *pasuk* adduced in support comes from Tehillim: *ta'iti kaseh oved bakesh avdecha ki mitzotecha lo shachachti* — "I have gone astray like a lost sheep; seek your servant, for I have not forgotten your commandments." The operative word is *bakesh*, seek — paralleling *ke'aveidah hamitbakeshet*. One might find it significant that the verse adds *ki mitzotecha lo shachachti*: unlike the other figures studied earlier in this *perek* who committed grave *aveirot* such as *avodah zarah*, there is no indication that *adat Korach* abandoned the rest of the *mitzvot*. Their sin was the rebellion itself — a quarrel over leadership, not apostasy. Perhaps that is precisely why Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira sees them as still reachable: God's point has been made in this world, but they need not be permanently cut off from *olam haba*.

## Korach's Story

### Vayikach Korach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקַּח [קֹרַח]״ – אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שֶׁלָּקַח מִקָּח רַע לְעַצְמוֹ. ״קֹרַח״ – שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה קׇרְחָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. ״בֶּן יִצְהָר״ – בֵּן שֶׁהִרְתִּיחַ עָלָיו אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כַּצׇּהֳרַיִם. ״בֶּן קְהָת״ – בֵּן שֶׁהִקְהָה שִׁינֵּי מוֹלִידָיו. ״בֶּן לֵוִי״ – בֵּן שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לְוָיָה בְּגֵיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Korah, the Gemara proceeds to interpret the verses written concerning him. **"And Korah,** son of Izhar, son of Kohath, son of Levi, **took [*vayikkaḥ*]"** (Numbers 16:1), **Reish Lakish says: He purchased [*lakaḥ*] a bad acquisition for himself,** as through his actions he drove himself from the world. **"Korah"** alludes to the fact **that** because of him **a void [*korḥa*] was created in** the children of **Israel. "Son of Izhar,"** is referring to **a son who incited the wrath of the entire world upon him like** the heat of **the afternoon [*tzohorayim*]. "Son of Kohath,"** is referring to **a son who blunted [*hik'ha*] the teeth of his parents,** i.e., he shamed them with his conduct. **"Son of Levi,"** is referring to **a son who became an escort [*levaya*] in Gehenna.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a sustained analysis of the rebellion narrative. The *parsha* opens with the grammatically peculiar phrase *vayikach Korach* — "and Korach took" — without specifying what he took. The following verse moves immediately to a new verb: *vayukumu lifnei Moshe*, and they arose before Moshe. What, then, did Korach take? The plain sense is that he gathered people around himself, yet other verbs — *vayasof*, *vayasfu* — would have served that purpose more naturally. The *Gemara* therefore reads *vayikach Korach* as *shelakach mekach ra le'atzmo*: he made a bad acquisition for himself, a ruinous bargain.

### Names Interpreted

From here the *Gemara* launches a series of *derashot* on the names in the verse, doing something unusual: rather than *darshening* the ancestors' names as reflecting the ancestors' own qualities — as is the more typical pattern — the *Gemara* applies each name to Korach himself, since it does not wish to impugn Yitzhar, Kehat, or Levi.

*Korach* himself: *she'na'asa korcha be'Yisrael* — he created a bald spot, a void, in Israel, where those people should have been.

*Ben Yitzhar*: *ben she'hirtiach alav et kol ha'olam ke'tzohorayim* — he caused all the world to boil against him like the heat of midday. The roots *yitzhar*, *hirtiach*, and *tzohorayim* all carry connotations of heat and burning.

*Ben Kehat*: *ben she'hikka shinei molidav* — he blunted or weakened the teeth of his forebears, bringing shame upon them.

*Ben Levi*: *mi she'na'asa levaya le'Gehinnom* — he had an entourage descending with him to *Gehinnom*, meaning himself and his followers. The *Gemara* is careful not to say this about Levi himself.

This mode of *derasha* is notably unusual. Ordinarily when the *Gemara* expounds an ancestor's name negatively it means to impugn the ancestor. Here, because it cannot do that with Yitzhar, Kehat, and Levi, it applies the *derashot* to Korach while formally reading them from the ancestors' names — an inventive move that the *Gemara* itself implicitly acknowledges by proceeding rather swiftly.

### Yaakov's Pass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, בֵּן שֶׁעָקַב עַצְמוֹ לְגֵיהִנָּם? אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: יַעֲקֹב בִּיקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בְּסֹדָם אַל תָּבֹא נַפְשִׁי בִּקְהָלָם אַל תֵּחַד כְּבֹדִי״. ״בְּסֹדָם אַל תָּבֹא נַפְשִׁי״ – אֵלּוּ מְרַגְּלִים. ״בִּקְהָלָם אַל תֵּחַד כְּבֹדִי״ – זֶה עֲדַת קֹרַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if Korah's lineage is being interpreted disparagingly, why not **let** the Torah **also include: Son of Jacob [*Ya'akov*],** and interpret it: **A son who contorted [*she'akav*] himself** until he reached **Gehenna? Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: Jacob prayed for mercy for himself** that his name would not be included in the lineage of Korah, **as it is stated: "Let my soul not come into their council; to their assembly let my honor not be united"** (Genesis 49:6). **"Let my soul not come into their council"; these are** the **spies.** Jacob prayed that his name would not be mentioned in their regard. **"To their assembly let my honor not be united"; this** is referring to **the assembly of Korah.**

Pushing further back in the genealogy, the *Gemara* notes: *uve'cheshbona nami ben Yaakov* — we could extend the *derasha* all the way to Yaakov, *ben she'ikkeiv atzmo le'Gehinnom*, he tumbled himself down toward *Gehinnom*, playing on the root *akeiv* within *Yaakov*. But Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak explains why Yaakov is not actually mentioned in connection with the Korach rebellion: *Yaakov bikkeish rachamim al atzmo* — Yaakov prayed to be spared the association. As the verse states, *be'sodam al tavo nafshi uve'kehalem al techad kevodi* — "let my soul not enter their counsel, let my glory not be joined with their assembly." The *Gemara* reads *be'sodam al tavo nafshi* as referring to the *meraglim*, and *uve'kehalem al techad kevodi* as referring to *adat Korach*. In both cases, Yaakov's name is conspicuously absent from the genealogy of those involved — and per Rashi, *meraglim dela ketiv bahem ploni ben Yaakov*: the *meraglim* are never identified as sons of Yaakov. His petition was granted.

### Companions Analyzed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דָּתָן״ – שֶׁעָבַר עַל דָּת אֵל, ״אֲבִירָם״ – שֶׁאִיבֵּר עַצְמוֹ מֵעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה, ״וְאוֹן״ – שֶׁיָּשַׁב בַּאֲנִינוּת, ״פֶּלֶת״ – שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ לוֹ פְּלָאוֹת, ״בֶּן רְאוּבֵן״ – בֵּן שֶׁרָאָה וְהֵבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to interpret the names of Korah's cohorts: "Dathan and Abiram, the sons of Eliab, and On, the son of Peleth, sons of Reuben" (Numbers 16:1). **"Dathan"** is one **who violated the precepts [*dat*] of God. "Abiram"** is one **who braced [*iber*] himself from repenting. "And On"** indicates one **who sat in acute mourning [*aninut*]** over the sin that he committed, and he repented and was spared. **"Peleth"** is one **for whom wonders [*pelaot*] were performed. Son of Reuben [*Reuven*],** is **a son who saw and understood [*ra'a vehevin*]** the nature of what was transpiring and repented.

The *Gemara* continues with *derashot* on Korach's named associates.

*Datan*: *she'avar al dat* — he transgressed the law.

*Aviram*: *she'ibber atzmo me'asot teshuvah* — he steeled himself, strengthened himself (*avirim* connoting strength, as in *Avir Yaakov*), against doing *teshuvah*. He hardened his resolve to continue down this path.

*On*: *sheyashav ba'aninut* — he sat in a state of *aninut*, mourning. Rashi explains: *she'asa teshuvah al shehayah imahem techilah be'eitzah* — he repented having initially joined their counsel, and so he survived. He mourns, perhaps for those who did not, or for how close he himself came to destruction.

*Ben Pelet*: *shenasu lo pla'ot* — wonders were done for him. Rashi: *shepeirash shenitzal mehem*, that he separated himself and was saved. The *Gemara* implies that the mere act of extricating oneself from such a committed conspiracy is itself something of a miracle — not that supernatural fire descended, but that when people are so deeply ensnared in a course of action, disengaging at all takes extraordinary effort.

*Ben Reuven*: *ben shera'ah vehevin* — he saw what was happening and understood, drawing the correct conclusion.

### On's Wife Saves Him

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: ״אוֹן בֶּן פֶּלֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ הִצִּילַתּוּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מַאי נָפְקָא לָךְ מִינַּהּ? אִי מָר רַבָּה – אַנְתְּ תַּלְמִידָא, וְאִי מָר רַבָּה – אַנְתְּ תַּלְמִידָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד? הֲוַאי בְּעֵצָה וְאִשְׁתְּבַעִי לִי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: יָדַעְנָא דְּכוּלַּהּ כְּנִישְׁתָּא קַדִּישְׁתָּא נִינְהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי כׇל הָעֵדָה כֻּלָּם קְדֹשִׁים״. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: תּוּב, דַּאֲנָא מַצֵּילְנָא לָךְ. אַשְׁקִיתֵיהּ חַמְרָא וְאַרְוִיתֵיהּ וְאַגְנִיתֵיהּ גַּוַּאי. וִיתִבָא עַל בָּבָא, וּסְתַרְתֵּהּ לְמַזְּיַהּ, כֹּל דַּאֲתָא חַזְיַהּ הֲדַר. אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי, אִבְּלַעוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: On, son of Peleth,** did not repent on his own; rather, **his wife saved him. She said to him: What is the difference to you? If** this **Master,** Moses, is **the great** one, **you** are **the student. And if** this **Master,** Korah, is **the great** one, **you** are **the student.** Why are you involving yourself in this matter? On **said to her: What shall I do? I was** one of those **who took counsel and I took an oath with them** that I would be with them. **She said to him: I know that the entire assembly is holy, as it is written: "For all the assembly is holy"** (Numbers 16:3), and they observe the restrictions of modesty. **She said to him: Sit, for I will save you. She gave him wine to drink and caused him to become drunk and laid him** on a bed **inside** their tent. **She sat at the entrance** of the tent **and exposed her hair** as though she were bathing. **Anyone who came** and **saw her** stepped **back. In the meantime** the assembly of Korah **was swallowed** into the ground, and On, son of Peleth, was spared.

Rav explains the mechanism by which On ben Pelet was saved: *On ben Pelet ishto hitzilato* — his wife rescued him.

She posed a hard-headed practical argument: *mai nafka lach minach* — what does it matter to you which way this goes? If Moshe prevails, you remain a subordinate; if Korach prevails, you remain a subordinate. The outcome for you personally is the same either way. On replied that he was already sworn in with them — *ishtavei li bahadeihu* — how could he withdraw? She told him to leave it to her.

Her reasoning: *yad'ana dekhulah kenishta qadisha ninhu* — I know that everyone here is so holy, as they themselves say, *ki khol ha'edah kulam kedoshim*. She then administered wine, he became intoxicated, and she brought him inside. She sat at the opening of the tent with her hair uncovered — in a state that rendered approach improper. *Kol de-ata chaza hadar*: whoever came to collect On, seeing her thus, turned away. By the time everything unfolded, he was sleeping it off, and the moment had passed.

One might note that On did not exactly do *teshuvah* in any formal sense — his wife simply maneuvered him out of harm's way. Yet even this partial disengagement, his willingness to be saved, earns him credit. The *Gemara* earlier credited him with *yashav ba'aninut* and *pla'ot* — at the very least he did not make things worse for himself or for others.

### Korach's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּתֵיהּ דְּקֹרַח אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: חֲזִי מַאי קָעָבֵיד מֹשֶׁה. אִיהוּ הֲוָה מַלְכָּא, לַאֲחוּהּ שַׁוְּויֵהּ כָּהֲנָא רַבָּא, לִבְנֵי אֲחוֹהִי שַׁוִּינְהוּ סְגָנֵי דְּכָהֲנָא. אִי אָתְיָא תְּרוּמָה אָמַר ״תֶּיהְוֵי לְכָהֵן״, אִי אָתוּ מַעֲשֵׂר דְּשָׁקְלִיתוּ אַתּוּן אָמַר ״הַבוּ חַד מֵעַשְׂרָה לְכָהֵן״. וְעוֹד דְּגָיֵיז לֵיהּ לְמָזַיְיכוּ וּמִיטַּלַּל בְּכוּ כִּי כוּפְתָּא, עֵינָא יְהַב בְּמָזַיְיכוּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: הָא אִיהוּ נָמֵי קָא עָבֵיד! אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלְּהוּ רְבוּתָא דִּידֵיהּ, אָמַר אִיהוּ נָמֵי ״תָּמֹת נַפְשִׁי עִם פְּלִשְׁתִּים״. וְעוֹד, דְּקָאָמַר לְכוּ עָבְדִיתוּ תְּכֵלְתָּא. אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ תְּכֵלְתָּא חֲשִׁיבָא [מִצְוָה], אַפֵּיק גְּלִימֵי דִּתְכֵלְתָּא וְכַסִּינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ מְתִיבְתָּךְ. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״חׇכְמוֹת נָשִׁים בָּנְתָה בֵיתָהּ״ – זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל אוֹן בֶּן פֶּלֶת, ״וְאִוֶּלֶת בְּיָדָהּ תֶּהֶרְסֶנָּה״ – זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל קֹרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Korah's wife said to him: See what Moses is doing. He is the king, he appointed his brother High Priest,** and **he appointed his brother's sons deputy priests. If *teruma* comes, he says: Let it be for the priest; if** the first **tithe comes, which you** as Levites **take, he says: Give one tenth to the priest. And furthermore, he shears your hair and waves you** as if you are as insignificant **as excrement** (see Numbers 8:5–11), as though **he set** his **sights on your hair** and wishes you to be shaven and unsightly. Korah **said to her: But didn't he also do so;** he shaved his hair like the rest of the Levites? **She said to him: Since it is all** done **for his own prominence, he also said** metaphorically: **"Let me die with the Philistines"** (Judges 16:30); he was willing to humiliate himself in order to humiliate you. She said to him: **And furthermore,** with regard to that **which he said to you, to prepare sky-blue dye** for your ritual fringes, one could respond to him: **If it enters your mind,** Moses, that using **sky-blue dye is considered a mitzva, take out robes** that are made entirely **of** material colored with **sky-blue dye, and dress all** the students **of your academy** in sky-blue robes without ritual fringes; why could one not fulfill the mitzva in that manner? Clearly, Moses is fabricating all this. **This is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The wisdom of women builds her house"** (Proverbs 14:1); **this** is referring to **the wife of On, son of Peleth.** And: **"Folly plucks it down with her hands"** (Proverbs 14:1); **this** is referring to **the wife of Korah.**

The contrast with Korach's wife could not be sharper. She actively incited him to rebellion with a calculated political critique: Look at what Moshe is doing — *ihu avei malka*, he has made himself king. *Le'achvei shaveiyei kahana raba* — and conveniently his brother is now *Kohen Gadol*. As for the *Levi'im*, for whom Korach speaks, their role is to serve the *Kohanim*. And when you give *terumah*, it goes to the *Kohen*; even the *maaser* that the *Levi'im* receive, they must give a portion of it to the *Kohen* — *dashkenu asit am hahu chai me-yud de-kohen*, the *Kohen*'s hand is in everything. And furthermore, *de-gaza lei le-mazyeichu* — he shaved all your hair as part of the purification process, making you all look ridiculous. Is that how he shows respect for you?

Korach responded that Moshe himself is a *Levi* and had to undergo the same. She dismissed this: *keivan de-kula dedei* — since everything is his anyway, he can afford to participate in the humiliation. Like Shimshon who said *tamut nafshi im pelishtim*, Moshe is willing to go down with the ship while consolidating his hold. She then pointed to the recent *mitzvah* of *techeilet* — the timing of *parshat tzitzit* and *parshat Korach* being proximate was not lost on her — suggesting that Moshe was using even the commandments as instruments of control.

The *Gemara* concludes this comparison with the verse *chachmon nashim banta beita* — the wise women build their homes — applied to the wife of On ben Pelet, who preserved her household. *Ve'ivelet be-yadeha tehersenu* — but the foolish one tears it down with her own hands — applied to the wife of Korach.

## Rebels' Character

### Nesi'ei Edah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּקֻמוּ לִפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה וַאֲנָשִׁים מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל חֲמִשִּׁים וּמָאתָיִם [נְשִׂיאֵי עֵדָה]״ – מְיוּחָדִים שֶׁבָּעֵדָה, ״קְרִיאֵי מוֹעֵד״ – שֶׁהָיוּ יוֹדְעִים לְעַבֵּר שָׁנִים וְלִקְבּוֹעַ חֳדָשִׁים, ״אַנְשֵׁי שֵׁם״ – שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶם שֵׁם בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"And they arose before Moses, with men from the children of Israel, two hundred and fifty** princes of the congregation, the elect men of the assembly, men of renown" (Numbers 16:2). These men were **the distinctive people of the assembly. "The elect men of the assembly [*keri'ei moed*]"** is referring to those **who knew** how **to intercalate the years and establish the months** in order to determine the time for each Festival [*moed*]. **"Men of renown [*shem*],"** is referring to those **who had a reputation [*shem*] throughout the world.**

The verse describes the 250 men who rose against Moshe as *nesi'ei edah kriei mo'ed anshei shem*. The *Gemara* expounds each phrase. *Meyuchadim she-be-eidah* — they were the distinguished ones of the community. *Keriei mo'ed: she'hayu yodeim le-aber shanim ve-likboa chodashim* — they possessed the calendar expertise required to intercalate years and establish new months, recalling a theme prominent at the very beginning of the *masechet*. *Anshei shem she-alehem shem be-chol ha'olam* — their reputations extended beyond Israel to the entire world.

This is a recurring and theologically significant theme: great *talmidei chachamim* can still be profoundly wrong. The *Gemara* is adding complexity rather than flattening these figures into simple villains. Knowing *Torah*, performing *mitzvot*, and being distinguished scholars does not automatically translate into moral rectitude.

### Adultery Rumors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּשְׁמַע מֹשֶׁה וַיִּפֹּל עַל פָּנָיו״. מָה שְׁמוּעָה שְׁמַע? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מֵאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיְקַנְאוּ לְמֹשֶׁה בַּמַּחֲנֶה״. אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד קִנֵּא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִמֹּשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמֹשֶׁה יִקַּח אֶת הָאֹהֶל וְנָטָה לוֹ מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And Moses heard and he fell on his face"** (Numbers 16:4), the Gemara asks: **What report did he hear** that elicited that reaction? **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** He heard **that they suspected him** of adultery **with a married woman, as it is stated: "And they were jealous of Moses in the camp"** (Psalms 106:16). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzḥak says:** This **teaches that each and every** man **warned his wife** to distance herself **from Moses** and not enter into seclusion with him, **as it is stated: "And Moses would take the tent and pitch it outside the camp"** (Exodus 33:7). It was due to this slander that he withdrew from the camp.

What did Moshe hear that caused him to fall on his face — *vayishma Moshe vayipol al panav*? Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *she'chashduhu me'eshet ish* — they suspected him of adultery.

The prooftext is *vayikanu le'Moshe ba'machaneh*. The root *kana* appears in two contexts: in the *Pinchas* narrative it means zealotry, but in *Sotah* — *ve'kina et ishto* — it refers to a husband asserting his claim over his wife through the process of *kinui ve'stira*. Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak elaborates: *melamed she'kol echad ve'echad kina et ishto mi'Moshe* — every single one of them performed the *kinui* process against his wife on account of Moshe. The basis was the verse *Moshe yikach et ha'ohel u'nata lo mi'chutz la'machaneh* — Moshe had placed his tent outside the camp during the period following *chet ha'egel*, before the *Mishkan* was erected. The whisper campaign asked: why is his tent so far from everyone? What is he doing there?

The *Gemara* makes clear that this was not necessarily believed literally. The purpose of a rumor campaign is not factual persuasion but erosion of credibility. Enough voices saying something generates its own momentum. The specific content matters less than the cumulative effect on a leader's standing.

### Machloket Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּקׇם מֹשֶׁה וַיֵּלֶךְ אֶל דָּתָן וַאֲבִירָם״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין מַחְזִיקִים בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת, דְּאָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמַּחְזִיק בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת עוֹבֵר בְּלָאו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא יִהְיֶה כְקֹרַח וְכַעֲדָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: **"And Moses arose and went to Dathan and Abiram"** (Numbers 16:25), **Reish Lakish says: From here** we derive **that one may not perpetuate a dispute, as Rav says: Anyone who perpetuates a dispute violates a prohibition, as it is stated: "And he will not be like Korah and his assembly,** as the Lord spoke by the hand of Moses to him" (Numbers 17:5). Even the aggrieved party must seek to end the dispute. Dathan and Abiram accused Moses and by right should have initiated the reconciliation. Nevertheless, Moses was not insistent on this; he went to them.

*Vayakom Moshe vayelech el Datan ve'Aviram* — Moshe rose and went to Dathan and Aviram despite everything they had done. The *Gemara* derives from this: *mikan she'ein machzikim be'machloket* — one must not persist in a dispute. Moshe was going to make peace. Rav states explicitly: *kol ha'machzik be'machloket over be'lav* — whoever entrenches himself in a dispute transgresses a negative prohibition, derived from the verse *lo yihyeh ke'Korach ve'che'adato*. While this may not be counted among the 613 *mitzvot* by the *Rambam*, the *Gemara* treats it as a genuine *lav* derived from the verse.

## Consequences

### Tzara'at Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״רָאוּי לִיצְטָרֵעַ״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״בְּיַד מֹשֶׁה לוֹ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ לוֹ עוֹד הָבֵא נָא יָדְךָ בְּחֵיקֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** One who perpetuates a dispute **is fit to be afflicted with leprosy. It is written here: "By the hand of Moses to him," and it is written there: "And the Lord said furthermore to him: Put now your hand into your bosom.** And he put his hand into his bosom; and when he took it out, behold, his hand was leprous, as white as snow" (Exodus 4:6). Based on the verbal analogy based on the term "to him" written in both verses, it is derived that the punishment for perpetuating a dispute is leprosy.

Rav Ashi continues along the same line: *kol ha'machzik be'machloket ra'uy le'hitstara'e* — whoever persists in dispute deserves *tzara'at*. The derivation is through *gezeirah shavah* of the word *yad*: the verse about the Korach rebellion ends *be'yad Moshe lo*, and the verse about Moshe's signs before Pharaoh — *va'yomer Hashem lo od ha've na yadecha be'cheikecha* — describes the sign of *tzara'at* appearing on the hand. The shared word *yad* connects the two contexts, implying that those who perpetuate the kind of rebellion described in the first verse are liable to the affliction described in the second. Other tractates discuss the causes of *tzara'at* without listing *machloket* among them, but the *Gemara* here is making a moral and homiletical rather than purely legal point.

### Nachash Bite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כׇּל הַחוֹלֵק עַל מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד רָאוּי לְהַכִּישׁוֹ נָחָשׁ. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיִּזְבַּח אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ צֹאן וּבָקָר וּמְרִיא עִם אֶבֶן הַזֹּחֶלֶת״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״עִם חֲמַת זֹחֲלֵי עָפָר״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the prohibition of perpetuating a dispute, **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **anyone who disputes the reign of the house of David, it is fitting for a snake to bite him.** As **it is written here: "And Adonijah slaughtered sheep and cattle and fatlings by the stone of Zoheleth"** (I Kings 1:9); **and it is written there: "With the poison of crawling things [*zoḥalei*] of the dust"** (Deuteronomy 32:24). Adonijah, who rebelled against his father, King David, was fit to be bitten by a snake.

Rav Yosei broadens the principle: *kol hacholek al hamalchut* — whoever rebels against *malchut Beit David* — *ra'ui lehachisho nachash*, deserves to be bitten by a venomous snake. The derivation plays on the word *zochelet*: Adoniyahu made his sacrifices at *even hazochelet*, and elsewhere the *klalah* includes *chamat zochalei afar* — the venom of those that crawl upon the ground. Adoniyahu, who rebelled against David, is the exemplar; the consequence for challenging legitimate kingship is thus likened to the bite of a serpent.

### Cholek Al Rabo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל הַחוֹלֵק עַל רַבּוֹ כְּחוֹלֵק עַל הַשְּׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּהַצֹּתָם עַל ה׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כָּל הָעוֹשֶׂה מְרִיבָה עִם רַבּוֹ כְּעוֹשֶׂה עִם שְׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵמָּה מֵי מְרִיבָה אֲשֶׁר רָבוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Anyone who disagrees with his teacher is like one who disagrees with the Divine Presence, as it is stated** with regard to Dathan and Abiram: **"When they strove against the Lord"** (Numbers 26:9), although their dispute was with Moses. **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Anyone who initiates a quarrel [*meriva*] with his teacher is like one who initiates a quarrel with the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "These are the waters of Meribah, where the children of Israel quarreled with the Lord"** (Numbers 20:13), although their quarrel was with Moses.

Rav Chisda extends the framework further: *kol hacholek al rabo kicholek al haShechinah* — whoever disputes against his *rav* is as one disputing against the *Shechinah*. Rashi explains *cholek al rabo* as *cholek al yishivato* — challenging his authority and legitimacy, not merely raising a respectful halachic question. The *Gemara* does this constantly; two *Amoraim* disagreeing is not the issue. The concern is a fundamental challenge to the teacher's standing. The prooftext is *bahatzotam al Hashem* — when *adat Korach* quarreled with Moshe, the Torah describes it as quarreling with God.

### Forms of Discord

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: כׇּל הַמִּתְרַעֵם עַל רַבּוֹ כְּאִילּוּ מִתְרַעֵם עַל הַשְּׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא עָלֵינוּ תְלֻנֹּתֵיכֶם כִּי אִם עַל ה׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כׇּל הַמְהַרְהֵר אַחַר רַבּוֹ כְּאִילּוּ מְהַרְהֵר אַחַר שְׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְדַבֵּר הָעָם בֵּאלֹהִים וּבְמֹשֶׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says: Anyone who expresses resentment against his teacher** for wronging him, **it is as though he is expressing resentment against the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "Your murmurings are not against us, but against the Lord"** (Exodus 16:8). **Rabbi Abbahu says: Anyone who suspects his teacher** of wrongdoing, **it is as though he suspects the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "And the people spoke against God, and against Moses"** (Numbers 21:5). The verse likens God and Moses with regard to this matter.

Several further distinctions are drawn among forms of discord, each paired with a prooftext and a different *Amora*:

Rav Chama b'Rabbi Chanina: *kol ha'oseh merivah im rabo k'oseh im Shechinah* — having fights with one's *rav* is like fighting with the *Shechinah*. Rashi distinguishes *merivah* from *machloket*: *merivah* is being obstructionist and combative, deliberately quarrelsome — *tagar v'histatev v'katfanim neged rabo*. Prooftext: *heimah Mei Merivah asher ravu Bnei Yisrael al Hashem* — even though the people were complaining to Moshe at the waters of *Merivah*, the Torah describes it as quarreling with God.

Chanina bar Pappa: *kol hamitra'em al rabo*, one who conducts himself cruelly or harshly toward his *rav*, is as if acting so toward the *Shechinah*. Rashi defines *mitra'em* as characterizing the *rav* as acting with *midah kevushah u'midat achzarit* — with a suppressed or cruel disposition — a personal attack on character rather than a halachic dispute. Prooftext: *lo aleinu telunoteichem* — "your complaints are not against us," spoken in the context of the *mann*, meaning that complaints directed at Moshe are really directed at God.

Rav Abbahu: *kol hamharher achar rabo k'meharher achar Shechinah* — even harboring bad thoughts about one's *rav* is like doing so against the *Shechinah*. Rashi explains *meharher* as *mechapeh alav devarim* — covering over or nursing suspicions about him. Prooftext: *vayidaber ha'am be'Elokim uve'Moshe* — when the people spoke out against their circumstances in the desert, the Torah places God and Moshe in the same construction, indicating that speech directed at Moshe reaches God Himself.

## Korach's Wealth

### Osher Shamur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֹשֶׁר שָׁמוּר לִבְעָלָיו לְרָעָתוֹ״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: זֶה עוֹשְׁרוֹ שֶׁל קֹרַח. ״וְאֶת כׇּל הַיְקוּם אֲשֶׁר בְּרַגְלֵיהֶם״ – אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: זֶה מָמוֹנוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם שֶׁמַּעֲמִידוֹ עַל רַגְלָיו. וְאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: מַשּׂוֹי שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת פְּרָדוֹת לְבָנוֹת הָיוּ מַפְתְּחוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית גְּנָזָיו שֶׁל קֹרַח, וְכוּלְּהוּ אַקְלִידֵי וְקִילְפֵי דְּגִילְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: **"Wealth is kept for the owner to his detriment"** (Ecclesiastes 5:12), **Reish Lakish says: This** is referring to **the wealth of Korah,** which was of no use to him. The fact that Korah was wealthy is derived from the verse: **"And all the substance that was at their feet"** (Deuteronomy 11:6), as **Rabbi Elazar says: This** is referring to **a person's property, which stands him on his feet. And Rabbi Levi says: The keys** alone **to Korah's treasury were a burden** requiring **three hundred white mules** to transport them, **and** moreover, **all the keys [*aklidei*] and locks were of leather.** This conveys the vastness of his wealth.

The verse *osher shamur liva'alav lera'ato* — wealth guarded for its owner to his own ruin — is read by Reish Lakish as referring specifically to the riches of Korach. The supporting verse is *ve'et kol hayikum asher beragleihem*: when the earth swallowed Korach's followers, it consumed not only them and their households but *kol hayikum asher beragleihem* — everything standing at their feet. Rabbi Elazar explains: *zeh mamono shel adam shema'amido al raglav* — this is a person's money, which is what gives him his standing. Korach's wealth was the source of both his standing and his ruin.

### Mules of Korach

Rabbi Levi provides the measure of that wealth: *masoy gimmel me'os perados livanos hayu maftechos shel beis genazav shel Korach* — three hundred white mules were required to carry the keys alone to Korach's treasure houses. These were not heavy iron keys but light leather ones — *kula klidei ve'klidei degilda* — so the weight of three hundred mule-loads represents an almost incomprehensible quantity of keys, let alone what they unlocked. The white mules likely served a secondary function as well: as noted elsewhere in the *Gemara*, white mules were known for their lethal bite, making them effective as guard animals against anyone who might approach the treasure.

### Yosef's Treasures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁלֹשׁ מַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת הִטְמִין יוֹסֵף בְּמִצְרַיִם. אַחַת נִתְגַּלְּתָה לְקֹרַח, וְאַחַת נִתְגַּלְּתָה לְאַנְטוֹנִינוּס בֶּן אַסְוִירוּס, וְאַחַת גְּנוּזָה לְצַדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Joseph concealed three buried treasures in Egypt** that he accumulated from the sale of grain during the years of famine. The location of **one was revealed to Korah, and** the location of **one was revealed to Antoninus, son of Asveirus,** emperor of Rome, **and one** remains **hidden for the righteous in the future,** i.e., in the messianic era.

Rav Chama b'Rabbi Chanina explains the origin of this wealth. Yosef, as viceroy of Egypt, accumulated the riches of the entire region during the years of famine. He hid three treasures: *achas nisgalta le'Korach* — one was discovered by Korach; *ve'achas nisgalta le'Antoninus ben Asveirus* — one was found by Antoninus (the Steinsaltz edition notes that the epithet *ben Asveirus* may identify this as Antoninus the son of Septimus Severus, known as Caracalla, who ruled 212–217 CE, rather than the *Antoninus* who is presented elsewhere in the *Gemara* as Rebbi's philosophical interlocutor, though Artscroll reads it as the latter); *ve'achas genuzah le'tzadikim le'asid lavo* — and one remains hidden, reserved for the righteous in the time to come.

## Korach's Fate

### Magefah Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קֹרַח לֹא מִן הַבְּלוּעִים וְלֹא מִן הַשְּׂרוּפִין. לֹא מִן הַבְּלוּעִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת כׇּל הָאָדָם אֲשֶׁר לְקֹרַח״ – וְלֹא קֹרַח. וְלֹא מִן הַשְּׂרוּפִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּאֲכֹל הָאֵשׁ אֵת חֲמִשִּׁים וּמָאתַיִם אִישׁ״ – וְלֹא קֹרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Korah was neither among the swallowed nor among the burned;** he died in a plague. He was **neither among the swallowed, as it is written:** "And the earth opened its mouth and swallowed them and their houses **and all the men who were with Korah"** (Numbers 16:32), from which it is inferred: **But not Korah** himself. **Nor** was he **among the burned, as it is written: "When the fire consumed two hundred and fifty men"** (Numbers 26:10), **but not Korah.**

A dispute exists among the *Amoraim* regarding what precisely happened to Korach. The background: in the Korach narrative there are two distinct punishments — the earth opens and swallows Dathan and Aviram and their households, and fire consumes the 250 men offering *ketoret*. Korach was positioned with the 250. So which fate was his?

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *Korach lo min ha-belu'im ve-lo min ha-serufim* — Korach suffered neither. For the *belu'im*: the verse says *va-tivla otam ve-et kol ha-adam asher le-Korach* — it swallowed all the people *belonging to* Korach, not Korach himself. For the *serufim*: *va-tetze esh mi-lifnei Hashem va-tochal et ha-chamishim u-matayim ish* — it consumed the 250 men, and if Korach had been among them, the count would have been 251. Rashi on this passage states that Korach died from a *magefah* — a plague — as the *meraglim* died: *va-yamutu ba-magefah*.

### Both Punishments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: קֹרַח מִן הַשְּׂרוּפִין וּמִן הַבְּלוּעִין. מִן הַבְּלוּעִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתִּבְלַע אֹתָם וְאֶת קֹרַח״. מִן הַשְּׂרוּפִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתֵּצֵא אֵשׁ מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳ [וְאֵשׁ יָצְאָה מֵאֵת ה׳] וַתֹּאכַל אֵת הַחֲמִשִּׁים וּמָאתַיִם אִישׁ״, וְקֹרַח בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught in a *baraita*: Korah was** both **among the burned and among the swallowed.** He was **among the swallowed, as it is written:** "And the earth opened its mouth **and swallowed them with Korah"** (Numbers 26:10). He was **among the burned, as it is written: "And fire came forth from the Lord, and devoured the two hundred and fifty men** that burned the incense" (Numbers 16:35), **and Korah was with them.**

The *Baraita* takes the opposite view: *Korach min ha-serufim u-min ha-belu'im* — Korach received both punishments. For the *belu'im*: *va-tivla otam ve-et Korach* — "and it swallowed them and Korach." For the *serufim*: Korach was present among the 250 and was therefore burned as well. One natural explanation would be that he was burned first and his body then fell into the opening earth. But this creates a problem: how does the first opinion escape the verse that explicitly mentions *ve-et Korach* in the context of the swallowing?

### Trope Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״שֶׁמֶשׁ יָרֵחַ עָמַד זְבֻלָה לְאוֹר חִצֶּיךָ יְהַלֵּכוּ״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָלוּ שֶׁמֶשׁ וְיָרֵחַ לִזְבוּל. אָמְרוּ לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה דִּין לְבֶן עַמְרָם נֵצֵא, וְאִם לָאו לֹא נֵצֵא. עַד שֶׁזָּרַק בָּהֶם חִצִּים. אָמַר לָהֶן: בִּכְבוֹדִי לֹא מְחִיתֶם, בִּכְבוֹד בָּשָׂר וָדָם מְחִיתֶם? וְהָאִידָּנָא לָא נָפְקִי עַד דְּמָחוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "The sun and moon stand still in their habitation [*zevula*], at the light of Your arrows as they go"** (Habakkuk 3:11)? This **teaches that the sun and moon ascended to *zevul*,** one of the seven firmaments, in which the upper Temple stands. **They said before** God: **Master of the Universe, if You perform justice for** Moses, **the son of Amram,** and prove his righteousness, **we will emerge** and illuminate the world. **And if not, we will not emerge.** They did not emerge **until** God **fired arrows at them** and **said to them: You did not protest with regard to My honor,** as people would see the sun and the moon each day and worship them, but **you protested for the honor of flesh and blood? And today,** the sun and the moon **do not emerge until they are struck,** as in deference to God they hesitate to emerge.

The answer lies in a careful reading of the *trop*. The verse in Bamidbar 26:10 — found not in *parshat Korach* itself but in *parshat Pinchas*, in the genealogical census after the plague — reads: *va-tiftach ha'aretz et piha va-tival otam ve-et Korach bemot ha'edah*. The question is where the syntactic break falls. If one reads *va-tival otam ve-et Korach* as a unit, then Korach was indeed swallowed. But the *trop* permits a different reading: *va-tival otam* — it swallowed them — *ve-et Korach bemot ha'edah* — and as for Korach, he died *bemot ha'edah*, with a different *edah*, namely the death of the *meraglim* by *magefah*. This is how the first opinion resolves the verse: the cantillation creates an ambiguity that allows *ve-et Korach bemot ha'edah* to be read as a separate clause, detaching Korach from the swallowing. This is the kind of moment where careful attention to *trop* resolves what appeared to be an insoluble textual contradiction.

### Gehennom Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְאִם בְּרִיאָה יִבְרָא ה׳ וּפָצְתָה הָאֲדָמָה אֶת פִּיהָ״? אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: ״אִם בְּרִיאָה״ גֵּיהִנָּם – מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו – ״יִבְרָא ה׳״. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְמִבְרְיַהּ מַמָּשׁ? וְהָא ״אֵין כׇּל חָדָשׁ תַּחַת הַשָּׁמֶשׁ״! אֶלָּא לְקָרוֹבֵי פִּיתְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "But if the Lord creates a new creation and the earth opens its mouth"** (Numbers 16:30)? **Moses said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: If Gehenna is** already **created, good, but if not, God should create** it now. The Gemara asks: **For what** was Moses asking? **If we say** that his request was for God **to actually create** Gehenna, **but isn't** it written: **"There is nothing new under the sun"** (Ecclesiastes 1:9)? There are no new creations after the six days of Creation. **Rather,** Moses asked God **to bring the opening** of Gehenna **close** to there, so that the assembly of Korah would be buried alive.

Rava offers a *derasha* on the verse *im b'riah yivra Hashem* — "if God creates a new creation." Moshe declared: if the mouth of the earth already exists and *Gehinnom* was already created, let it come to them — good. If not, let God create it now, so that they may descend there. The *Gemara* raises an objection: but *ein kol chadash tachat hashemesh* — there is nothing new under the sun, so how could Moshe speak of a new creation? The answer: Moshe was not speaking of creating *Gehinnom* itself, which had existed since before creation (as *Mishnat Avot* addresses). Rather, *ela lekuruvei pichon* — he was asking that the *entrance* to *Gehinnom* be brought near, to this very location, so that they could descend directly. That is the access point, not the place itself, that was new.

A remarkable aggadah accompanies this. The verse from Chavakkuk — *shemesh yareiach amad zevulah le'or chitzecha yehalechu* — is interpreted to mean that during the Korach episode, the sun and moon ascended to *zevul*, the highest heaven, and declared before God: *im ata oseh din le-ven Amram netzei ve'im lav lo netzei* — if you vindicate Moshe, we will go forth; if not, we will not emerge. God was displeased with this ultimatum and responded: *be'chevodi lo mechitem, be'chevod basar va-dam mechitem* — you were not zealous for My honor when people worshipped you as gods, yet now you are zealous for the honor of a human being? So He drove them out with arrows. And the *Gemara* adds: *ve'adin lo nafku ad demachu lehu* — even now, every day they must be driven out with those arrows, because from that moment they learned their lesson about going forth and never forgot it.

## Bnei Korach

### Sons Survive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְנֵי קֹרַח לֹא מֵתוּ״. תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: מָקוֹם נִתְבַּצֵּר לָהֶם בְּגֵיהִנָּם וְיָשְׁבוּ עָלָיו וְאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: זִימְנָא חֲדָא הֲוָה קָאָזֵילְנָא בְּאוֹרְחָא, אֲמַר לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא: תָּא וְאַחְוֵי לָךְ בְּלוּעֵי דְקֹרַח. אֲזַלִי חֲזַאי תְּרֵי בִּזְעֵי דַּהֲוָה קָא נָפֵק קִיטְרָא מִנַּיְיהוּ. שְׁקַל גְּבָבָא דְעַמְרָא, אַמְשְׁיֵיהּ מַיָּא, וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ בְּרֵישׁ רוּמְחֵיהּ, וְאַחְלְפֵיהּ הָתָם. אִיחֲרַךְ. אָמַר לִי: אַצֵּית מָה שָׁמְעַתְּ. וּשְׁמַעִית דַּהֲווֹ קָאָמְרִי הָכִי: ״מֹשֶׁה וְתוֹרָתוֹ אֱמֶת, וְהֵן בַּדָּאִים״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And the sons of Korah did not die"** (Numbers 26:11), it is **taught** in a *baraita* that **in the name of our teacher,** the Sages **said: A place was fortified for them in Gehenna and they sat upon it and recited songs** of praise. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: One time I was walking on the path,** and **a certain Arab said to me: Come and I will show you those** from the assembly **of Korah** who were **swallowed. I went** and **I saw two fissures** in the ground **from which smoke was emerging.** That Arab **took a woolen fleece** and **dampened it with water and placed it on the tip of his spear and passed** it over the fissures **there.** The fleece **was singed,** indicating the level of heat there. **He said to me: Listen; what do you hear? And I heard that this** is what **they were saying: Moses and his Torah are truth, and they,** referring to themselves, **are liars.**

The *Gemara* now notes that *bnei Korach lo metu* — the sons of Korach did not die. They are the authors of several *Tehillim*. A *Baraita* explains: *makom nitbatzer lahem b'Gehinnom ve-yashevu alav ve-amru shira* — a high place was secured for them in *Gehinnom*, and they sat upon it and sang.

This is a striking passage that requires some unpacking. The sons of Korach are sometimes understood to have played no active role in the rebellion, and their survival is thus straightforwardly comprehensible. But one can also read their situation differently: even if they participated to some degree and did *teshuvah*, the effects of *machloket* on that scale are long-lasting and not so easily undone. *Teshuvah* addresses the sin itself, but what of all those whose lives were disrupted, the anguish caused, the people incited? Since they were *mahzik be'machloket*, they still descend to *Gehinnom* — yet they occupy a high place within it, and from there they sing.

### Rabbah's Story

Rabbah bar Bar Hana recounts one of his characteristic traveler's tales: *zimna chada hava kazina b'orchot* — on one occasion while traveling, an Arab merchant said to him: *ta ve'achavei lach beluei Korach* — come, I will show you where those swallowed by Korach are. He was brought to a location where two cracks in the earth were visible, with smoke rising from them. To test them, Rabbah took a ball of wool — *amra*, cognate to *tzemer* — soaked it in water, placed it at the end of a long pole so as not to approach too closely, and inserted it into the cracks. Despite being dripping wet, it still came back singed. The merchant then said: *tzeit mai shamat* — stand still and listen to what you hear.

### Repentance Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין מְהַדְּרָא לְהוּ גֵּיהִנָּם כְּבָשָׂר בְּתוֹךְ קַלַּחַת, וְאָמְרִי הָכִי: ״מֹשֶׁה וְתוֹרָתוֹ אֱמֶת, וְהֵן בַּדָּאִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Arab **said to him: Every thirty days Gehenna returns them** to this place **like meat** cooking **in a cauldron, and they say this: Moses and his Torah are truth, and they are liars.**

And Rabbah heard them saying: *Moshe ve-torato emet, ve-hein badain* — Moshe and his Torah are true, and we are liars. The merchant added: *kol tlati yomei mahadri lehu Gehinnom ke-vasar be-toch kalachat ve-amri hachi* — every thirty days *Gehinnom* churns them around like meat in a pot, and at that moment they say again: *Moshe ve-torato emet ve-hein badain*. And you have timed your visit correctly — you will be able to hear it again in another thirty days.

## Dor Hamidbar

### Midbar No Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דּוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּמִּדְבָּר הַזֶּה יִתַּמּוּ וְשָׁם יָמֻתוּ״. ״יִתַּמּוּ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וְשָׁם יָמֻתוּ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַבָּא. וְאָמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי בְאַפִּי אִם יְבֹאוּן אֶל מְנוּחָתִי״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The members of **the generation of the wilderness have no share in the World-to-Come. The Sages taught:** The members of **the generation of the wilderness have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "In this wilderness they shall be consumed, and there they shall die"** (Numbers 14:35). **"They shall be consumed"** indicates **in this world; "and there they shall die"** indicates **for the World-to-Come. And** the verse **states** with regard to the generation of the wilderness: **"Wherefore I took an oath in My anger that they should not enter into My rest"** (Psalms 95:11), indicating that they will not be privileged to gain eternal rest; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *Gemara* now takes up another category mentioned in the *Mishnah*: *Dor HaMidbar ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the generation of the wilderness has no share in the World to Come. The prooftext: *be-midbar hazeh yitamu ve-sham yamutu* — *yitamu ba-olam hazeh*, they will be consumed in this world; *ve-sham yamutu*, and they will die in the World to Come as well.

### Akiva Excludes

A second verse strengthens this position: *asher nishbati be-api im yevo'un el menuchati* — I have sworn in My anger that they shall not enter My rest. This is the verse recited as part of *Kabbalat Shabbat*. Based on this divine oath, Rabbi Akiva excludes the *Dor HaMidbar* from *olam haba* entirely.

### Eliezer Includes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הֵן בָּאִים לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִסְפוּ לִי חֲסִידָי כֹּרְתֵי בְרִיתִי עֲלֵי זָבַח״. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי בְאַפִּי״? בְּאַפִּי נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי, וְחוֹזְרַנִי בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: They come to the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Gather My pious together to Me, those that have entered into My covenant by offering"** (Psalms 50:5). Those who left Egypt entered into an eternal covenant with God at Mount Sinai. **But how do I realize** the meaning of the phrase **"Wherefore I took an oath in My anger"?** It must be understood: **In My anger I took an oath, and I reconsidered** and dissolved the oath when My anger subsided.

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and includes them: *hein ba'im le-olam haba*, they will come to the World to Come. His prooftext: *isfu li chasidai koretay veriti alei zavach* — "gather to Me My faithful ones who made a covenant with Me over sacrifice." These are *Bnei Yisrael*, who entered into a covenant with God. Furthermore, the divine oath sworn *be-api* — in anger — is subject to the same mechanism as the annulment of vows: *min hatarat nedarim*, since if asked again under proper conditions God would have reconsidered, the oath can be treated as taken in haste and retracted. *Be-api nishbati u-vecharoni shavi* — He swore in anger but in His reconsidering He relented. Thus their place in *olam haba* is restored.

### All Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמַר פָּסוּק זֶה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד דּוֹרוֹת הַבָּאִים. ״אִסְפוּ לִי חֲסִידָי״ – אֵלּוּ צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּכָל דּוֹר וָדוֹר. ״כֹּרְתֵי בְרִיתִי עֲלֵי זָבַח״ – אֵלּוּ חֲנַנְיָה, מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה שֶׁמָּסְרוּ עַצְמָן לְתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ. ״עֲלֵי זָבַח״ – [אֵלּוּ] רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וַחֲבֵירָיו שֶׁמָּסְרוּ עַצְמָן לִשְׁחִיטָה עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: This verse was stated** to the generation of the wilderness **only with regard to future generations,** and should be understood in this manner: **"Gather My pious together to Me"; these are the righteous that are in each and every generation. "Those that have entered into My covenant by offering"; these are Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, who submitted themselves to the fiery furnace** to sanctify God's name. **"By offering"; this is** a reference to **Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues, who gave themselves up to slaughter over matters of Torah.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Perachia offers yet another reading: the verse Rabbi Eliezer cited — *isfu li chasidai koretay veriti* — was never spoken about the *Dor HaMidbar* at all. Rather, *lo ne'emar pasuk zeh ella keneged dorot haba'im* — it refers to the righteous of all coming generations. *Kol dor va-dor*, in every generation there are those who are *chasidai* and *koretai verit*. This reading has a generative quality: rather than being about the wilderness generation at all, the verse promises that God will always have those who remain faithful, in any era.

### Return to Zion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: בָּאִים הֵן לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּפְדוּיֵי ה׳ יְשֻׁבוּן וּבָאוּ צִיּוֹן בְּרִנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** The generation of the wilderness **comes to the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the redeemed of the Lord shall return and come singing into Zion"** (Isaiah 35:10), meaning that those whom God redeemed will yet come to Zion.

Shimon ben Azzai maintains that they do have a *chelek le-olam haba*, citing the verse *u-pduyei Hashem yeshuvun u-vau tziyon be-rina* — "those redeemed by God shall return and come to Zion with joy." *Bnei Yisrael* were redeemed by God; therefore they will return to *Tziyon*, understood here as the *olam haba*.

### Zechut Avot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שַׁבְקַהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לַחֲסִידוּתֵיהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָלֹךְ וְקָרָאתָ בְּאׇזְנֵי יְרוּשָׁלִַים לֵאמֹר זָכַרְתִּי לָךְ חֶסֶד נְעוּרַיִךְ אַהֲבַת כְּלוּלֹתָיִךְ לֶכְתֵּךְ אַחֲרַי בַּמִּדְבָּר בְּאֶרֶץ לֹא זְרוּעָה״. וּמָה אֲחֵרִים בָּאִים בִּזְכוּתָם, הֵם עַצְמָן לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Akiva abandoned his piety** and uncharacteristically interpreted the verses harshly, **as it is stated** with regard to the generation of the wilderness: **"Go and cry in the ears of Jerusalem saying,** so says the Lord: **I remember for you the affection of your youth, the love of your espousals, how you went after Me in the wilderness, in a land that was not sown"** (Jeremiah 2:2). **Now, if others come** into the World-to-Come **in the merit of** the generation that left Egypt and followed God in the wilderness, is it **not all the more so** that the generation of the wilderness **themselves** have a share in the World-to-Come?

Rabbi Yochanan observes that Rabbi Akiva has here abandoned his characteristic leniency — *shevakeih Rabbi Akiva le-chasiduteih* — for Rabbi Akiva is generally the more inclusive *Tanna*, yet he is the one excluding the *Dor HaMidbar*. Rabbi Yochanan himself appears to take the opposite view, invoking the verse *halokh ve-karata be-oznei Yerushalayim lemor zacharti lakh chesed ne'urayikh ahavat kelulotayikh lekhtekh acharai ba-midbar be-eretz lo zeruah* — God calls to mind the devotion of Israel's youth, their following Him through the wilderness. If we invoke *zechut avot* — the merit of the ancestors — on behalf of later generations, and God honors that merit, then how much more so should the *Dor HaMidbar* itself, with their own considerable *zechut*, merit entry into *olam haba*: *hinno hem lo kol sheken* — do they not have more than enough of their own merit?

The *Gemara* thus leaves the question unresolved in consensus, with Rabbi Akiva excluding the *Dor HaMidbar*, Rabbi Eliezer including them, and Rabbi Yochanan pressing the case for inclusion on grounds of *zechut avot*. The *Mishnah*'s ruling on this category, unlike some others, is not universally accepted.
