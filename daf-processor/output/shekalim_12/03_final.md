# Shekalim 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketoret & Kli Share (III)

### Ketoret: Kli Shares Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְפַּלְּגוֹן. פִיטְּמָהּ בַחוּלִין. רִבִּי יוֹסֶה בֵּירִבִּי חֲנִינָה אָמַר. פְּסוּלָה. רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לִֵוי אָמַר. כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** As they disagreed: If it was compounded as profane, Rebbi Yose ben Ḥanina said, it is disqualified; Rebbi Joshua ben Levi said, it is qualified.

The discussion opens mid-tractate with the question of what is done with the *ketoret* — how it is desanctified and resanctified for use in the coming year. The *Gemara* records a debate over whether the *ketoret* must be prepared in a *kli shares* (a consecrated service vessel). Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Chanina holds that it must be made in a *kli shares*, while Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds that this requirement does not apply.

### Shmuel & R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֵּירִבִּי בּוּן. אַתְיָא דְרִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן חֲנִינָה כִשְׁמוּאֵל וּדְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי כְרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּתַנִּינָן. הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן דְּבָרִים רְאוּיִין לְכָל קָרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. קְטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Bun said, Rebbi Yose ben Ḥanina's parallels Samuel and Rebbi Joshua ben Levi's Rebbi Joḥanan, as we have stated, "if one dedicated his property to the Temple and there were objects appropriate as public offerings." Rebbi Joḥanan said, incense.

Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Bun then aligns these positions with two Babylonian *amoraim*: the view of Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Chanina, who requires a *kli shares*, corresponds to the position of Shmuel, while the view of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who does not require one, corresponds to Rabbi Yochanan.

To demonstrate this alignment, the *Gemara* invokes the *mishnah* that will appear shortly: *hamakdish nechasav v'hayu bahen devarim reuyim lekorbanot tzibur* — someone who sanctifies all of his property, among which are items fit only for *korbanot tzibur* (public offerings). When one says generically "I sanctify all my property," that dedication goes by default to *bedek habayit*, the temple maintenance fund. Yet if the property contains animals or items fit for *korbanot*, a further question arises as to how their sanctity is treated — a matter the upcoming *mishnah* will address in detail. For now, the *Gemara* focuses on a specific line within that discussion.

Rabbi Yochanan explains that the only item in one's private property that is fit exclusively for a *korban tzibur* — and not for any *korban yachid* — is the *ketoret*. Animals, flour, and all other such items can always be brought as private offerings; *ketoret* alone is categorically restricted to communal use.

This raises an immediate difficulty. If the *ketoret* must be prepared in a *kli shares*, how could *ketoret* found in a private home be considered fit for Temple use? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָה. תִּיפְתָּר בְּאוֹמָּן מִשֶּׁלְבֵּית אֶבְטִינָס שֶׁהָיָה נוֹטֵל בִּשְׂכָרוֹ קְטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Hoshaia said, explain it about an artisan of the family Eutinos who took incense as his wages.

Rabbi Hoshaia resolves this by explaining that the *mishnah* can be read as referring to a craftsman from the family of Beit Avtinas — the hereditary guild entrusted with producing the *ketoret* — who received *ketoret* as his wages. It was established practice to compensate these workers with *ketoret*, meaning the worker would possess at home *ketoret* that had, in fact, been prepared inside the Temple in a *kli shares*. When he subsequently sanctifies all his property, this *ketoret* in his possession is genuinely fit for Temple use.

One might wonder: wasn't it established previously that the workers were not actually paid with the *ketoret* itself, but rather that the *ketoret* was used as part of an exchange in which they ultimately received money? The answer is that we will encounter precisely this question in the upcoming *mishnah*, where the possibility of paying workers directly with the *ketoret* — at least according to Rabbi Akiva's view — will be considered. For now, this explanation suffices to account for how a private individual could possess *ketoret* prepared in a *kli shares*.

The *Gemara* then raises a further question from Rabbi Chuni before Rabbi Yossi: if the *ketoret* was indeed made in a *kli shares*, the principle is that something sanctified through a *kli shares* cannot be redeemed. A standard item sanctified for *bedek habayit* can be redeemed by transferring its monetary value — that is *kedushat damim*, sanctity of monetary worth — but once an item has been sanctified through a *kli shares*, its intrinsic use has been consecrated and it may not be redeemed. How then can the entire mechanism of desanctifying and resanctifying the *ketoret* function, if the *ketoret* was produced in a *kli shares*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ. וְלֹא דִשְׁמוּאֵל הִיא. דִשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר. קַל הוּא בְמוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** He said to him, is that not Samuel's? Since Samuel said, one is lenient in the case of leftovers.

The answer given is: *velo deShmuel hi?* — is this not itself Shmuel's position? The question of how to redeem *ketoret* made in a *kli shares* is answered by Shmuel's general principle of *kal hu b'mosar* — that a leniency applies to leftover public offerings. Beit Din stipulates that when funds were used to purchase *korbanot tzibur* and those offerings turn out to be leftover at year's end, greater flexibility is permitted in handling the resulting *hekdesh*. The mechanics of this leniency are not made fully explicit, but the principle is clear: ordinary restrictions that apply to individual offerings are relaxed in the context of communal leftover offerings.

## Leftover Offerings

### Shmuel: Redeem Unblemished Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְפַּלְּגוֹן. הוֹתִירוּ תְמִימִים. שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר. נִפְדִּים כִתְמִימִים. וְרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. נִפְדִּין כִפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** As they disagreed: If unblemished animals were left over, Samuel says, they are redeemed unblemished. Rebbi Joḥanan said, they are redeemed as disqualified *sancta*.

The *Gemara* now demonstrates Shmuel's leniency directly. The case: *hossiru temimim* — at the end of the year, unblemished animals remain that had been sanctified with public funds from the prior year. By Rosh Chodesh Nisan, the new year commences and these animals, purchased with the old year's *terumah* funds, can no longer be used. What is to be done? Shmuel rules: *nifdin temimim* — they are redeemed even though they are unblemished, which completely contravenes the standard rule that a *korban* may only be redeemed once it has acquired a *mum* (a blemish). Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *lo nifdin kefsulei hamukdashim* — they are not redeemed as if they were disqualified offerings; rather, one waits until the animal acquires a *mum* and then redeems it in the normal manner.

This debate, it emerges, directly parallels the earlier question. Shmuel's position that *ketoret* made in a *kli shares* can nonetheless be redeemed finds its logical foundation in this same leniency: Shmuel allows latitude in redeeming public leftover offerings that would not be permitted by normal rules. Rabbi Yochanan, who requires waiting for a *mum*, correspondingly holds that the *ketoret* was not made in a *kli shares* — because if it were, the standard prohibition against redemption would apply and no mechanism would be available.

### Leftover Sin Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹתִירוּ שְׂעִירִים. עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. אִם עוֹלָה נִפְדִּית לֹא כָּל שֶׁכֵּן חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Leftover he-goats, in Samuel's opinion if elevation sacrifices are redeemed, a purification sacrifice so much more.

The *Gemara* extends the analysis: *hossiru se'irim* — what if what remains at year's end is not animals designated for an *olah* but male goats (*se'irim*) designated for a *chatat* (sin offering), as used in *musaf* services? According to Shmuel, would even these be redeemable while unblemished?

A *kal vachomer* (argument from the lenient to the stringent) is proposed: *im olah nifdeist lo kol sheken chatat?* — if an *olah* can be redeemed while unblemished, how much more so should a *chatat* be redeemable?

The *Korban HaEdah* explains the logic of this *kal vachomer* as follows. In the case of the *olah*: if we had not redeemed it while unblemished and had instead waited for it to acquire a *mum*, the animal would still ultimately have been repurchased and re-sanctified as an *olah*, remaining within the same category. The money from its redemption would return to fund another *olah*. In the case of the *chatat*, however, had we not redeemed it while unblemished and had waited for a *mum*, the animal could never have served as a *chatat* again — a *chatat* of an individual cannot be redeemed, but a *chatat tzibur* that becomes blemished is indeed redeemable, and that money would go to *kayitz hamizbeach* (supplementary burnt offerings), not back to a *chatat*. By permitting redemption while the animal is still *tamim* (unblemished), we allow the original animal to be reused as a *chatat* and kept within its proper category. This is why the *chatat* presents a *kal vachomer* over the *olah*: the benefit of early redemption is even greater for the *chatat* than for the *olah*.

### R. Yochanan: Normal Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דִּרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אָמַר רִבִּי זְעוּרָא. יִרְעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In Rebbi Joḥanan's opinion? Rebbi Ze`ira said, they shall graze.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, no such latitude exists. Rav Zeira rules *yireu* — they are left to graze until they acquire a blemish, at which point normal rules of redemption apply.

### Repurposing as Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק. מְקַיְיצִין בָּהֶן אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Samuel bar Rav Isaac said, one uses them to adorn the altar.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak then introduces a far more radical position: *m'kayitzin bahen es hamizbeach* — the leftover sin offering animals, rather than being redeemed and the money used for *kayitz hamizbeach*, are repurposed directly as *olot* (burnt offerings). He is not merely saying they are redeemed and the money goes toward an *olah*; he is saying the *chatat* animal itself is brought as an *olah*.

### Sin → Burnt Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא. יֵשׁ חַטָּאת קְרֵיבָה עוֹלָה. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה. שַׁנְיָיא הִיא. שֶׁאֶין קָרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר נִקְבָּעִין אָלָּא בִשְׁחִיטָה. אָמַר רִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא עַל הַמּוֹתָרוֹת שֶׁיָּקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** This is difficult. May a purification sacrifice be brought as elevation sacrifice? Rebbi Yose said, there is a difference, for public sacrifices are determined only by slaughter. Rebbi Ḥananiah said, it is a stipulation of the Court that all leftovers should be brought as elevation sacrifices.

The *Gemara* challenges this sharply: *yeish chatat shek'reiva olah?* — has one ever heard of a *chatat* being brought as an *olah*? The only precedent for transferring sanctity between categories is the ruling that a *pesach* can become a *shelamim* under certain circumstances. But a *chatat* becoming an *olah* is unheard of.

Two explanations are offered in response. Rabbi Yossi states: *shani she'ein korbanot tzibur nikva'in ela b'shechitah* — public offerings are different, in that their identity is not definitively fixed until the moment of slaughter. Even after an animal has been sanctified as a *chatat tzibur*, its precise identity remains open until *shechitah*. This is presented as a natural feature of *korbanot tzibur* — an inherent rule that was perhaps not previously known — which allows for a change of purpose right up to the moment of the offering.

Rabbi Chiyya offers a second explanation: *tnai beis din hi al hamotar sheyikrevu olot* — it is a stipulation of Beit Din that all leftover public offerings shall be brought as *olot*. This approach does not rely on a natural legal rule but on a proactive rabbinic stipulation that gives the institution the flexibility to redirect these funds and animals as circumstances require. This same principle — that Beit Din's stipulation underwrites the broader financial mechanics of the *terumah* funds — was already implicit in what was discussed regarding the use of *terumah halishkah* for purposes beyond the direct purchase of *korbanot*, such as support of *talmidei chachamim* who teach the laws of the offerings.

## Mishnah: Ketoret

### Sanctifying Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן דְּבָרִים רְאוּיִין לְקָרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּיבּוּר יִנָּתְנוּ לָאוּמָּנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָה. אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן עֲזַאי אֵינָהּ הִיא הַמִּידָּה אֶלָּא מַפְרִישִׁין מֵהֶן שְׂכַר הָאוּמָּנִין וּמְחַלְּלִין אוֹתָן עַל מָעוֹת הָאוּמָּנִין וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָן לָאוּמָּנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן וְחוֹזְרִין וְלוֹקְחִין אוֹתָן מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: If somebody gives his property to the Temple and in it there were items usable for public sacrifices, they shall be given to the artisans as their wages, the words of Rebbi Aqiba. Ben Azzai said to him, this is not they way, but from it one sets apart the artisans' wages and redeems it on the artisan's money; one gives it to the artisans as their wages and then buys it back from them from the new disbursement.

The *Gemara* now arrives at today's *daf*, 12a, and the *mishnah* itself. *Hamakdish nechasav v'hayu bahen devarim reuyim lekorbanot tzibur* — one who sanctifies all of his property and among those possessions are items fit for *korbanot tzibur*. As established, the only item in private hands that could be fit exclusively for a *korban tzibur* and not for a *korban yachid* is the *ketoret*. The *mishnah* therefore addresses what is to be done with such *ketoret*: *yinatnu l'umnin b'scharan* — it should be given to the craftsmen (the *umnin*, the workers who produce the *ketoret*) as their wages. This is the ruling attributed to Rabbi Akiva.

The rationale for this prescription reflects an important constraint. Even though the sanctification was the act of a private individual, and even though that individual's *ketoret* cannot actually be used for the Temple offering — since the *korban tzibur* must be purchased with public funds — the item cannot simply revert to *bedek habayit* either. As will emerge more clearly in the continuation of the *mishnah*, items that are fit for *korbanot* cannot simply be absorbed into the general *bedek habayit* pool without being channeled toward their proper purpose.

## Ketoret & Kli Share (III)

### R. Akiva's Position

Rabbi Akiva's position raises a difficulty in light of the earlier debate. If the *ketoret* was made in a *kli shares*, how can it be redeemed at all? The *Gemara* applies the earlier answer: Rabbi Akiva's position is consistent with Shmuel's leniency, which permits redemption of leftover public offerings under circumstances where standard rules would normally prohibit it.

## Mishnah: Ketoret (II)

### Paying Workers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן דְּבָרִים רְאוּיִין לְקָרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּיבּוּר יִנָּתְנוּ לָאוּמָּנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָה. אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן עֲזַאי אֵינָהּ הִיא הַמִּידָּה אֶלָּא מַפְרִישִׁין מֵהֶן שְׂכַר הָאוּמָּנִין וּמְחַלְּלִין אוֹתָן עַל מָעוֹת הָאוּמָּנִין וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָן לָאוּמָּנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן וְחוֹזְרִין וְלוֹקְחִין אוֹתָן מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: If somebody gives his property to the Temple and in it there were items usable for public sacrifices, they shall be given to the artisans as their wages, the words of Rebbi Aqiba. Ben Azzai said to him, this is not they way, but from it one sets apart the artisans' wages and redeems it on the artisan's money; one gives it to the artisans as their wages and then buys it back from them from the new disbursement.

Rabbi Akiva therefore rules that the *ketoret* itself is handed directly to the workers as payment: *shehayu notnim b'scharan ketoret* — they gave them *ketoret* as their wages. This is possible because Rabbi Akiva evidently holds that *ketoret* may be made outside of a *kli shares*, or that the *ketoret* in question was already produced in a *kli shares* and came into the worker's possession through prior payment, as explained earlier.

Ben Azzai objects to this approach and provides an alternative: *mafrishim mehen sechar ha'umnin* — one first sets aside the monetary amount to be paid to the workers; *umechallelin osam al hama'ot* — one then transfers the *kedushah* of the *ketoret* onto that money; *v'al ma'ot ha'umnin venotnim osam la'umnin bischaran* — one gives the *ketoret* to the workers as their wages now that it has been desanctified; *vechozrin velokechin osam mitrumah chadasha* — and then one repurchases the *ketoret* using new *terumah* funds. In this way, the workers are compensated, the *ketoret* is formally desanctified and resanctified with proper public funds, and it is now available for use in the Temple service.

## Ketoret & Kli Share (III)s…

### Ben Azzai's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה טַעֲמֵיהּ דָּבֶן עַזַּאי. שָׁאֵין הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ מִתְחַלֵּל עַל הַמְּלָאכָה אֶלֵּא עַל הַמָּעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** What is Ben Azzai's reason? For Temple property cannot be redeemed by work, only by coins.

The *Gemara* then explains why Ben Azzai rejects Rabbi Akiva's simpler approach of direct payment: *she'ein hekdesh mitchalel al hamelacha ela al hama'ot* — sacred property cannot be desanctified by transferring its *kedushah* onto abstract labor; it can only be transferred onto tangible money. If the *ketoret* is handed to a worker as wages, where does the *kedushah* go? It cannot transfer onto the act of work performed, since work is not an object. The *kedushah* has no recipient and the desanctification is therefore invalid.

What Rabbi Akiva's position would require is that *kedushah* can in fact transfer onto abstract *melachah* (labor), which is a genuinely striking idea. Alternatively, one might understand his position as relying on some form of stipulation — though it is noted that here we are dealing with a private *hekdesh*, not a public one, so the standard formulation of *kol mah she'daas Rabonan adaata* (all that one does is conditional upon rabbinic approval) may not straightforwardly apply.

## Mixed Property Animals

### R. Eliezer: All Bedek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָ‍יְתָה בָּהֶן בְּהֵמָה רְאוּיָה עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת. רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר זְכָרִים יִמָּ‍כְרוּ לִצְרִיכֵי עוֹלוֹת וּנְקֵיבוֹת יִמָּ‍כְרוּ לִצְרִיכֵי זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים וּדְמֵיהֶן יִפְּלוּ עִם שְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבָּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** If somebody gives his property to the Temple and in it there was an animal fit for the altar, males or females. Rebbi Eliezer says, males should be sold to those who need an elevation offering, and females be sold to those who need well-being offerings, and the revenue together with the remainder be for upkeep of the Temple.

The *mishnah* then addresses a different scenario: *hamakdish nechasav v'hayu bahen behemah re'uyah legabei hamizbeach* — one who sanctifies all of his property and among those possessions are animals fit for the altar. Unlike *ketoret*, animals could in principle be brought as either a *korban tzibur* or a *korban yachid*, so the issue is not which category of offering they serve but rather what type of *kedushah* attaches to them in the context of a generic, undifferentiated sanctification.

The *mishnah* specifies: *zecharim unekevot* — there were both male and female animals. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *zecharim yimachru l'tzorchei olot unekevot yimachru l'tzorchei zivchei shelamim* — the males are sold to people who need to bring an *olah*, and the females are sold to people who need to bring a *shelamim*. The money from these sales — *vedmeihem yiplu im she'ar nechasim l'bedek habayit* — is pooled with the rest of his property for *bedek habayit*.

Rabbi Eliezer's position rests on the principle that a generic sanctification is always for *bedek habayit*. Even these animals, though they are fit for the altar, are owned by the temple treasury in the capacity of *bedek habayit*. However, since they have been caught in the net of *hekdesh* and are capable of serving as *korbanot*, they cannot simply be absorbed into the general fund without being channeled toward the altar. The solution is to sell them to individuals who need those specific offerings: an unblemished male is sold to someone who needs to bring an *olah*. When that person purchases the animal, the *kedushah* of *bedek habayit* transfers to the money paid — which now goes to the *bedek habayit* fund — and the purchaser re-sanctifies the animal as his own *korban*. Both goals are thereby achieved: the animal reaches the altar, and the temple maintenance fund receives its value.

One might ask whether the animal could simply have been sold to the *shekalim* fund for a *korban tzibur*. The answer is that the *shekalim* funds are themselves already *kadosh*, and *kedushah* cannot be transferred onto something that is already consecrated. A transfer of *kedushah* requires unsanctified money as its recipient.

### R. Yehoshua: Olah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר. זְכָרִים עַצְמָן יִקָּרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת וּנְקֵבוֹת יִמָּכְרוּ לִצְרִיכֵי זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלוֹת. וּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים יִפְּלוּ לְבֶדֶק הַבָּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joshua says, the males themselves should be brought as elevation sacrifices, and the females be sold to those who need well-being offerings, and the remaining property be used for upkeep of the Temple.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees. In his view, the *kedushah* is not uniformly *kedushat bedek habayit* for all items; rather, a generic *kedushah* takes on the character appropriate to the object it attaches to. For the house, the car, and the furniture, the sanctity becomes *bedek habayit*. For the animals, however — since they are fit for the altar — the generic sanctification crystallizes into a *kedushat korban*. Rabbi Yehoshua therefore rules: *zecharim atzman yikrevu olot* — the males themselves are brought as *olot*. That is how the *kedushah* expresses itself when it encounters an unblemished male animal.

For the females, however, there is a further limitation. Since the generic sanctification was fundamentally directed toward *bedek habayit*, which means total dedication to the Temple with no benefit accruing to the owner, the only *korban* identity that can properly express this character is that of an *olah* — which is entirely consumed on the altar. A *shelamim*, most of which is eaten by the owner, is too different in character from a *bedek habayit* sanctification for the *kedushah* to crystallize in that form. Therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua rules that even the females receive a *kedushat olah* in terms of their sanctity — yet since females cannot be brought as an *olah*, the practical ruling is: *yimachru l'tzorchei zivchei shelamim* — they are sold to people who need to bring a *shelamim* (since the animal itself can still serve that function when re-sanctified by a buyer), *veyavi bidmeihem olot* — and the proceeds are used to purchase an *olah*. *U'shear nechasim yiplu l'bedek habayit* — everything else in the estate goes to *bedek habayit*.

### Akiva & Pappias

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִדִּבְרֵי רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שֶׁרִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִשְׁוָה אֶת מִדּוֹתָיו וְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חָלַק. אָמַר רַבִּי פַּפָּיָס שָׁמַעְתִּי אֶת דִּבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶן הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בְּפֵרוּשׁ כְּדִבְרֵי רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְהַמַּקְדִּישׁ סְתָם כְּדִבְרֵי רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ:
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aqiba says, I agree with the words of Rebbi Eliezer against the words of Rebbi Joshua, since Rebbi Eliezer is uniform in his rules but Rebbi Joshua splits. Rebbi Pappaeus said, I heard according to the words of both of them. If somebody dedicates explicitly, following the words of Rebbi Eliezer; if the dedication is unspecified, following the words of Rebbi Joshua.

Rabbi Akiva weighs in: *ro'eh ani es divrei Rebbe Eliezer midivrei Rebbe Yehoshua* — he prefers Rabbi Eliezer's position over Rabbi Yehoshua's, for the reason that *Rebbe Eliezer hishveh midosav* — Rabbi Eliezer is internally consistent, treating the entire sanctification uniformly as *bedek habayit*, whereas *Rebbe Yehoshua hillek* — Rabbi Yehoshua introduces a distinction, allowing the *kedushah* to bifurcate depending on the object. It is worth noting the verbal echo: at the opening of the *mishnah* Ben Azzai criticized Rabbi Akiva's approach as *eino min hamiddah*, and here Rabbi Akiva turns the same language back in a different direction, praising Rabbi Eliezer for *hishveh midosav* — measuring with a single consistent standard.

Rabbi Pappias then reports: *shamati k'divrei sheneihem* — I heard that in practice both approaches were applied, meaning the ruling depended on the circumstances.

### Explicit vs. Generic

The resolution is as follows. *Hamakdish b'feirush* — if the person was explicit in his sanctification, specifying his animals alongside the rest of his property, we follow Rabbi Eliezer. By making animals an explicit category in his declaration while not specifying that they should be for *korbanot*, he signals that he intends them all to be treated the same. *Hamakdish stam* — if the sanctification was entirely generic, without explicit mention of animals, we follow Rabbi Yehoshua, and the *kedushah* differentiates itself according to the nature of each object.

## Wine, Oil, Birds

### Non-Animal Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן דְּבָרִים רְאוּיִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ יֵינוֹת וּשְׁמָנִים וְעוֹפוֹת. רִבִּי לָעְזָר אוֹמֵר יִימָּכְרוּ לִצְרִיכֵי אוֹתוֹ הַמִּין וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלוֹת וּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים יִפְּלוּ לְבֶדֶק הַבָּיִת:
>
> **Translation:** If somebody gives his property to the Temple and in it there were things fit for the altar, wines, oils, or birds. Rebbi Eleazar says, they shall be sold to people needing these kinds and from the proceeds one shall bring elevation sacrifices; but the remainder of the property shall be used for the upkeep of the Temple.

The *mishnah* continues with a third scenario: *hamakdish nechasav v'hayu bahen devarim hara'uyim legabei hamizbeach* — one who sanctifies property that contains items fit for the altar, but not as animal *korbanot*. The examples given are *yeinot u'shmanim* — wine, which could serve as *nesachim* (libations), and oil, which could serve as *menachot* (meal offerings), and *ofot* — birds, which, though they are an animal offering, are placed in a distinct category here.

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *yimakru l'tzorchei oto hamin* — these items are sold for use in their respective categories. The wine is sold to someone who needs *nesachim*, the oil to someone who needs *menachot*, and the birds to someone who needs to bring a bird offering. In this way, the items are directed toward their proper Temple use.

### Attribution Problem

What becomes problematic is the end of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling here: *sheyavi bidmeihen olot* — the proceeds from these sales go toward an *olah*. This is striking, because a ruling that uses the proceeds for an *olah* sounds like Rabbi Yehoshua's approach, not Rabbi Eliezer's. Under Rabbi Eliezer's logic, the proceeds should go to *bedek habayit*.

Several commentators resolve this by emending the text's attribution, reading *Rabbi Elazar* rather than *Rabbi Eliezer* — treating this as an independent, third opinion. Alternatively, one might argue that Rabbi Eliezer himself distinguishes between large livestock and inanimate items or birds, conceding in this more limited context to an approach resembling Rabbi Yehoshua's. The logic for such a distinction, however, remains unclear. In any case, the structure of the ruling — sell the items for their proper use, and direct the proceeds toward an *olah* — is essentially a Rabbi Yehoshua-type position, whether or not it is Rabbi Eliezer himself saying it. *V'shear nechasim yiplu l'veit habayit* — and everything else in the estate goes to *bedek habayit*.

## Bedek Habayit Traits

### Generic → Bedek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּמָּן תַּנִּינָן. יֵשׁ בְּקָדְשֵׁי בֶדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁסְּתָם הֶקְדֵּישׁוֹת לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת. הֶקְדֵּשׁ בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת חָל עַל הַכֹּל וּמוֹעֲלִין בְּגִידּוּלֵיהֶן וְאֵין בָּהֶן הֲנָיָיה לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** There we have stated: "There is about gifts for the upkeep of the Temple that unspecified gifts to the Temple are for the upkeep of the Temple. Sanctification for the upkeep of the Temple falls on everything, and *me`ilah* applies to what grows from it, and priests have no usufruct from it."

The *Gemara* quotes a *baraita* from Temurah that enumerates the characteristics unique to *kodshei bedek habayit* as against *kodshei mizbeach*. The first principle: *stam hekdeshos l'bedek habayit* — a generic sanctification defaults to *bedek habayit*. This is the rule operative in our *mishnah*. It is worth pausing to note that this default is not as obvious as it might appear. The *Beit Hamikdash* maintained multiple funds, including the large and important fund of *shirei halishkah*, which financed walls and infrastructure. The question arises: when one writes a check to the Temple, as it were, why does it go specifically to *bedek habayit* and not to one of these other funds? The answer, it seems, is that those other funds were structurally tied to the *shekel* contributions and the surpluses of *terumah halishkah*, and were not open to voluntary individual contributions. The only fund available for an individual to donate to generically was *bedek habayit*.

### Universal Application

The second characteristic: *hekdesh bedek habayit chal al hakol* — the sanctity of *bedek habayit* can take effect on any object whatsoever. Unlike *korbanot*, which are restricted to unblemished animals meeting specific requirements, there is no categorical restriction on what can be sanctified for *bedek habayit*. Any item of value may be dedicated.

### Me'ilah on Growth

The third characteristic: *u'moalin b'giduleihen* — there is *me'ilah* (the prohibition against misappropriating sacred property) on things that grow from items sanctified as *bedek habayit*. This reflects a fundamental conceptual difference between *kedushat bedek habayit* and *kedushat korban*. The *kedushah* of *bedek habayit* is essentially *kedushat damim* — sanctity of monetary value and ownership. The Temple owns the item. Therefore, when that item produces fruit, offspring, or milk, those products also belong to the Temple and are subject to the laws of *me'ilah*.

The *kedushah* of a *korban*, by contrast, is about identity and purpose — not ownership in the ordinary sense. If I sanctify an animal as a *shelamim*, its identity is that of a sacrificial offering. But its offspring and its milk are extraneous to that identity; they are not caught up in the same *kedushah* and do not belong to the Temple by virtue of the animal's *korban* status. That is why *me'ilah* applies to the growth of *bedek habayit* items in a way it does not apply to the growth of *kodshei mizbeach*.

### No Kohen Benefit

The fourth characteristic: *v'ein bahem hana'ah l'kohanim* — the *kohanim* receive no benefit from *kodshei bedek habayit*, in contrast to *kodshei mizbeach*. Even from an *olah*, which is burned entirely on the altar, the *kohanim* benefit by receiving the hide. From *bedek habayit* items, however, everything goes entirely toward the repair and maintenance of the Temple, and no priestly benefit is derived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי לִעֶזֶר. וְאִישׁ כִּ‍י יַקְדִּשׁ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ קוֹדֶשׁ לַ‍יי. מִיכָּן שֶׁסְּתָם הֶקְדֵּישׁוֹת לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, the reason of Rebbi Eliezer : *If a man dedicate his house holy to the Eternal*. From here that unspecified dedications to the Temple are for the upkeep of the Temple.

The *Gemara* then observes that the *baraita* aligns with Rabbi Eliezer's position from our *mishnah*, as his ruling consistently treats generic sanctification as *bedek habayit* in all cases.

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the scriptural source for Rabbi Eliezer's approach in the verse *ish ki yakdish et beito kodesh l'Hashem* — "a man who sanctifies his house as sacred to God." The *Gemara* reasons that *beito* here cannot mean literally his residential dwelling, since that is addressed in a separate verse. Rather, *beito* means his household — all of his property. And since the verse discusses sanctification going to the Temple without specifying any particular type of offering, the default conclusion is that it all goes to *bedek habayit*.

## Property vs. Flock

### Rav Zeira's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי זְעוּרָה רַב חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רַב. פְּלִיגִין בַּמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו. אֲבָל בַּמַּקְדִּישׁ עֶדְרוֹ כָּל עַמָּא מוֹדֵיי שֶׁהוּא לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ze`ira, Rav Huna in the name of Rav: They disagree about one who gives his property to the Temple. But if he gives his flock to the Temple, everybody agrees that it is for the altar.

Rav Zeira, citing Rav Huna in the name of Rav, proposes a limiting principle regarding the scope of the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. According to Rav Zeira, the dispute applies only when one sanctifies mixed property — a household containing both animals and other items — in which case the question arises of whether the animals receive a differentiated *kedushah*. However, if one explicitly sanctifies only a flock of animals — *aval hamakdish edro* — then all agree that the sanctification goes *l'mizbeach*, since there are no non-animal items requiring a different treatment and no reason to split the *kedushah*.

### R. Ba's Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי בָּא רַב חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רַב. מַה פְלִיגִין. בַּמַּקְדִּישׁ עֶדְרוֹ. אֲבָל בַּמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו כָּל עַמָּא מוֹדֵיי שֶׁהוּא לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abba, Rav Huna in the name of Rav: Where do they disagree? About one who gives his flock to the Temple, but if he gave his property to the Temple, everybody agrees that it is for the upkeep of the Temple.

Rabbi Ba bar Huna, also citing Rav, reads the *mishnah* in the opposite direction. In his view, the debate in the *mishnah* occurs precisely in the case of *hamakdish edro* — one who sanctifies only his flock. That is the scenario where Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua disagree about whether the animals receive *kedushat bedek habayit* or *kedushat mizbeach*. But if one truly sanctified mixed property of all kinds, even Rabbi Yehoshua would agree that everything goes to *bedek habayit*.

This is a challenging reading of the *mishnah*, because the *mishnah* explicitly states that *she'ar nechasim* — the other property — goes to *bedek habayit*, implying that there is indeed other property present. Under Rabbi Ba's reading, one would need to say that the other property consists of animals that are not fit for *korbanot* — those that are too old, already blemished, or otherwise disqualified — and it is only those that go to *bedek habayit*. This is acknowledged to be a difficult reading. The *Gilyon HaShas* essentially supports this interpretation as the only way to make Rabbi Ba's position work, but the strain on the text is clear.

### R. Yochanan: Same Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. לֹא שַׁנְיָיא. הִיא הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו הִיא הַמַּקְדִּישׁ עֶדְרוֹ. הִיא הַמַּחֲלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, there is no difference. The disagreement is both about one who gives his property to the Temple as about one who gives his flock to the Temple.

Rabbi Yochanan takes a third approach, one that cuts through the entire distinction: *lo shanu* — it makes no difference. Whether one sanctified mixed property or only a flock of animals, the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua applies in exactly the same way. Rabbi Eliezer would always hold that everything is *l'bedek habayit*, and Rabbi Yehoshua would always hold that for animals the *kedushah* takes on a *korban* character.

## Redeeming Bedek

### Redemption Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רַב רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. קָדְשֵׁי בֶדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁפּוֹדִין תְּמִימִין יָצְאוּ לְחוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥuna in the name of Rav, Rebbi Abbahu in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: *Sancta* given for the upkeep of the Temple which were redeemed unblemished become profane.

With this the *Gemara* turns to a new question, one that cuts to the heart of the conceptual distinction between *kedushat bedek habayit* and *kedushat mizbeach*. Rav Huna in the name of Rav Avu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: *kodshei bedek habayit shepadaan temimim yatzu l'chulin* — if items consecrated as *bedek habayit* were redeemed while they were still unblemished, they exit their consecrated status completely and become fully *chulin* (profane).

This question has genuine depth. Everyone agrees that one can explicitly sanctify an unblemished animal as *bedek habayit* — that is, one can deliberately dedicate an unblemished sheep to the temple maintenance fund rather than as a *korban*. (The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerns only the generic case.) When such an animal is subsequently redeemed, does it become entirely *chulin*, or does some residual status remain?

The conceptual tension is as follows. The *kedushah* of *bedek habayit* is primarily a *kedushah* of ownership and monetary value — the Temple owns the item and derives value from it. When that monetary value is transferred to a substitute through redemption, in principle the *kedushah* should depart entirely from the original object, since the object's only relevance was its value. This contrasts with *kedushat korban*, where the *kedushah* is bound up with the animal's identity and purpose, such that even after redemption some residual identity remains. The *halacha* is well established that even after a *korban* with a *mum* has been redeemed, certain restrictions continue to apply — one may not shear it or milk it — because the identity of the animal as a former *korban* does not entirely dissolve.

The ruling of Rav Huna is that for *kodshei bedek habayit*, the redemption is complete: the item exits fully to *chulin*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתַא אָמְרָה כֵן. וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָם.
>
> **Translation:** A Mishnah says so. "their offspring and their milk are permitted after their redemption."

The *Gemara* then asks whether the *mishnah* confirms this. The *mishnah* in Temurah, which listed the characteristics unique to each type of sanctification, states concerning *kodshei mizbeach*: *veladan vechalavan asur l'achar pidyonan* — the offspring and the milk of a redeemed *korban* remain forbidden even after redemption. Implicitly, then, by *kodshei bedek habayit*, the offspring and milk would be *mutar* — permitted. This seems to support the ruling that *bedek habayit* sanctity does not leave behind a residual restriction.

### Rav Chisda's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה בְשֵׁם רַב חִסְדָּא. תִּיפְתָּר שֶׁפּוֹדִין תְּמִימִין וְהוּמְמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizqiah in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Explain it if they were redeemed unblemished and then became defective.

Rav Chizkiyah in the name of Rav Chisda clarifies the precise scope of the ruling. The statement that *kodshei bedek habayit shepadaan temimim yatzu l'chulin* applies when the animals were redeemed and subsequently acquired a *mum*. In that case, once the *mum* appears after redemption, the animal may be used without restriction. If, however, the animal remains unblemished after redemption, there is at least a rabbinic-level restriction against using it immediately — it would look inappropriate to take an unblemished animal that was sanctified and immediately put it to mundane use. So the blanket statement of Rav Huna requires qualification: full permissibility comes when the animal acquires a *mum* after having been redeemed.

### Milk & Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יּוֹסֵה בְשֵׁם רַב חִסְדָּא. מַתְנִיתַא אָמְרָה כֵן. וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Does the Mishnah say so? "Their offspring and their milk are forbidden after their redemption."

Rabbi Yose in the name of Rav Chisda notes that the *mishnah*'s implication — that by *kodshei bedek habayit* the offspring and milk are permitted after redemption — refers to the case before the animal acquired a *mum* after redemption. In that stage, when the animal itself cannot yet be freely used, the milk and offspring are nonetheless permitted. Only once the animal acquires a *mum* does the fuller permissibility apply, extending to the animal itself. This distinguishes the *mishnah*'s more limited *heter* (permissibility of milk and offspring) from Rabbi Yochanan's broader ruling (full permissibility), by placing them at different stages of the animal's post-redemption history.
