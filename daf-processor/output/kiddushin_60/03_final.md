# Kiddushin 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Voiding Pending Kiddushin

### Dibbur Mevattel Dibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן״. רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״ תְּנָאָה הָוֵי, מְקַיֵּים תְּנָאָה וְאָזֵיל. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן״ לְכִי יָהֵיב הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the text of this mishna: **Rav Huna says** that it reads: **And he shall give,** i.e., it is a valid betrothal from the outset, and the man is obligated to give her the promised amount. **Rav Yehuda says** that the correct version of the mishna is: **When he gives,** meaning that the betrothal takes effect only when he actually gives her the money. The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Huna says** that the phrase: **And he shall give,** indicates that this **is a condition** stipulated by the man, which means that the woman is immediately betrothed and he merely **proceeds to fulfill the condition. Rav Yehuda says** that the mishna reads: **When he gives,** which indicates that **when he will give** the money it **will be a betrothal,** but for **now at least** it **is not a betrothal.**

The daf opens by returning to the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning a case where a man designates that the effect of *kiddushin* will take place later — he gives the money now, but the *kiddushin* is not to take effect immediately. The question is whether such an arrangement can be voided through words alone, or whether some *ma'aseh* is required. The principle at stake is *ati dibbur u-mevattel dibbur* — can speech negate speech?

The underlying tension is that while Reish Lakish concedes that purely verbal acts can be negated verbally — appointing a *shaliach*, for instance, is a verbal act and can therefore be verbally revoked — the present case involves an actual *ma'aseh*: money was physically handed over as *kiddushin*. The question is to what degree that act is considered legally operative when its effect has been postponed, and whether it therefore falls into the category of *ma'aseh* or remains merely in the domain of *dibbur*.

### Reish Lakish's Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא קִדְּשָׁהּ אֶת עַצְמָּהּ וְחָזְרָה בָּהּ, מַהוּ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת, אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, לָא אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages discuss this *halakha*: If she did **not accept betrothal herself and retracted** her appointment of the agent, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: She can retract** her appointment; **and Reish Lakish says: She cannot retract** her appointment. The Gemara clarifies their respective opinions. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: She can retract** her appointment, as **a statement comes and nullifies** a previous **statement,** by which she appointed the agent. **Reish Lakish said: She cannot retract** her appointment, as **a statement does not come and nullify** a previous **statement.**

The *Gemara* presents two versions of Reish Lakish's position. In the first, he disputes only the case of the postponed monetary act. In the second, far more striking version, he disputes even the case of a woman who designated a *shaliach* to receive *kiddushin* on her behalf — arguing that she cannot verbally revoke the appointment. This position is difficult to the point of being untenable: it would mean a woman could be married against her will. Tosafot grapples with this reading, but the *Gemara* clearly concludes that it cannot be correct. If a woman designates a *shaliach*, she may verbally void the *shlichus*.

### Hilchesa Ke-Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ בַּשָּׁלִיחַ אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אַחֲרָיו שָׁלִיחַ וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״גֵּט שֶׁנָּתַתִּי לְךָ בָּטֵל הוּא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה בָּטֵל תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish:** With regard to **one who sends a bill of divorce to his wife, and** later the husband **encountered the agent or sent** another **agent after him and** in this manner **said to him: The bill of divorce that I gave you is nullified, it is thereby nullified.** This indicates that the appointment of an agent can be nullified even by a statement. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation** of the opinion **of Reish Lakish is** in fact **a conclusive refutation.**

The *Gemara* records a definitive contradiction to Reish Lakish — *tiuvta de-Reish Lakish tiuvta* — demonstrating that a *shaliach* appointment can indeed be verbally revoked. And then the *Gemara* goes further: *ve-hilchesa ke-Rabbi Yochanan ve-afilu be-kameita* — we rule like Rabbi Yochanan even in the first version of the dispute, even where money was physically given with the effect designated to take place thirty days later. Even that can be verbally voided before it takes effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּקַמַּיְיתָא. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: שָׁאנֵי נְתִינַת מָעוֹת לְיַד אִשָּׁה דִּכְמַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, and** this is **even according to the first** version of their dispute, with regard to a woman who retracts from her betrothal after accepting the money for it. **And even though there is** room **to say** that **giving money to a woman** for betrothal **is different, as it is considered like an action, even so, a statement comes and nullifies** a previous **statement** with regard to her consent to the betrothal, provided that the betrothal has yet to take effect.

The *Gemara* anticipates the objection: *shani nesinas mammon le-yad ha-ishah de-kama'aseh dami* — the actual giving of money into a woman's hands is like a real act. Nevertheless, the ruling stands: *afilu hachi ati dibbur u-mevattel dibbur*. As long as the *kiddushin* has not yet taken effect, words can void it.

### Status Change Analysis

The *Gemara* explains why. The classic case in which *dibbur* cannot negate a *ma'aseh* involves a situation where a status change has already occurred — for instance, once an object has been rendered a finished vessel, it becomes susceptible to *tum'ah*, and a subsequent mental intention to resume work on it is irrelevant, because the status has already changed. Here, by contrast, although money was given, no halachic status has yet changed. The woman's status has not shifted; the man's status has not shifted; everything has been projected into the future. Since nothing has yet been concretized halachically, the entire arrangement remains in the domain of *dibbur*, and words suffice to void it.

## Get vs. Shaliach Voiding

### Conflicting Piskei Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא הִילְכְתָא אַהִילְכְתָא. אָמְרַתְּ: הִילְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְקַיְימָא לַן הִילְכְתָא כְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחֲזוֹר וִיגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The *halakha*** recorded here is **difficult** with regard to **the *halakha*** recorded elsewhere. **You said** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, and** yet **we maintain** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman. As a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* in the case of a bill of divorce sent by an agent that the husband nullified before it was received by his wife? Can the husband **return and divorce** his wife **with** the very same bill of divorce?

The *Gemara* immediately raises a contradiction: *kashya hilchesa a-hilchesa*. On one hand, we have just established that we rule like Rabbi Yochanan — an arrangement to take effect later can be verbally voided. On the other hand, we also hold *hilchesa ke-Rav Nachman*, that in the case of voiding a *shaliach ha-get*, we rule like Rav Nachman. What was that ruling?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ. וְקַיְימָא לַן הִילְכְתָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** He **can return and divorce** her **with it,** and **Rav Sheshet says: He cannot return and divorce** her **with it. And we maintain** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Naḥman** in this case.

The case, discussed in Gittin, is as follows: a man sent a *shaliach* to deliver a *get* to his wife, then chased down the *shaliach* and verbally revoked the *shlichus*. The question is whether, having voided the *shaliach*, he may reuse the *get* document itself. Rav Nachman says yes; Rav Sheshet says no. We rule like Rav Nachman. But how is this consistent with our ruling that a verbal voiding dissolves everything? If the writing of the *get* is analogous to giving money to the woman's hands — both are acts done now with effect designated for later — then just as the *kiddushin* is voided, the *get* should be voided as well, and could not be reused.

### Get Remains Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּבַטְּלֵיהּ מִתּוֹרַת שְׁלִיחַ, מִתּוֹרַת גֵּט לָא בַּטְּלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, according to the ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan, since the husband has nullified the bill of divorce it should no longer be valid. The Gemara answers: This is not difficult, as, **although** the husband **has nullified** the agent **from his status as an agent, he has not nullified** the bill of divorce itself **from its status as a bill of divorce.**

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction directly: *nehi d'batlei mishum shlichus mishum get lo batlei* — yes, you voided the *shlichus*, but you did not void the *get* itself. The two are entirely separate. One might ask why: in both cases, has not the decisive act been done now, with effect pending?

The answer lies in a crucial distinction. When money is given for *kiddushin* with a postponed effect, nothing new has been created. The money remains money; the woman's status is unchanged; the man's status is unchanged. The act has been suspended in abstraction. But when a *get* is written, the act of writing creates something concrete here and now — this piece of paper acquires the halachic status of a *get*. That is a genuine *ma'aseh*, analogous to designating an object as *terumah* or rendering an item a completed vessel. The writing transforms the document's status in the present moment.

### Maasah Analysis

This distinction clarifies the ruling. Because the *get* document itself has undergone a status change — it has become a *get* — that status cannot be dissolved through words alone. The voiding of the *shaliach* affects only the agency, not the instrument. The *get* document remains fully valid, and may be reused. By contrast, the giving of *kiddushin* money with a postponed effect created no present status change whatsoever, and therefore *ati dibbur u-mevattel dibbur* applies in full. The two rulings are thus entirely consistent.

## Me'achshav U'Le'achar Zmanei

### Rav: Safek Le'olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לַשֵּׁנִי. אָמַר רַב: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לַשֵּׁנִי לְעוֹלָם, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אֲמַר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לַשֵּׁנִי עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם פָּקְעִי קִידּוּשֵׁי שֵׁנִי וְגָמְרִי קִידּוּשֵׁי רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman and stipulates that the betrothal will take effect in thirty days' time and another man betroths her within that period, **she is betrothed to the second** man. **Rav says: She is betrothed to the second forever,** i.e., the betrothal is fully effective, **and Shmuel says: She is betrothed to the second until** the **thirty days** pass. **After thirty days** have passed, **the betrothal of the second lapses, and the** earlier **betrothal of the first** man **is completed.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the precise case under discussion in the *Mishnah*: a man says *me-achshav u-le'achar sheloshim yom* — "as of now and after thirty days." A second man then comes and betroths this woman before the thirty days have elapsed. What is the legal status of both *kiddushin*?

Rav rules: *mekudeshet ve-einah mekudeshet le'olam* — she is betrothed and not betrothed, and this status of uncertainty is permanent. The *safek* — does she belong to man number one or man number two — never resolves. Even after day thirty arrives, the uncertainty endures.

### Shmuel: Tenai Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַב מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי תְּנָאָה הֲוַאי, אִי חֲזָרָה הֲוַאי. לִשְׁמוּאֵל פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דִּתְנָאָה הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **Rav is uncertain** what the first man meant. **Is it a condition,** i.e., the man is saying: If I do not change my mind within thirty days, you are betrothed to me from now, or **is it a retraction,** that is, he immediately retracted after he said: From now, in favor of a betrothal that will take effect only after thirty days have elapsed. This uncertainty remains even after the end of the thirty days. **It is obvious to Shmuel that it is a condition.** Consequently, the uncertainty whether or not she is betrothed to the second man applies during the thirty-day period, as the first man might yet change his mind. After the completion of thirty days, the betrothal from the first man retroactively takes effect from the moment he gave it, and therefore the betrothal of the second man is abrogated.

Shmuel disagrees: *mekudeshet ve-einah mekudeshet ella ad sheloshim yom* — the uncertainty lasts only until day thirty. After that, *paka kiddushei sheni ve-gamri kiddushei rishon* — man number two's *kiddushin* lapses, and man number one's *kiddushin* takes full effect.

Shmuel's reasoning is that the phrase *me-achshav u-le'achar sheloshim yom* functions as a *tenai* — a condition. The meaning is: "You are betrothed to me as of now, provided that day thirty arrives" — or more precisely, provided that nothing intervenes to prevent it (neither party dies, neither reverses). When day thirty does arrive and the *tenai* is satisfied, the *kiddushin* takes effect retroactively from the moment it was given. During the intervening period, therefore, the *safek* is genuine — perhaps the *tenai* will not be satisfied, perhaps man number one will withdraw or die — so man number two's *kiddushin* is uncertain. But once day thirty arrives and the *tenai* is fulfilled, all uncertainty dissolves in favor of man number one.

### Language Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט, דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav and Shmuel disagree **in the dispute between these *tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one says to his wife upon giving her a bill of divorce that it should take effect: **From today and after** my **death,** there is uncertainty whether it **is** a valid **bill of divorce or** whether it **is not a** valid **bill of divorce.** This is **the statement of the Rabbis.** The Rabbis are uncertain whether he meant that the bill of divorce should take effect retroactively from that moment, in which case it is valid; or whether he changed his mind and meant for it to take effect only after his death, which would mean that it is not a valid bill of divorce. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** A document **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce.** The husband meant his statement as a condition that the bill of divorce should, after his death, take effect retroactively from now, and therefore it is a valid bill of divorce.

Rav's position, by contrast, is that the phrase is genuinely ambiguous — not about subjective intent, but as a legal matter. The language could mean either: "as of now, with day thirty as a *tenai*" (Shmuel's reading), or: "not really as of now — when he added *u-le'achar sheloshim yom*, he effectively retracted the *me-achshav* and meant *le-achar sheloshim yom* alone." On this reading, the two readings are mutually exclusive extremes — either entirely now (retroactively upon fulfillment of the *tenai*) or entirely later — and we cannot determine which was meant. Therefore the *safek* is permanent: *mekudeshet ve-einah mekudeshet le'olam*.

The *Gemara* notes that this debate maps directly onto a *tannaitic* dispute. The *braita* records: *me-hayom u-le'achar mita get ve-eino get* — "from today and after I die, this is a *get* and is not a *get*" — *divrei Chachamim*. A man on his deathbed attempting to spare his wife from *yibum* says the *get* should take effect now, assuming he dies. The *Chachamim* hold it is uncertain — perhaps he meant *me-achshav* as a *tenai*, perhaps he retracted and meant *le-achar mita* only, in which case there is no valid *get*. *Rabbi* says: *kazeh get* — it is certainly a *get*, meaning Rabbi reads it definitively as *me-achshav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּנַן, וְנֵימָא שְׁמוּאֵל הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי! צְרִיכָא. דְּאִילּוּ אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּנַן, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָתָם דִּלְרַחוֹקַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֲבָל הָכָא, דִּלְקָרוֹבַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל דִּתְנָאָהּ הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let Rav,** instead of explaining his own opinion, **say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, and let Shmuel say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** for Rav and Shmuel to state their disagreement explicitly. **As had Rav** merely **said** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, I would say: There,** in the case of a bill of divorce, **when he comes to distance her** from himself, one can say that he has changed his mind with regard to the divorce. **But here,** in the case of betrothal, **when he comes to draw her near,** you might **say** that Rav **concedes to Shmuel that it is** certainly **a condition,** as he wants to betroth her as soon as possible.

Rav thus parallels the *Chachamim*; Shmuel parallels Rabbi. The *Gemara* explains why it is insufficient to simply state their positions as *psak* in one of the two cases: the contexts differ in a legally significant way. In *kiddushin*, the man is eager to create the relationship and might be presumed to prefer an earlier effective date; in *gittin*, the man is distancing himself and might be less eager, making the "later" reading more plausible. And in the *get* case, there is an additional pressure toward the immediate reading — because a *get le-achar mita* is legally impossible — that does not exist in *kiddushin*, where *kiddushin* taking effect after thirty days is perfectly valid. Hence both cases are independently necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָתָם הוּא דְּאֵין גֵּט לְאַחַר מִיתָה. אֲבָל הָכָא דְּיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **had Shmuel said** only that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **I would say: It is** in that case **there** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said it is a valid bill of divorce, **as there is no bill of divorce after death,** and therefore it is unlikely that he changed his mind and attempted to give a bill of divorce that will take effect only after the death of the husband. **But here, where there is** the possibility of **betrothal after thirty** days, and he might well have intended to retract the betrothal, you might **say** that **Shmuel concedes to Rav.** Consequently, it was **necessary** to state the dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning betrothal in explicit terms.

Abaye then raises a further case under Rav's framework: *ba echad ve-amar lah harei at mekudeshes li me-hayom u-le'achar lamed yom, u-va acher ve-amar lah harei at mekudeshes li me-hayom u-le'achar esrim yom*, and a third man who says *me-hayom u-le'achar asara yamim*. The ruling: *mi-rishon u-mi-acharon tzricha get, mi-emtzai eina tzricha get*. She requires a *get* from man number one and from man number three, but not from man number two.

The logic is straightforward: *mima nafshach* — whatever you say. If all three statements constitute a *tenai*, then all three take effect retroactively as of the moment of *kiddushin*, and man number one, having acted first, wins. Man number two and man number three are both excluded; she needs only a *get* from man number one. If, conversely, all three statements mean the effect is genuinely delayed, then whoever's designated date arrives first prevails — that is man number three, whose ten-day period expires first. Man number one and man number two are excluded; she needs only a *get* from man number three. Either way, man number two — the *meatzai*, the middle one — is never the winner.

### Fixed Legal Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הַאי לִישָּׁנָא מַשְׁמַע תְּנָאָה וּמַשְׁמַע חֲזָרָה וְתִיבְעֵי גִּיטָּא מִכֹּל חַד וְחַד, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case; what is the novelty of Abaye's statement? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this formulation means a condition and** also **means a retraction, and** therefore she should **require a bill of divorce from each and every one of them,** including the middle man, due to the uncertainty. It is possible the first man retracted while the second one imposed a condition, in which case only the betrothal of the second person takes effect. Abaye therefore **teaches us** that according to the opinion of Rav, the uncertainty concerns the meaning of this expression itself, and there is no uncertainty with regard to the intention of each individual person who says this formula. Consequently, there is no possibility that this woman is betrothed to the middle man.

The *Gemara* asks: isn't this obvious? Why state it? The answer is this: one might have thought that the phrase *me-achshav u-le'achar zman* has no single fixed legal meaning, but rather that each individual speaker might have meant it differently — man number one meaning *me-achshav*, man number two meaning *le-achar zman*, and so forth. If that were so, any one of them could have been the operative *kiddushin*, and all three would require a *get*. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that this is not the case. A legal phrase has one fixed meaning, not a range of personal interpretations. We may not know yet which of two possible fixed meanings it carries, but it carries only one. Whatever the phrase means, it means the same thing for every person who uses it. This is a foundational principle: *devarim she-belev einam devarim* — subjective intent that departs from the objective legal meaning of a term is legally inert. The legal system demands fixed meanings for its operative language.

## Shivura — Ongoing Process View

### Shivura Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה תּוֹפְסִין בָּהּ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה תּוֹפְסִין בָּהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שַׁוּוֹ נַפְשַׁיְהוּ כִּי שִׁרְגָּא דְלִיבְנֵי, דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד רְוָחָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ שְׁבַק.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if **one hundred** betrothed her in this manner, their betrothal **is effective with regard to her. And similarly, Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** the betrothal of **one hundred is effective with regard to her. Rav Mesharshiyya, son of Rav Ami, said to Rabbi Asi: I will explain to you the reason of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** These one hundred men **have rendered themselves like a row [*sheraga*] of bricks, each and every one** of whom **leaves a gap for the other.** Since there is a period of time before the betrothal of each man takes effect, another betrothal can intervene in the meantime.

The *Gemara* now introduces a third interpretive framework, presented by Ulla in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *afilu me'ah tofsim bah* — even a hundred men could be betrothed to this woman under *me-achshav u-le'achar sheloshim yom*. Rav Assi transmits the same ruling in Rabbi Yochanan's name.

This is a striking statement. It does not mean — Rashi is explicit on this — that there is a *safek* as to which one of a hundred men married her, such that any one of them might have been the operative betrothal. Rather, it means literally that all hundred could be simultaneously betrothed to her. The mechanism is explained by Rav Mesharshiya son of Rav Ami: *shavtah nafshah ki-sharga d'livni* — she made herself like a row of bricks, *de-chol chad ve-chad ravcha le-chavreih shavik* — each brick leaving space for the next one alongside it.

The brick-row metaphor captures a third reading of *me-achshav u-le'achar sheloshim yom* that is distinct from both Rav's and Shmuel's. This phrase does not mean either "now" (with a *tenai*) or "later" (with a retraction of *me-achshav*). It means that the *kiddushin* is a continuous process: it begins now and concludes after thirty days. It is not a binary event — a switch that is either on or off — but an ongoing, partially-actualized state. During the thirty-day period, the woman is not fully betrothed to man number one, but she is not unbetrothed either. The process is underway. When man number two arrives and says *me-achshav u-le'achar esrim yom*, she is not yet fully married, and so his *kiddushin* too can take hold. Just as bricks are laid in overlapping rows, each man's *kiddushin* begins before the previous one has fully concluded, and the partial availability of the woman at each moment allows the next *kiddushin* to attach.

### Partial Marriage Status

Tosafot raises what seems a natural objection: elsewhere we have established that if a man says "half of you is betrothed to me," the *kiddushin* does not take effect, because a woman cannot be half-married. How, then, can Rabbi Yochanan say that all hundred men are betrothed to her — is she not fractionally married to each?

Tosafot's answer distinguishes the two cases precisely. In the "half of you" case, the man leaves over a portion of the woman herself — he does not marry the full person. That is the defect: *kiddushin* requires taking the whole person, and if you attempt to marry only half of her, nothing takes effect. Here, by contrast, each man marries her completely — *lo sheyar be-gufa ha-ishah kelum ela gemurin kulam* — he did not leave over any part of her person; he married her in full. It is only that the marriage has not yet fully actualized, that the process is still ongoing. He left over time, not personhood. Since the remainder is temporal rather than personal, his *kiddushin* is not defective — it simply has not yet completed. And because it has not yet completed, the next man finds enough unresolved space to enter.

## Get L'achar Mita Debate

### Get After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חֲנִינָא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט, וְאִם מֵת – חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanina raises an objection** to this ruling from a *baraita*: If one says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death,** there is uncertainty whether it is a valid **bill of divorce or** whether it is **not** a valid **bill of divorce. And** therefore **if he dies she performs *ḥalitza*, but does not enter into levirate marriage.** Since she might not be divorced, the obligation of levirate marriage applies to her, but in practice she cannot perform levirate marriage in case she was in fact divorced and is thereby forbidden to her late husband's brother, a transgression which is punishable by *karet*.

The *Gemara* applies Rabbi Yochanan's framework — *kiddushin* as ongoing process — to the *get* case. The case of *me-hayom u-le'achar mita get ve-eino get*, a man on his deathbed who says "from today and after I die," produces a different result under Rabbi Yochanan's model than under Rav's or Shmuel's.

If the *get* is an ongoing process that begins now and concludes after the man dies, then by the time the process concludes — at the man's death — it arrives at a moment when the *get* cannot take effect, because a *get* must take effect while the husband is alive. The process started, but did not complete. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yochanan, there is no *safek* here at all: this is simply not a valid *get*. The woman is a widow, not a divorcée.

The halachic consequence is significant. If she is a widow, *yibum* should apply. The *Mishnah* records: *choletzes ve-lo mityabemes* — she performs *chalitza* but does not undergo *yibum*. This is fine according to Rav, whose position generates genuine *safek* — perhaps the *get* was valid, perhaps not. It is also fine according to Shmuel, who aligns with the *Rabbanan* of the *braita*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, there is no *safek* — she is definitively not divorced. Why, then, does she not simply undergo *yibum*?

### Get Models Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב – מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ, לִשְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי: הָא מַנִּי – רַבָּנַן הִיא, וַאֲנַן דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥanina explains his objection: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav,** the *baraita* **supports him,** as Rav maintains that the meaning of this expression is uncertain. **According to** the opinion of **Shmuel as well,** although he claims that the phrase is a condition rather than a retraction, he can explain as follows: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who claim in that case that it is uncertain if she is divorced, **and I,** Shmuel, **say** my ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

Abaye therefore says: yes, according to Rabbi Yochanan, she is definitively not divorced. The ongoing process did not complete before the husband died, and therefore there is no *get* at all. She is a widow and should by rights undergo *yibum* — or *chalitza*. The reason we do not permit *yibum*, and instead require *chalitza*, is not intrinsic to this case.

### Gezeira M'shum Leshon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר שִׁיּוּרָא הָוֵי, כֹּל גִּיטָּא דִּמְשַׁיַּיר בַּהּ וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא. יַבּוֹמֵי מְיַיבֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that this **is** a phrase that **leaves** room for additional betrothals, in a case where he uses this phrase with regard to a bill of divorce, this should not be the *halakha*, as there is a principle: **Any bill of divorce that leaves** a remnant of the marital bond **is not** worth **anything,** as a bill of divorce must completely sever the marital bond between the husband and the wife. If this bond is not fully severed, she is not divorced. Therefore, her late husband's brother should be allowed to **enter into levirate marriage** with her.

The reason is a *gezeira*: *gezeira mishum me-hayom im mati harei zeh get* — a protective ordinance against confusing this case with a different one. If a man says "from today *if* I die" — *im mati* — the *get* is unambiguously valid. This is a clear *tenai*: the *get* takes effect now on the condition that he dies from this illness. According to Rabbi Yochanan, this would be a complete, valid *get*, and there would be no *yibum* at all. Were we to permit *yibum* in the case of *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* — where the *get* failed because the process did not complete — people might confuse the two cases and permit *yibum* even in the *im mati* case, where she is definitively divorced and *yibum* would be *arayot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גֵּט לְהוֹצִיא, וּמִיתָה לְהוֹצִיא, מַה שֶּׁשִּׁיֵּיר גֵּט, גְּמָרַתּוּ מִיתָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? גֵּט מוֹצִיאה מֵרְשׁוּת יָבָם, מִיתָה מַכְנֶסֶת לִרְשׁוּת יָבָם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Since **a bill of divorce** serves **to remove** a wife from her husband, **and death** also serves **to remove** her and dissolve their marital ties, permitting the woman to remarry, one can say that **whatever part is left** over from that **bill of divorce** is nevertheless **completed by death,** and therefore there is a complete act of severance. **Abaye said to him: Are** the two **comparable;** can these stages be viewed as completing one another? **A bill of divorce removes her** not only from her husband's authority but also **from the authority of the *yavam*,** as a divorced woman does not perform levirate marriage, whereas **death brings her into the authority of the *yavam*,** because it renders her obligated in levirate marriage.

The *Gemara* then asks the reverse: since we require *chalitza* in the *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* case, should we not also require *chalitza* in the *me-hayom im mati* case — as a *gezeira* lest the cases be confused in the other direction? The answer: if we require *chalitza* in the *im mati* case, people will conclude that the *get* was doubtful, leading them to permit *yibum* there. And *yibum* in the *im mati* case, where the *get* is valid, would be catastrophic.

> **Hebrew/Aramար אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם טַעְמָא מַאי? גְּזֵירָה ״מִשּׁוּם מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** One must reject the above explanation, and ask **what is the reason there** for the concern about that bill of divorce, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan? It is not because the bill of divorce is in any way valid. Rather, it is a rabbinic **decree due to** the case of one who says to his wife: This is hereby your bill of divorce **from today if I die.** Everyone agrees that **this is a bill of divorce** that goes into effect from that day if he dies. Since one might confuse the two cases, the Sages decreed that if one says: This is hereby your bill of divorce from today and after my death, the bill of divorce is considered valid to the extent that the woman does not enter into levirate marriage.

The *Gemara* responds: so too with our *gezeira* — requiring *chalitza* in the *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* case might lead to *yibum*. But the *Gemara* dismisses this: *tityabbem ve-ein bekach kelum* — in the *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* case, *yibum* is in principle permitted; we merely said not to do it for reasons of confusion. If requiring *chalitza* there leads someone to do *yibum*, that is not a disaster, because *yibum* is fundamentally valid in that case. By contrast, permitting *yibum* in the *im mati* case — where she is divorced — would be genuinely catastrophic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִגְזוֹר ״מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״ דְּתַחְלוֹץ, אַטּוּ ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״! אִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת – מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. הָכָא נָמֵי אִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת – מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת! תִּתְיַיבֵּם וְאֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם, חֲשָׁשָׁא דְרַבָּנַן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let us** also issue **a decree** in a case where he said: This is hereby your bill of divorce **from today if I die,** that **she should perform *ḥalitza*, due to** its similarity to a bill of divorce that includes the stipulation: This is hereby your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death.** The Gemara answers: The Sages do not require her to perform *ḥalitza*, **as, if you say** that **she performs *ḥalitza*,** people will mistakenly think that the *yavam* performed *ḥalitza* because he did not want to marry her, and if he had wanted to **she** could have **entered into levirate marriage.** In fact, she is divorced and is forbidden to the *yavam*, and a decree of this kind could mislead people and result in a later transgression. The Gemara asks: **Here too,** in the case of where he says: This is hereby your bill of divorce from today and after my death, **if you say** that **she performs *ḥalitza*,** people will mistakenly say that **she** could have **entered into levirate marriage.** The Gemara answers: This concern is not important, as **let her perform levirate marriage and** there is **no** problem **with that.** The fact that she is treated as a divorcée and is forbidden to the brother-in-law **is** merely **a concern of the Sages,** who enacted a decree due to the similarity of the case where one says: This is hereby your bill of divorce from today if I die, whereas it is in fact not a bill of divorce and it is permitted for her to enter into levirate marriage.

The upshot is a three-way framework for *me-achshav u-le'achar sheloshim yom*: Shmuel holds it is unambiguously a *tenai*, uncertainty resolving at day thirty; Rav holds it is permanently ambiguous between "entirely now" and "entirely later"; and Rabbi Yochanan, via Ulla, holds it is an ongoing process — beginning now and concluding later — with all the attendant consequences for *kiddushin* and *get*.

## Al Menas Conditionality

### Mishnah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶתֵּן לִךְ מָאתַיִם זוּז״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְהוּא יִתֵּן. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶתֵּן לִךְ מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״, נָתַן לָהּ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me** with this *peruta* **on the condition that I will give you two hundred dinars, she is betrothed** immediately **and he shall give** her the money. If he said to her that the betrothal is: **On the condition that I will give you** a particular sum of money **from now and until thirty days,** if **he gave** the money **to her within thirty** days **she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed.**

The *Gemara* moves to the next *Mishnah*, which deals with *al menas* — conditional *kiddushin*. *Ha-omer le-ishah harei at mekudeshes li al menas she-etten lach masayim zuz* — "you are betrothed to me on the condition that I give you two hundred *zuz*." The *Mishnah* rules: *harei zo mekudeshes ve-hu yitten* — she is betrothed, and he must give. If he stipulated *al menas she-etten lach mikan ve-ad sheloshim yom* — within thirty days — then *nasan lah be-soch sheloshim yom mekudeshes, ve-im lav einah mekudeshes*. Further cases: *al menas she-yesh li masayim zuz* — on the condition I have two hundred *zuz* — she is betrothed if he does indeed have them. *Al menas she-arech masayim zuz* — on the condition I show you two hundred *zuz* — she is betrothed if he shows her, provided *ve-im her'ah lah al ha-shulchan einah mekudeshes* — if he showed her money on the table (Rashi: belonging to a money-changer, not his own), she is not betrothed, because it is evident he did not mean to show her someone else's money.

### Tenai Timing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן״. רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״ תְּנָאָה הָוֵי, מְקַיֵּים תְּנָאָה וְאָזֵיל. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן״ לְכִי יָהֵיב הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the text of this mishna: **Rav Huna says** that it reads: **And he shall give,** i.e., it is a valid betrothal from the outset, and the man is obligated to give her the promised amount. **Rav Yehuda says** that the correct version of the mishna is: **When he gives,** meaning that the betrothal takes effect only when he actually gives her the money. The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Huna says** that the phrase: **And he shall give,** indicates that this **is a condition** stipulated by the man, which means that the woman is immediately betrothed and he merely **proceeds to fulfill the condition. Rav Yehuda says** that the mishna reads: **When he gives,** which indicates that **when he will give** the money it **will be a betrothal,** but for **now at least** it **is not a betrothal.**

The operative question the *Mishnah* leaves unresolved — and which is now the subject of an *amoraic* dispute — is the precise meaning of *ve-hu yitten*. Does it mean "as of now, provided that he eventually gives" (a *tenai*, taking effect retroactively from the moment of *kiddushin*), or "when he gives" (taking effect only at the moment the money is transferred)?

Rav Huna rules: *ve-hu yitten — tenai havei, mekayem tna'eih ve-azil* — it is a *tenai*; he fulfills his condition whenever he fulfills it, but the *kiddushin* dates from now. Rav Yehuda rules: *lich'she-yitten — lich'yaiv havei kiddushin, hashta miha lo havei kiddushin* — only when he gives the money does the *kiddushin* take effect; as of now, it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְקִבְּלָה קִידּוּשִׁין מֵאַחֵר. לְרַב הוּנָא לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, לְרַב יְהוּדָה הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the explanations of Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda? Both agree that if he does not give her the money she is not betrothed. The Gemara answers: **The difference between them** is in a case **where she extended her hand and accepted betrothal from another** man during the period between the initial betrothal and the delivery of the money. **According to** the opinion of **Rav Huna** the betrothal of the second man **is not** a valid **betrothal,** as he maintains that when the first man gives her the money the betrothal takes effect retroactively from the time of his earlier statement. **According to** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** the betrothal of the second man **is** a valid **betrothal,** as the betrothal of the first man does not take effect until after he actually gives her the money.

The *nafka mina* is immediate: if she accepted *kiddushin* from a second man before the first gave the money. According to Rav Huna, she was already betrothed to man number one from the outset, so the second *kiddushin* is ineffective. According to Rav Yehuda, since no *kiddushin* yet exists, the second *kiddushin* is valid.

### Kiddushin vs. Geirushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי גַּבֵּי גִיטִּין כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְהִיא תִּתֵּן. אִיתְּמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהִיא תִּתֵּן״. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁתִּתֵּן״. רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״וְהִיא תִּתֵּן״, תְּנָאָה הָוֵי, מְקַיְּימָא תְּנָאָה וְאָזְלָה. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״לִכְשֶׁתִּתֵּן״, לְכִי יָהֲבָה לֵיהּ הוּא דְּהָוֵי גֵּט, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא הָוֵי גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And we also learned** a mishna **concerning bills of divorce like this case** (*Gittin* 74a): With regard to **one who says to a woman: This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, she is divorced and she shall give** him the two hundred dinars. **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disputed the correct version of this statement. **Rav Huna says: And she shall give; Rav Yehuda says: When she gives.** The reasons are as stated above in the discussion about betrothal: **Rav Huna says: And she shall give,** as **it is a condition,** and therefore **she proceeds to fulfill the condition. Rav Yehuda says: When she gives,** as **when she will give** the money **to him it will be** a valid **bill of divorce,** but **now, in any event,** it **is not** a valid **bill of divorce.**

The parallel *Mishnah* in *Gittin* presents the same question in the context of divorce. A man says: *harei zeh gittech al menas she-titni li masayim zuz* — "here is your *get*, on the condition you give me two hundred *zuz*." The *Mishnah* rules: *harei zo megureshes ve-hi titten* — she is divorced as long as she gives the money. Rav Huna applies the same reasoning: *ve-hi titten — tenai havei*, it is a *tenai*, and upon fulfilling the *tenai* she is retroactively divorced from when the *get* was given. Rav Yehuda says: *lich'she-titten* — only when she gives.

### Maasah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שֶׁנִּתְקָרַע הַגֵּט אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד, לְרַב הוּנָא הָוֵי גֵּט, לְרַב יְהוּדָה לָא הָוֵי גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the difference **between** the explanations of Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda? Even Rav Huna agrees that she cannot marry someone else until she gives him the money. Until that point the bill of divorce does not come into effect and she remains married to the first man. The Gemara answers: **The difference between them** is in a case **where the bill of divorce was torn or lost** after she received it but before she gave him the money. **According to** the opinion of **Rav Huna it is** a valid **bill of divorce,** as the divorce takes effect from the moment he gives her the document. **According to** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda it is not a** valid **bill of divorce,** as he maintains that the bill of divorce goes into effect only later, which is impossible as it is either torn or lost.

The *nafka mina* in the *get* case is the *Gemara*'s focus: the *get* is torn or lost before she gives the money. According to Rav Huna, this is no problem — the *tenai* can be fulfilled later, and the effect retroacts; the *get* need not be physically present at the moment of fulfillment. According to Rav Yehuda, since the *kiddushin* or *get* only takes effect at the moment of the condition's fulfillment, the operative instrument — money or document — must still exist at that moment. A *get* that has been destroyed cannot take effect even if the condition is later satisfied.

### Necessity of Two Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּלְקָרוֹבַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֲבָל גֵּירוּשִׁין, דִּלְרַחוֹקַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it is necessary** to state both disputes, the one with regard to betrothal and the one with regard to divorce, as neither *halakha* could be derived from the other. **As had** it **taught us** only that the two Sages disagree **with regard to betrothal,** one might have said that it is **there** that **Rav Huna says** that the betrothal takes effect immediately, despite the fact that he must still give her the money, **because** betrothal **comes to draw her near** to him. Therefore, it is likely that he meant for the betrothal to take immediate effect. **But** with regard to **divorce, when he comes to distance her,** you might **say** that Rav Huna **concedes to Rav Yehuda** that the divorce comes into effect only from the moment she gives him the money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָךְ: בְּהָךְ קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיהוּ לָא כְּסִיף לֵיהּ לְמִתְבְּעַהּ, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִיהִי כְּסִיפָא לַהּ לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the dispute had been **stated** only **with regard to this** case concerning a bill of divorce, one might have claimed that **Rav Huna says** that the bill of divorce comes into immediate effect only **in this** situation, **because he is not ashamed to demand** the money **from her** whenever he wants, and therefore he divorces her immediately. **But here,** concerning a betrothal, **when she is ashamed to claim** the money **from him,** you might **say that** Rav Huna **concedes to Rav Yehuda** that the woman does not accept the betrothal until she actually receives the money. It is therefore **necessary** for the dispute to be stated in both cases.

The *Gemara* notes that both cases must be stated independently, because there are contextual reasons to presume different intentions in each. In *kiddushin*, the man is seeking to bring the woman closer, which suggests eagerness to have the *kiddushin* take effect immediately — favoring Rav Huna's reading. But one might argue that the woman's reluctance to ask for money she was promised (*de-ichasfa lah le-mitbahyei*) means she would insist the *kiddushin* not take effect until payment is received — perhaps favoring Rav Yehuda. In *gittin*, the man is distancing himself and might prefer a delayed effect; but one might argue that he is not embarrassed to demand payment (*de-ihu lo kasif lei le-mitbah*) and therefore does not need to hold the *get*'s effect hostage to receipt of funds, again favoring Rav Huna's reading. Each case presents independent considerations, and therefore each must be stated.

## Al Menas Proof Texts

### Get Validity Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּתְקָרַע הַגֵּט אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, לְאַחֵר לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא עַד שֶׁתִּתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Yehuda from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Gittin* 5:5): If one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, even if the bill of divorce was torn or lost she is divorced.** But **she may not marry another** man **until she gives** him the money. This ruling apparently accords with Rav Huna's opinion that the bill of divorce goes into immediate effect from the moment it is given.

The *Gemara* now marshals decisive evidence in favor of Rav Huna. The first *braita*: a man says *harei zeh gittech al menas she-titni li masayim zuz* — even if the *get* is torn or lost, *harei zo megureshes* — she is divorced, as long as she gives the money. She should not, however, remarry until she has done so — not because she is not yet divorced, but because she must ensure she can satisfy the *tenai*. This *braita* clearly treats the *get* as taking effect from the moment of giving, with the condition functioning retroactively — precisely Rav Huna's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: אָמַר לָהּ ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״ וָמֵת, נָתְנָה – אֵין זְקוּקָה לַיָּבָם, לֹא נָתְנָה – זְקוּקָה לְיָבָם. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֶנֶת לְאָחִיו אוֹ לְאָבִיו אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַקְּרוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is further taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a man who **said to** his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, and he died** childless, if **she gave** him the money **she is not bound** by a levirate bond **to the *yavam*,** as she is already divorced. But if **she did not give** him the money **she is bound** by a levirate bond **to the *yavam*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If she did not give it to her husband during his lifetime **she** may **give** the money **to** her late husband's **brother, or to his father, or to one of the relatives** who inherit from him, and by doing so she fulfills the condition and is retroactively divorced.

A second *braita* reinforces this: a man gives his wife a *get al menas she-titni li masayim zuz* and then dies. *Natna ein zekuka le-yavam* — if she gave the money and fulfilled the *tenai*, she is divorced and not subject to *yibum*. *Lo natna zekuka le-yavam* — if she did not give it, the *tenai* was not fulfilled and she is a widow subject to *yibum*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: she may give the money to his brother, his father, or another relative — the *tenai* can be satisfied even post-mortem, by giving to heirs. The debate between *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is only whether *li* — "to me" — includes heirs. But both agree that the *tenai* can be satisfied after the man's death, and the *get* then takes effect retroactively to when it was given. This is a clear proof that *al menas* functions as a *tenai* that retroacts — *dkulei alma miha tnai havei*.

### Consensus on Al Menas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: ״לִי וְלֹא לְיוֹרְשַׁיי״, וּמָר סָבַר: ״אֲפִילּוּ לְיוֹרְשַׁיי״, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא תְּנָאָה הָוֵי, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this ruling: The *tanna'im* **disagree only with regard to** the following: One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that by the phrase: On the condition that you will give **me,** he meant: To me specifically **but not to my heirs.** Consequently, if she does not give the money to him she is not divorced, as she cannot fulfill the condition by giving the money to his heirs. **And** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that the husband meant: **Even to my heirs. In any event, everyone agrees** that **it is a condition** and the divorce takes effect from the moment the bill of divorce is given. This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Yehuda.**

These two *braitas* constitute a *tiuvta* — a decisive contradiction — to Rav Yehuda.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַב יְהוּדָה: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ. וַאֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Yehuda** could **say to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Anyone who states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** is **like** one who **states** that the agreement will take effect retroactively **from now,** even though the condition is fulfilled only later on. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that the phrase: On the condition, indicates that an action should take effect now provided that a certain action is fulfilled later. **And the Rabbis disagree with him,** claiming that this expression does not mean that the action should take immediate effect, but only from when the condition is fulfilled. And Rav Yehuda would conclude by adding: **And I spoke** in accordance **with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rav Yehuda deflects by arguing that both *braitas* may reflect only the position of Rabbi, who holds *kol ha-omer al menas ke-omer me-achshav dami* — whoever says "on the condition" is as if he said "as of now." This is Rav Huna's position, attributed here to Rabbi. Perhaps, Rav Yehuda claims, the *Rabbanan* dispute Rabbi and hold that *al menas* means *le-achar zman*, and he rules like the *Rabbanan*.

### Universal Agreement on Al Menas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בְּבָבֶל הֲוָה אָמְרִינַן: הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי, פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that **anyone who states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** is **like** one who **states** that the agreement will take effect retroactively **from now,** even though the condition is fulfilled only later on. **Rabbi Zeira said: When we were in Babylonia we would say:** With regard to **that** which **Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Anyone who states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** is **like** one who **states** that the agreement will take effect retroactively **from now,** that is Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, but **the Rabbis disagree with him.**

Rav Zeira then reports a tradition that resolves the matter entirely. When he was in Bavel, he had understood this to be a dispute between Rabbi and the *Rabbanan* — Rabbi holding *me-achshav*, the *Rabbanan* disputing. But when he made *aliyah* to Eretz Yisrael, he found Rabbi Assi sitting and teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סָלְקִי לְהָתָם אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַסִּי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי, לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **When I ascended to there,** to Eretz Yisrael, **I found Rabbi Asi sitting and saying** the following ruling **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: All concede** that **with regard to one who says** to his wife: **On the condition,** he is **like** one who **states** that the divorce will take effect retroactively **from now. They disagreed only** with regard to one who said to his wife: **From today and after** my **death,** whether he is considered to have added a condition or to have retracted from his initial statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט, דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* that if one says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death,** it is uncertain whether it is a valid **bill of divorce or** whether it is **not** a valid **bill of divorce.** This is **the statement of the Rabbis.** The Rabbis are uncertain whether he has changed his mind from his initial intention of giving the document that day, and now wishes to give a bill of divorce after his death, which is not effective, or whether he was merely adding a condition that the bill of divorce should take effect from today only when he dies. Conversely, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** A document **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce.**

The *Gemara* records a definitive contradiction to Reish Lakish — *tiuvta de-Reish Lakish tiuvta* — demonstrating that a *shaliach* appointment can indeed be verbally revoked. And then the *Gemara* goes further: *ve-hilchesa ke-Rabbi Yochanan ve-afilu be-kameita* — we rule like Rabbi Yochanan even in the first version of the dispute, even where money was physically given with the effect designated to take place thirty days later. Even that can be verbally voided before it takes effect.

The *Gemara* anticipates the objection: *shani nesinas mammon le-yad ha-ishah de-kama'aseh dami* — the actual giving of money into a woman's hands is like a real act. Nevertheless, the ruling stands: *afilu hachi ati dibbur u-mevattel dibbur*. As long as the *kiddushin* has not yet taken effect, words can void it. **And even though there is** room **to say** that **giving money to a woman** for betrothal **is different, as it is considered like an action, even so, a statement comes and nullifies** a previous **statement** with regard to her consent to the betrothal, provided that the betrothal has yet to take effect.

### Dispute Scope Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר בְּ״עַל מְנָת״ נָמֵי פְּלִיגִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בְּ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – נִיפְלְגֵי בְּ״עַל מְנָת״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, who said** that the *tanna'im* **also disagree with regard to** one who says to his wife: **On the condition,** instead **of disagreeing with regard to** the case where one says to his wife: **From today and after** my **death, let** them **disagree with regard to** a case where the husband used the expression: **On the condition.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחַ דְּרַבִּי, דְּ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ נָמֵי הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט. וְנִיפְלְגִי בְּ״עַל מְנָת״, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחַ דְּרַבָּנַן! כֹּחַ דְּהֶיתֵּירָא עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Yehuda maintains that they disagree over both cases, and the *baraita* taught the dispute in this manner **to convey to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who maintains that even if the husband says to his wife: **From today and after** my **death, this is also** a valid **bill of divorce.** The Gemara asks: **And let them disagree with regard to** a husband who says to his wife: **On the condition, to convey to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who hold that it is not a valid bill of divorce even in that case. The Gemara answers: It is **preferable** to emphasize **the power of leniency.** It is always better to formulate a dispute in a manner that emphasizes the extent of the lenient opinion.

The only genuine dispute, Rabbi Yochanan reports, is the case of *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* — and there, the debate is recorded in the *braita*: *get ve-eino get divrei Chachamim; Rabbi omer kazeh get*. That ambiguous construction — "from today and after my death" — is genuinely disputed. *Al menas* is not.

The *Gemara* then addresses Rav Yehuda's position directly: if the *Rabbanan* dispute Rabbi even regarding *al menas*, why does the *braita* record their debate specifically around *me-hayom u-le'achar mita*? Why not state the dispute regarding *al menas* and let us infer from there? The answer is *koach d-hetera adif* — it is preferable to demonstrate the force of the permissive ruling. Rabbi's position is strongest precisely in the hard case — *me-hayom u-le'achar mita*, where the language seems to push toward a later effect — and it is *koach d-hetera* to show that even there, Rabbi considers it a valid *get*. Conversely, demonstrating the force of the *Rabbanan*'s restrictive view in the easy case of *al menas* would serve no such purpose.

The upshot is clear: *al menas* universally means *me-achshav*. Nobody disputes this. The only case in which there is a genuine *tannaitic* dispute about whether a designation means now or later is *me-hayom u-le'achar mita* — the ambiguous formulation that stands at the center of all the debates traversed in this *daf*.
