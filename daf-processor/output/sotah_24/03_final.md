# Sotah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arusa & Shomeret Yavam

### New Perek Overview

With *daf* 24, we arrive at a new *perek*. Having completed the step-by-step description of the *sotah* procedure in the previous chapters, the *Mishnah* now returns to the more characteristically Talmudic mode of analysis: identifying categories, determining who qualifies and who does not, and working through the criteria that govern the *sotah* process. This is the kind of sustained halakhic analysis more familiar from tractates like *Kodashim*, where the procedure has already been described and the focus shifts to the underlying rules.

Before proceeding, it is worth noting a common misconception about the *sotah*: neither the *Torah*, the *Mishnah*, nor the *Gemara* actually states that she dies. The single dramatic line — "remove her lest she make the *Azarah tamei*" — is interpreted by the *Gemara* to mean that she may be about to become a *niddah*. The popular imagination has embellished this considerably beyond what the sources actually say.

### Arusa Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲרוּסָה וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם — לֹא שׁוֹתוֹת וְלֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׂטֶה אִשָּׁה תַּחַת אִישָׁהּ״, פְּרָט לַאֲרוּסָה וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם.
>
> **Translation:** **A betrothed woman** who secluded herself with another man after being warned by her betrothed, **and a widow waiting for her brother-in-law [*yavam*]** to perform levirate marriage who secluded herself with another man after being warned by her *yavam*, **they neither drink** the bitter water **nor collect** payment of their **marriage contracts.** The reason they are not entitled to payment of their marriage contracts is that the betrothed woman became forbidden to her betrothed or the widow became forbidden to her *yavam* due to her own actions of entering into seclusion with the paramour. And the fact that they do not drink the bitter water is **as it is stated:** "This is the law of jealousy, **when a wife, while under her husband, goes astray,** and is defiled" (Numbers 5:29). The verse **excludes a betrothed woman and a widow awaiting her *yavam*;** since they are not yet married, neither is considered as "under her husband."

The *arusa* is a woman who has accepted *kiddushin* — the ring or its equivalent — from her husband, rendering her fully married in law. If she commits adultery, it is a genuine act of adultery: the children would be *mamzerim*, she would be forbidden to her husband, and all attendant legal consequences apply. The sole distinction between *eirusin* and *nisuin* is that the couple has not yet begun a shared life together. They are not living together, they are not intimate, and the reciprocal obligations of married life have not yet commenced. She remains, in that sense, a marriage on paper — complete in its legal weight, but not yet actualized in lived reality.

### Shomeret Yavam

The *shomeret yavam* is the woman whose husband has died childless, leaving her bound by the tie of *zika* — a quasi-marital connection — to her deceased husband's brother, the *yavam*. She awaits either *yibbum* or *chalitzah*. She is prohibited by an *issur lav* from marrying or sleeping with any other man. Although this bond is not a full marriage, it is understood as a continuation, in potential form, of the marriage with the deceased brother, now existing in a kind of anticipatory relationship with the living brother.

### Cannot Drink/Ktuba

In both cases, if *kinui* was issued and the woman subsequently entered seclusion, she cannot be compelled to drink, and she does not collect her *ktuba*. The logic runs as follows: she becomes forbidden to her husband as a *sotah* — the *Mishnah* treats this as implicit — but since the process cannot be completed, the only resolution is divorce without *ktuba*. The general principle is that when the inability to complete the procedure is attributable to the wife, she forfeits her *ktuba*; when it is attributable to the husband, she collects it. In these cases, even though no one is formally at fault — she simply is an *arusa* or *shomeret yavam* — she is nonetheless considered responsible, because she need not have entered seclusion with another man after her husband warned her. She made that choice, and the consequence follows.

A question was raised about the language of the *Mishnah*: why does it say *lo notlot ktubatam* rather than the more familiar formula *v'teitzei blo ktuba* — "she goes out without a *ktuba*"? The answer lies in structure. The *Mishnah* is about to present cases where women *do* collect their *ktuba* despite not drinking — *lo shotot v'notlot*. The parallel formulation *lo shotot v'lo notlot* sets up that contrast neatly, and the stylistic symmetry justifies the phrasing.

### Rashi-Tosafot Dispute

The question of the *shomeret yavam* is particularly rich. *Rashi* explains that even if she were not halakhically forbidden to the *yavam* by virtue of her suspected conduct, the brother would still be entitled to declare that he refuses to marry such a woman. That refusal alone is sufficient to dissolve the *zika* without her collecting a *ktuba*.

*Tosafot* objects. The rule established earlier in the tractate is that if the act itself — had it actually occurred — would not have rendered her forbidden, then the suspicion of having done it cannot render her forbidden either. It would be strange if the *sotah* process made her status worse than actual proven misconduct would. *Tosafot* therefore resolves the matter by working from the premise that a *shomeret yavam* who sleeps with another man would in fact be forbidden to the *yavam*. On that premise, the *sotah* process is coherent: since proven infidelity would forbid her, suspected infidelity similarly triggers the *sotah* status.

A further question was posed: if there are multiple brothers, who among them is entitled to issue *kinui*? And if one brother does so and she becomes forbidden on account of his *kinui*, does she remain permissible to the other brothers? This is a genuinely difficult question that does not appear to have been resolved in the discussion.

### Textual Derivation

The textual basis for excluding the *arusa* and *shomeret yavam* from drinking comes from the phrase *asher tisteh ishah tachat ishah* — "that a woman strays from her husband" — where *ishah* denotes a complete husband, not merely an *arus* or a *yavam*. This excludes cases where the marriage is not yet full. The *Gemara* will explain how the beginning of the *parasha* — the phrase *daber el Bnei Yisrael v'amarta* — is interpreted to include the *arusa* and *shomeret yavam* within the ambit of *kinui* even while they are excluded from the drinking. The two phrases thus operate on two different parts of the process.

## Forbidden Marriages

### Widow/Divorcee Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין — לֹא שׁוֹתוֹת וְלֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A widow** who was married **to a High Priest,** or **a divorcée or *ḥalutza*** who was married **to a common priest,** or **a *mamzeret* or Gibeonite woman** who was married **to a Jew** of unflawed lineage, **or a Jewish woman** of unflawed lineage who was married **to a *mamzer* or a Gibeonite,** all of these women **neither drink** the bitter water **nor collect** payment of their **marriage contracts,** as the *sota* ritual applies only to permitted marriages.

The *Mishnah* turns to a different category: women in marriages that, while legally binding, are in violation of a negative prohibition. If a prohibition carries the weight of *issur kareit* — incest, for example — the marriage is not binding at all. But if the prohibition is merely an *issur lav*, the marriage takes legal effect even though it transgresses the law. The question is whether such a husband may charge his wife as a *sotah* and bring her through the procedure, when one might argue that the entire marriage ought to be dissolved regardless.

The examples given are: a *kohen gadol* married to a widow (*almanah*); a divorced woman or a *chalutza* married to an ordinary *kohen* (*kohen hedyot*) — with the note that the *Bavli* tends to treat the prohibition of a *chalutza* marrying a *kohen* as rabbinic rather than biblical; a *mamzeret* or *netina* married to a regular *Yisrael*; and conversely, a regular Jewish woman married to a *mamzer* or *netin*.

### Mamzer/Netin Cases

These cases all share the characteristic that the marriage involves a violation of a negative prohibition touching on personal status. The specific prohibition varies — in some cases it concerns priestly restrictions (*issurei kehunah*), in others it concerns lineage (*mamzer*, *netin*). In all of them, however, the marriage is technically valid even as it transgresses the law.

### Cannot Drink Rule

The ruling in all such cases is *lo shotot v'lo notlot ktubatam*: they do not drink and they do not collect the *ktuba*. The reason they do not drink is that the marriage is not recognized as a legitimate *ishah* — not a marriage that should remain in place. Since the whole framework of the *sotah* process presupposes a valid marriage worth protecting, these marriages fall outside that framework. And since the women themselves are held responsible for having entered seclusion after being warned, they forfeit their *ktuba* as well.

## Process Failures

### Confession/Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ לֹא שׁוֹתוֹת וְלֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה: הָאוֹמֶרֶת ״טְמֵאָה אֲנִי״, וְשֶׁבָּאוּ לָהּ עֵדִים שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה, וְהָאוֹמֶרֶת ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the following** women **neither drink** the bitter water **nor collect** payment of their **marriage contracts:** A woman **who** confesses and **says: I am defiled, and** a woman **with regard to whom witnesses came** and testified **that she is defiled, and** a woman **who says: I will not drink** the bitter water, even if she does not confess her guilt.

The *Mishnah* now shifts to a different class of cases — not those where the nature of the marriage disqualifies the woman, but those where the *sotah* process breaks down for other reasons. The list opens: if a woman confesses her guilt, or if witnesses testify that she committed adultery, there is nothing left to establish through drinking. The process becomes superfluous. In such cases, she does not drink and does not collect her *ktuba* — the outcome is determined by evidence rather than by the ordeal.

### Woman Refuses

If the woman simply refuses to drink, she is likewise forbidden without a *ktuba*. She is the obstacle. Even though she has not confessed and there are no witnesses, her refusal creates a situation that cannot be resolved in her favor, and since the inability to complete the process is attributable to her, she bears the consequence.

### Husband Default

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר בַּעְלָהּ ״אֵינִי מַשְׁקָהּ״, וְשֶׁבַּעְלָהּ בָּא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ — נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּה וְלֹא שׁוֹתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** However, a woman whose **husband said: I will not have** her **drink, and** a woman **whose husband engaged in sexual intercourse with her on the way** to the Temple, **collect** payment of their **marriage contracts** even though **they do not drink** the bitter water, as it is due to the husbands that they do not drink.

The cases shift when the fault lies with the husband. If the husband declares *eini mashkeh* — "I will not make her drink" — and withdraws from the process, or if the husband has relations with her after she has become a *sotah* and before the process was completed (which he is prohibited from doing, and which renders the *mei sotah* ineffective in any case), then the ruling changes: *notlot ktuba v'lo shosot*. She collects her *ktuba* even though she cannot drink. He is the responsible party. Notably, she remains forbidden to him in her status as *sotah*, since there is no mechanism to resolve the suspicion.

### Husband Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתוּ בַּעְלֵיהֶן עַד שֶׁלֹּא שָׁתוּ, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה וְלֹא שׁוֹתוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ שׁוֹתוֹת אוֹ לֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** If **the husbands** of *sota* women **died before** their wives **drank** the bitter water, **Beit Shammai say: They collect** payment of their **marriage contracts and they do not drink** the bitter water. **And Beit Hillel say: They either drink** the bitter water **or they do not collect** payment of **their marriage contracts.**

The most complex case in this section is where the husband dies before the process is completed — an act of God, without fault on either side. Here *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* disagree. *Beit Shammai* holds that she collects her *ktuba* and does not drink; *Beit Hillel* holds *o shotot o lo notlot ktubasan* — either drink or forfeit the *ktuba*, and since she cannot drink, she does not collect. The underlying question is how to allocate the consequences when no one is at fault. *Beit Shammai* appears willing to attribute the problem to the husband, since he is the one who died, even without moral fault; *Beit Hillel* takes the position that the inability to complete the process, absent a responsible party, falls on the wife.

## Forbidden Marriages (continued)

### Nursing/Pregnant Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וּמְנִיקַת חֲבֵירוֹ — לֹא שׁוֹתוֹת וְלֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָכוֹל הוּא לְהַפְרִישָׁהּ וּלְהַחְזִירָהּ לְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman who was pregnant** with the child **of another** man at the time of her marriage **and a woman who was nursing** the child **of another** man at the time of her marriage **neither drink** the bitter water **nor collect** payment of their **marriage contracts.** This is because by rabbinic law they may not marry for twenty-four months after the baby's birth, and therefore these also constitute prohibited marriages. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: He can separate** from **her, and remarry her after** the **time** of twenty-four months has elapsed, and therefore these are considered permitted marriages, and the women can drink the bitter water.

A further category is introduced: *u'uveres chavairta u'meinekes chavairta* — a woman who is pregnant with another man's child, or nursing another man's child, and whose husband married her during that period. The prohibition here is not about personal status or a violation of Torah law; it is rabbinic in origin and rooted in a specific concern. If a man marries a woman who is nursing an infant from her deceased first husband, the concern is that the new husband — for whom the child is not his — will not weigh the infant's welfare appropriately. He may engage in marital relations, she may become pregnant, and the demands of a new pregnancy and new infant will compromise her ability to nurse and care for the existing child. The *Gemara* here reflects a recognition that having children in close succession can be harmful to an infant, particularly when the decision is made by a party without skin in the game.

This emerges into a law: a man should not marry a nursing or pregnant woman until the child has passed the two-year nursing period. The *Gemara*'s acknowledgment of this concern, while framed as a prohibition rather than a permission to use contraception, resonates with the practice documented in the name of *Rav Yosef Eliyahu Henkin*, who would regularly grant *heterim* for women to use contraception for two or more years after giving birth, in order to allow focused care for the existing child.

If such a man then issued *kinui* and seclusion followed, is this treated like the cases of prohibited marriages — where the couple should not remain married and therefore the full *sotah* process cannot proceed — or is it different?

### Meir vs Sages

*Rabbi Meir* applies the same principle as in the cases of *issurei kehunah*: since they should not be married right now, the *sotah* procedure cannot be pursued. She neither drinks nor collects her *ktuba*.

The *Chachamim* disagree. They distinguish this case from the earlier prohibited marriages on a crucial ground: in this case, the man is not required to divorce his wife. He could live separately from her during the two-year period and then resume the marriage. Since we would not demand a divorce, the marriage retains its legitimacy, and therefore the *sotah* procedure may be carried out. *Rabbi Meir* applies the principle broadly — any reason they should not be married currently disqualifies the *sotah* process. The *Chachamim* apply it narrowly — only where the marriage is so defective that divorce is required does the process become unavailable.

## Infertile Women

### Infertile Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְילוֹנִית וּזְקֵינָה, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לֵילֵד — לֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָה וְלֹא שׁוֹתוֹת. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל הוּא לִישָּׂא אִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת וְלִפְרוֹת וְלִרְבּוֹת הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **A sexually underdeveloped woman** who is incapable of bearing children **[*ailonit*], and an elderly woman, and** a woman **who is incapable of giving birth** for other reasons, **neither collect** payment of their **marriage contracts nor drink** the bitter water, as marrying a woman who cannot give birth constitutes a violation of the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply. **Rabbi Elazar says: He can marry another woman and procreate through her;** therefore, these are considered permitted marriages, and women in these categories can drink the bitter water.

*Aylonit v'zkeinah u'she'eina re'uya l'yeled* — an *aylonit*, an elderly woman, and a woman who cannot bear children — these are the next cases considered. The *aylonit* is a woman with markedly masculine characteristics who is considered not only infertile but physically distinct in ways the *Gemara* associates with that condition. The *zkeinah* is simply an older woman past childbearing age. *She'eina re'uya l'yeled* — *Rashi* understands this as a woman who drank a sterilizing potion; *Tosafot* offers a different explanation, and also asks why the *Mishnah* does not simply use the standard term *akara* (barren woman), which the Torah uses for classic infertility.

One might ask: why does infertility raise a question about the *sotah* process at all? The issue is not the verse *v'nikta v'nizra zara* — that she will be purified and bear seed — though that might be a passing thought. The concern parallels the earlier cases: the man has a *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu*, an obligation to have children. If his wife cannot bear children, perhaps the marriage is one that should not continue, and therefore falls outside the framework of a legitimate *ishah* that the *sotah* process presupposes. This question arises even in a marriage of fifty years — he could theoretically always take an additional wife and thus fulfill his obligation, but the concern is whether this marriage, as it stands, is one the *Torah* views as a *sotah*-eligible union.

### Cannot Drink/Ktuba

The *Mishnah*'s ruling: *lo notlet ktuba v'lo shotah* — she does not collect her *ktuba* and does not drink.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

*Rabbi Eliezer* dissents: *yachol hu v'yissa isha acheret v'yifros v'ya'amod im zu* — he can simply marry another woman and thereby fulfill his obligation of *pru u'revu*, while remaining married to his current wife. On this view, there is no reason to dissolve the marriage or to bar the *sotah* process, since the man has an available remedy that does not require divorcing his wife. Intuitively, *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position has considerable force: there is nothing inherently wrong with this marriage, particularly in a halakhic framework that permits polygamy. If his *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* can be satisfied through a second marriage, the first marriage need not be undone, and the *sotah* process should apply normally.

## Mixed Cases

### Arayot Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל יְדֵי כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת מְקַנִּין, חוּץ מִן הַקָּטָן וּמִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** A husband **can issue a warning** to his wife forbidding her to seclude herself with any man, even **with regard to all those** men **with whom relations are forbidden,** e.g., her father or brother, with the **exception of a minor and of one who is not a man,** i.e., in a situation where a man suspects his wife of bestiality.

*Al yedai kol ha'arayot mikanyin* — the *kinui* process applies even when the woman is suspected not merely of adultery but of another forbidden sexual relationship. One might have supposed that when the suspicion involves an entirely separate category of prohibition — sleeping with a close relative, for instance — the case should be analyzed through that other framework rather than through the *sotah* framework. The *chiddush* here is that the *sotah* process is not dislodged by the presence of a concurrent prohibition. The *kinui* and the *sotah* status remain operative, and if she undergoes the ordeal and is cleared, this clears up both the adultery question and the *arayot* question simultaneously.

### Minor/Non-Man

The exception: a woman cannot be made a *sotah* based on suspicion of having been with a minor (*katan*) or with someone who is not a man (*mi she'eino ish*). The phrase *mi she'eino ish* is deliberately opaque, and the *Gemara* will address it on a subsequent *daf*.

The question of the *katan* is not about physical capacity but about legal significance. The act, even if it occurred, would not carry sufficient legal weight to create *sotah* status. A question was raised about a non-Jew: would suspicion of sleeping with a non-Jew similarly disqualify the *sotah* process? The suggestion that *mi she'eino ish* might encompass a non-Jew was floated, though this was not resolved. Both the *katan* and the potential non-Jew share the characteristic of not being *tofes kiddushin* — not being able to effect a legally binding betrothal — and that may be the relevant common denominator, though this remains to be seen in the *Gemara*.

## Beit Din Authority

### Cases for Intervention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁבֵּית דִּין מְקַנִּין לָהֶן: מִי שֶׁנִּתְחָרֵשׁ בַּעְלָהּ, אוֹ נִשְׁתַּטָּה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין. לֹא לְהַשְׁקוֹתָהּ אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא לְפוֹסְלָהּ מִכְּתוּבָּתָהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף לְהַשְׁקוֹתָהּ. לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא בַּעְלָהּ מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין יַשְׁקֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** are the women **to whom the court issues a warning** in place of their husbands: **One whose husband became a deaf-mute or became an imbecile, or was incarcerated in prison.** The Sages **said** that the court warns her **not** in order **to have her drink** the bitter water if she disobeys the warning, **but** in order **to disqualify her from** receiving payment of **her marriage contract. Rabbi Yosei says:** The court's warning **also** serves **to have her drink,** and **when her husband is released from prison he has her drink.**

*V'elu she'beis din mikanyin lahen* — the following women, whose husbands are not in a position to issue *kinui*, will have *beit din* step in on the husband's behalf. The cases are: the husband became a *cheresh* (deaf-mute, who lacks full legal competence); the husband became a *shoteh* (mentally ill, not in the sense of being demented but of having a mental condition that disrupts his ability to function legally); or the husband is *chavush b'veit ha'asurim* — imprisoned and unable to monitor or respond to his wife's conduct.

This raises an important conceptual question. Earlier in the tractate, there was a debate about whether it is *assur l'kanos* — forbidden for a husband to issue *kinui* — or *mutar*, or even a *chiyuv*. If it is *assur l'kanos* when the husband acts out of his own jealousy and subjective passion, perhaps when a third party — *beit din*, an objective body with no personal stake — observes concerning behavior, they bear a responsibility to address it that the emotionally invested husband does not.

### First Opinion

The first opinion sharply limits what *beit din* can accomplish: *lo lehashkotah amru ela lefoslah miktuvatah* — the *Sages* did not say that *beit din* can represent the husband to the point of compelling her to drink. All they can do is disqualify her from collecting her *ktuba*. The immediate consequence of *beit din*'s intervention, therefore, is that the situation moves toward divorce without *ktuba* — since there is no mechanism to complete the *sotah* process when the husband is incapacitated, and the *sotah* status cannot be resolved, the only exit is dissolution of the marriage.

This raises a searching question about what has actually been achieved. If the woman knows that the only possible outcome is divorce without *ktuba*, has her behavior been deterred? It seems unlikely. Perhaps the purpose is not deterrence of her current conduct but protection of his interests — ensuring that when and if he returns to capacity, he will not be required to pay a *ktuba* to a wife who behaved this way in his absence. But even that framing is not fully satisfying, since his financial interests seem an insufficient reason for *beit din* to intervene as actively as it does.

Another possibility is that *beit din* is acting on halakhic grounds: they believe this marriage should end, not merely for his financial protection, but because they suspect adultery has occurred and they do not want the couple to remain married in such circumstances. On this reading, *beit din* is acting to achieve a halakhic result — dissolution of a marriage they believe should not continue — and the *ktuba* consequence is a tool toward that end.

A further wrinkle: in the cases of *cheresh* and *shoteh*, a *get* cannot be arranged even from a distance, since the husband lacks legal competence to issue one. Only in the case of the imprisoned husband could a *get* potentially be arranged from prison. So what *beit din* has accomplished in the *cheresh* and *shoteh* cases is, in effect, a kind of conditional verdict — setting the stage so that if the husband ever recovers capacity, divorce without *ktuba* is the pre-determined result.

### Rabbi Yosi

*Rabbi Yosi* goes further: *af lehashkotah* — *beit din* is empowered to authorize her actually drinking, *liksheyeitzei ba'alah mibeit ha'asurim yashkenah* — when the husband is released from prison, he can make her drink on the strength of *beit din*'s prior *kinui*. This is a significant expansion. On *Rabbi Yosi*'s view, the *kinui* issued by *beit din* has full legal force, and the process is merely suspended — not foreclosed — until the husband regains the ability to bring it to completion. In the interim, the woman is in a kind of *agunah* state, unable to move forward, but the path to resolution remains open. The husband, upon release, could choose either to complete the *sotah* process or, if the *sotah* process vindicates her, to resume the marriage — he need not necessarily divorce her.

### Philosophy/Analysis

The conceptual architecture here is striking. The *Mishnah* has already shown, across several categories, that *sotah* status can be created even in cases where the process can never be completed — where from the very outset there is no possible *sotah* outcome. The *Torah* permits this intermediate status, even without any path to resolution, apparently because the suspicion and the conduct that created it are themselves legally significant, independent of whether the ordeal can be performed. *Beit din*'s intervention extends this logic to cases of an absent or incapacitated husband, authorizing the creation of that status through a surrogate.

The question of whose interests are being protected is not straightforwardly answered. If it is about deterrence, it is unlikely to deter, because once the *sotah* status is established and no resolution is possible, the woman has nothing further to lose. If it is about his financial interests, the magnitude of *beit din*'s involvement seems disproportionate to a monetary concern. The most coherent account may be halakhic: *beit din* acts because it views the continuation of this marriage under these circumstances as a problem the law demands they address, and stripping the *ktuba* is the mechanism available to them in his absence. And as a secondary consideration, there is also the deterrent function for future cases — if women know that their conduct during a husband's incapacity will be met with a consequence, even a delayed or partial one, that knowledge may shape behavior.

## Kinui Derivation

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִישְׁתָּא הוּא דְּלָא שָׁתְיָיא, הָא קַנּוֹיֵ[י] מְקַנֵּי לַהּ: מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָמַרְתָּ״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֲרוּסָה וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְקִינּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that a betrothed woman and a widow awaiting her *yavam* do not drink the bitter water. The Gemara infers: **She does not drink, but** the husband or *yavam* **can warn her** against secluding herself with another man, and if she violates his warning, she is forbidden to him. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: This is derived **as the Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"Speak to the children of Israel and say** unto them: If the wife of any man goes astray, and acts unfaithfully against him" (Numbers 5:12). The superfluous phrase "and say unto them" is an amplification, and serves **to include a betrothed woman and a widow awaiting her *yavam* in** the *halakhot* of **warning.**

The *Gemara* now takes up the question implicit in the *Mishnah*: since the *arusa* and *shomeret yavam* cannot drink, how can *kinui* apply to them at all? *Mishna hu d'lo shatya* — the *Mishnah* presupposes that *kinui* can be issued but drinking is unavailable. *Ha'kinui mikanei lah* — *kinui* does apply. *Mna hani mili* — from where do we derive this?

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: the phrase *daber el Bnei Yisrael v'amarta* at the opening of the *parshat sotah* — the doubled language of "speak" and "say" — is interpreted as an expansion. It includes the *arusa* and *shomeret yavam* within the *kinui* stage of the *parasha*, even though they are excluded from the drinking stage by the later phrase *tachat ishah*. The opening of the *parasha* draws them in; the closing phrase cuts them off before the final step.

### Yoshiya vs Yonatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי — רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״תַּחַת אִישֵׁךְ״, פְּרָט לַאֲרוּסָה. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And whose** opinion is expressed in the **mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And the priest shall cause her to swear, and shall say to the woman: If no man has lain with you, and if you have not gone aside to defilement **while under your husband"** (Numbers 5:19). This **excludes a betrothed woman,** who does not yet live with her betrothed, from the ritual of the bitter water. One **might** have thought **that I exclude even a widow awaiting her *yavam*;** therefore, **the verse states:** "If the wife of **any man** goes astray" (Numbers 5:12). The term "any man" serves to include a widow awaiting her *yavam* in the ritual of the bitter water. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״תַּחַת אִישֵׁךְ״ — פְּרָט לְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם. אוֹצִיא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת אֲרוּסָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׂטֶה אִשָּׁה תַּחַת אִישָׁהּ״ — פְּרָט לַאֲרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The phrase **"while under your husband" excludes a widow awaiting her *yavam*** from drinking the bitter water. Lest one would say that **I will exclude a widow awaiting her *yavam* but I will not exclude a betrothed woman, the verse** therefore **states:** "This is the law of jealousy, **when a wife, while under her husband, goes astray,** and is defiled" (Numbers 5:29). The term "while under her husband" **excludes a betrothed woman** from drinking the bitter water.

A *baraita* records a debate between *Rabbi Yoshiya* and *Rabbi Yonatan* that cuts against the *Mishnah*'s unanimous ruling. *Rabbi Yonatan* — whose position matches the *Mishnah* — excludes both *arusa* and *shomeret yavam* from drinking. He reads *tachat ishech* as excluding the *shomeret yavam* first (since that is the more obvious exclusion for him), and a second phrase, *asher tisteh isha tachat ishah*, as excluding the *arusa*.

*Rabbi Yoshiya* takes the opposing view: he uses the double language of *ish ish* to *include* the *shomeret yavam* in the full *sotah* process, up to and including drinking. For *Rabbi Yoshiya*, the *shomeret yavam* is sufficiently married — or nearly so — that she drinks just as a fully married woman would. It is only the *arusa* who is excluded.

### Conceptual Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ אֲרוּסָה, דְּקִידּוּשֵׁי דִּידֵיהּ, וְסוֹקְלִין עַל יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yonatan, holds that the bond with the **betrothed woman is stronger, as** it is **his** own **betrothal,** whereas in the case of a widow awaiting her *yavam*, the bond stems from his brother's betrothal. **And** furthermore, if the betrothed woman commits adultery, she **is stoned due to** her bond with **him,** whereas a widow awaiting her *yavam* who engages in sexual intercourse with another man is liable only to receive lashes. Since the bond with the betrothed woman is stronger, Rabbi Yonatan derives from the phrase "while under your husband" that a widow awaiting her *yavam* is excluded with respect to the bitter water, whereas a betrothed woman is excluded only because of the phrase "and say unto them."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, דְּלָא מְיחַסְּרָא מְסִירָה לְחוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoshiya, holds that the bond with the **widow awaiting her *yavam* is stronger, as she is not lacking entry into the wedding canopy,** as her marriage to the *yavam* is completed through sexual intercourse alone. Rabbi Yoshiya therefore excludes only a betrothed woman from drinking the bitter water.

The underlying conceptual disagreement is this: which of the two relationships — *eirusin* or *zika* — is more weighty, more fully an *ishut*?

*Rabbi Yoshiya* (and the position associated with him in the *Gemara*) holds that the *arusa* relationship is more of a marriage. The man actively chose it; he performed an act of *kiddushin*; she bears full capital liability for adultery. The relationship is enormously weighty, even if not yet a lived marriage. The *shomeret yavam*, by contrast, had this bond thrust upon her without choice, and her liability for sleeping with another man is more limited. So *eirusin* is closer to full *ishut* and harder to exclude; *zika* is more peripheral and more easily read out of the *parasha*.

*Rabbi Yonatan* takes the opposite view. The *shomeret yavam* is more obviously included — and thus more surprisingly excluded by the verse — because her path to full marriage requires no additional act. She does not need *kiddushin*, she does not need a formal wedding ceremony. Sexual relations alone, even unintentional ones, immediately actualize the *zika* into full marriage. The marriage is almost there; it could happen in an instant. The *arusa*, despite her weighty legal status, still requires the full process of *nisuin* to actualize. So the *shomeret yavam* is the one you would expect to be more included in *parshat sotah*, and therefore she is the first one the verse needs to exclude.

This conceptual debate is not merely academic — it maps onto a disagreement in the *Gemara* between *Rav* and *Shmuel* about the legal effect of sexual relations in the *yibbum* context, which the *Gemara* explores in the next section.

## Consummation Requirement

### Baraita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיבִי הָנֵי קְרָאֵי, הָא לָאו הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲרוּסָה שָׁתְיָא? וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא מִדָּרוֹמָא, אֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי מַתְנִיתָא בִּידֵיהּ: ״מִבַּלְעֲדֵי אִישֵׁךְ״, מִשֶּׁקָּדְמָה שְׁכִיבַת בַּעַל לַבּוֹעֵל, וְלֹא שֶׁקָּדְמָה שְׁכִיבַת בּוֹעֵל לַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to both opinions, **the reason** for the exclusion of a betrothed woman is **that these verses are written;** if it **were not so, I would say** that **a betrothed woman drinks. But when Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina came from the South, he came and brought** the following ***baraita* with him:** The verse states: "But if you have gone astray while under your husband, and if you are defiled, and some man has lain with you **besides your husband"** (Numbers 5:20). This indicates that the *halakhot* of a *sota* apply only **when sexual intercourse with the husband preceded** sexual intercourse with **the paramour, and not** in a case **when sexual intercourse with the paramour preceded** intercourse with **the husband.** In the case of a betrothed woman who committed adultery, intercourse with the paramour preceded intercourse with the betrothed, and this verse excludes her from drinking the bitter water of the *sota*.

*Rabbi Acha bar Chanina* came from the south and brought a *baraita* with him: *mishekadma shchivat ba'al l'vo'el* — the *sotah* process can only apply if the husband consummated the marriage before the suspected act of infidelity occurred. *V'lo shekadma shchivat bo'el l'ba'al* — if the suspected act came first and the marriage was never consummated, the husband cannot subject her to the *sotah* ordeal. This is derived from the phrase *miba'aladei ishech* — "other than your husband" — with emphasis on *u'nitein ish bach et shichvato*, implying that the husband's intimacy must precede the other man's.

This is a rule not stated explicitly in the *Mishnah* itself, but one the *Gemara* adopts as authoritative. It raises the question of what, precisely, consummation adds to the legal picture. One possibility is that it is simply another dimension of *ishut* — the marriage must be fully realized, not only in its formal structure but in its physical consummation, before the *sotah* framework applies.

### Zera Concern

A deeper dimension of this requirement may be found in the *Gemara*'s language. The *parasha* uses the phrase *shichvat zera* — "a lying of seed." Halakhically, intercourse without ejaculation would carry the same prohibition, so the phrase seems to have no strictly legal content. The *Ramban* explains this as pointing to the real engine of the husband's jealousy: the concern about *zera* — whose children these are. If another man has lain with his wife, the fundamental fear is that the children he is raising may not be his. The end of the *parasha* echoes this — *v'nikta v'nizra zara*, she will be cleansed and bear seed.

This same concern animates the position of the *Tzitz Eliezer* in the modern debate about artificial insemination with donor sperm. He cited precisely this *Ramban* to argue that another man's *zera*, even without a sexual act, constitutes a form of quasi-adultery. *Rav Moshe Feinstein*, by contrast, dismissed this entirely: halakha deals only with acts, and without a sexual act there is no adultery. He regarded the *Tzitz Eliezer*'s concern as imported from outside values rather than grounded in *Torah* — a remarkable response, given that it is *pshat* of the *psukim* and the *Ramban* says it explicitly.

Applied here: is it possible that the consummation requirement is connected to this concern about *zera*? If the husband has never been intimate with his wife, he has no reason to worry about the paternity of her children; any future children would obviously not be his. The concern about *zera* that drives the *sotah* process only becomes relevant once he has been intimate with her. One cannot say this with certainty — the *Gemara* does not state it this way — but the thematic resonance is present.

### Arusa Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמִי בַּר חָמָא: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּא עָלֶיהָ אָרוּס בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: You find** the necessity for an additional exclusion in a case **where** her **betrothed engaged in sexual intercourse with her in her father's house,** i.e., before they were married, and before the sexual intercourse with the paramour.

This creates a puzzle for the *Mishnah*'s discussion of the *arusa*. If consummation is required for the *sotah* process, why does the *Mishnah* need a separate verse to exclude the *arusa* from drinking? She has not yet consummated the marriage; she is automatically excluded under the consummation requirement.

The *Gemara* resolves this via *Rami bar Chama*: *mashkachat lah k'egon sheba al arusato b'veit aviha* — the exclusion is still needed for the case where the husband had relations with his *arusa* while she was still living in her father's house. She is still an *arusa* — they have not yet made a home together, she has not yet entered the *chuppah* — but the marriage has in fact been consummated. (The *Gemara* notes, citing what we will encounter in *Ketubot*, that in *Yehuda* it was the practice for a betrothed couple to spend private time together, and this sometimes led to sexual relations.) In that case, consummation alone does not transform her into a *nesua*. *Rashi* describes such relations as *b'znut* — not in the context of beginning a married life, and therefore not actuating *nisuin*. For that case, the verse *tachat ishah* is needed to exclude her from drinking.

## Yavam Marriage Status

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם — כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּא עָלֶיהָ יָבָם בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ. הָא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם קָרֵית לַהּ? אִשְׁתּוֹ מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא, דְּהָאָמַר רַב: קָנָה לַכֹּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **In the corresponding** situation **with regard to a widow awaiting her *yavam*,** whom Rabbi Yonatan excludes from the *sota* ritual due to the exclusion from the phrase "while under your husband," is the necessity for this exclusion due to a case **where** the ***yavam* engaged in sexual intercourse with her in her father-in-law's house** before the levirate marriage took place? **But do you call that** woman **a widow awaiting her *yavam*? She is his full-fledged wife, as didn't Rav say:** If a widow awaiting her *yavam* engaged in sexual intercourse with her *yavam*, even without the intention of implementing a levirate marriage, he **has acquired** her as his wife with respect **to all** aspects of marriage, including the *halakhot* of a *sota*.

The *Gemara* then turns the same question on the *shomeret yavam*: *kehavata gabei shomeres yavam* — how do you have a case of a *shomeret yavam* after sexual relations who is still a *shomeret yavam*? The challenge is sharp: the moment *yibbum* relations occur, she should automatically become his full wife — *ishto me'aleta hi*. Why would you ever need a verse to exclude a *shomeret yavam* if the moment she has relations with the *yavam* she is no longer a *shomeret yavam* at all?

### Rav's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: לֹא קָנָה אֶלָּא לִדְבָרִים הָאֲמוּרִים בַּפָּרָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yonatan's opinion is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as** Shmuel **says:** A *yavam* who engages in sexual intercourse with his *yevama* without intending to consummate the levirate marriage **does not acquire** her as his wife **except with regard to** those **matters that are stated in the passage** in the Torah that deals with levirate marriage, i.e., that he inherits his brother's estate, and he can free the widow with a bill of divorce without *ḥalitza* (see *Yevamot* 56a). He is not considered fully married to the woman, and, according to Rabbi Yonatan, the *halakhot* of a *sota* do not apply to her.

*Rav* holds *kanah l'chol davar* — that sexual relations between a *yavam* and his *yevama*, regardless of intent or context, create a complete and full marriage in every respect. On *Rav*'s view, there is never a case of a *shomeret yavam* after relations — she is immediately and fully his wife. The *Gemara* notes that this means *Rav* must say like *Rabbi Yoshiya*, who holds that the *shomeret yavam* does drink. But the *Gemara* will complicate this.

### Shmuel's View

*Shmuel* disagrees: *lo kanah ela l'devarim ha'amurim b'parsha* — relations in the context of *yibbum*, when not accompanied by the intent to begin a married life together, do not create full *nisuin*. She becomes legally his wife in certain respects specified in the *parsha* — adultery would be a capital crime, he inherits his brother's estate through her (*l'hakim shem achiv hamet*) — but she does not achieve the status of full *nisuin*. She remains, in some sense, more like an *arusa* than a *nesua*. On *Shmuel*'s view, there can be a *shomeret yavam* after relations, and that is precisely the case the verse needs to address.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לֵימָא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן? אָמַר לָךְ רַב: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי — אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן. מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטַהּ — מִכְּלַל דְּאִשְׁתּוֹ מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let us say** that **Rav stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya, and Shmuel stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan.** The Gemara rejects this interpretation: **Rav** could have **said to you: I state** my opinion **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan. Since** it **was necessary** for **the verse to exclude** a *yevama* who engaged in sexual intercourse with her *yavam* from the *sota* ritual, **by inference** one may derive **that she is his full-fledged wife** with regard to all other matters.

The *Gemara* draws the natural inference: *Rav* must hold like *Rabbi Yoshiya* and *Shmuel* must hold like *Rabbi Yonatan*. But both *Rav* and *Shmuel* push back against this assignment.

### Final Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי — אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְרַבּוֹיַהּ — מִכְּלָל דְּלָאו אִשְׁתּוֹ הִיא כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could have **said: I state** my opinion **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya. Since** it **was necessary** for **the verse to** specifically **include** in the *sota* ritual a *yevama* who engaged in sexual intercourse with her *yavam*, **by inference** one may conclude **that she is not his wife at all** with regard to any matters other than those explicit in the verse.

*Rav* says: I could actually say like *Rabbi Yonatan* — that the *shomeret yavam* does not drink even after relations. Here is why: *mideitztrich kra lemoutei, miklal d'ishto me'aleta hi* — the very fact that a verse was needed to exclude the *shomeret yavam* demonstrates, according to *Rav*, that without the verse she would be considered completely his wife. She is fully married after relations, as *Rav* maintains — and yet the verse nonetheless excludes her from the *sotah* process. The reason, on this reading, is not that the marriage is incomplete, but that a marriage entered through *yibbum* — through the continuation of the dead brother's bond rather than through an independent act of will — is categorically different from a marriage the man chose. Even though she is fully his wife, it is not the kind of marriage the *Torah* placed within the *sotah* framework.

*Shmuel* responds in kind: I could actually say like *Rabbi Yoshiya* — that the *shomeret yavam* does drink. *Mideitztrich kra l'rabuyei, miklal d'lav ishto hi klal* — the verse needed to include the *shomeret yavam* in the *sotah* process, which shows that without it she would not be considered fully his wife at all. That matches *Shmuel*'s view that relations without intent to begin a married life do not create full *nisuin*. Nevertheless, the verse includes her sufficiently in the *parsha* to make her drink — even this incomplete marriage is enough to trigger the *sotah* ordeal.

The final picture is thus: *Rav* says relations create full marriage, and yet a verse excludes the *yevama* from *sotah* because of the particular nature of *yibbum* marriages. *Shmuel* says relations without marital intent do not create full *nisuin*, and yet a verse includes the *yevama* in the *sotah* process because even that partial bond is sufficient for the *parsha*. Both amoraim can therefore align with either *tanna*, because each is reading the textual derivation through his own understanding of what kind of relationship *yibbum* creates.

With this, the *daf* concludes.
