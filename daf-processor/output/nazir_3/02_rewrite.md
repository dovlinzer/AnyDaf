# Nazir 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## I will be beautiful

### Ehei Na'eh classification

The daf opens with the phrase *ehei na'eh nazir* — "I will be beautiful, [I will be] a *nazir*." If someone declares "I will be beautiful," that person is considered to have accepted *nezirut*. The question is how to classify this statement. A *yad* is typically a truncated, elliptical sentence — "I will be" — where one must fill in the blank. Here, by contrast, a complete sentence has been uttered: "I will be beautiful." The issue is not incompleteness but rather what the word "beautiful" signifies in this context. One could therefore suggest that this functions more as a *kinuy* than as a *yad*, though it does not fit the standard definition of *kinuy* either. A *kinuy* normally involves a technical substitute word — *nazik*, *paziyach*, *naziyach* — standing in for the word *nazir*. The word *na'eh* does not mean *nazir*; it means beautiful. It is only by contextual interpretation that it is understood to refer to *nezirut*. The categorization therefore remains somewhat ambiguous, somewhere between *yad* and *kinuy*.

### Challenge to meaning

The Gemara immediately presses: how do we know that *na'eh* refers to being a *nazir*? Perhaps *ehei na'eh lefanav bamitzvot* — "I will make myself beautiful before God in *mitzvot*" — as the *Braisa* in the *Mekhilta* expounds the verse *zeh Eli ve'anveihu*: "This is my God and I will glorify Him." The *Braisa* explains *anveihu* as *ehei na'eh lefanav bamitzvot* — I will glorify myself before God through the performance of *mitzvot* — and gives examples: *sukkah na'eh*, *lulav na'eh*, *tzitzis na'eh*, *sefer Torah na'eh*. This is the concept of *noi mitzvah*, beautifying the objects of the commandment. Even within a religious register, then, the phrase *ehei na'eh* need not refer to *nezirut*. The challenge stands: even setting aside entirely secular interpretations, the phrase could carry a meaningful religious sense without triggering any vow of *nezirut*.

### Samuel: hair context

Shmuel resolves the difficulty by appeal to context: *she-tefus be-sa'aro ve'amar ena'eh* — the speaker is grabbing hold of his hair while saying "I will be beautiful." With that physical gesture providing the frame, what else could he mean? The long hair of a *nazir* is being contemplated as something beautiful, and the gesture of seizing one's hair gives the necessary context to interpret the declaration as an acceptance of *nezirut*.

### Nazir as sinner

This resolution, however, raises its own difficulty. The Gemara objects: *nezira milsa de'aveira* — being a *nazir* is a matter associated with sin. If *nezirut* is sinful, how can it be described as "beautiful," and how can we halakhically anchor that description? The Gemara acknowledges the point but deflects it: *de'afilu le-Rabbi Elazar HaKappar de'amar nazir chote, hani mili gabbei nazir tamei* — even according to Rabbi Elazar HaKappar, who holds that the *nazir* is a sinner, that characterization applies specifically to a *nazir tamei*, a *nazir* who has become ritually impure. That is the context of the Torah's language *asher chata al hanefesh* — "who sinned against the soul" — which appears in the portion dealing with a *nazir* who became *tamei*. Why is the *nazir tamei* a sinner in a way that goes beyond the mere fact of impurity? Because *de'aydi deba'ei mistar* — since he must now reverse his entire count and begin again, as the Torah says *vehayamim harishonim yipplu* — the first days fall away — there is a concern that *dilma ati leme'evar al nezirutei* — he will come to transgress his *nezirut* altogether. As the *Rishonim* note, drawing on a *gemara* encountered in *Nedarim*, a *nazir tamei* whose *nezirut* drags on indefinitely may come to regret having undertaken it at all, thereby undermining the *leshem shamayim* dimension of the vow. That compromised sincerity is what earns the designation *chote*.

### Tahor nazir not sinner

The Gemara then states: *aval nazir tahor lav chote kari bei* — a *nazir tahor*, one who has successfully maintained purity throughout, is not called a sinner. Consequently, according to everyone, including the more skeptical voices regarding *nezirut*, a *nazir tahor* can genuinely be described as *na'eh*. The statement "I will be beautiful," uttered while seizing one's hair, is therefore an entirely coherent acceptance of *nezirut*.

### Tosafot on mixed nature

Tosafot, however, raises a pointed difficulty. In every other context where Rabbi Elazar HaKappar is cited, his position is precisely the opposite: his *chiddush* is that the term *chote* — which the Torah applies textually to the *nazir tamei* — extends equally to a *nazir tahor*. That is the whole force of his ruling. It is therefore surprising that this Gemara restricts him to the *nazir tamei* case. Tosafot resolves this tension through a nuanced reading: Rabbi Elazar HaKappar holds that *nezirut* is always a mixed phenomenon. The *perishat atzmo min hayayin* — the self-denial of wine — or more broadly the aspiration to attain holiness through separating from society rather than engaging it, is genuinely problematic. Yet there is also something worthy in the gesture: a form of sincere religious striving, a personal acceptance of discipline. If the *nazir* managed to remain *tahor*, the positive element dominated, and we can say the overall experience was good — *hein na'eh*. But the *cheit* element was always present; the negative was not eliminated, merely outweighed. If the *nazir* became *tamei*, the negative dominates entirely and the designation *chote* is unambiguous. This reading allows Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's position to be consistently one of complexity: the *chote* element always applies even to the *nazir tahor*, but when purity is maintained, the balance tips toward the positive.

This picture of *nezirut* as an inherently ambivalent institution — neither straightforwardly praiseworthy nor simply condemned — is worth holding in mind as the tractate unfolds. In *Nedarim*, the dominant voice was decidedly negative toward vow-making: one who makes a *neder* is likened to one who builds a *bamah*, and the presumption was that *resha'im* make *nedarim*. Whether *Nazir* will sustain that register or modulate toward something more ambivalent remains to be seen.

## Incomplete expressions

### Harei kazeh context

The Gemara turns to the phrase *hareini kazeh* — "I am like this." Does this constitute a declaration of *nezirut*? The Gemara immediately asks the same question it posed before: how do we know what "like this" refers to? The answer given is *she-tefus be-sa'aro* — the speaker was holding his hair, the same contextualizing gesture used earlier. Without some such framing, the phrase is too open-ended to anchor.

### Complete vs incomplete

Tosafot draws an important structural distinction arising from these cases. When a sentence is left incomplete — "I will be" — we look to the external surroundings to fill it out. If a *nazir* is passing by, that context supplies the missing content: "I will be like that." However, when a complete sentence has been uttered, the interpretive logic changes. "I will be beautiful" is a full declaration about oneself; one does not naturally look outward to nearby figures to supply its meaning. The necessary context must therefore be intrinsic — the speaker holding his own hair — rather than external. Similarly, if one said "I will be" while holding his hair, Tosafot suggests that would not necessarily produce an acceptance of *nezirut*: an incomplete sentence looks to the passing *nazir* for completion, not to a gesture the speaker is making to himself. Conversely, "I will be beautiful" with a *nazir* walking past — but without the speaker touching his own hair — would likewise be insufficient, since the completed sentence points inward and should be interpreted in relation to the speaker's own words and actions. The asymmetry follows from the structural difference between an incomplete sentence reaching outward for completion and a complete sentence that is self-contained.

### Silsul discussion

The Gemara next considers *hareini mesalsel* — "I will *salsel*." What does *silsul* mean, and why should it refer to *nezirut*? The Gemara cites the maidservant of Rabbi, who appears in several contexts in *Megillah* and *Rosh Hashanah* as a repository of authentic biblical Hebrew — a language increasingly distant from the spoken vernacular of the period, which had shifted toward Aramaic. She said to a certain man, *ad matai ata mesalsel be-se'arekha* — "How long will you keep twirling your hair?" — thereby establishing that *silsul* can refer to playing with or twirling one's hair. However, the Gemara counters: perhaps *mesalsel* means one intends to devote himself to Torah study, as the verse says *salseleha u-teromemeka* — "delve into it and it will raise you up" — where *silsul* connotes deep engagement and immersion. Shmuel's answer is consistent with what he has said throughout: *hacha nami she-tefas be-sa'aro* — here too, the speaker must be holding his hair. With that gesture, *silsul* clearly refers to the hair and not to Torah study.

One might ask whether *silsul* — twirling or playing with one's hair — creates any tension with the *nazir*'s obligation *gadel pera se'ar rosho* — to let his hair grow wild and unruly. The underlying verse describes hair that grows loose and untamed, whereas *silsul* implies active handling. The Gemara does not raise this as a formal objection, and the discussion of *psik reisha* and intent would apply, but the juxtaposition is worth noting.

It is also worth pausing over the contextual framings the Gemara has consistently provided. Of the two standard contexts that render these ambiguous declarations meaningful, one involves seeing another person and wishing to emulate them — a motivation that is, at best, externally driven — and the other emphasizes the beauty or length of the hair. Neither framing represents the most elevated motivation for undertaking *nezirut*. The Gemara may not have had any other practical choice, but both the mimetic impulse and the focus on personal appearance are less than ideal as foundations for a sacred vow.

The Gemara then considers *hareini mechalkel* — a declaration apparently referring to some manner of tending the hair. The Gemara cites a *mishnah* about the minimum quantity of plaster carried into the public domain, where Rabbi Yehudah specifies *kedei la-sud kalkal* — enough to plaster down the temple hair. Rav Papa explains *bastidan* (which Rashi glosses *templa be-la'az* — "temple" in the vernacular) as the hair by the temples that one folds or flattens down in order to secure a covering over it. However, the Gemara counters that *kilkul* can also mean sustaining or feeding the poor, as in the verse *va-yechalkel Yosef es aviv ve-es echav* — "and Yosef sustained his father and brothers." Once again, Shmuel provides the same answer: *she-tefas be-sa'aro* — he was holding his hair. The consistency of Shmuel's approach throughout this passage underscores what is at stake in his position. Recall that the Gemara earlier asked what Shmuel holds regarding a *yad she-einah mochiachat* — an unclear *yad*. The systematic provision of a contextualizing condition for every case in the *mishnah*, conditions that the *mishnah* itself does not mention, reflects Shmuel's insistence on a higher threshold of clarity. The *mishnah* appears to accept these phrases as self-sufficient; Shmuel requires either the holding of hair or a *nazir* passing by to make them evidently meaningful.

### Shalach pera

The Gemara arrives at what seems the most obvious case: *hareini meshalleach pera* — "I will send forth my wild hair." This should be unambiguous. Nevertheless, the Gemara asks: *mimai de-hadin shiluach ribuya hi* — how do we know *shiluach* means to let grow? Perhaps it means to remove, as in *ve-shilach mayim al pnei chutzos* — "to cast the water outward" — meaning one intends to cut off and discard one's hair. The Gemara resolves this by invoking the *gezeira shava* of *pera-pera*: the *nazir*'s verse reads *kadosh yihyeh gadel pera se'ar rosho* — "holy shall be the growing of the wildness of his hair" — and the verse in *Yechezkel* regarding ordinary *kohanim* reads *u-pera lo yishalechu* — "they shall not send forth wild hair" — i.e., *kohanim* must cut their hair every thirty days. The *Tosafot Rosh* notes that the primary function of this *gezeira shava* is in fact to define the *kohen*'s restriction: just as a *nazir*'s *pera* is defined by the standard term of *nezirut*, which is thirty days, so too a *kohen* who allows his hair to grow for thirty days has transgressed. But the link also establishes that *shilu'ach pera* by the *kohanim* — letting the hair grow wild — is the same *pera* as the *nazir*'s. Therefore *shiluach pera* means the growing of wild hair, not its removal. The Gemara adds an alternative: *v'iba'is eima, ha'i meshale'ach mayim nami ribuya hu* — even the verse about casting water can be read as referring to growth, since *d'chimaski lehu maya l'peirei* — when one waters fruits, they grow. For *hareini meshalleach pera*, no additional contextual gesture is required; the phrase is sufficiently clear on its own.

## Birds dispute

### Rabbi Meir birds

The *mishnah* now introduces a significant dispute: *harei alai tzipporim* — "I accept upon myself birds." Rabbi Meir holds this constitutes *nezirut*; the Sages disagree. What is Rabbi Meir's reasoning? Reish Lakish explains: *tzipporim semuchim le-se'ar kibel alav* — birds appear in a verse juxtaposed to hair. The verse in Daniel reads: *ad di se'areh k'neshrin raba v'tiprohi k'tziporin* — "until his hair grew like an eagle's and his nails like birds'." The word *tzipporin* appears in the context of *se'ar*, hair, and therefore Rabbi Meir holds that birds carries the meaning of its adjacent term, referring to the *nazir*'s characteristic hair. It is a considerable interpretive leap — the more directly juxtaposed word in the verse is *neshrin*, eagle, not *tzipporin* — but one can also observe a linguistic connection: the Aramaic *tiprohi* shares resonance with *tzipornav*, fingernails, which in turn is connected to *tzipor*, bird. In any case, Rabbi Meir's principle is explicit: *matpisin b'davar ha-samuch lo* — one can perform a *hatpasa*, borrowing the meaning of a nearby word.

The term *hatpasa* carries technical weight from *Masechet Nedarim*, where it describes the transfer of status: if item A has been rendered *assur* by a *neder konam*, and one then says *zeh kazeh*, one is *matpis* the second item with the status of the first. Here the usage is somewhat different — one is not transferring a status but borrowing a semantic meaning from an adjacent word in a verse — yet the technical vocabulary is deliberately introduced.

### Sages reject borrowing

The Sages hold *lo matpis inish bemidi d'samich leh* — one does not borrow meaning from a nearby word. Their more straightforward understanding of the declaration *harei alai tzipporim* would be that the speaker intends to bring bird sacrifices in the context of *nezirut*: since a *nazir tamei* brings bird offerings as part of his *korban*, saying "I accept birds upon myself" might mean "I accept the type of *nezirut* that involves bringing birds." That is the more direct semantic connection, and notably it reflects somewhat negatively on the speaker's framing — accepting *nezirut* with anticipated impurity built into the conception is a troubling starting point, given everything the earlier discussion established about the *nazir tamei*. But that is the Sages' reading of what Rabbi Meir himself must mean. The Sages say that even this more direct connection is insufficient: you cannot anchor a declaration of *nezirut* in a term that is itself neutral simply because a related term appears nearby.

### Rabbi Yochanan view

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different account entirely. He holds *dekuli alma lo matpis* — everyone agrees that one cannot borrow meaning from a nearby word. The disagreement between Rabbi Meir and the Sages is not about the *hatpasa* principle at all. Rabbi Meir's reasoning, according to Rabbi Yochanan, is *de-chayshinan shema tzipporei nazir tamei kibel alav* — we are concerned that perhaps the speaker meant to accept upon himself the bird-sacrifices of a *nazir tamei*. That is the practical connection: a *nazir tamei* brings bird sacrifices, and saying "I accept birds" in that context may be understood as a declaration of *nezirut* with the attendant *korbanot* in mind.

The Gemara presses: if we are operating on "perhaps that is what he means," why not say *dilma tzipporei nedavah kibel alav* — perhaps he simply meant to bring a voluntary bird offering as an *olah*? The Gemara responds: if that were his intent, he would have said *harei alai kan* — the standard idiom for a pair of bird offerings. Since he said *tzipporim* without the standard term *kan*, the voluntary-offering reading is less natural. The Gemara continues: perhaps he meant to donate birds for a *metzora*'s purification ritual, where the verse uses the word *tzippor* repeatedly — *ve-shachat et ha-tzippor ha-chayah*, *ve-et ha-tzippor*, *be-dam ha-tzippor ha-shechutah*. Tosafot explains this as meaning: perhaps he intended that if any *metzora* in the world requires birds for his *tahara* process, he accepts upon himself to donate them.

### Reish Lakish differs

These challenges reveal that even Rabbi Yochanan's explanation requires an additional condition to eliminate alternative readings. The Gemara therefore concludes: *kegon shehaya nazir tahor over lefanav* — the case is one in which a *nazir tahor* was walking in front of the speaker. With that context, the declaration "I accept birds upon myself" is understood as relating to *nezirut*, and not to any of the alternative possibilities. This answer applies equally to Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning and to Reish Lakish's.

### Nazir context required

If both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish ultimately require a *nazir* walking in front of the speaker, the question arises: *mai beinaiahu* — what is the practical difference between their positions?

### Tosafot reconciles

The Gemara resolves this: *ika beinaiahu kegon d'amar tzipporim ha-semuchim le-se'ar alai* — the difference emerges in a case where there was no *nazir* passing by, but the speaker explicitly said "I accept upon myself birds that are written next to hair." According to Rabbi Yochanan, even this explicit formulation is insufficient without a *nazir* present; the contextual anchor is required regardless. According to Reish Lakish, this explicit invocation of the verse's juxtaposition is itself sufficient: by referencing birds in the specific context of that *pasuk* in Daniel, the speaker has made clear that "birds" carries the meaning of the adjacent "hair," and therefore of *nezirut* — without any need for a *nazir* to be passing by.

This allows Reish Lakish's account to explain the underlying debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages in the *mishnah* as stated, where no special external context is mentioned at all. Rabbi Meir holds that the invocation of a word whose neighboring term in a verse has the desired meaning is sufficient; the Sages hold it is not. On that reading, the *mishnah*'s formulation makes sense: Rabbi Meir requires no passing *nazir*; he relies on the semantic borrowing from the text. The Sages reject that borrowing.

The Gemara then raises a challenge to the Sages' position from a *Braisa*: *ha-omer yamin harei zo shevu'ah* — one who says "by my right hand" has made a valid oath. How can the mere utterance of "right hand" constitute a *shevu'ah*? Is that not precisely borrowing the meaning of a neighboring term — since the verse says *va-yarem yemino u-smolo el ha-shamayim va-yishava b'chai ha-olam*, where *yamin* appears alongside *shevu'ah*? The Gemara rejects this comparison: *mishum d'yamin gufei ikrei shevu'ah* — because the word *yamin* itself has acquired the independent meaning of oath, as the verse *nishba Hashem bi-yemino* demonstrates: God swore "by His right hand," meaning God took an oath in the form of "By my right hand I will do X." The meaning of *yamin* as oath is therefore intrinsic to the word through usage, not merely borrowed from an adjacent term. This distinction vindicates the Sages' position: they reject the borrowing of neighboring meaning, but acknowledge that words can organically develop new meanings through the contexts in which they consistently appear.

## Partial nezirut

### All-or-nothing rule

The *mishnah* introduces a new and conceptually important case: *harei ani nazir min ha-chartzanim* — "I am a *nazir* from the grape-skins," or *min ha-zagim* — from the seeds, or *min ha-tiglachat* — from shaving, or *min ha-tumah* — from impurity. The person has declared *nezirut* but attempted to limit it to only one of its constituent restrictions. The *mishnah* rules: *harei zeh nazir ve-chol dikdukei nezirut alav* — that person is nonetheless a *nazir*, and all the detailed laws of *nezirut* apply. It is an all-or-nothing institution: one cannot accept the status of *nazir* piecemeal.

### Not Rabbi Shimon

The Gemara immediately notes: *masnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon* — the *mishnah* does not reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Shimon view

A *Braisa* is cited: Rabbi Shimon holds *eino chayav ad she-yidom mikulam* — one is not bound by *nezirut* unless one vows regarding all of its restrictions. If someone attempts only a partial acceptance, he has done nothing at all.

### Sages' reasoning

The Sages hold the opposite: *afilu nazar ela be-echad min ha-nehon havei nazir* — even if one accepts only one restriction, one becomes a *nazir* subject to all of them. The logic is that *nezirut* is a unified package; a partial acceptance drags the whole structure along with it.

### Biblical sources

What are the biblical sources for these positions? Rabbi Shimon derives his view from *mikkol asher ye'aseh mi-gefen ha-yayyin me-chartzannim ve-ad zag* — "from everything that is made from the grapevine, from the skin to the pit." The word *mikkol* — from everything — implies that one must accept the full scope; a partial acceptance falls short of the whole and is therefore nothing. The Sages derive theirs from *mi-yayin ve-shachar yazir* — "from wine and intoxicating drink he shall become a *nazir*." The verse names only wine, yet we know full well that a *nazir* is bound by far more than wine alone. Evidently, naming only wine — only part of the obligation — is sufficient to constitute *nezirut* for everything. Even a partial declaration triggers the full status.

## Nazir and wine mitzvot

### Wine mizvot issue

The Gemara now asks: since the extra language *mi-yayin ve-shachar yazir* is not needed to tell us about the all-or-nothing rule (the Sages deriving their position from elsewhere), what does this verse come to teach? The answer: *la-shavyei mitzvah ke-reshut* — to equate even a *mitzvah* involving wine with an ordinary permitted act. That is, the *nazir*'s prohibition on wine applies even when drinking wine would fulfill a *mitzvah*. The verse's inclusive language covers wine consumed in the performance of commandments.

### Kiddush problem

What *mitzvah* would be implicated? *Kiddushta ve-avdalta* — the wine of *kiddush* and *havdala*. Even for those, the *nazir* is forbidden. This seems unremarkable. But the Gemara then produces what is, by any measure, a striking statement: *harei mushba ve-omed hu me-Har Sinai* — but is he not already sworn and obligated from Sinai? The implication appears to be that since one has a *Sinai*-level obligation to make *kiddush*, that obligation pre-empts the *nazir*'s prohibition: *mushba ve-omed* — a pre-existing oath or obligation cannot be overridden by a subsequent vow. The *Rishonim* are confounded. Wine for *kiddush* is not *d'Oraita* in the first place — there is no verse in the Torah requiring *kiddush* on wine specifically; one may make *kiddush* on bread. And even if there were a Torah-level obligation to make *kiddush* on wine, the principle of *mushba ve-omed* would not straightforwardly apply, because accepting *nezirut* does not constitute a direct declaration against the *kiddush* obligation. One could compare it to declaring a *sukkah* forbidden to oneself: such a declaration does not directly negate the obligation to dwell in a *sukkah*, but focuses on the object; and yet it effectively prevents fulfillment of the *mitzvah* when the time comes. The same logic applies to *nezirut*: by accepting the status of *nazir* one does not say "I will not make *kiddush*" — one merely takes on a status that renders wine *assur*, which then gets in the way.

### Kiddush not d'Oraita

The *Rishonim* therefore struggle to make sense of this *gemara* as a straightforward statement. The basic analytic objections are twofold: first, wine for *kiddush* is *de-Rabbanan*, not *d'Oraita*; second, even for a *d'Oraita* obligation, the *mushba ve-omed* principle does not apply when the newer vow operates obliquely rather than directly against the standing obligation.

### Rabbeinu Tam rhetorical

Rabbeinu Tam cuts through the difficulty with characteristic boldness: the Gemara's statement is not a declarative assertion but a rhetorical question expressed with astonishment — *bitmiha*. The Gemara is not saying that *mushba ve-omed* prevents *nezirut* from taking effect regarding *kiddush* wine. It is asking incredulously: do you really think there is a Sinai-level obligation to drink wine for *kiddush*? Of course there is not. The whole premise of the question collapses, and the verse's teaching that *nezirut* applies even to wine connected with *mitzvot* stands uncontested.

### Rava case

The Gemara then presents Rava's formulation of a related case: *shavu'ah she-eshteh* — if one takes an oath that he will drink, *ve-chazar ve-amar hareini nazir* — and then declares himself a *nazir*, *chalah nezirut al shavu'ah* — the *nezirut* takes effect notwithstanding the oath.

### Nezirut vs shevua

This case is the clean illustration of the principle at stake. A person has sworn an oath to drink wine and subsequently accepted *nezirut*. His *nezirut* is fully operative, and he is now forbidden to drink. But this does not mean the *nazir* simply overrides his *shevua*: it means the *nezirut* takes effect as a status, and when the time arrives to fulfill the oath, the *nazir* status stands in the way — just as making all *sukkot* forbidden to oneself does not directly violate the obligation to sit in a *sukkah* but renders fulfillment impossible when *Sukkot* arrives. The *nezirut* does not go head-to-head against the *shevua*; it operates laterally, focusing on the person's status rather than directly contradicting his prior commitment. That is why *nezirut* takes effect — it is not *mushba ve-omed* in the direct sense.

The overwhelming consensus of the *Rishonim*, including the extended discussion in the *Tosafot Rid* in *Arvei Pesachim*, is to follow Rabbeinu Tam's reading: the *gemara*'s *mushba ve-omed* statement is rhetorical, wine for *kiddush* is *de-Rabbanan*, and *nezirut* can take effect even in the face of a *shevua* because the two operate on different planes. A small number of *Rishonim* do attempt to read the *gemara* literally — concluding, for instance, that a *nazir* would be permitted to drink *kiddush* wine, or entertaining related anomalies — but these readings are widely regarded as untenable. The *sugya* resolves cleanly on Rabbeinu Tam's account, and with that the daf reaches its conclusion.
