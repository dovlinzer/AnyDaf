# Yevamot 92b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiddushin Yevamah

### Rav: No Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בִּיבָמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִהְיֶה אֵשֶׁת הַמֵּת הַחוּצָה לְאִישׁ זָר״ — לֹא תְּהֵא בָּהּ הֲוָיָה לְזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: From where** is it derived **that betrothal** by another man **does not take effect with a *yevama*? As it is stated: "The wife of the dead man shall not be married outside of the family to one not of his kin"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), which indicates: **She shall not have** the possibility of **becoming** married **to one not of his kin,** i.e., his betrothal is of no account.

The *mishnah* with which we concluded the previous *daf* addresses the case of a woman whose husband travels abroad, contradictory testimony is received regarding the order of deaths — first that the husband died and then the sons, leading to remarriage, and subsequently it emerged that the reverse was true. The woman thus turned out to have been a *shomeret yavam* who married another man, and the *mishnah* declares the child a *mamzer*. As was established, this ruling follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds that a child born of a union in violation of an *issur lav* is a *mamzer*, and indeed the status of *shomeret yavam* involves such a prohibition.

The *gemara* now opens a new discussion, citing the teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav. Rav asks: *minayin she'ein kiddushin tofsin b'yevamah* — from where do we know that *kiddushin* does not take effect at all with a *yevamah*? This question is significant because it steps back from the particular question of *mamzerut*, which may be limited to Rabbi Akiva's position, and addresses the more fundamental question of whether *kiddushin* with a *yevamah* is legally effective. Rav argues, and the *rishonim* make this clear, that even those who would not follow Rabbi Akiva with respect to *mamzerut* from *chayavei lavin* would agree that *kiddushin* simply does not take hold with a woman who is *zekukah l'yibum*. The logic runs as follows: the relationship between the *yevamah* and the *yavam* is not merely a prohibition standing in the way of her marrying another; rather, the *zikah* — the ongoing quasi-marital bond to her deceased husband that transfers to the *yavam* — means she is already in some sense bound to the *yavam*. She cannot become bound to another man while that *zikah* remains unresolved.

The *drashah* is from the verse *lo tihiyeh eishet ha-mes ha-chutzah l'ish zar* — the wife of the dead shall not be to the outside to a foreign man. Rav reads this not merely as a prohibition but as a statement that the *havayah*, the legal bond of marriage, will not take hold. The word *tihiyeh* is thus read as referring to the validity of the marriage itself: she cannot become legally bound to a stranger.

### Shmuel's Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בַּעֲנִיּוּתֵינוּ, צְרִיכָה גֵּט. מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל הַאי ״לָא תִהְיֶה אֵשֶׁת הַמֵּת״, אִי לְלָאו הוּא דַּאֲתָא, אִי דְּלָא תָּפְסִי בַּהּ קִדּוּשִׁין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: In our poverty** of knowledge, as we do not fully understand the verse, **she requires a bill of divorce.** The Gemara explains: **Shmuel was uncertain with regard to this** verse: **"The wife of the dead man shall not be married** outside," **whether it comes for a prohibition,** i.e., the woman is prohibited from marrying another man but the betrothal of that other man is effective, or **whether** it **comes** to teach **that betrothal** by any other man **does not take effect with her.**

Shmuel, however, responds *ba'aniyoseinu tzrichah get* — in our humility, she requires a *get*. The *gemara* reads this as an expression of doubt: *mesapkah lei l'Shmuel* — Shmuel is uncertain whether the verse is only establishing a prohibition, in which case the *kiddushin* might nonetheless take effect, or whether it is establishing that *kiddushin* will not take effect at all, as Rav maintains. Because of this doubt, Shmuel requires her to receive a *get* at least as a precaution, *mi-safek*.

### Rav Ashi Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרִי בַּר רָחֵל לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הֲלָכָה כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר אַמֵּימָר הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל — אִם הָיָה יְבָמָהּ כֹּהֵן, חוֹלֵץ לָהּ, וְשַׁרְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari bar Raḥel said to Rav Ashi** that **Ameimar said as follows: The *halakha* is in accordance** with the opinion of **Shmuel. Rav Ashi said: Now that Ameimar said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance** with the opinion of **Shmuel,** who maintains that one who betroths a *yevama* before she has performed *ḥalitza* must give her a bill of divorce, **if her *yavam* was a priest, he performs *ḥalitza* with her,** as she is forbidden to him after the man who betrothed her gave her a bill of divorce, **and she is** thereby **permitted to** the man to whom she was betrothed.

Rav Mari bar Rachel reports to Rav Ashi that Amemar rules the *halacha* follows Shmuel — she requires a *get*, at least as a matter of doubt. Rav Ashi then draws a practical distinction based on whether the *yavam* is a *kohen* or a *Yisrael*. If the *yavam* is a *kohen* and she subsequently accepted *kiddushin* from another man, then once that second man gives her a *get*, she becomes a *gerushah* and the *kohen* will no longer be permitted to perform *yibum*. Therefore, since *yibum* is in any event going to be impossible and only *chalitzah* can resolve the bond, Rav Ashi suggests: let the *yavam* perform *chalitzah* first, and she may then remain with the man she married. As the *gemara* puts it: *choletz lah v'sharya lei* — he performs *chalitzah* for her and she is permitted to the one she's with.

### Sinner Rewarded Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּגוֹרֵי אִיתְּגַר, אִם כֵּן מָצִינוּ חוֹטֵא נִשְׂכָּר! אֶלָּא, אִם הָיָה יְבָמָהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, נוֹתֵן לָהּ שֵׁנִי גֵּט, וְהוּתְּרָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this ruling: But in that case, she thereby **gains** from her prohibited betrothal, as she may subsequently marry the man who betrothed her illegally. **If so, we find a sinner benefiting** from his transgression. **Rather,** the Gemara emends the teaching: **If her *yavam* was** a regular **Israelite,** this **second** man who betrothed her **must give her a bill of divorce, and she is permitted to** the *yavam*, as a non-priest may marry a divorcée.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *im kein matzinu choteh niskar* — if so, we find that the sinner is rewarded. The man who married the *yevamah* when he had no right to do so would benefit from his transgression, since it now forces the *yavam*'s hand toward *chalitzah* — an outcome that might have been different had the *yavam* wished to perform *yibum*. The *gemara* therefore rejects the scenario of *chalitzah* first in the case of the *kohen* brother-in-law, lest the wrongdoer profit from his act.

In the case of a *Yisrael* brother-in-law, however, the rule is: *nosei la-sheni get v'hutra lo* — the second man gives a *get* and she becomes permitted to her *yavam* for *yibum*. The *chiddush* here, and the reason a lengthy *Tosafos* engages with it, lies in how different this resolution is from the treatment of the woman whose husband returned after being reported dead. In that earlier *mishnah*, there were sweeping negative consequences — she was forbidden to both men. Here, the *gemara* allows the situation to resolve itself cleanly: the second man gives a *get*, and she goes to her *yavam* for *yibum*.

### Tosafos Kiddushin/Nisuin

Tosafos raises a significant qualification: this clean resolution may be available only because the couple had completed no more than *kiddushin* with each other, not *nisuin*. Tosafos draws the parallel to the case of the husband reported dead — where *kiddushin* without *nisuin* to the second man allowed her to return to her original husband without the broader negative consequences. Similarly, in this case, where only *kiddushin* was contracted, we can allow her to get a *get* and proceed to *yibum* or *chalitzah* without imposing all the severe consequences familiar from the beginning of the *perek*. But if *nisuin* had been completed, Tosafos suggests the more severe consequences might apply.

The Tosafos text in *nosei la-sheni get* clarifies this with a ruling of Rabbeinu Tam in an actual case: a woman who was unaware that she had a *yavam* and was subsequently *mekudeshes* to another man. Rabbeinu Tam ruled that if the *yavam* was in any event inclined to perform *chalitzah* — meaning the second man had not forced his hand — then the *yavam* may perform *chalitzah* first, and she is permitted to remain with the man to whom she had been *mekudeshes*. The operative distinction is: we do not allow the second man to benefit from having compelled *chalitzah*. But if *chalitzah* would have been the *yavam*'s choice regardless, then the sequence of *chalitzah* followed by remaining with the second man is permitted — and, crucially, this applies only where the couple had *kiddushin* and not *nisuin*. Rashi, by contrast, appears to permit it even after *nisuin*, and the question of whether *nisuin* would trigger the full array of consequences known from the opening *mishnah* remains unresolved between these two views.

## Nisuin Consequences

### Rav Gidel Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב: יְבָמָה — קִדּוּשִׁין אֵין בָּהּ, נִשּׂוּאִין יֵשׁ בָּהּ. אִי קִדּוּשִׁין אֵין בָּהּ — נִשּׂוּאִין נָמֵי אֵין בָּהּ! אֵימָא: קִדּוּשִׁין וְנִשּׂוּאִין אֵין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **a *yevama*, betrothal does not** apply **to her,** but **marriage does** apply **to her.** The Gemara expresses puzzlement: **If betrothal does not** apply **to her, marriage also** should **not** apply **to her.** How can marriage take effect if the earlier and less binding stage of betrothal is of no consequence? Rather, emend the above statement and **say: Neither betrothal nor marriage** apply **to her.**

The *gemara* now cites two parallel traditions, both transmitted in the name of Rav. Rav Gidel says in the name of Rav, and Rav Chiya bar Yosef says in the name of Rav: *yevamah kiddushin ein bah, nisuin yesh bah* — with respect to a *yevamah*, *kiddushin* has no effect, but there is *nisuin*. The *gemara* immediately registers the problem: if *kiddushin* does not take effect, how can *nisuin* take effect? *Ei kiddushin ein bah, nisuin nami ein bah* — if *kiddushin* has no validity, *nisuin* likewise has no validity.

### Nisuin Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי ״נִשּׂוּאִין יֵשׁ בָּהּ״ — בִּזְנוּת, כִּדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁזִּינְּתָה — אֲסוּרָה לִיבָמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different explanation. **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **Marriage does** apply **to her?** It is referring **to** a case of **licentious sexual relations.** In other words, although Rav maintains that betrothal is ineffective for her, if she entered the wedding canopy with another man and had relations with him, her status changes and she is forbidden to the *yavam*. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna said** that **a widow awaiting her *yavam* who committed an act of licentious sexual relations is forbidden to her *yavam*.**

The *gemara* therefore offers multiple interpretations of what *nisuin yesh bah* means.

The first possibility, which Tosafos develops: *nisuin ein bah* — even in the interpretation that says there is no *nisuin* — might actually be the intended teaching, meaning that even if the couple proceeds through *nisuin*, we do not impose the negative consequences. There is no *tetzei mi-zeh u-mi-zeh*, no *mamzerut*, no stripping away of the *ketubah*. The statement *nisuin ein bah* would then be a statement of legal leniency: even *nisuin* does not trigger those severe outcomes.

The second possibility (*ve'ee ba'is teima*): *nisuin yesh bah* in the sense of *biznus* — following Rav Hamnuna, who taught that a *shomeret yavam* who engaged in sexual relations with another man is *asura* to her *yavam*. The act of *nisuin* is significant not as a valid marriage but as a sexual act that renders her forbidden to the *yavam*, precluding *yibum* and leaving only *chalitzah* as the means of release. There is an *issur lav*, but *yibum* is now foreclosed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, לְעוֹלָם כְּדַאֲמַרַן מֵעִיקָּרָא: קִדּוּשִׁין אֵין בָּהּ, נִשּׂוּאִין יֵשׁ בָּהּ. דְּמִיחַלְּפָא בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say: Actually,** it is **as we said initially,** that **betrothal does not** apply **to her** but **marriage does** apply **to her.** However, this does not mean that marriage is actually effective. Rather, the *halakha* is that he must give her a bill of divorce, **as** people might **confuse** this case **with** that of **a woman whose husband went overseas.** The Sages decreed that he must give her a bill of divorce so that people would not say that a woman who remarried after hearing that her husband had died likewise does not require a bill of divorce.

The third possibility (*ve'ee ba'is teima le'olam teima me'ikara*): *nisuin yesh bah* in the sense that it creates an appearance sufficiently resembling a real marriage to require a *get*. The analogy offered is to a woman whose husband has gone *limedinat ha-yam* and who then begins living with another man after a report of the husband's death. Even if the marriage is not legally valid, it looks like a marriage, and therefore consequences attach to it. Here too, the *yavam*-bond is not the most obvious social reality — many would not even know that *kiddushin* cannot take effect with a *shomeret yavam* — and once she goes through with *nisuin*, it appears to onlookers as though she is married to the second man. That appearance is significant enough that she requires a *get*. This interpretation ties back to the analysis offered earlier in the *masechta*: some of the consequences in the opening *mishnah* stem not from punitive *knassos* but from the legal reality that the second marriage looks genuine and must be unwound as such.

### Rabbi Yannai Vote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: בַּחֲבוּרָה, נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ: אֵין קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בִּיבָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai said: In the group** of Sages who discussed this matter, **they counted and concluded** that **betrothal** by another man **does not take effect with a *yevama*.**

Amar Rabbi Yannai: *bi-chavura nimnu ve-gamru* — in the rabbinic assembly they voted and concluded: *ein kiddushin tofsin b'yevamah* — *kiddushin* does not take effect with a *yevamah*. This is against Shmuel's position, which had left the matter in doubt. The *gemara* thus records a formal consensus rejecting the possibility that *kiddushin* takes hold. Whether a rabbinically-imposed requirement of a *get* remains applicable after *nisuin* is not resolved by this vote, but the fundamental legal nullity of *kiddushin* with a *yevamah* is here stated as the accepted position. It is striking that this ruling emerges so far into *Masechet Yevamot* — a masechta entirely devoted to *yibum* and *chalitzah* — and provides what is, in retrospect, one of the most foundational statements about the nature of *zikah*: the bond is strong enough to preclude any other man's *kiddushin* from taking effect.

## Future Transactions

### Rabbi Yochanan Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי, לֹא מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ הִיא זוֹ? דִּתְנַן, הָאוֹמֵר לָאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶתְגַּיֵּיר״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּתְגַּיְּירִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶשְׁתַּחְרֵר״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּחְרְרִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּמוּת בַּעְלִיךְ״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתָּמוּת אֲחוֹתִיךְ״, אוֹ ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיַּחְלוֹץ לִיךְ יְבָמִיךְ״ — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: My teacher, is this not** taught in **a mishna** that states that betrothal is of no account for such a woman? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 16b): With regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me after I convert; after you convert; after I am freed** from slavery; **after you are freed; after your husband dies; after your sister dies;** or **after your *yavam* performs *ḥalitza* with you, she is not betrothed.** The reason for the above ruling is that he is considered to be attempting to acquire an entity that is not yet in existence, as the betrothal cannot take effect at that point in time. This indicates that betrothal is entirely ineffective for a *yevama* until she performs *ḥalitza*. Rabbi Yannai **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Had I not lifted the earthenware shard for you, would you have discovered the pearl** [***marganita***] **beneath it?** It was only after I informed you of the *halakha* that you were able to cite a proof for it from a mishna.

Responding to Rabbi Yannai's report of the vote, Rabbi Yochanan says: *lo mishnatenu hi zu* — is this not simply our *mishnah*? We need not rely on a rabbinic vote; the matter is already stated explicitly in a *mishnah*. Which *mishnah*? *Ha'omer le'isha harei at mekudeshet li le'achar she'etgayer* — a man says to a woman: you are betrothed to me after I convert, or after you convert, or after I am freed from slavery, or after you are freed, or after your husband dies, or after my wife your sister dies, or — *o le'achar she-yachalotz yevamayich* — after your *yavam* performs *chalitzah*: in all these cases, *einah mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* does not take effect.

Why should the case of *chalitzah* be different from the others? In all the other cases, the man or woman is currently ineligible for *kiddushin* — one is not Jewish, one is enslaved, one's husband is alive, one's sister is alive. But the case of the *yevamah* presents a different profile: it is merely an *issur lav* that stands between her and another man. The fact that the *mishnah* places it in the same category as the others proves that *kiddushin* with a *yevamah* does not take effect — and this is the teaching of the *mishnah* itself, not merely a rabbinic vote.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אִי לָאו דְּקַלְּסָךְ גַּבְרָא רַבָּה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לָךְ אֲנָא: מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** Sometime later, **Reish Lakish said** to Rav Yoḥanan: **If** it were **not** for the fact **that a great man,** Rabbi Yannai, **praised you, I would say** that this is no proof, as it is possible that **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said** that **betrothal does not take effect for** those **liable for** violating regular **prohibitions,** and therefore it is not effective with a *yevama*. However, according to the opinion of the Rabbis, betrothal is effective for this woman, as is the case with all regular prohibitions.

Reish Lakish responds to Rabbi Yochanan: had this great man — Rabbi Yannai — not praised him for finding this proof, Reish Lakish would have argued that there is in fact no proof from that *mishnah* at all. For that *mishnah* may reflect only the position of Rabbi Akiva: *matnitin Rabbi Akiva hi, de'amar ein kiddushin tofsin be-chayavei lavin* — Rabbi Akiva holds that *kiddushin* does not take effect with those who are subject to a *lav*, and therefore the *mishnah* disqualifies *kiddushin* with a *yevamah*. But this does not establish the consensus view; it may simply be one expression of Rabbi Akiva's broader ruling about *chayavei lavin*, and not a universally agreed-upon position.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, כִּי אָמַר לַהּ ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיַּחֲלוֹץ לִיךְ יְבָמִיךְ״ לִיתְפְּסוּ בָּהּ קִידּוּשֵׁי! דְּהָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if** it is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, when he said to her: After your *yavam* performs *ḥalitza* for you, let the betrothal take effect with her, as we have heard that Rabbi Akiva said** that **a man can transfer an entity that has not** yet **come into the world.** In other words, Rabbi Akiva is of the opinion that an acquisition can take effect for something not yet in existence. If so, even if the betrothal cannot take effect now, it should be valid after she has performed *ḥalitza*. The proof that this is indeed Rabbi Akiva's opinion is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 59a):

The *gemara* however rejects the reading that the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Akiva. For if this were Rabbi Akiva's *mishnah*, the *kiddushin* should actually work — at least in the future, once *chalitzah* has been performed. This follows from the fact that Rabbi Akiva holds *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* — a person may transact with something not yet in existence, and the transaction takes effect when the necessary conditions are eventually met. If Rabbi Akiva were the *tanna* of that *mishnah*, then a man who says *harei at mekudeshet li le'achar she-yachalotz yevamayich* should be establishing a conditional *kiddushin* that takes effect once *chalitzah* is performed. Since the *mishnah* says it does not work, it cannot be expressing Rabbi Akiva's view. Therefore, the *mishnah* reflects the consensus, and the consensus is that *kiddushin* with a *yevamah* never takes effect.

This introduces the broader topic of *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*, to which the *gemara* now devotes sustained attention.

### Konamic Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי עוֹשָׂה לְפִיךָ — אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהָפֵר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר, שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲדִיף עָלָיו יוֹתֵר מִן הָרָאוּי לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a wife said: ***Konam*,** i.e., this is forbidden like consecrated property, in reference to anything **that I will prepare for your mouth,** that is, the payment for any work I perform shall be forbidden to you, **he is not required to nullify** the vow, as it is automatically void, since she was under a prior obligation to give him her earnings as part of their marriage agreement. **Rabbi Akiva says:** He should **nullify** the vow, **lest she exceed more than is fitting for him.** A husband is entitled only to a certain sum from his wife's earnings (see *Ketubot* 64b). If she earns only the sum of money to which he is entitled, the vow certainly does not go into effect. However, she might earn more, in which case the vow would apply with regard to the additional amount. To avoid this scenario, it is preferable for the husband to nullify the vow. This shows that according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva a vow applies even with regard to an entity that has not yet come into the world. In this case, the entity is the earnings for work she has yet to perform.

To establish that Rabbi Akiva indeed holds *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: *konam she'ani osa leficha* — a woman says to her husband that the labor she will produce should be *assur* to his mouth, consecrated in the manner of a *korban*. Ordinarily a husband is entitled to his wife's earnings; she is not the owner of that future labor and cannot render it forbidden by a *neder*. The *tanna kamma* rules *lo tzarich lehafer* — the husband need not annul it, because she has no right to what she produces. But Rabbi Akiva says *yafer* — he must annul it, *shema ta'adif alav yoter min ha-ra'uy lo* — lest she produce in excess of what he is legally entitled to, in which case the surplus belongs to her and the *neder* on that portion would be valid.

Now, the underlying question: how can a *neder* work on something that does not yet exist? A *neder* is not an oath of future behavior (*shevua*); it changes the legal status of an object. How can she alter the status of labor that has not yet been produced? The answer is that Rabbi Akiva permits this — and this is the expression of his position that *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*.

### Rav Huna Hands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: בְּאוֹמֶרֶת: ״יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַי לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם״, דְּיָדַיִם אִיתַנְהוּ בְּעָלְמָא. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **But wasn't it stated with regard to that** mishna that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** This mishna is not referring to a woman who renders the earnings for work she has yet to perform forbidden, but **to** a wife **who says: Let my hands be consecrated to their Maker.** In other words, she declares that it is as though her hands were sanctified, which means that anything produced by them is forbidden. **Since** these **hands** are **in the world,** she has not attempted to acquire something that does not exist. Therefore, there is no proof from here with regard to Rabbi Akiva's opinion on that issue. **And** this opinion, that Rabbi Akiva maintains that one cannot acquire an entity that has not yet come into the world, **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak.**

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua qualifies the proof: the *mishnah* need not depend on the principle of *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* at all. The woman is not making a *neder* on the non-existent labor; she is making a *neder* in the formulation of *yikdeshu yadai la'oseihen* — my hands are consecrated to their Maker. She is sanctifying her hands themselves, and her hands do exist. Just as one may sanctify a tree for the fruit it will produce, she sanctifies her hands for the labor they will generate. *Ve'yadayim isnhu ba'olam* — the hands do exist in the world. Therefore, Rabbi Akiva's ruling in that case does not necessarily prove that he accepts *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* in general.

This interpretation creates a tension with Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's subsequent claim, discussed in the following section, that all the authorities in a long chain hold of *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* — a claim that includes Rav Huna. The *gemara* therefore flags: *u-fliga d'Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* — this reading by Rav Huna (that Rabbi Akiva's case is explicable without recourse to *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*) conflicts with Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's position, which assumes that Rabbi Akiva indeed affirms *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* and places him at the head of a chain of authorities who all share that view.

## Tannaim Chain

### Rav Nachman Lineage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: רַב הוּנָא כְּרַב, וְרַב כְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי, וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי כְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא כְּרַבִּי, וְרַבִּי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments: **And** this opinion, that Rabbi Akiva maintains that one cannot acquire an entity that has not yet come into the world, **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, as Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Rav Huna** agrees **with** the opinion of **Rav, and Rav** agrees **with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yannai, and Rabbi Yannai with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Ḥiyya with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Meir with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said: A person can transfer** to another **an entity that has not** yet **come into the world.** This shows that Rabbi Akiva indeed maintains that one can acquire something that does not yet exist.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak presents a remarkable chain of tradition: *Rav Huna ke-Rav, ve-Rav ke-Rabbi Yannai, ve-Rabbi Yannai ke-Rabbi Hiyya, ve-Rabbi Hiyya ke-Rebbe, ve-Rebbe ke-Rabbi Meir, ve-Rabbi Meir ke-Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, ve-Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov ke-Rabbi Akiva* — each figure in order of *rav* and *talmid*, from *Amoraim* back through *Tannaim*, all sharing the same fundamental position: *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*, that one may transact on something not yet in existence, with the transaction taking effect when the necessary conditions are fulfilled. It is worth noting that Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak is not claiming that every case in this chain is literally identical; rather, they are related positions grouped under this broad principle.

### Rav Huna Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא מַאי הִיא? דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת דֶּקֶל לַחֲבֵרוֹ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם — יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם — אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אַף מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לָעוֹלָם — יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מוֹדֵינָא דְּאִי שָׁמֵיט וְאָכֵיל — לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara specifies the particular contexts in which the aforementioned opinions, all of which concur, were issued: **Rav Huna, what is** the source for his ruling? **As it was stated:** With regard to **one who sells the fruit of a palm tree to another** before the fruit has grown, **Rav Huna said: Until** the fruit **has come into the world, he can retract** the sale, as it has yet to take effect. However, **after** the fruit **has come into the world, he can no** longer **retract,** despite the fact the fruit had not yet sprouted when he made the acquisition. **And Rav Naḥman said: Even after they have come into the world he can retract,** as the acquisition was defective from the outset. He maintains that one cannot transfer ownership of an entity that does not yet exist. **Rav Naḥman said:** Even so, **I concede that if** the buyer **seizes** the fruit **and consumes** it, the court **does not remove them from him,** because despite the faulty acquisition he was promised a sale of fruit.

What is Rav Huna's case? The *gemara* records a dispute regarding one who sells the fruit of his palm tree to another before those fruit have come into existence. Rav Huna holds: *ad shelo ba la'olam yachol lachzor bo* — until the fruit comes into existence, either party may retract. But *misheba la'olam eino yachol lachzor bo* — once the fruit exists, neither party may retract; the transaction takes effect at that moment. Rav Nachman disagrees, holding that even once the fruit exists, the seller may retract, since the transaction never took legal effect.

Rav Nachman adds a concession: *modina d'ee shemit ve'achil lo mafkinan minei* — if the purchaser actually seized and consumed the fruit, we do not force him to pay it back. The basis for this concession is not entirely clear — it may reflect a principle that we do not disturb an already-consummated state of affairs even if the underlying transaction was legally invalid, or it may reflect some residual social enforceability of the agreement. In any event, the clear expression of Rav Huna's view is that *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* and the transaction takes effect when the object comes into the world.

### Rav Field Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב — דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵרוֹ ״שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁאֲנִי לוֹקֵחַ לִכְשֶׁאֶקָּחֶנָּה, קְנוּיָה לְךָ מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ — קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the proof that **Rav** also accepts the ruling that one can acquire an entity that does not yet exist, **as Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **one who says to another: This field that I am** about to **buy, when I buy it, it is acquired by you from now,** the addressee **has acquired** the field, despite the fact that it did not belong to the speaker at the time of his statement.

Rav's position is transmitted by Rav Huna in Rav's name — itself an illustration of the chain — and concerns a more complex case: *ha'omer le-chavero sadeh zo she'ani lokeach, lich'she'ekachenah knuyah lach me'achshav* — one who says to his fellow: "This field that I intend to purchase, when I purchase it, it shall be yours as of now." Rav rules *kana* — it works. When the seller subsequently acquires the field, it automatically transfers to the purchaser, even though at the time of the sale the seller did not own it.

Here several layers of complexity are present. This is not merely a case of an object that does not yet exist in the world; the object — the field — does exist. The seller simply does not own it. The *gemara* is placing these cases together under the umbrella of related positions, even while recognizing that they are not identical.

### Me'achshav

The word *me'achshav* — "as of now" — in the formulation *lich'she'ekachenah knuyah lach me'achshav* is given its own analysis. Tosafos explains that *me'achshav* serves a specific legal function: it ensures that the *maaseh kinyan* — the act of acquisition performed at the time of the sale — retains its legal force even if, by the time the seller actually acquires the field, the physical instrument of that *kinyan* (such as a *shtar*) has been destroyed. Ordinarily, one might argue that some remnant of the *maaseh kinyan* must still be present when the object comes into the buyer's control. If I gave you money and the field is acquired on Friday, you still hold the money (or owe it back), so the *kinyan* retains its foothold. But if I gave you a *shtar* and it was subsequently torn up, there may be nothing left of the act when the field is actually acquired. The word *me'achshav* — "as of now" — lends sufficient legal weight to the original act that the transaction takes effect upon acquisition even without a surviving instrument.

Crucially, the transfer of title to the buyer occurs when the seller acquires the field, not retroactively at the time of the sale. The buyer does not own the field earlier than Friday and does not benefit from any fruit produced before that point.

One might observe the proximity of *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* to *breira* — the principle that a current act may be clarified retroactively. These are distinct concepts. *Breira* operates retroactively once facts clarify themselves. *Adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* is not retroactive at all: the transaction does not take effect earlier than the moment when the conditions are met. Until *chalitzah* is performed, the *kiddushin* has no effect; once *chalitzah* is performed, it takes effect at that future time.

### Rabbi Yannai Sharecrop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַנַּאי כְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא — דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ אֲרִיסָא דַּהֲוָה מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ כַּנְתָּא דְפֵירֵי כׇּל מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא. הָהוּא יוֹמָא נְגַהּ לֵיהּ וְלָא אֲתָא. שְׁקַל עַשַּׂר מִפֵּירֵי דְבֵיתֵיהּ עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai** also agrees **with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya, as** demonstrated by the following episode: **Rabbi Yannai had a sharecropper** working his land **who would bring him a basket [*kanta*] of fruit every Shabbat eve. One day he was late and did not come.** Rabbi Yannai **took tithe from the fruit in his house for** the fruit he expected to receive. He did this in case the fruit arrived near the beginning of Shabbat, as one may not tithe on Shabbat. However, Rabbi Yannai was uncertain whether it is indeed possible to separate tithes for an entity that has not yet reached one's possession.

Rabbi Yannai's case follows: *de-Rabbi Yannai hava lei arisa de-hava maytei lei kinta de-peirei kol ma'alei shabta* — Rabbi Yannai had a sharecropper who would bring him a basket of fruit every *erev Shabbos*. On one occasion the sharecropper was delayed and *Shabbos* was approaching. Rabbi Yannai wished to eat the fruit on *Shabbos* but could not separate *tithes* on *Shabbos* itself, and the fruit had not yet arrived. He therefore took *ma'aser* from fruit in his own house *on behalf of* the fruit that would arrive — fruit not yet in his possession. This is parallel to *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*: he is designating *ma'aser* for produce that is not yet in his control and perhaps not even legally his.

### Rabbi Hiyya Dream

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ. דְּתַנְיָא: ״לְמַעַן תִּלְמַד לְיִרְאָה אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ כׇּל הַיָּמִים״ — אֵלּוּ שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** He did this in case the fruit arrived near the beginning of Shabbat, as one may not tithe on Shabbat. However, Rabbi Yannai was uncertain whether it is indeed possible to separate tithes for an entity that has not yet reached one's possession. He therefore **came before Rabbi Ḥiyya** to inquire whether his separation of tithes was effective. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: You acted well, as it is taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to a verse that discusses tithes: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God…**in order that you should learn to fear the Lord your God all the days"** (Deuteronomy 14:23). With regard to the emphasis of "all," **these are *Shabbatot* and Festivals.**

Rabbi Yannai was uncertain whether his action was valid and went to ask Rabbi Hiyya. Rabbi Hiyya said to him: *shapir avadt* — you did well. The *gemara* cites a *braita*: the verse *lema'an tilmad leyira es Hashem Elokecha kol ha-yamim* — "in order that you shall learn to fear Hashem your God all the days" — teaches that even on *Shabbatot* and *Yamim Tovim* one takes *ma'aser*. The question is what practical ruling this verse comes to allow on *Shabbos*, since separating *ma'aser* is in any case only a rabbinic prohibition and a scriptural verse would not be needed to override a rabbinic ruling. The *gemara* concludes that the verse comes to validate a case precisely like Rabbi Yannai's: one who takes *ma'aser she-lo min ha-mukaf* — not with all the produce physically present in one location — because circumstances compelled it. Despite the technical deficiency that the designated *ma'aser* and the produce it covers were not in the same place, the designation is valid.

Rabbi Yannai then tells Rabbi Hiyya: but in a dream they showed me the verse *kaneh ratzutz* — a weak reed — suggesting that what I did was unreliable, like resting one's weight on a broken reed (*hinei vatachta lecha al mish'enet ha-kaneh ha-ratzutz ha-zeh*). Rabbi Hiyya responds: they were citing you a different verse with the same phrase — *kaneh ratzutz lo yishbor, u-fishtah kehah lo yichebenah* — a bent reed shall not be broken, and a dimly burning wick shall not be extinguished. In other words, the heavenly message was one of reassurance, not rebuke: something that appears fragile and questionable is in fact sound and will not break.

This little narrative carries a significant implicit message about *halachic* methodology. The image of the flexible reed that does not snap — as opposed to something rigid and brittle — points toward a posture of finding valid grounds for leniency rather than mechanically rejecting what is imperfect. One is reminded of the well-known principle of *hanach lahem le-Yisrael im einam nevi'im hem b'nei nevi'im hem* — if they are not prophets, they are the children of prophets — where the question among later *acharonim* is whether a certain *rishon* was invoking the principle of *mutav sheyihyu shogegim* (tolerated inadvertent transgression) or the principle that the Jewish people intuited correctly. The *kaneh ratzutz* image here functions similarly: it can be read in either direction, and the proper reading depends on where the verse ends.

### Rebbe Slave Freedom

Rebbe's position is derived from a *braita* interpreting the verse *lo tasgir eved el adonav* — do not hand over a slave to his master. Rebbe reads this as referring to one who purchases a slave from another master on the condition of freeing him. Rebbe's reading implies that the act of manumission — the *shichrur* — takes effect at the moment of purchase. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak specifies the mechanism: *de-chtav lei lich'she'ekachecha harei atzmecha kanuy lach me'achshav* — the purchaser wrote a writ of manumission stating "when I purchase you, you own yourself as of now." Here again the *me'achshav* formulation is used, and the act of freedom takes effect upon acquisition even though the document was written when the seller had no ownership.

### Rabbi Meir Conditional

Rabbi Meir's position is drawn from the *mishnah* already cited above: *ha'omer le'isha harei at mekudeshet li le'achar she'agayer* etc. — where he rules *mekudeshet*, in contrast to the *tanna kamma* who rules *einah mekudeshet*. Rabbi Meir holds that where the condition (*le'achar she-yachalotz yevamayich*, for instance) is later fulfilled, the *kiddushin* that was performed before those conditions were satisfied retroactively or prospectively takes effect. This is the direct application of *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* to *kiddushin*.

### Rabbi Elazar Terumah

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov is cited from a *braita*: *afilu amar peirot arugah zu tlushin yihyu terumah al peirot arugah mechubarim* — even if one says that the picked fruit of this row shall be *terumah* on the attached fruit of the row, or conversely that the attached fruit shall be *terumah* on the picked fruit — to take effect when the attached fruit has ripened to a third and been picked — *devarav kayamim*, his words stand. One performs an act of designation for produce that does not yet meet the conditions required for *terumah*, and when those conditions are met, the designation takes effect. This is another variant of the broad principle.

### Rabbi Akiva Earnings

Finally, Rabbi Akiva's case returns us to the *mishnah* about *konam she'ani osa leficha* — the woman who makes her future earnings *assur* to her husband. Rabbi Akiva holds *yafer* — the husband must annul the *neder* — because she may produce in excess of what belongs to him, and a *neder* on non-existent future labor is valid. This is the *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* in the domain of *nedarim*. (Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua's interpretation — that she is actually consecrating her existing hands and not the non-existent fruit — was flagged earlier as creating tension with this chain, since Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explicitly includes Rav Huna in the list of those who affirm *adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam*.)

## One Witness Yibum

### Why Believe Ed Echad

The *gemara* opens a new inquiry: Rav Sheshet asks *ed echad b'yevamah mahu* — what is the *halacha* regarding one witness testifying for the purpose of *yibum*? That is, if a single witness testifies that a woman's husband has died, thereby allowing her to perform *yibum* with her *yavam*, do we accept that testimony? This question forces an explicit articulation of the foundations upon which we credit the testimony of a single witness (*ed echad*) in matters of marital status — a question central to the entire *perek*.

Two theories are advanced. The first: *ta'ama d'ed echad mishum d'milta d'avida l'igluye lo meshaker* — the reason we believe a single witness is that such matters have a way of coming to light. Because the truth about a husband's death will eventually emerge, the witness will not risk the disgrace of being exposed as a liar. This rationale operates independently of the woman; it is a feature of the social and epistemic environment. If this is the *ta'am*, it should apply equally to testimony enabling *yibum*: what is known will come to light, and the witness will not lie.

### Woman Loves Brother

The second theory: *ta'ama d'ed echad mishum d'hi daika u-minsva* — the reason we believe a single witness is that the woman herself will investigate the claim before proceeding to marry. Her personal stake in not unwittingly marrying a man whose first husband is alive motivates her to verify the testimony independently. If *this* is the *ta'am*, a question arises for the *yibum* scenario: *ve-hacha kivvan d'zimnin d'rachamah lei, lo daika u-minsva* — since she may have feelings for the *yavam*, her motivation to investigate might be compromised. She might not investigate as thoroughly as she ought to, precisely because she is eager to proceed with *yibum*.

### Mishnah Evidence

Rav Sheshet answers his own question: *tanitu'a* — we have already learned this. He points to the *mishnah*: *amru lah mes banayich ve'achar kach mes ba'alayich ve-nisyabma* — they told her your sons died and then your husband died, and she performed *yibum*; and then *amru lah chiluf ha-devarim* — they said it was the reverse — and the *mishnah* rules that she must leave the *yavam* and the children are *mamzerim*.

The *gemara* asks: what is the scenario? If the first testimony was two witnesses and the second testimony was also two witnesses, why would the second override the first? And furthermore, the ruling of *mamzer* would then be problematic — since there are conflicting testimonies, the child should at most be a *safek mamzer*. Yet the *mishnah* rules *mamzer* definitively and is precise about which children are *mamzerim* and which are not. Therefore, the *mishnah* must be describing a case where the first testimony that permitted *yibum* was given by a *single* witness, which was then contradicted by two witnesses. *Ve-ta'ama de-asu betrei akachshuha* — the reason the second report prevailed was only that two witnesses contradicted the single one. *Ha lav hachi ne'eman* — had there been no such contradiction, the testimony of the single witness would have been sufficient to permit *yibum*.

### Conclusion Ed Echad

The proof is thus established: a single witness is believed to permit *yibum*, even in circumstances where the woman might have an incentive to remarry her *yavam* and therefore might not investigate with full diligence. Whether this follows because *milta d'avida l'igluye* operates independently of the woman's investigation, or whether we simply do not treat the possibility of emotional attachment to the *yavam* as a sufficient reason to distrust her investigation — the practical conclusion is unambiguous. An *ed echad* is believed for *yibum* just as he is believed for ordinary remarriage.

The *gemara* will continue this discussion in the next *daf*, where it bears directly on the broader question — central to the entire *perek* — of why we credit a single witness at all in matters of marital status.
