# Bava Metzia 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Objects in Walls

### Rusted Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּשְׁתִיךְ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **where** the item is **extremely rusted,** indicating that it had been left there for a long time.

The *mishna* on daf 26a continues its discussion of objects found under rubble or within walls. As established by the *gemara* earlier, when an object is found in an old wall in a state of extreme rust, one may conclude that it was placed there even by the previous inhabitants of the land — the rust itself is evidence of abandonment, and we therefore treat it as a case where *ye'ush* has certainly occurred, allowing the finder to keep it.

The focus now turns to what the *mishna* says about a new wall. If the object is found on the half of the wall facing *reshut harabim*, the finder may keep it, since the object is presumed to have been left there by one of the people from the public domain. If, however, it is found on the half of the wall facing inward toward a person's private domain, it belongs to the homeowner — one must assume it was placed there by the owner, and it may not be kept.

### Hinuach vs. Aveidah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוֹתֶל חָדָשׁ, מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלַחוּץ – שֶׁלּוֹ, מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלִפְנִים – שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **one found** lost items **in a new wall from its midpoint and outward,** they belong **to him.** But if he found the items **from its midpoint and inward,** they belong **to the homeowner.**

Rashi notes that even where the object faces the *reshut harabim*, it was presumably placed there intentionally — this would make it a case of *hinuach*, something deliberately set down, not an *aveidah* in the classic sense. Because the *gemara* has already established that we are dealing with a rusted object, we may assume that even what began as *hinuach* has since been forgotten and abandoned, and it may therefore be kept. The inner half is a different matter entirely: if the object always belonged to the owner and is found within his own property, it never attains the status of an *aveidah* at all, even if he forgot about it. It remains his, and the finder has no claim.

### Knife & Pouch Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: סַכִּינָא בָּתַר קַתָּא, וְכִיסָא בָּתַר שִׁנְצֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The determination of ownership with regard to **a knife** found in a wall **follows the handle, and** the determination of ownership with regard to a money **pouch follows the laces** at the opening of the pouch. If the handle or laces face inward, they belong to the homeowner. If the handle or laces face outward, they belong to the finder.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלַחוּץ – שֶׁלּוֹ, מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלִפְנִים – שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. וְלֶחְזֵי, אִי קַתָּא לְגָאו אִי קַתָּא לְבַר! אִי שִׁנְצֵיהּ לְגָאו אִי שִׁנְצֵיהּ לְבַר! מַתְנִיתִין בְּאוּדְרָא וּנְסָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, what is the applicability of the ruling of **the mishna, which teaches:** If one found lost items in a new wall **from its midpoint and outward,** they belong **to him,** and **from its midpoint and inward,** they belong **to the homeowner? But** instead, to determine ownership, **let us see if its handle** faces **inward** or **if its handle** faces **outward,** or **if its straps** face **inward or if its straps** face **outward.** The Gemara answers: **The mishna** is referring to a case **where** one found **rags or metal strips.**

The *gemara* introduces a refinement attributed to Rav Ashi. The text states: *sakina batar kata* — for a knife, one follows the handle. Since a person inserting a knife into a wall would naturally hold it by the handle, the location of the handle determines who placed it there. If the handle faces the interior of the property, the owner of that property is presumed to have placed it; if the handle faces outward, it is presumed to have come from the public domain. Similarly, *vechisa batar shinsei* — a pouch or wallet is carried by its straps, so one follows the straps to determine the direction of entry.

The *gemara* then raises an obvious challenge: if this is so, why does the *mishna* speak of the outer and inner halves of the wall as the criterion? Why not simply examine the direction of the handle or the straps? The answer is straightforward: the knife and pouch rule applies only to those two specific objects. The *mishna* is dealing with cases of *udra venaska* — rags or metal strips, objects that give no indication of the direction in which they were inserted. For such items, one falls back on the spatial criterion of which half of the wall the object occupies.

### Full Wall Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אִם הָיָה כּוֹתֶל מְמוּלָּא מֵהֶן – חוֹלְקִין. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּמְשַׁפַּע בְּחַד גִּיסָא, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִשְׁתְּפוֹכֵי אִישְׁתְּפוּךְ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught: If** the hollow in the **wall was filled with** lost items, e.g., coins, the homeowner and the finder **divide** them. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this only in a case **where the** hollow in the **wall is inclined toward one side** of the wall. **Lest you say** that all the items were initially on the elevated side, and due to the incline they **slipped** and filled the entire space, the *tanna* **teaches us** that the homeowner and the finder divide them.

A *braita* addresses the case where an object fills the entire hole in the wall, occupying both the inner and outer halves simultaneously. Which owner is presumed to have placed it? The rule stated is *cholkin* — one divides. The portion on the outer half belongs to the finder, while the portion on the inner half is treated as belonging to the homeowner. This may seem somewhat arbitrary, since the object clearly entered from one side or the other, but when the matter cannot be resolved, the division is made down the middle.

The *gemara* notes *peshita* — this seems obvious — though one might reasonably question whether it is so obvious, given the arbitrary nature of the split. The *gemara* therefore clarifies that the ruling is necessary for a case where the object is situated at an angle, sloping in one direction toward, say, the *reshut harabim*. One might have thought to apply the logic that it all originated on the homeowner's side and simply rolled downward due to the incline — *ishtapuche ishtapach*. The *gemara* therefore teaches that even in this case, one conceptually divides the object at the midpoint. This is indeed a significant *chiddush*: since we cannot determine the origin, we split the object down the middle, with the outer portion available to the finder and the inner portion left as the presumed property of the *ba'al habayit*.

## Rental Properties

### Rental House Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה מַשְׂכִּירוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים, אֲפִילּוּ מָצָא בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. וְאַמַּאי? לֵיזִיל בָּתַר בָּתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If** the homeowner **would rent** the house **to others** on a regular basis and there was a steady turnover of residents, **even** if one found lost items **inside the house, these** belong **to him.** The Gemara asks: **And why** do they belong to the finder? **Let us follow the last** renter and determine that he is the owner of the items.

The *gemara* continues: *im haya maskir l'acheirim afilu b'toch habayis harei eilu shelo* — if the house was regularly rented out to others, then even if you find something inside the house itself, you may keep it. The scenario envisioned is that of a cleaner or similar person sent into the property who finds, for example, coins left behind. Since the property is a rental that turns over regularly, one cannot attribute the object to any specific person. Without a *siman*, and without any particular reason to assume it belongs to the landlord rather than one of the various tenants, the finder may keep it.

### Chatzer Awareness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא תְּנַן: מָעוֹת שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ לִפְנֵי סוֹחֲרֵי בְּהֵמָה – לְעוֹלָם מַעֲשֵׂר, בְּהַר הַבַּיִת – חוּלִּין;
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 7:2): With regard to **money that was found before animal merchants** in Jerusalem, it is **always** assumed to be money of the second **tithe,** as most of the animals purchased in Jerusalem were bought with second-tithe money. This *halakha* applies both during a Festival and throughout the year, as people would typically purchase animals for meat with their second-tithe money. If the money was found **on the Temple Mount** it is considered **non-sacred** money. This *halakha* applies even during a Festival, when people would come to Jerusalem with second-tithe money in hand, as it can be assumed that one who entered the Temple Mount had already spent that money and only non-sacred money is left in his possession.

This ruling prompts an important question: even granting that the object does not belong to an identifiable tenant, should the landlord not acquire it automatically by virtue of owning the property? A person's *chatzer* — their property or domain — can function as an agent of acquisition on their behalf. Why should the landlord not become the owner of whatever is found on his premises?

Tosafos addresses this question. Tosafos draws from this ruling the principle that *chatzer* cannot acquire an object for its owner in a case where the owner would have no awareness of the object's presence — where it is something that might never be found at all. The relevant language from Tosafos is: *ein chatzer koneh b'davar sheyachol lihyos shelo yimtza'enu l'olam k'mo hacha shehu mutman b'ovi hakosel* — a *chatzer* does not acquire ownership of something that one might never find, as is the case when an object is concealed within the thickness of a wall, or, by extension, left behind in a rented property in circumstances that prevent the landlord from knowing it is there. Tosafos also specifies that the context of the ruling involves an object whose character as a lost item — an *aveidah* — is clear; it is not merely some possession of the owner tucked away in a drawer.

## Majority Principle

### Animal Sellers Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם, בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – חוּלִּין. בִּשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל – הַכֹּל מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And** if the coins were found elsewhere **in Jerusalem,** the following distinction applies: If it was found **during the rest of the days of the year,** it is considered **non-sacred** money. But if the money was found **during the Festival,** when many people would come to Jerusalem with their second-tithe money, **all** money is presumed to be second-**tithe** money.

The *gemara* now poses a challenge to the straightforward reading of the rental property case. Given that the property turns over between tenants, should one not apply the principle of *batar basra* — follow the most recent occupant — and attribute the object to the last renter? To address this, the *gemara* invokes a well-known *mishna* about lost money in Jerusalem.

The *mishna* teaches: *maos shenimtzu lifnei socharei beheima* — money found in front of those who sell animals in Jerusalem is always considered *maaser*, i.e., *maaser sheini* money, regardless of the time of year. The rationale is that people would bring their *maaser sheini* funds to Jerusalem throughout the year to spend on food there, and the primary customers of the animal sellers were those purchasing animals with *maaser sheini* money. Therefore, at any point in the year, the majority of money circulating before the animal sellers was sanctified *maaser sheini* funds, and any coins found there are treated accordingly. Tosafos adds a refinement: one might have thought that money dropped after a purchase was completed would no longer be sanctified, since the sanctity transfers to the animal. But Tosafos notes that the sheer numerical majority of buyers over sellers means we presume the money fell from the buyers, not from the sellers, and thus it retains its status as *maaser sheini*.

### Jerusalem Sweeping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שְׁמַעְיָה בַּר זְעֵירָא: מַאי טַעְמָא? הוֹאִיל וְשׁוּקֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם עֲשׂוּיִן לְהִתְכַּבֵּד בְּכׇל יוֹם. אַלְמָא אָמְרִינַן קַמָּאֵי קַמָּאֵי אֲזַלוּ, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי: קַמָּא קַמָּא אֲזַל, וְהָנֵי דְּבָתְרָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Shemaya bar Ze'eira says** in explanation of the mishna: **What is the reason** that during the rest of the year the money is considered non-sacred, even on the day after the Festival? **Since the markets of Jerusalem tend to be cleaned every day,** any money left there would already have been found by the street cleaners. Consequently, any money found there must have been left there recently. **Apparently, we say** that **each of the first** coins **is gone, and these** coins **are other** ones, i.e., they were left there after the conclusion of the Festival. **Here too,** with regard to lost items found in a rented house, why not say that the items belonging **to each of the first** renters **are gone and these** items belong **to the last** renter?

The *gemara* explains the underlying logic through a statement of Rav Shemaya bar Zeira: *ho'il v'shukei Yerushalayim asuyim l'hitkabed b'chol yom* — because the marketplaces of Jerusalem were swept every single day. This daily sweeping is the key. If a coin had been dropped at an earlier time, it would have been picked up when the streets were swept. Therefore, any coin found now must have been dropped recently. This allows one to draw conclusions based on the present time of year: if it is currently the *regel*, the coin was dropped now, during the *regel*, and is therefore *maaser sheini*; if it is an ordinary time of year, the coin was dropped now, at an ordinary time, and is therefore *chullin*.

### Temple Mount Money

The rule is different for the Temple Mount. There, even during the *regel*, one presumes that found money is *chullin*. Rashi explains this by reference to two features of the Temple Mount. First, it was situated on a slope, so the wind would naturally displace items over time, reducing the need for systematic sweeping. Second, one was prohibited from entering the Temple Mount while dusty, which meant foot traffic was more limited and there was less practical need for daily sweeping. As a result, coins on the Temple Mount could have lain there from any prior time — there is no mechanism, analogous to the daily sweeping of the Jerusalem markets, that would have cleared out earlier coins. One therefore cannot say that a coin found there was dropped recently. The relevant temporal frame is the long term, and over the long term, the majority of money in circulation was *chullin*. Therefore, even during the *regel*, found coins on the Temple Mount are presumed *chullin*.

This principle — that daily sweeping enables one to attribute found objects to the most recent period — is precisely what Rashi applies to the rental property case as well. Just as the streets of Jerusalem were swept daily before each new group of visitors, a renter clears out a room upon leaving, checking under the bed and ensuring nothing has been left behind. Any object found by the cleaner after the last tenant departed is therefore attributable to that last tenant — it is analogous to the *shukei Yerushalayim*. This is exactly why the *gemara* pressed the question: if we can attribute found objects to the most recent party, why does the rental property ruling not simply assign the found object to the last renter?

## Last Renter Problem

### Three Renters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ פּוּנְדָּק לִשְׁלֹשָׁה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said in the name of bar Kappara:** The mishna that states that the item belongs to the finder is referring to a case **where** the homeowner **rendered** his house **an inn [*pundak*] for three Jews.** Since it is unclear to which of them the item belonged, the owner despairs of its recovery.

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, answers that the ruling of the *mishna* involves a specific case: *kegon she'asu pundak lishlosha Yisrael* — the lodging was rented out simultaneously to three people. Since three individuals occupied the space at the same time, there is no single "last person" to whom the object can be attributed. Assuming we are dealing with a case without a *siman*, this is a straightforward scenario of an unidentifiable lost object, and the finder may keep it.

However, the *gemara* then suggests that the case might involve an object with a *siman*, and attempts to derive from this ruling that we follow Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position — namely, that in a place where many people are present, even an object with a *siman* need not be returned, since the owner despairs of recovery given the crowds. This inference, it must be said, is somewhat puzzling, since Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's principle was formulated specifically for *makom sheharabbim metzuyim*, a place where masses of people congregate — not a modest rental room shared by a handful of individuals. Nevertheless, the *gemara* pursues this line of reasoning, suggesting that the three renters constitute a sufficient *rabbim* even among Jews. The *gemara* then rejects this inference and instead attributes the ruling to a case of three non-Jewish renters — *lishlosha ovdei kochavim* — since there is no obligation to return a *metziah* to a non-Jew according to the *ikar halacha*. This resolution is itself somewhat awkward, since it makes the ruling hinge on the identity of the renters rather than on the principle of high turnover that seems to animate the *mishna*. The simpler reading remains that the case involves Jewish renters without a *siman*, and since multiple people occupied the space simultaneously, no one person can be identified as the source of the loss.

### Ye'ush No Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לִשְׁלֹשָׁה יִשְׂרָאֵל, מַאי טַעְמָא? הָהוּא דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ מִיָּאַשׁ, מֵימָר אָמַר: מִכְּדֵי אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא לָא הֲוָה בַּהֲדַי אֶלָּא הָנֵי, אֲמַרִי קַמַּיְיהוּ כַּמָּה זִמְנֵי לַיהְדְּרוּ לִי, וְלָא [אַ]הְדַּרוּ לִי, וְהַשְׁתָּא לַיהְדְּרוּ?! אִי דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ אַהְדְּרוּהָ נִיהֲלִי, וְהַאי דְּלָא אַהְדְּרוּהָ לִי בְּדַעְתַּיְיהוּ לְמִיגְזְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman** said that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Even** if **you say** that the owner **rendered** his house **an inn for three Jews,** one cannot conclude that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar issued his ruling even in an area with a Jewish majority. **What is the reason** that the item belongs to the finder? It is because **the** person **from whom** the item **fell despairs** of its recovery. The one who lost the item **says: Now, no other person was with me** here, **only these** residents of the inn. **I said in their presence several times to return** the item **to me, and they did not return** it **to me; and** is it likely that **now** they are going **to return** it? **If their intention was to return** the item, **they would have** already **returned it to me, and** the fact **that they did not** yet **return it to me** indicates that it is **their intention to rob** me of the item.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabba bar Avuha, goes further: *afilu teima lishlosha Yisrael* — even if the three renters were Jews, the finder need not return the object. The reason is that the person who lost it is certainly *miyayeish* — he has despaired of recovery. The argument runs as follows. The loser knows that no one else was in the room aside from his two co-renters. He has asked them, repeatedly, whether they found his wallet, whether they have it — *amri kammaihu kamma zimni lehadru li* — and they denied it. If they were going to return it, they would have done so already. He therefore concludes that one of them has stolen it and will never admit to it, or that it has simply vanished and is unrecoverable. This despair is qualitatively different from losing an object in a public place where many strangers might find it. In a public setting with a *siman*, a person might reasonably hope that an honest stranger will identify the owner and return it. But here, the loser has already confronted the only people who could know, and they have denied any knowledge. He is therefore definitively *miyayeish*, even if the object carries a *siman*. This is Rav Nachman's reasoning, and it is consistent with a broader principle he articulates about the psychology of *ye'ush* in situations involving known associates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַב נַחְמָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: רָאָה סֶלַע שֶׁנָּפַל מִשְּׁנַיִם חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָהוּא דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ לָא מִיָּאַשׁ, מֵימָר אָמַר: מִכְּדֵי אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא לָא הֲוָה בַּהֲדַאי אֶלָּא הַאי, נָקֵיטְנָא לֵיהּ וְאָמֵינָא לֵיהּ: אַנְתְּ הוּא דִּשְׁקַלְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman follows his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rav Naḥman says:** If one **saw a *sela*** coin **that fell from** one of **two** people, he is **obligated to return** it. **What is the reason? The** person **from whom** the *sela* **fell does not despair** of recovering it. **He says: After all, no other person was with me, only this** one who was with me, as he is unaware that the *sela* was found by a third party. He therefore thinks: **I will seize him and say to him:** It **is you who took it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָהוּא דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ וַדַּאי מִיָּאַשׁ, מֵימָר אָמַר: מִכְּדֵי תְּרֵי הֲווֹ בַּהֲדַאי, אִי נָקֵיטְנָא לְהַאי אָמַר: ״לָא שְׁקַלְתֵּיהּ״, וְאִי נָקֵיטְנָא לְהַאי אָמַר: ״לָא שְׁקַלְתֵּיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **In** a case where the coin fell from one of **three** people, the finder **is not obligated to return** it. **What is the reason? The** person **from whom** the *sela* **fell certainly despairs** of recovering it. **He says: After all, two** other people **were with me. If I seize this** one, he will **say: I did not take it. And if I seize that** one, he will **say: I did not take it.** Since he cannot make a definitive claim, he despairs of recovering his coin.

That broader principle is elaborated immediately. Rav Nachman states: *raah sela shenafal mishnayim* — if one saw two people together and one of them dropped a coin, there is an obligation to return it. The reason is that the person who dropped it is not *miyayeish*. Even when he realizes the coin is gone, his reasoning is: there was only one other person with me — *inish achrina lo hava behadai ella hai* — I will confront him and demand to know whether he has my coin. He does not despair because there is a single identifiable party to hold responsible. The object has not dissolved into the anonymity of the crowd; there is someone who almost certainly either saw it or has it, and there is social pressure available to recover it.

The case with three people is the reverse. If one of three people dropped a coin, one does not have to return it, because the person who lost it is definitely *miyayeish*. His reasoning: there are two others with me — *trei havu behadai* — and whichever one I approach will simply say it wasn't him and point to the other. *Ei naktina lehai amar lo shaktilah, ve'ad naktina lehai amar lo shaktilah* — each one will deny it and redirect me to the third. He therefore concludes that he will never recover it; one of them has taken it and will hide behind the other. This despair is certain, and *ye'ush* is complete.

### Partners Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי דְּאָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה לְכׇל חַד וְחַד, אֲבָל אִית בֵּיהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה לְכׇל חַד וְחַד – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא? אֵימוֹר שׁוּתָּפֵי נִינְהוּ וְלָא מִיָּאֲשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the fact that by Torah law, one must return a lost item to its owner only if it is worth one *peruta*, **Rava said:** With regard to **that which you said,** that **in** a case where the coin fell from one of **three** people the finder **is not obligated to return** it, **we said** this **only** in a case **where** the total value of the lost coin, when divided by three, **does not** amount to the **value of one *peruta* for each and every one** of them; **but** if it amounts to the **value of one *peruta* for each and every one** of them, he is **obligated to return** it. **What is the reason? Say** that perhaps **they are partners,** i.e., they own the coin jointly; consequently, **they do not despair,** as each assumes that one of the other two found it and is holding it for the three of them.

Rava challenges the ruling that three people automatically generates *ye'ush*. He accepts the principle that where *ye'ush* applies, there is no obligation to return the object — but he questions when *ye'ush* can actually be presumed. His argument: *eimar shutfei ninhu velo miyayeish* — perhaps the three people were business partners. If they were partners and the money was jointly owned or carried together, the loser would not despair even if confronted by the dynamic Rav Nachman describes. Partners have a relationship of trust; if my partners have my money, I trust they will return it to me rather than lie about it. Therefore, the calculus of *ye'ush* shifts when there is a possibility of partnership, and the finder may have an obligation to return the object.

### Pruta Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אֶלָּא שָׁוֶה שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר, מַאי טַעְמָא? אֵימוֹר שׁוּתָּפִין נִינְהוּ וְחַד מִנַּיְיהוּ אַחוֹלֵי אַחֲלֵיהּ לִמְנָתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי חַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rava said: Even if its** total **value is only two *perutot*,** which is insufficient to provide each of the three partners with one *peruta*, one is **obligated to return** it. **What is the reason? Say** that perhaps **they are partners and one relinquishes his share to another.** In that case, the remaining two partners each have a one *peruta* share, rendering the finder liable to return it.

Rava therefore qualifies the ruling: *lo amran ella deleis beih shaveh perutah lechol chad vechad* — the principle that one need not return the object applies only when the total value is less than a *perutah* per person. If the object is worth less than three *perutos*, so that each person's share would be under a *perutah*, then there is no obligation even if they are partners, since there is no *chiyuv* to return an *aveidah* worth less than a *perutah* to each owner. But if the object is worth three *perutos* or more — a full *perutah* per person — one must return it, since the possibility of partnership cannot be excluded and *ye'ush* cannot be presumed.

Some authorities (*amri nasha*) report a further refinement by Rava: even if the object is worth only two *perutos*, there remains an obligation to return it. The reasoning: *eima shutfin ninhu, v'chad mineihu achulei achleih l'mansei gabei chavrei* — perhaps all three were partners, and one of them waived his share in favor of the other two. In that scenario, the remaining two partners each retain a stake of a full *perutah*, and there is a *chiyuv* to return it to them. Therefore, the threshold is effectively two *perutos*, not three. Rashi notes the logical possibility that only two of the three were partners and the third was not, though this particular permutation is not developed further by the *gemara*. The operative principle is that wherever a *perutah*-sized stake belonging to a potential partner cannot be ruled out, *ye'ush* is not presumed and the object must be returned.

## Three Intent Scenarios

### Steal Before Ye'ush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: רָאָה סֶלַע שֶׁנָּפְלָה, נְטָלָהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ עַל מְנָת לְגוֹזְלָהּ – עוֹבֵר בְּכוּלָּן: מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תִגְזוֹל״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״הָשֵׁב תְּשִׁיבֵם״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תוּכַל לְהִתְעַלֵּם״. וְאַף עַל גַּב (דַּחֲזָרָה) [דְּאַהְדְּרַהּ] לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ, מַתָּנָה הוּא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ, וְאִיסּוּרָא דַּעֲבַד – עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rava says:** In a case where **one saw a *sela*** coin **that fell** from another, if **he took** the coin **in order to steal it, before** the **despair** of the owner, **he violates all of** the following mitzvot: He is liable **due to** the prohibition: **"You shall not…rob"** (Leviticus 19:13); **and due to** the positive mitzva, stated with regard to found items, of: **"You shall return** them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1), **and due to** the prohibition, stated with regard to one who finds an item: **"You may not disregard"** (Deuteronomy 22:3). **And even if he returned it after** the **despair** of the owner, it is merely **a gift that he gave him; and the transgression that he performed, he performed,** and he remains in violation of these mitzvot.

Rava now articulates a systematic analysis of the *mitzvos* and prohibitions implicated in the finding of a lost object, depending on the finder's intent and the timing of *ye'ush*. This is a foundational passage for understanding the structure of *hashavas aveidah*.

The first scenario: *raah sela shenafala, natala lifnei ye'ush al m'nas l'gozlah* — one sees a coin drop and picks it up before *ye'ush* with the explicit intent to steal it. In this case, *avar b'chulhu* — one has transgressed all applicable prohibitions. First, *mishum lo sigzol* — one has violated the prohibition against theft, since the object belongs to another and is being deliberately appropriated. Second, *mishum hasheiv teshiveim* — one has failed to fulfill the positive commandment of returning a lost object, *hashavas aveidah*. Third, *mishum lo suchal l'hisalem* — one has transgressed the negative prohibition of turning away from a lost object, the prohibition against abandoning one's obligation to return it.

The *gemara* adds that even if the thief later returns the object after *ye'ush*, *matana b'alma hu d'yahiv leih v'issura d'avad avad* — he is merely giving a gift, and the transgression he committed remains. This line is noted by the *rishonim* as having been appended by the *geonim* rather than being original to Rava's statement, and it raises a genuine difficulty: if one steals an object and later returns it, does that not fulfill the positive commandment of *hashavas gezeilah* and perhaps also *hashavas aveidah*?

Tosafos engages this question directly. The *lav* of *lo sigzol* is connected to the positive commandment of return — it is *nitak l'asei*. If so, why should returning the object after *ye'ush* be regarded as merely a gift, without fulfilling the *mitzvah*? Tosafos answers that the phrase about giving a gift applies specifically to the prohibition of *lo suchal l'hisalem*. The negative prohibition of not turning away — the obligation not to abandon a lost object — was violated at the moment of abandonment, and that transgression is not rectified by the later return. The positive commandment of *hashavas aveidah* and the obligation of *hashavas gezeilah*, however, would indeed be fulfilled by returning the object, even belatedly.

The *Baal HaMaor* takes a different and more creative approach: after *ye'ush*, if we rule that *ye'ush* is *koneh* — that despair transfers ownership — then one cannot fulfill the *mitzvah* of *hashavas aveidah* through the return, since the object now legally belongs to the finder. This is an unconventional position.

The Ramban offers perhaps the most sophisticated analysis. He begins by articulating why *ye'ush* does not normally help after an object has already been picked up with the intent to return it. When a finder lifts an *aveidah* with the intention of returning it, he becomes a *shomer* — a guardian — on behalf of the owner. In this capacity he is, in effect, a *yad be'alim*, an extension of the owner's hand. The object is treated as already within the owner's constructive possession. When the owner then becomes *miyayeish*, the *ye'ush* is ineffective, because the owner does not realize the object is already in sympathetic hands — he is despairing of something he would not despair of if he knew the truth.

But in the case where the object was picked up with the intent to steal, the finder is not operating as a *shomer* for the owner. The object is not in the owner's constructive domain. When the owner becomes *miyayeish*, the *ye'ush* therefore takes full legal effect — even though the object was physically lifted before *ye'ush*, the finder's hostile intent means the owner's subsequent despair is operative, and the finder acquires the object. If the finder then returns it, he is returning something that has already become his own, and the *mitzvah* of *hashavas aveidah* cannot be fulfilled by returning one's own property. The Ramban acknowledges that this is a creative reading, and the *pshat* of the *gemara* is as Tosafos describes: returning the object does fulfill *hashavas aveidah* and *hashavas gezeilah*, and the phrase about "merely giving a gift" is limited to the irremedied transgression of *lo suchal l'hisalem*.

### Change Mind After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְטָלָהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ עַל מְנָת לְהַחְזִירָהּ, וּלְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ נִתְכַּוֵּין לְגוֹזְלָהּ – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״הָשֵׁב תְּשִׁיבֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: If **he took** the coin **in order to return it, before** the **despair** of the owner, **and** then, **after** the **despair** of the owner, **he intended to steal it; he violates** a commandment, due to his failure to fulfill the positive mitzva of: **"You shall return** them to your brother." He does not violate the prohibition: "You shall not…rob," because at the time he took the coin he did not intend to keep it. And he does not violate the prohibition: "You may not disregard," because he did not disregard the lost item. He took it with the intention of returning it.

The second scenario: *natul lifnei ye'ush al m'nas l'hachzirah, ul'achar ye'ush niskayvein l'gozlah* — one picked up the object before *ye'ush* with the intent to return it, a thoroughly proper act, but subsequently — after *ye'ush* has already occurred — changed one's mind and decided to keep it. In this case, *over mishum hasheiv teshiveim* alone — one transgresses only the failure to fulfill the positive commandment of returning the *aveidah*.

Why does one not also transgress *lo sigzol* and *lo suchal l'hisalem*? Rashi suggests that one cannot steal something already in one's possession. Tosafos challenges this: the principle that one who wrongfully withholds a worker's wages transgresses *lo sigzol* even while holding the money already demonstrates that *lo sigzol* can be violated with respect to objects in one's possession. Tosafos's answer is that the decisive factor here is timing: the decision to steal came only after *ye'ush*. At that post-*ye'ush* moment, the object has become so detached from the original owner's sphere that withholding it does not rise to the level of *lo sigzol*. Similarly, *lo suchal l'hisalem* — the prohibition against turning away — applies to the initial moment of encountering a lost object and failing to pick it up for return. Here, the finder did pick it up to return it. That initial act was proper. The later change of heart does not retroactively constitute *lo suchal l'hisalem*, which addresses the threshold decision of whether to engage with the *aveidah* at all.

### Wait for Ye'ush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִמְתִּין לָהּ עַד שֶׁנִּתְיָאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים וּנְטָלָהּ, אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תוּכַל לְהִתְעַלֵּם״ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** If **he waited until the owner despaired** of recovering the lost item **and** only then **took it, he violates** a commandment, but **only** due to his failure to fulfill the positive mitzva of: **"You may not disregard,"** as he took no action to return the lost item to its owner.

The third scenario: *himtin lah ad shenisyayshu habalim* — one saw the object drop, had the opportunity to pick it up and return it before *ye'ush*, but waited deliberately until the owner had despaired, and only then picked it up. Here, *eino over ella mishum lo suchal l'hisalem bilvad* — one transgresses only the prohibition of *lo suchal l'hisalem*.

The analysis is straightforward. One did not transgress *hasheiv teshiveim* — the positive commandment to return — because that commandment is triggered by picking up the object; since one had not yet picked it up, there was no active obligation to return it. One did not transgress *lo sigzol* — after *ye'ush*, one was legally permitted to take the object. But by waiting and watching while the owner stood before one in a state of uncertainty and loss, by doing nothing when one could have intervened to return the object before *ye'ush*, one violated the prohibition of *lo suchal l'hisalem* — the commandment not to turn away, not to abandon one's responsibility in the face of another's loss.

These three scenarios represent a precise mapping of how the prohibitions and positive commandments of *hashavas aveidah* — *hasheiv teshiveim*, *lo suchal l'hisalem*, and *lo sigzol* — interact differently depending on the sequence of intent and *ye'ush*.

## Lost on Beach

### Beach Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּחָזֵי דִּנְפוּל זוּזֵי מֵחַבְרֵיהּ בֵּי חָלָתָא וְאַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ וְשַׁקְלֵיהּ – לָא מִיחַיַּיב לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ, מַאי טַעְמָא? הָהוּא דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ מִיָּאַשׁ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** In the case of **this** person **who saw that a dinar** coin **fell from another into the sand, and** then he **found it and took it,** he **is not obligated to return it to** its owner. **What is the reason?** The reason is that **the one from whom the money fell despairs** of finding it.

Rava presents a striking case: *hai man dechazi denafal zuzei mechavrei becholasa* — one who sees his fellow drop coins in the sand, on the beach. He finds them and takes them. *Lo mechayav l'ahadorei lei* — he does not have to return them. The reason is *hu denafal minei mi'ayeish* — the moment the owner dropped the coins in the sand, he despaired. The nature of the beach — shifting sand, the near-impossibility of locating a small object in an undifferentiated expanse — produces immediate *ye'ush* the moment the object is lost.

### Sieve Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל גַּב דְּחַזְיֵיהּ דְּאַיְיתִי אַרְבְּלָא וְקָא מְרַבֵּל, מֵימָר אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּנְפוּל מִינַּאי דִּידִי הָכִי נְפוּל מֵאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא וּמַשְׁכַּחְנָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Even if** the finder **sees that** the owner **brought a sifter and is sifting** through the sand, ostensibly indicating that he did not despair of finding his coin, perhaps the owner **is saying: Just as** a coin **fell from me** in the sand, **so too,** a coin **fell from another person and I will find some item** to offset my loss.

The real *chiddush* follows. What if, after dropping the coins, the owner returns with a sieve and begins sifting through the sand methodically, clearly in an effort to recover what he lost? Does this active search demonstrate that *ye'ush* never in fact occurred — that the owner retained hope of recovery and therefore never despaired?

Rava rules that even this does not negate the *ye'ush*. The owner's reasoning, when he comes back with the sieve, is not that he expects to find his own lost coins: *ki heichi denafal minai didi hachi nafal me'inash achrina umeshachana midi* — just as I dropped something in the sand, others have dropped things in the sand too. I will come with my sieve and my metal detector, and even if I do not find my own item, I will find someone else's and thereby make up my loss. The very act of returning to sift is not evidence of confidence in recovering one's own object; it is evidence of a general hope to find *something* of value in the sand, using one's own loss as the occasion for a broader search. This reasoning is decisive: the sieve-and-sifting does not demonstrate that *ye'ush* was absent, and the finder therefore acquires the coins.
