# Avodah Zarah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## God's Judgment Nations

### Torah vs. Frivolous Talk

We are picking up at the bottom of 3b, deep in the middle of the *agadata*. The preceding sugya addressed how the nations will attempt to justify their actions before God, who will ultimately dismiss their claims as self-serving. God then gives them a final opportunity — the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* — which they fail entirely, and God laughs at them. The digression on divine laughter having concluded, the *gemara* now resumes with *Amar Rabbi Levi*.

### R. Levi: Punishment

Rabbi Levi teaches: *kol haposek midivrei Torah ve'osek bedivrei sicha, machilin lo gachalei resamim* — whoever interrupts words of *Torah* and involves himself in frivolous talk will be fed burning coals. The prooftext is *hakotfim maluach alei siach* — those who pluck *maluach*, which Rashi understands not in its literal sense of something salted, but as a reference to the *mei luchot*, the words inscribed on the tablets. The phrase *alei siach* is read homiletically as "for frivolous talk." The verse concludes: *veshoresh resamim lachmam* — the root of those burning coals will be their bread.

### Torah at Night

The *gemara* continues: *Amar Reish Lakish, kol ha'osek baTorah balayla, HaKadosh Baruch Hu moshech alav chut shel chesed bayom* — anyone who engages in *Torah* study at night, God draws over him a thread of *chesed* by day. The prooftext is *yomam yetzaveh Hashem chasdo uvalayla shiro imi* — by day God will command His kindness, and at night His song is with me. The verse was already cited earlier in reference to what God does at night — listening to the song of the heavenly angels. The *gemara* reads it now as asking: why does God grant a person favor in the eyes of others during the day? *Mishum devalayla shiro imo* — because at night His song is with him, meaning the person's *Torah* study.

There is something quite beautiful in the characterization of *Torah* as a *shira*, a song — a mode of expressing one's connection to God, a source of joy and praise. This idea is one that Rav Soloveitchik developed at great length. There is also a striking practical dimension: ordinarily, someone who stays up studying through the night looks haggard and exhausted the next morning. Here the *gemara* inverts that: the one who stays up learning *Torah* will glow, will find that others look upon him with admiration. The deeper point is that nighttime is unstructured, unobserved time. What a person does in those quiet hours reveals what he is truly about — whether he reaches for *Torah* or for distraction.

### Fish Metaphor

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: what is meant by the verse *vata'aseh adam kedigei hayam*, and he made humans like the fish of the sea, like crawling things that have no ruler over them? Why are human beings compared to fish of the sea? *Lomar lach* — to teach you that just as fish, the moment they are lifted out of the water onto dry land, immediately die, so too human beings: *kecheivan sheporshin midivrei Torah umin hamitzvos miyad meisun* — as soon as they separate themselves from *Torah* and *mitzvos*, they immediately die. This is the classic lesson associated with Rabbi Akiva, who continued learning even after the Roman decrees prohibited it, understanding that to cease learning *Torah* would itself be a form of death — spiritual death.

The theme here is worth pausing over. The tractate of *Avodah Zarah*, in the Tannaitic and biblical periods, was primarily concerned with the literal danger of pagan worship as a seductive force. But by the time of the *gemara*, the concern has shifted. It is now about the commitment to a life of *Torah* and *mitzvos* and the way in which the larger world, by not representing that commitment, can draw a person away from it.

## Sun Righteous Wicked

### Sun Health Advice

The *gemara* offers a second interpretation of the fish metaphor: *mah dagim shebayam keivan shekadrah aleihem chamah miyad meisim* — just as fish, when the sun beats down upon them, die immediately, so too human beings. This is presumably a reference to fish that have already been taken out of the water — the sun and exposure combining to destroy them. The *gemara* then offers two readings of this second metaphor. The first: *ei ba'is eima b'olam hazeh*, this applies in the present world, in line with the teaching of Rabbi Chanina.

Rabbi Chanina states: *hakol biydei shamayim chutz mitzinim u'pachim* — everything is in the hands of heaven except cold and heat, as it is written *tzinim u'pachim bederech ikeish, shomer nafsho yirchak meihem* — cold and heat lie along the crooked path; one who guards himself will distance himself from them. Tosafot explains that certain forces — hurricanes, tsunamis, certain illnesses — are decreed from above and offer the individual no recourse. But heat and cold are different: they are dangers a person can identify and take measures to avoid. If it will be hot, one seeks shade or cooling; if cold, one dresses warmly. The mention of exposure to the sun therefore serves as straightforward health advice. Know that you are vulnerable to the elements. Take care of yourself.

### Future Sun Judgment

The second reading: *v'ei ba'is eima l'olam haba*, this may refer instead to the world to come, in line with the teaching of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish. *Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: ein Gehennom le'asid lavo* — there will be no *Gehennom* in the future time. Instead, God will remove the sun from its sheath — from its protective covering. Presently we do not receive the sun at full intensity. In the time to come, God will unleash it entirely. For the wicked — *reshaim nidonin bah* — they will be scorched and destroyed by it. For the righteous — *tzaddikim misrapin bah* — that very same sun will bring healing.

### Destruction Healing

The *gemara* brings the prooftexts for both. For the wicked: *ki hinei hayom ba bo'er katanur, v'hayu kol zeidim v'chol osei rish'ah kash* — behold a day is coming burning like a furnace, and all the arrogant and all those who do wickedness will be stubble. *V'lo ya'azov lahem shoresh v'anaf* — no root in this world and no branch in the world to come. For the righteous: *v'zorcha lachem yirei shemi shemesh tzedakah u'marpei bichnafeha* — and the sun of righteousness will shine for those who fear My name, with healing in its wings. The identical phenomenon — the full, unsheathed power of the sun — produces entirely opposite experiences depending on who you are.

There is a famous parable illustrating precisely this point: the *gan eden* and *gehinnom* of the next world are the same hall, the same feast, the same abundance. The difference is that in *gehinnom* everyone tries to feed himself with arms that cannot bend; in *gan eden* everyone feeds his neighbor. It is one reality, experienced according to what one has become.

The *gemara* adds: *v'lo od ela shemisyadnin bah* — and not only will the righteous be healed, they will be pampered by the sun of the future time, as it says *v'yatzasem ufishtem k'eglei marbeik* — you will go out and spread out like the calves of the stall, Rashi understanding this to mean that they will be fattened, flourishing in full bodily pleasure.

## Government Law Fear

### Fish Swallow Gov't

*Davar acher* — a third explanation of the fish metaphor: *kol hagadol mechaveiro bolei'a es chaveiro* — every larger fish swallows the smaller one. So too with human beings: *ilmalei mora'ah shel malchus, kol hagadol mechaveiro bolei'a es chaveiro* — were it not for the fear of the government, the stronger would simply devour the weaker. It would be a completely predatory, lawless world.

### Prayer Gov't

This teaching connects directly to the *mishna* in *Avos*: *Rabbi Chanina segan hakohanim omer, hevei mispallel bishlomah shel malchus* — pray for the welfare of the government. *She'ilmalei mora'ah shel malchus, ish es rei'ehu chayim bela'u* — for were it not for the fear of the government, one man would swallow his neighbor alive. This *tefillah lishlom hamalchus* has been a persistent feature of Jewish liturgical history throughout the generations, and the *gemara* here roots it in a foundational recognition: structured authority, however imperfect, is what prevents total social collapse.

## God Strength Anger

### R. Chanina Strength

Rabbi Chanina bar Papa raises a contradiction. One verse states *Shaddai lo m'tzanuhu sagi koach* — the Almighty, we have not found Him great in strength. Yet other verses say *gadol adoneinu v'rav koach* — great is our Lord and abundant in strength — and *yeminecha Hashem nedari bakoach* — Your right hand, O Lord, is glorified in strength. Which is it?

### Judgment War

*Lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The first verse speaks of the time of judgment — *b'shas din* — while the others speak of the time of war — *b'shas milchamah*. When God sits in judgment, He does not bring His full strength to bear. This connects to the earlier discussion: God sits in judgment and then, recognizing that the world cannot survive strict justice, rises and moves to the seat of compassion, restraining Himself. But at the time of war and punishment of the wicked — as at *Shiras Hayam* — God acts with the full force of His power.

### R. Chama Anger

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina raises a parallel contradiction. One verse states *cheimah ein li* — I have no anger. Another says *nokem Hashem uva'al cheimah* — God takes vengeance and is full of anger. *Lo kashya.*

### Israel Nations

The contradiction is resolved by context: *kan b'Yisrael, kan b'ovdei kochavim* — the former verse concerns God's disposition toward Israel, the latter toward the nations. This echoes the central theme of the entire *agadata*: the differential way in which God relates to Israel versus the nations, in judgment and in punishment.

### R. Papa Oath

Rabbi Chanina bar Papa offers an alternative resolution. *Cheimah ein li* — I have no anger — means: yes, God is by nature *ba'al cheimah*, filled with the disposition toward anger, as the verse elsewhere states. But *cheimah ein li* — I have set my anger aside. *Shekvar nishbati* — because I have taken an oath. Rashi explains: God acknowledges the anger as real and perhaps even justified, but has voluntarily foresworn acting upon it. *Mi eini shelo nishbati, ashmeyah beshait* — had I not taken that oath, I would have already brought destruction upon the world. It is the oath — not the absence of anger — that preserves creation.

## Nations Future Exam

### R. Alexandri Destroy

Rabbi Alexandri asks: what is meant by the verse *v'haya bayom hahu avakesh l'hashmid et kol hagoyim* — and it will be on that day that I will seek to destroy all the nations? The question the *gemara* raises is almost amusing in its precision: *avakesh mimi* — God will *seek*? From whom does God need to seek permission? The answer: *avakesh b'pinkas shelahem* — God will search through their records, their historical accounts. *Im yesh lahem zechut, efdem* — if they have merit, He will redeem them. *V'im lav, ashmidem* — and if not, He will destroy them. Of course, as the preceding discussion made clear, when those records are examined, they will not pass muster.

### Rava Judgment

This connects to Rava's teaching on a difficult verse in *Iyov*: *al b'ei yishlach yad, im b'pido lahen shoa* — He does not send His hand against them; if in His *pido* it is their salvation. God says to Israel: *keshe'ani dan et Yisrael, ein ani dan otam k'ovdei kochavim* — when I judge the Jewish people, I do not judge them the way I judge the nations.

### Gradual Destruction

For the nations: *avah avah avah asimenah* — destruction, destruction, destruction — I bring all their punishment at once. But for Israel: *ani nifra mehem k'pid shel tarnegolet* — I exact from them like the pecking of a hen. The punishment comes in tiny installments, distributed over time. Therefore: *lav b'ei yishlach yad* — I do not send my full hand against the Jewish people; *b'pido* — like a light pecking — *shoa* — it becomes their salvation, precisely because it is spread out rather than delivered all at once.

### Small Mitzvot

*Davar acher*: even if the Jewish people perform only a little before God — *k'pid shel tarnegolim* — like the pecking of chickens that peck in the garbage heap — *ani metzarfan l'cheshbon gadol* — I aggregate it all into something significant. *Im b'pido lahen shoa* — even those few scattered good deeds accumulate and become their salvation.

### Payment Sins

*Davar acher*: in the merit of their crying out before God, He saves them. And as Rabbi Abba teaches: *v'anochi efdem vehama dibru alai k'zavim* — I will redeem them, yet they have spoken falsehood about Me. God says: *ani amarti efdem bemamonam b'olam hazeh* — I declared I would redeem them by taking payment from them in this world — that is, their sufferings in this world are God's way of settling accounts, so that they will be fully rewarded in the world to come.

This is the classic rabbinic understanding of the asymmetry between Jewish suffering and non-Jewish prosperity: God pays the nations their reward for whatever good they have done in this world, so that they will have no claim in the next; while for Israel, God collects their debts in this world so their portion in the world to come will be complete and unencumbered. The principle is coherent, but it raises a question that the *gemara* does not entirely resolve: the earlier discussion at least argued on the merits — the nations had their chance and failed, Israel made the right choices. Here, the asymmetry feels less like justice than like a prior determination, a favoritism that precedes the reckoning rather than following from it. The answer may lie in the covenant relationship itself.

*V'heim dibru alai k'zavim* — and yet the Jewish people responded by complaining, by attributing evil to God, not realizing that their suffering was intentional and remedial.

### Afflictions Strength

This is what Rav Papi teaches in the name of Rava: *va'ani yasarti, chizakti z'ro'otam* — I afflicted them and strengthened their arms — *v'elai yachsh'vu ra* — and they considered it evil and thought ill of Me. God says: *ani amarti ayasrim b'yisurin ba'olam hazeh kedai sheyechazku z'ro'otam l'olam haba* — I meant to afflict them in this world in order to strengthen their power in the world to come. It is like being put through a rigorous training regimen: in the moment one curses the trainer, but the result is strength and health. *V'elai yachsh'vu ra* — and instead of understanding this, they attributed evil to Me.

It is worth noting, as pointed out in the scholarly notes of the Soncino edition, that these *drashot* at the opening of *Avodah Zarah* serve not only as a theological framework for understanding the relationship between Israel and the nations. They are also — and perhaps primarily — acts of *nechamah*, consolation, addressed to an oppressed minority living inside a dominant pagan culture, watching the powerful prosper and struggling to understand why. The *Kaddish DeRabbanan*, recited after a *derasha*, includes the phrase *l'eila mikol shirata v'tushbechata v'nechemata* — above all songs and praises and *consolations*. The word *nechemata* appears there because this kind of rabbinic teaching — explaining suffering, promising ultimate reward, reversing the apparent verdict of history — *was* the consolation. Seen through that lens, the opening *agadata* of *Avodah Zarah* is an extended act of comfort to a people who needed it.

## R. Abbahu Heretic

### Abbahu Reputation

*Mistabeach havah Rabbi Abahu l'minei b'Rav Safra* — Rabbi Abbahu had been praising Rav Safra to a certain *min*, a heretic — likely in this context a Jewish Christian — describing him as a great *talmid chacham*. This government official, on the basis of that praise, waived Rav Safra's taxes for thirteen years. Then one day this *min* encountered Rav Safra directly.

### Heretic Challenge

The *min* presented a verse: *rak etchem yadati mikol mishpechot haadamah, al ken efkod aleichem et kol avonoteichem* — only you have I known from all the families of the earth, therefore I will visit upon you all your sins. And he challenged: *man deit lei sanya berachamei masik lei?* — does someone who loves another direct his anger specifically at that beloved? How does God's love for Israel explain the visitation of punishment?

*Ishtik* — Rav Safra was silenced. *Lo hava beyadei lemeimar lei velo midi* — he had nothing to answer. So they threw a cloth around his neck and began to drag him off — he who was supposed to be this great Torah scholar could not explain a simple verse. When Rabbi Abbahu arrived and found what was happening, he said: *amai mitzaaritu lei?* Why are you afflicting him? They told him: you praised him to us as a great *talmid chacham*, and he cannot even explain this verse. Rabbi Abbahu responded: *eima de'amri lachon betana'ei* — when did I say that? I told you he was great in rabbinic literature. *Bekra'ei mi amri lachon?* — I never claimed he could explain biblical verses.

There is something wry and very recognizable in this exchange. Even in the Talmudic period, the *gemara* understood the phenomenon of the *talmid chacham* who knows all of *Shas* but is unfamiliar with a verse of *Tanakh*. The *Rashash*, in his commentary to the opening of *Bava Batra*, notes precisely this point from an incident involving Rebbi: when Rebbi tested someone's credentials, he asked first whether the person had learned *Tanakh* and then whether he had learned *Mishna* — which implies that it was considered possible to have mastered rabbinic literature without having learned *Tanakh*, and that these were treated as distinct bodies of knowledge.

### Scripture Debate

The *min* then asked Rabbi Abbahu: *mai shena atun deyad'itun?* — if that is the case, how is it that *you* know *Tanakh* so well, if the rabbinic world is focused on its own literature? Rabbi Abbahu responded: *anan deshachichan gabayichun* — we are among you, meaning: we regularly have these discourses with you. This strongly suggests that the *minim* here are indeed Jewish Christians, and that the disputations Rabbi Abbahu engaged in were precisely the kind of debate — common throughout the medieval period and apparently already present in the Talmudic era — in which both sides argued from shared scriptures, with Christians attempting to demonstrate that their faith was foretold in the Jewish texts. *Raminan anafshin ume'aynan* — we make the effort to study *Tanakh* thoroughly in order to know how to respond. *Inhu lo me'aynei* — those who remain cloistered in the *yeshiva* have no such need.

### Parable Love

Having established his credentials, Rabbi Abbahu returned to the question. *Emshol lachem mashal* — let me offer a parable. A man has two creditors: one who loves him and one who hates him. The one who loves him allows repayment *me'at me'at* — a little at a time, on an installment plan. The one who hates him demands the full sum at once. Therefore: when God says *because I loved you, I will visit your sins upon you* — it means precisely this: I will take my repayment from you gradually, in small installments, across time. That is the expression of love, not of anger.

## Righteous Suffering

### R. Abba Avraham

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana raises the question from a different angle, returning to Avraham's argument with God regarding Sodom. The verse is: *chalila lecha me'asot kadavar hazeh, lehamis tzaddik im rasha* — far be it from You to do such a thing, to kill the righteous together with the wicked. Avraham argued before God: *chulin hu me'asot kadavar hazeh* — it is a profane act to kill the righteous with the wicked. Now, within the context of the larger discussion — all of this concerns the justice of future punishment and why the righteous suffer in this world — the question becomes pressing: is it actually true that God does not bring death to the righteous together with the wicked? Because another verse says: *vehichrasti mimcha tzaddik verasha* — I will cut off from you both the righteous and the wicked.

### Complete Righteous

The resolution: *betzaddik she'eino gamur* — that verse applies to one who is not completely righteous. But for someone who is *tzaddik gamur*, truly and fully righteous — God does not destroy them together with the wicked. Yet this too requires qualification, for the verse also says *umimikdashi tichalu* — from My sanctuary shall you begin. And Rav Yosef taught: *al tikri mimikdashi, ela mimikudashai* — do not read "from My sanctuary," read "from those sanctified to Me" — those who have fulfilled the entire *Torah* from *alef* to *taf*. It is precisely these most devoted people who are struck first when destruction comes.

### Silent Enablers

The resolution to this apparent injustice: *keivan she'haya beyadam limchos velo michu* — since it was within their power to protest the evil being done in the world and they did not, *havu lehu ketzaddikim she'einam gmurim* — they are reckoned as those who are not fully righteous. One cannot claim the status of *tzaddik gamur* solely on the basis of personal piety, if one remains a silent enabler of evil in the community. The righteous person who sees wickedness and says nothing — who does nothing to protest or resist — bears a portion of the moral responsibility. This principle is perennial; it is precisely what allows evil to succeed.

Within the larger narrative arc, this teaching also serves a pastoral function: it addresses the uncomfortable question of why even seemingly good people suffer. One answer is that God is paying debts in installments. But another answer, offered here, is that no one is as fully righteous as they may appear if they have stood by silently while wrong was being done.

## God Wrath Daily

### R. Papa Daily Anger

Rav Papa raises a contradiction. One verse states *El zo'em bechol yom* — God becomes wrathful every day. Another verse asks *lifnei za'amo mi ya'amod* — who can stand before God's wrath? If God's wrath is daily and nothing can withstand it, why does the world continue to exist?

*Lo kashya* — no difficulty. *Kan beyachid, kan betzibur* — the individual cannot stand before God's wrath, but when God judges the entire world, the collective merit of creation prevents God's anger from consuming it. The world is perpetually deserving of destruction and perpetually preserved — by the aggregate merit of the whole.

### Instant Wrath

*Tanu Rabbanan*: God's wrath is daily — but how long does that wrath last? *Rega* — an instant. And how long is an instant? *Achas michamesh ribo ushloshes alafim ushmonah me'os v'arba'im ushmonah besha'ah* — one 53,848th of an hour. Working this out: rounding the denominator to 60,000ths of an hour, and dropping one factor of 60, one arrives at something less than a 150th of a second. *V'ein kol biryah yecholah lechaven osah rega* — no creature can identify that precise instant of God's wrath.

### Bileam Knowledge

With one exception: *chutz miBilam harasha* — except for Bilam the wicked. It is written of him: *yode'a da'as Elyon* — he who knows the thought of the Most High. This is where the *gemara* becomes genuinely pointed. *Efshar da'as behemto lo hava yada* — is it possible that someone who could not even know what his own animal was thinking — *da'as Elyon mi hava yada* — could know the mind of the Most High? The claim to divine knowledge is punctured immediately by the absurdity of the comparison. What is the proof that Bilam did not know his own animal's thoughts?

### Donkey Bileam

When people saw Bilam riding on a donkey, they said to him: why are you riding on a donkey? You should be riding on a horse — that would be more fitting for a man of your stature. He answered: *be'irtiva shadya* — it is out grazing in the swamp, it was not available. Immediately the donkey answered back: *halo anochi asonecha* — am I not your donkey, the very one you always ride? He tried to claim he only used her as a beast of burden. She replied *asher rachavta alai* — no, I am the one you always ride. He said *akrai b'alma* — only occasionally. She replied: *me'odecha ve'ad hayom hazeh* — from your entire life I have been your regular mount. And moreover: *she'ani osa lecha rechiva bayom, v'ishus balaila* — I provide you riding by day and companionship at night. The *gemara* reads *hahasken hiskanti la'asos lecha koh* through the word *sochenes*, as it appears by David and Avishag Hashunamis, meaning intimate closeness. The point: Bilam could not win an argument with his own donkey. He could not know what she was thinking. And this is the man who claimed to know *da'as Elyon*?

*Ela mai yodeia da'as Elyon? Ela shehaya yodeia kivein osah sha'ah shehakadosh baruch hu ko'es bah* — what it actually means is that Bilam knew how to identify that precise instant when God became wrathful, and therefore he could time his curses to coincide with it and channel that divine anger toward destruction.

## Israel Protection Wrath

### R. Elazar Withhold

The *navi* says to Israel: *ami zechor na mah ya'atz Balak melech Moav, umah ana oso Bilam ben Be'or, min hashittim ad haGilgal, lema'an da'as tzidkos Hashem* — My people, remember what Balak king of Moav plotted and how Bilam son of Be'or responded, from Shittim to Gilgal — so that you may know the *tzidkos* of God. What *tzidkos* are we meant to learn from that episode?

Rabbi Elazar explains: *ami reu kama tzedakos asisi imachem* — My people, see how many acts of righteousness I performed for you. *Shelo kaas'ti aleichem kol osan hayamim* — I withheld that daily instant of wrath throughout all those days. *Lo nishtayer mishoneihem shel Yisrael sarid ufalit* — had I allowed even one such instant of wrath to occur while Bilam was poised to exploit it, not a single person among Israel's enemies — which is the *gemara*'s way of saying Israel itself would have been annihilated — would have survived. The entire *parasha* of Bilam, seen through this lens, is not merely a story of curses turning to blessings. It is an account of God actively restraining His own natural wrathful response, day after day, throughout the entire episode, specifically to prevent Bilam from tapping into it.

### Bileam Admission

This is precisely what Bilam himself admits to Balak: *mah ekov lo kaboh El* — how can I curse what God has not cursed? The verse continues with the word *ezom*, which connects directly to the *zaam* — the wrath — that is the subject of this entire discussion.

### Zaim Wrath

*Umah ezom lo zaam Hashem* — how can I bring wrath when God has not shown wrath? There is a brilliance here in the *agadata*'s construction. The word *za'am* appears both in *El zo'em bechol yom* — God has that moment of wrathful anger every day — and in *lifnei za'amo mi ya'amod* — nothing can survive God's wrath — and now in Bilam's own confession: *lo zaam Hashem*. God had not become wrathful. Because God had withheld it. Therefore Bilam could do nothing.

The *gemara* then returns to the question of duration: *vekamah za'amo? rega* — an instant. And *Amar Ameimar v'isema Ravina: rega kemimrei* — a *rega* is as long as it takes to pronounce the word *rega* itself.

The *gemara* asks: *umanalan derega havei resicha* — where do we derive that a *rega* corresponds to God's wrath? From the verse *ki rega b'apo chayim birtzono* — only a moment in His anger, a lifetime in His favor. Or, as the *gemara* adds, from an even more precise verse: *chavi chimat rega ad ya'avor za'am* — hide for just a moment until the wrath passes. Here the word *za'am* itself appears in conjunction with *rega*, which is the strongest textual support.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: what could Bilam possibly have accomplished in so small a window — an instant, a fraction of a second — even if he had identified it? Tosafot gives two answers. First: he could have said a single word, *kaleim* — destroy them — and one word is sayable in the time it takes to say *rega*. Indeed, God reversed the word: rather than *kaleim*, it became *melech*, as it says *v'sruas melech bo*. Second: Rabbi Eliyahu explains that Bilam would not have needed to complete his curse in that instant — he would only have needed to *begin* during that window. Once the curse had been initiated at the moment of divine wrath, it would have continued to draw on that opening. The *Rogatchover* applies a similar principle to *davvening*: if someone begins *tefillah* a moment before the *zman* expires, that beginning within the *zman* may be sufficient to count the prayer as having been recited in time.

## Wrath Moment

### Rooster Crown

So when, practically speaking, does that instant of divine wrath occur during the first three hours of the day? *Amar Abaye: b'slas sha'ei kmaisa, ki chivra karbalta d'tarnagola* — during the first three hours of the morning, at the moment when the comb of the rooster is white. The *gemara* objects: the rooster's comb is always white. *Kol sha'ata is beih serikei sumkei* — no, normally there are red streaks running through it. *Hahi sha'ata, leis beih serikei sumkei* — at that one precise instant, the comb is pure white, with no red streaks at all.

### R. Yehoshua Heretic

With this knowledge in hand, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi decided to act. A certain *min* had been giving him a hard time *bikrai* — in disputes over biblical verses, the kind of scriptural disputations we have already described — and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was struggling to hold his own. One day he took a rooster, placed it between the legs of his bed, and kept his eyes fixed on it, intending to identify that pure-white instant and at that moment curse the *min*. When that instant finally arrived — he had fallen asleep.

### Harm Not Just

Upon waking, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi drew a lesson: *lav orach ara lemeevad hachi* — it is not the right way to act. Even when someone is afflicting you, you do not seek to bring punishment upon them. *Verachamav al kol maasav kesiv* — God's compassion extends to all His creatures. *V'achsiv gam anosh latzaddik lo tov* — and it is written, even punishment is not appropriate for the righteous to bring.

This is not a call to passivity in the face of evil — the *gemara* already made clear that silent enabling is itself a form of complicity. The distinction is between *mecha*, protesting against evil in the world, which is obligatory, and personally seeking to bring punishment on those who have wronged you, which is categorically different. The latter draws on something more personal and less elevated: the desire for vengeance, which — even when the cause is just — belongs to the lower registers of human motivation. A righteous person does not cultivate or indulge it.

## Kings Sun Musaf

### Kings Crowns Sun

*Tana mishmeih de-Rabbi Meir*: when kings remove their crowns and prostrate themselves before the sun — setting aside their human dignity and sovereignty to worship the solar disk — God immediately becomes angry. This provides the practical context for when the moment of divine wrath occurs during the morning: it corresponds to the time when pagan kings bow to the sun.

### Musaf Alone

Rav Yosef rules: *lo litzli inish tzluta demmusafi betlas sha'ei kamaisa deyoma kama deRosh Hashanah beyachid* — a person should not pray *musaf* alone, without a *tzibbur*, during the first three hours of the day on the first day of *Rosh Hashanah*. The reason: that period is the time of judgment. *Musaf* on *Rosh Hashanah* — with *Malchiyos*, *Zichronos*, and *Shofros* — is the concentrated moment of divine reckoning. *Dilma me'ayni be'uvdai* — they may scrutinize your deeds, *vedachfu lei midchayei* — and you may be found guilty and condemned. Standing alone before that level of scrutiny is dangerous.

### Congregation Merit

But the *gemara* objects: if the first three hours are so dangerous, then even the *tzibbur* should not pray *musaf* that early. The answer: *detzibbur nefishei zechusei* — the congregation possesses far greater merit; the collective protects even those whose individual record might not withstand close examination.

The *gemara* then asks the inverse: if even an individual *davvening* alone is at risk during the first three hours, then *shacharis* on *Rosh Hashanah* should also be dangerous for someone *davvening* at home during that same period. The answer: *keivan dechi tzibura ka matzlu lo ka midchu* — since the entire community is *davvening shacharis* during those first three hours, even someone praying at home is understood to be connected to the communal prayer. He is, in effect, *davvening* together with the *tzibbur*, even if physically apart. *Musaf* is different: most congregations have not reached *musaf* in the first three hours, given the length of the *Rosh Hashanah* *davvening*. An individual who reaches *musaf* that early is therefore genuinely alone — not merely physically separate, but disconnected from the rhythm of congregational prayer — and that isolation during the height of divine judgment is what Rav Yosef cautions against.

## Lenient Torah

### Hours Judgment

The *gemara* circles back to address an apparent contradiction. Earlier it was stated that God spends the first three hours of the day in *Torah* learning and the next three hours sitting in judgment. But Rav Yosef's ruling assumed that the first three hours are the *dangerous* period of judgment. *Eifoch* — reverse the order. The first three hours are judgment; the next three are *Torah* learning.

### Torah Truth Law

*Ve'iba'is eima le'olam lo seifoch* — alternatively, one need not reverse the order at all. *Torah dechsiv bah emes* — *Torah* is truth, as it says *emes kneh ve'al timkor* — buy truth and do not sell it. When God is engaged in learning *Torah*, He operates strictly within the logic of pure truth. *Ein hakadosh baruch hu oseh lifnim mishuras hadin* — God does not go beyond the strict requirement of the law when learning *Torah*. Think of the dynamic inside a *beis medrash*: one does not argue from compassion or flexibility; one argues from rigor, from the force of the *sugya*, from what the text actually says.

### Flexibility Judge

*Din* — judgment — is different. Judgment applies law to human reality, which is not a purely abstract philosophical enterprise. Therefore: *hakadosh baruch hu oseh lifnim mishuras hadin* — when sitting in judgment, God can find latitude, can extend compassion, can look beyond the strict letter of the law. This produces the counterintuitive conclusion: it is actually *better* to approach God during the hours when He is sitting in judgment than during the hours when He is learning *Torah*. The *beis medrash* mode admits no flexibility; the *beis din* mode allows for mercy. The *gemara* acknowledges this is striking — and it is — but the underlying dynamic it describes is entirely recognizable to anyone who has observed the difference between *psak halacha* as an abstract exercise and *psak halacha* as a response to a human being standing before you with a real question.
