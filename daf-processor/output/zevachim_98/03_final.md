# Zevachim 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Absorption Source

### Absorption Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה לָעֹלָה וְלַמִּנְחָה וְלַחַטָּאת וְלָאָשָׁם וְלַמִּלּוּאִים וּלְזֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** It is stated: **"This is the law of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the inauguration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings"** (Leviticus 7:37).

The daf opens by returning to a central *halacha* established in the preceding *perek*: when a *chatat* comes into contact with another substance and its taste is absorbed, that substance takes on the status of the *chatat*. This principle, originally stated by the Torah only in connection with a *chatat* and a *mincha*, is extended more broadly to all *korbanot* by means of the verse *zos hatorah la'olah ula'mincha v'lachatat v'la'asham v'lamiuim ule'zevach hashelamim* — "This is the Torah of the *olah*, the *mincha*, the *chatat*, the *asham*, the inaugural offerings, and the peace-offering." By invoking this principle of *zos hatorah*, the Gemara establishes that each *korban* in this verse teaches something that applies across the entire range of sacrificial offerings. From the *chatat*, the absorption rule had been derived. The previous discussion had also noted other remarkable *halachot* drawn from this verse, including details concerning the *olah* — the use of a *kli*, the connection to *Akeidat Yitzchak*, and whether the paradigm was Yitzchak or the ram.

### Rabbi Akiva Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״מִנְחָה״ – מָה מִנְחָה מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבָלוּעַ, אַף כֹּל מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבָלוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Akiva:** From the term **"meal offering,"** it is derived: **Just as** with regard to **a meal offering,** whatever it touches **is sanctified through** the substance that becomes **absorbed,** as it is stated: "Whatever shall touch them shall be sacred" (Leviticus 6:11); **so too** for **all** offerings mentioned in this verse, whatever they touch **is sanctified through** the **absorbed** portions.

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* taught *b'masnisa* in the name of Rabbi Akiva, which takes a different approach. Rather than deriving the rule of *beli'ah* — absorption — from the *chatat*, Rabbi Akiva's *braita* derives it from the *mincha*. The reasoning follows the structure of the verse: *ma mincha miskadeshes b'valu'a* — just as a *mincha* imparts its sanctity to something that absorbs it, so too does this principle apply to all *korbanot*. This leaves the *chatat* reference free to teach other *halachot*, and shifts the *mincha*'s role from teaching that it is eaten by male *kohanim* — a point the *braita* proposes to derive instead from the portion of *Korach* — to teaching the absorption rule.

### Both Sources Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב מִנְחָה, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב חַטָּאת. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מִנְחָה – דְּאַיְּידֵי דְּרַכִּיכָא מִיבַּלְעָא, אֲבָל חַטָּאת אֵימָא לָא; וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן חַטָּאת – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָרִיר, אֲבָל מִנְחָה אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption with regard to **a meal offering, and it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption with regard to **a sin offering. As, had** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **a meal offering,** I would say **that since it is soft, it is absorbed** and, therefore it sanctifies what it touches. **But** with regard to the meat of **a sin offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. **And had it taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **a sin offering,** I would say that **because,** on account of its fattiness, it **oozes** into whatever it touches, it sanctifies it. **But** with regard to **a meal offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the Torah to write both.

The Gemara then asserts that in fact both are needed: *v'itztarech l'michtav mincha v'itztarech l'michtav chatat* — the Torah had to state the rule both by *mincha* and by *chatat*. Rather than choosing one at the expense of the other, the Gemara embraces both *braitot* simultaneously and asks why we cannot make do with just one source.

### Mincha Softness

The answer is that each source teaches something the other cannot. Had the Torah stated the absorption rule only by *mincha*, one might argue: *aydi d'rechicha mivla* — because the *mincha* is soft, it absorbs readily, and perhaps the rule applies only to soft substances. The softness of the *mincha* — whether referring to the *mincha* as the absorbing substance, or to whatever is placed in proximity to it — creates an especially strong fusion between the two materials. Perhaps such a deep, thorough absorption is required before the law is triggered; the lesser penetration characteristic of a *chatat* might not suffice. Hence *aval chatat eima lo* — one might have thought the rule does not apply to a *chatat*.

Conversely, had the Torah stated the rule only by *chatat*, one might reason *mishum d'karya* — because the *chatat* penetrates or adheres deeply — and conclude the rule applies only when absorption is that thoroughgoing. A *mincha*, by contrast, while soft and receptive, might not produce the same quality of fusion in every context. Hence *aval mincha eima lo*. The conclusion is *tzricha*: both are required. The principle of absorption applies regardless of whether the transference is easier or deeper in a particular case; as long as genuine absorption occurs, the *halacha* is triggered.

## Chatat Teachings

### Non-Sanctified Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חַטָּאת״ – מָה חַטָּאת אֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבַיּוֹם, וּבְיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית; אַף כֹּל אֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבַיּוֹם, וּבְיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית. וְחַטָּאת מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִקְרִיב אַהֲרֹן אֶת פַּר הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר וְלֹא מִשֶּׁל מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **"Sin offering"** teaches: **Just as a sin offering is brought only from non-sacred** animals **and** is sacrificed specifically **in the daytime, and** its service must be performed **with** the priest's **right hand; so too all** offerings mentioned **are brought only from non-sacred** animals, **and** are sacrificed specifically **in the daytime, and** each one's service must be performed **with** the priest's **right hand. And** with regard to **a sin offering, from where do we** derive that it is brought only from non-sacred animals? **Rav Ḥisda said: The verse states: "And Aaron shall present the bull of the sin offering, which is his"** (Leviticus 16:11). This teaches that the animal must come **from his** cattle, **and not from communal** property, **and not from** money upon which the second **tithe** has been redeemed.

Having established that the *mincha* teaches the absorption rule, the Gemara returns to the *braita* of Rabbi Akiva to ask what the *chatat* teaches in its place. The answer: *ma chatat eina ba'a ela min ha'chulin uvyom uvyado hayemanit* — just as a *chatat* may only be brought from non-sanctified funds, only during the day, and only using the right hand, so too these requirements apply to all *korbanot*.

The requirement of *chulin* funds means one may not satisfy a *korban* obligation using money or animals that already bear some sanctified status. The most vivid example is *ma'aser sheni* funds. The verse *vehikriv Aharon et par hachatas asher lo* — "and Aharon shall bring the bull of the *chatat* that is his" — is parsed by Rav Chisda as *me'asher lo*, from what is truly his own: *mishelo v'lo mishel tzibbur*, not from communal funds, *v'lo mishal ma'aser*, and not from *ma'aser sheni* money.

This *halacha* has a resonance beyond its immediate sacrificial context. The question of whether one may use *ma'aser* money — one's tithe set aside for charity or religious purposes — to fulfill other *mitzvah* obligations is a live discussion among the *Poskim*, and it turns in part on the precise model of *ma'aser* being applied: whether it is modeled on *ma'aser sheni* with its restrictions on designated use, or on *ma'aser ani*, or whether the function of monetary *ma'aser* is more akin to *tzedakah* in general. The Gemara's ruling here, that a *chatat* must come from funds entirely free of prior sanctification, feeds into those discussions.

### Day & Right Hand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיּוֹם – מִ״בְּיוֹם צַוֹּתוֹ״ נָפְקָא! כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In the daytime?** Is this principle not **derived from** the conspicuous expression: **"On the day of His commanding"** (Leviticus 7:38), which is understood to be referring to all offerings? The Gemara answers: Indeed, the *baraita* **cited** the principle from the model of a sin offering **for no reason [*kdi*],** and it was mentioned here on account of the other principles.

The *braita* continues: *uvyom* — in the daytime — and *uvyado hayemanit* — with the right hand. These two requirements, once derived from *chatat*, are then applied universally: *af kol eino ba ela min ha'chulin uvyom uvyado hayemanit*.

### Alternative Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית – מִדְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע וּכְהוּנָּה – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יָמִין! כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **With** the priest's **right hand?** Is this not **derived from** the statement **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana? As Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Reish Lakish says:** In **any place** in the Torah **that it is stated** that an action is performed with **a finger, or** that it is performed by **priesthood,** the *halakha* is that the rite is performed **only** with the **right** hand. This is derived from the Torah's statement with regard to the leper: "And the priest shall dip his right finger" (Leviticus 14:16). The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **cited** the principle from the model of a sin offering **for no reason,** since it is actually derived from Rabba bar bar Ḥana's statement.

The Gemara, however, challenges whether both of these additional *halachot* truly require the *chatat* as their source. The requirement of daytime: *mibayom tzavoto nafka* — it already emerges from the general verse that God commanded Moshe to bring the *korbanot* *bayom tzavoto*, "on the day He commanded." Thus, citing daytime as something we learn from *chatat* is, the Gemara says, *kideina nasba* — an unnecessary addition, since the principle is derivable elsewhere.

Similarly, the right-hand requirement: *miderabba bar bar Chana nafka*, it already emerges from the teaching of Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Reish Lakish — *kol makom shene'emar etzba u'chehuna eina ela yamin* — wherever the Torah uses both the word *etzba* (finger) and *kehuna* (priesthood), it means specifically the right hand. The Gemara reads the connecting *vav* as "or" rather than "and," so that either *etzba* alone or *kehuna* alone suffices to imply right hand. Since *kehuna* appears throughout the laws of *korbanot*, the right-hand requirement needs no derivation from *chatat*. Again, the Gemara concludes *kideina nasba*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: אֶצְבַּע – לָא בָּעֲיָא כְּהוּנָּה, כְּהוּנָּה – בָּעֲיָא אֶצְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggest: **And if you wish, say** that the *tanna* of the *baraita* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says:** Where the verse mentions **a finger,** it **is not necessary** for the verse to mention **priesthood;** but where it mentions **priesthood,** it **is necessary** for the verse to mention **a finger,** in order to teach that the rite must be performed with the right hand, which is not self-evident. With regard to the assorted offerings itemized in the verse (Leviticus 7:37), the Torah does not mention a finger; therefore, they must be derived from the *halakha* of a sin offering.

The Gemara then offers an alternative: *v'ei ba'it eima*, or one may say that the *braita* here follows the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *etzba lo ba'ei kehuna* — the appearance of the word *etzba* does not by itself imply right hand unless it is accompanied by the word *kehuna*. If *kehuna* alone is also insufficient, then *chatat* — where both terms appear — becomes the necessary source for the right-hand requirement across all *korbanot*. In that case, the derivation from *chatat* is genuinely needed.

The upshot: according to the *braita* of Rabbi Akiva, the absorption rule is derived from *mincha*, and from *chatat* we learn the prohibition of using *ma'aser* funds — while the daytime and right-hand requirements may or may not require the *chatat* as their source, depending on one's position in the parallel dispute.

## Asham Bones

### Permitted Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָשָׁם״ – מָה אָשָׁם עַצְמוֹתָיו מוּתָּרִין, אַף כֹּל עַצְמוֹתָיו מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **"Guilt offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to **a guilt offering, its bones** have no sanctity and **are permitted** for any use, **so too** with regard to **any** mentioned offering, **its bones are permitted.**

The *braita* now turns to what is derived from the *asham*. In contrast to the derivation discussed previously — where the *asham* as a male *korban* lacking a fetus taught a broader rule about the offspring of sacrificial animals — this *braita* derives a different principle: *ma asham atzmotav mutarin* — just as the bones of an *asham* are permitted for ordinary use after the offering has been consumed, *af kol atzmotav mutarin*, so too for all *korbanot*.

Why is this not simply derived by logic? Rashi explains that it is indeed logical in most cases: *notar* applies only to meat, not to bones, and once the *kohen* has received his portion and consumed the meat, the bones are naturally free of any remaining sanctity that would restrict their use. One could reason this way for any *korban* where the meat is distributed to *kohanim*.

### Olah Bones

The one case where the logical argument breaks down is the *olah*. Since the *olah* is entirely consumed on the *mizbeach* — save for the hide, which goes to the *kohanim* — the bones of an *olah* are burned along with the rest. One might think that the bones of an *olah*, since they are part of an offering that is entirely dedicated to the *mizbeach*, remain permanently sanctified and may never be appropriated for personal use. It is therefore specifically for the *olah* that the derivation from *asham* is needed: even the bones of an *olah*, once separated from the animal, are permitted — a striking *halacha*. The *kohen* who handles an *olah* may take the separated bones and use them as he wishes.

Rashi raises a further question: if the derivation is ultimately logical, why derive it specifically from *asham* rather than from any other *korban* whose meat is eaten? One answer is that by the *asham* there is an additional verse — *lo yihyeh* — which might have been read as restricting the bones. By making the explicit statement that the bones of the *asham* are permitted despite this verse, the Torah gives us a firm anchor from which to generalize. This applies equally to bird sacrifices, where there is no reason to distinguish.

## Blood & Garments

### Blood Layering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי – דַּם חַטָּאת לְמַטָּה וְדַם עוֹלָה לְמַעְלָה, טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **obvious to me** that when the **blood of a sin offering is below and** the **blood of a burnt offering is above,** in a case in which the blood of a burnt offering is sprayed as a second layer on top of the blood of a sin offering that has already been sprayed and absorbed into a garment, the garment **requires laundering.**

Rava opens with what he calls *peshita li* — a conclusion he regards as self-evident — before posing a genuine difficulty. The *halacha* of laundering applies specifically to the blood of a *chatat*, not to the blood of other *korbanot*. Rava's scenario: a *kohen*'s garment is struck first by a splash of *olah* blood and then, at the exact same spot, by a splash of *chatat* blood. The assumption built into the scenario is that the *chatat* blood arrives on top of a spot already coated and crusted with dried *olah* blood, and therefore rests on the surface without being absorbed into the fabric itself.

*Dam chatat lemata vedam olah lemala taun kivisa* — if the *chatat* blood came first and the *olah* blood second, Rava says, the obligation to launder is obvious. The *chatat* blood was directly absorbed into the garment, and the subsequent *olah* blood is irrelevant.

### Absorption Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: דַּם עוֹלָה לְמַטָּה וְדַם חַטָּאת לְמַעְלָה, מַהוּ? מִשּׁוּם נוֹגֵעַ הוּא – וְהָא נוֹגֵעַ; אוֹ דִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם בָּלוּעַ הוּא – וְהָא לָא בָּלַע (הוּא)? הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ, דְּאֵין טְעוּנִין כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asks:** When the **blood of a burnt offering is below and** the **blood of a sin offering is above, what is** the *halakha*? **Is** one required to launder a garment to remove the blood of a sin offering **because** the blood **touches** his garment, **and** in this case, **this** blood **is touching** the garment? **Or perhaps is** one required to launder it **because of** the **absorption** of the blood into the garment, **and,** in this case, since the garment has already absorbed the other blood, **this** garment **did not absorb** the blood? Rava **then resolves** his dilemma, ruling **that** such garments **do not require laundering.**

The genuinely difficult case is the reverse: *dam olah lemata vedam chatat lemala* — first the *olah*, then the *chatat*. *Mahu*? Does the obligation to launder apply? Rava frames the question around two possible rationales for the laundering requirement: *mishum nogea hu*, because the blood is in contact with the garment — in which case even contact without absorption suffices — or *mishum baluah hu*, because the blood has been absorbed into the garment — in which case surface contact alone does not obligate.

Rava then concludes: *de-ein taun kivisa* — laundering is not required. His reasoning, though stated without explicit textual citation, is rooted in the nature of the ritual itself. If the requirement were merely about contact, one would expect a different response — perhaps wiping the garment rather than laundering it. The fact that the Torah prescribes *kivus*, laundering, implies that the concern is absorption into the fibers of the garment. This parallels the earlier discussion about a *cheres* vessel used for cooking *chatat*: the reason a clay pot must be broken rather than merely rinsed is that the absorbed taste cannot be extracted, demonstrating that the underlying concern is absorption into the walls of the vessel. By the same logic, laundering is the appropriate remedy precisely because it addresses material that has been absorbed into fabric. Mere surface contact, which can be wiped away, does not trigger the laundering obligation.

### Mixed Stains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, דָּם עַל בִּגְדּוֹ – חוֹצֵץ, וְאִם טַבָּח הוּא – אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ. רְבָב עַל בִּגְדּוֹ – חוֹצֵץ, וְאִם מוֹכֵר רְבָב הוּא – אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ. בָּעֵי רָבָא: דָּם וּרְבָב עַל בִּגְדּוֹ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **obvious to me** that if there is **blood on** one's **garment,** it **interposes** between the water of immersion and the garment, such that the immersion is ineffective. **But if he is a butcher,** used to having blood on his garments, a bloodstain **does not interpose,** and the immersion is effective, since a substance is not considered an interposition if the one immersing is not particular about it. Similarly, if there is a stain of **fat [*revav*] on one's garment,** it **interposes. But if he is a fat seller,** such a stain **does not interpose. Rava asks:** If there is both **blood and fat on** one's **garment** when he immerses it, **what is** the *halakha*?

Rava then raises a related question that at first glance appears to be an aside but in fact raises a structurally parallel problem. *Peshita li dam al bigdo chotzetz* — it is obvious to Rava that blood on a garment constitutes a *chatzitzah* when that garment is immersed in a *mikveh*. If the garment has become *tamei* and requires immersion, the dried blood interposes between the fabric and the water. However, if the owner is a butcher for whom bloodstains on clothing are entirely routine and unremarkable — *eino makpid* — the blood is not a *chatzitzah*, since what one does not mind does not interpose.

Similarly: *revav al bigdo chotzetz* — a grease stain on a garment is a *chatzitzah*. But if the owner is someone who regularly deals in greasy merchandise — a *mocher revav*, which one might render as a short-order cook or someone who handles fatty goods as part of his livelihood — then *eino chotzetz*, it does not interpose.

### Two Occupations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם טַבָּח הוּא, תִּיפּוֹק לִי מִשּׁוּם רְבָב! וְאִי מוֹכֵר רְבָב הוּא, תִּיפּוֹק לִי מִשּׁוּם דָּם! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּעָבֵיד הָא וְהָא. אַחֲדָא לָא קָפֵיד, אַתַּרְתֵּי קָפֵיד; אוֹ דִּלְמָא אַתַּרְתֵּי נָמֵי לָא קָפֵיד? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the question: **If he is a butcher, let me derive** that the stain interposes **due to** the **fat** that he is not used to having on his garments; **and** conversely, **if he is a fat seller, let me derive** that the stain interposes **due to** the stain of **blood** that he is not used to having on his garments. The Gemara explains: **No,** this question is not superfluous; it is **necessary** with regard to a person **who works** both as **this,** a butcher, **and** as **that,** a fat seller. In such a case, the question is: Is it that **he is not particular with regard to one** stain, but **he is particular with regard to two** stains, so that the immersion is ineffective? **Or, perhaps,** is it that he is **not particular** even with regard **to two** stains, as neither is unusual for him? The Gemara provides no answer, and the question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava's question: *dam verevav al bigdo mahu*? What is the *halacha* when the garment bears both a blood stain and a grease stain? The Gemara is initially puzzled: if he is a butcher, the grease is the problem; if he is a *mocher revav*, the blood is the problem. Either way there is a *chatzitzah* — so what exactly is being asked?

The Gemara resolves this: *lo tzricha de-avad ha ve-ha* — the question applies to a person who does both jobs. He is both a butcher and a short-order cook, and he has both types of stains on his garment. The question is whether each type of stain is neutralized individually — blood is fine for a butcher, grease is fine for a *mocher revav* — or whether the combination of both stains on a single garment signals a level of negligence that renders the owner *makpid* even if he would not care about either stain in isolation. *A'chada lo kapid, a'tarti kapid* — one type of stain, no problem; but two different types of stains together might make even this double-professional look slovenly. Or, alternatively, *a'tarti nami lo kapid* — people understand he holds two jobs, and he cares no more about the combination than about each one separately. The Gemara leaves the question unresolved: *teiku*.

It is worth noting that one might reframe Rava's question in a way that makes it parallel to his earlier question about blood layering. Suppose there is a blood stain on the garment — unremarkable to a butcher, hence not a *chatzitzah* — and now a grease stain rests on top of that blood stain without being absorbed into the fabric itself. The grease is not sitting on the garment; it is sitting on the blood. Does the grease constitute a *chatzitzah* on something that is not itself a *chatzitzah*? This parallels the question of a cast on a woman's arm going to the *mikveh*: since the cast is not a *chatzitzah*, we treat it as an extension of her body — but then, is dirt encrusted on top of the cast a problem? That very question is debated among the *Poskim*. While the Gemara itself does not frame the question this way, the structural similarity is striking.

## Tvul Yom Intro

### Daytime Impurity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ טְבוּל יוֹם וּמְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים – אֵינָם חוֹלְקִין בַּקֳּדָשִׁים לֶאֱכוֹל לָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A priest who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, **and** a priest **who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete the purification process, e.g., a *zav* and a leper who did not bring their requisite atonement offerings, who are not yet permitted to partake of sacrificial meat, **do not receive a share of sacrificial** meat along with the other members of the patrilineal priestly family serving in the Temple that day, in order **to partake** of it **in the evening** after the offerings were sacrificed, even though after nightfall he would be permitted to partake of the offerings.

With *Hadran alach Dam Chatat*, the daf transitions to the twelfth *perek*, whose header — *Tvul Yom* — somewhat obscures its primary subject. The *perek* is fundamentally about the division of *korbanot* among *kohanim*, and the distinction it draws is crucial: there is a difference between being permitted to eat from a *korban* and being entitled to an equal portion when the *korbanot* are distributed at the end of the day. A *kohen* who was *tamei* during the day may become *tahor* by nightfall and be perfectly permitted to eat; yet the *mishnah* will rule that he is not entitled to a portion.

The backdrop is a broader question about how the *korbanot* were allocated. Many verses in the Torah appear to indicate that the *kohen* who performed the *avodah* with a given animal was the one who received it: *hakohen hamchatei otah yochlenah* — the *kohen* who atoned with it shall eat from it. This would be analogous to a waiter keeping the tips from his own table. The *Chachamim*, however, understood the allocation in communal terms: all members of the *mishmar* — the priestly division serving that week — pooled their *korbanot* and divided them equally, much as some restaurants pool tips. The *perek* is dedicated to establishing precisely who is included in that communal pool and who is excluded.

The *mishnah* lists *tvul yom* and *mchusar kippurim* as examples of *kohanim* who, despite being *tahor* by nightfall, do not receive a portion. A *tvul yom* is one who became *tamei* during the day, immersed in a *mikveh*, but whose purification is not complete until nightfall. A *mchusar kippurim* — one who lacked atonement — is typically a *zav*, *metzora*, or similar individual who completed a seven-day waiting period and was required to bring a *korban* before becoming fully *tahor*. There is a significant discussion among *Tosafot* as to whether, even after bringing that final *korban*, a *mchusar kippurim* remains *tamei* until nightfall; the *mishnah* clearly implies that he does.

The logic of the *mishnah* is straightforward: since these individuals were not fit for *avodah* during the day, they do not receive a portion, even though by nightfall they will be fully *tahor* and permitted to eat whatever a fellow *kohen* may choose to share with them.

### Onen Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹנֵן – (אֵינוֹ) נוֹגֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ מַקְרִיב, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק לֶאֱכוֹל לָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** A priest who is **an acute mourner,** i.e., if one of his relatives for whom he is obligated to mourn died that day, is permitted to **touch** sacrificial meat, as he is not ritually impure. **But he may not sacrifice** offerings, **and he does not receive a share** of sacrificial meat in order **to partake** of it **in the evening.**

An *onen* — a *kohen* who has lost a close relative but whose deceased has not yet been buried — presents a distinct case. An *onen* is not *tamei*; he may touch *korbanot* without disqualifying them. Nevertheless, he may not bring *korbanot* in his state of *aninut*. The *mishnah*'s formulation — *nogei'a v'eino makriv* — captures this precisely: he may touch but may not offer. Because he was not fit to perform the *avodah* on that day, he too does not receive a portion that evening.

### Blemished Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין, בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין עוֹבְרִין בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְבוּעִין – חוֹלְקִין וְאוֹכְלִין, אֲבָל לֹא מַקְרִיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Blemished** priests, **whether** they are **temporarily blemished** or **whether** they are **permanently blemished, receive a share and partake** of the offerings with their priestly brethren, **but do not sacrifice** the offerings.

*Ba'alei mumim* — *kohanim* with bodily blemishes — occupy an exceptional position. Whether the blemish is transient (*ba'alei mumim ovrim*), such as a broken limb that will heal, or permanent (*ba'alei mumim kvu'im*), such as a congenital deformity, these *kohanim* cannot perform the *avodah*. And yet, the *mishnah* rules *cholkin v'ochlin* — they receive a full portion and eat. This is the exception to the general rule, and the *mishnah* notes it explicitly before stating the rule itself.

### Unfitness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַעֲבוֹדָה – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק בַּבָּשָׂר. וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בַּבָּשָׂר – אֵין לוֹ בָּעוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The principle is: **Any** priest **who is unfit for the service** that specific day **does not receive a share of the** sacrificial **meat, and anyone who has no** share **of the meat has no** share **in the hides** of the animals, to which the priests are entitled as well.

The rule is then stated directly: *kol she'eino ra'uy la'avodah eino cholek babasar* — anyone who is not fit for *avodah* does not receive a portion of the meat. And lest one think to claim at least a share of the hides: *v'kol she'ein lo babasar ein lo ba'orot* — entitlement to meat and entitlement to hides are coextensive. A *kohen* who has no share in the meat has no share in the leather.

### Blood Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ טָמֵא בִּשְׁעַת זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, וְטָהוֹר בִּשְׁעַת הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק בַּבָּשָׂר; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים [וְאֶת הַחֵלֶב מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן] – לוֹ (יִהְיֶה) [תִהְיֶה שׁוֹק הַיָּמִין לְמָנָה]״.
>
> **Translation:** **Even** if the priest was **ritually impure** only **at the time of the sprinkling of the blood** of the offering **and** he was **pure at the time of the burning of** the **fats** of that offering, he still **does not receive a share of the meat, as it is stated: "He that sacrifices the blood of the peace offerings and the fat, from among the sons of Aaron, shall have the right thigh for a portion"** (Leviticus 7:33). One who cannot sprinkle the blood does not receive a share in the meat.

The *mishnah* then adds a critical clarification: *afilu tamei b'sha'at zrikat damim v'tahor b'sha'at haktar chalavim* — even if the *kohen* was *tamei* at the moment of *zrikat damim* but *tahor* by the time of *haktar chalavim* — the burning of the fats, which in some cases did not occur until nighttime — he does not receive a portion. The determining moment is *sha'at zrikat damim*, the throwing of the blood. Everything hinges on fitness at that moment; later participation in the *avodah* of the *imurim* does not confer entitlement. The *mishnah* derives this from the verse *hamakriv et dam hashelamim* — he who brings the blood of the *shelamim* — *lo tihiyeh shok hayamin l'manah*, shall receive the right thigh as his portion. The word *chalavim* in the verse is bracketed by the *mishnah*, emphasizing that it is the blood, not the fats, that is the operative criterion.

## Fit vs. Performing

### Pasuk Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא אֹתָהּ יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – כֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא יֹאכַל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחַטֵּא אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Reish Lakish said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states** about a sin offering: **"The priest who effects atonement shall eat it;** in a sacred place shall it be eaten, in the court of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 6:19). This teaches that only **a priest who effects atonement** by performing the rites of the offering **shall partake** of its meat, but a priest **who does not effect atonement does not partake** of its meat.

The Gemara opens its analysis with the question *mana hani milei* — what is the source for this rule? Reish Lakish cites the verse *hakohen hamchatei otah yochlenah*: the *kohen* who performs the *chituy* — the atoning service — shall eat from it. A straightforward reading suggests that only the *kohen* who actually performed the *avodah* eats from that *korban*.

### Fitness Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְלָלָא הוּא?! וַהֲרֵי מִשְׁמָרָה כּוּלָּהּ – דְּאֵין מְחַטְּאִין, וְאוֹכְלִין! רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And is** this an established **principle? But** there are **all the** priests of the **priestly watch** of that week in the Temple, **who do not effect atonement** for that offering, because the blood of a specific sin offering is presented by just one priest, **and** yet they all **partake** of its meat. The Gemara explains: **We** mean to **say** that any priest who is **fit for effecting atonement** may partake of it, even one who did not participate in the service.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *v'harei mishmar kullah de'einam mechatim v'ochlin* — the entire *mishmar*, the whole priestly division serving that week, eats from the *korbanot*, even those who did not personally perform the *chituy* with a given animal. The *halacha* of communal distribution is well-established. The Gemara therefore shifts its reading: *ra'uy l'chituy ka'amrinan* — we interpret *hamchatei* not as "the one who actually performed the *chituy*," but as "the one who is fit for performing the *chituy*." This is the first exegetical *chiddush*: the *kohen* need not have actually done the *avodah*, he must simply have been fit to do it.

### Minor Eats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי קָטָן – דְּאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי, וְאוֹכֵל! אֶלָּא מַאי ״יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – יְחַלְּקֶנָּה. רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי – חוֹלֵק, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** there is the case of **a minor, who is unfit for effecting atonement, and** who nevertheless **partakes** of sacrificial meat. The Gemara explains: **Rather, what** is meant by the term: **"Shall eat it"?** It means that he **shall receive a share of it.** The *halakha* is therefore that a priest who is **fit for effecting atonement receives a share** of the meat, but a priest **who is unfit for effecting atonement does not receive a share** of the meat. Minors do not receive a share, though they may partake of meat given to them by others.

The Gemara then presses further. A minor *kohen*, as will be established later in the tractate, is not fit for *avodah* — the verse *ish* indicates that only an adult male may serve — and yet a minor *kohen* is permitted to eat from the sacrificial meat. If *yochlenah* means merely "who is permitted to eat," then a minor would be included and the principle would not work as a limiting rule at all.

### Portion vs. Permitted

This leads to the second exegetical *chiddush*: *ela mai yochlenah yechalkenah* — *yochlenah* does not mean "shall eat" in the sense of permission; it means "shall receive a portion." The verse *hakohen hamchatei otah yochlenah* is to be read: the *kohen* who is fit for performing the *chituy* shall be entitled to a portion of it. The distinction between permission and entitlement is critical: a minor may be permitted to eat sacrificial meat, but he is not entitled to an allocated portion. The verse *kol zachar b'kohanim yochal otah* — "every male among the *kohanim* shall eat from it" — establishes who is permitted to eat. The verse *hakohen hamchatei* establishes who is entitled to a portion. Two distinct questions, two distinct texts.

## Blemish Exception

### Blemish Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי בַּעַל מוּם – דְּאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי, וְחוֹלֵק! בַּעַל מוּם רַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ: ״כׇּל זָכָר בַּכֹּהֲנִים״ – לְרַבּוֹת בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But there is **a blemished priest, who is unfit for effecting atonement, and** yet he **receives a share** of its meat. The Gemara replies: **The Merciful One included a blemished** priest as an exception, as the verse that states: **"Every male among the priests** shall eat it" (Leviticus 6:22), serves **to include a blemished** priest.

The Gemara now addresses the exception of *ba'alei mumim* head-on. The *mishnah* ruled that despite being unfit for *avodah*, a *ba'al mum* is entitled to a portion. The Gemara explains: *ba'al mum rachmana rabyei* — the Torah explicitly included the *ba'al mum* in the category of those who eat from the *kodashim*. The verse states that a *kohen* with a blemish may not enter behind the curtain and may not approach the *mizbeach*, *umin hakodashim yochel* — but he eats from the holy offerings. Since we now read *yochlenah* as *yechalkenah* — entitled to a portion — this explicit inclusion of the *ba'al mum* establishes not merely his permission to eat but his entitlement to a portion.

### Inclusion Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״כׇּל זָכָר״ – לְרַבּוֹת טְבוּל יוֹם! מִסְתַּבְּרָא בַּעַל מוּם הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But say** that the phrase **"every male"** serves **to include one who immersed that day,** teaching that he may also receive a share in the sacrificial meat. Why should it be understood as referring specifically to a blemished priest? The Gemara replies: **It stands to reason** that the Torah **should include a blemished** priest for receiving his own share of the meat, **because** he may **partake** of sacrificial meat in any event. By contrast, one who immersed that day is impure and may not touch or partake of sacrificial meat.

But this raises a further question. The verse *kol zachar b'kohanim yochal otah* — "every male among the *kohanim* shall eat from it" — is a broad generalization. Why does this language include the *ba'al mum* rather than, say, the *tvul yom*? Both are categories of *kohanim* who are male and who are at some point in the day disqualified from full participation. Why not apply *kol zachar* to include the *tvul yom* in the entitlement to a portion?

The Gemara reasons: *mistavra ba'al mum havah lei lirbuyei* — it is more logical to include the *ba'al mum* than the *tvul yom*, *she-ken ochel* — because the *ba'al mum* was actually permitted to eat during the daytime when the *korban* was being brought. He was excluded from the *avodah*, but he was never excluded from the eating. Within the framework of who is connected to the eating of this *korban*, the *ba'al mum* was already participating — or at least eligible to participate — at the very moment the *korban* was brought. The *tvul yom*, by contrast, was *tamei* and could not eat during the day at all. The *ba'al mum* thus stands in a more fundamental relation to the *korban*, even on the day it was offered.

### Tvul Yom Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה – טְבוּל יוֹם הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא מִיחְזָא חֲזֵי! הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא הָא לָא חֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **On the contrary,** the Torah **should include one who immersed that day, because,** unlike a blemished priest, **in the evening he will be fit** to perform the service. The Gemara replies: **Now, in any event,** the one who immersed himself **is not fit.**

Rava objects: *adderaba tvul yom havah lei lirbuyei de-le-orsa michezi* — if anything, one might prefer to include the *tvul yom*, because by nightfall he will be fully *tahor* and fit to perform *avodah* in the future. His disqualification is entirely temporary and will expire that very evening, whereas the *ba'al mum*'s disqualification from *avodah* is potentially permanent.

### Critical Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא הָא לָא חֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **Now, in any event,** the one who immersed himself **is not fit.**

The Gemara rejects this: *hashta miah lo chazi* — that is all well and good for tonight, but right now, at the critical moment, he is not fit. The operative question is: at *sha'at zrikat damim*, the moment the blood is thrown, who is more fundamentally connected to this *korban* — the *ba'al mum* or the *tvul yom*? The *ba'al mum*, even though permanently unfit for *avodah*, was nonetheless permitted to eat this *korban* at that very moment. The *tvul yom*, even though he will be fit for *avodah* in a matter of hours, was not permitted to eat this *korban* when it was brought. At the critical moment, the *ba'al mum* is more *b'farsha* — more fundamentally within the orbit of this *korban* — and therefore it is the *ba'al mum* who is included in the entitlement to a portion by *kol zachar*, not the *tvul yom*.
