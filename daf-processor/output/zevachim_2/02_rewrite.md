# Zevachim 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Seder Kodashim

### New masechet

We begin today a new *masechet* and a new *seder*, moving from Nezikin to Kodashim and opening with Zevachim — a large tractate of approximately 120 *daf* that will occupy us for four months.

### Three masechtos

Zevachim concerns the animal sacrifices. Following it comes Menachot, which deals with the grain offerings. After those two *masechtos* comes Chullin, known among the *rishonim* as *Shechitat Chullin* — the slaughtering of non-*kodshim* animals — which is the domain of the classical laws of *kashrut* of meat.

### Sources: Rishonim

An important practical note for the student: in Zevachim and Menachot one finds the standard Rashi and Tosafos, but virtually none of the other *rishonim*. In Chullin, by contrast, the full range of *rishonim* is available. In Kodashim generally there is very little *rishon* literature, though of course the Rambam and Ra'avad are present. There is also no *Yerushalmi* on Kodashim — and though the Rambam in his *Peirush Hamishnayot* alludes to one, and roughly 150 years ago a *Yerushalmi* on Kodashim was reportedly discovered, it proved to be a forgery. We therefore work essentially with the *gemara*, Rashi, and Tosafos.

## Kol hazevachim

### Shelo lishmah rule

The *masechet* opens with a sweeping principle: *kol hazevachim shenizbechu shelo lishman kesherim* — all sacrifices that were slaughtered not for their name, meaning not for the type of sacrifice they properly are, remain valid — *ela shelo alu laba'alim lesheim chovah* — except that they do not count for their owners toward the satisfaction of any obligation.

To appreciate this, one must understand the mechanics of the *avodah*. It is the *kohanim* who perform the slaughter, not the owners. One can easily imagine the practical complexity: vast numbers of people bring their animals, the *kohen* may lose track of which animal is which type of sacrifice, and slaughter a *chatat* with a *shelamim* in mind, or an *olah* with a *shelamim* in mind. The *mishnah* tells us that such an error, while it severs the connection between the sacrifice and the owner's obligation, does not render the animal itself invalid as a *korban*.

### Kesher vs. obligation

This distinction — between the validity of the sacrifice as an object and the satisfaction of the owner's personal obligation — is foundational and must be held clearly in mind from the outset. The owner may have brought a sacrifice because he sinned and owes a *chatat*, because he is undergoing a purification process, because he accepted a *neder*, or for any number of other reasons. Alternatively, he may have brought a sacrifice with no personal obligation at all — simply sanctifying an animal as an *olah* or *shelamim* without accepting any personal duty. If the animal is lost or something goes wrong, he owes nothing, because he has no personal *chovah*.

The validity of the *korban* as a *korban* is an entirely separate matter. When the *mishnah* says it is *kasher*, it means that all subsequent *avodos* — sprinkling the blood on the *mizbeach*, burning the fats, eating the meat — proceed in their normal course. The animal is not disqualified. The owner simply does not exit the sacrifice having fulfilled his obligation, and must therefore bring another. The *kohen*, separately, may face questions of liability — a discussion the *gemara* in Gittin takes up — but that is a different legal domain entirely.

One might further ask: if the sacrifice has been offered *shelo lishmah* and the owner therefore does not fulfill his obligation, yet the animal is still *kasher*, does the owner retain his status as *ba'alim* for other purposes — such as the *tenufa*, the waving ceremony for a *shelamim*, or the right to eat the meat? This is a genuinely interesting question. According to the view of Rabbi Yosi that *kodashim kalim mammon ba'alim hem*, the animal remains his property in *dinei mamonot* terms, and presumably those proprietary relationships would survive even if the obligation-fulfillment dimension does not. But it is worth flagging as a question that demands further investigation.

### Pesach, Chatat

The *mishnah* then identifies two exceptions: the *korban pesach* and the *chatat*. For these two, a *shechita* performed *shelo lishmah* does not merely fail to satisfy the owner's obligation — it renders the entire *korban* invalid.

The *pesach* rule applies *bizmano* — specifically during the window of the afternoon of the fourteenth of Nisan from midday until nightfall. Outside that window, an animal sanctified as a *pesach* and brought at the wrong time automatically converts to a *shelamim*, and the *pesach* rules do not apply to it. But within that window, a *pesach* slaughtered for the sake of another sacrifice is completely *pasul* and must be burned.

The *chatat* case is the more intuitive one. A *chatat* brought for a sin is highly specific: it atones for a particular sin of a particular person. Unlike an *olah* or *shelamim*, which can easily be imagined as a *nedava* — a gift with no personal obligation attached — a *chatat* is inherently bound up with its owner. Ownerless *shelamim* is a coherent concept. Ownerless *chatat* is not. A *chatat* is defined by the nexus of owner, sin, and atonement. Once that nexus is severed — because the *kohen* slaughtered it for some other purpose — the animal has no independent standing. It does not continue to live as generic *chatat* the way an *olah* or *shelamim* can continue as a valid *korban*. Divorced from its owner and its occasion, it must be burned.

### R. Eliezer, Yosi ben Cho…

Rabbi Eliezer extends this principle to the *asham* as well. His view — *af ha'asham* — follows directly from the same logic: an *asham*, like a *chatat*, is linked to a specific sin and a specific owner. Once detached from that owner, it has no independent standing. The *mishnah* states his position as: *haPesach bizmano, vehaChatas vehaAsham bechol zman* — *Pesach* in its time, *chatat* and *asham* at any time, are completely invalid when slaughtered *shelo lishmah*.

The Rabbis do not clearly answer this argument; the logic of Rabbi Eliezer appears strong. The question reduces to this: to what degree can a sacrifice maintain an identity independent of its owner?

Yosi ben Choni approaches the issue from a different angle entirely, driven by the desire for symmetry. He argues that the rules should be parallel in both directions. If a *chatat* slaughtered for an *olah* is *pasul*, then an *olah* slaughtered for a *chatat* should likewise be *pasul*. Similarly, within the applicable window, if a *pesach* slaughtered for another sacrifice is *pasul*, then another sacrifice slaughtered for a *pesach* during that period should also be *pasul*. He wants the schema to be fully reciprocal.

One should also note, as Tosafos emphasizes near the end of its extended discussion on this *mishnah*, that a crucial and separate question underlies this entire discussion: when we say the *kohen*'s wrong *machshavah* invalidates, must that intent be verbally articulated (*dibur*) or is even a silent mental thought sufficient? This debate between Rashi and Tosafos runs through all areas in which *shelo lishmah* is operative and will recur throughout the tractate.

## Gavo'ah vs. Namuch

### Higher vs. lower

Shimon Achi Azaryah — so called because his brother Azaryah supported him financially in a *Yissachar-Zevulun* arrangement — introduces a further refinement. He rules that if you slaughter a sacrifice for a *higher*-level sacrifice, it is *kasher*; if you slaughter it for a *lower*-level sacrifice, it is entirely *pasul* — not merely a failure of obligation, but a complete invalidity of the *korban*.

The word *kesherim* here, and correspondingly *pesulim*, must be read carefully. *Kesherim* in Shimon's formulation may carry even greater force than in the *tanna kamma*: there is a question in the *gemara* whether slaughtering upward toward a higher-level sacrifice not only preserves the *korban*'s validity but may even allow the owner to fulfill his obligation. *Pesulim*, by contrast, means the *korban* must be removed from the altar and burned — it has no standing at all as a sacrifice.

### Hierarchy examples

The *mishnah* then illustrates the hierarchy. *Kodshei kodashim* — the most holy sacrifices such as the *olah* — slaughtered *leshem kodashim kalim* such as *shelamim*: *pesulim*. *Kodashim kalim* slaughtered *leshem kodshei kodashim*: *kesherim*. Even within *kodashim kalim* there is an internal hierarchy: *bechor* and *maaser*, though functionally similar to *shelamim*, are considered a step below, because they lack several of the *avodos* associated with *shelamim* — the resting of the hand on the animal's head, the bringing of flour and wine, the waving of the thigh and breast. Because fewer ritual demands are made upon them, they are understood to occupy a lower rung of *kedushah*. Accordingly, *shelamim* slaughtered as *bechor* or *maaser* is *pasul*; *bechor* or *maaser* slaughtered as *shelamim* is *kasher*.

## Sanctity intact

### Language analysis

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishnah*'s language with a precise grammatical observation. Why does the *mishnah* use the word *ela* — "except"? It could simply have said: they are *kesherim* and did not count for the owners toward their obligation. The word *ela* signals something additional. And the *gemara* answers: *leba'alim hu delo olu lesheim chovah, aval bekadushataihu kaimi* — the only deficiency is that the owners did not fulfill their obligation. But in all other respects, the sacrifices retain their full *kedushah*, intact and uncompromised.

### Rava's ruling

This is a striking ruling, and Rava draws out its practical implication. One might think: since the sacrifice was already slaughtered *shelo lishmah*, and since the owner will not fulfill his obligation regardless, the *kohen* might as well continue doing the remaining *avodos* with wrong intent too. Rava rejects this categorically. *Olah sheshachtan shelo lishmah, asur lizrok damah shelo lishmah* — just because the *shechita* of an *olah* was done *shelo lishmah* does not license the subsequent *zerikah* to be done *shelo lishmah* as well.

The reason is profound: the demands of proper *avodah* — thinking about what type of *korban* this is throughout each of the four key acts — are not merely instrumental toward achieving a *kasher* status. They are expressions of the *kedushah* of the *korban* itself. The *olah* still is an *olah* in every sense. Its *kedushah* still demands that every *avodah* be performed with the proper orientation. Just as one who fails to sprinkle the blood on all four corners of the altar has done something wrong even if it does not *pasul* the sacrifice, so too here: you are still obligated to do things properly, even though departing from propriety will not retroactively invalidate what has already been done.

The *gemara* offers two bases for this ruling — *iba'it eima sevara* and *iba'it eima kra*. The logical argument: just because you made one thing wrong, does that entitle you to keep making things wrong? The verse-based argument comes from Deuteronomy: *motza sefatecha tishmor ve'asita ka'asher nadarta lashem elokecha nedava ka'asher dibarta beficha.*

### Neder vs. nedava

The distinction between *neder* and *nedava* in the usage of *Chazal* is important here. In the Torah the terms are relatively interchangeable, but *Chazal* read them carefully: a *neder* is a personal obligation — *harei alai* — whereby one accepts upon oneself a duty to bring a *korban*. A *nedava* is a gift: one sanctifies the animal without incurring a personal obligation. If the animal is lost, one who brought a *nedava* owes nothing further.

The *gemara* reads the verse as follows: *ka'asher nadarta* — if you executed the sacrifice exactly as you vowed — *yehei neder*, it functions as a *neder* and discharges the personal obligation. *Ve'i lav* — if you did not execute it in exact accordance with your vow — *nedava yehei*. It becomes a *nedava*. The *kedushah* and validity of the *korban* as a sacrificial object are not undermined; it simply no longer functions to discharge a personal *chovah*. The *gemara* notes that while this verse was cited primarily to establish Rava's point about not continuing with wrong intent, it also provides an elegant scriptural foundation for the basic principle of the *mishnah*: a sacrifice offered *shelo lishmah* is still *kasher* — it continues as a *nedava* — but it does not satisfy the owner's *neder*.

## Zevachim vs. Get

### Ravina's question

Ravina addresses Rav Pappa, noting that he had missed Friday night's session at the *beis medrash*, where Rava had posed what seemed to be a contradiction between two rulings and then offered a brilliant resolution. What was this teaching of Rava?

The *mishnah* states: *kol hazevachim shenizbechu shelo lishman* — all sacrifices slaughtered *shelo lishman* are *kasher* but do not satisfy the owner's obligation. This prompted Rava to observe a tension with a parallel rule from the laws of *gittin*.

### Language nuance

Rava's analysis begins with a close reading of the *mishnah*'s language. There is a precise difference between saying *shelo nizbechu lishmam* — "that were not slaughtered for their proper purpose" — and *shenizbechu shelo lishmam* — "that were slaughtered for their non-proper purpose." The former formulation would imply that any sacrifice lacking the affirmative right intent is invalid. The latter implies that only a sacrifice actively slaughtered *for a wrong purpose* is affected; if the *kohen* simply had no particular thought — a blank mind — that does not constitute a problem.

The *mishnah* uses the latter formulation. Its implication, therefore, is: *ta'ama d'shelo lishmam* — the reason for the deficiency is the active wrong intent — *ha stama, alu nami leba'alim lesheim chovah* — but if the mind was simply blank, the sacrifice also satisfies the owners' obligation. *Alma stamei nami k'lishmam dami* — blank intent is treated as equivalent to proper intent.

### Blank intent

Against this, the *gemara* raises a contradiction from the laws of *gittin*: *kol get shenichtav shelo l'shem isha pasul* — any *get* written not for this woman is invalid. And we know — as the *gemara* will establish through evidence below — that this extends to the case of blank intent as well. A *get* written with no particular woman in mind is *pasul*, even though the analogous sacrifice would be *kasher*. How are these two regimes to be reconciled?

Rava's answer: *zvachim b'stam lishma omdim, isha b'stama lav l'geirushin omedet* — sacrifices, when their intent is unexplicated, stand as though properly designated; a woman, when unexplicated, does not stand as though designated for divorce.

---

## Stam leshmah omed

### Sanctity defines act

The conceptual basis of Rava's answer is this: an animal is sanctified. It carries the *kedushah* of an *olah* or a *shelamim* as part of its very identity. Therefore, any act performed upon it — *shechita*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, *zerikah* — is automatically defined by what that animal is. The requirement for *lishmah* in the context of *zevachim* is not primarily about what thought happens to be passing through the *kohen*'s mind. It is about the *definition* of the act. Since the animal itself has the identity of an *olah*, any act done to it is an *olah*-act by virtue of what is being acted upon — unless something actively redefines it.

This has a striking implication: the relevant question is not whether the *kohen* is consciously thinking the right thought. The question is whether the act has been actively redirected toward a different purpose. As long as the *kohen* does not impose a different identity upon the act, the *kedushah* of the object does the definitional work. *Stam leshmah omed* — blank intent stands as proper designation.

One can test the logic with a borderline case. Tosafos raises the question of a *katan* — a minor — performing the *avodah*. Since minors lack halachically meaningful intent, does their *shechita* count? The answer, on this analysis, should be yes: the issue is not the *kohen*'s conscious thought; it is whether the act has been positively redefined. A minor who does nothing to redefine the act has not undermined it.

Similarly, Tosafos applies this principle to *brit milah*: *stam brit milah lishma omed* — an act of removing the foreskin is defined as an act of *brit milah* unless actively redefined as something else.

### Woman no default

A woman, by contrast, has no inherent designation toward any particular divorce. There is nothing in the nature of the object — the paper, the ink, the names written — that automatically defines this document as *the get* for *this woman*. Writing the name שרה בת רחל tells us nothing about which שרה בת רחל is intended. There may be countless women with that name, including multiple such women married to men with the same name. Nothing in the act of writing defines it as for *this* woman's *geirushin* rather than any other.

Rashi and Tosafos press this further: even if the woman is publicly known to be in the process of getting divorced, even if the husband has made his intention known, that context does not automatically define the *get* as being for her. The reason is that unlike the sanctified animal, the document has no intrinsic identity that would do the definitional work. Unless the scribe (*sofer*) has *her* specifically in mind, the act of writing remains unattached to any particular woman. *Isha lav stama lav l'geirushin omedet.*

## Evidence: stam works

### Mishnah language

Having established Rava's conceptual framework, the *gemara* now seeks evidentiary support for each part of the bifurcated rule — first, that blank intent is acceptable for *zevachim*, and second, that it is not acceptable for *gittin*.

The first attempt to prove that blank intent works for *zevachim* looks to the language of the opening *mishnah* itself — the same distinction we already noted between *shelo nizbechu lishmam* and *shenizbechu shelo lishmam*. But this attempt is deflected: the parallel *mishnah* about *gittin* uses the identical grammatical formulation, so one cannot derive a meaningful distinction from phrasing alone.

The *gemara* then turns to a later *mishnah* that discusses mixed-intent cases. *Keitzad lishmam v'shelo lishmam* — what counts as for the right purpose and for the wrong purpose? The example given is: the *shechita* was *leshem pesach* and the *kabbalah* was *leshem shelamim*. The *gemara* observes: the example of *shelo lishmah* is always an *actively wrong* identification — *shelamim* where *pesach* was intended — not the mere absence of right intent. This implies that blank intent would be fine.

But this is deflected as well. Perhaps, says the *gemara*, the reason blank-plus-right works in sequential cases is the principle that *kol ha-oseh al da'at rishonah hu oseh* — acts done without explicit redirection are presumed to follow the initial intent. So if you started with *pesach* and then had blank intent for *kabbalah*, the blank is defined by the opening *pesach* thought. That does not prove that blank at the outset, from the beginning, would work on its own.

The *gemara* then tries from the second half of the same *mishnah* — *shelo lishmam v'lishmam*: the *shechita* was *leshem shelamim*, the *kabbalah* was *leshem pesach*. The observation: the *shelo lishmah* example is actively naming a wrong sacrifice. But perhaps blank *shechita* followed by *pesach* at *kabbalah* would also be rescued by the final *pesach* thought — *yochiach sof al techilato*, the end retroactively defines the beginning. So again, the absence of blank intent at the outset is not proven from here.

### Six things; R. Yosi

The *gemara* then turns to a different *mishnah*: *leshem shisha devarim hazevach nizbach* — for six considerations a sacrifice is slaughtered. These are: *leshem zevach* (the type of sacrifice), *leshem zoveach* (the owner), *leshem Shem* (for God), *leshem ishim* (that the right portions be burned on the *mizbeach*), *leshem reiach* and *leshem nichoach* (the fragrant smoke ascending properly as an act pleasing to God — Rashi explains *nichoach* as referring not to the pleasant smell per se but to doing the *avodah* for God's *nachas ruach*, in fulfillment of His command). For *chatat* and *asham* there is an additional consideration: *leshem chet*, for the sake of the sin.

Rabbi Yosi then states: *af mi shelo haya belibo lesheim achas mikol elu — kasher* — even one who had none of these considerations in mind, the sacrifice is *kasher*. *She-tenai Beis Din hu* — it is a condition stipulated by Beis Din.

### Beis din rule

The phrase *tenai Beis Din* might naturally be read to mean that Beis Din has made an enactment that retroactively frames the *kohen*'s intent as though it were proper — analogous to declarations of *hefker* that apply to objects left in a defined context. On that reading, blank intent is validated only because of Beis Din's special framing, not because of any inherent principle.

But the *gemara* reads it differently. Beis Din, on this reading, instructed the *kohanim* not to try to articulate their intent. The reason: if they actively try to think *leshem olah* or *leshem chatat*, there will be occasions when they accidentally think or say the wrong sacrifice type. Better to keep a blank mind entirely. *Azkinhu Beis Din delo leima lishmo dilma asi leimei shelo lishmo* — Beis Din told them not to articulate *lishmah* lest they come to articulate *shelo lishmah*.

Now the *gemara* makes its proof: *ve'i salka da'atach stama pasul* — if it were true that blank intent invalidates a *korban* — *kaymi Beis Din u'masnu midi de'asi lidei pasul* — how could Beis Din give an instruction that would render every *korban* invalid? The fact that Beis Din could issue such an instruction presupposes that blank intent is perfectly valid. This is the clearest evidence that *stam* works for *zevachim*.

The *gemara* thus takes Rabbi Yosi's statement not as an independent enactment creating validity by fiat, but as evidence for the underlying *halacha* that *stam* is *kasher* — which then also explains why Beis Din could permissibly tell the *kohanim* to think nothing at all.

## Get requires lishma

### Marketplace get

Having established that blank intent is valid for *zevachim*, the *gemara* now seeks to establish the converse for *gittin* — that blank intent does *not* suffice for a *get*.

The first candidate: a man walking through a marketplace hears students (*sofrim*) reading aloud a sample *get* — "Reuven ben Shimon divorces Sarah bas Rachel from Tel Aviv" — and discovers that the names match his own and his wife's. *Pasul legaresh bo* — he cannot use it. But the *gemara* deflects this proof, citing Rav Pappa's explanation: *hacha b'sofrin ha'asuyim lilmod askinan, v'lo nichtav leshem krisus klal* — this case involves scribes practicing for educational purposes; the document was not written as a *get* at all. Its *get*-identity has not been established. So we cannot learn from this about a document written with the intent to serve as a *get* but with generic names.

### Generic names

The *gemara* therefore advances to a stronger case. A man wrote a *get* intending to divorce his wife, then changed his mind. A neighbor with identical names — same man, same wife's name — asks to use it. *Pasul legaresh bo*. But again the *gemara* deflects: the *get* was designated *l'geirushin d'hahi* — for the divorce of that specific first wife — not generically. It is not a case of blank, undirected intent; it is a case of intent that was positively directed at one woman and then redirected.

### Two wives case

The *gemara* then tries: a man has two wives with the same name. He wrote a *get* for the older one. He cannot use it for the younger one. *Lo yigaresh bo es hakatan*. But this too is deflected: the *get* was designated for the older wife's divorce, not the younger's. Again it is not a case of pure blank intent; it is a case of specific designation for one woman that cannot be transferred.

### Blank form gittin

Finally the *gemara* reaches a convincing proof. The *mishnah* discusses a scribe who writes *tufsei gittin* — boilerplate *get* forms. He must leave blank spaces for the man's name, the woman's name, the signatures of the witnesses, and the date. Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel: he must also leave blank the line *harei at muteres le'chol adam* — "behold you are permitted to all men" — the declaration of release that must be written with the specific woman in mind. The necessity of leaving that line blank demonstrates that writing it generically — even in a document that is being prepared as a *get* — is invalid. The document only becomes *kasher* when that line is filled in with the particular woman in mind.

This establishes that for a *get*, even a document whose *get*-identity is established, specific *lishmah* — affirmative intent directed at *this* woman — is required. Blank intent does not suffice.

## Object vs. act

### Sanctified identity

The underlying conceptual distinction now comes into sharp focus. A sanctified *korban* carries its identity within itself. It is an *olah* or a *shelamim*, and that status is real, objective, and legally operative. Any act performed upon it is defined by what it is, not by what the performer is thinking. The definitional work is done by the object. *Stam lishmah omed* — blank intent stands as proper designation — because the *kedushah* of the animal itself assigns meaning to the act. Only active, positive redirection of that act toward a different purpose can override this inherent definition.

### No inherent woman

A *get*, by contrast, is a document without inherent identity. There is nothing in a piece of paper bearing a name that ties it to any particular person. The act of writing does not define itself. No woman is inherently designated to be divorced by any document. The document must *acquire* its identity through the explicit, directed intent of the one creating it. Without that intent, there is nothing to do the definitional work, and the document remains unattached — and therefore *pasul* as a *get*.

The difference, then, is not that the two legal domains of *zevachim* and *gittin* operate under different formal rules about intent. It is that the nature of the objects involved differs fundamentally. Where the object already *is* something — an *olah*, a *shelamim*, a sanctified animal with real *kedushah* — blank intent does no harm. Where the object must *become* something through the act of creation — a *get* for *this* woman — blank intent is simply insufficient to constitute that becoming.
