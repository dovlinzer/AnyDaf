# Zevachim 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Seder Kodashim

### New masechet

We begin today a new *masechet* and a new *seder*, moving from Nezikin to Kodashim and opening with Zevachim — a large tractate of approximately 120 *daf* that will occupy us for four months.

### Three masechtos

Zevachim concerns the animal sacrifices. Following it comes Menachot, which deals with the grain offerings. After those two *masechtos* comes Chullin, known among the *rishonim* as *Shechitat Chullin* — the slaughtering of non-*kodshim* animals — which is the domain of the classical laws of *kashrut* of meat.

### Sources: Rishonim

An important practical note for the student: in Zevachim and Menachot one finds the standard Rashi and Tosafos, but virtually none of the other *rishonim*. In Chullin, by contrast, the full range of *rishonim* is available. In Kodashim generally there is very little *rishon* literature, though of course the Rambam and Ra'avad are present. There is also no *Yerushalmi* on Kodashim — and though the Rambam in his *Peirush Hamishnayot* alludes to one, and roughly 150 years ago a *Yerushalmi* on Kodashim was reportedly discovered, it proved to be a forgery. We therefore work essentially with the *gemara*, Rashi, and Tosafos.

## Kol hazevachim

### Shelo lishmah rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּזְבְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁרִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה; חוּץ מִן הַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת – הַפֶּסַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְהַחַטָּאת בְּכׇל זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their own sake,** i.e., during the slaughtering the slaughterer's intent was to sacrifice a different offering, **are fit,** and one may continue their sacrificial rites and partake of their meat where that applies. **But** these offerings **did not satisfy the obligation of the owner,** who is therefore required to bring another offering. This is the *halakha* with regard to all offerings **except for the Paschal offering and the sin offering.** In those cases, if the owner sacrificed them not for their own sake, they are unfit. But there is a difference between the two exceptions. **The Paschal offering** is unfit only when sacrificed not for its sake **at its** appointed **time,** on the fourteenth day of Nisan after noon, while **the sin offering** is unfit **any time** that it is sacrificed not for its sake.

The *masechet* opens with a sweeping principle: *kol hazevachim shenizbechu shelo lishman kesherim* — all sacrifices that were slaughtered not for their name, meaning not for the type of sacrifice they properly are, remain valid — *ela shelo alu laba'alim lesheim chovah* — except that they do not count for their owners toward the satisfaction of any obligation.

To appreciate this, one must understand the mechanics of the *avodah*. It is the *kohanim* who perform the slaughter, not the owners. One can easily imagine the practical complexity: vast numbers of people bring their animals, the *kohen* may lose track of which animal is which type of sacrifice, and slaughter a *chatat* with a *shelamim* in mind, or an *olah* with a *shelamim* in mind. The *mishnah* tells us that such an error, while it severs the connection between the sacrifice and the owner's obligation, does not render the animal itself invalid as a *korban*.

### Kesher vs. obligation

This distinction — between the validity of the sacrifice as an object and the satisfaction of the owner's personal obligation — is foundational and must be held clearly in mind from the outset. The owner may have brought a sacrifice because he sinned and owes a *chatat*, because he is undergoing a purification process, because he accepted a *neder*, or for any number of other reasons. Alternatively, he may have brought a sacrifice with no personal obligation at all — simply sanctifying an animal as an *olah* or *shelamim* without accepting any personal duty. If the animal is lost or something goes wrong, he owes nothing, because he has no personal *chovah*.

The validity of the *korban* as a *korban* is an entirely separate matter. When the *mishnah* says it is *kasher*, it means that all subsequent *avodos* — sprinkling the blood on the *mizbeach*, burning the fats, eating the meat — proceed in their normal course. The animal is not disqualified. The owner simply does not exit the sacrifice having fulfilled his obligation, and must therefore bring another. The *kohen*, separately, may face questions of liability — a discussion the *gemara* in Gittin takes up — but that is a different legal domain entirely.

One might further ask: if the sacrifice has been offered *shelo lishmah* and the owner therefore does not fulfill his obligation, yet the animal is still *kasher*, does the owner retain his status as *ba'alim* for other purposes — such as the *tenufa*, the waving ceremony for a *shelamim*, or the right to eat the meat? This is a genuinely interesting question. According to the view of Rabbi Yosi that *kodashim kalim mammon ba'alim hem*, the animal remains his property in *dinei mamonot* terms, and presumably those proprietary relationships would survive even if the obligation-fulfillment dimension does not. But it is worth flagging as a question that demands further investigation.

### Pesach, Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הָאָשָׁם – הַפֶּסַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְהַחַטָּאת וְהָאָשָׁם בְּכׇל זְמַן. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: הַחַטָּאת בָּאָה עַל חֵטְא, וְהָאָשָׁם בָּא עַל חֵטְא; מָה חַטָּאת – פְּסוּלָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, אַף הָאָשָׁם – פָּסוּל שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: The guilt offering too** is unfit when sacrificed not for its sake. According to his opinion, the correct reading of the mishna is: **The Paschal offering** is unfit only **at its** appointed **time,** while **the sin offering and the guilt offering** are unfit **at all times. Rabbi Eliezer said** in explanation: **The sin offering is brought for** performance of **a transgression and the guilt offering is brought for** performance of **a transgression. Just as a sin offering is unfit** when sacrificed **not for its sake, so too, the guilt offering is unfit** when sacrificed **not for its sake.**

The *mishnah* then identifies two exceptions: the *korban pesach* and the *chatat*. For these two, a *shechita* performed *shelo lishmah* does not merely fail to satisfy the owner's obligation — it renders the entire *korban* invalid.

The *pesach* rule applies *bizmano* — specifically during the window of the afternoon of the fourteenth of Nisan from midday until nightfall. Outside that window, an animal sanctified as a *pesach* and brought at the wrong time automatically converts to a *shelamim*, and the *pesach* rules do not apply to it. But within that window, a *pesach* slaughtered for the sake of another sacrifice is completely *pasul* and must be burned.

The *chatat* case is the more intuitive one. A *chatat* brought for a sin is highly specific: it atones for a particular sin of a particular person. Unlike an *olah* or *shelamim*, which can easily be imagined as a *nedava* — a gift with no personal obligation attached — a *chatat* is inherently bound up with its owner. Ownerless *shelamim* is a coherent concept. Ownerless *chatat* is not. A *chatat* is defined by the nexus of owner, sin, and atonement. Once that nexus is severed — because the *kohen* slaughtered it for some other purpose — the animal has no independent standing. It does not continue to live as generic *chatat* the way an *olah* or *shelamim* can continue as a valid *korban*. Divorced from its owner and its occasion, it must be burned.

### R. Eliezer, Yosi ben Cho…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹסֵי בֶּן חוֹנִי אוֹמֵר: הַנִּשְׁחָטִין לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם חַטָּאת – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Yosei ben Ḥoni says:** Not only are the Paschal offering and the sin offering unfit when slaughtered not for their sake, but also other offerings **that are slaughtered for the sake of a Paschal offering and for the sake of a sin offering are unfit.**

Rabbi Eliezer extends this principle to the *asham* as well. His view — *af ha'asham* — follows directly from the same logic: an *asham*, like a *chatat*, is linked to a specific sin and a specific owner. Once detached from that owner, it has no independent standing. The *mishnah* states his position as: *haPesach bizmano, vehaChatas vehaAsham bechol zman* — *Pesach* in its time, *chatat* and *asham* at any time, are completely invalid when slaughtered *shelo lishmah*.

The Rabbis do not clearly answer this argument; the logic of Rabbi Eliezer appears strong. The question reduces to this: to what degree can a sacrifice maintain an identity independent of its owner?

Yosi ben Choni approaches the issue from a different angle entirely, driven by the desire for symmetry. He argues that the rules should be parallel in both directions. If a *chatat* slaughtered for an *olah* is *pasul*, then an *olah* slaughtered for a *chatat* should likewise be *pasul*. Similarly, within the applicable window, if a *pesach* slaughtered for another sacrifice is *pasul*, then another sacrifice slaughtered for a *pesach* during that period should also be *pasul*. He wants the schema to be fully reciprocal.

One should also note, as Tosafos emphasizes near the end of its extended discussion on this *mishnah*, that a crucial and separate question underlies this entire discussion: when we say the *kohen*'s wrong *machshavah* invalidates, must that intent be verbally articulated (*dibur*) or is even a silent mental thought sufficient? This debate between Rashi and Tosafos runs through all areas in which *shelo lishmah* is operative and will recur throughout the tractate.

## Gavo'ah vs. Namuch

### Higher vs. lower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְעוֹן אֲחִי עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם גָּבוֹהַּ מֵהֶן – כְּשֵׁרִין, לְשֵׁם נָמוּךְ מֵהֶן – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Shimon, brother of Azarya, says** that this is the distinction: With regard to all offerings, if **one slaughtered them for the sake of** an offering whose level of sanctity is **greater than theirs, they are fit;** if one slaughtered them **for the sake of** an offering whose level of sanctity is **less than theirs, they are unfit.**

Shimon Achi Azaryah — so called because his brother Azaryah supported him financially in a *Yissachar-Zevulun* arrangement — introduces a further refinement. He rules that if you slaughter a sacrifice for a *higher*-level sacrifice, it is *kasher*; if you slaughter it for a *lower*-level sacrifice, it is entirely *pasul* — not merely a failure of obligation, but a complete invalidity of the *korban*.

The word *kesherim* here, and correspondingly *pesulim*, must be read carefully. *Kesherim* in Shimon's formulation may carry even greater force than in the *tanna kamma*: there is a question in the *gemara* whether slaughtering upward toward a higher-level sacrifice not only preserves the *korban*'s validity but may even allow the owner to fulfill his obligation. *Pesulim*, by contrast, means the *korban* must be removed from the altar and burned — it has no standing at all as a sacrifice.

### Hierarchy examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? קָדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים – פְּסוּלִין, קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים – כְּשֵׁרִין. הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים – כְּשֵׁרִין, שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשֵׁם בְּכוֹר וּלְשֵׁם מַעֲשֵׂר – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **How so? Offerings of the most sacred order,** e.g., sin offerings and burnt offerings, **that one slaughtered for the sake of offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., peace offerings, **are unfit. Offerings of lesser sanctity that one slaughtered for the sake of offerings of the most sacred order are fit.** Likewise, there is a distinction between different offerings of lesser sanctity. **The firstborn** animal **and the** animal **tithe that one slaughtered for the sake of a peace offering are fit,** as the sanctity of peace offerings is greater. **Peace offerings that one slaughtered for the sake of a firstborn** animal **or for the sake of** an animal **tithe are unfit.**

The *mishnah* then illustrates the hierarchy. *Kodshei kodashim* — the most holy sacrifices such as the *olah* — slaughtered *leshem kodashim kalim* such as *shelamim*: *pesulim*. *Kodashim kalim* slaughtered *leshem kodshei kodashim*: *kesherim*. Even within *kodashim kalim* there is an internal hierarchy: *bechor* and *maaser*, though functionally similar to *shelamim*, are considered a step below, because they lack several of the *avodos* associated with *shelamim* — the resting of the hand on the animal's head, the bringing of flour and wine, the waving of the thigh and breast. Because fewer ritual demands are made upon them, they are understood to occupy a lower rung of *kedushah*. Accordingly, *shelamim* slaughtered as *bechor* or *maaser* is *pasul*; *bechor* or *maaser* slaughtered as *shelamim* is *kasher*.

## Sanctity intact

### Language analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנָא ״אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ״? לִיתְנֵי ״וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה״!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their sake are fit, and one may continue their sacrificial rites and partake of their meat where that applies. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna **to teach** this *halakha* using the language: **But they did not [*ella shelo*] satisfy** the obligation of the owner? **Let it teach** simply: **And they did not [*velo*] satisfy the obligation of the owner.** What does the word: But [*ella*], add?

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishnah*'s language with a precise grammatical observation. Why does the *mishnah* use the word *ela* — "except"? It could simply have said: they are *kesherim* and did not count for the owners toward their obligation. The word *ela* signals something additional. And the *gemara* answers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: לַבְּעָלִים הוּא דְּלָא עָלוּ לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה – אֲבָל בִּקְדוּשְׁתַּיְיהוּ קָיְימִי, וְאָסוּר לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** By adding **this** word, the mishna **teaches us** that the only deficiency with regard to these offerings **is that they did not satisfy the obligation of the owner, but they retain their sanctity, and it is** still **prohibited to deviate** from the protocol **of their** sacrificial process, i.e., the remaining rites must be performed with proper intent.

*leba'alim hu delo alu lesheim chovah, aval bekadushataihu kaimi* — the only deficiency is that the owners did not fulfill their obligation. But in all other respects, the sacrifices retain their full *kedushah*, intact and uncompromised.

### Rava's ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: עוֹלָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ – אָסוּר לִזְרוֹק דָּמָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this *halakha* is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rava, as Rava says:** With regard to **a burnt offering that one slaughtered not for its sake,** it is still **prohibited to sprinkle its blood** on the altar **not for its sake.**

This is a striking ruling, and Rava draws out its practical implication. One might think: since the sacrifice was already slaughtered *shelo lishmah*, and since the owner will not fulfill his obligation regardless, the *kohen* might as well continue doing the remaining *avodos* with wrong intent too. Rava rejects this categorically. *Olah sheshachtan shelo lishmah, asur lizrok damah shelo lishmah* — just because the *shechita* of an *olah* was done *shelo lishmah* does not license the subsequent *zerikah* to be done *shelo lishmah* as well.

The reason is profound: the demands of proper *avodah* — thinking about what type of *korban* this is throughout each of the four key acts — are not merely instrumental toward achieving a *kasher* status. They are expressions of the *kedushah* of the *korban* itself. The *olah* still is an *olah* in every sense. Its *kedushah* still demands that every *avodah* be performed with the proper orientation. Just as one who fails to sprinkle the blood on all four corners of the altar has done something wrong even if it does not *pasul* the sacrifice, so too here: you are still obligated to do things properly, even though departing from propriety will not retroactively invalidate what has already been done.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּשַׁנִּי בַּהּ – כָּל הָנֵי לִישַׁנֵּי בַּהּ וְלֵיזִיל?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **If you wish, propose a logical argument** to support this statement, and **if you wish, cite a verse** as proof. The Gemara elaborates: **If you wish, propose a logical argument:** Just **because** one **deviated** from protocol **in its** sacrifice once, i.e., in its slaughter, could it be that **he should continue to deviate** from protocol **in all** the rest of the sacrificial rites? One deviation does not justify additional deviations.

The *gemara* offers two bases for this ruling — *iba'it eima sevara* and *iba'it eima kra*. The logical argument: just because you made one thing wrong, does that entitle you to keep making things wrong? The verse-based argument comes from Deuteronomy:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ, כַּאֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתָּ לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ נְדָבָה וְגוֹ׳״ – הַאי נְדָבָה?!
>
> **Translation:** And **if you wish, cite a verse: "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do; according to what you have vowed [*nadarta*] freely [*nedava*] to the Lord your God,** even that which you have promised with your mouth" (Deuteronomy 23:24). The Gemara interprets the words *nadarta* and *nedava* exegetically: Can **this** verse be referring to **a gift** offering [***nedava***]?

### Neder vs. nedava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶדֶר הוּא! אֶלָּא אִם כְּמָה שֶׁנָּדַרְתָּ עָשִׂיתָ – יְהֵא נֶדֶר, וְאִם לֹא – נְדָבָה יְהֵא; וּנְדָבָה מִי שְׁרֵי לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי בַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **It is** already referred to as **a vow** offering **[*neder*]. Rather,** the verse indicates that **if you did what you vowed** to do, i.e., you sacrificed your vow offering properly, **it shall be** a satisfactory **vow** offering; **and if** you did **not** sacrifice it properly, **it shall be** rendered a voluntary **gift** offering unrelated to the vow, and shall not satisfy the obligation of your vow. The Gemara concludes: **And is it permitted to deviate** from protocol **in** the sacrifice of **a gift** offering *ab initio*? Clearly it is not. Evidently, even if one of the sacrificial rites was performed for the sake of sacrificing a different offering, it is still prohibited to perform any of the other sacrificial rites in the incorrect manner.

The distinction between *neder* and *nedava* in the usage of *Chazal* is important here. In the Torah the terms are relatively interchangeable, but *Chazal* read them carefully: a *neder* is a personal obligation — *harei alai* — whereby one accepts upon oneself a duty to bring a *korban*. A *nedava* is a gift: one sanctifies the animal without incurring a personal obligation. If the animal is lost, one who brought a *nedava* owes nothing further.

The *gemara* reads the verse as follows: *ka'asher nadarta* — if you executed the sacrifice exactly as you vowed — *yehei neder*, it functions as a *neder* and discharges the personal obligation. *Ve'i lav* — if you did not execute it in exact accordance with your vow — *nedava yehei*. It becomes a *nedava*. The *kedushah* and validity of the *korban* as a sacrificial object are not undermined; it simply no longer functions to discharge a personal *chovah*. The *gemara* notes that while this verse was cited primarily to establish Rava's point about not continuing with wrong intent, it also provides an elegant scriptural foundation for the basic principle of the *mishnah*: a sacrifice offered *shelo lishmah* is still *kasher* — it continues as a *nedava* — but it does not satisfy the owner's *neder*.

## Zevachim vs. Get

### Ravina's question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לָרַב פָּפָּא: לָא הֲוֵית גַּבַּן בְּאוּרְתָּא בִּתְחוּמָא בֵּי חַרְמָךְ – דְּרָמֵי רָבָא מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא אַהֲדָדֵי, וְשַׁנִּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravina said to Rav Pappa:** Since **you were not with us last night within the** Shabbat **limit of Bei Ḥarmakh,** you did not hear **that Rava raises a contradiction between** two **superior** mishnaic **statements and teaches their** resolution.

Ravina addresses Rav Pappa, noting that he had missed Friday night's session at the *beis medrash*, where Rava had posed what seemed to be a contradiction between two rulings and then offered a brilliant resolution. What was this teaching of Rava?

### Language nuance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא? תְּנַן: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּזְבְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כּוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן; הָא סְתָמָא – עָלוּ נָמֵי לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה; אַלְמָא סְתָמָא נָמֵי כְּלִשְׁמָן דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **What** are these **superior statements? We learned** in the mishna: **All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their sake** are fit, but they did not satisfy the obligation of the owner. Rava infers: **The reason** they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner is specifically **that** they were slaughtered **not for their sake. But** if offerings were slaughtered **without specification** of intent, **they, as well, satisfied the obligation of the owner. Apparently,** if one performs any action **without specification** of intent, it is **also considered as** if he performed it expressly **for its sake.**

Rava's analysis begins with a close reading of the *mishnah*'s language. There is a precise difference between saying *shelo nizbechu lishmam* — "that were not slaughtered for their proper purpose" — and *shenizbechu shelo lishmam* — "that were slaughtered for their non-proper purpose." The former formulation would imply that any sacrifice lacking the affirmative right intent is invalid. The latter implies that only a sacrifice actively slaughtered *for a wrong purpose* is affected; if the *kohen* simply had no particular thought — a blank mind — that does not constitute a problem.

The *mishnah* uses the latter formulation. Its implication, therefore, is: *ta'ama d'shelo lishmam* — the reason for the deficiency is the active wrong intent — *ha stama, alu nami leba'alim lesheim chovah* — but if the mind was simply blank, the sacrifice also satisfies the owners' obligation. *Alma stamei nami k'lishmam dami* — blank intent is treated as equivalent to proper intent.

Against this, the *gemara* raises a contradiction from the laws of *gittin*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: כׇּל הַגֵּט שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִשָּׁה – פָּסוּל. וּסְתָמָא נָמֵי פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **raises a contradiction** from another mishna (*Gittin* 24a): **Any bill of divorce that was written not for the sake of** the **woman** in question is **not valid. And** it is derived from the continuation of that mishna that if a bill of divorce was written **without specification** as to which woman it is referring, it is **also not valid.**

*kol get shenichtav shelo l'shem isha pasul* — any *get* written not for this woman is invalid. And we know — as the *gemara* will establish through evidence below — that this extends to the case of blank intent as well. A *get* written with no particular woman in mind is *pasul*, even though the analogous sacrifice would be *kasher*. How are these two regimes to be reconciled?

### Blank intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁנִּי: זְבָחִים – בִּסְתָם לִשְׁמָן עוֹמְדִין, אִשָּׁה – בִּסְתָמָא לָאו לְגֵירוּשִׁין עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **resolves** the contradiction: **Ordinary slaughtered offerings stand** designated **for their** own **sake.** From the time that the offering is consecrated, its presumed end is that it will be slaughtered for the type of offering for which is was consecrated. Therefore, even if the one slaughtering it has no particular intention, it is in effect considered slaughtered for its own sake. By contrast, **an ordinary wife does not stand** designated **for divorce.** Therefore, a bill of divorce is never presumed to be referring to a given woman unless it is specified explicitly.

Rava's answer: *zvachim b'stam lishma omdim, isha b'stama lav l'geirushin omedet* — sacrifices, when their intent is unexplicated, stand as though properly designated; a woman, when unexplicated, does not stand as though designated for divorce.

## Stam leshmah omed

### Sanctity defines act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּזְבָחִים בִּסְתָמָא כְּשֵׁירִין מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive that **slaughtered offerings are fit** and even satisfy the obligation of the owner if slaughtered **without specification?**

The conceptual basis of Rava's answer is this: an animal is sanctified. It carries the *kedushah* of an *olah* or a *shelamim* as part of its very identity. Therefore, any act performed upon it — *shechita*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, *zerikah* — is automatically defined by what that animal is. The requirement for *lishmah* in the context of *zevachim* is not primarily about what thought happens to be passing through the *kohen*'s mind. It is about the *definition* of the act. Since the animal itself has the identity of an *olah*, any act done to it is an *olah*-act by virtue of what is being acted upon — unless something actively redefines it.

This has a striking implication: the relevant question is not whether the *kohen* is consciously thinking the right thought. The question is whether the act has been actively redirected toward a different purpose. As long as the *kohen* does not impose a different identity upon the act, the *kedushah* of the object does the definitional work. *Stam leshmah omed* — blank intent stands as proper designation.

One can test the logic with a borderline case. Tosafos raises the question of a *katan* — a minor — performing the *avodah*. Since minors lack halachically meaningful intent, does their *shechita* count? The answer, on this analysis, should be yes: the issue is not the *kohen*'s conscious thought; it is whether the act has been positively redefined. A minor who does nothing to redefine the act has not undermined it.

Similarly, Tosafos applies this principle to *brit milah*: *stam brit milah lishma omed* — an act of removing the foreskin is defined as an act of *brit milah* unless actively redefined as something else.

### Woman no default

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּזְבְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כּוּ׳ – וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״שֶׁלֹּא נִזְבְּחוּ לִשְׁמָן״; גַּבֵּי גֵּט נָמֵי, הָקָתָנֵי: כׇּל הַגֵּט שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִשָּׁה פָּסוּל – וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״שֶׁלֹּא נִכְתַּב לְשֵׁם אִשָּׁה פָּסוּל״!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** it is **from that which we learned** in the mishna: **All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their sake** are fit, but these offerings did not satisfy the obligation of the owner, **and** it **does not teach** this using the language: All slaughtered offerings **that were not slaughtered for their sake** are fit, but these offerings did not satisfy the obligation of the owner, this cannot be. One cannot infer from this language that slaughter without specification is valid, since the Mishna **also teaches with regard to a bill of divorce: Any bill of divorce that was written not for the sake of the woman is not valid, and it does not teach: That was not written for the sake of the woman is not valid,** and it is a given that a bill of divorce written without specification is not valid.

A woman, by contrast, has no inherent designation toward any particular divorce. There is nothing in the nature of the object — the paper, the ink, the names written — that automatically defines this document as *the get* for *this woman*. Writing the name שרה בת רחל tells us nothing about which שרה בת רחל is intended. There may be countless women with that name, including multiple such women married to men with the same name. Nothing in the act of writing defines it as for *this* woman's *geirushin* rather than any other.

Rashi and Tosafos press this further: even if the woman is publicly known to be in the process of getting divorced, even if the husband has made his intention known, that context does not automatically define the *get* as being for her. The reason is that unlike the sanctified animal, the document has no intrinsic identity that would do the definitional work. Unless the scribe (*sofer*) has *her* specifically in mind, the act of writing remains unattached to any particular woman. *Isha lav stama lav l'geirushin omedet.*

## Evidence: stam works

### Mishnah language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: כֵּיצַד לִשְׁמָן וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן? לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים, הָא לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּסְתָמָא – כָּשֵׁר; אַלְמָא סְתָמָן כְּלִשְׁמָן דְּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps it is derived **from that which we learned** in a mishna (13a): **How** are offerings slaughtered **for their sake and** then **not for their sake?** For example, one might slaughter the Paschal offering **for the sake of a Paschal offering and** then **for the sake of a peace offering.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** such an offering is unfit is **that he says** that he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a Paschal offering and** then he says that he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a peace offering. But** if he says that he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a Paschal offering, and** then slaughters it **without specification,** it is **fit. Apparently,** slaughtering an offering **without specification is considered as** if one slaughtered it **for its sake.**

Having established Rava's conceptual framework, the *gemara* now seeks evidentiary support for each part of the bifurcated rule — first, that blank intent is acceptable for *zevachim*, and second, that it is not acceptable for *gittin*.

The first attempt to prove that blank intent works for *zevachim* looks to the language of the opening *mishnah* itself — the same distinction we already noted between *shelo nizbechu lishmam* and *shenizbechu shelo lishmam*. But this attempt is deflected: the parallel *mishnah* about *gittin* uses the identical grammatical formulation, so one cannot derive a meaningful distinction from phrasing alone.

The *gemara* then turns to a later *mishnah* that discusses mixed-intent cases. *Keitzad lishmam v'shelo lishmam* — what counts as for the right purpose and for the wrong purpose? The example given is: the *shechita* was *leshem pesach* and the *kabbalah* was *leshem shelamim*. The *gemara* observes: the example of *shelo lishmah* is always an *actively wrong* identification — *shelamim* where *pesach* was intended — not the mere absence of right intent. This implies that blank intent would be fine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה – עַל דַּעַת רִאשׁוֹנָה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps there it is different, as** the mishna **is saying** that **anyone who performs** an action **performs** it **with** his **original intent** in mind. Therefore, since he specified initially that he was slaughtering the offering for the sake of a Paschal offering, there are no grounds to assume that he then changed his mind. Here, by contrast, he pronounced no initial statement of proper intent.

But this is deflected as well. Perhaps, says the *gemara*, the reason blank-plus-right works in sequential cases is the principle that *kol ha-oseh al da'at rishonah hu oseh* — acts done without explicit redirection are presumed to follow the initial intent. So if you started with *pesach* and then had blank intent for *kabbalah*, the blank is defined by the opening *pesach* thought. That does not prove that blank at the outset, from the beginning, would work on its own.

The *gemara* then tries from the second half of the same *mishnah* — *shelo lishmam v'lishmam*: the *shechita* was *leshem shelamim*, the *kabbalah* was *leshem pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא: שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן וְלִשְׁמָן – לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים וּלְשֵׁם פֶּסַח. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים וּלְשֵׁם פֶּסַח, הָא סְתָמָא וּלְשֵׁם פֶּסַח – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps this *halakha* is derived **from the latter clause** of that mishna: How are offerings slaughtered **not for their sake and** then **for their sake?** For example, one might slaughter the Paschal offering **for the sake of a peace offering and** then **for the sake of a Paschal offering.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** it is unfit **is that he says** he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a peace offering and** then he says he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a Paschal offering. But** if he started slaughtering it **without specification and** then slaughtered it **for the sake of a Paschal offering,** it is **fit.** Apparently, if one slaughters an offering without specification it still satisfies the obligation of the owner.

The observation: the *shelo lishmah* example is actively naming a wrong sacrifice. But perhaps blank *shechita* followed by *pesach* at *kabbalah* would also be rescued by the final *pesach* thought — *yochiach sof al techilato*, the end retroactively defines the beginning. So again, the absence of blank intent at the outset is not proven from here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר: יוֹכִיחַ סוֹפוֹ עַל תְּחִילָּתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps there it is different, as** the mishna **is saying** that **his ultimate** intent **proves** the nature **of his original** intent. Since his ultimate intent was to sacrifice a Paschal offering, that was presumably his original intent as well. Here, by contrast, there is no ultimate expression of proper intent.

The *gemara* then tries a different angle, suggesting that even the two parallel clauses of the same *mishnah* should teach us something:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא ״לִשְׁמָן וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן״, תְּנָא נָמֵי ״שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן וְלִשְׁמָן״!
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** it can be explained that even if one's ultimate intent is not considered proof of his original intent, the mishna still uses the same term in both clauses to preserve symmetry. **Since** in the former clause the *tanna* **taught** using the language: **For their sake and** then **not for their sake,** teaching that original intent is considered proof of ultimate intent, the *tanna* **also taught** the second clause using the language: **Not for their sake and** then **for their sake.** In any event, there is no proof from that mishna that an offering slaughtered without specification satisfies its owner's obligation.

### Six things; R. Yosi

The *gemara* then turns to a different *mishnah*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא: לְשֵׁם שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים הַזֶּבַח נִזְבָּח – לְשֵׁם זֶבַח, לְשֵׁם זוֹבֵחַ, לְשֵׁם שֵׁם, לְשֵׁם אִשִּׁים, לְשֵׁם רֵיחַ, לְשֵׁם נִיחוֹחַ. וְהַחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם – לְשֵׁם חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps this *halakha* is derived **from that** mishna (46b), which states: **The offering is slaughtered for the sake of six matters: For the sake of** the particular **offering; for the sake of the one sacrificing** the offering, i.e., the owner; **for the sake of God; for the sake of** consumption by the **fires** of the altar; **for the sake of** sacrificing it in a manner that gives **an aroma;** and **for the sake of pleasing** God. **And the sin offering and a guilt offering** are slaughtered **for the sake of** atonement for the **sin.**

*leshem shisha devarim hazevach nizbach* — for six considerations a sacrifice is slaughtered. These are: *leshem zevach* (the type of sacrifice), *leshem zoveach* (the owner), *leshem Shem* (for God), *leshem ishim* (that the right portions be burned on the *mizbeach*), *leshem reiach* and *leshem nichoach* (the fragrant smoke ascending properly as an act pleasing to God — Rashi explains *nichoach* as referring not to the pleasant smell per se but to doing the *avodah* for God's *nachas ruach*, in fulfillment of His command). For *chatat* and *asham* there is an additional consideration: *leshem chet*, for the sake of the sin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַף מִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ לְשֵׁם אַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁתְּנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא. אַתְנוֹ בֵּית דִּין דְּלָא לֵימָא ״לִשְׁמוֹ״, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְמֵימַר ״שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna on 46b continues: **Rabbi Yosei said: Even** in the case of **one who did not have in mind** to slaughter the offering **for the sake of** any **one of these,** the offering is **fit, since** this **is a stipulation of the court.** The Gemara explains Rabbi Yosei's opinion: The **court stipulated that** one **should not say** that he is slaughtering the offering **for its sake, lest he come to say** that he is slaughtering it **not for its sake.** Therefore one should not specify his intent at all.

Rabbi Yosi then states: *af mi shelo haya belibo lesheim achas mikol elu — kasher* — even one who had none of these considerations in mind, the sacrifice is *kasher*. *She-tenai Beis Din hu* — it is a condition stipulated by Beis Din.

### Beis din rule

The phrase *tenai Beis Din* might naturally be read to mean that Beis Din has made an enactment that retroactively frames the *kohen*'s intent as though it were proper — analogous to declarations of *hefker* that apply to objects left in a defined context. On that reading, blank intent is validated only because of Beis Din's special framing, not because of any inherent principle.

But the *gemara* reads it differently. Beis Din, on this reading, instructed the *kohanim* not to try to articulate their intent. The reason: if they actively try to think *leshem olah* or *leshem chatat*, there will be occasions when they accidentally think or say the wrong sacrifice type. Better to keep a blank mind entirely. *Azkinhu Beis Din delo leima lishmo dilma asi leimei shelo lishmo* — Beis Din told them not to articulate *lishmah* lest they come to articulate *shelo lishmah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ סְתָמָא פָּסוּל, קָיְימִי בֵּית דִּין וּמַתְנִי מִילְּתָא דְּמִיפְּסִיל בֵּיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **And if it enters your mind** that if one slaughters an offering **without specification** it is **unfit, would the court arise and stipulate a matter that disqualifies** the offering? Clearly, an offering slaughtered without specification is fit and satisfies the obligation of the owner.

Now the *gemara* makes its proof: *ve'i salka da'atcha stama pasul* — if it were true that blank intent invalidates a *korban* — *kaymi Beis Din u'masnu midi de'asi lidei pasul* — how could Beis Din give an instruction that would render every *korban* invalid? The fact that Beis Din could issue such an instruction presupposes that blank intent is perfectly valid. This is the clearest evidence that *stam leshmah omed* — blank intent works for *zevachim*.

The *gemara* thus takes Rabbi Yosi's statement not as an independent enactment creating validity by fiat, but as evidence for the underlying *halacha* that *stam* is *kasher* — which then also explains why Beis Din could permissibly tell the *kohanim* to think nothing at all.

## Get requires lishma

### Marketplace get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַבֵּי גֵּט דִּסְתָמָא פָּסוּל – מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And with regard to a bill of divorce, from where do we** derive **that** if it is written **without specification** as to which woman it is referring, it is **not valid?**

Having established that blank intent is valid for *zevachim*, the *gemara* now seeks to establish the converse for *gittin* — that blank intent does *not* suffice for a *get*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הָיָה עוֹבֵר בַּשּׁוּק, וְשָׁמַע סוֹפְרִים מַקְרִין: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי גֵּירַשׁ פְּלוֹנִית מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״זֶה שְׁמִי וְזֶה שֵׁם אִשְׁתִּי״ – פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** it is inferred **from that which we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 24a): In the case of a man who **was passing through the marketplace, and** he **heard scribes** who write bills of divorce **dictating** to their students: The **man so-and-so divorces so-and-so from** the **place** of **such and such, and** the man **said: This is my name and that is the name of my wife,** and he desires to use this bill for his divorce, this bill is **unfit to divorce** his wife **with it,** that is not a proof.

The first candidate: a man walking through a marketplace hears students (*sofrim*) reading aloud a sample *get* — "Reuven ben Shimon divorces Sarah bas Rachel from Tel Aviv" — and discovers that the names match his own and his wife's. *Pasul legaresh bo* — he cannot use it. But the *gemara* deflects this proof, citing Rav Pappa's explanation:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא כִּדְרַב פָּפָּא – דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא בְּסוֹפְרִים הָעֲשׂוּיִין לְהִתְלַמֵּד עָסְקִינַן; וְלָא אִיכְּתוּב לְשׁוּם כְּרִיתוּת כְּלָל!
>
> **Translation:** It can be explained: **Perhaps** the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rav Pappa. As Rav Pappa says: Here we are dealing with scribes who are wont to practice** writing bills of divorce; **and** this bill of divorce is a draft and **was not written for the sake of severance,** i.e., divorce, **at all.** But if a bill of divorce is written to be used for divorce, perhaps it is fit even if written without specifying the woman in question.

*hacha b'sofrin ha'asuyim lilmod askinan, v'lo nichtav leshem krisus klal* — this case involves scribes practicing for educational purposes; the document was not written as a *get* at all. Its *get*-identity has not been established. So we cannot learn from this about a document written with the intent to serve as a *get* but with generic names.

### Generic names

The *gemara* therefore advances to a stronger case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא: יָתֵר עַל כֵּן, כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְנִמְלַךְ, מְצָאוֹ בֶּן עִירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״שְׁמִי כְּשִׁמְךָ וְשֵׁם אִשְׁתִּי כְּשֵׁם אִשְׁתְּךָ״ – פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** derive it **from** the subsequent clause in **that** mishna: **Moreover,** if a husband **wrote** a bill of divorce with which **to divorce his wife but** later **reconsidered,** and **a resident of his town found him and said to him: My name is** the same **as your name, and my wife's name is** the same **as your wife's name,** and we reside in the same town; give me the bill of divorce and I will use it, the bill of divorce is **unfit** for the second man **to divorce** his wife **with it.** Evidently, even if the bill of divorce was written to be used for divorce, if it was not written specifically for the given woman it is not valid.

A man wrote a *get* intending to divorce his wife, then changed his mind. A neighbor with identical names — same man, same wife's name — asks to use it. *Pasul legaresh bo*. But again the *gemara* deflects:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִינְּתִיק לֵיהּ לְשֵׁם גֵּירוּשִׁין דְּהָהוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps there it is different, as** the bill of divorce **was** specifically **designated** for **the sake of that** man's **divorce,** and therefore it is not valid for the second man's divorce. But a bill of divorce that was written without specification might be valid if written for the sake of divorce.

the *get* was designated *l'geirushin d'hahi* — for the divorce of that specific first wife — not generically. It is not a case of blank, undirected intent; it is a case of intent that was positively directed at one woman and then redirected.

### Two wives case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא: יָתֵר עַל כֵּן, יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁשְּׁמוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת – כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה, לֹא יְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** derive it **from** the subsequent clause in **that** mishna: **Moreover,** if **he has two wives whose names are identical,** and he **wrote** a bill of divorce **to divorce the older** one, and then reconsidered, **he may not divorce the younger** one **with it.** Evidently, a bill of divorce must be written specifically to divorce a specific wife.

The *gemara* then tries: a man has two wives with the same name. He wrote a *get* for the older one. He cannot use it for the younger one. *Lo yigaresh bo es hakatan*. But this too is deflected:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִינְּתִיק לֵיהּ לְשֵׁם גֵּירוּשִׁין דְּהָהִיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps there it is different, as** the bill of divorce **was** specifically **designated for the sake of the divorce of that** other wife.

the *get* was designated for the older wife's divorce, not the younger's. Again it is not a case of pure blank intent; it is a case of specific designation for one woman that cannot be transferred.

### Blank form gittin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא: יָתֵר עַל כֵּן, אָמַר לְלַבְלָר: כְּתוֹב, וּלְאֵיזֶה שֶׁאֶרְצֶה אֲגָרֵשׁ – פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** derive it **from** the subsequent clause in **that** mishna: **Moreover,** if **he said to the scribe [*lalavlar*]: Write** a bill of divorce for one of my two wives who have identical names, **and I will** use it to **divorce whichever** one of them **that I want,** this bill of divorce is **unfit to divorce** either wife **with it.** Evidently, it must be written for the divorce of a specific woman.

Finally the *gemara* reaches a convincing proof. The *mishnah* discusses a scribe who writes *tufsei gittin* — boilerplate *get* forms. He must leave blank spaces for the man's name, the woman's name, the signatures of the witnesses, and the date. Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel: he must also leave blank the line *harei at muteres le'chol adam* — "behold you are permitted to all men" — the declaration of release that must be written with the specific woman in mind. The necessity of leaving that line blank demonstrates that writing it generically — even in a document that is being prepared as a *get* — is invalid. The document only becomes *kasher* when that line is filled in with the particular woman in mind.

But again the *gemara* deflects:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאֵין בְּרֵירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps there it is different, as there is no** retroactive **designation.** The designation of the bill of divorce cannot be determined retroactively. It cannot be used for either wife because it was possibly written for the sake of the other wife. But a bill of divorce written without specification may be valid.

The Gemara then turns to the definitive proof:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא: הַכּוֹתֵב טוֹפְסֵי גִיטִּין – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ מְקוֹם הָאִישׁ וּמְקוֹם הָאִשָּׁה וּמְקוֹם הָעֵדִים וּמְקוֹם הַזְּמַן. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַף צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ מְקוֹם ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** derive it **from that** mishna (*Gittin* 26a): With regard to a scribe **who writes the standard** part **of [*tofesei*] bills of divorce** in advance, so that when one requests a bill of divorce, he will have to add only the details unique to the case, **he must leave** empty **the place** of the name **of the man, and the place** of the name **of the woman, and the place** of the names **of the witnesses, and the place of the date. And** in addition, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** A scribe **must also leave the place of** the essential phrase: **You are hereby permitted to** marry **any man,** since it must be written for the sake of that specific woman. Evidently, a bill of divorce must be written for a specific husband and wife, and if not it is not valid, even if it was written for the sake of divorce.

This establishes that for a *get*, even a document whose *get*-identity is established, specific *lishmah* — affirmative intent directed at *this* woman — is required. Blank intent does not suffice.

## Object vs. act

### Sanctified identity

The underlying conceptual distinction now comes into sharp focus. A sanctified *korban* carries its identity within itself. It is an *olah* or a *shelamim*, and that status is real, objective, and legally operative. Any act performed upon it is defined by what it is, not by what the performer is thinking. The definitional work is done by the object. *Stam lishmah omed* — blank intent stands as proper designation — because the *kedushah* of the animal itself assigns meaning to the act. Only active, positive redirection of that act toward a different purpose can override this inherent definition.

### No inherent woman

A *get*, by contrast, is a document without inherent identity. There is nothing in a piece of paper bearing a name that ties it to any particular person. The act of writing does not define itself. No woman is inherently designated to be divorced by any document. The document must *acquire* its identity through the explicit, directed intent of the one creating it. Without that intent, there is nothing to do the definitional work, and the document remains unattached — and therefore *pasul* as a *get*.

The difference, then, is not that the two legal domains of *zevachim* and *gittin* operate under different formal rules about intent. It is that the nature of the objects involved differs fundamentally. Where the object already *is* something — an *olah*, a *shelamim*, a sanctified animal with real *kedushah* — blank intent does no harm. Where the object must *become* something through the act of creation — a *get* for *this* woman — blank intent is simply insufficient to constitute that becoming.
