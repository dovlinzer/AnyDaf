# Taanit 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Personal Fast & Aneinu

### Eating Before Fast

The daf opens with the continuation of a discussion about the nature of a personal fast, picking up at the two dots on 11b where the *mishnah* states *ochlin u'shotin mishechashecha* — one may eat and drink from nightfall. This follows from the broader conversation about the relationship of the *yachid* to the *tzibur* in the context of fasting: whereas the concern on a *ta'anit tzibur* is that one should not indulge in pleasure while the community is in distress, here the *yachid* accepts *tza'ar* upon himself while the community goes about its normal affairs. The nature of this personal *ta'anit* is fundamentally different from the communal *ta'anit* that forms the primary subject of the *masechet*, which concerns fasting in the context of rain.

The *mishnah* establishes that when the process of fasting begins — first with the *yechidim* and then, if it reaches Rosh Chodesh Kislev without rain, as a communal fast — the initial stages are of a lighter character. One may eat and drink the previous night, and other restrictions such as anointing, bathing, and marital relations are not forbidden. This applies to the first three individual fasts and the first three communal ones.

On this basis, the *gemara* states: *amar Rav Zeira amar Rav Huna yachid shekibel alav ta'anit afilu achal v'shata kol halailah l'machar hu mispallel tefillas ta'anit* — an individual who has accepted a *ta'anit* upon himself, even if he ate and drank the entire preceding night, the next day recites the prayer of a *ta'anit*. He is still considered to be observing a *ta'anit* and says *tefillas ta'anit* — that is, *Aneinu* — in the *shemoneh esrei* the following morning, even though the fast only begins with the day itself and does not include the preceding night. Tosafot already noted that this seems to have little by way of *chiddush*, since the *mishnah* itself establishes that this is the standard form of the first three individual and communal fasts — of course one recites *tefillas ta'anit*. The *gemara* therefore has likely framed it this way in order to set up the contrast that follows.

### No Kabbalah, No Prayer

The *gemara* then presents the contrast: *lan b'ta'aniso* — one who "slept in his fast," meaning that after the fast day concluded, he did not eat that night either. To use a concrete example: one ate the entire night leading into the 17th of Tammuz, fasted throughout the day of the 17th, and then when the night of the 17th arrived, did not eat dinner. He wakes up the following morning — the 18th of Tammuz — having now not eaten for a full twenty-four hours. Yet *eino mispallel shel ta'anit* — he does not recite *Aneinu* at *Shacharis* of the 18th.

This is striking. In the first case, a person may have eaten ten minutes before *amud hashachar* and still recites *Aneinu*. In the contrast case, a person has not eaten for twenty-four hours and does not recite *Aneinu*. The reason, as the *gemara* will develop, is that he has not accepted this continuation as a *ta'anit*. It is true that he has not eaten, but because he has not formally accepted the fast from the prior day, it lacks the status of a *ta'anit* entirely. The first statement of Rav Huna was therefore offered precisely to create this contrast — even eating moments before does not undermine the *ta'anit* when there is proper *kabbalah*; even twenty-four hours of fasting counts for nothing without it.

Rav Yosef then asks: what exactly is Rav Huna's position? Is it *ein mit'anin lasha'os* — that there is no such thing as a part-day fast, and therefore the nighttime extension in the contrast case has no status at all, and one could freely eat whenever one wished, as there is no binding fast in effect? Or does he hold *mit'anin lasha'os* — that a part-day fast does carry some weight — but *mit'aneh lasha'os eino mispallel tefillat Aneinu*, meaning that while a partial fast has some binding quality, it is not sufficient to generate the recitation of *Aneinu*, which requires a full-day fast? The practical difference between these two positions is whether one who is in the middle of such a part-day fast is at all bound by it — may one simply begin eating? — and whether such a fast might count toward some total number of fasts one has vowed to observe.

### Kabbalah from Before

Abaye resolves the question: *le'olam kasavar Rav Huna mit'anin lasha'os u'mit'aneh lasha'os mitpalel tefillat Aneinu* — Rav Huna actually holds that a part-day fast carries full weight, and one who fasts for a part of the day recites *Aneinu*. If someone accepts a fast until midday, that acceptance is binding and he recites *Aneinu* at *Shacharis*. Why then does the contrast case fail? *V'shani hacha d'ika shavata d'me'alya v'lo kibla alei mei'ikara* — the problem in the contrast case is not that it is only a part-day fast, but that there was no *kabbalah me'ikara*, no formal acceptance from the prior day.

This clarification identifies two distinct components that the *gemara* will continue to examine. The first is *kabbalah* — the formal acceptance of the fast, which must precede the day itself. The second is duration — how long the fast must last to carry its weight. For a *ta'anit tzibur*, the matter of *kabbalah* is moot, since the fast is *gozrin*, decreed communally without depending on individual acceptance. But for a personal *ta'anit*, these two factors — the prior *kabbalah* and the duration — are what give the fast its status.

Rav Chisda elaborates on the duration question: *ha d'amart mit'anin lasha'os vehu shelo ta'am klum ad ha'erev* — when we said one can have a part-day fast, that is only if one does not eat anything until nightfall. Abaye immediately objects: *ha tanita ma'alya'ta hi* — but then that is a full *ta'anit*! The *gemara* responds: *lo tzericha d'imlecha imluchi* — the novelty is the case where one changed one's mind midstream. Rashi explains: one had no intention of fasting at all, but the day became hectic and one simply did not eat until midday, at which point one decided to continue and complete the fast. The problem here is that even this account leaves the tension with the requirement of *kabbalah me'ikara* unresolved. Tosafot in *Avodah Zarah* offers a more elaborate explanation, but even so, it is difficult to reconcile the two passages. The easier reading may be that these two *sugyot* reflect different conceptions of *ta'anit sha'os*: the earlier passage envisions a genuinely partial fast — one accepted to fast only until noon — while this passage envisions a full day of not eating, but one whose fast-character only crystallized in the middle of the day, not from the outset. Whether what is missing for a *ta'anit sha'os* is the completion or the prior *kabbalah* may itself be the underlying dispute between these two sources.

### When to Say Aneinu

One of the participants observed that in our practice, individuals do not recite *Aneinu* during *Shacharis*. The *gemara* itself does not explicitly address the timing of *Aneinu*, but Tosafot raises it directly. At the end of the Tosafot entry *lama b'ta'aniso*, approximately ten lines in, beginning with the word *achal*, Tosafot writes: *umefareish b'Hilchos Gedolos* — the Bahag explains *de'eino omer Aneinu bitfillat Shacharis shema nimtza shakran bitfillato de'shema lo yashlim hata'anit* — one does not say *Aneinu* at *Shacharis* lest one be found a liar in one's prayer, since one might not complete the fast. Tosafot himself pushes back: *v'li neereh d'lo nikra shakran keivan shehaya b'da'ato l'hisanos afilu ira'o ones achar kach v'lo matsi l'hashlim nafshai* — if at the time of *Shacharis* one sincerely intended to fast the whole day, why should one be called a liar even if circumstances later prevented completion?

Five lines from the bottom, Tosafot records the actual practice: *v'nohagim she'shliach tzibur omero b'Shacharis k'dei l'hodia l'olam u'l'hazkir hata'anit* — the *shliach tzibur* says *Aneinu* at *Shacharis* in order to inform everyone that there is a fast day today. *Aval yachid eino omer ad ha'mincha* — the individual, however, does not recite it until *Mincha*, following the position of the Bahag. Tosafot also quotes the Tur *Orach Chaim* in this passage, which is of course a later insertion into the Tosafot. The Sephardic practice, it should be noted, is that all individuals recite *Aneinu* at *Shacharis* as well.

On the broader question of whether *kabbalah* must be verbal, and the question of whether *Maariv* of the night before a fast that begins only in the morning is a relevant opportunity for *kabbalah* — these points will be taken up further as the *gemara* continues. Much of the detail, as Tosafot acknowledges, remains imprecise even in the *rishonim*.

## Fasting Until Sunset

### Sunset Requirement

The *gemara* states: *amar Rav Chisda kol ta'anit she'lo shak'ah alav chama lo shemei ta'anit* — any fast upon which the sun did not set does not count as a *ta'anit*. This is a formulation that, on one hand, imposes a stringency — the fast must be completed through sunset — but implicitly also contains a *kula*, that one need not continue beyond *shki'ah*. It is interesting that this leniency is not the ruling we actually follow in practice. Some *rishonim* do hold that for minor fast days one may begin eating at *shki'at hachamah*, and the formulation of *shak'ah alav chama* would seem to support that view. However, Tosafot and the developed *psak* either interpret *shki'at hachamah* in accordance with Tosafot's conception of two *shki'ot* — the second of which corresponds closely to *tzeis* — or otherwise understand the phrase not literally.

The question of when exactly to end minor fasts is something that various *poskim* approach differently. The underlying principle from the *gemara*, however, is clear: the fast must extend through the completion of the day; ending before *shki'ah* categorically disqualifies it.

### Mishmar Exception

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a well-known practice: *anshei mishmar mit'anin v'lo mashlimin* — the *kohanim* and *levi'im* of the rotating priestly watch would fast on the day of their service in the *beit hamikdash* but would not complete the fast through the end of the day. This apparently contradicts Rav Chisda's ruling that an incomplete fast has no validity.

The *gemara* resolves this: *hasam l'tza'arei nafshei b'alma hu* — there, the *mishmar* was not observing a formal *ta'anit* at all. Their practice of not eating was a form of self-affliction, an expression of *tza'ar*, but not a formal *ta'anit* with the halakhic status that Rav Chisda was describing. The *gemara* will return to this distinction — the difference between afflicting oneself and a formally constituted *ta'anit* — in subsequent passages as well.

### Holiday Override

A second challenge is brought from the statement of Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok: he traces his lineage to the family of Sanav ben Binyamin, one of the families that, in the time of Ezra, donated wood to the *mizbeach* of the *beit hamikdash* and as a result established the tenth of Av as a family holiday. When Tisha B'Av fell on Shabbat and was postponed to the tenth of Av — coinciding exactly with this family's *yom tov* — Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok reports: *v'hisaneinu bo v'lo hishlamnuho mipnei she'yom tov shelanu hu* — we fasted but did not complete the fast, because it was our *yom tov*.

This seems to present a case of an incomplete but presumably valid *ta'anit*. After all, the *gemara* assumes that he observed some form of Tisha B'Av — otherwise why fast at all? — and if an incomplete fast were entirely without value, what was the point?

The *gemara* again responds: *hasam nami l'tza'arei nafshei b'alma hu* — here too, the family's *yom tov* could theoretically have overridden Tisha B'Av entirely; they could have eaten all day. What they observed was not a formal *ta'anit* but an act of self-affliction, participating in the suffering of the community even while their own holiday technically exempted them from the full fast. This is reminiscent of the earlier discussion in the *gemara* on 11b about *matsi l'tza'arei nafshei delo matsi l'tza'arei nafshei* — whether it is appropriate to afflict oneself — and the principle that one should not indulge when the community is in *tza'ar*. Here, he did not want to set himself apart from the *tzibur* even though his family's *yom tov* technically permitted it. One participant raised a further nuance: because Tisha B'Av was itself a *nidche* — postponed — it may not have had the same force as Tisha B'Av *bizmano*, which would make the family's *yom tov* all the more capable of overriding it.

The key conceptual distinction throughout these cases is that *tza'ar* without the formal frame of *ta'anit* is simply self-inflicted suffering. That can have its own value — particularly when the *tzibur* is *sharui b'tza'ar* and one does not wish to be seen indulging — but it is not a *ta'anit* in the halakhic sense and therefore does not carry its formal status or complete its formal requirements.

### Partial Day Fast

A third challenge comes from Rabbi Yochanan himself, who would say from time to time: *ehei b'ta'anit ad she'avo l'veisi* — I will be in a *ta'anit* until I get to my house. This appears to be a *ta'anit* that explicitly does not extend through the end of the day.

The *gemara* resolves this as well: *hacha mishum bi'utei nafshei mibei nesi'a hu d'avid* — Rabbi Yochanan said this out of concern that the *bei nesi'a*, the household of the *Nasi*, would invite him for a meal, so he would declare himself in a *ta'anit* as a way of declining. It was not an actual formal *ta'anit* at all, but a social stratagem. With that, the *gemara* has disposed of all three challenges, and Rav Chisda's ruling stands: a *ta'anit* must be completed through *shki'ah* to count.

## Kabbalah Requirements

### Mibe'od Yom Rule

Alongside Rav Chisda's ruling about *shki'ah*, Shmuel now states: *kol ta'anit shelo kibel alav mibe'od yom lo shemei ta'anit* — any *ta'anit* for which one did not formally accept upon oneself *mibe'od yom* — from while it is still day, meaning from the afternoon before — does not count as a *ta'anit*. Taken together, Rav Chisda and Shmuel establish the two boundary conditions: the fast must be accepted before the day begins and must be completed when the day ends. Once these two requirements are combined, it becomes exceedingly difficult to construct a coherent space for a *ta'anit sha'os* — which likely reflects the underlying tension between the different *sugyot*, as discussed earlier.

### Bellows Analogy

Rav Yosef raises a practical question: if someone went ahead and fasted the entire day without any prior *kabbalah*, what is the status of that fast? Rabbah bar Shila answers with a vivid image: *dami lemapucha demalya zika* — it is like a bellows filled with air. The body was filled with air, as it were — there was no eating, but there was also nothing of substance. The fast is entirely without value; it does not count, *Aneinu* is not recited, and it registers as nothing. This is a remarkably uncompromising formulation.

### Mincha Acceptance

When should one accept a *ta'anit*? Rava says: *b'Mincha* — at *Mincha* time, meaning by the time of the afternoon, though not necessarily during the prayer itself. Shmuel, the same authority who ruled that *mibe'od yom* acceptance is required, goes further: *bitfillas haMincha* — during the *Mincha* prayer itself, in the *shmoneh esrei*.

This second formulation is striking. If *kabbalah* of a *ta'anit* is essentially a personal vow — a *neder* of some kind — why should it be incorporated into the formal *davening* at all? One could simply say it. The fact that Shmuel situates it specifically within the *tefillah* already points toward a deep connection between *ta'anit* and *tefillah* that is not merely incidental.

### Davening Integration

Tosafot makes this connection explicit. In the Tosafot on *kol ta'anit shelo shaka*, Tosafot states: *kol ta'anit tzarich lekablo mibe'od yom bitfillat hamincha b'shomea tefillah v'im hu Shabbat b'Elokai netzor* — every *ta'anit* must be accepted during the *Mincha* prayer in the blessing of *Shomea Tefillah*, and on *Shabbat*, when *Shomea Tefillah* is not recited, one inserts it in *Elokai netzor*. The choice of *Shomea Tefillah* is particularly telling: why does the acceptance of a *ta'anit* — which is a personal *kabbalah* one is making upon oneself — belong in a blessing whose content is about God hearing prayer?

### Ta'anit & Tefillah Link

The answer must lie in the fundamental character of *ta'anit* as it is conceived in this *masechet* and in the personal fast as well. A *ta'anit* is not merely an act of behavioral abstinence, an exercise in not eating. It is, at its core, an intensifier of prayer — a means by which one's supplications are placed before God in a heightened mode. The formulation *lihyot b'ta'anit lefanecha*, which the *gemara* will cite shortly — to be in a *ta'anit* before You — evokes the idea of *da lifnei mi atah omed*, standing in awareness of the divine presence. Accepting the *ta'anit* in *Shomea Tefillah* is therefore not incidental; it is a declaration that the fast is an act directed toward God as a form of deepened prayer, not simply a personal dietary practice.

This connection between *ta'anit* and *tefillah* is clearer in the context of a communal *ta'anit* for rain, where the fast is explicitly an amplifier of the community's prayers. For a personal *ta'anit* — whether for a bad dream, *ta'anit chalom*, or for one's own religious development — the connection is less obvious but still present. The fact that Shmuel requires the *kabbalah* to occur within the *tefillah* itself, and specifically in *Shomea Tefillah*, suggests that the formal constitution of a *ta'anit* is inherently a prayerful act and not simply a personal resolution. The *kabbalah* also needs to precede nightfall — even though the fasting itself only begins in the morning — because the *ta'anit* is not merely a practice one undertakes during certain hours; it confers a status on the day itself. The entire day is a *yom ta'anit*. To give status to the day, the *kabbalah* must occur while it is still the previous day.

## Megillat Ta'anit

### Holidays & Vows

Rav Yosef now adduces support for Shmuel's position from *Megillat Ta'anit*, employing the language *dechativ b'Megillat Ta'anit* — "as it is written in *Megillat Ta'anit*" — the same locution one uses for a biblical verse, which is appropriate since *Megillat Ta'anit*, unlike the oral tradition, was a written text. This short scroll, dating from the *bayit sheni* period, lists the days on which fasting was prohibited — minor holidays of various kinds — and was later elaborated by a *scholion*. The *megillah* itself comprises only thirty-eight short lines, listing dates with a few words of explanation for each. The full text has recently been published in a careful critical edition by Vered Noam.

The last line of the *megillah* reads: *beasrin vetamnya bei atat besurta tava lihudai dela yadun min oraita vela lemispad* — on the 28th of Adar, good tidings came to the Jews that they would not be removed from the Torah, and it is not a day of mourning. And then the very final line states: *lahen inish deiti alohi yeaser betzalu* — "any person who brings this upon himself is forbidden in prayer." The word *betzalu*, "in prayer," appears in the text of the *megillah* itself.

### Textual Variants

The *gemara* quotes the relevant line from *Megillat Ta'anit* as: *lahen kol inish deyaasi alohi mikadmat dena yeaser* — "any person who accepted this upon himself from prior to this is forbidden." The question is how to understand *yeaser*. The *gemara* asks: *mai lav yeaser atzmo betzalo* — does "forbid himself" mean he does so through prayer? The *gemara* initially demurs, suggesting *yeaser* simply means a personal acceptance without prayer. However, as Rav Yosef notes, a number of manuscripts of the *gemara* actually included the word *betzalu* in the quotation itself — *lahen kol inish dyeaser alohi mikamatna yeaser betzalu* — meaning the debate was not about interpreting the word *yeaser* at all, but about which *girsa* of *Megillat Ta'anit* one held: did one's text include the word *betzalu* or not?

Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon berebbi dispute this: *chad amar yeaser v'chad amar yeaser* — one holds the text includes *betzalu* (in prayer) and one does not. The one who reads *yeaser* without the word for prayer understands it simply as *kedamran* — as we explained, a personal acceptance. The one who reads the text with *betzalu* takes that phrase to mean the acceptance occurs within *tefillah*.

### Monday Thursday Fasts

The *gemara* now turns to the *scholion* of *Megillat Ta'anit* — introduced with *detanya b'Megillat Ta'anit* rather than *dechativ*, since the *scholion* is regarded as a taught oral tradition elaborating on the written *megillah*, not as the written text itself. The *scholion* elaborates on the practical implications of the rule that one who accepted a fast "from prior" retains that acceptance even when it conflicts with a declared holiday.

The case the *scholion* presents is striking in its particularity: *yachid shekibel alav sheni v'chamishi shel kol hashana kula* — an individual who accepted upon himself to fast every Monday and Thursday of the entire year. This practice of regular Monday and Thursday fasting was evidently not uncommon. The question is: *v'iru bahem yamim tovim haktuvim b'Megillat Ta'anit* — what happens when one of these recurring fasts falls on a *yom tov* listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*?

The *scholion* answers: *im nidro kodem ligzeiratenu yevatel nidro et gezeiratenu* — if his *neder* preceded the communal *gezeira* declaring those days as *yamim tovim*, his *neder* overrides the *gezeira* and he fasts even on those days. Conversely, *v'im gezeiratenu kodemet lenidro tevatel gezeiratenu et nidro* — if the communal decree preceded his vow, the *gezeira* wins out. Both Rashi and Tosafot understand "preceded" not temporally within a single year but rather that the person made his *neder* before those days were ever declared as *yamim tovim*. Once those days are on the calendar, they are there permanently; the rule effectively means that only someone who made his vow in the year the decree was first promulgated could be "grandfathered in." For any subsequent year, the days were already established and would take precedence. This, then, is a narrow window — and really the ruling was only practically relevant in that first year of the decree.

### Making Up Fasts

If the *gezeira* overrides the individual's *neder*, what happens to the fast he was supposed to observe on that day? There is a discussion about whether he must *mashlim* — make it up on a different day. Rashi holds that the *neder* is simply set aside entirely: *yidache nidro mikol v'chol v'lo yashlim oto ta'anit* — the *neder* is deferred entirely and he does not make up that fast. Tosafot expresses uncertainty and suggests one should be *machmir*.

The very concept of "making up" a fast is worth pausing over. Where else do we encounter this idea of compensation for a missed religious obligation? In *tefillah* — one who missed a *tefillah* may say *tashlumin*. The parallel is not coincidental; it reinforces the deep structural affinity between *ta'anit* and *tefillah* noted earlier.

### Reification

But there is a further dimension to the *mashlim* concept. One only "makes up" something if there is a discrete unit to be made up. If the *neder* were simply a commitment to a behavioral practice — I will not eat on Thursdays — then a missed Thursday is simply a missed Thursday; there is nothing to carry forward. One does not "make up" a practice. But if the *ta'anit* is conceived as a thing — a unit, an object with status, like a *nazir*'s period of *nezirut* — then it makes sense to say that the unit was not fulfilled and can be discharged on another occasion. This is what is meant by the reification of the fast: the *ta'anit* is treated not merely as a behavioral practice but as a formal status, an object in the halakhic sense, that can be deferred, counted, and transferred. This connects to the earlier discussion about *neder* and *kabbalah*: a *neder* typically creates a status on a thing, not merely a commitment to a behavior. What the individual is doing when he accepts a *ta'anit* is creating the status of *yom ta'anit* on a day, in much the same way a *nazir* creates a status upon himself. That status, once created, can be fulfilled on an alternate day if circumstances prevent it on the designated one.

## Eating & Day Status

### Eating Until When

The *gemara* now addresses a different dimension of the personal fast: until what point in the night may one eat before the fast day begins? *Tanu Rabbanan*: *eimatai ochel v'shoteh* — until when may one eat and drink? *Ad she'ya'aleh amud hashachar* — until the *amud hashachar*, the break of dawn, approximately seventy-two minutes before sunrise, which is the halakhic onset of the day. This is the view of Rabbi. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon says: *ad krias hagever* — until the crowing of the cock, which is generally understood to be a somewhat later point than *amud hashachar*.

### Finishing the Meal

Abaye introduces a crucial qualification: *lo shanu ela shelo gamar seudaso* — the ruling that one may eat until *amud hashachar* applies only when one has not yet concluded one's meal. *Aval gamar seudaso eino ochel* — but once one has finished the meal, one may not return to eating. The logic is that finishing the meal implicitly signifies the beginning of the fast; the person has, in effect, concluded his pre-fast eating and begun his *ta'anit*, even if the formal halakhic start of the day has not yet arrived.

### Rising & Sleep

The *gemara* immediately challenges this. *Gamar v'amad harei zeh ochel* — if one finished and got up from the table, one may still eat. This seems to contradict Abaye's position. The resolution: *hasam k'shelo silek* — that case refers to when one has not yet cleared the table; the food is still present. Rising from the table while the food remains does not constitute the conclusion of the meal. But once one removes the food from the table, that seals the fast.

A second version of the dispute is then presented. Some say it was Rava who ruled: *lo shanu ela k'shelo yashan* — the rule that one may continue eating applies only when one has not yet slept. *Aval yashan eino ochel* — but once one has slept, one may no longer eat, as sleep signifies the transition into the new day. Abaye challenges this as well: *yashan v'amad harei zeh ochel* — if one slept and got up, one may still eat. The resolution: *hasam b'misnamnem* — that refers to the case of dozing, not full sleep.

### Dozing State

What constitutes *misnamnem* — dozing? Rav Ashi defines it: *nim v'lo nim teir v'lo teir* — asleep but not asleep, awake but not awake. The practical test: *d'kru leh v'anei* — if called, one responds, *v'lo yada ahadorei svara* — but one cannot formulate a coherent answer or reason. *V'chi madkru leh madkar* — if reminded of something, one recalls it. This is the liminal state that does not constitute true sleep and therefore does not seal the onset of the fast.

### Intent & Preparation

The practical *halakha* that emerges from this discussion is nuanced in terms of intent. If one woke before *amud hashachar* without having planned to do so — simply an unintended awakening — and one had not set an alarm or formed any prior intention to wake and eat, then one may not eat. But if one deliberately set an alarm, or had in mind when going to sleep that one might wake up and eat, then an unplanned awakening before *amud hashachar* would also permit eating. What governs is not merely the physical state of sleep or wakefulness but the prior intent and preparation.

This is itself illuminating about the nature of *ta'anit*. The fast formally begins only in the morning — it is a day fast — yet the determination of whether one may eat in the hours before dawn depends on one's earlier intentions and on whether one has concluded the pre-fast eating phase. This reinforces the concept discussed earlier: the entire day is a *yom ta'anit*, and even though eating is permitted the prior evening, the status of the day as a *yom ta'anit* means that once one's eating concludes, one enters the fast's orbit. The *seudah mafseket* — the final meal — is so called precisely because it "cuts off" the eating, not merely because it happens to be the last one eaten.

## Personal vs Communal

### Shoe Precaution

Rav Chana rules: *yachid shekibel alav ta'anit asur binedilat hasandal* — an individual who has accepted a personal fast is forbidden to wear shoes. This is surprising. A personal *ta'anit*, as the *gemara* has established, is a lighter form of fasting: other *inuyim* such as anointing, bathing, and marital relations are not prohibited. Why then should shoes be different?

The reason given is: *chashinan shema ta'anit tzibur kibel alav* — we are concerned that perhaps when he accepted the *ta'anit*, he was not specific and may have intended a *ta'anit tzibur*, not merely a personal *ta'anit yachid*. The uncertainty about which type of fast he accepted means that we must apply the stringencies of *ta'anit tzibur* in the area most publicly observable.

### Fast Types as Objects

This ruling contains within it a remarkable conceptual assumption: that it is possible for an individual to accept upon himself a *ta'anit tzibur*. How can a *yachid* accept a *ta'anit tzibur*? By definition, a *ta'anit tzibur* is something decreed by the community. Yet the *gemara* treats this as a coherent possibility. The reason is the reification of fast types discussed earlier: *ta'anit tzibur* is not merely a sociological description of who is fasting — it is a formal object, a type of fast with its own properties, that can be "selected" by a *yachid* in his *kabbalah*. One might say in one's *davening*: *hareini b'ta'anit tzibur l'machar* — specifying the more stringent variety, just as one might select among different halakhic categories of something. This is the full implication of treating the fast as a thing rather than merely a practice.

The specific properties of a full-blown *ta'anit tzibur* — as distinguished from the initial lighter communal fasts — include beginning from the night before, prohibiting shoes, bathing, anointing, and marital relations. It is this full-blown version that Shmuel had in mind when he said there is no *ta'anit tzibur* in Babylon except Tisha B'Av.

### Rav Sheshet Challenge

The *gemara* then recounts a sharp exchange. Rabbinic students came to *bei ta'anita* — the place where the community was observing a fast — while wearing shoes. Rav Sheshet noticed this and challenged them: *dilema meichol nami achol* — perhaps you are also eating? In other words, if you are wearing shoes during a fast, you might as well be eating — the shoes betray a failure to observe even the most publicly visible *inuy*.

### Compromise Measures

The *gemara* records a range of practices among the Amoraim for this situation. Abaye and Rava would come in wearing a *hapantza* — some form of slipper or partial footwear, not a full shoe — as a compromise. Mar Zutra would exchange his shoes from foot to foot, wearing the right shoe on the left foot and vice versa, as a *shinuy* — a modification that signals that this is not ordinary shoe-wearing.

### Babylonian Practice

The rabbinic students of Rav Ashi's academy went out *ki'orchayhu* — in their normal manner, with shoes on their proper feet. Their reasoning: *savri ki'ha d'amar Shmuel ein ta'anit tzibur b'Bavel ela Tisha B'Av bilvad* — they ruled in accordance with Shmuel's position that there is no *ta'anit tzibur* in Babylon except for Tisha B'Av. This does not mean there were never communal fasts in Babylon; clearly the *gemara* is describing a communal fast in progress. What it means is that no communal fast in Babylon ever reaches the halakhic weight and stringency of what is technically called a *ta'anit tzibur* — a fast that begins the night before and carries all the *inuyim*. Babylon simply does not need rain desperately enough for fasts to escalate to that level, and therefore the shoe prohibition, which belongs to that heavier category of fast, never applies.

### Community Perception

Yet this ruling creates a public perception problem. The individual who knows the *halakha* — that in Babylon a communal fast never acquires the full stringencies of *ta'anit tzibur* and shoes are therefore permitted — acts on that knowledge and goes about normally. But the community at large is observing the fast with a degree of stringency, refraining from shoes. When a rabbinic student walks into the *shul* with shoes on a fast day, it creates the appearance that he is not participating in the community's observance. This is the perennial tension between the *yachid* and the *tzibur*: the individual who knows a leniency and acts upon it individually risks a form of public separation from the community's practice — a concern that the *gemara* takes seriously throughout this discussion.
