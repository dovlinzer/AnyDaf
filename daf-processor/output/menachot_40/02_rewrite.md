# Menachot 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzitzit Obligation

### Wool & Linen Only

The *daf* properly begins a few lines before the top of 40, picking up from the bottom of 39b. There, Rav Nachman cites the *Tana Debei Rabbi Yishmael* on the question of which garments are obligated in *tzitzit*. The position of the school of Rabbi Yishmael runs as follows: wherever the Torah speaks of a *beged* without further specification, and then specifies wool or linen in one particular instance — as it does by *shatnez* ("*lo tilbash shatnez tzemer u'pishtim yachdav*") and by *nega'im* ("*beged tzemer o beged pishtim*") — we derive that whenever the Torah says *beged*, it means specifically a wool or linen garment. The conclusion is *af kol tzemer u'pishtim*: whenever the Torah mentions a garment in the context of a *mitzvah*, it intends only wool or linen. Accordingly, garments made of cotton, silk, or other materials would carry only a rabbinic obligation in *tzitzit*, not a biblical one.

### Contradictory Teachings

Abaye immediately raises a difficulty: *ve'hai Tana Debei Rabbi Yishmael mehapech me'idach Tana Debei Rabbi Yishmael* — this teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael appears to contradict another teaching from the very same school. That second teaching begins by acknowledging that when the Torah mentions *beged* in the context of *tum'ah* — whether in the laws of *nega'im* (as Rashi holds) or in the laws of what becomes *tamei* through contact with a *sheretz* (as Tosafot prefers) — one might think only a standard woolen garment is included. Yet the verse then says "*o beged*," and the inclusive word *o* is taken to broaden the category to include garments of camel hair, rabbit wool, goat hair, and various forms of silk. The very same school of Rabbi Yishmael that restricts *beged* to wool and linen in one context appears to expand it dramatically in another.

Tosafot presses further: by *tzitzit*, the Torah uses the phrase *al ha-kanaf*, from which the *Gemara* derives *min kanaf* — seemingly including all types of garments. By *tum'ah*, the broadening comes from the word *o beged*. It is therefore difficult to determine which ruling is the general principle and which is a particular exception. Tosafot acknowledges that the *Gemara*'s comparison across these contexts is puzzling, and offers no fully satisfying resolution. The underlying question — whether non-wool-and-linen garments are obligated in *tzitzit* at a biblical or merely rabbinic level — thus remains genuinely contested.

### Practical Stringency

The practical stakes of this debate are significant. If one simply happens to be wearing a four-cornered garment, the question of whether the obligation is biblical or rabbinic matters little in practice; either way one is obligated in *tzitzit* and recites a *beracha*. The question becomes acute because of the widespread *minhag* to go out of one's way to wear a four-cornered garment specifically in order to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. If one is deliberately seeking to fulfill the *mitzvah*, one would naturally prefer to fulfill it at the biblical level. This is the reasoning behind the practice of wearing wool *tzitzit* even on a *tallit katan* — since a wool garment is definitively obligated *de'oraita* according to all opinions.

That said, this *chumra* can paradoxically produce a *kula*. In warm weather, wool *tzitzit* on an undershirt can become sufficiently uncomfortable that a person abandons wearing *tzitzit* altogether. The *Mishnah Berurah* recommends the stringency of wool, but a person who finds it genuinely unmanageable is far better served by wearing cotton *tzitzit* — on which all agree there is at least a *derabbanan* obligation — than by resolving to wear wool and then wearing nothing at all.

### Ashkenazi/Sephardi

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules in accordance with the *Tana Debei Rabbi Yishmael* as cited by Rav Nachman — that only wool and linen garments are biblically obligated in *tzitzit*, and all other materials carry only a rabbinic obligation. The *Rama* rules the other way, holding that all garments are obligated at a biblical level. The question of synthetic materials — fabrics that are neither wool, linen, nor any traditional fiber — raises its own distinct set of considerations that extend beyond what the *Gemara* here directly addresses.

## Sadin Bitzitzit

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

Turning now to the top of 40a, the *Gemara* presents a *baraita*: *sadin bitzitzit* — the case of a linen garment in which one seeks to attach *tzitzit*. *Beit Shammai potrin u'Veit Hillel mechayvin*: Beit Shammai exempts such a garment from *tzitzit*, while Beit Hillel obligates it. The tension, as Rashi notes, is that if one is exempt from *tzitzit* in a linen garment and nevertheless attaches wool strings — and *techelet* must be wool — one will be wearing *shatnez*. So Beit Shammai's ruling amounts not merely to an exemption but to a prohibition against attaching wool *tzitzit* to a linen garment at all.

One might initially think the problem is narrower: if the non-blue strings need not be wool, why not simply attach white linen strings and omit the blue? The answer appears to be that Beit Shammai follows the position — consistent with the view of Rebbi, against the *Mishnah* — that *techelet* is *me'akev* the white: the blue is so integral to the *mitzvah* that without it, the white strings cannot stand alone. Since one cannot attach valid *techelet* in a linen garment without incurring *shatnez*, and since without valid *techelet* the white is also invalid, Beit Shammai concludes that a linen garment is entirely exempt. To put wool strings on it anyway would constitute a transgression of *shatnez*.

### Techelet Requirement

The deeper question is: given that the Torah itself juxtaposes *shatnez* and *tzitzit* — a juxtaposition understood to permit *shatnez* in the context of *tzitzit* — why does Beit Shammai not simply permit it? And whatever happened to *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*, the principle that a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition?

### Rashi's Explanations

Rashi offers two distinct readings of Beit Shammai's position. The first is that Beit Shammai simply does not accept the *drasha* that the Torah's juxtaposition of *shatnez* and *tzitzit* constitutes a blanket permission to wear *shatnez* in *tzitzit*. On this reading, Beit Shammai holds that *shatnez* remains prohibited even in this context, leaving the linen garment fully exempt — and any wool strings added to it a violation.

The second explanation, which fits more naturally with the continuation of the *Gemara*, is that Beit Shammai is speaking at a rabbinic level only. Biblically, Beit Shammai would agree that a linen garment is obligated in *tzitzit* and that *shatnez* is permitted in that context. But Beit Shammai is concerned: if one becomes accustomed to wearing *shatnez* strings in one's *tzitzit*, one may come to wear *shatnez* in situations where it is not permitted — for instance, when the blue strings are invalid. Better, therefore, to prohibit it rabbinically across the board. This second reading resolves a further difficulty: on the first reading, Beit Shammai's language says one is "exempt" rather than that one "transgresses," which is odd if wool strings in a linen garment are an outright biblical violation. On the second reading, there is no actual transgression — only a rabbinic exemption designed as a safeguard.

### Rabbinic Authority

Rashi himself raises the obvious objection: if Beit Shammai prohibits attaching *tzitzit* to a linen garment entirely, then one is left walking around with a four-cornered linen garment bearing no *tzitzit* at all. Does the rabbinic authority to protect against potential transgression extend to directing a person to passively fail to fulfill a biblical positive commandment? Rashi answers that yes, this is precisely a power the rabbis possess — to instruct one to *shev ve'al ta'aseh*, to passively refrain from a *mitzvah*, when they deem it necessary. The precedents are familiar: the rabbis prohibited sounding the *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* that falls on *Shabbat*, and taking the *lulav* on *Sukkot* that falls on *Shabbat*. Even in cases that might seem to involve a *bitul asei*, not merely passive restraint, as long as the person's own action is passive, the rabbinic authority extends that far.

This reading of Beit Shammai is, in a sense, its own philosophical statement: so concerned is Beit Shammai about the possibility that permitting *shatnez* in *tzitzit* will eventually lead to an actual *shatnez* violation, that they are willing to tell a person not to perform a biblical *mitzvah* entirely. Beit Hillel, by contrast, says: the Torah permits it, you are obligated, do it — and do not manufacture concerns about what might go wrong. *Ve'halacha k'divrei Beit Hillel*, and the ruling follows Beit Hillel.

## Shatnez & Ignorance

### Jerusalem Practice

The *baraita* itself records that the *halacha* follows Beit Hillel. Yet immediately afterward, Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok observes: *v'lo kol hamatilah techelet b'sadin b'Yerushalayim eino ela min hamitmitemin* — anyone who placed *techelet* in a linen garment in Jerusalem was regarded with shock and astonishment. Even according to Beit Hillel, the practice in Jerusalem was simply not done. The *Shita Mekubetzet* adds the words *techelet b'sadin*, making the linen garment explicit. Whatever the technical *halacha*, the pious circles of Jerusalem — where there appears to have been a norm of heightened stringency in such matters — maintained that one did not wear blue strings in a linen garment.

### Ignorance Problem

Rabbi then asks: *im ken lama asruha* — if it is technically permitted, indeed if it is a *mitzvah*, why was it treated as forbidden? The *Gemara* answers: *mipnei she'ein beki'in* — because people are not knowledgeable. Rashi explains: the general population does not know that *shatnez* is permitted in *tzitzit*. If they see someone wearing wool strings in a linen garment, they will not think "ah, here is someone fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* according to Beit Hillel." They will think: here is someone wearing *shatnez* — and they will conclude that *shatnez* must be generally permissible, and begin wearing *shatnez* themselves even where it is not a *mitzvah* context at all. This is a concern closely related to *maris ayin* — but with a crucial nuance: the ignorance here is ignorance of *halacha*, not ignorance of fact.

This distinction matters enormously. The classic case of *maris ayin* involves ignorance of fact: I melt soy cheese on a hamburger and the observer, not knowing that my cheese is pareve, thinks I am eating a genuine cheeseburger. There, the observer's error is about what is happening, not about what the law permits. In our *Gemara*, however, everyone can plainly see what is happening — wool strings in a linen garment — and the error is that they do not know this is permitted by law. Rav Moshe Feinstein rules that *maris ayin* does not apply when the ignorance is of the law rather than of the facts: we cannot be held hostage to people's lack of halakhic knowledge; the appropriate response is to educate them.

### Public Demonstration

Rava bar Rav Chana proposes to Rava: *v'lirmu beia asarah* — why not gather ten upstanding people, have them go out into the marketplace wearing linen garments with proper *techelet*, and *u'mefarsem l'milsa* — publicize the matter? Rather than accommodating the public's ignorance by restricting the practice, challenge that ignorance directly. Make it visible and widespread, and eventually people's assumptions will shift.

### Demonstration Fails

The *Gemara* rejects this: *kol shekein d'metamhei alayhu* — on the contrary, this would only increase the astonishment and censure directed at those individuals. Ignorance, when deeply ingrained, does not yield to counter-examples. Rather than reconsidering their assumptions, people will simply condemn those doing it — or, as a student perceptively noted, in an era of greater sectarianism, people might regard the practice as the mark of an aberrant group rather than as legitimate *halachic* behavior. The *Gemara* is making an astute psychological observation: deeply ingrained assumptions tend to reinterpret disconfirming evidence rather than revise themselves.

### Teaching Solution

The *Gemara* then proposes what seems like the correct solution: *v'lidrosh b'pirka* — why not address the ignorance directly through public teaching? Gather people at the communal learning sessions and expound clearly that *shatnez* is permitted in *tzitzit*, that this is a special dispensation carved out by the Torah, and then people will understand what they see and draw the right conclusions.

This is a striking moment in the *Gemara*. It insists that one should not give in to ignorance of the law. Public teaching — *limmud* — is the right instrument to address it, and it is a more appropriate response than either suppressing a legitimate practice or staging a public demonstration that will only backfire. The ultimate answer the *Gemara* offers, however, is that a different concern entirely prevents us from allowing this practice even if we were to teach about it: the problem of *kala ilan*.

## False Techelet

### Kala Ilan Concern

The *Gemara* now pivots to what it identifies as the real reason behind the restriction: *gezeirah mishum kala ilan* — a rabbinic decree on account of the artificial dye *kala ilan*. *Kala ilan* was a plant-based indigo dye that was visually indistinguishable from genuine *techelet* but was far cheaper to produce. As a result, a significant market developed in which merchants sold *kala ilan* at a discounted price for *techelet*, often deceiving buyers who believed they were purchasing the authentic dye. The concern is therefore not primarily about public perception or ignorance of *halacha* — it is about the real possibility that the wool strings a person attaches to a linen garment will not be genuine *techelet* at all, but *kala ilan*.

If the strings are *kala ilan* and not true *techelet*, the person is not fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *techelet*. And if they are not fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *techelet*, there is no *mitzvah* basis for the *shatnez* — meaning the wool strings in the linen garment constitute a prohibited mixture without any corresponding *mitzvah* to justify it.

### No Techelet = Shatnez

The *Gemara* then asks: even if the *techelet* turns out to be *kala ilan*, why not treat the strings as equivalent to white strings — *v'lo yehei ella lavan* — and still permit the practice on the basis of the *mitzvah* of white *tzitzit*? The *Gemara* rejects this by invoking the principle of Reish Lakish: *kol makom she'atah motzei asei v'lo ta'asei*, whenever a positive commandment conflicts with a negative prohibition, if one can fulfill the positive commandment without triggering the negative — *im ata yachol lekayeim et shneihem mutav* — one must find that alternative. Only where no alternative exists does *asei docheh lo ta'asei* apply. Here, since it is entirely possible to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* using linen strings in a linen garment — *keivan d'efshar b'minah lo* — there is no justification for using wool strings and thereby creating *shatnez*. The *mitzvah* does not override the prohibition when an alternative is available.

### Tosafot's View

On the surface, this seems to conflict with discussions elsewhere in the tractate that appeared to grant a blanket exemption from *shatnez* concerns whenever one is attaching *tzitzit* — for instance, the case where a wool garment is given linen *tzitzit* strings for the white, even though there was technically no compulsion to do so. Tosafot addresses this directly: *lav d'bamaskana dechutei pishtan potrim beshel tzemar* — that broader exemption, where linen strings in a wool garment are permitted despite the *shatnez*, applies specifically *hainu behadi techelet* — only when genuine *techelet* is also present. *Demigo d'techelet patra beshel pishtan lavan nami patar*: it is the presence of *techelet* that generates the free pass. *Techelet* is the paradigmatic case where *shatnez* is unambiguously permitted in *tzitzit* — wool strings in a linen garment, with no alternative. Once *techelet* is present, Tosafot argues, it elevates the entire *tzitzit* to a category in which *shatnez* concerns are wholly suspended — *hutra*, not merely *dechuyah*. All the other strings, including the white, ride along with that suspension.

But when *techelet* is absent — whether because it was never attached or because what was attached turns out to be *kala ilan* — there is no free pass. The *tzitzit* must then be evaluated under ordinary *asei docheh lo ta'asei* principles, and since an alternative exists (linen strings), the *shatnez* of wool strings is impermissible. Tosafot's reading carries a broader implication for the entire *perek*: genuine *techelet* is not merely an additional dimension of the *mitzvah*, an optional enhancement. It is qualitatively transformative. *Tzitzit* with *techelet* is a full and different category of *tzitzit*, one so complete that *shatnez* concerns have no foothold within it. This resonates with the *bigdei kehunah*, where *techelet* was interwoven with linen in the priestly garments — suggesting that *techelet*-bearing garments occupy a distinct halakhic space, beyond the ordinary reach of *kilayim*.

### Rejected Solutions

Given the problem of *kala ilan*, the *Gemara* considers several possible remedies. First: *u'livdeki* — why not institute rabbinic inspectors who circulate through the market, test the dye being sold, and certify its authenticity? There are indeed established tests for distinguishing *techelet* from *kala ilan*. The *Gemara* rejects this: *ella gezeirah mishum ta'ama* — there is a concern that in running the test, the inspector takes a sample, performs the test, and then pours the tested dye back into the main vat, thereby invalidating the entire vat. Even legitimate *techelet* would be corrupted by this procedure. So inspection would itself cause damage to the supply it was meant to protect.

Second: *lichtov adiski* — why not post public notices? These could inform buyers of the criteria for genuine *techelet*, or instruct those doing inspections not to pour the tested sample back into the vat. The *Gemara* dismisses this as well: *adiskatei liku u'lismoch* — one cannot rely on posted notices. People are inattentive and inconsistent; the kind of individual-level compliance required here, particularly in the moment after one has already run the test and is casually handling the remainder, cannot be secured by a placard. The *Gemara*'s sensitivity to human nature is notable: on the one hand, it refuses to capitulate to ignorance of law; on the other hand, it is realistic about the limits of what formal mechanisms — inspectors, notices — can actually achieve in practice.

## Spilling Back Dye

### Testing Invalidates

The *Gemara* now examines the *gezeirah mishum ta'ama* concern more closely — the problem that testing the dye and then spilling the tested portion back into the vat renders the vat invalid. This is the specific mechanism that defeats the inspector solution. One who runs the authenticity test on a sample of dye and then returns that sample to the main supply has, in the process, invalidated what would otherwise be *kosher techelet*. The *Gemara* later addresses what precisely makes pouring back the tested dye problematic; for now, the concern is established as the reason why a rabbinic inspection regime cannot adequately police the *techelet* market.

Alongside this concern runs the earlier proposal of posting notices — which could address the problem by instructing inspectors not to pour the sample back. The *Gemara* treats both solutions together: neither inspection alone nor posted notices accompanying inspection is sufficient to reliably protect the integrity of the dye supply.

### Holiday Announcements

Rava in the name of Rav Yosef pushes back: surely we can rely on notices. The *Gemara* has now turned to 40b. Rav Yosef argues: *chametz b'Pesach v'Yom HaKippurim d'kares samchinan* — when the rabbinic court intercalated a leap year, adding an extra month to the calendar, they sent notices out to the diaspora to inform communities of the change, and we relied on those communities receiving and heeding those notices for matters as serious as observing *Yom Kippur* on the correct day and avoiding *chametz* on the correct date of *Pesach* — transgressions punishable by *karet*. If we rely on notices in those high-stakes communal contexts, should we not certainly be able to rely on notices for a simpler matter like this?

There is, however, a meaningful distinction. The calendar announcement is received and acted upon communally: when a community receives the notice that a leap year has been declared, everyone adjusts together, and compliance is essentially built into the social structure. Here, by contrast, the question is whether individual inspectors — or individual consumers performing their own tests — will read and follow instructions about what to do with the small tested sample in the moment after the test. The communal and the individual are very different settings for reliance on a written notice. Rava himself, however, seems prepared to accept the analogy and to think that notices could in principle work — leading to the *Gemara*'s final reason for the prohibition, which overrides all proposed solutions.

## Corner Tearing

### Tearing & Sewing

The *Gemara*'s ultimate answer, cited by Rava as having been stated in Eretz Yisrael in the name of Rabbi Zeira, is the following: *shema yikara sadino betoch gimel ve'yitferenu, v'haTorah amrah ta'aseh velo min ha'asuy*. The concern is that the corner of one's linen garment — the very corner from which the *tzitzit* hang — will tear, and the owner will sew it back onto the garment. The Torah requires *ta'aseh*, an active making of *tzitzit*; it disqualifies *min ha'asuy*, a situation in which the *tzitzit* were not actively attached to the garment but effectively pre-existed the garment in its current form. When a person sews a torn corner — which still has its *tzitzit* — back onto the garment, the *tzitzit* have not been newly attached; rather, a piece of cloth bearing pre-existing *tzitzit* has been reincorporated. This violates *ta'aseh velo min ha'asuy*, and the *tzitzit* are therefore invalid.

In an ordinary case, invalid *tzitzit* are a problem of failing to fulfill the *mitzvah* — unfortunate, but the person walks around in a garment without proper *tzitzit*. Here, however, the garment is linen with wool strings, and the moment the *tzitzit* are invalid, there is no *mitzvah* to justify the *shatnez*. The person is now wearing *shatnez* without any offsetting *mitzvah* obligation, and is actively transgressing. This is why the linen-garment scenario raises concerns that go beyond the usual worry about improper *tzitzit*.

### Three Tefachim

The *Gemara* specifies that the concern arises when the corner tears *betoch gimel* — within three. Tosafot clarifies the meaning: this does not necessarily mean the corner tore off entirely within three *tefachim* of the edge. Rather, Tosafot reads it to mean that the corner is partially torn and is still attached to the garment by a strip of fabric. If that strip of remaining connection is at least three *tefachim* wide, the corner is still considered part of the garment, and sewing it more firmly in place does not constitute an act of *asiyah* that would be problematic. But if the strip of connection is less than three *tefachim* wide, the corner is effectively considered as if it has been severed entirely. When the person sews it back on in that state, it is *ta'aseh velo min ha'asuy* — the reattachment of a corner bearing pre-formed *tzitzit* — and the *tzitzit* are invalid.

### Shatnez Transgression

The practical consequence is as described above: once the *tzitzit* are invalid by virtue of *ta'aseh velo min ha'asuy*, the wool strings in the linen garment are no longer protected by the *mitzvah*, and the *shatnez* prohibition reasserts itself. This parallels the *kala ilan* analysis: in both cases, the *shatnez* is tolerable only as long as the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* is being genuinely fulfilled. Any defect in the *tzitzit* — whether in the dye's authenticity or in the manner of attachment — strips away the *mitzvah* justification and exposes the underlying *shatnez* violation.

It is worth noting the connection to the *Mishnah* in the previous chapter, which contained the opinion of Rabbi Yose HaGlili that each of the four corners constitutes a separate *mitzvah*. Even according to his view, the problematic fourth corner — the one that was torn off and sewn back on — may not qualify as part of the garment at all, placing it outside the category of *tzitzit* entirely. But since the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Yose HaGlili, this remains a side consideration.

### Shammai/Hillel

Returning to the broader frame, this final answer — the concern of *ta'aseh velo min ha'asuy* — is structurally identical to the *kala ilan* concern: in both cases, something goes wrong that invalidates the *tzitzit*, and the invalidation turns the *shatnez* from a permitted exception into an active transgression. Beit Shammai's approach, as the *Gemara* has illuminated it, embodies a general posture of extreme caution: it is better to passively forgo the *mitzvat asei* of *tzitzit* than to risk an active transgression of *shatnez*. Beit Hillel counters that the Torah itself sanctions the practice, one is obligated to perform it, and one should not manufacture anxiety about what might go wrong.

This disagreement captures something deep in the *machloket* between the two schools. Beit Shammai begins from the top — from the anxiety of transgression, from the imperative to avoid any possible violation — and is willing to accept passive non-fulfillment of a *mitzvah* as the price. Beit Hillel begins from the bottom — from the human endeavor of *mitzvah* fulfillment, from the obligation to act — and takes proper precautions without retreating from the command itself. The *halacha* follows Beit Hillel.
