# Yoma 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed Money Concern

### Dead Owner Problem

Today's *daf* is 56, and we pick up in the middle of 55b. The *Gemara* was trying to figure out why Rabbi Yehuda required only one pedestal rather than two. The first suggestion was that he did not trust labeling — that people fail to pay attention to writing — and that this concern was reflected in his position regarding the separate *shofrot* in the *Beis Hamikdash* for obligatory bird offerings, which are divided between *chatas* and *olah*, and those that are pure *nidavah*, which are entirely *olah*. The concern was about mixtures.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he offered a different explanation: *gezeira mishum chatas shemesu ba'aleha* — the concern is not about confusing the free-will money with the obligatory money, but specifically about a *chatas* whose owner has died. The underlying problem is that someone may contribute money for an obligatory *chatas* and then die before the sacrifice is brought. Even if this happens on the same day, if by the time the money is used the owner has died, the *korban* would be invalid. The "mixture" is not a mixture of two different designations, but rather a mixture within the pool of *chatas* money — some of it potentially belonging to a person who has since died.

### Overseas Sacrifice

This prompts an immediate objection: *umi chaishinan*? Are we really concerned about this? The *Mishnah* teaches that *hasholeach chatato mimedinat hayam* — one who sends his *chatas* from overseas — *makrivin oto be-chezkat she-hu kayam*: the sacrifice is brought on the presumption that the owner is still alive. That is precisely the same scenario. The person is not present, the sacrifice is brought in his absence, and we rely on the *chazaka* that someone alive when the money was given is presumed to still be alive. Why should placing money in a box and walking away be any different? Rabbi Yehuda presumably does not dispute the ruling about the overseas *chatas*.

### Definite Contamination

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela mishum chatas shemesu ba'aleha vadai* — the concern is not merely that the owner *might* have died, but that he *definitely* has died. Under ordinary circumstances, the principle of *chazaka* applies: if someone was alive when the money was deposited, we assume he remains alive, and the money may be used. But consider the following scenario: money is deposited throughout the day, and then word reaches the *Beis Hamikdash* that one of the contributors died that very day. Now the priests know with certainty that somewhere in that box is money that cannot be used. The problem is that they cannot identify which specific coins came from the deceased. The money is irretrievably mixed together, and this is the *ta'aroves* — the mixture — that creates the problem. Because such a scenario will occasionally arise, the practice of collecting money and using it without the contributor present was avoided entirely.

This is plainly distinct from the case of the overseas *chatas*, where there is no mixing — a single person sent a specific animal or specific funds, and nothing is intermingled with anyone else's contribution.

### Remove Money Solution

The *Gemara* then proposes a seemingly elegant solution: *v'nivror arba'a zuzei v'nishdi b'maya v'hanach mishtarin* — take four *zuz* (Rashi calculates this as roughly the average cost of a bird offering), remove that amount from the box, throw it into the ocean, and let the remainder be permissible. The idea is to simply remove from the pool an amount equivalent to the invalid contribution and declare the rest usable.

This is a striking suggestion. It would be analogous to saying: if some *treif* product is mixed into a pot, simply take a spoonful out and assume that was the *treif* portion. This is not how *kashrus* works. The underlying logic the *Gemara* may be reaching toward is that the money should in principle be *batel b'rov* — nullified within the majority — and perhaps we are only dealing with a rabbinic stringency that blocks that nullification. In situations where *bitul* ought to apply in principle but is blocked by stringency, the *Gemara* elsewhere sometimes permits the reasoning known as *she-ani omer* — "I can claim" — to achieve a similar result. For instance, if two boxes (one of *terumah* and one of *chulin*) fall into two larger containers (also one *terumah* and one *chulin*), and one cannot tell which box fell into which, the *Gemara* permits assuming the *chulin* fell into the *chulin* and the *terumah* into the *terumah* — reasoning that since *bitul* would have applied anyway given the rabbinic nature of the prohibition, we can allow ourselves to make favorable assumptions. The *Gemara* here may be suggesting something similar: in principle the money should be *batel*, and taking out a fixed sum and assuming it was the invalid portion functions as a kind of *she-ani omer*.

### Bittul Question

The reason this solution fails for Rabbi Yehuda is then given: *leis lei breira* — Rabbi Yehuda does not hold of *breira*. The *Gemara* will explain exactly what *breira* is and why its absence blocks this approach. But it is worth noting at this stage that the solution being proposed — taking four *zuz* and assuming they are the ones that must be removed — is actually not quite *breira* in the classical sense, a distinction the *Gemara* will return to.

## Breira Concept

### R. Yehuda & Breira

The *Gemara* now asks: *minalan*? How do we know that Rabbi Yehuda does not hold of *breira*? Before examining the proofs, it is worth establishing clearly what *breira* means. *Breira* is not the same as the quasi-magical *she-ani omer* maneuver just discussed. *Breira* is rather the concept that something undefined in the present can have its identity retroactively determined by what happens later. There is a certain deterministic or retrospective quality to it: something that is indeterminate now becomes determinate through a future event, and that future event is then treated as if it had already determined the matter from the beginning.

### Wine Separation Case

The proof is sought from a *Mishnah*: *haloke'ach yayin mibein hakutim* — one who purchases wine from Samaritans. Samaritans are assumed to have taken *terumah* and *maaser* for their own consumption, but they cannot be trusted to have done so on behalf of others. If someone purchases wine from them at *bein hashmashos* on *erev Shabbos* — just as it is becoming dark — what is to be done? One cannot separate *terumah* and *maaser* on *Shabbos*, but the wine cannot be drunk without those separations having been made.

### Wine Process Detail

The *Mishnah* records that Rabbi Meir permits the following procedure: one stands and declares, *shnei lugin she-ani asid le-hafrish harei hein trumah* — "the two *log* that I will separate [after *Shabbos*] shall be *trumah* from this moment." Similarly, one designates ten *log* as *maaser rishon* and nine as *maaser sheini*, all defined by reference to whatever amounts one will actually set aside after *Shabbos*. To make the arithmetic clear: if one has 102 *log* of wine, two *log* become *trumah*, leaving 100. Ten of those become *maaser rishon*, leaving 90. Nine of those — ten percent of the remaining 90 — become *maaser sheini*.

This declaration is made before *Shabbos* sets in, and then one may drink from the wine on *Shabbos* without concern, because whatever one drinks will not be the portion one will later designate. The portions one will later set aside are already deemed to have been the *trumah* and *maaser* from the moment of the original declaration. One then *u-meichal ve-shoseh mi-yad* — immediately goes ahead and drinks. Rashi interprets *meichal* as *mechaleil* — redeeming the *maaser sheini* onto a coin — while Tosafos reads it as *matchil*, meaning one simply begins drinking right away, since the act of redemption is beside the point at this stage.

Tosafos also notes the comparison to a parallel *Mishnah* dealing with wine purchased from an *am ha'aretz* on *erev Shabbos*, and works through the precise differences between the two cases. The key distinctions are: by an *am ha'aretz*, one does not need to take *terumah* since we assume the *am ha'aretz* already did so, whereas by a *Kuti* one must begin with *terumah*; and because the *am ha'aretz* case involves only a *safek* whether *terumos and ma'asros* were taken, greater latitude is afforded — one may even do this during *bein hashmashos* — whereas by a *Kuti*, where the failure to separate is certain, the declaration must be made before *bein hashmashos* arrives.

This ruling of Rabbi Meir is the principle of *breira*: at the moment of declaration, one is designating something whose precise identity is not yet determined. It will be defined later, when one actually sets the portions aside after *Shabbos*. Rabbi Meir holds that when the future designation occurs, it retroactively defines the *trumah* and *maaser* as having been those precise units from the time of the original declaration. Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosei, and Rabbi Shimon — as stated on 56b — forbid this practice. *Alma*, the *Gemara* concludes, they hold *ein breira*.

### Breira vs. Tenai

Before accepting this proof, the *Gemara* raises a challenge: *dilma shani hatam* — perhaps Rabbi Yehuda's objection to the wine case has nothing to do with *breira* at all. Perhaps he actually accepts *breira*, and his concern is simply practical: *shema yibaka ha-nod* — what if the barrel spills? If all the wine is consumed or lost before *Shabbos* ends, there will be nothing to designate, and the person will have been drinking *tevel* retroactively. Rabbi Meir responds *le-chisheyibaka* — I will deal with that if it happens; I am not troubled by the remote possibility. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, takes that risk seriously and therefore prohibits the procedure. On this reading, Rabbi Yehuda might still accept the principle of *breira*; he simply will not apply it in a situation where the retroactive designation might fail entirely.

This objection is important, and the *Gemara* accepts it: one cannot prove from the wine case alone that Rabbi Yehuda rejects *breira* in principle. A clearer proof is sought.

### Eiruv Conditions

The proof is brought from a teaching of Ayo: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, ein adam matneh al shnei devarim ke-echad* — a person cannot stipulate about two contradictory things at once. The specific case is the conditional *eiruv*. One wishes to visit one's *rebbe* on *Shabbos*, but does not know which direction the *rebbe* will be coming from — whether from the east or the west. A person wishes to place an *eiruv* accordingly and stipulate: if the *rebbe* comes from the east, my *eiruv* is to the east; if he comes from the west, my *eiruv* is to the west. Rabbi Yehuda forbids this. One may stipulate to a single direction — if the *rebbe* comes from the east, my *eiruv* is in the east — but one cannot stipulate for both directions simultaneously.

The *Gemara* asks: what is the difference? If Rabbi Yehuda rejects the two-direction stipulation because of *ein breira* — because what happens later on *Shabbos* cannot retroactively determine where the *eiruv* was placed on *erev Shabbos* — then why does he permit even the one-direction stipulation? That too seems to be a case of *breira*: the person places the *eiruv* in the east, and whether it takes effect depends on where the *rebbe* turns up on *Shabbos*.

### Yochanan's Resolution

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *kshe-kevar ba ha-chacham* — the case where Rabbi Yehuda permits the single-direction *eiruv* is when the *rebbe* has already arrived within the *techum* by *erev Shabbos*, even though the person placing the *eiruv* does not yet know this. In that case, the condition is already settled as a matter of fact — it is simply unknown to the person making the *eiruv*. The *eiruv* takes effect based on a present reality, not a future event. There is no *breira* here because nothing is being determined by what happens later; the situation is already determined, the person is merely ignorant of it.

The case that does not work — the two-direction *eiruv* — is one where the *rebbe* has not yet arrived in either zone. On *Shabbos* he will enter one of the two zones, and that event will then retroactively determine which of the two *eiruvim* was operative on *erev Shabbos*. That is a genuine *breira* case: a future occurrence gives retroactive identity to something that was genuinely indeterminate at the time. Rabbi Yehuda rejects this.

The distinction, then, is not really about one *eiruv* versus two. The operative distinction is: was the condition already met at the time the *eiruv* was placed, even if unknown? Then it works. Or does the condition come to be met only on *Shabbos*, retroactively defining what was placed on *erev Shabbos*? Then it fails, and this is *breira*.

From this passage, the *Gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yehuda definitively does not hold of *breira*.

### Mental State & Breira

This conclusion returns us to the original problem: since Rabbi Yehuda does not hold of *breira*, he cannot solve the problem of the mixed *chatas* money by removing four *zuz* and declaring those to be the invalid funds. Yet it is worth pausing to notice — as Tosafos grapples with at length — that the removal of four *zuz* is not really *breira* in the classical sense either. *Breira* applies where something was genuinely undefined at the outset and later becomes defined. But the four *zuz* given by the deceased contributor were perfectly well-defined from the moment of contribution; they were specific coins placed in a specific box. Designating a random handful of coins after the fact and declaring them to be "the ones" is something different — not retroactive definition of the previously undefined, but an arbitrary assumption. Even proponents of *breira* could not logically validate this maneuver.

The reason the *Gemara* invokes *breira* here is probably that this is the closest category available, and Tosafos's extended discussions on this *daf* work precisely to sort out which cases of *breira* are truly parallel and which are only superficially related.

A deeper and related question concerns how *breira* relates to *tenai* — conditional stipulation. On the surface they look similar: in both cases, some legal reality is determined by what happens at a later point. But there is an important distinction. A *tenai* is a condition attached to a legal *act*: I sell you this object, or I give you this *get*, on the condition that such-and-such occurs. If the condition is met, the act takes effect; the *tenai* framework governs this throughout *Maseches Gittin* and *Maseches Kiddushin*. *Breira*, by contrast, is not about whether an act takes effect — it is about the *identity* of the object or the *state of mind* of the person at the time.

This distinction becomes clearest in the context of the *eiruv*. The logic of *eiruv* is not that one performs a legal act that takes effect under a condition. Rather, *eiruv* expresses where one is choosing to begin *Shabbos* — it is a declaration of intent, a statement of one's current mental orientation. And one's mental state cannot be genuinely conditional in the *tenai* sense. Either one was intending to start *Shabbos* in the east or one was intending to start it in the west. To say "my intention depended on where the *rebbe* ends up" is to say that one's present state of mind was undefined and will be defined by a future event — and that is precisely *breira*, not *tenai*.

A parallel case that Tosafos cites involves *kiddushei biah* — betrothal effected through marital relations. If a man says to a woman: "this act will constitute *kiddushin* on the condition that your father approves," the *Gemara* frames this as a *breira* question rather than a simple *tenai*. One way to understand this is that *kiddushei biah* does not function like an act of sale that takes effect under a condition. Rather, the halachic significance of the act depends on how the parties conceive of it at the time — whether they are engaging in marital relations in a marital frame or in some other frame. That subjective orientation, like the subjective orientation of the *eiruv*, cannot be held in suspension pending a future event. It either was or was not a marital act at the time it occurred. *Breira* asks whether a future event can retroactively give definition to a state of mind or identity that was genuinely indeterminate — and Rabbi Yehuda answers: no.

From all of this, the *Gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yehuda's rejection of *breira* is the reason he cannot solve the problem of the mixed *chatas* money by any trick of retroactive designation. And his reliance on *kseiva* — labeling — which has now been established independently, leads us back to the original question: why, then, does he require only one pedestal on *Yom Kippur*?

## One Pedestal Reason

### Writing Question

Now that the *Gemara* has established that Rabbi Yehuda does hold of *kseiva* — that labeling normally suffices to distinguish one vessel from another — the original difficulty reasserts itself with full force. Rabbi Yochanan poses the question directly: *de-hashta de-amina de-Rabbi Yehuda kseiva is lei* — now that we have established that Rabbi Yehuda accepts the validity of labeling, *Yom Kippurim nami nahavi terei* — why not have two pedestals on *Yom Kippur* as well, *ve-nichtov aleihu* — and simply label them?

### High Priest Weakness

The *Gemara* answers: *mishum chulsha de-Kohen Gadol lav adaiteih* — because of the weakness and distraction of the *Kohen Gadol*, he will not be paying attention. The *chulsha* here is not mere physical exhaustion; it is the combined effect of being rushed, pressured, overwhelmed, and distracted by the enormity of the service. Under normal circumstances, labeling works and people pay attention to it. But on *Yom Kippur*, when the *Kohen Gadol* is running through one of the most complex and high-stakes ritual services imaginable while bearing the weight of the nation's sins, labeling cannot be relied upon. He will simply not stop to read and process what is written.

### Blood Distinction

One might ask why labeling is the only option — surely there are other ways to distinguish the two vessels. The *Gemara* considers this. First, one might simply look at the volume: *hana nafish bazya* — the blood of the ox is substantially greater in quantity than the blood of the goat. Just look at which bowl has more blood. The *Gemara* entertains the objection that perhaps not all the blood of the ox is caught, so volume comparison would be unreliable. But Rabbi Yehuda himself rules that the *shochet* must catch all the blood of the *par*, as the verse states: *ve-et kol dam ha-par yishpoch el yesod ha-mizbe'ach* — "all the blood of the ox shall be poured at the base of the altar." So volume should in principle be sufficient to distinguish the two.

Second, there is even a color difference: *cha-chivra ve-asumka* — the blood of the goat is whiter and the blood of the ox is redder. One could simply look at the color and tell them apart without any label at all.

Yet the *Gemara* concludes: *ela mishum chulsha de-Kohen Gadol lav adaiteih* — nevertheless, because of the *Kohen Gadol*'s weakness and distraction, he is not going to stop mid-service to discern whether one bowl is slightly larger or slightly redder than the other. He won't be in a state of mind to make those discriminations. This is why having two pedestals won't work — not because the labels or visual differences are inherently inadequate, but because this particular officiant, under these particular circumstances, cannot be trusted to notice them. One pedestal eliminates the ambiguity entirely: when there is only one available space, the act of placing one vessel down and picking up the other enforces the correct sequence automatically.

### Liturgy Application

This debate has a direct practical application in the *nusach* of the *tefilla* on *Yom Kippur*, specifically in the recitation of the *avodah* service. This is in fact among the first places in *Maseches Yoma* where we see a window into how the halachic debates of the *Gemara* shaped the actual liturgy recited on *Yom Kippur*.

### Rava's Correction

A certain *shaliach tzibbur* led the prayers before Rava on *Yom Kippur* and, when reciting this portion of the *avodah*, described the *Kohen Gadol* coming out and placing the blood on *kan sheni* — the second pedestal — in the *Heichal*, then taking the blood of the *par* and setting down the blood of the *sa'ir*. Rava immediately told him: *chada ke-rabanan ve-chada ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — you have mixed two incompatible positions. By saying there was a second pedestal, the *shaliach tzibbur* aligned himself with the *Rabanan*, who hold that two pedestals were used. But then by saying the *Kohen Gadol* first took the blood of the *par* and set down the blood of the *sa'ir*, he described the procedure appropriate to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s single-pedestal view.

The logic is as follows: if there are two pedestals, the *Kohen Gadol* can put the blood of the *goat* down on the second pedestal before doing anything else, and then walk over to collect the blood of the *ox* from the first pedestal. The sequence would be: put down the *sa'ir*, take the *par*. But if there is only one pedestal, the *Kohen Gadol* must first take the blood of the *par* that has been waiting there in order to free up the single pedestal for the blood of the *sa'ir*, and only then sets down the *sa'ir*. The sequence would be: take the *par*, set down the *sa'ir*.

The *shaliach tzibbur* had described two pedestals (consistent with the *Rabanan*) but the taking-before-setting-down sequence (consistent with Rabbi Yehuda). Rava corrected him: *eima hinnniach dam ha-sa'ir ve-natol dam ha-par* — rather say, he set down the blood of the *sa'ir* first, and then took the blood of the *par*. This is the sequence that follows from the *Rabanan*'s ruling of two pedestals. The ruling of the *Gemara* follows the *Rabanan*, and the *nusach* of the *avodah* in the liturgy must reflect this consistently.

## Parochet Sprinkling

### Verse Interpretation

Having completed the discussion of the pedestals, the *Gemara* moves to the next stage of the *Yom Kippur* service. After switching the blood vessels — having completed the one-and-seven sprinklings inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* for both the *par* and the *sa'ir* — the *Kohen Gadol* comes out and performs sprinklings on the *parochet*, the great curtain, while standing in the *Heichal*, outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. The *Mishnah* states: *ve-hizah mimenu al ha-parochet keneged aron mibachutz* — and he sprinkled from it upon the *parochet*, facing the *aron*, from outside.

The source for these sprinklings is derived from the verse: *ve-chen yaaseh le-ohel moed ha-shochen itam be-toch tumatam* — "and so shall he do to the Tent of Meeting that dwells among them in their impurity." The entire *Beis Hamikdash* — the *Heichal* and the *Kodesh Kodashim* together — is called the *ohel moed* in the Torah's terminology. The phrase *ve-chen yaaseh* — "and so shall he do" — is understood by *Chazal* to mandate sprinklings in the outer chamber as well, performed while standing in the *Heichal* facing the *parochet*.

### Mirrored Sprinklings

A *Baraita* elaborates: *ma talmud lomar*? What does the verse teach? *Keshem shemazzeh lifnai v'lifnim, kach mazzeh ba-heichal* — just as he sprinkles inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, so does he sprinkle in the *Heichal*. And the *ve-chen* — the "likewise" — equates the two sets of sprinklings precisely: just as inside it is one upward and seven downward from the blood of the *par*, so outside it is one and seven from the blood of the *par*; and just as inside it is one and seven from the blood of the *sa'ir*, so outside it is one and seven from the blood of the *sa'ir*. The Torah does not explicitly mandate separate ox-blood and goat-blood sprinklings on the *parochet*, but the principle of *ve-chen* — "and likewise" — extends the full protocol of the inner chamber to the outer.

### Divine Presence

The verse concludes: *ha-shochen itam be-toch tumatam* — who dwells among them in their impurity. The *Baraita* draws a theological conclusion: *afilu be-shaat she-hem tme'im shechina imahem* — even at the moment when Israel are *tamei*, the *Shechina* continues to dwell among them. This is understood as a fundamental statement about the nature of God's relationship with the Jewish people. Despite the accumulated *tumah* created by sin, God does not depart; the *Shechina* remains present.

### Sanctuary Cleansing

The theological significance of this service deserves emphasis. The instinct on *Yom Kippur* is to focus entirely on personal *teshuva* and individual atonement, and this is of course central to the day. But the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* as described in the Torah operates at a different level. When the verse says *ve-chiper al ha-kodesh mi-tumot b'nei Yisrael u-mi-pisheihem le-chol chatatam* — it is not speaking about atoning for individuals. It is speaking about cleansing the sanctuary itself from the *tumah* generated by the sins of Israel.

The dynamic described here is this: God committed Himself to dwell among His people — *ve-shachanti be-tocham*. But the people sin, and sin generates *tumah*, and that *tumah* accumulates in the sanctuary. God sustains the *Shechina* among them even in their impurity — *ha-shochen itam be-toch tumatam* — but the accumulation cannot go on indefinitely without attention. Once a year the *Kohen Gadol* performs this elaborate service to cleanse the *mikdash* of the *tumah* that has been deposited there by the nation's sins throughout the year, making it possible for God to continue to dwell among the people.

## Heretic Challenge

### Rejection Claim

A certain heretic (*mina*) said to Rabbi Chanina: *hashta bari tme'im atun* — you are certainly impure now. The claim, grounded in the verse *tumata be-shuleha* — "her impurity is on the hems of her garments" — is that the Jewish people have been rejected by God on account of their sins. This is a theological provocation: the argument that Israel's *tumah* signals divine abandonment, that God has departed and the covenant has been severed. Such claims were characteristic of certain heretical and Jewish-Christian circles, who read Jewish exile and suffering as divine rejection.

### Chanina Response

Rabbi Chanina's reply is incisive: *ta chazi ma kativ behu* — come and see what it says about them: *ha-shochen itam be-toch tumatam*. God dwells with us in our impurity. Whatever our *tumah*, however grave our sins, the *Shechina* does not abandon Israel. *Afilu be-zman she-hem tme'im shechina shruya beineihem* — even in the time when they are *tamei*, the divine presence rests among them.

It is worth noting the boldness of applying this verse beyond its original context. In its *pshat*, the verse speaks specifically of the *Shechina* dwelling in the *mikdash* — a presence sustained through the sanctuary and its service, and renewed each *Yom Kippur* through the *avodah* of cleansing. Without a *mikdash* and without the ritual, one might argue the verse no longer applies. Yet *Chazal* read it expansively: the assurance of *ha-shochen itam be-toch tumatam* is not contingent on the *mikdash*'s existence. God dwells with Israel in all times and in all conditions of *tumah*. The theological answer to the heretic's charge of divine rejection is the verse itself: God never leaves.
