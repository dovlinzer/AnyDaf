# Tamid 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chapter Four Opening

### Perek Dalet Overview

With this daf we begin the fourth *perek* of Masechet Tamid, which addresses the morning service in the Beit HaMikdash leading up to and through the *korban tamid*. The *masechet* as a whole is named for the *tamid*, and this chapter brings us at last to the actual *shechita* of the morning sacrifice — the culmination of all the preparatory activity described in the preceding chapters.

### Gate Opening Before Shec…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא הָיוּ כּוֹפְתִין אֶת הַטָּלֶה, אֶלָּא מַעֲקִידִין אוֹתוֹ. מִי שֶׁזָּכוּ בָּאֵבָרִים, אוֹחֲזִין בּוֹ. וְכָךְ הָיָה עֲקֵידָתוֹ: רֹאשׁוֹ לַדָּרוֹם, וּפָנָיו לַמַּעֲרָב. וְהַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹמֵד בַּמִּזְרָח, וּפָנָיו לַמַּעֲרָב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In preparing the lamb of the daily offering for sacrifice, the priests **would not tie the lamb** by fastening all four of its legs together; **rather, they would bind it** by fastening each hind leg to the corresponding foreleg. The priests **who won** the right **to** take **the limbs** up to the ramp would **hold** the lamb in place while it was being slaughtered. **And this was** the manner of **its binding:** The animal would be stood in the northern part of the courtyard while **its head** would be directed **to the south,** toward the altar, **and its face** would be turned **to the west,** toward the Sanctuary. **And the slaughterer** would **stand to** the **east** of the animal, **and his face** would be **to the west.**

Before reaching the *shechita* itself, the *mishnah* reminds us of the sequencing already established: the gate of the *heichal* had to be opened before the *tamid* could be *shechted*. The two *avodot* performed inside the *heichal* — the cleaning of the ashes of the *menorah* and the cleaning of the ashes of the golden *mizbe'ach* used for the *ketoret* — preceded the *shechita* as well, even though the *shechita* is the central act. Now, at last, we arrive at it.

## Binding & Positioning

### Ma'akdin vs. Koftin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל שַׁחַר הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּה. שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** Twenty-four rings were affixed to the courtyard floor north of the altar, designated for placement of the animal's neck during its slaughter. The daily offering **of the morning was slaughtered at the northwest corner** of the altar, **at** the **second ring.** The daily offering **of the afternoon was slaughtered at the northeast corner** of the altar, **at** the **second ring.**

The *mishnah* opens: *lo hayu koftin et ha-taleh ella ma'akdin oto* — they would not *kofet* the lamb, but rather they would be *ma'akdin* it. What is the difference? *Kofet* implies binding both hind legs together and both front legs together — or all four together — which is precisely how one would conveniently carry a sheep on one's shoulders. That is what they did *not* do. *Ma'akdin*, by contrast, means binding a front leg together with a hind leg on each side: right-front with right-hind, left-front with left-hind. This is quite different from the standard way of restraining an animal for transport or slaughter.

The *Rambam* has an alternative reading: that they did not bind the animal with a rope at all, but instead placed its legs through the metal *taba'at* — the ring used to hold the animal's neck — so that the ring itself served to restrain the limbs without any binding. However the precise mechanics of *ma'akdin* are understood, the binding method was specifically distinguished from the ordinary way.

### Akedat Yitzchak Link

The connection to *Aqeidat Yitzchak* is immediate: the *gemara* picks up on the word *aqad* — the same root as *aqeidah* — and notes explicitly that this manner of restraining the animal was like *aqeidat Yitzchak ben Avraham*. This linguistic and conceptual link is significant. The *aqeidah* serves in several places as a model for *qorbanot* — in Zevahim and Menahot we learned, for instance, that the *aqeidah* teaches the requirement of a *ma'akhelet* (a knife) and the necessity of a *kli sharet* for the *shechita*. The *aqeidah* thus functions as the primordial *qorban*, the originating template.

This raises a genuine question about how far the model extends. One might argue that the very point of the *aqeidah* was that God never intended it to be completed — that He does not desire human sacrifice — and therefore using it as a normative model is itself surprising. Yet the liturgy in several places treats the *aqeidah* as if it were in fact completed and as if it should be regarded as an actual *qorban*, with its merit standing for Israel. The degree to which *aqeidat Yitzchak* serves as a template for *qorbanot* is a rich question that reaches the heart of what the sacrificial system means and why it was instituted.

It is also worth noting that the binding of Isaac as envisioned in most contemporaneous imagery does not look at all like the *ma'akdin* described here — binding a front leg together with a hind leg in the manner one does with an animal — which would be a very strange thing to do to a person.

### Animal Orientation

The *mishnah* specifies: *rosho la-darom* — its head is to the south — *u-fanav la-ma'arav* — and its face is to the west. The animal is oriented lengthwise along the north-south axis, positioned to the north of the *mizbe'ach* since *kodshei kodashim* must be *shechted* in the north. Its rear faces north — away from the *mizbe'ach* — so that it does not, as it were, turn its back on the sacred precinct (and so that any dung dropped does not fall toward the *mizbe'ach*). The head is thus to the south, and the animal's face is turned to face the *heichal* in the west — toward the *kodesh* and *kodesh kodashim*. Dignity and orientation toward the sacred are maintained throughout.

### Shochet Position

The *shochet* stands to the east of the animal, *panav la-ma'arav* — also facing west. This ensures that neither the *shochet* nor the animal presents its back toward the *heichal*. Both face the *heichal*. The *shochet* stands behind the animal from the animal's perspective, to its east, facing the same direction as the animal — toward the west, toward the sacred center of the Temple.

## Shechita Location & Blood

### Morning Tamid Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט הַשּׁוֹחֵט, וְקִבֵּל הַמְקַבֵּל, בָּא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, וְנוֹתֵן מִזְרָחָה צָפוֹנָה. מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, וְנוֹתֵן מַעֲרָבָה דָּרוֹמָה. שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ אֶל יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית.
>
> **Translation:** After **the slaughterer has slaughtered** the lamb **and the receiver has received** its blood in a vessel to sprinkle on the altar, the priest **comes to** the **northeast corner** of the altar **and places** the first sprinkling in such a manner that the blood will reach the **eastern** and **northern** sides of the altar. Next, the priest comes to the **southwest** corner of the altar **and places** a second sprinkling in a manner such that the blood will reach the **western** and **southern** sides of the altar. With regard to **the remainder of the blood,** the priest **would pour** it **at** the **southern base** of the altar, at its southwest corner.

*Shacharit hayah nishchat al keren tzevonit ma'aravit al taba'at shniyah* — the morning *tamid* was *shechted* at the northwestern corner, at the second ring.

### Afternoon Tamid Location

The afternoon *tamid* — *shebe'ein ha'arbayim* — was *shechted* at the northeastern corner, also at the second ring.

The twenty-four rings in the *azarah* corresponded to the twenty-four *mishmarot* (priestly watches), each *mishmar* with its own designated ring; the ring of *Bilgah* was famously sealed. For the *tamid*, however, the same ring was always used: the morning one in the northwest, the afternoon one in the northeast.

### Ring Placement Rationale

Three considerations govern this placement. First, the north: the requirement that *kodshei kodashim* be *shechted* in the north is paramount, and the placement as far north as possible emphasizes and maximizes this requirement.

Second, the east-west distinction between morning and afternoon: the *mishnah* in other sources teaches that the *tamid* should be *opposite* the sun. In the morning the sun rises in the east, so the morning *tamid* is placed in the west — as far from the rising sun as possible, specifically to preclude any appearance of sun-worship. In the afternoon the sun sets in the west, so the afternoon *tamid* is in the east. One participant raised a natural objection: perhaps the point is not "opposite the sun" in the sense of facing away from it, but rather that one does it in a direction that specifically *contrasts* with the sun's position — i.e., as a deliberate counter-gesture to avoid any possible association with solar worship. The *gemara* will cite a *pasuk* to this effect.

Third, the second ring rather than the first (northernmost): the shadow cast by the north wall would fall over the first row, meaning that an animal placed there would not actually be in direct sunlight. Moving one ring inward puts the animal in the sun — which is necessary in order for the placement to be meaningfully "opposite" the sun at all.

To summarize: the placement is as far north as possible to emphasize *tzafon*, opposite the sun's position (east-west) to express non-worship of the sun, and specifically the *second* ring rather than the first to avoid the shadow of the north wall.

### Blood Sprinkling Diagona…

After the *shechita*, the one who caught the blood — a separate *kohen* from the *shochet* — proceeded to sprinkle it on diagonal corners of the *mizbe'ach*. The *mishnah* states: *ba lo le-keren mizrachit tzevonit ve-notein ve-notein mizracha tzevona; maaravit deromit ve-notein ve-notein maarava deroma*. He came first to the northeastern corner and threw the blood there; then he came to the southwestern corner and threw it there.

Why diagonal corners specifically? The blood must go *saviv* — all around. By throwing at diagonally opposite corners, each throw covers two walls — the blood spatters in two directions from each corner — and the two throws together reach all four walls. The reason these two specific diagonals are chosen and not others relates to the *yesod*: the foundation ledge of the *mizbe'ach* did not run all the way around. The *yesod* ran only along two sides — the south and the east — meaning that the northwestern and southwestern corners were available for diagonal throws only at southwest and northeast, since those are the only two opposite corners that each have a *yesod*. The northwest corner had no *yesod*, which is why the throw could not be made there.

### Blood Spilling Foundation

The remainder of the blood — *shearei hadam* — was poured out at the southern *yesod*: *haya shofech al yesod dromi*. Since the *yesod* itself ran only partway, the actual area available for this pouring was quite limited — essentially just the small portion of the southern foundation. The *kohen* would finish the blood service at the southwestern corner and then pour out the remaining blood there at the south.

## Flaying & Initial Cuts

### Flaying Through Knee

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הָיָה שׁוֹבֵר בּוֹ אֶת הָרֶגֶל, אֶלָּא נוֹקְבוֹ מִתּוֹךְ עַרְקוּבּוֹ, וְתוֹלֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** When the priest flayed the hide of the daily offering after its slaughter, **he would not break the** animal's **leg** in the typical manner of flaying an animal; **rather, he punctures** the leg **from within** each **knee** of the hind leg **and suspends** the animal **by** placing these holes on two hooks, in order to flay the animal's hide.

The *mishnah* opens this section: *lo haya shover bo et ha-regel* — they would not break the leg of the animal. In ordinary butchery it was apparently common practice to break a leg as a convenience in hanging or suspending the carcass. Here, instead, they would make a hole — *nokvo* — at the *arkuvo*, the knee joint of the hind leg, and suspend the animal through that hole. The animal thus hangs by its hind legs, and the flaying proceeds from top — the hind — downward toward the head.

### Skin Removal Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מַפְשִׁיט וְיוֹרֵד עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לֶחָזֶה. הִגִּיעַ לֶחָזֶה, חָתַךְ אֶת הָרֹאשׁ, וּנְתָנוֹ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בּוֹ. חָתַךְ אֶת הַכְּרָעַיִם, וּנְתָנָן לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהֶן. מֵרַק אֶת הַהֶפְשֵׁט. קָרַע אֶת הַלֵּב וְהוֹצִיא אֶת דָּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest began **flaying** from the top of the inverted animal, **descending until** he would **reach** the hide **of the breast.** Once **he reached the breast, he severed the** lamb's **head and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp. Next **he severed the** four **legs** below the knee **and gave them to** the priest **who won** the right to take **them** up to the ramp. **He completed the flaying** of the remaining hide from the breast down, and then the priest **cut the heart and drained its blood.**

*Haya mafshit veyored* — one flays it going downward — *ad she-magia la-chaze* — until reaching the chest area. At that point most of the skin has been removed from the hind region down through the upper body, leaving the head area — and the very extremities of the lower legs — still unskinned. Flaying the extremities near the bone is far more difficult, analogous to trying to remove skin from a chicken wing versus a breast, and so those areas were left. Once the flaying reached the chest, the head was removed.

### Head Removal

*Chatach et ha-rosh ve-natno le-mi she-zacha bah* — the head was cut off and given to the *kohen* who had won it in the lottery. It is given with the skin still on, since the head area was not flayed. As we will see, the head goes together with the right rear leg and is assigned to the first *kohen* in the procession.

### Lower Legs Cut

*Chatach et ha-kra'ayim ve-natno le-mi she-zacha bahen* — the lower extremities, the *kra'ayim*, were cut off and given to the one who merited them. These are not the primary legs (*yadayim* and *raglayim*) but rather the feet — the joints nearest the hooves. Sheep and cattle have an additional joint in the lower leg (analogous to the distinction between knee and ankle in humans, though positioned somewhat differently), and it is there that the cut was made. Notably, the *mishnah* does not at this point specify who received the feet; we will find out later that they went together with the inner organs.

### Heart Blood Removal

*Vekara et ha-lev ve-hotzi et damo* — and he tore open the heart and extracted its blood. This line appears somewhat out of place at this stage of the *mishnah*'s account. The animal has been flayed and the head and feet have been cut off, but the chest cavity has not yet been opened — so one wonders how the heart is being accessed. Various explanations are given: perhaps a specific incision was made to reach the heart, or perhaps the line is better understood as a prospective note indicating what one does when one eventually reaches the heart. The plain sense seems to be that it is describing what one does at that stage of the process when the heart is eventually exposed, even if the placement of the line here is somewhat surprising.

## Limb & Organ Removal

### Front Hands Cut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָתַךְ אֶת הַיָּדַיִם, וּנְתָנָן לְמִי שֶׁזָּכוּ בָּהֶן. עָלָה לָרֶגֶל הַיְמָנִית, חֲתָכָהּ, וּנְתָנָהּ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהּ, וּשְׁתֵּי בֵּיצִים עִמָּהּ. קְרָעוֹ, וְנִמְצָא כּוּלּוֹ גָּלוּי לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Next the priest **severed** the remaining upper parts of **the forelegs and gave them to** the priest **who won** the right to take **them** up to the ramp. Afterward **he moved up to** the remaining upper part of **the right** hind **leg, severed it, and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp; **and** the animal's **two testicles** were cut along **with** the right leg, leaving the animal suspended by its left hind leg. Then the priest **tore** open the animal's midsection, **resulting** in the innards of **the entire** animal being **exposed before him.**

*Chatach et ha-yadayim ve-natnam le-mi she-zacha bahen* — they cut off the front hands (*yadayim*) and gave them to the one who had won them. The *yadayim* — the front forelegs — are distinct from the *kra'ayim* (the feet removed earlier) and from the *raglayim* (the rear legs). Different *kohanim* received the front hands and the rear legs respectively, since these go to separate people in the lottery. The second *kohen* in the procession receives the *shtei yadayim*.

One might pause to note the order of processing: the animal is suspended by its hind legs; the head, feet, and hands have now been removed; the animal is partially flayed; and the orderly dismemberment is beginning to proceed from head toward the rear.

### Right Leg & Opening

*Ala le-regel ha-yemanit va-chatachah ve-netanah le-mi she-zacha bah* — he went to the right rear leg, cut it off, and gave it to the one who won it. *U-beitzim imah* — the two testicles came together with the right rear leg. The right leg together with the head goes to the *first kohen* (the first in the procession, *alef*). *Kera'o ve-nimtza kulo galui lefanav* — then the animal was cut open lengthwise, and the entire interior was exposed.

*Natal et ha-peder ve-natno al beit shechitat ha-rosh milmala* — he took the fat (*peder*) and placed it over the place where the head had been severed. This served an aesthetic purpose: the severing point was bloody and exposed, and covering it with fat — which was considered the most prized portion of the *qorban* — was regarded as dignified and pleasing. The head would subsequently be carried to the ramp with this fat arranged upon it.

### Fat & Organs Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶת הַפֶּדֶר, וּנְתָנוֹ עַל בֵּית שְׁחִיטַת הָרֹאשׁ מִלְּמַעְלָה. נָטַל אֶת הַקְּרָבַיִם, וּנְתָנָן לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהֶן, לַהֲדִיחָן. וְהַכָּרֵס מְדִיחִין אוֹתָהּ בְּבֵית הַמְּדִיחִין. וּמְדִיחִין אוֹתָהּ כׇּל צׇרְכָּהּ, וְהַקְּרָבַיִם מְדִיחִין אוֹתָן שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים בְּמִעוּטָן, עַל שׁוּלְחָנוֹת שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ שֶׁבֵּין הָעַמּוּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **He took the fats and placed** them **on the place of slaughter** on **the** animal's **head above** it, to conceal the place where it was severed while the priest would take the head to the altar. Then the priest **took the innards and gave them to** the priest **who won** the right to take **them** up to the ramp, in order **to rinse them** first. **And** with regard to **the stomach,** in which there is a significant amount of waste, the priests would **rinse it in the rinsing site** located in the south of the courtyard, east of the Gate of the Water, **and they rinsed it as much it required. And** with regard to **the innards,** the priests would **rinse them three times at a minimum, on** the **marble tables that** were positioned **between the pillars** in the slaughterhouse.

*Natal et ha-kirvayim ve-netanam le-mi she-zacha bahen le-hadichan* — he took the inner organs and gave them to the *kohen* who had won the right to rinse them. In the lottery distribution, the *kirvayim* went to the sixth *kohen* (*vav*). Those organs were now carried to the rinsing tables.

### Rinsing Organs

*Ve-ha-keres madichin oto be-veit ha-madichin* — the stomach (*keres*) was rinsed in the *beit ha-madichin*, a separate rinsing chamber. *Ve-ha-kirvayim madichin otam shalosh pe'amim* — the intestines were rinsed three times minimally — *al shulhanot ha-shayish she-bein ha-amudim* — on the marble tables between the pillars.

The distinction here is significant. The intestines were rinsed right there in the *beit ha-mitbehayim*, on the marble tables adjacent to the pillars used for flaying. The stomach, however, because it contained a particularly large amount of fecal matter and waste, was taken to a separate room — the *beit ha-madichin* — located at the other end of the *azarah*, away from the main slaughtering area. The *meforshim* confirm this separation. The intestines were processed close at hand for efficiency; the stomach, because of the quantity of waste involved, warranted removal to a dedicated rinsing chamber.

### Organ Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶת הַסַּכִּין, וְהִפְרִישׁ אֶת הָרֵיאָה מִן הַכָּבֵד, וְאֶצְבַּע הַכָּבֵד מִן הַכָּבֵד, וְלֹא הָיָה מְזִיזָהּ מִמְּקוֹמָהּ. נוֹקַב אֶת הֶחָזֶה, וּנְתָנוֹ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The priest then **took the knife and separated the lung from the liver, and** the **finger-**like protrusion from the lower edge **of the liver,** also known as the lobe of the liver, **from the liver. And** he **would not move** any one of the organs **from its place.** He would leave the lung attached to the neck, the lobe attached to the haunch, and the liver attached to the right flank. The priest would **puncture** around **the breast,** separating it from the flanks and the ribs, **and** he **gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp.

*Natal et ha-sakin ve-hiprish et ha-re'ah min ha-kaved ve-etzba ha-kaved min ha-kaved* — using a knife, one separated — not fully excised, but separated at their points of attachment — the lung from the liver, and the lobe of the liver from the liver. *Ve-lo haya mezizah mi-mekomah* — he did not actually remove the lobe of the liver from where it was. The reason is that the lung, the liver, and the lobe of the liver were each destined to accompany a *different* section of the animal in the procession. By separating them at their junctions without removing them, one prepared them to go their separate ways while leaving them in place until the appropriate moment.

## Chest, Flanks, Throat, T…

### Chest Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלָה לַדּוֹפֶן הַיְּמָנִית, הָיָה חוֹתֵךְ וְיוֹרֵד עַד הַשִּׁדְרָה, וְלֹא הָיָה נוֹגֵעַ בַּשִּׁדְרָה, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לְבֵין שְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת דַּקּוֹת. חֲתָכָהּ, וּנְתָנָהּ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהּ, וְהַכָּבֵד תְּלוּיָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** He then **moved up to the right flank** and **would cut** it and separate it from the animal's body. **And** he would continue to cut, **descending until** he would reach **the spinal column, and** the priest **would not touch the spinal column,** leaving the spine intact and attached to the left flank. He would continue cutting **until he reached** the space **between the two narrow ribs** near the neck, leaving them in place. The priest **cut** the right flank, separating it from the body of the animal, **and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp. **And the liver** was **suspended from it.**

*Nokev et ha-chaze ve-netanah le-mi she-zacha bah* — he made an opening in the chest area and gave it to the one who had merited it. The chest was assigned to the fourth *kohen* (*dalet*) in the lottery. It is described as *noikev* — making a hole or an incision — which is a somewhat striking phrase, but it refers to cutting out the chest section and conveying it to the appropriate person.

At this stage it is worth noting the interplay between the dismemberment order and the procession order. The animal has now yielded: the head (to *kohen alef*), the two front hands (to *kohen bet*), the inner organs for rinsing (to *kohen vav*), and the chest (to *kohen dalet*). The right rear leg went to *kohen alef* together with the head. The dismemberment does not proceed in a single strict sequence matching the procession order; rather, pieces are given out as they become ready, and each recipient waits with their portion until the entire animal has been processed.

A question arises about the *kirvayim* and the location of rinsing. The *mishnah* mentions the *beit ha-mitbehayim* and the tables *bein ha-amudim*, and one might wonder whether these refer to the same place. They do not. The *beit ha-madichin* for the stomach and the marble tables between the pillars for the intestines are two distinct locations. The marble tables were situated adjacent to the pillars where the flaying took place — the pillars served as suspension points, and the tables immediately beside them served as rinsing surfaces. The *beit ha-madichin* for the stomach was a separate, dedicated chamber at the far end of the *azarah*.

### Right Flank Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לַגֵּרָה, הִנִּיחַ בָּהּ שְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת מִכָּאן, וּשְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת מִכָּאן. חֲתָכָהּ, וּנְתָנָהּ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהּ, הַקָּנֶה וְהַלֵּב וְהָרֵיאָה תְּלוּיִם בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest then **came to the cud. He left** attached **to it,** in their entirety, the **two** narrow **ribs from here,** the right side, **and** the **two** narrow **ribs from there,** the left side. **He cut** the cud **and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp; and **the windpipe, the heart, and the lung** were **suspended from it.**

He then **moved up to the right flank** and **would cut** it and separate it from the animal's body. **And** he would continue to cut, **descending until** he would reach **the spinal column, and** the priest **would not touch the spinal column,** leaving the spine intact and attached to the left flank. He would continue cutting **until he reached** the space **between the two narrow ribs** near the neck, leaving them in place. The priest **cut** the right flank, separating it from the body of the animal, **and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp. **And the liver** was **suspended from it.**

The right flank thus went to the fifth *kohen* (*heh*), who would eventually receive both flanks. The *shidra* — the spinal cord — was left attached to neither flank yet, as it would go with the left flank.

### Throat Area Removal

*Ba lo la-gerah* — he came to the throat area. *Hini'ach bah shtei tzla'ot mi-kan u-shtei tzla'ot mi-kan* — he left in place the two little ribs on each side, which are at the top of the neck near the spinal cord. The ribs at the very bottom of the animal had gone with the rear section, and the ribs here at the top of the neck were left similarly undisturbed. *Ve-chatachah ve-netanah le-mi she-zacha bah* — he cut out the throat area and gave it to the one who had won it. *Ha-kaneh ve-ha-lev ve-ha-rei'ah teluyim bah* — the trachea, the heart, and the lungs were all attached to this section and went with it.

The throat area — *gerah* — went together with the chest, assigned to *kohen dalet* (*he-chazeh ve-ha-gerah*). So *kohen dalet* first received the chest section, and now received the throat with the heart and lungs hanging from it. The heart blood, as noted earlier, was extracted from the heart when the heart was reached — it was opened and its blood removed, though that step appeared somewhat out of sequence in the *mishnah*'s presentation.

### Left Flank Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לַדּוֹפֶן הַשְּׂמָאלִית, הִנִּיחַ בָּהּ שְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת דַּקּוֹת מִלְּמַעְלָן, וּשְׁתֵּי צְלָעוֹת דַּקּוֹת מִלְּמַטָּן, וְכָךְ הָיָה מַנִּיחַ בַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ. נִמְצָא מַנִּיחַ שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם מִלְּמַעְלָן, שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם מִלְּמַטָּן. חֲתָכָהּ וְנָתְנָה לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהּ, וְהַשִּׁדְרָה עִמָּהּ, וְהַטְּחוֹל תָּלוּי בָּהּ, וְהִיא הָיְתָה גְּדוֹלָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁל יָמִין קוֹרִין גְּדוֹלָה – שֶׁהַכָּבֵד תְּלוּיָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He came to** cut **the left flank** of the body and **left** attached **to it two narrow ribs above,** near the haunch, as the animal was suspended upside down, **and two narrow ribs below,** near the cud. **And he** also **did that with its counterpart,** the right flank, **resulting** in **two** narrow ribs **in each** flank **above and two** narrow ribs **in each** flank **below.** **He cut** the left flank **and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp, **and the spinal column** was **with it, and the spleen** was **suspended from it. And** the left flank **was greater,** i.e., the larger of the two, because it included the spine, **but** they **referred** to **the right** flank as the **greater** one, **as** in addition to the flank itself, **the liver was suspended from it.**

*Ba lo la-dofen ha-smalit* — he came to the left flank. *Hini'ach bah shtei tzla'ot dakot milmala u-shtei tzla'ot dakot milmata* — he left in place the two thin ribs at the top and two at the bottom, just as he had done on the right side. *Ve-khakh hayah maniach be-chaverta* — the same had been done on the other side (the right flank). *Nimtza maniach shtayim shtayim milmala shtayim shtayim milmata* — the result is that two and two were left on top and two and two on the bottom.

*Ve-chatachah ve-netanah le-mi she-zacha bah ve-ha-shidra imah* — he cut out the left flank and gave it to its recipient, with the spinal cord attached. *Ve-ha-techol talui bah* — the spleen was attached to it. *Ve-hi hayetah gedolah* — this section was in fact the larger one, since it included the spinal cord.

*Ela she-ha-yamin korin gedolah* — yet they call the right flank "the big one." *She-ha-kaved teluyah bah* — because the right flank has the liver attached to it, and the liver is a large and significant organ. The *meforshim* note simply that the liver was a substantial additional piece. (The kidneys, it should be noted, are traditionally regarded as the seat of intelligence; the liver is simply very large, which is why having it attached elevates the perceived importance of the right flank.)

### Tail Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לָעוֹקֶץ, חֲתָכוֹ, וּנְתָנוֹ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בּוֹ. הָאַלְיָה, וְאֶצְבַּע הַכָּבֵד, וּשְׁתֵּי כְּלָיוֹת עִמּוֹ. נָטַל אֶת הָרֶגֶל הַשְּׂמָאלִית, חֲתָכָהּ, וּנְתָנָהּ לְמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בָּהּ. נִמְצְאוּ כּוּלָּן עוֹמְדִים בְּשׁוּרָה, וְהָאֵבָרִים בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **He came to the haunch, cut it, and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp. And **the tail, and the finger-**like protrusion **of the liver, and the two kidneys** were **with it. He took** the remaining upper part of **the left** hind **leg, cut it, and gave it to** the priest **who won** the right to take **it** up to the ramp. This **resulted** in **all of** the nine priests who won the rights to take the limbs up to the ramp **standing in line, and the limbs** were **in their hands.**

*Ba lo la-oketz* — he came to the tail. *Chatcho ve-natno le-mi she-zacha bo* — he cut it and gave it to the one who won it. *Ve-ha-alyah ve-etzba ha-kaved* — the fat tail (characteristic of the broad-tailed sheep) and the lobe of the liver went with it. *U-shtei klayot imo* — and the two kidneys as well.

The *oketz* section with the fat tail, lobe of liver, and kidneys thus went to the third *kohen* (*gimel*) in the procession. The fat tail was considered especially prized — fat is consistently treated as the finest portion of the *qorban* — and the kidneys, traditionally the seat of understanding and discernment, were also attached to this section.

### Left Leg Final Cut

*Natal et ha-regel ha-smalit ve-chatachah ve-netanah le-mi she-zacha bah* — he took the left leg, cut it, and gave it to the one who won it. *Nimtze'u kulam omdim be-shurah* — they now all stood in a line.

The left leg is cut last because it is the leg from which the animal was suspended throughout the entire process of flaying and dismemberment. One could not remove it earlier without bringing the animal down. Once the left leg is finally severed, every piece has been distributed and every *kohen* is holding his portion. The left leg goes together with the tail section — assigned to the third *kohen* (*gimel*) — whose *kohen* had been waiting since receiving the tail and had to wait until the very end for his full portion.

The ordering throughout is driven entirely by practical efficiency rather than by any ritual prescription. As the *mefaresh* notes: *ve-khol ha-seder ha-zeh ein shum pisuk ki im sevara — lefi mah she-nakhon la'asot osim* — the entire sequence has no specific scriptural derivation, only logic. They did it in whatever way made the most convenient practical sense.

## Kohanim Procession

### First Kohen - Head & Leg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרִאשׁוֹן – בָּרֹאשׁ וּבָרֶגֶל. הָרֹאשׁ בִּימִינוֹ, וְחוֹטְמוֹ כְּלַפֵּי זְרוֹעוֹ, קַרְנָיו בֵּין אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, בֵּית שְׁחִיטָתוֹ מִלְּמַעְלָן, וְהַפֶּדֶר נָתוּן עָלֶיהָ. הָרֶגֶל שֶׁל יָמִין בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וּבֵית עוֹרָן לַחוּץ. הַשֵּׁנִי – בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, שֶׁל יָמִין בִּימִינוֹ, וְשֶׁל שְׂמֹאל בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וּבֵית עוֹרָן לַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **The first** priest stood **with the head and with the** right hind **leg** of the animal. Since it was more significant, **the head** was **in his right** hand, **and its nose** was turned **toward the** priest's **arm. Its horns** were **between his fingers,** and **the place of its slaughter** was **above, and the fats were placed upon it,** to conceal the bloody place of slaughter. **The right** hind **leg** was **in his left** hand, **and** the outer **side** of the leg, from which **its hide** was flayed, rather than the side on which the incision was made, was facing **out. The second** priest stood **with** the **two forelegs.** He held the **right** foreleg **in his right** hand **and** the **left** foreleg **in his left** hand, **and** the outer **side** of the leg, from which **its hide** was flayed, was facing **out.**

*Ha-rishon be-rosh u-ve-regel* — the first *kohen* carried the head and the right rear leg. *Ha-rosh be-yemino ve-chotmo klapei zero'o* — the head was in his right hand, nose oriented inward toward his arm rather than pointing outward. *Ve-karnav bein etzbe'otav* — the horns were held between his fingers for stability. *U-veit shehitato milma'alah* — the severed side — the place of the *shechita* — was facing upward. *Ve-ha-peder netunah aleihem* — and the fat was placed over it, covering the severed site in a dignified and aesthetically fitting manner, presenting the finest portion of the *qorban* outward.

*Ha-regel shel yamin be-smolo* — the right rear leg was in his left hand. *U-veit ha-or mi-bachutz* — the skin-side was facing outward, as this was the cleaner and more presentable side — the exterior of the limb as opposed to the cut surface.

### Second Kohen - Hands

*Ha-sheni bi-shtei yadav* — the second *kohen* held both front hands. *Shel yamin be-yemino ve-shel smol bi-smolo* — right in right, left in left. *U-veit ha-or mi-bachutz* — skin-side facing outward.

### Third Kohen - Tail & Leg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְּׁלִישִׁי – בָּעוֹקֶץ וּבָרֶגֶל. הָעוֹקֶץ בִּימִינוֹ, וְהָאַלְיָה מְדוּלְדֶּלֶת בֵּין אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, וְאֶצְבַּע הַכָּבֵד וּשְׁתֵּי כְּלָיוֹת עִמּוֹ. הָרֶגֶל שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וּבֵית עוֹרָן לַחוּץ. הָרְבִיעִי – בֶּחָזֶה וּבַגֵּרָה. הֶחָזֶה בִּימִינוֹ וְהַגֵּרָה בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וְצַלְעוֹתָיו בֵּין שְׁנֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו. הַחֲמִישִׁי – בִּשְׁתֵּי דְּפָנוֹת, שֶׁל יָמִין בִּימִינוֹ, וְשֶׁל שְׂמֹאל בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וּבֵית עוֹרָן לַחוּץ. הַשִּׁשִּׁי – בַּקְּרָבַיִם הַנְּתוּנִים בְּבָזֵךְ, וּכְרָעַיִם עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן מִלְּמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The third** priest stood **with the haunch and the** left hind **leg.** He held **the haunch in his right** hand, **and the tail** was **hanging between his fingers, and the finger-**like protrusion **of the liver and the two kidneys** were **with it.** He held **the left** hind **leg in his left** hand, **and** the outer **side** of the leg, from which **its hide** was flayed, was facing **out. The fourth** priest stood **with the breast and with the cud,** with **the breast in his right** hand **and the cud in his left** hand, **and its** two **ribs** were attached to the cud **between his two fingers. The fifth** priest stood **with** the **two flanks;** the **right** flank was **in his right** hand **and** the **left** flank **in his left** hand, **and** the outer **side** was facing **out. The sixth** priest stood **with the innards, which** were **placed in a vessel, and** the **lower legs** were placed **atop them from above.**

*Ha-shelishi be-oketz u-ve-regel* — the third *kohen* carried the tail section and the left rear leg. *Ha-oketz be-yemino* — the tail in his right hand. *Ve-ha-alyah medudelet bein etzbe'otav* — the fat tail was suspended between his fingers; it was placed in the right hand because the fat is considered the choicest portion of the *qorban*. *Ve-etzba ha-kaved u-shtei klayot imah* — the lobe of the liver and the two kidneys were with it. *Ve-ha-regel shel smol bi-smolo* — the left leg in the left hand. *U-veit ha-or mi-bachutz* — skin-side out.

### Fourth Kohen - Chest & T…

*Ha-revi'i ba-chaze u-va-gerah* — the fourth *kohen* carried the chest and the throat area. *Ha-chaze be-yemino ve-ha-gerah bi-smolo* — chest in the right, throat in the left. *Ve-tzla'otav bein shtei etzbe'otav* — the small ribs of the throat section — the two thin ribs that had been left at the top near the neck — were held between his fingers. Attached to the throat section were the trachea, the heart, and the lungs.

### Fifth Kohen - Flanks

*Ha-chamishi bi-shtei defanot* — the fifth *kohen* held the two flanks. *Shel yamin be-yemino ve-shel smol bi-smolo* — right in the right, left in the left. *U-veit ha-or mi-bachutz* — skin-side outward. The right flank carried the liver; the left flank carried the spinal cord and the spleen. Although the left was physically larger, the right was called "the big one" (*gedolah*) on account of the liver.

### Sixth Kohen - Organs & F…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְּׁבִיעִי – בַּסּוֹלֶת. הַשְּׁמִינִי – בַּחֲבִיתִּים. הַתְּשִׁיעִי – בַּיַּיִן. הָלְכוּ וּנְתָנוּם מֵחֲצִי כֶּבֶשׁ וּלְמַטָּה, בְּמַעֲרָבוֹ. וּמְלָחוּם, וְיָרְדוּ וּבָאוּ לָהֶן לְלִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית לִקְרוֹת אֶת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **The seventh** priest stood **with the fine flour** of the meal offering that accompanies the daily offering. **The eighth** priest stood **with the griddle-cake** offering sacrificed daily by the High Priest, half in the morning and half in the evening. **The ninth** priest stood **with the wine** for the libations that accompany the daily offering. The nine priests **went and placed** the items they were carrying on the area **from halfway** up **the ramp and below,** in the lower portion of the ramp, **on the west** side of the ramp, **and they salted** the limbs and the meal offering. **And they descended and came to the Chamber of Hewn Stone to recite** the morning ***Shema*** and the other texts that they would recite, as explained at the beginning of the next chapter.

*Ha-shishi ba-kirvayim* — the sixth *kohen* held the inner organs, *ha-nitunim ba-bazeich* — placed in a vessel. *Ve-ha-kra'ayim al gabeihen* — and the feet (*kra'ayim*) placed on top of them. This answers the question raised earlier as to who carried the feet: the feet went with the inner organs. Both were considered less prestigious portions — the feet were bony and difficult to flay, and the inner organs required extensive rinsing — and they traveled together in a single vessel.

### Flour, Mincha, Wine

The seventh *kohen* (*hashvi'i*) carried the *solet* — the flour offering. The eighth (*hashmini*) carried the *chavitin* — the daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*. The ninth (*hatishi*) carried the wine. It is noteworthy that the *solet* and wine, which are components of a single *qorban*, are separated between two people; the flour preparations appear to have been done first, with the wine following.

### Ramp Placement & Salt

*Halchu u-netanum me-chatzi ha-kevesh u-le-matah be-ma'aravo* — they walked and placed all of the pieces on the lower half of the ramp, on the western side. The ramp was ascended on the east, so placing the portions on the western side preserved the open lane for the next procession to travel up unimpeded. When the time came, the *kohanim* would come up the eastern side and retrieve the pieces from the west, bringing them up in a second stage to the top of the *mizbe'ach*. This two-stage process — first to the lower half, then to the top — reflects a broader pattern in the *avodah* of investing multiple steps in actions that could theoretically be done in one, lending greater weight and ceremony to the proceedings.

*U-melachum* — they salted everything, including the *menachot*, since everything placed on the *mizbe'ach* requires salt.

## Shema Recitation

### Lishkat Hagazit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: יָד וָרֶגֶל, כַּעֲקֵידַת יִצְחָק בֶּן אַבְרָהָם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the priests would bind the lamb for the daily offering. With regard to this procedure, the Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: The animal's **foreleg and** hind **leg** are bound together, **as** in the **binding of Isaac, son of Abraham.**

*U-va'u lahem le-lishkat ha-gazit* — and they came to the *lishkat ha-gazit*, the Chamber of Hewn Stone, where the *sanhedrin* sat. *Likro et shema* — to recite *shema*.

### Prayer & Sacrifices

The timing of *keriat shema* here is significant on more than one level. From a purely practical standpoint, the early morning light — just as the morning star has risen — is one of the ideal times for *keriat shema*, and so reciting it at this juncture is entirely appropriate. But there is a deeper dimension as well.

The *mefaresh* on this passage emphasizes that the *kohanim* recite *tefillah* at this precise moment — after the *zerikat ha-dam*, which technically completes the *korban*, but before the *ketoret* is burned, and before the limbs and fats are offered. His formulation is striking: *ve-hachi nakhon ha-davar she-yitpallelu kodem she-yaqtir shum haqter* — it is most fitting that they pray before any *haqtarah* (burning) takes place. And the reason: *veya'al qorbanam le-rei'ach nihoach* — so that the sacrifice will ascend as a *rei'ach nihoach*, as a pleasing fragrance. The idea is that the *tefillah* frames the burning of the *qorban*, contextualizing it and investing it with the intention and devotion that causes it to be received favorably. The *tefillah* comes first so that the *qorban* rises in its proper spirit.

This speaks directly to the fundamental relationship between *tefillah* and *qorbanot* — a question that runs through the entire architecture of Jewish prayer. Our *tefillot* are in many respects structured around the *qorbanot* they replaced and complemented. Here, in the Temple itself, the relationship is made explicit: the *tefillah* is not merely personal preparation, but a theological frame for the sacrificial act that follows.

## Gemara: Binding & Tables

### Binding Method Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הָיוּ כּוֹפְתִין אֶת הַטָּלֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בִּזְיוֹן קֳדָשִׁים. וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דִּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּחוּקֵּי הָעַמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the priests **would not tie the lamb** by fastening all four of its legs together. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers: This is a matter of dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda. One** of these Sages **said:** The animal is not tied **because** this would constitute **degradation of sacred items; and** the other **one said** that the animal is not tied **because that** method is the one adopted in pagan worship, and is therefore considered to be **walking in the statutes of the nations,** and the verse states: "You shall not walk in their statutes" (Leviticus 18:3).

*Lo hayu koftin et ha-taleh* — the *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for avoiding the standard binding? Two *amoraim* dispute the matter. Rav Huna and Rav Hisda disagree: one says *mishum bizui qodashim* — it is a disgrace to sacred offerings; the standard way of binding an animal (all four legs together, or front-to-front and hind-to-hind) is the ordinary, undignified way one moves an animal to the butcher, and it is beneath the dignity of a *qorban*. The other says *mishum de-mahalkhu be-huqqei ha-amim* — because that is how non-Jewish peoples bind their sacrificial animals, and we must do things differently.

It is notably striking that neither opinion gives the reason as *aqeidat Yitzchak ben Avraham* — which suggests that the reference to the *aqeidah* in the *mishnah* was more descriptive than prescriptive. The *aqeidah* language explains *what* was done; these two *amoraim* explain *why*.

*Mai beinaihu* — what practical difference follows from the two reasons? *Ika beinaihu de-kaftei be-shiraei* — a case where one bound it with silk scarves — *o be-shushalta de-dahava* — or with a golden chain. If the reason is *bizayon*, disgrace, then silk and gold are not disgraceful — they are luxurious — and such binding would presumably be permitted. But if the reason is *huqqei ha-amim*, the prohibition on imitating non-Jewish sacrificial practice, then even binding with gold chains would be forbidden, since the manner itself (not the material) is what must be avoided.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ דְּכַפְתֵיהּ בְּשִׁירָאֵי. אִי נָמֵי, בְּהוּצָא דְּדַהֲבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these opinions? The Gemara answers: There is a difference **between** them in a case **where** one **ties** the animal **with silk [*beshira'ei*],** which would be considered to be treating the offering in the manner of the nations, but it is not degrading. **Alternatively,** these opinions differ with regard to a case where the animal is tied **with a thread of gold.** As in the case of the silk, tying the animal with gold would be considered to be treating the offering in the manner of the nations, but it is not a degradation.

### Thirteen Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שׁוּלְחָנוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. שְׁמוֹנָה שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ בְּבֵית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִים, שֶׁעֲלֵיהֶם מְדִיחִין אֶת הַקְּרָבַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the innards were rinsed on marble tables in the slaughterhouse in the Temple. With regard to these tables, **we learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Shekalim* 17b) that there **were thirteen tables in the Temple. Eight** of them were fashioned **from marble** and were located **in the slaughterhouse,** north of the altar, **where** the priests would slaughter the offerings of the most sacred order. **Upon these** tables **they would wash the innards** of the offerings, as the cool marble preserved the freshness of the meat.

*Tnan hatam* — the *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from elsewhere: *shloshah asar shulhanot hayu ba-miqdash* — there were thirteen tables in the *miqdash*. The *gemara* then proceeds to enumerate them.

### Washing Tables

*Shemonah shel shayish be-veit ha-mitbehayim* — eight tables of marble in the slaughtering area. *She'aleihem madichin et ha-kirvayim* — these were the tables on which the inner organs were rinsed. As discussed in connection with the *mishnah*, these marble tables stood between the pillars used for flaying, and it was on them that the intestines were washed.

### Ramp Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם בַּמַּעֲרָב שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ, אֶחָד שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ וְאֶחָד שֶׁל כֶּסֶף. עַל שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ נוֹתְנִין אֶת הָאֵבָרִים, וְעַל שֶׁל כֶּסֶף כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** There were **two** more tables **on the western** side **of the ramp,** south of the altar, **one of marble and one of silver. On** the table **of marble** the priests would **place the limbs** before they would bring them up to the altar. **And on** the table **of silver** they would place the ninety-three **service vessels** brought out from the Chamber of Vessels each morning for the services of that day.

*Shnayim be-ma'aravah shel kevesh* — two tables at the west of the ramp. *Ehad shel shayish ve-ehad shel kesef* — one of marble, one of silver. *Al shel shayish notnim et ha-eivarim* — on the marble one, the limbs were placed. This would apply not only to the *tamid* but to other *qorbanot* as well, where the various pieces needed a resting place as they were being carried to the ramp. *Ve-al shel kesef klei sharet* — on the silver one, the sacred vessels were set down.

### Ulam Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָאוּלָם שְׁנַיִם מִבִּפְנִים עַל פֶּתַח הַבַּיִת, אֶחָד שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְאֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב. עַל שֶׁל כֶּסֶף נוֹתְנִין לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ, וְעַל שֶׁל זָהָב בִּיצִיאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And in the Entrance Hall** there were **two** tables **on** its **inside, near the opening to the Temple, one of silver and one of gold. On** the table **of silver** the priests would **place the shewbread before its entrance** to the Sanctuary, after it was baked on Shabbat eve. **And on** the table **of gold** they would place the old shewbread **upon its exit** from the Sanctuary, to be divided among the priests.

*U-ve-ulam shnayim mi-bifnim* — in the *ulam* (the hall before the *heichal*), two tables on the inside. *Al petach ha-bayit* — right by the entrance to the *heichal* proper. *Ehad shel kesef ve-ehad shel zahav* — one of silver, one of gold. *Al shel kesef notnim lechem ha-panim be-chnisato* — on the silver table, the *lechem ha-panim* was placed when being brought in. The new loaves of *lechem ha-panim* were baked, carried to the *heichal*, and set down on the silver table as a resting point — a two-stage process similar to what we observed with the ramp. *Ve-al shel zahav be-yetzi'ato* — on the gold table, the *lechem ha-panim* was placed when being taken out — when the old loaves were removed from the golden table inside the *heichal* and brought out.

Why one of silver for the inbound bread and one of gold for the outbound? *She-ma'alin ba-kodesh ve-lo moridin* — one ascends in holiness, one does not descend. The bread is going toward the golden table inside the *heichal*, so it must rest on a table of lesser sanctity — silver — so that its final placement on the golden table inside represents an ascent. On its way out, having rested on the gold table in the *heichal*, it cannot be placed on something lesser — and so the outbound resting table must also be gold. There was no material more prestigious than gold available to go higher, so both the inner table and the outbound table were gold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמַּעֲלִין בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין. וְאֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב בִּפְנִים, שֶׁעָלָיו לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים תָּמִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The shewbread was not placed on a silver table upon its exit from the Sanctuary, **as** one **promotes in** matters of **sanctity and** one **does not demote.** Since in the interim the shewbread had been placed on the golden Table for the shewbread inside the Sanctuary, upon its removal it was not placed on anything other than a golden table. **And** lastly, there was **one** table **of gold inside** the Sanctuary. This was the Table for the shewbread, **upon which the shewbread always** rested (see Exodus 25:23–30).

### Showbread Table

The thirteenth table is, of course, the golden table inside the *heichal* upon which the *lechem ha-panim* rested continuously. *She'alav lechem ha-panim tamid* — this is the table on which the showbread sat at all times. The enumeration: eight in the slaughtering area, two by the ramp, two in the *ulam*, and one inside the *heichal*, brings the total to thirteen.

### Marble vs. Metal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי אֵין עֲנִיּוּת בִּמְקוֹם עֲשִׁירוּת, אַמַּאי עָבְדִי דְּשַׁיִשׁ? נַיעְבְּדוּ דְּכֶסֶף, נַיעְבְּדוּ דְּזָהָב! אָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בְּשֵׁם רַבִּי אַסִּי, וְרַבִּי אַסִּי בְּשֵׁם רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַרְתִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since** there is a principle that **there** may be **no poverty in a place of wealth,** i.e., the Temple must always be run in a lavish manner, why did they **fashion** any tables **of marble? Let them fashion** all the tables **of silver,** due to the grandeur of the Temple, or **let them fashion** them all **of gold. Rav Ḥinnana says in the name of Rabbi Asi, and Rabbi Asi** says **in the name of Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak:** Gold and silver tables are unfit for the sacrificial limbs **because** metal **scalds.** Unlike marble, metal can become very hot in the sun, and this might cause the sacrificial limbs to deteriorate.

*Mikh-di ein aniyut bi-meqom ashirut* — surely there is no poverty in a place of wealth. Given the extraordinary resources devoted to the *miqdash*, why were any of the tables made of marble? Why not make all of them of silver or gold?

*Amar Rabbi Chanina be-shem Rabbi Assi, Rabbi Assi be-shem Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak: mipnei she-hu martiach* — because metals conduct heat; they literally "boil" — they warm whatever is placed on them and would cause the meat and inner organs to spoil. Marble is cold and keeps its surface cool, making it suitable wherever food was placed on it. Accordingly, every table used for limbs or intestines — in the slaughtering area and at the ramp — was of marble. Only where vessels or bread were set down — surfaces that did not need to keep food cold — could the tables be of silver and gold as befitted the *miqdash*'s splendor.

### Location & Opposite the Sun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל שַׁחַר הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״, כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the daily offering **of the morning was slaughtered at the northwest corner** of the altar, in the first ring of the second row from the south, which is called the second ring, whereas the daily offering of the afternoon was slaughtered at the northeast corner of the altar, at the second ring. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Ḥisda said: As the verse states,** with regard to the daily offering: "This is the offering made by fire that you shall bring to the Lord: Lambs of the first year without blemish, **two by day,** for a continual burnt offering" (Numbers 28:3). The phrase "two by day" indicates that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.** Since Eretz Yisrael is north of the equator, the sun is always in the southern part of the sky. The first ring, then, is always in the long shadow of the altar, and only the second ring falls under direct sunlight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״ – כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹם. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר נֶגֶד הַיּוֹם, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוֹבַת הַיּוֹם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ אֶחָד תַּעֲשֶׂה בַבֹּקֶר וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – הֲרֵי חוֹבַת הַיּוֹם אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״ – נֶגֶד הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*, that the phrase **"two by day"** teaches that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that this means **opposite** the light of **the day, or** does it **only** mean that two lambs must be sacrificed for the **obligation of** each **day?** The *baraita* answers that **when** the verse **states: "One lamb you shall sacrifice in the morning, and the other lamb you shall sacrifice in the afternoon"** (Numbers 28:4), **the obligation of** each **day is** thereby **stated** explicitly. **How,** then, **do I realize** the meaning of **"two by day"?** This teaches that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵיצַד? תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּה, וְשֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם – הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מִזְרָחִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **How so,** i.e., how can this principle be applied to both the morning and the afternoon offerings? The **daily offering of the morning was slaughtered opposite** the **northwest corner** of the altar, **on** the **second ring,** across from the sun, which rises in the east. **And** the daily offering **of the afternoon was slaughtered opposite** the **northeast corner** of the altar, **on** the fourth ring of the second row, also called the **second ring,** again across from the sun, which is located in the west in the afternoon.
