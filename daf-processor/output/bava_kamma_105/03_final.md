# Bava Kamma 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hashavah Below Peruta

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִפָּחוֹת שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בַּקֶּרֶן כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **excepting less than** the **value of one *peruta* of the principal,**

The daf opens approximately eight lines from the top, at the phrase *chutz me'hashavat shaveh perutah la'keren* — "except for returning the value of a *perutah* to the principal." The *mishnah* is addressing a specific scenario: a thief who not only stole but swore falsely about it bears a heightened obligation, beyond merely returning the stolen object. He must actively seek out the victim and restore the *gezeilah* wherever that person may be found. The *mishnah* qualifies, however, that this intensified obligation dissolves once the principal has declined to less than a *perutah*. The *gemara* now sets out to probe the contours of this rule: under what circumstances, if any, does an obligation to return stolen property persist even when the remaining value falls below the *perutah* threshold?

### Rav Papa: Gezeilah Gone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת, אֲבָל גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת – צָרִיךְ לֵילֵךְ אַחֲרָיו; חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תִּיַּיקֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: They taught** that the robber is not obligated to pursue the robbery victim **only when** the **stolen item is not extant, but** if the **stolen item is** still **extant, he must pursue him** to return it, as **we are concerned** that **perhaps** the stolen item **will appreciate** in value, and the debt he owes will exceed the value of one *peruta*.

Rav Papa addresses the case directly: *lo shanu ela she'ein gezeilah kayyemet* — the rule that no obligation remains applies only when the physical stolen object is no longer present and the entire claim has been reduced to a monetary one. A monetary obligation worth less than a *perutah* has no legal standing. But if the object itself is still physically present — *aval gezeilah kayyemet* — then *tzarich leilech achrav*, one must still pursue the victim and return it.

The reasoning given is *chashinan shema t'yaker* — we are concerned that the object's value may rise in the future. This rationale is admittedly a bit strange: if the concern is only about possible future appreciation, one might wonder why the obligation applies now rather than waiting until the value actually rises. Nevertheless, this is the first position.

### Future Appreciation

The *shema t'yaker* reasoning deserves a moment's attention. Even if the stolen object — say, a paper clip — currently has no *perutah*-worth of value, a court would not hear a claim for it, and the thief has no legal obligation to travel across town to return it. Yet the concern about future appreciation creates at least a possible future obligation, and this is offered as grounds for a present-day duty to return the object while it is still in hand.

One might press the point further: even setting aside future appreciation, the object is fundamentally the victim's property. The thief has no moral right to retain it, regardless of its market value. This dimension — the basic proprietary right to reclaim one's own object — is notably absent from the *gemara*'s explicit reasoning, a lacuna that is at least worth flagging. The related question of whether taking something worth less than a *perutah* even constitutes a transgression of *lo tigzol* is itself unresolved; the *Rambam*'s discussion of wounds worth less than a *perutah* creating *malkus* liability (since there is no monetary compensation, thus removing the principle of *nittan letashlumin*) does not have a parallel ruling in cases of theft below the *perutah* threshold, perhaps because taking something of negligible monetary value is not fully characterized as *gezeilas mamon*.

### Alt View: No Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא שְׁנָא גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת וְלָא שְׁנָא שֶׁאֵין גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֵילֵךְ אַחֲרָיו; לְשֶׁמָּא תִּיַּיקֵּר לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Pappa said:** The *halakha* **is not different** when the **stolen item is extant, and** it **is not different when** the **stolen item is not extant.** In either case **he does not need to pursue him** because **we are not concerned that perhaps it will appreciate** in value.

A second version of Rav Papa's position takes a stronger stance: *lo shna gezeilah kayyemet velo shna she'ein gezeilah kayyemet* — it makes no difference whether the stolen object is still present or not. Even if the thief is holding the actual paper clip, *eino tzarich leilech achrav*, he is not required to chase after the victim. And *leshema t'yaker lo chashinan* — we are not preemptively concerned about future appreciation. If the value rises, the obligation will arise at that point; there is no current duty to act on a merely hypothetical future value.

The practical and moral tension here is real. The thief is holding someone else's object without any right to it. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension directly, though it is clear that *beis din* will not hear the claim, and — according to this second view of Rav Papa — the affirmative legal duty to pursue and return is equally absent.

### Rava: Three Bunches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גָּזַל שָׁלֹשׁ אֲגוּדּוֹת בְּשָׁלֹשׁ פְּרוּטוֹת, וְהוּזְלוּ וְעָמְדוּ עַל שְׁתַּיִם; אִם הֶחְזִיר לוֹ שְׁתַּיִם – חַיָּיב לְהַחֲזִיר לוֹ אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If one **robbed** another of **three bundles** of goods that **were** worth **three *perutot*** in total, **and they depreciated** in value **and** their value **stood at two** *perutot*, even **if he returned two** bundles **to** the robbery victim he **is obligated to return the other** bundle.

Rava introduces a more nuanced case: *gazal shalosh agudot beshalosh prutot* — a thief stole three bunches of parsley, each bunch worth one *perutah* at the time of theft. Subsequently the market declined and the three bunches together are now worth only two *perutot*, meaning each bunch is now worth less than a *perutah* on its own.

The rule of *kol hagazlanim meshalmin kishat hagezeilah* establishes that if the stolen objects were lost or destroyed, the thief would owe the full three *perutot* — the value at the time of theft. But if the objects are still present, the question is whether the thief must return them. Rava rules: *im hehzir lo shtayim hayav lehahazir lo aheret* — if the thief returned two of the three bunches, he is still obligated to return the third, even though the third bunch, at current value, is worth less than a *perutah*. The *hayav lehahazir* here implies not merely a moral claim but a genuine legal obligation to return even that sub-*perutah* remainder.

### Hametz Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא: גָּזַל חָמֵץ וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״. טַעְמָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ; הָא לֵיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּהַשְׁתָּא לָאו מָמוֹנָא, כֵּיוָן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מָמוֹנָא הוּא – בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּהַשְׁתָּא לֹא שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה [כֵּיוָן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הָוֵי שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה] – בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *tanna*** of the mishna also **taught:** If one **robbed** another of **leavened bread, and Passover elapsed over it,** and it is therefore prohibited to derive benefit from it, the robber **says to** the victim: **That which is yours is before you,** and he is not required to pay compensation, despite the fact that the robbery victim has suffered a monetary loss. **The Gemara explains the proof from the mishna: It may be inferred that** the **reason** that he is not required to pay compensation is **that** the bread **is** still **there in its unadulterated** form; **but** if it **is not there in its unadulterated** form, **even though now it is not** worth **money, since it was** worth **money initially,** at the time of the robbery, **he is required to pay. Here too, even though** the third bundle is **not worth one *peruta* now, since initially it was worth one *peruta*, he is required to pay.**

The basis for this ruling is drawn from a parallel *mishnah*: *gazal hametz ve-avar alav ha-Pesach* — if one stole *chametz* and Passover passed, rendering it entirely worthless, the thief may say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — here is your property before you — return the object and be discharged of the debt. The *chametz* is now worth nothing, yet returning it suffices.

The *gemara* asks how this case illuminates Rava's ruling about the parsley. The answer lies in understanding the structure of the *chametz* case: *ta'ama de-isa be-inei* — the reason returning the worthless *chametz* exempts the thief is precisely because the object is still present. *Ha-leisa be-inei, af al gav de-hashta lav mamona* — if it were not present, even though it is currently worth nothing, the thief would still owe the original hundred-dollar value. Why? *Keivan de-me-ikara mamona hava bai shlumei* — because at the time of the theft it was worth real money, and that original monetary act of *gezeilah* obligates full restitution.

### Original Value Theory

This logic applies symmetrically to Rava's case: *hacha nami af al gav de-hashta lo shaveh perutah, keivan de-me-ikara hava shaveh perutah bai shlumei* — here too, even though the remaining bunch of parsley is currently worth less than a *perutah*, since at the time of theft it was worth a *perutah*, the thief is obligated to make restitution.

The underlying principle can be stated as follows: the original act of *gezeilah*, when it constituted a genuine *gezeilas mamon* worth at least a *perutah*, imposes a permanent obligation. That obligation must be discharged either by returning the original object as it now stands — regardless of its current value — or by paying the original value. Just as with the *chametz*, the ability to say *harei shelcha lefanecha* and discharge a debt by returning a worthless object is available precisely because the debt exists. The *gezeilah* was real, and therefore so is the duty of *hashavah*.

## Partial Return Problem

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: גָּזַל שְׁתֵּי אֲגוּדּוֹת, בִּפְרוּטָה וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ אַחַת מֵהֶן – מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הַשְׁתָּא לֵיכָּא גְּזֵילָה; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָא לָא [אַ]הְדַּר גְּזֵילָה דַּהֲוַאי גַּבֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** If one **robbed** another of **two bundles** of goods that **were** worth **one *peruta*** in total, **and he returned one of them to** the robbery victim, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **now there is no** longer **a stolen item** in the possession of the robber, as the remaining bundle is worth less than one *peruta*, and he has therefore fulfilled his obligation to return the stolen item; **or perhaps** we say that since the robber **did not return** the **stolen item that was in his possession** in its entirety, he is obligated to return the second bundle?

Rava now raises a more difficult question: *gazal shtei agudot beperutah* — suppose a thief stole two bunches of carrots that together were worth one *perutah*. Neither bunch alone was ever worth a *perutah*. The thief then returned one bunch. What is the status of the remaining bunch?

This is meaningfully different from the three-bunches case. There, each bunch was originally worth a *perutah* on its own, and the question was only about depreciation. Here, neither bunch was ever individually worth a *perutah*. The entire *gezeilah* was worth a *perutah* only as a combined transaction. The thief has returned half. Does the remaining half — which was never, even at the moment of theft, worth a *perutah* on its own — carry any obligation of return?

### Two Approaches

Two frameworks present themselves. The first focuses on the current status of what remains: *hashta leika gezeilah* — at this point, what the thief holds does not constitute a *gezeilah* in any legally cognizable sense. It was never worth a *perutah* independently, and therefore there is nothing that compels return. The second framework focuses on the original transaction: *ha la hadar degezeilah de hava gabeih* — the thief has not yet fully returned what he stole. He stole two bunches as a single act worth a *perutah* and has returned only one. His *hashavah* is incomplete, regardless of whether what remains would individually qualify as a minimum-value *gezeilah*.

### Gezeilah/Hashavah Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: גְּזֵילָה אֵין כָּאן, הֲשָׁבָה אֵין כָּאן. אִי גְּזֵילָה אֵין כָּאן – הֲשָׁבָה יֵשׁ כָּאן! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁגְּזֵילָה אֵין כָּאן, מִצְוַת הֲשָׁבָה אֵין כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava himself **then resolves** the dilemma: **There is no stolen item here,** as the remaining bundle is worth less than one *peruta*; **there is no returning** of a stolen item **here.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this expression: **If there is no stolen item here,** as what remains is insignificant, it follows that **there is** fulfillment of the mitzva of **returning** the stolen item **here,** and the robber ought to be exempt, as he is no longer considered to be in possession of a stolen item. The Gemara explains that **this is** what Rava **is saying: Even though** the robber is exempt from returning the second bundle, as **there is no stolen item here, there is** nevertheless **no** fulfillment of the **mitzva of returning** a stolen item **here,** since the returned bundle was worth less than one *peruta* as well.

Rava resolves the question with a striking formulation: *gezeilah ein kan, hashavah ein kan* — there is no *gezeilah* here, and there is no *hashavah* here. The *gemara* initially parses this in the most natural direction: if there is no *gezeilah* left in the thief's hand — because what remains is sub-*perutah* — then there is nothing outstanding to return, and presumably the *mitzvah* of *hashavah* has been fulfilled to whatever degree possible.

But that reading is rejected. The *gemara* reframes: *af al pi shegezeila ein kan, mitvat hashavah ein kan* — even though what remains is no longer considered a cognizable *gezeilah*, the thief has not fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *hashavah*. *Tosafot* captures the *nafka mina* directly: the thief has no remaining legal compulsion — *beis din* cannot force him — yet he has also not discharged his *mitvat hashavah*, because he never fully returned what he stole.

This creates an interesting intermediate space. The thief is not legally actionable for the sub-*perutah* remainder, yet the *mitzvah* of *hashavah* remains in some sense unfulfilled. What that unfulfillment means in practical terms — and whether, for instance, it blocks bringing a *korban* in a case where a false *shevuah* was involved — is a significant downstream question that emerges from the analysis.

### Nazir Hair Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא, הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: נָזִיר שֶׁגִּילַּח וְשִׁיֵּיר שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם; בָּעֵי רָבָא: גִּילַּח אַחַת וְנָשְׁרָה אַחַת, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: נָזִיר שֶׁגִּילַּח אַחַת אַחַת קָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא?!
>
> **Translation:** § Following Rava's previous dilemma, the Gemara cites a similar dilemma raised by Rava. **And Rava says:** The Sages **said** that **a nazirite who shaved** his head as required **but left two hairs** uncut **has done nothing,** and his obligation to shave his head has not been fulfilled. **Rava raises a dilemma:** If a nazirite shaved and left two hairs, and afterward he **shaved one** of them, **and** the other **one fell out** of its own accord, **what is** the *halakha*? Is this considered shaving one's entire head or not? **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: Is Rava raising a dilemma** as to whether one can **shave** his head **one** hair **by one** hair? How does this case differ from that of one who shaves his entire head one hair at a time, which is a fulfillment of his obligation?

Rava draws a parallel from an entirely different domain to illuminate this structure. The *mishnah* establishes that a *nazir* who shaves but leaves two hairs has not fulfilled his *mitzvah* of *giluach* — *lo asa velo klum*. Two hairs constitute a *shiur*, a minimum significant quantity; one hair alone does not.

Rava asks: suppose the *nazir* shaved everything except two hairs, and then one of those two hairs fell out on its own. He then goes back and shaves the remaining one. Has he fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *giluach*? The scenario matters because of the *order* of events. Rav Acha from Difti asks Ravina whether Rava's question concerns a case where one shaved one hair at a time — but if so, there would be no question, since shaving each hair individually is obviously valid. Ravina clarifies: the question is specifically where the order is reversed — *kegon shenashra achat ve'achar kach gilach achat* — one hair fell out first, and *then* he went back and shaved one.

### Hair Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּשְׁרָ[ה] אַחַת מֵהֶן, וְגִילַּח אַחַת; מִי אָמְרִינַן: הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת הָא לֵיכָּא שִׁיעוּר; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָא לָאו גִּילּוּחַ הוּא – דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הָא שַׁיַּיר שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְהַשְׁתָּא כִּי גִּילַּח לָא הָוֵי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: No,** a resolution to Rava's dilemma **is necessary** in a case **where one of** the hairs **fell out** first, **and** then **he shaved** the other **one. Do we say** that **now, in any event, there is no measure** of hair left on his head that requires shaving, as one remaining hair is not significant, and he has therefore fulfilled his obligation; **or perhaps** we say that **this is not** considered **shaving, as initially he left** the significant amount of **two hairs** uncut, **and now when he shaves** a second time, **there are not two hairs** left for him to shave, and this does not qualify as shaving?

The difficulty is this: when the *nazir* finally performs the shaving act, only one hair remains — below the *shiur* of two. The question is whether an act of *giluach* performed on a single hair counts at all. *Mi amrinan hashta miha leika shiur?* — do we say that since there is now no minimum quantity of hair left, the obligation is discharged, as nothing of substance remains? Or do we say — *o dilma hai lav giluach hu dimeikara* — that the *mitzvah* was not originally completed (two hairs were left), and the final act of shaving one hair does not constitute a valid *giluach* because at the moment of that act, there was already less than the required *shiur*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: שֵׂעָר אֵין כָּאן, גִּילּוּחַ אֵין כָּאן. אִי שֵׂעָר אֵין כָּאן – גִּילּוּחַ יֵשׁ כָּאן! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשֵּׂעָר אֵין כָּאן, מִצְוַת גִּילּוּחַ אֵין כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava himself **then resolves** the dilemma: **There is no hair here; there is no shaving here.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this expression: **If there is no hair here,** then **there is shaving here,** as no hair remains. The Gemara explains that **this is what** Rava **is saying: Even though there is no hair here,** as only one hair remains, nevertheless **there is no mitzva of shaving here,** as he failed to shave it all on the first attempt, and the second time he shaved less than the required amount.

Rava resolves with the same phrasing as before: *se'ar ein kan, giluach ein kan* — there is no hair, and there is no *giluach*. Again, the *gemara* notes that the natural reading of "there is no hair" would lead one to expect "therefore you are *yotzei*," since nothing remains. But the *gemara* reframes: *af al pi she'se'ar ein kan, mitzvat giluach ein kan* — even though there is nothing left, the *mitzvah* of *giluach* was not properly fulfilled. The original shaving left two hairs and thus was deficient; the subsequent shaving acted on fewer than the requisite *shiur* and thus does not constitute a valid completing act.

*Tosafot* notes that according to some *poskim*, *giluach* is not *me'akev* — it does not block the *nazir* from proceeding — in which case the practical consequence is that he has not fully performed the *mitzvah* but is not entirely barred. If *giluach* is *me'akev*, the *nazir* would have to wait for the hair to regrow and begin again. *Rashi*'s position, according to a discussion in the *shiur*, is that he does not need to wait for regrowth before proceeding to other aspects of completion.

The parallel back to the *gezeilah* case is now clarified: in both domains, the *gemara* is charting a middle territory — there is nothing left that the law can compel one to act upon, yet the core *mitzvah* has not been truly discharged. As one student observed, by tracing the outer limits of legal compulsion so precisely, the *gemara* implicitly identifies what remains in the moral domain: an obligation one cannot be constrained to fulfill, but that nonetheless exists. Whether this has further halakhic consequences — such as whether a *korban* in a *shevuah* case can be brought before full *hashavah* has been effected — is a question that deserves further attention in light of *Tosafot*'s discussion on 103a regarding delivery of payment to an agent of *beis din*.

## False Oaths & Monetary Obligation

### Hametz Oath Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא, הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: גָּזַל חָמֵץ וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״. בָּעֵי רָבָא: נִשְׁבַּע עָלָיו, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאִי מִיגְּנֵב בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ – מָמוֹנָא קָא כָפַר לֵיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת הָא מַנַּח וְעַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וְלָא כָּפַר לֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another dilemma raised by Rava. **And Rava says:** The Sages **said** that if one **robbed** another of **leavened bread, and Passover elapsed over it,** and it is therefore prohibited to derive benefit from it, the robber **may say to** the victim: **That which is yours is before you. Rava raises a dilemma:** If the robber **took** a false **oath concerning** the robbery of the leavened bread, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since, if it** were to be **stolen** from the robber, the robber **would need to pay** the initial value of the bread **to** the robbery victim, as he can no longer say: That which is yours is before you, and therefore **he has denied** a claim of **a monetary matter to** the robbery victim and must consequently pay the additional one-fifth payment and bring a guilt-offering? **Or perhaps** we say that **now, in any event, it is resting** in the robber's possession **and it is** considered as **mere dust,** since it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, and as such **he has not denied** a claim of **a monetary matter,** and the *halakhot* concerning a false oath do not apply.

Rava poses a fascinating question about the intersection of *shevuah* and monetary obligation. The facts: I steal David's *chametz* right before Passover when it is worth a thousand dollars. Once Passover passes, it becomes entirely worthless. Under normal circumstances, if the object is present, I may return it and discharge my obligation with *harei shelcha lefanecha*; if it is gone, I owe the original thousand dollars. Now suppose that when David confronted me, I swore falsely that I did not steal his *chametz* — and then admitted the truth. The question is whether this false *shevuah* carries all the consequences of a *shevuat mamon*: the obligation to bring a *korban*, to pay a *chomesh*, and so on.

The tension is acute. Normally, a false *shevuah* in a monetary dispute obligates these consequences because through the oath one is effectively stealing from the victim — denying him money that is rightfully his. Here, however, my oath only denied David his right to collect a pile of *chametz* that is now *afra be'alma*, dust, entirely without monetary value. Am I really denying him anything of substance? Perhaps: *mamona kachafar lei* — perhaps my oath does deny him a monetary claim, because if the *chametz* were to be stolen or lost, I would owe him the full original value. My false *shevuah* thus prevents him from establishing a claim that could translate into real money under certain future circumstances.

Or perhaps the opposite: since the *chametz* is right there, physically present and worthless — *ha manach, ve'afra be'alma hu, velo kafar lei mamona* — my oath is denying him nothing monetary whatsoever right now. Rava leaves this as a genuine question.

### Rabbah: Possible Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא, פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבָּה – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: ״שׁוֹרִי גָּנַבְתָּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״; ״מָה טִיבוֹ אֶצְלְךָ?״ ״שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם אֲנִי עָלָיו״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי פָּטַר עַצְמוֹ מִגְּנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The matter that was a dilemma for Rava is obvious to Rabba, as Rabba says** that in a scenario where one says to another: **You stole my ox, and** the other **says: I did not steal** it. The first asks: **What** is the **nature of its** presence **in your** possession? The second responds: **I am an unpaid bailee over it.** If the defendant then takes a false oath to that effect, after which he confesses that he stole the ox, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering for denying a claim of a monetary matter, **as he exempted himself from** liability in cases of **theft and loss.**

To Rabbah, the answer is obvious, and the resolution follows from a principle he had already stated. Rabbah's earlier case: David says to me, "You stole my ox." I reply, "I did not steal it — I am merely a *shomer chinam* on it." I take a *shevuah* to that effect, but I am lying. Rabbah rules I am *chayav* — obligated to bring the *korban* and all attendant consequences. The reason: *shepatar atzmo migneiva va'aveida* — through my oath I have exempted myself from a level of liability. A *gazlan* is liable even in cases of *ones* — unavoidable loss. A *shomer chinam* is not. So by falsely claiming to be a *shomer chinam*, I have — for the period between my oath and the animal's return — effectively insulated myself from a class of liability that David had a right to hold me to.

The key principle Rabbah derives is that even a *possible* future monetary liability, if one's false *shevuah* negates it, qualifies as *kafarat mamon* — denying a monetary claim — sufficient to trigger the full *korban* and *chomesh* obligations. Whether the animal actually dies or is stolen during that window is irrelevant; the false oath, at the moment it was taken, stripped the claimant of a genuine monetary right that could have materialized.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר אֲנִי עָלָיו״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי פָּטַר עַצְמוֹ מִשְּׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה. ״שׁוֹאֵל אֲנִי עָלָיו״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי פָּטַר עַצְמוֹ מִמֵּתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** If he responds instead: **I am a paid bailee over it,** and takes a false oath to that effect, after which he confesses that he stole the ox, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering for denying a claim of a monetary matter, **as he exempted himself from** liability in cases of **breakage and death.** If he responds instead: **I am a borrower of it,** and takes a false oath to that effect, after which he confesses that he stole the ox, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering for denying a claim of a monetary matter, **as he exempted himself from** liability in cases where the ox **died due to** ordinary **labor.**

Applied to Rava's *chametz* question: even though right now the *chametz* is present and worthless — *afra be'alma* — since if it were stolen I would owe a thousand dollars, *keivan de-mignav bayei shlumei leh, mamona ma'alya, ha nami mamona kachafar* — my false oath denies David that real potential claim. According to Rabbah, this is a fully valid *shevuah* on a monetary matter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּהָא קָאֵים – כֵּיוָן דְּאִי מִיגְּנֵיב מָמוֹנָא קָא כָפַר, הַשְׁתָּה נָמֵי מָמוֹנָא קָא כָפַר; הָכָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּעַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא – כֵּיוָן דְּאִי מִיגְּנִיב בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא מְעַלְּיָא, הַשְׁתָּה נָמֵי מָמוֹנָא קָא כָפַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In all of these cases, the ox is in the possession of the one who took the false oath, and he admits that it belongs to the other. The Gemara analyzes Rabbah's statement: **Apparently,** Rabbah holds that **even though** the ox is now **extant** and can be returned to its owner, **since, if it were to be stolen,** the robber **would** be found to **be denying** a claim of **a monetary matter,** as according to his claim he would then be exempt from paying, he is **now** considered to **be denying** a claim of **a monetary matter as well. Here,** in the case of the bread **also, even though it is** considered **mere dust, since, if it were to be stolen, he would be required to pay** the robbery victim **full-fledged monetary restitution,** he is **now** considered to **be denying** a claim of **a monetary matter as well.**

### Rav Amram's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רָבָא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב עַמְרָם לְרָבָא: ״וְכִחֵשׁ בָּהּ״ – פְּרָט לְמוֹדֶה בָּעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava sat and said this *halakha*,** that one who protected himself from potential liability for payment by taking a false oath that he is a bailee is liable to pay the additional one-fifth payment and bring a guilt-offering. **Rav Amram raised an objection to Rava** from a *baraita*: The verse states concerning one who is liable for taking a false oath concerning a monetary matter: **"And deals falsely with it"** (Leviticus 5:22); this serves to **exclude one who admits to the primary** feature of a claim.

Rav Amram raises a challenge against Rabbah's position from a *braita* derived from the *midrash halacha* on the *pesukim* in *Parshat Vayikra* dealing with false oaths: *ve-kichash ba'amito be-pikadon o bi-tesumas yad o ve-gazel...* — and the obligation of *hashavah* and *chomesh* that follows. The *midrash halacha* derives from the phrase *ve-kichash bah* — "he denied it" — *prat le-modeh be-ikar*: this excludes a case where one concedes the underlying claim (*modeh be-ikar*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? ״שׁוֹרִי גָּנַבְתָּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״; ״מָה טִיבוֹ אֶצְלְךָ?״ ״אַתָּה מְכַרְתּוֹ לִי״; ״אַתָּה נְתַתּוֹ לִי בְּמַתָּנָה״; ״אָבִיךָ מְכָרוֹ לִי״; ״אָבִיךָ נְתָנוֹ לִי בְּמַתָּנָה״; ״אַחַר פָּרָתִי רָץ״; ״מֵאֵלָיו בָּא אֶצְלִי״; ״תּוֹעֶה בַּדֶּרֶךְ מְצָאתִיו״; ״שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם אֲנִי עָלָיו״; ״שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר אֲנִי עָלָיו״; ״שׁוֹאֵל אֲנִי עָלָיו״ – וְנִשְׁבַּע וְהוֹדָה; יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִחֵשׁ בָּהּ״ – פְּרָט לְמוֹדֶה בָּעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How** is this **so?** In a scenario where one says to another: **You stole my ox, and** the latter **says: I did not steal** it. The first asks: **What** is the **nature of its** presence **in your** possession? The second responds with any of the following replies: **You sold it to me; you gave it to me as a gift; your father sold it to me; your father gave it to me as a gift; it chased after my cow** and entered my domain; **it came to me on its own; I found it straying on the road** and I brought it home; **I am an unpaid bailee over it; I am a paid bailee over it;** or **I am a borrower of it; and** the second **takes** a false **oath** to that effect, **and** then **admits** that he had done so; one **might** have thought that he **should be liable. The verse states: "And deals falsely with it,"** which serves to **exclude one who admits to the primary** feature of a claim. The *baraita* states explicitly that one who takes a false oath that he is a bailee is not liable; how can Rava state otherwise?

### Braita Reconciled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּדוֹרָא! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֵילָךְ״, כִּי קָאָמֵינָא – דְּקָיְימָא בַּאֲגַם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Fool, when that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is referring to a case **where** the robber **said to** the owner: **Here you are,** and presented the ox to him immediately, so that he did not profit by claiming that he was a bailee. **When I spoke,** I was referring to a case **where** the ox **was standing in** a different place, such as **a swamp,** and was not returned to the owner.

Rava responds — addressing Rav Amram sharply — *tadura*, and proceeds to distinguish the *braita*. The *braita*, he explains, is specifically discussing *de'amar leh heilach* — a case where at the moment of taking the *shevuah*, the respondent says "just take it." In other words, the object is immediately available for collection, the claimant can retrieve it at that very instant, and the oath is therefore not denying him anything at all — not even a potential future claim, because the object is being handed back in the very same breath.

*Ki ka'amina* — when Rabbah's rule applies, Rava clarifies, it is specifically *dekai be'agam* — when the object is out in the swamp, requiring time to go and retrieve. In that interval between the taking of the oath and the actual recovery of the object, the false oath is exposing the claimant to the risk of a period of uncompensated loss should something happen to the animal. It is that window of potential liability, foreclosed by the false oath, which triggers the obligation.

The *braita*'s *modeh be-ikar* exclusion thus applies only in the narrow case of *heilach* — immediate return — where there is genuinely no monetary interest being denied. Rabbah's cases, by contrast, all involve some meaningful gap between the oath and the actual recovery during which the false oath has real monetary consequences. The apparent contradiction is resolved.

### Conceding Principal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַתָּה מְכַרְתּוֹ לִי״ – מַאי מוֹדֶה בָּעִיקָּר אִיכָּא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֹא נָתַתִּי לְךָ דְּמֵי, שְׁקֹיל תּוֹרָךְ וְזִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** When the robber says: **You sold** the ox **to me,** he has asserted that it belongs to him, so **what admission to the primary** feature of the claim **is there?** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this in a case **where he said to him:** You sold the animal to me, but **I did not give you** the **money, take your ox and go.**

The *gemara* then revisits the earlier items in the *braita*'s list — those cases where the respondent claims sale or gift — to ask how those constitute *modeh be-ikar* at all. If I say "you sold it to me," I am claiming ownership, not conceding the claimant's title. How is that a concession of the principal?

The *gemara* answers: *lo natati lecha dameha* — "you sold it to me but I haven't paid you yet, so take it back." By acknowledging that payment was never made, the respondent is effectively conceding that title never fully transferred. *Shkol torech ve-zil* — take your animal and go. In this formulation, the claim of sale becomes a concession that the object is still the claimant's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַתָּה נְתַתּוֹ לִי״; ״אָבִיךָ נְתָנוֹ לִי״ – מַאי מוֹדֶה בָּעִיקָּר אִיכָּא? דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַל מְנָת דְּעָבֵידְנָא לָךְ נְיָיח דְּנַפְשָׁא, וְלָא עֲבַדִי לָךְ, שְׁקֹיל תּוֹרָךְ וְזִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** When the robber says: **You gave it to me,** or: **Your father gave it to me,** he has asserted that it belongs to him, so **what admission to the primary** feature of the claim **is there?** The Gemara explains: It is a case **where he said to him:** It was given to me **on the condition that I will afford you satisfaction, and I did not do** anything **for you; take your ox and go.**

Similarly, "your father gave it to me as a gift" — *al mnat de-avadat li niach nafsha*, you gave it to me expecting me to do you a service, *ve-lo avdi lach*, and I didn't do it. So take it back. Again, by acknowledging the conditional nature of the gift and declining to insist on it, the respondent concedes the principal and simultaneously offers immediate return, placing the case squarely within the *heilach* framework of the *braita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תּוֹעֶה בַּדֶּרֶךְ מְצָאתִיו״ – לֵימָא: אִיבְּעִי לָךְ לְאַהֲדוֹרֵיהּ לִי! אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, בְּאוֹמֵר: ״שְׁבוּעָה – אֲבֵידָה מָצָאתִי, וְלֹא הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהִיא שֶׁלְּךָ שֶׁאַחְזִירֶנָּה לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** When the robber says: **I found it straying on the road** and I brought it home, **let** the owner **say** to him: **You should have returned it to me. Shmuel's father said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **in** which **he says:** I take **an oath** that **I found** the ox as **a lost item, and I did not know that it was yours so that I could return it to you.**

The case of the wandering found animal — *to'eh baderech metzatziha* — is addressed by Abaye citing Shmuel: the person is saying *shvua aveda matzati ve-lo hayiti yodea she'ishach, she-hakhziranah lach* — "I swear I found it as a lost object and didn't know it was yours; now that you've told me, take it back." This is a classic *aveidah* scenario where the person held the animal pending identification of its owner. Once ownership is established, the willingness to return is immediate, and again no monetary claim is denied.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר בֶּן עַזַּאי: שָׁלֹשׁ שְׁבוּעוֹת הֵן – הִכִּיר בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּמוֹצְאָהּ, בְּמוֹצְאָהּ וְלֹא בָּהּ, לֹא בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּמוֹצְאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § After discussing a false oath taken by a robber in which he admits to the primary feature of a claim, the Gemara discusses a false oath taken by witnesses. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: When a witness takes an oath that he has no knowledge of the status of a lost item, **ben Azzai says** that **there are three** possible false **oaths,** i.e., the oath may be false for one of three reasons: The witness may have **recognized** the lost item **but** did **not** recognize **its finder,** or he may have recognized **its finder but** did **not** recognize the item, or he may have recognized **neither** the item **nor its finder.** In each of these cases, the witness took a false oath that he had no knowledge of the lost item, when in fact he had limited knowledge of it.

The *gemara* closes with Ben Azzai beginning to enumerate three types of oaths — a discussion involving false oaths in the context of lost objects — but this topic will be taken up in the subsequent session.
