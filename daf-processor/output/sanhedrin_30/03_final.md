# Sanhedrin 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witness Authorization

### Doc Without Auth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אוֹדִיתָא דְּלָא הֲוָה כְּתִב בַּהּ ״אֲמַר לַנָא, כְּתֻבוּ וַחֲתֻמוּ וְהַבוּ לֵיהּ״, אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הַיְינוּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **a certain** document of **admission in which** the following statement **was not written: He,** the one making the admission, **said to us: Write** a deed, **and sign** it, **and give** it **to** the creditor, **Abaye and Rava both say** that **this is** a case in which the principle **of Reish Lakish** is applicable.

The Gemara at the bottom of 29b introduces a case involving a *shtar* of admission — a document recording that a debtor acknowledged his obligation — in which the *shtar* did not contain the explicit language stating that the *eidim* had been authorized to write and sign it: *amar lanan kisvu v'chasmu v'havu lei*, "he said to us, write, sign, and give it to him." The problem is one we have already encountered: witnesses cannot create a *shtar* on their own initiative following an admission. They require explicit authorization from the admitting party. Yet here that authorization language is absent. What is the legal status of such a document?

### Reish Lakish Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חֲזָקָה אֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִין עַל הַשְּׁטָר, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נַעֲשָׂה גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **As Reish Lakish says:** There is **a presumption** that **witnesses do not sign on the document unless** the deal **was transacted with** each party being **an adult,** even if it is not mentioned explicitly in the document that the witnesses verified this. This reflects the principle that a document is not written by a scribe and signed by witnesses unless they know that the action to which it attests was performed appropriately. In light of this, the fact that a certain detail is missing from the wording of the document does not prove that the detail did not take place, as the action was presumably performed properly. Consequently, a document of admission lacking the sentence: He said to us: Write a deed, and sign it, and give it to the creditor, is valid.

Abaye and Rava both rule that we apply the principle of Reish Lakish: *chazaka ein eidim chotmim al ha-shtar ela im kein na'aseh gadol* — there is a presumption that witnesses do not sign a *shtar* unless the transaction was performed properly. The original context of this rule in Bava Basra concerns whether a seller of ancestral land was old enough to do so validly; we presume the witnesses would not have signed unless the seller had reached majority. Abaye and Rava apply the same reasoning here: we presume the witnesses would not have composed and signed the *shtar* without having received proper authorization, even if the *shtar* itself does not record that authorization explicitly.

### Rav Pappa Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פַּפֵּי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דַּאֲנַן לָא יָדְעִינַן, וְסָפְרֵי דְּבֵי דִינָא יָדְעִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi objects to this, and some say** it is **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** who raises this objection: **Is there anything that we,** the Sages, **do not know, and the scribes of the court** do **know?** Since not all of the Sages are aware that the witnesses of an admission cannot write a deed of admission unless the one making the admission asks them to, the scribes certainly cannot be expected to ensure that this condition is fulfilled.

Rav Pappa — and some attribute the objection also to Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua — challenges this application. The rule of Reish Lakish works when what is presumed is something universally understood. But the entire question of when witnesses must be authorized to write a *shtar* following an admission was itself the subject of extended Talmudic deliberation. If the matter was not obvious even to the scholars who debated it at length, can we assume that court scribes — *safrei d'bei dina* — automatically know this *halakha*? The scribe is, after all, the natural gatekeeper who would flag an improperly convened writing. If we cannot rely on him to know the requirement, then the absence of authorization language cannot be cured by presumption, and the *shtar* would be invalid.

### Scribes Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁאֵילִינְהוּ לְסָפְרֵי דְאַבָּיֵי, וְיָדְעִי. לְסָפְרֵי דְרָבָא, וְיָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye's scribes were asked** whether they were aware of this *halakha*, **and** they answered that **they were aware** of it. **Rava's scribes** were also asked, **and they were** also **aware** of it. Apparently, since writing documents is their profession, scribes are aware of the relevant *halakhot*.

The Gemara reports an empirical resolution: they actually went and tested the scribes of Abaye — and those scribes knew the *halakha*. They then tested the scribes of Rava — and those scribes knew as well. It is, of course, worth noting that it is Abaye and Rava themselves who invoke Reish Lakish's principle, and it is their own scribes who pass the test. There is something inherently circular about this; one might fairly observe that not every *beis din*'s scribes would necessarily be as well-trained. Nevertheless, the Gemara's conclusion stands: although explicit authorization language is preferred, its absence does not invalidate the *shtar*, since we may presume the scribes ensured the proper procedure was followed.

## Two Sig Confusion

### Mixed Signals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל לִישָּׁנֵי דְּבֵי דִינָא, וְלָא הֲוָה כְּתִב בַּהּ ״בְּמוֹתַב תְּלָתָא הֲוֵינָא וְחַד לֵיתוֹהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **and all of the formulations** of an enactment **of the court** were written in it. But only two were signed on it, **and** the following statement **was not written in it: We were** convened **in a session of three** judges, **and one** of the judges **is no** longer here, as he died or left for another reason. There was therefore room for concern that perhaps there were only two witnesses, and they wrote the document of admission improperly.

The Gemara now introduces a more complex case of an ambiguous *shtar*. This document was written using all the linguistic hallmarks of a *beis din* document — *duchran pisgamei*, a formal record of proceedings, and language structured in the manner of *lishnei d'bei dina*, the formulaic style courts use — as opposed to the style of *anan sahadi*, "we testify," which is characteristic of individual witnesses. The significance of this distinction is that an admission made before a *beis din* of three requires no authorization for a *shtar* to be written; authorization is only needed when witnesses are acting independently.

The problem is that despite all these signals pointing toward a *beis din* document, only two signatures appear at the bottom. A *beis din* requires three judges. One could imagine a scenario in which a third member died or was otherwise unavailable after the proceedings, in which case the proper practice would be to include a notation: *b'mosav tlata havina v'chad leiseih* — "we were convened as three, and one is no longer present." But no such notation appears. The document thus contains contradictory signals: the language of a *beis din* on one hand, and only two signatures without explanation on the other.

### Ravina View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר רָבִינָא לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina thought to say** that **this is** a case in which the principle **of Reish Lakish,** that witnesses do not sign a document unless the action was performed appropriately, applies.

Ravina proposes to resolve the ambiguity in favor of validity. He would apply Reish Lakish's presumption here as well, reasoning that we assume the *shtar* was done properly — that there was indeed a full *beis din* of three, and that the third member's absence from the signatures was simply an oversight, perhaps because he had passed away.

### Fear Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נָתָן בַּר אַמֵּי: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, כֹּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא חָיְישִׁינַן לְבֵית דִּין טוֹעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Natan bar Ami said to him: This** is what **we say in the name of Rava:** In **any cases like this, we are concerned for** the possibility of **an erroneous court** that thinks that two constitute a court.

Rav Natan bar Ami objects, citing a ruling in the name of Rava: *kol ki hai gavna chaishinan l'Beis Din to'eh* — in a case like this, we are concerned that this was a *beis din* in error, meaning that it may have operated with only two members who mistakenly considered themselves a proper *beis din*. The distinction from Reish Lakish's standard application is critical. Reish Lakish's presumption operates when everything in the *shtar* looks correct and there is no internal evidence of a problem. Here, however, the document itself provides affirmative evidence of a deficiency: there are only two signatures. This is not merely an absence of confirming language — it is a positive indication that something may be wrong. Accordingly, we cannot simply presume regularity.

### Explicit Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אִי כְּתִב בַּהּ ״בֵּי דִינָא״, תּוּ לָא צְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: If it was written in** the document: We, the members of **the court,** convened, it is **unnecessary** for the deed to **further** state that one of the judges is no longer there, as a standard court consists of three judges.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak refines the analysis. If the *shtar* explicitly contains the language *bei dina* — "the court" — indicating that the signatories identify themselves as acting in the capacity of a *beis din*, then even with only two signatures, Reish Lakish's presumption may be applied. The reasoning is that anyone who writes *bei dina* understands that a *beis din* requires three members, and therefore some explanation must account for why only two signed. We may presume the third member was present but did not sign for some reason.

### Two Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא בֵּית דִּין חָצוּף הוּא? דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁדָּנוּ, דִּינֵיהֶן דִּין, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ בֵּית דִּין חָצוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it was an impudent court, as Shmuel says:** With regard to **two** judges **who** convened a tribunal and **judged, their verdict is** a binding **verdict; but** because they contravened the rabbinic ordinance mandating that a court must be composed of three judges, **they are called an impudent court.**

But the Gemara raises a further complication. Perhaps it was indeed only two judges who referred to themselves as a *beis din*, relying on the opinion of Shmuel: *shnayim she'danu dineihem din* — if two people adjudicate, their ruling is valid, though they are called a *beis din chatzuf*, an impudent court. If we rule like Shmuel, might these two have legitimately considered themselves a *beis din* entitled to use that language?

### Two Not Auth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִב בַּיהּ: ״בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַבְנָא אָשֵׁי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It was a document **in which it was written:** We, the members of **the court of Rabbana Ashi,** convened. Rav Ashi's court presumably conformed to rabbinic protocol.

The problem with this reasoning is that even granting Shmuel's position, two judges would not suffice for the specific purpose of authorizing a *shtar* to be written following an admission. As we have established, that requires a *beis din* of three. So even if two can validly adjudicate in general, they cannot serve as the forum before which an admission is made in a manner that permits a *shtar* to be written without separate authorization. The document's validity therefore remains in question.

### Rav Ashi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא רַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב אָשֵׁי כִּשְׁמוּאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ? דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ: ״וַאֲמַרְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבַּנָא אָשֵׁי״, ״וַאֲמַר לַן רַבַּנָא אָשֵׁי.״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps the Sages of the court of Rav Ashi hold like Shmuel,** that the verdict of two judges is binding, and they convened an impudent court. The Gemara answers: It is a document **in which it is written: And we said to Rabbana Ashi, and Rabbana Ashi said to us.** Rav Ashi himself certainly would not have participated in the discussions of an impudent court.

The Gemara resolves the matter by reference to a specific case: the *shtar* contains the words *bei dina d'Rav Ashi* — the court of Rav Ashi — or alternatively, *v'amar lan Rav Ashi*, that Rav Ashi himself instructed the court members to serve and to write the *shtar*. Under these circumstances, no one would assume that the court of Rav Ashi would conduct itself in a manner that merited the characterization of *chatzuf*. We therefore presume that the *beis din* was composed of a full three members, and that the third simply did not sign — an oversight — while the substance of the proceeding was entirely proper.

## Binding Admit

### Hidden Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, אָמַר לָהֶן אֶחָד: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי אֲבִיכֶם שֶׁהִטְמִין מָעוֹת בְּשִׁידָּה, תֵּיבָה, וּמִגְדָּל, וְאָמַר ״שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי הֵן״, ״שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי הֵן״ – בַּבַּיִת לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, בַּשָּׂדֶה דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of when an admission is deemed credible. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **one said to** the children of another: **I saw that your father hid money in a chest, box, or cabinet, saying:** This money **belongs to so-and-so,** or: This money **is second tithe,** and the money was found where he said, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. If the chest, box, or cabinet was **in the house,** the witness has **said nothing.** His testimony about the status of the money is not accepted, as he is only one witness, and he could not have taken the money for himself had he wanted to. But if it was **in the field, his statement stands,** i.e., is accepted.

A *baraita* introduces a set of cases concerning the evidentiary weight of a statement made not in the context of a formal legal proceeding, but rather a private disclosure about the nature of hidden assets. The case: one person says to heirs, "I saw your father hide money in a chest, box, or cabinet, and he said that this money belongs to so-and-so" — or that it is *ma'aser sheni* funds. The question is not whether the heirs are being sued; no one is demanding they hand the money over. Rather, the heirs want to act correctly and need to know whether they are obligated to treat that money as belonging to another or as consecrated funds. Should they believe this single witness?

### Benefit Desire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: כֹּל שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ לִיטְּלָן – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין; אֵין בְּיָדוֹ לִיטְּלָן – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **The principle of the matter** is as follows: In **any** case **where it is in** the **power of** the witness **to take** the money, **his statement stands;** if **it is not in his power to take** the money, **he has said nothing.**

The resolution turns on two factors: whether the person disclosing has the practical ability to benefit from what he is describing, and whether his disclosure reflects a genuine intent to inform. These two considerations together determine whether the statement carries evidentiary weight sufficient to obligate the heirs to act upon it, even though the speaker is only a single witness and the declaration was not made before a *beis din* or in the formal context of an admission to an interested party.

### House vs Field

The *baraita* distinguishes between two scenarios based on where the money is hidden. If the witness says the money is concealed in the house — *be-bayit* — his words carry no weight: *lo amar klum*. The circumstances leave open too many possibilities; there is no corroborating factor that lends credibility to his claim. However, if the witness says the money is hidden in the field — *be-sadeh* — under a specific rock, for instance, then his words do carry weight: *devarav kayamin*.

### Migo Principle

The reason for the distinction lies in the principle of *migo*: if I could have gone to the field when no one was watching and taken the money for myself, the fact that I am instead disclosing its whereabouts and its true ownership is strong evidence that I am telling the truth. Why would I fabricate a story that actually works against my own material interest? The general principle is stated explicitly: *kol she-beyado litol devarav kayamin; ein beyado litol lo amar klum* — wherever the claimant has the practical power to take the asset himself, his words stand; where he lacks that power, his statement carries no legal weight.

### Children Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁרָאוּ אֶת אֲבִיהֶן שֶׁהִטְמִין מָעוֹת בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל, וְאָמַר: ״שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי הֵן״, ״שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי הֵן״ – אִם כְּמוֹסֵר, דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין; אִם כְּמַעֲרִים, לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** In a case **where** the children themselves **saw that their father hid money in a chest, box, or cabinet, and** the father **said:** This money **belongs to so-and-so,** or: This money **is second tithe, if** he said so **as one who relays** information to his own children, **his statement stands.** But **if** he said so **as one who employs artifice,** i.e., he appears to have told them that the money was not his only so that they would not take it, **he has said nothing,** and they may spend the money.

A further case: the heirs themselves saw their father hide the money, and the father declared in their presence that it belonged to someone else or was *ma'aser sheni*. One might think this is even more clearly binding — the children are direct witnesses to both the hiding and the declaration. Yet the *baraita* does not treat this as automatically conclusive, because the father's declaration was not made as an admission to the interested party, and questions arise as to whether the statement should be taken at face value.

### Sincere vs False

The *baraita* resolves this through an inquiry into the father's intent. If he spoke *ki-mesaper* — in the manner of someone genuinely informing the listeners because he wants the matter handled correctly — then *devarav kayamin*, his words are binding. The heirs must treat the money accordingly. However, if it is evident that he was being *ma'arim* — scheming or maneuvering — for instance, by fabricating a story to discourage his children from spending the money on themselves, then *lo amar klum*, his declaration carries no legal force. The threshold question is whether the declaration was a sincere disclosure or a strategic deception, and the answer depends on the surrounding circumstances.

### Dreams No Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה מִצְטַעֵר עַל מָעוֹת שֶׁהִנִּיחַ לוֹ אָבִיו, וּבָא בַּעַל הַחֲלוֹם וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״כָּךְ וְכָךְ הֵן״, ״בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הֵן״, ״שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי הֵן״. זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְאָמְרוּ: דִּבְרֵי חֲלוֹמוֹת לֹא מַעֲלִין וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** In a case **where** one **was distressed about money that his father left him** as an inheritance, because he could not find it, **and the master of the dream,** i.e., someone in his dream, **came and said to him: It is such and such** an amount of money and **it is in such and such a place,** but the money **is second tithe,** and he found this amount in the place of which he dreamed; and **this was** an actual **incident** that was brought before the Sages, **and they said** that he can spend the money, as **matters** appearing in **dreams do not make a difference** in determining the practical *halakha*.

A final variation: the heir was distressed because he knew his father had accumulated substantial sums but could not locate where the money was hidden. A figure appeared to him in a dream and told him precisely: this amount, in this location, and — the money is *ma'aser sheni*. The heir arose the next morning, looked in the specified place, and found exactly the sum that had been described. Everything about the dream's content was verified — except for the *ma'aser sheni* status. Must he treat the money as *ma'aser sheni*?

The Gemara reports: *ze haya ma'aseh* — this actually occurred — *v'amru divrei chalomot lo ma'alin ve-lo moridin*. Dreams have no legal standing. They neither establish nor undermine a halakhic claim. The fact that the amount and location turned out to be accurate is remarkable but does not transform the dream into a valid evidentiary source. Perhaps it was coincidence; perhaps it was the dreamer's own subconscious knowledge emerging — though one might wonder why the subconscious would also have concluded that the money was *ma'aser sheni*. Nonetheless, the *halakha* is clear: one may not selectively credit a dream for purposes of legal obligation.

## Court Dissent

### Write Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים זַכַּאי כּוּ׳. מִיכְתָּב הֵיכִי כָּתְבִי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **two** judges **say** the defendant is **exempt** and one says he is liable, he is exempt. The Gemara asks: When there is a dispute between the judges, **how do they write** the verdict?

Having concluded the discussion of what constitutes a binding admission, the Gemara turns to the process of rendering and recording a court judgment. The *mishnah* has established that when two judges vote for acquittal and one votes for conviction, the ruling is acquittal — and that the dissenting judge may not go to the losing party afterward and express private sympathy with his position, since doing so constitutes *holech rachil*, talebearing. This raises a procedural question: when the court's ruling is formally written out and issued as a judgment document, what exactly do they write in the case of a two-to-one decision?

### Yochanan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״זַכַּאי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** They write that he is **exempt,** without mentioning the dispute.

Rabbi Yochanan rules that the document is written in a unified voice: simply, that the court has found the party innocent — *zakai*. The entire court speaks as one body, and no trace of the internal division appears in the written record.

### Lakish View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי מְזַכִּין, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי מְחַיְּיבִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says** that they specify: **So-and-so and so-and-so deem** him **exempt, and so-and-so and so-and-so deem** him **liable;** they must mention that there was a dispute.

Reish Lakish disagrees. He holds that the minority opinion must be recorded explicitly: *ploni u-ploni mezakin u-ploni mechayev* — judge A and judge B ruled for acquittal, and judge C ruled for conviction. The actual positions of the individual judges are recorded in the written judgment.

### No Talebear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי אִיהוּ מְנָתָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, דִּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״זַכַּאי״ – מְשַׁלֵּם, וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי מְזַכִּין וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי מְחַיְּיבִין״ – לָא מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** these opinions, besides the wording of the verdict? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is **with regard to** whether or not, in a case where it is discovered that the verdict was erroneous, the judge who was in the minority must **pay** his **portion** of restitution **along with** the judges of the majority. **As according to the one who says** that they write that he is **exempt,** the minority judge **pays** as well, **and according to the one who says** that they specify: **So-and-so and so-and-so deem** him **exempt, and so-and-so and so-and-so deem** him **liable, he does not pay.**

The Gemara raises the obvious tension: how does Reish Lakish's position square with the *mishnah*'s prohibition of *lo telech rachil be-amecha* — the verse forbidding talebearing, which the *mishnah* invokes to prohibit a dissenting judge from privately expressing his disagreement to the losing party? If a dissenting judge may not reveal his minority position privately, how can it be written in the official judgment?

The Gemara does not make the reconciliation entirely explicit, but the natural distinction is between two fundamentally different modes of disclosure. What the *mishnah* prohibits is a judge going privately to one of the litigants and expressing personal allegiance to that party — sowing dissent, undermining the authority of the majority ruling, and signaling that the dissenter sided with one party against the other. This is what constitutes *rachilut*. By contrast, what Reish Lakish permits — indeed requires — is an official, transparent record in which the full court speaks with institutional authority, recording how each member voted as a matter of fact. The court document does not express sympathy or pick sides; it records the deliberation of the tribunal as a whole.

The underlying values driving each position can also be identified. Rabbi Yochanan insists on a unified voice in order to prevent any form of dissent-sowing — *mishum lo telech rachil*. Reish Lakish insists on accuracy because to write that all three judges ruled for acquittal when one did not would be *meiחzi ke-shikra* — it would have the appearance of falsehood. Beyond the prohibition of falsehood, there is also a positive reason to preserve the dissenting voice: minority opinions are recorded throughout the *Mishnah* so that future courts and future *halakhic* deliberations can draw upon them. The same logic may apply here, though the Gemara in this context focuses primarily on the *meiחzi ke-shikra* concern.

### Liability Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״זַכַּאי״, מְשַׁלְּמִי? וְלֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו אַתְּ בַּהֲדַן, לָא הֲוָה סָלֵיק דִּינָא מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who says** that they write that he is **exempt,** why **do they pay** his portion? **Let them say to him: If you had not been with us the judgment would have had no verdict at all,** as two judges cannot issue a verdict. Therefore, you share the responsibility with us and should participate in the payment.

The Gemara explores what practical difference might follow from the dispute regarding how to write the judgment. One possibility: if the court is subsequently found to have ruled incorrectly — which in certain categories of law triggers an obligation on the *beis din*'s part to compensate the injured party — does the dissenting judge who had voted correctly share in the financial liability?

The initial proposal is that according to Rabbi Yochanan, who requires the minority opinion to be subsumed into a unified ruling, the dissenting judge would be obligated to pay his share, since the written judgment reflects all three judges as having ruled together. But this seems deeply unjust: the dissenting judge can turn to his colleagues and say, *ilu didi tzaitan, atun nami lo shalumtun* — had you listened to me, you would not be paying either. He was right all along and only the coercive logic of majority rule required him to have his voice formally absorbed into the ruling.

Conversely, under Rabbi Yochanan's framework, the two majority judges might argue that they should not be required to pay the full amount of compensation alone, since the judgment was rendered by a court of three, not two — the dissenter's participation in the court is what gave the ruling its formal validity. Without his presence the court could not have reached a *psak din* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תֵלֵךְ רָכִיל בְּעַמֶּיךָ״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״זַכַּאי״ – מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תֵלֵךְ רָכִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the difference **between** the opinions is only with regard to the wording of the verdict, and is **due to** the prohibition of: **"You shall not go as a talebearer among your people"** (Leviticus 19:16). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that they write that he is **exempt due to** the prohibition of gossip, as derived from the verse: **"You shall not go as a talebearer."**

The Gemara steps back from this entire line of reasoning. Given the back-and-forth, the only real and stable *beinaihu* — practical difference — between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is the concern about *lo telech rachil be-amecha* itself: how concerned is each authority that recording the minority opinion, even in an official document, risks crossing into the territory of talebearing and undermining the integrity of the judicial process?

### Elazar Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמַר: ״מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם נִזְדַּכָּה פְּלוֹנִי״. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּמָר, וְאִית לֵיהּ דְּמָר. הִלְכָּךְ, כָּתְבִי הָכִי: ״מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם נִזְדַּכָּה פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says** that they do not specify the names of the judges, but rather they add the phrase: **From the statement of** the judges **so-and-so was deemed exempt,** to the wording of the verdict. This indicates that not all the judges agreed that he is exempt, but does not specify which judges came to which conclusion. **And Rabbi Elazar accepts** the opinion **of** this **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **and accepts** the opinion **of** that **Sage,** Reish Lakish. **Therefore, this** is what **they write: From the statement of** the judges, **so-and-so was deemed exempt.** This wording indicates that the ruling was not based on a consensus among the judges, so that it will not have the appearance of falsehood, but it also does not specify what each judge said, to avoid gossip.

Rabbi Elazar offers a compromise position that is sensitive to both concerns simultaneously. He rules that one writes: *mi-divreichem nizdaka ploni* — "from our deliberations, so-and-so has been found innocent." This formulation is notable for what it implies without stating explicitly. The phrase "from our deliberations" signals to any careful reader that there was a deliberative process — that the ruling was not self-evident, that some debate occurred — without identifying who voted which way or recording anyone's individual position. It preserves the unified voice of the court while hinting at the existence of internal disagreement.

Rabbi Elazar is thus concerned about both *lo telech rachil* and *meiחzi ke-shikra*: he will not simply write that all three unanimously ruled innocent when they did not, but he will not expose the particulars of individual votes either. Whether this language would be used even in cases of unanimous decisions — in which case it signals nothing — or only when there was a dissent — in which case it functions as a coded signal — is left open by the Gemara.

## Court Process

### Bring Parties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמְרוּ אֶת הַדָּבָר, הָיוּ מַכְנִיסִין כּוּ׳. לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לְבַעֲלֵי דִינִין – הָתָם קָיְימִי! אֶלָּא לְעֵדִים. כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that after the judges **finished the matter** and reached a decision, **they would bring** them **in.** The Gemara asks: **Whom** would they bring in? **If we say** they would bring in **the litigants,** this cannot be, as **they were there** the whole time; they never left the room. **Rather,** they would bring in **the witnesses. In accordance with whose** opinion is the mishna? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan.**

Having concluded the discussion of what constitutes a binding admission, the Gemara turns to the process of rendering and recording a court judgment. The *mishnah* has established that when two judges vote for acquittal and one votes for conviction, the ruling is acquittal — and that the dissenting judge may not go to the losing party afterward and express private sympathy with his position, since doing so constitutes *holech rachil*, talebearing. This raises a procedural question: when the court's ruling is formally written out and issued as a judgment document, what exactly do they write in the case of a two-to-one decision?

### Jerusalem Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם לְבַעֲלֵי דִינִין. וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר, כָּךְ הָיָה מִנְהָגָן שֶׁל נְקִיֵּי הַדַּעַת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: מַכְנִיסִין לְבַעֲלֵי דִינִין וְשׁוֹמְעִין דִּבְרֵיהֶן, וּמַכְנִיסִין אֶת הָעֵדִים וְשׁוֹמְעִין דִּבְרֵיהֶם, וּמוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן לַחוּץ וְנוֹשְׂאִין וְנוֹתְנִין בְּדָבָר. גָּמְרוּ אֶת הַדָּבָר, מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתָן כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reverses its interpretation of the mishna: **No, actually** it can be explained that the judges would bring in **the litigants; and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Neḥemya says: This was the custom of the scrupulous people of Jerusalem:** When they would judge, **they** would **bring in the litigants and hear their statements, and** then **they** would **bring in the witnesses and hear their statements** in the presence of the litigants, **and** then **they** would **take them** all **outside** of the courtroom **and discuss the matter** in their absence. Once **they finished the matter they** would **bring them,** i.e., the litigants, **in,** to hear their verdict.

The refined practice of Jerusalem is described by Rabbi Nechemia in a *baraita*: *kach hayu minhagam shel nekiyei ha-da'as she-bi-Yerushalayim* — this is how the people of clarity conducted themselves in Jerusalem. The proper sequence was as follows: first, the litigants are brought in and the court hears their respective claims; then the witnesses are brought in and their testimony is heard; then everyone — litigants and witnesses — is sent outside; the judges then deliberate; and when deliberations conclude, all parties are brought back in to receive the *psak*. This is presented as the most orderly and careful way to conduct the proceedings, ensuring that witnesses and litigants are not present during the judges' private deliberation and that all parties receive the ruling together in a formal setting.

## Witness Combine

### See Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: לְעוֹלָם אֵין עֵדוּתָן מִצְטָרֶפֶת עַד שֶׁיִּרְאוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּאֶחָד. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קָרְחָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself: The testimonies of** individual witnesses **are never combined** into a testimony of two witnesses **unless the two of them saw** the incident transpire together **as one. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** Their testimonies are combined **even in** a case where they saw the incident **one after the other.**

The Gemara now turns to a fundamental question in the law of testimony: when two witnesses observed related events — for instance, each witnessing the same defendant borrow money, but on different occasions — can their testimonies be combined to constitute valid proof? The *tanna kamma* holds that testimonies cannot combine unless both witnesses observed the same event together: *ein eidut mitztarefet ad she-yiru sheneihem ke-echad*.

### Sequential OK

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha disagrees: *afilu b'zeh achar zeh* — testimonies combine even when the witnesses observed events sequentially, not simultaneously. His ruling extends to both the viewing of the events and the presentation of testimony in court.

### Same Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to the interpretation of **a verse, and if you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to **logical reasoning.**

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the dispute? The disagreement may be grounded in either a textual argument from a *pasuk* or in a pure *svara*, a logical argument. It is worth pausing here to appreciate the Gemara's equanimity in presenting these alternatives. The formulation *i ba'it eima kra, i ba'it eima sevara* — "if you wish, cite the verse; if you wish, cite the logic" — and the fact that the *svara* is presented first, reflects something of the radical nature of *Torah she-ba-al peh*: a purely logical argument is treated as equally authoritative as a scriptural derivation. The Gemara elsewhere goes even further, asking *lama li kra, sevara hu* — why do I need a verse at all when logic alone suffices?

### Ed as One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶחָד״? זֶה בָּנָה אָב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵד״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "One witness shall not rise up against a man for any iniquity, or for any sin, in any sin that he sins; at the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15); **by inference, from that** which **is stated** in the verse: **A witness shall not rise up** against a man, even without the word "one," **do I not know that it is** referring to **one** witness? After all, the verse is written in the singular. Therefore, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states** explicitly: **"One** witness"? **This established a paradigm,** a basis for the principle that in **every place** in the Torah **where** the word **"witness" is stated,** it means that **there are two** witnesses, **unless the verse specifies for you** that it is referring to only **one** witness.

If one prefers the textual derivation: the verse in Vayikra states *ve-hu ed o ra'ah o yada* — "and he is a witness, whether he saw or he knew." A related verse states *lo yakum ed echad* — "a single witness shall not stand up." From the fact that the Torah uses *ed echad*, specifying "one witness," the Gemara derives that the word *ed* by itself — without the word *echad* — would already connote two. This is established through the principle that wherever the Torah says *ed*, read it as referring to two, unless the text specifies *echad* and means a single individual. The implication of using a singular noun — *ed* — to refer to the minimum quorum of two witnesses is that the two must function as one: they must have witnessed the same event at the same time, such that they are, in testimony terms, a single unified entity. This is the textual basis for the *tanna kamma*'s view.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן חַד, לְמֵימַר: עַד דְּחָזוּ תַּרְוַויְיהוּ כְּחַד. וְאִידַּךְ, ״וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדָע״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to the first *tanna*, **the Merciful One expresses it in the singular form,** i.e., "witness" and not "witnesses," **to say** that they are not combined into a testimony of two witnesses **unless the two of them saw** the incident transpire together **as one. And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, derives from the phrase: **"And he is a witness, whether he has seen or known,"** that **in any case** where one testifies about what he sees and knows, his testimony is valid.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha reads the same phrase — *o ra'ah o yada*, "whether he saw or he knew" — as indicating that the Torah is expansive about the mode of knowledge: *however* a witness came to know, it qualifies. The disjunctive *o* — "or" — signals that the specifics of how or when the knowledge was acquired are not determinative.

### Testify Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עֵדוּתָן מִתְקַיֶּימֶת בְּבֵית דִּין עַד שֶׁיָּעִידוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן כְּאֶחָד. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹמְעִין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל זֶה הַיּוֹם, וּכְשֶׁיָּבֹא חֲבֵירוֹ לְמָחָר שׁוֹמְעִין דְּבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** furthermore, **their testimony does not stand in court unless the two of them testify** together **as one. Rabbi Natan says:** They need not testify together. Rather, their testimonies are combined even if the judges **hear the statement of this** witness **today, and when the other** witness **comes tomorrow** the judges **hear his statement.** The mishna, by contrast, indicates that the verdict must be given with the two witnesses present together.

The *mishnah* contains a second clause: *ein eidutan mitkayemet be-veit din ad she-ya'idu sheneihem ke-echad* — testimony is not established in court unless both witnesses testify simultaneously. Rabbi Natan dissents: *shome'in devarav shel zeh hayom u-che-she-yavo chaveiro le-machar shome'in devarav* — one may hear the testimony of one witness today, and when the second witness arrives tomorrow, one hears his testimony separately. The question of whether witnesses must testify together in court is distinct from whether they must have observed the events together.

### Context Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: מָר סָבַר, עֵד אֶחָד כִּי אָתֵי – לִשְׁבוּעָה אָתֵי, לְמָמוֹנָא לָא אָתֵי. וְאִידַּךְ: אַטּוּ כִּי אָתוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, בְּחַד פּוּמָּא קָא מַסְהֲדִי? אֶלָּא מְצָרְפִינַן לְהוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי לִיצָרְפִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to **logical reasoning,** as one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **when one witness comes** to testify, **he comes to** render the defendant liable to take **an oath.** This is the *halakha* when there is one witness against the defendant in a case of monetary law. **He does not come to** render the defendant liable to pay **money,** because for this two witnesses are necessary. **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Natan, responds: **Is that to say** that **when they come together,** they render the defendant financially liable because **they testify with one mouth?** Obviously they testify one after the other. **Rather,** clearly it is the judges who **combine** their two testimonies into one. **Here too,** when the witnesses come to court at different times, **let** the judges **combine** their testimonies.

The logical debate underlying this question can be framed as follows. The *tanna kamma* reasons: if only one witness appears before the court on a given day, at that moment his testimony functions as that of a single witness — which under biblical law creates not a monetary obligation but an obligation on the defendant to take an oath. If his role is thus defined at the moment of his testimony as that of an *eid* for a *shvu'ah*, one cannot subsequently redefine it as an *eid* for monetary purposes when the second witness arrives.

Rabbi Natan's counter-argument is incisive: even when two witnesses appear together on the same day, they do not literally testify with one mouth simultaneously. The first speaks and then the second speaks. By the same logic one might say that when the first speaks, he is a lone *eid* whose testimony obligates an oath, not money — yet we do not say this. Rather, we say that what will come afterward provides the context for what was said first: the subsequent testimony of the second witness retroactively frames the first witness's testimony as part of a valid two-witness evidentiary unit. If we accept that principle when both come on the same day, the same logic should apply when they come on different days.

### Smicha Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְיָקִים הֲוָה מִשְׁתְּקִיד עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא לְמִסְמְכֵיהּ, וְלָא קָא מִיסְתַּיַּיע מִילְּתָא. יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי אִיכָּא דְּיָדַע הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְיָקִים: דֵּין יָדַע. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵימָא אֵיזוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִיסְמְכֵיהּ מָר בְּרֵישָׁא. סַמְכֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim was striving to ordain Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, and was not successful** in his attempts. **One day,** Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim **was sitting before Rabbi Yoḥanan** among Rabbi Yoḥanan's other students. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** his students: **Is there** anyone **who knows** whether the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, or not? Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim said to him: This** one, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, **knows.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: If so, let him say.** Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim **said to him: Let the Master ordain him first;** since we are all in need of his wisdom, he is fit for ordination. Rabbi Yoḥanan then **ordained** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina.

The Gemara relates a story that, beyond its narrative interest, bears on which ruling we follow. Rav Shimon ben Elyakim had long been trying to secure *smicha* — ordination — for Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina, but without success. One day, Rabbi Yochanan posed a question to those assembled: does anyone know whether the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha or not? Rav Shimon ben Elyakim indicated that Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina possessed that knowledge. Rabbi Yochanan asked that he be told. Rav Shimon ben Elyakim replied: if you want him to participate in the deliberations, grant him *smicha* first — and then he will tell you. Rabbi Yochanan granted Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina *smicha*, and then asked him how he had heard the ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנִי, אֱמוֹר לִי כֵּיצַד שָׁמַעְתָּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי, שֶׁמּוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה לְרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: My son, tell me what you heard.** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, **said to him: This is what I heard: That Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa concedes to** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** that it is unnecessary for the witnesses to testify together.

Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina responded: *kach shamati she-modin Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha le-Rabbi Natan* — I heard that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha concedes to Rabbi Natan. That is, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who permits witnesses to observe events separately, would certainly agree that witnesses may also testify separately — since if sequential observation is valid, sequential testimony is at least as permissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: לָזֶה הוּצְרַכְתִּי?! הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה עִיקַּר רְאִיָּה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה ״לָא בָּעֵינַן״, הַגָּדָה מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan was disappointed, and **said: For this I needed** to ordain him? **Now** that with regard to the **main** element of testimony, i.e., **observing** the incident, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says we do not need** both witnesses to see it **together,** with regard to their **statement** in court, **is** it **necessary** to explicate that there is no requirement that they testify together?

Rabbi Yochanan's response was sharp: *lezeh hutzrachti?* — for this I went to the trouble? The inference that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha would agree with Rabbi Natan is obvious: *uma ikar re'iyah be-hada de-amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha lo bainan, hagada mibaiya?* — if even the primary act of seeing does not need to be simultaneous according to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, is it even a question that the testifying does not need to be simultaneous? Rabbi Yochanan expected something that actually settled the disputed question of the *halakha*, not a trivially obvious implication.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוֹאִיל וְעָלִיתָ, לֹא תֵּרֵד. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, גַּבְרָא רַבָּה, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיסְתְּמִיךְ – אִיסְתְּמִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Although the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, was unnecessary, Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Since you ascended,** i.e., you were ordained, **you will not descend,** even though it was in error. **Rabbi Zeira said: Conclude from it** that with regard to **a great man, once he is ordained,** even if it was due to mistaken judgment, **he is ordained.** The ordination is not canceled.

Nonetheless, Rabbi Yochanan told him: *ho'il ve-alita lo tered* — since you have already been elevated, you shall not descend. The *smicha* stands. Rabbi Zeira draws a lesson from the episode: *gavra raba, kivan de-isamech samech* — once a great person has granted *smicha*, that ordination is firm and is not revoked, even if the occasion for which it was granted turns out to have yielded less than anticipated.

It is worth noting that the cast of characters in the parallel account in the Yerushalmi differs: there it is Rabbi Yonatan rather than Rabbi Yochanan, and the roles of Rav Shimon ben Elyakim and Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina appear to be reversed. The *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi* versions thus present strikingly different portraits of the same underlying event.

### Practical Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, בֵּין בְּקַרְקָעוֹת בֵּין בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** As for the *halakha* in this matter, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** that it is unnecessary for the two witnesses to observe the incident together, **both with regard to** cases of **land** and **with regard to** cases of **movable property.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Avin cites Rav: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha* — the practical ruling follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha — *bein be-karka bein be-metaltelin*, whether regarding land or movable property. The ruling applies across the board: even when two witnesses testify about events that occurred on different occasions, their testimonies may be combined.

### Ulla Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה בְּקַרְקָעוֹת, אֲבָל לֹא בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa with regard to land,** which is unmovable, and therefore both testimonies are certainly about the same piece of land; **but not with regard to movable property,** as there is a concern that they are not testifying about the same item.

Ulla limits this ruling: we follow Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha in cases involving *karka* — land — but not in cases involving *metaltelin* — chattel. Abaye objects to this formulation: if Ulla says we rule like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha for land, that implies there is a debate regarding land and that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha does not automatically prevail there. But a tradition in the name of Rav — *modim chachamim le-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha be-edut karka* — holds that when it comes to land, the *chachamim* themselves concede to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. The reason is straightforward: when two witnesses both testify that Reuven owns a particular parcel of land, they are by definition testifying about the same object. The testimonies inherently concern a single, identifiable thing. This is categorically different from two witnesses each testifying about a separate monetary transaction. According to this tradition, the question of combining testimonies is not disputed at all with respect to land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲלָכָה, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה בְּעֵדוּת קַרְקַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Ulla: If you say that the ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa with regard to land, **by inference** you hold **that they disagree** with regard to this matter. **But doesn't Rabbi Abba say** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: The Rabbis concede to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa with regard to testimony** concerning **land?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין בִּנְזִיקִין דְּבֵי קַרְנָא: מוֹדִין חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה בְּעֵדוּת בְּכוֹר, וּבְעֵדוּת קַרְקַע, וּבְעֵדוּת חֲזָקָה, וְכֵן שֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Idi bar Avin teaches in** the *halakhot* of **damages** that were taught **at the school of** the Sage **Karna: The Rabbis concede to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** that the witnesses do not need to see the incident together **with regard to testimony** concerning a blemish in a male **firstborn** kosher animal, which renders it permitted to derive benefit from it, **and with regard to testimony** concerning ownership of **land, and with regard to testimony** concerning **presumptive ownership** of land. Since it is clear that they are testifying about the same land, it is unnecessary for them to see it together. **And so** they concede with regard to testimony over the two pubic hairs **of a boy or of a girl,** which are a sign of adulthood.

A *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Idi bar Avin in the *yeshiva* of Karna confirms: *modim chachamim le-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha be-edut bechor, u-ve-edut karka, u-ve-edut chazaka* — the sages concede to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha in cases of testimony about a firstborn (whether of an animal or, according to Tosafot, whether a person is a firstborn son entitled to a double inheritance share), about land, and about *chazaka* — the established presumption of land ownership. In all these cases, the object of testimony is singular and identifiable, and so there is no dispute: the testimonies combine even if the witnesses observed different aspects at different times.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית? מָר סָבַר פְּלִיגִי, וּמָר סָבַר לָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Abaye's question: **Are you setting** the statement of one **man against** the statement of another **man?** One **Sage,** Ulla, **holds** that the Rabbis **disagree** with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa even with regard to land, **and** one **Sage,** i.e., Rav and Rav Idi, **holds** that **they do not disagree.**

The Gemara acknowledges that this *baraita* and Ulla's ruling represent different positions held by different authorities — *gavra a-gavra ka ramis* — and does not attempt to harmonize them further. One authority holds that land is contested and Rav rules for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha; another holds that land was never in dispute.

### Combination Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב: הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הוֹדָאָה, הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה – מִצְטָרְפִי. הַלְוָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה, הַלְוָאָה אַחַר הוֹדָאָה – לָא מִצְטָרְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rav** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rav says:** Testimonies of **an admission following an admission** are combined into one; if one witness testifies that the respondent admitted in his presence that he owes the claimant, and the other witness testifies that the respondent admitted in his presence that he owes the claimant in a separate incident, their testimonies are combined. Likewise, testimonies of **an admission following a loan are combined** into one; where one witness testifies that the respondent admitted in his presence that he owes the claimant, and the other one testifies that on a previous date the respondent borrowed money from the claimant in his presence, their testimonies are combined. Rav continues: But testimonies of **a loan following a loan** are not combined. If one testifies that the claimant lent the respondent one hundred dinars in his presence, and the other testifies that he lent him one hundred dinars in a separate incident in his presence, their testimonies are not combined, as they are clearly testifying about two separate loans. Similarly, testimonies of **a loan following an admission are not combined.** If one testifies that the claimant lent money to the respondent, and the other one testifies that on an earlier date the respondent admitted to owing the claimant, their testimonies are not combined.

The Gemara then presents Rav's own view on when testimonies combine in monetary cases. Rav rules: *hodaah achar hodaah, hodaah achar halvaah mitztarfi* — an admission following an admission, or an admission following a loan, can be combined. If one witness testifies that he heard the defendant admit to owing a sum on one occasion, and another witness testifies that he heard a similar admission on a different occasion, they may be combined, since the defendant may well have been admitting to the same underlying debt on both occasions. Similarly, if one witness testifies to having witnessed the loan itself, and another testifies that the defendant subsequently admitted to that loan, the two testimonies concern the same event and combine.

However, *halvaah achar halvaah lo mitztarfi* — a loan after a loan does not combine. If one witness says he saw money lent on Monday and another says he saw money lent on Wednesday, they are clearly testifying about two separate transactions. Nor does *halvaah achar hodaah* — a loan witnessed after an admission — combine, since in that configuration the sequence indicates that the admission on Monday concerned a prior debt, and the loan on Wednesday was a new and separate transaction.

### Know Same Thing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא הַלְוָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה דְּלָא? דְּמָנֶה דְּקָא חָזֵי הַאי, לָא קָא חָזֵי הַאי. הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הוֹדָאָה נָמֵי – אַמָּנֶה דְּקָא מוֹדֵי קַמֵּי הַאי, לָא מוֹדֵי קַמֵּי הַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak encountered Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua. He said to him: What is different** about **a loan following a loan,** in **which** case the testimonies are **not** combined, **as the one hundred dinars that this** witness **saw, that** witness **did not see?** In the case of **an admission following an admission as well,** perhaps **the one hundred dinars about which** the respondent **admitted in the presence of this** witness, **he did not admit in the presence of that** witness. Perhaps his admissions were in reference to two separate loans, and therefore the testimonies should not be combined.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak pushes back against even the permissive cases. He asks: if the reason a loan-after-a-loan does not combine is that *maneh d'kahazi hai lav kahazi hai* — the sum witnessed by the first witness is not the same sum witnessed by the second — then the same logic applies to an admission after an admission. The sum admitted before the first witness is not the same sum admitted before the second witness. The mere possibility that the defendant was admitting to the same debt on both occasions is insufficient; how do we know he was not admitting to two different debts?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְהַאי בָּתְרָא: בְּהַאי מָנֶה דְּאוֹדַיי לֵיהּ קַמָּךְ, אוֹדַיי לֵיהּ נָמֵי קַמֵּי פְּלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, answered him: The reference is to a case **where** the respondent **said to this last** witness: **With regard to the one hundred dinars that I admitted** to owing **in your presence, I admitted** owing **them in the presence of so-and-so,** the first witness, **as well.**

The Gemara suggests that the combining case involves a specific circumstance: when the defendant himself, in his second admission before the second witness, explicitly identified the prior admission — "this debt that I am admitting to you now, I also admitted before that other witness." In that case, there is no ambiguity about which debt is being discussed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכַּתִּי, בָּתְרָא יָדַע, קַמָּא לָא יָדַע. דַּהֲדַר אָזֵיל, אָמַר לֵיהּ לְקַמָּא: הַאי מָנֶה דְּאוֹדַיי לֵיהּ קַמָּךְ, אוֹדַיי לֵיהּ נָמֵי קַמֵּי פְּלוֹנִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּנוּחַ דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁהִתְנַחְתָּ אֶת דַּעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak asked: **Still, the last** witness **knows** that he is testifying about the same loan as the first witness, but **the first** witness **does not know** this. Since only one witness testifies that it is the same loan, the testimonies still cannot be combined. Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, answered: It is a case **where,** after his admission to the second witness, the respondent **went back** and **said to the first** witness: With regard to **those one hundred dinars that I admitted** to owing **in your presence, I admitted** owing **them in the presence of so-and-so as well.** Therefore, both witnesses know that they are testifying about the same loan. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to him: May your mind be settled, as you have settled my mind** and put it at ease by answering this question that was troubling me.

The Gemara then adds a further requirement: not only must the second witness know that the admission he heard concerned the same debt the first witness heard about, but the first witness must also know this. This would require the defendant to have subsequently informed the first witness: "the debt I admitted to you, I have now also admitted before a second witness." The need for each witness to independently know that both testimonies concern the same obligation is what the Gemara here demands.

### Rava Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי נִיחוּתָא? דְּרָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, שְׁדָא בַּהּ נַרְגָּא – לָאו הַיְינוּ הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, **said to him: What is settling** about this explanation? **As Rava, and some say Rav Sheshet, threw an ax at** my answer, i.e., he rejected my explanation, as follows: According to this interpretation of the case of an admission following an admission, **isn't this** the same as **an admission following a loan?** The case of an admission following an admission is now rendered superfluous, as it adds no new insight on the matter.

Rava — some versions say Rav Sheshes — objects sharply to this structure. If you require this entire machinery of mutual cross-notification even in the case of admission-after-admission, the same requirement would logically have to apply even to the simpler case of *hodaah achar halvaah* — an admission after a loan — which Rav himself treated as unproblematically combinable. Must the witness to the loan go inform the witness to the admission, and vice versa, before their testimonies can be joined? That would make Rav's original distinction between loan-after-loan (which does not combine) and admission-after-admission (which does) practically meaningless.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַיְינוּ דִּשְׁמִיעַ לִי עֲלַיְיכוּ, דְּרָמֵיתוּ דִּיקְלֵי וְזָקְפִיתוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to him: This is what I heard about you** Sages of Meḥoza, **that you knock down palm trees and erect them,** i.e., you build and then destroy what you built. After you came up with such an excellent explanation, you ruined it yourselves.

The respondent acknowledges the force of this critique with an idiom: *d'ramisu diklei ve-zakifisu lehu* — you chopped down trees to use for construction, and now you are taking them and replanting them. The vivid image captures the frustration: an elegant framework was built up and then effectively dismantled by the very person who constructed it. The Gemara recognizes that the attempt to limit Rav's ruling to cases of absolute certainty — where every witness knows precisely what the other witnessed — goes far beyond what Rav himself ever required. Rav's original ruling is more straightforward: testimonies combine when it is possible they concern the same event, and they do not combine when it is clear they concern different events.

### Nehardai All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: בֵּין הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הוֹדָאָה, בֵּין הוֹדָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה, בֵּין הַלְוָאָה אַחַר הַלְוָאָה, בֵּין הַלְוָאָה אַחַר הוֹדָאָה – מִצְטָרְפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Whether** it is a case of **an admission following an admission, or an admission following a loan, or a loan following a loan, or a loan following an admission,** in all these cases the testimonies **are combined.**

The scholars of Nehardei take the broadest possible position: *bein hodaah achar hodaah bein hodaah achar halvaah bein halvaah achar halvaah bein halvaah achar hodaah, mitztarfin* — all combinations, in every sequence, combine. Whether the witnesses observed admissions, loans, or any mixture thereof, and whether they did so simultaneously or at different times, the testimonies are joined. This is a straightforward and unqualified ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, without the additional distinctions introduced by Rav or the further restrictions proposed in the preceding discussion. The essential question is simply whether the combined testimonies establish that the defendant owes the amount claimed — and if they do, they are valid proof.
