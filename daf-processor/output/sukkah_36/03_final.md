# Sukkah 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Niklaf - Peeling

### Total vs. Partial Peeling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִקְלַף. אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי אֶתְרוֹגָא דְּאַגְלֵיד כַּאֲהִינָא סוּמָּקָא — כְּשֵׁרָה. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: נִקְלַף — פָּסוּל! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא בְּכוּלַּהּ, הָא בְּמִקְצָתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** An *etrog* that **was peeled** is unfit. **Rava said: This *etrog* that was peeled like a red date** so that only its thin, outer peel is removed but the rest remains intact, **is fit.** The Gemara objects: **But didn't we learn** explicitly in the mishna: If the *etrog* **was peeled** it is unfit? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; this** mishna, where it states that if the *etrog* was peeled it is unfit, is **in** a case where **all of it** was peeled. **That** statement of Rava that if it was peeled it is fit is **in** a case where only **part of it** was peeled.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 35b, continuing the Gemara's analysis of the mishna's aesthetic requirements for the *etrog*. Having addressed the issues of protrusions and the *pitam* in previous sessions, the Gemara now turns to the question of *niklaf* — peeling.

Rava rules: an *etrog* that has been peeled like a red date is *kosher*. Yet the mishna explicitly states that a peeled *etrog* is *pasul*. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction with the formula *lo kashya*: the two sources address different scenarios. Where the *etrog* is entirely peeled, it is *kosher*; where it is only partially peeled, it is *pasul*. That is the reading of the Gemara as stated.

### Rashi v. Tosafot

Rashi explains the logic perhaps somewhat ironically: when the *etrog* is entirely peeled, it presents a uniform appearance; when it is only partially peeled, it looks blotchy and *menumar* — spotted — which the Gemara elsewhere treated as a disqualifying defect. It is that mottled, uneven appearance that renders it *pasul*.

Tosafot, however, approaches the question differently, anticipating the Gemara's imminent comparison between the defects of the *etrog* and the signs of *treifa* in animals. In the laws of *treifos*, an animal whose skin has been entirely stripped — a condition called *gluda* — is a *treifa*, while an animal that retains some of its skin can potentially regenerate and heal, and is therefore not disqualified. Extrapolating from this parallel, Tosafot argues that the same logic applies here in reverse: if some skin remains on the *etrog*, it is *kosher*; if the *etrog* is entirely peeled, it is *pasul*.

A natural question arises: if any portion of the outer layer is missing, does that not create a problem of *chaser* — the invalidity of a physically incomplete *etrog*? This distinction between *niklaf* and *chaser* is not entirely resolved, and it may be that *niklaf* refers only to the outermost thin layer of the rind rather than to the rind as a whole — something closer to flaking than to peeling the fruit bare.

## Nikav - Holes

### Through-holes & Chaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִסְדַּק, נִיקַּב. תָּנֵי עוּלָּא בַּר חֲנִינָא: נִיקַּב נֶקֶב מְפוּלָּשׁ — בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְפוּלָּשׁ — בִּכְאִיסָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues discussing the *halakha* of an *etrog* that was **split** or **pierced. Ulla bar Ḥanina taught:** An *etrog* that was **pierced with a hole that** completely **goes through** its body is unfit **with any** size hole. If **the hole does not** completely **go through** the *etrog*, it is unfit only **with** a hole **the size of an *issar*** coin.

A hole that causes missing flesh is a problem; a hole without *chaser* is not. On this latter category, Ulla bar Chanina offers a more precise formulation: a hole that passes completely through the *etrog* — *nekev mefulash* — is *pasul* even if it is the smallest possible size, even if nothing physically fell away from the fruit. If the hole does not go through and through, then it must be at least the size of an *issar*, a coin of modest but not negligible size, to be disqualifying.

### Limiting the Mishna

Both Rashi and Tosafot understand Ulla bar Chanina's ruling as a qualification of the mishna's statement that a hole without any missing flesh is valid. They read him as saying: not all such holes are valid. If the hole passes from one side of the *etrog* to the other — even without any physical material falling away, though one imagines that some juice would escape — that degree of penetration is sufficient to render it *pasul*. Similarly, even a hole that does not go all the way through is *pasul* if it is as wide as an *issar*, even without any portion of the *etrog* being physically absent.

## Treifa Signs & Etrog

### Three Treifa Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: נוֹלְדוּ בָּאֶתְרוֹג סִימָנֵי טְרֵפָה, מַהוּ? מַאי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אִי נִקְלַף — תְּנֵינָא! אִי נִסְדַּק — תְּנֵינָא! אִי נִיקַּב — תְּנֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma:** If **signs of a *tereifa* developed in the *etrog*, what is** its halakhic status? The Gemara clarifies: **What is the dilemma** that **he is raising?** There are similarities between the *halakhot* of the *etrog* in the mishna and some of the *halakhot* of a *tereifa*, a bird or animal with a condition that will lead to its death within a year. **If** it is the case where the *etrog* was **peeled, we** already **learned** that case. **If** it is the case where the *etrog* was **split, we learned** that case as well. And **if** it is the case where the *etrog* was **pierced, we learned** that too. After ruling out those defects, the question remains: With regard to what is Rava's dilemma?

The Gemara records Rava's query: *nolda be-etrog simanei treifa mahu* — what is the law if an *etrog* displays the signs that would render an animal a *treifa*? The Gemara immediately asks what the question is, since the mishna already covers each of the relevant defects. If the skin is peeled (*niklaf*), that corresponds to the *treifa* category of *gluda*, and the mishna addresses it. If it is cracked (*nistak*), that corresponds to a cracked trachea in *hilkhot treifos*, and the mishna addresses that too. And if it has a hole (*nikav*), the vast majority of *treifa* cases involve holes — in the lung, the heart, the intestines — and again the mishna deals with this. So what exactly is Rava asking?

### Tosafot's Strict Reading

The Gemara responds: Rava's question was framed *kide-Ulla* — along the lines of the ruling of Ulla, specifically as qualified by Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yochanan stated that a lung that has liquefied on the inside, spilling out like liquid from a ladle, is nonetheless *kosher* — *ve-hu de-kaimi simponaha* — provided that the bronchial tubes, the inner structural channels of the lung, remain intact. If those structures are gone, then the animal is a *treifa*.

Before examining the substance of Rava's question, it is worth noting the remarkable interpretive stance of Tosafot across all three defect categories. Tosafot does not treat the analogy to *treifa* as a loose rhetorical parallel. He reads it with full legal precision. On *niklaf*, as noted, Tosafot holds that the *etrog* must be entirely peeled to be *pasul*, mirroring the rule for *gluda*. On *nistak*, Tosafot holds that the crack must run the entire length of the *etrog*, just as a disqualifying crack in the trachea must extend the full length from the lower to the upper cartilage. The parallel is being imported not merely in concept but in its specific parameters. Tosafot is assuming that the details of the *psulim* by *etrog* are to be learned directly from the corresponding *psulim* by *treifa*.

### Liquefied Insides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ כִּדְעוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רֵיאָה שֶׁנִּשְׁפְּכָה כְּקִיתוֹן — כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאָמַר רָבָא: וְהוּא דְּקָיְימִי סִימְפּוֹנַהָא. הָא לָא קָיְימִי סִימְפּוֹנַהָא — טְרֵפָה. הָכָא מַאי? דִּלְמָא הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא שָׁלֵיט בַּהּ אַוֵּירָא, הֲדַר בָּרְיָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּשָׁלֵיט בַּהּ אַוֵּירָא — סָרוֹחֵי מַסְרְחָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When he raises the dilemma,** it is with regard to a case **like** that **which Ulla** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A lung whose** contents **can be poured like a pitcher,** i.e., whose tissue dissolved to the point of liquefaction, is not a sign of *tereifa*, and the animal is **kosher. And Rava said: And that is** the *halakha* only **where the bronchia are intact.** However, **if the bronchia are not intact, it is** a sign of ***tereifa*.** The dilemma **here** is with regard to a comparable situation in an *etrog*, i.e., an *etrog* that liquefied from within: **What** is its halakhic status? **Perhaps it is there,** in the case of the lung, **where the air does not affect it** since it is completely enclosed in the body, that the lungs **can recover,** and that is why it is not a *tereifa*. **However, here,** in the case of the *etrog*, **where the air affects it, it** inevitably **decays** and spoils and therefore it is a *tereifa*. **Or, perhaps** the case of the *etrog* is **no different.**

Rava's actual question, then, concerns the *etrog* whose interior has liquefied — where the flesh and juice of the fruit have turned to liquid. The baseline assumption of the Gemara seems to be that total liquefaction would render the *etrog pasul*, by analogy to the *treifa* lung. The question is whether the qualification that applied to the lung — that if the inner structural chambers (*simponaha*) remain, the animal is *kosher* — would likewise apply to the *etrog*. In the *etrog*, the flesh exists within a chamber structure, much like any citrus fruit. If the flesh has liquefied but the chamber walls remain intact, would the *etrog* remain valid, paralleling the lung?

The Gemara considers an argument for distinguishing the two cases. *Dilma hatam hu delo shalit bei avira* — perhaps the reason the lung can recover is that air does not have direct access to the interior, and therefore the remaining structure can heal. This is admittedly a striking observation given that the lung is the very organ through which air passes, and the Gemara acknowledges the irony. The point seems to be that while air cycles through the lung, the interior tissue is not directly exposed in the same way the inside of an *etrog* would be if it began to liquefy. The *etrog*, being exposed to air — *shalit bei avira* — would only continue to rot: *srochi masracha*. On the other hand, perhaps the rule is the same regardless, and as long as the inner chamber structure is present, the *etrog* remains valid.

### Tapuach Saruach Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֶתְרוֹג תָּפוּחַ סָרוּחַ, כָּבוּשׁ, שָׁלוּק, כּוּשִׁי, לָבָן, וּמְנוּמָּר — פָּסוּל. אֶתְרוֹג כְּכַדּוּר — פָּסוּל, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַתְּיוֹם. אֶתְרוֹג הַבּוֹסֶר — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא פּוֹסֵל, וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין. גִּדְּלוֹ בִּדְפוּס וַעֲשָׂאוֹ כְּמִין בְּרִיָּה אַחֶרֶת — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma based on that which was taught in a *baraita*. **An *etrog* that is *tafuaḥ*, *saruaḥ*, pickled, boiled,** a black **Cushite** *etrog*, **a white** *etrog*, **or a speckled** *etrog* **is unfit. An *etrog*** shaped **like a ball is unfit, and some say even a twin,** conjoined, *etrog* is unfit. With regard to **an *etrog* that is unripe, Rabbi Akiva deems it unfit, and the Rabbis deem it fit. If he grew** the *etrog* **in a mold and shaped it** to appear **like a different entity,** and it is no longer shaped like an *etrog*, it is **unfit.**

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the mishna's list. The mishna mentions *tapuach saruach* — an *etrog* that is bloated or spoiled — as *pasul*. The Gemara suggests: perhaps *tapuach* refers to external bloating and *saruach* refers to internal rot or liquefaction, and since both render the *etrog pasul*, we have our answer regarding internal liquefaction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת תָּפוּחַ סָרוּחַ, מַאי לָאו: תָּפוּחַ מִבַּחוּץ, וְסָרוּחַ מִבִּפְנִים! לָא, אִידִי וְאִידִי מִבַּחוּץ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּתְפַח אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא סְרַח, הָא דִּסְרַח אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא תְּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event, it teaches** that an *etrog* that is ***tafuaḥ*** or ***saruaḥ*** is unfit. **What, is it not** that ***tafuaḥ*** means that it decayed **on the outside and *saruaḥ*** means that it decayed **on the inside?** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No,** both **this and that are** referring to decay **on the outside. And this** apparent redundancy is **not difficult,** as **this** case, *tafuaḥ*, **is where it swelled even though it did not decay, and that** case, *saruaḥ*, **is where it decayed even though it did not swell.**

The Gemara rejects this reading: *idi ve-idi min ha-chutz* — both terms describe external conditions, not internal ones. The proof therefore fails. What is striking, as the Gemara itself makes clear, is the underlying assumption driving the entire discussion: if the insides were totally liquefied, the Gemara takes for granted that it would be *pasul*, despite the fact that this is purely internal and would present no external aesthetic problem at all. There is no obvious *hadar* violation if the inside has liquefied and the outside remains intact. And yet the Gemara treats the parallel to *treifa* as compelling enough to assume the *etrog* would be disqualified even in such a case — not because of aesthetics, but because the *treifa* analogy demands it. This is the third occasion in these sugyas where the laws of *korbanos* and temple-related aesthetics seem to be doing genuine legal work in defining the *etrog's* validity.

Tosafot raises an additional point here regarding *nikav*. In *hilkhot treifos*, a hole in the lung does not need to go from one wall of the lung through to the opposite wall; it need only penetrate the wall of the lung into the interior chamber. Tosafot therefore suggests that the ruling requiring a *nekev mefulash* — a hole that goes all the way through — applies specifically to those parts of the *etrog* where there is only a single wall and no inner chamber: namely, the top and bottom of the fruit. But in the middle of the *etrog*, where there is a rind and then an inner chamber beyond, a hole that penetrates as far as the inner chamber would already be *pasul*, just like a hole in the wall of a lung. The through-and-through requirement applies only where the structure is entirely wall, with no inner space. Again, Tosafot insists on applying the *treifa* parameters with remarkable precision.

## Invalid Etrog Types

### Mishna's Full List

This lengthy list from the *baraita* catalogues additional categories of invalid *etrogim*: *tapuach saruach* — bloated or rotting; *kavush* — pickled; *shaluk* — boiled; *kushi* — Ethiopian or black; *lavan* — white; *menumar* — spotted; *ke-kadur* — perfectly spherical like a ball; *teyom* — a twin *etrog*, two fruits that grew joined together; *ha-boser* — unripe; and *gidlu b'dfus* — one that was grown in a mold and consequently looks like a completely different species of fruit.

This is a rich and varied list, encompassing defects of color, texture, shape, ripeness, and artificial manipulation. The remainder of this shiur works through these categories one by one.

One observation about the list as a whole: an attentive reader will notice that most of these defects are externally visible and relate directly to the *etrog*'s aesthetic presentation — its *hadar*. The internal liquefaction discussed in the previous segment stands apart as the only defect that would not necessarily be apparent from the outside, which underscores the observation that the *treifa* comparison is doing independent legal work beyond mere aesthetic judgment.

## Kushi - Black Etrog

### Ethiopian v. Black Defin…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אֶתְרוֹג כּוּשִׁי — פָּסוּל. וְהָתַנְיָא: כּוּשִׁי כָּשֵׁר, דּוֹמֶה לְכוּשִׁי — פָּסוּל! אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תְּנַן נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין, דּוֹמֶה לְכוּשִׁי תְּנַן. רָבָא אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא הָא לַן, וְהָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited above: **A Cushite *etrog* is unfit.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a different *baraita*: **A Cushite *etrog* is fit,** but an *etrog* that is **similar to a Cushite** *etrog* **is unfit. Abaye said: When we learned** this *halakha* **in the mishna** that it is unfit, **too, we learned** it not in reference to an actual Cushite *etrog*, but rather in reference to one that is **similar to a Cushite** *etrog*. **Rava said:** Actually, the mishna is referring to a Cushite *etrog*, and nevertheless, it is **not difficult; this,** the *halakha* that it is unfit, **is for us** in Babylonia because our *etrogim* are typically light, and the dark Cushite *etrogim* are conspicuously different. **And that,** the *halakha* that it is fit, **is for them** in Eretz Yisrael, whose *etrogim* are typically dark. In Eretz Yisrael the dark Cushite *etrog* is not conspicuously different, and it is therefore fit.

The Gemara addresses *kushi*. The mishna states that a *kushi etrog* is *pasul*. But a *baraisa* seems to contradict this, ruling that a *kushi etrog* is *kosher*, while one that merely resembles a *kushi* is *pasul*. The *baraisa* thus appears to draw a sharp distinction: an *etrog* that is genuinely Ethiopian is valid, whereas one that has merely turned black while growing locally is not.

Abaye's initial response is that when our mishna calls a *kushi etrog pasul*, it means one that resembles an Ethiopian *etrog* — i.e., one that is black — rather than one that actually originated in Ethiopia. On this reading, both sources condemn the same thing: a locally grown *etrog* that has turned black, not a genuinely Ethiopian one.

### Regional Familiarity

Rava offers a different resolution: *ha lan ve-ha lehu* — one ruling is for us and the other is for them. According to Rashi, the mishna's disqualification of the *kushi etrog* reflects the standard for *Eretz Yisrael*, where Ethiopian *etrogim* are unfamiliar and would appear strange. The *baraisa*'s validation reflects the standard for *Bavel*, where the Jewish community lived in geographic proximity to Ethiopia — or at least had more regular commercial contact with it — and Ethiopian *etrogim* were not an unusual sight.

Rashi adds an important clarification: regardless of geography, everyone would agree that a locally grown *etrog* that turns black is *pasul*. This case is described with the term *bilas* — counterfeit — because such an *etrog* appears to be pretending to be something it is not: a Babylonian *etrog* impersonating an Ethiopian one. The authentic Ethiopian *etrog*, by contrast, is black because that is its natural character. In *Bavel*, where people encounter such fruits regularly, the natural black color of a genuine Ethiopian *etrog* does not strike observers as foreign or defective.

The note in Steinsaltz regarding geography is worth acknowledging: the *Rashash* observes that Ethiopia is actually closer to *Eretz Yisrael* than to Babylonia when measured overland. The response offered is that the land route from Ethiopia to *Eretz Yisrael* crosses the Sinai Desert and is exceedingly difficult, while the route to Babylonia, proceeding by sea across the Red Sea and up through the Persian Gulf region or along the Tigris and Euphrates, was more navigable. Rashi's geography thus implies that trade and familiarity with Ethiopian produce was more characteristic of the Babylonian Jewish community than of those in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Subjectivity of Hadar

What makes this discussion particularly striking from a *halachic* standpoint is the implication that *hadar* — the beauty requirement for the *etrog* — is not a fixed, universal standard but contains a genuinely subjective, culturally relative element. The same *etrog* that is *kosher* in *Bavel* becomes *pasul* if transported to *Eretz Yisrael*, not because anything changed about the fruit itself, but because its appearance is unfamiliar and striking to a different population. Beauty, or at least the negative judgment that something is not beautiful, is partly a function of what one is accustomed to seeing. The same principle appears in other *halachic* contexts — most notably in the case of a *Kohen* with an unusual physical appearance who is exempt from *duchan* in his own city (*d'dash b'iro*), since his neighbors are accustomed to him and he will not cause distraction, but who may not lead services in a place where he is unknown.

### Bilas - Counterfeit

The distinction between the genuinely Ethiopian *etrog* and the locally grown black *etrog* turns on authenticity. The locally grown *etrog* that has turned black is *bilas* — counterfeit — because it is something presenting itself as what it is not. A Babylonian *etrog* that grows black carries an air of wrongness, a sense of inauthenticity, that is distinct from the natural character of a fruit that simply grows black because that is what its species does. The genuine Ethiopian *etrog* may look unusual to an *Eretz Yisrael* audience, but it is not pretending to be anything other than what it is.

## Boser - Unripe

### Akiva v. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶתְרוֹג הַבּוֹסֶר — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא פּוֹסֵל וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין. אָמַר רַבָּה: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי הִיא — (דְּתַנְיָא:) רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר אֶת הָאֶתְרוֹגִים בְּקוֹטְנָן.
>
> **Translation:** It was also taught in the *baraita*: With regard to **an unripe *etrog*, Rabbi Akiva deems it unfit, and the Rabbis deem it fit. Rabba said: Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Shimon said one** and the same **statement.** The Gemara elaborates: The statement of **Rabbi Akiva** is **that which we said;** an unripe *etrog* is unfit. **Rabbi Shimon, what is** his statement? It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon exempts *etrogim*** from the requirement to be tithed while **in their small** state. Apparently, Rabbi Shimon, too, holds that an unripe *etrog* is not a fruit.

The mishna records a dispute regarding the *boser* — the unripe *etrog*. Rabbi Akiva declares it *pasul*; the Sages declare it *kosher*. The Gemara notes that Rabba identifies a parallel between Rabbi Akiva's position here and that of Rabbi Shimon in *Ma'asros*, where Rabbi Shimon rules that small, unripe *etrogim* are exempt from *ma'aser*. Rabba suggests that both sages share a common underlying position: an unripe *etrog* does not yet have the status of a fruit, and therefore Rabbi Akiva disqualifies it for *lulav* and Rabbi Shimon exempts it from *ma'aser*.

### Hadar v. Ripeness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָכָא, דְּבָעֵינַן ״הָדָר״ וְלֵיכָּא, אֲבָל הָתָם — כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. אִי נָמֵי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָתָם, אֶלָּא דִּכְתִיב: ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר אֵת כׇּל תְּבוּאַת זַרְעֶךָ״, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם מוֹצִיאִין לִזְרִיעָה. אֲבָל הָכָא, כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **Perhaps that is not** the case and they do not share the same opinion. **Rabbi Akiva stated his opinion only here,** with regard to an unripe *etrog*, **as we require beauty [*hadar*]** in an *etrog* **and there is none** in the case of an unripe *etrog* due to its color or small size; **however, there,** with regard to tithes, perhaps **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that one is obligated to tithe even a half-ripe *etrog*. **Alternatively, Rabbi Shimon stated his opinion only there** with regard to the exemption of an unripe *etrog* from tithes, **as it is written: "You shall surely tithe all the produce of your planting,** which is brought forth in the field year by year" (Deuteronomy 14:22). From that verse it is derived that the obligation to tithe applies only to produce that has developed to the point where it is **typical for people to take it out** to the field **for sowing;** one is not obligated to tithe unripe fruit that is not suitable for planting. **However,** perhaps **here he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Akiva and would deem an unripe *etrog* fit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** there is **nothing more** to discuss here. Clearly, the opinions of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Shimon do not necessarily coincide.

Abaye challenges this linkage. The concern Rabbi Akiva addresses with the *boser* for *lulav* is aesthetic — the *etrog* must have *hadar*, and an unripe *etrog* lacks that beauty. This has nothing to do with whether the fruit has achieved full botanical status. Rabbi Akiva could simultaneously hold that it is indeed a *pri* — a fruit — and is *chayav* in *ma'aser*, while still ruling it *pasul* for *lulav* on purely aesthetic grounds.

Conversely, Rabbi Shimon's exemption of small *etrogim* from *ma'aser* does not necessarily mean he considers them non-fruits. The verse in *Ma'asros* requires *ma'aser* to be taken from produce *ke-derech she-b'nei adam motzi'im l'zri'ah* — in the manner that people use it for replanting — meaning the produce must have reached a stage of ripeness sufficient for its seeds to be viable for planting. An unripe *etrog*, even if it fully qualifies as a fruit, may not yet have reached the stage of *motzi l'zera*, and therefore Rabbi Shimon would exempt it from *ma'aser* without denying its status as a fruit. The two positions operate on entirely different axes and there is no necessary connection between them. Abaye's response is emphatic: *batlu midi* — enough, these are clearly unrelated concerns.

## Invalid Etrog Types…

### Molded Etrog (Dfus)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּדְּלוֹ בִּדְפוּס וַעֲשָׂאוֹ כְּמִין בְּרִיָּה אַחֶרֶת — פָּסוּל. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כְּמִין בְּרִיָּה אַחֶרֶת, אֲבָל כִּבְרִיָּיתוֹ — כָּשֵׁר. פְּשִׁיטָא! כְּמִין בְּרִיָּה אַחֶרֶת (תְּנַן)! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּעֲבִידָא דַּפֵּי דַּפֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If he grew** the *etrog* **in a mold and shaped it** to appear **like a different species,** it is **unfit. Rava said:** The Sages **taught** that it is unfit **only** if he shaped it to appear **like a different species; however,** if he shaped the *etrog* so it still appears **like its own species, it is fit.** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious;** the phrase: **Like a different species, is** explicitly **taught** in the *baraita*. If it shaped like its own species, it is fit. The Gemara answers: **No,** Rava's statement **is necessary** to deem fit an *etrog* **that is shaped** into the shape of **many planks,** i.e., pieces of wood attached to each other. Although its shape is not precisely that of a regular *etrog*, it sufficiently resembles a regular *etrog* and is fit.

The mishna disqualifies an *etrog gidlu b'dfus* — one grown in a mold — if the mold caused it to look like a completely different species. Rava clarifies: this disqualification applies only when the resulting shape makes the *etrog* look like an entirely different creature. If the mold kept the *etrog* looking like an *etrog*, it is obviously *kosher*. The Gemara asks: is that not self-evident? The *baraisa* itself specifies *k'min birya acheret* — something that looks like a different species. What does Rava add?

Rava's *chiddush* addresses an intermediate case. What if the mold produced an *etrog* with a surface pattern of overlapping plates or ridges — *dapei dapei* — somewhat like the paddles of a water wheel, as Rashi describes? One might have thought that such a dramatically altered surface texture would render it a different kind of creature. Rava teaches that even this remains within *k'vriyaso* — it still looks like an *etrog* — and is therefore *kosher*. The threshold for being disqualified as a different species is quite high. Rashi reads this *le-kula*: even a markedly unusual texture remains within the acceptable range. The *etrog* can look quite strange and still be *kosher*, so long as it has not crossed into looking like an entirely different fruit altogether.

## Chanina's Bite - Holidays

### Rat Bites & Hadar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אֶתְרוֹג שֶׁנְּקָבוּהוּ עַכְבָּרִים, אָמַר רַב: אֵין זֶה הָדָר. אִינִי? וְהָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מְטַבֵּיל בֵּהּ וְנָפֵיק בֵּהּ! וּלְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין!
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagree with regard to **an *etrog* that mice pierced. Rav said: That is not beautiful. Is that so? But wouldn't Rabbi Ḥanina dip** his *etrog*, eat part of it, **and fulfill his obligation with** what remained of **it?** The Gemara asks: **And for Rabbi Ḥanina, the mishna is difficult,** as it states that an incomplete *etrog* is unfit.

The Gemara introduces a dramatic case. An *etrog* in which rats have chewed — *esrog she-nikvu achbarim*. Rav rules: *ein zeh hadar* — such an *etrog* lacks *hadar* and is *pasul*. The Gemara immediately challenges this from the story of Rabbi Chanina: Rabbi Chanina was in the practice of dipping his *etrog* in salt — as was common, given the practice of dipping foods in condiments — and would take a bite from it, and then fulfill the *mitzvah* with that same bitten *etrog*. The question of *huktza l'mitzvah* — the prohibition against using a *mitzvah* object for personal use — is acknowledged but set aside for the moment.

If Rabbi Chanina took a bite from the *etrog* and used it, and the mishna states that a hole accompanied by missing flesh is *pasul*, how did Rabbi Chanina act as he did?

### Rashi: Two Problems

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מַתְנִיתִין לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן, כָּאן בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי. אֶלָּא לְרַב קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: שָׁאנֵי עַכְבָּרִים דִּמְאִיסִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, for Rabbi Ḥanina, the mishna is not difficult,** as it can be explained that **here,** when the mishna prohibits one from using an incomplete *etrog*, it is referring to performing the mitzva **on the first day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, when a complete taking of the species is required; and **there,** when Rabbi Ḥanina's conduct leads to the conclusion that an incomplete *etrog* is fit, it is referring to performing the mitzva on **the second day of the Festival** or thereafter. **However,** according **to Rav,** who said an *etrog* that was pierced by mice is unfit, Rabbi Ḥanina's conduct is **difficult,** as the requirement of beauty applies on all seven days. The Gemara answers: This is not difficult, as **Rav** could have **said to you: Mice are different, as they are repulsive.** When mice pierce an *etrog*, what remains is antithetical to beauty. When a person bites an *etrog*, what remains can still be considered beautiful.

The Gemara resolves: the mishna's disqualification for *chaser* and Rabbi Chanina's practice are not in conflict because they address different days. The mishna's *psul* of *chaser* applies to *Yom Tov rishon*; Rabbi Chanina used his bitten *etrog* on *chol ha-moed*.

But this raises the question: why can the same logic not reconcile Rav's ruling with Rabbi Chanina? If the hole-*chaser* disqualification is limited to the first day, perhaps Rav's ruling that a rat-bitten *etrog* lacks *hadar* is similarly limited to the first day, and Rabbi Chanina used his *etrog* on the other days?

According to Rashi's reading — and this is the dominant *girsa* — this solution is unavailable. The reason is that Rav specifically invoked the language of *hadar*. And *hadar* is a seven-day requirement. If Rav's disqualification is grounded in the absence of *hadar*, then it is a disqualification that extends across all seven days of *Sukkos*, not merely the first. Rabbi Chanina's case, therefore, must be distinguished on a different basis.

Rav's response is that rats are *meisin* — particularly repulsive — in a way that human bite marks are not. The implication is that a human bite does not destroy the *etrog*'s *hadar*, while a rat bite does. Therefore, Rabbi Chanina's bitten *etrog* was still considered *hadar* and valid for all seven days, while a rat-bitten *etrog* lacks *hadar* throughout.

Rashi further explains that the mishna's disqualification for a hole with missing flesh is not a *hadar* problem at all — it is a separate *psul* derived from the word *u'lekachtem* and its implication of *lekichah tamah*, the requirement that the taking be complete, meaning the *etrog* itself must be whole. This is a *Yom Tov rishon* problem only, rooted in the specific wording of *u'lekachtem lachem ba-yom ha-rishon*. The two categories are thus: defects of *hadar*, which are disqualifying for all seven days; and defects of wholeness (*chaser*), which are disqualifying only on the first day.

### Alternative Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב: זֶה הָדָר, דְּהָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מְטַבֵּיל בֵּהּ וְנָפֵיק בֵּהּ. וּלְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן, כָּאן בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Some say** this exchange differently. **Rav said** with regard to an *etrog* that mice pierced: **That is beautiful, as Rabbi Ḥanina would dip** his *etrog*, eat part of it, **and fulfill his obligation with** what remained of **it,** indicating that an incomplete *etrog* is fit. The Gemara asks: **And for Rabbi Ḥanina, the mishna is difficult,** as it states that an incomplete *etrog* is unfit. The Gemara answers: **The mishna is not difficult; here,** it is referring to performing the mitzva **on the first day of the festival** of *Sukkot*; **there,** it is referring to performing the mitzva on **the second day of the Festival** or thereafter.

Some *rishonim* possessed a different *girsa* of this Gemara, in which Rav's ruling — *ein zeh hadar* — is actually rejected by the Gemara. In this reading, the proof from Rabbi Chanina is not about reconciling rat bites with human bites; rather, the Gemara's conclusion is that even a *hadar* deficiency is only a first-day disqualification, not a seven-day one. Rabbi Chanina's use of the bitten *etrog* on *chol ha-moed* demonstrates not that the bite mark was still aesthetically acceptable, but that aesthetic deficiencies do not matter at all beyond the first day. Rav's principle — that *hadar* is a seven-day requirement — is simply rejected.

### Ashkenazi v. Sephardi

This dispute over *girsa* maps directly onto a major *halachic* disagreement between *Ashkenazim* and *Sephardim* that descends into the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Rama*. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules that the *psulim* of *etrog* are largely confined to *Yom Tov rishon*, and on *chol ha-moed* one may use an *etrog* that would otherwise be disqualified. The *Rama*, reflecting the Ashkenazi tradition, rules that the *psulim* continue to apply through *chol ha-moed*. The distinction in *girsa* is thus not merely a textual curiosity but the foundation for a practical *halachic* difference observed to this day.

The term *Yom Tov sheini* as it appears in this sugya refers to *chol ha-moed*, not to *Yom Tov sheini shel galuyos*. The *rishonim* so understand it, and while there is some discussion about the matter, it appears that *Yom Tov rishon* and *Yom Tov sheini* here contrast the first day of the *chag* with its intermediate days, not the first day of *Yom Tov* with the second *Yom Tov* day outside *Eretz Yisrael*.

## Size Requirements

### Egoz v. Beitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶתְרוֹג קָטָן וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת כָּאן, כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בַּאֲבָנִים מְקוּרְזָלוֹת. דְּתַנְיָא: בְּשַׁבָּת שָׁלֹשׁ אֲבָנִים מְקוּרְזָלוֹת מוּתָּר לְהַכְנִיס לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּרָן? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כֶּאֱגוֹז, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּבֵיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** A dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is cited in a mishna with regard to the minimum measure **of a small *etrog*. Rafram bar Pappa said: Like the dispute here, so is the dispute with regard to** the matter of **rounded stones, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **On Shabbat three rounded stones may be taken into the bathroom** in order to clean oneself with them. Although generally one may not move stones on Shabbat because they are set aside from use, the Sages permitted doing so in the interest of human dignity. However, they disagreed, with regard to the size of these stones. **And what is their measure? Rabbi Meir says: A walnut-bulk; Rabbi Yehuda says: An egg-bulk.** Clearly the rationales for these disputes are different; however, since the respective measures are identical, the analogy can serve as a mnemonic.

The mishna addresses *esrog katan* — a small *etrog* — and the question of the minimum size. The Gemara, through Ravin bar Papa, notes a parallel: the same debate that governs the size of the *etrog* mirrors the debate over *avanim mekurzalos* — the stones used for personal hygiene in place of toilet paper. On *Shabbos*, the Sages permitted one to carry such stones into a *beis hakissei*, waiving the *muktzah* prohibition out of consideration for *kevod ha-beriyot*. The question of how large these stones should be is debated: Rabbi Meir says the size of a walnut (*egoz*); Rabbi Yehuda says the size of an egg (*beitzah*). In the context of the stones, this represents a *maximum* size — larger stones would be impractical. For the *etrog*, the same measures represent a *minimum* size.

Remarkably, it is the same Rabbi Meir who sets the minimum *etrog* size at an *egoz* who also sets the maximum stone size at an *egoz*, and the same Rabbi Yehuda who requires a *beitzah*-sized *etrog* who sets the maximum stone size at a *beitzah*. The Gemara does not suggest any logical connection between the two disputes; it is simply an observation of a consistent parallel. The context is unmistakably reminiscent of the discussion in *Berachos*, as the lecturer wryly observes.

Tosafot in *Shabbos* raises the question of whether the leniency regarding carrying these stones applies in later times, when people have fixed indoor bathrooms and could have prepared their toilet paper before *Shabbos*. Tosafot entertains the view that since preparation is now easy, one cannot claim the necessity that justified the leniency in Talmudic times when the outhouses were far out in the fields. However, Tosafot then considers the counterargument: in a household with many people, the supply of pre-cut paper may be exhausted, and in such circumstances — *lo efshar* — the leniency might still apply. The practical *halakha* in cases of genuine need on *Shabbos*, where *kevod ha-beriyot* is at stake, remains governed by the principle that *kevod ha-beriyot* overrides rabbinic restrictions, particularly when the underlying prohibition is itself of rabbinic character.

### Huge Etrog Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַגָּדוֹל כְּדֵי שֶׁיֹּאחַז כּוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁבָּא לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְאֶתְרוֹגוֹ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? אַף הֵם אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין זֶה הָדָר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And in a large** *etrog*, the maximum measure is **so that one could hold** two in his one hand; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: It is fit even if it is so large that he can hold only one in his two hands. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Akiva, who came to the synagogue, and his *etrog*** was so large that he carried it **on his shoulder.** Apparently, one can fulfill his obligation with a large *etrog*. **Rabbi Yehuda said to him:** Is there **proof from there?** In that case, **too,** the Sages **said to him: That is not beauty.**

Rabbi Yosi cites a story to establish that a very large *etrog* can also be *pasul*. Rabbi Akiva once came to the *beis ha-knesses* carrying his *etrog* on his shoulder — so enormous was the fruit. Rabbi Yehuda challenged him: is that really a proof? The very people around Rabbi Akiva said to him, *ein zeh hadar* — this is not beautiful. Sheer size, beyond a certain threshold, does not enhance the *etrog*; it detracts from it.

Rashi notes that the Gemara had earlier framed the maximum-size concern in practical terms: the *etrog* must be small enough to hold together with the *lulav* in one hand without dropping. Rabbi Yehuda, Rashi explains, does not abandon that practical concern, but adds that even apart from practicality, an *etrog* of extreme size fails the *hadar* standard on aesthetic grounds alone.

## Binding the Lulav

### Yehuda v. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין אוֹגְדִין אֶת הַלּוּלָב אֶלָּא בְּמִינוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ (בְּחוּט) בִּמְשִׁיחָה. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹגְדִין אֶת לוּלְבֵיהֶן בְּגִימוֹנִיּוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: בְּמִינוֹ הָיוּ אוֹגְדִין אוֹתוֹ מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may bind the *lulav* only with its own species;** i.e., one of the four species taken with the *lulav*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** One may do so **even with a string** or **with a cord. Rabbi Meir said:** There was **an incident involving the men of Jerusalem who would bind their *lulavim* with gold rings.** The Sages **said to him: They would bind it with its own species beneath** the rings, which serve a merely decorative purpose and not a halakhic one.

The mishna introduces a new topic: the binding of the *lulav*. The rule *ein ogdin es ha-lulav ela b'mino* — one may bind the *lulav* only with one of the *arba minim* — reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir disagrees, permitting even a string or a band of any material.

### Jerusalem Practice

Rabbi Meir invokes a precedent: the people of Jerusalem would bind their *lulavim* with golden chains — *gimoniyos shel zahav*. The respondents to Rabbi Meir clarify: the golden chains were added decoratively, but the underlying binding at the base of the *lulav* was done with one of the *minim* themselves. The golden chain was an embellishment on top of a valid binding, not a substitute for it.

### Rava's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ בְּסִיב, אֲפִילּוּ בְּעִיקָּרָא דְּדִיקְלָא. וְאָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, קָסָבַר: לוּלָב צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, וְאִי מַיְיתֵי מִינָא אַחֲרִינָא — הָוֵה חַמְשָׁה מִינֵי. וְאָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ דְּסִיב וְעִיקָּרָא דְּדִיקְלָא מִינָא דְלוּלַבָּא הוּא — דְּתַנְיָא: ״בַּסּוּכּוֹת תֵּשְׁבוּ״, סוּכָּה שֶׁל כׇּל דָּבָר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין סוּכָּה נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבָּעָה מִינִים שֶׁבַּלּוּלָב. וְהַדִּין נוֹתֵן: וּמָה לוּלָב שֶׁאֵין נוֹהֵג בַּלֵּילוֹת כְּבַיָּמִים, אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּעַת מִינִין, סוּכָּה שֶׁנּוֹהֶגֶת בַּלֵּילוֹת כְּבַיָּמִים — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּעַת מִינִין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava said:** One may bind the *lulav* **even with fibers** that grow around the trunk of the date palm, **and even with** a piece of **the trunk of the date palm. And Rava said: What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda? He holds that a *lulav* requires binding, and if one brings another species** to bind the *lulav*, **there will be five species** and he will violate the prohibition against adding to the mitzvot. **And Rava** further **said: From where do I say** this *halakha* **that fibers and the trunk of the date palm are the species of the *lulav*?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"You shall reside in *sukkot*** for seven days" (Leviticus 23:42), which means **a *sukka* of any material,** as the Torah was not particular about the material to be used for the roofing; any species may be used as long as it grew from the ground and it is not susceptible to impurity. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The mitzva of ***sukka* is practiced only with the four species of the *lulav*** as roofing. **And,** he claims, **logic dictates** that it is so, as it is derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference: **Just as** the mitzva of ***lulav*, which is not practiced at night as it is during the day, is practiced only with the four species,** with regard to the mitzva of ***sukka*, which is practiced at night as it is during the day, is it not right that its** roofing **should be only from the four species?**

Rava explains Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *lulav tzarich egged* — the *lulav* requires formal binding to be valid; simply holding the three species together is insufficient. Given that the binding is a necessary component of the *mitzvah* object, Rava reasons that any material used for that binding becomes incorporated into the *mitzvah* object itself. Introducing material from outside the *arba minim* — a string, a cloth strip — would be adding a fifth species to what should be a four-species *mitzvah* object, contaminating it with something extraneous.

### Palm-tree Components

Rava continues: even within the requirement to use materials from the *minim*, the standard is generous. *Afilu b'siva afilu b'ikra d'dikla* — one may even use a vine-like strip from around a palm tree, or even a strip fashioned from the trunk of the palm tree itself. These materials need not be beautiful or aesthetically refined; the point is simply that they derive from the *lulav* tree. Rava's source for the principle that a vine or trunk strip from the palm counts as part of the same species comes from a *baraisa* to be discussed imminently.

## Sukkah Materials Debate

### Meir v. Yehuda

The *baraisa* Rava cites moves the discussion into the laws of *sukkah*. Rabbi Meir rules *sukkah shel kol davar* — one may construct the *s'chach* of the *sukkah* from any material. Rabbi Yehuda rules that the *sukkah* may only be constructed using the *arba minim*.

### Kal V'Chomer Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כׇּל דִּין שֶׁאַתָּה דָּן תְּחִלָּתוֹ לְהַחְמִיר וְסוֹפוֹ לְהָקֵל — אֵינוֹ דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** That is not an *a fortiori* inference, as **any** *a fortiori* **inference that you infer initially to be stringent, but ultimately** it is **to be lenient, is not a** legitimate *a fortiori* **inference.** If ultimately the stringency leads to a leniency, the entire basis of the inference is undermined.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a *kal v'chomer*: if the *lulav*, which is obligatory only during the day and not at night, must consist of the *arba minim*, then surely the *sukkah*, which is obligatory both day and night, should also require the *arba minim*. The *kal v'chomer* is bracing in its scope. The respondents reject it with a counter-principle: *kol din she-atah dan techilato le-hachmir ve-sofo le-hakel eino din* — any legal argument that begins as a stringency and ends as a leniency is invalid. What is the resultant leniency here? If a person cannot find *arba minim* to construct a *sukkah*, he will simply not sit in a *sukkah*. By setting an impossibly demanding standard, Rabbi Yehuda's stringency effectively permits people to exempt themselves from the *mitzvah* entirely. A *chumra* that prevents observance is, paradoxically, a *kula*. Such a *kal v'chomer* cannot stand.

### Torah's Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא מָצָא אַרְבַּעַת מִינִין יְהֵא יוֹשֵׁב וּבָטֵל, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״בַּסּוּכּוֹת תֵּשְׁבוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ — סוּכָּה שֶׁל כׇּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** According to your reasoning, if **one did not find** any of the **four species** to roof his *sukka*, **he will sit idly** and fail to fulfill the mitzva of *sukka*; **and the Torah states: "You shall reside in *sukkot*** for seven days" (Leviticus 23:42), meaning **a *sukka* of any material.**

The *baraisa* concludes by affirming Rabbi Meir's position in the most direct terms: *ba-sukkot teshvu shivat yamim sukkah shel kol davar* — the Torah commands dwelling in *sukkos* for seven days, and that *sukkah* may be made of any material. The Torah's purpose is to enable observance, not to erect barriers to it. The shiur closes with the note that this discussion will continue tomorrow, where the Gemara will develop further how Rava's broad definition of what qualifies from the palm tree informs the scope of what Rabbi Yehuda himself permits when binding the *lulav*.
