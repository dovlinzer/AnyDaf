# Shabbat 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Approaches

### Rav Chisda's Egg

The Mishnah at the bottom of 42b addresses the prohibition of placing a plate beneath a lamp to catch dripping oil. The Gemara on 43 opens by taking up this question through a related case, which will serve as the lens through which three distinct explanations emerge.

Rav Chisda establishes an initial distinction: although one may not place a vessel beneath a hen to catch its egg — out of concern that the egg will break — one may nevertheless cover the egg with a vessel to prevent it from being stepped on. The Gemara will now work through a series of explanations for why catching and covering are treated differently, and those explanations will in turn illuminate the prohibition in the Mishnah regarding the oil.

### Hatzala Metzuyah

Rabbah offers the first explanation. He reasons that Rav Chisda's distinction rests on the relative frequency of the two scenarios. A hen will typically lay her egg in a garbage heap or some random location — a common setting where someone might accidentally step on and break the egg. The need to protect an egg in such a situation is therefore a *hatzala metzuyah*, a common rescue scenario, and for that the Rabbis permitted the action. By contrast, a hen does not normally lay her egg on an incline where it would roll and break as it falls. Catching a rolling egg would address an uncommon scenario — a *hatzala she-eina metzuyah* — and for that they did not permit action.

Rashi, in explaining why any of this should require rabbinic dispensation in the first place, imports the later reasoning of the Gemara: that moving a vessel for the sake of a *muktzeh* object — and an egg born on Shabbat is *nolad* and therefore *muktzeh* — is itself prohibited. The principle, which the Gemara will make explicit shortly, is *ein kli nittal ela le-tzorech davar ha-nittal*: a vessel may only be moved for something that itself can be moved on Shabbat. Rashi thus understands the rabbinic permission for common scenarios as an override of this prohibition.

### Tosafot Tircha

Tosafot offers a different framing. According to Tosafot, the underlying concern is not the specific problem of moving something for a *muktzeh* object but the broader rabbinic principle against excessive exertion on Shabbat — *tircha*. One should not labor on Shabbat even in activities that are technically permitted; the spirit of Shabbat as a day of rest militates against undue effort. It is this concern alone, Tosafot argues, that the Rabbis suspended in cases of *hatzala metzuyah*. No distinct *muktzeh* problem need be invoked.

### Series of Cases

The Gemara proceeds to test this framework — that only common rescue scenarios are permitted — against a series of cases drawn from Mishnayot and Beraitot.

The first case: if a barrel of *tevel* (untithed grain) breaks on one's roof, one may bring a vessel and place it underneath to catch the contents. Does this not involve catching something in an uncommon scenario, one that should therefore be prohibited? The Gemara answers: we are speaking of brand-new barrels, which are known to burst with some frequency. This is therefore a *hatzala metzuyah*.

Note that the barrel contains *tevel*, which is significant for Rashi: since *tevel* is *muktzeh*, the problem is that one is moving a vessel for the sake of a *muktzeh* object. Tosafot, characteristically, argues that the word *tevel* did not originally appear in the text, since the *ikkar* concern is merely the *tircha* of exertion — even if the grain were not *muktzeh*, the question would remain.

Next, a Mishnah teaches that one may place a vessel beneath a lamp to catch sparks, even though the same Mishnah prohibits catching the dripping oil. The Gemara justifies catching sparks as a *hatzala metzuyah* — sparks present a common enough hazard.

Tosafot raises a pointed question that the Gemara itself leaves implicit: if what is common is permitted and what is uncommon is not, why is catching oil from a lamp treated as uncommon while catching sparks from that same lamp is treated as common? Tosafot's answer is striking. Dripping oil is so routine a phenomenon that one ought to have anticipated it and addressed it before Shabbat. The need to catch oil during Shabbat therefore reflects a failure of preparation — and an *avoidable* problem is not a *hatzala metzuyah* in the relevant sense. Sparks, by contrast, are not a routine occurrence; one has no particular reason to have anticipated them. When they appear, the scenario is sufficiently common to warrant intervention, yet not so foreseeable that one should have dealt with it beforehand.

A third case: one may invert a vessel over a lamp to prevent the flame from igniting the roof beam above it. The Gemara explains that this involves houses with low ceilings — *batei de-gachanei* — where fire spreading to the beams is a genuine and frequent concern, making this too a *hatzala metzuyah*.

A fourth case: if a beam begins to crack, one may support it with a bench or the side-rails of a bed. The Gemara accounts for this as involving new beams, which have a known tendency to split before they have been properly seasoned. Again, the scenario is common enough.

A fifth case: one may place a vessel beneath a roof leak on Shabbat to catch the dripping rainwater. The Gemara answers here that new houses — whose construction has not yet fully settled — are known to leak with some regularity. These answers, the Gemara itself seems to acknowledge, are becoming somewhat strained, which is part of what motivates the introduction of an entirely different explanation in the following section.

## Mevatlin Kli

### Vessel Unusable

Rav Yosef offers a second, independent explanation of Rav Chisda's distinction between catching the egg and covering it. The problem with catching the egg, says Rav Yosef, is that once the egg lands in the vessel, the vessel can no longer be moved — it now contains *muktzeh* — and one has thereby rendered the vessel unusable on Shabbat. This is the prohibition of *ein mevatlin kli me-heichano*: one may not negate a vessel from its normal use on Shabbat.

### Over vs Under

This framing explains the asymmetry precisely. When the vessel is placed over the egg to protect it, the vessel is not supporting the egg and does not contain *muktzeh* within it; one can still move the vessel away freely. But when the vessel is beneath the egg to catch it, the egg falls into the vessel and renders it unmovable for the duration of Shabbat. It is this end result — the negation of the vessel's usability — that is prohibited.

### Status at Shabbat

Tosafot, commenting on *mevatlin kli me-heichano* as it appears in this context, notes that the concept recurs in the final chapter of Shabbat. There, Rashi explains that the prohibition is because rendering something unusable constitutes a form of destruction — *michzei ke-soter*, it resembles demolishing. Here, however, in a different passage, Rashi explains that since the vessel cannot be moved once it contains *muktzeh*, it is as though one has cemented the vessel in place — *ke-ilu mechabro sham be-tit* — which resembles the opposite problem, an act of construction, *boneh*. Tosafot notes, with apparent interest, that Rashi gives almost directly contradictory explanations in these two places.

### Muktzeh Timing

One might ask: how can something that was not *muktzeh* at the onset of Shabbat become *muktzeh* during Shabbat? If the vessel was fully usable when Shabbat began, how does the subsequent landing of an egg in it transform its status?

### Two Types Muktzeh

This question points to a fundamental distinction within the category of *muktzeh*. There is one type of *muktzeh* that is rooted in a person's state of mind — in the intention or expectation formed before Shabbat. This *muktzeh* is determined at the onset of Shabbat, specifically during *bein hashmashot*, and the status fixed at that moment persists for the entirety of Shabbat. But there is a second type of *muktzeh* that stems from objective reality — from the fact that an object is simply not usable as things stand. This type can emerge during Shabbat itself. Just as a plate that shatters during Shabbat into unusable fragments becomes *muktzeh* at that moment of breakage, so too a vessel that now contains a *nolad* egg is *muktzeh* as a matter of current objective reality — not because of prior designation, but because of what it now holds. This *muktzeh* persists only as long as the conditions that produce it remain in place.

The question of *bosis* — the status of a vessel as a "supporter" of *muktzeh* — introduces further complexity. *Bosis* may partake more of the first type, a status defined at the onset of Shabbat, rather than one that fluctuates with changing reality throughout the day. Additionally, there is the independent concern that moving the vessel while it holds the egg constitutes an indirect movement of *muktzeh*, since the *muktzeh* travels with the vessel — a question of *tiltul* that the Gemara will address more directly later.

## Case Law Objections

### Tevel Case

The Gemara now returns to the cases cited earlier and re-examines them through the lens of *mevatlin kli me-heichano*. Abaye challenges Rav Yosef: the Beraita permits placing a vessel under a broken barrel of *tevel* to catch the grain. But *tevel* is *muktzeh* — so by catching it, one renders the vessel unusable. How is this permitted?

Rav Yosef answers: *tevel muchan hu eitzel Shabbat* — *tevel* is considered *muchan*, prepared and available, because if one were to separate *terumot u-ma'asrot* from it, even on Shabbat in violation, it would become edible. Since there is a theoretical path by which this grain becomes usable food, the vessel that holds it cannot be said to be truly and completely negated from use.

Tosafot raises two difficulties with this. First, if one were to separate *tevel* on Shabbat in violation, one would normally be forbidden to benefit from the result. Second, and more pointedly: right now the grain is still *tevel* and it remains forbidden to fix it on Shabbat — so how does this theoretical possibility affect its *muktzeh* status? Tosafot clarifies that the Gemara is not claiming *tevel* is not *muktzeh* — it clearly is. The Gemara's point is narrower: the question is not whether *tevel* is *muktzeh* but whether placing a vessel to catch *tevel* constitutes *mevatlin kli me-heichano* — rendering the vessel definitively unusable. Since there exists a hypothetical scenario, however remote, in which the *tevel* could be remedied and the vessel would again serve its purpose, one cannot say that catching the *tevel* has permanently negated the vessel.

One might ask whether there is any practically realistic circumstance in which separating *tevel* on Shabbat would be permitted — for instance, to feed someone in life-threatening danger who has no other food available. This does represent a genuine case, and Tosafot acknowledges scenarios where the separation might be permitted after the fact. The more pertinent point is that the theoretical possibility of the *tevel* being remedied is what prevents the vessel from being considered fully and irreversibly negated.

The basket with the bird case raises a related but distinct point. When the Gemara addresses whether moving a basket used as an exercise platform for birds is problematic — since animals are *muktzeh* and the bird sitting on the basket renders it unmovable — it concludes that if the bird stepped off, the basket would ordinarily be usable again. However, the Gemara adds a more fundamental ruling from Rabbi Abbahu: if the bird was on the basket for the entire *bein hashmashot* period, *migo de-itkatzei le-bein hashmashot, itkatzei le-chulei yoma* — since it was off-limits during *bein hashmashot*, it remains off-limits for all of Shabbat, even after the bird departs. This is the principle that when שבת begins, the status an object holds at that moment is fixed for the entire day. A Beraita that says one cannot use the basket even when the bird is not on it refers to this case — where the basket bore the bird's weight throughout *bein hashmashot*. A different Beraita that permits using the basket once the bird steps off refers to a case where the bird came onto the basket only during Shabbat itself, after *bein hashmashot* had passed. In that case, the basket became *muktzeh* only as a matter of current objective reality, and once the bird leaves, it reverts to being permitted.

### Sparks Mamash

The Gemara returns to the sparks case. Does catching sparks in a vessel not constitute *mevatlin kli me-heichano*, since now the vessel contains something that fell there on Shabbat? Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua answers: *nitzotzot ein bahem mamash* — sparks have no real substance. Once a spark burns out, almost nothing remains: perhaps a tiny burn mark, a thread of char. This negligible residue does not constitute a thing, and the vessel after the sparks extinguish is entirely unencumbered. There is therefore no *mevatlin kli me-heichano* concern.

### Beam Loose Fit

The case of supporting a cracking beam with a bench or bedpost: does placing the bench under the beam not render the bench unmovable — unusable in its normal capacity? The Gemara answers: *derafi* — the fit is loose. *De-i ba'ei shakil lei* — one can always remove the bench if one wishes. Even if doing so would leave the beam unsupported and risk its falling, nothing halachically prevents one from taking the bench back. Since the bench remains in principle removable, it has not been rendered definitively unusable.

The more interesting point here is that the concern of *ein mevatlin kli me-heichano* is not limited to cases where *muktzeh* falls into the vessel. Wedging a bench under a beam in a manner so tight that the bench cannot be extracted would constitute *mevatlin kli me-heichano* even absent any *muktzeh* element — the bench has simply been rendered unusable by the act of positioning it. One might observe that this also bears resemblance to an independent rabbinic prohibition of *boneh*: supporting a beam in one's house on Shabbat, however temporary, has the character of home construction.

### Rain Drinkable

In the case of catching rain dripping through a leaky roof: rain would seemingly be *nolad*, newly created, and therefore *muktzeh*. The Gemara explains that the permissibility here rests on the fact that the water in question is *haraui* — drinkable. It is therefore not *muktzeh* and presents no *mevatlin kli me-heichano* problem.

### Birds Basket Status

The basket and birds case has been addressed above in the context of the *tevel* discussion.

## Ein Kli Nital

### RYitzchak Challenge

Rabbi Yitzchak now introduces a third, more sweeping position. He disagrees with Rav Chisda's initial distinction and holds that one may neither place a vessel beneath the hen to catch the egg nor place a vessel over the egg to protect it. Both are prohibited. This is surprising — what is the problem with placing a vessel over the egg, given that the vessel does not become a *bosis*, does not hold *muktzeh*, and remains freely removable?

### Principle Stated

Rabbi Yitzchak's answer introduces the third and, in many ways, most fundamental principle of the sugya: *ein kli nittal ela le-davar ha-nittal be-Shabbat* — a vessel may be moved only for the sake of something that itself can be moved on Shabbat. One may not move a vessel whose sole purpose is to serve a *muktzeh* object. The newly born egg is *nolad* and *muktzeh*; moving the vessel for the egg's benefit is therefore prohibited regardless of whether the vessel subsequently becomes *muktzeh* itself.

This principle also explains the Mishnah directly. Why may one not place a vessel to catch the oil from the lamp? Because the oil, having been designated for the lamp, is *muktzeh*, and placing the vessel would mean moving it for the sake of *muktzeh*.

### Tzarich L'Makomo

The problem, of course, is that the cases cited earlier in the Gemara — catching the *tevel*, placing a vessel under the spark-producing lamp, supporting the beam with a bench, catching the rain — all appear to involve moving something for the sake of a *muktzeh* object. How can all of those be permitted according to Rabbi Yitzchak's principle?

The Gemara gives a blanket answer: *b'tzarich le-makomo* — in all those cases, one needed the place occupied by the vessel. The halachic technique available here is the same as with a *kli she-melachto le-issur*: just as one may move a hammer that is in one's way if one needs that space — even though one cannot move it merely to protect it — the same applies here. One may not move a vessel for the sake of *muktzeh*, but one may move a vessel because one needs its location for a permitted purpose. Once it is already in one's hands, one may set it down wherever one chooses — including directly over or under a *muktzeh* object. In all the cases discussed, the vessel was ostensibly moved because the person needed the space, and its placement adjacent to the *muktzeh* followed incidentally.

## Ein Kli Case Law

### Egg Case

The Gemara tests Rabbi Yitzchak's principle — and the *tzarich le-makomo* solution — against a series of further cases.

A Beraita states: an egg born on Shabbat or on Yom Tov may not be moved at all — not to use as a stopper for a vessel, not to prop up a bed leg — but one may place a vessel over it to prevent it from breaking. The Beraita thus seems to explicitly permit covering it "in order that it not break" — *bishvil shelo tishaver* — which would appear to directly contradict Rabbi Yitzchak's principle. The Gemara answers consistently: *hacha nami b'tzarich le-makomo* — here too, it means that the person moved the vessel because he needed the space, and then placed it down upon the egg.

### Stones Toilet

The Gemara cites a case where one may spread mats over stones on Shabbat — presumably to protect them from rain. One might expect the Gemara to invoke the *tzarich le-makomo* solution. Instead, it explains: we are speaking of *avanim mekurzalot*, stones that have been sharpened or shaped for use in the toilet. Such stones have been designated for a practical bodily function and are therefore not *muktzeh* at all. Since the stones themselves are permitted, there is no prohibition in covering them.

### Bricks Leftover

A Mishnah permits spreading mats over bricks on Shabbat. Again, rather than applying *tzarich le-makomo*, the Gemara explains that these are *de-ishtayaru mi-binyana* — bricks left over from a completed construction project, which are no longer earmarked for building use. People recline on such bricks; *de-chazei le-mizga alaihu* — they are suitable for leaning upon. Since they are usable and people do use them, they are not *muktzeh*.

### Beehive Honey

The Gemara then takes up a more complex case. One may spread a mat over a beehive on Shabbat, to protect it from sun or rain — as long as one does not intend to trap the bees. The bees are *muktzeh* like other animals; the concern would seem to apply. The Gemara proposes that the mat is being moved to protect not the bees but the honey in the hive — which is not *muktzeh*. The difficulty arises when the question turns to the rainy season, when, as the Gemara understands it, there is no honey being produced — only two loaves (*shtei chalot*) of stored honey left behind for the bees to sustain themselves through winter.

### Honey Intention

The Gemara addresses this by reasoning that those two loaves of honey are themselves *muktzeh* — they have been designated for the bees and the owner would never normally take them for his own use. However, the Gemara introduces a resolution: *de-chashiv alaihu* — if the owner had, before Shabbat, considered taking some of that honey for himself, then the honey is not *muktzeh* relative to him and the mat may be moved to protect it. This determination is made before Shabbat begins, and it is the owner's intent that transforms the honey's status.

### Trapping Allowed

A question arises about the Beraita's condition that one must not intend to trap the bees. If the entire permissibility depends on the honey not being *muktzeh* due to prior intent, why does the Beraita not simply contrast the case where the owner intended to use the honey with the case where he did not, rather than pivoting to the issue of trapping? The Gemara answers that the Beraita wishes to address the case where the owner did form the requisite intention — and in that scenario, the mat may be spread — but to add that even in that permitted case, one must ensure the mat is not set down in a way that will function as a trap. *Shelo ya'asenu ke-metzudah* — do not make it like a snare; *delishbok lehu revacha* — leave them an opening, *ki heichi de-lo litzudu* — so that they will not be trapped. The instruction is thus not about interior mental states — not an instruction about what to intend — but about how physically to position the mat.

The Gemara then asks: whose position is the Beraita following? If it is Rabbi Shimon, who does not generally hold of *muktzeh* and who permits unintended acts (*davar she-eino mitkaven*) provided the negative outcome is not inevitable, then the entire *muktzeh* apparatus is irrelevant and the Beraita need not have been taught at all. If it is Rabbi Yehuda, who does hold of *muktzeh* and who also prohibits unintended acts even when not inevitable, then why should "not intending" to trap protect one from the prohibition of trapping? The Gemara resolves that it is indeed Rabbi Yehuda — and that "not intending to trap" here means not arranging the mat in a manner that will trap, rather than a subjective caveat about mental intent.

### Rav Ashi Sun Rain

Rav Ashi offers a more elegant resolution to the entire beehive discussion. The Beraita's formulation — protect the hive *from the sun* and *from the rain* — does not specify summer or winter. It specifies sun and rain. Rav Ashi teaches: *beyomei Nisan u-veyomei Tishrei*, in the spring and fall months, there are days that are sunny and days with rain. In those seasons, the hive is full of honey that the owner could use for himself, so the honey is not *muktzeh* — and the entire difficulty about the two stored winter loaves does not arise.

## Rav Huna Citation

### Corpse Principle

Rav Sheshet addresses Rabbi Yitzchak: go tell him, he says to his students, that Rav Huna already anticipated his principle in Babylon. The principle *ein kli nittal ela le-davar ha-nittal* was not a new teaching — Rav Huna had already applied it in a concrete context. Rav Huna's formulation: *osin mechitzah le-met be-Shabbat bishvil chai, ve-ein osin mechitzah le-met bishvil met* — one may erect a partition for the sake of a corpse on Shabbat if it is done for the sake of the living people present, but not directly for the sake of the corpse. The corpse is *muktzeh*, and one cannot move or arrange items directly for the sake of *muktzeh*. Just as Rabbi Yitzchak taught that a vessel cannot be moved for a *muktzeh* object, Rav Huna had already applied the same reasoning to the case of protecting a *met* — and he did so in Babylon before Rabbi Yitzchak's formulation reached him.

## Rav Huna Citation (II)

### Corpse Story

The Gemara elaborates on how Rav Huna's principle plays out in practice. Rabbi Shmuel bar Yehuda and Shila Mari both taught the following scenario: if a corpse lies exposed in the sun and must be shielded, two people come and seat themselves beside it. They position themselves near the body, and their presence begins to shade it somewhat. They find that sitting on the hot ground is uncomfortable, so each brings his bed and sits upon it — the bed is being moved for the sake of the living person, not the corpse. Now they find that the sun is bearing down on them from above, so they bring mats and hold them overhead as an awning — since the mat is held by their arms and not by freestanding walls, it does not constitute an *ohel*. Having now established themselves with beds on either side of the body and mats extended over their heads, each person then has reason to leave. Before departing, each one flips his bed upright and slips away. The result is two vertical beds flanking the corpse, with the mat resting on top — a protected enclosure. The elaborate sequence is necessary because one must not create the protective structure directly for the *met*: the beds are moved for the living persons, and the awning is created in a reverse order — covering first, walls after — which is the halachically sanctioned method for forming a temporary *ohel* on Shabbat.

## Corpse Movement

### Bed Brigade

The Gemara now turns to the separate question of how a corpse lying in the sun on Shabbat may be moved when no other solution is available. Rav Yehuda, citing Shmuel, proposes: *hofcho mi-mitah le-mitah* — the corpse may be transferred from one bed to the next in a kind of bed brigade, with people passing it along until it reaches shelter. The degree to which this constitutes *kevod ha-met* is admittedly difficult to appreciate, but it is the method Shmuel permits.

### Infant Loaf

The alternative approach, cited by Rabbi Chanina bar Shmaya in the name of Rav, is: *maniach alav kikar o tinok u-metatlelto* — one places a loaf of bread or an infant on the corpse, and then moves the corpse ostensibly because one is moving the loaf or the infant. This is a permitted technique for the unique case of the *met*, and the Gemara emphasizes its exceptional character. Normally, placing a non-*muktzeh* object atop a *muktzeh* object does not transform the *muktzeh* object into something movable — one is still fundamentally moving the *muktzeh* item, and the presence of a loaf or child on top changes nothing. This technique is permitted only because of the special concern for the dignity of the dead. This is distinct from the principle that a tray bearing both *muktzeh* and permitted items is not itself *muktzeh* — there, the tray is not intrinsically *muktzeh*; its status depends on what it bears. But the corpse is intrinsically *muktzeh*; it does not shed that status because something non-*muktzeh* rests upon it. The Rabbis made an exception for the *met* out of the pressing concern to treat it with dignity.

### Tiltul Debate

The Gemara concludes with a dispute applicable when neither a loaf nor an infant is available to place on the corpse. Where both are present, all opinions agree the technique is valid. The disagreement is about *tiltul min ha-tzad* — indirect movement. The bed-brigade method of Rav Yehuda represents a form of indirect movement: one is not directly carrying the corpse but tipping it from bed to bed, so that the primary action is the movement of the bed and the corpse moves incidentally. One opinion holds *tiltul min ha-tzad shmei tiltul* — indirect movement is still considered movement and is prohibited for *muktzeh*. The other holds *lo shmei tiltul* — indirect movement is not halachically equivalent to direct movement and is therefore permitted.

It is worth noting that even the lenient opinion here does not sanction simply carrying the *met* by means of an intermediary like a bed that one physically holds — that would still constitute direct movement of the *muktzeh* corpse. *Tiltul min ha-tzad* in its precise form means that one performs an action primarily directed at something else, and the *muktzeh* object moves only as a side consequence — as when reaching for an apple one's arm incidentally nudges a wallet aside. The bed-brigade method, understood properly, approximates that structure: one focuses on repositioning the bed itself, and the corpse moves with it as a secondary effect. Whether that approximation is sufficient to escape the prohibition is the crux of the debate, which will be resolved in the following session.
