# Sukkah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Third Wall Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹתוֹ טֶפַח הֵיכָן מַעֲמִידוֹ? אָמַר רַב: מַעֲמִידוֹ כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: According to the opinion that a *sukka* can be built with two full-fledged walls and a third that is one handbreadth, **where does one position that** third wall that measures one **handbreadth? Rav said: He positions it** at the end of one of the standing walls **opposite** the wall **that emerges** from the other end of that wall.

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 6b, where we were introduced to the ruling of Rabbi Shimon that a *sukkah* requires two full walls and a third wall of even a single *tefach*. The *gemara* has until now been focused on the technical derivations of this position from the word *sukkah* as it appears in the *pasuk*, though there was also an intimation of a deeper issue — whether a *sukkah* is merely meant to provide *tzel*, shade, or whether it must provide more general protection, as suggested by the verse in Yeshayahu describing God's canopy as a shelter from storms. That theme will be revisited later. For now, the *gemara* establishes that the *halacha* follows the position of two walls plus a *tefach*, and turns to the question of placement: *ve-ota tefach heichan mamidoh* — where do you position that extra *tefach*?

Rav answers: *mamidoh keneged ha-yotze* — you place it opposite the protrusion, meaning at the beginning of the third wall, running perpendicular to the two complete walls. The logic is intuitive. Two walls alone do not suggest the idea of enclosure. But when a third wall begins — even just a *tefach* — the imagination can square off the space and perceive what its parameters would be. The mind's eye can complete the structure. The *tefach* is therefore placed at the corner, starting the third wall in the normal rectangular direction, suggesting the outline of an enclosed dwelling.

### Rav Kahana Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: וְיַעֲמִידֶנּוּ כְּנֶגֶד רֹאשׁ תּוֹר?! שְׁתֵיק רַב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav:** And let him position the wall measuring one handbreadth **opposite** the wall that emerges like the diagonal **line** formed by **the end** of the furrows as the field gradually narrows. This third partition would represent the third side of a triangle and would make the *sukka* appear more like a full-fledged structure, as the diagonal would represent closure of both unwalled directions. **Rav was silent** and did not respond.

Rav Kahana and Rav Assi posed an objection to Rav: *yamidenna keneged ha-yotze, keneged rosh tor* — why not place it opposite the head of a line, that is, at an angle forming what Rashi describes as a *rosh tor*, the pointed turn at the end of a furrow in a field? The suggestion is that placing the *tefach* at a diagonal angle would actually create a greater sense of enclosure, since the viewer would not need to imagine two virtual lines converging — the angled placement would suggest a triangular convergence more naturally. Some commentators understand this to mean placing the *tefach* further out along the diagonal, which would create an even stronger impression of being surrounded, albeit at the cost of implying a triangular *sukkah*. That is precisely the problem. A triangular *sukkah* is not the natural model of a dwelling. Rav's silence — *shatik Rav* — indicates he had no ready response, but his original intention was clearly to preserve the rectangular model.

### Consensus Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: מַעֲמִידוֹ כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹצֵא. וְכֵן מוֹרִין בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא: מַעֲמִידוֹ כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **Shmuel said in the name of Levi: He positions it** at the end of one of the standing walls **opposite** the wall **that emerges** from the other end of that wall. **And similarly, they rule in the study hall: He positions it** at the end of one of the standing walls **opposite** the wall **that emerges** from the other end of that wall.

Despite Rav's silence in the face of this challenge, the *gemara* records that Shmuel independently transmitted the same ruling in the name of Levi: *mamidoh keneged ha-yotze*, the *tefach* is placed opposite the protrusion in the standard perpendicular direction. And *veken morin be-bei midrashah* — this is also how they rule in the house of study. This closing phrase is always noteworthy, conveying a kind of communal consensus beyond any single named authority. The *pesak* is clear: the third-wall *tefach* is placed in the normal perpendicular fashion, beginning the rectangular third wall. One might ask whether this was merely theoretical or whether such cases arose in practice. It is quite plausible that scarcity of building materials forced people to ask in earnest how to construct a *sukkah* at the bare minimum.

## Laughing Tefach

### Rabbi Simon's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי סִימוֹן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ טֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, וּמַעֲמִידוֹ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים סָמוּךְ לַדּוֹפֶן, וְכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַדּוֹפֶן כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Simon** said, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** who **said,** that the third wall is positioned differently. **He establishes for** the third side a wall that measures **an expansive handbreadth,** measured with the fingers spread apart, which is slightly larger than a standard handbreadth. **And he** then **positions it less than three handbreadths** from and **adjacent to the wall** opposite the second wall. **And** the legal status of **any** item positioned **less than three** handbreadths from and **adjacent to the wall is like** an item **joined** to that wall. In this way, the handbreadth-wide wall is joined to the adjacent wall, and it is as if it is a wall of four handbreadths, which is the majority of the minimum measure of the wall of a full-fledged *sukka*, seven handbreadths.

Rav Simon — and some say Rav Yehoshua ben Levi — introduces a refinement: *oseh lo tefach socheik* — one makes a *tefach socheik*, a "laughing" *tefach*. The image is vivid: when one laughs, the fingers spread slightly apart from one another. A *tefach* is the width of four fingers, but a laughing *tefach* is four fingers spread a little — slightly more than a standard *tefach*, call it 1.1 *tefachim*. Beyond this, *u-maamido be-pachos mi-shlosha tefachim samuch la-dofen* — one positions it within less than three *tefachim* of the adjacent wall. The principle of *lavud* then applies: anything within three *tefachim* of a wall is considered legally joined to it, *ke-lavud dami*. 

The result is that this slightly-oversized *tefach*, placed within three of the wall, creates through *lavud* an effective wall of four *tefachim*. Rav Simon agrees with Rav that the placement is perpendicular and rectangular, but he wants to produce a more substantial wall through the mechanics of *lavud*.

### Four Tefachim Significance

The significance of four *tefachim* deserves attention. The minimum *sukkah* must accommodate *rosho rubo ve-shulchano* — one's head, most of one's body, and one's table — which works out to approximately seven and a half *tefachim*. Four *tefachim* is therefore already the majority of the minimum *sukkah* wall. This matters because a wall of four *tefachim* — the majority of the required minimum — is sufficient to define a wall that "creates" a *sukkah*. The entire *sukkah* space is thereby *kasher*, not only a portion of it. The *chiddush* of Rav Simon is thus that by using a laughing *tefach* combined with *lavud*, one attains a wall representing the majority of the third wall required for a minimum-sized *sukkah*, significantly strengthening the halachic standing of the structure.

## Parallel Walls Case

### Two Parallel Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סוּכָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה כְּמָבוֹי — כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאוֹתוֹ טֶפַח מַעֲמִידוֹ לְכׇל רוּחַ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: A *sukka* constructed like an alleyway,** with two parallel full-fledged walls, is **fit, and** with regard to **that** third wall that measures one **handbreadth, he positions it** adjacent to one of the walls **in any direction that he chooses,** as it is merely a conspicuous marker.

The preceding cases all began with two walls that meet at a right angle — the classic perpendicular configuration. Rav Yehuda now introduces a different scenario: *sukkah ha-asuyah ke-mavoi kesherah* — a *sukkah* constructed like an alleyway, with two long walls running parallel to each other, is *kasher*. As for the third partial wall, *ve-hahu tefach maamido be-chol ruach she-yirtzeh* — that *tefach* may be placed in any of the four directions the builder wishes, at any one of the four corners. The implication is that even when the two main walls are parallel rather than connected, a single *tefach* at any corner suffices. The *chiddush* here is considerable: parallel walls offer less of a visual suggestion of enclosure than perpendicular ones. When the two full walls are parallel and potentially very distant from one another, one cannot easily discern the shape or dimensions of the enclosed space until it is virtually completed. Nevertheless, Rav Yehuda maintains that the entire *sukkah* is *kasher* — not merely the corner where the *tefach* was placed, but the whole space — because the fundamental requirement is two walls plus a bit, and that requirement is met.

### Parallel vs Perpendicular

This ruling immediately raises the question of why the parallel case requires more than a single standard *tefach*, as the *gemara* goes on to clarify. In the perpendicular case, the two existing walls already meet at a corner, sketching out two dimensions of an enclosed space. The single *tefach* — even with *lavud* yielding only four *tefachim* — is sufficient because it builds on a configuration that already suggests a structure. In the parallel case, there is no connected corner to begin with. The two walls run alongside each other without intersecting, and no corner of the space is defined until an actual perpendicular wall is created.

### Four Plus Levud Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי סִימוֹן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ פַּס אַרְבָּעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וּמַעֲמִידוֹ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַדּוֹפֶן, וְכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַדּוֹפֶן כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Simon** said, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** who **said:** In the case of a *sukka* built like an alleyway open on two ends, a third wall measuring a single handbreadth is insufficient. Rather, **one establishes for** the third side **a board** with a width of **four** handbreadths **and a bit and positions it less than three** handbreadths from and **adjacent to** either **wall,** as a wall on either of the open ends. **And** the legal status of **any** item positioned **less than three** handbreadths from and **adjacent to the wall is like** an item **joined** to that wall. The result is a full-fledged seven-handbreadth *sukka* wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ סַגִּיא טֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ בָּעֲיָא פַּס אַרְבָּעָה? הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא שְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת כְּהִלְכָתָן — סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּטֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, הָכָא דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת, אִי אִיכָּא פַּס אַרְבָּעָה — אֵין, אִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different there,** in the case of two attached walls, **where you say** that a wall with the dimension of **an expansive handbreadth suffices** to complete the third wall, **and what is different here where you say** that **it requires a board** that measures **four** handbreadths and a bit? The Gemara answers: **There, where there are two walls in the standard sense,** as they are attached forming a type of structure, **it is sufficient to have** the third wall measure **an expansive handbreadth** in order to render the *sukka* fit; however, **here, where there are not two walls** in the standard sense, as they are not attached, **if there is a board** that measures **four** handbreadths as the third wall, **yes,** it is fit, and **if not, no,** it is unfit.

Rabbi Simon addresses the parallel case directly. He rules that a single *tefach* will not suffice here; one needs *pas arba'ah u-mashehu* — a plank of four-plus *tefachim* — placed within less than three of the adjacent wall, so that *lavud* brings the total to seven *tefachim*. Seven *tefachim* is the minimum wall size of a legitimate *sukkah*. Here, then, one is not relying on the special *halacha* of the third wall being a *tefach*; rather, one is creating an actual legitimate perpendicular wall through the ordinary halachic mechanism of *lavud*. Normal wall *halachot* produce a genuine seven-*tefach* wall, thereby giving the *sukkah* a real connected corner from which the parallel configuration can be read as a proper enclosure. The *gemara* states this explicitly: *hatam de-ika shtei defanot ke-hilchasan, sagai lei be-tefach socheik* — when you begin with two properly connected perpendicular walls, the special *halacha* of the *tefach* suffices. *Hacha de-leka shtei defanot* — when you do not have two connected walls, you must actually create a legitimate perpendicular wall, and only a plank of four *tefachim* plus *lavud* achieving seven will accomplish that.

## Rava's Objection

### Tzurat Hapesach Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְאֵינָהּ נִתֶּרֶת אֶלָּא בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** the *sukka* consisting of two adjacent walls with a third wall measuring one handbreadth **is permitted** and fit **only** if the third wall is **in** the **form of a doorway.** One can render the sukka fit only by splitting the one-handbreadth wall and attaching one half to the standing wall and one half across from the other wall that emerges from the standing wall, and then placing a pole across the two halves. By creating the form of a doorway, that third wall becomes like an open gate, which is considered a halakhic partition.

Having worked through the question of the *tefach*'s placement and size, the *gemara* now encounters a more fundamental challenge, introduced by Rava: *ve-eina niteres ela b'tzurat hapesach* — the *sukkah* cannot be validated except through a *tzurat hapesach*. This statement marks a significant conceptual pullback from the straightforward *tefach* position.

A *tzurat hapesach* is the shape of a doorframe: two vertical posts with a horizontal beam resting on top of them. This structure, familiar from the laws of *eruv*, constitutes a halachic wall because a doorway is part of a wall, and the *tzurat hapesach* simulates a doorway. It is this principle that underlies the entire institution of the *eruv* — we surround an area with symbolic doorframes, treating each as a segment of wall. The *tzurat hapesach* as a halachic wall is not a novelty invented for *sukkah*; it is a general wall-*halacha*. Rava's innovation is to insist that the *tefach* construct must somehow incorporate or be replaced by this sturdier halachic concept.

### Rava Version 1

The first version of Rava's position is a radical reinterpretation: when the earlier sources speak of a *tefach*, they do not mean a literal plank of one *tefach* at all. What they really mean is a *tzurat hapesach* constructed across the entire opening of the third wall. On this reading, the two half-*tefachim* posts of the doorframe structure together account for the "one *tefach*" mentioned in the sources, but the operative mechanism is the *tzurat hapesach* spanning the third side. Rava in this version entirely rejects the notion that a bare *tefach* plank possesses independent halachic force as a third wall.

### Rava Version 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: וְנִתֶּרֶת נָמֵי בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rava said: And** a *sukka* consisting of two adjacent walls **is also permitted** and fit if the third wall is **in** the **form of a doorway.** In other words, Rava does not reject the remedy of the expansive-handbreadth wall suggested by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi; rather, he suggests an alternative.

A second formulation of Rava's position, introduced with *ika d'amri*, takes a more moderate stance: *uniteres nami b'tzurat hapesach* — it is also validated by a *tzurat hapesach*. On this reading, the *tefach* retains its validity as an independent option; the *tzurat hapesach* is offered as a legitimate alternative. One may either use the *tefach* in the standard placement, or one may instead construct a *tzurat hapesach* across the third wall — either suffices. This version acknowledges the *tefach* construct as genuinely operative and introduces the *tzurat hapesach* as a parallel path. As noted, this is not much of a *chiddush* in isolation, since a *tzurat hapesach* always constitutes a wall; the novelty is only in presenting it as interchangeable with the *tefach*.

### Rava Version 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: וּצְרִיכָא נָמֵי צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** a third version of that which **Rava said: And** a *sukka* consisting of two adjacent walls, even with a third that is an expansive handbreadth wide as suggested by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **also requires the form of a doorway** to be fit. In other words, in addition to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's remedy, one must also create the form of a doorway to render the *sukka* fit.

The third version of Rava's position occupies the middle ground between the first two: *tzericha nami tzurat hapesach* — the *tzurat hapesach* is also required alongside the *tefach*. Neither alone is sufficient; both are needed together. On this view, one begins with the laughing *tefach* placed within three *tefachim* of the wall, but then must complete the third wall by extending a full *tzurat hapesach* the rest of the way across. The *tzurat hapesach* is not meant to run only the distance between the *tefach* and the wall, but — as Rashi explains — all the way to the opposite wall. This means Rava in versions one and three is effectively demanding a complete third wall, not a symbolic *tefach*. Only in version two does he genuinely acknowledge that the *tefach* alone can do the job.

A striking implication emerges from versions one and three: if one must construct a full *tzurat hapesach* across the third wall in any case, then the special *halacha* of the *tefach* contributes nothing beyond what ordinary wall-*halachot* would already require. A *tzurat hapesach* is a wall by standard *halacha*; the *tefach* plus *lavud* represents ordinary wall mechanics as well. There is no distinct *chiddush* of the third-wall *tefach* if version three is the operative ruling. The pullback from the *tefach* idea is thus almost complete.

### Rav Ashi Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב כָּהֲנָא דְּקָא עָבֵיד טֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, וְקָא עָבֵיד צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר מָר לְהָא דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: וְנִתֶּרֶת נָמֵי בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא כְּאִידַּךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּרָבָא סְבִירָא לִי. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: וּצְרִיכָא נָמֵי צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Ashi found Rav Kahana** establishing his *sukka*, which had two adjacent walls, **and establishing** a third wall that was **an expansive handbreadth** wide **and establishing the form of a doorway** as well. Rav Ashi **said to** him: **And does the Master not hold** in accordance **with that** opinion **of Rava, as Rava said: And** the *sukka* **is also permitted** and fit if the third wall is **in** the **form of a doorway?** Why are you establishing a wall that is an expansive handbreadth wide as well? Rav Kahana **said to him: I hold** in accordance **with the other version** of the opinion of **Rava, as Rava said: And** the *sukka* **also requires the form of a doorway,** in addition to the expansive handbreadth, to be fit.

The Gemara closes this discussion with a practical illustration. Rav Ashi found Rav Kahana constructing a *sukkah* in which he made the laughing *tefach* and simultaneously erected a full *tzurat hapesach* — precisely the practice prescribed by version three of Rava. Rav Ashi challenged him: does the master not hold like Rava's second formulation, which allows either the *tefach* or the *tzurat hapesach* as independent alternatives? Rav Kahana replied that he holds like Rava's third formulation — *tzericha nami tzurat hapesach* — the *tefach* alone is insufficient, and the *tzurat hapesach* is required to complete the wall. He therefore combined both elements.

## Sukkah vs Shabbat

### Migu Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתַּיִם כְּהִלְכָתָן כּוּ׳. אָמַר רָבָא: וְכֵן לְשַׁבָּת, מִגּוֹ דְּהָוְיָא דּוֹפֶן לְעִנְיַן סוּכָּה — הָוְיָא דּוֹפֶן לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the *Tosefta* that if the *sukka* has **two walls in the standard sense** and a third wall that measures one handbreadth, it is fit. **Rava said: And likewise** with regard **to Shabbat** that occurs during the festival of *Sukkot*, **since it is** considered **a wall with regard to** the *halakhot* of ***sukka* it is** considered **a partition with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat.** Were one to construct a *sukka* in that manner in a public domain adjacent to the entrance to his house, its legal status would be that of a private domain and one would be permitted to move objects from it to his house and vice versa on Shabbat that occurs during the Festival. However, that structure is not considered a private domain on any other Shabbat.

Having established the *halacha* of two-walls-and-a-*tefach* for *sukkah*, the *gemara* turns to its application on *Shabbat* through the principle of *migu*. Rava — or, as some emend the text given Rava's position above, Rabbah — states: *veken l'Shabbat*. The same configuration that works as a third wall for *sukkah* will work as a wall for *Shabbat* purposes as well. *Migo d'havei dofen l'inyan Sukkah havei dofen l'inyan Shabbat* — since it constitutes a wall for the category of *sukkah*, it constitutes a wall for the category of *Shabbat*. This is the principle of *migu*: once a structure is recognized as a wall in one halachic domain, that recognition carries over into another domain when the two intersect.

The text notes an apparent tension with Rava's own positions above, where he largely rejected the bare *tefach* as a third wall. Some therefore emend *rava* to *rabbah* in this passage to resolve the difficulty. Under this emendation, Rabbah holds that the *tefach* genuinely works for *sukkah*, and therefore argues that it works for *Shabbat* as well when the two intersect on *Shabbat Sukkot*.

### Baraita Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִי אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ? וְהָתַנְיָא: דּוֹפֶן סוּכָּה כְּדוֹפֶן שַׁבָּת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין קָנֶה לַחֲבֵרוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rava's opinion from a *baraita*: **And do we say** that this principle: **Since** it is considered, etc., applies in this area of *halakha*? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The specifications of the **wall of a *sukka*** are **like** those of a **partition** with regard to the *halakhot* **of Shabbat.** Just as with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, one forms a partition by establishing adjacent reeds, so too, one forms the wall of a *sukka* in the same manner, **provided that** the gap **from one reed to another will not be** as much as **three handbreadths.** If the gap is three handbreadths or greater, the legal status of the reeds is that they are not considered joined.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיתֵירָה, שַׁבָּת עַל סוּכָּה, שֶׁהַשַּׁבָּת אֵינָהּ נִתֶּרֶת אֶלָּא בְּעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּסוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** the stringency of the *halakha* with regard to **Shabbat** goes **beyond** the stringency of the *halakha* with regard to ***sukka*,** in terms of the criteria for effective partitions, **as** with regard to **Shabbat,** carrying **is permitted only in** a case where the total of **the standing** segments of the partition, the actual wall, **is greater than** the total of **the breached** segments of the partition, the gaps that are less than three handbreadths. **That is not so with regard to the *sukka*,** where, even if the breached segments total more than the standing segments, e.g., a *sukka* consisting of two walls in which there are gaps and a third wall measuring only a single handbreadth, it is still fit.

Abaye challenges the *migu* claim with a *baraita*: *dofen suka k'dofen Shabbat* — the walls of a *sukkah* are comparable to the walls of *Shabbat* — and the *baraita* proceeds to specify two points of divergence. First, both *sukkah* and *Shabbat* walls permit *lavud*: gaps of less than three *tefachim* between posts or horizontal strings are considered closed. So far they are equivalent. Second, and critically, *yesera Shabbat al ha-Suka* — *Shabbat* has an additional demand that *sukkah* does not: *she-Shabbat einah niteret ela b'omed merubeh al haparutz* — a *Shabbat* enclosure is only valid if the solid or *lavud*-closed portions of the wall exceed the open gaps. By *sukkah*, no such requirement exists.

To illustrate: a *sukkah* with the two-and-a-*tefach* configuration might have considerably more open space than closed space — the actual physical wall amounts to only slightly more than two sides out of a notional four. This would be *kasher* for *sukkah* but would not constitute a *reshut ha-yachid* for *Shabbat*, because the *omed* does not exceed the *parutz*. The distinction is real and the *baraita* appears to undercut the *migu* claim, since it demonstrates that *sukkah* walls and *Shabbat* walls operate under different standards.

### Shabbat Sukkot Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו: יְתֵירָה שַׁבָּת דְּסוּכָּה אַסּוּכָּה, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **What, isn't** the *baraita* **teaching that** the stringency with regard to **Shabbat that** occurs **during** the festival of ***Sukkot*** goes **beyond** the stringency with regard to the rest of the festival of ***Sukkot*? And** apparently, **we do not say** the principle: **Since** it is considered a fit partition for *sukka* let it also be considered a fit partition for Shabbat. This is difficult according to Rava, in whose opinion that principle is applied in this case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא: יְתֵירָה שַׁבָּת דְּעָלְמָא עַל שַׁבָּת דְּסוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava rejects that interpretation of the *baraita*. **No,** the *baraita* is teaching **that** the stringency with regard to **Shabbat in general** goes **beyond** the stringency with regard to **Shabbat that** occurs **during** the festival of ***Sukkot*.** On Shabbat during the festival of *Sukkot*, a partition where the total of the breached segments of the partition is greater than the total of the standing segments is effective, as, since it is effective as a wall in a *sukka*, it is effective as a partition for Shabbat as well. That is not the case on Shabbat during the rest of the year, when a partition of that sort is ineffective on Shabbat.

The *gemara* resolves this by clarifying what the *baraita*'s comparison actually addresses. The statement *yesera Shabbat al ha-Suka* is not comparing *Shabbat Sukkot* to *sukkah* on the rest of *Sukkot*; it is comparing general *Shabbat* standards to the *Shabbat Sukkot* context. The configuration with more open than closed space does not work for *Shabbat* during the rest of the year. On *Sukkot*, the very same configuration counts as a *sukkah* for all days, and on *Shabbat Sukkot* specifically, because it is recognized as a *sukkah* wall, the *migu* principle activates and it also counts as a *reshut ha-yachid* for *Shabbat* purposes. The two categories are not permanently equivalent — they converge only on *Shabbat Sukkot*.

### Shabbat Sukkot Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: יְתֵירָה סוּכָּה דְעָלְמָא אַסּוּכָּה דְשַׁבָּת, דְּאִילּוּ סוּכָּה דְעָלְמָא בָּעֲיָא טֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, וְאִילּוּ סוּכָּה דְשַׁבָּת לָא בָּעֲיָא טֶפַח שׂוֹחֵק, וְסַגִּי בְּלֶחִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye asked: **If** it is **so** that the distinction in the *baraita* is not a fundamental distinction between the *halakhot* of *sukka* and the *halakhot* of Shabbat, but is instead a distinction between the *halakhot* of Shabbat in general and the specific case of Shabbat during the festival of *Sukkot*, then **let** the *baraita* **also teach** a novel distinction involving Shabbat that occurs during the festival of *Sukkot*. The stringency with regard to ***sukka* in general** during the rest of *Sukkot* goes **beyond** the stringency with regard to ***sukka* on Shabbat** that occurs during the festival of *Sukkot*, **as a *sukka* in general** consisting of two parallel walls, like an alleyway, **requires** that its third wall measure **an expansive handbreadth, while a *sukka* on Shabbat** does **not require an expansive handbreadth** for this purpose, **and** it is **sufficient** for the third wall to be established **with a side post** ten handbreadths high and any width.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי מָבוֹי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לֶחִי — כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** Since the side post is effective as a partition with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, it should also be effective as a wall with regard to the *halakhot* of *sukka* although it is less than one handbreadth wide, **as you are** the **one who said: If one placed roofing over an alleyway which has a side post** on one of the open ends to permit carrying in that alleyway on Shabbat, **it is fit** as a *sukka* for that same Shabbat, although it would not be fit during the rest of the week of the Festival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא לָא אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, הַשְׁתָּא מִקִּילְּתָא לַחֲמִירְתָּא אָמְרִינַן, מֵחֲמִירְתָּא לְקִילְּתָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava replied to him: That is indeed my statement; however, the fact is that the *baraita* does not cite that distinction, because **it is not necessary** to state that there are circumstances in which the general *halakhot* of *sukka* are stricter than its *halakhot* on Shabbat, as there is no novelty in the concept that the *halakhot* of partitions on Shabbat should apply to a *sukka*. **Now** that **we say** that *halakhot* may be derived **from a leniency to a stringency,** as a *halakha* that applies to *sukka*, which is a positive mitzva, is applied to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, which is a stringent prohibition punishable by *karet*; then **from a stringency,** the *halakhot* of Shabbat, **to a leniency, all the more so** may *halakhot* be derived. Therefore, there is no reason for the *baraita* to mention that distinction explicitly.

The *migu* discussion also generates a converse scenario: *yeseira Sukkah de-alma a-Sukkah de-Shabbat* — something that works for *sukkah* on the rest of *Sukkot* but not for *Shabbat* in general. Should we not also list the reverse case, where something that works for *Shabbat* should work for *sukkah* on *Shabbat Sukkot*? The case in question involves a *sukkah* built over a *mavoi* fitted with a *lechi* — a post that serves as its fourth-wall marker. For *Shabbat*, this *lechi* constitutes a virtual fourth wall, creating a *reshut ha-yachid*. On *Shabbat Sukkot*, therefore, if one placed *schach* only at the front of the *mavoi*, that post — recognized as a wall for *Shabbat* — works as a third wall for *sukkah* on *Shabbat Sukkot*.

The *gemara* explains why this case need not be enumerated separately alongside the *migu* of *sukkah*-to-*Shabbat*: *hashta mikelita l'khamirta amrinan, me'khamirta l'kelita lo kol sheken* — if we say that from the lenient to the stringent — from *sukkah* to *Shabbat* — the *migu* applies, then all the more obviously from the stringent to the lenient — from *Shabbat* to *sukkah* — it applies. The greater *chiddush* is moving from the lenient category to the strict one; the converse follows automatically.

## Mavoi & Bira'ot

### Mavoi with Lechi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גּוּפָא. אָמַר רָבָא: סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי מָבוֹי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לֶחִי — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos roofing over an alleyway, the Gemara elaborates about **the** matter **itself. Rava said:** If **one placed roofing over an alleyway that has a side post, it is fit** for use as a *sukka*.

Rava states: *schach al gabei mavoi she-yesh lo lechi kesherah* — if one places *schach* over a *mavoi* that has a *lechi*, the *sukkah* is *kasher* on *Shabbat Sukkot*. The *mavoi* has two long parallel walls; the *lechi* is recognized for *Shabbat* purposes as a virtual fourth wall. On *Shabbat Sukkot*, the *migu* principle activates: since these walls are valid *Shabbat* boundaries, they count as *sukkah* walls as well, and the structure is a *kasher sukkah*.

### Psei Bira'ot Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **Rava said: If one placed roofing over** upright **boards** surrounding **wells, it is fit** for use as a *sukka*. A well is usually at least four handbreadths wide and ten handbreadths deep. Therefore, it is considered a private domain, and it is prohibited to draw water from it on Shabbat, as that would constitute a violation of the prohibition to carry from a private domain into a public one. In order to permit drawing water from the well, the surrounding area must be partitioned off and rendered a private domain. For the benefit of Festival pilgrims, the Sages instituted a special leniency that full-fledged partitions need not be constructed around the well for this purpose. Rather, it is sufficient if there are four double posts at the four corners of the area surrounding the well. Since these symbolic barriers are considered partitions for the *halakhot* of Shabbat, they are considered partitions for the *halakhot* of *sukka* on Shabbat as well.

Rava adds a second case: *schach al gabei pasei bira'ot kesherah* — placing *schach* over the frame of a well structure is also *kasher* on *Shabbat Sukkot*. The framework of *pasei bira'ot* consists of four partial corner-posts, each an *amah* by an *amah*, positioned at the corners of a square enclosure, with large open gaps — up to thirteen *amot* — between them. The Rabbis permitted this configuration as a *reshut ha-yachid* specifically to allow drawing water from wells. Placing *schach* over this framework on *Shabbat Sukkot* produces a *kasher sukkah* by *migu*, since the corner-posts are recognized as walls for *Shabbat*.

### Three Cases Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מָבוֹי, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא שְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת מְעַלְּיָיתָא. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת, דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת מְעַלְּיָיתָא — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** for Rava to state the *halakha* in each of the two similar cases, **as if he** had **taught us** only that the *sukka* is fit in the case of the **alleyway,** one could say that it is **due to** the fact **that there are two full-fledged walls; however,** in the case of upright **boards** surrounding **wells, where there are not two full-fledged walls** and most of the area is breached, **say no,** it is not considered a fit *sukka*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם אַרְבַּע דְפָנוֹת, אֲבָל סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי מָבוֹי, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם אַרְבַּע דְפָנוֹת — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he** had **taught us** only the case of upright **boards** surrounding **wells,** one could say that it is **due to** the fact **that** in that case **it is** in the **category of** a *sukka* with **four,** albeit virtual, **walls; however,** in the case where **one placed roofing over an alleyway, where it is not** in the **category of** a *sukka* with **four walls, say no,** it is not considered a fit *sukka*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מֵחֲמִירְתָּא לְקִילְּתָא, אֲבָל מִקִּילְּתָא לַחֲמִירְתָּא — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he** had **taught us** only **these two** cases, to teach that a partition with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat is a partition with regard to the *halakhot* of *sukka*, one could say that it is due to the fact that one can derive a *halakha* **from a stringency,** the *halakhot* of Shabbat, **to a leniency,** the *halakhot* of *sukka*; **however,** to derive a *halakha* **from a leniency to a stringency, say no.** Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach the third *halakha* with regard to a *sukka* consisting of two walls in the standard sense and a third wall measuring a handbreadth: Since the third wall is considered a wall with regard to the *halakhot* of *sukka*, a leniency, it is considered a wall with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, a stringency.

The *gemara* asks why all three cases — the open-space *sukkah* from the *baraita*, the *mavoi* case, and the *pasei bira'ot* case — must each be stated explicitly. Could not one or two suffice?

The answer is that each case makes a distinct point. Had we only been told the *mavoi* case — where one begins with two real full walls — one might think the *migu* works there because there is a foundation of two legitimate walls, but that *pasei bira'ot*, which lacks two full walls and offers only corner-posts, would not qualify. Conversely, had we only been told the *pasei bira'ot* case — where four corners are present, framing a full square — one might think that works because all four walls are at least nominally represented, but the *mavoi*, which lacks that complete frame, would not qualify. And had we only been told the two *migu*-from-*Shabbat*-to-*sukkah* cases, one might think *migu* works only from the stringent to the lenient, but not from the lenient *sukkah* to the stringent *Shabbat*. All three cases together, covering both directions of the *migu* and both structural configurations, are therefore necessary.

## Walls & Shade

### Shade Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁחֲמָתָהּ מְרוּבָּה מְצִלָּתָהּ — פְּסוּלָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמָתָהּ מֵחֲמַת סִיכּוּךְ, וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת דְּפָנוֹת. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אוֹמֵר: אַף מֵחֲמַת דְּפָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And** a *sukka* **whose sunlight,** i.e., the sunlight that passes through the roofing, **is greater than its shade, is unfit.** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that in the statement: **Whose sunlight** is greater than its shade, the reference is to sunlight that passes through **due to** sparse **roofing, and not** to the sunlight entering **due to** gaps in the **walls.** It is possible for a *sukka* to have more sunlight than shade due to sunlight passing through the sides and not the roofing, in which case the *sukka* is fit. **Rabbi Yoshiya says:** If the sunlight exceeds the shade the *sukka* is unfit, **even** if the sunlight is **due to** gaps in the **walls.**

The *gemara* records a *tannaitic* dispute about whether *sukkah* walls must block sunlight. The view of the Rabbis is that the requirement of shade applies only to the *schach*, not to the walls: *chamata machmat schach ve-lo machmat defanot* — the concern about excessive sunlight penetrating the *sukkah* is a concern about the *schach*, and the walls need provide no shade whatsoever. Rabbi Yoshia disagrees, ruling *af machmat defanot* — even the walls must provide shade.

### Parochet Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יֵימַר בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְסַכּוֹתָ עַל הָאָרוֹן אֶת הַפָּרוֹכֶת״, פָּרוֹכֶת מְחִיצָה, וְקָא קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא ״סְכָכָה״, אַלְמָא מְחִיצָה כִּסְכָךְ בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar bar Shelemya said in the name of Abaye: What is the rationale for** the statement of **Rabbi Yoshiya?** It is **as it is written: "And you shall screen [*vesakkota*] the Ark with the curtain"** (Exodus 40:3). **The curtain is a partition** and not a covering over the Ark, **and** nevertheless, **the Merciful One calls it roofing [*sekhakha*]. Apparently, we require** the purpose of **a partition** to be **similar** to the purpose of **roofing;** just as the roofing must be mostly impermeable by sunlight, so must the partition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא דְּנִיכּוֹף בַּיהּ פּוּרְתָּא, דְּמִחֲזֵי כִּסְכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yoshiya, interpret the term: And you shall screen [*vesakkota*]? **That** term teaches **that we** should **bend** the top of the curtain **a bit so that it appears as roofing** over the Ark.

Abaye transmits the reasoning of Rabbi Yoshia in the name of Rabbi Yeimar bar Shmaya. Rabbi Yoshia derives his position from the verse *ve-sakkota al ha-aron et ha-parochet* — "and you shall cover the ark with the *parochet*." The *parochet*, the curtain of the Sanctuary, is described here as a wall, and yet the verb used is from the root of *schach*, *sakkota*. This indicates, in Rabbi Yoshia's view, that walls are also a form of *schach* — they too carry the shading function. A wall worthy of a *sukkah* must therefore function like *schach* in providing shade, just as a normal dwelling's walls block out sunlight.

The Rabbis respond that the verse does not imply that the wall functions as *schach*; rather, it describes folding the top edge of the *parochet* slightly inward over the ark, so that the very tip of the curtain forms a small *schach*-like covering. The wall of the *parochet* itself does not bear the *schach* function — only the bent-over top does. Hence, walls of a *sukkah* are not required to block the sun. One might naturally ask whether Rabbi Yoshia's position would require walls to be constructed from the same materials as kosher *schach* — at least one *Rishon* does indeed draw that conclusion — but the *gemara* does not pursue that implication here.

## Permanent Dwelling

### Abaye's Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי, וְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וַאֲחֵרִים — כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: סוּכָּה דִּירַת קֶבַע בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi Yoshiya, and Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon, and Rabban Gamliel, and Beit Shammai, and Rabbi Eliezer, and *Aḥerim* all hold** that **we require** the ***sukka*** to be sturdy and fit for dwelling like **a permanent residence.**

At the close of the *daf*, Abaye introduces a sweeping observation: a significant number of *Tannaim* across widely different discussions of *sukkah* — Rebbi, Rabbi Yoshia, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon, Rabban Gamliel, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Eliezer, and others — all share a common underlying premise: *kuhlhu sevira lehu Sukkah dirat keva beinan* — they all hold that a *sukkah* must be a *dirat keva*, a permanent dwelling structure. This is not to say they agree on the specific consequences of that principle; different *tannaim* draw different halachic lines from it. But what Abaye identifies is a shared conceptual foundation: the *sukkah* is not a flimsy temporary shelter but something more enduringly house-like. The various stringencies they espouse are different expressions of this single animating idea.

### Rabbi's Dimension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי — דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye cites the relevant statements of the *tanna'im* listed above. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi states this opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Any *sukka* that does not have** an area of **four cubits by four cubits is unfit.** These are the dimensions of a permanent residence.

The case of Rebbi: *kol Sukkah she-ein bah arba amot al arba amot pesulah* — any *sukkah* that does not measure four *amot* by four *amot* is *pasul*. The standard ruling requires only seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*, which is considerably smaller. Rebbi's insistence on four by four *amot* reflects his understanding that a *sukkah* must approximate the dimensions of a real permanent dwelling.

### Rabbi Yehuda Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּתְנַן סוּכָּה שֶׁהִיא גְּבוֹהָה לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה פְּסוּלָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda** also holds that a *sukka* must be a permanent residence, **as we learned** in the mishna: **A *sukka* that is more than twenty cubits high is unfit; Rabbi Yehuda deems it fit.** As explained above, in constructing a *sukka* more than twenty cubits high, one cannot render his residence a temporary residence; rather, he must construct a sturdy permanent residence.

The case of Rabbi Yehuda: in the first *mishnah* of the tractate, the ruling is that a *sukkah* taller than twenty *amot* is *pasul*, because at that height one does not notice the *schach* and is therefore not dwelling meaningfully beneath it. Rabbi Yehuda declares it *kasher*. A *sukkah* of more than twenty *amot* can only function as a dwelling if one envisions it as a truly permanent structure — the kind of substantial building where great height is a mark of grandeur rather than a halachic defect.

### Rabbi Yoshia Opacity

The case of Rabbi Yoshia has already been discussed: requiring the walls to provide shade reflects the expectation that a *sukkah* resemble an actual house, whose walls protect its occupants from the elements including direct sunlight.

### Rabbi Shimon Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁתַּיִם כְּהִלְכָתָן וּשְׁלִישִׁית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ כְּהִלְכָתָן וּרְבִיעִית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** agrees, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The dimensions of a *sukka* are **two** walls **in the standard sense,** and **a third** wall that measures **even a handbreadth; Rabbi Shimon says: Three** of the walls must be walls **in the standard sense, and a fourth** wall is required that measures **even a handbreadth.** Apparently, a *sukka* must be surrounded on four sides like a permanent residence.

Finally, the case of Rabbi Shimon: where the consensus is two walls plus a *tefach*, Rabbi Shimon requires three complete walls plus a *tefach*. As the *gemara* noted earlier with its citation from Yeshayahu, the model of protection Rabbi Shimon has in mind is not merely shade but shelter from wind and rain — the kind of protection a genuine permanent structure provides. This, like the positions of the other *tannaim* in Abaye's collection, is an expression of the fundamental principle that a *sukkah* must aspire to the character of *dirat keva*.
