# Menachot 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Five Grains & Omer

### Five Grains Overview

We pick up on *daf* 71a, having introduced at the two-dot mark — approximately twelve lines from the bottom of the preceding *amud* — the foundational principle that the identity of the five grains carries profound halachic implications. The question of which grains fall into this category bears directly on the laws of *chametz*, *matzah*, the prohibition of *chadash*, *Hamotzi*, *Mezonos*, and related matters. The particular question of oats, though traditionally assumed to be among the five, continues to be debated in later authorities. With that background established, the *Mishnah* moves forward to address the next set of restrictions: until the *Omer* is brought, one may neither harvest nor eat any of the new grain. This prohibition is not limited to barley — it applies to all five grains.

### Cutting Before Omer

The *Mishnah* states that one may not *liktzor* — cut — the new grain before the *Omer*, and may not eat *chadash* before the *Omer* is brought. This raises the central question of when, precisely, the restriction on cutting lapses.

### Cut vs. Brought

Does the prohibition on cutting end when the *Omer* is cut, or only when the *Omer* is brought? This is the primary issue the *Gemara* now takes up, and there is a significant dispute between Rashi and Tosafot as to how to read the *Gemara*'s text. We proceed here according to Tosafot's reading.

---

## Reishit & Gezeira Shava

### Gezeira Shava Source

The *Gemara* opens with the question *mina hanei mili* — where is the prohibition on cutting derived? Rabbi Yochanan answers: from a *gezeira shava* of *reishit-reishit* linking the *Omer* to *challah*. The *Omer* is described as *reishit ktzirchem* — the first of your cuttings — which establishes that nothing may be cut before it, lest it compromise the *Omer*'s status as *reishit*. But this would seem to apply only to barley. The extension to all five grains comes through the *gezeira shava*: the *Omer* is called *reishit*, and *challah* is also called *reishit*; *challah* in turn is defined in terms of *lechem*, which connects to *chametz* and *matzah*, and from there we derive the full list of five grains. Consequently, the prohibition on cutting applies not to barley alone but to any of the five grains. In practice, of course, only barley would typically be ripe at that time of year — the chance of having ripe wheat or ripe oats at *Omer* time is remote — but in principle, if any of the five grains happened to be ripe, one could not cut it.

### Omer as Reishit

The *Gemara* then turns to the precise meaning of *kodem la'Omer* — before the *Omer*. The question is whether the *Omer*'s status as *reishit ktzirchem* means it must be the first thing cut when it is cut, or whether it need only represent the first bringing. Both readings are plausible from the *pesukim*: if the *Omer* is *reishit*, perhaps the critical moment is the cutting, since that is when the act of harvesting takes place; or perhaps it suffices that when it is brought it still represents a kind of firstness.

### Kodem Cutting vs Bringing

The *Gemara* records the dispute explicitly. Rabbi Yochanan says *kodem ketzirat ha'Omer* — the prohibition on cutting new grain extends only until the *Omer* is cut; once the *Omer* has been cut, others may begin to cut. Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Zavda says *kodem hava'at ha'Omer* — the restriction remains in place until the *Omer* is actually brought.

The *Gemara* then attempts to resolve this from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* states: *asurin b'chadash lifnei ha'Pesach v'liktzor lifnei ha'Omer* — the new grain is forbidden before *Pesach* and one may not cut before the *Omer*. This formulation is puzzling, since one cannot eat *chadash* until the second day of *Pesach*, not before *Pesach* itself. Tosafot's *girsa* reverses the two terms: *asurin b'chadash lifnei ha'Omer v'liktzor lifnei ha'Pesach* — one may not eat *chadash* until the *Omer*, and one may not cut until *Pesach*.

---

## Tosafot Text Emendation

### Tosafot's Girsa

Tosafot's reversal of the text is an emendation, but it produces a significantly cleaner reading of the *Gemara*, and we proceed accordingly. The eating prohibition is naturally stated as lasting until the *Omer* is brought, which is beyond dispute. The cutting prohibition is stated as lasting until *Pesach*.

### Eating vs Cutting

Why does the *Mishnah* say two things — don't eat until the *Omer*, don't cut until *Pesach* — rather than simply saying don't do anything until the *Omer* is brought? The answer depends directly on the dispute. If the prohibition on cutting ends when the *Omer* is cut rather than when it is brought, then the two prohibitions terminate at different moments: eating ends when the *Omer* is brought, cutting ends when the *Omer* is cut. It therefore makes perfect sense that the *Mishnah* states them separately. But if the prohibition on cutting likewise extends until the *Omer* is brought, then both restrictions share the same endpoint and the *Mishnah* should have combined them into a single statement: don't eat *chadash* or cut before the *Omer* is brought. The fact that the *Mishnah* distinguishes them is strong evidence that cutting and eating are governed by different thresholds.

### Yom Tov Restrictions

Why is the cutting restriction phrased as "until *Pesach*" rather than "until the *Omer* is cut"? The answer is that since *Pesach* itself is *Yom Tov*, no one would imagine that one may go out and harvest on the first day of *Pesach*. The *Yom Tov* prohibition on field labor — the principle that one may only perform *melachos* related to food preparation in the home domain, from the stage of *lisha* onwards, and not the *melachos* of the field — itself prevents any cutting on *Yom Tov*. Stating the prohibition as "until *Pesach*" is therefore entirely safe: one will not interpret it as permission to cut on *Pesach* itself.

### Motzei Yom Tov Cutting

The earliest practical moment to cut would therefore be *motzei Yom Tov*. By that time, the *Omer* would already have been cut — the Temple would have cut it the previous night, at the outset of the sixteenth. So by the time an individual could actually go out to his field after *Yom Tov*, the *Omer* would already have been cut. This dovetails precisely with the position of Rabbi Yochanan: that cutting is permitted from the moment the *Omer* is cut, not from when it is brought. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: the question of whether *kodem la'Omer* refers to cutting or bringing is resolved from the *seifa* — the final clause of the *Mishnah* — not from this earlier passage.

---

## Hashrashah Question

### Final Mishna Clause

The final clause of the *Mishnah* states: *im hishrishu kodem la'Omer, ha'Omer matiran* — if the grain took root before the *Omer*, the *Omer* permits it. The *Omer* does not permit grain that came into existence after it was brought; it only applies to grain that already existed — meaning grain whose seeds had already taken root — at the relevant time. The question now is: what is the relevant time?

### Unresolved Debate

*Mai kodem la'Omer* — when do we evaluate whether the grain has taken root? Rabbi Yochanan says *kodem hava'at ha'Omer*: the *Omer* permits all grain that had taken root before it was brought. Rabbi Yossi bar Zavda says *kodem ketzirat ha'Omer*: the operative moment is before the *Omer* was cut, since the process of the *Omer* begins with the cutting. The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved. In practice, the general assumption adopted in other contexts is that we judge by the grain that had taken root at the time the *Omer* was brought, not when it was cut.

The overall picture, according to Tosafot's reading, is therefore as follows: cutting new grain becomes permissible from the moment the *Omer* is cut; eating new grain is permitted only from the moment the *Omer* is brought; and the *Omer* renders permissible all grain that had taken root by the time it was brought.

---

## Sprouting Source Question

### Elazar's Challenge

We now arrive at the top of *amud alef*. Rav Elazar said to Rav Yoshia — his contemporary, not the earlier Tanna of the same name — *lo taysiv akara*, do not sit down until you explain this *Mishnah* to me: *menayin la'Omer she-matir b'hashrashah* — from where do we know that the *Omer* permits grain at the stage of having taken root? Perhaps the *Omer* only permits grain that is already standing and fully grown, and grain that sprouted later would simply have to wait until the following year's *Omer*. What is the source for the rule that mere *hashrashah* — taking root — is sufficient?

### Aviv Source

The answer begins with the word *Aviv*. The *Omer* is described as *Aviv* — ripened grain. The inference is: *lav mikhlal d'ika d'lav Aviv* — the very fact that the *Omer* must be brought from ripened grain implies that there is other grain in the field that is not yet ripe, and the *Omer* presumably relates to that grain as well — otherwise why would the *Torah* need to specify that the *Omer* itself must be ripened? So we see that the *Omer* applies to grain that has not yet fully ripened.

### Narrowing Scope

The *Gemara* pushes back at each stage. Very well — the *Omer* applies to grain that is not yet ripe. But perhaps at minimum it must have reached one-third ripeness (*shlish*), which is the critical threshold at which grain acquires a recognized status as a food product. The *Gemara* then adduces a second verse: *me-hachel chermesh ba-kamah tachel lispor* — from when the sickle is put to the standing grain you shall begin to count. This implies there is other grain that is not yet ready for the sickle, and the *Omer* relates to it. But perhaps that grain is still at the stage of *shachas* — stalks without ripened heads, fit for fodder but not for harvesting. Still, we have not reached the mere seedling in the ground. Rabbi Yitzchak adds: *kamah* — standing grain — implies there is grain not yet at the standing stage. But perhaps it is still above the ground, emerging as shoots. Each successive inference narrows the scope further without yet reaching the point of bare roots in the soil.

---

## Chametz to Planted

### Shtei HaLechem Proof

Rava offers the decisive proof. He turns not to the *Omer* itself but to the *Shtei HaLechem* — the Two Loaves brought on *Shavuot*. The verse states: *v'chag ha-katzir bikkurei maasecha asher tizra ba-sadeh* — the festival of harvest, the first of your deeds, that which you plant in the field. Rava understands this verse as referring back to grain that had already passed through the *heter* of the *Omer*: the *Shtei HaLechem* is brought only from grain that the *Omer* already permitted. Yet this grain is described as *asher tizra* — that which you plant. This means the *Omer* was *matir* the grain even when it was at the stage of *asher tizra* — when it had merely been planted.

### Asher Tizrah Stage

The implication is that the *Omer* renders permissible grain even from the earliest stage of having been planted — *mishaat zriah*, from the time of planting. This gets us far closer to mere *hashrashah*.

### Rav Pappa Objection

Rav Pappa challenges Rava: if the criterion is merely *asher tizra* — having been planted — then even a seed that was placed in the ground but has not yet taken root should be permitted by the *Omer*. Rava responds with a single word: *Sudani* — a term of address that Rashi understands as connoting a Torah scholar, though it may alternatively be a reference to Rav Pappa's trade as a brewer, either as praise or gentle rebuke. The response: *ba-sadeh k'siv* — the verse says *asher tizra ba-sadeh*, in the field. For something to be truly *in the field* — to have become part of the field — it must have taken root. A seed that was merely placed in the ground has not yet become part of the field.

### Conclusion Sprouting

The conclusion is thus established: the *Omer* permits all grain that has taken root — *hishrishu* — before the *Omer* was cut, and as noted earlier, the *Gemara* generally holds that the moment of evaluation is when the *Omer* was brought. This is a *drasha* that the *Gemara* takes seriously, and it must be correct in substance: if the *Omer* were limited to grain already standing and ready for harvest, then all the wheat and later-ripening grains that sprout after the *Omer* would never be permitted — which is plainly not the intent of the law.

## Permitted Pre-Omer Harve…

### Reishit Problem

Having established that cutting the new grain is permitted from the moment the *Omer* is cut, and that eating it is permitted from when the *Omer* is brought, the *Mishnah* turns to a further question: are there circumstances in which grain may be harvested even before the *Omer* is cut? The difficulty is that the *Omer* must be *reishit ktzirchem* — the first cutting — and any prior harvesting would appear to compromise that status.

### Non-Omer Fields

The *Mishnah* states: *kotzrin beis ha-shlachin she-ba'amakim* — one may cut irrigated fields in the valleys. But one may not *godesh* — make sheaves — from them. The principle is that from fields where the *Omer* cannot be brought, the *Omer*'s status as *reishit* is not impinged upon by an earlier harvesting. There are two reasons underlying the practical permission. First, there is a technical halachic basis: since the *Omer* is ideally not brought from these fields — either because their grain is considered lower quality, or possibly because irrigated fields are not sufficiently tied to the natural seasonal cycle of rain and therefore cannot serve as the *reishit* of the natural harvest — cutting from them does not compromise the *Omer*. Second, there was a practical concern of *hefsed* — financial loss — if the grain in these fields was not cut when ready. The combination of technical permissibility and practical necessity justified the allowance. The restriction against *godesh* — making sheaves — was maintained to prevent it from looking like a full-scale harvest operation prior to the *Omer*.

### Jericho Case

The *Mishnah* continues: *anshei Yericho kotzrin b'ratzon chachamim* — the people of Jericho would harvest with the approval of the Sages, since they apparently had these irrigated valley fields. However, *v'godshin shelo b'ratzon chachamim* — they would bundle the grain without the Sages' approval. *V'lo michu biyadam* — yet the Sages did not actually protest or attempt to stop them. There is an important distinction here between disapproval and active protest. The Sages were not happy with the bundling, but they tolerated it rather than making a formal objection. The reason for not bringing the *Omer* from the irrigated valley fields is not fully explicated in the *Gemara*, but it is treated as self-evident, and Tosafot and other *Rishonim* note that the qualifier *ba-amakim* — in the valleys — is disputed as to whether it is essential to the ruling.

---

## Fodder Harvesting

### Fodder Not Harvesting

The *Mishnah* next addresses a different category of permissible pre-*Omer* cutting: *kotzer le-shachas u-ma'achil li-vheima* — harvesting for animal fodder. In this case, the field in question might otherwise be a valid source for the *Omer*, but the manner of harvesting removes it from the category of prohibited cutting altogether. The rationale is that the halachic prohibition applies specifically to harvesting grain for human food consumption. A cutting done for animal fodder — *shachas* — is not classified as the type of *ketzira* the *Torah* restricts, and therefore it is entirely permissible before the *Omer*.

### Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehuda introduces a qualification: *eimasai* — when does this apply? Only *bizman she-hitchil ad she-lo hevi shlish* — when one began the fodder cutting before the grain had reached one-third ripeness. At that stage, the grain is objectively not fit for human consumption, and the entire cutting is therefore unambiguously for the sake of animal fodder. Even if the harvesting continues after the grain reaches a greater degree of ripeness, the character of the operation was defined at its outset. If, however, one began cutting only after the grain had already reached one-third ripeness, it is forbidden — since at that point the grain has already acquired the status of a food product.

### Shimon's Position

Rabbi Shimon takes a more expansive position: *yiktzor vi-ya'achil af mi-she-hevi shlish* — one may cut and feed to the animals even once the grain has reached one-third ripeness. For Rabbi Shimon, the determinative factor is not the degree of ripeness but the intention and nature of the cutting. If one's purpose is to provide animal fodder, then regardless of how ripe the grain is, it is not considered a halachic act of harvesting. The underlying principle shared by both opinions is that only a harvesting oriented toward human food consumption falls under the restriction; the dispute is whether intention alone suffices or whether objective ripeness also plays a role.

---

## Mitzvah Harvesting

### Kilayim Clearing

The *Mishnah* continues with additional categories of pre-*Omer* cutting that are permissible on account of *mitzvah* purposes. *Ha-kotzetz mipnei netiot* — cutting on account of plantings. Rashi offers two readings. The first is similar to the *beis ha-shlachin* case: the grain grows among tree saplings and there is concern of *hefsed*, of mutual destruction, if one does not cut early, and additionally the *Omer* cannot be brought from a field that contains trees. The second reading, which Rashi himself prefers as it coheres with the thematic thrust of what follows, is that the situation involves *kilayim*: grain is growing in a field where a tree has unexpectedly taken root — perhaps an acorn that had lain dormant in the ground — creating a prohibited mixture. One may cut the grain to resolve the *kilayim* concern. Cutting for the sake of a *mitzvah* constitutes a permissible *ketzira* before the *Omer*, and this is the interpretive frame for the cases that follow.

### Mourning Space

*Mipnei beis ha-avel* — cutting to create space for mourners. After a burial, it was customary to conduct *nichum avelim* in an outdoor courtyard near the burial site. If that space was occupied by grain, one could cut it to make room for the mourners to sit. This is a *ketzira* done for the sake of a *mitzvah* — the *mitzvah* of comforting mourners — and it is therefore permissible before the *Omer*.

### Study Hall Space

*U-mipnei bitul beis ha-midrash* — cutting to create space for Torah study. In an agricultural society, outdoor study spaces were common, and if a wheat field occupied a space needed for additional students or for the study hall to expand, one could cut the grain to make room. The cutting is for the sake of the *mitzvah* of Torah study, and is likewise permissible.

### Bundle Restriction

In all of these *mitzvah*-based cases, the *Mishnah* imposes a restriction parallel to the one imposed in the *beis ha-shlachin* case: *lo ya'asem kerichot* — do not form them into bundles. *Aval menichim tzvatim* — one may leave them in small piles. The distinction between *kerichot* and *tzvatim* — formal bound bundles versus informal loose piles — serves to ensure that the cutting does not take on the appearance of a full-scale harvest operation prior to the *Omer*.

## Omer Details

### Standing vs Sheaves

The *Mishnah* now describes the preferred and acceptable manners of bringing the *Omer* itself. *Mitzvas ha'Omer lavo min ha-kamah* — the *mitzvah* is ideally to cut the *Omer* from standing grain, grain that has not previously been cut. If standing grain was unavailable, *yavi min ha'amarim* — one may bring it from sheaves, even grain that had been cut previously and perhaps for other purposes. *Bedi'avad*, this is acceptable.

### Fresh vs Dry

*Mitzvasah lavo min ha-lach* — the *mitzvah* is to bring the *Omer* from fresh grain, as derived from the word *karmel*, which indicates softness and freshness. If fresh grain was unavailable, *yavi min ha-yavesh* — one may bring it from dry grain, as long as it is from the current year's crop.

### Night vs Day

*Mitzvasah liktzor ba-layla* — the *mitzvah* is to cut the *Omer* at night, on account of *temimut*, the requirement that the seven-week count be complete, which begins properly only when the cutting is done at the start of the sixteenth. *Niktza ba-yom kasher* — if the *Omer* was cut during the day, it is valid after the fact.

### Overrides Shabbat

Finally: *docheh et ha-Shabbat* — the cutting of the *Omer* overrides *Shabbat*. Since the *Omer* must be brought on the sixteenth of *Nissan*, it is the *mitzvah* of that specific day, and like other *korbanot* that have a fixed time, it overrides *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* when necessary. One does not say "in principle I could have cut it earlier"; the nature of *korbanot* is that the day's *mitzvah* is performed on that day. In the Temple period, since *Pesach* was set *al pi ha-re'iya* — according to the appearance of the moon — the sixteenth could fall on *Shabbat*.

## Rabbi Benjamin's Source

### Two Verses

A *baraisa* is cited: Rabbi Benjamin observes an apparent tension between two verses. One verse states *u-ktzartem et ktzira* — you shall cut its cutting — referring to the general act of harvesting. Another verse, appearing in the same context, states *reishit ktzirchem* — the first of your cuttings. The first verse, speaking of cutting without the qualifier *reishit*, seems to imply that other cuttings are occurring alongside or before the *Omer* cutting. The second verse insists that the *Omer* be the first. How are they reconciled?

### Geographic Resolution

*Mi-makom she-ata meivi, ee ata kotzer* — from a place from which you may bring the *Omer*, you may not cut before the *Omer*, because to do so would compromise its *reishit* status. *Mi-makom she-ee ata meivi, ata kotzer* — from a place from which you cannot bring the *Omer*, you may cut, since cutting there does not impinge on the *Omer*'s standing as the first cutting.

### Species vs Field

The *Gemara* considers an alternative reading of the same textual tension: perhaps the permission has nothing to do with which field but rather with which species. Perhaps one is prohibited from cutting barley — the grain of the *Omer* — but is free to cut wheat or oats before the *Omer*. This reading is rejected because of Rabbi Yochanan's *gezeira shava* established earlier: the prohibition on cutting before the *Omer* applies to all five grains, not barley alone. Since that *drasha* extends the prohibition across all five species, the permission must be geographic — based on the type of field — rather than species-based.

## Jericho & Pesachim

### Six Things

The *Gemara* now turns to a *baraisa* found in Pesachim that provides important context for understanding the status of the people of Jericho's practices. The *baraisa* states: *shisha devarim asu anshei Yericho* — the people of Jericho did six things. *Shelosha b'ratzon chachamim* — three with the approval of the Sages, and *shelosha shelo b'ratzon chachamim* — three without their approval.

### Three Approved

The three approved practices were: *markivin d'kalim kol ha-yom* — grafting palm trees all day on *Erev Pesach*. Normally, one is expected to refrain from significant labor on *Erev Pesach*, particularly from *chatzos* onwards, in order to prepare for bringing the *Korban Pesach*. However, Jericho was *ir ha-temarim* — the city of palm trees — and the particular type of grafting involved was considered relatively simple and was simultaneously at risk of severe *hefsed* if delayed. For these reasons the Sages approved. *Korchin et Shma* — they would recite the *Shema* in a wrapped or connected manner, without pausing between sections or between certain words. This was not considered exemplary practice but was sufficiently innocuous that the Sages did not disapprove. *V'kotzrin lifnei ha'Omer* — and they would harvest before the *Omer*, which, as established, fell under the approved category due to the combination of their *beis ha-shlachin* fields and concerns of *hefsed*.

### Three Tolerated

The three practices done without Rabbinic approval were: *godshin lifnei ha'Omer* — bundling before the *Omer*, which the Sages disapproved of but did not protest. *U-matirin gamziyot shel hekdesh shel charuv v'shel shikmah* — they would make use of the secondary growths (*gamziyot*) of carob trees and sycamore trees that had previously been consecrated as *hekdesh*. The reasoning of the people of Jericho was that since these growths appeared after the *hekdesh* designation, they were not themselves *hekdesh*. The Sages disagreed: *gidulei hekdesh* — growths from consecrated property — remain *hekdesh* and belong to the *Beis Ha-Mikdash*. *U-portzin pratzot b'ganoteihem u-fardeisethem k'dei l'ha'achil la-aniyim nesher b'Shabbatot u-v'yamim tovim* — and they made holes in the roofs and walls of their orchards and gardens, especially in years of famine, so that fruit falling from the trees would collect in accessible places and the poor could eat from it on *Shabbat*. While the humanitarian intention was praiseworthy, the Sages were concerned about the *muktzah* status of fruit that fell on *Shabbat* and the broader concern that eating fallen fruit on *Shabbat* might lead to actually picking fruit from a tree on *Shabbat*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Critique

Rabbi Meir is identified as the author of the *baraisa* as quoted above. Rabbi Yehuda responds: *im birtzon chachamim hayu osim, hayu kol adam osim kein* — if these practices had truly been approved by the Sages, why did everyone not do them? The fact that it was only the people of Jericho implies that these were not normatively approved practices. Rabbi Yehuda's counter-position is therefore: *eilu v'eilu shelo birtzon chachamim* — all six practices were done without the Sages' approval. The distinction, in his view, is not between approved and unapproved but between tolerated and actively protested: *shelosha michu biyadam v'shelosha lo michu biyadam* — three they protested and three they did not. The three they did not protest were: the grafting of palm trees, the wrapping of the *Shema*, and the cutting and bundling before the *Omer* — both the *kotzrin* and the *godshin* together. The three they did actively protest were: the *gamziyot shel hekdesh*, the holes in the rooftops for the poor on *Shabbat*, and — an additional item Rabbi Yehuda introduces — *v'notnin pe'ah la-yarak*, their practice of giving *pe'ah* from vegetables. Since vegetables are not subject to *pe'ah*, what they designated as *pe'ah* was not halachically valid *matanot aniyim*, and the poor who ate from it had therefore not exempted it from *terumot u-ma'asrot*, making the consumption problematic.

### Gemara Resolution

Rabbi Yehuda's position now creates an arithmetical difficulty: if all six original practices were wrong, but he added a seventh (*pe'ah* from vegetables), he would have seven total, not six, and the *baraisa*'s structure of six things breaks down. The *Gemara* resolves this by emending Rabbi Yehuda's list: *sami mikan k'tzira* — remove the cutting from Rabbi Yehuda's list of unapproved practices. According to Rabbi Yehuda's corrected position, cutting irrigated fields before the *Omer* is entirely and universally permissible — not a Jericho-specific anomaly but a practice open to anyone with such fields. The *Mishnah*'s formulation that cutting was done *b'ratzon chachamim* is therefore consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's position, because Rabbi Yehuda's point is precisely that if the Sages genuinely approved something, it should have been universally adopted — and indeed, the cutting of irrigated fields *was* universally available to anyone in that situation. What was specific to Jericho and what Rabbi Yehuda includes among the unapproved-but-tolerated practices is the *godesh* — the bundling — not the cutting. This resolves the consistency between our *Mishnah* and the *baraisa* from Pesachim.

## Field Division Pe'ah

### Pe'ah Introduction

The *Gemara* now introduces a *Mishnah* from tractate Pe'ah that will illuminate the question of whether fodder harvesting constitutes real harvesting. The background: *pe'ah* — the corner of the field left for the poor — must be given from each field separately. If a single large field is divided by some barrier or intervening feature, each section counts as a separate field requiring its own *pe'ah*. The question is: what divides a field for this purpose?

### Natural Barriers

The *Mishnah* in Pe'ah lists the dividers. *Ha-nachal* — a *nachal*, a riverbed or wadi, even if dry, divides the field, as the *Yerushalmi* clarifies. *Ha-shlulit* — a running stream. The distinction between *nachal* and *shlulit* is one of size and permanence: a *nachal* is a significant riverbed that divides even when dry; a *shlulit* is a smaller running stream that divides only when water flows through it. *Derech ha-yachid v'derech ha-rabim* — a private path or public road. *Shvil ha-rabim v'shvil ha-yachid* — a public or private pathway, provided it is *ha-kavu'a bi-ymot ha-chamah u-vi-ymot ha-geshamim*, established and usable in both summer and rainy seasons. *Ve-ha-bur ve-ha-nir* — an unplanted section (*bur*) or a fallow-but-plowed section (*nir*). The distinction between these two is that *nir* is a field left fallow but worked — the soil is turned over — while *bur* is simply abandoned and unworked. Either one, if running through the middle of a field, divides it. *Ve-zera acher* — a strip of a different crop in the middle likewise creates a division.

### Fodder Divides Field

*Ve-kotzer li-shachas mafsik, divrei Rabbi Meir* — and harvesting for fodder divides the field, so says Rabbi Meir. If one cuts a strip in the middle of one's field for animal fodder — which takes place earlier in the season before the grain is fully ripe — that harvested strip separates the two sides of the field for *pe'ah* purposes. *Va-chachamim omrim: ein mafsik ela im ken charash* — but the Sages say: it does not divide the field unless that section was subsequently plowed over. Merely cutting for fodder is not enough; only if one then turned the soil would the section become analogous to a *nir* and thereby divide the field.

## Pe'ah-Omer Connectio (II)

### Meir as Shimon

*Amar Rabba bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: Rabbi Meir k'shittas Rabbi Shimon amra* — Rabbi Meir's position in the *Mishnah* in Pe'ah was stated in the same framework as Rabbi Shimon's position in our *Mishnah* about the *Omer*. Recall that Rabbi Shimon held that cutting grain for animal fodder is not considered a real act of harvesting even if the grain has already reached one-third ripeness — intention alone determines whether it is *shachas* or genuine *ketzira*. Rabbi Meir likewise treats cutting for fodder as not a real harvesting regardless of ripeness, which is why it suffices to divide the field: it constituted a genuine transformation of that section — not a harvesting but a fodder-cutting — without any need for plowing.

### Fodder Not Real

Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon are therefore understood as operating on the same principle: a cutting done for the sake of fodder, regardless of the grain's state of ripeness, is categorically not a *ketzira* in the halachic sense. By the *Omer*, this means one has not compromised the *Omer*'s *reishit* status. By *pe'ah*, this means the fodder-cutting effectively redefines that strip as a different kind of section, dividing the field.

## Pe'ah-Omer Connectio (II)

### Locusts Test

The *Gemara* then tests this equation with a challenge from a *Tosefta* in Pe'ah. *Achlah chagav* — if locusts consumed a strip in the middle of one's field; *karsimuha nemalim* — or ants gnawed it down; *shivsah ha-ruach* — or the wind destroyed a section — *ha-kol modim* — everyone agrees that this alone does not divide the field. *Charash mafsik, lo charash eino mafsik* — only if one subsequently plowed it over does it divide the field; if not, it does not.

Now the *Gemara* asks: *ha-kol modim, man* — when the *Tosefta* says "everyone agrees," who has shifted position? The one who would normally not require plowing is Rabbi Meir — he said a fodder-cutting by itself divides the field. Yet here Rabbi Meir agrees that natural destruction requires plowing. Why the difference?

The *Gemara* proposes a resolution based on the degree of ripeness. One might reconcile Rabbi Meir's two positions as follows: when the *Mishnah* in Pe'ah says fodder-cutting divides the field without plowing, that is specifically where the cutting was done before the grain reached *shlish* — when it was objectively unripe and the cutting was unambiguously fodder-cutting with no resemblance to grain harvesting. In that case, the cutting suffices to redefine the section. When insects destroyed the grain, however, the *Tosefta* evidently deals with grain that was already one-third ripe — at which point its grain status is more firmly established, and therefore even natural destruction requires subsequent plowing to redefine the section.

### Meir as Yehuda

If this is correct, Rabbi Meir's position on fodder turns out to parallel not Rabbi Shimon but Rabbi Yehuda: it is only cutting before one-third ripeness that qualifies as true *shachas* and escapes the category of real harvesting. Rabbi Yehuda in our *Mishnah* similarly held that the fodder exemption applies only when cutting began before *shlish*.

However, a further difficulty arises. Our *Mishnah* specifies cutting for fodder *le-vheima* — to feed to animals — and the question of whether cutting for human consumption of fodder-like food also falls under this leniency is not addressed in our *Mishnah*. If Rabbi Meir's position corresponds to Rabbi Yehuda's and both only care about the grain being less than one-third ripe without any qualification about animals versus humans, then Rabbi Meir would be saying something different from the first opinion in our *Mishnah*, creating a three-way dispute among the *Tanna kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon.

### Meir as Akiva

The final resolution is brought by Rav Dimi — or in some versions Ravin — upon his return from *Eretz Yisrael*: *Rabbi Meir k'shittas Rabbi Akiva rabo amra* — Rabbi Meir's position follows the framework of his teacher Rabbi Akiva, and should not be compared directly to any of the opinions in our *Mishnah* at all. Rabbi Akiva held that even cutting for human use, when the grain is less than one-third ripe, is not considered real harvesting — *af le'adam nami lo hevei k'tzira*. Rabbi Meir, following Rabbi Akiva, does not draw a distinction between cutting for animals and cutting for humans, and does not engage with the animal-versus-human axis that structures the debate in our *Mishnah* between the *Tanna kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon. His criterion is simply the stage of ripeness: below one-third, it is *shachas* and not *ketzira* regardless of for whom it is intended. This disentangles Rabbi Meir from our *Mishnah* entirely and resolves the difficulties that arose from trying to identify him with either Rabbi Shimon or Rabbi Yehuda.

## Field Division Pe'ah (II)

### Natural Destruction

The *Tosefta*'s ruling about natural destruction — whether by locusts, ants, or wind — establishes that acts of nature alone do not redefine a section of a field for *pe'ah* purposes, even if they leave that section bare. Unlike a deliberate human act, whether harvesting or plowing, a natural event does not carry the intentional, definitional weight needed to divide a field. Only when such natural destruction is followed by deliberate plowing — which transforms the cleared section into something analogous to a *nir* — does the division take halachic effect. This ruling is accepted unanimously, as the *Tosefta* states *ha-kol modim*, and serves as the background against which the various positions on fodder-harvesting are ultimately measured.
