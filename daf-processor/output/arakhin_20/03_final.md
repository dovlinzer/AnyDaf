# Arakhin 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arachin vs Nedarim

### Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חוֹמֶר בָּעֲרָכִין מִבַּנְּדָרִים, כֵּיצַד? הָאוֹמֵר: "עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" וָמֵת — יִתְּנוּ הַיּוֹרְשִׁים, "דָּמַי עָלַי" וָמֵת — לֹא יִתְּנוּ הַיּוֹרְשִׁים, שֶׁאֵין דָּמִים לַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There are *halakhot* that are more **stringent with regard to valuations than with regard to vows** of assessment. **How so?** In the case of **one who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my valuation, and** then **dies,** his **heirs must give** his valuation to the Temple treasury. But one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment, and** then **dies,** his **heirs** need **not give** his assessment to the Temple treasury, **as there is no monetary** value **for the dead.**

The Mishnah on this daf continues the comparison between *arachin* and *nedarim*. To recall the basic framework: *arachin* are the special vows for which the Torah provides its own fixed pricelist, whereas *nedarim* — in the sense used here — are individual vows in which one pledges the value of a person or object as assessed on the slave market or the open market. The Mishnah opens with the declaration *chomer be'arachin mibinedarim* — in the following respects, *arachin* are stricter than *nedarim*.

### Death Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "עֵרֶךְ יָדִי", וְ"עֵרֶךְ רַגְלִי עָלַי" — לָא אָמַר כְּלוּם. "עֵרֶךְ רֹאשִׁי", וְ"עֵרֶךְ כְּבֵידִי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ כּוּלּוֹ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** One who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of my forearm, or: The valuation of my leg, has not said anything,** as there are valuations in the Torah only for a complete person. But if one says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of my head, or: The valuation of my liver, he gives the valuation of his entire** self. **This is the principle:** One who valuates **an item upon which the soul is dependent,** i.e., without which one will die, **gives the valuation of his entire** self.

The first case: *ha'omer erchi alai umet yitnu hayorshin* — if one takes a pledge to give one's *erach* to the *Beis Hamikdash* and then dies, the heirs must still pay. By contrast, if one said *damai alai* — "my value is upon me," which is *lashon neder* — and then dies, the heirs do not pay: *she'ein damim lemetim*, because dead people cannot be evaluated. Dead persons are not sold on the slave market, and it is the value of a living person that is required. There is also the consideration that a dead body is *assur be-hana'ah*, forbidden for benefit, rendering any such evaluation categorically inapplicable.

### Life-Dependent Limbs

The Mishnah continues: *erach yadi ve'erach ragli alai lo amar klum* — if one says "I wish to pledge the *erach* of my hand or of my foot," this is a meaningless statement and nothing is owed. By contrast: *erach roshi ve'erach kivedi alai noten erach kulo* — if one says "I wish to pledge the *erach* of my head or of my liver," one must give the full *erach* of the whole person. The *klal* is stated explicitly: *davar she'haneshama teluya bo noten erach kulo* — any limb upon which one's life depends obligates the full *erach*. Hand and foot, by contrast — *ein haneshama teluya bo* — one can live without them, and the pledge is therefore meaningless in the context of *arachin*.

### Half-Value Pledges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "חֲצִי עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן חֲצִי עֶרְכּוֹ, "עֵרֶךְ חֶצְיִי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ כּוּלּוֹ, "חֲצִי דָּמַי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן חֲצִי דָּמָיו, "דְּמֵי חֶצְיִי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי כוּלּוֹ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ — נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי כוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** One who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **half of my valuation, gives half of his valuation.** But one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of half of me, gives the valuation of his entire** self. Likewise, one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **half of my assessment, gives half of his assessment;** one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the assessment of half of me, gives the assessment of his entire** self. **This is the principle:** One who takes a vow with regard to **an item upon which the soul is dependent gives the assessment of his entire** self.

The Mishnah then addresses partial pledges. *Chatzi erchi alai noten chatzi ercho* — if one says "I pledge half of my *erach*," one gives half of the Torah's fixed valuation. But *erach chatzi alai noten erach kulo* — if one says "the *erach* of half of me," one must give the entire *erach*, for the same reason: one cannot live without half one's body, making this a *davar she'haneshama teluya bo*.

Turning to the parallel rules for *nedarim*: *chatzi damai alai noten chatzi damav* — pledging half one's value means paying half one's value. Yet *dmei chatzi alai noten dmei kulo* — pledging "the value of half of me" requires giving the full value, since the same principle of *davar she'haneshama teluya bo* applies: *ze haklal davar she'haneshama teluya bo noten dmei kulo*. This rule applies equally to *arachin* and to *nedarim*.

It is worth noting that yesterday's Mishnah established an important asymmetry: by *nedarim*, pledging the value of a limb *she'ein haneshama teluya bo* — such as a hand or a foot — does require payment of that limb's actual value, whereas by *arachin*, such a pledge is entirely meaningless and nothing is owed.

### Another's Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: "עֶרְכּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי עָלַי", מֵת הַנּוֹדֵר וְהַנִּידָּר — יִתְּנוּ הַיּוֹרְשִׁין. "דָּמָיו שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי עָלַי", וּמֵת הַנּוֹדֵר — יִתְּנוּ הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, מֵת הַנִּידָּר — לֹא יִתְּנוּ הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, שֶׁאֵין דָּמִים לַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of so-and-so,** and both **the one who vowed and the object of the vow die,** the **heirs** of the one who vowed **must give** the valuation of the object of the vow to the Temple treasury. With regard to one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the assessment of so-and-so, and the one who vowed dies,** his **heirs must give** his assessment to the Temple treasury. If **the object of the vow dies,** the **heirs** of the one who vowed need **not give** his assessment to the Temple treasury, **as there is no monetary** value **for the dead.**

The Mishnah addresses a further case: *ha'omer ercho shel ploni alai* — one who pledges the *erach* of another person. *Met hanoder vehanidar yitnu hayorshin* — if both the pledger and the one whose *erach* was pledged die, the heirs must still pay. By contrast, in a *neder* of *damav shel ploni*: if the pledger dies, the heirs still pay; but if the one whose value was pledged dies — *met hanidar lo yitnu hayorshin she'ein damim lemetim* — the heirs do not pay, again because there is no way to evaluate the value of someone already dead.

### Nedarim Strictures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חוֹמֶר בַּנְּדָרִים מִבַּעֲרָכִין, שֶׁהַנְּדָרִים חָלִין עַל בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף, וְאֵין נִדּוֹנִים בְּהֶשֵּׂג יָד, מָה שֶׁאֵין כֵּן בָּעֲרָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There are *halakhot* that are more **stringent with regard to vows** of assessment **than with regard to valuations, as vows** of assessment **take effect with regard to a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, and a bird.** If one says: It is incumbent upon me to donate the assessment of this bird, he must donate that assessment. This is not the case with regard to valuations, as they do not take effect with regard to animals and birds. **And** another *halakha* that is more stringent with regard to vows of assessment than with regard to valuations is that the destitute who take vows of assessment **are not assessed based on affordability. This is not the case with regard to valuations,** which are assessed based on whether the one who vows can afford to pay what they vowed.

The Gemara then cites a *Baraisa* that presents the reverse list: *chomer binedarim mibe'arachin* — the ways in which *nedarim* are stricter than *arachin*. *She'ha-nedarim chalin al behemah chaya va-of* — *nedarim* can apply to domestic animals, wild animals, and birds, whereas *arachin* apply only to human beings. Additionally, *nedarim* do not have the *heseg yad* discount that applies to *arachin*: if a person is too poor to afford the *arach* listed in the Torah, the *kohen* evaluates how much that person can afford, and that reduced amount suffices — *mah she'ein ken be-arach*. No such concession applies to *nedarim*; regardless of one's means, the full value must be paid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בָּעֲרָכִין מִבַּנְּדָרִים, כֵּיצַד? אָמַר: "עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" וָמֵת — יִתְּנוּ יוֹרְשִׁין, "דָּמַי עָלַי" וָמֵת — לֹא יִתְּנוּ יוֹרְשִׁין, שֶׁאֵין דָּמִים לַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** There is a *halakha* that is more **stringent with regard to valuations than with regard to vows** of assessment. **How so?** In the case of one who **said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my valuation, and dies,** his **heirs must give** his valuation to the Temple treasury. But one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment, and dies,** his **heirs** need **not give** his assessment to the Temple treasury, **as there is no monetary** value **for the dead.**

The *Baraisa* then restates the corresponding rules in which *arachin* are stricter, replicating the substance of our Mishnah: *erchi alai va-met yitnu yorshin* — the heirs pay the *erach*, whereas *dami alai va-met lo yitnu yorshin* — the heirs do not pay the *neder* value — *she'ein damim le-metim*.

## Oral Loans Analysis

### Oral Loan Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר "עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" וָמֵת — יִתְּנוּ יוֹרְשִׁין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין? שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּמִלְוָה כְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* taught with regard to a person who **said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my valuation, and dies,** that his **heirs must give** his valuation to the Temple treasury. The Gemara suggests one can **conclude from** this ruling that **one collects a loan by oral agreement from the heirs,** as no promissory note was written with regard to this obligation. The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Here** the heirs' obligation to pay the valuation of the deceased **is different,** i.e., it is not the same as a loan by oral agreement, **as** a valuation **is** considered **a loan,** i.e., debt, **written in the Torah,** in accordance with the deceased's age and sex at the time of the vow.

The Gemara fastens onto that last line of the *Baraisa* — that one who pledges *erchi alai* and then dies leaves the heirs obligated to pay — and asks: *shema mina milveh al peh goveh min ha-yorshin*? Can we derive from here that an oral loan — one made verbally with witnesses but without a written contract — may be collected from heirs? The Gemara's reasoning is that the *arach* obligation involved no formal contract; the person merely stated verbally that he wished to make the pledge. If the *Beis Hamikdash* can nevertheless collect from heirs, does this not establish that oral monetary obligations are likewise heritable?

### Torah-Written Status

The Gemara immediately deflects this inference: *shani hacha de-milveh kesuvah ba-Torah hi* — this case is different, because the obligation is written in the Torah. Even though no individual contract was drawn up, the *arach* is not strictly an oral loan in the relevant sense, since the Torah itself records the obligation. One therefore cannot derive any ruling about ordinary oral loans from this case.

### Baraisa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מִלְוָה הַכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה כִּכְתוּבָה בִּשְׁטָר דָּמְיָא? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: If so, can one **conclude from** the *baraita* that **a loan that is written in the Torah,** i.e., any financial obligation that applies by Torah law, **is considered** as though **it were written in a document?** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where he stood in judgment** before he died, and the court obligated him to donate his valuation. The court's ruling is considered like a financial obligation written in a document, and therefore the heirs are required to pay his valuation.

The Gemara then pivots to the other side of the *Baraisa*: since *dami alai va-met lo yitnu yorshin* — when the pledge was a *neder*, the heirs do not pay — might one not infer from here that *milveh al peh* cannot be collected from heirs? The Gemara responds: no, because the reason the heirs do not pay in the *neder* case is a technicality — *she'ein damim le-metim*. The evaluation cannot be performed because the person is dead. The implication, then, is striking: the distinction in the Mishnah between *arachin* and *nedarim* appears to be purely technical. *Arachin* carry a fixed price, so heirs can pay without any evaluation. *Nedarim* require evaluation of a living person — and once that person is dead, the evaluation is impossible. Had the evaluation already been completed before death, the heirs would seemingly be obligated to pay even in the *neder* case.

### Court Lien

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְוָותַהּ גַּבֵּי "דָּמַי עָלַי" שֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין, אַמַּאי לֹא יִתְּנוּ יוֹרְשִׁין "דָּמַי עָלַי"? מְחוּסַּר אוּמְדָּנָא. "עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" — לֹא מְחוּסָּר וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, then **in the corresponding** situation, **with regard to** one who said: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment,** the *baraita* must also be referring to one **who stood in judgment.** But in that case, **why don't the heirs have to donate** his assessment? The Gemara answers that in the case of one who said: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my assessment,** even if he stood in judgment and the court obligated him to pay, **the assessment** of the precise amount he must pay is still **lacking,** as that amount is determined only through the assessment of a court. By contrast, with regard to one who said: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **my valuation,** if he stood in judgment and the court obligated him to pay, **nothing is lacking,** as the amount he pays is fixed by the Torah.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty with an *okimta*: *hacha be-mai askinan ke-she'amad be-din* — we are dealing with a case where the person not only stated the pledge verbally but subsequently appeared in court, and the court ruled that his statement constituted a valid *arach* obligation. Once the court has so ruled, the obligation acquires the status of *ke-katuv be-shtar dami* — it is as though it were written in a contract — and the heirs are therefore bound. The mere verbal pledge, before any court ruling, may still retain the status of *milveh al peh*. Thus no general inference about oral loans can be extracted.

### Two Evaluations

The Gemara then challenges this answer: if the *ha'amada be-din* is what makes the obligation heritable even in *neder* cases, why does the Mishnah say the heirs do not pay in the *neder* case? Once the pledger has appeared in court and the court validated his *neder*, should the heirs not be obligated?

The answer is that *nedarim* require two distinct steps: the court must evaluate whether the obligation was validly contracted (*ha'amada be-din*), and then a separate evaluation of the value of the person whose *damim* were pledged must be carried out. *Dami alai mechusar umdana* — the *neder* pledge remains deficient until that second evaluation has been performed. By contrast, *erchi alai lo mechusar ve-lo klum* — once the court has ruled that the *erach* pledge was valid, no further evaluative step is needed, because the Torah itself supplies the valuation. Accordingly, the *seifa* of the Mishnah dealing with *damav shel ploni alai* comes to teach a precise point: *de-keivan d-amad be-din ishtabdu nikhsei* — once the court has ruled on the obligation, a lien is immediately created on the property. The subsequent evaluation of the pledgee's value merely reveals retroactively the amount of that lien; the lien itself existed from the moment of the court's ruling.

## Limbs & Meaningless

### Hand/Foot Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵרֶךְ יָדִי וְרַגְלִי עָלַי וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: וְנוֹתֵן דָּמֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of my forearm, or of my leg,** has not said anything, as there are valuations in the Torah only for a complete person. **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: And** although he is exempt from donating the valuation, **he must give the assessment** of the forearm or leg.

The Gemara turns to the Mishnah's case of *erach yadi ve'erach ragli alai* — pledging the *erach* of one's hand or foot — which the Mishnah had ruled *lo amar klum*, a meaningless statement under *arachin*. This stands in contrast to pledging the *erach* of one's head or liver, which requires full payment.

### Rav Gidel Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא "לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם" קָתָנֵי! לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם לְרַבָּנַן, וְנוֹתֵן דָּמֶיהָ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the mishna that **he has not said anything?** The Gemara answers: The ruling of the mishna that **he has not said anything** applies **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, but he must give the assessment according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Rabbi Meir maintains that a person does not utter a statement for naught, and therefore he must have intended to vow to donate the assessment of the limb, as there is no valuation for limbs.

*Amar Rav Gidel amar Rav ve-noten dameha* — Rav Gidel in the name of Rav rules that one who says *erach yadi ve'erach ragli* must give the value of the hand or foot. This appears to contradict the Mishnah's ruling of *lo amar klum*. The Gemara resolves the tension: the Mishnah reflects the position of the *Rabbanan*, who hold *adam motzi devarav le-vatalah* — people do sometimes make meaningless statements, and this is one such case. Rav Gidel's ruling reflects the view of Rabbi Meir, who holds *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatalah* — people do not normally make meaningless statements. Therefore, according to Rabbi Meir, if one said *erach yadi*, one must have meant to pledge the hand's monetary value (*damim*), and must pay accordingly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אַמְרַהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר "עֵרֶךְ כְּלִי זֶה עָלַי" נוֹתֵן דָּמָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **didn't** Rav Giddel already **say it once, as Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says:** In the case of **one who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the valuation of this vessel,** although he is exempt from donating the valuation of the vessel, according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir **he must give the assessment** of the vessel.

The Gemara then asks: but Rav Gidel himself had already told us about this *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan*. What does his new ruling here add? The reference is to an earlier statement of Rav Gidel: *ha'omer erech kli zeh alai noten damav* — one who says the *erach* of a vessel should be upon him must pay the vessel's value. Everyone knows that *arachin* do not apply to vessels; therefore, according to Rabbi Meir, since the person knew this, he must have intended the pledge as a *neder* and must pay the vessel's value.

### Vessel Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָתָם הוּא דְּאָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין עֵרֶךְ לִכְלִי, וְגָמַר וְאָמַר לְשֵׁם דָּמִים, אֲבָל הָכָא מִיטְעָא קָטָעֵי, דְּסָבַר כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִיכָּא "עֵרֶךְ רֹאשִׁי" וְ"עֵרֶךְ כְּבֵידִי״, אִיכָּא נָמֵי "עֵרֶךְ יָדִי" וְ"עֵרֶךְ רַגְלִי", אֲבָל דְּמֵי לָא קָאָמַר — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It was necessary for Rav Giddel to repeat that *halakha* with regard to one who vowed the valuation of his forearm or leg, **lest you say** this ruling **is** applicable only **there,** with regard to a vessel, **as a person knows that there is no valuation for a vessel, and he** therefore **intended and said** his vow **for the sake of assessments. But here,** with regard to his forearm or leg, **he erred, as he thought that just as there is** an obligation to donate the valuation in a case where one said: It is incumbent upon me to donate **the valuation of my head, or: The valuation of my liver, so too, there is** an obligation to donate the valuation if one said: It is incumbent upon me to donate the **valuation of my forearm or the valuation of my leg. But he did not** intend to **say** a vow of **assessments.** Therefore, Rav Giddel **teaches us** that even in the case of one who vowed to donate the valuation of his forearm or leg, he did not speak in error but intended to donate the assessment.

One might have thought that the vessel case and the limb case are distinguishable. In the vessel case, everyone knows that *arachin* do not apply to *kelim*, so when someone says *erech kli zeh*, we confidently assume he intended it as a *neder* for its market value — *gamar ve'amar le-shem damim*. In the limb case, however, one might argue that the person simply made an error: he may have assumed, by analogy with pledging the *erach* of one's head or liver, that pledging the *erach* of one's hand or foot was equally valid — *savar ki heichi de-ika erech roshi ve-erech kveidi, ika nami erech yadi ve-erach ragli*. In that case, he never intended to pledge the hand's monetary value at all — *aval damim lo ka'amar*. Rav Gidel therefore comes to teach us that even in this limb case, Rabbi Meir holds *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatalah*: the person must have intended to pledge the actual value of his hand or foot. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, hold that people do sometimes speak loosely, and the pledge is therefore meaningless.

## Life Dependency Rule

### Nefashot Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "עֵרֶךְ רֹאשִׁי" וְ"עֵרֶךְ כְּבֵידִי" — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ כּוּלּוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? "נְפָשׁוֹת" קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one says: It is incumbent upon me to donate the **valuation of my head, or: The valuation of my liver, he gives the valuation of his entire** self. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers that **the Merciful One states:** "A valuation of **living people"** (Leviticus 27:2), which indicates that in the case of one who valuates an item upon which the soul is dependent and without which one will die, he must give the valuation of his entire self.

The Mishnah had stated *erach roshi ve'erach kveidi noten erach kulo* — pledging the *erach* of one's head or liver requires paying the full *erach*. The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama*? The answer: *nefashot ka'amar Rachmana* — the Torah's passage concerning *arachin* employs the word *nefashot*, invoking a person's life. This grounds the rule that any limb upon which one's life depends obligates the full *erach*.

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ וכו׳. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that **this is the principle:** One who valuates **an item upon which the soul is dependent** and without which one will die gives the valuation of his entire self. The Gemara explains that the phrase: This is the principle, serves **to include** one who vowed to donate the valuation of his leg **from the knee and above.** Since one would die if his leg were severed above the knee, this is considered an item upon which the soul is dependent, and therefore he gives the valuation of his entire self.

The Mishnah then states the *klal*: *davar she'haneshama teluya bo — kulo*. The Gemara asks what this general rule comes to teach beyond the cases already explicit in the Mishnah — the head, the liver, and pledging half the body.

### Leg Application

The Gemara answers: *le-atuyei min ha-arkuvah ulema'lah* — the general rule comes to include the leg cut off above the knee, which is likewise something one cannot live without and thus constitutes a *davar she'haneshama teluya bo*. This was itself the subject of a *machloket* in Masechet Chullin in the chapter of *Elu Trefot* regarding whether loss of a leg above the knee renders an animal a *trefah*, but this Gemara clearly adopts the position that it does — and accordingly, pledging the *erach* of one's whole leg (not merely up to the knee) requires paying the full *erach* of the entire person.

### Half Distinction Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִי עֶרְכִּי עָלַי נוֹתֵן כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "חֲצִי עֶרְכִּי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן חֲצִי עֶרְכּוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה וְנוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **half of my valuation, gives** half of his valuation. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:3): One who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **half of my valuation, gives half of his valuation. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: He is flogged and must give** his **full valuation.**

The Mishnah's formulation regarding partial pledges — *chatzi erchi alai noten chatzi ercho*, *erach chatzi alai noten erach kulo* — is then examined through a *Baraisa*. The first position in the *Baraisa* simply restates the Mishnah: pledging half one's *erach* requires paying half one's *erach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקֶה?! אַמַּאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לוֹקֶה בְּעֵרֶךְ שָׁלֵם, מַאי טַעְמָא? גְּזֵירָה "חֲצִי (עֶרְכִּי) [עֶרְכּוֹ]" אַטּוּ "עֵרֶךְ חֶצְיוֹ", וְעֵרֶךְ חֶצְיוֹ הָוֵי דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since he did not commit a sin, **why is he flogged? Rav Pappa said** in response: **He is flogged,** i.e., punished, **in** that he must donate his **full valuation. What is the reason** he needs to give his full valuation? A rabbinic **decree** was issued that one who says: It is incumbent upon me to donate **half of my valuation,** must give the value of his entire self, **due to** a concern that this case might become confused with the similar one of a person who vows to donate **the valuation of half of himself. And** the *halakha* in such a case is that he must give the value of his entire self, as **the valuation of half of him is an item upon which the soul is dependent.**

However, Rabbi Yossi beRabbi Yehuda rules: *loke ve-noten erech shalem* — one receives punishment and must pay the full *erach*. The Gemara asks: *loke amai*? What transgression warrants lashes? No prohibition was violated. Rav Pappa explains that *loke* here does not mean literal lashes but rather that one is "punished" in the sense of being required to pay the full *erach*. The reason for this *gezeirah*: *gezeirah chatzi erchi atu erech chatzyi* — since *erach chatzyi* (the *erach* of half of me) obligates the full *erach*, and people can easily confuse that formulation with *chatzi erchi* (half of my *erach*), Rabbi Yossi beRabbi Yehuda rules *mi-derabanan* that either formulation requires full payment. And why does *erach chatzyi* itself require the full *erach*? *Havei davar she'haneshama teluya bo* — half of one's body is something one cannot live without.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "חֲצִי דָּמַי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן כּוּ׳. "דְּמֵי חֶצְיִי עָלַי" — נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי כּוּלּוֹ, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **half of my assessment, gives** half of his assessment. But one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **the assessment of half of me, gives the assessment of his entire** self, as one who takes a vow of assessment with regard to an item upon which the soul depends must give the assessment of his entire self. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** After all, it is only with regard to valuations that the Torah states: "A valuation of living people" (Leviticus 27:2), indicating that one who valuates an item upon which the soul depends must give the valuation of his entire self; but this is not stated with regard to assessments.

The Gemara then addresses the parallel *neder* case: *chatzi damai alai noten chatzi damav*; *dmei chatzyi alai noten dmei kulo*. The Gemara asks: how do we know this rule of *davar she'haneshama teluya bo* applies to *nedarim* as well? The answer lies in the *pasuk*: *neder be-erkecha nefashot la-Hashem* — the *pasuk* juxtaposes the language of *neder* and *erech*, creating a *hekesh* that allows us to derive the rule for *nedarim* from *arachin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ נְפָשׁוֹת" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **it is written:** "When a man shall clearly utter **a vow [*neder*] with regard to a valuation [*be'erkekha*] of living people"** (Leviticus 27:2). The verse juxtaposes vows of assessment [*neder*] and valuations [*erekh*], thereby teaching that just as one who takes a vow of valuation with regard to an item upon which the soul depends must give the valuation of his entire self, so too, one who takes a vow of assessment with regard to an item upon which the soul is dependent must give the assessment of his entire self.

The Gemara notes that the *klal* stated in the Mishnah for *nedarim* serves the same function as the *klal* for *arachin*: both come *le-atuyei min ha-arkuvah ulema'lah*, to include the leg above the knee as a *davar she'haneshama teluya bo* — applicable equally to both categories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that **this is the principle:** One who takes a vow of assessment with regard to **an item upon which the soul is dependent** gives the assessment of his entire self. The Gemara explains that the phrase: This is the principle, serves **to include** one who vowed to donate the assessment of his leg **from the knee and above.** Since one would die if his leg were severed above the knee, this is an item upon which the soul depends, and therefore he gives the assessment of his entire self.

## Vessel Pledge Dispute

### Vessel Half-Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּעֲרִיךְ חֲצִי עֵרֶךְ כְּלִי, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן דָּמָיו, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who valuates** in order to donate **half the valuation of a vessel, Rabbi Meir says: He gives** half **the assessment of** the vessel, **and the Rabbis say: He has not said anything.**

The Gemara now cites a *Baraisa* returning to the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* regarding vessels. *Ha-ma'arich chatzi erech kli* — one who takes an *erach* pledge of half the value of a vessel: *Rabbi Meir omer noten damav* — Rabbi Meir rules one must pay half the vessel's value, treating the pledge as a *neder*. *Ve-Chachamim omrim lo amar klum* — the *Chachamim* rule it is a meaningless statement.

### Rava Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא חֲלַשׁ, עוּל לְגַבֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי וְרַבָּנַן, וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר קָסָבַר אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה, לָא שְׁנָא כּוּלּוֹ וְלָא שְׁנָא חֶצְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava became sick,** and **Abaye and** other **Sages came to** visit **him.** As **they were sitting and** talking, **they said: Granted** that **Rabbi Meir holds** that **a person does not utter a statement for naught,** and therefore there **is no difference** whether one vowed to donate the **entire** valuation **of** a vessel, **and** there **is no difference** whether one vowed to donate **half its** valuation; in either case he must have intended to donate the assessment, as there is no valuation with regard to vessels.

When Rava fell ill, Abaye and a group of *Rabbanan* came for *bikur cholim*, and they occupied themselves with this *Baraisa*. They noted: *bishlama Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir's position is entirely consistent and easy to understand. He holds *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatalah*, and accordingly *lo shna kulo ve-lo shna chetzyo*: it makes no difference whether you pledged the whole vessel's value or half — in either case you must pay. But what is the position of the *Rabbanan* of this *Baraisa*? The Gemara observes that the *Baraisa* must be making a meaningful distinction between pledging half the value and pledging the whole value. If the *Rabbanan* here were the same *Rabbanan* who hold that pledging even the whole vessel's *erach* is meaningless, there would be no reason to formulate this *Baraisa* specifically around the half-value case. The fact that "half" is specified must mean that the *Rabbanan* here hold that pledging the full vessel's value does have effect — only the half-value pledge is meaningless.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבָּנַן מַאי קָסָבְרִי? אִי אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה — אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלּוֹ נָמֵי, וְאִי אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה — אֲפִילּוּ חֶצְיוֹ נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **the Rabbis** in the *baraita*, **what do they hold? If** they maintain that **a person utters a statement for naught,** then **even** if one vowed to donate the **entire** valuation **of** a vessel, he should be exempt and the Rabbis should disagree with regard to that case. **And if** the Rabbis maintain that **a person does not utter a statement for naught,** then **even** one who vowed to donate **half** the valuation of a vessel should be obligated to give half of the assessment.

### Combined Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא וְאַבָּיֵי: רַבָּנַן דְּהָכָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבְרִי לַהּ, וְסָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי׳ שִׁמְעוֹן. סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: "אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה".
>
> **Translation:** **Rava and Abaye said to** the Sages in response: **The Rabbis here** in the *baraita* **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, but they** also **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Rava and Abaye elaborated: **They hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a person does not utter a statement for naught,** and therefore one who vows to donate the valuation of a vessel must give its assessment.

The resolution offered jointly by Rava and Abaye: *rabbanan de-hacha ke-Rabbi Meir savri lei ve-savri lah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the *Rabbanan* of this *Baraisa* hold like Rabbi Meir on one *machloket* and like Rabbi Shimon on another. They hold like Rabbi Meir in that *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatalah* — people do not make meaningless statements — and this is why pledging the whole vessel's value is treated as a valid *neder*.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר שֶׁלֹּא הִתְנַדֵּב כְּדֶרֶךְ הַמִּתְנַדְּבִים; כּוּלֵּיהּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ לְאִיתְנַדּוֹבֵי, פַּלְגֵיהּ לָאו אוֹרְחֵיהּ לְאִיתְנַדּוֹבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And they** also **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** with regard to one who consecrated an item for a purpose for which it was unsuited, e.g., if one sought to bring a barley meal offering even though meal offerings may be brought only from wheat (see *Menaḥot* 103a). Rabbi Shimon **says** he is exempt from any obligation, **as** this person **did not donate in the manner** typical **of donors.** So too, with regard to one who vows to donate the valuation of a vessel, it is the typical **manner** for one **to donate an entire** vessel, and therefore one who does so is obligated to donate the assessment of the vessel. But it is **not** the typical **manner** for one **to donate half** a vessel, and therefore one who vows in this manner has not said anything and is exempt.

At the same time, they hold like Rabbi Shimon, who rules *she-lo hitnadev ke-derech ha-mitnadvim* — a pledge is only binding if it was made in a normal manner of pledging. *Kulei orchei le-mitnadvei* — it is entirely normal for people to pledge the full value of an object; *palgei lav orchei le-mitnadvei* — pledging half the value of something is not the normal way people make donations to the *Beis Hamikdash*. Accordingly, the half-value pledge is not a valid *neder* at all. Tosafot raises a question here: when exactly is pledging half the value of something not normal? Tosafot therefore prefers to emend the reading throughout this passage from "half the value of a vessel" to "the value of half a vessel" — meaning the value of a broken vessel, which is essentially worthless rubble. Pledging the value of worthless rubble to the *Beis Hamikdash* is plainly not a normal manner of making a donation, and Rabbi Shimon's principle therefore excludes it.

## Nedarim/Nedavot

### Object Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ "שׁוֹר זֶה עוֹלָה", וּ"בַיִת זֶה קׇרְבָּן", וּמֵת הַשּׁוֹר וְנָפַל הַבַּיִת — פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. "שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה", וּ"בַיִת זֶה עָלַי קׇרְבָּן", מֵת הַשּׁוֹר וְנָפַל הַבַּיִת — חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of one who says: **This bull** is consecrated as **a burnt offering, or: This house** is consecrated as **an offering, and the bull died or the house collapsed,** he **is exempt from paying** his commitment. But in the case of one who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to give **this bull** as **a burnt offering, or:** It is incumbent **upon me** to give **this house** as **an offering,** if **the bull died or the house collapsed,** he is **obligated to pay** its value.

The Gemara moves on to the next Mishnah, which shifts from *arachin* altogether and now compares *nedarim* and *nedavot* — two modes of donating animals or objects to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The Mishnah opens with two cases: *shor zeh olah* — "this ox should be a *korban olah*"; *bayit zeh korban* — "this house should be *korban*." The Mishnah teaches that by saying *korban* about something that cannot itself be brought as a *korban*, such as a house, the object is sanctified with *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* — it goes to the temple treasury. If the ox then dies or the house collapses, the pledger is *patur mi-lishalem* — exempt from paying — because all he pledged was this specific object, not a personal monetary obligation. This is the classic distinction between a *neder* formulated as *harei zu* (this object) and one formulated as *harei alai* (it is upon me): the former changes the status of the object; the latter creates a personal *chiyuv* on the pledger.

### Mishnah Reread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר "דְּמֵי שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה", אֲבָל אָמַר "שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה", כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר "זֶה" וָמֵת — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, "עָלַי לַהֲבִיאוֹ" קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav says:** The Sages **taught** in the mishna **only that** one who **said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to give **the monetary value of this bull** as **a burnt offering,** is obligated to pay its value if it dies. **But** if **he said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to give **this bull** as **a burnt offering, since he said: This,** thereby referring specifically to that animal, **and** then the bull **died, he does not bear** financial **responsibility for its** loss. And as for his statement: It is incumbent upon me, he did not intend to accept financial responsibility for the loss of the bull. Rather, he meant to **say:** It is incumbent **upon me to bring** this bull as an offering to the Temple.

Rabbi Chiya bar Rav interprets the Mishnah's language carefully: the case of *shor zeh alai olah* — "this ox should be upon me as an *olah*" — where the Mishnah says the pledger remains liable even if the ox dies, should not be read too literally. Rabbi Chiya bar Rav says: *lo shanu ela de'amar dmei shor zeh alai olah* — this applies only if one said "the value of this ox should be upon me." If one simply said "this ox should be upon me," the intent is *alai lehavio* — it is upon me to bring this animal, but not to replace it if it dies.

### Zeh vs Alai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שׁוֹר זֶה עוֹלָה — הַשּׁוֹר הֶקְדֵּשׁ וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מֵת אוֹ נִגְנַב — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ. "שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה" — הַשּׁוֹר הֶקְדֵּשׁ וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מֵת אוֹ נִגְנַב — חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:11): With regard to one who says: **This bull is** consecrated as **a burnt offering, the bull is consecrated property and** one who benefits **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. If the bull **dies or is stolen, he does not bear** financial **responsibility for its** loss. If one says: It is incumbent **upon me** to give **this bull** as **a burnt offering, the bull is consecrated property, and** one who benefits **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. If the bull **dies or is stolen, he bears** financial **responsibility for its** loss, despite the fact that he did not vow to give the monetary value of the bull.

The Gemara challenges this interpretation from a *Tosefta*. The *Tosefta* distinguishes three formulations. First, *shor zeh olah*: the ox itself becomes *hekdesh*, with *kedushat haguf*, and one can violate *me'ilah* by deriving benefit from it; if it dies or is stolen, the pledger owes nothing further. Second, *shor zeh alai olah*: again the ox itself is *hekdesh* and *me'ilah* applies, but now — since the word *alai* was used — if it dies or is stolen, *chayav be-achrayuto*, the pledger must pay its full value. Third, *dmei shor olah*: the ox itself is *chullin* and there is no *me'ilah* concern; if it dies or is stolen, the pledger is not liable for this specific ox — *eino chayav be-achrayuto* — but is liable for its value — *aval chayav be-achrayut damav* — since what was pledged was a monetary value, not a specific animal.

### Tosefta Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אַלִּימָא מִמַּתְנִיתִין, דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא דְּקָאָמַר "דְּמֵי"? הָכָא נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר "דְּמֵי".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Is** the *baraita* **stronger than the mishna,** with regard to which **we established that** it is referring to a case where **he says: The monetary value of** the bull? **Here too,** in the case of the *baraita*, **he says:** It is incumbent upon me to give **the monetary value of** this bull as a burnt offering.

The Gemara attempts to apply the same *okimta* to the *Tosefta* as Rabbi Chiya bar Rav applied to the Mishnah: that *shor zeh alai* in the *Tosefta* should also be read as *dmei shor zeh alai*. But this is more difficult here, because the *Tosefta* itself goes on to discuss a case where *dmei* is stated explicitly — and if both the second and third cases of the *Tosefta* refer to pledging the "value," the distinction between them collapses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּסֵיפָא דְּקָאָמַר "דְּמֵי", רֵישָׁא דְּלָא קָאָמַר "דְּמֵי"! דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: דְּמֵי שׁוֹר עוֹלָה — הַשּׁוֹר חוּלִּין וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מֵת אוֹ נִגְנַב — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, אָבֵל חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּת דָּמָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he says: Monetary value of,** it may be inferred that **the first clause** of the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he does not say monetary value. As it teaches** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:12): With regard to one who said: It is incumbent upon me to give **the monetary value of** this **bull** as **a burnt offering, the bull is non-sacred** property **and** one who benefits **from it** is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. If the bull **dies or is stolen, he does not bear** financial **responsibility for its** loss. **But** if he sold the bull and received money for the sale, **he does bear** financial **responsibility** for that money, since he accepted financial responsibility **for the monetary value of** the bull in his vow.

The Gemara therefore offers a more elaborate *okimta*: the second case — *reisha* — is where one said *yakdish ha-shor le-damav*, sanctifying the ox itself for its monetary proceeds, which both designated the specific ox as *hekdesh* and imposed a personal obligation to pay its value. The third case — *seifa* — is where one said *le-ch'she-yavo damav yikdashu*: when its value arrives — meaning, when I sell this ox, those proceeds will automatically become *hekdesh*. Accordingly, *eino chayav be-achrayuto* means the pledger does not owe the value of a whole live animal if it dies or is stolen; but *aval chayav be-achrayut damav* means that whatever proceeds the animal does fetch upon sale — even as a carcass — must go to the *Beis Hamikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא דְּקָאָמַר "דְּמֵי", רֵישָׁא דְּאָמַר: "יִקְדַּישׁ הַשּׁוֹר לְדָמָיו", וְסֵיפָא דְּקָאָמַר: "לִכְשֶׁיָּבֹאוּ דָּמָיו יִקְדְּשׁוּ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The first clause and the latter clause** of the *baraita* are both referring to one **who says: Monetary value of,** in his vow. The difference between them is that **the first clause** is dealing with **one who says: Let the bull be consecrated for its monetary value,** and therefore the bull is consecrated and he bears financial responsibility for its value. **But the latter clause** is referring to **one who says: When** this bull shall be sold, **the money received** from the sale **shall be consecrated** for use as a burnt offering.

## House Rental Case

### House Plague

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַהָא, הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵרוֹ וְנִתְנַגֵּעַ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחֲלָטוֹ כֹּהֵן, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: "הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ", נְתָצוֹ — חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ בַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **And some teach** this discussion **with regard to this** *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Metzia* 8:30): In the case of **one who rents a house to another and** the house **became ritually impure with leprosy, even though a priest confirmed** the house as impure, condemning it to be dismantled, as long as the house is still standing its owner may **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you,** and the owner is not required to compensate the renter. Nevertheless, in accordance with the *halakha* of a house afflicted with leprosy, once the priest has **dismantled** the house the owner is **obligated to provide** a new **house for** the renter.

The Gemara cites a *Baraisa* about a house rental situation: *ha-maskir bayit le-chaveiro ve-nitnaga* — one who rents a house to another person, and the house then contracts *nega'im* — the biblical plague that requires condemnation and eventual demolition. Even after *chalto kohen* — the *kohen* has formally condemned the house — the landlord may say *harei shelcha lefanecha*: the house is before you, as it was when I rented it to you. The tenancy was contracted for this specific house, and however unfortunate its fate, the landlord has technically delivered what was promised. However, *nitzo chayav le-ha'amid lo bayit*: once the house is actually demolished, the landlord is obligated to provide the tenant with another house to live in.

### Rent Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְדִּישׁוֹ הַדָּר בּוֹ — מַעֲלֶה שָׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ. הִקְדִּישׁוֹ הַדָּר בּוֹ? הֵיכִי מָצֵי מַקְדִּישׁ לֵהּ? "אִישׁ כִּי יַקְדִּישׁ בֵּיתוֹ קֹדֶשׁ" אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מָה בֵּיתוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ — אַף כֹּל בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מַשְׂכִּיר — הַדָּר בּוֹ מַעֲלֶה שָׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the one living in it,** i.e., the renter, **consecrates** the house, **he pays the rent to the Temple** treasury. The Gemara express surprise at this last statement of the *baraita*: If **the one living in** the house **consecrates** it? **How can** the renter **consecrate it? The Merciful One states: "When a man shall sanctify his house to be sacred"** (Leviticus 27:14), from which it is derived that **just as one's house is in his possession, so too, anything** that one consecrates must be **in his possession.** If so, the renter cannot consecrate the house, as he is not its owner. The Gemara answers that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If **the one renting out the house consecrates it, the one living in it pays the rent to the Temple** treasury.

The *Baraisa* continues with a separate case: *hikdisho ha-maskir* — the landlord was *makdish* the house after renting it out. In that circumstance, *hadar bo ma'aleh sechar le-hekdesh* — the one living there makes the rent payments to *hekdesh*. The Gemara asks: how can the tenant continue to live there at all? If the house itself is *hekdesh*, any benefit derived from it constitutes *me'ilah* — misuse of consecrated property. And once *me'ilah* has been committed — *ein me'ilah achar me'ilah* — the *kedushah* is removed from the object, so why would subsequent rent payments go to *hekdesh* at all?

### Future Value Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מַשְׂכִּיר, הֵיכִי דָּיַיר בֵּיהּ? בִּמְעִילָה קָאֵי! וְתוּ מַעֲלֶה שָׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ? כֵּיוָן דִּמְעַל בֵּיהּ, נָפֵיק לֵיהּ שָׂכָר לְחוּלִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **the one renting out the house consecrates it, how** is it possible for the renter to **live in it?** Since the house is consecrated property **he is standing** liable **for** the prohibition of **misuse of consecrated property** by living there. **And furthermore,** why must **he pay the rent to the Temple** treasury? **Since he misused consecrated property the payment** of the rent immediately **leaves** the state of consecration and becomes **non-sacred.**

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by reinterpreting the *Baraisa*: *de'amar le-ch'she-yavo sachar yikdash* — the landlord did not consecrate the house itself, but rather declared that whenever the rent payment arises, it should automatically become *hekdesh*. The house itself remains *chullin*; the tenant derives no forbidden benefit from it. Whatever rent is paid flows directly to *hekdesh* by virtue of the landlord's prior declaration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר: "לִכְשֶׁיָּבֹא שְׂכָרוֹ יִקְדַּשׁ", וְהָא אֵין אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the one renting out the house **says: When the payment** of the rent **will come** to me, the money **shall be consecrated.** The Gemara asks: **But** how is it possible for the one renting out the house to consecrate money he will receive in the future? After all, **a person cannot consecrate an entity that has not** yet **come into the world.**

This leads to the same question that arose earlier in the passage: *va-ha ein adam makdish davar she-lo ba la-olam* — how can one be *makdish* a rent payment that has not yet been paid and does not yet exist? One cannot consecrate something that has not yet come into the world.

### Rabbi Meir Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאֶלָּא כְּמַאן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: A person can consecrate an entity that has not** yet **come into the world. Some say** that **Rav Pappa said to Abaye, and some say** that **Rami bar Ḥama** said **to Rav Ḥisda: In accordance with whose** opinion is this *baraita*? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a person can consecrate an entity that has not** yet **come into the world? He said to him: Rather, in accordance with who** else's opinion could this *baraita* be? Certainly it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: ha mani? Rabbi Meir hi* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav answers: this *Baraisa* follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who holds *adam makdish davar she-lo ba la-olam* — a person can be *makdish* something that does not yet exist in the world. An alternate tradition records this same exchange as between Rav Papa and Abaye, or between Rami bar Chama and Rav Chisda. In either formulation, the questioner asks: *ke-man? Ke-Rabbi Meir?* — are you saying this *Baraisa* follows Rabbi Meir? And the response is: *amar lei, be-ela ke-man?* — of course; who else would it follow?
