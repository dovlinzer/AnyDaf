# Zevachim 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Outer Chattat Corners

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלָה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב, וּבָא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית, מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית. שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ עַל יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית, וְנֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **He ascended the ramp** of the altar **and turned** right **to the surrounding ledge** and he continued east, **and he came to the southeast corner** and sprinkled the blood of the sin offering there and then to the **northeast** corner and sprinkled the blood there, and then to the **northwest** corner and sprinkled the blood there, and the **southwest** corner, where he performed the fourth sprinkling and descended from the altar. **He would pour the remainder of the blood on the southern base** of the altar. **And** the meat portions of the offering **are eaten within the curtains,** i.e., in the Temple courtyard, **by the males of the priesthood.** And they are eaten prepared **in any** form of **food** preparation, **on** the **day** the offering is sacrificed **and** during the **night** that follows, **until midnight.**

We continue in *Eizehu Mekoman*, the *perek* that many recite daily in *davvening* each morning. The chapter addresses the general *chataot* — specifically the outer *chattat* — and on daf 53a the Gemara turns to the precise mechanics of how blood was applied to the altar's corners.

### Mishna Description

The *mishna* describes the outer *chattat* procedure as follows: the animal was slaughtered in the north, blood was applied to all four corners of the altar, and the remnant blood was spilled at the base — here, at the *south* base rather than the west base, a point to which we will return. The inner *chataot*, by contrast, had their remnant blood spilled at the west base. The *kohen* ascended the *mizbeach* and proceeded counterclockwise, since one always turns rightward with one's face toward the center — the same principle by which a *kallah* circles the *chosson*, and by which a *Sefer Torah* is taken out and returned to the *aron*. The outer *chattat* was eaten by male *kohanim* within the *Beis Hamikdash*.

## Keren Definition Debate

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; חַד אָמַר: נוֹתֵן אַמָּה אֵילָךְ וְאַמָּה אֵילָךְ, וְחַד אָמַר: מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד כְּנֶגֶד חוּדּוֹ שֶׁל קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **How does** the priest **perform** the rite of sprinkling the blood on the corners of the altar? There is a dispute between **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar. One says** that the priest **places** the blood within **a cubit on this** side **or a cubit on that** side. He places the blood wherever he wants, provided that it is within one cubit of the corner of the altar. **And one says** that **he sprinkles** the blood **on the** outer **edge of** the **corner** of the altar, **and** it flows **down** on both sides of the altar.

The Gemara opens with the question *heichi avid* — how exactly was the blood placed at the corner of the altar? The altar's four corners each had a horn (*keren*) measuring one *amah* by one *amah* at the top. The *kohen* would be standing on the *soveiv*, the walk-around, and applying the blood to the horns. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *nosen amah eilech v'amah eilech* — one may place the blood anywhere on the horn, an *amah* in one direction or an *amah* in the other. It need not be precisely at the outermost edge; anywhere on that horn counts. This is the position of one opinion.

### R. Elazar Position

The other opinion holds: *mechatei v'yored k'neged chudo shel keren* — one applies the blood in a downward motion directly opposite the actual edge of the horn. The downward motion is practical as well, preventing blood from soiling one's garments. The verb *mechatei* is drawn from the word *chatas* itself, echoing the verse's language about the *chattat* procedure. According to this view, the application must be at the corner's edge itself, not merely somewhere on the horn's surface.

### Debate Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: הִיא עַצְמָהּ אֵינָהּ נַעֲשֵׂית אֶלָּא בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי; כִּי פְּלִיגִי – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי. מָר סָבַר: אַמָּה אֵילָךְ וְאַמָּה אֵילָךְ – כְּנֶגֶד קֶרֶן הוּא; וּמָר סָבַר: כְּנֶגֶד חוּדָּהּ אִין, טְפֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says** that the sprinkling of the blood of a sin offering **itself is performed only on the actual corner,** the one-by-one-cubit projection above each corner of the altar, **everyone agrees** that the blood may be sprinkled anywhere on the corner, and this will be a fulfillment of the verse: "And put it upon the corners of the altar of burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:25). **When they disagree** is only **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that the blood may be sprinkled anywhere above the red line on the edge of the altar. One **Sage holds** that the blood may be sprinkled on the cubit on either side of the edge, because **a cubit on this** side **and a cubit on that** side **is** still considered to be **on** the **corner. And** one **Sage holds** that **on its edge, yes,** it is fit, but any **further** on either side is **not.**

The Gemara clarifies that the precise terms of this debate depend on a further dispute — that of Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon and *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) — concerning what qualifies as *keren* when the application is made *below* the horn itself.

To understand the geometry: the *soveiv* runs at a lower level than the horns. The question is whether, when one is not applying the blood at the horn itself but rather lower on the altar wall, the zone directly below the horn — the full height of the altar section beneath it — is still considered the *keren*. Imagine projecting the horn straight downward: is any point along that imaginary vertical line still considered the corner?

Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon holds that the *chattat* must be performed *b'gufo shel keren* — at the horn itself. *Rebbi* holds that it can be performed anywhere directly below the horn, along that vertical line, within the upper section of the altar.

Given this framework, the Gemara explains the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar as follows: *aleyba d'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon*, who requires the horn itself, everyone agrees — if you are at the horn, it matters not where on that *amah-by-amah* surface you place the blood; anywhere is acceptable. The debate concerns *aleyba d'Rebbi*, who permits the area directly below the horn. There, one opinion holds that an *amah* in either direction along the wall (directly below the horn) still counts as the *keren*; the other holds that below the horn, only the very edge itself qualifies as the *keren*. Outside that edge — even one *amah* to either side along the lower wall — would not be considered the corner.

## Braita Challenge

### Braita Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חַטַּאת הַצִּיבּוּר וְהַיָּחִיד – כֵּיצַד מַתַּן דָּמָן? הָיָה עוֹלֶה לַכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב, וּבָא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית; וְטוֹבֵל בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ הַיְמָנִית – הַמְיוּמֶּנֶת שֶׁבַּיָּמִין – מִן הַדָּם שֶׁבַּמִּזְרָק; וְחוֹמֵר בְּגוּדָלוֹ מִלְּמַעְלָה וּבְאֶצְבָּעוֹ קְטַנָּה מִלְּמַטָּה, וּמְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד כְּנֶגֶד חוּדָּהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן, עַד שֶׁמְּכַלֶּה כָּל הַדָּם שֶׁבָּאֶצְבַּע. וְכֵן כׇּל קֶרֶן וָקֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion that one cubit on either side of the edge is still considered on the edge, from a *baraita*: With regard to **communal sin offerings and individual** sin offerings, **how** is **the placement of their blood** on the altar performed? The priest **would ascend the ramp** of the altar **and turn** right **to the surrounding ledge** and continue east, **and** he would **come to** the **southeast corner. And** he would then **dip with his right [*haymanit*] finger, the** most **dexterous [*hamyumenet*] of the right** hand, i.e., the index finger, **of the blood that is in the bowl, and** would **gather** the blood **with his thumb above and his smallest finger below,** so that it would remain on his finger. **And** he would **sprinkle** the blood **and** move his hand **down on the** outer **edge of** the **corner, until** he would **finish** sprinkling **all the blood that** was **on** his **finger. And** he would act **similarly** at **each and every corner.** This *baraita* states explicitly that the blood must be sprinkled on the actual edge.

A challenge is raised from a *braita*. The *braita* describes the proper procedure for the communal and individual *chattat*: the *kohen* ascends the ramp (*kivesh*), proceeds to the *soveiv*, and comes to the southeast corner (*keren dromit mizrachit*). He dips with his right hand — specifically with *ha-miyumenet shebayamin*, which Rashi explains as the most dexterous and effective finger, namely the pointer finger — into the blood held in the *mizrak* (the service vessel). He then uses his thumb and his pinky (*godlo* and *etzba ketana*) to assist. Rashi explains this means he scrapes the blood along his pointer finger to ensure that all of it is applied; Rambam understands it as using the thumb to support the pointer finger. Either way, these additional fingers help facilitate clean and complete application.

The *braita* then specifies: *umechatei v'yored k'neged hudo shel keren* — he applies the blood going downward, directly opposite the actual corner of the horn. He continues until *kol hadam shebe'etzba'o* — all the blood on his finger is used up. The same is done at each corner in turn. This appears to settle the question: the *braita* explicitly says the application is opposite the edge of the horn, not an *amah* in either direction.

### Ideal vs. Acceptable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִצְוָה בְּחוּדָּהּ; אִי עֲבַד אַמָּה אֵילָךְ וְאַמָּה אֵילָךְ – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** The optimal manner to perform the **mitzva** is to sprinkle the blood **on the edge of** the corner. But **if he did** the sprinkling on the **cubit on this** side, **or** the **cubit on that** side, **we have no** problem **with it,** and he has still fulfilled the mitzva.

The Gemara resolves this: *mitzvah b'chuda* — the ideal is indeed at the edge itself, as described in the *braita*. However, *i avid amah eilech v'amah eilech leit lan bah* — if one applied it an *amah* in either direction, *b'dieved* it is acceptable. The *braita* describes the *lechatchila* procedure; the dispute concerns what is still valid after the fact. All agree that at the horn itself, an *amah* in any direction is acceptable *b'dieved*. The debate is whether, when one is below the horn, placing blood an *amah* to either side along that lower wall still counts as a valid application of *keren*.

## Upper/Lower Blood Lines

### Blood Division Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי וּמַאי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּתַנְיָא: דָּמִים הָעֶלְיוֹנִים נִיתָּנִין מִחוּט הַסִּיקְרָא וּלְמַעְלָה, דָּמִים הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים נִיתָּנִין מִחוּט הַסִּיקְרָא וּלְמַטָּן. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the aforementioned dispute. **What** is the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and what** is the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon?** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:11): The **blood** that must be placed on **the upper** half of the altar, e.g., the blood of a sin offering, **is placed** anywhere **from the red line** that circumscribed the middle of the altar **and above.** The **blood** that must be placed on **the lower** half of the altar **is placed** anywhere **from the red line or below it.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

The Gemara now introduces the fundamental division of *damim elyonim* and *damim tachtonim* — upper bloods and lower bloods. The blood of different *korbanot* is applied to different zones of the altar, divided at the *chut ha-sikra*, the red line that runs around the altar at its midpoint. The upper bloods, from the red line and above, include the *chattat behemah* — the animal sin-offering, applied specifically at the corners. All other large-animal *korbanot* — *ola*, *shelamim*, *asham*, and the like — are applied below the red line. By bird-offerings, the rule is reversed: the bird *chattat* goes below and the bird *ola* goes above.

### Altar Structure

To understand the red line's placement, the altar's structure must be described. At the bottom was a one-*amah* protruding *yesod* (base). Above that was a five-*amah* middle section. Above that was a three-*amah* upper section, and atop that were the horns, which were one *amah* tall — totaling ten *amot* overall, not counting the horns, with the horns bringing the full height to eleven. The red line would be at the midpoint, five *amot* up, which means it runs along the upper boundary of the middle section, at the point where the upper three-*amah* section begins.

When the *kohen* ascended the ramp, he could either continue all the way up to the top surface of the *mizbeach* to place the parts on the fire, or he could exit onto the *soveiv* — the walk-around created by the one-*amah* inset of the upper section — to apply the blood. Standing on the *soveiv*, which was roughly at the level of the upper section, the *kohen* would be at approximately the right height to reach the horns for the *chattat* application. A *chattat behemah* blood application was performed up here, near the top of the altar.

### Rabbi vs. R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִין – בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף, אֲבָל בְּחַטַּאת בְּהֵמָה – הִיא עַצְמָהּ אֵין נַעֲשֵׂית אֶלָּא עַל גּוּפָהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon says: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to a bird burnt offering,** as its blood may be placed anywhere above the red line. **But with regard to an animal sin offering,** the placement **itself may be performed only on the actual corner** of the altar. This is based on the verse that states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger, and put it upon the corners of the altar of burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:25).

The *braita* records the dispute: *damim elyonim nitanim mechut ha-sikra ulema'alah* — the upper bloods are given from the red line and above; *damim tachtonim nitanim mechut ha-sikra ulematah* — the lower bloods from the red line and below. This is the view of *Rebbi*. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon then qualifies: when does the rule of "from the red line and above" apply? Only for a *olas ha'of* — a bird burnt-offering. But for a *chattat behemah*, the blood application must be *b'gufo shel keren* — at the horn itself. One cannot satisfy the requirement merely by applying anywhere in the upper section.

This is the operative difference between the two positions. *Rebbi* permits the entire zone from the red line upward for the *chattat behemah* as well; Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon confines the *chattat behemah* to the corner itself, while reserving the broader upper zone for the bird *ola*.

### Four Amot Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַהַרְאֵל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּמֵהַהַרְאֵל וּלְמַעְלָה וְגוֹ׳״. אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת הוּא דְּהָוְיָא?! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: וּמְקוֹם קְרָנוֹת אַרְבַּע. מְקוֹם קְרָנוֹת אַרְבַּע הָוְיָא?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: רְשׁוּת קְרָנוֹת אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **As it is written: "And the altar shall be four cubits; and from the altar and above** there shall be four corners" (Ezekiel 43:15). The Gemara asks: **Was** the altar only **four cubits** wide? **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: And** the total **area** of the four **corners,** which were each one square cubit, was **four** cubits. The Gemara asks: **Was the area of** the **corners four** square cubits? If the four corners would be placed together the area would be only two square cubits. **Rather, say** that the **domain** of the **corners** is **four** cubits. In other words, the verse means that the blood may be sprinkled up to four cubits beneath the actual corners of the altar.

Rabbi Abahu asks: what is *Rebbi*'s basis for permitting the entire upper zone, and not just the horn itself? The *pshat* of the verses refers to *karnos ha-mizbeach* — the corners of the altar — and the natural reading is that this means the horn itself. Rabbi Abahu cites a verse from Yechezkel: *ve-ha-har'el arba amos* — the *har'el* (a name for the altar or its upper section) is four *amot*. Rabbi Adda bar Ahava explains: this does not mean the altar as a whole is four *amot* — it is clearly much taller. Rather, it means *reshus ha-karnos arba* — the zone of the corners occupies four *amot*. That is, the effective territory of the corner extends from the horn downward four *amot* — encompassing the one-*amah* horn plus the three-*amah* upper section, bringing one all the way down to the red line.

The Gemara briefly questions whether the four *amot* should be counted from the top of the horn down (yielding horn plus three *amot* of upper section) or from the bottom of the horn down (yielding three *amot* of upper section alone). Rashi understands the four *amot* to extend from the bottom of the horn down to the red line — that is, the horn itself plus the three *amot* below it — such that the entire upper section from the red line upward constitutes the *reshus ha-karnos*. This is a striking reading: it means that in principle, one could apply the *chattat* blood quite far below the horn, as long as it is within this four-*amah* zone above the red line, and it would count as *keren*. The precise question of whether one must still be at the literal edge of the altar in this lower zone, or may be an *amah* to either side, remains to be clarified.

### Red Line Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: חוּט שֶׁל סִיקְרָא חוֹגְרוֹ בָּאֶמְצַע, לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין דָּמִים הָעֶלְיוֹנִים לְדָמִים הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיְתָה הָרֶשֶׁת עַד חֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – הַתּוֹרָה נָתְנָה מְחִיצָה לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין דָּמִים הָעֶלְיוֹנִים לְדָמִים הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Middot* 35b): **A red line circumscribed** the altar **in the middle, to separate between** the area for the **blood** that must be placed on **the upper** part of the altar **and** the area for the **blood** that must be placed on **the lower** part of the altar. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Ketina said** it is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "And you shall put it under the ledge round the altar beneath, **and the net will reach halfway up the altar"** (Exodus 27:5). **The Torah provided** for **a separation** on the altar, **to separate between** the area for the **blood** placed on **the upper** part of the altar **and** the area for the **blood** placed on **the lower** part of the altar.

The Gemara then asks the foundational question: what is the conceptual basis for dividing the altar at the midpoint? The idea that certain blood goes on top and other blood goes below is clear from the *korbanot* themselves — but where does the notion of a dividing line at the precise midpoint come from? Rav Acha bar Rav Katina derives it from the verse *ve-haysa ha-reshes ad chatzi ha-mizbeach* — the grillwork (*reshes*, a lattice or grating) extended to the midpoint of the altar. The Gemara explains: *ha-Torah nesna mechitza lehavdil bein damim ha-elyonim ve-damim ha-tachtonim* — the Torah assigned this physical feature, the grillwork that runs to the middle, to serve as the dividing point separating upper-blood applications from lower-blood applications. The *chut ha-sikra*, the red line painted on the altar, corresponds to this midpoint and gives visible expression to the conceptual division.

## Shirei HaDam Base

### South Base Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבֵּחַ״ – זֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר זֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: He would pour **the remainder of the blood** on the southern base of the altar. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning the phrase: **"At the base of the altar"** (Leviticus 4:30), mentioned with regard to pouring the remainder of the blood of the sin offering of a king, **this** is referring to the **southern base** of the altar. The *baraita* challenges: **Do you say** that **this** is referring to the **southern base, or is it** referring **only** to the **western base?**

The *braita* states: when the verse says *el yesod ha-mizbeach* in connection with the outer *chattat*, this refers to the southern base — *zeh yesod dromi*. Could one think it refers to the western base, as by the inner *chattat*? The *braita* raises this and then resolves it.

### South Base Derived

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִלְמַד סָתוּם מִן הַמְפוֹרָשׁ? אָמַרְתָּ: יִלְמַד יְרִידָתוֹ מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ לִיצִיאָתוֹ מִן הַהֵיכָל; מָה יְצִיאָתוֹ מִן הַהֵיכָל – בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ, אַף יְרִידָתוֹ מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ – בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* suggests: **And** one can **derive** the meaning of this **unspecified** phrase **from** the meaning of **the explicit** phrase, as follows: **You said** that one can **derive** the location of the priest's **descent from the ramp** of the external altar after sprinkling blood of the sin offering by comparing it **to his exit from the Sanctuary** with the blood that remained after having sprinkled blood inside the Sanctuary. The verse states with regard to the bull offering of the High Priest: "And all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out at the base of the altar of burnt offering, which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:7). **Just as** upon **his exit from the Sanctuary** the priest pours the remainder of the blood **on** the side **closest to him,** the western side, **so too,** upon **his descent from the ramp** of the external altar after sprinkling blood from the sin offering, he pours the blood **on** the side **closest to him,** which is the southern side.

The Gemara's reasoning proceeds as follows. By the inner *chattat*, the verse says the remnant blood is poured at the base *petach ohel moed* — at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting. As you exit the *heichal*, the very first base you encounter is the western base, so the western base is explicit in that case. One might therefore apply the same logic to the outer *chattat* and conclude it too is the western base. But the Gemara says: *yilamad yeridato min ha-kevesh liyetziaso min ha-heichal* — learn from the inner *chattat* not the mechanical conclusion (western = western) but rather the underlying principle: the remnant is poured at the base closest to where you exit. Just as the *kohen* performing the inner *chattat* spills at the first base he meets upon leaving the *heichal*, the *kohen* performing the outer *chattat* spills at the first base he meets upon descending the ramp — and that is the southern base. *Mah yetziaso min ha-heichal b'samuch lo, af yeridato min ha-kevesh b'samuch lo.*

### Shimon vs. Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in another *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael says: This and that,** both the remainder of the blood sprinkled inside the Sanctuary and the remainder of the blood sprinkled on the external altar, are poured on the **western base** of the altar. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: This and that** are poured on the **southern base** of the altar.

This ruling, however, turns out to be subject to a three-way dispute recorded in a *braita*. Rabbi Yishmael holds: *zeh v'zeh yesod maaravi* — both the inner and the outer *chattat* remnants are poured at the western base. He learns the unexplicit from the explicit: since the inner *chattat* is explicitly western, we derive that the outer *chattat* is also western. Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai holds the opposite extreme: *zeh v'zeh yesod dromi* — both are the southern base.

### North Altar Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי – קָסָבַר: יִלְמַד סָתוּם מִן הַמְפוֹרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the blood is poured on the **western base** of the altar, it is clear how he derived his *halakha*. He **holds** that one can **derive** the meaning of **an unspecified** phrase **from** the meaning of **the explicit** phrase. Therefore, just as the remainder of the blood sprinkled inside the Sanctuary is poured on the western base of the external altar, the same is done with the remainder of the blood sprinkled on the external altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, קָסָבַר הַאי תַּנָּא: כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּצָפוֹן קָאֵי. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ פֶּתַח בְּדָרוֹם קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that the blood is poured on the **southern base** of the altar, **what is the reason? Rabbi Asi said: This *tanna* holds** that **the entire** external **altar stands in the north** of the Temple courtyard. Therefore, the southern base is in the center of the courtyard, opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary. This is what the verse is referring to when it states: "At the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:7). The Gemara presents **another formulation** of Rabbi Asi's explanation: **The entire entrance** of the Sanctuary **stands in the south** of the altar. Accordingly, when the priest exits the Sanctuary the southern base is the first one he encounters.

The Gemara asks: Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the outer *chattat* is understandable, since one exits the ramp toward the south. But how can the inner *chattat* also be the southern base? The verse explicitly ties it to *petach ohel moed*, and the natural exit from the *heichal* leads to the western base.

Rav Ashi explains: Rabbi Shimon holds that the entire *mizbeach* stood in the north of the courtyard — *kula mizbeach b'tzafon kai*. If the *mizbeach* was positioned entirely in the northern half of the courtyard, then the *petach ohel moed*, which was a ten-*amah*-wide doorway, was five *amot* in the north and five *amot* in the south. A *kohen* exiting through the southern portion of that doorway would not face the altar directly; positioned as he was, with the entire altar to the north, he would find himself adjacent not to the western face of the altar but rather to its southern base. It is therefore coherent, on this view, to say that even for the inner *chattat*, the *yesod* encountered upon exiting through the southern portion of the *petach* is the southern base.

### Tradition Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי. וְסִימָנֵיךְ: מָשְׁכוּ גַּבְרֵי לְגַבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, says:** Both **this and that,** i.e., the blood of an inner sin offering and that of an external sin offering, were poured at the **western base** of the altar. According to this version, Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael. The Gemara comments: **And your mnemonic** to remember this change in opinion is: **The men pulled the man.** In this case, the numerous students of Rabbi Yishmael claim that Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai agrees with their teacher.

The Gemara adds a remarkable note: *tana de-vei Rabbi Yishmael* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael — when transmitting Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's position, reported it as *zeh v'zeh yesod maaravi* — both at the western base — which is, of course, precisely Rabbi Yishmael's own position. The way to remember this, the Gemara notes, is *mashchu gavrei le-gavra*: the many drew the few toward them. The school of Rabbi Yishmael, being more numerous, effectively assimilated Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's position into their own when reporting it — whether through deliberate reframing or through a genuine misunderstanding of the opposing tradition. The result is that according to the house of Rabbi Yishmael, there was no dissent: everyone agreed the remnant blood of the outer *chattat* goes to the western base.

## Ola Blood Application

### Ola Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הָעוֹלָה קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, שְׁחִיטָתָהּ בַּצָּפוֹן, וְקִיבּוּל דָּמָהּ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בַּצָּפוֹן, וְדָמָהּ טָעוּן שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע, וּטְעוּנָה הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִיתּוּחַ, וְכָלִיל לָאִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The burnt offering** is **an offering of the most sacred order. Its slaughter** is **in the north** of the Temple courtyard **and the collection of its blood in a service vessel** is **in the north, and its blood requires two placements that are four, and** it **requires flaying** of its carcass **and the cutting** of the sacrificial animal into pieces, **and** it is consumed **in its entirety,** with the exception of its hide, **by the fire** of the altar.

The *mishna* now turns to the *ola* (burnt-offering). The *ola* is *kodshei kodashim*. It is slaughtered in the north, the blood is caught in a service vessel in the north, and the blood requires *shtei matanot she-hen arba* — two applications that count as four. The animal is then flayed, cut into pieces, and burned entirely on the altar. The Gemara asks: why does the *mishna* need to state explicitly that the *ola* is *kodshei kodashim*? The entire chapter deals with *kodshei kodashim*, so it is seemingly redundant. The answer: *mishum d'lo ksiv kodshei kodashim hu* — because the Torah does not actually use the phrase *kodshei kodashim* in connection with the *ola* in the relevant verses. The *ola* is the paradigmatic total-dedication offering, entirely consumed on the altar, and yet the Torah applies the specific designation *kodshei kodashim* explicitly to the *chattat* and other offerings, not to the *ola*. The *mishna* therefore makes the point explicitly.

### Two-That-Are-Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ עוֹלָה מַאי טַעְמָא תָּנֵי לֵיהּ קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא כְּתִיב בַּהּ ״(לַה׳) קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** that the *tanna* **taught** that **a burnt offering** is **an offering of the most sacred order,** when the mishna earlier (52b) did not mention that a sin offering is of the most sacred order? The Gemara answers: It is **because** in the Torah **it is not written** explicitly **with regard to** the burnt offering: **It is most sacred,** as it states concerning the sin offering and the guilt offering in a verse concerning a meal offering: "It shall not be baked with leaven. I have given it as their portion of My offerings made by fire; it is most sacred, as the sin offering, and as the guilt offering" (Leviticus 6:10). Consequently, the *tanna* explicitly states that a burnt offering is likewise of the most sacred order.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָמָהּ טָעוּן שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר רַב: נוֹתֵן וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹתֵן. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַתָּנָה אַחַת כְּמִין גַּמָּא נוֹתֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And its blood requires two placements** that are four. **How does** the priest **perform** the placements? **Rav says: He places** the blood on one side of the corner of the altar, **and places** it **again** on the other side of the corner of the altar. He repeats this on the diagonally opposite corner, so that he places on two corners but on all four sides of the altar. **And Shmuel says:** He **places one placement** on each of the two diagonally opposite corners, so that each placement is **similar to** the shape of the Greek letter **gamma,** which is bent at a right angle.

The Gemara asks: *heichi avid* — what exactly are these two applications that are four? The two diagonal corners of the altar were used: the northeast and the southwest. Rav says: *nosen v'chozer v'nosen* — the *kohen* stands at each of the two corners and applies the blood separately to each side of the corner. That is, he makes two distinct applications at each corner — one to each of the two walls meeting there — for a total of four separate applications at two corners. Shmuel says: *matana achat k'min gama* — a single application shaped like the Greek letter gamma (an L-shape), which strikes both walls simultaneously. The blood is thrown in one motion that angles around the corner, hitting both adjacent walls at once. This is done at each of the two diagonal corners.

### Braita Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי – יָכוֹל יִזְרְקֶנּוּ זְרִיקָה אַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״סָבִיב״. אִי סָבִיב, יָכוֹל יַקִּיפֶנּוּ כְּחוּט? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְזָרְקוּ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? כְּמִין גַּמָּא – וְדָמָהּ טָעוּן שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This dispute between Rav and Shmuel is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*:** One **might** have thought that a priest **should sprinkle one sprinkling** with the blood of a burnt offering. To counter this possibility, **the verse states:** "And sprinkle the blood **around** against the altar that is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 1:5). **If** the blood must be sprinkled **around** the altar, one **might** have thought that **he should circumscribe** the altar **as** one would do with **a thread,** and sprinkle the blood all around. To counter this possibility, **the verse states:** "Shall present the blood, **and sprinkle** the blood around against the altar" (Leviticus 1:5), and one cannot have the blood circumscribe the altar as a thread would without directly applying it with a finger. **How** can **these** verses be reconciled? He applies the blood in a shape that is **similar to** that of the Greek letter **gamma, and its blood requires two sprinklings that are four.** This opinion accords with the opinion of Shmuel.

A *braita* is cited as bearing on this dispute. The *braita* asks: perhaps one should perform a single throw of blood (*zerika achat*) that covers everything in one go, not bothering with separate corners at all? The verse says *saviv* — around about — which might imply a single encircling motion. But the verse also says *v'zarku* — and they shall throw — which implies distinct throwing motions, not a continuous smearing. And if *saviv* implies going all the way around like a thread (*kachut*), that would require an impractical quantity of blood thrown over the entire circumference. The resolution is *k'min gama* — like a gamma — which satisfies *saviv* (it covers around the corner) while remaining a genuine throwing motion. This confirms Shmuel's position: two gamma-shaped applications, one at each diagonal corner, yielding two applications that are four.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״סָבִיב״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״סָבִיב״; מָה לְהַלָּן – פִּיסּוּק וְאַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת, אַף כָּאן – פִּיסּוּק וְאַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yishmael says: It is stated here:** "Shall present the blood, and sprinkle the blood **around** against the altar" (Leviticus 1:5), **and it is stated there,** with regard to the sin offering sacrificed at the inauguration of the Tabernacle: "And when it was slain, Moses took the blood, and put it upon the corners of the altar **around** with his finger" (Leviticus 8:15). **Just as there,** with regard to the sin offering, the blood was placed **discretely and** with **four placements,** one on each corner, **so too here,** with regard to a burnt offering, it must be placed **discretely and** with **four placements.** This opinion accords with the opinion of Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת, אַף כָּאן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת?! אָמַרְתָּ: עוֹלָה טְעוּנָה יְסוֹד; וְקֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית לֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ יְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **If** these offerings are compared to each other, why not say that **just as there,** the blood of the sin offering must have **four placements on** the **four corners, so too here,** the blood of the burnt offering requires **four placements on** the **four corners? You said** that **a burnt offering requires** that the blood be placed on a part of the altar that has **a base, and** the **southeast corner** of the altar **had no base** beneath it. Therefore, the blood had to be placed on the northeast corner and the southwest corner.

Rabbi Yishmael then draws a further inference from the verbal parallel between *saviv* here (by the *ola*) and *saviv* by the *chattat*. Just as the *chattat* involves four distinct, separate applications — one per wall — so too the *ola* involves four distinct, separate wall-applications, not a single continuous motion. This aligns with Rav's position: *nosen v'chozer v'nosen*, four truly separate applications. One might then ask: just as the *chattat* applies blood to all four corners, should the *ola* also apply at all four corners? The answer is that the *ola* requires a *yesod* — a protruding base — beneath the application point, and as we now see, the southeast corner lacked such a base. Hence the *ola* is done at only two corners — northeast and southwest — producing the two-that-are-four.

### Two Corners Only

To understand the red line's placement, the altar's structure must be described. At the bottom was a one-*amah* protruding *yesod* (base). Above that was a five-*amah* middle section. Above that was a three-*amah* upper section, and atop that were the horns, which were one *amah* tall. The Gemara explains the altar's *yesod* configuration. The base protruded along the northern side and along the western side, but at the southeast corner — *keren mizrachit dromit* — there was no protruding *yesod*. The base wrapped around on the north and on the west but was inset at the southeast, leaving that corner without the necessary foundation for blood application. Therefore, while four corners exist on the altar, only two have the qualifying *yesod*: the northeast and the southwest. These two diagonal corners, each receiving an application that covers two walls, produce the required *shtei matanot she-hen arba*.

## Tribal Division

### Tribal Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל טוֹרֵף. דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: מִזְבֵּחַ אוֹכֵל בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל יְהוּדָה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that there was no base on the southeast corner of the altar? **Rabbi Elazar says: Because it was not in the portion** of land **of the one who tears,** i.e., the tribe of Benjamin, as he is described in the following manner: "Benjamin is a wolf that tears apart; in the morning he devours the prey, and in the evening he divides the spoil" (Genesis 49:27). **As Rav Shmuel, son of Rav Yitzḥak, says:** The **altar** would **consume,** i.e., occupy, **one cubit of the portion of Judah.** The part of the altar in Judah's portion was the southeast corner of the base, and therefore there was no base on that corner.

The Gemara now explains why the southeast corner lacked a *yesod*. Rav Elazar cites the principle that the southeast corner *lo haysa b'chelko shel toreif* — it was not within the portion of the devourer. The verse says *Binyamin ze'ev yitrof* — Binyamin is a devouring wolf — and the *mizbeach* was understood to be situated within Binyamin's tribal portion, functioning as the predator consuming its prey (the animals offered upon it). The broader *Beis Hamikdash* complex, by contrast — the *Ezras Nashim*, *Ezras Yisrael*, *Ezras Kohanim*, the *heichal* itself — was in the portion of Yehuda.

### Devouring Wolf

The verse continues: *baboker yochal ad ula'erev yechalek shalal* — in the morning he devours prey, and in the evening he divides the spoils. The image is of the altar as this consuming entity situated within Binyamin's territory, devouring the sacrifices brought to it. Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak specifies: *mizbeach ochel b'chelko shel Yehuda amah* — the altar encroached one *amah* into the portion of Yehuda.

### Southeast Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רְצוּעָה הָיְתָה יוֹצְאָה מֵחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל יְהוּדָה וְנִכְנֶסֶת בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל בִּנְיָמִין, וְהָיָה בִּנְיָמִין הַצַּדִּיק מִצְטַעֵר עָלֶיהָ בְּכׇל יוֹם לְנוֹטְלָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: A strip** of land **emerged from the portion of Judah and entered into the portion of Benjamin,** and the southeast corner of the base was on that strip. **And** the tribe of **Benjamin the righteous would agonize over it every day,** desiring **to take it** into its portion, due to its unique sanctity. **As it is stated** in Moses' blessing to the tribe of Benjamin:

Rabbi Levi bar Chama said in the name of Rabbi Chanina: a *retzuah* — a strip or band — would extend from the portion of Yehuda into the portion of Binyamin. That is, while Yehuda's territory nominally ended at the eastern boundary of the altar, it actually projected slightly inward at two points — just where the *yesod* of the southeast corner would have been. Because the zone where the southeast *yesod* would protrude falls within Yehuda's portion rather than Binyamin's, no *yesod* was constructed there. The altar's *yesod* belongs to the portion of the *toreif*, Binyamin; where Yehuda's strip intrudes, the *yesod* is absent.

### Binyamin's Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חֹפֵף עָלָיו כׇּל הַיּוֹם״. לְפִיכָךְ זָכָה בִּנְיָמִין הַצַּדִּיק וְנַעֲשָׂה אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָן לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבֵין כְּתֵפָיו שָׁכֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** **"He covers it throughout the day,** and He dwells between his shoulders" (Deuteronomy 33:12). The term "covers it" is understood to mean that Benjamin is continually focused upon that site. **Therefore, Benjamin the righteous was privileged and became the host of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** as the Holy of Holies was built in his portion. **As it is stated: "And He dwells between his shoulders."**

The Gemara adds a deeply moving midrashic passage. *Ve-haya Binyamin ha-tzadik mitzta'er aleha b'chol yom lenotlah* — Binyamin the righteous one was pained each and every day by this intrusion, wishing to claim that strip for his own portion. The motivation is framed as entirely religious: he wanted the entire space of the *mizbeach* to be in his territory. The verse cited is *chofeif alav kol hayom u'vein keseifav shachein* — He hovers over him all the day, and between his shoulders He dwells. Binyamin's constant distress over that small encroachment — his yearning for the divine presence to rest entirely within his portion — was rewarded: *zacha Binyamin ha-tzadik v'na'astu ushpizin l'Kadosh Baruch Hu* — Binyamin the righteous merited becoming the host of the Holy One, blessed be He, and God's presence dwelt between his shoulders.

### Shechina Dwelling

One might ask what exactly Binyamin gained through this anguish, if the divine presence was already situated in his portion before he was distressed about the encroachment. The point seems to be not that he received something he did not have, but that his *tzidkus* — his righteous yearning — was acknowledged and immortalized in the very language of the verse: *u'vein keseifav shachein*, the *Shechina* dwells between his shoulders. The image of *bein keseifav*, between the shoulders, may also evoke the slight inset — the *Kodesh Kodashim*, situated deeper within the structure, somewhat below and behind the summit, dwelling as it were nestled between the shoulders of Binyamin's land.

The practical halakhic consequence of this tribal division is the absence of the southeast *yesod*, which is why the blood of the *ola* is applied at only the northeast and southwest corners — the two corners that lie fully within Binyamin's portion and possess the required protruding base.
