# Keritot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Meir - Eating

### Kezayit Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כַּמָּה יִשְׁהֶא בָּאוֹכָלִין? כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל קְלָיוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף כְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How much** time **can one expend** while eating an olive-bulk **of** forbidden **food** and still be liable for violating the prohibition? The duration is calculated **as though he were eating toasted grain,** which one eats one kernel at a time. If he eats the olive-bulk of forbidden food within the amount of time it would take to eat an olive-bulk of toasted grain, he is liable. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Unless** the amount of time **he expends from beginning to end** is more than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf [*peras*]** of bread, he is **liable.**

The daf continues from the bottom of 12b. The *mishnah* presented a debate regarding the combination of a *kezayit* eaten over a period of time: when does it join together to create liability? Rabbi Meir's position was likened to eating nuts — but precisely what that means requires unpacking. The *gemara* opens with *iba'ya lehu*: they asked whether Rabbi Meir was being strict or lenient.

### Nuts Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי מֵאִיר, לְחוּמְרָא קָאָמַר אוֹ לְקוּלָּא קָאָמַר? לְחוּמְרָא קָאָמַר, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל קְלָיוֹת, דַּאֲפִילּוּ כּוּלֵּיהּ יוֹמָא. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף יוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס, כֵּיוָן דִּמְשִׁיכָה אֲכִילְתֵיהּ – מִחַיַּיב. וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: דְּעַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס הוּא דְּחַיָּיב יוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does **Rabbi Meir state** his opinion, that the duration of time is calculated as though one were eating toasted grain, **as a stringency or** does **he state** it **as a leniency?** The Gemara elaborates: **Does he state it as a stringency, and this** is what he **is teaching:** The duration is calculated **as though he were eating toasted grain** a little at a time, and therefore **even** if it takes him **the entire day** to consume an olive-bulk of the forbiden food, **and even though from beginning to end** he expends **more than** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **since** he consumed a full olive-bulk in one **extended** act of **eating, he is liable; and the Rabbis said to him that unless he expends** less than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **he is liable,** but if he expends **more than** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, he is **exempt?**

The question is framed as follows: *lechumra ka'amar o lekula ka'amar* — is Rabbi Meir being strict or lenient? On the strict reading, *ke'ilu ochel kelayot*, like eating nuts, means that even if one eats continuously throughout the entire day — *de'afillu kulo yoma* — even though the total duration from beginning to end exceeds *kdei achilas pras*, since the eating stretches on continuously, *kivan demashchei achilata*, one is liable. This would be a stringency: continuous eating, no matter how extended, combines.

### Continuous Eating

As Rabbeinu Gershom explains, Rabbi Meir's reference to *kelayot* — nuts — evokes the image of continuous eating, one piece immediately following the next, like popping sunflower seeds one after another without pause. The combination occurs because the eating never stops, not because of any fixed time limit. This reading yields a *chumra*: as long as one eats continuously, even if the process takes the entire day, the *kezayit* combines and one is liable.

### Kdei Achilas Pras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא לְקוּלָּא קָאָמַר, דְּהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל קְלָיוֹת, וְהוּא דְּלָא אַיפְסֵיק בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי, אֲבָל דְּאַיפְסֵיק בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – פָּטוּר. וְאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיוָן דְּמִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס הוּא – חַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** Rabbi Meir **says** his opinion **as a leniency, as this** is what he **is teaching:** The duration is calculated **as though he were eating toasted grain, which is** eaten continuously one kernel after another, and therefore he is liable only if **he did not interrupt** at all **in between** his bites. **But** if **he interrupted in between** them, then **although** the duration **from beginning to end** was less than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, he is **exempt; and the Rabbis said to him: Since** the amount of time **from beginning to end is** less than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, he is **liable,** despite the interruptions.

Against this, *amru lo rabanan* — the Rabbis respond: *ad she'yehei bekedei achilat pras hu dechayav yoter mikedei achilat pras patur*. They are unconcerned with whether the eating is continuous or not; what matters is the time window alone. Once the duration exceeds *kdei achilas pras*, one is exempt, regardless of continuity.

### No Break Requirement

On the lenient reading of Rabbi Meir, *lekula ka'amar*, he would be saying that combination requires both conditions: the eating must fall within *kdei achilas pras* and there must be no interruption — *delo pasak beinei ubeinei*. If there is a break, *aval pasak beinei ubeinei*, even if the total duration is within *kdei achilas pras*, one is exempt. This is a significant *kula*: even eating within the proper time window does not combine if there was any pause. The Rabbis in turn respond: as long as the process falls within *kdei achilas pras* from beginning to end, one is liable — continuity adds nothing.

The question, then, is whether Rabbi Meir's *kelayot* analogy means eating continuously regardless of total time, or continuously as an additional requirement on top of *kdei achilas pras*.

### Rabbi Meir Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עֵד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְחוּמְרָא, הַיְינוּ דְקָתָנֵי: ״עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא״, עַד דִּשְׁהִיָּ[ה] דִּילֵיהּ כְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְקוּלָּא, ״וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִם שָׁהָה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינָּה: רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְחוּמְרָא? שְׁמַע מִינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a resolution: **Come** and **hear** the mishna: **And the Rabbis say: Unless** the amount of time **he expends from beginning to end** is the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread. **Granted, if you say** that **Rabbi Meir** issued his ruling **as a stringency,** and that one is liable even if he ate the olive-bulk over a long period of time, **this** is the reason **that the** *tanna* **teaches** that the Rabbis say: **Unless** the amount of time **he expends,** meaning: **Unless his expenditure** of time is no more than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, he is exempt. **But if you say** that **Rabbi Meir** issued his ruling **as a leniency,** meaning that if one interrupts in the middle of eating he is exempt, the *tanna* **should** have stated: **And the Rabbis say: If he expended** more than the time it takes to eat a half-loaf, he is exempt, which would indicate that if he expended less than this amount of time he is liable, even if he interrupted his eating in the middle. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from it** that **Rabbi Meir** issued his ruling **as a stringency?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that this is correct.

The *gemara* resolves this through the language of the Rabbis' rejoinder: *vachachamim omrim ad she'yehei bekedei achilat pras mitchila ve'ad sof* — until the duration does not exceed *kdei achilas pras* from beginning to end. The word *ad*, "until," functions as a limit. *Iy amart bishlama Rabbi Meir lechumra* — if Rabbi Meir was being strict, saying even the entire day counts as long as the eating is continuous, then the Rabbis' use of *ad* makes perfect sense: they are capping the duration at *kdei achilas pras*, pushing back against Rabbi Meir's unlimited extension. But *ela iy amart Rabbi Meir lekula* — if Rabbi Meir was being lenient, and his innovation was merely adding a continuity requirement on top of *kdei achilas pras*, the Rabbis should have responded by saying continuity is irrelevant — not by simply restating the time limit. Their response only makes sense if Rabbi Meir was extending the window beyond *kdei achilas pras*. Therefore: *shema mina Rabbi Meir lechumra* — Rabbi Meir holds the stringent position, allowing combination even beyond *kdei achilas pras*, provided the eating is continuous, while the Rabbis limit it always to *kdei achilas pras*.

## Forbidden Foods

### Chelev/Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבְנַאי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּחֲלָבִים וּבִנְבֵילָה עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס. אוֹכָלִים טְמֵאִים, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים וּמַשְׁקִין – אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravnai says** that **Shmuel says: With regard to** forbidden **fats and with regard to** an unslaughtered **animal carcass,** one is liable for eating an olive-bulk even with interruptions **unless** the time **he expends from beginning to end** is more than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis in the mishna. If he ate **impure foods** in the volume of a quarter-loaf of bread, or he ate **repugnant creatures or creeping** animals, **or** drank a quarter-*log* of impure **liquids,** he becomes impure and may not partake of *teruma*, **even** if consumption extended for **the entire day,** provided that they are eaten within the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.

With Rabbi Meir's position clarified, the *gemara* moves to a new ruling. Ravannai said in the name of Shmuel: *bechalavim uveneveilot ad she'yehei mitchila ve'ad sof bekedei achilat pras* — when it comes to forbidden foods such as *chelev* or *neveilah* (noting that *neveilah* carries lashes rather than a *chatat*, though the principle applies to any *issur*), the eating combines only if from beginning to end it falls within *kdei achilas pras*. This much is straightforward.

### Tamei Foods

A more surprising ruling follows. When it comes to *ochlim tme'im* — *tamei* foods — and similarly *shkatzim u'remasim* and *mashkim*, the ruling is: *afilu kol hayom kulo, bekedei achilat pras* — even if the eating spans the entire day, it combines, as long as each *kezayit* is eaten within *kdei achilas pras*. This seems paradoxical: how can "the whole day" and "*kdei achilas pras*" coexist in a single ruling?

To understand this, one must recall the relevant *shiur* for *tamei* food. Unlike *chelev* where a *kezayit* triggers liability, a person does not become *tamei* by eating a *tamei* food unless he consumes *chatzi pras* — approximately four *kezaysim* (since a *pras* is roughly eight *kezaysim*). This *tum'ah* is rabbinic in nature: normally, a person becomes *tamei* only by touching an *av hatumah*; one cannot become *tamei* by touching a *rishon* or *sheini*. The Rabbis decreed that ingesting this amount of *tamei* food causes *tum'ah*. With this background, Ravannai's statement becomes meaningful — but still requires explanation.

### Individual Kezayit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, וְהוּא שֶׁאָכַל כְּזַיִת בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Shmuel **saying? Rav Pappa said** that **this** is what he **is saying: Even** if he eats the quarter-loaf of food over the course of **the entire day** he becomes impure, **but that** is the *halakha* only **where he ate** each **olive-bulk within** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.

Rav Pappa clarifies: *afilu kol hayom kulo, vehu she'achal kezayis kezayis bichdei achilas pras* — even if eating spans the entire day, one becomes *tamei*, provided that each individual *kezayit* was consumed within its own *kdei achilas pras*. In other words, if one ate a *kezayit* of *tamei* food at nine in the morning, another at noon, another at two, another at three, and another at five — as long as each *kezayit* individually was eaten within *kdei achilas pras*, all of them combine to render the person *tamei*, even though the total span is hours.

This is a conceptually striking ruling. One would have expected that since the *shiur* here is four *kezaysim* — a single combined act — the whole amount would need to be consumed within one *kdei achilas pras* window. Yet the *gemara* is saying otherwise, and it points toward a distinction between eating as an *act* and eating as a *process*. When the *shiur* is a *kezayit*, eating is viewed as a single act; when the *shiur* is a *chatzi pras*, involving multiple *kezaysim*, eating is conceived as a process — one that can extend across an entire day. However, the process is composed of discrete acts, and each act must itself be valid — meaning each *kezayit* must be consumed within its own *kdei achilas pras*. Without that, it does not count as an act of eating at all. Distinct valid acts then combine into the larger process.

A question arises whether *benching* in the middle would break the combination. Presumably it would not, since the operative measure here is *tum'ah*, governed by its own rules — though as one student noted, this is an interesting point that invites further analysis.

### Tumeah Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין מִצְטָרְפִין לִפְסוֹל הַגְּוִיָּיה בְּכַחֲצִי פְרָס – מַאי לַָאו דְּאַכְלֵיהּ לַחֲצִי פְרָס בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס? לָא, דַּאֲכַל כְּזַיִת כְּזַיִת בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס. מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין מִצְטָרְפִין לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה בְּכַחֲצִי פְרָס בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס, כֵּיצַד? אָכַל וְחָזַר וְאָכַל, אִם יֵשׁ מִתְּחִלַּת אֲכִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְעַד סוֹף אֲכִילָה אַחֲרוֹנָה כְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – מִצְטָרְפִין, יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **All** impure **foods combine** together **to disqualify** one's **body** from eating *teruma* **if** he ate **a quarter-loaf** of the impure food. **What, is** this **not** referring to a case **where he ate the quarter-loaf within** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread? The Gemara explains: **No,** it is referring to a case **where he ate each olive-bulk within** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, but he ate the full quarter-loaf in longer than that amount of time. The Gemara **raises an objection** from another *baraita*: **All** impure **foods combine to disqualify** one's **body** from eating *teruma* if he ate **a quarter-loaf** of the impure food **within** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.**How so?** In a case where **he ate and then ate again, if from the beginning of the first** period of **eating until the end of the last** period of **eating there is** no more than the amount of time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **they combine** together. If the time spent eating is **more than that, they do not combine** together.

The *gemara* now challenges this with a *meisivei*. The *mishnah* in *Mikvaos* states: *kol ha'ochlim mitztarfin lifsol es hagviyah b'chatzi pras* — all foods combine to make the body *tamei* at the measure of *chatzi pras*. The *gemara* initially reads this as meaning the entire *chatzi pras* must be eaten within *kdei achilas pras*. But the *gemara* itself deflects: *lo d'achal kezayis kezayis bichdei achilas pras* — it means each individual *kezayit* was eaten within *kdei achilas pras*, consistent with Rav Pappa.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraisa* that is fully explicit. *Kol ha'ochlim mitztarfin lifsol es hagviyah b'chatzi pras bichdei achilas pras* — with the gloss: *keitzad? achal v'chazar v'achal — im yesh mit'chilah ad t'chilah rishonah ad sof achilah achronah bichdei achilas pras, mitztarfin; yoter mikan ein mitztarfin*. How so? If you ate and then ate again — if from the beginning of the first eating to the end of the last eating the time is within *kdei achilas pras*, it combines; more than that, it does not combine.

### Mikvah Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִתִּירוּ לֶאֱכוֹל פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר לֵירֵד וְלִטְבּוֹל, יָרַד וְטָבַל וְעָלָה וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ – מִצְטָרֵף. הִתִּירוּ לָהּ לָעוּבָּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The Sages **did not permit** one **who ate less than the** minimum **measure** of impure foods, i.e., a quarter-loaf, **to descend and to immerse** in a ritual bath. If **he descended, immersed, ascended, and** then ate more impure food and thereby **completed** consumption of the full measure of a quarter-loaf, this second act **combines** with his previous consumption of impure food and renders the person unfit to consume *teruma*, despite the immersion in the interim. The Sages **permitted a pregnant woman to eat less than the** minimum **measure due to the danger** of her miscarrying.

The *baraisa* is thus fully explicit: even regarding *tamei* food — even when the *shiur* is four *kezaysim* rather than one — the combination window does not extend to the whole day but is bounded by *kdei achilas pras* from first to last eating. *Tiyuvta d'rabbana tiyuvta* — this constitutes a decisive refutation of Ravannai's statement that one has the whole day.

The *baraisa* continues with a series of related rulings. First: *lo hitiru le'echol pachos mikishiur lereid v'litvol* — if one has eaten less than the full *shiur* to become *tamei*, they did not permit going to the *mikvah* before completing the *shiur*. Rav Yehuda explains why: *she'im yarad vetaval ve'ala vehishlimo, mitztaref* — because if one ate two *kezaysim*, immersed, and then ate two more *kezaysim*, the *tum'ah* is still fully constituted. The immersion, occurring before one was yet *tamei*, is halachically meaningless and does not interrupt the process. The concern is that the person will assume the *mikvah* severed the sequence, *ve'asi lemeimar hani letevila kema'alya*, and will not realize that *she'ein tevila ela ba'akhrona* — immersion is only effective after *tum'ah* has been fully contracted, not in the middle of the process. Therefore, such an immersion was discouraged to prevent confusion.

Next, the *baraisa* permits a pregnant woman to eat less than the *shiur* repeatedly — *hutra la'uberet pachos mikishiur afilu tuva* — Rav Pappa explains: many half-*shiur* amounts, even numerous times, mipnei hasakana, because of the risk to the pregnancy. Since each individual eating is less than *kdei achilas pras* and the individual amounts do not combine, she never incurs *tum'ah*, and this is permitted for health reasons.

One might ask: since there is no *issur* in merely becoming *tamei*, what is the concern that necessitates the concept of *mipnei hasakana*? Tosafot suggests the case involves a woman who will ultimately need to eat *terumah*, and thus becoming *tamei* has practical consequence. But this too remains somewhat unclear — if it is a genuine *sakana*, full eating should presumably be permitted outright. And if it is not a genuine *sakana*, why invoke it? These tensions are noted but not fully resolved by the *gemara*.

The *baraisa* also rules that *mashkin* — *tamei* liquids — combine to make one *tamei* at a *revi'is*, and like the food case, they must be consumed within *kdei achilas pras* from beginning of first drinking to end of last. The use of the eating measure (*kdei achilas pras*) rather than a drinking measure here is itself noteworthy and receives more extended treatment in other *gemaras* such as *Nazir*.

### Whole Day Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין מִצְטָרְפִין לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה בִּרְבִיעִית בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס. כֵּיצַד? שָׁתָה וְחָזַר וְשָׁתָה, אִם יֵשׁ מִתְּחִלַּת שְׁתִיָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְעַד סוֹף שְׁתִיָּה אַחֲרוֹנָה כְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – מִצְטָרְפִין, יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: אִם יֵשׁ מִתְּחִלַּת אֲכִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְעַד סוֹף אֲכִילָה בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – מִצְטָרֵף. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבְנַאי, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **All** impure **liquids combine to disqualify** one's **body** from eating *teruma* if one consumes **a quarter-***log* **within** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.**How so?** In a case where **he drank and then drank again,** and in total he drank a quarter-*log*, **if from the beginning of the first** act of **drinking until the end of the last** act of **drinking there is** no more than the amount of time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **they combine** together. But if the period of drinking is **more than that** amount of time, **they do not combine.** The Gemara explains its objection from the *baraita*: **In any event,** the *baraita* **taught** that **if from the beginning of the first** period of **eating until the end of the last** period of **eating there is** no more than the amount of time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **they combine** together. This apparently contradicts the opinion of Ravnai, who says that if one eats a quarter-loaf of impure food in total and ate each olive-bulk within the time it takes to eat a half-loaf of bread, his acts of eating combine together and he is disqualified from consuming *teruma*. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Ravnai** is **a conclusive refutation.**

## Tamei Through Food (II)

### Waiting Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: לֹא הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר לֵירֵד וְלִטְבּוֹל. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אָכַל פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר – לֹא הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לֵירֵד וְלִטְבּוֹל, שֶׁאִם יָרַד וְטָבַל וְעָלָה וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ – מִצְטָרֵף. וְאָתֵי לְמֵימַר: אַהֲנִי לִי טְבִילָה קַמַּיְיתָא, וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין טְבִילָה אֶלָּא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. אָמַר מַר: אֲמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אָכַל אוֹכָלִים טְמֵאִים וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין טְמֵאִים, וְשָׁתָה רְבִיעִית יַיִן, וְשָׁהָה בַּאֲכִילָתָן וּבִשְׁתִיָּיתָן כְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס, וְנִכְנַס לַמִּקְדָּשׁ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further discusses the *baraita*. **The Master said** above: The Sages **did not permit** one **who ate less than the** minimum **measure** of impure foods, i.e., a quarter-loaf, **to descend and to immerse** in a ritual bath. The Gemara asks: **What is he saying;** why is this prohibited? **Rav Yehuda said** that **this** is what the *tanna* in the *baraita* **is saying:** If **he ate less than the** minimum **measure** that causes impurity, the Sages **did not permit him to descend and immerse. As, if he would descend and immerse and ascend and** eat more impure food and thereby **complete** the full measure of a quarter-loaf within the time it takes to eat a half-loaf, all the impure food he ate **would combine** together to disqualify him from eating *teruma*; **and** yet **he** might **come to say:** My **first immersion** following my first consumption of impure food **was effective for me,** and no additional immersion is required now that I ate merely another half-measure of impure food. **But** in fact, **he does not know that immersion is** effective in purifying him **only at the end,** and that if he now makes contact with *teruma* he will render it disqualified. Therefore, the Sages prohibited immersion in such a case to prevent one from reaching this erroneous conclusion. The *baraita* states: In the case of one who **ate** a quarter-loaf of **ritually impure foods or drank** a quarter-*log* of **impure liquids, or drank a quarter-***log* **of wine, and remained** involved **in eating them or drinking them** for no more than the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread, **and** then **entered the Temple,** he is **liable.**

Having refuted Ravannai, the *gemara* turns to a related question raised by the *mishnah*'s language. The *mishnah* used a formulation that sounded as though *shehiyah* — "waiting" — referred to time spent inside the *Beit HaMikdash* after entering. Why would the *mishnah* use waiting language in that context?

Rav Yehuda clarifies: *achal ochlin tme'im veshata mashkim tme'im*, if one ate *tamei* foods or drank *tamei* liquids or drank a *revi'is* of wine, *veshahah bachilatan uvshiyatan kedei achilat pras* — the "waiting," *shehiyah*, refers to the process of eating or drinking itself, not to standing inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. If that process did not exceed *kdei achilas pras*, and one then entered the *Beit HaMikdash*, *venichnas lamikdash chayav* — one is liable.

### Process vs Break

This clarification carries a significant practical and conceptual implication. The *baraisa* and the *gemara* are fully explicit that *kdei achilas pras* measures the total span of the process — from the beginning of the first eating to the end of the last — not the size of any break inserted between acts of eating. This distinction matters enormously in practice.

On Yom Kippur, for instance, when a person is ill and is permitted to eat less than the *shiur*, the common practice is to instruct them to wait a certain number of minutes between each small amount — four, eight, nine, or fifteen minutes depending on whom one asks. The underlying assumption of this practice is that *kdei achilas pras* measures the break. Yet this *gemara* is quite explicit that it measures the total duration of the process. The *baraisa* leaves no room for ambiguity: *im yesh mit'chilah ad t'chilah rishonah... bichdei achilas pras mitztaref* — from the first beginning to the final end.

Similarly, some people who seek to be stringent regarding eating *matzah* on the Seder night — first magnifying the *shiur* of *kezayit* to a very large size, and then rushing to consume it within two minutes — are measuring from first bite to last bite. That is the correct frame. The *gemara* here, and the explicit language of the *baraisa*, establish that it is the total duration of eating, not the interval between bites, that is measured.

In practice, a degree of *chumra* tends to be applied in both directions depending on the context: those trying to fulfill a *mitzvah* measure the shorter total-process time; those trying to avoid liability on Yom Kippur measure the break, taking the more stringent position in that direction. But the plain sense of the *gemara* is consistent: it is always the total process. This has clear implications as well for the notion, found in some Tosafot contexts, that *kdei achilas pras* measures the *hefsek*, the break — an understanding that this *gemara* substantially complicates.

## Nursing Mother

### Infant Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתִּירוּ לְמַגַּע טְמֵא מֵת לְהָנִיק בְּנָהּ, וּבְנָהּ טָהוֹר. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: אִם יֵשׁ מִתְּחִלַּת אֲכִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְעַד סוֹף אֲכִילָה בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס – מִצְטָרֵף. קָתָנֵי: הִתִּירוּ לְמַגַּע טְמֵא מֵת לְהָנִיק אֶת בְּנָהּ, וּבְנָהּ טָהוֹר – אַמַּאי טָהוֹר? כֵּיוָן דִּיינַק חָלָב אִיטַּמִּי לֵיהּ מֵחָלָב!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a woman who has the status of first-degree impurity because she came into **contact** with one who was **impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse,** the Sages **permitted her to nurse her child, and her child** remains **pure.** If the child touches *teruma* he does not render it disqualified, despite having consumed milk that presumably became impure upon leaving the body of the mother. This concludes the *baraita*. The Gemara further discusses the *baraita*: It was **taught** that with regard to a woman who came in **contact** with one who was **impure** due to **a corpse,** the Sages **permitted** her **to nurse her child, and her child** remains **pure** and may therefore be fed *teruma*. The Gemara asks: **Why** is he **pure? Once he nurses** from **the milk** of his mother, **he becomes impure from the milk.**

The *baraisa* concludes with the ruling: *hutru l'maga t'mei meit l'hanik et b'nah, u'vnah tahor* — they permitted a woman who had touched someone who touched a corpse, thereby rendering her a *rishon l'tumah*, to nurse her child, and her child is *tahor*. The reason she herself is only a *rishon* and not an *av hatumah* is that she did not touch the corpse directly but the person who touched it; touching a *rishon* does not render another person *tamei*, since a person can only become *tamei* by direct contact with an *av*. But the more pressing question concerns the milk.

*Amai tahor?* Why is the infant *tahor*? Since he is nursing *tamei* milk — *keivan d'yanak chalav, itmi lei meichalav* — should he not become *tamei* through what he drinks?

### Milk Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לָא אִיתַּכְשַׁר – נִתַּכְשַׁר בְּטִיפָּה מְלוּכְלֶכֶת עַל פִּי הַדַּד! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בִּתְקִיפָה אַחַת, לֹא הִנִּיחַ טִיפָּה מְלוּכְלֶכֶת עַל פִּי הַדַּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the mother's milk **was not rendered susceptible** to impurity because it never came into contact with a liquid, which is necessary in order to render a food item susceptible to impurity, that is not so, as **it** is **rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **due to the drop** of milk that is **smeared on the nipple.** Since this drop is not consumed by the child, it attains the status of a liquid rather than a food, and it subsequently renders the rest of the milk that passes through the nipple susceptible to impurity. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case where the child nursed **with one strong** suck, and therefore it did **not leave a drop** of milk **smeared on the nipple.**

The *gemara* raises the possibility that the milk was never rendered susceptible to *tum'ah* — *lo huchshar*. Recall the principle of *hechsher zera'im*: food does not become *tamei* until it has first come into contact with liquid. But milk is itself a liquid — so why would it need further contact with liquid to become susceptible? The answer lies in how we categorize milk. Rashi, drawing on a parallel from the *halakhot* of *Shabbat* — where liquid squeezed directly onto food is considered food rather than liquid — argues that mother's milk, from the infant's perspective, is food rather than liquid. The infant does not drink milk; he eats it, in the functional sense. If milk is categorized as food, it requires contact with liquid to become susceptible. If it were a liquid, no such step would be needed.

But one can immediately ask: does the milk not come into contact with liquid in the very act of nursing? *Nuchshar b'tipa m'luchlechet al pi hadad* — the answer proposed is that there is always a soiled drop of milk remaining on the breast itself. Not all the milk travels into the infant; some remains on the mother. That remainder is not food (it is not going into anyone's belly) and is therefore liquid. This liquid drop touches the milk flowing toward the infant and renders it susceptible. So the milk is *huchshar*, and if it is *tamei*, the infant should become *tamei* too.

### Rabbah Responses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁתֵּי תְשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר: חֲדָא, דְּקָחָזֵינָא לְפוּמֵּיהּ דְּיָנוֹקָא דִּמְלֵא חָלָב! וְעוֹד, מְקוֹם חָלָב מַעְיָין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There are **two refutations of** this **statement: One** is **that we see** that **the mouth of the infant is filled with milk,** which means that it is impossible for it to have sucked so powerfully that it immediately swallowed all the milk without leaving a drop on the nipple. **And furthermore,** the mother's milk does not need to be rendered susceptible to impurity like other foods in order to become impure and transmit impurity. This is because **the location** from which **the milk** emerges **is a spring,** i.e., it has the same status as the woman's body. Therefore, if the woman is impure, her milk is also impure.

Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha offers a response to this: *she'yanak b'tkifa achat v'lo hiniach tipa m'luchlechet al pi hadad* — the infant nursed in one continuous latch, never detaching, and thus no drop remained on the breast to render the milk susceptible. Rabbah himself then advances two rejoinders to this claim. 

### Wellspring Principle

The first: *d'chazina l'pumei d'yanuka d'malei chalav* — even when an infant nurses without interruption, when he finally detaches, his lips are visibly coated with milk. Milk has not all entered; some always remains outside. That remainder will always function as the liquid that renders the rest susceptible.

The second — and more fundamental — response: *makom chalav ma'ayan* — forget the entire framework of contact with liquid and susceptibility. That framework treats the milk as something external that happened to come into contact with the *tamei* woman. But that is wrong. Fluids produced from within a person's own body have a different status entirely — they are a *ma'ayan*, a wellspring. The principle, as Tosafot explains more clearly: individuals whose *tum'ah* derives from within their own bodies — a *niddah*, a *zavah*, a *zav*, a *yoledes* — transmit *tum'ah* through their own bodily fluids as an *av hatumah*. Their saliva, their blood-flow, their discharge — all are *av hatumah*, not a *rishon*. They do not require *hechsher*; they do not require any contact with external liquid. They are produced by the body itself and carry the body's *tum'ah* inherently.

Applying this to the nursing mother: if she is a *niddah* or *yoledes* (as is commonly the case with a new mother), her milk ought to carry the status of her *ma'ayan* and be *tamei* as an *av hatumah*, not merely a *rishon*. No *hechsher* is needed, and the infant ought to become *tamei* from consuming it. The question of the soiled drop becomes irrelevant.

### Safek Safeka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּבְנָהּ טָהוֹר – דְּסָפֵק יָנַק כַּשִּׁיעוּר וְסָפֵק לֹא יָנַק, וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר יָנַק, סָפֵק יְנָקוֹ בַּאֲכִילַת פְּרָס, סְפֵק יְנָקוֹ בְּיוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that **this is the reason that her child** remains **pure:** The reason is **that** it is **uncertain** whether it **nursed the measure** of milk necessary to disqualify it from consuming *teruma*, **or** whether it did **not nurse** a sufficient amount of milk. **And** even **if you say** that it **nursed** a sufficient amount of milk, it is still **uncertain** whether it **nursed** the required amount of milk **within** the time it takes to **eat a half-loaf** of bread, or whether it **nursed** that amount **in more than** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread and therefore is not disqualified from consuming *teruma*.

This double doubt also highlights the conclusion discussed earlier regarding the standard of *kdei achilas pras*: it applies uniformly as the measuring window regardless of the quantity of the *shiur* involved. Whether the relevant *shiur* is a single *kezayit* or four *kezaysim* or a *revi'is* of liquid, the time window used for combination is always *kdei achilas pras*. The *revi'is* of liquid, one might expect, should be measured by *kdei shtiyat revi'is*, the time to drink a *revi'is* — yet it too is measured by the eating standard. This unusual feature is noted as something that receives fuller treatment in other places, such as *Nazir*, but for present purposes the rule is clear: *kdei achilas pras* is the universal benchmark.

## Nursing Mother (II)

### Nine Fluids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא, מְקוֹם חָלָב מַעְיָין הוּא, וְלֹא צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר? וְהָתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּטַף חָלָב מִדַּדֶּיהָ וְנָפַל לַאֲוִיר תַּנּוּר – תַּנּוּר טָמֵא. וְקַשְׁיָא לַן: בְּמַאי אִיתַּכְשַׁר? וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּטִיפָּה מְלוּכְלֶכֶת עַל פִּי הַדַּד. וְכִי תֵּימָא, רָבָא לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְהָתַנְיָא: נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה אוֹמֵר, תִּשְׁעָה מַשְׁקִין בְּזוֹב: הַזֵּיעָה, וְלֵיחָה סְרוּחָה, וְרֶיעִי – טְהוֹרִין מִכְּלוּם. דִּמְעַת עֵינוֹ, וְדַם מַגֵּפָתוֹ, וַחֲלֵב אִשָּׁה – מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין בִּרְבִיעִית. רוּקּוֹ, זוֹבוֹ, וּמֵימֵי רַגְלָיו – מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, בְּכֹל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rava,** is it correct that **the location** from which the **milk** emerges **is** considered like **a spring, and** therefore if the woman is impure the milk is also impure, **and it does not need** to be **rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity by contact with a liquid? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kelim* 8:11): In the case of a menstruating **woman who had milk dripping from her nipples, and it fell into the airspace of an oven, the oven** becomes **impure. And** this poses **a difficulty for us: In what** way was the milk **rendered susceptible** to impurity, such that it can become impure or render the oven impure? **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It was rendered susceptible **by the drop** of milk **smeared on the nipple.** Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that a woman's milk must be rendered susceptible in order to contract impurity. **And if you would say** that **Rava does not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** as one *amora* is permitted to disagree with another, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **You are found saying** that there are **nine liquids with regard to a *zav*: The sweat, ill-smelling pus, and** liquid **excrement** are more **pure than all of them,** i.e., they do not become impure and do not render other items susceptible to impurity. **The tears** that emerge from his **eye, and the blood** that emerges from his **wound,** **and the milk of a woman** who is a *zava* **transmit impurity of liquids where** there is **a quarter-***log*. The **saliva, gonorrhea-like discharge** of a *zav*, **and urine transmit severe impurity in any amount.**

The *gemara* now examines Rabbah's *ma'ayan* principle more closely. *ולרבא מקום חלב מעיין הוא?* — Is it really true, according to Rava, that the source of milk is a *ma'ayan*? If so, does it not require *hechsher*? But the *gemara* notes that this appears to contradict Rabbi Yochanan, who, explaining a *mishnah* about milk from a *niddah* falling into the airspace of an oven, said that the milk became susceptible — *b'tibu v'luchluchis al pi yadeha* — through the soiled drop on the breast. Rabbi Yochanan's entire answer only makes sense if milk is not automatically an *av hatumah* as a *ma'ayan* but requires *hechsher* like any other food. Perhaps Rava simply disagrees with Rabbi Yochanan.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita* that is more conclusive: *nimtzas atah omer tisha mashkin b'zav* — there are nine fluids associated with a *zav*, and they fall into three categories of three.

### Milk Category Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְקוֹם חָלָב מְעַיֵּין [הוּא], חָלָב נָמֵי נְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה בְּכֹל שֶׁהוּא, כְּזוֹבוֹ וְרוּקּוֹ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מְקוֹם חֲלֵב אִשָּׁה לָאו מַעְיָין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** in accordance with the opinion of Rava that **the location** from which the **milk** emerges is considered **a spring,** then **milk also should transmit severe impurity in any amount, like the gonorrhea-like discharge** of a *zav* **and his saliva. Rather, conclude from it** that **the location** from which the **milk of a woman** emerges **is not** considered to be **a spring,** and the milk must be rendered susceptible to impurity in order to become impure or transmit impurity.

The three in the lowest category — sweat, putrid liquid, and feces — are *tehorin mikulam*, completely pure, because they are too repugnant to serve any purpose. The three in the highest category — *roko* (saliva), *zovo* (the *zav*'s flow, the *niddah*'s blood), and *mei raglav* (urine) — are *av hatumah b'chol shehu*: they carry the full *tum'ah* of the person, in even the smallest quantity, as a *ma'ayan*. Between these two extremes sit three fluids: tears, blood from a wound, and *chalav isha* — milk of a woman. These *metamin tumas mashkin b'revi'is*: they have the *tum'ah* of ordinary liquids, requiring a *revi'is* in quantity, and do not carry the elevated status of a *ma'ayan*.

*Ve'im amarta d'makom chalav ma'ayan — chalav nami nitma tumah chamurah b'chol shehu k'zovo v'roko* — if milk were truly a *ma'ayan*, it should have the same stringent status as saliva and the flow, *tumah chamurah b'chol shehu*. Since it does not, and instead occupies this middle category, the conclusion is *shema mina makom chalav ishah lav ma'ayan hu* — the source of a woman's milk is not a *ma'ayan*. Rava's earlier argument is thereby refuted: the infant is *tahor* not because of the *ma'ayan* principle, but because of the *safek safeka* — doubt about quantity and time.

The *gemara* also revisits the *baraita* that stated milk is *tamei* whether the woman desired it or not — *bein leratzon bein shelo leratzon* — which Rava had cited to support the *ma'ayan* principle. The *gemara* reinterprets *shelo leratzon*: it does not mean the woman actively did not want the milk; it means she had no active intent either way — *bistama*. If the infant wants the milk, *daiteih d'tinok krevah legabei chalav*, that suffices as the desired contact. Only if the woman actively declared she did not want the milk — *aval amar lo nicha lei* — would it be *tahor*. The requirement of *ratzon* is thus preserved, and the *baraita* does not require treating milk as a *ma'ayan*.

## Wine/Intoxication

### Yayin V'Shechar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: ״יַיִן אַל תֵּשְׁתְּ וְשֵׁכָר״ – אַל תִּשְׁתֵּהוּ כְּדֶרֶךְ שִׁכְרוּתוֹ, הָא אִם הִפְסִיק בּוֹ, אוֹ נָתַן לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם כֹּל שֶׁהוּא – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that the verse is interpreted to mean: **Wine you shall not drink, and intoxicating drink,** meaning that **you shall not drink it in the manner of its being intoxicating.** But **if one interrupted** his drinking, **or placed any amount of water into it** and drank it, he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* turns to the prohibition against entering the *Beit HaMikdash* while intoxicated, rooted in the verse *yayin veshichar al tesht atah uvaneykha itakh bevochem el ohel moed velo tamutu* — spoken immediately following the deaths of *Nadav va'Avihu*, which *Chazal* understand to imply that intoxication while performing *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* was among their sins. *Moshe* commands: do not drink wine or intoxicating drink when entering, or you will die — a punishment at the hands of Heaven, *bidei shamayim*.

### Revi'is Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יַיִן וְשֵׁכָר אַל תֵּשְׁתְּ״ – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ כֹּל שֶׁהוּא, אֲפִילּוּ מִגִּתּוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשֵׁכָר״ – אֵין אָסוּר אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לְשַׁכֵּר, וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי לְשַׁכֵּר? רְבִיעִית יַיִן בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יַיִן״? לוֹמַר לְךָ שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין עָלָיו כֹּל שֶׁהוּא, וּמוּזְהָרִין עָלָיו מִגִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the Torah instructs Aaron the High Priest: **"Drink no wine nor intoxicating drink,** you, nor your sons with you, when you go into the Tent of Meeting, that you shall not die" (Leviticus 10:9), one **might** have thought that this applies **even** if he drank **any amount,** and **even** if he drank wine **from its press,** i.e., wine that has not finished fermenting. Therefore, **the verse states: "Nor intoxicating drink,"** indicating that **only** the consumption of a quantity of wine sufficient **to intoxicate** is **prohibited. And how much** wine is sufficient **to intoxicate?** It is a minimum of **a quarter-***log* of **wine** that is **forty days old,** which has already fermented. **If so, why** must **the verse state "wine,"** when the term "intoxicating drink" would have sufficed? It is **to tell you that** although one is not liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven for it, it is **prohibited** to drink **any amount of it,** even less than a quarter-*log*, and then enter the Temple; **and** similarly, it is **prohibited** to drink it **from its press** and then enter the Temple, and one who does so is liable to be flogged, as is the case with any other prohibition by Torah law.

The *tanna kamma* reads *yayin veshichar* not as two separate objects but as a single compound: intoxicating wine. *Yachol afilu kol shehu* — one might think even the smallest amount triggers liability, and even freshly pressed grape juice — *yayin migito*, which has not yet fermented. The response: *talmud lomar veshichar* — *sheichar* qualifies the wine. *Ein assur ela kedei leshaker* — only wine that can intoxicate, meaning it must be fermented (at least forty days old) and must be a *revi'is*, roughly three and a half ounces. However, *yayin* adds a separate level: even the smallest amount of wine, even *migito* before fermentation, constitutes a transgression — just not liability for *mita*. Thus two tiers: transgression for any wine, liability for death only for a full intoxicating *revi'is*.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״יַיִן״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא יַיִן, שְׁאָר מְשַׁכְּרִין מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשֵׁכָר״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יַיִן״? עַל הַיַּיִן – בְּמִיתָה, וְעַל שְׁאָר מַשְׁקִין – בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** From the word **"wine" I have** derived **only** that **wine** is forbidden; **from where** is it derived that **other intoxicating beverages** are forbidden as well? **The verse states: "Nor intoxicating drink." If so, why** must **the verse state "wine"?** This comes to teach that **for** entering the Temple after drinking **wine** one is liable to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven, **but for** entering after drinking **other** intoxicating **drinks,** one violates only a regular **prohibition.**

Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse differently: *yayin* and *sheichar* are two independent terms. *Ein li ela yayin* — wine I know; *she'ar mashkin meshakrin minayin* — what about other intoxicating drinks? *Talmud lomar veshichar* — the second term includes all other intoxicants. His two tiers are not about quantity and fermentation but about category: wine triggers liability for *mita*; other intoxicating beverages trigger only the prohibition. He derives *sheichar* independently rather than learning it from the *gezeira shava* with *nazir*, which the *tanna kamma* uses to confine *sheichar* to wine.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

**Rabbi Eliezer takes yet another reading:** *yayin al tesht vesheichar al tesht kederekh shichruto* — do not drink wine in the manner of intoxication. The addition of *sheichar* qualifies not what is drunk but how it is drunk. *Ha im hifsik bo o shenatan letocho mayim kol shehu patur* — if one took a break in drinking, or added even the smallest amount of water to the wine, one is exempt. The prohibition attaches not to the wine as such but to drinking wine in a way that produces intoxication. Interruption or dilution changes the character of the act.

### Ruling Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי פְלִיגִי? תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: גָּמְרִינַן ״שֵׁכָר״ ״שֵׁכָר״ מִנָּזִיר. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא יָלֵיף ״שֵׁכָר״ ״שֵׁכָר״ מִנָּזִיר. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר: מַאי ״שֵׁכָר״? מִידֵּי דְּהוּא מְשַׁכֵּר. כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: אָכַל דְּבֵילָה קְעִילִית, וְשָׁתָה דְּבַשׁ אוֹ חָלָב, וְנִכְנַס לַמִּקְדָּשׁ וְשִׁימֵּשׁ – לוֹקֶה, כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אֲחוֹתַאי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what do they disagree? The first *tanna* holds: We learn** by verbal analogy that the term **"intoxicating drink"** is referring to wine, **from** the term **"intoxicating drink"** mentioned with regard to **a nazirite** in the verse: "He shall abstain from wine and intoxicating drink" (Numbers 6:3). There it is referring only to wine (see *Nazir* 4a). **But Rabbi Yehuda does not derive** that the term **"intoxicating drink"** refers only to wine by the verbal analogy **from** the term **"intoxicating drink"** stated with regard to **a nazirite.** Consequently, he interprets the word as referring to an intoxicating drink that is not wine. **And Rabbi Elazar holds: What** is the meaning of the phrase: "Wine and **intoxicating drink"?** It is not referring to two separate items, but rather to wine **in the manner that it intoxicates.** The Gemara comments: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which is taught** in the following *baraita*: In the case of one who **ate** a sweet **dried fig from Ke'ila, or drank honey or milk,** all of which can have an intoxicating effect, **and** he **entered the Temple and performed** the Temple service, he is **flogged. In accordance with whose** opinion is this? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that any item which intoxicates is included in the prohibition. **Rav Yehuda bar Aḥotai says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, and Rav referred to Rabbi Elazar as the most gratified of the Sages,** as the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion.

The *gemara* then asks which *tanna* the following *baraita* follows: one who eats pressed dates from *Ke'ilit*, or drinks honey, or milk, and enters the *Beit HaMikdash* and performs the *avodah* — this goes like Rabbi Yehuda, who extends the prohibition beyond wine to any intoxicating substance. Note the peculiarity of milk as an intoxicant — one commentator suggests a connection to the episode of Yael and Sisera, where milk produced drowsiness. Whatever the association, Rabbi Yehuda's position encompasses non-wine intoxicants, though without carrying the *mita* penalty.

Rav Yehuda bar Chusai rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer* — the law follows Rabbi Eliezer. If one drank only a *revi'is* and added a small amount of water or took a break, one is exempt. Rav, upon hearing this ruling about Rabbi Eliezer, called him *tuvina de'chakimei* — among the finest of the sages.

### Rav Acha Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא דְּהוּצָל הֲוָה נִידְרָא עֲלַהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל הָאִידָּנָא וְתָא לִמְחַר, דְּרַב לָא מוֹקֵי אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ מִיּוֹמָא טָבָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שִׁכְרוּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָאָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וּמָר הוּא דְּקָא רָמֵי בֵּיהּ מַיָּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בִּרְבִיעִית, הָא בְּיוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Aḥa** of the city **of Huzal had** taken **a vow** not to derive benefit **from his wife. He came before Rav Ashi** to request that he dissolve the vow. Rav Ashi **said to him: Go now and come** back **tomorrow,** as I have just drunk wine, and it is prohibited for me to issue a halakhic ruling, **as Rav** would **not place a disseminator before him** to communicate his lectures to the masses **from** the meal of one **Festival** day **until the other,** i.e., the next morning, **due to drunkenness.** Since it was customary to drink wine during Festival meals, Rav would not deliver public lectures on Festival days, as one who has consumed wine may not issue halakhic rulings. Rav Aḥa **said to him: But doesn't Rav say:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar** that if one diluted the wine he drank with water and entered the Temple he is exempt; **and the Master is** one **who puts water into** his wine? Rav Ashi **said to him: That** is **not difficult; that** ruling of Rabbi Elazar applies in a case **where** one drank precisely **a quarter**-*log* of wine, whereas in **this** case I drank **more than a quarter**-*log* of wine. In such a case one does not maintain a clear mind even if he mixed in a small amount of water.

Rav Acha mi-Hutza had a *neder* involving his wife that required annulment. He went before Rav Ashi to have it dissolved. Rav Ashi told him: go today and return tomorrow — *zil idna ve'ta le'machar*. The reason: *Rav lo moki amora aleih mi'yoma tava le'chavreih* — Rav would not appoint an *amora* to project his teaching from one *Yom Tov* to the next, meaning on consecutive days of *Yom Tov*. Why? Because after the festive meal with wine, he feared he might be mildly intoxicated, and the prohibition extends not only to entering the *Beit HaMikdash* but also, as the verse continues, *u'le'horot et benei Yisrael* — to giving *hora'ah*, halakhic instruction. Thus Rav Ashi declined to rule on the *neder*, citing that same concern.

The one raising the question pointed out: but Rav himself ruled that the *halacha* is like Rabbi Eliezer — and in Rav Ashi's case water had been added to the wine, so it should not be an issue. Rav Ashi replied: *ha lo kashya — ha birevi'is ha b'yoter mi'revi'is*. There is a distinction. If one drank only a *revi'is*, adding water or taking a break is sufficient. But if one drank considerably more than a *revi'is*, this leniency no longer applies — and Rav Ashi, having drunk more at the Yom Tov meal, could not rely on it. The irony is not lost: the very discussion of why halakhic rulings cannot be given while intoxicated is itself a halakhic discussion — though one could argue that insofar as the explanation is informational rather than active *hora'ah*, it falls in a different category.

## Drunk Prohibitions

### Kodesh/Chol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּלְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין הַחוֹל״ – אֵלּוּ דָּמִין וַעֲרָכִין, חֲרָמִין וְהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And that you may differentiate between the sacred and the common." These** terms are referring to the *halakhot* of **values and valuations, dedications and consecrations,** and the verse is teaching that it is prohibited to issue a ruling concerning these matters after drinking wine.

The *gemara* now elaborates the full scope of what is prohibited to an intoxicated person. The verse *u'lehavdil bein hakodesh u'vein hachol* — to distinguish between the holy and the profane — identifies one category of forbidden activity. What constitutes *kodesh v'chol* for these purposes? *Elu damim v'erchin charamim v'hekdeshos* — these are assessments of *damim* (monetary values for redemption), *erchin* (bodily assessments vowed to the Temple), *charamim* (consecrated items), and general *hekdesh*. Among the duties of the *kohen* was assessing the value of consecrated items to permit their redemption. Even this kind of monetary determination, requiring a clear head, may not be performed while intoxicated.

### Tamei/Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֵּין הַטָּמֵא וּבֵין הַטָּהוֹר״ – אֵלּוּ טְמָאוֹת וּטְהָרוֹת. ״וּלְהוֹרוֹת״ – זוּ הוֹרָאָה. ״אֵת כׇּל הַחוּקִּים״ – אֵלּוּ מִדְרָשׁוֹת. ״אֲשֶׁר דְּבַר ה׳״ – זוּ הֲלָכָה. ״בְּיַד מֹשֶׁה״ – זֶה תַּלְמוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **"Between the impure and the pure"; these** terms indicate that it is prohibited for one who drank wine to render decisions with regard to **ritually impure** items **and ritually pure** items. **"And that you may instruct"; this** is referring to **issuing a ruling** about what is permitted or prohibited. **"All the statutes"; this** is referring to the halakhic **expositions** of the Torah. **"Which the Lord has spoken"; this** is referring to ***halakha*** transmitted orally to Moses from Sinai. **"By the hand of Moses"; this** is referring to the **Talmud,** the deliberations on the Oral Law, from which halakhic conclusions are derived. It is prohibited to teach any of these subjects after drinking wine.

*Bein hatamei u'vein hatahor — elu tumos v'taharos* — similarly, one may not render rulings on *tum'ah* and *tahara* while intoxicated.

*U'lehoros — zu hora'ah*: and to give instruction — this is *hora'ah*, normative halakhic ruling. *Es kol hachukim — elu midrashos*: "all the statutes" refers to *midrashos*, expository teachings. Rashi explains this as referring to what is taught publicly in a *pirkah*, a formal lecture. That *midrash* — seemingly non-binding exposition — is included here as prohibited is somewhat surprising, but the verse subsumes it. *Asher diber Hashem — zu halacha*: the phrase "which God spoke" refers to received *halacha*. The implication is that even transmitting plain, established *halacha* without creative analysis is prohibited, not only *hora'ah* in the sense of novel judgment.

### Mishnah/Talmud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף הַמִּשְׁנָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּלְהוֹרוֹת״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל אַף תַּלְמוּד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּלְהוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that it is prohibited to teach **even Mishna** after drinking wine. Therefore, **the verse states: "And that you may instruct,"** indicating that the prohibition is limited to material that provides practical halakhic instruction, whereas one does not derive practical rulings from the Mishna. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** One **might** have thought that **even** teaching **Talmud** is prohibited. Therefore, **the verse states: "And that you may instruct,"** indicating that the prohibition is limited to issuing halakhic rulings, but it does not include teaching material such as Talmud, although halakhic conclusions may be derived from it.

*B'yad Moshe — zeh Talmud*: "through the hand of Moshe" refers to *Talmud*, meaning analytic study and reasoning. Could even *mishnah* be prohibited? *Talmud lomar u'lehoros* — the word *horaah* limits the prohibition to activities requiring judgment and application. Pure *mishnah* — the repetition of tannaitic statements with no ruling function — does not constitute *hora'ah* and is not prohibited.

### Rabbi Yose View

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda goes further: could even *Talmud* be prohibited? *Talmud lomar u'lehoros* — no, only things that carry actual *hora'ah*, halakhic application, are prohibited. Analytical learning that does not yet reach the level of practical ruling is permitted.

### Rav Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: יָצָא שֶׁרֶץ טָמֵא, וּצְפַרְדֵּעַ טָהוֹר, שֶׁשְּׁתוּיֵי יַיִן מוֹרִין בָּהֶן הוֹרָאָה. נֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְלָא רַבָּנַן? – אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּזִיל קְרִי בֵּי רַב הוּא. אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The rulings that a carcass of **a creeping animal is ritually impure and** that a carcass of **a frog is pure** are **excluded** from this principle, **as those who have drunk wine may issue** a halakhic **ruling** about these matters. **Let us say** that this **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** who says it is permitted to teach Talmud, and these conclusions are obvious from the Talmud, **and** that it is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara responds: **You** may **even say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** that **this** case **is different, as** it **is** a topic that one could **go learn in a children's school,** and teaching is not considered issuing a halakhic ruling. **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.** One may engage in analytical study even while intoxicated.

The *gemara* notes that this ruling aligns with what is taught in a *baraita*: to say, for example, that a *sheretz* is *tamei* and a *tzfardea* is *tahor* — even intoxicated people can give rulings on such blatantly obvious matters. Who is this like? Perhaps Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, who permits *Talmud* and restricts only actual *hora'ah*. But even according to the Rabbis, one could say that questions of that kind — *d'zil krei bei rav*, which any schoolchild knows — are so self-evident they do not even rise to the level of making a meaningful statement, let alone *hora'ah*. Rav rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* — one may engage in *Talmud* study even while intoxicated.

### Rav Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב לָא מוֹקֵים אָמוֹרָא מִיּוֹמָא טָבָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שִׁכְרוּת! שָׁאנֵי רַב, דְּאוֹרִי מוֹרֵי. וְנֹיקֵם (דְּלָא) [וְלָא] לוֹרֵי! – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּיָתֵיב רַב לָא סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּלָא הוֹרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But Rav** himself would **not place a disseminator before him** to communicate his lectures to the masses **from** the meal of one **Festival** day **until the other,** i.e., the next morning, **due to drunkenness.** The Gemara explains: **Rav is different, as he would issue** halakhic **rulings** during his lectures. The Gemara further objects: **And let him place** a disseminator and deliver a lecture **but not issue** halakhic **rulings.** The Gemara explains: **Wherever Rav sits** and delivers a lecture, it is **not possible** for him to do so **without** issuing a halakhic **ruling.**

But this creates an obvious tension. We just established that Rav refused to give his *shiur* between one *Yom Tov* and the next because of concern about intoxication — yet Rav himself ruled that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, permitting analytic study while intoxicated. Why, then, could Rav not simply give his *shiur* while making clear he would not render practical rulings?

The *gemara* resolves this: *shani Rav d'orei morei* — Rav is different, because *kol heicha deyativ Rav, lo sagi lei belo hora'ah* — wherever Rav sits, it is impossible for him to avoid *hora'ah*. Either his natural inclination draws him toward rendering rulings, or those in attendance will inevitably press him for answers. A shiur given by Rav cannot remain purely theoretical; it will inescapably produce practical *psak halacha*. For that reason alone, Rav would not give his *shiur* when he had reason to fear he was not fully sober. The ultimate concern throughout all of these prohibitions, as the sugya makes clear, is *psak halacha* — the practical ruling — and it is that which Rav was careful to avoid.
