# Hullin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shisuah & Torah

### Shisuah Definition

The daf is 61, though the shiur begins a page earlier on 60b. Approximately ten to twelve lines down, the Gemara records a statement of Rav Chana bar Rava concerning the *shisuah*. The *shisuah* appears in the Torah's list of non-kosher animals: *ach et zeh lo tocheilu mima'alei hageirah umimafrisei haparsah hashisuah* — do not eat from those that chew the cud and have split hooves: the *shisuah*, the camel, the hare, and so on. The Gemara elects to read *hashisuah* not as an adjective modifying *mafrisei haparsah* but as the first noun in the list of forbidden animals — a creature unto itself. And what is it? *Beryah bifnei atzmah hi* — it is an independent creature. *Yesh lah shnei gavim ushnei shidra'os* — it has two backs and two spinal cords. Whatever exactly it is, it belongs to that category of animals we have never encountered.

### Moshe's Knowledge

The Gemara then asks: *v'chi Moshe Rabbeinu kanigi hayah o balistri hayah?* Was Moshe Rabbeinu a trapper or a hunter? The word *balistri* derives from the same root as "ballista" — one who hurls projectiles — and *kanigi* is a word for a hunter. The question is pointed: how did Moshe know to include in his list of non-kosher animals a creature so bizarre that he could never have observed it in the natural world? The answer follows: *mikan teshuvah l'omer ein Torah min hashamayim* — here is a refutation of anyone who claims the Torah was Moshe's own composition. Had he invented it himself, how would he have known to mention the *shisuah*?

### Loanwords: Kanigi & Bali…

Having heard this *drasha*, Rav Chisda said to Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi: *zil kesov kanigi uvalistri b'aggadetecha* — go and write down the words *kanigi* and *balistri* in your *aggadeta*, *uperashah* — and make sure you explain them. These are foreign loanwords — Greek, in fact — and not everyone who encounters this *aggadeta* will know what they mean. Presumably when these teachings originated in Eretz Yisrael, the Greek terms were familiar to the audience. But for those transmitting the tradition in Babylonia, a marginal note or an explanation alongside the text was essential so that the unusual vocabulary would not become an obstacle to understanding.

## Philistines & Ivim

### Five Leaders, Six Named

In the spirit of recording strange vocabulary with care, the Gemara moves to a related case. The book of Yehoshua speaks of *chamesh sarnei Plishtim* — the five Philistine leaders — and then lists six: the Azzati, Ashdodi, Ashkeloni, Gitti, Ekroni, *v'ha'Ivim*. The text announces five and then enumerates six. How is this reconciled? Rav Yonasan says: *arunki shelahen chamishah* — their true, prominent leaders number only five. The word *arunki* is not Hebrew or Aramaic; it is a foreign term, with some manuscript variants reading *adindiki* or similar. The appearance of this foreign loanword is precisely the occasion for what follows.

### Arunki Explanation

Again Rav Chisda said to Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi: *kesov Arunki b'aggadetecha uperashah* — write down this word *arunki* in your collection of *aggadetos* and make sure to explain it. Like *kanigi* and *balistri*, this is a term that could easily become opaque in transmission, and the care taken to record and gloss such foreign expressions reflects the broader commitment to preserving the intelligibility of *aggadeta* across generations.

### Ivim from Teiman

Having raised the *drasha*, it is worth noting how Tosafot resolves the underlying textual difficulty. The verse immediately following the list of the six names reads: *miteiman kol eretz haKna'ani* — from Teiman, the whole land of Canaan. Tosafot points out that the period, so to speak, falls before the word *v'ha'Ivim*: the five are the five leaders of the Philistines, and the *Ivim* belong to the next verse — they came from Teiman, which is a territory associated with a grandson of Esav. This reading is reinforced grammatically: the five Philistine leaders each carry a *yud* at the end of their designation — *ha'Azzati*, *ha'Ashdodi*, *ha'Ashkeloni*, and so on — a form suggesting "leader of such-and-such." But *ha'Ivim* lacks that final *yud*; it does not follow the same grammatical pattern and therefore does not belong to the same list.

The Gemara confirms this with a *Baraita*: *Ivim miTeiman bau* — the Ivim came from Teiman. Why are they called *Ivim*? The *Baraita* offers several explanations. *She'ivtu et mekomam* — they either perverted or abandoned their place. Rashi understands this to mean they left their original land, which was either destroyed or abandoned. A second explanation: *she'ivu lelohut harbe* — they craved many gods, reading *Ivim* with an *alef*, meaning they lusted after the idolatrous practices of the Philistines and were drawn after them. A third: *shekol haro'am ochazato avit* — anyone who sees them is seized by trembling. Whatever the precise etymology, the upshot is clear: the Ivim were not originally Philistines at all but descended from Esav through Teiman, and this reading resolves the discrepancy between five and six.

## Torah's Hidden Meaning

### Ivim Description

Rav Yosef adds a vivid detail about the Ivim: *d'is lehu shitsa darei shinei lechol chad vchad* — each one of them has sixteen rows of teeth. This detail returns us to the theme of describing extraordinary creatures and beings encountered in the *aggadeta*. And it sets the stage for a broader point made by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish teaches: *harbe mikra'ot she're'uyin lisrof* — there are many verses that to an unlearned eye would seem fit to be discarded. They appear trivially historical, antiquarian digressions with no obvious relevance to *halacha* or theology. And yet upon closer examination they turn out to be *gufei Torah* — the very body of the Torah, central to critical ideas. What follows illustrates this principle through the very passage about the Ivim.

### Avimelech Covenant

The verse in Devarim reads: *v'ha'Ivim hayoshvim b'chatzerim ad Aza* — the Ivim who dwelt in Chatzerim until Gaza. One might dismiss this as mere geographical background. *Mai nafka lan minah* — what does it matter? The Gemara answers: it matters enormously. The reason goes back to the covenant between Avraham and Avimelech, in which Avraham swore: *im tishkor li velini ulenachdi* — you shall not deal falsely with me, nor with my children, nor with my grandchildren. Avraham had bound himself and his descendants not to betray Avimelech and the Philistines.

### Kaftoriim & Conquest

If the Ivim were Philistines — which, as we have seen, is the first *drasha* — then Bnei Yisrael would have been unable to conquer their territory, bound as they were by Avraham's oath. God therefore arranged a solution: *leisu Kaftorim lifku me'Ivim* — let the Kaftoriim come and displace the Ivim. The verse itself says: *Kaftorim hayotzim miKaftor hishmidum vayeshvu tachtam* — the Kaftoriim who came from Kaftor destroyed the Ivim and settled in their place. Once the Kaftoriim occupied that land, it was no longer Philistine territory. *Ve'leisu Yisrael velifku miKaftorim* — and then Israel came and took the land from the Kaftoriim, at which point there was no longer any oath-violation involved.

This is why the Torah bothers to record the seemingly irrelevant history of the Ivim: without it, one might think Bnei Yisrael had violated the covenant with the Philistines. The history reveals that the land had already passed through an intermediate conquest, severing any connection to the original oath. As to who exactly the Kaftoriim were: some identify Kaftor with Crete, which is historically interesting given that archaeologists have concluded that the later Philistines — the Plishtim of the conquest period — were themselves of Aegean or Greek origin. This could be precisely the point: a later Greek people displaced the earlier Ivim, themselves tied to the Philistine territory, so that when Israel arrived the land had already changed hands.

## Conquest Principle

### Sichon's Conquest

A parallel case follows. The Torah states: *ki Cheshbon ir Sichon melech Emori hi vehu nichlam bemelech Moav harishon* — Cheshbon was the city of Sichon king of the Emorites, and he had fought against the first king of Moav. Again the reaction is: *mai nafka minah* — who needs this history? The answer is the same in structure. God had commanded Israel: *al tatzar et Moav* — do not oppress Moav, for they are descendants of Lot, our kinsmen. Had Cheshbon remained Moavite territory, Israel could not have taken it. But God arranged that Sichon would conquer it from Moav first: *leysei Sichon leyfuk mi-Moav* — let Sichon come and seize the land from Moav, *veleysei Yisrael veleysei mi-Sichon* — and then let Israel come and take it from Sichon. Once Sichon had conquered it, the land was no longer Moav's, and the restriction against taking from Moav no longer applied.

This is why Rav Pappa states: *Amon u-Moav tiharu be-Sichon* — Amon and Moav were "purified" by Sichon. The territory that had originally belonged to Amon and Moav became permissible to Israel because Sichon had conquered it, breaking the chain of ownership. The Torah's seemingly tedious historical digression is in fact indispensable to the legal analysis of why Israel was permitted to settle those lands.

### Kibbush Milchamah

From this principle the Gemara in Gittin derives the broader concept of *kibbush milchamah* — territorial acquisition through warfare. One might have argued that conquest is simply theft on a national scale. But the Gemara's position is that conquest in war carries a recognized legal effect: territory captured in war genuinely belongs to the conqueror. It no longer belongs to its former owners. This is precisely what made Israel's possession of those transjordanian territories legitimate — not despite the original prohibitions against taking from Amon, Moav, and the Philistines, but consistent with them, because *kibbush milchamah* had already transferred ownership before Israel arrived.

## Chermon Names

### Siryon & Senir

Still in the spirit of explaining why the Torah records seemingly trivial historical details, the Gemara cites another example. The verse states that the Tzidoniim call Chermon *Siryon*, and the Emoriim call it *Senir*. Why does the Torah bother telling us what foreign nations call this mountain?

### Geography Reverence

The Gemara teaches: *Senir ve-Siryon meharei Eretz Yisrael* — Senir and Siryon are names of mountains in Eretz Yisrael. The nations built great cities for themselves on Mount Chermon, but when they named it, they borrowed the names of mountains from the Land of Israel. Just as Vilna was called the Jerusalem of Eastern Europe, or as people might describe a notable peak as the Alps of some other region — they are honoring a new place by associating it with the paradigmatic one. *Melamedkha she'afilu harei Eretz Yisrael chavivin al umot ha-olam* — this teaches that even the mountains of Eretz Yisrael are beloved to the nations of the world. That is why the Torah records the foreign names: to convey the esteem in which the geography of Israel was held even among those who never lived there.

## Yosef & Egypt

### Yosef's Compassion

A final example from this series: the verse states that Yosef *ve'et ha'am he'evir oto le'arim* — relocated the Egyptian population from city to city during the years of famine, after he had already taken their wealth and their persons. *Mai nafka minah* — why does the Torah record this administrative detail of Egyptian history? The Gemara explains: *delo likru achav galvusa* — so that the Egyptians would not call Yosef's brothers exiles or foreigners. If the Egyptians all remained rooted in their ancestral cities while Yosef's family arrived as newcomers, the Egyptians could say: you are strangers here, you do not belong. By relocating the Egyptians to new cities, Yosef ensured that they too would feel themselves displaced, and could not look down upon the family of Yisrael as foreign interlopers. This was an act of forethought on behalf of his brothers' dignity — and that is why the Torah saw fit to record it.

This concludes the *aggadeta* section of the shiur. It is also worth noting, as was observed in discussion, that the pattern of these *derashot* carries some contemporary relevance: the passages make clear that the territory of Gaza is within the borders of Eretz Yisrael, whereas the status of Mount Chermon is less straightforward from the Gemara's own presentation, even if the boundaries drawn at the end of Bamidbar would seem to include it.

## Bird Simanim Intro

### Simanim Not Explicit

With the *aggadeta* concluded, the Mishnah at the bottom of 60b — which is where the official daf of 61 properly begins — turns to the question of kosher and non-kosher birds. The Mishnah states: *simanei ha'of lo ne'emru* — the identifying characteristics of birds were not stated explicitly in the Torah. The Torah lists the non-kosher birds by name but does not articulate a set of formal *simanim* the way it does for animals (split hooves and chewing cud) or fish (fins and scales). The Mishnah then proceeds to offer some practical guidance: birds that are *dores* — predatory birds of prey — are non-kosher; birds that have certain positive characteristics are kosher.

### Nesher's Traits

The Gemara immediately challenges the Mishnah's premise. A *Baraita* is cited: consider the *nesher* — the bird most likely to be a vulture rather than an eagle, based on other descriptors. *Ma nesher meyuchad she'ein lo etzba yeteirah ve'zefek ve'ein kurkevano niklaf ve'dores* — what is distinctive about the *nesher*? It lacks an *etzba yeteirah* (an elongated or extra finger), it has no *zefek* (crop), its gizzard (*kurkavan*) cannot be peeled, and it is *dores* — a predatory bird. *Tamei* — it is non-kosher. *Af kol kayotzei bo tamei* — anything with those four characteristics is likewise non-kosher.

The *Baraita* continues with the *torim* — turtle doves or pigeons, the birds offered as *korbanot* — which display the opposite profile: they have an *etzba yeteirah*, a *zefek*, a gizzard that can be peeled, and they are not *dores*. *Tehorin* — they are kosher. *Af kol kayotzei bahem tehorin* — anything with all four positive characteristics is likewise kosher.

This *Baraita* appears to contradict the Mishnah's claim that the *simanim* were not stated — here they are being stated and used quite explicitly. Moreover, there is a striking gap: the *Baraita* describes the extreme cases, the bird with all four non-kosher characteristics and the bird with all four kosher characteristics. But the vast middle ground — birds that have some mix — is left unaddressed. Both of these issues require resolution.

## Simanim by Sofrim

### Torah vs. Rabbinic

Abaye resolves the contradiction between the Mishnah and the *Baraita* with precision: *lo ne'emru peirushan midivrei Torah ela midivrei Sofrim* — the Mishnah does not mean that no characteristics exist at all; it means that the characteristics were not made explicit in the Torah. The Sages had to come and identify which features are the operative ones. The *simanim* are real and usable for *halachic* determination, but they are a rabbinic inference, not an explicit biblical articulation.

This clarification matters enormously for the larger question the Gemara is building toward: can one determine whether an unknown bird is kosher on the basis of these characteristics alone, without being able to identify it by name against the Torah's list? Or is the list itself the only operative criterion, with the *simanim* being merely descriptive? Abaye's statement opens the door to the former position — the characteristics are substantive enough to adjudicate — while still acknowledging that they are a product of rabbinic analysis rather than explicit scriptural text.

## Bird Simanim Intro (II)

### Dores Definition

Before addressing the central gap in the *Baraita*, it is worth pausing on the meaning of *dores*. Rashi (*hakuntres*) explains: *she'ocheiz betzipornav umagbiha min hakarka ma she'ochel* — it grabs with its talons and lifts from the ground what it intends to eat. Tosafot objects that this description would apply even to domestic chickens, which also pick up food with their feet. Rabbeinu Tam therefore offers an alternative definition: *dores ve'ochel mechayim ve'eino mamtin ad shetamos* — it preys upon and eats living creatures, not waiting for them to die first. This distinguishes a true bird of prey from a scavenger.

The discussion is complicated by the fact that some birds that would seem to qualify as *dores* under at least one of these definitions are in fact kosher. Ducks, for example, are kosher, yet they plunge their heads into water and swallow live fish. This reinforces the possibility — which the Gemara will shortly develop at length — that *dores* is not the definitive criterion for non-kosher status; it is one of four *simanim*, and the interplay among all four is what ultimately determines a bird's status. The connection to *derusah* — one of the categories of *treifos*, an animal whose flesh has been clawed by a predator — is also worth noting, as the same root *d-r-s* underlies both concepts.

## Middle Category Birds

### One Siman Suffices

With this background established, the Gemara turns to the central problem: the *Baraita* describes only the two extremes. What about everything in between? Rabbi Chiya teaches: *of ha'ba b'siman echad tahor* — a bird that presents even one kosher *siman* is kosher. His reasoning: *lefi she'ein domeh la'nesher* — such a bird does not resemble the *nesher*. The *nesher* has no kosher *simanim* whatsoever — *nesher d'leis lei klal hu d'lo teichul* — and that is what makes it non-kosher. *Heicha d'is lei chad teichul* — if a bird has even one kosher *siman*, it may be eaten.

### Why List All Birds?

The Gemara immediately pushes back: but the *Baraita* describes the *torim* as having all four kosher *simanim*. Why not learn from the *torim*? Just as the *torim* are kosher by virtue of possessing all four positive characteristics — *mah torin d'ika kullu arba* — perhaps the rule is that only a bird with all four kosher *simanim* is kosher, and any single negative *siman* disqualifies it.

The Gemara's response is decisive: *im ken she'ar ofot teme'im d'kasav Rachmana lama li* — if a single non-kosher *siman* were sufficient to disqualify a bird, why would the Torah have needed to list the twenty-four non-kosher birds at all? If the rule were that simple — one non-kosher *siman* and you are out — then any bird with a non-kosher *siman* would obviously be prohibited, and there would be no need to enumerate them by name. The fact that the Torah provides a detailed list implies that the rule is not so straightforward, and that many birds with some non-kosher *simanim* remain kosher — which is why the list of exceptions was necessary.

This is a more radical claim than most people would intuitively assume. The lay assumption tends to be that all twenty-four non-kosher birds share the predatory characteristic of *dores* and perhaps other common features, and that the list simply identifies which birds these are. What emerges from the Gemara's analysis is something quite different: the *simanim* do not map cleanly onto the list. A bird could in principle be *dores* and still be kosher; a bird could be non-*dores* and still be non-kosher. The list functions as a set of exceptions to a general rule that needs to be derived from the *nesher* — and deriving that rule is the work of the extended Talmudic discussion that follows.

Tosafot's lengthy treatment of this passage makes two striking points explicit: first, that a predatory bird could be kosher if it is not on the list and has one positive *siman*; and second — and more startling — that the majority of the twenty-four non-kosher birds may not even be birds of prey at all. Tosafot infers from the Gemara's own distribution that twenty of the twenty-four actually possess three of the four kosher *simanim*, and argues that not being *dores* is one of those three positive characteristics they retain. This is admittedly difficult to square with the ornithological identifications typically proposed for those birds, but it is the logical consequence of the Gemara's framework, and Tosafot follows it through consistently.

## Inferring the Rule

### Two or Three?

The Gemara now works through the logical possibilities systematically. Assuming the Torah's list is not simply a brute enumeration but carries evidential weight for inferring a general rule, one might reason as follows. The majority of the listed birds — twenty of the twenty-four — apparently have only one non-kosher *siman* (equivalently, three kosher *simanim*). Why not infer from them that one non-kosher *siman* is sufficient to disqualify a bird? The Gemara's response: if that were true, the Torah would not have needed to mention the *orev* (raven), which has two non-kosher *simanim*. Once we know one suffices, why mention a bird with two? The presence of the *orev* on the list is superfluous under that hypothesis, and the Torah does not write superfluously.

Fine, says the Gemara — perhaps learn from the *orev* instead. Let the rule be two non-kosher *simanim*: *mah hatam trei lo af kol trei lo* — just as the *orev* with two cannot be eaten, so anything with two or more non-kosher *simanim* cannot be eaten. The Gemara objects: *im ken peres ve'ozniyah de'chasav Rachmana lama li* — if two sufficed, why would the Torah have mentioned the *peres* and *ozniyah*, which, according to the Gemara's tradition, have three non-kosher *simanim*? If the rule were two, those birds would be obviously non-kosher without needing to be named.

The argument advances one further step: let the rule be three, derived from the *peres* and *ozniyah*. The Gemara objects: *nesher de'chasav Rachmana lama li* — then why mention the *nesher*, which has all four non-kosher *simanim*? If three sufficed, the *nesher* would be redundant. The pattern is consistent: at every proposed threshold, the Torah's mention of birds with more non-kosher *simanim* than the threshold requires renders the proposed rule superfluous. The only threshold that avoids this problem is four — and at four, the *nesher* is the paradigm case, not a redundancy, because it is the only bird that fully embodies the rule.

A deeper difficulty underlies all of this, however. The Gemara was using the list of twenty-four birds as evidence from which to infer a general rule. But there is a well-established hermeneutical principle: *shenei kesuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — when the Torah provides two examples that yield the same rule, you cannot derive a general law from them, because the repetition itself signals that these are exceptional cases rather than paradigmatic ones. If the rule were, say, one non-kosher *siman*, then among twenty-four birds there would inevitably be multiple examples that each exhibit the same single non-kosher *siman* — and the presence of two examples of the same characteristic should label them as exceptions, not as evidence of a rule.

### Shnei Kesuvim

The hermeneutical principle of *shenei kesuvim habaim ke'echad* thus threatens to undermine the entire inference. If the Torah repeats any given characteristic among multiple birds — and with twenty-four birds and only four possible *simanim*, repetition is inevitable — then by this principle we should conclude that no general rule can be derived from any of them. The force of *binyan av*, paradigmatic inference, requires a singular example; repeated examples cancel each other's evidentiary weight.

The Gemara anticipates this objection and acknowledges its theoretical force, but then introduces a tradition that resolves it.

### Distribution Pattern

*Gmiri* — we have a received tradition. Of the twenty-four non-kosher birds, twenty of them each have three kosher *simanim* (one non-kosher *siman*). The *orev* has two kosher *simanim* (two non-kosher). The *peres* has one kosher *siman* (three non-kosher). The *ozniyah* also has one kosher *siman*, but crucially — *de'ika behai leka behai* — the *siman* it possesses is a different one from the *siman* possessed by the *peres*. The *peres* and the *ozniyah* each have three non-kosher *simanim*, but their three are not the same three.

This distribution matters enormously for the hermeneutical argument. The tradition establishes that among the twenty-four non-kosher birds, the *simanim* are distributed in such a way that certain combinations appear only once. In particular, if we label the four kosher *simanim* A, B, C, and D, then the twenty birds at the base level all possess A, B, and C (lacking D). The *orev* possesses A and B (lacking C and D). The *peres* possesses, say, A (lacking B, C, and D). The *ozniyah* possesses D — and D appears nowhere else on the list. This means there is no second example of a bird whose only kosher *siman* is D. The principle of *shenei kesuvim habaim ke'echad* is therefore not triggered for that combination: there is a single case, from which a rule could in principle be derived.

One might therefore have reasoned: let the *ozniyah* serve as a singular paradigm. Since it is the only bird with kosher *siman* D and no other kosher *siman*, infer the rule that any bird lacking A, B, and C (possessing all three of those as non-kosher *simanim*) is non-kosher. This is what the Torah forestalled by explicitly listing the *nesher*.

## Four = Non-Kosher

### Unique Combinations

The tradition establishes something quite precise: the distribution of *simanim* across the twenty-four non-kosher birds is not arbitrary. The various combinations are arranged so that for any given sub-threshold set of non-kosher *simanim*, it is either the case that there are multiple birds sharing that combination — in which case *shenei kesuvim* blocks inference — or there is a single unique example, but the Torah's inclusion of the *nesher* overrides any rule one might have derived from it. The *ozniyah*'s unique possession of kosher *siman* D (while lacking the other three) is the key: it represents the only potential singular paradigm from which one might have derived a rule of three non-kosher *simanim* being sufficient. But the Torah writes the *nesher* — which lacks all four kosher *simanim* — to preempt that inference.

### Nesher Proves Rule

The *nesher* is thus the definitive paradigm. *Nesher deleis lei klal hu delo teichol* — only a bird with no kosher *simanim* whatsoever, no A, no B, no C, no D, represents the general rule of non-kosher status. *Ha'ee de'is bei chad* — any bird that has even one kosher *siman* is, as a general rule, potentially kosher. *V'ela ta'ama de'chasav Rachmana nesher hava amina leilaf miperes ve'ozniyah* — had the Torah not written the *nesher*, we would have tried to derive the rule from the *peres* and *ozniyah*, concluding that three non-kosher *simanim* suffice. The Torah writes the *nesher* to preempt this, establishing that only the complete absence of kosher *simanim* defines the general rule.

## Three Bird Categories

### All Kosher Simanim

The Gemara's analysis yields a clean three-part structure. First: a bird that possesses all four kosher *simanim* — an *etzba yeteirah*, a *zefek*, a peelable gizzard, and not being *dores* — is definitively kosher. This is what we learn from the *torim*. Such a bird is certainly not among the twenty-four on the Torah's list, and one need not consult the list at all to establish its status. Four kosher *simanim* is a sufficient and self-contained determination of *kashrut*.

### Zero Kosher Simanim

Second: a bird that has none of the four kosher *simanim* — the profile of the *nesher* — is definitively non-kosher. This follows from the general rule that the Torah establishes through the *nesher* as paradigm. Here too, the list is irrelevant; the *simanim* alone determine the outcome.

### Mixed Simanim

Third: a bird that has some mix of kosher and non-kosher *simanim* — somewhere between zero and four on the kosher scale — occupies the ambiguous middle category. This is where the Torah's explicit list of twenty-four non-kosher birds becomes indispensable. The list consists entirely of birds in this middle category: birds that have at least one kosher *siman* but are nonetheless non-kosher because the Torah specifically names them. Conversely, any bird in this middle category that does not appear on the Torah's list is kosher. To know whether a particular bird with mixed *simanim* is or is not on the list, one requires either a reliable identification of the bird's name or a received tradition about it.

This structural conclusion has direct practical implications for the famous question of birds that have entered Jewish communities without a pre-existing tradition — such as the turkey — and whether possession of all four kosher *simanim* suffices to permit them. The Gemara's framework suggests that if all four kosher *simanim* are present, the bird is kosher regardless of whether its name appears in any received list. The list is only relevant for the middle category, where a mix of *simanim* leaves the determination open. A bird with a complete set of positive *simanim* falls outside the domain where the list operates at all.
