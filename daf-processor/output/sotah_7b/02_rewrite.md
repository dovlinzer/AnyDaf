# Sotah 7b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Warnings & Severity

### God's Name Erased

The Gemara opens with a discussion of the foundational concern underlying the *sotah* ritual: the erasure of God's name. The *mishnah* had established that the *beit din* impresses upon the woman the severity of what she is about to undergo — just as witnesses in capital cases are solemnly warned — because of the prohibition against erasing God's name unnecessarily. The relevant phrase is *assur lishmo hagadol shenichtav bikdushah shelo yimcha al hamayim*: the great name, written in holiness, must not be erased over the water without cause.

This concern is central to understanding the *sotah* ritual's inner logic. Part of the ritual's profound beauty, as reflected in *aggadata*, is that God is willing to have His name erased in order to restore *shalom bayit* between a husband and wife. Nevertheless, the prohibition against erasing God's name remains in force, and so the *beit din* works to prevent that erasure wherever possible.

### Paradox of Warnings

The Gemara then raises what appears to be a sharp contradiction. A *baraita* from the *Tosefta* expands on the notion of warning the woman and states: *kederech shema'amin aleha shelo tishteh kach ma'amin aleha shetishteh* — just as we warn her not to drink, so too we warn her to drink. This seems paradoxical. If the entire purpose of warning her is so that she will not go through with the ritual and thereby cause God's name to be erased, what could it possibly mean to warn her to drink?

The *baraita* itself elaborates: we tell her, "If it is clear to you that you are innocent, stand firm and drink, because the bitter water is like a dry medicine placed on raw flesh — if there is a wound, it dissolves into it and takes effect; if there is no wound, it does nothing." In other words, if she is innocent, the water will have no effect and it is actually beneficial for her to proceed with the ritual. The destruction of God's name will then serve a purpose.

### Before/After Erasure

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya kan kodem shenimchakah hamegillah kan le'achar shenimchakah hamegillah* — there is no difficulty, because these are two distinct stages. Before the *Megillat Sotah* has been erased into the water, the *beit din* tries to encourage the woman to admit guilt, so that the erasure of God's name is avoided altogether. Once the *megillah* has already been erased, however, we do not want that erasure to have been in vain, and so we now encourage her to drink and affirm her innocence, telling her the water will benefit rather than harm her.

The *mishnah* had stated that they say before her *devarim she'einam kedai lishoman* — things that are not suitable to be heard, yet are said to her anyway. The Gemara clarifies that these are *devarim shel aggadah*, aggadic narratives, stories that will move her emotionally. Why are they described as *einam kedai lishoman*? That becomes clear as the Gemara provides its examples.

### Judah & Reuben

The Gemara's *baraita* specifies the content of these narratives. We tell her stories from the earlier writings, from the lives of the founding figures of Israel, citing a verse from Iyov: *asher chakhamim yagidu velo kichadu me'avotam* — the wise ones have spoken of these things and have not hidden them from their ancestors. Two paradigmatic examples are then given.

*Yehudah hodah velo bosh* — Yehuda admitted his sin and was not embarrassed. *Mah hayah sofo? Nachal chayei ha'olam haba* — his end was that he inherited the life of the World to Come. Similarly, *Reuven hodeh velo bosh mah hayah sofo nachal chayei ha'olam haba* — Reuven admitted his sin and was not embarrassed, and he too inherited the World to Come.

Here the rabbis are making use of the *pshat* reading of narratives that, from the perspective of *Torah she-be-al peh*, are generally understood more carefully. We are told in *Megillah* that the story of Reuven is not translated into *Targum* during public reading, precisely so that people should not be misled by the *pshat* understanding — which sounds as though Reuven literally slept with his stepmother, while *Chazal* maintain the incident was something less severe. Yet here, the Gemara is explicitly drawing on the *pshat* reading of these stories as emotionally powerful examples of repentance and admission of guilt. This reflects a broader tension in rabbinic thought about how to read *Tanach* and how much to engage with the personalities of its protagonists as real, flawed human beings capable of sin and *teshuvah*.

The reason these narratives are *einam kedai lishoman* — not suitable to be heard — is precisely because they reflect badly on great *tzaddikim* or on figures who appear prominently in the *Torah*. Yet they are told to the *sotah* anyway, because inspiring her to confess and repent overrides that concern. The *Rema* cites this principle in one of his *teshuvot*, noting that the concern of embarrassing a *talmid chakham* can be overridden in the context of a process aimed at eliciting confession and restoring *shalom bayit*.

### Judah's Punishment

The Gemara then asks: we said both Yehuda and Reuven inherited *Olam Haba* — what further reward is there to discuss? *Ela mah secharan ba'olam hazeh* — what was their reward even in this world?

For Reuven, the *pasuk lahem levadam nitnah ha'aretz* hints at his being among the tribes who received the first inheritance on the east side of the *Yarden*, a share in *Eretz Yisrael* that was his own. For Yehuda, we know he admitted his sin because the *pasuk* says explicitly: *vayakker Yehuda vayomer tzadkah mimmeni* — Yehuda recognized and said, "She is more righteous than I." But how do we know that Reuven admitted his sin? The Gemara cites Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what does it mean when Moshe blesses Reuven at the end of *Sefer Devarim*, saying *yechi Reuven ve'al yamot*, "Reuven shall live and not die"? And immediately following is the blessing for Yehuda, introduced with the unusual word *vezot* — a connection that the Gemara draws upon to link the two.

The Gemara then presents a striking teaching: *kol otan shanim shehayu Yisrael bamidbar hayu atzmotav shel Yehuda mitgalgelin ba'aron* — throughout the forty years that the Jewish people were in the desert, the bones of Yehuda were rolling around in the ark, dislocated, until Moshe prayed on his behalf. The specific punishment was *eivarei deshapa* — his limbs were disjointed from their sockets — an image that Rashi connects to the *treifah* discussed in *Chullin* on daf 42b, *buka d'atma deshaph midukhtei*, the dislocated hip joint. Even in death, Yehuda was punished for his role in the episode with Tamar.

What prompted Moshe's prayer? *Amar lefanav Ribbono shel Olam mi garam leReuven shehodeh* — who was it that caused Reuven to do *teshuvah*? It was Yehuda. Yehuda's character, his willingness to admit wrongdoing publicly, inspired Reuven's own *teshuvah*. And so it was not even Yehuda's own *teshuvah* directly that earned him this redemption, but the fact that his example had inspired another person to repent. This is a striking teaching about the ripple effects of genuine admission of sin.

The Gemara then parses the blessing of Yehuda in *parashat v'zot habracha* as a series of remedies for the punishments Yehuda had suffered. *Shema Adonai kol Yehuda* — God heard Yehuda's cry, releasing him from his dislocated bones. *V'el amo tevi'enu* — bring him back to his people, meaning they finally allowed him into the *yeshivah* of heaven, the *metivta d'rakiya*, from which he had been excluded. *Yadav rav lo* — his hands were strengthened, corresponding to his now being permitted to participate in the *shakla v'tarya*, the back-and-forth of debate in the heavenly *beit midrash*, from which he had previously been barred even after gaining entry. And *v'ezer mizarav tihyeh* — let there be help against his enemies, meaning he could finally arrive at halakhic conclusions, *aliba d'hilkhata*, in his learning.

### Reuven's Public Apology

The Gemara then returns to Reuven and poses a question. *Bishlama biYehuda ashkechan de'odei* — we understand why Yehuda had to make a public admission of his sin: *ki heichi d'lo tisoraf Tamar*, so that Tamar would not be burned. Yehuda had no choice but to speak publicly to save her life. But why did Reuven need to make a public admission? *Lama lei de'odei* — there was seemingly no practical purpose; no one's life was at stake. Moreover, does not Rav Sheshet say that it is brazen to publicize one's own sins, *chatzip alai diprat chataei*?

The Gemara answers: *ki heichi d'lo lyechshadu achvohi* — Reuven was concerned that if he did not publicly acknowledge what he had done, his brothers would fall under suspicion. Something had become known, but without knowing who was responsible, people might have suspected any of his brothers. By making a public declaration, he ensured that no one else would be considered *chashud* for that sin.

This reflects the broader principle, noted especially in the *Rambam*, that for sins *bein adam lachavero*, there is an obligation to publicly acknowledge and apologize, because another person has been hurt and public rectification is part of the repair. For sins *bein adam lamakom*, by contrast, it is generally preferable to keep the matter private, between the person and God, without unnecessary public disclosure. Reuven's case was exceptional: the public nature of his admission was not self-serving, but was required specifically to protect his brothers from wrongful suspicion.

## Woman's Confession

### Ketubah Receipt

The Gemara then transitions to the case in which the woman admits to having committed adultery. *Im amra tmea ani* — if she says she is impure, *shovrim ktubatah* — we break her *ketubah*. The question is what exactly this means. *Shmaat mina kotvim shovar* — does it mean we write a receipt acknowledging that the *ketubah* has been settled, leaving the original document in place? Or does it mean something else?

Abaye proposes emending the reading: *tni mikrat*, we should read it as *koraat*, she tears up the *ketubah*. If you tear up the *shtar* itself, the instrument of debt is destroyed and cannot be claimed. Rava objects: *veha shoveret katani* — the *mishnah* uses the word *shoveret*, which implies breaking, not tearing. You cannot simply change the *mishnah*'s language.

### Ketubah Document

Rava therefore offers a different resolution: *bemakom she'ein kotvim ktubah askinan* — we are discussing a locale where it was not the practice to write a physical document for the *ketubah*. The *ketubah* obligation existed, but was conducted entirely orally. In such a case, there is no document to tear. The *mishnah*'s language of *shovrim* then means that we write a receipt — a *shovar* — to formally dissolve her claim.

This is a noteworthy window into the historical development of the *ketubah* as an institution. The *sugyot* across *Shas* present a complex picture: in some places the *ketubah* appears *d'Oraita*, in others *d'Rabbanan*. The *Rambam* at the beginning of *Hilchot Ishut* formulates it strikingly, stating that the *mitzvah* of *kiddushin* requires marriage *bekiddushin uvektubah* — the *ketubah* is part of the *mitzvah* of *kiddushin* itself.

Rashi at the bottom of the page notes two alternatives for how, in a place without a written *ketubah*, a woman would collect her entitlement. If divorced, *mishenitgarsha motzia gita vegova ba* — she brings the *get* itself as proof of her status and collects on the basis of that. If widowed, *u'chshenitalmena mevia edei mita vegova* — she must bring witnesses who testify to her husband's death before she can claim from his estate. This latter process is considerably more burdensome, requiring *edei mita* rather than simply producing a document. In a certain sense, one can trace in this the practical impetus for institutionalizing the written *ketubah*: the difficulty for a widow of having to produce testimony made it far simpler, ultimately, to give the woman a *shtar* she could hold in her hand.

## Eastern Gate Ritual

### Nikanor Gate

The *mishnah* continues: *de-in amarta tehorah ani ma'alin ota le-sha'ar hamizrach* — if she says she is innocent, they bring her up to the eastern gate, which is the *Sha'ar Nikanor*, the great gate at the boundary between the *Ezrat Nashim* and the main *Azarah* of the *Beit Hamikdash*. This gate had fifteen steps ascending to it and two smaller doors on either side. It was the central point for those who had no reason to enter the *Azarah* itself — *Yisraelim*, women, and others — who nevertheless came to the *Beit Hamikdash*.

The Gemara asks: *ma'alin ota* — what does it mean they bring her up? *Hatam ka'ima* — she was already there, since the earlier stages of the ritual had already taken place at the *Sha'ar Nikanor*. The Gemara clarifies: *ma'alin ota* refers to bringing her up from the *Beit Din Hagadol she-biYerushalayim*, the *Lishkat Hagazit*, which straddles the boundary between the *Azarah* and the *Ezrat Nashim* — half *kodesh* and half *chol* — but which is also adjacent to the *Sha'ar Nikanor*. The Gemara notes that it is precisely here, *lifnei Hashem*, that the woman must be made to drink, as the *pasuk* requires: *ve-he'emid ha-kohen et ha-ishah lifnei Hashem*.

The same location requirement applies to the *metzora*, whose purification process also requires the *kohen* to stand him at the entrance of the *Mishkan* or *Beit Hamikdash*: *ve-he'emid ha-kohen ha-mitaher*. The symbolic power of this is notable — the *metzora* is excluded even from walled cities, and then for his *taharah* he is brought back inside, to the very threshold of the sacred.

### Making Her Weary

The Gemara then states: *ma'akimim ota u-mekifim ota kedei le-yage'ah* — they make her walk all around the *Har Habayit* in order to tire her out. The purpose is to wear her down so that, if she is guilty, she will be more likely to admit it rather than proceed to drink the water. Conversely, if she is genuinely innocent, the fact that she is willing to drink even after this exhausting ordeal demonstrates the sincerity of her claim.

### Witnesses Comparison

A *tanna* draws an explicit comparison to the procedure in capital cases. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states: *Beit Din mesa'in et ha-edim mi-makom le-makom kedei she-titaref da'atan aleihem ve-yahzeru ba-hen* — the *beit din* makes witnesses in capital cases schlep from place to place in order to tire them out and induce them to reconsider whether they really wish to testify. The parallel is precise: in capital cases the witnesses may be putting an innocent person to death if they are lying, and we create disincentives for false testimony. At the same time, if they are telling the truth, they are absolutely obligated to testify — indeed, it is a great *mitzvah* to do so. The same dual dynamic applies to the *sotah*.

The Gemara also notes the broader question of who else must present themselves at the *Sha'ar Nikanor*. A *yoledet*, a woman who has given birth, must bring her *korban* there as well, by the rule that *ein korbano shel adam karev ela im ken omed al gabav* — a person's sacrifice cannot be offered unless that person is present. The Gemara raises *zavim* and *zavot* as additional examples of *mechusei kapparah*, and concludes that the *mishnah* simply listed one representative case — *tana chada mineihu nakat*.

### Two Sotahs Together

The *baraita* states: *ein mashkin shtei sotos ke'achas* — one may not administer the *sotah* water to two women simultaneously. The concern expressed by the first opinion is *kedei shelo yehei libah gas bechavertah* — one woman should not be emboldened by the presence of the other. If two women are undergoing the ritual together and both are stubborn, each one may be emboldened by the other's stubbornness into proceeding to drink the water even if guilty.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees not with the ruling but with its basis: *lo min hashem hu zeh ela amar kra osah levadah* — this is not derived from *sevara*, from logical reasoning, but directly from the *pasuk*, which says *osah levadah*, "her alone." This is a *gezeirat hakatuv*. This constitutes a good example of a *machloket* in which both *tanna'im* agree on the *halakhah* but differ on whether its source is logical or scriptural. Notably, the Gemara treats a compelling *sevara* as carrying *d'Oraita* force — *tana kamma*, identified as Rabbi Shimon, holds that we *darshin ta'ama dekra*, we interpret the *pasuk* in light of its reasoning, so the *gezeirat hakatuv* is bounded by the logic behind it.

The *nafka mina* between the two opinions concerns a woman who is *rotetet* — particularly fearful and visibly trembling. According to Rabbi Shimon, such a woman could theoretically be made to drink alongside another *sotah*, since there is no concern that she would be emboldened by the other woman's presence. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the *gezeirat hakatuv* is absolute regardless of the circumstances. The *Tosafot HaRosh* adds that one would still need to account for the case where one woman is fearful but the other is not — the latter could still embolden the former, requiring the prohibition to cover both scenarios.

The Gemara then introduces a broader principle: *ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos* — one may not bundle *mitzvot* together. Rashi explains that this is because doing so gives the appearance of treating *mitzvot* as a heavy burden one is trying to rush through, as though eager to discharge one's obligations and move on — *nire'eh kemo shehayah alav lemassoi u-memaher leforok masa'o*. *Tosafot* in *Moed Katan* offers a different and more positive framing: just as we do not combine one celebration with another — *ein me'arvin simcha besimcha* — so too each *mitzvah* deserves its own separate focus and its own *simcha shel mitzvah*. One of the *acharonim*, Rav Yosef Engel, notes an additional technical dimension: the principle of *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — one who is occupied with a *mitzvah* is exempt from another *mitzvah* — may itself create a structural barrier to performing two *mitzvot* simultaneously.

The *baraita* lists further applications of *ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos*: two *metzoraim* cannot undergo the purification ritual simultaneously; two *avadim Ivrim* cannot have their ears pierced at the same time; two *agalot arufot* cannot be performed together. Notably, all of these examples involve performing two instances of the *same mitzvah* rather than two different *mitzvot* in parallel — an important distinction, since no one would suggest that one cannot *daven minchah* while simultaneously shaking a *lulav* simply because they are two different *mitzvot*.

The *machloket* over whether the piercing of a slave's ear is a *mitzvah* at all — and therefore subject to *ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos* — is itself debated by later authorities. The *Chazon Ish* holds it is a *reshut*, entirely optional, and the master could refuse even if the slave requests to remain; the *Magen Avraham* holds that once the slave requests to be *nirtza*, it becomes obligatory for the master.

The Gemara ultimately resolves the apparent tension between the principle of *ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos* and the possibility of having two *sotahs* drink on the same day: *lo kashya kan bekohen echad kan beshnei kohanim* — there is no contradiction, since the prohibition applies to a single *kohen* who would be performing the ritual for two women. Two different *kohanim* could each administer the water to a different *sotah*, even at the same time, since neither *kohen* individually is bundling *mitzvot*.

## Exposure & Humiliation

### Uncovering Hair

The *baraita* cites the *pasuk* *u-farah et rosh ha-ishah* — the *kohen* unbraids or uncovers the woman's hair. The Gemara asks: from *roshah* alone, we know only about her head. From where do we derive that her body itself must also be exposed? The Gemara answers: the *pasuk* does not say merely *u-farah et roshah* but rather *u-farah et rosh ha-ishah* — the full word *ha-ishah*, "the woman," is included, extending the obligation to expose her body as well. And if so, what does the specific mention of *roshah* teach? *Melamed shehakohen soter et se'arah* — it teaches that the *kohen* undoes and loosens her hair specifically.

### Hirhurim Concern

Rabbi Yehuda raises a consideration: what if the woman is particularly attractive? This brings into focus a broader question: how concerned are we about *hirhurim*, inappropriate sexual thoughts, among those witnessing the *sotah* ritual? *L'meimra de'Rabbi Yehuda chaish l'hirhur* — it would appear that Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about *hirhurim* arising from the public exposure of the *sotah*. And *v'rabbanan lo chaishi* — the Rabbanan are less concerned.

### Execution Comparison

However, the Gemara notes that this apparent alignment seems to be precisely the reverse of what these same authorities hold elsewhere. In the context of execution by *skilah*, a *baraita* states that a man is stripped of all clothing except *perek echad milfanav* — one piece of cloth in front. A woman, by contrast, receives two pieces — *echad milfaneha v'echad milachareha* — because, as Rashi explains, *she'beit haboshet ne'erat mishnei tzadim*, a woman's private area is exposed from both sides and therefore requires coverage on both sides. But notably, even according to this *baraita*, a woman's entire torso would be uncovered during execution by *skilah* — indicating that the Rabbanan are not especially concerned about *hirhurim* in that context. Yet that is described as *divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda's position. Meanwhile, *v'chachamim omrim ha'ish niskal arom, v'ein ha'ishah niskalet aroma* — the Sages say a man is stoned completely naked, but a woman is not stoned naked at all; she must be clothed.

This presents a puzzling inversion: in the context of *sotah*, Rabbi Yehuda appears more protective of the woman from *hirhurim*, while in the context of *skilah* the Rabbanan are the ones who require that a woman not be exposed naked.

### Raba's Rationale

Rava resolves both the internal contradiction within Rabbi Yehuda's position and that within the Rabbanan's position simultaneously: *de-Rabbi Yehuda ad-Rabbi Yehuda lo kashya*, and *de-rabanan ad-rabanan nami lo kashya*. The concern in the *sotah* case is not *hirhurim* per se — it is *mishum venissaru kol hanashim*, because all women must witness the *sotah*'s fate and be warned by her example. The humiliation of the *sotah* serves a broader deterrent and educational purpose. In the case of execution, however, *ein lecha yisur gadol mizeh* — there is no humiliation greater than death itself, and there is no need to add further degradation.

Rava identifies a more specific concern driving the *sotah* case: *sh'ma totzi mibeit din zakai v'yitgaru bah pirchei kehunah* — perhaps she will leave the *sotah* ritual having been found innocent after drinking the water, and the young *kohanim* who are present in the *Azarah* (*metzuyin ba'azara*, as Rashi notes) will be stirred by inappropriate thoughts about her. If she has been exposed in public in the course of the ritual, these *kohanim* will have seen her, and that sight will provoke *hirhurim*.

But why not apply that concern to execution as well? *Hatam mistalka* — in the case of execution, she is gone; she has died. The *kohanim* will not see her again. *Gemiri d'ein yetzer hara sholet ela b'ma she'einav ro'ot* — it is a received principle that the *yetzer hara* only takes hold with respect to what a person's eyes have actually seen. Once she is dead, there is no ongoing exposure to provoke ongoing *hirhurim*. But in the *sotah* case, if she is found innocent, she returns to her husband and continues to live in the community, and the *kohanim* who saw her exposed will encounter her again.

### Bru Lo Mitah Yafah

The Gemara then raises the question of whether we should administer both punishments — expose her publicly *and* execute her — in the case of a guilty *sotah*. The answer draws on one of the most fundamental principles of rabbinic capital law: *ve'ahavta lere'acha kamocha beru lo mitah yafah* — from the verse "love your neighbor as yourself," the rabbis derive that one must provide an appropriate death, a *mitah yafah*, even to one who is executed. This *drashah* is invoked repeatedly throughout *Sanhedrin* in connection with all four *mitot beit din*, consistently functioning to pull back from what the *Torah* might appear to mandate explicitly, in order to preserve the physical integrity of the human body.

As Professor Lorberbaum has argued, the underlying principle is *tzelem Elokim* — the human being possesses a physical form that reflects the divine image, and capital punishment must therefore be carried out in a manner that does not deform or disfigure the body. *Beru lo mitah yafah* does not necessarily mean the most pleasant death, but the death that leaves the body most intact and unspoiled. By this standard, adding public humiliation on top of execution would violate the principle — we cannot require someone to be both publicly exposed and put to death.

### Pain vs. Humiliation

The Gemara then addresses a specific *machloket* regarding what *mitah yafah* means in practice, directly relevant to the question of execution: *mar savar bizyona adif lah tfei mitza'ara d'gufa* — one opinion holds that emotional humiliation is worse than physical pain, and therefore being executed naked (which accelerates death by removing cushioning from the stones but is more humiliating) is actually the *better* death — more pain, less humiliation. *U-mar savar tza'ara d'gufa adif lei tfei mibizyonei* — the other opinion holds that physical pain is worse, and therefore being clothed at execution (which cushions the impact but prolongs death) is preferable — more humiliation, but less physical suffering. Both opinions apply the principle of *mitah yafah*, but they disagree about which factor is the dominant consideration in determining the most appropriate death.

### Removing Jewelry

The *mishnah* states that if the woman was dressed beautifully — wearing white garments or fine jewelry — she is dressed in ugly or worn clothing for the ritual. The Gemara initially treats it as obvious (*pshita*) that her jewelry must be removed, since the entire purpose of the process is *nivvul*, to make her look degraded. But the Gemara raises a counterintuitive possibility: *behanei it lah bizyuna tfei* — perhaps leaving her jewelry on is actually *more* humiliating, because of the common proverb about a person who walks around naked but wearing shoes. That is, a figure who retains tokens of elegance and status while being otherwise degraded is in some respects more pitiable and embarrassing than one who is stripped completely. The Gemara concludes — *kamashma lan* — we do not apply this reasoning; the jewelry is removed.

### Egyptian Rope

After the tearing of the garment, the *kohen* brings a *chevel hamitzri*, an Egyptian rope, and ties it above her breasts to prevent her clothing from falling down entirely. The *Yerushalmi* offers two explanations for why specifically an Egyptian rope: either because her conduct recalled the licentiousness of Egypt, *k'ma'aseh eretz mitzrayim*, or alternatively the term is not *chevel mitzri* but *chevel matzui* — a common rope woven from palm fronds, rough and coarse, which was the standard material of the time.

The Gemara records a question posed by Rav Abba to Rav Huna: is the *chevel hamitzri* *me'akev* — is it an indispensable part of the *sotah* ritual, without which the process is invalid? Two possible explanations for its use are considered. If the reason is simply *shelo yishm'tu bgadeha me'aleha*, so that her clothes do not fall down, then even a small cord would suffice and the specific Egyptian rope would not be essential. But if the reason is rooted in *middah keneged middah*, corresponding to her having provocatively girded herself, then the specific rope carries substantive significance and might be *me'akev*.

The Gemara resolves the question by citing a more expanded version of the *mishnah*, a *baraita*, which makes explicit that the reason for the rope is purely functional — *k'dei shelo yishm'tu bgadeha me'aleha* — so that her garments do not fall. Since the reason is purely practical, the specific Egyptian rope is not *me'akev*.

*Tosafot* at the top of the following page raises an additional question prompted by the *Yerushalmi*: the rope is purchased from *mishirei halishkah*, public temple funds. This raises a parallel question about other instruments of capital punishment — the sword used for beheading, the cord used for *chenek* — whether those too are purchased with public funds, and how far the parallel between *sotah* and *mitat beit din* extends.

## Witnesses to Ritual

### Contradiction Resolved

The Gemara notes that the *mishnah* appears to contradict itself. On one hand it states *ve-chol ha-rotzeh lir'ot bah yera'eh* — whoever wishes to see may come and see, with no distinction between men and women. On the other hand, the *mishnah* also states *kol ha-nashim mutarot lir'otah* — all women are permitted to see her, implying that women yes, men no.

Abaye attempts to resolve the contradiction by suggesting *targemah anashim* — that even the first clause refers only to women. Rava objects: *ve-ha kol ha-rotzeh lir'ot bah yera'eh* — but the first clause clearly says "whoever wishes," which is inclusive language, not restricted to women.

### Women Obligated

Rava offers a different resolution: *kol ha-rotzeh lir'ot bah ro'eh lo shena gavrei ve-lo shena nashei* — whoever wishes to come and see, whether men or women, may come and see. Both are permitted. The second clause, however, conveys something more: *nashim chayavot lir'otah* — women are not merely permitted but *obligated* to attend.

### Women's Lesson

The obligation for women to witness the *sotah* ritual is derived from the *pasuk*: *ve-nissaru kol ha-nashim ve-lo ta'aseina ke-zimatchanah* — all women are to be chastened and warned so that they do not engage in the same *zimah*, the same prohibited sexual behavior, that the *sotah* may have committed. Women must be present specifically so that they will internalize the lesson and be deterred from similar conduct. Men are permitted to attend but are not obligated; women are not merely permitted but are required to come and witness.

## Middah Keneged Middah

### Measure for Measure

The final *mishnah* of the chapter introduces the principle of *middah keneged middah* — the manner in which a person conducts himself or herself is precisely the measure by which God responds. *Be-middah she-adam moded bah modedin lo.* The *sotah*'s punishment corresponds exactly to her sin. *Hi kishtah atzmah la-aveirah, ha-Makom nivvelah* — she adorned herself with jewelry for the sake of her sin; God therefore takes away her beauty. *Hi giltah et atzmah la-aveirah, ha-Makom gilah aleiha* — she revealed herself for her sin; God therefore reveals her.

### Order of Punishment

The *mishnah* specifies the sequence of the *sotah*'s physical punishment: *be-yarech hitchilah ba-aveirah* — she began her sin with her thigh, a euphemism for her genitalia. *Tcilah ve-achar kach ha-beten* — and then her womb, corresponding to the concern about pregnancy from the adulterous relationship. Therefore, *lokah ha-yarech tcilah ve-achar kach ha-beten* — the punishment begins with the thigh and only then affects the womb. *Ve-sha'ar kol ha-guf lo palat* — the rest of the body is entirely unaffected by the *sotah* water; only those specific parts of the body that were implicated in the sin are punished. The Gemara will elaborate on these *pesukim* and their implications in the discussion that continues on daf 8b.
