# Shabbat 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boneh vs. Makeh B'Patish

### Rav's Broad Boneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַאי חֲזֵי? חֲזֵי לְבִיזְרָא דְקַרָא. דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי מִשְׁכָּן, שֶׁכֵּן רָאוּי לְקֶלַח אֶחָד שֶׁל סַמָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: With regard to **any** small **amount** of building, **for what** use **is it suited?** The Gemara answers: It is **suited** for **a single pumpkin seed.** The **corresponding** situation **in the Tabernacle** was **as it is suitable** for planting **a single stalk of herbs** to make dyes.

We begin at the very bottom of 102b, in the middle of the dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning the respective scopes of *makeh b'patish* and *boneh*. Rav maintains a broad definition of *boneh* that extends even to *keilim* — for instance, fashioning a shovel from raw material. One might ask: if Rav includes even vessel-making within *boneh*, what remains for *makeh b'patish*? The answer is that *makeh b'patish* could apply, for example, when a vessel already exists and one performs some final engraving upon it. *Makeh b'patish* is commonly understood as the last act in the making of an object, even if the object was already functional in some sense beforehand. Rav's inclination, however, is to place acts into the *boneh* category rather than *makeh b'patish*. The consequence is that he recognizes the concept of *binyan b'keilim* — construction as applied to vessels — and the *rishonim* debate the precise scope of this: perhaps it applies only when something entirely new is produced, such as turning raw material into a finished tool, or perhaps only when the fit is especially tight or fixed. But at some level, Rav would apply *boneh* even to *keilim*.

### Shmuel's Makeh B'Patish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב — מֶיחְזֵי כְּמַאן דְּחַר חוֹרְתָא לְבִנְיָינָא, אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל — לָאו גְּמַר מְלָאכָה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** according **to Rav,** who said that one who makes a hole is liable due to the prohibited labor of building, here too, he should be liable because he **appears as one who is making a hole for** the purpose of **building. However,** according **to Shmuel,** drilling a hole is **not a completion of the labor.**

Shmuel, by contrast, holds a very narrow definition of *boneh* — he would not even classify chiseling a stone or making a hole in a chicken coop as *boneh* — and correspondingly broadens *makeh b'patish* to a much more expansive category.

### Rashi on Makeh B'Patish

Beyond the Rav–Shmuel debate about whether to apply *makeh b'patish* narrowly or broadly, the standard understanding of the term — as it becomes abstracted in the *gemara* — is that it denotes the final act of completing an object. But Rashi's comment in the *mishnah* formulates the concept somewhat differently. Rashi writes that *hamakeh b'patish* — which he notes is among the *avot melachot* — describes the moment when a stonehewer, having carved around a stone on all sides until it is nearly free of the mountain, delivers one great hammer blow, and the stone breaks loose and falls. He then states: *vezehu gmar melacha shel chotzvei ha'even* — this is the final act of the stonehewers' craft. And he concludes: anyone who completes a *melacha* on Shabbat has performed a *toladah* of *makeh b'patish*.

What is distinctive in Rashi is that he does not define *makeh b'patish* as the final act of completing the *object*. The stone still needs much further work — carvers and engravers may need to be called in. Rather, it is the final step of *this particular profession*, the stonehewer's craft. Rashi is emphasizing that it constitutes the completion of a stage in a process, not necessarily the finishing of the object itself.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot is not persuaded by Rashi's formulation. He objects in part because stonecutting was not a craft performed in the *mishkan*, which is the standard basis for identifying the *avot melachot*. Tosafot therefore cites the *Riva's* preferred definition: *makeh b'patish* is the *makosh acharon* — the final blow struck upon a vessel at the moment of the completion of the work. This is the more conventional formulation. That said, Rashi's version is worth noting because it coheres better with a broader sense present in the *mishnah* — that a *melacha* can consist not only of finishing an object, but of completing a stage within the larger process of making something. The *Rambam* reflects a similar principle: for instance, immersing garments in water is considered *melaben*, laundering, even though it is not the final stage of the process. The *Rambam* explains this because it is one of the recognized stages in the laundering process. Rashi's description of *makeh b'patish* resonates with that understanding — it is the final stage of a process, even when the object itself is not yet finished.

### Mesatet Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקּוֹדֵחַ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — חַיָּיב. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דְּבַזְעֵיהּ בְּרַמְצָא דְפַרְזְלָא וְשַׁבְקֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּויהּ, דְּהַיְינוּ גְּמַר מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who drills a hole of any size is liable. With what we are dealing here?** With a case **where one drilled a hole with an iron nail and left it inside** the surface in which he drilled the hole. **That is** considered **a completion of labor** because there is no intention to remove the nail from its hole.

We now pick up from the bottom of 102b, where Rav Natan Bar Oshaya poses a question to Rabbi Yochanan: *mesatet mishum mai chayav?* — the one who smooths or carves a stone, for what *melacha* is he liable? The *mishnah* lists *boneh*, *mesatet*, and *makeh b'patish* together, and one might assume *mesatet* falls under *boneh*. Rabbi Yochanan responded by gesturing with his hands, indicating *makeh b'patish*. The questioner pushes back: the *mishnah* lists *mesatet* and *makeh b'patish* as distinct categories, implying that *mesatet* belongs within *boneh* and *makeh b'patish* comes afterward. The *gemara*'s response is to collapse the distinction: perhaps *mesatet* is itself *makeh b'patish*.

This reflects the same tendency we saw in Shmuel — to reserve *boneh* for truly large-scale construction, and to classify acts like smoothing stone, making a shovel, or creating holes as *makeh b'patish* instead. One might wonder whether *makeh b'patish* requires the use of a *kli*, a tool. Normally, when the category is discussed in the abstract, no such requirement is mentioned — the definition centers on the final act of completing something. The example sometimes given is cutting the uncut pages of a book on Shabbat: this is cited by some as *makeh b'patish*, and it involves no tool. So ordinarily, the use of a *kli* is not presented as a defining feature.

### Hole with Peg Case

The *gemara* then introduces a proof text: *hakodeach kol shehu chayav* — one who makes even the smallest hole is liable. Rashi notes that *kodeach* specifically implies a hole intended to be filled, such as drilling into a wall to insert a nail or peg, as distinguished from *nokev*, which denotes making an opening meant to remain open. The question for the *gemara* is: under which *melacha* does this fall?

For Rav, this makes good sense as *boneh* — *mechzei ke'man de'charachar churtah l'vinyana*, it resembles someone making a hole in a structure, and such acts constitute part of the *boneh* category even without completing a full structure. For Shmuel, however, there is a problem: *makeh b'patish* in his view means *gmar melacha* — the completion of a *melacha*. Making a hole that is then going to be filled with a peg is not a completion; the process is ongoing. And since for Shmuel *boneh* requires a complete, significant act of construction, a simple hole in the wall fits neither category.

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying: *de'vazya b'ramtza de'farzla ve'shavka b'gavei* — you made the hole using an iron peg and left the peg inside it. In that case, the drilling and the insertion are accomplished in the same act, and the peg remaining in the wall constitutes the *gmar melacha* — the completed act. That is why one is liable under *makeh b'patish* according to Shmuel.

The broader point this entire discussion highlights is fundamental: there is a deep dispute about where the line runs between *boneh* and *makeh b'patish*. For Rav, *boneh* is a sweeping category encompassing all manner of acts that are part of the process of construction, including making holes in walls and creating vessels — acts of partial transformation that contribute to building. For Shmuel, *boneh* is reserved for completing a full and significant structure, so these intermediate acts fall to *makeh b'patish*, which then requires *gmar melacha*. This creates, as the *gemara* acknowledges, a gap in Shmuel's system: acts that are neither fully constructive nor complete — like drilling an unfilled hole — would be exempt according to Shmuel. This structural debate has practical implications for many real-world cases, since a great range of activities could fall differently depending on which position one follows.

## Mitkayem Principle

### Melacha Mitkayem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל. ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״ לְאֵתוּיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוּיֵי דְּחַק קְפִיזָא בְּקַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **this is the principle:** Anyone who performs a prohibited labor and his labor endures on Shabbat is liable. The Gemara asks: **What does** the phrase: **This is the principle,** come **to include?** The Gemara explains: It comes **to include** a case **where one carved out** a vessel with a capacity of **half a *kav* [*kefiza*]** into a piece of wood **in** which it was possible to chisel a vessel with a capacity of **a** whole ***kav*.** Since this labor endures on Shabbat and it can be used, it is considered a complete labor and he is liable.

With that extensive discussion behind us, the *mishnah* states the general rule: *zeh haklal* — anything that is a *melacha* and is *mitkayem b'Shabbos*.

### Rashi: Mitkayem Meaning

The ordinary sense of *mitkayem* is that the act is permanent rather than temporary. But Rashi's comment in the *mishnah* highlights a second dimension: *mitkayem* also refers to the question of minimum significance. It is not enough to have done something; one must have done something of sufficient substance that it could be considered, at least in some circumstances, complete. Rashi writes: *she'yesh mitkayemet k'yotzei bo v'ein mosif aleha* — there exist situations in which people leave something at this stage without adding to it. In other words, *mitkayem* connotes that one has reached a point that can be recognized as done, at least by some measure. It is another way of defining the threshold of minimum significance.

### Quart Containers Case

The *gemara* asks: *zeh haklal l'atuyei mai* — what new case does this general principle come to include? The answer: *l'atuyei d'chak kafiza b'kapya* — someone who carved containers of quart-volume in a mold. Even though some people might break down the interior walls and combine two quarts into a single gallon container, others do leave it at the quart size. Since some people would leave it in this state, this stage can be considered *mitkayem* — one could be regarded as done here, and therefore one has performed a complete act even if others would regard this as an intermediate stage.

## Banging Anvil Debate

### Anvil Striking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַמַּכֶּה בְּקוּרְנָס עַל הַסַּדָּן כּוּ׳. מַאי קָעָבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** Even **one who strikes an anvil with a sledgehammer** is liable. The Gemara wonders: **What has he done** by striking the anvil that would render him liable?

We now come to a remarkable position attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *hamakeh b'kurnas al hasadan* — one who strikes the hammer on the anvil. If Rashi's view of *makeh b'patish* allows for an act that completes a stage of a profession even without completing the object, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes considerably further. He is not speaking of any transformation of an object at all. He speaks of a craftsman performing a standard motion in the course of his work — striking the hammer on the anvil — without having changed anything. And yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that such a person is *chayav*.

### Challenge to Anvil

The *gemara* challenges this directly: *mai ka'avid?* — what has this person actually accomplished? Not only is nothing completed — nothing has been changed at all.

### Training Hand Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְאַמֵּן אֶת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was **Rabba and Rav Yosef who both said** in explanation: He is liable **because he trains his hand** for his work by striking the anvil.

Rabbah and Rav Yosef answer: *mipnei she'me'amen et yado* — because the craftsman is training his hand. By striking the anvil, he prepares and calibrates his hand for the more precise strikes that will follow — for example, hammering on a nail. The act prepares him for a productive *melacha* and is therefore part of the overall process.

### B'nei Rechava Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָשׁוּ בָּהּ בְּנֵי רַחֲבָה: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, חֲזָא אוּמָּנוּתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא וּגְמַר, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **The sons of** a man named **Raḥava** found **this** answer **difficult: If so, one who observed a craft** being performed **on Shabbat and learned** to perform that craft through observation, would **he also be liable?** Only one who performs an actual labor on Shabbat is liable.

The *bnei Rechava* object sharply: *ela me'ata chazu v'umnu b'shabta* — by this reasoning, if one goes and watches a craftsman work on Shabbat and thereby learns the skill, would that person also be liable? After all, learning by observation is also preparatory. The obvious answer is that there is a distinction: striking the anvil is a physical action that concretely improves one's hand's readiness, whereas merely watching is acquiring intellectual knowledge. Yet the fundamental problem remains — it is all preparatory, not itself a *melacha* in any conventional sense.

### Mishkan Gold Plates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שֶׁכֵּן מְרַדְּדֵי טַסֵּי מִשְׁכָּן עוֹשִׂין כֵּן. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמַּכֶּה בְּקוּרְנָס עַל הַסַּדָּן בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — חַיָּיב, שֶׁכֵּן מְרַדְּדֵי טַסֵּי מִשְׁכָּן עוֹשִׂין כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it was **Abaye and Rava who both said** in explanation: He is liable, **as those who flatten plates** of metal **for the Tabernacle do so.** They would strike the anvil with the sledgehammer in order to straighten the sledgehammer's handle, which became crooked. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even one who strikes an anvil with a sledgehammer during his labor is liable, as those who flatten plates** of metal **for the Tabernacle do so.**

Abaye and Rava resolve the matter differently. They say: *sheken meradedey tasey Mishkan osim ken* — this is precisely what was done in the *mishkan* when craftsmen thinned out the gold plates for the beams. They would alternate between striking the anvil and striking the gold, because striking the gold directly without first striking the anvil would have caused the gold to break. Striking the anvil, therefore, was not mere practice — it was a necessary step within the very act of producing the gold plates.

A *baraisa* confirms this: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that even striking the anvil *during the course of the work* renders one liable, because that is what those who thinned the gold plates in the *mishkan* would do.

This answer might be interpreted in one of two ways. Perhaps the *gemara* is saying: granted, in the abstract this accomplishes nothing, but since this specific act was performed in the *mishkan*, it carries the status of a *melacha* by virtue of that precedent alone — making it highly circumscribed and applicable only to the striking of the anvil in particular. Alternatively, and more satisfyingly, the point is that striking the anvil was integral to the process: without alternating it with the strikes on the gold, the gold would have been ruined. It was not abstract practice but a necessary component of the act of producing the gold. The difficulty that remains, however, is this: if the craftsman had struck the anvil and then stopped — without ever proceeding to strike the gold — would he have performed a *melacha*? He has not yet caused any change in any object. And it sounds as though Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say yes — that an act which is part of a process, even before any change has been effected in any material, can constitute a *melacha*. That is a striking *chiddush*.

We do not rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. But this entire discussion of *makeh b'patish* and *makeh al hasadan* is instructive precisely because it challenges the common assumption that *melacha* on Shabbat is fundamentally about creating something. Here we see that the scope of *melacha* can reach into stages of a process well before completion — and, according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, even before any change whatsoever has occurred.

## Choresh & Zoreya

### Choresh Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹרֵשׁ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, הַמְנַכֵּשׁ וְהַמְקַרְסֵם וְהַמְזָרֵד כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — חַיָּיב. הַמְלַקֵּט עֵצִים, אִם לְתַקֵּן — כׇּל שֶׁהֵן, אִם לְהֶיסֵּק — כְּדֵי לְבַשֵּׁל בֵּיצָה קַלָּה. הַמְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂבִים, אִם לְתַקֵּן — כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, אִם לִבְהֵמָה — כִּמְלֹא פִי הַגְּדִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who plows** is liable for plowing **any amount** of land on Shabbat. **One who weeds** and removes grass on Shabbat, **and one who removes dry branches and who prunes any amount is liable.** With regard to **one who gathers wood, if** he did so **to enhance** the tree or the land, he is liable for **any amount; if** he did so **for fuel,** he is liable for collecting a measure **equivalent** to that which is used **to cook an easily** cooked **egg.** With regard to **one who gathers grass, if** he did so **to enhance** the plants or the land, he is liable for **any amount; if** he did so to feed **an animal,** he is liable for collecting a measure **equivalent to a goat's mouthful.**

We now turn to an entirely different cluster of *melachot*. The *mishnah* states: *hachoresh kol shehu* — one who plows even the smallest amount is liable. The emphasis here is on the minimum *shiur* required for liability. *Charisha* — making a furrow in preparation for planting — even in the most minimal quantity renders one liable. Similarly, *hamenakesh* — weeding — *vehamikarsam* — removing dried branches — and *vehamizared* — Rashi explains this as removing moist branches — all of which encourage growth and cultivate conditions for planting, are likewise liable *kol shehu*. These latter three are *toladot* of *zoreya* rather than *choresh*, since they promote growth directly, though *choresh* itself also serves that broader purpose of enabling growth. The *mishnah* then rules that *kol shehu chayav* for all of these, in parallel with the earlier *melachot* of *boneh* and *makeh b'patish*.

### Wood Gathering

The *mishnah* turns to *hamelaket eitzim* — gathering sticks from the ground. Whether one is engaged in something resembling *kotzeir* (harvesting) or *me'ameir* (gathering), the minimum amount depends on the purpose. *Im l'takein kol shehein* — if the gathering is done in order to clear the ground for planting, it is classified as an act of *choresh*, and since *choresh* requires only *kol shehu*, even the smallest amount is sufficient. *Im l'haseik* — if the wood is gathered for use as fuel, it is more like *me'ameir*, an act of gathering for the sake of the gathered material, and then the minimum is a practical one: *kedei l'vashel beitza kalla* — enough to cook a light egg, meaning approximately a chicken's egg fried or scrambled to the size of a *grogeret*.

### Grass Gathering

The parallel case: *hamelaket asavin* — gathering grass. *Im l'takein kol shehu* — if done to improve the ground for planting, it is *kol shehu*. *Im l'beheima* — if done to feed one's animal, *k'mlo pi hagdi* — the minimum is the amount that fits in the mouth of a goat. If the grass is being cut rather than merely picked up, this could fall under *kotzeir*, in which case the minimum is set by the practical use rather than by *kol shehu*.

Tosafot makes a critical point here that applies throughout: when the *mishnah* says "if for this" or "if for that," it does not mean that the minimum is subjectively determined by what the person *intends* to do with the material. Rather, it is determined by what the material is *objectively fit for*. If it is fit for human consumption, the minimum is a *grogeret*. If it is only fit for animal consumption, it is *mlo pi hagdi*. If it cannot be used for any food and is only suitable as fuel, it is enough to cook an egg. This reading by Tosafot resolves apparent contradictions with other *gemarot* that set the *shiur* objectively.

### Ground Improvement Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ לָא לְיַפּוֹת אֶת הַקַּרְקַע נִינְהוּ? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בַּאֲגַם שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Aren't all these** done **to enhance the land?** Each stalk that a person uproots enhances the land. It was **Rabba and Rav Yosef who both said** in explanation: **They taught** this *baraita* **with regard to swampland,** where grass is not uprooted to enhance the land.

The *gemara* now raises a profound question with sweeping implications for the laws of Shabbat: *koolhoo lo leyapooyei et hakarka ninhu?* — do not all of these acts make the ground better? A person cuts wood intending to use it as fuel. But in doing so, he has pruned the tree and thereby encouraged its growth. Even if the amount is insufficient to meet the threshold for *kotzeir* when measured by use as firewood, he has still performed an act of *zoreya* or *choresh* by improving the conditions for growth — and those *melachot* require only *kol shehu*. Why is he not liable on that basis?

### Swamp Resolution

Rabbah and Rav Yosef answer: *be'agam shano* — the *mishnah* is speaking of a case in a swamp. In a swamp, no one cares what grows, nothing is being cultivated, and therefore clearing the area does not constitute an act of preparing the ground for growth. It is not *choresh* because the ground is not being readied for planting.

### Friend's Field Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשָׂדֶה דְּלָאו אֲגַם, וּכְגוֹן דְּלָא קָמִיכַּוֵּין. וְהָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, בִּ״פְסִיק רֵישֵׁיהּ וְלָא יְמוּת״! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָעָבֵיד בְּאַרְעָא דְחַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even if you say** that the *baraita* is referring to **a field that is not a swampland,** it can be referring to **a case where one did not intend** to enhance the land. The Gemara asks: **However,** is it not **Abaye and Rava who both say** that **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that one is liable only for performing an intentional action, **concedes** that one is liable in a case of **cut off its head, will it not die?** In any case where the outcome is inevitable, as in this case where the land will be enhanced, one's lack of intention does not exempt him. The Gemara answers: Abaye's statement **was** only **necessary** in a case **where one did so on another's land.** Since he did not intend for that outcome to eventuate and he derives no benefit from enhancing the land, he is not liable in that case.

Abaye, however, does not accept this narrow resolution. He maintains that even in an ordinary field — not a swamp — the concern applies: when one pulls grass with the intention of feeding a goat, one is in practice also improving the ground. Abaye then introduces the principle of *psik reisha*: even if one does not intend a result, if that result is certain to occur, one cannot claim exemption on grounds of lack of intent. The *gemara* responds: *lo tzericha deka'avid be'ara dechavrei* — the case in the *mishnah* is where one performs the act in a friend's field. In that circumstance, Rashi notes, *lo ikhpat lei leyapooyei* — one has no concern about improving the friend's field, it is simply not a matter of personal interest.

### Psik Reisha Concept

This brings us to the crucial category of *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei* or *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei*. The distinction between the two formulations: *lo nicha lei* means one actively does not want the result; *lo ikhpat lei* means the result is simply not a matter of concern either way. Here the case is the latter — not active objection, but complete indifference. And the *halacha* treats them identically.

### Psik Reisha Detail

The implication is enormous: although it is certain that the act will improve the ground (*psik reisha* — as definite as cutting off a head, which will certainly cause death), since the person has no interest in that result, we treat the act differently.

### Psik Reisha Status

The ruling that emerges — and this is one of the most consequential principles in all of *hilchot Shabbat* — is that *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei* is only rabbinically forbidden, not a biblical violation. This is a strikingly broad conclusion, and it is worth noting that it is never stated explicitly anywhere in the *gemara*. The entire category, upon which a very large portion of the leniencies permitted *besha'at hatzorech* on Shabbat depend, is ultimately derived from and hinges upon this single *gemara* and the answer *be'ara dechavrei*. Yes — it will definitely happen — but since one has no interest in it, it is only a *derabbanan*.

### Ritva's Challenge

This conclusion, however, is not universally accepted. The *Ritva* mounts a sharp challenge in the form of a *reductio ad absurdum*: suppose one wishes to give one's cattle exercise, and to do so, one attaches a plow to them and plows through a neighbor's field — thereby training them while accomplishing the plowing as a *psik reisha* in which one has no personal interest. Would one truly be exempt? He finds this completely untenable, and argues that the *gemara* here cannot be the basis for a general principle. Rather, the *Ritva* maintains that the *gemara* invokes the indifference of the person specifically because this is a case of *kol shehu* — an act so trivial that when combined with the person's total lack of concern, the act loses its minimal significance entirely. In his view, *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei* as a freestanding halachic category does not exist: generally, if it is a *psik reisha*, one is liable regardless of whether one cared about the result.

The *Ritva*'s position has been rejected in practice, but it represents a serious jurisprudential objection.

### Reconciliation

The point at which the argument works for the *Ritva*, even if it fails as a general principle, is precisely where the result is *kol shehu* — something so minimal that combined with the person's indifference, the act is truly negligible. That combination of triviality and disinterest, he allows, can support leniency.

### Melacha She'Eino

This raises a natural question about the relationship between *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei* and the category of *melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufa* — a *melacha* performed not for the object itself but for some incidental benefit. The classic example is digging a hole to obtain the dirt: one wants the dirt, and the hole is an incidental result that one does not care about. That is also a case of definite result (*psik reisha*) in which one has no interest in the primary *melacha* outcome. The question is whether these two categories are really the same thing.

### Tosafot Identity

Tosafot here, in what is one of the briefest yet most consequential notes in the tractate, states plainly: *peirush de-havei melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufa* — they are the same category. The *Rambam*, by contrast, rules that *melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufa* renders one liable *me'd'Oraisa*, while *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei* is only *derabbanan*, thereby treating them as distinct. The question of whether they are identical or separate remains a matter of significant dispute among the *rishonim*, but the practical upshot of the *gemara* here — whatever theoretical framework one applies — is that at minimum, *psik reisha d'lo ikhpat lei* is not a biblical violation.

## Writing Two Letters

### Two Letters Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת, בֵּין בִּימִינוֹ בֵּין בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, בֵּין מִשֵּׁם אֶחָד בֵּין מִשְּׁנֵי שֵׁמוֹת בֵּין מִשְׁתֵּי סַמָּנִיּוֹת, בְּכׇל לָשׁוֹן — חַיָּיב. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא חִיְּיבוּ שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם רוֹשֵׁם, שֶׁכָּךְ כּוֹתְבִין עַל קַרְשֵׁי הַמִּשְׁכָּן לֵידַע אֵיזוֹ בֶּן זוּגוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מָצִינוּ שֵׁם קָטָן מִשֵּׁם גָּדוֹל — ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן וּמִשְּׁמוּאֵל, ״נֹחַ״ מִנָּחוֹר, ״דָּן״ מִדָּנִיאֵל, ״גָּד״ מִגַּדִּיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who writes two letters** on Shabbat, **whether** he did so **with his right** hand **or his left, whether** they were **the same letter or two** different **letters, whether** he did so **using two** different types of **ink, in any language, he is liable. Rabbi Yosei said: One is deemed liable** for writing **two letters only due to marking, as they** would **write** symbols **on** adjacent **beams of the Tabernacle to know which** beam was another beam's **counterpart. Rabbi Yehuda said: We found** that one is liable for writing even if he did not complete what he was writing, so that he wrote **a small name** that constituted part **of a longer name,** e.g., **Shem [*shin mem*] from** the name **Shimon** or **from Shmuel; Noaḥ [*nun ḥet*] from Naḥor; Dan [*dalet nun*] from Daniel; Gad [*gimmel dalet*] from Gaddiel.** In all of these cases, the first two letters of the longer name constitute the shorter name.

The *mishnah* now turns to the *melacha* of *kosev* — writing. *Hakosev shetei osiyos* — one who writes two letters is liable: *bein b'yimino bein b'smolo* — whether with the right hand or the left; *bein mishem echad bein mishtei shemos* — whether it is one repeated letter or two different letters; *bein mishtei samaniyos* — whether using two different types of ink; *bechol lashon* — in any language and any script. The rule is two letters, and the other variables are immaterial.

### Roshem Opinion

Rabbi Yosi dissents: *lo chivu shetei osiyos ella mishum roshem* — the reason two letters incurs liability is not because one has written in a literary sense but because one has made a *roshem*, a mark. He is referring to the practice in the *mishkan* of marking the beams so that each beam could be matched to its corresponding partner — slot A with peg A, and so forth. For Rabbi Yosi, the underlying *melacha* is not *kosev* — writing proper script — but *roshem* — making identifying marks. The significance of this distinction: even a symbol that is not a letter at all, so long as it serves a marking function, would be sufficient, and there is no need for it to be proper writing. *Shekach kosvin al karshei HaMishkan lada'at eizo hi ben zugah* — because that is how they would mark the beams of the *mishkan*, to know which matched which.

### Partial Name Examples

Rabbi Yehuda contributes a further principle: *matzinu shem katan mishem gadol* — we have found that a small name can be taken from within a large name. He gives four examples: *shem* from *Shimon* or *Shmuel*, *Noach* from *Nachor*, *Dan* from *Daniel*, *Gad* from *Gadiel*. The implication is that even if one was intending to write a longer name and stopped after two letters, one is liable — provided those two letters themselves constitute a recognizable word or name. Incompleteness of intent does not exempt the writer, as long as what was actually written is itself meaningful. Conversely, two random letters that do not form any word would not suffice.

### Right-Left Hand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא אַיָּמִין לִיחַיַּיב מִשּׁוּם דְּדֶרֶךְ כְּתִיבָה בְּכָךְ, אֶלָּא אַשְּׂמֹאל אַמַּאי? הָא אֵין דֶּרֶךְ כְּתִיבָה בְּכָךְ! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בְּאִטֵּר יָד שָׁנוּ. וְתֶהֱוֵי שְׂמֹאל דִּידֵיהּ כְּיָמִין דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, וְאַשְּׂמֹאל לִיחַיַּיב, אַיָּמִין לָא לִיחַיַּיב! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשׁוֹלֵט בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the beginning of the mishna: **Granted, for** writing with **the right** hand **let one be liable, as that is the** typical **manner of writing. However, for** writing with **the left** hand, **why** is one liable? **That is not the** typical **manner of writing. Rabbi Yirmeya said:** When the mishna taught that one who writes with his left hand is liable, **they taught** it **with regard to** one who is **left-handed.** The Gemara asks: **And** if so, **let his left** hand have the same legal status **as everyone's right** hand; for writing **with** his **left** hand, **let him be liable,** for writing **with** his **right** hand, **let him not be liable. Rather, Abaye said:** This mishna refers to an **ambidextrous** person, who is liable for writing with either hand.

The *gemara* then focuses on the right-hand/left-hand provision of the *mishnah*. Writing with the right hand is clearly *derech ketiva* — the normal manner of writing. But why should writing with the left hand render one liable, given that *ein derech ketiva bechach* — it is not the normal manner of writing?

### Lefty Exception

Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *d'chulei alma b'itir yad shana* — the entire *mishnah* is discussing a left-handed person, for whom the left hand is the dominant writing hand, equivalent to the right hand of most people, and the right hand would be the weak hand. Under this reading, the *mishnah* is entirely about a lefty: liable for the left hand, exempt for the right.

### Ambidextrous Case

Abaye, however, proposes a different reading: *bisholeit bishtei yadav* — the *mishnah* is speaking of an ambidextrous person, one who writes equally well with both hands. This radically narrows the scope of the *mishnah*, but it reflects the *gemara*'s general insistence that acts performed in an unusual manner — *k'lachar yad*, with a *shinui* — do not incur biblical liability.

### Marking vs Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יַעֲקֹב בְּרֵהּ דְּבַת יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דַּאֲמַר לֹא חִיְּיבוּ שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם רוֹשֵׁם. וְהָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, רֵישָׁא לָאו רַבִּי יוֹסֵי! כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ya'akov, son of the daughter of Ya'akov, said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said: One is deemed liable** for writing **two letters only due to marking.** As such, one is liable for writing a letter even if he writes it imprecisely with his left hand. The Gemara asks: **From the fact that the latter clause** of the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, the first clause** of the mishna **is not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** The Gemara answers: That is not necessarily the case. **The entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and the attribution of his second statement was for emphasis alone.

Rav Yaakov son of the daughter of Yaakov offers yet another reading: this is the opinion of Rabbi Yosi, who holds that the *melacha* is fundamentally *roshem* rather than *kosev*. Since we are talking about making marks rather than writing proper script, one does not need to use one's strong hand — simple marks can be made with the weaker hand without that being considered an abnormal manner of performing the act. This is why Rabbi Yosi's opinion can encompass both hands naturally: if it is marking and not writing, the standard of what constitutes *derech* is different. The *gemara* then asks whether the end of the *mishnah* representing Rabbi Yosi's view means the beginning is not Rabbi Yosi, and resolves that the entire *mishnah* can be Rabbi Yosi — he first states the general rule and subsequently clarifies the underlying rationale.

## Baraita Analysis

### Baraita Achat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְעָשָׂה״ — אַחַת. יָכוֹל עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב כׇּל הַשֵּׁם, וְעַד שֶׁיֶּאֱרוֹג כׇּל הַבֶּגֶד, וְעַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה כׇּל הַנָּפָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵאַחַת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: "When a leader sinned, **and he** unwittingly **performed one** of any of the commandments which the Lord his God commanded not to do, and is guilty" (Leviticus 4:22)? The Sages taught: I **might** have thought that one is not guilty until he performs a complete labor, e.g., **until he writes the entire name** that he intended to write, **or until he weaves the entire garment, or until he crafts the entire sieve** made from the reeds of the warp and the woof; therefore, **the verse states:** "A soul who sins unintentionally in any of the Lord's commandments which one shall not perform, and did an action **from one** of these" (Leviticus 4:2).

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraisa* that examines the question of minimum amount in writing far more systematically than the *mishnah* did. The derivation begins from the verse *ve'asa me'achat mehena* — "and he did from one of them." The verse could have said simply *ve'asa achat* — "and he did one." Why *me'achat mehena*?

The *midrash halacha* first proposes that *achat* — one — might mean a complete *melacha* in its entirety. *Yachol ad sheyichtov kol hashem* — perhaps one is only liable if one wrote the entire name. *Ve'ad sheyereg kol habeged* — or wove the entire garment. *Ve'ad sheya'aseh kol hanafa* — or completed the entire basket.

### Me'achat Deduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי ״מֵאַחַת״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ לֹא כָּתַב אֶלָּא אוֹת אַחַת, וְלֹא אָרַג אֶלָּא חוּט אֶחָד, וְלֹא עָשָׂה אֶלָּא בַּיִת אֶחָד בַּנָּפָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַחַת״. הָא כֵּיצַד, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב שֵׁם קָטָן מִשֵּׁם גָּדוֹל — ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן וּמִשְּׁמוּאֵל, ״נֹחַ״ מִנָּחוֹר, ״דָּן״ מִדָּנִיאֵל, ״גָּד״ מִגַּדִּיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The emphasis on the phrase "from one" teaches that in order for one to be liable, it is sufficient that he perform only part of the prohibited labor. However, **if** that is derived from the use of the phrase **"from one,"** I **might** have thought that one is liable **even if he wrote only a single letter, or** even if **he wove only a single thread, or** even if **he crafted only a single eye of the sieve,** i.e., arranging the reeds to create a warp, and then interweaving a single reed as a woof; therefore, **the verse states "one."** Thus it must be explained that **one is liable only if he writes a small name** that constitutes part **of a longer name,** e.g., **Shem from** the name **Shimon** or **from Shmuel, Noaḥ from Naḥor, Dan from Daniel, Gad from Gaddiel.**

The *mem* prefix of *me'achat* — "from one" — teaches that even partial performance can create liability. One need not complete the entire object one was working toward. But then the *baraisa* asks whether this goes so far as to mean even one letter, one thread, or one unit of a wicker basket. On that the word *achat* — "one" — insists on a minimum of completeness.

### Name Hierarchy

The resolution: *eino chayav ad sheyichtov shem katan mishem gadol* — one is liable only when one has written a small name that is contained within a larger name. The *baraisa* gives the same examples as the *mishnah*: *shem* from *Shimon* or *Shmuel*, *Noach* from *Nachor*, *Dan* from *Daniel*, *Gad* from *Gadiel*. This is the minimum of significance: two letters that constitute a meaningful word or name, even if one's ultimate intention was a longer word.

### Same Letters Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא כָּתַב אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת וְהֵן שֵׁם אֶחָד — חַיָּיב, כְּגוֹן: ״שֵׁשׁ״ ״תֵּת״, ״רָר״, ״גַּג״, ״חָח״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: One is liable even** if **he wrote only two letters that are one type** of letter, **e.g., *shesh* [*shin shin*], *tet* [*tav tav*], *rar* [*reish reish*], *gag* [*gimmel gimmel*], *ḥaḥ* [*ḥet ḥet*].**

Rabbi Yehuda then adds: *afilu katav ela shtei otiyot shehen shem echad chayav* — even if one wrote two of the same letter and they constitute a word, one is liable. His examples: *sas*, *tat*, *rar*, *gag*, *chach* — all real words formed by doubling a letter. This is Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rabban Gamliel. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *mishnah* examples (all different letters) and this ruling by explaining that the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's own personal view, while the *baraisa* records his teaching in the name of his *rebbe*, Rabban Gamliel, who was more lenient — or rather, more inclusive — in accepting doubled letters. The question of why doubled letters should not be sufficient once they form a recognizable word is itself somewhat puzzling, but this is in fact the debate between Rabbi Yehuda's own position and that of Rabban Gamliel.

### Symbol Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי מִשּׁוּם כּוֹתֵב הוּא חַיָּיב?! וַהֲלֹא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם רוֹשֵׁם, שֶׁכֵּן רוֹשְׁמִין עַל קַרְשֵׁי הַמִּשְׁכָּן לֵידַע אֵיזוֹ הִיא בֶּן זוּגוֹ. לְפִיכָךְ, שָׂרַט שְׂרִיטָה אַחַת עַל שְׁנֵי נְסָרִין, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי שְׂרִיטוֹת עַל נֶסֶר אֶחָד — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: And is one liable due to** the labor of **writing? Isn't one liable only due to** the prohibition of **marking, as they** would **write** symbols **on** adjacent **beams of the Tabernacle to know which** beam was another beam's **counterpart? Therefore, one who made a single scratch on two boards, or two scratches on a single board, is liable.**

Rabbi Yosi returns to his position: *v'chi mishum kotev hu chayav? ela mishum roshem* — why are we troubling ourselves over same letters, different letters, words, incomplete words? The *melacha* is not *kosev* at all; it is *roshem* — marking. Therefore, what matters is whether one made marks that serve a function. *Sarat srita achat al shenei nesarim o shtei sritot al neser echad* — if one drew a single line across two beams (by which the alignment of one to the other can be determined) or two separate lines on a single beam — even if these are not letters, even if they carry no linguistic meaning, one is liable. The *melacha* is entirely about functional identification marks, not about script.

### Mitkayem Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת״, יָכוֹל עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב אֶת כָּל הַשֵּׁם, עַד שֶׁיֶּאֱרוֹג כׇּל הַבֶּגֶד, עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה אֶת כׇּל הַנָּפָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵאַחַת״. אִי ״מֵאַחַת״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ לֹא כָּתַב אֶלָּא אוֹת אַחַת, וַאֲפִילּוּ לֹא אָרַג אֶלָּא חוּט אֶחָד, וַאֲפִילּוּ לֹא עָשָׂה אֶלָּא בַּיִת אֶחָד בַּנָּפָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַחַת״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה מְלָאכָה שֶׁכַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ מִתְקַיֶּימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse states, "When a leader sinned, **and he** unwittingly **performed one** of any of the commandments which the Lord his God commanded not to do, and is guilty" (Leviticus 4:22), and from the word one, I **might** have thought that one is not guilty until he performs a complete labor, e.g., **until he writes the entire name** that he intended to write, or **until he weaves the entire garment,** or **until he crafts the entire sieve** made from the reeds of the warp and the woof; therefore, **the verse states "from one."** However, **if** that is derived from the use of the phrase **"from one,"** I **might** have thought that one is liable **even if he wrote only a single letter, or** even if **he wove only a single thread, or** even if **he crafted only a single eye of the sieve.** Therefore, **the verse states "one." But how** can we reconcile the two phrases? **One is only liable for performing a labor that** is **of the type** that **endures.** In that case it is considered a complete labor.

Rabbi Shimon's position draws again on the verse *ve'asa achat* and parallels the structure of the earlier *baraisa* — arguing that *achat* implies a whole and *me'achat* implies a partial — but his conclusion is different. He says: *eino chayav ad sheya'aseh melacha shekayotzei bah mitkayemet* — one is not liable unless one performed a *melacha* similar to which, in its completed state, lasts. As established earlier, *mitkayemet* here does not refer to whether the ink is permanent versus disappearing — that is a separate dimension of *mitkayem*. Rather, it refers to completeness: one must have done something that could in some circumstances be regarded as done, something that has sufficient internal completeness that one might leave it in that state.

The *gemara* now examines how Rabbi Shimon's view relates to the others. Is he simply restating what was already said? Perhaps the distinction is that Rabbi Shimon would go even further than the other opinions: he might hold that even two letters that do not form a recognizable word could be sufficient — for instance, two *alefin* taken from within the word *ezrach* — *alef alef de'azrach* — which on their own are meaningless as letters in any standard word but are used in a *kamea*. The *tanna kamma* would hold that two identical letters not forming a word are insufficient; Rabbi Shimon would hold that since they appear in some context — even an amulet — they have a minimum significance and one is liable.

### Shimon Consistency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַקּוֹדֵחַ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, הַמְגָרֵר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, הַמְעַבֵּד כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, הַצָּר בִּכְלִי צוּרָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּקְדַּח אֶת כּוּלּוֹ, עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹר אֶת כּוּלּוֹ, עַד שֶׁיְּעַבֵּד אֶת כּוּלּוֹ, עַד שֶׁיָּצוּר כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who drills** a hole of **any size** on Shabbat **is liable, one who scrapes** and smooths posts or parchments in **any amount** is liable, **one who tans any amount** of an animal hide is liable, **one who draws any size form on a vessel** is liable? **Rabbi Shimon says:** One is liable **only if he drills the entire** hole that he intended to drill, or if **he scrapes the entire** post or parchment that he intended to scrape, or if **he tans the entire hide** that he intended to tan, or if **he draws the entire form** that he intended to draw. Clearly, Rabbi Shimon's opinion is the lenient one.

But the *gemara* then raises a serious challenge to Rabbi Shimon's consistency. Elsewhere we find that Rabbi Shimon holds a stringent position on minimum amounts in other *melachot*: *ad sheyikdach et kulo*, *ad sheyigrar et kulo*, *ad sheye'abed et kulo*, *ad sheyatzur et kulo* — regarding drilling, scraping, tanning leather, and decorating a vessel, he requires that one complete the *entire* object or process one is working on. The position in those *melachot* would imply that partial writing — two letters out of a longer intended name — should also be insufficient. And yet the *baraisa* has Rabbi Shimon saying that even partial — *me'achat* — creates liability in writing.

### Tosafot's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָא אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן, עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב אֶת הַשֵּׁם כּוּלּוֹ. וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת״, יָכוֹל עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב אֶת הַשֵּׁם כּוּלּוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵאַחַת״! תָּרֵיץ וְאֵימָא הָכִי: יָכוֹל עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב אֶת הַפָּסוּק כּוּלּוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵאַחַת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Shimon comes to teach us this:** It is considered writing that endures only **if he writes the entire name.** The Gemara asks: **And how can you say this? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** From the phrase **"and he performed one"** I **might** have thought that one is liable **only if he writes the entire name;** therefore, **the verse states "from one."** Apparently, he does not require that the entire word be written in order to be liable. The Gemara answers: **Resolve** the contradiction between these statements **and say this:** I **might** have thought that one is liable only **if he writes the entire verse** that he intended to write; therefore, **the verse states "from one."** One is liable for writing less than that. However, one is certainly not liable for writing less than a complete word.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by reinterpreting Rabbi Shimon's position in the *baraisa*: perhaps his *hava amina* — the proposition he was rejecting — was not that you need the entire word, but that you need the entire *sentence* or *passage* one intended to write. The *mem* of *me'achat* then teaches that one complete word suffices, even if one was intending to write more. This reinterpretation resolves the tension — Rabbi Shimon requires completeness, but the unit of completeness in writing is a word, not a sentence.

Tosafot draws a conceptual line to explain why the standard differs between working leather and writing: when one is creating something out of nothing — weaving a garment, writing on a blank surface — performing part of the intended work can still have independent significance, since one is producing something new. But when one is working on a pre-existing object — tanning an existing piece of leather, engraving an existing vessel — what one does has less inherent significance on its own, because the object already exists, and therefore Rabbi Shimon demands that one complete the entire task. This is a fundamental distinction: *making new objects* versus *working on pre-existing items*. By that logic, for writing — which is producing something on a blank surface — even a complete word (not the entire intended text) meets Rabbi Shimon's standard of *mitkayemet*.

### New vs Existing

To restate Tosafot's principle: for acts that involve transforming or working a pre-existing thing, Rabbi Shimon requires completion of the whole. For acts that create something new — writing, weaving — a minimum unit of significance suffices.

### Korban Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת״ ״וְעָשָׂה הֵנָּה״, פְּעָמִים שֶׁחַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, וּפְעָמִים שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says** that the verse states: "And did an action from one of these" (Leviticus 4:2). This unusual expression indicates repetition: **And he did one, and he did these.** From here it is derived that **at times one is liable** to bring **one** sin-offering **for them all, and at times one is liable** to bring several offerings, one **for each and every one.**

Rabbi Yosi's contribution to this *baraisa* takes an entirely different direction. He emphasizes the words *ve'asa achat ve'asa hena* — sometimes one *korban* covers multiple transgressions and sometimes each transgression incurs a separate *korban*. Rav Yosi bar Chanina, the *amora*, explains: *achat she'hi hena ve'hena she'hi achat* — a single that is multiple, and a multiple that is single. *Achat* — one — might suggest a complete *melacha*; *me'achat* — from one — teaches that even a partial *melacha* generates liability. *Hena* — those — refers to the *avot* melachot, each of which incurs its own *korban*; *mehena* — from those — extends to the *toladot* as well. And the crossings: *zadon Shabbos v'shigag melachos* — one who knew it was Shabbat but forgot which acts are forbidden — incurs separate *korbanot* for each *melacha*, since each was a distinct forgetting. Conversely, *shiggag Shabbos v'zadon melachos* — one who forgot it was Shabbat but knew the acts were forbidden — incurs only one *korban*, because there was a single forgetting: the person forgot it was Shabbat. This returns us to the fundamental structure of *korbanot* for Shabbat violations discussed extensively at the beginning of the seventh *perek*.

To summarize the range of opinions in this *baraisa* on the *melacha* of writing: Rabbi Yosi holds it is entirely about *roshem* — making functional marks — and any two symbols, regardless of whether they are letters, suffice. At the other end, Rabbi Yehuda in his own name holds that it must be two *different* letters forming a word. Rabban Gamliel, as cited by Rabbi Yehuda, allows the same letter doubled so long as it forms a word. Rabbi Shimon requires a complete word but does not demand the entire intended text. Two letters that form part of a word but do not themselves constitute a word — that would not be enough.

## Open-Closed Letters

### Shem Shimon Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מָצִינוּ שֵׁם קָטָן מִשֵּׁם גָּדוֹל. מִי דָּמֵי? מֵ״ם דְּ״שֵׁם״ סָתוּם, מֵ״ם דְּ״שִׁמְעוֹן״ פָּתוּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda said: We found** that one is liable for writing even if he did not complete what he was writing and wrote **a small name** that constituted part **of a longer name,** e.g., Shem from Shimon. The Gemara asks: **Is it similar?** The ***mem*** in **Shem is closed and the *mem*** in **Shimon is open.**

We close with a discussion that will carry into tomorrow's *shiur*. Rabbi Yehuda's example of *shem mi'Shimon* — writing the two-letter word *shem* as part of the larger intended word *Shimon* — raises a technical problem.

### Mem Distinction

The *mem* of the word *shem* is a *mem sofit* — a closed *mem*, as it appears at the end of a word. But if one is in the middle of writing the word *Shimon*, the *mem* one would have written is an open *mem* — *mem* as it appears in the interior of a word. So the two letters written — *shin-mem* — do not actually spell the word *shem*, because the *mem* form is wrong. How then can one be liable for having written *shem*?

### Chisda's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת סָתוּם וַעֲשָׂאוֹ פָּתוּחַ — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: That is to say** that a **closed** letter that **one rendered open is valid** even in writing a Torah scroll, and it is not considered an irregularity in the writing. Therefore, one is liable for writing an open letter instead of a closed one.

Rav Chisda answers: *zos omeres satum va'aso pasuach kosher* — this teaches us that if something should have been a closed letter and was written as an open letter, it is nonetheless valid *bedi'avad*. An open *mem* can stand in for a closed *mem*, and therefore the two letters one wrote do constitute the word *shem* in a functional sense.

### Torah Scroll Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ — שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה, שֶׁלֹּא יִכְתּוֹב אַלְפִין עַיְינִין, עַיְינִין אַלְפִין. בֵּיתִין כָּפִין, כָּפִין בֵּיתִין. גַּמִּין צָדִין, צָדִין גַּמִּין. דַּלְתִין רֵישִׁין, רֵישִׁין דַּלְתִין. הֵיהִין חֵיתִין, חֵיתִין הֵיהִין. וָוִין יוֹדִין, יוֹדִין וָוִין. זַיְינִין נוּנִין, נוּנִין זַיְינִין. טֵיתִין פֵּיפִין, פֵּיפִין טֵיתִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raised an objection** from a *baraita* that interprets the verse: **"And you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house and on your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9). "And you shall write them [*ukhtavtam*]" means **that it should be perfect writing [*ketiva tamma*]** with no mistakes, and clear writing. This means that **one should not write** an ***alef* as** an ***ayin*,** an ***ayin* as** an ***alef*,** a ***beit* as** a ***kaf*,** a ***kaf* as** a ***beit*,** a ***gimmel* as** a ***tzadi*,** a ***tzadi* as** a ***gimmel*,** a ***dalet* as** a ***reish*,** a ***reish* as** a ***dalet*,** a ***heh* as** a ***ḥet*,** a ***ḥet* as** a ***heh*,** a ***vav* as** a ***yod*,** a ***yod* as** a ***vav*,** a ***zayin* as** a ***nun*,** a ***nun* as** a ***zayin*,** a ***tet* as** a ***peh*,** a ***peh* as** a ***tet*.**

The *gemara* then asks whether this is truly established: can one really substitute open for closed and vice versa? This brings us to a detailed set of *halachot* about the writing of *tefillin*, *mezuzot*, and *sifrei Torah*.

### Letter Confusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּפוּפִין פְּשׁוּטִין, פְּשׁוּטִין כְּפוּפִין. מֵימִין סָמְכִין, סָמְכִין מֵימִין. סְתוּמִין פְּתוּחִין, פְּתוּחִין סְתוּמִין. פָּרָשָׁה פְּתוּחָה לֹא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה סְתוּמָה, סְתוּמָה לֹא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה פְּתוּחָה. כְּתָבָהּ כְּשִׁירָה, אוֹ שֶׁכָּתַב אֶת הַשִּׁירָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בָהּ, אוֹ שֶׁכָּתַב שֶׁלֹּא בִּדְיוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁכָּתַב אֶת הָאַזְכָּרוֹת בְּזָהָב — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִגָּנְזוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, one should not write **bent** letters like *kaf* and *nun* found in the middle of a word as **straight** letters like *kaf* and *nun* found at the end of a word, nor should one write **straight** letters as **bent** letters. A final ***mem*** should not be written like a ***samekh*,** and a ***samekh*** should not be written like a ***mem*.** A **closed** *mem* should not be written **open,** and an **open** one should not be written **closed.** Similarly, if there is **an open paragraph** in the Torah **one may not render it closed,** and **one may not render a closed** paragraph **open.** If **one wrote** a *mezuza* or a Torah scroll **following the** Torah's format **for poetry or if one wrote poetry like** regular text, as a *mezuza* is typically written, **or if one wrote without ink** but with another material, **or if one wrote the mentions** of God's names **in gold,** all of **these must be suppressed.** Apparently, one may not write closed letters as open letters, contrary to the statement of Rav Ḥisda.

The *gemara* enumerates a series of prohibited letter confusions. *Shelo yichtov alfin ayinin* — do not write an *alef* in place of an *ayin* or vice versa: although the letters look different, those who do not pronounce the guttural *ayin* may confuse them aurally. The remaining pairs are visual: do not write a *beit* as a *kaf* or a *kaf* as a *beit*; not a *gimel* as a *tzadi* or a *tzadi* as a *gimel*; not a *dalet* as a *reish* or a *reish* as a *dalet* — these being distinguished only by a small protrusion on the *dalet*; not a *hei* as a *chet* or a *chet* as a *hei*; not a *vav* as a *yud* or a *yud* as a *vav*; not a *zayin* as a *nun sofit* or a *nun sofit* as a *zayin*; not a *tes* as a *pei* or a *pei* as a *tes*; not the bent forms written straight — so *chaf* or *nun* which are bent should not be written as *chaf sofit* or *nun sofit* which are straight — and not the straight forms written bent; not a *mem sofit* written as a *samach* or a *samach* as a *mem sofit*, these being visually very similar.

### Parshiyos Format

Regarding the formatting of *parshiyot*: a *parsha ptucha* — an open section, marked by a *pei* in the *chumash*, which denotes a blank line to the end — must not be written as a *stuma* — a closed section, marked by a *samach*, which means leaving a blank space and continuing the next *parsha* on the same line. And vice versa.

### Invalid Writings

The *baraisa* then lists cases where the *sefer Torah* is invalid and the writing must go into *genizah*: writing the poetic sections — such as *Az Yashir* or *Ha'azinu* — in the format of continuous prose rather than their prescribed columnar or spaced format; writing without *dyo* (ink); writing God's names in gold. All of these render the *sefer Torah* invalid, and the pages must be placed in *genizah*.

### Unresolved Question

This brings us to the question with which we conclude — and which will be taken up tomorrow. If a *sefer Torah* is invalid precisely because one must not write a closed *mem* as an open *mem* or vice versa, why does the *mishnah* assume that writing *shem* from *Shimon* — which involves writing an open *mem* in the place where a closed *mem* is required — is nonetheless sufficient to constitute the *melacha* of *kosev* and render one liable on Shabbat? The *sefer Torah* would be invalid, yet the person is *chayav*. We will resolve that question in tomorrow's *shiur*.
