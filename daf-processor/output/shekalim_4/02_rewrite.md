# Shekalim 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exemptions & Donations

### Kohanim Correction

We begin today with *daf* 4, picking up with the *mishna* at the bottom of 3b. Before proceeding, a brief correction to yesterday's reading of the final line preceding today's *mishna* is in order. The passage concerns the *Kohanim*'s claim that they were exempt from contributing the half-*shekel* on the grounds that doing so would render the communal *korban* a private one. The position of *Rav Ben Buchri* is that he challenged the *Chachamim* with the argument: *zu lo nidvas yachid* — is this not an individual donation? Would not the *Kohanim*'s contribution transform the *korban* into a private one? The *Chachamim*'s response is: *keivan she'nimtseru l'tzibur k'moshe hu nidvas tzibur* — once the funds are handed over for the communal *korban*, they become communal funds, regardless of their private origin. The *Kohanim*'s claim that they were exempt — a position associated with *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* — was therefore entirely without merit, even if self-servingly motivated. Once individual funds are designated for the communal offering, they assume the identity of communal property.

### Non-Seizure Rule

We now turn to the next *mishna*. *Af al pi she'amru ein memashkinin nashim va'avadim u'ketanim* — even though the rule is that one does not seize the property of women, slaves, and minors to compel payment of the half-*shekel*, since these individuals are not even obligated in the half-*shekel* to begin with. The property of free adult men, by contrast, could be seized as collateral once a certain period had passed without payment. But for the exempt categories — women, slaves, and minors — no *memashken* takes place.

### Voluntary Gifts

The *mishna* continues: *aval im shaklu mekablin mi'yadam* — but if they nonetheless wish to give voluntarily, their contribution is gladly accepted. Regarding *ketanim* specifically: as was noted in yesterday's discussion, once a minor reaches *bar mitzvah*, even though he is not obligated until the age of twenty — twenty being the age modeled after the *shekel* of the *Torah*, one of the rare areas in *halacha* where the age of twenty is halachically significant — one nonetheless encourages him to give. The present *mishna*, however, addresses those who are under *bar mitzvah*: we do not ask them to give, we will not be *memashken*, but if they wish to contribute, we accept their donation.

### Tosefta Mashal

The concept of *memashkenin* — seizing property — might initially seem incongruous in the context of contributing to the *korbanot*. The *Tosefta* in *Shekalim* (Perek 1, Tosefta 2) addresses this tension directly through a striking *mashal*. The *Tosefta* states: *mashkenu Yisrael al shikleihem k'dei she'yehu korbanot tzibur kreivin mei'hem* — the property of Israel was held as collateral so that the communal sacrifices would be brought from them. The *mashal* offered is that of a man who sustained an injury to his leg: *v'haya rofe kofeto u'mechattech b'visaro bishvil l'rafoso* — the doctor would bind him down and excise the infected flesh in order to heal him. So too, says God, we compel Israel to give their *shekel* — *k'dei she'yehu korbanot tzibur kreivin mei'hem mipnei she'korbanot tzibur mirtzin u'mechapprin bein Yisrael l'avihem she'bashamayim* — so that the communal sacrifices, which bring atonement and reconciliation between Israel and their Father in Heaven, will be brought from their own funds. The *mashal* captures something profound: what might appear coercive is in reality an intervention in Israel's deepest interest. The communal sacrifice is only truly communal — and thus truly atoning — when it is funded by all of Israel. The *memashken* ensures that each Jew has a genuine share in that offering, much as a physician's painful intervention serves the ultimate good of the patient.

## Non-Jewish Donors

### Shekel Rejection

The *mishna* now turns to an entirely different category: *ha'nochri v'ha'kuti she'shiklu ein mekablin mei'hen* — if a non-Jew or a Samaritan wishes to contribute their *shekel*, we do not accept it. This is not a general operating fund; the half-*shekel* goes directly toward the *korbanot tzibur*. The communal sacrifice by definition represents the Jewish community. Accepting contributions toward it from those outside the community would blur the very communal identity that the *korban tzibur* is meant to express. The *mishna* further specifies that we do not accept from them *kinei zavin v'kinei zavot* — bird offerings for a *zav* and a *zavah* — nor *kinei yoledet*, bird offerings for a woman who gave birth, nor *chatat* and *asham* offerings.

### Voluntary vs Obligatory

The operative principle is formulated clearly: *ze ha'klal — kol she'niddar v'nidav mekablin miyadan*, anything that is a *neder* or *nedavah*, a vow or free-will offering, we accept from them. *Kol she'eino niddar v'nidav, ein mekablin miyadan* — anything that is obligatory in nature, including the *shekel* itself, we do not accept. The distinction is between voluntary offerings, which a non-Jew may bring to acknowledge the God of Israel, and obligatory sacrifices, which are the expression of one's membership in the Jewish covenantal community. The former are welcomed; the latter are refused.

### Nidur Nidav Rule

The logic underlying this distinction bears emphasis. Obligatory sacrifices — *chatat*, *asham*, the birds of *zav* and *yoledet* — are intrinsically tied to a framework of Jewish religious obligation and identity. The *shekel* too, while a monetary contribution rather than an animal offering, partakes of this obligatory character. Accepting such contributions from non-Jews or Samaritans would imply that these individuals are members of the community whose obligations they are fulfilling — a claim the *mishna* categorically rejects.

### Ezra Precedent

The biblical grounding for this rejection is drawn from the book of *Ezra*: *lo lachem velanu livnot bayit l'Eloheinu* — "It is not for you and for us to build a house for our God." When the returnees from Babylonian exile began rebuilding the *Beit Hamikdash*, and the Samaritans offered to participate, they were refused. The Samaritans at that period presented themselves as willing partners, but the leadership of the Jewish community drew a sharp communal boundary. The concern was not merely abstract: accepting Samaritan funds for the *Beit Hamikdash* would have given them grounds to claim a stake in the Temple — that it was not solely a Jewish institution but a Samaritan one as well. The *mishna* invokes this precedent to anchor its own rejection of such contributions in explicit *pesukim*. While, as the *Gemara* will discuss, there are *Tannaic* opinions that Samaritans may hold a form of Jewish status, the communal dimension of the question — how a community defines its own boundaries — operates on a different register from the abstract question of individual personal status.

## Kolbon Fee

### Kolbon Obligation

The *mishna* now turns to an entirely distinct subject: the *kolbon*. The *kolbon* is a small surcharge — a money-changer's commission — paid when contributing the half-*shekel*. The practical context is that if one brings a full *sela* rather than an exact half-*shekel*, change must be made, and a fee is taken. More precisely — as will emerge from the analysis — it functions as a transaction fee rather than purely a change-making fee. The following are obligated in the *kolbon*: *Leviyim, Yisraeliyim, geirim*, and freed slaves — in short, all who are obligated in the half-*shekel*. Exempt are *Kohanim*, women, slaves, and minors.

### Kohanim Exempt

The exemptions for women, slaves, and minors are readily understood: they are exempt from the *shekel* obligation entirely, and if they choose to contribute voluntarily, we would not impose an additional surcharge. The exemption of *Kohanim*, however, requires a word of explanation. As discussed earlier, the *Kohanim* — according to *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* — are actually obligated in the half-*shekel*. Yet we do not seize their property to compel payment, out of *kavod*, a form of professional courtesy, so as not to publicly embarrass those who serve in the Temple. In the same spirit, we waive the *kolbon* fee for *Kohanim* even though they are technically obligated. The *Kohanim*, in any case, were already resistant to contributing; demanding a surcharge on top of their contribution would only further antagonize them.

Importantly, the money changers who collected the *kolbon* were not independent operators who could set their own commission. They were employees of the *Beit Hamikdash*, operating at the direction of the Temple administration. It was therefore within the institution's authority to waive the fee for certain categories of contributors.

*Ha'shokel al yedei Kohen al yedei isha al yedei eved al yedei katan patur* — if one gives a whole *shekel* on behalf of oneself and an exempt individual — say, oneself and a *Kohen*, or oneself and one's wife — one is exempt from the *kolbon*. The reasoning here is twofold: first, the other party would not have been subject to a *kolbon* had they contributed independently; second, one has given an exact *shekel* and no change need be made. One effectively piggybacks on the exemption of the party on whose behalf one is paying.

### Joint Payment

*V'im shakal al yado v'al yedei chavero chayav bekolbon echad* — but if one pays a full *shekel* for oneself and an obligated partner, one owes one *kolbon*, even though no change needs to be made and the payment is made in a single transaction. This is counterintuitive: if the logic of the *kolbon* were purely about the cost of making change, then a bundled payment should attract no fee at all. The better way to understand the *kolbon*, as emerges from this case, is as a transaction fee — not unlike a processing fee charged whenever a financial transaction takes place, regardless of whether change is made. Even when two people combine their payment into one efficient transaction, a single transaction fee remains due. What they save is the second fee; they do not escape the first. And when one bundles a contribution with an exempt party, one escapes the fee entirely by virtue of that party's exemption.

The case of *ha'noten sela v'notel shekel* — one who gives a full *sela* and receives back a half-*shekel* as change — incurs two *kolbonot*. On the transaction-fee understanding, this is intelligible: there is the standard transaction fee for the act of contributing, and there is an additional fee for the actual making of change.

---

## Kolbon Amount

### Fee Type

The *mishna* specifies the amount of the *kolbon*: *ma'ah kesef*, divrei *Rabbi Meir* — a *ma'ah*, which is a twenty-fourth of a *sela* and thus a twelfth of the half-*shekel*, according to *Rabbi Meir*. *Vachachamim omrim chatzi ma'ah* — the *Chachamim* say it is half a *ma'ah*, which amounts to approximately four percent of the half-*shekel*. Four percent is a meaningful surcharge; the typical money-changer's commission in other contexts was likely lower.

There is a further dispute regarding the destination of these collected *kolbon* funds. *Rabbi Meir* holds that the *kolbon* monies go to the same fund as the *shekel* itself — toward the *korbanot tzibur*. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, hold that it goes to the *shulchani* — to the money changer — or to other Temple funds. This dispute reflects a deeper disagreement about the nature of the *kolbon*: if it is truly an integral part of the *shekel* obligation (as *Rabbi Meir* maintains), it makes sense that it flows to the same purpose; if it is merely a service charge, it would naturally be directed elsewhere.

---

## Kolbon Fee (II)

### Rabbi Meir Dissent

*Rabbi Meir omer shenei kolbonot* — *Rabbi Meir* holds that even in the case of two individuals contributing jointly in a single transaction, two *kolbonot* must be paid rather than one. The *Gemara* will explain the basis of *Rabbi Meir*'s position at greater length in a later passage. In brief, *Rabbi Meir* holds that the *kolbon* is not merely a transaction fee but an inherent supplement to every half-*shekel* — a small margin added to ensure that the full, pure amount of the divine standard has been met. On this view, each half-*shekel* individually requires its own *kolbon*, and combining two contributions into a single payment does not merge the two separate obligations into one.

### Charity Waiver

*Ha'shokel al yedei ani v'al yedei shecheino v'al yedei ben iro patur* — one who pays on behalf of a poor person, a neighbor, or a fellow townsman — where the intent is genuine charitable giving rather than a lending arrangement — is exempt from the *kolbon*. The distinction from the earlier case of joint payment is meaningful: when two obligated individuals combine their contributions, both are truly giving, and the *kolbon* attaches to both. But when one person is simply being a *tzaddik* and paying on behalf of another without any expectation of repayment, that generosity exempts the payer from the surcharge. *V'im hilvan* — if, however, it turns out that one was merely lending the other the money, such that the recipient remains responsible for repayment, then the earlier rule applies and the *kolbon* is owed.

## Estate Partnership

### Undivided Estate

*Ha'achim hashutafin shechayavim bakolbon paturim mima'aser behema* — brothers who are partners — in the sense of an undivided inherited estate — and who are obligated in the *kolbon*, are exempt from *ma'aser behema*. The *mishna* here invokes one of the few contexts in *halacha* that recognizes something approximating the concept of a corporate entity. An estate that has not yet been divided among the heirs is not a conventional partnership between distinct individuals; it functions as a single unified entity. The animals belonging to such an undivided estate are subject to *ma'aser behema* as though they are owned by one entity. Conversely, such an estate, even if it must contribute multiple half-*shekalim* on behalf of each of the brothers, is treated as a single entity for purposes of the *kolbon* and is therefore exempt from paying multiple *kolbonot*. The analogy that some *meforshim* draw is to a father who pays on behalf of his children: just as a father's payment on his children's behalf does not multiply the *kolbon*, the estate's payment on behalf of the brothers does not multiply the fee.

### Corporate Entity

The inverse is also stated: *k'shechayavim b'ma'aser behema paturim min hakolbon* — when they are subject to *ma'aser behema* (that is, when the estate exists as a unified entity), they are correspondingly exempt from multiple *kolbonot*. The two obligations track one another: the status of the undivided estate as a single legal entity determines both its liability for *ma'aser behema* and its exemption from multiple *kolbon* payments.

### Partnership Corp

If, however, the brothers divided the estate and subsequently regrouped as a conventional partnership, the picture changes. A conventional partnership — as opposed to an undivided estate — is *patur* from *ma'aser behema* (since it is a *shutafut* of distinct individuals), but is *chayav* in the *kolbon*, because the partners are now multiple individuals who happen to be acting jointly, rather than a single corporate entity. It is worth noting that while most *meforshim* read *achim hashutafin* as a single case, the *Rambam* reads the phrase as referring to two distinct cases. The *Rambam* goes further and allows that an ordinary partnership — not only an undivided estate — can, under certain conditions, function as a corporate entity for these purposes. This is a striking position: the concept of a legal corporation, with all that it implies about the separate legal identity of the entity from its individual members, is exceedingly difficult to locate in *halachic* literature. That the *Rambam* finds room for it here is a matter of considerable interest.

## Kolbon Amount (II)

### Amount Dispute

As already quoted, the *mishna*'s definition of the *kolbon* amount is: *ma'ah kesef*, according to *Rabbi Meir*, and *chatzi ma'ah* according to the *Chachamim*. The *sela* is the full *shekel* of the *Torah*; the *shekel* in the language of the *Mishnah* refers to the half-*shekel* of the *Torah*. A *ma'ah* is a twenty-fourth of a *sela*, making it a twelfth of the half-*shekel*. A *chatzi ma'ah*, the *Chachamim*'s figure, is thus a twenty-fourth of the half-*shekel*, yielding approximately four percent. On the *Chachamim*'s view, the *kolbon* is a modest surcharge. On *Rabbi Meir*'s view, it is somewhat more substantial — and, as we shall see, carries a deeper theological significance.

## Samaritan Status

### Rabbi Ba Opinion

The *Gemara* now takes up the question of Samaritan status in greater depth. *Amar Rabbi Ba: tipater keman de'amar kuti kanochri* — the *mishna*'s ruling regarding Samaritans can be explained according to the opinion that a *kuti* has the status of a non-Jew. This is, however, a disputed position.

### Rebbi & Gamliel

The relevant *Tannaic* dispute is stated directly: *kuti kanochri divrei Rebbi* — Rebbi holds that Samaritans are like non-Jews in legal status, having not genuinely converted. *Rabban Gamliel omer kuti kiyisrael lechol davar* — Rabban Gamliel, by contrast, holds that Samaritans are to be treated as Jews in every respect.

### Rabbi Elazar View

*Amar Rabbi Elazar: matnitin banochri — habakutim lo* — Rabbi Elazar offers a more nuanced reading. The *mishna*, he argues, is speaking about non-Jews in its relevant provisions; with respect to Samaritans, the rules are different. On Rabbi Elazar's reading, Samaritans may well be Jewish in their individual status, but the context of *korbanot tzibur* and the communal *shekel* is governed by a different logic: even if Samaritans are technically Jews, the fact that they organized themselves as a distinct and often antagonistic community — as the episode in *Ezra* makes plain — justifies excluding them from communal boundaries even where their individual status might warrant inclusion. On the other hand, if a Samaritan presents himself as an individual wishing to bring an obligatory *korban*, Rabbi Elazar would accept it.

This position is reflected in the *Rambam*'s ruling as well. The *Rambam* holds that while Samaritans are excluded from the communal *shekel*, if a Samaritan brings a *korban chatat* — an obligatory sacrifice — it is accepted from him. The reasoning is that one should leave open the possibility that such an individual may wish to do *teshuva* and rejoin the broader Jewish community. This represents a fascinating interplay between the question of individual halachic status and the management of communal boundaries: the latter may be drawn more strictly than the former strictly requires, while still leaving certain doors open at the individual level.

The *Gemara* then cites the *drasha* on the word *adam* from the beginning of *Vayikra*: *adam ki yakriv mikem korban la-Hashem — adam lerabbos es hagerim*, the word *adam* comes to include *gerim*. That this needs to be derived through a *drasha* is itself somewhat notable, given that the Torah equates a *ger* with a native in numerous other contexts. An alternative version of the *drasha* reads *adam afilu nochrim* — even a non-Jew can bring a *korban*, consistent with our *mishna*'s ruling that free-will offerings are accepted from non-Jews. *Mikem*, "from among you," then serves to exclude apostates: *lehotzi es hamumrim* — an apostate cannot bring a *korban*. The *Korban HaEdah* suggests that *gerim* in this *drasha* would obviously include *Kutim*, though as the *Gemara* itself acknowledges, that somewhat begs the question of Samaritan status.

## Meir's Principle (III)

### Adam Derasha

The *drasha* on *adam ki yakriv mikem* thus establishes a range: *gerim* are included in the category of those who may bring offerings; apostates are excluded. The non-Jew occupies an intermediate position — welcome to bring a free-will *olah*, but unable to participate in obligatory communal offerings or the communal *shekel*. The *mikem* exclusion of apostates is particularly striking: the apostate, who was born Jewish, is denied a status that the voluntary *ger* and even the non-Jewish stranger enjoy. The internal logic of communal belonging, as these *derashot* articulate it, turns on commitment to the covenant — not merely biological origin.

## Meir's Principle (III)

### Gemara Parse

The *Gemara* now returns to parse the *mishna*'s ruling on non-Jews and Samaritans more precisely. The difficulty is that the *mishna* appears to address both non-Jews and Samaritans simultaneously throughout, while Rabbi Elazar's position requires the *mishna* to be read as addressing different groups in different clauses.

---

## Samaritan Status (II)

### Gemara Challenge

*Matnisin palig al Rabbi Eliezer* — the *Gemara* argues that the *mishna* itself contradicts Rabbi Elazar's position. Rabbi Elazar claims that the second portion of the *mishna* — the section listing obligatory sacrifices that are not accepted — addresses only non-Jews. The *Gemara* challenges this: *vechi yesh kinei zavim vezavot banochrim?* Do non-Jews even have the category of *zav* and *zavah*? These are categories of ritual impurity that simply do not apply to non-Jews. If the *mishna* says we do not accept the bird offerings associated with these conditions, it cannot be addressing non-Jews — for whom such offerings are entirely irrelevant. It must therefore be addressing Samaritans, for whom these categories do apply and from whom one might have thought such offerings would be accepted.

### Reisha Seifa

This leads to the *Gemara*'s resolution: *ella reisha banochrim vesifa beKutim* — the beginning of the *mishna* addresses non-Jews, and the end addresses Samaritans. The text invites the question of whether this division is satisfactory: *kein hu?* Is it truly the case that the beginning is about non-Jews and the end about Samaritans? And the *Gemara* affirms: *kein hu — reisha banochrim vesifa beKutim*.

This reading, however, creates its own difficulties. The beginning of the *mishna* explicitly lists both non-Jews and Samaritans together: *ha'nochri v'ha'kuti she'shiklu ein mekablin mei'hen*. To say the beginning is exclusively about non-Jews requires reading the explicit mention of *kuti* in the opening clause as part of the second section, which governs Samaritans. The best reading offered by the *meforshim* is that the *mishna* as a whole excludes both non-Jews and Samaritans from everything — the *shekel*, and the obligatory bird offerings alike. The beginning mentions both, and the end addresses Samaritans specifically with respect to the category of obligatory bird offerings, where one might have expected a more permissive ruling. Even so, fitting Rabbi Elazar's position into this framework remains a genuine challenge. It appears that the *mishna* explicitly refuses to distinguish between non-Jews and Samaritans in the way Rabbi Elazar would prefer. That said, the *Rambam* rules effectively in line with Rabbi Elazar — accepting an obligatory *korban chatat* from a Samaritan — suggesting that however he reads this *Gemara*, he does not consider the *mishna* to have definitively foreclosed that position.

## Stages & Specificity

### Yochanan Position

The *Gemara* now shifts to a related but distinct question: the basis on which contributions from non-Jews may be accepted or refused, depending on the stage of construction and the specificity of the donation. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: batchila ein mekablin meihem lo davar mesuyam velo davar she'eino mesuyam* — at the outset, meaning when construction is beginning (as in the case of *Ezra* and the initial building of the *Beit Hamikdash*), we accept neither a *davar mesuyam* nor a *davar she'eino mesuyam* from non-Jews. A *davar mesuyam* is a designated, specific object — "I want to donate this *menorah* and have it placed at the entrance with my name on it." A *davar she'eino mesuyam* is general funds, fungible money with no specific designation. At the outset, Rabbi Yochanan holds, neither is accepted.

*Uvesof*, however — once the *Beit Hamikdash* is built and established, and its Jewish identity is beyond dispute — *mekablin meihem davar she'eino mesuyam*, we may accept general, undesignated funds from non-Jews. What we cannot accept even then is a *davar mesuyam*: a specific, named donation that would allow the donor to claim a proprietary connection to a particular feature of the Temple. The concern with a *davar mesuyam* is precisely that it creates a lasting association between the donor and the sacred object — a plaque, an inscription, a named installation — which implies a stake in the institution's identity.

### Reish Lakish

*Reish Lakish* takes a considerably more stringent position: *bein batchila bein besof ein mekablin meihem lo davar mesuyam velo davar she'eino mesuyam* — whether at the beginning or at the end, we accept from them neither specific nor non-specific contributions. Reish Lakish would refuse all monetary donations from non-Jews to the Temple, regardless of their form or timing. This is a remarkably exacting standard; it would have made the practical management of the Temple's finances quite demanding, given the degree of interaction between Jewish and non-Jewish communities that we know existed in this period. Archaeological evidence from synagogues in *Eretz Yisrael*, for instance, sometimes includes inscriptions suggesting non-Jewish donors — suggesting that on the ground, a position closer to Rabbi Yochanan's may have prevailed.

### Braita Bedek

A *braita* is then introduced: *ein mekablin meihem hekdesh unedava l'vedek habayit* — we do not accept from non-Jews sanctified objects or donations designated for *bedek habayit*, Temple maintenance and repair funds. The *braita* makes no distinction between *davar mesuyam* and *davar she'eino mesuyam*, and makes no distinction between the beginning and the end of the construction process. This appears to contradict Rabbi Yochanan's position, which permits non-specific contributions after the Temple is built. The *Gemara* therefore attempts to resolve the tension: *patar la* — explain it as speaking of *davar mesuyam*, of concrete, specific designated objects. The *braita*'s prohibition, on this reading, is limited to specific objects intended for *bedek habayit*, not to generic funds. This would preserve Rabbi Yochanan's distinction between specific and non-specific donations even in the context of maintenance funds.

### Generic Funds

The *Gemara* then turns to the question of what happens when non-specific funds end up being used for specific purposes. The case of *nesachim* is raised: if a non-Jew brings a free-will *olah* — which Rabbi Yochanan and even Reish Lakish would accept, as established earlier — accompanying *nesachim* (wine and flour) are required. *U'motar nesachim lo li-chlei shareit inun?* The principle is that surplus *nesachim* funds are redirected toward purchasing *klei shareit*, sacred Temple vessels. If we accept the non-Jew's *olah* along with its *nesachim*, we may wind up using the surplus to buy a specific object — a *davar mesuyam*. How can this be permitted even according to Rabbi Yochanan?

*Heitiv Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Bun*: a related challenge is raised from the laws of *arachin*. The opinion that allows non-Jews to make an *erech* — declaring the monetary value of a person as a donation — directs those funds to *bedek habayit*. Even according to Reish Lakish, how is it possible that a non-Jew's *erech* funds, which go to *bedek habayit*, are permissible?

The resolution is: *li-shemayim hu mitkavein u-mei-aleihem heim ba'im o l'vedek habayit o l'chlei shareit* — the donor's intent is directed toward heaven in a general sense; he is giving a *korban* or a donation without specifying where the funds should go. We then decide how to allocate the money — whether to operating funds, *bedek habayit*, or *klei shareit*. Because the donor cannot claim ownership over or association with any specific object purchased with the funds, the concern that motivates the prohibition does not apply. The same logic resolves the *nesachim* case: the non-Jew intends to bring his *olah*, and the allocation of surplus *nesachim* funds is our decision, not his. He cannot claim that his money went to purchase any specific vessel.

### Reish Lakish Standard

On Reish Lakish's view, it emerges that his objection is more precisely targeted than it first appears. The *pasuk* that underlies the prohibition — *lo lachem velanu livnot bayit l'Eloheinu* — is specifically about construction of the house. Reish Lakish's concern, even regarding *davar she'eino mesuyam*, is limited to funds that actually contribute to the building or structure itself. Generic operating funds — funds that, from the donor's perspective, are simply an offering directed to heaven with no connection to any specific structure or object — Reish Lakish is prepared to accept. The critical factor is whether the donor can claim a stake in the *bayit* itself. When the money is genuinely generic and could be used for anything at all, Reish Lakish's concern does not apply and he would accept it. When it goes, even indirectly, to the construction of the building, the prohibition of *lo lachem velanu livnot bayit* is triggered.

The practical upshot is that both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree that a specifically designated donation — a *davar mesuyam* — is never acceptable from a non-Jew. Rabbi Yochanan permits non-specific donations after construction is complete; Reish Lakish limits even that, refusing non-specific donations that are directed toward the structure, while permitting fully generic contributions. In both cases, the decisive factor is whether the donor can assert a proprietary claim over any identifiable element of the *Beit Hamikdash*.

## Meir's Principle (III)

### Divine Standard

The *Gemara* finally returns to the basis of *Rabbi Meir*'s position on the *kolbon*. *Kasavar Rabbi Meir benoten shiklo shalem shehu chayav bekalbon* — Rabbi Meir holds that even one who pays a standard half-*shekel*, without requiring any change, is nonetheless obligated in the *kolbon*. The reason: *kemin matbe'a shel esh hotzi hakadosh baruch hu mitachat kisei kevodo v'her'ahu l'Moshe v'amar lo: zeh yitnu* — the Holy One, Blessed be He, drew forth something resembling a coin of fire from beneath His Throne of Glory and showed it to Moshe, saying: "This shall they give." The implication of *zeh*, "this" — the divine, perfectly pure half-*shekel* of fire — is that nothing produced in this physical world can meet that standard of purity without some supplementation. Every material coin carries impurities that fall short of the divine ideal. Therefore, *Rabbi Meir* holds, every half-*shekel* must be accompanied by a small additional amount — the *kolbon* — to ensure that the full, true value of the divine standard has been honored. This is not a transaction fee in Rabbi Meir's understanding; it is a built-in margin of error required by the metaphysical gap between the earthly coin and its heavenly archetype.

This is why, in *Rabbi Meir*'s view, when one gives a full *sela* and receives a half-*shekel* in change, two *kolbonot* are owed: one is the ordinary transaction fee (as the *Chachamim* would also acknowledge), and one is the supplemental amount that accompanies every half-*shekel* by Rabbi Meir's own principle. And this is also why *Rabbi Meir* directs the *kolbon* funds to the same destination as the *shekel* itself — toward the *korbanot tzibur* — since the *kolbon*, on his view, is not a service charge but an integral extension of the *shekel* obligation, required by the very nature of what the *shekel* represents.
