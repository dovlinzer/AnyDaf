# Sukkah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stolen & Dried Lulav

### Hadar & Aesthetics

The third *perek* opens with a subject already touched upon briefly: the invalidity of a stolen or dried *lulav*. A foundational principle underlies much of what follows. Many of the invalidities governing the *lulav*, the *etrog*, and the other *minim* derive from the use of the word *hadar* in the verse *peri etz hadar* — the fruit of a beautiful tree. The Gemara understands *hadar* to characterize all of the components of the *lulav* bundle. Rashi on the Mishnah situates this within the broader ideal of *zeh keli v'anvehu* — beautifying one's performance of *mitzvot*. While ordinarily that aspiration does not rise to the level of an absolute requirement whose absence invalidates the act, Rashi understands that the explicit use of *hadar* in the verse makes it central here, so that without it the object is disqualified entirely.

### Mishnah: No Distinction

The Gemara opens by noting that the Mishnah states its rule categorically — *ka pasik v'tani*, it simply states and states — without any qualification. A stolen or dried *lulav* is invalid. The Gemara observes: *lo shna b'yom tov rishon v'lo shna b'yom tov sheni* — no distinction is drawn between the first day of *Yom Tov* and the second day. It is clear from context and from the *Rishonim* that "the second day" here does not refer to the second day of *Yom Tov* in the *galus* sense, but rather to *Chol HaMoed*. The verse states *u'lekachtem lachem bayom harishon* — take on the first day — and concludes *u'smachtem lifnei Hashem Elokeichem shivat yamim*, rejoice before God seven days. The Rabbis understand those seven days to refer specifically to performance *lifnei Hashem* — in the Temple. Outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, only the first day carries a biblical obligation. The remaining days of *Chol HaMoed* are observed only as a *zecher l'mikdash*, a rabbinic enactment commemorating the Temple. The Mishnah's undifferentiated language, then, implies that both invalidity of *yavesh* and invalidity of *gazul* apply throughout the seven days, whether on the biblical first day or on the rabbinic *Chol HaMoed*.

### Hadar vs. Ownership

The Gemara then poses a pointed question. *Bishlama yavesh* — I understand why a dried-out *lulav* is invalid throughout: *hadar ba'inan ve'leika*, the requirement of aesthetic quality is simply absent. That logic applies uniformly across all seven days. But *gazul*? On the first day I understand: *u'lekachtem lachem* requires that the *lulav* be yours, and a stolen *lulav* is not yours. But why should it be invalid on *Chol HaMoed*, when the requirement of *lachem* — of personal ownership — is not in force? After all, we know as an accepted *halacha* that a borrowed *lulav* and *etrog* may be used on *Chol HaMoed*. So why should stolen be problematic?

This question pushes the *Rishonim* to articulate what principle distinguishes the aesthetic requirements from the ownership requirement, and why the former persist rabbinicaly on *Chol HaMoed* while the latter does not. Rashi and Tosafos offer the following framework: the remaining days are rabbinic in character, and we do not demand at the rabbinic level everything demanded at the biblical level. The key distinction is that *hadar* — and the related idea of *zeh keli v'anvehu* — touches a deeper theme, the notion of performing the *mitzvah* beautifully, which is important enough that the Rabbis insist upon it even on the other days. A question of ownership, by contrast, is a more technical and external requirement and is not demanded rabinically on *Chol HaMoed*.

### Central vs. Tangential

There is a further way to articulate this distinction. The aesthetic quality of *hadar* describes something about the very nature of the object being taken. A shriveled, dried-out *etrog* is a different physical object from a beautiful one; the nature of what you are doing is itself different. Ownership, by contrast, is not visible and is not part of the physical reality of the act. Whether the *lulav* belongs to you or is borrowed is an attribute entirely external to the object itself. This asymmetry explains why aesthetic requirements — being properties of the object — remain in force even on the rabbinic days, while the ownership requirement — being a more tangential, non-physical condition — is assumed not to apply. Rashi and Tosafos essentially embrace this approach, with some also noting a possible textual basis: in the verse, the word *lachem* precedes *bayom harishon*, suggesting it is limited to that day, while *hadar* appears after *yom rishon* and might apply to all seven.

### Sefarad vs. Ashkenaz

A significant *machloket Rishonim* surfaces here, rooted in a very practical historical reality: in many communities, particularly in *Ashkenaz*, a single valid *lulav* and *etrog* might serve an entire town, and often whatever *lulav* and *etrog* was available had long since dried out from prior years' use. The *Rishonim* of *Ashkenaz* largely held that all the *pesulim* remain in force on *Chol HaMoed* as well, and that if the *lulav* fails to satisfy them one cannot be fully *yotzei*. At most, one might take it and recite a *bracha* as a *zecher* — a commemoration — but not as a full discharge of the obligation. The *Rishonei Sefarad* took a starkly different view: even if a *lulav* is *yavesh*, even if it is dried out and fails the requirement of *hadar*, one is still *yotzei* with it on *Chol HaMoed*. This appears to run directly against the plain reading of our Gemara, which states that *yavesh* is a problem precisely because *hadar ba'inan ve'leika* — and this applies across all seven days.

### Ramban's Resolution

The Ramban offers a brilliant resolution — whether or not it is the *pshat* of the Gemara. He argues that when the Gemara speaks of "the second day of *Yom Tov*" as a day on which the aesthetic requirement of *hadar* applies, it is not referring to the rabbinic *mitzvah* of *Chol HaMoed* as we observe it today. Rather, it is speaking of the biblical *mitzvah* as performed in the *Beit HaMikdash* all seven days. In that context, one might say that the verse limits *lachem* to the first day — since *lachem* precedes *bayom harishon* — while *hadar* extends across all seven because it appears after *yom rishon*. But this entire discussion concerns the *mitzvah* as it exists in the *Mikdash*, not the rabbinic enactment of *zecher l'mikdash* that applies today. Therefore, when it comes to our rabbinic obligation, even a *yavesh lulav* would suffice on *Chol HaMoed*, consistent with the view of the *Rishonei Sefarad*. The Ramban's approach thus preserves the Gemara's logic for the biblical context while permitting the Sephardic leniency for the rabbinic context of *Chol HaMoed* in our times.

The essential takeaway from this opening section is twofold. First, *hadar* is understood as a deeper requirement than *lachem* — it touches the nature of the act itself and therefore persists even on the rabbinic days, while ownership is a more peripheral condition that does not. Second, the *Rishonei Sefarad* and *Ramban* effectively argue that our entire Gemara may be addressing the biblical *mitzvah* in the *Mikdash*, leaving the rabbinic obligation of *Chol HaMoed* considerably more lenient.

## Mitzvah B'Aveirah

### Lame Animal Analogy

The Gemara now supplies the source for why a stolen *lulav* is invalid even on *Chol HaMoed*, when there is no requirement of *lachem*. Rabbi Yochanan cites Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: the reason is *mishum de-havei leih mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — because it constitutes a *mitzvah* that comes through sin. One may not fulfill a *mitzvah* by means of an act of transgression.

The source is a verse: *v'heveisem gazul es hapisayach v'es hacholeh* — if you bring a stolen animal or a lame or sick animal to sacrifice, *hayertzeh lifnei Hashem* — will this be acceptable before God? The verse groups *gazul* and *pisayach* together. The Gemara draws a comparison: *gazul dumya d'pisayach* — stolen is placed in the same category as lame. Just as a lame animal has no remedy — it is permanently disqualified and can never be rectified for use as a *korban* — so too a stolen animal (and by extension, a stolen *lulav*) admits of no remedy: *leis lei takanta*, it cannot be fixed. And this applies regardless of whether we are speaking before the owner's despair or after: *lo shena lifnei ye'ush lo shena achar ye'ush*.

### Ye'ush & Ownership

This raises a significant question. *Bishlama lifnei ye'ush* — before the owner gives up hope — I understand why a stolen animal cannot be used as a *korban*: *adam ki yakriv mikem amar rachmana* — the verse at the opening of this week's *parasha* requires that the offering come *mikem*, from among you. A stolen animal is not the offerer's own. But *achar ye'ush* — after the original owner has despaired of recovering it? The principle of *ye'ush* holds that once an owner gives up hope of recovering a lost or stolen object, the thief may acquire ownership of it. If so, after *ye'ush* the stolen animal belongs to the one who took it — why then can it not be brought as a *korban*?

The Gemara's answer: *ela lav mishum de-havei leih mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — it can only be because of the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. Once stolen, the object is stigmatized. Even if the thief subsequently owns it, it remains categorically disqualified. The same logic applies to the *lulav*.

A closer look at the mechanics of *ye'ush* is warranted here, because the large Tosafos on this amud engages it directly. The standard rule is that *ye'ush* alone does not transfer ownership to a *ganav* or *gazlan*. If I steal an object and the owner subsequently despairs, that despair does not, by itself, cause me to own the object; I encountered it and took it with an obligation to return it, and his subsequent *ye'ush* cannot alter that. What transfers ownership is the combination of *ye'ush* followed by *shinui reshus* — a change of possession — specifically when a third party acquires the object after the owner has already despaired. So if I steal a sheep and sell it to a third party after *ye'ush*, that third party owns it because he encountered it when the owner had already given up hope. But I myself, having stolen it, never acquire it through *ye'ush* alone.

Yet the plain sense of this Gemara appears to assume that *ye'ush* does transfer ownership even to the *gazlan* himself. One approach is to accept that there is an opinion in the Gemara that *ye'ush kidei kani* — *ye'ush* alone does confer ownership in such a case. Another approach is that the act of sanctification — dedicating the animal to *hekdesh* — itself constitutes a kind of *shinui* that completes the acquisition in conjunction with *ye'ush*. The Gemara proceeds on the simpler assumption: the *gazlan* owns the animal after *ye'ush*, and yet it remains invalid because of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*.

### King's Parable

Rabbi Yochanan cites Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai a second time on this theme. The verse says: *ki ani Hashem ohev mishpat, sonei gezel b'olah* — I, God, love justice; I hate theft in a burnt-offering. What does this mean? The Gemara offers a parable: a human king was passing the tax house, the customs station. He instructed his servants: *tnu mekhes lamokhes* — pay the customs duty to the tax collector. His servants were astonished: *halo kol hamekhes kuloh shelkha hu* — all the tax revenue ultimately flows to you! Why are you paying taxes on your own property? He replied: *mimeni yilmedu kol ovrei derakhim, v'lo yavrikhu atzman min hamekhes* — let all those who travel this road learn from me, and they will not seek to evade their customs obligations. I wish to set the proper example.

It is worth pausing on the historical context. There are *mishnayot* in Nedarim that discuss whether one may make a technically misleading statement to a tax collector in order to avoid payment, and that discussion reflects a real and fraught reality in Roman Palestine, where taxation was often associated with oppression or illegitimate seizure. The parable here, by contrast, calls for scrupulous compliance — and for God to model that compliance. The parallel to our case is that someone might reason: all animals ultimately belong to God, so why should it matter to God whether the *korban* I bring was mine or stolen from someone else? I am returning it to its ultimate Owner. The parable answers: God refuses to condone the underlying sin, refuses to allow the act of coming before Him to serve as a retrospective justification for wrongdoing. Even when the act is ostensibly dedicated to Him, God does not wish to lend divine sanction to theft.

The *Yerushalmi* frames this principle in two distinct formulations, and both are illuminating. One formulation is *ein aveira mitzvah* — a sin cannot become a *mitzvah*, cannot be transformed into something meritorious through the mere fact that it enables a holy act. The other formulation is *ein mitzvah b'aveira* — a *mitzvah* cannot be performed through transgression. These two framings are not identical, and the tension between them is instructive. We do sometimes invoke the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'asei* — a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition. The blue thread of *tzitzit* must be wool even when attached to a linen garment; the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* overrides the concern of *shaatnez*. So there is a logic by which performing a *mitzvah* could override the transgression involved. *Mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* says no — at least in defined circumstances, doing a *mitzvah* does not condone the *aveirah* that enabled or accompanies it, and God does not wish to be party to that rationalization.

### Object vs. Action Focus

A fundamental conceptual question about *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* deserves attention. Does it operate by stigmatizing the *object*, or by invalidating the *act*? The first source — the comparison to a lame animal — clearly focuses on the object. A lame animal is permanently disqualified; the *object* itself carries the disability. By analogy, a stolen *lulav* is disqualified as an object. The second source — the king's parable — seems to focus on the *action*: what you are doing is intertwined with sin, and God refuses to accept it.

This distinction has practical consequences. Consider a stolen *shofar*: are you *yotzei* if you blow it? The Rambam, citing a *Yerushalmi*, rules that you are, reasoning that the *mitzvah* of *shofar* is technically fulfilled through the sound, not through the instrument itself, and the sound is not stolen — only the *shofar* is. This is a creative argument, but the underlying logic is clear: if *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* stigmatizes the *object*, we must identify what exactly is the operative object of the *mitzvah*; if the *mitzvah* is associated with something other than the stolen item itself, the principle may not apply. This reading also aligns with the language of the Mishnah, which says *lulav hagazul... pasul* — the *lulav* is *pasul* — not "one does not fulfill the obligation." The invalidity attaches to the object. The Rambam, similarly, categorizes *gezul* alongside physical blemishes of the *lulav*, which is entirely consistent with an object-focused understanding of the principle.

The action-focused interpretation, meanwhile, raises a different question: what does it mean for the sin to be sufficiently *intertwined* with the *mitzvah* to invalidate it? Carrying one's *lulav* on *Shabbat* and then shaking it in shul — one committed a sin that arguably facilitated the *mitzvah*, yet the *lulav* itself is not stigmatized, because the carrying does not alter the object's identity. With theft, however, the entanglement is direct and constitutive: one would not have the *lulav* at all were it not for the act of stealing. That connection is what makes it a genuine *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*.

### Scope & Application

This raises a further question about scope. Both sources for *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* are drawn from the context of *korban*. Yet the Gemara assumes without hesitation that the principle extends to the *lulav*. Some *Rishonim* accept this as a general principle operative across all *mitzvot*. Others note a difficulty: several other *Gemarot* seem unaware of the concept. A *Gemara* discussing a stolen *sukkah* raises ownership concerns but never invokes *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. A *Gemara* about eating *matzah* that is *tevel* — where eating it constitutes an *aveirah* — never raises the concept either. If the principle were truly global, one would expect it to surface in those discussions as well.

One response is that *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* outside the context of *korban* is only a rabbinic disqualification, and the other *Gemarot* are addressing the issue at the biblical level where the rabbinic principle does not apply. The Ramban takes a different and instructive approach. He argues that the principle is biblically operative specifically where the *mitzvah* has the character of *lehirtzot* — of seeking divine favor and acceptance — as the very language of the verse implies: *hayertzeh lifnei Hashem*. A *korban* is an act of direct supplication and connection with God. A *lulav*, shaken during *Hallel* and during *tefillah*, particularly in the context of praying for rain, similarly functions as a vehicle of *tefillah*, a form of direct appeal to God. Therefore, when you approach God in that mode and your means of doing so is tainted by transgression, the contradiction is biblically intolerable. By contrast, eating *matzah* or sitting in a *sukkah*, while profound *mitzvot*, do not carry that same immediate quality of direct divine appeal. The Ramban thus limits the biblical application of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* to acts that are functionally analogous to *korban* in their orientation toward divine acceptance.

## Yochanan vs. Shmuel

### Rabbi Ami's Statement

The Gemara records a parallel statement: *itmar nami amar Rabbi Ami* — Rabbi Ami stated as well that *yavesh pasul mipnei she'ein hadar*, a dried *lulav* is invalid because it lacks *hadar*, and *gazul pasul mishum de-havei leih mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*, a stolen *lulav* is invalid because of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. This formulation explicitly attributes the two invalidities to two distinct principles — one grounded in aesthetics, the other in the prohibition against sin — and, as stated, the implication is that the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* renders the stolen *lulav* invalid across all seven days.

### Shmuel's Limitation

Against this, the Gemara cites a competing position: *de'amar Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani amar Shmuel* — Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani quoted Shmuel as saying *lo shanu ela b'yom tov rishon* — the invalidity of *gazul* applies only on the first day of *Yom Tov*. But *mitoch she-yotzei b'shaul, yotzei nami b'gazul* — since on *Chol HaMoed* a borrowed *lulav* suffices and ownership is not required, a stolen *lulav* is equally permissible. On Shmuel's view, the invalidity of a stolen *lulav* derives entirely from the absence of ownership, and there is no independent operative principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* that would extend the disqualification to *Chol HaMoed*.

### Rav Nachman's Objection

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenged Shmuel's position from the Mishnah. The Mishnah says *lulav hagazul vehayavesh pasul* — stolen and dried is invalid. If the Mishnah lists *gazul* as invalid but never mentions *shaul* — a borrowed *lulav* — as invalid, the implication is that *shaul* is valid and *gazul* is not. When would this contrast be meaningful? If the Mishnah's ruling about *gazul* applies only to the first day, then the point of comparison with *shaul* would have to be made on the first day as well — but on the first day, *shaul* is itself invalid because of *lachem*. So if both *gazul* and *shaul* are invalid on the first day, there is nothing distinctive about listing *gazul* as invalid. The Mishnah's implied contrast between *gazul* (invalid) and *shaul* (valid) must therefore refer to *Chol HaMoed*. But this means the Mishnah is saying that on *Chol HaMoed*, when *shaul* is permissible, *gazul* is nonetheless invalid — directly contradicting Shmuel.

### Rava's Defense

Rava defended Shmuel's position. The Mishnah is in fact speaking only about the first day, he argued, and the contrast between *gazul* and *shaul* is entirely explicable: the Mishnah employs the rhetorical form of *lo mibaiya* — it goes without saying. Borrowed is invalid on the first day — it goes without saying that it is invalid, since you clearly have no ownership over a borrowed object. The Mishnah therefore mentions only *gazul*, which is the less obvious case. One might have thought that a stolen object, once the owner has despaired, belongs to the thief — and therefore that a stolen *lulav* could be used on the first day. The Mishnah teaches that this is wrong: *gazul* is invalid even on the first day. The Mishnah's omission of *shaul* reflects not that *shaul* is valid on *Chol HaMoed* while *gazul* is not, but rather that *shaul* is so obviously invalid on the first day that it needs no mention.

### Ye'ush in Robbery

Rava's reasoning carries an important implication. On the first day, the sole basis for the invalidity of *gazul* is the absence of *lachem* — the object is not truly yours. But one might think that with *gezeilah* — robbery — *ye'ush* comes almost automatically, since in the case of open robbery the owner witnesses the taking and is immediately aware of the loss. Perhaps then one should reason that *stam gezeilah ye'ush be'alim hu* — typically a robbery brings with it the owner's immediate despair — and therefore the robber immediately acquires ownership and can use it on the first day. Rava teaches that the Mishnah's ruling comes to exclude this reasoning: the stolen *lulav* is still invalid on the first day despite *ye'ush*. Whether this is because *ye'ush* alone is insufficient to confer ownership, or whether *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* independently invalidates it even on the first day, remains the crux of the deeper debate. What is clear is that on Rava's reading, Shmuel's position — that *gazul* is valid on *Chol HaMoed* because ownership is irrelevant there — stands unchallenged, and there is no need to invoke *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* as an independent ground of disqualification operating throughout the seven days.

## Hadasim from Non-Jews

### The Problem

The Gemara now moves to a striking practical application. Rav Huna addressed a group of *avankri* — merchants (or, according to the Aruch, something closer to royal officers) — with the following instruction concerning the purchase of *hadasim* for the community's *lulav* bundles.

### Rav Huna's Solution

*Ki zavnitu asa me-akum* — when you buy *hadasim* from non-Jews — *lo tagzu atun ela ligzoza inehu* — do not be the ones to cut the *hadasim* from the tree; let them cut the *hadasim* and then hand the branches to you. Why? *Stam ovdei kochavim gazlanei arata ninhu* — the non-Jews are generally assumed to be occupying land that is not rightfully theirs. This statement clearly reflects the particular historical and geographic reality of its time — Rav Huna was in *Bavel* — where land had been seized, whether through governmental confiscation or other means, in ways that *halacha* did not recognize as legitimate acquisition. The land was therefore considered *gazul*, and by extension the *hadas* trees growing on it were connected to stolen land. If a Jewish merchant cut the *hadasim* himself, he would be taking possession of an object directly connected to that status, and using such *hadasim* for the *mitzvah* would involve using a stolen object.

### Karka Eina Nigzelet

The resolution turns on the principle of *karka eina nigzelet* — land cannot be stolen. While one certainly transgresses by seizing another's property, the legal status of land does not change in the manner that movable property does. One cannot carry land away, place it in one's possession, or transfer it as chattel. Therefore, *halachically*, the land's identity never acquires the label of being stolen: you have sinned, but the land remains the original owner's land in terms of its legal status. As a consequence, the *hadas* trees that grow from the ground — while connected to that land — likewise do not yet bear the identity of stolen movable property. As long as they remain attached to the ground, the *karka eina nigzelet* principle shields them.

### Ye'ush & Shinui

When the *hadasim* are cut, the situation changes. They are now detached from the ground and have become movable property — *mitaltelim*. At the moment of cutting, they become capable of bearing the identity of stolen chattel. But crucially, the original owners of the land have long since despaired — *ye'ush* took effect long ago — and so these *hadasim*, the instant they are cut, simultaneously carry the status of stolen movable property *and* the owner's *ye'ush*. The *ye'ush* that had been legally inoperative while they were attached to the ground now becomes operative the moment they detach. If the non-Jew cuts them, then he is the first one holding them as movable property after *ye'ush* has taken effect. The Gemara frames this as *ye'ush* taking effect *b'yadaihu* — in their hands. When the Jewish merchant then purchases from the non-Jew, this constitutes *shinui reshus* — a change of possession — occurring after *ye'ush*. The combination of *ye'ush* plus *shinui reshus* is sufficient to confer clean ownership on the merchant. At the moment the merchant acquires the *hadasim*, their history as stolen objects is ancient and irrelevant; he is purchasing an object that, by the time it reaches him, is owned by the seller.

### Merchants' Interest

The Gemara raises an objection: why does all this require the *non-Jews* to be the ones cutting? Surely the merchants could cut the *hadasim* themselves, take possession of them as the first holders of a movable object after *ye'ush*, and then *shinui reshus* would take effect in their own hands — which would then be perfectly adequate for selling to others. The answer is: *tzricha b'oshana d'avanteri gufaihu* — the concern arises specifically for the merchants' own *hoshana*, their own use of the *hadasim* for the *mitzvah*. If a merchant cuts the *hadasim* himself, he is the first one to take hold of them as movable, stolen property. Even with *ye'ush* in effect, he himself is the one who encountered the object in its status as a *gezeilah* — and for him, even if *ye'ush* plus *shinui reshus* would eventually confer ownership, he cannot use it for a *mitzvah* because he originally handled it as a stolen object. But if the non-Jew cuts it first and then transfers it to the merchant, by the time the merchant encounters it, *ye'ush* has already taken effect in someone else's hands, *shinui reshus* takes place at the moment of purchase, and the merchant acquires an object whose problematic history has already been resolved. For purposes of his own *mitzvah*, his slate is clean.

This requires a careful look at the mechanics of *ye'ush ve'shinui reshus*. The standard *halacha* is that *ye'ush* alone does not transfer ownership to the *gazlan*. The *gazlan* who steals and then the owner despairs — the *gazlan* still does not own it; he still has an obligation to return it. What does work is *ye'ush* followed by a transfer to a third party who receives it after *ye'ush* has taken effect. It is that combination — *ye'ush* in one's hands, *shinui reshus* when transferring to another — that creates clean ownership for the recipient. This is exactly what the Gemara describes with the language *ye'ush be'alim b'yadaihu* and *shinui reshus b'yadaichem*. The *ye'ush* takes effect when they — the non-Jews — hold it; the *shinui reshus* happens when it passes into the merchants' hands. At that point the merchants acquire it fully. Notably, this *Gemara* does not invoke the language of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*, which is consistent with the possibility raised earlier that the invalidity of *gazul* in the context of the *lulav* may be entirely a function of ownership rather than of an independent principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. If ownership is the only concern, then once the merchant genuinely owns the *hadasim* at the moment he takes them, they may be used for the *mitzvah* without any further concern — regardless of what their history was before *ye'ush*.

A question arises in this context about the permissibility of purchasing goods that are reasonably assumed to be stolen at all — quite apart from the *mitzvah* application. The Gemara in Bava Kamma addresses this explicitly: purchasing objects one has reason to believe are stolen is itself prohibited, because doing so creates a market for the *ganav* and *gazlan* and renders the buyer complicit in the ongoing cycle of theft. That concern is a separate layer from the question of whether one acquires valid title after the fact. The scenario here involves a particular type of land seizure — governmental or large-scale — that does not fit neatly into the paradigm of someone stealing in order to sell *hadasim* to the Jewish community, and that distinction may be relevant. Nevertheless, the issue is real and should not be dismissed.

## Shinui Ma'aseh Debate

### Binding as Shinui

The Gemara now raises a further avenue by which the merchant might acquire ownership of the *hadasim*, independent of *ye'ush ve'shinui reshus*. The principle of *shinui ma'aseh* — a physical change to a stolen object — is another recognized mechanism by which a *gazlan* can acquire ownership. When the *gazlan* so substantially transforms the object that it is no longer recognizable as the original, that transformation severs the original owner's claim and the new identity of the object belongs to the one who created it. Could the merchant acquire the *hadasim* through *shinui ma'aseh* — specifically by binding them together into a *lulav* bundle?

### Change Reverts

The Gemara dismisses this on two grounds. First, *kasavar lulav ein tzarich eged* — the *lulav* does not actually require binding; the binding is not a *halachic* necessity. If so, the act of tying the *hadasim* into the *lulav* bundle does not create a new *halachic* identity for the object and cannot serve as a meaningful *shinui*. Second, even granting that the *lulav* does require *eged* for its validity, and that there is therefore some formal transformation occurring — *shinui hachozer livriyaso hu*: it is a change that reverts to its original form. After *Sukkot*, the *lulav* bundle is undone and the *hadas* branches return to being simple *hadasim*, indistinguishable from what they were before. A change that reverts is, by definition, not a permanent transformation of identity.

### Shinui Hachozer

The Gemara states the general principle explicitly: *shinui hachozer livriyaso lo shemei shinui* — a change that reverts to its original form is not considered a true *shinui*. The rationale is clear: the legal mechanism of *shinui* as a mode of acquisition depends on a genuine and lasting transformation of the object's identity. When the object will eventually revert — when the *hadas* branch will once again just be a *hadas* branch after the holiday — no lasting change has been effected and no new identity has been created. The temporary function the object serves during *Sukkot* does not alter what it fundamentally is.

### Shinui HaShem

The Gemara then considers a different type of change altogether: *shinui hashem*, a change of name or identity. Even without any physical transformation, could the object acquire a new identity — and thereby new ownership — simply by virtue of being called by a different name? The specific question is: before *Sukkot*, the branch was an *asa* — a myrtle branch, identified by its botanical name. Once *Sukkot* arrives and the branch is designated for the *mitzvah*, it is no longer called an *asa* — it is now a *hoshana*. It acquires a new name by virtue of its ritual function. Does this renaming constitute a *shinui hashem* sufficient to effect a change of ownership and allow the merchant to use it for the *mitzvah*?

### Name Existed Before

The Gemara rejects this as well. *Me'ikara nami le'asa hoshana kari lei* — in fact, people were calling the myrtle branch a *hoshana* even before it was cut, even before *Sukkot* arrived. As the month of Tishrei approaches, what was known a month earlier simply as a willow is now being called an *arava*, and what was simply a myrtle is already being called a *hadas* or *hoshana*. The renaming precedes the cutting. Since the name *hoshana* was already in use before the relevant moment — before the branch became detached from the ground and capable of being treated as stolen movable property — there is no point in time at which the name change could constitute a new *shinui hashem* that would effect acquisition. The name was not created by the act of taking; it already existed.

The conclusion, therefore, is that neither *shinui ma'aseh* (physical change) nor *shinui hashem* (change of name) provides a viable mechanism here. The only effective path to clean ownership of the *hadasim* is *ye'ush ve'shinui reshus* — ensuring that the owner's despair takes effect before the merchant takes possession, achieved practically by having the non-Jews cut the *hadasim* first and then selling them to the merchants.
