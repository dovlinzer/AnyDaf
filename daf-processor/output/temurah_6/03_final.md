# Temurah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye vs. Rava Debate

### Core Debate Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא, הַאי ״הוּא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי בְּכוֹר, דְּתַנְיָא: בִּבְכוֹר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה״ וְנִמְכָּר הוּא, בְּמַעֲשֵׂר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ וְאֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר, לֹא חַי וְלֹא שָׁחוּט, לא תָּם וְלֹא בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to** the opinion of **Rava, this** term: **"Is** most holy," serves **to exclude** the case of **a firstborn** offering from the prohibition of sale. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **It is stated with regard to a firstborn** offering: "But the firstling of an ox, or the firstling of a sheep, or the firstling of a goat, **you shall not redeem;** they are holy" (Numbers 18:17). **But** if it develops a blemish **it** may still be **sold.** By contrast, **it is stated with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering: **"It shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33), **and** the animal tithe may **not be sold, not** when **alive and not** when **slaughtered, not** when **unblemished and not** when **blemished.**

The daf for today continues the foundational debate between Abaye and Rava concerning whether a prohibited act nonetheless takes *halachic* effect. Abaye's principle — *ei avid mahani* — holds that even a violation produces its intended *halachic* consequence, while Rava's principle — *ei avid lo mahani* — holds that acting in violation prevents any such effect. The Gemara proceeds by testing this debate against a series of cases, each of which initially appears to vindicate one side, only for the other side to produce a scriptural or logical counter.

### Blemished Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי הִקְדִּישׁ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר בּוֹ מוּם לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״, וּתְנַן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But isn't there** the case of one who **consecrated blemished** animals **for** sacrifice on **the altar,** with regard to **which the Merciful One states:** "But **whatsoever has a blemish, that you shall not bring;** for it shall not be acceptable for you" (Leviticus 22:20)? **And we learned** in a *baraita*: If **one consecrates blemished** animals **for** sacrifice **on the altar, even though he has** transgressed **a prohibition, what he did is done,** and the consecration takes effect. This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rava.**

The Gemara opens with the case of one who sanctifies a blemished animal for the altar: *hikdish ba'al mum l'gabbei mizbe'ach*. The Torah states *kol asher bo mum lo takrivu* — anything with a blemish you shall not offer up. As the Gemara will later explain at length, this prohibition is understood to encompass not merely placing the animal on the altar but the initial act of sanctification itself. A Mishnah is cited: *hamakdish ba'al mum l'gabbei mizbe'ach, af al pi she'hu b'lo ta'aseh, mah she'asa asuy* — if one sanctifies a blemished animal for the altar, even though he acts in violation, what is done is done. The sanctification takes effect.

### Abaye's Position

This *Mishnah* seems to encapsulate Abaye's position: *ei avid mahani*. Even a prohibited act produces its *halachic* result. The force of this proof is considerable — we have not merely a theoretical principle but a direct *Mishnaic* ruling that the sanctification holds despite the prohibition.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וּלְנֶדֶר לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ — רִצּוּי הוּא דְּלָא מְרַצֵּה, הָא מִיקְדָּשׁ קָדְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rava** could **say to you: It is different there, as the verse states** with regard to blemished animals: **"But for a vow it shall not be accepted"** (Leviticus 22:23). Since the verse specifies only **that it is** its sacrifice **which does not effect acceptance,** one may **consequently** infer that if one **consecrates** them, **they are** still **consecrated.** But in general, transgressions are not effective.

The Gemara raises this as a *tiyuvta d'Rava* — a refutation of Rava. Rava responds: *shani hatam d'amar kra*, there it is different because we have a special verse. The verse states *u'l'neder lo yeratzeh* — it will not be acceptable as a vow offering. Rava's reading is precise: the verse must say that it will not be acceptable as a *korban* because the sanctification did take hold. Had the sanctity not taken effect at all, there would be no need to specify that it cannot be brought as an offering — it would simply be an ordinary animal. The very necessity of the verse implies that the sanctification succeeded, and it then adds the further restriction that the animal may not be brought to the altar. Thus Rava concedes the effect here, but attributes it to a special scriptural teaching rather than to any general principle.

Abaye, in turn, requires the verse for a different reason. Without *u'l'neder lo yeratzeh*, one might have thought: since the sanctification takes effect (*ei avid mahani*), and the animal is now *kodesh*, perhaps it may still be brought as a *korban*. The verse therefore comes to teach that although the sanctity holds, the animal remains prohibited from being offered. Each Amora thus uses the verse, but for opposite purposes: according to Rava, the verse is the source of the exceptional ruling that the sanctification works at all; according to Abaye, the verse is needed only to forestall the conclusion that the animal may now be sacrificed.

## Temple Treasury Case

### Mirror Case Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נְדָבָה תַּעֲשֶׂה אֹתוֹ״ — זֶה קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, וּתְנַן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ תְּמִימִין לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** "Either a bull or a lamb that has anything too long or too short, **that may you offer for a gift;** but for a vow it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 22:23). **This** verse teaches that only blemished animals may be **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance. And we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who consecrates unblemished** animals for **Temple maintenance, even though he has** transgressed **a prohibition, what he did is done.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rava.**

The Gemara now examines what amounts to the mirror image of the previous case. The earlier discussion concerned sanctifying a blemished animal *for the altar* — an animal unfit for a *korban* being designated as one. Now the Gemara turns to sanctifying an unblemished animal *for the temple treasury* (*bedek ha-bayit*) — an animal perfectly fit to be a *korban* being diverted to a different sacred purpose instead. This case is structurally parallel but ethically somewhat less acute: whereas sanctifying a blemished animal for the altar is doubly inappropriate (the animal is unfit and the designation is wrong), sanctifying an unblemished animal for the *bedek ha-bayit* is merely a misallocation of a perfectly serviceable animal.

### Applied to Treasury

The verse at the top of 6a reads: *nedavah ta'aseh oto u'l'neder lo yeratzeh* — make it a donation, it will not be accepted as a vow offering. The first half of the verse addresses a blemished animal: present it as a donation to the treasury, not as a *korban*. By implication, the reverse is also true: an unblemished animal should be designated as a *korban*, not given to the treasury. This implicit prohibition is what *Chazal* derive, and a corresponding teaching is cited: *hamakdish tmimim l'bedek ha-bayit, af al pi she'hu b'lo ta'aseh, mah she'asa asuy* — if one sanctifies unblemished animals for the treasury, even though he acts in violation, what is done is done. Here too, the designation takes effect. The Gemara again raises this as a *tiyuvta d'Rava*.

### Unified Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ [רָבָא]: מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁנִּתְרַבּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, נִתְרַבּוּ תְּמִימִין לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rava** could **say to you: From the** same **place** that it was derived earlier **that blemished** animals **are included,** i.e., that their consecration **for** sacrifice **on the altar** is effective after the fact, it is likewise derived that **unblemished** animals **are included,** that their consecration **for Temple maintenance** is effective. But in general, transgressions are not effective.

Rava answers by pointing to the unity of the verse. The explicit half of the verse — prohibiting blemished animals for the altar while permitting them for the treasury — was already resolved: the verse had to specify that the sanctification takes effect despite the prohibition. Now the implicit reverse — prohibiting unblemished animals for the treasury — is derived from the same verse: *mimakom shenisrabu ba'alei mumim l'gabbei mizbe'ach nisrabu tmimim l'bedek ha-bayit*. Since the explicit prohibition, though violated, nonetheless produces *halachic* effect (as established by the verse), the same logic applies to the implicit prohibition derived from the same verse. The two cases are inseparable: whatever the verse teaches about the explicit case applies equally to the implicit one. Rava thus accounts for both rulings without abandoning his general principle; both are exceptional cases grounded in a single scriptural source.

## Practical Examples

### Challenge to Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּשַׁנִּינַן כֹּל הָנֵי שִׁינּוּיֵי, דְּאַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: And now that we have given all of these answers,** explaining that Abaye and Rava concede to one another in all the above cases, **with regard to what** cases do **Abaye and Rava disagree?**

With each case having been either explained away by a special verse (according to Rava) or accounted for naturally (according to Abaye), the Gemara raises the obvious challenge: now that you have answered every one of these cases with these answers, what exactly do Abaye and Rava disagree about? This is a recognizable pattern in the Gemara, where an apparently sweeping principled debate ultimately yields very little *nafka mina* once the individual cases are examined. A comparable example appears in the second *perek* of Pesachim, where the debate between *Chizkiyah* and *Rabbi Abbahu* over whether *lo tochal* means only "do not eat" or also "do not derive benefit" proceeds through every applicable case until the Gemara similarly asks: what then is the difference?

A student raises the case of a *kohen* who marries a *grushah* in violation of his priestly status — does the marriage take effect? The answer is yes, the marriage does take effect. The Gemara yesterday employed a special verse to reconcile this with Rava's position. The personal and social consequences of such a marriage are notable: the man incurs *malkos*, the child is a *chalal*, and at the societal level the *kohen* is effectively stripped of his active priestly standing for as long as he remains in the marriage — as close to non-*kohen* status as is possible without formally losing the designation. A *chalal* is one who has lost his *kehunah* standing; a *chalalah* may not marry a *kohen*.

### Theft & Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי גָּזֵל, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״לֹא תִגְזוֹל״, וּתְנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל עֵצִים וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים, צֶמֶר וַעֲשָׂאָן בְּגָדִים — מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵילָה, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But there is** the case of **robbery,** with regard to **which the Merciful One states:** "You shall not oppress you neighbor, and **you shall not rob** him" (Leviticus 19:13), **and we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 93b): **One who robs** another of **wood and fashions it** into **vessels,** or one who robs another of **wool and fashions it** into **garments, pays** the victim **according to** the value of the goods at **the time of the robbery,** but he need not return the vessels or garments, since by changing the stolen items he acquired them. If he can acquire the stolen item, this is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rava.**

The Gemara next considers the case of theft (*gezel*): *lo tigzol* — do not steal. If one steals wood and fashions it into vessels, or steals wool and makes it into garments, the ruling (*ha-gozel etzim ve-asaan keilim, tzemer ve-asaan begadim, meshalem ki-sh'at ha-gezeilah*) is that he pays the value of what he stole at the time of the theft — he does not return the finished goods. He has in some sense acquired the transformed object. This appears to be a *tiyuvta d'Rava*: the prohibited act of theft produced a *halachic* consequence, namely a form of ownership.

### Quasi-Ownership Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״ — כְּמָה שֶׁגָּזַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rava** could **say to you: It is different there, as the verse states:** "He shall restore the stolen item **that he took by robbery"** (Leviticus 5:23). This teaches that he must return the original item only if it is still **like** that **which he took by robbery** and has not been altered. But in general, transgressions are not effective.

The *halachic* mechanism underlying this case merits attention. When one steals an object, certain authorities understand that at the moment of theft the thief acquires a form of quasi-ownership known as *kinyanei gneivah* or *kinyanei gezeilah*. He does not hold full legal title — the object is not *de jure* his — but he has *de facto* control, and this creates limited rights in the object. One well-known consequence: if someone borrows an object without permission, he must compensate the owner for use. But if he stole it, his *kinyanei gezeilah* means he need not pay for the use during the period of possession; he need only return or compensate for the object itself.

Now, when the object changes — when the thief transforms the stolen wood into vessels — ownership of the transformed object may be understood in one of two ways. Either the act of transformation itself constitutes an act of acquisition, rendering it his through the force of that physical change; or the act of change does not independently confer ownership, but rather removes the final connection between the new object and its original owner. On this second understanding, what actually makes it the thief's is the *kinyanim* he acquired at the moment of theft — those rights were always there, but the object retained a residual connection to the original owner so long as it remained identifiably the same thing. Once the transformation is complete, only the thief's original rights remain, and the object becomes fully his. The relevance to the Abaye–Rava debate now becomes clearer: if *ei avid mahani*, then the act of theft — itself a violation — can confer those initial quasi-ownership rights, and the subsequent change ripens them into full ownership. If *ei avid lo mahani*, then the theft confers no such rights and the *shinui* is legally ineffective.

### Pledge Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מַשְׁכּוֹן, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״לֹא תָבֹא אֶל בֵּיתוֹ לַעֲבוֹט עֲבוֹטוֹ״, וּתְנַן: מַחֲזִיר אֶת הַכַּר בַּלַּיְלָה וְאֶת הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה בַּיּוֹם — תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But there is** the case of **collateral,** with regard to **which the Merciful One states:** "When you lend your neighbor any manner of loan, **you shall not go into his house to fetch his pledge"** (Deuteronomy 24:10), teaching that a creditor may not seize collateral if the debtor delays payment. **And we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 113a): If the creditor nevertheless seized two items as collateral, **he returns a mattress by night,** as the debtor requires it for sleeping, **and a plow,** which is needed for his daytime work, **by day.** The creditor may keep possession of seized collateral when it is not being used, which is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rava.**

The Gemara turns next to the case of seizing a *mashkon* — a pledge or collateral — without permission. The creditor has a right to a pledge, but only through the proper legal channels: either the debtor willingly hands it over or *beis din* authorizes its seizure. If the creditor walks into the debtor's home and takes the *mashkon* on his own initiative, he has acted in violation. The question is whether his possession of the *mashkon* nonetheless carries any legal standing.

### Daily Return Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״הָשֵׁב תָּשִׁיב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rava** could **say to you: It is different there, as the verse states:** "And if he be a poor man, you shall not sleep with his pledge; **you shall restore [*hashev tashiv*]** to him the pledge" (Deuteronomy 24:12–13). The repetition of the verb teaches that the creditor must return the collateral many times, e.g., he must return a plow each morning and take it back each night, and return a mattress each night and take it back each morning, but he does not have to return the collateral permanently. But in general, transgressions are not effective.

The Torah mandates daily return of certain pledges — a cloak taken as collateral must be returned each night. The double language of *hashev tashiv* — "you shall surely return" — extends this obligation to the case of a *mashkon* seized without permission. Rava cites this as his special verse: *shani hatam d'amar kra hashev tashiv*. Had the Torah not said this, one might have concluded: the creditor acted outside the law by seizing the *mashkon* unlawfully, and since he acted outside the law, perhaps the daily return obligation no longer applies. The verse therefore comes to tell us that even a *mashkon* seized in violation must be returned daily — and the implication of that ruling is that the creditor retains possession (and his associated rights) every alternate period.

Abaye, who would have said the creditor retains rights even without the verse, needs *hashev tashiv* for a different reason. Had the Torah not said it, one might have thought: since the initial act was in violation, *ei avid mahani*, the creditor does have rights, but there is no *mitzvah* to return it. He may keep it as long as he likes. The verse comes to teach that even though the initial seizure was unlawful, the creditor must now return it daily. This is a striking Gemara: without it, we would have intuitively sided with Rava — one who marches into a debtor's house and takes his property unlawfully should surely have no legal standing. Yet the Gemara teaches otherwise, and the framework of *kinyanei gezeilah* and *ei avid mahani* explains why.

### Change & Ownership

Regarding Tosafos's brief observation here: when one steals an object and it subsequently undergoes *shinui* — a significant change — the question of whether the thief thereby acquires title is, as Rashi notes, a question whose emphasis lies on the limits of that principle. Tosafos reads the case somewhat differently and will be addressed more fully below.

### Pe'ah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי פֵּאָה, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״לֹא תְכַלֶּה פְּאַת שָׂדְךָ בְּקוּצְרֶךָ״, וּתְנַן: מִצְוַת פֵּאָה לְהַפְרִישׁ מִן הַקָּמָה, לֹא הִפְרִישׁ מִן הַקָּמָה — יַפְרִישׁ מִן הָעֳמָרִים, לֹא הִפְרִישׁ מִן הָעֳמָרִים — יַפְרִישׁ מִן הַכְּרִי עַד שֶׁלֹּא מֵירְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But there is** the case of **produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*],** with regard to **which the Merciful One stated:** And when you reap the harvest of your land, **you shall not wholly reap the corner of your field"** (Leviticus 23:22), but one must leave a corner of the field for the poor. **And we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Pe'a* 1:5): The optimal way to fulfill **the mitzva of *pe'a*** is for the owner **to separate** it **from the standing** grain, i.e., grain that has not been harvested. If **he did not separate** it **from the standing** grain, **he separates** it **from the sheaves** of grain that have already been harvested. If **he did not separate** it **from the sheaves, he separates** it **from the pile** of grain, **as long as he has not** yet **smoothed** the pile.

The Gemara considers the agricultural case of *pe'ah* — the corner of the field left for the poor. The Torah states *lo techaleh pe'at sadecha be-kotzrecha* — do not cut the *pe'ah* when you harvest. Alongside the positive obligation to leave the corner, there is a *lo ta'asei* against harvesting it. A Mishnah teaches: the *mitzvah* of *pe'ah* is ideally fulfilled from the standing grain; if one did not do so, he may designate it from the bundled grain; if not then, from the winnowed pile (*kari*), up until the grain has been smoothed over (*meiruach*). After *meiruach*, he takes *ma'aser* first and then gives *pe'ah* to the poor — this because at that stage the grain has become obligated in *terumos u-ma'asros*. *Pe'ah* given before *meiruach* is exempt from *ma'aser*; after *meiruach*, the *ma'aser* must be separated first so that the poor receive their *pe'ah* free of obligation.

### Double Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵירְחוֹ — מְעַשְּׂרוֹ וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ; מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ: אַף מַפְרִישׁ מִן הָעִיסָּה, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְאַבָּיֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Once **he smooths** the pile of grain, it becomes obligated in tithes. Therefore, **he** must first **tithe** the grain **and** then **give** a portion of the produce **to** the poor as *pe'a*, so that the poor need not tithe what they receive. Additionally, the Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** If he did not separate *pe'a* during any of these stages, and he milled the grain and kneaded it into dough, **he separates** *pe'a* **even from the dough** and gives it to the poor. Even if the owner harvested the grain, the *pe'a* is still not considered his, which is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Abaye.**

Rabbi Yishmael goes further, ruling that one must still give *pe'ah* even after the grain has been turned into dough — even after a *shinui* that might have terminated the obligation. Rashi reads the emphasis of this ruling as falling on Rabbi Yishmael's position: even after significant change, the *pe'ah* obligation persists, suggesting *ei avid lo mahani* — your act of harvesting did not successfully terminate the obligation. The *tiyuvta* here falls on Abaye.

Abaye responds with the double language of *ta'azov ta'azov* — "you shall surely leave" — appearing twice in the verses concerning gifts to the poor. This extra language teaches that even where one would otherwise find a way to escape the obligation (through *shinui* or through the technique of ownerless declaration), the doubled language forecloses that escape and insists on fulfillment.

### Ownerless Vineyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ אַבַּיֵּי: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״תַּעֲזוֹב״, ״תַּעֲזוֹב״ יַתִּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Abaye** could **say to you: It is different there, as the verse states: "You shall leave** them for the poor and for the stranger" (Leviticus 19:10), and subsequently, it again states: **"You shall leave** them for the poor and for the stranger" (Leviticus 23:22). The **superfluous** phrase teaches that the owner must give *pe'a* in any event, even if he harvests the grain and kneads it into dough. But in general, transgressions are effective.

Rava, who already holds that one cannot escape obligations through violation, uses the doubled *ta'azov* differently. He derives from it an additional *halacha*: *yesh lach aziva acheret k'zo* — there is another "leaving" like this one. The case is *mafkir karmo*: one who declares his vineyard ownerless (*hefker*) at night, then rises at dawn and retakes possession before harvesting. A *baraita* teaches that in such a case he is still obligated in all the agricultural gifts to the poor (*peret*, *olelot*, *shichecha*, and *pe'ah*), because the double language prevents this technical *hefker* from serving as an exemption. However, he is exempt from *ma'aser*, because by *ma'aser* no such doubled language exists. The *pe'ah* case is thus somewhat unlike the others in this series: the other cases involve *halachic* acts whose *halachic* status is in question — sanctifying, divorcing, seizing, stealing — whereas the *pe'ah* case is closer to the question of whether the doer benefits from his sin (*chotei niskar*). The harvesting itself does not generate a *halachic* change of status in the grain; it simply raises the question of whether the *mitzvah* is terminated by the violation, which sits somewhat more awkwardly in the framework of Abaye and Rava.

## Shinui Koneh Debate (II)

### Real Difference Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: יֵשׁ לְךָ עֲזִיבָה אַחֶרֶת שֶׁכָּזוֹ, וְאֵיזוֹ? זֶה הַמַּפְקִיר כַּרְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava,** who maintains that in general transgressions are not effective, could **say to you** that the extra verse teaches that **there is another** type of **leaving** over one's grain **that is like this. And what is that? It is** the case of **one who renounces ownership of his vineyard.**

After exhausting the list of cases and finding that each has been answered by a special verse or textual argument, the Gemara poses the question directly: *be-mai paligi?* — what do Abaye and Rava actually disagree about? The answer given is *be-shinui koneh* — the question of whether change confers ownership on the thief. This case, drawn from the discussions in Bava Kamma, is presented as one where the debate is not driven by a *pasuk* but by *sevara* — pure legal reasoning. There, the question of whether a *gazlan*'s act of transformation of the stolen object gives him title is a genuine dispute not resolved by any particular verse, and it is therefore a proper arena for the principles of Abaye and Rava to operate. If *ei avid mahani*, the theft confers quasi-ownership rights that the *shinui* then ripens; if *ei avid lo mahani*, the theft confers no such rights and the *shinui* is legally ineffective. So, after this entire discussion, the actual *nafka mina* of the debate between Abaye and Rava reduces to this one relatively obscure question in Bava Kamma.

## Ribit Case

### Ribit Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרִבִּית קְצוּצָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי, וְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רִבִּית קְצוּצָה יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין, אֲבַק רִבִּית אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ רִבִּית קְצוּצָה אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi answered: **They disagree with regard to fixed [*ketzutza*] interest, and** their dispute is **like** that **of Rabbi Elazar** and Rabbi Yoḥanan. **As Rabbi Elazar said:** If a debtor paid **fixed interest** and petitions the court to have it returned to him, since the Torah prohibits the charging of interest, it **is repossessed** from the creditor **by** the **judges** of the court. If a debtor paid **a hint of interest** and petitions the court to have it returned to him, it **is not repossessed** from the creditor **by** the **judges** of the court. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even fixed interest is not repossessed by** the **judges.**

The Gemara introduces the case of *ribit ketzutzah* — explicit, fixed interest on a loan — as another arena for the Abaye–Rava debate. Rav Acha the son of Rava poses the question to Rav Ashi: now that you have answered all those answers, what is the *nafka mina* between Abaye and Rava? He suggests that they disagree regarding *ribit ketzutzah*. The case is arresting in its structure: unlike *gezel*, where property is taken against the owner's will, *ribit* is a contractual arrangement entered into willingly by both parties. The lender charges fixed interest; the borrower agrees to pay it. Yet the entire arrangement violates a *lo ta'asei*. The question is whether the contractual obligation thus created is binding or whether the violation strips it of legal force.

### Elazar vs. Yochanan

Rabbi Elazar holds that *ribit ketzutzah yotzah bedayanim* — fixed interest is extracted by the courts; the courts will compel return of the interest. *Avak ribit* — the "dust of interest," referring to indirect forms of financial advantage that are rabbinically prohibited but not explicit biblical *ribit* — does not come out through the courts (*einah yotzah bedayanim*). Rabbi Yochanan holds that even *ribit ketzutzah* does not come out through the courts: *afilu ribit ketzutzah einah yotzah bedayanim*. This dispute maps directly onto the Abaye–Rava debate: if *ei avid mahani*, the contract is binding, and if binding, the interest need not be returned; if *ei avid lo mahani*, the contract lacks force and the courts may demand the money back.

### Interest Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם בִּסְבָרָא פְּלִיגִי? הָתָם בִּקְרָאֵי פְּלִיגִי! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״בַּנֶּשֶׁךְ נָתַן וְתַרְבִּית לָקַח וָחָי לֹא יִחְיֶה״, לְחַיִּים נִיתַּן וְלֹא לְהִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa **said to** Rav Ashi: **There,** in the dispute concerning fixed interest, do Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree with regard to logical reasoning,** i.e., the principle of whether transgressions are effective? Not so; rather, **they disagree there with regard to** the interpretation of the **verses, as Rabbi Yitzḥak said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan? The verse states: "[He] has given forth upon interest, and has taken increase; shall he then live? He shall not live;** he has done all these abominations; he shall be put to death; his blood shall be upon him" (Ezekiel 18:13). Apparently, a usurer **is subject** to divine punishment **for** his **life, but** the interest is **not** subject **to returning.** This is the source for the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The Gemara then examines what verses each position relies upon. Rabbi Yitzchak explains Rabbi Yochanan's position: a verse in Yechezkel states *be-neshech natan ve-tarbit lakach ve-chai lo yichyeh* — he gave his money at interest, took back increase, and shall not live. The Gemara reads this as *le-chayim nitan ve-lo le-hashavon* — the consequence of *ribit* is that it endangers the lender's life, but it does not create an obligation of repayment. The money is not considered stolen; no *hashavah* — return — is mandated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵּאֱלֹהֶיךָ... אֲנִי ה׳״ — לְמוֹרָא נְתַתִּיו, וְלֹא לְהִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Adda said** that the source for the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan is **from here:** "Take no interest of him or increase, **but fear your God,** that your brother may live with you. You shall not give him your money upon interest, nor give him your provisions for increase; **I am the Lord"** (Leviticus 25:36–38). In effect, God is saying: **I have made** a usurer **subject to** punishment for insufficient **fear** of Me, **but** the interest is **not** subject **to returning.**

Rav Adda bar Ahava derives the same conclusion from a different verse: *ve-yareta me'Elohecha Ani Hashem*, said in the context of *ribit* laws — the consequence is *mora*, fear of divine judgment, but not legal repayment. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״הַתּוֹעֵבוֹת הָאֵל עָשָׂה מוֹת יוּמָת דָּמָיו בּוֹ יִהְיֶה״, ״וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן פָּרִיץ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּם״ — הוּקְּשׁוּ מַלְוֵי רִבִּית לְשׁוֹפְכֵי דָמִים: מָה שׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לְהִישָּׁבוֹן, אַף מַלְוֵי רִבִּית לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לְהִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that the source for the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan is **from here:** "Has lent at advance interest, or exacted accrued interest; shall he live? He shall not live! **He has done all these abominations; he shall be put to death; his blood shall be upon him"** (Ezekiel 18:13). And an earlier verse states: **"If he fathers a son that is a robber, a shedder of blood"** (Ezekiel 18:10). **Usurers are** thereby **juxtaposed to shedders of blood,** i.e., murderers: **Just as** the sins of **shedders of blood cannot be undone, so too,** the sins of **usurers cannot be undone.**

Rava derives it from yet another verse in Yechezkel: *ha-to'evot ha'eleh asah, mot yumot, damav bo yihyeh*, followed by a description of the wicked son who is a murderer. From the juxtaposition, *hukshu malvei ribit le-shofchei damim* — lenders of *ribit* are compared to murderers. Just as a murderer's act cannot be undone — *mah shofech damim lo nitan le-hishiv* — so too *ribit* once collected need not be returned: *af malveh ribbis lo nitan le-hishiv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְחֵי אָחִיךָ עִמָּךְ״ — אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵיחֵי עִמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Elazar? As the verse states:** "Take no interest of him or increase; but fear your God, **that your brother may live with you"** (Leviticus 25:36). The verse teaches that one must **return** the interest **to** the debtor **so that he will** be able to **live** together **with you.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains Rabbi Elazar's position: *de'amar kra ve-chai achicha imach* — the verse says "your brother shall live with you." This is understood as: *ahadar lei ki heichi de-nichi imach* — return it to him so that he can get along with you. There is thus a separate *mitzvah* to restore the relationship by giving back the interest.

### Binding Contract

What is particularly striking here is that even according to Rabbi Elazar, who holds that *ribit* must be returned, the legal basis is not that the contract was void from the start or that the money is considered stolen. Rather, the contract is fundamentally binding — the lender is contractually entitled to the interest — but a separate *mitzvah* of *ve-chai achicha imach* imposes an obligation to return it as a moral-*halachic* gesture. This reading has concrete *halachic* consequences: the Gemara in Bava Kamma establishes that if the lender dies, his sons are not obligated to return the *ribit*. A debt arising from a void or stolen transaction would bind the heirs; an obligation arising from a separate *mitzvah* imposed personally on the transgressor does not. The sons inherit money to which their father was contractually entitled, and the personal *mitzvah* to restore harmony — *ve-chai achicha imach* — did not pass to them.

This means that both sides effectively agree that the *ribit* contract is binding as a matter of monetary law, which reflects the approach of Abaye. The fact that *ribit* laws appear in the *Shulchan Aruch* in *Yoreh Deah* (the realm of *issur ve-heter*) rather than in *Choshen Mishpat* (monetary law) — though the *Rambam* places them in *Hilchos Malveh Ve-Loveh* — reflects precisely this ambiguity: the prohibition is ritual in character, but the underlying transaction remains a binding contract. The broader principle, that a contract entered into in violation of a Torah prohibition is nonetheless enforceable, finds support across the *Rishonim*. The case of *esnan zona* — where the Torah grants *esnan* status even to payment made in exchange for relations with one's mother, the most severe of *arayos* — illustrates that even a transaction embedded in the gravest violation is recognized as a form of payment and not merely a gift.

One might ask whether *beis din* would enforce such a contract by compelling performance. The cases discussed here generally involve situations where the party holding the money willfully fulfills (or refuses to fulfill) the terms; no one would dream of summoning *beis din* to collect *ribit*. Whether *beis din* would actively enforce a contract made in violation of Torah law is a separate question, and one with significant implications.

## Shinui Koneh Debate (II)

### Rava's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא? בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this matter is not a consequence of the dispute of Abaye and Rava, **with regard to what** case **do Abaye and Rava disagree?** The Gemara answers: There is perhaps no practical dispute between them, and they disagree only **with regard to** the theoretical question of whether **deviation** from what is stated in the Torah is **effective.** According to the opinion of Abaye, if there is no verse teaching otherwise, a transgression is presumed to be effective. According to the opinion of Rava, it is presumed to be not effective. They, therefore, disagree as to which *halakhot* reflect the rule and which are exceptions.

The Gemara returns to the core question: *ve'ela be-mai paligi, be-shinui koneh*. Rashi explains the connection: earlier in the *sugya*, the case of a thief who transforms the stolen object was raised. In Bava Kamma there is an actual debate about whether *shinui koneh* — whether the change confers title on the thief. That debate is not resolved by any verse; it is a pure question of *sevara*. According to Abaye — *ei avid mahani* — the original act of theft generates *kinyanei gezeilah*, and the subsequent *shinui* completes the acquisition. According to Rava — *ei avid lo mahani* — the theft generates no such rights, and the *shinui* therefore cannot transform a nullity into ownership. It is here, in this case in Bava Kamma, that the debate between Abaye and Rava has genuine, verse-independent traction. The weight of evidence throughout the *sugya* points substantially toward Abaye's position — case after case is ruled in accordance with *ei avid mahani*, and even the *ribit* analysis ultimately converges on the bindingness of the contract. Yet the Gemara, with characteristic economy, locates the actual operative difference in this one obscure corner of *dinei geneivah*.

## Lishna Achrina

### Alternative Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא, בְּהָנֵי שִׁינּוּיֵי דְּשַׁנִּינַן, בְּרִבִּית קְצוּצָה — לְאַבָּיֵי לָא מַהְדַּר רִבִּית, לְרָבָא מַהְדַּר רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of the answer to this question: In accordance **with the answers we answered** above, Abaye and Rava disagree **with regard to fixed interest. According to** the opinion of **Abaye** that transgressions are effective, the creditor **does not** need to **return** the **interest,** as he has acquired it. But **according to** the opinion of **Rava** that transgressions are not effective, the creditor must **return** the **interest,** as it still belongs to the debtor.

The Gemara introduces at this point the formulation *lishna achrina* — an alternative version — before restating the conclusion about *ribit ketzutzah* and *shinui koneh*. This phenomenon deserves attention. The *lishna achrina* in our *masechta* is not merely an occasional textual variant; it is a recurrent feature that signals something linguistically distinctive about *Temurah*.

### Rashi's Analysis

Rashi, in his commentary here, identifies the *lishna achrina* as a specimen of *lashon Yerushalmi* — the language of the Yerushalmi. He notes: *ela zeh Yerushalmi ve-zeh Bavli* — one version is Yerushalmi and one is Bavli. The two versions are functionally identical in content — *uvein hai lishna le-lishna de-shami'a lei be-shinui koneh leika midi*, there is no substantive difference between them regarding *shinui koneh* — but their linguistic character differs.

### Scholarly Background

This observation is illuminated by the standard scholarly introduction to the Talmud, Strack and Stemberger's *Introduction to the Talmud and Midrash*, which surveys the linguistic and grammatical peculiarities of certain tractates. Five tractates — Nedarim, Nazir, Me'ila, Kerisot, and Tamid — differ from the rest of the Bavli in a range of linguistic and grammatical features, a phenomenon already noted by Tosafos. These tractates are characterized by what the authors call "extraordinary" or linguistically anomalous material, and the *lishna achrina* sections of Temurah fall into the same category.

### Temurah's Status

Temurah itself is specifically identified as belonging to this group by virtue of its *lishna achrina* passages, which exhibit not merely terminological differences but grammatical peculiarities characteristic of Palestinian Aramaic rather than standard Babylonian Aramaic. Rashi's identification of this language as *Yerushalmi* thus reflects a genuine philological reality.

The scholarly debate over the origin of this phenomenon is itself instructive. One view, associated with Yitzchak Halevi, proposes that these tractates were genuinely more studied in *Eretz Yisrael* than in Bavel, based on geonic testimony that Nedarim was not taught after *Yehudai Gaon*. On this account, the tractates were transmitted and shaped largely in a Palestinian milieu, and their language reflects that environment. The more commonly accepted contemporary view holds that the difference is one of editorial geography within Babylonia itself: while most of the Bavli was redacted in Sura under *Rav Ashi*, these linguistically unusual tractates were redacted in *Pumbedita* or *Nehardea*, whose Aramaic dialect approximates Palestinian Aramaic more closely. On this understanding, the language is Babylonian in provenance but reflects a regional dialect that sounds more "Palestinian" to the ear. The existence of parallel *sugyot* placed side by side — one in each dialect — is itself evidence of a redactional process in which independent transmissions were assembled rather than harmonized.

## Tosafos Question

### Other Cases

Before proceeding, Tosafos raises a question on 6a: now that the Gemara has explained away all the cases and located the *nafka mina* in *shinui koneh*, why does the Gemara not suggest other cases where the debate would have practical traction? Could one not propose, for example, cases where a person took an oath not to perform some otherwise-permitted act and then violated it?

### Oath & Divorce

Tosafos addresses this with a distinction between natural violations and imposed violations. The paradigm case Tosafos raises is: a man swore not to divorce his wife and then proceeded to do so — *nishba she-lo legaresh et ishto ve-achar kach avar ve-geirsha*. Should we say that according to Rava the *get* is invalid, since he acted in violation? Tosafos answers that this type of case does not fall within the scope of Rava's principle. The act of divorce is not inherently prohibited; the man himself artificially imposed a prohibition upon it through his oath. Where the violation arises not from the nature of the act but from an external constraint the person created, Rava would agree that the act takes effect. Rava's principle applies only where the Torah itself prohibits the act as such — where the violation is *natural* to the action — not where the person has constructed his own prohibition on top of an otherwise permissible deed.

Tosafos also raises the question of cases like redeeming a *bechor* or *cheirem* objects — whether there would be a *nafka mina* there — but notes a complication about whether lashes (*malkos*) would apply, which affects how cleanly those cases can serve as examples.

## Blemish Prohibitions

### Five Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בּוֹ מוּם לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אִם בְּ״לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ״ — הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר לְמַטָּה! אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּל תַּקְרִיבוּ״? — ״בַּל תַּקְדִּישׁוּ״. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה שֵׁמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the topic of blemished animals. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Why** must **the verse state: "But whatever has a blemish, you shall not offer;** for it shall not be acceptable for you" (Leviticus 22:20)? **If** it serves to teach that **you may not slaughter** a blemished animal as a sacrifice even if it has been consecrated, **that is** already **stated below,** later on in the passage, as the Gemara will soon explain. **Rather, why** must **the verse state** that **you may not offer** a blemished animal? This serves to teach that **you may not consecrate** it. **From here,** the Sages **stated** (*Tosefta* 1:10): **One who consecrates blemished** animals **for** sacrifice **on the altar violates five** separate **categories** of prohibition.

The Gemara now examines in detail the *halachos* of sanctifying a blemished animal, which was mentioned in passing earlier in the *sugya* and which has structural parallels to *temurah*: in both cases, one performs a prohibited act of sanctification that nonetheless takes *halachic* effect. A *baraita* teaches: *hamakdish ba'alei mumim l'gabbei mizbe'ach, over mishum chamisha sheimos* — one who sanctifies animals with a blemish for the altar transgresses five separate prohibitions.

### Verse Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּקְרִיבוּ״, ״בַּל תַּקְדִּישׁוּ״, ״בַּל תִּשְׁחֲטוּ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תִּזְרְקוּ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּקְטִירוּ כּוּלּוֹ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּקְטִירוּ מִקְצָתוֹ״. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמְרוּ: אַף קַבָּלַת הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** He is liable **for** violating the prohibitions: **You may not offer** a blemished animal as an offering, i.e., **you may not consecrate** it; **you may not slaughter** it; **and for** the prohibition: **You may not sprinkle** its blood; **and for** the prohibition: **You may not burn all of it** on the altar; **and for** the prohibition: **You may not burn part of it.** The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda:** One **also** violates a prohibition against **the collection of the blood.**

The derivation proceeds as follows. The verse *kol asher bo mum lo takrivu* appears to prohibit bringing such animals. A second verse, *iveres o shavur o charutz o yabeles lo takrivu eileh l'Hashem*, also prohibits bringing blemished animals. Since the Torah uses *lo takrivu* twice, each instance is interpreted separately. The first (*kol asher bo mum*) is taken to mean *lo takdishu* — do not sanctify; the second (*iveres o shavur*) is taken to mean *lo tishchatu* — do not slaughter. Thus the first two prohibitions are: (1) do not sanctify a blemished animal, and (2) do not slaughter it as a sacrifice. The verse then adds *ve-isheh lo sitnu meihem* — do not give it as a fire offering. The word *meihem* (from them) extends the prohibition from burning the full set of *eimurim* (innards burned on the altar) to burning even a part: (3) do not burn all of it; (4) do not burn even part of it. Finally, (5) *al ha-mizbe'ach* — do not place it upon the altar — is read as a prohibition against *zerikos dam*, sprinkling the blood, since that is what goes upon the altar.

### Blood Reception

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda adds a sixth prohibition: *af kabbalas ha-dam* — even receiving the blood (the step immediately preceding sprinkling). He reads an additional verse as prohibiting this distinct Temple service.

### Dual Meanings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִם ״בַּל תִּשְׁחֲטוּ״ — הֲרֵי אָמוּר לְמַטָּה. הֵיכָן אָמוּר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַוֶּרֶת אוֹ שָׁבוּר אוֹ חָרוּץ אוֹ יַבֶּלֶת לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ — מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אִם ״בַּל תַּקְדִּישׁוּ״ — הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר לְמַעְלָה, אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּל תַּקְרִיבוּ״? — ״בַּל תִּשְׁחֲטוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects: **The Master said** above: **If** one suggests that the prohibition in Leviticus 22:20 teaches that **you may not slaughter** a blemished animal as an offering, **that is** already **stated below,** later on in the passage. The Gemara asks: **Where is** it **stated?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Why** must **the verse state: "Blind, or broken, or maimed, or having a cyst,** or scabbed, or scurvy, **you shall not offer"** (Leviticus 22:22)? **If** this serves to teach that **you may not consecrate** them, **that is already stated above,** in verse 20. **Rather, why** must **the verse state** that **you may not offer** these animals? This serves to teach that **you may not slaughter** them if they have been consecrated.

The derivation that *lo takrivu* means both "do not sanctify" in one verse and "do not slaughter" in another is arrived at by exclusion: each verse is redundant if interpreted identically to the other. *Kol asher bo mum lo takrivu*, if understood as "do not slaughter," would merely duplicate *iveres o shavur lo takrivu*. Conversely, *iveres o shavur lo takrivu*, if understood as "do not sanctify," would duplicate *kol asher bo mum lo takrivu*. Therefore the two instances must carry distinct meanings: one refers to the initial act of designation (*kedushat ha-guf*) and the other to the Temple service of slaughter.

### Burning Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאִשֶּׁה לֹא תִתְּנוּ מֵהֶם״ — אֵלּוּ אִשִּׁים, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא כּוּלָּן, מִקְצָתָן מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The verse states: **"Nor make an offering by fire of them** upon the altar unto the Lord" (Leviticus 22:22). **These** words teach that one may not burn a blemished animal in the **fires** of the altar. **I have** derived **only** that it is prohibited to burn **all of** the animal. **From where** is it derived that it is prohibited to burn **some** portions **of** the animal? **The verse states: "Of them,"** which prohibits the burning of even part of them.

The dual prohibition regarding burning — both the whole and the part — is derived from the single phrase *ve-isheh lo sitnu meihem*. The word *isheh* refers to the burning of *eimurim* on the altar; the word *meihem* ("from them") extends the prohibition from the totality to any portion. Without *meihem*, one might have thought only a complete burning was prohibited, or conversely that only a partial burning was prohibited; the phrase encompasses both.

### Blood Sprinkling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְרִיקַת דָּמִים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, ״לַה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that **sprinkling the blood** of a blemished animal is prohibited? **The verse states: "Upon the altar,"** thereby indicating that one may not sacrifice any part of it, even its blood, on the altar. The verse continues: **"Unto the Lord,"** which serves **to include the scapegoat** of Yom Kippur in this prohibition, that it may not be blemished even though it is not sacrificed on the altar.

The prohibition of blood sprinkling is derived from *al ha-mizbe'ach* — upon the altar. Since the blood is what is placed upon the altar in the Temple service (*zerikos dam*), this phrase generates a distinct prohibition against sprinkling the blood of a blemished animal.

### Scapegoat Extension

The verse concludes *l'Hashem*, and this phrase is used to extend the prohibition of sanctifying blemished animals to the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* — the scapegoat sent to *Azazel* on Yom Kippur. One might have thought that since the scapegoat is not brought to the altar and is not a burnt offering, the prohibitions of the altar service do not apply to its sanctification. The word *l'Hashem* extends the prohibition of sanctifying a blemished animal even to this designation, since the *sa'ir* too is consecrated "to God" in the broad sense.

## Altar vs. Outside

### Outside Slaughter Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְ״לַה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת הוּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אִי ״קׇרְבָּן״ — שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אַף קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ ״קׇרְבָּן״, כְּעִנְיָן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects: **And** does the term: **"Unto the Lord,"** serve **to include** the scapegoat? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah condemns one who slaughters an offering outside the Temple "and has not brought it unto the door of the Tent of Meeting, to present it as an offering unto the Lord" (Leviticus 17:4)? **If** the verse had stated only **"offering," I** would **derive** that one is liable **even** for slaughtering animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance** outside the Temple, **for they are** also **called an offering, as it is stated: "And we have brought the Lord's offering,** what every man has found: Articles of gold" (Numbers 31:50).

The Gemara now raises a tension between the extension of the prohibition to the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* and a different *halachic* domain: the laws of *shechutei chutz*, the prohibition of slaughtering sacrifices outside the Temple (*Beis HaMikdash*). The Torah states *el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o, le-hakriv korban l'Hashem* — one who did not bring the animal to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting to offer it as a *korban* to God incurs liability for *shechutei chutz*.

### For God Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד וְגוֹ׳״ — הָרָאוּי לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹחֲטֵי חוּץ, שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹחֲטֵי חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And** has not brought it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 17:4). This teaches that **one is liable** only **for** sacrificing an animal **fit to** be sacrificed within the **entrance of the Tent of Meeting** or the Temple **due to** the prohibition of **slaughtering** offerings **outside** the Temple. But **one is not liable for** sacrificing an animal **not fit to** be sacrificed within the **entrance of the Tent of Meeting due to** the prohibition of **slaughtering** offerings **outside** the Temple.

One might have thought that *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* — animals sanctified to the temple treasury — are also subject to *shechutei chutz*, since there are sources that call such animals a *korban* (*va-nakrev et korban Hashem*, said in the context of the Midian booty). The Gemara resolves this by noting that *shechutei chutz* applies only to things *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed* — fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, i.e., proper *korbanos* for the altar. Things not fit for the *ohel moed* are exempt. And as Rashi notes, animals sanctified to *bedek ha-bayit* are by definition blemished animals — since sanctifying unblemished animals to the treasury is itself a violation — and blemished animals are not fit for the altar. Therefore they are exempt from *shechutei chutz* by reason of their unfitness.

### Scapegoat Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא פָּרַת חַטָּאת וְשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, שֶׁהֵן רְאוּיִין לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לַה׳״, מִי שֶׁמְּיוּחָדִין לַה׳, יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין מְיוּחָדִין לַה׳.
>
> **Translation:** **I** might **exclude these** animals consecrated for Temple maintenance, **but not exclude the** red **heifer of purification,** whose ashes are used to purge the impurity imparted by a corpse, **and** the **scapegoat,** despite the fact that neither are sacrificed on the altar, **as they are** unblemished and therefore **fit** to come **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Unto the Lord,"** to teach that one is liable only for **those** animals **that are exclusively the Lord's** as offerings. **These,** the red heifer of purification and the scapegoat, **are excluded, as they are not exclusively the Lord's** as offerings, but rather they serve unique ritual functions. In any event, the term "unto the Lord" is interpreted as excluding, rather than including, the scapegoat.

The Gemara then asks: having excluded *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* by the qualifier *ra'uy le-ohel moed*, what of animals that are technically fit — unblemished — but are not brought as conventional *korbanos* on the altar, such as the *parah adumah* and the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*? One might still include these in *shechutei chutz* since they are unblemished. The Gemara answers: *talmud lomar l'Hashem* — "for God." Only things that are fully and exclusively designated *la-Hashem* are subject to *shechutei chutz*. The *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*, which is sent *la-Azazel*, is not fully designated *la-Hashem* in this sense; nor is the *parah adumah*, which is burned outside the Temple in a procedure oriented differently from the normal service. These are therefore excluded.

### Context Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָתָם, מֵעִנְיָינָא דִּקְרָא, ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח״ — לְרַבּוֹת, ״לַה׳״ — לְהוֹצִיא; הָכָא, דְּ״אִשֶּׁה״ — לְהוֹצִיא, ״לַה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: There,** in the *baraita* discussing offerings slaughtered outside the Temple, the *halakha* is derived **from the context of the verse.** The verse states: **"And** has not brought it **to the entrance** of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:4), which serves **to include** any unblemished animal. Therefore, the following term: **"Unto the Lord,"** serves **to exclude** certain exceptions to the rule. **Here, where** the verse states **"fire"** (Leviticus 22:22), which serves **to exclude** animals not burned on the altar from the prohibition, the term: **"Unto the Lord,"** serves **to include** exceptions to that principle.

This creates an apparent contradiction. In the context of sanctifying blemished animals, the phrase *l'Hashem* was used to *include* the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* — one who sanctifies a blemished animal as the *sa'ir* transgresses the prohibition. But in the context of *shechutei chutz*, the same phrase *l'Hashem* is used to *exclude* the *sa'ir* — one who slaughters it outside the Temple does not incur *shechutei chutz* liability. How can the same phrase include in one context and exclude in another?

Rava resolves the contradiction: *ha ke-di'ita ve-ha ke-di'ita* — each case fits its own context, and *l'Hashem* acts in the direction required by the surrounding logic. By *shechutei chutz*, before we encounter *l'Hashem*, the criterion already established is *ra'uy le-pesach ohel moed*. Given that criterion, the natural inclination is to include the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*, which is unblemished and in principle fit. *L'Hashem* comes to counter that inclination and exclude: since the *sa'ir* is not fully dedicated to God's service on the altar, it is outside the scope of *shechutei chutz*. By the laws of sanctifying blemished animals, the operative term in the verse is *isheh* — a fire offering on the altar. Given that term, the natural inclination is to exclude the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*, since it is never burned on the altar. *L'Hashem* comes to counter that inclination and include: since the *sa'ir* is nevertheless consecrated within the framework of divine service, the prohibition of sanctifying it with a blemish applies. The phrase *l'Hashem* in each case moves against the default assumption established by its immediate context: including where one would have excluded, and excluding where one would have included.
