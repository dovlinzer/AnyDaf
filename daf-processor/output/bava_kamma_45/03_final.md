# Bava Kamma 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shor Haniskal & Sanctifi…

### Intro to Shor Haniskal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל, וְהִקְדִּישׁוֹ בְּעָלָיו – אֵינוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ. שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר. וְאִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ הִקְדִּישׁוֹ בְּעָלָיו – מוּקְדָּשׁ. וְאִם שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox that is leaving** court **to be stoned** for killing a person **and its owner** then **consecrated it, it is not** considered **consecrated,** i.e., the consecration does not take effect, since deriving benefit from the ox is prohibited and the ox is therefore worthless. If one **slaughtered it, its flesh is forbidden** to be eaten and it is prohibited to derive benefit from it. **But if its owner consecrated it before its verdict** the ox **is** considered **consecrated, and if he slaughtered it its flesh is permitted.**

The daf opens by picking up from where we left off at the bottom of 44b. The preceding discussion concerned the *drashot* on the *psukim* relating to *kofer* and whether the identity of the ox's owners matters for the obligation of stoning. The *chachamim* hold that the owners need not bear any culpability for the stoning itself — even a wild ox with no owner would be stoned — while Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the presence of owners is required. With that question resolved, the *mishna* at the bottom of 44b turns to the status of an ox that has become a *shor haniskal* — a condemned ox sentenced to stoning.

The *mishna* introduces its scenario with a striking image: *shor sheyotze leyisakel* — an ox being led out to be executed. As in *Sanhedrin*, where we encounter analogous rules about a person being taken out for execution, so too here we must ask what legal acts remain effective at that moment.

### Post-Judgment Sanctifica…

Once *gmar din* — the final judgment — has been passed and the ox is already being led out for stoning, the *mishna* rules: *eino mukdash* — a declaration of sanctification by the owner does not take effect. The reason is that once *gmar din* has been passed, the ox acquires the status of *issurei hana'ah*, forbidden in benefit. Because one cannot derive any benefit from it, the owner is effectively considered to have lost meaningful control over it, and a dedication to the *Beit Hamikdash* requires the owner to possess that control. Similarly, *shachato besaro assur* — if the ox was slaughtered rather than stoned after *gmar din*, the meat remains forbidden. This follows from the verse *lo ye'achel et besaro*: once *gmar din* has been issued, the flesh retains the status of *shor haniskal* even if the animal was not ultimately stoned.

### Pre-Judgment Sanctificat…

If, however, one performed these acts *before* the *gmar din* — even in the midst of proceedings when the outcome seemed clear — the law is different. *Hikdisho ba'alav mukdash*: a declaration of sanctification does take effect. The court will still proceed to judge the ox and stone it as a *shor haniskal* (except according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that sanctification actually prevents that outcome), but at least in the interim, the sanctification is valid. And *ve'im shachato besaro mutar*: if the ox was slaughtered before *gmar din*, the meat is entirely permissible. This is a meaningful avenue for subverting the court's judgment, since *gmar din* requires the animal to be alive at the time of ruling. Presumably the court would be holding the ox to prevent this, but if it occurred, the ox never attained the status of *shor haniskal* and its meat remains permitted.

## Ox Presence & Return

### Return Before/After Judg…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסָרוֹ לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וּלְשׁוֹאֵל לְנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וּלְשׂוֹכֵר – נִכְנְסוּ תַּחַת הַבְּעָלִים; מוּעָד – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְתָם – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner of an ox **conveyed it to an unpaid bailee, or to a borrower,** or **to a paid bailee, or to a renter,** and it caused damage while in their custody, **they enter** into the responsibilities and liabilities **in place of the owner.** Therefore, if it was **forewarned** the bailee **pays the full** cost of the **damage, and** if it was **innocuous he pays half** the cost of **the damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר שֶׁהָמִית; עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – מְכָרוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר, הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לְבֵית בְּעָלָיו – מוּחְזָר. מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – מְכָרוֹ אֵינוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ אֵינוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר, הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לְבֵית בְּעָלָיו – אֵינוֹ מוּחְזָר. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אַף מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לִבְעָלָיו, מוּחְזָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **an ox that killed** a person, if its owner **sold it before its verdict,** **it is** considered legally **sold** and belongs to the purchaser for all purposes. Similarly, if **he consecrated it, it is** halakhically **consecrated,** and all the *halakhot* of consecrated property apply to it. If **he slaughtered it,** one **is permitted** to eat **its meat.** If **a bailee** charged with safeguarding it **returned it to its owner's house** before the verdict **it is** considered to be **returned,** and the owner has no further claim against the bailee. By contrast, **once its verdict has been issued,** if the owner **sells it, it is not** considered **sold,** since the ox is no longer his. Similarly, if **he consecrates it, it is not** considered **consecrated.** If **he slaughters it, its meat is forbidden.** If **a bailee returns it to its owner's house it is not** considered to have been **returned,** since the ox is considered to have been killed. **Rabbi Ya'akov says: Even once its verdict has been issued,** if **the bailee returns it to its owner it is** considered to have been **returned.**

The *mishna* continues: *mesaro leshomer chinam* — if the ox was given over to a *shomer*, a guardian, before it became a *shor haniskal*. The *mishna* speaks of four categories: an unpaid guardian (*shomer chinam*), a borrower (*sho'el*), a paid guardian (*nosei sachar*), and a renter (*socher*). The *mishna* teaches that in all four cases, *nichnesu tachas habaalim* — they stand in the place of the owners. This means that when a *shomer* takes an animal into their care, they assume, by default and unless they explicitly stipulate otherwise, not only a duty to the owner but also liability toward the outside world for damage the animal causes. A *muad* requires *nezek shalem*, full payment; a *tam* requires *chatzi nezek*, half payment.

The *baraita* then addresses a specific and arresting case: *hechziro shomer l'veit be'alav* — if the *shomer* returned the ox to the owner. Before *gmar din*, the return is valid: *mukhzar*, the ox has been restored. The *shomer* can legitimately say to the owner, "Here is your ox — there is no visible damage to it, the court proceeding is not my problem." But after *gmar din*, the *baraita* says: *eino mukhzar* — the return is not valid. At that point, the ox has become *issurei hana'ah* and is effectively outside the owner's meaningful control. It is as though the *shomer* is returning a damaged object. Therefore, the *shomer* must reimburse the owner. Rabbi Yaakov, however, dissents: *af mishenigmar dino hechziro shomer l'va'alav mukhzar* — even after *gmar din*, the return is valid.

### Bailee Return Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״, וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this: The Rabbis hold** that **with regard to items from which it is prohibited to** derive **benefit one does not say: That which is yours is before you,** and no compensation is required. Once the deposited item was rendered forbidden, the bailee cannot return it as is to its owner, claiming that since it has not been physically damaged he has fulfilled his obligation to return it and therefore the owner has no further claims against him. **And Rabbi Ya'akov holds** that **one does say, with regard to items from which it is prohibited to** derive **benefit: That which is yours is before you.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״, דְּאִם כֵּן – נִפְלוֹג לְעִנְיַן חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** Clearly, according **to everyone, one says with regard to items from which it is prohibited to** derive **benefit: That which is yours is before you; as, if** this was the subject of disagreement, **let them dispute** this matter **with regard to leavened bread on Passover,** which is a more common case of an item from which it is prohibited to derive benefit.

The *gemara* asks: what exactly is the debate between the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Yaakov? One might think it concerns whether one can say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — "here is your object, I am returning it" — in a case of *issurei hana'ah*. The *rabbanan* would hold that you cannot use this formula for something forbidden in benefit, since it is treated as a damaged object; Rabbi Yaakov would hold that you can, since it is physically unchanged.

However, *Rabbah* rejects this framing. He argues that *dekulei alma omrin b'issurei hana'ah harei shelcha lefanecha* — everyone agrees that in a standard case of *issurei hana'ah*, a *shomer* can discharge their obligation by returning the object. Rashi demonstrates this from other sources: it appears universally accepted that a *shomer* who was dilatory in returning *chametz* and handed it back on *Pesach* is nonetheless exempt from liability to the owner. The *chametz* is physically the same object; the fact that it has become *issurei hana'ah* does not prevent the *shomer* from saying *harei shelcha lefanecha*. If the *rabbanan* disagreed with that principle in general, the *gemara* argues, the debate would have been presented in the context of *chametz b'Pesach* — a clearer and more canonical case of *issurei hana'ah* — rather than in this unusual context.

### Damage & Possession Deba…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא, בְּגוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו; דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אַהְדַּרְתֵּיהּ נִיהֲלִי – הֲוָה מַעְרֵקְנָא לֵיהּ לְאַגְמָא, הַשְׁתָּא – אַתְפַּשְׂתֵּיהּ לְתוֹרַאי בִּידָא דְּלָא יָכֵילְנָא לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, here they disagree with regard to** the matter of **issuing the verdict for an ox in its absence. The Rabbis hold that the verdict for an ox can be issued only in its presence.** Therefore, the bailee is not exempt by returning it after the verdict, **as** the owner **could say to him: If you had returned** the ox **to me** before the verdict **I would have smuggled it to the marsh,** and the court would not have been able to sentence it to stoning. **Now you** have **let my ox be seized by** the court, **with whom I cannot engage in litigation.**

The *gemara* therefore reframes the debate: *ela hacha bigmar dino shel shor shelo befanav kamifligi* — the dispute is whether the court can pass final judgment on an ox that is not physically present before it. This is the true crux of the disagreement.

*Rabbanan savri: ein gomrin dino shel shor ela befanav.* The *rabbanan* hold that *gmar din* on an ox cannot occur unless the ox is actually present in the *beit din*. Given this position, the *shomer* who allowed the court to obtain the ox bears direct responsibility for the *gmar din* that occurred. The owners can say: *ei ahadrateih nihali, havena mivrachna leh l'agma* — had you returned the ox to me in time, I would have spirited it away to the swamp. Now you have placed it *beyada d'lo yachilna l'mishtei dina bahadeih* — in the hands of someone I cannot call to account, and my ox is lost to me.

### Rabbi Yaakov: Presence N…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: סוֹף סוֹף מִיגְמָר הֲווֹ גָּמְרִי לֵיהּ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov holds** that **the verdict for an ox can be issued in its absence.** Therefore, the bailee is exempt, **as he can say to** the owner in response to his claim: **Ultimately, they would have issued the verdict** anyway, so I did not cause your ox to be stoned by not returning it to you before the verdict.

Rabbi Yaakov, by contrast, holds *gomrin dino shel shor shelo befanav* — the court can complete the judgment even without the ox present. Therefore, the *shomer* can respond: *sof sof migmar havei gamri leh l'dineih* — in the end, the court would have issued its judgment with or without possession of the ox. The owner cannot blame the *shomer* for having enabled the *gmar din*, since the *gmar din* was going to occur regardless.

One might ask: even granting that the *shomer* bears some blame — as with *chametz b'Pesach*, where the *shomer*'s negligence clearly caused the loss — why does that matter? Even in cases of fault, the *shomer* can say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. The debate is about damage, not blame. So what does the question of the ox's presence actually add?

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi addresses this directly. He explains that the case in the *gemara* is not simply that *beit din* seized the ox — if the court had merely grabbed it, the *shomer* could still say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. Rather, Rashi reads the *gemara* as describing a situation where the *shomer* actively and directly handed the ox over to the court. This is *hizeika d'veyadayim* — damage accomplished directly through the *shomer*'s own hands. Rashi's position is that when one *directly* causes something to become *issurei hana'ah*, one can no longer claim *harei shelcha lefanecha*, even though indirect fault does not prevent that claim. Had the *shomer* merely watched the ox become *chametz* by the passage of time, that is indirect; but actively delivering the object into the very hands that will condemn it is a direct act.

Tosafot take issue with this on two grounds. First, the *gemara* does not actually say that the *shomer* actively handed it over — *beit din* appears to have seized it. Second, Tosafot argue: even if we accept a distinction between direct and indirect acts, why should that matter if we view the object as fundamentally unchanged? The question is not about the *shomer*'s conduct but about the *object's* status.

Tosafot therefore explain the debate through the concept of *hezek nikar* — visible damage. The question is whether seeing the ox in the hands of *beit din* constitutes *seeing* the damage. If *beit din* could have passed judgment without possessing the ox, then the ox's presence in the court is not what makes it a *shor haniskal* — the damage is abstract and invisible, independent of its physical location. You can infer the ox's status from its being there, but you are not seeing the damage itself. In that case, the *shomer* can still say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, because the damage exists regardless of who holds the ox. But if the court *cannot* pass judgment without the ox being present — as the *rabbanan* hold — then the very fact of the ox being in the court's hands *is* the thing that causes the damage. Its presence there is not merely evidence of the damage; it is the mechanism of the damage. That constitutes *hezek nikar*, and the *shomer* cannot discharge the obligation with *harei shelcha lefanecha*.

This is a precise and elegant formulation. In both scenarios, one can logically deduce that the ox is a *shor haniskal* from the fact that *beit din* holds it. But in one case you are inferring the damage; in the other, you are seeing the very thing that produced it.

### Ox Presence for Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? ״הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל וְגַם בְּעָלָיו יוּמָת״ – כְּמִיתַת הַבְּעָלִים כָּךְ מִיתַת הַשּׁוֹר; מָה בְּעָלִים בִּפְנֵיהֶם, אַף שׁוֹר בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** According to this explanation, **what is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis** that the ox's verdict can be issued only in its presence? The verse states: **"The ox shall be stoned, and its owner also shall be put to death"** (Exodus 21:29), indicating that **as the death of the owner,** i.e., a person, for killing another person, **so** is **the death of the ox** for killing a person. **Just as the owner** is sentenced to death only **in his presence, so too, an ox** is sentenced to death only **in its presence.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב – בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּעָלִים, בְּנֵי טַעֲנָה נִינְהוּ; אֶלָּא שׁוֹר, בַּר טַעַנְתָּא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov** holds that the owner and the ox cannot be compared. **Granted, the owner** must be present when the verdict is issued, as people **are able to present claims** in their defense; **but** as for **an ox, is it capable of presenting claims?** Consequently, it makes no difference whether or not the verdict is issued in its presence.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai ta'ama deRabbanan* — what is the textual basis for the *rabbanan*'s view that the ox must be present for *gmar din*? The *gemara* answers from the verse: *ha-shor yisakel vegam be'alav yumat* — "the ox shall be stoned and also its owner shall die." From this, the rule is derived: *kemitas habaalim kach mitas hashor* — the execution of the ox is treated in law like the execution of the owner. Just as a person cannot be convicted in absentia, the ox cannot have *gmar din* issued against it unless it is present in court.

Rabbi Yaakov counters with a common-sense argument: *bishlama be'alim bnei ta'ana ninu* — it makes sense for the owners to be present, because they can make claims in their own defense, challenge testimony, and advocate for themselves. But *ella shor bar ta'ana hu*? Is the ox going to offer a defense? There is no reason to extend the analogy mechanically when its rationale does not apply. The *halacha* follows the *rabbanan*: the ox must be physically present for *gmar din*. Rabbi Yaakov's position may have the more intuitively compelling logic, but the majority ruling requires the ox's presence.

## Bailee Liability & Exemp…

### Four Types of Bailees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסָרוֹ לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וּלְשׁוֹאֵל כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה נִכְנְסוּ תַּחַת הַבְּעָלִים, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר. הָרְגוּ תַּמִּין – נֶהֱרָגִין, וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַכּוֹפֶר. מוּעָדִין – נֶהֱרָגִין, וּמְשַׁלְּמִין אֶת הַכּוֹפֶר. וְחַיָּיבִין לְהַחֲזִיר דְּמֵי שׁוֹר לִבְעָלָיו, חוּץ מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the ox's owner **conveyed it to an unpaid bailee, or to a borrower, or** to a paid bailee, or to a renter, they enter into the responsibilities and liabilities in place of the owner. **The Sages taught:** There are **four** people who **enter into** the responsibilities and liabilities **in place of the owner, and they are: An unpaid bailee, and a borrower, a paid bailee, and a renter.** If the oxen **killed** people while in the possession of one of these people, if the oxen were **innocuous** at the time they killed, **they are killed and** the bailees are **exempt from** paying **ransom.** If they were **forewarned, they are killed and** the bailees **pay ransom. And** regardless if they were innocuous or forewarned, the bailees **are liable to return the value of the ox to its owner, with the exception of an unpaid bailee.**

The *mishna* now introduces what is effectively a separate topic — one that happens to follow from the prior discussion — namely, the liability of the various categories of *shomrim* when an ox in their care causes damage. A *tanna* teaches: *arba'a nichnesu tachat habaalim* — four people stand in the place of the owners: a *shomer chinam*, a *sho'el*, a *nosei sachar*, and a *socher*. By accepting the animal into their care, each of these figures assumes — unless they explicitly waive it — the owner's liability for damage the animal causes to others.

### Bailee Liability & Stoni…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּנַטְרֵיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי לִיפַּטְרוּ! וְאִי דְּלָא נַטְרֵיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נִיחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: What are the circumstances** in which the bailees are liable, with the exception of an unpaid bailee? **If he safeguarded** the ox appropriately but the ox killed a person anyway, **all of the** other bailees **should also be exempt** from reimbursing the owner for the ox and not just an unpaid bailee, as they did everything that was required of them. **And if he did not safeguard it** properly, **even the unpaid bailee should be liable,** as he was also required to safeguard it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּנַטְרֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה פְּחוּתָה, וְלָא נַטְרֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה מְעוּלָּה. שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – כָּלְתָה לוֹ שְׁמִירָתוֹ, הָנָךְ – לָא כָּלְתָה שְׁמִירָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the bailee **provided reduced safeguarding and did not provide superior safeguarding.** In this case **the unpaid bailee has fulfilled his safeguarding** duties; since he does not receive anything in exchange this level of safeguarding is sufficient. **Those** other bailees, who have a greater responsibility, **have not fulfilled their** required level of **safeguarding.**

The *baraita* specifies the consequences when the ox in their care gores and kills:

In the case of a *tam*, the ox is stoned but no *kofer* is paid. In the case of a *muad*, the ox is stoned and the *shomrim* must pay *kofer* — for it is they who now occupy the role of *baalim*. The phrase *gam be'alav yumat* generates not only financial liability but a dimension of moral culpability for the loss of human life, which the *kofer* payment addresses. Beyond that, *vichayavim lehachzir dmei shor leba'alav* — the *shomrim* are also obligated to reimburse the owner for the value of the stoned ox. This is distinct from the earlier case of returning a *shor haniskal* before it was executed: there, the question was *hezek she'eino nikar*; here, the ox has already been stoned, and there is a concrete double liability — to the victim's family for *kofer* and to the owner for the lost ox.

### Unpaid Watchman Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כְּמַאן? אִי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: שׂוֹכֵר כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם דָּמֵי, לִיתְנֵי: חוּץ מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said,** in clarification of the *baraita*: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this *baraita*? **If it is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** **who says** that **a renter is** considered **like an unpaid bailee** with regard to his responsibility for the rented item, **let it teach** that bailees are liable to compensate the owner, **with the exception of an unpaid bailee and a renter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: שׂוֹכֵר כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר דָּמֵי – נִיתְנֵי: ״חוּץ מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, וְכוּלָּן בְּמוּעָדִין פְּטוּרִין לְעִנְיַן כּוֹפֶר״!
>
> **Translation:** **And if** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **a renter is** considered **like a paid bailee, let it teach: With the exception of an unpaid bailee,** as it in fact states; **but** it should also state that **in** a case where the oxen are **forewarned, all of them are exempt with regard to ransom,** as Rabbi Yehuda says in the next mishna that a forewarned ox requires only a reduced level of safeguarding. Therefore, this *baraita* does not appear to be in accordance with the opinion of either *tanna*.

There is, however, one exception: *chutz mishomer chinam* — the unpaid watchman is exempt from reimbursing the owner for the stoned ox. The *gemara* immediately investigates the logic of this exemption.

### Shmira Levels & Liability

The *gemara* explains: *amri heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the ox was watched as well as it could possibly be, *afillu kulhu nami liftaru* — everyone, not only the *shomer chinam*, should be exempt from reimbursing the owner. Conversely, if no *shmira* was performed at all, even the *shomer chinam* ought to be liable.

The *gemara* explains: *hacha b'mai askinan, denatrey shmira pechuta velo natrey shmira me'ula* — the case involves an ox that received minimum-level *shmira* but not the highest level. At this point it is necessary to distinguish between two dimensions of *shmira* liability. Toward the outside world — third parties harmed by the ox — one requires the highest level of *shmira* to be exempt. Minimum *shmira* is not enough to discharge the liability for damage the ox causes others. But toward the owner — whether the *shomer* must reimburse the owner for the loss of the stoned ox — the standard depends on the category of *shomer*. A *shomer chinam* is only liable for *pshia*, gross negligence — meaning he failed to perform even minimum *shmira*. Since in this case minimum *shmira* was performed, the *shomer chinam* can say to the owner: it was not gross negligence on my part that your ox became a *shor haniskal*; I therefore do not owe you reimbursement. The paid *shomrim* and *sho'el*, by contrast, are liable even for smaller negligence, and a minimum level is not sufficient to discharge their duty to the owner. Their obligation of maximum *shmira* was not fulfilled, and they must therefore reimburse the owner.

### Categorization Difficulties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין לוֹ שְׁמִירָה אֶלָּא סַכִּין; וּלְעִנְיַן שׂוֹכֵר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: שׂוֹכֵר כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says** that an ox **has no** sufficient **safeguarding** at all **other than** slaughtering it with **a knife;** i.e., there is no degree of safeguarding that exempts the ox's owner, or in the context of the *baraita*, the bailee, from liability if the ox causes damage or kills. **And with regard to a renter,** the *baraita* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **a renter is** considered **like a paid bailee.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר – וְכִדְמַחְלִיף רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, וְתָנֵי: שׂוֹכֵר כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֵּם? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually,** the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir; and** it is **as Rabba bar Avuh reversed** the two opinions **and taught: How does a renter pay?** In other words, what is his degree of responsibility? **Rabbi Meir says: Like a paid bailee. Rabbi Yehuda says: Like an unpaid bailee.**

This raises the question of categorization. If we follow Rabbi Meir's position that a *socher* is like a *shomer chinam*, then the *baraita* should also exempt the *socher*. And if we follow Rabbi Yehuda's position that a *socher* is like a *nosei sachar*, that too creates difficulties — because under Rabbi Yehuda's view, a *muad* ox requires only minimum *shmira* for the owner-guardian to be exempt from damage payments. If the *shomer* performed minimum *shmira*, which suffices for a *muad* according to Rabbi Yehuda, then no one should owe *kofer* either. The *gemara* asks: *lisni chutz mishomer chinam ... vechulam bim'uadim peturim min hakofer* — the *baraita* should say not only that the *shomer chinam* is exempt from reimbursing the owner, but that all of them are exempt from *kofer* in a case of a *muad* where minimum *shmira* was performed.

Rav Huna bar Chinana resolves the difficulty by attributing the *baraita* to Rabbi Eliezer's position: *ein lo shmira ela sakin* — for a *muad*, there is no adequate *shmira* other than slaughter. Under this view, no level of watchfulness ever exempts the guardian from liability for a *muad*'s actions. Combined with Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that a *socher* is like a *nosei sachar*, the picture becomes coherent: the *shomer chinam* is exempt because his duty to the owner required only minimum *shmira*, which he performed; but everyone owes *kofer* because Rabbi Eliezer holds that no *shmira* at all is sufficient for a *muad*.

Abaye then offers an alternative resolution: the *baraita* follows Rabbi Meir, but the tradition cited reverses the standard attributions, making Rabbi Meir hold that a *socher* is like a *nosei sachar*. Under Abaye's reading, the *shomer chinam*'s exemption follows from his having performed minimum *shmira*, while the others — held to the higher standard — still owe the owner reimbursement.

### Goring Ox & Asymmetric Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מָסַר שׁוֹרוֹ לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, הִזִּיק – חַיָּיב, הוּזַּק – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** If the owner **conveyed his ox to an unpaid bailee,** if the ox **caused damage,** the bailee is **liable** to pay damages; but if the ox **was injured,** he is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ שְׁמִירַת נְזָקָיו – אֲפִילּוּ הוּזַּק נָמֵי לִיחַיֵּיב! וְאִי דְּלָא קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ שְׁמִירַת נְזָקָיו – אֲפִילּוּ הִזִּיק נָמֵי לִיפְּטַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: What are the circumstances? If** Rabbi Elazar was referring to a bailee **who accepted** responsibility **upon himself** for **guarding it from** causing **damage, even** if the ox **was injured he should be liable. And if** he was referring to a bailee **who did not accept** responsibility for **guarding it from** causing **damage, even if it causes damage he should be exempt** from payment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו שְׁמִירַת נְזָקָיו, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִכִּיר בּוֹ שֶׁהוּא נַגְחָן. וּסְתָמָא דְּמִילְּתָא, דְּלָא אָזֵיל אִיהוּ וּמַזִּיק אַחֲרִינֵי – קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ, דְּאָתֵי אַחֲרִינֵי וּמַזְּקִי לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ – לָא אַסֵּיק אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually,** Rabbi Elazar was referring to a case **where he accepted** responsibility **upon himself** for **guarding it from causing damage; but here we are dealing with** a case **where** the bailee **recognized that** the ox **was a goring** ox, **and** in that case **the normal way of things is that** the bailee **accepted upon himself** responsibility for safeguarding it so **that it would not go and injure others,** since he knew that it was dangerous. But **it** presumably **did not enter his mind that other** oxen **would come and injure it.** Therefore, he did not accept responsibility for safeguarding against such an occurrence.

The *gemara* then explores a further case. Rabbi Eleazar rules: *masar shoro leshomer chinam, hizik chayav, huzak patur* — if one gave one's ox to a *shomer chinam* and the ox damaged others, the *shomer* is liable; if the ox itself was harmed, the *shomer* is exempt. The *gemara* asks how this can be: if the *shomer* performed adequate *shmira*, he should be exempt in both directions; if he did not, he should be liable in both. The resolution — offered by Rava — is that the case involves a *shomer* who knew the ox had a propensity to gore and accepted it for guardianship. In such a case, *stama d'milsa d'lo azil ihu umazik achrina kabil alay* — the presumed purpose of the *shmira* arrangement is to prevent the ox from harming others. That is what the owner was worried about, and that is what the *shomer* implicitly accepted liability for. That the ox might itself be harmed, *lo asik ada'atei* — was not something the *shomer* would have considered as part of the arrangement. Hence the asymmetric outcome.

## Tam vs Muad Watchfulness

### Four Positions on Shmira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קְשָׁרוֹ בְּעָלָיו בְּמוֹסֵרָה וְנָעַל בְּפָנָיו כָּרָאוּי, וְיָצָא וְהִזִּיק – אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד חַיָּיב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the** ox's **owner tied it with reins** to a fence **or locked** the gate **before it in an appropriate manner, but** nevertheless the ox **emerged and caused damage, whether** the ox is **innocuous or forewarned** the owner is **liable,** since this is not considered sufficient precaution to prevent damage; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תָּם חַיָּיב, וּמוּעָד פָּטוּר; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ בְּעָלָיו״ – וְשָׁמוּר הוּא זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** that if the ox is **innocuous** the owner is **liable** even if he safeguarded it appropriately, since the Torah does not limit the required safeguarding for an innocuous ox. **But** if the ox is **forewarned,** the owner is **exempt** from paying compensation for damage, **as it is stated** in the verse describing damage by a forewarned ox: **"And the owner has not secured it"** (Exodus 21:36), **and this** ox that was tied with reins or behind a locked gate **was secured.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ שְׁמִירָה אֶלָּא סַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** An ox **has no** sufficient **safeguarding** at all **other than** slaughtering it with **a knife;** there is no degree of safeguarding that exempts the ox's owner from liability.

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishna* that has been repeatedly referenced throughout this discussion — the one that spells out explicitly what level of *shmira* is required to be exempt when an ox goes and damages. *Kesharo be'alav bemosira* — the owner tied it up with a standard rope — *vena'al befanav kera'uy* — and locked it in the normal way — and it still went out and caused damage. *Echad tam ve'echad muad chayav* — this is the ruling of Rabbi Meir: both a *tam* and a *muad* require a higher level of *shmira* to be exempt; standard minimum *shmira* is insufficient. Rabbi Yehuda presents a striking inversion: *tam chayav umuad patur* — minimum *shmira* exempts the owner of a *muad* but not the owner of a *tam*. Rabbi Eliezer says: *ein lo shmira ella sakin* — for a *muad*, there is no *shmira* short of slaughter that suffices.

### Rabbi Meir: High Shmira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? קָסָבַר: סְתָם שְׁווֹרִים – לָאו בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי; וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא תָּם נִיחַיַּיב – דְּנִיבְעֵי לֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה פְּחוּתָה, הֲדַר אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ״ גַּבֵּי מוּעָד – דְּנִבְעֵי לֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה מְעוּלָּה; וְיָלֵיף נְגִיחָה לְתָם – נְגִיחָה לְמוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir? He holds** that **ordinary oxen are not presumed to be under safeguarding,** as their owners do not ordinarily safeguard them; **and the Merciful One stated** in the Torah that one **will be liable** even for damage caused by **an innocuous** ox inasmuch **as it requires** at least **a reduced** level of **safeguarding,** such as with reins. **The Merciful One then stated with regard to a forewarned** ox: **"And** the owner **has not secured it,"** to indicate that it is not sufficient to provide it with only a reduced level of safeguarding, **as it requires superior safeguarding. And** Rabbi Meir **derives** this requirement with regard to an innocuous ox from a verbal analogy between the term **goring** stated **with regard to an innocuous** ox and the term **goring** stated **with regard to a forewarned** ox. In both cases superior safeguarding is required; otherwise the owner is liable.

Rabbi Meir holds that both *tam* and *muad* require maximum-level *shmira*. His reasoning proceeds as follows. The *gemara* explains: *stam shorim lav bechezkat shmira kaymi* — Rashi reads this to mean that in the ordinary course of life, people do not bother carefully watching their oxen, since they are normally tame. Yet the *Torah* says *tam meshalem* — you pay for a *tam* — which implies you were expected to do more than the standard non-existent watching. You needed at least minimum *shmira*. Then the *Torah* adds, concerning the *muad*, *velo yishmerenu ba'alav* — "and the owner did not guard it" — suggesting that for the *muad*, you needed an even higher level still. Finally, the *gezerah shavah* of *negicha* and *negicha* — the word for goring appears in both the *tam* and *muad* contexts — links them together and raises the standard for the *tam* to match the *muad*. The result: both require maximum *shmira*.

### Rabbi Yehuda: Inverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: סְתָם שְׁווֹרִים – בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי; אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא תָּם נְשַׁלֵּם, דְּנִיבְעֵי לֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה מְעוּלָּה; הֲדַר אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ״ גַּבֵּי מוּעָד – דְּנַעֲבֵיד לֵיהּ שְׁמִירָה מְעוּלָּה. הָוֵי רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי, וְאֵין רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי אֶלָּא לְמַעֵט – מִיעֵט הַכָּתוּב לִשְׁמִירָה מְעוּלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **ordinary oxen are presumed to be under** some reduced level of **safeguarding.** Since **the Merciful One stated** nevertheless that even for damage caused by **an innocuous** ox the owner **will pay,** it may be inferred **that it requires superior safeguarding. The Merciful One then states with regard to a forewarned** ox: **"And** the owner **has not secured it,"** emphasizing again **that one must provide it with superior safeguarding. This constitutes** one **amplification following** another **amplification, and** the principle is that **an amplification following an amplification is** stated **only** in order **to restrict** its extent. Accordingly, **the verse excludes** the requirement of **superior safeguarding** with regard to a forewarned ox, and therefore reduced safeguarding is sufficient to exempt the owner from liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: נְגִיחָה לְתָם נְגִיחָה לְמוּעָד – הָא מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ״ – לָזֶה, וְלֹא לְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that through the verbal analogy between the term **goring** stated **with regard to an innocuous** ox and the term **goring** stated **with regard to a forewarned** ox the Torah compares their *halakhot*, nevertheless, **the Merciful One restricted** this *halakha* by emphasizing: **"And** the owner **has not secured it,"** referring specifically **to it,** a forewarned ox, **and not to the other,** i.e., innocuous, ox.

Rabbi Yehuda's position is precisely the opposite, and deliberately so. He holds *stam shorim bechezkat shmira kaymi* — the *Torah* presumed that people do watch their oxen. When it nonetheless made you liable for a *tam*, it was already requiring you to do *more* than what everyone normally does, i.e., the highest level of *shmira*. Then when it added *velo yishmerenu* for the *muad*, one might expect an even greater demand — but since you were already at the highest level, this creates a *ribui achar ribui*, an amplification upon an amplification, and the rule of *ribui achar ribui* is to restrict rather than expand. The additional mention therefore comes to reduce the standard: for a *muad*, minimum *shmira* suffices. This is deeply counterintuitive — more dangerous ox, less stringent watchfulness requirement — but there is a powerful underlying logic to it. The *gezerah shavah* of *negicha-negicha* would bring the *tam* down to the *muad*'s level, but the *gemara* notes that Rabbi Yehuda can resist this: *lo yishmerenu* is written specifically regarding the *muad* — *lizeh velo l'acher* — and limits the leniency to the *muad* alone. Thus the *tam* retains the higher standard.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Slaughter…

The ruling presented is Rabbi Eliezer's most extreme position: for a *muad*, *ein lo shmira ella sakin* — the only true *shmira* is slaughter. No fence, no chain, no enclosure suffices. Once an ox is *muad*, the owner is always liable for whatever damage it causes.

### Scriptural Derivation

The *gemara* derives these positions from two key textual features. The first is *yigach* — the word for goring appears in both the *tam* and *muad* passages, enabling a *gezerah shavah* that links the legal standards for the two. The second is *lo yishmerenu ba'alav* — "and its owner did not guard it" — which each opinion reads differently. For Rabbi Meir, it signals a heightened demand of maximum *shmira* for a *muad*, which via the *gezerah shavah* is then extended to the *tam*. For Rabbi Yehuda, the presence of *lo yishmerenu* after the maximum *shmira* was already required generates the *ribui achar ribui* logic, reducing the *muad* to minimum *shmira*.

### Tam vs Muad: Liability T…

The underlying conceptual tension that makes Rabbi Yehuda's position so striking is the question of whether payment for a *tam* is fault-based or strict liability. Rabbi Yehuda's view can be understood as holding that payment for a *tam* is a *knas*, a penalty — arbitrary in the sense that you were not truly at fault. Ordinary oxen do not gore. *Stam shorim bechezkat shmira kaymi* can be read to mean not merely that owners normally watch their oxen, but that oxen can be presumed to be self-contained — they do not go out and attack. Nevertheless, if your ox gored, you pay half. The payment is not based on fault; it is simply imposed. If that is so, then no degree of watchfulness short of maximum can get you out of it — because the payment is not responsive to fault in the first place. The only exemption is when the event was a complete *ones*, an act of God that you truly could not have prevented — which requires the highest *shmira*.

A *muad*, by contrast, is fault-based. You knew your ox was dangerous. You should have watched it. If you did at least the minimum required of a reasonable person, your fault is discharged and you are exempt. This is the normal logic of negligence liability: once you have fulfilled the duty of care, you bear no further obligation.

The apparent absurdity — that it is easier to be exempt for a more dangerous animal — dissolves once one grasps this distinction. The *tam* imposes strict liability; strict liability cannot be waived by reasonable care. The *muad* imposes fault liability; fault liability can be waived by reasonable care.

### Tam Half-Payment Remains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: לָא פָּטַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא צַד הַעֲדָאָה שֶׁבּוֹ, אֲבָל צַד תַּמּוּת בִּמְקוֹמָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Rabbi Yehuda deemed only the forewarned element of** the ox **exempt.** Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that reduced safeguarding is sufficient to exempt the owner of a forewarned ox relates only to the additional half of the damage that is paid for a forewarned ox beyond the half of the damage that one is liable to pay for an innocuous ox. **But** the liability for its **element of innocuousness remains in place.** Therefore, if the owner did not provide superior safeguarding for the forewarned ox he is still liable to pay half the cost of the damage as he would if it were innocuous.

This logic leads directly to Rav Adda bar Ahava's clarification: *lo patar Rebbi Yehuda ela tzad ha'ad she'bo* — when Rabbi Yehuda exempts the owner of a *muad* who performed minimum *shmira*, he exempts them only from the *muad* component of payment, that is, the additional liability that flows from fault. But *aval tzad tamut bimkomo omedet* — the *tam* dimension of liability remains in place. Even when an ox has become *muad*, the original strict-liability half-payment of a *tam* has never been waived. It was never fault-based to begin with, and therefore minimum *shmira* cannot discharge it. The owner who performed only minimum *shmira* for a *muad* will still owe *chatzi nezek* — half payment — as though the animal were still a *tam*.
