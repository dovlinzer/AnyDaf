# Shekalim 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## High Priest's Mincha

### Mincha Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא סוֹף דָּבָר שֶׁמֵּת אֶלָּא אֲפִילוּ נִיטְמָא. וַאֲפִילוּ נִדְחֶה מִמּוּם. תַּנָּה רִבִּי יוּדָה בַּר פָּזִי דְּבַר דָּלָיָה. וַאֲפִילוּ נִדְחֶה מִמּוּם. מְנַיִין לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת וְלֹא מִינוּ כֹהֵן אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו תְּהֵא מִנְחָתוֹ קְרֵיבָה מִשֶּׁלְיוֹרְשִׁין. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר מִבָּנָיו יַעֲשֶׂה. יָכוֹל יְבִיאֶנָּהּ לַחַצָּיִים. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר אוֹתָהּ. כּוּלָּהּ אָמַרְתִּי דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי יוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Not only if he died, but even if he became impure; and even if he became disabled by a defect. Rebbi Jehudah bar Pazi stated from Bar Delaia: Even if he became disabled by a defect. "From where that if a High Priest dies and no other was appointed in his stead that his flour offering is brought from the heirs? The verse says, *from his sons he shall make*. I could think that he should bring it in halves, the verse says, *it*, entire I said, the words of Rebbi Jehudah."

The penultimate *daf* of *Shekalim*, 21b, opens by continuing a discussion that arose from the *mishnah*'s concern with who brings the *korban* of the *kohen gadol* once he has died — specifically his daily *minchah*, the *asirit ha'eifah*, brought half in the morning and half in the afternoon. The *gemara* now turns to how this *minchah* is prepared under ordinary circumstances before addressing the exceptional cases.

The relevant *pesukim* in Vayikra are worth laying out in full. The Torah introduces the obligation with the words *zeh korban Aharon u-vanav asher yakrivu l'Hashem beyom himashach oto* — this is the offering of Aharon and his sons that they shall bring to Hashem on the day he is anointed. The offering itself is *asirit ha-eifah solet tamid, machatzitah va-voker u-machatzitah ba-arev* — a tenth of an *eifah* of fine flour as a *tamid*, half in the morning and half in the afternoon. The method of preparation is given as *al machavat ba-shemen te'aseh, murbachet tivienah* — on a frying pan with oil, brought *murbachet*, a term understood to mean repeatedly baked. The offering is then described as *tufinei minchat pitim*. The concluding *pasuk* states: *ve-ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav ya'aseh otah chok olam l'Hashem kalil tuktar* — the anointed *kohen* who succeeds him from his sons shall perform it, an eternal edict to Hashem, it shall be burned entirely. This final *pasuk* is the source for the requirement that the *kohen gadol* brings it every day, and it will also serve as the basis for several subsequent *derashot*.

### Frying vs. Baking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּפִינֵי. רִבִּי יָסָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. מְטַגְּנָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹפֶה אוֹתָהּ. רִבִּי אָחָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. אוֹפֶה אוֹתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְטַגְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** תּוּפִינֵי, Rebbi Yasa in the name of Rebbi Ḥanina, he fries them and after that bakes them. Rebbi Aḥa in the name of Rebbi Ḥanina, he bakes them and after that fries them.

The *gemara* records a dispute about the order of preparation. *Rabbi Yosi b'shem Rabbi Chanina* holds: *metagnah v'achar kach ofah otah* — first you fry it and then you bake it. *Rabbi Acha b'shem Rabbi Chanina* reverses this: *ofah v'achar kach metagnah* — first you bake it and then you fry it.

### Tufinei Wordplay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּפִינֵי. תּוּאֲפִינָה נָא. רִבִּי אָמַר. תּוֹפְנָהּ רַכָּה. רִבִּי דוֹסָא אָמַר. תּוֹפְנָהּ נוֹיי. אַתְיָין אֵילֵּין פְּלֻגְוָותָה כְּהִינֵּין פְּלֻגְוָותָה. מָאן דְּאָמַר. תּוֹפְנָהּ נוֹי. כְּמָאן דְּאָמַר. מְטַגְּנָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹפֶה. מָאן דְּאָמַר. תּוֹפְנָהּ רַכָּה. כְּמָאן דְּאָמַר. אוֹפֶה אוֹתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְטַגְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** תּוּפִינֵי, they shall be baked cooked. Rebbi said, they shall be baked softly; Rebbi Dosa said, they shall be baked beautifully. This disagreement is parallel to the other disagreement. He who says, they shall be baked beautifully, is like him who says, he fries them and after that bakes them. He who says, they shall be baked softly is like him who says, he bakes them and after that fries them.

The word *tufinei* then becomes the basis for a series of wordplays, each yielding a slightly different reading. One interpretation reads *tufinei* as *te'afena na* — bake it while it is still *na*, meaning partially raw or not fully baked. A second reading, attributed to *Rabbi*, is *te'afena na'eh* — bake it so that it looks nice. A third reading, attributed to *Rabbi Dosa*, is *te'afena riba* — bake it many times. There are variant textual readings noted in the margins throughout, but these three are the primary positions recorded.

### Linking Debates

The *gemara* then draws an explicit connection between these wordplays and the earlier dispute about the order of preparation: *atyan ilayn pelugta kehani pelugvata* — this debate flows from the same root issue as that debate. The one who says *te'afena na'eh*, bake it so it looks nice, corresponds to the position that you bake it first and fry it afterward (*ofah v'achar kach metagnah*), because baking first preserves the attractive appearance of the finished product. The one who reads *te'afena na*, bake it while quasi-raw, corresponds to the position that you fry it first and then bake it (*metagnah v'achar kach ofah*), since by the time you reach the baking stage it has already been partially cooked through frying and is thus no longer fully raw.

## Mincha Cont.

### Tamei & Mum

The *gemara* now returns to the exceptional cases raised by the *mishnah*: *lo sof davar shemet, ela afilu nitma ve'afilu nidcha mimum* — it is not only when the *kohen gadol* dies that this issue arises; even if he has become *tamei* or has been disqualified due to a physical *mum*, the *minchah* must still be brought. This observation carries its own implications. Normally, a person who is *tamei* can still have a *korban* brought on their behalf. But the *pasuk* says *ve-ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav ya'aseh otah* — he performs it. The assumption here is that the *kohen gadol* must himself be fit to bring the offering. When he is *tamei* or disqualified, the question of who brings it — and from whose funds — becomes relevant.

One might ask whether a *tamei kohen gadol* would simply be replaced. The answer is nuanced: on *Yom Kippur*, when the *kohen gadol* becomes *tamei* and is needed, we do indeed appoint another in his place — *memanim acher tachtav*. But if on an ordinary day he happens to become *tamei* and his presence in the *Beis HaMikdash* is not otherwise required, we do not routinely substitute him. As for who funds the *minchah* in such a case, it is not entirely clear from the text. If he is alive but *tamei*, having his heirs pay would be strange; presumably he would fund it himself, with another *kohen* performing the service — but the *gemara* does not spell this out.

### Sons Bring It

The *gemara* then poses the fundamental question: *minayin shemet velo minu acher tachtav she-tehe minchato keriva mishel yorshin?* — from where do we derive that when the *kohen gadol* dies and no replacement has been appointed, his *minchah* continues to be brought, and that it comes from the estate? The answer is a *derasha* on the concluding *pasuk*: *mibanav ya'aseh otah*. Reading the *pasuk* in isolation — not as a reference to the newly anointed *kohen gadol* who comes from the sons of the previous one, but simply as *mibanav ya'aseh otah*, his sons shall perform it — we derive that the sons bring the *minchah* after the *kohen gadol*'s death. Since the question of when sons would bring it only makes sense after their father's death, this constitutes both the source for the obligation and, by implication, the source for the estate's financial responsibility.

### Whole or Halves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר. אֵינָהּ בָאָה אֶלָּא מִשֶּׁלְצִיבּוּר. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר חוֹק עוֹלָם. מִמִּי שֶׁהַבִּרְיוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ. כָּלִיל תָּקְטָר. כָּלִיל לְהַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simeon says, it only comes from the public, for it says, *an eternal law*, (to whom the creatures belong.) *Totally it shall be burned*, all is for burning.

A further question follows: *yachol yevienah lachatza'in?* — perhaps when the sons bring it after the *kohen gadol*'s death, they should bring it in the same manner he did — half in the morning and half in the afternoon? The *gemara* answers from the same *pasuk*: *talmud lomar otah* — *ya'aseh otah*, it shall be performed as a whole. Since the final *pasuk*, which is being read to address the posthumous situation, does not specify the splitting into halves, we derive that when the sons bring it, they bring it as a single whole unit. *Kulah amarti* — as one complete offering. This is the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Funding Source

### Yehuda: Heirs

The *mishnah* records the dispute about the source of funds. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds: *mishlo yorshin* — it comes from the estate of the deceased *kohen gadol*. Furthermore, *shleima hayta kerevah* — it is brought as a complete unit, not in two halves.

### Shimon: Tzibur

*Rabbi Shimon* holds: *eina ba'ah ela mishel tzibur* — it comes only from communal funds. His proof is from the word *chok olam* at the end of the *pasuk*. The *derasha*, as cited by the *Korban HaEdah*, runs as follows: *mi she-berit kerutah lo* — from the one for whom the world is covenanted, that is, God. *Chok olam* is read as referring to the *Owner* of the *olam*, God, and hence the funding comes from what belongs to God — the communal treasury of the *Beis HaMikdash*. The word *kalil tuktar* — it is burned entirely — further confirms that it is brought as one whole unit. There is thus no dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* on the question of *kulah* versus *chatzi*; both agree it is brought whole. The dispute is exclusively about the source of funds.

### Scriptural or Rabbinic?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי בָּא בַּר מָמָל בָּעֵי . מִחְלְפָא שִׁיטָּתֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. תַּמָּן הוּא אָמַר. מִשֶּׁלְיוֹרְשִׁין. וְהָכָא הוּא אָמַר מִשֶּׁלְצִיבּוּר. אָמַר רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר בָּא. וְלֹא יְאוּת הוּא מַקְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abba bar Mamal asked, the system of Rebbi Simeon seems inverted. There he says, from the heirs, but here he says, from the public. Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said, is that not a pertinent question?

A difficulty now surfaces. In our *mishnah*, *Rabbi Shimon* presented the rule that the *minchah* comes from communal funds as one of the *takanot* enacted by *Beis Din*. Yet here the *gemara* has shown *Rabbi Shimon* deriving this very rule from a *pasuk*. Is it *de'oraita* or *takana*? *Rabbi Abba bar Mamal* raises this tension explicitly: *machlafa shitatei de-Rabbi Shimon* — *Rabbi Shimon* is contradicting himself. There it seemed that in principle it would come from the *yorshin*, with the *takana* shifting it to communal funds; here he derives from scripture that it comes from communal funds.

*Rabbi Chiya bar Ba* responds — though in the characteristic style of the Yerushalmi it is difficult to know if his remark is affirmation or further question — *v'lo ya'ut hu makshe?* Is this not a well-posed difficulty?

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתַא רִבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אָחָא רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּבַר תוֹרָה הוּא שֶׁתְּהֵא בָאָה מִן הַצִּיבּוּר. הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר. יִגְבּוּ לָהּ. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁתְּהֵא בָאָה מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** There came Rebbi Jacob bar Aḥa, Rebbi Abbahu in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: It is a word from the Torah that it should be brought from the public. I could say, one should tax for it; they instituted that it should come from what is taken from the lodge.

*Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha* in the name of *Rabbi Abbahu* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* provides the resolution. *Dvar Torah hu she-tehe ba min ha-tzibur* — it is indeed biblical that it comes from the *tzibur*. However, one might have thought this means a special appeal had to be made each time — *hayiti omer yigbu lah*, I would have thought they collect for it specifically. The *takana* enacted by *Beis Din* was *she-hitkinu she-tehe ba mi-trumat ha-lishka* — that it should be funded not through a special collection but from the *Trumas HaLishkah*, the standing treasury of the *Beis HaMikdash* used for communal sacrifices.

This is a subtle but important distinction. The *Trumas HaLishkah* is designated for *korbanos tzibur* — communal offerings. The *minchah* of the *kohen gadol* is formally his personal *korban*, not a communal one. The *takana* permits drawing on those funds even for this *korban*. The underlying biblical principle, then, is that the community bears financial responsibility; the *takana* specifies from which communal fund the money is drawn. The two positions are thus complementary rather than contradictory. This dual resolution has a certain conceptual elegance: the *minchah* of the *kohen gadol* occupies a liminal space, simultaneously personal and institutional. During his lifetime, the personal dimension is foregrounded — it is his *korban*, from his funds. After his death, when it continues to be brought, the institutional, *Beis HaMikdash* dimension comes to the fore, and the communal treasury absorbs the cost.

## Whole Unit Implications

### Tamid Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹסֵה אָמַר. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן בָּעֵי. מָה. שְׁלֵימָה בְשַׁחֲרִית וּשְׁלֵימָה בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם. אוֹ מֶחֱצָה בְשַׁחֲרִית וּמֶחֱצָה בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם. [אוֹ שְׁלֵימָה שַׁחֲרִית וּבְטֵילָה בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם.] כַּד תְּהֵא פְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ. שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִין מָה הֵן. שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִין בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּשְׁלשֶׁת לוּגִין בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. אוֹ לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה בְשַׁחֲרִית וְלוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose said, Rebbi Joḥanan asked: Entire in the morning and entire in the evening, or half in the morning and half in the evening, [or entire in the morning and nothing in the evening]? Then it should be obvious to him, what about three log? Three log in the morning and three log in the afternoon, or a log and a half in the morning and a log and a half in the afternoon?

With the ruling established that the *minchah* is brought as a whole unit, the *gemara* explores the practical implications. *Rabbi Yosei amar Rabbi Yochanan* raises a question: *sheleimah be-shacharis u-sheleimah bein ha-arbayim* — is it still brought in two distinct presentations, a whole in the morning and a whole in the afternoon? Or *sheleimah be-shacharis u-beteilah bein ha-arbayim* — is it brought whole in the morning and nothing in the afternoon?

The *gemara* appears to resolve this by invoking the word *tamid* — *minchat tamid* — which appears in the *pasuk*. The category of *tamid* by definition requires a morning and an afternoon component: the *korban tamid* is once in the morning and once in the afternoon, the *ketores* is twice daily, the *menorah* is *tamid* through the night. A single morning offering cannot constitute a *tamid*. Therefore, on the assumption that *tamid* demands two daily presentations, the conclusion is that a complete unit is brought in the morning and a complete unit in the afternoon.

### Oil & Frankincense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה. אוֹף הָדָא צְרִיכָה לֵיהּ שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין מָה הֵן. שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּשְׁנֵי קְמָצִין בֵּין הָעַרְבָיִם. אוֹ קוֹמֶץ אֶחָד בְּשַׁחֲרִית וְקוֹמֶץ אֶחָד בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה. כְּלוּם לָמְדוּ לַקוֹמֶץ לֹא מִמִּנְחַת חוֹטֵא. מַה לְהַלָּן שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין אַף כָּאן שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין. אָמַר רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה. כְּלוּם לָמְדוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִין לֹא מִתָּמִיד שֶׁלְבֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. מַה לְהַלָּן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִין. אַף כָּאן שְׁלשֶׁת לוּגִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizqiah said, also the following was a problem for him: Two handful in the morning and two handful in the evening or one handful in the morning and one handful in the evening? Rebbi Yose said, did they not the two handful infer from the sinner's flour offering? Just as there two handful, also here two handful. Rebbi Ḥizqiah said, did one not infer the three log from the daily afternoon sacrifice? Since there {are} three log, so also here three log.

With this resolved — at least as an *im timtzi lomar* — the *gemara* moves to the next question. Ordinarily, the *kohen gadol* brings half an *issaron* of flour with one and a half *log* of oil in the morning and half an *issaron* with one and a half *log* in the afternoon. Now that a full *issaron* is being brought in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon, what happens with the *shemen*? *Shlosha lugin shacharis u-shlosha lugin bein ha-arbayim* — is the oil also doubled to three *log* each time? Or does it remain *log u-mechetza shacharis v-log u-mechetza bein ha-arbayim* — one and a half in the morning and one and a half in the afternoon, unchanged?

The *gemara* further extends the question to the *levonah*. By a normal *minchah* two *kematzim* of *levonah* are brought. In the standard configuration, one *kometz* accompanied each half — one in the morning and one in the afternoon. Now that each presentation is a full *issaron*, does each presentation get two *kematzim*, so that *shnei kematzim shacharis u-shnei kematzim bein ha-arbayim*? Or does each presentation retain one *kometz*?

To illustrate the structure of the problem: the full recipe is one *issaron* of flour, three *log* of oil, and two *kematzim* of *levonah*. In the standard split, each half-presentation receives half an *issaron*, one and a half *log*, and one *kometz*. The question is whether, now that the half-presentation has been expanded to a full *issaron*, the oil and *levonah* scale up proportionally or remain at their split-level amounts.

### Oil Proportion

The proportionality disputes are resolved through citation of analogies. *Rabbi Chizkiyah* addresses the question of the oil: *kula lamdu shlosha lugin mi-tamid she-bein ha-arbayim* — we derive the three *log* per *issaron* from the standard *tamid* offering of a sheep, which requires one *issaron* of *soles* with a *revi'it ha-hin* of oil, equal to three *log*. *Mah lehallan shlosha lugin, af kan shlosha lugin* — just as there it is three *log* per *issaron*, so too here it is three *log* per *issaron*. Once the flour of each presentation constitutes a full *issaron*, the standard ratio demands three *log* of oil to accompany it.

*Rabbi Yose* addresses the *levonah* question analogously: *kula lamdu l-kometz mi-minchas chotei* — the principle of the *kometz* of *levonah* in this *minchah* is derived from the *minchas chotei*, and *mah lehallan shnei kematzim, af kan shnei kematzim* — just as there, two *kematzim*, so here, two *kematzim*. The exact logic of why *shnei kematzim* per full unit follows from the *minchas chotei* is less explicit in the text, and the commentators offer various explanations.

## Sacred Items Rules

### Wood & Salt Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן. בְּדִין הָיָה שֶׁיִּמְעֲלוּ בָהּ. וְהֵן גָּזְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא יִמְעֲלוּ בָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Samuel ben Naḥman in the name of Rebbi Jonathan: It would have been logical that one commit larceny with them, but they decided that one does not commit larceny with them.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of rabbinic ordinances governing *kedushas hamikdash* and the laws of *me'ilah*. The first concerns the *kohanim*'s use of wood and salt. The wood and salt designated for the *korbanos* — the wood for the altar fire and the salt for salting the sacrificial meat — were permitted by *Beis Din* for use by the *kohanim* to roast and salt the portions of the *korban* they themselves receive. *Rabbi Shimon Nachmani beshem Rabbi Yonasan* explains the legal background: *badin hayah she-yiheyu mo'alin bahen* — by rights there should have been *me'ilah* in using these sacred materials for personal consumption. *Ve-hen gazru shelo yim'alu bahen* — and they legislated that there would be no *me'ilah*. This is the *tnai Beis Din*, the condition under which the *kohanim* operate in their use of Temple property.

### Red Heifer Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַנֵּי חַטָּאת. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ. [בָּהּ מוֹעֲלִין] וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בַּאֲפָרָהּ. אָמַר רִבִּי אָבָּהוּ. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מִשְׁתַּקְּשְׁקִין בָּהּ וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָהּ גַּבֵּי מַכּוֹתֵיהֶן וְגָזְרוּ שֶׁיִּמְעֲלוּ בָהּ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּגְדְּרוּ גָּזְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא יִמְעֲלוּ בָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** But did we not state: "*it is ḥattat*, this teaches that one commits larceny with it. [With it one commits larceny,] with its ashes one does not commit larceny." Rebbi Abbahu said, at the beginning they were using it as a disinfectant and were putting it on their wounds and therefore they decided that one would commit larceny with it. When they were fenced in, they decided that one does not commit larceny with it.

The *gemara* then transitions to the ashes of the *parah adumah*. The *mishnah* had indicated that these ashes are exempt from *me'ilah*. The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that confirms: *chatasi* — the word *chatasi* in the relevant *pasuk* teaches that *me'ilah* applies to the animal itself once sanctified, *bah mo'alin v-ein mo'alin be-afrah* — you have *me'ilah* in it but not in its ashes. Since the animal is called *chatas*, *me'ilah* applies to the animal; once burned into ashes, the *me'ilah* falls away.

This raises a question: if the Torah itself exempts the ashes from *me'ilah*, why did the *mishnah* need to present this as a rabbinic enactment? *Rabbi Abbahu* explains a three-stage historical development. Originally, the ashes were biblically exempt from *me'ilah*, and in that period people were using them freely — applying them medicinally to wounds, for example. Because the absence of *me'ilah* led to misuse, *Beis Din* imposed rabbinic *me'ilah* on the ashes as a protective measure. However, once people became aware of this *gezeirah*, they became excessively reluctant to engage with the ashes at all — to the point that they would not even be sprinkled with them for purification. The rabbis therefore reversed the *gezeirah* and returned to the biblical baseline: no *me'ilah* in the ashes. The pattern here — biblical exemption, interim rabbinic stringency, and then reversal — parallels the structure we saw in the discussion of communal versus personal funding of the posthumous *minchah*: a biblical principle, a *takana* that modified practice, and a subsequent return (or refinement) that brings us back to the underlying rule.

### Bird Replacement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה הַזֹּאת בַּמֶּה הִיא מִתְכַּפֶּרֶת. אָמַר רִבִּי יִצְחָק. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא הַמְסַפֵּק אֶת הַקִּינִּים הוּא מְסַפֵּק אֶת הַפְּסוּלוֹת וְאֵת הָאוֹבְדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** By what is that woman atoned for? Rebbi Isaac said, it is a stipulation of the Court that the providers of nests also provide the disqualified and the lost ones.

The final *takana* in this section concerns the *kinnim* — bird offerings brought by a woman after *niddah*. The scenario is that a woman purchased birds from a supplier for her *korban*, but those birds died or were found to be *pesulos*. Without replacement, the woman would assume her *korban* had been brought and consider herself *tehorah*, while in fact it had not. *Rabbi Yitzchak* states: *tenai Beis Din hu — ha-maspek es ha-kinim hu maspek es ha-pesulos ve-es ha-avudos* — it is a condition imposed by *Beis Din* that the supplier who provides the birds bears responsibility to replace any that are lost or invalid. This was the arrangement under which suppliers conducted business with the *Beis HaMikdash*.

## Spittle Purity

### Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: כָּל הָרוּקִּין הַנִּמְצָאִין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם טְהוֹרִין חוּץ מִשֶּׁל שׁוּק הָעֶלְיוֹן דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: All spittle found in Jerusalem is pure except on the Upper Market, the words of Rebbi Meïr.

With the conclusion of this *perek*, the *gemara* enters the final chapter of *Shekalim*. The *mishnah* addresses the *halachic* status of *rokim* — spit found in Yerushalayim. The concern is real: the spit of a *zav*, *zavah*, or *metzora* carries the status of an *av ha-tumah*, and if one cannot determine the source of spit found in a public space, one must assess its presumptive status. *Rabbi Meir* rules: *kol ha-rokim ha-nimtza'im bi-Yerushalayim tehorin* — all spit found in Yerushalayim is presumed *tahor*, *chutz mi-shuk ha-elyon* — with the exception of spit found in the upper marketplace, which is presumed *tamei*.

### Yose: Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר בִּשְׁאָר כָּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁבָּאֶמְצַע טְמֵאִין שֶׁבַּצְּדָדִין טְהוֹרִין. וּבִשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל שֶׁבָּאֶמְצַע טְהוֹרִין שֶׁבַּצְּדָדִין טְמֵאִין שֶׁמִּפְּנֵי שֶהַמּוּעָטִין מִסְתַּלְּקִין לַצְּדָדִין:
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose says, on all other days of the year, those in the middle of the road are impure, and those on the sides pure. On holidays, those in the middle of the road are pure and those on the sides impure since the minority will remove themselves to the sides.

*Rabbi Yose* disagrees with this simple geographic division and instead distinguishes between the central and marginal paths throughout the city. *Bish'ar kol yemot ha-shanah* — during the rest of the year, outside the festival season, *she-ba-emtza temei'im* — spit found in the middle of the road is *tamei*, because the central path is where the general population travels without concern for *tumah* and *taharah*, and *zavim* and *zavos* will inevitably be found there. *She-ba-tziddin tehorin* — spit on the margins of the road is *tahor*, because those who walk on the sides of the road are presumed to be consciously avoiding the masses, which is attributed to a concern for *taharah* rather than mere preference.

### Festival Reversal

At the time of the *regel*, however, the presumptions reverse entirely. *She-ba-emtza tehorin, she-ba-tziddin temei'im* — the center is *tahor* and the margins are *tamei*. During the *regel*, the entire population is concerned with maintaining *taharah* for entry to the *Beis HaMikdash*, and thus the masses walking in the center are overwhelmingly *tahor*. Those on the margins, precisely because they are avoiding the crowds, are more likely to be among the minority who are *tamei* and who keep to the sides so as not to *metamei* others. As the *gemara* states: *mipnei she-hamu'atin mistalkin la-tziddin* — it is the minority that retreats to the margins.

## Vessel Purity

### Descent to Mikvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּלַ הַכֵּלִים הַנִּמְצָאִין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה לְבֵית הַטְּבִילָה טְמֵאִין וְדֶרֶךְ עֲלִיָּה טְהוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא כִּירִידָתָן עֲלִיָיתָן דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** All implements which are found in Jerusalem on the road descending to the place of immersion are impure, on the way ascending they are pure since they were brought down on a way different from that on which they were brought up, the words of Rebbi Meïr.

The *mishnah* turns from spit to *kelim* — vessels found in Yerushalayim. The question is whether such vessels may be presumed *tahor* for use with *kodashim* or *terumah*. The *mishnah* establishes a geographic framework based on proximity to the *mikvah*. A vessel found on the road that descends to the *mikvah* is presumed *tamei*: the evident reason it is there is that its owner was bringing it to be immersed.

### Ascent from Mikvah

A vessel found on the road ascending from the *mikvah* is presumed *tahor*, on the assumption that it has already been immersed. The *mishnah* attributes this ruling to *Rabbi Meir*, who states *she-lo ke-yeridatan aliyatan* — the descent and the ascent are not symmetric in what they imply. The physical layout of the *mikvah* apparently involved separate paths for those going down and those coming up — a feature consistent with excavated *mikvaos* at the *Beis HaMikdash* which contain a central divider on the stairway.

### All Vessels Pure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּן טְהוֹרִין חוּץ מִן הַסַּל וְהַמַּגְרֵיפָה הַמְיוּחָדִין לַקְּבָרוֹת:
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose says, all are pure except the bag and the rake set apart for graves.

*Rabbi Yose* takes a more sweeping position: *kulam tehorim* — all vessels found in Yerushalayim may be presumed *tahor*. This is a striking counterpoint to his position on spit, where he was more stringent than *Rabbi Meir* about *tumah* in public spaces. *Rabbi Meir* was more lenient about spit — presuming it *tahor* everywhere except the upper marketplace — while being more restrictive about which *kelim* may be presumed *tahor*. *Rabbi Yose* was more stringent about spit in the center of the road but more permissive about vessels. The positions do not align neatly by general tendency, which is worth noting.

### Grave Implements

*Rabbi Yose*'s blanket leniency for vessels has one exception: *chutz min ha-sal ve-ha-magrefah ve-ha-maritza ha-meyuchadin le-kevaros* — a basket, a shovel, and a *maritza* that are identifiably associated with graves are always presumed *tamei*. These implements are so specifically associated with *tumah* that no presumption of *taharah* can attach to them, whatever the general rule about vessels in the city.

## Slaughter Knife

### 14th Nisan Knife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַכִּין שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שׁוֹחֵט בָּהּ מִיָּד. בִּשְׁלְֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹנֶה וּמַטְבִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** If a knife was found on the Fourteenth, one may slaughter with it immediately; on the Thirteenth one has to repeat and immerse.

A *sakin* — a slaughtering knife — found on the fourteenth of Nisan presents a distinctive case. On the fourteenth, people are actively engaged in *shechitah* of the *Korban Pesach*, and any knife found that day may be used *miyad*, immediately, without first taking it to the *mikvah*. The reasoning is straightforward: anyone who wished to use a knife on the fourteenth would have had to immerse it on the thirteenth, because immersion requires *ha'arev shemesh* — waiting for nightfall — before the vessel is fully *tahor*. A knife immersed on the fourteenth could not be used until nightfall. Therefore, any knife one finds on the fourteenth of Nisan must have already been immersed on the thirteenth and is thus presumptively *tahor*.

### 13th Nisan Knife

If the knife is found on the thirteenth, the situation differs. *Shoneh u-matbil* — one must take it to the *mikvah*, because it is possible the owner had not yet immersed it and planned to do so later that day. Since immersion on the thirteenth would leave the knife *tahor* by nightfall and thus usable on the fourteenth, we cannot presume it has already been immersed. This requirement is somewhat puzzling given *Rabbi Yose*'s ruling that all *kelim* in Yerushalayim are presumed *tahor* — perhaps the *mishnah* here reflects *Rabbi Meir*'s position, or perhaps the heightened standards of *kodashim* impose special requirements regardless.

### Cleaver Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹפִיץ בֵּין בָּזֶה וּבֵין בָּזֶה שׁוֹנֶה וּמַטְבִּיל. חָל אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת שׁוֹחֵט בָּהּ מִיָּד. בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹחֵט בָּהּ מִיָּד. נִמְצֵאת קְשׁוּרָה לְסַכִּין הֲרֵי זוֹ כַּסַּכִּין:
>
> **Translation:** A dagger in either case one has to repeat and immerse. If the Fourteenth falls on a Sabbath, one may slaughter with it immediately; on the Fifteenth one may slaughter with it immediately. If it was tied to a knife its status is that of the knife.

A *kofitz* — a cleaver — is treated differently because of its different function. A cleaver is not used for *shechitah*; it is used for dismembering the *korban*. Moreover, by the *Korban Pesach* it cannot be used at all for dismembering, since *kol etzem lo yishberu bo* — no bone of the Pesach offering may be broken. The cleaver is therefore needed not on the fourteenth but on the fifteenth, for the other *korbanos* of the *regel*. This means the cleaver could legitimately be taken to the *mikvah* on the fourteenth — immersion on the fourteenth means it is *tahor* by nightfall and usable on the fifteenth. Accordingly, if one finds a cleaver on the fourteenth, its status is ambiguous: it may have been immersed already or may not yet have been immersed. *Baze u-vaze* — whether found on the thirteenth or the fourteenth, *shoneh u-matbil* — one must immerse it again.

If the fourteenth falls on *Shabbat*, the calculation shifts: since one cannot immerse on *Shabbat*, anyone who needed the cleaver for *Yom Tov* would have had to immerse it on the thirteenth. In that case, a cleaver found on that *Shabbat-Erev Pesach* may be used *miyad*. Similarly, a cleaver found on the fifteenth of Nisan may be used immediately, because the latest the owner could have immersed it was the fourteenth, and nightfall of the fourteenth renders it *tahor* for the fifteenth.

### Tied Together

If a cleaver is found tied together with a *shechitah* knife — *nimtzeit keshurah im sakin* — then *harei hu ke-sakin*, it is treated like the knife. Since the two implements are bound together, we presume they were brought to the *mikvah* at the same time. Therefore, the cleaver is presumed to share the *taharah* status of the knife: if found on the fourteenth, both may be used immediately.

## Spittle Purity (II)

### Non-Jewish Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הָרוּקִּין כול׳. רִבִּי אַבִּין בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. קַצְרָן שֶׁלְגּוֹיִם הָיָה שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** "All spittle," etc. Rebbi Abbin in the name of Rebbi Joshua ben Levi: A Gentile fortification was there.

Returning to explain *Rabbi Meir*'s exception for the *shuk ha-elyon*, the *gemara* now supplies the reason. *Rabbi Akiva beshem Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* explains: *katzin shel nochrim hayu sham* — there was a garrison of non-Jews stationed in the upper marketplace. The relevance is that *chazal* treated non-Jews *le-chumra* as having the status of a *zav*, a source of *tumah* in their spittle. Because of this significant non-Jewish presence — apparently a Roman garrison, which would also explain the dramatic detail about wild donkeys being killed there, presumably to feed the garrison's animals or as part of its activity — the upper marketplace was distinguished from the rest of the city as a place where one could not presume the spit to be *tahor*. *Rabbi Meir* accordingly exempted all other parts of Yerushalayim while singling out this one location.

## Carcass Blood

### Dead Donkey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי סִימוֹן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. מַעֲשֶׂה בְפִרְדָה מִשֶּׁלְבֵּית רִבִּי שֶׁמֵּתָה וְטִיהֲרוּ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ מִשֵׁם נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simon in the name of Rebbi Joshua ben Levi: It happened that a mule of Rebbi's household died and they declared its blood pure regarding the carcass.

The brief mention of the garrison and the animals killed there leads the *gemara* into an extended digression triggered by the subject of blood and *tumah*. The digression begins with a story from the household of *Rabbi* (*Rebbi*): *maaseh be-pirdah shel beis Rebbi she-meisah* — there was an incident with a donkey in *Rebbi*'s household that died. The ruling given was *u-tihara damah mishum neveilah* — its blood was declared *tahor* on the grounds that the *tumah* of *neveilah* applies to flesh, not to blood.

### Elazar's Frustration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר שְׁאִיל לְרִבִּי סִימוֹן. עַד כַּמָּה. וְלֹא אַגִּיבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar asked Rebbi Simon, how much? He did not take notice of him.

*Rabbi Elazar* asked *Rabbi Siman*: *ad kama?* — until how much blood does this *taharah* apply? Is the blood categorically *tahor* in all quantities, or is there a threshold beyond which the blood becomes significant enough to be treated as equivalent to a *kezayis* of flesh and thus *tamei*? *V-lo agivei* — *Rabbi Siman* gave him no answer. This non-response left *Rabbi Elazar* deeply frustrated, as the *gemara* records.

### Yehoshua Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁאִיל לְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. אָמַר לֵיהּ. עַד רְבִיעִית טָהוֹר. יוֹתֵר מִיכֵּן טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** He asked Rebbi Joshua ben Levi who told him, it is pure up to a *quartarius*. More than that is impure.

*Rabbi Elazar* then brought the question to *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, who answered: *ad revi'is tahor, yosei mikan tamei* — up to a *revi'is* the blood is *tahor*; beyond a *revi'is* it is *tamei*. A parallel account describes *Rabbi Bibi* telling over this incident, and *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Bisna* raising the same question — *ad kama?* — with the same answer given. In the parallel account, however, the respondent kicks the questioner, and *Rabbi Zerika* remonstrates: you kick someone who asks you a question? The respondent explains: *bigin d'lo havay da'ati bi* — because my mind was not settled. He then cites a teaching of *Rabbi Chanin* on the verse *ve-hayu chayecha telu'im lach mineged* — your life will be suspended before you — applied to three levels of precarious livelihood: one who buys a year's worth of grain but has no field of his own, one who buys from a grain supplier, and one who buys from a baker (*paltera*), each stage representing a more tenuous existence. *V-ana samichna a-paltera* — and I rely on the baker, meaning his own mental state was such that he could not compose himself to answer.

The *gemara* then addresses what appears to be a contradiction. The *mishnah* in *Taharos* — cited as *tman tninan* — states that *dam ha-sheret mitamei ke-vesaro* — the blood of a *sheretz* causes *tumah* like its flesh — and then adds *u-mitamei v-eino machshir*, it causes *tumah* but does not render food susceptible to *tumah*. And the *mishnah* concludes: *v-ein lanu ke-yotzei bo* — there is nothing else like this combination of being *metamei* without being *machshir*. This would seem to contradict the ruling that blood of *neveilah* beyond a *revi'is* is *tamei* — for that too would be *metamei* without being *machshir*, and yet the *mishnah* says there is nothing like *sheretz* blood in this regard. One might try to resolve the earlier *mishnah* about *neveilah* blood being *tahor* by reading *tahor* narrowly — meaning not *machshir* but still *tamei* — but the *sheretz* *mishnah*'s statement that nothing else shares this combination would undercut that reading. The *gemara*'s response is that *v-ein lanu ke-yotzei bo ke-shiyur tumato* — there is nothing else with this combination at the *same minimum measure*: the *sheretz* causes *tumah* at the tiny measure of *ka-adasha*, like a lentil, while *neveilah* blood — if it is indeed *tamei* — operates at a larger threshold. These are thus not the same phenomenon.

### Amoraim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף. מָאן דְּאָמַר טָמֵא. כְּרִבִּי יְהוּדָה. וּמָאן דְּאָמַר טָהוֹר. כְּרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן בָּתִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Joseph said, he who says "impure" follows Rebbi Jehudah; he who said "pure" follows Rebbi Joshua ben Bathyra.

Ultimately *Rabbi Yose* frames the matter as an explicit dispute between two *amoraim*: *peligi bah trei amora'in* — two *amoraim* disagree about this. *Chad amar tamei* — one holds that blood of *neveilah* is categorically *tamei*; *ve-chad amar tahor* — the other holds it is categorically *tahor*. *Man d'amar tamei ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the one who holds it is *tamei* follows the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*, who — drawing on a dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* — understands that both schools agree blood of *neveilah* is *tamei*, differing only about the measure. *U-man d'amar tahor ke-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Betira* — the one who holds it is *tahor* follows the position of the *mishnah* attributed to *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Betira* that the blood of *neveilah* is *tahor*.

The resolution for the story of *Rebbi*'s donkey follows: *Rav Dimi d'min Nechusha* notes that *Rabbi Yehuda* — the one who says *neveilah* blood is *tamei* beyond a *revi'is* — was *moreinah de-vei nesiah*, the *posek* for *Rebbi*'s own household. The ruling they gave, that the blood was *tahor*, therefore fits within *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own framework, since presumably the amount in question was under a *revi'is*.

## Road Behavior

### Normal Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה הַטְּמֵאִים מְהַלְּכִין שִׁיבּוֹלֶת וְהַטְּהוֹרִין מְהַלְּכִין מִן הַצַּד. וְהַטְּהוֹרִים מְהַלְּכִין סְתָם וְהַטְּמֵאִים אוֹמְרִים לָהֶם. פְּרוּשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the remainder of the days of a year the impure ones are walking on the path and the pure ones are walking on the shoulder. The pure ones walk without saying anything, the impure ones tell them, keep apart.

Having mentioned *Rabbi Yose*'s distinction between central and marginal roads, the *gemara* now elaborates the practical behavior expected in each period. During the ordinary year — outside the festival season — *ha-teme'im mehalchin shibbolet* — the *tamei* individuals walk *shibbolet*, meaning straight down the center path (*shibbolet* understood as *lashon shevil*, a path or throughway). *Ve-ha-tehorim mehalchin min ha-tzad* — the *tehorim* walk on the margins. *Ve-ha-tehorim mehalchin stam* — the *tehorim* walk without announcing themselves, *ve-ha-teme'im omrim lahem: peroshu* — and it is the *tamei* individuals in the center who call out to those at the margins: keep your distance.

### Festival Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל הַטְּהוּרִין מְהַלְּכִין שִׁיבּוֹלֶת וְהַטְּמֵאִין מְהַלְּכִין מִן הַצַּד. וְהַטְּמֵאִים מְהַלְּכִין סְתָם וְהַטְּהוֹרִין אוֹמְרִין לָהֶן. פְּרוּשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** During a holiday the pure ones are walking on the path and the impure ones are walking on the shoulder. The impure ones walk without saying anything, the pure ones tell them, keep apart.

During the *regel*, the dynamic inverts entirely. *Ha-tehorim mehalchin shibbolet* — the *tehorim* walk in the center. *Ve-ha-teme'im mehalchin min ha-tzad* — the *tamei* individuals walk on the margins. *Ha-teme'im mehalchin stam* — the *tamei* individuals walk without announcing themselves, *ve-ha-tehorim omrim lahem: peroshu* — and the *tehorim* in the center call out to those on the margins to keep their distance. The dominant group in each period — those who walk openly in the center — are the ones who announce their status to the minority at the margins.

## Grave Implements ID

### Abba Shaul Term

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל הָיָה קוֹרֵא אוֹתָן צִפּוֹרֶן. מָאן דְּאָמַר צִפּוֹרֶן. שֶׁהוּא דוֹמֶה לַצִּיפּוֹרֶן. מָאן דְּאָמַר מְרִיצָה. שֶׁהִיא מְרִיצָה אֶת הָאֶבֶן [לְבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת.]
>
> **Translation:** Abba Shaul used to call it "fingernail" since it is shaped like a fingernail. He who said מְרִיצָה because it quickly dispatches the stone [to the cemetery.]

The *mishnah* had identified three grave-related implements as always presumed *tamei*: the *sal* (basket), the *magrefah* (shovel), and the *maritza*. The *gemara* records that *Abba Shaul hayah korei otan tziporin* — *Abba Shaul* would call the *maritza* by the name *tziporin*, meaning fingernails or claws, because the implement was shaped like a fingernail. *Man d'amar maritza* — the one who uses the term *maritza* in the *mishnah* understands it as *she-maritza et ha-even le-veit ha-kevarot* — that it causes the stone to run or slide toward the gravesite, that is, it was used to push or move stones in a cemetery.

### Three Implements

The three implements — basket, shovel, and *maritza* — are thus defined by their function: they are tools specifically associated with burial work. Their identification with graves is so clear that no general presumption of *taharah* for vessels found in Yerushalayim can override the inference drawn from their form and function.

### Tied Implements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. הַסַּכִּין קְשׁוּרָה לוֹ הֲרֵי זוֹ כְמוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: If he knife was tied to it its status is that of the knife.

A *baraita* then addresses the case of tied implements in the grave-implement context: *ha-sakin keshurah lah harei zeh kemotah* — a knife tied to one of these grave implements is treated like the grave implement itself. This stands in apparent tension with the ruling in the *mishnah*, which held that a *kofitz* tied to a *sakin* takes on the *sakin*'s status. Here the *baraita* reverses the direction: when a knife is tied to a grave implement, the knife takes on the grave implement's status. Some commentators suggest these are not contradictory but address different configurations — perhaps the *baraita* speaks of two knives found together, where the status of one informs the presumption about the other. The tension between the *mishnah* and this *baraita* is left unresolved.
