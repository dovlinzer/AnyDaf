# Kiddushin 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodshei Kodashim

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּחֶלְקוֹ, בֵּין קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים, בֵּין קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a priest **who betroths** a woman **with his portion** of offerings, **whether** he did so with **offerings of the most sacred order** or **whether** he did so with **offerings of lesser sanctity, she is not betrothed.**

We pick up today with the *Mishnah* on 52b, a remarkably rich one that touches on several distinct categories of sanctified property and the question of whether any of them can serve as the object of *kiddushin*.

### Hamikadesh Bechelko

The *Mishnah* opens: *hamikadesh bechelko bein kodshei kodashim bein kodashim kalim* — if someone betroths a woman using his portion in either *Kodshei Kodashim* or *Kodashim Kalim*, she is not betrothed. The broader context of this *Mishnah* concerns not merely whether the object used for *kiddushin* has a *shaveh perutah* in the simple market-value sense, but deeper questions about the nature of ownership and the halachic status of the object being transferred.

To situate the discussion: a *kohen* who performs a *kiddushin* using his portion in a *Kodshei Kodashim* offering — for instance, his share of the meat of a *chatat*, which may only be eaten by male *kohanim* and only within the *Azarah* — is attempting to use that meat as a transactional object. Similarly, *Kodashim Kalim*, such as the meat of a *shlamim*, which may be eaten throughout Jerusalem by both *kohanim* and *Yisraelim* of either gender, is likewise the subject of the *Mishnah*'s ruling. In both cases: *lo kadesh*, the *kiddushin* does not take effect.

### Temple Layout

To appreciate why, it is worth mapping the relevant areas of the *Beit HaMikdash*. At the innermost sanctum stood the *Kodesh Kodashim* and the *Heichal*, fronted by the *Ulam*. Proceeding outward, one entered the *Azarah*, which comprised the *Ezras Kohanim* and the *Ezras Yisrael*. Beyond that lay the *Ezras Nashim*. The *Kodshei Kodashim* may never leave the *Azarah* for any purpose; the *Kodashim Kalim* may be eaten throughout Jerusalem. These distinctions will bear heavily on later discussions about the practicability of the *Mishnah*'s case.

### Mishulchan Gavoa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ נֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּמָעֲלָה מַעַל בַּה׳״ – לְרַבּוֹת קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹנוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה – לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּי קָא זָכוּ – מִשֻּׁלְחַן גָּבוֹהַּ קָא זָכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states with regard to the obligation to bring an offering for taking a false oath concerning unlawful possession of the property of another: "If any one sin, **and he commits a trespass against the Lord,** and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, or of robbery, or have oppressed his neighbor" (Leviticus 5:21). As the verse is discussing property belonging to another, the phrase "a trespass against the Lord" serves **to include** in the obligation of an offering a false oath with regard to possession of **offerings of lesser sanctity** of another person, **which are the property** of the owner; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** According to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, the portion of an offering of lesser sanctity that the priest receives belongs to him, so he should be able to betroth a woman with it. **You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, as Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says** that an offering of lesser sanctity belongs to its owner only **while** the animal is still **alive, but after** its **slaughter** it does **not** belong to the priest who receives portions from it. **What is the reason** for this? **When** the priests **receive** their portion after the animal has been slaughtered **they receive** their portion **from the table of the Most High,** and do not own the portion itself.

The *Gemara*'s short formulation for why neither category of *korban* portions can effect *kiddushin* is: *mishulchan gavoa ka zachu* — he is eating off of God's table. Even though the *kohen* has every right to eat that meat, it is not his meat. He has a right to eat *from* the meat that came, figuratively, from the *Mizbe-ach*; it is not his in any proprietary sense. Accordingly, he cannot use it to betroth a woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּחֶלְקוֹ, בֵּין קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים וּבֵין קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches:** With regard to a priest **who betroths** a woman **with his portion** of offerings, **whether** he did so with **offerings of the most sacred order or whether** he used **offerings of lesser sanctity, has not betrothed** her. The mishna does not speak of a priest who betroths a woman with a living offering of lesser sanctity but of one who betroths with the portion of the slaughtered animal he has received. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from it** that it is only in this case that she is not betrothed.

### Maaser Sheni/Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ. וּבַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ, וּבְשׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** One who betroths a woman **with second tithe, whether unwittingly** or **intentionally, has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he did so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her, but if he did so **intentionally he has betrothed** her. **And** with regard to one who betroths a woman **with consecrated** property belonging to the Temple treasury, if he does so **intentionally he has betrothed** her, **and** if he does so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** the opposite: If he does so **unwittingly he has betrothed** her, but if he does so **intentionally he has not betrothed** her.

The *Mishnah* then turns to two additional cases. First, *Maaser Sheni*: whether he betrothed the woman with *Maaser Sheni* fruit or money knowingly or unknowingly — *bein beshogeg bein bemezid* — the *kiddushin* does not take effect, according to Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: if it was unintentional (*shogeg*), it does not work; if it was intentional (*mezid*), it does. Second, *hekdesh*: if the object used for *kiddushin* was sanctified property — say, an object he had consecrated to the *Beit HaMikdash* and subsequently forgotten about — Rabbi Meir rules that if it was done intentionally (*mezid*), the *kiddushin* works; if unintentionally (*shogeg*), it does not. Rabbi Yehuda rules precisely the reverse: *shogeg* works, *mezid* does not.

This is a striking arrangement. By *Maaser Sheni*, Rabbi Yehuda says *mezid* is the operative case for valid *kiddushin*. By *hekdesh*, Rabbi Meir says *mezid* is operative. The two are exact mirror images of each other, and a great deal will need to be unpacked about the role of *shogeg* and *mezid* in each context. But the *Gemara* begins with *Kodshei Kodashim* and *Kodashim Kalim*.

One student raises a question about whether the *Mishnah* might be discussing *Maaser Sheni* money (*maaser kesef*) rather than produce — an interesting point, but not the subject of the current discussion. Another notices a shift in language: the *reisha* of the *Mishnah* uses *lo kadesh* rather than *eina mekudeshet*. This linguistic asymmetry is worth noting and will recur.

The *Gemara* raises an initial challenge: perhaps the *Mishnah* does not accord with the position of Rabbi Yosi HaGalili. A *Baraita* teaches that the verse *u-ma'ala ma'al b'Hashem* — which concerns one who swears falsely to deny possession of his neighbor's property, thereby using God's name to perpetrate a theft — is extended by Rabbi Yosi HaGalili to include *Kodashim Kalim*, because *Kodashim Kalim* is still considered the property of its owner (*mamono*). The argument: if Michael consecrates his sheep as a *shlamim*, entrusts it to someone for safekeeping, and that person falsely swears he never received it, is that person liable under this *parasha* of false oaths? Rabbi Yosi HaGalili says yes — because the verse's inclusion of *b'Hashem* extends the prohibition even to sanctified property, and *Kodashim Kalim* of all sanctified categories is the one most retaining its character as the owner's property (*Kodashim Kalim mamon ba-alim hem*).

According to Rabbi Yosi HaGalili, then, if someone consecrated a sheep as a *shlamim* and damages it, he would be liable to pay. If it is still his property, why should the *Mishnah*'s ruling that one cannot use it for *kiddushin* be in tension with Rabbi Yosi HaGalili's view?

The *Gemara* answers: *ki ka-amar Rabbi Yosi HaGalili mechayev* — Rabbi Yosi HaGalili only holds the animal belongs to you while it is still alive, prior to being offered. *Aval le-achar shechita lo* — once it has been slaughtered and offered, even according to Rabbi Yosi HaGalili, the principle of *mishulchan gavoa ka zachu* applies. One might think that after the slaughter, when one is actually eating and benefiting from the meat, one's proprietary connection would be stronger rather than weaker. But the *Gemara* is saying the opposite: at the moment the animal is offered on the *Mizbe-ach*, the owner has given it to God. Whatever portion returns to him in the form of meat is now received from God's table — it is God's bounty extended to the *kohen* or the owner, not the owner's own property reverting to him.

This does leave a conceptual puzzle — *v'tzarich iyun* — since intuitively, one's rights and practical control over the animal seem *less* clear before the offering than after, when one is actively eating it. Yet the principle stands: the living animal is still yours, the offered meat is not.

The *Mishnah* itself supports this reading: *deika nami diktani hamekadesh bechelko* — the *Mishnah* says "his *portion*," implying a share in the already-divided meat, which presupposes that the offering has already taken place. Thus *shma mina*, we can confirm the *Mishnah* is speaking post-offering.

One further linguistic puzzle: why does the *Mishnah* here say *lo kidesh* rather than *eina mekudeshet*? This question is noted and left for further examination of the different textual readings (*girsaot*).

## Rabbi Meir vs Yehuda

### Sumchos Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְאַחַר פְּטִירָתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְתַלְמִידָיו: אַל יִכָּנְסוּ תַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְכָאן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקַּנְתְּרָנִים הֵם, וְלֹא לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה הֵם בָּאִים אֶלָּא לְקַפְּחֵנִי בַּהֲלָכוֹת הֵם בָּאִים. דָּחַק סוֹמְכוֹס וְנִכְנַס. אָמַר לָהֶם: כָּךְ שָׁנָה לִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּחֶלְקוֹ, בֵּין קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים וּבֵין קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: After the death of Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda said to his students: Do not let the students of Rabbi Meir enter here** into our house of study, **because they are vexatious [*kanteranim*]. And they do not come to study Torah,** but **rather they come to overwhelm me with *halakhot*. Sumakhos,** a student of Rabbi Meir, **pushed and entered** anyway. **He said to them: This** is what **Rabbi Meir taught me:** With regard to a priest **who betroths** a woman **with his portion** of the offerings, **whether** he did so with **offerings of the most sacred order or whether** he used **offerings of lesser sanctity, he has not betrothed** her.

The *Gemara* now presents a striking narrative. The *Mishnah* as we have it is anonymous (*stam*), and *stam Mishnah* is Rabbi Meir. But behind the surface of the *Mishnah* lies a live dispute. *Tanu rabanan*: after Rabbi Meir's death, Rabbi Yehuda said to his students, "Do not allow Rabbi Meir's students to enter here." His reasoning: *mipnei shekantarnin hen* — they are troublemakers. *V'lo lilmod Torah hen ba-im* — they are not coming sincerely to learn. *Ela lekofachni behalachot hen ba-im* — they come to challenge me, to show off their dialectical virtuosity, not out of genuine pursuit of *halacha*.

Despite this prohibition, *Sumchos* — one of Rabbi Meir's most distinguished students — forced his way in and declared: "Here is what Rabbi Meir taught me: *hamekadesh bechelko bein kodshei kodashim bein kodashim kalim lo kadesh*." Once again, notably, *lo kadesh* rather than *eina mekudeshet*.

### Yehuda's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּעַס רַבִּי יְהוּדָה (עֲלֵיהֶם). אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא כָּךְ אָמַרְתִּי לָכֶם: אַל יִכָּנְסוּ מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְכָאן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקַּנְתְּרָנִים הֵם, וְלֹא לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה הֵם בָּאִים אֶלָּא לְקַפְּחֵנִי בַּהֲלָכוֹת הֵם בָּאִים. וְכִי אִשָּׁה בַּעֲזָרָה מִנַּיִן?!
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, **Rabbi Yehuda became angry with** his students. **He said to them: Didn't I say this to you: Do not let the students of Rabbi Meir enter here** into our house of study, **because they are vexatious? And they do not come to study Torah,** but **rather they come to overwhelm me with *halakhot*.** Rabbi Yehuda explained his objection to the statement of Rabbi Meir: This *halakha* is not relevant, **as from where** would **a woman** appear **in the** Temple **courtyard?** Women may not enter the area of the Temple courtyard where the priests eat the offerings of the most sacred order, so there is no reason to address an impossible scenario.

Rabbi Yehuda became angry at his own students for having allowed Sumchos in, and he questioned why this case was worth discussing at all. His challenge: *Kodshei Kodashim* cannot leave the *Azarah*. Women, Rabbi Yehuda presumes, have no business being in the *Azarah*. Therefore this entire case — a woman receiving *kiddushin* with *Kodshei Kodashim* — is a pure impossibility, a theoretical construct with no *halacha le-ma'aseh* application whatsoever. Far from reflecting genuine Torah inquiry, raising such a case proves, in Rabbi Yehuda's view, that Rabbi Meir's students are simply engaging in intellectual gamesmanship.

### Yosi Defends Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: יֹאמְרוּ: מֵאִיר שָׁכַב, יְהוּדָה כָּעַס, יוֹסֵי שָׁתַק – (דִּבְרֵי) תוֹרָה מָה תְּהֵא עָלֶיהָ? וְכִי אֵין אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין לְבִתּוֹ בַּעֲזָרָה? וְאֵין אִשָּׁה עֲשׂוּיָה לַעֲשׂוֹת לָהּ שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ בַּעֲזָרָה? וְעוֹד: דָּחֲקָה וְנִכְנְסָה מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei,** who was present, **said: They will say: Meir died, Yehuda grew angry,** and **Yosei remained silent; what will become of the words of Torah?** He said: In fact, this *halakha* is relevant; **but isn't it common** for **a man to accept betrothal for his daughter in the** Temple **courtyard?** There is no need to give the betrothal item directly to the woman; it can be given to her father. **And** additionally, **isn't it common** for **a woman to designate an agent for herself to accept her betrothal in the courtyard? And furthermore: What** would be the *halakha* if the woman **pushed and entered?** Since it is possible for her to do so, the *halakha* in such a case must be determined.

Rabbi Yosi would not let this stand. He reasoned that if he stayed silent, it would effectively dishonor Rabbi Meir's entire approach to learning. *Tifei Torah mah tehei aleiha* — what will become of the honor of Torah? He therefore rose to defend the case on three grounds. First: *v'chi ein adam masur lekabel kiddushin l'vito b'Azarah?* — perhaps a man is betrothing off his minor daughter, and *he* is the one in the *Azarah*, receiving the *kiddushin* on her behalf — no woman need be present at all. Second: *v'ein isha asuya la-asos lah shaliach lekabel kiddusheha b'Azarah?* — can a woman not appoint a male agent (*shaliach*) to receive her *kiddushin* within the *Azarah* on her behalf? Third: *v'od dachaka v'nichnesa mah hi?* — and if she forced her way in, what would the *halacha* be? — a pointed echo of Sumchos himself having forced his way into the *beit midrash*.

### Women in Azara

This brief exchange opens a window onto a broader and theologically weighty question: what was the status of women in the *Azarah*? The *Gemara* seemingly assumes women were not normally present in the *Azarah* proper — that it was essentially male space. *Rashi* states explicitly, citing the first chapter of *Keilim*, that women cannot enter the *Azarah*. *Tosafos*, however, sharply disagrees and says *Rashi* was not careful here (*v'lo dak*): nowhere is it stated that women are forbidden from entering the *Azarah*. *Tosafos* marshals several proofs: the *Mishnah* in *perek Kol HaPesulim* states that a woman may perform *shechita* of *kodashim* — which requires being in the *Azarah*; and both the *Sotah* and the *Nezirah* must enter the *Azarah* to perform *tenufah* with their *korbanot*, as they cannot send a representative for that rite.

*Tosafos* therefore reformulates the question: the issue is not that a woman cannot enter the *Azarah*, but rather that a woman entering the *Azarah* for the purpose of getting married there would be unusual and inappropriate — *milsa d'lo sh'chicha*, an uncommon and undignified circumstance — and it is the combination of a woman's presence and that particular purpose that strikes Rabbi Yehuda as absurd. All the *rishonim* and *poskim* accept *Tosafos*' position that women do enter the *Azarah* when bringing *korbanot* and similar occasions; the *Ezras Nashim* exists not as an exclusionary barrier but as a designated area. What remains is a question of degree: does a woman require a specific reason — like bringing a *korban* — to enter the *Azarah*, or is only a completely trivial or inappropriate purpose insufficient justification?

This discussion carries real relevance for the question of *mechitzah* in synagogues. Some authorities ground the requirement for a *mechitzah* in the model of the *Beit HaMikdash*, where the *Ezras Nashim* was separate from the *Ezras Yisrael*. The *Gemara* here has been read as suggesting the *Azarah* proper was exclusively male space. But even according to *Tosafos*' reading — which is the accepted view — there remains a presumption that the *Azarah* was predominantly male space during worship, with women entering only for specific ritual needs. Even Rav Moshe Feinstein, in addressing the question of whether a woman may enter an all-male *shul* without a *mechitzah* to say *Kaddish*, permitted it in principle while noting that in *Lita* women would do precisely that, standing in the back without incident.

The resolution of the case is in any event clear: Rabbi Yosi's three answers demonstrate that a woman receiving *kiddushin* in the *Azarah* is neither impossible nor purely theoretical. A father, an agent, or even an intrepid woman herself could place the scenario squarely within the realm of *halacha le-ma'aseh*, and the discussion is eminently worthwhile.

## Biblical Sources

### Baraita Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ: ״זֶה יִהְיֶה לְךָ מִקֹּדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים מִן הָאֵשׁ״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: ״לְךָ״ – וּלְכׇל צְרָכֶיךָ. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: כְּאֵשׁ. מָה אֵשׁ לַאֲכִילָה, אַף הוּא נָמֵי לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that the Sages discussed the issue of a priest who betroths a woman with his portion of offerings of the most sacred order: **Rabbi Yehuda says she is betrothed,** and **Rabbi Yosei says she is not betrothed. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Both of them derived** their opinions from **one verse,** which states that the priests have a right to a portion of offerings of the most sacred order, but they explained it in different ways. The verse states: **"This shall be yours of the most holy things, reserved from the fire"** (Numbers 18:9). **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the term **"yours"** indicates that the portion the priest receives is intended for you, i.e., a priest, **and for all your needs,** including betrothing a woman. **And Rabbi Yosei holds** that the verse compares the priest's portion **to the fire** on the altar: **Just as** the portion burned on the **fire** is **for** the fire's **consumption, so too,** the priest's portion **is also for consumption** alone, and not for any other purpose.

A *Baraita* at the bottom of 52b records the dispute directly: Rabbi Yehuda says *mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* takes effect; Rabbi Yosi says *eina mekudeshet* — it does not. Rabbi Yehuda thus consistently fights against Rabbi Meir's position: not only should we be discussing the case, but Rabbi Meir is wrong — she *is* betrothed. Rabbi Yosi sides with Rabbi Meir: *eina mekudeshet*.

### Two Interpretations

Rabbi Yochanan observes: *shneihem mikra echad darshu* — both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi derive their positions from the very same verse: *vezeh yiheyeh lecha mikodesh hakodashim min ha-esh* — "and this shall be yours from the holy of holies, from the fire" — referring to the *kohen*'s portion in the *chatat* meat. Rabbi Yehuda reads the word *lecha* — "to you" — as granting broad proprietary rights: this is yours, to use as you will, within whatever constraints govern its consumption. If you can find a legitimate use for it beyond eating it yourself, you may do so. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, reads *ke-esh* — "like the fire." Just as fire is there to consume, this portion too is solely for consumption. You have no ownership over it in any sense beyond eating it; it is *mishulchan gavoa*, God's table.

### Nimmnu v'Gamru

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּחֶלְקוֹ, בֵּין קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים וּבֵין קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ. וְרַב אֲמַר: עֲדַיִן הִיא מַחְלוֹקֶת. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִסְתַּבְּרָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They counted** the opinions among the Sages, **and they concluded:** With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with his portion** of the offerings, **whether** he did so with **offerings of the most sacred order or whether** he used the **offerings of lesser sanctity,** he **has not betrothed** her, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. **And Rav says: It is still a** matter of **dispute** and they did not reach that conclusion. **Abaye said: It is reasonable** to rule **in accordance with** the report **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who says that the *halakha* was decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei.

Rabbi Yochanan then reports, at the top of 53a: *nimnu ve-gamru* — they took a vote and reached a binding conclusion. *Hamekadesh bechelko bein kodshei kodashim bein kodashim kalim lo kadesh* — whether the betrothal was made with a portion in *Kodshei Kodashim* or even *Kodashim Kalim*, it does not take effect. The principle of *mishulchan gavoa* applies across the board: none of it is yours.

Rav disagrees: *adayin hi machlokes* — no vote was taken and no conclusion was reached; Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda remain in dispute, and there persists a position that one may indeed have a valid proprietary interest in the *korban* portions sufficient to effect *kiddushin*.

Abaye, however, aligns himself with Rabbi Yochanan: *ke-vasei de-Rabbi Yochanan mistabra* — it is more reasonable that Rabbi Yehuda ultimately reversed his position. The evidence for this comes from a *Baraita* drawn from the *Sifra* — *Toras Kohanim*, the *Tannaitic* commentary on *Vayikra* — whose anonymous author (*stam Sifra*) is assumed to be Rabbi Yehuda himself.

## Sifra Evidence

### Menachot Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְקִים מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה אֲשֶׁר תֵּאָפֶה בַּתַּנּוּר... לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״. יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁלֹּא קָמוּ תַּחְתֵּיהֶם בְּדַלּוּת, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עוֹפוֹת, שֶׁהֲרֵי קָמוּ תַּחְתֵּיהֶן בְּדַלּוּת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל נַעֲשָׂה בַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת... לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye quotes an extended *baraita* indicating that even Rabbi Yehuda accepted that the *halakha* was decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** priests **may not receive a share** of **meal-offerings in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings,** i.e., they may not exchange their portion of a meal-offering for the meat of an animal sacrifice of which they did not receive a portion? **The verse states: "And every meal-offering that is baked in the oven…shall all the sons of Aaron have"** (Leviticus 7:9–10). This verse emphasizes that the sons of Aaron must divide the meal-offering equally among themselves, without exchanging it for a portion of any other offering. One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **meal-offerings in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings since they do not substitute for them in** the case of **poverty.** Even one who is too poor to afford to bring an animal offering, e.g., in the case of a sin-offering determined on a sliding scale, does not bring a meal-offering in its stead. Since meal-offerings can never be brought in place of animal offerings, there is clearly no connection between them. **But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of **meal-offerings in exchange for** portions of **bird**-offerings, **since they do substitute for them in** the case of **poverty.** If one is so destitute that he cannot afford to bring a bird-offering he brings a meal-offering. Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "And all that is prepared in the deep pan…shall all the sons of Aaron have,"** again stressing that all must have an equal share in that meal-offering.

The *Baraita* asks: *minayin she-ein cholkin mincha kineged zvachim* — how do we know that when dividing *korbanos* among the *kohanim* at the end of the day, one may not swap a *mincha* offering against animal sacrifices? One might imagine that a *kohen* who prefers vegetarian food could say to his colleague: give me your share of the *mincha* and take my share of the meat in exchange. The answer derives from the verse: *v'chol mincha asher te'afeh ba-tanur l'chol bnei Aharon tihyeh ish ke-achiv* — every *mincha* shall belong to all the sons of Aaron, each equal to his brother. This teaches that each *korban* must be divided equally among the *kohanim*, and no one may substitute his portion in one type of offering for another's portion in a different type.

### No Swapping Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד עוֹפוֹת, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי דָמִים וְהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי קְמָחִים, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ עוֹפוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ וְהַלָּלוּ מִינֵי דָמִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעַל מַחֲבַת״. יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ עוֹפוֹת כְּנֶגֶד זְבָחִים, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם בַּיָּד וְהַלָּלוּ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם בִּכְלִי, אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ מְנָחוֹת כְּנֶגֶד מְנָחוֹת, שֶׁהַלָּלוּ וְהַלָּלוּ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם בַּיָּד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **meal-offerings in exchange for** portions of **bird**-offerings **since these,** bird-offerings, **are types of** offerings that involve **blood** sprinkled on the altar, **and those,** meal-offerings, **are types** of offerings made **of flour. But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of portions of **bird**-offerings **in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings, since both** categories are **types of** offerings that involve **blood** sprinkled on the altar. Therefore, **the** same **verse states: "And on a griddle,"** a seemingly superfluous phrase, which teaches that one may not receive a share even of bird-offerings in exchange for portions of animal offerings. The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **they may not receive a share** of **bird**-offerings **in exchange for** portions of animal **offerings because the actions,** i.e., killing, of **these** birds is **by hand,** by pinching the neck, **and the actions,** i.e., killing, of **those** animals is **with a utensil,** by slaughtering with a knife. **But** perhaps **they may receive a share** of **meal-offerings in exchange for** portions of other **meal-offerings, since the actions with** both **these and those** are **by hand.** Therefore, **the** next **verse states: "And every meal-offering mingled with oil…shall all the sons of Aaron have"** (Leviticus 7:10).

One might argue that the prohibition on swapping a *mincha* for animal sacrifices is specifically because a *mincha* can never fully substitute for an animal sacrifice in most contexts (*shelo bau tachteihem be-dalus*). But perhaps one could swap a *mincha* for bird sacrifices, since a *mincha* does substitute for a bird on the sliding-scale *korban* for the destitute. The *Sifra* counters this with a separate verse: *v'chol na-aseh ba-markheshet ul'chol benei Aharon tihyeh* — another instance of the same formulaic phrase applied to a different type of *mincha*, teaching that this type of swapping is also forbidden. The repetition is instructive: each additional invocation of "to all the sons of Aaron" comes to exclude a different proposed form of exchange.

### No Cross-Type Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְלְקוּ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים אֲבָל יַחְלְקוּ בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ כְּאָחִיו״ וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אִם עַל תּוֹדָה״ – כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְקִין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים כָּךְ אֵין חוֹלְקִין בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that they **may not receive a share of offerings of the most sacred order,** e.g., meal-offerings, in exchange for a portion of another similar offering, **but they may receive a share of offerings of lesser sanctity** in exchange for a portion of another similar offering. Therefore, **the** same **verse states** with regard to meal-offerings: "Shall all the sons of Aaron have, **one as well as another"** (Leviticus 7:10), **and near it** appears the verse: **"If** he offers it **for a thanks-offering"** (Leviticus 7:12), from which is derived: **Just as one may not receive a share** of one offering in exchange for a portion of another similar offering in the case **of offerings of the most sacred order, so too, one may not receive a share** of one offering in exchange for a portion of another similar offering in the case **of offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., a thanks-offering.

The *Sifra* continues to elaborate. Perhaps one cannot swap a *mincha* for animal sacrifices because one involves flour and the other blood (*hallu minei damim ve-hallu minei kemachim*), but perhaps bird sacrifices and animal sacrifices could be swapped since both involve blood. Another verse forecloses this. Perhaps *menachos* of different types could be swapped among themselves — surely one baked *mincha* could be exchanged for another baked *mincha*, since they are in the same category. Yet another verse prohibits this. Perhaps a *machavat mincha* — a hard, fried wafer — could be exchanged for another *machavat mincha*, since they are identical in type. The *Sifra* rules even this out, deriving from yet another verse that even within the same sub-genre, you cannot combine all offerings of that type and distribute them globally. Each individual *korban* must be divided among the *kohanim* equally — every offering brought that day must be apportioned separately.

### No Ownership Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִישׁ״ אִישׁ חוֹלֵק, אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם, וְאֵין הַקָּטָן חוֹלֵק וַאֲפִילּוּ תָּם. סְתָם סִפְרָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְהוּא קָאָמַר דְּלֵית בַּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ כְּלָל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* expounds this verse further: It states: **"One** as well as another [***ish*** *ke'aḥiv*]," which teaches that with regard to priests, **a man [*ish*]** who is an adult **receives a share even** if he is **blemished, but** a priest who is **a minor may not receive a share even** if he is **unblemished.** This *baraita* is found in the *Sifra*, a collection of halakhic *midrashim* on the book of Leviticus. And **who is** the *tanna* to whom **unattributed** statements in the ***Sifra*** are assigned? It is **Rabbi Yehuda, and** here **he says that no law of receiving a share** applies **to it at all,** which means that priests cannot exchange one portion of any type of offering for another portion, so they cannot use it to betroth a woman. **Conclude from it** that Rabbi Yehuda retracted his opinion and subscribes to the opinion that a priest does not own the portion of the offerings he receives.

The logical consequence of this *Sifra* passage is decisive. If a *kohen* cannot even swap his portion in one *mincha* for his colleague's portion in a different *mincha* of exactly the same type, he plainly has no real proprietary rights over his portion. If it were truly *his* property, he could do with it as he wishes — trade it, sell it, use it. The fact that no such latitude exists demonstrates that the *korban* portions do not belong to the *kohanim* in any meaningful sense of ownership. And if they have no ownership, they cannot use those portions to effect *kiddushin*.

Since the *stam Sifra* is Rabbi Yehuda, this passage demonstrates that Rabbi Yehuda ultimately conceded the point and agreed with Rabbi Meir: *chazar bo*. This is what the *Gemara* earlier meant by *nimnu ve-gamru*.

### Lechem Hapanim Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּכְרַב מִי לָא תַּנְיָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַצְּנוּעִים מוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶם, וְהַגַּרְגְּרָנִים חוֹלְקִים. מַאי חוֹלְקִים? חוֹטְפִים, כִּדְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁחָטַף חֶלְקוֹ וְחֵלֶק חֲבֵרוֹ, וְהָיוּ קוֹרְאִין אוֹתוֹ: ״בֶּן חַמְצָן״ עַד יוֹם מוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sota* 13:7): When the priests receive their portion of the shewbread each week, **the modest ones withdraw their hands** and do not take it, **and the gluttons receive** all the **shares** of the bread. This indicates that offerings may be apportioned according to the priests' wishes. The Gemara rejects this: **What** is the meaning of **receive** the **shares?** It does not mean that they exchange one portion for another with halakhic sanction; it means that **they** would **snatch** their colleagues' portions, **as it teaches** in the **latter clause** of that same *baraita*: **An incident** occurred **involving one who snatched his share and his colleague's share, and they called him ben Ḥamtzan,** son of the snatcher, **until the day he died.**

Rava challenges this conclusion. He cites a *Baraita* that describes the period following the death of Shimon HaTzaddik, when the divine blessing that had rested upon the *Lechem HaPanim* — which had sufficed to satisfy all the *kohanim* — departed. The showbread now produced no miraculous satiety, and each *kohen* received only a tiny portion. The *Baraita* says the *tzenu-im* — the modest, self-effacing *kohanim* — would withdraw their hands rather than scramble for a slightly larger crumb. *Ve-hagargarnim* — the ravenous ones — *cholkin*, would divide it. Rava initially reads *cholkin* as meaning they would subdivide and trade among themselves, implying that the *kohanim* did exercise proprietary rights over the *Lechem HaPanim* and could arrange exchanges — which would seem to support Rav's position that there remains a valid sense of ownership.

### Chotfim Clarified

The *Gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the word *cholkin*. *Mai cholkin? Chotfin* — "dividing" here does not mean trading or swapping portions across different *korbanos*. It means grabbing. The *Baraita*'s continuation confirms this: *ma'aseh be-echad she-chataf chelko ve-chelek chaveiro* — there was an incident where someone grabbed not only his own portion but his colleague's as well, and he was called *ben hamtzan* — "the grabber" — for the rest of his life. That is what *cholkin* describes: jostling for a larger share within the same *korban*, not exchanging portions across different offerings.

*Tosafos* adds a further nuance: even according to the view that you may not swap portions across different *korbanos*, it remains permissible to reassign your portion *within* a single *korban* — if you received your share of one *mincha* and you choose to give it to a colleague, that is a permissible act of generosity, not a prohibited trade. What is forbidden is substituting your portion in one *korban* for your colleague's portion in a different one. The *Lechem HaPanim* story describes no such cross-*korban* exchange; it describes pure grabbing within the same offering. Accordingly, it provides no support for the notion that *kohanim* have proprietary ownership over *korban* portions sufficient to use them for *kiddushin*.

The conclusion stands: *stam Sifra* Rabbi Yehuda, and he says in the clearest possible terms that there is no right of division or exchange with regard to *korban* portions — proving that even Rabbi Yehuda in the end agrees one cannot betroth a woman with them, whether the offering is *Kodshei Kodashim* or *Kodashim Kalim*.

## Maaser Sheni Status

### Rabbi Meir on Maaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג, בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, מֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּגְמָרָא: ״וְכׇל מַעְשַׂר הָאָרֶץ מִזֶּרַע הָאָרֶץ מִפְּרִי הָעֵץ לַה׳ הוּא קֹדֶשׁ לַה׳״ – לַה׳ הוּא, וְלֹא לְקַדֵּשׁ בּוֹ אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: One who betroths a woman with **second tithe, whether unwittingly** or **intentionally, has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he did so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her, but if he did so **intentionally he has betrothed** her. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? From where does Rabbi Meir derive that second tithe cannot be used for betrothal? **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, says in the name of tradition:** The verse states: **"And all the tithe of the land, whether of the seed of the land, or of the fruit of the tree, is the Lord's; it is holy to the Lord"** (Leviticus 27:30). This verse indicates that **it is for the Lord, but not for betrothing a woman with it.**

The *Mishnah* states: *Maaser Sheni bein beshogeg bein bemezid lo kadesh divrei Rabbi Meir* — whether knowingly or unknowingly, *Maaser Sheni* does not effect *kiddushin*, according to Rabbi Meir.

### Yehuda Disagrees

Rabbi Yehuda says: if it was done unknowingly (*shogeg*), it does not work; if it was done knowingly (*mezid*), it does.

### Kodesh La-Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, דִּכְתִיב: ״כֵּן תָּרִימוּ גַם אַתֶּם תְּרוּמַת ה׳״, וּתְנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! – דְּלָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״לַה׳״. וַהֲרֵי חַלָּה, דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ: ״תִּתְּנוּ לַה׳״, וּתְנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמוֹת – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! – דְּלָא כְּתִיב בַּיהּ ״קֹדֶשׁ״. וַהֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית, דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ: ״יוֹבֵל הִיא קֹדֶשׁ תִּהְיֶה לָכֶם״, וּתְנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! – דְּלָא כְּתִיב בַּיהּ ״לַה׳״. וַהֲרֵי תְּרוּמָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״קֹדֶשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל לַה׳ רֵאשִׁית תְּבוּאָתֹה״, וּתְנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! הָהוּא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתִיב. וּבַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ. וּבַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ, וּבְשׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this derivation from several instances where similar wording is employed in a different verse, yet one may use those items for betrothal. But **there is *teruma* of the tithe,** about **which it is written: "So you also shall set apart a gift of the Lord** of all your tithes" (Numbers 18:28)? **And we learned** in a mishna (58a): With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *teruma*, she is betrothed.** The Gemara answers: *Teruma* of the tithe is different, **as "to the Lord" is not written with regard to it,** indicating that it can be used for betrothal. The Gemara asks: **But there is *ḥalla*,** the portion of dough that one must separate and give to a priest, **about which it is written:** "Of the first of your dough **you shall give to the Lord"** (Numbers 15:21)? **And we** also **learned** in that same mishna: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *terumot*,** of which *ḥalla* is one type, **she is betrothed.** The Gemara answers: Although the verse does write "to the Lord," *ḥalla* is different, **as** the word **"holy" was not written with regard to it.** The Gemara asks: **But there is** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year, **about which it is written: "It is a Jubilee, it shall be holy to you"** (Leviticus 25:12)? **And we learned** by inferring from a mishna (50b) that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with produce of the Sabbatical** Year, **she is betrothed.** The Gemara answers: With regard to produce of the Sabbatical Year, the verse writes "holy," **but "to the Lord" is not written with regard to it.** The Gemara asks: **But there is *teruma*,** about **which it is written: "The Jewish people are the Lord's holy portion, His first fruits of the increase"** (Jeremiah 2:3)? **And we learned** in a mishna (58a) that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with *teruma*, she is betrothed.** The Gemara answers: **That** verse **was written with regard to the Jewish people,** who are called the Lord's holy portion, but not with regard to *teruma* itself. **And** with regard to one who betroths a woman **with consecrated** property belonging to the Temple treasury, if he does so **intentionally he has betrothed** her, **and** if he does so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** the opposite: If he does so **unwittingly he has betrothed** her, but if he does so **intentionally he has not betrothed** her.

Where does Rabbi Meir derive his position? The *Gemara* cites Rav Acha son of Rava, speaking in the name of the *Gemara* — meaning in the name of accepted tradition in the *beit midrash*: the verse *v'chol maaser ha-aretz mi-zera ha-aretz mi-pri ha-etz la-Hashem hu* — "and all the *maaser* of the land, from the seed of the land and from the fruit of the tree, belongs to God" — together with the characterization *kodesh la-Hashem*, "holy to God." The combination means *Maaser Sheni* is not yours; you are eating God's produce. Just as with *korban* portions the operative concept was *mishulchan gavoa ka zachu*, here the parallel formulation is *mamon gavoa hu* — it is entirely God's property — and you cannot use it to betroth a woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו מִמֵּילָא שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ? תַּרְגְּמַהּ רָבִין סָבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא״ – בַּהֲוָיָיתוֹ יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But don't you understand it by itself** from this verse? Since the verse compares the Jewish people to *teruma*, the categorization of "holy" evidently applies to *teruma* as well. **Ravin the Elder interpreted it before Rav:** The difference is that with regard to tithe **the verse states: "It is** for the Lord," which indicates: **As it is, it should be,** as consecrated.

The *Gemara* tests this logic by considering several other items that carry similar designations. *Trumat ma'aser* is called *trumas Hashem* — yet the *Mishnah* implies that one can use *terumah* to effect *kiddushin*. The *Gemara* distinguishes: *trumas Hashem* means it is God's *terumah* in the sense that God commanded it be given, not that it belongs to God. It is given to the *kohen* and remains his property. *Challah* carries the language *titnu la-Hashem* — "give it to God" — but it lacks the word *kodesh*, which would indicate that it is fully God's. *Shevi-it* produce carries the phrase *kodesh tihyeh lachem* — "it shall be holy to you" — but lacks the phrase *la-Hashem*. And while one *pasuk* equates the Jewish people to *terumah* and calls Israel *kodesh la-Hashem*, that is metaphorical; the *Gemara* notes this usage pertains to the Jewish people, not to actual *terumah*.

One might object that the metaphor implicitly confirms that *terumah* itself is *kodesh la-Hashem*. The *Gemara* accepts this logical inference but adds: even granting that *terumah* carries the designation *kodesh la-Hashem*, the verse by *Maaser Sheni* adds the crucial word *hu* — "it is" — specifically: *la-Hashem hu*. *Ravin Sava* explains in front of Rav that *hu* signifies permanence of status — it remains God's, with no movement away from that state. The combination of *kodesh*, *la-Hashem*, and *hu* in the *Maaser Sheni* verse places it in a uniquely inviolable category of divine ownership. That is Rabbi Meir's source.

### Mamon Gavoa

Thus, according to Rabbi Meir, *Maaser Sheni* is *mamon gavoa hu* — completely God's property, not yours. You may eat it, but eating it does not make it yours in a proprietary sense; you are partaking of God's *maaser*, and you have no right to transfer it to another.

## Intent Analysis

### Woman's Consent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב: שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן תַּרְתֵּי: שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֵׂר דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שִׁגְגַת הֶקְדֵּשׁ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בָּהֶם. חֲדָא – לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה רוֹצָה, וַחֲדָא – לְפִי שֶׁאֵין שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** if he betroths a woman **with consecrated** property belonging to the Temple treasury, if he does so **intentionally he has betrothed** her, and if he does so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** the opposite: If he did so **unwittingly he has betrothed** her, but if he does so **intentionally he has not betrothed** her. **Rabbi Ya'akov said: I learned two** *halakhot* **from Rabbi Yoḥanan,** the *halakha* of **an unwitting** error involving **tithe** according to the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** and the *halakha* of **an unwitting** error involving **consecrated** items according to the opinion **of Rabbi Meir. In both of these** cases **the woman is not betrothed with** the items. He proceeds to explain. **One** of these *halakhot* is **because the woman does not want** to become betrothed with the item, **and** the reason for the **other** is **because neither** the man nor the woman **wants** it. He adds: **And I do not know which of them** is due to the woman alone, and which is due to both parties.

Having established why Rabbi Meir holds *Maaser Sheni* never effects *kiddushin*, the *Gemara* turns to why Rabbi Yehuda draws a distinction between *shogeg* and *mezid*. Rabbi Yehuda fundamentally disagrees with Rabbi Meir's characterization of *Maaser Sheni* as *mamon gavoa*; in Rabbi Yehuda's view, *Maaser Sheni* belongs to the owner. If so, why should the distinction between knowing and unknowing matter at all?

The key insight is that the *shogeg/mezid* distinction here is not about the man's state of knowledge but about the woman's. If a man betroths a woman with what appears to be an ordinary apple and it turns out to be *Maaser Sheni* fruit, she faces a significant burden she never accepted: that apple cannot be eaten at home; it must be brought to Jerusalem. Had she known this, she would not have agreed to receive it as her *kiddushin*. We therefore presume — as a matter of *umdena*, an assessment of intent — that she would never have consented had she understood what she was receiving. Accordingly, when she is in a state of *shogeg*, the *kiddushin* fails. But if she knows it is *Maaser Sheni* and agrees nonetheless, then she has knowingly accepted the conditions — she is willing to take the apple to Jerusalem — and the *kiddushin* takes effect.

One might ask, as a *nafka mina* from knowing whose consent is the operative factor: if in practice the woman later says she would not have minded, does that change the outcome? *Rashi* notes that this is indeed the practical implication.

### Rav Yeimar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן: מַעֲשֵׂר – אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ מִשּׁוּם טִרְחָא דְאוֹרְחָא, אִיהוּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיקְנֵי אִיתְּתָא מִמֵּילָא. אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ – תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לָא נִיחָא לְהוּ דְּנִתַּחִיל הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל יְדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Let us see** which reason is applicable to which case. It must be that with regard to second **tithe,** which may be eaten only in Jerusalem, it is **not satisfactory for her** to be betrothed with it **due to the trouble of** transporting it all **the way** to Jerusalem to enjoy it, whereas it is **satisfactory for him** to betroth her with it, **since he acquires a woman by itself,** i.e., by the tithe alone, without having to spend additional money of his own. **But** in the case of **consecrated** items, it is **not satisfactory for the two of them that consecrated** property **be desacralized by their** using it for betrothal.

Rav Yeimar offers a clean resolution to the puzzle Rabbi Yochanan raised — which case involves only one unwilling party, and which involves both? By *Maaser Sheni*, according to Rabbi Yehuda, *the man has no problem* using his *Maaser Sheni* fruit for *kiddushin*. On the contrary, he benefits from it: *de-mitparka minei* — he is thereby relieved of the obligation to schlep the fruit to Jerusalem himself. The woman, however, would not have wanted it had she known. Thus *shogeg* does not work because of the woman's presumed non-consent, while *mezid* works because she knew and agreed.

### Me'ilah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִי לָא אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: מַעֲשֵׂר, אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא דְאוֹרְחָא, אִיהוּ לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם אוּנְסָא דְאוֹרְחָא?! אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ, בִּשְׁלָמָא אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ דְּנִתַּחִיל הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל יְדַהּ, אֶלָּא אִיהוּ מִי לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיקְנֵי אִיתְּתָא מִמֵּילָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov said the opposite is** more **reasonable. Couldn't it be said** that in the case of second **tithe** it is **not satisfactory for her** to be betrothed with it **due to the trouble of** transporting it all **the way,** and it is **not satisfactory for him** to betroth her with it **due to the** danger of an **accident along the way.** If the second tithe gets lost on the way to Jerusalem, she will not have derived any benefit from it. It will cause her distress to think she received nothing for her betrothal, and he does not want to cause her distress. **But** in the case of **consecrated** items, **granted** that with regard to **her,** it is **not satisfactory for her that consecrated** property **be desacralized through her, but why wouldn't** it be **satisfactory for him to acquire a woman by itself,** without the expenditure of redeeming a consecrated item? Therefore, the reasons for Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement cannot be determined by logic alone.

By *hekdesh*, the analysis is different. Here, *traveihu lo nicha lehu* — neither party would have consented had they known. Why not? *Lo nicha lehu de-nafik hekdesh le-chulin al yadeihu* — neither would have wanted to be the instrument by which consecrated property was transferred to non-sacred use, thereby transgressing the *issur me'ilah*. When the transaction is done unknowingly (*shogeg*), we presume retroactively that neither party would have wanted to participate in an act of *me'ilah* — and so the *kiddushin* is void. It is only when done intentionally (*mezid*) — when both parties knew it was *hekdesh* and proceeded anyway — that Rabbi Meir considers the *kiddushin* valid, since at that point both parties accepted the transaction with full knowledge of its nature. We will continue tomorrow with further analysis of why *shogeg* fails in these two cases and other approaches to understanding the interplay between intent and the underlying halachic status of *Maaser Sheni* and *hekdesh*.
