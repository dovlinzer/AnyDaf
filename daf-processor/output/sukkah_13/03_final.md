## Habitability Standard

### Shushe/Shavtzire Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָנֵי שׁוּשֵׁי וּשְׁווֹצְרֵי — מְסַכְּכִין בְּהוּ. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּשׁוּשֵׁי מְסַכְּכִין, בִּשְׁווֹצְרֵי לָא מְסַכְּכִין. מַאי טַעְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּסָרֵי רֵיחַיְיהוּ — שָׁבֵיק לְהוּ וְנָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **these** wide **licorice and wormwood** leaves, **one** may **roof his *sukka* with them,** since these are not consumed by people. Their legal status is that of any other plant; they are not susceptible to ritual impurity. **Abaye said: With licorice** leaves, **one** may **roof** his *sukka*; **with wormwood** leaves, **one** may **not roof** his *sukka*. **What is the reason** for this distinction? **Since their odor grows offensive** over time, **one abandons** the *sukka* **and exits.** It is inappropriate to establish a *sukka* in which it is impossible to remain.

We pick up at the bottom of 12b, dealing with various materials that are *kasher* or *pasul* for *sechach*, with an eye toward the question of *mekabel tumah*. Having discussed the different stages of processing flax into string, the *gemara* now presents a statement of Rav Yehuda: *hani shushe v'shavtzire* — two types of broad-leafed vegetables — may be used for *sechach*. Since the leaves grow from the ground and are not themselves food, they are not *mekabel tumah*, and *mesachchin bahem*, one may cover a *sukkah* with them.

Abaye disagrees in part. *Beshushe mesachchin*, with *shushe* one may use it, *beshavtzire lo mesachchin*, with *shavtzire* one may not. The reason: *shavtzire d'tzari reichaihu* — once the leaves of *shavtzire* begin to wither, they emit a foul odor, *v'shavik lehu v'nafik*, and one will abandon the *sukkah* and walk out. This reflects a consistent position of Abaye that we have encountered before: the question is not merely whether a given material satisfies the technical criteria for *sechach*, but whether it creates an actually habitable dwelling. In an earlier context, Abaye similarly held that strands of *sechach* hanging within ten *tefachim* render the *sukkah* uninhabitable. The implication here is stronger than a mere *lechatchila* recommendation — Abaye appears to hold that a structure set up in conditions that will foreseeably render it uninhabitable does not achieve the status of a *dirah*, and one is not *yotzei*.

### Hizmi/Higi Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: הָנֵי הִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי מְסַכְּכִין בְּהוּ. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּהִיזְמֵי — מְסַכְּכִינַן, בְּהִיגֵי — לָא מְסַכְּכִינַן. מַאי טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דְּנָתְרִי טַרְפַיְיהוּ, שָׁבֵיק לַהּ וְנָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said:** With regard to **these thorns and shrubs, one** may **roof** the *sukka* **with them. Abaye said: With thorns, one** may **roof** his *sukka*; **with shrubs, one** may **not roof** his *sukka*. **What is the reason** for this distinction? **Since their leaves fall** over time and they are apt to fall into the food and disturb those in the *sukka*, **one abandons** the *sukka* **and exits.**

The same dynamic appears at the top of 13a. Rav Hanin bar Rava rules that *hizmi* and *higi* — two varieties of thorn bush — may both be used for *sechach*, since once cut from the ground they are not *mekabel tumah*. Abaye again splits the ruling: *behizmi mesachchin*, the first type of thorn bush is acceptable, *higi lo mesachchin*, the second is not. Why? *Keivan d'natri tarfaihu shavik la v'nafik* — the leaves of *higi* naturally drop off once the branch is cut and left exposed, and one will be left not only with diminishing *sechach* but with leaves falling into one's food and onto one's head, making the *sukkah* uninhabitable. Abaye's position, here as before, is that these practical conditions of habitability are halachically determinative, not merely advisory — whether his ruling operates on a biblical or rabbinic level remains an open question, but the consistency of the principle is clear.

## Natural vs Man-Made

### Palm Shoots Bundle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: הַאי אַפָּקוּתָא דְּדִיקְלָא מְסַכְּכִין בְּהוּ. אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲגִידִי, אֶגֶד בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד. אַף עַל גַּב דַּהֲדַר אָגֵיד לְהוּ, אֶיגֶד בְּחַד לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **this offshoot of the** trunk of the **palm tree,** from which several branches emerge; **one** may **roof the *sukka* with it. Although** the branches **are** naturally **bound, a binding at the hand of Heaven is not considered a binding.** Furthermore, **although one then binds** the branches together at the end removed from the trunk, where they grow apart into separate branches, and roofs with them, the *sukka* is fit, since if **one binds** a bundle that is already bound **into one** unit **it is not considered a binding.**

The *gemara* returns to the central concern of the *mishnah*: a bundle of wood may not be used for *sechach*, and the task now is to define precisely what constitutes a bundle. Rav Gidel in the name of Rav teaches: *hai afkusta d'dikla* — a cluster of palm shoots that grow together from a common base — *mesachchinan*, may be used for *sechach*. Rashi explains this as a natural bundle, a collection of shoots joined organically at their source. A useful analogy is a bunch of celery stalks, all naturally joined at the bottom — though of course celery itself would be disqualified as *mekabel tumah* since it is food. The ruling here is that *af al gav de'agidi*, even though these shoots are bound together, *eged bidei Shamayim lo shmei eged* — a bundle formed by heaven, that is, joined naturally rather than by human action, does not carry the legal status of a bundle. There is therefore no problem using it for *sechach*.

### String Addition

Rav Chisda in the name of Ravina bar Shila adds a further refinement. Suppose one takes such a naturally joined cluster — already united at the base — and ties a string around it at the top, thereby making what appears to be a human-made bundle. Does this create an *eged*? The answer is no: *eged bechad lo shmei eged* — bundling something that is already a single unit does not constitute an act of bundling. Since the shoots were already treated as one organic unit by virtue of their natural connection, adding a cord does not transform them into multiple discrete units artificially held together. Even after the string is added, no *eged* has been created, and the material remains usable for *sechach*.

### Reed Stalks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: הָנֵי דּוּקְרֵי דְקָנֵי מְסַכְּכִין בְּהוּ. אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲגִידִי נִינְהוּ — אֶגֶד בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד. אַף עַל גַּב דַּהֲדַר אָגֵיד לְהוּ — אֶיגֶד בְּחַד לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Ravina bar Sheila said:** With regard to **these offshoots of reeds, one** may **roof the *sukka* with them. Although** the branches **are** naturally **bound, a binding at the hand of Heaven is not considered a binding.** Furthermore, **although one then binds** the reeds together at the other end, the *sukka* is fit, since if **one binds** a bundle that is already bound **into one** unit **it is not considered a binding.**

Rav Chisda in the name of Ravina bar Shila applies the identical principle to a different plant: *hani dukrei de'kanei* — a cluster of reeds growing together from a common source — *mesachchinan behu*, may be used for *sechach*, *af al gav de'agidi*, even though they are naturally joined, *eged bidei Shamayim lo shmei eged*. And similarly, *af al gav de'hadar agdei lehu*, even if one subsequently ties a cord around these reeds, *eged bechad lo shmei eged* — binding what is already one unit creates no *eged*. The principle is identical to the palm shoot case; the *chiddush*, if any, would be appreciated by one who knew the practical difference between the two plants, but the legal logic is the same.

### Kanim/Dukranim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: קָנִים וְדוּקְרָנִין מְסַכְּכִין בָּהֶן. קָנִים פְּשִׁיטָא! אֵימָא: קָנִים שֶׁל דּוּקְרָנִין מְסַכְּכִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **reeds and spades, one** may **roof** a *sukka* **with them.** The Gemara asks: The fact that one may roof his *sukka* with **reeds** is **obvious.** After all, they meet all the criteria of fit roofing. Rather, **say:** With regard to **these offshoots of reeds, one** may **roof the *sukka* with them.**

A *baraita* is then cited in support: *kanim ve'dukranim mesachchinan bahen* — reeds and poles, one may use for *sechach*. The *gemara* objects that reeds alone are obvious — of course one may use plain reeds for *sechach*, since they are not a bundle at all. The *gemara* therefore reads the phrase as a compound: *kanim shel dukranin mesachchinan bahen* — it refers to reeds that are *of* the type of naturally joined clusters, i.e., reeds growing together in a naturally united bunch. The *baraita* thus confirms the principle already stated: a naturally joined cluster of reeds is not disqualified as a bundle, and is valid *sechach*.

## Shem Livai Doctrine

### Swamp Maror

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: הָנֵי מְרָרְיָתָא דְאַגְמָא — אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the above *halakha*, the Gemara cites another statement that **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Ravina bar Sheila said: With these bitter herbs of a marsh, a person fulfills his obligation on Passover.**

Rav Chisda in the name of Ravina bar Shila — the same chain of tradition that produced the rulings about palm shoots and reeds — now states a *halacha* in an entirely different area: *hani maruata de'agma*, the bitter herbs of the swamp, *adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato be'Pesach*, one may use them to fulfill the obligation of *maror* on Pesach. The *gemara* flags this as a digression from the topic of *sechach* — it appears here only because it was transmitted by the same Sage. The *chiddush* is that this variety has what is called a *shem livai*, a qualifying or attendant name: it is not simply called *maror* but *maror* of the swamp. One might have thought that this descriptive qualifier creates a distinct subspecies, excluded from the Torah's requirement of *maror*, just as certain varieties of *ezov* are excluded.

### Ezov Varieties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵזוֹב, וְלֹא אֵזוֹב יוֹן, וְלֹא אֵזוֹב כּוֹחֳלִי, וְלֹא אֵזוֹב מִדְבָּרִי, וְלֹא אֵזוֹב רוֹמִי, וְלֹא אֵזוֹב שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שֵׁם לְוַוי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection to his** opinion. With regard to every mitzva that requires use of hyssop, one takes standard **hyssop and neither a hyssop** that grows in **Greece, nor stibium hyssop, nor desert hyssop, nor Roman hyssop, nor** any other kind of **hyssop whose** name is accompanied by **a modifier.** The same should hold true for the mitzva of bitter herbs; bitter herbs of the marsh, whose name is accompanied by a modifier, are not the bitter herbs mentioned in the Torah.

This concern is borne out by a *baraita*: when the Torah says to use *agudat ezov*, a bundle of hyssop — both in the context of the *Korban Pesach* and in the sprinkling of the ashes of the *Parah Adumah* — this excludes *Eizov Yavan* (Greek hyssop), *Eizov Koachli* (bluish hyssop), *Eizov Midbari* (wilderness hyssop), *Eizov Romi* (Roman hyssop), and in general *velo eizov sheyesh lo shem livai* — any hyssop that carries a qualifying attendant name. Such a name signals that the variety is a distinct subspecies, not the standard *ezov* intended by the Torah. If the Torah intended these varieties it would have said so; its silence in favor of the unqualified term means only the plain species is acceptable. The question is then unavoidable: why should swamp *maror* not be similarly excluded?

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֹּל שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּנָּה שְׁמוֹ קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה, וּבָאתָה תּוֹרָה וְהִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שֵׁם לְוַוי. וְהָנֵי לֹא נִשְׁתַּנָּה שְׁמַיְיהוּ קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in response: There is a distinction between the cases. **Every** species **whose name was differentiated prior to the giving of the Torah,** i.e., the distinction between its different subspecies predated the Revelation at Sinai, **and the Torah** then **came and was particular about** one specific subspecies, **it is known that** the species **has** other subspecies identified with **a modifier** that are unfit for use in fulfilling the mitzva. **And these** bitter herbs, **their names were not differentiated prior to the giving of the Torah at all;** all the subspecies were known simply as bitter herbs. Therefore, when the Torah requires bitter herbs, one may fulfill the mitzva with all subspecies of bitter herbs.

Abaye answers with a historical principle: *kol shenishtanah shemo kodem Mattan Torah* — any species whose variant name already existed *before* the giving of the Torah is excluded. The Torah used the unqualified term *ezov*, and if at that time there already existed species known distinctively as Greek hyssop, Roman hyssop, and the rest, then the Torah's unqualified usage is understood as deliberately exclusive of those named varieties. *Uva haTorah vehikpida aleha* — the Torah, by using only the generic term, implicitly rejected those variants that had already been distinguished by name.

By contrast, the bitter herbs of the swamp were not recognized as a distinct subspecies at the time of *Mattan Torah* — *lo nishtanah shemaihu kodem Mattan Torah klal*. They received their descriptive label only later. The Torah's word *maror* was therefore never understood to exclude them, and they remain within its scope. This is a striking answer — historically grounded, turning on the question of which plant names were established prior to *Mattan Torah* — and one might fairly ask how the Sages determined which names antedated the giving of the Torah. That is indeed the challenge implicit in the reasoning, and it is acknowledged to be a somewhat unusual type of argument.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָנֵי — מְרָרְיָתָא סְתָמָא שְׁמַיְיהוּ, וְהַאי דְּקָרֵי לְהוּ מְרָרְיָתָא דְאַגְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּמִשְׁתְּכַח בְּאַגְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different explanation. Actually, the **name** of this plant **is merely bitter herbs** without a modifier. **And** the fact **that one calls them bitter herbs of the marsh is because they are** typically **found in the marsh.** Therefore, there is no reason that they may not be used to fulfill the mitzva on Passover.

Rava offers a different approach entirely. *Hani merirata stama shemaihu* — these plants that go by the name "swamp *maror*" are, in ordinary usage, simply called *maror*. *Ve'hai dekari lehu merirata de'agma*, the reason people add the word "swamp" is *mishum demishtachach be'agma* — because that is where they happen to be found. The qualifier is purely descriptive, like saying "the peppers from the market" rather than identifying a formal subspecies. It is an adjective, not a proper noun; a geographical description, not a taxonomic category.

This distinction between a merely descriptive modifier and a species-defining name is the heart of the *shem livai* doctrine. The rejected varieties of *ezov* — Greek hyssop, Roman hyssop — carry names that identify them as formally distinct from plain *ezov*, names by which people categorize them as a different thing altogether. The swamp *maror* is not named as a different species; people still understand it as *maror*, and the modifier does no more than tell you where it grows. The *gemara* notes wryly that this same distinction between a proper category name and a merely descriptive one recurs in various contexts — the question, as Rava frames it, is essentially whether a qualifier functions as a proper noun or only as an adjective. One might also note that the Torah uses the word *merorim* — bitter herbs in the plural — which on its face suggests a broad and inclusive category, making it somewhat surprising that the *gemara* does not invoke this as an independent basis for permitting multiple varieties. The *gemara* does not raise this point here, though it is addressed elsewhere.

## Minimum Bundle Size

### Three Stalks Bundle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֶיגֶד בְּחַד — לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד. שָׁלֹשׁ — שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד. שְׁנַיִם — מַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבָּנַן. דִּתְנַן: מִצְוַת אֵזוֹב, שְׁלֹשָׁה קְלָחִים וּבָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה גִבְעוֹלִין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִצְוַת אֵזוֹב שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּבְעוֹלִין, וּשְׁיָרָיו שְׁנַיִם, וְגַרְדּוּמָּיו כׇּל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: If one bound one** item, even if he did so with a knot, **it is not considered a binding.** If one bound **three** items together, everyone agrees that **it is considered a binding.** If one bound **two** items, it is the subject of **a dispute** between **Rabbi Yosei and the Rabbis, as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to all matters that involve **the mitzva of hyssop,** the requirement is to have **three stalks** with their roots, **and on them three stems,** one on each stalk. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The **mitzva of hyssop** fundamentally requires **three stems.** If the bundle of hyssop was rendered incomplete, **its remnants** are fit for use with **two** stems. If all the stems broke, the hyssop is fit for use, as long as the **stumps of its** central stem remain **any size.**

Returning to the central topic of bundles and *sechach*, Rav Chisda restates the principle established earlier — *eged bechad lo shmei eged*, binding something that is already a single natural unit does not create a bundle — and now extends it in the other direction: *eged shalosh shemei eged*, binding three items together does constitute an *eged* and would be problematic. This sets a lower bound: three stalks artificially bound together is a bundle in the legal sense. The question then becomes: what about two? Is that a bundle?

### Hyssop Mitzvah

Rav Chisda notes that the question of two versus three is in fact a *machloket* — the dispute of Rabbi Yossi and the *Rabanan* — not by the laws of *sukkah* directly, but in the context of another *halacha* that turns on the concept of an *agudah*, and the same principles presumably apply to *sechach* as well.

The relevant *mishnah* is cited: *mitzvat ezov* — the obligation of using a bundle of hyssop (whether for the blood of the *Korban Pesach* or for the sprinkling of the ashes of the *Parah Adumah*) — requires *bishloshah kelachim*, three stalks, each bearing its ear. Rabbi Yossi then adds: *mitzvat ezov sheloshah givolim* — the *mitzvah* does indeed begin with three stalks, *veshirav shenayim* — but if one stalk breaks off after the bundle is assembled, two suffice to retain the bundle's valid status. And *vegardumo kol shehu* — even a remnant of one of the broken stalks, however small, preserves the identity of the bundle.

This last point reflects a broader principle in *halacha*: the threshold required to achieve a legal status is often higher than the threshold required to maintain it. Something must meet a high standard to be first classified as food susceptible to *tumah*, but once classified, it retains that status until it falls below a far lower threshold — unfit even for animal consumption. The same dynamic governs *tzitzit*: a string must meet minimum length requirements when first tied, but if it subsequently breaks, even a small remnant is valid. Once identity is established, it is sticky.

### Yossi vs Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין מִדִּשְׁיָרָיו שְׁנַיִם, תְּחִילָּתוֹ נָמֵי שְׁנַיִם, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה — לְמִצְוָה. וּמִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה לְמִצְוָה, לְרַבָּנַן שְׁלֹשָׁה לְעַכֵּב. — וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵזוֹב תְּחִילָּתוֹ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁיָרָיו אֶחָד פָּסוּל, וְאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא תְּחִילָּתוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה וּשְׁיָרָיו שְׁנַיִם! אֵיפוֹךְ: לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה לְעַכֵּב, לְרַבָּנַן שְׁלֹשָׁה לְמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It enters our minds** to say: **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Yosei said that for the bundle of hyssop to be fit for the mitzva after the fact **its remnants are two,** apparently **its origins** were **also two** stalks. **And** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** that the binding includes **three** plants, that is the requirement **for** the **mitzva** to be performed *ab initio*. **And from** the fact **that Rabbi Yosei** requires **three** plants only **for** the **mitzva** to be performed *ab initio*, conclude that **the Rabbis,** who disagree with him, hold that failure to include **three** stalks in the bundle **renders it unfit** for the mitzva. Apparently, the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosei dispute whether it is two or three items that are necessary to be considered a binding. — **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said:** With regard to the **hyssop** bundle, if **its origins** were **two** stalks **and its remnants are one,** it is **unfit. And it is fit only when its origins** were **three and its remnants are two.** Rather, **reverse** the opinions in the mishna: According **to Rabbi Yosei,** failure to include **three** stalks in the bundle **renders it unfit** for the mitzva; according **to the Rabbis, three** is the requirement **for** the **mitzva** to be performed *ab initio*.

At first glance, one might read Rabbi Yossi's position as follows: since he allows the bundle to retain validity when only two stalks remain, perhaps he actually considers two stalks sufficient from the start, and the mention of three is merely a *lechatchila* recommendation. On this reading, *sheloshah l'mitzvah* — three is the ideal but not strictly required. The *Rabanan*, by contrast, might hold that three is absolutely required *b'dieved* — *sheloshah l'akev*. This framing would yield a genuine dispute about the minimum size of a bundle: two according to Rabbi Yossi, three according to the *Rabanan*.

However, a *baraita* forecloses this reading of Rabbi Yossi: *ezov techilato shenayim u'shiyarav echad pasul* — a bundle of hyssop that began with only two stalks is invalid even if one remains; *v'eino kasher ad she'techilato shlosha u'shiyarav shenayim* — it is valid only if it began with three and at least two remain. Rabbi Yossi thus unambiguously requires three stalks to begin with. The *gemara* therefore reverses the framing: *l'Rabbi Yossi shlosha l'akev* — for Rabbi Yossi, three is absolutely required; *ul'rabanan shlosha l'mitzvah* — for the *Rabanan*, three is the ideal but two suffices even at the outset.

### Debate Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵזוֹב תְּחִילָּתוֹ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁיָרָיו אֶחָד — כָּשֵׁר, וְאֵינוֹ פָּסוּל עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא תְּחִלָּתוֹ וּשְׁיָרָיו אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* supporting this understanding. **And this was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the **hyssop** bundle, if **its origins** were **two** stalks **and its remnants** are **one,** it is **fit. And it is unfit only when its origins and its remnants** are **one.** Clearly, this is the opinion of the Rabbis.

The settled reading of the dispute is therefore: Rabbi Yossi holds that an *agudah* requires three items minimally — one must begin with three and may finish with two. The *Rabanan* hold that two items at the outset suffice — *techilato shenayim*, with one remaining still valid. This is confirmed by a *baraita* supporting the *Rabanan's* position: *techilato shenayim veshiuro echad kasher*, beginning with two and ending with one is valid; *v'eino pasul ad shetechilato veshiuro echad*, it is only invalid if one starts with a single stalk from the beginning. So the *machloket* is genuine: Rabbi Yossi sets the minimum at three; the *Rabanan* set it at two. Presumably this same dispute governs the minimum number of pieces of wood that would constitute a bundle problematic for *sechach* — though one should note that the *Yerushalmi*, as cited by *Tosafot* earlier, takes a far more expansive practical approach and sets the disqualifying bundle size at twenty-five, reflecting not an abstract minimum bundle definition but the realistic size of a wood bundle that one would carry and place on a roof.

### Pirkei Avot Groups

A *Tosafot* worth noting raises a difficulty here. The *gemara*'s assertion that three constitutes a minimum *agudah* appears to be in tension with the *mishnah* in Pirkei Avot, which expounds the minimum size of a group using the word *agudah* and arrives at five. The *mishnah* there states: ten people studying Torah together have the *Shechinah* among them, as the verse says *Elokim nitzav ba'adat E-l* — God stands in the divine congregation, and a congregation is ten. How do we know even five? From the verse *va'agudato al eretz yesadah* — His *agudah* is established upon the earth, teaching that an *agudah* is five. How do we know even three? From a verse implying a middle, which requires three. *Tosafot* notes that if *agudah* in Pirkei Avot means five, this seems inconsistent with the *gemara*'s ruling that *agudah* begins at three.

*Tosafot* records an alternative textual tradition that reverses the derivations — using the "midst" verse for five and the *agudah* verse for three — but finds this unsatisfying. His preferred resolution is that for *halacha*, an *agudah* is indeed three, as the *gemara* here establishes, and this is the standard for *agudat ezov* and for the *sechach* question. The verse in Pirkei Avot — *va'agudato al eretz yesadah* — refers to five for a different reason: the adjacent verse says *af yadi yisadah aretz*, "My hand has shaped the earth," and a hand has five fingers, so *agudah* there is understood by context as a fistful of five. The *halacha* of minimum bundle size at three thus stands.

## Bundle Classification

### Counting Bundle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ מָרִימָר: הָנֵי אִיסּוּרְיָיתָא דְסוּרָא — מְסַכְּכִין בְּהוּ. אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲגִדָן — לְמִנְיָנָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּאֲגִדָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Mareimar taught:** With regard to **these bundles** of reeds **from Sura** that are bound for sale, **one** may **roof** the *sukka* **with them. Although** the seller **bound them, he bound them merely to** ascertain **the number** more readily, and they will not remain bound.

Having established the minimum size of an *eged*, the *gemara* turns to further refinements of what constitutes a bundle in the first place. Maremar teaches: *hani isarei yasda de-Sura* — those bundles of stalks from Sura — *mesachchinan behu*, one may use them for *sechach*. The question is why, given that they are physically bound together. The answer: *le-minyana be-alma hu de-agdan* — they are bundled solely for the purpose of counting, not for the purpose of carrying or transport. The analogy would be something like a dozen roses or a rubber-banded bunch of broccoli at a market — the binding exists merely to constitute a countable unit for sale, not because the items need to be held together for any other purpose.

This is a striking refinement of the law. The original concern with bundles used for *sechach* was a rabbinic decree rooted in the scenario of someone carrying large bundles of wood and placing them overhead — the *gezeirah* targets the reality of wood being bundled for transport and then used as *sechach* without being disassembled. Where the bundling was never done for that purpose — where it exists solely to define a unit for commercial counting — it does not carry the character of a true *eged*, and the material may be used freely.

### Trappers' Huts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: הָנֵי צְרִיפֵי דְאוּרְבָּנֵי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוּתְּרָה רָאשֵׁי מַעֲדַנִּים שֶׁלָּהֶן — כְּשֵׁרִין. וְהָא אֲגִידִי מִתַּתַּאי! אֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּשָׁרֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said:** With regard to **these huts** made **of willow branches, once their upper ties** holding them together **are undone,** they are **fit** roofing. The Gemara asks: **But aren't they** still **tied from below? Rav Pappa said:** Rabbi Abba is referring to a case **where he unties them** from below as well.

Rabbi Abba introduces another case: *hanei tzrifei de-orbanei* — huts used by trappers or hunters, which are constructed from willow branches arranged in a conical form like a blind or teepee. Rashi describes the structure as large willow branches assembled in a cone, with the top knotted together and the base woven or interlaced to maintain the shape. Rabbi Abba rules: *keivan she-hutru rashei madaneihem keshayrin* — once the knot at the top is undone, the material is valid for *sechach*.

The *gemara* immediately asks: but even after the top knot is removed, the branches remain woven together at the base — *ve-ha agidi mi-tataei*. This is precisely the question raised in another context about woven mats: does weaving constitute bundling? Rav Pappa answers simply: one unweaves the bottom as well. But this generates an obvious objection — if both the top knot and the bottom weave must be undone, what is the *chiddush* of the teaching?

### Structural Weaving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וְאָמַר) רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּלָא שָׁרֵי לְהוּ, כׇּל אֶגֶד שֶׁאֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי לְטַלְטְלוֹ — לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֶגֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Even** if **you say** that Rabbi Abba is referring to a case **where one does not untie them** from below, they are fit for *sukka* roofing, as **any binding** that is **not destined to be moved is not considered a binding.** Since these huts are untied from above, were one to attempt to move them, they would fall apart.

Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehuda resolves the difficulty with a principled ruling: *kol eged she-eino asui le-taltelo lo shmei eged* — any bundling that is not made for the purpose of carrying the object does not constitute an *eged* in the legal sense. The weave at the base of the trapper's hut is not there to bundle the branches together for transport; it is there to give the structure its conical shape. It is form-giving, not bundle-making. Discrete units held together by an outer cord for ease of carrying — that is the paradigm of an *eged*. Structural interlacing that creates a shape is a different physical and legal reality.

This principle also clarifies why woven mats, when not themselves *mekabel tumah*, are valid *sechach* even though they are woven: the weaving serves a structural function, not a bundling function, and does not render the mat an *eged*. Rashi's formulation of the principle adds a related point: the bottom weave of the trapper's hut is not designed to keep the object together when moved — if one were to try to carry the structure, it would fall apart without the top knot. The weave alone does not hold it together for transport, reinforcing that it cannot be considered a carrying-bundle. Even if one reads Rashi more narrowly on this point, the broader ruling of Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehuda stands: purpose determines classification.

## Maror/Tumah

### Maror Barrier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְרָקוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח, מְבִיאִין אֶת הַטּוּמְאָה, וְאֵין חוֹצְצִין בִּפְנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה, וּפוֹסְלִין בַּסּוּכָּה מִשּׁוּם אֲוִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דִּלְכִי יָבְשִׁי פָּרְכִי וְנָפְלִי, כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Abba said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **vegetables** about **which the Sages said: One fulfills his obligation** to eat bitter herbs **on Passover,** if they are spread over a source of ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, they **transmit ritual impurity,** and the impurity spreads to objects beneath them. **And,** nevertheless, the Sages decreed that **they do not serve as a barrier before the spread of ritual impurity.** The impurity breaches roofing made of these vegetables and rises upward, as if there were no covering over it. If one roofs a *sukka* with these vegetables, it is as if they were not there at all, **and they render a *sukka* unfit due to** the unfitness of **airspace.** Just as three handbreadths of airspace in the roofing renders a *sukka* unfit, so too, three handbreadths of these vegetables in the roofing renders a *sukka* unfit. **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? **Since when they dry they crumble and fall,** even while fresh, they are **as one that is not there.**

Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav Huna now transitions to a topic that doubles back on the earlier discussion of *maror* and *tumah* — an example of the *gemara*'s characteristic way of returning to seemingly digressive themes and finding new applications for them. The ruling: *yerakot she-amru chachamim adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato ba-Pesach* — the vegetables with which the Sages ruled one may fulfill the obligation of *maror* on Pesach — these vegetables *mevi'in et ha-tumah*, they transmit *tumah*, *ve-ein chotzin bifnei ha-tumah*, but they do not block *tumah* from proceeding past them.

To understand this, one must recall the laws of *ohel tumah*: when a corpse lies beneath a structure that is at least one *tefach* by one *tefach* and elevated off the ground, the *tumah* spreads underneath that structure but does not pass above it — the structure is *mevi* below and *chotzetz* above. Normally these two functions go together. Some materials, however, have the worst of both worlds: they spread *tumah* underneath while also allowing *tumah* to pass above. These *maror* vegetables fall into that category.

### Avir Disqualification

The same properties that govern the *tumah* rules for these vegetables also govern their status for *sechach*: *u-poslin ba-sukkah mi-shum avir* — they invalidate the *sukkah* as though they were air, not merely as *pasul sechach*. The reason: *dikivan dechi yavshi parchi ve-nafli* — once these vegetables dry out, they crumble and fall away. Since they will inevitably disintegrate, *keman deleitnehu dami* — the Sages regard them even now as though they were no longer there. This same logic drives the *tumah* ruling: because the material will crumble, and one might not notice when it does, the rabbis preemptively treat it as already crumbled — hence no barrier above, though it still generates *tumah* spread below by virtue of its present physical existence. The explanations for the two rulings are one and the same.

### Air vs Pasul

This leads to a clarification of why air is worse than *pasul sechach*. *Pasul sechach* — invalid *sechach* such as food, materials that are *mekabel tumah* — invalidates the *sukkah* only when it constitutes four *tefachim* or more. Moreover, even larger quantities of *pasul sechach* can sometimes be accommodated through the principle of the bent wall: if *pasul sechach* is placed contiguous to a wall, it can be treated as a continuation of the wall for up to four *amos*, leaving the *sukkah* valid. Air, by contrast, becomes problematic at three *tefachim* — because once there is a gap of three *tefachim*, one cannot invoke *lavud*, and the *sechach* is no longer connected to the walls. In the middle of the *sukkah*, three *tefachim* of air breaks the continuity of the roof, splitting the *sukkah* into two disconnected sections. Even by a wall, air cannot benefit from the bent-wall principle, which requires actual physical *sechach* — however *pasul* — to be present.

The upshot is genuinely counterintuitive: if one has a large gap in one's *sechach*, one is better off filling it with *pasul sechach* — even vegetables or food, which are technically invalid — than leaving it as open air. *Pasul sechach* is more forgiving than nothing at all. And so, the ruling that these *maror* vegetables are treated as air rather than as *pasul sechach* represents the more stringent classification. The *chiddush* of the *gemara* is that despite their physical presence, because they will inevitably crumble, these vegetables are not merely *pasul sechach* — they are, in the *gemara*'s view, already functionally absent.

## Yad Doctrine

### Wine Harvest Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַבּוֹצֵר לַגַּת — אֵין לוֹ יָדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the statements of Rabbi Abba, the Gemara cites another. **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rav Huna said:** In the case of **one who harvests** bunches of grapes **for the winepress,** these bunches **do not have handles.** The stems, which connect the grapes to the clusters, are not required for the production of wine. Therefore, their legal status is not that of a handle in terms of ritual impurity; they are merely waste. Consequently, if these stems come into contact with a source of ritual impurity, they do not become impure and they do not transmit impurity to the attached grapes.

Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav Huna states: *habotzer la-gat ein lo yadot* — one who harvests grapes for the wine press has no principle of *yad* (handle) applying. The *yad* principle is a fundamental concept in the laws of *tumah*: just as a handle of a vessel is an extension of the vessel for purposes of transmitting *tumah*, so too a stem or stalk attached to food serves as a "handle" for that food. If one touches *tumah* to the stem, the food becomes *tamei*, because the stem is treated as part of the food. The classic example is a grape stem — if one picks up a grape by its stem, the stem serves as the grape's *yad* and transmits *tumah* to the grape even though the stem itself is mere wood.

However, this principle applies only where there is a desire for the stalk to be connected to the food — where the stalk is functionally serving as a handle one actually uses. When one harvests grapes for the wine press, one explicitly does not want those stems: *de-la nimtzei leh chamra* — they would absorb the wine in the pressing vat, diminishing the yield and affecting the wine's quality. Since the stems are entirely unwanted in this context, they do not function as *yadot*, and *tumah* touching the stem does not communicate to the grape.

### Sechach Harvest Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַקּוֹצֵר לִסְכָךְ — אֵין לוֹ יָדוֹת. מַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹצֵר, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בּוֹצֵר — דְּלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ, דְּלָא נִימְצְיֵיהּ לְחַמְרֵיהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר בּוֹצֵר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ יָדוֹת, אֲבָל קוֹצֵר יֵשׁ לוֹ יָדוֹת — דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלִיסַכֵּךְ בְּהוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיבַּדְּרָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Menashya bar Gadda said** that **Rav Huna said:** In the case of **one who harvests** grain **for roofing** a *sukka*, the grain **does not have handles.** The legal status of the straw is not that of a handle for the grain. Since his interest is roofing his *sukka*, he wants only the straw, which is fit roofing, and not the grain, which is unfit. Therefore, in this context, the straw does not facilitate moving the grain. **The one who said** that in the case of one who **harvests** grain, the straw is not a handle, **all the more so** would he say so in the case of one who **harvests** grapes, **since** the stems **are not suitable for his** needs. Stems are not wanted in the winepress, **so that they will not absorb wine.** By contrast, **the one who said** in the case of one who **harvests** grapes **that it has no handles,** he said so only in that case; **however,** in the case of **one who harvests** grain, he would say that **it has handles, since** the grain attached to the straw **is suitable for his** needs. He can **roof** the *sukka* **with them** and weigh down the straw, **so that it does not scatter** in the wind.

Rav Menashia bar Gadda in the name of Rav Huna adds: *ha-kotzetz le-sechach ein lo yadot* — one who cuts branches for *sechach* has no principle of *yad* applying either. Here the situation is somewhat different. When one cuts a grapevine for *sechach*, the branches are what one wants; the grapes attached to them are, from the perspective of *sechach*, *pasul* — food is *mekabel tumah* and thus *pasul sechach*. The stalk of the branch is *kasher sechach*; the grape is *pasul*. The question is how to calculate the proportion of *kasher* to *pasul*: does the small stalk connecting the grape to the branch count as part of the *pasul* (the grape) or part of the *kasher* (the branch)?

If *yad* applies, the stalk connecting the grape to the branch would be counted as part of the grape — *pasul sechach*. If *yad* does not apply, that stalk counts as part of the branch — *kasher sechach*. Rav Menashia bar Gadda holds that *ein lo yadot* — when cutting for *sechach*, the *yad* principle does not apply, and those stalks count as *kasher sechach*.

One might wonder why one would want the fruit attached to the branch at all when using it for *sechach*. In fact, there may be a reason: *ki heichi de-la livdru* — so that the grapes should not scatter. A heavy cluster of fruit anchors the branch and keeps it in place on the *sukkah*. In that case, one does have a reason to want the fruit attached, which could reintroduce the *yad* concept. This tension sets up the dispute to follow.

### Branches/Fruit Mixture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְתַנְיָא: סוֹכֵי תְאֵנִים וּבָהֶן תְּאֵנִים, פַּרְכִּילִין וּבָהֶן עֲנָבִים, קַשִּׁין וּבָהֶן שִׁבֳּלִים, מַכְבֵּדוֹת וּבָהֶן תְּמָרִים, כּוּלָּן, אִם פְּסוֹלֶת מְרוּבָּה עַל הָאוֹכָלִין — כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִם לָאו — פְּסוּלָה. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ קַשִּׁין מְרוּבִּין עַל הַיָּדוֹת וְעַל הָאוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Let us say that the statement of Rav Menashya bar Gadda is subject to a dispute between *tanna'im*, as it is taught in a *baraita*: **Fig branches, and** there are **figs on them; vines, and** there are **grapes on them; straw, and** there are **stalks of grain on them; palm branches, and** there are **dates on them,** with regard to **them all, if the** amount of **waste is greater than** the amount of **the food,** a *sukka* roofed with them is **fit. And if not,** the *sukka* is **unfit. *Aḥerim* say:** The *sukka* is unfit **until the** amount of **straw is greater than** the combined amount of the handbreadth of **the handles** attached to the food that is susceptible to ritual impurity **and the food.**

A *baraita* is cited: branches of a fig tree with figs attached, clusters of grapevine with grapes, stalks of grain with their ears, and palm fronds with dates — in all these cases the woody part is *kasher sechach* and the fruit or grain is *pasul*. The rule is: *im psolet merubbah al ha-ochalim kesherah* — if the waste product (the *kasher* woody parts) is greater than the food (*pasul*) portion, the *sukkah* is valid; *ve-im lav psulah* — if not, it is invalid. One goes by the majority.

It is worth noting the irony embedded in the terminology: *kosher sechach* is here called *psolet*, from *psolet goren ve-yekev*, the remnants of the threshing floor, while the food — technically the "good" part of the plant — is what disqualifies. A different opinion in the *baraita* — *acherim omerim* — sets a higher bar: *ad she-ye'hei kash merubbeh al ha-yadot ve-al ha-ochalim* — the *kasher* material must outweigh not only the food but also the *yadot*, the stalks attached to the food, which *acherim* count as part of the *pasul sechach*.

### Stalk Proportion Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר יֵשׁ לָהֶן יָדוֹת, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין לָהֶן יָדוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** *Aḥerim*, who said that the straw must be greater than the handles as well, **holds** that the produce designated for roofing **have handles; and** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, who disagrees, **holds that they do not have handles?**

The *gemara* identifies this as precisely the debate in question: *de-mar savar yesh lahen yadot* — one view holds that the stalks are *yadot*, part of the fruit, and thus *pasul sechach*; *u-mar savar ein lahen yadot* — the other holds they are not *yadot* and count as *kasher sechach*. This appears to be a dispute of *Tannaim* about whether the *yad* principle applies when cutting branches for *sechach*.

### Initial Intent Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא מִי לֵימָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? אָמַר לָךְ רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא סָבְרִי הַקּוֹצֵר לִסְכָךְ אֵין לוֹ יָדוֹת. וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁקְּוצָצָן לַאֲכִילָה, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לְסִיכּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **According to** the opinion of **Rav Menashya bar Gadda,** who says that grain harvested for roofing does not have handles, **shall we say** that **it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** and that he holds in accordance with the first *tanna* of the *baraita*? **Rav Menashya** could have **said to you that everyone agrees:** With regard to **one who harvests** grain for **roofing,** the grain **does not have handles. And here** in the *baraita*, **with what are we dealing?** It is a case **where one** initially **cut** the stalks **for food, and reconsidered** his plan **for them,** and decided to use them **for roofing.** Since initially, as food, the grain had handles, its status does not change despite his change of intent.

The *gemara* asks: does this *baraita* establish that the ruling of Rav Menashia bar Gadda is itself a *tannaitic* dispute? For Rabbi Abba's formulation — that even when cutting for *sechach* the stalks are considered *yadot* of the fruit — there is certainly a *tannaitic* precedent in the *acherim* opinion. But for Rav Menashia bar Gadda, who rules *ein lo yadot* when cutting for *sechach*, is he contradicted by the *baraita*?

Rav Menashia bar Gadda resolves this: *de-chulei alma sevira hakotzo le-sichuch ein lo yadot* — everyone agrees that when one cuts material specifically for *sechach*, the *yad* principle does not apply and the stalks count as *kasher sechach*. The case in the *baraita* where *acherim* count the stalks as *pasul* is a different scenario entirely: *kegon she-kotzan le-achilah ve-nimlach aleihen le-sichuch* — it refers to a case where the branches were originally cut with the intention of eating the fruit, and only afterward was it decided to use them for *sechach*. Where the original intent was to eat the fruit, the *yad* concept takes hold from the moment of cutting, and it is that prior intent that causes the stalks to be counted as part of the fruit even when the material is subsequently repurposed as *sechach*. If one cuts for *sechach* from the outset, there is no controversy — the stalks are unambiguously *kasher sechach*.
