# Sukkah 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review & Context

### Daf Overview

Today's *daf* is 42. We pick up at the last line of 41b and emerge from what was a difficult and challenging section on *shemittah*. We are now back to the basic *mitzvah* structure of *lulav* and the *sukkah*.

### Prior Topics

The previous discussions established that the *mitzvah* of *lulav* is a seven-day obligation in the *Beis HaMikdash* and a one-day obligation outside it — a question the *Gemara* chose not to pursue here, reserving it for later. The discussion also touched on the scenario where the first day of *Yom Tov* coincides with *Shabbos* and people brought their *lulavim* to the *beis haknesses* in advance; the requirement of owning one's *lulav*, and the concept of *matana al menas lehachazir*; and the position of Rabbi Yose that one who inadvertently violates *Shabbos* in the course of performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from a *korban* — even in a case where the *mitzvah* itself was not ultimately fulfilled. With those discussions concluded, the *Gemara* turns to the *Mishnah* at the opening of 42a, which touches on *Shabbos* themes in a different key.

---

## Women & Water

### Woman Taking Lulav

The *Mishnah* states: *mekabeles isha miyad b'nah umiyad ba'alah* — a woman may take the *lulav* from her husband's or son's hand. The context makes clear that in the normative practice depicted by the *Mishnah*, it would be the males who took the *lulav* and *esrog*; the men would pass their *lulavim* along to their wives afterward. Although we know that women are not obligated, it is common enough in *Ashkenazi* circles for women to perform this *mitzvah*, but the *Mishnah* reflects a world where that was not the standard practice.

The *Mishnah* further states: *u'machaziraso l'mayim b'Shabbos* — she may return it to water on *Shabbos*, because one keeps the *lulav* in water to preserve its freshness. This is not analogous to watering a plant still growing from the ground, where there would be a genuine prohibition; rather, one places it in a container of water, and this is permitted on *Shabbos*.

### Yehuda Water Rules

Rabbi Yehuda elaborates with a three-part distinction: *b'Shabbos machazirin* — on *Shabbos* one may only return the *lulav* to water it was already in; *b'Yom Tov mosifin* — on *Yom Tov* one may add fresh water; and *uvemoed machalifin* — on *Chol HaMoed* one may even exchange the water entirely. Rashi goes so far as to say that on *Chol HaMoed* it is actually a *mitzvah* to exchange the water, presumably in order to keep the *lulav* as fresh as possible. The precise nature of the problem — what exactly constitutes the forbidden *tircha* on *Shabbos* — is not spelled out in the *Mishnah*, and Rashi simply characterizes it in general terms as *tircha*. The degree of the concern remains somewhat unclear from the text alone.

### Minor's Obligation

Notably, the *Gemara* skips entirely over the *Shabbos* water laws and instead homes in on the final line of the *Mishnah*: *katan hayodeia l'na'aneia chayav belulav* — once a minor knows how to shake the *lulav*, he becomes obligated in it. This is a rabbinic obligation of *chinuch*. As we will see in the *Gemara*, the general principle of *chinuch* is that it begins when the child is capable of performing the physical act of the *mitzvah*. The *Mishnah's* framing in terms of the ability to shake — rather than merely hold — the *lulav* underscores the centrality of *na'anu'a* to the act of the *mitzvah*. While *b'dieved* one is *yotzei* even without shaking, the shaking is the defining gesture. Tosafos pointed out that the *Mishnah* does not say the child must be able to recite *Hallel*, and from this wished to infer that the shaking of the *lulav* during *Hallel* is not so central — though one might respond that the child can be *yotzei* through *shomei'a k'oneh* while others recite. In any case, the centrality of the shaking is unmistakable.

## Muktzah & Objects

### Woman Can Take

The *Gemara* opens with *pshita* — isn't it obvious that the woman can take the *lulav* from her husband? The question, says the *Gemara*, is that one might have thought: since a woman is not obligated in *lulav*, perhaps she should not be permitted to take it — *eima lo tikabel* — because from her perspective, it might be no different from *muktzah*, a mere stick. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that she may take it.

### Muktzah Analysis

Rashi explains the reasoning: the *lulav* is not *muktzah* because it is a *mitzvah* object for her husband. For him, it has the full status of a sacred ritual implement, and that status protects it from *muktzah* classification even when the woman handles it. This analysis naturally raises interesting questions. For *Ashkenazim*, who follow the practice that women may take the *lulav* and recite a *beracha*, the *lulav* would presumably not be *muktzah* vis-à-vis women either, since it functions as a *mitzvah* object for them as well. But where the practice is that women do not take it and it would not be considered a *mitzvah* for them, the only reason it is not *muktzah* in a woman's hands is because of its status vis-à-vis the husband.

### Shofar Parallel

This raises a parallel question regarding the *shofar* on *Yom Tov*. The *Mishnah* elsewhere discusses children who blow the *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* — *misaskin bahem*, they may engage in blowing — but what is the conceptual framework? Ordinarily one may not play musical instruments on *Yom Tov*. So when someone blows the *shofar* outside the context of a *mitzvah* on *Rosh Hashanah*, are they violating the prohibition against playing musical instruments? Or is the *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* categorically outside that concern — does it receive a blanket exemption from the musical-instrument prohibition simply by virtue of being the *mitzvah* object of the day? Is this fundamentally a *muktzah* question, or a separate question about the categorization of the object? The parallel with the *lulav* and the woman's ability to take it sits within the same family of questions, and the *Gemara* here does not resolve it fully — but it is worth noting the structural similarity.

## Chinuch Braita

### Tanu Rabanan Intro

The *Gemara* continues: *tanu rabanan* — a *braita* now presents a comprehensive list of the various *mitzvos* in which a minor, upon becoming capable of performing the defining physical act, becomes rabbinically obligated — or more precisely, his father becomes obligated to train him. The list is significant not only for the *chinuch* framework it establishes, but because each entry implicitly identifies what the central act of that *mitzvah* is.

### Lulav Waving

*Katan hayodeia l'na'aneia chayav belulav* — once a minor knows how to wave, he is obligated in *lulav*. This echoes the *Mishnah* exactly and confirms that the wave is the defining act of the *mitzvah*.

### Tzitzit Wrapping

*Hitateif* — once he is able to wrap himself in a garment, he is obligated in *tzitzis*. This is striking. One might have thought the *mitzvah* of *tzitzis* consists simply in having fringes attached to a four-cornered garment. Yet the *beracha* we recite is *lehitateif batzitzis* — to wrap oneself — and in fact a number of *Geonim* and *Rishonim* hold that the biblical obligation of *mitzvas tzitzis* applies only to a garment that is genuinely a *bar ituf*, something one wraps around oneself and over one's head. The centrality of *ituf* is thus reflected already in the *braita's* criterion for *chinuch*.

### Tefillin Body Guard

*Lishmor tefillin* — once he is able to guard his *tefillin*, meaning, as Rashi explains, that he is capable of not entering a bathroom while wearing them and — the more demanding concern noted by the *Gemara* — of not passing gas while wearing them, then *aviv lokeach lo tefillin*, his father purchases *tefillin* for him. One notices a linguistic difference: by *lulav* and *tzitzis* the *braita* uses the word *chayav*, whereas by *tefillin* it says *aviv lokeach lo tefillin* — his father acquires them for him. The marginal note flags this discrepancy. The likely explanation is practical: with *lulav* and *tzitzis*, a garment or an extra *lulav* may already be available in the home, but *tefillin* must be purchased fresh, hence the language of acquisition.

### Tefillin Delay

The *tefillin* entry is qualitatively different from the others. For *lulav* and *tzitzis*, the onset of *chinuch* corresponds to the child's ability to perform the central act. Here, however, the issue is not the inability to perform the act at a young age — a young child could physically put on *tefillin* — but rather the concern that he cannot yet maintain the required bodily state, *shomer gufo*, while wearing them. This is precisely why in practice we begin training children in *tzitzis* from the age of three, and in *lulav* from a similarly young age, but we wait until just before *bar mitzvah* to begin training in *tefillin* — because the *shomer gufo* requirement is so demanding.

### Torah Learning

*Yodea ledaber aviv lomdo Torah* — once he knows how to speak, his father must begin teaching him *Torah*. It is worth noting that this entry appears in a somewhat curious position in the list, since one assumes that knowing how to speak comes chronologically before all the other entries. The obligation of teaching him *Torah* may also be categorically different from the other items: it may not be *mitzvas chinuch* per se but rather the father's own personal obligation of *v'shinantam l'vanecha*. The *braita* then adds *u'krias shema*, training him in the *mitzvah* of *krias shema* — this clearly is the son's own *mitzvah*. The *Gemara* asks: what is the *Torah* that the father begins with? Rav Hamnuna answers: *Torah tziva lanu Moshe morasha kehilas Yaakov*. There is something deeply beautiful about this. The first line of *Torah* a child learns is not a verse about God's power or the creation of the world, but a verse about the nature of *Torah* itself as a *morasha*, an inheritance, a handed-down tradition. The father, in teaching the child, enacts the very idea the verse describes — one generation transmitting to the next. And notably, even the mention of God is absent from this verse; it is *Moshe* and *Klal Yisrael* who stand at the center.

### Rambam's Method

The approach of the Rambam here is characteristically distinctive and worth examining. When the Rambam codifies the obligation of teaching one's child, he writes: *me'eimatai aviv chayav lelamdo Torah? mishe'yachal ledaber melamdo Torah tziva lanu Moshe u'shma Yisrael. V'achar kach melamdo me'at me'at pesukim pesukim ad sheyehei ben shesh ben sheva hakol kefi buryo u'molicho eitzel melamed tinokos.* The Rambam folds *shema Yisrael* into the *mitzvas talmud Torah* rather than treating it as a separate instance of *mitzvas chinuch* for *krias shema*. He is not simply quoting the *Gemara*; he appears to be making a deliberate move consistent with his broader approach. Throughout the Mishneh Torah, the Rambam consistently foregrounds *emunah* and theological first principles in places where the *Gemara* discusses only *mitzvos*. The clearest parallel is in the laws of conversion: the *Gemara* says you teach the prospective convert the lighter and weightier *mitzvos*; the Rambam says you teach him about belief in God and the prohibition of idolatry, spending substantial time there before turning to *mitzvos*. Here too, the Rambam seems to be saying that you cannot begin teaching *Torah* without immediately bringing *Shma Yisrael* — the declaration of *emunah* — into the equation. The *Shma* is not merely another *mitzvah* to train him in; it is inseparable from the first act of Torah transmission itself.

### Tumah Guards

Returning to the *braita*: *hayodea lishmor gufo* — once a minor knows how to guard his body from encountering *tumah* and from touching *tamei* things — *ochlin al gufo taharos*. Foods in a state of *taharah* that his body has come into contact with may still be eaten; one can trust him. This is a remarkable standard. It is one thing to say we begin training a child in *tefillin* once he can be trusted not to pass gas while wearing them. It is another to say: once he reaches this level of bodily awareness, even if he touches your *tahor terumah*, you may eat it. He need not be an adult; his demonstrated capacity for *shmirat ha-guf* is sufficient.

*Lishmor es yadav* represents a further, more demanding level: one's hands are constantly moving and touching things without conscious awareness. Once a minor can also guard what his hands are touching, *ochlin al yadav taharos* — even food he has handled with his hands may be eaten in its *tahor* state.

### Safek Tamei Principle

*Hayodea lishol b'reshus hayachid sefeko tamei b'reshus harabim sefeko tahor.* If a child has reached the developmental level where one can meaningfully question him — "was there anything *tamei* near this *terumah* on the street?" — and he can give a coherent and reliable answer, then the general rule applies: a doubtful *tumah* in *reshus hayachid* is judged *tamei*, and a doubtful *tumah* in *reshus harabim* is judged *tahor*. One might ask: what difference does it make how old he is or what he can answer? Once he says "I don't know," you simply apply the standard rule — *safek* in *reshus hayachid* is *tamei*, *safek* in *reshus harabim* is *tahor*. The *halacha*, however, is that this rule applies only when the knowledge was theoretically accessible to someone who was there. If the only witness was someone too young to give a coherent account, even a *safek* in *reshus hayachid* is *tahor*.

The source of the principle that *safek tumah* in *reshus hayachid* is *tamei* is derived from a completely different domain: the laws of *sotah*. A woman who enters private seclusion with a man against her husband's explicit warning acquires a doubtful status — if the seclusion was in *reshus hayachid*, she is forbidden to her husband pending the *sotah* ritual; if in *reshus harabim*, she is not. From there we abstract the general principle about doubtful *tumah* in *reshus hayachid* versus *reshus harabim*, even though the underlying subject matter is entirely different. The logic of why *reshus hayachid* produces a *tamei* ruling is that the information is in principle accessible: someone was there who could know. The ruling of *tamei* creates a kind of normative pressure to produce that information. The logic therefore applies only when there actually is someone present who could in theory have known. If the only person present was a child too young to give a reliable answer, the very premise collapses — the knowledge was not knowable — and we revert to the default that a *safek* is *tahor*. Hence it matters greatly that the child has reached the developmental level where he could in principle have given an accurate answer; only then does a *safek* in *reshus hayachid* yield a ruling of *tamei*.

### Kohen Duchenan

*Hayodea lifros kapav* — once he knows how to spread his hands for *duchening*, *cholkin lo terumah b'veis hagranot* — one may give him *terumah* grain publicly at the threshing floor. If, however, he does not yet *duchen* in public, even if he is a *kohen* and is trusted to eat the *terumah* in a *tahor* state, one may send it to his home but not give it to him publicly. The distinction is meaningful: once he regularly *duchens* in public — and recall that the *Gemara* describes *duchening* as a daily practice, as is the *Sefardic* custom today — everyone in the community knows he is a *kohen*, and there is no confusion or misrepresentation in giving him grain at the threshing floor. But if his status as a *kohen* is not yet publicly established, a public distribution of *terumah* to him would be confusing or potentially misleading to onlookers. Only once he is publicly identified as a *kohen* through the act of *duchening* can one give him *terumah* in public.

One should note that much of the latter part of this *braita* has moved beyond the domain of *chinuch* strictly speaking. The earlier entries concerned training a child in *mitzvos* he will perform as an adult. The later entries — guarding *tahor* food, *duchening*, the rules of *safek tumah* — concern questions of trustworthiness and halachic status in various ritual contexts. The *tefillin* entry was already a transitional case: not that he cannot physically perform the act, but that we cannot yet trust him to maintain the required bodily state. From there the *braita* widens its scope to encompass the broader question of when a minor's conduct and testimony can be relied upon.

### Shechita Skill

The *Gemara* at the top of 42b states: *hayodea lishchot, ochlin mishchitato* — once a minor knows how to slaughter, one may eat his *shechita*. Rav Huna adds: *vehu shegadol omed al gabav* — this is only when an adult is standing over him observing.

### Shechita Knowledge

Rashi explains *hayodea lishchot* as meaning he has the physical skill of *shechting* — *le'amen yadav lishchita* — even if he does not have expert knowledge of the laws of *shechita*. In other words, the physical capability is the threshold, and one then supplements it through adult observation to verify that the laws were followed. Tosafos develops this point explicitly. He first quotes Rashi's formulation — *shelo shahah velo daras*, that the observer confirms there was no *shehiya* or *derasa* — and then notes what is implicit: that even complete observation from beginning to end is not sufficient if the child lacks the physical skill to perform *shechita* properly. You cannot compensate for physical incapacity through observation alone. One might have argued the opposite: perhaps you need knowledge of the laws, because certain violations cannot be observed, while the physical act is entirely visible. But Rashi and Tosafos hold that the physical skill is what the child must possess, and observation supplies verification of legal compliance.

One student raised an observation worth noting: common to all the entries in the *braita* is an emphasis on behavioral and physical developmental capacity rather than on theoretical or conceptual knowledge. The criteria are consistently about what the child can *do*. Even *lishmor gufo* and *lishmor es yadav*, which involve a kind of awareness, are about vigilant behavioral practice rather than intellectual mastery. The one apparent exception — *yodea ledaber*, the ability to speak, as the threshold for *talmud Torah* — may itself point in this direction: *Torah* learning is not merely internal cognition but an act of articulation, of *vedibarta bam*.

### Grain Odor

*Yachol leechol kezayis dagan* — once a minor can eat a *kezayis* of grain, *merchikin mitzoaso umimei raglav* — one must distance oneself from his excrement and urine before reciting *davening* or *Shema*. This has immediate practical application: when an infant is still nursing, his excrement does not yet carry the halachically relevant odor that creates a problem. Only once he has begun eating grain products does the excrement become malodorous and halachically problematic. The requirement applies to the urine as well, not only to the excrement. Rav Chisda refines this: *vehu sheyachol leechol bichdei achilas pras* — the criterion is not just that he can eat grain, but that he can eat a *kezayis* within the time span of *achilas pras*, the halachically defined act of eating. Rav Chiya the son of Rav Yeiva then adds an ironic coda: by an adult, even if he cannot manage a *kezayis* within *achilas pras* due to age or infirmity, his excrement still poses the problem — as the verse says, *yosif da'as yosif makhov*, one who adds knowledge adds pain. Apparently, it seems, with age comes a different kind of potency.

### Pesach Meat

*Yachol leechol kezayis tzali* — once he can eat a *kezayis* of roasted meat, *shochtin alav es ha'Pesach* — he can be counted in the group for a *korban Pesach*. The verse says *ish lefi ochlo*, each person according to their eating capacity; one must be able to eat at least a minimum amount. Roasted meat requires more developed teeth than soft or cooked meat, and this serves as the developmental marker for being counted in the *Pesach* group.

### Food Discernment

Rabbi Yehuda adds a further criterion: *ad she'yachol l'varer achila* — until he can identify and distinguish food from non-food. How so? *Nosnim lo tzror v'zorka, egoz v'notla* — if you give him a stone he throws it away; if you give him a walnut he takes it. He recognizes the walnut as something to eat. The implied reasoning seems to concern the nature of eating itself: if a child merely chews and swallows whatever is placed in his mouth, that is being fed, not eating. The act of *eating* in the relevant sense requires the subject to make a volitional identification — *this is food, this is not* — and to act accordingly. That capacity for discernment is what Rabbi Yehuda considers the threshold for being meaningfully counted among those who eat the *korban Pesach*.

## Perek 4 Overview

### Perek Structure

With the *Hadran* on *Lulav V'Arava*, the *Gemara* concludes the third *perek*. To step back: the first two *perakim* were devoted largely to the physical structure of the *sukkah* — a full *perek* and a half on the actual construction — and the latter half of the second *perek* to the *mitzvah* of dwelling in it. The third *perek* was devoted to *lulav*, *hadas*, *arava*, and *esrog*, primarily their physical specifications, with the final sections addressing the act of taking them — notably emphasizing the shaking of the *lulav* and its performance during *Hallel* — before moving into discussions of *shemittah*, ownership, and the *Shabbos* complications. The fourth *perek* now takes a different structural approach. Rather than focusing on one *mitzvah* at a time, it surveys all the *mitzvos* of *Sukkos* together and examines how many days each one applies, creating a kind of master map of the festival's obligations.

---

## Days & Rituals

### Lulav & Arava

The *Mishnah* begins: *lulav v'arava shisha v'shiva* — the *mitzvah* of *lulav* and the *mitzvah* of *arava* each apply for either six or seven days. The *mitzvah* of *arava* here refers to what we call *Hoshanos* — the procession with the *lulav* in which the *arava* is the central element. The variability between six and seven days for both *lulav* and *arava* is a function of where *Shabbos* falls during *Sukkos*, as we will see below.

### Hallel & Simcha

*HaHallel v'hasimcha* — reciting *Hallel* and the obligation of *simcha*, which in the context of the *Beis Hamikdash* refers specifically to *shalmei simcha*, the peace-offerings brought on the *Yom Tov* — these apply for *shmonah*, all eight days. That is, both throughout the seven days of *Sukkos* and including *Shmini Atzeres*. One brings *shalmei simcha* and recites *Hallel* on all eight days — even if, as will be clarified, one might bring the offerings on the seventh day and eat them extending into the eighth.

### Sukkah & Water

*Sukkah v'nisuch hamayim* — dwelling in the *sukkah* and the *nisuch hamayim*, the water-pouring ceremony performed in the *Beis Hamikdash* during the morning sacrifices, apply for *shiva*, only the seven days of *Sukkos* proper and not *Shmini Atzeres*.

### Chalil Days

*Hachalil chamisha v'shisha* — the *chalil*, a wind instrument, was played to accompany the *Simchas Beis Hashoeva* for either five or six days. The logic: across the seven days of *Sukkos*, you immediately lose the first day of *Yom Tov*, on which musical instruments are not played. That leaves at most six days. If *Shabbos* additionally falls during the week of *Chol HaMoed*, you lose another day, bringing the total to five.

---

## Lulav Days Detail

### Lulav 7 Days

*Lulav shiva keitzad?* The *Mishnah* explains: *Yom Tov harishon shel chag shechal lihyos b'Shabbos* — if the first day of *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbos*, one takes the *lulav* on all seven days, including *Shabbos*, as we saw in the previous *perek* — that is when the procedure of bringing it to the *beis haknesses* in advance was employed.

### Lulav 6 Days

*Ushar kol hayamim shisha* — if *Shabbos* falls on any other day of the week, i.e., during *Chol HaMoed*, one does not take *lulav* on that *Shabbos*, and the total is six days.

---

## Arava Days

### Arava 7 Days

*Arava shiva keitzad? Yom shevii shel arava shechal lihyos b'Shabbos* — if the seventh day of *arava*, which is *Hoshana Rabbah*, falls on *Shabbos*, one performs the *arava* ritual even on *Shabbos*, yielding seven days. *Hoshana Rabbah* stands in relation to *arava* the way the first day of *Sukkos* stands in relation to *lulav*: it is the primary day of the *mitzvah*. Just as taking *lulav* on the first day overrides *Shabbos*, so too the *arava* on *Hoshana Rabbah* would be performed even on *Shabbos*.

### Arava 6 Days

*Ushar kol hayamim shisha* — if *Shabbos* falls on any day other than *Hoshana Rabbah*, one does not perform *arava* on that *Shabbos*, and the *mitzvah* is performed for six days only.

## Temple Procedure

### Bringing to Temple

The *Mishnah* now returns to describe the procedure when the first day of *Yom Tov* coincides with *Shabbos* — specifically in the context of the *Beis Hamikdash*, which is distinct from the *beis haknesses* procedure described in the previous *perek*. *Molichim es lulaveihem l'Har HaBayis* — people would bring their *lulavim* to the Temple Mount before *Shabbos*. The *chazzanim* — the functionaries, the sextons — would receive them.

### Portico Storage

*V'sodrin osan al gabei itztava* — they would arrange them on the *itztava*, the portico. Rashi explains that this was not the roof in the open sun, which would dry out the *lulavim*, but rather the benches under a roofed colonnade in the courtyard of *Har HaBayis*, where they would be shaded. *V'hazekeinim manichim es shelahem b'lishka* — the elders were accorded the privilege of placing their *lulavim* and *esrogim* in private chambers, which, as will become clear, spared them from the chaos of the masses.

### Gift Declaration

*Umelamdim osam lomar: kol mi shemagia lulavi l'yado, harei hu lo b'matana* — people were taught to declare: whoever picks up my *lulav* receives it as a gift. This was necessitated by the requirement established earlier that one must own one's *lulav*. In a small *shul* of fifty or a hundred people, it was realistic to find one's own *lulav* after *Shabbos*. But in the *Beis HaMikdash*, with enormous crowds, that was simply impossible. The declaration of gift solved the ownership problem pre-emptively.

### Shabbat Chaos

*Lemachor, mashkimim u'va'in* — on *Shabbos* morning, people would rise early to be ahead of the crowds. *V'hachazzanim zorkim osam lifneihem* — the *chazzanim* would throw the *lulavim* down in front of the assembled multitudes. *V'hein machatfin u'makin ish es chaveiro* — and people would grab and strike one another trying to secure a *lulav*, presumably either their own or the best available. The scene was a complete *balagan*. One cannot help but note the jarring contrast: the *Mishnah* describes this elaborate, careful procedure — receiving the *lulavim*, arranging them on the benches, teaching the declaration — and then arrives at: and they threw them in front of the crowd. One wonders why a more orderly system was not devised from the outset, in the way that the *Gemara* in Yoma describes a different crisis — people falling off the *mizbeiach* in the rush to perform *terumas hadeshen* — being resolved not by canceling the practice but by instituting a lottery.

### Takkanah Decision

*Uchesherau beis din sheba'u lidei sakana, hitkinu sheyehei kol echad v'echad notel b'veiso* — when the *beis din* saw that people were coming to real physical danger, they enacted that everyone should take the *lulav* at home. This is one of those striking *takanos* in which the response to a dangerous situation is not to fix the procedure but to relocate the practice entirely. How exactly this affected what was already being done in the *shuls* outside the Temple precinct — where, as we saw, people brought their *lulavim* ahead of time but the practice did not descend into violence — is a question the *Mishnah* does not fully address here.

## Shabbat Question

### Tiltul Question

The *Gemara* now presses on the fundamental question underlying the entire discussion: *amai? Tiltul b'alma hu v'lidchei Shabbos?* Why isn't the *lulav* taken on *Shabbos* of *Chol HaMoed*? It is only a question of moving an object — let the *mitzvah* override whatever *Shabbos* concern there might be. If it is a *muktzah* concern, should not the *mitzvah* simply exempt it from *muktzah* status altogether?

### Tosafos Muktzah

Tosafos addresses this directly. He notes that the *Gemara's* phrase *tiltul b'alma hu* raises the very question of whether *muktzah* applies here: *af al gav d'chazi l'mitzvah* — even though it is a *mitzvah* object. And Tosafos then answers that *muktzah* does indeed apply, because the *lulav* is not a *kli* — it is not a formally designated implement — *v'ein assui kli*, and he then points to a parallel case involving the poles of the *lechem hapanim*. So the *Gemara* proceeds on the understanding that *muktzah* does apply to the *lulav* on *Shabbos*, even on *Yom Tov*. This generates the fascinating question: is the *lulav* intrinsically *muktzah*, with permission to handle it on *Yom Tov* derived solely from the *mitzvah* obligation? Or is it that on *Yom Tov* of *Sukkos*, the *lulav* is simply not *muktzah* at all? The *Gemara's* framing — letting the *mitzvah* override the *muktzah* concern — seems to suggest the former: fundamentally, it is *muktzah*, and the *mitzvah* creates an override. This is a nuanced and unresolved question.

### Rabbah Gezeirah

Rabbah provides the answer: *gezeirah shema yitlenu b'yado v'yeilech etzel baki lilmod* — it is a *gezeirah* lest he take it in his hand and go to an expert to learn how to perform the *mitzvah* — perhaps how to shake it properly, or how to recite the *beracha* — *v'ya'avirenu daled amos b'reshus harabim* (continuing at the top of 43a), and in the process carry it four *amos* in *reshus harabim*, violating *Shabbos*. The concern is not *muktzah* at all; it is accidental *Shabbos* carrying. This makes excellent sense in light of all the preceding *mishnayos*, which devoted great attention to precisely this concern — bringing the *lulav* to the *beis haknesses* or *Har HaBayis* before *Shabbos*, all the elaborate procedures designed to prevent carrying on *Shabbos*. Rabbah is saying: those procedures reflect the underlying rationale for not permitting *lulav* on *Shabbos Chol HaMoed* at all.

The *Gemara* then extends this reasoning: *v'hainu ta'ama d'shofar v'hainu ta'ama d'megillah* — and this is likewise the reason for not blowing *shofar* on *Shabbos*, and not reading *megillah* on *Shabbos*. This is worth pausing over. The prohibition on blowing *shofar* on *Shabbos* is assumed by the *Mishnayos* but not explained there. The *Mishnayos* simply present it as given, and one could have understood it on intrinsic grounds — blowing *shofar* is playing a musical instrument, and there is something inherently conflicting with the character of *Shabbos* in that; similarly, handling a *lulav* might be conceived as fundamentally inconsistent with *Shabbos* rather than merely instrumentally risky. It is Rabbah, as an *amora*, who for the first time articulates the rationale as a *gezeirah* against carrying — and moreover, it is the *Gemara* that extends Rabbah's stated logic to *shofar* and *megillah*, going beyond Rabbah's own formulation. The historical development of these explanations is worth appreciating in its own right.

## First Day Exception

### First Day Home Rule

The *Gemara* now raises an immediate challenge: *ei hachi, yom rishon nami?* If that is the reason — fear of carrying — then we should not permit *lulav* even on the first day of *Yom Tov* when it coincides with *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* responds: *rishon hitkinu lei rabanan b'veiso* — for the first day, the *Rabbanan* enacted that one takes the *lulav* at home. After the *takkanah*, one is instructed to take it at home, so the concern that one will go to an expert and carry in *reshus harabim* is nullified.

### Biblical vs Rabbinic

But the *Gemara* presses further: *kodem takkanah mai ika lemeimar?* — before the *takkanah* was enacted, when people were bringing their *lulavim* to the *shul* and later to the *Beis HaMikdash*, they were clearly not taking it at home. How was the first day permitted then? The *Gemara* answers: *ela rishon d'isay mineh hatorah bigvulin, lo gazru bah rabanan* — the first day, which carries biblical force everywhere, not only in the *Beis HaMikdash*, was not subject to the rabbinic *gezeirah*. *Hanach delisnehu mineh hatorah bigvulin, gazru bah rabanan* — the remaining days, which are biblically obligatory only within the *Beis HaMikdash* but not outside it, were subject to the *gezeirah*, and therefore *lulav* on those days is not permitted on *Shabbos* — even in the *Beis HaMikdash* itself, where technically the *mitzvah* on those days is biblical. The criterion for applying or not applying the *gezeirah* is not whether the *mitzvah* is biblical in that specific location, but whether it is biblical everywhere. The first day, being universally biblical, was left untouched by the *gezeirah*; all other days, having at most a biblical dimension limited to the *Beis HaMikdash*, were subject to the *gezeirah*.

### Shofar Puzzle

This leads to the natural and pressing question with which the *Gemara* closes — and which we leave as a *cliffhanger* for the next *daf*: if the first day is exempt from the *gezeirah* because its *mitzvah* is biblical everywhere, then why do we not blow *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* when it falls on *Shabbos*? The *mitzvah* of *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* is equally biblical everywhere and on the first day. Why is *lulav* on the first day exempt from the *gezeirah* while *shofar* is not? That question awaits tomorrow's *shiur*.
