# Kiddushin 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yichud w/ Relatives

### Parent-Child Sleeping

We pick up in the middle of 81b — roughly twenty to twenty-five lines down — where the Mishnah states: *mityached adam im imo*, a man may be in *yichud* together with his mother, and a father with his daughter. The Mishnah continues that they may even sleep together in the same bed without nightclothes, until the children become older — though the Mishnah does not specify exactly what "older" means.

### Rav Assi vs. Shmuel

The Gemara then examines other familial relationships. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav Assi: *mityached adam im achoto* — a man may even be in *yichud* together with his adult sister. This is striking, since a sister falls squarely within the major category of *arayot* in the Torah. One might explain this leniency on the grounds that the *yetzer hara* for incest is presumed weaker — and as Freud observed, you do not need taboos about something that nobody is doing. Alternatively, it may simply be an acknowledgment of domestic reality: brothers and sisters grow up in the same household, and it is not feasible to require them to vacate the premises whenever a parent steps out. The Gemara understands the permission as stemming from either a lesser concern about sexual temptation or from practical necessity.

Rav Assi adds that regarding a mother-son or father-daughter relationship, the leniency is even greater — they are permitted not merely *yichud* but actual cohabitation in the same dwelling. The precise distinction between living with one's mother versus living with one's sister once one is an independent householder (*baal habayit*) is not entirely spelled out, though the Gemara seems to assume that marriage or bar mitzvah marks a relevant threshold.

When Rav Yehuda transmitted Rav Assi's ruling before Shmuel, however, Shmuel objected sharply: *asur l'hityached im kol arayot shebaTorah* — it is forbidden to be in *yichud* with any of the forbidden relations enumerated in the Torah. Shmuel's position apparently includes even one's mother and daughter, which creates obvious practical difficulties regarding living arrangements. Moreover, Shmuel extends the prohibition even further: *ve'afilu im behemah* — one may not even be in *yichud* with an animal.

### Baraita on Witnesses

The Gemara then tests Shmuel's position against the Mishnah. The Mishnah explicitly permits *yichud* between parent and child and even allows them to sleep skin to skin — a clear contradiction of Shmuel's blanket prohibition. The Gemara presents Shmuel's response: you cite one source in my favor, but there is also a Baraita, which teaches that for one's sister, mother-in-law, and all other *arayot* of the Torah, one may not be in *yichud* with them unless witnesses are present. This Baraita is consistent with the Mishnah — which permits only the parent-child exception — and simultaneously contradicts Rav Assi, who permitted *yichud* with a sister. Thus Rav Assi's position conflicts with the Baraita, while Shmuel's position conflicts with the Mishnah. Better, the Gemara notes, to be against a Baraita than against a Mishnah.

### Tannaitic Positions

The Gemara resolves the tension by declaring: *ela tannai hi* — this is simply a dispute among Tannaim. Three positions emerge: (1) a lenient view, consistent with Rav Assi, that even non-parental relatives such as a sister present no *yichud* concern; (2) a middle position, that of the Mishnah and Baraita, permitting *yichud* only for parent-child relationships while prohibiting it for other relatives; and (3) Shmuel's strict position, prohibiting *yichud* with any *arayot* whatsoever, including one's own mother or daughter.

To illustrate the strictest position, the Gemara cites a Baraita in which Rabbi Meir said to the members of his household: "Be careful with me regarding my daughter" — meaning, never leave me alone in the same room with her. Rabbi Tarfon similarly said: "Be careful with me regarding my daughter-in-law." A certain student mocked this stringency, suggesting that these great Sages surely had no such *yetzer hara*. The Gemara records that not many days passed before that same student himself committed a sexual transgression with his own mother-in-law. The principle *kol haposel bemumo posel* — one who imputes a fault to others betrays his own fault — is apt here. The power of the *yetzer hara* for sexual sin is not something to be dismissed, and those who mock others' caution most loudly may be the least honest with themselves.

### Amoraim's Stringencies

Regarding Shmuel's ruling that even *yichud* with an animal is prohibited, the Gemara records that several Amoraim adopted similarly stringent practices. Abaye would clear all animals from the field before sitting down to learn. Rav Sheshet would have them moved to the other side of the bridge or river. The Gemara then recounts that Rav Chana mi-Neharde'a once visited Rav Kahana in Fum Nahara and found him sitting and learning with a cow standing right in front of him. Rav Chana challenged: "Does the Master not hold that even with an animal one should not be in *yichud*?" Rav Kahana replied: *lav adaitai* — "I wasn't thinking about it." This is not without irony: precisely because he was absorbed in learning and distracted, the concern had not occurred to him — yet someone felt it necessary to point it out.

One should note that these scenarios are quite extreme. In an open field where passersby can wander through at any moment, it is debatable whether *yichud* even applies — after all, a space open to the public (*petach patuach lerashut harabbim*) is generally not considered *yichud*. Indeed, in the very case of Rav Kahana, someone did come by.

### Tosafot's Ruling

Tosafot addresses the question of how we rule in practice. Regarding the cases of Abaye, Rav Sheshet, and Rav Chana, Tosafot writes: *nireh d'hanei amoraye chumra b'alma ka avdi* — it appears that these Amoraim were merely adopting an extra stringency. The operative ruling follows the Tannaitic position that *lo nechshadu Yisrael al mishkav zachar velo al habehemah* — Jews are not suspected of relations with another man or with an animal — and therefore the concern Shmuel expressed about animals is not a formal prohibition binding on all. We rule like the Mishnah that parent-child *yichud* is permitted. As for the question of other relatives — a sister, grandmother, or the like — those remain subjects of debate among the *poskim*, and practical questions should be addressed accordingly.

## Physical Maturity Limits

### Age Thresholds Debate

Returning to the Mishnah's statement that parent and child may sleep together even without nightclothes until the child becomes "older," the Gemara asks: *vekhamah* — how old is older? Rava bar Abba said in the name of Rav Assi: a girl (*tinokes*) once she reaches nine years old and one day, and a boy (*tinok*) once he reaches twelve years and one day. The asymmetry is notable — a daughter's threshold is earlier than a son's. One way to read this is that we are more concerned about a father taking advantage of a daughter than a mother of a son, which connects to broader questions about gendered power in sexual matters.

A second version — *ika d'amri* — sets both thresholds at the standard ages of *bar* and *bat mitzvah*: twelve years and a day for a girl, thirteen years and a day for a boy. This parallel formulation is linked to the onset of halachic adulthood and its associated physical maturity.

### Physical Development Sig…

The Gemara adds a qualification: even once these ages are reached, the prohibition on sleeping unclothed together applies only when the child has in fact reached physical maturity — when the breasts have developed and pubic hair has appeared, as described with reference to a verse in Yechezkel. If those signs of maturity have not yet appeared, the concern is not triggered, even at those ages.

One might ask: why not simply rely on the signs of physical maturity alone, regardless of age? And further, the Gemara's description of maturity is framed entirely in terms of the daughter's body — which reflects a shift to an exclusively male perspective. No parallel description of the son's physical development is offered, despite the earlier parallel discussion of mother-son sleeping arrangements.

### Embarrassment Standard

Rav Raffin bar Pappa said in the name of Rav Chisda: the Gemara's ruling applies specifically to a case where the daughter would not be embarrassed to stand naked before her father. But if she *would* be embarrassed — *aval bosha la'amod lefanav aruma assur*. Why? *Yetzer ala bah* — the *yetzer* has risen within her; she now understands what it means to be unclothed before a member of the opposite sex. Once that awareness exists, even apart from any formal age threshold or physical sign, the concern is triggered. Notably, the concern here is entirely about consensual sexual awareness. There is no mention of the risk of coercion or abuse — the prohibition kicks in when *she* experiences the *yetzer*, not when *he* might harm her.

### Modern Concerns

This section of Gemara can be read through two very different lenses. One reading appreciates the Gemara's capacity to acknowledge domestic reality without excessive vigilance: in the vast majority of cases, a parent and child sleeping together is entirely innocent, and the Gemara reflects that. Another reading notices what the Gemara does not address: the documented reality of parental abuse of children, particularly fathers of daughters. The Gemara seems unconcerned with coercion or rape as a category of risk here — the entire framework presupposes a concern about willing participants. The question of whether the halachic system should have legislated more protectively in this space — as it did in some areas (like *yichud* with non-relatives) but not here — is worth sitting with honestly.

## Boundary Violations

### Grandfather Lap Case

The Gemara recounts: Rav Acha bar Abba came to the home of his son-in-law Rav Chisda. He took his granddaughter and sat her on his lap — *oseiv beichnafeih*, placing her in his cloak. His son-in-law challenged him.

### Marriage Status Issue

The son-in-law said: *lo savar lah mar de'eishes ish* — does the Master not realize that she is a married woman? The granddaughter, young as she was — apparently a minor, *ktana* — had already been betrothed. Rav Acha responded by going on the offensive: *avarta lach adei-Rav* — you have violated the ruling of Rav, who taught (as cited earlier in the second *perek* of this masechta) that a father may not betroth his minor daughter until she grows up and can say, "I want this particular man." You have married her off against Rav's ruling — so do not lecture me.

### Shmuel's Services Ban

The son-in-law replied: you, too, are violating a teaching of Shmuel — *de'amar Shmuel, ein mishtamshin be'isha*, one may not make use of the services of a woman. Shmuel's principle is that a man should not have a woman attend to his personal domestic needs, even when there appears to be nothing sexual about the arrangement. Here, the granddaughter is not exactly serving him in that sense, but the same principle applies: even seemingly innocent physical proximity to women should be avoided. Shmuel, true to form throughout this tractate, is maximally stringent — no animals, no relatives, and now no female domestic attendants.

This raises a question: at what age does *yichud* or physical proximity with a young girl become a concern? The son-in-law appears to have raised the issue specifically because she was married, suggesting that without that status, sitting a young granddaughter on one's lap would not have been considered problematic. The precise age threshold for these concerns — whether it follows the age of *biah* (nine for a girl), *bat mitzvah*, or the signs of physical maturity discussed above — remains an open question.

### Sake of Heaven

Rav Acha responded: *ana k'idach de'Shmuel sevira li* — I follow this other teaching of Shmuel. For Shmuel also taught: *hakol leshem shamayim* — when one's intentions are entirely for the sake of heaven, there is no cause for concern. This creates a remarkable tension within Shmuel's own corpus: on one side, sweeping prohibitions against any proximity to women or *arayot*; on the other, a principle that pure intent renders such proximity permissible.

### Rashi on Intent

Rashi explains *hakol leshem shamayim* in this context: *ve'ein da'ati achareiha le'chibas ishus* — my intention is not in any way sexual; *ela le'chibas kurva* — it is the love of kinship, *vela'asos koras ruach le'imah k'she'ani mechabev es bitah* — and to give pleasure to her mother by showing how much I cherish her daughter. A grandfather embracing his granddaughter is an expression of familial love, not sexuality.

This is an important observation because it challenges the binary that sometimes appears in *halachic* discourse — as if there are only two kinds of touch: neutral/transactional and sexual. Rashi here acknowledges a third category: warm, intimate, loving touch that carries no sexual charge. The question then becomes whether *hakol leshem shamayim* is a principle available to all, or only to those who are genuinely *tzaddikim gemurim* with complete confidence that no *hirhur* will follow.

## Touch & Intent

### Neutral Touch Status

This Gemara opens a broader question: is there a formal *issur de'rabbanan* on neutral touch — a handshake, a casual greeting — between an unmarried man and a woman he is not married to? The case of a married woman who is a *niddah* is different: any touch there carries the weight of *issur arayot*. But what of neutral physical contact in ordinary social contexts?

### Handshake Cases

A number of *rishonim* and *poskim* hold that if the touch is genuinely non-sexual, there is no technical *issur de'rabbanan* — the prohibition, if any, is entirely contingent on the intent and likely consequence of the contact. A relevant contemporary case arose in Switzerland, where Muslim boys refused to shake their female teacher's hand. The school initially accommodated them consistently — neither male nor female teacher would receive a handshake. When this became publicly known, there was widespread outcry, and the court ruled that the students must shake the teacher's hand. The intersection of cultural norms, religious conscience, and civil law in that case mirrors the *halachic* complexity of this question.

### Two Approaches

Two distinct approaches emerge from the *rishonim* and *poskim*. One approach holds that there is a formal *issur de'rabbanan* on any physical contact with an *ervah*, regardless of intent, though it may be overridden in cases of *kevod habriyot* — for instance, in a business context where refusing to shake hands would cause significant embarrassment. Under this approach, the practice of waiting for the woman to extend her hand first is sometimes invoked, though in many situations *kevod habriyot* may itself require the man to initiate.

The second approach, grounded in this Gemara and parallel sources, holds that where the touch is genuinely neutral — carrying no sexual charge for either party — there is no formal prohibition at all. The permission is not an override; there is simply nothing to override. Under this view, the question is entirely case-by-case, evaluated honestly based on the nature of the contact and the realistic likelihood of *hirhurim*.

### Self-Deception Risk

The vulnerability in the second approach is precisely the problem illustrated by the student who mocked Rabbi Tarfon. The very mechanism of denial — "I have no such *yetzer hara*, this concerns me not at all" — is the most dangerous posture one can adopt. The Gemara's recurring theme is that sexual temptation is more powerful and more subtle than people acknowledge, and self-assessment in this domain is notoriously unreliable. One can balance these two teachings of Shmuel — the sweeping formal prohibitions on one hand, and *hakol leshem shamayim* on the other — either by saying the formal rules always apply as defaults and only genuine special cases may invoke the principle of pure intent; or by saying there are no formal rules at all, only an obligation to exercise honest personal judgment. Either way, the risk of self-deception looms large.

## Multiple People Yichud

### Two Women Rule

The Gemara records that Rava taught: a man may be in *yichud* with two *yevamot* — sisters-in-law — or with two *tzarot* (co-wives), or with a woman and her mother-in-law, or with a woman and her stepdaughter. The common thread in all these cases is mutual animosity: the two women despise one another, and therefore each functions as an effective watchdog on the other's behavior. If a man were to attempt to seduce one of them, the other would immediately expose him, since she has every incentive to ruin his reputation. The general rule is that one man cannot be alone with two women — because he might seduce both, or seduce one while the other remains silent. But where the two women hate each other, that mutual hostility renders any misconduct effectively impossible to conceal.

### Yevamot & Tzarot

The specific examples — *shtei yevamot* and *shtei tzarot* — are particularly apt. Two sisters-in-law are in tension because if one husband dies, levirate marriage (*yibbum*) may make the survivor a co-wife to the other. Co-wives are structurally rivals, and their animosity is presumed. A woman and her mother-in-law, and a woman and her stepdaughter, are similarly natural adversaries whose mutual surveillance creates a reliable deterrent.

### Woman & Child

The Gemara adds another case: a woman together with a young child who understands what sexual relations are but would not herself willingly participate — *sheyoda'at ta'am biah ve'ein moseret atzmah l'biah*. Rashi explains: *sheyoda'at ta'am biah* — she understands enough about what sex is that she would report something inappropriate if she witnessed it — *sheteda l'saper devarim bashuk*, she will blurt it out in the marketplace. Children have no discretion; they will innocently repeat anything they have seen. But *einah moseret atzmah l'biah* — she will not willingly participate in the act herself, because she has not yet developed a sexual inclination: *mitoch kotan adayin lo lavsha yetzer*. The child's involuntary transparency thus makes the situation safer than two adult women whose silence might be secured.

### Child as Witness

The child functions, in other words, as an involuntary watchdog — not a formal witness in the legal sense, but an unavoidable deterrent. It is worth noting that the Gemara's entire analysis here assumes the concern is about consensual relations. The observation that the child lacks the inclination to participate (*einah moseret atzmah l'biah*) implicitly sets aside any concern about coercion or abuse — an absence that modern readers will find striking.

## Unmarried Teachers

### Teacher Restrictions

The Mishnah states: *lo yilamed adam ravak sofrim* — an unmarried man (*ravak*, meaning never-married) should not serve as a school teacher. The grammar is unusual — literally "he should not teach scribes" — but the meaning is that he should not be a *melamed*, a teacher of young children. The Mishnah adds a parallel case: *velo telamed isha sofrim* — a woman likewise should not be a school teacher. This is particularly striking given that the overwhelming majority of primary school teachers today are women.

### Rabbi Eliezer Extension

Rabbi Eliezer extends the prohibition: *af mi she'ein lo isha lo yilamed sofrim* — even a man who currently lacks a wife, whether through divorce or widowhood, may not serve as a school teacher. One might have argued that a man who has previously been married and has had an outlet for his sexuality is no longer at risk — but Rabbi Eliezer rejects this reasoning. The conclusion is that under all these positions, the only person who may serve as a school teacher is a married man currently living with his wife.

A Baraita further refines Rabbi Eliezer's position: *af mi sheyesh lo ve'einah sheruya etzlo lo yilamed soferim* — even a man who is married but whose wife is temporarily away (traveling, visiting family, etc.) may not teach. Even that temporary separation creates sufficient concern that he should take leave from teaching while she is absent.

### Shepherds & Animals

The Mishnah also records Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: *lo yir'eh ravak behemah* — an unmarried man should not be a shepherd, and *velo yishnu shenei ravakim betallit achat* — two unmarried men should not sleep under the same blanket. The *chachamim*, however, permit these, and a Baraita supports them: *lo nechshadu Yisrael al mishkav zachar velo al habehemah* — Israel is not suspected of relations with another male or with an animal. The *chachamim*'s position is that since Jews are presumed not to engage in these acts, there is no basis for the restrictions Rabbi Yehuda imposes.

This statement — *lo nechshadu Yisrael al mishkav zachar* — carries significant conceptual weight. It reflects that *Chazal* treated sex between two men as so utterly foreign to the Jewish norm that they saw no need to legislate *yichud* rules for it, just as they saw no need for such rules regarding animals. This creates genuinely complex questions for contemporary *halachic* discussion: if a gay man is known to be sexually active with other men, does the formal *yichud* framework now apply? Some *poskim* argue that once the concern is real for a particular individual, formal rules do kick in; others maintain that *Chazal* never legislated in this area at all, and therefore the only applicable framework is the general principle of not exposing oneself to temptation — not a formal *issur*, but basic *halachic* responsibility.

Related questions arise as well: does the absence of formal legislation mean a gay man may be in *yichud* with a woman without concern? These are live questions debated in contemporary *posekim*, with views on multiple sides.

### Parent Attraction

The Gemara asks: what precisely is the concern that an unmarried male teacher poses? One might assume it is about the children — but the Gemara immediately dismisses this: we have just established that *lo nechshadu Yisrael al mishkav zachar*, so the children in *cheder* (who are all boys) cannot be the concern. The Gemara answers: *ela ravak mishum immahot deyenokei* — the concern is the mothers who bring the children to school. An unmarried male teacher will have daily, intimate, emotionally charged conversations with these mothers — about their children's progress, their struggles, their needs — and such sustained personal contact creates temptation. Similarly, *ve'ishah mishum avot deyenokei* — an unmarried female teacher poses the same risk vis-à-vis the fathers.

### Gender Asymmetry

This raises an important question: why is a *married* man a permissible teacher, but a *married* woman is not? The Mishnah says an unmarried woman cannot teach — not a married one who lacks a wife, but *any* woman. The logic for the male teacher is clear: a married man is less susceptible to the temptation presented by the mothers, or less likely to pursue it, whether because he is sexually satisfied at home or because he fears his wife's reaction. But why does marriage provide no equivalent protection for a female teacher? Why is the married female teacher still considered a risk to the fathers who come to collect their children?

The most plausible reading is that the Mishnah's primary concern is the vulnerability of the male party — the fathers — rather than the female teacher's inclinations. We seem to be more worried about the fathers than about the married male teacher. The Mishnah may not have fully incorporated later Amoraic rulings such as *ba'alah ba'ir* (the principle that a woman is deterred by concern for her absent husband) — and even if it had, that principle addresses the woman's behavior, not the fathers' temptation. The underlying structure reflects the patriarchal framework of the society in which these rules were formed.

## Women-Focused Trades

### Rashi-Rambam

Before proceeding to the Mishnah's next case, the earlier question of whether three women together eliminate *yichud* deserves resolution. Rashi reads the coming Mishnah as built on the premise that as a general rule, one man together with three women does *not* constitute *yichud*. It is only for the person whose trade involves constant contact with women — a dressmaker, a jeweler — that the normal leniency of three women is removed, because familiarity has lowered his inhibitions. Rambam and Tosafot, by contrast, hold that two women, three women, and any larger number of women are equally subject to *yichud* with a single man, unless a second man is present. Under this reading, the Mishnah is simply affirming that even the professional who deals with women daily must observe the standard *halachot* and ensure another man is present.

The practical ruling among Ashkenazim generally follows Rashi: one man with three women does not constitute *yichud*. Some authorities, however, follow the Rambam.

### Business with Women

The Mishnah states: *kol she'asakav im hanashim lo yityached im hanashim* — anyone whose business involves women should not be in *yichud* with women. The examples that follow in the Gemara include jewelers and goldsmiths, weavers and embroiderers, those who clean hand-mills, traveling perfumers and fragrance vendors, tailors, barbers (since mothers bring children to barbers), launderers, bloodletters, bathhouse attendants, and tanners. This is a broad list of tradespeople whose professional work puts them in sustained personal contact with women. Regarding some of these — particularly the goldsmith and jeweler who adjusts rings and bracelets, and the tailor who fits garments — the nature of the work itself involves physical proximity of an intimate kind.

The Gemara adds that neither a king nor a *kohen gadol* should be appointed from among those who practice these lowly trades — not because their practitioners are technically disqualified (*pasul*) as witnesses or sinners, but because *dezilu umnasaihu* — the trades themselves are considered socially degraded. In a patriarchal society, occupations requiring constant interaction with and attendance upon women carried a certain stigma.

### Avoid Women-Trades

The Mishnah concludes: *velo yilamed adam b'no umanut hanashim* — a man should not teach his son a trade that deals primarily with women, because of the ongoing exposure to temptation that such work entails. This sets up the Mishnah's positive teaching, which follows immediately.

## Clean Livelihood

### Rabbi Meir Teaching

Rabbi Meir teaches: *l'olam yilamed adam b'no umanut nekiyah v'kalah* — a man should always teach his son a clean and easy trade — one that does not require him to soil his hands, that is not physically exhausting, and that does not put him in uncomfortable social situations. Why not choose a more demanding and lucrative trade? Rabbi Meir answers: *veyitpallel l'mi sheha'osher vehanechasim shelo* — one should pray to the One to Whom all wealth and possessions belong, because *she'ein umanut she'ein bah aniyut ve'ashirut* — there is no trade that lacks both rich and poor practitioners. You can find stockbrokers who earn millions and stockbrokers who barely survive. Therefore *shelo aniyut min ha'umanut velo ashirut min ha'umanut* — poverty and wealth are not functions of one's profession; *ela hakol lefi zechuto* — it is all according to one's merit. Tosafot interestingly interprets *zechut* here as referring to one's *mazel*, based on a parallel Gemara, though ordinarily *zechut* and *mazel* are quite distinct concepts.

### Lottery Parable

The point is captured by the familiar parable of the man who prays his whole life, keeps all the *mitzvot*, and cannot understand why he has never won the lottery — only to hear a heavenly voice respond: "Idiot, buy a ticket." One obviously cannot wait for money to fall from heaven without working. Rabbi Meir is saying: yes, you must work; but choose a job that will not exhaust you or entangle you in spiritual danger, that will leave you time to learn *Torah* — for that, as the Maharsha explains, is the implicit point — and trust that God controls the financial outcome. The size of one's income is not determined by how demanding one's profession is.

### Animal Sustenance

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar asks: have you ever seen an animal or a bird that has a profession? *Vehem mitparnesim shelo b'tza'ar* — they are sustained without effort. *Vehalo lo nivreu ela leshamsheni* — and these animals were created merely to serve me. *Ve'ani nivrati leshamesh et koni* — I was created for a higher purpose, to serve my Creator. Does it not stand to reason, then, that I should be sustained without effort, if even beings of lower purpose are provided for effortlessly?

The answer: *ela she'hare'oti ma'asai vekippachti et parnasati* — because I have corrupted my deeds and thereby forfeited that easy sustenance. The obligation to labor is the consequence of the primordial sin — *beze'at apecha tochal lechem* — not the natural order of creation. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar appears to be reinforcing Rabbi Meir's message: do not imagine that professional choice is what determines your income. The world as it was meant to be required no effort for sustenance; the effort we now expend is the consequence of sin. So work, yes — but focus on leading a righteous life, pray to God, and do not overinvest your energies in professional advancement at the expense of *Torah* and *mitzvot*.

## Disreputable Trades

### Robber Trades

Abba Guryon of Tzaidon taught in the name of Abba Gurya: *lo yilamed adam et b'no chammar gamal kaddar sappan ro'eh v'chanvani* — a man should not teach his son to be a donkey driver, a camel driver, a wagon driver, a sailor, a shepherd, or a storekeeper, *she'umanutan umanut listim* — because their trade is the trade of robbers. Rashi explains: those who drive animals and travel on long journeys graze their animals in others' fields and collect firewood from others' land. The storekeeper cheats with weights and measures.

### Refined Judgment

Rabbi Yehuda then offers a more nuanced reading in the name of Abba Guryon: *hachammarim rubban resha'im* — donkey drivers, most of them are wicked, since they regularly trespass and appropriate from others. *Vegamalim rubban ksheirim* — but camel drivers, most of them are upright. Why? First, because they travel through the desert, where there is little opportunity to steal from others. Second, as Rashi notes, the constant exposure to mortal danger makes them more pious — when one's life is perpetually at risk, one prays more sincerely and lives more carefully. *Hasapanim rubban chasidim* — and sailors are mostly genuinely righteous, for the dangers of the sea are even more acute and their dependence on God more immediately felt.

The Maharsha reconciles the two statements by arguing that the original condemnation of all these trades as the work of robbers (*listim*) applied specifically to shepherds and storekeepers, who have the most structural opportunity for theft. The camel drivers and sailors were included in the list not because they rob but because their professions take them far from home for extended periods — which carries the concerns about instability, absence from family, and separation from *Torah* life that Rabbi Meir also addressed. They are discouraged not as criminals but as impractical for a life of Jewish normalcy.

### Doctors to Gehinnom

*Tov shebi-rofin l'Gehennom* — the best of the doctors go to *Gehinnom*. Rashi offers two explanations: first, the excellent doctor takes such pride in his own vitality and health that he does not humble his heart before God — unlike the sailors, who are constantly face to face with death and therefore pray earnestly. Second, the best doctor sometimes causes death through negligence or overconfidence, and at other times has the ability to treat a poor patient but withholds care. The Maharsha's reading is particularly pointed: *tov shebi-rofin* — the doctor who considers himself the *best* is the one destined for *Gehinnom*. It is the physician who will not consult colleagues, who trusts his own judgment above all, who is most dangerous. The arrogance of thinking oneself the best of doctors is itself the spiritual flaw that sends one to *Gehinnom*.

### Butcher as Amalek

*Vehakasher shebatabbachim shutfo shel Amalek* — and the most apparently upright of butchers is a partner with Amalek. Rashi suggests this refers to giving people *treifa* meat rather than lose the *parnassah*. But the identification with Amalek points toward something deeper: the butcher who appears pious but channels a redirected bloodlust. The association with Amalek — the archetype of gratuitous violence — implies that even the *kosher* butcher participates in a culture of bloodshed, however redirected it may be through the structures of *shechita*.

## Torah Over Trade

### Nehorai's Position

Rabbi Nehorai declares: *maniach ani kol umanut sheba'olam ve'einey melameid et bni ela Torah* — I would set aside all the professions in the world and teach my son nothing but *Torah*.

### Maharsha's Reading

The Maharsha clarifies that Rabbi Nehorai is not arguing that a profession is unnecessary or that one should simply wait for sustenance to arrive miraculously. Rather, he is reinforcing Rabbi Meir's emphasis: yes, one acquires some profession, but the entirety of one's educational priority — the focus of what one teaches and prepares one's son for — should be *Torah*. One invests minimally in professional training and maximally in *Torah* formation.

### Torah's Rewards

The reason is practical as much as theological: *she'adam ochel mis'chara ba'olam ha-zeh vehakeren kayemet lo l'olam ha-ba* — a person enjoys the rewards of *Torah* in this world and the principal remains for *olam ha-ba*. By contrast, every other profession fails in the end: *keshe'adam ba lidei choli o lidei zikna o lidei yisurim ve'eino yachol la'asok bim'lachto, harei hu meit bara'av* — when illness, old age, or suffering strikes and a person can no longer practice his trade, he starves. *Aval Torah einah khen* — but *Torah* is not like that. It *mishmarto mikol ra b'na'aruto* — protects a person from all harm in youth (and presumably from sexual temptation as well, which has been the recurring theme of this *perek*), *venotenet lo acharit vetikva b'ziknuto* — and gives him hope and meaning in old age.

### Biblical Support

Two prooftexts are adduced. For youth: *vekovei Hashem yachalifu koach ya'alu ever kanesharim* — those who hope in God will renew their strength, they will soar on wings like eagles. For old age: *od yenuvun b'seivah, dashanim vera'ananim yihiyu* — they will still be fruitful in old age, still full of sap and vigor. Torah learners never become obsolete.

## Aggadic Expansion

### Abraham Prooftext

The Gemara then brings: *vekhen hu omer b'Avraham Avinu* — Abraham our forefather fulfilled the entire *Torah* before it was given, as it says, *eikev asher shama Avraham b'koli vayishmor mishmarti mitzvotai chukotai vetorotai* — because Abraham hearkened to My voice and observed My safeguards, My commandments, My statutes, and My teachings. This prooftext appears to be something of a gloss — it is not immediately obvious how Abraham's precocious *Torah* observance connects to the preceding argument about *Torah*'s rewards in youth and old age.

### Tannaitic Authenticity

Indeed, some scholars argue that the entirety of the section from Rabbi Meir onward may be a later *aggadic* addition — perhaps a Baraita or redactional gloss — rather than part of the original Mishnah itself. The real conclusion of the *perek*, on this view, was *kol she'asakav im nashim lo yityached im hanashim*, and possibly the line immediately following about not teaching one's son a women's trade. The *aggadic* material from Rabbi Meir through Rabbi Nehorai, along with the Abraham prooftext at the end, would then represent a later accretion that organically closed the tractate on a broader thematic note.

### Life Organization

Whether or not the entire section is a later addition, its thematic fitness as a conclusion to Masechet Kiddushin is undeniable. This tractate is often thought of as being simply about the laws of betrothal. But a broader view reveals something more comprehensive: Kiddushin opens with the paradigm of a father betrothing his daughter — *kiddushin* as the primal act of establishing a family unit — and proceeds through *kinyanim*, the acquisition structures of Jewish law, to the question of parental obligations toward children. And what are those obligations? To marry off one's child, to teach him *Torah*, and to teach him a trade. Masechet Kiddushin turns out to be, on this reading, a guide to organizing a Jewish life: marriage, *Torah*, and *parnassah* — the three pillars.

### Three Obligations

The final *aggadic* section thus makes sense as the natural culmination of the tractate. Having established the *halachot* of marriage and family formation, and having devoted much of this last *perek* to the dangers of sexual temptation in professional contexts, the Mishnah closes by asking: what kind of work should you do, and how do you balance the obligations of *parnassah* and *Torah*? The answer — choose a clean and easy trade, pray to God for wealth, and focus your deepest educational energies on *Torah* — integrates the tractate's practical and spiritual concerns into a single unified vision of how to live.

## Disreputable Trades (II)

### Bloodletter Traits

A Baraita lists ten characteristics attributed to bloodletters: *mehalach al tzido* — he walks with an erect, prideful bearing; *verucho gassa* — he is haughty; *veniklat veyoshev* — when he sits, he reclines with exaggerated ease, displaying his arrogance; *ve'eino tzara* — he is stingy. Rashi explains the stinginess: a bloodletter is always being hosted and fed wherever he practices, so he grows accustomed to never spending his own money. *Ve'eino ra'ah* — he has an evil eye against others; he dislikes seeing healthy people, since healthy people are bad for business. *Ochel harbeh umotzi kima* — he eats abundantly (from the fine meals to which he is always treated) but produces very little waste, because, Rashi notes, the rich food — fatty meats, white bread — has that effect.

Most significantly: *vechashud al ha'arayot* — he is suspect of sexual sin. Rashi explains: *mito shehanashim tzrichot lo ve'asakav imahem* — because women need his services and his work is with them. The bloodletter is in a position of power over vulnerable people, and that power creates opportunity. This is the dynamic of abuse that recurs throughout this *perek*: the professional who has structured access to women, who is seen as an authority, who meets with them in relatively private circumstances. The remaining traits — *ve'al hagezel*, he is prone to theft through overcharging, and *al shefichut damim*, he is careless with life — complete the picture of a professional whose work breeds dangerous moral habits.

## Necessary Work

### Rebbe on Trades

Rebbe teaches: *ein lecha umanut she'overet min ha'olam* — there is no profession that disappears from the world. Every trade has its role; someone will always need to do every kind of work. *Ashrei mi she'roeh et horav be'umanut me'ula* — praised is the person who sees his parents engaged in a refined profession. *Oy lo lemi she'roeh et horav be'umanut pguma* — woe to the one who sees his parents in a degraded trade. Rebbe — who was the *nasi* and a man of considerable means — may have in mind something different from Rabbi Meir's "clean and easy trade." Whether Rebbe means social prestige, moral cleanliness, or simply not the disreputable trades catalogued in this *perek* is not entirely clear.

### Perfumer & Tanner

*Iy efshar la'olam belo basam uvelo borski* — the world cannot exist without a perfumer or a tanner. Both are necessary. But *ashrei mi she'umanuto basam* — praised is the one whose trade is perfumery, *oy lo lemi she'umanuto borski* — and woe to the one whose trade is tanning. Someone must be a tanner — the world cannot function otherwise — but you personally should aspire to be the perfumer.

### Final Gender Statement

The Mishnah closes with a statement of breathtaking scope and undeniable provocation: *iy efshar la'olam belo zacharim uvelo nekevot* — the world cannot exist without males and females. *Ashrei mi shebanav zacharim* — praised is the one whose children are male. *Oy lo lemi shebanav nekevot* — woe to the one whose children are female.

### Dowry Reading

The most charitable and probably most historically accurate reading of this statement concerns economics, not ontology. In the world of the Talmud, daughters required dowries and were not expected to earn a living independently. A father with daughters faced the burden of providing for their marriages and had no expectation that they would support him in old age. A father with sons, by contrast, had financial assets who would provide for the household, carry on his trade, and support him in his later years. The statement is about the financial anxiety of raising daughters in a patriarchal economic structure — along the lines of Tevye in *Fiddler on the Roof*, who has five daughters and no sons, and whose central preoccupation is marrying them off. It is emphatically not a statement about the intrinsic worth of daughters, and should not be read as one.

## Masechet Closure

### Meir Restated

The Gemara restates Rabbi Meir's teaching: *l'olam yilamed adam livno umanut nekiyah v'kalah veyevakesh rachamim lemi she'ha'osher vehanechasim shelo* — always teach your son a clean and easy trade, and pray for mercy to the One to Whom wealth and possessions belong. *She'ein aniyut min ha'umanut ve'ein ashirut min ha'umanut ela lemi she'ha'osher shelo* — poverty and wealth are not functions of one's trade but of God's will — *shenemar li hakesef veli hazahav neum Hashem Tzevaot* — as it says, "Mine is the silver and Mine the gold, says the Lord of Hosts."

### Elazar Restated

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's teaching is then restated: *miyami lo raiti tzvi kayatz, ve'ari sabal, veshual chanvani* — never have I seen a deer who dried figs for a living, or a lion who was a porter, or a fox who was a storekeeper. *Vehem mitparnesim belo tza'ar* — and they are provided for without effort. *Vehem lo nivreu ela leshamshani, va'ani nivrati leshamesh et koni* — they were created only to serve me, and I was created to serve my Creator. Does it not follow that I should be sustained without effort? *Ela she'hareti et ma'asai vekipachti et parnasati* — only because I have corrupted my deeds have I forfeited that effortless sustenance — *shenemar avonotechem hitu* — as it says, "Your sins have deflected these things." One wonders, in closing this tractate with a return to the primal consequences of sin, whether the echo of *Gan Eden* is intentional — the perfect marriage of *Gan Eden*, the effortless sustenance of *Gan Eden*, both lost through that first transgression, and *Masechet Kiddushin* in some sense tracking the long human effort to reconstruct a sanctified life within those fallen conditions.

### Nehorai Final

A final Baraita records Rabbi Nehorai's teaching once more: *maniach ani kol umanut sheba'olam ve'einey melamed et bni ela Torah, shekol umanut sheba'olam eina omedet lo ela bimei yalduto* — I set aside all the professions of the world and teach my son nothing but *Torah*, because every other profession serves him only in his youth. *Bimei ziknuto harei hu mutal bara'av* — in old age, he will starve if all he has is his trade. *Aval Torah einah khen* — but *Torah* is not like that. *Omedet lo la'adam be'et yalduto venotenet lo acharit vetikva be'et ziknuto* — it sustains him in youth and gives him hope in old age. *Be'et yalduto ma hu omer? Vekovei Hashem yachalifu koach ya'alu ever kanesharim* — in youth the verse says: "Those who hope in God will renew their strength, they will soar on wings like eagles." *Beziknuto ma hu omer? Od yenuvun besivah, dashanim vera'ananim yihiyu* — in old age: "They will still be fruitful in old age, still full of sap and still fresh."

### Completion

*Hadran alach asarah yuchasin* — we have completed the final *perek* of Masechet Kiddushin. The formal *siyum* will await a proper *minyan*, but the learning is done. Tomorrow we begin Bava Kamma. *Yasher koach* to all.
