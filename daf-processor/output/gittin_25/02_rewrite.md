# Gittin 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lishmah & Same Names

### Review Lishmah

The daf begins on 24b, picking up mid-*mishnah* in the discussion of *lishmah* — the requirement that a *get* be written with specific intent for a particular woman. The *mishnah* had established that a husband cannot instruct a *sofer* to write a *get* in an open-ended fashion, leaving the determination of which wife it is intended for to be resolved later. The underlying concern is whether such a *get*, lacking the concrete *lishmah* required at the moment of writing, can be valid at all.

### Identity Clarifies

The *gemara* had raised a related question: if both wives share the same name, and a *get* is written naming that shared name, does the identity of the woman actually holding the *get* serve to clarify and establish its validity? On the surface this seems reasonable — the *get* is in her hand, and that physical reality might suffice to resolve any ambiguity.

### R. Eliezer Position

The *gemara* ultimately suggested that this question may need to be understood according to the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who does not regard the *get* as functioning primarily as a *shtar re'ayah* — a document that must itself bear evidentiary testimony as to its subject. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *adei mesirah* — the witnesses to the act of delivery — are what effect the divorce, not the text of the document as a self-sufficient proof-text. Under that view, the identity of the recipient, confirmed by those witnesses, could supply what the document alone does not.

## Rei'ach Haget

### Rav's Position

The *gemara* now introduces a question of considerable practical consequence: if one of these invalid *gittin* — written *shelo lishmah* but bearing the correct names and physically indistinguishable from a valid *get* — was actually delivered to the wife, what is her status? The problem is invisible. Everything observable about the *get* and its delivery appears perfectly proper. The *pasul* consists entirely in what was — or was not — in the mind of the *sofer* at the time of writing.

Rav rules: *kulam poslin lakehunah chutz min harishon* — all of these *gittin* render her *pasul* for *kehunah*, with the sole exception of the first case, namely a *get* written purely as scribal practice in a *sofer*'s school, never intended to serve as an actual *get*. That first case, Rav maintains, is sufficiently well understood in context — it was never treated with the weight of a real *get* — and therefore creates no impression that a divorce took place.

The severity of this ruling deserves emphasis. If such a *get* was delivered, the woman finds herself in the worst of both worlds: she is not actually divorced (*lo megureshet*), since the *get* is invalid; yet because it looks entirely valid, she is treated *miderabbanan* as a *gerusha*. If her husband is a *kohen*, he must now divorce her — she can no longer remain with him — even though the original *get* accomplished nothing legally. As Rashi notes: *im ba'alah kohen nifselah alav mishum grusha*. One might even imagine a perverse scenario in which a vindictive husband exploits this mechanism to ensure his wife cannot remarry a *kohen* even after his death, without actually effecting a valid divorce.

### Rei'ach Concept

This doctrine is known as *rei'ach haget* — the *get* "smells" valid. If a *get* walks like a *get*, talks like a *get*, and smells like a *get*, are we to treat the woman as though she were *miderabbanan* a *gerusha*? The answer, at least for Rav, is yes — with the one exception noted above.

What makes the cases in this *mishnah* particularly apt for this concern is precisely the invisibility of the defect. The problem is entirely internal, residing in the mind of the *sofer* during writing. There is nothing in the physical document, in the names, or in the manner of delivery that signals anything is wrong. It is this imperceptibility that generates the *rei'ach haget* problem.

### Shmuel's View

Shmuel disagrees with Rav and goes further: *af rishon nami posel* — even the first case, the *get* written in a *sofer*'s school, creates the *rei'ach haget* problem. For Shmuel, the criterion is whether the *get* looks valid, and since all of these cases appear valid on their face, all of them render her *pasul lakehunah*.

### Shmuel Terminology

Shmuel's position reflects a broader terminological principle. Wherever the Sages use the phrase *get pasul*, Shmuel understands that formulation to mean *pasul u'posel* — invalid, but invalidating her status as well. Similarly, wherever the Sages describe a *chalitza* as *psula*, they mean *psula u'posaltah* — it does not constitute a valid *chalitza*, yet it disqualifies her from *yibum* with the remaining brothers and, like a *gerusha*, renders her *asura lakehunah*.

This creates the characteristic irony of the *rei'ach* doctrine: the act was legally insufficient to accomplish its purpose, yet it is sufficiently valid-looking to impose the most serious consequences.

### Rambam Pasul/Batel

The Rambam, with characteristic precision, systematizes the terminology that the *gemara* itself does not always apply consistently. In *Hilchos Gittin*, Rambam reserves the term *pasul* for that which is rabbinically defective, and *batel* for that which is void at the Torah level. This distinction helps clarify what Shmuel may be driving at: when Shmuel says a *get pasul* is *pasul u'posel*, the *pasul* language signals that while the *get* may be *batel de'oraisa* — not a valid Torah-level divorce — it looks enough like a valid act that rabbinic law imposes the consequences of one. The *gemara*'s own usage is less consistent, but Rambam's framework provides an analytic grid for understanding the phenomenon.

## Geographic Dispute

### R. Eliezer Chalitzah

In *Marava* — the Land of Israel — they transmit in the name of Rabbi Eliezer a parallel ruling regarding *chalitza*: if she removed the shoe from his left foot rather than his right, or if the *chalitza* was performed at night rather than during the day, these *chalitzos* are *psulos u'poslos* — invalid, but disqualifying. The defect is not obvious enough to eliminate all legal significance; the act resembled a valid *chalitza* closely enough that its effects linger in a negative form.

### Obvious vs Subtle

The contrast with cases involving a *katan* or an *anfilya* — a minor performing *chalitza* or a soft slipper used instead of a proper shoe — is instructive and rather striking. A minor is obviously disqualified, since the *pasuk* requires *ish*; a slipper obviously fails the requirement of *na'alo*, his shoe. These defects are self-evident from the text itself. And yet, precisely because they are so obvious, those *chalitzos* are *psulos ve'ein poslos* — invalid, but not disqualifying. They create no impression of validity; no one could mistake them for the real thing.

Here the law produces a counterintuitive result: the more egregious and transparent the defect, the less damage it causes. It is the subtle, nearly-invisible *pasul* — one that mimics the valid act convincingly — that creates the most serious problems. An act that looked completely proper, even if internally defective, generates the worst-of-both-worlds status. An act that announced its own invalidity produces no consequences at all.

This logic applies equally to *gittin*. A *get* whose defect is entirely hidden — the *sofer*'s internal state of mind at the time of writing — looks precisely like a valid *get* and thus falls squarely within the *rei'ach haget* concern. The Babylonian *amora'im* (Rav and Shmuel) disagreed about exactly which cases clear the threshold, but both accepted the category.

The sages of the Land of Israel preferred to examine each case individually rather than apply a global rule based on the word *pasul*. Their criterion was whether the defect was or was not self-evident from the face of the act itself. Something explicitly disqualified by the *pasuk* — a *katan*, an *anfilya* — is too obviously invalid to create an impression. Something less explicit, something that requires looking beneath the surface, is precisely the kind of *pasul* that creates a *rei'ach* problem.

### Zeiri's Position

Zeiri takes a much narrower position. He limits the *rei'ach haget* category to the final case in the *mishnah* alone — the case where a husband has two wives with the same name and declares that he will decide later which one he wishes to divorce. This is, Zeiri argues, the single case that looks most like it could be a valid *get*: the document was not written for someone else, there is no technical scribal error, and nothing on its face is wrong. It was simply left open-ended. Moreover, the scenario of two wives sharing an identical name is itself rare. Only this case, in Zeiri's view, comes close enough to the appearance of validity to trigger the *rei'ach haget* consequence. All other *pasul gittin* are simply *pasul* — invalid — without imposing any additional *gerusha* status.

### Rav Assi Agrees

Rav Assi endorses Zeiri's position verbatim: *kulan ein poslin chutz min ha'achrona* — none of these cases create the *rei'ach haget* problem except the last one.

### R. Yochanan Rejects

Rabbi Yochanan goes further still — and in a direction that might seem surprising given the general pull of the discussion. He rules: *af achrona nami eino posel* — even the last case does not create the *rei'ach haget* problem. For Rabbi Yochanan, if a *get* is invalid, it is simply invalid, full stop. The law does not create a middle category in which an act is simultaneously not a *get* and yet treated as one for the purpose of imposing *gerusha* status. Either she is divorced or she is not.

## Yochanan Consistency

### Ein Breira Principle

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is consistent with his broader jurisprudential position. *Ve'azal Rabbi Yochanan leta'ameih* — Rabbi Yochanan goes according to his own reasoning, as transmitted by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha'achin shechalku lekuchot hen umachzirin zeh lazeh beyovel* — brothers who divide an inherited estate are considered to have purchased from one another, and they must return their respective portions to one another at *yovel*.

The connection requires unpacking. The last case in the *mishnah* — the husband who declares he will decide which wife's *get* this is — is at its core a question of *breira*: can a determination made after the fact retroactively define what was in the writer's mind at an earlier moment? *Yesh breira* holds that such retroactive determination is legally operative; *ein breira* holds that it is not.

The inheritance case illustrates the same structure. When a man dies and leaves his estate to multiple brothers, no one has designated which portion belongs to whom. When the brothers subsequently divide the estate, two conceptual frameworks are possible. Under *yesh breira*, one would say that the later division retroactively reveals what was always destined to be each brother's portion — he always "owned" the house, his brother always "owned" the fields, and the division merely makes explicit what was always the case. Under *ein breira*, no such retroactive determination is possible. Instead, the estate was held as a communal entity, and when the brothers divide it they are effectively purchasing one another's shares — I give you my half-interest in the house in exchange for your half-interest in the fields. That is a sale. And since it is a sale, at *yovel* it must return — *umachzirin zeh lazeh beyovel*.

Rabbi Yochanan rules that the brothers are *lekuchot* — purchasers — and must return their portions at *yovel*. This means he holds *ein breira*. Since he holds *ein breira* as a general principle, the last case of the *mishnah* is not merely invalid under some special rule; it is obviously invalid under the same logic Rabbi Yochanan applies universally. There is nothing impressive about it, no near-miss with validity that would generate a *rei'ach haget* concern. For someone who holds *yesh breira*, that last case might look almost kosher — which is precisely why it could be a *rei'ach haget* problem for others. But for Rabbi Yochanan, it never looked close.

### Yovel Inheritance

The *gemara* adds that it was necessary to state Rabbi Yochanan's position in both contexts — the *mishnah*'s case and the inheritance case — and explains why neither can be inferred from the other alone. If we only knew his ruling in the *mishnah*, one might say: Rabbi Yochanan holds that even the last case is not a *rei'ach haget* problem, but that is only because of the special *lishmah* requirement for *gittin*. Perhaps even Rabbi Yochanan would agree that in general *breira* does not apply to *gittin*, given the Torah's explicit demand that the *get* be written concretely *lishma* — for her sake specifically, at the moment of writing — a requirement that forecloses any retroactive determination regardless of one's general *breira* position. That says nothing about what Rabbi Yochanan holds regarding inheritance and *yovel*.

Conversely, if we only knew his ruling about inheritance — *aval yerusha umattanah lo* — one might argue differently. The Torah's law of *yovel* applies specifically to sales: *mecher hu de'amar Rachmana lihadar beyovel*. Inherited portions and gifts are not subject to *yovel*. So Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that the brothers must return at *yovel* may not reflect a strong *ein breira* principle at all — it may simply reflect the stringency of treating the division as a sale-like transaction, or the fact that there is an identifiable original estate to restore. One could therefore hold that in general Rabbi Yochanan accepts *breira*, and it would still not follow that the *mishnah*'s final case fails to create a *rei'ach haget* impression. Both statements are needed.

### Lishmah vs Breira

The *gemara*'s resolution is precise. The *mishnah*'s case operates on two distinct tracks. First, there is the *lishmah* requirement: regardless of one's position on *breira*, the Torah's demand that a *get* be written with concrete, specific intent for a particular woman at the moment of writing may be a special requirement that *breira* cannot satisfy even according to those who generally accept it. One who holds *yesh breira* might still concede that *lishmah* cannot be supplied retroactively, because the Torah demands present and concrete intentionality. Second, there is the *rei'ach haget* question: does the last case of the *mishnah*, which fails only because of this open-ended framing and not for any visible reason, create enough of an appearance of validity to impose *gerusha* status? For Rabbi Yochanan, who holds *ein breira* as a general matter, the answer is clearly no — it was never even close to valid. For those who hold *yesh breira*, however, the case may genuinely look like it could have worked, and that appearance is precisely what generates the *rei'ach haget* problem.

This explains the range of opinions. Rav held that all cases in the *mishnah* — except the purely academic scribal exercise — create a *rei'ach haget* problem because in all of them the defect is invisible. Shmuel went further, applying *rei'ach haget* wherever the Sages use the word *pasul*. Zeiri and Rav Assi restricted *rei'ach haget* to the final case alone. And Rabbi Yochanan, who holds *ein breira* universally, finds no *rei'ach haget* anywhere.

## Breira Sugya Start

### Hoshaya Question

Having established the connection between Rabbi Yochanan's rejection of *rei'ach haget* and his principle of *ein breira*, the *gemara* now enters the classic *sugya* of *breira* in its own right. Rav Hoshaya poses a question to Rabbi Yehuda: if a husband instructs a *sofer*, *kesov le'eizo she'teitzei be'pesach techila* — write the *get* for whichever wife walks out of the door first — does that work? This is what the *gemara* calls the *Yiftach* version of a *get*: the husband has made up his mind to divorce one of his wives, but the identity of that wife will be determined by an external event — who happens to exit first.

### Toleh Distinction

The *gemara* introduces the fundamental distinction the question is probing: the difference between *toleh bedaat atzmo* — leaving the matter to one's own later decision — and *toleh bedaat acherim* — leaving the determination to the action of someone or something else. In the *mishnah*'s case, the husband says "I'll decide which wife I want to divorce" — that is *toleh bedaat atzmo*. Rav Hoshaya's question is whether the case of "whichever wife walks out first" — *toleh bedaat acherim* — is legally different and might support *breira*.

### Rashi Indecisive

Rashi captures the logic precisely. When a man says "I'll decide later which wife I mean," he is demonstrating that at the moment of writing he is *pose'ach al shtei hasefim* — jumping between two doorsteps, unable to commit. The phrase comes from *Tanach* and conveys radical indecision. If he could not make up his mind then, we cannot say that later, when he does decide, it retroactively reveals what he had in mind at the moment of writing. What he had in mind then was precisely that he had not made up his mind. There is no hidden intent to be revealed; there is only unresolved ambiguity. *Breira* depends on the premise that a later determination uncovers what was always true; it cannot function when the earlier moment was itself defined by the absence of any determination.

*Toleh bedaat acherim* is entirely different. When the husband says "the wife who walks out of the door first is the one I am divorcing," he has made a complete decision. He does not know which wife that will be, but his will is fully formed and specific: he wants to divorce the wife who acts first. There is no indecision; there is only an unknown fact. And *breira* may be able to work when a later fact retroactively specifies what was always — from a determined perspective — the intended object.

## Pesach Korban

### Father's Offer

To explore whether *toleh bedaat acherim* can indeed support *breira*, the *gemara* adduces a *mishnah*: a father declares that he will slaughter the *korban Pesach* for whichever of his sons arrives first in Jerusalem on *Erev Pesach*. He then slaughters the *Pesach*.

### Retroactive Inclusion

The *mishnah* rules: as soon as the first son enters Jerusalem — *rosho verubo*, his head and the majority of his body — he retroactively becomes included in the *korban Pesach* that was already slaughtered. More strikingly, *umezakeh et echav imo* — through him, all of his brothers become included as well. This seems to be a clear case of *yesh breira*: the identity of the beneficiary is determined by a later event, yet it retroactively fixes the intent of the slaughter.

### Not True Breira

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *vechi pesachim etzel gittin* — can you compare the *korban Pesach* to *gittin*? But more fundamentally, he explains that the *korban Pesach* case is not genuine *breira* at all. The father's declaration — "for the son who arrives first" — was not an expression of genuine uncertainty about who should receive the *korban*. It was *kedei lezarzen bemitzvah* — a motivational device, intended to spur his sons to move with alacrity. In reality, the father intended the *korban* for all of them from the outset.

### Daughters Proof

The *gemara* finds textual support for this in a *baraita*: there was once a case where daughters arrived in Jerusalem before the sons. The *baraita* notes this as a demonstration that the daughters showed greater *zerizus* — alacrity — and the sons were comparatively lax. Crucially, the *baraita* does not say the sons were therefore excluded from the *korban Pesach*. They were still included. They were simply shamed by their sisters' swiftness. If the father's declaration were genuine *breira* — if the identity of the beneficiary truly depended on who arrived first — then the sons, having arrived later, would have been excluded entirely. The fact that they were not excluded confirms that the father always intended the *korban* for all of them, and the "first to arrive" language was purely about inspiring *zerizus*, not about limiting participation. Accordingly, the *korban Pesach* case cannot serve as a proof that *breira* works for *toleh bedaat acherim*.

---

## Abaye's Critique

### Different Cases

Abaye objects sharply to how the exchange unfolded. Rav Hoshaya's question was specifically about *toleh bedaat acherim* — the case where the husband has fully decided and the outcome is determined by external events. The response he received cited the *mishnah*'s ruling about *toleh bedaat atzmo* — the case where the husband himself defers his own decision — as if that settled the matter. But these are clearly distinct categories, and answering a question about one by citing a ruling about the other simply misses the point.

### Question Remains

In consequence, Abaye notes that the question stands. The *korban Pesach* case that Rav Hoshaya had adduced — before Rabbi Yochanan explained it away as *zerizus* rather than true *breira* — was in fact directly relevant to *toleh bedaat acherim*. That was the right category of case to examine. The dismissal of that case on grounds of *zerizus* may or may not be satisfying, but at least it was engaging with the correct question. The earlier answer, citing the *mishnah* about *toleh bedaat atzmo*, was simply a non-answer — it addressed a different category entirely.

Rava then interjects that perhaps the respondent who cited the *mishnah* simply rejected the premise that there is any meaningful legal distinction between the two categories. If one holds *breira* at all, one holds it across the board — *lo shna toleh bedaat atzmo velo shna toleh bedaat acherim*; and if one rejects *breira*, one rejects it across the board as well. On that view, citing the *mishnah* — which shows *breira* fails when the husband says "I'll decide" — is a valid response to any *breira* question, because the distinction Rav Hoshaya drew simply does not exist. But this, of course, is itself the position to be proven, and Rav Mesharshiya sets out to challenge it directly.

## Mesharshiya Proof 1

### Wine Separation

Rav Mesharshiya presses Rava: Rabbi Yehuda, he argues, is a tanna who holds *ein breira* for *toleh bedaat atzmo* but *yesh breira* for *toleh bedaat acherim* — demonstrating that the distinction Rava would collapse is in fact legally operative.

The first proof comes from the *baraita* about wine purchased from Samaritans on *Erev Shabbat*. Since the Samaritans are assumed certainly not to have separated *trumot u'ma'asrot*, the wine is *vadai tevel*. If there is no time to perform a proper *hafrasha* before *Shabbat*, a person might attempt to declare: the two *lugim* I will separate after *Shabbat* shall be *terumah*; ten *lugim* shall be *maaser rishon*; nine of the remaining shall be *maaser sheni* — and then begin drinking immediately.

### Meir vs Others

Rabbi Meir permits this procedure, holding *yesh breira*: the designations made now, with the specific quantities identified later, are legally effective. Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yose, and Rabbi Shimon all prohibit it. For them, this is a case of *toleh bedaat atzmo* — the person is deferring his own completion of the act, pushing off to a later moment his own identification of which physical wine corresponds to the designations he has made now. That is *ein breira*.

### Conditional Get

But Rabbi Yehuda does hold *yesh breira* in a different context. A man on his deathbed says to his wife: this shall be your *get* if I do not recover from this illness. What is her status *mahi ba'osun yamim* — during the intervening period, before his fate is known? Rabbi Yehuda rules she is *ke'ishet ish lechol devareha* — treated as a married woman for all purposes during that period. Yet when he dies, *havei gitta* — it is a valid *get*.

### Time TBD

One might initially think this is simply a standard conditional *get* — a *tenai* — and has nothing to do with *breira*. But Tosafot explains why this is, in fact, a *breira* case. Rabbi Yehuda reads the husband's words not as a condition ("if I die, the *get* takes effect from now") but as a temporal specification: the *get* takes effect *when* I die — meaning, a minute before I die. The moment of the *get*'s effectiveness is thus undefined at the time of writing; it is a real time, but a time to be determined by events entirely outside the husband's control. The *get* is not contingent — it will certainly take effect — but the precise moment is, in the language of *breira*, to be specified later by events external to the husband. This is *toleh bedaat acherim* (here, in a sense, the determination rests with God — *mi shehachayyim vehamavet teluyim beyado*), and Rabbi Yehuda accepts it. The *breira* consists in the retroactive fixing of the moment at which the *get* became effective.

## Mesharshiya Proof 2

### Father Agreement

Rav Mesharshiya then offers a second proof, this time from Rabbi Shimon. A man says to a woman: *hareini bo'alaich al menas sheyirtzeh abba* — I am having relations with you on condition that your father agrees. Marital relations (*bi'ah*) constitute one of the three recognized modes of *kiddushin*. The question is whether this act of *bi'ah* creates *kiddushin* contingent on the father's later agreement.

### Father Determines

The first opinion in the *baraita* holds she is *mekudeshet* even if the father does not agree, on the grounds of *ein adam oseh bei'alato bei'lat zenut* — a man does not intend for sexual relations to occur outside the framework of marriage. Accordingly, we presume he truly wants the *kiddushin* unconditionally, and the reference to the father's agreement is not genuinely operative. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, transmitting in the name of Rabbi Shimon, disagrees: if the father agrees, she is *mekudeshet*; if not, she is not.

### Context Not Condition

Why is this a case of *breira* rather than a simple *tenai*? The *gemara* explains that *kiddushei bi'ah* operates differently from other forms of *kiddushin*. An ordinary *tenai* works by imposing a condition on a deliberate act of will: "I choose to effect *kiddushin* on this condition." *Kiddushei bi'ah*, by contrast, does not depend on explicit intent to create marriage; rather, it is the marital context of the act itself — the fact that the relations occur in a framework of husband and wife — that constitutes the *kiddushin*. The act automatically creates marriage when done in a marital context; it does not require a formal declaration of intent.

Given this, a *tenai* in the ordinary sense cannot function here — you cannot say "I will these relations to be *kiddushin* only if X." What matters is not your will but the nature of the context. The question, then, is not whether a condition has been met, but what context the act occurred in — marital or not? And that context, Rabbi Shimon holds, is to be determined retroactively by the father's agreement. If the father agrees, the act took place in a marital context; if not, it did not. The father's later decision retroactively defines the nature of the act at the time it occurred — which is precisely *breira*. And since this is a case where the determination rests with another (*toleh bedaat acherim*), Rabbi Shimon accepts it, just as he rejects *breira* in the wine case where the person himself defers his own decision.

## Rava's Counter

### Rava All Breira

Rava responds to both proofs from Rav Mesharshiya with a unified counterargument. He rejects the premise that the two cases — wine separation and the conditional *get* or *kiddushei bi'ah* — demonstrate a principled distinction between *toleh bedaat atzmo* and *toleh bedaat acherim*. Rather, Rava maintains, both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon hold *yesh breira* across the board — whether *toleh bedaat atzmo* or *toleh bedaat acherim*. *Bein le'Rabbi Yehuda bein le'Rabbi Shimon, lo shna tola bedaat atzmo velo shna tola bedaat acherim — is lei breira.* The distinction Rav Mesharshiya is pressing, in Rava's view, does not reflect a difference in the underlying *breira* principle but rather a separate practical concern.

### Wine Spill Risk

If that is so, why do Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yose, and Rabbi Shimon prohibit the wine procedure in the *baraita*? The *gemara* explains: the prohibition is not grounded in *ein breira* but in a different concern entirely. They said to Rabbi Meir: *shema yibaka hanod* — perhaps the barrel will burst. *Venimtza shoteh tvalim lemafrea* — and it will turn out retroactively that one was drinking *tevel* the whole time. The entire *breira* mechanism depends on the wine remaining intact until after *Shabbat*, when the actual *hafrasha* can be performed. If the barrel breaks and all the wine spills, the designations made before *Shabbat* never get actualized — there is nothing left to separate — and one will have consumed untithed produce retroactively. It is this risk, not a fundamental rejection of *breira*, that motivates their ruling.

### Meir Not Concerned

Rabbi Meir's response is almost laconic in its confidence: *lichsheyibaka* — when it breaks, I'll worry about it then. He is not concerned about the risk in advance. This is, incidentally, somewhat ironic, since Rabbi Meir is generally known for *chaish lemiutei* — taking seriously even minority-probability risks. Here, however, he dismisses the concern entirely.

### Practical Safeguard

Rava's resolution is therefore elegant. The other Tannaim — Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yose, Rabbi Shimon — accept *breira* in principle, just as Rabbi Meir does. Their prohibition of the wine procedure is not a statement about *breira* but a practical safeguard against the risk of barrel-breakage leading retroactively to the consumption of *tevel*. They would permit the procedure if that risk could be eliminated. The apparent distinction between *toleh bedaat atzmo* and *toleh bedaat acherim* that Rav Mesharshiya identified dissolves on this reading: there is no principled distinction between the two categories, and the Tannaim hold *breira* universally or reject it universally.

The opposing view, of course, remains on the table: perhaps there is indeed a principled difference between the two categories, such that *breira* is available when external events supply the determination, but not when the deciding party is the same person who originally left the matter unresolved. The *sugya* closes with these two positions in tension, the question of which framework accurately describes the views of the individual Tannaim left for continued analysis.
