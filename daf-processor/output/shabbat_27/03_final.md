# Shabbat 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Yishmael contradic…

### Intro: Two baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אִית לֵיהּ, לְתַנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לֵית לֵיהּ — שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים
>
> **Translation:** And according to the opinion of Rava, who said that the practical difference between the two opinions is with regard to cloth three by three handbreadths in other garments, that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is of the opinion that they can become ritually impure, whereas the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael is not of the opinion that they can become ritually impure, in the case of a cloth that is three by three handbreadths in other garments,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא מֵ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״. דְּתַנְיָא: ״בֶּגֶד״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֶּגֶד, שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** from where does Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar derive that it can become ritually impure? The Gemara answers: In his opinion, it is derived from the verse that speaks of the ritual impurity of creeping animals: "Or a garment, or skin, or sack" (Leviticus 11:32). The additional "or" comes to include items that are not generally included in the definition of garment. As it was taught in a *baraita*: From the fact that it says garment, I have derived nothing other than a whole garment; however, a swatch that is three by three handbreadths in other garments, from where is it derived that it can become ritually impure? The verse states: Or a garment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מַפֵּיק מֵאִידָךְ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. דְּתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל ״בֶּגֶד״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֶּגֶד צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים, מִנַּיִין לְרַבּוֹת צֶמֶר גְּמַלִּים וְצֶמֶר אַרְנָבִים, נוֹצָה שֶׁל עִזִּים וְהַשִּׁירִין וְהַכָּלָךְ וְהַסְּרִיקִין? — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: This statement of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael diverges from another statement of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, as the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: From the fact that the verse says garment, I have derived nothing other than the *halakha* that a garment of wool or linen can become ritually impure. However, from where is it derived to include garments made of camels' hair and rabbits' wool, goats' hair or the types of silk, the *shirayin*, the *kalakh*, and the *serikin* among the fabrics that can become ritually impure? The verse states: Or a garment. The word "or" serves as an amplification to include all types of fabric.

The daf begins at 27a, picking up from where yesterday's shiur left off. The preceding discussion had established that certain Tannaim hold that only wool and linen fabrics are susceptible to *tum'ah*, while materials deriving from other sources — and in particular from trees — are excluded. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar had stated that anything derived from a tree, other than flax (which is treated as a tree-derivative for this purpose), cannot become *tamei*. Rabbi Yishmael had stated similarly that anything that is not wool or linen cannot become *tamei*. The *Gemara* had proceeded to treat these two positions as essentially equivalent, and the only remaining question was whether Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would acknowledge a distinction based on the size of the fabric — whether a large piece of another fabric, three *tefachim* by three *tefachim*, might cross a threshold that a small piece of three fingers by three fingers would not.

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* today introduces a *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael that appears to say precisely the opposite of what Rabbi Yishmael was understood to hold yesterday. The *baraita* is drawn from the laws of *nega'im* — the *tzara'at* afflictions — and reads: the verse says *beged tzemer o be-veged pishtim*, a garment of wool or a garment of linen. One might think that only wool and linen garments can become *tamei*. But from where do we include *tzemer gemalim* — camel's wool — and *tzemer anavim* — rabbit fur — and the down of goats, and the various silks? The verse says *o veged*, "or a garment," the word *o* coming to broaden the category to include these other materials. This is clearly a teaching attributed to the school of Rabbi Yishmael that extends *tum'ah* susceptibility well beyond wool and linen — even to silks and other fabrics that have no connection to wool at all.

### Abaye's contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מַפֵּיק מֵאִידָךְ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: This statement of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael diverges from another statement of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael.

This creates a sharp internal contradiction. Abaye observes: *hai Tanna de-bei Rabbi Yishmael mafik mi-idach Tanna de-bei Rabbi Yishmael* — one *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael contradicts another *tanna* from the same school. Yesterday's *baraita* from Rabbi Yishmael's school excluded all fabrics other than wool and linen from *tum'ah*; today's *baraita* from the same school includes them. So how are we to reconcile these two statements?

### Rava: size matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כִּי לֵית לֵיהּ לְהַךְ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בִּשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Whereas Rava said: There is no need to say that there is a dispute in this case between two *tanna*'*im* from a single school. Rather, when this *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael, quoted above, is not of the opinion that there is ritual impurity in other garments, it is only with regard to a swatch that is three by three fingerbreadths; however, with regard to a cloth that is three by three handbreadths he is of the opinion that it becomes ritually impure. His previous statement came to exclude a small garment from becoming ritually impure. This statement is referring to a larger garment that is three by three handbreadths.

Rava offers a resolution: the ruling from yesterday — that other fabrics cannot become *tamei* — was stated only with respect to small pieces, specifically *shalosh al shalosh*, three by three fingers. But for a large piece, *shalosh al shalosh tefachim* — roughly nine inches by nine inches — even Rabbi Yishmael would agree that other fabrics can become *tamei*. This is precisely the same distinction Rava had applied yesterday to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: small swaths of non-wool, non-linen material are not treated as fabrics, but large pieces are.

### Rava reverses himself

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אִית לֵיהּ, לְתַנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לֵית לֵיהּ! הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵהַהִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't Rava the one who said above that, in the case of three by three handbreadths in other garments, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is of the opinion that they can become ritually impure, whereas the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael is not of the opinion that they become ritually impure? The Gemara answers: Rava retracted that opinion in order to reconcile the opinions of the *tanna*'*im* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this, and the challenge is pointed: *ve-ha Rava hu de-amar shalosh al shalosh bi-sh'ar begadim le-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar it lei le-Tanna de-bei Rabbi Yishmael leit lei* — Rava himself said yesterday that this very size distinction applied to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, but that Rabbi Yishmael categorically rejected it. Yesterday Rava explicitly held that Rabbi Yishmael was across the board: other fabrics were simply out, with no size-based exception. How can Rava now use precisely that distinction to resolve a contradiction within Rabbi Yishmael's own school?

### Rava/Rav Papa revision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא רַב פָּפָּא אַמְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And if you wish, say instead a different answer: Rav Pappa said this statement and not Rava. Since Rav Pappa was the primary disciple of Rava, the Gemara attributed his statement to Rava.

The *Gemara* answers: *hadar bei Rava me-ha-hu* — Rava reversed himself. Yesterday Rava had maintained that Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagreed, with Rabbi Yishmael rejecting the size distinction entirely. Today, confronted with the internal contradiction in the Yishmael school, Rava reconsiders. He now holds that Rabbi Yishmael also embraces the size distinction, just as Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar does, and therefore Abaye was right that they ultimately agree — though not in the way Abaye originally framed it. Abaye had said they both categorically exclude other fabrics; Rava now says they both make the same size-based distinction: three fingers by three fingers — other fabrics excluded; three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* — other fabrics included.

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative: *ve'iba'it eima*, perhaps it was Rav Papa, not Rava, who made this revised statement. This is not surprising: Rav Papa was Rava's *talmid muvhak*, his primary disciple and eventual successor as head of the *yeshiva*, and it often happened that statements originating with Rav Papa were attributed to Rava simply because of the closeness of the relationship. Under this reading, Rava himself maintained until the end that Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagreed, while Rav Papa — seeking to reconcile the internal contradiction in the Yishmael *baraitot* — proposed that even Rabbi Yishmael accepts the size distinction.

The practical significance of this entire discussion extends beyond *tum'ah* to the question of which garments are obligated in *tzitzit*. If other fabrics, when large enough, are considered garments in the full halakhic sense, then presumably that same principle applies when determining the *tzitzit* obligation.

## Rav Papa: Shatnez angle

### Shatnez vs. Tuma'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אַף כֹּל, לְאֵתוּיֵי כִּלְאַיִם. כִּלְאַיִם בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיבִי בֵּיהּ: ״לֹא תִלְבַּשׁ שַׁעַטְנֵז צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים יַחְדָּיו״! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי דֶּרֶךְ לְבִישָׁה, אֲבָל בְּהַעֲלָאָה כֹּל תְּרֵי מִינֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa himself understood the first statement of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael and stated it in a completely different manner. In his opinion, the derivation from the *halakhot* of leprosy, which concluded that even all nonspecific mentions of garments in the Torah refer to wool or linen, came to include the *halakhot* of diverse kinds, the Torah prohibition to wear clothing made from a mixture of wool and linen threads. He sought to prove that the *halakhot* of prohibited mixtures of threads apply only to wool and linen. The Gemara asks: Why does he require this derivation with regard to the prohibition of diverse kinds? The fact that the prohibition is limited to wool and linen is explicitly written, as it is stated: "You shall not wear diverse kinds, wool and linen together" (Deuteronomy 22:11). The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, an additional derivation was necessary, as it would have entered your mind to say that this, the restriction of the prohibition of diverse kinds to wool and linen, applies specifically to a case when one uses them together in the manner of wearing them; however, in merely placing the garments upon oneself, any two kinds are prohibited. Therefore, it was necessary to derive that the garment mentioned is restricted to wool and linen.

The *Gemara* now presents a second approach to resolving the contradiction within the Yishmael school, attributed to Rav Papa in his own right: *af kol le-atoyei kilayim* — what did yesterday's *baraita* mean when it emphasized "only wool and linen"? Not that other fabrics are excluded from *tum'ah* — today's *baraita* shows clearly that they are included. Rather, yesterday's emphasis on wool and linen was addressing the prohibition of *sha'atnez*, the *issur* of *kilayim*. Rabbi Yishmael was teaching that the definition of forbidden *sha'atnez* is limited to wool and linen — not any mixture of two different fabrics. The question of *tum'ah* is entirely separate, and there, other fabrics are certainly included.

### Shatnez is explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? וּמָה לְבִישָׁה דְּקָא מִיתְהֲנֵי כּוּלֵּי גּוּפֵיהּ מִכִּלְאַיִם, אָמְרַתְּ צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים — אֵין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא. הַעֲלָאָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֶלָּא דְּרַב פָּפָּא בְּדוּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** This claim is rejected: And is it not an *a fortiori* inference? Just as in the case of wearing the garment, where one's entire body derives benefit from the diverse kinds, you said that wool and linen, yes, are included in the prohibition, other materials, no, are not included; in the case of merely placing the garment upon himself, all the more so that the *halakha* should not be more stringent. Rather, certainly the *halakha* that was attributed to Rav Pappa is a mistake, and he did not say it.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *behedya ketiv behu* — this is explicit in a *pasuk*! The Torah states *lo tilbash sha'atnez tzemer u-fishtim yachdav* — do not wear *sha'atnez*, wool and linen together. Why would we need Rabbi Yishmael to teach what the Torah states outright?

### Kol V'Chomer logic

The *Gemara* answers with a *salka daitach amina*: I might have thought that the definition of *sha'atnez* as wool and linen applies only to the verse's explicit case of *levisha*, wearing. But there is a second prohibition derived from the verse *u-veged kilayim sha'atnez lo ya'aleh alecha* — a garment of *kilayim sha'atnez* shall not come upon you — which prohibits not just wearing *sha'atnez* but even placing it on one's body as a covering, like a blanket. Since that second verse does not mention wool and linen, I might have thought that in the context of *ha'ala* — placing without wearing — any two types of fabric mixed together would be forbidden. Therefore I need Rabbi Yishmael to teach me that the definition of *sha'atnez* as wool and linen applies equally whether one wears it or merely places it on oneself.

But the *Gemara* presses further: is even this needed? *Velao kal vachomer hu?* Is this not self-evident from a *kal vachomer*? By *levisha*, where one's entire body benefits from wearing the *kilayim*, the Torah still limits the prohibition to wool and linen. Certainly *ha'ala*, the weaker mode where one merely places it on oneself, should also be limited to wool and linen — and the *kal vachomer* alone would tell us that without any need for Rabbi Yishmael. The *Gemara* concludes: *ella de-Rav Papa bedusa hi* — the approach of Rav Papa resolves nothing, because the point he attributes to Rabbi Yishmael is derivable independently without Rabbi Yishmael at all.

So the ledger stands: the first resolution (size distinction) works; the second (limiting to *sha'atnez*) is rejected. The discussion moves to a third approach.

## Rav Nachman: Tzitzit only

### Tum'ah vs. Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אַף כֹּל לְאֵתוּיֵי צִיצִית. צִיצִית בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב: ״לֹא תִלְבַּשׁ שַׁעַטְנֵז צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים״, וּכְתִיב: ״גְּדִילִים תַּעֲשֶׂה לָךְ״! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא כִּדְרָבָא. דְּרָבָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״הַכָּנָף״, מִין כָּנָף. וּכְתִיב: ״צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים יַחְדָּיו״, הָא כֵיצַד? צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים פּוֹטְרִין בֵּין בְּמִינָן, בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בְּמִינָן. שְׁאָר מִינִין בְּמִינָן פּוֹטְרִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּמִינָן אֵין פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak also said that those statements of the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael do not refer to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity. They refer to another topic. In his opinion, the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael came to say that just as the *halakhot* of leprosy are limited to garments made from wool or linen, so too, all garments mentioned in the Torah are made from wool and linen. This comes to include the law of ritual fringes; the obligation of ritual fringes applies only to those materials. The Gemara asks: Why is that derivation necessary? With regard to ritual fringes it is written explicitly: "You shall not wear diverse kinds, wool and linen together" (Deuteronomy 22:11); and juxtaposed to it, it is written: "You shall make for you twisted fringes upon the four corners of your covering, with which you cover yourself" (Deuteronomy 22:12). From the juxtaposition of these two verses it is derived that the mitzva of ritual fringes applies only to garments to which the laws of diverse kinds apply. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak responded that the matter is not so clear, as it could have entered your mind to say in accordance with the statement of Rava. As Rava raised a contradiction: On the one hand, it is written: "And that they put with the fringe of each corner a thread of sky blue" (Numbers 15:39); apparently, the threads of the ritual fringes must be of the same type of fabric as the corner of the garment. However, in Deuteronomy, in the laws of ritual fringes, it is written in juxtaposition to the laws of diverse kinds: Wool and linen together. The ritual fringes may only be made of those materials. How can that contradiction be resolved? Rather, Rava says: Ritual fringes made of wool and linen exempt the garment and fulfill the obligation of ritual fringes whether the garment is of their own type, wool or linen, whether it is not of their own type. Whereas with regard to other types, a garment of their own type, they exempt; a garment not of their own type, they do not exempt. It would have entered your mind to explain this in accordance with the approach of Rava. Therefore, the *tanna* taught us that the obligation of ritual fringes applies only to wool and linen and not to other materials.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak now proposes a third resolution, found at the top of 27b. *Af kol la'asuyei tzitzit* — when Rabbi Yishmael yesterday taught that the category of wool and linen is definitive, he was not speaking about *tum'ah* at all. As today's *baraita* shows, for *tum'ah* Rabbi Yishmael includes other fabrics without reservation. What Rabbi Yishmael was teaching yesterday, from the emphasis on wool and linen in the context of *tzara'at*, is that the obligation of *tzitzit* applies only to wool and linen garments. The *nega'im* passage's insistence on wool and linen tells us not about *tum'ah* susceptibility — where other fabrics are fine — but about which garments carry the biblical *tzitzit* obligation.

### Garment inclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כִּדְרָבָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It would have entered your mind to explain this in accordance with the approach of Rava. Therefore, the *tanna* taught us that the obligation of ritual fringes applies only to wool and linen and not to other materials.

The *Gemara* challenges this as well: is the *tzitzit* limitation to wool and linen garments not already explicit from the Torah? The verse *lo tilbash sha'atnez tzemer u-fishtim yachdav* is immediately followed by *gedilim ta'aseh lach* — make for yourself *gedilim*, fringes — and from this juxtaposition we derive that the *tzitzit* obligation applies to wool or linen garments. Why would we need Rabbi Yishmael to teach this?

### Rava on Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְתַנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מַאי שְׁנָא לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה דִּמְרַבֵּי שְׁאָר בְּגָדִים, דִּכְתִיב — ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״, הָכָא נָמֵי לֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת שְׁאָר בְּגָדִים מֵ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״? הַהוּא — לְאֵתוּיֵי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא הוּא דַּאֲתָא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״ — פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פְּרָט לִכְסוּת סוֹמֵא? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״, הֲרֵי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״ — פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: According to the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, what is different about ritual impurity that he includes other garments not made of wool and linen because it is written: Or a garment, which is a term of amplification? Here too, in the matter of ritual fringes, say that it comes to include other garments from the phrase: Of your covering, with which you cover yourself. Rav Ashi answered: That amplification is necessary to include the garment of a blind person in the obligation of ritual fringes. As it was taught in a *baraita*, with regard to ritual fringes it is stated: "And it shall be unto you for a fringe, that you may look upon it and remember all the mitzvot of the Lord" (Numbers 15:39). The phrase: That you may look, comes to exclude a night garment, which cannot be seen and is therefore exempt from the mitzva of ritual fringes. The *tanna* continues: Do you say that the verse comes to exclude a night garment? Or is it only to exclude the garment of a blind person who is also unable to fulfill the verse: That you may look upon it? The *tanna* explains: When it says in Deuteronomy: Of your covering, with which you cover yourself, the garment of a blind person is mentioned, as he too covers himself with a covering. If so, then how do I fulfill the exclusion: That you may look upon it? It comes to exclude a night garment.

The *Gemara* responds that this is not nearly as settled as it sounds. *Lo salka daitach amina ki-deRava* — one might have thought otherwise, based on the position of Rava himself. Rava presents a contradiction between two verses. One verse states *hakanaf min kanaf* — the *tzitzit* of the corner can be made *from* the corner, meaning from the same fabric as the garment, whatever that fabric may be. This implies that any garment — silk, cotton, any material — can carry *tzitzit* and that the *tzitzit* strings should be made from that same fabric. But a second verse, juxtaposing *tzitzit* with *tzemer u-fishtim*, wool and linen, implies that the strings must be of wool or linen. How does Rava reconcile these?

### Wool/linen universal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים פּוֹטְרִין בֵּין בְּמִינָן, בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בְּמִינָן. שְׁאָר מִינִין בְּמִינָן פּוֹטְרִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּמִינָן אֵין פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Ritual fringes made of wool and linen exempt the garment and fulfill the obligation of ritual fringes whether the garment is of their own type, wool or linen, whether it is not of their own type. Whereas with regard to other types, a garment of their own type, they exempt; a garment not of their own type, they do not exempt.

Rava's resolution is elegant: *tzemer u-fishtim potrin bein be-minan bein shelo be-minan* — wool and linen strings exempt the garment whether the garment is made of those materials or not. A wool string on a silk garment, a linen string on a cotton garment — all are valid. Wool and linen are, so to speak, the universal donor of *tzitzit* strings, valid on any garment of any fabric. However, *she'ar minim*, other fabrics — silk, cotton, and the like — *be-minam potrin shelo be-minam einam potrin*: they are valid only if used on a garment of the same type. Silk strings on a silk garment — valid. Silk strings on a cotton garment — not valid.

Critically, what Rava takes entirely for granted is that the garment itself does not need to be wool or linen. Any fabric garment carries a *tzitzit* obligation. The debate between the verses concerns only the material of the *tzitzit* strings, not the garment. Given that Rava holds any-fabric garments are obligated in *tzitzit*, the *Gemara* concedes that it was not self-evident from the *pasuk* alone that the obligation is limited to wool and linen garments. Therefore, Rabbi Yishmael's teaching does add something — it tells us that only wool and linen garments are biblically obligated, against Rava's expansive reading. *Ka mashma lan* — that is what he is teaching us.

### Blind person vs. night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת סוֹמֵא וּלְהוֹצִיא כְּסוּת לַיְלָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָהּ בִּרְאִיָּיה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי כְּסוּת לַיְלָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְאִיָּיה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since there is one verse that includes and another verse that excludes, what did you see that led you to include a blind person and to exclude a night garment in the obligation of ritual fringes? The Gemara answers: I include the garment of a blind person because it is, at least, visible to others, and I exclude a night garment because it is not even visible to others.

The *Gemara* then examines the phrase *asher techaseh ba*, "the garment that you cover yourself with," which appears in the *tzitzit* context and is linguistically inclusive. Rav Acha the son of Rava asks Rav Ashi: since the school of Rabbi Yishmael's *baraita* today used the phrase *o veged* to include other fabrics for *tum'ah*, why shouldn't the inclusive phrase *asher techaseh ba* similarly extend the *tzitzit* obligation to other fabric garments? The *Gemara* answers that *asher techaseh ba* is not teaching us about fabric types at all — it is teaching us that the garment of a blind person (*suma*) is also obligated in *tzitzit*.

This requires explanation. The verse says *u-re'item oto* — "you shall see them" — the *tzitzit*. One might think that since a blind person cannot see, his garment is exempt. The *baraita* teaches: no, *asher techaseh ba* includes the garments of a *suma*. The counter-exclusion works as follows: from *u-re'item oto* we exclude *ksut lailah*, a nighttime garment such as pajamas, because these are not garments that are normally seen — not by the wearer, and not by the world at large, since daytime visibility was the assumed context. But the garments of a blind person, worn during the day, are visible to everyone else and thus remain within the obligation. The logic is that *tzitzit* is *chovat mana* — an obligation of the garment, not of the person — and a daytime garment worn in public is an inherently visible garment regardless of whether its wearer can see.

The *Gemara* then asks: why use *asher techaseh ba* to include the *suma's* garments rather than to include other-fabric garments? The *Gemara* answers: *mistavra* — it makes sense to read the inclusion as remaining within the category already established. The verse leads with *lo tilbash sha'atnez tzemer u-fishtim yachdav* and immediately continues with *gedilim ta'aseh lach al arba kanfot ksutcha asher techaseh ba*. Since the context is already anchored in wool and linen, it is more natural to read the inclusive language as expanding within that category — to include, for example, garments worn by people who cannot personally appreciate their visibility — rather than as opening the door to an entirely different category of fabrics.

### Wool/linen emphasized

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת שְׁאָר בְּגָדִים? מִסְתַּבְּרָא קָאֵי בְּצֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים מְרַבֵּה צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים. קָאֵי בְּצֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים מְרַבֵּה שְׁאָר בְּגָדִים?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And say that this amplification does not come to include a blind person's garments, but rather, as Rava said, to include other garments not made from wool or linen in the obligation of ritual fringes. The Gemara answers: It is logical to say that since the Torah is standing and discussing a garment made of wool or linen, it is certainly including another garment made of wool or linen. Therefore, an amplification with regard to the garment of a blind person made of wool or linen is derived. However, when the Torah is standing and discussing a garment made from wool or linen, is it reasonable to say that it is including other garments with them? Rather, other garments are certainly not derived from there.

In summary: the emphasis on wool and linen in the *nega'im* passage teaches us, according to Rav Nachman's resolution, that the biblical *tzitzit* obligation applies exclusively to wool and linen garments. For *tum'ah*, other fabrics are included, as today's *baraita* from Rabbi Yishmael's school demonstrates. The inclusive phrase *asher techaseh ba* does not override this limitation — it is directed at a different question altogether, namely the inclusion of a blind person's garments within the obligation. With this, the contradiction between the two *baraitot* of Rabbi Yishmael's school is fully resolved.

## Tzitzit ruling

### Tosafot/Rava ruling

This entire discussion has immediate *halakhic* significance: how do we rule in practice? Are garments made from fabrics other than wool and linen — cotton, silk, and the like — biblically obligated in *tzitzit* or not? Tosafot (the second new Tosafot on the page) addresses this directly. Despite the position of Rav Nachman, who holds that other fabrics are exempt, and despite Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak in *Menachot* ruling that silk garments are exempt from *tzitzit*, Tosafot rules: *kaima lan ke-Rava de-basrai hu* — we hold like Rava, who is the later authority. And according to Rava, *chiyuv be-tzitzit mide-oraisa* — other-fabric garments are biblically obligated in *tzitzit*. This is a major debate spanning the *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* that continues to the present day.

### Wool preference custom

The practical upshot is this: it is widely accepted that non-wool, non-linen garments are at minimum rabbinically obligated in *tzitzit*, and one may recite a *berachah* on them. However, for those who wish to fulfill the biblical *mitzvah* with certainty — and since *tzitzit* is after all *chovat mana*, an obligation that arises only when one actually wears such a garment, the entire exercise of wearing *tzitzit* being voluntary in the first place — many people are particular to wear a wool *tallit katan* in order to ensure they are fulfilling a *mitzvah de-oraita*. This is the reasoning behind the prevalent custom of wearing wool *tzitzit*.

One should note, however, that the stringency of wearing wool can sometimes create its own leniency: if the wool *tzitzit* is uncomfortably hot in summer, one is more likely to remove it. A more balanced approach is to maintain both wool and cotton *tzitzit* — wearing the wool when comfortable and the cotton when the wool is too warm — so that one is at least performing the biblical *mitzvah* on the occasions one wears the wool. For a *tallit gadol* made of wool, worn in the morning prayers, the biblical *mitzvah* is being fulfilled in any case.

### Rav Moshe polyester

Rav Moshe Feinstein addresses the question of entirely man-made fabrics such as polyester, which is petroleum-based and wholly non-natural. Rav Moshe rules that polyester *tzitzit* is not even rabbinically obligated, and one may not recite a *berachah* on polyester *tzitzit*. The fact that polyester *tzitzit* are commercially available and that some people use them and recite a *berachah* on them notwithstanding, Rav Moshe's position is that such fabrics fall entirely outside the halakhic category of garments for this purpose.

## Sechach discussion

### Sumchus agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר וְסוֹמְכוֹס אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. סוֹמְכוֹס — דְּתַנְיָא: סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר סִיכְּכָהּ בִּטְוִוי פְּסוּלָה מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and Sumakhos said the same thing. The Gemara specifies: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar; that which we stated above. Sumakhos; as it was taught in a *baraita*: Sumakhos says: A *sukka* that he roofed with roofing made from spun thread is disqualified because spun thread can become ritually impure from leprosy.

Returning now to the discussion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, Abaye draws a comparison between two Tannaitic positions: *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar u-Sumchus amru davar echad* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and Sumchus said the same thing. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's relevant ruling was already cited: flax — even unprocessed, not yet woven into fabric — cannot be used as *sechach* for a *sukkah*, because it is susceptible to *tum'ah* through *tzara'at*. Sumchus teaches analogously in a *baraita*: *sichecha be-tavui pesula* — if one uses spun thread as *sechach*, even though it has not yet been woven into fabric, the *sechach* is invalid, because such thread can become *tamei* through *nega'im*. The Torah states *ba-shti o va-erev* — even warp and woof threads, not yet made into a complete fabric, are susceptible to the *tum'ah* of *tzara'at*.

## Tree-derived materials…

### Flax is a tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הָעֵץ אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בּוֹ, אֶלָּא פִּשְׁתָּן. וְכׇל הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הָעֵץ אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֹהָלִים, אֶלָּא פִּשְׁתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Of all substances that emerge from the tree, one may light only with flax on Shabbat (*Tosafot*) because the other substances do not burn well. And of all substances that emerge from the tree, the only substance that becomes ritually impure with impurity transmitted by tents over a corpse is flax. If there is a dead body inside a house or a tent that is made from any materials that originate from a tree, everything in the house becomes ritually impure. However, only in the case of flax does the tent itself become impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָלַן דְּפִשְׁתָּן אִיקְּרִי ״עֵץ״? אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְהִיא הֶעֱלָתַם הַגָּגָה וַתִּטְמְנֵם בְּפִשְׁתֵּי הָעֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna mentioned flax as a material that comes from a tree. The Gemara asks: From where do we derive that flax is called a tree? Based on appearance, it does not resemble a tree at all. Mar Zutra said: It is derived from that which the verse said: "And she had taken them up to the roof and hidden them under the trees of flax" (Joshua 2:6).

The *Mishnah* states: *kol ha-yotzei min ha-etz ein madlikin bo ella pishtan* — anything that derives from a tree may not be used for Shabbat wicks, except for flax. And similarly: *ve-chol ha-yotzei min ha-etz eino mitamei tum'at ohalim ella pishtan* — anything that derives from a tree cannot become *tamei* with *tum'at ohalim* except for linen. The immediate question is: why is flax called a tree-derivative? Flax is a plant. The answer, supplied by Mar Zutra, is a *pasuk* from the story of Rachav: *va-hi he'elatan ha-gagah va-titmem be-fishtei ha-etz* — she brought the spies up to the roof and hid them among the stalks of flax, which the verse calls *pishtei ha-etz*, the wood of the flax. The word *etz* — tree, wood — is applied to flax, establishing that flax falls into the tree category for halakhic purposes.

## Sechach discussion……

### Warp/woof tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן — כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דִּתְנַן: שְׁתִי וָעֵרֶב מִטַּמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים מִיָּד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַשְּׁתִי מִשֶּׁיִּשָּׁלֶה, וְהָעֵרֶב מִיָּד, וְהָאוּנִּין שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן מִשֶּׁיִּתְלַבְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In accordance with whose opinion is Sumakhos' statement? It is in accordance with the opinion of this *tanna*, as we learned in a mishna: Warp and woof can become ritually impure from leprosy immediately after they are spun; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: The warp can become ritually impure only after it is removed from the cauldron in which it is boiled, and it is only the woof that can become ritually impure immediately. However, the bundles of unprocessed flax can become ritually impure after they are bleached in the oven and their processing is at least half-completed. Sumakhos, the student of Rabbi Meir, adheres to his position.

The *Gemara* continues the parallel between Sumchus and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. The *Mishnah* teaches: *sheti va-erev mitamei bi-nega'im miyad divrei Rabbi Meir* — warp and woof become *tamei* through *nega'im* immediately, meaning as soon as they are spun; this is Rabbi Meir's position. Rabbi Yehuda holds a more nuanced view: *ha-sheti mi-she-yishaleh* — the warp thread becomes *tamei* only once it has been drawn from the dyeing vat, since the process of dyeing is part of what completes that thread's preparation. The woof, however, becomes *tamei* immediately upon spinning. And *unim shel pishtan*, threads of flax, become *tamei* only after they are dried and whitened in the oven, since that bleaching process is what finalizes their preparation. The reading consistent with Sumchus and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position is that the *Gemara* at minimum confirms that warp and woof — even prior to weaving — can become *tamei*, which is why unspun thread used as *sechach* would be problematic.

## Tree-derived materials…

### Flax wood proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִן הָעֵץ אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֹהָלִים, אֶלָּא פִּשְׁתָּן. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: גָּמַר ״אֹהֶל״ ״אֹהֶל״
>
> **Translation:** And we also learned in the mishna that with regard to any substance that emerges from the tree, the only substance that becomes ritually impure with impurity transmitted by tents over a corpse is flax. The Gemara asks: From where do we derive this? Rabbi Elazar said: The *tanna* learned a verbal analogy [*gezera shava*] between the word tent, written in the context of ritual impurity, and the word tent,

The *Gemara*'s derivation that flax is called a *tree*, based on the verse in Rachav — *va-titmem be-fishtei ha-etz* — establishes the conceptual foundation for the *Mishnah*'s ruling about wicks. Because flax is categorized as an *etz*-derivative, the statement "anything from a tree except flax" can be understood coherently: all other tree-derivatives are excluded from use as wicks or as *tum'at ohalim* sources; flax alone, despite being a tree-derivative by the logic of this verse, is included.

### Plant vs. tree

Rashi understands the *Mishnah* as also excluding cotton from use as a wick, reasoning that once we call flax a tree and then carve out an exception only for flax, cotton — which is similarly a plant-derived fiber — is implicitly excluded. Tosafot vigorously disagrees. Tosafot asks: are we to say that a cotton wick may not be used? Cotton wicks are used universally and are widely regarded as particularly well-suited for wicks. Tosafot therefore argues that the *Mishnah*'s exclusion is directed at actual tree-derivatives — bark fibers, wood-derived materials — not at plants. The reason the *Gemara* must establish that flax is called a *tree* is not to place it alongside other plants as equally excluded, but rather because flax might have been categorized as a tree-derivative in which case it would have been excluded. The verse calling it *etz* is what allows the *Mishnah* to carve it out as an exception even within the tree category. But plants generally, including cotton, are not subject to the exclusion at all — they are valid wick materials without any need for an exception.

## Tumat Ohalim sources

### Tent vs. building

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמִּשְׁכָּן. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַיִּפְרוֹשׂ אֶת הָאֹהֶל עַל הַמִּשְׁכָּן״. מַה לְּהַלָּן שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן קָרוּי ״אֹהֶל״ — אַף כָּאן שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן קָרוּי ״אֹהֶל״. אִי מַה לְּהַלָּן שְׁזוּרִין וְחוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה, אַף כָּאן שְׁזוּרִין וְחוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה? — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֹהֶל״ ״אֹהֶל״ רִיבָּה. אִי ״אֹהֶל״ ״אֹהֶל״ רִיבָּה, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל מִילֵּי נָמֵי! אִם כֵּן גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה מַאי אַהֲנִי לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** written in the context of the Tabernacle. It is written here, in the discussion of the laws of ritual impurity: "This is the law: When a man dies in a tent, every one that comes into the tent, and everything that is in the tent, shall be impure seven days" (Numbers 19:14), and it is written there: "And he spread the tent over the Tabernacle, and put the covering of the tent above upon it; as the Lord commanded Moses" (Exodus 40:19). Just as below, with regard to the Tabernacle, the tent was made of linen and is considered a tent, so too, here, with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, only a tent made of linen is considered a tent. The Gemara asks: If so, derive the following from that same verbal analogy: Just as below the linen threads in the Tabernacle were specifically threads that were twisted and the threads were folded six times, so too, here, in all of the *halakhot* pertaining to a tent over a corpse, the threads must be twisted and their threads folded six times. The verse states the word tent, tent several times to amplify and include even a tent made of linen not identical to the Tabernacle. The Gemara asks: If the repetition of the word tent, tent several times amplifies, even all things should be included among those items that can receive ritual impurity as a tent. The Gemara answers: This amplification cannot be that far-reaching, as, if so, the verbal analogy of tent, tent, that teaches us to derive the tent over a corpse from the Tabernacle, what purpose does it serve? Rather, certainly the amplification is not absolute. Through the combination of the verbal analogy and the amplification, it is derived that this *halakha* applies specifically to linen.

Returning to *tum'at ohalim*: the underlying principle is that a fabric-based covering over a corpse becomes *tamei* itself — *tum'at ohalim* — while a structure of building materials attached to the ground does not. If a dead body lies within a house, the walls and roof of that house do not themselves become *tamei*, because *mechubar la-karka* — something fixed to the ground — is not susceptible. However, a fabric covering, even if pitched like a tent with stakes in the ground, does become *tamei* through *tum'at ohalim*. The verse *ve-hizah al ha-ohel ve-al kol ha-kelim* — one sprinkles the *parah adumah* ashes upon the tent itself — indicates that the tent covering itself contracts *tum'ah*. Wool, linen, leather — these are fabric or fabric-adjacent materials that fall into this category. Building materials — wood beams, stone, metal — do not.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: מַה לְּהַלָּה קְרָשִׁים, אַף כָּאן קְרָשִׁים! אָמַר קְרָא ״וְעָשִׂיתָ קְרָשִׁים לַמִּשְׁכָּן״. מִשְׁכָּן קָרוּי מִשְׁכָּן, וְאֵין קְרָשִׁים קְרוּיִין מִשְׁכָּן. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״וְעָשִׂיתָ מִכְסֶה לָאֹהֶל״ — הָכִי נָמֵי מִכְסֶה לָא אִיקְּרִי ״אֹהֶל״. אֶלָּא הָא דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מַהוּ שֶׁיִּטַּמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל הַמֵּת. הַשְׁתָּא עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה לָא מִטַּמֵּא, עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מִיבַּעְיָא?! — שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דַּהֲדַר אַהְדְּרֵיהּ קְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָשְׂאוּ אֶת יְרִיעוֹת הַמִּשְׁכָּן וְאֶת אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד מִכְסֵהוּ וּמִכְסֵה הַתַּחַשׁ אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו״. מַקִּישׁ עֶלְיוֹן לְתַחְתּוֹן — מַה תַּחְתּוֹן קָרוּי ״אֹהֶל״, אַף עֶלְיוֹן קָרוּי ״אֹהֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** And perhaps say: Just as below, in the Tabernacle, there were beams supporting the tent, so too, here, in the laws of ritual impurity, a tent made of beams should also be considered a tent. The Gemara responds that the verse said: "And you shall make the beams for the Tabernacle of acacia wood, standing up" (Exodus 26:15). From the language of the verse, it is derived that the Tabernacle, i.e., the curtains alone, is called Tabernacle, and the beams are not called Tabernacle, because they merely facilitate the Tabernacle. The Gemara rejects this: But if that is so, based on an analysis of the language of the verse, it says there: "And you shall make a covering for the tent of rams' skins dyed red and a covering of *teḥashim* above" (Exodus 26:14), then in that case, too, say that the covering is not considered a tent. If so, however, what of the dilemma raised by Rabbi Elazar: With regard to the hide of a non-kosher animal over a corpse, what is the ruling? Can it become ritually impure as a tent over a corpse? If the covering of the Tabernacle is not considered a tent, now, the hide of a kosher animal that covered the Tabernacle cannot become ritually impure. If that is so, is it necessary to mention that the hide of a non-kosher animal cannot become ritually impure? The Gemara answers: The cases are not comparable because it is different there, in the case of the covering of animal hides, because the verse subsequently restored its status as a tent by uniting the tent and its covering, as it is written: "They shall bear the curtains of the Tabernacle, and the Tent of Meeting, its covering, and the covering of *taḥash* that is upon it" (Numbers 4:25). The verse juxtaposes the upper to the lower covering; just as the lower covering is considered a tent, so too, the upper covering is considered a tent.

How do we know that linen becomes *tamei* through *tum'at ohalim*? Rabbi Eliezer derives this from a *gezeirah shavah* between the word *ohel* in the *tum'at ohalim* passage — *zot ha-Torah adam ki yamut ba-ohel* — and the word *ohel* in the *Mishkan* passage — *va-yifros et ha-ohel al ha-Mishkan*. Just as there, the *ohel* was made of linen — the lowest layer of the *Mishkan* was *shesh moshzar*, twisted linen — so too here, the *ohel* that becomes *tamei* is one made of linen. This *gezeirah shavah* links the law of *tum'at ohalim* to the *Mishkan* and thereby establishes linen as the paradigmatic fabric for this *tum'ah*.

The *Gemara* then asks whether the *gezeirah shavah* would require that the linen be configured precisely as it was in the *Mishkan* — with threads twisted and wound six-fold, as implied by the word *shesh* meaning both "linen" and "six." The answer is no: the word *ohel* appears multiple times — *ohel ohel ribba* — and this repetition serves to broaden the application beyond the specific configuration of the *Mishkan*. On the other hand, if the repetition is so broadly inclusive, why limit the *tum'ah* to linen at all? The *Gemara* answers that the *gezeirah shavah* retains linen as the reference point but opens all configurations of linen; the inclusive language does not extend to an entirely different material category.

### Beams vs. fabric

The *Gemara* raises a further question: perhaps the *tum'at ohalim* of the *Mishkan* requires not only a fabric but one supported by the wooden beams (*krashim*), since the *Mishkan* itself stood on such beams. The *Gemara* responds: *ve-asita krashim la-Mishkan* — you shall make beams *for* the *Mishkan*. The beams are *for* the *Mishkan*, not the *Mishkan* itself. *Mishkan karui Mishkan ve-ein krashim kruyin Mishkan* — the fabric curtain is what is called the *Mishkan*; the beams are merely its support structure. Since it is the fabric — the *Mishkan* — that is called the *ohel*, and since it is the *ohel* that becomes *tamei*, the beams are irrelevant. The fabric covering alone, without beams, constitutes the *ohel* for purposes of *tum'ah*.

### Mishkan materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מַהוּ שֶׁיִּטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֹהָלִין. מַאי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: תַּחַשׁ שֶׁהָיָה בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ — טָמֵא הָיָה אוֹ טָהוֹר הָיָה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִמְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם אֶלָּא עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בִּלְבַד!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar's dilemma was mentioned above, and now the Gemara discusses the matter itself. Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma: With regard to the hide of a non-kosher animal over a corpse, what is the ruling? Can it become ritually impure as a tent over a corpse? The Gemara clarifies: What is the essence of his dilemma? Rav Adda bar Ahava said: The *taḥash* that existed in the time of Moses is at the crux of Rabbi Elazar's dilemma. Was it non-kosher or was it kosher? Rav Yosef said: What is his dilemma? Didn't we learn explicitly: Only the hide of a kosher animal was deemed suitable for heavenly service? Certainly, the *taḥash* was a kosher species.

This then raises a question about the leather coverings of the *Mishkan*. The verse states *ve-asita michseh la-ohel* — you shall make a covering for the *ohel* — consisting of red-dyed rams' skins and the skins of the *tachash*. Since these are described as coverings *for* the *ohel*, not the *ohel* itself, one might think that leather coverings are not categorized as *ohel* and therefore do not become *tamei* through *tum'at ohalim*. The *Gemara* resolves this by citing the verse *va-yisu et yeriat ha-Mishkan ve-et ohel mo'ed michsehu u-michseh ha-tachash asher alav mi-lmalah* — they shall carry the curtains of the *Mishkan*, and the *ohel mo'ed*, and its covering, and the covering of the *tachash* that is above it. Here the coverings are listed together with the *ohel mo'ed* in a single clause: *makish elyon le-tachton* — the upper covering is equated with the lower. Just as the lower fabric is called *ohel*, so too the upper leather coverings are called *ohel*, and therefore leather also has *tum'at ohalim*.

## Non-kosher leather

### Rabbi Elazar query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי אַבָּא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי מִכְסָאוֹת הָיוּ, אֶחָד שֶׁל עוֹרוֹת אֵילִים מְאָדָּמִים וְאֶחָד שֶׁל עוֹרוֹת תְּחָשִׁים. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: מִכְסֶה אֶחָד הָיָה, וְדוֹמֶה כְּמִין תְּלָא אִילָן. וְהָא תְּלָא אִילָן טָמֵא הוּא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: כְּמִין תְּלָא אִילָן הוּא, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ גְּווֹנִין הַרְבֵּה, וְלֹא תְּלָא אִילָן. דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם טָמֵא, וְהָכָא טָהוֹר. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ דִּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן ״סָסְגּוֹנָא״, שֶׁשָּׂשׂ בִּגְווֹנִין הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba raised an objection. Rabbi Yehuda says: There were two coverings for the Tabernacle, one made of the reddened hides of rams and one of the hides of *teḥashim*. Rabbi Neḥemya says: There was only one covering for the Tabernacle, half of which was made of rams' hides and half from the hides of *teḥashim*. And *teḥashim* were similar to the species of undomesticated animals called *tela ilan*. The Gemara asks: But isn't a *tela ilan* a non-kosher creature? The Gemara emends this statement: This is what Rabbi Neḥemya intended to say: It was like a *tela ilan* in that it was multicolored; however, it was not an actual *tela ilan*. There, the *tela ilan* is non-kosher, and here, the covering of the tent was made from kosher animals. Rav Yosef said: If so, that is the reason that we translate the word *taḥash* as *sasgona*, which means that it rejoices [*sas*] in many colors [*gevanim*].

Having established that leather is susceptible to *tum'at ohalim* based on its inclusion in the *Mishkan* coverings, the *Gemara* now addresses the question posed by Rabbi Elazar: *or behemah teme'ah mahu she-yitamei tum'at ohalim?* Does the leather of a non-kosher animal also become *tamei* through *tum'at ohalim*? The source for leather's susceptibility is the *tachash* skin used in the *Mishkan* covering. The question is: was the *tachash* a kosher animal or a non-kosher animal? If it was kosher, we have no basis from the *Mishkan* to extend the *tum'ah* to non-kosher leather. But if the *tachash* was non-kosher, the *Mishkan* itself would establish that non-kosher leather becomes *tamei*. The framing of Rabbi Elazar's question — asking specifically about non-kosher leather, not about leather in general — implies that the susceptibility of kosher-animal leather is already taken for granted. The question is only whether non-kosher leather is included as well.

### Kosher only for Mishkan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִמְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם אֶלָּא עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בִּלְבַד!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef said: What is his dilemma? Didn't we learn explicitly: Only the hide of a kosher animal was deemed suitable for heavenly service? Certainly, the *taḥash* was a kosher species.

Rav Yosef responds by citing a *baraita*: *lo huchsheru li-mlechet shamayim ella or behemah tehorah bilvad* — only the skin of kosher animals was used for sacred purposes, i.e., construction of the *Mishkan*. This would imply that the *tachash* was a kosher animal, and therefore Rabbi Elazar's question about non-kosher leather remains unresolved from the *Mishkan* alone.

### Covering debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי אַבָּא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי מִכְסָאוֹת הָיוּ, אֶחָד שֶׁל עוֹרוֹת אֵילִים מְאָדָּמִים וְאֶחָד שֶׁל עוֹרוֹת תְּחָשִׁים. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: מִכְסֶה אֶחָד הָיָה, וְדוֹמֶה כְּמִין תְּלָא אִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba raised an objection. Rabbi Yehuda says: There were two coverings for the Tabernacle, one made of the reddened hides of rams and one of the hides of *teḥashim*. Rabbi Neḥemya says: There was only one covering for the Tabernacle, half of which was made of rams' hides and half from the hides of *teḥashim*. The Gemara asks: Since there is one verse that includes and another verse that excludes, what did you see that led you to include a blind person and to exclude a night garment in the obligation of ritual fringes? The Gemara answers: I include the garment of a blind person because it is, at least, visible to others, and I exclude a night garment because it is not even visible to others.

The *Gemara* challenges this through a *baraita* cited by Rabbi Abba. Rabbi Yehuda holds that there were two separate coverings: *echad shel orot eilim me-odamim ve-echad shel orot techashim* — one of red-dyed ram skins and one of *tachash* skins, each a distinct layer. Rabbi Nechemiah disagrees, holding that it was *michseh echad* — a single combined covering, a hybrid of two types of leather arranged together in a distinctive configuration.

### Tachash identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תְּלָא אִילָן טָמֵא הוּא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: כְּמִין תְּלָא אִילָן הוּא, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ גְּווֹנִין הַרְבֵּה, וְלֹא תְּלָא אִילָן. דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם טָמֵא, וְהָכָא טָהוֹר. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ דִּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן ״סָסְגּוֹנָא״, שֶׁשָּׂשׂ בִּגְווֹנִין הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But isn't a *tela ilan* a non-kosher creature? The Gemara emends this statement: This is what Rabbi Neḥemya intended to say: It was like a *tela ilan* in that it was multicolored; however, it was not an actual *tela ilan*. There, the *tela ilan* is non-kosher, and here, the covering of the tent was made from kosher animals. Rav Yosef said: If so, that is the reason that we translate the word *taḥash* as *sasgona*, which means that it rejoices [*sas*] in many colors [*gevanim*].

The *tachash* is further described in the *baraita* as resembling *tala ilan*, a multi-colored creature. The *Gemara* clarifies: the *tachash* was not literally a *tala ilan* — that is a non-kosher animal — but rather resembled it in its multi-colored appearance, while itself potentially being kosher. Rav Yosef notes that once one understands the *tachash* as multi-colored, the standard Aramaic translation — *sasgona* — makes sense: *she-sas bi-gevanim harbeh*, it rejoices in its many colors. Two identifications have been proposed for the *tachash*: a narwhal, whose skin reportedly displays colors when removed from water (perhaps washed ashore after the splitting of the *Yam Suf*), and the giraffe, an animal that is notably multi-colored and in fact kosher.

## Sechach discussion……

### Nega'im standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה דְּמִטַּמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל הַמֵּת מֵהָכָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״עוֹר״ ״אוֹ בְּעוֹר״ — רִיבָּה עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה. וְשֶׁלָּקָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, קָצַץ מִכּוּלָּן וְעָשָׂה אַחַת מֵהֶן, מִנַּיִן? — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ בְּכׇל מְלֶאכֶת עוֹר״. וְאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ, מָה לִנְגָעִים שֶׁכֵּן שְׁתִי וָעֵרֶב טָמֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said that the proof that the hide of a non-kosher animal becomes ritually impure in a tent over a corpse is derived from here, as it was taught in a *baraita* that it is stated in the *halakhot* of ritual impurity of leprosy that the leprosy could be: "Either in the warp, or in the woof, whether they be of linen, or of wool; or in a hide, or in any thing made of hide" (Leviticus 13:48). The verse could have simply stated: Or hide, and it said instead: Or in a hide. The Sages said: These words, or in a hide, amplify to include the hide of a non-kosher animal as well as hide that was afflicted in the hands of a priest, i.e., before the owner showed it to the priest there was no leprosy but it became leprous while in the hands of the priest, that they too become ritually impure. If one cut pieces from each of these types and made of them a single cloth, from where is it derived that it can become ritually impure? The verse states from the broader amplification: Or in anything made of hide. The Gemara remarks: There is room to refute this parallel, rendering it impossible to derive the laws of ritual impurity imparted by a corpse from the laws of leprosy. What is the comparison to leprosy with regard to which the Torah is stringent, as even the warp and woof that have not been woven into a garment can become ritually impure from it, which is not the case in impurity imparted by a corpse?

The *Gemara* resolves the *Sumchus* discussion by ruling in accordance with the position that allows warp and woof to become *tamei* through *nega'im*, consistent with Rabbi Meir's ruling in the *Mishnah* that warp and woof become *tamei* immediately upon spinning. This supports both Sumchus — whose ruling that spun thread renders *sechach* invalid rests on this principle — and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who similarly prohibits unprocessed flax as *sechach*. Rabbi Yehuda's more graduated approach, which delays susceptibility until various stages of processing are complete, is not the ruling we follow.

## Non-kosher leather…

### Kal V'Chomer logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא מִבַּרְנִישׁ: קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִנּוֹצָה שֶׁל עִזִּים. נוֹצַת עִזִּים שֶׁאֵין מִטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים מִטַּמְּאָה בְּאֹהֶל הַמֵּת, עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁמִּטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁיִּטַּמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל הַמֵּת?!
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rava from Barnish said it can be derived in the following manner: Through an *a fortiori* inference from goats' hair. The Mishkan's second-level covering was made of goats' hair. Goats' hair is not listed among the materials susceptible to *tzara'at tum'ah*, and yet it is it does cause *tum'at ohalim*, by virtue of its inclusion in the *Mishkan*. Now, the leather of non-kosher animals is listed among materials susceptible to *tzara'at* — and is therefore at least as significant a material as goats' hair. The *kal vachomer* follows: if goats' hair, which is not susceptible to *tzara'at*, nonetheless causes *tum'at ohalim*, then certainly the leather of non-kosher animals, which is susceptible to *tzara'at*, causes *tum'at ohalim* as well.

Returning now to Rabbi Elazar's original question about non-kosher leather: Rava of Barnish presents a *kal vachomer* argument that bypasses the uncertainty about the *tachash* entirely. The *Mishkan*'s second-level covering was made of goats' hair — *notzah shel izim*. Goats' hair is notably *she-ein metamei be-nega'im* — it is not listed among the materials susceptible to *tzara'at tum'ah* — and yet it is *metamei be-ohel ha-met*, it does cause *tum'at ohalim*, by virtue of its inclusion in the *Mishkan*. Now, the leather of non-kosher animals is listed among materials susceptible to *tzara'at* — *or behemah teme'ah metamei be-nega'im* — and is therefore at least as significant a material as goats' hair. The *kal vachomer* follows: if goats' hair, which is not susceptible to *tzara'at*, nonetheless causes *tum'at ohalim*, then certainly the leather of non-kosher animals, which is susceptible to *tzara'at*, causes *tum'at ohalim* as well.

The *Gemara* had earlier attempted to derive the same conclusion from a *hatzad ha-shaveh* between *nega'im* and *shratzim* — both teach that leather of non-kosher animals becomes *tamei*, and one could extend that principle to *tum'at ohalim*. But Rava of Barnish noted that this *hatzad ha-shaveh* is subject to refutation: both *nega'im* and *shratzim* involve *tum'ah* at sizes smaller than a *kezayit*, whereas *tum'at met* requires a *kezayit*, so the materials may not be analogous. The *kal vachomer* from goats' hair avoids this problem and provides a cleaner derivation.

### Tefillin practical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מַהוּ שֶׁיִּטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֹהָלִין. מַאי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: תַּחַשׁ שֶׁהָיָה בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ — טָמֵא הָיָה אוֹ טָהוֹר הָיָה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִמְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם אֶלָּא עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בִּלְבַד! לְתֵפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar's dilemma was mentioned above, and now the Gemara discusses the matter itself. Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma: With regard to the hide of a non-kosher animal over a corpse, what is the ruling? Can it become ritually impure as a tent over a corpse? The Gemara clarifies: What is the essence of his dilemma? Rav Adda bar Ahava said: The *taḥash* that existed in the time of Moses is at the crux of Rabbi Elazar's dilemma. Was it non-kosher or was it kosher? Rav Yosef said: What is his dilemma? Didn't we learn explicitly: Only the hide of a kosher animal was deemed suitable for heavenly service? Certainly, the *taḥash* was a kosher species. The practical application of the rule that only kosher-animal leather is used for sacred purposes is the law of *tefillin*. The *batim* and *retzuot* of *tefillin* must be made from the leather of kosher animals.

The *Gemara* concludes by establishing that non-kosher leather does indeed become *tamei* through *tum'at ohalim*. This raises a final question about the *baraita* cited by Rav Yosef — *lo huchsheru li-mlechet shamayim ella or behemah tehorah bilvad* — that only kosher-animal leather was used for sacred purposes. If we have now established that non-kosher leather is susceptible to *tum'at ohalim* just like kosher leather, what does this *baraita* practically mean? Whatever we may speculate about the *tachash* historically, the ruling about *tum'at ohalim* is settled. The *Gemara* answers: *le-tefillin* — the practical application of the rule that only kosher-animal leather is used for sacred purposes is the law of *tefillin*. The *batim* and *retzuot* of *tefillin* must be made from the leather of kosher animals. This practical application, rather than any historical question about the *Mishkan* coverings, is what the *baraita* is coming to teach us.
