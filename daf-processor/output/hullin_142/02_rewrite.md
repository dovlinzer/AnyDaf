# Hullin 142 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiluach Ha-Em Definition

### Distance of Shiluach

The daf opens — formally 141b, the final pages of *Masechet Chullin* — with the question *ad kama meshalecha*: how far must the mother bird be sent away? This is distinct from the *mishnah*'s discussion of how many times one must send her; the question here is what constitutes a valid act of sending in the first place. Rav Yehuda rules: *kedei shetitzei mitachat yado* — it suffices that the bird leaves one's hand. The practical implication extends beyond the mere fulfillment of the *mitzvah*. As soon as one has discharged the obligation of *shiluach*, the mother bird is no longer *al ha-banim*, and one may then immediately recapture her. The tension between the technical *halacha* and its underlying values — a tension that will recur throughout this *perek* — surfaces here at the outset.

One might ask about the case where the mother returns after having been sent away. The answer depends on whether possession of the chicks has already been acquired. Once *shiluach* has been performed and the chicks taken, even if the mother returns she is no longer in the context of the *pasuk*; the prohibition of *lo tikach ha-em al ha-banim* no longer applies. If *shiluach* was performed but the chicks have not yet been taken, and the mother returns, the *mishnah* indicates one would need to send her away again. But once *shiluach* is complete and she has not yet returned, one may immediately seize her — which, as the *Gemara* will illustrate, creates obvious opportunities to subvert the spirit of the commandment while satisfying its letter.

### Walking vs Flying

The *Gemara* proceeds to ask *bemei meshalecha* — by what means is the bird considered to have been sent away? Two Amoraim dispute this. Rav Huna rules *beragleha* — by her legs — and Rav Yehuda rules *beagapicha* — by her wings.

Rashi's preferred interpretation of this dispute is not that it concerns how one holds the bird before releasing her, but rather what mode of departure is required. If the bird's wings are broken and she can only walk, does that count as *shiluach*? Rav Huna says yes, deriving from the verse *meshalechai regel ha-shor veha-chamor* — "those who send forth the foot of the ox and the donkey" — where *shaleach* refers to animals going on foot. Rav Yehuda disagrees: *deha regel deha knafeha ninu* — for this creature, the "leg" is her wings. For a bird, meaningful departure means flight; walking does not constitute the requisite *shiluach*.

### Clipped Wings Case

The *Gemara* now presents a case that tests these principles in a pointed way. A certain individual *gazinehu lgapah veshilcha* — sheared the bird's wings and then released her. The verb *gazinachu* is cognate with *gez reishit ha-gez*, the shearing discussed earlier in the tractate; the wings were clipped, not physically broken off. The motive is transparent: by clipping the wings, the person could release the bird — technically performing *shiluach* — while ensuring she could not fly away, allowing him to immediately recapture her. *Ve-acharach kach tafsa* — and afterward he seized her.

Rav Yehuda, who requires actual flight for valid *shiluach*, lashed him. The *Gemara* asks an apparent contradiction: if the ruling follows the *rabbanan* of the *mishnah*, who hold that performing *shiluach* after transgression retroactively fixes the violation, then he should send the bird away and be exempt from lashes; while if the ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda of the *mishnah*, who holds that transgression cannot be remedied by subsequent *shiluach*, then lashes are appropriate but sending her away again is pointless. The *Gemara* resolves this: *le'olam k'rabbanan* — the ruling does follow the *rabbanan*. This person is indeed exempt from biblical lashes precisely because the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* can still be fulfilled. However, Rav Yehuda administered *makkat mardut mi-derabbanan* — rabbinic disciplinary lashes — because the man's conduct was openly subversive. These are lashes administered at rabbinic discretion, independent of the formal biblical criteria, as a measure of social and religious control.

Rav Yehuda accordingly told him: *zil rabei lei gadfei* — go wait for the wings to grow back, and then properly send her away. Only then will the *mitzvah* have been genuinely performed.

## Mitzvah Status

### Rabbinic Lashes

The structure of the *Gemara*'s resolution — that the *rabbanan* hold *shiluach* can retroactively fix the transgression, yet rabbinic lashes are nonetheless appropriate — reflects a broader principle: even where a person is technically exempt from biblical consequences, conspicuously evasive conduct invites rabbinic sanction. The lashes here are not for transgressing *lo tikach ha-em al ha-banim* per se, but for behaving in a manner that undermines the integrity of the commandment.

### Mitzvah Timing

This brings us to a deeper question that the *Gemara*'s framing invites. The *rabbanan* describe the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* as operative after transgression — it is the mechanism by which the violation is remedied. But does this mean the *mitzvah* applies *only* after transgression, or does it also apply *le-chatchila*, from the outset, when one wishes to take the chicks without having transgressed?

Rashi's comment *ad kama meshalecha* reads: *im netalah* — "if one has taken it." This phrasing suggests that the *mitzvah* of sending away is triggered by the act of taking the mother. Similarly, the Rambam, in his introduction to *Hilchot Shechita* where he enumerates the *mitzvot* to be covered, writes *le-shaleach es ha-em im netalah al ha-banim* — to send away the mother if one has taken her with the young. Both formulations seem to frame *shiluach* as responsive to transgression. Yet as one reads further in the Rambam, and in the *Sefer Ha-Chinuch* and other *rishonim*, the *mitzvah* is described as applying even from the outset — that when one approaches the nest intending to take the chicks, there is an affirmative obligation to send away the mother.

The tension is genuine. Rabbi Yehuda of the *mishnah* holds that *shiluach* is only meaningful *before* transgression — once you have taken the mother together with the young, sending her away accomplishes nothing. The *rabbanan*, who hold that *shiluach* after transgression does remedy the violation, naturally emphasize the *mitzvah*'s role as a corrective. The question is whether, according to the *rabbanan*, there is also an independent positive *mitzvah* of *shalach tishalach* when one simply wishes to take the chicks in an orderly way — or whether one could, in theory, take only the chicks while ignoring the mother entirely, having violated no prohibition, with the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* irrelevant because no transgression occurred. Intuitively, one assumes there is always an affirmative *mitzvah* to send the mother away when taking the chicks. But the *rabbanan*'s emphasis on *shiluach* as remedying transgression introduces a subtle ambiguity, and the *rishonim*'s formulations reflect that ambiguity. As the *Gemara* will soon make clear, this question has practical implications for ownership of the eggs — and the discussion there will help resolve it.

## Single Egg/Chick

### Single Egg Question

A person came before Rava with a question about a *teima* bird — some species of kosher bird. Rava's initial reaction was dismissive: does this person not know that kosher birds are subject to *shiluach*? But sensing there was something more specific behind the question, Rava probed further. *Dilma chada beita hahu de-rami* — perhaps the case involved only a single egg. The inquirer confirmed: yes, that was precisely the question — does the obligation of *shiluach* apply when there is only one egg or one chick, given that the verse speaks in the plural?

### Rava's Ruling

Rava responded that the answer is explicit in a *mishnah*: *afilu ein sham ela efroach echad o beitza achat, chayav le-shaleach* — even if there is only a single chick or a single egg, one is obligated to send away the mother. Rava directed the man to perform *shiluach*, and once the man sent the bird away, Rava laid a net — *parshatika* — and captured the bird for himself.

### Suspicion Concern

The *Gemara* immediately raises the question of *chashada*: does Rava's conduct not look improper? He issued the ruling, and then personally benefited from its implementation. Even if one's *pesak* is entirely correct and not influenced by personal interest, the appearance of a conflict of interest is itself a problem. The *Gemara* answers *ke-le-achar yad* — Rava acted indirectly. Rashi explains that Rava set the net and then was not personally present when the bird was caught; the capture happened without his direct involvement at the decisive moment.

This episode opens a broader reflection. Technically, a *posek* is permitted to rule on matters in which he has a personal stake — unlike a *dayan* in monetary litigation, who is disqualified when he has an interest in the outcome. But the appearance of self-interest remains a concern. The *Gemara* here is not questioning the legitimacy of Rava's *psak* — it was entirely straightforward — but is addressing the optics of benefiting visibly from one's own ruling.

## Wild Doves

### Doves Control Status

A *beraita* is cited: *yonei shovach ve-yonei aliyah* — doves of the dovecote and doves of the attic — are subject to *shiluach* and are also prohibited to take because of *gezel*. These birds, while they have taken up residence in someone's premises, are not truly under the owner's control; they can fly away at any moment and are therefore not considered *nishmar betoch chatzer* — contained and protected within one's domain. Consequently, one does not technically own them.

This raises an immediate question. When someone walks into another person's dovecote and takes these doves, it is not technically *gezel* in the biblical sense, since the owner has no legal title. Nevertheless, because the owner regards these birds as his own and expects others to respect that, taking them violates *darchei shalom* — the rabbinic principle of preserving peaceful social relations, which treats such taking as if it were theft.

### Egg Emergence

The *Gemara* presses further. There is a general principle, stated by Rav Yosei bar Chanina, that *chatzeiro shel adam koneh lo shelo mida'ato* — a person's field or yard can acquire ownerless property on his behalf, even without his active awareness. If so, shouldn't the owner of the dovecote genuinely own both the birds themselves and certainly the eggs they lay in his attic? And if he genuinely owns the eggs, there would be no *mitzvah* of *shiluach*, since *ki yikarei* — "if it chances upon you" — excludes cases where the nest is already under one's ownership and control.

The *Gemara* addresses this by focusing on the moment of the egg's emergence. Rav rules: *beitzah im yetziat rubah hu de-chayav be-shiluach* — the obligation of *shiluach* attaches from the moment the majority of the egg has emerged from the mother. At that point, however, *mikna lo kani ad de-tippol le-chatzeiro* — the owner does not yet acquire the egg until it has fully dropped and landed within his domain. So there is a window: the egg is majority-emerged, its existence is already legally cognizable for purposes of *shiluach*, yet it has not landed and thus has not been acquired. In that precise moment, if the owner wishes to take the eggs, he has an obligation of *shiluach* — but because the egg has not yet landed, his yard has not acquired it on his behalf.

This explains why the owner genuinely lacks title to the eggs at the moment *shiluach* would apply: acquisition by one's *chatzer* has not yet occurred.

### Possession Prohibited

But the *Gemara* develops an even more fundamental answer. Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Rav: *assur li-zekot be-beitzim she-ha'em rovetzet aleihen* — it is prohibited to take possession of eggs while the mother bird is sitting upon them. The source is the verse *shalach tishalach es ha-em ve-es ha-banim tikach lach* — first send away the mother, then take the young. The Torah mandates a specific order: *shiluach* must precede acquisition.

This ruling speaks directly to the ambiguity raised earlier. According to the *rabbanan*, the *mitzvah* of *shalach tishalach* is not merely a corrective mechanism for one who has transgressed. It is an affirmative obligation that applies even *le-chatchila*, before any transgression has occurred, and it defines the only permissible sequence: send away the mother first, then acquire the eggs. One cannot take possession of the eggs — not even passively through one's *chatzer* — while the mother is present.

The principle is then stated explicitly: *kol heikha de-ihu matzi zachah, chatzeiro nami zachyah; ve-chol heikha de-ihu lo matzi zachah, chatzeiro nami lo zachyah lei* — wherever a person would himself be entitled to acquire, his field can acquire on his behalf; wherever he would not be entitled to acquire, his field equally cannot acquire on his behalf. Since he is prohibited from taking possession of the eggs while the mother is hovering, his *chatzer* cannot acquire them either, regardless of whether the eggs have fully landed. This is why he does not own the eggs despite their presence in his domain.

This also resolves the *darchei shalom* question entirely. Once the eggs have been laid and are sitting in his attic, the owner subjectively feels that he owns them and that others should not take them. That psychological expectation, even absent legal title, is sufficient to generate the *darchei shalom* dimension of *gezel*. The more elaborate answer involving the egg that is only majority-emerged turns out to be one possible construction, but the *ikkar* is the simpler principle: the prohibition on acquisition while the mother hovers prevents ownership, and that simultaneously creates the conditions for both a *mitzvah* of *shiluach* and a *darchei shalom* concern.

The *Gemara* then considers a further wrinkle: what about a *katan*, a minor, who owns the attic? A minor has no obligation of *shiluach*, so the prohibition on acquisition does not apply to him. Yet a *katan* also cannot effect full legal acquisition. And there would not normally be a *darchei shalom* concern vis-à-vis a minor's claimed property. The *Gemara* resolves this by reframing: the *darchei shalom* concern is not between the world and the *katan*, but between the *katan* and his own father — *aviv shel katan chayav le-hachzir lo mipnei darchei shalom*. If the father takes the eggs from his minor son, he must return them, because the relationship of father and child itself generates the *darchei shalom* obligation. It is admittedly a highly constructed case, and one might have preferred the simpler explanation that the owner's strong subjective sense of entitlement — even knowing the *mitzvah* prevents formal acquisition — is itself sufficient to generate *darchei shalom*. The *Gemara*, however, declines that route, pressing instead to a more technically precise resolution.

## Ownership Transfer

### Dovecote Transfer

The discussion turns to a practical mechanism for circumventing the impediment to acquisition. Levi bar Shimon transferred the *peira de-shovcha* — the "fruit of the dovecote," meaning the eggs and chicks — to Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda then came before Shmuel to ask how to actually take possession of these chicks without having to deal personally with the *mitzvah* of *shiluach ha-ken*.

### Bang Dovecote

Shmuel's advice was: *zil trof akan, d'ligvahu* — go bang on the dovecote, so that the birds will be lifted up. The act of the chicks rising slightly off the nest constitutes *hagbahah* — one of the classic modes of *kinyan* — on behalf of the owner. By causing the chicks to lift even an inch or two, the owner effectively performs *hagbahah* on all of them simultaneously, and thereby acquires them all at once.

### Hagbaha Method

One might ask: why use *hagbahah* at all? If the goal is simply to acquire ownership, why not use *kinyan sudar* — the handkerchief exchange — which requires no physical interaction with the objects at all? The *Gemara* explains that the mechanism matters because of the underlying problem. Levi bar Shimon himself had never acquired these chicks in the first place. As established above, his *chatzer* was prevented from acquiring on his behalf so long as the mothers were hovering. The chicks, and indeed the doves themselves, were never legally his.

The solution therefore had to address the root impediment: getting the mother birds to lift off, even for a moment. Once the mothers rise — even by an inch or two in response to the banging — they are no longer hovering over the nest. At that instant, the prohibition lapses, and Levi bar Shimon's yard automatically acquires the chicks on his behalf. Now that he actually owns them, he can transfer ownership to Rabbi Yehuda — and that transfer can indeed be accomplished with a *kinyan sudar*. The *hagbahah* was not itself the instrument of transfer to Rabbi Yehuda; it was the mechanism by which Levi bar Shimon first acquired the chicks so that he had something to transfer.

The conclusion is somewhat unsettling in its tidiness: by simply banging on the dovecote, causing the mothers to flutter upward for a moment, one sidesteps entirely the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* as it would otherwise apply. The *perek* has accumulated a number of such technical workarounds, and this is among the most efficient of them.

## Final Prohibition

### Tzaraat Exception

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* states: *lo yitol adam ima al banim afilu le-taher et ha-metzora* — one may not take the mother bird with the chicks even for the purpose of purifying a person afflicted with *tzaraat*. The purification ritual for a *metzora* requires two birds, and one might have thought that the urgency or sacred character of that *mitzvah* would override the prohibition. It does not. Of course, as the *Gemara* has demonstrated at length, there are ample technical means to arrange matters so that the mother is no longer hovering and the prohibition does not apply — but if such workarounds are not available, the *mitzvah* of *taharas metzora* does not override *lo tikach ha-em al ha-banim*.

## Reward Theology

### Long Days Promise

Having concluded the legal content of the *perek*, the *mishnah* closes with an ethical and theological observation. *Umah mitzvah kallah she-hi ke-issar* — what a small *mitzvah* this is, costing no more than the equivalent of a dollar; and yet the Torah concludes it with *lema'an yitav lach ve-ha'arachta yamim* — that it will be good for you and you will have long days. The inference is *kal va-chomer*: if so modest a commandment as *shiluach ha-ken* promises such reward, how much more so the weightier *mitzvot* of the Torah, such as *kibbud av va-em*, which demands genuine sacrifice and effort. And indeed *kibbud av va-em* carries precisely the same promise: *lema'an ya'arichun yamecha u-lema'an yitav lach*.

### Tower Case

A *beraita* is then introduced in the name of Rabbi Yaakov: *ein lecha kol mitzvah u-mitzvah she-ba-Torah she-mattan sechara be-tzidah she-ein techiyat ha-metim teluyah bah* — there is no *mitzvah* in the Torah whose reward is stated alongside it that does not have *techiyat ha-metim* as its ultimate referent. The premise behind this statement is the well-known problem of theodicy: the Torah consistently promises this-worldly rewards for the *mitzvot*, yet observation plainly reveals that those rewards do not consistently materialize, certainly not in the dark period following the Hadrianic persecutions. The answer proposed is that all such promises must be understood as referring to the world to come.

Rabbi Yaakov does not speak abstractly. He grounds his ruling in an event he personally witnessed. *Harei she-amar lo aviv: aleh le-bira, ve-havi li gozalot* — a father told his son to climb a tower and bring him chicks. The boy performed two *mitzvot* simultaneously: ascending at his father's command was an act of *kibbud av va-em*, and when he reached the nest, he sent away the mother and took the chicks — *shiluach ha-ken*. Both *mitzvot* carry the explicit promise of long days. Yet on his way back down, *nafal va-met* — he fell and died. *Heikhan arichut yamav shel zeh? Heikhan tovato shel zeh?* Where is his long life? Where is it being good for him?

### Olam Ha-Ba Reward

The resolution is categorical. *Lema'an ya'arichun yamecha* must mean *ba-olam she-kulo aroch* — in the world that is entirely long, the world to come. *Lema'an yitav lach* must mean *ba-olam she-kulo tov* — in the world that is entirely good. These promises simply cannot be interpreted as guarantees of this-worldly longevity, because reality contradicts that reading. They must point to *olam ha-ba*.

The *Gemara* asks: how does Rabbi Yaakov even know to pose this question? Perhaps such a case never actually occurs — perhaps everyone who performs *shiluach ha-ken* and *kibbud av va-em* does in fact live a long life. The answer: *Rabbi Yaakov ma'aseh chaza* — Rabbi Yaakov saw exactly such a case. His ruling was not theoretical; it was forged from direct observation.

The *Gemara* then probes further. Perhaps the boy's death was due to his harboring sinful thoughts. *Machshava ra'ah ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu mitzarfah le-ma'aseh* — God does not count sinful thoughts as if they were sinful deeds, so this does not explain the punishment. What about thoughts of *avoda zara*? For idolatrous thoughts God does hold one accountable, as the verse states *lema'an tafos et beit Yisrael be-libam* — to seize the house of Israel in their hearts — which Rav Acha bar Yaakov applies to thoughts of *avoda zara*. But the *Gemara* sets that aside as well, articulating the deeper point: *im ita de-ika schar mitzvah be-hai alma* — if there truly were this-worldly reward for *mitzvot*, then performing these two *mitzvot* should have *tehanei lei ve-tagnei lei* — it should have benefited him and protected him even against that. The very fact that it did not demonstrates that *schar mitzvah be-hai alma leka* — there is simply no this-worldly translation of *mitzvah* reward.

### Mitzvah Protection

The *Gemara* raises a further difficulty. Even granting that *mitzvot* do not generate reward in this world, Rabbi Elazar teaches *shluchei mitzvah einan nizokin* — those who go to perform a *mitzvah* are not harmed. This is not reward for the *mitzvah*; it is a separate principle of protective providence that attaches to the act of performing a commandment. So why was the boy not simply protected while engaged in these *mitzvot*?

The *Gemara* first suggests *be-chazaratam shani* — on the return journey, after completing the act, one is no longer engaged in the *mitzvah* and thus no longer protected. But this is immediately countered: Rabbi Elazar explicitly teaches that the protection extends both *be-halichatan ve-lo be-chazaratam* — both on the way there and on the way back. The *Gemara* then resolves with *sulam ra'ua hava* — it was a shaky, unstable ladder. *U-makom de-kavia heizika shani* — where there is a fixed, established danger, the protective principle does not apply. When the hazard is genuinely grave and present, the protection afforded by *sheluchei mitzvah* does not override it. The proof is from Shmuel's own hesitation: *eich elech ve-shama Shaul ve-haragani* — Shmuel himself said, "How can I go? Shaul will hear and kill me." Even though Shmuel was performing a *mitzvah*, he feared a tangible and present danger — demonstrating that *shchiach heizika*, prevalent danger, falls outside the protective scope of *shliach mitzvah*.

### Elisha Loss Faith

Having established that the verse's promise of long days must refer exclusively to *olam ha-ba*, the *Gemara* turns to *Elisha ben Avuyah* — known in Talmudic literature simply as *Acher*, "the Other." Rav Yosef states: *ilmale darsha Acher le-hai kra ke-Rabbi Yaakov bar Bartei* — had Acher interpreted this verse as Rabbi Yaakov did, understanding the promise of long days as pointing to the world to come rather than to this world, *lo chata* — he would not have sinned, would not have left the path and become a heretic.

What did he see that broke his faith? One tradition holds that he witnessed precisely the case described above — a child performing both *shiluach ha-ken* and *kibbud av va-em* and dying — and could not reconcile it with the Torah's explicit promises. Another tradition, more viscerally powerful and historically situated, holds that he saw the tongue of Rabbi Chutzpit Ha-Meturgeman — one of the *Asarah Harugei Malchut*, the Ten Martyrs executed during the Hadrianic persecution — cast in a dunghill. *Peh she-hepik margaliyot yilachech afar?* — Can the mouth from which pearls issued now lick the dust? This was not an abstract philosophical challenge but a direct confrontation with the suffering of the greatest Torah scholars. He concluded that the Torah's promises were false, and he abandoned his faith entirely.

*Ve-hu lo yada* — he did not know to interpret *lema'an yitav lach be-olam she-kulo tov, u-lema'an ya'arichun yamecha be-olam she-kulo aroch*. Whether he was unaware of this interpretation or refused to accept it in the face of what he had seen, the *Gemara* does not fully resolve. The broader cultural context — the pull of Hellenistic philosophy and its this-worldly orientation — may have made it impossible for him to accept a theological resolution grounded in an unseen future world.

The *Gemara* frames *Acher*'s break not primarily as a product of philosophical speculation or mystical overreach — though the tradition of *arba'ah nichnesu le-pardes* points in that direction — but as a response to witnessed suffering and the apparent failure of divine promise. The answer that the *rabbanan* insisted upon, and that Rabbi Yaakov articulated from his own experience, is that the Torah's rewards are real — but they belong to *olam she-kulo aroch* and *olam she-kulo tov*, the world that is entirely long, the world that is entirely good.

With this the *perek* concludes — *lema'an yitav lach be-olam she-kulo tov, u-lema'an ya'arichun yamecha be-olam she-kulo aroch* — and with it, *Masechet Chullin* reaches its end.
