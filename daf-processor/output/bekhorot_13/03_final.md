# Bekhorot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemita Produce

### Shemita Animal Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִפְדּוֹת בְּבֶהֱמַת שְׁבִיעִית? וַדַּאי — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, לַאֲכִילָה אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא לִסְחוֹרָה. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* **with regard to redeeming** a firstborn donkey **with an animal** that was purchased with produce **of the Sabbatical** Year, which caused the sanctity of the produce to be transferred to the animal? The Gemara clarifies the question: With regard to redeeming a donkey that has **definite** firstborn status with an animal that was purchased with produce of the Sabbatical Year, **you should not raise the dilemma,** as one certainly may not redeem it in this manner, since the owner acquires his firstborn donkey in exchange for the animal that he gives to the priest, and this is prohibited with regard to such an animal. This is because **the Merciful One states** with regard to the Sabbatical Year: "The rest of the land shall be for you for consumption" (Leviticus 25:6), indicating that the produce may be used **for consumption but not for commerce.** Rather, **when should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it in the case of a donkey with **uncertain** firstborn status.

The daf opens on 12b with a series of questions about what may be used to redeem a firstborn donkey. The *Gemara* raises the question: *ma'u lifdot b'behema shevi'it* — may one use a sheep that was purchased with *shemita* money? The *Gemara* immediately resolves one dimension of the question: *vaday lo tiba'ya lach* — if the donkey is definitely obligated, one certainly may not use a *shemita* sheep for its redemption. The reason is that giving the sheep to the *kohen* has the appearance of a commercial transaction, as though one is purchasing the donkey with *shemita* produce — and using *shemita* produce in commerce is forbidden.

### Safek vs. Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ סָפֵק, כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. מַאי כֵּיוָן דְּמַפְרֵישׁ טָלֶה וְהוּא לְעַצְמוֹ, ״לְאׇכְלָה״ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּכַמָּה דְּלָא מַפְקַע אִיסּוּרֵיהּ לָא מִישְׁתְּרֵי, כִּסְחוֹרָה דָּמֵי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בֶּהֱמַת שְׁבִיעִית אֵין פּוֹדִין בָּהּ אֶת הַוַּדַּאי, אֲבָל פּוֹדִין בָּהּ אֶת הַסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies further: **And according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon you should not raise the dilemma, as he does not hold** that redemption is necessary in the case of a donkey with **uncertain** firstborn status. **When should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that in such a case the owner of the donkey must designate a lamb for its redemption in order to abrogate its forbidden status, but he is not required to give it to a priest. **What** is the *halakha*? Perhaps **since he designates a lamb and it is his, we call** this act of redemption **"for consumption,"** and it is not considered commerce if he does not give it to the priest in exchange for his donkey. **Or perhaps, since as long as** the lamb **does not abrogate the forbidden status of** the firstborn donkey **it is not permitted** to derive benefit from the donkey, when the owner designates the lamb as the replacement of the donkey **it is tantamount to** engaging in **commerce** with it. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that **which Rav Ḥisda says: One may not redeem** a donkey with **definite** firstborn status **with an animal** that was purchased with produce **of the Sabbatical** Year, **but one may redeem** a donkey with **uncertain** firstborn status **with it.**

The harder question is where the redemption is doubtful — that is, where one performs a *safek* redemption and retains the sheep oneself rather than transferring it to a *kohen*. The *Gemara* notes that according to *Rabbi Shimon*, this question is moot entirely: *Rabbi Shimon* holds that a doubtful firstborn donkey carries no forbidden status, so there is no point in redeeming it in the first place. The question is therefore posed exclusively according to *Rabbi Yehuda*.

Two possibilities are weighed. On one hand, *keivan de'mafrish leh le'atzmo la'achila karinan bei* — since the owner designates the sheep for himself and will ultimately slaughter and eat it, the *shemita* sanctity is satisfied by its being eaten, and the incidental use of the sheep to remove the donkey's forbidden status is negligible. On the other hand, *keivan de'kama de'lo mafki issura lo mishtarei, k'sechora dami* — since without this act the donkey would remain forbidden, using the sheep to effect that removal resembles commerce, which is prohibited even where the sheep is never handed to a *kohen*.

### Bechora Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בֶּהֱמַת שְׁבִיעִית פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְחַיֶּיבֶת בַּמַּתָּנוֹת. פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה — ״לְאׇכְלָה״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא לִשְׂרֵיפָה, וְחַיֶּיבֶת בַּמַּתָּנוֹת — דִּ״לְאָכְלָה״ קָרֵינָא בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says** an additional ruling: **An animal** that was purchased with produce **of the Sabbatical** Year is **exempt from** its offspring being counted **a firstborn, but is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it. These are the gifts to which members of the priesthood are entitled when a non-sacred animal is slaughtered, It is **exempt from** its offspring being counted **a firstborn** because **the Merciful One states** with regard to produce of the Sabbatical Year that it should be used **"for consumption," but not for burning,** and portions of a kosher firstborn animal are burned on the altar. **And it is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it, **because we call** this case **"for consumption,"** as the priest eats them.

The resolution comes from *Rav Chisda*, who rules: *behema shevi'it ein podin bah vaday* — one may not use a *shemita* animal for definite redemption, since that entails giving it to the *kohen* in a manner resembling commerce. *Aval podin bah safek* — one may, however, use it for a doubtful redemption according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, since the sheep is kept by the owner, and as long as it is not given to the *kohen* it is not treated as a commercial transaction.

*Rav Chisda* continues with a related ruling: *behema shevi'it petura min habechora vechayevet bamatnot* — an animal purchased with *shemita* money is exempt from the *bechora* obligation but remains obligated in the priestly gifts (*zro'a lechayayim vekeiva*). The exemption from *bechora* requires explanation. The *mitzvah* of a firstborn kosher animal is to bring it as a *korban*, which entails burning the innards on the *mizbe'ach*. Yet the Torah requires that *shemita* produce be eaten — *le'achla amar rachmana velo lesreifa* — not burned. Since the *mitzvah* of *bechora* necessarily involves burning, it contradicts the *shemita* sanctity that demands the animal be fully eaten. Therefore, an animal whose *shemita* sanctity is operative cannot be treated as a *bechor* brought as a *korban*. The priestly gifts, by contrast, are given to the *kohen* to be eaten, and since *le'achla karinan bei* — the *kohen* will eat them — giving those gifts does not conflict with *shemita* and remains obligatory.

### Challah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָאוֹכֵל מֵעִיסַּת שְׁבִיעִית עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמָה חַלָּתָהּ — חַיָּיב מִיתָה. וְאַמַּאי? כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ מִטַּמְּיָא בַּת שְׂרֵיפָה הִיא, ״לְאׇכְלָה״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא לִשְׂרֵיפָה! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דִּכְתִיב ״לְדוֹרוֹתֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Shevi'it* 9:9): **One who eats from dough of** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year **before its *ḥalla*,** i.e., the portion of the dough given to the priests, **has been separated, is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven, just like one who consumes any dough before *ḥalla* is separated. **But** according to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda, **why** is dough of produce of the Sabbatical Year subject to the obligation of separating *ḥalla*? It should not be subject to this mitzva, **since if** the dough **becomes ritually impure** after being separated as *ḥalla*, **it is subject to burning,** and **the Merciful One states: "For consumption," but not for burning.** This is similar to Rav Ḥisda's claim that a firstborn animal that was purchased with produce of the Sabbatical Year is not subject to the obligations of firstborn status. The Gemara answers: **Here,** with regard to *ḥalla*, **it is different, as it is written:** "Of the first of your dough you shall give to the Lord a portion for a gift **throughout your generations"** (Numbers 15:21), which indicates that all dough, including that of produce of the Sabbatical Year, is subject to the mitzva of *ḥalla*.

The *Gemara* then challenges this logic. A *baraita* teaches: *ha'ochel me'isat shevi'it ad she'lo hurma challah chayav misa* — one who eats from *shemita* dough before *challah* has been separated incurs the penalty of death at the hands of Heaven. This implies that *shemita* dough is obligated in *challah*. But if *shemita* produce may not be burned, one might ask: since *challah* acquires the status of *terumah*, and if it becomes *tamei* it must be burned, why should *shemita* dough be obligated in *challah* at all? Imposing the *challah* obligation creates the possibility that one will eventually be compelled to burn something that *shemita* forbids from being burned.

The *Gemara* answers first with the verse *l'doroteichem* — the Torah states that *challah* applies "for your generations," indicating that it applies even during the *shemita* year. This textual answer is then followed by what the *Gemara* identifies as a *baraita* that also cites *l'doroteichem* and raises the question of why the same logic should not extend to a *bechor* — why not say that the *bechora* obligation too applies notwithstanding *shemita*, just as *challah* does?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מִנַּיִן לָאוֹכֵל מֵעִיסַּת שְׁבִיעִית עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמָה חַלָּתָהּ שֶׁחַיָּיב מִיתָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְדֹרֹתֵיכֶם״. וְלִגְמַר מִינַּהּ! הָתָם עִיקָּר לַאֲכִילָה, הָכָא עִיקָּר לִשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that one who consumes dough of** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year **before its *ḥalla* was separated is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "Throughout your generations,"** which indicates the inclusion of Sabbatical Years as well. By contrast, this formulation is not used with regard to a firstborn animal. Therefore, since portions of a kosher firstborn animal are burned on the altar, an animal that was purchased with produce of the Sabbatical Year is exempt from its offspring being counted a firstborn. The Gemara challenges: **And let** Rav Ḥisda **derive from** the *halakha* of *ḥalla* that such an animal is subject to its offspring being counted a firstborn, and that the interpretation of: "For consumption" but not for burning, is not applied. The Gemara explains: **There,** with regard to *ḥalla*, the **primary** purpose of the dough is **for consumption,** and it is burned only if it becomes impure. **Here,** with regard to a firstborn animal, the **primary** purpose of the portions that are consumed on the altar is **for burning.**

### Ikar vs. Tafel

The answer is categorical: *hatam ikar achilah, hacha ikar lesreifa* — in the case of dough, the primary outcome is eating: the *kohen* eats the *challah*, the owner eats the dough, and nothing need be burned unless something goes wrong. Since burning is only a secondary and contingent outcome, the *shemita* sanctity is not fundamentally undermined by the *challah* obligation. By contrast, the very essence of the *bechora mitzvah* is to bring the animal as a *korban* and burn its innards on the altar. The burning is not a secondary possibility but the *ikar* — the core act. Since the *mitzvah* of *bechor* is inherently incompatible with the *shemita* requirement that the animal be fully eaten, an animal with *shemita kedusha* cannot be subject to the *bechora* obligation at all.

## Giving to Kohen

### Kohen Suspicion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ לְכֹהֵן כּוּ׳. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ הַכֹּהֵן: ״תְּנֵהוּ לִי וַאֲנִי אֶפְדֵּהוּ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִתְּנוּ לוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן פְּדָאוֹ בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the owner **gave** the firstborn donkey itself **to a priest,** the priest may keep it only after he designates a lamb in its stead for redemption. This language indicates that there is concern lest the priest will be negligent in redeeming it. The Gemara comments: **We learn** in the mishna **that which the Sages taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: In the case of **an Israelite who had a firstborn donkey in his house and the priest said to him: Give it to me and I will redeem it, he may not give** the donkey **to** the priest **unless** the priest **redeems it in his presence.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to concluding the previous *mishna* through a *baraita*. The *mishna* stated that if one gives a firstborn donkey directly to a *kohen*, one must see the *kohen* redeem it before handing it over. The reason, as the *Gemara* makes clear, is that one cannot simply trust the *kohen* to perform the redemption. The *baraita* elaborates: *Yisrael she'hayah lo peter chamor b'toch beito, v'amar lo ha'kohen tenehu li, v'ani efdenu* — if a non-*kohen* has a firstborn donkey and the *kohen* says, give it to me and I will redeem it, *harei zeh lo yitnenu lo* — one should not hand it over. The concern is that the *kohen* will simply use the donkey without bothering to redeem it, since a donkey is forbidden in benefit even to a *kohen* who has not redeemed it. The *baraita* continues: *ela im ken padahu b'fanav* — one may only comply if the *kohen* performs the redemption visibly, in the presence of the owner.

### Kohanim Nechshedim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת נֶחְשְׁדוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים עַל פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּמַחְזַק לַן דְּמִיקְּרֵי, אֲבָל בִּסְתָמָא לָא — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמוֹרֵה בַּהּ הֶיתֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: That is to say** that **priests are suspect with regard to** using **firstborn donkeys** without redemption. The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious?** That is exactly what the *baraita* states. The Gemara explains: Rav Naḥman's statement is necessary **lest you say** that **this statement** of the *baraita* applies only in a case **where we have a presumption that it** occasionally **happens** that this priest uses a firstborn donkey without redeeming it first, **but in** the case of **an ordinary** priest, there is **no** concern for this. Therefore, Rav Naḥman **teaches us that** this concern is relevant to every priest, **as** a priest is suspected of **granting** himself **permission** to use the firstborn donkey without first redeeming it with a lamb, as in any case the lamb will remain his.

*Rav Nachman* derives a broader conclusion from this: *zot omeret nechshedu kohanim al pitrei chamorim* — this demonstrates that *kohanim* are suspect with respect to the laws of firstborn donkeys. The *Gemara* objects: *p'shita* — is this not self-evident? That is precisely why the *mishna* required oversight. The *Gemara* answers: one might have thought the rule applies only where there is an established pattern of misconduct — where we have concrete evidence that a particular *kohen* has previously misused a donkey. One might then have said that in the ordinary case, absent such a history, we trust the *kohen* and need not require the redemption to be performed in one's presence.

### Rationalization Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּמַחְזַק לַן דְּמִיקְּרֵי, אֲבָל בִּסְתָמָא לָא — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמוֹרֵה בַּהּ הֶיתֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** This passage's translation appears above in the previous section's blockquote, but the key point is: Rav Naḥman **teaches us that** this concern is relevant to every priest, **as** a priest is suspected of **granting** himself **permission** to use the firstborn donkey without first redeeming it with a lamb, as in any case the lamb will remain his.

The *Gemara* therefore teaches: *d'moreh b'heteira* — the *kohen* will rationalize his behavior. He will reason that the sheep is ultimately his in any case once the redemption is performed, and that therefore there is no practical difference between using the donkey now and waiting until after the formal redemption. Since *kohanim* are prone to this rationalization, one may never hand over the donkey without witnessing the act of redemption, even in the absence of any established pattern of misconduct.

### Donkey Ownership

This discussion raises a conceptual point that the *Gemara* does not make fully explicit, yet it is worth noting. The Torah does not entitle the *kohen* to the donkey itself. The *mitzvah* is either to give a sheep to the *kohen* or to break the donkey's neck. One might therefore have thought that even if one trusted the *kohen* completely and watched the redemption unfold, one would not fulfill the *mitzvah* by giving the donkey to the *kohen* in the first place — since the *mitzvah* requires giving a sheep, not a donkey. Yet the *Gemara*'s discussion assumes throughout that one does fulfill the *mitzvah* by this arrangement, as though the donkey is treated quasi-conceptually as belonging to the *kohen*. This assumption, as one of the *acharonim* articulated, is not in fact accurate. The proof is that if the donkey dies, the dead donkey cannot be given to the *kohen*, since it remains *assur b'hana'ah*. It is the sheep, not the donkey, to which the *kohen* is entitled. The assumption that handing over the donkey constitutes a valid *mitzvah* framework is therefore not entirely explicit in the sources, and remains something of an open question.

## Pidyon Completion

### R. Eliezer Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ פִּדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר וָמֵת, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיבִין בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, כַּחֲמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שֶׁל בֵּן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַיָּיבִין בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, כְּפִדְיוֹן מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who designates** a lamb for the **redemption of a firstborn donkey and** the lamb **dies, Rabbi Eliezer says:** The owner **bears** financial **responsibility** and must give the priest another lamb in its place. This is **like** the case of the **five *sela*** for redemption **of** a firstborn **son,** where if the money is lost before one gives it to the priest, he must give the priest another five *sela*. **And the Rabbis say:** The owner **does not bear** financial **responsibility.** This is **like** the case of money designated for **redemption of second-tithe** produce, where once the owner designates the money for redemption, the produce is desanctified.

The next *mishna* introduces a fundamental dispute about when the act of redemption of a firstborn donkey is considered complete. The *mishna* states: *ha'mafrish pidyon peter chamor u'met* — one designated a sheep for the redemption and then the sheep died. *Rabbi Eliezer* rules: *chayavin b'achrayutan* — the owner bears liability for the sheep, meaning the *mitzvah* of redemption is not complete until the sheep is physically handed to the *kohen*, just as by the redemption of a firstborn son, the *mitzvah* is not accomplished until the five *shekel* are handed over. The practical implication is that if the designated sheep dies before it reaches the *kohen*, the owner has not yet fulfilled the *mitzvah* and must provide another sheep.

### Chachamim Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי צָדוֹק עַל פִּדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר שֶׁמֵּת, שֶׁאֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם. מֵת פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יִקָּבֵר, וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָתוֹ שֶׁל טָלֶה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהִקָּבֵר, וְטָלֶה לַכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Tzadok testified about** a lamb designated for **redemption of a firstborn donkey that died, that the priest has nothing here,** i.e., in such a case, as the firstborn donkey has already been redeemed, and the owner no longer bears financial responsibility for the dead lamb, in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. If after the lamb was designated, **the firstborn donkey died, Rabbi Eliezer says:** The donkey **must be buried, and** the owner is **permitted to** derive **benefit from the lamb. And the Rabbis say:** It **does not need to be buried, and the lamb** is given **to the priest.**

The *Chachamim* disagree: *ein chayavin b'achrayutan, ke'fidyon ma'aser sheni* — one is not liable for the sheep, analogous to the redemption of *ma'aser sheni*. When one redeems produce of *ma'aser sheni* by transferring its sanctity onto money, and that money is subsequently lost, one is not required to use new money — the act of redemption was complete at the moment of designation. Here too, once the owner designates the sheep for the redemption of the donkey, the act of redemption is complete. The owner is then obligated to give the sheep to the *kohen*, but the status of the donkey has already been removed. If the sheep is thereafter lost or dies, it is the *kohen*'s loss, not the owner's.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* and *Rabbi Tzadok* give corroborating testimony: if the donkey died, the *kohen* receives nothing — the *mitzvah* has already been fulfilled through the designation of the sheep.

### Sheep Dies First

The halakhic upshot under the *Chachamim*'s position is that if the designated sheep dies before it is transferred to the *kohen*, the *kohen* receives nothing and the owner is not required to replace it — the redemption was completed upon designation. This is the ruling we follow.

*Tosafot* draws a practical consequence from this: since we rule like the *Chachamim* that the *mitzvah* of *pidyon peter chamor* is accomplished upon designation of the sheep, one recites the *berachah* at the moment of designation — before the sheep is handed to the *kohen*. This contrasts with *pidyon ha'ben*, where the *mitzvah* is complete only upon handing the money to the *kohen*, and the *berachah* is accordingly recited at that moment.

### Donkey Dies First

The positions are symmetrically reversed in the case where the donkey dies before the sheep has been transferred to the *kohen*. *Rabbi Eliezer* rules: *yikaver u'mutar b'hana'ah shel peled* — the donkey must be buried, but the owner may keep the sheep. His reasoning is consistent: since the redemption is not complete until the sheep is handed over, and the sheep has not yet been handed over, the donkey died without having been redeemed. A donkey that was never redeemed remains *assur b'hana'ah* and must be buried. But since the donkey died before the redemption was complete, the owner retains the sheep — there is no longer a *mitzvah* to redeem a dead donkey, so the owner need not hand it over.

The *Chachamim* rule: *ein tzarich le'ikaver* — the dead donkey need not be buried. Since the redemption was complete the moment the owner designated the sheep, the donkey's forbidden status was removed at that point, and even a dead donkey from which one might otherwise have been forced to derive no benefit is now permitted. The corollary is that since the redemption already occurred, the sheep must be given to the *kohen*.

### Textual Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? דִּכְתִיב: ״אַךְ פָּדֹה תִפְדֶּה וְגוֹ׳״, מָה בְּכוֹר אָדָם חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, אַף בְּכוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that the owner bears financial responsibility for the lamb if it is designated and dies before the priest receives it? **As it is written: "Yet you shall redeem** the firstborn of man, and the firstborn of non-kosher animals you shall redeem" (Numbers 18:15). It is derived from the comparison between the two cases that **just as** with regard to redeeming a woman's **firstborn son,** the father **bears** financial **responsibility** to compensate for the five *sela* in the event of their loss, **so too,** with regard to redeeming **a firstborn non-kosher animal,** the owner **bears** financial **responsibility** to compensate the priest in the event **of their** loss.

The *Gemara* inquires into *Rabbi Eliezer*'s reasoning: *mai ta'ama de'Rabbi Eliezer?* The verse states *ach padoh tifdeh* — "you shall surely redeem," and *Rabbi Eliezer* derives that just as redemption of a firstborn human is not accomplished until the money is handed to the *kohen*, so too redemption of a firstborn donkey is not accomplished until the sheep is handed over.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי מָה בְּכוֹר אָדָם מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף בְּכוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מוּתָּר! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתְנַן: מֵת פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יִקָּבֵר. מַאי יִקָּבֵר? לָאו דְּאָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If** they are compared, then **just as** deriving **benefit** from a woman's **firstborn son is permitted** if he has not yet been redeemed, **so too,** deriving benefit from **the firstborn of a non-kosher animal** should be **permitted** prior to redemption, including shearing and working it. **And if you would say** that **indeed** Rabbi Eliezer does permit deriving benefit from a firstborn donkey, that is difficult: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that if after the lamb was designated **the firstborn donkey died, Rabbi Eliezer says: It must be buried?** Now, **what** is the reason that **it must be buried?** Is it **not because** deriving **benefit** from it **is prohibited?**

*Abaye* presses a challenge: if we are drawing this comparison to a human *bechor*, we would expect the comparison to extend fully, including the fact that a firstborn human is not *assur b'hana'ah* before redemption — yet *Rabbi Eliezer* clearly agrees that a firstborn donkey is forbidden in benefit. The *Gemara* considers whether one could read *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling that the donkey must be buried as simply meaning that it is buried in the manner one buries a human who dies — not because of its *bechor* status, but by analogy to humans. The *Gemara* rejects this: burying humans is not limited to firstborn humans; a non-firstborn human is also buried. The analogy therefore cannot be the reason for burial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, יִקָּבֵר כִּבְכוֹר אָדָם. אֶלָּא, אָדָם בְּכוֹר הוּא דְּבָעֵי קְבוּרָה, פָּשׁוּט לָא בָּעֵי קְבוּרָה? וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ סְפֵק פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ — שֶׁמַּפְרִישׁ טָלֶה עָלָיו, וְהוּא שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef responded: **No, it must be buried like** a woman's **firstborn son.** There is a mitzva to bury a woman's firstborn son if he dies, and due to the comparison of the verse, the same applies to a firstborn donkey. The Gemara asks: **But** according to this answer, does **a person** who died **require burial** only if he **is a firstborn** son, while **an ordinary** person who is not a firstborn **does not require burial?** That is certainly not correct. Therefore, no such distinction should exist with regard to a donkey either. **And furthermore,** Rabbi Eliezer clearly holds that deriving benefit from a firstborn donkey prior to redemption is forbidden, as **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer concedes with regard to an Israelite who has a donkey in his house** whose status as **a firstborn** is **uncertain, that he designates a lamb for it and** the lamb **is his.** Designating a lamb in this case is necessary only to remove the prohibition against deriving benefit from the donkey.

A *baraita* further complicates the picture: *Rabbi Eliezer* concedes that for a *safek* firstborn donkey one designates a sheep and retains it, and the redemption is complete at that point without ever handing the sheep to the *kohen*. This shows that *Rabbi Eliezer* does not hold as an absolute principle that redemption requires physically handing over the sheep — only in cases where the sheep must ultimately be transferred does the *mitzvah* remain incomplete until transfer. When the owner is not obligated to transfer the sheep — because it is a doubtful case and *ha'motzi me'chavero alav ha'ra'aya* governs — the designation alone suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״אַךְ פָּדֹה תִפְדֶּה״, לִפְדִיָּיה הִקַּשְׁיתִּיו, וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says** that although the verse compares the status of a firstborn donkey to that of a woman's firstborn son, this is only with regard to bearing financial responsibility for the lamb, but not with regard to deriving benefit from the donkey. This is because **the verse states: "Yet you shall redeem [*pado tifde*]** the firstborn of man, and the firstborn of non-kosher animals you shall redeem." The double verb "*pado tifde*" indicates that **I have compared it with regard to redemption, but not with regard to another matter.**

*Rava* resolves the broader tension: *amar kra ach padoh tifdeh, le'fdiya hi hukash velo le'davar acher* — the verse compares firstborn donkeys and firstborn humans with respect to the act of redemption alone. They are comparable in that both require a formal act of redemption, not in every detail. Thus the donkey remains *assur b'hana'ah* while the human does not, and *Rabbi Eliezer* draws on the comparison only to establish that for the donkey, as for the human, the *mitzvah* is not complete until the redemptive object is handed over — when it must be handed over.

### Human Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הָעֲרָכִין — בִּשְׁעָתָן, וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן — אַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, פִּדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר — לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a *baraita* **elsewhere: Valuations** are determined based **on the time of** the vow. Therefore, if one vowed to consecrate the valuation of an infant who is less than one month old, he is not obligated to give any money even after the infant is one month old, as an infant less than one month old has no valuation. **And** with regard to **redeeming** a woman's firstborn **son,** one must give the required sum to the priest **after** the infant is **thirty days** old, as it is stated: "And their redemption money, from a month old shall you redeem them" (Numbers 18:16). With regard to **redeeming a firstborn donkey,** it must be performed **immediately.**

The *Gemara* now draws explicitly on the comparison to *pidyon ha'ben* in order to frame the broader question of when the *mitzvah* of *pidyon peter chamor* begins. We know that *pidyon ha'ben* begins at thirty days. The question is whether the same threshold applies to a firstborn donkey. A *mishna* elsewhere had stated that *erchin* are evaluated at the time of the vow, that *pidyon ha'ben* takes place after thirty days, and that *pidyon peter chamor* takes place *l'altar* — immediately. The *Gemara* will now explore whether this is indeed the case and what the underlying dispute is.

## When Obligation Starts

### Mishna Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפִדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר לְאַלְתַּר? וּרְמִינְהִי: אֵין בַּעֲרָכִין, וּבְפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן, וּבִנְזִירוּת, וּבְפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר — פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים. וּמוֹסִיפִין עַד עוֹלָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** must **redeeming a firstborn donkey** be performed **immediately? But** one can **raise a contradiction** from another *baraita*: The *halakhot* **of valuations, and of redemption of** a woman's firstborn **son, and of nazirite vows, and of a firstborn donkey, do not** apply to **less than thirty** days. In other words, the *halakhot* of valuations, of the redemption of a woman's firstborn son, and of a firstborn donkey do not apply to a person or animal that is less than thirty days old, and a term of naziriteship is a minimum of thirty days. **But one may add** to this amount **forever.** Meaning, the *halakhot* of valuations apply to a person of any age beyond thirty days, the redemption of a woman's firstborn son and of a firstborn donkey apply even if the firstborn in question is already several years old, and one can vow to be a nazirite for any length of time beyond thirty days. This *baraita* indicates that a firstborn donkey is not redeemed immediately, but only after thirty days.

The *mishna* states explicitly: *pidyon peter chamor l'altar* — the redemption of a firstborn donkey is performed immediately, with no thirty-day waiting period. The Torah provides no thirty-day threshold for donkeys; it simply obligates one to redeem. The *Gemara*, however, challenges this from a *baraita*: *ein b'erchin u'vidyon ha'ben u'vnezirut u'vpeter chamor pachot mishloshim yom* — the minimum period applicable to *erchin*, *pidyon ha'ben*, *nezirut*, and *peter chamor* is thirty days. This seems to indicate that, as with the others, the firstborn donkey obligation does not begin until the animal is thirty days old — directly contradicting the *mishna*'s ruling that it begins immediately.

The *baraita* also states *v'osifin alav* — these obligations extend indefinitely beyond thirty days. For *nezirut* one can undertake a *nezirut* of a lifetime; for a *bechor adam*, the *mitzvah* of *pidyon ha'ben* can be performed even if the son is already an adult. The thirty-day figure is a minimum threshold, not a cutoff. But by placing *peter chamor* in this category, the *baraita* appears to say that the donkey obligation too only begins at thirty days.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לוֹמַר שֶׁאִם פְּדָאוֹ — פָּדוּי, מִכְּלָל דִּבְנוֹ אִם פְּדָאוֹ אֵינוֹ פָּדוּי? וְהָאִיתְּמַר: הַפּוֹדֶה אֶת בְּנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַב אָמַר: בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי! לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רָבָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ — אֵין בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** When the first *baraita* states that a firstborn donkey must be redeemed immediately, it means **to say that if one redeemed it** immediately, **it is redeemed,** although it should be redeemed only after thirty days *ab initio*. The Gemara asks: **By inference,** in the case **of** a woman's firstborn **son,** where the first *baraita* states that he is redeemed after thirty days, **if one redeemed him** prior to that time, **is he not redeemed? But wasn't it stated:** In the case of **one who redeems his son within thirty days** of birth, **Rav says: His son is redeemed?** The Gemara responds: **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** matter that **Rava says: Everyone** agrees that if one says within thirty days of his son's birth that he is redeeming him **from now, his son is not redeemed.** Such a redemption would be effective only if he gave the money on condition that the redemption will take effect after his son turns thirty days old.

*Rav Nachman* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lomar she'im pedao padui* — the *mishna* saying the obligation applies immediately does not mean that one is obligated *lechatchila* before thirty days. Rather, it means that if one did perform the redemption before thirty days, *b'diavad* it is valid. The *baraita*'s statement of thirty days describes the normative timing; the *mishna*'s statement of "immediately" tells us that post-facto, early performance is effective.

The *Gemara* challenges: if one may redeem a donkey before thirty days post-facto, by analogy one should be able to redeem a firstborn son before thirty days post-facto as well. But did we not rule that *Rav* said *bno padui* — a son redeemed before thirty days is redeemed? *Rava* clarifies *Rav*'s ruling: *d'khulei alma me'akhshav ein bno padui* — everyone agrees that if one declares his son redeemed immediately, before thirty days, the redemption does not take effect. What *Rav* permitted is a more refined case: one takes out the five *shekel* now and declares that they should take effect as redemption when the son reaches thirty days. In that case, the act is performed now but the effectuation is deferred. This is not entirely obvious and would merit further discussion when the relevant material on *bechor adam* is studied. The upshot is: for a human firstborn, even *b'diavad* the redemption cannot take effect before thirty days. For a firstborn donkey, *b'diavad* it does take effect.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו. מֵתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מִצְוָתוֹ כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — אוֹ פּוֹדֵהוּ אוֹ עוֹרְפוֹ. מַאי לָאו מִצְוָה לְשַׁהוֹתוֹ כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם? לָא, מִצְוָה לִפְדּוֹתוֹ כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** The mitzva of redeeming a firstborn donkey takes effect immediately after it is born, as is stated in the first *baraita*. The second *baraita*, which states that it applies only after thirty days, means **to say that** until that time, **one does not transgress** a prohibition if he has not yet performed the redemption. After thirty days, though, one transgresses a prohibition if he still has not redeemed his firstborn donkey. **Rami bar Ḥama raises an objection** to Rav Sheshet from a *baraita*: **The mitzva of** a firstborn donkey applies **throughout** the period of **thirty days** from its birth; **from that** point **forward he must either redeem it** immediately **or break its neck. What,** does it **not** mean that there is **a mitzva to wait** to redeem **it** until **all thirty days** have passed? The Gemara answers: **No,** it means that there is **a mitzva to redeem it throughout** the period of **thirty days,** and after that point one has transgressed a prohibition.

*Rav Sheshet* offers a different resolution. He maintains that the obligation to redeem a firstborn donkey does in fact begin immediately — from the moment of birth. What does the thirty-day figure in the *baraita* mean, then? *She'eino over alav* — one does not transgress by failing to redeem within the first thirty days. So the structure is the inverse of *pidyon ha'ben*: for a human firstborn, the obligation begins at thirty days and continues indefinitely; for a firstborn donkey, the obligation begins immediately, but one first incurs transgression only after thirty days.

The *Gemara* objects to *Rav Sheshet*'s position from a *Tosefta*: *mitzvato kol shloshim yom* — the *mitzvah* of the firstborn donkey applies for all the first thirty days. *Mikan va'eilakh* — after thirty days, one must choose: either redeem it or break its neck. The *Gemara* reads this to mean: *mitzva lehashoto kol shloshim yom* — the *mitzvah* to wait begins, and the obligation kicks in, at day thirty. The end of the *Tosefta* further undermines *Rav Sheshet*: after thirty days, *o podehu o orefo* — either redeem or break its neck. According to *Rav Sheshet*, the text should have said "after thirty days either redeem it or you will have already transgressed," not that one then gets to make a fresh decision. The *Tosefta* reads as though one still has an open choice after thirty days, which is inconsistent with *Rav Sheshet*'s view that the transgression begins at thirty days.

### Rava Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אוֹ פּוֹדֵהוּ אוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּמַקֵּשׁי, הָא רַבָּנַן דְּלָא מַקְּשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** it **should have** stated: **From that** point **forward, either he redeems it or he transgresses** a prohibition, rather than stating: From that point forward he must either redeem it or break its neck. **Rather, Rava says:** The contradiction between the two *baraitot* is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita*, which states that the redemption is performed after thirty days, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who compares** the redemption of a firstborn donkey to the redemption of a woman's firstborn son, where it is stated: "From a month old shall you redeem them" (Numbers 18:16). **That** *baraita*, which states that one must perform the mitzva immediately, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who do not compare** the two mitzvot of redemption.

*Rava* offers a resolution that ties together the dispute about when redemption takes effect with the larger debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *Chachamim*: *ha Rabbi Eliezer demakish, ha rabanan d'lo makshi* — one source reflects *Rabbi Eliezer*, who compares firstborn donkeys to firstborn humans, and the other reflects the *Chachamim*, who do not draw that comparison. *Rabbi Eliezer*, who holds that donkeys are comparable to humans and that redemption is not complete until the sheep is handed over, would also hold that just as the obligation for humans begins at thirty days, so too for donkeys the obligation begins at thirty days. The *Chachamim*, who reject the full comparison and hold that the redemption is complete upon designation of the sheep, likewise reject the parallel timing rule. Since they do not compare donkeys to humans, there is no thirty-day threshold for donkeys, and the obligation begins immediately.

### Rambam Position

The *Rambam* adopts this resolution and understands the *Tosefta* accordingly. The *Tosefta* reflects the *Chachamim*'s position: the *mitzvah* begins immediately and is ongoing throughout the first thirty days. What happens after thirty days? The *Rambam* rules that there is no biblical cutoff — since the obligation does not derive from a comparison to humans, thirty days has no independent biblical significance. After thirty days, we insist that the owner make a decision and not delay indefinitely, but if he continues to delay he is guilty of *shihuy mitzvah* — postponing a *mitzvah* — without having technically transgressed. Some *acharonim* raise the difficulty that the *Gemara* itself uses the language *over alav*, but the *Rambam*'s reading confines that language to *Rav Sheshet*'s position, not to the *maskana*. According to the *Rambam*, the *maskana* is that the obligation begins immediately, and while one ought not delay beyond thirty days, doing so does not constitute a biblical transgression — there is simply no scriptural basis for thirty days as an absolute cutoff when donkeys are not compared to humans.

The practical *halakhic* consequence follows: since we rule like the *Chachamim* that the *mitzvah* is accomplished at designation, the *berachah* for *pidyon peter chamor* is recited at the moment one designates the sheep — not when one hands it over to the *kohen*.

## Arifah Mitzvot

### Pidyon Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא רָצָה לִפְדּוֹתוֹ — עוֹרְפוֹ מֵאֲחוֹרָיו וְקוֹבְרוֹ. מִצְוַת הַפְּדִיָּיה קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת עֲרִיפָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא תִפְדֶּה וַעֲרַפְתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one did not wish to redeem** the firstborn donkey, **he breaks its neck from behind and buries it. The mitzva of redeeming** the firstborn donkey **takes precedence over the mitzva of breaking the neck, as it is stated: "If you will not redeem it, then you shall break its neck"** (Exodus 13:13).

The *daf* concludes with a *mishna* that has no accompanying *Gemara* — an unusual occurrence that is in part a function of the printer's arrangement. The *mishna* states: *lo ratza lifdoto, arfo me'achorav, vekovro* — if one does not wish to redeem the donkey, one breaks its neck from behind (using a hatchet, not a saw, as discussed earlier) and buries it, since the animal is *assur b'hana'ah*. The *mishna* then articulates a hierarchy: *mitzvas pidyah kodemet lemitzvas arifah* — the *mitzvah* of redemption takes precedence over the *mitzvah* of breaking the neck. Breaking the neck is not a free-standing alternative of equal standing. It is a penalty imposed when one fails to redeem. The verse makes this explicit: *shene'emar im lo tifdeh ve'arafto* — if you do not redeem, then you break its neck. The act of *arifah* is conditional on the failure to redeem.

This leads the *Rambam* to count *mitzvas arifah* as a distinct entry among the 613 *mitzvot* in *Sefer HaMitzvos*. The *Rambam* himself acknowledges the difficulty: ordinarily, details of a *mitzvah* are not counted separately. He explains that he counts it separately because the *mishna* explicitly refers to it as *mitzvas arifah* — the *mitzvah* of breaking the neck — suggesting it has independent standing. The *Ra'avad* objects forcefully: how can one call this a *mitzvah*? It is a consequence of failing to do what one should have done; it is the mechanism for dealing with a forbidden animal when the owner has neglected the proper *mitzvah*. Some *meforshim* respond that calling something a *mitzvah* does not necessarily mean it represents a positive or desirable act. There is, for example, a *mitzvah* governing how a man divorces his wife, though divorce itself may not be the ideal choice — the *mitzvah* prescribes the proper procedure for an act one has chosen to perform. *Arifah* functions similarly: given that redemption was not performed, here is the required procedure. Yet the *Ra'avad*'s discomfort with calling it a *mitzvah* even given this context remains striking, especially since the *mishna* itself uses that term.

### Mitzvot Priorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוַת יְעִידָה קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת הַפְּדִיָּיה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יְעָדָהּ וְהֶפְדָּהּ״. מִצְוַת הַיִּיבּוּם קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁהָיוּ מִתְכַּוְּונִין לְשֵׁם מִצְוָה, וְעַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוְּונִין לְשֵׁם מִצְוָה, אָמְרוּ: מִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִצְוַת יִבּוּם. מִצְוַת הַגְּאוּלָּה בָּאָדוֹן, הוּא קוֹדֵם לְכׇל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם לֹא יִגָּאֵל וְנִמְכַּר בְּעֶרְכֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna proceeds to enumerate other mitzvot in which one option takes precedence over another. **The mitzva of designating** a Hebrew maidservant to be betrothed to her master **takes precedence over the mitzva of redeeming** the maidservant from her master with money, **as it is stated:** "If she does not please her master, **who has not betrothed her to himself, then he shall let her be redeemed"** (Exodus 21:8). **The mitzva of levirate marriage takes precedence over the mitzva of *ḥalitza*,** which dissolves the levirate bond, as it is stated: "And if the man does not wish to take his brother's wife" (Deuteronomy 25:7). The mishna adds: This was the case **initially, when** people **would intend** that their performance of levirate marriage be **for the sake of the mitzva. But now that they do not intend** that their performance of levirate marriage be **for the sake of the mitzva,** but rather for reasons such as the beauty of the *yevama* or for financial gain, the Sages **said** that **the mitzva of *ḥalitza* takes precedence over the mitzva of levirate marriage.** With regard to a non-kosher animal that was consecrated to the Temple, **the mitzva of redemption by the owner** who consecrated it **takes precedence over** redemption by **any** other **person, as it is stated:** "And if it is of a non-kosher animal…**and if it is not redeemed, it shall be sold according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:27).

The *mishna* continues with a series of analogous priority statements. *Mitzvas yi'ud kodemet lemitzvas pidyah* — the *mitzvah* of *yi'ud* takes precedence over the *mitzvah* of redemption. The context is a young Jewish girl sold by her father as a slave: the master may either designate her as a wife (*yi'ud*) or release her. The *mishna* states that *yi'ud* should come first. The underlying logic reflects the social and economic realities within which this institution was embedded: the goal of her sale was to ensure her protection and welfare, and marriage was the mechanism intended to achieve that. Redemption and release are appropriate only if the path to *yi'ud* is not taken. The verse corroborates: *asher lo ye'adah, vehefdah* — if he does not designate her as a wife, she shall be redeemed.

*Mitzvas yibum kodemet lemitzvas chalitzah* — *yibum* (levirate marriage) takes precedence over *chalitzah*. The *chalitzah* ceremony, in which the widow removes the shoe from her deceased husband's brother and spits before him, is described by the Torah as a mark of disgrace upon one who refuses to perpetuate his brother's name. This framing itself demonstrates that *yibum* is the preferred path. The *mishna* acknowledges, however, that this was true *barishonah* — in earlier times, when men performed *yibum* for the sake of the *mitzvah*. Now that *yibum* is often motivated by attraction to the widow's appearance or property rather than by *lishmah* intent, the ruling changed: *amru mitzvas chalitzah kodemet* — *chalitzah* now takes precedence. The *Gemara* in Yevamot goes as far as to suggest that *yibum* performed for improper motives borders on transgression, since the *mitzvah* of *yibum* redefines what would otherwise be a forbidden relationship — and if the defining intent is absent, the act falls closer to the category of *arayot* it was meant to transcend. It should be noted that *Sefardim* rule *mitzvas yibum kodemet* and practice *yibum* to this day, making this a live *machlokes* in practical *halakha*, to be discussed fully when we reach Yevamot.

*Mitzvas hage'ulah b'adon hu kodem l'chol adam* — one who consecrated property to the *Beis HaMikdash* has the first right to redeem it before others may do so, as the verse states *v'im lo yiga'el v'nimkar b'erkecha* — if he does not redeem it, the Temple treasury will sell it. The Temple is better served financially when the original owner redeems, because the owner must add one fifth to the redemption price; the owner also retains a prior right to reclaim what was his.

With these rulings the entire discussion of *peter chamor* concludes. As noted, virtually everything the *Gemara* has to say about *peter chamor* has been gathered within this *perek*.

## Bechora Tehora

### Non-Jew Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַלּוֹקֵחַ עוּבַּר פָּרָתוֹ שֶׁל נׇכְרִי, וְהַמּוֹכֵר לוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי, וְהַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף לוֹ, וְהַמְקַבֵּל מִמֶּנּוּ, וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה — פָּטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״, אֲבָל לֹא בַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who purchases the fetus of a cow** that **belongs to a gentile; one who sells** the fetus of his cow **to** a gentile, **even though one is not permitted** to sell a large animal to a gentile; **one who enters into a partnership with** a gentile with regard to a cow or its fetus; **one who receives** a cow **from** a gentile to tend to it in exchange for partnership in its offspring; **and one who gives** his cow to a gentile **in receivership,** so that the gentile owns a share of the cow's offspring; in all of these cases, one is **exempt from** the obligation of redeeming **the firstborn** offspring, **as it is stated:** "I sanctified to Me all the firstborn **in Israel,** both man and animal" (Numbers 3:13), indicating that the mitzva is incumbent upon the Jewish people, **but not upon others.** If the firstborn belongs even partially to a gentile, the sanctity of firstborn does not apply to it.

The *Gemara* now transitions to the laws of *bechora tehora* — the firstborn of kosher animals. The new *mishna* closely parallels the opening *mishna* of the discussion of *peter chamor*. The governing principle is that non-Jewish ownership of either the mother or the offspring at any stage exempts the animal from the *bechora* obligation. The *mishna* lists the relevant cases: buying the fetus of a non-Jew's cow (where the non-Jew retains ownership of the cow itself); selling a fetus to a non-Jew (so that he is part owner of the firstborn); entering a partnership with a non-Jew; taking an animal from a non-Jew in *kabalut* (whereby one raises the animal and acquires a share of the offspring); or transferring an animal to a non-Jew in *kabalut*. In all these configurations, the non-Jew holds at least a partial ownership in either the mother or the firstborn, and that suffices for exemption. The verse provides the basis: *b'Yisrael* — the Torah sanctified firstborn animals specifically among Israel, *aval lo ba'acherim* — not among non-Jews.

## Bechora Tehora…

### Kohanim Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם חַיָּיבִים, שֶׁלֹּא נִפְטְרוּ מִבְּכוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה, אֶלָּא מִפִּדְיוֹן הַבֵּן וּמִפֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **The priests and the Levites are obligated** in the mitzva, i.e., their animals have firstborn sanctity, **as they were not exempted from** the mitzva of the male **firstborn of a kosher animal; rather,** they were exempted only **from redemption of the** firstborn **son and from** the redemption of **the firstborn donkey.**

The *mishna* continues: *Kohanim u'Leviyim chayavim* — *Kohanim* and *Leviyim* are obligated in the *bechora* of kosher animals, even though they are exempt from *pidyon ha'ben* and from *peter chamor*. The exemption of *Kohanim* and *Leviyim* from the redemption obligations derives from the swap in the desert, whereby the *Leviyim* were substituted for the firstborn Israelites, and the animals belonging to *Leviyim* were similarly substituted for the firstborn animals of Israel. That swap exempts them from the redemption-based *mitzvot*. However, it does not exempt them from the obligation regarding firstborn kosher animals, which are brought as *korbanos* — an independent and intrinsic sanctity (*kedushat haguf*) rather than a redemption-based one.

## Kinyan Methods

### Money vs. Meshicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת לְגוֹי בִּבְהֶמְתּוֹ בְּדִינֵיהֶם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא מָשַׁךְ — קָנָה, וְחַיֶּיבֶת בִּבְכוֹרָה. וְגוֹי שֶׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּבְהֶמְתּוֹ בְּדִינֵיהֶם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא מָשַׁךְ — קָנָה, וּפָטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the mishna's discussion of sales involving Jews and gentiles, **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani says** that **Reish Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya:** In the case of **a Jew who gave money to a gentile to** buy the gentile's **animal** from him **according to their laws,** as explained further on, **even though** the Jew **did not pull** the animal in the manner of a formal acquisition, as required by *halakha*, **he has** legally **acquired** it, **and** the Jewish owner **is obligated** to give the animal's **firstborn** offspring to a priest. **And** in the case of **a gentile who gave money to a Jew to** buy the Jew's **animal** from him **according to their laws, even though** the gentile **did not pull** the animal **he has acquired** it, **and** the Jew **is** therefore **exempt from** giving the animal's **firstborn** offspring to a priest.

The *Gemara* now enters a discussion that, while framed as arising from the *mishna* about non-Jewish ownership, is one of the most practically consequential topics in the entire *perek*. At stake is the precise mechanism of acquisition when transacting with a non-Jew — a question central to the contemporary practices of selling the ear of a firstborn animal, selling *chametz*, and *heter mechira*. The discussion opens with a *baraita* cited by *Rav Gidel* in the name of *Rav* (also attributed to *Resh Lakish* in the name of *bar Kappara*): *Yisrael shenatan ma'ot la'akum b'behemtam b'dineihem* — if a Jew gives money to a non-Jew to purchase his flock in accordance with their laws, *af al pi shelo mashach kanah* — even though the Jew has not yet drawn the flock to him (i.e., taken physical possession), he acquires it through the giving of money, and the animal is obligated in *bechora* from that moment. Conversely, if a non-Jew gives money to a Jew for his flock, even without *meshicha*, the non-Jew acquires from the moment the money is given, and the animal is exempt from *bechora* thereafter.

### Peshat in Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בְּדִינֵיהֶם״? אִי נֵימָא בְּדִינֵיהֶם דְּגוּפֵיהּ, דְּאָתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר: אִם גּוּפֵיהּ קָנֵי לֵיהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּכֶסֶף, דִּכְתִיב ״לָרֶשֶׁת אֲחוּזָּה״, הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לַאֲחוּזָּה, מָה אֲחוּזָּה נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, אַף עֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי נִקְנֶה בְּכֶסֶף. מָמוֹנוֹ — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן הוּא? אִם כֵּן, אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה נָמֵי! וְעוֹד, יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל יוֹכִיחַו, דְּגוּפֵיהּ קָנֵי לֵיהּ בְּכֶסֶף, מָמוֹנוֹ בִּמְשִׁיכָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּדִינֵיהֶם שֶׁפָּסְקָה לָהֶם תּוֹרָה, ״אוֹ קָנֹה מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ״ — מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ הוּא דְּבִמְשִׁיכָה, הָא מִיַּד גּוֹי בְּכֶסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by: **According to their laws? If we say** that it is referring **to the laws** relating **to** the purchase of a gentile's **person** as a slave, i.e., the *halakhot* of purchasing gentiles themselves rather than the methods by which gentiles can acquire property, this must be based on the assumption that the *halakha* with regard to the purchase of a gentile's animal **is derived by** the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **If** a gentile's **person is acquired by a Jew** as a Canaanite slave **through** payment of **money, as it is written:** "And you may make them an inheritance for your children after you, **to hold for an ancestral possession"** (Leviticus 25:46), the same *halakha* should apply to acquiring a gentile's property. The Gemara elaborates: **The verse** thereby **compares** a gentile slave **to an ancestral possession,** i.e., land: **Just as an ancestral possession is acquired by** the transfer **of money, or with a bill of sale, or through taking possession, so too, a Canaanite slave is acquired by** the transfer of **money.** If this is the *halakha* with regard to a gentile's person, **is it not all the more so** reasonable that a gentile's **property** is acquired through the transfer of money? The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **If so,** then according to *halakha* it should be possible to acquire a gentile's property **even with a bill of sale or by taking possession,** which is not so. The Gemara adds: **And furthermore,** the *halakha* pertaining to **a Jew** purchasing goods **from a Jew proves** that the entire premise of the *a fortiori* inference is false, **as he acquires** a Jew's **person with money** alone, but he acquires **his property** only **by pulling.** It is therefore evident that the ability to acquire one's person with money does not indicate that his property can be purchased in the same manner. **Rather, Abaye says** that the term: Their laws, is referring to **their laws that the Torah prescribed for their** methods of purchase, by stating: **"Or buy of your counterpart's hand"** (Leviticus 25:14), which teaches that **it is** only **from the hand of your counterpart,** i.e., a Jew, **that** property **is** acquired **by** the formal act of acquisition of **pulling.** This indicates that acquiring property **from the hand of a gentile** is accomplished **through** payment of **money.**

The *Gemara* inquires into the meaning of *b'dineihem* — "in their laws." One possibility is that this phrase alludes to the law governing the acquisition of non-Jewish slaves, and that a *kal vachomer* can be constructed: if a Jew acquires a non-Jewish person through money (based on the verse *le'reshet achuza*, comparing a slave to a plot of land, and we know land is acquired through *kesef*, *shtar*, or *chazaka* — therefore *af eved kena'ani nikna b'kesef* — a non-Jewish slave is acquired through money), then certainly one may acquire their chattel through money. The *Gemara* rejects this: one cannot construct such a *kal vachomer*, as different types of property are acquired through different acts and those distinctions are not transferable by inference. Moreover, the comparison is disproven by the case of a Jew acquiring a fellow Jew as a slave — which is also done through money — whereas Jewish-owned chattel is acquired through *meshicha*, not money. If the *kal vachomer* were valid, Jewish chattel should also be acquirable through money. *Abaye* therefore provides the correct interpretation: *b'dineihem shepasak lahem Torah* — this is simply the law the Torah assigned for transactions involving non-Jews, as derived from the verse.

The verse states: *o kanei miyad amitecha* — "or if you purchase from the hand of your fellow." *Miyad amitecha* — from your fellow Jew — the emphasis on the hand implies physical transfer, i.e., *meshicha*, as the mode of acquisition. Therefore: *hu d'meshicha, ha miyad oved kochavim, b'kesef* — with a Jew, acquisition is through *meshicha*; with a non-Jew, the opposite applies, and acquisition is through *kesef*. *Tosafot* explains why only these two are in play: when transacting in cattle, there are two fundamental acts — one party gives money and the other takes possession of the object. These are the primary two modes, *kesef* and *meshicha*, and the verse addresses them specifically. When the Torah emphasizes that acquisition from a fellow Jew proceeds through *meshicha*, it necessarily implies that acquisition from a non-Jew proceeds through the other primary act: *kesef*.

### Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: מִיַּד גּוֹי — כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא! אָמְרִי: לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא — אִם גּוּפוֹ קוֹנֶה, מָמוֹנוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? וְאֵימָא: מִיַּד גּוֹי — עַד דְּאִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי! אָמְרִי: וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? גּוּפוֹ בְּאַחַת, מָמוֹנוֹ בִּשְׁתַּיִם?! וְאֵימָא: אוֹ בְּהָא אוֹ בְּהָא, דּוּמְיָא דַּ״עֲמִיתֶךָ״. מָה ״עֲמִיתֶךָ״ בַּחֲדָא, אַף גּוֹי נָמֵי בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But** one can **say** that it should be inferred from the verse that one **cannot** purchase anything **at all** from the hand of a gentile. The Sages **said** in response: That **cannot enter your mind,** as there **is an *a fortiori*** inference which indicates otherwise: **If** a Jew can **purchase** a gentile's **person** as a slave, is it **not all the more so** reasonable that he can purchase a gentile's **property?** The Gemara raises another objection: **But** one can **say** that the verse teaches that whereas a Jew's property is acquired through pulling, property purchased **from the hand of a gentile** is not acquired **until two** formal acts of acquisition have been performed, both pulling and payment of money. The Sages **said** in response: **Isn't it** indicated otherwise by **an *a fortiori*** inference: If **his person** is acquired **through** only **one** act of acquisition, could it be that **his property** must be acquired **through two** acts? The Gemara further suggests: **But** one can **say** that it is derived from the verse that one can acquire property from a gentile **either** through **this** method of pulling **or** through **that** method of money. The Gemara explains: The *halakha* derived from the phrase "of your counterpart" must be **similar to** an acquisition from **your counterpart:** **Just as** from the hand of **your counterpart** it is **with one** act, with pulling, **so too,** from the hand of **a gentile,** it is **also with one** act, **with money.**

The *Gemara* then addresses several alternative readings of the verse. Could one argue that nothing at all may be transacted with a non-Jew? A *kal vachomer* forecloses this: since one may acquire a non-Jew's person, *a fortiori* one may acquire his property. Could one argue that with a non-Jew, both *kesef* and *meshicha* must be performed? The *kal vachomer* again forecloses it: since acquiring a non-Jewish person requires only one act (*kesef*), it makes no sense that acquiring mere property should require two acts. Could one argue that with a non-Jew, either *kesef* or *meshicha* suffices? The *Gemara* says no — *dumya d'amitecha*: just as with a fellow Jew there is one exclusive mode of acquisition, so too with a non-Jew there is one exclusive mode. With a Jew it is *meshicha*; with a non-Jew it is *kesef*. The same sequence of arguments applies symmetrically to the reverse case — the non-Jew purchasing from a Jew — and the *Gemara* works through each step in identical fashion, concluding that money alone effects the non-Jew's acquisition.

### Non-Jew Buys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְגוֹי שֶׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּבְהֶמְתּוֹ בְּדִינֵיהֶן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא מָשַׁךְ — קָנָה, וּפָטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה. מַאי בְּדִינֵיהֶן? אִילֵּימָא בְּדִינֵיהֶן דְּגוּפוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל קָנֵי לֵיהּ בְּכֶסֶף, דִּכְתִיב ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״, מָמוֹנוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל יוֹכִיחַו, דְּגוּפוֹ קָנֵי בְּכֶסֶף, מָמוֹנוֹ בִּמְשִׁיכָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּדִינֵיהֶם שֶׁפָּסְקָה לָהֶם תּוֹרָה, ״וְכִי תִמְכְּרוּ מִמְכָּר לַעֲמִיתֶךָ״ — לַעֲמִיתֶךָ בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וּלְגוֹי בִּמְשִׁיכָה, ״לַעֲמִיתֶךָ״ לְמָה לִי? וְאֵימָא: לְגוֹי כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא! אָמְרִי: לָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? אִם גּוּפוֹ קָנָה, מָמוֹנוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֵימָא: לְגוֹי עַד דְּאִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי! לָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? גּוּפוֹ בְּאֶחָד, מָמוֹנוֹ בִּשְׁתַּיִם?! וְאֵימָא: אוֹ בְּהָא אוֹ בְּהָא, דּוּמְיָא דַּ״עֲמִיתֶךָ״; מָה ״עֲמִיתֶךָ״ בְּאַחַת, אַף גּוֹי נָמֵי בְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the statement of Rabbi Oshaya. **The Master said: And a gentile who gave money to a Jew to** buy the Jew's **animal** from him **according to their laws, even though** the gentile **did not pull** the animal **he has acquired** it, **and** the Jew **is** therefore **exempt from** giving the animal's **firstborn** offspring to a priest. **The Gemara clarifies: What is** the meaning of **according to their laws? If we say** that it means: **According to their laws of the person** of the Jew himself, i.e., the manner in which he purchases the person of a Jew with money, and it is reasonable to say so, **as it may be derived** through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** a gentile **acquires the body of a Jew with money, as it is written** in that regard: **"From the money of his purchase"** (Leviticus 25:51), then with regard to the **property** of a Jew, is it **not all the more so** reasonable that the gentile can acquire it with money? This can be rejected, as the acquisition of **a Jew from a Jew will prove** that the inference is not valid, as the **person** of a Hebrew slave **one acquires with money, while the property** of a Jew can be acquired only **by pulling. Rather, Abaye said:** That which it states, that a gentile purchases an animal with money **according to their laws,** means: With the acts of acquisition **that the Torah prescribed** to gentiles, the manner in which they acquire items from Jews, i.e., with money, as it is stated: **"And if you sell a sale item to your counterpart"** (Leviticus 25:14), from which it may be inferred: **To your counterpart,** i.e., a Jew, property **is** acquired **by pulling,** but property **from the hand of a gentile** is acquired **with money.** Accordingly, the phrase "of your counterpart" remains extraneous. The Gemara raises an objection: Isn't it indicating otherwise by **an *a fortiori*** inference to the contrary: **If** a gentile can **purchase** a Jew's **person** as a slave, then is it **not all the more so** reasonable that he can purchase the Jew's **property?** The Gemara further suggests: **Say** and infer as follows: To your counterpart you sell with pulling, **but to a gentile** you do not sell **until two** formal acts of acquisition have been performed: Pulling and money. The Sages **said** in response: **But isn't it an *a fortiori*** inference to the contrary: If the **person** of a Jew is acquired **with one** act of acquisition, is it reasonable that **his property** is acquired **with two** acts? The Gemara further suggests: **But say** that a gentile acquires property from a Jew **either with this** act of pulling **or with that** act of **paying** money. The Gemara explains: The acquisition of the gentile must be **similar to** the acquisition of **your counterpart,** from which it is derived:

The *Gemara* examines the second half of the *baraita*: when a non-Jew purchases from a Jew, acquisition is likewise effected by the giving of money. The same derivation from *o kanei miyad amitecha* applies in this direction: when a Jew sells to a fellow Jew, the transaction is effected through transfer of possession (*meshicha*). When a Jew sells to a non-Jew, the transaction is effected through money. The same *kal vachomer* arguments are worked through: one cannot say no transaction with a non-Jew is possible, since a non-Jew can acquire a Jew as a slave (*mikessef miknato* — from the money of the purchase); one cannot require two acts for the non-Jew to acquire, since acquiring a person requires only one; and one cannot permit either act, since the parallel to the Jewish case demands exactly one exclusive mode.

### Ameimar Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וּלְאָמֵימָר דְּאָמַר מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה, הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת, מְשִׁיכָה לֹא, אַהֲנִי ״לַעֲמִיתֶךָ״ — לַעֲמִיתֶךָ בְּכֶסֶף, לְגוֹי בִּמְשִׁיכָה. אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר: מְשִׁיכָה מְפוֹרֶשֶׁת מִן הַתּוֹרָה, לַעֲמִיתֶךָ בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וּלְגוֹי בִּמְשִׁיכָה, ״לַעֲמִיתֶךָ״ לְמָה לִי? אָמְרִי: לַעֲמִיתֶךָ אַתָּה מַחְזִיר אוֹנָאָה, וְאִי אַתָּה מַחֲזִיר אוֹנָאָה לְגוֹי. לְגוֹי — מֵ״אַל תּוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת אָחִיו״ נָפְקָא! חַד בְּגוֹי, וְחַד בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חַד — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לְגוֹי הוּא דְּאֵין לוֹ אוֹנָאָה, אֲבָל הֶקְדֵּשׁ — יֵשׁ לוֹ אוֹנָאָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: And according to** the opinion of **Ameimar, who says** that **pulling effects acquisition in** the case of **a gentile, this works out well if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says: By Torah law, money effects acquisition,** but **pulling** does **not.** If Ameimar accepts Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, then it can be derived from the phrase **"of your counterpart"** that transactions **with your counterpart,** i.e., a Jew, are effected **through** payment of **money,** whereas transactions **with a gentile** are effected **by pulling.** **But if** Ameimar **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, who says:** The act of acquisition of **pulling is explicit in the Torah,** then the *halakha* is that transactions **with your counterpart** are effected **by pulling, and** transactions **with a gentile** are also effected **by pulling.** Accordingly, **why do I** need the phrase **"of your counterpart"?** The Sages **said** in explanation: The phrase "of your counterpart" does not teach a *halakha* with regard to the method of acquisition. Rather, it is referring to the latter part of the verse: "And if you make a sale to your counterpart, or buy of your counterpart's hand, a man shall not exploit his brother" (Leviticus 25:14). The verse is derived as follows: **You** must **return** the sum of **exploitation to your counterpart, but you do not** need to **return** the sum of **exploitation to a gentile.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: The exemption from returning the sum of exploitation **to a gentile is derived from** the latter part of the verse alone: **"A man shall not exploit his brother."** A gentile is not included in the category of "his brother." Accordingly, the phrase "of your counterpart" remains extraneous. The Gemara responds: **One** phrase is required **to** exclude **a gentile, and one** serves **to** exclude **consecrated** property. **And** both derivations **are necessary, as, had the Merciful One written** only **one** exclusionary phrase, **I would say: It is** with regard to transactions **with a gentile that one is not** subject to the prohibition of **exploitation; but** with regard to transactions involving **consecrated** property, **one is** subject to the prohibition of **exploitation.** Therefore, the other derivation **teaches us** that one is not subject to the prohibition of exploitation with regard to transactions involving consecrated property either.

*Ameimar* dissents from the *baraita*'s conclusion: *meshicha b'oved kochavim konei* — it is *meshicha*, not *kesef*, that effects acquisition from a non-Jew. This dissent is not problematic in principle because, as the *Gemara* notes, there is an underlying unresolved debate about which mode of acquisition governs Jewish transactions at the biblical level. *Ameimar*'s position becomes coherent depending on which side of that debate one adopts.

If one holds like *Rabbi Yochanan* — *devar Torah ma'ot konot* — that biblically, for Jews, acquisition is through *kesef*, then *meshicha* is only a rabbinic innovation. The verse *miyad amitecha* therefore addresses a Jew and says acquisition proceeds through *kesef*; it implies that with a non-Jew acquisition proceeds through *meshicha* — and this is what *Ameimar* holds. The rabbis then reversed the Jewish rule from *kesef* to *meshicha* (to prevent the situation where purchased goods are lost before the buyer takes possession), so that in practice both Jews and non-Jews now operate through *meshicha* — one by rabbinic enactment, the other by biblical mandate.

If, however, one holds like *Resh Lakish* — *meshicha meforeshet min haTorah* — that *meshicha* is the biblical mode for Jews, then the verse implies the opposite for non-Jews, namely *kesef*. For *Ameimar* to say *meshicha* works for a non-Jew while holding like *Resh Lakish* would mean both Jews and non-Jews operate through *meshicha* — and *lama li le'amitecha*? What then does the verse's emphasis on *amitecha* accomplish? The *Gemara* resolves: *le'amitecha ata machzir ona ve'i ata machzir ona li'chnani* — the word *amitecha* is not addressing the mode of acquisition at all. Rather, it is the basis for the rule that the prohibition of *ona'ah* (overcharging) and the obligation to return excess payment applies to transactions with a fellow Jew but not to transactions involving a *kena'ani* (substituted in our printed text for the original *goy*). On this reading, *Ameimar* holds that acquisition from a non-Jew is through *meshicha*, in accord with *Rabbi Yochanan* that biblical Jewish acquisition is through *kesef*.

### Ona'at Akum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵילוֹ אָסוּר — הַיְינוּ דְּאִצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמִישְׁרֵי אוֹנָאָה, אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵילוֹ שֶׁל גּוֹי מוּתָּר, אוֹנָאָה מִיבַּעְיָא? אָמְרִי: אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵילוֹ מוּתָּר — עַל כּוּרְחָיךְ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara remarks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **his robbery,** i.e., robbing a gentile, is **prohibited,** as **this is** the reason **that a verse is necessary to permit** keeping the sum of the **exploitation** obtained from a gentile. **But if** Ameimar **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **his robbery is permitted, is it necessary** to state that one may keep the sum of the **exploitation** obtained from a gentile? The Gemara accepts this reasoning: The Sages **said: If** Ameimar **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **his robbery is permitted, perforce he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yoḥanan that money effects acquisition by Torah law, and therefore the difficulty does not arise.

The *Gemara*'s discussion of *ona'ah* vis-à-vis non-Jews raises profound questions — moral, ethical, and historical — that cannot be fully addressed here. What should be noted is that already among the *Rishonim*, and continuing throughout the *kitvei Acharonim*, the apparent civil disparity between Jews and non-Jews with respect to *ona'ah*, *gezel*, and similar laws was subjected to significant qualification. Multiple *Rishonim* — not only the *Meiri* — held that these rules do not apply to contemporary law-abiding non-Jews. The *maskana* in Bava Kamma is that stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden. These modifications appear to reflect sincere legal and moral reasoning rather than mere concession to external pressure.

As for the formal *Gemara* text: the *Gemara* asks whether the verse's mention of *amitecha* is even necessary to exclude *ona'ah* with a non-Jew, given that the verse elsewhere says *al tonu ish et achiv* — do not overcharge a man his brother — which already implies that the prohibition is limited to a Jew. The answer is: *chad li'chnani v'chad le'hekdesh* — one exclusion addresses a non-Jew and the other addresses *hekdesh* (consecrated property), for which the prohibition of *ona'ah* likewise does not apply. *De'tzricha* — both are needed: without the second verse, one might have assumed that while overcharging a non-Jew is permitted, overcharging *hekdesh* is surely forbidden given the gravity of that relationship. The two verses work together to establish both exclusions.

The *Gemara* then notes: *hani'cha le'man de'amar gezelo assur* — this analysis works if one holds that stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden, because it then makes sense that a separate verse is needed to permit the lesser offense of overcharging. *Ela i savrat le'man de'amar gezelo mutar* — but if one holds that stealing from a non-Jew is actually permitted, no verse is needed to permit overcharging, since the lesser includes the greater. The conclusion follows: *al korchacha ke'Rabbi Yochanan sevira lei* — if *Ameimar* holds that stealing is forbidden, and therefore needs a verse to permit overcharging, and he also holds that *meshicha* governs acquisition from a non-Jew, then he must hold like *Rabbi Yochanan* that biblical Jewish acquisition is through *kesef* — since that is the only way the verse's *amitecha* is left free to address *ona'ah* rather than the mode of acquisition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַלּוֹקֵחַ גְּרוּטָאוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם, וּמָצָא בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יַחְזִיר, וְאִם מִשֶּׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יוֹלִיךְ הֲנָאָה לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת, מְשִׁיכָה לְמָה לִּי? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו לָדוּן בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִי הָכִי, מָעוֹת לְמָה לִי? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת, אִי מָשֵׁיךְ — אִין, וְאִי לָא — לָא. אִי הָכִי, קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מִקָּח טָעוּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: רֵישָׁא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מִקָּח טָעוּת, סֵיפָא לֵיכָּא מִקָּח טָעוּת? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא מִקָּח טָעוּת, רֵישָׁא דְּלָא יָהֵיב זוּזֵי — לָא מִיחְזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, סֵיפָא דְּיָהֵיב זוּזֵי — מִיחְזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְאַבַּיֵּי אָמַר לָךְ: רֵישָׁא מִקָּח טָעוּת, דְּלָא יָדַע, דְּהָא לָא יְהַיב לֵיהּ זוּזֵי. סֵיפָא לָאו מִקָּח טָעוּת הוּא, דְּכֵיוָן דִּיהַיב זוּזֵי, כִּי קָא מָשֵׁיךְ — אִיבְּעִי לְעַיּוֹנֵי וַהֲדַר מִימְשָׁךְ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִדְּרֵישָׁא מְשִׁיכָה אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, סֵיפָא נָמֵי מְשִׁיכָה אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״מָשַׁךְ״, תְּנָא סֵיפָא נָמֵי ״מָשַׁךְ״. רָבִינָא אָמַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה, וְרֵישָׁא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם לֹא נָתַן וְלֹא מָשַׁךְ — יַחְזוֹר. מַאי יַחְזוֹר? יַחְזוֹר בִּדְבָרִים. קָסָבַר: דְּבָרִים יֵשׁ בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם מְחוּסְּרֵי אֲמָנָה, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי — יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, דְּקָיְימִי בְּדִבּוּרַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹיִם, דְּאִינְהוּ לָא קָיְימִי בְּדִבּוּרַיְיהוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion that property is acquired from a gentile by monetary payment. It is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who buys broken vessels [*gerutaot*]** made of gold or silver **from the gentiles and finds among them** an object of **idol worship, if he pulled** the object of idol worship, thereby performing an act of acquisition, **before he gave** the **money** to the gentile, **he may return** the object of idol worship to the gentile. But **if he pulled** it **after he gave** the **money** to the gentile, he may not return it. Since the idol's status was not revoked, he must **take** it and cast it **into the Dead Sea.** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **And if you say** that payment of **money acquires** items from gentiles, **why do I** need **pulling** in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol? Pulling is not in fact required. The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the gentile **accepted upon himself to** have this transaction **adjudicated by Jewish law.** The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, why do I** need the payment of **money** in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol? Pulling alone should be enough, in accordance with *halakha*. The Gemara explains that **this is what** the *baraita* **means: Even though** the Jew **gave money** to the gentile, the *halakha* still depends on the act of pulling: **If he pulled** the broken vessels, **yes,** he has acquired them, **and if not,** he has **not** acquired them. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **the first clause** of the *baraita* is **difficult,** as it states: If he pulled the object of idol worship before he gave the money to the gentile, he may return the object of idol worship to the gentile. Evidently, pulling alone does not effect acquisition. **Abaye said** in explanation: The *halakha* in **the first clause** is ruled as it is **because there is a mistaken transaction,** as the Jew did not wish to purchase the idol. **Rava said to** Abaye: You maintain that the *halakha* of **the first clause** is ruled as it is **because there is a mistaken transaction.** Is that to say that in **the latter clause there is no mistaken transaction?** After all, the Jew does not wish to purchase the idol in either case. **Rather, Rava said:** The purchase in **the first clause and** the purchase in **the latter clause** are both **a mistaken transaction, but** in the case presented in **the first clause, where** the Jew **did not pay** him the **dinars, it does not appear as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and so he may return it. By contrast, in **the latter clause, where** the Jew **did pay him the dinars, it appears as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and therefore he must cast away the object rather than return it. **And Abaye** could have **said to you: The first clause** involves **a mistaken transaction, as he did not know** that the idol was included, **as he did not give him the dinars** and therefore had not yet inspected the property being purchased. Conversely, **the latter clause is not** a case of **a mistaken transaction, as since he** already **gave the dinars** to the seller, **when he** came to **pull it, he should have inspected** the property **and then pulled it.** Since the Jew did not bother to inspect the property even after paying, the transaction is valid. The *baraita* is in fact discussing a case where the transaction was subject to gentile law, according to which only monetary payment effects acquisition, and as for the aforementioned difficulty raised to the opinion of Rabbi Oshaya from the latter clause: If so, why do I need pulling in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol; pulling is not in fact required. Rather, **the *baraita* is in fact discussing a case where it was agreed that the transaction would be subject to Jewish law.** **From** the fact that in **the first clause, pulling does not effect acquisition,** in **the latter clause too, pulling does not effect acquisition. But since the first clause taught** the word: **Pulled,** in order to teach that this method is ineffective, **the latter clause also taught** the word: **Pulled,** despite the fact that its mention is unnecessary. **The *baraita* is discussing a case where it was agreed that the transaction would be subject to Jewish law.** **From** the fact that in **the latter clause, pulling effects acquisition,** in **the first clause too, pulling effects acquisition. And** as for the difficulty with regard to **the first clause,** which states that the Jew may return the item even though he already pulled it, **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: If** the buyer **did not give** the money to the gentile **and** also **did not pull** the item, **he returns** the item to the seller. The Gemara adds: **What** does the term: **Returns,** mean in this context? It means that the Jew **returns,** i.e., reneges, **on** his **verbal** commitment. In other words, he is not required to complete the purchase despite the fact he initially agreed to do so. It was necessary for the *tanna* to teach this explicitly, as **he holds:** Reneging on **a verbal** commitment that was unaccompanied by an act of acquisition **constitutes an act of bad faith** between two parties. **And** he holds that **this statement** applies only to a case where **a Jew** purchases an item **from** another **Jew, as they** are assumed to **uphold their verbal** commitments. **But** in a case of **a Jew** who purchases an item **from gentiles, as they** are **not** assumed **to uphold their verbal** commitments, reneging on one's word does **not** constitute an act of bad faith between the parties, since neither party relies upon the commitment of the other in the first place.

This debate — whether acquisition from a non-Jew is through *kesef* (per the *baraita* and *Rashi*) or through *meshicha* (per *Ameimar* and the overwhelming majority of *Rishonim*) — is of direct practical consequence for the *halakha* of *bechora*, *heter mechira*, and the sale of *chametz*. The accepted practice is to follow *Ameimar*: acquisition from a non-Jew proceeds through *meshicha*, and therefore when selling an animal's ear to exempt from *bechora*, the transaction must be effected through an act of *meshicha*. In practice many *poskim* recommend performing both *kesef* and *meshicha* to cover all positions. This question will continue to be explored in the *shiurim* that follow.
