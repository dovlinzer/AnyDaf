# Yevamot 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Forced Divorce: Nursing

### Child Viability & 30 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא גַּבֵּי מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וּמֵינֶקֶת חֲבֵירוֹ הַנְּשׂוּאָה לְכֹהֵן, מַאי? מִי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא לְכֹהֵן, אוֹ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Here, with regard to a woman who is pregnant with the child of another man or a woman who is nursing the child of another man, who is married to a priest, what is the *halakha*? Did the Sages enact an ordinance for the benefit of a priest and say that it is sufficient for him to separate from his wife and he does not need to divorce her, or not?

The daf opens at 37a with a continuation of the discussion concerning forced divorce in cases where someone married against a rabbinic concern — specifically, the case of a woman who was pregnant or nursing a child from a previous relationship and subsequently remarried. The Gemara had established that such a woman must separate from her new husband, and if she was widowed or divorced she must wait until the child is no longer nursing before remarrying. The question now is what happens when the man who married her in violation of this concern is a *kohen*, for whom a divorce creates a permanent bar to remarriage.

This discussion overlaps with a parallel case involving a *yevamah* whose infant died within thirty days of birth, leaving uncertain whether the child was viable. The relevant background: if the child was born at full term, we assume viability without requiring the thirty-day period. If it was not a full-term pregnancy, we require thirty days to establish viability. Furthermore, the context assumes the child died from an external cause — some accident or tragedy — not a natural death, since a natural death within thirty days would itself be evidence of non-viability. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, in the non-full-term case, a death within thirty days leaves the child's status uncertain, and consequently, the mother's obligation of *chalitza* or *yibbum* remains unclear. If she then remarried, the question of whether to demand *chalitza* from her current situation is live — with the added complication that if she married a *kohen*, demanding *chalitza* at this point would permanently separate them.

### Kohen & Chalitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא רַבָּנַן דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּאָמְרִי: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שְׁהָא וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הָוֵי, גַּבֵּי אֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר — עָבְדִינַן כְּרַבָּנַן,
>
> **Translation:** He said to him: How can these cases be compared? Granted, there, in the case where the offspring died during its first thirty days of life, since there are the Rabbis who disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerning it, as they say that although the offspring did not survive for thirty days it is nevertheless considered a full-fledged, i.e., viable, offspring, therefore, with regard to the wife of a priest, since it is not possible for her to perform *ḥalitza* and remain permitted to her husband, we will be lenient and act in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis.

The Gemara asks: *hacha gabei me'uberet chaveiro umineket chaveiro deniseit lekohen mai?* — what is the ruling when the woman who was pregnant or nursing has married a *kohen*? *Mi avdi Rabbanan takanta lekohen o lo?* — did the Rabbis make some accommodation to avoid forcing a divorce where remarriage would become permanently impossible?

The Gemara responds by sharply distinguishing the two cases: *amar lei hachi hashta?* — "How can you compare them?" In the *yevamah* case, where we refrain from demanding *chalitza*, there is a genuine halakhic position to rely upon. *Keivan de'ika Rabbanan dephaligi aleih deRabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — since there are Rabbis who disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and hold that even a child who did not survive thirty days was a viable child (*velad malya hu*), we are not simply waiving the requirement because of the *kohen*'s predicament. Rather, we are relying on the existing rabbinic opinion — the principle that *kedai hu ploni lismokh alav bish'at hadechak*, a given opinion is worthy of reliance in a time of pressing need. Since demanding *chalitza* would force a permanent separation from her husband, we apply this minority — or in this case, majority — opinion when we need it.

### Why No Exception for Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא, כְּמַאן נַעֲבֵיד? אִי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא אָמַר: יוֹצִיא וְלֹא יַחְזִיר עוֹלָמִית, וְאִי כְּרַבָּנַן — הָאָמְרִי בְּגֵט.
>
> **Translation:** However, here, in the case of a woman who is pregnant with or nursing the child of another man, in accordance with whose opinion should we act? If we act in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, it will be of no benefit to the priest because Rabbi Meir said even with regard to the wife of an Israelite that he must divorce her and may never take her back. And if we act in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, it will also be of no benefit to the priest because they say he must send her out with a bill of divorce and only remarry her at a later point. Since there is no opinion that does not require a bill of divorce to be given, there is no possibility to be lenient in this case by not requiring a bill of divorce to be given.

In the case of the nursing or pregnant woman, however, this logic does not apply. *Aval hacha, keman abdinnan?* — according to which opinion would we refrain from demanding a divorce? If we follow Rabbi Meir, he says *yotzi velo yachzir olamit* — he must divorce and may never remarry. If we follow the *Rabbanan*, they too require a *get*, even if remarriage is later permitted. There is simply no opinion in the debate over the nursing and pregnant case that permits the man to remain married without any divorce at all. We cannot invoke the "time of need" principle where no halakhic opinion actually supports the result we are trying to reach. Accordingly, even when the husband is a *kohen*, the divorce is required — and since the divorce creates a permanent bar to remarriage, the couple is permanently separated. This is a striking result: a *kohen* who technically entered a legitimate marriage, whose only violation was the timing with respect to the nursing period, loses his wife permanently.

## Kiddushin in 3 Months

### 3-Month Wait & Forced Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קִדְּשָׁהּ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה וּבָרַח, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרַפְרָם, חַד אָמַר: מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ, וְחַד אָמַר: עֵירוּקֵיהּ מִסָּתְיֵיהּ. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וַאֲמַר לְהוּ רַפְרָם: עֵירוּקֵיהּ מִסָּתְיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** An amoraic dispute was stated: In a case in which a man betrothed a woman during the three months following her divorce or her husband's death, and then he fled, Rav Aḥa and Rafram disagree over what should be done. One said: We excommunicate him for violating the prohibition. And the other one said: His flight is sufficient for him, since it proves that he does not intend to marry her until it is determined that she is not pregnant. Therefore, there is no need to penalize him further. The Gemara relates: There was an incident like that, and Rafram said to those who asked what to do: His flight is sufficient for him.

The Gemara introduces a related case: *kiddesh b'toch shlosha u'varach* — a man performed *kiddushin* with a woman within the three-month waiting period required after the death or divorce of her prior husband, and then ran away. The rationale for the three-month waiting period is to establish clarity of paternity: if the woman remarries immediately, it will be unclear whose child she is carrying. The question is what happens when someone violated this by performing *kiddushin* within the three months, but did not yet proceed to *nisu'in*.

### Rafram's Running Away

*Pligi bah Rav Acha v'Rafram* — Rav Acha and Rafram disagree. One opinion holds that we place him in *cherem* until he gives a *get*. The assumption is that even *kiddushin* alone within the three-month period is serious enough to compel a forced divorce. The other opinion holds that *arukei mistayei* — his running away suffices. Since the fundamental concern is *nisu'in* — actual cohabitation which could result in a pregnancy and confusion about paternity — and since by running away he has made clear that *nisu'in* will not be taking place anytime soon, we need not pursue him and force a *get*.

The Gemara notes that a practical case came before Rafram, *havei uvda ramia l'Rafram*, and he ruled in accordance with his own position: *arukei mistayei*, the fact that he ran away is sufficient.

This raises the practical question — especially relevant for a *kohen* who performed *kiddushin* within the three-month period — of whether running away solves the problem. For a *kohen*, running away would be far preferable to being compelled to give a *get*, since a *get* would permanently bar him from remarrying this woman.

### Tosafot: Kiddushin Alone

The earlier discussion about the nursing mother gave no indication that *kiddushin* alone, without *nisu'in*, would be any different — the Gemara seemed to demand a *get* regardless, even when the husband was a *kohen*. Tosafot, however, on 36b, cite an interesting leniency. Rabbi Yosef of Orléans (*v'haRav Rabbi Yosef m'Orlinesh*) ruled that a man who performed *kiddushin* with a nursing woman (*b'echad shekiddesh meineket chaveiro*) need not be required to give a *get* — merely separation suffices (*ela hafrasha b'alma*). His reasoning: *keivan d'belav hachi assura lei* — since in any event they are prohibited from cohabiting, because *kallah belo bracha assura l'ba'alah k'niddah* — a bride without the recitation of *sheva brachot* and the consummation of *nisu'in* is forbidden to her husband like a *niddah* — there is no concern that they will live together as husband and wife during the nursing period. Without *nisu'in*, the fundamental problem does not arise, and therefore a formal *get* is not required. This constitutes a significant *kula*, though it does require reconciliation with the later Gemara that implies a *get* is necessary even in the *kohen* scenario.

## Unknown Paternity

### Case: 7 vs 9 Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק בֶּן תֵּשַׁע וְכוּ׳. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: לֵימָא הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב נָשִׁים, וְרוֹב נָשִׁים לְתִשְׁעָה יָלְדָן!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that if a *yevama* consummated the levirate marriage and gave birth seven months later, there is an uncertainty whether the child is nine months old, counting from conception, and is the offspring of the first husband, i.e., the deceased brother, or whether the child is only seven months old and is the offspring of the second husband. If it is the child of the first husband, then there was never any obligation of levirate marriage, and the supposed consummation was in fact forbidden by penalty of *karet*. Due to that possibility, both the man and the woman are obligated to bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty. Rava said to Rav Naḥman: How can they bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty? Let us say: Follow the majority of women, and since the majority of women give birth after nine months, it should be presumed that the child is the offspring of the deceased brother. Accordingly, the couple would be obligated to bring a certain sin-offering, not a guilt-offering for uncertainty.

The Gemara now turns to a fascinating and detailed discussion about a woman who performed *yibbum* with her brother-in-law and subsequently gave birth at a time that left uncertain whether the child was fathered by the first husband or the *yavam*. The scenario: the first husband died, and the woman did *yibbum* before the three-month waiting period had elapsed. She then gave birth at a point in time that was consistent with either a seven-month pregnancy from the *yavam* or a nine-month pregnancy from the first husband. The child is not a *mamzer* in either case — if from the first husband, the child is legitimately the first husband's son; if from the *yavam*, then the *yibbum* was valid and the child is the *yavam*'s legitimate son. But the uncertainty of paternity has important practical consequences for inheritance and for determining whether the *yavam* is currently violating an *issur kares* by continuing to cohabit with his wife.

As Rashi notes, another implication concerns the *chatas* obligation. The Mishnah rules they bring an *asham talui* — the conditional guilt offering brought when one is unsure whether a sin was committed. But if we could establish that the child is indeed from the first husband, then the *yibbum* was clearly invalid, there is no *mitzvah* of *yibbum*, and the *yavam* is definitely cohabiting with an *arayos* — he would be required to bring a definite *chatas kavu'a* rather than a mere *asham talui*.

### Rava's Challenge

*Amar lei Rava l'Rav Nachman* — Rava challenged Rav Nachman: *leima halach achar rov nashim* — why not simply go by the majority? Most women give birth after nine months. If we apply the principle of *rov*, the majority, we should assume this child is a nine-month baby and therefore the son of the first husband. Why is there any doubt at all?

### Not Visibly Pregnant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְשֵׁי דִּידַן, לְשִׁבְעָה יָלְדָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְשֵׁי דִּידְכוּ הָווּ רוּבָּא דְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said to him: The women of our family regularly give birth after seven months. Therefore, how can you presume that this woman gave birth after nine months? Rava said to him: Do the women of your family constitute the majority of the women of the world? Ultimately, the majority of women give birth after nine months, and one should therefore presume accordingly in a case of uncertainty.

Rav Nachman's initial response — *nashei didan beshiva yaldan*, our women give birth after seven months — provoked a sharp rejoinder from Rava: *nashei didichu havu ruba d'alma?* — are the women of your town the majority of the world? Even if a non-trivial minority of women give birth in the seventh month, the majority of women worldwide still give birth after nine months, so why not apply *rov*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא: רוֹב נָשִׁים יָלְדָן לְתִשְׁעָה, וּמִיעוּט לְשִׁבְעָה, וְכׇל הַיּוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה, עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ. וְזוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְלֹא הוּכַּר עוּבָּרָהּ לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ — אִיתְּרַע לֵיהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said to him: This is what I am saying: Although it is true that the majority of women give birth after nine months and only a minority give birth after seven, still, in the case of every woman who gives birth after nine months, her fetus is already recognizable after a third of her days, i.e., in the third month of her pregnancy. Accordingly, in the case of this woman, since her fetus was not recognizable after a third of her days, as were it already recognizable at that point then it would be obvious that the child was the offspring of the first husband, therefore, the ability to presume she is like the majority of women is compromised, and the uncertainty as to who the father of the child is remains. Consequently, the *yavam* and *yevama* should each bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty.

Rav Nachman then refined his answer. He said: *hachi ka'amina* — this is what I mean to say. *Rov nashim yaldan l'tisha* — yes, most women give birth after nine months, *u'miyuta l'shiva* — but a minority give birth in the seventh month. However, there is an additional critical factor: *v'chol hayoledes l'tisha ubra nikar l'shlish yameha* — any woman who will give birth after nine months will show visible signs of pregnancy by the end of the third month.

Now consider the case before us. If this woman had become visibly pregnant three months after the death of her first husband — that is, one month after the *yavam* married her — it would have been immediately apparent that she was pregnant from the first husband, and there would have been no uncertainty. The very existence of the doubt in this case means — implicitly — that she was *not* visibly pregnant three months after the first husband's death. And that fact undermines the *rov*. On the one hand, the majority suggests a nine-month pregnancy from the first husband; on the other hand, if it were a nine-month pregnancy, she would (in most cases) have been visibly pregnant by three months, which she was not. *V'zu ho'il v'lo hukeir ubra l'shlish yameha, itrei lah ruba* — since she was not visibly pregnant after three months, the majority is thrown into question.

### Visible Pregnancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כׇּל הַיּוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ, הָא מִדְּלֹא הוּכַּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ, עוּבָּרָהּ וַדַּאי בַּר שִׁבְעָה לְבָתְרָאָה הוּא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: רוֹב הַיּוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה — עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ, וְהַאי מִדְּלֹא הוּכַּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ — אִיתְּרַע לֵיהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If it is true that in the case of every woman who gives birth after nine months, her fetus is already recognizable after a third of her days, then with regard to this woman, from the fact that her fetus was not recognized after a third of her days, it follows that her fetus was certainly only seven months old and is the offspring of the latter husband, i.e., the *yavam*. If so, it is clear the *yavam* is the father of the child and there should be no need to bring an offering at all. Rather, one must emend Rav Naḥman's words and say: In the majority of cases, with regard to a woman who gives birth after nine months, her fetus is already recognizable after a third of her days, and with regard to this woman, from the fact that her fetus was not recognized after a third of her days, the ability to presume she is like the majority of women is compromised.

The Gemara presses the logic further. If every woman carrying a nine-month baby is visibly pregnant after three months (*kol hayoledes l'tisha ubra nikar l'shisha meha*), then the absence of visible pregnancy after three months would be conclusive proof in the *opposite* direction — we should know *for certain* that this is a seven-month baby from the *yavam*. *Ha me'delo u'kar l'shlisha meha, vadai bar sheva l'vastra hu* — if she was not visibly pregnant after three months, certainly the child is from the second husband.

The Gemara therefore amends the formulation: *ela eima rov* — say rather that *most* women carrying nine-month babies show visible pregnancy after three months, but not all. This yields the correct result: the absence of visible pregnancy at three months undermines but does not eliminate the *rov* that most babies are nine-month babies. We thus have two countervailing considerations — the *rov* pointing toward a nine-month pregnancy from the first husband, and the absence of visible pregnancy at three months creating serious doubt about that. The result is a genuine *safek*, and that is why the Mishnah treats the child's paternity as unknown.

## First & Second Child

### First: Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רִאשׁוֹן רָאוּי לִהְיוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְשֵׁנִי מַמְזֵר מִסָּפֵק. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַמְזֵר מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: The first child is even fit to become a High Priest. However, since it is possible that the child is the offspring of the deceased husband, in which case the widow remains forbidden to the *yavam* as his brother's wife, if she has a second child with her *yavam* then that child is a *mamzer* due to an uncertainty with regard to his status. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: One is not rendered a *mamzer* due to uncertainty.

The Gemara now turns to the legal consequences for the children born into this uncertain situation. The *Baraita* states: *rishon ra'uy lihyot kohen gadol* — the first child born in this ambiguous situation is fit to be a *kohen gadol*. Since both brothers are *kohanim* — if one is a *kohen* the other necessarily is as well — the child is certainly a *kohen* regardless of which man is his father. Moreover, the child was born within a legitimate marriage in any case: either he is the first husband's child, entirely legitimate; or he is the *yavam*'s child from a valid *yibbum*, equally legitimate. Tosafot raises the question of whether a *kohen* must be *miyuchas* — with fully known paternity — to serve as *kohen gadol*, but concludes that the requirement of *miyuchas* applies only where there is a question of illegitimate birth, not where the child was unquestionably born within marriage and the only uncertainty is which of two legitimate marriages he derives from.

### Second: Safek Mamzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רִאשׁוֹן רָאוּי לִהְיוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְשֵׁנִי סְפֵק מַמְזֵר, וְאָסוּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ סְפֵק מַמְזֵר, אֶלָּא וַדַּאי מַמְזֵר, וּמוּתָּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** What is the *baraita* saying? Abaye said: This is what it is saying: The first child is even fit to become a High Priest. And if she has a second child, his status as a *mamzer* is uncertain and therefore he is both prohibited from marrying an Israelite woman of unflawed lineage, since he might actually be a *mamzer*, and he is also prohibited from marrying a *mamzeret*, since he might not be a *mamzer*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: He is not treated like one whose status as a *mamzer* is uncertain; rather, due to the uncertainty concerning his status he is treated like one who is definitely a *mamzer*, and he is permitted to marry a *mamzeret*. In other words, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov holds that even one whose status as a *mamzer* is uncertain is permitted to marry one who is definitely a *mamzeret*.

The *Baraita* continues: *v'sheni mamzer misafek* — but a second child, born after the first, is a doubtful *mamzer*. The reasoning: if the first child was indeed the first husband's son, then there was no obligation of *yibbum* and the *yavam* was living with a woman who is *arayos* to him. Any subsequent child born of that relationship would be a *mamzer*. If the first child was the *yavam*'s own child from a valid *yibbum*, then a subsequent child would also be legitimate. Since we do not know which is the case, the second child is a *safek mamzer*.

The striking contrast is thus established: the first child could be a *kohen gadol*, and yet his sibling is a *safek mamzer* — perhaps the most legally disadvantaged status in Jewish law.

## Safek Mamzer Debate

### Tanna Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רִאשׁוֹן רָאוּי לִהְיוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְשֵׁנִי מַמְזֵר וַדַּאי מִסָּפֵק וּמוּתָּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֵין וַדַּאי מַמְזֵר מִסָּפֵק. אֶלָּא סְפֵק מַמְזֵר, וְאָסוּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: This is what the *baraita* is saying: The first child is even fit to become a High Priest. And if she has a second child, he is treated as though he is definitely a *mamzer* due to the uncertainty concerning his status, and therefore he is permitted to marry a *mamzeret*, i.e., this *tanna* holds that even one whose status as a *mamzer* is uncertain is permitted to marry one who is definitely a *mamzeret*. And Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: He is not treated as though he is definitely a *mamzer* due to an uncertainty concerning his status; rather, his status as a *mamzer* is uncertain and he is treated accordingly, and therefore he is both prohibited from marrying an Israelite woman of unflawed lineage since he might be a *mamzer*, and he is also prohibited from marrying a *mamzeret* since he might not be a *mamzer*.

The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is that the second child is a *safek mamzer*, and as such is *asur b'mamzeret* — he cannot marry even a *mamzeret*. The logic of this is painful but straightforward: a *safek mamzer* cannot marry a regular *Yisrael* because he might be a *mamzer*; he cannot marry a *mamzeret* because he might not be a *mamzer*, and a *mamzeret* is forbidden to one who is not a *mamzer*. He is caught on both sides.

### R. Eliezer: Treat as Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: וַדָּאָן בְּוַדָּאָן — מוּתָּר, וַדָּאָן בִּסְפֵקָן, וּסְפֵקָן בְּוַדָּאָן, וּסְפֵקָן בִּסְפֵקָן — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** And Abaye and Rava disagree with regard to whether the *halakha* is decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. As we learned in a mishna: With regard to the prohibition against marrying people with certain types of flawed lineage, Rabbi Elazar said: The marriage of those whose flawed lineage status is certain to those whose status is certain is permitted, but the marriage of those whose status is certain to those whose status is uncertain, and the marriage of those whose status is uncertain to those whose status is certain, and even the marriage of those whose status is uncertain to those whose status is uncertain, is prohibited.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov states: *ein mamzer misafek* — there is no such thing, halakhically, as a *mamzer from doubt*. This is not a factual dispute — he agrees the child's status is uncertain. It is a halakhic dispute about how to categorize and treat a person of doubtful *mamzer* status. His position, as elaborated by the Gemara, is *vadai mamzer u'mutar b'mamzeret* — we treat him as a definite *mamzer*, and therefore he is permitted to marry a *mamzeret*. The reasoning: the verse *lo yavo mamzer b'kehal Hashem* is understood to forbid entry into the "unblemished community." A person about whom there is a question mark — even if he may in truth be completely untainted — is by definition not part of the category of the "unblemished." The doubt itself removes him from the pristine category and places him in the category of the *mamzer*, so he may marry within that category. Thus, saying *vadai mamzer* is not a factual claim but a halakhic assignment: given the doubt, we treat him as a full *mamzer*, which is paradoxically a *leniency*.

### Abaye vs Rava Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן סְפֵקָן: שְׁתוּקִי וַאֲסוּפִי וְכוּתִי.
>
> **Translation:** And these are those who are considered to have an uncertain status: A child of unknown paternity [*shetuki*], although his mother's identity is known; and a foundling who was found abandoned in the streets; and a Samaritan [*Kuti*], who is possibly a *mamzer* since the Samaritans do not accept and abide by the *halakhot* of marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לִי: הִלֵּל שָׁנָה — עֲשָׂרָה יוּחֲסִין עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל: כָּהֲנֵי, לְוִיֵּי, וְיִשְׂרְאֵלֵי, חֲלָלֵי, גֵּירֵי, חֲרוֹרֵי, מַמְזֵרֵי, נְתִינֵי, שְׁתוּקֵי וַאֲסוּפֵי. וְכוּלָּן מוּתָּרִין לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** And with regard to this mishna Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. But when I said this *halakha* of Rav's in the presence of Shmuel, he said to me: Hillel taught in a *baraita* that ten categories of lineage came up from Babylon to Eretz Yisrael: Priests; Levites; and Israelites; priests disqualified due to flawed lineage [*ḥalallim*]; converts; freed slaves; *mamzerim*; Gibeonites; *shetukei*; and foundlings. And it is permitted for all men and women in these categories to marry one another, i.e., the list is arranged such that the marriage between people in any two categories that are adjacent to one another is permitted. This is possible only if one assumes that it is permitted for one whose flawed lineage status is uncertain to marry one whose flawed lineage status is certain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר!
>
> **Translation:** After citing the baraita taught by Hillel, which assumes that it is permitted for one whose flawed lineage status is uncertain to marry one whose flawed lineage status is certain, Shmuel concluded: The *halakha* is certainly decided in accordance with the opinion of Hillel, and yet you, Rabbi Yehuda, said the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, which states that a marriage between two people whose flawed lineage status is uncertain is prohibited; your ruling is incorrect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי סָבַר לַהּ כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּהִלֵּל, וּמוֹקֵי לַהּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אַלִּיבָּא דְהִלְכְתָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּקְשֵׁי הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye holds in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel, who said that the *halakha* is decided in accordance with the opinion of Hillel that it is permitted for one whose flawed lineage status is uncertain to marry one whose flawed lineage status is certain. Therefore, Abaye establishes that opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov to be in accordance with this *halakha*, in order that there should not be a contradiction between one *halakha*, i.e., that the *halakha* is always decided in accordance with the opinion of Hillel, and another *halakha*, i.e., that the *halakha* is always decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב, דְּאָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וּמוֹקֵי לַהּ לִדְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אַלִּיבָּא דְּהִלְכְתָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּקְשֵׁי הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava, however, holds in accordance with the opinion of Rav, who said: The *halakha* is decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar that the marriage of those whose status is certain to those whose status is uncertain is prohibited. Therefore, Rava establishes that opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov to be in accordance with this *halakha*, so that there should not be a contradiction between one *halakha*, i.e., that the *halakha* in this case is in accordance with the opinion of Rav, and another *halakha*, i.e., that the *halakha* is always decided in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.

The question is then raised: who in this dispute is saying what? The Gemara notes that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Tanna Kamma* is parallel to a debate recorded in the name of Rabbi Elazar, who states: *vadan be'vadan muttar* — a definite *mamzer* may marry a definite *mamzer*; but *vadan bi'sfekan, sfekan be'vadan, sfekan be'sfekan asur* — a definite *mamzer* may not marry a *safek mamzer*, and two *safek mamzerim* may not marry each other. The categories of *safek* mentioned by Rabbi Elazar are *shtuki* (a child whose mother is known but whose father is not — she deflects the question), *asufi* (a foundling whose parents are unknown), and *kuti* (a Samaritan, since Samaritan practice regarding *kiddushin* differs from halakha and their children may unknowingly be *mamzerim*).

Rav Yehuda quoted Rav ruling *halacha ke'Rabbi Elazar* — that a *safek mamzer* is *asur*, cannot marry either a regular *Yisrael* or a definite *mamzer*. But when Rav Yehuda reported this ruling to Shmuel, Shmuel responded: *Hillel shana asara yuchasin* — Hillel taught that there are ten genealogical categories that came up from Babylonia, and all of them can intermarry in some combination (*v'chulan mutarin lavo zeh ba'zeh*). If a *safek mamzer* could marry no one at all, Hillel's teaching would be internally incoherent, for there would be no valid marriage partner for the *safek* categories.

This sets the stage for the central interpretive dispute. *Abaye savar lah k'Shmuel, d'amar halacha k'Hillel* — Abaye holds like Shmuel that we rule like Hillel, meaning a *safek mamzer* is *mutar* to marry another *mamzer*, and therefore he reads Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov as the one who is *lenient* — the one who says *vadai mamzer u'mutar b'mamzeret* — in order that the halakhic ruling in favor of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov not conflict with the ruling like Hillel. *Rava savar lah k'Rav, d'amar halacha ke'Rabbi Elazar* — Rava holds like Rav that the *safek mamzer* is *asur*, and he therefore reads Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov as the one who is *stringent* — the one who says *safek mamzer ve'asur b'mamzeret* — so that the ruling in favor of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov aligns with the ruling like Rabbi Elazar. Each Amora interprets the ambiguous dispute of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov so that it coincides with the halakhic ruling he holds, in order to avoid an internal contradiction between rulings.

## Ten Categories

### Halacha Ruling

The operative ruling is stringent: we rule that a *safek mamzer* is *assur bisafek, assur b'mamzeret, ve-assur b'Yisrael* — prohibited on all sides.

### Hillel's Ten

Hillel's teaching lists ten genealogical categories that ascended from Babylonia: *kohanim, levi'im, yisraelim, chalalim* (a *kohen* who lost his priestly sanctity through prohibited union, such as the son of a *kohen* and a *grusha*), *geirim* (converts), *meshucharim* (freed slaves), *mamzerim, nesinim* (a group reduced to servitude in the time of Yehoshua or David, whose status as Jews is uncertain), *shtuki*, and *asufi*.

### Marriage Groups

Hillel's statement that all these groups can intermarry (*v'chulan mutarin lavo zeh ba'zeh*) does not mean that every group may marry every other group — it means that one can identify overlapping sub-groupings within which marriage is permitted. A male *kohen* cannot marry a convert; but *kohen*, *levi*, and *yisrael* may marry within their own group, and *chalal* may marry within that group as well. *Yisrael*, *chalal*, *ger*, and *meshuchrar* may marry one another. *Mamzer*, *ger*, and *meshuchrar* may marry one another. And the question is where the *safek* categories of *shtuki* and *asufi* fit.

If a *safek mamzer* is *mutar* to marry a definite *mamzer*, then the *shtuki* and *asufi* can marry *mamzer*, *ger*, and *meshuchrar* — an expansive sub-group. If, however, a *safek mamzer* is *asur* even to a definite *mamzer*, then the only option is *geirim*, but to reach *geirim* on the list, one would have to jump over *mamzer* and *ner*, which the Gemara does not contemplate. The *Rambam* (Hilchot Issurei Bi'ah 15:21) clarifies the underlying structure: *d'oraita*, a *safek mamzer* may actually marry into the general Jewish community, since the verse *lo yavo mamzer b'kehal Hashem* excludes only a *vadai mamzer* (*mamzer vaday assur lavo b'kahal v'lo safek*). Rabbinically, however, the sages enacted a higher standard for *yuchsin* and prohibited *safek mamzerim* from marrying *Yisrael* as well. A *vadai mamzer* may marry a *vadai mamzeret*. But a *safek mamzer, shtuki*, or *asufi* is forbidden to marry a *bas Yisrael* and, the *Rambam* rules, *v'assur lisa mamzeres, v'afilu mamzeres mi-safek assura lo* — even a *mamzeret misafek* is forbidden, lest one be a *mamzer vaday* and the other not. *V'ein li-sfekos elu takanah* — there is no remedy for such persons, except marriage to *geirim*. And even then, *vehavlad holech achar hapgam* — the child carries the same compromised status as the parent, so the problem is never fully resolved.

## R. Eliezer's Teachings

### World Filled Mamzerim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ דְּכֹל סְפֵיקָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב — כְּוַדַּאי מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי שֶׁבָּא עַל נָשִׁים הַרְבֵּה וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ עַל אֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן בָּא, וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁבָּאוּ עָלֶיהָ אֲנָשִׁים הַרְבֵּה, וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן קִבְּלָה — נִמְצָא אָב נוֹשֵׂא אֶת בִּתּוֹ, וְאָח נוֹשֵׂא אֶת אֲחוֹתוֹ, וְנִתְמַלֵּא כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מַמְזֵרִין. וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּמָלְאָה הָאָרֶץ זִמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: From where do I say that concerning anyone whose status as a *mamzer* is uncertain, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov they are treated equivalently to one who is definitely a *mamzer*? As it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: With regard to one who engaged in intercourse with and impregnated many women, but he does not know with which women he had intercourse, and similarly, with regard to a woman, if many men had intercourse with her and she became pregnant, but she does not know from which man she received the seed that caused her to become pregnant, since the identities of the parents of those children are not known, it could emerge that a father marries his daughter, and a brother marries his sister. And in this way, the entire world could become filled with *mamzerim*. And concerning this, it is stated: "And lest the land become full of lewdness" (Leviticus 19:29). Abaye demonstrates his claim from the fact that even though it is not certain that the children in this situation are *mamzerim*, nevertheless, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov labels them as *mamzerim* and not as those whose status as a *mamzer* is uncertain.

This teaching is important on its own terms. It touches on the question of whether casual or promiscuous sexual relations outside marriage — even where neither party is formally married and thus no *mamzer* status would technically result from adultery — are nonetheless forbidden. The *Rambam* holds that any sexual union outside the context of marriage falls under the prohibition of *kedeshah*. Even for those who permit a *pilegesh* relationship in the context of commitment, it is clear from this passage that casual, indiscriminate sexual activity is the source of the prohibition *umalah ha-arets zimah* — the concern that the world will be filled with prohibited relationships, since no one knows who is related to whom.

But for our purposes, Abaye draws his proof from the phrasing: Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov calls these children *mamzerim* even though technically they are *safek mamzerim*. This, says Abaye, demonstrates that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov treats *safek* status as *vadai* for halakhic purposes — which in context means a *leniency*, since a *vadai mamzer* may marry a *mamzeret*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״זוֹ מָה הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** And Rava could have said to you: This is what the verse is saying: The word "lewdness [*zima*]" can be understood as an acronym of the words: *Zo ma hi*, meaning: What is this. It is plausible to say that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov's citation of this verse indicates that he regards their status to be uncertain.

Rava disputes this reading. He says: *hachi ka'amar* — when Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says the world will be filled with *mamzerim*, he does not mean they will be halakhically categorized as *vadai mamzerim*. Rather, *zimah* in that verse is to be read as a *roshei teivos* (*ro'shei teivos*): *zo ma hi* — "who is this person?" — meaning the world will be filled with people of unknown parentage, genuine *safek mamzerim* whose identity is uncertain. There is no halakhic assignment of *vadai* status; there is only a description of a world in which doubt and uncertainty pervade.

### Different Countries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר עַל כֵּן, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם אִשָּׁה בִּמְדִינָה זוֹ וְיֵלֵךְ וְיִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה בִּמְדִינָה אַחֶרֶת, שֶׁמָּא יִזְדַּוְּוגוּ זֶה לָזֶה, וְנִמְצָא אָח נוֹשֵׂא אֶת אֲחוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov said: A man should not marry a woman in this country and then go and marry another woman in a different country, lest a son from one marriage and a daughter from the other, unaware that they are both children of the same father, unite with one another, and it could emerge that a brother marries his sister, the children of whom would be *mamzerim*.

The Gemara continues with additional teachings of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. *Lo yisa adam isha mi-medinah zu v'yelech v'yisa isha mi-medinah acheres* — a man should not marry a woman in one country and then go and marry a woman in another country. *Shema yizdavgu zeh l'zeh* — in an era without telecommunications or systematic record-keeping, the children of the two wives may one day meet and, with no awareness that they share a father, a brother may marry a sister. *Ve-nimtza ach nosei et achoto* — the result would be incest. The problem is not limited to illegitimate unions; it arises even from legitimate marriages when children are raised in ignorance of their full family structure.

### Rav's Temp Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא רַב כִּי אִיקְּלַע לְדַרְדְּשִׁיר, [מַכְרֵיז] וְאָמַר: מַאן הָוְיָא לְיוֹמָא. וְרַב נַחְמָן כִּי אִיקְּלַע לְשַׁכְנְצִיב, [מַכְרֵיז] וְאָמַר: מַאן הָוְיָא לְיוֹמָא!
>
> **Translation:** Is that so; is there really such a prohibition? But didn't Rav, when he happened to come to Dardeshir, make a public announcement saying: Which woman will be my wife for the day, i.e., for the duration of his visit? Since his wife did not accompany him to Dardeshir, he wished to be married to another woman while he was there, in order to avoid a situation that could lead him to having forbidden thoughts. And also Rav Naḥman, when he happened to come to Shakhnetziv, make a public announcement saying: Which woman will be my wife for the day?

One might object that the great Amoraim themselves seem to have practiced something like this. When Rav would travel to Darshier, he would announce: *man havya lei leyoma* — who wishes to be my wife for the day? Similarly, Rav Nachman, when he came to Shnichziv, would make the same announcement. These were not permanent unions but temporary marriages contracted for the duration of a visit. Did they not risk exactly the kind of confusion Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov warned against?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי רַבָּנַן, דִּפְקִיעַ שְׁמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Sages are different, as their names are renowned, and therefore their children are always identified by their connection to their father. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's concern does not apply to them.

The Gemara answers: *shani rabbanan de-faki'a shemayhu* — great Torah scholars are so famous that their travels and temporary arrangements are publicly known. A child born of such a union would know: my mother was Rav's wife during his visit to this town. There would be no mystery about paternity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: תְּבָעוּהָ לִינָּשֵׂא וְנִתְפַּיְּיסָה — צְרִיכָה לֵישֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים?
>
> **Translation:** But didn't Rava say: With regard to a woman who had an offer of marriage and accepted, the emotional excitement may have caused her to have a flow of menstrual blood, which would make her ritually impure and prohibit her from engaging in intercourse. Even if she was unaware of any flow, she must consider the possibility that it occurred. To purify herself, she needs to wait seven consecutive days that are clean from any flow of menstrual blood and then immerse in a ritual bath. Only then may she marry. If so, how could Rav and Rav Naḥman marry women on the day they arrived?

A further question arises from Rava's ruling that *tevu'a linasei* — if a woman's *chatan* suddenly advanced the date of *nisu'in* without her prior expectation — *ve-nifaysa*, and she agreed — *tzrikha leishev shiva neki'im* — she must count seven clean days and go to the *mikvah*. The Gemara's assumption is that the sudden prospect of imminent intimacy may cause a woman to experience discharge sufficient to render her a *niddah*. If that is the halakha, how could Rav and Rav Nachman arrange same-day marriages?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן שְׁלוּחַיְיהוּ הֲווֹ מְשַׁדְּרִי וּמוֹדְעִי לְהוּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְרַבָּנַן, יַחוֹדֵי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דִּמְיַיחֲדִי לְהוּ, דְּאָמַר מָר: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ לְמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** These Sages would send messengers seven days ahead of their arrival and they would inform the women of the Sage's arrival. In this way, the woman who agreed to marry the Sage would have time to count the seven clean days. And if you wish, say that the Sages' intentions were merely to be in seclusion [*meyaḥadi*] with the woman but not to engage in intercourse with her. Therefore, it was permitted to marry her even if she became ritually impure. Being in seclusion with a woman was sufficient to help the Sages avoid any forbidden thoughts, as the Master said: One who has bread in his basket is incomparable to one who does not have bread in his basket, i.e., just as the knowledge that food is readily available is sufficient to psychologically alleviate one's feelings of hunger, so too, the knowledge that one's sexual desires could be met lessens the strength of the desire itself.

The Gemara offers two answers. First, *rabbanan shluchei havu meshaderu u'modu lehu* — they would send advance notice, giving the woman adequate time to prepare and immerse, so that there would be a seven-day lead time. Second, *ve-iba'is eima, le-rabbanan yichuda be'alma hu de-meyachadi lehu* — alternatively, these arrangements involved *yichud* alone, not actual cohabitation. The Gemara then cites the principle *eino domeh mi she-yesh lo pas be-salo le-mi she-ein lo pas be-salo* — it is not the same to have bread in one's basket as to have none; the mere availability and closeness of a woman addresses the scholar's impulse even without consummation. The Rishonim offer various explanations for how *yichud* in this context could be permitted and how it might differ from the *tevu'a* scenario — some suggesting that a gradual, non-sudden transition to intimacy (as in *yichud* without an explicit announcement of imminent relations) would not cause the physiological response that triggers the concern.

### Don't Plan Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם אִשְׁתּוֹ וְדַעְתּוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תַּחֲרֹשׁ עַל רֵעֲךָ רָעָה וְהוּא יוֹשֵׁב לָבֶטַח אִתָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A man should not marry his wife when at the same time his intention is to divorce her, because it is stated: "Do not devise evil against your neighbor, as he dwells securely with you" (Proverbs 3:29). It is wrong for one to intend to undermine the feelings of security that another has with him.

A final teaching of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: *lo yisa adam et isha v'daato l'garsha* — a man should not marry a woman — or remain married to and cohabit with a woman — while planning to divorce her. *Mishum shene'emar: al tachrosh al re'acha ra'a v'hu yoshev lavetach itach* — as it is said, "Do not plot evil against your neighbor while he dwells in security with you." The treachery of a man who continues to enjoy the benefits of marriage — the companionship, the intimacy, the shared life — while privately planning to end it, without any transparency toward his wife, is a fundamental betrayal of trust. The verse addresses the corruption of such deceptive planning and demands that one not weaponize another person's confidence.

## Inheritance Dispute

### Safek vs Yavam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק וְיָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי מִיתָנָא, סָפֵק אָמַר: אֲנָא בַּר מִיתָנָא הוּא, וְנִכְסֵי דִּידִי (הוּא) [נִינְהוּ]. וְיָבָם אָמַר: אַתְּ בְּרַאי דִּידִי אַתְּ, וְלֵית לָךְ וְלָא מִידֵּי בְּנִכְסֵי. הָוֵי מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק, וּמָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק — חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The one of uncertain descent said: I am the son of the deceased, and therefore, as the only heir, his possessions are mine. And the *yavam* said to him: You are my son, and you have absolutely no rights to the possessions; rather, by virtue of the fact that I consummated the levirate marriage with the widow of the deceased, I should inherit him. The Gemara rules on this case: This is a case of property of uncertain ownership, as there is no way to determine who is the rightful heir, and the *halakha* is that property of uncertain ownership the claimants divide up between them.

The Gemara now turns to the property implications of the uncertain paternity case. The child of unknown paternity — let us call him Gershon — and the *yavam* (let us call him Shimon) come to divide the estate of the deceased first husband (Reuven). Gershon claims: *ana bar mitna hu, v'nichsei didi hu* — I am Reuven's son, and the entire estate is mine. Shimon the *yavam* claims: *at beri didi at, v'leit lach v'lo midi b'nichsei* — you are my son, and you have no share in Reuven's estate. Both claims are entirely dependent on the unresolvable question of paternity; neither party has *chazakah* — prior possession — of the property, since Reuven held it and both Gershon and Shimon are claiming from Reuven's estate equally. This is *mamon hamutal b'safek* — money in doubtful ownership — and the rule is *cholkin*, they divide.

### Safek vs Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק וּבְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי מִיתָנָא, סָפֵק אָמַר: הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בַּר מִיתָנָא הוּא, וְנִכְסֵי דִּידִי (הוּא) [נִינְהוּ]. בְּנֵי יָבָם אָמְרִי: אַתְּ (אַחֵינוּ) [אֲחוּנָא] אַתְּ, וּמְנָתָא הוּא דְּאִית לָךְ בַּהֲדַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara brings another case, that of one concerning whom there is uncertainty whether he is the son of the deceased or of the *yavam* and the sons of the *yavam*, who consummated the levirate marriage with the *yevama* and has since died, who came to divide up the possessions of the deceased, and each one makes claim to the inheritance. The one of uncertain descent said: That man, referring to himself, is the son of the deceased, and therefore, as his sole heir, his possessions are mine. And the sons of the *yavam* said to him: You are our brother, and our uncle, the deceased, was not survived by any offspring and so by virtue of our father's levirate marriage he inherited our uncle's possessions, and now that our father has died and we are dividing up his possessions you have a right to inherit only a portion of the inheritance together with us.

A second scenario: suppose Shimon himself has since died, leaving his own sons — Kehat and Merari. Now Gershon comes to divide Reuven's estate not with Shimon but with Shimon's sons. Gershon claims the entire estate as Reuven's son; Shimon's sons claim that Gershon is their brother, Shimon inherited Reuven's estate through valid *yibbum*, Shimon subsequently died, and therefore the three of them — Gershon, Kehat, and Merari — should divide it equally, with each getting a third.

### Mishnah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא לְמֵימַר: מַתְנִיתִין הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הוּא אֵינוֹ יוֹרֵשׁ אוֹתָם, וְהֵם יוֹרְשִׁין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis who studied before Rav Mesharshiyya thought to say: This case is analogous to a case in a mishna, as we learned a similar case in a mishna in which a woman gave birth shortly after remarrying and there is uncertainty whether the child's father is the first or second husband. The mishna considers a case in which the husbands died and were each survived by a set of sons: If a son from either set died, the other sons of that set will inherit from him because as brothers they have an uncontested claim to the inheritance. However, he, the son of uncertain descent, does not inherit from them because his claim as a brother is uncertain and is therefore not powerful enough to allow him to take part of the inheritance from the other sons. However, if the son of uncertain descent died, they, the sons of both husbands, will jointly inherit from him. The claims of each set of sons to be his brothers are equally uncertain; therefore, since there is no one who has a definite claim to his inheritance, his possessions are split between them.

The Rabbis before Rav Mesharsheya thought to apply the principle of a different Mishnah: *hu eino yoresh otam v'hem yorshim oto* — he does not inherit them but they inherit him. That Mishnah involves a child of unknown paternity — say, the son of either Yehuda or Barak — who comes to claim a portion of Yehuda's estate. Yehuda's children say: you are Barak's son, go claim from him. He goes to Barak's children and they say: you are Yehuda's son, go claim from him. Each party deflects him to the other, *ayti ra'aya u'shkol* — bring proof and collect. Since he cannot prove his case, he collects nothing from either side. But when he dies, both families inherit from him, since each is a possible sibling. By analogy, the Rabbis suggested, Gershon should likewise get nothing — they will each tell him to prove his case, and he cannot.

### Both Parties Doubtful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, אִינְהוּ וַדַּאי וְאִיהוּ סָפֵק. הָכָא, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mesharshiyya said to them: Is the case in the mishna really comparable? There, in the mishna's case, when one of the sons dies, they, the other sons in that set, have a definite claim to the inheritance, since their claim is based on the fact that they are the dead son's brothers, which is certainly true, and he, the son of uncertain descent, only has an uncertain claim. However, here, each party has only an uncertain claim. Although the son of uncertain descent claims that ultimately, whatever the nature of his relationship with the deceased is, he should have the right to inherit, nevertheless, since it is not actually known what that relationship is, his claim in reality is merely a composite of uncertain claims.

Rav Mesharsheya rejects this comparison: *mi dami?* — the cases are not comparable. *Hatam inhu vaday v'ihu safek* — in the Mishnah case, each family he approaches has a definite, unquestionable claim as legitimate children of their father, and he alone has the uncertain claim. They are *vaday*; he is *safek*. They can therefore say: *ayti ra'aya* — you want from us, prove you belong. *Hacha idi v'idi safek* — here, however, all parties are in some sense doubtful. Gershon's claim is uncertain, but so is the claim of Shimon's sons, since they can only inherit Reuven's estate through Shimon's *yibbum*, and that *yibbum* is only valid if Gershon is not Reuven's son. Rav Mesharsheya adds — and Rashi amplifies this — that Gershon's claim, even if he is certain to have some stake in the property, rests on an uncertain basis: he cannot say whether he holds it as Reuven's son or as Shimon's son. The entirety of his claim is without a definite legal foundation, unlike the *vaday* claimants in the Mishnah case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי דָּמְיָא לְמַתְנִיתִין — [לְהָא] דָּמְיָא: לְסָפֵק וּבְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחְלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי יָבָם גּוּפֵיהּ, דְּהָתָם אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דַּאֲחוּנָא אַתְּ וּשְׁקוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** Having rejected the analogy offered by the Rabbis, Rav Mesharshiyya offers his own analogy to the case in the mishna that the Rabbis cited: Rather, if there is a case that is analogous to the case in the mishna, then it is to this following case that it is analogous: It is comparable to a case in which following the levirate marriage a son was born, and there is uncertainty whether he is the son of the deceased or of the *yavam*, and that son of uncertain descent and the sons of the *yavam* come to divide up the possessions of the *yavam* himself. As there, those who are unquestionably the sons of the *yavam* have a definite claim; therefore, they can say to him, the son of uncertain descent: Bring proof that you are actually our brother and only then can you take a portion. Since he cannot prove this, he will not receive any of the inheritance.

Rav Mesharsheya then notes what the present scenario actually does resemble: *l'safek u'vnei yavam sheba'u lachloke b'nichsei yavam gufei* — it resembles a case where Gershon and Shimon's sons come to divide *Shimon*'s estate, not Reuven's. There, Gershon claims to be their brother and demands a portion of their father Shimon's estate. They respond: *ayti ra'aya de'achuna at u'shkol* — prove you are our brother and collect. Since Gershon cannot prove paternity, he gets nothing from Shimon's estate, exactly parallel to the Mishnah.

### Rashi's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק וּבְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי סָבָא. סָפֵק אָמַר: הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בַּר מִיתָנָא הוּא, וּפַלְגָא דִּידִי הוּא. יָבָם אָמַר: אַתְּ, בְּרַאי דִּידִי אַתְּ, וְלֵית לָךְ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara brings another case, that of one of uncertain descent, who is either the son of the deceased or the son of the *yavam*, and the *yavam* who came to divide up the possessions of the grandfather, i.e., the father of the *yavam* and the deceased, and each one made a claim to the inheritance. The son of uncertain descent said: That man, referring to himself, is the son of the deceased, and therefore half of the possessions are mine because the inheritance should be split between the two sons, i.e., the deceased and the *yavam*, and since I am the sole heir of the deceased, I should receive his portion. The *yavam* said to him: You are my son, and therefore you have absolutely no rights to the possessions; rather, I should receive all the inheritance. One half is mine because I am the grandfather's son, and the other half, which would have gone to my deceased brother, I should receive by virtue of the fact that I consummated the levirate marriage with his widow.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי יָבָם וַדַּאי, וְסָפֵק סָפֵק, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rules on this case: This is a case in which the *yavam* has a definite claim because he is the grandfather's son, and the son of uncertain descent has only an uncertain claim, and the *halakha* is that one with an uncertain claim cannot extract property from one who has a definite claim to it. Therefore, the *yavam* receives all the inheritance.

For the original question — Gershon and Shimon's sons dividing Reuven's estate — Rashi proposes the following resolution. *Venireh be'einai, dehu mantsa dekamodu leh shakil* — the portion that all parties concede Gershon is entitled to, he takes outright. Since both Gershon and Shimon's sons would concede that Gershon holds at least a one-third share (Shimon's sons' own claim is that Gershon gets a third as a co-heir; Gershon claims the whole), that one-third is undisputed and goes directly to Gershon. *Ve'idach havei mamon hamutal besafek vecholkin* — the remaining two-thirds are in genuine dispute: Gershon claims all of it, Shimon's sons claim all of it as their father's inheritance. That disputed portion is divided equally, with Gershon receiving half and Shimon's sons receiving half between them. The practical result: Gershon receives the undisputed third plus half of the disputed two-thirds, giving him two-thirds of the total estate, while Shimon's sons together receive the remaining third.
