# Hullin 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fetus Tumah Status

### Recap: Mother's Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בֶּן חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים וְחוֹרֵשׁ בַּשָּׂדֶה – שְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ מְטַהַרְתּוֹ. קְרָעָהּ וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי – טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁחֲטָה אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says: Even** if the fetus emerged alive and **is** now **five years old and plowing in the field,** the earlier **slaughter of its mother rendered it permitted** and it does not require slaughter before it is eaten. But if **one tore** an animal, i.e., he killed it without slaughtering it, **and** inside **he found a live nine-**month**-old** fetus, everyone agrees that the fetus **requires** its own **slaughter because its mother was not slaughtered.**

The daf opens at 74b, where we have been discussing the case of a fetus found alive in its mother's womb after the mother has been *shechted*. We have already established the dominant rabbinic position — against Rabbi Meir — that the mother's *shechita* works for the fetus. The *gemara* then probed how completely the fetus is treated as meat: on one hand, its fat (*chelev*) and the *gid hanasheh* are not forbidden as they would be in an independent animal, since the fetus is treated like a limb of the mother; on the other hand, there is a prohibition of blood. For matters outside the domain of food — such as plowing with a donkey, or questions of redeeming a firstborn donkey — the fetus is treated as a live animal in its own right. With that background in place, the *gemara* now takes up a further dimension of the same underlying question.

### Tumah: Rishon or Sheini

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִמְנוֹת בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According to the Rabbis, a *ben pekua* is permitted for consumption and is therefore susceptible to ritual impurity. If the *ben pekua* was still inside the slaughtered mother and the body of the mother came in contact with a primary source of ritual impurity, **what is** the *halakha* **with regard to counting** the mother as having **first-**degree ritual impurity **and** the *ben pekua* as having **second-**degree ritual impurity? If the fetus is considered independent of the mother, it is rendered impure only through its contact with the mother and so would have second-degree impurity. But if it is considered part of the mother it would have the same first-degree impurity as the mother.

The *gemara* asks: *mahu limnos bo rishon v'sheini* — once the mother has been *shechted* and both she and the fetus are dead, do we treat them as one unit or two for purposes of *tumah*? Concretely: if the mother's carcass comes into contact with something *tamei* and becomes a *rishon* (a first-degree derivative of *tumah*), what is the status of the fetus? Is it also a *rishon*, as part of the same body? Or is it a *sheini*, because it is a separate entity that the mother is merely transmitting *tumah* to?

The analogy to a single animal body is instructive. If a *shechted* cow touches something *tamei*, every part of that animal — head, torso, tail — becomes *tamei* as a *rishon*. One does not say the head is a *rishon* and by the time *tumah* reaches the tail it has descended to a *sheini* or *shelishi*. Everything is one unit. The question here is whether the fetus inside the mother participates in that same unity.

One clarification is in order. If the fetus were still alive, it could not become *tamei* in any case, since the concept of *tumah* as applied to food presupposes that the item is fit to be eaten, and a living animal — even one that does not require *shechita* — is not currently food in the relevant sense. So the question arises only when both mother and fetus are dead.

### RY vs. RL Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי, נַעֲשָׂה כֶּאֱגוֹז הַמִּתְקַשְׁקֵשׁ בִּקְלִיפָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One counts** the mother as having **first-**degree impurity **and** the *ben pekua* as having **second**-degree impurity. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: One does not count** the mother as having **first-**degree impurity **and** the *ben pekua* as having **second**-degree impurity. Rather, the fetus has first-degree impurity like its mother, as **it is considered like a nut rattling in its shell;** this is considered a single entity such that if impurity touches the shell, both the nut and the shell are rendered impure with the same degree of impurity.

Rabbi Yochanan rules that *monin bo rishon v'sheini* — one does count down, the mother is a *rishon* and the fetus is a *sheini*. They are separate enough that *tumah* must pass from one to the other in a graduated way. Reish Lakish rules the opposite: *ein monin bo rishon v'sheini* — one does not count down degrees; the fetus and mother are treated as a unified whole and the fetus receives *tumah* directly, not as a second-degree transmission.

### Nut Analogy & Shomer

The reason Reish Lakish offers is not that mother and fetus are literally one body — indeed, earlier evidence suggests they are not fully unified. Rather, Reish Lakish invokes the principle of *shomer*: a protective covering that surrounds an item is considered sufficiently bound up with that item that *tumah* touching the covering reaches the item inside directly, without losing a degree. His analogy is *na'aseh ke'egoz hamitkashkesh bekliptah* — it is like a nut rattling around inside its shell (think of a pistachio or acorn, where the inner nut can be felt to move within the outer shell). If something *tamei* touches the shell, one does not say the shell is a *rishon* and the nut inside is a *sheini*. Because the shell functions as a *shomer* — a protector and extension of the nut — the *tumah* is considered to have reached the nut directly; the nut becomes a *rishon* just like the shell. Similarly, says Reish Lakish, the mother's body and womb function as a *shomer* for the fetus. Even granting that the fetus is not strictly one unit with the mother, the mother nonetheless envelops and protects the fetus sufficiently that *tumah* reaching the mother reaches the fetus as a *rishon*, without stepping down to a *sheini*.

### Pregnant Cow Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא חַד גּוּפָא הוּא – הַיְינוּ דְּאִיתַּכְשַׁר בִּדְמָא דְּאִמֵּיהּ, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, בְּמַאי אִיתַּכְשַׁר?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish explains his objection: **Granted, according to my** opinion, **as I say** that the mother and fetus together **are one entity; that is** why the flesh of the fetus **is rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **through the blood of its mother** that spilled onto the body of the mother. When the blood from the mother's slaughter renders the mother susceptible to impurity, the fetus is also rendered susceptible to impurity, as it is considered part of the mother. **But according to your** opinion, that the fetus is an independent entity, **through what** means is **it rendered susceptible** to contracting impurity from its foreleg?

Reish Lakish then challenges Rabbi Yochanan from a different angle, raising the issue of *hechsher* — the requirement that food come into contact with liquid before it can become susceptible to *tumah*. The *gemara* cites the case of a limb protruding from the womb at the time of *shechita*. Rabbi Meir says that limb is a *neveilah*, and the body of the fetus connected to it has therefore touched a *neveilah*. But for the fetus to have become *tamei* through that contact, it must first have been rendered susceptible (*huchshar*) through contact with liquid. When the mother is *shechted*, blood flows and contacts the mother's exterior, rendering the mother susceptible. If the fetus is considered one body with the mother, then that same *hechsher* applies to the fetus. But if — as Rabbi Yochanan insists — they are two separate bodies, what made the fetus susceptible? Reish Lakish presses: for me it works well, since I say they are *chad gufa* for *tumah* purposes, and the blood of the mother renders the fetus susceptible as well. But according to you, Rabbi Yochanan, how did the fetus become susceptible?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וּכְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: It is rendered susceptible **through the slaughter** of its mother, which also permits the consumption of the fetus, **and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that the flesh of a slaughtered animal is rendered susceptible to ritual impurity by virtue of the fact that it is permitted for consumption, even if it did not come in contact with blood or one of the other six liquids. Accordingly, the fetus will be susceptible to impurity even if it is not considered part of its mother.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *bi'shchita k'Rabbi Shimon* — through the act of *shechita* itself, following the position of Rabbi Shimon, who held that *shechita* renders meat susceptible to *tumah* even without any blood making contact, since the act of *shechita* that converts the animal into food substitutes for the usual *hechsher*. The *shechita* worked on the mother and worked on the fetus; that act itself made the fetus susceptible, with no need to appeal to the mother's blood. This answer, Rabbi Yochanan maintains, says nothing about whether the fetus is a separate unit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבַר בְּנָהָר – הוּכְשַׁר, הָלַךְ לְבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת – נִטְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** from a *baraita*: If a *ben pekua* grew up and **passed through a river, it was** thereby **rendered susceptible** to impurity. Therefore, if it **went** from there **to a cemetery, it is rendered impure.**

Rabbi Yochanan then challenges Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: a pregnant cow is *shechted*, a live fetus is found inside — one that now does not require its own *shechita* — and later this grown-up fetus walks through a river (*avar b'nahar*) and thereby becomes susceptible (*huchshar*), then enters a cemetery and becomes *tamei*. The remarkable feature of this case is that even as a live animal, this *ben pakua* can become *tamei* as food. But the key point Rabbi Yochanan draws from it is that the fetus needed to pass through a river to become susceptible — meaning the blood of the mother at the time of *shechita* did not render it susceptible. This proves the fetus was a separate body, not united with the mother for *tumah* purposes, which is Rabbi Yochanan's position. For Reish Lakish — who holds they are one body — the fetus should already have been rendered susceptible through the mother's blood at *shechita*; why would it need the river?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁחִיטָה יַבִּישְׁתָּא, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish answers: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the **slaughter** of the mother was **dry,** i.e., where no blood was emitted, and therefore, even the mother was not rendered susceptible to ritual impurity. **And** the *baraita* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that the flesh of a slaughtered animal is rendered susceptible to ritual impurity by virtue of the fact that it is permitted for consumption, regardless of whether or not it came into contact with blood or other liquids.

Reish Lakish answers that the *baraita* is dealing with a case of *shechita yaveshta* — a miraculous dry *shechita* in which no blood was produced at all — and moreover, not according to Rabbi Shimon, so neither the blood nor the act of *shechita* rendered it susceptible. Hence the need for the river. The *gemara* notes that this is a difficult resolution: the case of a bloodless *shechita* is already nearly impossible to imagine, let alone to assume that the *baraita* was taking it for granted. The straightforward reading of the *baraita* thus supports Rabbi Yochanan: the fetus is a separate unit from the mother.

### Living Fetus Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא עָבַר בְּנָהָר הוּכְשַׁר, הָלַךְ לְבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת נִטְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים, וְצָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא חַי, וְכׇל שֶׁהוּא חַי אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this *baraita*: If a *ben pekua* grew up and **passed through a river, it was** thereby **rendered susceptible** to impurity, and therefore if it **went** from there **to a cemetery, it is rendered impure? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili:** The flesh of a *ben pekua* **can become impure with the ritual impurity of food, but it** first **needs to be rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity by coming in contact with liquid. **But the Rabbis say: It cannot become impure with the ritual impurity of food because it is alive, and any live** animal **cannot become impure with the ritual impurity of food,** even if it is permitted for consumption.

The *baraita* introduces something striking beyond the *hechsher* debate: the fully grown *ben pakua* can become *tamei* as a live animal. The *gemara* at the top of 75a asks: who is the *tanna* that holds a live animal can become *tamei* as food? Rabbi Yochanan answers: it is Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Beit Shammai said the same thing,** i.e., they both hold that even a live animal can become impure with the ritual impurity of food.

A *baraita* records Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yose HaGlili: this live *ben pakua* that has grown up *metamei tumat ochlin* — it can become *tamei* with the *tumah* of foodstuff — and it does require *hechsher* (contact with liquid) first. The Sages, however, disagree: *eino metamei tumat ochlin*, *mipnei shehu chai* — it does not become *tamei*, because it is alive, and anything alive (*v'chol shehu chai*) does not become *tamei* as food. For *kashrus* purposes, the *shechita* of the mother defined this animal's identity as a limb of the mother — so no *shechita* is required, no *chelev* prohibition applies, no *gid hanasheh*. But for *tumah v'taharah*, one cannot ignore the plain fact that this is a breathing, living animal. Just as we said for other domains — plowing with a donkey, for instance — the live animal must be treated as a live animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, דִּתְנַן: דָּגִים, מֵאֵימָתַי מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁיִּצּוֹדוּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁיָּמוּתוּ. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁאֵין יְכוֹלִין לִחְיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** is **that which we stated** in the *baraita*. The opinion of **Beit Shammai** is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:8): With regard to **fish, from when are they susceptible to impurity** as food? **Beit Shammai say: From when they are caught** in a trap, as at this point they are considered food, since they do not require slaughter. **And Beit Hillel say: From when they die. Rabbi Akiva says: From when they are no** longer **able to live.**

Rabbi Yochanan then connects Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position to a parallel dispute: *Rabbi Yose HaGlili u-Veit Shammai amru davar echad* — Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Beit Shammai said the same thing. The parallel is the question of when a fish becomes susceptible to *tumah* as food, since fish require no *shechita* and can be eaten alive. The *mishnah* in *Uktzin* records the dispute: Beit Shammai says *mishe'yitzadu* — from the moment they are caught in a net they are already considered food, even while still alive and in the water. Beit Hillel says *mishe'yamutu* — they must actually die before they become food. Rabbi Akiva offers a middle position: *mishe'she'ein yecholim lichyot* — they must reach a point at which they can no longer survive, even if they have not yet died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דָּג מְקַרְטֵעַ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes that mishna: **What** is the difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Akiva and Beit Hillel? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The difference **between them** is the case of **a convulsing fish.** Rabbi Akiva holds that such a fish can already become ritually impure, while Beit Hillel require it to have actually died.

The *gemara* asks what distinguishes Rabbi Akiva from Beit Hillel. Rashi understands the question to concern the case between Rabbi Akiva and Beit Hillel. Rabbi Yochanan answers: *dag mikarteia* — a fish that is thrashing. Rashi explains this as a fish in its last throes, still alive and moving, but already dried out to the point that it cannot be revived even if thrown back in the water. Tosafot reads the question differently — as distinguishing Rabbi Akiva from Beit Shammai — and explains that vigorous thrashing is Beit Shammai's indicator that the fish is already fully under human control and irrevocably destined for consumption, while Rabbi Akiva requires something stronger: the fish must be past the point of survival. Either way, the underlying question is a fascinating one: something can be edible while alive, and yet the question remains whether it counts as food for purposes of *tumah*.

Beit Hillel's position maps directly onto the Sages' ruling about the *ben pakua*: as long as it is alive, it is not food and cannot become *tamei*. Beit Shammai's position maps onto Rabbi Yose HaGlili's: something edible while alive is already considered food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב חִסְדָּא: נוֹלְדוּ בְּדָגִים סִימָנֵי טְרֵפָה, מַהוּ? תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה חַיָּה, וְתִיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raises a dilemma:** According to Rabbi Akiva, if **symptoms of a *tereifa* developed in a fish,** e.g., a perforation in the intestines, which will certainly result in the animal's death (see 42a), **what is** the *halakha*? Can it already be rendered ritually impure as if it were now dead? The Gemara clarifies: One can **raise this dilemma according to the one who says** that an animal that is **a *tereifa*** can **live,** i.e., it will not necessarily die within a year, **and** one can **raise this dilemma according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* cannot live,** but will die within a year.

The *gemara* then raises a further question prompted by the discussion of fish survival: *noldu bedagim simanei treifa mahu* — what is the law if a fish displays signs of a *treifa* injury? This question is novel; the entire earlier section of *Chullin* discussed *treifos* by cattle and sheep, but never by fish. The reason it matters here is that according to Rabbi Akiva, the threshold for the fish becoming food is whether it can survive. If a fish has a *treifa*-type injury, perhaps it cannot survive, and would therefore already be considered food. The *gemara* explores this from both angles: according to those who hold *treifa chaya* — a *treifah* animal can survive — perhaps the fish's lesser vitality (*denafish chayusa*) means the same injury that a cow could survive would be fatal for a fish. And according to those who hold *treifa einah chaya* — a *treifah* cannot survive — perhaps the concept of *treifa* is simply inapplicable to fish since they have no *shechita*, and so the list of *treifah* injuries has no traction there. The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved. The discussion also illuminates something about the nature of the *treifah* list itself: even those who hold *treifa chaya* seem to admit that the listed injuries carry a high likelihood of death, even if not an absolute certainty, which is why the *treifa* injuries remain relevant even on that view.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה חַיָּה, בְּהֵמָה הוּא דִּנְפִישָׁא חַיּוּתַהּ, אֲבָל דָּגִים דְּלָא נְפִישָׁא חַיּוּתַיְיהוּ – לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה, הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּהֵמָה דְּיֵשׁ בְּמִינָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל דָּגִים דְּאֵין בְּמִינָן שְׁחִיטָה – אֵימָא לָא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: One can **raise this dilemma according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa*** can **live,** as perhaps that opinion is limited to **an animal, as** an animal has **a strong life force,** and it could live despite such symptoms. **But** with regard to **a fish, whose life force is not strong,** all would agree that it will **not** survive. **Or perhaps, even according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* cannot live,** it is possible that **this matter,** that a *tereifa* is regarded as dead, applies only to **an animal, as** the requirement to **slaughter** it in order to permit it for consumption **applies to this type** of creature, i.e., to animals, and so to invalidate its slaughter, the Torah classifies an animal that is a *tereifa* as though it is dead. But with regard to **fish, where** the requirement to **slaughter** it in order to permit it **does not apply to this type** of creature, **I would say** that a fish with symptoms of a *tereifa* is **not** considered as though it were dead. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

## Fetus Status: Birth and Gestation

### Miscarriage Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵטִילָה נֵפֶל, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Rabbis state in the mishna that a nine-month-old fetus is considered to be part of its mother. Therefore, when the mother is slaughtered, the entire fetus is permitted, including all its fats. The Gemara asks: If an animal **expelled a non-viable newborn,** what is the status of the fetus's fat? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Its fat is like the fat** of any other **domesticated animal,** and one is liable to receive *karet* if he eats it. **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Its fat is like the fat of an undomesticated animal,** which is not prohibited.

The *gemara* now turns to a different case: *hetila nefel* — the mother miscarries a fetus. This fetus emerges dead — a stillbirth — and the question is whether its *chelev* is forbidden like the *chelev* of a *beheima* (a domesticated animal), or not. If this fetus has attained the status of an independent animal, then cutting and eating its fat would violate the prohibition of *chelev*. If it has not, the fat is unrestricted (though the flesh itself would still be problematic as a *neveilah* or as *basar min hachai* from the living mother — the *gemara* does not suggest the fetus is entirely ownerless meat, but rather whether it has its own fully realized identity as a *beheima*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה – אַוֵּירָא גָּרֵים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב חַיָּה – חֳדָשִׁים גָּרְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Its fat is like the fat of** any other **domesticated animal,** as he maintains that the exit of a fetus through **the airspace** of the opening of the womb **causes** it to be regarded as an independent animal. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Its fat is like the fat of an undomesticated animal,** as he maintains that the completion of the **months** of gestation **causes** a fetus to be regarded as an independent animal, and this stillborn did not reach this stage.

Rabbi Yochanan says *chelbo ke-chelev beheima* — its fat is like the forbidden fat of an animal. His reasoning: *avira garam* — the air causes it. The moment the fetus exits the womb and enters the air, it acquires independent status as its own *beheima*, and its fat is forbidden.

Reish Lakish disagrees: *chelbo ke-chelev chayah* — its fat is like the fat of a *chayah* (a wild animal), which is not forbidden. The fetus is not yet a fully formed *beheima*. Why? *Chadashim garmu* — the months of gestation determine its status, not the act of birth. This fetus was miscarried before completing its gestation, and since the months were not completed, it cannot be called a *beheima* regardless of the fact that it was born.

It is worth pausing on the larger implications: Rabbi Yochanan holds that birth is the decisive moment of independent identity, while Reish Lakish holds that full gestation is what matters, and birth alone is insufficient. Both agree that before gestation is complete, nobody considers the fetus a *beheima* — that is the uniform position. The debate concerns either a miscarried but incompletely gestated fetus (first version of the dispute) or — in a second formulation — a fully gestated fetus.

### In-Womb Fat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּלֹא כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו – לָא כְּלוּם הוּא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – הֵיכָא דְּהוֹשִׁיט יָדוֹ לִמְעֵי בְּהֵמָה, וְתָלַשׁ חֵלֶב שֶׁל בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי וְאָכַל. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה, חֳדָשִׁים גָּרְמִי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חֶלְבּוֹ כְּחֵלֶב חַיָּה, חֳדָשִׁים וְאַוֵּירָא גָּרְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that there is another explanation of this dispute: In **any case where its months** of gestation **were not completed,** everyone agrees that **it is nothing,** i.e., it is not an independent animal and its fat is not included in the prohibition of forbidden fats. **When they disagree** it is with regard to a case **where** one **inserted his hand into the womb of an animal and removed the fat of a live nine-**month**-old** fetus **and ate** it. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Its fat is like the fat of** any other **domesticated animal,** as the **months** of gestation alone **cause** it to be regarded as an independent animal. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Its fat is like the fat of an undomesticated animal,** as it is the **months** of gestation **and** its exit through the **airspace** of the opening of the womb that together **cause** it to be regarded as an independent animal. Since this fetus was not yet born, it is not subject to the prohibition of forbidden fats.

The *gemara* records a second version of the dispute (*ika d'amri*), in which the two sides agree that an ungestated fetus is clearly *lav klum hu* — nothing whatsoever, not to be considered a *beheima* — and the debate concerns a more extreme thought experiment: *hoshit yado l'mei beheima v'talash chelev shel ben tish'ah chai* — one reaches a hand into the womb of a living mother and tears out the fat from a fully gestated, living fetus still inside the womb, and eats it. Is that fat forbidden as the fat of a *beheima*?

According to Rabbi Yochanan in this version, *chadashim garmu* — the months of gestation are decisive. Since the fetus has completed its full nine months, it is already its own *beheima*, and its fat is forbidden *chelev*, even though it has not yet been born and remains inside the mother. According to Reish Lakish, *chadashim v'avira garmu* — you need both full gestation and birth. A fully gestated fetus that has not yet been born is still not an independent *beheima*, and its fat carries no special prohibition.

The human parallel here is impossible to ignore: at what point does the fetus attain its own identity? Upon birth, as Rabbi Yochanan holds, or upon completed development in the womb, as Reish Lakish holds in this version? And both agree that well before completion of gestation, there is no independent identity to speak of.

### Asham Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מָה חֵלֶב וּשְׁתֵּי כְלָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּאָשָׁם – מוּצָא מִכְּלַל שְׁלִיל, אַף כֹּל – מוּצָא מִכְּלַל שְׁלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** from a *baraita* that discusses the sacrifice of the fat surrounding a fetus inside a pregnant animal: **Just** as with regard to the requirement to sacrifice **the fat and two kidneys** (see Leviticus 7:3–4), **which is stated with regard to a guilt offering,** the fat of **a fetus is excluded from the category** of fats that one is required to sacrifice because a guilt offering is always a male, **so too,** with regard to **every** offering, even in the case of a female offering, the fat of **a fetus is excluded from the category** of fats that one is required to sacrifice.

Rabbi Yochanan now challenges Reish Lakish from a *baraita* in the *Torat Kohanim* (the *Sifra* on *Vayikra*): *mah chelev ushtei kelayot ha'amur ba'asham mutza miklalo shel ubar* — the fat and two kidneys mentioned in connection with the *asham* offering are entirely excluded from any discussion of a fetus. The *asham* is by definition a male animal; it has no fetus. The *baraita* uses this to derive that in all cases — even female animals that could in theory contain a fetus — the fat of a fetus is never brought on the altar and is never forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אַמַּאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דִּידִי נָמֵי מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains his objection: **Granted, according to my** opinion that the fat of a fetus is forbidden for consumption, **this is why a verse was necessary to exclude** it from being sacrificed on the altar. **But according to your** opinion that it is not forbidden, **why is** a verse **necessary** to exclude it? Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **said to him: My explanation is also** derived **from** the verse **here,** which serves as the source for the fact that the fat of a fetus is not forbidden.

Rabbi Yochanan argues that this verse requires explanation only according to his own position. If, as he holds, the fat of a fully gestated fetus in the womb is forbidden *chelev*, then there is indeed something to exclude — the verse must tell us that even though in theory such fat could be forbidden, it is not to be placed on the altar and (in the *korban* context where the mother was *shechted*) is not subject to the *chelev* prohibition. The verse is needed because there is a genuine case where the fat has significance. But according to Reish Lakish, who holds the fat never has significance under any circumstances, why would the verse need to exclude it at all? There is nothing to exclude.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָה חֵלֶב וּשְׁתֵּי כְלָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּאָשָׁם מוּצָא מִכְּלַל שְׁלִיל, אַף כֹּל – מוּצָא מִכְּלַל שְׁלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that there is another version of this objection in which **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish raised** an **objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan** from that *baraita*: **Just** as with regard to the requirement to sacrifice **"the fat and two kidneys" that is stated with regard to a guilt offering,** the fat of **a fetus is excluded from the category** of fats that one is required to sacrifice, **so too,** with regard to **every** offering, the fat of **a fetus is excluded from the category** of fats that one is required to sacrifice.

Reish Lakish responds: *hai teima didi nami hacha* — that is precisely where he derives his principle from. He would have thought, like Rabbi Yochanan, that the fat had some significance; the verse teaches him that it does not, and that is his proof that even a fully gestated in-womb fetus has no status as a *beheima*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מַיעֲטֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ – לִיקְרַב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמְּחוּסַּר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish explains the objection: **Granted, according to my** opinion that the fat of the fetus is not prohibited like the other fats, it is **due to that** reason that **the Merciful One excludes it** from the requirement of being sacrificed. **But according to your** opinion, that the fat of the fetus is considered one of the forbidden fats, **let it** also **be sacrificed.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** The reason that this fat is not sacrificed is **just as it is** with regard to an offering **whose time has not yet** arrived, i.e., it is not yet seven days old, which may not be sacrificed (see Leviticus 22:27) despite being of a species of animal that may be sacrificed. Similarly, with regard to the fat of the fetus, despite being included among those fats that are prohibited, it may not be sacrificed.

The *gemara* then records the reverse version (*v'ika d'amri*), in which Reish Lakish uses the same *baraita* to challenge Rabbi Yochanan: if the fat of the fetus truly has significance in your system, why is it never brought on the altar? Rabbi Yochanan responds with *midi d'hava a'mechusar zman* — it is no different from an animal less than eight days old. Even a valid animal that has genuine sacrificial potential is not offered before its time is ripe. Similarly, the fat of the fetus, while perhaps forbidden in theory if extracted from the womb, is nonetheless not yet in a state fit for the altar. The *gemara* notes that this answer is somewhat strained, since in the actual *korban* scenario both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree the mother's *shechita* renders the fetus's fat fully permitted — so the question of why that fat is not offered on the altar answers itself. But that is the exchange as the *gemara* records it.

## Treifa Mother's Fetus

### Rav Chisda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹסֵר – מַתִּיר, לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּתִּיר – אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute in the mishna with regard to a nine-month-old fetus found alive inside its slaughtered mother. Rabbi Meir holds that the fetus itself must be slaughtered in order to permit it, whereas the Rabbis hold that it is permitted by virtue of the slaughter of its mother. **Rabbi Ami said:** With regard to **one who slaughtered a *tereifa* and found inside it a live nine-**month**-old** fetus: **According to the statement of** Rabbi Meir, **who prohibits** a nine-month-old fetus found inside a kosher animal until it is slaughtered itself, if it is found inside a *tereifa* **he permits** the fetus once it is slaughtered itself. But **according to the statement** of Rabbi Yehuda, referred to as the Rabbis in the mishna, **who permits** a nine-month-old fetus found inside a kosher animal by virtue of the slaughter of its mother, if it is found inside a *tereifa* **he prohibits** it, even if it is slaughtered itself.

The *gemara* now introduces what Rabbi Ami frames as a problem: *hashochet et hatreifah umatza bah ben tisha chai* — one *shechts* a mother animal that is a *treifa* and finds inside it a live, fully gestated nine-month fetus. What is the status of this fetus?

The difficulty is this. According to Rabbi Meir, the mother's *shechita* never works for a fetus — the *shechita* is irrelevant to the fetus's identity. In that case, the situation here is straightforward: the *shechita* of the *treifa* mother was always irrelevant, and you simply have a live fetus; proceed to *shecht* it independently. But according to the Sages, who hold that the mother's *shechita* does work for the fetus — and that is the dominant view — a problem arises. The *shechita* took place and it did act on the fetus, but the *shechita* was that of a *treifa*, which means the animal is not *kosher*. The fetus may now be defined by that *shechita* — as already *shechted* — but in a way that rendered it non-*kosher*. And one cannot simply *shecht* it again.

Rabbi Ami thus says: *lidivei oser matir, lidivei matir oser* — the position that normally forbids (Rabbi Meir, who says the mother's *shechita* does not work) here permits, since the *shechita* never applied and you can *shecht* the fetus anew. The position that normally permits (the Sages, who say the mother's *shechita* does work) here forbids, since the *shechita* of the *treifa* mother has defined the fetus's status and locked it into non-*kashrut*.

### Rava: Arba Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּתִּיר נָמֵי מוּתָּר, אַרְבָּעָה סִימָנִין אַכְשַׁר בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: According to the statement of** Rabbi Yehuda, **who permits** a nine-month-old fetus found inside a kosher animal by virtue of the slaughter of its mother, he **also permits** it if it is found inside a *tereifa*, once the fetus itself is slaughtered. The reason is that **the Merciful One considers four *simanim* to be fit** for slaughter, i.e., the windpipe and gullet of the mother and those of the fetus, with the fetus being permitted by the cutting of either pair.

Rava disagrees: *lidivei dematir nami mutar* — even according to the Sages, the fetus can be eaten. Why? *Arba simanim achshar beih Rachmana* — the Torah provided four *simanim* for it. This pithy formulation — memorable precisely because it is somewhat imprecise — means the following: the *shechita* of the mother is not the only avenue through which a fetus can be permitted. The mother's *shechita* can work for the fetus, but it is not compulsory that it do so. There is an alternative: *shecht* the fetus independently, with its own *shechita* (two *simanim*), in addition to or instead of relying on the mother's *shechita*. The fetus is not so completely fused with the mother that only the mother's *shechita* can ever define it. It retains its own independent potential for *shechita*.

This principle connects back to the broader debate about whether the fetus is a unitary part of the mother or a connected-but-distinct entity. Rava's position is that the fetus is connected but not fully absorbed — it can be defined by the mother's *shechita* when that *shechita* is valid, but when it is not, the fetus can stand on its own. In the *treifa* mother case: the mother's *shechita* is not efficacious for purposes of *kashrut*, so the fetus is simply treated on its own terms, and one *shechts* it independently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה, וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי – טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה, וְחַיָּיב בִּזְרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה, וְאִם מֵת – טָהוֹר מִלְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **one who slaughtered** an animal that is **a *tereifa* and found inside it a live nine-**month**-old** fetus, the fetus **requires** its own **slaughter** to permit its consumption, **and it is subject to the obligation to** give **the foreleg and the jaw and the maw** to a priest, as is required for a non-sacred animal that is slaughtered. **But** nevertheless, **if it dies,** the fact that its mother was slaughtered serves to render it **pure with regard to imparting impurity** to people **through** their **carrying** of it, as it does not impart the impurity of a carcass.

Rav Chisda then elaborates: *hashochet et hatreifah umatza bah ben tisha chai, te'un shechita* — the fetus requires and can receive its own *shechita*, and accordingly, once that *shechita* is performed, one incurs the obligation of giving the priestly gifts (*zero'a, lechayayim, u'keiva*). Since the mother's *shechita* did not define the fetus's *shechita*, when one now performs a genuine *shechita* on the fetus, it is a full act of *shechita* with all attendant obligations. One could add that *chelev* and *gid hanasheh* would presumably be forbidden in this case as well, since the mother's *shechita* did not impart its special limb-of-the-mother status. Furthermore: *v'im meis tamei v'metamei b'masa* — if the fetus subsequently dies, it is not a *neveilah* but rather has the status of a *treifa*, since the mother's *shechita*, while not defining its *kashrut*, did exist and can be relevant when the animal ultimately dies: the *shechita* of a *treifa* mother means the fetus's death is not the death of an un-*shechted* animal.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה – כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְאִם מֵת טָהוֹר מִלְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא – כְּמַאן? כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Ḥisda: Your ruling is inconsistent. When you say it **requires slaughter, in accordance with whose** opinion is that? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** that a live nine-month-old fetus is not permitted by virtue of its mother's slaughter. **But** when you say that **if it dies,** it is **pure with regard to imparting impurity through carrying, in accordance with whose** opinion is that? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rava (or, as the *gemara* suggests, Rabba — since Rava already spoke) challenges Rav Chisda: *te'un shechita, keman ke-Rabbi Meir?* — saying it requires *shechita* sounds like Rabbi Meir, who denies that the mother's *shechita* ever works. Yet *v'im meis tamei v'metamei b'masa*, which says the fetus's death is not a *neveilah* (because the mother's *shechita* already defined it to some extent), sounds like the Sages. How can you hold both simultaneously?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי – טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה, וְחַיָּיב בִּזְרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה, וְאִם מֵת – טָהוֹר מִלְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא. טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְאִם מֵת – טָהוֹר מִלְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא, כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda replied: **And according to your reasoning,** the same difficulty arises with **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who slaughtered** an animal that is **a *tereifa* and found inside it a live nine-**month**-old** fetus, it **requires** its own **slaughter, and it is subject to the obligation to** give **the foreleg and the jaw and the maw** to a priest. **But** nevertheless, **if it dies,** it is **pure with regard to imparting impurity through carrying.** When he says that it **requires slaughter, in accordance with whose** opinion is that? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. But** when he says that **if it dies,** it is **pure with regard to imparting impurity through carrying,** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rav Chisda's reply: the identical formulation appears verbatim in a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya — *hashochet et hatreifa*, *u'matza bah ben tesha chai, te'un shechita, v'chayav b'zroa v'lechayayim u'v'keiva, v'im meis tamei v'metamei b'masa* — word for word what he said. So why challenge him? Ask the same question on Rabbi Chiya's *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, רַבִּי חִיָּיא אִם כְּבָר מְצָאוֹ מֵת קָאָמַר, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rava responded: **That** is **not difficult,** as it is possible that **Rabbi Ḥiyya was speaking** about **if one found** that the fetus **had already died** inside the mother. In such a case, Rabbi Meir concedes that its mother's slaughter renders it pure, as stated in the mishna. Accordingly, the *baraita* is entirely in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir. **But according to your** opinion, that even if it was found alive and died later it is pure from the impurity of a carcass, it is **difficult.**

Tosafot notes wryly: if the *baraita* says this, why did Rav Chisda state it as his own ruling rather than simply quoting the *baraita*? That is a broader phenomenon in the *Bavli* — *amoraic* statements are sometimes followed by a *baraita* that says the identical thing (*tanya nami hachi*) — and scholars have puzzled over what precisely is happening in such cases.

The questioner responds: the *baraita* itself is not difficult, because *Rabbi Chiya im kevar matzo meis ka'amar* — Rabbi Chiya was speaking of a case where the fetus was already dead when found inside the *shechted treifa* mother. In that case, both halves of the ruling make sense without contradiction: there is no *shechita* question for a dead fetus, and the dead fetus takes on *treifa* status (not *neveilah*) from the mother's *shechita*. There is no tension. The problem is specifically with Rav Chisda's formulation, in which the fetus was found alive, grew up, and then died — and yet Rav Chisda says it is a *treifa* and not a *neveilah*. That suggests the mother's *shechita* remained relevant even for a fetus that walked around independently. But if the mother's *shechita* was relevant, how could one perform a new *shechita* on it in the first place?

### Zeira-Assi Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידִי נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא, אַרְבָּעָה סִימָנִים אַכְשַׁר בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to** Rava: **According to my** opinion **also** it is **not difficult,** as I claim that the entire *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, and although the slaughter of its mother permits the fetus, its own slaughter can also permit it, as **the Merciful One considers four *simanim* to be fit** for slaughter, two of the mother and two of the fetus, the fetus being permitted through the cutting of either pair.

Rava resolves the apparent tension, restating the principle: *arba simanim achshar bei Rachmana* — the Torah gave four *simanim*. The mother's *shechita* is not the exclusive means of defining the fetus's status; one can perform an independent *shechita* on the fetus. When the fetus is then alive and walking, you *shecht* it and it is fully *kosher*. When it dies without having been *shechted*, the mother's *shechita* — which always existed in the background — kicks in: it is not a *neveilah* but a *treifa*. The two halves of the ruling are fully consistent because the two eventualities draw on different operative principles.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סָלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב אַסִּי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Zeira ascended** to Eretz Yisrael, **Rav Asi found him sitting and saying this *halakha*** of Rav Ḥisda, i.e., that the Merciful One considers four *simanim* to be fit for slaughter. Rav Asi **said to him: That is correct, and so says Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rabbi Zeira asked him: Can I conclude **by inference** from your statement **that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's colleague, **disagrees with him?**

This resolution is confirmed by a report from Rabbi Zeira. When Rabbi Zeira went up to Eretz Yisrael, he found Rabbi Assi *yasiv v'ka'amar lah le'hai shamaita* — sitting and teaching over precisely this principle: that when the mother is a *treifa*, the fetus is not locked into the status of a *treifa*; one can *shecht* the fetus and eat it. Rabbi Zeira said to him: *yasher koach* — well done, that is a sound ruling — *v'chen amar Rabbi Yochanan*, and Rabbi Yochanan holds the same way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁהָא הֲוָה שָׁהֵי לֵיהּ וְשָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִשְׁתָּא הֲוָה שָׁתֵי וְשָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi answered: I do not know, as after Rabbi Yoḥanan made his statement, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **was waiting for him** to see if he would retract, **and** he therefore **remained silent** about whether or not he disagreed. But it is possible that after I left, he disagreed. **And some say** that Rav Asi said: At that time, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **was drinking, and** that is why he **remained silent.** Therefore, I do not know whether or not he disagreed.

The *gemara* then asks: since you cited Rabbi Yochanan, did Reish Lakish disagree? Rabbi Assi explains: *mesha hava shehi lei v'shatik lei* — Reish Lakish was delayed in responding and fell silent, and Rabbi Assi left before hearing what Reish Lakish would say. An alternate version: *mishtei hava sheti lei v'shatik lei* — Reish Lakish was in the middle of drinking when the statement was made and could not immediately respond. Rashi adds that Reish Lakish had a practice of waiting before voicing any disagreement with Rabbi Yochanan — to make sure he had thought through his position fully, and to see whether Rabbi Yochanan would reconsider on his own. In any case, Rabbi Assi reports only Rabbi Yochanan's position; whether Reish Lakish agreed or disagreed remains unknown.

## Ben Pekua Maris Ayin

### Shimon Shezuri Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הִפְרִיס עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri **is** identical to that of **the first *tanna*,** i.e., the Rabbis. What difference is there between them? **Rav Kahana said:** The difference **between them** is a case where the fetus **stood upon the ground.** According to the opinion of the first *tanna*, once the fetus walks on the ground there is a rabbinic decree requiring that it be slaughtered before it is consumed, lest people mistakenly permit other animals without slaughter. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri disagrees and holds that it does not require slaughter.

The *mishnah*'s second striking statement — that even if the *ben pakua* grew up and was plowing the fields for five years, the mother's *shechita* is still effective and no new *shechita* is required — is now subjected to scrutiny. The *gemara* asks: is that not identical to the position of the first *tanna*, who already established that the mother's *shechita* works?

Rav Kahana answers: *hiphis agavi karka ika beineihum* — the difference is whether the animal's hooves (*parsah*) have touched the ground, i.e., whether the animal has actually stood up and walked around. The first *tanna* says no new *shechita* is required in principle; Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says even when the animal has been walking around for years, still no *shechita* is needed. The implication is that the first *tanna* does require *shechita* — at least rabbinically — once the animal has walked around, because of *maris ayin*: it no longer looks like a piece of meat, it looks like a full-grown animal, and slaughtering it without *shechita* creates a problematic appearance of impropriety. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri dismisses this concern entirely.

It is thus a dispute about *maris ayin*. Does the *ben pakua* that has grown up and been walking around require *shechita* to avoid giving the wrong impression to onlookers who will not know its unusual history? The Sages say yes; Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says no.

### Hybrid Ben Pekua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְזֶרַע הָאָב, בֶּן פְּקוּעָה הַבָּא עַל בְּהֵמָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא – הַוָּלָד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya said: According to the statement of the one who says** that when defining the status of an animal **one** needs **to be concerned with its paternity,** if **a *ben pekua* copulated with a full-fledged animal, the offspring has no rectification.** Although when the mother and father are each a *ben pekua* the offspring is permitted without ritual slaughter, if the father is a *ben pekua* but the mother is not, the offspring is simultaneously defined as requiring slaughter, based on the mother, and being excluded from the requirement for slaughter, based on the father. Therefore, no act of slaughter can permit it.

The *gemara* introduces a further complication: *Rav Mesharshaya* reports that according to the view that we are concerned about paternal lineage (*chosheshin lezera ha'av*) — meaning that the identity of an animal's father carries *halachic* implications — a *ben pakua* that has mated with a non-*ben-pakua* female produces offspring whose status is irresolvable. The offspring is half *ben pakua* (from its *ben pakua* father, whose *shechita* is already defined and irrelevant) and half normal animal (from its non-*ben-pakua* mother, whose *shechita* is still required). You cannot forgo *shechita* on account of the *ben pakua* father, since the mother's side demands it. But performing a *shechita* on account of the mother's side is meaningless on the father's side, since the father was already *shechted* and the *shechita* for this offspring is considered to have already been performed from that lineage. The result: *ein lo takana* — there is no remedy for this animal.

### Abaye Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּקָלוּט בֶּן פְּקוּעָה שֶׁמּוּתָּר, מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל מִלְּתָא דִּתְמִיהָא מִידְכָּר דְּכִירִי לַהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Everyone,** i.e., even the first *tanna*, who requires the slaughter of a *ben pekua* that stood upon the ground, **agrees with regard to a *ben pekua*** with **non-cloven** hooves that was found inside a kosher animal, **that it is permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of its mother. **What is the reason** for this? It is that **people remember any bizarre matter,** and there is no concern that if it is permitted without slaughter, people will mistakenly permit regular animals without slaughter.

Abaye qualifies the debate about *maris ayin*: *hakol modim bekalut ben pakua* — everyone agrees that a *ben pakua* born with unsplit hooves (*kalut*) can be eaten without *shechita* even when fully grown. The rationale: *kol milta detmiha midkeri inshi* — anything that is astounding, people remember. An animal with conspicuously fused, unsplit hooves is so unusual that all who see it will recall its peculiar status and will not mistake it for an ordinary animal requiring *shechita*. The concern about *maris ayin* evaporates when the animal's bizarre appearance ensures it will be remembered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּקָלוּט בֶּן קְלוּטָה בֶּן פְּקוּעָה שֶׁמּוּתָּר, מַאי טַעְמָא? תְּרֵי תְּמִיהֵי מִידְכָּר דְּכִירִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Abaye said: Everyone agrees with regard to a *ben pekua*** with **non-cloven** hooves found inside an animal with **non-cloven** hooves that was itself born to a kosher animal, **that** the fetus **is permitted** without slaughter even if it stood on the ground. **What is the reason?** It is that **people remember two bizarre matters.**

A second version of Abaye's ruling (*ika d'amri*) is more restrictive: only a *kalut ben keluta ben pakua* — a *ben pakua* that had unsplit hooves, and that itself gave birth to another *ben pakua* that also had unsplit hooves — generates sufficient memory. Two astounding things compounding across generations (*tartei tmihei midkeri inshi*) are what people remember; a single astounding feature may not be enough. Rashi clarifies that this second version describes a *ben pakua* with unsplit hooves whose own offspring — itself a *ben pakua* by virtue of its mother being *shechted* — was also born with unsplit hooves. That doubled rarity is what triggers the universal exemption.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי מַתִּיר בִּבְנוֹ וּבֶן בְּנוֹ עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הוּא מוּתָּר, וּבְנוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ze'eiri says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri** that a *ben pekua* is permitted without slaughter even if it stood upon the ground. **And similarly, Rabbi Shimon Shezuri would permit** without slaughter **the offspring** of a *ben pekua* **and the offspring of its offspring,** and so on **to the end of all** future **generations. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It,** the *ben pekua* itself, **is permitted, but its offspring is prohibited** unless it is slaughtered.

Rabbi Zeira cites Rav Chanina for the *halacha*: *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon Shezuri* — we rule like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, and moreover, the general principle is that wherever Rabbi Shimon Shezuri appears in our *mishnah*, the *halacha* follows him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדָּא בַּר חָבוּ הֲוָה לֵיהּ בֶּן פְּקוּעָה דִּנְפַל דֵּיבָא עֲלֵיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שַׁחְטֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָאָמַר זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי מַתִּיר בִּבְנוֹ וּבֶן בְּנוֹ עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא בְּנוֹ, אֲבָל אִיהוּ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Adda bar Ḥavu had a *ben pekua* that was attacked by a bear** and was about to die. **He came before Rav Ashi** to inquire what to do. Rav Ashi **said to him: Go** and **slaughter it** before it dies so that you can eat it, in accordance with the opinion of the first *tanna* that if a *ben pekua* stood upon the ground it requires slaughter. Adda bar Ḥavu **said to** Rav Ashi: **But doesn't Ze'eiri say** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri** that a *ben pekua* is permitted without slaughter even if it stood upon the ground, **and similarly, Rabbi Shimon Shezuri would permit** without slaughter **the offspring** of a *ben pekua* **and the offspring of its offspring,** and so on **to the end of all** future **generations? And even Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who disagreed, **said** his dissenting opinion **only** with regard to **its offspring, but** with regard to a *ben pekua* **itself,** he did **not** disagree that it is permitted.

The case of Adda bar Chavu tests this. He had a *ben pakua*; a wolf attacked it and rendered it a *treifa*. He came before Rav Ashi. Rav Ashi told him: go and *shecht* it. His instruction was twofold in its implication: (a) *shechita* is required — ruling like the *Rabbanan* against Rabbi Shimon Shezuri for *maris ayin* purposes — but (b) the *treifa* injury from the wolf does not prohibit eating it, since the *ben pakua*'s exemption from the *treifa* category is a genuine *halachic* benefit of its status, not merely a procedural nicety. Adda bar Chavu objected: don't we rule like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri? No *shechita* should be needed at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Adda bar Ḥavu: **Rabbi Yoḥanan was speaking according to the statement of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri,** i.e., he said that even Rabbi Shimon Shezuri permits only a *ben pekua* itself, but not its offspring. But Rabbi Yoḥanan himself agrees with the first *tanna* that a *ben pekua* that stood upon the ground is prohibited without slaughter.

Rav Ashi replied: the ruling *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon Shezuri* applies only in the two specific cases where Rabbi Yonatan explicitly invokes it — *b'mesukan u'vitrumat ma'aser shel demai* — and not in our case. We rule like the *Chachamim*: *shechita* is required for appearances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי בִּמְסוּכָּן, וּבִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁל דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Adda bar Ḥavu: **I hold in accordance with this** statement of Rabbi Yonatan, **as Rabbi Yonatan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in** the case of one who is **dangerously ill, and in** the case of ***teruma* of the tithe of doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*],** but not in other cases, e.g., in the case of his disagreement with the first *tanna* concerning a *ben pekua*.

The final *halacha*, as codified by both the Rambam and the Shulchan Aruch, follows this ruling of the *Chachamim*. Once the *ben pakua* has stood up and walked around, *shechita* is required — but only for *maris ayin*, not because the animal is genuinely subject to the full range of *shechita* concerns. Accordingly, the *ben pakua* retains its distinctive *halachic* benefits: one need not worry about *treifa*, *chelev*, or *gid hanasheh*. The *ben pakua* farm is thus not the revolutionary enterprise that the Torah's plain permission might suggest — no onlooker will accept seeing an animal slaughtered without any *shechita* at all — but it retains real *halachic* advantages: the burdens of checking for *treifos*, avoiding *chelev*, and removing the *gid hanasheh* are entirely eliminated.
