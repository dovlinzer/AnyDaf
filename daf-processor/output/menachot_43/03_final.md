# Menachot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Techelet Testing & Purchase Standards

### Braisa Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תְּכֵלֶת אֵין לָהּ בְּדִיקָה, וְאֵין נִיקַּחַת אֶלָּא מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה. תְּפִילִּין יֵשׁ לָהֶם בְּדִיקָה, וְאֵין נִיקָּחִין אֶלָּא מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה. סְפָרִים וּמְזוּזוֹת יֵשׁ לָהֶן בְּדִיקָה, וְנִיקָּחִין מִכׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There is no** reliable method of **testing sky-blue wool, and** therefore **it may be purchased only from an expert. There is** a method of **testing phylacteries** to ensure they were written properly, **but** nevertheless **they may be purchased only from an expert. There is** a method of **testing Torah scrolls and *mezuzot*, and they may be purchased from anyone.**

The *daf* opens with a *braisa* whose central ruling sets the terms for everything that follows: *techelet ein lah bedika ve'einah nikneis ela min hamumche* — there is no valid means of testing *techelet*, and therefore it may only be acquired from a *mumche*. At first glance, the statement seems to say that *techelet* is simply untestable. The *gemara* will challenge that assumption directly, since tests do in fact exist, but the *braisa*'s ruling stands: purchase only from a *mumche*.

The word *mumche* here carries a double meaning. It denotes not merely expertise — someone who knows what they are doing — but also trustworthiness, a person of established standing in the community whose reliability can be assumed. The practical concern motivating the ruling is the *kala ilan*, the counterfeit blue dye that is visually indistinguishable from genuine *techelet*. A *mumche* is one who both knows that *kala ilan* is *assur* and can be trusted not to deceive.

### Testing Comparisons

The *braisa* continues by contrasting *techelet* with two other sacred items. *Tefillin yesh lahem bedika* — *tefillin* can be tested, and yet they too may only be purchased from a *mumche*. Why the additional requirement of a *mumche* if testing is possible? Rashi explains that a visual inspection of the script can verify whether the letters are correctly formed, but it cannot detect whether the *ibud* — the tanning of the hide — was performed *lishma*, with proper intent. Since proper intent is not discernible to the eye, one must rely on a *mumche* who both knows the *halachos* and is presumed to have performed them correctly.

*Sefarim* and *mezuzot*, by contrast, *yesh lahem bedika ve'nikchin mi'kol adam* — they can be tested and may be purchased from anyone. According to Rashi, this is because *mezuzot* do not require *ibud lishma*, so everything relevant is in principle visible and verifiable by inspection. The question of whether *mezuzot* require *ksiva lishma* might seem to complicate this, but Rashi's position is that no hidden element remains.

### Tosafot: Opening Analysis

Tosafot presses on Rashi's distinction. If the real concern with *tefillin* is *ibud lishma* and the concern with *mezuzot* is absent because *lishma* is not required there, Tosafot challenges this directly by citing the *Rambam*, who rules that *mezuzot* do not require *lishma*. This is, notably, one of the very few places in *Shas* where Tosafot quotes the Rambam — and does so with a telling editorial note: *lo peirush mena ley*, the Rambam did not explain the source for his ruling within the *Mishneh Torah*. Tosafot speculates that perhaps the Rambam derived it from this very passage.

Tosafot himself rejects the view that *mezuzot* do not require *lishma*, and accordingly offers a different account of the entire distinction. He argues that we are not actually concerned that a seller will deceive us regarding *lishma* — whether for *tefillin* or *mezuzot* — since there is no significant profit motive for such deception and we can assume people are honest about it. The operative distinction between *tefillin* and *mezuzot* is therefore not about *lishma* at all, but about the practicality of opening and inspecting them.

*Mezuzot* are easy to open and examine; one simply unrolls the parchment. *Tefillin*, by contrast, are sealed, and it is not considered proper to open them repeatedly. Consequently, since one will not in practice be checking one's *tefillin* through personal inspection, it is especially important to acquire them from a *mumche* in the first instance, so that one is not forced into the uncomfortable position of having to open them to verify. This leads Tosafot into a related discussion of how frequently *tefillin* and *mezuzot* must be checked — a question not addressed directly in the *gemara* itself, drawing instead on other sources. The checking interval for *tefillin* is subject to different opinions precisely because of the reluctance to open them.

## Testing Methods Debate

### Rav Yitzchak's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה בָּדֵיק לֵיהּ! (סִימָן בְּגֶשֶׁם) מַיְיתֵי מַגְבְּיָא גִּילָא, וּמַיָּא דְּשַׁבְלִילְתָּא, וּמֵימֵי רַגְלַיִם בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם, וְתָרֵי לַהּ בְּגַוַּויְיהוּ מֵאוּרְתָּא וְעַד לְצַפְרָא. אִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ – פְּסוּלָה, לָא אִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And is there no** method for **testing sky-blue wool? But didn't Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, test it** to ensure it was dyed with *tekhelet*? The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for the test, which was carried out **with** items whose names contain the letters ***gimmel*, *shin*,** or ***mem*.** He would **bring alum clay [*megavya gila*], and water of fenugreek [*shavlilta*], and urine [*meimei raglayim*] that was forty days old.** He would **soak** the sky-blue wool **in** this solution **from night until morning.** If **its color would fade [*ipparad ḥazutei*],** the sky-blue wool was determined to be **unfit,** as it was not dyed with *tekhelet* derived from a *ḥilazon*. If **its color would not fade,** the sky-blue wool was determined to be **fit.**

The *gemara* challenges the *braisa*'s claim that *techelet* has no *bedika*: *v'ha Rav Yitzchak brei d'Rav Yehuda badik lei* — Rav Yitzchak son of Rav Yehuda did in fact test it. His method involved combining a certain metal substance (*magvia gila*, identified by Rashi as a type of metal using an Aramaic term meaning *sefer alum*), water in which certain vegetables had been cooked (*maya d'shavlilta*), and urine that was forty days old. Rashi notes that the forty days refers not to an infant's age but to the age of the urine itself. The cloth containing the absorbed *techelet* was submerged in this mixture from evening until the following morning. If the color separated — *iprach chazusei* — the *techelet* was *pesulah*; if the color held fast, it was *keshera*.

### Rav Adda's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַדָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב עַוִּירָא אָמַר: מַיְיתֵי חֲמִירָא אַרְכְּסָא דִּשְׂעָרֵי וְאָפְיָא לַהּ בְּגַוֵּויהּ, אִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִמְעַלְּיוּתָא – כְּשֵׁרָה, לִגְרִיעוּתָא – פְּסוּלָה, וְסִימָנָיךְ: שִׁינּוּי שֶׁקֶר, שִׁינּוּי אֱמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Adda said before Rava in the name of Rav Avira: One brings hard [*arkesa*] leavened barley** dough **and bakes** the sky-blue wool **in it.** If the color of the sky-blue wool **changes for the better,** meaning that the process intensifies the color of the sky-blue wool, then it is **fit.** If the color of the sky-blue wool changes for the **worse,** i.e., it fades, then it is **unfit. And your mnemonic** is: **Change** reveals **falsehood** and **change** reveals **truth.** All of this indicates that it is possible to test whether sky-blue wool has been dyed with real *tekhelet*, contrary to the *baraita*.

A second method is attributed to Rav Adda, cited before Rava in the name of Rav Avya. This test uses a very sharp barley yeast (*chamira arkasa d'se'arei*), in which the dyed cloth is baked into bread. If the process improved the color — *ishtani l'maalyusa* — the *techelet* was valid; if it made matters worse — *l'griyusa* — it was *pesulah*. The mnemonic offered is *shinui sheker shinui emes*: change for the worse bespeaks falseness, change for the better bespeaks truth. Rashi's explanation of this mnemonic is not entirely clear, but the practical principle is straightforward.

## No Testing Meaning

### Testing Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֵין לָהּ בְּדִיקָה״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר? אַטְּעִימָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the *baraita*: **What** does it mean **when it says: There is no** reliable method of **testing** sky-blue wool? It means that there is no way to test whether it was dyed for the sake of the mitzva or **for** the purpose of **testing** the dye.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *braisa*'s ruling (*techelet ein lah bedika*) and the existence of these two tests. *Mai ein la bdika nami d'ka'amar a'teima* — the *braisa* does not mean that testing is impossible. Rather, it means that testing the full vat of dye would *invalidate* it. The act of *te'ima* — the tasting or sampling of the dye itself — renders the whole batch *pesulah*. One can test a sample, but one cannot test the actual thread or the full production without causing the very invalidation one is trying to avoid. That is the meaning of *ein lah bedika*: not that there is no way to check, but that checking in the natural sense destroys what you are checking.

## Testing Methods Debate (Continued)

### Mamshichi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר מִמִּשְׁכִּי אַיְיתִי תְּכֵלְתָּא בִּשְׁנֵי רַב אַחַאי, בַּדְקוּהָ בִּדְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְאִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ, בִּדְרַב אַדָּא, וְאִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִמְעַלְּיוּתָא. סְבַר לְמִיפְסְלַהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב אַחַאי: אֶלָּא הָא לָא תְּכֵילְתָּא הִיא, וְלָא קָלָא אִילָן הִיא, אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁמוּעָתָא אַהֲדָדֵי אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Mar,** a Sage **from Mashkhei, brought sky-blue** wool **in the years** when **Rav Aḥai** was a preeminent Sage. **They tested it in** the manner described by **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, and its color faded.** They then tested it **in** the manner described by **Rav Adda and** the color **changed for the better.** The Sages **thought** that the sky-blue wool should be **deemed unfit** because it did not pass the first test. **Rav Aḥai said to them: But** how could it be that **this** wool **is not *tekhelet*,** as it failed one of the tests, **and is** also **not indigo,** as it passed the other? This is impossible, because it must be one or the other. **Rather, conclude from it** that these ***halakhot* were stated together.**

This interpretation is immediately complicated by a case from the years of Rav Achai: *Mar Mamshichi* brought *techelet* and it was subjected to both tests. The first test — Rav Yitzchak's method with urine and the metal substance — produced a result of *iprach chazusei*, meaning the color separated, suggesting invalidity. But the second test — Rav Adda's method with sharp yeast and bread — yielded *ishtani l'maalyusa*, improvement, suggesting validity. The tests produced contradictory results, and the natural instinct was to declare the *techelet pesulah* on the grounds that it had failed at least one test.

### Sequential Test Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דִּבְדַקְנָא בִּדְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, לָא אִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ – כְּשֵׁרָה; אִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ – בָּדְקִינַן לַהּ בִּדְרַב אַדָּא בַּחֲמִירָא אַרְכְּסָא: אִישְׁתַּנַּי לִמְעַלְּיוּתָא – כְּשֵׁרָה, לִגְרִיעוּתָא – פְּסוּלָה. שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: שְׁמוּעָתָא אַהֲדָדֵי אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: In a case **where we tested** the wool **in** the manner described **by Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda,** and **its color did not fade,** it is **fit** and requires no further testing. If **its color faded,** then **we test it in** the manner described **by Rav Adda, with hard leavened** barley dough. If the color **changed for the better** it is **fit;** if the color changed **for the worse** it is **unfit.** The Gemara adds: **They sent** a message **from there,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael: These ***halakhot* were** in fact **stated together,** as explained by Rav Aḥai.

Rav Achai rejected this approach and resolved the contradiction by establishing that the two tests are not parallel alternatives: *shmu'ah ahadadi itmar* — they were stated in juxtaposition and must be understood as a sequence. The proper procedure is as follows. One first applies Rav Yitzchak's test. If the color does not separate, the *techelet* is definitively *keshera*. If the color does separate, this is not itself conclusive, since even genuine *techelet* can produce that result under certain conditions. One then proceeds to Rav Adda's second test with the yeast. If that test shows improvement, the *techelet* is *keshera*; if it deteriorates further, it is *pesulah*.

On this reading, the case of Mar Mamshichi is not a contradiction but a confirmation: the first test was ambiguous, the second test resolved the ambiguity in favor of validity, and the *techelet* was therefore declared *keshera*. The two tests are not competing methods but a sequential protocol, the second serving as a tie-breaker when the first is inconclusive.

## No Testing Meaning (Continued)

### Tosafot Solution

With the sequential test logic established, one might ask: if tests exist and can be run on a sample, why does the *braisa* insist that *techelet* can only be purchased from a *mumche*? Rashi's note that *te'ima* invalidates the entire batch does not fully resolve the question, since one could presumably test a sample rather than the whole vat.

Tosafot addresses this directly. The passage in Tosafot on 43a — *u-vedika de-Rav Yitzchak u-de-Rav Adda ahani le-loke'ach min ha-mumcheh she-yode'a de-te'imah pesulah aval lo yada mi-kala ilan* — makes a careful distinction. The tests of Rav Yitzchak and Rav Adda are useful precisely for someone who is buying from a *mumche*. Why? Because the danger of *kala ilan* can be detected by these tests. You test your sample, confirm it is genuine *techelet* and not counterfeit, and then you know you have the real article.

But a separate problem remains: even if the dye is genuine *techelet*, perhaps the craftsman who prepared it invalidated the entire vat by performing *te'ima* improperly during production — that is, by testing the dye in a way that caused *pesul*. If the craftsman did not know the *halachos* governing *te'ima*, he might have unknowingly ruined everything he produced, even if the raw material was authentic. This is what *ein lah bedika* means for Tosafot: not that you cannot test whether the color is genuine, but that you have no way of knowing after the fact whether the producer invalidated the vat through improper *te'ima*. The *mumche* requirement addresses this second concern — you need a seller who knows the *halachos* well enough not to have made that mistake.

The *Rambam*'s formulation runs parallel: *Ha'techeiles einan nilkachas ella min hamumche choshesh shema lo nitpal lishma* — one acquires *techelet* only from a *mumche* out of concern that it was not processed *lishma*. The *lishma* concern in the Rambam functions analogously to Tosafot's *te'ima* concern: the *mumche* is necessary because some form of invalidation — either improper intent or improper testing — cannot be detected by the consumer's inspection alone, even if the tests confirm the dye is genuine.

## Purchase Standards

### From Non-Jews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ טַלִּית מְצוּיֶּיצֶת מִן הַשּׁוּק, מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל – הֲרֵי הִיא בְּחֶזְקָתָהּ, מִן הַגּוֹי, מִן הַתַּגָּר – כְּשֵׁרָה, מִן הַהֶדְיוֹט – פְּסוּלָה. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאי לִמְכּוֹר טַלִּית מְצוּיֶּיצֶת לְגוֹי עַד שֶׁיַּתִּיר צִיצִיּוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who purchases a cloak with ritual fringes from the marketplace,** if he purchased it **from a Jew it retains its presumptive status** that it is fit for the mitzva. If he purchased it **from a gentile,** then if he purchased it **from a merchant** it is presumed to be **fit,** as the merchant would want to maintain his credibility and would therefore purchase the sky-blue strings only from a reliable source. But if he purchased it **from** a gentile who is **an ordinary** person rather than a professional merchant, the sky-blue strings are **unfit,** as the seller presumably dyed them himself. **And even though** the Sages **said: A person is not permitted to sell a cloak with ritual fringes to a gentile until he unties** and removes **its ritual fringes,** it is permitted to purchase such a cloak from a gentile merchant, as it is assumed that the merchant acquired the cloak from a Jew who ignored this *halakha*.

The *gemara* then introduces a *braisa* governing the purchase of a *tallis metzuyetzes* — a garment already fitted with *tzitzit* — from different sources. If purchased from a Jew in the marketplace, it is presumed *keshera*; the Jew is assumed to have attended to the requirements properly. If purchased from a non-Jew, the ruling is more nuanced: from a merchant (*tagar*) it is *keshera*, but from a layperson (*hediyot*) it is *pesulah*.

The distinction rests on the nature of the transaction. The non-Jewish merchant is in the business of reselling goods; the garment he is selling was almost certainly acquired second-hand from a Jewish owner who had already outfitted it with proper *tzitzit*. His role is that of a reseller, not a manufacturer, and so the *tzitzit* on the garment can be presumed to have been attached correctly by the original Jewish owner. By contrast, the non-Jewish layperson who has a single garment to sell might be attempting to enhance its appeal to Jewish buyers by attaching *tzitzit* himself — a suspect motive — and so no presumption of validity attaches.

### From Jews

The purchase of a *tallis metzuyetzes* from a Jew in the marketplace carries a presumption of *keshera* by virtue of the general assumption that Jews attend to their *mitzvot* properly. The seller is presumed to have used the garment for his own observance, and the *tzitzit* on it are therefore assumed to have been placed there correctly.

### Selling Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ מִשּׁוּם זוֹנָה, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא יִתְלַוֶּה עִמּוֹ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְיַהַרְגֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the prohibition against selling a cloak with ritual fringes to a gentile? The Gemara answers: **Here they interpreted** that it is prohibited **because** of the concern that the gentile will visit **a prostitute** and observers will think that he is a Jew. Alternatively, **Rav Yehuda said:** It is prohibited **lest** a Jew mistake the gentile for a Jew and **accompany him on a journey** thinking that he is also Jewish, due to his ritual fringes, **and** the gentile might then **kill him.**

The *braisa* notes an apparent tension: it is forbidden to sell a *tallis metzuyetzes* to a non-Jew without first removing the *tzitzit*, yet the above ruling presupposes that such garments do sometimes pass into non-Jewish hands. The *gemara* resolves this by saying that although the sale is prohibited, it does nonetheless occur on occasion, and the *tagar*'s stock is therefore plausibly composed of such garments.

The *gemara* gives two reasons for the prohibition on selling a *tzitzit* garment to a non-Jew. The first (*mishum zona*) is that the non-Jew might give the garment to a prostitute, who would then appear to her Jewish customers to be associating with a Jew — evoking the famous aggadic narrative that will appear later in the *daf* about a man saved from sin by his *tzitzit*. The second reason, attributed to Rav Yehuda, is the danger of murder: a non-Jew wearing a garment with *tzitzit* could pass as Jewish and thereby gain the trust of Jews who would otherwise not travel with him into dangerous circumstances.

Both concerns reflect a broader theme of the *daf*: that clothing is a fundamental marker of identity. A garment with *tzitzit* identifies its wearer as Jewish in a palpable, visible way. The *gemara* notes the parallel to *tefillin*, about which it is written *u'rau kol amei ha'aretz ki shem Hashem nikra alecha* — all the nations of the earth shall see that God's name is called upon you. *Tzitzit* operates somewhat differently — *v'zachartem*, it is addressed to the wearer's internal remembering — but in practice, anyone wearing *tzitzit* visibly is no less clearly marked as Jewish.

## Rav Yehuda's Practice

### Women & Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה רָמֵי תְּכֵילְתָּא לְפַרְזוּמָא דְּאִינָשֵׁי בֵּיתֵיהּ, וּמְבָרֵךְ כֹּל צַפְרָא ״לְהִתְעַטֵּף בַּצִּיצִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda would affix** white and **sky-blue** strings **to the garment [*pirzuma*] of his wife. And every morning** he would **recite the blessing: To wrap ourselves in** garments with **ritual fringes.**

The *gemara* reports that Rav Yehuda would affix *techelet* to the *parzuma* — a type of cloak — belonging to the women of his household. From this practice, the *gemara* infers that Rav Yehuda held *tzitzit* to be *mitzvas asei she-lo hazman grama* — a positive commandment not limited by time — since if it were time-bound, women would be exempt. The question of whether *tzitzit* is time-bound turns on whether nighttime is excluded from the obligation: if night garments are exempt, then time limits the *mitzvah*, and women are accordingly exempt by the general rule that women are *peturot* from positive time-bound commandments.

### Daily Blessing Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרָמֵי, קָסָבַר: מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, אַמַּאי מְבָרֵךְ כֹּל צַפְרָא וְצַפְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From** the fact **that** he would **affix** ritual fringes to his wife's garments, it is apparent that **he holds** that the obligation of ritual fringes is **a positive mitzva that is not time-bound,** and therefore women are also obligated in it. But if that is his opinion, **why** did **he recite the blessing** on ritual fringes **each and every morning?** In order for the mitzva to not be time-bound, it must apply at night, in which case a new blessing should not be recited in the morning.

The *gemara* then notes a second practice of Rav Yehuda: *mevarech kol tzafra lihit'atef betzitzit* — every morning he recited the blessing *lihit'atef betzitzit*. This creates a puzzle. If Rav Yehuda holds that *tzitzit* is *she-lo hazman grama* — that the obligation is continuous and unrestricted by time — why does he make a new *beracha* each morning? He is presumably always under the same unbroken obligation. One might push the question further: should he not have made a single *beracha* his entire life, since the obligation never lapses?

### Blessing Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי, דְּתַנְיָא: תְּפִילִּין כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּנִּיחָן מְבָרֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. אִי הָכִי, כֹּל שַׁעְתָּא נָמֵי! רַב יְהוּדָה אִינִישׁ צְנִיעָא הֲוָה, וְלָא שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ יוֹמָא. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִצַּפְרָא? כִּי מְשַׁנֵּי מִכְּסוּת לַיְלָה לִכְסוּת יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Yehuda was acting **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **phylacteries, every time one dons them he recites the blessing over them,** even several times in one day; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara asks: **If so,** he should have **also** recited a blessing **every time** that he took the cloak off and put it back on, and not merely once a day in the morning. The Gemara answers: **Rav Yehuda was a modest man and he did not remove his cloak the entire day.** The Gemara asks: In **what** way is it **different from the morning,** i.e., why did he recite a blessing in the morning? The Gemara answers: He recited the blessing in the morning **when he changed from a nighttime garment to a daytime garment.**

The *gemara* answers by appeal to the ruling of *Rebbi*: *kol zman shemenichan mevarech aleihem divrei Rebbi* — every time one puts on *tefillin* one recites the *beracha*, according to Rebbi. The point is that making a new *beracha* upon each donning is not contingent on a lapse in obligation. Even within an unbroken period of *chiyuv*, the act of removing and replacing the garment is sufficient to generate a new *beracha*.

The *gemara* then asks: if every re-donning triggers a new *beracha*, Rav Yehuda should have been reciting blessings throughout the day every time he put his garment back on, not merely in the morning. The *gemara* responds: *Rav Yehuda inish tznia hu* — Rav Yehuda was a man of exceptional modesty who never removed his garment throughout the day. He dressed once in the morning and kept his garment on until nightfall. Consequently, for him, *kol tzafra* — every morning — was the only time he put on his garment anew, and that is when he recited the blessing. When the *gemara* says he blessed each morning, this does not reflect a principle unique to mornings; it reflects a principle about every fresh donning, which for Rav Yehuda happened to occur only once daily.

One might raise a further question: if Rav Yehuda held *laila zman tzitzit*, he should also be making a *beracha* when putting on his night garments. The *gemara* notes *ina hashi nami* — indeed that would follow. The discussion that flows from this bears on the broader question of whether *tzitzit* is *chovat gavra* — a personal obligation that activates upon wearing — or *chovat mana* — an obligation that inheres in the garment itself. If the latter, the *mitzvah* is in some sense accomplished when the *tzitzit* are tied to the garment, and each morning's *beracha* might be understood not strictly as a *birkas hamitzvah* triggered by performance, but more as a *birkas hashevach* — a blessing of praise upon encountering the *mitzvah* — analogous in certain respects to *birchas haTorah*, which is recited once daily and is understood by some authorities to carry a dimension of praise beyond the strictly obligatory.

## Women's Obligation

### Universal Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בְּצִיצִית, כֹּהֲנִים, לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים, גֵּרִים, נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר בְּנָשִׁים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָאּ הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָאּ נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of **ritual fringes,** including **priests, Levites, Israelites, converts, women, and** Canaanite **slaves. Rabbi Shimon deems women exempt, because** the mitzva of ritual fringes **is a positive, time-bound mitzva, and women are exempt** from **every positive, time-bound mitzva.**

The *gemara* now turns to a *braisa* that states *hakol chayavin betzitzit* — everyone is obligated in *tzitzit*: *kohanim, leviyim, v'yisraelim*, as well as converts, women, and slaves. The inclusion of converts is notable since converts are almost never singled out as a distinct category in this kind of context. Women and slaves are mentioned because this *braisa* follows the view that *tzitzit* is *she-lo hazman grama*, so women are fully obligated alongside men. Rabbi Shimon is then cited as dissenting: *poter benashim mipnei shemitzvas asei shehazman grama hi* — he exempts women on the grounds that *tzitzit* is a time-bound positive commandment.

### Kohanim Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בְּצִיצִית, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּאִי כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים פְּטִירִי, מַאן לִיחַיַּיב? כֹּהֲנִים אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״לֹא תִלְבַּשׁ שַׁעַטְנֵז צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים יַחְדָּו. גְּדִילִים תַּעֲשֶׂה לָךְ״, מַאן דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּרִי כִּלְאַיִם לְגַבֵּיהּ בִּלְבִישָׁה הוּא דְּמִיחַיַּיב בְּצִיצִית, הָנֵי כֹּהֲנִים הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתְּרִי כִּלְאַיִם לְגַבַּיְיהוּ – לָא לִיחַיְּיבוּ. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: נְהִי דְּאִישְׁתְּרִי בְּעִידָּן עֲבוֹדָה, בְּלָא עִידָּן עֲבוֹדָה לָא אִישְׁתְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of **ritual fringes,** including **priests, Levites, and Israelites.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious? As, if priests, Levites, and Israelites were exempt** from the mitzva, **who** then **is to be obligated?** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary for** the *baraita* to mention that **priests** are obligated to fulfill the mitzva, as **it may enter your mind to say** as follows: **Since it is written: "You shall not wear diverse kinds, wool and linen together. You shall prepare yourself twisted cords** upon the four corners of your covering" (Deuteronomy 22:11–12), only **one who is not permitted to wear diverse kinds is obligated in** the mitzva of **ritual fringes.** With regard to **these priests, since diverse kinds are permitted for them** when they perform the Temple service, as the belt of the priestly vestments contains diverse kinds, **they should not be obligated** in the mitzva of ritual fringes. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that **although** the prohibition of diverse kinds is **permitted** for them **at the time** when they perform the Temple **service, when** it is **not the time of** the Temple **service** it is **not permitted,** and therefore priests are obligated in the mitzva of ritual fringes.

The *gemara* asks an obvious question: *peshita* — it is self-evident that *kohanim, leviyim,* and *yisraelim* are obligated. Who else would be? Why does the *braisa* need to state this? The *gemara* answers that the inclusion of *kohanim* is in fact a genuine *chiddush*, because one might have thought to exclude them. The reasoning runs as follows: the juxtaposition in the *parasha* places the prohibition of *shaatnez* (*lo silbash shaatnez, tzemer u'fishtim yachdav*) immediately before the commandment to make *gedilim* (*tzitzit*). One might infer that the obligation of *tzitzit* applies only to those who are fully governed by the prohibition of *kilayim* — that is, to ordinary Israelites. *Kohanim*, however, are permitted to wear *kilayim* in the context of *bigdei kehunah*. Since *kilayim* is effectively waived for them in their priestly garments, one might have thought they are not in the category addressed by the *parasha* and therefore not obligated in *tzitzit*.

The *gemara* rejects this: *kohanim* are only permitted *kilayim* during *idan avodah*, when they are actively performing the Temple service. *B'lo idan avodah* — when not engaged in the *avodah* — they are no more permitted *kilayim* than anyone else. Since the exemption from *kilayim* is not categorical but situational, they cannot be considered outside the ambit of the *parasha*, and the *braisa* must therefore tell us explicitly that they are included in the obligation of *tzitzit*.

This reasoning contains a striking subtext worth noting. The *gemara* frames the *hava amina* for exempting *kohanim* by associating them with *kilayim*-bearing garments — that is, with *bigdei kehunah*. The implicit logic is that *tzitzit* and *bigdei kehunah* occupy analogous conceptual space: one who already wears *bigdei kehunah* might not need *tzitzit*, and conversely, *tzitzit* functions for the *yisrael* as a kind of substitute for *bigdei kehunah*. This parallel is reinforced by Tosafot's observation, noted in connection with an earlier discussion, that the *shaatnez* present in *tzitzit* — wool strings tied to a linen garment or vice versa — is explicitly modeled on *bigdei kehunah*, and the garment bearing such *tzitzit* is categorically excluded from the laws of *kilayim* in the same way *bigdei kehunah* is. The two central priestly garments — the *kesones* (a linen garment, the *sadin*) and the *me'il* (which is entirely blue, *kulah techeles*) — correspond suggestively to the two garments that the *gemara* elsewhere treats as paradigmatic for *tzitzit*: the *sadin betzitzit* and the *tallis shekulah techeles*.

The implication — that *tzitzit* democratizes the sanctity of *bigdei kehunah*, extending to all of Israel a form of priestly identity that was formerly restricted to one group in one place — is one of the *daf*'s deepest themes. We no longer have a *Mikdash* and no longer have *kohanim* in active service; what we do have is *tefillin* (corresponding to the *tzitz*, as noted elsewhere in the *gemara*) and *tzitzit*, which serve as our *bigdei kehunah* and our form of sanctity extended throughout the world.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר בְּנָשִׁים. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״ – פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פְּרָט לִכְסוּת סוֹמֵא? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״, הֲרֵי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״? פְּרָט לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה. וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא וּלְהוֹצִיא כְּסוּת לַיְלָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי כְּסוּת סוֹמֵא שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָהּ בִּרְאִיָּה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי כְּסוּת לַיְלָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְאִיָּה אֵצֶל אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states that **Rabbi Shimon deems women exempt.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to ritual fringes it is stated: "And it shall be unto you for a fringe, **that you may look upon it** and remember all the commandments of the Lord" (Numbers 15:39). The term "that you may look" **excludes a nighttime garment,** as it is dark at night and it is therefore difficult to see. The *baraita* continues: **Do you say** that the verse serves to **exclude a nighttime garment? Or is it** to **exclude only the garment of a blind person,** who is also unable to see his ritual fringes? The *tanna* explains: **When** the verse **states:** "Of your covering, **with which you cover yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:12), the **garment of a blind person is mentioned** as being included, as the verse already stated: "Of your covering," and did not need to state: "With which you cover yourself." If so, **how do I realize** the meaning of the exclusion: **"That you may look upon it"?** It must **exclude a nighttime garment.** The Gemara asks: **But what did you see** that led you **to include the garment of a blind person** from the phrase: "With which you cover yourself," **and to exclude a nighttime garment** from the phrase: "That you may look upon it," rather than including a nighttime garment in the obligation and excluding the garment of a blind person? The Gemara answers: **I include the garment of a blind person, which is visible to others,** even though the blind person himself cannot see it, **and I exclude a nighttime garment, which is not visible** even **to others.**

Rabbi Shimon's exemption of women is grounded in a careful exegesis. The *pasuk* says *u're'isem oso* — you shall see it. Rabbi Shimon reads this as excluding garments that are not fit to be seen or are not normally seen by others, which he identifies as night garments (*khasuso layla*). A competing derivation might have suggested that the exclusion targets garments of a blind person, who cannot see the *tzitzit* at all. The *gemara* resolves this through a *ribui*: the phrase *asher t'khasse bah* — the garment you cover yourself with — is an expansion that includes even the garment of a blind person, since *others* can see it. The limiting principle is therefore not about the wearer's capacity to see but about whether the garment is the kind worn during daytime — when others see it — or nighttime — when it is not normally visible. *U'motzi ani khasuso layla she'einah b're'iyah etzel acherim* — I exclude night garments because they are not seen by others.

The Rabbis, who do not accept this exclusion, require a different reading of *asher t'khasse bah*. They use it instead to include five-cornered garments within the obligation of *tzitzit*: since a five-cornered garment contains four corners within it, *arba v'lo shalosh* — the Torah's specification of four corners excludes only three-cornered garments, while five-cornered garments are included. For the Rabbis, *u're'isem oso* does not exclude anything based on visibility; it generates the *mitzvah* of seeing and remembering generally. Rabbi Shimon, who derives the inclusion of five-cornered garments from the word *asher* alone, is free to use *asher t'khasse bah* for the exclusion of night garments.

The practical *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon that nighttime creates some form of exemption, though the *rishonim* debate whether the exemption applies to the time of night itself or to garments designated for nighttime use. This question has significant practical implications: it determines whether one makes a *beracha* upon donning *tzitzit* at night, and whether four-cornered pajamas would require *tzitzit* at all.

## Seeing & Remembering

### Shema Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ וּזְכַרְתֶּם״, רְאֵה מִצְוָה זוֹ וּזְכוֹר מִצְוָה אַחֶרֶת הַתְּלוּיָה (בּוֹ) [בָּהּ], וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? זוֹ קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע, דִּתְנַן: מֵאֵימָתַי קוֹרִין אֶת שְׁמַע בְּשַׁחֲרִית? מִשֶּׁיַּכִּיר בֵּין תְּכֵלֶת לְלָבָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, what do they do with this** phrase: **"That you may look upon it"** (Numbers 15:39), from which Rabbi Shimon derives that a nighttime garment is exempt? The Gemara answers: **They require it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"That you may look upon it and remember"** (Numbers 15:39), teaches that one should **see this mitzva** of ritual fringes **and remember another mitzva that is contingent on it. And which** mitzva **is that? It is** the mitzva of **the recitation of *Shema*. As we learned** in a mishna (Berakhot 9b): **From when may one recite *Shema* in the morning? From when** one can **distinguish between** the **sky-blue** strings **and** the **white** strings of his ritual fringes.

The *gemara* now shifts into more explicitly *aggadic* territory. Having disposed of the technical question of night-garment exemption, it asks what the Rabbis do with the phrase *u're'isem oso uzachartem* — you shall see it and remember — if they do not use it to exclude night garments or blind persons. The answer is that they use it for an entirely different *drasha*, preserved in a *braisa*: *re'eh mitzvah zo u'zchor mitzvah acheres hatluyah bah* — see this *mitzvah* and remember another *mitzvah* that is connected to it. And which *mitzvah* is that? *Krias Shema*.

The connection to *Shema* is explained by the *mishnah* in Berachot: *me'eimasai korin es Shema b'shacharis* — when does one begin reciting *Shema* in the morning? When one can distinguish between the blue and the white of the *tzitzit* strings. *Tzitzit* thus supplies the measure for when *Shema* becomes obligatory; the two *mitzvot* are functionally intertwined. Tosafot goes further and notes that *Shema* itself, according to some opinions, is rabbinic in origin — and yet the *gemara* draws the connection back to a *pasuk* in the *tzitzit* portion. The deeper point is not about the *shiur* for *Shema*, but about the *parshiyot* of *Shema* — which contain *kol mitzvos Hashem* — being evoked each time one looks at one's *tzitzit*. The same *parshiyot* appear in *tefillin* and are inscribed in *mezuzot*: they are the distillation of all the *Torah*'s obligations, the *zecher l'yetzias mitzrayim*, the reason we observe every *mitzvah*.

### Kilayim Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ וּזְכַרְתֶּם״ – רְאֵה מִצְוָה זוֹ וּזְכוֹר מִצְוָה אַחֶרֶת הַסְּמוּכָה לָהּ, וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? זוֹ מִצְוַת כִּלְאַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תִלְבַּשׁ שַׁעַטְנֵז צֶמֶר וּפִשְׁתִּים יַחְדָּו. גְּדִילִים תַּעֲשֶׂה לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The phrase **"that you may look upon it and remember"** teaches that one should **see this mitzva** of ritual fringes **and remember another mitzva that is adjacent to it** in the Torah. **And which** mitzva **is that? It is the mitzva of diverse kinds** of wool and linen, **as it is written: "You shall not wear diverse kinds, wool and linen together. You shall prepare yourself twisted cords"** (Deuteronomy 22:11–12).

A second *braisa* identifies a different *mitzvah* connected to *tzitzit*: *mitzvas kilayim*. The textual basis is the juxtaposition in the *parasha* — *shaatnez, tzemer u'fishtim yachdav* is immediately followed by *gedilim ta'aseh lach* — and the implication is that one should see the *tzitzit* and be reminded to guard against *kilayim* in other contexts. One reading of this is cautionary: be careful not to let the permitted *kilayim* of *tzitzit* lead you into a slippery slope toward *shaatnez* elsewhere. Another reading sees a broader message: the *tzitzit* reminds you of both the positive and the negative dimensions of obligation — the *asei* of doing and the *lo ta'aseh* of refraining. The juxtaposition of *kilayim* and *tzitzit* thus encapsulates the full structure of Jewish religious obligation.

This also illuminates a tension in how *tzitzit* is understood. Is its function to strengthen one's sense of positive Jewish identity and draw one into engaged, joyful observance? Or is it primarily a protective device, a reminder to guard against transgression when going out into the world? The *shema* connection leans toward the former; the *kilayim* connection leans toward the latter.

### Causation Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ וּזְכַרְתֶּם אֶת כׇּל מִצְוֹת ה׳״, שְׁקוּלָה מִצְוָה זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states: **"That you may look upon it and remember all the commandments of the Lord";** this teaches that **this mitzva** of ritual fringes **is equivalent to all the mitzvot** of the Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ וּזְכַרְתֶּם... וַעֲשִׂיתֶם״ – רְאִיָּה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי זְכִירָה, זְכִירָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי עֲשִׂיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states: **"That you may look upon it and remember** all the commandments of the Lord **and do** them." This teaches that **looking** at the ritual fringes **leads to remembering** the mitzvot, and **remembering** them **leads to doing** them.

A further *braisa* makes the sequence explicit: *u're'isem oso uzachartem va'asitem* — you shall see, you shall remember, and you shall do. This is a chain of causation. *Re'iyah mevi'ah lidei zechirah, uzechirah mevi'ah lidei asiyah* — seeing leads to remembering, and remembering leads to doing. The sequence has a striking counterpart in the verse that closes the *parasha* of *tzitzit*: *velo taturu acharei levavchem v'acharei eineichem asher atem zonim achareihem* — do not stray after your hearts and after your eyes. *Chazal* teach that the eyes and the heart are *sirsurei aveirah*, middlemen to sin: one sees something desirable, one comes to desire it, and desire leads to action. *Tzitzit* inverts this chain. Instead of the eye drawing the person toward transgression, the eye is directed toward the *tzitzit*, which draws the person toward remembering and doing. The same mechanism — seeing, desiring, acting — is redirected from the pull of *aveira* toward the pull of *mitzvah*.

### Divine Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַזָּרִיז בְּמִצְוָה זוֹ זוֹכֶה וּמְקַבֵּל פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא וְאֹתוֹ תַעֲבֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai says: Anyone who is diligent in this mitzva** of ritual fringes **merits receiving the Divine Presence.** It is **written here: "That you may look upon it [*oto*]"** (Numbers 15:39), **and it is written there: "You shall fear the Lord your God; and Him [*oto*] shall you serve"** (Deuteronomy 6:13). Just as *oto* in that verse is referring to the Divine Presence, so too in this verse it is referring to the Divine Presence.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai closes this section with the teaching: *kol hazahir bemitzvah zo zocheh umekabel pnei Shechinah* — whoever is careful with this *mitzvah* merits receiving the Divine Presence. The exegetical basis is a *gezerah shavah* between *u're'isem oto* — you shall see it — and *et Hashem Elokecha tira ve'oto ta'avod* — the *oto* of seeing God. What one ultimately sees, through the chain of *tzitzit* → remembrance → observance, is God Himself.

## Israel's Identity

### Seven Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲבִיבִין יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁסִּיבְּבָן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּמִצְוֹת, תְּפִילִּין בְּרָאשֵׁיהֶן וּתְפִילִּין בִּזְרוֹעוֹתֵיהֶן וְצִיצִית בְּבִגְדֵיהֶן וּמְזוּזָה לְפִתְחֵיהֶן, וַעֲלֵיהֶן אָמַר דָּוִד: ״שֶׁבַע בַּיּוֹם הִלַּלְתִּיךָ עַל מִשְׁפְּטֵי צִדְקֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The Jewish people are beloved, as the Holy One, Blessed be He, surrounded them with mitzvot:** They have **phylacteries on their heads, and phylacteries on their arms, and ritual fringes on their garments, and a *mezuza* for their doorways. Concerning them David said: "Seven times a day I praise You, because of Your righteous ordinances"** (Psalms 119:164). This alludes to the two phylacteries, the four ritual fringes, and the *mezuza*, which total seven.

The *gemara* presents a teaching of the Rabbis: *chavirim Yisrael shesivvan Hakadosh Baruch Hu bemitzvot* — how beloved is Israel, that God surrounded them with *mitzvot*. *Tefillin* on the head and on the arm, *tzitzit* on their garments, and a *mezuzah* on their doorpost. David expressed this in the verse *sheva bayom hillalticha al mishpetei tzidkecha* — seven times each day I praise You for Your just laws. The seven correspond to: four corners of *tzitzit*, two *tefillin*, and one *mezuzah*.

What is most striking about this teaching is not the enumeration of *mitzvot* but its framing: these are not merely obligations, they are a surrounding, an envelopment of the person in sacred markers. The *mezuzah* on the doorpost is not a personal adornment but it too marks the Jewish space, the Jewish home. The theme is identity — being recognizably, constitutively Jewish in one's person and in one's dwelling.

### David's Comfort

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּוִד לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ וְרָאָה עַצְמוֹ עוֹמֵד עָרוֹם, אָמַר: אוֹי לִי שֶׁאֶעֱמוֹד עָרוֹם בְּלֹא מִצְוָה! וְכֵיוָן שֶׁנִּזְכַּר בַּמִּילָה שֶׁבִּבְשָׂרוֹ, נִתְיַישְּׁבָה דַּעְתּוֹ. לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּצָא, אָמַר עָלֶיהָ שִׁירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַמְנַצֵּחַ עַל הַשְּׁמִינִית מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד״, עַל מִילָה שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בַּשְּׁמִינִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And when David entered the bathhouse and saw himself standing naked, he said: Woe to me that that I stand naked without** any **mitzva. But once he remembered the** mitzva of **circumcision that was in his flesh his mind was put at ease,** as he realized he was still accompanied by this mitzva. **After he left** the bathhouse, **he recited a song about** the mitzva of circumcision, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"For the leader, on the *Sheminith*: A Psalm of David"** (Psalms 12:1). This is interpreted as a psalm **about circumcision, which was given** to be performed **on the eighth [*bashemini*]** day of the baby's life.

This theme is illustrated immediately by the account of David entering the bathhouse. Upon seeing himself naked — *arom*, unadorned — he cried out: *oy li she'e'emod arum belo mitzvah* — woe is me that I stand naked without *mitzvot*. The very absence of the physical markers of Jewish identity caused him distress. Then he remembered the *brit milah* inscribed in his flesh, and *nityashvah da'ato* — his mind was settled. He was not, after all, without a *mitzvah*. The *brit milah* is indelible in a way that *tefillin* and *tzitzit* are not: one can remove a garment, one cannot remove the mark of the covenant.

After leaving the bathhouse, where Torah speech would have been inappropriate, David sang — and the psalm he sang is *lamenatze'ach al hashminit mizmor ledavid*, understood as referring to the eighth, the *brit milah* given on the eighth day. The *Maharsha* adds that *milah* was also the eighth *mitzvah* given to the world, after the seven *mitzvot* of the children of Noah.

This narrative raises a question — implicit in the *gemara*, though the lecturer notes it explicitly — about what this framework says about women, who are not obligated in the full complement of these surrounding *mitzvot* and for whom no mark is inscribed in the flesh. The question is left open, an important one to sit with.

### Three-Cord String

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ תְּפִילִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ וּתְפִילִּין בִּזְרוֹעוֹ וְצִיצִית בְּבִגְדוֹ וּמְזוּזָה בְּפִתְחוֹ, הַכֹּל בְּחִיזּוּק שֶׁלֹּא יֶחֱטָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַחוּט הַמְשֻׁלָּשׁ לֹא בִמְהֵרָה יִנָּתֵק״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״חֹנֶה מַלְאַךְ ה׳ סָבִיב לִירֵאָיו וַיְחַלְּצֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: Anyone who has phylacteries on his head, phylacteries on his arm, ritual fringes on his garment, and a *mezuza* on his doorway is strengthened** from **all** sides **so that he will not sin, as it is stated** in the verse: **"And a threefold cord is not quickly broken"** (Ecclesiastes 4:12). This is interpreted as an allusion to the three mitzvot of phylacteries, ritual fringes, and *mezuza*. **And** the verse **states: "The angel of the Lord encamps round about them that fear Him, and delivers them"** (Psalms 34:8). This is interpreted to mean that the angel of the Lord surrounds those who fulfill the mitzvot and saves them from sin.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds: *kol sheyeish lo tefillin berosho utefillin bizro'o vetzitzit bevigdo umezuzah befitcho* — one who has *tefillin* on his head and arm, *tzitzit* on his garment, and a *mezuzah* on his doorpost — *hakol bechizuk shelo yecheta*, it is with certainty he will not sin. The proof text is *vehachut hameshulash lo bimehera yinatek* — a three-cord string is not easily broken. The three cords are *tzitzit*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzah*. Together they form a reinforcing structure that protects a person from sin.

The *gemara* appends: *omer choneh malach Hashem saviv liyre'av vayechaltzem* — God's angel encamps around those who fear Him and delivers them. This is the point the Rambam makes in his discussion of *mezuzah*: the protective force is not magical. It is the function of these *mitzvot* as reminders — constant, embodied reminders of obligation — that keeps a person from sin. The concern is always that the *mezuzah* in particular may come to be treated as an amulet, a talisman, rather than as a reminder, and the *gemara* here is insisting on the latter understanding.

## Cosmic Blue

### Rabbi Meir Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מָה נִשְׁתַּנָּה תְּכֵלֶת מִכׇּל מִינֵי צִבְעוֹנִין? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַתְּכֵלֶת דּוֹמֶה לַיָּם, וְיָם דּוֹמֶה לָרָקִיעַ, וְרָקִיעַ לְכִסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתַחַת רַגְלָיו כְּמַעֲשֵׂה לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר״, וּכְתִיב: ״כְּמַרְאֵה אֶבֶן סַפִּיר דְּמוּת כִּסֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: What is different about *tekhelet* from all** other **types of colors** such that it was chosen for the mitzva of ritual fringes? It is **because *tekhelet* is similar** in its color **to** the **sea, and** the **sea is similar to** the **sky, and** the **sky is similar to the Throne of Glory, as it is stated:** "And they saw the God of Israel; **and there was under His feet the like of a paved work of sapphire stone, and the like of the very heaven for clearness"** (Exodus 24:10), indicating that the sky is like a sapphire brickwork. **And it is written: "The likeness of a throne, as the appearance of a sapphire stone"** (Ezekiel 1:26).

The *daf* closes with a teaching of Rabbi Meir: *mah nishtanah techeilet mikol minei tzeva'onim* — what distinguishes *techelet* from all other colors? *Mipnei shehatecheilet domeh layam veyam domeh larakia, verakia lekise hakavod* — because *techelet* resembles the sea, and the sea resembles the sky, and the sky resembles the *Kisse Hakavod*, the Throne of Glory. The derivation is supported by two *pesukim*: *vetahat raglav kema'aseh livnat hasappir uke'etzem hashamayim latohar* — under God's feet was like a sapphire pavement, like the essence of the heavens in its purity — and *kemareh even sappir demut kisei* — like the appearance of sapphire stone was the likeness of the throne.

The connection is not direct. *Techelet* is not literally the color of the *Kisse Hakavod*. It is blue like the sea, the sea is blue like the sky, the sky is blue like the *sapir* of the divine pavement, and that *sapir* is the blue of the Throne of Glory. Each link in the chain is one of resemblance, not identity.

### Keren Ora Insight

The *Keren Ora* offers an insight that deepens the significance of this chain. The point, he argues, is not merely that *techelet* eventually, through many derivations, evokes the divine. The chain works in both directions. If *techelet* leads upward — from ocean to sky to throne — then the throne leads downward: the sky, the ocean, the world of nature, all relate to God. Seeing the *techelet* on one's *tzitzit* does not merely prompt a thought of God as an abstract terminal point; it links everything in the natural world to God. The ocean reflects God; the sky reflects God; the entire visible world is a manifestation of the divine presence.

This returns us to the tension the *daf* has traced throughout. Is *tzitzit* primarily about protection — a reminder to guard against *kilayim*, a cord that binds one against sin? Or is it about identity and engagement — a garment that marks the wearer as Jewish, that connects every aspect of the natural world to God, that turns the act of going out into the world into an encounter with the divine? The *daf* holds both possibilities in view without resolving them. To wear *tzitzit* is to be protected, and to be marked, and to see God in the sea, the sky, and the world through which one moves.
