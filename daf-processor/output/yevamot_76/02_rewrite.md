# Yevamot 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Penile injuries

### Prior interpretations

Today's daf is 76, and we continue the important discussion surrounding the halachic definition of *patzua daka u'krut shofcha*. Yesterday we noted how some of these definitions were changed radically — or interpreted in a radically narrow way — after the time of the Gemara, in order to permit men with these conditions to marry. Tosafot addresses the distinction between injury and complete removal, suggesting that the latter may not fall under the prohibition. Rav Moshe takes the position that even conditions explicitly mentioned in the Gemara are not disqualifying if we now know that they do not lead to infertility, since the Gemara's physical descriptions assumed infertility as their operative concern. Rav Herzog goes further, arguing that if a medical procedure was performed to treat a pre-existing medical condition, the entire situation is considered *b'yedei shamayim* and therefore excluded from the prohibition. With that context established, we continue in the Gemara at the bottom of 75b.

### Komus and Mardik cases

Mar Zutra, in the name of Rav Papa, states the *halacha*: whether the penis was thinned and tapered like a quill (*kekomus*) or hollowed out like a drainpipe (*kemardik*), either way it is *kashera* — valid. Presumably, neither condition constitutes a sufficiently serious wound, likely because neither necessarily leads to infertility, which is the Gemara's central concern.

Ravina then transmitted a question to Mareimar in the name of Mar Zutra: when we say these injuries are *kashera*, are we referring to injuries above the corona — meaning toward the body — or below the corona, toward the tip? The Gemara immediately answers: obviously we are discussing injuries above the corona, toward the body. Injuries below the corona, toward the tip, are not a problem even if the organ is completely severed at that point. As the Gemara remarks, *pshita d'l'mata me'hatara* — it is self-evident that such injuries would not be a concern. The Gemara then adds that Ravina's purpose in posing the question to Mareimar was not truly to resolve a doubt, but to test him.

There follows a case from Masa M'chasya: Mar Bar Rav Ashi performed a procedure on a man whose condition resembled the *mardik* case, and surgically converted it into what resembled the *komus* case, thereby rendering it valid. Rashi explains that this was the nature of the intervention — transforming one configuration into the other — though the Gemara moves forward without elaborating further.

### Blocked semen passage

The Gemara then introduces a case from Pumbedita. The pathway of the semen had become blocked (*istatam*), and the semen was emerging from the urinary tract rather than through its natural channel. Rav Bibi bar Abaye initially ruled that the man was still valid — after all, the semen was still emerging, so what difference did the pathway make? Rav Papa rebuked him, and Rashi notes that his formulation alludes to Abaye's lineage from the house of Eli, which carried a divine curse — the implication being that Rav Bibi spoke carelessly. Rav Papa's substantive point is: *bimkomo mevashelah shelo bimkomo lo mevashelah* — when semen travels through its natural passage, it is properly warmed by the body and is capable of causing conception; when it travels through the urinary tract, this does not occur, and the man is therefore infertile.

What is striking about this case is that there is no obvious physical injury of the kind the Gemara elsewhere describes — no severing, no crushing. The passage was simply *istatam*, blocked or clogged. One might ask: since when does a blocked duct fall into the category of *kerus shofcha* or *patzua daka*? The Gemara seems to imply that any physical condition causing infertility in this manner is disqualifying, even without a classic wound. One possibility, raised in discussion, is that the blockage may itself have resulted from a physical injury rather than occurring *b'yedei shamayim* — which would explain both why it is disqualifying and why the *b'yedei shamayim* exemption does not apply here.

### Corona hole test

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: if a hole existed in the penis — the classic case of *nikaiv* — and it subsequently became sealed (*nistam*), the determining criterion is *kol she'ilu nikri v'nikra pasul* — if, at the moment of seminal emission (*keri*), the hole would reopen, then the man is invalid. The reasoning is presumably that a hole that reopens upon ejaculation demonstrates that the channel has not truly healed, with direct implications for fertility. If it does not reopen, he is *kasher*.

Rava then raised the question: where exactly is this hole located? If it is below the corona — toward the tip — even complete severance would be permissible, so that cannot be the scenario. It must therefore be speaking of a hole in the corona itself. A parallel statement is then cited in the name of Shmuel through Rav Mari bar Mar citing Mar Ukva, specifying explicitly that we are discussing a hole *in the corona itself* (*nikaiv ba'atarah atzmah*), with the same rule applying: if it would reopen upon ejaculation, *pasul*; if not, *kasher*.

Rabbah bar Brei d'Rabba then sent an inquiry to Rav Yosef: *heichi avdinan* — how do we actually determine this in practice? The problem is circular: one cannot simply instruct the man to examine himself after relations with his wife, because the entire question is whether he is permitted to have relations with his wife in the first place. Some procedure for inducing ejaculation in a controlled way is needed.

### Ejaculation methods

Rav Yosef replied: bring hot barley bread, place it near the anus, and this will induce ejaculation (*mikrei*), after which the organ can be inspected. Abaye objected: does one assume that everyone is as righteous as Yaakov Avinu? The Gemara understands *kochi v'reishit oni* — Reuven is described as the first of his father's strength — to mean that the conception of Reuven was Yaakov's first seminal emission ever; he was so scrupulous that he had never experienced one prior to his first conjugal act with Leah. Most men, Abaye observed, require far less inducement. Instead, Abaye suggested: pass brightly colored women's garments before him (*bigdei tzvonin*), and for most men that will be sufficient stimulation to cause ejaculation.

Rava countered in the opposite direction: does one assume that everyone is as licentious as Barzillai the Gileadite, who the Gemara elsewhere describes as *shatuf b'zimah* — immersed in licentiousness? Colored garments may not be sufficient for everyone; many men require more. Since no satisfactory middle ground could be identified between the colored garments and the more invasive medical procedure, the Gemara concludes *mechvarta k'dishninan me'ikara* — the original method, the barley bread procedure, stands.

What is noteworthy here is that the Gemara has no apparent concern about *zera l'vatala* or *hirhurim* in this context. The barley procedure, being purely physiological, may not involve *hirhurim* at all. But the *bigdei tzvonin* presumably do. The Gemara's silence suggests that when ejaculation is induced for a legitimate medical or halachic purpose, neither concern applies. This has obvious relevance to the contemporary responsa literature on procuring semen from a husband for fertility treatment involving that same couple — a topic for which this *sugya* serves as a foundational model.

## Healing defects

### Hole closure

The Gemara now presents a *Baraita*: *nikaiv pasul mipnei shehu shotet* — if there is a hole, it is invalid because the semen dribbles rather than flowing in a proper stream. This is a restatement and formalization of what was discussed previously. *Nistam kasher mipnei shemolid* — if it becomes sealed, it is valid because the man can now father children. *Ve-zehu pasul she-chozer l'hechsheiro* — and this is a case of invalidity that can revert to validity through healing. The man was in the category of *kerus shofchah*, but if the hole closes, he is restored to fitness.

### Scab coverage issue

The Gemara then asks: *zehu l'mautei mai* — what does the word *zehu* ("this is") come to exclude? The answer: it excludes a case where what appears to be closure is in fact only a *kerum she'alah machamas makah* — a membrane or scab that has grown over a wound. In the laws of *treifot* pertaining to the lung, such a covering is not considered a genuine seal; the lung is still regarded as perforated, and the animal remains *treifah*. The analogy here is that a mere scab over a hole in the relevant organ does not constitute true closure. The hole remains, and the man's status is unchanged.

This connects to the broader halachic question of *glatt* — literally "smooth." The entire debate around the smoothness of the lung turns on whether abrasions, *sirchos*, or membranous growths are indicative of an underlying perforation or are independently problematic. A *kerum*, properly understood, is not a scab but a membrane — the *sircha* is closer to what one would call a scab in common usage. In any case, the principle is clear: genuine physical closure restores validity; superficial coverage does not.

### Wound healing methods

Rav Idi bar Avin sent an inquiry to Abaye: *heichi avdinan* — if there is a hole and we want the body to close it, what do we do to facilitate that healing? Abaye replied with the following protocol. Take a barley kernel and scratch the area around the hole, drawing some blood and stimulating the body's healing response. Then apply fat to the area, rubbing it in, to promote scabbing and filling. Finally, obtain a large ant — *gamla*, camel-sized — place it at the wound site, allow it to bite the area, and then sever its head, leaving the head embedded. As the head decomposes, it may serve as raw material for the body to seal the wound.

An important clarification: one must scratch specifically with barley and not with iron, because *de-mizraf zarif* — iron causes inflammation rather than the beneficial scabbing one wants. This reflects a practical awareness that metal contact with a wound produces a different physiological response than a gentler abrasion. And all of this applies only to a small hole (*katana*); for a large one, any such closure will ultimately peel away and prove ineffective.

### Dual urination

Rava bar Rav Huna ruled: if a man urinates from two places — apparently from some opening in the penis in addition to its normal tip, possibly indicating that the urine is passing through two internal channels — he is *pasul*. The same underlying puzzle applies here as in the case of the blocked semen passage: why does a condition that is not obviously a classic physical wound fall under these categories? Unless, again, the presumption is that such a condition results from some form of physical injury rather than arising *b'yedei shamayim*.

Rava then ruled: *leit hilcheta* — the halacha does not follow Rava bar Rav Huna in either of his rulings. Neither regarding the son's statement about urination from two places, nor regarding the father Rav Huna's statement, which held that women who engage in sexual contact with one another (*nashim hamisolelot zo b'zo*) are *psulot l'kehuna*, disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. Rashi understands the disqualification to affect eligibility for a *kohen gadol*, implicating the requirement that he marry a *betulah*. Tosafot suggests it relates to the category of *zona* for an ordinary *kohen*. The Gemara rules that neither position is accepted as *halacha*.

The discussion here opens onto a range of fascinating contemporary questions. The Gemara's treatment of female same-sex contact as *pritzuta b'alma* — licentiousness but not halachically constituted sexual intercourse — reflects the principle that *halacha* defines sexual intercourse as requiring penile penetration, whether vaginal or anal. Without that element, there can be no *halachic* act of sex, and consequently no *zona*, no loss of *betulah* status in the operative halachic sense, and arguably no *zera l'vatala* and no *hirhurim* in the same way that male sexual activity generates those concerns. The Gemara confirms this by invoking even Rabbi Elazar's stringent position — that extramarital relations between an unmarried man and an unmarried woman constitute *znut* — and limiting it explicitly: *hatam mili ish*, that applies when there is a man, meaning an act of halachically defined intercourse. *Aval isha* — when only women are involved, it cannot rise to that level.

## Ptzua marriage

### Marriage restrictions

Having worked through the physical definitions, the Gemara turns to the halachic consequences for marriage. A *patzua daka* or *krut shofcha* is *mutar b'giyoret u'meshuchreret* — permitted to marry a convert or a freed slave, women who were not born into the Jewish people. The basis is *de'eino assur ella m'lavo b'kahal* — the prohibition is specifically against entering the *kahal*, meaning against marrying those who are natural-born members of the community. A *giyoret* is not included in that category. The verse is: *lo yavo patzua daka u'krut shofcha b'kehal Hashem*.

This leniency, while welcome, does prompt discomfort. The Torah's principle of *k'ger k'ezrach* implies that a convert stands fully equal to a born Jew, yet in several halachic contexts — including the law of *tum'at yoledet* which required a separate derivation to include a *giyoret*, and here — converts are treated as outside the primary *kahal*. This tension between the ideal of full conversion and the practical halachic categorizations is a recurring theme.

### Priestly status

The question is then posed to Rav Sheshet: a *patzua daka* who is also a *kohen* — what is his status regarding marriage to a *giyoret* or *meshuchreret*? We already know from an earlier *mishnah* that a *patzua daka kohen* retains his priestly status and may eat *terumah*. His *kedushat kehuna* is intact. Given that, should the ordinary restrictions on whom a *kohen* may marry still apply to him? Or, since he is now excluded from the *kahal* with respect to marriage, does that exclusion open up categories of women who would ordinarily be forbidden to a *kohen*?

This is a genuine tension: on one side, his *kedushah* remains in every domain where a physical injury is irrelevant; on the other, the very fact that he is now categorized as outside the *kahal* for marriage purposes may simultaneously remove certain barriers. As was clarified in discussion: the question is not about a *gerushah*, whom a *patzua daka kohen* still cannot marry regardless, but specifically about a *giyoret* — a woman who is excluded from the *kahal* but would otherwise be permissible to a *yisrael*.

### Excluded status benefit

Rav Sheshet responded: *tanituha* — the answer has already been taught. The parallel case involves a *patzua daka yisrael* and a *netina* — a woman descended from the Gibeonites, members of the seven Canaanite nations who converted in the time of Yehoshua by subterfuge. Ordinarily a *yisrael* may not marry someone from the *shivat amamim*, just as a *kohen* may not marry a *giyoret*. Yet a *patzua daka yisrael* is permitted to marry a *netina*. If he still retained his full *kedushah* — including the prohibition *lo titchaten bam* — this should be forbidden. Since it is permitted, it must be that exclusion from the *kahal* opens up marriage to those who are themselves outside that category.

Rava, however, raised a complication. The prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* for the *shivat amamim* is not straightforwardly biblical once they convert. When they are still non-Jews, the verse *lo titchaten bam* cannot mean prohibiting marriage in the halachic sense, since their *kiddushin* would not take effect. The prohibition must therefore be speaking of after conversion. But Rava then suggests that the rabbinic extension of this prohibition — covering converts from the *shivat amamim* — was enacted only where children are in the equation, since the Torah's concern (*ki yasir et bincha me'acharai*) is about children being led astray. A *patzua daka* who cannot have children was never included in that rabbinic decree. So the leniency regarding the *netina* may be rabbinic in character and cannot be used to prove the case of a *kohen* and a *giyoret*.

## Mamzer and Netin

### Excluded analogy

But this argument is then challenged by Rava himself. If the *netina* case is merely rabbinic, consider: a *mamzer* can certainly have children, and yet the *mishnah* teaches *mamzerim u'nesinim mutarim lavo zeh b'zeh* — a *mamzer* and a *netin* may marry one another. If the criterion were about fertility, a *mamzer* should be forbidden to a *netina*. Evidently the principle is different: *ki gazru Rabbanan b'ksherim, b'psulim lo gazru* — when the Rabbis enacted the prohibition against marrying *nesinim*, they only applied it to those who are halachically *kasher*. Those who are themselves in a category of *pesul* — like *mamzer* and *patzua daka* — were not included. So the leniency has a different basis than fertility.

### Biblical vs rabbinic

Rava then reconsidered his own earlier position entirely: *lav milsa hi d'amri* — what I said was not correct. He had claimed that the biblical prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* applies only to the *shivat amamim* while they are non-Jews, and that the prohibition post-conversion is rabbinic. But in fact, when the nations are non-Jews, their *kiddushin* has no binding effect — *leis lehu chasnus*. The very concept of marriage (*chasnus*) only becomes relevant after conversion. Therefore, the Torah's *lo titchaten bam* must be speaking of the prohibition even after conversion — which means it is a *d'oraisa* prohibition. And yet, despite this being biblical, a *patzua daka* and a *mamzer* are permitted to marry *nesinim*. This demonstrates the principle clearly: once someone is excluded from *kahal Hashem*, they are freed from certain restrictions vis-à-vis others who are similarly outside that category. And this principle applies equally to a *kohen* who is a *patzua daka* — despite his *kedushat kehuna*, his exclusion from *kahal Hashem* for marriage purposes opens the door to marry a *giyoret*. This is indeed the ruling recorded in the *Shulchan Aruch*, Even HaEzer, siman 5, se'if 1, which states explicitly that even a *kohen* who is a *patzua daka* is permitted to marry a *giyoret* or *meshuchreret*, since he is no longer within his [full marital] *kedushah* in that domain.

### Shlomo and Egypt

Rav Yosef challenged the position that the biblical *lo titchaten bam* applies only after conversion. If that were so, he asked, how could the verse say *vayitchaten Shlomo es bat Paroh melech Mitzrayim* — that Shlomo married the daughter of Pharaoh? The Gemara responds that she converted (*digayara*). But one might raise an obvious objection: was it not a rule that converts were not accepted in the times of David and Shlomo due to suspicion of ulterior motives — joining the king's household for material benefit? The Gemara answers: the concern about *shulchan melachim* does not apply to bat Paroh herself, since she already came from a royal table; she had nothing to gain materially from conversion. So she was accepted.

But the Gemara presses further: even if she converted, she was a *mitzrit* of the first generation. The verse states *lo tita'ev mitzri ki ger hayita b'artzo*, and then *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem* — only the third generation of a Mitzri convert may enter the *kahal*. A first-generation *mitzrit* converting would not be eligible to marry into the *kahal* for two more generations. So how was Shlomo permitted to marry her?

To illustrate that the Mitzri prohibition was still operative in that era, the Gemara cites a *Baraita* in which Rabbi Yehuda reports: "I had a colleague, Minyamin the Mitzri convert, from among the students of Rabbi Akiva. He told me: I am a first-generation Mitzri convert, and I married a first-generation Mitzrit. I intend to have my son marry a second-generation Mitzrit, so that my grandson will be a third-generation [convert of Mitzri descent] and permitted to enter the *kahal*." This shows that even in the time of Rabbi Akiva — well after Sancheriv had displaced populations — the *halacha* still treated the Mitzri prohibition as operative. The governing principle is *holchin achar hapesul*: if a first-generation marries a second-generation, the child's status follows the more disqualified line and is counted as second-generation; only when a second-generation marries a second-generation does the child achieve third-generation status and full eligibility.

Since the Mitzri prohibition was still in effect in the era of the Talmudic Sages, it must have been in effect in Shlomo's time as well, making bat Paroh's status highly problematic. How then did Shlomo marry her?

Rav Papa answered: *anan miShlomo leika d'lisof* — are we really going to ask questions from Shlomo? The implication is that Shlomo transgressed. But the Gemara then revisits this and suggests: *Shlomo lo nasiv midi* — Shlomo did not actually marry them. The verse says: *min hagoyim asher amar Hashem el bnei Yisrael lo tavo'u bahem... bahem davak Shlomo l'ahava* — unto them Shlomo cleaved for love. The word *davak*, cleaved, indicates passionate attachment, not formal marriage. He was bound to them deeply, but did not legally marry them.

The Gemara then acknowledges the residual difficulty: but the verse explicitly says *vayitchaten* — he married. The answer: *mitoch ahava yeseirah she'ahava, ma'alah alav ke'ilu yitchaten bah* — because of the exceptional love he bore her, it was as though he had married her, but technically he had not. This is, the Gemara acknowledges, a somewhat forced reading.

Tosafot raises an important related point: after Sancheriv displaced the nations, shouldn't we simply say that the people living in Egypt are no longer the halachic *mitzrim* of the Torah? Tosafot quotes a *Tosefta* in which Rabbi Akiva told Minyamin directly: *ta'isa* — you made a mistake. You think you are a *mitzri*, but Sancheriv already came and scrambled all the nations (*bilbel kol ha'olam*), and therefore no one living now can be considered a halachic *mitzri*. The Gemara itself, however, maintains that the identification persisted — not just in Shlomo's time but even in the era of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Akiva.

One broader halachic point emerges from this entire discussion. The prohibitions we have been examining — *patzua daka*, *mamzer*, *mitzri*, *amoni* — concern the formal institution of *nisu'in*, marriage. The question of whether the prohibition is violated by sexual relations outside the framework of marriage is a distinct and nuanced question. As the Gemara implies here, the *lo titchaten* formulation presupposes a formal act of marriage. By contrast, prohibitions that involve *kares* — the severe biblical penalties for incestuous or adulterous relations — are entirely about the act of intercourse, and marriage is not even a relevant concept in those contexts since *kiddushin* would not take effect.

## Intermarriage rules

### Amoni/Moavi rules

The *mishnah* now turns to the generational restrictions on various nations joining the *kahal*. *Amoni u'Moavi asurin v'isuran issur olam* — an Ammonite and a Moabite are forbidden forever. Even *gam dor asiri* — a tenth generation — may not enter the *kahal* of Hashem. Their females, however, are *mutarot miyad* — immediately permissible upon conversion.

### Mitzri/Edom rules

A Mitzri or an Edomite is forbidden only *ad shelosha dorot* — for three generations, after which *dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem*. Here the *mishnah* specifies *echad zecharim v'echad nekivot* — both males and females must wait three generations. This is notable: the more severe prohibition of *amoni u'moavi*, which is permanent for males, is actually more lenient for females. The less severe prohibition of *mitzri* and *edomi* applies equally to both genders.

### Shimon's logic

Rabbi Shimon rules that females of Egyptian and Edomite descent should be *mutarot miyad*, just as female Ammonites and Moabites are. He advances a *kal vachomer*: if in the case where males are permanently forbidden — *amoni u'moavi* — the females are immediately permitted, then certainly in the case where males are forbidden only for three generations — *mitzri v'edomi* — the females should be permitted immediately. The Sages responded: if you have a tradition (*halacha*) we will accept it, but if you are arguing by logic (*ledin*), we have a refutation of your *kal vachomer*. Rabbi Shimon replied: I am not relying solely on logic; I am stating this as a received *halacha*. Whether that tradition was ultimately accepted will emerge from the Gemara's continued discussion.

## David and Ruth

### Saul's inquiry

The Gemara now grounds the rule that a female Ammonite or Moabite is *muteret miyad* in a narrative from *Navi*. Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse: *v'chirot Shaul et David yotzei likrat haplishti* — when Shaul saw David going out to confront the Philistine, Goliath, *amar el Avner sar hatzava* — he said to Avner, his general — *ben mi zeh hana'ar* — whose son is this young man?

This seems puzzling. Surely Shaul knew who David was — David had been his personal armor-bearer, and the verse itself records *vayeehavehu me'od vayehi lo nosei keilim* — Shaul loved him greatly and David bore his weapons. Furthermore, Yishai was not an obscure figure: the verse describes him as *zaken ba b'anashim*, a man of great stature in the time of Shaul, and the Gemara cites Rav (and some say Rabbi Abba): *zeh Yishai avi David shenichnas b'uchlusa v'yatza b'uchlusa* — whenever Yishai entered or departed, he was accompanied by tens of thousands. Rashi notes that Yishai commanded 600,000. How could Avner not have known him?

The Gemara resolves: Shaul was not asking about David's identity or about Yishai. *Ei mi'Peretz asi ei mi'Zerach asi* — what he wanted to know was: does David descend from Perez or from Zerach, the two lineages of Yehuda? *Ei mi'Peretz asi, malka havei* — if from Perez, Shaul had reason for concern. The king is said to *poretz*, to burst through boundaries without restraint — that is the nature of royal power. Descent from Perez would indicate David was destined for kingship. *Ei mi'Zerach asi, chashiva b'alma havei* — if from Zerach, the shining one, David would have prestige but not threaten the throne.

What provoked Shaul's anxiety at precisely this moment? The verse records that Shaul dressed David in his own garments — *madav kemidoto* — and David wore them to measure, fitting him as perfectly as they fit Shaul, who was *mishichmo vamala gavoah mikol ha'am*, a head taller than all the people. David matched Shaul physically, and Shaul, seeing before him a young man of equal stature and evident charisma, felt genuinely threatened.

### Doeg's objection

At this point, *Doeg ha'Edomi* intervened. He said to Shaul: *ad she'ata mash'il alav im hagun hu l'malchus im lav* — before you worry whether he is of royal lineage, ask a more fundamental question: *sha'al alav im ra'uy lavo b'kahal im lav* — is he even eligible to be part of the *kahal*? *Mai ta'ama deka'asi me'Rus haMoaviyah* — because he descends from Ruth the Moabitess.

Avner responded: *tanina, Amoni velo Amonit, Moavi velo Moavit* — we have a tradition: the prohibition is *Amoni*, not *Amonit*; *Moavi*, not *Moavit*. A female Moabitess is immediately permitted. Doeg challenged Avner's silence with a counter-argument: very well, but then say *Mitzri velo Mitzrit* — why don't we apply the same logic to Egypt and permit female Egyptian converts immediately?

## Intermarriage rules (II)

### Greeting logic

The Gemara explains the textual basis for distinguishing between male and female Ammonites and Moabites, and thereby answering Doeg's challenge about Egypt. The verse gives a specific reason for the prohibition: *al asher lo kidmu etchem balechem uvamayim* — because they did not come out to greet you with bread and water. This reason generates the limited application: *darko shel ish lekadem velo darko shel isha lekadem* — it is the way of a man to go out and greet, not the way of a woman. Therefore, only the men can be held culpable for the failure to extend hospitality; the women cannot. This is the basis for reading *Amoni* and not *Amonit*, *Moavi* and not *Moavit*.

## David and Ruth (II)

### Beis HaMedrash

Doeg was not satisfied. He pressed: if men should have gone out to greet the men and women should have gone out to greet the women — *anashim likrat anashim venashim likrat nashim* — then the women of Moab were equally remiss for not greeting the women of Israel. Why should they be exempt? Avner fell silent. He had no ready answer.

Shaul then directed: *tze ushal b'veis hamedrash* — go and pose this question in the *Beis HaMedrash*. The Gemara notes the wordplay: Shaul called David an *elem* in this verse rather than a *na'ar* as he did earlier, and the Gemara reads this as Shaul saying *halacha nit'almah mimcha* — the *halacha* has been hidden from you; Avner does not know the answer. Go to the *Beis HaMedrash* and find out.

When they arrived at the *Beis HaMedrash*, the ruling was the same as Avner had stated: *Amoni velo Amonit, Moavi velo Moavit*. But Doeg was there in the *Beis HaMedrash* and challenged everyone with all the same objections. *Ba'i le'achruzei alei* — he sought to make a public proclamation disqualifying David. The entire *Beis HaMedrash* fell silent before his challenge. How David's lineage was ultimately vindicated and his legitimacy as king of Israel established — that we will take up tomorrow.
