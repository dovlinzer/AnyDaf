# Yevamot 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Penile injuries

### Prior interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמּוּלָאֵי, אָמְרִיתוּ מִילֵּי מוּלְיָתָא? בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — מְבַשְּׁלָה, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — לָא מְבַשְּׁלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **from truncated [*mimula'ei*] people,** as Rav Beivai's family traced their lineage to the house of Eli, all of whose descendants were destined to be short-lived (see I Samuel 2:31), **you speak truncated [*mulayata*]** and unsound **matters.** When the semen passes through **its** proper **place, it fertilizes;** but if it does **not** pass through **its** proper **place, it does not fertilize.**

Today's daf is 76, and we continue the important discussion surrounding the halachic definition of *patzua daka u'krut shofcha*. Yesterday we noted how some of these definitions were changed radically — or interpreted in a radically narrow way — after the time of the Gemara, in order to permit men with these conditions to marry. Tosafot addresses the distinction between injury and complete removal, suggesting that the latter may not fall under the prohibition. Rav Moshe takes the position that even conditions explicitly mentioned in the Gemara are not disqualifying if we now know that they do not lead to infertility, since the Gemara's physical descriptions assumed infertility as their operative concern. Rav Herzog goes further, arguing that if a medical procedure was performed to treat a pre-existing medical condition, the entire situation is considered *b'yedei shamayim* and therefore excluded from the prohibition. With that context established, we continue in the Gemara at the bottom of 75b.

### Komus and Mardik cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְמָרִימָר, הָכִי אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הִלְכְתָא, בֵּין כְּקוּלְמוֹס בֵּין כְּמַרְזֵב — כְּשֵׁרָה. מִיהוּ, מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: לְמַטָּה מֵעֲטָרָה, אוֹ לְמַעְלָה? פְּשִׁיטָא דִּלְמַעְלָה מֵעֲטָרָה, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לְמַטָּה מֵעֲטָרָה — אֲפִילּוּ נִכְרַת הַגִּיד נָמֵי. וְרָבִינָא לְשַׁבּוֹשֵׁי לְמָרִימָר הוּא דְּבָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Mareimar** that **Mar Zutra said in the name of Rav Pappa as follows: The *halakha* is** that **whether** the man's member was cut **like a quill or like a gutter,** he is **fit. However, he raised a dilemma** as to whether this is referring to a cut **below the corona,** i.e., on the corona itself, **or above** it, on that part of the penis that is closer to the body. The Gemara answers: It **is obvious** that this is referring **to** a cut **above the corona, as, if it enters your mind** that it is referring **to below the corona,** i.e., on the corona itself, this would be difficult because **even** if the end of **the member was** completely **severed,** he would **also** be fit, provided that a hairsbreadth of the corona remained. The Gemara adds: **And Ravina** asked this question only because **he wanted to mislead Mereimar** and test his understanding of the issue.

Mar Zutra, in the name of Rav Papa, states the *halacha*: whether the penis was thinned and tapered like a quill (*kekomus*) or hollowed out like a drainpipe (*kemardik*), either way it is *kashera* — valid. Presumably, neither condition constitutes a sufficiently serious wound, likely because neither necessarily leads to infertility, which is the Gemara's central concern.

Ravina then transmitted a question to Mareimar in the name of Mar Zutra: when we say these injuries are *kashera*, are we referring to injuries above the corona — meaning toward the body — or below the corona, toward the tip? The Gemara immediately answers: obviously we are discussing injuries above the corona, toward the body. Injuries below the corona, toward the tip, are not a problem even if the organ is completely severed at that point. As the Gemara remarks, *pshita d'l'mata me'hatara* — it is self-evident that such injuries would not be a concern. The Gemara then adds that Ravina's purpose in posing the question to Mareimar was not truly to resolve a doubt, but to test him.

There follows a case from Masa M'chasya: Mar Bar Rav Ashi performed a procedure on a man whose condition resembled the *mardik* case, and surgically converted it into what resembled the *komus* case, thereby rendering it valid. Rashi explains that this was the nature of the intervention — transforming one configuration into the other — though the Gemara moves forward without elaborating further.

### Blocked semen passage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עוֹבָדָא דַּהֲוָה בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, אִיסְתְּתִים גּוּבְתָּא דְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, וְאַפֵּיק בִּמְקוֹם קְטַנִּים. סְבַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי לְאַכְשׁוֹרֵי. אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאַתּוּ מִמּוּלָאֵי, אָמְרִיתוּ מִילֵּי מוּלְיָתָא? בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — מְבַשְּׁלָה, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — לָא מְבַשְּׁלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A certain incident** actually **occurred in** the town of **Pumbedita,** where **the seminal duct** of a certain man **became blocked, and he emitted** semen **through the urinary duct. Rav Beivai bar Abaye thought to render him fit,** as his genitals were neither crushed nor severed. **Rav Pappi said: Because you come from truncated [*mimula'ei*] people,** as Rav Beivai's family traced their lineage to the house of Eli, all of whose descendants were destined to be short-lived (see I Samuel 2:31), **you speak truncated [*mulayata*]** and unsound **matters.** When the semen passes through **its** proper **place, it fertilizes;** but if it does **not** pass through **its** proper **place, it does not fertilize.**

The Gemara then introduces a case from Pumbedita. The pathway of the semen had become blocked (*istatam*), and the semen was emerging from the urinary tract rather than through its natural channel. Rav Bibi bar Abaye initially ruled that the man was still valid — after all, the semen was still emerging, so what difference did the pathway make? Rav Papa rebuked him, and Rashi notes that his formulation alludes to Abaye's lineage from the house of Eli, which carried a divine curse — the implication being that Rav Bibi spoke carelessly. Rav Papa's substantive point is: *bimkomo mevashelah shelo bimkomo lo mevashelah* — when semen travels through its natural passage, it is properly warmed by the body and is capable of causing conception; when it travels through the urinary tract, this does not occur, and the man is therefore infertile.

What is striking about this case is that there is no obvious physical injury of the kind the Gemara elsewhere describes — no severing, no crushing. The passage was simply *istatam*, blocked or clogged. One might ask: since when does a blocked duct fall into the category of *kerus shofcha* or *patzua daka*? The Gemara seems to imply that any physical condition causing infertility in this manner is disqualifying, even without a classic wound. One possibility, raised in discussion, is that the blockage may itself have resulted from a physical injury rather than occurring *b'yedei shamayim* — which would explain both why it is disqualifying and why the *b'yedei shamayim* exemption does not apply here.

### Corona hole test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נִיקַּב וְנִסְתַּם, כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ נִקְרֵי וְנִקְרָע — פְּסוּל. וְאִי לָאו — כָּשֵׁר. הָוֵי בֵּהּ רָבָא: הֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא לְמַטָּה מֵעֲטָרָה — אֲפִילּוּ נִכְרַת נָמֵי, אֶלָּא בַּעֲטָרָה עַצְמָהּ. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב מָרִי בַּר מָר אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נִיקַּב בַּעֲטָרָה עַצְמָהּ וְנִסְתַּם, כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ נִקְרֵי וְנִקְרָע פָּסוּל, וְאִי לָאו — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** If a man's member **had been punctured and it** later healed and the hole **closed** up with flesh, in **any** case **where,** if **he would emit semen, it would tear** open again, he is **unfit** to enter into the congregation; **but if not,** he is **fit. Rava discussed** this ruling and raised a question: **Where** is this perforation? **If we say** it is **below the corona,** at the end of the man's member, why should this perforation render him unfit? **Even if** the member **was** entirely **severed,** he would **also** be fit. **Rather,** the hole is **in the corona itself,** that is, at the point where the corona meets the rest of the member. **It was also stated** explicitly that this is the case, as **Rav Mari bar Mar said** that **Mar Ukva said** that **Shmuel said:** If a man's member **had been punctured in the corona itself, and it** later healed and the hole **closed** up with flesh, in **any** case **where** if **he would emit semen it would tear** open again, he is **unfit; but if not,** he is **fit.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: if a hole existed in the penis — the classic case of *nikaiv* — and it subsequently became sealed (*nistam*), the determining criterion is *kol she'ilu nikri v'nikra pasul* — if, at the moment of seminal emission (*keri*), the hole would reopen, then the man is invalid. The reasoning is presumably that a hole that reopens upon ejaculation demonstrates that the channel has not truly healed, with direct implications for fertility. If it does not reopen, he is *kasher*.

Rava then raised the question: where exactly is this hole located? If it is below the corona — toward the tip — even complete severance would be permissible, so that cannot be the scenario. It must therefore be speaking of a hole in the corona itself. A parallel statement is then cited in the name of Shmuel through Rav Mari bar Mar citing Mar Ukva, specifying explicitly that we are discussing a hole *in the corona itself* (*nikaiv ba'atarah atzmah*), with the same rule applying: if it would reopen upon ejaculation, *pasul*; if not, *kasher*.

Rabbah bar Brei d'Rabba then sent an inquiry to Rav Yosef: *heichi avdinan* — how do we actually determine this in practice? The problem is circular: one cannot simply instruct the man to examine himself after relations with his wife, because the entire question is whether he is permitted to have relations with his wife in the first place. Some procedure for inducing ejaculation in a controlled way is needed.

### Ejaculation methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה לְרַב יוֹסֵף: יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, הֵיכִי עָבְדִינַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַיְיתִינַן נַהֲמָא חַמִּימָא דִּשְׂעָרֵי, וּמַנְּחִינַן לֵיהּ אַבֵּי פוֹקְרֵי, וּמִקְּרֵי, וְחָזֵינַן לֵיהּ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ הֲוַאי, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״כֹּחִי וְרֵאשִׁית אוֹנִי״, שֶׁלֹּא רָאָה קֶרִי מִיָּמָיו! אֶלָּא, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְעַבְּרִינַן קַמֵּיהּ בִּגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִין. אָמַר רָבָא: אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בַּרְזִילַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי הוּא?! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **Rava, son of Rabba, sent** the following question **to Rav Yosef: Let our teacher teach us, what** should **we do** to verify whether or not the perforation was adequately closed? Rav Yosef **said to him: We bring warm barley bread and place it upon his anus [*bei pukrei*], and** owing to the heat **he emits semen, and we observe** what happens and see whether or not the perforation remains closed. **Abaye said: Is that to say** that **everyone is** like **our Patriarch Jacob, with regard to whom it is written:** "Reuben, you are my firstborn, **my might and the first fruits of my strength"** (Genesis 49:3), implying **that** Jacob **never experienced an emission of semen in all his days,** so that his eldest son Reuben was conceived from his first drop of seed, i.e., "the first fruits of my strength." The implication is that there is certainly no need for such measures in order to bring a man to ejaculate. **Rather, Abaye said** that a different method is used: **We pass before him colorful garments** of a woman, and thereby bring him to arousal, so that he will experience an emission. **Rava said: Is that to say** that **everyone is** like **Barzilai the Gileadite,** traditionally known for his licentious character? Not all men are brought to excitement when they merely see such clothes. **Rather,** the Gemara rejects this proposal and states that **it is clear as we initially answered,** that we follow the former procedure even though not all men require it.

Rav Yosef replied: bring hot barley bread, place it near the anus, and this will induce ejaculation (*mikrei*), after which the organ can be inspected. Abaye objected: does one assume that everyone is as righteous as Yaakov Avinu? The Gemara understands *kochi v'reishit oni* — Reuven is described as the first of his father's strength — to mean that the conception of Reuven was Yaakov's first seminal emission ever; he was so scrupulous that he had never experienced one prior to his first conjugal act with Leah. Most men, Abaye observed, require far less inducement. Instead, Abaye suggested: pass brightly colored women's garments before him (*bigdei tzvonin*), and for most men that will be sufficient stimulation to cause ejaculation.

Rava countered in the opposite direction: does one assume that everyone is as licentious as Barzillai the Gileadite, who the Gemara elsewhere describes as *shatuf b'zimah* — immersed in licentiousness? Colored garments may not be sufficient for everyone; many men require more. Since no satisfactory middle ground could be identified between the colored garments and the more invasive medical procedure, the Gemara concludes *mechvarta k'dishninan me'ikara* — the original method, the barley bread procedure, stands.

What is noteworthy here is that the Gemara has no apparent concern about *zera l'vatala* or *hirhurim* in this context. The barley procedure, being purely physiological, may not involve *hirhurim* at all. But the *bigdei tzvonin* presumably do. The Gemara's silence suggests that when ejaculation is induced for a legitimate medical or halachic purpose, neither concern applies. This has obvious relevance to the contemporary responsa literature on procuring semen from a husband for fertility treatment involving that same couple — a topic for which this *sugya* serves as a foundational model.

## Healing defects

### Hole closure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נִיקַּב — פָּסוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹתֵת. נִסְתַּם — כָּשֵׁר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מוֹלִיד. וְזֶהוּ פְּסוּל שֶׁחוֹזֵר לְהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If a man's member **was punctured,** he is **unfit** to enter into the congregation of Israel **because** his semen **is discharged gently** and does not fertilize; if the perforation later **closed** up with flesh, he is **fit, because** now **he can father** children. **And this is** an instance of one who is **unfit who returns to his** previous state of **fitness.**

The Gemara now presents a *Baraita*: *nikaiv pasul mipnei shehu shotet* — if there is a hole, it is invalid because the semen dribbles rather than flowing in a proper stream. This is a restatement and formalization of what was discussed previously. *Nistam kasher mipnei shemolid* — if it becomes sealed, it is valid because the man can now father children. *Ve-zehu pasul she-chozer l'hechsheiro* — and this is a case of invalidity that can revert to validity through healing. The man was in the category of *kerus shofchah*, but if the hole closes, he is restored to fitness.

### Scab coverage issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶהוּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי קְרוּם שֶׁעָלָה מֵחֲמַת מַכָּה בָּרֵיאָה, דְּאֵינוֹ קְרוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the word **this** come **to exclude?** The Gemara explains that it comes **to exclude** a case involving an entirely different matter, that of **a membrane that formed on the lung** of an animal **in the wake of a wound, which is not** considered **a** proper **membrane,** as it is likely to rupture. If a puncture in the lung became covered with such a membrane the animal does not regain its former kosher status.

The Gemara then asks: *zehu l'mautei mai* — what does the word *zehu* ("this is") come to exclude? The answer: it excludes a case where what appears to be closure is in fact only a *kerum she'alah machamas makah* — a membrane or scab that has grown over a wound. In the laws of *treifot* pertaining to the lung, such a covering is not considered a genuine seal; the lung is still regarded as perforated, and the animal remains *treifah*. The analogy here is that a mere scab over a hole in the relevant organ does not constitute true closure. The hole remains, and the man's status is unchanged.

This connects to the broader halachic question of *glatt* — literally "smooth." The entire debate around the smoothness of the lung turns on whether abrasions, *sirchos*, or membranous growths are indicative of an underlying perforation or are independently problematic. A *kerum*, properly understood, is not a scab but a membrane — the *sircha* is closer to what one would call a scab in common usage. In any case, the principle is clear: genuine physical closure restores validity; superficial coverage does not.

### Wound healing methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין לְאַבָּיֵי: הֵיכִי עָבְדִינַן? מַיְיתִינַן שְׂעָרְתָּא וּמְסָרְטִינַן לֵיהּ, וּמַיְיתִינַן תַּרְבָּא וְשָׁיְיפִינַן, וּמַיְיתִינַן שׁוּמְשָׁנָא גַּמְלָא וּמְנַכְּתִינַן לֵיהּ, וּפָסְקִינַן לֵיהּ לְרֵישֵׁיהּ. וְדַוְקָא שְׂעָרְתָּא, אֲבָל פַּרְזְלָא — מִזְרָף זָרֵיף. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי קָטָן, אֲבָל גָּדוֹל — אִיקַּפּוֹלֵי מִיקַּפַּל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **Rav Idi bar Avin sent** the following question **to Abaye: What** should **we do** to expedite the healing of such a perforation? Abaye answered: **We bring** a sharp-edged grain of **barley and lacerate** the area around the hole with it. **We** then **bring fat and rub it** on the spot, **and** afterward **we bring a large ant [*shumshena*] and let it bite** inside the hole. This leads to bleeding and the formation of a scab, which eventually heals as new flesh grows there. **We** also **cut off** the ant's **head** so that it should remain in place until the wound is fully healed. The Gemara comments: **And** this procedure must be done **specifically** with a grain of **barley, but** an **iron** tool would **cause inflammation [*zareif*].** The Gemara adds: **And this applies only** to **a small** perforation, **but a large one will** eventually **peel** off and reopen.

Rav Idi bar Avin sent an inquiry to Abaye: *heichi avdinan* — if there is a hole and we want the body to close it, what do we do to facilitate that healing? Abaye replied with the following protocol. Take a barley kernel and scratch the area around the hole, drawing some blood and stimulating the body's healing response. Then apply fat to the area, rubbing it in, to promote scabbing and filling. Finally, obtain a large ant — *gamla*, camel-sized — place it at the wound site, allow it to bite the area, and then sever its head, leaving the head embedded. As the head decomposes, it may serve as raw material for the body to seal the wound.

An important clarification: one must scratch specifically with barley and not with iron, because *de-mizraf zarif* — iron causes inflammation rather than the beneficial scabbing one wants. This reflects a practical awareness that metal contact with a wound produces a different physiological response than a gentler abrasion. And all of this applies only to a small hole (*katana*); for a large one, any such closure will ultimately peel away and prove ineffective.

### Dual urination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הַמֵּטִיל מַיִם מִשְּׁתֵּי מְקוֹמוֹת, פָּסוּל. אָמַר רָבָא: לֵית הִלְכְתָא לָא כִּבְרָא וְלָא כְּאַבָּא, בְּרָא — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. אַבָּא — דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: נָשִׁים הַמְסוֹלְלוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ, פְּסוּלוֹת לַכְּהוּנָּה. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: פָּנוּי הַבָּא עַל הַפְּנוּיָה שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה — הָנֵי מִילֵּי אִישׁ, אֲבָל אִשָּׁה פְּרִיצוּתָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: One who passes water from two places,** so that he appears to have a hole or some other blemish in his member, is **unfit** to enter into the congregation of Israel, as is a man with crushed testicles. **Rava said:** With regard to these matters, the ***halakha* is in accordance neither with** the opinion of **the son nor with** that of **the father. The son,** this refers to **that** opinion of Rabba bar Rav Huna, **which we** just **stated.** As for the ruling of **the father,** this is referring to that which **Rav Huna said: Women who rub against one another** motivated by sexual desire **are unfit to** marry into **the priesthood,** as such conduct renders a woman a *zona*, whom a priest is prohibited from marrying. It was about this that Rava said that the *halakha* is not in accordance with Rav Huna's opinion. **And even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said that an unmarried man who has intercourse with an unmarried woman not for the sake of marriage renders her a *zona*,** a woman who has had sexual relations with a man forbidden to her by the Torah, **this applies only to** intercourse with **a man, but** lewd behavior with another **woman is mere licentiousness** that does not render her a *zona*, and therefore she is still permitted to marry into the priesthood.

Rava bar Rav Huna ruled: if a man urinates from two places — apparently from some opening in the penis in addition to its normal tip, possibly indicating that the urine is passing through two internal channels — he is *pasul*. The same underlying puzzle applies here as in the case of the blocked semen passage: why does a condition that is not obviously a classic physical wound fall under these categories? Unless, again, the presumption is that such a condition results from some form of physical injury rather than arising *b'yedei shamayim*.

Rava then ruled: *leit hilcheta* — the halacha does not follow Rava bar Rav Huna in either of his rulings. Neither regarding the son's statement about urination from two places, nor regarding the father Rav Huna's statement, which held that women who engage in sexual contact with one another (*nashim hamisolelot zo b'zo*) are *psulot l'kehuna*, disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. Rashi understands the disqualification to affect eligibility for a *kohen gadol*, implicating the requirement that he marry a *betulah*. Tosafot suggests it relates to the category of *zona* for an ordinary *kohen*. The Gemara rules that neither position is accepted as *halacha*.

The discussion here opens onto a range of fascinating contemporary questions. The Gemara's treatment of female same-sex contact as *pritzuta b'alma* — licentiousness but not halachically constituted sexual intercourse — reflects the principle that *halacha* defines sexual intercourse as requiring penile penetration, whether vaginal or anal. Without that element, there can be no *halachic* act of sex, and consequently no *zona*, no loss of *betulah* status in the operative halachic sense, and arguably no *zera l'vatala* and no *hirhurim* in the same way that male sexual activity generates those concerns. The Gemara confirms this by invoking even Rabbi Elazar's stringent position — that extramarital relations between an unmarried man and an unmarried woman constitute *znut* — and limiting it explicitly: *hatam mili ish*, that applies when there is a man, meaning an act of halachically defined intercourse. *Aval isha* — when only women are involved, it cannot rise to that level.

## Ptzua marriage

### Marriage restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פְּצוּעַ דַּכָּא וּכְרוּת שׇׁפְכָה מוּתָּרִין בְּגִיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת, וְאֵינָן אֲסוּרִין אֶלָּא מִלָּבֹא בַּקָּהָל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָבֹא פְצוּעַ דַּכָּא וּכְרוּת שׇׁפְכָה בִּקְהַל ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A man with crushed testicles** or with other wounds to his genitals **and one whose penis has been severed are permitted** to marry a female **convert or an emancipated** maidservant, **and they are prohibited only from entering into the congregation** and marrying a woman who was born Jewish, **as it is stated: "A man wounded with crushed testicles or a severed penis shall not enter into the congregation of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 23:2).

Having worked through the physical definitions, the Gemara turns to the halachic consequences for marriage. A *patzua daka* or *krut shofcha* is *mutar b'giyoret u'meshuchreret* — permitted to marry a convert or a freed slave, women who were not born into the Jewish people. The basis is *de'eino assur ella m'lavo b'kahal* — the prohibition is specifically against entering the *kahal*, meaning against marrying those who are natural-born members of the community. A *giyoret* is not included in that category. The verse is: *lo yavo patzua daka u'krut shofcha b'kehal Hashem*.

This leniency, while welcome, does prompt discomfort. The Torah's principle of *k'ger k'ezrach* implies that a convert stands fully equal to a born Jew, yet in several halachic contexts — including the law of *tum'at yoledet* which required a separate derivation to include a *giyoret*, and here — converts are treated as outside the primary *kahal*. This tension between the ideal of full conversion and the practical halachic categorizations is a recurring theme.

### Priestly status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: פְּצוּעַ דַּכָּא כֹּהֵן מַהוּ, בְּגִיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת? בִּקְדוּשְׁתֵּיהּ קָאֵי וַאֲסִיר, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָאו בִּקְדוּשְׁתֵּיהּ קָאֵי וּשְׁרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **They raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What is** the *halakha* governing **a priest with crushed testicles with respect to** a female **convert or an emancipated** maidservant? The Gemara clarifies the two sides of this dilemma: Does **he retain his** state of **sanctity** like any other priest **and is** therefore **prohibited** from marrying either one of these women, **or perhaps he does not retain his** state of **sanctity and** therefore **is permitted** to marry a convert, like an ordinary Israelite with crushed testicles?

The question is then posed to Rav Sheshet: a *patzua daka* who is also a *kohen* — what is his status regarding marriage to a *giyoret* or *meshuchreret*? We already know from an earlier *mishnah* that a *patzua daka kohen* retains his priestly status and may eat *terumah*. His *kedushat kehuna* is intact. Given that, should the ordinary restrictions on whom a *kohen* may marry still apply to him? Or, since he is now excluded from the *kahal* with respect to marriage, does that exclusion open up categories of women who would ordinarily be forbidden to a *kohen*?

This is a genuine tension: on one side, his *kedushah* remains in every domain where a physical injury is irrelevant; on the other, the very fact that he is now categorized as outside the *kahal* for marriage purposes may simultaneously remove certain barriers. As was clarified in discussion: the question is not about a *gerushah*, whom a *patzua daka kohen* still cannot marry regardless, but specifically about a *giyoret* — a woman who is excluded from the *kahal* but would otherwise be permissible to a *yisrael*.

### Excluded status benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: פְּצוּעַ דַּכָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל — מוּתָּר בִּנְתִינָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בִּקְדוּשְׁתֵּיהּ קָאֵי, אִקְרִי כָּאן ״לֹא תִתְחַתֵּן בָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said to them: You** already **learned** the answer to **this** question in the following *baraita*: **It is permitted for an** ordinary **Israelite with crushed testicles** to marry **a Gibeonite woman. Now, if it enters your mind** that he **retains his sanctity** as a Jew, one should **apply here** the prohibition stated with regard to Canaanites: **"You shall not marry them"** (Deuteronomy 7:3). Apparently, then, one whose testicles have been crushed loses his former sanctity, and the same should apply to a priest.

Rav Sheshet responded: *tanituha* — the answer has already been taught. The parallel case involves a *patzua daka yisrael* and a *netina* — a woman descended from the Gibeonites, members of the seven Canaanite nations who converted in the time of Yehoshua by subterfuge. Ordinarily a *yisrael* may not marry someone from the *shivat amamim*, just as a *kohen* may not marry a *giyoret*. Yet a *patzua daka yisrael* is permitted to marry a *netina*. If he still retained his full *kedushah* — including the prohibition *lo titchaten bam* — this should be forbidden. Since it is permitted, it must be that exclusion from the *kahal* opens up marriage to those who are themselves outside that category.

Rava, however, raised a complication. The prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* for the *shivat amamim* is not straightforwardly biblical once they convert. When they are still non-Jews, the verse *lo titchaten bam* cannot mean prohibiting marriage in the halachic sense, since their *kiddushin* would not take effect. The prohibition must therefore be speaking of after conversion. But Rava then suggests that the rabbinic extension of this prohibition — covering converts from the *shivat amamim* — was enacted only where children are in the equation, since the Torah's concern (*ki yasir et bincha me'acharai*) is about children being led astray. A *patzua daka* who cannot have children was never included in that rabbinic decree. So the leniency regarding the *netina* may be rabbinic in character and cannot be used to prove the case of a *kohen* and a *giyoret*.

## Mamzer and Netin

### Excluded analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, מַמְזֵר, דְּבַר אוֹלוֹדֵי, הָכִי נָמֵי דַּאֲסִיר? וְהָא תְּנַן: מַמְזֵרִים וּנְתִינִים מוּתָּרִים לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה! אֶלָּא: כִּי גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן — בִּכְשֵׁרִים. בִּפְסוּלִים לָא גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection against Rava: **However, if that is so,** then with regard to **a *mamzer*, who is capable of having children, so too** one would say **that he is prohibited** from marrying a Gibeonite. **But didn't we learn** otherwise in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 69a): ***Mamzerim* and Gibeonites are permitted to marry one another. Rather,** retract this explanation and replace it with the following: **When the Sages decreed** that one may not marry a Gibeonite, they limited their decree **to** those who are **fit,** so as to prevent them from mingling with Gibeonites; but **with regard to** those who are **unfit** to enter into the congregation, **the Sages did not issue a decree.**

But this argument is then challenged by Rava himself. If the *netina* case is merely rabbinic, consider: a *mamzer* can certainly have children, and yet the *mishnah* teaches *mamzerim u'nesinim mutarim lavo zeh b'zeh* — a *mamzer* and a *netin* may marry one another. If the criterion were about fertility, a *mamzer* should be forbidden to a *netina*. Evidently the principle is different: *ki gazru Rabbanan b'ksherim, b'psulim lo gazru* — when the Rabbis enacted the prohibition against marrying *nesinim*, they only applied it to those who are halachically *kasher*. Those who are themselves in a category of *pesul* — like *mamzer* and *patzua daka* — were not included. So the leniency has a different basis than fertility.

### Biblical vs rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, בְּגוֹיוּתָן — לֵית לְהוּ חַתְנוּת, נִתְגַּיְּירוּ — אִית לְהוּ חַתְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava then** reconsidered and **said** that what he had previously argued, that the prohibition against marrying them applies only when they are gentiles, **is not correct.** The prohibition cannot be referring to gentiles, as **when they are gentiles there can be no** valid **marriage with them** at all. It is only after **they have converted** that **there can be** valid **marriage with them,** and therefore the prohibition against entering into marriage with them applies. Nevertheless, it is permitted for a man with crushed testicles to marry a Gibeonite woman.

Rava then reconsidered his own earlier position entirely: *lav milsa hi d'amri* — what I said was not correct. He had claimed that the biblical prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* applies only to the *shivat amamim* while they are non-Jews, and that the prohibition post-conversion is rabbinic. But in fact, when the nations are non-Jews, their *kiddushin* has no binding effect — *leis lehu chasnus*. The very concept of marriage (*chasnus*) only becomes relevant after conversion. Therefore, the Torah's *lo titchaten bam* must be speaking of the prohibition even after conversion — which means it is a *d'oraisa* prohibition. And yet, despite this being biblical, a *patzua daka* and a *mamzer* are permitted to marry *nesinim*. This demonstrates the principle clearly: once someone is excluded from *kahal Hashem*, they are freed from certain restrictions vis-à-vis others who are similarly outside that category. And this principle applies equally to a *kohen* who is a *patzua daka* — despite his *kedushat kehuna*, his exclusion from *kahal Hashem* for marriage purposes opens the door to marry a *giyoret*. This is indeed the ruling recorded in the *Shulchan Aruch*, Even HaEzer, siman 5, se'if 1, which states explicitly that even a *kohen* who is a *patzua daka* is permitted to marry a *giyoret* or *meshuchreret*, since he is no longer within his [full marital] *kedushah* in that domain.

### Shlomo and Egypt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וַיִּתְחַתֵּן שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בַּת פַּרְעֹה מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם״! גַּיּוֹרֵי גַּיְּירַהּ. וְהָא לֹא קִבְּלוּ גֵּרִים לֹא בִּימֵי דָוִד וְלֹא בִּימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה! מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא — אֶלָּא לְשׁוּלְחַן מְלָכִים, הָא לָא צְרִיכָה לֵיהּ. [וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ] דְּהָא מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה הִיא! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנָךְ אָזְלִי לְעָלְמָא וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ, וְהָא תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִנְיָמִין גֵּר מִצְרִי הָיָה לִי חָבֵר מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמַר: אֲנִי מִצְרִי רִאשׁוֹן, וְנָשָׂאתִי מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה. אַשִּׂיא לִבְנִי מִצְרִית שְׁנִיָּה כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן בְּנִי רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection** from the verse that states: **"And Solomon married the daughter of Pharaoh, king of Egypt"** (I Kings 3:1), which indicates that there can, in fact, be valid marriage even with gentiles. The Gemara answers: Before Solomon took Pharaoh's daughter as his wife, **he converted her.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it so that **they did not accept converts, neither in the days of David nor in the days of Solomon?** The Gemara answers: **But isn't the reason** that they did not accept converts during those periods **only** due **to** concern that the converts were not acting for the sake of Heaven but in fact desired the power of **the table of kings,** David and Solomon? **But this one,** Pharaoh's daughter, **did not require such** things, as she herself was the daughter of royalty, and therefore there would have been no reason to doubt the sincerity of her conversion. **But let him derive** that Pharaoh's daughter was forbidden to Solomon for a different reason, **as she** was **a first-**generation **Egyptian** convert. Even if she converted, she would still have been an Egyptian convert of the first generation, and as such neither she nor her children would have been permitted to marry a Jew by birth (Deuteronomy 23:8–9). **And if you would say** that **those** whom the Torah rendered forbidden have already **left** Egypt and are now living elsewhere **in the world, and those** currently living in Egypt **are others,** there is a difficulty. As, **isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Minyamin, an Egyptian convert, was a friend of mine from among the students of Rabbi Akiva,** and **he said:** After I converted **I** was **a first-**generation **Egyptian** convert, **and** so **I married** another **first-**generation **Egyptian** convert. **I will marry off my son,** who is a second-generation Egyptian convert, **to** another **second-**generation **Egyptian** convert, **so that my grandson will be fit to enter into the congregation.** This indicates that first- and second-generation converts of Egyptian extraction were prohibited from entering into the congregation even during the period of the Mishna.

Rav Yosef challenged the position that the biblical *lo titchaten bam* applies only after conversion. If that were so, he asked, how could the verse say *vayitchaten Shlomo es bat Paroh melech Mitzrayim* — that Shlomo married the daughter of Pharaoh? The Gemara responds that she converted (*digayara*). But one might raise an obvious objection: was it not a rule that converts were not accepted in the times of David and Shlomo due to suspicion of ulterior motives — joining the king's household for material benefit? The Gemara answers: the concern about *shulchan melachim* does not apply to bat Paroh herself, since she already came from a royal table; she had nothing to gain materially from conversion. So she was accepted.

But the Gemara presses further: even if she converted, she was a *mitzrit* of the first generation. The verse states *lo tita'ev mitzri ki ger hayita b'artzo*, and then *banim asher yivaldu lahem dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem* — only the third generation of a Mitzri convert may enter the *kahal*. A first-generation *mitzrit* converting would not be eligible to marry into the *kahal* for two more generations. So how was Shlomo permitted to marry her?

To illustrate that the Mitzri prohibition was still operative in that era, the Gemara cites a *Baraita* in which Rabbi Yehuda reports: "I had a colleague, Minyamin the Mitzri convert, from among the students of Rabbi Akiva. He told me: I am a first-generation Mitzri convert, and I married a first-generation Mitzrit. I intend to have my son marry a second-generation Mitzrit, so that my grandson will be a third-generation [convert of Mitzri descent] and permitted to enter the *kahal*." This shows that even in the time of Rabbi Akiva — well after Sancheriv had displaced populations — the *halacha* still treated the Mitzri prohibition as operative. The governing principle is *holchin achar hapesul*: if a first-generation marries a second-generation, the child's status follows the more disqualified line and is counted as second-generation; only when a second-generation marries a second-generation does the child achieve third-generation status and full eligibility.

Since the Mitzri prohibition was still in effect in the era of the Talmudic Sages, it must have been in effect in Shlomo's time as well, making bat Paroh's status highly problematic. How then did Shlomo marry her?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲנַן מִשְּׁלֹמֹה לֵיקוּ וְנֹתֵיב? שְׁלֹמֹה לָא נְסֵיב מִידֵּי, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״מִן הַגּוֹיִם אֲשֶׁר אָמַר ה׳ אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא תָבוֹאוּ בָּהֶם וְהֵם לֹא יָבוֹאוּ בָכֶם אָכֵן יַטּוּ אֶת לְבַבְכֶם אַחֲרֵי אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, בָּהֶם דָּבַק שְׁלֹמֹה לְאַהֲבָה״. אֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא ״וַיִּתְחַתֵּן״! מִתּוֹךְ אַהֲבָה יְתֵירָה שֶׁאֲהֵבָהּ, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ נִתְחַתֵּן בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Shall we stand up and raise an objection from Solomon? Solomon did not marry anyone, as it is written in his regard: "Of the nations concerning which the Lord said to the children of Israel, You shall not go among them, neither shall they come among you; for surely they will turn away your heart after their gods; Solomon cleaved to these in love"** (I Kings 11:2). Solomon cleaved to these women in love, but was not legally married to them. As Solomon had other forbidden wives, the case of Pharaoh's daughter presents no special difficulty. In fact, none of these marriages were valid at all. **But** the phrase **"and** Solomon **married"** (I Kings 3:1) that appears in connection with Pharaoh's daughter is **difficult,** as it indicates that this marriage was in fact valid. **Due to the extraordinary love that he had for her, the verse relates to him as if he had married her** through a legally valid marriage, even though this was not the case.

Rav Papa answered: *anan miShlomo leika d'lisof* — are we really going to ask questions from Shlomo? The implication is that Shlomo transgressed. But the Gemara then revisits this and suggests: *Shlomo lo nasiv midi* — Shlomo did not actually marry them. The verse says: *min hagoyim asher amar Hashem el bnei Yisrael lo tavo'u bahem... bahem davak Shlomo l'ahava* — unto them Shlomo cleaved for love. The word *davak*, cleaved, indicates passionate attachment, not formal marriage. He was bound to them deeply, but did not legally marry them.

The Gemara then acknowledges the residual difficulty: but the verse explicitly says *vayitchaten* — he married. The answer: *mitoch ahava yeseirah she'ahava, ma'alah alav ke'ilu yitchaten bah* — because of the exceptional love he bore her, it was as though he had married her, but technically he had not. This is, the Gemara acknowledges, a somewhat forced reading.

Tosafot raises an important related point: after Sancheriv displaced the nations, shouldn't we simply say that the people living in Egypt are no longer the halachic *mitzrim* of the Torah? Tosafot quotes a *Tosefta* in which Rabbi Akiva told Minyamin directly: *ta'isa* — you made a mistake. You think you are a *mitzri*, but Sancheriv already came and scrambled all the nations (*bilbel kol ha'olam*), and therefore no one living now can be considered a halachic *mitzri*. The Gemara itself, however, maintains that the identification persisted — not just in Shlomo's time but even in the era of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Akiva.

One broader halachic point emerges from this entire discussion. The prohibitions we have been examining — *patzua daka*, *mamzer*, *mitzri*, *amoni* — concern the formal institution of *nisu'in*, marriage. The question of whether the prohibition is violated by sexual relations outside the framework of marriage is a distinct and nuanced question. As the Gemara implies here, the *lo titchaten* formulation presupposes a formal act of marriage. By contrast, prohibitions that involve *kares* — the severe biblical penalties for incestuous or adulterous relations — are entirely about the act of intercourse, and marriage is not even a relevant concept in those contexts since *kiddushin* would not take effect.

## Intermarriage rules

### Amoni/Moavi rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי — אֲסוּרִים, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם. אֲבָל נְקֵבוֹתֵיהֶם — מוּתָּרוֹת מִיָּד. מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי אֵינָם אֲסוּרִים אֶלָּא עַד שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת, אֶחָד זְכָרִים וְאֶחָד נְקֵבוֹת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר אֶת הַנְּקֵבוֹת מִיָּד. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, קַל וָחוֹמֶר הַדְּבָרִים: וּמָה אִם בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁאָסַר אֶת הַזְּכָרִים אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם — הִתִּיר אֶת הַנְּקֵבוֹת מִיָּד, מָקוֹם שֶׁלָּא אָסַר אֶת הַזְּכָרִים אֶלָּא עַד שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנַּתִּיר אֶת הַנְּקֵבוֹת מִיָּד? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם הֲלָכָה — נְקַבֵּל, וְאִם לַדִּין — יֵשׁ תְּשׁוּבָה. אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא כִּי, הֲלָכָה אֲנִי אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Ammonite and Moabite** converts **are prohibited** from entering into the congregation and marrying a woman who was born Jewish, **and their prohibition is eternal,** for all generations. **However, their female** counterparts, even the convert herself, **are permitted immediately. Egyptian and Edomite** converts **are prohibited** from entering into the congregation **only for three generations, both males and females. Rabbi Shimon renders permitted** Egyptian and Edomite **females immediately. Rabbi Shimon said: The matter** may be derived by way of **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If in a place where** the Torah rendered **prohibited the males with an eternal prohibition,** i.e., Ammonites and Moabites, it **rendered permitted the females immediately,** then in **a place where it rendered prohibited the males for only three generations,** i.e., Egyptians and Edomites, **is it not right that we should render permitted the females immediately?** Rabbi Shimon's colleagues **said to him: If** you are reporting a ***halakha*** that you received from your teachers, **we will accept** it from you. **But if** you merely wish to prove your case with an *a fortiori* **inference** based on your own reasoning, **there is a refutation** of your argument. Rabbi Shimon **said to them:** That is **not so.** I disagree with your claim that the *a fortiori* inference can be refuted, but in any case **I am stating a *halakha*** handed down to me by my teachers.

The *mishnah* now turns to the generational restrictions on various nations joining the *kahal*. *Amoni u'Moavi asurin v'isuran issur olam* — an Ammonite and a Moabite are forbidden forever. Even *gam dor asiri* — a tenth generation — may not enter the *kahal* of Hashem. Their females, however, are *mutarot miyad* — immediately permissible upon conversion.

A Mitzri or an Edomite is forbidden only *ad shelosha dorot* — for three generations, after which *dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem*. Here the *mishnah* specifies *echad zecharim v'echad nekivot* — both males and females must wait three generations. This is notable: the more severe prohibition of *amoni u'moavi*, which is permanent for males, is actually more lenient for females. The less severe prohibition of *mitzri* and *edomi* applies equally to both genders.

Rabbi Shimon rules that females of Egyptian and Edomite descent should be *mutarot miyad*, just as female Ammonites and Moabites are. He advances a *kal vachomer*: if in the case where males are permanently forbidden — *amoni u'moavi* — the females are immediately permitted, then certainly in the case where males are forbidden only for three generations — *mitzri v'edomi* — the females should be permitted immediately. The Sages responded: if you have a tradition (*halacha*) we will accept it, but if you are arguing by logic (*ledin*), we have a refutation of your *kal vachomer*. Rabbi Shimon replied: I am not relying solely on logic; I am stating this as a received *halacha*. Whether that tradition was ultimately accepted will emerge from the Gemara's continued discussion.

## David and Ruth

### Saul's inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִרְאוֹת שָׁאוּל אֶת דָּוִד יוֹצֵא לִקְרַאת הַפְּלִשְׁתִּי אָמַר אֶל אַבְנֵר שַׂר הַצָּבָא בֶּן מִי זֶה הַנַּעַר אַבְנֵר וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְנֵר חֵי נַפְשְׁךָ הַמֶּלֶךְ אִם יָדָעְתִּי״. וְלָא יְדַע לֵיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֶּאֱהָבֵהוּ מְאֹד וַיְהִי לוֹ נוֹשֵׂא כֵּלִים״! אֶלָּא אַאֲבוּהּ קָא מְשַׁאֵיל. וְלָא יְדַע לֵיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהָאִישׁ בִּימֵי שָׁאוּל זָקֵן בָּא בַאֲנָשִׁים״, וְאָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אַבָּא: זֶה יִשַׁי אֲבִי דָוִד, שֶׁנִּכְנַס בְּאוּכְלוּסָא וְיָצָא בְּאוּכְלוּסָא! הָכִי קָאָמַר שָׁאוּל: אִי מִפֶּרֶץ אָתֵי, אִי מִזֶּרַח אָתֵי? אִי מִפֶּרֶץ אָתֵי — מַלְכָּא הָוֵי, שֶׁהַמֶּלֶךְ פּוֹרֵץ לַעֲשׂוֹת דֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין מְמַחִין בְּיָדוֹ. אִי מִזֶּרַח אָתֵי — חֲשִׁיבָא בְּעָלְמָא הָוֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שַׁאֵל עֲלֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּלְבֵּשׁ שָׁאוּל אֶת דָּוִד מַדָּיו״, כְּמִדָּתוֹ. וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּשָׁאוּל: ״מִשִּׁכְמוֹ וָמַעְלָה גָּבוֹהַּ מִכׇּל הָעָם״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ דּוֹאֵג הָאֲדוֹמִי: עַד שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁאֵיל עָלָיו אִם הָגוּן הוּא לַמַּלְכוּת אִם לָאו, שְׁאַל עָלָיו אִם רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל אִם לָאו. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּקָאָתֵי מֵרוּת הַמּוֹאֲבִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived that female Ammonites and Moabites are permitted immediately? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: As the verse states: "And when Saul saw David go forth against the Philistine, he said to Abner, the captain of the host: Abner, whose son is this youth? And Abner said: As your soul lives, O king, I cannot tell"** (I Samuel 17:55). This verse is puzzling: **Did** Saul really **not recognize him? But isn't it** previously **written:** "And David came to Saul, and stood before him; **and he loved him greatly; and he became his armor-bearer"** (I Samuel 16:21)? **Rather,** it must be that **he was asking about** David's **father. And did** Saul **not recognize** David's **father? But isn't it written** with regard to Jesse, David's father: **"And the man in the days of Saul was old, and came among men"** (I Samuel 17:12), **and Rav, and some say Rabbi Abba, said: This** is referring to **Jesse, father of David, who** always **entered with multitudes [*ukhlusa*] and left with multitudes.** As he was clearly a man of importance, everyone must have known who he was. Rather, **this is** what **Saul was saying,** in his attempt to clarify David's lineage: **Does** he **come from** the descendants of **Perez, or does** he **come from** the descendants of **Zerah?** What is the significance of this question? **If** he **comes from Perez he will be king, as a king may breach [*poretz*] a way** for himself **and no one can stop him.** And **if he comes from Zerah he will be merely a man of importance,** but not a king. **For what reason did** Saul **say to** Abner that he should **inquire about** David? **As it is written: "And Saul clad David with his apparel [*maddav*]"** (I Samuel 17:38), which indicates that the clothes were **of** David's **size [*kemiddato*]. And it is written with regard to Saul: "From his shoulders and upward he was higher than any of the people"** (I Samuel 9:2). Upon seeing that his clothes fit David, Saul began to fear that it might be David who was destined for the throne, and he therefore inquired into his background. At that point, **Doeg the Edomite said to** Saul: **Before you inquire as to whether or not he is fit for kingship, inquire as to whether or not he is** even **fit to enter into the congregation. What is the reason** for such doubts? It is **that** he **descends from Ruth the Moabite,** and Moabites are permanently barred from entering the congregation.

The Gemara now grounds the rule that a female Ammonite or Moabite is *muteret miyad* in a narrative from *Navi*. Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse: *v'chirot Shaul et David yotzei likrat haplishti* — when Shaul saw David going out to confront the Philistine, Goliath, *amar el Avner sar hatzava* — he said to Avner, his general — *ben mi zeh hana'ar* — whose son is this young man?

This seems puzzling. Surely Shaul knew who David was — David had been his personal armor-bearer, and the verse itself records *vayeehavehu me'od vayehi lo nosei keilim* — Shaul loved him greatly and David bore his weapons. Furthermore, Yishai was not an obscure figure: the verse describes him as *zaken ba b'anashim*, a man of great stature in the time of Shaul, and the Gemara cites Rav (and some say Rabbi Abba): *zeh Yishai avi David shenichnas b'uchlusa v'yatza b'uchlusa* — whenever Yishai entered or departed, he was accompanied by tens of thousands. Rashi notes that Yishai commanded 600,000. How could Avner not have known him?

The Gemara resolves: Shaul was not asking about David's identity or about Yishai. *Ei mi'Peretz asi ei mi'Zerach asi* — what he wanted to know was: does David descend from Perez or from Zerach, the two lineages of Yehuda? *Ei mi'Peretz asi, malka havei* — if from Perez, Shaul had reason for concern. The king is said to *poretz*, to burst through boundaries without restraint — that is the nature of royal power. Descent from Perez would indicate David was destined for kingship. *Ei mi'Zerach asi, chashiva b'alma havei* — if from Zerach, the shining one, David would have prestige but not threaten the throne.

What provoked Shaul's anxiety at precisely this moment? The verse records that Shaul dressed David in his own garments — *madav kemiduto* — and David wore them to measure, fitting him as perfectly as they fit Shaul, who was *mishichmo vamala gavoah mikol ha'am*, a head taller than all the people. David matched Shaul physically, and Shaul, seeing before him a young man of equal stature and evident charisma, felt genuinely threatened.

### Doeg's objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבְנֵר, תְּנֵינָא: ״עַמּוֹנִי״, וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית. ״מוֹאָבִי״, וְלֹא מוֹאָבִית. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״מַמְזֵר״, וְלֹא מַמְזֶרֶת? ״מַמְזֵר״ כְּתִיב — מוּם זָר. ״מִצְרִי״, וְלֹא מִצְרִית! שָׁנֵי הָכָא, דִּמְפָרֵשׁ טַעְמָא דִקְרָא: ״עַל אֲשֶׁר לֹא קִדְּמוּ אֶתְכֶם בַּלֶּחֶם וּבַמַּיִם״ — דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ לְקַדֵּם, וְלֹא דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה לְקַדֵּם. הָיָה לָהֶם לְקַדֵּם אֲנָשִׁים לִקְרַאת אֲנָשִׁים וְנָשִׁים לִקְרַאת נָשִׁים! אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abner said to him: We** already **learned** that there is no room for such concern. As the verse states: "An Ammonite or a Moabite shall not enter into the congregation of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:4), teaching that **an Ammonite** man is barred from entering into the congregation, **but not an Ammonite woman;** and similarly, **a Moabite** man is barred from entering into the congregation, **but not a Moabite woman.** Doeg said to him: **However, if that is so,** say that the verse that renders it prohibited for a *mamzer* to enter the congregation renders prohibited only a male ***mamzer*, but not a female *mamzer*.** Abner replied: **It is written: "A *mamzer*,"** which should be understood not as a noun but as an adjective, denoting **a strange blemish [*mum zar*],** one who is defective due to a forbidden relationship, and this applies to males and females alike. Doeg retorted: If so, say that it is prohibited for only **an Egyptian** man to enter into the congregation, **but not an Egyptian woman.** Abner answered: **Here it is different, as the reason for** the prohibition recorded in this **verse** with regard to Ammonites **is explicit: "Because they did not meet you with bread and with water** on the way, when you came forth out of Egypt" (Deuteronomy 23:5). Since **it is the way of a man** to go forth **to meet** guests **but it is not the way of a woman** to go forth, females were not included in this prohibition. Doeg countered: Still, **the men should have gone** forth **to meet the men, and the women to meet the women.** Abner **was silent,** as he did not know how to respond to this objection.

Avner responded: *tanina, Amoni velo Amonit, Moavi velo Moavit* — we have a tradition: the prohibition is *Amoni*, not *Amonit*; *Moavi*, not *Moavit*. A female Moabitess is immediately permitted. Doeg challenged Avner's silence with a counter-argument: very well, but then say *Mitzri velo Mitzrit* — why don't we apply the same logic to Egypt and permit female Egyptian converts immediately?

## Intermarriage rules…

### Greeting logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ שְׁאַל אַתָּה בֶּן מִי זֶה הָעָלֶם״. הָתָם קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״נַעַר״, הָכָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״עֶלֶם״? הָכִי קָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲלָכָה נִתְעַלְּמָה מִמְּךָ — צֵא וּשְׁאַל בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. שָׁאַל, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: ״עַמּוֹנִי״, וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית. ״מוֹאָבִי״, וְלֹא מוֹאָבִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately: "And the king said, inquire you whose son is this lad"** (I Samuel 17:56). The Gemara comments: **There,** in the previous verse, Saul **calls him youth [*na'ar*],** and **here he calls him lad [*elem*].** This change in the wording hints at the following discussion. Saul **said to** Doeg **as follows:** The ***halakha* is hidden [*nitalma*] from you,** and you are ignorant of the law. **Go and inquire** about the matter **in the study hall. He** went to the study hall and **asked. They said to him:** The *halakha* is: **An Ammonite** man is forbidden, **but not an Ammonite woman; a Moabite** man is forbidden, **but not a Moabite woman.**

The Gemara explains the textual basis for distinguishing between male and female Ammonites and Moabites, and thereby answering Doeg's challenge about Egypt. The verse gives a specific reason for the prohibition: *al asher lo kidmo etchem balechem uvamayim* — because they did not come out to greet you with bread and water. This reason generates the limited application: *darko shel ish lekadem velo darko shel isha lekadem* — it is the way of a man to go out and greet, not the way of a woman. Therefore, only the men can be held culpable for the failure to extend hospitality; the women cannot. This is the basis for reading *Amoni* and not *Amonit*, *Moavi* and not *Moavit*.

## David and Ruth…

### Beis HaMedrash

Doeg was not satisfied. He pressed: if men should have gone out to greet the men and women should have gone out to greet the women — *anashim likrat anashim venashim likrat nashim* — then the women of Moab were equally remiss for not greeting the women of Israel. Why should they be exempt? Avner fell silent. He had no ready answer.

Shaul then directed: *tze ushal b'veis hamedrash* — go and pose this question in the *Beis HaMedrash*. The Gemara notes the wordplay: Shaul called David an *elem* in this verse rather than a *na'ar* as he did earlier, and the Gemara reads this as Shaul saying *halacha nit'almah mimcha* — the *halacha* has been hidden from you; Avner does not know the answer. Go to the *Beis HaMedrash* and find out.

When they arrived at the *Beis HaMedrash*, the ruling was the same as Avner had stated: *Amoni velo Amonit, Moavi velo Moavit*. But Doeg was there in the *Beis HaMedrash* and challenged everyone with all the same objections. *Ba'i le'achruzei alei* — he sought to make a public proclamation disqualifying David. The entire *Beis HaMedrash* fell silent before his challenge. How David's lineage was ultimately vindicated and his legitimacy as king of Israel established — that we will take up tomorrow.
