# Hullin 140 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiluach Haken Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּכִסּוּי וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא קַן צִפּוֹר לְפָנֶיךָ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: There are more **stringent** elements **in the covering** of the blood than in the sending away of the mother from the nest, as the covering of the blood applies to undomesticated animals and birds, to animals and birds that are readily available in one's home, and to animals and birds that are not readily available; and the sending of the mother from the nest applies only to birds that are not readily available. With regard to the mitzva of sending away the mother, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"If a bird's nest happens before you** on the way, in any tree or on the ground, with fledglings or eggs, and the mother is resting upon the fledglings, or upon the eggs, you shall not take the mother with the young; you shall send the mother, but the young you may take for yourself" (Deuteronomy 22:6–7). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** all the various details contained in it?

The daf proper is 140, though the discussion opens at the very top of 139b, in the midst of the *mitzvah* of *shiluach haken*. The Gemara begins with the heading *chomer b'kisuy*, marking the transition into a new comparative discussion. The preceding material had explored the case of a mother bird that is *hekdesh* — consecrated property — and whether the *mitzvah* applies in such a circumstance. The Gemara there had worked through interesting questions about how ownership of a bird is dependent not merely on its creation but on its ongoing maintenance under one's control. With that as backdrop, the Gemara now proceeds.

### Ki Yikarei Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שַׁלֵּחַ תְּשַׁלַּח אֶת הָאֵם וְאֶת הַבָּנִים תִּקַּח לָךְ״, יָכוֹל יַחְזוֹר בְּהָרִים וּגְבָעוֹת כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּמְצָא קֵן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא״ – בִּמְאוֹרָע לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Since it is stated: "You shall send [*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*] the mother, but the young you may take for yourself,"** one **might** have thought that the doubled verb "*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*" indicates that one must **search** even **in the mountains and hills in order to find a nest** with which to perform this mitzva. Therefore, **the verse states: "If** a bird's nest **happens,"** indicating that one is obligated to send away the mother only **when it confronts you;** one is not required to seek out a nest.

Six or seven lines into the page, the Gemara introduces a *baraita*: *tanu rabanan* — our rabbis taught. The verse reads, *ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha* — "if a bird's nest chances upon you." The *baraita* asks: *mah talmud lomar* — what is the significance of this phrasing? Since the verse also states *shaleach tishalach es ha'eim v'es habanim tikach lach* — "you shall send away the mother and take the children for yourself" — one might have entertained the notion that there is an obligation to actively seek out this *mitzvah*, to go roaming through the mountains and hills in order to find a mother bird with eggs. After all, one might reason, should I not pursue all the *mitzvos* of the *Torah*?

The *baraita* dispels this reading: *talmud lomar ki yikarei* — the verse teaches, *bim'ora lefanecha*, that the obligation arises only when the situation presents itself before you. There is no requirement to go out and engineer these circumstances.

### Kan Mikol Makom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קַן״ – קֵן מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״צִפּוֹר״ – טְהוֹרָה וְלֹא טְמֵאָה, ״לְפָנֶיךָ״ – בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, ״בַּדֶּרֶךְ״ – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, בָּאִילָנוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּכׇל עֵץ״, בְּבוֹרוֹת שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אוֹ עַל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The word **"nest"** indicates that this mitzva applies **in any case,** even to a nest with only a single chick or egg. The word **"bird's"** indicates that the mitzva applies only to **kosher** birds, **and not** to **non-kosher** birds. The term **"before you"** indicates that the mitzva applies to a nest that is **on private property,** e.g., an unguarded orchard or field, such that the owner's property does not acquire the nest for him. The term **"on the way"** indicates that the mitzva also applies to a nest found **in a public thoroughfare. From where** is it derived that the mitzva also applies to nests found **in trees? The verse states: "In any tree."** And with regard to nests found **in pits, ditches, and caves, from where** is it derived that the mitzva also applies to them? **The verse states: "Or on the ground,"** indicating that the mitzva applies to a nest on any type of ground.

The *baraita* continues with the phrase *kan mikol makom* — the nest, in all cases. This inclusive reading addresses the variety of locations where a nest might be found. The verse's mention of *tzippor* implies, as the *baraita* will develop, that the bird must be *tehorah v'lo tmeiah* — kosher and not non-kosher. The word *lefanecha* — "before you" — points toward something within your domain or under your control. And *baderech* — "on the way" — extends the obligation to the public thoroughfare.

The *baraita* then asks: if we know it applies on the road and in your property, how do I know it applies in trees? *Talmud lomar b'chol etz* — "in any tree," as the verse continues. And how do I know it applies in a well or cave? *Talmud lomar o al ha'aretz* — "or on the ground," reading this expansively to include anything sunk into the ground. Once we have established that the obligation extends to any location a nest may be found, the *baraita* returns to ask: if so, why do the words *lefanecha* and *baderech* appear at all, since they seem to be limiting? The answer is: *mah derech she'ein kano b'yadcha af kol she'ein kano b'yadcha* — just as a path implies that the nest is not literally in your hand, so too the *mitzvah* applies only when the nest is not under your direct control.

## Lefanecha Baderech

### Property & Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁסּוֹפֵנוּ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר, ״לְפָנֶיךָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ״ לְמָה לִי? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה דֶּרֶךְ שֶׁאֵין קִנּוֹ בְּיָדְךָ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין קִנּוֹ בְּיָדְךָ. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: יוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּיה שֶׁקִּנְּנוּ בִּטְפִיחִין וּבְבִירוֹת, וַאֲוָוזִין וְתַרְנְגוֹלִין שֶׁקִּנְּנוּ בַּפַּרְדֵּס – חַיָּיב בְּשִׁילּוּחַ, אֲבָל קִנְּנוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, וְכֵן יוֹנֵי הַרְדָּסִיָּאוֹת – פָּטוּר מִשִּׁילּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And since, in the end, we will include everything,** i.e., every location of the nest, from the verse: "In any tree or on the ground," **why do I** need the earlier statement: **"Before you on the way"?** It is **to say to you: Just as** a nest on the **way** is a case in **which** the bird's **nest is not in your possession** and is not readily available for you, **so too,** with regard to **all** other cases, one is obligated only **when its nest is not in your possession. From here** the Sages **stated:** With regard to **pigeons of a dovecote or pigeons of an attic that nested in** small **wall niches or in buildings, and geese or chickens that nested in an orchard, one is obligated in** the mitzva of **sending** the mother bird away, because such birds are not in one's possession. **But** with regard to birds that **nested inside the house, and likewise** with regard to **domesticated pigeons, one is exempt from** the mitzva of **sending** the mother bird away.

From this *drasha* of *baderech*, the *baraita* derives a practical rule. It lists cases that are still obligatory despite falling within one's property: pigeons of a dovecote (*yonei shovach*) and pigeons of an attic (*yonei aliyah*) that made their nest in protruding beams or in various structures; ducks and chickens that nested in a garden or orchard. In all these cases, even though the birds are on one's property, they are not sufficiently under the owner's control to exempt from *shiluach*. Rashi explains that the term *tafiach* refers to protrusions — places where birds might naturally nest — though some translate it as jars placed into walls for that purpose.

The principle at work is that *lefanecha* and *baderech* are both pointing to the same underlying condition: the nest must not be *b'yadcha* — in your hand, figuratively speaking. Being in one's yard or attic is not enough to constitute that level of control. A bird nesting on a balcony, for instance, that would fly away if one approached, falls squarely in this category of obligatory *shiluach* — the bird remains functionally free.

### Free vs. Captured

By contrast, the *baraita* exempts birds that nested inside the house itself (*kanenu betoch habayit*), and similarly the so-called Herodian doves (*yonei hordasiyot*), associated with the elaborate underground structures from the Herodian period — intricate networks of cavities where birds were kept in a manner that was fully captured and controlled. Such birds are *patur mishiluach* — exempt from the obligation.

The conceptual distinction, then, is not property versus public domain — it is freedom versus captivity. A bird in your orange grove, in your dovecote's open reaches, in your attic's rafters: still free, still obligatory. A bird kept in an enclosed cage, in a fully enclosed structure under your management: captured, exempt.

### Gemara Rejects Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מָה ״דֶּרֶךְ״ – שֶׁאֵין קִנּוֹ בְּיָדְךָ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין קִנּוֹ בְּיָדְךָ. הָא לְמָה לִי? מִ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא״ נָפְקָא, ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא״ פְּרָט לִמְזוּמָּן. וְעוֹד, ״לְפָנֶיךָ״ לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the above *baraita*: **The Master said: Just as** a nest on the **way** is a case in **which** the bird's **nest is not in your possession, so too,** with regard to **all** other cases, one is obligated only **when its nest is not in your possession.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this** derivation? **It** may be **derived from: "If** a bird's nest **happens,"** as it is taught: **"If** a bird's nest **happens" excludes** a nest that is readily **available. And furthermore, why do I** need the term **"before you"** to include even a nest found on private property? It is already derived from the verse: "In any tree or on the ground."

The Gemara then revisits the *baraita*'s logic. It notes: *ha lama li? Miki yikarei nafka* — why do I need *baderech* at all? We already learn the exclusion of a controlled bird from *ki yikarei* — "if it chances," implying it must not be something already designated and under your control. Furthermore, the Gemara asks: *ve'od lefanecha lama li* — and what then is the role of *lefanecha*, "before you"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״לְפָנֶיךָ״, לְאֵתוֹיֵי שֶׁהָיוּ לְפָנֶיךָ וּמָרְדוּ, ״בַּדֶּרֶךְ״ – כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מָצָא קֵן בַּיָּם חַיָּיב בְּשִׁילּוּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הַנּוֹתֵן בַּיָּם דָּרֶךְ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the term **"before you"** is necessary **to include** a case **where** the birds **were before you,** i.e., they had an owner and were readily available to him, **and** they then **rebelled** and fled and nested elsewhere. **"On the way"** is necessary for **that which Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** If one **found a nest in the sea,** e.g., in a case where a tree was washed out to sea with a nest in its branches, one **is obligated in** the mitzva of **sending** the mother bird away, **as it is stated: "So said the Lord, who makes a way in the sea,** and a path in the mighty waters" (Isaiah 43:16). The term "way" applies even to the sea.

The Gemara answers: *ella lefanecha le'atuyei shehayu lefanav umardu* — rather, *lefanecha* comes to include a case where the bird had previously been under your control but escaped and returned to a wild state. Even though it once was "before you," now that it has reasserted its freedom and you are chancing upon it anew, you remain obligated. *Lefanecha* tells us that even former ownership does not create a permanent exemption once the bird is no longer under your control.

With *ki yikarei* excluding the currently controlled, and *lefanecha* including the formerly owned but now free, the Gemara sets aside the *baraita*'s reading of *baderech* as the source for exclusion, and instead takes *baderech* to serve a different function — which Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav will now explain.

## Baderech Extensions

### Ocean Case

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: if one found a nest floating in the ocean, *chayav beshiluach* — one is obligated to perform *shiluach haken*. The source: the verse states, *ko amar Hashem hanoten bayam derech* — "so says God who places a path in the sea." Since the *Torah* uses the word *derech* in connection with the sea, the *baderech* of the *shiluach haken* verse includes even this unusual location.

### Sky & Vulture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, מָצָא קֵן בַּשָּׁמַיִם, דִּכְתִיב ״דֶּרֶךְ נֶשֶׁר בַּשָּׁמַיִם״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיחַיַּיב בְּשִׁילּוּחַ הַקֵּן? ״דֶּרֶךְ נֶשֶׁר״ אִיקְּרִי, ״דֶּרֶךְ״ סְתָמָא לָא אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If that is so,** then if **one found a nest in the sky,** e.g., in a case where a bird carries the nest as it flies, about **which it is written: "The way of an eagle in the sky"** (Proverbs 30:19), one should **also be obligated in** the mitzva of **sending** away the mother bird from **the nest.** The Gemara responds: The sky **is called "the way of an eagle,"** but **it is not called: A way,** in **an unspecified** manner. The sea, by contrast, is referred to simply as: A way.

The Gemara pushes further: *ela me'ata matza kan bashamayim* — if so, what if one found a nest in the sky? Rashi explains this refers to a *nesher* — more accurately a vulture — carrying a nest in its talons while in flight. Perhaps one would be obligated there as well, since it says *derech nesher bashamayim* — "the way of the vulture in the heavens"?

The Gemara rejects this: *derech nesher ikri, derech stam lo ikrei* — in the sky, it is called "the way of the vulture," a qualified expression, not simply "a path." Only a plain, unqualified *derech* is included by the *baderech* of the *shiluach* verse. The ocean is called simply a *derech*; the sky is called only *derech nesher*. Hence the ocean qualifies, the sky does not.

One noted halakhic application of this distinction: the Rogatchover Gaon reportedly argued, based on this very *Gemara*, that one need not recite *Tefillat Haderech* upon an airplane flight, since *ba'avir* — through the air — is not categorized as *derech* but only as *derech nesher*.

### Nest on Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ פַּפּוּנָאֵי לְרַב מַתְנָה: מָצָא קֵן בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מַהוּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״וַאֲדָמָה עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״. מֹשֶׁה מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? ״בְּשַׁגַּם הוּא בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The** residents **of Pappunya said to Rav Mattana:** If one **found a nest on the head of a person, what is** the *halakha* with regard to the mitzva of sending away the mother? Is the nest considered to be on the ground, such that one is obligated in the mitzva? Rav Mattana **said** to them that one is obligated in the mitzva in such a case because the verse states: **"And earth upon his head"** (II Samuel 15:32), rather than: Dirt upon his head, indicating that one's head is considered like the ground. Since the verse applies the term *adama* to what is upon the person's head, it suggests the person himself is treated as being connected to the ground, and thus the nest is considered as resting upon the earth. Within the parameters of *ki yimatze ki yikarei lefanecha baderech*, such a case falls within the obligation.

The people of Papuna (*Papunai*) posed a question to Rav Masna: *matza kan berosho shel adam mahu* — what is the ruling if a bird made its nest on a person's head? The question has a whimsical quality to it — alongside the ocean and the sky, they are now asking about a human head — yet the answer is substantive. Rav Masna replied: *va'adama al rosho* — and the ground was upon his head, citing a verse about Shmuel. Rashi explains that the word *adama* — as opposed to *afar* (dust) or *aphar* (earth) — is used specifically for earth that is still connected to the ground. Since the verse applies the term *adama* to what is upon the person's head, it suggests the person himself is treated as being connected to the ground, and thus the nest is considered as resting upon the earth. Within the parameters of *ki yimatze ki yikarei lefanecha baderech*, such a case falls within the obligation.

This naturally raises the question of a nest found on a wagon or a moving vehicle — cases the Gemara does not address, but which the logic of the *Papunai* exchange would seem to leave unresolved.

## Biblical Hints Digr.

### Name Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֹשֶׁה מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? ״בְּשַׁגַּם הוּא בָּשָׂר״. הָמָן מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? ״הֲמִן הָעֵץ״. אֶסְתֵּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? ״וְאָנֹכִי הַסְתֵּר אַסְתִּיר״. מׇרְדֳּכַי מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? דִּכְתִיב: ״מׇר דְּרוֹר״, וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: ״מֵירָא דַּכְיָא״.
>
> **Translation:** They also asked Rav Mattana: **From where in the Torah** is the existence of **Moses [*Moshe*]** alluded to before his birth? He replied that the verse states: **"For that he also [*beshaggam*] is flesh;** therefore shall his days be one hundred and twenty years" (Genesis 6:3). The numerical value of *beshaggam* is the same as that of the Hebrew name *Moshe*, and it is known that Moses lived a total of 120 years (see Deuteronomy 34:7). They also asked Rav Mattana: **From where in the Torah** can one find an allusion to the hanging of **Haman?** He replied: The verse states after Adam ate from the tree of knowledge: **"Have you eaten of [*hamin*] the tree,** about which I commanded you that you should not eat?" (Genesis 3:11). *Hamin* is spelled in the same manner as Haman: *Heh*, *mem*, *nun*. They also asked Rav Mattana: **From where in the Torah** can one find an allusion to the events involving **Esther?** He replied to them that the verse states: "Then My anger shall be kindled against them on that day, and I will forsake them, and I will hide My face from them, and they shall be devoured, and many evils and troubles shall come upon them; so that they will say in that day: Have not these evils come upon us because our God is not among us? **And I will hide [*haster astir*]** My face on that day for all the evil which they shall have wrought, in that they are turned to other gods" (Deuteronomy 31:17–18). They also asked him: **From where in the Torah** can one find an allusion to the greatness bestowed upon **Mordecai?** He replied: **As it is written** with regard to the anointing oil in the Tabernacle: "And you shall also take the chief spices, of **flowing myrrh [*mor deror*]"** (Exodus 30:23); **and we translate** *mor deror* into Aramaic as: ***Mira dakhya*,** which resembles the name Mordecai.

The *Papunai* continued with a series of further questions to Rav Masna. *Moshe min haTorah minayin* — where is Moshe hinted at in the *Torah*? The answer: the verse in *Parshas Noach* reads *beshagam hu basar* — "for he too is flesh." The *gematria* of *beshagam* equals that of *Moshe*, and the continuation — *vehayu yamav me'ah ve'esrim shana* — "his days shall be one hundred and twenty years" — matches the lifespan of Moshe Rabbeinu exactly.

*Haman min haTorah minayin* — where is Haman hinted? From *hamina ha'etz* — "have you eaten from this tree?" — and indeed Haman was hanged from a tree. The allusion is not merely verbal but contextual: as Moshe's hint includes his lifespan, Haman's hint includes his end.

*Esther min haTorah minayin* — where is Esther hinted? From *ve'anochi haster astir* — "and I will surely hide my face from them on that day." Rashi notes that the hidden face of God — *hester panim* — is a central theme of the period of the Megillah: God's name does not appear in the Megillah, and the Jewish people faced a decree of annihilation. The continuation of that verse, *umatze'u tzaros rabbos vera'os* — "and they will find many great evils" — corresponds to the travails of that period.

*Mordechai min haTorah minayin* — where is Mordechai hinted? The verse speaks of *mar dror*, one of the spices of the *shemen hamishcha*, the anointing oil. *Umitargeminan mira dachya* — and we translate this as "pure myrrh," but *mira dachya* in Aramaic resembles *Mordechai*. Rashi notes that this hints at Mordechai's greatness — the association with something precious and aromatic foretells his ultimate honor.

### Maharsha Validation

The Maharsha offers a sophisticated framework for understanding why the Gemara bothers with these derivations. The underlying question, he explains, is whether these names — which are not originally Hebrew — can be said to be the proper, fitting names for these individuals. The *Torah*'s allusions to their names serve to validate them: even though Moshe was given an Egyptian name by Pharaoh's daughter, the *Torah* hints to that very name, showing it to be divinely appropriate. Similarly, the Persian names of Esther and Mordechai are ratified by their appearance in the text of *Torah*.

### Non-Hebrew Names

The Maharsha makes the striking observation that Moshe is indeed an Egyptian name — the suffix of Pharaoh's names (*Thutmose*, *Ramesses*) derives from the same root, meaning "son of." *Ki min hamayim meshisihu* — "for I drew him from the water" — was spoken by Pharaoh's daughter, who was speaking Egyptian, not Hebrew. Esther and Mordechai, the Maharsha further notes, are Persian names. He then cites a remarkable position of the Ibn Ezra: that God's name does not appear in the Megillah because the Megillah was written by Persians. This claim is startling — if Esther herself wrote it, as the verse *vatichtov Esther hamalka* implies, the Ibn Ezra's reasoning remains puzzling. And if the entire text were composed in Persian by non-Jews, why is it written in Hebrew? The Ibn Ezra's position is left as a curiosity without full resolution.

The connection of all this to the surrounding sugya is that these questions were posed by the *Papunai* to Rav Masna — seemingly as a collection of riddles or proofs, perhaps with a playful character, beginning with the nest on a person's head and continuing through a series of Torah allusions.

## Herodian Doves

### Rav Kahana Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לְדִידִי חַזְיָין לִי, וְקָיְימָן שִׁיתְּסַר דָּרֵי בִּפְתֵי מִילָא, וַהֲוָה קָרָא קִירִי קִירִי, הֲוָה חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ דְּלָא הֲוָה קָרֵי קִירִי קִירִי, אֲמַרָה לַהּ חֲבֶרְתַּהּ: סוֹמָא, אֱמַרִי קִירִי קִירִי, אֲמַרָה: סוֹמָא, אֱמַרִי קִירִי כִּירִי, אַתְיוּהָ וְשַׁחְטוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: I myself saw** these pigeons, **and they** were **standing** in **sixteen rows,** each **a *mil* wide, and they were calling** out: **My master, my master. There was one of them who was not calling** out: **My master, my master. Another** one **said to it: Blind** one, i.e., fool, **say: My master, my master,** so that you will not be punished for refusing to acknowledge the authority of the king. The pigeon **said** in response: **Blind** one, you should **say: My master, my slave,** as Herod is not a king but a slave. **They brought** that pigeon to a slaughterhouse **and slaughtered it** for speaking against the king.

The Gemara returns to the case of *yonei hordasiyot* — the Herodian doves — as a paradigm of *mezuman*, fully controlled birds exempt from *shiluach*. Rav Kahana reported: *l'didi hazin* — I myself saw these birds — and they were arrayed in sixteen rows stretching across the width of a mile. The scale itself suggests something extraordinary. These birds would cry *kiri kiri*, and one among them refused to do so. Another bird turned to it and said, *suma* — "blind one," meaning fool — *imri kiri kiri*, say what you are supposed to say. The recalcitrant bird replied: *suma, imri kiri biri* — "say rather *kiri biri*." Rashi explains: *kiri* means master, and *biri* means slave. The birds had been trained to acknowledge Herod as their master, but this one declared that their master was himself a slave — a reference to the well-known tradition in the Gemara that Herod descended from slaves. So deeply embedded was this memory that even these birds, generations removed, carried it. *Asyuve veshachtua* — so they took the rebellious bird and slaughtered it, presumably Herod's household slaves who were still around.

### Rav Ashi Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמַר לִי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִילִּין. מִילִּין סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: בְּמִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Rabbi Ḥanina said to me:** This story is no more than mere **words,** as no such incident took place. The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** that Rabbi Ḥanina dismisses as mere **words** an incident reported by Rav Kahana? **Rather, say** that those pigeons acted as described above **through words** of witchcraft.

Rav Ashi reported in the name of Chanina that the story was *milat* — nonsense, or fabrication. The Gemara pushes back: would Rav Kahana simply make something up? *Ela eima bemilin* — rather, it was done through words, meaning through magic or incantation. Some interpret this to mean the birds were not speaking in human language, but in bird-speech, and Rav Kahana possessed knowledge of bird-speech. Either way, the Gemara openly acknowledges the fantastical character of the account.

## Herodian Doves (II)

### Mezuman Status

The story of the Herodian doves serves its halakhic purpose: they are the example of fully *mezuman* — captured and controlled — birds that are exempt from *shiluach haken*. The entire elaborate episode is the Gemara's illustration of what it means for birds to be so thoroughly under human dominion that the *mitzvah* does not apply.

## Of Tamei Debate

### Tzippor = Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹף טָמֵא פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא קֵן צִפּוֹר לְפָנֶיךָ״, עוֹף מַשְׁמַע לַן בֵּין טָהוֹר בֵּין טָמֵא. ״צִפּוֹר״ – טָהוֹר אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּאִיקְּרִי ״צִפּוֹר״, טָמֵא לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּאִיקְּרִי ״צִפּוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: If one encounters a nest of **a non-kosher bird,** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother bird. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: As the verse states: "If a bird's [*tzippor*] nest happens before you"** (Deuteronomy 22:6), and not: If a bird's [*of* ] nest happens before you. The word ***of* indicates to us** that all birds are included, **whether kosher or non-kosher.** But with regard to the word ***tzippor*, we have found** that **a kosher** bird **is called a *tzippor*,** but **we have not found** that **a non-kosher** bird **is called a *tzippor*.**

The Gemara turns to the explicit ruling: *of tamei patur belishloach* — a non-kosher bird, one need not send away. *Minani mili* — from where is this derived? Rabbi Yitzchak answers: from the verse *ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha*. The word *of* — bird in general — encompasses both kosher and non-kosher species, as evidenced by the verse *kol of tahor tocheilu* — "eat every kosher bird" — which implies that there is also an *of tamei*. But the verse of *shiluach haken* does not say *of*; it says *tzippor*. And *tzippor tahor ashkechan d'ikrei, tzippor tamei lo ashkechan d'ikrei* — we find kosher birds referred to as *tzippor*, but we never find a non-kosher bird described as a *tzippor*.

### Challenges to Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״תַּבְנִית כׇּל צִפּוֹר כָּנָף״, מַאי לָאו ״צִפּוֹר״ – בֵּין טָהוֹר בֵּין טָמֵא, ״כָּנָף״ – חֲגָבִים? לָא, ״צִפּוֹר״ – טָהוֹר, ״כָּנָף״ – טָמֵא וַחֲגָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to reject the above assertion: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the prohibition against fashioning idols: **"The likeness of any winged bird [*tzippor*]"** (Deuteronomy 4:17). **What, is it not** that the word **"*tzippor*"** is referring to any bird, **whether kosher or non-kosher,** and the word **"winged"** adds the likeness of **grasshoppers** to the prohibition? If so, we have found that a non-kosher bird is called a *tzippor*. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the word **"*tzippor*"** means **a kosher** bird, while the word **"winged"** is referring to **non-kosher** birds **and** to **grasshoppers.**

The Gemara systematically challenges this claim with a series of verses. First: *tavnit kol tzippor kanaf* — "the image of any *tzippor* of wing," the prohibition against making graven images. Surely this must include non-kosher birds. Second: *hachaya vechol habeheima, remes vetzippor kanaf* — the verse enumerating all creatures that praise God, which must include everything. Third: *kol tzippor kol kanaf* — all birds, all winged creatures entering Noach's ark, which was all-inclusive. Fourth: *ve'ata ben adam, emor letzippor kol kanaf* — "and you, son of man, say to the bird, to all of wing."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״הַחַיָּה וְכׇל בְּהֵמָה רֶמֶשׂ וְצִפּוֹר כָּנָף״, מַאי לָאו ״צִפּוֹר״ – בֵּין טָהוֹר בֵּין טָמֵא, וְ״כָנָף״ – חֲגָבִים? לָא, ״צִפּוֹר״ – טָהוֹר, ״כָּנָף״ – טָמֵא וַחֲגָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the passage describing how all of creation praises God: **"The undomesticated animal and all domesticated animals, creeping things and winged birds [*tzippor*]"** (Psalms 148:10). **What, is it not** that the word **"*tzippor*"** is referring to all birds, **whether kosher or non-kosher, and** the word **"winged"** is referring to **grasshoppers?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the word **"*tzippor*"** means **a kosher** bird, while the word **"winged"** is referring to **non-kosher** birds **and** to **grasshoppers.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כׇּל צִפּוֹר כׇּל כָּנָף״, מַאי לָאו כִּדְמַקְשִׁינַן? לָא, כְּדִמְשַׁנֵּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the verse about the animals that entered Noah's ark: **"Every bird [*tzippor*] of every type of wing"** (Genesis 7:14). **What, is it not** the same **difficulty that we posed** previously, that the word "*tzippor*" is referring to all birds, kosher and non-kosher, and the word "wing" is referring to grasshoppers? The Gemara rejects this as well: **No,** it is actually **as we resolved** it, that the word "*tzippor*" is referring only to kosher birds, and the word "wing" is referring to non-kosher birds and to grasshoppers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְאַתָּה בֶן אָדָם אֱמֹר לְצִפּוֹר כׇּל כָּנָף״, מַאי לָאו כִּדְאַקְשִׁינַן? לָא, כִּדְשַׁנֵּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the passage describing the war of Gog and Magog: **"And you, son of man,** so said the Lord God: **Speak to the birds [*tzippor*] of every type of wing,** and to every animal of the field: Assemble yourselves, and come" (Ezekiel 39:17). **What, is it not** the same **difficulty that we posed** previously? The Gemara responds: **No,** it is actually **as we resolved** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וּבְעַנְפוֹהִי יְדוּרָן צִפְּרֵי שְׁמַיָּא״ – ״צִפְּרֵי שְׁמַיָּא״ אִיקְּרוֹ, ״צִפְּרֵי״ סְתָמָא לָא אִיקְּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** stated in the verse describing a tree: **"And in its branches will dwell the birds [*tzipparei*] of the heaven"** (Daniel 4:9). The verse states only: "The birds [*tzipparei*]," and not: The birds of any type of wing. Accordingly, the term "the birds [*tzipparei*]" must be referring to all birds, whether kosher or non-kosher, since all birds dwell in tree branches. The Gemara rejects this: Non-kosher birds **are called: Birds [*tzipparei*] of the heaven,** but **they are not called: *Tzipparei*,** in **an unspecified** manner.

In each case, the Gemara provides the same structural answer: *tzippor* refers to kosher birds, while *kanaf* — the additional modifier — extends the verse to include non-kosher birds and other winged creatures such as locusts (*chagavim*). The pairing is consistent: *tzippor* alone denotes the kosher species; *kanaf* or *tzippor kanaf* is the broader inclusive term.

One further challenge: *tzipparei shamaya* — birds of the heaven in the vision of Nevuchadnezzar, all of which dwelled in the great tree — surely this includes non-kosher birds. The Gemara responds: *tzipparei shamaya ikri, tzipparei stama lo ikrei* — "birds of the heaven" is a qualified expansive phrase; the plain word *tzippor* without qualification remains limited to kosher species. The pattern established here mirrors the earlier ruling about *derech*: a plain term has a narrower scope; a qualified compound expression has a broader one.

## Tehora Drasha

### Kosher Only?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כׇּל צִפּוֹר טְהוֹרָה״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא טְמֵאָה? לָא, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the passage discussing kosher and non-kosher animals: **"Every kosher bird [*tzippor*]** you may eat" (Deuteronomy 14:11). Since it was necessary for the verse to specify that it is referring to a kosher bird [*tzippor*], **by inference,** one may conclude **that there is a non-kosher** bird [*tzippor*], in contradiction to the statement of Rabbi Yitzḥak. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** this is not the correct inference. Rather, **by inference** one may conclude **that there is** a *tzippor* that is **prohibited** for consumption, despite its being a kosher bird.

The Gemara now brings a verse that seems more directly challenging: *kol tzippor tehora tocheilu* — "eat every pure bird." If *tzippor* already means only kosher birds, why does the verse need to add the word *tehora* — pure, or kosher? The very presence of that word suggests that *tzippor* on its own is not limited to kosher species, and *tehora* is what narrows it. This would imply there are non-kosher *tzipporim* — which contradicts Rabbi Yitzchak's entire position.

### Tehora Excludes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? אִי טְרֵפָה – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב! וְאִי בִּשְׁחוּטָה דִּמְצוֹרָע – מִסֵּיפָא דִּקְרָא נָפְקָא, ״וְזֶה אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֹאכְלוּ מֵהֶם״ – לְרַבּוֹת שְׁחוּטַת מְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this kosher bird whose prohibition must be derived from this verse? **If** it is a bird **with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** that would be superfluous, as that **is written explicitly** in the verse: "An unslaughtered animal carcass, or a *tereifa*, he shall not eat" (Leviticus 22:8). **And if** it is referring **to the slaughtered** bird from the purification ritual **of a leper, which is prohibited for consumption despite being kosher, that would also be superfluous, since it** is **derived from the latter clause of the verse** in Deuteronomy, as the verse states: **"But these are they of which you shall not eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:12), which serves **to include the slaughtered** bird **of the leper.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בִּשְׁחוּטָה דִּמְצוֹרָע, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בַּעֲשֵׂה וּבְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. וְלוֹקְמַהּ בִּטְרֵפָה, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה? דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָנוֹ, וּבְעִנְיָנָא דִּשְׁחוּטָה כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Actually,** the verse: "Every kosher bird you may eat," is referring **to the slaughtered** bird **of a leper, and** the verse indicates that one **transgresses** the **positive mitzva** of: "You may eat," in addition to the **prohibition** of: "But these are they of which you shall not eat," **on account of it.** The Gemara objects: **But** why not **interpret** the verse as referring **to a *tereifa*,** and explain that the verse teaches that one **transgresses a positive mitzva and a prohibition on account of it?** The Gemara responds: The verse should be interpreted as referring to the slaughtered bird of a leper, since it is **a matter derived from its context,** as that verse **is written in the context of a slaughtered** bird. By contrast, a *tereifa* is not properly slaughtered, and interpreting the verse in that manner does not fit the context.

The Gemara's answer is elegant: *lo miklal de'ika issura* — the inference is not that *tzippor* includes non-kosher species, but that *tehora* excludes certain kosher-species birds that are nonetheless forbidden. What would that be? A *treifah* bird is one possibility, but that is explicitly prohibited elsewhere. More significantly: *bishchuta de'metzora* — the bird slaughtered as part of the *metzora* purification process. That bird is a kosher species, properly slaughtered, yet forbidden in consumption. The word *tehora* — eat the *tehora* — comes to exclude this specific case.

### Asei & Lo Ta'asei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שְׁתֵּי צִפֳּרִים חַיּוֹת״, מַאי ״חַיּוֹת״? לָאו שֶׁחַיּוֹת בְּפִיךְ? מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא לָאו שֶׁחַיּוֹת בְּפִיךְ! לָא, מַאי ״חַיּוֹת״? שֶׁחַיִּין רָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that which is stated in the passage discussing the purification ritual of a leper: "Then shall the priest command to take for him that is to be cleansed **two living birds [*tzipporim*]** that are kosher" (Leviticus 14:4). Since the birds are obviously alive before the purification ritual begins, **what** is the meaning of the word **"living"?** Is it **not that they are living in your mouth,** i.e., permitted for consumption? If so, **by inference,** one may conclude **that there are** birds [*tzipporim*] **that are not living in your mouth,** i.e., that are not kosher. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** that is not its meaning. Rather, **what** is the meaning of the word **"living"?** It means **that their extremities are living,** i.e., attached to their bodies. The verse indicates that the kosher birds must have all their limbs attached to their bodies in order to be used in the purification ritual.

The Gemara then asks: if *tehora* comes to exclude the *shechuta de'metzora*, then what does the other verse — *ve'zeh asher lo tochelu meihem* — come to add? The answer: the presence of both a positive commandment (*kol tzippor tehora tocheilu* — eat the kosher ones) and a negative commandment (*ve'zeh asher lo tochelu meihem* — do not eat these) creates a redundancy that is read as applying specifically to the *shechuta de'metzora*. Eating such a bird would violate both a *lo ta'asei* and an *asei*, making the prohibition doubly reinforced. This structure highlights the special status of the *metzora* bird among otherwise eligible kosher species.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע מִסֵּיפָא: ״טְהוֹרוֹת״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא טְמֵאוֹת? לָא, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא טְרֵפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof **from the latter clause** of that verse: "Two living birds [*tzipporim*] that are **kosher."** Since it was necessary for the verse to specify that it is referring to kosher birds, **by inference** one may conclude **that there are non-kosher** birds, in contradiction to the statement of Rabbi Yitzḥak. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** that is not the correct inference. Rather, **by inference** one may conclude **that there are** other birds of kosher species that may not be used for this ritual, i.e., ***tereifot*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְרֵפוֹת מֵ״חַיּוֹת״ נָפְקָא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה חַיָּה, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְעוֹד, בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה חַיָּה, בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ חַיָּה, מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But the disqualification of ***tereifot* is** already **derived from** the word **"living"** in that verse. The Gemara elaborates: Granted, **this works out well according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* can live** (see 42a); it is therefore necessary for the verse to state "kosher" to exclude *tereifot*, because one could not have derived it from the word "living." **But according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa*** animal **cannot live, what is there to say?** Let it be derived from the word "living." **And furthermore, whether according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* can live or according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* cannot live,** it is not necessary to derive the exclusion of *tereifot* from the word "kosher," since it **is derived from that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: נֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר בִּפְנִים, וְנֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר בַּחוּץ. מָה מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר הָאָמוּר בִּפְנִים – עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַכְשִׁיר כִּמְכַפֵּר, אַף מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר הָאָמוּר בַּחוּץ – עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַכְשִׁיר כִּמְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** It is **stated** in the Torah that there are offerings that **enable** one to partake of sacrificial foods, e.g., the sin offering of a woman who has given birth or the guilt offering of a leper, **and** there are offerings that **atone,** e.g., a sin offering or guilt offering, all of which are brought **inside** the Temple. **And** it is also **stated** in the Torah that there are offerings that **enable** one to partake of sacrificial foods, e.g., the birds of a leper's purification ritual, **and** offerings that **atone,** e.g., the scapegoat of the Yom Kippur service, that are brought **outside** the Temple. The *baraita* continues: Therefore, the offerings brought outside the Temple are compared to those offered inside: **Just as** with regard to the offerings that **enable or atone stated** in the Torah that are offered **inside** the Temple, the Torah **made** the offering that **enables like** the offering that **atones,** as even the former has portions of it that are burned on the altar, **so too,** with regard to offerings that **enable or atone stated** in the Torah that are offered **outside** the Temple, the Torah **made** the offering that **enables like** the offering that **atones.** Accordingly, just as the scapegoat must not be a *tereifa*, so too, the birds of a leper's purification ritual must not be *tereifot*. If so, there is no need to derive the exclusion of *tereifa* birds from the word "kosher."

## Metzora Birds

### Chayot Meaning

The Gemara turns to the specific language of the *metzora* purification: *shtei tzipporim chayot* — "two living birds." The question is: *mai chayot* — what does the word *chayot* ("living" or "alive") come to add? The initial suggestion: *she'chayot be'ficha* — that they are alive in your mouth, meaning edible, which would imply *tzippor* on its own could include non-kosher species. This reading would mean *chayot* is what restricts the birds to those that can be consumed.

### Chayot vs Tehorot

The Gemara rejects that reading. *Ma chayot? She'chayot rashei evarim shelahem* — "alive" means that all their limbs are intact, alive and whole. For birds brought as *korbanot*, while there is no concept of *mum* (blemish) as with animals, there is the category of *mechusar evier* — lacking a limb. Thus *chayot* establishes that the birds must be complete, not that they must be kosher species. That requirement — kosher species — comes from the separate word *tehorot* (pure), which appears in the full verse: *shtei tzipporim chayot tehorot*. The inference from *tehorot* would be that *tzippor* on its own could include non-kosher species; but as before, the Gemara rejects this inference by explaining that *tehorot* is not excluding non-kosher species but something else entirely.

### Machshir Mechaper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְמַעוֹטֵי צִפּוֹרֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. לְמַאי? אִי לְשִׁילּוּחַ – לֹא אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה שַׁלַּח לְתַקָּלָה, אֶלָּא לִשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The word "kosher" serves **to exclude birds from an idolatrous city.** Such a city must be burned to the ground, and it is prohibited for one to derive benefit from any of its contents. The verse indicates that such a bird is unfit for use in the leper's purification ritual. The Gemara asks: **For what** function are such birds rendered unfit by the word "kosher"? **If** the verse means **to** render them unfit for **sending** away as part of the ritual, this is unnecessary, since the **Torah did not say** to **send** a bird only **to create** a **stumbling block.** It is obvious that any bird prohibited for consumption is unfit for sending, since the Torah would not allow for the possibility that the bird sent away will be trapped and consumed by unwitting individuals. **Rather,** the verse means **to** render them unfit for use as the bird intended for **slaughter.**

The Gemara invokes the principle taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ne'emar machshir umechaper bifnim, v'ne'emar machshir umechaper bachutz* — just as inside the *Beis Hamikdash* the requirements for a *korban* that *machshir* (purifies) and one that *mechaper* (atones) are identical, so too outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the things used for *machshir* and *mechaper* must meet the same standards. Inside, whether a *korban* is of a *zav* seeking purification or a *chatas* seeking atonement, all the same rules apply — no blemish, proper species, intact. Outside, the paradigmatic *machshir* is the *metzora* purification birds, and the paradigmatic *mechaper* is the *se'ir la'azazel* — the scapegoat. Since the *se'ir la'azazel* must be free of any disqualifying defect (for it could be designated for either purpose on *Yom Kippur* and thus must qualify for the *Beis Hamikdash*), the same standard applies to the *metzora* birds. They cannot be a *treifah*, and they must have all their limbs. This *limud* renders both *chayot* and *tehorot* available to exclude other things.

## Metzora Exclusions

### Avoda Zara Proceeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְמַעוֹטֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְזַוֵּוג לָהּ אַחֶרֶת קוֹדֶם שִׁלּוּחֶיהָ. לְמַאי? אִי לִשְׁחִיטָה – הָא בָּעֲיָא שִׁילּוּחַ! אֶלָּא לְשִׁילּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The word "kosher" serves **to exclude** the repeated use of a bird, i.e., a leper may **not pair** a bird **with another** bird from a previous leper, **before its** being **sent** away. The Gemara asks: **For what** function does the word "kosher" indicate that a bird may not be reused? **If** it means **to** indicate that the bird intended for being sent away by the first leper may not be used for **slaughter** by the second leper, the verse is unnecessary, as this bird **requires** being **sent** away as part of the first leper's purification ritual. **Rather,** the verse means **to** indicate that the bird intended for being sent away by the first leper may not be used for **sending** simultaneously by the second leper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: לְצִפֳּרִים שֶׁהֶחְלִיפוּ בְּצִפֳּרֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיִיתָ חֵרֶם כָּמֹהוּ״, כׇּל מַה שֶּׁאַתָּה מְהַיֶּיה הֵימֶנּוּ כָּמוֹהוּ. לְמַאי? אִי לְשִׁילּוּחַ – לֹא אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה שַׁלַּח לְתַקָּלָה, אֶלָּא לִשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The word "kosher" serves **to** exclude the use of **birds that were exchanged for birds of idol worship,** i.e., a gentile paid a Jew for his idol by giving the Jew birds. Such birds are prohibited from use in the leper's ritual, **as it is written** about objects of idol worship: "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house, **and become [*vahayita*] accursed like it"** (Deuteronomy 7:26), indicating that **anything that you generate [*mehayye*] from it,** e.g., through exchange, is prohibited **like it.** The Gemara asks: **For what** function does the word "kosher" indicate that such birds are prohibited? **If** it serves **to** exclude such birds from being **sent** away, this is unnecessary, because **the Torah did not say: Send** it away, if doing so could lead **to a mishap.** If the bird were prohibited, the Torah would not have commanded one to send it away, as others might eat it unwittingly. **Rather,** the word "kosher" serves **to** disqualify such birds for **slaughter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּעוֹף שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּגְמַר דִּינֵהּ – בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! אֶלָּא קוֹדֶם גְּמַר דִּינֵאּ, וּלְמַאי? אִי לְשִׁילּוּחַ – בָּעֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי לְבֵי דִינָא וְקַיּוֹמֵי ״וּבִעַרְתָּ הָרַע מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״! אֶלָּא לִשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Here we are dealing with a bird that killed a person** and is therefore subject to being killed. The verse indicates that such a bird is unfit for use in the leper's ritual. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** this is a case **where its verdict** of execution **was issued,** then **it is subject** to being **killed,** and there is no need for the verse to teach that it cannot be used in the ritual. **Rather,** it must be referring to a bird **before its verdict** of execution **was issued. And for what** function does the verse exclude such a bird? **If** it serves **to** exclude the bird from **sending** away, this is unnecessary, since one is **required to bring it to the court to fulfill** the verse: **"And you shall eradicate the evil from your midst"** (Deuteronomy 13:6). Obviously, one may not send it away. **Rather,** the verse serves **to** disqualify such a bird for **slaughter.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes: *l'ma'utei tzipporei ir hanidachat* — *tehorot* or *chayot* comes to exclude birds of an *ir hanidachat*, the subverted city condemned to destruction. This is a striking suggestion, for it implies that absent this verse one might have thought such birds could be used for a *korban* — which runs against intuition. Tosafot observes that the Gemara indeed seems to imply that birds of an *ir hanidachat* could otherwise have been valid for a *korban*, since the prohibition is against deriving benefit from them, and *mitzvot lav lehenot nitnu* — the performance of *mitzvos* is not considered personal benefit. The Gemara clarifies: such birds cannot be used for the *shiluach* bird (*lav deshiluach*) — this we would know independently, since the *Torah* would never command one to send away a bird that would then be found and impermissibly benefited from by another (*lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah*). Rather, it is the *shechitah* bird — the one slaughtered in the ritual — that *tehorot* comes to exclude.

Rav Pappa adds: *letzipporim shehichlifu betzipporei avoda zara* — birds obtained through exchange with idolatry, or purchased with proceeds of idol-selling. The verse states *vehayita charem kamohu* — it shall be banned like it; anything obtained through the proceeds of *avoda zara* is itself forbidden in benefit. One might have supposed that since using such a bird for *metzora* purification does not constitute personal *hana'ah*, it could still be used. The verse teaches otherwise. Again, the Gemara specifies it is the *shechitah* bird, not the *shiluach* bird, from which this is excluded — since sending away a forbidden-benefit bird is self-evidently impossible.

Ravina adds the case of a bird that has killed a person — *of sheharag es hanefesh*. Such a bird has the status of *shor haniskal* and is forbidden in benefit. One might have thought it could still be used for *metzora* purification prior to the formal judicial verdict (*kodem gmar din*), when it has not yet been formally condemned and killed. The Gemara asks: if the *gmar din* has already occurred, obviously it cannot be used. And before *gmar din*, the case for exclusion is this: one might have supposed that since there is a *mitzvah* to eliminate the dangerous animal (*uvi'arta hara mikirbecha*), shechting it for the *metzora* would serve that purpose at the same time. The verse *tehorot* comes to exclude this as well — only the *shechitah* bird, not the *shiluach* bird, since one would be obligated to bring the bird to court rather than send it away.

### Summary Tzippor

The cumulative picture is clear: *tzippor* — whether in the verse of *shiluach haken* or in the context of *metzora* birds — refers exclusively to kosher species. The word *tehorot* in the *metzora* passage does not come to reiterate what *tzippor* already establishes; it excludes specific, contextually relevant categories of disqualified birds: birds of an *ir hanidachat*, birds obtained with proceeds of *avoda zara*, and birds that have killed a person. In each case, the Gemara works out which of the two *metzora* birds — the one to be slaughtered or the one to be sent away — is the target of the exclusion, with the rule *lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah* serving to rule out the *shiluach* bird in most cases.

## Eggs vs Mother

### Species Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹף טָמֵא רוֹבֵץ עַל בֵּיצֵי עוֹף טָהוֹר. בִּשְׁלָמָא עוֹף טָמֵא רוֹבֵץ עַל בֵּיצֵי טָהוֹר – בָּעֵינַן ״צִפּוֹר״ וְלֵיכָּא, אֶלָּא עוֹף טָהוֹר רוֹבֵץ עַל בֵּיצֵי עוֹף טָמֵא – הָא ״צִפּוֹר״ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: If **a non-kosher bird is resting upon the eggs of a kosher bird,** or a kosher bird is resting upon the eggs of a non-kosher bird, one is exempt from sending away the bird. The Gemara objects: **Granted,** it is understandable that one is exempt in a case where **a non-kosher bird is resting upon the eggs of a kosher** bird, since **we require** a kosher bird, as the verse uses the word **"*tzippor*"** (Deuteronomy 22:6) in this context, which the Gemara earlier (139b) interpreted as a reference to a kosher bird, **and** this bird **is not** kosher. **But** in a case where **a kosher bird is resting upon the eggs of a non-kosher bird, it is a *tzippor*,** a kosher bird, and one should be required to send it away.

With the principle that *tzippor* means only a kosher species now firmly established, the Gemara addresses cross-species scenarios. *Of tamei rovetz al betzei of tahor, v'of tahor rovetz al betzei of tamei* — a non-kosher bird brooding over kosher eggs, or a kosher bird brooding over non-kosher eggs: both cases are exempt. The first case is straightforward — *be'inan tzippor v'leika* — the verse requires a *tzippor* (the mother), and a non-kosher mother is not a *tzippor*. But the second case requires more analysis: the mother is indeed a *tzippor*, a kosher bird. Why then is it exempt?

### Treifa Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: ״תִּקַּח לָךְ״ – וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ, הָכָא נָמֵי: ״תִּקַּח לָךְ״ – וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that this is in accordance with **that which Rav Kahana said** in a different context: The verse states: "But the young **you may take for yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:7), indicating that one is required to send away the mother only if the eggs are fit for consumption, **but not** if they are fit only **for your dog. Here too,** with regard to non-kosher eggs, the mitzva applies only if **you may take for yourself, but not** if they are fit only **for your dog** because they are not kosher.

This is where Rav Kahana's teaching is applied. The verse states *tikach lach* — "take for yourself" — which implies *lecha v'lo l'kalbecha*: take something that is directly usable by you, not merely fit for dog food. Non-kosher eggs, even under a kosher mother, cannot be directly consumed by a Jew. Therefore, the *tikach lach* condition is not met, and one is exempt. The *baraita* records the following: if the mother is a *treifah*, one is still obligated in *shiluach*; if the chicks or eggs are *treifos*, one is exempt. Each is governed by a separate principle. The mother's *treifah* status does not affect the obligation, since the mother is being sent away, not consumed. The chicks' *treifah* status does affect the obligation, since *tikach lach* requires that the chicks be fit for direct human benefit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא? אַהָא דְּתַנְיָא: אִם טְרֵפָה – חַיָּיב בְּשִׁילּוּחַ, אֶפְרוֹחִים טְרֵפוֹת – פָּטוּר מִשִּׁילּוּחַ. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״תִּקַּח לָךְ״ – וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where was** this statement **of Rav Kahana stated?** The Gemara answers that it was stated **with regard to that which is taught** in a *baraita*: Even if the **mother** bird is **a *tereifa*,** one is **obligated to send** away the mother from the nest. But if the **fledglings** are ***tereifot*,** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother. **From where is this matter** derived? **Rav Kahana said: As the verse states: "You may take for yourself,"** indicating that you are required to send away the mother only if the fledglings are fit for consumption, **but not** if they are fit only **for your dog** because they are *tereifot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַקִּישׁ אֵם טְרֵפָה לְאֶפְרוֹחִים, מָה אֶפְרוֹחִים טְרֵפוֹת – פָּטוּר מִשִּׁילּוּחַ, אַף אֵם טְרֵפָה נָמֵי – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** why not **compare a *tereifa* mother** bird **to** *tereifa* **fledglings** and say: **Just as** with regard to ***tereifa* fledglings** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother, **so too,** with regard to **a *tereifa* mother** one should **also** be **exempt from sending** her away.

This ruling contains a subtlety worth noting: when the eggs are *treifah* and there is no *mitzvah* of *shiluach*, one is permitted to simply take everything — eggs and mother alike. There is no obligation to send the mother away first. The exemption, while grounded in the logic of *tikach lach*, produces a result that sits in some tension with the broader values often associated with *shiluach haken* — the compassion for the mother bird. Yet the *Torah*'s framework, as the Gemara presents it, is technical: when the chicks do not qualify, the whole edifice of obligation collapses.

## Half-Slaughter Case

### Rav Hoshaia Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הוֹשִׁיט יָדוֹ לַקֵּן, וְשָׁחַט מִיעוּט סִימָנִים, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the statement of Rav Kahana above, **Rav Hoshaya raises a dilemma:** If one **stretched his hand into a nest** containing a mother bird and its fledglings **and slaughtered,** i.e., severed, **a minority of** the **two organs that must be severed in ritual slaughter [*simanim*],** i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, of the fledglings,**what is** the *halakha* with regard to sending away the mother bird?

Rav Hoshaia raises a probing edge case. *Hoshit yado l'kan v'shachat miut simanim mahu* — what if one reached into the nest and partially slaughtered one of the chicks, cutting a minority of the *simanim* (trachea and esophagus required for kosher *shechitah*)? At this moment, if one were to stop, the chick would be a *treifah*. If one were to continue, the *shechitah* would be completed and the chick would be fully kosher. The question is: at this intermediate moment, is the obligation of *shiluach* suspended? If so, one could grab the mother, complete the *shechitah*, and avoid the *mitzvah* altogether.

### Two Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ שָׁבֵיק לְהוּ מִטַּרְפִי, בָּעֵינַן ״לָךְ וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ״, אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּבְיָדוֹ לְמִגְמַר שְׁחִיטָה, ״תִּקַּח לָךְ״ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ, וְחַיָּיב בְּשִׁילּוּחַ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The two sides of the dilemma are explained: **Do we say: Since if those** fledglings **are left** as they are, with only partially severed *simanim*, **they will** eventually be rendered ***tereifot*,** one is therefore exempt from sending away the mother because **we require** that the fledglings be taken **"for yourself," and not for your dog? Or perhaps, since it is in his power to complete the** act of **slaughter,** thereby permitting the fledglings for consumption, **we** may **call** this case: **"Take for yourself," and one is obligated to send** away the mother bird. Since no answer is given, the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Two positions are articulated. The first: *keivan d'ilu shavik lehu mitrefi* — since at this moment, if you were to leave it, it would be a *treifah*, and *tikach lach* requires edibility, perhaps the obligation is suspended right now, allowing one to take the mother. The second position: *keivan d'veyadcha l'migmar shechita* — since it remains entirely within your power to complete the *shechitah* and render the chick fully kosher, it is still in the category of *tikach lach* and one remains obligated in *shiluach*.

The Gemara leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved. Intuitively, the overwhelming sense is that one should not be able to engineer an exemption through this kind of manipulation — the ability to complete the *shechitah* at will seems to keep the obligation in force — but the Gemara declines to rule definitively.

## Nest Scenarios

### Yirmiya Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מַטְלֵית מַהוּ שֶׁתָּחוֹץ? כְּנָפַיִם מַהוּ שֶׁיָּחוֹצּוּ? בֵּיצִים מוּזָרוֹת, מַהוּ? שְׁנֵי סִדְרֵי בֵיצִים זוֹ עַל גַּב זוֹ, מַהוּ? זָכָר עַל גַּבֵּי בֵיצִים וּנְקֵבָה עַל גַּבֵּי זָכָר, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** In a case where **a rag** is lying in the nest between the mother bird and the eggs, **what is** the *halakha*? Does **it interpose**between them,such that the mother is not considered to be resting upon the eggs? If so, one would be exempt from sending away the mother. Similarly, if feathers are detached from a bird's **wings** and are lying between the mother bird and the eggs, **what is** the *halakha*? Do **they interpose** between the eggs and the mother? Rabbi Yirmeya continues: If **unfertilized [*muzarot*] eggs** separate between the mother bird and fertilized eggs, **what is** the *halakha*? Additionally, in a case where **two sets of** fertilized **eggs** are lying **one on top of the other, what is** the *halakha*? Does the upper set of eggs interpose between the mother and the lower set? Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* in a case where **a male** bird is resting **on top of** the **eggs and a female** bird is resting **on top of** the **male?** Does the male bird interpose between the female and the eggs? No answers are given, so the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved in all of these cases.

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a series of questions probing the boundaries of the requirement that the mother be *rovetzes* — hovering over or crouching upon — the eggs. Does there need to be direct contact, or can intervening objects still satisfy the condition? He asks: *matlis* — what if a cloth was placed between the mother and the eggs? *Mahu shetachotz* — does such an interposition constitute a *chatzitza*, a division that breaks the relationship between mother and nest? Similarly, what about detached bird feathers (*knafayim*) lying on top of the eggs — does that divide mother from nest?

He further asks about *betzim muzarot* — unfertilized eggs. A separate principle will establish that only eggs capable of producing chicks (*betzim dumya d'efrochim*) qualify; if unfertilized eggs are mixed with fertilized ones, what is the ruling? And if two rows of eggs are stacked one atop the other, with the mother brooding over the upper row — is the upper row considered a division between mother and lower row? What if the father bird is directly on top of the eggs and the mother is on top of the father — is the mother considered to be *rovetzes* on the eggs? Or what if the reverse configuration applies?

Every one of Rabbi Yirmiya's questions is left as *teiku* — unresolved. The Gemara does not venture an answer to any of them.

## Korei Exception

### Zeira Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: יוֹנָה עַל בֵּיצֵי תָסִיל, מַהוּ? תָּסִיל עַל בֵּיצֵי יוֹנָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma:** If **a pigeon** is resting **upon the eggs of a *tasil*,** a kosher bird resembling a pigeon, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to sending away the mother bird from the nest? Likewise, if **a *tasil*** is resting **upon the eggs of a pigeon, what is** the *halakha*?

Rabbi Zeira asks: *yona al betzei tasiya mahu* — a dove brooding over the eggs of a *tasiya* (a bird of similar species), or the reverse — *tasiya al betzei yona mahu*? The scenario is one where a kosher mother bird is brooding over the eggs of a different kosher species — not her own offspring. Does the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* apply?

### Baraita Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תָּא שְׁמַע, עוֹף טָמֵא רוֹבֵץ עַל בֵּיצֵי עוֹף טָהוֹר, וְטָהוֹר רוֹבֵץ עַל בֵּיצֵי עוֹף טָמֵא – פָּטוּר מִשִּׁילּוּחַ. הָא טָהוֹר וְטָהוֹר – חַיָּיב! דִּלְמָא בְּקוֹרֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** that which is taught in the mishna: In a case where **a non-kosher bird is resting upon the eggs of a kosher bird, or a kosher** bird **is resting upon the eggs of a non-kosher bird,** one is **exempt from sending** away the bird. One may infer from the mishna **that** in a case involving **a kosher** bird **and kosher** eggs, e.g., a *tasil* resting on the eggs of a pigeon, one is **obligated** to send away the mother bird. The Gemara rejects this: **Perhaps** this inference applies only **to** the case of a female **pheasant,** which normally rests upon the eggs of other birds. Since this is its normal behavior, one is obligated to send it away even if it rests upon the eggs of another kosher bird. This may not be the case with regard to a *tasil* or pigeon.

Abaye brings a *baraita* in response: *of tamei rovetz al betzei of tahor v'of tahor rovetz al betzei of tamei patur* — the mixed-species cases (kosher on non-kosher, non-kosher on kosher) are exempt. The logical implication of this *baraita* is significant: *ha tahor v'tahor chayav* — a kosher mother on kosher eggs, even if they are not her own, would be obligatory. The inference from the *baraita* strongly supports the position that as long as both mother and eggs are kosher species, the obligation exists regardless of biological parentage.

### Korei Exception

The Gemara qualifies this inference: *dilma b'korei* — perhaps the *baraita*'s implied ruling of obligation for "kosher on kosher" refers specifically to the *korei*, the partridge, which as we shall see is explicitly described in a biblical verse as brooding over eggs it did not lay. The *korei* might be a special exception — a case where the *Torah* itself acknowledges and addresses this phenomenon. For other species, perhaps even kosher-on-kosher would require biological parentage. Nevertheless, the plain reading of the *baraita*'s implication, and the ruling of the Rambam, is that kosher mother on kosher eggs is obligatory even without biological parentage.

## Mishnah Korei

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹרֵא זָכָר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to the case of **a male pheasant** that rests upon the eggs of its species, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** one **obligated** to send away the pheasant, **and the Rabbis deem** one **exempt** from sending it away.

The Mishnah states: *korei zachar* — the male partridge — *Rabbi Eliezer mechayev v'chachamim potrin*. Rabbi Eliezer obligates the *mitzvah* of *shiluach*; the Sages exempt. The case combines two unusual features: the bird is male, and it is brooding over eggs it did not produce.

### Gara Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָתְיָא ״דְּגִירָה״ ״דְּגִירָה״, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״קוֹרֵא דָגַר וְלֹא יָלָד״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״וּבָקְעָה וְדָגְרָה בְצִלָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute, **Rabbi Abbahu said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer?** According to Rabbi Eliezer, a verbal analogy between **brooding** stated with regard to a male pheasant and **brooding** with regard to a female bird **comes** to indicate that the resting of a male pheasant upon its eggs is considered an effective resting. **It is written here: "As the pheasant that broods over young that he has not brought forth"** (Jeremiah 17:11), **and it is written elsewhere** about a female bird: "There shall the great owl make her nest, and lay, **and hatch, and brood under her shadow"** (Isaiah 34:15). Just as a female bird broods over its nest, so too, a male pheasant broods over a nest. Therefore, one is required to send away the male bird from the nest as well.

Rabbi Abahu explains Rabbi Eliezer's position through a *gezeirah shavah* — a hermeneutical equivalence based on the shared verb *dagra* / *gara*, meaning "to brood." One verse in Yirmiyahu reads: *korei dagar v'lo yalad* — "the partridge broods over what it did not give birth to," describing the *korei* sitting on another's eggs. A verse in the prophets elsewhere uses the same root — *u'vaka'ah v'dagrah b'tzilah* — for a bird brooding over its own eggs. Since the identical verb applies in both cases, Rabbi Eliezer derives that the *korei* brooding over another's eggs is treated equivalently to any bird brooding over its own. Therefore, even this male partridge on non-own eggs is subject to *shiluach haken*.

### Male & Korei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּקוֹרֵא זָכָר, אֲבָל בְּקוֹרֵא נְקֵבָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״קוֹרֵא זָכָר״ תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא רַבָּנַן אֲפִילּוּ קוֹרֵא נְקֵבָה פָּטְרִי, וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״זָכָר״ – לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis, **Rabbi Elazar says:** Their **dispute** is only **with regard to a male pheasant, but** with regard **to a female pheasant all agree** that one is **obligated** to send away the bird. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious,** given that **we learned** in the mishna: **A male pheasant?** The Gemara responds: **Lest you say** that **the Rabbis deem** one **exempt** from the mitzva of sending away the mother bird **even** in the case of **a female pheasant, and** as for **that which** the mishna **teaches** the dispute in the case of **a male** pheasant, the reason is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** in that he requires one to send away even a male pheasant, therefore, Rabbi Elazar **teaches us** that the Rabbis agree that one must send away a female pheasant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּקוֹרֵא זָכָר, אֲבָל בְּזָכָר דְּעָלְמָא – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״קוֹרֵא זָכָר״ תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֲפִילּוּ זָכָר דְּעָלְמָא מְחַיֵּיב, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״קוֹרֵא זָכָר״ – לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּרַבָּנַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar** also **says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis is only **with regard to a male pheasant, but** with regard **to male** birds **in general, all agree** that one is **exempt** from the mitzva of sending away the bird from the nest. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious,** given that **we learned** in the mishna: **A male pheasant?** The Gemara responds: **Lest you say** that **Rabbi Eliezer deems** one **obligated** to send away **even male** birds **in general, and that which** the mishna **teaches: A male** pheasant, is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** in that they also deem one exempt from sending a male pheasant, therefore, Rabbi Elazar **teaches us** that even according to Rabbi Eliezer, one is exempt from sending away male birds in general.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זָכָר דְּעָלְמָא – פָּטוּר, קוֹרֵא זָכָר – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This** explanation of Rabbi Elazar **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a male** bird **in general,** one is **exempt** from the mitzva of sending it away, but with regard to **a male pheasant, Rabbi Eliezer deems** one **obligated** to send it away from the nest, **and the Rabbis deem** one **exempt** from sending it away.

The Gemara asks: why does the Mishnah specify both *zachar* (male) and *korei* (partridge)? Is it not obvious that the Mishnah is addressing a male? The Gemara explains: the dual specification is needed to convey the full scope of the dispute. One might have thought the Sages exempt even a female *korei* — that the unusual brooding behavior of the *korei* as a species categorically removes it from the *mitzvah*. The Mishnah's mention of *zachar* teaches that only the male is disputed; a female *korei*, all agree, is obligated. Conversely, one might have thought Rabbi Eliezer obligates any male bird. The specification of *korei* teaches that only the male partridge — because of the explicit biblical verse — is within Rabbi Eliezer's ruling; all other male birds are exempt by universal consensus.

Rabbi Eliezer himself states: *machlokes b'korei zachar* — the dispute applies only to the male partridge. *Aval b'zachar d'alma, divrei hakol patur* — for any other male bird, all agree it is exempt. The Gemara anticipates the objection that this is obvious, since the Mishnah explicitly says *korei zachar*, and confirms: the specification is needed to prevent the inference that Rabbi Eliezer might obligate even an ordinary male. The *baraita* confirms the conclusion: an ordinary male (*zachar d'alma*) — exempt by all; a male partridge — Rabbi Eliezer obligates, the Sages exempt; and what remains on the margins of the discussion, though not explicitly disputed, is whether a female of any species brooding over another's eggs would be obligated — with the stronger implication of the *sugyah* being that it would.
