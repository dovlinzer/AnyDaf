# Hullin 140 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiluach Haken Intro

### Opening Context

The daf proper is 140, though the discussion opens at the very top of 139b, in the midst of the *mitzvah* of *shiluach haken*. The Gemara begins with the heading *chomer b'kisuy*, marking the transition into a new comparative discussion. The preceding material had explored the case of a mother bird that is *hekdesh* — consecrated property — and whether the *mitzvah* applies in such a circumstance. The Gemara there had worked through interesting questions about how ownership of a bird is dependent not merely on its creation but on its ongoing maintenance under one's control. With that as backdrop, the Gemara now proceeds.

### Ki Yikarei Drasha

Six or seven lines into the page, the Gemara introduces a *baraita*: *tanu rabanan* — our rabbis taught. The verse reads, *ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha* — "if a bird's nest chances upon you." The *baraita* asks: *mah talmud lomar* — what is the significance of this phrasing? Since the verse also states *shaleach tishalach es ha'eim v'es habanim tikach lach* — "you shall send away the mother and take the children for yourself" — one might have entertained the notion that there is an obligation to actively seek out this *mitzvah*, to go roaming through the mountains and hills in order to find a mother bird with eggs. After all, one might reason, should I not pursue all the *mitzvos* of the *Torah*?

The *baraita* dispels this reading: *talmud lomar ki yikarei* — the verse teaches, *bim'ora lefanecha*, that the obligation arises only when the situation presents itself before you. There is no requirement to go out and engineer these circumstances.

### Kan Mikol Makom

The *baraita* continues with the phrase *kan mikol makom* — the nest, in all cases. This inclusive reading addresses the variety of locations where a nest might be found. The verse's mention of *tzippor* implies, as the *baraita* will develop, that the bird must be *tehorah v'lo tmeiah* — kosher and not non-kosher. The word *lefanecha* — "before you" — points toward something within your domain or under your control. And *baderech* — "on the way" — extends the obligation to the public thoroughfare.

The *baraita* then asks: if we know it applies on the road and in your property, how do I know it applies in trees? *Talmud lomar b'chol etz* — "in any tree," as the verse continues. And how do I know it applies in a well or cave? *Talmud lomar o al ha'aretz* — "or on the ground," reading this expansively to include anything sunk into the ground. Once we have established that the obligation extends to any location a nest may be found, the *baraita* returns to ask: if so, why do the words *lefanecha* and *baderech* appear at all, since they seem to be limiting? The answer is: *mah derech she'ein kano b'yadcha af kol she'ein kano b'yadcha* — just as a path implies that the nest is not literally in your hand, so too the *mitzvah* applies only when the nest is not under your direct control.

## Lefanecha Baderech

### Property & Control

From this *drasha* of *baderech*, the *baraita* derives a practical rule. It lists cases that are still obligatory despite falling within one's property: pigeons of a dovecote (*yonei shovach*) and pigeons of an attic (*yonei aliyah*) that made their nest in protruding beams or in various structures; ducks and chickens that nested in a garden or orchard. In all these cases, even though the birds are on one's property, they are not sufficiently under the owner's control to exempt from *shiluach*. Rashi explains that the term *tafiach* refers to protrusions — places where birds might naturally nest — though some translate it as jars placed into walls for that purpose.

The principle at work is that *lefanecha* and *baderech* are both pointing to the same underlying condition: the nest must not be *b'yadcha* — in your hand, figuratively speaking. Being in one's yard or attic is not enough to constitute that level of control. A bird nesting on a balcony, for instance, that would fly away if one approached, falls squarely in this category of obligatory *shiluach* — the bird remains functionally free.

### Free vs. Captured

By contrast, the *baraita* exempts birds that nested inside the house itself (*kanenu betoch habayit*), and similarly the so-called Herodian doves (*yonei hordasiyot*), associated with the elaborate underground structures from the Herodian period — intricate networks of cavities where birds were kept in a manner that was fully captured and controlled. Such birds are *patur mishiluach* — exempt from the obligation.

The conceptual distinction, then, is not property versus public domain — it is freedom versus captivity. A bird in your orange grove, in your dovecote's open reaches, in your attic's rafters: still free, still obligatory. A bird kept in an enclosed cage, in a fully enclosed structure under your management: captured, exempt.

### Gemara Rejects Baraita

The Gemara then revisits the *baraita*'s logic. It notes: *ha lama li? Miki yikarei nafka* — why do I need *baderech* at all? We already learn the exclusion of a controlled bird from *ki yikarei* — "if it chances," implying it must not be something already designated and under your control. Furthermore, the Gemara asks: *ve'od lefanecha lama li* — and what then is the role of *lefanecha*, "before you"?

The Gemara answers: *ella lefanecha le'atuyei shehayu lefanav umardu* — rather, *lefanecha* comes to include a case where the bird had previously been under your control but escaped and returned to a wild state. Even though it once was "before you," now that it has reasserted its freedom and you are chancing upon it anew, you remain obligated. *Lefanecha* tells us that even former ownership does not create a permanent exemption once the bird is no longer under your control.

With *ki yikarei* excluding the currently controlled, and *lefanecha* including the formerly owned but now free, the Gemara sets aside the *baraita*'s reading of *baderech* as the source for exclusion, and instead takes *baderech* to serve a different function — which Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav will now explain.

## Baderech Extensions

### Ocean Case

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: if one found a nest floating in the ocean, *chayav beshiluach* — one is obligated to perform *shiluach haken*. The source: the verse states, *ko amar Hashem hanoten bayam derech* — "so says God who places a path in the sea." Since the *Torah* uses the word *derech* in connection with the sea, the *baderech* of the *shiluach haken* verse includes even this unusual location.

### Sky & Vulture

The Gemara pushes further: *ela me'ata matza kan bashamayim* — if so, what if one found a nest in the sky? Rashi explains this refers to a *nesher* — more accurately a vulture — carrying a nest in its talons while in flight. Perhaps one would be obligated there as well, since it says *derech nesher bashamayim* — "the way of the vulture in the heavens"?

The Gemara rejects this: *derech nesher ikri, derech stam lo ikrei* — in the sky, it is called "the way of the vulture," a qualified expression, not simply "a path." Only a plain, unqualified *derech* is included by the *baderech* of the *shiluach* verse. The ocean is called simply a *derech*; the sky is called only *derech nesher*. Hence the ocean qualifies, the sky does not.

One noted halakhic application of this distinction: the Rogatchover Gaon reportedly argued, based on this very *Gemara*, that one need not recite *Tefillat Haderech* upon an airplane flight, since *ba'avir* — through the air — is not categorized as *derech* but only as *derech nesher*.

### Nest on Head

The people of Papuna (*Papunai*) posed a question to Rav Masna: *matza kan berosho shel adam mahu* — what is the ruling if a bird made its nest on a person's head? The question has a whimsical quality to it — alongside the ocean and the sky, they are now asking about a human head — yet the answer is substantive. Rav Masna replied: *va'adama al rosho* — and the ground was upon his head, citing a verse about Shmuel. Rashi explains that the word *adama* — as opposed to *afar* (dust) or *aphar* (earth) — is used specifically for earth that is still connected to the ground. Since the verse applies the term *adama* to what is upon the person's head, it suggests the person himself is treated as being connected to the ground, and thus the nest is considered as resting upon the earth. Within the parameters of *ki yimatze ki yikarei lefanecha baderech*, such a case falls within the obligation.

This naturally raises the question of a nest found on a wagon or a moving vehicle — cases the Gemara does not address, but which the logic of the *Papunai* exchange would seem to leave unresolved.

## Biblical Hints Digr.

### Name Sources

The *Papunai* continued with a series of further questions to Rav Masna. *Moshe min haTorah minayin* — where is Moshe hinted at in the *Torah*? The answer: the verse in *Parshas Noach* reads *beshagam hu basar* — "for he too is flesh." The *gematria* of *beshagam* equals that of *Moshe*, and the continuation — *vehayu yamav me'ah ve'esrim shana* — "his days shall be one hundred and twenty years" — matches the lifespan of Moshe Rabbeinu exactly.

*Haman min haTorah minayin* — where is Haman hinted? From *hamina ha'etz* — "have you eaten from this tree?" — and indeed Haman was hanged from a tree. The allusion is not merely verbal but contextual: as Moshe's hint includes his lifespan, Haman's hint includes his end.

*Esther min haTorah minayin* — where is Esther hinted? From *ve'anochi haster astir* — "and I will surely hide my face from them on that day." Rashi notes that the hidden face of God — *hester panim* — is a central theme of the period of the Megillah: God's name does not appear in the Megillah, and the Jewish people faced a decree of annihilation. The continuation of that verse, *umatze'u tzaros rabbos vera'os* — "and they will find many great evils" — corresponds to the travails of that period.

*Mordechai min haTorah minayin* — where is Mordechai hinted? The verse speaks of *mar dror*, one of the spices of the *shemen hamishcha*, the anointing oil. *Umitargeminan mira dachya* — and we translate this as "pure myrrh," but *mira dachya* in Aramaic resembles *Mordechai*. Rashi notes that this hints at Mordechai's greatness — the association with something precious and aromatic foretells his ultimate honor.

### Maharsha Validation

The Maharsha offers a sophisticated framework for understanding why the Gemara bothers with these derivations. The underlying question, he explains, is whether these names — which are not originally Hebrew — can be said to be the proper, fitting names for these individuals. The *Torah*'s allusions to their names serve to validate them: even though Moshe was given an Egyptian name by Pharaoh's daughter, the *Torah* hints to that very name, showing it to be divinely appropriate. Similarly, the Persian names of Esther and Mordechai are ratified by their appearance in the text of *Torah*.

### Non-Hebrew Names

The Maharsha makes the striking observation that Moshe is indeed an Egyptian name — the suffix of Pharaoh's names (*Thutmose*, *Ramesses*) derives from the same root, meaning "son of." *Ki min hamayim meshisihu* — "for I drew him from the water" — was spoken by Pharaoh's daughter, who was speaking Egyptian, not Hebrew. Esther and Mordechai, the Maharsha further notes, are Persian names. He then cites a remarkable position of the Ibn Ezra: that God's name does not appear in the Megillah because the Megillah was written by Persians. This claim is startling — if Esther herself wrote it, as the verse *vatichtov Esther hamalka* implies, the Ibn Ezra's reasoning remains puzzling. And if the entire text were composed in Persian by non-Jews, why is it written in Hebrew? The Ibn Ezra's position is left as a curiosity without full resolution.

The connection of all this to the surrounding sugya is that these questions were posed by the *Papunai* to Rav Masna — seemingly as a collection of riddles or proofs, perhaps with a playful character, beginning with the nest on a person's head and continuing through a series of Torah allusions.

## Herodian Doves

### Rav Kahana Story

The Gemara returns to the case of *yonei hordasiyot* — the Herodian doves — as a paradigm of *mezuman*, fully controlled birds exempt from *shiluach*. Rav Kahana reported: *l'didi hazin* — I myself saw these birds — and they were arrayed in sixteen rows stretching across the width of a mile. The scale itself suggests something extraordinary. These birds would cry *kiri kiri*, and one among them refused to do so. Another bird turned to it and said, *suma* — "blind one," meaning fool — *imri kiri kiri*, say what you are supposed to say. The recalcitrant bird replied: *suma, imri kiri biri* — "say rather *kiri biri*." Rashi explains: *kiri* means master, and *biri* means slave. The birds had been trained to acknowledge Herod as their master, but this one declared that their master was himself a slave — a reference to the well-known tradition in the Gemara that Herod descended from slaves. So deeply embedded was this memory that even these birds, generations removed, carried it. *Asyuve veshachtua* — so they took the rebellious bird and slaughtered it, presumably Herod's household slaves who were still around.

### Rav Ashi Response

Rav Ashi reported in the name of Chanina that the story was *milat* — nonsense, or fabrication. The Gemara pushes back: would Rav Kahana simply make something up? *Ela eima bemilin* — rather, it was done through words, meaning through magic or incantation. Some interpret this to mean the birds were not speaking in human language, but in bird-speech, and Rav Kahana possessed knowledge of bird-speech. Either way, the Gemara openly acknowledges the fantastical character of the account.

## Herodian Doves…

### Mezuman Status

The story of the Herodian doves serves its halakhic purpose: they are the example of fully *mezuman* — captured and controlled — birds that are exempt from *shiluach haken*. The entire elaborate episode is the Gemara's illustration of what it means for birds to be so thoroughly under human dominion that the *mitzvah* does not apply.

## Of Tamei Debate

### Tzippor = Kosher

The Gemara turns to the explicit ruling: *of tamei patur belishloach* — a non-kosher bird, one need not send away. *Minani mili* — from where is this derived? Rabbi Yitzchak answers: from the verse *ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha*. The word *of* — bird in general — encompasses both kosher and non-kosher species, as evidenced by the verse *kol of tahor tocheilu* — "eat every kosher bird" — which implies that there is also an *of tamei*. But the verse of *shiluach haken* does not say *of*; it says *tzippor*. And *tzippor tahor ashkechan d'ikrei, tzippor tamei lo ashkechan d'ikrei* — we find kosher birds referred to as *tzippor*, but we never find a non-kosher bird described as a *tzippor*.

### Challenges to Rule

The Gemara systematically challenges this claim with a series of verses. First: *tavnit kol tzippor kanaf* — "the image of any *tzippor* of wing," the prohibition against making graven images. Surely this must include non-kosher birds. Second: *hachaya vechol habeheima, remes vetzippor kanaf* — the verse enumerating all creatures that praise God, which must include everything. Third: *kol tzippor kol kanaf* — all birds, all winged creatures entering Noach's ark, which was all-inclusive. Fourth: *ve'ata ben adam, emor letzippor kol kanaf* — "and you, son of man, say to the bird, to all of wing."

### Defense Sustained

In each case, the Gemara provides the same structural answer: *tzippor* refers to kosher birds, while *kanaf* — the additional modifier — extends the verse to include non-kosher birds and other winged creatures such as locusts (*chagavim*). The pairing is consistent: *tzippor* alone denotes the kosher species; *kanaf* or *tzippor kanaf* is the broader inclusive term.

One further challenge: *tzipparei shamaya* — birds of the heaven in the vision of Nevuchadnezzar, all of which dwelled in the great tree — surely this includes non-kosher birds. The Gemara responds: *tzipparei shamaya ikri, tzipparei stama lo ikrei* — "birds of the heaven" is a qualified expansive phrase; the plain word *tzippor* without qualification remains limited to kosher species. The pattern established here mirrors the earlier ruling about *derech*: a plain term has a narrower scope; a qualified compound expression has a broader one.

## Tehora Drasha

### Kosher Only?

The Gemara now brings a verse that seems more directly challenging: *kol tzippor tehora tocheilu* — "eat every pure bird." If *tzippor* already means only kosher birds, why does the verse need to add the word *tehora* — pure, or kosher? The very presence of that word suggests that *tzippor* on its own is not limited to kosher species, and *tehora* is what narrows it. This would imply there are non-kosher *tzipporim* — which contradicts Rabbi Yitzchak's entire position.

### Tehora Excludes

The Gemara's answer is elegant: *lo miklal de'ika issura* — the inference is not that *tzippor* includes non-kosher species, but that *tehora* excludes certain kosher-species birds that are nonetheless forbidden. What would that be? A *treifah* bird is one possibility, but that is explicitly prohibited elsewhere. More significantly: *bishchuta de'metzora* — the bird slaughtered as part of the *metzora* purification process. That bird is a kosher species, properly slaughtered, yet forbidden in consumption. The word *tehora* — eat the *tehora* — comes to exclude this specific case.

### Asei & Lo Ta'asei

The Gemara then asks: if *tehora* comes to exclude the *shechuta de'metzora*, then what does the other verse — *ve'zeh asher lo tochelu meihem* — come to add? The answer: the presence of both a positive commandment (*kol tzippor tehora tocheilu* — eat the kosher ones) and a negative commandment (*ve'zeh asher lo tochelu meihem* — do not eat these) creates a redundancy that is read as applying specifically to the *shechuta de'metzora*. Eating such a bird would violate both a *lo ta'asei* and an *asei*, making the prohibition doubly reinforced. This structure highlights the special status of the *metzora* bird among otherwise eligible kosher species.

## Metzora Birds

### Chayot Meaning

The Gemara now turns to the specific language of the *metzora* purification: *shtei tzipporim chayot* — "two living birds." The question is: *mai chayot* — what does the word *chayot* ("living" or "alive") come to add? The initial suggestion: *she'chayot be'ficha* — that they are alive in your mouth, meaning edible, which would imply *tzippor* on its own could include non-kosher species. This reading would mean *chayot* is what restricts the birds to those that can be consumed.

### Chayot vs Tehorot

The Gemara rejects that reading. *Ma chayot? She'chayot rashei evarim shelahem* — "alive" means that all their limbs are intact, alive and whole. For birds brought as *korbanot*, while there is no concept of *mum* (blemish) as with animals, there is the category of *mechusar eiver* — lacking a limb. Thus *chayot* establishes that the birds must be complete, not that they must be kosher species. That requirement — kosher species — comes from the separate word *tehorot* (pure), which appears in the full verse: *shtei tzipporim chayot tehorot*. The inference from *tehorot* would be that *tzippor* on its own could include non-kosher species; but as before, the Gemara rejects this inference by explaining that *tehorot* is not excluding non-kosher species but something else entirely.

### Machshir Mechaper

The Gemara invokes the principle taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ne'emar machshir umechaper bifnim, v'ne'emar machshir umechaper bachutz* — just as inside the *Beis Hamikdash* the requirements for a *korban* that *machshir* (purifies) and one that *mechaper* (atones) are identical, so too outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the things used for *machshir* and *mechaper* must meet the same standards. Inside, whether a *korban* is of a *zav* seeking purification or a *chatas* seeking atonement, all the same rules apply — no blemish, proper species, intact. Outside, the paradigmatic *machshir* is the *metzora* purification birds, and the paradigmatic *mechaper* is the *se'ir la'azazel* — the scapegoat. Since the *se'ir la'azazel* must be free of any disqualifying defect (for it could be designated for either purpose on *Yom Kippur* and thus must qualify for the *Beis Hamikdash*), the same standard applies to the *metzora* birds. They cannot be a *treifah*, and they must have all their limbs. This *limud* renders both *chayot* and *tehorot* available to exclude other things.

## Metzora Exclusions

### Avoda Zara Proceeds

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes: *l'ma'utei tzipporei ir hanidachat* — *tehorot* or *chayot* comes to exclude birds of an *ir hanidachat*, the subverted city condemned to destruction. This is a striking suggestion, for it implies that absent this verse one might have thought such birds could be used for a *korban* — which runs against intuition. Tosafot observes that the Gemara indeed seems to imply that birds of an *ir hanidachat* could otherwise have been valid for a *korban*, since the prohibition is against deriving benefit from them, and *mitzvot lav lehenot nitnu* — the performance of *mitzvos* is not considered personal benefit. The Gemara clarifies: such birds cannot be used for the *shiluach* bird (*lav deshiluach*) — this we would know independently, since the *Torah* would never command one to send away a bird that would then be found and impermissibly benefited from by another (*lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah*). Rather, it is the *shechitah* bird — the one slaughtered in the ritual — that *tehorot* comes to exclude.

Rav Pappa adds: *letzipporim shehichlifu betzipporei avoda zara* — birds obtained through exchange with idolatry, or purchased with proceeds of idol-selling. The verse states *vehayita charem kamohu* — it shall be banned like it; anything obtained through the proceeds of *avoda zara* is itself forbidden in benefit. One might have supposed that since using such a bird for *metzora* purification does not constitute personal *hana'ah*, it could still be used. The verse teaches otherwise. Again, the Gemara specifies it is the *shechitah* bird, not the *shiluach* bird, from which this is excluded — since sending away a forbidden-benefit bird is self-evidently impossible.

### Shor Haniskal

Ravina adds the case of a bird that has killed a person — *of sheharag es hanefesh*. Such a bird has the status of *shor haniskal* and is forbidden in benefit. One might have thought it could still be used for *metzora* purification prior to the formal judicial verdict (*kodem gmar din*), when it has not yet been formally condemned and killed. The Gemara asks: if the *gmar din* has already occurred, obviously it cannot be used. And before *gmar din*, the case for exclusion is this: one might have supposed that since there is a *mitzvah* to eliminate the dangerous animal (*uvi'arta hara mikirbecha*), shechting it for the *metzora* would serve that purpose at the same time. The verse *tehorot* comes to exclude this as well — only the *shechitah* bird, not the *shiluach* bird, since one would be obligated to bring the bird to court rather than send it away.

### Summary Tzippor

The cumulative picture is clear: *tzippor* — whether in the verse of *shiluach haken* or in the context of *metzora* birds — refers exclusively to kosher species. The word *tehorot* in the *metzora* passage does not come to reiterate what *tzippor* already establishes; it excludes specific, contextually relevant categories of disqualified birds: birds of an *ir hanidachat*, birds obtained with proceeds of *avoda zara*, and birds that have killed a person. In each case, the Gemara works out which of the two *metzora* birds — the one to be slaughtered or the one to be sent away — is the target of the exclusion, with the rule *lo amrah Torah shalach letakalah* serving to rule out the *shiluach* bird in most cases.

## Eggs vs Mother

### Species Mismatch

With the principle that *tzippor* means only a kosher species now firmly established, the Gemara addresses cross-species scenarios. *Of tamei rovetz al betzei of tahor, v'of tahor rovetz al betzei of tamei* — a non-kosher bird brooding over kosher eggs, or a kosher bird brooding over non-kosher eggs: both cases are exempt. The first case is straightforward — *be'inan tzippor v'leika* — the verse requires a *tzippor* (the mother), and a non-kosher mother is not a *tzippor*. But the second case requires more analysis: the mother is indeed a *tzippor*, a kosher bird. Why then is it exempt?

### Treifa Rules

This is where Rav Kahana's teaching is applied. The verse states *tikach lach* — "take for yourself" — which implies *lecha v'lo l'kalbecha*: take something that is directly usable by you, not merely fit for dog food. Non-kosher eggs, even under a kosher mother, cannot be directly consumed by a Jew. Therefore, the *tikach lach* condition is not met, and one is exempt. The *baraita* records the following: if the mother is a *treifah*, one is still obligated in *shiluach*; if the chicks or eggs are *treifos*, one is exempt. Each is governed by a separate principle. The mother's *treifah* status does not affect the obligation, since the mother is being sent away, not consumed. The chicks' *treifah* status does affect the obligation, since *tikach lach* requires that the chicks be fit for direct human benefit.

### Tikach Lach

This ruling contains a subtlety worth noting: when the eggs are *treifah* and there is no *mitzvah* of *shiluach*, one is permitted to simply take everything — eggs and mother alike. There is no obligation to send the mother away first. The exemption, while grounded in the logic of *tikach lach*, produces a result that sits in some tension with the broader values often associated with *shiluach haken* — the compassion for the mother bird. Yet the *Torah*'s framework, as the Gemara presents it, is technical: when the chicks do not qualify, the whole edifice of obligation collapses.

## Half-Slaughter Case

### Rav Hoshaia Query

Rav Hoshaia raises a probing edge case. *Hoshit yado l'kan v'shachat miut simanim mahu* — what if one reached into the nest and partially slaughtered one of the chicks, cutting a minority of the *simanim* (trachea and esophagus required for kosher *shechitah*)? At this moment, if one were to stop, the chick would be a *treifah*. If one were to continue, the *shechitah* would be completed and the chick would be fully kosher. The question is: at this intermediate moment, is the obligation of *shiluach* suspended? If so, one could grab the mother, complete the *shechitah*, and avoid the *mitzvah* altogether.

### Two Positions

Two positions are articulated. The first: *keivan d'ilu shavik lehu mitrefi* — since at this moment, if you were to leave it, it would be a *treifah*, and *tikach lach* requires edibility, perhaps the obligation is suspended right now, allowing one to take the mother. The second position: *keivan d'veyadcha l'migmar shechita* — since it remains entirely within your power to complete the *shechitah* and render the chick fully kosher, it is still in the category of *tikach lach* and one remains obligated in *shiluach*.

### Teiku

The Gemara leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved. Intuitively, the overwhelming sense is that one should not be able to engineer an exemption through this kind of manipulation — the ability to complete the *shechitah* at will seems to keep the obligation in force — but the Gemara declines to rule definitively.

## Nest Scenarios

### Yirmiya Questions

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a series of questions probing the boundaries of the requirement that the mother be *rovetzes* — hovering over or crouching upon — the eggs. Does there need to be direct contact, or can intervening objects still satisfy the condition? He asks: *matlis* — what if a cloth was placed between the mother and the eggs? *Mahu shetachotz* — does such an interposition constitute a *chatzitza*, a division that breaks the relationship between mother and nest? Similarly, what about detached bird feathers (*knafayim*) lying on top of the eggs — does that divide mother from nest?

### Stacking Cases

He further asks about *betzim muzarot* — unfertilized eggs. A separate principle will establish that only eggs capable of producing chicks (*betzim dumya d'efrochim*) qualify; if unfertilized eggs are mixed with fertilized ones, what is the ruling? And if two rows of eggs are stacked one atop the other, with the mother brooding over the upper row — is the upper row considered a division between mother and lower row? What if the father bird is directly on top of the eggs and the mother is on top of the father — is the mother considered to be *rovetzes* on the eggs? Or what if the reverse configuration applies?

### All Teiku

Every one of Rabbi Yirmiya's questions is left as *teiku* — unresolved. The Gemara does not venture an answer to any of them.

## Korei Exception

### Zeira Query

Rabbi Zeira asks: *yona al betzei tasiya mahu* — a dove brooding over the eggs of a *tasiya* (a bird of similar species), or the reverse — *tasiya al betzei yona mahu*? The scenario is one where a kosher mother bird is brooding over the eggs of a different kosher species — not her own offspring. Does the *mitzvah* of *shiluach* apply?

### Baraita Inference

Abaye brings a *baraita* in response: *of tamei rovetz al betzei of tahor v'of tahor rovetz al betzei of tamei patur* — the mixed-species cases (kosher on non-kosher, non-kosher on kosher) are exempt. The logical implication of this *baraita* is significant: *ha tahor v'tahor chayav* — a kosher mother on kosher eggs, even if they are not her own, would be obligatory. The inference from the *baraita* strongly supports the position that as long as both mother and eggs are kosher species, the obligation exists regardless of biological parentage.

### Korei Exception

The Gemara qualifies this inference: *dilma b'korei* — perhaps the *baraita*'s implied ruling of obligation for "kosher on kosher" refers specifically to the *korei*, the partridge, which as we shall see is explicitly described in a biblical verse as brooding over eggs it did not lay. The *korei* might be a special exception — a case where the *Torah* itself acknowledges and addresses this phenomenon. For other species, perhaps even kosher-on-kosher would require biological parentage. Nevertheless, the plain reading of the *baraita*'s implication, and the ruling of the Rambam, is that kosher mother on kosher eggs is obligatory even without biological parentage.

## Mishnah Korei

### Mishnah Text

The Mishnah states: *korei zachar* — the male partridge — *Rabbi Eliezer mechayev v'chachamim potrin*. Rabbi Eliezer obligates the *mitzvah* of *shiluach*; the Sages exempt. The case combines two unusual features: the bird is male, and it is brooding over eggs it did not produce.

### Gara Drasha

Rabbi Abahu explains Rabbi Eliezer's position through a *gezeirah shavah* — a hermeneutical equivalence based on the shared verb *dagra* / *gara*, meaning "to brood." One verse in Yirmiyahu reads: *korei dagar v'lo yalad* — "the partridge broods over what it did not give birth to," describing the *korei* sitting on another's eggs. A verse in the prophets elsewhere uses the same root — *u'vaka'ah v'dagrah b'tzilah* — for a bird brooding over its own eggs. Since the identical verb applies in both cases, Rabbi Eliezer derives that the *korei* brooding over another's eggs is treated equivalently to any bird brooding over its own. Therefore, even this male partridge on non-own eggs is subject to *shiluach haken*.

### Male & Korei

The Gemara asks: why does the Mishnah specify both *zachar* (male) and *korei* (partridge)? Is it not obvious that the Mishnah is addressing a male? The Gemara explains: the dual specification is needed to convey the full scope of the dispute. One might have thought the Sages exempt even a female *korei* — that the unusual brooding behavior of the *korei* as a species categorically removes it from the *mitzvah*. The Mishnah's mention of *zachar* teaches that only the male is disputed; a female *korei*, all agree, is obligated. Conversely, one might have thought Rabbi Eliezer obligates any male bird. The specification of *korei* teaches that only the male partridge — because of the explicit biblical verse — is within Rabbi Eliezer's ruling; all other male birds are exempt by universal consensus.

### Final Dispute Scope

Rabbi Eliezer himself states: *machlokes b'korei zachar* — the dispute applies only to the male partridge. *Aval b'zachar d'alma, divrei hakol patur* — for any other male bird, all agree it is exempt. The Gemara anticipates the objection that this is obvious, since the Mishnah explicitly says *korei zachar*, and confirms: the specification is needed to prevent the inference that Rabbi Eliezer might obligate even an ordinary male. The *baraita* confirms the conclusion: an ordinary male (*zachar d'alma*) — exempt by all; a male partridge — Rabbi Eliezer obligates, the Sages exempt; and what remains on the margins of the discussion, though not explicitly disputed, is whether a female of any species brooding over another's eggs would be obligated — with the stronger implication of the *sugyah* being that it would.
