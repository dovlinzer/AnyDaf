# Avodah Zarah 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine Press Drying

### Two/Three Stages

The *gemara* opens with a dispute regarding the precise number of stages required when drying out and cleansing a wine press purchased from non-Jews. The preceding context established that water combined with ashes functions as a kind of soap or scouring agent, and the discussion now turns to how many applications are needed.

According to *Bei Rav mishmei deRav* — in the academy of Rav, in the name of Rav — the answer is two or three stages, depending on the initial condition of the press. Following Rashi's first reading: if the press is still moist and retains some surface wine, two stages suffice — ashes first (which combine with the moisture) and then water to scour and complete the process. If the press is dry, it ironically requires three stages, since even without surface wine one apparently always needs liquid to initiate the process: water, then ashes, then water again. The ashes must always be followed by a rinse of water.

### Three/Four Stages

*VeShmuel amar tlas arba* — Shmuel disagrees and holds that three or four stages are required. According to Rashi, Shmuel adds a full additional round of ashes and water. For a moist press, one performs ashes, water, ashes — two applications of ashes in total — with a final water rinse implicit but not enumerated. For a dry press, the sequence is water, ashes, water, ashes, again with a final water rinse that is not counted.

### Sura vs Pumbedisa

A textual tradition preserved in the two major academies diverges here. *Besura masnu hachi* — in Sura, the tradition was taught as just described. But *BePumbedisa masnu*, in Pumbedita they taught that Rav's school itself held three or four — the same count Shmuel had just given — while Shmuel's position becomes four or five.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent conflict between Rav and Shmuel in the Pumbedita recension: *v'lo pligi* — they do not actually dispute. The difference is only whether one counts the final water rinse. Rav (three or four) does not count it; Shmuel (four or five) does. Both require two stages of ashes with appropriate water applications, and both agree on the substance of the process. As the *gemara* puts it: *mar kachashiv maya basraye umar lo kachashiv maya basraye* — one master counts the final water, the other does not.

## Press Components

### Planks & Baskets

The *gemara* turns from the wine press itself to other components of the wine and olive press. Rabbi Abahu is asked about *gorgi de-aramai* — the wicker frames used by non-Jews to hold grapes together during pressing — and what must be done to render them fit for use.

Rabbi Abos responds by citing a *baraita* from the laws of *taharos*: the scenario is one where a person purchased a wine press or olive press from an *am ha'aretz* who handled everything as *tamei*, and now wishes to produce *tahor* wine or oil. The *baraita* addresses several categories. *Hadapin* — the planks used for pressing olives — as well as what Rashi identifies as the *gat* itself, the vessel that collects the wine, and the palm-branch brooms used to sweep the grapes back into place: for all of these, *midichan* — rinsing is sufficient. This is notable, because the *mishna* had required drying for the press, but that was a plastered press; this *baraita* concerns an unplastered one, and each case differs in its particulars.

### Vat vs Basket

The *baraita* then distinguishes the *akalim* — the pressing basket — from the vat. Rashi indicates that the *gorgi* asked about earlier and the *akalim* here are the same thing: the wicker basket into which grapes are loaded, upon which the pressing beam bears down directly. Because this is the site of actual pressing rather than mere collection, it is subject to far greater pressure and absorption than the vat, and accordingly requires more intensive treatment.

### Basket Materials

The required treatment depends on the material of the basket. *Ha'akalim shel netsarim* — if the pressing basket is made of palm leaves — *veshel bitzbutz* — or of hemp — *menagvan*, one must dry them out, using the process with ashes described earlier. But *veshel shifa veshel gemi* — if made of bullrush or reeds — *meyashnan yod beis chodesh*, one must leave them for twelve months. The reason is that these materials absorb deeply, and no amount of surface drying will suffice; only extended aging allows the absorbed moisture to dissipate. This is an important point of reference for the broader concept of twelve-month aging as a means of *kashering*, though there is a significant qualification: here everything was absorbed in cold, and whether twelve months can similarly neutralize absorption that occurred through heat is a separate and much-discussed question.

### Season vs Months

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer*: rather than twelve months, one may wait from one pressing season to the next — *migat legat umibad lebad* — from one wine pressing to the next, or from one olive pressing to the next.

The *gemara* initially objects: if pressing is annual, is that not the same as twelve months? The *gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu chorfi v'afli* — it matters because of early and late seasons. One year's pressing season may begin earlier or later than the previous year's, and depending on the season, waiting until the next pressing may be more or less than twelve months. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the first *tanna* thus differ in cases of seasonal variation.

### Boiling/Hot Oil

*Rabbi Yossi omer*: for one who does not wish to wait twelve months — *harotzeh letaheran miyad* — there is an immediate alternative. *Magilan berotchin* — one may immerse the baskets in boiling hot water. This is of course the standard principle of *kashering* vessels that absorbed through heat. Alternatively, *o choltan bimei zeitim* — one may scald them in *mei zeitim*, the water used for softening olives in preparation for pressing, since olives must be cooked to soften them before pressing. That water, apparently, accomplishes the same kashering effect immediately.

### Running Water 12hrs

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers yet another option: *menichan tachat tzinor shemeimav mekalchim* — placing them under a pipe or conduit through which water flows continuously — *o bemaayan shemeimav rodfim* — or in a wellspring whose waters run. The constant flow of water draws out the absorbed material. *Vekama* — how long must they remain there? *Ona* — an *ona*, which will be defined as twelve hours.

The *baraita* concludes: *kederech sheamru beyayin nesech kach amru betaharos*. The *gemara* is puzzled by this formulation — we are speaking in the context of *taharos*, not *yayin nesech* — and resolves it by reversing: what the *baraita* means is that just as these rules were stated for *taharos*, the same rules apply to *yayin nesech*. The *baraita* that addresses *taharos* explicitly teaches us the method applicable to wine as well, and this directly answers the original question about how to kasher the *gorgi* pressing baskets.

### Ona Definition

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: an *ona* is *o yom o layla* — either a day or a night. Rabbi Chana of Sha'an (or Rabbi Chana bar Sheina) cites in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *chatzi yom vechatzi layla* — half a day and half the following night.

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak velo pligi* — they do not dispute. The difference is entirely a function of the season. In *tekufat Nissan* and *tekufat Tishrei* — the vernal and autumnal equinoxes — days and nights are each exactly twelve hours, so one full day or one full night suffices. But in *tekufat Tammuz* or *tekufat Teves* — midsummer or midwinter — days and nights are unequal, and to reliably accumulate twelve hours one must take half the day and half the night together. The practical bottom line is uniform: one must leave the object under running water for twelve hours.

## Sieves/Strainers

### Hair vs Wool

*Amar Rav Yehuda*: regarding *rivki de-Aramai* — sieves or straining bags of non-Jews, which Rashi describes as sacks used to squeeze remaining wine from the grape skins and lees — the required treatment again depends on material. *Demazya* — if made of hair — *medichan*, rinsing is sufficient, since hair does not genuinely absorb. *De'amra* — if made of wool, which absorbs more readily — *menagvan*, one must dry them out using the more vigorous process with ashes.

### Flax & Knots

*Dechitna* — if made of flax, which absorbs most deeply — *meyashnan*, one must let them age, presumably the twelve months discussed earlier. *Veika kitrei* — and if there are knots — *sharei lehu*, one must undo them, because knotted material will retain absorbed substance in the buried folds, and unless the material is fully exposed, no method of rinsing or drying can reach it.

### Woven Baskets

The *gemara* continues at the top of 75b with a parallel treatment of baskets belonging to non-Jews. *De-chiti be-chavlei de-tzari* — if woven from strips of palm — *madichan*, rinsing suffices, as such material does not genuinely absorb. *De-tzaftza* — if made of a type of reed identified by Rashi — *lenagvan*, it must be dried out, since reeds absorb more. *De-kitna* — if made of flax — *meyashnan*, it must be aged, presumably twelve months again. And as with the sieves: *ve-ika behu kitrei sharei lehu* — if there are knots, they must be untied first.

## Tumah/Contact

### Cluster Tamei

The *gemara* next takes up a question involving an *am ha'aretz* — who by definition handles things as *tamei* — who extends his hand into a wine press and touches one of the grape clusters lying atop the wine. This is a case of *taharos*, and there is a dispute between Rebbi and Rabbi Chiya.

One holds: *eshkol v'chol sevivotav tmein, v'chol ha-gat kula tehora* — the cluster touched and everything in its immediate surrounding area is *tamei*, but the rest of the wine press remains *tahor*. The other holds: *kol ha-gat kula nami tmeia* — no, because everything in the vat is in contact with everything else, and touching the cluster renders the entire contents *tamei*.

### Rodent Analogy

The *gemara* challenges the position that only the cluster and its surroundings are *tamei*. It cites an established ruling: *sheretz she-nimtza be-reychaim* — a dead rodent, which transmits *tumah*, found in an olive press — *eino metamei ela mekom maga'o* — renders *tamei* only the items it directly contacts. However, *ve-im haya mashke mehalech ha-kol tamei* — if there was a layer of liquid olive oil flowing and connecting everything, then everything becomes *tamei*, because the liquid unifies all the contents into a single connected mass. Why, then, when an *am ha'aretz* touches a cluster in the wine vat — where liquid wine is present — does the first opinion hold that the rest of the vat remains *tahor*?

### Liquid Flow

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty. *Hatam lo mafsik ve-lo midi* — in the case of the olive press with the flowing oil, nothing interrupts the liquid; the oil forms a single continuous layer connecting all parts. *Hacha mafsei eshkolot* — but here, the clusters themselves interrupt the flow of wine. The wine is not one unified continuous body; the clusters are distributed throughout the press and effectively partition the liquid so that it cannot serve as a single transmitting medium. The wine trapped immediately around a cluster is thus physically separated from the wine elsewhere in the press, which is why only the cluster and its immediate surroundings become *tamei* while the rest remains *tahor*.

The precise physical picture is admittedly not entirely clear, but the principle appears to be that when the press is full of clusters with wine between them rather than a pure undivided liquid mass, the clusters act as barriers to the connected flow that would otherwise transmit *tumah* throughout. The *gemara* concludes by teaching that the ruling should follow the position that only the cluster and its surroundings are *tamei* while the rest of the wine press remains *tahor*.

The discussion also reinforces a broader pattern visible throughout this section of the *masechta*: the language and conceptual framework of *tumah ve-tahara* is repeatedly invoked in the context of *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam*. This is not coincidental — the *gemara* has explicitly noted that the rules governing these different domains apply in parallel, since the underlying physical realities of wine presses and olive presses are the same.

## Vessel Kashering

### Mishna Methods

The *gemara* now moves away from wine presses entirely and addresses the classic laws of *kashering keilim* — a welcome transition to more familiar *kashrus* territory.

The *mishna* states: *halokeach k'lei tashmish min ha'ovdei kochavim* — one who acquires cooking utensils from a non-Jew, presumably ones that were used with *treif*, must kasher them. *Es shedarko l'hatbil yatbil* — whatever is normally immersed in a *mikvah*, one immerses. *Lehag'il yag'il* — whatever requires *hagalah* with boiling water, one boils. *Lelaben ba'or lelaben ba'or* — whatever requires *libun*, whitening in fire, one places in fire. The *mishna* then gives two examples: the spit and the grate (*hashapud v'ha'askala*) must be made white-hot in fire. A knife, which may not have absorbed anything through heat but has grease and residue embedded in its surface, requires *shefah* — scrubbing with an abrasive — and then it is *tahor*.

The *gemara* explains that the *mishna*'s opening clause — "whatever is normally immersed" — actually refers to something used only in cold that has absorbed nothing through heat, and therefore requires no *kashering* at all. In that case, the only obligation is *tevilat keilim* — immersion in a *mikvah*. This buries within the *mishna* what the *gemara* will develop at length: the requirement to immerse vessels acquired from non-Jews is an independent obligation distinct from *kashering*, and applies even to brand new vessels never used with *treif*.

## Tevilat Keilim

### Forty Seahs

A *baraita* is cited: *v'chulan tzrichin tevilah b'arbaim seah* — all vessels acquired from non-Jews require immersion in a *mikvah* of forty *seah*.

### Klei Midyan

The *gemara* asks *mana hanei mili* — what is the source for this? Rava cites the passage in *Parashat Matot* regarding the vessels taken from Midian: *ach es hazahav v'es hakesef es hanechoshes es habarzel es habdil v'es ha'oferes kol davar asher yavo va'eish ta'aviru va'eish v'taher ach b'mei nidah yitchata v'chol asher lo yavo va'eish ta'aviru vamayim* — anything that comes into fire, pass through fire and it will be purified, but with *mei niddah* it shall be cleansed; and anything that does not come into fire, pass through water.

In its plain meaning, this passage addresses *tumah ve-tahara*: the Israelites had just been in battle with the dead all around them, and the verse instructs them to purify their belongings. The phrase *b'mei nidah yitchata* — sprinkle with the *mei nidah* — would naturally refer to the ash-water of the *parah adumah*, used for purification from corpse-*tumah*. And the straightforward sense of *kol asher yavo va'eish ta'aviru va'eish* — pass through fire — would be part of that same *tumah* process.

But this creates a difficulty: where do we ever find fire as part of a *taharah* procedure from *tumah*? Because of this, *chazal* read the verse differently. The command to pass through fire is not about *taharah* from *tumah* at all — it is about *kashering*: anything normally used with fire must be kashered with fire, and anything used with water (such as a pot boiled in) must be kashered through boiling water. This is in fact the *Torah* basis for the principle of *k'volo kach polto* — that one reverses the method of absorption to extract it. So the fire and water in this verse address *kashering* from *treifus*, not purification from *tumah*.

### Mei Nidah

This leaves the middle clause — *ach b'mei nidah yitchata* — to be explained. Bar Kappara teaches in a *baraita*: one might have read *b'mei nidah* as requiring the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ash-water on the third and seventh days, which is indeed the standard *tumah* purification procedure. *Talmud lomar ach* — the word *ach* ("however") comes to exclude: this is not actually referring to the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes and does not relate to *tumah* purification at all. *Im kein mah talmud lomar b'mei nidah?* — so what then does the phrase mean? The *gemara* offers a distinctly non-*pshat* reading: *mayim shenidah tovelet bahen* — the waters in which a *niddah* immerses herself. *B'mei nidah yitchata* thus means: purify in the waters that a *niddah* would use for purification. *Hevei omer arbaim se'ah* — that is a full *mikvah* of forty *seah*.

It is worth noting the remarkable interpretive move here. The *Torah* nowhere explicitly states that a *niddah* immerses in a *mikvah* — yet that practice is taken as axiomatic and read back into the verse to establish *tevilat keilim*. Some *Rishonim* hold that because of this highly non-*pshat* derivation, *tevilat keilim* is only *d'rabbanan* with the verse serving as *asmachta*. The *gemara* itself, however, treats it as *d'Oraita*.

### Two Requirements

The *gemara* now demonstrates why both *drashos* were necessary. *Ach es hazahav...ta'aviru va'eish v'taher* — the word *v'taher* ("and it shall be pure") is read as requiring an additional act of purification beyond the *kashering*: one must also immerse in a *mikvah*. And *b'mei nidah yitchata* teaches that this immersion must be in a full forty-*seah mikvah*.

Had the verse written only *v'taher*, one might have thought any body of water would suffice — even less than forty *seah*, since in the laws of *tumah ve-tahara* a vessel can actually be immersed in as little as a *revi'it* of water. As Tosafot notes, when it comes to *tevilat keilim* in the sense of purifying a vessel from *tumah* — for instance, a sewing needle that became *tamei* — a *mikvah* of only a *revi'it* suffices according to *d'Oraita* law. But *tevilat klei Midyan* — immersing vessels acquired from non-Jews — is a different matter entirely, representing not a removal of *tumah* but a transformation of identity, a passage from non-Jewish to Jewish. This more fundamental change requires a full human-grade *mikvah* of forty *seah*. Hence the verse needed to specify *b'mei nidah*.

Conversely, had the verse written only *b'mei nidah* without *v'taher*, one might have thought it was insufficient simply to immerse — like a *niddah* herself, for whom immersion alone is not enough to permit contact with *terumah* until sunset. The *mitzva* of waiting for sunset (*he'erev shemesh*) would apply. *Kasav Rachmana v'taher* — the Torah writes *v'taher* to teach that the vessel becomes *tahor l'altar*, immediately upon immersion, without any further waiting.

### Brand New

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha*: the obligation of *tevilah* applies even to brand new vessels — *afilu keilim chadashim b'mashma*. The proof: when the Israelites took the *klei Midyan* from battle and had to kasher them, a vessel that was put back in fire until it was white-hot was as if it had been remade — *k'chadashim damu*, like new. Yet even so, *bei tveila* — it still required *tevilah*. Therefore *tevilah* cannot be grounded solely in the absorption of *treifus*; it applies to the vessel's identity as something owned by non-Jews and now entering Jewish use.

One might still argue that the case of *libun* is not fully parallel to a brand new vessel: perhaps the requirement of *tevilah* exists precisely when a vessel carries the identity of having been used for *treif*, and *libun* creates a conceptual link between kashering and *tevilah* as two parts of a single transformative process. A truly brand new vessel, which never carried *treif* and never acquired that identity, might not need *tevilah* at all. Indeed, some *Rishonim* hold that *tevilah* should precede *kashering* only when the vessel is no longer *treif*, reasoning that immersing a *treif* vessel would be like a convert entering the *mikvah* before *bris milah* — the transformation cannot take place while the old status persists. That is not the *halacha*, however. The ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabba bar Avuha makes clear that the two requirements — *kashering* and *tevilah* — are entirely independent, and *tevilah* applies even to vessels that are brand new and require no *kashering* whatsoever.

### Cookware Only

*Maskif lah Rav Sheshes*: if *tevilah* is required even for brand new vessels with no *treif* absorption, does that mean one must immerse scissors or other metal implements purchased from a non-Jew? *Amar lei klei seudah amra parasha* — the Torah is specifically speaking about cookware and vessels used for eating and food preparation. This is a *gzeiras hakasuv*. The connection to *treifus* is not through actual use but through category: cookware is associated with the domain of non-Jewish food, and it is that categorical association — not actual absorption — that generates the obligation of *tevilah*. A vessel entirely unrelated to the world of *treif* food is outside the scope of the verse entirely.

This principle is reflected in the practical *halacha* as well: a can opener does not require *tevilah* because it comes into contact only with the can, not the food itself. The operative criterion is whether the vessel is the type that directly contacts food — and therefore belongs to the category that can become *treif* — even if in this instance it never did.

### Purchased

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha lo shanu ela b'lekuchin k'maaseh she'haya* — this entire discussion applies only to purchased vessels, like the original episode of *klei Midyan* in which the Israelites acquired the vessels through battle and became their owners. *Aval sheulin lo* — but for borrowed vessels, no *tevilah* is required. A borrowed vessel does not change its identity — it remains a non-Jewish vessel temporarily in Jewish hands — and therefore requires neither *tevilah* nor the transformation that *tevilah* represents.

## Materials

### Metal Only

Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef purchased a vessel made from dried animal dung from a non-Jew and considered whether *tevilah* was required. One of the rabbis, named Yaakov, clarified in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *klei matachot amorin baparashah* — the Torah passage about *klei Midyan* speaks specifically of metal vessels, enumerating gold, silver, copper, iron, tin, and lead. The *tevilah* obligation applies to metal, and metal alone, by *d'Oraita* derivation. This produces a result that is admittedly difficult to explain conceptually: pottery vessels, for instance, can absorb *treif* fully and cannot be kashered by any means once absorbed, yet pottery does not require *tevilah*. Wood similarly does not require *tevilah*. The obligation remains tethered to the specific materials enumerated in the verse, and is in that sense a *gzeiras hakasuv*.

### Glass

*Amar Rav Ashi*: glass vessels are treated like metal and require *tevilah*, because *ho'il vechi nishtabru yesh lahen takanah* — when broken, glass can be repaired by melting and reforming, just as metal can be reshaped through heat. This functional analogy to metal justifies treating glass in the same halachic category, and accordingly glass requires *tevilah* as well — though by rabbinic extension rather than the direct *d'Oraita* derivation applying to metal.

### Glazed Pottery

What about *kunya* — glazed pottery, which Rashi describes as pottery coated with a lead glaze? *Pligi bah Rav Acha uRavina* — Rav Acha and Ravina dispute this. *Chad amar k'techilato* — one holds that glazed pottery is defined by its original material, namely pottery, and therefore does not require *tevilah*. *Vechad amar k'sofo* — the other holds that one goes by the final state: the glaze itself is lead, and lead is an enumerated metal in the verse, so the vessel's outer surface is metallic and *tevilah* is required. *Vehilchata kesofo* — the *halacha* follows the second position, that we go by the end product.

In practice, many people extend this ruling to standard modern glazed pottery and glazed dishes. However, the lead glaze of the *gemara* is quite different from modern glazes, which typically contain silica — a form of glass — as a primary ingredient. And even glass itself was only a rabbinic extension of the metal category, and even then it involves a very thin surface layer. The question of whether contemporary glazed pottery genuinely falls within the *gemara*'s category is seriously debated among *poskim*, and there are grounds to hold that standard modern glazed dishes do not require *tevilah* under this ruling. Many people do immerse such dishes, though typically without a *brachah*.

### Collateral

*Iba'ya lehu* — a question is raised regarding *mashkanta* — what if someone takes an object as collateral from a non-Jew on a loan? Does *tevilah* apply?

*Amar Mar bar Rav Ashi*: my father took a silver goblet as collateral from a non-Jew, immersed it in a *mikvah*, and drank from it personally — exercising the creditor's prerogative to make use of a seized pledge until the debt is repaid. However, the *gemara* does not know whether the father required *tevilah* for a general reason — *mashkanta kezveini dami*, that collateral is in general treated like a purchase since the holder may ultimately retain it in satisfaction of the debt — or whether it was a specific case: he observed that this particular non-Jew had no intention of repaying and effectively meant to cede the goblet permanently, making it functionally a sale. If the latter, no general rule can be derived for collateral. The question is left unresolved.
