# Yevamot 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Safek Veyavam

### Gershon vs. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק וְיָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי מִיתָנָא, סָפֵק אָמַר: הַאי גַּבְרָא בַּר מִיתָנָא הוּא, וּפַלְגָא דִּידִי הוּא. יָבָם אָמַר: אַתְּ, בְּרַאי דִּידִי אַתְּ, וְלֵית לָךְ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara brings another case, that of one of **uncertain** descent, who is either the son of the deceased or the son of the *yavam*, **and** the ***yavam* who came to divide up** the **possessions of the grandfather,** i.e., the father of the *yavam* and the deceased, and each one made a claim to the inheritance. The son of **uncertain** descent **said: That man,** referring to himself, **is the son of the deceased, and** therefore **half** of the possessions **are mine** because the inheritance should be split between the two sons, i.e., the deceased and the *yavam*, and since I am the sole heir of the deceased, I should receive his portion. **The *yavam* said** to him: **You are my son, and** therefore **you have absolutely no** rights to the possessions; rather, I should receive all the inheritance. One half is mine because I am the grandfather's son, and the other half, which would have gone to my deceased brother, I should receive by virtue of the fact that I consummated the levirate marriage with his widow.

The *gemara* opens at the top of 38a with the case of *safek veyavam* — a doubtful son and a *yavam* — who come to divide the inheritance of their grandfather Yaakov. The scenario is as follows. Yaakov owns a field. Gershon claims to be the son of Reuven, who has died, and on that basis demands half the field. Shimon, the *yavam*, is still alive, and his position is straightforward: Gershon is my son, not Reuven's, and therefore you have no claim to anything while I am living — the entire field is mine.

The *gemara* frames the competing claims in its own terms. Gershon declares, *hai gavra bar mitna hu u-palga didi hu* — I am the son of the deceased and half the field is mine. Shimon responds, *at bera didi at v-lo shayech lach v-lo midi* — you are my son and you have no portion in that field whatsoever.

### Safek Motzi Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי יָבָם וַדַּאי, וְסָפֵק סָפֵק, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** This is a case in which **the *yavam* has a definite** claim because he is the grandfather's son, **and** the son of **uncertain** descent has only **an uncertain** claim, and the *halakha* is that one with **an uncertain claim cannot extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it. Therefore, the *yavam* receives all the inheritance.

The *gemara* resolves this with a clean application of the principle *ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai* — a doubtful claim cannot displace a certain one. Shimon has a definite claim: he is unquestionably an heir to this field, whether he takes half or the whole, his status as heir is certain. Gershon's claim, whatever he may assert with confidence, is in reality a *safek* — he is possibly an heir. Since one party has a definite standing and the other only a doubtful one, we apply *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-re'aya* and Shimon takes the entire field.

### Three-Way Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק וּבְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי סָבָא. סָפֵק אָמַר: הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בַּר מִיתָנָא הוּא, וּפַלְגָא דִּידִי הוּא. וּבְנֵי יָבָם אָמְרִי: אֲחוּנָא אַתְּ וּמְנָתָא אִית לָךְ בַּהֲדַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises yet another case, that of one of **uncertain** descent, who is either the son of the deceased or the son of the *yavam*, **and the sons of the *yavam* who came to divide up** the **possessions of the grandfather,** and each one made a claim to the inheritance. The son of **uncertain** descent **said: That man,** referring to himself, **is the son of the deceased, and** therefore **half** of the possessions **are mine** because the inheritance should be split between the two sons, i.e., the deceased and the *yavam*, and since I am the lone heir of the deceased I should receive his portion. **And the sons of the *yavam* said: You are our brother, and** therefore **you** should **receive** only **a portion together with us.**

The *gemara* then proceeds to *safek u-vnei yavam sheba'u lachlok bi-nichsei sabba* — the case where Shimon has died, and now Gershon is disputing with Shimon's sons Kehat and Merari over Yaakov's field. Gershon again claims to be Reuven's son and demands half. Kehat and Merari counter: you are our brother, *achuna*, and you take an equal share alongside us.

### Concessions Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּלְגָא דְּקָמוֹדֵי לְהוּ — שָׁקְלִי. תִּילְתָּא דְּקָא מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ — שָׁקֵל. פָּשׁ לְהוּ דַּנְקָא — הָוֵי מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק וְחוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rules on this case: **The half** of the grandfather's possessions **that** the son of uncertain descent **concedes** belongs **to them,** the sons of the *yavam*, **they take** for themselves. By claiming to be the son of the brother who died first, he forfeits any rights to the other brother's portion. **The third** of the grandfather's possessions **that** the sons of the *yavam* **concede** belong **to him,** the son of uncertain descent, **he takes** for himself. By claiming he is their brother, they admit that he should at least receive an equal portion to them, which would mean one-third if they are three. This **leaves them** with **one-sixth [*danka*]** of the possessions that **is property of uncertain ownership, and** so **they** should **divide** it **up** between them.

Here the analysis turns on mutual concessions. Gershon concedes that the other half belongs to Kehat and Merari — his claim is only to his half. They in turn concede a third of the entire field to him — they acknowledge he is at least a brother entitled to a third. So each side has conceded something. Half and a third together account for five sixths of the field, leaving one sixth in dispute. About this remaining sixth, no one has a stronger claim than the other, and the rule is *havei mamon hamutal b'safek v-cholkin* — when money rests in genuine doubt, we divide it. So the contested sixth is split, giving Kehat and Merari together a half plus a twelfth — that is, seven twelfths — while Gershon receives a third plus a twelfth, which is five twelfths.

### Yaakov & Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבָא וְיָבָם בְּנִכְסֵי סָפֵק. אוֹ סָבָא וְסָפֵק בְּנִכְסֵי יָבָם — הָוֵי מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק וְחוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents two additional cases. One is a case where a son of uncertain descent, who is either the son of the deceased or the son of the *yavam*, died, and **the grandfather and the *yavam*** come to divide up the **possessions of the** son of **uncertain** descent. In the absence of any children, a father inherits from his son. The grandfather claims that the son of uncertain descent was actually the son of the deceased, and since the deceased has already died, the grandfather should be next in line to inherit from him. The *yavam* claims that the son of uncertain descent was his own son, and therefore he should inherit from him. **Or,** the second case is one in which the *yavam* died and **the grandfather and the** son of **uncertain** descent come to divide up the **possessions of the *yavam*.** The son of uncertain descent claims to be the lone son of the *yavam* and therefore he should inherit, whereas the grandfather claims that the son of uncertain descent was the son of the deceased and that the *yavam* died childless, and therefore the grandfather, who is the father of the *yavam*, should inherit from him. This **is** a case of **property of uncertain ownership, and** so **they** should **divide up** the possessions between them.

The *gemara* then considers the inverse scenario: *sabba v-yavam b-nichsei safek* — what happens when Gershon himself dies. Now Yaakov the grandfather and Shimon the *yavam* are both alive and both want to inherit Gershon's estate. Recall that Gershon had received half of Reuven's property. Shimon argues that Gershon was like his son and he therefore inherits from him. Yaakov argues that Gershon was Reuven's son, and since Reuven is gone, the inheritance flows back up to him. Neither claim is stronger than the other. And the parallel case — *sabba v-safek b-nichsei yavam* — arises when Shimon dies without Kehat and Merari yet in the picture: Gershon claims Shimon was his father, while Yaakov claims Reuven was Gershon's father and that Shimon's estate should come to him. In both configurations the *gemara* concludes: *havei mamon hamutal b'safek v-cholkin* — each side presents a claim without decisive advantage, and the property is divided.

## Shomeret Yavam

### Nafal Nechasim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לָהּ נְכָסִים — מוֹדִים בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל שֶׁמּוֹכֶרֶת וְנוֹתֶנֶת, וְקַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a widow waiting for her *yavam*** to either consummate a levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza* with her, i.e., a *yevama*, **to whom property was bequeathed: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel** both **agree that she may sell or give** away that property *ab initio*, **and** that if she did, the transfer **is valid.** Since she has only a levirate bond with the *yavam*, she retains total control of the property. This is in contrast to a betrothed woman, concerning whom Beit Hillel rule that she may not sell such property because her betrothed also has rights to it (*Ketubot* 78a).

The *gemara* turns to the *mishna* on *shomeret yavam* — a woman waiting for *yibbum* or *chalitzah* after her husband Reuven has died, while Shimon stands as the *yavam*. The question is: if she inherits property during this period of waiting — *naflo lah nechasim* — what is the *halacha* governing that property?

### Nechsei Milog

To understand the stakes, one must recall the framework of *nechsei milog*: property to which a wife holds title, while the husband enjoys the *peirot*, the usufruct. The principle of *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf* — that the right to the fruit is treated like ownership of the body of the asset itself — means that a husband's right to *peirot* is substantial. The question for the *shomeret yavam* is whether the *zika*, the bond tying her to Shimon, is strong enough to confer upon him the rights of a husband over her property. If the *zika* effectively makes her like his wife, he would be entitled to the *peirot*. If it does not, the property remains fully in her domain.

### Buying & Selling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹדִים בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל שֶׁמּוֹכֶרֶת וְנוֹתֶנֶת, וְקַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel both **agree that she may sell or give** away that property *ab initio*, **and** that if she did, the transfer **is valid.**

The *mishna* opens with a ruling on which Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree: *modim Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel she-mocheres u-nosenet v-kayam* — she may sell, give gifts, and transact with the property, and it all stands. This is itself a *chiddush*: not only does Shimon have no claim to the *peirot*, he lacks even the standing to block her sale of the principal. In this state, Shimon is entirely out of the picture — she holds the property freely, and the *zika* confers upon him no effective right.

### Upon Her Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתָה, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וּבִנְכָסִים הַנִּכְנָסִים וְיוֹצְאִין עִמָּהּ? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נְכָסִים בְּחֶזְקָתָן. כְּתוּבָּה — בְּחֶזְקַת יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל, נְכָסִים הַנִּכְנָסִים וְיוֹצְאִין עִמָּהּ — בְּחֶזְקַת יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** If she **died, what should be done with** the money assured to her in **her marriage contract** by her deceased husband **and with** her **property that enters and leaves** the marriage **with her,** in which a husband only ever has a usufructuary interest? **Beit Shammai say: The husband's heirs,** i.e., the *yavam*, who stands to inherit from the husband when he consummates the levirate marriage, **should divide up** the property **together with** her **father's heirs,** i.e., the woman's family. **And Beit Hillel say:** The **property** retains **its** previous ownership **status.** Therefore, money assured to her in her **marriage contract** remains **in the possession of the husband's heirs.** Since it was to be paid from the husband's own property, the money is retained by his estate and passes to his heirs. And her **property that enters and leaves** the marriage **with her** remains **in the possession of the father's heirs.** Since those properties belonged to her, upon her death they are inherited by her father or his heirs.

The *mishna* then asks: *meisa — ma ya'aseh bi-kesuvatah u-vi-nechasim ha-nichnasim v-yotzim imah* — if she dies, what becomes of her *kesubah* and the property she carried in and out? Two competing claimants emerge. Lavan, her father, argues that the property was hers, she died without heirs in the relevant sense, and it therefore reverts to him. Shimon argues that she was in some sense his wife, and though he may not have had rights to the property while she was alive, upon her death he ought to inherit.

The *kesubah* requires a word of clarification. When Reuven died, Rachel became entitled to collect her two hundred *zuz*. However, if Shimon performs *yibbum*, the marriage to Reuven is treated as continuing — it is not a new marriage — and she does not collect. The *kesubah* becomes collectable only if Shimon performs *chalitzah*, releasing her from the bond. So the question of who controls the uncollected *kesubah* is bound up with Shimon's ambiguous standing: if Shimon stands in Reuven's place, the *kesubah* has not yet been released and remains within Shimon's estate; if the *zika* relationship is not considered a genuine marriage, her claim against it is doubtful. One might also observe — and this point was raised in the course of study — that since this is a continuation of her *kesubah* against Reuven rather than a fresh obligation from Shimon, the amount remains two hundred *zuz* rather than one hundred, reflecting that this is not treated as the *kesubah* of a widow marrying anew, but as the original obligation carrying forward.

### Shammai Divide

Beit Shammai rules *yachloku yoresh ha-ba'al v-yoresh ha-av* — the husband's heirs and the father's heirs divide. Beit Hillel rules *nechasim b'chezkasan* — the property remains in its *chazakah*, its presumptive status. The underlying conceptual question is whether we apply *mamon hamutal b'safek cholkin* — disputed assets are split — or whether we follow *chazakah* and leave things where they stood.

Beit Hillel elaborates: the *kesubah* is *b'chezkat yorshei ha-ba'al* — it was never collected, it remained under Shimon's control, so it stays there. The *nechasim ha-nichnasim v-yotzim imah* — the property she carried — is *b'chezkat yorshei ha-av*, for it never entered Shimon's domain. She received it, held it, and controlled it throughout; it rightfully passes to her heirs. Beit Hillel's position is thus internally coherent: follow the *chazakah* in each case, and each category of property flows to the party who definitively held it. Beit Shammai, by contrast, holds that since Shimon's status is genuinely uncertain — he may or may not be treated as a husband — we treat this as a *safek* and divide.

### Arusa vs. Nesua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא דְּלָא פְּלִיגִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא דִּפְלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the first clause,** concerning a *yevama* who is still alive, **that** Beit Shammai **do not disagree** with Beit Hillel that the woman has full possession of the property since there is only a levirate bond but no marriage, **and what is different** about **the latter clause that** Beit Shammai **disagree** with Beit Hillel and rule that the *yavam* does take a share of the property, which would imply that the levirate bond alone is sufficient to afford the *yavam* rights over her property?

The *gemara* now asks: *mai shna reisha de-lo paligei u-mai shna seifa de-paligei* — why does Beit Shammai agree completely in the first case, where she is alive and in full control, yet contest the property in the second case after she dies? If Shimon has a doubtful claim as quasi-husband, why does that claim not surface while she is alive?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: רֵישָׁא דְּנָפְלָה כְּשֶׁהִיא אֲרוּסָה, וְסֵיפָא דְּנָפְלָה כְּשֶׁהִיא נְשׂוּאָה. וְקָסָבַר עוּלָּא: זִיקַּת אֲרוּסָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק אֲרוּסָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** The two clauses concern different cases: **The first clause** concerns a case **where she happened** before her *yavam* for levirate marriage at a time **when she was a betrothed woman** and only then did she come into the possession of property. Since when she was betrothed her husband did not have any rights to the property, neither does the *yavam*. **And the latter clause** concerns a case **where she happened** before her *yavam* at a time **when she was a married woman.** In such a case, were her husband still alive, he would have full rights to the property; therefore, so does the *yavam*. The Gemara explains: **And Ulla holds** that a levirate **bond** formed **with a betrothed woman affords** her a status equivalent to **a woman** about whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **betrothed,**

Ulla answers by introducing a distinction not stated explicitly in the *mishna*: *reisha d'nafla k'shehi arusa* — the first case is where she fell to *yibbum* having been an *arusa* to Reuven; *seifa d'nafla k'shehi nesua* — the second case is where she had been a *nesua*. Ulla holds *zikas arusa osa safek arusa* — the *zika* that derives from an *eirusin* relationship produces a doubtful *arusa* status for Shimon — and *zikas nesua osa safek nesua* — the *zika* that derives from a *nisuin* relationship produces a doubtful *nesua* status. Since a husband in an *eirusin* has no right to *peirot* in the first place, even a certain *eirusin* status would give Shimon no claim to the property. Hence in the *arusa* case, Beit Shammai fully concedes that she controls the property. But in the *nesua* case, the transferred *zika* creates at least a doubtful *nesua* bond, and upon her death Shimon can press a claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִיקַּת אֲרוּסָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק אֲרוּסָה — דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ וַדַּאי אֲרוּסָה, מוֹדִים בֵּית הִלֵּל שֶׁמּוֹכֶרֶת וְנוֹתֶנֶת וְקַיָּים?
>
> **Translation:** It must be that a levirate **bond** formed **with a betrothed woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **betrothed, because if it enters your mind** to suggest that her status is equivalent to that of **a definitely betrothed woman, would Beit Hillel concede that she may sell or give** away her property *ab initio*, **and that** if she does the transfer **is valid?**

The *gemara* supplies the proofs. How do we know *zikas arusa osa safek arusa* and not *vadai arusa*? Because a *mishna* elsewhere teaches that if a woman received property *after* she became a regular (non-*yibbum*) *arusa*, Beit Hillel holds she may not sell it *l'chatchila*, even though *b'dieved* the sale stands. Yet here, the *shomeret yavam* may sell *l'chatchila*. This can only be because her *arusa* status through *zika* is merely a *safek*, and the weaker doubtful status does not even prohibit her from selling outright. Similarly, how do we know *zikas nesua osa safek nesua* and not *vadai nesua*? Because a *mishna* elsewhere teaches that if a woman received property after she became a *nesua*, both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that if she sold it the husband can reclaim it from the buyers — the husband is fully in control. Yet here, when she dies, Beit Shammai says only to split the property, not to award it entirely to Shimon. This confirms his status is only a *safek nesua*, not *vadai nesua*: a *vadai* husband would take everything; since he takes only half, the *zika* must have produced only *safek nesua* standing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: נָפְלוּ לָהּ נְכָסִים מִשֶּׁנִּתְאָרְסָה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תִּמְכּוֹר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר. אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם מָכְרָה וְנָתְנָה — קַיָּים. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זִיקַּת אֲרוּסָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק אֲרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 78a): If **property was bequeathed to** a woman **after she was betrothed, Beit Shammai say: She may sell** that property, **and Beit Hillel say: She may not sell** that property. However, **both agree that if she sold** it **or gave** it **away,** the transfer **is valid.** The mishna clearly states that according to Beit Hillel, a woman who is definitely betrothed may not sell the property *ab initio*. **Rather, conclude from here,** from the fact that here Beit Hillel permit the *yevama* to sell her property *ab initio*, that a levirate **bond** formed **with a betrothed woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **betrothed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִיקַּת נְשׂוּאָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ וַדַּאי נְשׂוּאָה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים יַחְלוֹקוּ יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, it must be that a levirate **bond** formed **with a married woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **married, because if it enters your mind** to suggest that her status is equivalent to that of **a definitely married woman,** would **Beit Shammai say** that **the husband's heirs should divide up** the property **together with the father's heirs?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: נָפְלוּ לָהּ נְכָסִים מִשֶּׁנִּישֵּׂאת, אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם מָכְרָה וְנָתְנָה — שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זִיקַּת נְשׂוּאָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 78a): If **property was bequeathed to** a woman **after she was married, both** Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai **agree that if she sold** the property **or gave** it **away,** then **the husband repossesses** it **from the purchasers. Rather, conclude from here,** from the fact that here Beit Shammai assume the rights of the *yavam* are limited, that a levirate **bond** formed **with a married woman affords** her a status equivalent to **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **married.**

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: אַדְּמִפַּלְגִי בְּגוּפַהּ וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה, לִפַּלְגוּ בְּחַיֶּיהָ וּלְפֵירוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba challenges Ulla's understanding of the mishna: **Rabba said to him:** If your explanation is correct, then in the latter clause, **instead of disagreeing** with regard to who has the rights to the property **itself,** which necessitates considering the case **after** her **death, let** Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai **disagree with regard to** the more immediate case **when she is** still **alive and** dispute who has the rights **to** the use and **produce** of the property.

Rava challenges Ulla's construction directly: *atu mafalgi b'gufa v-le'achar mita* — is the *mishna* really discussing the body of the property and what happens after her death merely as a vehicle for distinguishing *arusa* from *nesua*? If that were the operative distinction, *liflug b'chayeha u-l'peirot* — the *mishna* should simply have discussed what happens to the *peirot* while she is alive, and distinguished between the two relationships there. Why does the *mishna* shift entirely to a case where she has died? Ulla's reading requires us to say that the difference between case one and case two has nothing to do with whether she is alive or dead — but the *mishna* plainly presents that as the pivotal distinction. Rava's objection is that you cannot explain away the *mishna*'s own structure.

### Title vs. Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּנָפְלָה כְּשֶׁהִיא נְשׂוּאָה, וְזִיקַּת נְשׂוּאָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה. רֵישָׁא דְּאִיהִי קָיְימָא — הָוְיָא לַהּ אִיהִי וַדַּאי וְאִינְהוּ סָפֵק, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי. סֵיפָא דְּמֵתָה, הַלָּלוּ בָּאִין לִירַשׁ, וְהַלָּלוּ בָּאִין לִירַשׁ — וְיַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba said** a different resolution to the apparent inconsistency in Beit Shammai's rulings: Both **this** first clause **and that** latter clause of the mishna concern a case in **which she happened** before her *yavam* for levirate marriage **once she was** already **a married woman, and** a levirate **bond** formed **with a married woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** about whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **married.** The distinction between the two clauses is as follows: In the **first clause, where she is alive, she has a certain** claim to the property, **while they,** i.e., the *yavam*, are considered to have only **an uncertain claim** to the property, as she has the status of a woman for whom there is an uncertainty whether she is married. **And** since one who has **an uncertain claim cannot extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it, she therefore retains full possession of the property. In **the latter clause,** however, **where she died,** neither party has a definite claim; rather, **these** heirs of the father **come to inherit, and those** heirs of the husband **come to inherit, and** therefore **they should divide up** the property.

Rava therefore offers his own resolution: *eidi v'eidi d'nafla k'shehi nesua* — both cases of the *mishna* concern a woman who had been a *nesua* to Reuven. In both cases, *zikas nesua osa safek nesua*, and Shimon has a doubtful *nesua* claim throughout. So why does Beit Shammai concede that she controls the property while alive?

The answer is elegant and turns on a precise analysis of what is being claimed in each scenario. When she is alive: *reisha d'hee kayma, havya lah hee vadai v'ihu safek* — she is a *vadai* and he is a *safek*. She is the definite holder of *title* to the property — that is unquestioned even in *nechsei milog*. Shimon's claim is only to the *peirot*, the usufruct, and even that claim is doubtful because it depends on whether the *zika* is treated as a genuine *nisuin*. She says: I definitively own the land; you have a doubtful claim to the fruit. *V'ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai* — a doubtful claim cannot displace a certain one. Therefore, Beit Shammai fully concedes while she is alive: her *vadai* title overrides his *safek* claim to *peirot*.

When she dies, the situation fundamentally changes. Now the debate is no longer about *peirot* but about the *title* itself — who inherits the principal. At that point, both Shimon and Lavan are coming to claim ownership. She is no longer present to assert her *vadai* title. Both claimants arrive with uncertain standing: Shimon may have been her husband, Lavan is her father. This is *mamon hamutal b'safek*, and Beit Shammai says *yachloku* — divide it.

### Abaye Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּלְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי? וְהָתְנַן: נָפַל הַבַּיִת עָלָיו וְעַל אָבִיו, עָלָיו וְעַל מוֹרִישָׁיו, וְהָיוּ עָלָיו כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה וּבַעַל חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba's opinion: **But** is it true that **according to Beit Shammai,** one with **an uncertain claim cannot extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 157a): In a case where **a house collapsed upon** a person **and upon his father,** or **upon him and upon those** from **whom he stood to inherit, and there were** outstanding debts **against** that person from his **wife's marriage contract and** to **a creditor,** but he had no money with which to pay those debts, and it is not known who died first, the following situation arises: If the father died first, then before the son died he had already inherited the father's property and therefore the son's creditors gained a lien over that property and have the rights to collect their debts from that property even after the son's death.

Abaye presses a challenge: *u-l'Veit Shammai ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai*? Does Beit Shammai truly hold that a *safek* cannot displace a *vadai*? The following *mishna* seems to present a counterexample.

### House Collapse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב אוֹמְרִים: הַבֵּן מֵת רִאשׁוֹן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת הָאָב, וּבַעַל חוֹב אוֹמֵר: הָאָב מֵת רִאשׁוֹן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת הַבֵּן — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נְכָסִים בְּחֶזְקָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, the father's heirs and the creditor offer opposing claims: **The father's heirs say: The son died first and** only **afterward the father died.** Therefore, the creditor never gained any rights to collect from the property. **And the creditor says: The father died first and** only **afterward the son died.** Therefore, the father's property was liened to the son's debts, and the creditor has a right to collect. The mishna continues: **Beit Shammai say: They should divide up** the property between them. **And Beit Hillel say:** The **property** retains **its** previous ownership **status,** which in this case means that since the last known possessor was the father, so the father's heirs gain full rights to it.

*Nafal ha-bayit alav v'al aviv* — a house collapsed on Reuven and his father Yaakov simultaneously, and it is unknown who died first. Reuven had creditors — a wife's *kesubah* or other debts. The *yorshim*, the heirs, argue that Reuven died first and then Yaakov, so the property never passed through Reuven and the creditors have no lien. The *ba'alei chov*, the creditors, argue that Yaakov died first, Reuven inherited from Yaakov, and the creditors acquired a lien on that inherited property before Reuven himself died. Beit Shammai rules *yachloku* — divide the contested property. Beit Hillel rules *nechasim b'chezkasam* — maintain the *chazakah*, which in this case favors the heirs, since the property was definitively under Yaakov's control and the creditors are outside claimants.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב וַדַּאי, וּבַעַל חוֹב סָפֵק, וְקָאָתֵי סָפֵק וּמוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explains his proof: **Isn't** it the case **here** that **the father's heirs** have a **definite** claim **and the creditor** has only **an uncertain** claim? Therefore, since Beit Shammai rule that the property should be divided up, it is apparent that they hold that one with **an uncertain claim can extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it.

The *gemara* notes the apparent contradiction: *v'hacha yorshim vadai u-ba'alei chov safek* — the heirs have definite standing and the creditors have a doubtful claim. Yet Beit Shammai says divide! If Beit Shammai holds *ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai*, why does a *safek* creditor get to press a claim against the *vadai* heirs?

### Shtar Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: שְׁטָר הָעוֹמֵד לִגְבּוֹת כְּגָבוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Beit Shammai's ruling in this case cannot be adduced as a proof because **Beit Shammai hold:** A debt recorded in **a bill** of debt **that is awaiting collection is as though it was already collected** to the extent that the creditor is considered to be in possession of the debt. Therefore, the creditor is considered to be in possession of the property to the same extent as the father's heirs; consequently, the property is divided between them.

The *gemara* resolves this with an important principle: *kasvari Veit Shammai shtar ha'omed ligvos k'gavuy dami* — Beit Shammai holds that an outstanding *shtar*, a written debt instrument, is treated as though it has already been collected. This is what distinguishes the creditor case from the *shomeret yavam* case. Normally, one might conceptualize the creditor's situation as follows: the heirs inherit, and then the creditors come as outsiders attempting to take the property away — in which case the creditors would indeed be a *safek* pressing against the heirs' *vadai*, and *ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai* would apply. But Beit Shammai does not conceptualize it that way. Because the creditors hold a *shtar*, their claim is treated as already realized — *k'gavuy dami*. When the heirs and the creditors present themselves before *beit din*, neither party is an outsider pressing against the other's established *chazakah*. Both stand at the same level, and since the facts are in doubt, you divide.

By contrast, in the *shomeret yavam* case while she is alive, Shimon holds no *shtar*. He has a doubtful claim to *peirot*, nothing more. She holds definite title. There is no instrument that elevates his standing to equal hers, and so *ein safek motzi mi-yedei vadai* applies in full force — he cannot press his doubtful claim against her certain ownership.
