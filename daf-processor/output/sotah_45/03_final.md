# Sotah 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Egla Arufah Overview

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא חָלָל בָּאֲדָמָה ... וְיָצְאוּ זְקֵנֶיךָ וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״. שְׁלֹשָׁה מִבֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ יוֹצְאִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֲמִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ — שְׁנַיִם, ״וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ — שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין בֵּית דִּין שָׁקוּל, מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In certain cases of unsolved murder, the Torah prescribes a ritual performed with **a heifer whose neck is broken.** During the course of this ritual, the judges say a confession **in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"If one be found slain in the land** which the Lord your God has given you to possess it, lying in the field, and it is not known who has smitten him; **then your Elders and your judges shall come forth"** (Deuteronomy 21:1–2). What is the procedure for this ritual? **Three members of the High Court [Sanhedrin] that is in Jerusalem would go forth** to see the corpse. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Five** would go out, **as it is stated: "Your Elders,"** in the plural form, indicating at least **two;** and it is written: **"And your judges,"** in the plural form, indicating another **two** judges; **and a court may not** be comprised of **an even** number of judges because they need to be able to issue a majority ruling. Consequently, **they add to them one more** Elder.

The final *perek* of *Sotah* turns to *egla arufa*, the ritual of the beheaded calf performed when a murder victim is found and the killer cannot be identified. Like the *sota* procedure, *egla arufa* involves an unresolved situation — in the case of *sota*, it is uncertainty about whether a woman committed adultery; here, the act of murder is known but the perpetrator is not. The *mishna* establishes that the declaration made by the elders over the calf must be recited in *lashon hakodesh*, and it cites the verse *ki yimatzei chalal* as its source. The *gemara*, however, immediately asks: *mai ka'amar*? What is the *mishna* actually saying? The verse *ki yimatzei chalal* does not appear to contain any indication that the declaration is made in Hebrew.

### Abbahu's V'anu V'amru

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְעָנוּ וְאָמְרוּ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְעָנוּ הַלְוִיִּם וְאָמְרוּ וְגוֹ׳״, מָה עֲנִיָּיה הָאֲמוּרָה לְהַלָּן בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ — אַף כָּאן בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the first sentence in the mishna **saying?** The mishna is attempting to prove that the verses read during the ritual of breaking a heifer's neck are to be recited in Hebrew, yet the verse does not offer any proof of this. **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** The confession is recited in Hebrew, **as it is stated** with regard to the ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken: **"And they shall speak and say"** (Deuteronomy 21:7), **and later it is stated** with regard to the curses stated on Mount Ebal: **"And the Levities shall speak and say"** (Deuteronomy 27:14). **Just as** the word **"speak" that is said later** with regard to the Levites is referring to a speech that **is** recited **in the sacred tongue, so too here** the declaration is recited **in the sacred tongue.**

Rabbi Abbahu resolves the difficulty. The *mishna*'s citation of *ki yimatzei chalal* is not the source for the Hebrew-language requirement — that source is derived elsewhere, through a *gezerah shavah* based on the phrase *v'anu v'amru*. The verse in the *egla arufa* passage reads *v'anu v'amru: yadeinu lo shafchu es hadam hazeh* — "they shall respond and say: our hands did not spill this blood." The same linguistic formula, *v'anu v'amru*, appears in the context of the blessings and curses on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eval*, where the *Levi'im* respond and say — a context already established as requiring *lashon hakodesh*. The parallel phrasing creates the equation: just as the response there is in Hebrew, so too here. The *mishna*'s citation of *ki yimatzei chalal* is therefore not addressing the language requirement at all.

### Procedure & Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסֵדֶר עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה כֵּיצַד — ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא חָלָל בָּאֲדָמָה ... וְיָצְאוּ זְקֵנֶיךָ וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ — שְׁלֹשָׁה מִבֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ יוֹצְאִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר חֲמִשָּׁה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues to answer the question of: **And how** is the ritual of the **heifer whose neck is broken ordered?** The mishna states: It is written in the Torah: **"If one be found slain in the land** which the Lord your God has given you to possess it, lying in the field, and it is not known who has smitten him; **then your Elders and your judges shall come forth,** and they shall measure unto the cities which are round about him that is slain" (Deuteronomy 21:1–2). **Three members of the High Court that is in Jerusalem would go forth. Rabbi Yehuda says: Five.**

Rather, the *mishna* is introducing the procedure of *egla arufa* — *seder egla arufa keitzad*. The verse *ki yimatzei chalal ba'adamah v'yatzu zkeinecha v'shoftecha u'madadu svivos hachalal* describes how the elders and judges go out and measure around the corpse to determine which city is nearest. The *mishna* teaches that *shlosha mibeis din hagadol shebiYerushalayim hayu yotzin* — three representatives from the central *beis din* in Jerusalem would go out to perform this measurement. It is these central figures who are identified by the words *zkeinecha v'shoftecha*, not the *ziknei ha'ir*, the elders of the nearby city, who come later to perform the actual ritual once the measurement has established which city is closest.

## Three vs. Five Dispute

### Initial Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְיָצְאוּ זְקֵנֶיךָ וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״. ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ — שְׁנַיִם, ״וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ — שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין בֵּית דִּין שָׁקוּל, מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד, הֲרֵי כָּאן חֲמִשָּׁה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ — שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין בֵּית דִּין שָׁקוּל, מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד, הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "And your Elders and judges shall come forth." "Your Elders"** indicates **two; "and your judges"** also indicates **two; and a court may not** be composed of **an even** number of judges, so **they add to them one more** judge. Therefore, **there are five** judges. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: "Your Elders"** indicates **two, and** as **a court may not** consist of **an even** number of judges **they add to them one more.** Therefore, **there are three.**

The *mishna* states that three representatives go out, but Rabbi Yehuda says five. A *braita* sets out the reasoning behind each position. The word *zkeinecha*, "your elders," is in the plural, indicating a minimum of two. The word *v'shoftecha*, "and your judges," likewise indicates two. But a *beis din* cannot have an even number — it requires an odd number to allow for a deciding vote and to structurally represent judicial authority. Therefore, one more is added: *masifin aleihem od echad harei chan chamisha* — that gives five, in the view of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon, identified as the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna*, holds three: he counts only from *zkeinecha*, yielding two, and adds one to make an odd number.

### Zkeinecha Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״! הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִמְיוּחָדִין שֶׁבְּשׁוֹפְטֶיךָ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִ״זִּקְנֵי״ ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rabbi Shimon as well, isn't it written: "And your judges";** why does he not agree that this indicates a need for two more judges? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** additional word "Elders" in order **to** teach that they must be **the distinguished among your judges,** i.e., judges from the Sanhedrin. **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda,** who derives from the phrase "and your judges" that there is a need for two additional judges, know that they need to be from the Sanhedrin? **He derives it from** an extra letter. The verse could have simply stated: **The Elders.** Instead, it adds a letter and states: **"Your Elders,"** to teach that they must be the distinguished among the Elders of Israel.

The *gemara* presses Rabbi Shimon: the verse explicitly says *v'shoftecha* — what does he do with that word? The answer is that Rabbi Shimon reads *v'shoftecha* not as adding to the count but as identifying the character of those going out — *l'meyuchadim shebishoftecha*, the distinguished ones among your judges, meaning the central court, the *beis din hagadol*. The word tells you *who*, not *how many*.

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, draws the identification of the *beis din hagadol* from a different source — the possessive form *zkeinecha* itself, which he parses in two stages. Had the Torah written simply *ziknei*, one might have thought this meant any elderly men found in the marketplace; the possessive "yours" signals that these are men of judicial standing. But even knowing they are judges, one might have assumed a local *beis din* — a *sanhedrei ktana* of twenty-three — suffices. The additional word *v'shoftecha* then clarifies that it must be the *hamiyuchadin shebishoftecha*, the most distinguished among your judges, meaning the *beis din hagadol*. Since Rabbi Yehuda uses *v'shoftecha* for this qualitative identification, he needs another mechanism to arrive at the count of five.

### Rabbi Yehuda Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זִקְנֵי״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ זִקְנֵי הַשּׁוּק, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ סַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ — לִמְיוּחָדִין שֶׁבְּשׁוֹפְטֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** could say: **If the Merciful One had written** in the Torah simply: **The Elders, I would say** that it includes **even the Elders of the marketplace,** meaning any honorable people. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"Your Elders,"** to indicate specifically Torah Sages, who are revered by all. **And if the Merciful One had written** in the Torah only: **"Your Elders," I would say** that it includes **even** Elders from **a lesser Sanhedrin.** Therefore, **the Merciful One** also **writes: "And your judges," to** teach that they must be **the distinguished among your judges,** from the Great Sanhedrin.

The *gemara* considers whether Rabbi Yehuda could learn both the identity of the *beis din hagadol* and the number five from *ziknei ha'eidah*, the verse in *Vayikra* concerning the *sanhedrin* that rules erroneously and must bring a special *korban*. From that context Rabbi Yehuda derives that only five members of the *beis din hagadol* need to be present — not the entire court. The *gemara* suggests that if Rabbi Yehuda learns both identity and number from that source, he would have no need to use *v'shoftecha* for numerical purposes at all. This challenge is acknowledged and the reasoning is refined accordingly.

### Vav Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי גָּמַר, לִגְמְרַהּ לְכוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא מֵהָתָם, ״זְקֵנֶיךָ וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא, וָיו ״וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ לְמִנְיָנָא. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — וָיו לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If he derives** this *halakha* from this verbal analogy, then **he should derive the entire matter from there,** including the requirement for five judges. If so, **why do I** need the phrase **"your Elders and your judges"? Rather,** it must be that Rabbi Yehuda does not derive the *halakha* from this verbal analogy. Instead, he understands that the phrase "your Elders" indicates two, and that "your judges" indicates that they must be the distinguished among your judges, and the letter ***vav*** in the phrase **"and your judges [*veshofetekha*]" is to** add two more to **the count** of the judges. **And Rabbi Shimon** does not accept this derivation because **he does not learn** anything from the letter ***vav*.**

The *gemara* resolves the matter by focusing on the conjunction *vav* at the beginning of *v'shoftecha*. Had the verse read *zkeinecha shoftecha* — without the connecting *vav* — it would read naturally as an appositive: "your elders who are your judges," a single group described in two ways, yielding no additional count. The extra *vav* signals that *v'shoftecha* is to be counted as a distinct unit, adding two more to the two of *zkeinecha*, and requiring one more for an odd number — hence five. *V'shoftecha l'minyana*. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, does not derive significant weight from the additional *vav*, and therefore holds three.

The *gemara* also considers whether the plural verbs *v'yatzu* ("and they shall go out") and *u'madadu* ("and they shall measure") should independently add to the count — potentially yielding nine for Rabbi Yehuda or seven for Rabbi Shimon. The answer is that those verbs are needed for their own teaching, as derived from a *braita*: *hen v'lo shlucheihen* — they themselves must go out, not send proxies, and *she'afillu nimtza ba'alil la'ir* — even if it is obvious which city is nearest, they are still required to measure, because *shemitzvah la'asok bemedidah*, the act of measurement is itself part of the *mitzvah*.

## Rabbi Eliezer Position

### Eliezer's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: ״זְקֵנֶיךָ״ — זוֹ סַנְהֶדְרִין, ״שֹׁפְטֶיךָ״ — זֶה מֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל. מֶלֶךְ — דִּכְתִיב: ״מֶלֶךְ בְּמִשְׁפָּט יַעֲמִיד אָרֶץ״, כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל — דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבָאתָ אֶל הַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם וְאֶל הַשֹּׁפֵט אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: "Your Elders," this is the Sanhedrin; "your judges," this is the king and the High Priest.** According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, the king and the High Priest need to participate in the ritual of breaking the neck of the heifer. From where is it learned that they are called judges? **A king** is called a judge, **as it is written: "A king by justice establishes the land"** (Proverbs 29:4). **A High Priest** is called a judge, **as it is written:** "If there arise a matter too hard for you in judgment…**And you shall come to the priests the Levites, and to the judge who shall be** in those days" (Deuteronomy 17:8–9).

The *gemara* notes that the *mishna* is not in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, as a *braita* teaches. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reads the verse entirely differently: *zkeinecha zo sanhedrin* — "your elders" refers to the full *sanhedrin*; *shoftecha ze melech v'kohen gadol* — "and your judges" refers to the king and the *kohen gadol*. The textual support for each: the king is called *shofet* from the verse *melech bemishpat ya'amid aretz*, the king upholds the land through justice; the *kohen gadol* is likewise included under *shoftecha* from the verse *u'vata el hakohanim haleviyim v'el hashofet asher yihyeh bayamim hahen*.

### National Responsibility

This interpretation carries profound weight. It is already striking that when a body is found in some remote location, representatives of the highest court in the land must travel out to measure and identify the nearest city. But Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov takes this further: not only the *sanhedrin*, but the political leader and the religious leader of the entire nation must become involved. The reason lies in the nature of the ritual itself. A murder has occurred and gone unresolved — the killer has not been identified, justice has not been served, and the verse speaks of *v'nitchaper lahem hadam*, an expiation for the blood. The principle that *la'aretz lo yechuppar badam asher shupach bah ki im bedam shofcho* — the land cannot be fully atoned for without justice being done — means that this unresolved guilt operates at a national level. There is a murderer somewhere at large; responsibility for that has not been discharged. The entire national leadership — the courts, the king representing civil order, the *kohen gadol* representing divine law — must together take ownership of that failure. It is not simply that a crime occurred, which would be handled locally; it is that a crime occurred and was never resolved, and that demands a national response.

### Entire Sanhedrin Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב בְּמֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל הוּא דִּפְלִיג, אֲבָל בְּסַנְהֶדְרִי, אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא בְּסַנְהֶדְרִי נָמֵי פְּלִיג, עַד דְּאִיכָּא כּוּלְּהַּ סַנְהֶדְרִי?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is it so that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov disagrees** only **with regard to a king and a High Priest,** contending that they too must be present at the measurement, **but** that **with regard to the Sanhedrin he holds either in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** that five Elders are needed **or in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** that three suffice? **Or, perhaps he also disagrees with regard to the Sanhedrin** and claims that the ritual is not performed **unless there is the entire Sanhedrin** participating.

The *gemara* raises the further question: when Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says *zkeinecha zo sanhedrin*, does he mean only that *sanhedrin* representatives — three or five, as debated by Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda — must participate, with his dispute being only about the addition of the king and *kohen gadol*? Or does he mean the entire *sanhedrin* must go out? The phrasing *zkeinecha zo sanhedrin* seems to suggest the whole body, which would be consistent with the overarching theme of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position — that the entire national leadership must assume communal ownership of this unresolved murder. The question is left open for the *gemara* to resolve.

## Zaken Mamre Analogy

### Zaken Mamre Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: מְצָאָן זָקֵן מַמְרֵא אַבֵּי פַגֵּי וְהִמְרָה עֲלֵיהֶן, יָכוֹל תְּהֵא הַמְרָאָתוֹ הַמְרָאָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקַמְתָּ וְעָלִיתָ אֶל הַמָּקוֹם״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַמָּקוֹם גּוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If **a rebellious Elder found** the Sanhedrin not in the Hewn Chamber, which was their usual place, but **in Beit Pagei,** near Jerusalem, **and he rebelled against them** there, by teaching in opposition to their ruling, one **might** have thought **his rebellion should be** considered **a rebellion,** rendering him liable to punishment. **The verse** therefore **states: "And you shall arise and go up to the place"** (Deuteronomy 17:8), which **teaches that** it is **the place** where the Sanhedrin resides that **causes** the *halakha* of a rebellious Elder to take effect.

Rav Yosef introduces a proof from the laws of *zaken mamrei*, the rebellious elder. If an elder of sufficient standing disputed a ruling of the central court, received their ruling against him, returned to his community, and continued to teach and rule in accordance with his own position, he is liable as a *zaken mamrei*. The case the *gemara* presents is: *matza zakein mamrei abei pagi v'himrah aleihem* — he encountered the *sanhedrin* not in the *lishkas ha-gazis*, their official chamber in the *beis hamikdash*, but in the *abei pagi*, the environs or surroundings of Jerusalem outside that chamber, and he voiced his dissenting position there. The question: *yachol tehei hamraso hamraah* — could that constitute actionable rebellion?

The answer is no, because the verse introducing *zaken mamrei* reads *v'kamta v'alisa el ha-makom asher yivchar*, you shall arise and ascend to the place that God has chosen — *melamed she-ha-makom goreim*, the place determines the law. Only when the court is sitting in their designated location does the confrontation carry the full weight of divine authority necessary to make defiance of their ruling a capital offense.

### Why They Left

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּנְפוּק כַּמָּה? אִילֵימָא דִּנְפוּק מִקְצָתָן, דִּלְמָא הָנָךְ דְּאִיכָּא גַּוָּאֵי כְּווֹתֵיהּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דִּנְפוּק כּוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: In this case, **how many** members of the Sanhedrin were there **who went out** to Beit Pagei? **If we say that** only **a minority of them went out,** why should he be considered a rebellious Elder? **Perhaps those** judges **who are inside** the Hewn Chamber **hold in accordance with his** opinion, which would mean that the Elder in question ruled with the majority. **Rather,** it **is obvious that they all went out.**

This raises a critical question: for the *sanhedrin* to have been found outside the *lishkas ha-gazis* — which this scenario assumes — why were they there? The *gemara* cites the verse *sarech agan ha-sohar al yechsar ha-mazeg* from *Shir HaShirim*, understood as a metaphor for the *sanhedrin*. Rashi explains that the image of a round goblet corresponds to the *sanhedrin*'s semicircular seating arrangement. From the phrase *al yechsar ha-mazeg* — "the mixed wine shall not be lacking" — the *gemara* derives a rule: if any member of the *sanhedrin* needs to leave, it is permitted only if twenty-three members remain, corresponding to a full *sanhedrei ktana* capable of adjudicating capital cases. The proportion of twenty-three out of seventy-one approximates a one-to-three ratio, paralleling the one-to-three wine-to-water dilution of mixed wine.

Now, could the *sanhedrin* have left their chamber for a *dvar reshus*, an ordinary non-*mitzvah* purpose? The *gemara* concludes they could not have — at minimum, the scenario of the *zaken mamrei* encountering the entire court outside implies that the whole *sanhedrin* had left, and the entire *sanhedrin* cannot leave for a *dvar reshus*. They must have had a *mitzvah* purpose. What *mitzvah* purpose would bring the entire *sanhedrin* outside? *Lav lemedidat egla* — is it not for the measurement of the *egla arufa*? This, the *gemara* suggests, must be the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: that the entire *sanhedrin* goes out for the measurement, consistent with his view that *zkeinecha zo sanhedrin* means the full court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי? אִי לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת — מִי מָצוּ נָפְקִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״שׇׁרְרֵךְ אַגַּן הַסַּהַר אַל יֶחְסַר הַמָּזֶג״, שֶׁאִם נִצְרַךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶם לָצֵאת, אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה כְּנֶגֶד סַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה — יוֹצֵא, וְאִם לָאו — אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The assumption that they all went out leads to the following question: **And for what** purpose did they all leave the Hewn Chamber? **If** it was **for an optional matter, may they leave? But isn't it written: "Your navel [*shorerekh*] is like a round goblet, let no mingled wine be wanting"** (Song of Songs 7:3). This verse is interpreted as referring to the Sanhedrin, the ministers [*sarei*] of Israel who sit in a semicircle, like half of a round goblet. The words "let no mingled wine be wanting" teach **that if one of** the great Sanhedrin **needed to leave,** then **if there are** still present in the Hewn Chamber **twenty-three** members, **corresponding** to the number of **a lesser Sanhedrin, he may leave; and if not, he may not leave.** This indicates that it is prohibited for the entire Sanhedrin to leave for an optional matter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לִדְבַר מִצְוָה. לְמַאי? לָאו לִמְדִידַת עֶגְלָה, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לָא, דִּלְמָא לְהוֹסִיף עַל הָעִיר וְעַל הָעֲזָרוֹת. כְּדִתְנַן: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הָעִיר וְעַל הָעֲזָרוֹת אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather** it **is obvious** that they left **for a matter of a mitzva. For what** particular mitzva did they venture out? **Is it not for the measurement** associated **with** the ritual of **the heifer, and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov?** This would prove that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov holds that the entire Sanhedrin goes out to measure the distance from the corpse to the nearby cities. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **No,** this is not a proof, as **perhaps** they went out for a different mitzva, **to expand the city** of Jerusalem **or the courtyards** of the Temple, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 2a): **They may expand** the sanctified area of **the city** of Jerusalem **or of the** Temple **courtyards only with the court of seventy-one.**

Abaye challenges this inference: there is another *mitzvah* purpose for which the entire *sanhedrin* is required outside the chamber — the expansion of the sanctified boundaries of Jerusalem or the *azarot* of the *beis hamikdash*, as the *mishna* teaches, *ein mosifin al ha-ir v'al ha-azarot ela be-veis din shel shivim v'echad* — such expansion requires all seventy-one members. So the *zaken mamrei* scenario does not uniquely point to *egla arufa*; the *sanhedrin* might have left for boundary expansion. The question of whether Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov requires the entire *sanhedrin* for *egla arufa* therefore remains unresolved from this reasoning alone.

### Baraita Egla Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: מְצָאָן אַבֵּית פַּגֵּי וְהִמְרָה עֲלֵיהֶן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לִמְדִידַת עֶגְלָה אוֹ לְהוֹסִיף עַל הָעִיר וְעַל הָעֲזָרוֹת, יָכוֹל תְּהֵא הַמְרָאָתוֹ הַמְרָאָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקַמְתָּ וְעָלִיתָ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַמָּקוֹם גּוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef,** that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov disagrees even with regard to the number of judges: If the rebellious Elder **found** the Sanhedrin **in Beit Pagei and rebelled against them, for example, if they went out for the measurement** of the ritual **of the heifer or to expand** the area of **the city or** that of **the courtyards,** one **might** have thought that **his rebellion should be a rebellion.** Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the requirement to follow the rulings of the Sanhedrin: **"And you shall arise and go up to the place"** (Deuteronomy 17:8). This **teaches that the place causes** the *halakha* of a rebellious Elder to take effect, indicating that the entire Sanhedrin participates in the measurement of the distance from the corpse to the cities.

A *braita* is then cited in support of Rav Yosef's original position: *tanya kevateih d'Rav Yosef*. The *braita* itself states — *matzan beis pagi v'himrah aleihem* — that if the *zaken* found the *sanhedrin* outside the *beis hamikdash* and rebelled against them, *kegon she-yatzu li-medidat egla o le-hosif al ha-ir v'al ha-azarot* — as for example when they went out for the measurement of the *egla* or to add to the city or the *azarot* — he is nevertheless not a *zaken mamrei*, since the verse requires that the confrontation occur in their designated place.

The significance of this *braita* is that it explicitly lists the measurement of *egla arufa* as a legitimate reason for the *sanhedrin* to be found outside — not as the only reason, but as a named scenario. And this scenario is coherent only on the view of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who holds that the entire *sanhedrin* participates in the *egla arufa* measurement. On the views of Rabbi Shimon or Rabbi Yehuda, where only three or five representatives go out, the remaining *sanhedrin* members would have no reason to leave the *lishkas ha-gazis*. The *braita* thus provides strong support that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov requires the full court, consistent with his broader vision of total national leadership assuming responsibility for unresolved murder.

## Body Conditions

### Chalal Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא טָמוּן בַּגַּל אוֹ תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן אוֹ צָף עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּאֲדָמָה״ — וְלֹא טָמוּן בַּגַּל, ״נֹפֵל״ — וְלֹא תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן, ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — וְלֹא צָף עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** If the corpse **was found concealed in a pile** of stones, **or hanging on a tree, or floating on the surface of the water,** then the judges **would not break the neck** of the heifer, **as it is stated:** "If one be found slain **in the land"** (Deuteronomy 21:1), **and not concealed in a pile** of stones; **"lying"** on the ground **and not hanging on a tree; "in the field," and not floating on the surface of the water.**

The *mishna* and accompanying *baraita* enumerate the conditions under which a body qualifies for *egla arufa*: it must be a *chalal*, understood as one killed by a sword (*be-chalal cherev*) and not merely strangled (*v'lo chanuk*); it must be fully still and not still convulsing on the ground (*v'lo mefarper ba-adamah*); it must not be buried under a pile (*v'lo tamun be-gal*); and it must be lying fallen on the ground (*nofel ba-adamah*) rather than suspended from a tree (*v'lo talui be-ilan*). The body must also be found in a field (*ba-sadeh*) and not floating on water (*v'lo tzaf al penei ha-mayim*).

### Tamun & Tzaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא טָמוּן בַּגַּל אוֹ תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁכַחְתָּ עֹמֶר בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — פְּרָט לְטָמוּן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַטָּמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the corpse **was found concealed in a pile** of stones **or hanging on a tree,** they would not perform the ritual of the heifer. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and not** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the *halakha* that sheaves of grain forgotten in the fields must be left for the poor: **"And you forget a sheaf in the field"** (Deuteronomy 24:19). This **excludes a concealed** sheaf; it is not considered forgotten and it may be collected by the owner, even if he did forget it. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say:** The phrase **"in the field"** serves **to include the concealed** sheaves, and these must be left for the poor. Rabbi Yehuda holds, like the ruling in the mishna here, that when the verse states "in the field" it excludes a concealed corpse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, הָכָא מֵעִנְיָינֵיהּ דִּקְרָא, הָתָם מֵעִנְיָינֵיהּ דִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as **here** they expound the *halakha* **based on the context of the verse,** and **there** too they expound the *halakha* **based on the context of the verse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא חָלָל״ — הֵיכָא דְּמִשְׁתְּכַח, ״בָּאֲדָמָה״ — פְּרָט לְטָמוּן. וְהָתָם מֵעִנְיָינֵיהּ דִּקְרָא — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי תִקְצֹר קְצִירְךָ בְשָׂדְךָ וְשָׁכַחְתָּ עֹמֶר״. שִׁכְחָה דּוּמְיָא דְּקָצִיר: מָה קָצִיר בְּגָלוּי — אַף שִׁכְחָה בְּגָלוּי, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַטָּמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, **as it is written: "If one be found slain"** (Deuteronomy 21:1), the default assumption is that the *halakha* applies no matter **where it is found.** When the verse then states: **"In the ground,"** it must be serving to **exclude** a matter, i.e., **a concealed** corpse. **And** similarly, **there,** in the case of forgotten sheaves, they also expound **based on the context of the verse, as it is written: "When you reap your harvest in your field, and you forget a sheaf"** (Deuteronomy 24:19). This indicates that **forgotten** sheaves **are similar to** those **of** the **harvest: Just as** the **harvest** is performed **with revealed** objects, i.e., the growing grain, **so too,** the *halakhot* of **forgetting** pertain **to revealed** sheaves. Consequently, when **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah **"in the field,"** it must be in order **to include the concealed** sheaves.

The *gemara* asks whether the *mishna*'s requirement that the body be exposed — not buried and not suspended — reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda specifically, as against the *rabbanan*. The comparison is to the laws of *shichcha*, the forgotten sheaf: the verse *ki tikzor ketzircha besadecha v'shachachta omer basadeh* — when you harvest your field and forget a sheaf in the field — is the basis for *shichcha*. There, a *braita* records that the word *basadeh* is interpreted by Rabbi Yehuda as *perat le-tamun* — excluding a sheaf that is buried; whereas the *rabbanan* say *basadeh le-rabot et ha-tamun* — including even a buried sheaf within the laws of *shichcha*.

If so, the *mishna*'s apparent exclusion of a buried body from *egla arufa* sounds like Rabbi Yehuda. Rav responds: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even the *rabbanan* could agree that the body must be exposed, because the derivation in each case depends on its own contextual logic. By *egla arufa*, the verse reads *ki yimatzei chalal ba'adamah* — "if a corpse is found on the ground." Before reaching the word *ba'adamah*, one would have assumed inclusively that the corpse could be found anywhere. The word *ba'adamah* therefore comes to narrow — *perat le-tamun*, excluding what is buried. The direction of the derivation is from broad to narrow.

By *shichcha*, the context is different. The verse reads *ki tikzor ketzircha* — your harvest — and then *v'shachachta*. The act of forgetting is juxtaposed to the act of cutting, and just as cutting involves grain that is exposed and visible, so too forgetting in that context initially suggests an exposed sheaf — narrow from the outset. The word *basadeh* then comes to broaden — *le-rabot et ha-tamun* — including even a buried sheaf. Each derivation responds to its own starting assumption, and the parallel positions by *shichcha* do not necessarily map onto *egla arufa*.

### Sheaves Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשִּׁכְחָה דּוּמְיָא דְּקָצִיר! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In light of this explanation, the Gemara asks: **According to Rabbi Yehuda as well, let him derive** that covered sheaves are not included **from** the fact that **forgotten** sheaves **are similar to** those **of** the **harvest.** Why does he have to derive it from the words "in the field"? The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so,** he does not derive that *halakha* from the phrase "in the field."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״, לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹת שִׁכְחַת קָמָה. וְרַבָּנַן שִׁכְחַת קָמָה מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִ״כִּי תִקְצֹר קְצִירְךָ בְּשָׂדְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore inquires: **But** then **why do I** need the phrase **"in the field,"** which indicates either an exclusion or an inclusion? The Gemara answers: **He requires** it in order **to include the forgotten** stalks **of standing** grain. Produce that one forgot to reap is considered forgotten, even if it is still attached to the ground. The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, from where** do they learn the *halakha* of **forgotten** stalks **of standing** grain? The Gemara answers: **They derive** it **from** the verse: **"When you reap your harvest in your field,** and you forget," which indicates that the *halakha* includes one who forgot to harvest part of his field.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: פְּרָט לְשֶׁצָּפוּ עוֹמָרִין לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן: מִ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ ״בְּשָׂדֶךָ״. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ ״בְּשָׂדֶךָ״ לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive from this verse? The Gemara answers: It is **necessary for him** in order **to** learn **that which Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Elazar** says, **as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** This verse **excludes sheaves that floated** through the air from his field **into another's field.** Such sheaves are not classified as forgotten and may be retrieved later by the owner. **And the Rabbis,** from where do they derive this *halakha*? They derive it **from** the fact that the verse could have stated: **In the field,** but instead states **"in your field,"** to include sheaves situated only in one's own field. The Gemara continues to ask: **And** what does **Rabbi Yehuda** learn from this phrase? The Gemara answers: **He does not learn** anything from the difference between: **In the field,** and **"in your field,"** as he holds it is not a significant difference.

The *gemara* then develops the *shichcha* analysis further. If the juxtaposition of forgetting to cutting already suggests that only an exposed sheaf qualifies — which is what Rabbi Yehuda holds — then why does he need the word *basadeh* to tell him so? The *gemara* answers: Rabbi Yehuda uses *basadeh* for a different purpose — *le-rabot shikchat kama*, to include the case of standing grain (*kama*) that has not yet been cut. If someone forgets unharvested grain still standing in the field, that too is *shichcha*. The word *besadecha* — "in your field" — teaches that even grain still *in* the field, not yet harvested, is subject to *shichcha*.

The *rabbanan*, who use *basadeh* to include buried sheaves, derive the inclusion of *shikchat kama* differently — from the juxtaposition *besadecha v'shachachta*, the very collocation of "your field" and "you forgot" suggesting that forgetting pertains to whatever remains *in* your field, including standing grain. Conversely, the *rabbanan* derive the exclusion of sheaves that flew into a neighbor's field — *perat le-shetzafu omarim le-toch sadeh chaveiro* — from the possessive *besadecha*, your field, not your neighbor's. Rabbi Yehuda, who does not place the same weight on the possessive, gets this exclusion from the juxtaposition rather than the possessive form.

### Tamun Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: צָפוּ עוֹמָרִין לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ, מַהוּ? אֲוִיר שָׂדֶה כְּשָׂדֶה דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָאו כְּשָׂדֶה דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **sheaves floated into his field,** i.e., if one sheaf landed on another sheaf or on another item, **what is** the *halakha*? Is the **airspace of a field considered to be like** it is the **field** itself, in which case one sheaf on top of another meets the legal requirements to be deemed forgotten? **Or,** is it **not considered like** the **field,** in which case one sheaf on top of another does not meet the legal requirements to be deemed forgotten?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עוֹמֶר שֶׁהֶחְזִיק בּוֹ לְהוֹלִיכוֹ לָעִיר, וְהִנִּיחוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ, וּשְׁכָחוֹ — הַתַּחְתּוֹן שִׁכְחָה, וְהָעֶלְיוֹן אֵינוֹ שִׁכְחָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵינָן שִׁכְחָה, הַתַּחְתּוֹן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא טָמוּן, וְהָעֶלְיוֹן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא צָף. עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּתַחְתּוֹן, אֲבָל בְּעֶלְיוֹן — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא הָוֵאי שִׁכְחָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again attempts to resolve the dilemma: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Pe'a* 3:7): In the case of **a sheaf that had been held** by its owner in order **to take it to the city, and he placed it on top of another** sheaf, **and he forgot** both sheaves, **the lower** sheaf is deemed to be **a forgotten sheaf, and the upper one is not** deemed to be **a forgotten sheaf. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Both of them are not** deemed to be **forgotten sheaves; the lower one because it is concealed, and the upper one because it is floating** and not directly touching the field. The Gemara points out: **They disagree only with regard to** the **lower** sheaf, **but with regard to** the **upper** one **all agree** that **it is not** deemed **a forgotten sheaf.** This demonstrates that a sheaf located in the airspace of the field is not deemed to be forgotten.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְזֵיק בֵּיהּ זְכָה בֵּיהּ. אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ? אֲפִילּוּ בַּשָּׂדֶה נָמֵי! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ״, מִשּׁוּם תַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **There it is different, as, since** the owner **had held it** to take it to the city, **he acquired it.** The reason it is not deemed to be a forgotten sheaf is not because it is not touching the field but because the owner had already acquired that particular sheaf before forgetting it. The Gemara raises an objection to this explanation: **If so, why specifically** state the case of a sheaf that was placed **on top of another? The same would hold true** in a case where one put the sheaf down directly **in the field as well,** as he has already acquired it. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so, and** the reason **that** the *baraita* **teaches: On top of another,** is **due to the lower** sheaf rather than the upper one, in order to teach the dispute concerning the lower sheaf.

Having worked through those exegetical debates, the *gemara* circles back to the question of *min b'mino* — whether being buried or suspended under an object of the same type affects the determination of *tamun* or *tzaf*. A *baraita* discusses the case of a sheaf that was picked up to be carried to the city, placed on top of a fellow sheaf, and then forgotten. The *tanna kamma* rules: *hatachton shichcha v'ha-elyon eino shichcha* — the bottom sheaf is *shichcha*, the top is not. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon says both are exempt: *ha-tachton mipnei she-hu tamun*, the bottom because it is buried, *v'ha-elyon mipnei she-hu tzaf*, the top because it is floating.

The *gemara* initially assumes that both *tannaim* agree with Rabbi Yehuda that *tamun* creates an exemption for *shichcha*, and that their debate is specifically about *min b'mino* — whether being buried under a similar object (another sheaf) counts as *tamun*. On this reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda holds that *min b'mino havei tamun*, while the *tanna kamma* holds it does not.

The *gemara* rejects this reading. If both agreed that *tamun* is an exemption, everyone would agree that being buried under another sheaf constitutes *tamun*, since that is clearly a form of burial. The more defensible reading is that they are not debating the definition of *tamun* at all — they are debating whether *tamun* creates any exemption from *shichcha* in the first place. The *tanna kamma* holds like the *rabbanan* — buried sheaves are still subject to *shichcha* — while Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda holds like his father Rabbi Yehuda — buried sheaves are exempt. The scenario involving one sheaf on top of another is chosen not to illustrate the minimum threshold of what counts as *tamun*, but to highlight the full scope of Rabbi Yehuda's position: *odi'acha kocho d'Rabbi Yehuda*, to show that even burial under a similar object — *min b'mino* — constitutes *tamun* in his view.

## Exposure & Location

### Nofel Ba'adamah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״חָלָל״ — וְלֹא חָנוּק, ״חָלָל״ — וְלֹא מְפַרְפֵּר, ״בָּאֲדָמָה״ — וְלֹא טָמוּן בַּגַּל, ״נֹפֵל״ — וְלֹא תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן, ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — וְלֹא צָף עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בְּכוּלָּן, אִם הָיָה חָלָל — עוֹרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss when the ritual of breaking the neck of the heifer is performed. **The Sages taught,** expounding the verse "If one be found slain in the land which the Lord your God has given you to possess it, lying in the field" (Deuteronomy 21:1): **"Slain"** indicates one killed by a sword, **but not** one who was **strangled; "slain," but not** one who was found **twitching** in his death throes; **"in the land," but not concealed in a pile** of stones; **"lying," but not hanging on a tree; "in the field," but not floating on the surface of the water. Rabbi Elazar says: In all these** cases, **if** a person **was slain** by the sword, the judges **break the neck** of the heifer, and it does not matter where the corpse was found.

As enumerated in the *baraita*, the body qualifying for *egla arufa* must be *nofel ba'adamah* — fallen and lying on the ground. If it is buried under a pile (*tamun be-gal*) or suspended from a tree (*taluy be-ilan*), the obligation does not apply. These requirements parallel, in their structure, the *tamun* and *tzaf* conditions explored in the *shichcha* discussion, though as demonstrated above, the derivations for *egla arufa* and *shichcha* proceed from different contextual starting points and cannot simply be read across from one to the other.

## Stacked Corpses

### Problem Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵינִי כְּבֶן עַזַּאי בְּשׁוּקֵי טְבֶרְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן לְאַבָּיֵי: שְׁנֵי חֲלָלִים זֶה עַל גַּבֵּי זֶה, מֵהֵיכָן הוּא מוֹדֵד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara tells of a related incident. On one occasion **Abaye said: I am hereby** as sharp and expert **as ben Azzai,** who taught Torah **in the markets of Tiberias,** and am ready to answer any question that might be posed to me. **One of the Sages said to Abaye,** with regard to the ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken: If there were **two slain people** found **one on top of the other** not precisely aligned, **from where does one measure** the distance to the surrounding cities?

Abaye, in a moment of confident self-assessment — declaring *hareini k'Ben Azai b'shukei Tveria*, I am ready to take on any question as Ben Azai was in the marketplaces of Tiberias — is challenged by Rabbah bar Abba with the following problem: *shenei chalalim zeh al gabei zeh* — two corpses, one lying on top of the other. *Meihechan hu moded* — from which one does one measure for the purposes of *egla arufa*, and does either, or both, or neither qualify for the ritual?

### Min B'Mino Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי טָמוּן, וּמִין בְּמִינוֹ לָא הָוֵי צָף, וּמֵעֶלְיוֹן מוֹדֵד. אוֹ דִּלְמָא: מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי צָף, וּמִין בְּמִינוֹ לָא הָוֵי טָמוּן, וּמִתַּחְתּוֹן מוֹדֵד. אוֹ דִּלְמָא: מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי טָמוּן, וּמִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי צָף, וְלֹא מִתַּחְתּוֹן מוֹדֵד וְלֹא מֵעֶלְיוֹן מוֹדֵד?
>
> **Translation:** The various aspects of the dilemma are as follows: **Is a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance** considered to be **concealed, and is a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance not** considered to be **floating,** which would mean that the lower, concealed corpse is ignored because one does not measure the distance from a concealed corpse, as taught in the mishna, **and one measures** the distance **from the upper one? Or perhaps a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered to be **floating, and a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is not** considered to be **concealed, and he measures** the distance **from the lower one,** because it is not considered to be concealed, but not from the upper one, which is considered to be floating. **Or perhaps a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered to be **concealed, and a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered to be **floating, and he** therefore **does not measure** the distance **from the lower one nor does he measure** the distance **from the upper one.** According to this last option, in such a situation no measuring is done, and the ritual is not performed.

The question unfolds into multiple possibilities. Does one say *min b'mino havei tamun* — that being under another corpse counts as buried — such that the bottom corpse is excluded, while *min b'mino lavei tzaf* — being on top of another corpse does not count as suspended — leaving the top corpse as the qualified one, and one measures from it? Or conversely, *min b'mino havei tzaf* — the top one is considered suspended — but *min b'mino lavei tamun* — the bottom one is not considered buried — leaving the bottom as the qualified one? Or *min b'mino havei tamun u'min b'mino havei tzaf* — both determinations apply — leaving neither corpse qualifying, and no *egla arufa* is performed at all? Rashi adds the implicit fourth possibility: that *min b'mino* does not count as either buried or suspended, and both corpses qualify independently, potentially each triggering an obligation tied to a different nearby city.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵיתוּהָ: עוֹמֶר שֶׁהֶחְזִיק בּוֹ לְהוֹלִיכוֹ לָעִיר, וְהִנִּיחוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ וּשְׁכָחוֹ — הַתַּחְתּוֹן שִׁכְחָה וְהָעֶלְיוֹן אֵינוֹ שִׁכְחָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שְׁנֵיהֶן אֵינָן שִׁכְחָה, הַתַּחְתּוֹן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא טָמוּן, וְהָעֶלְיוֹן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא צָף. סַבְרוּהָ דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לְהוּ, דְּאָמַר: ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — פְּרָט לְטָמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **You learned it** in the *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Pe'a* 3:7): In the case of **a sheaf that had been held** by its owner in order **to take it to the city, and he placed it on top of another** sheaf, **and he forgot** both sheaves, **the lower** sheaf is deemed to be **a forgotten sheaf, and the upper one is not** deemed to be **a forgotten sheaf. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Both of them are not** deemed to be **forgotten sheaves; the lower one because it is concealed and the upper one because it is floating** and not directly touching the field. The Sages **assumed that these *tanna'im*** in the *baraita* basically **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who **says** that the phrase **"in the field"** serves **to exclude a concealed** sheaf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי טָמוּן, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא הָוֵי טָמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues Abaye's statement: **What, is it not** the case **that they disagree about this, as** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered to be **concealed,** and therefore the lower sheaf is not deemed to be a forgotten sheaf, **and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **it is not** considered to be **concealed,** which means it is deemed to be a forgotten sheaf. The same dispute would apply to the two corpses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לְהוּ, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי טָמוּן, וְהָכָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבָּנַן כְּרַבָּנַן. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this claim: **No, if they hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** then it could be **that everyone agrees** that **a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered to be **concealed,** and they would maintain this with regard to the two corpses as well. **And here they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis,** in **that** the opinion of **the Rabbis** of this *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** there, which claims that the *halakha* of a forgotten sheaf applies even to a concealed sheaf, **and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that a concealed sheaf is exempt from the *halakha* of forgotten sheaves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ? אֲפִילּוּ בֶּעָפָר וּבִצְרוֹר נָמֵי! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וּלְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִין בְּמִינוֹ הָוֵי טָמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** that is **so,** that their dispute is with regard to the *halakha* of concealed sheaves, **why** did they specifically disagree in the case of a sheaf that was **on top of another** sheaf; **the same would hold true even** in a case where the sheaf was concealed **in dirt and pebbles?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** They disagree with regard to all concealed sheaves, **and** their dispute is stated with regard to a case of one sheaf on top of another in order **to convey to you the far-reaching** nature of the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **even a substance** that is in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance is** considered **concealed.** Therefore, a substance concealed in a different type of matter is all the more so considered concealed.

Abaye responds by analogy to the sheaf *baraita*: the case of the sheaf placed on top of another sheaf and forgotten, where the bottom is *shichcha* and the top is not. At minimum, the *tanna kamma* of that *baraita* holds that the top sheaf is exempt while the bottom qualifies. This, Abaye suggests, reflects the debate about *min b'mino* that we explored in the *shichcha* context. However, the *gemara* has already established that the top sheaf's exemption in that case derives not from its being suspended but from the fact that the carrier had taken physical possession of it — *keivan de'achzik bah zachah bah* — and taken possession removes the category of *shichcha* entirely, regardless of where the sheaf comes to rest. The question of whether *min b'mino* suspension exempts for *egla arufa* therefore remains formally unresolved.

## Exposure & Location…

### Ba'Sadeh Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא סָמוּךְ לַסְּפָר אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ נׇכְרִים אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. אֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא לְעִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: If a corpse **was found close to the border** of the country **or** close **to a city in which the majority** of its inhabitants **are gentiles, or** close **to a city that is without** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges, the judges **would not break the heifer's neck.** Additionally, the Elders **measure** the distance from the corpse **only to a city that has** a rabbinical **court** with twenty-three judges.

The *baraita* further specifies *ba-sadeh* — the body must be in a field. The verse *ki yimatzei chalal ba'adamah* is read to exclude a body floating on the surface of water: *v'lo tzaf al penei ha-mayim*. Being on water is not *ba-sadeh* and not *ba'adamah*.

### Rabbi Elazar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״חָלָל״ — וְלֹא חָנוּק, ״חָלָל״ — וְלֹא מְפַרְפֵּר, ״בָּאֲדָמָה״ — וְלֹא טָמוּן בַּגַּל, ״נֹפֵל״ — וְלֹא תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן, ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — וְלֹא צָף עַל גַּבֵּי מַיִם. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״חָלָל״ יַתִּירָא כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** the technical requirements: From the various words in the verse they derive: **"Slain"** means one killed by a sword, **but not** one who was **strangled; "slain,"** again, means **but not** one who was found **twitching** in his death throes; **"in the land," but not concealed in a pile** of stones; **"lying," but not hanging on a tree; "in the field," but not floating on the surface of water. **And Rabbi Elazar** holds that those other situations are not excluded, and that because in that first case the Torah **writes "slain" an extra** time in the next verse: "About him that is slain" (Deuteronomy 21:2), this repetition teaches that a victim of strangulation is not included in this *halakha*.

Rabbi Elazar dissents from the collection of technical exclusions. He holds: *be-chulan im hayu chalal orfin* — in all of these scenarios, as long as the body is a *chalal* — killed by a sword — the *egla arufa* obligation applies. He does not accept the technical exemptions based on burial, suspension, or location on water. The *gemara* challenges him: surely he agrees that a strangled victim lying on a garbage heap would not trigger *egla arufa*, since the requirement of *chalal v'lo chanuk* — a sword-slain victim, not a strangled one — is derived from the repeated use of the word *chalal* throughout the *parasha*, and he accepts that *derasha*. If so, he should similarly accept the *drashot* derived from *ba'adamah v'lo tamun be-gal*, *nofel v'lo taluy be-ilan*, and *ba-sadeh v'lo tzaf al gabei mayim*. Rabbi Elazar replies: *chalal yeteira k'tiv* — the word *chalal* is written multiple times in the *parasha*, and he therefore gives weight to the *derasha* of *chalal v'lo chanuk*. The other words — *nofel*, *ba'adamah*, *ba-sadeh* — he does not accord the same exegetical significance.

### Border Town Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא סָמוּךְ לַסְּפָר אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ נׇכְרִים כּוּ׳. דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא״ — פְּרָט לְמָצוּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: If a corpse **was found close to the border** of the country **or** close **to a city in which the majority** of its inhabitants **are gentiles,** the judges would not break the heifer's neck, **as it is written: "If one be found** slain" (Deuteronomy 21:1). This **excludes** places where murdered bodies **are** commonly **found,** such as the aforementioned locations.

If the body is found near a border town (*samuch le-sefer*) or near a city whose majority population is non-Jewish, *egla arufa* is not performed. The source is *d'chtiv ki yimatzei* — "if it is found" — *perat le-matzui*, excluding what is commonly found. Along a border with a hostile neighboring territory, corpses are a frequent occurrence from border conflicts; in a majority non-Jewish city under Roman rule, the unfortunate reality is that murders were not uncommon. In either case, the finding of a body is not a rare event that demands the extraordinary national response of *egla arufa*.

### Beis Din Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין. דְּבָעֵינָא ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר״, וְלֵיכָּא. אֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא לְעִיר כּוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! כֵּיוָן דִּתְנָא לְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין, אֲנָא יָדַעְנָא דְּאֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא לְעִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **Or** if the victim was discovered close **to a city that is without** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges, they would not measure the distance to that city. The Gemara explains: This is **because** the verse **requires "the Elders of that city"** (Deuteronomy 21:3), **and** this is **not** the case here; therefore the rite was not performed. The mishna also taught that the Elders **measure** the distance from the corpse **only to a city** that contains a rabbinical court of twenty-three judges. The Gemara asks: This **is obvious. Since** the mishna **taught** that they do not measure the distance **to a city that does not have** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges, **I know** that **they measure** the distance **only to a city that has** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם נִמְצָא סָמוּךְ לְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין, שֶׁמַּנִּיחִין אוֹתָהּ וּמוֹדְדִין לְעִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלָקְחוּ זִקְנֵי הָעִיר הָהִיא״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** *tanna* **teaches us** the *halakha* **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that if** the corpse **was found close to a city that does not have** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges, **that they leave** the city aside **and measure** the distance from the corpse **to a city that has** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges? **The verse states: "And the Elders of that city shall take"** (Deuteronomy 21:3), which indicates that the Elders of a city are involved **in any case,** and the measurement is taken even if it is not to the city closest to the body.

Similarly, if the body is found near a city that has no *beis din*, the ritual is not performed — *d'ba'ina ziknei ha'ir v'leika*, because the verse requires the *ziknei ha'ir* to participate, and without a *beis din* there are no *zekeinim* of that city in the relevant sense. This leads to the final ruling: *ein modedin ela le-ir she-yesh bah beis din* — one measures only to a city that has a *beis din*. The *gemara* notes that this might appear obvious given the preceding rule, but it carries an independent teaching: if the closest city lacks a *beis din*, one does not simply abandon the entire ritual — one passes over that city and measures to the next-closest city that does have a *beis din*. The *talmud lomar* derives this from *v'lakchu ziknei ha-ir ha-hi mi-kol makom* — the *zekeinim* of *that* city, wherever it may be relative to the body, take responsibility. The point is not merely topographical proximity but judicial accountability: responsibility must remain anchored to a body capable of bearing it, even if that body is not the geographically nearest settlement.
