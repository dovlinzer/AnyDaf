# Zevachim 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Begadim Fit & Coverage

### Invalid Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְטוּשְׁטָשִׁין אוֹ מְקוֹרָעִין – וְעָבַד; עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **soiled or ripped, and he performed** sacrificial **rites, his service is disqualified.**

The daf opens with the continuation of a broader discussion about the *bigdei kehuna* — the priestly garments — and the ways in which deficiencies in those garments render the *avoda* invalid. The Gemara had already explored a range of fitting questions: what happens if the garments are too long or too short, if they are held up by the belt so that they do not reach the proper position, or if they are the correct size only because they are being gathered. All of these scenarios bear on the fundamental requirement that a *kohen* must wear his prescribed garments in the prescribed manner. The *avoda* performed without the proper *begadim* — *chutz mibegadim* — is *pasul*.

### Bandaging on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: כֹּהֵן שֶׁלָּקָה בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ – כּוֹרֵךְ עָלֶיהָ גֶּמִי בְּמִקְדָּשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא בַּמְּדִינָה. וְאִם לְהוֹצִיא מִמֶּנָּה דָּם – כָּאן וְכָאן אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the priestly vestments, **we learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Eiruvin* 103b): **A priest who was injured on his finger** on Shabbat may temporarily **wrap it with a reed** so that his wound is not visible while he is serving in the Temple. This leniency applies **in the Temple, but not in** the rest of **the country,** as the reed also heals the wound, and medical treatment is prohibited on Shabbat by rabbinic decree. **But if** his intention is **to draw blood from** the wound, **it is prohibited** both **here and there.**

The Gemara cites a teaching: *kohen she'laka be'etzba'o* — a *kohen* who sustained a cut on his finger. We are not dealing with a *psul* of the service per se at the outset; the concern is more fundamental hygiene of the Temple rite. The *kohen* may wrap the wound with a *gemi* (a light cloth bandage) in the *Beis Hamikdash*, even on *Shabbos* — *bamikdash aval lo bamedina* — though not outside, where the prohibition applies. The reason this is an issue on *Shabbos* at all is that keeping a wound covered assists the healing process and constitutes a form of *refuah*, which is rabbinically prohibited on *Shabbos*. However, *ein shevus bamikdash* — the general principle that rabbinic restrictions are waived within the Temple — licenses the practice there. It is worth noting the apparent irony: the holiest place on the holiest day grants more latitude, not less. The explanation is that *kohanim* are presumed to be scrupulous regarding Torah-level prohibitions, and the rabbinic fence need not be extended into that domain.

The Gemara clarifies that if, while wrapping the wound, the *kohen* intends to squeeze out blood, this is forbidden in both locations — *kan vekan assur* — because that crosses into the category of *chavala*, causing a wound, which is an independent prohibition entirely unrelated to the rabbinic healing concern.

### Extra Garments Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא גֶּמִי, אֲבָל צִילְצוֹל קָטָן – הָוֵי יִתּוּר בְּגָדִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא אָמְרוּ יִתּוּר בְּגָדִים אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים – לָא הָוֵי יִתּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: They taught only** that **a reed** is permitted. **But a small sash [*tziltzul*]** as a bandage **is** considered **an extra garment** and is therefore forbidden, since it is prohibited for a priest to add to the priestly vestments prescribed by the Torah. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They said** that wearing **extra garments** is prohibited **only** if the extra garment is worn **in a place** on the priest's body where the requisite **vestments** are worn. **But** if the sash is **in a place** on his body where the **vestments** are **not** worn, e.g., on his hand, it **is not** considered **an extra** garment.

The Gemara now pivots to the *begadim* issue raised by the case of the bandage. *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya* teaches: *lo shanu ela gemi* — the ruling that the bandage is permissible applies only to a light cloth bandage. But *tziltzul katan havei yitur begadim* — if the item is something more garment-like, such as a small decorative band or bracelet-like object, it constitutes an extra garment — *yitur begadim* — and is therefore forbidden. A *kohen* may wear only the prescribed *bigdei kehuna*; anything additional, if it constitutes a *beged*, invalidates.

This naturally raises the question of whether a *kohen* could perform the *avoda* wearing a ring, eyeglasses, or other everyday items. The *Gemara* will develop these questions further, but the initial framework is that any item that qualifies as a *beged* creates a problem.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and introduces a critical distinction: *lo amru yitur begadim ela bemakom begadim* — the prohibition of wearing an extra garment applies only to those parts of the body where the *bigdei kehuna* are designed to cover. On parts of the body not covered by the priestly garments — such as a finger, or the face — *shelo bemakom begadim lo havei yitur*, wearing something there does not constitute an extra garment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה! בִּשְׂמֹאל. אִי נָמֵי, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **derive** that a sash is prohibited **because** it acts as **an interposition** between the priest's hand and the sacred vessel he grips, which disqualifies the service. The Gemara rejects this: Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to a case where the wound is **on** the priest's **left** hand. Since the entire service is performed exclusively with his right hand, a bandage on his left hand is not an interposition. **Alternatively,** the wound is on the priest's right hand, but **not in a place** used for the **service,** such that the bandage does not interpose between his hand and the sacred vessel.

The Gemara then raises the obvious challenge: even granting *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position on *yitur begadim*, why isn't the bandage problematic as a *chatzitza* — an interposition between the *kohen*'s hand and whatever he is handling during the *avoda*? The principle that the service must be performed *be'itzumo shel kohen* — directly by the essence of the *kohen* himself, with direct skin contact — would seem to apply. The Gemara resolves this: *bismolo* — the wound is on the left hand, and the *avoda* is performed with the right. Alternatively, the bandage is positioned on the back of the hand, which *Rashi* notes is not the operative surface that contacts the vessels or blood. These answers point to two distinct categories of concern: *chatzitza* between the skin and the object of the *avoda*, and *yitur begadim* as a separate issue applying even where no direct *avoda* contact occurs.

## Chatzitza & Size Limits

### Single Thread Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים – אֲפִילּוּ נִימָא אַחַת חוֹצֶצֶת. שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים – שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ חוֹצְצוֹת, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן אֵינָן חוֹצְצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagrees** with the opinion **of Rava, as Rava says** that **Rav Ḥisda says: In a place** on the priest's body where the **vestments** are worn, **even one** extra **thread interposes** and is prohibited, whereas **in a place** on his body where the **vestments** are **not** worn, if the fabric is **three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths **it interposes,** but if it is **less than that it does not interpose.**

*Rava*'s position sharpens the entire discussion considerably. He teaches — citing *Rav Chisda* — that *bemakom begadim afilu nima achas chotzetzes* — in the place where the priestly garments are worn, even a single thread constitutes a *chatzitza*. The rationale derives from the verse *michnesei vad yihyu al besaro* — the garments must rest directly upon the skin. Any intervening object, however small, violates this requirement. A loose thread, a hair that has separated from the body and settled on the skin — all of these are problematic.

It is worth pausing to note, as *Rashi* flags throughout this section, that the term *chatzitza* is used somewhat loosely in the Gemara. In its technically precise sense, *chatzitza* refers to interposition either between the garment and the skin, or between the skin and the object of the *avoda*. But sometimes the Gemara uses the term to describe what is functionally a *yitur begadim* problem. The reader must attend carefully to context.

### Three-by-Three Rule

*Rava* continues: *shelo bemakom begadim shalosh al shalosh chotzetzes, pachos mikan eina chotzetzes* — in a place on the body where the priestly garments are not worn, something constitutes a problem only if it is at least three fingerbreadths by three fingerbreadths in size. This measurement is the standard threshold in *hilchos tumah vetaharah* for a piece of cloth to be classified as a significant garment. Below that threshold, the item does not rise to the level of a *beged* and presents no concern of *yitur begadim*.

The positions can be mapped out as follows. According to *Rava*: where the *begadim* are worn, even a single *nima* is a *chatzitza*; where they are not worn, the threshold is three by three for *yitur begadim*. According to *Rabbi Yochanan* (in his initial formulation): where the *begadim* are worn, a genuine alternative *beged* would be required to constitute *yitur begadim*; where they are not worn, there is no concern at all. According to *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya*: the determining factor for the second zone is whether the item is a functional *tziltzul* — something with genuine garment character — regardless of its size.

### Reconciling Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – וַדַּאי פְּלִיגָא; אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא מִי לֵימָא דִּפְלִיגָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: Rava **certainly disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that fabric of any size that is in a place on his body where the vestments are not worn is not considered an interposition. **Shall we say** that **he** also **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who deems any sash an interposition, even one smaller than three by three fingerbreadths?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי צִילְצוֹל קָטָן, דַּחֲשִׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Even according to Rava, **a small sash is different, as it is significant,** and it is therefore considered a garment even if it is less than three by three fingerbreadths.

The Gemara asks whether *Rava* necessarily disagrees with *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya*. The answer is that they need not be in conflict: *shani tziltzul katan dechashiv* — a small *tziltzul* is significant because of its inherent garment character. The two positions represent two independent criteria for classifying something as a *beged*: functional character (*tziltzul*) and minimum size (three by three). These are complementary rather than contradictory standards, and either one could render an item a *beged* for the purposes of *yitur begadim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא גֶּמִי, אֲבָל צִילְצוֹל קָטָן – חוֹצֵץ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא אָמְרוּ חֲצִיצָה בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ – אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם בְּגָדִים – שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ חוֹצְצוֹת, פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן אֵינָהּ חוֹצֶצֶת. וְהַיְינוּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that there is **another version** of the dispute: **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says** that **they taught only** that **a reed** is permitted, **but a small sash interposes. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They said** that an item acts as **an interposition when** it is **less than three by three** fingerbreadths **only in a place** on the priest's body where the **vestments** are worn. **But in a place** on his body where the **vestments** are **not** worn, the following distinction applies: If the fabric is **three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths, **it interposes,** but if it is **less than this, it does not interpose. And this is** the same ruling **that Rava** says that **Rav Ḥisda says.**

The Gemara then presents a second formulation (*lishana acharina*) of the positions. In this version, *Rabbi Yochanan* himself takes a stance very close to *Rava*: *bemakom begadim* even less than three by three is problematic, while *shelo bemakom begadim* requires three by three — *ve'hainu de'Rava*, which the Gemara notes is effectively identical to *Rava*'s position. The difference between this version of *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya* is that *Rabbi Yochanan* rejects the *tziltzul* criterion as independently sufficient below the three-by-three threshold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מַאי אִירְיָא גֶּמִי? לַשְׁמְעִינַן צִילְצוֹל קָטָן! מִילְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּגֶמִי מַסֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, why** does the mishna teach the *halakha* **specifically** with regard to **a reed? Let** the mishna **teach us** that a priest may wrap his wounded finger with **a small sash,** since this would teach the greater novelty that although a sash is significant, it does not constitute an interposition. The Gemara responds: **It teaches us a matter in passing, that a reed heals.**

The Gemara raises a question: according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, why did the *Mishnah* in *hilchos Shabbos* specifically choose the case of a *gemi* rather than illustrating the rule with a small *tziltzul*, which would also be permitted? The answer is: *milta agav urchia kamashma lan de'gemi masi* — the *Mishnah*'s interest was in teaching the law of *Shabbos* healing; it chose the bandage specifically because it wanted to convey that this material actually promotes healing, which is why it is permitted in the *Beis Hamikdash* but not outside it. Incidentally teaching a *chiddush* in *bigdei kehuna* was not its purpose.

## Objects & Chatzitza

### Air Gaps & Normal Wear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: נִכְנְסָה לוֹ רוּחַ בְּבִגְדוֹ, מַהוּ? ״עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן – וְהָא לֵיכָּא; אוֹ דִלְמָא דֶּרֶךְ לְבִישָׁה בְּכָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raises a dilemma:** If a gust of **wind entered** the priest's **vestment,** raising it slightly off his body, **what is** the *halakha*? Do **we require** that the vestment be: **"Upon his body"** (Leviticus 6:3), in a literal sense, **and this** is **not** the case when the wind raises his vestment? **Or perhaps** the service is valid because **this** is the normal **manner of wearing** clothes.

Having established the framework, *Rava* proceeds to pose a series of open-ended questions about what precisely constitutes a *chatzitza*. The first: *nichnesa lo ruach bevigdo mahu* — if a wind lifts the tunic slightly off the leg, creating a momentary air gap between garment and skin, is this a *chatzitza*? The Gemara frames the question as: do we require *al besaro* to be understood literally at every point of contact, or does *derech levisha bekach* — the normal manner of wearing a garment — include the natural and unavoidable variation in contact? The logical answer is clearly the latter; no garment maintains uniform contact with every inch of skin at all times, and to demand otherwise would make the priesthood functionally impossible.

### Lice Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּינָּה – מַהוּ שֶׁתָּחוֹץ?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to **a louse** found under the priest's vestments? **Does it interpose** between the vestments and his body, disqualifying the service?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתָה לָא תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּוַדַּאי חָיְיצָא; חַיָּה מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן (דאתא) [דְּאָתְיָא] וְאָזְלָא – רְבִיתָא הִיא וְלָא חָיְיצָא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּקָפֵיד עֲלַהּ – חָיְיצָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Do not raise a dilemma** with regard to **a dead** louse, **as** it **certainly interposes,** like any other item. Rather, **what** is the *halakha* with regard to **a live** louse? **Do we say** that **since it comes and goes,** i.e., it moves around on his body, **it is** like **a growth and does not interpose? Or perhaps, since he objects to its** presence, **it interposes?**

The next question: *kinah mahu shetachlotz* — what is the status of a louse on the *kohen*'s skin between the garment and the body? *Metah lo tiba'i lach* — a dead louse requires no deliberation; it is clearly a *chatzitza*, as it is something unwanted on the body. *Chayah mai* — but a living louse? *Mi amrinan keivan d'azla ve'atya ravita hi* — perhaps since it moves about on its own, it is considered quasi-organic and not truly an interposition? *Rashi* notes that the argument would be analogous to something that is part of one's body — it comes and goes naturally. Or: *keivan dekapid aleih chotzetzet* — since one obviously objects to its presence, it is a *chatzitza*. The question is left open.

Similar unresolved questions are raised about fine dust (*avak afar*) on the skin and about the armpit area, where the structure of the garment necessarily creates a permanent air pocket.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָפָר – מַהוּ שֶׁיָּחוֹץ? עָפָר וַדַּאי חָיֵיץ! אֶלָּא אֲבַק עָפָר מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to **dirt** found under the priest's vestments? **Does it interpose?** The Gemara objects: But **dirt certainly interposes.** The Gemara clarifies: **Rather,** the question is: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **dust of dirt,** i.e., a minute amount of dust?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הַשֶּׁחִי – מַהוּ שֶׁיָּחוֹץ? ״עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן – וְהָא לֵיכָּא; אוֹ דִּלְמָא דֶּרֶךְ לְבִישָׁה בְּכָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the gap between the underarm of the vestment and the priest's **armpit? Does it interpose?** Do **we require** that the vestment is **"upon his body"** in a literal sense, **and this** is **not** the case? **Or perhaps** the service is valid since **this** is the normal **manner of wearing** clothes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִכְנִיס יָדוֹ לְתוֹךְ חֵיקוֹ, מַהוּ? גּוּפוֹ מִי חָיֵיץ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, if the priest **inserted his hand into** his vestments and touched **his chest, what is** the *halakha*? **Does his body interpose or not?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִימָא – מַהוּ שֶׁתָּחוֹץ? נִימָא וַדַּאי חָיְיצָא! אֶלָּא נִימָא מְדוּלְדֶּלֶת מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to **a thread [*nima*]? Does it interpose?** The Gemara interjects: But **a thread certainly interposes. Rather,** the question is: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a thread** that **hangs** off the vestment itself and will soon fall off? Is such a thread considered as though it has already detached from the vestment, in which case it interposes?

### Hair as Barrier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: יָצָא שְׂעָרוֹ בְּבִגְדוֹ, מַהוּ? שְׂעָרוֹ כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָאו כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If **his hair emerged** from his head and extended **into his vestment** and separated it from his skin, **what is** the *halakha*? **Is his hair considered like his body,** in which case it does not interpose, **or is it not considered like his body?**

*Mar bar Rav Ashi* poses the question: *yatza se'aro bevigdo mahu* — if the *kohen*'s hair exits through the garment and lies between the garment and the skin, what is the status? *Se'ar kegufo dami* — is hair to be considered an extension of the body, and therefore not an interposition, in the same way one might argue that inserting one's own hand into the fold of a garment (*hichnis yado letoch cheiko*) does not constitute *chatzitza* because *gufo michai*? Or is hair sufficiently separate from the body that it functions as a barrier? These too are left unresolved by the Gemara.

### Tefillin Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: תְּפִילִּין – מַהוּ שֶׁיָּחוֹצּוּ? אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לַיְלָה לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא – לָא תִּבְעֵי לָךְ; כֵּיוָן דְּלַיְלָה חָיְיצִי, יוֹם נָמֵי חָיְיצִי. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין; מַאי? מִצְוָה דְגוּפֵיהּ – חָיֵיץ אוֹ לָא חָיֵיץ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **phylacteries? Do they interpose?** The Gemara clarifies: **According to** the opinion **of one who says** that **night is not** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries, do not raise the dilemma. Since they interpose** at **night, they also interpose** during the **day.** Rather, **when you raise the dilemma,** do so **according to the one who says** that **night is** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries.** According to this opinion, **what** is the *halakha*? **Does a mitzva** that one fulfills **with his body interpose, or does it not interpose?**

The Gemara raises the case of *tefillin*. *Rashi* clarifies that the question here is not primarily about *chatzitza* in the sense of interposition between the skin and the object of the *avoda*, but rather whether *tefillin* worn during the *avoda* constitute an extra *beged* — *yitur begadim* — or a *chatzitza* between the garment and the skin.

The Gemara distinguishes by opinion: *aliba deman d'amar layla lav zman tefillin* — according to the view that night is not the time of the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*, *lo tibay lach* — there is no question. At night, *tefillin* are worn without a *mitzvah* context, making them plainly a foreign garment. *Keivan de'layla chaitz yom nami chaitz* — since at night they constitute an extra *beged*, and the daytime *avoda* is presumably more weighty, they would remain a problem during the day as well. The real question arises *lema'an d'amar layla zman tefillin* — according to the view that *tefillin* may always be worn. In that case, are *tefillin* to be viewed as a perpetual *mitzvah* object — perhaps comparable in status to the body itself — or as a foreign *beged*? *Mitzvah degupei chaitz o lo chaitz?*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, וּמְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תַּלְמוּד עָרוּךְ הוּא בְּיָדֵינוּ: תְּפִילִּין חוֹצְצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** This **matter circulated and** eventually **came before Rabbi Ami,** who **said to him: It is a settled tradition in our possession** that **phylacteries interpose.**

The matter reached *Rabbi Ami*, who ruled decisively: *talmud aruch hu beyadenu tefillin chotzetzet* — we have a longstanding teaching that *tefillin* do constitute a *chatzitza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כֹּהֲנִים בַּעֲבוֹדָתָן ולְוִיִּם בְּדוּכָנָן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַעֲמָדָן – פְּטוּרִין מִן הַתְּפִלָּה וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין. מַאי, לָאו אִם הִנִּיחָן אֵינָן חוֹצְצוֹת? לָא, אִם הִנִּיחָן חוֹצְצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Priests in their service; and Levites on their platform** in the Temple, where they recite songs; **and Israelites at their watches,** where they observe the sacrifice of the daily offering, **are** all **exempt from prayer and from** donning **phylacteries. What, is it not** that the term: Exempt, indicates that **if they donned** phylacteries anyway, **they do not interpose?** Apparently, priests may wear phylacteries while performing the Temple service. The Gemara responds: **No, if they donned** phylacteries, **they interpose.**

A *braita* is then raised in apparent contradiction: when *kohanim* are performing their *avoda*, *levi'im* are on their platform, and *Yisraelim* are present at the *ma'amad*, they are *peturim* — exempt — from wearing *tefillin*. This implies they could wear *tefillin* without invalidating. The Gemara resolves this: the *braita* says *peturim* rather than *asurin* because the ruling applies equally to *levi'im* and *Yisrael*, for whom there is no question of invalidation; the language of exemption rather than prohibition accommodates all three groups, even though for a *kohen* performing the *avoda*, wearing *tefillin* would in fact invalidate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״פְּטוּרִים״?! ״אֲסוּרִים״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרָאֵל – דְּלָא מַתְנוּ לֵיהּ ״אָסוּר״, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי תְּנָא ״פְּטוּרִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does the *baraita* use the word: **Exempt? It should have** used the word: **Prohibited,** since wearing phylacteries disqualifies the priests' service. The Gemara responds: **Since there are** also **Levites and Israelites** mentioned in the *baraita*, **concerning whom** the *baraita* could **not teach** the word: **Prohibited,** as it is permitted for them to don phylacteries, **due to that** reason the *baraita* **taught** the word: **Exempt,** which is applicable to all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אִם הִנִּיחָן אֵינָן חוֹצְצוֹת! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּיָד, הָא דְּרֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **If** a priest **donned** phylacteries **they do not interpose?** The Gemara responds: That is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that phylacteries interpose is referring to the phylacteries **of the hand,** whereas **that** *baraita*, which teaches that they do not is referring to the phylacteries **of the head.**

The Gemara then reconciles an explicit *braita* stating that *im henicham einam chotzetzim* — wearing *tefillin* does not constitute a *chatzitza* — by distinguishing between *tefillin shel yad* and *tefillin shel rosh*. The *shel yad* is a *chatzitza*, since it is positioned where the priestly garment must rest directly against the skin. The *shel rosh* is not a *chatzitza* — there is no garment pressing against that part of the head — and does not constitute *yitur begadim* either, because the *mitznefet* does not cover the entire skull, and the space between the *tzitz* and the *mitznefet* is where the *kohen gadol*'s hair was visible, and it is there that *tefillin* are placed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא דְּיָד, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִלְבַּשׁ עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ״ – שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּינוֹ לִבְשָׂרוֹ; דְּרֹאשׁ נָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ הַמִּצְנֶפֶת עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about the phylacteries **of the hand** that only they interpose? The verse indicates the difference, **as it is written** with regard to the vestments covering the body: **"Shall he put upon his body"** (Leviticus 6:3), indicating **that nothing may interpose between** the vestment **and his body.** The Gemara challenges: But there is **also** a verse **written with regard to the head: "And you shall set the mitre upon his head"** (Exodus 29:6), indicating that there must be no interposition between the mitre and the head. If so, the phylacteries of the head should be considered an interposition as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: שְׂעָרוֹ הָיָה נִרְאֶה בֵּין צִיץ לְמִצְנֶפֶת, שֶׁשָּׁם מַנִּיחִין תְּפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The Sages **taught: The hair of** the High Priest **was visible between the frontplate and the mitre.** The frontplate was set on the forehead, below the hairline, while the mitre was set above it; it was **there that** the High Priests would **don** their **phylacteries.** Consequently, the phylacteries of the head did not interpose between the mitre and the priest's head.

## Purification & Washing

### Mechusar Kippurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִפֶּר עָלֶיהָ הַכֹּהֵן וְטָהֵרָה״ – ״טָהֵרָה״ מִכְּלָל שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that rites performed by **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete the purification process are disqualified. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rav Huna says: The verse states** with regard to the offering brought by a woman who has given birth: **"And the priest shall make atonement for her, and she shall be pure"** (Leviticus 12:8). Since the verse states: "And **she shall be pure,"** one learns **by inference that she is ritually impure** to some extent until she brings her offering, even though she has already immersed. Anyone who has not yet brought an atonement offering is likewise impure to some extent, and rites performed by one who is impure are disqualified (see 17a).

The Gemara moves to another category of invalidation: *mechusar kippurim* — one who has completed the immersion process but has not yet brought the required *korban* that finalizes the *tahara* process. Such a person — whether a *zav*, a *yoledes*, or any other individual whose purification requires a concluding sacrifice — remains in a state of personal *tumah* until that offering is brought. *Rav Huna* derives this from the verse *vichiper aleha hakohen vetahera* — the *kohen* shall atone for her and she will become *tehorah* — which implies that until the atonement is effected, her status of *tumah* persists. Since a *tamei* person performing the *avoda* invalidates it, a *mechusar kippurim* doing so likewise invalidates.

### Morning Washing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁלֹּא רָחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם. אָתְיָא ״חוּקָּה״–״חוּקָּה״ מִמְּחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that rites performed by **one whose hands and feet are not washed** are disqualified. The Gemara explains: This *halakha* **is derived** by verbal analogy between **"statute"** mentioned in this context and **"statute" from** the case of **one lacking** the requisite **vestments,** whose rites are disqualified (see 17b).

The next item on the Mishnah's list: *shelo rachutz yadayim veraglayim* — a *kohen* who did not wash his hands and feet before beginning the service. This too renders the *avoda* invalid. The derivation proceeds by a *gezeira shava* — the word *chok* — linking the washing requirement to the requirement for *bigdei kehuna*, which is itself linked to a *chukas olam* formulation. Just as lacking the proper *begadim* invalidates, so too does lacking the morning washing.

## Yom Kippur Washings

### Teaching Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁלֹּא טָבַל וְשֶׁלֹּא קִידֵּשׁ בֵּין בֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד וּבֵין עֲבוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה, וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאֶחָד כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁלֹּא קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו שַׁחֲרִית, וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **a High Priest who did not immerse or did not sanctify** his hands and feet during the Yom Kippur service **between** donning the golden **garments and** the white linen **garments, or between** performance of one **rite and** another **rite, and he performed** the **service** in this state, **his service is valid** after the fact. **But** with regard to **either a High Priest or an ordinary priest who did not sanctify his hands and feet** at all in the **morning and performed** the **service, his service is disqualified.**

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that defines the scope of the washing requirement with precision, particularly as applied to the *Yom Kippur* service. On *Yom Kippur*, the *kohen gadol* changes his garments five times and is required, as described explicitly in the Torah, to immerse in the *mikveh* and wash his hands before and after each change — potentially ten washings in total over the course of the day. The *baraita* rules: *kohen gadol shelo taval veshelo kidesh bein beged lebeged uvein avoda la'avoda ve'avad — avodaso kesheira* — if the *kohen gadol* omitted these intermediate immersions and washings between the garment changes, the *avoda* remains valid. By contrast: *ve'echad kohen gadol ve'echad kohen hedyot shelo kidesh yadav veraglav shacharis ve'avad — avodaso pesulah* — whether a *kohen gadol* or an ordinary *kohen*, if the initial morning washing was not performed, the *avoda* is invalid.

The *chiddush* is the first clause. One might have assumed — given that *Yom Kippur* is governed by the phrase *chukas olam*, meaning that every prescribed element of the service is indispensable — that missing an intermediate washing would also invalidate. The question is why it does not.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַסִּי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִכְּדִי חָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת וַעֲשָׂרָה קִדּוּשִׁין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְ״חוּקָּה״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ – לִיעַכְּבוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Now,** there are **five immersions and ten sanctifications** of the hands and feet during the Yom Kippur service **by Torah law, and** the word: **"Statute"** (Leviticus 16:34), **is written with regard to them.** Accordingly, **they should be indispensable** and should disqualify the service if not performed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּלְבֵשָׁם״ – לְבִישָׁה מְעַכֶּבֶת, וְאֵין דָּבָר אַחֵר מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** After stipulating that the High Priest must wear the requisite vestments before performing the Yom Kippur service, **the verse states: "And put them on"** (Leviticus 16:4). This superfluous term serves to indicate that **wearing** the requisite vestments **is indispensable, but nothing else is indispensable.**

*Rav Assi* poses this question directly to *Rabbi Yochanan*: the five immersions and ten washings are *de'Oraita*, and the word *chok* is written regarding *Yom Kippur* — which ordinarily signals that every detail is *le'akeiv*, preventing validity if omitted. Why then do the intermediate washings not invalidate?

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds with an exegetical reading of the verse: *verachatz es besaro bamayim velavasham* — "and he shall wash his body in water and wear them." The verse already listed the garments in full detail earlier; the repetition of *velavasham* — "and wear them" — following the mention of immersion conveys a specific message: *levisha me'akevet ve'ein davar acher me'akeiv* — wearing the garments is what prevents validity; nothing else mentioned in that verse — specifically the immersion — carries the same force. The word *velavasham* reasserts the centrality of *levisha* and implicitly limits the scope of *ikkuv* to the donning of the garments alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צָהֲבוּ פָּנָיו, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וָי״ו אַאוּפְתָּא כְּתַבִי לָךְ – אִי הָכִי, דְּצַפְרָא נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi's **face lit up** with joy after hearing this response. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: I have** merely **written** the letter ***vav* on a piece of wood for you,** i.e., I have not given you a satisfactory answer. As, **if that is so,** that only the wearing of the vestments is indispensable, then failure to sanctify the hands and feet **in the morning** should **also** not disqualify the service, yet according to the *baraita* it disqualifies the service.

The reaction of *Rav Ashi* is telling: *tzahavu fanav* — his face lit up with admiration for the reading. Yet *Rabbi Yochanan* himself then challenges it: *vav aufsa kesivi lach* — an idiomatic expression of dismissal, roughly meaning "did I write you a fine letter on a log?" — indicating that the answer is not satisfactory. The objection: *ei hachi detzafra nami* — if that verse downplays the immersions, then by the same logic the morning washing should also not be *me'akeiv*. Yet the morning washing clearly is *me'akeiv*. The answer that *Rabbi Yochanan*'s *drasha* provides cannot distinguish between the morning washing and the intermediate ones, and therefore it collapses under scrutiny — even though, as one might note, the morning washing is a general requirement for all *kohanim* and the verse in question specifically addresses the *Yom Kippur* context. Nevertheless, *Rabbi Yochanan* finds the answer insufficient.

### Chizkiyah's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לָהֶם חׇק עוֹלָם לוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ לְדֹרֹתָם״ – דָּבָר הַמְעַכֵּב בְּזַרְעוֹ, מְעַכֵּב בּוֹ; דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב בְּזַרְעוֹ, אֵין מְעַכֵּב בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya says** there is an alternative answer: **The verse states** with regard to sanctification of the hands and feet: **"And it shall be a statute forever to them, even to him and to his seed throughout their generations"** (Exodus 30:21). From the comparison of Aaron to his children one may derive that **anything that is indispensable with regard to his seed,** the priests, **is indispensable with regard to him,** the High Priest during the Yom Kippur service, which only he may perform. But **anything that is not indispensable with regard to his seed is not indispensable with regard to him.** Therefore, the sanctification of the hands and feet between each rite, which is unnecessary for priests during the daily service, does not disqualify the High Priest's Yom Kippur service if not done.

*Chizkiyah* offers an alternative source: *vehayesa lahem chok olam lo ulezaro ledorosam* — "it shall be for them an eternal statute, for him and for his descendants throughout their generations." This verse accompanies the commands regarding the *kiyor* and the morning washing. The *drasha*: *davar hame'akeiv bizaro me'akeiv bo* — a washing that applies to and prevents validity for ordinary *kohanim* likewise prevents validity for the *kohen gadol*. Conversely, *davar she'eino me'akeiv bizaro eino me'akeiv bo* — a washing that does not apply to ordinary *kohanim* — namely the intermediate *Yom Kippur* washings, which are unique to the *kohen gadol* — does not carry the force of *ikkuv* even for him. The morning washing is thus the only one that is *me'akeiv* universally; the *Yom Kippur* intermediate washings, while prescribed, do not prevent validity if omitted.

### Rabbi Yonatan's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְרָחֲצוּ מִמֶּנּוּ מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו״ – דָּבָר הַמְעַכֵּב בְּבָנָיו מְעַכֵּב בּוֹ, דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב בְּבָנָיו אֵין מְעַכֵּב בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The principle is derived **from here:** The verse states with regard to the Basin in the Temple: **"That Moses and Aaron and his sons might wash their hands and their feet from it"** (Exodus 40:31). One may derive from the verse that **anything that is indispensable with regard to his sons is indispensable with regard to him.** But **anything that is not indispensable with regard to his sons is not indispensable with regard to him.**

*Rabbi Yonatan* arrives at the same conclusion from a different verse: *verachatzu mimenu Moshe ve'Aharon uvanav* — "and Moshe and Aharon and his sons shall wash from it." The parallel structure — *Moshe ve'Aharon* together with *vanav*, his sons — teaches the same principle. A washing that is *me'akeiv* for the sons is *me'akeiv* for *Aharon*; a washing that is not operative for the sons does not carry *ikkuv* for *Aharon* either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מִדְּחִזְקִיָּה? אָמַר לָךְ: הָהוּא לְדוֹרוֹת הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **Rabbi Yonatan did not say** to derive this **from** the verse cited **by Ḥizkiyya?** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Yonatan could have **said to you: That** verse cited by Ḥizkiyya **is written** to teach that the *halakha* applies even **for** future **generations,** not to draw a parallel between the High Priest and ordinary priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מֵהַאי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל כִּיּוֹר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְקַדֵּשׁ אַרְבָּעָה כֹּהֲנִים מִמֶּנּוּ – אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרָחֲצוּ מִמֶּנּוּ מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** Sage, Ḥizkiyya, **what is the reason** that **he did not say** to derive the *halakha* **from that** verse cited by Rabbi Yonatan? The Gemara responds: **He requires** that verse **for** the *halakha* taught **by Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina. As Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** With regard to **any** circumstance in **which** the **Basin does not contain enough** water **for four priests to sanctify** their hands and feet **from it,** the priests **may not sanctify** their hands and feet **with it, as it is stated: "That Moses and Aaron and his sons might wash** their hands and their feet **from it."** Moses, Aaron, and Aaron's two sons total four.

The Gemara then asks why *Rabbi Yonatan* did not use *Chizkiyah*'s verse, and vice versa. *Rabbi Yonatan* does not use *Chizkiyah*'s verse because *hu ledoros hu dikesivi* — that verse is simply establishing the eternal applicability of the washing requirement for all generations; it does not specifically address the question of *me'akeiv*. *Chizkiyah*, for his part, could not use *Rabbi Yonatan*'s verse because he needs it for a different teaching: that of *Rabbi Yose beRabbi Chanina*, who derives from *Moshe ve'Aharon uvanav* that any *kiyor* must be large enough for four *kohanim* to wash from it simultaneously.

## Washing Procedure

### Washing Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מִצְוַת קִידּוּשׁ? מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלוֹ הַיְמָנִית, וְיָדוֹ הַשְּׂמָאלִית עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלוֹ הַשְּׂמָאלִית, וּמְקַדֵּשׁ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מַנִּיחַ שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו זוֹ עַל גַּב זוֹ, וְעַל גַּבֵּי שְׁתֵּי רַגְלָיו זוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי זוֹ, וּמְקַדֵּשׁ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הִפְלַגְתָּה, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: How is the mitzva of sanctification** of the hands and feet performed? The priest **lays his right hand on top of his right foot, and his left hand on top of his left foot, and sanctifies** them with the water flowing from the Basin. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: He lays both his hands one on top of the other, and** lays them together **on top of both his feet,** themselves laid **one on top of the other, and sanctifies** them. **They said to him: You have gone too far; it is impossible to do so.**

Having established that the morning washing is *me'akeiv*, the Gemara now explores the proper method of performing it. The *baraita* teaches: *maniach yado hayemanit al gabei raglo hayemanit veyado hasmolit al gabei raglo hasmolit veshocheh u'mekadesh* — the *kohen* places his right hand on his right foot and his left hand on his left foot simultaneously, bends down, and sanctifies — that is, he washes — in this position.

*Rabbi Yose beRebbi Yehuda* offers an alternative: *maniach shtei yadav zo al gabei zo ushtei raglav zo al gabei zo u'mekadesh* — he places both hands together and both feet together and then washes. The response is *amru lo hiflagta* — "you have gone too far" — meaning this posture is practically untenable; standing with one foot on top of the other is physically impossible without support.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וַחֲבֵירוֹ מְסַיְּיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **They speak well to him;** it seems impossible to assume such a position without losing one's balance. **Rav Yosef says:** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, meant that the priest would assume the position **while another** priest would **help him** maintain his balance.

*Rav Yosef* resolves this: a fellow *kohen* may support him so he does not topple — *chavero mesay'o*. The disagreement then, as *Abaye* clarifies, is whether *amida min hatzad* — supported standing — counts as *amida* for halachic purposes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: עֲמִידָה מִן הַצַּד אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** the reasoning of the Sages and that of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda? **Abaye says:** The difference **between them is** their opinion with regard to **standing** with support **from the side.** According to the Sages, this is not considered standing and the priest may not sanctify his hands and feet while in such a position.

### Standing Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סַמָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבִינָא: וְלִיתֵּיב מִיתָּב וּלְקַדֵּשׁ! אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְשָׁרֵת״ – וְשֵׁירוּת מְעוּמָּד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Samma, son of Rav Ashi, said to Ravina: And let him sit and sanctify** his hands and feet while seated, and in this manner he may sanctify them all at once. Ravina said to him: **The verse states:** "When they go into the Tent of Meeting, they shall wash with water, that they not die; or when they come near to the altar **to minister"** (Exodus 30:20), **and ministration is** performed while **standing,** as the verse states: "To stand to minister" (Deuteronomy 18:5). Therefore, sanctification must also be performed while standing.

This raises the underlying question: why must the washing be done standing at all? The Gemara answers from the verse *la'amod leshareit* — "to stand to serve" — and the general principle that *sherut* in the *Beis Hamikdash* must be performed standing. Since the washing of hands and feet is classified as part of the Temple service, it must be done in a standing position.

*Tosafot* extends this principle: *amida min hatzad ke'amida* — whether supported standing counts as standing — is relevant beyond the Temple context. When receiving an *aliyah* to the Torah, one should not lean on the *bimah* for support, since the Torah reading requires standing, both because of the principle derived in *Megilla* that reading Torah requires *amida*, and because, as the Yerushalmi states, just as the Torah was given with reverence and awe, so it should be read with reverence and awe. This applies as a *lechatchila* requirement; for those who are unable to stand due to illness or disability, it is not *me'akeiv*.

### Night-to-Day Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו בַּיּוֹם – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ בַּלַּיְלָה, בַּלַּיְלָה – צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ בַּיּוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי; שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If a priest **sanctified his hands and feet during the day, he does not need to sanctify** them that **night,** but if he sanctified them **at night, he must sanctify** them **during the** following **day.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **would say:** The disqualification of being **left overnight is determinative with regard to sanctification of** the **hands and feet,** like any sacrificial item that is sanctified. Accordingly, once night has passed, the priest must sanctify them again. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** The disqualification of being **left overnight is not determinative with regard to sanctification of** the **hands and feet.** Therefore, as long as the priest continues to serve, he need not sanctify them again.

The Gemara turns to the question of when the washing must be repeated. *Tanu rabbanan*: *kidesh yadav veraglav beyom ein tzarich lekadesh balayla* — if a *kohen* washed in the day and then continued into the night, he need not wash again simply because night has arrived. But *balayla... tzarich lekadesh beyom — divrei Rabbi* — if he was working through the night and dawn breaks, *Rabbi* holds that the new day requires a new washing.

*Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* disagrees: *ein lina mo'elet bekidush yadav veraglav* — the passage of the night has no effect on the washing; as long as the *kohen* did not leave the *Beis Hamikdash* and was not distracted (*hesech hadaat*), his original washing remains valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה לָאוֹרָה – טָעוּן קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו מִתְּחִילַּת עֲבוֹדָה – אֲפִילּוּ מִיכָּן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If the priest **was standing and sacrificing** offerings **on top of the altar all night, in the morning he requires sanctification of** the **hands and feet** again. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Once he sanctified his hands and feet at the beginning of** the **service, even** if he continues to perform rites **for the next ten days, he does not need to sanctify** them again.

A second *baraita* presents a parallel case: a *kohen* who spent the entire night placing limbs on the altar, and at daybreak continues this same activity. *Rabbi* again requires a new washing. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* holds that since the washing was performed at the outset of the *avoda* and the *kohen* has remained continuously engaged — *afilu mikan ve'ad asarah yamim* — even for ten days without interruption — *eino tzarich lekadesh*, no fresh washing is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא – בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי, דִּפְסַק לֵיהּ מֵעֲבוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה; אֲבָל בְּהָא, דְּלָא פְּסַק לֵיהּ – אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא – בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; אֲבָל בְּהָא – אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** both of the above *baraitot* are **necessary,** though they seem to present the same dispute. **As, if it had taught us** only **the first** *baraita*, one might have thought that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **stated** his opinion only **with regard to that** case, **as** there the priest **paused between** one **rite and** another **rite. But in this** case, i.e., the second *baraita*, **where he did not pause** between rites, **say** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** that the priest need not sanctify his hands and feet again. **And** conversely, **if it had taught us** only **with regard to that** case, i.e., the second *baraita*, one might have thought: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, stated** his opinion only **in that** case. **But in this** case, **say** that **he concedes to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. Therefore, both *baraitot* are **necessary.**

The Gemara notes that both cases are necessary to state (*tzericha*). If only the first case had been given, one might think that *Rabbi* requires a fresh washing only when the *kohen* shifts to a new *avoda* in the morning, not when he continues the same task through the transition. And one might think *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* concedes a new washing when the *avoda* itself is new. By stating both cases, we learn that *Rabbi* requires the morning washing even in mid-task continuity, and *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* exempts even when the task changes, as long as the *kohen* never departed.

### Textual Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּגִשְׁתָּם״. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּבוֹאָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **As it is written:** The priests must sanctify their hands and feet: **"When they come near** to the altar to minister" (Exodus 30:20), i.e., when they begin the service in the morning. And **what is the reasoning of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon? As it is written** in the same verse: **"When they go into** the Tent of Meeting," indicating that one sanctification suffices for the duration of a priest's service in the Tent of Meeting.

*Rabbi*'s basis: the verse *bigishtam el hamizbeach leshareit* — "upon their approach to the altar to serve." The word *bigishtam* — a new encounter — points to the fact that at daybreak, when fresh wood is placed on the altar (*maaracha chadasha*), the altar has been renewed, and this constitutes a new *gisha*, a new encounter, requiring a new washing. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*'s basis: the verse *bevo'am el ohel moed leshareit* — "upon their entry into the Tent of Meeting to serve." Entry into the sanctuary is the operative trigger; as long as there has been no new entry, no new washing is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״בְּבוֹאָם״! אִי כְּתִיב ״בְּגִשְׁתָּם״ וְלָא כְּתִיב ״בְּבוֹאָם״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: עַל כׇּל גִּישָׁה וְגִישָׁה; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״בְּבוֹאָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **the other** Sage, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **as well, isn't it written: "When they go into** the Tent of Meeting"? How does he interpret this term? The Gemara responds: **If** only **"when they come near" had been written, and "when they go into" had not been written, I would say** that the priest must sanctify his hands and feet **for each and every approach,** i.e., every rite. Therefore **the Merciful One wrote: "When they go into,"** to indicate that it is necessary only once a day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״בְּגִשְׁתָּם״! אִי כְּתִיב ״בְּבוֹאָם״ וְלָא כְּתִיב ״בְּגִשְׁתָּם״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ אַבִּיאָה רֵיקָנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **the other** Sage, Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **as well, isn't it written: "When they come near"?** How does he interpret this term? The Gemara responds: **If** only **"when they go into" had been written, and "when they come near" had not been written, I would say** that **even for an entrance with no purpose,** i.e., where the priest has no intention of performing rites, he must sanctify his hands and feet. Therefore, the Merciful One wrote: "When they come near."

Each *amora* must account for the verse invoked by the other. *Rabbi* reads *bevo'am* as limiting the *bigishtam* principle: without *bevo'am*, one might think a new washing is required every single time the *kohen* approaches the altar during the day. *Bevo'am* tells us that presence within the *Mikdash* is the baseline, and *bigishtam* adds only the one exception — the renewed altar at dawn. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* reads *bigishtam* as teaching a different *halacha* entirely: that the washing must be juxtaposed with the actual approach to service (*bigishtam*), which leads to the ruling discussed below.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבִּיאָה רֵיקָנִית?! הָא כְּתִיב: ״לְשָׁרֵת״! אֶלָּא בְּגִשְׁתָּם מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, לְכִדְרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב. דְּאָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּקִידּוּשׁ שֵׁנִי – כְּשֶׁהוּא לָבוּשׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ; דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ בְגִשְׁתָּם״ – מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּסָּר אֶלָּא גִּישָׁה בִּלְבַד, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁמְחוּסָּר לְבִישָׁה וְגִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: How could one have thought that the priest must sanctify his hands and feet **for an entrance with no purpose? Isn't is written** in the same verse: **"To minister"? Rather,** according to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **"when they come near" is necessary for** the *halakha* taught **by Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov. As Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says: All concede with regard to the second sanctification** performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur after each immersion that he must **sanctify** his hands and feet **while he is dressed** in the priestly vestments, **as the verse states: "Or when they come near** to the altar to minister." The verse indicates that **one who is lacking only to come near** to the altar, i.e., one who is in all other ways prepared for the service, may perform this sanctification. **Excluded is this** one, **who is lacking** both **dressing and coming near.**

The verse *bigishtam* is applied by *Rav Acha brei deRav Yaakov* to a different question: *hakol modim bekiddush sheni keshehu lavush mekadesh* — all agree that the second washing of the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — performed after changing from one set of garments to another — must be done while already wearing the new garments, not before donning them. The derivation: *mi sheino mechusar ella gisha bilvad* — the washing must be the final act before the *gisha*, the approach to service. If the *kohen* washes before dressing, the washing is not immediately contiguous with the encounter; he must still don the garments. Therefore, one must first put on the new *begadim*, and only then wash the hands and feet — so that the sole remaining step between the washing and the *avoda* is the approach itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְהַקְטִיר אִשֶּׁה״ לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the continuation of the verse: **"To cause an offering by fire to smoke"?**

This is framed as *kiddush sheni* — the secondary washing between garment changes — and all opinions agree on this point of sequencing, whatever their broader disagreements about which washings are *me'akeiv* and which are not.
