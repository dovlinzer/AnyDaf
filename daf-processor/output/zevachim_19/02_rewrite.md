# Zevachim 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Begadim Fit & Coverage

### Invalid Service

The daf opens with the continuation of a broader discussion about the *bigdei kehuna* — the priestly garments — and the ways in which deficiencies in those garments render the *avoda* invalid. The Gemara had already explored a range of fitting questions: what happens if the garments are too long or too short, if they are held up by the belt so that they do not reach the proper position, or if they are the correct size only because they are being gathered. All of these scenarios bear on the fundamental requirement that a *kohen* must wear his prescribed garments in the prescribed manner. The *avoda* performed without the proper *begadim* — *chutz mibegadim* — is *pasul*.

### Bandaging on Shabbat

The Gemara cites a teaching: *kohen she'laka be'etzba'o* — a *kohen* who sustained a cut on his finger. We are not dealing with a *psul* of the service per se at the outset; the concern is more fundamental hygiene of the Temple rite. The *kohen* may wrap the wound with a *gemi* (a light cloth bandage) in the *Beis Hamikdash*, even on *Shabbos* — *bamikdash aval lo bamedina* — though not outside, where the prohibition applies. The reason this is an issue on *Shabbos* at all is that keeping a wound covered assists the healing process and constitutes a form of *refuah*, which is rabbinically prohibited on *Shabbos*. However, *ein shevus bamikdash* — the general principle that rabbinic restrictions are waived within the Temple — licenses the practice there. It is worth noting the apparent irony: the holiest place on the holiest day grants more latitude, not less. The explanation is that *kohanim* are presumed to be scrupulous regarding Torah-level prohibitions, and the rabbinic fence need not be extended into that domain.

The Gemara clarifies that if, while wrapping the wound, the *kohen* intends to squeeze out blood, this is forbidden in both locations — *kan vekan assur* — because that crosses into the category of *chavala*, causing a wound, which is an independent prohibition entirely unrelated to the rabbinic healing concern.

### Extra Garments Debate

The Gemara now pivots to the *begadim* issue raised by the case of the bandage. *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya* teaches: *lo shanu ela gemi* — the ruling that the bandage is permissible applies only to a light cloth bandage. But *tziltzul katan havei yitur begadim* — if the item is something more garment-like, such as a small decorative band or bracelet-like object, it constitutes an extra garment — *yitur begadim* — and is therefore forbidden. A *kohen* may wear only the prescribed *bigdei kehuna*; anything additional, if it constitutes a *beged*, invalidates.

This naturally raises the question of whether a *kohen* could perform the *avoda* wearing a ring, eyeglasses, or other everyday items. The *Gemara* will develop these questions further, but the initial framework is that any item that qualifies as a *beged* creates a problem.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and introduces a critical distinction: *lo amru yitur begadim ela bemakom begadim* — the prohibition of wearing an extra garment applies only to those parts of the body where the *bigdei kehuna* are designed to cover. On parts of the body not covered by the priestly garments — such as a finger, or the face — *shelo bemakom begadim lo havei yitur*, wearing something there does not constitute an extra garment.

The Gemara then raises the obvious challenge: even granting *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position on *yitur begadim*, why isn't the bandage problematic as a *chatzitza* — an interposition between the *kohen*'s hand and whatever he is handling during the *avoda*? The principle that the service must be performed *be'itzumo shel kohen* — directly by the essence of the *kohen* himself, with direct skin contact — would seem to apply. The Gemara resolves this: *bismolo* — the wound is on the left hand, and the *avoda* is performed with the right. Alternatively, the bandage is positioned on the back of the hand, which *Rashi* notes is not the operative surface that contacts the vessels or blood. These answers point to two distinct categories of concern: *chatzitza* between the skin and the object of the *avoda*, and *yitur begadim* as a separate issue applying even where no direct *avoda* contact occurs.

## Chatzitza & Size Limits

### Single Thread Issue

*Rava*'s position sharpens the entire discussion considerably. He teaches — citing *Rav Chisda* — that *bemakom begadim afilu nima achas chotzetzes* — in the place where the priestly garments are worn, even a single thread constitutes a *chatzitza*. The rationale derives from the verse *michnesei vad yihyu al besaro* — the garments must rest directly upon the skin. Any intervening object, however small, violates this requirement. A loose thread, a hair that has separated from the body and settled on the skin — all of these are problematic.

It is worth pausing to note, as *Rashi* flags throughout this section, that the term *chatzitza* is used somewhat loosely in the Gemara. In its technically precise sense, *chatzitza* refers to interposition either between the garment and the skin, or between the skin and the object of the *avoda*. But sometimes the Gemara uses the term to describe what is functionally a *yitur begadim* problem. The reader must attend carefully to context.

### Three-by-Three Rule

*Rava* continues: *shelo bemakom begadim shalosh al shalosh chotzetzes, pachos mikan eina chotzetzes* — in a place on the body where the priestly garments are not worn, something constitutes a problem only if it is at least three fingerbreadths by three fingerbreadths in size. This measurement is the standard threshold in *hilchos tumah vetaharah* for a piece of cloth to be classified as a significant garment. Below that threshold, the item does not rise to the level of a *beged* and presents no concern of *yitur begadim*.

The positions can be mapped out as follows. According to *Rava*: where the *begadim* are worn, even a single *nima* is a *chatzitza*; where they are not worn, the threshold is three by three for *yitur begadim*. According to *Rabbi Yochanan* (in his initial formulation): where the *begadim* are worn, a genuine alternative *beged* would be required to constitute *yitur begadim*; where they are not worn, there is no concern at all. According to *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya*: the determining factor for the second zone is whether the item is a functional *tziltzul* — something with genuine garment character — regardless of its size.

### Reconciling Positions

The Gemara asks whether *Rava* necessarily disagrees with *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya*. The answer is that they need not be in conflict: *shani tziltzul katan dechashiv* — a small *tziltzul* is significant because of its inherent garment character. The two positions represent two independent criteria for classifying something as a *beged*: functional character (*tziltzul*) and minimum size (three by three). These are complementary rather than contradictory standards, and either one could render an item a *beged* for the purposes of *yitur begadim*.

The Gemara then presents a second formulation (*lishana acharina*) of the positions. In this version, *Rabbi Yochanan* himself takes a stance very close to *Rava*: *bemakom begadim* even less than three by three is problematic, while *shelo bemakom begadim* requires three by three — *ve'hainu de'Rava*, which the Gemara notes is effectively identical to *Rava*'s position. The difference between this version of *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rav Yehuda brei deRav Chiya* is that *Rabbi Yochanan* rejects the *tziltzul* criterion as independently sufficient below the three-by-three threshold.

The Gemara raises a question: according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, why did the *Mishnah* in *hilchos Shabbos* specifically choose the case of a *gemi* rather than illustrating the rule with a small *tziltzul*, which would also be permitted? The answer is: *milta agav urchia kamashma lan de'gemi masi* — the *Mishnah*'s interest was in teaching the law of *Shabbos* healing; it chose the bandage specifically because it wanted to convey that this material actually promotes healing, which is why it is permitted in the *Beis Hamikdash* but not outside it. Incidentally teaching a *chiddush* in *bigdei kehuna* was not its purpose.

## Objects & Chatzitza

### Air Gaps & Normal Wear

Having established the framework, *Rava* proceeds to pose a series of open-ended questions about what precisely constitutes a *chatzitza*. The first: *nichnesa lo ruach bevigdo mahu* — if a wind lifts the tunic slightly off the leg, creating a momentary air gap between garment and skin, is this a *chatzitza*? The Gemara frames the question as: do we require *al besaro* to be understood literally at every point of contact, or does *derech levisha bekach* — the normal manner of wearing a garment — include the natural and unavoidable variation in contact? The logical answer is clearly the latter; no garment maintains uniform contact with every inch of skin at all times, and to demand otherwise would make the priesthood functionally impossible.

### Lice Status

The next question: *kinah mahu shetachlotz* — what is the status of a louse on the *kohen*'s skin between the garment and the body? *Metah lo tiba'i lach* — a dead louse requires no deliberation; it is clearly a *chatzitza*, as it is something unwanted on the body. *Chayah mai* — but a living louse? *Mi amrinan keivan de'azla ve'atya ravita hi* — perhaps since it moves about on its own, it is considered quasi-organic and not truly an interposition? *Rashi* notes that the argument would be analogous to something that is part of one's body — it comes and goes naturally. Or: *keivan dekapid aleih chotzetzet* — since one obviously objects to its presence, it is a *chatzitza*. The question is left open.

Similar unresolved questions are raised about fine dust (*avak afar*) on the skin and about the armpit area, where the structure of the garment necessarily creates a permanent air pocket.

### Hair as Barrier

*Mar bar Rav Ashi* poses the question: *yatza se'aro bevigdo mahu* — if the *kohen*'s hair exits through the garment and lies between the garment and the skin, what is the status? *Se'ar kegufo dami* — is hair to be considered an extension of the body, and therefore not an interposition, in the same way one might argue that inserting one's own hand into the fold of a garment (*hichnis yado letoch cheiko*) does not constitute *chatzitza* because *gufo michai*? Or is hair sufficiently separate from the body that it functions as a barrier? These too are left unresolved by the Gemara.

### Tefillin Issues

The Gemara raises the case of *tefillin*. *Rashi* clarifies that the question here is not primarily about *chatzitza* in the sense of interposition between the skin and the object of the *avoda*, but rather whether *tefillin* worn during the *avoda* constitute an extra *beged* — *yitur begadim* — or a *chatzitza* between the garment and the skin.

The Gemara distinguishes by opinion: *aliba deman d'amar layla lav zman tefillin* — according to the view that night is not the time of the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*, *lo tibay lach* — there is no question. At night, *tefillin* are worn without a *mitzvah* context, making them plainly a foreign garment. *Keivan de'layla chaitz yom nami chaitz* — since at night they constitute an extra *beged*, and the daytime *avoda* is presumably more weighty, they would remain a problem during the day as well. The real question arises *lema'an d'amar layla zman tefillin* — according to the view that *tefillin* may always be worn. In that case, are *tefillin* to be viewed as a perpetual *mitzvah* object — perhaps comparable in status to the body itself — or as a foreign *beged*? *Mitzvah degupei chaitz o lo chaitz?*

The matter reached *Rabbi Ami*, who ruled decisively: *talmud aruch hu beyadenu tefillin chotzetzet* — we have a longstanding teaching that *tefillin* do constitute a *chatzitza*.

A *braita* is then raised in apparent contradiction: when *kohanim* are performing their *avoda*, *levi'im* are on their platform, and *Yisraelim* are present at the *ma'amad*, they are *peturim* — exempt — from wearing *tefillin*. This implies they could wear *tefillin* without invalidating. The Gemara resolves this: the *braita* says *peturim* rather than *asurin* because the ruling applies equally to *levi'im* and *Yisrael*, for whom there is no question of invalidation; the language of exemption rather than prohibition accommodates all three groups, even though for a *kohen* performing the *avoda*, wearing *tefillin* would in fact invalidate.

The Gemara then reconciles an explicit *braita* stating that *im henicham einam chotzetzim* — wearing *tefillin* does not constitute a *chatzitza* — by distinguishing between *tefillin shel yad* and *tefillin shel rosh*. The *shel yad* is a *chatzitza*, since it is positioned where the priestly garment must rest directly against the skin. The *shel rosh* is not a *chatzitza* — there is no garment pressing against that part of the head — and does not constitute *yitur begadim* either, because the *mitznefet* does not cover the entire skull, and the space between the *tzitz* and the *mitznefet* is where the *kohen gadol*'s hair was visible, and it is there that *tefillin* are placed. *Tam searo hayah nir'eh bein tzitz lemitznefet vesham manichin tefillin* — and in that uncovered space, *tefillin* of the head may be worn without any concern of *chatzitza* or *yitur begadim*.

## Purification & Washing

### Mechusar Kippurim

The Gemara moves to another category of invalidation: *mechusar kippurim* — one who has completed the immersion process but has not yet brought the required *korban* that finalizes the *tahara* process. Such a person — whether a *zav*, a *yoledes*, or any other individual whose purification requires a concluding sacrifice — remains in a state of personal *tumah* until that offering is brought. *Rav Huna* derives this from the verse *vichiper aleha hakohen vetahera* — the *kohen* shall atone for her and she will become *tehorah* — which implies that until the atonement is effected, her status of *tumah* persists. Since a *tamei* person performing the *avoda* invalidates it, a *mechusar kippurim* doing so likewise invalidates.

### Morning Washing

The next item on the Mishnah's list: *shelo rachutz yadayim veraglayim* — a *kohen* who did not wash his hands and feet before beginning the service. This too renders the *avoda* invalid. The derivation proceeds by a *gezeira shava* — the word *chok* — linking the washing requirement to the requirement for *bigdei kehuna*, which is itself linked to a *chukas olam* formulation. Just as lacking the proper *begadim* invalidates, so too does lacking the morning washing.

## Yom Kippur Washings

### Teaching Overview

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that defines the scope of the washing requirement with precision, particularly as applied to the *Yom Kippur* service. On *Yom Kippur*, the *kohen gadol* changes his garments five times and is required, as described explicitly in the Torah, to immerse in the *mikveh* and wash his hands before and after each change — potentially ten washings in total over the course of the day. The *baraita* rules: *kohen gadol shelo taval veshelo kidesh bein beged lebeged uvein avoda la'avoda ve'avad — avodaso kesheira* — if the *kohen gadol* omitted these intermediate immersions and washings between the garment changes, the *avoda* remains valid. By contrast: *ve'echad kohen gadol ve'echad kohen hedyot shelo kidesh yadav veraglav shacharis ve'avad — avodaso pesulah* — whether a *kohen gadol* or an ordinary *kohen*, if the initial morning washing was not performed, the *avoda* is invalid.

The *chiddush* is the first clause. One might have assumed — given that *Yom Kippur* is governed by the phrase *chukas olam*, meaning that every prescribed element of the service is indispensable — that missing an intermediate washing would also invalidate. The question is why it does not.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Drasha

*Rav Assi* poses this question directly to *Rabbi Yochanan*: the five immersions and ten washings are *de'Oraita*, and the word *chok* is written regarding *Yom Kippur* — which ordinarily signals that every detail is *le'akeiv*, preventing validity if omitted. Why then do the intermediate washings not invalidate?

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds with an exegetical reading of the verse: *verachatz es besaro bamayim velavasham* — "and he shall wash his body in water and wear them." The verse already listed the garments in full detail earlier; the repetition of *velavasham* — "and wear them" — following the mention of immersion conveys a specific message: *levisha me'akevet ve'ein davar acher me'akeiv* — wearing the garments is what prevents validity; nothing else mentioned in that verse — specifically the immersion — carries the same force. The word *velavasham* reasserts the centrality of *levisha* and implicitly limits the scope of *ikkuv* to the donning of the garments alone.

The reaction of *Rav Ashi* is telling: *tzahavu fanav* — his face lit up with admiration for the reading. Yet *Rabbi Yochanan* himself then challenges it: *vav aufsa kesivi lach* — an idiomatic expression of dismissal, roughly meaning "did I write you a fine letter on a log?" — indicating that the answer is not satisfactory. The objection: *ei hachi detzafra nami* — if that verse downplays the immersions, then by the same logic the morning washing should also not be *me'akeiv*. Yet the morning washing clearly is *me'akeiv*. The answer that *Rabbi Yochanan*'s *drasha* provides cannot distinguish between the morning washing and the intermediate ones, and therefore it collapses under scrutiny — even though, as one might note, the morning washing is a general requirement for all *kohanim* and the verse in question specifically addresses the *Yom Kippur* context. Nevertheless, *Rabbi Yochanan* finds the answer insufficient.

### Chizkiyah's Drasha

*Chizkiyah* offers an alternative source: *vehayesa lahem chok olam lo ulezaro ledorosam* — "it shall be for them an eternal statute, for him and for his descendants throughout their generations." This verse accompanies the commands regarding the *kiyor* and the morning washing. The *drasha*: *davar hame'akeiv bizaro me'akeiv bo* — a washing that applies to and prevents validity for ordinary *kohanim* likewise prevents validity for the *kohen gadol*. Conversely, *davar she'eino me'akeiv bizaro eino me'akeiv bo* — a washing that does not apply to ordinary *kohanim* — namely the intermediate *Yom Kippur* washings, which are unique to the *kohen gadol* — does not carry the force of *ikkuv* even for him. The morning washing is thus the only one that is *me'akeiv* universally; the *Yom Kippur* intermediate washings, while prescribed, do not prevent validity if omitted.

### Rabbi Yonatan's Drasha

*Rabbi Yonatan* arrives at the same conclusion from a different verse: *verachatzu mimenu Moshe ve'Aharon uvanav* — "and Moshe and Aharon and his sons shall wash from it." The parallel structure — *Moshe ve'Aharon* together with *vanav*, his sons — teaches the same principle. A washing that is *me'akeiv* for the sons is *me'akeiv* for *Aharon*; a washing that is not operative for the sons does not carry *ikkuv* for *Aharon* either.

The Gemara then asks why *Rabbi Yonatan* did not use *Chizkiyah*'s verse, and vice versa. *Rabbi Yonatan* does not use *Chizkiyah*'s verse because *hu ledoros hu dikesivi* — that verse is simply establishing the eternal applicability of the washing requirement for all generations; it does not specifically address the question of *me'akeiv*. *Chizkiyah*, for his part, could not use *Rabbi Yonatan*'s verse because he needs it for a different teaching: that of *Rabbi Yose beRabbi Chanina*, who derives from *Moshe ve'Aharon uvanav* that any *kiyor* must be large enough for four *kohanim* to wash from it simultaneously.

## Washing Procedure

### Washing Methods

Having established that the morning washing is *me'akeiv*, the Gemara now explores the proper method of performing it. The *baraita* teaches: *maniach yado hayemanit al gabei raglo hayemanit veyado hasmolit al gabei raglo hasmolit veshocheh u'mekadesh* — the *kohen* places his right hand on his right foot and his left hand on his left foot simultaneously, bends down, and sanctifies — that is, he washes — in this position.

*Rabbi Yose beRebbi Yehuda* offers an alternative: *maniach shtei yadav zo al gabei zo ushtei raglav zo al gabei zo u'mekadesh* — he places both hands together and both feet together and then washes. The response is *amru lo hiflagta* — "you have gone too far" — meaning this posture is practically untenable; standing with one foot on top of the other is physically impossible without support.

*Rav Yosef* resolves this: a fellow *kohen* may support him so he does not topple — *chavero mesay'o*. The disagreement then, as *Abaye* clarifies, is whether *amida min hatzad* — supported standing — counts as *amida* for halachic purposes.

### Standing Requirement

This raises the underlying question: why must the washing be done standing at all? The Gemara answers from the verse *la'amod leshareit* — "to stand to serve" — and the general principle that *sherut* in the *Beis Hamikdash* must be performed standing. Since the washing of hands and feet is classified as part of the Temple service, it must be done in a standing position.

*Tosafot* extends this principle: *amida min hatzad ke'amida* — whether supported standing counts as standing — is relevant beyond the Temple context. When receiving an *aliyah* to the Torah, one should not lean on the *bimah* for support, since the Torah reading requires standing, both because of the principle derived in *Megilla* that reading Torah requires *amida*, and because, as the Yerushalmi states, just as the Torah was given with reverence and awe, so it should be read with reverence and awe. This applies as a *lechatchila* requirement; for those who are unable to stand due to illness or disability, it is not *me'akeiv*.

### Night-to-Day Work

The Gemara turns to the question of when the washing must be repeated. *Tanu rabbanan*: *kidesh yadav veraglav beyom ein tzarich lekadesh balayla* — if a *kohen* washed in the day and then continued into the night, he need not wash again simply because night has arrived. But *balayla... tzarich lekadesh beyom — divrei Rabbi* — if he was working through the night and dawn breaks, *Rabbi* holds that the new day requires a new washing.

### Rabbi vs. Rabbi Elazar

*Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* disagrees: *ein lina mo'elet bekidush yadav veraglav* — the passage of the night has no effect on the washing; as long as the *kohen* did not leave the *Beis Hamikdash* and was not distracted (*hesech hadaat*), his original washing remains valid.

A second *baraita* presents a parallel case: a *kohen* who spent the entire night placing limbs on the altar, and at daybreak continues this same activity. *Rabbi* again requires a new washing. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* holds that since the washing was performed at the outset of the *avoda* and the *kohen* has remained continuously engaged — *afilu mikan ve'ad asarah yamim* — even for ten days without interruption — *eino tzarich lekadesh*, no fresh washing is needed.

The Gemara notes that both cases are necessary to state (*tzericha*). If only the first case had been given, one might think that *Rabbi* requires a fresh washing only when the *kohen* shifts to a new *avoda* in the morning, not when he continues the same task through the transition. And one might think *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* concedes a new washing when the *avoda* itself is new. By stating both cases, we learn that *Rabbi* requires the morning washing even in mid-task continuity, and *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* exempts even when the task changes, as long as the *kohen* never departed.

### Textual Basis

*Rabbi*'s basis: the verse *bigishtam el hamizbeach leshareit* — "upon their approach to the altar to serve." The word *bigishtam* — a new encounter — points to the fact that at daybreak, when fresh wood is placed on the altar (*maaracha chadasha*), the altar has been renewed, and this constitutes a new *gisha*, a new encounter, requiring a new washing. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*'s basis: the verse *bevo'am el ohel moed leshareit* — "upon their entry into the Tent of Meeting to serve." Entry into the sanctuary is the operative trigger; as long as there has been no new entry, no new washing is required.

Each *amora* must account for the verse invoked by the other. *Rabbi* reads *bevo'am* as limiting the *bigishtam* principle: without *bevo'am*, one might think a new washing is required every single time the *kohen* approaches the altar during the day. *Bevo'am* tells us that presence within the *Mikdash* is the baseline, and *bigishtam* adds only the one exception — the renewed altar at dawn. *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* reads *bigishtam* as teaching a different *halacha* entirely: that the washing must be juxtaposed with the actual approach to service (*bigishtam*), which leads to the ruling discussed below.

### Garment Change Order

The verse *bigishtam* is applied by *Rav Acha brei deRav Yaakov* to a different question: *hakol modim bekiddush sheni keshehu lavush mekadesh* — all agree that the second washing of the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — performed after changing from one set of garments to another — must be done while already wearing the new garments, not before donning them. The derivation: *mi sheino mechusar ella gisha bilvad* — the washing must be the final act before the *gisha*, the approach to service. If the *kohen* washes before dressing, the washing is not immediately contiguous with the encounter; he must still don the garments. Therefore, one must first put on the new *begadim*, and only then wash the hands and feet — so that the sole remaining step between the washing and the *avoda* is the approach itself.

This is framed as *kiddush sheni* — the secondary washing between garment changes — and all opinions agree on this point of sequencing, whatever their broader disagreements about which washings are *me'akeiv* and which are not.
