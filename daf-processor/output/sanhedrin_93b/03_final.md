# Sanhedrin 93b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## False Prophets & Fire

### Nebuchadnezzar's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִפִּיל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע אֶת חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה לְכִבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: לֵךְ וְהַחְיֵיה מֵתִים בְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֶחְיָיה אוֹתָן, בָּאוּ עֲצָמוֹת וְטָפְחוּ לוֹ לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע עַל פָּנָיו. אָמַר: מָה טִיבָן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: חַבְרֵיהֶן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ מְחַיֶּיה מֵתִים בְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **At the moment that Nebuchadnezzar the wicked cast Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah into the fiery furnace, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Ezekiel: Go and revive the dead in the Dura Valley. Once** Ezekiel **revived them, the bones came and struck** Nebuchadnezzar, **that wicked** man, **in his face.** Nebuchadnezzar **said: What is the nature of these?** His servants **said to him: The friend of these** three, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, **is reviving** the **dead in the Dura Valley.**

The sugya opens near the bottom of 93a, picking up the narrative of two false prophets who had been exploiting their prophetic claims to take advantage of women. Nebuchadnezzar decided to test them as he had tested Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — by throwing them into the fire. The false prophets, knowing they could not survive on their own merits, proposed bringing a third person whose merit might protect them: Yehoshua Kohen Gadol. They reasoned, *savri leisye Yehoshua de-nevitz zechusei u-magen alan* — let Yehoshua come, for his great merit will shield us. So they brought him, and all three were thrown into the furnace. The false prophets were consumed entirely; Yehoshua Kohen Gadol, however, had only his garments singed. The Gemara cites the verse, *vayar'eini es Yehoshua ha-kohen ha-gadol omeid lifnei malach Hashem*, and the subsequent verse, *halo zeh ud mutzal me-eish* — is this not a brand plucked from the fire? — as the scriptural basis for this account.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָדַעְנָא דְּצַדִּיקָא אַתְּ, אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אַהְנְיָא בָּךְ פּוּרְתָּא נוּרָא? חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה לָא אַהְנְיָא בְּהוּ כְּלָל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִינְהוּ תְּלָתָא הֲווֹ, וַאֲנָא חַד. אָמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אַבְרָהָם יָחִיד הֲוָה? הָתָם לָא הֲווֹ רְשָׁעִים בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וְלָא אִתְיְהִיב רְשׁוּתָא לְנוּרָא. הָכָא הֲווֹ רְשָׁעִים בַּהֲדַי, וְאִתְיְהִיב רְשׁוּתָא לְנוּרָא. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: תְּרֵי אוּדֵי יַבִּישֵׁי וְחַד רַטִּיבָא, אוֹקְדוּהּ יַבִּישֵׁי לְרַטִּיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuchadnezzar **said to** Joshua the High Priest: **I know you are righteous** because you were delivered from the fire, **but what is the reason** that **the fire was slightly effective concerning you,** and for **Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah** the fire **was not effective concerning them at all,** and their garments were not singed? Joshua the High Priest **said to him: They were three** righteous people, **and I am one.** Nebuchadnezzar **said to him: But wasn't Abraham one** person, and when he was cast into the furnace the fire had no effect? Joshua answered him: **There,** in the case of Abraham, **there were no wicked people with him, and license** to cause damage **was not given to the fire. Here,** in this case, **there were wicked** people **with me, and license** to cause damage **was given to the fire.** The Gemara adds that **this** explains the adage **that people say:** If there are **two dry firebrands and one moist one** in a fire, **the dry** firebrands **burn the moist one.**

Nebuchadnezzar then addressed Yehoshua directly: *yadana de-tzaddika at* — I know you are a righteous man. But then he asked: *ela mai ta'ama ahanya becha purta nura* — why did the fire affect you even slightly, when it had no effect whatsoever on Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah? Yehoshua replied: *innu tlatah havu va-ana chad* — they were three and I was only one. Nebuchadnezzar countered: but Avraham was alone when he was saved from the fire, according to the *midrash*. Yehoshua answered: *hasam lo havu resha'im ba-adei ve-lo is-yehav reshusa l'nura* — there, no wicked men were present with him, and the fire was given no permission to operate. Here, by contrast, the wicked ones were present, and the fire had been granted permission — it burned them, and since it was genuinely burning, it touched me slightly as well. The Gemara adds the folk saying: *trei udi yaveshi ve-chad rativa* — two dry logs and one wet one: the dry ones, once alight, cause even the wet one to catch.

### Yehoshua's Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא אִיעֲנַשׁ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁהָיוּ בָּנָיו נוֹשְׂאִים נָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינָן הֲגוּנוֹת לִכְהוּנָּה וְלֹא מִיחָה בָּהֶן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ הָיָה לָבֻשׁ בְּגָדִים צוֹאִים״. וְכִי דַרְכּוֹ שֶׁל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לִלְבּוֹשׁ בְּגָדִים צוֹאִים? אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ בָּנָיו נוֹשְׂאִים נָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינָן הֲגוּנוֹת לִכְהוּנָּה, וְלֹא מִיחָה בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that Joshua the High Priest **was punished** and was cast into the furnace? **Rav Pappa says:** It was due to the fact **that his sons would marry women who were not fit for** marriage into the **priesthood, and he did not reprimand them, as it is stated: "And Joshua was clothed in filthy garments,** and he stood before the angel" (Zechariah 3:3). **And was it the** typical **manner of Joshua to wear filthy garments? Rather** this verse **teaches** an allusion **that his sons would marry women who were not fit for** marriage into the **priesthood, and he did not reprimand them.** That is why he appeared with soiled garments in the vision of the prophet Zechariah.

Yet the Gemara presses further: even granting that the presence of the *resha'im* explains the fire's general power, why did Yehoshua specifically suffer any effect at all? There must have been something in his own record that warranted it. Rav Papa answers: *she-hayu banav nosim nashim she-einam hagunot li-khunah ve-lo michah ba-hen* — his sons had married women who were not fitting for *kehunah*, and he did not protest. The verse that describes him as wearing *begadim tzo'im* — soiled garments — is read not literally but symbolically. As the Gemara asks rhetorically: would Yehoshua actually go about in soiled clothes? Rather, it teaches that his sons' unsuitable marriages splattered back upon him, not as a personal transgression but as a failure to rebuke. A measure of that defilement clung to his garment — something external yet attached — and that accounts for the slight singeing he suffered.

## Ruth & Six Blessings

### Barley Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם: דָּרַשׁ בַּר קַפָּרָא בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״שֵׁשׁ הַשְּׂעֹרִים הָאֵלֶּה נָתַן לִי״? מַאי ״שֵׁשׁ הַשְּׂעֹרִים״? אִילֵּימָא שֵׁשׁ שְׂעוֹרִים מַמָּשׁ, וְכִי דַרְכּוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז לִיתֵּן מַתָּנָה שֵׁשׁ שְׂעוֹרִים? אֶלָּא שֵׁשׁ סְאִין. וְכִי דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה לִיטּוֹל שֵׁשׁ סְאִין? אֶלָּא רֶמֶז [רָמַז] לָהּ, שֶׁעֲתִידִין שִׁשָּׁה בָּנִים לָצֵאת מִמֶּנָּה שֶׁמִּתְבָּרְכִין בְּשֵׁשׁ [שֵׁשׁ] בְּרָכוֹת. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: דָּוִד וּמָשִׁיחַ, דָּנִיֵּאל, חֲנַנְיָה, מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, the Gemara cites that which **Rabbi Tanḥum says** that **bar Kappara taught in Tzippori: What** is the meaning of that **which is written** with regard to the statement of Ruth to Naomi concerning Boaz: **"These six** grains of **barley he gave me"** (Ruth 3:17). **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"six** grains of **barley"? If we say** that the reference is to **six actual** grains of **barley, is it the** typical **manner of Boaz to give** a minimal **gift of six** grains of **barley?** **Rather,** he gave her **six *se'a*.** The Gemara asks: **And is** it **the** typical **manner of a woman to take** a heavy burden of **six *se'a*** of barley? **Rather,** Boaz **alluded to** Ruth **that six descendants are destined to emerge from her who would each be blessed with six blessings, and these are they: David, and** the **Messiah, Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah.**

Rabbi Tanchum cites a *derashah* of Bar Kappara in Tzippori: what is the meaning of the verse *sheish se'orim ha-eileh nasan li* — "these six barleys he gave me," spoken by Ruth? The Gemara considers and rejects both the plain reading — that Boaz gave her six literal kernels, which would be no gift at all — and the alternative that it was six *se'ah*, which would be too heavy for a woman to carry. The answer: *ela remez ramaz lah* — it was six kernels, but he was giving her a symbolic hint, *she-atidim shishah banim latzei mimmenah she-mitbarchin be-sheish sheish berachot* — that six distinguished descendants would emerge from her, each blessed with six blessings. Those six descendants are: David, Mashiach, Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — all, the Gemara will explain, from the Davidic household or at minimum from the tribe of Yehudah.

### David's 6 Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּוִד, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַן אֶחָד מֵהַנְּעָרִים וַיֹּאמֶר הִנֵּה רָאִיתִי בֵּן לְיִשַׁי בֵּית הַלַּחְמִי יֹדֵעַ נַגֵּן וְגִבּוֹר חַיִל וְאִישׁ מִלְחָמָה וּנְבוֹן דָּבָר וְאִישׁ תֹּאַר וַה׳ עִמּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **David** was blessed with six virtues, **as it is written: "And one of the servants answered and said: Behold, I have seen a son of Yishai of the house of Bethlehem who knows to play, and is a fine warrior, and a man of war, and prudent in speech, and a comely man, and the Lord is with him"** (I Samuel 16:18).

The six blessings of David derive from the verse in which one of the lads — identified later as Doeg — spoke to Shaul about the son of Yishai: *yode'a nagen, ve-gibor chayil, ve-ish milchama, u-nevon davar, ve-ish to'ar, va-Hashem imo* — he knows how to play music, he is valorous, a man of war, understanding in speech, handsome, and God is with him. Six attributes, six blessings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַפָּסוּק הַזֶּה לֹא אֲמָרוֹ דּוֹאֵג אֶלָּא בְּלָשׁוֹן הָרָע. ״יֹדֵעַ נַגֵּן״ – שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לִישָּׁאֵל, ״גִּבּוֹר״ – שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לְהָשִׁיב, ״אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״ – שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לִישָּׂא וְלִיתֵּן בְּמִלְחַמְתָּהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, ״אִישׁ תֹּאַר״ – שֶׁמַּרְאֶה פָּנִים בַּהֲלָכָה, ״וּנְבוֹן דָּבָר״ – שֶׁמֵּבִין דָּבָר מִתּוֹךְ דָּבָר, ״וַה׳ עִמּוֹ״ – שֶׁהֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Doeg** the Edomite, one of Saul's servants, **stated this entire verse only as malicious speech,** in an attempt to incite Saul to be jealous of David. **"Who knows how to play"** means **that he knows** how **to ask** complex and germane questions about Torah matters. "A fine **warrior"** means **that he knows** how **to answer** questions raised with regard to matters of Torah. **"A man of war,"** means **that he knows to negotiate** his way **in the battle** to understand the **Torah. "A comely man"** is one **who displays understanding in** facets of ***halakha*** and explains it well. **"And prudent in speech [*davar*]"** means **that he infers** one **matter [*davar*] from** another **matter. "And the Lord is with him"** means **that the *halakha* is** ruled **in accordance with his** opinion **in every area** of *halakha*.

Yet Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav makes a striking observation: *kol ha-pasuk hazeh lo amaro Doeg ela bi-leshon hara* — this entire verse was said by Doeg with an evil tongue. The praise is genuine in content, but the intent was to make Shaul jealous of David. This is *lashon hara* not because the words are false, but because of the malicious purpose animating them. Rashi explains that each praise was framed to provoke royal envy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוּלְּהוּ אֲמַר לְהוּ יְהוֹנָתָן בְּנִי כָּמוֹהוּ. כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״וַה׳ עִמּוֹ״, מִילְּתָא דִּבְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא הֲוָה בֵּיהּ, חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ וְאִיקַּנִּי בֵּיהּ. דִּבְשָׁאוּל כְּתִib: ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִפְנֶה יַרְשִׁיעַ״, וּבְדָוִד כְּתִיב: ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִפְנֶה יַצְלִיחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **In** response to **all of** these virtues listed in praise of David, Saul **said to** his servants: **My son Jonathan is his equal. Once** Doeg **said to** Saul: **"And the Lord is with him,"** meaning that the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with his opinion in every area of *halakha*, **a matter that did not** apply **even to** Saul **himself, he was offended and grew jealous** of David. **As with regard to Saul it is written: "And wherever he turned he put them to the worse"** (I Samuel 14:47), **and with regard to David it is written: And wherever he turns he does prosper.** Although the verse about Saul is referring to his victories and his prominence in Torah, he was not privileged to have all of his conclusions accepted as *halakha*.

The Gemara then unpacks how each praise, read through this lens, actually refers to David's Torah brilliance: *yode'a nagen* — he knows how to ask penetrating questions in learning; *gibor* — he knows how to answer them; *ish milchama* — he knows how to engage in the *give and take* of Torah debate, *lisah u-litein be-milchamtah shel Torah*; *nevon davar* — he can infer one matter from another, *mevin davar mitoch davar*; *ish to'ar* — he can demonstrate the correct *halacha*, *mareh panim ba-halacha*; *va-Hashem imo* — *she-halacha ke-moto be-chol makom*, the *halacha* follows his opinion everywhere.

As each praise was reported to Shaul, the king brushed it aside: *efes, Yonatan beni kamohu* — my son Yonatan is just as good. But when Doeg said *va-Hashem imo*, Shaul could not dismiss it, for that was something even he himself lacked — *bi-milta de-bidida nami lo hava bei*. At that point, *chalash da'atei ve-itkana bei* — Shaul despaired and became jealous of David — which was, the Gemara says, precisely Doeg's goal. The contrast is sharpened by the verses: by Shaul it says *u-ve-chol asher yifneh yarshia* — wherever he turned he acted wickedly — while by David it says *u-ve-chol asher yifneh yatzliach* — wherever he turned he succeeded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן דְּדוֹאֵג הֲוָה? כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיַּעַן אֶחָד מֵהַנְּעָרִים״ – מְיוּחָד שֶׁבַּנְּעָרִים, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְשָׁם אִישׁ מֵעַבְדֵי שָׁאוּל בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא נֶעְצָר לִפְנֵי ה׳ וּשְׁמוֹ דֹּאֵג הָאֲדֹמִי אַבִּיר הָרֹעִים אֲשֶׁר לְשָׁאוּל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** it **was Doeg** who listed the virtues in this verse? The Gemara answers that **it is written here: "And one [*eḥad*] of the servants answered,"** meaning **the most notable [*meyuḥad*] of the servants. And it is written there: "And a certain man of the servants of Saul was there that day, detained before the Lord, and his name was Doeg the Edomite, the chief of the herdsmen that belonged to Saul"** (I Samuel 21:8).

The Gemara then asks: how do we know the anonymous speaker was Doeg? The verse here says *echad mi-ha-ne'arim*, and the same superlative language, *meyuchas she-ba-ne'arim*, appears in connection with Doeg ha-Edomi, called *avir ha-ro'im* — the chief among Shaul's attendants — in a parallel passage.

## Mashiach 6 Blessings

### Mashiach Smells Truth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשִׁיחַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָחָה עָלָיו רוּחַ ה׳ רוּחַ חׇכְמָה וּבִינָה רוּחַ עֵצָה וּגְבוּרָה רוּחַ דַּעַת וְיִרְאַת ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַהֲרִיחוֹ בְּיִרְאַת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The **Messiah** was blessed with six virtues, **as it is written: "And the spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him, the spirit of wisdom and understanding, the spirit of counsel and might, the spirit of knowledge and of the fear of the Lord"** (Isaiah 11:2); **and it is written: "And his delight [*vahariḥo*] shall be the fear of the Lord,** and he shall neither judge after the sight of his eyes, nor decide after the hearing of his ears" (Isaiah 11:3).

The six blessings of Mashiach are drawn from the passage in Yeshayahu: *ve-nachu alav ruach Hashem* — and the spirit of God will rest upon him — followed by *ruach chochmah u-vinah, ruach eitzah u-gevurah, ruach da'at ve-yirat Hashem*. The counting yields six when *da'at* and *yirat Hashem* are treated as a pair or when *ruach Hashem* functions as the overarching heading rather than a numbered item. Rashi, sensitive to the same difficulty, brackets the concluding clause *ve-chtiv va-haricho be-yirat Hashem* as a separate point about Mashiach's special faculty — not an additional blessing in the count, but an explication of how *yirat Hashem* manifests in him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִטְעִינוֹ מִצְווֹת וְיִסּוּרִין כְּרֵיחַיִים. רָבָא אָמַר: דְּמוֹרַח וְדָאֵין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא לְמַרְאֵה עֵינָיו יִשְׁפּוֹט״, ״וְשָׁפַט בְּצֶדֶק דַּלִּים וְהוֹכִיחַ בְּמִישׁוֹר לְעַנְוֵי אָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Alexandri says** that the term *hariḥo* **teaches that** God **burdened** the Messiah **with mitzvot and afflictions like millstones [*reiḥayim*]. Rava says** that *hariḥo* teaches **that** the Messiah will **smell [*demoraḥ*] and** then **judge** on that basis, sensing who is right, **as it is written: "And he shall neither judge after the sight of [*lemareh*] his eyes,** nor decide after the hearing of his ears; **and with righteousness shall he judge the poor, and decide equity for the meek of the earth"** (Isaiah 11:3–4).

Rav Alexandri interprets this faculty homiletically: *melamed she-hit'ino mitzvot ve-yissurin ke-reichayim* — Mashiach will be laden with *mitzvot* and afflictions like a millstone, suggesting that his path will not be untroubled. This resonates with the motif of the afflicted servant and the wars of *Gog u-Magog* that precede the final redemption.

Rava, however, stays close to the plain sense: *de-morach ve-dayin* — Mashiach will be able to smell out the truth. The verse makes this explicit: *ve-lo le-mar'eh einav yishpot ve-lo le-mishma oznav yochiach* — he will not judge by what his eyes see or his ears hear — yet *ve-shafat be-tzedek dalim* — he will judge the poor with righteousness. How, if not through sight or sound? Through a supernatural sense of smell, a divine intuition that bypasses the normal channels of evidence.

### Bar Koziba Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר כּוֹזֵיבָא מְלַךְ תַּרְתֵּין שְׁנִין וּפַלְגָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן: אֲנָא מָשִׁיחַ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בְּמָשִׁיחַ כְּתִיב דְּמוֹרַח וְדָאֵין, נֶחְזֵי אֲנַן אִי מוֹרַח וְדָאֵין. כֵּיוָן דְּחַזְיוּהּ דְּלָא מוֹרַח וְדָאֵין, קַטְלוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Bar Koziva,** i.e., bar Kokheva, **ruled for two and a half years. He said to the Sages: I am** the **Messiah. They said to him: With regard to the Messiah it is written that he** is able to **smell and judge,** so **let us see ourselves whether he,** bar Kokheva, is able to **smell and judge. Once they saw that** he was **not** able to **smell and judge,** the gentiles **killed him.**

Bar Koziba — whose very name is the Gemara's play on *kazav*, falsehood, in contrast to the honorific *Bar Kochba* — ruled, or led his rebellion, for two and a half years. He declared himself the *mashiach*. The rabbis responded by citing precisely this Gemara: by the *mashiach* it is written *de-morach ve-dayin* — he can smell out the truth. *Nechezi anan i morach ve-dayin* — let us see whether he can do this. When they observed that he could not, *katluhu* — they killed him.

This passage raises several difficulties. First, why would the rabbis kill him? Even the Rambam, who writes at the end of *Hilchot Melachim* that Mashiach is not required to perform miracles, addresses this question differently: he says they knew Bar Kochba was not the *mashiach* because he died without fulfilling the messianic promises. The Ra'avad challenges the Rambam directly, pointing to this very Gemara: they knew because he failed the test of *de-morach ve-dayin*. Commentators suggest that even according to this Gemara, *katluhu* need not mean the rabbis themselves executed him; it may mean they did not protect him when the Romans moved against him — a re-reading that softens the tension with historical record and with the question of what legal category a false messianic claim would occupy. Some suggest it resembles *navi sheker*, a false prophet, though Mashiach is not the same as *navi*.

It is worth noting that even this test is not a demand for wholesale transformation of reality. It is a supernatural capacity, but a specific and limited one — which brings it somewhat closer to the Rambam's general orientation than might first appear.

## Daniel's Group

### Exiles' Perfection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִיֵּאל חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: ״אֲשֶׁר אֵין בָּהֶם כׇּל מְאוּם וְטוֹבֵי מַרְאֶה וּמַשְׂכִּילִים בְּכׇל חׇכְמָה וְיֹדְעֵי דַעַת וּמְבִינֵי מַדָּע וַאֲשֶׁר כֹּחַ בָּהֶם לַעֲמֹד בְּהֵיכַל הַמֶּלֶךְ וּלְלַמְּדָם סֵפֶר וּלְשׁוֹן כַּשְׂדִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** Six virtues were ascribed to **Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, as it is written in their** regard: "Youths **in whom was found no blemish, and well favored, and skillful in all wisdom, and discerning in knowledge, and perceptive in understanding, and who had strength in them to stand in the king's palace, and whom they might teach the learning and the tongue of the Chaldeans"** (Daniel 1:4).

The Gemara now turns to Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — four of Ruth's six descendants — each of whom received six blessings. The verse at the opening of Sefer Daniel describes those taken into exile: *asher ein bahem kol mum* — without any blemish — one; *ve-tovei mareh* — of good appearance — two; *u-maskilim be-chol chochmah* — wise in all wisdom — three; *yodei da'at* — knowledgeable — four; *u-mevinei mada* — understanding — five; *va-asher koach bahem la'amod be-heichal ha-melech* — possessing the stamina to stand in the king's palace — six.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֲשֶׁר אֵין בָּהֶם כׇּל מוּם״? אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנַנְיָא: אֲפִילּוּ כְּרִיבְדָּא דְכוּסִילְתָּא לָא הֲוָה בְּהוּ. מַאי ״וַאֲשֶׁר כֹּחַ בָּהֶם לַעֲמֹד בְּהֵיכַל הַמֶּלֶךְ״? אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹנְסִין אֶת עַצְמָן מִן הַשְּׂחוֹק, וּמִן הַשִּׂיחָה, וּמִן הַשֵּׁינָה, וּמַעֲמִידִין עַל עַצְמָן בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּצְרָכִין לְנִקְבֵיהֶם מִפְּנֵי אֵימַת מַלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"in whom was found no blemish"? Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina says: Even** a scratch **like the wound** that remains **after bloodletting was not** found **in them. What** is the meaning of the phrase **"and who had strength in them to stand in the king's place"?** What strength is needed to do so? **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** This **teaches that they would force themselves** to refrain **from laughter, and from conversation, and from sleep, and would restrain themselves when they** felt the **urge** to relieve themselves via **their orifices, due to fear of the monarchy.**

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina explains the phrase *ein bahem kol mum* with striking specificity: *afilu ke-rivda de-chusilta lo hava behu* — they did not even have the puncture mark left by a blood-letting instrument. They had never needed medical bloodletting; their physical perfection was absolute.

He explains *va-asher koach bahem la'amod be-heichal ha-melech* as well: *melamed she-hayu onsin et atzmam min ha-schok u-min ha-sicha u-min ha-sheina, u-ma'amidin al atzmam be-sha'ah she-nitzrechin le-nekaveihem mipnei eimas malchut* — they disciplined themselves to suppress laughter, idle talk, and sleep, and even to hold their bodily needs, out of awe of the royal court. Like sentinels who cannot break formation, they stood in absolute self-mastery.

### Saris Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִבָּנֶיךָ אֲשֶׁר יֵצְאוּ מִמְּךָ אֲשֶׁר תּוֹלִיד יִקָּחוּ וְהָיוּ סָרִיסִים בְּהֵיכַל מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל״. מַאי סָרִיסִים? רַב אָמַר: סָרִיסִים מַמָּשׁ, וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: שֶׁנִּסְתָּרְסָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בִּימֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Isaiah prophesied to Hezekiah: **"And of your sons that shall issue from you, they shall be taken away; and they shall be officers [*sarisim*] in the palace of the king of Babylonia"** (Isaiah 39:7). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***sarisim*? Rav says:** It means **literally eunuchs,** whom the Babylonians castrated to render them suitable for employment in all aspects of the king's service. **And Rabbi Ḥanina says:** It means **that idol worship was emasculated during their lifetime,** as it became clear to all that it lacks substance.

The Gemara brings the prophecy, *u-mi-vanecha asher yetzu mimecha asher tolid yikachu ve-hayu sarisim be-heichal melech Bavel* — your children will be taken and become *sarisim* in the palace of the king of Babylonia — and debates what *sarisim* means here.

Rav says *sarisim mamash* — they were literally castrated, as was standard for those serving in close proximity to the royal harem. Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *she-nistarsa avodah zarah bi-ymeihem* — idolatry was "eunuchized" in their days, rendered impotent through the events described in Sefer Daniel, where the false gods were exposed and defeated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁנִּסְתָּרְסָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בִּימֵיהֶם, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וַחֲבָל לָא אִיתַי בְּהוֹן״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר סָרִיסִים מַמָּשׁ, מַאי ״וַחֲבָל לָא אִיתַי בְּהוֹן״? חַבְלָא דְּנוּרָא. וְהָכְתִיב ״וְרֵיחַ נוּר לָא עֲדָת בְּהוֹן״? לָא חַבְלָא וְלָא רֵיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says that idol worship was emasculated during their lifetime, that is as it is written: "And they have no hurt"** (Daniel 3:25), indicating that the bodies of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were intact and they were not castrated. **But according to the one who says** that they were **literally eunuchs, what is** the meaning of the phrase **"And they have no hurt"?** The Gemara answers: It means they had no **hurt from the fire** of the furnace, not that their bodies were completely intact. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it** already **written: "Nor had the odor of fire passed over them"** (Daniel 3:27)? There was no need to repeat that they were unaffected by the fire. The Gemara answers: This means that there was **neither hurt** from the fire **nor** harm from the **odor** of the fire.

One might ask: does not the phrase *saris ha-melech* in contexts like Esther unambiguously denote literal castration? The Gemara acknowledges this — yes, castration was the practice — but entertains the metaphorical reading as an alternative for these particular individuals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁנִּסְתָּרְסָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בִּימֵיהֶם, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ לַסָּרִיסִים אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁמְרוּ אֶת שַׁבְּתֹתַי וְגוֹ׳״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר סָרִיסִים מַמָּשׁ, מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא בִּגְנוּתָא דְּצַדִּיקֵי? הָא וְהָא הֲוָה בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says that idol worship was emasculated during their lifetime, that is as it is written: "For so says the Lord to the *sarisim* who observe My *Shabbatot*,** and choose what pleases Me, and keep My covenant" (Isaiah 56:4). This verse calls them *sarisim* due to their miraculous deliverance from the furnace. **But according to the one who says** that they were **literally eunuchs,** would **the verse speak in denigration of the righteous?** Would the verse identify Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah by their blemish rather than by their names? The Gemara answers: According to the one who says that they were literally eunuchs, both **this,** the physical imperfection, **and that,** the fact that idol worship was emasculated during their lifetime, **were** true **concerning them.**

The Gemara then tests each position. *Bishlama* for Rabbi Chanina's view: the verse in Yeshayahu, *koh amar Hashem la-sarisim asher yishmeru et shabatotai*, makes sense only if the *sarisim* is being used metaphorically — why would the Torah highlight a shameful physical condition as the defining characteristic of these great men? For Rav's view — that they were literally castrated — the verse from Yeshayahu poses a challenge: why single them out as *sarisim* if that is simply a degrading physical state? The Gemara answers: *ha ve-ha havu behu* — according to Rav, both things were true of them: they were literally castrated, *and* idolatry was rendered impotent in their days. The verse highlights the latter.

The earlier verse's praise that they had no *mum* refers to when they were taken into captivity, before castration occurred. The later verse, *ve-chaval lo isai behon*, refers to the fire: it does not mean they had no wound in general, but that *lo chavala ve-lo reicha* — the fire left neither burn nor smoke on them. The double language indicates two different types of freedom from fire-damage, not a general absence of bodily blemish.

### Book Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי בָהֶם מִבְּנֵי יְהוּדָה דָּנִיֵּאל חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כּוּלָּן מִבְּנֵי יְהוּדָה הֵם. וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: דָּנִיאֵל מִבְּנֵי יְהוּדָה, חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה מִשְּׁאָר שְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara explores more of their attributes. The verse states: **"Now among these were, of the descendants of Judah: Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah"** (Daniel 1:6). **Rabbi Eliezer says: They were all of the descendants of Judah. And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: Daniel was of the descendants of Judah, and Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were** descendants **of other tribes.**

The concluding verse, *va-yihyu va-hem mi-bnei Yehudah Daniel Chananyah Mishael va-Azaryah*, prompts a dispute. Rabbi Elazar says *kulam mi-bnei Yehudah hen* — all four were descendants of Yehudah, consistent with the midrash of Ruth's six descendants from the Davidic line. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani disagrees: Daniel was from Yehudah, but Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were from the other tribes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי כׇּל מִילֵּי דְּעֶזְרָא, נְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה אַמְרִינְהוּ, וּנְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אִיקְּרִי סִיפְרָא עַל שְׁמֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֶחְזִיק טוֹבָה לְעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״זׇכְרָה לִּי אֱלֹהַי לְטוֹבָה״. דָּוִד נָמֵי מֵימָר אָמַר ״זׇכְרֵנִי ה׳ בִּרְצוֹן עַמֶּךָ פׇּקְדֵנִי בִּישׁוּעָתֶךָ״! דָּוִד רַחֲמֵי הוּא דְּקָבָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos books of the Bible named for a prominent person, the Gemara asks: **Now** with regard to **all the matters of** the book of **Ezra, Nehemiah, son of Hacaliah, said them** and wrote most of them; **and** with regard to **Nehemiah, son of Hacaliah, what is the reason** that **a book was not called by his name?** Over the course of many generations, extending many years after the talmudic period, the book that is today named for Nehemiah was not a separate book and was included in the book of Ezra. **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says:** The book was not named for Nehemiah **because he took credit for himself** and boasted about his good deeds, **as it is stated: "Remember me, God, for good"** (Nehemiah 13:31). The Gemara asks: Is that a shortcoming? King **David also said: "Remember me, Lord, when You show favor to Your people; visit me with Your salvation"** (Psalms 106:4). The Gemara answers: **David was asking for compassion** and formulated his words as a prayer. Nehemiah stated them as a fact and a demand.

The Gemara pivots to a discussion of why the book containing the words of Nechemya ben Chachalyah is called Ezra rather than Nechemya. Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba answers: *mipnei she-hechzik tovah le-atzmo* — because Nechemya claimed credit for himself, as evidenced by the verse *zochrah li Elokai le-tovah* — remember this for me, O God, for good. One who demands to be remembered does not end up remembered, at least not by having the book named after him.

The Gemara challenges: did not David say something similar — *zochreini Hashem bi-rtzon amecha, pakdeini bi-shu'asecha*? The answer distinguishes the two: David was *rachamei de-ka-baei* — petitioning for mercy, not demanding credit. The end of the verse, *pakdeini bi-shu'asecha* — visit me with Your salvation — reveals the supplicatory register, not a demand. There is also a grammatical distinction: *zochreini* — remember me — differs from *zochrah li* — remember this [accomplishment] for me — the latter implying a specific claim to credit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסִּיפֵּר בִּגְנוּתָן שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַפַּחוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אֲשֶׁר לְפָנַי הִכְבִּידוּ עַל הָעָם וַיִּקְחוּ מֵהֶם בְּלֶחֶם וָיַיִן אַחַר כֶּסֶף שְׁקָלִים אַרְבָּעִים וְגוֹ׳״. וְאַף עַל דָּנִיֵּאל, שֶׁגָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ, סִיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** Nehemiah was punished **because he spoke in denigration of** his **predecessors, as it is stated: "But the former governors who were before me placed burdens upon the people, and took from them for bread and wine beyond forty shekels** of silver; even their servants ruled over the people; but I did not do so, due to the fear of God" (Nehemiah 5:15). **And he related** these disparaging statements **even about Daniel, who was greater than he** was.

A second reason is advanced: *she-sipar bi-gnusan shel rishonim* — Nechemya spoke disparagingly of his predecessors, complaining that the earlier governors had burdened the people, as stated in the verse *ve-ha-pachot ha-rishonim asher hayu le-fanai hichbidu al ha-am*. One who disparages earlier generations forfeits the right to have the book named after him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּגָדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָאִיתִי אֲנִי דָנִיֵּאל לְבַדִּי אֶת הַמַּרְאָה וְהָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר הָיוּ עִמִּי לֹא רָאוּ אֶת הַמַּרְאָה אֲבָל חֲרָדָה גְדֹלָה נָפְלָה עֲלֵיהֶם וַיִּבְרְחוּ בְּהֵחָבֵא״. ״וְהָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר הָיוּ עִמִּי לֹא רָאוּ אֶת הַמַּרְאָה״ – וּמַאן נִינְהוּ אֲנָשִׁים? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: זֶה חַגַּי, זְכַרְיָה, וּמַלְאָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** Daniel was **greater than he** was? The Gemara answers: It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And I, Daniel, alone saw the vision, and the men who were with me did not see the vision; but a great trembling fell upon them, and they fled to hide"** (Daniel 10:7). The Gemara asks: **"And the men who were with me did not see the vision"; and who were** these **men? Rabbi Yirmeya, and some say Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, says: That** is referring to **Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi,** who were with him and did not see. Evidently, Daniel was greater than these prophets, and all the more so was he greater than Nehemiah, who was never privileged to prophesy.

The Gemara then asks: it says *ve-af al Daniel she-kadmu mimenu siper* — even Daniel, who preceded him, Nechemya disparaged. But how do we know Daniel was greater than those who were Nechemya's contemporaries? The verse in Daniel states: *va-er'ei ani Daniel levadi et ha-mar'eh ve-ha-anashim asher hayu imi lo ra'u et ha-mar'eh aval charadah gedolah naflah aleihem va-yivrachu be-hechavei* — I, Daniel, alone saw the vision, while the men with me did not see it, yet great terror fell upon them and they fled to hide. Rabbi Yirmeyah (or Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba) identifies those men as Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — the prophets of the Second Temple era who were Nechemya's contemporaries. Since Daniel surpassed them in vision, he was surely greater than Nechemya as well. Rashi notes that this passage may have been imported from Tractate Megillah and was not originally part of this sugya, and indeed acknowledges that it is absent from many manuscripts.

### Daniel's Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינְהוּ עֲדִיפִי מִינֵּיהּ, וְאִיהוּ עֲדִיף מִנַּיְיהוּ. אִינְהוּ עֲדִיפִי מִינֵּיהּ – דְּאִינְהוּ נְבִיאֵי, וְאִיהוּ לָאו נְבִיָּא. וְאִיהוּ עֲדִיף מִנַּיְיהוּ – דְּאִיהוּ חֲזָא, וְאִינְהוּ לָא חֲזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Daniel, Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, the Gemara notes: In certain respects, the latter three **were greater than** Daniel, **and** in certain respects, Daniel **was greater than** the latter three. **They were greater than he, as they were prophets and he was not a prophet.** Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi were designated to transmit their visions of God to the Jewish people, but Daniel was not designated to share his visions with others. **And he was greater than they, as he saw** this vision, **and they did not see** this vision.

The Gemara refines the comparison: *inhu adifi minei ve-ihu adif minhaihu* — in one respect those prophets were greater than Daniel, and in another Daniel was greater than them. They were greater because *de-inhu nevi'ei ve-ihu lo navi* — they were full prophets with direct divine communication, whereas Daniel was not. Daniel was greater because *de-ihu chaza ve-inhu lo chazu* — he saw the vision while they could not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּלָא חֲזוֹ, מַאי טַעְמָא אִיבְּעוּת? אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִינְהוּ לָא חֲזוֹ מִידֵּי, מַזָּלַיְיהוּ חָזֵי. אָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַאי מַאן דְּמִבְּעִית, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיהוּ לָא חָזֵי, מַזָּלֵיהּ חָזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since they did not see** the vision, **what is the reason** that **they were frightened?** The Gemara answers: **Although they did not see** the vision, **their guardian angels saw** it, and they were overcome with fear and fled. **Ravina said: Conclude from it** that in the case of **this** person **who becomes frightened** with no apparent cause, **although he does not see** what causes his fear, **his guardian angel sees** it.

This raises an obvious question: if they did not see the vision, why were they overcome with terror? The answer: *af al gav de-inhu lo chazu mazlaihu chazu* — even though they themselves did not see, their *mazal* — some non-conscious, intuitive dimension of their perception — registered the supernatural event, producing overwhelming fear even without conscious awareness of what was occurring.

### Fear & Demons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַאי מַאן דְּמִבְּעִית, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיהוּ לָא חָזֵי, מַזָּלֵיהּ חָזֵי. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לִינְשׁוֹף מִדּוּכְתֵּיהּ אַרְבְּעָה גַּרְמִידֵי. אִי נָמֵי, לִיקְרֵי קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע. וְאִי קָאֵי בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּנּוֹפֶת, לֵימָא הָכִי: ״עִיזָּא דְּבֵי טַבָּחָא שַׁמִּינָא מִינַּאי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Conclude from it** that in the case of **this** person **who becomes frightened** with no apparent cause, **although he does not see** what causes his fear, **his guardian angel sees** it. **What is his remedy? Let him leap four cubits from his** current **location** to distance himself from the perceived danger. **Alternatively, let him recite *Shema*,** which will afford him protection. **And if he is standing in a place of filth,** where it is prohibited to recite verses from the Torah, **let him say this** formula: **The goat of the slaughterhouse is fatter than I,** and let the demon harm the goat instead.

Ravina draws a practical inference: *ha-i man de-miv'as* — one who is suddenly, inexplicably gripped by fear — *af al gav de-ihu lo chazi, mazlei chazi* — even though he sees nothing consciously, his *mazal* perceives something real. The fear is not irrational; something is actually present.

What should one do? *Linshof mi-duchtei arba garmidei* — jump four *amot* from where one stands, for a *shed* or evil spirit may be about to attack, and moving disrupts its proximity. Alternatively, one may recite *Keri'at Shema*, which affords protection. If one is in a place of *tino'fet* — a lavatory or similarly impure location — where *Shema* cannot be recited, one should say: *iza de-vei tavcha shamina minai* — the goats of the slaughterhouse are fatter than I am — meaning: if you are a *sheid* looking for a victim, I am not worth your trouble; go to the slaughterhouse.

## Chizkiyahu Mashiach

### No Shirah = No Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְמַרְבֵּה הַמִּשְׂרָה וּלְשָׁלוֹם אֵין קֵץ וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם: דָּרַשׁ בַּר קַפָּרָא בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, מִפְּנֵי מָה כׇּל מֵם שֶׁבְּאֶמְצַע תֵּיבָה פָּתוּחַ, וְזֶה סָתוּם? בִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַעֲשׂוֹת חִזְקִיָּהוּ מָשִׁיחַ, וְסַנְחֵרִיב גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Hezekiah, the Gemara cites that which is stated: **"That the government may be increased [*lemarbe*] and of peace there be no end,** upon the throne of David, and upon his kingdom, to establish it and uphold it through justice and through righteousness, from now and forever; the zeal of the Lord of hosts does perform this" (Isaiah 9:6). **Rabbi Tanḥum says** that **bar Kappara taught in Tzippori: Due to what** reason **is it that every** letter ***mem* in the middle of a word is open and this** *mem*, of the word *lemarbe*, **is closed?** In the Masoretic text, the letter *mem* in the word "*lemarbe*" is written in the form of a *mem* that appears at the end of a word, closed on all four sides. This is because **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to designate** King **Hezekiah** as the **Messiah and** to designate **Sennacherib** and Assyria, respectively, as **Gog and Magog,** all from the prophecy of Ezekiel with regard to the end of days (Ezekiel, chapter 38), and the confrontation between them would culminate in the final redemption.

The Gemara now turns to the verse *le-marbeh ha-misrah u-le-shalom ein ketz* — "of the increase of dominion and peace there will be no end" — a passage that evokes messianic abundance. The curiosity the Gemara addresses is orthographic: the word *le-marbeh* is written in Yeshayahu with a *mem sofit* — a closed *mem* — in the middle of the word, where an open *mem* would normally appear. Why the anomaly?

Rav Tanchum, citing a *derashah* of Bar Kappara in Tzippori, explains: *bikesh ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu la'asot Chizkiyahu Mashiach* — God wished to make Chizkiyahu into the Mashiach, and to transform the war of Sancherev into the war of *Gog u-Magog*. The historical moment, in other words, was poised to become the messianic denouement of history.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה מִדַּת הַדִּין לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, וּמָה דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר כַּמָּה שִׁירוֹת וְתִשְׁבָּחוֹת לְפָנֶיךָ – לֹא עֲשִׂיתוֹ מָשִׁיחַ, חִזְקִיָּה שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ לוֹ כׇּל הַנִּסִּים הַלָּלוּ וְלֹא אָמַר שִׁירָה לְפָנֶיךָ – תַּעֲשֵׂהוּ מָשִׁיחַ? לְכָךְ נִסְתַּתֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **The attribute of justice said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, and if** with regard to **David, king of Israel, who recited several songs and praises before You, You did not designate him** as the **Messiah,** then with regard to **Hezekiah, for whom You performed all these miracles,** delivering him from Sennacherib and healing his illness, **and he did not recite praise before You, will You designate him** as the **Messiah?** It is **for that** reason that the *mem* **was closed,** because there was an opportunity for redemption that was thwarted.

What prevented it? *Midat ha-din* spoke before God: You created David, who composed all of Sefer Tehillim — *kama shirot ve-tishbachot* — and yet You did not make him Mashiach. Now Chizkiyahu, for whom You performed all these miracles against Sancherev, and who did not respond with *shirah* — you would make *him* Mashiach? *L'kach nistatem* — therefore the *mem* was sealed, closed. Either Chizkiyahu's own voice was sealed — he did not sing — or God's intention to crown him as Mashiach was sealed.

This raises a profound question the lecturer flags explicitly: what exactly is the criterion for Mashiach? Is it not about the righteousness of the generation, the spread of Torah, the merit of the people? How is the presence or absence of *shirah* the pivotal factor? And what does it mean that this was almost a "bottom-up" process — that a particular historical confrontation with Sancherev could have *become* Gog u-Magog, as if the eschatological script were being improvised from unfolding events rather than determined in advance from above?

### Earth's Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד פָּתְחָה הָאָרֶץ וְאָמְרָה לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֲנִי אוֹמֶרֶת לְפָנֶיךָ שִׁירָה תַּחַת צַדִּיק זֶה, וַעֲשֵׂהוּ מָשִׁיחַ. פָּתְחָה וְאָמְרָה שִׁירָה לְפָנָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִכְּנַף הָאָרֶץ זְמִרֹת שָׁמַעְנוּ צְבִי לַצַּדִּיק וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately, the earth began and stated before Him: Master of the Universe, I** will **recite song before You in place of that righteous person,** i.e., Hezekiah, **and designate him** as the **Messiah.** The earth **began and recited a song before Him, as it is stated: "From the uttermost part of the earth have we heard songs: Glory to the righteous.** But I said, my secret is mine, my secret is mine, woe unto me. The treacherous deal treacherously; the treacherous deal very treacherously" (Isaiah 24:16).

The land itself then opened and sang *shirah* on Chizkiyahu's behalf: *patchah ve-amrah shirah lefanav, she-ne'emar mi-kanaf ha-aretz zemirot shamanu, tzevi la-tzaddik* — from the edge of the earth we have heard songs, glory to the righteous one. The earth offered to discharge the *shirah* that Chizkiyahu had omitted, petitioning God: *asehu Mashiach* — still make him the Mashiach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שַׂר הָעוֹלָם לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, צִבְיוֹנוֹ עֲשֵׂה לַצַּדִּיק זֶה! יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: רָזִי לִי, רָזִי לִי. אָמַר נָבִיא: אוֹי לִי, אוֹי לִי, עַד מָתַי? יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״בֹּגְדִים בָּגָדוּ וּבֶגֶד בּוֹגְדִים בָּגָדוּ״. וְאָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עַד דְּאָתוּ בָּזוֹזֵי וּבָזוֹזֵי דְּבָזוֹזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The angel** appointed to oversee **the world said before Him: Master of the Universe, perform the will of this righteous** person. **A Divine Voice emerged and said: "My secret is Mine, My secret is Mine";** this matter will remain secret, as I am not yet bringing the redemption. **The prophet said: "Woe unto me," woe unto me; until when** will the exile continue? **A Divine Voice emerged and said: "The treacherous deal treacherously; the treacherous deal very treacherously"** (Isaiah 24:16). **And Rava, and some say Rabbi Yitzḥak, says: Until looters and looters of looters come,** the Messiah will not come.

Then *sar ha-olam* — the angel charged with the ongoing governance of the world, according to Rashi — added his own petition: *tzevyono aseh la-tzaddik zeh* — do the will of this righteous man. A *bat kol* responded: *razi li razi li* — the secret is Mine, the secret is Mine. The message: it is not merely about the *shirah*. There are reasons, inscrutable to all these pleading advocates, why the moment of redemption has not yet come.

The *navi* — presumably Yeshayahu — cried out: *oy li oy li ad matai ad matai* — woe to me, woe to me, how long, how long? The *bat kol* replied with the verse from Yeshayahu: *bogdim bagadu u-voged bagadu* — the treacherous have dealt treacherously. Rava (or Rav Yitzchak) reads this with a *zayin* rather than a *dalet*: *ad de-asu bezuzai u-vezuzai de-vezuzai* — until the despoilers and despoilers of despoilers come. The point: the wars of *Gog u-Magog* must involve real anguish and suffering for Israel. The episode of Sancherev, for all its drama, left the population of Judea completely unscathed — and that cannot serve as the tribulation that precedes final redemption. The Mashiach cannot come until Israel has passed through a genuine period of collective anguish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשָּׂא דּוּמָה אֵלַי קֹרֵא מִשֵּׂעִיר שֹׁמֵר מַה מִּלַּיְלָה שֹׁמֵר מַה מִּלֵּיל וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתוֹ מַלְאָךְ הַמְּמוּנֶּה עַל הָרוּחוֹת דּוּמָה שְׁמוֹ. נִתְקַבְּצוּ כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת אֵצֶל דּוּמָה, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״שֹׁמֵר, מַה מִּלַּיְלָה? שֹׁמֵר, מַה מִּלֵּיל?״ ״אָמַר שֹׁמֵר אָתָא בֹקֶר וְגַם לָיְלָה אִם תִּבְעָיוּן בְּעָיוּ שֻׁבוּ אֵתָיוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, Isaiah said: **"The burden of Dumah. One calls to me out of Seir: Watchman, what of the night? Watchman, what of the night?** The Watchman said: The morning comes and also the night; if you will inquire, inquire; return, come" (Isaiah 21:11–12). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That angel, who is appointed over the spirits [*seirim*], his name is Dumah. All the spirits assembled near Dumah and said to him: "Watchman, what of the night? Watchman, what of the night?** Does God, Guardian of Israel, say that the time for redemption has arrived?" The angel answered: **"The Watchman said: The morning comes and also the night; if you will inquire, inquire; return, come."** The Holy One, Blessed be He, said that the morning of redemption has come as well as the night of the exile. If you inquire and seek repentance, inquire and repent, and return to God and redemption will come.

The passage from *Massa Dumah* — the prophecy addressed to *Dumah*, identified by Rabbi Yochanan as the angel charged with the souls — follows: *shomer mah mi-laylah, shomer mah mi-leil* — watchman, what of the night? All the souls gathered before *Dumah* asking when the long night of exile would end. The response: *ata voker ve-gam lailah* — morning is coming, but there must first be night. *Im tiv'ayun be'ayu* — if you want it, pray for it. *Shuvu asayu* — return and come, which Rashi reads as: do *teshuvah* and the redemption will follow. The passage closes with a gesture toward longing and active preparation for the Mashiach's arrival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי פַּפְּיָיס: גְּנַאי הוּא לְחִזְקִיָּה וְסִייעָתוֹ שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ שִׁירָה, עַד שֶׁפָּתְחָה הָאָרֶץ וְאָמְרָה שִׁירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִכְּנַף הָאָרֶץ זְמִרֹת שָׁמַעְנוּ צְבִי לַצַּדִּיק וְגוֹ׳״. כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יִתְרוֹ בָּרוּךְ ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הִצִּיל אֶתְכֶם״. תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי פַּפְּיָיס: גְּנַאי הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה וְשִׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ ״בָּרוּךְ״ עַד שֶׁבָּא יִתְרוֹ וְאָמַר ״בָּרוּךְ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in the name of Rabbi Pappeyas: It is a disgrace for Hezekiah and his associates that they did not recite a song** themselves and that a song was not recited **until the earth began and recited a song, as it is stated: "From the uttermost part of the earth have we heard songs: Glory to the righteous"** (Isaiah 24:16). **On a similar note, you say: "And Jethro said: Blessed be the Lord, Who has delivered you** out of the hand of Egypt and out of the hand of Pharaoh" (Exodus 18:10). **It was taught in the name of Rabbi Pappeyas: It is a disgrace for Moses and the six hundred thousand** adult men of the children of Israel whom he led out of Egypt **that they did not say: Blessed, until Yitro came and said: "Blessed be the Lord."**

A *baraita* adds in the name of Rabbi Pappias: it was a *gnai* — a disgrace — for Chizkiyahu and his entire circle that they did not sing *shirah*, to the point that the earth itself had to sing on their behalf. The same *baraita* draws a parallel: it was a *gnai* for Moshe and the six hundred thousand who crossed the sea that they did not say *baruch* — until Yitro came and said *baruch Hashem asher hitzil eschem*. The Gemara explores the two opinions of Rav and Shmuel about the word *vayichad Yitro* — Rav reads it as Yitro cutting his own flesh in circumcision; Shmuel reads it as his flesh becoming covered with goosebumps. Rav then adds the folk proverb: *giura ad asara darei lo tivzei Armaya kamei* — do not disparage a non-Jew in the presence of a convert for ten generations. Yitro, despite his praise of God, still felt some vestigial bond with the Egyptians who had been drowned — illustrating how deeply prior identity runs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּחַדְּ יִתְרוֹ״ – רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל. רַב אָמַר: שֶׁהֶעֱבִיר חֶרֶב חַדָּה עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה חִדּוּדִים חִדּוּדִים כׇּל בְּשָׂרוֹ. אָמַר רַב: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי, גִּיּוֹרָא עַד עַשְׂרָה דָּרֵי לָא תִּבְזֵה אַרְמַאי קַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is written in the previous verse: **"*Vayyiḥad* Yitro** for all the goodness that the Lord had done to Israel, whom He had delivered out of the hand of Egypt" (Exodus 18:9). **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed with regard to the meaning of *vayyiḥad*. **Rav says: He passed a sharp [*ḥad*] sword over his flesh,** i.e., he circumcised himself and converted. **And Shmuel says:** He felt **as** though **cuts [*ḥiddudim*] were made over his flesh,** i.e., he had an unpleasant feeling due to the downfall of Egypt. **Rav says** with regard to this statement of Shmuel that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say:** With regard to **a convert, for ten generations** after his conversion **one should not disparage a gentile before him** and his descendants, as they continue to identify somewhat with gentiles and remain sensitive to their pain.

## Sancherib Account

### Sancherib's 8 Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָכֵן יְשַׁלַּח הָאָדוֹן ה׳ צְבָאוֹת בְּמִשְׁמַנָּיו רָזוֹן״. מַאי ״בְּמִשְׁמַנָּיו רָזוֹן״? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יָבֹא חִזְקִיָּהוּ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁמוֹנָה שֵׁמוֹת, וְיִפָּרַע מִסַּנְחֵרִיב שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁמוֹנָה שֵׁמוֹת. חִזְקִיָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יֶלֶד יֻלַּד לָנוּ בֵּן נִתַּן לָנוּ וַתְּהִי הַמִּשְׂרָה עַל שִׁכְמוֹ וַיִּקְרָא שְׁמוֹ פֶּלֶא יוֹעֵץ אֵל גִּבּוֹר אֲבִי עַד שַׂר שָׁלוֹם״. וְהָאִיכָּא ״חִזְקִיָּה״? שֶׁחִזְּקוֹ יָהּ. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״חִזְקִיָּה״ – שֶׁחִיזֵּק אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"Therefore shall the Master, the Lord of hosts, send among his fat ones [*mishmanav*] leanness"** (Isaiah 10:16). **What** is the meaning of the phrase "send among **his fat ones leanness"?** The meaning is that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Let Hezekiah, who has eight [*shemona*] names, come, and exact retribution from Sennacherib, who has eight names.** The Gemara elaborates: The eight names of **Hezekiah** are **as it is written: "For to us a child is born, to us a son is given; and the government is upon his shoulder; and his name is called Pele Joez El Gibbor Abi Ad Sar Shalom"** (Isaiah 9:5). The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** an additional name, **Hezekiah?** The Gemara explains: That was not a given name; rather, it is an appellation based on the fact **that God strengthened him [*ḥizzeko*]. Alternatively,** he was called **Hezekiah** due to the fact **that he strengthened** the devotion of **the Jewish people to their Father in Heaven.**

The Gemara cites the verse *lachen yeshalach Adon Hashem Tzeva'os be-mishmanav razon* — therefore the Lord God of Hosts will send among his fat ones leanness — and interprets it as a play on the word *shemoneh*, eight: let Chizkiyahu, who has eight names, come and take account of Sancherev, who likewise has eight names. The word *be-mishmanav* is read as alluding to both.

Chizkiyahu's eight names come from the verse in Yeshayahu: *ki yeled yulad lanu ben nitan lanu* — a child is born to us, a son is given to us — followed by *pele, yo'etz, el, gibbor, avi, ad, sar, shalom*. Each word is treated as a distinct name, yielding eight. The Gemara anticipates the objection: does that not give nine, including the name Chizkiyahu itself? It answers that *Chizkiyahu* — meaning *chizko Yah*, God strengthened him — is merely a descriptive epithet, or alternatively, *Chizkiyahu she-chizak et Yisrael la-avihem she-ba-shamayim* — he strengthened Israel's bond with their Father in Heaven — which is again a description, not a proper name.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַנְחֵרִיב, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: תִּגְלַת פִּלְאֶסֶר, פִּלְנְאֶסֶר, שַׁלְמַנְאֶסֶר, פּוֹל, סַרְגוֹן, אׇסְנַפַּר רַבָּא וְיַקִּירָא. וְהָאִיכָּא ״סַנְחֵרִיב״? שֶׁסִּיחָתוֹ רִיב. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁסָּח וְנִיחֵר דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The eight names of **Sennacherib** are **as it is written in his** regard among the kings of Assyria: **"Tiglath-Pileser"** (II Kings 15:29), **"**Tiglath-**Pilneser"** (II Chronicles 28:20), **"Shalmaneser"** (II Kings 17:3), **"Pul"** (II Kings 15:19), **"Sargon"** (Isaiah 20:1), and **"the great and noble Asenappar"** (Ezra 4:10). And the Gemara asks: **But isn't there** an additional name, **Sennacherib [*Sanḥeriv*]?** The Gemara explains: That was not a given name; rather, it is an appellation based on the fact **that his speech is contentious [*siḥato riv*],** leading to quarrel and shame. **Alternatively,** he was called Sanḥeriv due to the fact **that he spoke [*saḥ*] and snorted out [*niḥer*]** contemptuous **statements vis-à-vis the Transcendent.**

Sancherev's eight names are drawn from the various royal names and epithets he bore in the biblical record: Tiglath Pileser (read as one name), Pulneser, Shalmaneser, Pul, Sargon, Asnappar, Rabba, and Yakira — eight in total. Similarly, the name Sancherev itself is treated as a descriptive: either *she-sicho riv*, meaning he was always contentious, or *she-sach ve-nichar devarim klapei ma'alah* — he spoke and snorted blasphemies against Heaven.

### Paroh vs. Sancherib

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע לִקְרוֹתוֹ ״אׇסְנַפַּר רַבָּא וְיַקִּירָא״? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא סִיפֵּר בִּגְנוּתָהּ שֶׁל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַד בֹּאִי וְלָקַחְתִּי אֶתְכֶם אֶל אֶרֶץ כְּאַרְצְכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: For what** reason **was that wicked** person **privileged to be named "the great and noble Asenappar"?** It was **due to** the fact **that he did not speak [*sipper*] in disparagement of Eretz Yisrael, as it is stated: "Until I come and take you to a land like your own"** (II Kings 18:32), and he did not say that he was taking them to a superior land.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: why was Sancherev merited to be called *Asnappar Rabba ve-Yakira* — the great and glorious one? Because *shelo sipar bi-gnushah shel Eretz Yisrael* — he did not speak disparagingly of the Land of Israel. His declaration — *ad bo'i ve-lakachti eschem el eretz ke-artzechem* — I will take you to a land *like* your land — was notably restrained. He acknowledged that their land was good but promised an equivalent, without claiming the destination was superior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר מֶלֶךְ פִּקֵּחַ הָיָה, וְחַד אָמַר מֶלֶךְ טִיפֵּשׁ הָיָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֶלֶךְ פִּקֵּחַ הָיָה – אִי אָמֵינָא לְהוּ ״עֲדִיפָא מֵאַרְעַיְיכוּ״, אָמְרוּ ״קָא מְשַׁקְּרַתְּ״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מֶלֶךְ טִיפֵּשׁ הָיָה – אִם כֵּן, מַאי רְבוּתֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed with regard to that statement of Sennacherib: **One says he was a clever king and one says he was a foolish king. According to the one who says he was a clever king,** he said that he is taking them to a land like their own, as he thought: **If I say to them:** I am taking you to a land **that is superior to your land, they will say: You are lying. And** as for **the one who says he was a foolish king,** he explains: **If so,** if he said that he is not taking them to a superior land, **what is his greatness** and how would they be convinced to go into exile?

Rav and Shmuel debate whether this restraint was cunning or stupidity. According to the view that he was shrewd: had he claimed to offer a better land, the Israelites would have called him a liar, since nothing surpasses the Land of Israel. According to the view that he was foolish: if the new land is only equivalent, what incentive do the people have to leave? The Gemara then notes ruefully that the ten tribes themselves behaved worse than Sancherev in this respect. When they arrived at their place of exile and found it pleasant, they said it was *just as good as*, or even *twice as good as*, the Land of Israel — expressing contentment with exile. This attitude is offered as a partial explanation for why the ten tribes remained lost.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְתַחַת כְּבֹדוֹ [יֵקַד] יְקֹד כִּיקוֹד אֵשׁ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ, וְלֹא כְּבוֹדוֹ מַמָּשׁ. כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קָרֵי לֵיהּ לְמָאנֵיהּ ״מְכַבְּדוֹתַי״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: תַּחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ מַמָּשׁ, כִּשְׂרֵיפַת בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים, אַף כָּאן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional verses written with regard to Sennacherib. **"And beneath his glory shall be kindled a blaze like the blaze of a fire"** (Isaiah 10:16). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** A blaze was kindled **beneath his glory, but** it was **not actually** kindled on **his glory.** The blaze consumed the bodies of the troops of Sennacherib beneath their garments, which were referred to as glory, **as** in **that** practice **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who would **call his garments: My glory. Rabbi Elazar says:** It means **beneath his actual glory,** i.e., the blaze consumed their souls, and their flesh was not consumed, **like the burning of the sons of Aaron. Just as there,** the death of the sons of Aaron entailed **the burning of the soul, and the body** remained **intact, so too here,** the death of the troops of Sennacherib entailed **the burning of the soul, and the body** remained **intact.**

The Gemara addresses the verse *tachas kevodo yekad yekod ki-ykod esh* — beneath his glory shall burn a burning like the burning of fire. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *tachas kevodo ve-lo kevodo mamash* — the *kavod* is not literal. Just as Rabbi Yochanan would call his garments *machbadutai* — that which honors me — so *kavodo* here refers to Sancherev's garments, and the verse means his body will burn beneath his clothes. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *tachas kevodo mamash* — *kavod* refers to the body itself, and beneath the body will burn — meaning the souls will be burned, as in the case of Nadav and Avihu, where *srefat neshama ve-guf kayam* — the soul was consumed and the body remained intact.

### Isaiah's Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָתָה הַמִּבָּלְדַּיּ ה׳ עַל הַמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה לְהַשְׁחִיתוֹ — וְאָמַר: ״אָתָה הַמִּבָּלְדַּיּ ה׳ הִשְׁמַעְנִי אֶל צִדְקִיָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Sanhedrin 94a) With this verse regarding Sennacherib's claim to God:

The Gemara raises the verse: *atah ha-mibaledei Hashem alisi al ha-makom ha-zeh le-hashchiso* — was it without God that I came up against this place to destroy it? *Hashem amar elai: aleh el ha-aretz ha-zot ve-hashchisei* — God told me: go up and destroy it. Sancherev claims divine mandate. What is the basis for this claim? He had heard Yeshayahu's prophecy: *ya'an ki ma'as ha-am ha-zeh et mei ha-Shiloach ha-holchim le-at u-mesos et Retzin u-ben Remalyahu* — because this people has despised the gentle waters of Shiloach and rejoiced in Retzin and the son of Remalyahu.

Rav Yosef observes that without the Aramaic *targum* of this verse one would not understand what it means. The *targum* explains: since this people has despised the kingdom of the House of David — which governs them with gentleness, like the soft-flowing waters of Shiloach — and has instead embraced the rulership of Retzin and the son of Remalyahu, that is, the kings of the northern kingdom, God will bring the King of Assyria against them.

Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse *me'eras Hashem be-veit rasha u-nevei tzaddikim yevorach* — the curse of God is in the house of the wicked and the dwelling of the righteous will be blessed. He identifies the wicked man as Pekah ben Remalyahu, who despite eating forty *se'ah* of doves for dessert was never satisfied, and the righteous man as Chizkiyahu, who was content with a *litra* of vegetables. The spiritual condition of a person is mirrored in how much suffices him.

Yeshayahu prophesied: *ve-Hashem ma'aleh aleihem et mei ha-nahar ha-atzumim ve-ha-rabim et Melech Ashur* — God will bring upon them the mighty waters of the river, the King of Assyria. The subsequent verse adds: *ve-chalaf bi-Yehudah shataf ve-avar ad tzavar yagi'a* — he will pass through Yehudah, flood and pass, reaching up to the neck. Sancherev read this prophecy as his license to conquer Yehudah as well.

The Gemara then asks: if Sancherev was fulfilling a divine mandate, why was he punished? The answer: *navi a'aseret shevatim itnabi* — the prophet was speaking about the ten tribes. *Ihu yahav da'atei a-kula Yerushalayim* — Sancherev extended his ambitions to all of Yerushalayim. The prophecy said the flood would reach the neck — not drown the head. He overreached.

The prophet then came and told Sancherev: *ki lo mu'af la'asher mutzak lah* — there is no weariness for those who are distressed by her. Rabbi Elazar bar Berachyah interprets: *ein am zu ayef mu'af* — this people, weary as it is, weary in Torah — *lo yimasru b'yad mi ha-metzik lah* — will not be delivered into the hands of the one besieging them. You conquered the ten tribes, who *heikilu me'aleihem ol Torah* — treated the yoke of Torah lightly. But now you face the later ones, *Yehudah* and *Binyamin*, who *hichbidu aleihem ol Torah* — who bore the yoke of Torah with full weight. They are *re'uyim la'asot lahem nes* — fit to have a miracle performed for them — like those who crossed the *Yam Suf* and the *Yarden*. If Sancherev retreats, good. If not, *ani e'eseh lo galil ba-goyim* — I will make him as dung among the nations.

## Chizkiyahu Mashiach...

### God's Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָחַר הַדְּבָרִים וְהָאֱמֶת הָאֵלֶּה בָּא סַנְחֵרִיב מֶלֶךְ אַשּׁוּר. אָמַר רָבִינָא: לְאַחַר שֶׁקָּפַץ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְנִשְׁבַּע. וּמַאי טַעְמָא נִשְׁבַּע? בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּמְתִנַּת הַחִנָּם שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, אֲבָל בִּמְתִנַּת קֳּנַס לַיִּשְׂרָאֵל לָא שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. אֲמַר לוֹ: לָא הוּא בְּעִינָא וְלָא בִּיעוּתֵיהּ בְּעִינָא. לָא שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ אִלָּא אִם כֵּן שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ בִּישְׁבוּעָה. דְּמַיְתִינָא לֵךְ לְסַנְחֵרִיב דְּמִטּוּל כִּסְלָךְ תִּיתּוּב עָלָךְ, וְעֻל אַשּׁוּר אוֹדָם מִשְׁנִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Sanhedrin 94a, beginning with the continuation of the verse about Hezekiah:) **The verse states: "After these words and this truth, Sennacherib King of Assyria came and besieged Yehudah."** It seems troubling to reconcile this with the earlier statement of the truth that Chizkiyahu was in the right. **Ravina resolves this:** **After God jumped up and swore.** Why did God swear? **Even if** we accepted **a gift given for free,** we would listen to Chizkiyahu's refusal, **but** with regard to **a gift given as a penalty** levied **on Israel,** we do **not** listen to someone's refusal. Chizkiyahu said to God: **I want neither him nor the dread of him.** God's servants **do not listen to** Chizkiyahu **except if** they **listen to** God **through an oath.** God said: **I will bring Sennacherib to you so that** by virtue of your daring, the **yoke of Assyria will be lifted from your shoulders.**

The verse *acharei ha-devarim ve-ha-emet ha-eileh ba Sancherev Melech Ashur* — after these words and this truth, Sancherev King of Assyria came and besieged Yehudah — seems troubling. After the *emet*, the truth that Chizkiyahu was in the right, Sancherev is rewarded with the ability to lay siege? Ravina resolves this: *l'achar she-kafatz ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ve-nishba* — after God jumped up and swore. Why did God swear? Because God recognized that if He offered Chizkiyahu the gift of Sancherev's defeat in advance, Chizkiyahu would decline: *lo hu be'ina ve-lo bi'utei be'ina* — I want neither him nor the dread of him. No one wants to endure a siege, even for a glorious outcome. So God did not consult Chizkiyahu. He simply swore: *d'mayiteina lei le-Sancherev* — I will bring Sancherev — so that Sancherev would be destroyed on Chizkiyahu's mountain, *ve-al harai avusenu*, and the yoke of Assyria would be lifted from Chizkiyahu's shoulders. The verse's *emet* refers to God's unilateral oath, and only afterward did Sancherev come. Then the miracle occurred and wiped out Sancherev's army.

Rabbi Yochanan adds: *yavo Sancherev ve-si'ato ve-ya'aseh avus le-Chizkiyahu ve-si'ato* — let Sancherev and his company come and serve as a feeding trough for Chizkiyahu and his company. The bodies of the fallen army would lie in the field and become fodder for animals. This is the reading of *avusenu* — we will make of them a feeding trough.

The verse *va-Hashem yeshalach bo davar va-yipol be-Yisrael* — God will send a word against him and it will fall upon Israel — leads to the concluding image: *ve-chuval ol mipnei shamen* — and the yoke will be destroyed because of oil. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha interprets: *chuval ol shel Sancherev mipnei shamno shel Chizkiyahu* — the yoke of Sancherev was broken by the merit of Chizkiyahu's oil, *she-hayah dolek be-vatei knesiyot u-ve-vatei midrashot* — which burned perpetually in synagogues and study halls. This brings us back to the theme of Torah learning as the decisive merit.

## Chizkiyahu's Torah

### Sword at Door

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָעַץ חֶרֶב עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְאָמַר: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה יִדָּקֵר בַּחֶרֶב הַזֹּאת.
>
> **Translation:** (Based on continuation from Talmud) **He planted a sword at the entrance of the *Beit ha-Midrash* — and declared: Whoever is not engaged in Torah will be run through by this sword.**

The Gemara now elaborates on what Chizkiyahu actually did to earn this merit. *Na'atz cherev al petach Beit ha-Midrash* — he planted a sword at the entrance of the *Beit ha-Midrash* — and declared: *kol mi she-eino osek ba-Torah yidaker ba-cherev ha-zot* — whoever is not engaged in Torah will be run through by this sword. Once everyone was inside, the sword at the door ensured they stayed.

### Universal Learning

The results were sweeping. *Badku mi-Dan ve-ad Be'er Sheva ve-lo matzu am ha-aretz* — they searched from the northern border of Israel to the south and found no ignoramus. *Mi-Gevat ve-ad Antipatris ve-lo matzu tinok ve-tinoket, ish ve-isha, she-lo hayu beki'in be-hilchot tuma ve-tahara* — from city to city they found not a single child, man, or woman who was not fully conversant in the laws of *tuma ve-tahara*, the most intricate and recondite domain of halachic knowledge.

This is the picture that now comes into focus: it was not merely that Chizkiyahu was personally righteous. Torah had genuinely permeated the entire nation. The Rambam's own criterion for messianic times — a king who returns the people to Torah and spreads the knowledge of God — was being met. The lecturer emphasizes that this makes the loss of the messianic moment all the more poignant. In the end, the Gemara acknowledges that the failure to sing *shirah* may not have been the only factor preventing the redemption; but now, at least, we understand why the generation of Chizkiyahu was considered genuinely worthy of it.
