# Sanhedrin 93b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## False Prophets & Fire

### Nebuchadnezzar's Test

The sugya opens near the bottom of 93a, picking up the narrative of two false prophets who had been exploiting their prophetic claims to take advantage of women. Nebuchadnezzar decided to test them as he had tested Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — by throwing them into the fire. The false prophets, knowing they could not survive on their own merits, proposed bringing a third person whose merit might protect them: Yehoshua Kohen Gadol. They reasoned, *savri leisye Yehoshua de-nevitz zechusei u-magen alan* — let Yehoshua come, for his great merit will shield us. So they brought him, and all three were thrown into the furnace. The false prophets were consumed entirely; Yehoshua Kohen Gadol, however, had only his garments singed. The Gemara cites the verse, *vayar'eini es Yehoshua ha-kohen ha-gadol omeid lifnei malach Hashem*, and the subsequent verse, *halo zeh ud mutzal me-eish* — is this not a brand plucked from the fire? — as the scriptural basis for this account.

Nebuchadnezzar then addressed Yehoshua directly: *yadana de-tzaddika at* — I know you are a righteous man. But then he asked: *ela mai ta'ama ahanya becha purta nura* — why did the fire affect you even slightly, when it had no effect whatsoever on Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah? Yehoshua replied: *innu tlatah havu va-ana chad* — they were three and I was only one. Nebuchadnezzar countered: but Avraham was alone when he was saved from the fire, according to the *midrash*. Yehoshua answered: *hasam lo havu resha'im ba-adei ve-lo is-yehav reshusa l'nura* — there, no wicked men were present with him, and the fire was given no permission to operate. Here, by contrast, the wicked ones were present, and the fire had been granted permission — it burned them, and since it was genuinely burning, it touched me slightly as well. The Gemara adds the folk saying: *trei udi yaveshi ve-chad rativa* — two dry logs and one wet one: the dry ones, once alight, cause even the wet one to catch.

### Yehoshua's Punishment

Yet the Gemara presses further: even granting that the presence of the *resha'im* explains the fire's general power, why did Yehoshua specifically suffer any effect at all? There must have been something in his own record that warranted it. Rav Papa answers: *she-hayu banav nosim nashim she-einam hagunot li-khunah ve-lo michah ba-hen* — his sons had married women who were not fitting for *kehunah*, and he did not protest. The verse that describes him as wearing *begadim tzo'im* — soiled garments — is read not literally but symbolically. As the Gemara asks rhetorically: would Yehoshua actually go about in soiled clothes? Rather, it teaches that his sons' unsuitable marriages splattered back upon him, not as a personal transgression but as a failure to rebuke. A measure of that defilement clung to his garment — something external yet attached — and that accounts for the slight singeing he suffered.

## Ruth & Six Blessings

### Barley Midrash

Rabbi Tanchum cites a *derashah* of Bar Kappara in Tzippori: what is the meaning of the verse *sheish se'orim ha-eileh nasan li* — "these six barleys he gave me," spoken by Ruth? The Gemara considers and rejects both the plain reading — that Boaz gave her six literal kernels, which would be no gift at all — and the alternative that it was six *se'ah*, which would be too heavy for a woman to carry. The answer: *ela remez ramaz lah* — it was six kernels, but he was giving her a symbolic hint, *she-atidim shishah banim latzei mimmenah she-mitbarchin be-sheish sheish berachot* — that six distinguished descendants would emerge from her, each blessed with six blessings. Those six descendants are: David, Mashiach, Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — all, the Gemara will explain, from the Davidic household or at minimum from the tribe of Yehudah.

### David's 6 Blessings

The six blessings of David derive from the verse in which one of the lads — identified later as Doeg — spoke to Shaul about the son of Yishai: *yode'a nagen, ve-gibor chayil, ve-ish milchama, u-nevon davar, ve-ish to'ar, va-Hashem imo* — he knows how to play music, he is valorous, a man of war, understanding in speech, handsome, and God is with him. Six attributes, six blessings.

Yet Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav makes a striking observation: *kol ha-pasuk hazeh lo amaro Doeg ela bi-leshon hara* — this entire verse was said by Doeg with an evil tongue. The praise is genuine in content, but the intent was to make Shaul jealous of David. This is *lashon hara* not because the words are false, but because of the malicious purpose animating them. Rashi explains that each praise was framed to provoke royal envy.

The Gemara then unpacks how each praise, read through this lens, actually refers to David's Torah brilliance: *yode'a nagen* — he knows how to ask penetrating questions in learning; *gibor* — he knows how to answer them; *ish milchama* — he knows how to engage in the *give and take* of Torah debate, *lisah u-litein be-milchamtah shel Torah*; *nevon davar* — he can infer one matter from another, *mevin davar mitoch davar*; *ish to'ar* — he can demonstrate the correct *halacha*, *mareh panim ba-halacha*; *va-Hashem imo* — *she-halacha ke-moto be-chol makom*, the *halacha* follows his opinion everywhere.

As each praise was reported to Shaul, the king brushed it aside: *efes, Yonatan beni kamohu* — my son Yonatan is just as good. But when Doeg said *va-Hashem imo*, Shaul could not dismiss it, for that was something even he himself lacked — *bi-milta de-bidida nami lo hava bei*. At that point, *chalash da'atei ve-itkana bei* — Shaul despaired and became jealous of David — which was, the Gemara says, precisely Doeg's goal. The contrast is sharpened by the verses: by Shaul it says *u-ve-chol asher yifneh yarshia* — wherever he turned he acted wickedly — while by David it says *u-ve-chol asher yifneh yatzliach* — wherever he turned he succeeded.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know the anonymous speaker was Doeg? The verse here says *echad mi-ha-ne'arim*, and the same superlative language, *meyuchas she-ba-ne'arim*, appears in connection with Doeg ha-Edomi, called *avir ha-ro'im* — the chief among Shaul's attendants — in a parallel passage.

## Mashiach 6 Blessings

### Mashiach Smells Truth

The six blessings of Mashiach are drawn from the passage in Yeshayahu: *ve-nachu alav ruach Hashem* — and the spirit of God will rest upon him — followed by *ruach chochmah u-vinah, ruach eitzah u-gevurah, ruach da'at ve-yirat Hashem*. The counting yields six when *da'at* and *yirat Hashem* are treated as a pair or when *ruach Hashem* functions as the overarching heading rather than a numbered item. Rashi, sensitive to the same difficulty, brackets the concluding clause *ve-chtiv va-haricho be-yirat Hashem* as a separate point about Mashiach's special faculty — not an additional blessing in the count, but an explication of how *yirat Hashem* manifests in him.

Rav Alexandri interprets this faculty homiletically: *melamed she-hit'ino mitzvot ve-yissurin ke-reichayim* — Mashiach will be laden with *mitzvot* and afflictions like a millstone, suggesting that his path will not be untroubled. This resonates with the motif of the afflicted servant and the wars of *Gog u-Magog* that precede the final redemption.

Rava, however, stays close to the plain sense: *de-morach ve-dayin* — Mashiach will be able to smell out the truth. The verse makes this explicit: *ve-lo le-mar'eh einav yishpot ve-lo le-mishma oznav yochiach* — he will not judge by what his eyes see or his ears hear — yet *ve-shafat be-tzedek dalim* — he will judge the poor with righteousness. How, if not through sight or sound? Through a supernatural sense of smell, a divine intuition that bypasses the normal channels of evidence.

### Bar Koziba Test

Bar Koziba — whose very name is the Gemara's play on *kazav*, falsehood, in contrast to the honorific *Bar Kochba* — ruled, or led his rebellion, for two and a half years. He declared himself the *mashiach*. The rabbis responded by citing precisely this Gemara: by the *mashiach* it is written *de-morach ve-dayin* — he can smell out the truth. *Nechezi anan i morach ve-dayin* — let us see whether he can do this. When they observed that he could not, *katluhu* — they killed him.

This passage raises several difficulties. First, why would the rabbis kill him? Even the Rambam, who writes at the end of *Hilchot Melachim* that Mashiach is not required to perform miracles, addresses this question differently: he says they knew Bar Kochba was not the *mashiach* because he died without fulfilling the messianic promises. The Ra'avad challenges the Rambam directly, pointing to this very Gemara: they knew because he failed the test of *de-morach ve-dayin*. Commentators suggest that even according to this Gemara, *katluhu* need not mean the rabbis themselves executed him; it may mean they did not protect him when the Romans moved against him — a re-reading that softens the tension with historical record and with the question of what legal category a false messianic claim would occupy. Some suggest it resembles *navi sheker*, a false prophet, though Mashiach is not the same as *navi*.

It is worth noting that even this test is not a demand for wholesale transformation of reality. It is a supernatural capacity, but a specific and limited one — which brings it somewhat closer to the Rambam's general orientation than might first appear.

## Daniel's Group

### Exiles' Perfection

The Gemara now turns to Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah — four of Ruth's six descendants — each of whom received six blessings. The verse at the opening of Sefer Daniel describes those taken into exile: *asher ein bahem kol mum* — without any blemish — one; *ve-tovei mareh* — of good appearance — two; *u-maskilim be-chol chochmah* — wise in all wisdom — three; *yodei da'at* — knowledgeable — four; *u-mevinei mada* — understanding — five; *va-asher koach bahem la'amod be-heichal ha-melech* — possessing the stamina to stand in the king's palace — six.

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina explains the phrase *ein bahem kol mum* with striking specificity: *afilu ke-rivda de-chusilta lo hava behu* — they did not even have the puncture mark left by a blood-letting instrument. They had never needed medical bloodletting; their physical perfection was absolute.

He explains *va-asher koach bahem la'amod be-heichal ha-melech* as well: *melamed she-hayu onsin et atzmam min ha-schok u-min ha-sicha u-min ha-sheina, u-ma'amidin al atzmam be-sha'ah she-nitzrechin le-nekaveihem mipnei eimas malchut* — they disciplined themselves to suppress laughter, idle talk, and sleep, and even to hold their bodily needs, out of awe of the royal court. Like sentinels who cannot break formation, they stood in absolute self-mastery.

The concluding verse, *va-yihyu va-hem mi-bnei Yehudah Daniel Chananyah Mishael va-Azaryah*, prompts a dispute. Rabbi Elazar says *kulam mi-bnei Yehudah hen* — all four were descendants of Yehudah, consistent with the midrash of Ruth's six descendants from the Davidic line. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani disagrees: Daniel was from Yehudah, but Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were from the other tribes.

### Saris Dispute

The Gemara brings the prophecy, *u-mi-vanecha asher yetzu mimecha asher tolid yikachu ve-hayu sarisim be-heichal melech Bavel* — your children will be taken and become *sarisim* in the palace of the king of Babylonia — and debates what *sarisim* means here.

Rav says *sarisim mamash* — they were literally castrated, as was standard for those serving in close proximity to the royal harem. Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *she-nistarsa avodah zarah bi-ymeihem* — idolatry was "eunuchized" in their days, rendered impotent through the events described in Sefer Daniel, where the false gods were exposed and defeated.

One might ask: does not the phrase *saris ha-melech* in contexts like Esther unambiguously denote literal castration? The Gemara acknowledges this — yes, castration was the practice — but entertains the metaphorical reading as an alternative for these particular individuals.

The Gemara then tests each position. *Bishlama* for Rabbi Chanina's view: the verse in Yeshayahu, *koh amar Hashem la-sarisim asher yishmeru et shabatotai*, makes sense only if the *sarisim* is being used metaphorically — why would the Torah highlight a shameful physical condition as the defining characteristic of these great men? For Rav's view — that they were literally castrated — the verse from Yeshayahu poses a challenge: why single them out as *sarisim* if that is simply a degrading physical state? The Gemara answers: *ha ve-ha havu behu* — according to Rav, both things were true of them: they were literally castrated, *and* idolatry was rendered impotent in their days. The verse highlights the latter.

The earlier verse's praise that they had no *mum* refers to when they were taken into captivity, before castration occurred. The later verse, *ve-chaval lo isai behon*, refers to the fire: it does not mean they had no wound in general, but that *lo chavala ve-lo reicha* — the fire left neither burn nor smoke on them. The double language indicates two different types of freedom from fire-damage, not a general absence of bodily blemish.

### Book Names

The Gemara pivots to a discussion of why the book containing the words of Nechemya ben Chachalyah is called Ezra rather than Nechemya. Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba answers: *mipnei she-hechzik tovah le-atzmo* — because Nechemya claimed credit for himself, as evidenced by the verse *zochrah li Elokai le-tovah* — remember this for me, O God, for good. One who demands to be remembered does not end up remembered, at least not by having the book named after him.

The Gemara challenges: did not David say something similar — *zochreini Hashem bi-rtzon amecha, pakdeini bi-shu'asecha*? The answer distinguishes the two: David was *rachamei de-ka-baei* — petitioning for mercy, not demanding credit. The end of the verse, *pakdeini bi-shu'asecha* — visit me with Your salvation — reveals the supplicatory register, not a demand. There is also a grammatical distinction: *zochreini* — remember me — differs from *zochrah li* — remember this [accomplishment] for me — the latter implying a specific claim to credit.

A second reason is advanced: *she-sipar bi-gnusan shel rishonim* — Nechemya spoke disparagingly of his predecessors, complaining that the earlier governors had burdened the people, as stated in the verse *ve-ha-pachot ha-rishonim asher hayu le-fanai hichbidu al ha-am*. One who disparages earlier generations forfeits the right to have the book named after him.

The Gemara then asks: it says *ve-af al Daniel she-kadmu mimenu siper* — even Daniel, who preceded him, Nechemya disparaged. But how do we know Daniel was greater than those who were Nechemya's contemporaries? The verse in Daniel states: *va-er'ei ani Daniel levadi et ha-mar'eh ve-ha-anashim asher hayu imi lo ra'u et ha-mar'eh aval charadah gedolah naflah aleihem va-yivrachu be-hechavei* — I, Daniel, alone saw the vision, while the men with me did not see it, yet great terror fell upon them and they fled to hide. Rabbi Yirmeyah (or Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba) identifies those men as Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — the prophets of the Second Temple era who were Nechemya's contemporaries. Since Daniel surpassed them in vision, he was surely greater than Nechemya as well. Rashi notes that this passage may have been imported from Tractate Megillah and was not originally part of this sugya, and indeed acknowledges that it is absent from many manuscripts.

### Daniel's Vision

The Gemara refines the comparison: *inhu adifi minei ve-ihu adif minhaihu* — in one respect those prophets were greater than Daniel, and in another Daniel was greater than them. They were greater because *de-inhu nevi'ei ve-ihu lo navi* — they were full prophets with direct divine communication, whereas Daniel was not. Daniel was greater because *de-ihu chaza ve-inhu lo chazu* — he saw the vision while they could not.

This raises an obvious question: if they did not see the vision, why were they overcome with terror? The answer: *af al gav de-inhu lo chazu mazlaihu chazu* — even though they themselves did not see, their *mazal* — some non-conscious, intuitive dimension of their perception — registered the supernatural event, producing overwhelming fear even without conscious awareness of what was occurring.

### Fear & Demons

Ravina draws a practical inference: *ha-i man de-miv'as* — one who is suddenly, inexplicably gripped by fear — *af al gav de-ihu lo chazi, mazlei chazi* — even though he sees nothing consciously, his *mazal* perceives something real. The fear is not irrational; something is actually present.

What should one do? *Linshof mi-duchtei arba garmidei* — jump four *amot* from where one stands, for a *shed* or evil spirit may be about to attack, and moving disrupts its proximity. Alternatively, one may recite *Keri'at Shema*, which affords protection. If one is in a place of *tino'fet* — a lavatory or similarly impure location — where *Shema* cannot be recited, one should say: *iza de-vei tavcha shamina minai* — the goats of the slaughterhouse are fatter than I am — meaning: if you are a *sheid* looking for a victim, I am not worth your trouble; go to the slaughterhouse.

## Chizkiyahu Mashiach

### No Shirah = No Mashiach

The Gemara now turns to the verse *le-marbeh ha-misrah u-le-shalom ein ketz* — "of the increase of dominion and peace there will be no end" — a passage that evokes messianic abundance. The curiosity the Gemara addresses is orthographic: the word *le-marbeh* is written in Yeshayahu with a *mem sofit* — a closed *mem* — in the middle of the word, where an open *mem* would normally appear. Why the anomaly?

Rav Tanchum, citing a *derashah* of Bar Kappara in Tzippori, explains: *bikesh ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu la'asot Chizkiyahu Mashiach* — God wished to make Chizkiyahu into the Mashiach, and to transform the war of Sancherev into the war of *Gog u-Magog*. The historical moment, in other words, was poised to become the messianic denouement of history.

What prevented it? *Midat ha-din* spoke before God: You created David, who composed all of Sefer Tehillim — *kama shirot ve-tishbachot* — and yet You did not make him Mashiach. Now Chizkiyahu, for whom You performed all these miracles against Sancherev, and who did not respond with *shirah* — you would make *him* Mashiach? *L'kach nistatem* — therefore the *mem* was sealed, closed. Either Chizkiyahu's own voice was sealed — he did not sing — or God's intention to crown him as Mashiach was sealed.

This raises a profound question the lecturer flags explicitly: what exactly is the criterion for Mashiach? Is it not about the righteousness of the generation, the spread of Torah, the merit of the people? How is the presence or absence of *shirah* the pivotal factor? And what does it mean that this was almost a "bottom-up" process — that a particular historical confrontation with Sancherev could have *become* Gog u-Magog, as if the eschatological script were being improvised from unfolding events rather than determined in advance from above?

### Earth's Song

The land itself then opened and sang *shirah* on Chizkiyahu's behalf: *patchah ve-amrah shirah lefanav, she-ne'emar mi-kanaf ha-aretz zemirot shamanu, tzevi la-tzaddik* — from the edge of the earth we have heard songs, glory to the righteous one. The earth offered to discharge the *shirah* that Chizkiyahu had omitted, petitioning God: *asehu Mashiach* — still make him the Mashiach.

Then *sar ha-olam* — the angel charged with the ongoing governance of the world, according to Rashi — added his own petition: *tzevyono aseh la-tzaddik zeh* — do the will of this righteous man. A *bat kol* responded: *razi li razi li* — the secret is Mine, the secret is Mine. The message: it is not merely about the *shirah*. There are reasons, inscrutable to all these pleading advocates, why the moment of redemption has not yet come.

The *navi* — presumably Yeshayahu — cried out: *oy li oy li ad matai ad matai* — woe to me, woe to me, how long, how long? The *bat kol* replied with the verse from Yeshayahu: *bogdim bagadu u-voged bagadu* — the treacherous have dealt treacherously. Rava (or Rav Yitzchak) reads this with a *zayin* rather than a *dalet*: *ad de-asu bezuzai u-vezuzai de-vezuzai* — until the despoilers and despoilers of despoilers come. The point: the wars of *Gog u-Magog* must involve real anguish and suffering for Israel. The episode of Sancherev, for all its drama, left the population of Judea completely unscathed — and that cannot serve as the tribulation that precedes final redemption. The Mashiach cannot come until Israel has passed through a genuine period of collective anguish.

The passage from *Massa Dumah* — the prophecy addressed to *Dumah*, identified by Rabbi Yochanan as the angel charged with the souls — follows: *shomer mah mi-laylah, shomer mah mi-leil* — watchman, what of the night? All the souls gathered before *Dumah* asking when the long night of exile would end. The response: *ata voker ve-gam lailah* — morning is coming, but there must first be night. *Im tiv'ayun be'ayu* — if you want it, pray for it. *Shuvu asayu* — return and come, which Rashi reads as: do *teshuvah* and the redemption will follow. The passage closes with a gesture toward longing and active preparation for the Mashiach's arrival.

A *baraita* adds in the name of Rabbi Pappias: it was a *gnai* — a disgrace — for Chizkiyahu and his entire circle that they did not sing *shirah*, to the point that the earth itself had to sing on their behalf. The same *baraita* draws a parallel: it was a *gnai* for Moshe and the six hundred thousand who crossed the sea that they did not say *baruch* — until Yitro came and said *baruch Hashem asher hitzil eschem*. The Gemara explores the two opinions of Rav and Shmuel about the word *vayichad Yitro* — Rav reads it as Yitro cutting his own flesh in circumcision; Shmuel reads it as his flesh becoming covered with goosebumps. Rav then adds the folk proverb: *giura ad asara darei lo tivzei Armaya kamei* — do not disparage a non-Jew in the presence of a convert for ten generations. Yitro, despite his praise of God, still felt some vestigial bond with the Egyptians who had been drowned — illustrating how deeply prior identity runs.

## Sancherib Account

### Sancherib's 8 Names

The Gemara cites the verse *lachen yeshalach Adon Hashem Tzeva'os be-mishmanav razon* — therefore the Lord God of Hosts will send among his fat ones leanness — and interprets it as a play on the word *shemoneh*, eight: let Chizkiyahu, who has eight names, come and take account of Sancherev, who likewise has eight names. The word *be-mishmanav* is read as alluding to both.

Chizkiyahu's eight names come from the verse in Yeshayahu: *ki yeled yulad lanu ben nitan lanu* — a child is born to us, a son is given to us — followed by *pele, yo'etz, el, gibbor, avi, ad, sar, shalom*. Each word is treated as a distinct name, yielding eight. The Gemara anticipates the objection: does that not give nine, including the name Chizkiyahu itself? It answers that *Chizkiyahu* — meaning *chizko Yah*, God strengthened him — is merely a descriptive epithet, or alternatively, *Chizkiyahu she-chizak et Yisrael la-avihem she-ba-shamayim* — he strengthened Israel's bond with their Father in Heaven — which is again a description, not a proper name.

Sancherev's eight names are drawn from the various royal names and epithets he bore in the biblical record: Tiglath Pileser (read as one name), Pulneser, Shalmaneser, Pul, Sargon, Asnappar, Rabba, and Yakira — eight in total. Similarly, the name Sancherev itself is treated as a descriptive: either *she-sicho riv*, meaning he was always contentious, or *she-sach ve-nichar devarim klapei ma'alah* — he spoke and snorted blasphemies against Heaven.

### Paroh vs. Sancherib

Rabbi Yochanan asks: why was Sancherev merited to be called *Asnappar Rabba ve-Yakira* — the great and glorious one? Because *shelo sipar bi-gnushah shel Eretz Yisrael* — he did not speak disparagingly of the Land of Israel. His declaration — *ad bo'i ve-lakachti eschem el eretz ke-artzechem* — I will take you to a land *like* your land — was notably restrained. He acknowledged that their land was good but promised an equivalent, without claiming the destination was superior.

Rav and Shmuel debate whether this restraint was cunning or stupidity. According to the view that he was shrewd: had he claimed to offer a better land, the Israelites would have called him a liar, since nothing surpasses the Land of Israel. According to the view that he was foolish: if the new land is only equivalent, what incentive do the people have to leave? The Gemara then notes ruefully that the ten tribes themselves behaved worse than Sancherev in this respect. When they arrived at their place of exile and found it pleasant, they said it was *just as good as*, or even *twice as good as*, the Land of Israel — expressing contentment with exile. This attitude is offered as a partial explanation for why the ten tribes remained lost.

The Gemara addresses the verse *tachas kevodo yekad yekod ki-ykod esh* — beneath his glory shall burn a burning like the burning of fire. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *tachas kevodo ve-lo kevodo mamash* — the *kavod* is not literal. Just as Rabbi Yochanan would call his garments *machbadutai* — that which honors me — so *kavodo* here refers to Sancherev's garments, and the verse means his body will burn beneath his clothes. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *tachas kevodo mamash* — *kavod* refers to the body itself, and beneath the body will burn — meaning the souls will be burned, as in the case of Nadav and Avihu, where *srefat neshama ve-guf kayam* — the soul was consumed and the body remained intact.

### Isaiah's Prophecy

The Gemara raises the verse: *atah ha-mibaledei Hashem alisi al ha-makom ha-zeh le-hashchiso* — was it without God that I came up against this place to destroy it? *Hashem amar elai: aleh el ha-aretz ha-zot ve-hashchisei* — God told me: go up and destroy it. Sancherev claims divine mandate. What is the basis for this claim? He had heard Yeshayahu's prophecy: *ya'an ki ma'as ha-am ha-zeh et mei ha-Shiloach ha-holchim le-at u-mesos et Retzin u-ven Remalyahu* — because this people has despised the gentle waters of Shiloach and rejoiced in Retzin and the son of Remalyahu.

Rav Yosef observes that without the Aramaic *targum* of this verse one would not understand what it means. The *targum* explains: since this people has despised the kingdom of the House of David — which governs them with gentleness, like the soft-flowing waters of Shiloach — and has instead embraced the rulership of Retzin and the son of Remalyahu, that is, the kings of the northern kingdom, God will bring the King of Assyria against them.

Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse *me'eras Hashem be-veit rasha u-nevei tzaddikim yevorach* — the curse of God is in the house of the wicked and the dwelling of the righteous will be blessed. He identifies the wicked man as Pekah ben Remalyahu, who despite eating forty *se'ah* of doves for dessert was never satisfied, and the righteous man as Chizkiyahu, who was content with a *litra* of vegetables. The spiritual condition of a person is mirrored in how much suffices him.

Yeshayahu prophesied: *ve-Hashem ma'aleh aleihem et mei ha-nahar ha-atzumim ve-ha-rabim et Melech Ashur* — God will bring upon them the mighty waters of the river, the King of Assyria. The subsequent verse adds: *ve-chalaf bi-Yehudah shataf ve-avar ad tzavar yagi'a* — he will pass through Yehudah, flood and pass, reaching up to the neck. Sancherev read this prophecy as his license to conquer Yehudah as well.

The Gemara then asks: if Sancherev was fulfilling a divine mandate, why was he punished? The answer: *navi a'aseret shevatim itnabi* — the prophet was speaking about the ten tribes. *Ihu yahav da'atei a-kula Yerushalayim* — Sancherev extended his ambitions to all of Yerushalayim. The prophecy said the flood would reach the neck — not drown the head. He overreached.

The prophet then came and told Sancherev: *ki lo mu'af la'asher mutzak lah* — there is no weariness for those who are distressed by her. Rabbi Elazar bar Berachyah interprets: *ein am zu ayef mu'af* — this people, weary as it is, weary in Torah — *lo yimasru b'yad mi ha-metzik lah* — will not be delivered into the hands of the one besieging them. You conquered the ten tribes, who *heikilu me'aleihem ol Torah* — treated the yoke of Torah lightly. But now you face the later ones, *Yehudah* and *Binyamin*, who *hichbidu aleihem ol Torah* — who bore the yoke of Torah with full weight. They are *re'uyim la'asot lahem nes* — fit to have a miracle performed for them — like those who crossed the *Yam Suf* and the *Yarden*. If Sancherev retreats, good. If not, *ani e'eseh lo galil ba-goyim* — I will make him as dung among the nations.

## Chizkiyahu Mashiach…

### God's Oath

The verse *acharei ha-devarim ve-ha-emet ha-eileh ba Sancherev Melech Ashur* — after these words and this truth, Sancherev King of Assyria came and besieged Yehudah — seems troubling. After the *emet*, the truth that Chizkiyahu was in the right, Sancherev is rewarded with the ability to lay siege? Ravina resolves this: *l'achar she-kafatz ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ve-nishba* — after God jumped up and swore. Why did God swear? Because God recognized that if He offered Chizkiyahu the gift of Sancherev's defeat in advance, Chizkiyahu would decline: *lo hu be'ina ve-lo bi'utei be'ina* — I want neither him nor the dread of him. No one wants to endure a siege, even for a glorious outcome. So God did not consult Chizkiyahu. He simply swore: *d'mayiteina lei le-Sancherev* — I will bring Sancherev — so that Sancherev would be destroyed on Chizkiyahu's mountain, *ve-al harai avusenu*, and the yoke of Assyria would be lifted from Chizkiyahu's shoulders. The verse's *emet* refers to God's unilateral oath, and only afterward did Sancherev come. Then the miracle occurred and wiped out Sancherev's army.

Rabbi Yochanan adds: *yavo Sancherev ve-si'ato ve-ya'aseh avus le-Chizkiyahu ve-si'ato* — let Sancherev and his company come and serve as a feeding trough for Chizkiyahu and his company. The bodies of the fallen army would lie in the field and become fodder for animals. This is the reading of *avusenu* — we will make of them a feeding trough.

The verse *va-Hashem yeshalach bo davar va-yipol be-Yisrael* — God will send a word against him and it will fall upon Israel — leads to the concluding image: *ve-chuval ol mipnei shamen* — and the yoke will be destroyed because of oil. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha interprets: *chuval ol shel Sancherev mipnei shamno shel Chizkiyahu* — the yoke of Sancherev was broken by the merit of Chizkiyahu's oil, *she-hayah dolek be-vatei knesiyot u-ve-vatei midrashot* — which burned perpetually in synagogues and study halls. This brings us back to the theme of Torah learning as the decisive merit.

## Chizkiyahu's Torah

### Sword at Door

The Gemara now elaborates on what Chizkiyahu actually did to earn this merit. *Na'atz cherev al petach Beit ha-Midrash* — he planted a sword at the entrance of the *Beit ha-Midrash* — and declared: *kol mi she-eino osek ba-Torah yidaker ba-cherev ha-zot* — whoever is not engaged in Torah will be run through by this sword. Once everyone was inside, the sword at the door ensured they stayed.

### Universal Learning

The results were sweeping. *Badku mi-Dan ve-ad Be'er Sheva ve-lo matzu am ha-aretz* — they searched from the northern border of Israel to the south and found no ignoramus. *Mi-Gevat ve-ad Antipatris ve-lo matzu tinok ve-tinoket, ish ve-isha, she-lo hayu beki'in be-hilchot tuma ve-tahara* — from city to city they found not a single child, man, or woman who was not fully conversant in the laws of *tuma ve-tahara*, the most intricate and recondite domain of halachic knowledge.

This is the picture that now comes into focus: it was not merely that Chizkiyahu was personally righteous. Torah had genuinely permeated the entire nation. The Rambam's own criterion for messianic times — a king who returns the people to Torah and spreads the knowledge of God — was being met. The lecturer emphasizes that this makes the loss of the messianic moment all the more poignant. In the end, the Gemara acknowledges that the failure to sing *shirah* may not have been the only factor preventing the redemption; but now, at least, we understand why the generation of Chizkiyahu was considered genuinely worthy of it.
