# Megillah 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Sale Conditions

### Shiva Tuvei Ha'ir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא מָכְרוּ שִׁבְעָה טוֹבֵי הָעִיר בְּמַעֲמַד אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר, אֲבָל מָכְרוּ שִׁבְעָה טוֹבֵי הָעִיר בְּמַעֲמַד אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר, אֲפִילּוּ
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava said: They taught** that there is a limitation on what may be purchased with the proceeds of the sale of a synagogue **only when the seven representatives of the town** who were appointed to administer the town's affairs **had not sold** the synagogue **in an assembly of the residents of the town. However,** if **the seven representatives of the town had sold** it **in an assembly of the residents of the town,** then **even**

The *shiva tuvei ha'ir* — the seven good people of the city — represent the lay leadership of the community, the upstanding citizens who functioned as city elders. The requirement of *b'maamad anshei ha'ir*, the presence of the city's inhabitants, evokes the model of Ephron the Hittite's transaction conducted *b'oznei bo'ei sha'ar iro* — before all who came to the gate of his city — the paradigmatic public and formally witnessed sale. When neither condition obtains — when the sale was conducted by some subset of the community without the elders, or by the elders without the public presence of the entire city — the restrictions on use of proceeds remain in force. But when both conditions are met, the official backing of both the popular will of the city and its recognized authorities combines to effect something more complete.

### Desacralizing Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמִישְׁתֵּא בֵּיהּ שִׁיכְרָא — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **to drink beer with** the proceeds **seems well** and is permitted. The seven representatives have the authority to annul the sanctity of the synagogue, and therefore the proceeds of its sale do not retain any sanctity.

The *gemara* states on 26b: *aval machru shiva tuvei ha'ir b'maamad anshei ha'ir, afilu lemishta bei shichra shapir dami* — but if it was sold by the seven elders of the city in the presence of the people of the city, then even to drink beer in it is permissible. This ruling is striking in its permissiveness, and it raises the question of precisely what process has taken place.

### Lemishta Interpretation

The phrase *lemishta bei shichra* admits of two readings that carry significantly different implications. One reading takes *bei* to mean "in it" — in the synagogue itself — permitting drinking beer on the premises after the sale. The other reads *bei* as "with it" — with the proceeds — permitting the use of the sale money to purchase beer. The immediate context of the *mishnah* is primarily concerned with what may be done with the proceeds, and many *rishonim* accordingly adopt the second reading. Rashi, however, reads the phrase as permitting drinking beer in the synagogue itself, meaning that a properly authorized sale by the elders in the presence of the entire community completely desacralizes the physical building, removing all residual restrictions on its subsequent use.

### Kedusha Mechanisms

This raises a profound conceptual question: what is the mechanism by which the *kedushah* is removed? If *kedushah* operates through classical modes of transfer, one would expect the sanctity to migrate from the building onto the proceeds. But Rava's ruling suggests that when the sale is conducted with sufficient communal authority, the money can be used for entirely unrestricted purposes. Where, then, does the *kedushah* go?

The Ramban addresses this difficulty by largely stepping back from the classical language of *kedushah* transfer. Rather than treating the synagogue as an object of genuine *kedushah* in the manner of objects bearing *shem Hashem*, the Ramban frames the restrictions primarily in terms of *kavod* — respect and propriety — for something that was once used for a *mitzvah*. Under that framework, when the entire community formally and publicly assents to a change of use, there is no violation of propriety, because the community's collective authorization reshapes what is considered respectful in these circumstances. This explains why broad communal consensus can free the proceeds for any use, without needing to account for a transfer of sanctity to a specific object.

Other *rishonim* prefer to maintain a *kedushah* model but apply it more flexibly, treating the *kedushah* of a synagogue as rabbinic rather than biblical in nature and therefore amenable to mechanisms that would not suffice for objects of intrinsic sanctity such as a *sefer Torah*. The mechanisms here genuinely do not operate as they classically do, which is precisely why the *rishonim* grapple with the appropriate model to apply.

## Ravina's Mound Case

### Fallen Synagogue Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא תִּילָּא דְּבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְמִיזְרְעֵהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל זַבְנֵיהּ מִשִּׁבְעָה טוֹבֵי הָעִיר בְּמַעֲמַד אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר, וְזַרְעֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ravina had a certain** piece of land on which stood **a mound** of the ruins **of a synagogue. He came before Rav Ashi** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to sowing** the land? **He said to him: Go, purchase it from the seven representatives of the town in an assembly of the residents of the town, and** then you may **sow it.**

This case is somewhat puzzling. The text says Ravina "had" the mound, yet Rava instructs him to go purchase it, suggesting he was interested in acquiring it. More significantly, it is not immediately obvious that the discussion here is about the proceeds of sale at all — there is no money mentioned. This case lends support to Rashi's reading of the earlier ruling: that conducting a formal sale with both the elders and the people present allows broad latitude with respect to the synagogue itself, not merely the proceeds. Various *rishonim* who wish to confine the discussion exclusively to the use of money attempt to reinterpret this case, but it does sit more comfortably with the reading that the formal communal sale process also liberates the synagogue building for other uses.

## Demolition Debate

### Material Salvage Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא הֲוָה קָא בָנֵי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא. הֲוָה הָהִיא כְּנִישְׁתָּא עַתִּיקָא, הֲוָה בָּעֵי לְמִיסְתְּרַיהּ וּלְאֵתוֹיֵי לִיבְנֵי וּכְשׁוּרֵי מִינַּהּ וְעַיּוֹלֵי לְהָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Abba was** once **building a synagogue. There was a certain old synagogue** that **he wished to demolish, and bring bricks and beams from it, and bring them to there,** to construct a new synagogue.

Why incur the cost of new materials when suitable materials were available from a defunct building?

### Rav Chisda's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא לִיסְתּוֹר בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא עַד דְּבָנֵי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי — הָתָם מִשּׁוּם פְּשִׁיעוּתָא
>
> **Translation:** **He sat and considered that which Rav Ḥisda** said, **as Rav Ḥisda said: One should not demolish a synagogue until one has built another synagogue.** Rami bar Abba reasoned that Rav Ḥisda's ruling **there** is **due to** a concern of **negligence,**

Before acting, Rami bar Abba recalled the ruling of Rav Chisda from Bava Batra: *lo listrei bei knishta ad d'bani bei knishta achriti* — one may not demolish a synagogue until another synagogue has been built.

### Negligence Concern

The rationale given is *mishum peshiuta* — out of concern for negligence. The worry is that once the old synagogue is torn down, the community may never get around to completing the new one, leaving the community without a synagogue and the demolished building without having been replaced.

### Queries to Authorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא מַאי? אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא וַאֲסַר לֵיהּ. לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא וַאֲסַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **a case like this,** where the new synagogue is to be built directly from the materials of the old one, **what** is the *halakha*? **He came before Rav Pappa** to ask his opinion, **and he prohibited him** from doing so. **He** then came **before Rav Huna, and he** also **prohibited him** from doing so.

The *gemara* does not explain the precise grounds for their rulings. One possibility is *lo plug* — the rabbis issued a blanket rule and did not create exceptions even for cases where the rationale does not clearly apply. Another possibility is that the issue is not only *peshiuta* but also a question of disrespect: perhaps an unsold synagogue retains its status as a synagogue, and even if it is defunct and falling apart, it cannot simply be dismantled so long as it has not been formally sold. Until a proper sale has taken place, the building retains its status — and that status may restrict what can be done to it even by its owner. The question of exactly what is permitted before a sale has occurred will resurface in a subsequent discussion.

## Sale & Exchange

### Chalufa & Zvuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא, חַלּוֹפַהּ וְזַבּוֹנַהּ — שְׁרֵי, אוֹגוֹרַהּ וּמַשְׁכּוֹנַהּ — אֲסִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא: בִּקְדוּשְׁתַּהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With regard to **this synagogue, exchanging it** for a different building **or selling it** for money **is permitted,** but **renting it** out **or mortgaging it is prohibited. What is the reason** for this? When a synagogue is rented out or mortgaged, it **remains in its sacred state.** Therefore, it is prohibited to rent it out or mortgage it, because it will then be used for a non-sacred purpose. However, if it is exchanged or sold, its sanctity is transferred to the other building or to the proceeds of the sale, and therefore the old synagogue building may be used for any purpose.

An exchange — a property swap — is treated as a form of sale, and through either mechanism the building loses its status as a synagogue. Once it has been sold or exchanged, the building itself is no longer subject to any restrictions based on its former sanctity.

### Rental & Collateral

By contrast, *ogura u'mashkona* — renting the building out or using it as collateral — is forbidden. Renting and pledging are not sales: the building remains a synagogue throughout. *B'kdushatah kai* — it retains its sanctity until it has actually been sold. Until a genuine transfer of ownership occurs, the status of the building remains intact, and no use inconsistent with that status is permitted.

### Bricks Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיבְנֵי נָמֵי, חַלּוֹפִינְהוּ וְזַבּוֹנִינְהוּ — שְׁרֵי, אוֹזוֹפִינְהוּ — אֲסִיר. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעַתִּיקָתָא, אֲבָל בְּחַדְתָּ[תָ]א — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The same *halakha* is **also** true of the **bricks** of a synagogue; **exchanging them or selling them is permitted,** but **renting them out is prohibited.** The Gemara comments: **This applies to old** bricks that have already been part of a synagogue, **but as for new** bricks that have only been designated to be used in a synagogue, **we have no** problem **with it** if they are rented out for a non-sacred purpose.

The same analysis applies to bricks. If one has bricks from an old synagogue — bricks that have already been set into the structure and used as part of the building — they follow the same rule: selling or exchanging them is permitted, and thereby desacralizes them; lending them out (*azfun'hu*) is forbidden. These are *atikta* — old, previously used bricks that have already acquired their status.

### Hazmanah & Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַזְמָנָה מִילְּתָא הִיא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי כְּגוֹן הָאוֹרֵג בֶּגֶד לַמֵּת. אֲבָל הָכָא, כְּטָווּי לְאָרִיג דָּמֵי, וְלֵיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And even according to the one who said** that mere **designation is significant,** i.e., although a certain object was not yet used for the designated purpose, the halakhic ramifications of using it for that purpose already take hold, **this applies** only in a case where it was created from the outset for that purpose, **for example, one who weaves a garment** to be used as shrouds **for a corpse. However, here** the bricks are **comparable to** already **spun** thread that was then designated to be used **to weave** burial shrouds. Concerning such designation, where nothing was specifically created for the designated purpose, **there is no one who said** that the designation is significant.

However, *aval b'chadta* — if one has simply accumulated new bricks that were purchased with the intention of building a synagogue but have not yet been incorporated into one, *leit lan bah* — they carry no sanctity whatsoever. The mere intention to use them does not confer any sacred status.

This ruling touches on a broader conceptual debate: *ve'afilu man d'amar hazmanah milta hi* — even according to the opinion that *hazmanah*, designation or preparation, has legal significance even before an object is actually used. That opinion holds, for example, that if someone weaves a shroud specifically for a corpse, it acquires the status of burial shrouds even before it is placed on the body. But the *gemara* clarifies that this applies *k'gon d'oreg beged l'meit* — to something already fully fashioned and ready for its purpose. Here, having a pile of bricks is not analogous to a woven garment; it is analogous only to having spun the thread. *Aval hacha ketavya le'arig dami* — it corresponds to the preparatory stage before weaving, not to the completed garment. Even the opinion that *hazmanah* is significant would agree that unbuilt bricks carry no sanctity.

### Gift Controversy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתָּנָה, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָסַר, וְחַד שָׁרֵי. מַאן דְּאָסַר: בְּמַאי תִּפְקַע קְדוּשְׁתַּהּ?! וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי: אִי לָאו דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ — לָא הֲוָה יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, הֲדַר הָוֵה לַיהּ מַתָּנָה כִּזְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree about** whether it is permitted to give away a synagogue as **a gift** to then be used for a non-sacred purpose. **One** of them **prohibited** it, **and** the other **one permitted** it. **The one who prohibits** it says: Is it possible that **with this** act of giving alone **its sanctity is removed?** This cannot be the case. Since the synagogue was not exchanged for anything else, there is nothing to which the sanctity may be transferred. Consequently, the synagogue remains sacred. **And the one who permitted** it does so because he reasons that **if** the donor **did not** receive any **benefit from** giving the synagogue, **he would not have given it.** Therefore, **the gift has reverted to being like a sale,** and the sanctity is transferred to the benefit received.

A related question is whether giving the synagogue as a gift — rather than selling or exchanging it — suffices to remove its sanctity. *Pligi Rav Acha u'Ravina* — Rav Acha and Ravina disputed this. One forbade it and one permitted it. The one who forbade asked: *b'hei tifka kedushateh* — through what mechanism would the *kedushah* be removed? In a sale or exchange, the *kedushah* is transferred onto something of concrete value. In a gift, nothing is received in return, and it is unclear onto what the *kedushah* migrates.

The one who permitted argued that a gift is never truly unilateral: *ei lav de'hava lei hana'ah minei, lo hava yahiv lei* — if the giver had not previously derived some benefit from the recipient, he would not now be giving him a gift. Some form of reciprocal exchange underlies every gift, and therefore the mechanism of transfer is present.

Yet, as the Ramban notes, this argument is difficult to sustain within a classical *kedushah* framework. The fact that a year ago the recipient lent one a tool does not explain where the *kedushah* of the synagogue is actually going when one gives it away today. There is no identifiable object onto which the *kedushah* is transferred. This is precisely why the Ramban resists the language of classical *kedushah* transfer entirely and frames the entire discussion around *kavod* and propriety rather than ontological sanctity.

The difficulties are compounded further when one considers the earlier ruling that when the sale is authorized by *shiva tuvei ha'ir b'maamad anshei ha'ir*, even the proceeds carry no restricted status. If *kedushah* were genuinely transferred onto the money, one would expect the proceeds always to be sanctified regardless of who conducts the sale. The fact that the proceeds go free under certain conditions makes it genuinely hard to explain the mechanism in classical terms. This is why the *rishonim* either argue for a more flexible, rabbinic model of *kedushah* here, or follow the Ramban in stepping back from the *kedushah* framework altogether.

## Tashmishei Categories

### Mitzvah vs. Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי מִצְוָה — נִזְרָקִין. תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי קְדוּשָּׁה — נִגְנָזִין. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי מִצְוָה: סוּכָּה, לוּלָב, שׁוֹפָר, צִיצִית. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי קְדוּשָּׁה: דְּלוֹסְקְמֵי סְפָרִים, תְּפִילִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת, וְתִיק שֶׁל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, וְנַרְתִּיק שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין וּרְצוּעוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Articles** used in the performance **of a mitzva may be thrown out** after use. Although these items were used in the performance of a mitzva, they are not thereby sanctified. However, **articles** associated with the **sanctity** of God's name, i.e. articles on which God's name is written, and articles that serve an article that has God's name written on it, even after they are no longer used, **must be interred** in a respectful manner. **And these** items **are** considered **articles of a mitzva: A *sukka*; a *lulav*; a *shofar*;** and **ritual fringes. And these** items **are** considered **articles of sanctity: Cases** of **scrolls,** i.e. of Torah scrolls; **phylacteries; and *mezuzot*; and a container for a Torah scroll; and a cover for phylacteries; and their straps.**

The *braisa* enumerates the *tashmishei mitzvah*: *sukkah, lulav, shofar, tzitzit*. Once these have fulfilled their purpose, one is not required to treat them with ongoing reverence; while one should not treat them with outright disrespect (*minhag bizayon*), they may be discarded.

### Kedusha Objects

The *tashmishei kedushah* are: *dluska'mei sfarim* — sacks used for scrolls, *tefillin u'mezuzot*, and specifically the *tik shel sefer Torah* — the mantle of the *sefer Torah*, the *nartik shel tefillin* — the bag for *tefillin*, and *retzuoteihen* — the straps of the *tefillin*. All of these stand in immediate association with the *sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* themselves. When these objects wear out, they must be buried — placed into *sheimos*.

This is precisely the category into which the Ramban wants to place — or, more accurately, to avoid placing — the synagogue. If the synagogue were *tashmishei kedushah*, its sanctity would be intrinsic and inalienable, its proceeds always restricted, and there would be no mechanism of desacralization. The Ramban therefore insists that the synagogue belongs in the *tashmishei mitzvah* category, where respect is required but formal sanctity is not, and where a community's decision to change its use does not implicate *kedushah* transfer at all.

### Bimah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הַאי כּוּרְסְיָא, תַּשְׁמִישׁ דְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הוּא, וּשְׁרֵי. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזֵינָא דְּמוֹתְבִי עִלָּוֵיהּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, אָמֵינָא: תַּשְׁמִישׁ קְדוּשָּׁה הוּא, וַאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Initially, I used to say** that **this lectern** in the synagogue upon which the Torah is read **is** only **an article of an article** of sanctity, as the Torah scroll does not rest directly upon the lectern but rather upon the cloth that covers it. **And** the *halakha* is that once an article of an article of sanctity is no longer used, **it is permitted** to throw it out. However, **once I saw that the Torah scroll is** sometimes **placed** directly **upon** the lectern without an intervening cloth. **I said** that **it is an article** used directly for items **of sanctity, and** as such **it is prohibited** to simply discard it after use.

Rava traces the evolution of his own thinking on specific objects. *Me'reish hava amina* — initially, Rava said, *hai korsaya tashmish de'tashmish hu ve'shari* — the *korsaya*, which Rashi identifies as the *bimah*, is a *tashmish* of a *tashmish*, two degrees removed from the *sefer Torah* itself. The *sefer Torah* rests on a cover; the cover rests on the *bimah*. Being twice removed, it should be freely usable. But Rava revised this assessment: *keivan de'hazina de'mosvei lei sefer Torah* — since he observed that sometimes a *sefer Torah* is placed directly on the *bimah*, without any intervening cover, *amina tashmish kedushah hu ve'asar* — he concluded it must be treated as *tashmish kedushah*, and its use is forbidden. Notably, this applies even though it is only an occasional practice to place the *sefer Torah* directly on the *bimah*.

### Parochet Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא, מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הַאי פְּרִיסָא, תַּשְׁמִישׁ דְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הוּא. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזֵינָא דְּעָיְיפִי לֵיהּ וּמַנְּחִי סִיפְרָא עִלָּוֵיהּ, אָמֵינָא: תַּשְׁמִישׁ קְדוּשָּׁה הוּא, וַאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** similarly **said: Initially, I used to say** that **this curtain,** which is placed at the opening to the ark as a decoration, **is** only **an article of an article** of sanctity, as it serves to beautify the ark but is not directly used for the Torah scroll. However, **once I saw that** sometimes the curtain **is folded over and a Torah scroll is placed upon it. I said** that **it is an article** used directly for items **of sanctity and** as such **it is prohibited** to simply discard it after use.

Rava applied the same reconsideration to the *prisa* — the curtain hung before the *aron*. Initially he had classified it as *tashmish de'tashmish*: the *aron* serves the *sefer Torah*, and the curtain serves the *aron*. But upon observing that *de'aifilei u'manchi sifa lei* — sometimes they wrap it and place the *sefer Torah* directly on it or against it — *amina tashmish kedushah hu ve'asar* — he concluded it too is *tashmish kedushah*. The *parochet* comes into direct contact with the *sefer Torah*, if only occasionally, and that suffices to elevate its status.

## Roman Synagogue Case

### Dead Body Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא (דִּיהוּדָאֵי) [דְּ]רוֹמָאֵי דַּהֲוָה פְּתִיחַ לְהָהוּא אִידְּרוֹנָא דַּהֲוָה מַחֵית בֵּיהּ מֵת, וַהֲווֹ בָּעוּ כָּהֲנֵי לְמֵיעַל לְצַלּוֹיֵי הָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain synagogue of the Jews of Rome that opened out into a room in which a corpse was lying,** thereby spreading the ritual impurity of the corpse throughout the synagogue. **And the priests wished to enter** the synagogue **in order to pray there.** However, it was prohibited for them to do so because a priest may not come in contact with ritual impurity of a corpse.

The *gemara* presents a practically urgent case. *Hahu bei knishta deromai* — there was a synagogue belonging to Roman Jews. Rashi explains that this was in Babylonia, built by Jewish immigrants from Rome who maintained their own communal synagogue, as was common throughout history when diaspora communities within a diaspora would maintain the practices of their place of origin. *D'hava patiach lehu idrona* — this synagogue had an opening into an adjoining room. *D'hava manach bei meis* — and a dead body lay in that room, presumably used for preparing the deceased for burial. *V'havu ba'u kohanei lemei'al litzloyei hatam* — the *kohanim* wished to enter the synagogue to pray. They did not wish to pray elsewhere; this was their synagogue, their communal house of prayer. But with an opening directly connecting to a room containing a corpse, the *kohanim* faced a problem of *tum'at ohel* — the *tumah* of the corpse spreading through the shared airspace.

### Ark as Barrier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: דַּלּוֹ תֵּיבוּתָא אוֹתְבוּהָ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּלִי עֵץ הֶעָשׂוּי לְנַחַת, וּכְלִי עֵץ הֶעָשׂוּי לְנַחַת אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה וְחוֹצֵץ בִּפְנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: Lift up the ark and put it down** in the opening between the two rooms, **as it is a wooden utensil that is designated to rest** in one place and not be moved from there, **and** the *halakha* is that **a wooden utensil that is designated to rest is not susceptible to ritual impurity, and** therefore it **serves as a barrier to** prevent **ritual impurity** from spreading.

Rava proposed a solution: *dalu teivusa v'osvuha* — take the ark and place it in the doorway opening onto the other room. The halakhic basis for this was *kli etz ha'asuy l'nachas* — a wooden vessel made to remain in one place. Such a vessel cannot become *tamei*, and a vessel that cannot become *tamei* can serve as *chotzetz lifnei hatumah* — a barrier that prevents the *tumah* from penetrating into the synagogue.

### Portability Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: וְהָא זִמְנִין דִּמְטַלְטְלִי לֵיהּ כִּי מַנַּח סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עִלָּוֵיהּ, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִיטַּלְטֵל מָלֵא וְרֵיקָם! אִי הָכִי לָא אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to Rava: But isn't** the ark **sometimes moved when a Torah scroll is** still **resting inside it, and** therefore **it is** a utensil that **is moved** both **when it is full and when it is empty;** such a utensil is susceptible to ritual impurity and cannot prevent ritual impurity from spreading. He said to them: **If so,** if it is as you claim, then **it is not possible** to remedy the situation.

The rabbis challenged Rava's proposal: *v'ha zimnin demitalteli lei ki manach sefer Torah b'gavieh* — but there are times when the ark is moved even when a *sefer Torah* is inside it. *V'ha havei mitaltel malei v'reikam* — and it is a vessel that is moved both when full and when empty. This is the precise opposite of a *kli ha'asuy l'nachas*. The *kli etz* exemption from receiving *tumah* applies specifically to a large wooden vessel that is made to remain stationary — a vessel that is regularly transported does not qualify.

### Concession

Rava conceded the point. *Ei hachi lo efshar* — if that is the case, there is no solution. The situation remains unresolved.

## Tashmishei Mitzvah (Continued)

### Worn Wraps Reuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: מִטְפְּחוֹת סְפָרִים שֶׁבָּלוּ — עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן תַּכְרִיכִין לְמֵת מִצְוָה, וְזוֹ הִיא גְּנִיזָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said:** With regard to **wrapping cloths of** Torah **scrolls that have become worn out, they may be made into shrouds for a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*], and this is their** most appropriate manner for being **interred.**

Mar Zutra rules: *mitpachot sefarim shebalu, osim otam tachrichin l'meit mitzvah, v'zu hi genizatan* — wraps that were used for *sefarim* and have become worn out may be made into burial shrouds for the deceased, and this constitutes their *genizah* — their proper burial. Since these wraps are *tashmishei kedushah* that must be buried rather than discarded, making them into shrouds accomplishes two purposes simultaneously: they are buried, and they are used for a *mitzvah*. This sidesteps the question of *ma'alin bakodesh* entirely — one need not worry about whether this represents an elevation or a diminution, since the primary halakhic act being performed is the required burial itself.

### Torah Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁבָּלָה — גּוֹנְזִין אוֹתוֹ אֵצֶל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם, וַאֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹנֶה הֲלָכוֹת. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: וּבִכְלִי חֶרֶס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּנְתַתָּם בִּכְלִי חָרֶשׂ לְמַעַן יַעַמְדוּ יָמִים רַבִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: A Torah scroll that became worn out is interred** and buried **next to a Torah scholar, and** in this regard, a Torah scholar is defined **even** as **one who** only **studies the *halakhot*** in the Mishna and the *baraitot* but is not proficient in their analysis. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: And** when it is buried, it is first placed **in an earthenware vessel, as it is stated: "And put them in an earthenware vessel, that they may last for many days"** (Jeremiah 32:14).

Rava rules: *sefer Torah shebala, gonzim oto etzel talmid chacham* — a *sefer Torah* that has worn out should be buried near a *talmid chacham*. Even if the person in question is not a great analytical scholar but merely one who has memorized a body of *halachot* — *afilu shoneh halachot* — it is still appropriate and respectful to bury the physical Torah scroll near one who himself represented Torah. Rav Acha bar Yaakov adds: *uvichli cheres* — it should be buried in a pottery vessel, as the verse states, *u'natatam bichli cheres lema'an ya'amdu yamim rabim* — place it in a pottery vessel so that it should endure for many days. The goal is preservation, not dissolution. This resonates with the discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls, preserved in pottery vessels in the Judean desert.

## Synagogue vs. Study

### Conversion Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וְאָמַר) רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: מִבֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא לְבֵי רַבָּנַן — שְׁרֵי, מִבֵּי רַבָּנַן לְבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava:** To convert a building **from a synagogue into a study hall** is **permitted,** but **from a study hall into a synagogue** is **prohibited,** as he holds that a study hall has a higher degree of sanctity than a synagogue.

Rav Pappa reports in the name of Rava: *mibei knishta l'bei rabanan* — converting a synagogue into a *beit midrash* — a house of study — *shari* — is permitted. This represents an ascent in *kedushah*. The reverse — *mibei rabanan l'bei knishta* — converting a *beit midrash* into a synagogue — *assur* — is forbidden, because one may not descend in *kedushah*. This implies that the *beit midrash* stands at a higher level than the synagogue.

### Ben Levi's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנִי אִיפְּכָא. אָמַר רַב אַחָא: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פַּפֵּי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת — מוּתָּר לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa in the name of Rava teaches the opposite,** as he holds that a synagogue has a higher degree of sanctity than a study hall. **Rav Aḥa said:** **It stands to reason** to rule **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Pappi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** **It is permitted** for **a synagogue to be made** into **a study hall.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that he was the one who said the term is referring to a place where the Torah is made great.

Yet Rav Pappa himself then reports the opposite tradition in the name of Rava — *masni ipcha* — teaching the reverse. Rav Acha weighs in: *kevateih d'Rav Pappa mistabra* — it is the position of Rav Pappa that is more reasonable, namely that the *beit midrash* has the higher *kedushah*. The supporting proof: *de'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, beit haknesset mutar la'asoto beit hamidrash* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explicitly ruled that one may convert a *beit haknesset* into a *beit midrash*, which straightforwardly implies that the *beit midrash* is the higher institution.

### Bayit Gadol Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ בַּר קַפָּרָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׂרֹף אֶת בֵּית ה׳ וְאֶת בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ וְאֵת כׇּל בָּתֵּי יְרוּשָׁלִַם וְאֶת כׇּל בֵּית גָּדוֹל שָׂרַף בָּאֵשׁ״. ״בֵּית ה׳״ — זֶה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ. ״בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ״ — אֵלּוּ פַּלְטֵרִין שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ. ״וְאֵת כׇּל בָּתֵּי יְרוּשָׁלִַם״ — כְּמַשְׁמָעָן. ״וְאֶת כׇּל בֵּית גָּדוֹל שָׂרַף בָּאֵשׁ״ — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, חַד אָמַר: מָקוֹם שֶׁמְּגַדְּלִין בּוֹ תּוֹרָה, וְחַד אָמַר: מָקוֹם שֶׁמְּגַדְּלִין בּוֹ תְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Bar Kappara interpreted** a verse **homiletically: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And he burnt the house of the Lord, and the king's house, and all the houses of Jerusalem, and every great house he burnt with fire"** (II Kings 25:9)? He explained: **"The house of the Lord"; this is the Holy Temple. "The king's house"; these are the king's palaces [*palterin*]. "And all the houses of Jerusalem"; as** understood in **its literal sense.** With regard to the final phrase: **"And every great house he burnt with fire," Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** disagree about the meaning of "great house": **One** of them **said:** It is referring to **a place where the Torah is made great,** i.e., the study hall; **and** the other **one said:** It is referring to **a place where prayer is made great,** i.e., the synagogue.

The *gemara* brings a *derashah* of Bar Kappara on the verse from the destruction of Jerusalem: *v'yisrof et beit Hashem v'et beit hamelech v'et kol batei Yerushalayim v'et kol beit gadol saraf ba'esh* — Nebuzaradan burned the Temple, the palace, all the houses of Jerusalem, and every great house. *Beit Hashem* is the *Beit Hamikdash*; *beit hamelech* refers to the king's palace; *kol batei Yerushalayim* is straightforward. But what is *kol beit gadol*, every great house? Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagree. One says: *makom shemegadlin bo Torah* — a place where Torah is magnified, i.e., a *beit midrash*. The other says: *makom shemegadlin bo tefillah* — a place where prayer grows and increases, i.e., a synagogue.

### Scriptural Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר תּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״ה׳ חָפֵץ לְמַעַן צִדְקוֹ יַגְדִּיל תּוֹרָה וְיַאְדִּיר״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִלָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״סַפְּרָה נָּא הַגְּדוֹלוֹת אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אֱלִישָׁע״, וֶאֱלִישָׁע דַּעֲבַד — בְּרַחֲמֵי הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **The one who said** that the reference is to where the **Torah** is made great bases his opinion on a verse that describes Torah study as great, **as it is written: "The Lord was pleased, for His righteousness' sake, to make Torah great and glorious"** (Isaiah 42:21). **And the one who said** that the reference is to where **prayer** is made great bases his opinion on a verse that describes prayer as great, **as it is written: "Tell me,** I pray you, all **the great things that Elisha has done"** (II Kings 8:4), **and that which Elisha did,** i.e., restored a boy to life, **he did through prayer.**

Each view draws support from Scripture. The one who says *beit gadol* refers to a place of Torah cites: *Hashem chafetz lema'an tzidko yagdil Torah veya'adir* — God desired, for the sake of His righteousness, to make the Torah great and glorious. *Yagdil Torah* — to magnify Torah — shows that Torah is what is associated with *gedulah*, greatness. The one who says *beit gadol* refers to prayer cites: *saprah na hagedolot asher asah Elisha* — tell now the great things that Elisha did. Elisha was not a scholar of law; his great deeds were accomplished *berachamei* — through prayer. Thus *gedolot* is associated with *tefillah*.

### Primacy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הוּא דְּאָמַר מָקוֹם שֶׁמְּגַדְּלִין בּוֹ תּוֹרָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת מוּתָּר לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Conclude that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is the one who said** that "great house" is referring to **a place where the Torah is made great, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** elsewhere: **It is permitted** for **a synagogue to be made** into **a study hall.** This ruling indicates that he holds that a study hall has a higher degree of sanctity than a synagogue. It is therefore reasonable that he assumes that "great house" is referring specifically to a study hall. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that he was the one who said the term is referring to a place where the Torah is made great.

The *gemara* concludes: *tistayim d'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi hu d'amar makom shemegadlin bo Torah* — we may infer that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is the one who holds that a *beit gadol* is a place of Torah — since it was Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi who earlier permitted converting a *beit haknesset* into a *beit midrash*, demonstrating that in his view the *beit midrash* stands higher. This is a remarkable passage. The rabbis, whose own life's work was the study and transmission of Torah, were nonetheless divided on whether a *beit haknesset* or a *beit midrash* holds the higher status — and some of them gave the greater weight to the synagogue. One way to understand this is that the *beit haknesset* is the entry point for the entire community, a place of *tefillat rabbim* and *tefillah betzibur* in a way that even a large *beit midrash* may not be. Torah study, however powerful and however broad the community gathered around it, may not fully capture the recognized communal status of the house of prayer. The question of whether this reflects a ranking of *tefillah* above *Torah* or simply recognizes the unique communal character of the synagogue remains unresolved and genuinely contested.

## Torah Proceeds Use

### Same Level Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מָכְרוּ תּוֹרָה לֹא יִקְחוּ סְפָרִים וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִמְכּוֹר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה יָשָׁן לִיקַּח בּוֹ חָדָשׁ? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מְעַלֵּי לֵיהּ — אָסוּר, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא לְעַלּוֹיֵי עִילּוּיָיא אַחֲרִינָא — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **However,** if **they sold a Torah** scroll, **they may not** use the proceeds to **purchase scrolls** of the Prophets and the Writings. Similarly, the proceeds of the sale of any sacred item may not be used to purchase an item of a lesser degree of sanctity. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether it is permitted **to sell an old Torah scroll** in order **to purchase a new one?** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: On the one hand, **since** the proceeds **are not raised** to a higher degree of sanctity by doing so, maybe **it is prohibited; or, perhaps** in this case, **since there is no** possibility of **raising** it to **another, higher** degree of sanctity, it **seems well** and should be permitted?

The *mishnah* has established that one must sell sacred objects only toward a higher level of sanctity. The question posed is: *mahu limkor sefer yashan likach bo chadash* — may one sell an old *sefer Torah* in order to purchase a new one? The *sefer Torah* is the highest level of sanctity. There is nothing higher to purchase. Is one permitted to use the proceeds to remain at the same level?

### Upgrade Necessity

The operative tension is this: if the rule is that one must always ascend in *kedushah*, then selling a *sefer Torah* could never be used to fund a different *sefer Torah*, since there is no ascent. But if the rule is that one may not *descend*, then when there is no higher level available, remaining at the same level should be permitted.

### Mishnah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֲבָל מָכְרוּ תּוֹרָה לֹא יִקְחוּ סְפָרִים. סְפָרִים הוּא דְּלָא, הָא תּוֹרָה בְּתוֹרָה — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. מַתְנִיתִין דִּיעֲבַד. כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַן, לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from the mishna: **However, if they sold a Torah** scroll, **they may not** use the proceeds to **purchase scrolls** of the Prophets and the Writings. One may infer: **It is** only **scrolls** of the Prophets and the Writings **that may not** be purchased with the proceeds, **but** to purchase a new **Torah** scroll **with** the proceeds of an old **Torah** scroll **seems well** and is permitted. The Gemara rejects this proof: **The mishna** discusses the *halakha* that applies only **after the fact** that a Torah scroll was sold. Perhaps it is only in that case where the proceeds may be used to purchase another Torah scroll. **When the dilemma was raised to us,** it was with respect to permitting the sale of one Torah scroll in order to purchase another ***ab initio*.**

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishnah*: *aval machru Torah lo yikchu sefarim* — if they sold a *sefer Torah*, they may not use the proceeds to purchase other *sefarim* of *Tanach*. From this one could read: *sfarim hu delo* — other *sefarim* one may not buy, but Torah for Torah — *HaTorah beTorah shapir dami* — perhaps it is permitted. The *gemara* rejects this inference: the *mishnah* speaks *bediavad* — after the fact, once the *sefer Torah* has already been sold. In that case, of course, using the proceeds for a new *sefer Torah* is the only option remaining. The *mishnah* cannot teach us anything about *lechatchila* — whether one may *ab initio* sell a *sefer Torah* in order to buy a new one at the same level.

### Wrapping Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: גּוֹלְלִים סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בְּמִטְפְּחוֹת חוּמָּשִׁין, וְחוּמָּשִׁין בְּמִטְפְּחוֹת נְבִיאִים וּכְתוּבִים. אֲבָל לֹא נְבִיאִים וּכְתוּבִים בְּמִטְפְּחוֹת חוּמָּשִׁין, וְלֹא חוּמָּשִׁין בְּמִטְפְּחוֹת סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita*: **A Torah scroll may be rolled up in wrapping cloths** that are used for scrolls of **one of the five** books of the Torah. **And** scrolls of **one of the five** books of the Torah may be rolled up **in wrapping cloths** that are used for scrolls **of the Prophets or Writings,** since in each case the wrapping cloths are being used for something with a greater degree of sanctity. **However,** a scroll of **the Prophets or Writings may not** be rolled up **in wrapping cloths** that are used for scrolls **of one of the five** books of the Torah, and scrolls of **one of the five** books of the Torah **may not** be rolled up **in wrapping cloths** that are used **for a Torah scroll.**

The *gemara* then attempts a proof from the law of wrappings. *Golelim sefer Torah bematpachot chumashim* — one may wrap a *sefer Torah* with wrappings that were previously used for *chumashim* (individual Torah scrolls). *Vechumashim bematpachot Neviim u'Ketuvim* — and *chumashim* may be wrapped with wrappings previously used for *Neviim* and *Ketuvim*. The reverse is forbidden in each case. Now: one cannot wrap *Neviim* and *Ketuvim* with wrappings used for *chumashim*, and one cannot wrap *chumashim* with wrappings used for *sifrei Torah*. The implication seems to be that even wrappings of a *sefer Torah* cannot be repurposed for another *sefer Torah* — which would indicate that staying at the same level is not permitted.

### Inconclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: גּוֹלְלִים סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בְּמִטְפְּחוֹת חוּמָּשִׁין, מִטְפְּחוֹת חוּמָּשִׁין — אִין, מִטְפְּחוֹת סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה — לָא. הָא תּוֹרָה בְּתוֹרָה — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי! אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִישְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **In any event,** the *baraita* **is teaching: A Torah scroll may be rolled up in wrapping cloths** that are used for scrolls **of one of the five** books of the Torah. One may infer: A Torah scroll may be rolled up only in **wrapping cloths** that are used for scrolls **of one of the five** books of the Torah; but to roll it up in **wrapping cloths of** another **Torah scroll, no,** it is not permitted. By extension, one Torah scroll may certainly not be sold in order to purchase another. The Gemara rejects the proof: If this inference is valid, one should be able to **say the latter clause** and make a similar inference from it. The latter clause teaches: **And** scrolls of **one of the five** books of the Torah **may not** be rolled up **in wrapping cloths** that are used for **a Torah scroll.** It may be inferred from this that it is prohibited only to roll up scrolls of one of the five books of the Torah in wrapping cloths that are used for a Torah scroll, **but** to roll up one **Torah** scroll **in** the wrapping cloths of another **Torah** scroll **seems well.** By extension, one should be permitted to sell a Torah scroll to purchase another. **Rather,** perforce one must conclude that **no** inference beyond its basic meaning **can be deduced from** the *baraita*, as the inferences are contradictory.

But the *gemara* immediately challenges this inference: *eima seifa* — look at the end of the clause. *Velo chumashim bematpachot sefer Torah* — one may not use *sefer Torah* wrappings for *chumashim*. *Ha Torah beTorah shapir dami* — from which it follows that using a *sefer Torah* wrapping for a different *sefer Torah* would be permitted. This is the persistent problem: the sources address only the case of going down and the case of going up; the case of remaining at precisely the same level is never directly addressed. *Ella meha leka lemishma minah* — from this source, no conclusion can be drawn either way. The question of whether the proceeds of a sold *sefer Torah* may *lechatchila* be used to purchase a replacement *sefer Torah* at the same level remains unresolved, and the discussion will continue.
