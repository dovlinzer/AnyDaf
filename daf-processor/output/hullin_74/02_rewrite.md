# Hullin 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dangling Limbs

### Dangling Limbs Overview

The daf opens by continuing the discussion of *eiver talui* — a limb hanging from a slaughtered animal, in a manner analogous to the arm protruding from a fetus when the mother is slaughtered. In the case of the fetus, the protruding limb is forbidden on account of the verse *basar basadeh tereifah*. The *gemara* now applies the same concern to the dangling limb of a slaughtered animal: it cannot be eaten. However, the *gemara* introduces a qualification: *ein bahem ella mitzvas poresh bilvad* — there is only, as it were, a requirement to abstain, a *mitzvas poresh*, and not a full-fledged prohibition.

### Mitzvas Poresh

What does *mitzvas poresh bilvad* mean? The standard interpretation, shared by Rashi and Tosafot, is that the prohibition against eating the dangling limb is merely rabbinic in force. This is striking, given that the *gemara* anchors the prohibition in a biblical verse and treats the dangling limb in the same category as the protruding limb of a fetus. Yet Rashi and Tosafot read the phrase as signaling that unlike the fetal limb — which carries a biblical prohibition — the dangling limb is only a rabbinic concern.

### Rambam on Mitzvas Poresh

The Rambam offers an alternative reading. In his view, *mitzvas poresh bilvad* applies equally to the dangling limb and the fetal limb alike. Even where the Torah derives a prohibition from the verse, it does not carry the full weight of a standard negative commandment. The Torah is, in effect, instructing avoidance — *mitzvas poresh* at the biblical level — but without the legal consequences that accompany a classic biblical prohibition. It is a directive to separate from something that is not appropriate to eat, but it is not the kind of prohibition that generates *malkos*.

### Eating Dangling Limbs

The practical consequence of this distinction surfaces when one asks: what happens if someone eats the dangling limb? The *gemara* is about to rule that one does not receive *malkos* for eating the dangling limb. By contrast, some *rishonim* hold that eating the fetal limb does incur *malkos*, either for violating *basar basadeh tereifah* or, according to the Ra'avad, because the limb has the status of *tereifah* — an act of *shechitah* was performed that was ineffective, leaving the limb in a state from which one could receive *malkos* for eating a *tereifah*. The Rambam, however, draws no such distinction and holds that neither the dangling limb nor the fetal limb exposes the consumer to *malkos*.

### Dangling Limbs & Tumah

A further consequence follows from this: since the dangling limb is not so severely prohibited, it certainly does not convey *tumat neveilah*. The *shechitah* was effective enough to prevent the animal — and by extension this limb — from acquiring the status of *neveilah*. Its status is closer to that of a *tereifah* than to that of a *neveilah*, which was the substance of the preceding discussion. A *neveilah* and a *tereifah* both carry *malkos* if eaten — they represent distinct negative prohibitions — but the *shechitah* of the animal ensures that the dangling limb does not generate *tumah* as a *neveilah* would.

## Rav Yosef Dispute

### Rav Yosef's Position

The *gemara* reports that Rav Yosef was sitting before Rav Huna and stated the following tradition: Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav — *achalo l'zeh lokeh* — if one eats this limb, one does receive *malkos*. This is seemingly surprising, because it was Rav Yosef himself, just the day before, who cited the ruling of *mitzvas poresh bilvad*, which implies no *malkos*.

### Anonymous Objection

An anonymous member of the assembled group — designated only as *hahu me-rabbanan*, one of the other rabbis present — immediately spoke up and said: do not listen to Rav Yosef. He then cited a competing tradition: Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta said in the name of Rav — *achalo l'zeh eino lokeh* — if one eats this, one does not receive *malkos*, consistent with the principle that it is only *mitzvas poresh bilvad*.

### Rav Huna's Question

Rav Huna, faced with two traditions both transmitted in the name of Rav, responded by saying: *anan amai nishmoch* — upon whom should we rely? He appeared to weigh the two traditions against each other, treating them as equally credible competing reports. Rav Yosef turned his face away — *ahadrei Rav Yosef l'appei* — a gesture of offense. Rashi explains that he turned away in anger, *b'kaas*. One might wonder why the presence of a competing tradition would provoke such a reaction; the *gemara* is replete with disputes. It seems Rav Yosef was offended on two counts. First, the competing tradition came from an unnamed individual of no apparent stature, and Rav Huna was according it equal weight with his own. Second, and perhaps more pointedly, Rav Yosef felt that Rav Huna had assumed there was a contradiction in the first place rather than simply asking him for clarification — a reasonable expectation when two traditions bearing the same authority appear to conflict.

### Clarification: Death vs.…

Rav Yosef then explained himself: *mai kashya* — what difficulty did you see? When I said in the name of Rav that one receives *malkos*, I was speaking *b'misah* — when the animal died naturally. What happens when an animal dies with a limb dangling from it? The death of the animal causes the limb to be treated as though it had already fallen off — *d'ahosei nipel* — rendering it *ever min hachai*, a limb from a living animal, for which one does indeed incur *malkos*. The other tradition, the one this anonymous figure cited in the name of Rav, speaks *bishchitah* — the case in which the animal was slaughtered. There, the *shechitah* does not cause the limb to be treated as having fallen off, and one does not receive *malkos*; the prohibition is only the *mitzvas prosh* discussed earlier. The two traditions are therefore not in conflict at all — they address entirely different scenarios.

### Reconciliation

The reconciliation that Rav Yosef offers is thus fully consistent with his position from the previous day. When the animal dies naturally, the dangling limb is treated as *ever min hachai* — a limb detached from a living animal — and eating it carries *malkos*. When the animal is slaughtered, the *shechitah* does not produce this effect of *nifol* — of treating the limb as fallen — and the prohibition remains in the lesser category of *mitzvas poresh bilvad*, without *malkos*. The key conceptual distinction underlying the entire discussion is thus: *misah osei nifol, shechitah eino osei nifol* — natural death creates the legal effect of a fallen limb, *shechitah* does not.

## Misah Osei Nifol

### Rava's Question

Rava now presses further: *minah m'yosaf d'amru Rabbanan* — what is the source for this principle that death creates the effect of *nifol* while *shechitah* does not? The principle has been invoked and relied upon, but where does it originate?

### Verses on Shratzim

The *gemara* answers from a verse concerning dead *shratzim* — the small creeping creatures listed as sources of *tumah* in the Torah. The verse states: *v'chol asher yipol alav meihem b'mosam yitma* — whatever falls upon them from among them in their death shall become *tamei*. The *gemara* reads this verse creatively. The word *b'mosam*, "in their death," might appear redundant, since the verse already uses the word *mi-nivlatam*, "from their carcasses," earlier. The question becomes: what does the additional phrase *b'mosam* exclude or add?

If the answer were simply that something falling from a live creature does not cause *tumah*, that would already be derivable from *mi-nivlatam* — "from their carcasses," implying only when dead. So the word *b'mosam* must be teaching something else. The *gemara* therefore derives: *misah osei nifol, v'ein shechitah osei nifol* — death creates the legal status of a fallen limb; *shechitah* does not. The word *yipol*, which normally means "falls upon," is read here as referring to something that falls off the creature — *asher yipol meihem* — a limb that falls from them. And this occurs *b'mosam*, in their death. The implication is that natural death is what generates the legal fiction that the dangling limb has already fallen off, whereas *shechitah* does not produce this effect.

### Rav Ada's Challenge

Rav Ada bar Abba objected to Rava: *veha k'ra b'shratzim ksiv* — but this verse speaks about *shratzim*, creatures that cannot be subject to *shechitah* at all! They are never *kosher*, so there is no meaningful contrast between dying naturally and being slaughtered. How, then, can one derive from this verse any distinction between death and *shechitah*?

### Applying to Animals

Rava responded: *einu inyan l'shratzim d'lav bnei shechitah ninhu* — you are correct that the teaching cannot be applied to *shratzim* themselves, since *shechitah* is simply irrelevant to them. But precisely because the verse's contrast has no application there, *t'neihu inyan l'vehemah* — apply the teaching to animals, where *shechitah* is a meaningful category. The verse's emphasis on death as the operative moment is thus redirected to teach us something about animals, where *shechitah* presents a genuine alternative to natural death.

### Ke-ein Mitah

At this point, a further question arises. The word *bemotam* is also needed to teach another principle regarding *tumah*: *ke-ein mitah* — the creature must be in a state resembling the moment of death in order to convey *tumah*. Concretely: *lachim metamin, yeveshin ein metamin* — moist dead creatures convey *tumah*, but desiccated ones do not. A dead creature that has completely dried out is no longer in the condition it was at the moment of death, and so it no longer generates *tumah*. This principle is also derived from the word *bemotam*.

### Double Bemotam

The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the word *bemotam* appears twice in the relevant verses. One instance teaches *ke-ein mitah* — the moisture requirement for *tumah*. The second instance teaches *misah osei nifol, shechitah eino osei nifol* — the principle that death creates the status of a fallen limb while *shechitah* does not. Each usage of *bemotam* carries its own independent teaching.

## Dead vs. Live Fetus

### Rav Chisda's Rule

Rav Chisda now introduces a distinction that applies specifically to the case of a fetus with a limb protruding from the mother's womb when the mother is slaughtered. He differentiates between a live fetus and a dead fetus in this scenario.

### Live Fetus Limb

When the fetus is alive and a limb is protruding, and the mother is then slaughtered, a debate among the *rishonim* concerns the status of that limb. Some hold the limb is *neveilah*, while others hold it is more analogous to *tereifah*. The precise categorization carries consequences for *tumah*, *malkos*, and related questions, and this is the substance of the earlier discussion the *gemara* has been working through.

### Dead Fetus Limb

The case of the dead fetus is treated differently. If the fetus has already died in utero, and a limb is protruding, and the mother is then slaughtered — according to Rav Chisda, in this case *divrei hakol shechitah osei nipul* — all opinions agree that even the *shechitah* of the mother creates the effect of *nifol* with respect to that limb. The reasoning is that since the fetus itself is already dead, the limb is attached to something whose natural death already occurred; the *shechitah* of the mother cannot undo that, and the limb is treated as though it had already fallen off, acquiring the status of *ever min hachai*.

### Rava's Disagreement

Rava disagrees with this conclusion. In his view, *kemachlokes be-zeh kach machlokes be-zeh* — whatever dispute exists regarding the live fetus applies equally to the dead fetus. The principle of *misah osei nifol* refers to the natural death of the animal being discussed — the mother. The fetus's prior death does not change the analysis when the mother is then slaughtered. *shechitah* of the mother never creates the effect of *nifol*, regardless of whether the fetus itself was alive or dead at the time. The practical stakes of this dispute, as throughout these discussions, concern primarily whether the limb generates *tumah* as a *neveilah* or *ever min hachai*, not whether one may eat it — eating it is forbidden in any case.

## Eight-Month-Old

### Eight-Month Non-Viable

The *gemara* now turns to the case of the *ben shemonah* — an eight-month-old fetus, which the *gemara* assumes to be non-viable. When such a creature is born and then slaughtered, the *shechitah* does not help. It retains the status of *neveilah*, not *tereifah*. The underlying assumption is that since the eight-month-old baby is effectively already dead — it will not survive — the *shechitah* performed on it has no more effect than *shechitah* performed on a corpse.

This assumption reflects the *gemara*'s settled view, found in other contexts as well, that an eight-month-old baby is entirely non-viable. So certain was this assumption that the *gemara* rules one may not even pick up such an infant on *shabbos*, because it is treated as *ke-even* — like a stone — *muktzah* and without the status of a living being. Contemporary practice departs from this position, accommodating the reality that medical technology can sustain premature infants, but the *gemara*'s halachic framework proceeds on the assumption that an eight-month baby categorically cannot survive.

### Baraita Challenge

A *baraita* challenges this framework. The *baraita* invokes the case of the *ben shemonah* as proof that *shechitah* being relevant to a species does not automatically exempt the animal from causing *tumah* upon death. The *baraita* states: *ben shemonah chai yochiach* — an eight-month-old baby should prove the point. For even though *shechitah* is applicable within its species (*she-af al pi she-yesh bemino shechitah*), *shechitah* performed on this creature does not purify it from *tumah* (*ein shechitato metaharato*).

The larger context here is the question of how we know that a *tereifah* that was slaughtered does not cause *tumah* as a *neveilah* would. The *mishnah* reasoned: at least for this species, *shechitah* is meaningful, and therefore it is effective enough to prevent *tumat neveilah*. The *baraita* presses back: an eight-month-old baby is a case where *shechitah* is applicable to the species, and yet *shechitah* of this particular creature does not protect it from *tumah*. Why, then, should the argument work for *tereifah*?

### Ein Bemino Shechitah

The *mishnah*'s position answers this challenge by treating the eight-month-old as a species unto itself — *ein bemino shechitah*. Since this type of creature can never be rendered *kosher* through *shechitah*, it is classified like a non-*kosher* species for which *shechitah* is simply irrelevant. It therefore presents no counter-example to the principle that *shechitah*, when relevant to the species, protects against *tumat neveilah*.

### Shechitah of Mother

The *baraita*, however, rejects this classification. Rav Kahana explains: *yesh bemino shechitah agav imo* — there is *shechitah* applicable to this type of creature by virtue of its mother's slaughter. When the eight-month fetus is still in utero, *shechitah* of the mother renders it permitted. Thus *shechitah* is not entirely without application to this *min* — it can take effect upon the creature indirectly. The *baraita* therefore contends that the eight-month-old cannot be classified as *ein bemino shechitah*, and it does present a legitimate counter-example: here is a creature to which *shechitah* is in principle applicable, and yet slaughtering it does not protect from *tumah*. The dispute between the *mishnah* and the *baraita* thus turns on whether *shechitah* that operates only through the mother counts as *shechitah* applicable to the species of the eight-month-old itself.

### Trefah & Shechitah

The *gemara* asks: according to the *tanna* of the *baraita*, who accepts the eight-month-old as a counter-example, how does he know that a *tereifah* that is slaughtered does not cause *tumah*? The answer is derived from a verse. The Torah states *v'chi yamut min ha-behemah* — when one of the animals dies. The *gemara* reads *min ha-behemah* as implying that only some animals cause *tumah* upon dying. The distinction is not between species but between manners of death: *zo tereifah she-shachatah* — a *tereifah* that was slaughtered does not cause *tumah* upon its death. The verse itself thus provides the source, independent of the eight-month-old analogy.

## In Utero Shechitah

### Rav Hoshaya's Question

Rav Hoshaya poses a striking question: what if a *shohet* inserted his hand into a mother's womb and slaughtered the fetus while it was still in utero? This is understood as a thought experiment — it does not require resolving the practical impossibility of the act. The question is conceptual: can *shechitah* be meaningful for a fetus while it is still within the mother? Or, at that stage, is the fetus simply not yet its own animal, such that *shechitah* has no purchase on it?

### Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbanan

Rav Hoshaya notes that the question can be posed both according to Rabbi Meir and according to the *rabbanan* — the debate we will encounter in the *mishnah* regarding whether the mother's *shechitah* is effective for the fetus. According to Rabbi Meir, who holds that the fetus requires its own *shechitah* upon being removed from the womb, one might reason that Rabbi Meir recognizes the fetus as a sufficiently independent entity such that *shechitah* is relevant to it. But this recognition could cut either way. One might say: Rabbi Meir views the fetus as independent enough that once it enters the world, its own *shechitah* is necessary — but while still in utero, even he would agree it is not yet sufficiently its own entity for *shechitah* to be meaningful. Alternatively, one might argue: even according to the *rabbanan*, who hold that the mother's *shechitah* suffices, perhaps the Torah provided four *simanim* — two for the mother, two for the fetus — such that either pair can serve as the operative *shechitah*. In that case, the fetus's own *shechitah* in utero might also be valid. The question thus remains unresolved.

### Born Treifah

Rava resolves the question by means of a *baraita*. The *baraita* discusses an animal that was born as a *tereifah* from birth — *harei shenolad trefah min habeten* — a creature that was never permissible to eat. If it were possible to slaughter a fetus in utero, Rava points out, there would have been a moment prior to its emergence from the womb during which such an in utero *shechitah* could theoretically have been performed, and the animal would at that point have been permissible. The *baraita* would then be inaccurate in stating that the creature never had a moment of permissibility — *sha'at kosher*.

Rava resolves this by emending the scenario: *tani shenotzra trefah min habeten* — the *baraita* must be speaking of an animal that was a *tereifah* from conception, not merely from birth. This is possible in the case of an animal born with five legs — *ba'alat hamesh raglayim* — a congenital defect that constitutes a *tereifah* from the very moment of conception. In such a case, there was never a moment, even in utero, at which the animal was permissible. This resolves the challenge and implies that in utero *shechitah* is in principle possible; otherwise, there would be no need to specify that the animal was a *tereifah* from conception in order to eliminate any moment of permissibility.

## Mishnah Debate

### Case 1: Non-Viable

The *mishnah* now lays out the various cases systematically. *hashochet et habehemah umatza ba ben shemonah chai o met* — if one slaughters a mother animal and finds within it an eight-month-old baby, whether alive or dead — *o ben tisha met* — or a nine-month-old that is dead. In all of these cases, the creature is either not viable or not alive, and in every such case, all opinions agree: the mother's *shechitah* is effective for it. It is treated as a limb of the mother. The practical consequence is *koro umotzi et damo* — one tears it open and removes the blood, just as one would do for any other limb of the animal, and then it may be eaten. The requirement to remove the blood is no different here than with any other part of the slaughtered animal.

### Rabbi Meir Position

If, however, one finds a nine-month-old viable baby alive — *matza ben tisha chai* — then, says Rabbi Meir, *taun shechitah* — it requires its own *shechitah*. The mother's *shechitah* does not work for it. Furthermore, *vechayav be'oto ve'et beno* — one must not slaughter it and the mother on the same day, because this fetus is its own independent animal for whom *shechitah* is both required and significant. The prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* applies.

### Rabbanan Position

The sages disagree: *shechitat imo metaherato* — even for a nine-month-old viable fetus, the mother's *shechitah* is fully effective. The fetus is treated as part of the mother, and no separate *shechitah* is required.

### Rabbi Shimon Shezuri

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri goes further: *afilu ben chamesh shanim vechoresh basadeh* — even if this viable nine-month-old fetus was allowed to grow up, becoming a five-year-old cow plowing in the field, the *shechitah* of its mother from five years earlier still renders it permitted — *shechitat imo metaherato*. He is not disputing the *chachamim* but elaborating on the extent of their position: the leniency is not limited to the period immediately after birth; it follows the animal throughout its life.

### Mother Not Slaughtered

Where all opinions converge is in the case where the mother was not slaughtered. If the fetus was removed by Caesarean section and the mother was not subject to *shechitah*, then the viable nine-month-old fetus requires its own *shechitah* — everyone agrees. The permissibility of the fetus is entirely contingent on the mother's *shechitah* having been performed; in its absence, the fetus stands as its own animal and requires its own act of *shechitah*.

## Chelev & Gid

### Rabbi Elazar

The *gemara* now asks whether the leniency that extends to the fetus — the *shechitah* of the mother working for it — applies also to other prohibitions associated with the animal: specifically *chelev* (forbidden fat) and *gid hanasheh* (the sciatic nerve). Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *lo hilchu bo ella al iskei shechitah bilvad* — the leniency applies only to matters of *shechitah*, not to the *chelev* or *gid* of the fetus. Those remain forbidden.

### Baraita on Chelev

The *gemara* immediately challenges this. It cites a *baraita*: *gid hanasheh noheg beshlil vechelbo assur divrei Rabbi Meir* — the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* applies to the fetus and its fat is forbidden, says Rabbi Meir. *rabbi yehuda omer eino noheg beshlil vechelbo mutar* — Rabbi Yehuda says the *gid hanasheh* prohibition does not apply to the fetus and its fat is permitted. This is an explicit debate — they are not in agreement. So how can Rabbi Elazar say the leniency is confined to *shechitah* when it seems to extend, at least according to Rabbi Yehuda, to *chelev* and *gid* as well?

### Logic of Dispute

The *gemara* explains that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are each following their own positions consistently. Rabbi Meir holds that the nine-month-old viable fetus requires its own *shechitah* — he views it as an independent animal — and therefore its *chelev* and *gid hanasheh* are subject to the standard prohibitions. Rabbi Yehuda (who aligns with the *chachamim*) holds that the fetus is treated as a limb of the mother. If that is so, then the *chelev* forbidden by the Torah is only fat found in specific locations on the animal — around the kidneys and so forth. The fat in this limb, embedded in the uterus, is not in those locations. Similarly, the *gid hanasheh* prohibition applies to the sinew in the animal's own hind leg — not to the corresponding sinew in some limb that happens to be inside the mother. Therefore, if it is treated as a limb, neither the *chelev* nor the *gid hanasheh* is a problem. Blood, on the other hand, is always a problem — blood in any limb must be removed.

### Clarification

The *gemara* clarifies that the debate in the *baraita* concerns the *chelev* that surrounds the *gid hanasheh* — a separate question about the fat around the sciatic nerve in a normal animal — and not, as might first appear, about the general status of fetal *chelev*. An independent *baraita* records a dispute on this specific question: Rabbi Meir holds one must dig after all the fat surrounding the *gid hanasheh* and remove it from its very root, whereas Rabbi Yehuda holds one need only skim the surface fat — the deeper fat is not forbidden. In either case, the question of fetal *chelev* and the question of fat around the *gid hanasheh* are distinct debates that should not be conflated.

### Kashrut Issues

The *gemara* therefore revises Rabbi Elazar's formulation. What he meant was not that the leniency applies only to *shechitah* in the narrow sense, but rather: *lo hilchu bo ella al iskei achila bilvad* — the leniency applies only to matters that relate to eating, to *kashrut*. This includes the question of *shechitah* itself, and also *chelev* and *gid hanasheh*, all of which relate to the permissibility of the food. It does not extend to matters that have nothing to do with eating.

## Blood Issues

### Resh Lakish

Resh Lakish now addresses the blood of the fetus. He states: *l'divrei hamatiri b'chelbo matiri b'damo* — according to those who permit the *chelev* of the fetus (i.e., those who follow Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim*), they also permit its blood. Conversely, *v'divrei ha'oser b'chelbo oser b'damo* — those who forbid the *chelev* also forbid the blood. This appears to contradict what was established earlier, namely that even according to Rabbi Yehuda, the blood of the fetus must be removed (*koro umotzi et damo*), as the *mishnah* itself states.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan objects to Resh Lakish: even according to those who permit the *chelev*, the blood should still be forbidden. The reasoning is straightforward: even if the fetus is treated as a limb of the mother, blood in any limb remains prohibited. The *mishnah* makes precisely this point. *af l'divrei hamatiri b'chelbo oser b'damo* — even by those who permit the fat, the blood is forbidden.

### Rav Zeira

Rav Zeira resolves the apparent contradiction between Resh Lakish and this obvious point. Resh Lakish was not saying the blood is outright permitted. What he meant was: *lomar she'ein eno kareis* — the blood does not carry the punishment of *kareis*. The distinction is between *dam hanefesh* — the lifeblood, the blood that flows at the moment of slaughter, for which *kareis* is incurred — and blood absorbed within the limbs, which is forbidden by a negative prohibition but not subject to *kareis*. Resh Lakish's point was specifically about *kareis*, not about whether the blood is forbidden at all.

### Fetus Blood & Kareis

This resolution requires one further step. Rabbi Yehuda holds, in a *baraita*, that even *dam hatamtzis* — the residual blood that remains in the flesh, not the lifeblood — carries *kareis*, derived from the verse *v'chol dam lo socheilu* ("and all blood you shall not eat"). If so, how can Resh Lakish say there is no *kareis* for the fetal blood even according to Rabbi Yehuda?

Rav Yosef, son of Rav Salla Chasida, explains before Rav Pappa: Rabbi Yehuda derives *kareis* for residual blood from *v'chol dam* — "all blood" — but this extension depends on the concept of lifeblood. *kol heicha d'mechayev adam hanefesh* — wherever *kareis* applies to the lifeblood, the prohibition extends also to cover the residual blood of the limbs with *kareis*. But *v'chol heicha d'lo mechayev adam nefesh* — wherever *kareis* does not apply to the lifeblood, the residual blood of the limbs also does not carry *kareis*. In the case of this fetus, since it is treated as a limb of the mother and the *shechitah* of the mother rendered it permitted, there is no concept of *dam hanefesh* — lifeblood — for the fetus itself. Without a *dam hanefesh* context, even Rabbi Yehuda's extension to residual blood does not apply. Therefore, the blood of the fetus is forbidden, and must be removed as the *mishnah* states, but eating it does not incur *kareis*. The mother's *shechitah* works for the fetus; within the logic of permissibility, the fetus is treated as a limb, and since a limb has no lifeblood of its own, *kareis* does not attach to its blood.

## Pidyon Bechor

### Question Posed

The *gemara* now raises a question about a *ben pakuah* — the name given to a fetus found alive in the womb when the mother is slaughtered. According to the *chachamim*, the mother's *shechitah* renders it permitted, and it is treated as a limb of the mother for food purposes. The question is: can one use a *ben pakuah* of a sheep to redeem a firstborn donkey? The Torah requires that a firstborn donkey be redeemed with a sheep (*seh*). Is a *ben pakuah* sheep sufficiently a *seh* to serve this purpose?

### Rabbi Meir's View

According to Rabbi Meir, there is no question. Since Rabbi Meir holds that the fetus requires its own *shechitah*, it is unambiguously its own independent animal — *seh ma'alyahu* — a proper sheep. One may certainly redeem a firstborn donkey with it.

### Rabbanan Debate

The question arises only according to the *rabbanan*, who hold that the mother's *shechitah* works for it. If it is treated as a limb of the mother — effectively as *basar b'dikula*, meat in a pot — then perhaps it is not sufficiently an independent animal to function as the *seh* required for redemption. On the other hand, the *gemara* notes with characteristic directness: *keivan d'rahit v'azil v'rahit v'ati* — since it runs back and forth, one can hardly call it merely meat; it is a living, running creature, and perhaps *seh karina beih* — we can still call it a sheep.

### Mar Zutra

Mar Zutra holds that one cannot redeem a firstborn donkey with a *ben pakuah*.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet holds that one can.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi addresses Mar Zutra's reasoning. Mar Zutra's position is apparently based on a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy — connecting the word *seh* used in the context of redeeming a firstborn donkey to the word *seh* used in the context of the *korban pesach*. Just as a *ben pakuah* cannot serve as a *korban pesach* — since it is already in a state of *shechitah* and one cannot bring something already slaughtered as a *korban* — so too it cannot be used for *pidyon*. Rav Ashi presses back: if you apply the *gezeirah shavah* fully, you would require the sheep used for redemption to be *zachar tamim uven shanah* — male, unblemished, and one year old, as the *korban pesach* must be. Since no one applies those conditions to *pidyon*, the *gezeirah shavah* cannot be used this comprehensively. The answer is that the word *tifdeh tifdeh* — the doubled language of redemption in the Torah — broadens the obligation to include any type of sheep. And yet the *gezeirah shavah* must serve some purpose; it cannot be entirely discarded. Its function, according to this analysis, is precisely to exclude the *ben pakuah* — not by importing the specific requirements of *korban pesach*, but by establishing that the *ben pakuah* falls outside the category of *seh* usable for this purpose.

### Conclusion

The *gemara*'s final position resolves cleanly with the principle established throughout this discussion. For matters of *kashrut* — for food — the *ben pakuah* is treated as meat; the mother's *shechitah* has rendered it permitted and it is considered a limb of the mother. For other halachic purposes — *kil'ayim*, bestiality, plowing with disparate animals, and, as the *gemara* concludes here via the *gezeirah shavah*, *pidyon bechor* — the *ben pakuah* is considered an animal in its own right. The leniency of *shechitat imo* is bounded: it governs matters of eating and *kashrut*, but it does not dissolve the creature's independent legal existence in other domains.
