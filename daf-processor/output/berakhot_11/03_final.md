# Berakhot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Positioning in Prayer

### High Place Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: אַל יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בְּמָקוֹם גָּבוֹהַּ וְיִתְפַּלֵּל, אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם נָמוּךְ וְיִתְפַּלֵּל. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִמַּעֲמַקִּים קְרָאתִיךָ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: A person should not stand in a high place and pray; rather, he should stand in a low place and pray, as it is stated: "I called to You, Lord, from the depths"** (Psalms 130:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: לֹא יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי כִּסֵּא, וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי שְׁרַפְרַף, וְלֹא בְּמָקוֹם גָּבוֹהַּ, וְיִתְפַּלֵּל, אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם נָמוּךְ, וְיִתְפַּלֵּל. לְפִי שֶׁאֵין גַּבְהוּת לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מִמַּעֲמַקִּים קְרָאתִיךָ ה׳״. וּכְתִיב: ״תְּפִלָּה לְעָנִי כִי יַעֲטֹף״.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught in a *baraita*: One should neither stand upon a chair nor upon a stool, nor in a high place and pray. Rather, one should stand in a low place and pray, for there is no haughtiness before God. As it is stated: "I called to You, Lord, from the depths" and it is written: "A prayer for the impoverished, when he is faint and pours out his complaint before God"** (Psalms 102:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין אֶת רַגְלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרַגְלֵיהֶם רֶגֶל יְשָׁרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: When praying, one should align his feet next to each other, as a single foot, in order to model oneself after the angels, with regard to whom it is stated: "And their feet were a straight foot"** (Ezekiel 1:7).

We pick up at the bottom of 10b with a series of statements transmitted in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov. The first: *V'amar Rav Yosef b'Rebbi Chanina mishum Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov — al yaamod adam b'makom gavoah v'yitpalel ela b'makom namuch v'yitpalel.* One should not stand on a high place and pray, but rather on a low place. The Scriptural basis is *mimaamakim kraticha Hashem* — "I have called to You from the depths" — which evokes not only the principle of humility but also the experience of Chizkiyahu's prayer. A *tanya nami hachi* reinforces this: one must not stand on a chair, a platform, or any elevated place to pray, because *she'ein gaphut lifnei hamakom* — there is no haughtiness before God. The verse *tfila l'ani ki yaatof*, a prayer of the poor person when he collapses or faints, further underscores the posture of destitution appropriate to prayer.

This principle is implicitly embedded in the language of the Mishnah and Gemara itself. When discussing being called to the Torah, the text uses the language of *oleh l'Torah* — going up — and *aliyah*. When describing leading the davening, it speaks of *yoreid lifnei hateivah* — going down before the bimah. In certain Sefardi shuls, this was given architectural expression through a lowered platform in the center of the floor from which tefillah was conducted, while the Torah was read from an elevated bimah — perhaps because the Torah comes from on high, proclaimed downward to the congregation, while the human being speaks upward from a position of lowliness and awe.

### Torah vs. Prayer Position

The contrast between the elevated bimah for kriat haTorah and the lowered position for tefillah reflects a genuine theological distinction. Torah reading is associated with *aliyah*, with ascent, with God's word being proclaimed from on high. Tefillah, by contrast, is the human voice reaching upward from below. The phrase *she'ein gaphut lifnei hamakom* captures this precisely: drawing close to God in prayer is an extraordinary intimacy, and the very act of prayer demands that we not allow that closeness to inflate our sense of our own importance. The question of why contemporary practice does not enforce the requirement to pray from a low position is a separate discussion, but the Gemara's own position on the matter is unambiguous.

### Humility & Aspiration

The same tradition then continues with a further requirement: *hamitpalel tzarich she'yichavein et raglav* — one who prays must direct his feet, holding them straight, as derived from *v'ragleihem regel yeshara* — the legs of the angels were straight. Here a notable tension presents itself. Having just insisted on the lowliness and humility appropriate to prayer, the Gemara now invokes the angels as a model for how to comport oneself. This is not necessarily a contradiction. Much of our davening is consciously patterned after angelic worship — we recite *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh* in imitation of the Seraphim, and we aspire to pray as the angels pray. The aspiration toward angelic form and the cultivation of human humility are two simultaneous orientations in tefillah, each true in its own register, and the tension between them is part of what gives prayer its texture.

## Eating & Prayer Order

### Eating Before Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב, ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ עַל הַדָּם״ — לֹא תֹאכְלוּ קוֹדֶם שֶׁתִּתְפַּלְּלוּ עַל דִּמְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: What is the meaning of that which is written: "You shall not eat with the blood"** (Leviticus 19:26)? **You may not eat before you pray for your blood.** One may not eat before he prays.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: כָּל הָאוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מִתְפַּלֵּל עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֹתִי הִשְׁלַכְתָּ אַחֲרֵי גַוֶּךָ״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״גַּוֶּיךָ״ אֶלָּא ״גֵּאֶיךָ״. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְגָּאָה זֶה קִבֵּל עָלָיו מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Others say that Rabbi Yitzḥak said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: One who eats and drinks and later prays, about him the verse states the rebuke of the prophet in the name of God: "And Me you have cast behind your back"** (I Kings 14:9). One who sees to his own bodily needs by eating and drinking before prayer casts God aside, according his arrogance and ego priority over God (Maharsha). Indeed, **do not read your back [*gavekha*]; rather, your pride [*ge'ekha*]. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: After this one has become arrogant and engaged in satisfying his own needs, he only then accepted upon himself the kingdom of Heaven.**

This is a profound statement about the ordering of priorities. Two dimensions are at stake. The first is how one frames the day: does the day begin with tefillah and *ol malchut shamayim*, with the acknowledgment that everything comes from God, or does it begin with attending to one's own body and needs? The second dimension concerns the very nature of the tefillah itself. When a person eats first, satisfies his own needs, and feels fully embodied and self-sufficient, there is a natural swelling of ego that accompanies that experience. Prayer that follows from such a state is qualitatively different — it reflects a posture of *ve'oti hishlachta acharei gavecha*, of having already positioned oneself and one's body as primary. The real point is that one's total dependence on God must be fully acknowledged before one begins to attend to one's own needs. This is *halacha*, with its own parameters regarding what kinds of eating and drinking may be permitted before davening in cases of necessity — but the underlying principle is clear.

## Shema Times Ruling

### Priorities & Divine Acce…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר עַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: One may recite the morning *Shema* until three hours of the day. Rav Yehuda said that Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקּוֹרֵא מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, לֹא הִפְסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **One who recites *Shema* from that time onward loses nothing; although he does not fulfill the mitzva of reciting of *Shema* at its appointed time, he is nevertheless considered like one who reads the Torah, and is rewarded accordingly.**

The Gemara returns to the times of kriat Shema. Rabbi Yehoshua says you may recite Shema until three hours into the day. And the Gemara declares: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halacha ke-Rabbi Yehoshua* — the halacha follows Rabbi Yehoshua. The rationale offered earlier in the masechet was that kings sleep until three hours into the day, and reading Shema within that window still qualifies as the time when people are typically arising.

### Three Hour Limit

This ruling generates one of the most practically significant machloket in the laws of Shema. The Gra holds that *sha'ot zmaniyot* — seasonal hours — are calculated from sunrise to sunset, and that the three-hour limit is therefore measured from sunrise. The Magen Avraham holds that seasonal hours are calculated from *alot hashachar* to *tzeit*, a longer span, and that the three hours begin from *alot hashachar*. Although the Magen Avraham's seasonal hours are individually longer, beginning the count 72 minutes earlier means that his *sof zman kriat Shema* typically falls a good half hour or more before that of the Gra. In practice, the assumption is that *sof zman kriat Shema* follows the Gra — the most lenient reading — and this is compounded by the further question raised earlier in the masechet of whether three hours means the beginning of the third hour or the end. We rule leniently that it is the end of three hours from sunrise.

### Gra vs. Magen Avraham

A conceptual asymmetry warrants attention. For the nighttime Shema, the halacha is that it may be recited throughout the entire night — meaning we interpret *beshochbecha* as referring to the time when people are asleep, not the time when they go to sleep. For the daytime Shema, however, none of the Tannaitic opinions permits recitation throughout the entire day. Even the most lenient view — three hours, because kings sleep until then — treats the relevant period as only the time of arising, not all of waking hours. So the nighttime reading focuses on the period of sleeping, while the daytime reading focuses on the time of waking. The *Kesef Mishneh* suggests in one place that perhaps *de'oraisa* the Shema could be said all day long, and that the three-hour limit is only rabbinic, intended to maintain some symmetry — but the asymmetry in how the verse is read remains noteworthy.

### Night/Day Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמַר יוֹצֵר אוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said that Mar Ukva said: This only applies provided one does not recite: Who forms light [*yotzer or*], or the rest of the blessings recited along with *Shema*, as they pertain only to the fulfillment of the mitzva of reciting of the morning *Shema*; after the third hour, they are inappropriate.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַקּוֹרֵא מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לֹא הִפְסִיד, כְּאָדָם שֶׁהוּא קוֹרֵא בַּתּוֹרָה, אֲבָל מְבָרֵךְ הוּא שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וְאַחַת לְאַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection to Rav Ḥisda's statement from a *baraita*: One who recites *Shema* from that time onward loses nothing, and is considered like one who reads Torah, but he recites two blessings beforehand and one thereafter.** This directly contradicts Rav Ḥisda's statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: מַאי ״לֹא הִפְסִיד״ — שֶׁלֹּא הִפְסִיד בְּרָכוֹת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַקּוֹרֵא מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לֹא הִפְסִיד כְּאָדָם שֶׁקּוֹרֵא בַּתּוֹרָה, אֲבָל מְבָרֵךְ הוּא שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וְאַחַת לְאַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say that Rav Ḥisda said that Mar Ukva said the opposite: What is the meaning of: Loses nothing, in the mishna? This means that one who recites *Shema* after the third hour does not lose the opportunity to recite the blessings and is permitted to recite them although the time for the recitation of *Shema* has passed. That was also taught in a *baraita*: One who recites *Shema* after this time loses nothing, and is considered like one who reads the Torah, but he recites two blessings beforehand and one thereafter.**

*Hakorei mikan ve'eilach lo hifsid* — one who reads Shema after the three-hour mark has not lost entirely; it is as if one has read from the Torah. But Rav Chisda in the name of Mar Ukva adds: *uvilvad shelo yomar Yotzer Or* — provided one does not recite Yotzer Or. Since Yotzer Or is a bracha of Shema, if one is no longer fulfilling the mitzvah of Shema, one cannot recite it.

This position is challenged by a *braisa*: *hakorei mikan ve'eilach lo hifsid ke'adam shekorei baTorah umevorech hu shtayim lefaneha ve'achas leachareha* — one who reads after the time still recites the brachot before and after. This is a *tiyuvta* of Rav Chisda's first position. The resolution emerges from the dual status of these brachot: they are simultaneously brachot of kriat Shema *and* brachot of Shacharit. Yotzer Or functions as a bracha of the morning service — about God creating light and darkness — even independent of the mitzvah of Shema. Therefore one can and does recite it even after the time of Shema has passed. This is how we rule. An alternative version of Rav Chisda's statement already reflects this conclusion, and the *tanya nami hachi* confirms it.

### Late Shema Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי: גָּדוֹל הַקּוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע בְּעוֹנָתָהּ יוֹתֵר מֵהָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Mani said: Greater is one who recites *Shema* at its appropriate time than one who engages in Torah study.**

Rav Mani then draws a striking inference: *gadol hakorei Krias Shema be'onasa yoseir meha'oseik baTorah* — one who recites Shema in its proper time is of greater value than one engaged in Torah study. The logical basis is simple: the Gemara itself says that reciting Shema after the time is equivalent to reading Torah. If the lesser state is equivalent to Torah study, then the greater state — reading Shema in its proper time — must exceed it. And this is indeed reflected in halacha: one interrupts Torah study in order to recite Shema. The *mitzvah* of Shema in its time, even when it lacks the intellectual engagement of Torah study, takes precedence.

## Shammai vs. Hillel

### Dual Status Brachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בָּעֶרֶב — כָּל אָדָם יַטֶּה וְיִקְרָא. וּבַבֹּקֶר יַעֲמוֹד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA: Beit Shammai say: One should recite *Shema* in the manner indicated in the text of *Shema* itself. Therefore, in the evening every person must recline on his side and recite *Shema*, in fulfillment of the verse: "When you lie down," and in the morning he must stand and recite *Shema*, in fulfillment of the verse: When you rise, as it is stated: "When you lie down, and when you rise."**

The next Mishnah introduces a new dimension: not just *when* to recite Shema but *how* to physically position oneself during its recitation. *Beit Shammai* holds: *ba'erev kol adam yatei veyikra uvaboker ya'amod* — in the evening every person must recline and read, and in the morning one must stand. The derivation is from *beshochbecha uvekumecha* — when you lie down and when you rise up — read literally as a prescription for physical posture.

### Shema vs. Torah Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כָּל אָדָם קוֹרֵא כְּדַרְכּוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ בַדֶּרֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: Every person recites *Shema* as he is, and he may do so in whatever position is most comfortable for him, both day and night, as it is stated: "And when you walk along the way," when one is neither standing nor reclining (*Me'iri*).**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״וּבְשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״ — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם שׁוֹכְבִים וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם עוֹמְדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **If so, according to Beit Hillel, why was it stated: "When you lie down, and when you rise"? This is merely to denote time; at the time when people lie down and the time when people rise.**

Beit Hillel disagrees: *kol adam korei k'darko* — every person reads however he happens to be situated. The basis is **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** — and when you go on the way — which demonstrates that Shema may be recited even while walking, i.e., in whatever position or circumstance one finds oneself. When the Torah says *beshochbecha uvekumecha*, Beit Hillel understands this as a reference to time: *b'sha'ah she'bnei adam shochvim u'v'sha'ah she'bnei adam omdim* — the time when people are lying down and the time when people are rising. It specifies *when* to say Shema, not *how* to be positioned.

### Shammai Literal Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל — קָא מְפָרְשִׁי טַעְמַיְיהוּ וְטַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, אֶלָּא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמְרִי כְּבֵית הִלֵּל?
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA: Granted, Beit Hillel explain the rationale for their opinion and the rationale for Beit Shammai's opinion. However, what is the reason that Beit Shammai do not state their opinion in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לָךְ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא ״בַּבֹּקֶר וּבָעֶרֶב״, מַאי ״בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״? בִּשְׁעַת שְׁכִיבָה — שְׁכִיבָה מַמָּשׁ, וּבִשְׁעַת קִימָה — קִימָה מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai could say to you: If so that the verse means only to denote the time for the recitation of *Shema*, as claimed by Beit Hillel, then let the verse say: "In the morning and in the evening." What is the meaning of the ambiguous formulation: "When you lie down, and when you rise"? It must mean that at the time of lying down one must recite *Shema* while actually lying down, and at the time of arising one must recite *Shema* while actually risen.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי: הַאי ״וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ בַדֶּרֶךְ״, מַאי עֲבִיד לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And what do Beit Shammai do with this verse: "And when you walk along the way," which Beit Hillel use to prove that every person recites *Shema* as he is?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִבְּעֵי לְהוּ, לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״בְּשִׁבְתְּךָ בְּבֵיתֶךָ״ — פְּרָט לְעוֹסֵק בְּמִצְוָה, ״וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ בַדֶּרֶךְ״ — פְּרָט לְחָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai need this verse in order to derive other *halakhot*, as it was taught in a *baraita* which interpreted this verse that the obligation to recite *Shema* applies when you sit in your home, to the exclusion of one who is engaged in performance of a mitzva, who is exempt from the recitation of *Shema*; and when you walk along the way, to the exclusion of a groom, who is also exempt from the recitation of *Shema*.**

The Gemara clarifies both positions. Beit Hillel's reasoning is internally consistent: *beshochbecha uvekumecha* denotes time, and **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** confirms that physical position is irrelevant. But why is *Beit Shammai* not persuaded by **U'v'lechtecha vaderech**? *Beit Shammai* responds: if the Torah only meant to indicate *time*, it could have simply said "in the morning and in the evening." The fact that it says *beshochbecha uvekumecha* — when you lie down and when you rise up — implies *shchiva mamash* and *kima mamash* — actual reclining and actual standing. The use of this formulation rather than the simpler alternative is what drives the literal reading.

Furthermore, the observation that the Torah *could* have said "morning and evening" has an additional implication: *Beit Shammai* apparently treats *beshochbecha uvekumecha* and "morning and evening" as referring to the same time period. The emphasis of *beshochbecha uvekumecha*, then, is not to convey a different time but to convey the required posture. *Beit Shammai* then also derives from **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — but that belongs to the following discussion.

## Osek B'Mitzvah

### Hillel Time Emphasis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן, אָמְרוּ: הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת הַבְּתוּלָה — פָּטוּר, וְאֶת הָאַלְמָנָה — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **From here, they said: One who marries a virgin is exempt from the recitation of *Shema* on his wedding night, but one who marries a widow is obligated.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע?
>
> **Translation:** **From where may it be inferred that the verse, when you walk along the way, exempts a groom from the obligation to recite *Shema*?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּי דֶרֶךְ. מָה דֶּרֶךְ רְשׁוּת, אַף כֹּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said that we learn: Like the way; just as the journey along a specific way described in the verse is voluntary and involves no mitzva, so too all of those who are obligated to recite *Shema* are engaged in voluntary activities. However, one engaged in the performance of a mitzva, like a groom, is exempt from the obligation to recite *Shema*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָא אָזֵיל לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: לִקְרֵי. אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּשֶׁבֶת וּבְלֶכֶת״ מַאי ״בְּשִׁבְתְּךָ וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ״? — בְּשֶׁבֶת דִּידָךְ וּבְלֶכֶת דִּידָךְ הוּא דְּמִחַיְּיבַתְּ, הָא דְּמִצְוָה פְּטִירַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Are we not dealing with a case where one walks along the way to perform a mitzva? The Torah did not designate the objective of his walk and, nevertheless, the Torah said to recite *Shema*, indicating that one is obligated even if he set out to perform a mitzva. Rather, the proof is from the formulation of the verse. If so, that the intention was to obligate in all cases, let the Torah write: When sitting and when walking. What is the meaning of: When you sit and when you walk? Certainly these additions come to emphasize in your sitting down and in your walking, meaning that when one does this for his own purposes and of his own volition, he is obligated to recite *Shema*, but when he does with the objective of performing a mitzva, he is exempt from reciting *Shema*, as in that case he is sitting or walking at God's behest.**

Since Beit Hillel uses **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** to establish that physical position does not matter, *Beit Shammai* must derive something else from it. The Gemara identifies what: *b'shivtecha b'veitecha — prat l'osek b'mitzvah* — "when you dwell in your house" excludes one who is occupied with a mitzvah. *U'v'lechtecha vaderech — prat l'chatan* — "when you walk on the way" excludes a groom on his wedding night, who is mentally preoccupied with the mitzvah that is about to be performed, and is therefore considered an *osek b'mitzvah* and exempt from reciting Shema.

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the logic of deriving the exemption from **U'v'lechtecha vaderech**? Rav Pappa explains: *ma derech reshut — af kol reshut* — just as going on a journey is an optional, non-obligatory activity, so too the obligation to recite Shema applies when one is engaged in optional activities. If one is occupied with a mitzvah, the Torah's obligation of Shema does not apply. The Gemara then presses: perhaps someone walking on the way is *also* going to perform a mitzvah — why should *derech* imply *reshut*? The answer: if the Torah had simply wanted to say "sitting and walking," it would have written *b'sheves u'v'leches*. The elaboration *b'shivtecha b'veitecha u'v'lechtecha vaderech* — *your* sitting and *your* walking, when you are engaged in your own affairs — is what limits the obligation. When you are doing your own things and the time for a mitzvah arrives, you stop and do the mitzvah. But when what you are doing *is* the mitzvah — when it is God's business rather than your own — you are exempt. This is the source for the broad principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah*.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אֲפִילּוּ כּוֹנֵס אֶת הָאַלְמָנָה נָמוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **If so, even one who marries a widow should be exempt, for he, too, is engaged in performance of a mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי טְרִיד, וְהַאי לָא טְרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **One who marries a virgin is preoccupied by his concern lest he discover that his bride is not a virgin, while one who marries a widow is not preoccupied.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם טִרְדָּא, אֲפִילּוּ טָבְעָה סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם נָמֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, אַלְּמָה אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: אָבֵל חַיָּיב בְּכָל הַמִּצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה, חוּץ מִן הַתְּפִילִּין, שֶׁהֲרֵי נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶם ״פְּאֵר״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פְּאֵרְךָ חֲבוֹשׁ עָלֶיךָ״?!
>
> **Translation:** **If the exemption is due to preoccupation, then even one who is preoccupied because his ship sank at sea should also be exempt. The Gemara reinforces its question: And if you say that in this case as well, when one's ship sank at sea, one is exempt, why then did Rabbi Abba bar Zavda say that Rav said: A mourner is obligated in all the mitzvot mentioned in the Torah except for phylacteries, from which a mourner is exempt, as the term splendor is stated with regard to phylacteries, as it is stated: "Make no mourning for the dead, bind your splendor upon yourself"** (Ezekiel 24:17). If a mourner, who is clearly pained and preoccupied by his loss, is obligated to recite *Shema*, then certainly all others who are preoccupied, even one whose ship sank at sea, whose loss was merely monetary, should be obligated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם טְרִיד טִרְדָּא דְמִצְוָה, הָכָא טְרִיד טִרְדָּא דִרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **There, he is preoccupied with the preoccupation of a mitzva that he must perform; here, he is preoccupied with the preoccupation of a voluntary act that he chooses to perform.**

The Gemara resolves the question with a precise formulation: *chatan tarid tirda d'mitzvah — hacha tarid tirda d'reshut*. The groom is preoccupied with a preoccupation that is itself a mitzvah; the avel and the shipwrecked man are preoccupied with matters of *reshut* — personal concerns, not mitzvah activity. What defines *osek b'mitzvah* is not distraction as such, but mental engagement with an imminent or ongoing mitzvah act. The mind of the groom is genuinely caught up in the mitzvah — even if the motivation has a personal dimension — and that mental preoccupation in the context of a mitzvah constitutes *osek*. The avel, despite sitting shiva — which is itself a mitzvah in some sense — is not mentally preoccupied with a specific mitzvah *act* about to be performed, and his distress therefore does not constitute *osek b'mitzvah*.

A line appears in some versions of the text that **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** also exempts *shluchei mitzvah* — those who are on their way to perform a mitzvah — which is conceptually similar to the chatan case, since one need not be in the midst of the mitzvah act itself but be actively on the path toward it. Rashi apparently did not have this line, and it is textually difficult; the core point, however, is now established.

Beit Hillel, for their part, derives *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* differently. They read **U'v'lechtecha vaderech** at face value — you recite Shema when you are walking on the road, in whatever position. This *pshat* itself implies that physical circumstance is no barrier to the obligation, and from within this reading they simultaneously extract the exemption for one occupied with a mitzvah, without abandoning the plain meaning of the verse.

## Shammai/Hillel Stories

### Derivation from Uvlechet…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עוֹמְדִין וְקוֹרִין, יוֹשְׁבִין וְקוֹרִין, וּמַטִּין וְקוֹרִין, הוֹלְכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְקוֹרִין, עוֹשִׂין בִּמְלַאכְתָּן וְקוֹרִין. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, שֶׁהָיוּ מְסוּבִּין בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וְהָיָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מוּטֶּה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה זָקוּף. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַן קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, הִטָּה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְזָקַף רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָחִי, אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — מָשָׁל לְאֶחָד שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ זְקָנְךָ מְגוּדָּל! אָמַר לָהֶם: יִהְיֶה כְּנֶגֶד הַמַּשְׁחִיתִים. אַף כָּךְ אַתָּה, כָּל זְמַן שֶׁאֲנִי זָקוּף — אַתָּה מוּטֶּה, עַכְשָׁיו כְּשֶׁאֲנִי הִטֵּתִי אַתָּה זָקַפְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in a *baraita* that Beit Hillel say: One may recite *Shema* in any situation: Standing and reciting, sitting and reciting, reclining and reciting, walking and reciting, and even working and reciting. And an incident is related where two *tanna'im*, Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who were both disciples of Beit Hillel, were reclining at a meal in one place together with their students, and Rabbi Yishmael was reclined as was the customary dining position, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was upright. When the time to recite the evening *Shema* arrived, Rabbi Elazar reclined to recite *Shema* in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, while Rabbi Yishmael sat upright to recite *Shema*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya appeared to take offense, and said to Rabbi Yishmael: Yishmael, my brother, I will tell you a parable to which this is similar. It is comparable to a situation where one to whom people say as a compliment: Your beard is full and suits you. That man says to them: May it be against those who shave and destroy their beards. You are the same. As long as I am upright, you are reclined, and now when I reclined lauding your conduct and emulating you, you sat upright as if to demonstrate that whatever I do, you do the opposite.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי עָשִׂיתִי כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, וְאַתָּה עָשִׂיתָ כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא יִרְאוּ הַתַּלְמִידִים וְיִקְבְּעוּ הֲלָכָה לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael said to him: I acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, while you acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai. I am the one who acted in accordance with the *halakha*. And furthermore, I was concerned lest the students see your conduct and establish the *halakha* for generations accordingly.**

Having established the dispute between *Beit Shammai* and Beit Hillel, the Gemara presents a *Baraita* that lays out Beit Hillel's position in full: one may stand and recite Shema, sit and recite it, recline and recite it, walk on the road and recite it, or do so while engaged in one's work — all deriving from **U'v'lechtecha vaderech**. A story then follows.

Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah were reclining at a meal. Rabbi Yishmael was reclining and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was sitting upright. When the time for the evening Shema arrived, Rabbi Elazar reclined and Rabbi Yishmael sat upright. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah said to Rabbi Yishmael: *Yishmael achi* — my brother Yishmael — let me offer you a comparison. It is like someone whose friends admire his beautiful beard, and he responds by saying he will go to the barber and cut it off. So too here: as long as I was sitting upright, you were reclining — you were doing the right thing, since you happened to be in the proper position for the nighttime Shema according to *Beit Shammai*. Now that I reclined in order to be properly positioned, you sat up?

Rabbi Yishmael explained: I was reclining before because I was eating my meal — that was Beit Hillel, who permits any position. When the time for Shema came, I sat up — also Beit Hillel, since I was not deliberately reclining for Shema. You, however, deliberately reclined at the moment of Shema, acting like *Beit Shammai*. And moreover — *v'lo od* — I was concerned that the students would see you reclining and conclude that one is *required* to recline, thereby establishing the halacha of *Beit Shammai* for future generations.

The Gemara probes this further: why did Rabbi Yishmael need to sit up? If Beit Hillel permits reclining too, what is wrong with remaining reclined? The answer: had Rabbi Yishmael also been reclining from the start and continued to recline, there would have been no problem. But Rabbi Elazar was sitting upright *until* the moment of Shema and then deliberately reclined — demonstrating through his action that he was being *makpid* like *Beit Shammai*, that he held reclining at night was required. In that context, Rabbi Yishmael needed to sit up as a counterdemonstration, to prevent students from drawing the wrong halachic conclusion.

### Groom Preoccupation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב יְחֶזְקֵאל: עָשָׂה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — עָשָׂה, כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל — עָשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeḥezkel taught: One who acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai has acted appropriately and is not in violation of the *halakha*. One who acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel acted appropriately as well.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: עָשָׂה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם. דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשֻׁלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: One who acts in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai has done nothing and must repeat *Shema* in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, as we learned in the mishna with regard to the *halakhot* of a *sukka*: One who had his head and most of his body in the *sukka*, and his table inside the house, Beit Shammai invalidate his action. And Beit Hillel validate his action.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָלְכוּ זִקְנֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְזִקְנֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבַקֵּר אֶת רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן הַחוֹרָנִית, מְצָאוּהוּ שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשֻׁלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לוֹ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai as a proof: There was an incident where the elders of Beit Shammai and the elders of Beit Hillel went on *Sukkot* to visit Rabbi Yoḥanan ben HaḤoranit. They found him with his head and most of his body in the *sukka* and his table inside the house and they said nothing to him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: וּמִשָּׁם רְאָיָה?! אַף הֵם אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם כֵּן הָיִיתָ נוֹהֵג לֹא קִייַּמְתָּ מִצְוַת סוּכָּה מִיָּמֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai said to them: And is there proof from there? That is not what happened, rather they said to him explicitly: If you have been accustomed to act in this manner, you have never in your life fulfilled the mitzva of *sukka*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: עָשָׂה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — חַיָּיב מִיתָה. דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: אֲנִי הָיִיתִי בָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְהִטֵּתִי לִקְרוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְסִכַּנְתִּי בְּעַצְמִי מִפְּנֵי הַלִּסְטִים, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כְּדַאי הָיִיתָ לָחוּב בְּעַצְמְךָ, שֶׁעָבַרְתָּ עַל דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak stated an even more extreme opinion: One who acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai has acted so egregiously that he is liable to receive the death penalty, as we learned in our mishna that Rabbi Tarfon said to his colleagues: Once, I was coming on the road when I stopped and reclined to recite *Shema* in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai. Yet in so doing, I endangered myself due to the highwaymen who accost travelers. The Sages said to him: You deserved to be in a position where you were liable to pay with your life, as you transgressed the statement of Beit Hillel.**

The Gemara then records a range of opinions about the practical consequence of acting like *Beit Shammai*. *Tani Rav Yechezkel*: one who acted like *Beit Shammai* has acted acceptably; one who acted like Beit Hillel has also acted acceptably — perhaps even now, even after halacha always follows Beit Hillel, Beit Hillel's own position does not technically prohibit what *Beit Shammai* permits.

Rav Yosef, however, says: *asa kedivrei *Beis Shammai* lo asa velo klum* — one who deliberately acts like *Beit Shammai* has accomplished nothing and has not fulfilled the mitzvah. The proof comes from a Mishnah in Sukkah: if one sat with his head and most of his body inside the sukkah but left his table inside the house, *Beit Shammai* invalidates it and Beit Hillel validates it. When the elders of both schools visited Rabbi Yochanan ben Hachoranis and found him in precisely this situation, *Beit Shammai* told him: if this is how you have always arranged yourself, you have never in your life fulfilled the mitzvah of sukkah.

The point, as Tosafot explains, is significant. The problem of having one's table inside the house is only rabbinic — the concern is that one will be drawn after one's table and will not remain in the sukkah. Biblically, one is already *yotzei*. Yet *Beit Shammai* was so insistent on their ruling that they declared one who violated even this rabbinic position to have never fulfilled the sukkah mitzvah at all. By analogy, Beit Hillel would similarly declare that one who goes out of his way to act like *Beit Shammai* in the matter of Shema has not fulfilled the mitzvah. The argument by analogy is acknowledged as not fully conclusive — it shows Beit Shammai's stringency about their own rabbinic rulings, but it does not prove Beit Hillel would respond symmetrically — yet this is the reasoning advanced.

### Avel's Obligation

*Asa kedivrei *Beis Shammai* chayav misa* — one who acts like *Beit Shammai* is liable to death. This harsh formulation, echoing language used elsewhere in the masechet about reading Shema after midnight, reflects how seriously the rabbis treated violations of their communal rulings. The proof text is the Mishnah we already encountered: Rabbi Tarfon relates that he was on the road and deliberately reclined to read Shema in the manner of *Beit Shammai*, thereby endangering himself by exposing himself to highway robbers. The Sages said to him: you deserved what happened to you, because you violated the words of Beit Hillel.

This is a striking position. Beit Hillel is typically associated with leniency and accommodation. Here, however, they draw an unusually firm line: someone who deliberately goes out of their way to practice like *Beit Shammai* — in the matter of physical positioning for Shema — is told he had it coming when he was endangered. Part of what is at stake, it seems, is the nature of the reading itself. Once one insists on reading *beshochbecha uvekumecha* as requiring a literal physical posture, one is committed to a much more literal interpretive approach to the entire verse — an approach that might, for example, lead one to conclude that Shema need only be said when one actually goes to sleep and when one actually rises, and might not apply on nights when one does not sleep. The firmness of Beit Hillel's position here may reflect precisely this concern: the reading of *Beit Shammai* opens a fundamentally different interpretive framework for the entire obligation of Shema.

## Brachot Structure

### Mental Engagement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּשַּׁחַר מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וְאַחַת לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וּבָעֶרֶב מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וּשְׁתַּיִם לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. אַחַת אֲרוּכָּה וְאַחַת קְצָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA: In the morning when reciting *Shema*, one recites two blessings beforehand, the first on the radiant lights and the second the blessing on the love of Torah, and one thereafter, which begins with: True and Firm [*emet veyatziv*]. And in the evening one recites two blessings beforehand, on the radiant lights and on the love of God, and two thereafter, the blessing of redemption: True and Faithful [*emet ve'emuna*], and the blessing: Help us lie down. With regard to the blessing: True and Faithful, whether one recites it in its long formula and whether one recites it in its short formula, he fulfills his obligation.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַאֲרִיךְ — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְקַצֵּר, לְקַצֵּר — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְהַאֲרִיךְ. לַחְתּוֹם — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי שֶׁלֹּא לַחְתּוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא לַחְתּוֹם — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לַחְתּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **However, the general principle is: Where the Sages said to recite a long blessing, one may not shorten it, and so too, wherever they said to recite a short blessing, one may not lengthen it. Where the Sages said that a blessing must conclude with a second blessing at the end, he may not fail to conclude with that blessing. Similarly, if the Sages said that a blessing must not conclude with a second blessing, one may not conclude with a blessing.**

The next Mishnah addresses the liturgical context of Shema. *Bashachar mevarech shtayim lefaneha ve'achas leachareha* — in the morning, two brachot before Shema and one after. *Uvaerev mevarech shtayim lefaneha ushtayim leachareha* — in the evening, two brachot before and two after. Then the Mishnah adds: *achas arucha ve'achas ktzara* — one long and one short.

### Two Scholars Tale

There is a debate between Rashi and Tosafot over how to read *achas arucha ve'achas ktzara*. Rashi takes it straightforwardly: of the two brachot after Shema at night, the first — *Emet Ve'emunah* — is long, and the second — *Hashkiveinu* — is short. Tosafot objects: technically speaking, a "short" bracha means a single-line blessing like *shehakol nihyah bidvaro*, while all the brachot surrounding Shema are elaborate paragraphs that open and close with a *Baruch Atah* formulation — all of them are "long" in the technical sense. What, then, does it mean that one is long and one is short?

Tosafot, in the name of Rabbeinu Tam, reads *achas arucha ve'achas ktzara* as an idiom — it means "whether long or whether short," an expression indicating flexibility. The Mishnah is telling us that in the case of *Emet Ve'emunah*, there was a degree of textual latitude: *bein yaarich bein yiktzar bah* — whether one wishes to extend it or abbreviate it. This reflects the broader reality that in the period of the Mishnah the precise texts of brachot had not been fully fixed, and there was genuine flexibility in how one formulated them.

### Sukkah Precedent

The Mishnah then concludes: *makom she'amru leha'arich eino rashai lekatztzar; lekatztzar eino rashai leha'arich* — where the rabbis said to lengthen, one may not abbreviate; where they said to abbreviate, one may not lengthen. *Velachtom eino rashai shelo lachtom; shelo lachtom eino rashai lachtom* — where a *chatimah* is required, one may not omit it; where no *chatimah* is prescribed, one may not add one. Short and long here refer to whether a bracha is a full paragraph ending with a *Baruch Atah* formulation or a single-line blessing without such an ending. When the structure is mandated, one adheres to it; only where the Mishnah explicitly signals flexibility — as with *Emet Ve'emunah* according to Tosafot — does latitude exist.

## Yotzer Or Blessing

### Tarfon Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי מְבָרֵךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA: The Gemara begins by determining the formula of the two blessings preceding the morning *Shema*. The Gemara asks: What blessing does one recite?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא חֹשֶׁךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: "Who forms light and creates darkness,** Who makes peace and creates evil, I am the Lord Who does all these things" (Isaiah 45:7).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא נוֹגַהּ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Let him say: Who forms light and creates brightness, so as not to mention darkness, which has negative connotations.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִכְתִיב קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **We say the blessing as the verse is written in the Bible and do not alter the formula that appears in the verse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה ״עֹשֶׂה שָׁלוֹם וּבוֹרֵא רָע״, מִי קָא אָמְרִינַן כְּדִכְתִיב?! אֶלָּא כְּתִיב ״רַע״ וְקָרֵינַן ״הַכֹּל״ לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא, הָכָא נָמֵי לֵימָא ״נוֹגַהּ״, לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But if so, what about the continuation of the verse: "Who makes peace and creates evil"? Do we say this blessing as it is written in the Bible? Rather, it is written evil and we euphemistically recite the blessing all things to avoid mention of evil. Here, too, let us euphemistically say brightness instead of darkness.**

*Mai mevarech* — what blessing is recited? Rabbi Yaakov says in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *Yotzer or uvorei choshech* — who creates light and creates darkness. The Gemara then raises a question at the top of 11b: why say *borei choshech* — creates darkness — which sounds unpleasant? Why not say *borei nogah* — creates radiance — as a euphemism? The Gemara answers: *kedichtiv ka'amrinan* — we say it as it is written. The verse in Yeshayahu reads *yotzer or uvorei choshech*, and we follow the Scriptural formulation.

### Morning/Night Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כְּדֵי לְהַזְכִּיר מִדַּת יוֹם בַּלַּיְלָה וּמִדַּת לַיְלָה בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: The reason we recite: "Who creates darkness" is in order to mention the attribute of day at night and the attribute of night during the day, and thereby unify day and night as different parts of a single entity.**

The Gemara then presses further: if we follow the verse, the verse also says *oseh shalom uvorei ra* — God makes peace and creates evil. Yet we do not say that in the liturgy; we substitute *uvorei et hakol* — and creates everything — as a euphemism. Why then not similarly use *nogah* as a euphemism for darkness? The asymmetry is pointed: evil is a theologically charged and troubling concept — far more fraught than darkness, which is a simple physical reality. Indeed, part of the theological power of the original verse is precisely to counter the Zoroastrian dualist claim of two deities, one of good and one of evil. Asserting that one God creates both light and darkness, and indeed both peace and evil, is a deliberate theological statement. The hesitation to say *borei ra* in the liturgy is therefore understandable; it is much harder to explain why *borei choshech* should require euphemism.

The Gemara offers a different reason entirely. The answer is not rooted in the aesthetics of the language but in a liturgical goal: *kedei lehasmich midas yom balaila umidas laila bayom* — in order to juxtapose the quality of day with night and the quality of night with day. In the morning bracha, we mention both light *and* darkness; in the evening bracha, we will similarly mention both. The goal is to create a symmetrical awareness of God's creation of the full cycle of day and night, and this purpose overrides any preference for euphemism.

### Tosafot Flexibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִדַּת לַיְלָה בַּיּוֹם כִּדְאָמְרִינַן: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא חֹשֶׁךְ״, אֶלָּא מִדַּת יוֹם בַּלַּיְלָה, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, the attribute of night is mentioned during the day, as we say: Who forms light and creates darkness, but where do you find the attribute of day mentioned at night? In the blessing over the radiant lights recited at night there is no mention of "Who forms light."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״גּוֹלֵל אוֹר מִפְּנֵי חֹשֶׁךְ וְחֹשֶׁךְ מִפְּנֵי אוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Nevertheless, the attribute of day is mentioned at night in the words: Rolling away light before the darkness and darkness before the light.**

But where in the nighttime brachot do we mention the creation of day? The morning bracha mentions darkness — that covers *midas laila bayom*, bringing night into day. *Ela midas yom balaila heichi mashkachas lah* — where do we find the day mentioned in the evening? Abaye answers: *goleil or mipnei choshech v'choshech mipnei or* — the evening bracha of *Maariv Aravim* speaks of God rolling light away before darkness and darkness away before light. This description of the continuous alternation of day and night accomplishes the goal: even in the nighttime bracha, the creation and quality of day is explicitly referenced. Beyond the poetic beauty of this image of God turning over light into darkness and darkness into light, there is again the theological dimension: it is one God who controls both, and that unity of divine governance is explicitly articulated in both the morning and evening prayers.

## Ahava Rabba

### Fixed Structure Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. וְכֵן אוֹרִי לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי פְּדָת בְּרֵיהּ, ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the formula of the other blessing recited before *Shema*? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: An abounding love [*ahava rabba*]. And Rabbi Elazar instructed his son, Rabbi Pedat, to also say: An abounding love.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵין אוֹמְרִים ״אַהֲבַת עוֹלָם״, אֶלָּא ״אַהֲבָה רַבָּה״. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי אַהֲבַת עוֹלָם, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאַהֲבַת עוֹלָם אֲהַבְתִּיךָ עַל כֵּן מְשַׁכְתִּיךָ חָסֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught in a *baraita*: One does not recite: An eternal love [*ahavat olam*]; rather, one recites: An abounding love. And the Rabbis say that one recites: An eternal love, and so it says: "And an eternal love I have loved you, therefore I have drawn you with kindness"** (Jeremiah 31:3).

*U'v'idach mai hi* — what is the second bracha? Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel: *Ahava Rabba* — great love. Rabbi Elazar similarly instructed his son Rabbi Padas: it is *Ahava Rabba*. A *tanya nami hachi* confirms: one does not recite *Ahavat Olam* in the morning but rather *Ahava Rabba*. The Rabbanan, however, say *Ahavat Olam*, and support their view with the verse: *ve'ahavat olam ahavtich al kein meshachtich chased* — "I have loved you with an eternal love, therefore I have drawn you with loving kindness." For the evening, the bracha used is *Ahavat Olam*.

### Darkness Language

The structure of the second bracha, in both its morning and evening forms, flows naturally into Shema. *Ahava Rabba* and *Ahavat Olam* speak of God's love for Israel; that love is expressed above all through the gift of Torah. The Shema then responds: *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad — ve'ahavta et Hashem Elokecha* — hear O Israel and love God in return. *Ahava Rabba* is therefore more intrinsically a bracha of Shema than *Yotzer Or* is: it establishes the love relationship between God and Israel, the gift of Torah at the center of that relationship, and leads directly into the declaration of Shema and its command of love.

### Day/Night Symmetry

The split — *Ahava Rabba* in the morning and *Ahavat Olam* at night — carries theological meaning. In the morning, one feels the abundant blessings of God: the light, the new day, the overwhelming sense of divine largesse. *Ahava Rabba* — great, abundant love — captures this morning orientation. At night, the mood shifts. The full bracha of *Hashkiveinu* speaks of God's protection through the darkness, asking that we be guarded through the night. In that context, what is most comforting is not the abundance of God's love but its *constancy* — that God's love is eternal, *ahavat olam*, and will accompany us through the darkness, through the hard times, through the fear. It is not the overwhelming brightness of the morning's gift that is invoked, but the steady, enduring love that will never abandon us. *Ahavat olam ahavtich* — "I have loved you with an eternal love" — is the word of comfort for the night.
