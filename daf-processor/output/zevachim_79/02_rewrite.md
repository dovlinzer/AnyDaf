# Zevachim 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bitul Principles

### Ein Dam Mevatel Dam

The daf begins in the middle of 79a, approximately halfway down the page, where the *gemara* presents Rava's summary statement. By way of context, the preceding discussion had been devoted to the principles of *bitul* — nullification — and specifically to the critical distinction between *min b'mino* (like nullifying like) and *min b'eino mino* (unlike nullifying unlike).

The most important position encountered in this context is that of Rabbi Yehuda: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood does not nullify blood. This is an application of the broader principle that *min b'mino lo batel* — when a substance mixes with something identical to itself, it can never lose its identity, because there is no discernible difference between them. If *treif* chicken soup falls into kosher chicken soup, it is all the same substance; one cannot speak of the forbidden element dissolving into the permitted, since they are indistinguishable. Rashi's view is that we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda — though, as will be noted, this is not the accepted *psak*.

### Rashi vs. Chachamim

The accepted ruling follows the *Chachamim*, who hold that *min b'mino* is fundamentally *batel* through *rov* — through majority. If the permitted substance constitutes the majority, the forbidden minority is nullified. This position is what the *halacha* follows in practice.

### Min Be'eino Mino

When the mixture involves *min b'eino mino* — unlike substances — the principle that governs food prohibitions is *notein ta'am*: if the forbidden substance can be tasted, it is discernible and therefore not *batel*; once it falls below the threshold of perceptibility to the palate, it is nullified. This is the standard framework for *issur* in the domain of forbidden foods.

### Bitul in Korbanot

In the context of *korbanot*, however, the relevant sense is not taste but appearance. When the question is not whether to refrain from eating something but whether blood may be applied to the *mizbeach*, the operative criterion is visual discernibility: if the presence of the blood can be detected by sight, it is not *batel*, and — crucially — in the context of *korbanot*, this works in a lenient direction. If the blood is not *batel*, it is still present and may therefore be used for the *zrika*.

### Lenient Eino Mino

A striking and somewhat surprising ruling emerges from the preceding discussion: even when *korban* blood becomes mixed with wine, water, or other blood — a case that is in reality *min b'mino* where one would normally expect the simpler majority-based rule — the *gemara* applies the *ro'in* principle leniently, imagining the case as though it were *eino mino*. In the domain of *issur*, one invokes the *eino mino* framework stringently — even when substances are alike, one imagines they were unlike, in order to disqualify. Here, the very same framework is applied in reverse, to permit: since had the blood been mixed with water it could have been visually discerned, the blood is deemed still present and may be placed on the *mizbeach*. That one can use this reasoning *leniently* is, as noted, quite unexpected.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot, in his commentary toward the end of 79a (the passage running in narrow columns alongside the *gemara* text), raises the difficulty directly. The question — beginning with the line *v'tzarich ledakdek* — is: what distinguishes the realm of *kodashim*, *tumah*, and *mikveh*, where the *ro'in* principle is applied, from the realm of *issur*, where *min b'mino* goes by *rov* and we do not invoke *ro'in*?

Tosafot's answer — *heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar* — is essentially that the rule was stated where it was stated, and was not stated where it was not stated. This is an acknowledgment that no overarching principle reconciles the two domains; the application is category-specific. The result remains striking: in the case of *korban* blood, *min b'mino* does not prevent the use of *ro'in* for a lenient outcome.

### Rava's Summary

Rava now presents a clean summary of the three approaches: *zimnin amri rabbanan b'ta'ama, v'zimnin amri rabbanan b'ruba, v'zimnin amri rabbanan b'chazuta* — sometimes the rabbis rule by taste, sometimes by majority, and sometimes by appearance.

*Min b'she'eino mino* — unlike mixed with unlike — goes by taste. A drop of *chazir* soup that falls into chicken soup is *batel* only if it cannot be tasted; otherwise it is still present. *Min b'mino* — like with like — goes by *rov*, by majority. A drop of non-kosher chicken soup (from an animal not properly *shachted*) that falls into kosher chicken soup is nullified by the majority. And where appearance is the operative criterion — *v'heicha d'ika chazuta b'mora* — one goes by visual discernibility.

### Rashi on Chazuta

Rashi explains the third category: *midi d'lo tali b'tama ella b'chazuta* — where the matter does not depend on taste but on appearance. Sight is the primary sense through which the world is experienced, and therefore in all domains outside of forbidden foods — such as the laws of *mikveh* — presence is determined by whether something is visible or visually detectable. When taste is the governing criterion (as in *issur* of food), one asks whether it can be tasted; when taste is irrelevant, one asks whether it can be seen.

### Notein Ta'am D'Oraita

Tosafot makes an important additional observation from this very *gemara*. Rashi holds that the principle of *ta'am k'ikar* — that a substance which imparts taste is treated as though still present — is only *d'rabbanan*, a rabbinic enactment. Tosafot disagrees, maintaining that it is *d'oraita*. Among his proofs is the present *sugya*: since Rava's summary identifies *min b'mino* going by *rov* as the *d'oraita* standard — and we know that in practice we always require *shishim* (one-in-sixty), a *d'rabbanan* stringency — the discussion here is clearly operating at the *d'oraita* level. And at that same *d'oraita* level, the *gemara* applies *notein ta'am* to the case of *min b'eino mino*. From here Tosafot derives that *ta'am k'ikar* is biblical in origin.

## Issurin Mevatlin

### Reish Lakish

The *gemara* now transitions: *u'pliga d'Rabbi Elazar* — and this disagrees with Rabbi Elazar. The reference is to the principle of Reish Lakish, introduced in the preceding daf, that *issurin mevatlin zeh et zeh* — forbidden substances can nullify one another. If, for example, one has three distinct *issurim* — *pigul*, *notar*, and *tamei* — mixed together, the majority of two *issurim* can nullify the third, even though the nullifying substances are themselves forbidden. The key point is that the *issurim* are different from one another; heterogeneous *issurim* can function as the *mevatel* against another *issur*.

### Rabbi Elazar

Rabbi Elazar disagrees. His principle: *k'shem she'ein mitzvot mevatlot zo et zo* — just as *mitzvot* do not nullify one another, so too *issurin* do not nullify one another. For *bitul* to operate, one needs *hetter* — permitted substance — against *issur*, or in the *korban* context, a neutral substance such as wine or water against *korban* blood. But when everything present is forbidden — even if under different prohibitions — no nullification can take place.

### Hillel's Korech

The source for the principle that *mitzvot* do not nullify one another is attributed to Hillel. The *gemara* cites the *baraita*: *amru alav al Hillel Hazaken shehayah korchan b'vat achat v'ochlan* — they said of Hillel the Elder that he would wrap them together and eat them, based on the verse *al matzot u'merorim yochluhu* — upon *matzot* and *maror* they shall eat it — which Hillel understood as requiring an actual sandwich. In this *korech*, all three components — *korban Pesach*, *matzah*, and *maror* — are chewed and swallowed together. Yet we do not say that the majority of *matzah* and *maror* nullifies the *korban Pesach*, leaving one without having eaten it. Each *mitzvah* retains its identity. From this principle, Rabbi Elazar extends the same logic to *issurim*: just as *mitzvot* are not *mevatel* one another, neither are *issurim*.

### Mitzvot Levels

Tosafot adds an important qualification. *Amru al Hillel shehaya korchan b'vat achat* — this practice of Hillel occurred *bizman habayit*, at the time of the Temple, when all three components — *matzah*, *maror*, and *korban Pesach* — were *d'oraita* obligations. Today, *matzah* is *d'oraita* while *maror* is *d'rabbanan* and *korech* is a *zecher* — a memorial practice. This distinction in levels matters: the reason we eat *maror* separately before *korech*, and the reason *korech* is performed as a separate act, is precisely because *matzah* and *maror* now operate at different levels. If a *d'oraita mitzvah* and a *d'rabbanan mitzvah* were to be conflated in a single act, the *d'rabbanan* one would not carry the same weight as a *mitzvah* in that context, and *bitul* could theoretically occur. The principle that *ein mitzvot mevatlot zo et zo* applies in full only when all the *mitzvot* involved are recognized as operating at the same halachic level. When they are at different levels, the lower-level *mitzvah* might indeed be subject to *bitul* by the higher-level one.

## Porous Vessels

### Zav Urine

At the top of 79b, the *gemara* opens a new *baraita*: *chosin shel zav v'zavah* — a chamber pot made of pottery that was used by a *zav* or *zavah* for urination. The urine of a *zav* or *zavah* is an *av hatumah*, a primary source of *tumah*. Because pottery is porous, the urine is absorbed into the walls of the vessel. Since the urine is no longer *b'ayin* — no longer visibly present — one might think the problem is resolved. It is not, because if the vessel were placed in an oven, or if liquids were placed inside it, the absorbed urine would seep back out through the pores and contaminate those liquids, rendering the vessel effectively *tamei*.

### Rinsing Requirements

*Pa'am rishonah u'shniyah tamei* — rinsing the vessel once or twice is insufficient; trace amounts of urine remain in the pores and will still emerge. *Shlishi tahor* — after three rinsings with water, the vessel is *tahor*, as by that point all remaining urine has been fully expelled. This is noteworthy in itself: ordinarily we say that vessels absorb only through heat, but pottery is sufficiently porous to absorb even cold liquids — a principle that also arises in *Avodah Zarah* in the context of wine absorbed in pottery vessels.

However, this three-rinse rule applies specifically *shenatan letocho mayim* — when one rinsed with water. If instead one used the vessel for ordinary urine — not from a *zav* or *zavah*, which is *tahor* — then even ten times (*afilu asiris tamei*) it remains *tamei*.

### Rashi on Urine

Rashi explains why: after three rinsings, there will still be trace amounts of urine seeping out of the pores. When rinsing with water, those trace amounts are *batel* — nullified by the majority of water. But when rinsing with ordinary urine, any trace of *zav* urine that emerges is *min b'mino* with the ordinary urine surrounding it, and according to Rabbi Yehuda, *min b'mino lo batel* — like substances do not nullify one another. Therefore the vessel remains *tamei* indefinitely, regardless of how many times ordinary urine is used.

### Rabbi Eliezer Ben Yaakov

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees: *shlishi af al pi shelo natan letocho mayim tahor* — three rinsings suffice even without water, because his position is that *min b'mino* is *batel*. In his view, even the trace amounts of *zav* urine that remain after three rinsings are nullified by the ordinary urine, and the vessel is *tahor*.

### Tanna Kamma = R. Yehuda

The *gemara* then identifies the *Tanna Kamma* — the first, anonymous opinion — with Rabbi Yehuda, since it is Rabbi Yehuda who maintains *min b'mino lo batel*, and it is precisely this principle that drives the stringent ruling that ordinary urine cannot effect *tahara* even after multiple uses.

## Flax & Nidda

### Dry vs. Wet Flax

An analogous *baraita* is now cited: *pishtan she-tavaso nidda* — flax spun by a woman who is a *nidda*. In the process of spinning flax, one would moisten the thread with saliva — *mashkeh pihah* — and the saliva of a *nidda* is an *av hatumah*. *Mesito tahor* — if the spun flax is now dry, moving it does not render one *tamei*, since the saliva has been absorbed into the fibers and is not presently accessible; there is nothing actively contaminating. *V'im haya lach mesito tamei* — but if it is still wet from her original saliva, then moving it conveys *tumah*, since the moisture is still present.

### R. Yehuda on Water

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af harok b'mayim tamei mishum mashkeh piha* — even if the thread had dried out completely, if one subsequently wets it again with water, it is once again *tamei*. The introduction of water draws the absorbed saliva back out of the fibers and into the liquid medium, so that the saliva is no longer merely absorbed but is now effectively *b'ayin*, mixed with the water, and the *tumah* is reactivated. Moreover, the *gemara* specifies *v'afilu tuva* — even if one has rinsed the thread many times, according to Rabbi Yehuda it remains a problem.

### Apparent Contradiction

This creates an apparent tension in Rabbi Yehuda's positions. In the case of the chamber pot, the *gemara* identified the *Tanna Kamma* with Rabbi Yehuda, and that *Tanna Kamma* held that three rinsings with water suffice to render the pot *tahor*. But here, Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states that no matter how many times one wets the flax thread and allows it to dry, it remains *tamei*. How are these consistent?

### Rav Pappa's Answer

Rav Pappa resolves the contradiction: *shani rok d'karir* — saliva is different, for saliva is adhesive, almost viscous, and clings to the fibers of the thread far more tenaciously than urine clings to the pores of pottery. Urine, being more fluid and runny, is fully expelled from pottery after three rinsings. Saliva, being sticky, remains bound within the fibers of the thread no matter how many times one wets and dries it.

One might observe that there is a simpler answer available: in the thread case, the rinsing medium is water, which is not *min b'mino* with saliva, and so the trace amounts of saliva are not *batel* in it; whereas in the pottery case, after three rinsings with water, the trace amounts of urine are *batel* in the water since they are *eino mino*. This would explain the discrepancy without any appeal to the physical properties of saliva vs. urine. Indeed, the *acharonim* raise precisely this question, and note that the only way to deflect it is perhaps to say that Rabbi Yehuda does not consider saliva and water to be distinct substances — a strained position. The question stands, though Rav Pappa's physical distinction is what the *gemara* itself offers.

## Mixed Blood Issues

### Pasul Blood Mixture

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* at the end of the chapter. *Nit'arev b'dam pasul yishafech l'amah* — if *korban* blood becomes mixed with *pasul* blood — whether the blood of an animal rendered *piggul*, or *ba'al mum*, or with *dam tamtzit* — the blood is spilled into the drainage channel of the *azarah*, rather than sprinkled. This ruling requires explanation, for we have already established that *korban* blood mixed with other substances is not *batel* and can still be used. Why then must it be discarded?

The *mishnah* presents two categories: blood mixed with *dam psulim* (invalid sacrificial blood) and blood mixed with *dam tamtzit*. *Dam tamtzit* is the blood that continues to drain from a slaughtered animal after the initial surge of *dam ha-nefesh* — the vital blood — has ceased. *Chazal* understood that it is only the initial spurting blood that is valid for the *zrika*; the residual blood that seeps out afterward, the *tamtzit*, may not be placed on the *mizbeach*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Lenient

Rabbi Eliezer takes a lenient position and permits the mixed blood to be used, consistent with his general principle of *ro'eh ani k'ilu hu mayim* — I regard the extraneous component as though it were water. The *Chachamim* disagree and require the blood to be spilled.

### Gezira in Mikdash

The *gemara* asks: what is the underlying dispute? Zvid explains: *b'gozin gezira b'mikdash kamiflagi* — they disagree about whether we enact preventive rabbinic decrees within the *Beit HaMikdash*. It is a well-known principle that certain *d'rabbanan* restrictions do not apply in the *mikdash* (for example, *shvut* of *Shabbat* does not apply to *kohanim* engaged in the *avodah*), because *kohanim* are presumed to be careful. The question here is whether we extend that principle to refrain from enacting *gezerot* in the *mikdash* generally, especially when the cost of a *gezera* is the waste of a *korban*.

What is the specific *gezera* at issue? Rashi explains: we are concerned that if we permit the use of mixed blood in cases where the *korban* blood is not yet *batel*, a *kohen* may come to use it in a case where the *korban* blood actually has been *batel* and the mixture is invalid. The earlier cases — blood mixed with wine, water, or the blood of non-sanctified animals — are rare occurrences in the *mikdash*, and we are not concerned that leniency there will lead to error. But *pasul* blood and *dam tamtzit* arise regularly in the *mikdash*, and so there is reason to be concerned.

### Rav Pappa on Frequency

Rav Pappa offers a different resolution: *d'chulei alma gozin* — everyone agrees that in principle we make *gezerot* even in the *mikdash* when a situation is common. The question is empirical: how common is it that *dam tamtzit* accumulates in such quantity that it would cause the valid *korban* blood to be *batel*? *Mar savar shchiach* — one opinion holds this is a realistic concern, occurring with sufficient frequency to warrant a decree. *Mar savar lo shchiach* — the other holds it is not common enough to be worried about. According to Rav Pappa, the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that common cases require a *gezera*; they simply disagree about whether the case of *dam tamtzit* reaches the threshold of frequency to trigger that concern.

### Separate Cases

The *gemara* notes that according to Rav Pappa's reading, it makes perfect sense that the *mishnah* separates the two cases — *dam psulim* listed independently from *dam tamtzit* — because Rabbi Eliezer concedes the first case and debates only the second. Everyone agrees that *dam psulim* is a sufficiently common occurrence that we decree not to use the mixture; Rabbi Eliezer argues only that *dam tamtzit* causing *bitul* is not common enough to warrant a decree. The *mishnah*'s division of the cases thus neatly maps onto the scope of their dispute.

By contrast, according to Zvid's reading — where the dispute is about whether any *gezera* applies at all in the *mikdash* — Rabbi Eliezer should reject both categories equally, and the *mishnah* need not have separated them. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this is a difficulty for Zvid's position.

### Tosafot on Gezira

Tosafot raises a further question: why does the *gemara* need to frame the dispute as being about a *gezera* at all? Why not simply say this is the longstanding dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* — one holds that non-*korban* blood going onto the *mizbeach* is problematic, and the other says you regard it as water? If the *Chachamim* are categorically opposed to placing non-valid blood on the *mizbeach*, that would explain why they require the mixture to be spilled; and Rabbi Eliezer would permit it because he regards the problematic component as water.

Tosafot answers in two ways. First: if the dispute were framed as a fundamental objection to placing *pasul* blood on the *mizbeach*, Rabbi Eliezer should have explicitly stated his catchphrase — *ro'eh ani k'ilu hu mayim* — but the *mishnah* does not report him saying that here, suggesting the disagreement is based on something other than that principle. Second, and more substantively: the debate also encompasses *dam tamtzit*, and *dam tamtzit* has no more inherent connection to the *mizbeach* than wine or water — it is simply a byproduct of the slaughter process, no different in kind from any other non-sacred liquid. Therefore, placing it on the *mizbeach* is not a *fundamentally* problematic act in the same sense as offering genuinely *pasul* blood. The fact that they even debate the *tamtzit* case demonstrates that the argument cannot be about a fundamental prohibition against non-valid blood on the *mizbeach*; it must instead be grounded in the question of whether a rabbinic *gezera* applies.

## Temimim vs. Mumin

### Temimim & Mumin

The next *mishnah* addresses *dam tmimim b'dam ba'alei mumin* — the blood of unblemished animals (*tmimim*) mixed with the blood of blemished animals (*ba'alei mumin*). This parallels the category of *dam psulim* from the previous *mishnah*, insofar as the blood of a blemished animal may not be offered on the *mizbeach*.

### Unanimous Spilling

*Yishafech l'amah* — the mixture must be spilled, and this ruling is unanimous. Even Rabbi Eliezer, who permits use of mixed blood in the *dam tamtzit* case, does not debate this ruling. This observation connects back to Tosafot's point: the cases are not structured around a fundamental debate over whether *pasul* blood may go on the *mizbeach*, but around the more nuanced question of how common the relevant scenario is and whether a *gezera* is warranted. In the case of blemished animal blood — which is a meaningful and distinct category of *pasul* — perhaps everyone agrees that a *gezera* is appropriate, and Rabbi Eliezer's principle of *ro'eh k'ilu hu mayim* does not override the unanimous concern.

### Kos B'Kosot

A new case: *kos b'kosot* — one basin of *korban* blood among several basins, where one is the blood of a *ba'al mum* and one cannot identify which. This is structurally parallel to the case in other tractates of a prohibited limb mixed indistinguishably among permitted limbs.

### One Offered Logic

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *im kariv kos echad yikrevu kol hakosos* — if one basin was already offered, the rest may be offered as well. One may presume that the basin which was offered was the problematic one, and the remaining basins are all *kasher*. Underlying this position is Rabbi Eliezer's broader willingness to work with the fiction that the problematic element has been discharged — perhaps because he fundamentally accepts that in a case of *lach b'lach* (liquid mixed with liquid), *bitul* can operate or the problematic element is regarded as water, which gives conceptual coherence to his reliance on the presumption that the *pasul* basin was the one offered.

### Chachamim Strict

The *Chachamim* are far more stringent: *afilu karvu kulan chutz me'echad mehen yishafech l'amah* — even if all the basins save one have been offered, and it is nearly certain that the *pasul* blood has already been used, the final remaining basin must still be spilled. There remains a residual possibility that the last basin is the *pasul* one, and the *Chachamim* do not permit acting on the probability alone. This mirrors similar debates throughout the tractate about *vadai* and *safek* in the domain of *kodashim*.

## Above vs. Below

### Yabesh vs. Lach

The *mishnah* now turns to a qualitatively different type of mixture: *hanitannim lemata shenisarvu benitannim lemala* — blood that is applied *below* the *chut hasikra* (the red line midpoint of the *mizbeach*) that has become mixed with blood applied *above* the line. The paradigm case is the blood of a *chatat*, which is applied above the line, becoming mixed with the blood of an *olah* or *shelamim*, which is applied below.

This is also the appropriate point to introduce the conceptual framework that the *rishonim* employ: *yavesh b'yavesh* versus *lach b'lach*. *Yavesh b'yavesh* refers to distinct, individually identifiable items that have been commingled but not merged — one limb among other limbs, one basin among other basins. Each item retains its discrete identity even though one cannot determine which is which. *Lach b'lach* refers to liquids that have been poured together and have become fully intermingled; there is no longer any discrete particle that can be identified as belonging to one source or the other.

Rabbi Eliezer's principle of *ro'eh k'ilu hu mayim* — treating the extraneous blood as though it were water — operates specifically in cases of *lach b'lach*. In *yavesh b'yavesh* cases (distinct cups, distinct limbs), Rabbi Eliezer does not invoke this principle *lechatchila*; he requires that one basin actually be offered before permitting the rest. This distinction is significant: when the bloods have fully merged and every drop of what is sprinkled necessarily contains some of each, Rabbi Eliezer permits it because he treats the extraneous component as water and every act of sprinkling is necessarily an act of sprinkling the valid blood. But when the items remain discrete, one could theoretically sprinkle only the *pasul* one and never the *kasher* one, and so he requires the safeguard of one being done before proceeding.

### Rabbi Eliezer Lach

In the *lach b'lach* case before us, Rabbi Eliezer rules: *yiten lemala, v'roeh ani es hatachtonim milmala ke'ilu hem mayim* — one should first give the blood above, and the blood that belongs below (the *olah* blood) I regard as though it were water. Then proceed to give below as well.

### Two Applications

Rashi explains why this works both ways: when applying above the line, the *chatat* blood is valid for that application and the *olah* blood component is regarded as water. When then applying below the line, the *olah* blood is valid for the lower *zrika*, and the *chatat* blood that is included in the mixture constitutes *shfichat shirayim* — the pouring out of the blood remnants (*shirayim*) of the *chatat*, which is itself a required act that takes place at the base of the *mizbeach*. Thus both applications are legitimized, each in its appropriate way.

### Chachamim Oppose

The *Chachamim* disagree: *yishafech l'amah* — the mixed blood must be spilled entirely. They do not accept the principle of treating the extraneous blood as water, and they are not prepared to allow blood designated for one location on the *mizbeach* to be applied at a different location, even when commingled. They hold that the mixture is irremediably compromised and must be disposed of.

### B'Dieved Kosher

Nevertheless, the *mishnah* records an important *b'dieved* ruling: *v'im lo nimlach v'na'asah kasher* — if the *kohen* did not ask a *halachic* authority and simply went ahead and applied the mixed blood, it is *kasher* after the fact. Even according to the *Chachamim*, if *chatat* blood was applied above and *olah* blood below — all in one commingled application — the *korban* is not disqualified retroactively. The *Chachamim*'s position is *lechatchila*; *b'dieved*, the application stands.

## Matan Arba & Achat

### One Application

The *mishnah* now discusses a related question involving the number of blood applications (*matanot*) required for different *korbanot*. *Hanitannim b'matanah achat* — *korbanot* that require only one application (such as *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach*): if their bloods become mixed together, *yinatnu b'matanah achat* — one application suffices and counts for all of them, since each requires only one, and every sprinkling contains some of each blood.

### Four Applications

*Matan arba b'matan arba* — two *korbanot* that each require four applications (understood as two diagonal corners): if, for example, the blood of an *olah* and the blood of *shelamim* become mixed, *yinatnu bematan arba* — one performs the two diagonal applications and both *korbanot* are discharged. Since both require the same structure, there is no difficulty.

### Four Mixed One

The complex case: *matan arba b'matan achat* — blood of a *korban* requiring four applications (such as an *olah*) becomes mixed with blood of a *korban* requiring only one (such as a *bechor*). Two options present themselves: perform all four applications — which gives the *olah* what it requires but subjects the *bechor* blood to three applications beyond its requirement — or perform only one application — which discharges the *bechor* but gives the *olah* only a fraction of what it requires, though this is valid *b'dieved*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Four

Rabbi Eliezer rules *yinatnu bematan arba* — perform all four applications. This follows consistently from his principle: the *bechor* blood that is mixed in is treated as water for the three applications beyond the first. He is doing the four applications for the sake of the *olah*, and the extraneous *bechor* blood is not a concern.

### Rabbi Yehoshua One

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees: *yinatnu b'matanah achat* — only one application should be performed. He is unwilling to adopt the fiction that the *bechor* blood is water; in his view, applying blood at three additional corners where it does not belong constitutes a prohibited act, and he would rather do the minimum — one application — which serves both *korbanot b'dieved*.

## Bal Tosif vs. Tigra

### Rabbi Eliezer Challenge

Rabbi Eliezer challenges Rabbi Yehoshua's position: *harei hu over al bal tigra* — by performing only one application when the *olah* requires four, Rabbi Yehoshua causes a transgression of the prohibition *lo tigre'u mimenu* — do not diminish from it. The verse *lo tosifu al hadavar asher anochi metzaveh etchem velo tigre'u mimenu* — do not add to the matter which I command you and do not subtract from it — is understood by *Chazal* not only as a prohibition against adding or subtracting from the corpus of *mitzvot*, but against altering the quantitative structure of an individual *mitzvah*.

### Bal Tigra Explained

*Bal tosif* in its most familiar form means: do not take five species for *lulav*, do not construct *tefillin* with five compartments. *Bal tigra* is a subtler category. One might ask: is there ever a way to be transgress *bal tigra*? If one simply does not do the *mitzvah*, one has merely failed to perform it — one has not actively subtracted from it. The answer is that *bal tigra* applies when one begins the *mitzvah* but performs it in a quantitatively lesser way — doing one corner instead of two, for example — while still technically fulfilling the *mitzvah*. The act was done, but a quantifiable dimension of it was diminished. This is a striking application of the principle, and notable precisely because the *mitzvah* is not entirely omitted but performed inadequately in a measurable respect.

### Counter to R. Eliezer

Rabbi Yehoshua responds in kind: *harei hu over al bal tosif* — according to Rabbi Eliezer's position, performing four applications when the *bechor* blood is present causes the *bechor* blood to be applied at three corners where it does not belong, constituting an addition to the *mitzvah* of *bechor* blood application — a transgression of *bal tosif*.

### Done for Olah

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *lo ne'emar bal tosif ela keshehu be'atzmo* — *bal tosif* is violated only when one takes the problematic element and performs a distinct act with it in its own right. Here, the blood of the *bechor* is not being applied for the sake of the *bechor*; it is fully intermingled with the *olah* blood, and every application is performed entirely for the sake of the *olah*. There is no independent act being done with the *bechor* blood. Rabbi Eliezer here may be extending his principle beyond the specific formulation of *ro'eh k'ilu hu mayim*: even setting aside that principle, he argues that there is no *ma'aseh* of adding to the *bechor*'s *mitzvah* because the intention and the act are directed entirely toward the *olah*. At the end of the day, one is not doing something extra with the *bechor* — one is doing what the *olah* requires.

### Passive vs. Active

Rabbi Yehoshua offers a final response: *lo ne'emar bal tigra k'sheu be'atzmo* — and similarly, one should not say I am transgressing *bal tigra* with the *olah* blood, because the reason I am performing only one application is not a desire to diminish the *olah*; it is because performing more applications would involve the *bechor* blood in a prohibited way. My abstention is forced by circumstances.

And beyond that structural argument, Rabbi Yehoshua articulates a broader principle: *keshenatata avarta al bal tosif asita ma'aseh beyadecha; keshelo natata avarta al bal tigra velo asita ma'aseh beyadecha* — if you perform four applications, you have transgressed *bal tosif* by a positive act; if you perform only one, you have transgressed *bal tigra* by omission. Given that one must choose between the two, it is preferable to commit a passive sin — failing to do what is required — than an active sin — doing what is prohibited. A sin of omission is less grave than a sin of commission, and therefore Rabbi Yehoshua holds that one should do only the one application and accept the passive shortcoming in the *olah*'s *avodah* rather than actively apply blood where it does not belong.

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, appears to hold that this is not merely a forced choice between two bad options, but that his position avoids *bal tosif* entirely: since the extra applications are done for the sake of the *olah* and not as an independent act with the *bechor* blood, there is no transgression at all on that side, and therefore it is clearly preferable to do the full four applications and fulfill the *olah*'s *mitzvah* completely.
