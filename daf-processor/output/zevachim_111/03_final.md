# Zevachim 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Water & Kli Sharet

### Three Logs Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְנַסֵּךְ שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין מַיִם בֶּחָג בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר (בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן) אוֹמֵר: אִם מִלְּאָן לְשֵׁם חַג – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who pours as a libation three *log* of water during the festival** of *Sukkot* **outside** the courtyard is **liable. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: If he filled** a service vessel with the three *log* in order to consecrate **them for the sake of** the **Festival,** he is **liable.**

We pick up at the top of 111a, continuing a discussion of the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* regarding whether one who pours three *log* of water outside the Temple is liable. The dispute, as we have been exploring, turns on the precise conditions under which such liability attaches.

### Kli Sharet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: בֵּירוּצֵי מִידּוֹת אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the difference **between them? Rav Adda bar Rav Yitzḥak said:** The difference **between them is** with regard to whether the **overfill of measuring vessels** is also consecrated. Both agree that one is liable for pouring a libation outside the courtyard only if it was first consecrated in a service vessel. The first *tanna* holds that the liquid that rises above the rim of a vessel is also consecrated, and if one collects three *log* of that liquid and pours it as a libation outside the courtyard he is liable. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds that only the wine within the walls of the vessel itself is consecrated.

One way of defining the core of this debate is whether a *kli sharet* — a sacred service vessel — is required to sanctify the water in order to create liability. In the Temple itself, a *kli sharet* is certainly required. But the operative question is whether such a vessel was required on a private *bamah* during the period when private altars were permitted. The mechanism by which one becomes liable outside the Temple is not necessarily identical to the Temple rite; as we know, the standard for liability is *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* — fitness for offering at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting — and that standard need not mirror every detail of the Temple service. The example is raised of placing an offering on a rock without fire, which would not be valid in the Temple but may have been valid on a *bamah*.

### Private Altars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה אָמַר: קָרְבוּ נְסָכִים בְּבָמָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – דְּתַנְיָא: בָּמַת יָחִיד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה נְסָכִים. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: טְעוּנָה נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava, son of Rabba, said:** The difference **between them is** with regard to whether one is liable for pouring a libation outside the courtyard that was not first consecrated in a service vessel. This dispute is based on a disagreement as to whether wine **libations were offered on** private **altars.** He explains: **And** they disagree **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: An offering sacrificed on **a private altar does not need** to be accompanied by wine **libations;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say:** It does **require** wine **libations.** Service vessels are not used in the context of private altars. Therefore, if libations are brought on private altars then it is apparent that libations can be valid even if they were not first consecrated in a service vessel. Accordingly, one would be liable for pouring a libation outside the Temple even if it had not first been consecrated in a service vessel. If libations are not brought on private altars, then there is no precedent of a libation that was not first consecrated in a service vessel, and one would not be liable for pouring a non-consecrated libation outside the Temple.

This question — whether wine libations could be brought on a *bamah* without a *kli sharet*, and whether water libations could similarly be brought — frames the entire discussion. We will return to develop it further as the Gemara continues.

## Wine Libations

### Wine Rule

The Gemara introduces a closely parallel *baraita*: one who pours three *log* of wine outside the Temple — *hamenaseach shlosha lugin yayin bachutz*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon declares him liable. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon then qualifies: *vehu shekidshen bikli* — only if he sanctified the wine in a vessel. This closely parallels the earlier formulation involving water, and the similarity is what led some authorities to emend the earlier text from "Rabbi Eliezer" to "Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon," or to read the earlier ruling as referring to a *kli sharet* rather than merely a vessel.

### Heaping Liquid

*Mai beinaihu* — what is the practical or conceptual crux of their disagreement? Rav Adda bar Abba explains: *beirutzei midos ika beinaihu* — they disagree about *beirutzei midos*, the heaping of liquid at the rim of a vessel. When a liquid is sanctified in a *kli sharet*, does the sanctification extend to the small overflow held in place at the rim by surface tension, or only to the liquid within the vessel walls? The *chachamim* hold that any measure of three *log* — however that quantity came together in the vessel — suffices for liability. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon holds that sanctification in a vessel means precisely within the vessel walls; the overflow is not included. One might ask whether this same dispute applies to the *minchah* offering, and the answer is yes — it is not specific to wine or water libations.

### Libations Bamah

*Rava bar Rabbah offers an alternative: *kirvu nesachim babamah ika beinaihu* — they disagree about whether libations were in fact brought on private altars when *bamot* were permitted. This is essentially equivalent to asking whether they were brought in the wilderness, as the Gemara treats these as related questions determined by the same sources. If libations were brought on a *bamah*, they were necessarily brought without a *kli sharet*, since *bamot* had no *kli sharet*. Thus the *Tanna Kamma* holds that three *log* of wine creates liability even without a *kli sharet*, because such a rite existed on a *bamah* and is therefore *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* — or rather, fit for a *bamah* in the time when *bamot* were permissible. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon holds that *bamot* never had libations, and therefore without a *kli sharet* the act is not sufficiently recognized as a sacrificial rite to generate liability.

## Private Altar Libations

### Rabbi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּי תָבֹאוּ״ – לְהַטְעִינָהּ נְסָכִים לְבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בָּמָה קְטַנָּה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֶל אֶרֶץ מוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי נֹתֵן לָכֶם״ – הֲרֵי בְּבָמָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְכוּלְּכֶם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the opinion of **these *tanna'im* is like** the opinion of **those *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In introducing the mitzva to bring wine libations together with animal offerings, the verse states: **"When you come** into the land of your dwellings, which I give to you" (Numbers 15:2), which indicates that the mitzva to bring libations began only once the Jewish people entered Eretz Yisrael. **The verse speaks** in order **to require** that **libations** be brought with animal offerings that are brought **upon a great** public **altar.** This assumes libations were not brought on a public altar in the wilderness. Therefore, it is necessary to teach that upon entering Eretz Yisrael they are required. **Do you say** that the verse is referring **to** offerings brought on **a great** public **altar, or is it even** referring **to** offerings brought on **a small** private **altar?** Perhaps libations were brought on the public altar in the wilderness, and it is therefore unnecessary to state that after entering Eretz Yisrael libations should continue to be brought on a public altar. Accordingly, the verse must be teaching that after the Jewish people have entered Eretz Yisrael, libations are required even on private altars. This suggestion is rejected: **When** the verse **states: "Into the land of your dwellings, which I give to you [*lakhem*],"** using the plural form of the word "you," it is apparent that **the verse is speaking of** a public **altar that is used by everyone;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The Gemara notes that this debate connects to a dispute among *tannaim*, introduced with *detanya*. The *baraita* states: *bamas yachid einah tzericha nesachim* — a private altar does not require libations. This is the position of Rabbi. The question here is not merely whether libations were permitted on a *bamah*, but whether they were obligatory. However, the Gemara treats permissibility and obligation as two sides of the same coin in this context: if libations are not required, they are equally not practiced.

### Sages' View

The *chachamim* disagree: *te'unah nesachim* — a private altar does require libations. This is the opposing position. The debate is thus about whether the phenomenon of libations on a private *bamah* ever existed as a recognized institution.

### Debate Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי תָבֹאוּ״ – לְהַטְעִינָהּ נְסָכִים (בְּבָמָה) [לְבָמָה] קְטַנָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְבָמָה קְטַנָּה; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֶל אֶרֶץ מוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם״ – הֲרֵי בְּבָמָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת בְּכׇל מוֹשָׁבוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. כְּשֶׁתִּמְצָא לוֹמַר; לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – לֹא קָרְבוּ נְסָכִים בַּמִּדְבָּר, וּלְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – קָרְבוּ נְסָכִים בַּמִּדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** Through its introductory clause: **"When you come," the verse speaks** in order **to require** that **libations** be brought with animal offerings that are brought **upon a small** private **altar.** This assumes libations were already brought in the wilderness, and the verse must be teaching that libations are required even on private altars. **Do you say** that the verse is speaking of **a small** private **altar** outside the Temple? **Or is it only** referring **to a great** public **altar?** Perhaps libations were not brought on the public altar in the wilderness and the verse is necessary in order to teach that upon entering Eretz Yisrael they are required. **When** the verse **states: "Into the land of your dwellings,"** it is apparent that **the verse is speaking of an altar that is used in all your dwellings,** which certainly must be referring to private altars, as there was only one central public altar. The Gemara explains: **When** you analyze the matter **you will find** that you can **say** that **according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, libations were not offered in the wilderness.** Therefore, it is necessary to teach that upon entering Eretz Yisrael they are required. **And according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva libations were offered in the wilderness.** Therefore, the verse must be teaching that libations are required even on private altars.

The connection between these two *tannaitic* disputes is direct. Whether libations were brought on a private *bamah* — which, by definition, had no *kli sharet* — determines whether one can be liable for performing such a rite outside the Temple without a *kli sharet* today. If the *chachamim* hold that libations were practiced on private altars, then offering libations without a *kli sharet* is a form of worship that was once legitimate, and one who does so outside the Temple in a forbidden time becomes liable. If Rabbi holds that libations were never part of the private *bamah* rite, then performing them without a *kli sharet* has no template of legitimacy, and there is no liability.

## Scriptural Debate

### Yishmael Central

The Gemara now traces this dispute about libations on private altars back to a fundamental *tannaitic* disagreement about the meaning of the verse at the end of Parshas Shlach: *ki tavo el eretz moshvoseichem asher ani nosen lachem* — "when you come to the land of your dwellings that I give you" — followed by the requirement to bring wine, flour, and oil together with one's offering. Rabbi Yishmael reads the verse as follows: the default assumption is that the Torah is speaking of a *bamah gedolah*, a central altar. The phrase "when you come to the land" introduces the obligation of *nesachim* — libations — which applies only upon entry into the land and only at a central location. The phrase *moshvoseichem*, "your dwellings," is read collectively: the land given to you as a people, implying a single, communal place of worship. On this reading, libations were never obligatory — or practiced — in the wilderness, nor on private altars.

### Akiva Private

Rabbi Akiva reads the verse differently. His initial assumption is that the Torah is speaking of a *bamah ketanah*, a private altar. The word *moshvoseichem*, he argues, emphasizes the individual dwellings of each person: wherever you dwell, that is where you may bring your offering together with libations. This is consistent with the well-known practice of the period of the *bamot*, when, as the verse in Kings describes, the people sacrificed and offered incense on *bamot* everywhere — each person at his own private altar.

### Wilderness Inference

The Gemara draws a further inference: *keshtimtzai lomar* — one may say — that according to Rabbi Yishmael, libations were not brought in the wilderness at all, while according to Rabbi Akiva, they were. The connection runs as follows: if libations were already being brought in the *mishkan* in the wilderness, then the verse's instruction "when you come to the land" must be introducing something new — namely, the permission or obligation to bring libations on private altars throughout the land. If, however, libations were not yet brought in the wilderness, then "when you come" introduces the basic obligation for the first time, and it is directed toward the central location. The verse does not proceed to extend that obligation to private altars. This reasoning, while not immediately obvious from the verse alone, is elaborated by Rashi at length.

## Remaining Blood

### Nechemya Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** For the **remainder of the blood** of an offering that was supposed to be poured at the base of the altar and **that** instead **one sacrificed outside** the courtyard, one is **liable.**

The Gemara now turns to the end of the *mishnah*, where Rabbi Nechemya states: *shearei hadam shehikriv bachutz chayav* — one who sprinkles the remaining blood outside the Temple is liable. The *shirayim* — the remainder of the blood poured at the base of the altar — is not among the primary blood rites that effect atonement. Why, then, should sprinkling this remainder outside create liability?

### Yochanan Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: [תַּנָּא] רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שִׁירַיִים מְעַכְּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Neḥemya taught** this *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood at the base of the altar **disqualifies** the offering.

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *tana d'Rabbi Nechemya k'divrei haomer shearayim me'akvim* — Rabbi Nechemya's teaching follows the position of those who hold that the pouring of the *shirayim* is essential, *me'akev*, for atonement. Only if the *shirayim* is indispensable to the *kapara* does it qualify as an act that is *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed*, and only then does performing it outside generate liability. This is a striking position when applied to an outer offering, since we are accustomed to hearing that the *shirayim* is *me'akev* only in the context of inner *chata'ot*, where every stage of the blood service is essential.

### Akiva Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שְׁיָרֵי מִצְוָה הֵם! אָמַר לוֹ: אֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים יוֹכִיחוּ – שֶׁהֵן שְׁיָרֵי מִצְוָה, וְהַמַּקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב! אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּאֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים – שֶׁהֵן תְּחִלַּת עֲבוֹדָה, תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁאֵינָן תְּחִלַּת עֲבוֹדָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Neḥemya says** that for the **remainder of the blood** of an offering **that one sacrificed outside** the courtyard, one is **liable. Rabbi Akiva said to him: Isn't** pouring **the remainder of the blood** considered **a non-essential mitzva,** which is not indispensable to the validity of the offering? Accordingly, one should not be liable for sacrificing the blood outside the Temple courtyard. Rabbi Neḥemya **said to him:** Sacrificial **limbs and fats** of a burnt offering **will prove** the matter, **as they are** considered **a non-essential mitzva, and** yet **one who sacrifices them outside** the courtyard is **liable.** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: No, if you said** that one is liable **with regard to** the burning of the **limbs and fats, which is the start of** a sacrificial **rite,** i.e., burning them is a sacrificial rite in and of itself, **shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the pouring of the **remainder of the blood, which is not the start of** a sacrificial **rite,** but is just the conclusion of the sprinkling of the blood?

The Gemara challenges this interpretation by citing the *baraita* in which Rabbi Akiva engages Rabbi Nechemya directly. Rabbi Akiva asks: *v'halo shearei hadam shearei mitzvah hein* — surely the *shirayim* are merely the residual conclusion of the blood rite, not its essential core. Rabbi Nechemya responds: *evarim u'fedarim yochichu* — let the entrails and fats prove the point. One who offers the entrails and fats outside is liable, yet their offering does not effect atonement. Why should the blood *shirayim* be any different?

Rabbi Akiva replies: *im amarta b'evarim u'fedarim sheheim techilat avoda* — when you speak of the entrails and fats, you are speaking of an act that is the beginning and substance of its own distinct ritual. The offering of the *evarim* is not a coda to the blood service; it is itself an independent act with its own integrity. The blood *shirayim*, by contrast, is precisely that — a coda, a conclusion, a wrap-up of the primary blood rite. *Tomar b'shearei dam sheeinam techilat avoda* — can you say the same of the remaining blood, which is not the beginning of any act?

The discussion touches briefly on the view that *im basar ein dam* — that the validity of the blood may depend on the offering of the inner organs — which would give the *evarim* an additional significance in relation to the blood. But this position is itself disputed, and the Gemara does not pursue it further here.

The implication is clear: the straightforward reading of the *baraita* is that Rabbi Nechemya does not require the *shirayim* to be *me'akev* in order to establish liability outside. He holds simply that any blood rite, as long as it is still *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed*, generates liability. It is Rabbi Akiva who introduces the distinction between the *shirayim* and the *evarim* — a logical distinction, though perhaps not the only possible one. On this simple reading, Rabbi Nechemya's position does not depend on the claim that *shirayim* are *me'akev*.

### Inner vs Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: הָנֵי נָמֵי מְעַכְּבִי! תְּיוּבְתָּא. וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשִׁירַיִים הַפְּנִימִיִּים, אֲבָל בְּשִׁירַיִים הַחִיצוֹנִים – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא מְעַכְּבִי; כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – בְּשִׁירַיִים הַפְּנִימִים, כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – בְּשִׁירַיִים הַחִיצוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the challenge from the *baraita*: **And if it is so** that Rabbi Neḥemya holds that that failure to pour the remainder of the blood at the base of the altar disqualifies the offering, **let** Rabbi Neḥemya **say** in response **to** Rabbi Akiva: **These too,** i.e., the pouring of the remainder of the blood, are considered to be a sacrificial rite in and of themselves because failure to pour out the remainder **disqualifies** the offering. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this is **a conclusive refutation.** The Gemara qualifies its rejection: **And now that Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The **dispute** between the *tanna'im* with regard to whether failure to pour the remainder of the blood disqualifies the offering is only **with regard to** the **remainder of** blood that was presented on **the inner** altar, **but with regard to** the **remainder of** blood that was presented on **the external** altar **everyone agrees** that failure to pour it **does not disqualify** the offering; the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement and the *baraita* can therefore be resolved. **When Rabbi Neḥemya says** in the mishna that one is liable for sacrificing the remainder of the blood outside the courtyard, he is referring **to** the **remainder of** blood that was presented on **the inner** altar. The pouring of that blood is considered a rite in and of itself, and one is liable for sacrificing it outside the Temple. **When that** statement of Rabbi Neḥemya **is taught** in the *baraita*, it is referring **to** the **remainder of** blood that was presented on **the external** altar. Concerning such blood, Rabbi Neḥemya concedes that the pouring is not considered a rite in and of itself.

At this point the Gemara introduces the resolution of Rav Ada bar Ahava: *machlokes beshirayim pnimiyim aval beshirayim chitzoniyim divrei hakol lo me'akvei* — the debate about whether *shirayim* are *me'akev* applies only to the inner *chatas*, offered on the inner altar. By the outer *korbanot*, everyone agrees that the *shirayim* are not *me'akev*, which is entirely consistent with our understanding of the law. Once this is established, the Gemara can defend Rabbi Yochanan.

Rabbi Nechemya's original statement — that one is liable for sprinkling *shirayim* outside — was made in the context of an inner *chatas*, where he holds that *shirayim* are indeed *me'akev*. The debate between him and Rabbi Akiva, however, was conducted in the context of an outer *korban*. This explains why Rabbi Nechemya did not say to Rabbi Akiva "they are *me'akev*" — because by an outer *korban*, he agrees they are not. Of course, this raises a further question: why did Rabbi Nechemya not simply clarify to Rabbi Akiva that he was only speaking of an inner *chatas* and that the debate was therefore beside the point? The Gemara answers: *Rabbi Nechemya lidvarav de'Rabbi Akiva ka'amar* — Rabbi Nechemya was engaging Rabbi Akiva on his own terms. He was effectively saying: even if we speak about an outer *korban*, I will argue with you that a case could be made for liability. Rabbi Akiva then responds by drawing the distinction between the *evarim* and the *shirayim*, and that distinction stands for the outer *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לָא יָדַע מַאי קָאָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה; הוּא סָבַר: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה שִׁירַיִים חִיצוֹנִים אָמַר, וְקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ שִׁירַיִים הַחִיצוֹנִים. וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** In light of this, the Gemara explains the discussion between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Neḥemya: **And Rabbi Akiva did not know what Rabbi Neḥemya was saying.** Rabbi Akiva **thought** that **Rabbi Neḥemya was stating** a ruling about the pouring of the **remainder of** blood that was presented on the **external** altar. Therefore, Rabbi Akiva **responded to him** with a claim relating to the **remainder of** blood that was presented on **the external** altar and said that it is a non-essential mitzva. **And** then **Rabbi Neḥemya** answered him by **saying** a defense of his opinion **in accordance with** the misconception underlying **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The underlying question remains as to whether this is a *mesorah* — a received tradition — or whether Rav Ada bar Ahava and the other *amoraim* are simply applying logical reasoning to resolve the difficulties. Since no source is cited, it appears to be the latter.

## Bird Slaughter

### Melikah Pnimis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹלֵק אֶת הָעוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. מָלַק בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הָעוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. שָׁחַט בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who pinches** the nape of **a bird** offering **inside** the Temple courtyard **and** then **offers** it **up outside** the courtyard is **liable.** But if **one pinched** its nape **outside** the courtyard **and** then **offered** it **up outside** the courtyard he is **exempt,** as pinching the nape of a bird outside the courtyard is not considered valid pinching. **One who slaughters,** with a knife, **a bird** offering **inside** the courtyard **and offers** it **up outside** the courtyard is **exempt,** as slaughtering a bird offering in the Temple courtyard disqualifies it as an offering. But if one **slaughtered** a bird offering **outside** the courtyard **and** then **offered** it **up outside,** he is **liable.**

The Gemara turns to a new *mishnah*: *hamolek et ha-of bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz chayav* — one who performs *melikah* inside the Temple and then raises the bird outside is liable. *Melikah* — the pinching of the bird's neck — is the valid method of slaughter for bird offerings inside the Temple. Performing it there constitutes a valid act, and the subsequent *ha'ala'ah* outside therefore generates liability.

### Melikah Chutz

*Malak bachutz ve-he'ela bachutz* — if one performed *melikah* outside the Temple and then raised the bird outside, one is exempt on both counts. One is exempt on the *melikah* itself because the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* applies only through the framework of *halacha*, and *melikah* performed outside has no recognized standing. More significantly, the *melikah* performed outside invalidates the bird entirely, rendering the subsequent *ha'ala'ah* not *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* — the bird is now disqualified, and lifting it outside does not constitute a meaningful sacrificial act.

### Shechitah Pnimis

*Hashochet et ha-of bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz* — if one slaughtered a bird inside the Temple and then raised it outside, one is exempt. Here the logic is reversed: *shechitah* — knife slaughter — is the valid method for animal offerings, but for birds inside the Temple, the proper method is *melikah*. Performing *shechitah* on a bird inside the Temple is therefore an invalid act, equivalent to striking the bird in the head. Since the initial act inside was meaningless, the bird is *pasul*, and the subsequent *ha'ala'ah* outside is also exempt.

### Shechitah Chutz

*Shachat bachutz ve-he'ela bachutz chayav* — if one slaughtered a bird outside and then raised it outside, one is liable. This is the counterintuitive case. *Shechitah* for a bird is entirely invalid inside the Temple, yet outside the Temple it is treated as the operative act that triggers liability for the *ha'ala'ah* that follows. The analogy to *piggul* is instructive: within the framework of what one is doing — performing a sacrificial rite outside the Temple — the fact that it takes place outside is bracketed, and the act is evaluated on its own terms. *Shechitah* of a bird outside fits the parameters of *shechitah* in a way that *melikah* outside does not. *Melikah* outside the Temple is not a recognizable ritual act at all; *shechitah* outside, while forbidden, is at least formally coherent as an act of slaughter.

### Location Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא דֶּרֶךְ הֶכְשֵׁירוֹ בִּפְנִים – פְּטוּרוֹ בַּחוּץ, דֶּרֶךְ הֶכְשֵׁירוֹ מִבַּחוּץ – פְּטוּרוֹ בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Evidently, the manner of its preparation inside** the courtyard, i.e., pinching, effects **its exemption outside** the courtyard, and **the manner of its preparation outside** the courtyard, i.e., slaughter, effects **its exemption inside** the courtyard.

The *mishnah* draws this together with the principle: *nimtza derech hechshero bifnim paturo bachutz* — the manner in which an act is valid inside is the manner in which performing it outside creates exemption, and vice versa. The Gemara will emend *hechshero* to *chiyuvo* for the second half of the formulation: *derech chiyuvo bifnim paturo bachutz*, *derech chiyuvo bachutz paturo bifnim*. Following Rashi's reading: the act that makes one liable when started inside — *melikah* — makes one exempt when started outside; the act that makes one liable when started outside — *shechitah* — makes one exempt when started inside. Everything is defined by its context.

## Shimon Principle

### Shimon Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיבִין עַל כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ בִּפְנִים שֶׁהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ; חוּץ מִן הַשּׁוֹחֵט בִּפְנִים וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **outside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also **liable for** having **offered** the animal **up outside** the courtyard after performing **a similar** act of killing **inside** the courtyard. This is the *halakha* **except with regard to one who slaughters** a bird **inside** the courtyard **and offers** it **up outside** the courtyard; he is exempt.

Rabbi Shimon states: *kol shechayavin alav bachutz chayavin al kayotzei bo bifnim* — whatever one is liable for when performed outside the Temple, one is liable for the equivalent act performed inside, provided the sequence ends with *ha'ala'ah* outside. He then adds the exception: *chutz min hashochet et ha-of bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz* — except for the case of one who slaughtered a bird inside and raised it outside.

### Melikah Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ הַאי ״הֶכְשֵׁירוֹ״?! חִיּוּבוֹ הוּא! תְּנִי ״חִיּוּבוֹ״. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר [וְכוּ׳]. אַהֵיכָא קָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In summarizing its rulings the mishna states: The manner of its preparation outside the courtyard effects its exemption inside the courtyard. The Gemara comments: **This** term: The manner of **its preparation,** is inappropriate when referring to the slaughter of a bird offering, as a bird offering is not prepared by slaughtering; on the contrary, it is disqualified if slaughtered. The slaughter of a bird offering outside the courtyard **is** the reason for **its liability.** The Gemara concedes: Emend the mishna and **teach: Its liability.** § The mishna cites the ruling of Rabbi Shimon: **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to any act of killing an animal concerning which, when it was performed outside the courtyard, one is liable for subsequently offering it up outside the courtyard, one is also liable for having offered the animal up outside the courtyard after performing a similar act of killing inside the courtyard. This is the *halakha* except with regard to one who slaughters a bird inside the courtyard and offers it up outside the courtyard; he is exempt. The Gemara asks: **To what does he refer?**

The Gemara asks: *ahi kai* — to which case in the *mishnah* is Rabbi Shimon responding? Could he be addressing the *melikah* case? If his principle is "what makes you liable outside makes you liable inside," and we apply it to *melikah*, it would mean: since *melikah* inside makes you liable (when followed by *ha'ala'ah* outside), therefore *melikah* outside should also make you liable. But Rabbi Shimon said the reverse — *if you're liable outside, you're liable inside* — which implies starting from the *chutz* case and deriving the *bifnim* case. That would mean he is trying to extend liability to something done inside based on what is liable outside. If he were discussing *melikah*, he should have said the opposite: *kol shechayavin alav bifnim chayavin al kayotzei bo bachutz* — what makes you liable inside makes you liable outside. The language of the *mishnah* does not fit *melikah*.

Alternatively, perhaps he is trying to *exempt* by analogy: since *melikah* outside does not make you liable, neither should *melikah* inside. But then he should have said *kol she'ein chayavin aleha bachutz* — what you are exempt for outside, you are exempt for inside. Again, the language does not fit.

### Bird Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִילֵּימָא אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי: הַמּוֹלֵק עוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, מָלַק בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּפְנִים מִיחַיַּיב, בַּחוּץ נָמִי מִיחַיַּיב. הַאי ״כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ״?! ״כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בִּפְנִים״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְחוּץ לָא מִיחַיַּיב, בִּפְנִים נָמֵי לָא לִיחַיַּיב? הַאי ״כֹּל שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אַסֵּיפָא קָאֵי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. שָׁחַט בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּבִפְנִים לָא מִיחַיַּיב, בַּחוּץ נָמֵי לָא מִיחַיַּיב. הַאי ״כֹּל שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say that he is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, which states: **One who pinches** the nape of the **bird** offering **inside and** then **offers** it **up outside** is **liable,** but if **one pinched** its nape **outside and** then **offered** it **up outside,** he is **exempt; and** it is with the second part of this clause that **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees and **says to** the first *tanna*: **Just as** one who pinches the nape of a bird offering **inside** and then offers it up outside is **liable, so too** one who pinches its nape **outside** and then offers it up outside is **liable,** this is difficult. If **that** is what Rabbi Shimon intended, then instead of saying in the mishna: With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **outside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, **he should have** said: With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **inside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also liable for a similar act of killing done outside the courtyard. Evidently, Rabbi Shimon does not disagree with the first *tanna* on this issue. **Rather,** perhaps Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the first part of the first clause and says to the first *tanna*: **Just as** one who pinches the nape of a bird offering **outside** and then offers it up outside is **not liable, so too** one who pinches its nape **inside** and then offers it up outside is **not liable.** The Gemara rejects this: If **that** is what Rabbi Shimon intended, then **he should have** said: With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **outside** the courtyard, one is **not liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also not liable for having offered the animal up outside the courtyard after performing a similar act of killing inside the courtyard. Evidently, Rabbi Shimon does not disagree with the first *tanna* on this issue either. **Rather, he is referring to the latter clause** of the mishna: **One who slaughters a bird** offering **inside and offers** it **up outside is exempt,** but if **one slaughtered** a bird offering **outside and** then **offered** it **up outside,** he is **liable. And** it is with the second part of this clause that **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees and **says to** the first *tanna*: **Just as** one who slaughters a bird offering **inside** and then offers it up outside is **not liable, so too** one who slaughters it **outside** and then offers it up outside is **not liable.** The Gemara rejects this: If **that** is what Rabbi Shimon intended, then **he should have** said: With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed inside the courtyard, one is **not liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also not liable for having offered the animal up outside the courtyard after performing a similar act of killing outside the courtyard. Evidently, Rabbi Shimon does not disagree with the first *tanna* on this issue.

The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Shimon must be addressing the *shechitah* case, and his principle reads as follows: *ki heichi d'bechutz mechayev bifnim nami mechayev* — just as *shechitah* of a bird outside makes one liable, so too *shechitah* of a bird inside should make one liable. The language fits perfectly: whatever you are liable for outside — *shechitah* — you are liable for the equivalent inside. But then he immediately carves out the exception: *chutz min hashochet ha-of bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz*, except for the case of one who slaughtered a bird inside and raised it outside. The very need to state this exception confirms that without it, his principle would have implied liability in that case. This is a striking move: the *mishnah* establishes a general rule — if liable outside, liable inside — and then immediately negates its most obvious application.

### Night & Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר זְעֵירִי: שְׁחִיטַת בְּהֵמָה בַּלַּיְלָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: [וְכֵן] הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּהֵמָה בִּפְנִים בַּלַּיְלָה וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. שָׁחַט בַּחוּץ בַּלַּיְלָה וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיבִין עַל כְּיוֹצֵא בּוֹ בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ, חוּץ מִן הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** If Rabbi Shimon is not referring to any of the rulings mentioned in the mishna, he must be disagreeing with the first *tanna* with regard to another issue. **Ze'eiri said:** The difference **between** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon **is** with regard to the **slaughter of an animal** offering **at night** inside the courtyard, **and this** is what the mishna **is saying:** The first *tanna* said: **And so too, one who slaughters an animal** offering **inside** the courtyard **at night and** then **offers** it **up outside** is **exempt,** as by slaughtering the animal at night he caused it to become disqualified. But one who **slaughtered** an animal **outside** the courtyard **at night and** then **offered** it **up outside** is **liable.** It is with this ruling that **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees and **says:** With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **outside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also **liable for** having **offered** the animal **up outside** the courtyard after performing **a similar** act of killing **inside** the courtyard. This is the *halakha* **except with regard to one who slaughters** a bird **inside** the courtyard **and offers** it **up outside** the courtyard; he is exempt.

Zeiri provides an example of where Rabbi Shimon's principle does apply: *shechitas behemah balaylah ika beinaihu* — the slaughter of an animal at night. The *Tanna Kamma* might implicitly apply the bird model, treating the *bifnim/bachutz* distinction as determinative even here. On that view: *hashochet behemah bifnim balaylah ve-he'ela bachutz patur* — if one slaughtered an animal inside the Temple at night, the *ha'ala'ah* outside generates no liability, since *shechitah* at night is *pasul* and does not withstand the test of *im alah teired* (whether an offering that ascended the altar must come back down — which Rashi understands as underlying the liability analysis throughout this section, though the Gemara does not address it explicitly). But *shachat bachutz balaylah ve-he'ela bachutz chayav* — if one slaughtered outside at night and raised it outside, one is liable, since we bracket the irregularity within the framework of the external act. Rabbi Shimon says: the general principle is that if you are liable outside — even in the *laylah* case — you are equally liable when you started the same act inside. The bird case alone is the exception.

Rava offers a parallel: *kabalah bikli chol ika beinaihu* — the reception of blood in a non-sacred vessel (*kli chol*) rather than a *kli sharet*. On the *Tanna Kamma*'s implicit model, reception in a *kli chol* inside the Temple would lead to exemption for any subsequent *ha'ala'ah* outside, but reception in a *kli chol* outside would generate liability. Rabbi Shimon again applies his principle: whatever generates liability outside — including reception in a *kli chol* — generates liability when the same act is performed inside, with the bird case alone excepted.

### Reversed Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רָבָא אָמַר: קַבָּלָה בִּכְלִי חוֹל אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: [וְכֵן] הַמְקַבֵּל בִּכְלִי חוֹל בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. הַמְקַבֵּל בִּכְלִי חוֹל בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ, חַיָּיבִין עַל כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלוֹ בַּחוּץ, חוּץ מִן הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלוֹ בַּחוּץ. הַשְׁתָּא דְּתָנֵי אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: הַמּוֹלֵק עוֹף בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלוֹ בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, מָלַק בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִיחַיַּיב; רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָתָם קָאֵי – וְתָנֵי: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides another suggestion: **Rava said** that the difference **between** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon **is** with regard to the **collection** of the blood of an offering **in a non-sacred vessel, and this** is what the mishna **is saying:** The first *tanna* said: **And so too, one who collects** the blood of an offering **in a non-sacred vessel inside** the courtyard **and** then **offers up** that offering **outside** is **exempt,** as receiving blood in a non-sacred vessel disqualifies the offering. But **one who collects** the blood of an offering **in a non-sacred vessel outside** the courtyard **and** then **offers up** that offering **outside** is **liable.** It is with this ruling that **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees and **says:** With regard to **any** act done with an animal, i.e., the collecting of its blood, concerning **which,** when it was performed **outside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up outside the courtyard, one is also **liable for** having **offered** the animal **up outside** the courtyard after performing **a similar** act **inside** the courtyard. This is the *halakha* **except with regard to one who slaughters** a bird **inside** the courtyard **and offers** it **up outside** the courtyard; he is exempt. The Gemara comments: **Now that Shmuel's father, son of Rav Yitzḥak, has taught** a *baraita*, another explanation can be provided. The *baraita* teaches: **One who pinches** the nape of a **bird inside** the courtyard **and** then **offers it up outside** is **liable,** but one who **pinched** the nape of a bird **outside and** then **offered** it **up outside** is **exempt. And Rabbi Shimon** says that he is **liable.** It is apparent then that **Rabbi Shimon** in the mishna **is referring to there,** i.e., to the first clause of the mishna, and disagreeing with it. Therefore, one should emend the mishna **and teach:** With regard to **any** act of killing an animal concerning **which,** when it was performed **inside** the courtyard, one is **liable for** subsequently offering **it** up **outside** the courtyard, one is also **liable** for having offered the animal up **outside** the courtyard after performing a similar act of killing outside the courtyard.

The Gemara then introduces an alternative tradition: *avuha di-Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak* teaches a *baraita* in which Rabbi Shimon's principle is inverted. He teaches: *hamolek et ha-of bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz chayav* — we know this. *Malak bachutz ve-he'ela bachutz patur* — we know this. But then: *ve'Rabbi Shimon mechayev* — Rabbi Shimon declares him liable in the *melikah bachutz* case. On this reading, Rabbi Shimon's principle is not "what is liable outside is liable inside" but the reverse: "what is liable inside is liable outside." Since *melikah bifnim* makes one liable, *melikah bachutz* should also make one liable. The Gemara therefore reads Rabbi Shimon's words in the *mishnah* as follows: *kol shechayav alav bifnim ve-he'ela bachutz chayav alav bachutz* — whatever you are liable for when started inside, you are also liable for when started outside. This is a direct reversal of the earlier reading, and requires rereading the language of the *mishnah* as its mirror image.

## Blood Vessels

### One Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חַטָּאת שֶׁקִּבֵּל דָּמָהּ בְּכוֹס אֶחָד; נָתַן בַּחוּץ וְחָזַר וְנָתַן בִּפְנִים, בִּפְנִים וְחָזַר וְנָתַן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ רָאוּי בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a sin offering where one collected its blood in one cup,** if he first **placed** its blood on an altar **outside** the courtyard **and then placed** the remaining blood on the altar **inside** the courtyard, or if he first placed its blood on the altar **inside** the courtyard **and then placed** the remaining blood on an altar **outside** the courtyard, in both cases he is **liable** for placing the blood outside the courtyard, **as** the blood in **its entirety is fit** to be placed on the altar **inside** the courtyard.

The *mishnah* now presents a series of cases involving the blood of a *chatas* caught in one or two vessels. When the blood is received in a single vessel, it is entirely *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* at the moment of reception. Therefore, regardless of the subsequent sequence — whether one pours some outside and then brings some inside, or brings some inside and then pours some outside — liability attaches in either case. The *mishnah*'s ruling of *chayav* in both directions, noted Rashi, follows Rabbi Nechemya's position: even the blood remaining after an initial *zerikah bifnim* retains the status of *shirayim* fit for their own rite, and pouring them outside thereafter generates liability.

### Two Vessels Inside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִבֵּל דָּמָהּ בִּשְׁתֵּי כּוֹסוֹת; נָתַן שְׁנֵיהֶם בִּפְנִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **one collected its blood in two cups** and **placed** the blood from **both of them** on the altar **inside** the courtyard he is **exempt** as he acted appropriately.

When the blood is received in two separate vessels and both are used inside the Temple, the ruling is *patur* — exempt. The first vessel effects *kapara*; the second vessel's blood, having been overtaken by the atonement already achieved, adds nothing and generates no liability.

### Two Vessels Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵיהֶם בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If he placed the blood from **both of them** on an altar **outside** the courtyard, he is **liable,** as both are fit to be placed inside.

If both vessels are used outside the Temple, one is liable for each. The reason is that each vessel's contents were independently *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* at the time of the act. Had one vessel's blood been used inside, the other vessel's blood could still have served that function. The mere fact that one vessel's blood was poured outside in a forbidden manner does not invalidate the other vessel's blood — it remains available for the proper rite. Therefore each act of *zerikah bachutz* generates its own liability.

### Mixed Inside First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד בִּפְנִים וְאֶחָד בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If he first placed the blood from **one** cup **inside and** then placed the blood from the other **one outside,** he is **exempt.**

If one vessel is used inside and one outside — in that order — one is exempt. The use of the first vessel's blood inside effects the *kapara*, rendering the second vessel's blood into a status of *dichuy* — rejected and no longer usable for any sacrificial purpose. When one then takes that second vessel's blood and pours it outside, it is no longer *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed*, and no liability attaches.

### Mixed Outside First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד בַּחוּץ וְאֶחָד בִּפְנִים – חַיָּיב עַל הַחִיצוֹן, וְהַפְּנִימִי מְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** If he first placed the blood from **one** cup **outside and** then placed the blood from the other **one inside,** he is **liable for the external** placement as that blood was fit to be placed inside, **and the internal** placement **atones** for the transgression for which the sin offering was brought.

If, however, one vessel is used outside first and the second vessel inside — the ruling is *chayav* for the first act, and the second vessel *mechaper*. When the first vessel's blood was poured outside, it was fully *ra'uy le-pesach ohel mo'ed* at that moment, and liability attaches. The question is what happens to the second vessel. One might think that using the first vessel's blood in an act of *zerikah bachutz* — a forbidden act — should invalidate the remaining blood of the same *korban*. The Gemara demonstrates that it does not: the second vessel's blood remains valid and effects atonement when used properly inside.

### Two Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמַפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת. שָׁחַט שְׁתֵּיהֶן בִּפְנִים – פָּטוּר. שְׁתֵּיהֶן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. אַחַת בִּפְנִים וְאַחַת בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. אַחַת בַּחוּץ וְאַחַת בִּפְנִים – חַיָּיב עַל הַחִיצוֹנָה, וְהַפְּנִימִית מְכַפֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to** a case where one **separated** an animal for **his sin offering and it was lost, and he separated another** animal **in its place, and thereafter, the first** animal **was found.** In that case, **both of them stand** before him and he must sacrifice one as his sin offering. If **he slaughtered both of them inside** the courtyard, he is **exempt.** If he slaughtered **both of them outside** the courtyard, he is **liable,** as each was fit to be slaughtered in the courtyard. If he first slaughtered **one inside and** then slaughtered the other **one outside** he is **exempt** from liability for slaughtering the second, as he has already fulfilled his obligation with the first, thereby rendering the second one unfit for sacrifice. If he first slaughtered **one outside and** then slaughtered the other **one inside** he is **liable for** slaughtering **the external** animal outside the courtyard, as it was fit to be slaughtered inside, **and the internal** animal **atones** for the transgression for which the sin offering was brought.

The *mishnah* provides an analogy to clarify this: one who designated a *chatas*, which was then lost, and designated a replacement. Both animals are then found standing together. If both are slaughtered inside, one is exempt — the first effects atonement, the second becomes *chatas hameisah* — a sin offering whose owners have already been atoned for, which must die. If both are slaughtered outside, one is liable for each — the first was fully *ra'uy le-korban*, and slaughtering the first outside does not render the second *chatas hameisah*; its owners could still retrieve it and bring it properly. If one is brought inside and one outside, the one brought inside effects atonement, the second becomes *chatas hameisah*, and one is exempt for the act outside. If one is slaughtered outside first and the second brought inside, one is liable for the first, and the second effects atonement — because slaughtering the first outside does not have the same invalidating effect as bringing one properly inside.

The parallel between the two-animal case and the two-vessel case is precise. The *chiddush* of the two-vessel case is that even though both vessels contain blood from the same *korban*, using one vessel's blood in an improper *zerikah bachutz* does not contaminate or invalidate the blood in the second vessel. There is no basis to say otherwise. The *mishnah* concludes with a cryptic remark — *keshem she-damah poteres basarah kach hi poteres et basar chavertah* — just as the blood of one animal releases its own flesh from the laws of *me'ilah*, so too does it release the flesh of the companion animal — which we will leave for further analysis in tomorrow's session.
