# Sotah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moshe's Death & Burial

### Perek Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: לֹא מֵת מֹשֶׁה, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַיְהִי שָׁם עִם ה׳״, מָה לְהַלָּן עוֹמֵד וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ, אַף כָּאן עוֹמֵד וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say: Moses did not** actually **die,** as **it is written here: "And** Moses, the servant of the Lord, **died there"** (Deuteronomy 34:5), **and it is written there: "And he was there with the Lord** forty days and forty nights" (Exodus 34:28). **Just as there,** where it says: "And he was there with the Lord," it means that **he was standing and serving** before God; **so too, here,** when it says: "And Moses, the servant of the Lord, died there," it means that **he was standing and serving** before God.

Daf 14 brings us to the close of the first *perek* and the opening of the second, situated within a sustained aggadic passage surrounding the death of Moshe. The thematic thread has been the chain of burial: Yosef buried his father Yaakov, Moshe buried Yosef, and God Himself buried Moshe. The preceding material contained a vivid description of Moshe's *levayah*, carried on divine wings and accompanied by the angels. We now pick up at the bottom of 13b.

### Lo Meis Moshe

Four lines from the bottom of 13b, the Gemara introduces a striking teaching: *V'yesh omrim lo meis Moshe* — and some say Moshe did not die. The famous parallel is of course *Yaakov Avinu lo meis*, but the case of Moshe is harder. By Yaakov, the verse omits any explicit statement of death — it says *vayei'asef el amav* but never says *vayamas*. By Moshe, however, the Torah states explicitly *vayamas sham*. How, then, can the Gemara maintain that he did not die?

The Gemara's resolution is a *gezeirah shavah*: *kesiv hacha vayamas sham* — it says he died there — *ukesiv hasam vayehi sham im Hashem* — and it says there he was with God. Just as when Moshe was *sham* with God at Sinai he was in attendance before God, so too here he is in attendance before God. The implicit reasoning is worth dwelling on: Moshe spent forty days and forty nights at Sinai without eating or drinking, existing in some manner beyond normal physicality. Perhaps during that time something fundamental shifted in the nature of his body, and perhaps what occurred at his death was similarly an apparent departure — his body lies there, but his spirit remains in attendance before God, connected as it was during those forty days. It is not that all souls are with God after death, but rather that Moshe had not completely departed; his connection to God mirrors what occurred at Sinai. In this reading, the death is real in some external sense but not in the ultimate spiritual sense.

This teaching arises naturally in the wake of Moshe's *levayah* and *hesped*. There is a profound difficulty in accepting the finality of Moshe's death — he is the lawgiver, the supreme *navi*, the figure through whom all Torah flows. The Torah's own language wraps his end in mystery: *velo yada ish et kevurato*. Once the Torah signals that mystery, once God is the sole witness to the burial, it becomes possible — even understandable — that a tradition would develop holding that he never truly died at all.

### Hidden Grave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְבָר שָׁלְחָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה אֵצֶל גַּסְטְרָא שֶׁל בֵּית פְּעוֹר: הַרְאֵנוּ הֵיכָן מֹשֶׁה קָבוּר. עָמְדוּ לְמַעְלָה — נִדְמָה לָהֶם לְמַטָּה, לְמַטָּה — נִדְמָה לָהֶם לְמַעְלָה. נֶחְלְקוּ לִשְׁתֵּי כִיתּוֹת, אוֹתָן שֶׁעוֹמְדִים לְמַעְלָה — נִדְמָה לָהֶן לְמַטָּה, לְמַטָּה — נִדְמָה לָהֶן לְמַעְלָה, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְלֹא יָדַע אִישׁ אֶת קְבֻרָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **the garrison [*gastera*] of Beth Peor** and said to them: **Show us where Moses is buried.** As the men **stood above** on the upper section of the mountain, **it appeared to them** as if the grave was **below** in the lower section. As they stood **below, it appeared to them** to be **above. They divided into two groups,** one above and one below. To **those who were standing above,** the grave **appeared to them** to be **below;** to those who were standing **below,** the grave **appeared to them to be above, to fulfill that which is stated: "And no man knows of his grave** to this day" (Deuteronomy 34:6).

The Torah provides three identifying markers for Moshe's burial place: in the valley, in the land of Moav, opposite Beis Peor. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Brachya observed that there is a *siman b'soch siman* — an identifier nested within an identifier. And yet, *va'afilu hachi velo yada ish et kevuraso*: even with all these markers, no one has ever been able to find it. The text proceeds to explain why this is not accidental.

The evil kingdom — Rome — sent to the leader of Beis Peor demanding: *hareinu heichan Moshe kavor* — show us where Moshe is buried. When the searchers stood at a high elevation, the site appeared to be below them. When they descended, it appeared to be above. They divided into two groups to cover both possibilities, but those above still saw it below, and those below still saw it above. A miracle prevented discovery regardless of approach.

The meaning of this is clear. If Moshe's burial place were known, it would become a site of pilgrimage and veneration, and Moshe would be at risk of being deified. The entire point of the transition into the Land of Canaan was to happen without Moshe — indeed, the Torah closes with the words *lo kam navi od beYisrael*, and specifically because he was the greatest, the people had to be capable of living their lives without him. Thus: *lekayem ma shene'emar velo yada ish et kevuraso*.

### Rabbi Chama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: אַף מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ הֵיכָן קָבוּר. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְלֹא יָדַע אִישׁ אֶת קְבֻרָתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְזֹאת הַבְּרָכָה אֲשֶׁר בֵּרַךְ מֹשֶׁה אִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה נִקְבַּר מֹשֶׁה אֵצֶל בֵּית פְּעוֹר — כְּדֵי לְכַפֵּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה פְעוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Even Moses our teacher** himself **does not know where he is buried. It is written here: "And no man knows of his grave," and it is written there: "And this is the blessing wherewith Moses the man of God blessed** the children of Israel before his death" (Deuteronomy 33:1). In other words, even Moses, as he is referred to by the term "man," does not know his burial place. **And Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: For what** reason **was Moses buried near Beth Peor? In order to atone for the incident** that transpired at Beth **Peor** (Numbers, chapter 25).

Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Chanina adds a further dimension: even Moshe himself did not know where he was buried. The textual basis is a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *ish*: *velo yada ish et kevuraso* — no *ish* knew his burial — and *vezot haberachah asher berach Moshe ish ha'Elokim* — Moshe is called *ish*. Thus the verse itself tells us that even Moshe the *ish* did not know.

Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Chanina then asks why Moshe was buried specifically near Beis Peor. The answer: *kedai lechaper al ma'aseh Peor* — to atone for the sin of Peor, the sin of Bnos Moav, which occurred in that very location.

## Imitatio Dei

### Walking After God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אַחֲרֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם תֵּלֵכוּ״, וְכִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְאָדָם לְהַלֵּךְ אַחַר שְׁכִינָה? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״כִּי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ אֵשׁ אוֹכְלָה הוּא״!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "After the Lord your God shall you walk,** and Him shall you fear, and His commandments shall you keep, and unto His voice shall you hearken, and Him shall you serve, and unto Him shall you cleave" (Deuteronomy 13:5)? **But is it** actually **possible for a person to follow the Divine Presence? But hasn't it already been stated: "For the Lord your God is a devouring fire,** a jealous God" (Deuteronomy 4:24), and one cannot approach fire.

The same Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Chanina continues with a teaching that, while not directly linked to what precedes it, is introduced at this point because it is his. He asks: what does it mean when the Torah says *acharei Hashem Elokeichem telechu* — you shall walk after Hashem your God? Is it literally possible for a person to walk after the *Shechinah*? The Torah itself declares *ki Hashem Elokecha esh ochlah hu* — God is a consuming fire. How can one aspire to closeness with or imitation of such a God?

### Six Acts Enumerated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְהַלֵּךְ אַחַר מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מָה הוּא מַלְבִּישׁ עֲרוּמִּים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים לְאָדָם וּלְאִשְׁתּוֹ כׇּתְנוֹת עוֹר וַיַּלְבִּשֵׁם״ — אַף אַתָּה הַלְבֵּשׁ עֲרוּמִּים. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בִּיקֵּר חוֹלִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּרָא אֵלָיו ה׳ בְּאֵלֹנֵי מַמְרֵא״ — אַף אַתָּה בַּקֵּר חוֹלִים. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נִיחֵם אֲבֵלִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי אַחֲרֵי מוֹת אַבְרָהָם וַיְבָרֶךְ אֱלֹהִים אֶת יִצְחָק בְּנוֹ״ — אַף אַתָּה נַחֵם אֲבֵלִים. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא קָבַר מֵתִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְבֹּר אוֹתוֹ בַּגַּי״ — אַף אַתָּה קְבוֹר מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: **Rather,** the meaning is **that one should follow the attributes of the Holy One, Blessed be He.** He provides several examples. **Just as He clothes the naked, as it is written: "And the Lord God made for Adam and for his wife garments of skin, and clothed them"** (Genesis 3:21), **so too,** should **you clothe the naked.** Just as **the Holy One, Blessed be He, visits the sick, as it is written** with regard to God's appearing to Abraham following his circumcision: **"And the Lord appeared unto him by the terebinths of Mamre"** (Genesis 18:1), **so too,** should **you visit the sick.** Just as **the Holy One, Blessed be He, consoles mourners, as it is written: "And it came to pass after the death of Abraham, that God blessed Isaac his son"** (Genesis 25:11), **so too,** should **you console mourners.** Just as **the Holy One, Blessed be He, buried the dead, as it is written: "And he was buried in the valley** in the land of Moab" (Deuteronomy 34:6), **so too,** should **you bury the dead.**

The resolution: *leilech achar midotav* — to follow after God's *attributes*, the concrete ways in which God acts in the world. The Gemara enumerates these:

God clothed the naked, as it is written that He made for Adam and his wife garments of leather and dressed them — so too you should clothe the naked. God visited the sick, as it is written that God appeared to Avraham at the plains of Mamre after his *brit milah* — so too you should visit the sick. God consoled mourners, as it is written that after Avraham's death God blessed Yitzchak his son — and that blessing is understood as a visit of consolation — so too you should console mourners. God buried the dead, as it is written *vayikbor oto bagai* — and this ties directly back to the preceding material — so too you should bury the dead.

### Character vs. Actions

This *drasha* is worth holding up against another classical formulation of the same obligation: *mah hu rachum af ata rachum, mah hu chanun af ata chanun* — as God is merciful so you should be merciful, as God is gracious so you should be gracious. The two formulations represent fundamentally different approaches to ethics. One focuses on character traits — *rachum*, *chanun* — asking the person to cultivate an inner disposition that mirrors God's. The other, as in the Gemara before us, focuses entirely on concrete actions: it does not matter whether you are naturally inclined toward compassion; what matters is what you actually do in the world.

This maps onto a broader debate in ethical thought. One tradition, exemplified by the Rambam in his *Peirush Hamishnayos* on *Pirkei Avot*, holds that the perfection of character is the ultimate goal, and that actions serve primarily as a means to that end. This is the consistent thread of the *Shemonah Perakim*: every ethical deed is described as a means toward perfecting one's character rather than as an end good in itself. A famous illustration is the Rambam's reading of the dictum *hakol lefi rov hama'aseh velo lefi godel hama'aseh* — it is all according to the quantity of the deed, not the size. Rambam explains that it is better to give one dollar of *tzedakah* a hundred times than a hundred dollars at once, because the repeated act cultivates the character of generosity more effectively. The goal is the internal transformation.

By contrast, the formulation in our Gemara emphasizes what you actually do. In a Kantian register, one might even argue that doing a good deed against one's inclination, out of sheer sense of obligation, reflects a purer ethical commitment — what matters is the good accomplished, not the personality of the one accomplishing it. The two approaches are genuinely in tension: is *imitatio Dei* a program for self-cultivation, or a program for making the world better through concrete action?

One might note an additional dimension here: the concretization itself is significant. The general commandment of *ve'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* encompasses all of these obligations in principle, but left at that level of abstraction, no one would reliably fulfill them. Because *Chazal* speak with great concreteness about *nichum aveilim*, *bikkur cholim*, *hachnasat orchim*, and *halvayat hames*, these obligations become identifiable, actionable, and deeply embedded in communal practice. There is a real power in the concrete over the abstract, and in that sense the emphasis on actions rather than character traits also has a practical pedagogical valence.

The connection to the surrounding material is suggestive as well. The passage follows the account of Moshe's burial — an event at which the impulse to worship and deify Moshe had to be actively prevented. The response is not to explain Moshe philosophically but to say: figure out how to act like him. You cannot be God, you cannot be Moshe, but you can do what God and Moshe did.

### Torah Bookended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: תּוֹרָה תְּחִלָּתָהּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, וְסוֹפָהּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים. תְּחִילָּתָהּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים לְאָדָם וּלְאִשְׁתּוֹ כׇּתְנוֹת עוֹר וַיַּלְבִּשֵׁם״, וְסוֹפָהּ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְבֹּר אוֹתוֹ בַּגַּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Samlai taught:** With regard to the **Torah, its beginning is an act of kindness and its end is an act of kindness. Its beginning is an act of kindness, as it is written: "And the Lord God made for Adam and for his wife garments of skin, and clothed them"** (Genesis 3:21). **And its end is an act of kindness, as it is written: "And he was buried in the valley** in the land of Moab" (Deuteronomy 34:6).

Rabbi Simlai continues the theme. His *drasha*: *Torah techilasah gemilus chasadim vesofah gemilus chasadim* — the Torah begins with acts of loving-kindness and ends with acts of loving-kindness. At the beginning: *vaya'as Hashem Elokim le'adam ule'ishto katnos or vayalbishem* — God clothed Adam and his wife. At the end: *vayikbor oto bagai* — God buried Moshe. Rabbi Simlai is not listing these as two examples among many; he is pointing to the structural architecture of the Torah itself. Whatever else the Torah contains — all its commandments, all its narratives — it opens and closes with *gemilus chasadim*. This should tell us something essential about what the Torah is fundamentally about.

## Moshe & Eretz Yisrael

### Mitzvot in Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה נִתְאַוָּה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לִיכָּנֵס לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל? וְכִי לֶאֱכוֹל מִפִּרְיָהּ הוּא צָרִיךְ?! אוֹ לִשְׂבּוֹעַ מִטּוּבָהּ הוּא צָרִיךְ?! אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמַר מֹשֶׁה: הַרְבֵּה מִצְוֹת נִצְטַוּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֵין מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֶכָּנֵס אֲנִי לָאָרֶץ כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְקַיְּימוּ כּוּלָּן עַל יָדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Samlai taught: For what** reason **did Moses our teacher** greatly **desire to enter Eretz Yisrael? Did he need to eat of its produce, or did he need to satisfy himself from its goodness? Rather, this** is what **Moses said: Many mitzvot were commanded** to the **Jewish people, and** some of **them can be fulfilled only in Eretz Yisrael,** so **I will enter the land in order that they can all be fulfilled by me.**

Rabbi Simlai continues, now turning to a different question: *mipnei mah nisaveh Moshe Rabbeinu lehikanes le'Eretz Yisrael* — why did Moshe so desperately desire to enter the Land of Israel? Was it to eat its fruit? To enjoy its goodness? The Gemara answers in Moshe's own words: *harbeh mitzvos nitzvu ve'ein mitkaymim ella be'Eretz Yisrael* — many *mitzvot* were commanded and can only be fulfilled in the Land of Israel. *Ekanes ani la'aretz kedei sheyitkaymu kulan alai* — let me enter so that all of them may be fulfilled through me.

### God's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: כְּלוּם אַתָּה מְבַקֵּשׁ אֶלָּא לְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר? מַעֲלֶה אֲנִי עָלֶיךָ כְּאִילּוּ עֲשִׂיתָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָכֵן אֲחַלֶּק לוֹ בָרַבִּים וְאֶת עֲצוּמִים יְחַלֵּק שָׁלָל תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱרָה לַמָּוֶת נַפְשׁוֹ וְאֶת פּוֹשְׁעִים נִמְנָה וְהוּא חֵטְא רַבִּים נָשָׂא וְלַפֹּשְׁעִים יַפְגִּיעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Do you seek** to enter the land to perform these mitzvot **for any** reason **other than to receive a reward? I will ascribe you** credit **as if you had performed them** and you will receive your reward, **as it is stated: "Therefore will I divide him a portion among the great, and he shall divide the spoil with the mighty; because he bared his soul unto death, and was numbered with the transgressors; yet he bore the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors"** (Isaiah 53:12).

God's response is striking: *kelum atah mevakesh ella lekabel sachar?* — is it nothing but reward that you seek? And then, immediately: *ma'aleh ani alecha ke'ilu asitam* — I will credit you as if you had done them. The Gemara then expounds a passage from Isaiah 53 to support this.

### Isaiah 53 Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָכֵן אֲחַלֶּק לוֹ בָּרַבִּים״. יָכוֹל כָּאַחֲרוֹנִים וְלֹא כָּרִאשׁוֹנִים, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת עֲצוּמִים יְחַלֵּק שָׁלָל״, כְּאַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, שֶׁהֵן עֲצוּמִים בַּתּוֹרָה וּבְמִצְוֹת. ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱרָה לַמָּוֶת נַפְשׁוֹ״, שֶׁמָּסַר עַצְמוֹ לְמִיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם אַיִן מְחֵנִי נָא וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Samlai proceeds to expound the verse **"Therefore will I divide him a portion among the great"** to mean that he will receive reward. One **might** have thought that he will receive reward **like the later ones and not like the earlier ones,** so **the verse states: "And he shall divide the spoil with the mighty,"** meaning **like Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who were mighty in Torah and in mitzvot. "Because he bared his soul unto death,"** meaning **he gave himself over to death** on behalf of the Jewish people, **as it is stated:** "Yet now, if You will forgive their sin; **and if not, blot me, I pray** You, out of Your book that You have written" (Exodus 32:32).

The verse *lachen achalek lo varabbim ve'et atzumim yachalek shalal* — therefore I will divide his portion among the many and with the mighty he will divide the spoil — is parsed in detail. *Lachen achalek lo varabbim*: one might think this means Moshe's reward will be like that of later great figures, but not like the earliest great ones. *Talmud lomar ve'et atzumim yachalek shalal*: no, with the mighty — meaning Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, who were mighty in Torah and *mitzvot*.

*Tachat asher he'erah lamavet nafsho*: because he exposed himself to death — *she'masar atzmo lemitah* — referencing his declaration *ve'im ayin mecheni na misifrecha*, his readiness to be destroyed along with Israel rather than see them destroyed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת פּוֹשְׁעִים נִמְנָה״ — שֶׁנִּמְנָה עִם מֵתֵי מִדְבָּר. ״וְהוּא חֵטְא רַבִּים נָשָׂא״ — שֶׁכִּיפֵּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל. ״וְלַפֹּשְׁעִים יַפְגִּיעַ״ — שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל פּוֹשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיַּחְזְרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה. וְאֵין פְּגִיעָה אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And was numbered with the transgressors,"** meaning **that he was counted among those who died in the desert,** for, just like them, he did not enter Eretz Yisrael. **"Yet he bore the sin of many," as he atoned for the incident of the Golden Calf. "And made intercession [*yafgia*] for the transgressors," as he requested mercy for the sinners of Israel** so **that they should engage in repentance. And** the word ***pegia*** means **nothing other** than **prayer, as it is stated: "Therefore pray not you for this people, neither lift up cry nor prayer for them, neither make intercession [*tifga*] to Me;** for I will not hear you" (Jeremiah 7:16).

*Ve'et poshim nimnah*: he was counted among sinners — *she'nimnah im metei midbar* — he was reckoned among those who died in the wilderness, like a captain who does not abandon his ship.

*Vehu chet rabbim nasa*: he carried the sin of the many — *she'kiper al ma'aseh ha'egel* — he atoned for the sin of the golden calf.

*Velafoshim yapgia*: for sinners he would entreat — *she'bikesh rachamim al poshei Yisrael she'yachzeru biteshuvah*, he prayed for God's mercy that sinners should repent. The word *pegiah* means entreaty, as in the verse *ve'al tifga bi* — do not entreat me — directed at Moshe when God wished him to stop praying.

### Reward & Mitzvot

The exchange raises an immediate question. The Mishnah in *Pirkei Avot* states *al tihyu ka'avadim hameshameshim et harav al menat lekabel peras* — do not be like servants who serve the master in order to receive a reward. How, then, does God's response — *kelum atah mevakesh ella lekabel sachar?* — make sense as a critique of Moshe? Who said Moshe was only in it for the *sachar*?

A famous story about the Vilna Gaon is relevant here. The Gaon once saw someone on *Sukkot* with an exceptionally beautiful *etrog* and asked to use it. The owner agreed on the condition that he receive all of the Gaon's *sachar* for the *mitzvah*. The Gaon agreed, took the *etrog*, and performed the *mitzvah* — and his students had never seen him so joyful. When they asked why, he said: every other time he could not be certain that some part of his motivation was the *sachar*; this time he knew with certainty he was doing it purely *leshem hamitzvah*, because there was no *sachar* to be had.

The deeper question is what this passage suggests about the value of *Eretz Yisrael*. The Gemara reduces Moshe's desire to enter the Land entirely to the issue of fulfilling *mitzvot hateluyot ba'aretz* — the commandments dependent on the land. But one might have expected something grander: the actualization of Israel as a national entity under God, defined by Torah, building a civilization with a *beit hamikdash*, realizing the whole vision of what it means to be the people of God in their land. Is the entire point really just to accumulate more *mitzvot*?

This connects to Rabbi Simlai's teaching at the end of Masechet Makkos — the same Rabbi Simlai who authored the teaching here. There it says *darash Rav Simlai: sheish me'os ushlosh esrei mitzvos ne'emru lo leMoshe mi'Sinai*, and the preceding *mishnah* states *ratza HaKadosh Baruch Hu lezakot et Yisrael lefichach hirbah lahem Torah umitzvot*. The abundance of *mitzvot* is itself the mechanism of *zekhut*; the more you do, the greater the *sachar*. This framework — the quantity of *mitzvot* as a measure of spiritual standing — pervades both contexts, and it is possible that Rabbi Simlai is deploying it consistently as part of a larger theological program emphasizing the *ribbu'i mitzvot* as the core of Jewish identity and divine favor.

Some scholars have suggested that the statement *hirbah lahem Torah umitzvot lezakot et Yisrael* is a polemical response to Christian theology, which characterized the law as reflecting bondage rather than liberation — *the covenant from Mount Sinai bears children who are slaves*. The counter-claim is precisely that the abundance of *mitzvot* is not bondage but the vehicle of merit and spiritual elevation.

One might also read God's response to Moshe — that he will receive credit as though he had performed the *mitzvot* without having entered the land — as a post-*churban* consolation of sorts. Jewish identity and the *kiyum mitzvot* do not depend on actually being in *Eretz Yisrael*. Moshe himself, who could not enter, receives the full credit. This reading understands the passage as addressing the exilic condition: one need not be in the Land to realize one's full spiritual potential through *mitzvot*.

## Sotah's Minchah

### Egyptian Basket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מֵבִיא אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ בְּתוֹךְ כְּפִיפָה מִצְרִית וְנוֹתְנָהּ עַל יָדֶיהָ כְּדֵי לְיַגְּעָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The husband of the *sota* **would bring** his wife's **meal-offering** to the priest **in an Egyptian wicker basket** made of palm branches, **and he would place** the meal-offering **in her hands** for her to hold throughout the ritual **in order to fatigue her.** This might lead her to confess her guilt and not drink the water of a *sota* unnecessarily.

We now turn to the second *perek*. The *mishnah* states: *haya mevi et minchatah besoch kefifah mitzrit* — the husband would bring her *minchah* inside an Egyptian basket. Note that it is the husband who is the actor here; looking at the *pesukim*, it is the husband who brings the woman to the *beit hamikdash* and brings her *minchah*. *Venosan al yadeha* — and he places it on her hands — *kedei leyaga'ah* — in order to weary her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת תְּחִילָּתָן וְסוֹפָן בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, וְזוֹ תְּחִלָּתָהּ בִּכְפִיפָה מִצְרִית, וְסוֹפָהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists differences between this meal-offering and other meal-offerings. Generally, **all meal-offerings,** from **their beginnings,** i.e., the moment they are consecrated, **and** until **their ends,** i.e., the moment they are sacrificed, must be **in a service vessel. But** in the case of **this one, its beginning is in a wicker basket and** only at **its end,** immediately before it is offered, is it placed **in a service vessel.**

The *mishnah* then notes the contrast with all other *menachot*: *kol menachot techilatan vesofan bichli sharet* — all *menachot* begin and end with a *kli sharet*, a sanctified vessel. But this *minchah* begins not in a *kli sharet* but in this lowly, mundane vessel — *vezo techilasah bekefifah mitzrit* — and only at the end, *vesofah bichli sharet*, when it must actually be offered, is it placed in a *kli sharet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת טְעוּנוֹת שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה, וְזוֹ אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֹא שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה. כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת בָּאוֹת מִן הַחִטִּין, וְזוֹ בָּאָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין. מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבָּאָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין — הִיא הָיְתָה בָּאָה גֶּרֶשׂ, וְזוֹ בָּאָה קֶמַח. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׂיהָ מַעֲשֵׂה בְּהֵמָה, כָּךְ קׇרְבָּנָהּ מַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **All** other **meal-offerings require oil and frankincense, and this one requires neither oil nor frankincense.** Furthermore, **all** other **meal-offerings are brought from wheat, and this one is brought from barley. Although** in fact **the *omer* meal-offering** is also **brought from barley,** it is still different in that **it was brought as groats,** i.e., high-quality meal. The meal-offering of the *sota*, however, **is brought as** unsifted barley **flour. Rabban Gamliel says:** This hints that **just as her actions** of seclusion with another man **were the actions of an animal, so too her offering is animal food,** i.e., barley and not wheat.

Similarly, all *menachot* require oil and frankincense, while this *minchah* requires neither. (The Gemara will note that the *minchat chotei*, the sinner's meal-offering, also lacks oil and frankincense, and will address this.) All *menachot* come from wheat; this one comes from barley. There is one other *minchah* that uses barley — the *minchat ha'omer* — but even that barley is ground into *geresh*, a very fine and refined barley flour comparable in quality to the *soles* of wheat offerings. The *sotah's* offering, by contrast, comes as a coarse bran — *kemach* — not refined at all.

Rabban Gamliel supplies the interpretive key: *kesheim shema'aseha ma'aseh behemah kach korbanah ma'achal behemah* — just as her actions were the actions of an animal, so her sacrifice is the food of an animal. This is a striking formulation. The *mishnah* throughout has been conveying degradation through ritual means — the lowly vessel, the coarse grain, the absence of oil and frankincense — but it is Rabban Gamliel who connects the dots explicitly: the deliberate lowliness of her offering mirrors a moral judgment about her conduct.

This also reinforces the posture of presumed guilt that pervades the *sotah* procedure. The repeated emphasis on *kedei leyaga'ah* — wearying her — operates on the assumption that she sinned and should be induced to confess. And the *middah keneged middah* logic that ran through the earlier material in the *masechet* continues here: each degrading element of the procedure corresponds to her presumed transgression.

### Tosafot Reordering

Tosafot raises a pointed question on the *mishnah's* organization. *Ein seder hamishnah keseder haparshah mechuvonim* — the order in the *mishnah* is not aligned with the order of the *parshah* in the Torah. Tosafot then reconstructs the Torah's sequence:

First comes *kinuy* and *setirah*. Then the husband brings her and brings her *minchah*. Then the *kohen* prepares the water — the earthenware vessel, the half-*log* of water from the *kiyor*, the dust from the floor. Then the woman is brought to the Sha'ar Nikanor and her garments are disheveled. Then the *minchah* is placed on her hands while the *kohen* holds the bitter waters. Then the oath is administered. Then the *megillah* is written, the name erased into the water, and she drinks. Then the *minchah* is transferred from the Egyptian basket to a *kli sharet*, the *kometz* is taken, and it is offered on the *mizbe'ach*.

But in our *mishnah*, the disheveling of the woman's garments and the rending of her clothing — discussed in the previous *perek* — came before the *minchah* is introduced. So the *mishnah* has reordered the material. Tosafot raises this without fully resolving it: either there is some literary or pedagogical reason for the *tanna*'s reorganization, or we should assume that the *halakhic* sequence in practice follows the Torah's order, and the *mishnah* has organized things differently for its own expository purposes. What seems untenable is that the *tanna* would silently invert the Torah's sequence and expect us to understand that the Torah's own order should be disregarded — without explicitly signaling that, as the Gemara does in other contexts, such as the *Kohen Gadol's avodah*.

### Wearying Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, אַבָּא חָנִין אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: [וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה —] כְּדֵי לְיַגְּעָהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁתַּחֲזוֹר בָּהּ. אִם כָּכָה חָסָה תּוֹרָה עַל עוֹבְרֵי רְצוֹנוֹ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר עַל עוֹשֵׂי רְצוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Abba Ḥanin says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: And why** is **so much** done to her? It is **in order to fatigue her, so that she will retract** and confess her guilt and be spared death. And **if the Torah is so protective of those who transgress His will,** i.e., the *sota*, who secluded herself with the man she was warned against, then by ***a fortiori*** inference He is protective **of those who do His will.**

A *baraita* is cited: *tanya Abba Chanin omer mishum Rabbi Eliezer* — why does the procedure use the Egyptian basket and place the *minchah* on her hands? *Kedei leyaga'ah* — to weary her — *kedei she'tachzor bah* — so that she should repent and confess her sin. And from this Abba Chanin draws a *kal va'chomer*: if God shows such concern for those who transgress His will — wanting her to confess and be spared rather than suffer the punishment of the *mei hamarim* — how much more so does God care for those who do His will.

### Water & Minchah Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי מִשּׁוּם דְּחָסָה הוּא? דִּילְמָא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֹא תִּימָּחֵק מְגִילָּה! קָסָבַר
>
> **Translation:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁקָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ. דְּאִי מִשּׁוּם מְגִילָּה הָא אִימְּחִיקָא לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** that the priest would first **give** her the water of the *sota* **to drink, and** only **afterward** would he **sacrifice her meal-offering.** Therefore, **if** the concern were **due to the scroll,** it would no longer be applicable, as **it was** already **erased** in the water of the *sota* before the meal-offering was brought. The efforts to fatigue her by making her hold the meal-offering must indicate that the Torah is protective of her.

The Gemara immediately challenges this interpretation. *Umimai mishum de'chasah hu?* — how do you know the reason for wearying her is concern for her welfare? *Dilma ki heichi de'lo timachak megillah?* — perhaps the intent is to prevent the unnecessary erasure of God's name? This is a very different concern. Other *mishnayot* make clear that there is a strong prohibition against erasing God's name unnecessarily; perhaps the entire effort to induce confession is motivated by that concern, not by concern for the woman herself.

The Gemara resolves this: the *tanna* who attributes the motivation to concern for the woman must hold the position that *mashke ve'achar kach makriv et minchatah* — she drinks the water first, and only afterward is the *minchah* offered. If that is so, then by the time we are still urging her to confess and spare herself, God's name has already been erased — the *megillah* has already been scraped into the water she has drunk. There is no longer any protective purpose regarding God's name. The only remaining reason to continue urging her to confess would be concern for her life and well-being.

Rashi explains the mechanics: she drinks, and then the *minchah* is brought — and critically, the physical effects of the water are suspended until the *minchah* is offered. The *minchah*, in this view, is the final trigger. She has drunk the water, but nothing will happen until the *kometz* is placed on the fire. So even after drinking, she can confess, the *minchah* will not be offered, and she will be spared.

This sounds counterintuitive — we naturally imagine the drinking as the climactic act — but as Tosafot's careful reconstruction of the *pesukim* confirms, this is precisely what the Torah's own sequence suggests: *ve'hishkah... venatan al yadeha* — she drinks, and then the *minchah* is waved and offered. The Gemara is drawing out an opinion that takes that sequence seriously, however strange it appears to us.

## Menachot Comparison

### Standard Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: סֵדֶר מְנָחוֹת, כֵּיצַד? אָדָם מֵבִיא מִנְחָה מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ בִּקְלָתוֹת שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְשֶׁל זָהָב, וְנוֹתְנָהּ לְתוֹךְ כְּלִי שָׁרֵת, וּמְקַדְּשָׁהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, וְנוֹתֵן עָלֶיהָ שַׁמְנָהּ וּלְבוֹנָתָהּ, וּמוֹלִיכָהּ אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן. וְכֹהֵן מוֹלִיכָהּ אֵצֶל מִזְבֵּחַ, וּמַגִּישָׁהּ בְּקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מַעֲרָבִית כְּנֶגֶד חוּדָּהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן, וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **All meal-offerings,** from their beginning until their end, are placed in service vessels and remain there. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the *Tosefta* (*Menaḥot* 1:16): **What is the procedure for meal-offerings? A person brings his meal-offering from his property in baskets [*kelatot*] of silver and of gold, and** when he reaches the Temple **he places it in a service vessel and sanctifies it in** the **service vessel, and he puts its oil and frankincense on it, and he carries it to the priest. And the priest** then **carries it to the altar and brings it near to the southwest horn** of the altar, **opposite the corner of the horn** of the altar. **And** this **is sufficient.**

The *mishnah* stated that all other *menachot* begin in a *kli sharet* while the *sotah's* begins in an Egyptian basket. The Gemara challenges this from a *mishnah* in Menachot describing the standard procedure for bringing a *minchah*: a person brings it from home *bekaltot shel kesef veshel zahav* — in vessels of gold and silver — and only upon arriving at the Temple does he transfer it into a *kli sharet*. This suggests that even ordinary *menachot* do not begin in a *kli sharet* but rather in mundane home vessels. So what is distinctive about the *sotah's* basket?

### Kli Sharet Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא בִּקְלָתוֹת שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וּבִקְלָתוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֵימָא בְּכֵלִים הָרְאוּיִים לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that the meal-offering is first placed **in baskets of silver and baskets of gold** brought from one's home. This seems to contradict the mishna's statement that all other meal-offerings are initially in service vessels. **Rav Pappa said:** The mishna means to **say** that meal-offerings are placed **in vessels** of silver and gold, as **these are suitable to be service vessels** if consecrated.

Rav Papa resolves the apparent contradiction: the *mishnah's* claim should be read as saying that all other *menachot* begin in vessels that are *fit* to serve as a *kli sharet* — meaning gold and silver vessels — even if they are not formally sanctified as such. The *kefifah mitzrit*, the Egyptian wicker basket, is not merely unsanctified but is categorically unfit to serve as a *kli sharet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלַל דִּכְפִיפָה מִצְרִית לָא חַזְיָא, כְּמַאן — דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן שֶׁל עֵץ, רַבִּי פּוֹסֵל וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: Since the mishna distinguishes in this regard between the meal-offering of the *sota* and all other meal-offerings, one may learn **by inference that an Egyptian wicker basket is not suitable** to be a service vessel even if it is consecrated. **In accordance with whose** opinion is this the case? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **service vessels that were made of wood, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems them unfit, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems them fit.**

This implies that a wicker vessel is disqualified from being a *kli sharet* altogether. The Gemara asks: like whom is this position? It aligns with Rebbi (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) against Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, as a *baraita* teaches: if *klei sharet* were made of wood, Rebbi disqualifies them while Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda validates them. Since wicker is treated as a form of wood, the *mishnah's* assumption that the *kefifah mitzrit* is disqualified from *kli sharet* status follows Rebbi's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֵימַר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּחֲשׁוּבִין, בִּפְחוּתִין מִי אָמַר? לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״הַקְרִיבֵהוּ נָא לְפֶחָתֶךָ״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **You** can **even say that** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda. Say that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says** that wooden vessels are deemed fit **with regard to** those of **superior** quality; but **does he say** likewise **with regard to** vessels of **lesser** quality, e.g., a basket made of palm branches? **Doesn't Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, hold** to the principle articulated in the verse: "And when you offer the blind for a sacrifice, is it not evil! …If you would **present it now unto your governor,** will he be pleased with you or show you favor?" (Malachi 1:8)? Nothing that is unfit for presentation to a ruler may be brought to the Temple. Therefore, even Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, must agree that a basket made of palm branches cannot be a service vessel.

The Gemara then attempts to reconcile the *mishnah* even with the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda: even he would only validate wooden *klei sharet* that are *chashuvim* — finely crafted and respectable. He would not validate lowly, crude wicker vessels. And why not? Because of the principle *hakrivehu na le'pechatecha* — would you offer this to a governor? — meaning that what is not suitable to bring before a human authority is certainly unfit as a vessel for divine service. This principle is not merely an ethical consideration; it has operative *halakhic* force, actually disqualifying an otherwise potentially valid vessel.

### Respect Principle

The principle *hakrivehu na le'pechatecha* operates as a genuine standard. What is fit to be presented to a person of authority is fit for the *mizbe'ach*; what would be embarrassing to offer a governor is certainly unfit for God's altar. This raises a question of application: who determines whether a given vessel meets this standard? If a gold vessel were mangled or degraded, would it be disqualified despite being made of precious metal? The principle seems to address both the material and the manner of craftsmanship together.

There is also a notable tension here with the principle of *ein aniyut bemakom ashirut* — there is no poverty in a place of wealth — which governs the *beit hamikdash* and precludes the use of cheap or inadequate materials in divine service. The *sotah's* basket is thus doubly notable: not only is it not a *kli sharet*, it is the kind of vessel that would be disqualified from ever becoming one.

### Sanctification Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנָהּ לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת, וּמְקַדְּשָׁהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ — כְּלִי שָׁרֵת אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא מִדַּעַת? אֵימָא: נוֹתְנָהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת לְקַדְּשָׁהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* states: **He places it in a service vessel and sanctifies it in** the **service vessel.** The Gemara asks: Can one **learn from** the unnecessary repetition of the term service vessel, that **service vessels can sanctify** their contents **only with intention?** Must one place the meal-offering in the service vessel with express intent to sanctify it? The Gemara answers: **Say: He** simply **places it in the service vessel** in order **to sanctify it in the service vessel.** He need not intend to sanctify it.

The *mishnah* describing the standard *minchah* procedure states that once placed in a *kli sharet*, you sanctify it — *umekadsho bichli sharet*. The Gemara notes that this language implies an act of sanctification distinct from the mere placing into the vessel. Does the *kli sharet* automatically sanctify whatever is placed in it, or must there be a specific intention to sanctify?

The Gemara suggests that the language *notna bichli sharet lekadsho bichli sharet* — placing it in the *kli sharet* in order to sanctify it — could reflect intention, but equally could simply mean that the result of placing is sanctification. The question remains open as to whether intentional sanctification is a prerequisite. It is noted that the owner's earlier verbal declaration — *harei zu minchas* — constitutes *kedushat peh*, verbal sanctity, which does not yet confer the full *kedushat ha-guf*, intrinsic sanctity, that determines whether the object can be redeemed, whether removal from the Temple precincts is consequential, and so forth. That full *kedushat ha-guf* comes only through placement in the *kli sharet*.

## Altar Placement

### Two Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַגִּישָׁהּ בְּקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מַעֲרָבִית כְּנֶגֶד חוּדָּהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן וְדַיּוֹ. מְנָלַן —
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: The priest **brings it near to the southwest horn** of the altar, **opposite the corner of the horn. And** this **is sufficient.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה הַקְרֵב אוֹתָהּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, וְתַנְיָא: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״, יָכוֹל בַּמַּעֲרָב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. אִי אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, יָכוֹל בַּדָּרוֹם — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״. הָא כֵּיצַד — מַגִּישָׁהּ בְּקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מַעֲרָבִית כְּנֶגֶד חוּדָּהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן, וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **As it is written: "And this is the law of the meal-offering: The sons of Aaron shall offer it before the Lord in front of the altar"** (Leviticus 6:7). **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"Before the Lord,"** one **might** have understood this to mean **on the western side** of the altar, opposite the Holy of Holies. Therefore, **the verse states: "In front of the altar."** This must be the south of the altar, where the ramp is located. **If** the verse had stated only: **In front of the altar,** one **might** have understood it to mean specifically **on the southern side.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Before the Lord,"** indicating the western side. **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? The priest **brings it near to the southwest corner** of the altar, **opposite the corner of the horn. And** this **is sufficient.**

The standard *minchah* procedure includes the act of *hagashah*: *magishah bekeren dromit ma'aravit keneged chudah shel keren vedayo* — you touch it at the southwest corner of the altar, at the very tip of the corner, and that suffices.

To understand why the southwest corner, the Gemara draws on two verses. One verse says *lifnei Hashem* — before God — which directs us toward the west side of the *mizbe'ach*, the side facing the *Heichal*. The other verse says *el pnei hamizbeach* — at the face of the altar — which directs us toward the south, where the ramp ascends and the altar faces when you approach it. Neither verse alone is sufficient:

A *baraita* articulates the tension precisely: *lifnei Hashem yachol bema'arav* — perhaps in the west? *Talmud lomar el pnei hamizbeach* — the face of the altar, meaning the south. *I el pnei hamizbeach yachol badarom* — perhaps in the south? *Talmud lomar lifnei Hashem* — before God, meaning the west. *Ha keitzad?* — how then? *Magishah bekeren dromit ma'aravit* — you place it at the southwest corner, satisfying both directions simultaneously.

To visualize this: the *Heichal* stood on the west end of the *Azarah*, opening eastward. The *mizbe'ach* stood in the courtyard east of the *Heichal*. The *ramp* of the *mizbe'ach* ascended from the south, making the south face the *pnei hamizbeach*. The *Heichal* was to the west, making the west side of the *mizbe'ach* the side facing *lifnei Hashem*. The southwest corner is the precise geometric point that satisfies both demands simultaneously.

### Hermeneutic Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַגִּישֶׁנָּה בְּמַעֲרָבָהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן אוֹ לִדְרוֹמָהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן — אָמַרְתָּ: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁנֵי מִקְרָאוֹת, אֶחָד מְקַיֵּים עַצְמוֹ וּמְקַיֵּים דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאֶחָד מְקַיֵּים עַצְמוֹ וּמְבַטֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוֹ — מַנִּיחִין אֶת שֶׁמְּקַיֵּים עַצְמוֹ וּמְבַטֵּל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְתוֹפְסִין אֶת שֶׁמְּקַיֵּים עַצְמוֹ וּמְקַיֵּים חֲבֵירוֹ. כְּשֶׁאַתָּה אוֹמֵר ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״ בַּמַּעֲרָב — בִּטַּלְתָּה ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ בַּדָּרוֹם, וּכְשֶׁאַתָּה אוֹמֵר ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ בַּדָּרוֹם, קִיַּימְתָּה ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״ בַּמַּעֲרָב. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַגִּישָׁהּ לִדְרוֹמָהּ שֶׁל קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** another interpretation: One **might** have thought that **he offers it up on the western side of the corner or on the southern side of the corner. Say: Anywhere you find two verses, one** of which **fulfills itself and fulfills the statement of the other, and one** of which **fulfills itself and nullifies the statement of the other, leave** the verse **that fulfills itself and nullifies the other, and seize the one that fulfills itself and fulfills the other.** The principle is applied as follows: **When you say: "Before the Lord," on the western side, you have nullified** the other part of the verse: **"In front of the altar," on the southern side. But when you say: "In front of the altar," on the southern side, you have** also **fulfilled: "Before the Lord," on the western side. How so? He brings it near to the southern side of the corner.**

Rabbi Eliezer offers a different approach, though one that reaches the same conclusion through a broader hermeneutic principle. Rather than looking for a compromise position that satisfies both verses simultaneously, he articulates a general rule: *kol makom she'atah motzeh shenei mikra'ot* — wherever you find two verses — *echad mekayem atzmo umekayem divrei chaveiro ve'echad mekayem atzmo umevatel divrei chaveiro* — one of which can satisfy itself and also satisfy the other, while the other can only satisfy itself at the expense of the first — *menichim et zeh shemekayem atzmo umevatel et chaveiro vetofsim et shemekayem atzmo umekayem chaveiro* — you set aside the exclusive one and follow the inclusive one.

In this case: if you follow *lifnei Hashem* in the west literally, you are on the west side of the *mizbe'ach* and have no element at all of *pnei hamizbeach* in the south — the two are mutually exclusive. But if you follow *el pnei hamizbeach* — the south face — and specifically at the southern end, the question is whether that position can still be described as *lifnei Hashem*, before God. According to Rabbi Eliezer's view, it can, because even from the south face of the *mizbe'ach* one stands facing the *Heichal*. Thus the south position satisfies itself and also preserves something of *lifnei Hashem*, while the west position satisfies only itself and sacrifices the south entirely.

### Vedayo & Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְדַיּוֹ״? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: תִּיבְּעֵי הַגָּשַׁת מִנְחָה גּוּפַהּ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is taught by the phrase: **And** this **is sufficient? Rav Ashi said:** This phrase **was necessary,** as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say: Require** the priest **to bring the meal-offering itself near** to the corner of the altar without the use of a vessel. The *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not so, and one can bring it to the altar in its service vessel.

The word *vedayo* — and that is sufficient — requires explanation. One might think that beyond touching the vessel at the corner, the *minchah* itself must physically touch the *mizbe'ach*. *Vedayo* tells us: no, touching the vessel is sufficient; the *minchah* within the vessel need not itself contact the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִקְרִיבָהּ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן וְגוֹ׳ וְהִגִּישָׁהּ אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ — מָה הַקְרָבָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן בִּכְלִי, אַף הַגָּשָׁה אֵצֶל מִזְבֵּחַ בִּכְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why not **say** that this is **indeed** so? The Gemara responds: **The verse states:** "And you shall bring the meal-offering that is made of these things unto the Lord; **and it shall be presented unto the priest, and he shall bring it unto the altar"** (Leviticus 2:8); **just as presentation to the priest is in a vessel, so too bringing** it **to the altar** must be **in a vessel.**

Rav Ashi draws a parallel: just as when the *kohen* sanctifies the blood of a *korban*, the sanctification comes through the vessel that catches the blood — the *kli sharet* of the *shechitah* being the knife itself — and not through the blood physically touching the altar, so too by the *minchah*, contact of the vessel with the altar suffices.

### Altar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהֵיכָן קִיַּימְתָּה? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: קָסָבַר הַאי תַּנָּא כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּצָפוֹן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But where have you fulfilled** the phrase "before the Lord"? **Rav Ashi said: This *tanna* holds** that **the entire altar stands in the north** of the Temple courtyard. Therefore, the entire southern side of the altar stood opposite the Holy of Holies in the west, and it can therefore be called: Before the Lord.

Rav Ashi offers yet another resolution to the two verses, independent of the southwest-corner solution. He argues that the *tanna* who generated this *drasha* held that *kulo mizbe'ach batzafon kai* — the entire altar was situated in the northern portion of the *Azarah*. If the *mizbe'ach* was shifted entirely northward, then its southern face would align precisely with the midpoint of the *Azarah*, directly opposite the opening of the *Heichal*. In that configuration, standing at the south face of the *mizbe'ach* places you squarely *lifnei Hashem* — directly before the *Heichal* — while also satisfying *el pnei hamizbeach* by being at the face of the altar. Both verses are satisfied without recourse to the corner at all.

## Kometz Preparation

### Frankincense Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְסַלֵּק אֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה לְצַד אֶחָד, וְקוֹמֵץ מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁנִּתְרַבָּה שַׁמְנָהּ וְנוֹתְנוֹ לְתוֹךְ כְּלִי שָׁרֵת, וּמְקַדְּשׁוֹ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **And he removes the frankincense to one side, and he removes a handful from the place where its oil has accumulated** and mixed with the flour, **and he puts** the handful **into a service vessel and consecrates it in** the **service vessel.**

The *mishnah* on the standard *minchah* procedure states *umesalek et halevonah letzad echad* — the *kohen* moves the frankincense to one side before taking the *kometz*. The reason is straightforward: the *kometz* must consist solely of the flour mixed with oil. *Ki heichi dela tikmotz behadi mincha* — so that when he scoops the handful, he does not inadvertently include any frankincense. As the Gemara elsewhere teaches, if when the *kohen* took the *kometz* he found in his hand a pebble, a grain of salt, or a single granule of *levonah*, the *kometz* is *pasul* — because his handful must be entirely of the *minchah* itself, nothing else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמְסַלֵּק אֶת לְבוֹנָתָהּ לְצַד אֶחָד. כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּקְּמוֹץ בַּהֲדֵי מִנְחָה, כְּדִתְנַן: קָמַץ וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ צְרוֹר אוֹ גַּרְגֵּר מֶלַח אוֹ קוֹרֶט לְבוֹנָה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **And he removes its frankincense to one side.** The Gemara explains: This is done **in order that** the frankincense **not be removed along with the meal-offering** when the priest removes a handful. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 6a): If he **removed** the **handful and a pebble, or a grain of salt, or a crumb [*koret*] of frankincense came out in his hand, it is invalid.** The handful must be entirely fine flour.

Similarly, *vekometz mimakom shenitrabah shamanah* — the *kometz* is taken from the portion of the *minchah* where the oil is most thoroughly mixed in. The Scriptural basis is *misaltah umishmanah* — from its flour and from its oil — which the Gemara reads as mandating that the *kometz* come from a portion rich in oil.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹמֵץ מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁנִּתְרַבָּה שַׁמְנָהּ. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב ״מִסׇּלְתָּהּ וּמִשַׁמְנָהּ״, ״מִגִּרְשָׂהּ וּמִשַׁמְנָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And he removes a handful from the place where its oil has accumulated.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written:** "And he shall take from there his handful **of the fine flour thereof, and of the oil thereof"** (Leviticus 2:2). The Torah also states: "And the priest shall make the memorial part of it smoke, even **of the groats thereof, and of the oil thereof"** (Leviticus 2:16). The handful should be taken from the area where there is an abundance of oil.

### Double Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנוֹ לְתוֹךְ כְּלִי שָׁרֵת וּמְקַדְּשׁוֹ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת. לְמָה לִי? הָא קַדְּשַׁהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא! מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַדָּם; דָּם, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקַדֵּישְׁתֵּיהּ סַכִּין בְּצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה, הֲדַר מְקַדֵּישׁ לֵיהּ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And he puts** the handful **into a service vessel and sanctifies it in** the **service vessel.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need this sanctification? **He has** already **sanctified it once,** when he initially brought it to the Temple. The Gemara responds: The sanctification here is **just as with** the **blood** of the offerings. **Although the knife sanctifies blood** by contact with **the neck of the animal,** since the knife itself is a service vessel, the priest **sanctifies it again** when he collects it **in the service vessel. Here too, it is not different;** the meal-offering must be sanctified twice.

Once the *kohen* has taken the *kometz*, *venotno letoch kli sharet umekadsho bichli sharet* — he places the *kometz* into a *kli sharet* and sanctifies it therein. The Gemara asks: the *minchah* was already sanctified in the *kli sharet* when it was first transferred from the home vessels — why does the *kometz* require yet another act of sanctification in a second *kli sharet*?

The Gemara answers by drawing a parallel to the blood of a *korban*: *dam af al gav dekadisheih sakin betzavar behemah* — the blood is already sanctified by the knife, which is a *kli sharet* and which sanctifies the blood at the moment of *shechitah* — *hadar mikadshei leih bichli sharet* — and yet the blood is caught again in a *kli sharet*, which constitutes a second act of sanctification. The first sanctification establishes the sacred status of the blood; the second elevates it to the level required for application to the *mizbe'ach*. So too by the *minchah*: the first placement in the *kli sharet* sanctifies the *minchah* as a whole; placing the *kometz* in a second *kli sharet* brings it to the level of sanctity required for offering on the altar. *Hacha nami lo shana* — here too, it is no different.
