# Menachot 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav's Shelo Lishmo

### Rav's Rule

The *daf* opens by returning to the teaching of Rav, who introduced a position that appeared to cut against the silence of the opening *mishnah* in both Zevachim and Menachot. Rav's claim was that *korbanot* brought *l'hachshir* — to change a Halachic status — are subject to a special rule: if such a *korban* is performed *shelo lishmo*, not only is it ineffective in changing that status, but the *korban* itself is entirely invalid. The rationale is that when a *korban*'s whole identity and purpose is bound up with its effectiveness as a means of changing status, and that effectiveness is absent, the *korban* has no meaningful identity at all.

### Status vs. Atonement

This principle applies to specific *korbanot*. In the context of *minchat omer*, the *omer* offering exists precisely to render the new grain permissible — it is, in its essence, an act of *hachsharah*. Similarly, Rav focused on the *asham* of a *nazir* who became *tamei* and the *asham* of a *metzora*, both of which serve to change the status of the individual as part of a purification process. Notably, Rav did not need to include the *chatat* in his ruling, since we already know independently that a *chatat shelo lishmo* is invalid.

What emerges as counterintuitive is that *korbanot* brought *l'chaper* — to atone — would remain valid even when brought *shelo lishmo* and therefore ineffective. One might expect atonement, being equally outcome-dependent, to follow the same logic. Yet Rav holds otherwise.

### Korban Nature

The explanation offered is rooted in the fundamental nature of each type of *korban*. Something brought *l'chaper* is, at its core, an offering to God; the atonement is an outgrowth of that offering, not its defining essence. Therefore, even when the *korban* fails to achieve *kapparah*, it retains its identity as a *korban*. By contrast, something brought *l'hachshir* — like the *asham metzora* or the *minchat omer* — belongs to a broader category of status-changing rituals, a category that includes non-*korban* acts such as *mikvah* and *parah adumah*. When effectiveness is the entire point and that effectiveness is absent, the *korban* loses its identity entirely. The *minchat omer* is somewhat unusual in this framing, since it is genuinely an offering to God — the new grain is brought before Him and that act renders it permissible. But Rav's position treats it nonetheless as a *machshir* whose validity depends on its efficacy.

---

## Asham Metzora

### Mishna Citation

The *Gemara* now raises a direct refutation of Rav's position. The *Mishnah* states: an *asham metzora* that was slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, or whose blood was not applied to the thumbs and toes of the *metzora* as required, is nonetheless offered on the *mizbeach*. Furthermore, it requires *nesachim* — libations — with it, which is noteworthy because ordinarily an *asham* does not come with *nesachim*. The *asham metzora* retains its specific identity even in this case. At the same time, the *metzora* remains unpurified, and a second *asham* must be brought to actually change his status.

### Contradiction

This is a direct and clean contradiction of Rav's position. Here is precisely the case Rav described — an *asham metzora shelo lishmo* that fails to accomplish *hachsharah* — and the *Mishnah* rules unambiguously that it remains a valid *korban*. The *Gemara* declares: *tiyuvta de-Rav*. Rav's position is rejected. The *Mishnah* at the opening of both Zevachim and Menachot had in fact been silent about this case in a way that pointed against Rav all along, and now the explicit *Mishnah* of *asham metzora* confirms that silence was significant.

---

## Reish Lakish

### Omer Kosher

With Rav's broad position set aside, the *Gemara* now turns to Reish Lakish, who focuses specifically on the *minchat omer*. Reish Lakish rules that a *minchat omer* whose *kemitzah* was performed *shelo lishmah* is nevertheless *kasher*. This too contradicts Rav's position, though now narrowed to the *omer* case. Reish Lakish does not, however, reach Rav's conclusion that such an *omer* is *pasul*.

### Remainder Issue

Reish Lakish does add a significant qualification: while the *korban* itself is valid, the *shiyarim* — the remainder — cannot be eaten until another *minchat omer* is brought. The reason is that the *minchat omer* performed *shelo lishmah* was not effective as a *matir* — it did not render the new grain permissible. Consequently, the grain used for this *omer*, taken from the new crop before any effective *omer* was brought, is itself *treif*: it is grain that a Jew is forbidden to eat. The *shiyarim* therefore cannot be consumed. The remainder must either wait until it becomes *pasul* through *linah* and be burned, or the community brings a second, properly performed *minchat omer* later that day, which then renders the new grain permissible and allows the *shiyarim* to be eaten.

### Valid Omer

This situation raises a pointed question directed at Reish Lakish himself. The *Gemara* cites the principle derived from the verse in Yechezkel requiring that a *korban* be brought from *min ha-muttar l'Yisrael* — from food that is permissible for a Jew to eat. If the *omer shelo lishmah* was not effective as a *matir*, then the *kometz* placed on the *mizbeach* was literally *treif* grain. Why, then, is the *korban* valid? The grain from which it was brought was forbidden at the moment of its offering.

The response is supplied by Rav Adda bar Ahava, who explains that Reish Lakish holds a specific principle regarding timing.

---

## Ein Mechusar Zman

### Future Status

Rav Adda bar Ahava articulates that Reish Lakish operates on the basis of *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom*: if something will become fit later that same day, it is not considered as though its time has not yet arrived. Applied here, since the grain will be permissible later that day — when a proper *minchat omer* is brought — the *korban* is evaluated based on that future status and is considered as having been brought from *kasher* grain even now.

### Principle Limits

This is a more expansive application of *ein mechusar zman* than its familiar usage. The principle is well known from Zevachim in the context of *korban Pesach*: if one sanctifies an animal as a *korban Pesach* on the morning of the fourteenth of Nisan, it is not rejected as *nidcheh* — pushed away as unfit — even though it cannot be brought until the afternoon, because the time will arrive later that very day. There, the only problem is premature timing, and time alone resolves it naturally.

Here, however, the situation is more complex. The grain is not merely *too early*; it is actively *treif* in the present moment. Eating it would incur a *lav*. Moreover, the grain will not become permissible simply through the passage of time — an act must be performed, namely the bringing of another *minchat omer*. As one present at the *shiur* observed, this is a double *chiddush*: not only is there an active prohibition in force right now rather than merely a timing issue, but the resolution of that prohibition requires a human act rather than the natural arrival of the appropriate moment. Yet Reish Lakish holds that both of these differences are bridged by the principle, and since the grain's permissibility will arrive later today, the *korban* is treated as valid even now.

### Pesach Distinction

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a *Beraita* which, in listing the distinctions between bird *korbanot* and *menachot*, states that bird offerings apply in cases of *mechusar kapparah* and are permitted where otherwise they would be *treif* — via *melikah* — whereas this is not true of *menachot*. The *Beraita* implies that a *minchah* cannot be brought from grain that is not permissible to eat. The *Gemara* responds: once we accept *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* as Reish Lakish applies it, the grain of the *minchat omer shelo lishmah* is not genuinely treated as *treif*, because we are evaluating its status based on what it will be later today. Therefore, this case cannot serve as an example of something *treif* being offered on the *mizbeach*, and the *Beraita*'s contrast between birds and *menachot* remains intact.

---

## Rav Sheshet

### Oil Before Blood

Rav Sheshet challenges the application of *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* from a different direction, drawing on the laws of *metzora* purification. The *Beraita* states that if the *kohen* applied the oil before the blood in the purification ritual — reversing the required order — the procedure must be corrected: the *log* of oil is refilled and a second application of oil is performed after the blood is properly placed.

### Thumbs Order

Similarly, if the oil was placed on the thumbs and toes of the *metzora* before the oil was sprinkled seven times — again an inversion of the proper sequence — the *log* is refilled and the thumb application is redone after the sprinkling.

### Order Problem

Rav Sheshet's question is: why should these inversions of order constitute a problem at all, given the principle of *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom*? If we reason broadly that since today is the day for these rituals, any act performed earlier in the day should be deemed timely — just as the *minchat omer* was considered brought at the right time even before the proper *omer* was offered — then performing one step before another within the same day should similarly be valid. The *Gemara* acknowledges that this is indeed the logical extension of the principle as applied by Reish Lakish.

---

## Metzora Sequence

### Fixed Sequence

Rav Papa resolves Rav Sheshet's challenge. The laws of *metzora* purification are governed by a verse that fixes their sequence: *ve-zos tihiyeh Torat ha-Metzora* — this is how the ritual of the *metzora* shall be. The word *tihiyeh* implies that the procedure must be carried out precisely as described, in the order described. Therefore, within the *metzora* ritual, *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* is simply inapplicable — the *Torah* has imposed a mandatory sequence on the *avodot* of the *metzora*, and performing them out of that sequence is a violation of the *Torah*'s requirement regardless of the timing principle.

---

## Chatat Order

### Slaughter Order

Rav Papa himself then raises a new difficulty. The purification of the *metzora* requires bringing an *asham* before a *chatat*. What happens if the *chatat* was slaughtered first? One might propose the following solution: have someone continuously stir the blood of the *chatat* to prevent it from congealing, then slaughter the *asham*, offer the *asham*, and only afterward offer the *chatat* — thereby preserving the correct order of the *avodot* even after an out-of-order *shechita*.

### Linah Solution

The *Beraita* rules against this approach. Rather than stirring the blood of the *chatat* and proceeding, one should allow the meat of the *chatat* to become *pasul* through *linah* — remaining overnight until it becomes invalid — and then burn it. A new *chatat* is then brought in the correct sequence. The problem Rav Papa raises is: why is the premature *shechita* of the *chatat* a disqualifying problem in the first place? If *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* applies, why not say that since today is the day for the *chatat*'s *shechita*, even performing it before the *asham* should be acceptable?

---

## Metzora Sequence (II)

### Shechita Status

Before Rav Papa can even fully articulate his question, the *Gemara* interrupts: but Rav Papa himself already ruled that the *metzora* rituals follow a fixed Torah-mandated sequence. Should that not resolve his own question?

Rav Papa clarifies that his question was more precise than it appeared. He was asking specifically about *shechita*, and whether the principle of a fixed sequence applies to it. *Shechita*, he argues, is not an *avoda* in the technical sense. The *Torah*'s mandate of a fixed sequence for the *metzora* rituals governs the *avodot* — the formal acts of Temple service — but *shechita* does not belong to that category.

The *Gemara* then cites three reasons why *shechita* is not an *avoda*. First, as Rashi notes, *shechita* does not require a *kohen* — a non-*kohen* may perform it — which is a fundamental marker distinguishing *avodah* from other acts. Second, as Tosafot emphasizes, *shechita* applies equally outside the *Beit Hamikdash*: the laws of *shechita* are not unique to the *korban* context but govern all animal slaughter, demonstrating that *shechita* is not conceptually a Temple ritual but simply what one must do to render meat permissible. Third, the *shochet* need not stand within the *Beit Hamikdash*; one could stand outside with an extended knife, whereas a genuine *avoda* requires standing within the Temple precincts.

Given that *shechita* is not an *avoda*, Rav Papa reasons that the fixed-sequence ruling does not apply to it, and therefore the only apparent problem with slaughtering the *chatat* prematurely is timing. Hence his question: why not invoke *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* and permit it?

---

## Clarification

### Time-Only Problem

The *Gemara*'s resolution, given by Rav Papa himself, abandons *ein mechusar zman* as the explanation for why the *minchat omer shelo lishmah* is valid. The principle of *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* remains fully operative, but only in its proper and limited domain: when the *sole* problem is that the time has not yet arrived, and that time will arrive of its own accord later that day. In that narrow case — the paradigmatic example being the *korban Pesach* sanctified on the morning of the fourteenth of Nisan — we say it is not regarded as premature, because its time is coming today.

### Other Defects

When the problem is not mere timing but something else — whether it is an active *treif* status, an inversion of required sequence, or any other substantive defect — *ein mechusar zman* does not apply. The grain of the *minchat omer shelo lishmah* is actively *treif* at the moment of offering, and this is not a pure timing issue. The out-of-order *shechita* of the *chatat* is an issue of sequence, not timing. In neither case can *ein mechusar zman* be invoked.

This leaves Reish Lakish's ruling — that the *minchat omer shelo lishmah* is *kasher* — without its earlier justification. If *ein mechusar zman* cannot excuse the *treif* grain, why does Reish Lakish hold the *korban* valid? Rav Papa supplies a new explanation.

---

## Daybreak Matir

### Daybreak Rule

Rav Papa now reveals the true basis of Reish Lakish's position. Reish Lakish holds that the grain of the new crop does not become permissible through the bringing of the *omer* per se; rather, the *matir* is the daybreak of the sixteenth of Nisan. Both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish rule — and this applies *even when the Beit Hamikdash stands* — that *he'ir mizrach matir*: the dawn of the sixteenth is what renders the *chadash* permissible. The bringing of the *omer* on that day is a *mitzvah* and there remains an obligation to wait for the *korban* before eating, but strictly speaking the *issur* of *chadash* dissipates at daybreak regardless of whether the *omer* has yet been brought.

### Mishna Implication

The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan stated this explicitly, while Reish Lakish expressed it only implicitly, derived from the language of the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* rules that one may not bring *menachot* or *bikkurim* before the *omer*, and that if brought before the *omer* they are *pasul* — because the grain from which they are made is not *kasher* food. However, if brought before the *shtei ha-lechem* of Shavuot, they are *kasher*, because the grain is permissible: from the time the *omer* was brought on the sixteenth of Nisan, the grain is permissible for eating, and the *shtei ha-lechem* requirement is one of sequence and honor for the new grain rather than a prohibition on consuming it. The ruling regarding *bikkurim* in this list is itself noteworthy — since *bikkurim* are fruit rather than grain and are not subject to the *chadash* prohibition, their inclusion may relate to the *shtei ha-lechem* rule rather than to the *omer*.

Then comes the decisive statement attributed to Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Reish Lakish: when is it that *menachot* brought before the *omer* are *pasul*? Only when brought on the fourteenth or fifteenth of Nisan. But if brought on the sixteenth of Nisan — even before the *omer* has been offered — they are *kasher*. This must mean that at daybreak of the sixteenth the grain is already permissible, for otherwise a *korban* brought on the morning of the sixteenth from the new crop would be *pasul* just as one brought on the fourteenth would be.

The *Gemara* notes that this statement is not considered fully explicit, because one could attempt to explain the validity of a *korban* brought on the morning of the sixteenth through *ein mechusar zman le-vo ba-yom* — the approach we have now rejected. Only once *ein mechusar zman* is set aside does the ruling clearly imply that daybreak is the true *matir*. This also resolves David's question about Reish Lakish's position: if the grain is permissible from daybreak, why does Reish Lakish say the *shiyarim* cannot be eaten? Rashi answers that there remains a *mitzvah* to wait until the *omer* is brought before eating — based on the verse *ad havi'achem et korban Eloheichem* — even though the strict *issur* has already dissipated at dawn. Just as a person in his home ideally waits until the *omer* is brought before eating the new grain, the *shiyarim* of the *minchat omer shelo lishmah* similarly should not be eaten until a properly performed *omer* is brought.

---

## Rava's View

### Rava Ruling

Rava takes a different and more far-reaching position. He rules that a *minchat omer* performed *shelo lishmah* is *kasher*, and moreover, the *shiyarim* may be eaten — and this without requiring another *minchat omer* to be brought at all. This goes beyond Reish Lakish, who held the *korban* valid but required a second *omer* before the remainder could be eaten.

### Bad Thought Rule

Rava's reasoning rests on the principle that a *machshava* — an improper intention — is only operative and able to invalidate when it occurs in a context where it has traction: *she-ein machshava mo'eles ela be-mi she-ra'uy la-avoda, u-ve-davar ha-ra'uy la-avoda, u-ve-makom ha-ra'uy la-avoda*. Three conditions must be met for a disqualifying intention to take effect. First, the person performing the *avoda* must be fit for *avoda* — hence if a non-*kohen* or a *kohen ba'al mum* performed an *avoda* with an improper intent such as *piggul*, the intent would not invalidate. Second, the object must itself be fit for *avoda*. Third, the location and context must be appropriate — hence if the *mizbeach* had a chip rendering *korbanot* temporarily *pasul*, an improper intent expressed at that moment would not invalidate.

### Omer Abnormal

Rava applies the second condition to the *minchat omer*: the *minchat omer* is not a *davar ha-ra'uy la-avoda* — it is not something fit to be offered — because the grain from which it is made is itself *treif* at the time it is being brought. This is the *chiddush* of the *minchat omer*: the very grain placed in the *kohen*'s hand for the *kemitzah* is, at that moment, grain that is forbidden to eat. The *minchat omer* is abnormal in that it itself renders the grain permissible through the act of being brought — it is *matir* itself in the process. As such, it exists in a categorically unusual position: by all normal principles, this grain should not qualify as a *korban* at all, and it is only by virtue of the *Torah*'s specific command to bring it in this way that it is accepted. Because the *minchat omer* operates outside the normal framework of *korbanot*, the ordinary rules about invalidating intentions do not apply to it. The *machshava shelo lishmah* therefore has no traction, and the *korban* is entirely valid — *shiyarim* and all.

---

## Treifa Exclusion

### Derasha

The *Gemara* now pivots to a *Beraita* that expounds the verse *min ha-bakar* — from the cattle. The *Torah* uses the phrase *min ha-bakar* in more than one place; the second occurrence, the *Beraita* teaches, serves to exclude a *treifah* animal from being brought as a *korban*. A *treifah* — an animal with a fatal physical defect that renders it forbidden for consumption — is invalid as a *korban*.

### Logic Question

The *Beraita* itself immediately raises the obvious difficulty: do we truly need a verse to exclude a *treifah*? One could construct a *kal va-chomer*: if a *ba'al mum* — a blemished animal — which is *muttar* for a person to eat, is nonetheless forbidden as a *korban*, then a *treifah*, which is itself *assur* for a person to eat, should certainly be forbidden as a *korban*. The *Beraita* answers: *chelev va-dam yokhichu* — the fat and blood of an animal serve as a counterexample. Fat and blood are forbidden to a person yet are permissible for the *mizbeach*. Hence the *kal va-chomer* breaks down, and one might have thought a *treifah* would similarly be permissible for a *korban*.

The *Gemara* refines this: *chelev va-dam* come from an otherwise kosher animal — the animal as a whole is permissible, and it is only these particular components that carry an *issur*. That is quite different from a *treifah*, where the entire animal is forbidden. Once this distinction is noted, the logic resumes: of course a *treifah*, where the whole animal is *assur*, cannot be offered on the *mizbeach*. And therefore, it would seem, one should not need a verse — logic alone would dictate the *treifah*'s invalidity.

### Counterargument

But the *Beraita* concludes: *ve-im hashivokha* — and yet, if you pressed me with a retort. The *Beraita* concedes that some argument *could* have been made in favor of a *treifah* being valid, and it is in order to exclude this that the verse *min ha-bakar* is required. The *Gemara* asks: what could that argument possibly be? Rav answers: the *minchat omer* could have served as a proof. Here is a case where something *assur le-hedyot* and *muttar le-gavoha* — something forbidden to a person and permissible for the *mizbeach* — is not *chelev* or *dam*, but grain that is wholly *treif* and yet brought as a valid *korban*. One might have argued from this that a *treifah* animal should similarly be acceptable. Hence a verse is needed to exclude it.

---

## Omer Distinction

### Omer Matir

The *Gemara* responds to this proposed proof immediately: *mah le-minchat ha-omer she-ken materet chadash* — the *minchat omer* is categorically different because it is *matir chadash*. It does not merely involve *treif* material being permitted in a *Mikdash* context; rather, the *minchat omer* is itself the mechanism by which the grain becomes permissible. It is *matir atzmah* — it renders itself permissible in the course of being offered. A *treifah* animal carries no such character: it does not render itself permissible through being offered, and the *Torah* has not designated it as the vehicle for any *heter*.

### Test Cases

The *Gemara* then runs through a series of proposed analogies that might similarly have served as arguments in favor of a *treifah*. Reish Lakish proposes *pitum ha-ketoret* — the compounding of the incense — which is *assur le-hedyot* yet *muttar le-gavoha*. The response: *mitzvatah bekach* — that is specifically how the *Torah* commands it to be done. Rav Papa's student Mar son of Ravina proposes *Shabbat*: certain *korbanot* override *Shabbat*, and even a private individual may perform *milah* on *Shabbat*. The response: *milah* is not for personal benefit but for the fulfillment of a *mitzvah*, and in any case *mitzvatah bekach* applies. Rav Adda bar Abba proposes *kilayim*: the belt of a *kohen* is *shaatnez*, yet it is required. Even a private individual may wear a woolen *tzitzit* on a linen garment. The response: *tzitzit* too is for a *mitzvah*, and the *kohen*'s belt is specifically mandated by the *Torah* to be made this way — *mitzvatah bekach*.

The pattern is striking. In each case, the *Torah* explicitly commands the act in question despite its ordinarily forbidden character. That explicit command is the source of the permission. A *treifah* animal, by contrast, carries no such command and no such designation. The *Torah* has never said: bring this *treifah* as a *korban*. Absent that explicit authorization, there is no logical basis to permit it, and the principle of *mi-mashkeh Yisrael* — that a *korban* must be brought from food fit for a Jew — would naturally exclude it. Yet the *Beraita* insisted that a verse was necessary, and the *Gemara* is struggling to identify what argument could have remained in favor of the *treifah*'s validity even after all these distinctions. The *daf* concludes with this question open.
