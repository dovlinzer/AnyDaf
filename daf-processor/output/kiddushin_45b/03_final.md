# Kiddushin 45b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketana, Father Away

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְהָלַךְ אָבִיהָ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְעָמְדָה וְנִישֵּׂאת
>
> **Translation:** If a minor **became betrothed with her father's consent, and her father left for overseas, and she went ahead and got married** of her own accord in her father's absence,

The *gemara* picks up in the middle of 45b with a case involving a *ketana* — a minor girl — whose *kiddushin* was performed with her father's knowledge and approval. Whether the father himself arranged the match or she acted on her own initiative in a way that made his assent clear, the binding character of the *kiddushin* is not in question; the father was demonstrably on board. Subsequently, however, *ve-halach aviha le-medinat ha-yam* — the father traveled to a distant land — and while he was away, *ve-amda ve-niset*, she proceeded to *nisuin*.

### Nisuin & Da'at

The resulting question is whether *nisuin* performed *she-lo le-da'at aviha* — without the father's knowledge or consent — is legally effective. This raises two analytically distinct considerations. First, one might argue that *nisuin* is categorically different from *kiddushin* as a form of *kinyan*: *kiddushin* creates the marriage as a matter of law, whereas *nisuin* translates it into lived reality, and might therefore require less of the formal *da'at* ordinarily demanded of a *kinyan*. On the other hand, *nisuin* is precisely the moment when the minor daughter passes out of her father's legal control and into her husband's domain. Even if an adult woman's *nisuin* requires less formal *da'at* than an initial *kinyan*, one would expect that a *ketana* cannot make that transition — from the father's authority to the husband's — without the father's explicit consent. The *nafka mina* is multiple and concrete: whether she may eat *terumah* if her husband is a *kohen*, and whether if she dies her husband inherits her estate, along with the full range of reciprocal financial obligations that attach to full *nisuin*.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** If she is an Israelite who married a priest, **she may partake of *teruma* until her father comes and protests,** explicitly stating that he does not agree to the marriage.

Rav holds that she is presumed to be a *nesuah* and may eat *terumah* until her father returns and objects. The presumption runs in favor of the *nisuin* being valid; the father, knowing that *kiddushin* had taken place and having approved it, can be assumed to acquiesce in the natural continuation to *nisuin*. Should he return and protest — presumably while she is still a *ketana* or *na'arah* and within the window of paternal authority — he can retroactively undo the status of *nesuah*.

### Rav Asi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, שֶׁמָּא יָבוֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת זָרָה אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה לְמַפְרֵעַ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Perhaps her father will come and protest, and it will be found retroactively that a non-priest has partaken of *teruma*.**

Rav Asi disagrees. His concern is *shema yavo aviv ve-yimcheh* — the father might protest upon returning — and therefore one cannot presume the *nisuin* valid. The consequence is stark: *ve-nimtza zara ochelet be-terumah le-mafreia* — were she eating *terumah* and the father then protested, she would retroactively have been a non-*kohen*'s wife consuming *terumah* improperly.

### Rav's Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְחַשׁ לַהּ רַב לְהָא דְּרַב אַסִּי
>
> **Translation:** **There was an incident** of this kind, **and Rav was concerned** for this opinion **of Rav Asi** and did not allow a girl in this situation to partake of *teruma*.

Although Rav's theoretical ruling permits her to eat *terumah*, the *gemara* records that *havei uvda ve-chash leh Rav le-hadei Rav Asi* — in a practical case that arose, Rav was stringent in accordance with Rav Asi's position and did not permit her to eat. Some *rishonim* understand this to mean that Rav did not abandon his ruling but simply exercised *chumra* in practice. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak clarifies that Rav himself is the one who held, as a matter of law, that she could eat *terumah*; it is merely that when an actual case presented itself, he deferred to the stricter view of Rav Asi.

### Inheritance & Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: וּמוֹדֶה רַב שֶׁאִם מֵתָה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ. אוֹקִי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: And Rav concedes that if** the minor girl **dies,** the husband **does not inherit** from **her,** because of the principle: **Establish money in the possession of its owner.**

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak u-modeh Rav: she-im metah einah yorshah.* Even Rav concedes that if she were to die, her husband would not inherit her estate. Despite holding that she has sufficient status as a *nesuah* to eat *terumah*, Rav acknowledges that the *nisuin* is not certain enough to transfer property: *uki mamona be-chezkas mara* — the money remains in the presumptive possession of its original owner, and one does not transfer an estate on the basis of a doubtful *nisuin*.

This creates an apparent tension: if Rav is prepared to concede a *safek* as to whether there is *nisuin* — sufficient doubt that he will not allow property to transfer — how can he simultaneously permit her to eat *terumah*? Here Rashi and Tosafot both point to an important underlying principle. According to the *Bavli*, the right of a *kohen*'s wife to eat *terumah* derives *mid'oraisa* already from *kiddushin* alone, without waiting for *nisuin*; the requirement that she first undergo *nisuin* before eating *terumah* is only *mid'rabanan*. Therefore, Rav and Rav Asi may agree that her status as a *nesuah* is a *safek* — and indeed both accept that framework — but when it comes to the *d'rabanan* restriction, Rav is willing to be lenient. Tosafot further clarifies that even according to Rav Asi, the practical reasons for the *gezera* do not apply in the same way here, since she is already living with the man; Rav Asi's position is therefore a form of *lo plug* rather than a strict application of the underlying rationale.

## Elopement Cases

### Kidushin Good, Nisuin Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה לְדַעַת וְנִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וְאָבִיהָ כָּאן
>
> **Translation:** If a minor **became betrothed with** her father's **consent, and she married without** his **consent, and her father is here,** i.e., present,

The *gemara* now turns to a different configuration: *niskadsha le-da'at ve-niset she-lo le-da'at*, with *avia kan* — the father present. The *kiddushin* was performed with the father's knowledge, but the *nisuin* was not. The distinction from the prior case is that there the father was simply absent and could not be consulted; here the father is available and his response — or rather his lack of explicit response — is the crux of the matter. She has, it seems, acted behind his back, and when he found out, said nothing. The question is how to interpret that silence.

### Huna: Silence = No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר אוֹכֶלֶת – הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא אִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, הַאי דְּאִישְׁתִּיק – מִירְתָּח רָתַח
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said she may not partake** of *teruma*, **and even according to Rav, who said** that if her father is overseas **she may partake** of *teruma*, this is so only **there,** in the case **where the father is not present. But here, where the father is present,** he does not consent to the marriage; the reason **that he was silent** in **this** situation and refrained from protesting is that he was **so angry** that he did not wish even to speak to her.

*Rav Huna amar: eino ochelet.* She may not eat *terumah*. Even though Rav permitted eating when the father was away in a distant land, *hasam hu de-leika le-av* — there the father was simply unreachable, and we could presume he would be accepting when he eventually learned of it. But *aval hacha de-ika le-av* — here the father is present and presumably has now learned what occurred. His silence cannot be read as acquiescence. *Hai de-ishtik mirtach ratach* — one who falls silent in this situation may well be simply too angry to speak.

### Yirmiah: Silence = Yes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַב אַסִּי דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, הָתָם הוּא דְּשֶׁמָּא יָבוֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה, אֲבָל הָכָא מִדִּשְׁתֵיק – אִיתְנוֹחֵי אִיתְנְחָא לֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may partake** of *teruma*, **and even according to Rav Asi, who said** that **she may not partake** of *teruma*, it is **there** that there is a concern **that perhaps her father will come and protest. But here, from** the fact **that he was silent** the assumption is that **he is comfortable** with the marriage.

*Rav Yirmiah bar Abba amar: ochelet.* She may eat. Even Rav Asi, who would not permit eating when the father was far away on grounds that *shema yavo aviv ve-yimcheh*, said so only because *hatam hu de-shema yavo aviv ve-yimcheh* — when the father is absent, the concern is that upon returning he will raise an objection. But *aval hacha mi-de-shatik, isnochi asnach lei* — here, where the father is present and has heard what happened and said nothing, that silence is itself an indication of acceptance. The general principle of *shtika ke-hoda'ah* — silence functions as acknowledgment — supports the inference that a person who genuinely objects would say so; one who remains silent has made his peace, however reluctantly.

### Ulla's Analysis

As the *gemara* itself notes, there is a structural asymmetry in these two positions. The stance of Rav Yirmiah bar Abba is coherent: ordinarily we fear the father will protest when he hears the news, but actual silence in the face of known facts changes that presumption. By contrast, Rav Huna's position seems to move in the opposite direction — starting from a presumption that the father would approve when absent, yet interpreting his silence once present as anger and opposition. Ulla observes that the position of Rav Yirmiah, who was Rav Huna's student, appears considerably more consistent.

## Total Elopement

### Ulla Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְנִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וְאָבִיהָ כָּאן, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת, רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. אָמַר עוּלָּא: הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא ״כַּחֹמֶץ לַשִּׁנַּיִם וְכֶעָשָׁן לָעֵינָיִם״
>
> **Translation:** If a minor **became betrothed without the consent of her father, and married without** his **consent, and her father is here, Rav Huna said: She may partake** of *teruma*. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Ulla said: This** opinion **of Rav Huna,** that in this case she may partake of *teruma*, is irritating **"as vinegar to the teeth, and as smoke to the eyes"** (Proverbs 10:26), as it contradicts his earlier ruling.

Moving to the most extreme scenario — *niskadsha she-lo le-da'at avia ve-niset she-lo le-da'at avia ve-avia kan* — both the *kiddushin* and the *nisuin* were performed entirely without the father's knowledge, and he is present. Rav Huna holds *ochelet*, she may eat; Rav Yirmiah bar Abba holds *eina ochelet*, she may not. Ulla characterizes Rav Huna's position here as *ke-chometz la-shinayim u-ke-ashan la-einayim* — intolerable, like vinegar on the teeth or smoke in the eyes.

### Logical Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם דְּקִידּוּשֵׁי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אָמְרַתְּ לָא אָכְלָה, הָכָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי תַלְמִיד
>
> **Translation:** **Now** consider: **And what** about **there,** i.e., in a case where her betrothal was with her father's consent, **when** there is at least **betrothal by Torah law, you said she may not partake** of *teruma* in case he did not consent to the marriage. Is it **not all the more so** the case that **here,** i.e., where even the betrothal was performed without her father's consent, she should not be permitted to partake of *teruma*? Rather, **the statement of** the **student seems** reasonable.

The *gemara* articulates why. In the immediately preceding case — where *kiddushin* was done with the father's knowledge and only the *nisuin* was without — Rav Huna himself said she could not eat, reading the father's silence as opposition. How then, in the more extreme case where even the *kiddushin* was done without his knowledge, can one say she may eat? *Hashta u-mah hatam... amrat lo ochlah, hacha lo kol she-ken* — if even when the *kiddushin* was clearly valid and the father was silent about the *nisuin* alone, Rav Huna said no, surely when neither act was done with his knowledge, the answer must be no all the more. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *nirin divrei talmid* — the position of the student (Rav Yirmiah bar Abba) appears more logically compelling.

## Total Elopement…

### Rava Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא – הוֹאִיל וְנַעֲשָׂה בָּהּ מַעֲשֵׂה יְתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason of Rav Huna? Since an action was done with her** that renders her like **an orphan in the lifetime of her father,** she is considered married and may partake of *teruma*.

Rava, however, defends Rav Huna's position by offering a more nuanced reading of the underlying logic. *Mai ta'ama de-Rav Huna: ho'il ve-na'asah be-ma'aseh yatom be-chayei av* — the reason is that this girl has conducted herself as though she were an orphan in her father's lifetime. Two major life events — *kiddushin* and *nisuin* — have occurred without any paternal involvement or reaction. The absence of any assertion of authority across both events is itself a form of relinquishment. The father, by allowing the pattern to continue unchallenged, has in effect yielded his position and left her to make her own decisions. His silence is not opposition in this case; it is acquiescence born of having ceded control entirely.

### Setup

The contrast with the prior case now becomes clear: when the *kiddushin* was performed under his auspices and with his approval, there was never a moment when he had stepped back from his role. His silence about the *nisuin* alone would then read against a backdrop of active, continuing paternal authority — and that silence is harder to interpret as acceptance of something done behind his back. But when both acts proceed without him and he says nothing, the cumulative picture is that he has let her be.

---

## Elopement Cases…

### Rava Defends Huna

This is the key to Rava's defense of Rav Huna. When the *kiddushin* was done *le-da'at av* and only the *nisuin* was not, the father has never forfeited his standing. His silence regarding *nisuin* is therefore ambiguous — and Rav Huna reads it as tacit objection, in keeping with the view that *mirtach ratach*. But when neither act was within his purview, the father has effectively allowed his daughter to function as though she had no father, and the consistent pattern of non-response amounts to a relinquishment of control. Rava thus reconciles Rav Huna's two rulings by identifying the relevant variable not as whether there was one unapproved act or two, but rather whether there was any prior basis of paternal authority that was then bypassed.

## Conclusion on Ketana

### Both Can Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קְטַנָּה שֶׁנִּתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, אָמַר רַב: בֵּין הִיא וּבֵין אָבִיהָ יְכוֹלִין לְעַכֵּב
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* disputed the following issue: In the case of **a minor girl who became betrothed without her father's consent, Rav says: Either she or her father is able to prevent** the betrothal from taking effect.

*Amar Rav: bein hi u-vein aviha yecholim limchot.* Rav states that in the case of a *ketana* who was betrothed without her father's knowledge, both the father and the daughter herself are capable of objecting and thereby invalidating the *kiddushin*. This statement should be read in context: Rav had previously held that such *kiddushin* creates a concern (*chashash*) of valid betrothal — *shema yitratze ha-av* — meaning that if the father acquiesces, the *kiddushin* might take effect. What Rav now clarifies is the other side of that picture: if the father explicitly objects, that *chashash* evaporates entirely. And not only the father — the daughter, too, can object.

## Total Elopement…

### Huna: Yes

In the case of full elopement — *kiddushin* and *nisuin* both *she-lo le-da'at ha-av*, with the father present — Rav Huna holds that she may eat *terumah*.

### Yirmiah: No

Rav Yirmiah bar Abba holds that she may not eat *terumah* in this case.

## Conclusion on Ketana…

### Rashi's Limitation

Rashi understands the daughter's right to object as temporally limited: she can only object prior to the father finding out about the *kiddushin*. Once the *kiddushin* is known to the father, the question of its validity passes into his domain, and her prior acceptance of the *kiddusin* money can no longer be undone by her protest. The reasoning is that before the father learns of it and before his reaction can ratify the *kiddushin*, her ongoing willingness remains a live factor; once she retracts before that window closes, the acceptance is undermined and the *kiddushin* cannot take effect.

### Tosafot Clarification

Tosafot clarifies the scope of the entire preceding discussion. When Rav said earlier that we are *choshesh shema yitratze ha-av*, that concern applies specifically *be-stama she-shama ve-shatak* — when the father heard and was initially silent. The *sugyot* about interpreting the father's silence are thus unified: the entire discussion addresses the ambiguous case of an unexplained non-reaction. *Aval heicha de-michah gila be-da'ato* — if the father explicitly objected, his intent is manifest and there is no *kiddushin*; if he explicitly accepted, the *kiddushin* is valid. The interpretive work only begins when the father's response is silence.

### Explicit Intent

When the father makes his position clear — whether by explicit approval or explicit objection — his stated intent governs the validity of the *kiddushin*. The debates among Rav, Rav Asi, Rav Huna, and Rav Yirmiah bar Abba all operate within the space of ambiguity created by silence alone.

## Torah Case Analysis

### Torah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב אַסִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ חִיָּיא בַּר רַב לְרַב אַסִּי: ״אִם מָאֵן יְמָאֵן אָבִיהָ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיהָ, הִיא עַצְמָהּ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם מָאֵן יְמָאֵן״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna raised an objection to Rav Asi, and some say** it was **Ḥiyya bar Rav** who raised an objection **to Rav Asi:** The *baraita* states in the case of a seduced woman: **"If her father refuses** to give her to him, he shall pay money according to the dowry of virgins" (Exodus 22:16). The Sages expounded: **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* that **her father** can refuse to give her to the seducer in marriage; **from where** do I derive that **she herself** can also refuse? **The verse states: "If he refuses [*ma'en yema'en*]."** The repetition of the verb indicates that the right of refusal exists **in any case.**

Chiya bar Rav challenges Rav Asi by grounding the case in a *pasuk* from the Torah. The verse in question — *im ma'en yema'en aviha* — concerns the case of the seducer, where the father refuses to give his daughter in marriage to the man who seduced her, and the man must therefore pay fifty *kesef*. Chiya bar Rav reads this case as the precise scenario the *gemara* has been discussing: a *ketana* or *na'arah* who underwent what amounted to *kiddushin* without her father's prior knowledge, with the father now reacting.

### Deriving Daughter's Right

From the *pasuk* alone, only the father's right to refuse is explicit — *ein li ela aviha, hi atzma minayin?* How do we know the daughter herself may refuse? The *midrash* derives this from the doubled language of *im ma'en yema'en*, reading the repetition as an inclusion — *mi-kol makom*, from whatever source, a refusal is valid. This would suggest that her objection also carries legal force in the case of *kiddushin she-lo le-da'at aviha*, and would support Rav's position that *bein hi u-vein aviha yecholim limchot*.

### Rav's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ רַב: לָא תֵּיזְלוּ בָּתַר אִיפְּכָא. יָכֵול לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי לְכוּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁפִּיתָּהּ שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to those** who tried to offer support for his opinion: **Do not go after the opposite,** i.e., do not cite a proof from a source that could be understood in the opposite manner. Rav Asi **can answer your** argument by claiming that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he** initially **seduced her not for the sake of marriage.**

Rav, however, deflects this argument: *lo tizul batar ipcha — yacholni le-shannoye lach k'gon she-pita le-shem ishus*. The *pasuk*, Rav insists, need not be understood as a case of *kiddushin* at all. The more straightforward reading is that the seduction occurred *she-lo le-shem ishus* — not in the context of marriage — and the sexual act was simply that, with no accompanying *kinyan kiddushin*.

### No Kiddushin Here

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לוֹמַר שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם קְנָס כִּמְפוּתָּה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** It serves **to say that** if she refuses to marry him despite her father's consent to the match, the seducer nevertheless **pays the fine like** a standard case of **a seduced woman.**

If that is the case, why does the Torah need to tell us that the father can refuse? Of course the father can refuse to allow his daughter to marry this man — the father's authority over whom his daughter may marry is a fundamental premise that requires no *pasuk*. The *gemara* therefore explains that the *pasuk*'s point is not to establish the father's authority to veto the marriage but rather to specify the financial consequence: even when the father refuses and the marriage does not take place, the seducer must still pay the fifty *kesef — amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: lomar she-meshalem knas ke-meputah*. The *pasuk* teaches the law of the *knas*, not a ruling about *kiddushin*.

### Seduction L'Shem Ishus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּתְנֵינָא: ״מָהֹר יִמְהָרֶנָּה לּוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״ – שֶׁצְּרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ קִידּוּשִׁין. וְאִם פִּיתָּהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת, קִידּוּשִׁין לְמָה לִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: צְרִיכָה קִידּוּשִׁין לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to him: If so,** if the *baraita* is discussing a case where intercourse was not performed to effect betrothal, **this is what we learned** in a *baraita* with regard to a seduced woman: **"He shall pay a dowry for her to be his wife"** (Exodus 22:15). This teaches **that she requires betrothal from him. And if** the verse is speaking of one who **seduced her for the sake of marriage, why do I** need **betrothal?** The act of intercourse can serve as the betrothal. Rather, the *baraita* must be referring to a case where he seduced her not for the sake of marriage. **Abaye said to him:** Even if he seduced her for the sake of marriage, **she requires** an additional **betrothal with her father's consent,** since she is a minor and became betrothed without it.

Rav Asi then responds: *ee hachi* — if that is indeed the reading — *hainu de-tanina etzlo: mahor yimharena lo le-ishah she-tzricha himenu kiddushin*. The language *mahor yimharena lo le-ishah* implies that a formal act of *kiddushin* now needs to take place, which confirms that no *kiddushin* had yet occurred — consistent with Rav's reading that the seduction was not *le-shem ishus*. Abaye, however, addresses the reading in which the seduction *was* for the sake of marriage — and on that reading, *mahor yimharena lo le-ishah* would mean that a new *kiddushin* must now be performed with the father's explicit approval: *tzricha kiddushin le-da'at aviha*.

### Tosafot's Question

This resolution raises a question that Tosafot presses. If the daughter can refuse, what does her refusal accomplish in practical terms? Consider the scenario as Rav reads it: the seduction was not *le-shem ishus*, the father has the right to refuse the marriage, and if the father refuses, the seducer pays fifty *kesef*. Now suppose the daughter objects. Does her objection prevent the marriage from going forward? Normally a *ketana* or *na'arah* has no independent right to veto a match her father has sanctioned. Is it really possible that simply because she was seduced she now acquires that right?

### Tosafot's Solutions

Tosafot offers two answers. The first, which Tosafot itself finds problematic, is that her refusal does not actually prevent the marriage but does obligate the payment of the *knas* — the fifty *kesef* — as though the father had refused, even if the wedding ultimately proceeds. Tosafot acknowledges the strangeness of this: presumably the father and husband could simply coordinate so that the daughter refuses, the *knas* is paid to the father, and the marriage goes forward regardless. The second and preferable answer is more straightforward: *nafka mina heicha de-ba aleha ve-achar kach nityatmah* — the daughter's refusal is operative in a case where the father has since died. Once there is no father to exercise paternal authority, *mahani miun didah le-akeiv she-lo tehei ishto* — her own objection can now function to block the marriage entirely. The broader point, which the *gemara* never fully resolves, is that once *chazal* recognized the daughter's right of refusal, the precise circumstances in which it is operative — when the father is present, when he has approved, and what weight her voice carries — remain somewhat underdeveloped.

## Multiple Dates Case

### Separate Statements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בִּתְמָרָה זוֹ״, ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בָּזוֹ״ אִם יֵשׁ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to a woman: Be betrothed to me with this date,** and adds: **Be betrothed to me with that** one, then **if one of** the dates **is worth one *peruta* she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed,** since he mentioned betrothal in connection with each date.

The *gemara* now turns from the dynamics of *kiddushin* of a *ketana* to a *mishna* dealing with the mechanics of *kesef kiddushin* in the case of an adult woman. The *mishna* records: *ha-omer le-ishah hiskadshi li be-tamarah zo hiskadshi li be-zo* — a man who says "marry me with this date, marry me with this date," separately, offering each date with its own declaration. *Im yesh be-achas mehen shaveh perutah mekudeshet* — if any single one of them is worth a *perutah*, she is betrothed; *ve-im lav einah mekudeshet* — and if not, she is not, even if their combined value reaches a *perutah*. Since each statement of *hiskadshi* constitutes a separate act, the dates do not combine.

### Combined Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ״ אִם יֵשׁ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בְּכוּלָּן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת
>
> **Translation:** But if he said: Be betrothed to me **with this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one, then even **if all of them** together **are worth one *peruta* she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed.**

If instead he said *b'zo u-v'zo u-v'zo* — "with this and this and this" — combining everything into one declaration, then *im yesh shaveh perutah be-chulan mekudeshet*, if together they are worth a *perutah*, she is betrothed; *ve-im lav einah mekudeshet*, and if not, she is not. Here the single composite statement ties all the dates into one act of *kiddushin*, and their values aggregate accordingly.

### Eating While Given

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אוֹכֶלֶת רִאשׁוֹנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה
>
> **Translation:** If he gave her dates with the intention of betrothing her with them, and **she was eating** them **one by one** as she received them, **she is not betrothed unless one of them is worth one *peruta*.**

The *mishna* then adds: *haysah ochelet rishonah rishonah* — if she was eating each date as he placed it before her — *ve-einah mekudeshet ela im ken yesh be-achas mehen shaveh perutah* — the combined-statement rule does not apply, and one of the dates by itself must be worth a *perutah*. Even in the composite-statement case, the fact that she is consuming each date as it is offered changes the legal analysis.

### Why Eating Matters

The *gemara* asks: on which clause of the *mishna* does this final condition apply? And why does the act of eating matter at all?

### Textual Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַרֵישָׁא, מַאי אִירְיָא אוֹכֶלֶת? אֲפִילּוּ מִנְחַת נָמֵי, דְּהָא ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בָּזוֹ״ קָאָמַר! אֶלָּא אַסֵּיפָא
>
> **Translation:** The mishna subsequently states that if she was eating the dates one by one, she is betrothed only if one of them is worth one *peruta*. The Gemara asks: **To which** clause of the mishna is the case of the woman eating the dates one by one referring? **If we say** it **is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, when he betrothed her separately with each date, **why** would the mishna **specifically** state that **she ate** them? **Even** if **she placed** them down without eating them the same *halakha* would **also** apply, **as he said: Be betrothed to me with this** date, so each date must be worth one *peruta*. **Rather,** it must be referring **to the latter clause,** when he said that he is betrothing her with all the dates.

*Ilei'ma areisha* — if the clause about eating applies to the first case, where he says *hiskadshi* separately before each date, then the condition seems redundant: even without the eating, each date must individually be worth a *perutah*, since the statements were never combined. Why would eating change or add anything? The *gemara* therefore proposes *ela aseifa* — the clause applies to the final case of the combined statement. But even then, the question presses: if she was eating the dates as he gave them, why can she not be betrothed on the basis of their combined value?

## Milveh Concept

### Rabba & Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לְעוֹלָם אַרֵישָׁא, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא מַנַּחַת, דְּאִי אִיכָּא שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא. אֲבָל אוֹכֶלֶת, הוֹאִיל וּמִיקָּרְבָא הֲנָיָיתַהּ, אֵימָא גָּמְרָה וּמַקְנְיָא נַפְשַׁהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel both said: Actually,** the case of the woman eating the dates one by one **is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, where one betrothed the woman with each date separately, **and it is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** The mishna stated, for stylistic reasons, a *halakha* that it did not need to state, and should be understood as follows: **It is not necessary** to state the *halakha* in the case of a woman who **placed** the dates down, **that if** one of them **is worth one *peruta*, yes,** she is betrothed, and **if not,** she is **not** betrothed. **But** in a case where she was **eating** them, one might think that **since her benefit is immediate, say that she has decided to transfer herself** to him by means of that date, although it is worth less than one *peruta*. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that she is nevertheless not betrothed.

Rabba and Shmuel both hold *le'olam areisha* — the clause about eating applies to the first case, where each date carries a separate *hiskadshi* declaration. The apparent redundancy is resolved as a *lo miba'ya* construction.

### Objective Value

The *gemara* explains: it goes without saying that when she leaves the dates in front of her — *manacha* — and one of them is worth a *perutah*, she is betrothed, and if not, she is not. But one might have thought that when she is eating each date eagerly as it is placed before her — *ho'il u-mekarva hana'asa*, since she derives immediate pleasure from it — perhaps even a date worth less than a *perutah* suffices, on the theory that she has demonstrated by her conduct that she values it at *perutah* or more. *Ka mashma lan* — the *mishna* teaches that this is not so; the requirement remains an objective valuation of *shaveh perutah*. Tosafot notes that this is a rather weak *chidush*, since an earlier passage in the *gemara* had already established clearly that subjective satisfaction cannot substitute for the minimum objective value, but the answer stands.

### Rabbi Ami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אַסֵּיפָא, וּמַאי ״עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said** a different explanation: **Actually,** the case of the woman eating the dates one by one **is referring to the latter clause** of the mishna, where he betrothed her by giving her all of the dates. **And what** is the meaning of: **Unless one of them is worth one *peruta*?** It means: **Unless the last** of them **is worth one *peruta*.**

Rabbi Ami offers a different reading: *le'olam seifa* — the eating clause does apply to the last case, the combined statement. And his explanation resolves the structural difficulty by reinterpreting the phrase *im yesh be-achas mehen shaveh perutah*: it does not mean that any one of the dates needs to be worth a *perutah*, as the plain reading would suggest. Rather, it means *ad she-yavo be-achrona shaveh perutah* — the last date given must itself be worth a *perutah*.

### Act Completion

The logic is as follows. When he says "this and this and this," the act of *kiddushin* is not complete until the final word of the declaration. At the moment the declaration is complete, all the dates he gave beforehand have already been consumed. Those earlier dates no longer exist in any concrete form; they are, at best, outstanding obligations — money she would owe him if the *kiddushin* were to fail. Something given as *kesef kiddushin* that would have to be returned if the *kiddushin* does not take effect functions as a loan, a *milveh*, not as a present object. And one cannot effect *kiddushin* with a *milveh*. The only item that is concretely present and not yet consumed at the moment the declaration is finished is the last date. Therefore, only if that final date is worth a *perutah* can the *kiddushin* take effect.

### Milveh Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה, דַּעְתַּהּ אַפְּרוּטָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Conclude three** conclusions **from** this statement **of Rabbi Ami: Conclude from it** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed,** since she is not betrothed with a date that she has already eaten. **And conclude from it** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **by** forgiving **a loan and** giving her **one *peruta*, her mind** is focused **on the *peruta*** although the debt is far larger, and she will be betrothed.

*Amar Rava: shema mina mi-de-Rabbi Ami tlat.* From Rabbi Ami's reading of the *mishna*, Rava draws three inferences. The first: *shema mina ha-mekadesh be-milveh eina mekudeshet* — since the *mishna*'s analysis treats the eaten dates as *milveh* and therefore ineffective for *kiddushin*, we learn that marrying with a *milveh* does not work. This will be the subject of extended subsequent *gemara*.

### Milveh + Perutah

The second inference: *shema mina ha-mekadesh be-milveh u-perutah da'ata a-perutah*. Suppose a man says to a woman: "I marry you with the hundred dollars you owe me — keep that debt — and with this penny." The *milveh* component cannot serve as *kesef kiddushin*; the question is whether the penny alone can carry the act. One might argue that since he invoked the *milveh* as part of the package and that part is invalid, the entire declaration is compromised. Alternatively, the *milveh* drops out as irrelevant, and the concrete *perutah* before her does the work — provided that both parties are focused on it. The *gemara* phrases this as *da'ata a-perutah* (and in parallel passages *da'ateih*), indicating that the mental focus of both parties on the concrete *perutah* is what allows the *kiddushin* to function despite the invalid *milveh* component.

### Milveh vs Matana

The third inference concerns the status of the money in the first place. *Maot be-alma chozrin* — when *kesef kiddushin* is given and the *kiddushin* fails to take effect, the money must ordinarily be returned. This is the premise of calling the early consumed dates a *milveh*: they would need to be returned if the *kiddushin* did not go through. Tosafot raises the question of whether this premise is itself self-evident. What if we were to say instead that *kesef kiddushin* is a *matana* — a gift — regardless of whether the *kiddushin* takes effect? If the eaten dates were hers to keep in any event, we would not frame them as a debt; we might rather say she owns them outright and the subsequent dates are simply part of one ongoing *kinyan*. Tosafot argues that if the money were indeed a *matana*, the *mishna* itself would be inexplicable: there would then be no reason why the last date specifically must be worth a *perutah*, since the aggregate of everything she received — all hers in any event — could simply combine to meet the threshold. The fact that the *mishna* requires the last date to carry a *perutah* only makes sense if the earlier dates are a *milveh* and thus disqualified from serving as *kesef kiddushin*. Rava's third inference is therefore that the premise of *maot chozrin* — money given for *kiddushin* must be returned if the *kiddushin* fails — is the correct one.

## Sister Marriage Case

### Setup

The *gemara* now presents a vivid illustration of these principles. *Ha-mekadesh achoto* — a man who attempts to betroth his own sister, saying *harei at mekudeshet li be-taba'at zo*. The *kiddushin* is of course void. The question is what happens to the ring.

### Rava: Return It

Rava amar: maot chozrin* — she must return the ring; it is a *milveh*, and he retains legal title. *Ve-gamar ve-nasan le-shum pikadon* — and the legal framing of what occurred is that he gave it to her as a deposit, a *pikadon*, held on his behalf.

### Why Pikadon

But if that is what he intended, why not simply say outright: "hold this for me"? *Savar lo mekablah* — she would refuse. By framing it as *kiddushin*, even a transparently void *kiddushin*, the ring passes into her hand. The practical purpose is to get the object into her possession while preserving his legal ownership, rather than seeking her cooperation in an ordinary bailment.

### Shmuel: Gift

*U-Shmuel savar: maot matana.* Shmuel holds the ring is a gift. *Adam yodeya she-ein kiddushin tofsin be-achoto* — he obviously knew that *kiddushin* cannot take effect with one's own sister, *ve-gamar ve-nasan le-shum matana* — and so his giving of the ring, whatever form it took, was in substance a gift. Again the *gemara* asks: why not simply say "this is a gift"? *Savar kesifa lei milsa* — it would be embarrassing for her to receive it framed as a gift, so the form of *kiddushin* provides a face-saving vehicle for the transfer.

### Ravina's Question

Ravina challenges the underlying logic of this entire dispute. If all parties understand that the *kiddushin* is void — and indeed everyone knows that *kiddushin* with one's sister is invalid — then the debate between *milveh* and *matana* might seem academic. Both Rava and Shmuel agree that no valid *kiddushin* has occurred; the only question is the legal characterization of the transfer itself.

## Hallah Case

### Flour Not Hallah

The *gemara* introduces a parallel case. *Ha-mafrish chalato kemach* — one who separates *challah* while the grain product is still flour, before it has become dough. *Challah* can only be designated from dough, not from flour; the separation is therefore legally ineffective, *eino challah*.

### Kohen Keeping

*Ve-gezel be-yad kohen* — if the flour was nonetheless given to a *kohen*, and the *kohen* retained it, that would constitute theft. This is the position of the *gemara*. The challenge to Shmuel is immediate: *naima adam yodeya she-ein mafrishin challah kemach* — according to Shmuel's principle, since everyone knows that *challah* cannot be taken from flour, the transaction should be read as a *matana* and the *kohen* should be entitled to keep the flour.

### Confusion Risk

The *gemara* responds: *shani hasam de-nafik churba mineih* — that case is different because permitting the *kohen* to keep the flour would cause serious harm. If the *kohen* retains it, he will conclude that the separation was in fact valid *challah*.

### Partial Knowledge

But this concern seems to contradict itself: if everyone knows you cannot take *challah* from flour, why would the *kohen* draw a false conclusion? The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between knowing a rule and understanding its reason. *Yada de-ein mafrishin challah kemach ve-eino yodea tama mai* — people know that one is not supposed to separate *challah* from flour, but they do not necessarily know *why*. They may think the reason is only *tircha de-kohen* — an inconvenience to the *kohen*, who would have to convert the flour to dough — and that perhaps the separation is valid *be-di'eved*. If the *kohen* is then permitted to keep the flour, they will infer that the separation did take effect retroactively.

### Force Return

To prevent this confusion, the flour must be returned. Allowing the *kohen* to retain it would implicitly signal that the *challah* designation was valid, leading people to draw incorrect halachic conclusions.

### Hydroponics Parallel

The *gemara* then asks why the same concern does not prevent a comparable ruling in the case of *terumah min ha-nakuv al she-eino nakuv* — terumah taken from produce grown in a perforated pot on behalf of produce grown in an unperforated pot. Produce grown without a perforation in the pot is not biblically obligated in *terumah*, so the designation is technically invalid. Nevertheless, the *halacha* there is that the *kohen* may keep the produce, though he may not eat it until *terumah* has been properly separated from another source: *de-tehavei terumah ve-lo tochal ad she-yotzi aleha terumah u-ma'aser mi-makom acher*. Why does this not create the same confusion?

### One vs Two Vessels

*Bitrei mane tsaytei, be-chad mana lo tsaytei* — with two vessels, people will listen and understand; with one vessel, they will not. In the hydroponics case, there are two distinct containers involved — the perforated and unperforated pots — and the distinction between them is tangible enough that people can comprehend why the *terumah* taken from one does not cover the other. Knowing that two vessels were involved, they will accept the teaching that the *kohen* must separate again and will understand that the original designation was somehow incomplete. By contrast, in the flour case there is only one vessel and one substance throughout; telling the *kohen* that he may keep the flour while insisting that it is not really *challah* will not register. People will simply take the *kohen*'s possession as confirmation that the separation was valid. Therefore, in the flour case, the only way to prevent the false inference is to demand return of the flour entirely.
