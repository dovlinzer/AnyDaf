# Bava Kamma 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinui & Gezeila Framewo…

### Aged Animals & Slaves

The daf begins mid-*mishna* on 97b, continuing the theme of *kinyanei gezeila*. The *mishna* states: *gazal beheima vehizkinah* — one who steals an animal and it grows old — or similarly *avadim vehizkinu*, slaves who grow old in the thief's possession — *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila*, he pays according to the value at the time of the theft.

The conceptual difficulty here is worth pausing over. The *mishna* invokes the principle that one pays based on the original value, but it does not explicitly state *which* of two possible legal frameworks underlies this rule. One possibility is *shinui koneh*: the object has changed so fundamentally that the thief has acquired it, and therefore pays its original value as the price of that acquisition. But there is a second, distinct possibility that the *mishna* leaves unexplored — namely, that the animal or slave has changed enough that the thief *cannot* say *harei shelcha lefanecha* ("here is your object, unchanged"), and yet has not changed so drastically that the thief has acquired it outright. In that intermediate case, the object would be returned *and* the thief would pay the difference in value.

This is a logically coherent middle category. When a *mishna* says *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila*, that formulation could mean either (a) the thief owns the object and pays its original value, or (b) the thief returns the object and pays the difference. The *gemara* has not yet resolved this ambiguity explicitly, and it is worth keeping this conceptual space in mind as the sugya develops.

### Three Response Categories

It helps to lay out the full spectrum of possible responses to theft, as the *mishna* and *gemara* together implicitly define three positions along a continuum.

At one extreme: *harei shelcha lefanecha* — the thief returns the object and owes nothing beyond the return, because the object is effectively the same as when it was stolen. The change, if any, is too minor or too invisible for the owner to demand compensation.

At the other extreme: *nasah shinui b'yado u-kna'o* — the object has transformed so significantly that the thief has acquired it through *shinui*. He no longer returns the object; he pays the owner the original value and keeps what is now, in effect, his own object.

In the middle — and this is the category the *mishna* may be describing — there is a case where the change is real and noticeable enough that the thief cannot simply say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, but is *not* drastic enough to constitute *shinui koneh*. The thief returns the object and pays the difference. The phrase *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila* in the *mishna* is compatible with this reading: return the object and compensate the owner for the diminution in value.

This threefold structure — *harei shelcha lefanecha*, intermediate payment-plus-return, and full *shinui koneh* — is what the *mishna* cases must be evaluated against. What is clear is that a slave who has grown old, or an animal that has aged, cannot be returned with a breezy *harei shelcha lefanecha*. What is less clear is whether these cases cross the threshold into full *shinui koneh*, or whether they occupy the middle ground.

### Visible Damage Cases

The *mishna* continues with cases that appear far more dramatic: *gazal matbe'a venistak* — a stolen coin that developed a crack; *peiros vehirkivu* — fruit that rotted; *yayin vehechimitz* — wine that turned to vinegar. In all these cases, *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila*, the thief pays based on the original value at the time of the theft.

These cases seem to involve objects that have changed so thoroughly that the thief surely cannot say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. Cracked coins, rotted fruit, and vinegared wine are categorically different from what was taken. Whether these constitute *shinui koneh* — such that the thief has outright acquired the damaged item — or whether they fall into the intermediate category of return-plus-compensation, is again left implicit. The *mishna*'s language is neutral on that point.

### Non-Visible Damage

The *mishna* then pivots to a strikingly different set of cases: *matbe'a venifsal* — a coin that was not physically cracked but was invalidated by the government; *truma venitmeis* — *teruma* that became *tamei*; *chametz she'avar alav hapesach* — leavened grain that passed through *Pesach* in the thief's possession, rendering it rabbinically worthless; *beheima sheni'avda bah aveira* — an animal used for a sexual transgression, which thereby becomes unfit for the altar; and *shor haniskal* — an ox condemned to stoning. In all these cases, the *mishna* rules *omer lo harei shelcha lefanecha* — the thief simply returns the object and is exempt from further payment.

This is a remarkable ruling. These objects may be worth almost nothing — or in the case of *chametz* after *Pesach*, effectively nothing at all by rabbinic law. And yet, since the damage is not visible, since there is no physical change perceptible to the senses, the thief may return the object and declare, "here is your thing." The owner receives back something essentially worthless, and the thief bears no further obligation. This is a significant *chiddush*.

The unifying principle of this second list is *hezek she'eino nikar* — damage that is not visible. The owner cannot point to any perceptible physical difference in what has been returned. The legal status has changed — the coin is invalid, the *teruma* is *tamei*, the animal is disqualified — but none of these changes manifest in the object's physical appearance.

One might note that a government-invalidated coin still has its raw metal value and could theoretically be melted down, and an animal unfit for the altar is not worth nothing. These are genuinely cases of diminished but nonzero value. The conceptual question of what makes non-visible damage categorically different from visible damage will return later in the sugya.

## Permanent Change Criteri…

### Rav Pappa's Weakening

The *gemara* opens this section with Rav Pappa's interpretive move. When the *mishna* says *hizkinah* — that the animal grew old — it does not mean literally that the animal became decrepit and useless. Rather, Rav Pappa says *afilu kichashah* — even if it merely became weaker. The *gemara* immediately pushes back: but our *mishna* says *hizkinah*, old age, which implies a significant degree of change. How can mere weakness trigger the same rule?

Rav Pappa's answer: *kichashah ke'ein hizkinah* — a weakness that resembles old age in the relevant sense, meaning *dilo hadra bari* — that it will not recover. What matters is not the magnitude of the change, but its permanence. A temporary injury or a temporary weakness would perhaps allow the thief to return the object and say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — wait, it will recover. But a permanent change, even a subtle one, crosses the threshold. The thief cannot say the object is effectively the same if it has been permanently diminished, even if that diminishment is modest. This is *dilo hadra bari* as the operative criterion: permanent, non-reversible change.

### Lamb to Ram Example

Mar Keshisha, the son of Rav Chisda, cited to Rav Ashi a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *afilu ganav taleh ve-na'asah ayil, egel ve-na'asah shor, nasah shinui beyado u-kna'o* — even if one stole a lamb and it became a ram, a calf and it became an ox, this constitutes a *shinui* and the thief acquires it. The consequence: *tevacho u-mechoro, shelo hu tovei'ach shelo hu mocher* — if he subsequently slaughtered or sold it, he is slaughtering and selling his own property, and does not incur the four- and fivefold penalty of *geneivah*.

This is the same teaching we have encountered earlier in the *masechet*, now being introduced here in the context of the *shinui* discussion. It is worth observing that the lamb-to-ram transformation is in some respects more dramatic than aging, in that it involves a categorical change in the animal's identity — the creature now has a different name, *shinui hashem* — and yet it is also more natural, in that no human action caused it. Both features together constitute sufficient *shinui*: a qualitative, categorical change that is permanent, even though it came about through the passage of time rather than through the thief's physical act.

It is also worth noting that a lamb becoming a ram does not make the animal worthless — if anything, it may have become more valuable. This demonstrates that *shinui* for purposes of *koneh* does not require the object to have deteriorated. The category is about qualitative transformation, not about loss of value.

### Source Clarification

Rav Ashi replied to Mar Keshisha: *lav amina lach, lo tachlif gavrei* — have I not told you not to mix up the names? The teaching about the lamb and the ram was not said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, but in the name of Rav Ila. This is a correction of attribution, not of substance.

### Middle Category Debate

Returning to the conceptual question raised by the earlier *mishna* cases: when the *mishna* says *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila* — whether in the case of an aging animal, a weakened animal, or a cracked coin — does this implicitly mean the thief has *acquired* the object through *shinui*, or does it mean that the object is returned and the difference is paid?

The *gemara* has generally treated *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila* as implying *shinui koneh*, but this has never been stated explicitly. The case of the lamb becoming a ram *did* explicitly use the language *nasah shinui beyado u-kna'o* — he acquired it. But the cases of aging and weakening simply say to pay based on the original value, without specifying whether the thief retains the object or returns it.

This is precisely the middle ground that remains unclarified: a case where the change is real and permanent enough that the thief cannot say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, but where the *gemara* has not explicitly said the thief is *koneh* the object. The resolution lies somewhere between those two poles. The *mishna*'s *meshalem k'sha'as hagezeila* is consistent with either interpretation — pay the original amount because you own it now, or return it and pay the difference — and the *gemara* has not forced a choice between them.

## Slaves as Karka Debate

### Rabbi Meir on Slaves

The *mishna* records Rabbi Meir's position: *ba'avadim omer lo harei shelcha lefanecha* — with respect to slaves, the thief simply returns the slave and owes nothing more. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that slaves are like *karka*, real property, and the laws of *gezeila* do not apply to land. Since there is no act of *gezeila* with respect to slaves, there is no liability arising from it; the thief simply returns the slave and is done. Whatever has happened to the slave in the interim — including aging — generates no additional obligation.

### Rav's Text Reversal

The *gemara* reports that Rav bar Avdimi said in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Meir* — we rule like Rabbi Meir, that slaves are like *karka*. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: does Rav simply abandon the Rabbis and rule with a *da'as yachid*? Rav's reasoning, the *gemara* explains, is that he found a *baraita* in which the positions were reversed — a *baraita* in which it was the Rabbis who said that slaves are like *karka* and Rabbi Meir who said they are not.

The *gemara* presses further: if there is a conflict between the *mishna* and the *baraita*, why follow the *baraita* and adjust the *mishna* rather than follow the *mishna* and adjust the *baraita*? The answer: Rav had not one but two *baraitot* in which the positions were reversed. *Chada mikamei tartei* — one text against two, he reversed the *mishna* in light of two consistent external sources. When Rav says *halacha k'Rabbi Meir*, he is saying: according to your reading of the *mishna*, the position I am endorsing belongs to Rabbi Meir; but in truth, based on my reading, it is actually the Rabbis' position, and we rule that slaves are like *karka*.

### Birth During Sale Case

The second *baraita* that drove Rav to reverse the positions reads as follows: *ha-machlif parah b'chamor ve-yaldah* — one who swaps a cow for a donkey, and the cow gave birth during the process; or similarly *ha-mocher shifchato ve-yaldah* — one who sold his female slave, and she gave birth during the sale. The *baraita* is addressing a situation where possession was transferred through *chalipin* (in the case of the cow) or through money (in the case of the slave), and a birth occurred somewhere in the interval — but it is unclear whether it occurred before or after the transfer took effect.

The *baraita* sets out the legal analysis: if the buyer claims definitively that the birth occurred after he took possession and the seller is uncertain, the buyer wins — *bari ve-shema, bari adif*. If both parties are uncertain, they divide. If both claim definitively, the seller must take an oath: *yishava ha-mocher she-bi-reshuto yaldah* — the seller swears that the birth occurred while it was still in his possession and thereby avoids paying the calf or baby. This is consistent with the rule that in Torah law, oaths get the oath-taker out of payment rather than entitle him to collect.

This is Rabbi Meir's position. The Rabbis, by contrast, say: *ein nishba'in lo al ha-avadim ve-lo al ha-karka'ot* — there is no oath imposed in cases involving slaves or land. Why? Because slaves are like *karka*, and the Torah's oath mechanism does not apply to real property.

The reversal is now plain: in this *baraita*, Rabbi Meir holds that you *can* take an oath about slaves — meaning slaves are *not* like *karka* — while the Rabbis hold that you *cannot* — meaning slaves *are* like *karka*. In our *mishna*, the same positions appear with the names switched. Rav, armed with two *baraitot* presenting the reversed attribution, concluded that the *mishna*'s version of the positions is a scribal error and that the Rabbis are truly the ones who equate slaves with *karka*. Hence his ruling: *halacha k'Rabbi Meir* — meaning, according to the *mishna*'s erroneous labeling, we rule like "Rabbi Meir," but in substance we rule like the Rabbis.

It is worth noting, as one sharp observer pointed out during the discussion, that this reversal of names occurs immediately after Rav Ashi corrected Mar Keshisha for mixing up attributions in the preceding *sugya* — a striking coincidence of theme.

### Kinyan Agreement

Tosafot raises a penetrating question here. The *baraita* uses two different acquisition mechanisms: *chalipin* for the cow and *kesef* for the slave. One might ask why the *baraita* needed to shift from *chalipin* to *kesef* when moving from the animal to the slave case. The answer is that slaves cannot be acquired through *meshicha* (pulling) or *hagbahah* (lifting), as movable chattel typically is. Slaves are acquired through *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah* — the same modes used for *karka*.

This raises a deeper question: if everybody in the *baraita* — both Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis — treats the slave as being acquired through the same modes as *karka*, does that mean there is universal agreement that slaves are like *karka* for purposes of *kinyan*, even while they debate whether slaves are like *karka* for purposes of *gezeila* and *shevua*? Tosafot concludes that the answer is yes, and this is not contradictory. The categorization of slaves as *karka* is not necessarily a single all-or-nothing determination. For *kinyan*, perhaps everyone agrees that slaves follow the rules of real property. For *gezeila* and *shevua*, the exclusion is derived from specific *drashos* — *klal u'ferat u'klal* — which create a more formal, categorical exclusion. When the exclusion is based on formal derivation rather than on logic, we apply it consistently: if slaves fall into the category, they fall in across the board. If they do not, they do not. But this is distinct from the *kinyan* question, where the agreement may be universal regardless of the *gezeila* and *shevua* debate.

## Slave Seizure & Use

### Rav's Exemption

The *gemara* now introduces a ruling of Rav cited by Rav Daniel bar Rav Katina: *hatokef b'avdo shel chaveiro v'asa bo melakha patur* — one who seizes his friend's slave and uses him to perform labor is exempt from payment. This ruling sets up the central tension of the entire passage.

### Nigzal vs. Karka

The exemption makes immediate sense if a slave is treated like a stolen animal — that is, if the slave is *nigzal*. In that case, the principle of *kinyanei gezeila* applies: by seizing physical possession of the object, the *gazlan* acquires a kind of de facto quasi-ownership, and as a result, when he returns the object, he owes nothing for its use. This is the counterintuitive consequence of *gezeila* law: the act of theft, paradoxically, generates a form of entitlement to use the stolen object without paying. Yesterday's discussion illustrated this with the case of a stolen cow used to plow a field.

But the question is exactly the opposite of what one might expect. If Rav holds that slaves are like *karka* — as he apparently does — then a slave is *not nigzal*. The person who seizes the slave has not committed an act of *gezeila*. He is simply using someone else's property without permission, much like someone who takes up residence in another's house without authorization. In the house case, the relevant framework is *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser* versus *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser*. If the house was posted for rental and the unauthorized occupant displaced paying tenants, it is *zeh chaser* and payment is required. If the house was not available for rental, the question is more complex — there may be minimal damage counterbalanced by benefit to the property from being occupied. But the question is: why is the slave-seizer *patur*?

### She'lo B'Shas Melakha

The *gemara* answers: *she'lo b'shas melakha* — the exemption applies specifically when the slave was not being used for labor at the time of the seizure. This is a case of *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser*. If the slave was on his break, not engaged in any productive work for his master, the master has lost nothing — it is a case where the taker benefited but the owner was not harmed. Under *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser*, no payment is owed.

This reframes Rav's ruling significantly. Rav is not making a statement about slaves being like stolen chattel. He is making a statement about when the use of an idle slave causes no loss — which is the same principle that would apply if one lived, without permission, in a friend's house that was not available for rent. The *chiddush* of the ruling is thus not that slaves are like *karka* and the stranger principle somehow applies, but rather that when the slave is not working, the master's loss is zero, and so is the obligation to pay.

### Debtors' Slaves Case

The *gemara* now describes a practice in the household of Rav Yosef bar Chama: they would seize the slaves of people who owed them money — *d'masik behu zuzei* — and have those slaves perform labor for them. This goes beyond the theoretical case. It is a regular practice of taking the slaves of one's debtors and putting them to work.

### Worth Their Bread

Rabba, Rav Yosef bar Chama's son, objected: what is the basis for this practice? Rav Yosef bar Chama replied by citing Rav Nachman's statement: *avda nahom karseh lo shavei* — a slave is not worth the bread in his stomach. The attitude here is stark: slaves are inherently lazy, so any opportunity to put them to work is actually welcome. This is offered as the justification for why seizing and using someone's slave is not a *chaser* — the master presumably *wants* his slaves to be kept busy and productive rather than idle.

### Defense of Use

Rabba pressed further: Rav Nachman said that about a specific slave — Daru, who was *de'markid be'kubeih*, who danced about idly in the tavern. That contemptuous remark was about one particularly worthless slave, not a blanket statement about all slaves. Most slaves do real work. On what basis, then, does one have the right to seize another person's slave?

Rav Yosef bar Chama responded: *ana ke'Rav Daniel sevirah li* — I hold like Rav Daniel. Rav Daniel bar Rav Katina said in the name of Rav: *hatokef b'avdo shel chaveiro v'asa bo melakha, patur*. You are exempt. And the reason you are exempt, if slaves are like *karka*, must be that this is a case of *zeh lo chaser* — the master actually benefits from having his slave kept busy. *Nicha lei d'lo listerei avdei* — it is good for the master that his slave does not become lazy. Therefore, not only is payment not owed after the fact; Rav Yosef bar Chama argued that it is permissible to do this *lechatchilah*.

### Ribbis Concern

The *gemara* objects: even if all of that is true in a normal case — even if using someone's idle slave is *zeh lo chaser* and permissible — it does not apply here. *Keivan d'masik behu zuzei nechzei ke'ribbis* — since these people owe you money, seizing their slaves and enjoying their labor looks like interest. You are extracting benefit from people who owe you money. Even if the benefit would ordinarily be something you are entitled to for free — even if technically there is no *chaser* — the optics of a creditor taking something of value from a debtor create the appearance of usury.

### Debt Exception

This is precisely the principle stated by Rav Yosef bar Minyumi in the name of Rav Nachman: *af al pi she'amru hadar b'chatzer chaveiro shelo mi-da'ato eino tzarich l'haalos lo sachar* — even though we rule that one who lives in a friend's yard without permission owes no rental fee in a standard *zeh lo chaser* case — *halvehu ve-dar b'chatzaro, tzarich l'haalos lo sachar* — if he happened to borrow money from that friend and is now living in the friend's yard, he *does* owe rent. The normal exemption is suspended because the presence of a debt changes the legal and moral character of the arrangement entirely.

Rav Yosef bar Chama accepted this: *hadari bi* — I retract. He acknowledged that the practice, however defensible in the abstract, was impermissible when applied to his own debtors.

The broader conceptual point the *gemara* has now clarified is worth summarizing. By *metaltelin* — movable chattel — if one steals a cow and uses it, one pays nothing for the use because *kinyanei gezeila* create a kind of de facto ownership in the *gazlan*. By *karka* and by slaves (who are like *karka*) — the property is not *nigzal*, and one who occupies or uses it is not a *gazlan*. In such a case, the question is *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser* or *zeh lo chaser*. If *chaser*, one pays; if *lo chaser*, one does not. For slaves, the *gemara* posits that the standard case is *lo chaser* — the master is taken to prefer his slave remain active — and therefore the slave-user does not pay. But when the user is a creditor of the slave-owner, the entire analysis collapses: even a technically free benefit becomes forbidden because it resembles *ribbis*.

## Boat Seizure Case

### Rav: Sechar or Pachis

The *gemara* now turns to a parallel case: *hatokef sefinato shel chaveiro v'asa bah melakha* — seizing a friend's boat, which is *metaltelin*, and using it for labor. Here the person who seized the boat *is* a *gazlan* — it is movable chattel, not *karka*. Rav rules: *rotzeh sechar notel, rotzeh pachas notel* — the owner has a choice, he can collect either the rental fee or the depreciation in the boat's value. The owner would naturally take whichever is higher.

The question is: why should the owner be able to collect a rental fee? If this is a case of *gezeila*, the *gazlan* should have de facto rights to the object — the standard principle should be that the *gazlan* returns the object and owes nothing for use. Why is there an option to collect *sechar*?

### Shmuel: Pachis Only

Shmuel disagrees: *eino notel ela pachas* — the owner collects only the depreciation, not the rental fee. This is consistent with the *gazlan* principle: the *gazlan* is liable for damage to the object but not for its rental value during the period of unauthorized use.

### Rental Status Matters

Rav Pappa resolves the dispute: *lo pligi* — Rav and Shmuel do not actually disagree. Their rulings apply to different cases. *Ha de'avida le-agra, ha de-lo avida le-agra* — one rule applies when the boat is the type that is regularly rented out, the other when it is not. If the boat is normally available for hire, the owner can collect the rental fee; if it is just a private boat sitting in someone's yard, only depreciation is owed.

### Intent & Implicit Terms

The *gemara* refines this further. Even assuming the boat is one that is normally rented out, the framework shifts based on the intent of the person who took it. *Ha de-nachis lah ada'ata de-igra, ha de-nachis lah ada'ata de-gazlanuta* — if the person took the boat with the understanding that he was borrowing it and would return it, not stealing it, then because he is not positioning himself as a *gazlan*, he does not acquire the de facto rights of a *gazlan*. He is, in effect, an unauthorized renter. Since the boat is normally available for rental, he is implicitly agreeing to rental terms, and the owner collects *sechar*.

But if the person took it with the intent of a *gazlan* — genuinely seizing it — then the *kinyan gezeila* analysis applies, and paradoxically the owner collects only depreciation, not rent. The greater the wrongdoing, the less the *gazlan* owes for the use. This is the counterintuitive irony the *gemara* has been tracking throughout: the more culpable the taking, the greater the de facto rights it generates, and therefore the less one owes for the use of the object.

## Visible vs. Invisible

### Crack vs. Invalidation

The *gemara* revisits the distinction between a physically cracked coin and a coin that has been invalidated by government decree. Rav Huna states: *nistak nistak mamash* — a crack means a literal physical crack, and in that case the thief cannot return the coin and declare *harei shelcha lefanecha*. But *nifsal* — invalidation — specifically means *psalto malchus*: the government has withdrawn it from circulation. In *that* case, the thief can return the coin.

### Yehuda's Challenge

Rav Yehuda challenges this: is government invalidation really different from a physical crack? If the entire Roman Empire has declared these coins invalid, then functionally, practically, this coin is worthless — just as worthless as one with a physical crack. Anyone who encounters it will treat it as garbage. Should we not say that the status has changed so completely that it is equivalent to visible physical damage, such that the thief cannot return it?

Rav Yehuda therefore proposes a different distinction for what *nifsal* means when the thief *can* say *harei shelcha lefanecha*: it must be a case of *psalto medina* — invalidated in one region only, but still usable in another region of the empire. In that case the coin retains genuine value, even if not locally. But if the entire government has invalidated it everywhere, that is treated like a crack.

### Chisda's Question

Rav Chisda addresses a question to Rav Huna: according to Rav Huna, who says that even full empire-wide invalidation still permits the thief to say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, how is this different from the cases of rotten fruit and vinegared wine, where the *mishna* rules that the thief must pay the original value? After all, rotted fruit and vinegared wine are similarly "invalidated" — they have become something categorically different, something of far diminished or zero practical value. Why should those cases require payment while a government-invalidated coin does not?

### Sense Perception Test

Rav Huna's response: *hasam nishtana ta'amo ve-reicho* — there, with the rotten fruit and the vinegared wine, the taste and smell have changed. These are perceptible, sensory differences. You can taste that the wine is vinegar; you can smell that the fruit is rotten. The physical object itself has transformed in ways detectable by the senses. A government-invalidated coin, by contrast, *lo nishtana* — has not physically changed at all. It looks, feels, weighs, and smells exactly the same. The change is entirely in the legal and social status assigned to it by external decree, not in the coin itself.

### Rava on Teruma

Rava then challenges Rav Yehuda from the opposite direction. According to Rav Yehuda, who says that empire-wide invalidation *is* like a physical crack and the thief cannot return the coin — how does Rav Yehuda distinguish this from *teruma* that became *tamei* or *chametz* that passed through *Pesach*? Those cases also involve complete functional invalidation: *tamei teruma* cannot be eaten, and *chametz* after *Pesach* has no permitted use (at least rabbinically). Yet the *mishna* rules in those cases that the thief says *harei shelcha lefanecha*. Why should an empire-wide coin invalidation be treated differently?

### Minkar Heizika

The answer resolves the entire set of disputes and formulates the governing principle: *hasam lo minkar heizika, hacha minkar heizika* — there, with *tamei teruma* and *chametz*, the damage is not noticeable; here, with empire-wide coin invalidation, the damage *is* noticeable.

This is the key conceptual distinction. The category of damage that triggers the inability to say *harei shelcha lefanecha* is not simply physical change, nor is it simply functional invalidity. It is *minkar heizika* — damage that is perceptible, that registers to observers as a change. Rotten fruit passes the test because taste and smell betray the change. Government-invalidated coins, in Rav Yehuda's view, pass the test because everyone who encounters the coin immediately recognizes it as worthless — even without any physical alteration. The social and legal consensus that this is no longer money is itself a form of perceptible change, even if the physical object is untouched.

*Tamei teruma* and *chametz*, by contrast, are cases of *lo minkar heizika* — the damage is invisible. You cannot look at *tamei teruma* and tell it is *tamei*; you cannot look at *chametz* left over from *Pesach* and distinguish it from ordinary grain. In those cases, the thief may return the object and invoke *harei shelcha lefanecha*, because there is no perceptible difference visible to the observer.

## Old Currency Repayment

### Loan Setup

The *gemara* now presents a new case: *ha-malveh chaveiro al ha-matbe'a ve-nifsal ha-matbe'a* — one who lends his friend money (or according to Rashi, enters into a commercial transaction or investment where repayment is owed), and subsequently the coins that were the currency at the time of the transaction are invalidated by the government. When repayment falls due, must the borrower pay with currently valid currency, or may he pay with the old invalidated coins?

### Rav: Current Currency

Rav rules: *nosein lo matbe'a ha-yotzei be-osah sha'ah* — the debtor must give the creditor the currency that is in circulation *now*, at the time of payment. When you owe someone ten thousand, you owe them ten thousand units of real, usable money. If the currency has changed, you must pay in the new currency.

### Shmuel: Old Currency OK

Shmuel rules differently: *yachol lomar lo hotzio be-Meishan* — the debtor may say, "take this money and spend it in Meishan," another region where the old coins are still in circulation. Since the debt was incurred in the old currency and that currency retains genuine value in other locales, tendering the old coins constitutes valid repayment. This is the *psalto medina* scenario — invalidated locally, usable elsewhere.

### Travel Condition

Rav Nachman qualifies Shmuel's ruling significantly: *mistavra milta de-Shmuel de-is lay orcha le-meizal le-Meishan* — Shmuel's position that old coins can be tendered as valid payment makes sense only if the creditor has plans to travel to the place where those coins are usable. If the creditor already has a business trip to Meishan scheduled in the near term, these coins genuinely are money to him — he will be able to spend them. In that case, Shmuel's ruling applies and the old currency constitutes legitimate repayment.

But *aval leis lay orcha* — if the creditor has no plans to travel there — then these coins are effectively useless to him, and one cannot call them valid repayment of a debt. Shmuel's principle holds, but only when the geographic limitation of the currency is a practical inconvenience the creditor can readily address, not when it renders the coins genuinely inaccessible in value.
