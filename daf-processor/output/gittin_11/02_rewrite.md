# Gittin 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Courts

### Mishnah Overview

The *mishna* on 10b continues the discussion of ways in which *gittin* for women and *shtar shichrur* for slaves share similarities — or possibly differences. The *mishna* states: *kol shtaros ha'olim be'arka'os shel ovdei kochavim af al pi she'chosemim ovdei kochavim kesherim* — all monetary documents that have come out of non-Jewish courts, even though they are signed by non-Jews, are *kesherim*. This is striking. The word *arka'os* denotes an established court, and the *mishna* is saying that documents processed through such courts are valid.

### Get & Manumission

The *mishna* then excepts: *chutz mi'gittei nashim u'shichrurei avadim* — with the exception of a *get* for a woman and a writ of manumission for a slave. This exception is not surprising. Monetary matters are one thing, but a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* — the latter derived from and governed by the same laws as *gittin* — change personal status. The entire *masechet* is concerned with the precise *halachic* requirements for how such documents must be executed, so naturally they cannot be valid when issuing from a non-Jewish court. What is surprising, as we will see, is that even the monetary documents are *kasher*, and the *Gemara* will explain why.

### Rabbi Shimon

For a still greater surprise, *Rebbi Shimon* states: *af elu kesherim* — even a *get* for a woman and a *shtar shichrur* for a slave are *kosher* when they come from a non-Jewish court and are signed by non-Jews. *Lo huzkeru ela bizman shena'asu behedyot* — the only time it is a problem is when they are executed in a private court rather than an established communal court.

How could this possibly be *kosher*? The *Gemara* will explain that *Rebbi Shimon* follows the position of *Rebbi Elazar*, who holds *eidi mesirah karshei* — that the witnesses who matter for a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* are not the witnesses who sign the document but the witnesses present when it is handed over. The case therefore assumes that the actual writing of the document was done properly by a qualified Jewish *sofer* with the correct intent, and all relevant *halachos* were followed. The genuine *chidush* concerns only the signatures: since *eidi mesirah* are what effect the divorce, non-Jewish signatories do not invalidate the document, provided that valid Jewish witnesses are present at the handing over. The document itself must have been composed correctly; it is only the signatures whose disqualification is being set aside.

## Monetary Evidence

### Sales vs. Gifts

The *Gemara* opens by noting that the *mishna* is categorical — *lo shna mecher lo shna matana* — it makes no distinction between a document attesting to a sale and one attesting to a gift. *Bishlama mecher*: a document of sale is understandable, because a non-Jewish court system would never have written a document of ownership had payment not been made in its presence. The act of acquisition *halachically* occurred through the payment of money, and the existence of this court document is absolute evidence that the sale took place. It is not functioning as a formal *shtar*; it functions as evidence — as reliable as a videotape of the transaction. Most *Rishonim* understand it precisely this way. Some frame it in terms of a rabbinic *takana*, but even without that, one can distinguish between formal testimony and evidence. The *Rishonim* in *Ashkenaz*, for instance, where women were involved in commerce, would accept a woman's word in monetary matters not as formal *eidus* but as solid evidentiary weight. That is all that is needed here: this document functions as compelling evidence that the transaction occurred, even if it does not satisfy the technical requirements of a formal *shtar*.

The difficulty arises with a gift — *ella matana, b'mai kakani?* — how does one acquire the gifted property? One might say through *chazaka*, but there is no reason to think that is what the non-Jewish court is attesting to. The presumption must be that the acquisition is effected by this *shtar*. But how can a *shtar* function as an instrument of transfer if it was not executed in a Jewish court with Jewish witnesses? The *shtar*, in that case, is, as the *Gemara* says, *chaspa b'alma hu* — nothing more than a shard of pottery.

### Dina Malchusa

The answer is *Shmuel*'s famous principle: *dina d'malchusa dina* — the law of the sovereign is the law. Technically, the document might not work as a *halachic* instrument, but it works because it is valid under secular law, and secular law is binding. This is of course a major *halachic* principle, subject to considerable debate among the *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* as to its precise scope. But as applied here, if one simply reflects on ordinary life — when one purchases something in a store, one does not pause to wonder whether *halachic* acquisition through *meshicha* or *hagba'ah* has occurred — the underlying assumption is that the modes by which society recognizes ownership transfers are *halachically* binding, and *dina d'malchusa dina* is one principled way of grounding that assumption.

### Function vs. Instrument

The *Gemara* offers a further resolution: *v'iba'is eima tani chutz migitei nashim v'shichrurei avadim* — if one prefers not to invoke *dina d'malchusa dina*, or if its scope is understood more narrowly, then the *mishna*'s exception for a *get* is best read as defining a category. The *mishna* does not merely mean a *get* literally — it means the category of *shtar* that functions as an instrument. Documents from non-Jewish courts can work when they serve as evidence; they cannot work when they must serve as instruments, as a *get* must, and as a *shtar matana* must. On this reading, the *mishna*'s exception implicitly encompasses any document that must function as a formal legal instrument rather than merely as evidence of an underlying transaction.

## Edei Mesira

### Non-Jewish Witnesses

Having explained the *mishna*'s first ruling, the *Gemara* returns to the position of *Rebbi Shimon* — that even a *get* signed by non-Jews and issuing from an established non-Jewish court is *kosher*. The *Gemara* challenges this immediately: *v'ha lo bnei krisus ninu* — non-Jews are not in the category of *krisus*. When the *Torah* speaks of a *sefer krisus*, a document of severance in divorce, it implies that those who create and attest to this instrument must themselves be subject to the laws of marriage and divorce. Non-Jews, standing outside this framework, would seem categorically incapable of producing a valid instrument of divorce.

*Tosafos* notes that the *Gemara* could have raised an even more basic objection — *havi matzi limifrach ha lo bnei eidus ninu* — that non-Jews are not valid witnesses at all. But the *Gemara* presses the more specific point: beyond their general disqualification as witnesses, non-Jews cannot create this type of instrument because they are entirely outside the framework of *krisus*. It is worth noting that some *Rishonim* raise the theoretical possibility that non-Jews, if one addresses only the concern about truthfulness, might technically qualify as witnesses in some sense — a striking suggestion that will receive further discussion on 12a.

### Witnesses Matter

*Rebbi Zeira* explains: *amar Rabbi Shimon le-shitato de-Rabbi Elazar de-amar eidi mesirah karshei* — *Rebbi Shimon* has fully embraced the position of *Rebbi Elazar*, who holds that it is the witnesses present at the handing over of the *get* who effect the divorce, not the signatories. There are two sets of witnesses relevant to a *get*: *edei chatimah*, those who sign it, and *edei mesirah*, those present at its delivery. For *Rebbi Elazar*, only the latter are decisive. Accordingly, if one ensures that qualified Jewish witnesses are present at the handing over, the non-Jewish signatures on the document are not an obstacle.

### Forgery Risk

The *Gemara* immediately raises a complication. *Ve-ha-amar Rabbi Abba: modeh Rabbi Elazar bi-mzuyaf mi-tocho she-pasul* — did not *Rebbi Abba* say that even *Rebbi Elazar* concedes that a document considered *mezuyaf mi-tocho*, forged from within, is invalid? The problem here is not that the document is actually forged, but that having invalid signatories on a *get* creates a functionally analogous concern: the document may be relied upon improperly.

The danger operates on two levels. Some *Rishonim* hold that according to *Rebbi Elazar*, while *eidi mesirah* are primary, one may in the absence of *eidi mesirah* fall back on the *edei chatimah*. From that perspective, a *get* signed by non-Jews is acutely dangerous: one might hand it over without any Jewish witnesses present, relying instead on the non-Jewish signatories. Even for those who hold that *eidi mesirah* are always required and *edei chatimah* can never stand alone, a different danger remains: when the *get* is handed over, one might look at the signatures — say, "John Smith" — locate those people and have them stand as witnesses at the handing over, not realizing they are non-Jews. The *Gemara* makes clear that in its era, there were no reliable external markers distinguishing Jews from non-Jews, making this a genuine risk.

## Clear Names Req.

### Ambiguous Names

The *Gemara* therefore asks, at the top of 11a: given this risk of reliance on non-Jewish signatories, how can *Rebbi Shimon* permit a *get* from a non-Jewish court at all?

### Implicit Criteria

The answer is: *bi-shemos muvhakim* — we are dealing only with cases where the names on the document are unmistakably, unambiguously non-Jewish names. If the signatures are recognizably non-Jewish, no one will mistakenly seek out those signatories as witnesses for the handing over, and no one will rely on the document without ensuring that Jewish witnesses are present.

### Name Examples

*Rav Pappa* provides illustrations: names like *Hurmiz*, *Avudina*, *bar Shiptai*, *bar Kidri*, *Bati*, and *Nakimuna*. These are examples of names so thoroughly non-Jewish in character that their non-Jewish origin is beyond question.

### Both Criteria

The *Gemara* then presses: if the requirement of clearly non-Jewish names is so central, *Rebbi Shimon* should have stated it explicitly when enumerating the conditions for validity. Why does the *mishna* instead only mention the private court (*hedyot*) as the disqualifying factor? The *Gemara* responds: *hachi nami ka'amar* — the *mishna* is indeed implying this. *Ba-meh devarim amurim bi-shemos muvhakim* — when is a *get* from a non-Jewish court valid? Only when the names are clearly non-Jewish. *Aval shemos she-ein muvhakim* — where the names are ambiguous, *na'aseh ke-mi-she-na'asu be-hedyot u-fesulin* — it is treated as if it were done in a private court, and it is invalid.

This is admittedly a strained reading of the *mishna*, since one would expect such a central criterion to be stated explicitly rather than folded into the reference to a private court. This is what is sometimes called *ikar chaser min ha-sefer* — the most essential point is not stated outright. The *Ran* offers a cleaner reading: there are genuinely two independent criteria. The document must bear clearly non-Jewish names, and it must emanate from a public established court. Both are required because together they make it unmistakably clear that the signatories are non-Jews — the established court giving the matter a public, well-known character, and the unambiguous names leaving no room for confusion about identity. Either condition alone is insufficient; both together satisfy the standard. On the *Ran*'s reading, the private court is problematic for exactly the same reason as an ambiguous name: neither provides sufficient public clarity that the document was signed by non-Jews.

The *Gemara* offers a further alternative reading: *seifa ata legitei mammon* — the final clause of *Rebbi Shimon*'s statement, the part about a private court being invalid, is actually not part of *Rebbi Shimon*'s statement at all but rather a continuation of the *mishna*'s earlier ruling about monetary documents. That is: monetary documents from public *arka'os* are valid; monetary documents from private courts are not. *Rebbi Shimon*'s actual statement concerns only *gittin*, and he is simply saying those are *kosher* in a public court. Under this reading, *ve-hachi ka'amar*: monetary documents (*gitei mammon*) in public courts are valid; *gittin* in non-Jewish courts are generally not valid — but *Rebbi Shimon* holds they are valid — and even regarding monetary documents, public courts are required, not private ones.

## Public vs. Private

### Public Courts

A *beraita* now elaborates the positions. *Rebbi Elazar b'Rebbi Yose* reports that *Rebbi Shimon* said to the *Chachamim* in *Tzidan*: *lo necheleku Rabbi Akiva ve-chachamim al kol ha-shtaros ha-olim be-arkaot shel Akko* — there was never any disagreement between *Rabbi Akiva* and the *Chachamim* regarding documents issued by the public established courts of non-Jews. Everyone agreed such documents are *kosher* — and not only monetary documents: even *gittin* and *shichrurei avadim* are valid, just as *Rebbi Shimon* holds in the *mishna*.

### Private Dispute

The dispute was only regarding documents executed in private courts: *sheRabbi Akiva machshir ve-chachamim poslin* — *Rabbi Akiva* validates documents from private non-Jewish courts, while the *Chachamim* invalidate them. We are speaking here of monetary documents; the *Chachamim* are concerned about the trustworthiness of such documents when they do not emanate from an established public institution.

*Chutz mi-gitei nashim ve-shichrurei avadim* — excepted from the debate are *gittin* and *shichrurei avadim*. Whether this exception attaches to the *Chachamim*'s ruling or to *Rabbi Akiva*'s is the subject of a debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* on one side and the *Ran* on the other. Following the *Ran*, the cleaner reading is: in private courts, *Rabbi Akiva* holds monetary documents are valid, and the *Chachamim* disagree — but everyone agrees that in private courts, *gittin* are certainly not valid. Conversely, in public courts, everything is valid, even *gittin*, as stated above.

### Ben Gamliel

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* then goes further: *af elu kesherim* — he will even permit a *get* from a private court. The immediate difficulty is that in a private court one could not be certain the document was signed by non-Jews, which reintroduces all the concerns about reliance on invalid signatories.

### Clear Status

The *Gemara* explains his position: *be-makom she-ein Yisrael chotmim* — he is speaking of municipalities where Jews are not permitted to sign official documents in the secular courts. In such a place, even a private court document's signatures are self-evidently non-Jewish, so there is no fear of misplaced reliance. *Aval be-makom she-Yisrael chotmim lo* — but where Jews are permitted to sign, even *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* agrees that a private court creates too much ambiguity.

The *Gemara* asks: even in municipalities where Jews are forbidden to sign, should we not make a *gezeira* — a rabbinic precautionary decree — prohibiting such documents lest people come to rely on private court documents from places where Jews are permitted to sign? The *Gemara* answers: *shma bi-shma michlaif, atra be-atra lo michlaif* — names can be confused with other names, and one might lose track of which names are clearly non-Jewish and which are ambiguous, so such a decree might make sense for names. But a municipality is not confused with another municipality. The rule about whether Jews are permitted to sign in a given location is unambiguous and well-known — there is no sliding scale, no blurry boundary. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position may therefore rest entirely on the criterion of location: where Jews cannot sign, it is simply obvious that all signatures are non-Jewish, regardless of the nature of the names. That is a completely binary criterion and carries no risk of extension by analogy.

## Practical Cases

### Communal Gathering

*Ravina* considered permitting a monetary document produced by a large communal gathering of non-Jews — something resembling an *ad hoc* public proceeding, perhaps analogous to the market-day gatherings described in the *mishna* when villagers would assemble on market days and courts would convene. It was not an established *arka'os*, but it was public and communal in character, and *Ravina* thought it might be close enough.

### Rafram Rejects

*Rafram* pushed back decisively: *arkaot tnan* — the *mishna* says *arka'os*, which means a fixed, established court. An *ad hoc* gathering, however large and public, does not meet that standard.

## Persian Document

### Loan Document

*Rava* introduces a practical case: *hai shtar parsa'a de-masri nihalei be-apei sahadei Yisrael* — a Persian loan document that was handed over in the presence of Jewish witnesses. The document, written in Persian and signed by Persian witnesses, attests to a loan — say, that *Reuven* borrowed a sum of money from *Shimon*.

A standard *halachic* document of this kind serves three functions: it proves the loan occurred, it demonstrates the loan has not been repaid (since one does not ordinarily retain a document after repayment, unless a receipt has been produced), and it creates a lien on the borrower's property, allowing the lender to collect from real estate the borrower may subsequently sell if he has no unencumbered assets remaining. The third function is the most far-reaching, and the *Gemara* will have something to say about it.

### Witness Testimony

The question is: if Jewish witnesses were present and can testify to the transaction, why is the document needed at all? The answer is that witnesses present at a transaction might not spontaneously remember the details a year or a decade later — the amount, the date, the conditions. But when the document is produced and the witnesses see it, their recognition of the document refreshes and grounds their testimony. Their recollection combined with the document gives the document its evidentiary force. That is how a Persian document handed over before Jewish witnesses can function as a valid instrument of evidence.

### Requirements

The *Gemara* challenges this on several grounds. First: *ve-ha lo yadei le-mikrarei* — the Jewish witnesses cannot read Persian and thus do not know what they are witnessing. The *Gemara* responds: *be-didyei* — we are speaking of witnesses who do in fact read Persian and who have read and understood the document's contents.

Second: *ve-ha ba'inan ktav she-eino yachol le-hizayef ve-leika* — does not *halacha* require that the document be written in a manner that cannot be forged, meaning on parchment treated such that any erasure would be visible? Otherwise the creditor could later alter the amount stated. The *Gemara* responds: *be-dafitza* — the parchment was treated with gallnut, making it tamper-evident.

Third: *ve-ha ba'inan toretz she-yachzor me-inyano shel shtar be-shita acharona* — does not a valid *shtar* require a closing line restating the essence of the document? The purpose of this requirement, as seen for instance in the concluding formula of a *ketubah* — *ve-kanyana mi-kol mah de-ktav u-mforash le-eila be-mana de-chasher le-makanya beih ve-hakol sharir ve-kayam* — is to prevent anyone from adding text between the end of the document and the witnesses' signatures. The final line declares itself to be the final line, so anything appearing below the witnesses' signatures cannot be appended and attributed to the original document. The *Gemara* responds: *bi-mahadeir* — this requirement too has been satisfied.

### No Liens

Having confirmed that all technical requirements are met, the *Gemara* asks: *i hachi, mi-mishababdei nami* — if this is such a complete and properly formed document, why can it not also create liens, enabling collection from property the borrower has sold? The *Gemara* answers: *leit lei kala* — even though *eidi mesirah* combined with a technically sound document may suffice *halachically*, the document lacks the public resonance of a genuinely Jewish *shtar* executed and signed in accordance with Jewish law. Property liens depend on public knowledge — the buyer of the borrower's land must be presumed to have been on notice of the prior debt. A Persian document witnessed by Jews in this fashion does not achieve the level of public record that would make it fair to charge third parties with constructive notice. Therefore it may be used to collect from the borrower's own unencumbered property, but it creates no lien on property he has transferred to others.

## Ambiguous Signatures

### Reish Lakish Q.

The *Gemara* returns to the question of *gittin* and non-Jewish signatures. *Reish Lakish* posed a question to *Rabbi Yochanan* — both of them *Eretz Yisrael* *Amoraim*. The case: a *get* is presented for validation and the signatures on it resemble the names of non-Jews. The question is what to presume. If these are Jews who happen to bear non-Jewish sounding names, the *get* is valid. If they are non-Jews, the *get* is not valid — not because non-Jewish signatories are an absolute bar under *Rebbi Elazar*'s view, but because a *get* signed by non-Jews with ambiguous names is the paradigm of a dangerous document: one cannot tell by looking at it whether the signatories are Jewish or not, making it susceptible to misuse, either by relying on the signatories alone without *eidi mesirah*, or by enlisting those very signatories as the witnesses for handing over.

### R. Yochanan

*Rabbi Yochanan* responded: *lo ba le-yadeinu ela Lukis ve-Lus ve-hichsharnu* — the only case of this kind that came before us was a document signed by someone named *Lukis* and *Lus*, and we ruled it valid. *Tosafot* explains that *Lukis* and *Lus* were so unmistakably non-Jewish names that the document fell into the category of *shemos muvhakim* — clearly non-Jewish names — and was therefore treated as valid: clearly non-Jewish, so the rule of *Rebbi Shimon* applies, and one ensures that Jewish witnesses are present at the handing over. For ambiguous cases, *Rabbi Yochanan* offers no ruling — signaling that they are not valid.

### Ambiguity Rule

The *Gemara* clarifies: *ve-davka Lukos ve-Lus de-la shechichi Yisrael de-maski bishmayhu* — specifically *Lukis* and *Lus*, names so foreign to Jewish usage that no Jew is commonly known by them, so that seeing such names one knows immediately they are non-Jewish. *Aval shemata achrinei de-shchichi Yisrael de-maski bishmateihu* — but other names that Jews are also known to use, where there is genuine ambiguity as to whether the signatory is Jewish or not — in such cases, *lo*, they cannot be relied upon. If one knew they were Jews there would be no problem; if one knew they were non-Jews, *shemos muvhakim* would render the document safe. But ambiguity itself is the disqualifying factor, since a document that might have been signed by non-Jews with names indistinguishable from Jewish names is too dangerous to validate.

## Overseas Gettin

### Tosefta Rule

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a *Tosefta*: *gittin ha-ba'im mi-medinat ha-yam ve-ha-edim chatumim aleihem* — *gittin* that arrive from overseas, bearing witness signatures, *af al pi she-shmoteihen ki-shmotei ovdei kochavim kesherim* — even if the names on those signatures are non-Jewish names, they are valid.

### Presumption

The reason given: *mipnei she-rov Yisrael she-be-chutz la-aretz shmoteihen ki-shmotei ovdei kochavim* — because the majority, or at any rate a very large proportion, of Jews living outside *Eretz Yisrael* bear non-Jewish names. In a community where non-Jewish names are so prevalent among Jews, a *get* arriving from that community with non-Jewish sounding signatures should obviously be presumed to have been signed by Jews who happen to bear those names, not by actual non-Jews. This seems to contradict *Rabbi Yochanan*'s rule, which disqualified ambiguous names.

### Geographic Limit

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *hatam ki-detani ta'ama* — the *Tosefta* itself supplies its reason, and that reason is specific to *chutz la-aretz*. Outside *Eretz Yisrael* — and the reference here is to the diaspora more broadly, places of greater integration with the surrounding society and with less dense *halachic* infrastructure than *Bavel* — it is so common for Jews to bear non-Jewish names that the presumption straightforwardly favors Jewish signatories. Within *Eretz Yisrael*, by contrast, non-Jewish names are far less prevalent among Jews, and therefore the same ambiguity cannot be resolved the same way. There, a document with non-Jewish sounding names cannot enjoy the same presumption, and one must be concerned that the signatories were indeed non-Jews.

The *Gemara* offers an alternative formulation as well: *ve-ika de-amri, ki matnita ba'a mineih pashat lei mi-matnita* — according to some, *Reish Lakish*'s question to *Rabbi Yochanan* was specifically about *gittin* arriving from *chutz la-aretz*, and *Rabbi Yochanan*'s answer was the *Tosefta* itself: when the documents come from *medinat ha-yam*, even names that sound non-Jewish are presumed to be those of Jews, because that is the nature of Jewish life in *chutz la-aretz*, and such documents are entirely valid.

## Secular Names

### Criticism Q.

What is striking about this entire *sugya* is the *Gemara*'s matter-of-fact recognition, without any critical editorial comment, of how prevalent it was for Jews to bear non-Jewish names. This prompts a question worth raising: should one criticize Jews for giving their children secular names? The practice was widespread across many generations — a concern not merely theoretical, as the *Gemara* itself treats it as an entirely ordinary feature of diaspora Jewish life.

### Moshe Solution

There is a famous *teshuva* in *Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s *Igrot Moshe* where someone urged that Jews who give their children secular names should be criticized, citing the well-known *midrash* that the Israelites were redeemed from Egypt in part because they did not change their names, their dress, or their language. *Rav Moshe* goes through the *Tannaim*, *Amoraim*, *Rishonim*, and *Acharonim* who bore secular names and demonstrates that the practice was entirely accepted. He then addresses the *midrash* directly with a compelling insight: that *midrash* was speaking of the period before *matan Torah*. Before *matan Torah*, the Jewish people had no *taryag mitzvos* to sustain their distinct identity; the only markers of that identity were external — language, names, dress. Holding onto those markers was what preserved them as a people, and it was in that *zchus* that they merited redemption. After *matan Torah*, however, *taryag mitzvos* themselves maintain and sustain Jewish identity. The external markers are no longer the load-bearing structure of *am Yisrael*'s distinctiveness, and therefore the question of secular names does not carry the same weight it did before *matan Torah*.
