# Hullin 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fetal Limb Status

### Debate Setup

The Gemara opens on 73a with the question *keman* — according to whom? — probing the tannaic dispute regarding a fetal limb that protrudes from the womb at the moment the mother is slaughtered. The central question is whether such a limb is classified as a *neveilah* or as a *treifah*. These two categories carry a crucial conceptual distinction: *neveilah* describes an animal upon which no valid *shechitah* was performed, while *treifah* describes an animal upon which *shechitah* was performed but was halachically ineffective. Applied to the protruding limb, the question becomes: did the *shechitah* of the mother relate to the limb at all — making it, at worst, a *treifah* — or did the *shechitah* not apply to the limb whatsoever, leaving it a *neveilah*? Underlying this is the question of *gufah*: whether the limb is considered part of the mother's body. The practical stakes are primarily in the realm of *tumah*, since a *neveilah* conveys ritual impurity while a *treifah* does not.

### Rabbi Meir Position

According to Rabbi Meir, the protruding limb has the status of a *neveilah* and therefore conveys *tumah*. His position is that the limb is not *gufah* — not part of the mother — and therefore the *shechitah* of the mother did not apply to it at all, leaving it as if it died without *shechitah*. Consequently, Rabbi Meir holds that the limb renders the remainder of the fetus *tamei*.

### Tumah Transmission

A difficulty immediately arises, however. The limb that protrudes from the womb is not touching the rest of the fetus on an exposed surface; it touches only through the *beis hastarim*, the concealed surface. How then does it transmit *tumah* to the fetus? The Gemara resolves this by invoking Rabbi Meir's own principle: since the limb is destined to be cut off — one would not eat it in any case, as it is forbidden, and would naturally remove it — it is considered as though it already stands to be cut. And the operative rule is *kol ha-omed lachatoch ke-chatuach dami*: something that stands to be cut is legally regarded as though it has already been cut. Thus, there is already, in legal contemplation, an exposed surface, and the limb is treated as if it is in contact with the fetus through that surface.

### Mikvaos Analogy

The Gemara traces this principle to a *mishnah* in *mikvaos*. If a vessel has an unusually long handle that its owner intends to shorten by cutting, one need not immerse the entire handle in the *mikveh* — only up to the point that will be retained. The reasoning is that the excess portion, since it stands to be cut off, is already regarded in law as though it has been severed; it is no longer considered part of the vessel. This is the position attributed to the *tanna kamma*, identified with Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, holding that until the handle is actually cut, it remains part of the vessel and must be immersed in its entirety.

The same principle functions differently in our context. Whereas in the *mikvaos* case it operates as a leniency — exempting one from immersing the surplus handle — here it can serve as a stringency: the limb is considered already severed, an exposed surface exists in legal contemplation, and *tumah* is therefore transmitted to the fetus through that virtual contact.

### Food Logic

The Gemara then extends this reasoning beyond Rabbi Meir alone. *afilu teima rabbanan* — perhaps even the Rabbis would agree in the case of the fetal limb. The principle invoked here is *chiburei ochlin ke-man de-mifrash dami* — items that are foodstuff are more readily regarded as already separated than solid vessels. The logic is that food is inherently in the process of being consumed and destroyed; it lacks the structural unity of a vessel. Since both the fetus and the limb are, in principle, food, and since the limb is destined to be discarded or given to dogs, it is easier to regard the limb as already detached from the fetus. Even the Rabbis might therefore concede that the limb is considered as though already cut off, rendering it in virtual contact with the fetus and capable of transmitting *tumah* — whereas by a vessel, where the integrity of the whole is more pronounced, only Rabbi Meir would apply this logic.

### Ulla vs Ravina

Two distinct explanations emerge for the mechanism by which the limb transmits *tumah* to the fetus. The first, associated with Ulla, holds that *tumah* is transmitted at the moment of actual severance. When the limb is physically cut off, an exposed surface is created; in that very instant of separation, the surfaces make contact and *tumah* passes. This explains why the *mishnah* uses the formulation *chatacha* — "if one cut it" — because the transmission occurs at that moment of cutting. The second explanation, Ravina's, holds that the legal fiction of *ke-chatuach dami* is sufficient: since the limb is considered already cut, the surfaces are already deemed to be touching, and the *tumah* is transmitted even before any physical severance. On this view, the *mishnah*'s use of *chatacha* is merely stylistic, following the language of the earlier clause which discussed actually cutting the limb prior to *shechitah*.

The conceptual irony here is worth noting. Ordinarily one might suppose that the very unity of a body would be the conduit of *tumah* — that things that are fully joined would more readily convey impurity to one another. Yet the opposite is true: it is precisely because the limb and the fetus are one continuous whole that no *tumah* passes between them. *tumah* requires one object to come into contact with another. Only when there is a demarcation — actual or legal — does contact and transmission become possible.

### Tosafot Paradox

*Tosafot* raises a striking difficulty. In the *mikvaos* analogy, the principle is that the excess handle need not be immersed because it is treated as already cut off — it is not considered part of the vessel. Fine. But consider the implication: if the excess handle is treated as already severed and now lies against the vessel, it would cover the virtual surface at the cut point, creating a *chatzitzah* — an interposition — that might block the *mikveh* waters from reaching the vessel's own surface. Can one apply the *ke-chatuach* logic selectively? Can one say it is cut for purposes of not requiring immersion of the extra portion, yet not cut for purposes of *chatzitzah* — so that the virtual exposed surface at the cut point is not treated as covered?

*Tosafot* finds this untenable and resolves the problem by stipulating that the handle in question is not a solid wooden or metal handle but a chain-link handle. Because the links of a chain allow water to pass between them, even when the excess portion remains physically attached, the *mikveh* water can reach every part of what is being retained. There is therefore no *chatzitzah* concern regardless of how one applies the *ke-chatuach* principle. This solution is ingenious, but the underlying question remains conceptually significant: when we say something is *ke-chatuach*, how thoroughgoing is that fiction? Is it sufficient to generate some legal consequences — exemption from immersion — while not generating others, such as treating a virtual cut surface as obstructed?

## Sages Response

### Treifah Model

Having established Rabbi Meir's position, the Gemara turns to the view of the Sages. The Sages hold that the protruding fetal limb is not a *neveilah* but a *treifah*. Their reasoning is that the *shechitah* of the mother did apply to the limb; it simply was not effective in permitting the limb to be eaten. The practical consequence is that the limb does not convey biblical *tumah*. The Gemara notes that a slaughtered *treifah* ordinarily carries no *tumah* at all. If the Sages mean only that touching such a limb is like touching a slaughtered *treifah* — which conveys nothing — why does the *mishnah* invoke the *treifah* comparison rather than simply declaring the limb *tahor*? The Gemara resolves this by citing the position of Abaye's father, Shmuel's father, who held that a *treifah* that was slaughtered carries a rabbinic degree of *tumah* with respect to sanctified objects and items of the Temple. The *mishnah* thus invokes the *treifah* analogy not to declare full biblical *tumah* but to acknowledge this limited rabbinic impurity.

### Braita Exchange

The Gemara then presents a *braita* that reconstructs the full exchange between Rabbi Meir and the Sages in greater detail, filling in what the *mishnah* left elliptical.

Rabbi Meir opens: *ve-chi mi tiharuhu le-eiver zeh midei neveilah* — what has purified this limb from the category of *neveilah*? The Sages respond: *shechitat imo* — the *shechitah* of the mother applies to it. Rabbi Meir presses: if so, *tatirenu ba'achila* — why not permit the limb to be eaten? The Sages respond: *treifah tochich* — the *treifah* case proves the point. A *treifah* that is slaughtered is purified from the category of *neveilah* by virtue of the *shechitah*, yet it cannot be eaten. There is therefore an established category of an act of *shechitah* that removes the designation of *neveilah* without rendering the animal permissible. The same applies here: the *shechitah* of the mother applied to the limb and removed it from the category of *neveilah*, even though it cannot be eaten.

Rava notes — and some attribute the observation to the text anonymously — that the exchange in the *mishnah* was abbreviated, *chisorei mechasra*, and that the *braita* supplies the missing steps.

### Conceptual Basis

Rabbi Meir responds to the Sages' *treifah* analogy by drawing a sharp conceptual distinction. He says: I accept that the *shechitah* of a *treifah* can remove the *neveilah* designation even though the animal cannot be eaten, because in that case the *shechitah* was performed upon the *gufah* — the body of the animal itself. The *shechitah* genuinely related to the *treifah* as its subject. But the limb protruding from the fetus is *she-einah gufah* — not part of the body. If the limb is not part of the mother's body, then the *shechitah* of the mother was simply not performed upon the limb at all. One cannot say the *shechitah* applied but was ineffective; it never applied to begin with. And if no *shechitah* applied, the limb is a *neveilah*.

This exchange clarifies the precise point of contention. Rabbi Meir holds that *shechitah* can only relate to a subject that is *gufah* of the slaughtered animal. If something is not *gufah*, no act of *shechitah* can be said to have been done to it. The Rabbis must therefore be maintaining either that the limb is still *gufah*, or that *shechitah* can relate to something even if it is not strictly *gufah*.

### Fetus vs Organs

The *braita* continues with the Sages' rejoinder, which demonstrates that *shechitah* can indeed function more effectively for something that is *not* integral to the body than for something that is. *harbe matzinu she-eino gufah yotzei mi-gufah* — we find many instances where *shechitah* is more efficacious for something that is not part of the body than for something that is.

The proof is from the first *mishnah* of the *perek*: if one dismembers a fetus inside the mother's womb and then slaughters the mother, the dismembered fetus pieces are permitted for consumption. But if one cuts off the spleen or kidney from the inside of the mother and then slaughters the mother, those organs cannot be eaten. Here is the paradox: the inner organs, which were entirely and integrally part of the mother, lose the benefit of the mother's *shechitah* once they are severed from her body. But the fetus, which was never fully *gufah* of the mother, retains the benefit of the mother's *shechitah* even after being dismembered.

This demonstrates that the Sages' position does not require the fetus to be *gufah* for *shechitah* to apply to it. *shechitah* can relate to something connected to the mother even if it is not an intrinsic part of her body. The underlying question is whether the *shechitah*'s efficacy for the fetus is because the fetus is *gufah* — which would be the position assumed by Rabbi Meir — or whether it is because the fetus is sufficiently connected to the mother even without being *gufah*, which is the Sages' position. The proof from the dismembered fetus shows that even after severance, the *shechitah* of the mother reaches it — indicating that the rationale was never *gufah* in the strict sense.

Importantly, however, the Sages' response does not directly address why the protruding limb cannot be eaten. Rabbi Meir challenged: if the *shechitah* applies, eat the limb. The Sages' rejoinder about the dismembered fetus shows that *shechitah* can apply to something that is not *gufah*, but does not explain what prevents eating the limb. The Rabbis presumably hold that there is some independent prohibition — perhaps derived from the verse *u-vasar basadeh treifah lo tocheilu* — that disqualifies the limb without negating the application of *shechitah*. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, may maintain that the limb's disqualification flows entirely from its not being *gufah*, and that the same fact that disqualifies it also negates the application of *shechitah* entirely.

### Meir Rejoinder

Rabbi Meir's final response in the exchange, as preserved in the *braita*, insists that the categories are mutually exclusive. If the limb is not *gufah* and that is why *shechitah* does not work for it, then one cannot simultaneously claim that *shechitah* did apply but was ineffective — that is only coherent when the *shechitah* was genuinely performed on the subject, as with a *treifah*. The Sages' appeal to the dismembered fetus does not persuade him, because from his perspective the fetus *is gufah* — that is precisely why *shechitah* works for it. For Rabbi Meir, *shechitah* works for the fetus because the fetus is considered part of the mother's body. It follows that the only reason *shechitah* fails for the protruding limb is that the limb is no longer considered part of the body — and once that conclusion is reached, the *shechitah* cannot be said to have applied to the limb at all.

### Hanging Limb Intro

Within this *braita* exchange, a new case is introduced: *ever ha-meduldal*, the hanging or dangling limb of the animal itself. This is distinct from the fetal limb; here we are discussing a limb of the mother animal that has been partially severed — torn perhaps by a predator or other trauma — yet remains attached by some ligament, hanging but not fully detached. Rabbi Meir, in the course of his argument, concedes that the *shechitah* of the mother works for the dangling limb. His reasoning: *davar she-hu gufah* — the dangling limb is part of the body of the animal itself. This concession will be significant for the subsequent analysis. The dangling limb is poised between two statuses: it is not yet *eiver min ha-chai*, since that requires complete detachment, yet it is not fully integrated either. What is its status after *shechitah*, and what happens to it if the animal dies naturally? These questions carry over into the next section.

## Dangling Limbs

### Lakish vs Yochanan

The Gemara now presents a dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan concerning whether the controversy between Rabbi Meir and the Sages over the fetal limb extends equally to the *ever ha-meduldal*, the dangling limb of the animal. Reish Lakish maintains: *ke-machlokes be-ubarim kach machlokes be-evarim* — just as Rabbi Meir and the Sages debate the fetal limb, so too they debate the dangling limb of the animal. Rabbi Yochanan initially appears to say that the debate is confined to the fetal limb, while concerning the dangling limb of the animal *divrei ha-kol shechitah osa nipul* — all agree that *shechitah* makes it as though the limb has fallen off, rendering it an *eiver min ha-chai*. The Gemara will eventually reverse Rabbi Yochanan's formulation, but the tension is first worked through analytically.

### Pull Back Logic

Rav Yosef bar Chanina explains Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning as follows. With respect to the fetal limb, there remains a theoretical possibility that the limb could be pushed back into the womb and the mother could then be slaughtered, permitting the entire animal including the limb. This possibility — *takanta be-chazarah*, the remedy through retraction — means that the protruding limb remains, in some sense, within the ambit of a valid *shechitah*. The Rabbis therefore say the *shechitah* relates to it, even as it protrudes. The dangling limb of the animal, by contrast, can never be restored. Once it is dangling and partially severed, no retraction is possible; it will never be fully permissible. For this reason, Rabbi Yochanan reasons, the Rabbis would concede that the *shechitah* does not apply to the dangling limb in the same way, and all would agree it becomes *nipul* — as though it had fallen.

### Meir Concedes

The Gemara challenges this reading from the *braita* itself. There, Rabbi Meir explicitly says — in addressing the Sages — that *shechitah* applies to the dangling limb (*ever ha-meduldal*) because it is *davar she-hu gufah*, part of the body of the animal. This appears to contradict both Reish Lakish and the initial formulation of Rabbi Yochanan, since both had suggested the dangling limb is subject to dispute. If Rabbi Meir himself concedes the dangling limb, how can it be said that the Sages dispute it?

### Resolution

The Gemara resolves the difficulty for Reish Lakish as follows. Rabbi Meir's concession in the *braita* was dialectical: *le-divreichem ka-amar* — he was speaking according to the Sages' own premises, not expressing his own view. Rabbi Meir's personal position, *lidi*, is that whether it is the fetal limb or the dangling limb, the *shechitah* does not apply to either and both are *neveilah*. He said *le-divreichem*: even according to your logic, I might grant you the dangling limb — since at least that is *gufah* of the animal — but I cannot grant you the fetal limb, which is not. Reish Lakish thus maintains that the true debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages encompasses the dangling limb as well; Rabbi Meir's concession was rhetorical only.

### Yochanan Position

For Rabbi Yochanan, however, the Gemara acknowledges a problem and concludes that his statement was transmitted in inverted form. The corrected version is: the debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies only to the fetal limb. Regarding the dangling limb of the animal, *divrei ha-kol ein shechitah osa nipul* — all agree that *shechitah* does *not* make the limb as though it fell off. All agree that the *shechitah* of the mother applies to the dangling limb, removing it from the category of *neveilah*, and making it, at worst, like a *treifah* — forbidden for consumption but not *tamei*. As Rashi clarifies: *de-tahor mi-letamei af al pi she-assur be-achilah* — it is pure from conveying *tumah*, even though it is forbidden to eat.

This gives Rabbi Yochanan's position a clean logic. The dangling limb is precisely the case that the Sages invoke to prove their point against Rabbi Meir. Since the dangling limb is clearly *gufah* of the mother, everyone agrees the *shechitah* applies to it — yet everyone also agrees it cannot be eaten. This is exactly the Sages' model: *shechitah* can relate to something and yet not render it permissible. Rabbi Meir himself concedes this in the *braita*. The remaining debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns only the fetal limb — whether *shechitah* can apply to something that is not *gufah* at all.

## Death vs Shechitah

### Death Principle

Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that there is universal agreement on two related principles concerning the dangling limb. The first is *mita osa nipul* — natural death makes the limb as though it has fallen off. When an animal with a dangling limb dies naturally, the process of dying is treated as completing the limb's detachment. This is admittedly a strange idea: the limb becomes *eiver min ha-chai* precisely at the moment the animal ceases to be alive. But this is the established rule, and it applies regardless of the debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages about the fetal limb.

### Shechitah Principle

The second universal principle is *ein shechitah osa nipul* — *shechitah* does not make the limb as though it has fallen off. When one slaughters an animal with a dangling limb, the act of *shechitah* does not cause the limb to be treated as fully detached. The *shechitah* relates to the limb, removes it from the category of *neveilah*, and makes it — at worst — like a *treifah*. It does not complete the detachment in the way that natural death does.

### Mishna Evidence

The Gemara asks: if these two principles are universally agreed upon, what *chiddush* does Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef add? Both points are already explicit in a *mishnah* — one that appears much later in the tractate, at 127b. The *mishnah* there states: *meta ha-behemah* — if the animal with the dangling limb died naturally — *ha-basar tzarich hechsher* — the meat of the animal requires susceptibility through contact with liquid — *ve-ha-eiver metamei mishum eiver min ha-chai* — and the limb conveys *tumah* as an *eiver min ha-chai* — *ve-eino metamei mishum eiver min ha-neveilah divrei Rabbi Meir* — but it does not convey *tumah* as a *neveilah*, according to Rabbi Meir.

This explicitly establishes *mita osa nipul*: the limb becomes *eiver min ha-chai* when the animal dies. The difference between *eiver min ha-chai* and *eiver min ha-neveilah* in this context is a matter of the type of *tumah* conveyed. *neveilah* conveys *tumah* through its flesh alone — a *kezayis* of *neveilah* meat is *tamei*. *eiver min ha-chai* conveys *tumah* as a whole limb — if the flesh is separated from the bone, it no longer conveys *tumah*, since it is no longer a complete limb. This distinction is not merely academic; it determines whether separating flesh from the dangling limb creates an independent source of *tumah*.

### Huchsharu Proof

The same *mishnah* continues: *nishchata ha-behemah huchsharu be-dameha divrei Rabbi Meir* — if the animal was slaughtered, the animal and the limb become susceptible to *tumah* through its blood. Rabbi Meir holds that the blood of *shechitah* functions as the liquid that renders the slaughtered animal's meat susceptible to becoming *tamei*. The significant word is *huchsharu* — "they became susceptible," in the plural. If the dangling limb were itself a *neveilah* — already *tamei* — there would be no reason to say it "became susceptible," since it would already be in a state of *tumah*. The use of the plural therefore implies that the limb itself is not *tamei*, that it is susceptible but not yet *tamei*, which in turn means the *shechitah* did apply to it and it is not a *neveilah*.

### Meat vs Limb

The Gemara pushes back. Perhaps *huchsharu* does not refer to the dangling limb at all, but to two types of meat: flesh that separates from the animal's body (*basar ha-poresh min ha-behemah*) and flesh that separates from the dangling limb itself (*basar ha-poresh min ha-eiver*). The *mishnah* might be teaching that both of these become susceptible through the blood of *shechitah* — not that the dangling limb itself is non-*tamei*.

The Gemara further imagines why one might have thought these two types of meat require separate mention: since flesh that separates from an *eiver min ha-chai* was at one moment itself a source of *tumah chamura* by virtue of being attached to the *eiver*, one might have thought it remains susceptible to *tumah* even without contact with liquid — that its prior connection to a *tamei* source exempts it from the ordinary requirement of *hechsher*. The *mishnah* therefore informs us this is not the case; even such flesh requires contact with liquid before it can become *tamei*.

The Gemara acknowledges this is a possible reading of the *mishnah*. The conclusion is that on its own, the *mishnah* might not have been sufficient to establish *ein shechitah osa nipul* with respect to a genuine limb; one might have argued the *mishnah* speaks only of dangling meat, and that a true dangling limb might still be treated as having fallen off upon *shechitah*. Nevertheless, taken together — and ultimately with the explicit statement of Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef in Rabbi Yochanan's name — both principles are established: death makes the limb *nipul*, and *shechitah* does not.

### Flesh Susceptibility

The analysis concludes with a clarification about flesh removed from an *eiver min ha-chai*. Such flesh — once separated from the limb — is no longer a *tamei* object in its own right, since *tumah* of *eiver min ha-chai* requires a complete limb with its associated bone and tissue. The separated flesh, however, is susceptible to becoming *tamei* and requires *hechsher* like other foodstuffs. The prior association with a *tamei* object does not grant it an automatic susceptibility independent of liquid contact. This point is established by the *mishnah* and is itself a *chiddush* worth noting.

## Eating Prohibition

### Braita Source

Having established that *shechitah* applies to the dangling limb and does not render it *neveilah*, the Gemara turns to the question of why the dangling limb cannot be eaten. The *braita* derives the prohibition from the verse *u-vasar basadeh treifah lo tocheilu* — flesh in the field that is *treifah* you shall not eat. This verse was already invoked in the discussion of the fetal limb. The *braita* extends it: *lehavi es ha-eiver ve-es ha-basar ha-miduldalin bi-behemah u-vi-chayah u-va-of* — to include the dangling limb and hanging flesh of an animal, wild animal, or fowl. Even if one slaughters the mother — *she-hen asurin* — they remain forbidden.

### Dangling Forbidden

Rabba bar bar Chana records this ruling: the dangling limb of the animal, even after valid *shechitah* of the mother, remains forbidden for consumption. The *braita* associates this prohibition with the verse of *basar basadeh treifah*, placing the dangling limb in the same category as the fetal limb in terms of the prohibition against eating it.

### Rabbinic Severity

Rabbi Yochanan, however, as cited by Rabba bar bar Chana, draws a critical distinction between the two cases. When it comes to the dangling limb — *ein bahem ela mitzvas peirush bilvad* — the prohibition is merely a matter of appropriate conduct, a rabbinic-level requirement to refrain. Although the *braita* invokes a biblical verse, Rabbi Yochanan maintains that at the biblical level the *shechitah* of the mother is fully effective for the dangling limb; one could technically eat it. The prohibition against doing so is rabbinic in character — the dangling limb presents as something that should not be eaten, and the Rabbis accordingly prohibited it, but there is no biblical disqualification.

The fetal limb, by contrast, involves a genuine biblical dispute. Rabbi Meir holds it to be *neveilah* at the Torah level; the Sages hold it to be a *treifah*-like case that is biblically forbidden. Either way, the fetal limb's prohibition is of biblical severity. The dangling limb, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, stands apart: *shechitah* is fully effective for it, it is not *tamei*, and its prohibition is entirely rabbinic. This distinction — between biblical severity for the fetal limb and rabbinic severity for the dangling limb — is the concluding conceptual point of the *sugya*.
