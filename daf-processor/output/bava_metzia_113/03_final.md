# Bava Metzia 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mitzvot Overview

### Topic Introduction

Daf 113 marks a transition within the *masechet* from the laws governing the employer-employee relationship to the laws governing the borrower-lender relationship. Beyond the earlier *perek* on charging interest, the Torah articulates a distinct set of *mitzvot* concerned with the ways in which a lender might exert power and control over a borrower. These *mitzvot* are the focus of the present daf.

### Torah Sources

Because the Gemara does not systematically enumerate the relevant *mitzvot* — and because the material can become difficult to follow without an orientation — it is worth beginning with the *psukim* in Devarim 24. Several prohibitions appear in close succession. The Torah states: *lo tateh mishpat ger yatom velo tachavol beged almanah* — do not take as collateral the garment of a widow. Somewhat earlier in the same chapter, the Torah states: *ki tasheh vere'acha mashat me'umah lo tavo el beito la'avot avoto* — if you have lent money to your fellow, do not enter his home to take the collateral. The *pasuk* continues: *bachutz ta'amod* — stand outside — *veha'ish asher attah nosheh bo yotzi elecha et ha'avot hachutzah* — and the person to whom you have lent will bring the collateral out to you. The *pasuk* then addresses the case of a poor borrower: *ve'im ish ani hu lo tishkav ba'avoto* — if he is a poor man, do not sleep with his collateral — *hashev tashiv lo et ha'avot kevo hashemesh* — return it to him before sunset. Also in Devarim 24 appears the prohibition: *lo yachavol reichayim varachev ki nefesh hu chovel* — do not take a millstone or its upper stone as collateral, for that would be taking a life as collateral.

### Rambam's Framework

The Rambam, in the opening of *Hilchos Malveh VeLoveh*, provides a useful enumeration of these *mitzvot*. Regarding the widow, the Rambam states: *almanah bein she'aniyah bein she'ashirah ein memashkenin otah* — whether she is poor or wealthy, one may not take collateral from a widow. This prohibition applies *lo besha'at halva'ah velo shelo besha'at halva'ah* — neither at the time of the loan nor after the time of the loan. The prohibition on taking a widow's collateral is thus broader in scope than its literal phrasing (*beged almanah* — her garment) and applies to any collateral whatsoever. More significantly, it is not limited to the poor. That special rule of the *almanah* will not be the direct subject of the present discussion, but it establishes an important benchmark.

Similarly, the Rambam frames the prohibition on *reichayim varachev* as *shelo yachavol kelim sheosin bahem ochel nefesh* — not to take as collateral utensils used in the preparation of food — and this prohibition likewise is not restricted to a poor borrower. The Rambam's general formulation of the prohibition on entering the borrower's home is *lo lemashkeno bizroa* — do not use force to take collateral — which frames the concern as one of coercive power rather than merely physical trespass.

### Rambam/Ra'avad Debate

A significant debate between the Rambam and the Ra'avad concerns whether these *mitzvot* apply at the time of the loan itself or only afterward. The Rambam holds that all of these restrictions — including those on taking collateral — apply even when collateral is given at the very moment the loan is made. The Ra'avad disagrees, arguing that collateral provided at the time of the loan is fundamentally different in character. At the moment of lending, the parties are, in some sense, on equal footing. The lender has not yet disbursed the money; the relationship of indebtedness and dependence has not yet crystallized. It is only *shelo besha'at halva'ah* — after the loan has been made and the money disbursed — that the lender begins to exert a kind of dominion over the borrower, and it is this exertion of power that the Torah is concerned with restraining. Collateral given at the time of the loan is more akin to a standard business arrangement than to a creditor leveraging the power imbalance to compel compliance.

### Power Dynamic

The underlying concern of these *mitzvot* is precisely that power relationship. As the *pasuk* in Mishlei phrases it — *eved loveh le'ish malveh* — the borrower becomes, in a sense, a servant to the lender. Once I have lent you money, I hold a kind of authority over you; you are literally indebted to me. The Torah's *mitzvot* in this area are largely about preventing the abuse of that relationship. The prohibition on entering the borrower's home is not framed as a general right of privacy; it is specifically addressed to the creditor precisely because of the power he holds. Even the prohibition on *reichayim varachev*, though not explicitly limited to the poor, reflects this concern: certain things are so essential to a person's livelihood that seizing them as collateral would constitute an abuse of the creditor's position.

### Elon Framework

This distinction between the Torah's framing and contemporary legal discourse is worth pausing over. Justice Menachem Elon, the architect of the *mishpat ivri* enterprise, which seeks to bridge the *halachic* heritage with modern democratic legal thinking, once argued that the Torah's prohibition on entering the borrower's home reflects an implicit right of privacy — analogous to protections against unlawful search and seizure in modern constitutional law. One might push back on this framing at two levels. First, what appears in the *pasuk* is a prohibition — a *lo taseh* — not an articulation of a right. There is a meaningful conceptual difference between a system built on obligations and a system built on rights, even where both systems converge on the same practical outcome. The Torah frames the matter as what the lender *may not do*, not as what the borrower *is entitled to demand*. Second, and perhaps more fundamentally, this particular prohibition is grounded in the specific power dynamic of the creditor-borrower relationship. If no loan exists between us, the prohibition simply does not apply; there is no general prohibition on entering someone's home derived from this *pasuk*. Whether or not one could derive a broader right of privacy through *kal vachomer* — as Elon might argue — the *pshat* of the *pasuk* is specifically about restraining the leverage that a creditor holds over a debtor, and it would be premature to read into it a universal right of privacy without qualification.

### Additional Prohibitions

Two additional prohibitions deserve mention before turning to the Mishnah. First, the prohibition of *lo yachavol reichayim varachev* — which the Rambam frames as *shelo yachavol kelim sheosin bahem ochel nefesh* — forbids taking as collateral any implement used in the preparation of food. This prohibition, as noted, is not limited to the poor. Second, and distinct from all the collateral-related *mitzvot*, is the prohibition of *lo tihiyeh lo k'nosheh*, found in Shemos 22:24 in the context of the laws of lending. The Rambam defines this prohibition as follows: *kol hanogesh et he'ani v'hu yodeya she-ein lo* — whoever presses the poor person when he knows that person has no means to repay — *over b'lo taseh shene'emar lo tihiyeh lo k'nosheh* — transgresses a negative commandment. The Rambam extends this further: it is *assur* even to walk where you know the debtor will see you, if your intent is to remind him of the debt or to cause him embarrassment, even if you make no explicit demand. This *mitzvah* captures the essence of the power-dynamic concern most directly: it is not only about the mechanics of seizing collateral or entering homes, but about the very posture of dominance that a creditor may adopt toward a borrower who cannot pay.

## Mishnah Analysis

### Mishnah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ – לֹא יְמַשְׁכְּנֶנּוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין. וְלֹא יִכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ לִיטּוֹל מַשְׁכּוֹנוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמֹד״. הָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנֵי כֵלִים – נוֹטֵל אֶחָד וּמַנִּיחַ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to one **who lends** money to **another** and the debtor fails to repay it at the end of the term of the loan, the creditor **may take collateral from him** to ensure payment **only by means of** an agent of **the court,** not of his own accord. **And he may not enter** the debtor's **house to take his collateral, as it is stated:** "When you lend your neighbor any manner of loan, you shall not go into his house to take his collateral. **You shall stand outside,** and the man to whom you lend shall bring forth the collateral to you outside" (Deuteronomy 24:10–11). If the debtor **had two utensils** of the same kind, the creditor **takes one and leaves** the other **one** in the debtor's possession.

The prohibition on entering the borrower's home flows directly from the *pasuk*. The Mishnah's addition that collateral may only be seized through *beit din* goes beyond the explicit text of the *pasuk*, and as we will see, the Gemara will explore precisely what role *beit din* plays: whether it is merely required to authorize the seizure, or whether only *beit din* — and not the creditor himself — may perform the actual seizure.

### Poor Person's Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְזִיר אֶת הַכַּר בַּלַּיְלָה וְאֶת הַמַּחְרֵישָׁה בַּיּוֹם. וְאִם מֵת – אֵינוֹ מַחְזִיר לְיוֹרְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in addition, the creditor **must return a pillow at night,** as the debtor requires it for sleeping, **and a plow,** which is needed for his daytime work, **by day. If** the debtor **died,** he is **not** required to **return** it **to** the debtor's **heirs.**

If the debtor had only two garments — in effect, his daytime clothing and his nighttime covering — the creditor takes one and leaves the other: *notel echad umaniach echad*. Moreover: the creditor returns the pillow at night and the plow during the day. The specific implements will vary by circumstances, but the principle is that when the collateral is something the poor person needs regularly, the creditor must return it at the time it is needed.

### RSbG Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף לְעַצְמוֹ אֵינוֹ מַחְזִיר אֶלָּא עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּמִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם וּלְהַלָּן מוֹכְרָן בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even to** the debtor **himself** he needs to **return** the collateral each day **only until thirty days** have passed, **and from thirty days onward,** the creditor can **sell them in court,** with the proceeds going toward payment of the debt.

The Mishnah then cites the opinion of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: even for the debtor himself one does not need to continue the daily return of collateral beyond thirty days. After thirty days one may sell the collateral through *beit din*. At that point the creditor is no longer in the phase of holding collateral pending repayment; he is entitled to liquidate the collateral and collect the loan.

### Death Exception

The Mishnah further states: if the debtor dies, one does not return the collateral to his heirs. The obligation to return the collateral on a daily basis is personal to the debtor; once he dies, the creditor may retain the collateral indefinitely and liquidate it to satisfy the loan.

### Timing & Beit Din

The timing of these events merits clarification. As Rashi explains, the scenario of entering the house and seizing collateral arises *after* the loan has come due and the borrower has not paid. During the term of the loan, there is no basis for the creditor to demand collateral. It is only once the loan is in arrears that the creditor acquires a right to take steps to secure repayment. Once that point is reached, the creditor may not act unilaterally — not by entering the borrower's home, and not even by forcibly seizing property in the street. The role of *beit din* is precisely to provide the institutional mechanism for legitimate enforcement, replacing the individual creditor's coercive power with the authority of the court.

### Return Benefits

One might ask: if the creditor is required to return the collateral every night (or every day), what practical value does the collateral serve? The Rambam, drawing on the Gemara, addresses this directly. The daily return and daily re-seizure creates what the Gemara calls *zichron devarim be'alma* — a kind of memorial of the matter, a subtle but persistent reminder of the outstanding debt. Additionally, the Rambam notes two technical legal benefits. Because the creditor holds the collateral, the debt is not annulled by *shemittah* — the Sabbatical year — since the creditor already has the borrower's property in his possession. And if the debtor dies, holding the collateral similarly protects the creditor's ability to collect from the estate. So the daily-return arrangement serves both a psychological and a technical legal function, even if from the borrower's perspective it may seem to offer little protection beyond a nightly reminder.

## Beit Din Entry

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין מְנַתַּח נַתּוֹחֵי – אִין, אֲבָל מַשְׁכּוֹנֵי – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says: An agent** of **the court** who was granted permission to appropriate items from a debtor up to the sum of the loan **may seize** these items from him in the marketplace, **but** is **not** permitted to enter the debtor's house and **take collateral.**

The Gemara opens with a statement of Shmuel: the agent of the *beit din* may seize collateral in the public domain, but he may not enter the home to take the collateral. Shmuel thus distinguishes between two modalities of enforcement. Forcible seizure in the street requires *beit din* involvement and is not available to the private creditor. But even the *shliach beit din*, Shmuel maintains, is limited to the public domain and may not enter the borrower's home.

### Mishnah Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לֹא יְמַשְׁכְּנֶנּוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, מִכְּלָל דִּבְבֵית דִּין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין! וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לֹא יְמַשְׁכְּנֶנּוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, מִכְּלָל דִּבְבֵית דִּין מְמַשְׁכְּנִים, וּבַעַל חוֹב אֲפִילּוּ נַתּוֹחֵי נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ לִיטּוֹל מַשְׁכּוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that one **who lends** money to **another may take collateral from him only by means of** an agent of **the court,** which proves **by inference that** when it is taken **by means of** an agent of **the court** the agent of the court may enter the debtor's house and **take collateral?** [Shmuel's response:] One can then infer: **But** as for the **creditor** himself, he may **not even seize** collateral outside the debtor's house. This is a rabbinic decree so **that** he should **not enter** the debtor's **house to take his collateral.**

The Gemara immediately challenges Shmuel from the Mishnah. The Mishnah states *lo yimshaknenu ela b'veit din* — one may not take collateral except through *beit din* — implying by inference (*miklal d'be-veis din memashkenin*) that through *beit din* one may indeed do so. If the Mishnah implies that *beit din* can take collateral, and if the term *memashkenin* carries the connotation of entering the home (which Rashi confirms, on the grounds that items like *reichayim varachev* would otherwise be found nowhere but inside the home), then the Mishnah appears to permit *beit din* entry into the home.

Shmuel responds that the Mishnah should be read differently: *lo yenatchenu ela be-veis din* — one may not seize in the public domain except through *beit din* — but even *beit din* is restricted to the street and may not enter the home.

The Gemara then offers a further argument that the second clause of the Mishnah — *lo yikanes l'veito litol mashkono* — may itself support Shmuel's position. If the first clause already prohibits the creditor from taking collateral without *beit din*, and the second clause adds *do not enter his home*, who is the subject of that second prohibition? If it is the creditor, the Mishnah has already told us he cannot do anything at all without *beit din*, so the additional prohibition seems redundant. The inference is that the second clause addresses the *shliach beit din* — even he may not enter the home. The Gemara, however, deflects this proof: the Mishnah could be read so that the first clause refers to the creditor (who may not seize at all on his own) and the second clause is an extension (*gezeira*) prohibiting the creditor even from street-level seizure, lest it lead to him entering the home.

### Rav Yosef's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: ״לָא יַחֲבֹל רֵיחַיִם וָרָכֶב״, הָא דְּבָרִים אֲחֵרִים – חֲבֹל. ״לֹא תַחֲבֹל בֶּגֶד אַלְמָנָה״, הָא שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים – תַּחְבֹּל, מַאן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection** to Shmuel's statement from a *baraita*: The Torah states: **"He may not take the lower or upper millstone as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:6). **But** it may be inferred that **other items** may be **taken as collateral.** Similarly, it states: **"You may not take a widow's garment as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:17), **but** clothing that **belongs to others you may take as collateral.** The Gemara analyzes these statements: **Who** is permitted to do so?

Rav Yosef challenges Shmuel from a different direction. The *pasuk* states *lo yachavol reichayim varachev* — do not take millstones as collateral. By implication, one *may* take other items as collateral through entering the home (*hadevarim acherim chavol*). Similarly, *lo tachavol beged almanah* implies that one may take collateral from others. But if the creditor is categorically prohibited from entering the home — as the *pasuk* *lo tavo el beito la'avot avoto* makes clear — then who is the party that is sometimes permitted to enter? The answer must be the *shliach beit din*. The very structure of these prohibitions, which carve out exceptions, presupposes that there is a party — the *shliach beit din* — for whom entry is ordinarily permitted.

### Rav Papa Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב פָּפָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: לְעוֹלָם בְּבַעַל חוֹב, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa, son of Rav Naḥman, interpreted** the *baraita* **before Rav Yosef; and some say** it was **Rav Pappa, son of Rav Yosef,** who interpreted the *baraita* **before Rav Yosef: Actually,** it is referring **to a creditor, and** the Torah's additional prohibition against appropriating certain items is given **so that he will violate two prohibitions** for this action.

Rav Papa responds on behalf of Shmuel: one need not read these prohibitions as implying a permitted party. The Torah may be addressing the creditor throughout and simply adding additional transgressions (*lo taseh* violations) in the specific cases of *reichayim varachev* or *beged almanah*. So if a creditor were to enter the borrower's home and seize his millstone, he would transgress not only the general prohibition on entering but also the specific prohibition on *reichayim varachev*. The existence of a second prohibition does not imply that there exists a party for whom the first prohibition does not apply. This is a sufficient response to Rav Yosef's proof, though it does not affirmatively resolve the question of whether *beit din* may enter.

## Textual Proofs

### Bachutz Ta'amod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמֹד״ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁ״הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר אַתָּה נוֹשֶׁה בוֹ יוֹצִיא״! אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהָאִישׁ״ – לְרַבּוֹת שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a different *baraita* that contradicts Shmuel: **From the implication of that** which **is stated: "You shall stand outside," do I not know that: "And the man to whom you lend shall bring forth outside"** (Deuteronomy 24:11)? **Rather, why** must **the verse state** the inclusive phrase **"And the man** to whom you lend shall bring forth outside"? This serves **to include the agent of the court.**

The Gemara presses further with textual analysis of the *pasuk*. The *pasuk* states *bachutz ta'amod* — stand outside — and it is then self-evident at the *pshat* level that the borrower will bring out the collateral to the creditor who waits outside. The simple reading of the text is that the creditor retains the right to receive collateral, but must receive it outside; the borrower must carry it out. The import of the instruction is not that the creditor forfeits his right to collateral — he does not — but that the manner of collection must respect the borrower's home.

### Ve'ha'ish Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו: שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין כְּלֹוֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **What, is it not** that **the agent of the court** has the same status **as the debtor** himself, indicating that just as the debtor may enter his own house at any time, the court agent may act likewise?

The Gemara observes that the word *veha'ish* in the *pasuk* — *veha'ish asher attah nosheh bo yotzi elecha et ha'avot hachutzah* — appears superfluous. If the *pasuk* has already said *bachutz ta'amod*, it is obvious that someone must bring the collateral out. Why specify *veha'ish*? The Gemara derives: *le'rabos sheliach beit din* — to include the *shliach beit din* as someone who may bring out the collateral.

This leads to two possible readings of the *pasuk*. One reading holds that the word *veha'ish* indicates that either of two parties may bring out the collateral: *asher attah nosheh bo* — the borrower himself — or *ha'ish* — the *shliach beit din*. On this reading, the *shliach beit din* is authorized to enter the home and bring out the collateral. The alternative reading holds that neither the creditor nor the *shliach beit din* may enter; only the borrower himself (*veha'ish* together with *bachutz ta'amod* excludes the *shliach beit din* from entry), and the *shliach beit din* may only stand outside. The question turns on where in the *pasuk* the extra word *veha'ish* is placed interpretively.

### Baraita Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אִם חָבֹל תַּחְבֹּל שַׂלְמַת רֵעֶךָ״ – בִּשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts a further proof. **Come** and **hear** that which the Sages taught: The verse states: **"If you take as collateral your neighbor's garment,** you shall restore it to him until the sun goes down" (Exodus 22:25). **The verse is speaking of an agent of the court.**

The Gemara brings a *baraita* bearing on this question. The *pasuk* states *im chavo tachvol salmas re'echa ad bo hashemesh teshiveno lo* — if you take his garment as collateral, return it before sunset. The *baraita* asks: who has seized this collateral? The *pasuk* uses the language of *chavol*, which implies entering the home — for why else would *chavol* be used? If the creditor is forbidden from entering the home, who performed this entry?

### Baraita 1: Entry Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁבָּא לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ – לֹא יִכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, אֶלָּא עוֹמֵד מִבַּחוּץ, וְהַלָּה מוֹצִיא לוֹ מַשְׁכּוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמֹד וְהָאִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This issue **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **An agent of the court who comes to take collateral** from a debtor may **not enter his house to take the collateral from him. Rather,** the agent **stands outside and the other,** i.e., the debtor, **brings out** the **collateral to him, as it is stated: "You shall stand outside, and the man** to whom you lend shall bring forth the collateral" (Deuteronomy 24:11).

One *baraita* resolves the question explicitly: the *pasuk* speaks of the *shliach beit din*. But then it adds a restriction: the *shliach beit din* who comes to take collateral may not enter the home; he must stand outside, and the borrower brings out the collateral. This *baraita* reads *bachutz ta'amod veha'ish* as prohibiting *both* the creditor *and* the *shliach beit din* from entering; the only party who may enter is the borrower himself, to retrieve and hand over the collateral.

### Baraita 2: Entry Barred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בַּעַל חוֹב שֶׁבָּא לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ – לֹא יִכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, אֶלָּא עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ, וְהַלָּה נִכְנָס וּמוֹצִיא לוֹ מַשְׁכּוֹנוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמֹד״. וּשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁבָּא לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִכְנָס לְבֵיתוֹ וּמְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **A creditor who comes to take collateral from** the debtor **may not enter his house to take his collateral. Rather,** he **stands outside, and the other,** i.e., the debtor, **enters and brings out the collateral to him, as it is stated: "You shall stand outside,** and the man to whom you lend shall bring forth the collateral" (Deuteronomy 24:11). **But** as for **an agent of the court who comes to take collateral from** the debtor, **this** agent **may enter his house and take his collateral.**

A second *baraita*, however, takes the opposite view. The *shliach beit din* who comes to take collateral may not enter the home; he must stand outside, and the borrower brings out the collateral. This *baraita* reads *bachutz ta'amod veha'ish* as prohibiting *both* the creditor *and* the *shliach beit din* from entering; the only party who may enter is the borrower himself, to retrieve and hand over the collateral. But a third *baraita* then completes the picture from the perspective of the creditor: the creditor must stand outside while the borrower enters and brings out the collateral, but the *shliach beit din* in this formulation *is* permitted to enter. The Gemara concludes that this is a dispute among *tannaim* — one position permits the *shliach beit din* to enter the home to seize collateral, while the other prohibits even this, requiring both parties to remain outside and only the borrower to bring the collateral out.

## Restrictions & Needs

### Millstone Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יְמַשְׁכְּנֶנּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁעוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן אוֹכֶל נֶפֶשׁ. וְנוֹתֵן מִטָּה וּמִטָּה וּמַצָּע – לְעָשִׁיר. מִטָּה וּמִטָּה וּמַפָּץ – לְעָנִי. לוֹ – אֲבָל לֹא לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְלֹא לְבָנָיו וְלִבְנוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The agent of the court may **not take** as **collateral from** the debtor **items that** people **use in the preparation of food,** as the debtor needs such items, and the Torah explicitly forbade their removal. **And** the agent **gives,** i.e., leaves behind, **a bed, and** a second **bed, and blankets, for a wealthy person;** and **a bed, and** a second **bed, and a mat, for a poor person.** These items are left **for** the debtor himself, **but not for his wife, and not for his sons or for his daughters,** as the Torah did not obligate the creditor to care for the debtor's family.

The *baraita* that permits the *shliach beit din* to enter the home adds an important qualification: *velo yemashkenenu devarim she'osim bahem ochel nefesh* — even when the *shliach beit din* enters, he may not seize implements used in the preparation of food. This is the prohibition of *lo yachavol reichayim varachev* extended to all items that qualify as *ochel nefesh*, and it applies regardless of whether the debtor is poor or wealthy. The *baraita* then specifies what must be left behind for the debtor when collateral is seized. For a wealthy debtor: *mita umita umatza* — two beds and a fine mattress. For a poor debtor: *mita umita umapatz* — two beds and a simple mat. Crucially, this obligation runs only to the debtor himself: *aval lo l'isha v'lo l'banim v'livnot* — but not to his wife, sons, or daughters. The creditor has a legal relationship with the debtor and is obligated to leave him the bare minimum for himself; the debtor's family obligations are his own concern.

This is the principle of *mesadrin* — arranging or setting aside the debtor's basic necessities — applied in the context of collateral seizure, and the *baraita* draws an explicit parallel: *kederech shemesadrin b'baal chov kach mesadrin b'erchin* — just as one arranges for the debtor in the context of a loan, so too does one arrange in the context of *erchin* (pledges of valuation to the Temple).

### Two Beds Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא דְּאָכֵיל עֲלַהּ וַחֲדָא דְּזָג עֲלַהּ, כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל מִילֵּי יָדַעְנָא אָסוּתַיְיהוּ, לְבַר מֵהָנֵי תְּלָת: מַאן דְּאָכֵיל אֲהִינָא מְרִירָא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, מַאן דְּאָסַר מִיתְנָא דְּכִיתָּנָא רַטִּיבָא אַחַרְצֵיהּ, וּמַאן דְּאָכֵיל נַהֲמָא וְלָא מְסַגֵּי אַרְבְּעָה גַּרְמִידֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why does** the debtor need **two** beds when one should suffice for all his needs? The Gemara answers: **One** is **for** him to **eat on it and one** is **for** him to **sleep on it,** and this is **in accordance** with the opinion of **Shmuel. As Shmuel,** who was a doctor by profession, **said:** With regard to **all items** that cause illness, **I know their cure, apart from these three: One who eats a bitter date [*ahina*] on an empty stomach, one who girds a wet linen belt around his loins, and one who eats bread and does not walk four cubits** afterward.

One might ask: why two beds? The Gemara explains. In the Roman context one would understand immediately: a person eats while reclining on one surface and sleeps on another. But in the Babylonian context, where one does not recline while eating, why does a single person require two beds?

The Gemara answers by invoking a teaching of Shmuel: *kol milei yadana asiyuta'ihu l'bar mehani tlat* — I know the remedy for every ailment except for three. One of those three is *u'man d'achil nahama v'lo masgi arba garmidei* — someone who eats bread and does not walk four *amot* before lying down to sleep. It is for this reason that one requires two beds so that the debtor has one bed in his eating area and one in his sleeping area, ensuring that he must walk between them and thereby maintain his health. The law thus builds in a concern for the debtor's physical wellbeing even in the mechanics of collateral seizure.

### Mesadrin Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּסַדְּרִין בְּעֲרָכִין – כָּךְ מְסַדְּרִין בְּבַעַל חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, say** that **in the manner that arrangements are made for valuations** as explained by the Torah, **so arrangements are made for a debtor.**

The *baraita*'s closing line — *kederech shemesadrin b'baal chov kach mesadrin b'erchin* — is corrected by the Gemara to read in the other direction: *kederech shemesadrin b'erchin kach mesadrin b'baal chov*. The source for the *mesadrin* principle is not the laws of debt collection but the *psukim* of *erchin* in *Vayikra*. The principle of *mesadrin* — leaving the obligated party his basic needs before assessing what he owes — originates in the context of Temple pledges, and the Gemara derives the application to debt collection from there.

### Erchin Analogy

The *erchin* framework works as follows. If a person declares *erech ploni alay* — the valuation of so-and-so is upon me — the Torah fixes set amounts based on age and gender: fifty *shekel*, thirty *shekel*, and so forth. But the Torah also says *v'im mach hu me'erkecha* — if he cannot afford the full amount — *v'he'emido lifnei hakohen* — he is brought before the *kohen*, who assesses his finances and determines what he can actually afford: *al pi asher tasig yad hanoder ya'archenu hakohen*. The *chiddush* is that if a person cannot afford the full *erech*, the *kohen* reassesses and sets the obligation at what the person can genuinely pay, and once that reduced amount is paid, the obligation is discharged in full. The assessment process necessarily begins by establishing what constitutes the person's basic needs — the *mesadrin* — before calculating what remains available for the obligation. This same framework, the Gemara holds, is applied by analogy to the case of debt collection.

## RSbG Reconciliation

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּסַדְּרִין בַּעֲרָכִין – כָּךְ מְסַדְּרִין בְּבַעַל חוֹב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא זַבּוֹנֵי מְזַבְּנִינַן לֵיהּ, סַדּוֹרֵי מְסַדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ?! וּמִי מְזַבְּנִינַן לֵיהּ? וְהָתְנַן: מַחְזִיר אֶת הַכַּר בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֶת הַמַּחְרֵישָׁה בַּיּוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** § **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman: In the manner in which arrangements are made for valuations, so arrangements are made for a debtor.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Now** that it states in the mishna that **we sell** his collateral, do **we arrange for him** to keep part of it? The Gemara asks: **And do we sell it? But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **he returns a pillow at night and a plow by day,** which demonstrates that such items are not sold?

The Gemara immediately raises a sharp challenge. Rav Nachman's student has just taught the principle of *mesadrin l'baal chov* in the name of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — that one must leave the debtor his basic necessities when collecting a debt. But Rav Nachman objects: *hashta zevunei mezavninan lei, sidurei mesadrinan lei?* — if we are liquidating his possessions to pay the debt, how can we simultaneously be obligated to leave things behind for him? The difficulty arises because the student attributed *mesadrin* to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, and the Mishnah records *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* saying that after thirty days, the collateral may be sold through *beit din*. That ruling appears to contemplate liquidating the very items — the pillow, the basic necessities — that were being returned to the debtor daily. There seems to be a direct tension within *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s own position.

### Distinction: Luxury vs. Basic Needs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל זַבּוֹנֵי מְזַבְּנִינַן לֵיהּ, סַדּוֹרֵי מְסַדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ? דִּתְנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף לְעַצְמוֹ אֵינוֹ מַחְזִיר אֶלָּא עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מוֹכְרָן בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The *tanna* taught** the *baraita* **before him in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and this is what** Rav Naḥman **was saying to him: Now,** since **according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel we sell** the collateral, do **we make arrangements for him** to keep it? **As we learned** in the mishna: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even to** the debtor **himself** he needs to **return** the collateral each day **only until thirty days** have passed, **and from that point onward,** the creditor can **sell them in court,** with the proceeds going toward payment of the debt.

One possible resolution is that liquidating collateral and leaving the debtor his basic needs are not mutually exclusive. One can sell *some* of the debtor's property to satisfy the creditor while simultaneously setting aside what is necessary for the debtor's basic subsistence. The analogy is instructive: if a debtor owns a large mansion, one might sell it and use the proceeds to satisfy the loan while using the remainder to house the debtor in more modest accommodation. Liquidation does not necessarily mean leaving the debtor with nothing — it means selling property beyond what is needed for basic needs.

### Bnei Melachim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַאי סְבָרָא, לֵיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ. דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּנֵי מְלָכִים הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If it enters your mind** that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel maintains this reasoning, there is nothing that is unfit for him. As Abaye said: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva all hold** that **all Israel are the children of kings.**

The Gemara, however, objects that this resolution cannot work for *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* specifically. If *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that one must leave the debtor his basic needs, what counts as a basic need? The answer, for *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, is governed by his broader principle — and therein lies the problem: *leit leh midi d'lo chazi leh* — there is nothing that would not qualify as fitting for him.

### Bnei Melachim Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דִּתְנַן: לֹא אֶת הַלּוּף וְלָא אֶת הַחַרְדָּל. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַתִּיר בַּלּוּף, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַאֲכָל לְעוֹרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the cases in which the *tanna'im* apply the above principle. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** applies this principle, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 126b): One may **not** move either raw **arum or** raw **mustard** on Shabbat, as these are unfit for consumption when they are raw, and are therefore set-aside [*muktze*]. **In** the case of **arum, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits** moving it **because it is** considered **food for ravens,** which wealthy Jews would breed for purposes of ornamentation and amusement.

This is because Abaye establishes that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, Rabbi Shimon, Rabbi Yishmael*, and *Rabbi Akiva* all hold *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein* — all Jews are regarded as descendants of royalty and are entitled to a correspondingly elevated standard of living. The Gemara demonstrates this from several sources. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position on *muktzeh* holds that *luf* (a certain legume) is not *muktzeh* on *Shabbat* because it can serve as food for hunting birds — *mipnei shehu ma'achal l'orvin* — since all Jews are in principle like princes who might keep such birds. *Rabbi Shimon* explicitly states *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein* in the context of applying the leniency of anointing with rose oil on *Shabbat* to all Jews, not only to actual royalty, since all Jews share that elevated status.

### Royal Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ נוֹשִׁין בּוֹ אֶלֶף זוּז וְלָבוּשׁ אִיצְטְלָא בַּת מֵאָה מָנֶה – מַפְשִׁיטִין אוֹתָהּ מִמֶּנּוּ וּמַלְבִּישִׁים אוֹתוֹ אִיצְטְלָא הָרְאוּיָה לוֹ. וְתָנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְתָנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל רְאוּיִן לְאוֹתָהּ אִיצְטְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva** also hold this opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If creditors **were claiming one thousand dinars** from someone, **and** he was **wearing a cloak [*itztela*] worth ten thousand dinars,** the court **strips it from him** and sells it for his debt, **and dresses him** in **a cloak appropriate for him,** as one who is in debt does not have the right to withhold payment while possessing such an expensive garment. **And** it was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, and** it was similarly **taught in the name of Rabbi Akiva: All of the Jewish people are fit for that cloak.**

The consequence for debt collection is stark. If *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein*, then when the court asks what constitutes the debtor's basic necessities — what must be left behind after liquidation — the debtor could in principle claim that his gilded bed with embroidered silk sheets is a basic necessity befitting his royal status. One could not simply sell his expensive bed and replace it with a simpler one, because the doctrine of *bnei melachim* means one cannot downgrade the debtor's standard of living. The distinction between luxury items and basic necessities, which would otherwise provide a clean resolution to the tension, collapses under the weight of this doctrine.

### Unresolved Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא, דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ מַאי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ, וּמְזַבְּנִינַן מַאי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ? בִּשְׁלָמָא כַּר וָכֶסֶת חֲזֵי לֵיהּ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי. אֶלָּא מַחְרֵישָׁה לְמַאי חַזְיָא? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַבָּה: מַחְרֵישָׁה דְּכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the issue at hand: **And with regard to what entered our minds initially, that** according to the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the creditor **returns to him that which is fit for him and we sell what is not fit for him,** there is a difficulty, as the examples given in the mishna are bedding and a plow. **Granted,** this is understandable with regard to a pillow or cushion, as it can mean that the court sells these items only if the difference in cost **between** the ones he has and less expensive ones that are also **fit for him** suffices to repay the debt. **But for what is a plow fit?** In other words, how can there be a difference in price in this case? **Rava bar Rabba said:** This is referring to **a silver plow,** which is an ornament and not used for work.

The Gemara thus leaves standing a genuine tension within *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position. On one hand, he holds the daily return obligation for a full thirty days, after which the creditor may liquidate the collateral. On the other hand, he holds *mesadrin* — that the debtor must be left with his basic necessities. And on the third hand, his doctrine of *bnei melachim* renders every item a candidate for "basic necessity." How these three positions are to be reconciled — particularly for *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva*, whose positions the Gemara begins to cite — is left as an open question that appears to continue to the following daf.
