# Meilah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Rashi Auth.

### Opening

Daf 8 marks the beginning of the second chapter of Maseches *Me'ilah*. One reason this *daf* is notably short is that *Tosafot* addresses all of the *mishnayos* of the chapter together at the outset, providing a unified explanation at the opening of the *perek*.

### Rashi Auth.

This occasion also calls for a clarification about the *rishonim* on *Me'ilah*. An earlier suggestion that the *Rashi* in Maseches *Kerisos* was not authentic *Rashi* turns out to be mistaken. Although Zunz advanced that claim, contemporary scholarship appears to have largely rejected it, and the *Rashi* in *Kerisos* is generally accepted as genuine — even if one particularly lengthy *Rashi* there may be a later gloss. The situation is quite the reverse in *Me'ilah*: there is substantial scholarly opinion holding that the *Rashi* commentary on *Me'ilah* is not actually *Rashi*'s work. Supporting this, *Tosafot* in *Kerisos* frequently cites *Pirush Hakontres* or *Pirush Rashi*, whereas *Tosafot* in *Me'ilah* appears never to do so. This may explain why *Tosafot* in *Me'ilah* so often performs the explanatory function normally associated with *Rashi* — because, in all likelihood, the *rishonim* composing *Tosafot* here did not have an authoritative *Rashi* commentary on which to rely.

## Tumah & Pesul

### Ch. 2 Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשָׁה, נִמְלְקָה – הוּכְשְׁרָה לְהִפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים וּבְלִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One** who derives benefit **from a bird sin offering is** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property **from** the moment **that it was consecrated.** Once the nape of its neck **was pinched, it was rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed** in a ritual bath **that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, **and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete his purification process, e.g., a *zav* and a leper, who are not yet permitted to partake of sacrificial meat; **and through** its blood being **left overnight,** i.e., if its blood was not sprinkled before sunset.

The first chapter of *Me'ilah* devoted considerable attention to *pesulim* — cases of disqualification and how they affect status changes — and concluded with the fundamental rule: that *zerikas hadam* by *kodshei kodashim* removes the meat from *me'ilah*, while *zerikas hadam* of *kodshim kalim* brings the animal more fully into the domain of *me'ilah*. The second chapter now proceeds in a more apodictic mode, setting out the straightforward law — without special cases — of when and how the status of *me'ilah* changes across the full range of *korbanos*. Interestingly, *Tosafot* chooses to open not with the most standard type of *korban* but with the *chatas ha'of*, the bird sin-offering. The *mishnah* states: *chatas ha'of mo'alin bah* — there is *me'ilah* on a bird *chatas* — *mishehukdeshah*, from the moment it is sanctified. It is *kodshei kodashim*, and therefore subject to *me'ilah* from the outset.

### Av Hatumah

To follow the *Gemara*'s analysis, it is necessary to understand the basic framework of *tumah* transmission. A source of *tumah* — a corpse, a *zav*, and the like — is called an *av hatumah*. The exception is a *meis*, which stands at the highest level, *avi avos hatumah*. When an *av hatumah* comes into contact with a person or object, that person or object becomes a *rishon*, a first-degree derivative of *tumah*. When the *rishon* then contacts something else, that becomes a *sheni*. In principle, only food can become a *sheni*; people and vessels do not become *sheni* from contact with a *rishon*. Most categories stop at *sheni* — food touching food does not produce a further degree of *tumah*. However, when one deals with items of higher *kedushah*, the capacity to receive *tumah* extends further. *Terumah* can become a *shlishi*, and *kodshim* can reach a *revi'i*.

### Pesul vs. Tamei

When something has contracted *tumah* but is no longer capable of transmitting it further, it is described not as *tamei* but as *nifsul* — invalidated. A *shlishi* of *terumah* is called *nifsul*, not *tamei*, because although it may not be eaten, it cannot pass the *tumah* on. Similarly, a *revi'i* of *kodshim* is *nifsul* rather than *tamei*. The distinction is significant: calling something *tamei* implies it retains the capacity to convey *tumah* to another object; calling it *pasul* means it is disqualified for use but cannot propagate the *tumah* further.

### Tevul Yom

Two special categories require attention here. A person who has immersed in a *mikveh* but is awaiting nightfall to complete the purification process is called a *tvul yom*. A person who has immersed but still needs to bring a *korban* — applicable to a *zav*, *zavah*, *yoledes*, or *metzora* — is called a *mechusar kippurim*, one lacking the *korban* that will complete their purification. Both of these individuals carry an extremely residual form of *tumah*. A *tvul yom* functions at roughly the level of a *sheni*, meaning that in ordinary circumstances contact with *chullin* would have no effect, since a *sheni* cannot transmit *tumah* to non-sacred food. However, because *terumah* and *kodshim* possess elevated *kedushah*, even this residual *tumah* has an effect on them — though only a disqualifying one, not a full *tumah*. The *mishnah*'s statement that the animal is *huchshara l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim* — rendered susceptible to disqualification through a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim* — means it becomes *pasul*, not *tamei*. The *tumah* of a *tvul yom* is so attenuated that it can disqualify, but it cannot pass the disqualification further.

### Shechita Process

Before the animal is slaughtered it remains a living creature; it is not yet considered food and has not achieved the status of something fit to be brought or eaten. Therefore, even if something *tamei* comes into contact with it, nothing happens. Once *melikah* is performed on the bird — the *kohen* cuts the nape of the neck, rendering the bird dead — it now becomes fit to be placed on the *mizbe'ach* and, in principle, to be eaten, and the word *huchshara* applies: it has become *hechsher*, susceptible to *tumah*. The same word *hechsher* is used when liquids touch food and render it susceptible, as derived from the verse *ve-chi yitten mayim al zera* (Vayikra 11). Once the bird is dead and in the category of food or of something fit for the *mizbe'ach*, it has the *hechsher* needed to become *pasul* through contact with a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*.

The *mishnah* adds *u-ve-linah* — there can also be *linah*, the disqualification that results from the blood or the meat being left overnight. *Tosafot* emphasizes that the *linah* mentioned at this stage refers specifically to *linat ha-dam*, leaving the blood overnight. *Linat ha-basar*, the *linah* of the meat, presents a problem: the clock for *linat ha-basar* ordinarily starts only after *zerikas hadam*, since it is only then that the meat becomes fit to be eaten. This is confirmed by the final *mishnayos* of the previous chapter, which state *le-achar zerikah chayav alav mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei* — that it is only after *zerikah* that the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* apply, and only then is one *chayav kares* for eating the meat. Thus the *linah* here is *linat ha-dam*: if the blood was caught but left until the next morning, it is *pasul* and cannot be used.

Once the blood is squirted — *huzah damah* — the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* all take effect: *chayavin alav mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. After *zerikas hadam*, when the *korban* is valid and the meat is fit to be eaten, these three statuses are established, and one is *chayav kares* if one eats the meat in a state of *piggul*, *nosar*, or *tamei*. And: *ve-ein bo me'ilah* — there is no *me'ilah*. By a bird *chatas*, there are no *emurim*; the *kohanim* eat all of the meat. So once *zerikah* renders the meat permissible to the *kohanim*, *me'ilah* ceases entirely.

## Chatas Ha'Of

### Chatas Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּזָּה דָּמָהּ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא, וְאֵין בָּהּ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Once **its blood was sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while **ritually impure. But there is no** liability for **misuse** of consecrated property, because after the blood is sprinkled it is permitted for priests to partake of its meat and it is no longer consecrated exclusively to God.

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* returns to examine what the *mishnah* teaches. The general picture is now familiar: *zerikas hadam* changes the status for *me'ilah* with respect to the meat, and *kabalas hadam* — the catching of the blood — is the moment that renders the bird *kodshim* susceptible to *tumah* and *linah*.

### Pesul/Tamei Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי הוּכְשְׁרָה לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה. לִיפָּסֵל – אִין, אֲבָל לְטַמּוֹיֵי – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna **teaches:** From the time the nape of its neck was pinched, the bird sin offering **was rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.** It can be inferred from here that **yes,** it is susceptible **to disqualification, but** it is **not** fit **to** render other items ritually **impure.**

The *Gemara* homes in on a conceptual difficulty. The *mishnah* states that after *melikah*, the bird is rendered susceptible to *hipasel bitvul yom* — disqualification through a *tvul yom*. The language is precise: *yipasel*, it becomes *pasul*, but not *yitamei*, it does not become *tamei* in the full sense. Now, given that a *tvul yom* functions as a *sheni*, touching *kodshim* should produce a *shlishi*, which in the domain of *kodshim* would be called *tamei* — because *kodshim* can reach a *revi'i*. Why then does the *mishnah* use only the language of *pesul* and not *tumah*? This is precisely the tension the *Gemara* addresses: the *mishnah* implies the *tvul yom* only *posels* — disqualifies — the *kodshim*, but does not render it *tamei* in the sense of a *tumah* that can be transmitted further.

### Tannaim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: טְבוּל יוֹם תְּחִלָּה לַקֹּדֶשׁ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מְטַמֵּא אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, וּפוֹסֵל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהוּא פּוֹסֵל מַשְׁקֵה תְרוּמָה וְאוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה, כָּךְ הוּא פּוֹסֵל מַשְׁקֵה קֹדֶשׁ וְאוֹכְלֵי קֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** In light of the above inference, the Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** The Gemara answers: It **is** the opinion of **the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Teharot* 1:4) that **Abba Shaul says:** With regard to **one who immersed that day,** until sunset he is treated as one who is impure with **first-**degree impurity with regard **to sacrificial** food. In other words, an item of sacrificial food that he touches assumes the status of second-degree impurity. A second item that comes into contact with the first item of food assumes third-degree impurity. If a third item comes into contact with the second item, it assumes fourth-degree impurity, i.e., it may not be eaten but does not impart impurity to other items. **Rabbi Meir says:** One who immersed that day is considered impure with second-degree impurity, even with regard to sacrificial food, and therefore he **renders sacrificial** food **impure and disqualifies *teruma*. And the Rabbis say: Just as he** merely **disqualifies *teruma* liquids and *teruma* foods,** without them becoming impure to a degree that their impurity is transferred to another item, **so too, he** only **disqualifies sacrificial liquids and sacrificial foods.** Apparently, the mishna here is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, not the opinions of Abba Shaul and Rabbi Meir.

The *Gemara* asks: *matnisin mani?* — whose view does our *mishnah* reflect? It concludes: *Rabanan hi* — the position of the *Rabbanan* in a *braisa* cited from the *Tosefta Teharos*, where there is a three-way dispute.

*Abba Shaul* holds that when a *tvul yom* touches *kodshim*, he is treated stringently — as a *rishon*, not a *sheni*. This means whatever he touches becomes a *sheni*, and the *korban* is in that way *tamei* at the level of *sheni*.

*Rabbi Meir* holds that a *tvul yom* is a *sheni*. When he touches *terumah*, it becomes a *shlishi* — which is called *pasul* for *terumah*, since *terumah* goes no further than *shlishi*. When he touches *kodshim*, it becomes a *shlishi* as well, but since *kodshim* can reach *revi'i*, a *shlishi* of *kodshim* is called *tamei* — it can still transmit the *tumah* one more step to a *revi'i*. So *Rabbi Meir* says *metamei es hakodesh ufosel es haterumah* — he renders *kodshim* *tamei* and *terumah* *pasul*.

The *Rabbanan* of the *braisa* take a third position: *keshem shehu posel mashkeh trumah ve-ochlei trumah, kach hu posel mashkeh kodesh ve-ochlei kodesh* — just as he *posels* the food and drink of *terumah*, so too he only *posels* the food and drink of *kodshim*, without making it *tamei* in a transmissible sense. This is puzzling at first glance: if he is a *sheni*, he should render *kodshim* a *shlishi*, which is *tamei*. But the *Rabbanan*'s position is that whatever one calls the formal level of the *tvul yom*, his *tumah* is so residual — so attenuated after the *mikveh* immersion — that it can only go one step further and then stops, regardless of whether that one step is into *terumah* or *kodshim*.

### Rava's Resol.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְאַבָּא שָׁאוּל מַעֲלָה עָשׂוּ בַּקֳּדָשִׁים, שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן לִטְבוּל יוֹם כָּרִאשׁוֹן, לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר כְּאוֹכֶל שֵׁנִי. לְרַבָּנַן, כֵּיוָן דִּטְבַל – קְלַשׁ טוּמְאָה, פָּסוּל – מְשַׁוֵּי, טָמֵא – לָא מְשַׁוֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** rejects this analysis and **says:** The mishna can be explained even in accordance with the opinions of Abba Shaul and Rabbi Meir, as they might agree that by Torah law one who immersed that day only disqualifies the food and does not render it impure. But **according to Abba Shaul,** the Sages **established a higher standard with regard to consecrated** items, and therefore the **Sages equated** one who **immersed that day to** one who is impure with **first-**degree ritual impurity. **According to Rabbi Meir,** the Sages equated the impurity of one who immersed that day **to food** of **second-**degree ritual impurity, but **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis** there is no additional impurity by rabbinic law. Their reasoning is that **since he has immersed,** although he is not completely pure, **his** level of ritual **impurity is** relatively **weak.** Therefore, he **renders** a sin offering **disqualified,** but he does **not render** it ritually **impure.**

*Rava* resolves the apparent tension between the tannaitic positions and our *mishnah* by distinguishing between the *d'Oraisa* level and the *d'rabbanan* level. *D'Oraisa*, it is clear from the Torah — *uva ha-shemesh ve-taher* and *ve-achar yochal min ha-kodashim* — that a *tvul yom* may not eat *kodshim* and has some effect on their status. At the *d'Oraisa* level, everybody agrees that a *tvul yom* is *posel* *terumah* and *kodshim*: he renders them unusable, disqualified, but the *tumah* does not propagate further. This is precisely what our *mishnah* says, and it reflects the agreed-upon *d'Oraisa* position.

The debate among *Abba Shaul*, *Rabbi Meir*, and the *Rabbanan* is entirely at the *d'rabbanan* level — a rabbinic stringency regarding how to characterize the *tvul yom*'s status more precisely. *Abba Shaul* holds that *d'rabbanan* the Rabbis treated him like a *rishon*, so that he makes things into a *sheni*. *Rabbi Meir* holds that *d'rabbanan* he functions as a *sheni*, making *kodshim* into a *shlishi* that is *tamei*. But all of this is *d'rabbanan*. Our *mishnah*, which speaks only of *pesul*, represents the *d'Oraisa* baseline on which everyone agrees. The conceptual explanation for why the *d'Oraisa* effect is only *pesul* and not transmissible *tumah* is that the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* after immersion is so residual that it can act on one further object but cannot propagate beyond it — not because of the number of degrees removed, but because the intrinsic weakness of this *tumah* limits its reach. The same *din* applies equally to *mechusar kippurim*, as the *braisa* concludes: *ve-hu ha-din bi-mechusar kippurim*.

### Me'ilah Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּזָּה דָּמָהּ – חַיָּיבִין כּוּ׳. מְעִילָה הוּא דְּלֵיכָּא, אֲבָל אִיסּוּרָא – אִיכָּא. וְאַמַּאי? הָא מָמוֹנָא דְכֹהֲנִים הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to a sin offering: Once **its blood was sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* for its consumption due to violation of the prohibition of *piggul*, and the prohibition of *notar*, and the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while ritually impure, but there is no liability for misuse of consecrated property. The Gemara infers: **There is no** liability for **misuse** of consecrated property, **but there is a prohibition** against deriving benefit from it even after the blood has been sprinkled. **But why** is there such a prohibition? **Isn't** the meat of the sin offering **the property of the priests** after the blood has been sprinkled? Accordingly, they would therefore be permitted to consume this meat.

Having established the *tumah* framework, the *Gemara* turns to the *me'ilah* dimension. The *mishnah* states *ve-ein bo me'ilah* — after *zerikas hadam* there is no *me'ilah* — which implies, by its very formulation, that there is nevertheless still some prohibition (*issura ika*). The *Gemara* asks: why should there be any remaining prohibition? After you sprinkle the blood, the *kohanim* are supposed to eat the meat — it belongs to them. What problem remains?

The *Gemara* answers with *Rabbi Chanina*'s explanation, the same answer given on the preceding *daf*: this follows the position of *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds that *zerikah* takes meat that left the *Beis Hamikdash* — *yotzei* — out of the category of *me'ilah*, and yet such meat remains forbidden for consumption (*de-lav l'achilah hi*). So even after *zerikah*, meat that had exited the *Beis Hamikdash* is no longer subject to *me'ilah* but is still *assur* to eat. The *mishnah*'s implication that some prohibition remains is thus accounted for by this specific case of *yotzei* according to *Rabbi Akiva*.

## Mitzuy Debate

### Rav Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב. דְּתָנֵי רַב: ״הוּזָּה דָּמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the *halakha* of a bird sin offering. After the nape of its neck has been pinched and the blood sprinkled, the neck of the bird is pressed onto the side of the altar so that the blood is squeezed out and trickles down to the base of the altar. **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** Failure to **squeeze out** the blood from **a bird sin offering** after sprinkling the blood **does not invalidate** the offering or prevent atonement, **as Rav teaches** in his version of the mishna: Once **its blood was sprinkled.**

With the *me'ilah* analysis of the *chatas ha'of* concluded, the *Gemara* transitions to a related and fundamental question: is *mitzuy* — the squeezing out of the blood after the initial squirt — an indispensable act for the *korban*? The Torah describes two distinct actions by the bird *chatas*: first the blood squirts from the severed neck against the wall of the *mizbe'ach*, and then the neck is pressed against the wall and the remaining blood is squeezed out. The question is whether this squeezing is part of *zerikas hadam* proper — and therefore *me'akev*, indispensable — or whether it functions analogously to *shfichas shirayim* in an animal *korban*, namely the pouring of the remaining blood at the base of the *mizbe'ach* after the main *zerikah*, which is not *me'akev*.

*Rav Huna* in the name of *Rav* states: *mitzuy chatas ha'of eino me'akev* — the squeezing out is not indispensable. In support he notes that our *mishnah* uses the language *huzah damah* — the blood was squirted — as the critical moment after which all the status changes occur. If the *mishnah* marks the squirting as the pivotal act, the squeezing that follows cannot be what is *me'akev*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב. וְתָנֵי רַב: ״מִיצָּה דָּמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says:** Failure to **squeeze out** the blood from **a bird sin offering** after sprinkling the blood **does invalidate** the offering and prevents atonement. **And Rav teaches** in his version of the mishna: Once **its blood was squeezed out.** Only after the blood has been squeezed out is the atonement complete and the bird may be eaten.

*Rav Ada bar Ahava*, also in the name of *Rav*, takes the opposite position: *mitzuy chatas ha'of me'akev* — the squeezing is indispensable. He argues that our *mishnah* should be read differently — *mitzah damah*, the blood was squeezed out — so that the pivotal moment is the completed *mitzuy*, not merely the initial squirt.

### Mishna Proof

The two *Amoraim* thus differ over the precise language of the *mishnah*. According to *Rav Huna*, the *mishnah* says *huzah damah*, the blood was squirted, and this is the critical moment. According to *Rav Ada bar Ahava*, the *mishnah* reads *mitzah damah*, the blood was squeezed, making the full *mitzuy* the determinative act.

### Baraita Cite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה אֶל יְסוֹד הַמִּזְבֵּחַ חַטָּאת הִיא״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה... חַטָּאת הִיא״, אֶלָּא לְרַב הוּנָא, מַאי ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to the statement of Rav Huna. **Come** and **hear** a verse in the Torah: "And he shall sprinkle of the blood of the sin offering upon the side of the altar; **and the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out at the base of the altar, it is a sin offering"** (Leviticus 5:9). **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav Adda bar Ahava,** who holds that the squeezing out of the blood is essential, **this is as it is written: "And the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out** at the base of the altar, it **is a sin offering."** This clause indicates that only after the blood has been squeezed out is it considered a valid sin offering. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Huna, what** does the verse mean when it states: **"The remainder** of the blood shall be squeezed out at the base of the altar, it is a sin offering"?

The *Gemara* now attempts to resolve the dispute from a *baraisa*. The Torah states regarding the bird *chatas* (Vayikra 5): *u-malak et rosho mi-mul orpo ve-lo yavdil ve-hizah mi-dam ha-chatas al kir ha-mizbe'ach ve-ha-nishar ba-dam yimatze el yesod ha-mizbe'ach chatas hi*. The language *chatas hi* — it is a *chatas* — appears at the end of the verse, after the instruction to squeeze the remaining blood to the base. The *Gemara* considers whether this formulation means that only after the squeezing is performed does the offering attain the status of a valid *chatas*, which would make the *mitzuy* indispensable.

*Bishlama l'Rav Ada bar Ahava haynu d'chtiv ve-ha-nishar ba-dam yimatze chatas hi* — according to *Rav Ada bar Ahava* this reads naturally: the verse tells you that only after squeezing the blood is it a *chatas*, confirming that *mitzuy* is *me'akev*. But according to *Rav Huna*, what is the significance of *ve-ha-nishar* and *chatas hi*? The answer given, following the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*, is that *she-im nish'ar* — the verse speaks conditionally: *if* there is remaining blood, you should squeeze it out. It does not mandate that there must be remaining blood; it only instructs what to do when there happens to be some. As for *chatas hi*, the *Gemara* explains that this refers back to the beginning of the verse — *ve-hizah mi-dam ha-chatas al kir ha-mizbe'ach* — and it is after the *hazaah*, the initial squirting, that the offering is declared a *chatas*. The concluding *chatas hi* thus confirms the status established by the squirting, not by the squeezing.

### Repetition Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: שֶׁאִם נִשְׁאַר. וּמַאי ״חַטָּאת הִיא״? אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rav Huna explains this verse **as it is taught** in a *baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael:** The verse teaches **that if** any of the blood **remains** inside the bird it must be squeezed out, but there is no requirement to ensure that blood must remain so that it can be squeezed out. Consequently, even if one does not squeeze out any blood on the side of the altar, the offering is valid. **And what** is the meaning of the phrase: "It **is a sin offering"?** This is referring **to the first clause** of the verse, i.e., it is a valid sin offering only if the blood is sprinkled on the side of the altar.

This invokes a broader principle of *kodshim* interpretation. Normally, whenever the Torah mentions a requirement, one might assume it is *me'akev* — that failure to fulfill it invalidates the *korban*. But in the domain of *kodshim*, the general assumption is that a detail mentioned once is a *mitzvah* but not *me'akev*, and only when the Torah repeats the requirement — *shana alav ha-katuv le-akev* — do we conclude that it is indispensable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גַּבֵּי מִנְחָה דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת״. הָכִי נָמֵי שֶׁאִם נִיתּוֹתַר? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא: ״מִסׇּלְתָּהּ וּמִשַּׁמְנָהּ עַל כׇּל לְבֹנָתָהּ״ – פְּרָט שֶׁחָסְרָה סׇלְתָּהּ וְחָסְרָה שַׁמְנָהּ וְחָסְרָה לְבוֹנָתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: If that is so,** consider the fact **that it is written with regard to a meal offering: "But that which is left** of the meal offering shall be Aaron's and his sons'; it is a thing most holy of the offerings of the Lord made by fire" (Leviticus 2:3). Does this **also** mean **that if** some of the meal offering **remains** then it is given to the priests, but there is no need to ensure that some of it remains *ab initio*? **And if you would say** this is **indeed** the case, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And he shall bring it to Aaron's sons the priests; and he shall remove his handful **of its fine flour, and of its oil, together with all its frankincense;** and the priest shall make its memorial part smoke upon the altar, an offering made by fire, of a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Leviticus 2:2)? This verse **excludes** those situations **where** some of **its flour was missing, or** some of **its oil was missing, or** some of **its frankincense was missing,** in which case the priest may not place the meal offering on the fire. This indicates that one must ensure that some of the flour, oil, and frankincense remain.

*Rav Acha brei de-Rava* raised a challenge to *Rav Ashi*: apply the same logic to the *mincha*. The Torah says *ve-ha-noteret min ha-mincha yochlu Aharon u-vanav* — the remainder of the *mincha* shall be eaten by *Aharon* and his sons. By the same reasoning, should we say that if nothing remains — say, all the *shirayim* spilled — there is no problem? A *baraisa* on 17a explicitly rejects this: *mi-soltah u-mi-shamnah al kol levonatah* — if some of the flour, oil, or *levonah* was missing when the *komatz* was offered, it is invalid. There must be a proper *noteret*, a remainder. The *Gemara* responds by distinguishing the two cases. By the *mincha*, the Torah uses the word *ve-ha-noteret* twice, and the repetition is what makes the requirement *me'akev*. By the *chatas ha'of*, the Torah uses the formulation *ve-ha-nishar* only once, and therefore it is not *me'akev* — it is a *mitzvah* to perform the *mitzuy* if there is remaining blood, but the *korban* is nevertheless valid if the *mitzuy* is not performed.

### Menachos Par.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם כְּתִיב ״וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת״, קְרָא יַתִּירָא כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response to the difficulty of Rav Aḥa, son of Rava: In general, the terms "remainder" or "leftover" refer to a situation where there happened to be some of the item remaining. Yet, the case of the meal offering is unique, as **there it is written: "But that which is left** of the meal offering shall be Aaron's and his sons'; it is a thing most holy of the offerings of the Lord made by fire" (Leviticus 2:3). This part of **the verse** is **superfluous,** as it appears in Leviticus 2:10 as well. It is therefore derived from the repetition of this phrase that in the specific case of a meal offering one must ensure that some of the items remain. This requirement does not apply to a bird sin offering.

The challenge from *Rav Acha bar Rava* using the case of *menachos* thus serves to sharpen the principle: the *me'akev* status of a repeated requirement is demonstrated by the *mincha*, where the double mention of *ha-noteret* establishes indispensability, whereas the single mention of *ve-ha-nishar* by the bird *chatas* is not sufficient to establish the same.

### Mitzuy Concl.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: שֶׁאִם נִשְׁאַר בַּדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself,** i.e., the *baraita* cited above: **The *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael** taught **that if** any **of** the **blood remains** inside the bird it must be squeezed out, but there is no requirement to ensure that blood remains for this purpose. Consequently, even if one does not squeeze out the blood on the side of the altar, the offering is valid.

The *Gemara* also presents a *baraisa* in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael*'s school: the squeezing is required only if blood remains — *she-nishtayyer ba-dam*. Even then, according to one version, it is not *me'akev*. The *Gemara* also notes a parallel dispute in *Zevachim*, where *Rav Pappa* identifies the question of whether *mitzuy chatas ha'of* is *me'akev* as the subject of a disagreement attributed to *Rabbi Yishmael* himself — with one tradition saying it is not *me'akev* and another saying it is. The *Gemara* concludes: *trei tannaei aliba d'Rabbi Yishmael* — there are two tannaitic traditions in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael* on this point, just as there are two *Amoraic* traditions in the name of *Rav*. The final determination is thus: *mitzuy* is not *me'akev* if there is no remaining blood, and even when blood does remain, the dominant view is that failure to perform *mitzuy* does not invalidate the *korban*.

## Olat Ha'Of

### Olat Ha'Of

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹלַת הָעוֹף – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשָׁה. נִמְלְקָה – הוּכְשְׁרָה לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה. מִיצָה דָּמָהּ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא. וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One is** liable for **misusing a bird burnt offering from** the moment **that it was consecrated.** When the nape of its neck **was pinched, it was rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed** in a ritual bath **that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.** Once **its blood was squeezed out, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating **it, due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while **ritually impure. And** as it may not be eaten, **one is** liable for **its misuse until it leaves to the place of the ashes,** where it is burned.

The *mishnah* now proceeds to the *olat ha'of*, the bird *olah*. The structure of status changes parallels that of the *chatas ha'of*: *mo'alin bah mishehikdshah* — *me'ilah* applies from the moment of sanctification. Once *melikah* is performed — the bird is killed — *huchshera l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim* — it becomes susceptible to disqualification through a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*, and *linah* applies to the blood. Note that by the *olah*, the Torah speaks only of *mitzuy* — the squeezing out of the blood — rather than of an initial squirting. Therefore the *mishnah* states: *mitzat damah* — once you squeeze out the blood — *chayavin aleha mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. At that point the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* all take effect, and one is *chayav* for those if applicable.

Crucially, unlike the *chatas ha'of*, *me'ilah* does not end here. Since a bird *olah* is entirely consumed on the *mizbe'ach* and nobody — not even the *kohanim* — benefits from its meat, *zerikah* does not change the *me'ilah* status. *Me'ilah* continues — *u-mo'alin bah* — *ad she-tetzei l'veit ha-deshen*, until the ash and the remains are taken out to the place of ashes outside the camp. The underlying principle is that *me'ilah* persists as long as the *mitzvah* of the *korban* has not been fully completed — *na'aseis mitzvato*. This is actually the source for the broader rule applied to *issurei hana'ah*: something remains in the domain of *Kodshei Hashem* as long as its appointed purpose has not yet been fulfilled. By a bird *olah*, the final *mitzvah* is the removal of the ashes to the *beis ha-deshen*, and only then does *me'ilah* cease.

### Olah Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעוֹלָה – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשָׁה. נִשְׁחֲטָה – הוּכְשְׁרָה לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה. מִשֶּׁנִּזְרַק דָּמָהּ – חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא. וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּעוֹרוֹת, אֲבָל מוֹעֲלִין בַּבָּשָׂר עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of the burnt offering from** the moment **that it was consecrated.** Once **it was slaughtered it was rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.** Once **its blood was sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating **it, due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while **ritually impure. And one is not** liable for **misuse of the hides, but one is** liable for **misuse of the flesh until it leaves to the place of the ashes.**

The *mishnah* turns to the ordinary animal *olah* — a sheep or cow. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification: *mo'alin bah mishehikdshah*. After *shechitah*, the animal is dead, no longer alive, and *huchshera l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim uvlinah* — susceptible to disqualification from a *tvul yom*, *mechusar kippurim*, and *linah* of the blood. After *zerikas hadam* — *mishezarak damah* — the *korban* is valid and the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* take hold: *chayavin alav mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. These statuses apply even though the meat of an *olah* may not be eaten; the *korban* is fit for the *mizbe'ach*, and that suffices.

Regarding *me'ilah*, there is one significant change after *zerikah*: *ve-ein mo'alin ba-oros* — there is no *me'ilah* on the hide. The *kohanim* receive the hide of the *olah*, as it does not get burned. Thus *zerikah* removes the hide from *me'ilah*. But the meat — *aval mo'alin ba-basar* — remains under *me'ilah* because even after *zerikah*, the meat of an *olah* is destined for the *mizbe'ach* and may not be used by anyone. *Me'ilah* on the meat continues *ad she-yetzei l'veit ha-deshen* — until the ashes are carried outside the camp. The hide is the sole dimension of an *olah* where *zerikah* produces a *me'ilah* status change.

### Burned Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים, וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ. נִשְׁחֲטוּ – הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה. הוּזָּה דָּמָן – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא, וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן בְּבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן עַד שֶׁיַּתִּיךְ הַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of bulls that are burned and goats that are burned from** the moment **that they were consecrated.** Once **they were slaughtered, they were rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.** Once **its blood was sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating **it, due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while **ritually impure. And one is** liable for **its misuse** even when it is **in the place of the ashes, until the flesh has been** completely **scorched.**

*Parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* — the bulls and goats that are burned — are those special *korbanos* whose blood is brought inside the *Heichal*, into the innermost sanctuary, and whose carcasses are burned outside the camp entirely. The paradigmatic example is *Yom Kippur*; another case is *par he-elem davar shel tzibbur*. Since nobody ever benefits from these offerings — they are never eaten and not burned on the *mizbe'ach* itself — they resemble an *olah* in that *me'ilah* never diminishes. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification. After *shechitah*, the animals are *huchsharu l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim uvlinah*. After *zerikas hadam*, *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei* — the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* all apply. This is striking: even for offerings that are never eaten and are burned outside the camp, one can only be *chayav* for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* once *zerikas hadam* has rendered the *korban* valid. The onset of these statuses is always tied to the *kashrut* of the *korban*, not to the edibility of the meat.

*Mo'alin bahem be-veit ha-deshen* — even in the *beis ha-deshen*, the place outside the camp where they are burned, there remains *me'ilah*. This is because these *korbanos* begin their process outside the camp — they are burned there, not on the *mizbe'ach*. The *mitzvah* is not complete until the meat has fully dissolved: *ad she-yitach ha-basar*, which *Rashi* explains means until it becomes ash — *she-na'aseh eifer*. In a sense this is the symmetrical counterpart of the regular *olah*: the *olah* is burned on the *mizbe'ach* and its *me'ilah* ends when the ashes are taken outside; the *parim hanisrafim* begin outside and their *me'ilah* ends when they turn to ash there. Both conditions — ashes and the appropriate location — are required for *me'ilah* to cease, though the logical structure is inverse.

### Chatas & Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת, וְאָשָׁם, וְזִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִיבּוּר – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ. נִשְׁחֲטוּ – הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה. נִזְרַק דָּמָן – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא. אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בַּבָּשָׂר, אֲבָל מוֹעֲלִין בְּאֵימוּרֵיהֶן עַד שֶׁיֵּצְאוּ לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of a sin offering, and a guilt offering, and communal peace offerings from** the moment **that they were consecrated.** Once **they were slaughtered they were rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.** Once **their blood was sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating **them, due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of sacrificial meat while **ritually impure. One is not** liable for **misuse of the flesh, but one is** liable for **misuse of their sacrificial portions,** i.e., the portions that are to be consumed on the altar, **until they leave to the place of the ashes.**

The *mishnah* now addresses *chatas*, *asham*, and *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* — all *kodshei kodashim* that are eaten rather than entirely burned. *Zivchei shalmei tzibbur* refers primarily to the communal *shelamim* brought on *Shavuos*. These are like the *olah* in being *kodshei kodashim*, but unlike the *olah* their meat is eaten by the *kohanim*. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification. After *shechitah*, *huchsharu l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim uvlinah*. After *nizrak daman* — *zerikas hadam* — the *korban* is valid: *chayavin alav mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. And now, *ve-ein mo'alin ba-basar* — there is no *me'ilah* on the meat, because *zerikah* renders the meat permissible to the *kohanim*. However, *aval mo'alin be-eimureihem* — there remains *me'ilah* on the *emurim*, the portions designated for the *mizbe'ach*, such as the fats and specified organs. These retain *me'ilah* status *ad she-yeitzu l'veit ha-deshen* — until the ashes of those portions are taken outside. The pattern is now established: *zerikah* removes *me'ilah* from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* that may be eaten, but the *emurim* that go onto the *mizbe'ach* remain subject to *me'ilah* until they are burnt and their ashes removed.

## Bread & Menachos

### Shtei Halechem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ. קָרְמוּ בַּתַּנּוּר – הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה, וְלִישְׁחוֹט עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַזֶּבַח. נִזְרַק דָּמָן שֶׁל כְּבָשִׂים – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא, וְאֵין בָּהֶן מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of the two loaves** brought on the festival of *Shavuot* **from** the moment **that they were consecrated.** Once **they formed a crust, they were rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** it being **left overnight, and** they are rendered eligible **to slaughter with them the** accompanying **offering** of the two lambs. Once **the blood of** the **lambs is sprinkled, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating the loaves, **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of consecrated food while **ritually impure. And they are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** as at that point their consumption is permitted.

The *mishnah* now turns to offerings that are not animal *korbanos*: *shtei halechem*, the two loaves of bread brought on *Shavuos* together with the communal *shelamim*. *Mo'alin bahen mishehukdeshu* — *me'ilah* applies from the moment the dough or flour is sanctified. The parallel to *shechitah* — the moment that renders the animal susceptible to *tumah* — is the baking. Specifically: *kadshu ba-tanur huchsharu l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim* — once the loaves achieve a crust in the oven, they become susceptible to disqualification through a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*.

Note that *linah* is not mentioned here, since *linah* pertains to blood, which is irrelevant for bread. Equally notable is the absence of *linah* as a concern intrinsic to these loaves themselves: the *shtei halechem* was eaten two to three days after it was made, and the *lechem hapanim* was made on *Erev Shabbos* and eaten the following *Shabbos* — a span of nine days. The concept of *linah* would be quite foreign to these *korbanos*, since they are naturally expected to remain for extended periods from baking until consumption.

The reason susceptibility to *tumah* requires the crust stage is conceptually significant. Although the dough is sanctified from the outset, the *kedushah* at that stage is more in the nature of *kedushas damim* — a sanctity of monetary value — rather than *kedushas haguf*, the intrinsic sanctity of the object that will serve as the actual *korban*. Until the dough achieves a crust and becomes bread — *ad she-kramu paneha ba-tanur* — it is not yet the thing that will be the *shtei halechem*. *Kedushas haguf* is only achieved once it is actual bread fit to serve as the offering, and it is that elevated *kedushah* that enables susceptibility to even the residual *tumah* of a *tvul yom*.

Once the loaves have a crust and have reached the status of actual bread, *lishchoto aleiha et ha-zevach* — it is now possible to slaughter the *korban* for the sake of the bread. The lambs brought with the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuos* must be slaughtered in conjunction with the bread; if the bread is not yet bread, the *shechitah* cannot be performed in relation to it. Once *nizrak daman shel kvasim* — the blood of the lambs is thrown — the bread's status is linked to the *korban* and changes accordingly: *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. One is *chayav* for *piggul* if the *korban* was *piggul*, for *nosar* if the bread was left beyond the allotted time, and for *tamei* if one ate it in a state of *tumah*. *Ve-ein bahem meila* — there is no *me'ilah* on the bread, because after *zerikas hadam* it is fit to be eaten by the *kohanim*.

### Lechem Hapanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשָׁה. קָרַם בַּתַּנּוּר – הוּכְשַׁר לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּלְהִסָּדֵר עַל גַּבֵּי הַשּׁוּלְחָן. קָרְבוּ הַבָּזִיכִּין – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא, וְאֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of the shewbread,** which is arranged on the Golden Table in the Sanctuary each Shabbat, **from** the moment **that it was consecrated.** Once **it formed a crust in the oven** it assumes the status of bread and its halakhic status is like that of offerings of the most sacred order after the animal was slaughtered, in that **it was rendered susceptible to disqualification through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and** it is rendered eligible **for arrangement upon the Table** in the Sanctuary. Once **the bowls** of frankincense brought with the shewbread of the previous week **were sacrificed, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating the loaves **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of consecrated food while **ritually impure. But it is not** subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** as at that point its consumption is permitted.

*Lechem hapanim* — the showbread — follows a parallel structure. *Mo'alin bo mishehukdshah* — *me'ilah* from the time of sanctification. *Kramu ba-tanur* — once the loaves have a crust in the oven, *huchsharu l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim* — susceptibility to disqualification follows. The absence of *linah* in this *mishnah* is even more pronounced: the *lechem hapanim* was placed on the *shulchan* on *Shabbos* and eaten the following *Shabbos*, nine days later. Once the loaves have achieved the crust stage, they are now fit to be arranged on the *shulchan* — *u-le-hasdir al gabei ha-shulchan*. The parallel to the *shtei halechem*'s *shechitah* is here the offering of the *bazichim* — the small ladles of *levonah*, the frankincense. *Karvu ha-bazichim* — when the *bazichim* are offered on the *mizbe'ach*, that is the act which renders the *lechem hapanim* fit to be eaten. After the *bazichim*: *chayavin alav mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*, and *ve-ein bo meila* — there is no *me'ilah*, since the bread is now permissible for the *kohanim* to eat.

### Menachos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְּנָחוֹת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן מִשֶּׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ. קָדְשׁוּ בִּכְלִי – הוּכְשְׁרוּ לִיפָּסֵל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם, וּבִמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּבְלִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One is** liable for **misuse of the meal offerings from** the moment **that they were consecrated.** Once **they were consecrated** through placement of the flour **in** a service **vessel, they were rendered susceptible to disqualification** for sacrifice **through** contact with **one who immersed that day, and through** contact with **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, **and through** its blood being **left overnight.**

A standard *mincha* follows a similar but distinct structure. *Mo'alin bah mishehukdshah* — *me'ilah* from sanctification. The pivotal moment for susceptibility to *tumah* is not baking but rather placement in the *kli shares*: *kidshu be-kli, huchsharu l'hipasel bitvul yom uvimechusar kippurim uvlinah*. Here *linah* is reintroduced, because once the *mincha* has been placed in the *kli shares*, it must be offered that same day; if left until the next morning, it is *pasul be-linah*. This is the operative *linah* clock for the *mincha* — the *kli shares* placement starts it.

The *kedushah* dimension here is likewise conceptually layered. A *mincha* is dough, which can contract *tumah* in ordinary circumstances. But *kedushas haguf* — the intrinsic *kedushah* that enables susceptibility to the residual *tumah* of a *tvul yom* and *mechusar kippurim* — is not achieved merely by sanctification. By an animal *korban*, *kedushas haguf* was present from sanctification but the animal still required *shechitah* to become susceptible. By the bread offerings, *kedushas haguf* for purposes of *tumah* required the crust stage. By the *mincha*, *kedushas haguf* is achieved through placement in the *kli shares*, which is when the *mincha* is formally designated as the thing that will be offered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא. וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בַּשִּׁירַיִם, אֲבָל מוֹעֲלִין בַּקּוֹמֶץ עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **Once the handful** taken from the meal offering **was sacrificed, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** eating the meal offering **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** and the prohibition of ***notar*, and** the prohibition of partaking of consecrated food while **ritually impure. And one is not** liable for **misuse of the remainder** of the meal offering, which is eaten by the priests, **but one is** liable for **misuse of the handful** that is sacrificed, **until it leaves to the place of the ashes.**

Once the *komatz* is offered — *karav ha-komatz* — the *korban* is valid: *chayavin lo mishum piggul nosar ve-tamei*. *Ve-ein mo'alin ba-shirayim* — there is no *me'ilah* on the remainder, the *shirayim*, which is eaten by the *kohanim*. *Aval mo'alin ba-komatz* — the *komatz* which is burned on the *mizbe'ach* retains *me'ilah ad she-tetzei l'veit ha-deshen*, until the ashes are taken outside, following the same principle as all things burned on the *mizbe'ach*.

## Me'ilah Ending

### Trumat Hadeshen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, הַנֶּהֱנֶה מֵאֵפֶר תַּפּוּחַ שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, רַב אָמַר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that there is a dispute between *amora'im* with regard to **one who derives benefit from the ash of the round** heap **that is on top of the altar. Rav says:** One who derives benefit **from it** is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** One who derives benefit **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property.

The *Gemara* now opens an explicit discussion about when exactly *me'ilah* ceases for ashes on the *mizbe'ach*. On the *mizbe'ach* there would accumulate over time a large mound of ashes, called the *tapuach* — literally "apple," referring to the dome-like shape of the mound. Each morning the *kohen* would perform *trumat ha-deshen*, the symbolic removal of a small portion of ashes, placing them at the side of the *mizbe'ach*. Only periodically, when the mound grew large enough to require actual cleaning, would the ashes be fully removed and carried outside the camp to the *beis ha-deshen*. The *Gemara* asks: *hanehene mi-eifer tapuach she-al gabei ha-mizbe'ach* — one who derived benefit from the ashes in the mound — is there *me'ilah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְנֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – לְאַחַר תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this dispute: **Before the removal of the ashes, everyone agrees that** one who derives benefit **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. The reason is that the mitzva of the removal of the ashes has not yet been completed. **When they disagree** it is with regard to the *halakha* **after the removal of the ashes,** at which point the mitzva has been completed.

*Lifnei trumat ha-deshen* — before the morning *trumat ha-deshen* is performed — *kulei alma lo peligi de-mo'alin bo* — everyone agrees there is *me'ilah*. The ashes have not yet had *trumat ha-deshen* performed to them; *na'aseis mitzvato* has not occurred, and the *mitzvah* of *trumat ha-deshen* conceptually applies to the ashes every morning. So before that morning *mitzvah* is discharged, the ashes remain fully within *me'ilah*.

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ – הֲרֵי נַעֲשָׂה מִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says** that one who derives benefit **from it** is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **as its mitzva has been performed** and completed.

The dispute concerns the period *le-achar trumat ha-deshen* — after the morning *trumat ha-deshen* has been performed. *Rav amar ein mo'alin bo* — *Rav* holds that once *trumat ha-deshen* is done, there is no longer *me'ilah*. The reasoning: *harei na'aseis mitzvato* — the last *mitzvah* that pertains to these ashes has been completed. The subsequent removal of the ash mound to the *beis ha-deshen* is merely maintenance and cleanup, not a *mitzvah* in the halachic sense. Once the *mitzvah* is done, *me'ilah* ceases.

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלָבַשׁ הַכֹּהֵן מִדּוֹ בַד וְגוֹ׳״ – כֵּיוָן דִּצְרִיךְ לְבִגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה, בִּקְדוּשְּׁתֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that one is liable for misuse, **since it is written: "And the priest shall put on his linen garment,** and his linen breeches shall he put upon his flesh; and he shall take up the ashes to where the fire has consumed the burnt offering on the altar, and he shall put them beside the altar. And he shall remove his garments, and don other garments, and carry forth the ashes outside the camp" (Leviticus 6:3–4). Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: **Since** taking the ashes outside the Temple also **requires the priestly vestments,** albeit garments of lesser quality than those used to remove the ashes from the altar, evidently the ash **remains in its consecrated** state.

*Rabbi Yochanan amar mo'alin bo* — *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that *me'ilah* continues even after *trumat ha-deshen*. He derives this from the verse (Vayikra 6): *ve-lavash ha-kohen mido vad u-michnesei vad yilbash al besaro ve-herim et ha-deshen... ve-lavash begadim acherim ve-hotzi et ha-deshen el mi-chutz la-machaneh*. The verse indicates that the *kohen* changes into different garments (*begadim acherim*) to carry the ashes outside. *Rabbi Yochanan* understands *begadim acherim* to mean a different set of *bigdei kehunah* — priestly vestments of lesser expense but still formal priestly garb. *Keivan d'tzarich bigdei kehunah bi-kdusheih kai* — since the act of removing the ashes requires *bigdei kehunah*, it constitutes an *avodah*, a formal Temple service, not mere cleanup. So long as this *avodah* remains uncompleted — so long as the ashes have not actually been brought outside — the ash retains its *kedushah* and *me'ilah* continues.

### Mishna Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן, קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: עַד שֶׁתֵּרָאֶה לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against the opinion of Rav. **We learned** in the mishna, with regard to the bird burnt offering: **One is** liable for **its misuse until it leaves to the place of the ashes,** where it is burned. This indicates that the *halakhot* of misuse apply while the ashes remain on the altar, even after the removal of ashes, which is **difficult for** the opinion of **Rav.** The Gemara answers that **Rav** could have **said to you:** The mishna means that one is liable **until** the ash **is fit** to be taken out **to the place of ashes,** i.e., once it is burned on the altar and has had a shovel of ashes removed from it.

The *Gemara* notes that our *mishnah* appears to support *Rabbi Yochanan* clearly: *mo'alin bahen ad she-tetzei l'veit ha-deshen* — *me'ilah* continues until the ashes go out to the *beis ha-deshen*. This is a direct challenge to *Rav*. The *Gemara* resolves: *amar lach Rav* — *Rav* would explain the *mishnah* as: *ad she-tehei re'uyah l'veit ha-deshen* — until the ashes are *fit* to be taken out to the *beis ha-deshen*. That fitness is achieved once *trumat ha-deshen* is performed, because after *trumat ha-deshen* the ashes are now in a state where they may be removed. The *mishnah*'s language of *going out* is thus read not as requiring actual removal, but as requiring that the ashes have reached the stage at which removal is halachically appropriate. This is analogous to the principle sometimes applied in *zerikah*: *ad she-tehei re'uyah li-zerikah* — until it is *fit for zerikah* — rather than until *zerikah* is actually performed. On this reading, *Rav* maintains that *me'ilah* ends once the ashes are *eligible* for removal following *trumat ha-deshen*, while *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that *me'ilah* continues until the ashes are actually removed and carried outside the camp. The *shiur* will continue with this dispute and the fuller analysis of when *me'ilah* concludes in the case of ashes and the *beis ha-deshen*.
