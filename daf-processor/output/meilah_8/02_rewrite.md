# Meilah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Textual Issues

### Greeting & Daf Structure

Daf 8 opens the second *perek* of Meilah, and one reason this *daf* is notably short is that Tosafot gathers all the *mishnayot* of the *perek* together and explains them collectively on the first *daf*, resulting in a more compact treatment than usual.

### Rashi on Meilah Debate

A further reason for Tosafot's expansive role here deserves attention. In Keritot, Tosafot frequently cites *Perush HaKuntres* or *Perush Rashi*, and while there had been scholarly speculation — associated with Zunz — that the Rashi on Keritot was not authentic, that view is no longer widely accepted. The Rashi on Keritot is generally regarded today as genuine. The situation in Meilah is different: a substantial scholarly opinion holds that the Rashi on Meilah is not Rashi's own commentary. Notably, Tosafot on Meilah never appears to quote Rashi by name — a conspicuous absence when compared to Tosafot's practice in Keritot. This may well explain why Tosafot in Meilah so often performs the explanatory function normally left to Rashi: perhaps they simply did not have a Rashi on this tractate.

### Perek Overview

The first *perek* was largely devoted to *psulim* — disqualifications — and the question of which flaws prevent a *korban* from undergoing a change of status. Toward the end of that *perek*, the discussion arrived at the normative rule: that *zrikat hadam* (the sprinkling of the blood) by *kodshei kodashim* removes the meat from the category of *me'ilah*, while by *kodshim kalim* it draws the animal more fully into *me'ilah*. The second *perek* now proceeds in an apodictic, systematic manner — setting out the straightforward law, without exceptional cases, of how and when the status of *me'ilah* changes, and when the status of a *korban* changes, in the normal course of events.

## Chatat Ha'Of & Tumah

### Tumah Categories

The second *perek* opens, for reasons Tosafot explains, with a case that is not entirely standard: the *chatat ha'of*, the bird sin-offering. The *mishnah* states that a *chatat ha'of* is subject to *me'ilah* from the moment of sanctification (*mishehukdshah*), since it is *kodshei kodashim*.

To understand what follows, the basic structure of *tumah* classification must be kept in mind. A *met*, a human corpse, is *avi avot hatumah*, the highest degree; other standard sources of *tumah* — such as a *zav* — are called *av hatumah*. Whatever an *av hatumah* touches becomes a *rishon*, first-degree derivative *tumah*. The *rishon* in turn can transmit *tumah* one further stage, making a *sheni*. Normally the chain stops there: only food can become a *sheni*, and *sheni* does not transmit further in the case of ordinary *chullin*. However, when *terumah* is involved, *tumah* can continue one level further to a *shlishi*, and when *kodshim* are involved, it can reach a *revi'i*. When something has become *tamei* but is too derivative to transmit further, the technical term used is *nifsal* — it is invalidated but does not itself render other objects *tamei*. Thus a *terumah* at the level of *shlishi* is called *nifsal*, not *tamei*, and the same applies to *kodshim* at the *revi'i*.

### Tvul Yom & Michusarei Kipurim

Two additional categories of persons occupy a transitional state of purity. A *tvul yom* is someone who has immersed in a *mikvah* that very day but has not yet waited for nightfall to complete the *taharah* process. A *mechusar kipurim* is someone — a *zav*, *zavah*, *yoledes*, or *metzora* — who has immersed but still awaits the *korban* that will complete their purification. These individuals function like a *sheni* in relation to *terumah* and *kodshim*: they cannot affect ordinary *chullin*, but they render *terumah* and *kodshim* *pasul*. The *mishnah*'s phrase *lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim* signals that after certain preparatory acts the *korban* becomes susceptible to invalidation by contact with such persons — not that it becomes fully *tamei* in the transmissible sense, but *pasul*: unfit for use.

### Shechita & Hechsher

The *mishnah* continues: *nimlekah huchshara lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim*. Once the *melikah* (the wringing of the bird's neck) is performed, the animal is dead and is now fit (*ra'uy*) to be eaten or to be placed on the *mizbeach*; it is no longer a living creature. This transition renders it *huchshar* — susceptible to *tumah* — just as the contact of liquid with food creates susceptibility to *tumah* as described in the verse *vechi yutan mayim al zera*. While the bird was alive, contact with something *tamei* would have had no effect; now that it has been slaughtered and has become *ra'uy*, contact with a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kipurim* can render it *pasul*.

### Chatat Status Stages

The *mishnah* adds that *linah* — leaving something overnight — also creates *pasul* at this stage. Tosafot emphasizes that the *linah* referred to here is specifically *linat hadam*, leaving the blood overnight, because the *linah* clock for the meat does not begin until after *zrikat hadam*. The blood, once caught, must be sprinkled; if it is left until the next morning, it can no longer be used. The question of whether the meat itself would be considered *nosar* before *zrikat hadam* is addressed by Tosafot: before *zrikat hadam* there may be an *issur* in eating the meat, but it would not constitute the prohibited category of *nosar* with its attendant obligation of *kares*. The *linah* mentioned at this stage of the *mishnah* therefore refers to the blood.

Once the blood has been squeezed out or sprinkled — *huzah damah* — a further set of statuses becomes operative.

### Zrikat Hadam Threshold

The final clause of the *mishnah*'s account of the *chatat ha'of* states: *chayav alav mishum piggul nosar v'tamei* — one is liable on account of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei*. These three statuses all crystallize at the moment of *zrikat hadam*. The thought that creates *piggul* may have occurred earlier, but *piggul* only takes effect once the blood is sprinkled; *nosar* — the prohibition of leaving over sacrificial meat — and the prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodshim* both only attach to the meat once the *korban* has been rendered valid through *zrikat hadam*. Since by a *chatat ha'of* the *kohanim* eat all of the bird (there are no *imurin* to be burned separately), *zrikat hadam* marks the moment the meat becomes fit for eating, and it is from that moment that these three statuses apply. As a direct consequence, the *mishnah* states *v'ein bo me'ilah* — there is no *me'ilah* — for the same reason: once *zrikat hadam* renders the meat fit for the *kohanim*, it exits the domain of *me'ilah*.

## Psul vs. Tamei Analysis

### Psul Contradiction

The *Gemara* now focuses on a specific phrase in the *mishnah*: *nimlekah huchshara lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim* — it became fit to be rendered *pasul* by a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kipurim*. The *Gemara* sharpens the issue: *lehipasel in, aval letammuye lo* — yes it can be rendered *pasul*, but it cannot be rendered *tamei*. This is puzzling. A *tvul yom* is like a *sheni*. If he touches *kodshim*, which can reach the level of *revi'i*, he ought to make them a *shlishi* — which would be called *tamei*, not merely *pasul*, since *kodshim* can still go one further step to *revi'i*. Why then does the *mishnah* say *pasul* and not *tamei*?

### Three Positions

The *Gemara* answers: *matnitin mani? Rabbanan hi* — our *mishnah* reflects the view of the Sages as expressed in a *baraita* drawn from the *Tosefta Taharot*. That *baraita* presents three positions. Abba Shaul holds that a *tvul yom* who touches *kodshim* is treated as a *rishon*, making the *kodshim* a *sheni*. Rabbi Meir holds that he is a *sheni*, and thus makes *kodshim* a *shlishi* — which by *kodshim* is called *tamei* (since it can still transmit one further level), and makes *terumah pasul* (since *shlishi* of *terumah* cannot go further). The Sages hold: *keshem shehu posel mashkeh terumah v'ochlei terumah, kach hu posel mashkeh kodesh v'ochlei kodesh* — just as when he touches food and drink of *terumah* he renders them *pasul* (without further transmission), so too when he touches food and drink of *kodshim* he only renders them *pasul*, not *tamei* in the transmissible sense.

This last position is conceptually striking. If the *tvul yom* is a *sheni*, he should make *terumah* a *shlishi* (which is *pasul* for *terumah*, unable to go further) and make *kodshim* a *shlishi* (which is *tamei* for *kodshim*, capable of transmitting one more stage). The Sages, however, say he only *posel* even the *kodshim*, which seems to collapse the distinction between *terumah* and *kodshim* with respect to his effect.

### Raba's Resolution

Raba resolves the apparent difficulty. He explains that our *mishnah* is operating at the *de'oraita* level. The positions of Abba Shaul (treating the *tvul yom* as a *rishon* for *kodshim*) and of Rabbi Meir (treating him as a *sheni*) are both *derabbanan* stringencies. At the *de'oraita* level, everyone agrees with the *mishnah*'s formulation: the *tvul yom* renders *terumah* and *kodshim* *pasul* and does not transmit beyond that. *Shavinu Rabbanan l'tvul yom kerishon* — the position of Abba Shaul that he is like a *rishon* by *kodshim* — is a rabbinic stringency. *V'Rabbi Meir ke'hasheni* — Rabbi Meir's assignment of *sheni* status — is likewise rabbinic. Our *mishnah* reflects what is true *de'oraita*, and *de'oraita* the *tvul yom* is *posel* only.

### Residual Tumah Concept

The conceptual underpinning is then articulated: *kivan detaval, kalush tumah* — once he has immersed in the *mikvah*, his *tumah* is so residual, so weakened, that it can *posel* things but not transmit *tumah* further. *Pasul mashvei, tamei lo mashvei* — it can invalidate, but cannot render something *tamei* in the fully transmissible sense. Ordinarily, what prevents *tumah* from cascading further is that it has become too derivative — it has passed through too many links in the chain. Here, the logic is different: it is not that the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* is many degrees removed, but rather that it is intrinsically attenuated at its source. Because it began so weakened, it can reach *terumah* and *kodshim*, but it stops there — it does not continue further. At the *derabbanan* level, the debate between Abba Shaul and Rabbi Meir concerns what label to attach to this attenuated *tumah* and how many further stages it generates as a matter of rabbinic decree. But at the *de'oraita* level, the *tvul yom* simply *posel*: he invalidates whatever he touches and the *tumah* does not propagate further.

## Meilah Post-Zrika

### Meilah Question

The *Gemara* then turns to the final clause of the *mishnah*'s account of the *chatat ha'of*: after *zrikat hadam*, *v'ein bo me'ilah* — there is no *me'ilah*. The *Gemara* presses: *me'ilah hu deleka, aval issura ika* — the *mishnah*'s phrasing implies that while *me'ilah* is gone, some prohibition remains. But why should any prohibition remain? After the blood is sprinkled, the *kohanim* are supposed to eat the meat. And yet it seems the *mishnah* is implying the meat is still somehow problematic. The natural answer might simply be that the meat remains prohibited to non-*kohanim* — it has exited *me'ilah* precisely because it is now designated for the *kohanim*, yet it is still forbidden to everyone else.

### Rabbi Chanina Answer

Rabbi Chanina answers with the same response he gave in a parallel discussion: *leyotzei k'Rabbi Akiva hi, de'amar zrikah mo'elet leyotzei* — this follows the position of Rabbi Akiva regarding the case of *yotzei*, meat that was taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash* before *zrikat hadam*. According to Rabbi Akiva, even in that case *zrikah* removes the *me'ilah* status, and yet the meat remains forbidden for consumption — *delav bat achila hi*. The same principle applies here: *zrikah* removes *me'ilah*, but does not automatically render the meat permitted in all respects. The *me'ilah* and the prohibition of eating are distinct legal categories that can move independently of each other.

## Mitzuy Debate

### Mitzuy Question

The *Gemara* now introduces a distinct and important question about the *chatat ha'of*: what is the status of *mitzuy* — the act of squeezing out the remaining blood after the initial squirt? After *melikah*, blood exits the bird's neck; the *kohen* then presses the exposed neck against the wall of the *mizbeach* to squeeze out whatever blood remains. Does this squeezing constitute an indispensable act of the *avodah* — is it *me'akev* — such that if it is not performed, the *korban* is invalid? Or is it more analogous to *shfichas shirayim*, the pouring of residual blood at the base of the *mizbeach* in a standard animal *korban*, which is not *me'akev*?

### Rav's Position

Rav Huna transmits in the name of Rav: *mitzuy chatat ha'of eino me'akev* — the squeezing is not *me'akev*. The proof he offers is from our *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* marks the critical moment as the squirting of the blood (*huzah damah*), and derives the status change from that act. Since the *mishnah* grounds all the status changes in the squirting, and not in the subsequent squeezing, the squeezing cannot be the pivotal act. Accordingly, even if the squeezing is not performed, the *korban* remains valid.

### Rav Adda Position

Rav Adda bar Ahava, also transmitting in the name of Rav, disagrees: *mitzuy chatat ha'of me'akev* — the squeezing is indispensable. He resolves the apparent conflict with our *mishnah* by positing a different *girsa*: in his version of the *mishnah*, the status change is triggered not by *huzah damah* (squirting) but by *mitzuy damah* (squeezing). It is thus a textual dispute embedded within the *shita* about whether *mitzuy* is *me'akev*.

### Textual Evidence

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the matter from a *pasuk*. The relevant verse, in *Vayikra* 5, reads: *v'hanish'ar badam yimatze el yesod hamizbeach, chatas hi* — and what remains of the blood should be squeezed out to the base of the *mizbeach*; it is a *chatas*. The *Gemara* initially reads this as support for Rav Adda bar Ahava: *bishlama l'Rav Adda bar Ahava, hainu d'chtiv v'hanish'ar badam yimatze, chatas hi* — it makes sense according to Rav Adda, since only after squeezing out the remaining blood does it say *chatas hi* — "it is a *chatas*" — implying that the squeezing constitutes the act that renders it a valid *chatas*. But according to Rav Huna, the phrase *chatas hi* following the description of squeezing is difficult to explain.

### Conditional Interpretation

The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a reading that relieves the pressure on Rav Huna's position. *She'im nish'ar* — "if there remains" — the verse is conditional, not imperative. It does not demand that there be leftover blood and that it be squeezed; it merely says: if there is leftover blood, this is what you do with it. And as for the phrase *chatas hi* — "it is a *chatas*" — Rabbi Yishmael reads this as referring back not to the squeezing but to the beginning of the verse: *v'hizzah midam hachatat al kir hamizbeach*, the squirting of the blood on the wall of the *mizbeach*. It is that act which makes it a *chatas*. The verse thus says: squirt the blood on the wall — for it is the *chatas* (i.e., that is the critical act) — and if blood remains, squeeze it to the base.

### Repetition Principle

A challenge is then raised: if we read *v'hanish'ar* as conditional — meaning "if there is leftover" — then we might apply the same reasoning to the *mincha*, where the verse says *v'hanoteret min hamincha yochlu Aharon uvanav*. Should we say there too that the *kohanim* need only eat the remainder *if there is* remainder — and that if the *shirayim* spilled or were lost, the *korban* is still valid? A *baraita* is cited to refute this: *misoltah u'mishamanah al kol levonatah* — a verse teaches that the *kometz* must be offered with all its components, and if the flour, oil, or *levonah* of the *shirayim* were missing, the offering is invalid. This indicates that the *noteret* is genuinely required, not merely conditional.

The *Gemara* resolves this distinction by invoking a principle specific to *kodshim*: *shana alav hakatuv l'akev* — when the Torah repeats a requirement in the context of *kodshim*, the repetition signals that the act is *me'akev*. In the case of the *mincha*, the word *v'hanoteret* appears twice, and the second instance comes to establish that the act is *me'akev*. In the case of the bird *chatat*, there is only one mention of the squeezing — it is not repeated — and therefore, at least according to the view that *mitzuy* is not *me'akev*, the single mention creates only a *mitzvah*, not an indispensable requirement.

### Piggul Baraita

A further *baraita* is cited as a challenge. The *baraita* states that whether dealing with a *chatat ha'of* or an *olat ha'of*, if one performed *melikah* and squeezed out their blood with an intention of *chutz lizmanno*, it is *pasul* without *kares*; if with an intention of *chutz limkomo*, it is *piggul* and one is liable for *kares*. The *baraita* mentions squeezing (*mitzuy damah*) in the context of both the *chatat* and the *olah*. This appears to treat *mitzuy* as a critical act of the *avodah* even for the *chatat*, since *piggul* can only be generated through the performance of a valid sacrificial act. If *mitzuy* is not *me'akev* and is merely a supplementary act, it would not seem capable of generating *piggul*.

### L'Atztedin Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this by the principle of *l'itztadin* — "respectively." The *baraita* mentions two types of bird-offerings — *chatat ha'of* and *olat ha'of* — and two acts — *melikah* and *mitzuy damah* — and the verse should be read distributively: *melikah* applies respectively to the *chatat ha'of*, and *mitzuy damah* applies respectively to the *olat ha'of*. By the *chatat ha'of*, the pivotal act generating *piggul* is the *melikah* (combined with the subsequent squirting of blood); by the *olat ha'of*, since the Torah specifies only squeezing and not squirting, the act generating *piggul* is the *mitzuy*. The *baraita* therefore does not require *mitzuy* for the *chatat* to generate *piggul* — it is discussing the *olas ha'of* in that half of the sentence.

### Rabbi Yishmael Versions

A final complication: the *Gemara* notes that there appear to be two versions of Rabbi Yishmael's position. The version quoted in the *baraita* here treats *mitzuy* as non-essential and conditional (*she'im nish'ar*). But a *baraita* in *Zevachim* — cited in the name of Rabbi Yishmael — states that *shirayim me'akvin*, that the residual act is *me'akev*. Rav Pappa explains the *Zevachim* dispute: what is at issue there is specifically whether the *mitzuy* of the *chatat ha'of* is *me'akev* — a position the *baraita* there attributes to Rabbi Yishmael. The conclusion is *trei tana'ei aliba d'Rabbi Yishmael*: there are two *tanna'im* who disagree about what Rabbi Yishmael's position actually was. Just as Rav himself is transmitted with two conflicting rulings by Rav Huna and Rav Adda bar Ahava, so too Rabbi Yishmael is transmitted with two conflicting versions — one holding *mitzuy* is not *me'akev*, the other holding it is.

## Olat Ha'Of System

### Olat Ha'Of Stages

The *mishnah* proceeds to the *olat ha'of*, the bird burnt-offering, following the same structural template. *Me'ilah* begins from the moment of sanctification. After *melikah*, the bird becomes susceptible to *tumah* — *nimlekah huchshara lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim* — and *linah* of the blood also applies. After the blood is squeezed out (*mitza damah*), the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* become operative, and one is *chayav* for those categories.

### Continuous Meilah

The *mishnah* then states *u'molkin bah* — *me'ilah* continues even after *mitzuy damah*. This contrasts with the *chatat ha'of*, where *me'ilah* terminated once the blood was thrown, because at that point the meat became fit for the *kohanim* to eat. By the *olat ha'of*, nothing is ever eaten — the entire bird is burned on the *mizbeach*. Since no part of it passes into the domain of the *kohanim* or any other permitted consumption, there is no moment at which *zrikah* or *mitzuy* changes the *me'ilah* status. The general principle at work here is that something remains in the category of *kodshei Hashem* — subject to *me'ilah* — as long as its *mitzvah* has not yet been completed. Since the bird's *mitzvah* is to be burned entirely, *me'ilah* persists throughout.

### Meilah Termination

*Me'ilah* for the *olat ha'of* ends only *ad she-tetzei l'veit hadeshen* — until the ashes are taken out to the place of the ashes outside the camp. Even after the bird has been burned and reduced to ashes on the *mizbeach*, its *mitzvah* is not yet complete: the Torah prescribes that the *kohen* perform *trumas hadeshen* — the ritual removal of ashes — and that when necessary, the ashes be carried in priestly garments to the *beis hadeshen* outside the *machaneh*. Until that final act is performed, the *mitzvah* of the *korban* is not yet discharged, and therefore *me'ilah* remains in force.

### Burned Offerings

The *mishnah* then addresses *parim hanisrafim u'se'irim hanisrafim* — the bulls and goats that are burned outside the camp, the paradigmatic cases being the special inner-sanctuary offerings of *Yom Kippur* and related categories such as the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur*. Like the *olat ha'of*, these offerings are never eaten and are never brought to the *mizbeach* to be burned there; instead, they are burned outside the camp from the outset. *Me'ilah* begins with sanctification. After *shechitah*, the animal can become *pasul* through *tvul yom*, *mechusar kipurim*, and *linah* of the blood. After *zrikat hadam*, the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* apply.

With regard to *me'ilah*: since these animals begin and end their service outside the camp, when is their *mitzvah* completed? The *mishnah* states *u'mo'alin bahem b'veit hadeshen* — *me'ilah* continues even when they are in the place of ashes being burned, because the *mitzvah* is not yet finished. Only when *ad sheyituch habasar* — when the meat has dissolved into ashes — is the *mitzvah* complete. The commentary of Rashi (*perush Rashi*) explains this as *sheni'aseh eifer*, that it has become ash. There is an interesting asymmetry here: for the *olat ha'of*, *me'ilah* ends when ashes are taken outside, because the animal was first burned on the *mizbeach* and then the ashes are removed. For the *parim hanisrafim*, since the animal begins outside the camp and is burned there, it is not the removal but the actual completion of burning — the dissolution into ashes — that marks the end.

## Normal Korbanot

### Olah Status

The *mishnah* now turns to the standard *olah* of a large animal — a sheep or ox. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification. After *shechitah*, the animal can become *pasul* through *tvul yom*, *mechusar kipurim*, and *linah* of the blood. After *zrikat hadam*, one is *chayav* for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei*. These statuses apply even though the meat cannot be eaten, because the *korban* has been rendered *kasher* through *zrikat hadam* and the meat is now fit for the *mizbeach*.

### Hide Exception

One notable distinction: *v'ein mo'alin ba'orot* — there is no *me'ilah* on the hide after *zrikat hadam*. The leather of an *olah* is given to the *kohanim*; it does not go onto the *mizbeach*. Once *zrikat hadam* has been performed and the *korban* is valid, the hide moves into the domain of the *kohanim*, and *me'ilah* no longer applies to it. This is the one element of the *olah* where *zrikat hadam* does change the *me'ilah* status.

### Chatat & Asham

By contrast, the meat of the *olah* continues to carry *me'ilah* since it is burned on the *mizbeach* and never eaten — *me'ilah* on the meat persists until the ashes are taken to the *beis hadeshen*. For the *chatat* and *asham* — which are *kodshei kodashim* but whose meat is eaten by the *kohanim* — the situation is different. *Me'ilah* begins with sanctification; after *shechitah*, susceptibility to *tumah* and *linah* of the blood apply; after *zrikat hadam*, *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* all apply. And *v'ein mo'alin babasar* — there is no *me'ilah* on the meat — because the meat is now fit for the *kohanim* to eat. However, *aval mo'alin be'imureihen* — *me'ilah* continues on the *imurin*, the fat portions and other parts that are designated for the *mizbeach* — and this continues until those portions are burned and the ashes are removed.

### Zivchei Shalmei

The same rule applies to *zivchei shalmei tzibbur*, the communal peace-offerings brought on *Shavuot* together with the *shtei halechem*. These are also *kodshei kodashim* eaten by the *kohanim*, and thus follow the identical pattern: *me'ilah* from sanctification, *pasul* through *tvul yom* and *linah* after *shechitah*, and then after *zrikat hadam*, *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* apply; no *me'ilah* on the meat, but *me'ilah* continues on the *imurin*.

## Bread Korbanos

### Shtei Halechem

The *mishnah* now addresses a category qualitatively different from animal offerings: the bread-offerings. *Shtei halechem* — the two loaves brought on *Shavuot* — are subject to *me'ilah* from the time the flour is sanctified. The parallel to *shechitah* for animals is *karmu batanur* — when the loaves form a crust in the oven. From that point, *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim*, they become susceptible to being rendered *pasul* by a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kipurim*. The word *linah* does not appear in this clause, because *linah* in the earlier *mishnayot* referred specifically to the blood, and there is no blood here; and the concept of *nosar* (leaving over past the permitted time) begins much later, after the *korban* is *kasher*.

The *mishnah* continues: the status of the bread is tied to the *korban* that accompanies it — the lambs of *Shavuot*. After *nizrak daman shel kvasim* — once the blood of the lambs is sprinkled — the bread becomes bound up with the status of the *korban*, and from that moment *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul nosar v'tamei*. Notably, *nosar* is applicable here: if the bread were left beyond its permitted time after the *korban* became *kasher*, it would be *nosar*. If the *korban* was rendered *piggul* through improper intent, the bread is *piggul*. And *v'ein bahem me'ilah* — once the bread is fit to be eaten by the *kohanim*, there is no *me'ilah*.

### Kedusha Threshold

An important conceptual clarification underlies this *mishnah*. The bread, unlike a living animal, can technically become *tamei* at any stage — dough contracts *tumah* in ordinary circumstances. Nevertheless, the bread does not reach the status of *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity as the sacrificial object itself — until the crust forms in the oven. Before that point, the sanctity is more in the nature of *kedushat damim*, monetary sanctity, rather than the full intrinsic sanctity of the item as a functional *korban*. This is directly parallel to the animal: a living animal before *shechitah* cannot contract *tumah* for purposes of *kodshim*, because it is not yet *ra'uy* as a *korban*; here, the unbaked flour or raw dough is not yet *lechem* and therefore has not yet reached *kedushat haguf* sufficient to make it susceptible to *tumah* in the way *kodshim* are. Only when the crust forms and it becomes true *lechem* — fit to serve as *shtei halechem* — does it acquire *kedushat haguf* and become susceptible. At that point, too, it becomes fit to accompany the *shechitah* of the lambs; if the bread is not yet ready, the *korban* cannot be brought.

### Lechem Hapanim

The *mishnah* addresses *lechem hapanim* — the showbread — with a similar structure. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification. *Karmu batanur* — once the crusts form in the oven — *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim*. (Here again, *linah* is not mentioned, since the entire cycle of the *lechem hapanim* extends across many days — baked on *erev Shabbos*, placed on the table, and consumed the following *Shabbos* — and the category of *linah* is essentially foreign to this framework.) *U'lehaseder al gabei hashulchan* — the crust formation also renders the bread ready to be arranged on the table, just as in the case of the *shtei halechem* the crust formation made the bread ready to accompany the *shechitah*. 

The critical status-change for the *lechem hapanim* comes when *karvu habazichin* — the small spoons of *levonah* are offered up. This is the *avodah* that renders the bread fit for eating, serving as the functional parallel to *zrikat hadam* for animal offerings. After the *bazichin* are offered, *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul nosar v'tamei*, and once again *nosar* is operative — if the bread is not consumed within the proper time after the *bazichin* are brought, it becomes *nosar*.

### Meilah on Food

Both *shtei halechem* and *lechem hapanim* share the final ruling: *v'ein bahem me'ilah* — once they become fit to be eaten by the *kohanim*, there is no *me'ilah*. The underlying principle is consistent: *me'ilah* ceases when the item enters the domain of permitted consumption. Since the bread in both cases is consumed by the *kohanim*, once it becomes fit for that consumption, *me'ilah* ends.

## Minchos

### Mincha Stages

The *mishnah* turns finally to the *minchos* — ordinary meal-offerings — following the same structure. *Me'ilah* begins from sanctification. The critical threshold for *kedushat haguf* by a *mincha* is not the formation of a crust (since many *minchos* are unbaked) but *kadeshu b'kli* — the moment the offering is placed into a sacred vessel (*kli shares*). From that moment, *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u'mechusar kipurim u'vlinah*. The inclusion of *linah* here is significant: once the *mincha* has been placed in the *kli shares*, it must be offered that same day, and if left overnight the entire offering is *pasul* through *linah*. This is the point at which the *linah* clock genuinely starts for the *mincha*.

### Kometz Offering

Once *karev hakometz* — the *kometz* (the handful that is separated and burned) is offered — *chayav alav mishum piggul nosar v'tamei*. The offering of the *kometz* is the act that renders the *mincha* a *kasher korban*, parallel to *zrikat hadam* for animal offerings.

### Kometz vs. Shirayim

The *mishnah* then draws the familiar distinction: *v'ein mo'alin ba'shirayim* — there is no *me'ilah* on the *shirayim*, the remainder of the *mincha* left after the *kometz* is taken, because the *shirayim* are eaten by the *kohanim*. However, *aval mo'alin ba'kometz* — *me'ilah* continues on the *kometz* itself, which is burned on the *mizbeach* — *ad she-yeitzei l'veit hadeshen*, until the ashes are taken to the *beis hadeshen*. The principle is consistent throughout the *perek*: portions designated for eating lose *me'ilah* when they become fit for that eating; portions burned on the *mizbeach* retain *me'ilah* until their final act of removal is complete.

## Ashes & Meilah End

### Ashes Debate

The *Gemara* closes the *daf* with a discussion about the *tapuach* — the mound of accumulated ashes on top of the *mizbeach*. The *mizbeach* was an exceptionally busy place, and the ashes were not cleaned out daily; they would accumulate into a large mound. Each morning the ritual of *trumas hadeshen* was performed — a *kohen* would take a symbolic quantity of ash, place it at the side of the *mizbeach*, and the formal rite was complete. Only when the *mizbeach* required a genuine cleaning would the ashes be carried in full priestly garments to the *beis hadeshen* outside the camp. The question posed by the *Gemara* is: what is the *halacha* regarding benefit derived from the ashes in the *tapuach* — is there *me'ilah*?

### Rav: Meilah Ends

Rav rules: *ein mo'alin bo* — there is no *me'ilah* on the ashes in the mound. His reasoning: *harei na'asis mitzvato* — the *mitzvah* has been completed. *Trumas hadeshen* is the final *mitzvah* performed in relation to these ashes; what comes after — carrying them outside — is merely a janitorial function, not a *mitzvah* act. Once *trumas hadeshen* has been performed the following morning, the last *mitzvah* associated with these ashes has been discharged, and *me'ilah* therefore ceases.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Meilah C…

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *mo'alin bo* — there is *me'ilah*. His argument derives from the verse (*Vayikra* 6): *v'lavash hakohen mido vad u'michnesei vad yilbash al b'saro v'herim es hadeshen* — the *kohen* dons his linen vestments and removes the ashes — followed by: *u'fashat es bgadav v'lavash bgadim acherim v'hotzi es hadeshen el michutz lamachaneh*. The phrase *bgadim acherim* — "other garments" — Rabbi Yochanan understands to mean a different set of priestly garments (*bigdei kehunah*), not ordinary weekday clothing. If carrying the ashes outside requires the wearing of *bigdei kehunah*, then it is itself an *avodah*, a genuine priestly service. And if it is an *avodah*, then the *mitzvah* associated with the ashes is not completed until that act is performed. *Kivan detzarich bigdei kehunah, bikdushatah kayei* — since it requires priestly garments, the ashes retain their *kedushah* and *me'ilah* continues until they are fully taken outside.

### Mishna Challenge

The *Gemara* challenges Rav from our very *mishnah*: *mo'alin bahen ad shetotzi l'veit hadeshen* — *me'ilah* applies until the ashes are taken to the *beis hadeshen*. This formulation clearly supports Rabbi Yochanan's position, not Rav's. Rav responds: *ad shetehei reuia l'veit hadeshen* — "until it is fit to be taken to the *beis hadeshen*." In other words, Rav reads the *mishnah*'s phrase not as a literal description of the removal, but as a reference to the state of eligibility for removal — the state the ashes reach once *trumas hadeshen* has been performed the following morning, after which they are *ra'uy* (fit) to be taken outside. This is analogous to the formulation *ad shetehei reuia l'zerikah* — until it is fit for sprinkling — which does not require the actual sprinkling to have occurred, only that the state of eligibility has been reached.

The discussion concludes here, with the question of when exactly *me'ilah* terminates for the ashes — and the degree to which removing ashes to the *beis hadeshen* constitutes a *mitzvah* act — to be continued in the next *daf*.
