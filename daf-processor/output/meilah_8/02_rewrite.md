# Meilah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Structure

### Opening

We begin today with Daf 8, the fifteenth day of the *omer*, and the opening of the second *perek*. One reason this *daf* is notably compact is that *Tosafot* addresses all of the *mishnayot* of the *perek* together on the first *daf*, explaining them in sequence — and so the *daf* moves quickly.

### Rashi Authorship

A related observation concerns the unusual role *Tosafot* plays throughout this tractate. In Keritot, *Tosafot* frequently cites *perush haKuntres* or *perush Rashi* by name. Here in Meilah, that practice appears to be entirely absent. The reason may be significant: there is a substantial scholarly opinion — held by many academics — that the *Rashi* printed on Meilah is not in fact *Rashi*'s own commentary. (The question about Keritot, which Zunz once raised, is largely dismissed today; the scholarly consensus holds that the *Rashi* on Keritot is genuine, though a particular initial *Rashi* there may have been a gloss.) In Meilah, however, the authorship question remains very much open. This would explain why *Tosafot* in Meilah so often takes on the explanatory burden normally carried by *Rashi* — they may simply not have had an authentic *Rashi* before them.

### Perek 2 Overview

The first *perek* was preoccupied largely with *psulim* — what categories of disqualification prevent a *korban* from undergoing the standard status changes relevant to *me'ilah*. At the end of that *perek*, we arrived at the normal rule: *zrikat hadam* in *kodshei kodashim* takes the meat out of *me'ilah*, while in *kodshim kalim* it brings the items more fully into *me'ilah*. The second *perek* now proceeds in a straightforwardly apodictic manner — systematic, case-by-case law about when and how the status of *me'ilah* changes under normal conditions, without special edge cases. *Tosafot* begins, perhaps surprisingly, not with the most standard *korban* but with the *chatat ha'of* — the bird sin-offering.

## Chatat Ha'Of

### Tumah Levels

The *mishnah* opens: *chatat ha'of mo'alin bah* — a bird *chatat*, which is *kodshei kodashim*, carries *me'ilah* from the moment it is sanctified. To understand the subsequent stages described in the *mishnah*, it is necessary to recall the basic framework of *tumah* transmission.

An *av ha-tumah* — a primary source of impurity such as a *zav* or a carcass — imparts to whatever it touches the status of *rishon*, a first-degree derivative. That *rishon* can then impart *tumah* to food, making it a *sheni*, a second-degree derivative. Most things stop there — food at the level of *sheni* does not pass *tumah* further under ordinary circumstances. However, *terumah* can become a *shlishi* (third degree), and *kodshim* can become a *revi'i* (fourth degree). Beyond *revi'i*, the chain stops entirely. When something becomes *tamei* but can no longer transmit that *tumah* further, the technical term is *nifsal* — invalidated — rather than *tamei*. So *terumah* that reaches *shlishi* is called *nifsal*, and *kodshim* that reach *revi'i* are called *nifsal*.

### Nifsal vs Tamei

Two special categories of partially-purified persons are relevant to this *mishnah*. The first is the *tvul yom* — one who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day and awaits nightfall to become fully *tahor*. The second is the *mechusar kippurim* — one who has immersed and awaits sunset but still lacks the *korban* that completes purification (applicable to a *zav*, *zavah*, *yoledes*, and *metzora*, as we learned at length in Keritot). These individuals are themselves at a reduced level of *tumah*: they function like a *sheni*, capable of affecting *terumah* and *kodshim* but not *chulin*.

When such a person touches *kodshim*, the *mishnah* does not say the *kodshim* becomes *tamei* — it says it becomes *nifsal*, invalidated. The distinction, as the *gemara* will clarify, is not merely terminological but reflects a conceptual point about the residual and attenuated nature of their *tumah*.

Now, an important qualification: before the bird *chatat* is slaughtered, while it is still alive, it is not yet *ra'uy* — not yet fit — to become *tamei* in the relevant sense. It has not yet become food or anything fit for the *mizbeach*. Therefore *tumah* cannot yet attach to it.

### Melikah & Linah

Once *melikah* is performed — the priest wrings the bird's neck — the animal is dead and *ra'uy* to be placed on the *mizbeach* or eaten. It is now *huchshar*, susceptible to *tumah*. The *mishnah* uses the language of *hechsher* — a concept familiar from the verse *vechi yutan mayim al zera*, where liquids render food susceptible. Here, once the bird is no longer a living animal but is fit for use, it can become *pasul* through contact with a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*: *nimlekah, huchshrah lehipasel b'tvul yom u-v'mechusar kippurim*.

The *mishnah* also mentions *linah* at this stage. *Tosafot* emphasizes that this *linah* refers to the *linah* of the *dam* — leaving the blood overnight, which renders it unfit for use. The *linah* of the meat in the sense of *nosar* does not technically begin until after *zrikat hadam*, since it is only once the *korban* is *kasher* — once the blood has been thrown — that the meat becomes fit for eating, and only then does the clock for *nosar* begin. This point is explicit in the *mishnayot* at the end of the first *perek*: *l'achar zrikah* is when *chiyuv* for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* applies. If one ate the meat before *zrikat hadam*, that may be an *issur*, but it is not the *issur* of *nosar* with its *kares*.

### Post-Zrikah Status

Once *zrikat hadam* — the sprinkling of the blood — is performed, *chayav alav mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei*: liability for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* now applies. *Piggul* — the intent to offer at the wrong time — can be formed earlier, but it only takes effect and generates *chiyuv kares* once *zrikah* has been completed. *Nosar* — meat left past its permitted time — only applies after the meat is fit to be eaten, which begins after *zrikat hadam*. Similarly, a *tamei* person who eats *kodshim* after *zrikat hadam* is liable for *kares*. All three statuses thus turn on the same pivot point.

And then: *v'ein bo me'ilah* — there is no *me'ilah*. This is because the bird *chatat* is entirely eaten by the *kohanim*; there are no *imurin* (the parts that go on the *mizbeach* in a large animal). The entirety of the meat becomes permitted to the *kohanim* after *zrikat hadam*, and so the *me'ilah* status dissolves at that moment.

To summarize the structure: *zrikat hadam* is the critical dividing line for *me'ilah* and for the activation of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei*. The *shechitah* (or *melikah* in the case of a bird) is the dividing line for susceptibility to *tumah*. The *linah* relevant here is that of the blood.

## Tvul Yom Debate

### Posel Query

The *gemara* focuses on a striking phrase in the *mishnah*: *huchshrah lehipasel b'tvul yom* — the bird became fit to be *disqualified* by a *tvul yom*. It uses the word *lehipasel* — to become *pasul* — and not *lehitamei* — to become *tamei*. The *gemara* asks: if a *tvul yom* is considered like a *sheni*, then when he touches *kodshim*, the *kodshim* should become *shlishi* — and *shlishi* of *kodshim* is indeed called *tamei*, not merely *pasul*, since *kodshim* can go all the way to *revi'i*. Why then does the *mishnah* say only *pasul*?

### Tanna'im Views

The *gemara* answers: *matnitin mani* — whose opinion does our *mishnah* represent? *Rabbanan hi* — it follows the position of the *rabbanan* in the following *tosefta* from *Taharot*, where three opinions are recorded.

*Abba Shaul* holds: *tvul yom techilla l'kodesh* — with respect to *kodashim*, a *tvul yom* is treated as a *rishon*. He thus makes whatever he touches into a *sheni*, imposing a real *tumah* that can propagate further.

*Rabbi Meir* holds that the *tvul yom* is a *sheni*: he therefore makes *kodesh* into a *shlishi* — called *tamei* since *kodshim* can reach *revi'i* — and makes *terumah* into *shlishi* — called *pasul* since *terumah* stops there and cannot continue.

The *chachamim* hold a third position: *keshem she-hu posel mashkeh terumah ve-ochlei terumah, kach hu posel mashkeh kodesh ve-ochlei kodesh* — just as he makes the food and drink of *terumah* merely *pasul* (rather than *tamei*), so too he makes the food and drink of *kodesh* merely *pasul*, not *tamei*. This seems puzzling: if he is a *sheni*, he should make *terumah* into *shlishi* — which by *terumah* is *pasul* (since *terumah* stops there) — but by *kodesh* the same *shlishi* should be called *tamei* since it can propagate to *revi'i*.

The answer the *gemara* works toward is that the *chachamim* are saying something conceptually different. Regardless of what numerical "level" one assigns to the *tvul yom*, his *tumah* is so *kalush* — so residual and attenuated — that it propagates only one step and stops. It does not matter whether you call him a *rishon* or a *sheni*; by *terumah* he makes it *pasul*, and by *kodesh* he likewise makes it *pasul* — not because of how many levels of derivation remain, but because his *tumah* is inherently weak. It can invalidate one step further but cannot drive another step after that.

### Rava's Synthesis

*Rava* provides the synthetic framework: *le-Abba Shaul, shavinu rabbanan l'tvul yom k'rishon* — making the *tvul yom* into a *rishon* with respect to *kodashim* is itself a rabbinic stringency, a *ma'aleh* in *kodashim*. And *Rabbi Meir*'s position that he is a *sheni* is similarly a rabbinic characterization. But our *mishnah* is operating at the *de'oraita* level, and *de'oraita*, everyone agrees: the *tvul yom* is *posel* — he disqualifies *terumah* and *kodashim* — but he does not make them *tamei* in the sense that they can transmit further. The *de'oraita* rule is that his *tumah* is *posel* and no more.

The debate between *Abba Shaul*, *Rabbi Meir*, and the *chachamim* concerns the *derabbanan* level — what additional rabbinic stringency we impose, and whether we model his *tumah* as a *rishon* or a *sheni* for the purposes of those additional rabbinic laws. But at the *de'oraita* level, the position of our *mishnah* — *posel* only, not *metamei* — is universally held.

The *gemara* also makes clear that the same analysis applies to *mechusar kippurim*. And the principle about *shlishi* and *revi'i* operates exclusively with respect to food and drink (*ochel u-mashkeh*) — not vessels or persons — and only when we are speaking of *terumah* or *kodshim*.

## Meilah Post-Zrikah

### Meilah Query

The *gemara* skips over the *linah* clause without extended comment — which, as noted, is somewhat curious, since *nosar* properly starts only after *zrikat hadam* — and focuses instead on the clause that says after *zrikat hadam*, *ein bo me'ilah*: there is no *me'ilah*. The *gemara* notes that the phrasing implies there remains *some* problem even after *zrikat hadam* — after all, the *mishnah* first establishes that there *is* *me'ilah* before *zrikat hadam*, and now says there is *no* *me'ilah*, implying some residual concern.

The *gemara* asks: *amai v'amai* — why is there any remaining problem? *Mamona d'kahanei hu* — after you sprinkle the blood, the *kohanim* are supposed to eat it. One might have expected the *gemara* simply to invoke the answer it offered earlier in the tractate — that the *mishnah* frames the entire structure by first saying *mo'alin bah*, and the ending of *ein bo me'ilah* is merely the conclusion of that framing. *Tosafot* does address this, but the *gemara* here seems uninterested in that resolution and proceeds along a different path.

### Yotzei Case

*Amar Rabbi Chanina* — the same answer he offered in an earlier discussion: *le-yotzei, ke-Rabbi Akiva, de-amar zrika mo'elet le-yotzei* — the case is one of *yotzei*, meat that was taken outside the *Beit Hamikdash*. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, *zrikah* is effective even for meat that left the *Beit Hamikdash* — meaning *zrikah* removes such meat from *me'ilah* — yet the meat remains *assur* to be eaten, since *delav bat achilah hi*, it is not fit for eating. This is the sense in which there is no *me'ilah* yet a prohibition remains: the change of status through *zrikah* is real, but the underlying *issur* persists because the meat is unfit for consumption.

## Mitzuy Debate

### Huzah Damah

The *gemara* now introduces a distinct and important dispute. *Amar Huna amar Rav*: *mitzuy chatat ha'of eino me'akev* — squeezing out the blood of the bird *chatat* after the initial squirting is not *me'akev*, not a prerequisite that prevents the *korban* from being valid if omitted. To understand the question: after *melikah*, blood squirts from the exposed neck of the bird onto the wall of the *mizbeach*. The Torah then instructs that the remaining blood be squeezed out (*mitzuy*). Is this *mitzuy* an integral part of the *zrikah* process — without which the *korban* is invalid — or is it analogous to the *shfichat shirayim* of a regular *korban* (pouring the remaining blood at the base of the *mizbeach*), which everyone agrees is not *me'akev*?

*Rav Huna* in the name of *Rav* says it is not *me'akev*, and his proof is our *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* states that after *huzah damah* — after the blood was squirted — the status changes. This phrasing indicates that the squirting alone is the critical act, and the subsequent *mitzuy* is not essential.

### Alt Girsa

*Rav Adda bar Ahava*, also citing *Rav*, disagrees: *mitzuy chatat ha'of me'akev* — the squeezing is indeed a prerequisite. If it is not performed, the *avodah* is incomplete and the *korban* is invalid. To reconcile this with our *mishnah*, *Rav Adda bar Ahava* posits a different *girsa* — a different textual reading. In his version of the *mishnah*, the status changes not after *huzah damah* (the squirting) but after *mitzuy hadam* (the squeezing). The entire dispute thus hinges on which reading of the *mishnah* is authoritative.

### Textual Proof

The *gemara* attempts to resolve the matter from a *pasuk*. The verse in Vayikra 5 states: *v'hanish'ar ba-dam yimatze el yesod ha-mizbeach — chatat hi* — what remains of the blood shall be squeezed out to the base of the *mizbeach*, it is a *chatat*. The *gemara* argues: *bishlama l'Rav Adda bar Ahava*, this verse fits perfectly — it is only after you complete the *mitzuy* that the text declares *chatat hi*, indicating that this act is what renders it a valid *chatat*. But according to *Rav Huna*, what does *v'hanish'ar* mean, and why does the verse say *chatat hi* at this point?

*Rav Huna* responds by invoking the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*: *she-im nish'ar* — this is not a command to ensure there is leftover blood and squeeze it; rather, it means *if* there is leftover blood, this is what one does. And *chatat hi* does not refer to the completion of the *mitzuy* making it a *chatat*; rather, it refers back to the beginning of the verse — *v'hizzah mi-dam ha-chatat al kir ha-mizbeach*: once you do the initial sprinkling, *chatat hi*, it is now a *chatat*. The final clause simply closes a syntactic bracket opened at the start of the verse.

The *gemara* concedes, however, that the verse is ambiguous — it cannot be proven either way with certainty. *Rav Acha berei d'Rava* makes this point to *Rav Ashi*: the *pasuk* can be read to support either position. The emphasis on *chatat hi* following the *mitzuy* could suggest *me'akev*, but the language *v'hanish'ar* — the remnant — and the fact that the blood goes to the *yesod* (base) sounds more like *shirayim*, which all agree is not *me'akev*. The textual debate is unresolved from the verse alone.

### Mincha Parallel

*Rav Acha berei d'Rava* presses further with a parallel from *mincha*. If we follow the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*'s reading that *v'hanish'ar* means "if there is leftover," then by the same logic, the verse *v'ha-noteret min ha-mincha yochlu Aharon u-vanav* should mean: if there is leftover *mincha*, the *kohanim* eat it — but if there is no leftover (if it all burned or spilled), no problem. Would we really say that?

The *gemara* responds: *hatam ktiv v'hanoteret kra yiteira* — in the case of the *mincha*, the word *noteret* (or its equivalent) appears *twice* in the relevant context. When the Torah repeats a detail, the principle of *shana alav ha-katuv la'akev* applies — the repetition signals that this element is *me'akev*. If you lack the remainder and do not fulfill this, the *korban* is invalid. There is indeed a *baraita* (cited from 68b in Zevachim) that enumerates: if the *kometz* lacked some of the flour, or the oil, or the *levonah* had been lost before it was offered — all of these are invalid, confirming that the remnant's availability is *me'akev* by *mincha*. Whereas by the *chatat ha'of*, the relevant phrase appears only once, and the standard principle for *kodshim* is that a single mention, without repetition, is not *me'akev b'dieved*. Even if the *mitzuy* is a *mitzvah*, one does not invalidate the *korban* for its absence.

## Bird Piggul

### Braita Quote

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that seems to challenge the position that *mitzuy* by the *chatat ha'of* is not *me'akev* — or at least, that bears on how we understand the critical act in bird *korbanos* for purposes of *piggul*. The *baraita* states: if one performed *melikah* and squeezed out the blood (*mitzuy daman*) of a *chatat ha'of* or an *olat ha'of* with the intent of *chutz li-zmano* (wrong time), it is invalid without *kares*; with intent of *chutz li-mkomo* (wrong place), it is *piggul* and one incurs *kares*.

This is striking: the *baraita* mentions both *melikah* and *mitzuy* as the relevant acts for both the *chatat* and the *olah* together. If *mitzuy* were irrelevant to the *chatat ha'of*, one would expect the *baraita* to distinguish between the two bird-offerings.

### Resolution

The *gemara* resolves this with a single word: *le-itztadin* — respectively. The *baraita* is speaking about two cases in parallel. When it says *she-malkan u-mitzah daman* — that one performed *melikah* and *mitzuy* — it means: by the *chatat ha'of*, the critical act for *piggul* is the *melikah* (and the squirting of blood); by the *olat ha'of*, the critical act is the *mitzuy* (since the Torah by the bird *olah* speaks only of squeezing, not squirting). The *baraita* addresses each animal's act respectively, not both acts for both animals. Therefore no proof can be drawn for or against whether *mitzuy* is *me'akev* by the *chatat ha'of*.

### Rabbi Yishmael

The *gemara* then returns to a statement in the school of *Rabbi Yishmael* cited earlier — that *v'hanish'ar ba-dam* means "if there is leftover blood," which implies *mitzuy* is not *me'akev*. But this is complicated by another *baraita* from the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*, cited in Zevachim, which states that *shirayim me'akvin* — the pouring out of the remnant blood is *me'akev*. *Rav Pappa* explains: the subject of debate in that *baraita* in Zevachim is precisely the *mitzuy* of the *chatat ha'of* — not the *shirayim* of an ordinary animal *korban*, which no one considers *me'akev*. The conclusion is *trei tana'ei aliba de-Rabbi Yishmael* — there are two *tanna'im* who disagree about what *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position actually was on whether *mitzuy chatat ha'of* is *me'akev*. Just as we have two versions of what *Rav* said, we have two versions of what *Rabbi Yishmael* held.

## Olat Ha'Of

### Olah Bird Stages

The *mishnah* proceeds to the *olat ha'of*, the bird burnt-offering. *Mo'alin bah mishe-kideshu* — *me'ilah* begins from the moment of sanctification. *Nimlekah* — once *melikah* is performed — *huchshrah lehipasel b'tvul yom u-v'mechusar kippurim* — it can now become *pasul* through contact with a *tvul yom* or *mechusar kippurim*, for the same reasons as by the *chatat ha'of*: the bird is now no longer alive, it is *ra'uy* for the *mizbeach*, and so it carries the susceptibility to *tumah*. *U-linah* also applies — here again referring to the *linah* of the blood. Once *mitza damah* — once the blood is squeezed out — the *korban* is *kasher*, and *chayavin alav mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — the statuses of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* now apply. For the *olat ha'of*, note that the Torah speaks of *mitzuy* (squeezing) as the operative act with the blood, not squirting — and so by this bird-offering it is the *mitzuy* that marks the critical transition.

### Meilah Duration

Unlike the *chatat ha'of*, after *zrikat hadam* the *me'ilah* does not end. A bird *olah* is entirely burned on the *mizbeach* — no part is eaten by the *kohanim* — and so there is no moment at which the *me'ilah* status dissolves into a permission for anyone to benefit. However, there is one exception: the feathers and crop (*mora'ah v'notzah*) that are removed and cast at the eastern side of the *mizbeach* near the place of ashes. The *mishnah* does not explicitly address whether *me'ilah* continues on those once they are discarded — a question worth further investigation.

For the body of the bird, *me'ilah* continues *ad she-tetzei le-veit ha-deshen* — until the ashes are removed to the place outside the camp. This is an expression of the general principle that the *me'ilah* status of something *assur be-hana'ah* continues as long as *mitzvato* has not yet been completed. Something remains under the domain of *kodshei Hashem* as long as any required act connected to it remains undone. Even after the bird *olah* is burned on the *mizbeach*, the *mitzvah* of removing the ashes (*terumat ha-deshen* and ultimately bringing them outside) remains. Only once that is accomplished does the *me'ilah* status end.

## Burned Offerings

### Par Hanisraf

The *mishnah* now turns to the *parim ha-nisrafim u-se'irim ha-nisrafim* — the bulls and goats that are burned. These are cases in which the blood is brought inside the *heichal* — most notably on *Yom Kippur* — and as a consequence, the animal itself must be burned outside the camp rather than on the *mizbeach*. The *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur* and similar *korbanos* follow this pattern. In these cases, just as with the *olat ha'of*, no one will ever benefit from these animals; they are entirely burned and therefore *me'ilah* continues without any break point analogous to *zrikat hadam* permitting the meat.

*Mo'alin bahem mishe-hukdeshu* — *me'ilah* from the time of sanctification. *Nishchetu* — after *shechitah*, *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u-mechusar kippurim u-v'linah* — the standard susceptibilities to *tumah* and the *linah* of the blood apply. *Huzah daman* — once the blood is sprinkled, *chayavin alav mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — those three statuses become applicable. One can become liable for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* even in connection with things that are never eaten — and indeed even for things burned on the *mizbeach* or burned outside the camp — as long as the *korban* has been made *kasher* through *zrikat hadam*. The critical trigger for these three statuses is not the edibility of the item but the valid completion of the blood service.

*U-mo'alin bahem b'veit ha-deshen* — *me'ilah* continues even in the place of ashes. This is the interesting contrast with the regular *olah*: the *olah* was burned on the *mizbeach* and then the ashes taken outside — so the *mizbeach* burning is the *mitzvah* and the removal is the completion. For the *parim ha-nisrafim*, the burning itself takes place outside the camp, in the *beit ha-deshen*. So the *mitzvah* begins in the very place where ashes accumulate. When is the *mitzvah* complete? *Ad she-yituch ha-basar* — until the flesh dissolves. The *Rashi* here glosses this as *sheni'aseh eifer* — until it turns to ash. There is an interesting asymmetry: the *olah* continues in *me'ilah* even after the ashes form, until those ashes are physically taken outside; the *parim ha-nisrafim* already start outside, so *me'ilah* ends once the flesh has turned to ash, since there is no further act of removal required. Yet both ultimately terminate at the combination of ash formation and completed removal from the sacred domain.

## Regular Olah

### Olah Intro

The *mishnah* now arrives at the standard large-animal *olah* — a sheep or ox offered as a burnt-offering. *Me'ilah* begins *mishe-hukdeshah* — from the moment of sanctification. *Nishchata* — after *shechitah*, *huchshrah lehipasel b'tvul yom u-mechusar kippurim u-v'linah* — the susceptibilities to *tumah* become active, and *linah* applies to the blood. Then *mishe-nizrak damah* — after *zrikat hadam* — the *korban* becomes *kasher*, and *chayav aleha mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei*.

### Leather vs Meat

Here a nuance emerges. A regular *olah* is entirely burned — no part is eaten — and so one might expect *me'ilah* to continue throughout without interruption, just as by the *olat ha'of*. And indeed, *me'ilah* continues on the meat and the *imurin* after *zrikat hadam*, all the way until the ashes are taken to the *beit ha-deshen*. However, there is one exception: *v'ein mo'alin ba-oros* — there is no *me'ilah* by the leather after *zrikat hadam*. The skin of the *olah* goes to the *kohanim*; it is not burned. Therefore *zrikat hadam* does change the *me'ilah* status of the leather specifically, because at that moment the leather passes to the *kohanim* and is no longer *kodshei Hashem* in the *me'ilah* sense. For the meat and *imurin*, however, *me'ilah* continues *ad she-tetzei le-veit ha-deshen*.

## Chatat & Asham

### Chatas Intro

The *mishnah* turns next to the *chatas*, the *asham*, and the *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* — all of which are *kodshei kodashim* but, unlike the *olah*, have portions eaten by the *kohanim*. *Mo'alin bahem mishe-hukdeshu* — *me'ilah* from the moment of sanctification. *Nishchetu* — after *shechitah*, *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u-mechusar kippurim u-v'linah* — the standard *tumah* susceptibilities and *linah* of the blood apply. After *zrikat hadam*, *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — the three statuses apply. This is consistent with everything we have seen: the liability for eating them while *tamei*, or eating them as *piggul* or *nosar*, only begins once the *korban* is *kasher*.

### Meat vs Imurin

Now the *me'ilah* picture diverges from the *olah*. *V'ein mo'alin ba-basar* — there is no *me'ilah* on the meat after *zrikat hadam*, because the meat is now permitted to be eaten by the *kohanim*. Once it is permitted, *me'ilah* no longer applies. *Aval mo'alin b'imureihem* — but the *imurin*, the portions of fat and limbs that go on the *mizbeach*, retain their *me'ilah* status after *zrikat hadam* since they are to be burned, not eaten. *Me'ilah* on the *imurin* continues until those parts are burned and the ashes are taken out to the *beit ha-deshen*, just as with the *olah*.

## Shtei Halechem

### Bread Stages

The *mishnah* now addresses *shtei ha-lechem* — the two loaves of bread brought on *Shavuot* together with the communal *shelamim* offering. *Mo'alin bahem mishe-hukdeshu* — *me'ilah* applies from the moment the flour (or dough) is sanctified. The parallel question to the animal *korbanos* — when does the item become susceptible to *tumah*? — is answered differently here. With animals, the dividing line was *shechitah*, when the animal ceased to be alive and became *ra'uy* for the *mizbeach*. With bread, the animal is not alive to begin with; the dough can become *tamei* at any time in principle. However, for the bread to attain the specific *kedushah* befitting a *korban* — what might be called *kedushah of the body* (*kedushat ha-guf*) — it must actually become *lechem*. That moment is *karmu ba-tanur* — when a crust forms in the oven. Before that, it is dough, not bread, and does not have the intrinsic *kedushah* of the *shtei ha-lechem*. Therefore: *karmu ba-tanur, huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u-mechusar kippurim*.

Note also that *linah* is not mentioned here — the concept of *linah* in this context pertains to the blood of the accompanying *korban*, and the discussion is about bread rather than blood.

Once the crust has formed, the bread is ready for the next stage: *lishchitat ha-zevach* — it is now fit for the *shechitah* of the accompanying lamb to be performed. If the bread were not yet ready, the *shechitah* could not be done in coordination with it.

### Bread Status

*Nizrak daman shel kevasim* — once the blood of the lambs accompanying the *shtei ha-lechem* on *Shavuot* is thrown, the bread's status is bound up with the completion of the *korban*: *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — liability for *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* now applies to the bread. This is striking: *nosar* applies to the bread itself — if one does not eat the bread within its permitted time, it becomes *nosar*. Similarly, *piggul* of the *korban* renders the bread *piggul*, and eating the bread while *tamei* generates liability. *V'ein bahem me'ilah* — and there is no *me'ilah* by the bread, because after *zrikat hadam* the bread is permitted to the *kohanim* and *me'ilah* does not apply to something that is permitted.

## Lechem Hapanim

### Show Bread Intro

The *mishnah* continues with *lechem ha-panim* — the show bread placed on the golden table in the *Heichal* each week. *Mo'alin bahem mishe-hukdeshu* — *me'ilah* from the time of sanctification. Once again, *karmu ba-tanur* — the formation of a crust in the oven — is the moment when *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u-v'mechusar kippurim* applies, for the same reasons as by the *shtei ha-lechem*.

### Arrangement Stage

Once the crust has formed, the bread is ready for the next stage: *u-lehasder al gabei ha-shulchan* — to be arranged on the table in the *Heichal*. Just as with the *shtei ha-lechem*, where the crust made the bread fit for *shechitah* of the accompanying *korban*, here the crust makes the bread fit to be placed on the table. *Karvu ha-bazichin* — once the small spoons of frankincense (*bezichin*) that accompany the *lechem ha-panim* are offered up, the bread becomes fit to be eaten. This is the parallel to *zrikat hadam* in the other *korbanos*: the offering of the *bezichin* is what renders the bread permitted. At that point, *chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — those three statuses apply. *V'ein bo me'ilah* — and there is no *me'ilah* on the bread, since it is now permitted to the *kohanim*.

## Menachot

### Mincha Intro

The *mishnah* now addresses *menachot* — standard meal-offerings. *Mo'alin bahen mishe-hukdeshu* — *me'ilah* from sanctification. Here the analogue to the *shechitah* of animals or the *crust* of bread is: *kadshu ba-kli* — when the *mincha* is placed into a *kli shares* (a sacred service vessel). That is when it attains *kedushat ha-guf* as the thing that will actually be the *korban*. Prior to that, even though the flour may have *kedushah*, it is more akin to *kedushat damim* (a monetary *kedushah*) rather than the intrinsic *kedushah* of the *korban* itself. Once placed in the *kli shares*, *huchsharu lehipasel b'tvul yom u-v'mechusar kippurim*.

### Kometz Offering

*U-v'linah*: the *linah* clock starts running from when the *mincha* is placed into the *kli shares*, because from that moment the *mincha* must be brought that same day. If it is left overnight without the *kometz* being offered, the entire *mincha* is *pasul* through *linah*. This is in contrast to the animal *korbanos*, where the *linah* discussed earlier was the *linah* of the blood (collected but not yet sprinkled), and in contrast to the bread-offerings where *linah* is not a meaningful category. Here, *linah* refers to the *mincha* itself remaining overnight without the *kometz* being burned.

*Karev ha-kometz* — once the *kometz* (the handful separated from the *mincha*) is offered on the *mizbeach*, the *mincha* becomes a *kasher korban*, and *chayav alav mishum piggul, nosar, v'tamei* — those three statuses become applicable.

### Kometz Meilah

*V'ein mo'alin ba-shirayim* — there is no *me'ilah* by the remainder of the *mincha* (*shirayim*) after the *kometz* is offered, because the *shirayim* are eaten by the *kohanim*. *Aval mo'alin ba-kometz* — but there is *me'ilah* on the *kometz* itself, which is burned on the *mizbeach*. That *me'ilah* continues *ad she-yeitzei le-veit ha-deshen* — until the ashes of the *kometz* are taken out to the *beit ha-deshen*, consistent with the pattern for everything burned on the *mizbeach*.

## Ashes Debate

### Ashes Query

The *gemara* now takes up an *itmar* — a reported dispute among *Amoraim*: what is the law for one who benefits (*neheneh*) from the *eifer tapuach* — the mound of ashes that accumulates on top of the *mizbeach*? The *mizbeach* was a very active place; ashes were not removed daily in a comprehensive way. The *terumat ha-deshen* performed each morning was largely symbolic — a small amount was taken and placed at the side of the *mizbeach*. A full cleaning, in which the ashes were taken outside the camp to the *beit ha-deshen*, only occurred when necessary. In the meantime, ashes built up into a large mound (*tapuach*), and that mound had potential agricultural value — as fertilizer, for example. The question is: does *me'ilah* apply to those ashes?

### Rav vs YY

*Rav amar ein mo'alin bo* — *Rav* holds there is no *me'ilah* once the item has become ashes. *Rabbi Yochanan amar mo'alin bo* — *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that *me'ilah* continues even then.

### Trumas Hadeshen

*Lifnei terumat ha-deshen, kulei alma lo pligi d'mo'alin bo* — before the *terumat ha-deshen* is performed the following morning, everyone agrees there is *me'ilah*. The reasoning is *lo na'asis mitzvato*: the *mitzvah* connected to this item has not yet been completed. The ashes from a *korban* burned the previous day have not yet had the morning ritual of *terumat ha-deshen* performed in connection with them, and so their *mitzvah* process is ongoing. *Ki pligi l'achar terumat ha-deshen* — the dispute applies after *terumat ha-deshen* has been performed the following morning. Does the completion of that ritual constitute *na'asis mitzvato*, ending the *me'ilah* status? Or does *me'ilah* continue until the ashes are physically removed to the *beit ha-deshen*?

*Rav amar ein mo'alin bo* — once *terumat ha-deshen* is done, the last *mitzvah* has been performed. The subsequent removal of ashes from the *mizbeach* to the outside is merely janitorial — maintenance of the *mizbeach* — not itself a *mitzvah*. Therefore *harei na'asis mitzvato*, the *mitzvah* is complete, and *me'ilah* no longer applies.

### Clothing Proof

*Rabbi Yochanan* argues from the verse in *parshat Tzav*: *v'lavash ha-kohen mido vad u-michnesei vad yilbash al besaro, v'herim et ha-deshen* — and he shall wear his linen garments and his linen trousers, and he shall lift the ashes. The verse then continues: *u-fashat et begadav v'lavash begadim acherim, v'hotzi et ha-deshen el mi-chutz la-machaneh* — he shall remove his garments and put on other garments, and bring the ashes outside the camp. *Rabbi Yochanan* maintains that *begadim acherim* — the "other garments" — refers not to weekday clothes but to a different set of *bigdei kehunah*, less expensive priestly vestments but priestly vestments nonetheless. If the removal of ashes to outside the camp required *bigdei kehunah*, it is not mere janitorial work — it is an *avodah*, a sacred act. *Keivan d'tzarich bigdei kehunah, bi-kdushatah kayei* — since it still requires priestly garments, the item retains its *kedushah*, and *me'ilah* continues until the ashes are physically brought outside.

This position is strongly supported by our *mishnah*, which consistently states *ad she-tetzei le-veit ha-deshen* — until it goes out to the *beit ha-deshen* — as the terminus of *me'ilah*.

### Rav Response

The *gemara* challenges *Rav* from this *mishnah*: *tnan, mo'alin bahen ad she-totzi le-veit ha-deshen* — the *mishnah* explicitly says *me'ilah* continues until the removal to the *beit ha-deshen*. This appears to directly contradict *Rav*'s position. *Amar lach Rav*: *ad she-tehei re'uya le-veit ha-deshen* — one should read the *mishnah* as saying not that the ashes must literally be brought out, but that *me'ilah* continues until they are *fit* to be brought out. Once *terumat ha-deshen* has been performed, the ashes are now in a state where they can be brought out — *re'uya le-veit ha-deshen* — and at that point, *Rav* holds, *mitzvato ne'eset* and *me'ilah* ends. The parallel *Rav* draws is to *ad she-tehei re'uya li-zrikah* — just as we sometimes say that a *halacha* applies until something is fit for the next stage (even if that stage has not yet literally occurred), so too here: *re'uya le-veit ha-deshen* rather than literally *yatzah*. The question of how far this reading can be sustained — and to what degree the removal of ashes itself constitutes an independent *mitzvah* — will continue in the discussion to follow.
