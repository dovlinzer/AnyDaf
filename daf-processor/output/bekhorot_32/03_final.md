# Bekhorot 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Redeeming Korbanot

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כָּל פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין הֲנָאָתָן לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנוֹ — הֲנָאָתָן לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ? הֲנָאָתָן דִּבְעָלִים הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that with regard to **all disqualified consecrated** animals that were disqualified for sacrifice due to blemishes and were redeemed, all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the Temple** treasury, and the animals may consequently be sold in the manner of non-sacred meat. The Gemara asks: **When** does this apply? **If we say** that it applies to an animal **after its redemption,** i.e., after the owner redeemed it from the Temple treasury, does the **benefit** accrued belong **to the Temple?** Certainly not. Once a consecrated animal is redeemed from the Temple treasury, it is in the possession of its owner, which means that the **benefit** accrued belongs **to the owner.**

The *mishna* distinguishes between *korbanot* that bear a *mum* on the one hand, and *bechor* and *ma'aser* on the other, with respect to redemption and marketplace sale. Because ordinary blemished *korbanot* must be redeemed, the process requires an estimate of their value. To maximize that estimated value, the *mishna* permits the owners to sell such animals in the marketplace after redemption — at retail rather than wholesale — thereby raising the figure at which the redemption was originally assessed. *Bechor* and *ma'aser*, by contrast, are not redeemed at all; the *kohen* or the owner simply eats them. Accordingly, there is no purpose served by inflating their market value, and they may not be sold through the marketplace.

### Hekdesh Benefit Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנוֹ. נִשְׁחָטִין? הָא בָּעֵי הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it applies to an animal **before its redemption,** in which case the money accrued from its sale, which renders it non-sacred, belongs to the Temple treasury. But if so, the mishna's statement that the animals **may be slaughtered** in the butchers' market is problematic. The Gemara explains the difficulty: How can the animal be evaluated after having been slaughtered? Redeeming a sanctified animal **requires standing and valuation,** i.e., it has to be set standing before a priest for him to appraise its monetary value and only then is it redeemed (see Leviticus 27:11–12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹן, וּמַאי הֲנָאָתָן לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ? אַדְּמֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the ruling of the mishna **actually** applies to a case where the animals are sold and slaughtered **after their redemption** by the owner. **And what** is the meaning of the mishna's statement that all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the Temple** treasury? This is not referring to the profit from the sale in the butchers' market, as was previously assumed. Instead, it is referring **to** their **initial** redemption from the Temple treasury.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכֵיוָן דְּשָׁרֵי לְהוּ מָר, נִמְכָּרִין בָּאִיטְלִיז וְנִשְׁחָטִין בָּאִיטְלִיז וְנִשְׁקָלִין בְּלִיטְרָא, טָפֵי וּפָרֵיק מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Since the Sage** in the mishna **deems** it **permitted** for the redeemed animals to be subsequently **sold in the butchers' market and slaughtered in the butchers' market, and weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** there is a higher probability that the owner will **redeem** the animal for **more** money **initially.** The owner's later ability to sell the redeemed animal for the highest market value is in effect a benefit that eventually accrues to the Temple treasury, as he will be willing to spend more money to redeem the animal.

The *Gemara* at the top of 31b opens by pressing on the phrase *kol pesulei hamukdashin hanas'am lehekdesh* — that *hekdesh* benefits from all invalid *korbanot*. When exactly does *hekdesh* receive that benefit? If you say after redemption (*le'achar pidyono*), then once the animal has been redeemed it is the owners who benefit from it, not *hekdesh*. So it must mean before redemption (*lifnei pidyonam*), when *hekdesh* still owns the animal. But that creates a different difficulty: before redemption, how can the *mishna* speak of slaughtering the animal in the marketplace to raise its value? The moment it is still *hekdesh*, it requires *ha'amadah veha'arachah* — the animal must be alive when redeemed. According to the opinion that *kodshei mizbei'ach* do not require *ha'amadah veha'arachah*, one could simply slaughter first and then redeem. But according to the opinion that *korbanot* with a *mum* can only be redeemed while alive, the slaughter would have to follow redemption, and the difficulty remains.

### Retroactive Valuation

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension by reading the two moments together. *Hekdesh* benefits at the moment of redemption — that is when the transfer of value occurs. But the *amount* of that value is determined by what the animal can fetch afterward. Since we permit the redeemed animal to be slaughtered and sold in the marketplace (*be'itliz*) and weighed out pound by pound (*venishkalin belitra*), the retail value is higher than a wholesale figure, and that higher retail value is the basis of the original estimate at the time of redemption (*tafi ufarek meikara*). The *mishna* thus refers to two points in time: the point of redemption, where *hekdesh* is actually benefited, and the subsequent marketplace sale, which retroactively justifies the higher valuation at the moment of redemption.

## Bechor vs Ma'aser

### Conceptual Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וּמִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר שֶׁהֲנָיָיתָן לַבְּעָלִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּכוֹר — בָּאִיטְלִיז הוּא דְּלָא מִיזְדַּבַּן, הָא בְּבֵיתֵיהּ מִיזְדַּבַּן, אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂר — בְּבֵיתֵיהּ מִי מִיזְדַּבַּן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the meat of blemished, consecrated animals may be sold in the same manner as non-sacred meat, **except for the firstborn** animal **and** animal **tithe** offering, **as** all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the owner.** Accordingly, even if it is blemished, the meat may be sold only in the owner's house. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** in the case of **a firstborn** offering, **it is in the butchers' market that it may not be sold,** whereas **it may be sold in its** owner's **house. But** with regard to the animal **tithe** offering, **may it be sold in its** owner's **house?**

The *mishna* continues: *chutz min habechor umin hama'aser shenas'am leba'alim* — except for *bechor* and *ma'aser*, whose benefit accrues to their owners. Since neither is ever redeemed, there is no valuation procedure to maximize, and the dignity owed to a sanctified animal is ill-served by a public marketplace transaction. They are therefore to be sold, if at all, privately rather than displayed in the meat stalls.

### Bechor Sales Question

The *Gemara* notes that the restriction on *bechor* is readily intelligible: *be'itliz hu delo mizdaben* — it simply cannot be sold in the meat marketplace. But *bevayis mizdaben* — it may be sold from one's home. A *bechor ba'al mum* is owned by the *kohen*, and once it has a *mum* and has been slaughtered outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, its *kedusha* does not restrict who may eat it. The *kohen* may therefore sell it to another *kohen* or even to a *Yisrael*, provided it is eaten in the normal fashion. No verse prohibits sale as such, only redemption, so private sale is straightforwardly permitted.

The more pressing question is *ma'aser behemah*. The *mishna* implies that *ma'aser* likewise cannot be sold in the marketplace but can be sold from one's home — yet a closer look at the sources raises the question of whether *ma'aser behemah* may be sold at all.

### Torah Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּבְכוֹר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה״ וְנִמְכָּר חַי, בְּמַעֲשֵׂר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ וְאֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר, לֹא חַי וְלֹא שָׁחוּט, לֹא תָּם וְלֹא בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **With regard to a firstborn** animal it **is stated: "You shall not redeem"** (Numbers 18:17), indicating that its sanctity can never be removed from it and transferred to non-sacred objects. **But it can be sold** after it comes into the possession of the priest, while it is **alive.** By contrast, **with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, it **is stated: "It shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33), which teaches that its sanctity can never be removed from it, **and it cannot be sold** either, as the Gemara will explain (32a–b), **not** when **alive and not** when **slaughtered, not** when **unblemished and not** when **blemished.** This *baraita* clearly states that the animal tithe offering may not be sold no matter what its status, which seems to contradict the mishna.

A *braisa* sets out the relevant verses. By *bechor* the Torah says *lo tipadeh* — it shall not be redeemed — but says nothing to prohibit sale. The *bechor* may therefore be sold even while alive, once it has acquired a *mum*. By *ma'aser*, however, the verse reads: *vehaya temurato yihiyeh kodesh lo yiga'el* — its exchange shall be holy, it shall not be redeemed. And the *braisa* derives from this that *ma'aser behemah* cannot be sold: neither alive (*lo chai*) nor after slaughter (*velo shachut*), neither unblemished (*lo tam*) nor blemished (*velo ba'al mum*). This is a sweeping prohibition, and it immediately creates a tension with our *mishna*, which appears to allow at least private sale.

### Orphans Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִילְּתָא אִיקַּשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּאוּרְתָּא, וְשַׁנְּיַיהּ בְּקַדְמוּתָא מִבָּרַיְיתָא, בְּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים עָסְקִינַן, וּמִשּׁוּם הֲשָׁבַת אֲבֵידָה נָגְעוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **At night, Rav Sheshet found this matter difficult, and he resolved it in the morning from a *baraita*.** He explains that in the mishna **we are dealing with** a case of **an animal tithe** offering **belonging to** young **orphans** that was blemished and slaughtered. Since the orphans are unable to consume the entire animal and would therefore suffer a monetary loss if the meat were to spoil, the Sages permitted its sale, **and** it is **due to** the principle of **returning a lost item** that **they touched upon it,** i.e., allowed for this.

The question of how a sale of *ma'aser behemah* could ever be permitted kept Rav Sheshes up at night, and the answer came to him early in the morning: *bema'aser behemah shel yesomim askinan* — we are dealing with the *ma'aser behemah* of orphans. The concern is *hashavas aveidah* in the figurative sense: if a young animal is slaughtered and the orphans have no means of consuming or preserving all of the meat, a great financial loss will result. Because these are minors with no breadwinner to manage their affairs, the Rabbis permitted the sale so that the loss could be prevented. Of course, this leniency can only operate where the prohibition against sale is rabbinic rather than biblical — and the *Gemara* will demonstrate below that the biblical prohibition applies only while the animal is alive.

## Orphans & Ma'aser

### Rav Idi's Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אִידִי סַרְסְיֵהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת הֲוָה שַׁמְעַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אֲזַל אַמְרַהּ בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא וְלָא אַמְרַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ, שְׁמַע רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אִיקְּפַד, אֲמַר: מַאן דְּעָקֵיץ לִיעְקְצֵיהּ עַקְרַבָּא, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אָגוּרָה בְּאׇהׇלְךָ עוֹלָמִים״? וְכִי אִיפְשָׁר לְאָדָם לָגוּר בִּשְׁנֵי עוֹלָמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Idi, the attendant [*saraseih*] of Rav Sheshet, heard** this explanation **from** Rav Sheshet. Rav Idi **went and stated** the matter **in the study hall, but did not say it in his** master's **name. Rav Sheshet heard** what he had done and **was annoyed.** Rav Sheshet **said: The one who stung me, let him be stung by a scorpion.** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rav Sheshet, what difference does it make to him** whether or not his interpretation was cited in his name? The Gemara answers that this is **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "I will dwell in Your tent forever [*olamim*]"** (Psalms 61:5)? Rav asked: **But is it possible for a person to live in two worlds [*olamim*],** this world and the next, simultaneously?

Rav Idi was the *shamash* of Rav Sheshes — his personal attendant. He heard the answer that Rav Sheshes had worked out overnight and went ahead and stated it in the *Beit Midrash* without attributing it to Rav Sheshes, creating the impression that it was his own *chiddush*. Rav Sheshes heard of this and became deeply upset, declaring, *man d'akitz l'aktei akrava* — may the one who stung me be stung by a scorpion. This is a striking passage: why should a Torah scholar care so much whether a teaching is transmitted in his name?

### Torah Transmission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר דָּוִד: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, יֹאמְרוּ דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּי בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁאוֹמְרִים דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּיו בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה — שִׂפְתוֹתָיו דּוֹבְבוֹת בַּקֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, David,** who recited this psalm, **said** to God: **Master of the Universe, let** people **say a matter of *halakha* in my name in this world** after I have passed on to another world. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** With regard to **any Torah scholar in whose name a matter of *halakha* is stated in this world, his lips mouth** the words **in the grave,** as though he were speaking.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר זְעֵירִי: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״וְחִכֵּךְ כְּיֵין הַטּוֹב הוֹלֵךְ לְדוֹדִי לְמֵישָׁרִים דּוֹבֵב שִׂפְתֵי יְשֵׁנִים״, כְּכוֹמֶר שֶׁל עֲנָבִים, מָה כּוֹמֶר שֶׁל עֲנָבִים כֵּיוָן דְּאָדָם נוֹגֵעַ בּוֹ – דּוֹבֵב, אַף תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָדָם אוֹמֵר דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּיו – דּוֹבְבוֹת שְׂפָתָיו בַּקֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Ze'eiri says: What is the verse** from which this is derived? The verse states: **"And the roof of your mouth is like the best wine that glides down smoothly for my beloved, moving gently the lips of those who are asleep"** (Song of Songs 7:10). Here, words of Torah in the mouths of the Jewish people are likened **to a pile [*kekhomer*] of grapes** left to warm before they are pressed, which causes the wine to exude from them easily: **Just as** with regard to **a pile of grapes, when a person** places his finger on it and **touches it, it moves** and issues a sound as the wine bursts forth, **so too** with regard to **Torah scholars, when a matter of *halakha* is stated in their name, their lips mouth** the words **in the grave.**

The *Gemara* answers with a famous teaching. Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: what does it mean when David says, *agura be'ohalcha olamim* — I will dwell in Your tents for worlds, in the plural? Can a person dwell in two worlds simultaneously? Rather, David is praying: Master of the Universe, may people transmit Torah teachings in my name in this world, and through that I will dwell in Your tents in worlds — both this world and the next — simultaneously. This rests on the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: *kol talmid chacham she'omrim davar shmuah mipi ba'olam hazeh dovvot siftotav bakever* — any Torah scholar in whose name a teaching is repeated in this world, even after his death, his lips continue to murmur in the grave. The *Gemara* then cites Rav Yitzchak ben Birya, who finds this in the verse *v'chichech k'yayin hatov holech l'dodi l'meisharim doveiv siftei yesheinim* — your palate is like good wine flowing to my beloved, causing the lips of the sleeping to speak. Just as touching a cluster of ripe grapes causes the juice to run freely, so too the moment a Torah teaching is mentioned in a scholar's name, his lips begin to murmur again in the grave. David's prayer, then, was precisely for this: that his Torah be transmitted by name so that he would remain alive in both worlds at once. This is why Rav Sheshes was wounded so personally — had the teaching been transmitted in his name, his lips would have moved in the grave; robbed of the attribution, he was robbed of that immortality.

One might wonder: the phrase is *omrim davar shmuah mipi* — they say a teaching from his mouth — rather than simply repeating what he said. It seems clear that the *Gemara* understands this to mean saying it over specifically in his name, not merely repeating his idea without attribution.

### Hashavas Aveidah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בָּרַיְיתָא? דְּתַנְיָא: מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים — מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ, וּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ — מַבְלִיעוֹ בְּעוֹרוֹ, בְּחֶלְבּוֹ, וּבְגִידוֹ, וּבְקַרְנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Rav Sheshet's explanation: **What** is the ***baraita*** mentioned by Rav Sheshet, upon which he based his resolution? The Gemara explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an animal tithe** offering **of** young **orphans, one may sell it. And** with regard to **an animal tithe** offering **that one has slaughtered,** whose meat may not be sold, **one may include** the cost of the meat **in** the cost of its negligent parts, thereby inflating the cost of those parts, i.e., the animal's **hide, its fat, its sinews, and its horns,** which are permitted to be sold.

Returning to the legal discussion: the underlying rationale for permitting orphans to sell *ma'aser behemah* is *hashavas aveidah*, the concern for preventing loss. When the animal is slaughtered, if there is more meat than the orphans can consume before it spoils, and they are unable to manage the situation as a capable adult might — salting the meat, arranging reciprocal hospitality, and so on — the entire financial value of the animal may simply be wasted. To prevent this, the Rabbis permitted the sale.

### Havla'ah & Orphans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָאָמַר — מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ בְּהַבְלָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **saying?** Initially, the *baraita* stated that one may sell the meat of an animal tithe offering belonging to young orphans, which indicates that this may be done in the normal fashion. The *baraita* then proceeds to state that the sale of the meat must be performed in the indirect manner of inclusion. **Abaye said:** The *baraita* is actually referring to only one case, and **this** is what it **is saying:** With regard to the meat of **an animal tithe** offering **of** young **orphans, it may be sold,** but only **by** means of **inclusion.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּגָדוֹל בְּהַבְלָעָה לָא? מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ לוּלָב מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁבִיעִית — נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶתְרוֹג בְּמַתָּנָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְלוֹקְחוֹ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: One can conclude **by inference** from this *baraita* **that** in the case of an animal tithe offering of **an adult,** i.e., a non-orphan, its meat may **not** be sold even **by** means of **inclusion. What is different** in this case **from that which is taught** in a mishna (*Sukka* 3:11): In the case of **one who purchases a *lulav* from another,** who is an *am ha'aretz*, **during the Sabbatical** Year, the seller **gives him an *etrog*** together with the *lulav* **as a gift, as it is not permitted for one to purchase** the *etrog* **during the Sabbatical** Year, and one may not give the value of Sabbatical-Year produce to an *am ha'aretz*, lest he engage in commerce with it, which is prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בָּהּ: לֹא רָצָה לִיתֵּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה מַאי? וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַבְלִיעַ לוֹ דְּמֵי אֶתְרוֹג בַּלּוּלָב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And we discussed this** mishna and asked: If the seller **did not want to give him** the *etrog* **as a gift, what** is the *halakha*? How can the buyer receive the *etrog*? **And Rav Huna said:** The seller **includes the value of the *etrog* in** the cost of **the *lulav*.** The buyer should purchase the *lulav* at an elevated price to cover the cost of the *etrog* as well. If inclusion is allowed in the case of the *etrog*, why would it not be permitted in the case of the meat of an animal tithe offering belonging to an adult?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא, הָכָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of the *etrog*, **the matter is not evident,** whereas **here,** with regard to the meat of an animal tithe offering, **the matter is evident.** The inclusion of the cost of the *etrog* in the elevated cost of the *lulav* is not evident, as it is reasonable that the price of a *lulav* might fluctuate. By contrast, inflating the cost of the negligible parts of the animal tithe offering to include the cost of its expensive meat is obviously an artifice and is therefore not permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, ״מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה״ תְּרֵי זִמְנֵי לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר, מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים מוֹכְרִין אוֹתוֹ כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה דְּגָדוֹל שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ מַבְלִיעוֹ בְּעוֹרוֹ בְּחֶלְבּוֹ בְּגִידוֹ וּבְקַרְנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** to Abaye in response: **If so,** that the *baraita* is referring to only one case, **why do I** need it to use the expression: **Animal tithe** offering, **twice? Rather, Rava said** that the *baraita* is discussing two distinct cases, and **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying:** With regard to the meat of **an animal tithe** offering **of** young **orphans, one may sell it in its** usual **manner, but** in the case of **an animal tithe** offering **belonging to an adult that was slaughtered,** one may sell its meat only by means of **including its** cost **in** the elevated price of the animal's **hide, its fat, its sinews, and its horns.**

The *braisa* presents two cases: first, *ma'aser behemah shel yetomim mochrin oso* — orphans may sell their *ma'aser behemah*. Second, for *ma'aser behemah* that has been slaughtered (*shesha'chato*), one may absorb its cost (*mavlio*) into the sale of the skin (*b'oro*), the fat (*uv'chelvo*), the sinews (*uv'gido*), and the horns (*uv'karnav*). In other words, one may price the non-meat parts at an inflated figure and include the meat as a free addition, thereby technically not selling the *ma'aser* meat at all.

### Abaye vs Rava

Abaye and Rava dispute the proper reading of these two clauses. Abaye reads them together: *ma'aser behemah shel yetomim mochrin oso b'havla'ah* — orphans may sell *ma'aser behemah*, but only using the technique of absorption. This implies that a normal adult may not use *havla'ah* at all — doing so would be too transparent a circumvention to be permitted. Rava objects that if that were the meaning, why would the text repeat the phrase *ma'aser behemah* a second time? The repetition signals a second, independent case. Rava therefore reads the *braisa* as presenting two distinct rulings: for orphans, outright sale (*k'darko*) without any workaround is permitted; for a normal adult who slaughters *ma'aser behemah*, absorption into the cost of the other parts is permitted.

### Lulav-Etrog Analogy

Abaye's position raises a difficulty from another *mishna*: one who purchases a *lulav* from a neighbor during *shvi'is* — where there is concern about transacting with produce that has *kedushat shvi'is* — may give the *esrog* as a gift. If the seller refuses to give it as a gift, Rav Yehuda rules that one may absorb the cost of the *esrog* into the price of the *lulav* (*mavli'a lo d'mei esrog b'lulav*). If *havla'ah* is permitted there, why should it be prohibited for an adult with *ma'aser behemah*? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam lo mukcha milta* — in the *lulav* case it is not so obvious, since the various components of the *arba'ah minim* are normally bundled together and the relative pricing is not transparent. *Hacha mukcha milta* — in the *ma'aser behemah* case it is entirely obvious: no one would genuinely pay thousands for the horns and sinews of a cow while receiving all the meat for free. The blatant artificiality of such an arrangement makes it impermissible for an ordinary adult, and Abaye therefore restricts *havla'ah* to the orphan case. Rava, reading the two clauses as separate rulings, allows orphans to sell outright and allows adults to use *havla'ah* — because for Rava the *braisa* is presenting two distinct leniencies, not one modified leniency.

## Biblical vs Rabbinic

### Tmura Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ יִהְיֶה קֹדֶשׁ לֹא יִגָּאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** that the meat of an animal tithe offering belonging to an adult may be sold by inclusion? **As it is written** with regard to the animal tithe offering: **"Then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be holy; it shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33). This indicates that a non-sacred animal designated as a substitute for an animal tithe offering assumes sacred status, while the animal tithe offering retains its sacred status as well. As stated earlier, the phrase "it shall not be redeemed" is interpreted as referring to the prohibition against selling the animal tithe offering. The verse's juxtaposition of substitution and the prohibition against selling an animal tithe offering indicates a similarity between the two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה — מֵחַיִּים, אֵימָתַי אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל — מֵחַיִּים, הָא לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — נִגְאָל, וְרַבָּנַן (ההוא) [הוּא] דִּגְזַרוּ לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה, אַטּוּ לִפְנֵי שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **When** does an animal tithe offering **render** consecrated as **a substitute** a non-sacred animal for which it is exchanged? Only **when** the animal tithe offering is **alive,** as substitution is invalid after its death. Similarly, **when may** the animal tithe offering **not be redeemed,** i.e., sold? **When** it is **alive.** It may be inferred from here that **it may be redeemed,** i.e., sold, **after** its **slaughter.** This teaches that by Torah law, the meat of an animal tithe offering may be sold once the animal has been slaughtered. **The Sages decreed** that an animal tithe offering may not be sold **after** its **slaughter, due to** the concern that one might sell it **before** its **slaughter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַנִּישּׁוֹם מֵחַיִּים — גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה אַטּוּ לִפְנֵי שְׁחִיטָה,
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: It was only with regard to **an item that is appraised when** the animal is **alive,** i.e., its meat, which gives the animal its primary value, that **the Sages decreed** that it may not be sold even **after** its **slaughter, due to** the concern that one might sell the animal **before** its **slaughter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִישּׁוֹם מֵחַיִּים — לָא גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן, וּבְיַתְמֵי אוֹקְמוּהָ רַבָּנַן אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** But with regard to **an item that is not appraised** when an animal is sold **when alive,** e.g., the hide and sinews, as these are not primary contributing factors to the animal's value, **the Sages did not decree** that one may not sell such items after the animal's slaughter. Consequently, the meat of an animal tithe offering may be sold by means of inclusion in the animal's hide, fat, sinews, and horns. **And with regard to** a case of young **orphans, the Sages established** the *halakha* **as** if **by Torah law,** and therefore the meat may be sold in its usual manner.

Rava offers a proof for his position that orphans may sell *ma'aser behemah* outright. The relevant verse reads: *v'hayah hu u'tmurato yihiyeh kodesh lo yiga'el* — it and its exchange shall be holy, it shall not be redeemed. The verse invokes *tmura*, the act of exchanging one animal for another, transferring sanctity. This act is only possible *mechayim* — while the animal is alive. Once it is dead, *tmura* cannot take effect. The verse thus situates the prohibition within a context that is inherently limited to the animal's life. Accordingly, the restriction encoded in *lo yiga'el* — which Rava (following Rashi's reading) interprets as *lo yimacher*, cannot be sold — applies only while the animal is alive.

### Rabbinic Extension

The conclusion follows: biblically, the prohibition on selling *ma'aser behemah* applies only *mechayim*, while it is alive. Once it has been slaughtered, sale is biblically permitted. It is the Rabbis who extended the prohibition to cover the slaughtered animal as well, as a fence against the biblical prohibition of selling it alive (*gazru rabanan le'achar shechita atu lifnei shechita*). This rabbinic extension is qualified: it applies only to *davar ha'nishom mechayim* — something that was valued while the animal was alive, meaning the meat itself, which is the primary commodity of a living animal. The Rabbis did not extend their prohibition to parts that do not factor into the living animal's market value — the skin, fat, sinews, and horns. That is why the *havla'ah* technique works at all: the parts whose sale is absorbed into the transaction are not subject to the rabbinic restriction. Furthermore, in the case of orphans, the Rabbis waived even this extension of their own decree, permitting outright sale of the slaughtered animal, since the underlying biblical prohibition does not apply after *shechita*.

### Alternate Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק סָבַר לַהּ לְהָא דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים שֶׁמּוֹכְרִים אוֹתוֹ כְּדַרְכּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And even Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak holds** in accordance with **that** opinion **of Rava,** that by Torah law, the meat of a blemished animal-tithe offering may be sold in the normal manner. **As Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that one may sell** the meat of a blemished **animal-tithe** offering **of** young **orphans in its** usual **manner,** without having to resort to the method of inclusion?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רַק בְּכׇל אַוַּת נַפְשְׁךָ תִּזְבַּח וְאָכַלְתָּ בָשָׂר״, אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ בְּרָכָה מֵחַיִּים אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** in a verse discussing consecrated animals that became blemished: **"Notwithstanding, after all the desire of your soul, you may slaughter and eat flesh,** according to the blessing of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:15). Now **which is the item that has no blessing when** it is **alive,** i.e., it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, **but** it does have a blessing **after** its **slaughter,** as indicated by the verse "You may slaughter…according to the blessing of the Lord your God"? **You must say** that **this is** the meat of the **animal tithe** offering.

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak holds the same substantive position as Rava — that orphans may sell outright — but derives it from a different verse. The Torah says (*Devarim* 12): *rak be'chol avat nafshcha tizbach ve'achalta vasar ke'virkat Hashem Elokecha* — with all the desire of your soul you shall slaughter and eat meat, as God has blessed you. This verse is understood in the context of a blemished *bechor* or *ma'aser*: once the animal bears a *mum*, you need not bring it as a *korban* but may slaughter and eat it. The phrase *ke'virkat Hashem Elokecha* — as God has blessed you — implies that the divine blessing comes specifically after slaughter. Since the animal is subject to various restrictions while alive, the blessing is manifest only once it has been slaughtered. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak derives from this that the post-*shechita* state is one of expanded permission — not merely eating but even selling is now included within the divine blessing. Selling from one's home is thus biblically permitted; only in the marketplace is it restricted, and that restriction itself is rabbinic.

### Bones Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לְהַבְלִיעוֹ בַּעֲצָמוֹת? רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי, חַד אָמַר: מַבְלִיעוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין מַבְלִיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* cited earlier teaches that the Sages permitted the inclusion of the value of a blemished animal-tithe offering's meat in the cost of its hide, fat, sinews, and horns. In this regard, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* **with regard to including** the value of the meat **in** the cost of **the** animal's **bones? Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, disputed this matter. **One says** that one may **include** the value in the cost of the bones, **and one says** one may **not include** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי — הָא בְּדַקָּה, הָא בְּגַסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And they do not disagree. This** one, who maintains that one may not include the value in the cost of the bones, is referring **to a small, domesticated animal,** whose bones are incapable of being fashioned into utensils and are therefore never sold. **That** one, who holds that one may include the value in the cost of the bones, is referring **to a large, domesticated animal,** whose bones can be used and are therefore sold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּגַסָּה, וְלָא פְּלִיגִי — מָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ, וּמָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that both **this** one **and that** one are referring even **to a large, domesticated animal, and** still they **do not disagree.** This **Sage** rules **in accordance with** the custom of **his locale, and** that **Sage** rules **in accordance with** the custom of **his locale.** In other words, in certain places the custom is to use the bones and sell them, while elsewhere they do not.

The *Gemara* raises a further question about *havla'ah*: the *braisa* mentions absorbing the cost of the meat into the skin, fat, sinews, and horns. What about bones? May one price the bones at an inflated value and absorb the cost of the meat there as well, or are bones too similar to the meat itself — or alternatively, too valueless — to serve as the vehicle for absorption?

### Sheep vs Cow

Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi dispute the question, one permitting *havla'ah* in bones and the other prohibiting it. The *Gemara* resolves their dispute by noting that they are not actually in disagreement: *ha b'daka ha b'gasa* — one is speaking of sheep and the other of cattle. Sheep bones are generally not used for anything and have no independent commercial value; absorbing the price of the meat into their cost would be transparently artificial. Cattle bones, on the other hand, are large, hard, and used in the production of bone vessels — there is a genuine market for them, and charging an elevated price for them can be done with a straight face. The *Gemara* adds that even if both sages are discussing cattle, they may simply reflect the practice in their respective locales: in one scholar's location, bones were regularly used for vessels, while in the other's they were not.

## Gezerah Shavah

### Verse Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: בִּבְכוֹר הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יִפְדֶּה״, וְנִמְכָּר; בְּמַעֲשֵׂר נֶאֱמַר: ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״, וְאֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר, לֹא חַי וְלֹא שָׁחוּט, וְלֹא תָּם וְלֹא בַּעַל מוּם. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself,** stated in the *baraita*: **With regard to a firstborn** male animal offering the verse **states: "You shall not redeem"** (Numbers 18:17), **but it may be sold** while alive, by the priest to whom it belongs. By contrast, **with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, **it is stated: "It shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33), indicating that its sanctity can never be removed from it, **and it cannot be sold, not** when **alive and not** when **slaughtered, not** when **unblemished and not** when **blemished.** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived, that the phrase "it shall not be redeemed" is referring to the prohibition against selling the animal tithe offering?

The *braisa* states the relevant prohibitions in full: by *bechor* the Torah says *lo tipadeh* — it shall not be redeemed; by *ma'aser* it says *lo yiga'el* — it shall not be redeemed; by *charamim* the Torah says *kol cherem lo yiga'el v'lo yimacher kodesh kodashim hu l'Hashem* — any *cherem* shall neither be redeemed nor sold, it is holy of holies to God. The question is: from where do we derive that *lo yiga'el* by *ma'aser* prohibits selling as well as redeeming?

### Rav Chanina Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן אָמַר רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, וְנֶאֱמַר ״וְלֹא יִגָּאֵל״ בַּחֲרָמִים, מָה לְהַלָּן מְכִירָה עִמּוֹ, אַף כָּאן מְכִירָה עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana says** that **Rav says, and similarly, Rav Dimi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is stated: "It shall not be redeemed," with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, **and it is stated: "And it shall not be redeemed," with regard to dedications [*baḥaramim*],** i.e., property that one consecrated by means of the expression: *Ḥerem*. With regard to dedications, the verse states: "Notwithstanding, any dedicated item…may neither be sold nor redeemed" (Leviticus 27:28). This is a verbal analogy: **Just as there,** with regard to dedications, the prohibition against **selling is** mentioned together **with** the prohibition against redeeming, **so too here,** with regard to the animal tithe offering, the prohibition against **selling is** mentioned together **with** the prohibition against redeeming.

Rav Chanina says in the name of Rav, and Rav Dimi repeats this in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the verse uses the phrase *lo yiga'el* both by *ma'aser* and by *charamim*. By *charamim*, *lo yiga'el* stands alongside *lo yimacher* — selling and redeeming are juxtaposed and both prohibited. The *gezeirah shavah* links the two: just as by *charamim* the prohibition on redemption comes paired with a prohibition on sale, so too by *ma'aser* the prohibition on redemption carries with it a prohibition on sale. The *Gemara* confirms: *mah hallan mechira imo, af kan mechira imo* — the same way selling accompanies the redemption prohibition by *charamim*, it accompanies the redemption prohibition by *ma'aser*.

For this *gezeirah shavah* to function, the linking word must be *mufni* — unattached, available for the purpose of linking — rather than essential to its own context. If the word is needed in its own verse, it cannot serve as a free link; the comparison would then be merely analogical and subject to logical challenge. A truly extra word, by contrast, signals that its entire purpose is to establish the link, and logical critique cannot dissolve it.

### Ha'avarah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַפְנֵי, דְּאִי לָא מַפְנֵי, אִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לַחֲרָמִים, שֶׁכֵּן חָלִים עַל הַכֹּל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua:** It must be that at least one of the phrases "It shall not be redeemed" is **free,** i.e., at least one of these terms is superfluous in its context, which means that it is included for the specific purpose of the verbal analogy. In such a case the verbal analogy cannot be refuted by logic, whereas if neither phrase is free one can refute the analogy if there is a significant difference between the two cases. **As, if** these terms are **not free,** the verbal analogy **can be refuted: What** is notable **about dedications,** in contrast to the animal tithe offering? They are notable in **that** they **apply to all** items one wishes to dedicate, whereas an animal tithe offering applies only to kosher animals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי אַפְנוֹיֵי מַפְנֵי, לֹא יֵאָמֵר ״וְלֹא יִגָּאֵל״ בַּחֲרָמִים, וְיִגְמַר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל, אַף חֲרָמִים קְדוֹשִׁים וְאֵינָם נִגְאָלִין. ״וְלֹא יִגָּאֵל״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This is **not so [*la'ai*], as** at least one of the phrases: "It shall not be redeemed," is certainly **free.** How so? **Let** the verse **not state: "It shall not be redeemed," with regard to dedications, and** instead one could **derive** the prohibition **from** an animal **tithe** offering: **Just as** an animal **tithe** offering **is sacred and may not be redeemed, so too, dedications are** also **sacred and may not be redeemed. Why** then **do I** need the phrase **"It shall not be redeemed" that the Merciful One writes,** in the case of dedications? It is evidently **to** be considered **free** to enable the verbal analogy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְמַעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁכֵּן קָדוֹשׁ לְפָנָיו וּלְאַחֲרָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion. It is still possible that the phrase "It shall not be redeemed," written with regard to dedications, is not free, as the suggested derivation stated above **can be refuted: What is** notable **about** the animal **tithe** offering? It is notable **in that** there is **sanctity before it and after it.**  If, instead of proclaiming as the tithe the tenth animal that one counted, one inadvertently proclaimed either the ninth animal or the eleventh animal as the tithe, the mistakenly proclaimed animal assumes the sacred status of the animal tithe offering. By contrast, one cannot inadvertently dedicate property to the Temple. Since the case of the animal tithe offering includes a stringency not shared by dedications, the phrase "It shall not be redeemed" is required to teach that dedications may not be redeemed, and is therefore not free for the verbal analogy.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenges Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: at least one side of the *gezeirah shavah* must be unattached. Can we establish that *lo yiga'el* is free — that is, extra — by *ma'aser*? One might have argued that the prohibition on redeeming *ma'aser* could have been derived from *bechor* via the verbal bridge of *ha'avarah* (passing over): both *bechor* and *ma'aser* use the language of *ha'avarah*, and since *bechor* cannot be redeemed, the same follows for *ma'aser* by analogy. If so, the explicit *lo yiga'el* by *ma'aser* is unnecessary and is thereby freed up to teach the prohibition on selling.

The *Gemara* tests whether the word is truly extra by *ma'aser* rather than by *charamim*, and whether the *ha'avarah* bridge genuinely makes the *ma'aser* restriction derivable without the explicit verse. The answer turns on whether each category possesses a unique feature that would defeat an inference from another category: *bechor* has automatic sanctity from birth (*kedushah merachem*), *ma'aser* sanctifies accidentally and symmetrically before and after (*kadesh lefanav ule'acharayiv*), and *charamim* apply to anything and everything. Because each has a distinctive stringency, one cannot straightforwardly derive one from another, and each needs its own explicit prohibition — unless we can find an exact verbal parallel that licenses the link without relying on logical analogy.

### Exact Phrase Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לֹא יֵאָמֵר ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ בַּחֲרָמִים, וְיִגְמַר מִבְּכוֹר: מָה בְּכוֹר קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל, אַף חֲרָמִים קְדוֹשִׁין וְאֵינָן נִגְאָלִין. ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, let** the verse **not state: "It shall not be redeemed," with regard to dedications, and** instead one can **derive** the prohibition **from** the **firstborn** offering: **Just as** the **firstborn** offering **is sacred and may not be redeemed,** as the verse states: "You shall not redeem it" (Numbers 18:17), **so too, dedications are** also **sacred and may not be redeemed. Why do I** need the phrase **"It shall not be redeemed" that the Merciful One writes** in the case of dedications? It is evidently **to** be considered **free** to enable the verbal analogy to the animal tithe.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לִבְכוֹר, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion as well: It is possible that the phrase "It shall not be redeemed" is not free, as the above derivation **can be refuted: What** is notable **about** the **firstborn** offering? It is notable **in that its sanctity is from the womb,** whereas dedications require an act of consecration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לֹא יֵאָמֵר ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, וְיִגְמַר הַעֲבָרָה הַעֲבָרָה מִבְּכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל. ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר לְמָה לִי? לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, let** the verse **not say: "It shall not be redeemed," with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, **and** instead one can **derive** the prohibition **from a verbal analogy** from the term of **passing stated there** and the term of **passing** stated in the case of a firstborn. With regard to an animal tithe offering the verse states: "Whatsoever passes under the rod" (Leviticus 27:32), and in the case of the firstborn offering it is stated: "And you shall cause to pass all that opens the womb, to the Lord" (Exodus 13:12). **Just as** the **firstborn** offering **is sacred and may not be redeemed, so too,** an animal **tithe** offering **is** also **sacred and may not be redeemed. Why do I** need the phrase **"It shall not be redeemed" that the Merciful One writes** with regard to animal tithe offerings? It is evidently **to** be considered **free** to enable the verbal analogy to dedications.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, בְּמַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי לָא, מַפְנֵי דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ כִּדְפָרְכִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** the phrase "It shall not be redeemed," written **with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, is itself **not free, as** the analogy between the animal tithe offering and the firstborn offering **can be refuted as we refuted** the analogy between the firstborn offering and dedications, since the firstborn offering is notable in that it is sanctified from the womb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַעֲבַרְתָּ״ קְרָא יַתִּירָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The term **"And you shall cause to pass,"** written in the context of the firstborn, **is a superfluous verse,** as it could have simply stated: All that opens the womb is to be for the Lord. Consequently, it is free to enable the verbal analogy. In sum, the Gemara is suggesting that due to the term "And you shall cause to pass," there is a verbal analogy between the animal tithe offering and the firstborn offering, from which it is derived that the animal tithe offering may not be redeemed. This means that the phrase "It shall not be redeemed," written with regard to the animal tithe offering, is free to enable the verbal analogy with dedications, from which the prohibition against selling the meat of the animal tithe offering is derived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוֹר נָמֵי נֵילַף ״גְּאוּלָּה״ ״גְּאוּלָּה״ מֵחֲרָמִים! דְּמַעֲשֵׂר מַפְנֵי, דִּבְכוֹר לָא מַפְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: With regard to **a firstborn** offering **as well, let us derive** that one may not sell its meat from a verbal analogy between the expression of **redemption** written there and the expression of **redemption** written in the context **of dedications.** This would contradict the ruling of the *baraita* that a firstborn offering can be sold after it enters the possession of the priest. The Gemara answers: The expression of redemption in the context **of** the animal **tithe** offering is **free** to enable the verbal analogy to dedications, but the expression of redemption in the context **of a firstborn** offering **is not free,** as it is required in its own context to teach that the firstborn offering may not be redeemed and as the source for the *halakha* of the animal tithe offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חָזֵית דְּלֹא תִפְדֶּה דִּבְכוֹר — לְגוּפֵיהּ, וּדְמַעֲשֵׂר — לְאַפְנוֹיֵי? וְאֵימָא: דְּמַעֲשֵׂר — לְגוּפֵיהּ, דִּבְכוֹר — לְאַפְנוֹיֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see that** you said that the phrase **"you shall not redeem"** written in the context **of a firstborn** offering is not free, but is necessary **for itself,** i.e., to teach that it may not be redeemed, **and** yet the expression of redemption written **with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering **is free** to enable the verbal analogy, as the *halakha* of its redemption is derived from the firstborn? **But** one can **say** the reverse, **that** the expression of redemption written in the context **of** the animal **tithe** offering is **for itself,** whereas **that** written **with regard to a firstborn** offering is **free.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין גְּאוּלָּה מִגְּאוּלָּה, וְאֵין דָּנִין פְּדִיָּיה מִגְּאוּלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We derive** the expression of **redemption [*geula*]** written in the context of the animal tithe offering **from** the expression of **redemption [*geula*]** written in the context of dedications, **but we do not derive** the expression of **redemption [*pediya*]** written in the context of a firstborn offering **from** the different expression of **redemption [*geula*]** written in the context of the dedications.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? הָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״ ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״ — זוֹ הִיא שִׁיבָה זוֹ הִיא בִּיאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What difference** is there whether or not an identical expression is used for a verbal analogy? **Didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** a verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses? The verse states: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]** on the seventh day" (Leviticus 14:39), and another verse with regard to the priest's visit seven days later states: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]** and look" (Leviticus 14:44). **This returning** and **this coming** have the same meaning and one can therefore derive by verbal analogy that the same *halakha* that applies if the leprosy had spread at the conclusion of the first week also applies if it had spread again by the end of the following week. Here too, the different terms of *pediya* and *geula* should not prevent the application of a verbal analogy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי, הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל אִיכָּא דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ — מִדְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This statement,** that the phrases used for a verbal analogy do not have to be identical, applies only **where there are no terms** that are **equivalent to it. But where there are terms that** are **equivalent to it, we derive** the verbal analogy **from** the terms **that** are **equivalent to it,** rather than from the terms that are not equivalent.

The *Gemara* establishes that when there exists an exact verbal parallel, we prefer that link over an approximate analogy. *Lo yiga'el* appears identically by both *ma'aser* and *charamim*. *Lo tipadeh* by *bechor* means the same thing, but in different words. The principle derived from the school of Rabbi Yishmael is that even words with the same meaning can sometimes anchor a *gezeirah shavah* — the example being *uva hakohen* and *veshav hakohen* in the laws of *nega'im*, where both mean the same thing and the *gezeirah shavah* is nonetheless valid. However, the *Gemara* qualifies this: that principle applies *heicha de'lika dedami lei* — only when there is no more exact match available. Where an identical phrase exists, *mididami lei yalfinan* — we prefer to link through the exact match. Since *lo yiga'el* by *ma'aser* is identical to *lo yiga'el* by *charamim*, and that word is rendered extra by *ma'aser* through the *ha'avarah* connection to *bechor*, we link the two identical phrases and derive the prohibition on selling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵילַף בְּכוֹר ״הַעֲבָרָה״ ״הַעֲבָרָה״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, דְּהָא מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי גָּמַר ״גְּאוּלָּה״ ״גְּאוּלָּה״ מֵחֲרָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But let one derive** that it is prohibited to sell the meat of a blemished **firstborn** offering from the verbal analogy between the expression of **passing** and the expression of **passing, from** the case of the animal **tithe** offering. This should be possible, **as** the sale of the meat of a blemished animal-**tithe** offering is **also** prohibited, as **derived** by a verbal analogy between the expression of **redemption** written in its context and the expression of **redemption** written **with regard to dedications,** as explained earlier.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי חֲרָמִים, ״הוּא״ — ״הוּא״ וְלֹא בְּכוֹר. וְאֵימָא ״הוּא״ וְלֹא מַעֲשֵׂר! מַעֲשֵׂר גְּאוּלָּה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One limits** the extension of the prohibition against selling **with regard to dedications** by adding the term **"it"** in the verse: "It is most holy to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:28). The term **"it"** teaches that the prohibition against selling applies only to it, i.e., dedications, **but not** to **a firstborn** offering. The Gemara raises a difficulty: But one can **say** instead that the term **"it"** teaches that the prohibition against selling applies to dedications, **but not** to the animal **tithe** offering. The Gemara answers: The prohibition against selling applies to the animal **tithe** offering, with regard to which the expression of **redemption** is written, **just like** dedications, while the term "it" limits the prohibition to the firstborn offering.

### Rava's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ דַּחֲרָמִים לָא צְרִיךְ, דְּאִיתַנְהוּ הֵיכָא? אִי בֵּי בְעָלִים — הֶקְדֵּשׁ נִינְהוּ, אִי בֵּי כֹהֵן — חוּלִּין נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says** that there is an alternative explanation for the prohibition against selling the animal tithe. The phrase **"It may not be redeemed,"** written **with regard to dedications, is unnecessary** and is therefore free to enable a verbal analogy with the animal tithe offering. Rava explains: **As where are** the items of dedication, designated for the priests, located? **If** they are **in the owner's possession, they are** considered **consecrated** and may not be redeemed. **And if they are in the priest's possession, they are** considered **non-sacred** and the priest may sell them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: חֲרָמִים, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהֵן בְּבֵית בְּעָלִים — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל חֵרֶם קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים הוּא לַה׳״. נְתָנָן לְכֹהֵן — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּחוּלִּין לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל חֵרֶם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לְךָ יִהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Dedications, as long as they are in the owner's possession, are like consecrated** property **in all respects, as it is stated: "Every dedicated item is most holy to the Lord"** (Leviticus 27:28). Once the owner **gave them to the priest, they are like non-sacred property in all respects, as it is stated: "Every dedicated item in Israel shall be for You"** (Numbers 18:14).

Rava offers an entirely different approach, departing from the *gezeirah shavah* methodology. Following Rashi's reading, Rava argues that *lo yiga'el* by *charamim* is not the relevant word — the operative word is *lo yimacher*, the explicit prohibition on selling. And that word, Rava contends, is extra by *charamim*. Why? Because *charamim* presents two distinct states: while the animal is still in the owner's possession, it has the full status of *hekdesh* — *harei hein k'hekdesh l'chol divreihem*. Once it is transferred to the *kohen*, it loses all *kedushah* entirely and becomes *chullin* — *harei hein k'chullin l'chol divreihem*, as the *braisa* teaches, citing the verse *kol cherem b'Yisrael lecha yihiyeh*. Given this framework: if the *cherem* is still with its owner, it is *hekdesh*, and the owner has already divested himself of legal ownership — he cannot sell what is no longer his. If it has passed to the *kohen*, it is *chullin* — of course the *kohen* may sell it. In neither state is the explicit prohibition on selling doing any work. The word *lo yimacher* is therefore genuinely superfluous by *charamim*. *Im eino inyan l'charamim* — since it is not needed there — *tneihu inyan l'ma'aser*, apply it to teach the prohibition on selling *ma'aser*. And why *ma'aser* rather than *bechor*? Because *lo yiga'el* by *ma'aser* provides the connecting thread to *charamim*, whereas *bechor* uses *lo tipadeh*, a different formulation.

## Rav Ashi's Proof

### Tmura Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ דְּמַעֲשֵׂר — לֹא יִמָּכֵר הוּא. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ יִהְיֶה קֹדֶשׁ לֹא יִגָּאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ashi says** that the prohibition against selling the animal tithe offering is not derived from dedications, but rather from the case of the animal tithe offering itself. The phrase **"It may not be redeemed,"** written **with regard to** the animal **tithe** offering, is to be understood as meaning: **It may not be sold. Rav Ashi** further **says: From where do I say this? As it is written: "Then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be holy; it shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה — מֵחַיִּים, אֵימָתַי אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל — מֵחַיִּים, הָא לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — נִגְאָל, הָא בָּעֵי הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi elaborates: **When** does the animal tithe offering **render** a non-sacred animal for which it is exchanged consecrated as **a substitute?** Only **when** the animal tithe offering is **alive.** Similarly, **when may** the animal tithe offering **not be redeemed** by its owner? Only **when** it is **alive,** which indicates that **it may be redeemed after** its **slaughter.** But when redeeming a sanctified animal, **it requires standing and valuation,** i.e., it has to be set standing before a priest for him to evaluate it and only then is it redeemed (see Leviticus 27:11–12). How, then, can the animal tithe offering be redeemed after having been slaughtered?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ — לֹא יִמָּכֵר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi continues: **Rather, conclude from** this verse that the phrase **"it may not be redeemed"** is not referring to redemption. Rather, it **is** actually to be understood as: **It may not be sold.** Accordingly, the prohibition against selling the animal tithe offering is in effect only while it is alive, as is the *halakha* with regard to a substitute; but once it is slaughtered, it may be sold.

Rav Ashi takes the most direct interpretive route of all. Rather than constructing a *gezeirah shavah* or deriving the prohibition through an extra word, Rav Ashi reads *lo yiga'el* by *ma'aser* as simply meaning *lo yimacher* — it shall not be sold — by virtue of contextual logic alone. The proof: the verse says *v'hayah hu u'tmurato yihiyeh kodesh lo yiga'el* — it and its *tmura* shall be holy, it shall not be redeemed. *Tmura* — the transfer of sanctity through exchange — is only operative *mechayim*, while the animal is alive. Once it has been slaughtered, *tmura* is impossible. The verse's prohibition is thus embedded in a context that is inherently limited to the living animal.

### Alive vs Dead Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This works out well according to the one who says** that animals **consecrated** to be sacrificed on the **altar** that were disqualified due to a blemish **were included in** the requirement of **standing and valuation. But according to the one who says** that animals **consecrated to** be sacrificed on the **altar** that were disqualified due to a blemish **were not included in** the requirement of **standing and valuation, what is there to say?** According to this opinion, the phrase "It may not be redeemed" can be interpreted literally, in contrast to Rav Ashi's claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן הָכִי קָאָמְרִינַן: מִי אִיכָּא דְּמֵחַיִּים לָא מִיפְּרִיק, וּלְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה מִיפְּרִיק? אַלְּמָה לָא מֵחַיִּים דְּאַלִּימָא קְדוּשְׁתֵּיהּ — לָא מִיפְּרִיק, לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה דְּאַקֵּיל לֵיהּ קְדוּשְׁתֵּיהּ — מִיפְּרִיק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This is what we said,** i.e., this is what we meant. The phrase "It may not be redeemed" cannot be understood literally, as **is there** ever an instance **where** an animal **may not be redeemed when alive, and** yet it **may be redeemed after** its **slaughter?** Since such a scenario is impossible, the verse must be referring to the prohibition against selling. The Gemara asks: But **why** can it **not** be said that an animal may be redeemed only after its slaughter? One can claim that **when** the animal is **alive, since its sanctity is strong,** it is logical to say that it **may not be redeemed.** Conversely, **after** its **slaughter, when its sanctity is weak,** it is logical to claim that **it may be redeemed.** If so, the phrase can be understood literally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא כֹּל דְּכֵן הוּא? וּמָה מֵחַיִּים דְּאַלִּים לְמִיתְפַּס פִּדְיוֹנוֹ — לָא מִיפְּרִיק, לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה דְּלָא אַלִּים לְמִיתְפָּס פִּדְיוֹנוֹ — מִיפְּרִיק? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ — לֹא יִמָּכֵר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: **But isn't** the opposite claim, that a slaughtered animal tithe offering may not be redeemed, based on **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If, when** the animal tithe offering is **alive and** its sanctity **is strong** enough **to transfer** that sanctity to **its redemption** money, just as it renders a non-sacred animal consecrated as a substitute, nevertheless **it may not be redeemed,** then **after** its **slaughter, when** its sanctity **is not strong** enough **to transfer** that sanctity to **its redemption** money, as at this stage it cannot render a non-sacred animal consecrated as a substitute, should it be able to **be redeemed?** In other words, the fact that substitution can be effected only with a living animal indicates that a strong sanctity is more easily transferable to another item than a weak sanctity. **Rather, conclude from this** that the phrase **"It may not be redeemed" is** actually to be understood as meaning that **it may not be sold.**

Now apply this to the literal meaning of *lo yiga'el*: if the word truly means "shall not be redeemed," then contextualizing it within *tmura* would yield the rule that redemption is prohibited only while the animal is alive, and once slaughtered it could be redeemed. But this is incoherent: redemption of a *korban* requires *ha'amadah veha'arachah* — the animal must stand before its assessors — and once slaughtered it cannot be redeemed regardless. The *Gemara* tests whether one might nonetheless say that while the animal lives its *kedushah* is stronger and therefore redemption is restricted, while after slaughter the *kedushah* weakens and redemption becomes available. The *Gemara* rejects this with a *kal vachomer* in the opposite direction: *u'mah mechayim d'alim l'mifas pidyon lo mifrak* — if even when the *kedushah* is strong and the animal is alive, when it has the power to extend to and take hold of new money, redemption is still prohibited; then *l'achar shechita d'lo alim l'mifas pidyon* — after slaughter, when the *kedushah* is weak and lacks the power to transfer — all the more so it cannot be redeemed. Stronger *kedushah* enables redemption; weaker *kedushah* forecloses it. A rule of "prohibited alive, permitted dead" makes sense for selling — it is a meaningful distinction — but makes no sense for redeeming. *Ela shma mina lo yiga'el lo yimacher hu* — we learn from this that *lo yiga'el* in this context means *lo yimacher*, shall not be sold.

### Why Use Lo Yiga'el

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יִמָּכֵר״! אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יִמָּכֵר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אִיזְדַּבּוֹנֵי הוּא דְּלָא מִזְדַּבַּן, דְּקָא עָבֵיד עוֹבָדִין דְּחוֹל, אֲבָל אִיפְּרוֹקֵי — מִיפְּרִיק, דְּהָא עָיְילִי דָּמָיו לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ. לְהָכִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל״, דְּלָא אִיזְדַּבּוֹנֵי מִיזְדַּבַּן וְלָא אִיפְּרוֹקֵי מִיפְּרִיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if so, **let the Merciful One write** explicitly: **It may not be sold.** The Gemara answers: **Had the Merciful One written: It may not be sold, I would say** that the animal tithe offering only **may not be sold, as one** who does so **performs an act** of **a non-sacred** item, by treating the consecrated animal in the same manner as a non-sacred animal and transferring its value to non-sacred money. **But** I would say that **it may be redeemed, as** in this manner **its value becomes consecrated. Therefore,** in order to counter this notion, **the Merciful One writes: "It may not be redeemed,"** which teaches both **that** the animal tithe offering **may not be sold and** that it **may not be redeemed.**

One might then ask: if the Torah means to prohibit selling, why does it write *lo yiga'el* at all? Why not simply say *lo yimacher*? The *Gemara* answers: *ei katav rachmana lo yimacher, hava amina zivuni hu d'lo mizdaban* — if it had only written "shall not be sold," one might have thought that the prohibition is limited to the act of sale proper, treating the animal as though it were mundane property. But regarding redemption — *aval mifrak mifrak* — one might naturally have assumed that redeeming *ma'aser* is permitted. After all, other *korbanot* can be redeemed, and redemption transfers the value to a sacred purpose; it does not profane the *kedushah* but rather fulfills it. *D'ayilu dmei l'hekdesh* — the money paid in redemption goes to a sanctified use. Had the Torah not explicitly prohibited redemption, one would have assumed it was permitted. Therefore, *l'hachi katav rachmana lo yiga'el* — the Torah writes "shall not be redeemed" precisely to close off that assumption, while the contextual logic of *tmura* teaches us that the word also prohibits sale. Both prohibitions — selling and redeeming — are communicated by the single phrase *lo yiga'el*, the one explicitly and the other through context.

The upshot of the entire sugya: *bechor* may be sold because the Torah's only restriction is *lo tipadeh*, prohibiting redemption without restricting sale; *ma'aser* may not be sold, because *lo yiga'el* means *lo yimacher*, but that prohibition is grounded in the context of *tmura* and applies only while the animal is alive. After *shechita*, the prohibition is rabbinic. It is that rabbinic character of the post-*shechita* restriction that allows the Rabbis to waive it for orphans, permitting them to sell outright to prevent financial loss — and it is that entire structure of biblical permission and rabbinic restriction that the *mishna* on which we opened presupposes.
