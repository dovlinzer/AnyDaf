# Bava Metzia 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heilech & Karka

### Keilim/Karka Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב מַר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: טְעָנוֹ כֵּלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת, הוֹדָה בַּכֵּלִים וְכָפַר בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת, הוֹדָה בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת וְכָפַר בַּכֵּלִים – פָּטוּר. הוֹדָה מִקְצָת קַרְקָעוֹת – פָּטוּר, מִקְצָת כֵּלִים – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Sheshet from a mishna (*Shevuot* 38b): If one **claimed** that another owed him **vessels and land,** and the defendant **admitted to** owing him **vessels but denied** that he owes him **land,** or conversely, if **he admitted to** owing him **land but denied** that he owes him **vessels,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath with regard to what he denies. If **he admitted** that he owes him **part of the land,** he is **exempt.** If he admitted to owing **some of the vessels,** he is **obligated** to take an oath with regard to the remainder.

The daf opens in the middle of an ongoing discussion of *heilech* — the teaching of Rabbi Chiya that when someone admits to part of a claim while denying the rest, that constitutes *modeh be-miktzas* and creates an obligation to take a *shevua* on the denied portion. More specifically, Rabbi Chiya holds that if *edim* testify to fifty of a claimed hundred, that functions like a *modeh be-miktzas* and obligates a *shevua* on the remainder. It is Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman who raises a challenge from a *mishnah*.

The *mishnah* under examination deals with a case where someone was claimed against for both *keilim* and *karka*. The Gemara walks through four permutations. If the defendant admitted to the *keilim* and denied the *karka*, he is exempt (*patur*) from a *shevua*. If he admitted to the *karka* and denied the *keilim*, he is likewise exempt. Even if he admitted to part of the *karka* while denying another part, he remains exempt, because *karka* — whether it appears in the admission (*hoda'ah*) or the denial (*kfirah*) — categorically exempts from *shevua*. The one case of obligation (*chayav*) is *miktzas keilim*: where the defendant denied all of the *karka* and admitted to only some of the *keilim* while denying others. Here, because both the admission and the denial relate entirely to *keilim*, one can treat the *karka* as a separate matter and apply *modeh be-miktzas* to the *keilim* alone.

### Why Karka Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר הֵילָךְ פָּטוּר, אַמַּאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי קַרְקַע מִשְּׁבוּעָה? הָא כׇּל קַרְקַע הֵילָךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to** Rav Sheshet, with regard to **the one who says that** in a case where the debtor says: **Here you are,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, **why was** it **necessary** for the **verse to exclude** a claim of **land** ownership **from** the defendant's obligation to take **an oath** when he admits part of the claim? The exclusion of land is derived from the verse: "For any matter of trespass, for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment, for any lost item about which one shall say: This is it, the claims of both of them shall come before the judges" (Exodus 22:8). **But all** cases involving one who admits to part of the claim involving **land** are cases where the defendant effectively says: **Here you are,** as land always remains in its location. Therefore, the derivation from the verse appears to be unnecessary.

The Gemara proceeds to analyze the *mishnah*'s structure. The reason one is exempt in the cases involving *karka*, says the Gemara, is stated directly: *de-karka lav bar shevua hi* — *karka* is not subject to a *shevua*, regardless of whether it appears in the admission or the denial. But this raises a pointed question. *Karka* possesses two distinct features: it belongs to a category formally excluded from *shevua*, and it always carries a *heilech* component — since *karka* cannot be hidden or moved, admitting to *karka* is always tantamount to saying "here it is, take it." So why did the *mishnah* choose to illustrate the exemption using *karka* specifically? Perhaps it implies that were you dealing only with the *heilech* aspect — say, *keilim* that functioned like *karka* because they were present and acknowledged — you would still be *chayav*.

The Gemara therefore raises the possibility: *ha keilim ve-keilim dumya de-keilim ve-karka'os chayav* — what about a case of *keilim* and *keilim*, where the *keilim* resemble *karka* because the defendant says *heilech*, "here are the five chairs I admit to, take them," while denying the other five? Would that not imply that *heilech* is *chayav*? The Gemara sees in this formulation a seeming proof in favor of Rabbi Chiya's position on *heilech*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא הֵיכָא דְּחָפַר בָּהּ בּוֹרוֹת שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rav Sheshet could have **said to you: The verse was necessary** in a case **where** the defendant **dug pits, ditches, and caves in** the land that he is returning to the plaintiff. Since the land has been damaged and is no longer in its prior state, the defendant is not effectively saying: Here you are.

The Gemara immediately deflects: *le-olam eima lach keilim ve-keilim nami patur* — one might in truth say that even *keilim ve-keilim* where the admitted *keilim* are right there in front of you would be exempt. The *mishnah* did not choose *karka* to signal that *keilim* with a *heilech* quality would be *chayav*. Rather, it chose *karka* to teach a different and important law — that even *karka*, which is categorically exempt from *shevua*, can in certain circumstances be pulled into the *shevua* obligation.

### Gilgul Shevua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, הֵיכָא דִּטְעָנוֹ כֵּלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת, וְהוֹדָה בַּכֵּלִים וְכָפַר בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there is the case **where** one **claimed** that another owed him **vessels and land, and** the defendant **admitted to** the claim involving **the vessels and denied** the claim involving **the land.** The verse teaches that although the defendant denied part of the claim and did not effectively say: Here you are, he is not obligated to take an oath with regard to the land.

The mechanism by which *karka* is drawn into the *shevua* is the principle of *gilgul shevua*, also called *zokekin*. The *mishnah* teaches that if the defendant admitted to some *keilim* while denying others — thereby becoming obligated in a *shevua* on the *keilim* — then once that *shevua* is in place, one can drag along (*zokekin*) the *karka* and require a *shevua* on that as well, notwithstanding the general exemption. This is the operative point: *ha kamashma lan hoda'ah bemiktzas keilim chayav af al ha-karka'os* — the admission of part of the *keilim* obligates him in a *shevua* that encompasses the *karka* as well.

### Shevuos Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִין צְרִיכִין כְּפִירָה בְּמִקְצָת וְהוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת. שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר, וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof for Rabbi Ḥiyya's opinion, **as Rami bar Ḥama teaches** a *baraita*: All **four** types of **bailees** mentioned in the Torah **require denial of part** of the claim **and admission of part** of the claim in order to incur liability to take an oath when someone claims to have given them an item as a deposit. This applies to **an unpaid bailee,** who receives no payment in exchange for safeguarding the item, **and a borrower,** who does not pay the owner in exchange for the right to use the item, as well as **a paid bailee,** who receives payment in exchange for safeguarding the item, **and a renter,** who rents an item in order to use it and pays the owner in exchange for that right.

But the Gemara observes that this principle of *zokekin* — that property without liens (*metaltelin*) can drag along property with liens (*karka*) into a *shevua* — was already taught in *Kiddushin*: *zokekin ha-nichasim she-ein lahem achrayus es ha-nichasim she-yesh lahem achrayus lishava aleihem*. If so, what does the *mishnah* in *Shevuos* add?

The Gemara answers: *hacha ikkar* — this is the primary place for the teaching. *Masechet Shevuos* is precisely the tractate that deals with the laws of *shevua*, defining when one is obligated and when exempt. *Keilim* — obligated; *karka* — exempt; yet once the *shevua* is triggered by the *keilim*, the *karka* is dragged along. That is the teaching that belongs here. The mention of *zokekin* in *Kiddushin*, by contrast, is incidental — *Kiddushin* is primarily concerned with modes of acquisition, and the *shevua* material appears there only as an aside (*agav grara nasba*).

### Damaged Karka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? לָאו דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵילָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances** in which the bailees both deny part of the claim and admit to part of the claim? **Is it not** referring to a case where the bailee **said to** the one who deposited the item: With regard to the items that I admit to having received from you: **Here you are,** and as to the rest, I never received them and I am therefore not obligated to return them? This supports Rabbi Ḥiyya's opinion that one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath.

The Gemara then transitions to a further question. According to the view that *heilech patur* — that handing back the admitted item exempts one from a *shevua* — why did the Torah need to establish a *pasuk*-based exclusion to exempt *karka* from *shevua*? Since *karka* always constitutes *heilech* (it sits immovably before the claimant), the *karka* exemption should follow automatically from the *heilech* exemption. Why invoke a *klal prat u'klal* derivation?

The Gemara offers two answers. First: not every admission of *karka* is in fact *heilech*. If the defendant damaged the land — digging pits, channels, or caves in it — and admits liability, he is not returning the very thing he received. There is an admission, but it is not the clean *heilech* of handing back an intact object. Hence a *pasuk* is still needed for such a case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁלֹשׁ פָּרוֹת מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ וּמֵתוּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּפְשִׁיעָה. וְאָמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ: חֲדָא – לָא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם, וַחֲדָא – מֵתָה בְּאוֹנֶס, וַחֲדָא – מֵתָה בִּפְשִׁיעָה דִּבְעֵינָא שַׁלּוֹמֵי לָךְ. דְּלָאו ״הֵילָךְ״ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** Rami bar Ḥama is referring to a different case, for example, a case **where** the depositor **said to** the unpaid bailee: **I gave you three cows and they all died due to** your **negligence.** You are therefore liable to pay for them. **And** the bailee **said to him:** With regard to **one** cow, this **matter never happened.** In other words, I received only two cows, not three. **And one** of the cows **died due to** an unavoidable **accident,** and I am exempt from paying for it. **And** the other **one died due to** my **negligence,** for **which I need to pay you.** In that case, although he admits to part of the claim, **it is not** a case of: **Here you are,** as he is not returning the cow itself.

Second — and more straightforwardly — *karka* is only *heilech* when one *admits* to the *karka*. When the defendant *denies* the *karka*, there is no *heilech*: the land sits there but is not being acknowledged or tendered. The *pasuk* is necessary to cover precisely that scenario, where *karka* is the subject of the *kfirah* rather than the *hoda'ah*, and there is still an exemption from *shevua*.

## Four Shomrim

### Shomrim Obligation

The Gemara continues to probe the *heilech* question. Rami bar Chama's *braisa* is introduced, teaching that the obligation of the four *shomrim* — *shomer chinam*, *sho'el*, *nosei sachar*, and *socher* — to make a *shevuat ha-shomrim* is conditional. Each category of *shomer* carries a defined level of liability: the unpaid watchman is exempt from everything except negligence; the borrower is liable for virtually everything save death in the course of normal use; the paid watchman and the renter are liable for theft and loss but exempt from genuine accident.

The *braisa* teaches that a *shomer* does not simply take a *shevua* when claiming exemption in the ordinary sense. Rather, the *shevua* of the *shomrim* must be embedded within a *modeh be-miktzas* structure. There must be a *kfirah be-miktzas* and a *hoda'ah be-miktzas* — a partial denial and a partial admission — surrounding the *shevuat ha-shomrim* claim.

### Three Cows Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? לָאו דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵילָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances** in which the bailees both deny part of the claim and admit to part of the claim? **Is it not** referring to a case where the bailee **said to** the one who deposited the item: With regard to the items that I admit to having received from you: **Here you are,** and as to the rest, I never received them and I am therefore not obligated to return them? This supports Rabbi Ḥiyya's opinion that one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath.

The Gemara poses the scenario: *heichi dami* — what is the actual case? The initial reading is that the bailor entrusted three cows and the *shomer* admits to one and denies one — a straightforward *modeh be-miktzas* — while the *shevuat ha-shomrim* concerning what happened to the third cow is wrapped within that structure. The Gemara asks whether this might be a case of *heilech*: since these are physical animals being returned, would not the admitted cow constitute *heilech*, and yet the *shevua* still applies?

### Pikadon vs Loan

This distinction is highlighted: a *pikadon*, a deposit of an actual physical object, is by its nature *heilech* when the *shomer* acknowledges it, because the object is right there. A loan, by contrast, is typically not *heilech*, since money can be spent and is not presumed present. Yet even in this scenario, the Gemara holds the *shevua* to be valid.

### Dead Cows Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁלֹשׁ פָּרוֹת מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ וּמֵתוּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּפְשִׁיעָה. וְאָמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ: חֲדָא – לָא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם, וַחֲדָא – מֵתָה בְּאוֹנֶס, וַחֲדָא – מֵתָה בִּפְשִׁיעָה דִּבְעֵינָא שַׁלּוֹמֵי לָךְ. דְּלָאו ״הֵילָךְ״ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** Rami bar Ḥama is referring to a different case, for example, a case **where** the depositor **said to** the unpaid bailee: **I gave you three cows and they all died due to** your **negligence.** You are therefore liable to pay for them. **And** the bailee **said to him:** With regard to **one** cow, this **matter never happened.** In other words, I received only two cows, not three. **And one** of the cows **died due to** an unavoidable **accident,** and I am exempt from paying for it. **And** the other **one died due to** my **negligence,** for **which I need to pay you.** In that case, although he admits to part of the claim, **it is not** a case of: **Here you are,** as he is not returning the cow itself.

The Gemara immediately deflects this reading. *Lo* — this is not a *heilech* case. The actual scenario is: the bailor says, "I gave you three cows and all three died due to your negligence — pay me." The *shomer* responds: one cow you fabricated, I never received three, only two (*lo hayu devarim me'olam*); of the remaining two, one died by genuine accident (*ones*), and for that I claim exemption via *shevuat ha-shomrim*; and one died by my negligence (*peshia*), for which I concede I must pay. This is a *modeh be-miktzas* — he admits liability for one, denies one altogether — but not a *heilech*: he is not handing back a live cow, he is admitting that a cow died due to his negligence and that he owes money. *Lav heilech hu* — it is not *heilech*.

## Rabbi Chiya II

### Edim Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי לִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא קַמַּיְיתָא: מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי כְּלוּם, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז, יָכוֹל יִשָּׁבַע עַל הַשְּׁאָר –
>
> **Translation:** § **Come** and **hear** a challenge **to the first** *halakha* **of Rabbi Ḥiyya, as the father of Rabbi Aptoriki taught** in a *baraita*: If one says to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says: Nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession, and the witnesses testify that he has fifty dinars in his possession** that he owes the plaintiff, one **might** have thought that **he should take an oath about the remainder.**

The Gemara now turns to Rabbi Chiya's first teaching — distinct from the *heilech* principle — which concerns the role of *edim* in creating an obligation equivalent to *modeh be-miktzas*. The *braisa* of Aviha de-Rabbi Aptoriki relates directly to this. The case: Reuven says to Shimon, "You owe me a hundred," and Shimon replies, "I owe you nothing." Witnesses then testify that Shimon is in fact obligated in fifty. The *braisa* raises the question of whether this should obligate Shimon in a *shevua* on the remaining fifty, treating the witnesses' confirmation of fifty as the functional equivalent of a *modeh be-miktzas*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל כׇּל אֲבֵידָה אֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר כִּי הוּא זֶה״. עַל הוֹדָאַת פִּיו אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל הַעֲדָאַת עֵדִים!
>
> **Translation:** To counter this, **the verse states** with regard to bailees: "For any matter of trespass, for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment, **for any lost item about which one shall say: This is it,** the claims of both of them shall come before the judges" (Exodus 22:8). This indicates that **you obligate** the defendant to take an oath based **on the admission** to part of a claim by **his mouth, but you do not obligate him** to take an oath based **on the testimony of witnesses.**

The *braisa* answers in the negative, citing the *pasuk*: *al kol aveda asher yomar ki hu zeh* — "regarding any lost object that he shall say, 'this is the one.'" From *asher yomar* — "that he shall say" — the *braisa* derives: *al hoda'as piv atah mechayvo ve'ee atah mechayvo al hoda'as edim* — you obligate him based on his own admission, not based on the testimony of witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתָא קָא רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא?! רַבִּי חִיָּיא תַּנָּא הוּא וּפָלֵיג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **Are you raising an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya** from **a *baraita*? Rabbi Ḥiyya** himself **is a *tanna*, and** as such, he has the authority to **dispute** the determination in a *baraita*.

This appears to be a direct contradiction of Rabbi Chiya, who holds that *edim* do create a *modeh be-miktzas* obligation. The Gemara dismisses the contradiction with a foundational point: *Rabbi Chiya tanna hu u'palig* — Rabbi Chiya was himself a *tanna*, among the last of that generation, and possesses the authority to dispute a *braisa*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קְרָא קָאָמַר! הָהוּא לְמוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** that *tanna* **cite a verse?** The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Ḥiyya, **that** verse teaches that **one who admits to part of the claim** is obligated to take an oath.

But the Gemara presses further: the *braisa* does not merely assert a ruling — it derives it from a *pasuk*. Surely a *pasuk* cannot simply be overridden? The Gemara responds: the *pasuk* of *ki hu zeh* establishes the obligation of *modeh be-miktzas* — it does not necessarily exclude *edim*. Rabbi Chiya reads the *pasuk* as generating the *modeh be-miktzas* obligation and then constructs a *kal va-chomer*: if when you yourself admit to part, you are obligated in a *shevua* on the remainder, then certainly when witnesses — who have no personal stake in the matter and whose testimony carries great weight — oblige you in part, you should be obligated in a *shevua* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי אָמַר לָךְ, כְּתִיב ״הוּא״ וּכְתִיב ״זֶה״, חַד לְמוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה, וְחַד לְהַעֲדָאַת עֵדִים דְּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Aptoriki's father** could have **said to you** that both *halakhot* are derived from the expression "This is it." Since **"this" is written and "it" is** also **written,** it is interpreted that **one** word is stated **to** teach that **one who admits to part of the claim** is obligated to take an oath, **and one** word is stated **to** teach **that** in a case that involves the **testimony of witnesses,** one is **exempt** from taking an oath.

Aviha de-Rabbi Aptoriki, by contrast, reads both words *hu* and *zeh* in the *pasuk ki hu zeh* as limiting terms. One limits the application to a genuine *modeh be-miktzas* — partial admission of the very claim being made. The other limits it to cases where the defendant himself speaks (*asher yomar*), excluding cases where *edim* do the obligating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ: חַד לְמוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה, וְחַד לְמוֹדֶה מִמִּין הַטַּעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** Sage, Rabbi Ḥiyya, interpret the double qualification in the verse? The Gemara answers: In his opinion, **one** word is stated **to** teach that **one who admits to part of the claim** is obligated to take an oath, **and one** word is stated **to** teach that one is obligated to take an oath only if he **admits** that he owes an item that is **of** the same **type** as the subject of **the claim.** If the plaintiff claims one type of item and the defendant admits to owing a different type of item, he is not obligated to take an oath.

Rabbi Chiya reads those two limiting terms differently. He agrees that one is needed to establish the basic principle of *modeh be-miktzas*. But he uses the second limiting word to address a different case: *modeh she-lo minin ha-tana'ah* — an admission that does not correspond to the substance of the claim. The case is illustrated by the *mishnah*: if Reuven claimed that Shimon owes him a measure of wheat, and Shimon admits to owing a measure of barley — which is worth less — is that a *modeh be-miktzas*? The admission is of something less valuable, but it is not the same item being claimed. Rabbi Chiya says this is not a valid *modeh be-miktzas*, using the word *zeh* in the *pasuk* as the exclusion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ מוֹדֶה מִמִּין הַטַּעֲנָה לֵית לֵיהּ, וְסָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דִּתְנַן: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין, פָּטוּר. וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Aptoriki's father, **does not accept** the principle that one is required to take an oath only if he **admits** that he owes an item that is **of** the same **type as** the subject of **the claim, but he holds like Rabban Gamliel** in this matter. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 38b): If one **claimed** that another owes **him wheat, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him barley,** which is less expensive than wheat, he is **exempt** from taking an oath despite his admission to part of the claim, as his admission does not correspond to the claim. **And Rabban Gamliel deems** him **liable** to take an oath.

Rabban Gamliel disagrees, ruling that such a case is *chayav*: the claimant demanded something worth a hundred and the defendant admitted to something worth fifty — that is partial admission and partial denial, regardless of whether the item itself matches. Aviha de-Rabbi Aptoriki, who uses *zeh* to exclude *edim*, would presumably hold that the *chitim/se'orim* case does not require its own exclusion. Rabbi Chiya, who needs *zeh* for *chitim/se'orim*, derives the *edim* exclusion via *kal va-chomer* — and then, following Rabban Gamliel, rejects the *kal va-chomer* and retains the *edim* obligation.

## Shepherd Case

### Theft Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא רָעֲיָא דְּהָווּ מָסְרִי לֵיהּ כׇּל יוֹמָא חֵיוָתָא בְּסָהֲדֵי. יוֹמָא חַד מְסַרוּ לֵיהּ בְּלָא סָהֲדֵי. לְסוֹף אָמַר לְהוּ: לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם. אֲתוֹ סָהֲדֵי אַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ דַּאֲכַל תַּרְתֵּי מִינַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִם אִיתָא לִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא קַמַּיְיתָא, מִשְׁתְּבַע אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain shepherd to whom** people **would give** their **animals** for safekeeping **every day in** the presence **of witnesses. One day, they gave him** their animals **without witnesses. At the end** of the day **he said to** the owners of the animals: This **matter never occurred;** I never received the animals. **Witnesses came** and **testified against him that he ate two of them. Rabbi Zeira said: If Rabbi Ḥiyya's first** *halakha* **is so,** the shepherd must **take an oath with regard to the remainder,** or else he must pay the value of the animals to their owners.

The Gemara now presents an actual case that brings together Rabbi Chiya's teachings. There was a shepherd whom the flock's owners did not trust. Every day, when entrusting him with the animals, they arranged for witnesses to observe the handoff — noting exactly how many sheep he received, much as one marks scratches on a rental car. Tosafos adds that the witnesses were also necessary to confirm that the *pikadon* had in fact been received, lest the shepherd later claim he had already returned the animals.

One day, *chad yoma*, the owners could not find witnesses and handed over the sheep without them. At the end of that day, the shepherd denied everything: *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — you never gave me any sheep. Witnesses then came forward and testified that they had seen him consuming two of the sheep. The owners claimed ten sheep; the shepherd denied everything; witnesses established his liability for two.

### Shevua Question

The question posed by Rabbi Zeira is: *im isa le-Rabbi Chiya kamaisa* — if we rule in accordance with Rabbi Chiya's first teaching, that *edim* obligating a defendant in part creates the same structure as *modeh be-miktzas*, then the shepherd should be obligated in a *shevua* on the remaining eight sheep. *Mishtava a-shara* — let him swear regarding the others.

### Gazel Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם אִיתָא מִשְׁתְּבַע?! וְהָא גַּזְלָן הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If** Rabbi Ḥiyya's first *halakha* **is so,** the shepherd **takes an oath? Isn't he a robber?** The witnesses established through their testimony that he took and ate some of the animals, and consequently his oath lacks credibility. Rabbi Zeira **said to him:** I did not mean that the shepherd takes an oath; **I was saying** that the party **opposing him** takes an oath and collects payment.

Abaye objects immediately. *Gazlan hu* — the witnesses have proven that he stole. A *gazlan* whose theft is established by testimony is *pasul* to take a *shevua* — his oath cannot be relied upon. So the shepherd is not in a position to swear at all.

### Shekenegdo Nishba

Rabbi Zeira clarifies: *shekenegdo kamina* — that is precisely the point. The shepherd is indeed obligated in a *shevua* as a result of the *modeh be-miktzas* structure created by the witnesses' testimony. But since he cannot take that *shevua* — because he is a proven *gazlan* — the principle of *shekenegdo nishba ve-notel* applies: the opposing party, the owners, take the *shevua* in his stead and collect. The owners swear that there were indeed ten sheep and collect accordingly.

### Takanta vs Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי דְּלֵיתָא לִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, נְחַיְּיבֵיהּ מִדְּרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Now, too,** if it is so **that** the *halakha* **is not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** and testimony supporting part of the claim does not obligate the defendant to take an oath with regard to the rest, the court **should** still **obligate** the shepherd to take an oath **due to** the ordinance **of Rav Naḥman,** and since his oath is not deemed credible the plaintiff should take an oath and collect payment.

The Gemara raises a further point. Even without Rabbi Chiya's ruling, one might argue that the shepherd is obligated in a *shevua* by virtue of Rav Nachman's principle that even a *kofer ha-kol* — one who denies everything — is required to take a *shevuat heiset*, a rabbinic oath. If so, even absent a Torah-level *modeh be-miktzas* obligation, the shepherd would be required to swear, he could not, and the owners could swear and collect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי – פָּטוּר. וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעַת הֶיסֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 38b): If one says to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession,** and that person replies: **Nothing of yours is in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath. **And Rav Naḥman says:** Nevertheless, the judges **administer an oath of inducement to him.** Rav Naḥman instituted an ordinance that even if the defendant completely denies the claim, he is obligated to take an oath that the claim is false. Consequently, the shepherd is obligated to take that oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַב נַחְמָן תַּקַּנְתָּא הִיא, וְתַקַּנְתָּא לְתַקַּנְתָּא לָא עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This *halakha* **of Rav Naḥman is** a rabbinic **ordinance** and not an oath required by Torah law, **and we do not institute** one rabbinic **ordinance upon** another rabbinic **ordinance.** Therefore no oath is administered.

The Gemara rejects this: *shevuat de-Rav Nachman takanta hi* — the *shevuat heiset* is purely a rabbinic enactment, not a Torah-level obligation. The underlying logic of Rav Nachman's enactment is to pressure the defendant into not making a false denial, by ensuring that at minimum he must back up his denial with an oath. But the principle of *shekenegdo nishba ve-notel* — the opposing party swearing and collecting — applies only where the original obligation to swear is rooted in Torah law. *Ve-takanta le-takanta lo avdinan* — we do not layer one rabbinic enactment upon another. It would be one thing to require the *kofer ha-kol* to take a rabbinic oath; it is another thing entirely to say that because he cannot take that rabbinic oath, his opponent now swears and collects. That second step goes beyond what the rabbinic enactment was designed to accomplish.

The broader principle here is important: a Torah-level *shevua* serves as a genuine mechanism of exemption — the *shomer* or defendant needs the oath to free himself from liability, and if he cannot make it, the logic of shifting the oath to the other side follows naturally. A rabbinic oath, by contrast, is a deterrent tool, and its non-performance does not automatically empower the claimant in the same way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ רוֹעֶה! וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סְתָם רוֹעֶה פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The reason cited for the lack of credibility of the oath of the shepherd is that he is guilty of robbery. The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let** Rabbi Zeira **derive** that he is disqualified from testifying or taking an oath **because he is a shepherd; and Rav Yehuda says** that **an ordinary shepherd** is **disqualified** from testifying? A shepherd is presumed to be a robber since shepherds allow the animals under their care to graze in the fields of other people.

A further question arises. Why was the shepherd's *shevua* disqualification grounded in the witnessed proof of theft rather than simply in his status as a *ro'eh*? Rav Yehuda teaches: *stam ro'eh pasul* — a generic shepherd, absent information to the contrary, is presumed disqualified, because shepherds routinely graze their flocks on others' land and are thereby presumed to be *gazlanim*. Should not that blanket presumption have sufficed to disqualify him from the outset?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דְּעָלְמָא. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, אֲנַן חֵיוָתָא לְרוֹעָה הֵיכִי מָסְרִינַן? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״לִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״! אֶלָּא חֲזָקָה: אֵין אָדָם חוֹטֵא וְלֹא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: This is **not difficult. That** case, where he is presumed a robber, is a case where he herds **his** own animals, and **this** case, where he is not presumed a robber, is a case where he herds animals that **belong to others. As if you do not say so,** if even one who herds the animals of others is presumably a robber, **how do we give** our **animals to a shepherd? Isn't it written: "Do not put a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14)? It is prohibited to cause others to commit a transgression. **Rather, there is a presumption** that **a person sins only for his own** benefit, and one would not commit robbery for the benefit of animals that are not his.

The Gemara resolves the tension: *ha d'didi, ha d'alma* — the presumption of *stam ro'eh pasul* applies when the shepherd grazes his own sheep on others' land, since he directly benefits from the theft. When he is watching someone else's sheep, however, the assumption is reversed: *chazaka ein adam chotei ve-lo lo* — people do not sin when they themselves derive no benefit. A shepherd watching another person's flock will not graze those animals on land that is not his, since he gains nothing from it. Were we to assume otherwise, it would be prohibited to ever entrust one's animals to a *ro'eh*, in violation of *lifnei iver lo titein michshol*. The fact that we routinely do so demonstrates that the working presumption is that a *ro'eh* watching others' flocks is not a *gazlan*.

Accordingly, in the case at hand, the shepherd's disqualification from taking a *shevua* was not automatic from his status as a *ro'eh* watching others' sheep. It required the positive evidence of witnesses testifying that he had consumed two of the flock. Once that proof was established, he became a proven *gazlan*, and Rabbi Chiya's rule — that witnesses obligating in part create a *modeh be-miktzas* structure — meant he was obligated in a *shevua* on the remainder, which, because he could not take it, shifted to the owners.

## Tallit Shevua

### No Less Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ [וְכוּ׳]. עַל דְּאִית לֵיהּ מִשְׁתְּבַע, אוֹ עַל דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מִשְׁתְּבַע! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר: שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בָּהּ וְאֵין לִי בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **This** one **takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than half of it,** and that one takes an oath that he does not have ownership of less than half of it, and they divide it. The Gemara asks: **Does he take an oath with regard to** the part **that he has** in his grasp, taking an oath that it is his, **or does he take an oath with regard to** the part **that he does not have,** i.e., that he does not have a claim to less than half of it? The latter wording of the oath is problematic, as he may mean that he does not have a claim to the garment at all. **Rav Huna said:** He must take an oath in **which he says:** I hereby take **an oath that I have** a claim to **it, and** I hereby take an oath that **I do not have** a claim to **less than half of it.**

The Gemara now returns to the *mishnah* at the opening of the tractate. Two people come before the court each holding a *tallis*, each claiming it is entirely theirs. The *mishnah* rules: divide it, and each one takes a *shevua* with the formulation *she-ein lo vah pachos mi-chetzi* — that he has no less than half.

The Gemara probes the precise meaning of this oath. *Al d'is lei mishtava, o al d'leis lei mishtava?* — is he swearing regarding what he has or what he does not have? This is a subtle but important point. The formulation *ein li vah pachos mi-chetzi* — "what I have in this *tallis* is no less than half" — could technically be true even if he has nothing at all: if one has nothing, then what one has is certainly not less than half, because there is nothing to quantify in that direction. A person who owns zero of the *tallis* could truthfully say "what I have is no less than half" in the same way one might say an empty set has no members below any threshold.

To foreclose this reading, Rav Huna rules that the *shevua* must be formulated as: *shevua she-yeish li vah* — "I swear that I do have an ownership share in this *tallis*" — *ve-ein li vah pachos mi-chetzi* — "and what I have is no less than half." The oath thus consists of two components: an affirmative declaration of ownership, and a quantitative declaration about its scope.

### Beit Din Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי. וּמִי יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ כּוּלַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let him say:** I hereby take **an oath that all of it is mine,** as that is his claim. Why does he take an oath that merely half of it belongs to him? The Gemara answers: **And would we give him all of it** if he took such an oath? Since he will not be awarded the entire garment, it would be inappropriate for the court to administer to him an oath that he owns all of it.

The Gemara then asks a simpler question: why not just have each person swear to owning all of it, consistent with his actual claim? The answer: *umi yahavinan lei kullah* — the *beit din* is not going to give him all. If the claimant swears he owns the entire *tallis* and the court then awards him only half, the court's own credibility is undermined. It would appear that the court believed his oath — since it made him swear — and then acted contrary to it. The formulation of the *shevua* must be compatible with what the court will actually award.

### Own Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁחֶצְיָהּ שֶׁלִּי – מַרַע לֵיהּ לְדִיבּוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let him say:** I hereby take **an oath that half of it is mine.** Why is the complicated formulation suggested by Rav Huna necessary? The Gemara answers: If he takes an oath to that effect **he compromises his** initial **statement,** i.e., his claim that the entire garment is his.

The alternative — having each person simply swear to owning exactly half — is also problematic: *marea lei lediburei* — it forces him to contradict his own claim. He is claiming the entire *tallis*; having him swear that he owns exactly half compels him to take an oath directly at odds with what he genuinely believes and asserts.

### Limited Shevua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי מַרַע לֵיהּ לְדִיבּוּרֵיהּ. דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי, וּלְדִבְרֵיכֶם – שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בָּהּ וְאֵין לִי בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Now too,** when he takes an oath according to Rav Huna's formulation, **he compromises his** initial **statement,** as he takes an oath only with regard to his claim to half the garment. The Gemara answers: This is not so, **as he makes** the following **statement** to the court: **All of it is mine; but according to your statement,** I hereby take **an oath that I have** a claim to **it and I do not have** a claim to **less than half of it.**

The resolution is elegant. The claimant declares: *kullah sheli* — I maintain that this *tallis* is entirely mine. *U-le-divreichem* — but since you, the court, do not accept that and will not award me the full *tallis*, I am prepared to take a *shevua* that I have no less than half. This is not a contradiction of his claim: "no less than half" is fully consistent with owning the whole. He does not concede that he has only half; he swears to a floor, not a ceiling. He refuses, however, to swear that he has exactly half, because that would be an oath that genuinely contradicts what he believes and asserts. The formulation of *she-ein lo vah pachos mi-chetzi* thus threads the needle: it is compatible with the court's award, it does not undermine the claimant's own position, and — per Rav Huna's addition — it affirmatively asserts ownership, eliminating the loophole of swearing truthfully while owning nothing.
