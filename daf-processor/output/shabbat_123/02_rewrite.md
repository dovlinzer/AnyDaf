# Shabbat 123 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kli Shemelachto L'Issur

### Hammer & Nuts

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion about *kli shemelachto l'issur* — a vessel whose primary function involves a prohibited activity — and the question of whether such an object may be used on Shabbat for a permitted purpose. The Mishnah had already illustrated the principle with concrete examples: using a hammer to crack nuts, or a saw to cut cheese. These examples raise the conceptual question of what exactly is meant by *l'tzorech gufo* — using the object itself for some legitimate purpose.

*L'tzorech gufo* must be understood against the backdrop of two other possible modes of handling such an object. The second is *l'tzorech mekomo* — one does not wish to use the object at all, but simply needs to move it out of the way to clear a space. The third is *mechama l'tzel* — moving the object from sun to shade in order to protect it from damage. These three categories structure the entire analysis of *kli shemelachto l'issur* that unfolds across the daf.

### Mishna vs Rav Yehuda

The Mishnah's ruling appeared quite clear: even vessels designated for prohibited activities may be handled on Shabbat for a permitted purpose. Yet Rav Yehuda sought to read the Mishnah in a far more limited sense, arguing that it addressed only a vessel designated for a *heter* use — which would seem to undermine the Mishnah's entire point.

### Rava's Position

Rava pushed back against this restrictive reading. In his view, the Mishnah's plain intent is that even a vessel whose *melacha* is forbidden may be used on Shabbat for a permitted purpose. This, he argued, is precisely what the examples of the hammer and the saw are meant to illustrate. With Rava's position established, the Gemara turns to an *eitivei* — a challenge from a *braita* — that opens five lines into the daf.

## Pestle/Cutting Board

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

The *eitivei* cites a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerning the *ali* — the pestle, which in its broad, flat form could serve as an improvised cutting board. Beit Shammai rules: *ein notlin et ha-ali liktzava alav basar* — one may not take the pestle to use it as a surface for cutting meat on Yom Tov. Beit Hillel permits this, reasoning that it serves *simchat Yom Tov*.

### Post-Use Restriction

Crucially, however, both schools agree: *veshavin she-im katzav alav basar she-assur letaltelo* — if one has already cut meat on it, it may not subsequently be moved. This presents a challenge to Rava's position. If the pestle is a *kli shemelachto l'issur*, why should it become forbidden after its permitted use? At worst, it should remain in the category of *kli shemelachto l'issur* — a hammer used for cracking nuts is still moveable after one finishes cracking. Why should the pestle be different?

The Gemara initially considers answering according to Rabbi Nechemia. Rabbi Nechemia represents an extremely restrictive position: any vessel, even one *shemelachto l'heter*, may be used only for its intended purpose — *l'tzorech tashmisho*. A fork may be used to eat, but not to scratch oneself, even though scratching is entirely permitted. Whatever one wishes to use on Shabbat may only be used for its classic, designated function. According to Rabbi Nechemia, then, the pestle's special *heter* to serve as a cutting board — granted only for *simchat Yom Tov* — expires once that use is concluded.

### Rabbi Nechemia

This answer holds that both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel may be operating within the framework of Rabbi Nechemia — a striking conclusion in its own right. The Gemara, however, moves past this resolution upon hearing a report in the name of Rav.

## Professional Tools

### Fragrance/Gold Tools

Rabbi Chinana bar Shlamya said in the name of Rav: *ha-kol modim be-sichi zavi u-mezorei* — everyone agrees with respect to these three implements. Rashi offers two readings of what these implements are: instruments used in the production of fragrances, or instruments used in weaving. Whatever their precise identity, they are professional tools of a specialized character, and all authorities agree that they occupy a stricter category than ordinary *kli shemelachto l'issur*.

The reason is *d'keivan d'kapid aleihu* — because the owner is exacting about them. These are tools one guards carefully, keeps designated for their professional function, and would not want anyone handling casually. Consequently, *miyachid lehu makom* — a place is set aside exclusively for them, and they become effectively off-limits. This category is what is generally described as *muktzah machmat chisron kis* — *muktzah* born of concern over financial loss, though the concern need not be specifically monetary. The operative condition is that the owner regards the implement as untouchable for any other purpose. This is what explains why the pestle, as a professional grinding instrument, cannot be moved after its exceptional Yom Tov use: it falls closer to this category of professional tools that one does not wish handled indiscriminately.

This prompts an important conceptual question: does *muktzah machmat chisron kis* apply only vis-à-vis others, or can it apply even for the owner himself? One might imagine a case where the owner is personally comfortable handling the implement but insists that others not touch it. Whether this asymmetry is legally meaningful is indeed a subject of discussion in the *rishonim*, touching on the question of *muktzah l'aniyim* and *muktzah l'ashirim*.

### Tool Gradation

The Gemara then records a *machloket* regarding how broadly this category of untouchable professional tools extends. Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kurnas shel zehavim shanu* — the Mishnah's ruling that a hammer may be used to crack nuts applies not only to an ordinary blacksmith's hammer, which is large and resilient and no one worries about damaging, but even to a goldsmith's hammer — a delicate instrument used to thin out gold. One might have assumed that the precision required of such a tool would render its owner sufficiently exacting about it to prohibit casual use. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is thus a significant *chiddush*: even that degree of professional delicacy does not push the implement into the forbidden category.

Rav Shemen bar Abba goes further still: *kurnas shel besamim shanu* — the Mishnah's ruling applies even to a hammer used in producing fragrances. Such an implement is doubly precious: it is a professional instrument, and it must remain pristine and free of foreign odors. Even so, one may use it to crack nuts. The Gemara then notes the logical relationship between these two rulings. Whoever holds that the fragrance hammer is permitted *a fortiori* permits the gold hammer, since the fragrance hammer is the more carefully guarded of the two. Conversely, whoever holds that the gold hammer is permitted need not necessarily extend the ruling to the fragrance hammer, since the owner's *kapeidah* regarding the fragrance hammer may be greater.

What emerges is that the category of implements so precious that one refuses to let anyone touch them is an extreme case — far more extreme than one might initially have assumed. The realm of *kli shemelachto l'issur* turns out to be quite broad, reaching even into instruments that seem obviously protected by professional concern.

This is directly connected to a case in the Mishnah that speaks of using a heavy needle to pick a lock, illustrating that instruments are evaluated by their actual function and use-context rather than by reflex categorization.

### Spindles & Pokers

The Gemara adds that spindles and certain pointed weaving tools — *hakush* and *hakarkar* — may also be used on Shabbat, in this case to pierce fruit in a manner resembling a toothpick, connecting back to the principle that instruments with identifiable permitted uses remain in the category of *kli* that may be handled.

## Tilul Min Hatzad

### Fig in Straw

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* that shifts the discussion toward the mechanics of indirect handling of *muktzah*. The case: an unripe fig — *paggah*, which Rashi identifies as specifically a fig — was buried in straw in order to hasten its ripening, much as one today might place fruit in a paper bag. The fig itself is not *muktzah*. But the straw surrounding it may be. Straw designated for animal fodder is not *muktzah*, since feeding one's animals on Shabbat is permitted. Straw designated for use in plaster-making, however, is *muktzah*, since that is a prohibited activity. The *baraita* rules: *muttar letaltela* — the fig may be moved, despite the surrounding *muktzah*.

### Wafer in Coals

A parallel case: a wafer buried in coals to keep it warm. Here the rule turns on exposure. If *im megula miktzatah* — part of the wafer is exposed — *muttar letaltela*: one may remove it, because one grasps the exposed, permitted portion and extracts it. The coals surrounding it collapse incidentally as it is removed, but one is not directly moving the *muktzah*. If, however, the wafer is completely buried, *assur letaltela*: one may not move it, because reaching it would require directly moving the *muktzah* coals out of the way.

### Spearing Method

Rabbi Elazar ben Tadai offers an elegant solution: *tochsim bekush bekarkar* — one may take a spindle or pointer and spear the buried item, whether fig or other object, and extract it. *Vehen minaroat me'alehen* — the surrounding material falls away of its own accord. The point of the spindle naturally displaces the *muktzah* material as it is inserted, but this displacement is incidental to the act of reaching the permitted object. This is the paradigmatic case of *tiltul min hatzad* — indirect movement of *muktzah* — and it is permitted when performed *l'tzorech davar hamuttar*, in the course of accessing a non-*muktzah* item.

The principle can be illustrated in familiar terms: if a permitted object sits behind books on a shelf, pulling out the books will naturally push aside whatever else is there — and this is acceptable, since one is acting on the permitted items and the incidental displacement of other objects is secondary. Alternatively, one could use a body part — an elbow, a breath of air — to move the *muktzah* directly. This mode, *tiltul begufo*, is permitted even more broadly, including in cases where one simply needs the item moved.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

The Gemara records: *amar Rav Nachman halacha k'Rabbi Elazar ben Tadai* — the ruling follows Rabbi Elazar ben Tadai, establishing that *tiltul min hatzad* is indeed permitted in such cases. The operative principle, as the Gemara formulates it, is *tiltul min hatzad lo shemei tiltul* — indirect movement is not legally considered movement.

### Radish Orientation

The Gemara then introduces a complication via a ruling Rav Nachman himself stated elsewhere. A radish buried in the ground: if oriented *mil'mala l'mata* — wider toward the top and narrowing downward — it may be extracted without difficulty, as it simply slides out of the hole and the surrounding earth collapses naturally inward. But if oriented *mil'mata l'mala* — the narrow end upward and the wider portion below — extraction is forbidden, because pulling it upward would require physically displacing the earth around the wider lower portion. In that case, one is not merely permitting the earth to collapse incidentally; one is actively moving it to create space.

This ruling initially appears to contradict Rav Nachman's endorsement of Rabbi Elazar ben Tadai's method. The Gemara resolves the tension by noting that *hadar beih Rav Nachman* — Rav Nachman retracted. The final established principle is that *tiltul min hatzad* is permitted when one is in pursuit of a *davar heter*, with the incidental displacement of *muktzah* material being entirely secondary.

## Broken Needle

### Needle Query

The Mishnah states that a standard sewing needle may be used on Shabbat to remove a splinter. Rava the son of Rabba posed a question to Rav Yosef by way of a written inquiry: *machat shenitlah chararah o uktzah* — what is the law regarding a needle that is missing its eye, or missing its point? Is such an object *muktzah*?

Rav Yosef responded from the Mishnah itself: since a needle may be used to remove a splinter, and a splinter does not care whether the needle has an eye or not, the absence of the eye presents no obstacle. The needle without an eye can still perform this function perfectly well. Regarding the missing point, the matter is less obvious — a blunted needle may be less effective. But the missing eye, at minimum, does not compromise the needle's utility for splinter removal.

This ruling carries a significant implication. One might have argued that the determination of whether an object qualifies as a *kli* — and thus falls outside the basic *muktzah* prohibition — should be made at the onset of Shabbat based on whether the object is conventionally classified as a vessel. A broken needle might be thought to have fallen out of the *kli* category entirely, rendering it like discarded refuse — genuine *muktzah*. Rav Yosef's answer is that as long as there is some functional use to which it may be put, it retains its status as a vessel for Shabbat purposes.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye raises a challenge from the *Mishnah* in *Keilim*: *machat shenital chararah o uktzah — tehorah* — a needle missing its eye or its point is ritually pure. For the laws of *tumah v'taharah*, it has ceased to be a *kli* and, if it had previously contracted impurity, that impurity departs. The implication is that the laws of vessels should be uniform: what is not a vessel for *tumah* should not be a vessel for Shabbat either.

### Tumah vs Shabbat

Abaye himself answers the challenge he has raised — defending Rav Yosef's position. *Tumah ashabbat karmit?* — one may not simply import the definition of vessel from the laws of *tumah* into the laws of Shabbat. For *tumah*, the Torah requires *kli ma'aseh* — a vessel that is, in Rashi's formulation, still in the state as it was made, intact and complete. For Shabbat, the operative criterion is *midi d'chazi* — functionality. As long as the object is usable for something, it retains its vessel status and is not *muktzah*. The two bodies of law employ different definitional frameworks for what constitutes a vessel.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava complicates the picture further. A *tosefta* in *Keilim* directly addresses the Shabbat question and rules: *bein nekuvah bein she'einah nekuvah, muttar l'taltela b'shabbat* — whether the needle has an eye or not, it may be moved on Shabbat. And the requirement of an eye was stated *ela l'inyan tumah bilvad* — exclusively for the laws of *tumah*. This appears to be a direct vindication of Rav Yosef and Abaye.

Abaye, however, interprets this *tosefta* in a manner consistent with Rava's position. *B'golmei askinan* — the *tosefta* speaks of an unfinished needle, one that has not yet had its eye bored. *Zimnin d'mimlich aleihu u'mashvei lehu mana* — the owner may yet decide to complete it and make it into a full vessel. Since it is not broken but merely unfinished, it retains sufficient vessel status for Shabbat purposes even though it lacks the completeness required for *tumah*. Here the gap between the two definitional systems is relatively modest: an unfinished vessel may be functional enough for Shabbat, though not technically complete enough for *tumah*.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava's view, however, is that a genuinely broken needle — one whose eye or point has been removed — is different in kind. *Adam zarkah l'vein gruta'ot* — a person would throw such a needle into his scrap-metal pile. It is not merely incomplete; it has become refuse. In Rava's view, that object has lost its vessel status for Shabbat as well. If it is not a *kli* for *tumah*, it is not a *kli* for *muktzah* purposes either.

### Functional Status

The upshot of this discussion is that all opinions agree the definition of *kli* for *muktzah* is not identical to the definition for *tumah v'taharah*, but they disagree about the degree of divergence. For Rava, the gap is limited: an unfinished vessel may qualify for Shabbat even if not for *tumah*, but a broken vessel falls out of the Shabbat category along with the *tumah* category. For Abaye and Rav Yosef, the gap is wider: even a broken needle that has lost its eye retains functional vessel status for Shabbat purposes, so long as it can still be put to some use.

This discussion crystallizes an important conceptual point about *muktzah*. The category of *kli shemelachto l'issur* is fundamentally distinct from classic *muktzah*. Classic *muktzah* — whether *machmat mi'us*, *machmat chisron kis*, or simple objects like sticks and stones that were never part of one's mental universe of usable things — means the object is entirely off-limits, completely hands-off. The category of *keilim* is radically different: these are normal household objects that one uses regularly. At most, they carry certain restrictions on the manner in which they may be moved. It is therefore essential to establish whether an object qualifies as a *kli* — because doing so moves it out of the realm of genuine *muktzah* entirely and into the far more permissive world of *keilim*, where only specific types of movement are restricted.

## Infant Care

### Limb Straightening

The Gemara introduces what appears to be a tangential matter: *asuvei yanuka* — the question of whether one may manipulate the limbs of an infant on Shabbat, some form of physical adjustment of its limbs, as Rashi and Tosafot both explain. Rav Nachman forbids this; Rav Sheshet permits it.

### Purgatives

Rav Nachman grounds his position in the Mishnah that follows shortly: *ein osin apiktoizin b'Shabbos* — one may not take a purgative on Shabbat. Rashi notes that this is not strictly a *refu'ah* question in the usual sense — taking a purgative to eat more is not healing an illness — but it nonetheless falls under the rabbinic category of *metakein*, of "fixing" or adjusting the body in a way that resembles prohibited weekday activity. Tosafot, it should be noted, actually locates Rav Nachman's proof in the end of that Mishnah, which explicitly addresses the straightening of an infant's limbs.

### Splinter Analogy

Rav Sheshet responds: *hasam lav orcha hacha orcha* — taking a purgative to eat more is not a normal mode of bodily care; it is unusual behavior. Straightening an infant's limbs, by contrast, is ordinary care — comparable to feeding and hydrating the child. And the proof that normal bodily care is permitted on Shabbat comes from the Mishnah that one may use a needle to remove a splinter. Removing a splinter is not a *refu'ah* in the halakhic sense, nor is it *metakein*. It is simply the normal care of one's body. Rav Sheshet's position is that ordinary physical care of any kind falls outside the prohibition. Rav Nachman's rebuttal is precise: *hasam pakid* — a splinter is a foreign object lodged in the body; removing it is extracting something extrinsic, not fixing the body itself. Straightening limbs, by contrast, involves working directly on the body's own structure, which is why he regards it as forbidden.

## Historical Development

### Oleaster Stick

The Gemara turns to a Mishnah concerning *kaneh shel zeitim* — a stick used to turn over olives in a barrel, allowing the owner to assess their readiness for pressing by examining the oil that collects on the rod. The Mishnah rules: *im yesh kesher berosho mekabel tumah* — if the stick has a knot or bulge at its top, it can become *tamei*. *Ve-im lav ein mekabel tumah* — if it has no such feature, it cannot. Yet *bein kach u-vein kach nital b'Shabbos* — in either case it may be moved on Shabbat.

The Gemara asks: why should a smooth stick become *tamei* just because it has a knot? After all, *pshutei klei etz hem* — it is a flat wooden vessel with no receptacle, and *pshutei klei etz ein mekabelin tumah* — flat wooden vessels cannot become *tamei*, since the Torah's paradigmatic vessel is a sack, and just as a sack has a receptacle, wooden vessels must have one as well. The resolution, taught in the name of Rav Nachman, is that the knot functions as a minimal receptacle: *besha'ah shemehapech bezeitim hofcho vro'eh bo* — when one stirs the olives, one draws out the rod and examines the small amount of oil that collects in the hollow formed by the knot, thereby assessing the olives' readiness. This constitutes a *beis kibul*.

This Mishnah proves directly that the definition of *kli* for *tumah* and for Shabbat are not identical. The smooth stick without a knot clearly functions as a useful tool; it simply lacks a technical requirement for *tumah* status. Its Shabbat status is unaffected.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi states a sweeping principle: *kol hakeilim nitlin* — all vessels may be moved on Shabbat, *chutz min hamassar hagadol vehayated shel machareisha* — except for the great saw and the peg of the plow. These represent the narrow category of professional implements so large and specialized — and so guarded by their owners — that they fall into genuine *muktzah machmat chisron kis* and cannot be moved for any purpose.

### Expanded Vessels

Rav Nachman extends this category: *hai uchla dekatzri ke-yated shel machareisha dami* — the launderer's metal basket, whether used to apply water to garments or to perfume them with incense, is similarly off-limits, as no one would use it for any other purpose. Abaye likewise identifies: *charva de-ushkafi* — the leatherworker's knife; *vesakina de-ashkavta* — the butcher's knife; *de-chatzina de-nargi* — the carpenter's plane. All of these are *ke-yated shel machareisha dami*, treated as the equivalent of the plow peg. They are the sorts of professional tools whose owners would not permit casual use, and they belong to the category of complete *muktzah*.

### Three Vessels

The Gemara then introduces the historical dimension: *barishona hayu omerim sheloshah keilim nitlin beshabbat* — originally, the Sages ruled that only three vessels may be moved on Shabbat. These were: the knife used for cutting pressed figs, a vessel used to skim the froth from a cooking pot, and the small table knife. Every other vessel was forbidden to move — a restriction that strikes one as nearly paralyzing. One naturally wonders how daily life on Shabbat was possible. Yet this was the initial decree.

It should be noted that Tosafot suggests that vessels with receptacles — cups, plates, spoons — were presumably never included in the prohibition. The decree concerned other types of implements. Nonetheless, the scope of the initial restriction was vast.

*Hitiru v'chazru v'hitiru, v'chazru v'hitiru* — then they relaxed the decree, and relaxed it again, and relaxed it further, *ad she'amru kol hakeilim nitlin beshabbat* — until they reached the position with which the Mishnah opens: all vessels may be moved, with the sole exception of the great saw and the plow peg.

This entire progression belongs to what is properly called *gezeirat keilim* — a rabbinic enactment concerning vessels — and it is conceptually distinct from the basic category of *muktzah*. The Gemara now turns to Abaye and Rava's reconstruction of what these stages of liberalization actually were.

---

## Four Stages

### Stage One

Abaye reconstructs the stages of liberalization as follows. The first relaxation permitted *davar shemilachto l'heter* — any vessel whose primary function is a permitted activity — to be moved *l'tzorech gufo*: that is, for the purpose of using the object itself for some permitted function.

### Stage Two

The second relaxation extended permission for *kli shemelachto l'heter* to *l'tzorech mekomo* as well — one may move such a vessel simply because one needs its space, even without intending to use the object itself.

### Stage Three

The third relaxation addressed *davar shemilachto l'issur* — vessels whose primary function is forbidden. These were now permitted to be moved *l'tzorech gufo* — to use the object for a permitted purpose — but not *l'tzorech mekomo*. Moving a hammer to clear a table, on this view, remained forbidden. Additionally, at this stage the permission extended only to moving such an object with one hand; moving it with two hands was still prohibited as too overtly a weekday activity.

### Stage Four

The fourth and final stage was the proclamation of *kol hakeilim nitlin b'Shabbat afilu bishtei yadayim* — all vessels may be moved, even when doing so requires two people. This is the position that now stands as the framework of the Mishnah.

Abaye's reconstruction, however, is stricter than the position we rule by in one important respect: he holds that *kli shemelachto l'issur* cannot be moved *l'tzorech mekomo*, and that *kli shemelachto l'heter* cannot be moved *mechama l'tzel*. Rava will disagree with both distinctions, and the *halacha* follows Rava.

---

## Rava's Stages

### Gufo=Mekomo

Rava challenges Abaye's construction at its foundation. The Gemara records his argument: *ma li l'tzorech gufo ma li l'tzorech mekomo?* — once the Sages declared something permitted, what halakhic distinction can be drawn between using the object for its own sake and simply needing its space? Abaye evidently believed these to be meaningfully different; Rava maintains they are not.

### Rava Stage 1

According to Rava, the first stage of liberalization was the full permission of *davar shemilachto l'heter* — vessels whose primary function is permitted — for all uses: *bein l'tzorech gufo bein l'tzorech mekomo*, whether one is using the object or merely needs its place.

### Rava Stage 2

The second stage extended permission to *mechama l'tzel* for *kli shemelachto l'heter* as well. One may move a vessel whose function is permitted even solely in order to protect it from damage — from sun to shade, for example. This is the ruling we follow.

The third stage then addressed *davar shemilachto l'issur*, permitting movement both *l'tzorech gufo* and *l'tzorech mekomo* — equating these two, as Rava does for *shemelachto l'heter* — but stopping short of *mechama l'tzel*. At this stage, as with Abaye's parallel formulation, individual movement was permitted but movement requiring two people was not.

### Final Ruling

*Ad she'amru kol hakeilim nitlin b'Shabbat afilu bishnei benei adam* — the final ruling reached is that all vessels may be moved even when two people are required. The *halacha* follows Rava throughout. The single remaining restriction on *kli shemelachto l'issur* is *mechama l'tzel* — one may not move it solely to protect it from damage.

An illustrative practical application: pens, pencils, and crayons are *keilim she'melachtam l'issur* — vessels whose primary function is a prohibited one. They are not *muktzah* in the classic sense. They may therefore be moved *l'tzorech gufo* — for instance, to use a pen to manipulate a lock mechanism — and *l'tzorech mekomo*, to clear them from a surface. What one may not do is move them solely to protect them from being damaged. Similarly, even large objects requiring two people to move, such as a computer, fall within the permitted category of *kli shemelachto l'issur* for purposes of *l'tzorech mekomo*, unless they have entered the category of *meyuchad limkomo* — designated to a fixed location in a manner that takes them outside the realm of ordinary *kelim* handling.

## Mechama L'Tzel

### Definition

*Mechama l'tzel* literally denotes moving an object from sun to shade — that is, moving it not to use it and not because it is in the way, but solely to preserve it from damage. For *kli shemelachto l'heter*, this is permitted. For *kli shemelachto l'issur*, it is forbidden.

### Conceptual Basis

The conceptual basis for this distinction is important to grasp. When one uses a hammer to crack nuts, one is relating to the hammer as a nutcracker — a tool for the present permitted purpose. When one moves a hammer off a table because it is in the way, one is relating to it as an obstacle or an annoyance, with no particular regard for its nature as a hammer. In neither case is one relating to the object *qua* hammer in its primary forbidden capacity. But when one moves a hammer from the rain because one does not want it to get damaged — when one's motivation is concern for the hammer as a hammer, as one's professional tool — one is at that moment relating to the object in precisely the way one relates to it throughout the week. It is that mode of relating, that self-positioning as the hammer's owner and guardian, that is the problem. The person is not treating Shabbat differently from the rest of the week with respect to this object.

### Hammer Example

The practical implication is pointed: if one's hammer is outside in the rain, the appropriate solution is to find some nuts that need cracking, thereby giving oneself a *l'tzorech gufo* justification for moving the hammer inside. This is not a legal fiction — it is a genuine permitted use. But moving the hammer inside purely to protect it, without any permitted use in view, remains forbidden for *kli shemelachto l'issur*.

This principle also explains the mortar-and-pestle case (*medocha*) that the Gemara revisits at the end of the *amud*. The Mishnah rules that the mortar may be moved if it contains garlic — because then one is in effect moving the garlic and the mortar incidentally — but not if it is empty. Rava resolves the difficulty by identifying the case as one of *mechama l'tzel*: the mortar is being moved purely to protect it or to clear it for safekeeping, and that is the one category forbidden even according to the final, most lenient stage of the rulings on *keilim*.

## Nehemiah Era

### Crisis Context

The Gemara provides explicit historical grounding for the entire phenomenon of *gezeirat keilim*. Rabbi Chanina reports: *bimei Nechemia ben Chachalia nishnet mishnah zu* — this Mishnah and the entire restrictive framework it encapsulates was enacted in the days of Nehemiah son of Hachaliah. The verse from the Book of Nehemiah is cited: *bayamim hahema raiti biyehuda dorchim gittot beshabbat* — in those days I saw in Judea that they were treading wine vats on Shabbat, and bringing in bundles of grain. *Chilul Shabbat* was rampant and open.

### Corrective Measure

The rabbinic response was correspondingly extreme: all vessels were declared off-limits. The logic was that vessel-handling is the gateway to productive weekday activity. If people cannot touch their tools, they cannot perform their work. The decree was a sweeping corrective — an overcorrection, by design — intended to erect a sharp barrier between Shabbat behavior and weekday behavior at a moment when that barrier had nearly collapsed entirely.

### Gradual Liberalization

Once the crisis had passed and the decree had achieved its corrective purpose, the process of gradual liberalization began, proceeding through the stages described by Abaye and Rava until reaching the permissive position of *kol hakeilim nitlin b'Shabbat* that stands as normative *halacha*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer closes the discussion with an observation about the chronological relationship between several restrictive *mishnayot* and *braitot* and the general permission: *kanim u'maklot glustra u'meducha kulam kodem hataras kelim nishnu* — the Mishnah concerning reeds and staffs, the ruling about the *glustra* (a bar or bolt with a rounded protrusion), and the ruling about the mortar and pestle were all formulated during the period before the general permission of vessels was granted. Their stringency, which might otherwise seem puzzling against the backdrop of the lenient ruling, reflects the historical moment of maximum restriction — the period in Nehemiah's era when all vessels were off-limits. The restrictive tone of those passages is thus not a contradiction of the current ruling but a historical artifact of an earlier, emergency enactment.

The essential point to carry forward is the conceptual one. *Kol hakeilim* — all ordinary vessels — occupy a fundamentally different category than classic *muktzah*. Classic *muktzah* is an object that is mentally off-limits, pushed aside, out of one's world of intended Shabbat use. *Keilim* are the normal objects of daily life, and the entire history of the laws governing them is a history of a decree — *gezeirat keilim* — that was imposed in extreme circumstances and then progressively relaxed until the broad permission of the Mishnah was established. Within that broad permission, only one genuine restriction remains on *kli shemelachto l'issur*: it may not be moved *mechama l'tzel*. Everything else is permitted.
