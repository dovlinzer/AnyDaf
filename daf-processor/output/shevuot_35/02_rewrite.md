# Shevuot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashbia Ani Aleichem

### Shevuat HaEdut Intro

We continue the *mishna* from where we left off yesterday, adding new dimensions to the laws of *shevuat ha'edut* — the oath of testimony — which operates primarily in the domain of monetary disputes. The *mishna* presents five distinct cases, each introduced by the formula *mashbia ani aleichem*, "I administer an oath upon you," illustrating the various circumstances under which this oath can or cannot generate liability.

### Promise vs Exchange

The first case: *mashbia ani aleichem im lo tavo'u v'te'iduni she'amar ish ploni liten li matayim zuz v'lo natan* — I place an oath upon you that if you do not come and testify that so-and-so promised to give me two hundred *zuz* and did not give them. The *mishna* rules: *harei elu p'turim*, they are exempt, *she'ein chayavim ela al tvi'at mamon k'pikadon* — liability attaches only to a monetary claim comparable to a deposit, where actual money was exchanged.

As *Rashi* explains in just a few lines: had the witnesses testified, they would not have rendered the other party liable for anything, because he could simply say, "I changed my mind." A mere promise, even one heard by multiple people, creates no binding monetary obligation. This is our first case of *mashbia ani aleichem*.

### Oath Precedence

The second case: *mashbia ani aleichem k'shetid'u li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — I place an oath upon you that when you know testimony for me, you will come and testify. Here the *mishna* rules them exempt *mipnei shekadmah shvuah la'edut* — because the oath preceded the testimony. The proper order requires that one first witness an event and only then be bound by oath regarding it. An oath administered prospectively, before the witnesses have seen anything, is fundamentally out of sequence and generates no liability.

### Synagogue & Specificity

The third case: *amad b'vet haknesset v'amar mashbia ani aleichem she'im atem yod'im li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — he stood in the synagogue and declared: I place an oath upon you that if you know testimony for me, you will come and testify. The *mishna* rules them exempt — and then adds, in what some read as a parenthetical: *ad sheyehe meyuchad lahem*, unless the witnesses are specifically designated.

One might ask why the *mishna* situates this case in the *bet haknesset* specifically. The *bet haknesset* is a place of public assembly, a gathering of the community — and the scenario is precisely that the speaker addresses an undifferentiated crowd. The *gemara* will develop this point further, but the essential problem is already visible: even if witnesses are present in that crowd, addressing a general audience without specifying who the witnesses are is insufficient to bind them.

### Disqualified Witnesses

The fourth case addresses a situation where the plaintiff explicitly designates two individuals — *mashbia ani aleichem, Ploni u'Ploni, im atem yod'im li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — and the two witnesses do in fact know the relevant testimony, yet their testimony is halachically invalid. The *mishna* and the *Tosafot* at the opening of this *daf* recall what was already stated at the beginning of the *perek*: *ed mipi ed*, hearsay testimony, or a situation where one of the witnesses is a relative or otherwise *pasul*, disqualifies the testimony. Furthermore, the principle *edut she'batla miktzatah batla kulah* — testimony that is partially invalid is entirely invalid — applies here as well.

### Messenger & Defendant

The fifth and final case: *shalach b'yad avdo* — he sent the summons via his servant — or where the defendant himself, *ha'nitba*, administered the oath to the witnesses. In both situations the witnesses are exempt, *ad she'yishm'u mipi hatove'a* — unless they hear the oath directly from the plaintiff. The oath must come from the claimant himself; neither an unauthorized agent nor the opposing party can administer it in his stead.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Promise Obligation

The *gemara* now interrogates the first case. Could witnesses ever be liable for failing to testify about a mere promise? The question is: *yachol yehu chayavin?* — might one think they are?

### Shevua Comparison

The *gemara* answers by invoking a *gezeirah shavah*: *talmud lomar techeta techeta*. The word *techeta* appears in two places in *Vayikra* chapter 5 — once in the opening *pasuk* concerning *shevuat ha'edut* (Pasuk 1), and once in the context of *shevuat ha'pikadon*. We compare the two: just as *shevuat ha'pikadon* requires an actual monetary claim — money that was in fact exchanged — so too *shevuat ha'edut* requires an actual monetary claim, *tvi'at mamon*, backed by real exchange. A promise alone, without actual transfer, cannot generate liability.

---

## Order Requirement

### Witness Hearing

The *gemara* addresses the second case with a *baraita*: *mashbiani aleichem k'shetid'u li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni — yachol yehu chayavin? Talmud lomar: v'shama kol alah v'hu ed o ra'ah o yada*. The *pasuk* says "and he heard the sound of the oath, and he is a witness, or he saw, or he knew." The *gemara* derives from this that the person must already be a witness at the time the oath reaches him — *mi she-kadma edut li-shvuah v'lo she-kadma shvuah la'edut*. The testimony must precede the oath, not the other way around. One cannot be sworn regarding testimony one has not yet acquired.

## Witness Specification

### Samuel's Rule

The *gemara* turns to the third case. *Shmuel* teaches: *afilu ed beineihem* — even if the actual witnesses happen to be standing among the crowd in the *bet haknesset*, the witnesses are still exempt. This might seem obvious, yet the *gemara* explains why it needs to be stated: *de-ka'i pilavei abrai* — one might have thought that since the witnesses are standing right beside the speaker, it is as if he addressed them directly. *Shmuel* therefore teaches that this is not so.

### General vs Specific

The critical distinction is between a general address to the assembled crowd and a specific designation of individuals. Simply declaring "whoever among you knows testimony" — even if the two witnesses are present and know full well that they are the relevant witnesses — does not constitute sufficient designation. Until the plaintiff actually approaches and specifies the witnesses, they are not bound.

### Baraita Distinction

A *baraita* sharpens the distinction further. *Ra'ah si'ah shel bnei adam omdim v'adav beineihem* — if he saw a group of people standing, with his witnesses among them, and said *mashbiani aleichem im atem yode'im li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni* — might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *v'hu ed* — "and he is a witness" — implies that the individual witness must have been specifically designated. Since *lo yiched adav*, he did not specify his witnesses, they are exempt.

However, the *baraita* continues: *yachol afilu amar kol ha-omdim kan* — what if he said "all who are standing here"? In that case, *harei yiched adav* — he has in effect specified his witnesses, because he designated everyone present. Even though this is not highly specific — it is "all of you here" rather than calling out names — it is nonetheless sufficient to constitute designation. The line between general and specific address, then, is not merely about naming individuals but about whether the language clearly embraces the witnesses as a defined, identified group.

## Valid Testimony

### Hearsay & Disqualificati…

Moving to the fourth case, the *gemara* presents the *baraita*: *amar l'shnayim mashbia ani aleichem Ploni u'Ploni im atem yode'im li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni* — and they do know testimony, but their testimony is either *ed mipi ed*, hearsay, or one of them is a relative or otherwise *pasul*. Might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *im lo yagid venasa avono*.

### Capable Witnesses

The verse says: "if he does not speak, he shall bear his iniquity." The *gemara* derives: *b're'uyim lehagada hakasuv medabeir* — the text speaks only of those who are capable of giving valid testimony. A witness whose testimony is inherently disqualified — whether because it is hearsay, or because he is a relative, or because he is *pasul* for another reason — is not within the category of persons the verse addresses. Such witnesses are therefore exempt.

## Messenger Authorization

### Servant & Defendant

The *gemara* now addresses the fifth case. *Tanu rabbanan*: if one sent the oath *b'yad avdo* — through his servant without authorization — or if *ha'nitba*, the defendant, administered the oath to the witnesses saying *mashbiani aleichem she'im atem yode'im lo edut she-tavo'u v'te'idu*, might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *im lo yagid venasa avono*.

### Double Lo Interpretation

The *gemara* asks: *mai talmuda?* How exactly does this *pasuk* teach us about the requirement of direct address by the proper party? *Amar Rabbi Elazar*: the word in the *Torah* appears spelled with both a *vav* and an *alef* — that is, both *lo* (to him) and *lo* (not) are simultaneously present in the written text. Reading it as *im lo lo yagid* — "if to *him* he does not say it" — teaches that liability arises specifically when the oath was not delivered to the witness by the one to whom the witness is obligated to speak. If someone else, an unauthorized party, administered the oath, the binding relationship between the witness and the person owed testimony is not established.

### Plaintiff Authority

The requirement, then, is that the plaintiff himself — *ha'tove'a* — administer the oath. This is not merely a technical formality. The *pasuk* is structured around the relationship between the witness and the party who has standing to demand testimony. A servant without *harsha'a*, without formal power of attorney, has no such standing. Neither does the defendant: the *nitba* cannot place the witnesses under oath on behalf of the *tove'a*. It is worth noting that the exclusion of a servant is not absolute — had the servant come with proper *harsha'a*, the situation would be different. But absent authorization, neither an agent nor the opposing party can fulfill what the *Torah* requires of the plaintiff himself.

## Oath Language

### Binding Words

The *mishna* now shifts to a closely related topic: the precise language of an oath — what words are binding, and what words are not. The *mishna* states: *mashbia, metzaveh, osrechem — harei elu chayavin*. If one uses any of these three formulas — I administer an oath upon you, I command you, I forbid you — the witnesses are liable should they deny their testimony.

### Non-Binding Phrases

By contrast, *bashamayim uva'aretz — harei elu peturin*. Invoking heaven and earth does not constitute a binding oath.

### God's Names

The *mishna* then lists the divine names and titles that do render an oath binding: *alef-dalet* (the name *Adon*), *yud-heh* (the divine name), *Shaddai*, *Tzva'ot*, *Chanun veRachum*, *Erech Apayim*, *Rav Chesed* — and all *kinuyim*, all appellations. Invoking any of these, *harei elu chayavin*. This immediately raises a question the *gemara* will take up: *alef-dalet*, the word *adon*, is an ordinary word in Hebrew — one can say *adon* in a grocery store and simply mean "sir." The question of when this word constitutes invocation of God's name and when it is mundane will require further analysis.

## Curses & Names

### Cursing Dispute

Since we are dealing with the power of God's names in oaths, the *mishna* addresses an adjacent question: cursing using God's name. *Ha'mikalel bekulam chayav, divrei Rabbi Meir; ve'Chachamim potrin* — one who curses using all of these names is liable according to *Rabbi Meir*, while the *Chachamim* exempt him. The case at issue is whether the *kinuyim* and divine attributes, not just the explicit names, render a curse actionable.

### Self & Family Cursing

The *mishna* continues: *ha'mikalel aviv ve'imo bekulam chayav divrei Rabbi Meir, ve'Chachamim potrin* — if one curses his father or mother using all of these names, *Rabbi Meir* holds him liable and *Chachamim* exempt. And: *ha'mikalel atzmo ve'chavero be'chulan over be'lo ta'aseh* — one who curses himself or his fellow transgresses a prohibition in all cases. The *gemara* will supply the relevant scriptural source, drawn from the *klalot* of *Ki Tavo* (Devarim 28:22): *yakecha Hashem Elohim* — and by extension: *al yakecha, ve'yivarechecha, ve'yeitiv lach* — whether the formulation is a direct curse or its inverse, *Rabbi Meir* holds one liable, while *Chachamim* exempt.

## Mashbia Formula

### Rav Yehuda Formula

The *gemara* now probes the *mishna*'s opening words — *mashbia ani aleichem, metzaveh ani aleichem, osrechem* — asking what exactly is being said. *Amar Rav Yehuda*: this is what the formula means: I administer an oath upon you in the manner of the oath spoken in the *Torah*; I command you in the manner of the command spoken in the *Torah*; I forbid you in the manner of the prohibition spoken in the *Torah*. According to *Rav Yehuda*, each formulation is understood as a reference to a specific category in the *Torah*.

### Abaye's Correction

*Abaye* challenges this. He points to a *baraita* in which *Rabbi Chiya* adds a fourth term — *kovelachem*, I bind you, from the root *kevel*, a chain or rope. The question is: *ve'hai kovel be'Oraita michtiv?* — where does *kovel* appear in the *Torah* as a binding formula? Since no such term appears in the *Torah*, *Rav Yehuda*'s framework — that each word points to its counterpart in the *Torah* — cannot accommodate *Rabbi Chiya*'s addition.

*Abaye* therefore reframes the entire formula: *mashbia ani aleichem bishvu'ah, metzaveh ani aleichem bishvu'ah, oserchem ani bishvu'ah, kovel ani bishvu'ah* — in each case, the operative word is *shvu'ah*, and the other terms — commandment, prohibition, binding — all qualify as invocations of oath-power. The witnesses are liable in all these formulations because in each case a *shvu'ah* is being administered. Whether this fully resolves the analytical difficulty is another question, but the practical conclusion is clear: if the word *shvu'ah* underlies any of these formulas, the witnesses are bound.

## Erasable Names

### Names vs Descriptions

The *mishna* listed both explicit divine names and *kinuyim* — appellations such as *Chanun veRachum* — as names that bind an oath. The *gemara* now challenges this by citing a contradiction: *yesh shemot she'nimchakim ve'yesh shemot she'ein nimchakim* — some names may be erased, and others may not. The *gemara* is thus not merely asking whether *Chanun veRachum* is a name or a description, but more precisely: what may be erased from a sacred text, and what must be preserved?

### Unerasable List

The *baraita* provides the definitive list of names that may not be erased: *El, Elohecha, Elohim, Eloheichem, Ehyeh asher Ehyeh, alef-dalet, yud-heh, Shaddai, Tzva'ot — harei elu ein nimchakim*. This list reflects the *halacha* as we observe it to this day.

### Erasable List

By contrast, the descriptive attributes — *ha'gadol, ha'gibor, ha'nora, ha'adir, ve'ha'chazak, ve'ha'amitz, ve'ha'izuz, Chanun, veRachum, Erech Apayim, veRav Chesed* — these may all be erased, *harei elu nimchakim*, because they describe qualities rather than name the divine essence.

*Abaye* explains: when the *mishna* includes *Chanun veRachum* among the binding names, it means *be'mi shehu chanun, be'mi shehu rachum* — invoking "He who is gracious, He who is compassionate," understood as pointing to God. *Rava* immediately presses: if that is the logic, then *bashamayim uva'aretz* — "by heaven and earth" — should also be understood as invoking "He whose heaven and earth it is," and should likewise be binding. The *gemara* distinguishes: *Chanun veRachum* can refer to nothing other than God — nothing else is called gracious and compassionate in this sense — and so the reference is unambiguous. Heaven and earth, however, exist as independent entities in their own right; one might construe an oath "by heaven and earth" as referring to those entities rather than to their Creator. Therefore *bashamayim uva'aretz* remains exempt.

### Heaven & Earth

This distinction might strike one as somewhat forced — everything in existence belongs to God, and it borders on a kind of conceptual heresy to treat heaven and earth as standing independently of their Creator. Yet the *halacha* draws this line: the concern is with how a speaker might be understood, and since heaven and earth can be conceived as independent referents, an oath invoking them is not sufficiently anchored to the divine name to be binding.

## Letter Combinations

### Alef-Lamed, Yud-Hey

A *baraita* addresses written letters: *katav alef-lamed me'Elohim, yud-heh me'Hashem — harei ze eino nimchak*. If one writes the letters *alef-lamed* with the intention of writing God's name, or *yud-heh* with the intention of writing the divine name, that text may not be erased. The key qualifier is intent. One might note that *alef-lamed* appears constantly in ordinary Hebrew — *ani holech el ha'cheder* — and *yud-heh* begins many names entirely unrelated to God. The ruling therefore is specifically conditioned on the writer's intention to inscribe a divine name.

### Shin-Dalet, Alef-Dalet

The *baraita* continues: *shin-dalet me'Shaddai, alef-dalet me'Adonai, tzadi-beit me'Tzva'ot*. Here the situation is somewhat different. *Alef-lamed* and *yud-heh* are themselves recognized divine names, which is why intent alone is decisive. But *shin-dalet* standing alone spells *sheid* — a demon — which is decidedly not a divine name. Similarly, *alef-dalet* alone means *eid* — as in *ve'eid ya'aleh min ha'aretz* — a vapor or mist, an entirely mundane word. The partial letters thus carry their own independent non-divine meanings, which makes the question of intent even more pointed in these cases.

### Tzvaot Dependency

*Rabbi Yosei* goes further: even the complete word *Tzva'ot* in its entirety may be erased, *kulo nimchak*, because the word *Tzva'ot* acquired its divine association only through its connection to Israel: *shene'emar ve'hotzeiti et tzivotai et ammi Benei Yisrael me'eretz Mitzrayim* (Shemot). The *armies* in question are the armies of Israel; the divine name is therefore contingent on that context and does not stand as an independent divine appellation. *Shmuel*, however, rules that *ein halacha ke'Rabbi Yosei* — the law does not follow *Rabbi Yosei* — without elaboration, and so we do not accept this position.

## Name Affixes

### Prefixes Erasable

The *gemara* addresses prepositions and other letters attached to God's name: *kol ha'tefel la'Shem bein milfanav uvein mil'acharav harei zeh nimchak* — anything appended to God's name, whether before it or after it, may be erased. The examples of prefixes: *la'Shem* — the *lamed* may be erased; *ba'Shem* — the *beit* may be erased; *ve'Hashem* — the *vav* may be erased; *me'Hashem* — the *mem* may be erased; *she'Hashem* — the *shin* may be erased; *ha'Shem* — the *heh* may be erased; *ke'Hashem* — the *kaf* may be erased. The prefix is grammatically attached but remains external to the name itself.

### Suffixes Erasable

Similarly for suffixes: *Eloheinu* — the *nu* may be erased; *Eloheihem* — the *hem* may be erased; *Eloheichem* — the *chem* may be erased. The possessive suffix is likewise external to the divine name proper.

### Suffix Sanctity

However, *acherim omrim le'acharav eino nimchak shekevar kidsho ha'Shem* — others say that what follows God's name may not be erased, because God's name has already sanctified it. And *Rav Huna* rules: *halacha ka'acherim* — the law follows this anonymous opinion. It is worth noting that the *gemara* does not identify *acherim* by name here, and a comment is made that this is one of the places where *Rabbi Meir*'s opinions were attributed to *acherim* following a dispute with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, as discussed in *Horayot*.

## Abraham & God's Names

### Abraham Overview

The *gemara* now embarks on a sustained analysis of instances in *Tanakh* where God's name appears in a context that could be read as referring either to God or to a human figure. The *siman* for this series of passages is: *Avraham, deLotia, leNavot, beGivat Binyamin, Shlomo, Daniel*. The first principle: *kol shemot ha'amurim ba'Torah be'Avraham kodesh, chutz mizeh she'hu chol* — all the divine names that appear in the *Torah* in the context of Avraham are *kodesh*, sacred, except for one instance which is *chol*, mundane.

### Adonim Ambiguity

That one case is found in the opening of *Vayera*: the word that in many texts appears spelled *yud-yud* — indicating *Hashem* — but which in the *Torah* itself reads *alef-dalet-nun-yud*, in the plural: *Adonim*, my masters. The context is *im na matzati chein be'einecha* — "if I have found favor in your eyes." This is the moment when Avraham encounters the three angels. Grammatically, the word is plural — *my masters*, a perfectly ordinary Hebrew honorific, the kind one might use addressing a group of gentlemen. And yet the suffix *einecha* — "your eyes" — is singular. The sentence begins in the plural and resolves into the singular. This grammatical oscillation between plural address and singular reference is striking.

One might note that this kind of shift is in fact common throughout the *Torah* — a plural entity is addressed in the singular or vice versa, sometimes because of the unity of the group, sometimes because the speaker turns to address the chief of a delegation. The classic *Talmudic* example here — that Avraham turns to address the most significant of the three visitors — is one reading, but the grammatical phenomenon itself appears widely in biblical literature.

### Abraham Interpretation

*Chanina ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua* and *Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* in the name of *Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i* hold that even this instance is *kodesh* — Avraham was not addressing the angels but speaking to God Himself. This reading aligns with the famous teaching: *amar Rav Yehudah amar Rav, gedolah hachnasat orchim yoter mehakbalat pnei ha'Shechinah* — hospitality toward guests is greater than receiving the *Shechinah*. That teaching depends on understanding that Avraham was in the midst of a divine encounter when the three men arrived, and that he said to God: wait for me while I attend to these guests. The *gemara* therefore notes: *keman azlah* — this teaching goes according to *Chanina* and *Rabbi Elazar*, who read the word as addressed to God. *Keman ke'oto hazug* — just like that pair of Sages.

## Lot's Address

### Lot Overview

*Kol shemot ha'amurim beLot chol, chutz mizeh she'hu kodesh* — all the divine names that appear in Lot's narrative are *chol*, mundane, except for one instance which is *kodesh*. In Lot's story, the names generally refer to the angels or to Lot's human interlocutors.

### Lot Escape

The one *kodesh* instance: *vayomer Lot aleihem, al na Adonai, hineh na matza avdecha chein be'einecha* (Bereshit 19:18). The angels have told Lot to flee to the mountain, but Lot demurs and requests to escape to a nearby city instead. The opening of the verse seems to indicate he is addressing the angels — *vayomer Lot aleihem*, he said to them — and yet the *gemara* rules this instance *kodesh*. The explanation: *mi sheyesh beyado lehamit ulehachayot zeh haKadosh Baruch Hu* — the one who has the power to give life and to take it is God alone. Since Lot is appealing to the power to save him, he must be addressing God, not an angel. The word *aleihem* is itself a difficulty the *gemara* does not fully dissolve — but based on the content of Lot's appeal, the *gemara* reads the name as directed to God.

## Navot & Micah

### Navot Sacred

*Kol shemot ha'amurim beNavot kodesh, beMicha chol* — all the names in the Navot story (*Melachim Aleph*) are *kodesh*, while the names in the story of *Micha* (the figure from *Shoftim*) are *chol*. *Micha* built a private temple and priestly garments in competition with the legitimate service — a figure whose acts were sinful — and in that narrative the names refer to human or contextual usage. By contrast, the names in the story of *Navot HaYizre'eli*, whose vineyard Achav sought to seize, are all sacred.

*Rabbi Eliezer* adds a refinement for the case of *Micha*: *beNavot kodesh; beMicha yesh mehem chol veyesh mehem kodesh — alef-lamed chol, yud-heh kodesh*. In *Micha*'s narrative, *alef-lamed* is mundane while *yud-heh* is sacred. The one exception where *alef-lamed* in *Micha*'s story would be *kodesh* is the phrase *kol yemei heyot beit ha'elohim be'shilo* — "all the days that the house of God was in Shilo" — because here the reference is to the *Mishkan* at Shilo, an unambiguously sacred context.

### Gibeah Dispute

The *gemara* introduces the dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* regarding the narrative of *Givat Binyamin* (*Shoftim* 20 — the tragic story of *pilegesh be'giv'ah*, where a concubine was brutalized, the dismembered portions sent to the twelve tribes, and a devastating civil war erupted between Israel and the tribe of Binyamin). *Rabbi Eliezer* says the names in this passage are *chol*; *Rabbi Yehoshua* says they are *kodesh*. *Rabbi Eliezer* presses *Rabbi Yehoshua*: *vechi mavtiach ve'eino oseh?* — would God promise and not fulfill? If the names are *kodesh*, they represent divine guarantees — and those guarantees were apparently not honored in the early battles. *Rabbi Yehoshua* responds: *mah she'hivtiach asah* — what He promised, He did. The people, however, did not ask the right questions.

---

## Gibeah Promise

### Eliezer: Mundane

*Rabbi Eliezer*'s position is that the names in the *Givat Binyamin* narrative are *chol* — mundane — because the failures of the early campaigns suggest that no genuine divine promise was in effect. To call these names *kodesh* would imply that God promised victory and then withheld it, which *Rabbi Eliezer* finds untenable.

### Yehoshua: Sacred

*Rabbi Yehoshua* counters that the names are indeed *kodesh*, and that the problem lay not with God but with the people. *Ve'hem lo bachan im lintoach o lenatzeach* — they did not properly inquire whether they would emerge victorious. They went out to battle without asking the right question.

### Three Battles

The proof: *be'acharonah she'bachanu hiskimu al yadam* — in the final campaign, when they inquired properly, the answer came. The verse (*Shoftim* 20): *u'Pinchas ben Elazar ben Aharon ha'kohen omed lefanav ba'yamim ha'hem lemor: ha'osif od latzet la'milchamah im Binyamin achi im echdal* — Pinchas son of Elazar son of Aharon the priest stood before God in those days and asked: shall we continue to go out to battle against Binyamin our brother, or shall we desist? This was a complete and proper inquiry through the *Urim veTumim*, and a complete answer came. Only in this third campaign, when the question was properly formulated and directed through the high priest, did God's response align with the outcome. Therefore the names in the passage are *kodesh* — they reflect genuine divine engagement, contingent on proper human inquiry.

## Shlomo & Names

### Shlomo Overview

*Kol Shlomo ha'amurim be'Shir ha'Shirim kodesh, shir le'mi she'ha'shalom shelo* — every instance of *Shlomo* in *Shir ha'Shirim* is *kodesh*, a song to the One to whom peace belongs, since *Shalom* is one of the names of God and *Shlomo* contains that name. One exception: *karmi sheli lefanai, ha'elef lecha Shlomo* — my vineyard is before me, the thousand is yours, *Shlomo*. That refers specifically to King Shlomo, and is *chol*.

The *gemara* also considers *ve'yesh omrim af zeh chol*: the verse *hineh mitato she'lishomo shishim* — behold the bed of *Shlomo*, sixty — might also be *chol* according to some.

### One-Sixth Rule

This discussion opens onto *Shmuel*'s ruling: *malchuta d'katla chad mi'shita b'alma lo mi'ansha* — a kingdom that takes one-sixth does not incur divine punishment. The proportion of a thousand to *Shlomo* and two hundred to the *notrim*, the guards who watch the fruit, is understood through this arithmetic: the two hundred plus the thousand total twelve hundred, and two hundred is one-sixth of twelve hundred. A kingdom may legitimately claim one-sixth of its subjects' productivity without transgressing.

The *gemara* also offers an alternative reading: the thousand goes to the heavenly kingdom, *malchuta d'rakia*, and the two hundred to the earthly kingdom, *malchuta d'ara*, with the *notrim* — the guardians — representing earthly governance. *Shmuel* aligns with *yesh omrim* on this question. The precise interplay between *Tanna Kamma* and *yesh omrim* — whether one *pasuk* or two are *chol*, and how to account for the numbers — remains in some tension, but *Shmuel*'s ruling orients us toward the *yesh omrim* position.

## Daniel & Malchim

### Daniel Mundane

*Kol malchaya ha'amurim beDaniel chol* — most instances of *malka* (king) in *Daniel* are *chol*, referring to Nevuchadnezzar or other earthly rulers.

### Daniel Sacred

One exception is *kodesh*: *ant malka, melech malchaya, di elah shmaya, malchuta chasna ve'takpa ve'ykara yehav lach* — you, O king, king of kings, to whom the God of heaven has given kingdom, power, might, and glory. In this passage Daniel praises — but the object of the deepest praise is God, not the human king, and *yesh omrim* would extend this status to an additional verse.

### Ambiguous Address

That additional verse: *mari chalma l'sanach u'fishreh l'arach* — my master, let the dream be for your enemies and its interpretation for your adversaries. *Yesh omrim* say this too is *kodesh* — Daniel was addressing God. The difficulty with reading it as addressed to Nevuchadnezzar is: *sinata mai ninhu?* — who would be Nevuchadnezzar's enemies? If Daniel were speaking to Nevuchadnezzar, he would be saying that the terrible dream should befall Nevuchadnezzar's enemies — which would be Israel. *Tanna Kamma* responds: Nevuchadnezzar had many enemies among the other nations; being an enemy of Nevuchadnezzar does not necessarily identify one as Israel. Therefore, the verse can plausibly be read as addressed to Nevuchadnezzar, and the word *malka* there remains *chol* according to the *Tanna Kamma*. *Yesh omrim* disagree and hold it is *kodesh*. The passage thus closes in the familiar mode of the *gemara* — an ambiguity preserved, two legitimate readings held in tension, the ruling to be determined elsewhere.
