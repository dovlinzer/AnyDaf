# Shevuot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashbia Ani Aleichem

### Shevuat HaEdut Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁאָמַר אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי לִיתֵּן לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז וְלֹא נָתַן״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִים, שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל תְּבִיעַת מָמוֹן כְּפִקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said to witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that so-and-so said** he is going **to give me two hundred dinars and he did not give** them to me, and they take false oaths that they have no knowledge of the matter, **they are exempt** from liability to bring an offering for taking a false oath of testimony, **as one is liable** for taking a false oath of testimony **only** in a case involving **a monetary claim like a deposit** in the sense that were the witnesses to testify, the individual would be liable to pay. In the case of a promise to give a gift he could claim that he merely reconsidered.

We continue the *mishna* from where we left off yesterday, adding new dimensions to the laws of *shevuat ha'edut* — the oath of testimony — which operates primarily in the domain of monetary disputes. The *mishna* presents five distinct cases, each introduced by the formula *mashbia ani aleichem*, "I administer an oath upon you," illustrating the various circumstances under which this oath can or cannot generate liability.

### Promise vs Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁאָמַר אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי לִיתֵּן לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז וְלֹא נָתַן״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִים, שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל תְּבִיעַת מָמוֹן כְּפִקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said to witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that so-and-so said** he is going **to give me two hundred dinars and he did not give** them to me, and they take false oaths that they have no knowledge of the matter, **they are exempt** from liability to bring an offering for taking a false oath of testimony, **as one is liable** for taking a false oath of testimony **only** in a case involving **a monetary claim like a deposit** in the sense that were the witnesses to testify, the individual would be liable to pay. In the case of a promise to give a gift he could claim that he merely reconsidered.

The first case: *mashbia ani aleichem im lo tavo'u v'te'iduni she'amar ish ploni liten li matayim zuz v'lo natan* — I place an oath upon you that if you do not come and testify that so-and-so promised to give me two hundred *zuz* and did not give them. The *mishna* rules: *harei elu p'turim*, they are exempt, *she'ein chayavim ela al tvi'at mamon k'pikadon* — liability attaches only to a monetary claim comparable to a deposit, where actual money was exchanged.

As *Rashi* explains in just a few lines: had the witnesses testified, they would not have rendered the other party liable for anything, because he could simply say, "I changed my mind." A mere promise, even one heard by multiple people, creates no binding monetary obligation. This is our first case of *mashbia ani aleichem*.

### Oath Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם כְּשֶׁתֵּדְעוּ לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקָּדְמָה שְׁבוּעָה לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** If one said to witnesses: **I administer an oath to you that when you will know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf, these** witnesses **are exempt** from liability for taking a false oath of testimony even if they do not testify, **due to** the fact **that the oath preceded** their knowledge of the relevant **testimony.**

The second case: *mashbia ani aleichem k'shetid'u li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — I place an oath upon you that when you know testimony for me, you will come and testify. Here the *mishna* rules them exempt *mipnei shekadmah shvuah la'edut* — because the oath preceded the testimony. The proper order requires that one first witness an event and only then be bound by oath regarding it. An oath administered prospectively, before the witnesses have seen anything, is fundamentally out of sequence and generates no liability.

### Synagogue & Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְאָמַר: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, שֶׁאִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא מִתְכַּוֵּין לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** If one **stood in the synagogue and said** for all to hear: **I administer an oath to you that if you know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf, these** witnesses **are exempt until he directs** his demand **to** specific individuals.

The third case: *amad b'vet haknesset v'amar mashbia ani aleichem she'im atem yod'im li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — he stood in the synagogue and declared: I place an oath upon you that if you know testimony for me, you will come and testify. The *mishna* rules them exempt — and then adds, in what some read as a parenthetical: *ad sheyehe meyuchad lahem*, unless the witnesses are specifically designated.

One might ask why the *mishna* situates this case in the *bet haknesset* specifically. The *bet haknesset* is a place of public assembly, a gathering of the community — and the scenario is precisely that the speaker addresses an undifferentiated crowd. The *gemara* will develop this point further, but the essential problem is already visible: even if witnesses are present in that crowd, addressing a general audience without specifying who the witnesses are is insufficient to bind them.

### Disqualified Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי, שֶׁאִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת״; וְהֵם יוֹדְעִין לוֹ עֵדוּת עֵד מִפִּי עֵד, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one said to two** people: **I administer an oath to you, so-and-so and so-and-so, that if you know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf,** and they respond: On our **oath we do not know** any **testimony** relevant **to you, and they know testimony** relevant **to him,** not based on an incident they witnessed but based on **hearsay testimony,** which is not valid testimony, **or** if **one of** the witnesses **is found** to be **a relative or disqualified, these** witnesses **are exempt** from liability for taking a false oath of testimony because they are unfit witnesses.

The fourth case addresses a situation where the plaintiff explicitly designates two individuals — *mashbia ani aleichem, Ploni u'Ploni, im atem yod'im li edut shetavo'u v'te'iduni* — and the two witnesses do in fact know the relevant testimony, yet their testimony is halachically invalid. The *mishna* and the *Tosafot* at the opening of this *daf* recall what was already stated at the beginning of the *perek*: *ed mipi ed*, hearsay testimony, or a situation where one of the witnesses is a relative or otherwise *pasul*, disqualifies the testimony. Furthermore, the principle *edut she'batla miktzatah batla kulah* — testimony that is partially invalid is entirely invalid — applies here as well.

### Messenger & Defendant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶן הַנִּתְבָּע: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, שֶׁאִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לוֹ עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּהוּ״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁמְעוּ מִפִּי הַתּוֹבֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** If the plaintiff **sent** the demand for their testimony **by means of his slave, or** in a case **where the defendant said to** the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you that if you know** any **testimony** relevant **to** the plaintiff **that you will come and testify on his behalf,** and they took a false oath that they have no knowledge of the matter, **these** witnesses **are exempt** from liability for taking a false oath of testimony **until they will hear** the demand to testify directly **from the mouth of the plaintiff.**

The fifth and final case: *shalach b'yad avdo* — he sent the summons via his servant — or where the defendant himself, *ha'nitba*, administered the oath to the witnesses. In both situations the witnesses are exempt, *ad she'yishm'u mipi hatove'a* — unless they hear the oath directly from the plaintiff. The oath must come from the claimant himself; neither an unauthorized agent nor the opposing party can administer it in his stead.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Promise Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁאָמַר אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי לִיתֵּן לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז וְלֹא נָתַן״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תֶּחֱטָא״–״תֶּחֱטָא״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites proof for the *halakhot* in the mishna from verses in the Torah. **The Sages taught: I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that so-and-so said** that he is going **to give me two hundred dinars and he did not give** them to me; in such a case one **might** have thought that **they would be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states** the term **"shall sin"** with regard to an oath of testimony and **"shall sin"** with regard to an oath on a deposit in order **to** derive **a verbal analogy.**

The *gemara* now interrogates the first case. Could witnesses ever be liable for failing to testify about a mere promise? The question is: *yachol yehu chayavin?* — might one think they are?

### Shevua Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״תֶּחֱטָא״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״תֶּחֱטָא״; מָה לְהַלָּן בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן – וְיֵשׁ לוֹ, אַף כָּאן בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן – וְיֵשׁ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here, it is stated** with regard to an oath of testimony **"shall sin"** (Leviticus 5:1), **and there, it is stated** with regard to an oath on a deposit **"shall sin"** (Leviticus 5:21). **Just as there,** with regard to an oath on a deposit, the verse is speaking only **with regard to a monetary claim and** in a case where the plaintiff **has** a legitimate claim, **so too here,** with regard to an oath of testimony, the verse is speaking only **with regard to a monetary claim and** in a case where the plaintiff **has** a legitimate claim. In the case cited in the *baraita* and in the first clause of the mishna, the plaintiff does not have a legitimate claim.

The *gemara* answers by invoking a *gezeirah shavah*: *talmud lomar techeta techeta*. The word *techeta* appears in two places in *Vayikra* chapter 5 — once in the opening *pasuk* concerning *shevuat ha'edut* (Pasuk 1), and once in the context of *shevuat ha'pikadon*. We compare the two: just as *shevuat ha'pikadon* requires an actual monetary claim — money that was in fact exchanged — so too *shevuat ha'edut* requires an actual monetary claim, *tvi'at mamon*, backed by real exchange. A promise alone, without actual transfer, cannot generate liability.

## Order Requirement

### Witness Hearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם כְּשֶׁתֵּדְעוּ לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשָׁמְעָה קוֹל אָלָה וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדַע״ – מִי שֶׁקָּדְמָה עֵדוּת לִשְׁבוּעָה, וְלֹא שֶׁקָּדְמָה שְׁבוּעָה לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: I administer an oath to you that when you will know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf;** in such a case one **might** have thought that **they would be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And he heard the voice of an oath and he is a witness or he saw or he knew"** (Leviticus 5:1). One is liable to bring an offering for a false oath only in a case **where** knowledge of the **testimony preceded the oath, and not** in a case **where the oath preceded** knowledge of the **testimony.**

The *gemara* addresses the second case with a *baraita*: *mashbiani aleichem k'shetid'u li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni — yachol yehu chayavin? Talmud lomar: v'shama kol alah v'hu ed o ra'ah o yada*. The *pasuk* says "and he heard the sound of the oath, and he is a witness, or he saw, or he knew." The *gemara* derives from this that the person must already be a witness at the time the oath reaches him — *mi she-kadma edut li-shvuah v'lo she-kadma shvuah la'edut*. The testimony must precede the oath, not the other way around. One cannot be sworn regarding testimony one has not yet acquired.

## Witness Specification

### Samuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְאָמַר ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם״. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲפִילּוּ עֵדָיו בֵּינֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If one **stood in the synagogue and said** for all to hear: **I administer an oath to you** that if you know testimony relevant to me you will come and testify on my behalf, these witnesses are exempt. **Shmuel says:** That is the *halakha* **even** if **his witnesses** were **among the** people in the synagogue.

The *gemara* turns to the third case. *Shmuel* teaches: *afilu ed beineihem* — even if the actual witnesses happen to be standing among the crowd in the *bet haknesset*, the witnesses are still exempt. This might seem obvious, yet the *gemara* explains why it needs to be stated: *de-ka'i pilavei abrai* — one might have thought that since the witnesses are standing right beside the speaker, it is as if he addressed them directly. *Shmuel* therefore teaches that this is not so.

### General vs Specific

The critical distinction is between a general address to the assembled crowd and a specific designation of individuals. Simply declaring "whoever among you knows testimony" — even if the two witnesses are present and know full well that they are the relevant witnesses — does not constitute sufficient designation. Until the plaintiff actually approaches and specifies the witnesses, they are not bound.

### Baraita Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: רָאָה סִיעָה שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם עוֹמְדִין, וְעֵדָיו בֵּינֵיהֶן, וְאָמַר לָהֶן: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם אִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּא עֵד״ – וַהֲרֵי לֹא יִיחֵד עֵדָיו. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״כׇּל הָעוֹמְדִין כָּאן״? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּא עֵד״ – וַהֲרֵי יִיחֵד עֵדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: If **one saw a group of people standing, and his witnesses** were **among them, and he said to** the entire group: **I administer an oath to you that if you know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf,** in such a case one **might** have thought that **they would be liable. The verse states: "And he is a witness"** (Leviticus 5:1), **and** in this case **he did not specify his witnesses;** therefore, they are not liable. One **might** have thought that the *halakha* is the same **even** if **he said:** I administer an oath to **everyone who is standing here** that you will come and testify on my behalf. **The verse states: "And he is a witness"** (Leviticus 5:1), **and** in this case, although he spoke to a group, **he specified his witnesses;** therefore, they are liable.

A *baraita* sharpens the distinction further. *Ra'ah si'ah shel bnei adam omdim v'adav beineihem* — if he saw a group of people standing, with his witnesses among them, and said *mashbiani aleichem im atem yode'im li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni* — might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *v'hu ed* — "and he is a witness" — implies that the individual witness must have been specifically designated. Since *lo yiched adav*, he did not specify his witnesses, they are exempt.

However, the *baraita* continues: *yachol afilu amar kol ha-omdim kan* — what if he said "all who are standing here"? In that case, *harei yiched adav* — he has in effect specified his witnesses, because he designated everyone present. Even though this is not highly specific — it is "all of you here" rather than calling out names — it is nonetheless sufficient to constitute designation. The line between general and specific address, then, is not merely about naming individuals but about whether the language clearly embraces the witnesses as a defined, identified group.

## Valid Testimony

### Hearsay & Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי, אִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״; וְהֵם יוֹדְעִין לוֹ עֵדוּת עֵד מִפִּי עֵד, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם לוֹא יַגִּיד וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״ – בִּרְאוּיִן לְהַגָּדָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if **one said to two** individuals: **I administer an oath to you, so-and-so and so-and-so,** that **if you know testimony** relevant **to me you will come and testify on my behalf, and they know testimony** relevant **to him** based on **hearsay testimony, or** if **one of** the witnesses is **a relative or disqualified,** in such a case one **might** have thought that **they would be liable. The verse states: "If he does not state it, then he shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:1), teaching that it is **with regard to** those **eligible for** the **statement** of testimony that **the verse is speaking,** not those who are ineligible.

Moving to the fourth case, the *gemara* presents the *baraita*: *amar l'shnayim mashbia ani aleichem Ploni u'Ploni im atem yode'im li edut she-tavo'u v'te'iduni* — and they do know testimony, but their testimony is either *ed mipi ed*, hearsay, or one of them is a relative or otherwise *pasul*. Might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *im lo yagid venasa avono*.

### Capable Witnesses

The verse says: "if he does not speak, he shall bear his iniquity." The *gemara* derives: *b're'uyim lehagada hakasuv medabeir* — the text speaks only of those who are capable of giving valid testimony. A witness whose testimony is inherently disqualified — whether because it is hearsay, or because he is a relative, or because he is *pasul* for another reason — is not within the category of persons the verse addresses. Such witnesses are therefore exempt.

## Messenger Authorization

### Servant & Defendant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁלַּח בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶן הַנִּתְבָּע: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם שֶׁאִם אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לוֹ עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּהוּ״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם לֹא יַגִּיד וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If the plaintiff **sent** the demand for their testimony **by means of his slave, etc. The Sages taught:** If the plaintiff **sent** the demand for their testimony **by means of his slave, or** in a case **where the defendant said to** the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you that if you know** any **testimony** relevant **to** the plaintiff **that you will come and testify on his behalf, might** one have thought that **they would be liable? The verse states: "If he does not state it, then he shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:1); therefore, they are not liable.

The *gemara* now addresses the fifth case. *Tanu rabbanan*: if one sent the oath *b'yad avdo* — through his servant without authorization — or if *ha'nitba*, the defendant, administered the oath to the witnesses saying *mashbiani aleichem she'im atem yode'im lo edut she-tavo'u v'te'idu*, might one think they are liable? The *talmud lomar*: *im lo yagid venasa avono*.

### Double Lo Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״אִם לוֹא יַגִּיד״ כְּתִיב; אִם לוֹ לֹא יַגִּיד – וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ, וְאִם לְאַחֵר לֹא יַגִּיד – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** biblical **derivation** that leads to that conclusion? **Rabbi Elazar said:** The derivation is based on the fact that it **is written: *Im lo yaggid*.** In the verse the word *lo* is spelled *lamed*, *vav*, *alef* and interpreted as both "not," spelled *lamed alef*, and "to him," spelled *lamed vav*. It is derived: **If** the witness who knows the testimony **does not state** it **to** the plaintiff, **he shall bear his iniquity,** and he is liable; **but if he does not state** it **to another,** he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai talmuda?* How exactly does this *pasuk* teach us about the requirement of direct address by the proper party? *Amar Rabbi Elazar*: the word in the *Torah* appears spelled with both a *vav* and an *alef* — that is, both *lo* (to him) and *lo* (not) are simultaneously present in the written text. Reading it as *im lo lo yagid* — "if to *him* he does not say it" — teaches that liability arises specifically when the oath was not delivered to the witness by the one to whom the witness is obligated to speak. If someone else, an unauthorized party, administered the oath, the binding relationship between the witness and the person owed testimony is not established.

### Plaintiff Authority

The requirement, then, is that the plaintiff himself — *ha'tove'a* — administer the oath. This is not merely a technical formality. The *pasuk* is structured around the relationship between the witness and the party who has standing to demand testimony. A servant without *harsha'a*, without formal power of attorney, has no such standing. Neither does the defendant: the *nitba* cannot place the witnesses under oath on behalf of the *tove'a*. It is worth noting that the exclusion of a servant is not absolute — had the servant come with proper *harsha'a*, the situation would be different. But absent authorization, neither an agent nor the opposing party can fulfill what the *Torah* requires of the plaintiff himself.

## Oath Language

### Binding Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי (אֲנִי) עֲלֵיכֶם״; ״מְצַוֶּה אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם״; ״אוֹסֶרְכֶם אֲנִי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna discusses the formula of an oath of testimony. If the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify if you do not come and testify on my behalf, or even if he said: **I command you,** or **I bind you,** although he did not employ an unequivocal formula of an oath, **these** witnesses **are liable** for taking a false oath of testimony.

The *mishna* now shifts to a closely related topic: the precise language of an oath — what words are binding, and what words are not. The *mishna* states: *mashbia, metzaveh, osrechem — harei elu chayavin*. If one uses any of these three formulas — I administer an oath upon you, I command you, I forbid you — the witnesses are liable should they deny their testimony.

### Non-Binding Phrases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין. ״בְּאָלֶף דָּלֶת״; ״בְּיוֹד הֵי״; ״בְּשַׁדַּי״; ״בִּצְבָאוֹת״; ״בְּחַנּוּן וְרַחוּם״; ״בְּאֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״; ״בְּרַב חֶסֶד״; וּבְכָל הַכִּנּוּיִין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** If one administered the oath to the witnesses **in the** name of **heaven and in the** name of **earth, these** witnesses are **exempt** from liability for taking a false oath of testimony, as that is not an oath in the name of God. If one administered the oath to the witnesses **in** the name of ***alef dalet*,** i.e., *Adonai*; **in** the name of ***yod heh*,** the Tetragrammaton; **in** the name of the **Almighty [*Shaddai*]; in** the name of the Lord of **Hosts [*Tzevaot*]; in** the name of the **Gracious and Compassionate** One; **in** the name of He Who is **Slow to Anger; in** the name of He Who is **Abounding in Loving-kindness; or in** the name of **any of the appellations** of God, even though he did not mention the ineffable name of God, **these** witnesses are **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony.

By contrast, *bashamayim uva'aretz* — invoking heaven and earth does not constitute a binding oath.

### God's Names

The *mishna* then lists the divine names and titles that do render an oath binding: *alef-dalet* (the name *Adon*), *yud-heh* (the divine name), *Shaddai*, *Tzva'ot*, *Chanun veRachum*, *Erech Apayim*, *Rav Chesed* — and all *kinuyim*, all appellations. Invoking any of these, *harei elu chayavin*. This immediately raises a question the *gemara* will take up: *alef-dalet*, the word *adon*, is an ordinary word in Hebrew — one can say *adon* in a grocery store and simply mean "sir." The question of when this word constitutes invocation of God's name and when it is mundane will require further analysis.

## Curses & Names

### Cursing Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְקַלֵּל בְּכוּלָּן – חַיָּיב; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **One who curses** God **employing any of** these names or appellations of God is **liable** to be executed through stoning; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt,** as they hold that one is liable for cursing God only if he employs the ineffable name of God.

Since we are dealing with the power of God's names in oaths, the *mishna* addresses an adjacent question: cursing using God's name. *Ha'mikalel bekulam chayav, divrei Rabbi Meir; ve'Chachamim potrin* — one who curses using all of these names is liable according to *Rabbi Meir*, while the *Chachamim* exempt him. The case at issue is whether the *kinuyim* and divine attributes, not just the explicit names, render a curse actionable.

### Self & Family Cursing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ בְּכוּלָּן – חַיָּיב; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **One who curses his father or his mother employing any of** these names or appellations of God is **liable** to be executed through stoning; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt,** as they hold that one is liable for cursing his father and his mother only if he employs the ineffable name of God.

The *mishna* continues: *ha'mikalel aviv ve'imo bekulam chayav divrei Rabbi Meir, ve'Chachamim potrin* — if one curses his father or mother using all of these names, *Rabbi Meir* holds him liable and *Chachamim* exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְקַלֵּל עַצְמוֹ וַחֲבֵירוֹ בְּכוּלָּן – עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. ״יַכְּכָה ה׳ אֱלֹהִים״, וְכֵן ״יַכְּכָה אֱלֹהִים״ – זוֹ הִיא אָלָה הַכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who curses himself or another employing any of** these names or appellations of God **violates a prohibition.** If one says: **The Lord God shall strike you** (see Deuteronomy 28:22), **and likewise** if one says: **God shall strike you** if you do not come to testify, **that is a curse that is written in the Torah,** and in such a case one is certainly liable if he fails to testify.

And: *ha'mikalel atzmo ve'chavero be'chulan over be'lo ta'aseh* — one who curses himself or his fellow transgresses a prohibition in all cases. The *gemara* will supply the relevant scriptural source, drawn from the *klalot* of *Ki Tavo* (Devarim 28:22): *yakecha Hashem Elohim* — and by extension: *al yakecha, ve'yivarechecha, ve'yeitiv lach* — whether the formulation is a direct curse or its inverse, *Rabbi Meir* holds one liable, while *Chachamim* exempt.

## Mashbia Formula

### Rav Yehuda Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם״ – מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם בִּשְׁבוּעָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּתּוֹרָה״; ״מְצַוֶּה אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם בְּצַוָּואָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּתּוֹרָה״; ״אוֹסֶרְכֶם אֲנִי בְּאִיסּוּר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you,** I command you, or I bind you, these witnesses are liable for taking a false oath of testimony. The Gemara asks: **What is** the plaintiff **saying?** He merely said that he is administering an oath, but he did not say in the name of what he is administering the oath. **Rav Yehuda said: This** is what the plaintiff **is saying: I administer an oath to you with the oath mentioned in the Torah, I command you with the command mentioned in the Torah, I bind you with the bond mentioned in the Torah,** and in the Torah all of these include mention of the name of God.

The *gemara* now probes the *mishna*'s opening words — *mashbia ani aleichem, metzaveh ani aleichem, osrechem* — asking what exactly is being said. *Amar Rav Yehuda*: this is what the formula means: I administer an oath upon you in the manner of the oath spoken in the *Torah*; I command you in the manner of the command spoken in the *Torah*; I forbid you in the manner of the prohibition spoken in the *Torah*. According to *Rav Yehuda*, each formulation is understood as a reference to a specific category in the *Torah*.

### Abaye's Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, אֶלָּא הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: ״כּוֹבֶלְכֶם אֲנִי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין; כּוֹבֵל בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מִי כְּתִיב?! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם בִּשְׁבוּעָה״; ״מְצַוֶּה אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם בִּשְׁבוּעָה״; ״אוֹסֶרְכֶם אֲנִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה״; ״כּוֹבֶלְכֶם אֲנִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yehuda: **But** according to that interpretation, concerning **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches,** that if the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I chain you, these** witnesses are **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony, **is chaining written in the Torah?** Apparently, the reference in the mishna and the *baraita* is not to similar language in the Torah. **Rather, Abaye said** that **this** is what the plaintiff **is saying: I administer an oath to you with an oath, I command you with an oath, I bind you with an oath, I chain you with an oath.** The witnesses are liable in all these cases provided that the plaintiff mentions an oath.

*Abaye* challenges this. He points to a *baraita* in which *Rabbi Chiya* adds a fourth term — *kovelachem*, I bind you, from the root *kevel*, a chain or rope. The question is: *ve'hai kovel be'Oraita michtiv?* — where does *kovel* appear in the *Torah* as a binding formula? Since no such term appears in the *Torah*, *Rav Yehuda*'s framework — that each word points to its counterpart in the *Torah* — cannot accommodate *Rabbi Chiya*'s addition.

*Abaye* therefore reframes the entire formula: *mashbia ani aleichem bishvu'ah, metzaveh ani aleichem bishvu'ah, oserchem ani bishvu'ah, kovel ani bishvu'ah* — in each case, the operative word is *shvu'ah*, and the other terms — commandment, prohibition, binding — all qualify as invocations of oath-power. The witnesses are liable in all these formulations because in each case a *shvu'ah* is being administered. Whether this fully resolves the analytical difficulty is another question, but the practical conclusion is clear: if the word *shvu'ah* underlies any of these formulas, the witnesses are bound.

## Erasable Names

### Names vs Descriptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּאָלֶף דָּלֶת״; ״בְּיוֹד הֵי״; ״בְּשַׁדַּי״; ״בִּצְבָאוֹת״; ״בְּחַנּוּן וְרַחוּם״; ״בְּאֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״; ״בְּרַב חֶסֶד״. לְמֵימְרָא דְּחַנּוּן וְרַחוּם שֵׁמוֹת נִינְהוּ?! וּרְמִינְהִי: יֵשׁ שֵׁמוֹת שֶׁנִּמְחָקִין, וְיֵשׁ שֵׁמוֹת שֶׁאֵין נִמְחָקִין – אֵלּוּ הֵן שֵׁמוֹת שֶׁאֵין נִמְחָקִין: כְּגוֹן ״אֵל״, ״אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, ״אֱלֹהִים״, ״אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״, ״אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה״, ״אָלֶף דָּלֶת״ וְ״יוֹד הֵי״, ״שַׁדַּי״, ״צְבָאוֹת״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֵין נִמְחָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one administered the oath to the witnesses **in** the name of ***alef dalet*, in** the name of ***yod heh*, in** the name of the **Almighty [*Shaddai*], in** the name of the Lord of **Hosts [*Tzevaot*], in** the name of the **Gracious and Compassionate** One, **in** the name of He Who is **Slow to Anger,** or **in** the name of He Who is **Abounding in Loving-kindness,** these witnesses are liable for taking a false oath of testimony. Is that **to say that gracious and compassionate are** sacred **names?** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **There are names** of God **that may be erased and there are names** of God **that may not be erased** due to their inherent sanctity. **These are names that may not be erased: For example,** several variations of the name God [*Elohim*]: ***El*, *Elohekha*** with a second person singular suffix, ***Elohim*, *Eloheikhem*** with a second person plural suffix; **I Shall Be As I Shall Be, *alef dalet*, *yod heh*, Almighty [*Shaddai*],** Lord of **Hosts [*Tzevaot*], these** names **may not be erased.**

The *mishna* listed both explicit divine names and *kinuyim* — appellations such as *Chanun veRachum* — as names that bind an oath. The *gemara* now challenges this by citing a contradiction: *yesh shemot she'nimchakim ve'yesh shemot she'ein nimchakim* — some names may be erased, and others may not. The *gemara* is thus not merely asking whether *Chanun veRachum* is a name or a description, but more precisely: what may be erased from a sacred text, and what must be preserved?

### Unerasable List

The *baraita* provides the definitive list of names that may not be erased: *El, Elohecha, Elohim, Eloheichem, Ehyeh asher Ehyeh, alef-dalet, yud-heh, Shaddai, Tzva'ot — harei elu ein nimchakim*. This list reflects the *halacha* as we observe it to this day.

### Erasable List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל ״הַגָּדוֹל״, ״הַגִּבּוֹר״, ״הַנּוֹרָא״, ״הָאַדִּיר״ וְ״הֶחָזָק״ וְ״הָאַמִּיץ״, ״הָעִזּוּז״, ״חַנּוּן וְרַחוּם״, ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״ וְ״רַב חֶסֶד״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נִמְחָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **But** adjectives that describe the Holy One, Blessed be He, e.g., **the Great, the Mighty, the Awesome, the Prodigious, the Powerful, the Courageous, the Strong, gracious, compassionate, slow to anger, or abounding in loving-kindness; these may be erased.** Apparently, gracious and compassionate are adjectives and not actual names of God; how, then, does an oath or a curse in the name of gracious and compassionate take effect?

By contrast, the descriptive attributes — *ha'gadol, ha'gibor, ha'nora, ha'adir, ve'ha'chazak, ve'ha'amitz, ve'ha'izuz, Chanun, veRachum, Erech Apayim, veRav Chesed* — these may all be erased, *harei elu nimchakim*, because they describe qualities rather than name the divine essence.

### Abaye's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַתְנִיתִין ״בְּמִי שֶׁהוּא חַנּוּן״, ״בְּמִי שֶׁהוּא רַחוּם״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In **the mishna,** it is with regard to one who administers an oath or curses **in** the name of **He Who is Gracious,** or **in** the name of **He Who is compassionate,** that the *tanna* **is stating** the *halakha*. Although gracious and compassionate are not names of God, the reference in the mishna is to an oath in the name of God.

*Abaye* explains: when the *mishna* includes *Chanun veRachum* among the binding names, it means *be'mi shehu chanun, be'mi shehu rachum* — invoking "He who is gracious, He who is compassionate," understood as pointing to God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, ״בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ״ נָמֵי – ״בְּמִי שֶׁהַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ שֶׁלּוֹ״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **If so,** in the case of one who administered the oath to the witnesses **in the** name of **heaven and in the** name of **earth as well,** say that it is with regard to an oath **in** the name of **He for Whom the heaven and the earth are His** that the *tanna* **is stating** the *halakha*. Why, then, does the mishna say that for an oath in the name of heaven and in the name of earth, these witnesses are exempt from liability?

*Rava* immediately presses: if that is the logic, then *bashamayim uva'aretz* — "by heaven and earth" — should also be understood as invoking "He whose heaven and earth it is," and should likewise be binding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא דְּאִיקְּרִי רַחוּם וְחַנּוּן – וַדַּאי ״בְּמִי שֶׁהוּא חַנּוּן״, וַדַּאי ״בְּמִי שֶׁהוּא רַחוּם״ קָאָמַר. הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ – ״בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There, since there is no other entity that is called gracious and compassionate, certainly** it is **in** the name of **He Who is gracious,** and **certainly** it is **in** the name of **He Who is compassionate** that the *tanna* **is speaking.** By contrast, **here, since there are heaven and earth** that exist as independent entities, perhaps when he administers an oath in the name of heaven and in the name of earth, it is **in the** name of the actual **heaven and in the** name of the actual **earth** that **he is speaking,** and not in the name of He for Whom the heaven and the earth are His.

The *gemara* distinguishes: *Chanun veRachum* can refer to nothing other than God — nothing else is called gracious and compassionate in this sense — and so the reference is unambiguous. Heaven and earth, however, exist as independent entities in their own right; one might construe an oath "by heaven and earth" as referring to those entities rather than to their Creator. Therefore *bashamayim uva'aretz* remains exempt.

This distinction might strike one as somewhat forced — everything in existence belongs to God, and it borders on a kind of conceptual heresy to treat heaven and earth as standing independently of their Creator. Yet the *halacha* draws this line: the concern is with how a speaker might be understood, and since heaven and earth can be conceived as independent referents, an oath invoking them is not sufficiently anchored to the divine name to be binding.

## Letter Combinations

### Alef-Lamed, Yud-Hey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כָּתַב אָלֶף לָמֶד מֵ״אֱלֹהִים״, ״יָהּ״ מֵ״יְיָ״ – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ נִמְחָק. שִׁין דָּלֶת מִ״שַּׁדַּי״, אָלֶף דָּלֶת מֵ״אֲדֹנָי״, צָדִי בֵּית מִ״צְּבָאוֹת״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִמְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the names of God that may be erased and those that may not be erased, the Gemara discusses the details of the matter. **The Sages taught:** If **one wrote** the letters ***alef lamed* from** the name ***Elohim*,** or ***yod heh* from the Tetragrammaton, this** pair of letters and that pair of letters **may not be erased.** But if one wrote the letters ***shin dalet* from *Shaddai*,** or ***alef dalet* from *Adonai*,** or ***tzadi beit* from *Tzevaot*, this may be erased.**

A *baraita* addresses written letters: *katav alef-lamed me'Elohim, yud-heh me'Hashem — harei ze eino nimchak*. If one writes the letters *alef-lamed* with the intention of writing God's name, or *yud-heh* with the intention of writing the divine name, that text may not be erased. The key qualifier is intent. One might note that *alef-lamed* appears constantly in ordinary Hebrew — *ani holech el ha'cheder* — and *yud-heh* begins many names entirely unrelated to God. The ruling therefore is specifically conditioned on the writer's intention to inscribe a divine name.

### Shin-Dalet, Alef-Dalet

The *baraita* continues: *shin-dalet me'Shaddai, alef-dalet me'Adonai, tzadi-beit me'Tzva'ot*. Here the situation is somewhat different. *Alef-lamed* and *yud-heh* are themselves recognized divine names, which is why intent alone is decisive. But *shin-dalet* standing alone spells *sheid* — a demon — which is decidedly not a divine name. Similarly, *alef-dalet* alone means *eid* — as in *ve'eid ya'aleh min ha'aretz* — a vapor or mist, an entirely mundane word. The partial letters thus carry their own independent non-divine meanings, which makes the question of intent even more pointed in these cases.

### Tzvaot Dependency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״צְבָאוֹת״ כּוּלּוֹ נִמְחָק – שֶׁלֹּא נִקְרָא צְבָאוֹת אֶלָּא עַל שֵׁם יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוֹצֵאתִי אֶת צִבְאֹתַי אֶת עַמִּי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם״. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** The word ***tzevaot* may be erased** in **its entirety, as** God **is called *Tzevaot* only in the context of** the children of **Israel,** and it is not an independent divine appellation. It is derived **as it is stated: "And I shall bring forth My hosts [*tzivotai*], My people the children of Israel, out of the land of Egypt"** (Exodus 7:4). The *armies* in question are the armies of Israel; the divine name is therefore contingent on that context and does not stand as an independent divine appellation. **Shmuel, however, rules that *ein halacha ke'Rabbi Yosei*** — the law does not follow *Rabbi Yosei* — without elaboration, and so we do not accept this position.

*Rabbi Yosei* goes further: even the complete word *Tzva'ot* in its entirety may be erased, *kulo nimchak*, because the word *Tzva'ot* acquired its divine association only through its connection to Israel: *shene'emar ve'hotzeiti et tzivotai et ammi Benei Yisrael me'eretz Mitzrayim* (Shemot). The *armies* in question are the armies of Israel; the divine name is therefore contingent on that context and does not stand as an independent divine appellation. *Shmuel*, however, rules that *ein halacha ke'Rabbi Yosei* — the law does not follow *Rabbi Yosei* — without elaboration, and so we do not accept this position.

## Name Affixes

### Prefixes Erasable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַטָּפֵל לַשֵּׁם, בֵּין מִלְּפָנָיו וּבֵין מִלְּאַחֲרָיו – הֲרֵי זֶה נִמְחָק. לְפָנָיו כֵּיצַד? ״לַייָ׳״ – ל׳ נִמְחָק; ״בַּייָ׳״ – ב׳ נִמְחָק; ״וַייָ׳״ – ו׳ נִמְחָק; ״מֵיְיָ׳״ – מ׳ נִמְחָק; ״שֶׁיְיָ׳״ – ש׳ נִמְחָק; ״הַיְיָ׳״ – ה׳ נִמְחָק; ״כַּייָ׳״ – כ׳ נִמְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Any** letters **ancillary to the name** of God, **whether** as a prefix **preceding** the name **or** as a suffix **succeeding** the name, **this** addition **may be erased. Preceding it, how so?** If one wrote the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***lamed*,** meaning: To the Lord, the ***lamed* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***beit*,** meaning: By the Lord, the ***beit* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***vav*,** meaning: And the Lord, the ***vav* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***mem*,** meaning: From the Lord, the ***mem* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***shin*,** meaning: That the Lord, the ***shin* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***heh*,** meaning: Is the Lord, the ***heh* may be erased;** the **Tetragrammaton** with the prefix ***kaf*,** meaning: Like the Lord, the ***kaf* may be erased.**

The *gemara* addresses prepositions and other letters attached to God's name: *kol ha'tefel la'Shem bein milfanav uvein mil'acharav harei zeh nimchak* — anything appended to God's name, whether before it or after it, may be erased. The examples of prefixes: *la'Shem* — the *lamed* may be erased; *ba'Shem* — the *beit* may be erased; *ve'Hashem* — the *vav* may be erased; *me'Hashem* — the *mem* may be erased; *she'Hashem* — the *shin* may be erased; *ha'Shem* — the *heh* may be erased; *ke'Hashem* — the *kaf* may be erased. The prefix is grammatically attached but remains external to the name itself.

### Suffixes Erasable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחֲרָיו כֵּיצַד? ״אֱלֹהֵינוּ״ – נוּ נִמְחָק, ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״ – הֶם נִמְחָק, ״אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ – כֶם נִמְחָק. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: לְאַחֲרָיו אֵינוֹ נִמְחָק, שֶׁכְּבָר קִדְּשׁוֹ הַשֵּׁם. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Succeeding it, how so?** If one wrote ***Eloheinu*,** meaning: Our God, the ***nun vav*** suffix **may be erased; *Eloheihem*,** meaning: Their God, the ***heh mem*** suffix **may be erased; *Eloheikhem*,** meaning: Your God, second person plural, the ***kaf mem*** suffix **may be erased. *Aḥerim* say:** The suffix **succeeding** the name of God **may not be erased as the name** of God to which it is appended **already sanctified it** and it is considered as though it is part of the name. **Rav Huna says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of ***Aḥerim*.**

Similarly for suffixes: *Eloheinu* — the *nu* may be erased; *Eloheihem* — the *hem* may be erased; *Eloheichem* — the *chem* may be erased. The possessive suffix is likewise external to the divine name proper.

### Suffix Sanctity

However, *acherim omrim le'acharav eino nimchak shekevar kidsho ha'Shem* — others say that what follows God's name may not be erased, because God's name has already sanctified it. And *Rav Huna* rules: *halacha ka'acherim* — the law follows this anonymous opinion. It is worth noting that the *gemara* does not identify *acherim* by name here, and a comment is made that this is one of the places where *Rabbi Meir*'s opinions were attributed to *acherim* following a dispute with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, as discussed in *Horayot*.

## Abraham & God's Names

### Abraham Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אַבְרָהָם, דְּלָטְיָא, לְנָבוֹת, בְּגִבְעַת בִּנְיָמִן, שְׁלֹמֹה, דָּנִיאֵל – סִימָן) כׇּל שֵׁמוֹת הָאֲמוּרִים בַּתּוֹרָה בְּאַבְרָהָם – קֹדֶשׁ; חוּץ מִזֶּה שֶׁהוּא חוֹל – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמַר, אֲדֹנָי אִם נָא מָצָאתִי חֵן בְּעֵינֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abraham; who cursed Naboth; in Gibeah of Benjamin; Solomon; Daniel;** this is **a mnemonic** for the *halakhot* that follow. **All names** that could be understood as the name of God **that are stated in the Torah with regard to Abraham** are **sacred** and are referring to God, **except for this** name, **which is non-sacred, as it is stated: "My lords, if I have found favor in your eyes"** (Genesis 18:3). In that passage, Abraham is addressing the angels who appeared to him in the guise of men, not God.

The *gemara* now embarks on a sustained analysis of instances in *Tanakh* where God's name appears in a context that could be read as referring either to God or to a human figure. The *siman* for this series of passages is: *Avraham, deLotia, leNavot, beGivat Binyamin, Shlomo, Daniel*. The first principle: *kol shemot ha'amurim ba'Torah be'Avraham kodesh, chutz mizeh she'hu chol* — all the divine names that appear in the *Torah* in the context of Avraham are *kodesh*, sacred, except for one instance which is *chol*, mundane.

### Adonim Ambiguity

That one case is found in the opening of *Vayera*: the word that in many texts appears spelled *yud-yud* — indicating *Hashem* — but which in the *Torah* itself reads *alef-dalet-nun-yud*, in the plural: *Adonim*, my masters. The context is *im na matzati chein be'einecha* — "if I have found favor in your eyes." This is the moment when Avraham encounters the three angels. Grammatically, the word is plural — *my masters*, a perfectly ordinary Hebrew honorific, the kind one might use addressing a group of gentlemen. And yet the suffix *einecha* — "your eyes" — is singular. The sentence begins in the plural and resolves into the singular. This grammatical oscillation between plural address and singular reference is striking.

One might note that this kind of shift is in fact common throughout the *Torah* — a plural entity is addressed in the singular or vice versa, sometimes because of the unity of the group, sometimes because the speaker turns to address the chief of a delegation. The classic *Talmudic* example here — that Avraham turns to address the most significant of the three visitors — is one reading, but the grammatical phenomenon itself appears widely in biblical literature.

### Abraham Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲנִינָא בֶּן אֲחִי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי אָמְרוּ: אַף זֶה קֹדֶשׁ. כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: גְּדוֹלָה הַכְנָסַת אוֹרְחִין יוֹתֵר מֵהַקְבָּלַת פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה – כְּמַאן? כְּאוֹתוֹ הַזּוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥanina, son of the brother of Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya in the name of Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i, say: This too** is **sacred.** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Hospitality** accorded to **guests is greater than receiving the Divine Presence? In accordance with whose** opinion is that statement? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **that pair** of *tanna'im*, Ḥanina, son of the brother of Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who understood that Abraham was speaking to God.

*Chanina ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua* and *Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* in the name of *Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i* hold that even this instance is *kodesh* — Avraham was not addressing the angels but speaking to God Himself. This reading aligns with the famous teaching: *amar Rav Yehudah amar Rav, gedolah hachnasat orchim yoter mehakbalat pnei ha'Shechinah* — hospitality toward guests is greater than receiving the *Shechinah*. That teaching depends on understanding that Avraham was in the midst of a divine encounter when the three men arrived, and that he said to God: wait for me while I attend to these guests. The *gemara* therefore notes: *keman azlah* — this teaching goes according to *Chanina* and *Rabbi Elazar*, who read the word as addressed to God. *Keman ke'oto hazug* — just like that pair of Sages.

## Lot's Address

### Lot Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֵׁמוֹת הָאֲמוּרִים בְּלוֹט – חוֹל; חוּץ מִזֶּה שֶׁהוּא קֹדֶשׁ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹט אֲלֵהֶם, אַל נָא אֲדֹנָי, הִנֵּה נָא מָצָא עַבְדְּךָ חֵן בְּעֵינֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״ – מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ לְהָמִית וּלְהַחֲיוֹת, זֶה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **All names** that could be understood as the name of God **that are stated** in the Torah **with regard to Lot** are **non-sacred** and are referring to angels, **except for this** one, **which is sacred, as it is stated: "And Lot said to them: Please, not so *Adonai*. Behold your servant has found favor in your eyes,** and you have magnified Your mercy that You have performed for me by saving my life" (Genesis 19:18–19). It is apparent from the context that Lot is addressing **He Who has the capacity to kill and to vivify; that is the Holy One, Blessed be He.**

*Kol shemot ha'amurim beLot chol, chutz mizeh she'hu kodesh* — all the divine names that appear in Lot's narrative are *chol*, mundane, except for one instance which is *kodesh*. In Lot's story, the names generally refer to the angels or to Lot's human interlocutors.

### Lot Escape

The one *kodesh* instance: *vayomer Lot aleihem, al na Adonai, hineh na matza avdecha chein be'einecha* (Bereshit 19:18). The angels have told Lot to flee to the mountain, but Lot demurs and requests to escape to a nearby city instead. The opening of the verse seems to indicate he is addressing the angels — *vayomer Lot aleihem*, he said to them — and yet the *gemara* rules this instance *kodesh*. The explanation: *mi sheyesh beyado lehamit ulehachayot zeh haKadosh Baruch Hu* — the one who has the power to give life and to take it is God alone. Since Lot is appealing to the power to save him, he must be addressing God, not an angel. The word *aleihem* is itself a difficulty the *gemara* does not fully dissolve — but based on the content of Lot's appeal, the *gemara* reads the name as directed to God.

## Navot & Micah

### Navot Sacred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל שֵׁמוֹת הָאֲמוּרִים בְּנָבוֹת – קֹדֶשׁ, בְּמִיכָה – חוֹל. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בְּנָבוֹת – קֹדֶשׁ, בְּמִיכָה – יֵשׁ מֵהֶן חוֹל וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן קֹדֶשׁ. אָלֶף לָמֶד – חוֹל, יוֹד הֵי – קֹדֶשׁ; חוּץ מִזֶּה, שֶׁאָלֶף לָמֶד וְהוּא קֹדֶשׁ: ״כׇּל יְמֵי הֱיוֹת בֵּית הָאֱלֹהִים בְּשִׁילֹה״.
>
> **Translation:** **All names that are stated with regard to Naboth** are **sacred,** e.g., in the verse: "Naboth blasphemed *Elohim* and the king" (I Kings 21:13), and those stated **with regard to Micah** are **non-sacred** and are referring to the graven image that he fashioned (see Judges, chapters 17–18). **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Indeed, all names that are stated **with regard to Naboth** are **sacred;** but those stated **with regard to Micah, some of them are non-sacred and some of them are sacred.** The names beginning with the letters ***alef lamed*,** i.e., *Elohim*, are **non-sacred,** as the reference is to the idol that he crafted, and all the names beginning with the letters ***yod heh*,** i.e., the Tetragrammaton, are **sacred, except for this** name that begins with the letters ***alef lamed* and it is sacred: "All the time that the house of *Elohim* was in Shiloh"** (Judges 18:31).

*Kol shemot ha'amurim beNavot kodesh, beMicha chol* — all the names in the Navot story (*Melachim Aleph*) are *kodesh*, while the names in the story of *Micha* (the figure from *Shoftim*) are *chol*. *Micha* built a private temple and priestly garments in competition with the legitimate service — a figure whose acts were sinful — and in that narrative the names refer to human or contextual usage. By contrast, the names in the story of *Navot HaYizre'eli*, whose vineyard Achav sought to seize, are all sacred.

### Micha Qualification

*Rabbi Eliezer* adds a refinement for the case of *Micha*: *beNavot kodesh; beMicha yesh mehem chol veyesh mehem kodesh — alef-lamed chol, yud-heh kodesh*. In *Micha*'s narrative, *alef-lamed* is mundane while *yud-heh* is sacred. The one exception where *alef-lamed* in *Micha*'s story would be *kodesh* is the phrase *kol yemei heyot beit ha'elohim be'shilo* — "all the days that the house of God was in Shilo" — because here the reference is to the *Mishkan* at Shilo, an unambiguously sacred context.

## Gibeah Promise

### Gibeah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל שֵׁמוֹת הָאֲמוּרִים בְּגִבְעַת בִּנְיָמִין – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: חוֹל, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: קֹדֶשׁ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וְכִי מַבְטִיחַ וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה?!
>
> **Translation:** **All names that are stated in** the passage concerning **Gibeah of Benjamin,** where the rest of the tribes consulted God to determine whether they should go to war against the tribe of Benjamin (see Judges, chapter 20), **Rabbi Eliezer says:** They are **non-sacred,** as they were consulting an idol, not God. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** They are **sacred.** **Rabbi Eliezer said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: How can you say that those names are sacred? **Does** God **promise and not fulfill** the promise? Twice the tribes received the response to go to war against Benjamin, and twice they were vanquished.

The *gemara* introduces the dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* regarding the narrative of *Givat Binyamin* (*Shoftim* 20 — the tragic story of *pilegesh be'giv'ah*, where a concubine was brutalized, the dismembered portions sent to the twelve tribes, and a devastating civil war erupted between Israel and the tribe of Binyamin). *Rabbi Eliezer* says the names in this passage are *chol*; *Rabbi Yehoshua* says they are *kodesh*. *Rabbi Eliezer* presses *Rabbi Yehoshua*: *vechi mavtiach ve'eino oseh?* — would God promise and not fulfill? If the names are *kodesh*, they represent divine guarantees — and those guarantees were apparently not honored in the early battles.

### Yehoshua Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַה שֶּׁהִבְטִיחַ עָשָׂה; וְהֵם לֹא בִּיחֲנוּ אִם לִנְצוֹחַ אִם לִנָּצֵחַ. בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה שֶׁבִּיחֲנוּ – הִסְכִּימוּ עַל יָדָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּפִנְחָס בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַהֲרֹן (הַכֹּהֵן) עֹמֵד לְפָנָיו בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם לֵאמֹר, הַאוֹסִף עוֹד לָצֵאת לַמִּלְחָמָה עִם [בְּנֵי] בִנְיָמִן אָחִי אִם אֶחְדָּל וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said to** Rabbi Eliezer: **That which** God **promised, He fulfilled.** In each case, He responded to their question. The first time they consulted God through the *Urim VeTummim*, **but they did not seek to ascertain if** they are **to triumph** in the war or **if** they are **to be defeated. In the last** time that they consulted God through the *Urim VeTummim*, **where they sought to ascertain** whether they would emerge triumphant, **they consented** in Heaven **to their** endeavor, **as it is stated: "And Pinehas, son of Elazar, son of Aaron was standing before it in those days, saying: Shall I yet again go out to battle against the children of Benjamin my brother, or shall I cease?** And the Lord said: Go up, as tomorrow I will deliver them into your hand" (Judges 20:28).

*Rabbi Yehoshua* counters that the names are indeed *kodesh*, and that the problem lay not with God but with the people. *Ve'hem lo bachan im lintoach o lenatzeach* — they did not properly inquire whether they would emerge victorious. They went out to battle without asking the right question.

The proof: *be'acharonah she'bachanu hiskimu al yadam* — in the final campaign, when they inquired properly, the answer came. The verse (*Shoftim* 20): *u'Pinchas ben Elazar ben Aharon ha'kohen omed lefanav ba'yamim ha'hem lemor: ha'osif od latzet la'milchamah im Binyamin achi im echdal* — Pinchas son of Elazar son of Aharon the priest stood before God in those days and asked: shall we continue to go out to battle against Binyamin our brother, or shall we desist? This was a complete and proper inquiry through the *Urim veTumim*, and a complete answer came. Only in this third campaign, when the question was properly formulated and directed through the high priest, did God's response align with the outcome. Therefore the names in the passage are *kodesh* — they reflect genuine divine engagement, contingent on proper human inquiry.

## Shlomo & Names

### Shlomo Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל ״שְׁלֹמֹה״ הָאֲמוּרִין בְּשִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים – קֹדֶשׁ, שִׁיר לְמִי שֶׁהַשָּׁלוֹם שֶׁלּוֹ; חוּץ מִזֶּה: ״כַּרְמִי שֶׁלִּי לְפָנָי הָאֶלֶף לְךָ שְׁלֹמֹה״ – שְׁלֹמֹה לְדִידֵיהּ, ״וּמָאתַיִם לְנֹטְרִים אֶת פִּרְיוֹ״ – רַבָּנַן. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים, אַף זֶה חוֹל: ״הִנֵּה מִטָּתוֹ שֶׁלִּשְׁלֹמֹה שִׁשִּׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** **All** mentions of the name **Shlomo that are stated in the Song of Songs,** such as: "The song of songs that is Shlomo's" (Song of Songs 1:1), are not references to King Solomon; rather, they are **sacred,** meaning **a song to** the **One for Whom peace [*shehashalom*] is His, except for this** mention: **"My vineyard, which is mine, is before me; you, Solomon shall have the one thousand,"** i.e., one thousand are **for Solomon himself; "and two hundred for those who guard its fruit"** (Song of Songs 8:12), which is a reference to **the Sages. And some say: This** verse **too is non-sacred: "Behold, the bed of Solomon; sixty** mighty men are around it" (Song of Songs 3:7).

*Kol Shlomo ha'amurim be'Shir ha'Shirim kodesh, shir le'mi she'ha'shalom shelo* — every instance of *Shlomo* in *Shir ha'Shirim* is *kodesh*, a song to the One to whom peace belongs, since *Shalom* is one of the names of God and *Shlomo* contains that name. One exception: *karmi sheli lefanai, ha'elef lecha Shlomo* — my vineyard is before me, the thousand is yours, *Shlomo*. That refers specifically to King Shlomo, and is *chol*.

The *gemara* also considers *ve'yesh omrim af zeh chol*: the verse *hineh mitato she'lishomo shishim* — behold the bed of *Shlomo*, sixty — might also be *chol* according to some.

### One-Sixth Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף זֶה – וְלָא מִיבְּעֵי הַאיְךְ?! אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַלְכוּתָא דְּקָטְלָא חַד מִשִּׁיתָּא בְּעָלְמָא לָא מִיעַנְשָׁא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּרְמִי שֶׁלִּי לְפָנָי הָאֶלֶף לְךָ שְׁלֹמֹה״ – לְמַלְכוּתָא דִרְקִיעָא, ״וּמָאתַיִם לְנֹטְרִים אֶת פִּרְיוֹ״ – לְמַלְכוּתָא דְּאַרְעָא; שְׁמוּאֵל לָא כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא וְלָא כְּיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: זֶה – קֹדֶשׁ; וְזֶה הוּא חוֹל – דְּמִטָּתוֹ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does this mean: **This** verse **too** is non-sacred, **and it is not necessary** to say that the verse cited earlier is non-sacred? **But that which Shmuel says: A monarchy that kills one of** every **six** individuals **in the world is not punished** for doing so, as that is the prerogative of a monarch, **as it is stated: "My vineyard, which is mine, is before me; you, Shlomo shall have the one thousand,"** this is a reference **to the monarchy of Heaven; "and two hundred for those who guard its fruit,"** this is a reference **to the monarchy of earth.** Of the 1,200 mentioned in the two parts of the verse, two hundred, or one-sixth, are the prerogative of the earthly monarch. **Shmuel,** who interprets the mention of Shlomo in this verse as referring to God, holds **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna* nor in accordance with** the opinion introduced with the term: **Some say.** Both *tanna'im* agree that the reference in the verse is to Solomon and not to the Holy One, Blessed be He. **Rather,** Shmuel cites a different version of the opinion introduced with the term: Some say, according to which **this** is what **it is saying. And some say: This** Shlomo that appears in the verse with regard to the one thousand is **sacred, and that** Shlomo that appears in the verse **with regard to the bed of** Solomon **is non-sacred, and** it is **Shmuel who states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion introduced with the term: **Some say.**

This discussion opens onto *Shmuel*'s ruling: *malchuta d'katla chad mi'shita b'alma lo mi'ansha* — a kingdom that takes one-sixth does not incur divine punishment. The proportion of a thousand to *Shlomo* and two hundred to the *notrim*, the guards who watch the fruit, is understood through this arithmetic: the two hundred plus the thousand total twelve hundred, and two hundred is one-sixth of twelve hundred. A kingdom may legitimately claim one-sixth of its subjects' productivity without transgressing.

The *gemara* also offers an alternative reading: the thousand goes to the heavenly kingdom, *malchuta d'rakia*, and the two hundred to the earthly kingdom, *malchuta d'ara*, with the *notrim* — the guardians — representing earthly governance. *Shmuel* aligns with *yesh omrim* on this question. The precise interplay between *Tanna Kamma* and *yesh omrim* — whether one *pasuk* or two are *chol*, and how to account for the numbers — remains in some tension, but *Shmuel*'s ruling orients us toward the *yesh omrim* position.

## Daniel & Malchim

### Daniel Mundane

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל מַלְכַיָּא הָאֲמוּרִים בְּדָנִיֵּאל – חוֹל; חוּץ מִזֶּה שֶׁהוּא קֹדֶשׁ: ״אַנְתְּ מַלְכָּא [מֶלֶךְ] מַלְכַיָּא דִּי אֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא, מַלְכוּתָא חִסְנָא וְתׇקְפָּא וִיקָרָא יְהַב לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **All kings that are stated with regard to Daniel** are **non-sacred, except for this** one, **which is sacred: "You, O king, king of kings, unto whom the God of heaven has given you the kingdom, the power, and the strength, and the glory"** (Daniel 2:37).

*Kol malchaya ha'amurim beDaniel chol* — most instances of *malka* (king) in *Daniel* are *chol*, referring to Nevuchadnezzar or other earthly rulers.

### Daniel Sacred

One exception is *kodesh*: *ant malka, melech malchaya, di elah shmaya, malchuta chasna ve'takpa ve'ykara yehav lach* — you, O king, king of kings, to whom the God of heaven has given kingdom, power, might, and glory. In this passage Daniel praises — but the object of the deepest praise is God, not the human king, and *yesh omrim* would extend this status to an additional verse.

### Ambiguous Address

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף זֶה קֹדֶשׁ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מָרִי חֶלְמָא לְשָׂנְאָךְ וּפִשְׁרֵהּ לְעָרָךְ״ – לְמַאן קָאָמַר? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לִנְבוּכַדְ נֶצַּר קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, שָׂנְאוֹתֵיהּ מַאי נִינְהוּ – יִשְׂרָאֵל; מֵילָט קָא לָיֵיט לְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל?! וְתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: שׂוֹנְאֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אִיכָּא, שׂוֹנְאֵי גוֹיִם לֵיכָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** **And some say: This too** is **sacred, as it is stated: "My Lord, the dream shall be for your enemy and its interpretation for your foe"** (Daniel 4:16). **To whom is** Daniel **saying** this? **If it enters your mind** that when Daniel says: "My lord," it is **to Nebuchadnezzar** that **he is saying it, his enemy, who are they?** They are the **Jewish people. Would** Daniel **curse the Jewish people?** **And the first *tanna*,** who understands that Daniel is referring to Nebuchadnezzar, **holds: Are there Jewish enemies** for Nebuchadnezzar and **there are no gentile enemies** for him? Daniel was cursing the gentile enemies, not the Jewish enemies.

That additional verse: *mari chalma l'sanach u'fishreh l'arach* — my master, let the dream be for your enemies and its interpretation for your adversaries. *Yesh omrim* say this too is *kodesh* — Daniel was addressing God. The difficulty with reading it as addressed to Nevuchadnezzar is: *sinata mai ninhu?* — who would be Nevuchadnezzar's enemies? If Daniel were speaking to Nevuchadnezzar, he would be saying that the terrible dream should befall Nevuchadnezzar's enemies — which would be Israel. *Tanna Kamma* responds: Nevuchadnezzar had many enemies among the other nations; being an enemy of Nevuchadnezzar does not necessarily identify one as Israel. Therefore, the verse can plausibly be read as addressed to Nevuchadnezzar, and the word *malka* there remains *chol* according to the *Tanna Kamma*. *Yesh omrim* disagree and hold it is *kodesh*. The passage thus closes in the familiar mode of the *gemara* — an ambiguity preserved, two legitimate readings held in tension, the ruling to be determined elsewhere.
