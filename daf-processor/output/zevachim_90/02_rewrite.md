# Zevachim 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Holiness Principle

### Ma'aser vs. Birds

The daf picks up on 90b at the point where the *Mishnah* introduces a new organizing principle for determining precedence among sacrifices. Having earlier established that the more frequently offered sacrifice takes priority, the *Mishnah* now turns to the principle of holiness: the holier offering takes precedence. The *Gemara* will complicate this considerably, since holiness does not always fall along a single linear scale — different offerings possess different types of sanctity, and the *Gemara*'s general approach is to establish which feature of holiness is most salient, even when the resulting hierarchy seems counterintuitive.

The *Mishnah* states that *ma'aser behemah* — the animal tithe — comes before birds (*ofot*). The presumption is that mammals are more significant than birds as sacrificial animals. Rashi adds that the reason includes not only the general weight of mammal offerings, which constitute the bulk of all *korbanot*, but also the fact that in a *zevach* — a slaughtered offering — the *imurim*, the fats and entrails, are also placed on the *Mizbe'ach*, whereas by birds, only the blood goes on the *Mizbe'ach*.

### Adderaba Argument

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this with an *adderaba* — quite the contrary. Birds should come before *ma'aser behemah*, the *Gemara* argues, because birds are offered exclusively as either a *chatat* or an *olah*, both of which are *kodshei kodashim* — most holy offerings — whereas *ma'aser behemah* has the status of *shelamim*, which is *kodashim kalim*, a lesser degree of sanctity. If holiness determines precedence, birds should win.

The *Gemara* answers: *afillu hachi min zevach adif* — nevertheless, the fact that a *ma'aser behemah* is a *zevach*, a full slaughtered offering, is more decisive. Rashi explains that the *zevach* is the paradigmatic sacrificial act. And beyond that, as the *Mishnah* itself formulates it, a *ma'aser behemah* possesses *kodshei kodashim dama ve'imurim* — two elements that carry the status of *kodshei kodashim*: the blood and the *imurim*. Birds, by contrast, have only the blood on the *Mizbe'ach*.

### Imurim Status Debate

This formulation prompts Ravina bar Shila to articulate a specific halakhic position: *imurei kodashim kalim she'yatze'u lifnei zrikat dam psulim* — the *imurim* of a *kodashim kalim* offering that were taken outside the *Beit Hamikdash* before the *zerikat hadam* (the throwing of the blood on the altar) are rendered invalid.

To understand the weight of this claim, the background must be clear. *Kodashim kalim* offerings are fundamentally under the owner's control; they are not subject to the laws of *me'ilah* (the prohibition against misusing consecrated property). The one exception is the *imurim*, which are destined for the altar and, once the blood is thrown, carry full *kodshei kodashim* status. The question Ravina is addressing is: what is the status of the *imurim* in the window between *shechita* and *zerikat hadam*? Are they still treated as part of the *kodashim kalim* corpus — such that removing them from the *Beit Hamikdash* does not invalidate them — or have they already attained enough of a *kodshei kodashim* character that removal renders them *pasul*?

Ravina holds that they are already sufficiently in the *kodshei kodashim* category that taking them out invalidates them, even though there is still no *me'ilah* at this stage. The *korban* itself remains valid; the question is only whether the *imurim* can subsequently be placed on the *Mizbe'ach*.

The *Gemara* finds support for Ravina's position from the *Mishnah*'s own wording. When the *Mishnah* explains why *ma'aser behemah* outranks birds, it mentions both *dama ve'imurim* — the blood and the *imurim* — as *kodshei kodashim*. The mention of the blood is seemingly redundant: both birds and *ma'aser behemah* involve blood on the *Mizbe'ach*, so why mention it at all? The answer, via a *diyuk* — a textual inference — is that the *Mishnah* is coupling the blood and the *imurim* together to signal that they share the same legal status. Just as blood is fully consecrated to God even before *zerikat hadam* — such that it becomes *pasul* upon being taken outside — so too the *imurim*, even before the blood is thrown, carry enough sanctity that removal from the *Beit Hamikdash* invalidates them. The *Gemara* acknowledges this reading without fully embracing it, and proceeds to test Ravina's position through other sources.

### Meat Removal Debate

The *Gemara* attempts to bring a proof from the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning the meat (*basar*) of *kodashim kalim*. If the meat of a *shelamim* is taken out of the *Beit Hamikdash* before the blood is thrown, what is its status? Rabbi Yochanan says it remains valid — *kasher* — because *sofo latzeit*, it will eventually go out anyway once the blood is thrown and the owner takes the meat home. Reish Lakish says it is *pasul*, because *adayin lo higia zmano latzeit* — the time for it to leave has not yet arrived.

The logic behind Rabbi Yochanan's position is worth unpacking. A *shelamim* begins fundamentally as an offering under the owner's control. Unlike a *chatat*, which is fully consecrated to God from the outset, a *shelamim* returns to the owner's domain for eating after the blood rite. There is no *me'ilah* on the meat and no conceptual basis for treating it as *kodshei kodashim*. Where then would the prohibition on removing it before *zerikat hadam* come from? Rabbi Yochanan finds none — which is why he permits it. Reish Lakish, however, sees the pre-*zerikah* period as a distinct phase during which the meat must remain in place.

### Eliezer vs. Akiva

One might expect that if Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish debate the meat — and if the *imurim* are more clearly consecrated than the meat — then at least the *imurim* would be universally agreed to be *pasul* upon removal before *zerikat hadam*, confirming Ravina's position. The *Gemara* attempts this inference: *aval be'emurim lo* — but regarding the *imurim*, all agree they would become *pasul*. This would be a proof for Ravina.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference, noting that Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning — the meat is eventually going out — could in principle extend to the *imurim* as well, or rather that Rabbi Yochanan would simply maintain that neither the meat nor the *imurim* becomes *pasul* by being removed. The mention of the meat in the debate is specifically to showcase *kocho de'Reish Lakish* — the full scope of Reish Lakish's stringency: even meat that is destined to go out eventually, Reish Lakish holds, is *pasul* if removed before the blood is thrown.

The *Gemara* then turns to an explicit *tannaitic* dispute from *Masechet Me'ilah*: *imurei kodashim kalim she'yatze'u lifnei zrikat damam* — *imurim* of *kodashim kalim* that were removed before their blood was thrown. Rabbi Eliezer says there is no *me'ilah* applicable to them, and moreover, one is not liable for *pigul*, *nosar*, or *tum'ah* in relation to them. Since *pigul*, *nosar*, and *tum'ah* liability applies only to valid (*kasher*) items, the implication is that Rabbi Eliezer holds they have become *pasul*. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that *me'ilah*, *pigul*, *nosar*, and *tum'ah* do apply — meaning they remain valid in his view.

This looks like a direct *tannaitic* parallel to Ravina's debate: Rabbi Eliezer agrees with Ravina (removal before *zerikah* invalidates), and Rabbi Akiva disagrees.

### Rav Pappa Clarification

Rav Pappa, however, reframes the dispute entirely. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva, he argues, is not about whether the act of removal itself constitutes *psul*. In fact, everyone may agree that if you remove the *imurim* and bring them back before the blood is thrown, they are perfectly valid. The debate is about a different scenario: *de'isnehu avrai* — the *imurim* are still outside the *Beit Hamikdash* at the moment the blood is being thrown. Does the act of *zerikah* raise the status of the *imurim* while they are outside? Can the blood-throwing be *mo'el* — effective — for something that is not present inside the *Beit Hamikdash*?

This explains why the *tannaitic* debate is framed in terms of *me'ilah*, *pigul*, *nosar*, and *tum'ah* rather than *psul*. The *imurim* did not become *pasul* upon removal — that is not in dispute. The question is whether the *zerikah* reaches them in their external location and thereby elevates their status to trigger these liabilities. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *zerikah* is not *mo'el le'yotzei* — the blood-throwing is not effective for what is outside. Rabbi Akiva holds that it is.

The upshot of Rav Pappa's reading is that both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva actually reject Ravina's position. Neither tanna holds that removal before *zerikah* per se invalidates the *imurim*. The matter thus remains unresolved, and Ravina's view has no confirmed *tannaitic* support.

### Imurim vs. Bread

The *Gemara* notes that Rav Pappa himself applied the reverse logic in a parallel case involving the *shtei halechem* — the two loaves brought on Shavuot — when they are outside the *Beit Hamikdash* at the time the blood of the accompanying animal is thrown. In that context, Rav Pappa said the opposite: when the *shtei halechem* are outside, everyone agrees the *zerikah* does not reach them; the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva there concerns what happens when the bread was taken out and then brought back in before the *zerikah*.

The *Gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction: *hani milei gabei shtei halechem delav gufei dezivcha* — this distinction applies to the *shtei halechem* because the bread is an entirely separate entity from the animal being offered. It is inherently unusual for the *zerikah* of an animal's blood to have any legal effect on something as foreign as bread. Because the connection is tenuous to begin with, everyone agrees that the bread must be inside the *Beit Hamikdash* for the *zerikah* to be effective for it. If the bread is outside at that moment, the *zerikah* simply does not apply to it, and the debate is only about what happens when it is brought back in.

The *imurim*, by contrast, are *gufei dezivcha* — literally part of the animal itself. Because of this intrinsic connection, it is plausible that the *zerikah* could be effective for the *imurim* even when they are outside — which is precisely what Rabbi Akiva holds. The debates thus turn on different axes depending on whether we are dealing with an integral part of the animal or a conceptually separate item accompanying the sacrifice. Neither case resolves the question of Ravina's position, which the *Gemara* ultimately leaves open.

## Birds vs. Mincha

### Blood Priority

The *Mishnah* states that *ofot* — bird offerings — come before a *mincha* — a meal offering. The *Gemara* anticipates the *adderaba* argument: a *mincha* should come first, since it can be brought as a communal offering (*minchat tzibur*), such as the *shtei halechem* of Shavuot and the *omer*, which gives it greater institutional weight. The *Gemara* dismisses this: *afillu hachi minei damim adifei* — nevertheless, an offering that involves blood is considered more significant. The capacity for blood to effect *kaparah* — atonement — gives offerings involving blood a categorical advantage. These prioritizations are not always derivable from first principles; the *Mishnah* is making determinations about which features of a sacrifice carry the most weight, and blood comes out on top.

### Mincha Types

Having established the general ranking of birds over *mincha*, the *Mishnah* introduces a distinction within *mincha* itself. A *minchat chotei* — a meal offering brought for sin — takes precedence over a standard *minchat nedava* — a voluntary meal offering. This parallels the principle established earlier that a *chatat* comes before an *olah*, since the function of atoning for sin is given priority.

The *Gemara* is somewhat struck by this, and rightly so. A *minchat chotei* is notably plain — it contains no oil and no *levonah* (frankincense). A standard *minchat nedava*, by contrast, carries both. On purely formal grounds, the *minchat nedava* appears richer and more substantial. The *Gemara* raises the *adderaba*: the *minchat nedava* should come first precisely because it is *teinah shemen ulevonah* — it requires oil and frankincense. The *Gemara* responds that even so, the *minchat chotei* takes precedence because it effects *kaparah*. The same logic that drives a *chatat* to come before an *olah* applies here: the function of atonement overrides external markers of weight or richness.

This discussion touches on something worth pausing over. The halakhic focus throughout this entire chapter is on the blood as the instrument of *kaparah*, and yet the instinct — both from a reading of the *Torah*'s narrative and from the broader sacrificial literature — is to see the *olah*, the fully burnt offering, as the paradigmatic *korban*. It is the first *korban* type introduced in *Sefer Vayikra*, the *olat tamid* is brought every day, and the act of fully offering something up to God carries an intuitive sense of maximal devotion. The Ramban's approach to *korbanot* centers on the *chatat*, emphasizing the vicarious experience of judgment. Others might read the *olah* as the primary expression of devotion — the total giving of oneself back to God. The *Gemara* here resolves decisively in favor of the *chatat* and its atoning function, without diminishing the intuitive pull of the *olah*.

### Sota Mincha Status

The *Gemara* then raises an unresolved question: what is the ranking of a *minchat sota* — the meal offering brought by a woman suspected of adultery — relative to a standard *minchat nedava*?

On one side: the *minchat nedava* contains oil and frankincense, marking it as a more elaborate and weighty offering. On the other side: the *minchat sota*, while it does not effect *kaparah* in the strict sense, does serve a vital function — *ba'ah levarer avon*, it comes to clarify and determine a matter of sin, to restore *shalom* between husband and wife.

The *Mishnah*'s language, the *Gemara* notes, says that a *minchat chotei* comes first because *ba'ah al cheit* — it comes because of sin. Crucially, the text does not say *mishum de'hi mechaperet* — because it atones. And that subtly different formulation — "comes because of sin" rather than "atones for sin" — could include a *minchat sota* as well, since it too arises in the context of sin. The *Gemara* then examines a *baraita* that ranks one *mincha* over another on the grounds that one comes from wheat and one from barley — the *minchat sota* being uniquely made of barley, which the *Torah* describes as animal fodder. This would seem to imply the *minchat sota* is ranked lower. But the *Gemara* cannot determine from this whether it is being ranked below a *minchat chotei* or below a *minchat nedava*, since either comparison could have invoked the wheat-barley distinction. The question of where the *minchat sota* falls in the hierarchy is left unresolved.

## Chatat vs. Olah

### Bird Chatat First

The *Mishnah* states: *chatat ha'of kodemes le'olat ha'of* — a bird *chatat* comes before a bird *olah*. The *Gemara* asks: what is the source for this? It is answered from a *baraita* that cites the explicit verse in *Vayikra* 5:8, in the context of the *korban oleh ve'yored* — the variable cost offering — where someone who cannot afford an animal brings two birds, one as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*. The verse states: *ve'hikriv et asher la'chatat rishona* — he shall bring the one that is for the *chatat* first. And the subsequent verse makes the *olah* secondary: *ve'et hasheni ya'aseh olah kamishpat* — the second he shall make an *olah* according to the ordinance. The precedence of the *chatat* over the *olah* in this case is, remarkably, explicit in the *pessukim*.

### Chatat General Rule

The *Gemara* notes that this explicit verse appears redundant: if the second bird is an *olah*, the first is implicitly a *chatat*. Why then does the *Torah* explicitly say *rishona* — first? Because the extra formulation is not merely descriptive — it is generative. The verse is a *binyan av*, a prototype establishing a universal rule: *lechol chata'ot she'yikrevu le'olot haba'ot imahem* — for all *chata'ot* that are brought together with *olot*, the *chatat* comes first. The *Gemara* spells out the scope of this rule:

Whether it is a bird *chatat* before a bird *olah* — that is derived from the secondary implication of *ve'et hasheni*. Whether it is a mammal *chatat* before a mammal *olah* — that was established from an earlier verse about large animals. And *va'afilu chatat ha'of le'olat behemah* — even a bird *chatat* comes before a cow or sheep *olah*. This is the real *chiddush*, and it is derived from the *binyan av* of the extra *rishona* verse: a *chatat* comes first categorically, regardless of the species of the animal. Even if the *olah* is a large mammal and the *chatat* is a small bird, the *chatat* goes first.

### Eliezer Exception

Rabbi Eliezer, however, disputes the universality of this rule. He states: *kol makom she'nitchalfah chatat, chatat kodemes* — wherever a *chatat* was substituted (i.e., wherever the normal offering was a *chatat* and the variable-cost substitute involves a *chatat*), the *chatat* comes first. *Ve'khan be'yoledet olah kodemes* — but in the case of the woman who gave birth (*yoledet*), the *olah* comes first.

### Yoledis Case

The case of the *yoledet* presents a sharp test. A wealthy woman after childbirth brings an *olat behemah* — a mammal *olah* — and a *chatat ha'of* — a bird *chatat*. A poor woman brings an *olat ha'of* and a *chatat ha'of*. Rabbi Eliezer's position, as he articulates it in three parallel formulations, is that by the *yoledet*, the *olah* comes first.

His reasoning operates on three levels. First, the *chatat ha'of* here is not a substitute for a *chatat behemah* — unlike the *oleh ve'yored* case where the transition is from a large-animal *chatat* to a bird *chatat*, in the *yoledet* case the *chatat* was always a bird. The *olah*, however, is a large animal and thus weightier. Therefore, the *olah* takes precedence. Second, the *yoledet*'s offering is not brought for sin in the conventional sense — it is part of a *taharah* process, not atonement for transgression. When sin is involved, the *chatat* must come first to achieve atonement before the gift-offering of the *olah* can be presented. When no sin is at issue, that logic does not apply. Third, by the *oleh ve'yored*, both the *chatat* and the *olah* are substitutes for what would originally have been a *chatat behemah* alone — so the *chatat* carries the full weight of that substitution. By the *yoledet*, they are not both substitutes for a single *chatat*, and so again the larger animal's *olah* can come first.

In sum, Rabbi Eliezer holds that when the relevant conditions are present — the *chatat* is a substitute for another *chatat*, or the offering is sin-related — the *chatat* comes first. When those conditions are absent, the weightier animal takes precedence.

### Rava Reinterpretation

The *Gemara* does not ultimately accept Rabbi Eliezer's position at face value. Rava offers a reinterpretation: *lemikra hi hikdima hakatuv* — Rabbi Eliezer is not saying that the *olah* is actually *brought* first in the case of the *yoledet*. Rather, he is saying only that the *Torah*'s text mentions the *olah* first. Tosafot extends this to suggest that Rabbi Eliezer is speaking about the order of *sanctification* — which animal the woman should designate first as her *korban* — not the order in which they are actually offered. These are admittedly weak answers that seem to work against the plain reading of Rabbi Eliezer's words, but the *Gemara* adopts them to preserve the overarching rule: a *chatat* always comes before an *olah*, even a bird *chatat* before a mammal *olah*.

## Animal Size Priority

### Musaf Order

A new challenge to the supremacy of the *chatat-olah* hierarchy comes from the order of the *musaf* offerings. The *Mishnah* notes that cows come before rams, rams before sheep, sheep before goats, reflecting a hierarchy of animal species. One might ask: what type of *korban* are these? The goats in the *musaf* of *Sukkot* are, in fact, the *chatat*. Yet the listing moves through cows, rams, and sheep — all *olot* — before reaching the goat *chatat*. This would seem to indicate that the larger animal takes precedence over the smaller, regardless of whether it is a *chatat* or an *olah*.

The rationale given within the *Mishnah* for the animal hierarchy is as follows: cows come before rams because their accompanying *nesachim* — the flour and wine libations — are greater in quantity. Rams come before sheep for the same reason. Sheep come before goats even though their *nesachim* are equal, because sheep have an *alyah* — a fat tail — that in other offerings is placed on the *Mizbe'ach*, giving them an additional measure of weight.

The *Gemara* deflects the apparent challenge by suggesting that perhaps the *musaf* ordering is not about a *chatat* coming last — perhaps the debate concerns only different *olot* brought by different people, and the goats as a *chatat* are a separate matter not addressed by this ranking. But the *Gemara* is not fully comfortable with this evasion.

### Avodah Zarah Case

A sharper case arises from the *musaf* of *avodah zarah* — the communal sin offering brought when the *Beit Din* erred in a ruling involving idolatry. The *Gemara* cites a sequence: the ox of the *kohen gadol* for his sin offering comes before the ox for the communal *he'elam davar*; the ox for *he'elam davar* comes before the ox of *avodah zarah*. So far this tells us little, since all are *chatat* offerings.

But then: *par avodah zarah kodem lase'ir avodah zarah* — the ox of *avodah zarah* comes before the goat of *avodah zarah*. The ox of *avodah zarah* is an *olah*; the goat is a *chatat*. Here, explicitly, an *olah* of a larger animal comes before a *chatat* of a smaller animal. This appears to be precisely the proof that when species differ, size trumps the *chatat-olah* principle.

### Final Chatat Rule

The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: why not infer the opposite from the earlier part of the same sequence, where the *chatat* of the communal *he'elam davar* comes before the *olah* of *avodah zarah*? The *Gemara* responds: when the species is the same, everyone agrees the *chatat* comes first — that is not in dispute. The question is specifically whether a larger-species *olah* overrides a smaller-species *chatat*. And the case of *avodah zarah* seems to prove it does.

The *Gemara*, however, refuses to let this overturn the general rule.

### Avodah Zarah Exception

The scholars of the West (*amri be'ma'arava*, those in *Eretz Yisrael*) offer the resolution in the name of Rava bar Mari: the word *chatat* in the *avodah zarah* passage is written *chaser aleph* — without the letter *aleph* — which the *Gemara* understands as a textual demoting of its status. This orthographic irregularity signals that the *chatat* of *avodah zarah* occupies a diminished position and therefore does not claim the usual priority over the *olah*.

Ravina offers a complementary explanation: the *Torah* uses the phrase *kamishpat* — "according to the ordinance" — in the *avodah zarah* passage when listing the *olah* first, indicating that this ordering is an explicit and specific enactment for that case, overriding the general rule.

The conclusion from both explanations is that the general rule stands: a *chatat* always comes first, even a bird *chatat* before a cow *olah*. The *avodah zarah* case is an exception grounded in specific textual signals — the missing *aleph* and the use of *kamishpat* — and does not dislodge the universal principle.

The *Gemara* then notes that this same framework disposes of the earlier challenge from the *musaf* of *Sukkot*. Even granting that the *musaf* ordering places the goat *chatat* last after all the *olot*, this is not a counterexample to the general rule — it too falls under the *kamishpat* language of the *Torah*'s listing of the *musaf* offerings, which explicitly places the *chatat* at the end. An exception rooted in an explicit *Torah* sequence does not undermine the default halakha.

## Trilemma Resolution

### Circular Problem

The *Gemara* now raises an unresolved and logically vexing problem. Three offerings are in competition: *chatat ha'of* (a bird *chatat*), *olat behemah* (a mammal *olah*), and *ma'aser behemah* (the animal tithe). Each pair has an established ordering, but taken together they generate a circular contradiction.

A *chatat ha'of* comes before an *olat behemah* — this we have established: a *chatat* always comes first regardless of species. An *olat behemah* comes before *ma'aser behemah* — an *olah* outranks a *kodashim kalim* offering like *ma'aser*. So logically, one would conclude: *chatat ha'of* is first, *olat behemah* second, *ma'aser behemah* third. Simple enough.

But: *ma'aser behemah* comes before *chatat ha'of* — or so one might argue — because *ma'aser* is a *behemah*, a mammal, and in the species hierarchy, a mammal takes precedence over a bird. This is what the *Gemara* hesitates to simply override. If we grant that, the three offerings form a cycle: *chatat ha'of* before *olat behemah*, *olat behemah* before *ma'aser behemah*, and *ma'aser behemah* before *chatat ha'of*. There is no linear ordering consistent with all three pairwise relations simultaneously. The *Gemara* arrives at a genuine logical impasse.

### Package Solution

The resolution proposed by the scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* is elegant: *min zevach adif* — treat the *chatat ha'of* and the *olat behemah* as a package. The *chatat-olah* pair comes as a unit, and this unit takes precedence over the *ma'aser behemah* because the unit contains a *zevach*, a slaughtered mammal offering. The *olat behemah* within the package carries the unit past the *ma'aser behemah*, which is also a *behemah* but a *kodashim kalim*, less weighty than an *olah*. Within the package, the *chatat ha'of* retains its internal priority over the *olat behemah*.

The ordering thus becomes: first the *chatat ha'of* and *olat behemah* as a combined unit (with the *chatat ha'of* leading internally), and after them the *ma'aser behemah*. This avoids the circular problem by not requiring a direct *chatat ha'of* vs. *ma'aser behemah* comparison — the bird *chatat* never has to beat the *ma'aser* on its own merits as a bird against a mammal.

An alternative formulation offered in the *Gemara* is: *aydi de'ba'ah olat behemah be'chatat ha'of ve'agbah mi'ma'aser* — because the *olat behemah* comes along with the *chatat ha'of*, it pulls the entire package ahead of the *ma'aser*.

### Package Scope

A final question turns on how broadly to read the package principle. Does the priority of the *behemah* within the package — the recognition that the *olat behemah* is what gives the package its advantage over *ma'aser* — extend beyond the package itself? That is, should we also conclude that a *chatat ha'of* standing alone, without any accompanying *olat behemah*, would come before a *ma'aser behemah* because we have now implicitly recognized that *chatat* always beats *ma'aser*?

Rashi appears to suggest that such an inference is possible: if the logic of the package is that the *chatat-olah* unit supersedes the *ma'aser*, then perhaps even the *chatat ha'of* in isolation carries enough weight to beat the *ma'aser*. But the *Gemara*'s final position seems to resist this extension. The rule about a *chatat* before an *olah* is a specific *halakha* governing that pairing; its application to the *chatat* versus *shelamim* (or *ma'aser*) comparison is not automatic. The package solution preserves the established hierarchies without requiring us to resolve whether a bird *chatat* alone would defeat a mammal *ma'aser* — and the *Gemara* leaves that direct comparison unaddressed. The discussion will continue with the *Mishnah* in the following session.
