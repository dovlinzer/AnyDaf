# Bava Batra 135 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Biyado Legarsha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ: ״זֶה בְּנִי״ – נֶאֱמָן? הוֹאִיל וּבַעַל שֶׁאָמַר: ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מָרֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם! תָּלֵי תַּנְיָא בִּדְלָא תַּנְיָא?! אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ: ״זֶה בְּנִי״ נֶאֱמָן? הוֹאִיל וּבְיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן, הוֹאִיל וּבְיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that one who says: **This is my son,** is **deemed credible? Since a husband who says: I divorced my wife,** is **deemed credible,** and his wife is thereby exempt from levirate marriage, he is deemed credible with regard to this claim as well. **Rav Yosef said: Master of Abraham!** This reasoning **makes** that which **is taught** in the Mishna **dependent upon that which is not taught,** as the credibility of one who claims: This is my son, is stated in the mishna, while the *halakha* that a husband's claim that he divorced his wife is accepted is the statement of an *amora*. **Rather, if** this explanation **was stated, it was stated like this: Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that one who says: **This is my son,** is **deemed credible** with regard to rendering his wife exempt from levirate marriage? **Since it is in his power to divorce her** and thereby render her exempt from levirate marriage, he is deemed credible with regard to this claim as well. **Rav Yosef said** in addition: **Now that you said** that **we say** that the husband is deemed credible **since** he has the power to divorce her, **a husband who says: I divorced my wife,** is also **deemed credible, since it is in his power to divorce her** at any time.

### Paternity Claims

The *mishnah* on our daf continues the discussion begun earlier regarding the scope of a father's credibility in establishing paternity. A previous *mishnah* had established that a man is believed to identify someone as his *bechor*, his firstborn, on the basis of a *pasuk*. Here the *mishnah* addresses a broader claim: a man is believed to say that a certain person is his son — meaning that person receives a share in this man's estate. One might think this is obvious, since he could simply gift the property to him. The *gemara* therefore clarifies that the genuine *chiddush* lies elsewhere.

Even regarding property, there is an important limitation: a person is not believed about paternity in any absolute sense. His credibility rests entirely on the principle that he could have gifted the property to this person anyway. Consequently, his declaration of paternity covers only the property he currently holds — not property he will acquire later. If one wishes to ensure the child receives a full and proper share of the estate going forward, he must periodically renew the declaration so that it covers property accumulated since the last statement. But when a child is already *muchzak* — known and recognized in the community as his son or daughter — no declaration is needed at all.

The *gemara* identifies the real *chiddush* as the credibility of this declaration for purposes of *yibum*. If a man has known brothers and it is not known that he has children — a category that includes daughters as well — he is believed to claim that a certain person is his son or daughter, thereby exempting his wife from *yibum*. This is remarkable because it runs directly against the *chazaka* that his wife is obligated in *yibum*.

### Yibum Credibility

The *gemara* explains this credibility through the principle of *biyado l'garsha*: since he has the power to divorce his wife and thereby free her from *yibum* on his own initiative, he is believed to assert that she is already exempt. This might superficially resemble a standard *migo* — why would he lie when he could accomplish the same result through divorce? But that framing is not entirely satisfying, since there may be many reasons a man would not want to divorce his wife, making the lie potentially more attractive than the act. A better reading is that *biyado l'garsha* functions as a principle of authority: because he has control over this area of *halacha* — because it is within his power to free his wife from *yibum* — that very position of power confers upon him a degree of credibility within this domain. He is, in effect, a recognized actor in this legal sphere, and that standing gives his claims believability.

### Divorce Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. מְנַפַּח רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בִּידֵיהּ: אֲזַל לֵיהּ ״הוֹאִיל״ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן! לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן לְמַפְרֵעַ, כָּאן לְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A husband who says: I have divorced my wife,** is **not deemed credible. Rav Sheshet waved his hand** disparagingly, as if to say that **Rav Yosef's** statement that he is deemed credible **since** it is in his power to divorce her **is gone** due to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement. The Gemara asks: **Is that so?** Did Rabbi Yoḥanan actually say that? **But doesn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A husband who says: I have divorced my wife,** is **deemed credible?** The Gemara answers: That is **not difficult. Here,** in the statement that the husband is not deemed credible, Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring to **a retroactive** testimony. **By contrast, the statement** **there,** where Rabbi Yoḥanan said that the husband's testimony is deemed credible, refers to testimony **for the future,** e.g., where he says that he divorced her on that same day, or without specifying a date, in which case his statement is relevant only for the future.

Once this principle is established, Rav Yosef extends it significantly. *Hashta d'amarta amrinan ho'il* — now that we say we invoke the principle of *ho'il*, since he could have divorced his wife to achieve the same result, a husband who simply declares *gerashti et ishti*, "I divorced my wife," is believed — *ho'il u'biyado l'garsha* — even without producing any documentation. The *nafka mina* is substantial: upon his death, his wife could remarry freely, and any children born thereafter would not be *mamzeirim*, all on the basis of his say-so alone.

One might ask whether this applies even when the wife herself denies having received a *get*. While there are parallel cases — such as a seller who is believed to say to whom he sold something, since he was in a position to decide — counterclaims would presumably complicate matters. The principle of *biyado* here cannot be read as a simple *migo*, since it is always easier to claim one did something than to actually do it. The *biyado* principle must therefore be understood as something distinct: a kind of domain-authority that confers credibility, not merely a strategic inference from available alternatives.

## Divorce Retroactivity

### Yitzchak's Rejection

However, this extension of *ho'il biyado l'garsha* is immediately contested. When Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef came from Eretz Yisrael, he cited a ruling in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that is the direct opposite: *ba'al she'amar gerashti ishti eino ne'eman* — a husband who claims he divorced his wife is not believed. Upon hearing this, Rav Sheshes dismissed Rav Yosef's reasoning with an expressive wave of his hand — pfft — as if to say: there goes Rav Yosef's *ho'il*. Goodbye to that principle.

But a wholesale rejection of *biyado l'garsha* is impossible, since the very *mishnah* we are explaining relies on it to explain why a husband is believed to identify his child for *yibum* purposes. The principle must therefore survive in some form.

### Limited Application

Both the Rashbam and Tosafos explain that what is being rejected is not the principle itself but its application to the divorce claim specifically. The question is: what distinguishes the case of *yibum* — where *biyado l'garsha* works — from the case of a divorce claim, where it does not?

The answer, drawn from another *gemara*, returns to a point about counter-evidence. A claim that one has children is inherently plausible: a man can father children in circumstances that remain entirely private, with no public knowledge and no communal record. But a divorce is a different matter entirely. The absence of a *get*, the absence of any knowledge in the community, the absence of any *kol* — these are not merely neutral silences. The absence of evidence here functions as meaningful evidence against the claim. If a man had truly divorced his wife, there would be a *get*, a *beis din* record, some trace in the community. The very invisibility of the divorce undercuts the claim's believability, even granting the principle of *biyado l'garsha*.

One might add that the weight of what is at stake also differs: exempting a woman from *yibum* avoids an *issur lav*, whereas believing a false divorce claim would expose a woman to the *issur* of *eishes ish*, with the children potentially being *mamzeirim*. The Rashbam and Tosafos, however, focus specifically on the evidentiary asymmetry — the conspicuous absence of documentation — rather than on the relative severity of the consequences.

So the principle of *biyado l'garsha* stands, but it operates only where the claim itself is inherently plausible. The divorce claim is not sufficiently plausible, given the complete absence of supporting evidence, and therefore fails.

### Lemafrea Lehaba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אָמַר לְמַפְרֵעַ, מַהוּ לְהֵימוֹנֵיהּ לְהַבָּא? מִי פָּלְגִינַן דִּבּוּרָא, אוֹ לָא פָּלְגִינַן דִּבּוּרָא? רַב מָארִי וְרַב זְבִיד; חַד אָמַר: פָּלְגִינַן, וְחַד אָמַר: לָא פָּלְגִינַן. מַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרָבָא? דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי בָּא עַל אִשְׁתִּי״ – הוּא וְאַחֵר מִצְטָרְפִין לְהוֹרְגוֹ. לְהוֹרְגוֹ, וְלֹא לְהוֹרְגָהּ! בִּתְרֵי גוּפֵי פָּלְגִינַן, בְּחַד גּוּפָא לָא פָּלְגִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the husband **said** that he divorced his wife on a certain date, as **a retroactive** testimony, **what is** the *halakha* **concerning** his testimony being **deemed credible** and accepted **with regard to the future,** rendering her divorced from that time onward, despite the fact that his claim is not accepted with regard to the past? The dilemma is based on the following fundamental question: **Do we divide the** husband's **statement,** accepting that he divorced his wife inasmuch as she is considered divorced from that time onward, since it is in his power to divorce her now, even though his claim concerning the past cannot be accepted? **Or do we not divide the statement,** and say instead that his claim is rejected altogether, since his claim concerning the past cannot be accepted? **Rav Mari and Rav Zevid** engaged in a dispute with regard to this issue. **One says** that **we divide** the husband's statement, **and one says** that **we do not divide** it. The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from Rava's** statement? **As Rava says** that if a man says: **So-and-so engaged in sexual intercourse with my wife,** the husband **and another** witness **combine to kill him,** i.e., to have him sentenced to death for adultery. The Gemara infers: He combines with another witness **to kill him, but not to kill her.** The wife is not sentenced to death based on this testimony, even if they testified that she engaged in sexual intercourse willingly, as a husband is disqualified from bearing witness concerning his wife. Evidently, the husband's testimony is divided; his testimony concerning the man is accepted even though the testimony concerning his wife's part in the same action is rejected. The Gemara answers: **With regard to two** separate **bodies we divide** the statement. Therefore the husband's testimony is accepted with regard to the man but rejected with regard to his wife. **With regard to one body we do not divide** it. That is why one Sage holds that a husband's claim that he divorced his wife in the past cannot be divided, so that his claim that he divorced her would be accepted while his claim as to when he divorced her would be rejected.

The *gemara* then resolves the apparent contradiction between the two traditions in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. *Lo kashya: kan l'mafre'a, kan l'haba.* The tradition that a husband is not believed refers to a retroactive claim — *l'mafre'a* — as when a man, faced with his wife's suspected adultery, suddenly announces that he had divorced her a year ago. In that scenario, there is no *biyado l'garsha* at the operative moment: the power to divorce applies going forward, not backward. He cannot now, from where he stands, divorce his wife a year ago. So his claim about the past carries no credibility.

But going forward — *l'haba* — a husband is believed to say he has divorced his wife, because from this moment onward he retains the power to do so, and that power confers credibility from this point in time.

## Splitting Words

### Last Week Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָא שָׁכֵיב, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִתְּתֵיהּ לְמַאן? אֲמַר לְהוּ: חַזְיָא לְכָהֲנָא רַבָּה. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? הָא אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: (וְלָאו) מִי לָא שַׁנִּינְהוּ – כָּאן לְמַפְרֵעַ, וְכָאן לְהַבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man, who was presumed to have brothers but no children, **who was dying.** His wife was therefore presumed to be obligated in levirate marriage. Those with him **said to him: To whom** may **his wife,** i.e., your wife, be married? Is she required to enter into levirate marriage, or is she permitted to marry whomever she wishes? **He said to them: She is fit to** marry even **a High Priest.** She is not required to enter into levirate marriage. **Rava said:** With **what** possibility need **we be concerned with regard to her? Doesn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A husband who says: I have divorced my wife,** is **deemed credible,** and she is exempt from levirate marriage? Therefore, this woman will be exempt as well. **Abaye said to him: But when Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A husband who says: I have divorced my wife,** is **not deemed credible.** Rava **said to him: But didn't we resolve** the contradiction, concluding that **here** Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring to **a retroactive** testimony **and there** he was referring to testimony **for the future?** Therefore, according to both versions of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, the husband should be deemed credible in this case.

This resolution immediately generates a new question. If a husband announces *gerashti et ishti* last week — a claim we cannot credit retroactively — can we at least accept the statement as effective going forward? The practical stakes are significant: does the wife become free to remarry from today? If the husband dies, is she subject to *yibum*? If she does remarry and the retroactive claim cannot be believed, then children born in the interim could be *mamzeirim* — a serious *nafka mina*.

The structure of the question is: must we treat his statement as a single indivisible unit, rejecting it entirely because part of it is beyond credibility, or can we accept it as effective prospectively even though we cannot accept it retrospectively?

### Split Application

This is the question of *mi palginan dibura o lo palginan dibura* — do we split his words or not? Notably, "splitting" need not be taken literally, as though we excise the words "last week" from his sentence. The deeper question is whether we can apply his statement selectively — crediting it for some legal purposes while disallowing it for others — regardless of exactly how it was phrased.

The *gemara* records a debate between Rav Mari and Rav Zvid: one says we split his words and one says we do not. The *gemara* then raises a challenge: does not Rava's ruling in a classic case already demonstrate that we do split testimony? Rava rules that if a man says that Reuven committed adultery with his wife, this testimony is accepted against Reuven — with whom the witness has no disqualifying relationship — but rejected regarding his own wife, against whom he is a *karov*. The testimony is thus accepted for one party and rejected for another. Why, then, should we not similarly accept this husband's claim going forward while rejecting it for the past?

### Adultery Parallel

The *gemara* answers: *bitrei gufai palginan, b'chad gufa lo palginan.* When we split testimony across two different persons — two separate defendants — we do so because the testimony stands complete and unchallenged as applied to each individual in their own right. The witness is not disqualified from testifying against Reuven; the question of whether to credit him against the wife is a separate legal question about a separate person. In each individual case, the testimony either stands fully or falls fully; we are not dividing the testimony itself in half.

But in the case of the husband's divorce claim, we are being asked to apply a single statement to a single person — his wife — crediting it for the present while discrediting it for the past. That is a different kind of division. There, we are not choosing between two defendants; we are fragmenting the application of a single claim to a single subject. That is what the *gemara* means by *chad gufa*, and it is there that the split may not be permissible.

It is worth noting that, intuitively, one might have expected the analysis to run in the opposite direction. In the adultery testimony case, the witness has a clear personal stake that should, arguably, taint the entire enterprise. In the divorce claim, the concern is simply that *biyado l'garsha* applies only prospectively, not that anything inherently corrupts the claim. Yet the *gemara* permits the split in the adultery case and questions it in the divorce case — operating, it seems, on formal structural grounds rather than intuitive ones.

Given this unresolved question, the Rashbam concludes that even according to the opinion that a husband is believed to say he divorced his wife, this credibility clearly operates only when he either says "I divorced her today" or gives no timeframe at all. The moment he specifies a past moment — even yesterday, even moments ago — the statement becomes retroactive and the entire claim becomes questionable.

## Dying Man Case

### Wife Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַשִּׁנּוּיֵי נֵיקוּם וְלִסְמוֹךְ?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נָתָן בַּר אַמֵּי: חוּשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye responded: **But shall we arise and rely on answers** in a halakhic ruling? Although the contradiction can be resolved in this manner, there is no guarantee that this resolution is correct. Therefore Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef's statement should still be taken into consideration. **Rava** subsequently **said to Rav Natan bar Ami: Be concerned about it.** There may be a dispute with regard to this issue, as the resolution cannot be relied upon.

Rava initially rules: *mai nichush lah* — what is there to worry about? He should be believed. But the question is how to interpret this vague statement. Is he claiming he divorced her, or is he claiming he has children? The Rashbam explains that since we have a *chazaka* that this man has no sons, we should not read this ambiguous statement as a claim that contradicts that *chazaka*. We would rather interpret his words as a claim of divorce — something he always retained the power to do — rather than a claim of children, which would run against our standing presumption. Under this reading, his statement is treated as *gerashti et ishti*, and since he gave no specific timeframe, it is credited going forward.

### Abbaye Challenge

Abbaye challenges this. He points out that there is a conflicting tradition in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — that a husband who says *gerashti et ishti* is *not* believed. If that tradition is correct, then reading this vague statement as a divorce claim actually makes things worse, not better, since it invokes a claim that won't be credited. Why should we choose the interpretation that ends in disbelief?

In response, the earlier resolution is invoked: the two traditions in Rabbi Yochanan's name were already reconciled — one refers to retroactive claims, the other to prospective ones. Since this dying man gave no specific timeframe, his statement can be read as a present-moment divorce declaration and therefore should be believed going forward.

Abbaye, however, pushes back: should we really stake a practical ruling on what amounts to a reconciliation of conflicting *amora'im* traditions? Perhaps the contradiction was never truly resolved, and represents a genuine dispute about Rabbi Yochanan's position. If so, according to the stricter tradition, this woman would not be exempt and would require *chalitza*. Rava responds to Rav Natan bar Ami: *chushla* — we must be concerned. The ruling seesaws back. Rather than being confident the woman is exempt, we must entertain the possibility that she is not and that *chalitza* is required.

This brings out a striking irony that deepens the difficulty. Given the ambiguity of the dying man's statement, why not read it as a claim of children rather than divorce? If he claimed children, *all* opinions would agree he is believed, since that falls squarely within the *mishnah*'s ruling. The answer is that since our *chazaka* is that he has no children, we will not read his vague statement as a direct claim against that *chazaka* unless he states it explicitly. We therefore default to reading it as a divorce claim — but that is precisely the claim that is now in doubt. The result is that interpreting his statement in the way that does not challenge our presumptions leads to a claim that may not be believed, while the interpretation that would have been unambiguously believed — children — requires him to have directly said so.

## No Brothers Case

### Dying Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה מוּחְזָק לַן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אַחֵי, וַאֲמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אַחֵי. אֲמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי לֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? חֲדָא, דְּמוּחְזָק לַן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אַחִין. וְעוֹד, הָא אָמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה דְּלֵית לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָא אָמְרִי דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם דְּיָדְעִי דְּאִית לֵיהּ אַחֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who was presumed by us,** i.e., the court, **to have no brothers, and he said at the time of his death that he has no brothers. Rav Yosef said:** With **what** possibility need **we be concerned with regard to** his wife, in terms of her requiring levirate marriage? For **one, he is presumed by us to have no brothers, and furthermore, he said at the time of** his **death that he has none. Abaye said to him:** But **don't** people **say that there are witnesses overseas who know that he has brothers?** Therefore, we should be concerned that this report is accurate.

A parallel case is now presented. A man was *muchzak* in the community as having no brothers — that was the longstanding perception, based on years of communal life in which no brother ever appeared. As he lay dying, he reinforced this: he explicitly declared that he has no brothers. His wife is thus doubly exempt from *yibum*: first by communal presumption, and second by his own credible dying declaration. The Rashbam adds, notably, that even if the man had been *muchzak* to have brothers — a widespread perception, though not definitive knowledge — he would still be believed to say he does not, for the same reason that he is believed to say he has children: *biyado l'garsha* is not defeated by mere perception, only by actual established fact.

Rav Yosef asks: *mai leichosh lah* — what is there to worry about? We have the *chazaka* that he has no brothers, and we have his explicit dying declaration reinforcing it. Two independent grounds for permitting his wife to remarry without *chalitza*.

### Distant Witnesses

Abbaye interjects with a complication. People are saying — *amri* — that there are witnesses in distant lands who have concrete knowledge that this man does have a brother. Not that we have established testimony; not that anyone has come before *beis din* with evidence. But there is a rumor that somewhere, witnesses exist who know of a brother. Should this rumor require us to delay, to investigate, before permitting the wife to remarry?

### No Rumors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת הָא לֵיתַנְהוּ קַמַּן; לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: עֵדִים בְּצַד אַסְתָּן, וְתֵאָסֵר?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef responded: **In any event, now** the witnesses **are not** present **before us,** so this possibility does not need to be taken into account. **Isn't this** the same as **Rabbi Ḥanina's** ruling in a case where women who were captured and subsequently liberated claimed that they were not raped in captivity (*Ketubot* 23a)? **As** although people said there were witnesses elsewhere who could testify that they were raped, **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that they were permitted to marry a priest, reasoning: Just because there may be **witnesses in the north [*istan*],** i.e., in a distant place, **will** the woman **be forbidden?**

The *gemara* responds: *hashta meihacha leis ninhu kamman* — right now, those witnesses are not here. We have no obligation to be paralyzed by rumors of potential future evidence. The principle is *ein ladayan ela mah she'einav ro'os*: a judge rules based on what stands before him, not on rumors about what might someday appear.

This is compared to the ruling of Rabbi Chanina in the case of Shmuel's daughter, who was taken captive and testified that she was *tehorah ani* — untouched by her captors. When it was rumored that witnesses in a distant city knew otherwise, Rabbi Chanina ruled: *eidim be-tzad Etztan vete'aser* — should she be forbidden because witnesses sit in some far city? They are not here; we do not wait for them.

### Concrete Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם הֵקַלְנוּ בִּשְׁבוּיָה – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְנַוְּולָא נַפְשָׁהּ לְגַבֵּי שַׁבַּאי; נָקֵל בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ?! אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נָתָן בַּר אַמֵּי: חוּשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If we were lenient with regard to a captive woman, due to,** among other reasons, the fact that **she makes herself repulsive before the captor** so that he will not want to rape her, and we assume that she was not raped, **should we be lenient with regard to a married woman? Rava said to Rav Natan bar Ami: Be concerned about it.** Do not permit this woman to remarry until the matter is clarified.

Abbaye draws a distinction, however. The case of the *shvuyah* — the captive woman — involves at most a rabbinic prohibition, since even if she had been violated, her status as *eishes ish* is not in question, and her defilement would only rabbinically disqualify her from a *kohen*. The principle *in hikalnu bishvuyah, neikul b'eishes ish?* — just because we are lenient about the captive, should we be equally lenient in matters of *eishes ish*? The *yibum* question, while not identical to an adultery case, is still a weightier matter than the *shvuyah* scenario.

Rava concludes — again in a message to Rav Natan bar Ami — *chush lah*: we must be concerned. Where there is a concrete hint of hard evidence about a weighty matter, we cannot simply proceed. We must take some measure of concern seriously, even if the precise parameters — how widespread must the rumor be, how much investigation is required, what happens if inquiries yield nothing — remain undefined by the *gemara*.

## Brother Dispute

### Levi Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶה אָחִי״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. וְאִידַּךְ מַאי קָאָמְרִי? אִי קָאָמְרִי ״אֲחוּנָא הוּא״, אַמַּאי יִטּוֹל עִמּוֹ בְּחֶלְקוֹ וְתוּ לָא? אֶלָּא דְּקָא אָמְרִי ״לָאו אָחִינוּ הוּא״? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: נָפְלוּ לוֹ נְכָסִים מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר – יִירְשׁוּ אֶחָיו עִמּוֹ. הָא אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״לָאו אֲחוּנָא הוּא״! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָא אָמְרִי: ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that one who says: **This is my brother,** is **not deemed credible** with regard to his other brothers' share of their father's inheritance. Rather, the man in question receives a portion only from the portion of the one who testified concerning him. The Gemara clarifies: **And what do the other** brothers **say? If they say: He is our brother, why** does the man in question **take** a portion of the inheritance only **with** the one who testified, **from his portion, and nothing more?** If the other brothers admit that he is their brother, they should give him a share of their portions as well. **Rather, they are saying: He is not our brother.** The Gemara questions this: **Say the last clause** of the mishna: If **property came into** the possession of the man in question **from somewhere else,** and he died, **the brothers of** the one who testified **shall inherit** the man in question's property **with him.** Why should they inherit with him? **Didn't they say to him: He is not our brother?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this ruling only in a case **where they say: We do not know** if he is our brother. Therefore, they are not obligated to give him a portion of their inheritance, as he cannot prove to them that he is their brother. Nevertheless, they can claim a portion of his inheritance after his death, on the basis of their brother's testimony.

The *mishnah* rules: Reuven's claim is not believed as against Shimon, so Shimon is not compelled to give Levi any portion of his inheritance. Reuven, however, is bound by his own claim, and so Levi takes a share from Reuven's portion. Moreover, if Levi later dies, Reuven and Shimon split his estate equally as his presumptive brothers.

### Inheritance Split

The *gemara* asks: what exactly is Shimon's position? If Shimon were conceding that Levi is a brother, the question would be why Levi receives only from Reuven and not from Shimon as well. So Shimon must be denying it. But if Shimon is denying that Levi is a brother, why would Shimon be entitled to inherit from Levi when Levi dies?

The answer: *lo tzricha deka amri einu yodim* — Shimon takes the strategically sound position of *I don't know*. He does not affirmatively deny the brotherhood, and he does not affirm it. Because he does not know, and because *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the burden of proof lies on the claimant — Shimon need not give Levi anything from his own share. But when Levi dies, Reuven's claim is now in Shimon's favor: Reuven is already asserting that all three are brothers. As long as Shimon does not contradict that claim — since he says only *eini yodeia* — he stands to benefit from it. The estate of Levi is therefore split between them.

### Bari Veshema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: That is to say** that if one says to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other** person **says: I do not know,** he is **exempt,** similar to this case, where the brothers who claim that they do not know if this person is their brother are not obligated to share their inheritance with him.

Rava draws a broader principle from this case. Reuven is essentially saying to Shimon: you owe Levi a half field. Shimon says: I don't know. Is this not a classic case of *bari veshema* — a certain claim versus a doubtful one — and does *bari* not ordinarily prevail? Rava concludes: *zos omeres mane li beyadecha vehala omer eini yodeia patur* — this teaches that where someone says you owe me a hundred *maneh* and the other says he doesn't know, the respondent is exempt.

Abbaye disagrees. He argues that ordinarily, a *bari* claim does override a *shema* — it is only here that Shimon is exempt, for a specific reason: *deka maneh le'acher beyadcha dami* — this resembles a case where Reuven says "you owe the other person a hundred *maneh*." Reuven is not making a claim for himself; he is making a claim on behalf of Levi. And crucially, Levi himself cannot come with a *bari* claim — Levi does not know with certainty who his father was. The only *bari* voice in this entire proceeding is Reuven's, and Reuven is asserting a claim on someone else's behalf. A *bari* claim can overcome a *chazaka* when the claimant is asserting his own interest directly. It is not powerful enough to extract property from another person's possession on behalf of a third party who cannot himself assert certainty. Therefore, whatever one holds in general about *bari veshema*, Shimon is exempt here — not because *bari* is categorically insufficient, but because the *bari* here belongs to Reuven rather than to Levi.

### Return Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת – יַחְזְרוּ נְכָסִים לִמְקוֹמָן. בָּעֵי רָבָא: שֶׁבַח שֶׁשָּׁבְחוּ נְכָסִים מֵאֲלֵיהֶם, מַהוּ? בְּשֶׁבַח הַמַּגִּיעַ לַכְּתֵפַיִם – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּכִי נָפְלוּ לוֹ נְכָסִים מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר דָּמֵי. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – בְּשֶׁבַח שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַגִּיעַ לַכְּתֵפַיִם; כְּגוֹן דִּיקְלָא וַאֲלֵים, אַרְעָא וְאַסְּקָא שִׂרְטוֹן; מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the man in question **dies,** the **property** he received from the father's inheritance **shall return to its place,** i.e., to the possession of the brother who testified on his behalf, and if the man in question received property from elsewhere, it is inherited by all the brothers equally. **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to **the enhancement of the property** received by the man in question from the portion of the brother who testified, **where** its **enhanced** value was the result of an enhancement that **happened naturally,** as opposed to one that resulted from exertion, **what is** the *halakha*? Who inherits it? The Gemara elaborates: **With regard to enhancement that reaches shoulders,** i.e., ripe produce that needs only to be harvested from the field, **do not raise the dilemma, as it is** considered **like property that came into his possession from elsewhere.** It is not considered part of the land that was given to him by the brother who testified, and it is therefore divided among all the brothers. Rather, **let the dilemma be raised with regard to enhancement that does not reach shoulders,** and is not considered separate from the ground, **such as a palm tree that thickened,** or **land that yielded silt. What** is the *halakha* in this case? Is the enhancement included in the property itself, or is it considered separate property? The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

When Levi dies without issue, having earned his own money independently, Reuven and Shimon divide his estate equally — since Shimon said *eini yodeia* and Reuven claims brotherhood, and that claim now works in both brothers' favor. But the half field that Reuven had given Levi — the portion Reuven himself transferred to him — returns to Reuven. The logic is that Shimon retained the half field he should, according to Reuven's claim, have given Levi long ago. Now that Levi is dead, Shimon inherits what he was always holding. Reuven, correspondingly, reclaims what he himself had given. Any independently earned money of Levi's is divided equally between the two brothers, since both stand as equal heirs.

### Shevach Value

Rava then poses an unresolved question: *shevach she-shivchu nechasim me'alehem mahu* — what about an increase in the value of Reuven's half field that occurred naturally, without anyone's effort? If the real estate value rose, or a palm tree grew thicker, or the land became more fertile due to flooding — none of it through Levi's investment — does that natural appreciation simply return to Reuven along with the field, or must it be split?

The *gemara* clarifies that appreciation which is the result of visible, active effort — *shevach hamagi'a liktefayim*, meaning either that the crop has visibly grown tall or that it required putting one's shoulder into the work — clearly constitutes new property and would be divided equally. That is not in question. The unresolved *sha'aleh* concerns passive appreciation: a change in value that no one caused, that simply happened by itself. Does Reuven recover the field in its current enhanced state, or must Shimon receive half of the natural appreciation as a co-inheritor of Levi? The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — this remains unresolved.

## Will Doctrine

### Diatiki Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁמֵּת וְנִמְצְאָה דְּיָיתֵיקֵי קְשׁוּרָה עַל יְרֵיכוֹ – הֲרֵי זוֹ אֵינָהּ כְּלוּם. זִיכָּה בָּהּ לְאַחֵר – בֵּין מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, בֵּין שֶׁאֵינָן מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who died, and a will written by a person on his deathbed [*dayetikei*] is found bound to his thigh,** which clearly indicates that it was written by him and was not forged, **this is nothing.** The will is not valid, as he did not give it to anyone, and he may have reconsidered. If **he transferred ownership of** the will to the designated recipient **through another** person, **whether one of the heirs** or **whether not one of the heirs, his statement stands.**

With this the *gemara* transitions fully into the institution of *matnat shchiv meira* — the deathbed bequest — though in a form that has been mentioned in passing throughout the tractate and is now addressed directly. The instrument under discussion is the *diatiki*, a Greek word meaning "will" — a written testament. In the conceptual framework of *chazal*, the primary legal instrument is the verbal deathbed bequest: a dying person declares orally what he wishes done with his property, and that declaration is binding by rabbinic enactment. The *diatiki* is a variation: instead of the oral declaration, or alongside it, the person writes out his wishes. This seems like the prudent course — put it in writing, have a record — but the *halacha* treats it differently from a standard *shtar kinyan*.

The *diatiki* does not function as a *shtar kinyan*. It is not a formal deed of acquisition in which witnesses attest to a transaction. It is a written expression of the person's desires and intentions regarding his estate — a will in the fullest sense of the word. As such, it is subject to different rules. A person can revise his will repeatedly, just as a person on his deathbed can retract a verbal bequest and issue a new one. The prime model in *halacha* remains the oral declaration, with the written will as a derivative form.

### No Effect

The *mishnah* states: if a person died and a *diatiki* — a written will — was found bound to his thigh, it has no legal effect whatsoever. The binding to his thigh removes any question about authorship; this is clearly his will. Yet *harei zeh einah klum* — it accomplishes nothing — because it was never handed over. The act of handing over is the required act of legal execution for a written will.

### Handed Over

If, however, he performed *zichah bah l'acher* — he caused someone else to take hold of the will on his behalf — whether that person is among his heirs or not among his heirs, *devarav kayamim* — his words stand and the will is binding. The Rashbam notes that the will need not name specific beneficiaries. If the will makes bequests without naming the recipient, the person who receives the document is presumed to be the beneficiary, like a blank check that becomes payable to its holder. As long as someone has received the document in this act of *zicha*, the will takes legal effect.

### Execution Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶה הִיא דְּיָיתֵיקֵי? כֹּל שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהּ: ״דָּא תְּהֵא לְמֵיקַם וְלִהְיוֹת״. וְאֵיזֶה הִיא מַתָּנָה? כֹּל שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהּ: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״. אֶלָּא ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – הוּא דְּהָוְיָא מַתָּנָה, ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ – לָא הָוְיָא מַתָּנָה?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵיזוֹ הִיא מַתְּנַת בָּרִיא שֶׁהִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע – דְּלָא קָנֵי אֶלָּא לְאַחַר מִיתָה? כֹּל שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהּ: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״. יָתֵיב רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא בְּאַכְסַדְרָא דְּבֵי רַב, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר ״כִּתְבוּ וּתְנוּ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי״, וּמֵת – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין וְנוֹתְנִין; שֶׁמָּא לֹא גָּמַר לְהַקְנוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר, וְאֵין שְׁטָר לְאַחַר מִיתָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אִיזְדְּהַרוּ בַּהּ. רַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמְרַהּ, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִיזְדְּהַרוּ בַּהּ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁיזְבִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אַמְרַהּ, אִצְטְרִיךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְאַסְהוֹדֵי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַמְרַהּ, אִצְטְרִיךְ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְאַסְהוֹדֵי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רַבֵּיהּ?! וְעוֹד, תָּא שְׁמַע דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אַמְרַהּ, דִּשְׁלַח רָבִין מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הֱווּ יוֹדְעִים שֶׁשָּׁלַח רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לַגּוֹלָה מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר ״כִּתְבוּ וּתְנוּ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי״, וּמֵת – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין וְנוֹתְנִין; שֶׁמָּא לֹא גָּמַר לְהַקְנוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר, וְאֵין שְׁטָר לְאַחַר מִיתָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: תִּיבָּדֵק. מַאי ״תִּיבָּדֵק״? כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: דְּיָיתֵיקֵי מְבַטֶּלֶת דְּיָיתֵיקֵי. שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר: ״כִּתְבוּ וּתְנוּ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי״, וּמֵת – רוֹאִין; אִם כִּמְיַפֶּה אֶת כֹּחוֹ – כּוֹתְבִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 8:10): **Which** deed **is** considered **a *dayetikei*,** and is collected by the designated recipient after the death of the giver? **Any** deed **in which it is written: This will be to stand and exist** after my death. **And which** type **is** considered a deed of **gift? Any** deed **in which it is written: From today and after** my **death.** The Gemara asks: **Is it** considered a deed of **gift only** if the expression: **From today and after** my **death,** is written, whereas if it is written only: **From now, it is not** considered a deed of **gift? Abaye said** that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: Which** deed of **gift of a healthy person is** considered **like** the deed of **gift of a person on his deathbed,** in **that** the recipient **acquires** it **only after** the **death** of the giver? **Any** deed **in which it is written: From today and after** my **death.** § **Rabba bar Rav Huna was sitting in the balcony of Rav's study hall, and sat and said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If there is **a person on his deathbed who says: Write** a deed of transfer, granting property of mine to another, **and give** with it **one hundred dinars to so-and-so, and** he then **died, one does not write and give** it. This is because **perhaps he resolved to transfer it** to him **only with a deed** of transfer, **and** since the deed was not written in his lifetime it cannot be written after his death, as **a deed** of transfer is **not** effective **after the death** of the owner. Rabba bar Rav Huna continued that when Rabbi Yoḥanan stated this *halakha*, **Rabbi Elazar said to** the other Sages: **Heed this** *halakha*; it is correct. **Rav Sheizevi said:** That is not what happened; **Rabbi Elazar** is the one who **said this** *halakha*, **and** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who **said to them: Heed this** *halakha*. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It **is reasonable** to say that the incident was **in accordance with** the version presented **by Rav Sheizevi. Granted, if you say** that **Rabbi Elazar said** the *halakha*, **Rabbi Yoḥanan needed to affirm** the ruling **of Rabbi Elazar. But if you say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** was the one who **said it, did Rabbi Elazar need to affirm** the ruling **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, his teacher?** **And furthermore, come** and **hear** proof **that Rabbi Elazar** was the one who **said** this *halakha*, from another statement of his, **as Ravin sent** a message **in the name of Rabbi Abbahu: Know that Rabbi Elazar sent** a ruling **to the exile in the name of our teacher,** stating that if there is **a person on his deathbed who says: Write** a deed of transfer **and give** with it **one hundred dinars to so-and-so, and** he then **died, one does not write and give** it. This is because **perhaps he resolved to transfer it only with a deed** of transfer, **and** since the deed was not written in his lifetime it cannot be written after his death, as **a deed** of transfer is **not** effective **after the death** of the owner. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This ruling is correct; however, the wording of the deed **should be examined.** The Gemara asks: **What** did Rabbi Yoḥanan mean by saying that the wording **should be examined?** The Gemara answers: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he **stated** two *halakhot*. The first was that **a *dayetikei* cancels** a previous ***dayetikei*.** The second explains the examination to which Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring: If there is **a person on his deathbed who said: Write** a deed of transfer **and give** with it **one hundred dinars to so-and-so, and** he then **died,** the court must **see** what his intention was in instructing the other person to write a deed of transfer; **if** it was **to enhance** the recipient's **power** by writing a document proving that he was given the gift, **one writes** the document even after his death, as he intended to give the money anyway. **But if not,** rather it was the giver's intention to transfer the gift specifically through a deed of transfer, **one does not write** it and give the money, as a deed of transfer is not effective after the death of the owner.

This leads to a pointed question: why should a written document be ineffective when all other *shtarot* serve as evidence or instruments of transfer? The answer lies in the fundamental nature of the document. A normal *shtar* bears the signatures of witnesses who are testifying to a fact or transaction. This *diatiki* is not that. It is a written expression of the person's wishes — a declaration of intent, not a witnessed transaction. Anyone could theoretically have composed such a document; the question of authorship may be resolved, but the question of legal execution remains. Without the act of handing over, there is no legally recognized execution of the will.

More fundamentally, the *halacha* requires a recognized legal instrument to transfer property. The fact that someone wishes to give his property to another person, however well-documented, does not by itself effect a transfer. What makes a deathbed bequest legally operative is either the verbal oral declaration — which is heard by another person and thus communicated — or the written will that is handed over. Both require that the intent be communicated and actualized through some recognized act. A document sitting tied to a man's thigh, however clearly his own work, lacks *gmirat daat* — the finalization of intent as manifested through a completed legal act. The document has not yet been communicated to anyone.

Indeed, there is something counterintuitive here: an oral statement is more immediately binding than a written document that has not been handed over. The reason, however, is clear: the oral deathbed bequest is spoken to someone else, it is received, it is communicated. Even a verbal declaration made to oneself in private would not qualify. The *gmirat daat* requires that the act of giving be, in some real sense, completed — and handing over the will is the written equivalent of the verbal declaration heard by another. Until that act occurs, the will remains, legally, a private expression of intention with no binding force.
