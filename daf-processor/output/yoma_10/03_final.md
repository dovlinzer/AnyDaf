# Yoma 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yeridat HaDorot

### Intro: Yeridat HaDorot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוֵי סָחֵי בְּיַרְדְּנָא אֲתָא רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה יְהַב לֵיהּ יְדָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֱלָהָא! סָנֵינָא לְכוּ — דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם חוֹמָה הִיא נִבְנֶה עָלֶיהָ טִירַת כָּסֶף וְאִם דֶּלֶת הִיא נָצוּר עָלֶיהָ לוּחַ אָרֶז״. אִם עֲשִׂיתֶם עַצְמְכֶם כְּחוֹמָה וַעֲלִיתֶם כּוּלְּכֶם בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא — נִמְשַׁלְתֶּם כְּכֶסֶף שֶׁאֵין רָקָב שׁוֹלֵט בּוֹ, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁעֲלִיתֶם כִּדְלָתוֹת — נִמְשַׁלְתֶּם כְּאֶרֶז שֶׁהָרָקָב שׁוֹלֵט בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish was swimming in the Jordan River** when **Rabba bar bar Ḥana came and gave him a hand** to help him out. Reish Lakish **said to him: My God! I hate you** Babylonians, **as it is written: "If she be a wall we will build a silver turret upon her, if she be a door we will cover her with boards of cedar"** (Song of Songs 8:9). This is the meaning of the verse as it applies to the Jewish people: **Had you rendered yourselves** a solid bloc **like a wall and all ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **in the days of Ezra, you would have been likened to silver, which rot does not infest,** in the sense that you would have merited experiencing the Divine Presence in all its glory. **Now that you ascended like doors,** and only some of you came to Eretz Yisrael, **you are likened to cedar, which rot infests,** and you merit experiencing only partial revelation of the Divine Presence.

Today's *daf* is 10, and we pick up on 9b, where the *gemara* has been engaged in a rich and far-reaching dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning *yeridat hadorot* — the question of whether earlier generations were spiritually greater than later ones. This discussion is deeply connected to the question of the *binyan beit hamikdash*: why was the First Temple rebuilt after seventy years, whereas the Second Temple, once destroyed, has never been rebuilt? It is important to keep that backdrop in mind as we turn to the following narrative.

### Reish Lakish in Jordan

*Reish Lakish hava sachi b'Yardena* — Reish Lakish was bathing in the Jordan River. Rabbah bar Bar Chana, a Babylonian *amora* who was spending time learning in Eretz Yisrael, approached and stretched out his hand to help Reish Lakish out of the water. Reish Lakish's response was, to say the least, unexpected: *Eloha sanei lechu* — your God hates you. As a proof-text, he cited the verse from Shir HaShirim: *Im chomah hi nivneh aleha tirat kasef v'im delet hi natzur aleha luach arez* — "If she is a wall, let us build upon her a tower of silver; and if she is a door, let us board her up with a plank of cedar."

### Wall vs. Door Metaphor

The interpretation Reish Lakish draws from this verse is as follows: *Im asitem atzmechem k'chomah v'alitem kulchem b'yimei Ezra* — had you made yourselves like a wall and ascended together in the time of Ezra, for the majority of Jews living in Babylonia chose to remain there rather than return for the building of the Second Temple. A wall is a single unified structure; had all of Israel come up together, *nimshaltem k'kasef she'ein rakav sholeit bo* — you would have been likened to silver, into which no rot can penetrate.

But as it was, *achshav she'alitem k'dalatot* — now that you came up like doors. Rashi explains the metaphor: a door is only a small piece of the wall, the part that opens and closes; it is something partial and incomplete. Now that you came up like a door, *nimshaltem k'arez she'harakav sholeit bo* — you are likened to cedar, into which rot can set.

The *gemara*'s implication, as Rashi frames it, is that this parable addresses the very question animating the prior discussion: why has there been no *binyan beit hamikdash*? Why was the Second Temple destroyed and not rebuilt? Reish Lakish answers: it is the fault of the *bnei bavel* who did not come up in the time of Ezra. It is not that later generations are of lesser spiritual stature — indeed, Torah learning under oppression may be at a higher level than in earlier times. The reasons for our diminished national status lie elsewhere, in historical and communal failures rather than individual decline. The *Maharsha* develops this reading beautifully by connecting the wall-and-door imagery to the actual historical circumstances of Sefer Ezra: had so many of Israel ascended, they would have constituted a wall in the literal sense, making the fortifications unnecessary against the hostile Samaritans and others who sought to obstruct the rebuilding. As it was, only enough came up to guard the doors — a partial and vulnerable presence.

### Arez & Sasmagor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֶרֶז? אָמַר עוּלָּא: סַסְמָגוֹר. מַאי סַסְמָגוֹר? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: בַּת קוֹל. כִּדְתַנְיָא: מִשֶּׁמֵּתוּ נְבִיאִים הָאַחֲרוֹנִים חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי נִסְתַּלְּקָה רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וַעֲדַיִין הָיוּ מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בְּבַת קוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** rot infests **cedar? Ulla said: It is *sasmagor*,** a type of worm. The Gemara asks: **What** does ***sasmagor*** have to do with the Divine Presence during the Second Temple era? **Rabbi Abba said:** Just as little remains from a cedar tree infested by this worm, similarly, all that remained from the Divine Presence during the Second Temple period was a **Divine Voice, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **After the last prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi died, the Divine Spirit** of prophetic revelation **departed from the Jewish people, and they were still utilizing a Divine Voice,** which they heard as an echo of prophecy.

*Mai arez?* — what exactly is the *arez* being referred to? Ulla answers: *sasmagor*. This term may simply be a Babylonian name for a variety of cedar, but Rashi also understands it to denote a particular worm that eats through the cedar from the inside, leaving the outer shell intact while the interior rots away. *Mai sasmagor?* — what is the metaphor of *sasmagor*? Rabbi Abba answers: *bat kol* — the Second Temple had the outer shell of sanctity but lacked the inner fullness. During the period of the Second Temple, there was no true *nevuah*, no complete divine presence — only the distant echo of a *bat kol*.

This is grounded in a *baraita*: *mishemetu nevi'im acharonim Chaggai Zechariah u'Malachi nistalka Ruach HaKodesh m'Yisrael v'adayin hayu mishtamshim b'bat kol* — once the later prophets, Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — who bridged the transition from the First to the Second Temple — passed away, the *Ruach HaKodesh* departed from Israel; yet they still made use of a *bat kol*. The *bat kol* is not the actual *Ruach HaKodesh*; it is, as the name suggests, the daughter of a voice — a distant echo, some residual form of divine inspiration. Saul Lieberman noted that in the Bavli, the *bat kol* carries genuinely metaphysical connotations, whereas in the Yerushalmi the term often bears no supernatural weight, referring simply to a rumor or emerging consensus.

So the point is not that the Second Temple was built by spiritually inferior people, but that the circumstances of its construction — the incomplete *aliyah*, the subjugation to foreign powers — meant that the *shechina* could not rest there as it had in the First Temple. The cedar is still cedar; but the *sasmagor* has done its work from within.

## Mezuzah in Mikdash

### Reish Lakish's Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מִי מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵי רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה? וּמָה רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר), דְּמָרָא דְּאַרְעָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲוָה, וְלָא הֲוָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בַּהֲדֵיהּ, דְּמַאן דְּמִשְׁתַּעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בַּהֲדֵיהּ בְּשׁוּקָא, יָהֲבוּ לֵיהּ עִיסְקָא בְּלָא סָהֲדֵי — בַּהֲדֵי רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And would Reish Lakish speak with Rabba bar bar Ḥana** in public? **Just as Rabbi Elazar, who was the master of Eretz Yisrael** in wisdom and character, **and** nevertheless, **Reish Lakish would not speak with him** in public, as Reish Lakish was sparing in his speech and extended friendship to only a select few prominent, righteous people, to the extent that **a person to whom Reish Lakish** was seen **speaking in the marketplace, one would give him** a loan and **do business** with him **without witnesses;** would he **have spoken with Rabba bar bar Ḥana?**

The *gemara* now raises a pointed internal question about the story just told: *Reish Lakish mi mishata b'haday Rabba bar bar Chana?* — would Reish Lakish really have spoken with Rabbah bar Bar Chana at all? The issue is this: *u'ma Rebbi Elazar d'marei d'artza d'Yisrael hava v'lo hava mishata Reish Lakish badei* — Rabbi Elazar, who was regarded as the master of the sages of Eretz Yisrael, a figure of towering stature, and yet Reish Lakish would not speak with him in public. The reason: *d'man d'mishata Reish Lakish badei beshuka yavlu lei iska b'lo sa'adi* — whoever Reish Lakish would speak with publicly, people would consider that person so completely trustworthy that they would conduct business with him without witnesses. Therefore, Reish Lakish was extraordinarily careful about whom he would be seen speaking with in public, because his public acknowledgment of a person served as a powerful character endorsement. If he would not extend this to Rabbi Elazar, he certainly would not have extended it to Rabbah bar Bar Chana.

### Rav Pappa's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, שְׁדִי גַּבְרָא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: אוֹ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה וּזְעֵירִי, אוֹ רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה הֲוָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Cast a man between them,** and say that the incident did not involve Reish Lakish and Rabba bar bar Ḥana. It **was either Reish Lakish** bathing in the river **and Ze'iri,** the prominent Babylonian Sage, who extended him a hand, **or** it was **Rabba bar bar Ḥana** who was in the river **and Rabbi Elazar** extended a hand to him.

Rav Pappa resolves the problem: *shadi gavra beinaihu* — one of the figures in the story must be exchanged. Either it was Reish Lakish swimming in the river and it was Zeiri who helped him out, or it was Rabbah bar Bar Chana who stretched out his hand and it was Rabbi Elazar who was swimming. The story as originally told must have involved a pairing that is historically plausible — one of the two names as transmitted to us must be a substitution for the original.

The *gemara* notes that when they came before Rabbi Yochanan — which, Rashi observes, suggests it was Reish Lakish involved, given the famous relationship between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan — Rabbi Yochanan responded to the substance of the teaching.

## Yeridat HaDorot…

### Rabbi Yochanan's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, אִי נָמֵי סְלִיקוּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא לָא הֲוָה שָׁרְיָא שְׁכִינָה בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יַפְתְּ אֱלֹהִים לְיֶפֶת וְיִשְׁכֹּן בְּאׇהֳלֵי שֵׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** In any event, **when** the Sage who heard what Reish Lakish said **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and related it, Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: That is not the reason; even had they all ascended in the days of Ezra, the Divine Presence would not have rested in the Second Temple, as it is written: "God will enlarge Japheth, and dwell in the tents of Shem"** (Genesis 9:27).

*Amar lei lav hai hu ta'ama* — Rabbi Yochanan says: that is not the reason. *Ee nami seliku kulhu b'yemei Ezra, lo havi sharya Shchina b'mikdash sheni* — even if all of Israel had ascended in the time of Ezra, the *shechina* would not have rested in the Second Temple. This is not a question of demography or communal solidarity. The reason lies in a deeper theological principle, derived from the verse: *Yaft Elokim l'Yefet v'yishkon b'ahalei Shem* — God will beautify Yefet, but He will dwell in the tents of Shem. Even though God grants expansion and even beauty to the descendants of Yefet, the divine presence rests only in the tents of Shem. Since the Second Temple was built under Persian auspices — and the *gemara* will now examine whether Persia descends from Yefet — its construction was not purely within the domain of Shem, and therefore the *shechina* could not rest there fully, regardless of how many Jews ascended. The classic *midrash* reads *yaft Elokim l'Yefet* as a synthesis — the beauty of Yefet finding its home in the tents of Shem — but Rabbi Yochanan reads it as a dichotomy, a structural limitation on where the *shechina* can dwell.

## Yaft Elokim Leyafet

### Persia & Yafet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפָרְסָאֵי מְנָא לַן דְּמִיֶּפֶת קָאָתוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּנֵי יֶפֶת גּוֹמֶר וּמָגוֹג וּמָדַי וְיָוָן וְתוּבָל וּמֶשֶׁךְ וְתִירָס״. גּוֹמֶר — זֶה גֶּרְמַמְיָא, מָגוֹג — זוֹ קַנְדִּיָּא, מָדַי — זוֹ מַקֵדוֹנְיָא, יָוָן — כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, תּוּבָל — זֶה בֵּית אוּנַיְיקִי, מֶשֶׁךְ — זוֹ מוּסְיָא, תִּירָס, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי סִימַאי וְרַבָּנַן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי סִימוֹן וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אָמַר: זוֹ בֵּית תְּרַיְיקִי, וְחַד אָמַר: זוֹ פָּרַס. תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: תִּירָס — זוֹ פָּרַס.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **the Persians descend from Japheth?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "The sons of Japheth were Gomer and Magog and Madai and Javan and Tuval and Meshech and Tiras"** (Genesis 10:2). The Gemara explains: **Gomer, that is Germamya; Magog, that is Kandiya; Madai, that is Macedonia; Javan, in accordance with its plain meaning,** Greece; **Tuval, that is** the nation called **Beit Unaiki; Meshech, that is Musya.** With regard to **Tiras, Rabbi Simai and the Rabbis disagree, and some say** the dispute is between **Rabbi Simon and the Rabbis: One said: That is Beit Teraiki, and one said: That is Persia.** According to that approach, Persia is listed among the descendants of Japheth. **Rav Yosef taught: Tiras is Persia.**

The *gemara* raises the foundational question behind Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning: *u'Parsayei minalan d'miYefet ka'asu* — from where do we know that the Persians descend from Yefet? The verse that enabled the second *beit hamikdash* to be built was given by Koresh, a Persian king. For Rabbi Yochanan's argument to stand, Persia must be identified as a nation of Yefet's lineage. To establish this, the *gemara* turns to the *pasuk* listing the sons of Yefet: *Bnei Yefet Gomer u'Magog u'Madai v'Yavan v'Tuval u'Meshech v'Tiras.*

### Sons of Yafet

The *gemara* proceeds to identify each of these nations. *Gomer* is *Garmamya* — a term our *girsa* records as *Garmamya*; the *Gra* has the reading *Germanya*. Steinsaltz identifies it with a region north of Assyria, likely Phrygia or a neighboring area; on geographic grounds, an identification with a Near Eastern or Caucasian region is far more plausible than a Western European one, since all of these nations belong to the ancient Near Eastern world.

*Magog* is *Kandya*. The Steinsaltz identifies this with Scythia; there is also a *girsa* of *Gothya*, referring to the Goths, a people of Asia Minor or further north. *Madai* is *Makdonya* — Macedonia, the region north of Greece proper; notably, Alexander the Great was Macedonian, representing a distinct ethnic and political identity from the Greek city-states to the south.

*Yavan k'mashmao* — Yavan is Greece, as is well known. *Tuval* is *Beis Onaiki*, identified as Bithynia in northwest Asia Minor. *Meshech* is *Mosya*, associated with the region along the Sea of Marmara and the Dardanelles.

*Tiras* is the subject of a dispute. *Pleigei bar Simai ve-Rabbanan* — one opinion holds it is *Beis Taryaiki*, identified as Thrace, which fits within the Greek geographic sphere. The other opinion, cited by Rav Yosef in a *baraita* — *Tiras zu Pras* — holds that *Tiras* is Persia. This identification, of course, is the linchpin of the entire argument. If Persia descends from Tiras, and Tiras is the youngest of the sons of Yefet, then the verse *yaft Elokim l'Yefet* applies to the Persian kingdom, and Rabbi Yochanan's point stands.

One might ask: how do we know to identify *tzeirei hatzon* — "the young of the flock" — in the verse from Yirmiyahu as Persia and not as some other nation? A *talmid* visiting Eretz Yisrael raised this question before one of the *rabbinan*, citing the verse in Daniel where the ram (*ayil*) represents the kings of Media and Persia, and the he-goat (*tzfir hasayir*) represents Greece. If *tzon* can include both rams and goats, perhaps the "young of the flock" refers to Greece rather than Persia? The *talmid* was told he was asking a question born of ignorance of the *peshat*. The identification of *tzeirei hatzon* as Persia does not rest on the *pasuk* in Daniel at all. Rather, *tzeirei hatzon* means simply "the youngest of the sons" — and the youngest of the sons of Yefet is Tiras, which is Persia. No metaphor of rams or goats is involved.

### Sakastan Geography

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״סַבְתָּה וְרַעְמָה וְסַבְתְּכָא״, תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: סַקִּיסְתָּן גַּוָּיְיתָא וְסַקִּיסְתָּן בָּרַיְיתָא, בֵּין חֲדָא לַחֲדָא מְאָה פַּרְסֵי, וְהֶיקֵּפַהּ אַלְפָּא פַּרְסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The list of nations continues: "And **Sabtah and Raamah and Sabteca"** (Genesis 10:7). **Rav Yosef taught:** These are **the inner Sakistan and the outer Sakistan. Between one and the other** there was a distance of **one hundred parasangs, and the circumference** of the land was **one thousand parasangs.**

Moving through the same passage of *Parshat Noach*, the *gemara* notes, by way of Rav Yosef's teaching, that *Sabta* and *Sabtecha* — which appear in the verse listing the sons of Kush — are identified with *Skistan Gavyata*, the outer Sakastan, and *Skistan Brayta*, the inner Sakastan. This region has been identified by scholars with Sakastan or Drangiana, a territory spanning west Afghanistan and eastern Iran. The outer and inner designations envision concentric rings; the distance between them — the gap between the outer and inner circles — is a hundred *parsi*, approximately a mile, while the circumference of the outer circle is a thousand *parsi*. It should be noted that these are the sons of Kush, descendants of Cham, not sons of Yefet — a distinction the *gemara* implicitly acknowledges as it moves through the *pesukim*.

### Nimrod's Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתְּהִי רֵאשִׁית מַמְלַכְתּוֹ בָּבֶל וְאֶרֶךְ וְאַכַּד וְכַלְנֵה״, בָּבֶל — כְּמַשְׁמְעָה, אֶרֶךְ — זֶה אוֹרִיכוּת, וְאַכַּד — זֶה בַּשְׁכַּר, כַּלְנֵה — זֶה נוּפַר נִינְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues interpreting the verses. It is stated: **"And the beginning of his kingdom was Babel, and Erech, and Accad, and Calneh,** in the land of Shinar" (Genesis 10:10). **Babel in accordance with its plain meaning,** Babylonia; **Erech, that is** the city known then as **Orikhut; and Accad, that is** the place known then as **Baskar; Calneh, that is Nofer Ninefi.**

The *gemara* continues working through the genealogical narrative of *Parshat Noach*. After the listing of Yavan's descendants, the *gemara* arrives at Kush, who fathered Nimrod. The *pasuk* states: *va-tehi reishit mamlachto Bavel v'Erech v'Akad v'Calneh* — the beginning of his kingdom was Babylon, and Erech, and Akad, and Calneh. *Bavel kemashmao* — Babylonia is Babylonia, no further identification needed. *Erech* is *Orichot*, identified by archaeologists as Uruk, a major ancient city in what is now Iraq. *Akad* is *Bashkar* in the *gemara*'s terminology, corresponding to the ancient Akkadian civilization. *Calneh* is *Nofar Ninphi*, whose precise identification remains uncertain.

### Ashur's Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִן הָאָרֶץ הָהִיא יָצָא אַשּׁוּר״, תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: אַשּׁוּר — זֶה סִילַק. ״וַיִּבֶן אֶת נִינְוֵה וְאֶת רְחוֹבוֹת עִיר וְאֶת כָּלַח״, נִינְוֵה — כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, רְחוֹבוֹת עִיר — זוֹ פְּרָת דְּמֵישָׁן, כָּלַח — זוֹ פְּרָת דְּבוֹרְסִיף. ״וְאֶת רֶסֶן בֵּין נִינְוֵה וּבֵין כָּלַח הִיא הָעִיר הַגְּדוֹלָה״, רֶסֶן — זֶה אַקְטִיסְפוֹן. ״הִיא הָעִיר הַגְּדוֹלָה״ — אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם נִינְוֵה הָעִיר הַגְּדוֹלָה, אִם רֶסֶן הָעִיר הַגְּדוֹלָה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְנִינְוֵה הָיְתָה עִיר גְּדוֹלָה לֵאלֹהִים מַהֲלַךְ שְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: נִינְוֵה הִיא הָעִיר הַגְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah continues: **"Out of that land went forth Asshur"** (Genesis 10:11). **Rav Yosef taught: Asshur, that is Silek,** meaning that is the region where the town Silkiya was built. **"And built Nineveh and Rehoboth-ir and Calah"** (Genesis 10:11). **Nineveh, in accordance with its plain meaning; Rehovoth-ir, that is** the town later known as **Perat of Meishan; Calah, that is Perat of Bursif. "And Resen between Nineveh and Calah, it is the great city"** (Genesis 10:12). **Resen, that is** the town later known as **Akteisfon. It is the great city; I do not know whether** this means that **Nineveh** is **the great city,** or **whether** it means that **Resen** is **the great city. When it says: "And Nineveh was a great city of God, a three-day journey across"** (Jonah 3:3), **you must say** that **Nineveh is the great city.**

From that land, *Ashur* went out. Rav Yosef teaches that *Ashur* is *Silak* — Seleucia, one of the successor kingdoms established after Alexander divided his empire among his generals, encompassing Syria, Mesopotamia, Iran, and India, though the eastern portions quickly broke away.

*Vayiven et Ninveh* — and he built Ninveh. *V'et Rechovot Ir v'et Kalach*. Ninveh requires no identification; it is the famous city known from Sefer Yonah. *Rechovot Ir* is identified as *Perat de'Meshan* — a region along the Euphrates. *Kalach* is *Perat de'Borsi*, another location along the Euphrates.

Regarding the final clause — *v'et Resen bein Ninveh uvein Kalach hi ha'ir hagedolah* — which is the great city, Ninveh or Resen? The *Maharsha* notes that had *Kalach* been the great city, the verse would have said *v'et Kalach ha'ir hagedolah* explicitly. The word *hi* — "it is" — refers back to one of the previously named cities, making the referent either Ninveh or Resen. The *gemara* resolves this: since Yonah later says *v'Ninveh hayetah ir gedolah l'Elohim mahalach sheloshet yamim*, we derive that Ninveh is *ha'ir hagedolah*. Resen is identified as *Aktispon* — Ctesiphon, the Persian capital, a striking identification. Of course, the relative size of cities could have shifted between *Bereishit* and Yonah, but the *gemara*'s point is that the textual witness of Yonah settles the question.

### The Giants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשָׁם אֲחִימַן שֵׁשַׁי וְתַלְמַי יְלִידֵי הָעֲנָק״, תָּנָא: אֲחִימַן — מְיוּמָּן שֶׁבָּאַחִים, שֵׁשַׁי — שֶׁמֵּשִׂים אֶת הָאָרֶץ כִּשְׁחִיתוֹת, תַּלְמַי — שֶׁמֵּשִׂים אֶת הָאָרֶץ תְּלָמִים תְּלָמִים. דָּבָר אַחֵר: אֲחִימַן בָּנָה עֲנָת, שֵׁשַׁי בָּנָה אָלוּשׁ, תַּלְמַי בָּנָה תַּלְבּוּשׁ. ״יְלִידֵי הָעֲנָק״ — שֶׁמַּעֲנִיקִין הַחַמָּה בְּקוֹמָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the interpretation of names in the Bible. The Torah says: **"And there were Ahiman, Sheshai, and Talmai, the children of Anak"** (Numbers 13:22). **It was taught: Ahiman** was so called because he was the greatest and most **skillful [*meyuman*] of his brothers.** Ahiman is a contraction of brother [*aḥ*] and right [*yamin*], which is the skilled hand. **Sheshai** was so called because **he renders the ground like pits** [***sheḥitot***] with his strides. **Talmai** was so called because **he renders the ground** filled with **furrows [*telamim*]** with his strides. **Alternatively: Ahiman built** the city of **Anat; Sheshai built** the town **Alush; Talmai built** the city of **Talbush. The children of Anak** is referring to the fact that it appears **that the sun is a necklace [*shema'anikin*]** around their necks because of **their height.**

Still within the broader survey of nations and notable figures, the *gemara* turns to a passage from Bamidbar: *v'sham Achiman Sheshai v'Talmai yelidei ha'anak* — and there were Achiman, Sheshai, and Talmai, the sons of the giant, whom the scouts encountered upon entering Canaan.

A *tanna* derives their attributes from their names. *Achiman* — from the root *yamin*, the right hand — was *meyuman shebe'achin*, the strongest among the brothers. *Sheshai* — *she'mesim et ha'aretz keshchitut* — he lays the earth waste, so massive that his every step destroys what lies beneath him. *Talmai* — *she'mesim et ha'aretz telamim telamim* — he makes the earth into furrows wherever he walks, such is his enormous weight. 

A *davar acher* adds that each of these giants built a city: Achiman built the city of Anat, Sheshai built Alush, and Talmai built Talbosh. And as for *yelidei ha'anak* in general — *she'ma'anikim hachamah bekumatan* — they were so tall that the sun appeared to hang around their necks like a necklace, the *anak* suggesting a necklace or collar in Hebrew.

## Rome & Persia

### Rome Falls to Persia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר רַבִּי: עֲתִידָה רוֹמִי שֶׁתִּפּוֹל בְּיַד פָּרַס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָכֵן שִׁמְעוּ עֲצַת ה׳ אֲשֶׁר יָעַץ (עַל) אֱדוֹם וּמַחְשְׁבוֹתָיו אֲשֶׁר חָשַׁב (עַל) יוֹשְׁבֵי תֵימָן אִם לֹא יִסְחָבוּם צְעִירֵי הַצֹּאן אִם לֹא יַשִּׁים עֲלֵיהֶם נְוֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the opinion that Tiras is Persia, the Gemara addresses a related matter. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Rome is destined to fall into the hands of Persia, as it is stated: "Now hear the plan that the Lord has devised for Edom, and the thoughts He has considered for the residents of Teiman. Surely the youngest of the flock will drag them away, surely their habitation will be appalled due to them"** (Jeremiah 49:20).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said in the name of Rabbi: *atidah Romi shetipol beyad Pras* — Rome is destined to fall into the hands of Persia. To understand the significance of this statement, one must appreciate that from the perspective of *Chazal*, Rome was a colossus of apparently limitless power. The dream of Yaakov with the ladder, as interpreted in the famous *midrash*, envisions the successive kingdoms that would dominate Israel, with Rome ascending and seemingly never descending. The question — when would Roman dominion ever end? — was urgent and without an obvious answer.

At the same time, Persia during the *talmudic* period was a genuine rival power. The Sassanian Persian Empire was not a theoretical threat but a real military force on Rome's eastern frontier. Indeed, in the early seventh century, the Persians conquered the entire Middle East from Rome before eventually losing it. So the prospect of Persian dominance over Rome was not entirely fanciful. It is also worth noting the historical irony that the Babylonian *amoraim* who had this discussion were themselves living under Persian rule, giving the question of which empire would prevail an immediate and personal dimension.

The proof text is the verse from Yirmiyahu: *lachen shim'u atzat Hashem asher ya'atz el Edom... im lo yis'chavum tze'irei hatzon* — the young of the flock (*tzeirei hatzon*) will drag them away, referring to Edom, which *Chazal* identify with Rome. The identification of *Edom* with Rome, and later with Christianity through the continuity of the Holy Roman Empire, represents a layered transference in rabbinic thought: *Esav* in the Torah represents *Edom*, *Edom* in the biblical period was Rome as the subjugator of Israel, and Rome eventually became Christianity.

### Kal VaComer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאי: עֲתִידָה רוֹמִי שֶׁתִּפּוֹל בְּיַד פָּרַס, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁבְּנָאוּהוּ בְּנֵי שֵׁם וְהֶחְרִיבוּהוּ כַּשְׂדִּיִּים — נָפְלוּ כַּשְׂדִּיִּים בְּיַד פָּרְסִיִּים. מִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי שֶׁבְּנָאוּהוּ פָּרְסִיִּים וְהֶחְרִיבוּהוּ רוֹמִיִּים — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּפְּלוּ רוֹמִיִּים בְּיַד פָּרְסִיִּים?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Elai: Rome is destined to fall into the hands of Persia.** This is derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as the First Temple, that the descendants of Shem built it and the Chaldeans destroyed it,** and in turn **the Chaldeans,** ruled by Belshazzar, **fell to Persians,** ruled by Darius the Mede and his son-in-law Cyrus the Persian; **the Second Temple, that the Persians built it and the Romans destroyed it, is it not right that the Romans will fall into the hands of the Persians?**

Rabbah bar Bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yehuda be-Rabbi Ilai: *atida Romi she-tipol be-yad Pras* — Rome will fall in the hands of Persia. A *kal vachomer* is offered in support. The first *beit hamikdash* was built by the descendants of Shem — built by Shlomo — and destroyed by the Kasdim; yet the Kasdim subsequently fell before the Persians. The second *beit hamikdash* was built under Persian auspices — under Koresh — and destroyed by Rome. How much more so, then, should Rome fall to the Persians.

Even on its own terms the *kal vachomer* is not entirely compelling. One might object that the very historical sequence it cites — Persians defeating Kasdim, then Romans defeating Persians — argues against the conclusion rather than for it. The *kal vachomer* seems to be working with the logic that if the Persians, who were merely the *enablers* of the *beit hamikdash*, were capable of toppling those who destroyed the *first* Temple, how much more so will they be able to topple those who destroyed something they themselves built. But the argument's force is at best suggestive rather than probative.

### Rav's Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: עֲתִידָה פָּרַס שֶׁתִּפּוֹל בְּיַד רוֹמִי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: בָּנוֹיֵי בְּיַד סָתוֹרֵי?! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין, גְּזֵירַת מֶלֶךְ הִיא. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר (לֵיהּ): אִינְהוּ נָמֵי הָא קָא סָתְרִי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** In contrast, **Rav said: Persia is destined to fall into the hands of Rome. Rav Kahana and Rav Asi,** Rav's students, **said to Rav: The builders** will fall **into the hands of the destroyers?** Is that justice? **He said to them:** Although it seems unjust, **yes, that is the King's decree. Some say** that **he said** this **to them: They, too, are destroyers of synagogues,** and they are no better than the Romans.

Rav disagrees entirely: *atida Pras she-tipol be-yad Romi* — it is Persia that will fall into the hands of Rome, not the reverse. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged him: *banuyei be-yad saturei?* — will the builders fall in the hands of the destroyers? The Persians are the ones who allowed the *beit hamikdash* to be rebuilt; Rome destroyed it. How can it be that the builders would be overcome by the destroyers?

### Builders vs. Destroyers

Rav's response is terse: *in, gezerat ha-melech hi* — yes, it is God's decree. That is simply how history will unfold, regardless of the moral asymmetry between builders and destroyers. Some voices add that Persia is not entirely innocent in any case — *inhu nami ka satrei bei knishta* — they too destroy synagogues, and should not be idealized as the permanent good guys. A *baraita* supports Rav's position: *atida Pras she-tipol be-yad Romi*, and two reasons are given — their destruction of synagogues, and the divine decree that builders should sometimes fall before destroyers. The destroyers, after all, are proficient at destruction.

### Mashiach & Rome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁתִּפְשׁוֹט מַלְכוּת רוֹמִי הָרְשָׁעָה בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ תִּשְׁעָה חֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָכֵן יִתְּנֵם עַד עֵת יוֹלֵדָה יָלָדָה וְיֶתֶר אֶחָיו יְשׁוּבוּן עַל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **Persia is destined to fall into the hands of Rome. One** reason is that **they destroyed synagogues. And furthermore, it is the King's decree that the builders** will fall **into the hands of the destroyers, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The son of David will come only when the wicked kingdom of Rome spreads** its dominance **throughout the world for nine months, as it is stated: "Therefore He will give them up until she who is to bear has borne; then the remnants of his brethren will return with the children of Israel"** (Micah 5:2). The duration of Rome's rule over the world will be the duration of a pregnancy, nine months.

Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav: *ein Ben David ba ad she-tifshot malchut Romi ha-resha'ah be-chol ha-olam kulo tish'ah chodashim* — the son of David, the *mashiach*, will not come until the wicked kingdom of Rome has spread its dominion over the entire world for nine months. This is a sobering statement. Rather than imagining an immediate counterbalance to Roman supremacy, Rav Yehudah envisions the most desperate possible scenario: Rome reaching its maximum extent across the entire world, and only then, at the nadir, the redemption arriving.

The duration of nine months is derived from the verse in Michah: *lachen yitnem ad eit yoledah yaladah v'yeter achiv yeshuvun al bnei Yisrael* — therefore He will deliver them until the time that the woman giving birth gives birth, and the remnant of his brothers will return. The birth process takes nine months, and so Rome's universal dominion will last nine months before the *mashiach* comes.

With that, the *aggadic* passage concludes and the *gemara* returns to the *mishnah*.

## Mezuzah in Mikdash…

### Mishnah: Chambers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַלְּשָׁכוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ בְּמִקְדָּשׁ לֹא הָיוּ לָהֶן מְזוּזָה, חוּץ מִלִּשְׁכַּת פַּרְהֶדְרִין, שֶׁהָיָה בָּהּ בֵּית דִּירָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes the discussion of the High Priest's relocation to the *Parhedrin* chamber. **The Rabbis taught: None of the chambers in the Temple had a *mezuza* except for the Chamber of *Parhedrin*, in which there was a place of residence of the High Priest.** Only residences in which one sleeps require a *mezuza*, and the only chamber in the Temple that fits that description was the *Parhedrin* chamber.

*Tanu Rabbanan: kol ha-lishkot she-hayu ba-mikdash lo hayah lahen mezuzah* — all the chambers in the Temple did not have a *mezuzah*, *chutz milishkat parhedrin* — except for the *Lishkat Parhedrin*, which was designated as the residence of the *Kohen Gadol* in the seven days before Yom Kippur and therefore had a *mezuzah*.

### Rabbi Yehudah's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא כַּמָּה לְשָׁכוֹת הָיוּ בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן בֵּית דִּירָה, וְלֹא הָיָה לָהֶן מְזוּזָה? אֶלָּא לִשְׁכַּת פַּרְהֶדְרִין גְּזֵירָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said:** That is not the reason; after all, **weren't there several chambers in the Temple in which there was a place of residence** designated for priests to sit and sleep, and yet **they did not have a *mezuza*? Rather,** the *mezuza* in **the Chamber of *Parhedrin*** was there because there **was a** rabbinic **decree.**

Rabbi Yehudah objects: were there not many chambers in the *beit hamikdash* in which people lived — *kohanim* sleeping there during their watches — and none of those had a *mezuzah*? His argument is that the mere fact of living in a chamber within the *beit hamikdash* does not by itself create a *mezuzah* obligation. The case of the *Lishkat Parhedrin* was therefore exceptional, not because it was genuinely lived in but for a different reason. According to Rabbi Yehudah, the *mezuzah* in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* was not a biblical obligation at all: *ela Lishkat Parhedrin gzeira hayta* — it was a rabbinic decree, and we will see shortly what that decree was meant to accomplish. The other chambers, although inhabited, did not receive a *mezuzah*, and Rabbi Yehudah's position implies that there is a fundamental reason why chambers in the *beit hamikdash*, even inhabited ones, are exempt. Notably, in the *Tosefta* Rabbi Yehudah's position appears in the exact opposite formulation — there he holds that the inhabited chambers of the *beit hamikdash* do require a *mezuzah* — making this a significant textual tension.

## Mezuzah Analysis

### Definition of Bayit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר (רָבָא): קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כׇּל בַּיִת שֶׁאֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי לִימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְלִימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים — אֵינוֹ בַּיִת. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהִכֵּיתִי (אֶת) בֵּית הַחוֹרֶף עַל בֵּית הַקָּיִץ״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״בֵּית חוֹרֶף״ וּ״בֵית קַיִץ״ אִיקְּרִי, ״בַּיִת״ סְתָמָא לָא אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** that there was no fundamental obligation to affix a *mezuza* in the *Parhedrin* chamber, and that one was affixed there due to a decree? **Rava said** that **Rabbi Yehuda holds:** The legal status of **any house that is not designated** for residence both **for the summer and for the rainy season is not** that of **a house** and therefore does not require a *mezuza*. **Abaye raised an objection to his** opinion from a verse. How could you suggest that the legal status of a residence occupied for only part of the year is not that of a house? **Isn't it written: "I will strike the winter-house with the summer-house"** (Amos 3:15)? Apparently, even a residence occupied only half the year is a house. Rava **said to him:** A residence occupied only part of the year may be **called the winter-house or the summer-house. It is not called a house unmodified.** A house is a structure used year round.

*Amar Rabbah kasavar Rabbi Yehudah*: Rabbah explains Rabbi Yehudah's reasoning. Rabbi Yehudah holds that *kol bayit she'eino asui limot hachama ulimot hageshamim eino bayit* — any structure that is not made to be lived in throughout the year, both summer and winter, does not have the legal status of a *bayit* and therefore does not require a *mezuzah*. No room in the *beit hamikdash* is inhabited year-round; people sleep there for a few days at most. Accordingly, no room qualifies as a *bayit* for purposes of *mezuzah*.

### Winter/Summer House

Abaye challenges Rabbah's formulation. The *pasuk* in Amos states: *vehikeiti beit hachoref al beit hakayitz* — I will strike the winter house along with the summer house. These structures are called *batim* — houses — even though each is used for only part of the year. Rabbah responds: *beit choref ubeit kayitz ikri, bayit stam lo ikri* — it is called a winter house or a summer house with a modifier, but it is not called simply a *bayit* without qualification. A house that requires a seasonal descriptor to identify its function has not attained the full *halachic* status of *bayit*. The modifier itself signals its limitation. Just as a place might be called a winter house or summer house, it is still not simply a house — much as a dish might be labeled "Chicago-style pizza" rather than simply "pizza."

### Sukkah & Mezuzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: סוּכַּת הֶחָג בֶּחָג, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב בְּעֵירוּב וּבִמְזוּזָה וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a** different **objection to** the opinion of Rava, from a mishna: If one brought produce from the field into **the *sukka*** that he constructed for **the festival** of *Sukkot* **on the festival** of *Sukkot*, **Rabbi Yehuda obligates** him to tithe the produce **and the Rabbis exempt** him from tithing the produce. **And it was taught concerning** the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda obligates** the owner of that *sukka* to include the *sukka* **in** the **joining** of courtyards, like any of the houses in the courtyard; **and in** the mitzva of affixing a ***mezuza*** in the *sukka*; **and in** separating **tithes** from produce brought into the *sukka*. One is obligated to tithe his produce only when its processing has been completed. When he brings the produce into the house, he is obligated to tithe it. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the legal status of a *sukka*, in which one resides for a mere seven days, is that of a house in terms of the mitzva of *mezuza*.

Abaye then raises a more pointed challenge to Rabbah's construction of Rabbi Yehudah's position. If Rabbi Yehudah holds that only a structure suited for year-round habitation requires a *mezuzah*, why does Rabbi Yehudah rule that a *sukkah* during *Sukkot* requires a *mezuzah*? *Rabbi Yehudah mechayiv, veChachamim potrin* — Rabbi Yehudah obligates, and the *chachamim* exempt. Here the positions are exactly reversed from what we might expect: Rabbi Yehudah is the more demanding opinion regarding the *sukkah*, yet apparently more lenient regarding the chambers of the *beit hamikdash*. Furthermore, a *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehudah would require the *sukkah* to participate in an *eruv*, to have a *mezuzah*, and to have *ma'aser* separated from produce brought into it — treating the *sukkah* in all respects as a genuine dwelling.

### Ma'aser Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, בִּשְׁלָמָא עֵירוּב וּמְזוּזָה אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִדְּרַבָּנַן, אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂר, מִי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִדְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that Rabbi Yehuda rules that **by rabbinic law** the status of the *sukka* is like that of a house, but that by Torah law his opinion is consistent with Rava's opinion, **granted,** with regard to the **joining** of courtyards **and *mezuza*, it is** possible **to say** that the obligation is **by rabbinic law; however,** with regard to **tithes, is it** possible **to say** that according to Rabbi Yehuda the obligation is **by rabbinic law?**

The *gemara* pauses to clarify the *ma'aser* requirement, which is the most legally telling of the three obligations Rabbi Yehudah imposes on the *sukkah*. One might suggest that the *eruv* and *mezuzah* requirements are merely rabbinic. But if the *ma'aser* obligation applies, it must be biblical in nature — and this proves that Rabbi Yehudah treats the *sukkah* as a genuine *bayit* with full biblical force.

Why can't the *ma'aser* obligation in such a case be merely rabbinic? Because we would never institute a rabbinic *ma'aser* obligation in a category that closely resembles a biblical *ma'aser* obligation — doing so risks the person separating *trumot u'ma'asrot* from this grain to cover other grain, where one is rabbinic and the other biblical, and that cross-application would be invalid. The concern is precisely that nothing obvious distinguishes the grain brought into a *sukkah* from grain brought into a permanent house; an observer would easily assume both carry the same status, leading to the serious error of applying a *hafrashat ma'aser* that does not work across different categories of obligation.

Tosafot raise an important objection here: there are in fact documented cases of rabbinic *ma'aser* obligations, so why is the *gemara* so confident that we would not institute one here? Tosafot's answer is that when there is something that clearly marks the category as different — such as a distinct species of food — the risk of cross-application is minimal. But when grain enters a *sukkah* rather than a permanent home, there is nothing immediately apparent to an observer that signals a different legal category. Therefore in such a case we could never make the obligation merely rabbinic, and it must be biblical.

This confirms that Rabbi Yehudah treats the *sukkah* as a biblical *bayit* during *Sukkot*. Yet the *gemara* has just explained Rabbi Yehudah's leniency toward the chambers of the *beit hamikdash* on the grounds that they are not year-round dwellings. How can both positions be attributed to Rabbi Yehudah?

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּפְטוּרָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּשִׁבְעָה. וְסוּכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד, וְלִשְׁכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: During the rest of the days of the year, everyone agrees that** the *Parhedrin* chamber **is exempt** from the obligation to affix a *mezuza* there. **When they disagree** is with regard to the **seven** days that the High Priest lives there, and with regard to a *sukka* during the Festival. **And** in order to resolve the contradiction between the opinions about the obligation of the chamber and of the *sukka*, the Gemara asserts: With regard to the ***sukka* the reason is discrete, and** with regard to the **chamber the reason is discrete.**

Abaye proposes that the debate about the *sukkah* and the debate about the *Lishkat Parhedrin* are not about the same time period: during the seven days of actual use — whether the seven days of *Sukkot* or the seven days in which the *Kohen Gadol* resides in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* — everyone agrees that the obligation applies *de-oraisa*. The debate would then be only about the rest of the year.

Rava rejects this: *ve-Sukkat heChag be-chag katani* — the *mishnah* or *baraita* explicitly frames the debate about the *sukkah* as occurring during *Sukkot* itself. The disagreement between Rabbi Yehudah and the *chachamim* is not about an empty *sukkah* in the off-season; it is about the *sukkah* during the *chag*, when someone is actively living there. Therefore one cannot resolve the tension by localizing each debate to a different time period.

### Distinct Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּפְטוּרָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּשִׁבְעָה. וְסוּכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד, וְלִשְׁכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד. סוּכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: סוּכָּה דִּירַת קֶבַע בָּעֵינַן, וּמִיחַיְּיבָא בִּמְזוּזָה. וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאָמְרִי: סוּכָּה דִּירַת עֲרַאי בָּעֵינַן, וְלָא מִיחַיְּיבָא בִּמְזוּזָה. וְלִשְׁכָּה טַעְמָא לְחוּד, רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: דִּירָה בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ שְׁמָהּ דִּירָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: דִּירָה בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ לֹא שְׁמָהּ דִּירָה, וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא דְּתַקִּינוּ לַהּ, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: During the rest of the days of the year, everyone agrees that** the *Parhedrin* chamber **is exempt** from the obligation to affix a *mezuza* there. **When they disagree** is with regard to the **seven** days that the High Priest lives there, and with regard to a *sukka* during the Festival. **And** similarly, with regard to the **chamber, the reason is discrete. Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he said:** In order to fulfill the mitzva of ***sukka*, we require a** well-built **permanent residence.** A permanent residence is **obligated in** the mitzva of ***mezuza*. The Rabbis** conform **to their** standard line of **reasoning, as they say:** In order to fulfill the mitzva of ***sukka*, we require a temporary residence,** not a full-fledged house. A temporary residence is **not obligated in** the mitzva of ***mezuza*.** **And** similarly, with regard to the **chamber, the reason is discrete. The Rabbis hold: A residence** in which one resides **involuntarily is** nevertheless **considered a residence.** Although the High Priest resides in the *Parhedrin* chamber due to a mitzva and not of his own volition, its legal status is that of a residence and a *mezuza* must be affixed. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds: A residence** in which one resides **involuntarily is not considered a residence.** Therefore, there should be no obligation to affix a *mezuza* in the *Parhedrin* chamber, just as there is no obligation to do so in the other Temple chambers in which priests reside. However, the Sages **instituted** this obligation **by rabbinic law so that** people **will not say: The High Priest is imprisoned in jail,** as only in substandard residences that appear unfit for residence is there no obligation to affix a *mezuza*.

*Amar Rava*: the resolution is that each case has its own entirely distinct reasoning. *Sukkah taima le-chud ve-lishka taima le-chud* — the *sukkah* debate and the *lishka* debate are each driven by their own independent *halachic* logic, and the reversal of positions between Rabbi Yehudah and the *chachamim* in each case is not a contradiction.

Regarding the *sukkah*: *Rabbi Yehudah le-taimei de-amar sukkah diras keva ba'inan* — Rabbi Yehudah follows his own well-known position, debated at the beginning of *Masechet Sukkah*, that a *sukkah* must be a permanent structure (*diras keva*). He holds that a *sukkah* can be one hundred *amot* tall because he understands the *mitzvah* as requiring genuine permanent-style dwelling. If the *sukkah* is treated as a permanent dwelling during the *chag*, it has the full status of a *bayit* and is obligated in a *mezuzah*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold *diras arai ba'inan* — the *mitzvah* is specifically fulfilled through a temporary dwelling; a *sukkah* by definition is constructed for seven days and then dismantled. Precisely because it is inherently temporary, it never achieves *bayit* status and is exempt from *mezuzah*. This debate is purely about the *halachic* character of a *sukkah* as such.

### Forced Dwelling

Regarding the *lishka* in the *beit hamikdash*, the *chachamim* hold that *dira be'al korcha shemeih dira* — even a forced dwelling — a dwelling one is compelled to occupy against one's will — constitutes a *dira* in the full legal sense. The *kohanim* who sleep in the chambers of the *beit hamikdash* are required to be there; the *Kohen Gadol* is sequestered in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* for seven days under compulsion. Yet the *chachamim* maintain that compulsion does not negate the status of *dira*, and therefore the *mezuzah* obligation applies biblically.

*Rabbi Yehudah savar dira be'al korcha lo shemeih dira* — Rabbi Yehudah disagrees. A forced dwelling is not a genuine *dira*. Part of what constitutes a *bayit dira* is that the occupant has real authority and autonomy over the space. One who is effectively imprisoned there — even if the "imprisonment" is the sacred duty of the *Kohen Gadol* — does not truly inhabit that space in the legal sense. Therefore, no chamber in the *beit hamikdash*, however lived-in it may appear, carries a biblical *mezuzah* obligation.

### Decree on Lishkat

If Rabbi Yehudah exempts all *beit hamikdash* chambers from *mezuzah* on biblical grounds, why is there a *mezuzah* in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* at all? *U'miderabbanan hu detiknu lah shelo yomru Kohen Gadol chavush be'veis ha'asurim* — it is a rabbinic enactment, instituted so that people should not say that the *Kohen Gadol* is being held like a prisoner in a jail. Here the reasoning is internally coherent in a striking way: Rabbi Yehudah exempts the chambers precisely *because* he views forced habitation as analogous to imprisonment. And it is *because* he views the *Kohen Gadol's* sequestration as resembling imprisonment that he mandates the *mezuzah* — to visibly dispel that impression, to signal that this is a genuine dwelling place of the leader of Israel and not a detention cell.

The *chachamim* and Rabbi Yehudah thus agree on the *practical* outcome for the *Lishkat Parhedrin* — it has a *mezuzah* — but disagree on the legal basis. The *chachamim* hold it is a biblical obligation, because forced habitation is still *dira*. Rabbi Yehudah holds it is a rabbinic enactment, because forced habitation negates *dira* status, but the enactment is necessary to ensure the dignity and perceived freedom of the *Kohen Gadol*. We will continue with this analysis on the next *daf*.
