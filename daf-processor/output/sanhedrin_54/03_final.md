# Sanhedrin 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mother vs. Eshet Av

### Opening: Dual Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתַנְיָא עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם הָאֵם בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Yitzḥak came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he taught** a *baraita* **as we learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One who engages in intercourse with his mother who is also his father's wife **is liable** to bring **only** one sin-offering, **due to the** fact that she is his **mother,** whether or not her marriage to his father was legitimate.

Daf 54 continues the discussion of those liable to *sekilah*, working through a range of capital sexual transgressions. We pick up at the bottom of 53b with the debate between Reb Yehuda and the *chachamim* regarding a man who has intercourse with his mother — where his mother is simultaneously his father's wife. Is he liable for two distinct identities, or only one? Reb Yehuda maintains he is liable only for the category of *ema* — mother — and not for the category of *eshet av*, father's wife. His implicit reasoning is that the prohibition of *eshet av* concerns a stepmother specifically, and the overlap with one's biological mother does not multiply the liability.

### Reb Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אִמְּךָ הִיא״. מִשּׁוּם אִמּוֹ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** for this? **Abaye says:** It is **because the verse states:** "The nakedness of your father and the nakedness of your mother you shall not uncover: **She is your mother;** you shall not uncover her nakedness" (Leviticus 18:7). Rabbi Yehuda derives from this verse that **you shall render him liable due to** the fact that she is **his mother, but you shall not render him liable** to bring another sin-offering **due to** the fact that she is his **father's wife.**

The *Gemara* asks: *ta'ama mai?* — what is the basis for Reb Yehuda's position? Abaye answers that the verse states *imcha hi* — "she is your mother" (Vayikra 18:7). The word *hi* functions to limit the transgression: you hold him liable *mishum ima*, on account of her being his mother, *v'ei ata mechayvo mishum eshet av* — and you do not hold him liable on account of her being his father's wife.

### Contradiction in Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״עֶרְוַת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ הִיא״, מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָב אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אִמּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** it should be derived from the verse: **"The nakedness of your father's wife you shall not uncover; it is your father's nakedness"** (Leviticus 18:8), that **you shall render him liable due to** the fact that she is his **father's wife, but you shall not render him liable due to** the fact that she is **his mother.**

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reading. The verse concerning *eshet av* states: *ervat eshet avicha lo tegaleh, ervat avicha hi* — "the nakedness of your father's wife you shall not uncover, for she is your father's nakedness" (Vayikra 18:8). Here too, the word *hi* appears. If the *hi* in the verse about the mother excludes the *eshet av* identity, then by the same logic the *hi* in the verse about the father's wife would exclude the mother identity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו, קָאֵי הָכָא וּמְמַעֵט לַהּ, קָאֵי הָכָא וּמְמַעֵט לַהּ. הַשְׁתָּה, אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו – מִיחַיַּיב, אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁאֵינָהּ אִמּוֹ – מִיחַיַּיב, אִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו – לָא מִיחַיַּיב כְּלָל?
>
> **Translation:** **But** if so, with regard to one's **mother who is his father's wife,** it is found that the verse **stands,** i.e., is written, **here,** with regard to intercourse with one's mother, **and excludes** liability due to the fact that she is his father's wife, and **stands there,** with regard to intercourse with one's father's wife, **and excludes** liability due to the fact that she is his mother. The result **now** is that for **his mother who is not his father's wife he is rendered liable,** and for **his father's wife who is not his mother he is rendered liable,** but for **his mother who is his father's wife he is not rendered liable at all,** which seems absurd.

The result is a direct contradiction. When one encounters a woman who is both his mother and his father's wife, the verse about the mother would say: hold him liable only for *ima*, not for *eshet av*. The verse about the father's wife would say: hold him liable only for *eshet av*, not for *ima*. The two verses cancel each other out — the woman who is both is apparently excluded from both categories. The *Gemara* states this explicitly: *hashta imo she'einah eshet aviv mechayve, eshet aviv she'einah imo mechayve* — a plain mother and a plain father's wife are each clear cases of liability; yet *imo she'hi eshet aviv, lo mechayve klal* — the woman who is both winds up excluded from liability entirely?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, לְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״אִמְּךָ הִיא״? אֶלָּא, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, according to the Rabbis too,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda and hold that one who engages in intercourse with his mother who is married to his father is liable to bring two sin-offerings, **isn't** the verse: **"She is your mother," written;** i.e., how do they explain this verse? **Rather, that** phrase is **necessary for** the *halakha* derived from it by **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi,** as the Gemara will soon explain (54a). Therefore, **according to Rabbi Yehuda as well, that** phrase is **necessary for** the *halakha* derived by **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi,** and Rabbi Yehuda's opinion in the mishna cannot be derived from it.

Furthermore, the *Gemara* notes that even for the *rabbanan* who disagree with Reb Yehuda, the phrase *imcha hi* requires some interpretation — and so neither reading of the *hi* can simply stand in isolation.

### Ervata Reading Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״עֶרְוָתָהּ״, מִשּׁוּם עֶרְוָה אַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי עֲרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says** that there is a different source for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **The verse states:** "You shall not uncover **her nakedness [*ervatah*],"** in the singular form, from which it is derived that **you shall render him liable for one forbidden sexual relationship [*erva*], but you shall not render him liable for two** types of **forbidden sexual relationships.**

The *Gemara* then searches for an alternative basis for Reb Yehuda's position. Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika proposes reading the word *ervata* at the end of the verse — *lo tegaleh ervata*, "you shall not uncover her nakedness" — as restricting liability: *mishum ervah achat ata mechayvo v'ei ata mechayvo mishum shtei arayos* — you hold him liable for one identity, not two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״עֶרְוַת כַּלָּתְךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה אֵשֶׁת בִּנְךָ הִיא לֹא תְגַלֶּה עֶרְוָתָהּ״ – הָכִי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם עֶרְוָה אַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי עֲרָיוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then from the verse: **"You shall not uncover the nakedness of your daughter-in-law; she is your son's wife, you shall not uncover her nakedness"** (Leviticus 18:15), should it **also** be derived that **you shall render him liable for one forbidden sexual relationship, but you shall not render him liable for two** types of **forbidden sexual relationships?**

The *Gemara* immediately objects: the very same word *ervata* appears in the verse concerning the daughter-in-law — *ervat kalatcha lo tegaleh, eshet bincha hi, lo tegaleh ervata*. If *ervata* means liability is limited to one identity, then by the same reasoning a man who has intercourse with his daughter-in-law should be liable only for the status of *kallah* and not for the additional status of *eshet ish*, a married woman. Yet the *Mishnah* explicitly states that one who has intercourse with his daughter-in-law is liable *mishum kalato u'mishum eshet ish* — for both identities — and Reb Yehuda does not dispute that ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַבָּא עַל כַּלָּתוֹ חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם כַּלָּתוֹ וּמִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, בֵּין בְּחַיֵּי בְנוֹ בֵּין לְאַחַר מִיתַת בְּנוֹ, וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One who engages in intercourse with his daughter-in-law** is **liable** to bring two sin-offerings **for** his intercourse with **her:** One **due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with one's **daughter-in-law, and** one **due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with **a married woman;** the former liability applies **both during his son's lifetime and after his son's death. And Rabbi Yehuda does not dispute** this *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כֵּיוָן דְּחַד גּוּפָא הוּא, אַף עַל גַּב דִּתְרֵי אִיסּוּרֵי נִינְהוּ, כְּתִיב ״עֶרְוָתָהּ״. הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּחַד גּוּפָא הוּא, אַף עַל גַּב דִּתְרֵי אִיסּוּרֵי נִינְהוּ, כְּתִיב ״עֶרְוָתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be explained that **since** the daughter-in-law has **one body, although** engaging in intercourse with her involves **two prohibitions,** the term **"her nakedness [*ervatah*],"** in the singular, **is written. Here too,** with regard to one's father's wife, **since** she has **one body, although** there **are two prohibitions,** the term **"her nakedness [*ervatah*]" is written.**

The *Gemara* resolves the daughter-in-law case by noting: *keivan de'chad gufa hi af al gav de'trei issurei ninhu, ktiv ervata* — since it is one body, even though there are two prohibited statuses, the word *ervata* is written. The single *ervata* does not exclude a second identity; it simply reflects that we are speaking of a single person. But this resolution defeats the whole reading: if *ervata* is consistent with double liability, it cannot be the source for Reb Yehuda's limiting principle by the case of the mother either.

### Rava's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ״ – זוֹ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ, וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says** that there is an alternative source for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that concerning the meaning of the phrase **"the nakedness of your father," this is** referring to **your father's wife. And he derives** this interpretation **by means of a verbal analogy** from the verse: "And the man who lies with his father's wife, he has uncovered his father's nakedness" (Leviticus 20:11).

With both the *hi* reading and the *ervata* reading rejected, Rava offers a third and ultimately successful solution. *Ela amar Rava kasvar Rav Yehuda: ervat avicha zo eshet avicha* — Reb Yehuda understands the phrase *ervat avicha* to mean *eshet avicha*, "your father's wife." This is, in fact, a natural reading: the prohibition in the verse for *eshet av* reads *ervat eshet avicha lo tegaleh, ervat avicha hi*, where the suffix *eruption of your father* is explained by the earlier phrase as referring to the father's wife. Rava proposes that this equivalence — *ervat avicha* means *eshet avicha* — is established through a *gezeirah shavah* and carries over to the prohibition verse as well.

### How Verses Align

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַשְׁמַע, בֵּין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אִמּוֹ בֵּין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא אִמּוֹ. אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת אִמְּךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה אִמְּךָ הִיא״. מִשּׁוּם אִמּוֹ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Torah **indicates** that she is forbidden **whether** she is **his father's wife who is his mother** or **whether** she is **his father's wife who is not his mother. From where** is it derived that one who engages in intercourse with **his mother who is not his father's wife** is liable? **The verse states** subsequently: **"The nakedness of your mother you shall not uncover."** And from the ensuing phrase: **"She is your mother,"** it is derived that in a case where his mother is also his father's wife, **you shall render him liable due to** the fact that she is **his mother, but you shall not render him liable due to** the fact that she is **his father's wife.**

Under Rava's reading, the verses work together as follows. The prohibition verse in *Acharei Mot* reads: *ervat avicha v'ervat imcha lo tegaleh, imcha hi, lo tegaleh ervata*. The phrase *ervat avicha* — now understood to mean *eshet avicha*, father's wife — includes all cases of father's wife: whether she is also the man's mother or not. *V'ervat imcha* continues, and *imcha hi* tells you that for all categories of mother — whether she is also the father's wife or not — the relevant liability is only *mishum ima*.

This structure cleanly accounts for the entire picture. *Ervat avicha* (= *eshet avicha*) encompasses father's wife who is also a mother, and father's wife who is not a mother. *V'ervat imcha... imcha hi* then covers all mothers, including the mother who is the father's wife and the mother who is not. For Reb Yehuda, *imcha hi* functions precisely here: once a woman who is both mother and father's wife has been drawn into the category of *ervat avicha* (because she is father's wife), the verse then says that for all such women, the only operative liability is *imcha* — mother. This is not a contradiction but a hierarchy: the verse first includes her and then specifies the exclusive category of her liability.

A natural question arises: if this is the intent, why didn't the verse simply say *ervat eshet avicha* instead of the shorter *ervat avicha*? The answer is precisely that the vaguer phrasing *ervat avicha* allows the verse to capture the mother who is the father's wife, not merely the stepmother. By leaving the phrase open, the *drasha* can then clarify that she is covered — but covered exclusively under the category of mother.

### Tannaitic Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״אִישׁ״ – פְּרָט לְקָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava:** With regard to the verse: "And the man who lies with his father's wife, he has uncovered his father's nakedness; both of them shall be put to death, their blood shall be upon them" (Leviticus 20:11), the term: **"The man," excludes a minor.**

The *Gemara* concludes this section with *tanya kavatei d'Rava* — a *braita* is taught in accordance with Rava's interpretation. This *braita* derives from the *Sifra*, the Tannaic midrash on *Vayikra*, whose anonymous voice (*stam Sifra*) is identified by the *Gemara* as representing the position of Reb Yehuda. Rashi notes this point explicitly, making clear that this *drasha* is Reb Yehuda's own reading, which will now also allow us to understand how the *rabbanan* depart from it.

## Azharah & Onesh

### Braita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו״ – מַשְׁמָע בֵּין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אִמּוֹ, וּבֵין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא אִמּוֹ. אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו גִּלָּה״, מוּפְנֶה לְהַקִּישׁ וְלָדוּן מִמֶּנּוּ גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"who lies with his father's wife" indicates** that he is liable to receive capital punishment **whether** she is **his father's wife who is his mother or whether** she is **his father's wife who is not his mother. From where** is it derived that he is liable in a case where she is **his mother who is not his father's wife? The verse states: "He has uncovered his father's nakedness."** Although this phrase does not relate directly to the case of one's mother who is not his father's wife, the *halakha* in this case is derived from this phrase as it is **free,** i.e., the phrase is superfluous in this context, and is evidently included in the verse in order **to compare** between the two cases **and learn a verbal analogy from it,** as the *baraita* will elaborate below.

The *braita* cited in this section moves the discussion from the prohibition verses (*acharei mot*) to the punishment verses (*kedoshim*). The key verse is: *va'ish asher yishkav et eshet aviv, ervat aviv gillah, mot yumatu, dmeihem bam* — "and a man who lies with his father's wife has uncovered his father's nakedness; they shall both be put to death, their blood is upon them" (Vayikra 20:11). This verse is the source for *sekilah* in the case of *eshet av*. However, mother is entirely absent from the punishment verses. The *braita* proceeds to establish both the punishment and prohibition for all relevant cases.

### Ervat Avicha Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹת יוּמְתוּ״ – בִּסְקִילָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּסְקִילָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל מִיתוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״דְּמֵיהֶם בָּם״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּאוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי ״דְּמֵיהֶם בָּם״. מָה לְהַלָּן בִּסְקִילָה, אַף כָּאן בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** From the phrase "both of them **shall be put to death"** it is derived that they are executed **by stoning.** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that that they are executed **by stoning, or is it rather by one of all the** other types of the **death** penalty **that are stated in the Torah?** The *baraita* answers: **It is stated here: "Their blood shall be upon them," and it is stated with regard to a necromancer and a sorcerer: "Their blood shall be upon them"** (Leviticus 20:27). **Just as there** the verse states that a necromancer and a sorcerer are executed **by stoning, so too here,** with regard to one who engages in intercourse with his father's wife, the transgressors are executed **by stoning.**

The *braita* begins: *ish* — a man, *prat le'katan*, excluding a minor. *Asher yishkav et eshet aviv* — this phrase encompasses *bein eshet aviv she'hi imo bein eshet aviv she'einah imo* — whether the father's wife is also the man's mother or not. The father's wife is thus included in the punishment verse regardless of the dual-identity question.

### Ervat Aviv Gila Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנֶשׁ שָׁמַעְנוּ, אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה״. ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ״ – זוֹ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **We have learned** the **punishment** for one who engages in intercourse with his father's wife. **From where** is the **prohibition** against doing this act derived? The *baraita* answers: **The verse states: "The nakedness of your father** and the nakedness of your mother **you shall not uncover"** (Leviticus 18:7); the phrase **"the nakedness of your father" is** referring to **your father's wife.**

What remains unaddressed is the case of a mother who is not the father's wife. She is simply absent from the punishment verse. The *braita* derives her inclusion from the extra phrase *ervat aviv gillah* — "he has uncovered his father's nakedness." This phrase, which follows the explicit prohibition of sleeping with the father's wife, is treated as containing additional meaning. Ironically — given that in the prohibition verse *ervat avicha* referred to the father's wife — in this punishment context, *ervat aviv gillah* comes to include the plain mother, the woman who is not the father's wife at all. The *braita* identifies this phrase as *mufneh* — free, available for use in a *gezeirah shavah* — and reserves it for that derivation to follow.

### Prohibition Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ״, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ מַמָּשׁ? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו גִּלָּה״. מָה לְהַלָּן בְּאִישׁוּת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אַף כָּאן בְּאִישׁוּת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that the reference is to **your father's wife, or is it rather** referring to **the nakedness of your father literally,** i.e., to homosexual intercourse with one's father? The *baraita* answers: **It is stated here: "The nakedness of your father…you shall not uncover," and it is stated there,** in the verse that describes the punishment: "And the man who lies with his father's wife, **he has uncovered his father's nakedness"** (Leviticus 20:11). **Just as there, the verse is speaking of marriage,** i.e., it is not referring to the father himself but to his wife, **so too here, the verse is speaking of marriage,** i.e., his father's wife.

The *braita* then turns to the *azharah*, the prohibition. *Onesh shamanu, azharah minayin?* — We have the punishment, but where is the prohibition established? The answer comes from *Acharei Mot*: *talmud lomar ervat avicha lo tegaleh v'ervat imcha* — the verse that opens with *ervat avicha* and continues with *v'ervat imcha*. The *braita* asks: when the verse says *ervat avicha*, does it mean literally intercourse with the father himself, or does it mean *eshet avicha*, father's wife? To resolve this, it invokes a verbal analogy: the verse here says *ervat avicha lo tegaleh*, and the punishment verse says *ervat aviv gillah*. Since *ervat aviv gillah* in context clearly speaks of a woman — the man slept with a woman — so too *ervat avicha* in the prohibition verse speaks of a woman, specifically, one defined as *ervat avicha*, the father's nakedness, which means the father's wife. The *drasha* concludes: *u'mashma bein eshet aviv she'hi imo bein eshet aviv she'einah imo* — and this covers father's wife whether or not she is also the mother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַשְׁמָע, בֵּין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אִמּוֹ בֵּין אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא אִמּוֹ. אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת אִמְּךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the verse **indicates** that one's father's wife is forbidden to him **whether** she is **his father's wife who is his mother** or **whether** she is **his father's wife who is not his mother. From where** is it derived that she is forbidden to him in a case where she is **his mother who is not his father's wife? The verse states: "The nakedness of your mother you shall not uncover"** (Leviticus 18:7).

Mothers who are not the father's wife are then added from the continuation of the verse: *v'ervat imcha lo tegaleh* — which the *braita* takes as separately establishing that case. The *braita* then draws the equalization: the Torah has made a mother who is not the father's wife equivalent to a mother who is the father's wife. Both are prohibited. The *braita* concludes this portion by affirming: *im ken lama ne'emar* — why was it stated — meaning, what does the explicit inclusion of both cases in this single verse accomplish? It establishes that both are treated identically with respect to the prohibition.

### Gezeirah Shavah Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּאַזְהָרָה, שֶׁעָשָׂה הַכָּתוּב אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו כְּאִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו. עוֹנֶשׁ מִנַּיִין?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **I have** derived **only with regard to** the **prohibition that the verse renders** the *halakha* of **his mother who is not his father's wife like** that of **his mother who is his father's wife.** But with regard to the **punishment, from where** do I derive that they share the same *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו גִּלָּה״. מָה בְּאַזְהָרָה עָשָׂה הַכָּתוּב אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו כְּאִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו, אַף בְּעוֹנֶשׁ עָשָׂה הַכָּתוּב אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו כְּאִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers, expounding on the verbal analogy it mentioned earlier: **It is stated here,** in the verse that describes the prohibition: **"The nakedness of your father…you shall not uncover"** (Leviticus 18:7), **and it is stated there,** in the verse that describes the punishment: **"He has uncovered his father's nakedness"** (Leviticus 20:11). It is derived from this verbal analogy that **just as with regard to** the **prohibition, the verse renders his mother who is not his father's wife like his mother who is his father's wife,** i.e., both are forbidden, **so too, with regard to** the **punishment, the verse renders his mother who is not his father's wife like his mother who is his father's wife.**

The *gezeirah shavah* left pending earlier now operates: the phrase *ervat avicha lo tegaleh* in the prohibition verse and *ervat aviv gillah* in the punishment verse share the same terminology, making them formally linked. This link allows the rules of the punishment verse — including its scope — to carry over to the prohibition verse and vice versa. Specifically, just as the prohibition verse is shown to include mother who is not the father's wife, the same extension applies in the context of the punishment: *af b'onesh asa ha'katuv imo she'einah eshet aviv k'imo she'hi eshet aviv* — just as in the prohibition the Torah treated a mother who is not the father's wife like a mother who is the father's wife, so too in the punishment both receive the same consequence.

This is exactly the point the *braita* was holding in reserve when it identified *ervat aviv gillah* as *mufneh l'gezeirah shavah*. It serves double duty: first, it introduces the plain mother into the punishment verse in a preliminary way; second, it anchors the *gezeirah shavah* that formally unifies the two verses and extends punishment to the plain mother on the basis of the equalization established in the prohibition verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִמְּךָ הִיא״ – מִשּׁוּם אִמּוֹ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָב.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from the phrase: **"She is your mother"** (Leviticus 18:7), that **you render him liable due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with **his mother, but you do not render him liable due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with his **father's wife.** The *baraita* ends here. Since the *halakhot* in the collection of *baraitot* where this *baraita* appears, *Torat Kohanim*, are in accordance with the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda, the *baraita* supports the opinion of Rava, who explains the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda in this manner.

The *braita* then completes Reb Yehuda's position: *mishum imo ata mechayvo v'ei ata mechayvo mishum eshet aviv* — you hold him liable on account of his mother, and you do not hold him liable on account of the father's wife. Once the mother who is also father's wife has been included in the prohibition verse under *ervat avicha*, and the verse concludes *imcha hi* — equating all mothers — the only operative liability is the mother identity. This is Reb Yehuda's full position, and the *Gemara* confirms that it appears in the anonymous voice of the *Sifra*, which *halacha* identifies with Reb Yehuda.

## Rabbanan Position

### Literal Father Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ״ – מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the *baraita*, asking: **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda, interpret the phrase **"the nakedness of your father"?** The Gemara answers: They hold that this phrase is meant **literally,** i.e., that it is referring to homosexual intercourse. They do not accept the verbal analogy from which Rabbi Yehuda derives that the reference is to intercourse with one's father's wife.

Having established Reb Yehuda's reading in full, the *Gemara* turns to the *rabbanan*. The critical divergence concerns the phrase *ervat avicha* in the prohibition verse. For Reb Yehuda, as we have seen, *ervat avicha* means *eshet avicha* — the father's wife. For the *rabbanan*, however, *ervat avicha mamash* — *ervat avicha* is taken literally, as referring to intercourse with the father himself. This reading forecloses Reb Yehuda's entire structure: the *rabbanan* cannot use *ervat avicha* to include the mother who is also father's wife and then conclude *imcha hi* limits liability to one category, because for them the phrase is not about the father's wife at all.

### Double Liability Principle

As a consequence of this reading, the *rabbanan* hold that when a woman is both one's mother and one's father's wife, both identities generate independent liability. There is no verse limiting the transgression to a single status. This is the *rabbanan*'s straightforward position: *chayav shtayim* — liable twice, once for the mother prohibition and once for the *eshet av* prohibition.

### Rav Yehuda's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִ״וְּאֶת זָכָר״ נָפְקָא? לְחַיֵּיב עָלָיו שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't **this** prohibition against homosexual intercourse with one's father **derived from** the verse: **"And** you shall not lie **with a male** as with a woman; it is an abomination" (Leviticus 18:22)? The Gemara answers: The prohibition is stated specifically with regard to one's father in order **to render him liable** to bring **two** sin-offerings for unwittingly engaging in intercourse with his father.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: נׇכְרִי הַבָּא עַל אָבִיו – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, הַבָּא עַל אֲחִי אָבִיו – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Yehuda, as Rav Yehuda says: A gentile who engages in intercourse with his father** is **liable** for committing **two** transgressions. Likewise, **one who engages in intercourse with his father's brother** is **liable** for committing **two** transgressions.

The *Gemara* introduces the teaching of Rav Yehuda (not to be confused with Reb Yehuda the *tanna*): *nokhri ha'ba al aviv, chayav shtayim* — a non-Jew who has intercourse with his father is liable twice. Similarly, one who has intercourse with his father's brother (*achi aviv*) is liable twice — once for intercourse with a male and once under a separate prohibition derived from *ervat akhi avicha*. This is the teaching that supports the *rabbanan*'s reading of *ervat avicha mamash* as referring to literal intercourse with the father, generating a double prohibition.

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּשׁוֹגֵג, וּבְקׇרְבָּן. וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר נׇכְרִי – לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ נׇכְרִי מַמָּשׁ, דִּינוֹ מַאי נִיהוּ? קְטָלָא! בִּתְרֵי קְטָלֵי קָטְלַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** It **stands to reason** that **the statement of Rav Yehuda is with regard to a Jew** who does this **unwittingly. And** the statement that he is liable for committing two transgressions **concerns** his liability to bring **an offering,** i.e., he is liable to bring two sin-offerings. **And** even though the fact remains **that he said gentile, it is a euphemism,** as he did not want to attribute such a sin to a Jew. **As if it enters your mind** that the reference is **literally** to **a gentile,** the statement that he is liable for committing two transgressions is meaningless; **what is his punishment** for such a transgression? It is **death.** Could **you kill him twice?** Rather, it must be referring to a Jew who acted unwittingly.

Rava clarifies: *mistabra milta de'Rav Yehuda b'Yisrael, be'shogeg, u've'korban* — the practical import of Rav Yehuda's ruling is in the case of a Jew, acting unintentionally, with respect to the obligation of a sin offering. This is the context in which counting separate liabilities actually matters. The *Gemara* explains why the non-Jew reading cannot be taken literally: *dino mai nihu? katala* — the only legal consequence for a non-Jew in the *Bnei Noach* framework is execution, and there is no *hatra'ah* (formal warning) required and no *korbanot* system. Saying someone is liable twice is meaningless if the only consequence is a single execution. Therefore, the discussion must concern a Jew acting *be'shogeg*, where the number of separate liabilities determines the number of *korbanot*.

### Tannaitic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַבָּא עַל אָבִיו – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, הַבָּא עַל אֲחִי אָבִיו – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who engages in intercourse with his father** is **liable** for committing **two** transgressions. **One who engages in intercourse with his father's brother** is **liable** for committing **two** transgressions, as it is stated: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of your father's brother" (Leviticus 18:14).

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* that corroborates this: one who comes upon his father is liable twice; one who comes upon his father's brother is liable twice. The *braita* then records a dispute. One view holds this teaching goes against Reb Yehuda, since Reb Yehuda reads *ervat avicha* as *eshet avicha* and would therefore have no basis for treating intercourse with the father as a separate prohibition. A second view says even Reb Yehuda could agree that intercourse with the father is prohibited, deriving it through a *kal vachomer* from the case of the father's brother: if one is doubly liable for intercourse with the father's brother, who is merely a relative of the father, then certainly one would be doubly liable for intercourse with the father himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say that** this is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as in his opinion there is no special prohibition against homosexual intercourse with one's father. He interprets the verse: "The nakedness of your father…you shall not uncover" as referring to one's father's wife. Accordingly, one who engages in homosexual intercourse with his father or with his father's brother is liable only due to the general prohibition against homosexual intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵאֲחִי אָבִיו. וּמָה אֲחִי אָבִיו, דְּקוּרְבָה דְּאָבִיו הוּא, חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם – אָבִיו לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **And some say: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** that **he derives** that there is a specific prohibition against homosexual intercourse with one's father **by an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the prohibition concerning one's **father's brother. And** the inference is as follows: **If** for intercourse with one's **father's brother, who is** merely **his father's relative,** one is **liable** for committing **two** transgressions, for intercourse with **his father,** is it **not** clear **all the more so** that he should be liable for committing two transgressions?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּאַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא. מָר סָבַר: עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Sages who provide these two interpretations of Rabbi Yehuda's opinion **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Abaye and Rava.** One **Sage,** he of the second interpretation, **holds** that **one administers punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.** Even with regard to a prohibition that is derived *a fortiori*, one who transgresses it is liable. **And** one **Sage,** he of the first interpretation, **holds** that **one does not administer punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.**

The *Gemara* identifies this as part of a broader dispute between Abaye and Rava: *onshin min hadin* — whether a punishment can be derived through a *kal vachomer*. Those who permit such derivations would allow Reb Yehuda to hold this position; those who do not would say that without an explicit verse Reb Yehuda has no basis to include the father as a separate prohibition.

### Eshet Av After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, אַזְהָרָה לְאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ מֵ״עֶרְוַת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** who disagree with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, **from where do they** derive **the prohibition of** intercourse with one's **father's wife?** The Gemara answers: **They derive it from** the verse: **"The nakedness of your father's wife you shall not uncover;** it is your father's nakedness" (Leviticus 18:8).

The *Gemara* then asks: where do the *rabbanan* derive the prohibition (*azharah*) for *eshet av*? Since for the *rabbanan* the phrase *ervat avicha* in the prohibition verse refers to literal intercourse with the father — not the father's wife — they cannot use that verse as their source for the *eshet av* prohibition. The answer is that they derive it from a separate verse: *ervat eshet avicha lo tegaleh* (Vayikra 18:8) — a verse that explicitly names the father's wife. This verse is entirely available to the *rabbanan* for that purpose precisely because they did not need to redirect it toward the literal-father reading the way Reb Yehuda does.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאַזְהָרָה לְאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive from this verse, since he derives the prohibition from the verse: "The nakedness of your father…you shall not uncover"? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is necessary for the prohibition of** one's **father's wife after** his father's **death;** even though his father is dead, his father's wife remains forbidden to him.

For Reb Yehuda, by contrast, the verse *ervat eshet avicha lo tegaleh* is needed for an additional derivation: it establishes that the prohibition of *eshet av* applies *l'achar mitat aviv* — even after the father has died. This is a significant *chiddush*: one might have assumed the prohibition ends with the marriage, i.e., when the father is alive and she is currently his wife. The separate verse teaches that even after the father's death the prohibition remains in force.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַהוּא מִסֵּיפָא דִּקְרָא נָפְקָא: ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיךָ הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where do **the Rabbis** derive that *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha* **is derived from the last clause of the verse: "It is your father's nakedness."**

The *rabbanan*, not needing that verse for the prohibition of *eshet av* in general (since they derive it from the literal reading of *ervat avicha*), derive the post-death prohibition from elsewhere — from the end of the verse itself: *ervat avicha hi*, which for them is a superfluous phrase that extends the prohibition to the period after the father's death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָב אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive from that clause? The Gemara answers: **That** clause **is necessary to** teach the *halakha* that if one engages in intercourse with his father's wife, **you render him liable due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with his **father's wife, but you do not render him liable due to** the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with **a married woman.**

In turn, the *Gemara* asks: what does Reb Yehuda do with the phrase *ervat avicha hi* at the end of that verse, if he has already used the verse for the post-death extension through the *gezeirah shavah*? Reb Yehuda uses it for the limiting principle parallel to *imcha hi*: just as *imcha hi* says that for a mother who is also a father's wife, you are liable only for the mother identity, *ervat avicha hi* analogously says that if the father's wife is also currently a married woman (*eshet ish*), you are liable only for the *eshet av* identity, not additionally for *eshet ish*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת אָב, חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָב וּמִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, בֵּין בְּחַיֵּי אָבִיו בֵּין לְאַחַר מִיתַת אָבִיו, וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One who engages in intercourse with** his **father's wife** is **liable** to bring two offerings, one **due to** the fact that she is his **father's wife and** one **due to** the fact that she is **a married woman,** and he is liable due to the former prohibition **both during his father's lifetime** and **after his father's death?** The Gemara notes: **And Rabbi Yehuda does not dispute** this. So how can it be suggested that in Rabbi Yehuda's opinion, one who engages in intercourse with his father's wife is not liable for engaging in intercourse with a married woman?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּלִיג בְּבָרַיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: He disputes** this ruling **in a *baraita*.** Although it is not mentioned in the mishna that Rabbi Yehuda disputes this ruling, it is mentioned in a different source.

The *Gemara* challenges this: the *Mishnah* rules explicitly that one who has intercourse with his father's wife transgresses both *mishum eshet av u'mishum eshet ish*, whether the father is alive or dead — and Reb Yehuda does not dispute the *Mishnah* in that case. Abaye resolves this: even though Reb Yehuda did not register disagreement with the *Mishnah*, a *braita* records that he in fact holds this limiting position there as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, עוֹנֶשׁ דְּאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו לְאַחַר מִיתָה מְנָא לְהוּ? בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַיְיתֵי לַהּ בִּגְזֵרָה שָׁוָה, אֶלָּא רַבָּנַן מְנָא לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, from where do they** derive **the punishment for** one who engages in intercourse with **his father's wife after** his father's **death? Granted,** according to **Rabbi Yehuda, it is derived by means of a verbal analogy. But from where do the Rabbis,** who do not accept the verbal analogy, derive it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לָךְ: הָהוּא ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו גִּלָּה״ דְּמַפֵּיק לַהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מַפְּקִי לֵיהּ אִינְהוּ לְעוֹנֶשׁ דְּאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The Rabbis can **say to you** that it is derived as follows: With regard to **that** phrase, **"he has uncovered his father's nakedness," from which Rabbi Yehuda derives a verbal analogy, they derive from it the punishment for** one who engages in intercourse with **his father's wife after** his father's **death.**

The section concludes with the question of where the *rabbanan* derive the punishment (*onesh*) for *eshet av* after the father's death. For Reb Yehuda this follows from the *gezeirah shavah*: once the *gezeirah shavah* links the prohibition and punishment verses, the extension of the prohibition to the post-death period carries over to the punishment as well. For the *rabbanan*, who do not employ that *gezeirah shavah* (having used the free phrase *ervat aviv gillah* for the post-death extension of *eshet av* rather than for the *gezeirah shavah* itself), the punishment for *eshet av* after death is derived from that very phrase — *machaziki lehu l'onesh eishet aviv l'achar misa*.

## Azharah & Onesh (II)

### Mother's Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, עוֹנֶשׁ לְאִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מְנָא לְהוּ? אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: אָמַר קְרָא ״אִמְּךָ הִיא״. עֲשָׂאָהּ הַכָּתוּב לְאִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו כְּאִמּוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, from where do they** derive **the punishment for** one who engages in intercourse with **his mother who is not his father's wife? Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, says: The verse states: "She is your mother"** (Leviticus 18:7). **The verse renders** the *halakha* of **his mother who is not his father's wife like** that of **his mother who is his father's wife.**

The *Gemara* resolves this with the ruling of Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi: *amar kra, imcha hi*. The *rabbanan* use the phrase *imcha hi* — "she is your mother" — to equate all categories of mother: *asao ha'katuv l'imo she'einah eshet aviv k'imo she'hi eshet aviv* — the Torah has treated a mother who is not the father's wife identically to a mother who is the father's wife. Since the punishment verse includes the mother who is also the father's wife (by virtue of *asher yishkav et eshet aviv*), the phrase *imcha hi* — which the *rabbanan* deploy for this equalization rather than for Reb Yehuda's limiting principle — teaches that all the same laws that apply to a mother who is also the father's wife apply equally to a plain mother who is not. In this way, the *rabbanan* derive the punishment of *sekilah* for the plain mother from *imcha hi*, while Reb Yehuda uses the same phrase to restrict double liability. The two positions share a text but read its function in precisely opposite directions.

## Mishkav Zachor

### Actor and Passive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ זָכָר מְנָא לַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״ – פְּרָט לְקָטָן, ״אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב אֶת זָכָר״ – בֵּין גָּדוֹל בֵּין קָטָן, ״מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״ – מַגִּיד לְךָ הַכָּתוּב שֶׁשְּׁנֵי מִשְׁכָּבוֹת בָּאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where do we** derive the prohibition and punishment for homosexual intercourse with **a male?** It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And if a man lies with a male as with a woman, both of them have committed an abomination; they shall be put to death, their blood shall be upon them" (Leviticus 20:13): The word **"man" excludes a minor boy.** The phrase **"lies with a male"** is referring to any male, **whether** he is **an adult man** or **whether** he is **a minor boy.** The phrase **"as with a woman [*mishkevei isha*],"** referring to lying with a woman, appears in the plural. **The verse teaches you that** there are **two** manners of **lying with a woman** for which one who engages in intercourse with a woman forbidden to him is punished, vaginal and anal intercourse.

The *Gemara* now transitions to the prohibition and punishment for *mishkav zachor* — intercourse between males. The terminology here is notable: the word *zachur* (as distinct from *zachar*) is understood by Rashi as referring specifically to the one who is penetrated — the passive participant. The form *zachur* connotes the passive, as in *shamur* or *sagur*, suggesting one who has been "maled." When the verse or *Gemara* refers to the active penetrator, it uses *zachar*.

With that distinction in mind, the *Gemara* proceeds to examine both the source for the punishment and the source for the prohibition, and to determine which categories of person — actor, passive participant, adult, minor — are covered by each verse.

## Bestiality Laws (III)

### Why Stone Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבָּא עַל הַזְּכוּר, וְעַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וְהָאִשָּׁה הַמְּבִיאָה אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה – בִּסְקִילָה. אִם אָדָם חָטָא, בְּהֵמָה מֶה חָטְאָה? אֶלָּא לְפִי שֶׁבָּאָה לְאָדָם תַּקָּלָה עַל יָדָהּ, לְפִיכָךְ אָמַר הַכָּתוּב תִּסָּקֵל. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא בְּהֵמָה עוֹבֶרֶת בַּשּׁוּק וְיֹאמְרוּ ״זוֹ הִיא שֶׁנִּסְקַל פְּלוֹנִי עַל יָדָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A man **who engages in intercourse with a male or with an animal, and a woman who engages in intercourse with an animal,** are executed **by stoning.** The animal is likewise stoned to death. The mishna asks: **If** the **person sinned** by doing this, **how did the animal sin? Rather, because a calamity was caused to a person by it, therefore the verse states** that **it should be stoned,** so that it does not cause another to sin. **Alternatively,** it is **so that** this **animal will not pass through the marketplace, and** those who see it **will say: This is** the animal **because of which so-and-so was stoned,** and its existence would shame his memory.

Before continuing with *mishkav zachor*, the *Gemara* addresses the stoning of the animal involved in an act of bestiality. The question is deceptively simple: *im adam chata, behemah mah chata?* — if the human sinned, what did the animal do wrong? Two rationales are offered. The first: *lefi she'ba'ah le'adam takalah al yadah* — because it caused a stumbling block for a human being, and therefore it must be destroyed. Here the animal is not morally culpable but is treated as an instrument of harm. The second rationale: *she'lo te'he behemah overet ba'shuk v'yomru zo hi she'niskal ploni al yadah* — so that the animal not walk through the marketplace and people say, "This is the animal on account of which so-and-so was executed." The concern here is not about the animal's metaphysical status but about the memory of the sin. The two rationales point in meaningfully different directions: the first treats destruction of the animal as a response to what it caused; the second treats it as a practical measure to prevent public recollection of the transgression.

## Mishkav Zachor (II)

### Age Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנֶשׁ שָׁמַעְנוּ, אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה תּוֹעֵבָה הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** the **punishment** for homosexual intercourse, but **from where** is the **prohibition** derived? **The verse states: "And you shall not lie with a male as with a woman; it is an abomination"** (Leviticus 18:22).

Returning to *mishkav zachor*, the *braita* begins: *ish* — a man, *prat le'katan* — excluding a minor from liability. The verse then continues: *asher yishkav et zachar* — the one being penetrated may be *bein gadol bein katan*, whether an adult or a minor. The actor must be an adult to be punishable, but the passive participant need not be. Conversely — and this is a crucial structural point — if the passive participant is the adult, the one doing the penetrating may in principle be a minor of sufficient age, since the age threshold for defining an act of intercourse with a male is nine years old (parallel to the three-year threshold for a female). Both parties need not be adults; what matters is that the act meets the minimum definition of intercourse and that the liable party is an adult.

### Types of Intercourse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ אַזְהָרָה לַשּׁוֹכֵב. אַזְהָרָה לַנִּשְׁכָּב מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְגַם קָדֵשׁ הָיָה בָאָרֶץ עָשׂוּ כְּכֹל הַתּוֹעֲבֹת הַגּוֹיִם אֲשֶׁר הוֹרִישׁ וְגוֹ׳״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** from here the **prohibition for the one who engages in** homosexual **intercourse** actively. **From where** do we derive **the prohibition for one who engages in** homosexual **intercourse** passively? **The verse states: "There shall not be a sodomite [*kadesh*] among the children of Israel"** (Deuteronomy 23:18). **And** another verse, cited to clarify the meaning of the term *kadesh*, **states: "And there were also sodomites [*kadesh*] in the land, they did according to all the abominations of the nations which the Lord drove out** before the children of Israel" (I Kings 14:24). This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The phrase *mishkevei ishah* — "the lyings of a woman" — appears in the prohibition verse and serves a broader halakhic function. The plural form *mishkevei* (rather than *mishkev*) signals that there are two modes of intercourse with a woman: vaginal and anal. *Megaleh lach ha'katuv she'shem mishkevot ishah* — the Torah reveals that the term "lyings of a woman" encompasses both. This teaches a general principle operative throughout the laws of *arayot*: intercourse is legally defined as either vaginal or anal penetration. By anal penetration being the only mode relevant in the case of two men, and the verse nonetheless designating it by the terminology of *mishkevei ishah*, the *drashot* extract the principle that both modes count as intercourse in all forbidden-relations contexts. Oral intercourse is not included in this definition.

### Punishment Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״. קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״לֹא תִשָּׁכֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: It is not necessary** to derive this *halakha* from the verse: "There shall not be a sodomite." Rather, **it says: "And you shall not lie [*tishkav*] with a male as with a woman." Read into** the verse: **You shall not enable your being lain with [*tishakhev*]** by a male.

For the punishment, the verse in *Kedoshim* reads: *mot yumatu, dmeihem bam* — they shall be put to death, their blood is upon them. The *braita* asks: is this *sekilah*, or might it be one of the other capital punishments listed in the Torah? The derivation proceeds through *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kan dmeihem bam, v'ne'emar b'ov v'yidoni dmeihem bam* — the same phrase *dmeihem bam* appears here and by the case of *ov v'yidoni* (sorcery), which is established to be *sekilah*. *Mah lehalan be'sekilah, af kan be'sekilah* — just as there it is stoning, so here it is stoning.

### Actor's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנֶשׁ שָׁמַעְנוּ, אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה תּוֹעֵבָה הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** the **punishment** for homosexual intercourse, but **from where** is the **prohibition** derived? **The verse states: "And you shall not lie with a male as with a woman; it is an abomination"** (Leviticus 18:22).

The prohibition for the active partner — the penetrator — is derived from the verse in *Acharei Mot*: *v'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei ishah, to'evah hi* — you shall not lie with a male as one lies with a woman, it is an abomination. *Lamadnu azharah la'shochev* — this establishes the prohibition for the one who sleeps with, i.e., the actor.

### Passive's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ אַזְהָרָה לַשּׁוֹכֵב. אַזְהָרָה לַנִּשְׁכָּב מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְגַם קָדֵשׁ הָיָה בָאָרֶץ עָשׂוּ כְּכֹל הַתּוֹעֲבֹת הַגּוֹיִם אֲשֶׁר הוֹרִישׁ וְגוֹ׳״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** from here the **prohibition for the one who engages in** homosexual **intercourse** actively. **From where** do we derive **the prohibition for one who engages in** homosexual **intercourse** passively? **The verse states: "There shall not be a sodomite [*kadesh*] among the children of Israel"** (Deuteronomy 23:18). **And** another verse, cited to clarify the meaning of the term *kadesh*, **states: "And there were also sodomites [*kadesh*] in the land, they did according to all the abominations of the nations which the Lord drove out** before the children of Israel" (I Kings 14:24). This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

Where is the prohibition for the passive participant — the one who is penetrated? This is where Reb Yishmael and Reb Akiva diverge.

Reb Yishmael derives it from the verse *lo yihyeh kadesh mi'bnei Yisrael* — there shall be no *kadesh* among Israel (Devarim 23:18). The *kadesh* — commonly rendered "male prostitute" — is understood here as one who offers himself for penetration. This is not an arbitrary extension: male prostitutes in the ancient world characteristically served as the passive partner in male-male intercourse, and the verse prohibiting the *kadesh* is therefore understood as specifically addressing the act of being penetrated. The *Gemara* then supports this reading through an additional verse: *v'gam kadesh hayah ba'aretz, asu ke'chol to'avot ha'goyim asher horish* — there were also *kedeshim* in the land, they did like all the abominations of the nations. The keyword *to'avah* — abomination — links this to the verse on intercourse between males, which also uses *to'evah hi*. Reb Yishmael reads the connection: the *kadesh* is associated with a forbidden sexual act labeled *to'evah*, specifically the act between males, and therefore the prohibition of *kadesh* is the source for prohibiting the passive participant in *mishkav zachor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״. קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״לֹא תִשָּׁכֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: It is not necessary** to derive this *halakha* from the verse: "There shall not be a sodomite." Rather, **it says: "And you shall not lie [*tishkav*] with a male as with a woman." Read into** the verse: **You shall not enable your being lain with [*tishakhev*]** by a male.

Reb Akiva holds that no separate verse is needed. He reads the verb of the original prohibition in both its active and passive forms: *v'et zachar lo tishkav* — *karei bei lo tishachev* — you shall also not be lain with by a male. By reading *lo tishkav* passively, Reb Akiva derives the prohibition for the passive participant from the same verse as the actor's prohibition, making the two a single unified prohibition rather than two separate ones. This will have consequences for how many *korbanot* are owed for multiple acts, as we shall see.

## Bestiality Laws (III)

### Bestiality Age Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵמָה מְנָא לַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״ – פְּרָט לְקָטָן, ״אֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן שְׁכׇבְתּוֹ בִּבְהֵמָה״ – בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that one who engages in intercourse with **an animal** is liable to receive capital punishment? It is **as the Sages taught:** "And if a man lies with an animal, he shall be put to death, and you shall kill the animal" (Leviticus 20:15). The word **"man" excludes a minor boy.** The phrase **"lies with an animal"** is referring to any animal, **whether old or young.**

The *braita* on bestiality follows the same structure. *Ish, prat le'katan* — a man, excluding a minor. The animal, by contrast, is subject to no age qualification: *bein gedolah bein ketanah* — whether the animal is large or small, adult or young, the act is defined as intercourse and the man is liable.

### Punishment Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹת יוּמָת״ – בִּסְקִילָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּסְקִילָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל מִיתוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״תַּהֲרֹגוּ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״כִּי הָרֹג תַּהַרְגֶנּוּ״. מָה לְהַלָּן בִּסְקִילָה, אַף כָּאן בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"shall be put to death"** refers to execution **by stoning. Do you say** that they are executed **by stoning, or is it rather by one of all the** other types of **death** penalty **that are stated in the Torah? It is stated here: "You shall kill," and it is stated there,** with regard to an inciter: **"But you shall kill** him…and you shall stone him with stones and he shall die" (Deuteronomy 13:10–11). **Just as there** the verse states that an inciter is executed **by stoning, so too here,** one who engages in bestiality is executed **by stoning.**

For the punishment, the verse reads *mot yumat* — he shall be put to death. A *gezeirah shavah* is applied: *ne'emar kan taharogu, v'ne'emar lehalan ki harog tahargenu* — the same language of *harog/taharogu* appears here and in *Devarim* 13 in the case of the *mesis*, the one who incites others to idolatry. That case is established as *sekilah*, and so by the verbal analogy, the punishment for bestiality is also *sekilah*. *Mah lehalan be'sekilah, af kan be'sekilah*.

### Actor's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ עוֹנֶשׁ לַשּׁוֹכֵב. עוֹנֶשׁ לַנִּשְׁכָּב מְנָלַן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל שֹׁכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה מוֹת יוּמָת״. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַשּׁוֹכֵב, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לַנִּשְׁכָּב.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** the **punishment for one who engages in bestiality** actively, but **from where** do we derive **the punishment for one who engages in bestiality** passively? **The verse states: "Whoever lies with an animal shall be put to death"** (Exodus 22:18). **If** this verse **is not** needed for the **matter of the one who** actively **lies** with an animal, i.e., a male who sexually penetrates an animal, **apply it** to the **matter of the one who** causes an animal to **lie with** him, by being penetrated by the animal, i.e., any type of intercourse with an animal is punishable by death.

For the actor — the man who penetrates an animal — the prohibition is derived from: *u've'chol behemah lo tittein shechavtecha le'tum'ah bah* — you shall not give your lying with any animal to defile yourself thereby (Vayikra 18:23). *Lamadnu azharah la'shochev* — this establishes the prohibition for the penetrating party.

### Passive's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ עוֹנֶשׁ בֵּין לַשּׁוֹכֵב בֵּין לַנִּשְׁכָּב. אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבְכׇל בְּהֵמָה לֹא תִתֵּן שְׁכׇבְתְּךָ לְטׇמְאָה בָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **We have** therefore **learned** the **punishment for both one who engages in bestiality** actively **and one who engages in bestiality** passively, but **from where** is the **prohibition** derived? **The verse states: "And you shall not lie with any animal to defile yourself with it"** (Leviticus 18:23).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ אַזְהָרָה לַשּׁוֹכֵב. לַנִּשְׁכָּב מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְגַם קָדֵשׁ הָיָה בָּאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **We have learned** the **prohibition for one who engages in bestiality** actively. **From where** do we derive the prohibition **for one who engages in bestiality** passively? **The verse states: "There shall not be a *kadesh* among the children of Israel," and** another verse **states: "And there were also *kadesh* in the land,"** which shows that anyone who engages in intercourse in a way that is like the abominations of the nations is called a *kadesh*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

Where is the prohibition for a man who is penetrated by an animal? Reb Yishmael uses the verse *lo yihyeh kadesh mi'bnei Yisrael* here as well, extending the *kadesh* prohibition beyond the case of *mishkav zachor* to include a man penetrated by an animal. The *to'evah* connection operates analogously. However, the *Gemara* notes that the word *to'evah* does not in fact appear directly in the verse prohibiting bestiality — unlike the case of *mishkav zachor* where *to'evah hi* is explicit. This is the opening for Abaye's subsequent correction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא תִתֵּן שְׁכׇבְתְּךָ״ – לֹא תִתֵּן שְׁכִיבָתְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: It is not necessary** to derive the *halakha* prohibiting passively engaging in bestiality from this verse. Rather, **it says: "You shall not lie [*shekhovtekha*],"** which can be read as follows: **You shall not enable your being lain with [*shekhivatkha*].**

Reb Akiva, consistent with his earlier reasoning, holds that no separate verse is required. The same verse that prohibits the actor — *lo tittein shechavtecha* — can be read as *lo tittein shechivtecha*, passively: you shall not allow your being lain with. Both active and passive are captured by the same *pasuk*, so there is again only a single prohibition covering both sides.

## Multiple Acts Analys (II)

### Male-Male Both Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא עַל הַזְּכוּר, וְהֵבִיא עָלָיו זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses cases to which these halakhic expositions are relevant: What is the *halakha* of **one who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with a male, and** unwittingly **causes a male to engage in intercourse with him,** within one lapse of awareness, i.e., without realizing in the interim that these behaviors are forbidden? Is he considered to have transgressed two separate prohibitions and therefore liable to bring two sin-offerings, or is he considered to have transgressed one prohibition twice and liable to bring only one sin-offering?

Now that the prohibitions have been established and the debate between Reb Yishmael and Reb Akiva laid out, the *Gemara* examines a man who performs multiple acts in a single state of forgetfulness (*shogeg*) — specifically, acts on different sides of the same category. The question throughout is how many *korbanot chatat* he owes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, חֲדָא מִ״לֹּא תִשְׁכַּב״ וַחֲדָא מִ״לֹּא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ״. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״לֹא תִשְׁכַּב״ ״לֹא תִשָּׁכֵב״ חֲדָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael,** he is **liable** for transgressing **two** different prohibitions: **One** is derived **from** the verse: **"You shall not lie," and** the other **one** is derived **from** the verse: **"There shall not be a sodomite,"** which includes one who engages in homosexual intercourse passively. But **according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, he is liable** for **only one** prohibition, as the prohibitions of **"you shall not lie [*tishkav*]"** and **you shall not enable your being lain with [*tishakhev*] are one** statement in the verse.

The first case: *haba al ha'zachur v'heivi alav zachur* — a man who both penetrates a male and is penetrated by a male (note: Rashi points out that the first is called *zachur*, the passive form, and the second *zachar*, indicating which role is meant in each phrase). How many sin offerings does he owe?

Reb Avahu states: *lidivre Reb Yishmael, chayav shtayim* — for Reb Yishmael, two. The act of penetrating a male violates *v'et zachar lo tishkav*, and the act of being penetrated violates *lo yihyeh kadesh* — two entirely separate verses and thus two separate prohibitions. He owes a separate *korban* for each. *Lidivre Reb Akiva, eino chayav ela achat* — for Reb Akiva, only one. Since both sides of the act are derived from the single verse *lo tishkav / lo tishachev*, they constitute a single prohibition done twice, yielding only one *korban*.

### Bestiality Both Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה וְהֵבִיא בְּהֵמָה עָלָיו, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, חֲדָא מִ״לֹּא תִתֵּן שְׁכׇבְתְּךָ״ וַחֲדָא מִ״לֹּא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ״. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, ״שְׁכׇבְתְּךָ״ וּ״שְׁכִיבָתְךָ״ חֲדָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **one who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with an animal, and** then unwittingly **causes an animal to engage in intercourse with him** within one lapse of awareness, **Rabbi Abbahu says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael** he is **liable** for transgressing **two** different prohibitions. **One** is derived **from** the verse: **"You shall not lie," and** the other **one** is derived **from** the verse: **"There shall not be a sodomite."** But **according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva he is liable** for **only one** prohibition, as the prohibitions of "you shall not **lie [*shekhovtekha*]"** and you shall not enable **your being lain with [*shekhivatkha*] are one** statement in the verse.

The second case: *haba al ha'behemah v'heivi behemah alav* — a man who both penetrates an animal and is penetrated by an animal.

Again, *lidivre Reb Yishmael, chayav shtayim*: one for *u've'chol behemah lo tittein shechavtecha* and one for *lo yihyeh kadesh* — two separate prohibitions. *Lidivre Reb Akiva, eino chayav ela achat*: both sides are subsumed in the single verse read as *shechavtecha / shechivtecha*, so only one *korban*.

## Multiple Acts Analys (II)

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל נָמֵי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, דְּכִי כְתִיב ״לֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ״ – בְּגַבְרֵי כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Even according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael he is liable** for **only one** prohibition, **as when it is written: "There shall not be a sodomite," it is written** only **with regard to** intercourse with **men,** but not with regard to bestiality.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אַזְהָרָה לַנִּשְׁכָּב מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״כׇּל שֹׁכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה מוֹת יוּמָת״. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַשּׁוֹכֵב, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לַנִּשְׁכָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to** Abaye, **from where** does **Rabbi Yishmael** derive the **prohibition for one who engages in bestiality** passively? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: **"Whoever lies with an animal shall be put to death." If** this verse **is not** needed for the **matter of the one who** actively **lies** with the animal, **apply it** to the **matter of the one who** causes the animal to **lie with** him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַנִּשְׁכָּב בִּלְשׁוֹן שׁוֹכֵב: מָה שׁוֹכֵב עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר, אַף נִשְׁכָּב עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is derived from the fact that **the Merciful One expresses** the *halakha* **of one who engages in bestiality** passively **using the term** for **one who engages in bestiality** actively: **Just as** with regard to **one who engages in bestiality** actively the Torah both **punishes** for this action **and prohibits** it, **so too,** with regard to **one who engages in bestiality** passively, the Torah both **punishes** for this action **and prohibits** it.

Abaye now intervenes. He maintains that even according to Reb Yishmael, the case of a man penetrated by an animal is *not* included in the prohibition of *kadesh*. *Dichi ketiv lo yihyeh kadesh, be'gavre ketiv* — when the verse says *lo yihyeh kadesh*, it is speaking about human beings, not about animals. The connection through *to'evah* that Reb Yishmael used to extend *kadesh* to *mishkav zachor* cannot work for bestiality because the verse prohibiting bestiality does not use the word *to'evah* in the same way.

If so, where does Reb Yishmael derive the prohibition for a man penetrated by an animal? From the extra verse *kol shochev im behemah mot yumet* — "whoever lies with an animal shall be put to death" (Shemot 22:18). This verse appears redundant as a punishment source (the punishment is already established from other verses), so it is redirected: *im eino inyan la'shochev, tenehu inyan la'nishkav* — if it is not needed for the actor, apply it to the passive participant. Furthermore, *v'afkeih rachmana la'nishkav b'lashon shochev* — the Torah expressed the passive participant using the language of the active one (*shochev*), and by analogy: just as for the actor there is both a prohibition and a punishment, so too for the passive participant there is both a prohibition and a punishment.

### Mixed Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנִּרְבָּע לְזָכָר וְהַנִּרְבָּע לִבְהֵמָה, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, חֲדָא מִ״לֹּא תִשְׁכַּב״ וַחֲדָא מִ״לֹּא תִתֵּן שְׁכׇבְתְּךָ״. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, אִידֵּי וְאִידִי ״לֹא יִהְיֶה קָדֵשׁ״ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the case of **one who was** unwittingly **sodomized by a male and** then unwittingly was **one with whom an animal copulated** within one lapse of awareness, **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva** he is **liable** for transgressing **two** prohibitions. **One** is derived **from** the verse: **"You shall not lie** with a male," **and** the other **one** is derived **from** the verse: **"You shall not lie** with any animal." **According to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael he is liable** for transgressing **only one** prohibition, as both **this** prohibition **and that** prohibition **are** derived from the verse: **"There shall not be a sodomite."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל נָמֵי חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, דִּכְתִיב ״כׇּל שֹׁכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה מוֹת יוּמָת״. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַשּׁוֹכֵב, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לַנִּשְׁכָּב. וְאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַנִּשְׁכָּב בִּלְשׁוֹן שׁוֹכֵב: מָה שׁוֹכֵב – עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר, אַף נִשְׁכָּב – עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Even according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael** he is **liable** for transgressing **two** prohibitions, **as it is written: "Whoever lies with an animal shall be put to death." If** the verse **is not** needed for the **matter of one who engages in bestiality** actively, as this prohibition is stated explicitly in the verse: "And you shall not lie with any animal" (Leviticus 18:23), **apply it** to the **matter of one who engages in bestiality** passively. **And** it is derived from the fact that **the Merciful One expresses** the *halakha* **of one who engages in bestiality** passively **using the term** for **one who engages in bestiality** actively: **Just as** with regard to **one who engages in bestiality** actively the Torah both **punishes** for this act **and prohibits** it, **so too,** with regard to **one who engages in bestiality** passively, the Torah both **punishes** for this act **and prohibits** it.

A new case: *ha'nirba la'zachar v'ha'nirba la'behemah* — a man who is penetrated by a male and also penetrated by an animal, in one lapse of awareness.

For Reb Akiva, this is *chayav shtayim* — two *korbanot*. Being penetrated by a male is governed by *lo tishachev* (the passive read of the *mishkav zachor* verse), while being penetrated by an animal is governed by *shechivtecha* (the passive read of the *lo tittein shechavtecha* verse). These are entirely separate sources.

For Reb Yishmael — prior to Abaye's correction — it would have been only one *korban*, since both types of being-penetrated were encompassed under the single prohibition of *lo yihyeh kadesh*. But under Abaye's correction, Reb Yishmael no longer places the animal case under *kadesh*. He derives it instead from *kol shochev im behemah*. Thus for Abaye's reading of Reb Yishmael, these are now two separate sources — *lo yihyeh kadesh* for being penetrated by a male, and *kol shochev im behemah* redirected for being penetrated by an animal — and therefore Reb Yishmael too would say *chayav shtayim*.

### All Four Acts Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַבָּא עַל הַזְּכוּר, וְהֵבִיא זָכָר עָלָיו, הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וְהֵבִיא בְּהֵמָה עָלָיו – בֵּין לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ בֵּין לְאַבָּיֵי, לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ, לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **one who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with a male, and** then unwittingly **causes a male** to engage in intercourse **with him,** and **who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with an animal, and** then unwittingly **causes an animal** to engage in intercourse **with him,** performing all of these actions in one lapse of awareness, in this case, **both according to Rabbi Abbahu and according to Abaye,** the *halakha* **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael** is that he is **liable** for transgressing **three** prohibitions; the ones mentioned above and the prohibition of: "There shall not be a sodomite," whereas **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** he is **liable** for transgressing only **two** prohibitions.

The *Gemara* concludes with the case of a man who does all four acts in a single lapse of awareness: he penetrates a male, is penetrated by a male, penetrates an animal, and is penetrated by an animal.

For Reb Yishmael (*lidivre Rabbi Avahu*, i.e., before Abaye's correction), the answer is *chayav shalosh* — three *korbanot*. The prohibition of *lo yihyeh kadesh* covers both cases of being penetrated (whether by a male or an animal): that is one. The prohibition of *v'et zachar lo tishkav* covers penetrating a male: that is two. The prohibition of *u've'chol behemah lo tittein shechavtecha* covers penetrating an animal: that is three. Three distinct prohibitions cover the four acts.

For Reb Akiva, the answer is *chayav shtayim* — two. He has no *kadesh* prohibition at all. The prohibition of *lo tishkav / lo tishachev* covers both sides of *mishkav zachor* (penetrating and being penetrated by a male): that is one. The prohibition of *shechavtecha / shechivtecha* covers both sides of the animal acts (penetrating and being penetrated by an animal): that is two. Two prohibitions, each transgressed twice in each direction, yield two *korbanot*.

## Bestiality Laws (III)

### Abaye's Correction

Abaye's intervention — already integrated into the discussion above — is formalized here as a direct correction to the position attributed to Reb Yishmael. Abaye insists that *lo yihyeh kadesh* applies *be'gavre* — only to human beings — and cannot be extended to the case of an animal. The *to'evah* link that Reb Yishmael used to connect *kadesh* to *mishkav zachor* does not carry over to bestiality, since the bestiality verse does not use *to'evah* in the applicable way. Reb Yishmael's source for the passive participant in the animal case must therefore be located in *kol shochev im behemah*, not in *kadesh*, bringing his position closer to Reb Akiva's than the initial presentation suggested.
