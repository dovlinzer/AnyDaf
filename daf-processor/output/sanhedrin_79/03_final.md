# Sanhedrin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intent & Different Victim

### Intent Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה, וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם; לַנׇּכְרִי, וְהָרַג אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל; לַנְּפָלִים, וְהָרַג אֶת בֶּן קַיָּימָא – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If one **intended to kill an animal, and he killed a person** standing adjacent to it, or if he intended **to** kill **a gentile,** for whose murder he is not liable to be executed in court, **and he killed a Jew,** or if he intended **to** kill **non-viable newborns,** for whose murder one is not liable, **and he killed a viable person,** the assailant is **exempt** from execution, since his intent was to kill one for whose murder he is not liable.

The daf opens with the next *mishna* in the tractate, which continues the broader project of defining the precise *halachic* requirements for the act of *retzicha*. The preceding *gemara* had established one dimension of that definition: the physical act itself must be assessed as having the capacity to kill — there must be a *shiur*, a threshold of lethality, which depends on the nature of the instrument used and the manner of the blow, and the victim must in fact die as a result of that wound. Now the *mishna* turns to a second dimension: intention.

### Non-Capital Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַכּוֹתוֹ עַל מׇתְנָיו, וְלֹא הָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לַהֲמִיתוֹ עַל מׇתְנָיו, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל לִבּוֹ, וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לַהֲמִיתוֹ עַל לִבּוֹ, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַכּוֹתוֹ עַל לִבּוֹ וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית עַל לִבּוֹ, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל מׇתְנָיו, וְלֹא הָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית עַל מׇתְנָיו, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If one **intended to strike** another **on his loins, and** the blow **was not** powerful **enough to kill him** if it were to land **on his loins, but** instead the blow landed **on** his chest over the victim's **heart, and it was** powerful **enough to kill him** when it landed **on** his chest over **his heart, and** the victim **died** as a result of the blow, the assailant is **exempt** from execution, as he did not intend to strike the victim a blow that would cause his death. **and** the blow **was** powerful **enough to kill him** were it to land **on his heart, and** instead the blow **landed on his loins, and it was not** powerful **enough to kill him** when it landed **on his loins, and** nevertheless the victim **died,** the assailant is **exempt.**

The *mishna* opens with three cases in which the perpetrator's intent was directed at a target other than the person who actually died. *Nitkaven laharog et habehema veharag et ha-adam* — he was trying to kill an animal and killed a human being. This case is obviously exempt. One might ask how *hatra'ah*, the formal warning required before any capital offense, could even apply here, since the person had no intention of sinning against a human being. Rashi addresses this by imagining a case in which the witnesses warn the shooter: "Do not shoot at that animal — you might miss and hit the person standing nearby." The person replies, *af al pi chen*, he will shoot anyway knowing the risk. This would constitute *hatra'at safek*, a warning about a doubtful outcome. Whether *hatra'at safek* qualifies as valid *hatra'ah* is itself a matter of dispute, but in any case, since the person's primary intention was not to kill a human being, he is exempt. Intention is constitutive of the act.

The second case: *l'ovdei kochavim veharag et Yisrael* — he was aiming at a non-Jew and killed a Jew. The original text in manuscripts likely read *l'oved avodah zarah*, but this was among the self-censored passages. Regardless of how one defines the gravity of killing a non-Jew, it is not an act that incurs the death penalty under Jewish law, and therefore the perpetrator was not intending a capital offense.

Third: *nefalim veharag et ben kayama* — he was attempting to abort a fetus and instead killed a born, viable person. This case does not resolve the question of how severe the killing of a fetus is in its own right. Killing an animal is certainly permitted, yet the *mishna* lists killing an animal as a paradigm case of non-capital intent. What matters for the *mishna*'s purposes is only that in none of these cases was the perpetrator intending an act that carries the death penalty — and therefore in all of them, he is exempt.

### Severity & Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַכּוֹת אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְלֹא הָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית הַגָּדוֹל, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל הַקָּטָן, וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית אֶת הַקָּטָן, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. נִתְכַּוֵּן לְהַכּוֹת אֶת הַקָּטָן, וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית אֶת הַקָּטָן, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל הַגָּדוֹל, וְלֹא הָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **one intended to kill an adult and** the blow **was not** powerful **enough to kill the adult,** and instead the **blow landed on a minor, and** the blow **was** powerful **enough to kill the minor and** the minor **died,** the assailant is **exempt.** If **one intended to kill a minor and** the blow **was** powerful **enough to kill a minor, and** the blow **landed on an adult and** the blow **was not** powerful **enough to kill the adult, and** nevertheless, the adult **died,** the assailant is **exempt.**

Up to this point, there is nothing unique to the laws of *retzicha*. In virtually any area of *halacha*, if a person is attempting act X and accidentally performs act Y, he may be a *shogeg* or a *misasek* but he is not a *mezid* — not intentionally committing a capital transgression. So far, the *mishna* has simply established that intention toward a capital act is necessary.

Now the *mishna* moves into more interesting territory. *Nitkaven lehakoto al motnav velo haya bo kedei lehamito* — he was intending to strike his friend on the side, which would not have killed him under ordinary circumstances. The blow missed and landed on the chest — *vehalcha lo al libo* — which would normally be a lethal blow. The victim died. The ruling: *patur*, exempt, because the act the perpetrator was attempting was not one assessed as having the capacity to kill. Conversely: *l'hakos al libo v'hayah bo k'dei l'hamit* — he was aiming at the chest, a blow with genuine lethal capacity, but the blow went astray and struck the side — *v'halcha al mosnav* — which would not normally be lethal. He had murderous intent, the victim died — and yet he is exempt. Intent is necessary but not sufficient. The actual blow as it landed must also be independently assessed as having had the capacity to kill.

The *mishna* then introduces cases involving a difference in the identity of the victim. *Nitkaven l'hakos es ha-gadol v'lo hayah bo k'dei l'hamis es ha-gadol* — he was aiming at an adult with a force insufficient to kill an adult, and he missed and hit a minor — *v'halcha al ha-katan*: exempt. He had no murderous intent, and the act was not lethal toward its intended target. *Nitkaven l'hakos es ha-katan v'hayah bo k'dei l'hamis es ha-katan* — aiming at the minor with force sufficient to kill a minor, the blow went astray and struck an adult — *v'lo hayah bo k'dei l'hamis es ha-gadol* — but the force would not have been lethal to an adult, and the adult nonetheless died. Again, exempt: the act as it landed lacked the capacity to kill.

But then: *aval nitkaven l'hakos al mosnav v'hayah bo k'dei l'hamis al mosnav v'halcha al libo v'meis chayav* — he was intending a lethal blow to the thigh, the blow landed on the heart, and the victim died. Here all three conditions are met: murderous intent, a lethal blow as assessed, and actual death. *Chayav.* Similarly, *nitkaven l'hakos es ha-gadol v'hayah bo k'dei l'hamis es ha-gadol v'halcha al ha-katan v'meis chayav* — intending to strike an adult with lethal force, the blow struck a minor and killed the minor. The intent was murderous, the blow was lethal, the person died. *Chayav.*

### Specific Person Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַכּוֹת עַל מׇתְנָיו, וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית עַל מׇתְנָיו, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל לִבּוֹ, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב. נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַכּוֹת אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְהָיָה בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ עַל הַקָּטָן, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת זֶה וְהָרַג אֶת זֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **one intended to strike** another **on his loins, and** the blow **was** powerful **enough to kill him** were it to land **on his loins, and** instead the blow **landed on** his chest over **his heart, and he died,** the assailant is **liable,** since in any event, his intent was to kill the victim and the blow was powerful enough to kill him wherever it struck him. If **one intended to strike an adult and** the blow **was** powerful **enough to kill the adult, and** the blow **landed on a minor and he died,** the assailant is **liable. Rabbi Shimon says: Even** if **one intended to kill this** one **and he killed that** one, although he would be liable for killing either, he is **exempt,** because one is executed only if his action completely corresponded with his intent.

At this point, Rabbi Shimon intervenes. Even in this last case — intending to kill an adult with lethal force and succeeding in killing a minor — he is *patur*. And Rabbi Shimon's principle is stated in the broadest possible terms: *afilu nitkaven la-harog es zeh v'harag es zeh patur* — even if a person was trying to kill one specific individual and killed a different specific individual, he is exempt.

This is a striking position. The perpetrator was trying to commit the *aveira* of murder; he succeeded in committing the *aveira* of murder. Why should it matter which person was killed? One might compare it to other areas of *halacha*: if a person was trying to pluck one apple off a tree on *Shabbos* and accidentally plucked a different apple, we would not ordinarily say that this exempts him from liability for *chillul Shabbos*. True, by *Shabbos* there is a heightened requirement of *meleches machsheves* — purposeful, thoughtful labor — such that even a small miss can break the connection between intent and act. But in other *aveiros*, we generally assume that if one intended to violate a prohibition and did in fact violate it, the specific object involved is largely irrelevant.

The best reading of Rabbi Shimon's position may be that murder, unlike other *aveiros*, is not primarily an abstract transgression. It is not, as it were, simply an act of eating *cheilev* or improperly cutting hair — where one might say the act is fundamentally the same regardless of which piece or which person. Murder is an offense against a person. It is the violation of this individual's life. Seen that way, trying to murder Reuven is a fundamentally different act from trying to murder Shimon — because the identity of the person whose life is being violated is constitutive of the crime itself. The *Chachamim*, presumably, view murder more as an abstract forbidden act, where intent to commit murder followed by success in committing murder is sufficient. Rabbi Shimon views it as a crime against a specific individual, and the perpetrator must therefore be specifically targeting the person who was actually killed.

## Intent & Different Victim — Gemara Clarification

### Gemara Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַסֵּיפָא, ״רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אַרֵישָׁא: נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם, לַנׇּכְרִי וְהָרַג אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, לַנְּפָלִים וְהָרַג אֶת בֶּן קַיָּימָא – פָּטוּר. הָא נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת זֶה וְהָרַג אֶת זֶה – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת זֶה וְהָרַג אֶת זֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **With regard to which** clause of the mishna does **Rabbi Shimon** disagree? **If we say** that he disagrees **with the latter clause** of the mishna, concerning one who intended to kill an adult and killed a minor, the mishna **should have** employed the formulation: **Rabbi Shimon exempts** him, not the extended statement of: Even if one intended to kill this one and he killed that one, he is exempt. **Rather,** he disagrees **with the first clause** of the mishna, which states: If **one intended to kill an animal, and he killed a person,** or he intended **to** kill **a gentile and he killed a Jew,** or he intended **to** kill **non-viable newborns and he killed a viable person,** the assailant is **exempt** from execution; and from which it may be inferred: **But** if **one intended to kill this** one **and he killed that** one, the assailant is **liable. Rabbi Shimon** disagrees and **says: Even** if **one intended to kill this** one **and he killed that** one, he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* now asks a textual question about Rabbi Shimon's statement: which case within the *mishna* is he responding to? On the surface, it appears he is addressing the *seifa* — the final case of intending to strike an adult and striking a minor. But the *gemara* objects: *Rabbi Shimon patur mibaye lei* — if he were only responding to that case, he need not have formulated an abstract general principle. He could simply have said: "In that case I say *patur*." Why the sweeping reformulation?

The *gemara* answers: *ela reisha* — he is also responding to the *reisha*, to what is implicit in the opening cases. The first half of the *mishna* lists cases where the perpetrator was aiming at a non-capital target — animal, non-Jew, fetus — and rules *patur*. The underlying implication of those cases is: the reason for exemption is that the intended act was not a capital offense. The corollary implication is that if one were intending to kill a person whose death would constitute a capital offense, and one instead killed a different such person — *hanitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh* — one would be *chayav*.

It is to that implicit *chiyuv* lurking in the *reisha* that Rabbi Shimon responds. He abstracts his position into a general principle — *afilu nitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh patur* — precisely to make clear that he disagrees not only with the explicit ruling of the *seifa* but with the implied ruling of the *reisha* as well. The bottom line is that Rabbi Shimon disputes the *mishna* at every level: wherever it is implied or stated that intending one capital victim and killing a different capital victim results in liability, Rabbi Shimon maintains exemption.

## Rabbi Shimon's Principle

### Abstract Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! קָאֵי רְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן, וְאָמַר: ״אֲנָא לִרְאוּבֵן קָא מִיכַּוַּונָא, לְשִׁמְעוֹן לָא קָא מִיכַּוַּונָא״ – הַיְינוּ פְּלוּגְתַּיְיהוּ. אָמַר: ״לְחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ״ – מַאי? אִי נָמֵי, כְּסָבוּר רְאוּבֵן וְנִמְצָא שִׁמְעוֹן – מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״לִפְלוֹנִי אֲנִי מִתְכַּוֵּין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: If **Reuven and Shimon were standing, and** before throwing the stone or shooting the arrow, the assailant **said: I intend to** kill **Reuven,** and **I do not intend to** kill **Shimon,** it is **obvious** that **this is the dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon. If the assailant **said:** I intend **to** kill **one of them,** and he did not specify whether it is Reuven or Shimon, **what** is the *halakha*? **Alternatively,** in a case where he intended to kill one whom **he thought** was **Reuven** and he killed him **and he was found** to be **Shimon, what** is the ruling? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this matter, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** He is not liable **until he says: I intend to** kill **so-and-so,** and he then kills him.

The *gemara* now seeks to define the precise parameters of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim*. The baseline case is clear: *kaimi Reuven v'Shimon* — Reuven and Shimon are both standing there. The shooter declares, *ana l'Reuven kamchavna l'Shimon lo kamchavna* — I am aiming at Reuven, I am not aiming at Shimon — and then misses Reuven and kills Shimon. That, *hainu plugtaihu*, is the crux of their debate. The *Chachamim* say that since the person intended a capital act of murder and succeeded in committing a capital act of murder, he is *chayav*. Rabbi Shimon says that intending to murder Reuven is a fundamentally different act from murdering Shimon, and therefore he is *patur*.

But the *gemara* then raises two additional cases whose status within this dispute is less obvious. First: *amar lach chad minaihu mai* — what if the person said, in effect, "I do not care which of you I kill"? Perhaps he even has a primary intention toward Reuven, but is equally content to kill Shimon. Rashi suggests there may even be a primary preference combined with indifference to the alternative. Second: *inami kasvar Reuven venimtza Shimon* — what if the person was shooting at a figure in the distance, believed the figure was Reuven, aimed at and killed that exact figure, and only upon inspection discovered that the victim was in fact Shimon — a case of mistaken identity?

These cases occupy an intermediate space. In one sense they differ from the core case of clearly wrong intent: the shooter did not definitively intend a different person than the one he killed. Yet in another sense they may fall short of the specific intent that, at least according to Rabbi Shimon, is required for liability. The *gemara* thus asks: is Rabbi Shimon's position that *wrong* intent acquits, or that *specific* intent to the actual victim is a positive requirement for conviction?

### Positive Intent Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָרַב לוֹ וְקָם עָלָיו״, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּון לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify the rationale underlying the opinions of the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon, and asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon? The verse states: "But if there will be a man who hates his neighbor, and he lies in wait for him and rises up against him,** and strikes him mortally and he dies" (Deuteronomy 19:11). Based on the repeated use of pronouns in the phrase: "And lies in wait for him and rises up against him," Rabbi Shimon derives: One is not liable to be executed **until he intends to** kill specifically **him,** the actual victim.

The *gemara* resolves the question by citing a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon omer, ad sheyomar leploni ani mitkavein* — Rabbi Shimon requires that one positively declare, in effect, "I am intending to kill this specific person." This is a remarkable ruling. It is not merely that wrong intent exculpates; affirmative, specific intent directed at the actual victim is a precondition for liability. Both the case of indifference — "I am content to kill either one" — and the case of mistaken identity — "I thought he was Reuven" — would therefore be insufficient to establish the *chiyuv* under Rabbi Shimon's view.

This is a demanding standard, and it is notable that even *Shabbos*, which famously requires *meleches machsheves*, would not necessarily impose such a requirement. If a person intended to pick one apple but would have been equally content to pick another, that would still be *chillul Shabbos*. Rabbi Shimon's position seems to go further: murder, understood as the violation of a specific person's life, demands that the killer's intent be anchored to the very individual who was killed.

### Roosevelt Assassination

A striking real-world parallel to this Talmudic case arose on February 15, 1933, when an assassination attempt was made against President-elect Franklin Roosevelt. The gunman missed Roosevelt, and his bullets struck and killed the Mayor of Chicago and another bystander. The perpetrator was prosecuted for first-degree murder — not felony murder — and was executed within ten days of the verdict. This is precisely the scenario of *nitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh*: intending to kill one person and killing another. Under Rabbi Shimon's ruling, this would result in exemption from capital punishment. The *Chachamim* would hold otherwise. Secular law, it turns out, sided with the *Chachamim*.

### Ve'arav Lo Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: פְּרָט לְזוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** what do they derive from the repetitive pronouns in that phrase? The Gemara answers: The Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai say** that one derives: This is **to the exclusion of** one who **casts a stone into the midst** of a group of people and one of them is killed.

The *gemara* asks: *mai taama de-Rabbi Shimon* — what is the source for Rabbi Shimon's position? *Amar kra*, the verse states: *ve'arav lo vekam alav* — "and he lies in wait for him and rises against him" (Devarim 19:11). This verse distinguishes the intentional murderer from the accidental killer. Rabbi Shimon reads the phrase *ve'arav lo* — lying in wait *for him* — as requiring specific intent directed at this particular individual. The word *lo*, "for him," is doing interpretive work: it is not sufficient that the killer lay in wait with murderous intent in the abstract; the intent must be specifically toward the victim. *Ad sheyicaven lo* — until one's intention is directed at that specific person.

## Sages' Position

### Capital Offense Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה גּוֹיִם וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּינֵיהֶם, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּרוּבָּא גּוֹיִם נִינְהוּ. אִי נָמֵי פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא, סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת לְהָקֵל. לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶן, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ גּוֹי קָבוּעַ, וְכׇל קָבוּעַ כְּמֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say that** it is a case where **there are nine gentiles and one Jew among them,** there is no need for a derivation; **derive** that he is exempt from the fact **that they are a majority of gentiles,** and based on that majority, the probability is that the stone will strike a gentile. **Alternatively,** if the group of people comprises **half** gentiles **and half** Jews, the principle is that the ruling in a case of **uncertainty** with regard to cases of **capital** law is **to be lenient,** and therefore the one who threw the stone will be exempt from punishment. **No,** it is **necessary** to have this derivation only in a case **where there are nine Jews and one gentile among them, as** this is a case where the **gentile is a fixed** presence in the group, **and** the principle is: The legal status of **any** item **fixed** in its place **is like** that of an uncertainty that is **equally balanced,** and one does not follow the majority.

The *Rabbanan* read the same verse differently. For them, *ve'arav lo* requires not specific intent toward the particular victim, but intent to commit a capital act — intent to kill someone whose killing would constitute a capital offense. The defining question is not whether the killer intended this person specifically, but whether the killer intended an act of murder in the capital sense.

### Kavua & Mixed Crowd

This reading raises an immediate question: what work, then, does the verse do according to the *Rabbanan*? If one had intent to kill a Jew and killed a different Jew, they already hold that this is *chayav* as the *Reuven-Shimon* case. What additional case does *ve'arav lo* come to exclude? The *gemara* answers: *prat lezorek even legov* — it excludes the case of one who throws a stone into a mixed group of people.

The *gemara* asks: what exactly is the configuration of this group? If the group consisted of nine non-Jews (*Cutim* in the text, which replaced the censored original) and one Jew, then obviously the perpetrator is exempt, because the majority of the group was non-Jewish and we would define the intended act as most likely not a capital offense. And if it was half and half — *palga u-palga* — then *safek nefashot lehakel*: in capital cases we rule leniently, and so the doubt alone would exempt him.

What the verse comes to teach, then, is the case where there were nine Jews and one non-Jew. Were it not for the verse, we might have said: ninety percent probability that the act would result in killing a Jew, therefore define the intent as intent for a capital offense, and hold the perpetrator liable. The verse teaches otherwise.

### Kol Kavua Principle

The underlying principle is one of the most far-reaching in *Shas*: *kol kavua k'mechtzah al mechtzah dami* — any fixed presence is treated as a fifty-fifty proposition. When the non-Jew is physically present in the group and is, as it were, standing there before us, we cannot simply erase that presence by appeal to statistical probability. The act of throwing the stone into the group must always be assessed with the non-Jewish presence as part of the equation. Therefore, no matter how numerically small the non-Jewish presence is within the group, the act cannot be defined as definitively intended for a capital victim. One cannot pretend the non-Jew is not there.

This principle can be illustrated through a classic *kashrut* case. Suppose a person goes to buy meat from a street with ten shops, nine of them *kosher* and one *treif*, and returns home unable to remember which shop he entered. One might think: apply *rov*, the majority rule — ninety percent probability it was a *kosher* shop, so the meat is permitted. The principle of *kavua* says otherwise. Because the shops are in fixed locations, and the question is which specific shop he entered, the *treif* shop cannot be excluded from the analysis by appeal to statistics. Its presence is constant and real; it is not an abstraction. Therefore the question is treated as fifty-fifty, and the meat is forbidden.

The intuition behind *kavua* is this: *rov* works when one is assessing the abstract identity of an item that has separated from its context — asking, in effect, "what most likely is this?" But when all possibilities remain physically present and visible, when one is confronting the full array and the problematic option is standing there in front of us and cannot be ignored, the assessment cannot reduce to a simple statistical probability. As long as the *treif* store is part of the landscape one is looking at, one cannot reason it away. The bad possibility, being fixed and present, permanently remains part of the reckoning.

### Defarish Principle

This contrasts with the principle of *kol de-parish me-rubah parish* — whatever separates from a group separates from the majority. If a single individual walked away from the mixed group and one subsequently threw a stone at that individual without knowing his identity, the *kavua* logic no longer applies. Now the question is simply: who is this person? And in assessing the identity of an individual who has separated, one applies *rov*. If ninety percent of the original group was Jewish, then there is a ninety percent probability this individual is Jewish, and the intent to kill him is treated as intent for a capital act. The distinction turns precisely on whether one is confronting the group as a whole — with all its elements present — or assessing the identity of a single separated individual.

### Contemporary Application

This distinction between *kavua* and *de-parish* has genuine application in contemporary *halacha*. One area in which *poskim* invoke these principles involves questions about the composition of a population — for instance, whether a woman had relations with a non-Jewish man, which would disqualify her from marrying a *kohen*. Questions arise about how to assess the likelihood when the man's identity is unknown: Was the relevant population being viewed as a fixed group in its entirety, with all its elements — Jewish and non-Jewish — simultaneously before us? Or is one assessing the identity of a specific individual who has separated from that population? The *kavua/de-parish* framework shapes how these questions are approached, though the application to specific cases can be complex and is not always straightforwardly parallel to the Talmudic source.

## Woman vs. Man Case

### Exodus Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת זֶה וְהָרַג אֶת זֶה – חַיָּיב, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי יִנָּצוּ אֲנָשִׁים וְנָגְפוּ אִשָּׁה הָרָה״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּמַצּוּת שֶׁבְּמִיתָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם אָסוֹן יִהְיֶה וְנָתַתָּה נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the Rabbis, who say** that if **one intended to kill this** individual **and he killed that** individual he is **liable,** there is support for their opinion from that **which is written: "If men struggle and they hurt a pregnant woman** so that her child departs from her, and there is no tragedy, he shall be punished, as the husband of the woman shall impose upon him, and he shall give as the judges determine" (Exodus 21:22). It can be inferred form the verse that if there is a tragedy, i.e., if the woman dies, there is no payment of restitution. **And Rabbi Elazar says:** It is **with regard to a quarrel that involves** the intent of each to cause the **death** of the other that **the verse is speaking, as it is written: "But if there shall be a tragedy then you shall give a life for a life"** (Exodus 21:23). This is proof that in a case where one intended to kill his male opponent and he killed a pregnant woman instead, he is liable to be executed, which is why he does not pay restitution.

The *gemara* now turns to the practical consequences of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan*, focusing on a specific biblical passage. *Dichtiv v'chi yinatzu anashim v'nagfu ishah harah* — "If men are fighting and they strike a pregnant woman" (Shemos 21:22-23). Two men are fighting with lethal intent — *Rabbi Eliezer* understands *ki yinatzu* as referring to a fight to the death, as evidenced by the verse's own conclusion — and one of the fighters accidentally strikes and kills the pregnant woman who was nearby. The verse then states: *v'lo yihyeh ason* — if there is no tragedy, meaning the woman survived but miscarried, the perpetrator pays a fine to the husband. *V'im ason yihyeh v'natata nefesh tachat nafesh* — but if there is a tragedy, meaning the woman died, "you shall give life for life."

### Sages' Explanation

The *Rabbanan* have no difficulty with this verse. The case is a straightforward instance of *nitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh*: the fighter was intending to kill his opponent and instead killed the woman. Since the *Rabbanan* hold that intending any capital act of murder and succeeding in killing any person renders one liable, this case supports their position. The verse explicitly calls for *nefesh tachat nefesh* when the woman dies — meaning execution — which is exactly what the *Rabbanan* would predict.

### Rabbi Shimon's Challenge

For Rabbi Shimon, the verse presents a difficulty. If the killer intended to kill his male opponent and instead killed the woman, Rabbi Shimon would exempt him from execution — *nitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh patur*. So how does Rabbi Shimon explain the verse's apparent mandate of *nefesh tachat nefesh*? He cannot read it as execution, since his own principle would rule out execution in this case.

### Monetary Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַאי ״וְנָתַתָּה נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מָמוֹן. וְכִדְרַבִּי, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר, ״וְנָתַתָּה נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״ – מָמוֹן. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מָמוֹן, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא נֶפֶשׁ מַמָּשׁ? נֶאֶמְרָה נְתִינָה לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֶמְרָה נְתִינָה לְמַעְלָה: מָה לְהַלָּן מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rabbi Shimon, this** verse: **"Then you shall give a life for a life," what does he do with it?** According to his opinion, the party to the quarrel is exempt from the punishment of execution in this case. The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Shimon, "a life for a life" is not referring to execution; rather, the reference is to **monetary restitution. And** this understanding is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that the phrase **"then you shall give a life for a life"** does not mean execution, but rather **monetary restitution** for the life that he took. **Do you say** that it means **monetary restitution, or** does **it** mean **only** the taking of **an actual life?** Based on the language employed in the verse, it can be determined that the reference is to monetary payment. In these verses, a term of **giving is stated below:** "And you shall give a life for a life" (Exodus 21:23). **And** additionally, a term **is stated** of **giving above:** "He shall be punished, as the husband of the woman shall impose upon him, and he shall give as the judges determine" (Exodus 21:22). **Just as there,** in the phrase: "Give as the judges determine," the reference is to **monetary restitution, so too here,** in the phrase: "Give a life for a life," the reference is to **monetary restitution.**

Rabbi Shimon's solution is to interpret *nefesh tachat nefesh* not as execution but as monetary payment — *mamon*. This is analogous to the well-established rabbinic reading of *ayin tachat ayin*, "an eye for an eye," as referring to monetary compensation rather than literal retaliation. Here too, *nefesh tachat nefesh* means that the perpetrator must pay the monetary value of the life he wrongfully took.

This resolution is striking. The standard assumption in *halacha* is that wrongful death never generates a civil monetary claim — unlike in secular law, which distinguishes criminal prosecution from wrongful death suits. In the *halachic* system, the paradigm is that a killer either faces capital punishment, or is exiled to an *ir miklat*, or is otherwise exempt — but never pays money for the life itself. Rabbi Shimon introduces a category that departs from this assumption: when a person commits an act that would constitute murder but cannot be prosecuted as such under capital law — because he killed the wrong person — he nonetheless owes a monetary payment for the life he took. The value of a life is presumably assessed by a standard such as the person's market value, which will typically be substantially less than a full tort claim for pain, suffering, and lost wages. But the principle stands: wrongful death, when not prosecutable as capital murder, generates monetary liability.

The *gemara* supports Rabbi Shimon's reading from a *gezerah shavah*: the word *nesina*, "giving," appears both in *v'natata nefesh tachat nefesh* and in the payment for the miscarriage (*v'nasan b'plilim*), which is explicitly monetary. Just as "giving" in the miscarriage context refers to monetary payment, so too "giving" in the *nefesh tachat nefesh* context refers to monetary payment.

## Chizkiyah's View

### Animal vs. Human

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָאי דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה מַפְּקָא מִדְּרַבִּי, וּמַפְּקָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן. דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: ״מַכֵּה אָדָם״ וּ״מַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: That** which **the school of Ḥizkiyya taught diverges from** the statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and diverges from** the statement **of the Rabbis.** The school of Ḥizkiyya holds that in the case where one intended to kill one individual and killed another individual, he is exempt from both liability to be executed, counter to the opinion of the Rabbis, and from the obligation to pay monetary restitution. **As the school of Ḥizkiyya taught:** The verse states: "And he who kills an animal shall pay for it, and he who kills a man shall be put to death" (Leviticus 24:21), indicating that **one who kills a man and one who kills an animal** are comparable.

Rava introduces a *baraita* from the school of Chizkiyah that, he says, rejects both Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan*. The *baraita* draws on the parallel structure of two biblical verses: *makeh nefesh behemah yeshalmena u-makeh adam yumat* — one who strikes and kills an animal pays for it; one who strikes and kills a person is put to death (Vayikra 24:21). The parallel phrasing is understood to generate an analogy: the laws of killing an animal are placed alongside the laws of killing a human being in order to derive principles that apply in both domains.

### Rejection of Both Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה מַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בָּהּ בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד, בֵּין מִתְכַּוֵּין לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְכַּוֵּין, בֵּין דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה לְדֶרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה – לְפוֹטְרוֹ מָמוֹן אֶלָּא לְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן. אַף מַכֵּה אָדָם לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד, בֵּין מִתְכַּוֵּין לְשֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין, בֵּין דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה לְדֶרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה – לְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן אֶלָּא לְפוֹטְרוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** This teaches that **just as** with regard to **one who kills an animal,** the Torah **did not differentiate** between **whether** he does so **unwittingly or intentionally, whether** he acts **with intent or with no intent,** or **whether** he strikes **in** the course of **a downward motion or in** the course of **an upward motion,** and this is not **to exempt him from** paying **monetary restitution** in all these cases but **rather to render him liable** to pay **monetary restitution,** as one who kills an animal is liable in any event; **similarly, with regard to one who kills a person, do not differentiate concerning him whether** he does so **unwittingly or unintentionally, whether** he acts **with intent or with no intent,** or **whether** he strikes **in** the course of **a downward motion or in** the course of **an upward motion,** and this is not **to render him liable** to pay **monetary restitution** for the damage he causes in the process of killing him but **rather to exempt him from** paying **monetary restitution** in any event.

The *tanna* from the school of Chizkiyah uses this analogy to derive two paired principles, and it is precisely in these two principles that both Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan* are contradicted.

### Adam Muad Principle

Beginning with the animal: *lo chilakta bah bein shogeg le-mezid* — the *halacha* of killing an animal does not distinguish between unintentional and intentional killing. This is the principle of *adam muad le'olam* — a person is always fully liable for physical damages regardless of circumstance. Whether you killed the animal *shogeg* or *mezid*, whether you intended it or not, whether you were going up a ladder or coming down, you always pay. *Poter mamon* never applies; the obligation to pay is constant. By analogy: just as by the animal there are no distinctions that exempt from payment, so too by the human there are no distinctions that generate payment. In every case involving human death — *shogeg, mezid*, classic case, *galus* case, wrong victim — there is never monetary payment.

### Distinctions Irrelevant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין״? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין כְּלָל – הַיְינוּ שׁוֹגֵג! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין לָזֶה אֶלָּא לָזֶה, וְקָתָנֵי לְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן אֶלָּא לְפוֹטְרוֹ מָמוֹן. וְאִי בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא, מַאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיפְטְרֵיהּ מָמוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, it is derived from here that one who commits a transgression carrying the death penalty is exempt from paying damages for his action, even if he is not given the death penalty in practice. The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **with no intent? If we say** that it means **that he had no intent** to kill **at all** and he happened to kill a person, **that is unwitting** murder that is mentioned explicitly in the *baraita*. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the reference in the *baraita* is to **one who does not intend to** kill **this** individual, **but** does intend **to** kill **that** individual. **And it is taught:** This is not **to render him liable** to pay **monetary restitution** for the damage he causes in the process of killing him but **rather to exempt him from** paying **monetary restitution. And if** according to the school of Ḥizkiyya he is **liable to be executed,** for **what** reason **was** it **necessary to exempt him** from **monetary restitution?** Clearly the *halakha* is that one who is executed is exempt from payment.

The *baraita* lists specific distinctions that, by this analogy, become irrelevant to the question of monetary liability in the context of human death. *Bein shogeg le-mezid*: whether the killing was unintentional or intentional — this affects whether one goes to *galus* or receives the death penalty, but not monetary liability. *Bein miskaven le-she'eino miskaven*: this phrase, the *gemara* explains, refers specifically to the *Reuven-Shimon* scenario — the killer was *mezid* in intent, intending to take a life, but the life he took was not the one he intended. This is the category of *miskaven le-she'eino miskaven*, deliberate intent toward the wrong victim.

*Bein derech yerida le-derech aliya*: whether the fatal act occurred during a downward or upward motion. This distinction matters for *galus* — one is obligated to go to *ir miklat* only if the killing occurred through a downward motion (as derived from the case of an axehead flying off its handle). It does not affect the *halachic* question of monetary liability.

### Kim Leh Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָאו בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא, וְלָאו בַּר מָמוֹנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that **he is neither liable to be executed, nor is he liable** to pay **monetary restitution.** With regard to liability to be executed in a case where one intended to kill one individual and he killed another, the school of Ḥizkiyya holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon that he is exempt from the death penalty, and holds that he is exempt from paying monetary restitution as well.

This directly contradicts two positions. It contradicts the *Rabbanan*, since by including *miskaven le-she'eino miskaven* — the wrong-victim scenario — as a case where the perpetrator is nonetheless exempt from monetary payment, the *baraita* presupposes that this scenario is *not* a capital offense. If it were a capital offense, there would be no need to state that payment is exempt — *kim leh bid'rabbah mineh*, the principle that a greater punishment absorbs a lesser one, would self-evidently preclude payment. The very fact that the *baraita* feels the need to rule out payment in this case demonstrates that the *tanna* holds it is not a capital case — which is to say, he agrees with Rabbi Shimon that *nitkaven laharog et zeh veharag et zeh* is exempt from capital punishment.

But the *baraita* equally contradicts Rabbi Shimon's own ruling. Rabbi Shimon held that in the *Reuven-Shimon* scenario, one pays *mamon* for the life of the person killed. *Nefesh tachat nefesh*, he said, means monetary payment in such cases. The *tanna* from the school of Chizkiyah disagrees: even in the *Reuven-Shimon* case, there is no monetary payment for the life itself.

Tosafos makes an important qualification. The *tanna* from the school of Chizkiyah agrees with everyone regarding the *kim leh* principle as applied to external monetary damages. If, in the course of a *Reuven-Shimon* killing, some collateral property was destroyed — a garment torn, some object broken — everyone would agree that this incidental damage is not payable; the capital nature of the act, or the act's connection to taking a life, overrides any ancillary monetary claims. What is unique to the *tanna* from the school of Chizkiyah is the ruling that even the payment specifically *for the life itself* — which Rabbi Shimon would have allowed in the wrong-victim scenario — is equally precluded. Once a human life has been taken, the system never generates a civil monetary claim for that life, under any set of circumstances. The *halachic* categories of capital punishment and exemption exhaust the possibilities; wrongful death as a civil cause of action for the value of the life does not exist.

## Mixed Murderers

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בַּאֲחֵרִים – כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָן לַכִּיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a murderer who was intermingled with others** and it is not possible to identify the murderer, **all of them are exempt** from liability to be executed. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The court **gathers them into the vaulted chamber [*lakippa*]** where they will eventually die.

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna*. *Rotzeach shenisarev b'acheirim kulam peturim* — a murderer who becomes mixed in with others — all of them are exempt from execution.

### Sages vs. Rabbi Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ זֶה בָּזֶה – נִידּוֹנִין בַּקַּלָּה. הַנִּסְקָלִין בַּנִּשְׂרָפִין – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹנִין בִּסְקִילָה, שֶׁהַשְּׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נִידּוֹנִין בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, שֶׁהַסְּקִילָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all** those **liable to** be executed with different court-imposed **death** penalties **who became intermingled with each other** and it cannot be determined which individual was sentenced to which death, **they are** all **sentenced to the** most **lenient** form of execution to which any of them was sentenced. In a case where those **who are** liable to be **stoned** were intermingled **with** those **who are** liable to be **burned, Rabbi Shimon says: They are** all **sentenced** to be executed **by stoning, as burning** is a more **severe** form of execution than stoning. **And the Rabbis say: They are** all **sentenced** to be executed **by burning, as stoning** is a more **severe** form of execution than burning.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer konasin osam lakippa* — Rabbi Yehuda says they are all placed in prison, in a *kippa*. The word *kippa* here should be understood simply as a holding cell; the more elaborate discussion of what happens to a murderer placed in *kippa* — where he is eventually fed a diet designed to cause his death through intestinal rupture — belongs to a different section of the tractate and is not the focus here.

The immediate question is: who are these *acheirim* with whom the murderer became mixed? You cannot imprison innocent people. What population is being described?

### Mixed Execution Types

Before resolving that question, the *mishna* continues with a related but more tractable case. *Kol chiyuvei misos shenisarvu zeh bazeh nidonim be'kala* — if people liable to different forms of execution become intermingled, they are all executed by the lesser form. The four modes of execution — *skila*, *sreifa*, *hereg*, and *chenek* — carry varying degrees of severity, and when those destined for different modes become mixed, all are executed by whichever mode is assessed as least severe. This does not raise a problem of innocence, since everyone in the group is a convicted murderer liable to death; the only question is which mode applies.

At this point the *mishna* records the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim* on the relative severity of *skila* and *sreifa* that was referenced in earlier discussions of the tractate. *Rabbi Shimon omer nidonim be-skila shehashreifa chamura* — Rabbi Shimon holds they receive *skila*, because *sreifa* is the more severe punishment, and therefore when in doubt one chooses the less severe mode. *Vachachamim omrim nidonim be-sreifa shehaskila chamura* — the *Chachamim* hold they receive *sreifa*, because *skila* is the more severe punishment. Rabbi Shimon's proof: the *Torah* assigns *sreifa* to the *bas kohen* who commits adultery, introducing it with particular rhetorical intensity — *ba'eish tisaref* — which implies it is the most severe penalty. The *Chachamim's* counter: the most morally weightiest sins, *avoda zara* and blasphemy, are assigned *skila*, which demonstrates that *skila* is the gravest punishment. Similarly for *haregin* and *nechekakim* — those liable to execution by sword vs. strangulation — Rabbi Shimon says the sword is the lesser punishment, the *Chachamim* say strangulation is.

### Meaning of Acharim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן אֲחֵרִים? אִילֵּימָא אֲחֵרִים כְּשֵׁרִים, פְּשִׁיטָא! וְתוּ, בְּהָא לֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָן לְכִיפָּה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches the *halakha* of a murderer who was intermingled with others. The Gemara asks: **Who are the others** with whom he was intermingled? **If we say** that **the others are upstanding** individuals, it is **obvious** that they are exempt; why would they all be punished for the transgression of one who happened to be among them? **And furthermore, shall Rabbi Yehuda say in that** case: The court **gathers them into the vaulted chamber** where they will eventually die? Can innocent people be left to die in this manner?

Returning to the opening case: *mai acheirim*? Who are these others with whom the murderer became mixed? The *gemara* dismisses the possibility that they are innocent bystanders — obviously you cannot execute innocent people, and Rabbi Yehuda's suggestion to imprison them would be equally untenable.

### Gmar Din Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא בְּרוֹצֵחַ שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ, שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בְּרוֹצְחִים אֲחֵרִים שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינָן, עָסְקִינַן. רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו, הִלְכָּךְ כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִיפְטְרִינְהוּ לִגְמָרֵי נָמֵי לָא, כֵּיוָן דְּרוֹצְחִין נִינְהוּ. הִלְכָּךְ כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָן לַכִּיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Shmuel says: Here we are dealing with a murderer** about whom witnesses testified and the judges deliberated, but **his verdict was not** yet **finalized;** and he was incarcerated until the end of his trial, and he **was intermingled with other murderers whose verdicts were** already **finalized.** It cannot be determined which of them is the murderer whose verdict was not yet finalized so that he may be brought to court for the verdict. The reason for the dispute is: **The Rabbis hold** that a court **finalizes a person's verdict only in his presence.** Since the identity of the one whose verdict was not finalized is unknown, he cannot be sentenced to death. Since one of them cannot be executed, none of them can be executed; **therefore, they are all exempt. And Rabbi Yehuda** holds: **Completely exempting them is also not** possible, **since they are murderers. Therefore,** the court **gathers them into the vaulted chamber.**

The *gemara* therefore cites Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Shmuel: *hacha be-rotzeach shelo nigmar dino shenisarev be-rotzchim acheirim shenigmar dinam askinan* — the case involves one murderer whose trial was completed but whose final judgment had not yet been formally pronounced, who became mixed in with a group of murderers who had already received their final pronouncements. The one unsentenced murderer is factually guilty — the court had completed all deliberations, heard all the testimony, and reached unanimous agreement. All that remained was the formal act of *gmar din*, the pronouncement of sentence directly to the convicted person. Before that pronouncement could occur, he ran out and merged with a group of already-sentenced murderers.

*Ein gomrin dino shel adam ela befanav* — final judgment cannot be pronounced except in the person's direct presence. This is not merely a matter of physical proximity within the building; it requires that the judgment be addressed specifically and identifiably to this individual. Once the murderer has been absorbed into the group of sentenced murderers and cannot be distinguished from them, the court cannot perform *gmar din* on any individual member of the group and be certain they are pronouncing sentence on the correct man.

This creates a striking parallel to the *Reuven-Shimon* problem discussed earlier in the daf. Just as a killer cannot be held liable for murdering the wrong person, a court cannot pass judgment on the wrong person. The requirement of specificity — *befanav*, in his presence and directed at him — is the court's analogue to the killer's requirement of specific intent. The *Rabbanan* therefore hold *kulam peturin*: since valid *gmar din* cannot be performed on any member of the group without risking that the judgment falls on the wrong person, no one in the group can be executed. The result — all convicted murderers released — is striking, but that is the ruling.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. *Keivan de-rotzchinin hinu, hilkach konasin lakippa* — since they are all murderers, they are all imprisoned. He holds that the release of convicted murderers is unacceptable, and that the impossibility of performing *gmar din* on the unsentenced murderer does not mean the group must be set free. Imprisoning the group is an extrajudicial measure — *halacha* does not formally recognize imprisonment as a standard penalty — but it is a societal necessity when the conventional legal process has been blocked. Since we know the unsentenced murderer has committed the act, confining him along with the others is appropriate even absent a formal sentence.

### Reish Lakish's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: בְּאָדָם, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּפְטִירִי. אֲבָל הָכָא, בְּשׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בִּשְׁוָרִים אֲחֵרִים שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינָן, קָמִיפַּלְגִי. רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כְּמִיתַת בְּעָלִים כָּךְ מִיתַת הַשּׁוֹר, וְאֵין גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו, הִלְכָּךְ כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָן לַכִּיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: In** the case where **a person** who was accused of killing another was intermingled with others, **everyone agrees that they are** all **exempt,** because one is not punished for a murder committed by others. **But here,** it is **with regard to an ox whose verdict** for goring a person to death **was not** yet **finalized, that was intermingled with other oxen whose verdict was** already **finalized,** that **they disagree. The Rabbis hold** that based on the juxtaposition between them (see Exodus 21:29), the *halakha* is that **like the death of the owner, so is the death of the ox, and** just as a court finalizes an individual's verdict only in his presence, a court **finalizes the verdict of an ox only in its presence. Therefore, all** the oxen **are exempt. And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the court **gathers them into the vaulted chamber.** Since it is an ox that gored, and the other oxen were sentenced to be stoned, they are incarcerated until they die.

Reish Lakish offers an alternative reading of the *mishna*. He maintains that regarding humans, everyone would agree with the *Rabbanan* — if a person is mixed into a group, and formal judgment cannot be performed, all are released. The dispute between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Yehuda, according to Reish Lakish, concerns a case not of humans but of oxen. *Aval hacha, be-shor shelo nigmar dino shenisarev be-shorim acheirim shenigmar dinan kamifligi* — here we are dealing with an ox, an animal adjudicated as a *shor haniskal* (one sentenced to stoning for goring a person to death), which had not yet received its final *gmar din*, and which became intermingled with other oxen that had already received their final sentence of stoning.

On this reading, the *mishna*'s use of the word *rotzeach* refers to a "murderous ox." The *Rabbanan* hold that just as human judgment requires the defendant's identified presence, *ke-mitas ba'alim kach mitas hashor* — the ox's judgment is treated with the same procedural rigor — *ein gomrin dino shel shor ela befanav* — and therefore the entire group of oxen goes free. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the group of oxen should all be confined to a pen and left to die — which raises the obvious question, addressed to the next session, of why they should not simply all be stoned, given that all of them are guilty.
