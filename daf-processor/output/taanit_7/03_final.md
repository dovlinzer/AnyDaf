# Taanit 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rain's Greatness (II)

### Rain vs Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים מִתְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, דְּאִילּוּ תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים — לַצַּדִּיקִים, וְאִילּוּ גְּשָׁמִים — בֵּין לַצַּדִּיקִים בֵּין לָרְשָׁעִים. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהִיא שְׁקוּלָה כִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים — קְבָעוּהָ בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites statements in praise of rainfall. **Rabbi Abbahu said: The day of rain is greater than the resurrection of the dead.** The reason is that **while the resurrection of the dead** benefits only **the righteous, rain** benefits **both the righteous and the wicked.** The Gemara comments: **And** this statement **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rav Yosef, as Rav Yosef said: Since** rainfall **is equivalent to the resurrection of the dead,** the Sages **established** its recitation **in** the second blessing of the *Amida*, the blessing of **the resurrection of the dead.** According to Rav Yosef, rainfall is the equivalent to, but not superior to, the resurrection of the dead.

The *gemara* on daf 7 opens a broader discussion of rain's power and religious significance — if not the central theme of *Masechet Taanit*, then certainly one of its defining concerns. Rabbi Abahu declares: *gadol yom geshamim mi-techiyat ha-metim* — the day of rain is greater than the resurrection of the dead. The reasoning is that *techiyat ha-metim* will benefit only the righteous, whereas rain benefits both the righteous and the wicked alike. One might object that this universality is actually a weakness — why should the wicked benefit? But the *gemara* treats it as a strength: rain brings life to all. This stands in tension with Rav Yosef, who had said that rain was placed in the *beracha* of *techiyat ha-metim* because the two are of equivalent weight — *shkulah k'techiyat ha-metim*. Rabbi Abahu goes further still, asserting rain's superiority over resurrection.

### Rain & Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים כְּיוֹם שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בּוֹ תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַעֲרֹף כַּמָּטָר לִקְחִי״, וְאֵין ״לֶקַח״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֶקַח טוֹב נָתַתִּי לָכֶם תּוֹרָתִי אַל תַּעֲזֹבוּ״. רָבָא אָמַר: יוֹתֵר מִיּוֹם שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בּוֹ תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַעֲרֹף כַּמָּטָר לִקְחִי״, מִי נִתְלֶה בְּמִי? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: קָטָן נִתְלֶה בַּגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Yehuda said: The day of the rains is as great as the day** on which **the Torah was given, as it is stated: "My doctrine [*likḥi*] shall drop as the rain"** (Deuteronomy 32:2), **and *lekaḥ* means nothing other** than **Torah, as it is stated: "For I give you good doctrine [*lekaḥ*]; do not forsake My Torah"** (Proverbs 4:2). **Rava said:** Rainfall is even **greater than the day on which the Torah was given, as it is stated: "My doctrine shall drop as the rain,"** and when one makes a comparison, **which** object **is** made **dependent upon which? You must say** that **the lesser** object **is dependent upon the greater** one. If Torah is compared to rain, it follows that rain is greater than Torah.

Rav Yehuda reads the verse as an equivalence: the giving of *Torah* is *like* rain. Rava reads it as an elevation of rain itself.

### Mi Natleh B'Mi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״יַעֲרֹף כַּמָּטָר לִקְחִי״, וּכְתִיב: ״תִּזַּל כַּטַּל אִמְרָתִי״! אִם תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הָגוּן הוּא — כַּטַּל, וְאִם לָאו — עוֹרְפֵהוּ כַּמָּטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another interpretation of the verse from Deuteronomy. **Rava raised a contradiction:** At the beginning of the verse **it is written: "My doctrine shall drop [*ya'arof*] as the rain,"** in a harsh manner, **and** yet later in the verse, **it is written: "My speech shall distill as the dew,"** in a gentle tone. He resolves this apparent contradiction as follows: **If he is a worthy Torah scholar,** the Torah flows through him **like the dew, but if** he is **not** worthy, **it snaps his neck [*orfehu*] like the** powerful **rain.**

This brings us to a fascinating question of hermeneutic logic: *mi natleh b'mi* — which entity depends on which in a comparison? The principle is *katan nitleh b'gadol*: when you say "A is like B," it is A whose stature you are trying to communicate, and B is the more powerful, emotionally resonant image. If *Torah* is compared to rain — "let it flow like rain" — then rain is the *gadol*, the primary reference point. The comparison is being made to help the audience appreciate something they don't already viscerally grasp by invoking something they do.

This is a striking observation about the *gemara*'s own frame of reference. In the *pesukim*, rain is used to make *Torah* vivid — "you understand rain, so understand that *Torah* is like that." The *gemara* reverses the direction entirely: "you understand *Torah*, so understand that rain is *like that*." The image that serves as the ground of comparison has shifted. For the authors of the biblical verses, rain was the common, deeply felt reality; *Torah*'s greatness needed to be communicated by invoking rain. For the *gemara*, or at least for the audience the *gemara* is addressing, the experience of *Torah* is the more immediate felt reality, and it is now rain whose significance needs to be conveyed.

This is not merely a rhetorical curiosity. It reflects something deep about shifts in collective experience and framing. Most of us are not farmers. We do not feel in our bones what it means to wait for rain, to need it, to have it arrive or fail to arrive. The *gemara* therefore has to work harder to communicate rain's weight, and it does so by reaching for the things its audience does understand and hold sacred.

## Torah Lishma

### Sam Chayim/Sam Mavet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי בְּנָאָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה לִשְׁמָהּ — תּוֹרָתוֹ נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם חַיִּים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵץ חַיִּים הִיא לַמַּחֲזִיקִים בָּהּ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״רִפְאוּת תְּהִי לְשָׁרֶּךָ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי מֹצְאִי מָצָא חַיִּים״. וְכׇל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם הַמָּוֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַעֲרֹף כַּמָּטָר לִקְחִי״, וְאֵין עֲרִיפָה אֶלָּא הֲרִיגָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָרְפוּ שָׁם אֶת הָעֶגְלָה בַּנָּחַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Bena'a would say: Anyone who engages in Torah for its own sake, his Torah** study **will be an elixir of life for him, as it is stated: "It is a tree of life to them who lay hold upon it"** (Proverbs 3:18), **and it says: "It shall be health to your navel"** (Proverbs 3:8), **and it says: "For whoever finds Me finds life"** (Proverbs 8:35). **And anyone who engages in Torah not for its own sake,** e.g., for self-aggrandizement, his Torah **will be an elixir of death for him, as it is stated: "My doctrine shall drop [*ya'arof* ] as the rain," and *arifa*** means **nothing other** than **killing, as it is stated: "And they shall break the heifer's neck [*arefu*] there in the valley"** (Deuteronomy 21:4).

### Tosafot's Answer

This is obviously a difficult *gemara*, because the Rabbis elsewhere operate on the principle of *mitoch shelo lishma ba lishma* — that beginning from impure motivations can lead to pure ones. Tosafot raises the question explicitly: we say *le-olam ya'asok adam ba-Torah af al gav de-eina lishma, she-mitoch shelo lishma ba lishma* — a person should always engage in *Torah* even if not *lishma*, because from *shelo lishma* one arrives at *lishma*. How can the same text call *shelo lishma* a poison?

Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing two entirely different types of *shelo lishma*. The case where we say *mitoch shelo lishma ba lishma* — where we are lenient and even encourage *Torah* study despite impure motives — refers to someone learning *kedei she-yikarei rabbi o kedei she-yekabduhu*, for honor, for social recognition, for selfish personal interest. That is a human failing, but it is not a perversion of *Torah* itself. The case where *Torah* becomes *sam ha-mavet* is where *lomed le-kanteir* — learning in order to attack, to disprove, to subvert, to lead others astray. That is not merely impure motivation; it is weaponization.

### Literal vs Spiritual

One might also ask what *sam ha-mavet* means here — is this a literal punishment, a divine smiting, or something understood at a more spiritual register? The same question applies to the image of *Torah* as light and life — does that refer to physical life, to *techiyat ha-metim*, or to spiritual vitality? One reading would understand *sam ha-mavet* to mean a spiritual death: you may believe you are engaged in a religious enterprise, but the very activity is hollowing you out from within, leading to spiritual ruin. One student offers a comparison to the *Rambam*'s understanding of *hashgacha pratit* — that the world's consequences are built in rather than externally imposed, and that just as misuse of the body brings physical harm, misuse of *Torah* brings its own natural spiritual consequence. That framing does not require a supernatural punishment and captures the sense that *Torah* taken the wrong way genuinely destroys.

Rashi's comment is also worth noting. When he explains *lishma*, he writes *ka'asher tzivani Hashem Elokai velo kedei le-hikarei rabbi* — as God commanded me, and not in order to be called *rabbi*. Rashi thus defines *lishma* in straightforward positive terms: the fulfillment of a divine command for its own sake.

This entire passage initiates a larger *sugya* about *Torah* and its multiple valences, which the *gemara* expresses not through a single image but through an expanding series of metaphors. The transition is natural: we started with rain, rain was compared to *Torah* and *Torah* to rain, and now, with *Torah* as the central concern, the *gemara* feels it cannot be contained by a single image. The richness and complexity of *Torah* demands a range of metaphors, and that is precisely what follows.

## Torah Metaphors

### Tree Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: לֵיתֵי מָר לִיתְנֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲלַשׁ לִבַּאי וְלָא יָכֵילְנָא. לֵימָא מָר מִילְּתָא דְּאַגַּדְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הָאָדָם עֵץ הַשָּׂדֶה״, וְכִי אָדָם עֵץ שָׂדֶה הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya** once **said to Rabbi Zeira: Let the Master come and teach** a halakhic discourse. Rabbi Zeira **said to him: My heart is weak and I cannot** strain myself over a halakhic discourse. Rabbi Yirmeya replied to him: In that case, **let the Master tell us a matter of *aggada*,** which does not require as much effort. Rabbi Zeira **said to him** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "For man is a tree of the field"** (Deuteronomy 20:19)? **And is man** actually **a tree of the field?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מִמֶּנּוּ תֹאכֵל וְאֹתוֹ לֹא תִכְרֹת״, וּכְתִיב: ״אֹתוֹ תַשְׁחִית וְכָרָתָּ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אִם תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הָגוּן הוּא — ״מִמֶּנּוּ תֹאכֵל וְאֹתוֹ לֹא תִכְרֹת״, וְאִם לָאו — ״אֹתוֹ תַשְׁחִית וְכָרָתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **because it is written** earlier in the same verse: **"You may eat of them but you may not cut them down," and it is written** in the next verse: **"Them you may destroy and cut down"** (Deuteronomy 20:20). This indicates that there are certain trees which may be cut down, while others may not be destroyed. **How so? If a Torah scholar is worthy: "You may eat of them but you may not cut them down," but if** he is **not** worthy: **"He you may destroy and cut down."**

Rabbi Ami asks Rabbi Zeira to teach some *Torah*, and when Rabbi Zeira says he is too weak for a formal *shiur*, Rabbi Ami invites him to share *aggadta* instead. Rabbi Zeira then transmits in the name of Rabbi Yochanan a reading of the verse *ki ha-adam etz ha-sadeh* — "for a person is a tree of the field" (Devarim 20:19). Taken at face value, the verse is rhetorical — is a person a tree that you should besiege the tree? But Rabbi Yochanan reads it non-rhetorically. Yes, a person is like a tree. How?

The verse continues: *ki mimenu tochal ve-oto lo tichrot* — from it you shall eat, and you shall not cut it down (if it bears fruit). But if it is not fruit-bearing: *oto tashchit ve-charat* — you shall destroy and cut it down. Translated into human terms: *im talmid chacham hagun hu* — if he is a fitting *talmid chacham*, one whose learning bears fruit and nourishes others — *mimenu tochal*, you can eat from him, you should learn from him. *Ve-oto lo tichrot* — do not cut off your connection to him. Tosafot adds: even if you are not actively learning from him at the moment, do not sever the relationship. But *ve-im lav* — if he is not fitting, if his *middot* are flawed — *oto tashchit ve-charat*, cut off your relationship with him entirely.

This raises a point of tension with the earlier teaching, which suggested that if a student has poor character you should teach *Torah* more forcefully, presumably to correct him. Here, the advice runs in the opposite direction: if the *talmid chacham* himself is of poor character, you should not benefit from his Torah. The throughline in both cases is that *Torah* and character are deeply intertwined, and the quality of what is transmitted is inseparable from the character of the one transmitting or receiving it.

### Iron Sharpening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּרְזֶל בְּבַרְזֶל יָחַד״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה בַּרְזֶל זֶה — אֶחָד מְחַדֵּד אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, אַף שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים — מְחַדְּדִין זֶה אֶת זֶה בַּהֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other expositions that deal with Torah study. **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Iron sharpens iron,** so a man sharpens the countenance of his friend" (Proverbs 27:17)? This verse comes **to tell you** that **just as** with **these iron implements, one sharpens the other** when they are rubbed against each other, **so too,** when **Torah scholars** study together, they **sharpen one another in *halakha*.**

Rabbi Chama berebi Chanina asks about the verse *barzel be-varzel yachad* — "iron sharpens iron together" (Mishlei 27:17). Just as two blades sharpen one another when rubbed together, so too *shenei talmidei chachamim mechadedim zeh et zeh be-halachah* — two students of the sages sharpen one another in the study of *halachah*. The shift from *Torah* to *halachah* here is worth noting, though no explicit explanation is given. It may reflect the particular quality of precision and sharpness that halachic reasoning demands — the give and take of *chavruta* in *halachah* as distinct from the more expansive mode of *aggadta*. This is the first explicit turn toward *chavruta* learning as a theme.

### Fire Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה כָּאֵשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלֹא כֹה דְבָרִי כָּאֵשׁ נְאֻם ה׳״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה אֵשׁ אֵינוֹ דּוֹלֵק יְחִידִי, אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה אֵין מִתְקַיְּימִין בִּיחִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: Why are matters of Torah compared to fire, as it is stated: "Is not My word like fire, says the Lord"** (Jeremiah 23:29)? **To tell you: Just as fire does not ignite** in **a lone** stick of wood but in a pile of kindling, **so too, matters of Torah are not retained** and understood properly by **a lone** scholar who studies by himself, but by a group of Sages.

Rabbah bar bar Chanah asks: *lama nimshalu divrei Torah ka-esh?* — why is *Torah* compared to fire? The verse says *halo koh devarim ka-esh ne'um Hashem* — are not My words like fire, says God. Just as fire cannot sustain itself from a single log of wood — *ma esh eino dolek yechidi*, fire does not burn alone — so *Torah* cannot sustain itself through one person alone. *Torah* requires a community; it is not a solitary enterprise.

### Chavruta Imperative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״חֶרֶב אֶל הַבַּדִּים וְנֹאָלוּ״, חֶרֶב עַל שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בַּד בְּבַד בַּתּוֹרָה. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּטַּפְּשִׁין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנֹאָלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "A sword is upon the boasters [*habaddim*], and they shall become fools [*noalu*]"** (Jeremiah 50:36)? This verse can be interpreted homiletically: There is a **sword upon the enemies of Torah scholars,** a euphemism for Torah scholars themselves, **who sit alone [*bad bevad*] and study Torah. And not only that, but** those who study by themselves **grow foolish** from their solitary Torah study, **as it is stated: "And they shall become fools."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁחוֹטְאִין. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְנֹאָלוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אֲשֶׁר נוֹאַלְנוּ וַאֲשֶׁר חָטָאנוּ״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא — מֵהָכָא: ״נוֹאֲלוּ שָׂרֵי צֹעַן [וְגוֹ׳] הִתְעוּ אֶת מִצְרַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And not only that, but they sin, as it is written here: "And they shall become fools," and it is written there: "For that we have done foolishly [*noalnu*] and for that we have sinned"** (Numbers 12:11). **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived **from here: "The princes of Zoan have become fools [*noalu*]…they have caused Egypt to go astray"** (Isaiah 19:13).

Rabbi Yose berebi Chanina sharpens this further with an interpretation of *cherev el ha-badim ve-no'alu* — "a sword against the *badim*, and they shall be foolish." The *gemara* reads *badim* as those who learn *bad be-vad* — singly, alone, without a *chavruta*. *Cherev al soneihem shel talmidei chachamim* — a sword against those sages who study in isolation. And not only is this a failure of method; such scholars do not merely fail to grow sharper — *ela she-mitapshin* — they become stupider. *Shene'emar ve-no'alu* — and they will be foolish. And not only foolish — it is a *chet*, a sin, because *no'alu* is connected to the verse *asher no'alnu va'asher chatanu* — "we acted foolishly and we sinned." Foolishness in *Torah*, particularly in *halachah*, leads to going astray oneself, to misleading others, and ultimately to sin.

### Small Sharpens Large

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּעֵץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵץ חַיִּים הִיא לַמַּחֲזִיקִים בָּהּ״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה עֵץ קָטָן מַדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, אַף תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים קְטַנִּים מְחַדְּדִים אֶת הַגְּדוֹלִים. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַרְבֵּה לָמַדְתִּי מֵרַבּוֹתַי, וּמֵחֲבֵירַי יוֹתֵר מֵרַבּוֹתַי, וּמִתַּלְמִידַי יוֹתֵר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Why are Torah matters likened to a tree, as it is stated: "It is a tree of life to them who lay hold upon it"** (Proverbs 3:18)? This verse comes **to tell you** that **just as a small** piece of **wood can ignite a large piece, so too, minor Torah scholars can sharpen great** Torah scholars and enable them to advance in their studies. **And this is what Rabbi Ḥanina said: I have learned much from my teachers and even more from my friends, but from my students** I have learned **more than** from **all of them.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak asks: *lama nimshalu divrei Torah ke-etz?* — why is *Torah* compared to a tree? The verse says *etz chayim hi la-machazikm bah*. Just as kindling a great fire requires small twigs to ignite the large logs, *af talmidei chachamim ktanim mechadedim et ha-gedolim* — the younger, smaller students sharpen the greater ones. This is a nuance beyond the earlier teaching about *chavruta* between peers; here it is the junior students who provide the spark that gets the larger fire burning.

### Three Sources

This connects naturally to the famous statement of Rabbi Chanina: *harbeh lamadti me-rabotai, u-me-chaverai yoter me-rabotai, u-mi-talmidai yoter mi-kulam* — I have learned much from my masters, more from my colleagues than my masters, and more from my students than from all of them. Why is it that students outstrip even colleagues? Rashi, at the bottom of *amud bet*, explains: *she-sho'alim kol sha'ah* — they ask constantly, relentlessly, with the insistence and perhaps the naivety of those not yet bound by the conventions of the discipline. Sometimes a student asks what the more senior scholar would not dare ask, and those turn out to be the most important questions. The enthusiasm of the student, the freshness of the question, forces the teacher to think more carefully, more fundamentally.

Looking back at this entire collection of images, what is most striking is their multiplicity and their dynamism. Rain is nurturing, but it is somewhat static in its imagery — water falls, things grow. Fire sharpening, iron against iron, small twigs igniting large logs — these images carry urgency, friction, energy. For those of us removed from agricultural reality, they may communicate something about *Torah*'s vitality that rain alone cannot.

## Water Metaphor

### Water Seeking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״לִקְרַאת צָמֵא הֵתָיוּ מָיִם״, וּכְתִיב: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״. אִם תַּלְמִיד הָגוּן הוּא — ״לִקְרַאת צָמֵא הֵתָיוּ מַיִם״, וְאִי לָא — ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa raised a contradiction.** In one verse **it is written: "To him who is thirsty bring water"** (Isaiah 21:14), which indicates that the one who has water must bring it to the thirsty person, **and it is written** elsewhere: **"Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water"** (Isaiah 55:1), from which it may be inferred that the thirsty person must seek out water himself. Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa resolves this apparent contradiction by explaining that **if he is a worthy student** the teacher must seek him out, as in **"to him who is thirsty bring water," but if** the student is **not** worthy, then **"Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water,"** i.e., this student must seek out a teacher himself.

The *gemara* now returns to water as a metaphor for *Torah*, and Rabbi Hanina bar Pappa surfaces a contradiction between two verses. One says *likrat tzameh hatayu mayim* — "go out to meet the thirsty and bring them water." Another says *hoy kol tzameh lechu la-mayim* — "come, all who are thirsty, go to the water." One verse says bring the water to the thirsty person; the other says let the thirsty person come. The resolution follows the now-familiar pattern: *im talmid hagun hu* — if the student is fitting — go out and bring him the water, go out of your way to teach him. *Ve-im lav* — and if not — let him come to the water himself; let him demonstrate his motivation, prove his perseverance.

One should note, however, that even the student who must come to the water is still described as *tzameh* — thirsty. He still desires *Torah*. The question of *eino hagun* is not about a student who is indifferent or hostile, but perhaps about one who has not yet proven his readiness, or whose character needs some tempering before he is given full access. The withholding of the teacher's outreach may itself be a form of pedagogy.

### Wellsprings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר חָמָא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״יָפוּצוּ מַעְיְנֹתֶיךָ חוּצָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״יִהְיוּ לְךָ לְבַדֶּךָ״. אִם תַּלְמִיד הָגוּן הוּא — ״יָפוּצוּ מַעְיְנֹתֶיךָ חוּצָה״, וְאִם לָאו — ״יִהְיוּ לְךָ לְבַדֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama raised** another **contradiction.** In one verse **it is written: "Let your springs be dispersed abroad"** (Proverbs 5:16), whereas in the next verse **it is written: "Let them be your own"** (Proverbs 5:17). Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama explains: **If the student** sitting before you **is worthy,** then **"Let your springs be dispersed abroad,"** as you should teach him, but **if** he is **not** worthy, then **"Let them be your own."**

Rabbi Hanina bar Hama raises a similar tension between two verses. One says *yafutzu ma'aynotecha chutza* — "let your wellsprings burst forth to the outside" — the famous verse later invoked in the letter of the *Ba'al Shem Tov*, where it is said that *mashiach* will not come until *yafutzu ma'aynotecha chutza*, until the teachings spread outward to the world. The other says *yihyu lecha levadecha* — keep it for yourself alone. Again the same resolution: *im talmid hagun hu*, share your *Torah* freely, let the wellsprings burst forth. *Ve-im lav*, keep it to yourself. Rashi specifies that what must be withheld from the unworthy student is the esoteric — *ma'aseh merkavah*, *ma'aseh bereshit*. But Tosafot suggests a broader concern: some students hear only what they want to hear, and discernment in what one shares and how one says it is always called for.

### Humility Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר אִידִי: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה לְמַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה מַיִם מַנִּיחִין מָקוֹם גָּבוֹהַּ וְהוֹלְכִין לְמָקוֹם נָמוּךְ, אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה אֵין מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ שְׁפָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥanina bar Idi said: Why are matters of Torah likened to water, as it is written: "Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water"** (Isaiah 55:1)? This verse comes **to tell you: Just as water leaves a high place and flows to a low place, so too, Torah matters are retained only by one whose spirit is lowly,** i.e., a humble person.

Rabbi Hanina bar Idi asks explicitly: *lama nimshalu divrei Torah la-mayim?* — why is *Torah* compared to water? *De-ketiv: hoy kol tzameh lechu la-mayim.* The answer is structural: *ma mayim manichim makom gavo'ah ve-holchim le-makom namuch* — water flows away from high places and seeks out low places. *Af divrei Torah ein mitkaymim ela be-mi she-da'ato shfela* — *Torah* is only sustained in one whose mind is *shfela*, humble, lowly. The humble person is the low place into which *Torah* naturally flows and in which it finds its home.

### Three Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה לִשְׁלֹשָׁה מַשְׁקִין הַלָּלוּ: בְּמַיִם, וּבְיַיִן, וּבְחָלָב. דִּכְתִיב: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״, וּכְתִיב: ״לְכוּ שִׁבְרוּ וֶאֱכֹלוּ וּלְכוּ שִׁבְרוּ בְּלוֹא כֶסֶף וּבְלוֹא מְחִיר יַיִן וְחָלָב״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה שְׁלֹשָׁה מַשְׁקִין הַלָּלוּ אֵין מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בַּפָּחוּת שֶׁבַּכֵּלִים — אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה אֵין מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ שְׁפָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Oshaya said: Why are matters of Torah likened to these three liquids: To water, wine and milk? As it is written** with regard to water: **"Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water," and it is written** in the same verse: **"Come, buy and eat; yea, come, buy wine and milk without money and without price."** This verse comes **to tell you: Just as these three liquids can be retained only in the least of vessels,** e.g., clay pots, but not vessels of silver and gold, as they will spoil, **so too, matters of Torah are retained only by one whose spirit is lowly.**

Rabbi Oshaya extends the metaphor further. Why is *Torah* compared not just to water but to three liquids — water, wine, and milk? The verse for water is *hoy kol tzameh lechu la-mayim*; for wine and milk: *lechu shivru ve-acholu, lechu shivru belo chesef u-velo mechir, yayin va-chalav*. The answer: *mah shlosha mashkin halalu ein mitkaymim ela ba-pachot she-ba-keilim* — these three liquids are best preserved not in elegant vessels of gold and silver, but in the simplest pottery vessels. *Af divrei Torah ein mitkaymim ela be-mi she-da'ato shfela* — similarly, *Torah* is only truly preserved in the humble person. The lowliness of the vessel is what keeps the contents fresh and alive.

### Caesar's Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה דְּקֵיסָר לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: אִי חׇכְמָה מְפוֹאָרָה בִּכְלִי מְכוֹעָר! אֲמַר לַהּ: אָבִיךָ רָמֵי חַמְרָא בְּמָנֵי דְפַחְרָא? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא בְּמַאי נִירְמֵי? אֲמַר לַהּ: אַתּוּן דַּחֲשִׁיבִיתוּ, רְמוֹ בְּמָאנֵי דַּהֲבָא וְכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related incident: This **is as the daughter of the** Roman **emperor said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya,** who was an ugly man: **Woe to glorious wisdom** such as yours, which is contained **in an ugly vessel.** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to her,** in a seemingly unrelated response: Does **your father keep his wine in** simple **clay vessels?** The emperor's daughter **said to him: Rather, in what,** then, **should he keep it?** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to her: You, who are so important,** should **put it in vessels of gold and silver.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַלָה וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ רַמְיֵיהּ לְחַמְרָא בְּמָנֵי דַּהֲבָא וְכַסְפָּא, וּתְקֵיף. אֲתוֹ וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ לִבְרַתֵּיהּ: מַאן אֲמַר לָךְ הָכִי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה. קַרְיוּהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי אֲמַרְתְּ לַהּ הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דַּאֲמַרָה לִי, אֲמַרִי לַהּ. וְהָא אִיכָּא שַׁפִּירֵי דִּגְמִירִי!
>
> **Translation:** The emperor's daughter **went and said** this **to her father. He put the wine in vessels of gold and silver and it turned sour.** When his advisors **came and told the emperor** that the wine had turned sour, **he said to** his daughter: **Who told you** to do **this?** His daughter **responded: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya.** The emperor **summoned him** and **said to him: Why did you say this to her?** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to him: Just as she said to me, so I said say to her,** to demonstrate to her that fine material is best preserved in the least of vessels. The emperor said to him: **But there are handsome people who are learned.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הֲווֹ סְנוּ — טְפֵי הֲווֹ גְּמִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua replied: **Had they been ugly, they would have been even more learned.**

This teaching leads directly into one of the more memorable anecdotes in the *sugya*. The daughter of Caesar said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanania: *ai chochma me'uara bi-chli mechoar* — what beautiful wisdom is kept in such an ugly vessel! Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanania, apparently, was not a handsome man. He replied: does your father keep his wine in pottery vessels? She was taken aback — what else would one use? He said: you are the Caesar's household, people of great importance — why not keep your wine in vessels of gold and silver?

She went and told her father, who duly transferred the wine into gold and silver vessels — and it turned sour. The Caesar summoned Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanania and demanded to know who had given this advice. When Rabbi Yehoshua explained, the Caesar pointed out that Rabbi Yehoshua had not explicitly told her to change the vessels. Rashi's comment is important here: Rabbi Yehoshua had not actually recommended the change — that would have been imprudent in the Caesar's household. He had spoken rhetorically, responding in kind to her rhetorical provocation, and she had taken it as a literal suggestion.

The *gemara* then raises an empirical objection: *ve-ika shapiri de-gmiri* — but there are handsome, beautiful people who are accomplished Torah scholars! The response: *ei havu sanu tfei havu gmiri* — if they were uglier, they would know even more. Tosafot softens this: it is not that ugliness as such is the desired state, but rather that being excessively drawn to one's own beauty, or living in a society that prizes beauty excessively, diverts attention and self-investment away from the self-abnegation that full *Torah* commitment requires. Our sense of our own importance, our self-regard, prevents us from becoming the genuinely lowly vessel that can hold everything.

### Hesech Hadaat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: מָה שְׁלֹשָׁה מַשְׁקִין הַלָּלוּ אֵין נִפְסָלִין אֶלָּא בְּהֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת — אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה אֵין מִשְׁתַּכְחִין אֶלָּא בְּהֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the Torah is likened to water, wine, and milk because **just as these three liquids are spoiled only by diversion of attention, so too, are Torah matters forgotten only through diversion of attention.** If water, wine and milk are guarded, they will not spoil or have dirty objects fall into them.

The *gemara* adds a second interpretation of the three-liquids teaching. *Mah shlosha mashkin halalu ein nifsalim ela be-hesech ha-da'at* — these three liquids spoil only through *hesech ha-da'at*, through inattention, through failing to cover them and put them away properly. *Af divrei Torah ein mishtakchim ela be-hesech ha-da'at* — *Torah* is forgotten only through *hesech ha-da'at*. This is subtly different from the vessel teaching. There the emphasis was on the character of the person who holds the *Torah* — the humble vessel preserves it. Here the emphasis is on continuity of attention: as long as one does not remove one's focus, the *Torah* will not be lost.

There is something quietly reassuring in the phrasing. Rather than saying *Torah* must be constantly repeated or it will certainly be forgotten, the formulation is its reverse: it will only be forgotten if you stop paying attention. The onus shifts slightly; the assumption is that *Torah* tends to stay with you as long as you do not actively disengage.

## Rain's Greatness (II)

### Rain & Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים כְּיוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַרְעִיפוּ שָׁמַיִם מִמַּעַל וּשְׁחָקִים יִזְּלוּ צֶדֶק תִּפְתַּח אֶרֶץ וְיִפְרוּ יֶשַׁע וּצְדָקָה תַצְמִיחַ יַחַד אֲנִי ה׳ בְּרָאתִיו״. ״בְּרָאתִים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״בְּרָאתִיו״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of rain. **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: The day of the rains is** as **great as the day on which the heavens and earth were created, as it is stated: "Drop down, heavens, from above, let the skies pour down righteousness; let the earth open that they may bring forth salvation, and let it cause righteousness to spring up together; I, the Lord, have created it"** (Isaiah 45:8). The Gemara explains that the verse **does not say: I have created them,** in the plural, **but: I have created it.** In other words, the verse is referring to rain, rather than to the heavens and the earth, which indicates that rainfall is as important as the creation of the world.

After the extended digression into metaphors for *Torah*, the *gemara* circles back to where it began — the greatness of rain. Rabbi Chama bar Chanina now adds a third point of comparison: *gadol yom ha-geshamim ki-yom she-nivreu shamayim va-aretz* — the day of rain is as great as the day of the creation of heaven and earth. The proof is from the verse: *har'ifu shamayim mi-ma'al u-shchakim yizlu tzedek, tiftach eretz ve-yifru yesha u-tzedakah tatzmia yachad, Ani Hashem berativ* — the heavens shall drip from above, and the clouds shall flow with righteousness; the earth shall open, and salvation and righteousness shall blossom together — I, God, have created *it*. The *gemara* notes that the text says *berativ* — He created *it* (singular) — not *beratim* (plural). The singular referent is the rain, and the verb *bara* connects the rain to the act of creation.

This is a fitting moment to articulate what may be the *sugya*'s underlying theological current. There are two ways one might understand why rain occupies such an exalted place in *Masechet Taanit*. The first is the straightforward physical argument: rain sustains life, therefore it is important, therefore we pray for it. But the *gemara* consistently reaches for comparisons — *techiyat ha-metim*, the giving of *Torah*, the creation of the world — that go well beyond practical necessity. These comparisons all share a common character: they are moments of divine self-disclosure, of God's direct and manifest engagement with the world. *Techiyat ha-metim* is the ultimate eschatological intervention; *matan Torah* is the paradigmatic revelation; creation is the founding act. Rain, in this reading, is not merely a physical phenomenon that sustains agriculture. It is a medium through which God's presence, closeness, and active care are made palpable in the world.

This is not simply our reading back onto the text. It is the texture of the *sugya* itself. The authors of the *gemara* lived in a world without scientific distance from nature; rain was not explained by meteorological models but was experienced as a direct expression of divine relationship. *Geshamim* arriving meant God was close; their absence meant something in that relationship had gone wrong. And that is precisely the frame within which the remainder of the *masechet* operates.

## Rain = Forgiveness

### Salvation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ יְשׁוּעָה פָּרָה וְרָבָה בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּפְתַּח אֶרֶץ וְיִפְרוּ יֶשַׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya** likewise **said: The day of rain is great, as** rain **even** facilitates **salvation,** which **is fruitful and multiplies on** that day. It is a time of God's favor, when salvation is brought forth into the world, **as it is stated: "Let the earth open that they may bring forth salvation"** (Isaiah 45:8).

Rabbi Oshaya says: *gadol yom geshamim she-afilu yeshua para ve-raba bo* — great is the day of rain, for even salvation is fruitful and multiplies on a day of rain. The verse: *tiftach eretz ve-yifru yesha* — the earth will open, and salvation will flourish. Rain is not merely the precondition for agricultural growth; it is the moment when *yeshua*, divine salvation, is most abundantly present.

### Sins Forgiven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים יוֹרְדִים אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִמְחֲלוּ עֲוֹנוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רָצִיתָ ה׳ אַרְצֶךָ שַׁבְתָּ שְׁבִית יַעֲקֹב. נָשָׂאתָ עֲוֹן עַמֶּךָ כִּסִּיתָ כׇל חַטָּאתָם סֶלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai said: Rain falls only if the Jewish people's transgressions have been forgiven, as it is stated: "Lord, You have been favorable to Your land; You have turned the captivity of Jacob; You have forgiven the iniquity of Your people; You have pardoned all their sin. Selah"** (Psalms 85:2–3). This chapter proceeds to discuss rainfall: "And righteousness has looked down from Heaven" (Psalms 85:12), in the form of rain.

Rabbi Tanchum bar Chanilai takes this a striking step further: *ein geshamim yordim ela im ken nimchalu avonoteihem shel Yisrael* — rain descends only when the sins of Israel have been forgiven. The proof verse is *ratzita Hashem artzecha, shavta shevut Yaakov, nasata avon amecha, kisita kol chatatam sela* — You have desired Your land, You have returned the captivity of Jacob, You have lifted off the sin of Your nation, You have covered all their sins.

This is worth pausing over. The structure of *Masechet Taanit* most naturally invites the reading of sin as the cause of drought — we sinned, rain was withheld, we do *teshuva*, rain returns. But Rabbi Tanchum's formulation is not about a drought-and-response cycle. It begins with rain falling and reads from that falling the conclusion that sins have been forgiven. Nobody first acknowledged having sinned. The rain itself is the announcement of divine forgiveness, an expression of unconditional grace and divine desire.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ זְעֵירִי מִדִּיהֲבַת לְרָבִינָא: אַתּוּן, מֵהָכָא מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ. אֲנַן, מֵהָכָא מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ: ״וְאַתָּה תִּשְׁמַע הַשָּׁמַיִם וְסָלַחְתָּ לְחַטָּאת וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **Ze'iri from** the town of **Dihavat said to Ravina: You learned this** idea **from here,** whereas **we learned it from here,** a different verse: "When heaven is closed up, and there is no rain, when they sin against You, if they pray toward this place and confess Your name and turn from their sin, when You afflict them, **then You, hear in heaven and forgive the sin of** Your servants and of Your people Israel, when You teach them the good way in which they should walk, and send rain upon Your land, which You have given to Your people as an inheritance" (I Kings 8:35–36).

*Ze'iri mi-Dehavat* (as Rashi notes, a different *Ze'iri* from the earlier *amora* of the same name) says to *Ravina*: you derive this from that verse; we derive it from here — *ve-atah tishma ha-shamayim ve-salachta le-chatat amecha* — and You shall hear from the heavens and shall forgive the sins of Your people. That verse, familiar from the *tefilla* of Shlomo at the opening of *Masechet Taanit*, does follow the classic sequence: drought, cry, forgiveness, rain. But Rabbi Tanchum's formulation reads in the opposite direction: the mere presence of rain is itself already the sign of forgiveness and divine closeness.

## Rain Withheld

### Withheld Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִתְחַיְּיבוּ שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּלָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִיָּה גַם חֹם יִגְזְלוּ מֵימֵי שֶׁלֶג שְׁאוֹל חָטָאוּ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ זְעֵירִי מִדִּיהֲבַת לְרָבִינָא: אַתּוּן, מֵהָכָא מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ. אֲנַן, מֵהָכָא מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ: ״וְעָצַר אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם וַאֲבַדְתֶּם מְהֵרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tanḥum, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya of the village of Akko, said: The rains are withheld only if the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people, **have been sentenced to destruction** for their sins, **as it is stated: "Drought and heat will steal the snow waters; to the grave those who have sinned"** (Job 24:19). According to this interpretation, snow water will be stolen by drought, i.e., there will be none available, when people have sinned to the point that they deserve the grave. **Ze'iri from Dihavat said to Ravina: You learned this** idea **from here; we learned it from here:** "And the anger of the Lord will be kindled against you, **and He will close up the heavens,** and there will be no rain, and the earth will not give its fruit, **and you will perish quickly"** (Deuteronomy 11:17).

Rabbi Tanchum bereih de-Rabbi Chiya ish Kefar Akko turns to the obverse: *ein geshamim ne'etzarim ela im ken nitchayvu soneihem shel Yisrael kelaya* — rains are withheld only when the enemies of Israel (a circumlocution for Israel itself) deserve destruction. The verse: *tziya gam chom yigzelu meimei sheleg, sheol chatu* — the thirst and the heat have stolen the waters of the snow, they have sinned to the very depth of *sheol*. Rains withheld, no snow waters — they deserve to descend to *sheol* because of their sins.

*Ze'iri mi-Dehavat* again addresses *Ravina*: you derive it from that verse; we derive it from *ve-atzarta ha-shamayim ve-lo yihyeh matar va-avadtem meheira* — I will hold back the heavens, there will be no rain, and you shall be quickly driven from the land. This second verse is plainer and more direct — indeed, it comes from the *Torah* itself. Why the first derivation favored the less obvious verse remains somewhat unexplained; the first verse points toward utter destruction and *sheol*, while the second is about exile. Perhaps they are understood as pointing to different dimensions of consequence.

### Terumot Maasrot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בִּיטּוּל תְּרוּמוֹת וּמַעַשְׂרוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִיָּה גַם חֹם יִגְזְלוּ מֵימֵי שֶׁלֶג״. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בִּשְׁבִיל דְּבָרִים שֶׁצִּוִּיתִי אֶתְכֶם בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְלֹא עֲשִׂיתֶם — יִגָּזְלוּ מִכֶּם מֵימֵי שֶׁלֶג בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: The rains are withheld only due to** the sin of **the nullification of *teruma* and tithes, as it is stated: "Drought and heat will steal the snow waters"** (Job 24:19). The Gemara asks: **From where** in the verse may this idea **be inferred** from the verse? **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Due to matters that I have commanded you to do in the summer,** e.g., take *teruma* and tithes from the summer produce, **and that you did not do, the snow waters will be stolen from you in the rainy season.**

Rav Chisda says: *ein geshamim ne'atzarim ella bishvil bitul trumos u-ma'asros* — rains are withheld because of the neglect of *terumot* and *ma'asrot*. The verse is the same as before — *tziya gam chom yigzelu meimei sheleg* — and the derivation comes from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *bishvil devarim she-tzivisi eschem bi-ymot ha-chama*, because of things I commanded you in the warm months — the harvest season, when *terumot u-ma'asrot* are separated — *ve-lo asisem*, and you did not do them, *yigzlu mi-kem meimei sheleg bi-ymot ha-geshamim*, the rains of the winter season will be stolen from you.

The inner logic here is transparent and even elegant. *Terumot u-ma'asrot* are the obligatory gifts given from the grain harvest, acknowledging that God's hand produced the abundance. To take the agricultural bounty and fail to acknowledge its divine source is a kind of ingratitude and a denial of the relationship that the rain itself represents. The punishment therefore corresponds: those who refuse to acknowledge God's generosity in the summer will not receive it in the winter. It is a *middah ke-neged middah* of the most coherent kind.

### Lashon Hara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל מְסַפְּרֵי לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רוּחַ צָפוֹן תְּחוֹלֵל גָּשֶׁם וּפָנִים נִזְעָמִים לְשׁוֹן סָתֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said: The rains are withheld only due to** the sin of those **who speak slander, as it is stated: "The north wind brings forth rain, but a backbiting tongue, an angry countenance"** (Proverbs 25:23). This verse indicates that if the countenance of the heavens is angry, with neither clouds nor rain, it is due to slanderous speech.

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says: *ein geshamim ne'atzarim ella bishvil mesaperei lashon hara* — rains are withheld because of those who speak *lashon hara*. The verse: *ruach tzafon techolel geshem u-fanim niz'amim lashon sater* — the north wind drives away rain, and an angry face is like a secret speech. The *lashon sater* — speech spoken in hiding, in private — is *lashon hara*, and like the north wind it drives the rain away.

The correspondence here is less immediately obvious than with *terumot u-ma'asrot*, but the connection can be drawn. *Lashon hara* is often described as a kind of destructive power that, like the wind, operates invisibly. The *ruach* of *lashon hara* mirrors the *ruach* that disperses the rain clouds. More directly: just as rain gives life, the tongue can take it away; the one who speaks *lashon hara* deploys a life-destroying wind and therefore forfeits the life-giving rain.

### Brazenness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סַלָּא אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל עַזֵּי פָנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּמָּנְעוּ רְבִבִים וּמַלְקוֹשׁ לוֹא הָיָה וּמֵצַח אִשָּׁה זוֹנָה הָיָה לָךְ וְגוֹ׳״. וְאָמַר רַב סַלָּא אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עַזּוּת פָּנִים — סוֹף נִכְשָׁל בַּעֲבֵירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמֵצַח אִשָּׁה זוֹנָה הָיָה לָךְ״. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁנִּכְשַׁל בַּעֲבֵירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָיָה לָךְ״, וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Salla said** that **Rav Hamnuna said: The rains are withheld only due to impudent** people, **as it is stated: "Therefore the showers have been withheld, and there has been no last rain, yet you had a harlot's forehead,** you refused to be ashamed" (Jeremiah 3:3). **And Rav Salla said** that **Rav Hamnuna said,** with regard to the same verse: **Any man who is insolent will ultimately stumble over** the **transgression** of prostitution, **as it is stated: "Yet you had a prostitute's forehead." Rav Naḥman said:** The verse does not mean that he will commit a sexual transgression in the future; rather, **it is known that he has** already **stumbled over** this **transgression, as it is stated: "You had,"** in the past tense, **and it is not stated: You will.**

Rav Sala in the name of Rav Hamnuna: *ein geshamim ne'atzarim ella bishvil azei fanim* — rains are withheld on account of brazenness, *azut panim*. The verse: *va-yimane'u revivim u-malkosh lo haya u-metzach isha zona haya lach* — the early rains were withheld, and there was no *malkosh* late rain, and the forehead of a brazen woman, of a *zona*, you had. The *metzach*, the brazen forehead, appears in the same verse as the withholding of rain.

The same *amora* then adds a further teaching: *kol adam she-yesh lo azus panim, sof nichshal ba-aveirah* — any person who possesses brazenness will ultimately stumble into sin, specifically sexual sin as indicated by the *zona* of that verse. A further nuance from the *pasuk*: the text uses *haya lach* — you *had* — rather than *yihyeh lach* — you *will have*. Brazenness is therefore read not as a predictor of future sin but as an indicator of existing sin; the shameless bearing is the outward expression of a hidden transgression already committed, a kind of cover.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עַזּוּת פָּנִים — מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵעֵז אִישׁ רָשָׁע בְּפָנָיו״. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מוּתָּר לִשְׂנאוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעֹז פָּנָיו יְשֻׁנֶּא״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״יְשֻׁנֶּא״, אֶלָּא ״יִשָּׂנֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** With regard to **any man who is insolent, it is permitted to call him wicked** to his face, **as it is stated: "A wicked man makes his face insolent"** (Proverbs 21:29). **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: It is permitted to hate him, as it is stated: "And the insolence of his face is changed"** (Ecclesiastes 8:1). **Do not read** it as: **"Is changed [*yeshunne*]"; rather,** read it as: **Is hated [*yissane*],** as the two words are spelled the same way in Hebrew, albeit with different vocalization and pronunciation.

Rabbah bar Avuna then adds: any person who possesses *azut panim* may be called a *rasha* — as the verse says *he'ez ish rasha be-fanav*, the wicked man hardens his face. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak goes further: *mutar lisno'o* — one is permitted to hate him — citing *ve-oz panav yishuneh*, reading *yishuneh* not as "will be changed" but through *al tikri* as *yisaneh*, "shall be hated." Whether this carries practical behavioral implications or remains primarily an internal permission, in the manner of the verse *lo sisna et achicha bi-lvavecha*, is not elaborated here, though *Gemaras* elsewhere do draw practical consequences from such permissions.

### Bitul Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בִּיטּוּל תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּעֲצַלְתַּיִם יִמַּךְ הַמְּקָרֶה״ — בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְלוּת שֶׁהָיָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא עָסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה — נַעֲשֶׂה שׂוֹנְאוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מָךְ. וְאֵין ״מָךְ״ אֶלָּא עָנִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם מָךְ הוּא מֵעֶרְכֶּךָ״. וְאֵין ״מְקָרֶה״ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַמְקָרֶה בַמַּיִם עֲלִיּוֹתָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ketina said: The rains are withheld only due to** the sin of **dereliction in** the study of **Torah, as it is stated: "By slothfulness the rafters [*hamekare*] will sink in [*yimakh*],** and through idleness of the hands the house leaks" (Ecclesiastes 10:18). **Due to slothfulness that was** present **amongst the Jewish people, that they did not occupy** themselves **with Torah, the enemy of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** a euphemism for God Himself, has **sunk. And sunk [*makh*] means nothing other** than **poor, as it is stated: "But if he is too poor [*makh*] for your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:8). **And "rafters [*mekare*]"** means **nothing other** than a reference to **the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "Who lays the beams [*hamekare*] of Your upper chambers in the water"** (Psalms 104:3).

Rav Katina brings the *sugya* full circle: *ein geshamim ne'etzarin ella bishvil bitul Torah* — rains are withheld because of the neglect of *Torah* study. This is a fitting conclusion to a *daf* that began by comparing rain to the giving of *Torah*. Having identified rain with *Torah*, *Torah* with life, and the relationship between Israel and God as mediated through both, it follows that the abandonment of *Torah* disrupts the very channel through which that relationship flows.

The verse: *ba-atzaltayim yimach ha-mikreh* — through laziness the *mikreh* will become weak. *Bishvil atzlus she-haya be-Yisrael shelo asuku ba-Torah* — because of the laziness of Israel in not engaging with *Torah* — *na'asah sono'o shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu*, it is as if God's enemy — which is a circumlocution meaning God Himself — became weak, *mach*. The *gemara* derives that *mikreh* refers to God from *ha-mikreh ba-mayim aliyosav* — He who spreads His upper chambers with water. And so the circle closes: rain (*mayim*) and *Torah* (compared to *mayim*) and God — the failure of our *Torah* weakens, as it were, God's presence in the world, and the result is the absence of rain.

The connection here runs deeper than a simple correspondence between sin and punishment. Our learning of *Torah* is our way of receiving *devar Hashem* — the word that comes down from heaven as the rain comes down. *Yizal ka-matar likchi*: God's giving descends like rain. When we weaken our side of that relationship, we weaken the channel itself, and the rain — the manifest expression of God's closeness — is withheld.

### Torah as Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְעַתָּה לֹא רָאוּ אוֹר בָּהִיר הוּא בַּשְּׁחָקִים וְרוּחַ עָבְרָה וַתְּטַהֲרֵם״, וְאֵין ״אוֹר״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי נֵר מִצְוָה וְתוֹרָה אוֹר״ — ״בָּהִיר הוּא בַּשְּׁחָקִים״. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁרָקִיעַ נַעֲשֶׂה בְּהוֹרִין בְּהוֹרִין לְהוֹרִיד טַל וּמָטָר — ״רוּחַ עָבְרָה וַתְּטַהֲרֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that this idea is derived **from here: "And now that men do not see the light, it is bright in the skies, but the wind passes and cleanses them"** (Job 37:21). **And "light"** means **nothing other** than **Torah, as it is stated: "For a mitzva is a lamp and Torah is a light"** (Proverbs 6:23). According to this interpretation, the verse means that when "men do not see the light," i.e., when they are not occupied with Torah, **"it is bright in the skies,"** as there are no rainclouds. With regard to this verse, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Even when the sky is comprised of bright clouds** that serve **to bring down dew and rain,** no rain will fall, as **"the wind passes and cleanses them."**

Rav Yosef adds a final image, drawing from the verse *ve-atah lo ra'u or bahir hu ba-shchakim, ve-ruach avra va-tihaarem* — and they did not see the clear light in the heavens, and a wind passed through and cleared them. *Ve-ein or ella Torah* — the light is nothing other than *Torah* — as the verse says *ki ner mitzvah ve-Torah or*. *Bahir hu ba-shchakim* — it is clear in the heavens. The school of Rabbi Yishmael adds: even at the moment when the sky is becoming overcast, cloud upon cloud, and it appears rain is imminent — if Israel has not seen the light of *Torah* — *ruach avra va-tihaarem*, a wind passes through and sweeps the clouds away, and no rain falls.

The image is vivid and, in this context, theologically precise: *Torah* is the light in the heavens that causes the clouds to hold, the rain to descend. When that light is absent, the clouds cannot sustain themselves; the wind scatters them before a drop falls.
