# Chagigah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kelim Hanigmarim

### Three Stringencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלִים הַנִּגְמָרִים בְּטָהֳרָה כּוּ׳. דְּגַמְרִינְהוּ מַאן? אִילֵימָא דְּגַמְרִינְהוּ חָבֵר, לְמָה לְהוּ טְבִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **Vessels that were** fashioned and **completed in purity** require immersion for sacrificial food but not for *teruma*. The Gemara asks: **Who completed** the work of these vessels? **If we say that** it was **a *ḥaver*,** an individual who is meticulous about the *halakhot* of ritual purity, who **completed them, why** should **they** require **immersion,** given that he is meticulous about the *halakhot* of purity?

The daf opens at roughly the midpoint of 23a, with the *mishnah* introducing a third category of stringency distinguishing *kodesh* from *terumah*: *kelim hanigmarim betaharah* — vessels completed in a state of purity. Even such vessels, the *mishnah* rules, must be immersed before use for *kodesh* purposes, though this requirement does not apply to *terumah*.

### Supervision Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּגַמְרִינְהוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ — נִגְמָרִין בְּטׇהֳרָה קָרֵי לְהוּ? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לְעוֹלָם דְּגַמְרִינְהוּ חָבֵר, וּמִשּׁוּם צִינּוֹרָא דְּעַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must mean **that an *am ha'aretz* completed them.** But can one **call these** vessels **completed in purity?** The very touch of an *am ha'aretz* renders them impure. **Rabba bar Sheila said** that **Rav Mattana said** that **Shmuel said: Actually,** the mishna is dealing with vessels **that a *ḥaver* completed,** and the Sages declared them impure for sacrificial food **due to** a concern that the **spittle of an *am ha'aretz*** might have fallen on them, which would render them impure if he were a *zav*.

The *gemara* immediately presses the *mishnah* with a sharp question: who, exactly, supervised the completion of these vessels? If it was a *chaver* — someone scrupulous in matters of *tumah* and *taharah* — then why should immersion be required at all? The entire process was conducted under trustworthy oversight. But if the supervision was entrusted to an *am ha'aretz*, how can the *mishnah* even describe these vessels as having been completed *betaharah*? The scenario seems internally contradictory: genuine purity supervision precludes the need for immersion, while inadequate supervision precludes the claim of purity.

### Tzinora Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּנְפַל אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִיגְמְרֵיהּ — הָא לָאו מָנָא הוּא. אֶלָּא בָּתַר דְּגַמְרֵיהּ — מִיזְהָר זְהִיר בֵּהּ! לְעוֹלָם מִקַּמֵּיהּ דְּגַמְרֵיהּ, וְדִלְמָא בְּעִידָּנָא דְּגַמְרֵיהּ עֲדַיִין לַחָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **When did** this hypothetical spittle **fall** on the vessel? **If we say** it was **before he completed** the vessel, in **that** case **it is not** yet **a vessel,** and therefore cannot contract impurity at that stage at all. **Rather,** it must have sprayed onto the vessel **after he completed it.** But at that point, since he is a *ḥaver*, **he is careful about it.** He would ensure that no spittle would fall on it, so there is no danger that it might have become defiled. The Gemara responds: **Actually,** we must explain that the spittle fell **before he completed it,** when the *ḥaver* was not yet guarding it against impurity, **and** the Sages were concerned that **perhaps at the moment when he completed** the vessel the spittle **was still moist,** and thus still capable of defiling the finished vessel, for the continued presence of the spittle on the vessel might have escaped the attention of the *ḥaver* craftsman.

The *gemara* resolves this by citing a teaching of Rabba bar Shila in the name of Rav Matna in the name of Shmuel: in truth, a *chaver* did supervise the completion of the vessels. The reason immersion is nevertheless required is *mishum tzinora de'am ha'aretz* — out of concern for the fluids (*tzinora*) of an *am ha'aretz* that may have contacted the vessel. The term *tzinora* — literally something like a faucet or conduit — refers in rabbinic idiom to the bodily fluids of a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah*, which carry the status of *av hatumah*, a primary source of *tumah*. Although such fluids are ordinarily discussed in connection with people, the concern here is that such fluid from a person of uncertain *taharah* standards might have landed on the vessel during the production process.

### When Contamination?

This answer, however, raises a further difficulty. When exactly would this contaminating contact have occurred? If the *tzinora* fell on the vessel before it was complete — *mikmi degamrei* — then it cannot have imparted *tumah*, because the vessel was not yet a vessel; an incomplete vessel cannot contract *tumah*. But if the contact happened after completion — *batar degamrei* — that is precisely the moment when the *chaver*'s supervision intensifies. The *chaver* may be less vigilant while the vessel is still being shaped, since it has no legal standing yet, but once the item becomes a finished vessel it is capable of receiving *tumah*, and that is when he becomes most careful.

### Moist Spit Theory

The *gemara* resolves this with a nuanced scenario: the *tzinora* landed on the vessel *before* it was complete — when the *chaver* was not yet maintaining strict watch — but perhaps at the moment of completion, that fluid was still moist (*adayin lecha ha'ei*), and it was in that instant of becoming a vessel that it contracted *tumah* from the still-damp residue. The vessel was completed into *tumah*, so to speak, because the contaminating fluid had never fully dried.

### Tosafot's Question

One might ask what an *am ha'aretz* was doing in the workshop of someone attempting to produce vessels in a state of *taharah* in the first place. *Tosafot* raises precisely this concern. Their proposed resolution is creative: perhaps the text does not mean a literal *am ha'aretz* in the ordinary sense, but rather a *chaver* who maintains standards one level lower than the craftsman in question — that is, someone who is careful about *terumah* but not about *kodesh*. As we have seen earlier in the tractate, one's garments are treated as a *midras* — a seat-type impurity source — relative to someone maintaining a higher standard of *taharah*. The spit of such a person, under these norms, would be treated as fully *tamei* and would generate the same concern.

### Social Reality

In practice, of course, craftsmen producing *kelim betaharah* did interact with *amei ha'aretz*. The broader social reality of the rabbinic period involved complex and layered relationships. Being an *am ha'aretz* reflected a specific ranking within the system of *tumah* and *taharah* observance, not necessarily a lower socioeconomic or broader religious standing. A wealthy patron or merchant might have been an *am ha'aretz* by these criteria while being perfectly respectable in every other sphere. Workers in a shop need not all have been *chaverim*, and the *chaver* craftsman would have managed this by intensifying his supervision as the vessel approached completion — though, as the *gemara* has now shown, a trace of earlier contact could still linger.

### Remote Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְבִילָה אִין, הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The mishna states that the vessel requires immersion, implying: **Immersion, yes,** but **the setting of the sun** after immersion, **not,** i.e., it is considered pure immediately after immersion, even though generally the purification engendered by immersion does not take effect until sunset.

The *mishnah*'s requirement, notably, is only immersion — not immersion followed by waiting for nightfall (*ha'erev shemesh*). The *gemara* infers from this that the requirement does not include *ha'erev shemesh*. This makes sense given that the entire concern rests on a *chashash rachok* — a remote, attenuated worry — built on a rabbinic stringency: treating the fluid of an *am ha'aretz* as though it were the *tzinora* of a *zav*. Since the concern is so indirect, only the lighter imposition of immersion is required, and that only for *kodesh*, not for *terumah*.

## Parah Tube Case

### Eliezer vs Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דִּתְנַן: שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת שֶׁחֲתָכָהּ לְחַטָּאת, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יִטְבּוֹל מִיָּד. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יְטַמֵּא וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִטְבּוֹל. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: דְּחַתְכַהּ מַאן? אִילֵימָא דְּחַתְכַהּ חָבֵר — לְמָה לִי טְבִילָה? וְאֶלָּא דְּחַתְכַהּ עַם הָאָרֶץ, בְּהָא לֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ יְטַמֵּא וְיִטְבּוֹל? הָא (טָמֵא וְקָאֵי)!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As we learned** in a mishna (*Para* 5:4): Regarding **a tube** of reed, **which one cut for** storing ashes of **purification, Rabbi Eliezer says: He should immerse** it **immediately; Rabbi Yehoshua says: He should** first **render** it **impure** by touching it to an actual source of impurity **and** only **after that immerse** it. **And we discussed** the mishna's case, asking: **Who cut** this tube? **If we say a *ḥaver* cut it, why do I** need **immersion?** The *ḥaver* prepared it with the strictest adherence to the *halakhot* of purity. **Rather,** it must be that an *am ha'aretz* **cut it.** But **in that case, would Rabbi Yehoshua say** that **he should** first **render** it **impure and** only then **immerse** it? **Isn't it already impure,** due to the handling of the *am ha'aretz*?

The *gemara* now notes that the *mishnah* — which requires immersion but not waiting for nightfall — does not follow the position of Rabbi Eliezer, and introduces the relevant dispute. The case is a tube (*shefoferet*) that has been cut down to serve as a container for the water of the *parah adumah*. One might imagine a piece of bamboo or similar material fashioned into a small vessel for distributing or applying the purifying water mixed with the ashes. Rabbi Eliezer says: immerse it immediately (*yitbol miyad*). Rabbi Yehoshua says: first get it *tamei*, and only then immerse it (*yitma ve'achar kach yitbol*).

### Sadducees Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מְטַמְּאִין הָיוּ אֶת הַכֹּהֵן הַשּׂוֹרֵף אֶת הַפָּרָה, לְהוֹצִיא מִלִּבָּן שֶׁל צַדּוּקִין. שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: בִּמְעוֹרְבֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ הָיְתָה נַעֲשֵׂית.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Para* 3:7): **They would render impure the priest who burns the** red **heifer, in order to counter the opinion of** the **Sadducees, who used to say:** The ritual of the red heifer **was** to be **performed by those who had experienced sunset** after their immersion. The Sadducee opinion was that all those involved in the preparation of the red heifer must be completely pure, having undergone immersion as well as having waited until after sunset, when the purification takes full effect. The Sages, however, maintain that the red heifer may be prepared by people immediately after immersion, without waiting for sunset. In order to clearly demonstrate their rejection of the Sadducee opinion they would deliberately defile the people involved with preparing the red heifer and then immerse them. It is for this reason as well that Rabbi Yehoshua ruled that the tube must be rendered impure before it is immersed.

Why would Rabbi Yehoshua prescribe intentionally rendering *tamei* a vessel about to be used for the *parah adumah* service? The answer lies in a well-known historical controversy with the *Tzaddukim* (Sadducees) regarding the conditions under which the *parah adumah* service could be performed. The Sadducees maintained that the *kohen* who burned the *parah* had to be in a state of complete *taharah*, meaning he must have immersed *and* waited for nightfall. The Sages deliberately controverted this position by getting the *kohen* performing the service *tamei*, having him immerse in a *mikvah*, and then performing the *avodah* before nightfall — rendering him a *tevul yom*, someone who has immersed but for whom the day has not yet concluded. This is explicitly recorded in a *mishnah*: *metam'im hayu et hakohen hashoref et haparah* — they would render *tamei* the *kohen* who was to burn the *parah*, in order to refute the position of the Sadducees who said it could only be performed *beme'orev shemesh* — after nightfall.

Rabbi Yehoshua is extending this same logic to the tube. It is not sufficient simply to immerse the tube in accordance with the general *ma'alah* of the *mishnah* and then use it — that would look like ordinary procedure, not a deliberate act of leniency undermining the Sadducean position. Instead, to make a visible halakhic statement, one must first render the vessel genuinely *tamei*, then immerse it, and use it before nightfall as a *tevul yom* vessel.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּעָלְמָא בָּעֵינַן הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא הֶיכֵּירָא לְצַדּוּקִין. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּעָלְמָא לָא בָּעֵינַן הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ — מַאי הֶיכֵּירָא לְצַדּוּקִין אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rabbi Eliezer, granted, if you say** that **generally, the setting of the sun is required** after a vessel is completed, **it is** found **that there is** some act of **recognition** in opposition **to** the **Sadducees,** as according to him, the tube used for the red heifer ashes, which should normally have required the setting of the sun, is used without waiting for sunset. **But if you say** that **generally the setting of the sun is not required** to remove the impurity caused by the touch of an *am ha'aretz*, and immersion alone is sufficient, **what** act of **recognition** in opposition **to** the **Sadducees is there** here? It must be, therefore, that Rabbi Eliezer requires the setting of the sun for purification from the impurity imparted by an *am ha'aretz*. Therefore, the mishna, which implies that immersion alone is sufficient, does not accord with Rabbi Eliezer's opinion.

This explanation works well for Rabbi Yehoshua, but the *gemara* now presses the position of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer says simply to immerse the vessel — but immersing a vessel completed *betaharah* before *kodesh* use is the standard requirement of our *mishnah*, which stipulates only immersion without waiting for nightfall anyway. If using the vessel immediately after immersion is just the normal procedure, what precisely is Rabbi Eliezer doing to undermine the Sadducean position? Where is the leniency that makes the *kohen's* or the vessel's status analogous to a *tevul yom*?

The *gemara* reconstructs the internal logic: if in the ordinary case this *ma'alah* required not merely immersion but immersion *and* waiting for nightfall, then Rabbi Eliezer's insistence on immediate use after immersion would itself constitute the leniency that counters the Sadducees. But if the *ma'alah* never required waiting for nightfall, then there is no visible leniency in Rabbi Eliezer's position at all.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: עֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּטְמֵא שֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** Indeed, Rabbi Eliezer does not require the setting of the sun for vessels that have been completed in purity, but there is nevertheless a demonstrative sign that the opinion of the Sadducees is rejected, as **they treated** the tube with severity **as** though it had been **rendered impure** by contact with a dead **creeping animal.** Therefore, in not requiring the setting of the sun they made it noticeable that they were opposed to the Sadducees.

Rava resolves this — the relevant discussion appears at 23b — by explaining that for vessels designated for the *chatas* (*parah adumah*) service, the Sages treated them with a heightened *stringency*: *asauha ke'tamei sheretz* — they made such a vessel as though it had touched a *sheretz* (a creeping creature that is a primary source of *tumah*), rendering it a *rishon le'tumah*. This is a significantly more severe status than the vague concern of the *ma'alah* as normally understood for *kodesh* vessels. Because these vessels are treated as *rishon*, someone who handles such a vessel before its immersion becomes *tamei* — and that is a genuine stringency. Permitting immediate use after immersion despite this elevated *rishon* status is therefore a real and visible leniency, sufficient to make the Sadducean point.

### Sheretz Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לֹא תְּטַמֵּא אָדָם, אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: חוֹתְכָהּ וּמַטְבִּילָהּ — טָעוּן טְבִילָה! וְאֶלָּא: עֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּטְמֵא מֵת. אִי הָכִי תִּיבְעֵי הַזָּאַת שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **However, if** that is **so,** that the tube is treated as if defiled by a creeping animal, **it should not render a person impure,** as something defiled by a creeping animal is impure to the first degree of ritual impurity, which cannot impart ritual impurity to people. **Why,** then, **is it taught** in a *baraita*: The one who **cuts and immerses** the tube for the red heifer ashes **requires immersion** himself, which shows that the tube does defile people? **But rather,** say that **they treated it as** something **made impure by** contact with **a corpse,** for such objects do defile people as well. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** the tube **should** also **require the sprinkling** of the water of purification on the **third and seventh** days of its purification, like all things defiled by a corpse.

The *gemara* immediately objects: if these vessels are treated like something that touched a *sheretz* — i.e., as a *rishon* — then they should require the third-day and seventh-day sprinklings of *parah adumah* ashes (*haza'ah shlishi ve'shvi'i*) before they can be used. The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: the one who cuts the tube and the one who immerses it each require immersion (*te'un tevilah*), but the *baraita* specifies that only immersion is needed — not the sprinkling on the third and seventh days. This rules out the *sheretz* model, which would not require sprinkling anyway since that relates to *tumah* from a corpse rather than a *sheretz*, but it also shows that making the person *tamei* from handling it is consistent with the vessel being treated as an *av*, not merely a *rishon*.

### Day Seven Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: חוֹתְכָהּ וּמַטְבִּילָהּ — טָעוּן טְבִילָה. טְבִילָה אִין, הַזָּאַת שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי לָא! אֶלָּא: עֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּטְמֵא מֵת בִּשְׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **Why,** then, **is it taught** in a *baraita*: The one who **cuts and immerses it requires immersion,** which indicates that **immersion** for the tube, **yes,** this is required, but **sprinkling of the third and the seventh** day, **no. Rather,** you must say that **they treated it as** something **made impure by** contact with **a corpse** that is already **in its seventh** day, after its sprinklings, when it is still impure and imparts impurity to those who touch it, but requires only immersion and no further sprinkling.

The *gemara* therefore revises the resolution: *asauha ke'tamei meis bishvi'i shelo* — they made this tube as though it were a vessel that had contracted *tumah* from a corpse, had undergone the requisite sprinklings on day three and day seven, and was now on the seventh day requiring only immersion to complete its purification. This is a very particular category: an *av hatumah* that can be purified through immersion alone. Ordinarily, primary sources of *tumah* — a corpse, an animal carcass, a *zav* or *zavah* — cannot simply be immersed in a *mikvah* and declared *tahor*. But a vessel that had genuinely touched a corpse, been sprinkled with the ashes on day three and day seven, and now needs only immersion, is exactly such an *av hatumah* that immersion suffices for. This hybrid status — stringent enough that anyone who handles the vessel before immersion becomes *tamei* (treating it as an *av*), yet lenient enough that immediate use after immersion is permitted — is what allows the Sages to make their point against the Sadducees.

The practical question raised is how the Sadducees would be moved by a stringency they do not accept as real. The answer may be experiential rather than theoretical: in practice, on the ground, every time such vessels are produced, those who handle them before immersion go to the *mikvah*. The weight of *tumah* being assigned to these vessels is observable, whatever the theoretical basis.

### New Category?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא חִידְּשׁוּ דָּבָר בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **never made any innovations** in the *halakhot* of impurity **with regard to the** red **heifer** procedure. In other words, although the Sages added stringencies to the red heifer procedure, they never created new *halakhot* for it that do not exist elsewhere in other areas of *halakha*. We have said that the tube is treated as if it had had contact with a corpse even though it did not; this is an innovation that is not found anywhere else.

At this point, the *gemara* raises a different objection. A *baraita* is cited: *me'olam lo chiser davar baparah* — they never invented anything entirely new for the *parah* service; they worked within existing halakhic categories. The concern here is that by declaring an ordinary tube cut yesterday to be equivalent to a seventh-day *tamei meis* vessel — a status the tube never actually acquired — the Sages may be creating a category that simply does not exist in halakhah.

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ קוּרְדּוֹם מְטַמֵּא מוֹשָׁב. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְהַיּוֹשֵׁב עַל הַכְּלִי״. יָכוֹל כָּפָה סְאָה וְיָשַׁב עָלֶיהָ, תַּרְקַב וְיָשַׁב עָלֶיהָ — יְהֵא טָמֵא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Abaye said:** When they said that the Sages did not make innovations, they were not referring to something of this nature, but meant that **they did not say** that **a spade** upon which a *zav* sits **can become impure as a seat.** They thereby preserved the basic *halakhot* of impurity, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It states with regard to a *zav*: **"And he who sits on any object** whereon the *zav* sits shall wash his clothes and bathe himself in water, and be unclean until the evening" (Leviticus 15:6). One **might** have thought that if a *zav* **turned over** a vessel used to measure **a *se'a* and sat on it,** or if he turned over a vessel used to measure **a *tarkav*,** i.e., a half-*se'a*, **and sat on it,** that the vessel **should be** rendered **impure** as a seat upon which a *zav* sat.

Abaye answers that this is not a completely new category. The category of a seventh-day *tamei meis* vessel that requires only immersion does exist as an established *halakhic* concept. What the Sages did was assign the tube to that existing category. The *baraita*'s statement that "nothing new was created for the *parah*" refers to something genuinely without precedent.

### Mishkav Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַיּוֹשֵׁב עַל הַכְּלִי אֲשֶׁר יֵשֵׁב עָלָיו ... יִטְמָא״ — מִי שֶׁמְיוּחָד לִישִׁיבָה, יָצָא זֶה, שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: עֲמוֹד וְנַעֲשֶׂה מְלַאכְתֵּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: Therefore, **the verse states: "And he who sits on any object whereon** the *zav* **sits…and be unclean** until the evening" (Leviticus 15:6). The wording of the verse indicates that it is speaking of an object **that is designated for sitting,** i.e., upon which people generally sit, **excluding such** a vessel for **which we would say to** someone sitting on it: **Stand up, so we can do our work.** A spade and a measuring bowl, then, are not subject to the impurity of the seat of a *zav*, even if a *zav* sat on them. Rather, they are considered to be on the lower level of impurity transmitted through simple contact with a *zav*. The same *halakha* was applied to the vessels used in preparation of the red heifer; the Sages did not add stringency and decree that a vessel not generally used for sitting should be considered as the seat of a *zav*.

What would constitute creating a truly unprecedented category? Abaye illustrates: *shelo amru kordom metamei moshav* — the Sages never said that a hammer, for example, acquires *tumat moshav* (the *tumah* of a seat or cushion imparted by a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* sitting on something). An object that sits on something becomes an *av hatumah* — *mishkav* or *moshav* — if and only if it is designated for sitting. One who sits on a vessel a *zav* sat on contracts *tumah* only if that vessel is *meyuchad liyeshivah*, designated for sitting. A *baraita* makes this explicit: even if someone upturned a bushel-measure and sat on it, the vessel would not acquire *tumat moshav*, because one would tell him to get off — *emod vena'aseh melachtenu* — "stand up, we need to use this." The tube for *parah* water cannot become a *mishkav* vessel because it was never designed to be sat upon. Declaring it a seventh-day *tamei meis* vessel, by contrast, fits an existing category even if the tube never actually underwent that process — because at least the legal category of such a vessel is coherent and established.

### Parah Conclusion

The upshot for the *parah* case is a deliberate halakhic construction: vessels prepared for the *parah adumah* service are treated with the stringency of an *av hatumah* (so that anyone handling them before immersion is rendered *tamei*), but with the leniency that immersion alone suffices and they may be used immediately afterward without waiting for nightfall. It is this combination — elevated pre-immersion severity combined with immediate post-immersion permission — that visibly counters the Sadducean insistence that the service could only be performed by someone who had immersed *and* waited for nightfall.

## Vessel Unifies

### Unification Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכְּלִי מְצָרֵף מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לַתְּרוּמָה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כַּף אַחַת עֲשָׂרָה זָהָב מְלֵאָה קְטֹרֶת״, הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאוֹ לְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבַּכַּף — אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **A vessel combines** all the food that is in it with regard **to sacrificial** food **but not** with regard **to *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? What is the source for this stringency? **Rabbi Ḥanin said: The verse states** with regard to the sacrificial donations of the tribal princes: **"One golden pan of ten shekels, full of incense"** (Numbers 7:14), which teaches us that **the verse treats everything inside the pan** as **one** unit, even if the items are not attached to each other.

The *mishnah* now turns to a further stringency distinguishing *kodesh* from *terumah*: *hakli mitzaref mah shebetokho le'kodesh aval lo leterumah* — a vessel unifies everything inside it for *kodesh* purposes, but not for *terumah* purposes. The practical implication is significant. If a bowl contains many apples designated for *kodesh* and something *tamei* touches one of them, are the other apples also rendered *tamei*? For *terumah*, each apple would be evaluated separately, the *tumah* spreading only by contact from one to the next. For *kodesh*, everything in the vessel is treated as a single unified mass (*mitzaref*), so that contaminating any part contaminates the whole equally.

### Incense Verse

This would appear to ground the principle in a *de'oraita* — a biblical source.

### Rabbinic vs Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הַסֹּלֶת, וְהַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהַגֶּחָלִים, שֶׁאִם נָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם בְּמִקְצָתוֹ — פָּסַל אֶת כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raised an objection** against this based on the mishna that teaches (*Eduyyot* 8:1): **Rabbi Akiva added** to the list of items that are considered combined when in the same vessel **fine flour, incense, frankincense, and coals,** saying **that if one who immersed himself that day** but has not waited until sunset **touched** a **part** of the contents of a vessel containing these substances, **all of** the vessel's contents **are disqualified,** as the vessel combines them.

Rav Kahana immediately raises a challenge. He cites a *mishnah* in Eduyot: *hosif Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva added several further items to the list of things where touching part renders the whole invalid. Rabbi Akiva's list includes the *solet* (flour) of a *minchah*, *ketoret* (incense), *levonah* (frankincense), and *gechalim* (coals used for the incense service). In each case, if a *tevul yom* — someone who has immersed but for whom nightfall has not yet arrived, possessing the status of a *sheni* — touches part, the whole becomes *pasul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא. מִמַּאי, מִדְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: הֵעִיד רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן בְּתֵירָא עַל אֵפֶר חַטָּאת שֶׁנָּגַע הַטָּמֵא בְּמִקְצָתוֹ — שֶׁטִּימֵּא אֶת כּוּלּוֹ, וְקָתָנֵי: הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Isn't this** *halakha* that these substances are considered combined **by rabbinic law? From where** do we know that this is so? **From** the fact **that it teaches** in **the first clause** of that mishna: **Rabbi Shimon ben Beteira testified with regard to ashes of purification** in a vessel **that** if **an impure** person or object **touched part of it, it renders all of it impure.** The purification ashes of the red heifer are neither food nor a sacrificial item, so Rabbi Ḥanin's verse does not apply to the ashes, and the vessel certainly does not combine the ashes together by Torah law, but by rabbinic law. **And it is taught** immediately following this: **Rabbi Akiva added,** which shows that Rabbi Akiva's *halakha*, like the previous *halakha*, deals with an additional level of impurity instituted by the Sages rather than a Torah law.

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Akiva's additions are clearly rabbinic (*de'rabbanan*). The proof is that the immediately preceding statement in the *mishnah*, to which Rabbi Akiva is appending his additions, concerns the ashes of the *parah adumah*. The ashes of the *parah adumah*, though they purify others from *tumah*, do not themselves carry *kedushah* — after the burning, the ashes are not *kodesh*. Yet the *mishnah* applies the unification principle even to the *parah* ashes. Since that first case cannot be grounded in the biblical verse about incense — which pertains to actual *kodesh* items — the entire list, including Rabbi Akiva's additions, must be rabbinic. But this creates tension: if the unification principle is rabbinic, how can we cite a biblical verse as its source?

### Parah Ashes

The point is made more precise through Rashi's framing: since the opening of the *mishnah* invokes *efer parah* — and those ashes carry no *kodesh* status — the principle of unification as applied there cannot be derived from the verse about the incense-ladle. The rabbinic nature of that first case establishes that Rabbi Akiva's additions are equally rabbinic. A *chiddush* announced as an addition to a rabbinic list is itself rabbinic. The verse, then, must be understood differently from a straightforward *de'oraita* derivation.

### Leftover Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִשְׁיָרֵי מִנְחָה. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, צָרִיךְ לִכְלִי — הַכְּלִי מְצָרְפוֹ, שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לִכְלִי — אֵין כְּלִי מְצָרְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said in the name of Bar Kappara: Rabbi Akiva's testimony is not needed to teach the basic *halakha* that a vessel combines its ingredients, which is Torah law; **it is necessary only for the remainders of the meal-offering,** the part of a meal-offering left over after a fistful of it and its frankincense have been sacrificed on the altar, which is eaten by a priest. In such a case the *halakha* of combining applies only by rabbinic law, for **by Torah law** only when an item **requires a vessel** in order for it to be sanctified does **the vessel combine it** with regard to impurity, even if its parts are not touching each other. But in the case of something **that does not require a vessel,** the **vessel does not combine it.** The remainder of a meal-offering no longer requires a vessel, since it is given to a priest after the fistful is sacrificed, so the flour in a vessel would not be considered combined according to Torah law.

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, clarifies where the rabbinic dimension enters: *lo nitzrecha ela le'shirei minchah* — the novelty introduced by the rabbis applies specifically to leftover portions. Once the *kometz* (the fistful of flour) of a *minchah* has been offered, what remains (*shirayim*) is eaten by the *kohanim*. At that stage, the leftover flour no longer halakhically *needs* to be in a *kli shareis* (a sacred service vessel); one could, in principle, transfer it to an ordinary plate. The question is whether the vessel still unifies the contents at this stage.

### Biblical Criteria

The biblical principle of unification, Reish Lakish explains, applies when the item *needs* the vessel to confer its halakhic status — *tzarich likli hakli mitzarfo*. Biblically, a *kli shareis* unifies its contents when the item must be placed in it and the vessel has an interior (*toch*). Both conditions — halakhic necessity and the presence of an interior space — are required for biblical unification. When either condition is absent, the biblical principle does not apply.

### Rabbinic Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן וּגְזַרוּ דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִכְלִי — כְּלִי מְצָרְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Sages came and decreed that even if** something **does not require a vessel,** such as the leftover flour of the meal-offering, the **vessel** nevertheless **combines it.**

The rabbis then expanded the principle in two directions. First: even when the item no longer needs to be in the vessel — as in the case of the *shirayim* that can be transferred to an ordinary vessel — the rabbis decreed that the vessel still unifies. Second: even when the vessel lacks an interior — such as a flat platform (*kartavla*) — the rabbis decreed that placing *kodesh* items upon it still unifies them. The *gemara* illustrates the second case with the scenario of incense or frankincense spread out on a flat surface: biblically, a vessel without a cavity (*ein lo toch*) does not unify; rabbinically, it does.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח סֹלֶת, קְטוֹרֶת וּלְבוֹנָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁצְּבָרָן עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְטְבֻלָא. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, יֵשׁ לוֹ תּוֹךְ — מְצָרֵף, אֵין לוֹ תּוֹךְ — אֵינוֹ מְצָרֵף, וַאֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן וְתַקִּינוּ דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵין לוֹ תּוֹךְ — מְצָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: It **works out well** in the case of **flour,** which can be said to refer to the flour left over from meal-offerings, but with regard to **incense and frankincense, what is there to say?** In these cases a vessel is certainly required, but if the *halakha* of combining applies to them from the Torah, why did Rabbi Akiva include them in his list? **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: For example, if he piled them up on a leather board [*kartavla*],** rather than in a containing vessel. **By Torah law** a vessel that **has an inside combines** its ingredients, but **one that** is flat and **does not have an inside does not combine. And the Sages came and decreed that even if it does not have an inside it** nevertheless **combines** what is placed on it.

The combined picture is thus: biblical unification requires a *kli shareis*, halakhic necessity for the vessel, and the presence of an interior. Any case that lacks one or more of these features still carries rabbinic unification for *kodesh* purposes.

### Mishnah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חָנִין אַדְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵעֵדוּתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נִשְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this opinion **of Rabbi Ḥanin's,** that impurity by combining is derived from the Torah, **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba. For Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** in reference to our mishna: **This mishna was taught based on Rabbi Akiva's testimony.** In other words, the mishna's teaching that a vessel combines its contents follows the statement of Rabbi Akiva, indicating that it is by rabbinic law, unlike Rabbi Ḥanin, who said that it is based on a source from the Torah.

The *gemara* concludes this section by noting a dispute about the nature of our *mishnah* itself. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, says: *me'eduso shel Rabbi Akiva nishneis mishnah zo* — our *mishnah* was formulated following the position of Rabbi Akiva. Since Rabbi Akiva's statements on this matter have been established as rabbinic (*de'rabbanan*), this position holds that the unification rule in our *mishnah* is *entirely* rabbinic — there is no biblical dimension at all. This directly contradicts the approach that distinguishes between cases where the principle applies biblically and cases where it is only rabbinic. The *gemara* thus leaves us with a genuine dispute: either the *halakhah* of *kli mitzaref* has both a biblical core and rabbinic extensions, or it is wholly rabbinic from the outset.

## Revi'i BeKodesh

### Contamination Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרְבִיעִי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ פָּסוּל. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִנַּיִן לָרְבִיעִי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל — וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים שֶׁמּוּתָּר בַּתְּרוּמָה — פָּסוּל בַּקֹּדֶשׁ, שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁפָּסוּל בַּתְּרוּמָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה רְבִיעִי לַקֹּדֶשׁ? וְלָמַדְנוּ שְׁלִישִׁי לַקֹּדֶשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וּרְבִיעִי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **The fourth degree** of impurity, **with regard to sacrificial** food, is **disqualified. It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei said: From where** is it derived with regard **to the fourth degree** of ritual impurity, that **with regard to sacrificial** food it **is disqualified? It is a logical derivation,** by *a fortiori*: **If one who is lacking atonement,** an impure person who is obligated to bring an offering to complete his purification process, **who is permitted to** eat ***teruma*,** is nevertheless **disqualified with regard to** the consumption of **sacrificial** food, as specified in the Torah, then concerning something that is impure to the **third** degree of ritual impurity, **which is disqualified** if it is ***teruma*, is it not right that it should engender a fourth** degree of ritual impurity when it touches **sacrificial** food? Therefore, **we have learned** that there is a **third** degree of impurity with regard **to sacrificial food from the Torah, and** that there is a **fourth** degree of impurity **from *a fortiori*** reasoning.

The final *mishnah* under discussion introduces what is arguably the most fundamental structural difference between *kodesh* and *terumah*: *ha'revi'i ba'kodesh pasul* — a fourth-degree derivation of impurity invalidates *kodesh*. For *terumah*, the chain of *tumah* stops at the third degree (*shlishi*); for *kodesh*, it extends one step further to the fourth degree (*revi'i*).

### Derivation Chart

The general framework is well known. A primary source of *tumah* — *av hatumah* — such as the carcass of an animal, a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah*, or someone who has touched a corpse, contacts a person or vessel, rendering them a *rishon* (first-degree). Persons and vessels can become no worse than *rishon* in the chain of derivation. From *rishon* onward, transmission continues only through food.

A *rishon* food renders other *chullin* (non-sacred, non-priestly food) into *sheni* (second-degree), and the chain stops there for ordinary food. *Terumah* can become *shlishi* (third-degree). *Kodesh* can become *revi'i* (fourth-degree). Beyond *revi'i*, the chain terminates even for *kodesh*. One can note additionally that above the *av hatumah* stands the *avi avot hatumah* — most notably the human corpse — which is the ultimate source. A person who touches a corpse becomes an *av*, not merely a *rishon*.

### Category Distinctions

The practical boundaries are thus: *chullin* can become *sheni*, *terumah* can become *shlishi*, and only *kodesh* can become *revi'i*. The key question the *gemara* now addresses is whether the rule that *kodesh* can reach *revi'i* is biblical or rabbinic.

### Meat Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלִישִׁי לַקֹּדֶשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״, מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דִּנְגַע בְּשֵׁנִי, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל״. רְבִיעִי מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** The above *baraita* taught that there is a **third** degree of impurity **for sacrificial** food **from the Torah.** The Gemara asks: **From where** is this derived? **As it is written: "And the meat that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 7:19). **Is it not** so that **we are not dealing** in that verse with meat that touches any "impure thing" at all, even **if it touched** something that is of the **second** degree of ritual impurity, which is also called an "impure thing"? **And** yet **the Merciful One states** with regard to that meat, which having touched a second-degree impurity is now impure to the third degree: **"It shall not be eaten,"** meaning that it has been rendered unfit due to impurity. And as for the *baraita's* statement that the **fourth** level of impurity is derived **by an *a fortiori* inference –** it is **as we said** just above, the *a fortiori* inference put forth by Rabbi Yosei.

A *baraita* records Rabbi Yosi's derivation. He proceeds by *kal vachomer* (an argument from the lighter case to the more stringent), but first he establishes a biblical foundation for the *shlishi* in *kodesh*. The verse states: *vehabasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo ye'achel* — "meat [of a sacrifice] that touches anything *tamei* shall not be eaten" (Vayikra 7:19). "Anything *tamei*" encompasses any halakhic *tumah* established by the Torah — and among those, the Torah itself speaks of food becoming *sheni*. If sacrificial meat touches a *sheni*, it becomes *shlishi* and is forbidden. This is the biblical source establishing that *kodesh* can reach *shlishi*.

### Mechusar Kippurim

Rabbi Yosi then introduces the figure of the *mechusar kippurim* — one who has completed all the required stages of purification from an extended *tumah* (such as *zav*, *zavah*, *metzora*, or *yoledet*), has immersed after nightfall, but has not yet brought the purification *korban* that formally completes the process. Such a person is permitted to eat *terumah* — a critical datum — but is forbidden to eat *kodashim*. This status is what is meant by *mechusar kippurim*: still lacking the closing *korban*, lacking atonement (*kippurim*), and thus not yet fully admitted to the realm of *kodesh*.

To place this figure within the framework of *tumah* derivations: a *tevul yom* — one who has immersed but for whom the day has not ended — is effectively treated as a *sheni*, which is why he cannot eat *terumah* (a *sheni* would render *terumah* into *shlishi*, making it *pasul*). The *mechusar kippurim*, by contrast, *can* eat *terumah*. This means he cannot be as impure as a *sheni*, or he would disqualify *terumah* by handling it. He must therefore be, in effect, at the level of *shlishi* — still carrying residual impurity that disqualifies *kodashim*, but not enough to affect *terumah*. The terminology of *sheni* and *shlishi* is not formally applied to the *mechusar kippurim* — the conceptual basis is different, being residual rather than derived — but the practical *halakhic* equivalence holds.

### Deriving Revi'i

From this established point — that the *mechusar kippurim* is functionally a *shlishi* and yet is barred from eating *kodashim* — Rabbi Yosi derives the existence of *revi'i bekodesh*.

### Kal Vachomer

The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: the *mechusar kippurim*, who is effectively a *shlishi* and yet is *muttar beterumah* (permitted to eat *terumah*), is nonetheless *pasul bekodesh* (disqualified from *kodesh*). If such a person — only a *shlishi* status — disqualifies *kodesh*, then actual *shlishi* food that is *pasul biterumah* (genuinely *terumah tmeiah* at the *shlishi* level, which renders *terumah* invalid) must certainly generate an additional degree of *tumah* in *kodesh* — creating a *revi'i*. The *shlishi* of *terumah* that cannot eat *terumah* should certainly have enough force to render *kodesh* one step further *tamei*, producing the *revi'i*.

The *gemara* concludes: *lamadnu shlishi lakodesh min haTorah ve'revi'i mikal vachomer* — we have learned that *kodesh* can reach *shlishi* from the biblical verse about *basar*, and we have derived *revi'i* by *kal vachomer* from the case of *mechusar kippurim*. Whether the *revi'i* is therefore considered *de'oraita* by virtue of the *kal vachomer* or remains in some sense rabbinic is a question the *gemara* leaves available for further discussion, but the derivation itself is presented as a formal logical inference from Torah principles.
