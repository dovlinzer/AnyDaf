# Bava Metzia 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chalipin & Coinage

### Chalipin Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב וְלֵוִי, חַד אָמַר: מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין – מִשּׁוּם דְּדַעְתֵּיהּ אַצּוּרְתָּא, וְצוּרְתָּא עֲבִידָא דְּבָטְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there is a dispute between **Rav and Levi. One said: Money can be** an item used to effect **exchange. And one said: Money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange,** as that form of transaction is effective only with regard to items such as produce and vessels. **Rav Pappa said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of the one who says** that **money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange?** It is **because** the **mind of** the one acquiring the coin is **on the form** minted on the coin, not the value of the metal, **and** the value due to **the form is apt to be canceled** by the authorities. Therefore, in the eyes of the party acquiring it, the coin itself has no real value and therefore cannot be an item used to effect exchange.

The sugya on Bava Metzia 46b opens with the institution of *chalipin*, the mechanism of symbolic exchange by which one party transfers ownership to another not through the direct payment of money but through a symbolic act — most classically, the handing over of a *sudar*, a handkerchief or similar article of modest value. The *sudar* itself is not the price being paid; it is merely the instrument that effects the legal transfer. With that framework in place, the Gemara turns to the specific question of coinage and its relationship to *chalipin*.

### Rav Papa's Thesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין – מֶיעְבָּד הוּא דְּלָא עָבֵיד חֲלִיפִין, אַקְנוֹיֵי מִיקְּנוּ בַּחֲלִיפִין, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַפֵּירֵא לְרַב נַחְמָן. פֵּירֵא לְרַב נַחְמָן, לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִינְהוּ לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין – אַקְנוֹיֵי מִקְּנוּ בַּחֲלִיפִין, טִבְעָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: Even according to the one who says: Money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange,** this means only that money **does not effect** a transaction of **exchange;** but he concedes that money **is acquired by** means of a transaction of **exchange.** If one party pulls a vessel into his possession, the other party acquires silver coins in exchange, **just as it is with regard to produce, according to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman.** Is it **not** the case that **even though** according to the opinion of Rav Naḥman **produce itself does not effect** a transaction of **exchange,** nevertheless produce **is acquired by** means of a transaction of **exchange? Currency, too,** is **no different.**

Rav Papa opens with a carefully formulated position: even according to the view that a *matbea* — a coin — cannot be used to *enact* a *chalipin* (that is, cannot serve as the instrument of transfer), there remains the separate question of whether money can be *acquired* through *chalipin*. In Rav Papa's formulation: *afilu man de-amar ein matbea na'aseh chalipin, mi'avad hu d'lo avad, chalipin aknu'ei miknu b'chalipin* — even he who holds that a coin cannot function as the operative object of *chalipin* must concede that coins can nonetheless be the *subject* of a *chalipin* transfer, that is, the thing being conveyed from one party to another by means of a *sudar*.

### Rav Nachman Position

The Gemara draws an analogy to Rav Nachman's position on commodities. Rav Nachman holds that any produce or commodity — *peirot* — can be *transferred through chalipin* even though, in his view, *peirot* cannot serve as the operative instrument of the *chalipin* itself. The Gemara reasons that if Rav Nachman allows *peirot* to be the object of transfer through *chalipin* — *af al gav d'inhu lo avdi chalipin, aknu'ei miknu b'chalipin* — then coinage should be no different: *tav'a nami lo shna*. Money, like fruit, is a form of commodity, and what is true of fruit in this regard ought to be true of coins as well.

## Maaser Sheni Challenge

### Silo Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה עוֹמֵד בַּגּוֹרֶן וְאֵין בְּיָדוֹ מָעוֹת, אוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: הֲרֵי פֵּירוֹת הַלָּלוּ נְתוּנִים לְךָ בְּמַתָּנָה, וְחוֹזֵר וְאוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הֵן מְחוּלָּלִין עַל מְעוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בַּבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Pappa from a *baraita*: One **was standing on the threshing floor and had no money in his hand,** and wanted to desacralize his second-tithe produce without paying an additional one-fifth. The *halakha* is that one who desacralizes his own produce must add one-fifth to its value. This man wants to engage in artifice as if he sold the produce to another, thereby enabling him to desacralize it without adding one-fifth. To that end, **he says to another: This produce is hereby given to you as a gift, and then he says:** This second-tithe produce that now belongs to you **is hereby desacralized on coins that I have at home.**

This case operates within the laws of *ma'aser sheni*, the second tithe. Produce designated as *ma'aser sheni* must be consumed in Jerusalem; if it cannot be transported in its current form, it may be "redeemed" — that is, its sanctity may be transferred onto money, which is then brought to Jerusalem and used to purchase food there. However, the Torah imposes a penalty on one who redeems his *own* produce: he must add a *chomesh*, a surcharge of one-quarter of the produce's value (which, when calculated against the total sum paid, amounts to one-fifth — hence the name, which literally means "fifth"). The penalty reflects a concern about cavalier treatment of the sanctity of *ma'aser sheni*.

To avoid this surcharge, a person might transfer the fruit to a friend as a gift — the fruit now legally belonging to the friend — and then have that friend redeem it. Since the redemption is being performed on someone else's produce, the owner is not subject to the *chomesh* penalty. In the Baraita's case, Reuven stands in his granary with his *ma'aser sheni* produce but has no money at hand. He gives the fruit to Shimon as a gift, and then declares that the fruit is redeemed on the money he has at home. This accomplishes the desired result: the fruit technically belongs to Shimon at the moment of redemption, so Reuven does not incur the surcharge. The Gemara explains that the more ideal form of this workaround would have been for Reuven to give Shimon the *money* rather than the fruit — Shimon would then use that money to redeem the fruit, making the transaction resemble a genuine marketplace sale as closely as possible.

### Mishna Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאֵין בְּיָדוֹ מָעוֹת, הָא אִם יֵשׁ בְּיָדוֹ מָעוֹת – לַיקְנֵי לְהוּ לְאִידַּךְ בִּמְשִׁיכָה וּפָרֵיק, דְּהָכִי עֲדִיף, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ נֻכְרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that it is necessary to employ this artifice is **that he does not have coins in his hand. But if he has coins in his hand let him transfer ownership** of the coins **to the other** person **by means of** the transaction of **pulling, and** then that other person will **desacralize** the second-tithe produce. The reason for this is **that this** procedure **is preferable, as** the one who desacralizes the produce **is a stranger,** not the owner of the produce. Therefore, it appears less like artifice designed to circumvent paying the additional one-fifth.

The Gemara presses the inference: *ta'ama de-ein b'yado ma'ot* — the only reason the case proceeds by transferring the fruit first is because he lacks money at hand. Had he had money in hand, he would have given the money to Shimon and proceeded from there. This implies that the possibility of using *chalipin* to transfer the money that sits at home — thereby notionally placing that money in Shimon's possession so that Shimon could use it to redeem the fruit — was not considered a viable option. If money could be acquired through *chalipin*, Reuven could have simply used a *sudar* to symbolically transfer the money in his house to Shimon, and Shimon would then redeem the fruit with it. The Baraita's silence on this approach seems to indicate that money cannot be acquired through *chalipin*.

### Gemara Defenses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מַטְבֵּעַ נִקְנֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין: נִיקְּנוֹ לֵיהּ מָעוֹת לְהַיְאךְ אַגַּב סוּדָר, וְלִפְרוֹק! דְּלֵית לֵיהּ סוּדָר. וְנַקְנִינְהוּ נִהֲלֵיהּ אַגַּב קַרְקַע! דְּלֵית לֵיהּ קַרְקַע. וְהָא עוֹמֵד בַּגּוֹרֶן קָתָנֵי! בְּגוֹרֶן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ. וְאִיכְּפַל תַּנָּא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן גַּבְרָא עַרְטִילַאי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ וְלָא כְּלוּם? אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נִקְנֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to state its objection to the opinion of Rav Pappa. **And if you say a coin is acquired by** means of a transaction of **exchange,** then even if the owner of produce has no money in his hand, **let** the owner **transfer ownership** of the coins, wherever they are, **to the other** person **by means of a cloth,** as he can perform a transaction of symbolic exchange with a cloth; **and** then **let** the other person **desacralize** the produce. The Gemara answers: The case in the *baraita* is one in **which he does not have a cloth.** The Gemara challenges: **And let** the owner **transfer ownership** of the coins **to the other** person **by means of land,** i.e., perform an act of transaction concerning the land and include the coins in the transaction. The Gemara answers: The case in the *baraita* is one in **which he does not have land. But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* that he is **standing on the threshing floor?** The Gemara answers: He is standing **on a threshing floor that is not his.** The Gemara asks: **And did the *tanna* go to all that trouble just to teach us** the case **of a naked man,** i.e., one **who has nothing at all?** It is unlikely that a *baraita* would be devoted to so remote a case. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that **money is not acquired by means of** a transaction of **exchange?** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the *halakha*.

The Gemara attempts to deflect this inference. Perhaps money could in principle be transferred through *chalipin*, and the reason the Baraita does not suggest it is simply that the man lacked a *sudar* — he had no article to use for the symbolic transfer. The Gemara then considers the possibility of a *kinyan agav*, the mode of acquisition by which movable property is transferred by means of a symbolic conveyance of land — perhaps he could have transferred the money in his house by conveying a corner of his home to Shimon. This too is deflected: perhaps he owned no land. But the Gemara points out that the Baraita describes him as standing in a granary, which would seem to be real property. This in turn is deflected: it was someone else's granary, not his own. The Gemara finally presses the point to its logical limit: *v'chi batna l'ashmi'inan gavra artilai d'leit lei v'lo klum*? Surely the Baraita is not describing a man standing there utterly destitute, without even a piece of clothing he could remove to use as a *sudar*? Even a shirt or a sock would suffice for *kinyan sudar*. It is therefore impossible to maintain that the failure to suggest *chalipin* was merely circumstantial.

### Conclusion

The Gemara concludes: *shma mina ein matbe'a nikneh b'chalipin, shma mina*. The Baraita is to be read as evidence that money cannot be acquired through *chalipin*. This directly contradicts Rav Papa's initial thesis.

## Rav Papa Reversal

### Rav Papa Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַב פָּפָּא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ. כִּי הָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ תְּרֵיסַר אַלְפֵי דִּינָרֵי בֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אַקְנִינְהוּ לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחָא אַגַּב אַסִּיפָּא דְּבֵיתֵיהּ. כִּי אֲתָא, נְפַק לְאַפֵּיהּ עַד תְּווֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **And even Rav Pappa retracted his** previous statement that coins are acquired by means of a transaction of exchange, **as** in **this** incident in **which Rav Pappa had twelve thousand dinars** that he lent to another in **Bei Ḥozai. He transferred ownership of** the dinars **to** his agent, **Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa, by means of** granting acquisition of **the threshold of his house** to him, to enable the agent to demand repayment of the loan on his own behalf. This obviated the need for him to consult Rav Pappa in the case of every contingency, which would complicate matters. It is apparent from the fact that the transaction was effected by means of granting acquisition of the threshold that Rav Pappa concedes that coins are not acquired by means of a transaction of exchange. **When** Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa **came** after repayment of the loan, Rav Pappa was so pleased that he **went out as far as Tevakh to** meet **him.**

The Gemara records that Rav Papa himself ultimately reversed his position. The vehicle for this reversal is a story: Rav Papa had twelve thousand *dinar* deposited at a place called *Bei Chozai*, some distance away. He wished to retrieve them via an agent, Rav Shmuel bar Acha, who would travel there and bring the funds back. The difficulty was that the custodian at *Bei Chozai* would not release the money to someone who was not legally its owner — if Rav Shmuel were merely a messenger rather than an owner, and the money were lost or stolen in transit, Rav Pappa could still hold the custodian liable for having released the funds to an unauthorized party. The solution was to make Rav Shmuel the legal owner of the money for the duration of the transfer. Rav Papa accordingly performed a *kinyan agav*, transferring the threshold of his home to Rav Shmuel along with — *agav*, "by means of" — the money at *Bei Chozai*. In this way the money was legally conveyed to Rav Shmuel, who then assumed full responsibility for it during transport. When Rav Shmuel arrived, Rav Papa went out to meet him at *itztoka*.

The significance of this story is in what Rav Papa chose *not* to do: he did not use a *kinyan sudar* to effect this transfer. *Kinyan sudar* is precisely the classic form of *chalipin*, involving the symbolic handover of a piece of cloth or similar article. Had Rav Papa believed that money could be transferred through *chalipin*, he would presumably have used the simpler and more readily available mechanism of *kinyan sudar* rather than going to the trouble of arranging a *kinyan agav* through real property. His deliberate choice of *kinyan agav* over *kinyan sudar* demonstrates that he no longer held that money could be transferred through *chalipin*.

### Amoraic Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר עוּלָּא: אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And likewise, Ulla says: Money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange. And likewise, Rav Asi says: Money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange. And likewise, Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange.**

With Rav Papa's reversal in view, the Gemara marshals additional Amoraic support. Ulla, Rabbi Assi, and Rabba bar Bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan all independently state: *ein matbea na'aseh chalipin* — a coin cannot be used to effect *chalipin*. These three parallel statements, all converging on the same ruling, are now introduced in the context of *chalipin* as an instrument — that is, money cannot serve as the operative object that enacts the symbolic transfer.

### Money-Changer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ חֲמָרָיו וּפוֹעֲלָיו תּוֹבְעִין אוֹתוֹ בַּשּׁוּק, וְאָמַר לַשּׁוּלְחָנִי: תֵּן לִי בְּדִינָר מָעוֹת וַאֲפַרְנְסֵם, וַאֲנִי אַעֲלֶה לְךָ יָפֶה דִּינָר וּטְרֵיסִית מִמָּעוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּבֵיתִי. אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ מָעוֹת – מוּתָּר, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין, הָוְיָא לֵיהּ הַלְוָאָה, וְאָסוּר! אִשְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba raised an objection to** the opinion of **Ulla** from a *baraita*: With regard to **one whose donkey drivers or laborers were demanding** payment of their wages **from him in the marketplace, and he said to the money changer: Give me coins** worth **a dinar and I will provide for them, and** later **I will give you** coins **worth a dinar and a *tereisit*,** a coin of lesser value, **from money that I have at home,** then **if he has money** at home at that time it is **permitted,** as it is not considered a loan and therefore the additional payment is not interest. **But if** he does **not** have money at home at that time, it is a loan and the additional payment is **forbidden** as interest. **And if it enters your mind** that **money cannot be** the item used to effect a transaction by means of **exchange,** then even in the case where the employer had money at home, since the money changer does not acquire the employer's money at home by means of exchange, **it is a loan and** the additional payment is **forbidden** as interest. Ulla **was silent.**

Rabbi Abba then challenges Ulla's position from the following case. A donkey-driver and his workers are pressing a man for payment in the marketplace. The man turns to the ever-present money changer and says: *ten li be-dinar ma'ot ve-afarneseim* — give me a *dinar*'s worth of coins so that I may pay my workers. He then adds: *va-ani a'aleh lecha yafeh dinar u-tarsais mi-ma'ot she-yesh li be-veiti* — and I will repay you a *dinar* and a *tarsais* (a small additional sum) from the money I have at home.

The ruling follows: if he in fact has the money at home, it is permitted; if he does not, it is forbidden.

The reason the case might be *asur* is immediately apparent: if the money changer gives the man a *dinar* now and the man promises to repay more later, this has the structure of a loan with a premium — which would constitute *ribbit*, the prohibition on charging interest.

### Ribbit Logic

The Gemara presses this logic. If *chalipin* cannot be effected through money, then what the money changer is doing when he hands over a *dinar* is extending a loan — and the extra amount the man promises to return would then be *ribbit*, forbidden in all circumstances. The permissibility of the transaction when money is available at home must therefore rest on the claim that the money at home is immediately transferred to the money changer through some mechanism, making this a trade — a simultaneous exchange of value — rather than a loan. And if the mechanism is *chalipin* through coinage (the man receiving the *dinar* triggers the transfer of his coins at home to the money changer), the case seems to imply that money *can* in fact function within a *chalipin* transaction. This challenges Ulla's blanket ruling.

### Unstamped Coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְמָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּפְרוּטָטוֹת שָׁנוּ, דְּלֵיכָּא עֲלַיְיהוּ טִבְעָא. וְאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי פֵּירָא הָווּ, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי נִקְּנוֹ בַּחֲלִיפִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: יָפֶה דִּינָר וּטְרֵיסִית, וְלָא קָתָנֵי: דִּינָר יָפֶה וּטְרֵיסִית.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba then **said** the following suggestion **to** Ulla: **Perhaps** the Sages **taught** this *halakha* **in** a case where both **these** coins that are in his house **and those** coins that he took from the money changer are ***perotetot*,** small *perutot* **that are unminted, and** the legal status of both **these** coins **and those** coins **is** that of **a commodity; and due to that** status **they are acquired by means of** a transaction of **exchange.** Ulla **said to him: Yes,** that is the case in the *baraita*, and the language of the *baraita* **is also precise, as it teaches:** I will give you coins **worth a dinar and a *tereisit*, and it does not teach:** I will give you **an unflawed dinar and a *tereisit*.** Evidently, the *baraita* is not referring to his giving an actual dinar coin but to other coins of lesser value that equal that value. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the case.

Rabbi Abba himself resolves the challenge. He proposes that both parties in the transaction were dealing not with actual stamped currency but with *prottotat* — pre-minted metal pieces that have not yet received a royal stamp or official designation as currency. Such pieces, though they may be spoken of loosely as having a *dinar*'s worth of value, are not legally coins; they are metal commodities, no different in legal status from produce or any other tradeable good. And as previously established, commodities — *peirot* — can be transferred through *chalipin*. Ulla accordingly accepts this answer. As supporting evidence, the Gemara notes that the text of the case says *yafeh dinar*, "a good dinar's worth," rather than *dinar yafeh*, "a good coin" — implying that what is being referenced is a value equivalent, not a stamped coin. This linguistic observation is taken to support the interpretation that the transaction involved commodity-metal rather than legal currency.

### Rav Ashi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בְּדָמִים וּבִפְרוּטָטוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לֵיהּ, נַעֲשֶׂה כְּאוֹמֵר: הַלְוֵינִי עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא בְּנִי אוֹ עַד שֶׁאֶמְצָא מַפְתֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** it can be explained that there is no transaction by means of exchange in this case. Rather, it is a purchase **with money and** the *tanna* is referring **to *perotetot*,** and nevertheless there is no violation of the prohibition against interest. **Since he has** money at home, **it is tantamount to saying: Lend me** money **until my son comes or until I find** the **key.** That is not a loan, and the *halakhot* of interest do not apply.

Rav Ashi offers an independent resolution. He maintains that the transaction actually did involve ordinary currency, but that the reason it is not *ribbit* has nothing to do with *chalipin*. Rather, the case falls under a recognized exception to the *ribbit* prohibition: *keivan deit lei na'aseh k'omer halvini ad sheyavo beni o ad she-emtza mafteach* — since the man genuinely has the money available at home, the situation is treated as though he said, "lend me until my son arrives" or "until I find my key." When the funds are readily accessible and the delay is purely logistical rather than reflecting genuine inability to pay, this is not considered a true loan bearing the moral character of *ribbit*. The case in the Baraita is therefore consistent with the position that money cannot be used for *chalipin*; the permissibility of the transaction arises from an entirely separate principle.

## Value Transfer Mishna

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כׇּל הַנַּעֲשֶׂה דָּמִים בְּאַחֵר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה – נִתְחַיֵּיב זֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a mishna (*Kiddushin* 28a): With regard to **all** items **used as monetary** value **for another** item, i.e., instead of a buyer paying money to the seller, they exchange items of value with each other, **once one** party in the transaction **acquires** the item he is receiving, **this** party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged for it.**

The Gemara introduces another source, a Mishna from *Kiddushin*: *kol hane'aseh damim be'acher keivan shezacha zeh nitchayev zeh b'chalipin* — whoever effects a transfer of value with another party, once that party acquires the value, the first party incurs the attendant liability through *chalipin*. The practical upshot is that a sale can be considered legally consummated — placing the risk of loss on the buyer — before the buyer has taken physical possession of the item, if some form of value transfer has already occurred.

### Initial Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַנַּעֲשֶׂה דָּמִים בְּאַחֵר, מַאי נִיהוּ – מַטְבֵּעַ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מַטְבֵּעַ נַעֲשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this mishna: With regard to **all** items **used as monetary** value **for another** item, **what does** the mishna mean in **this** phrase? It means **a coin, and learn from** the mishna that **a coin can be** an item used to effect **exchange.**

One might read the phrase *kol hane'aseh damim* — "whatever constitutes payment" — as a reference to coinage. If so, the Mishna would be saying that money can function within a *chalipin* framework to effect legal transfer, and this would constitute a proof that *ein matbea na'aseh chalipin* is incorrect. The Gemara entertains this reading before subjecting it to scrutiny.

### Rav Yehuda Reinterp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל הַנִּישּׁוֹם דָּמִים בְּאַחֵר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזָּכָה זֶה נִתְחַיֵּיב זֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: This** is what the mishna **is saying: With regard to** all items **that** can be **appraised** when used **as monetary** value **for another** item, i.e., their value can be appraised relative to the value of another item, excluding a coin, whose value is apparent, **once one** party in the transaction **acquires** the item he is receiving, **this** party **is obligated** with regard to the item being **exchanged for it.** The novelty of the mishna is that all items, not only vessels, can be used to perform the act of acquisition of exchange. Therefore, one should not infer that the *halakha* is the same with regard to coins.

Rav Yehuda rejects this reading. He argues that the Mishna should be understood as: *kol hanishom damim be'acher keivan shezacha zeh nitchayev zeh b'chalipin* — whoever *appraises* value with another, meaning, once a price is agreed upon and a symbolic act of *chalipin* (through a *sudar* or similar instrument) is performed, the legal transfer takes effect. The *damim* in the Mishna refers not to the actual physical transfer of coinage but to the valuation — the act of pricing — that precedes and occasions the *chalipin*. The money itself is never the operative instrument of transfer.

This interpretation is supported by the *seifa* of the Mishna, which asks *keitzad* — how so? — and answers: if one exchanged an ox for a cow or a donkey for an ox. The *seifa* uses examples of commodity-for-commodity exchange, which fits Rav Yehuda's reading that the Mishna is about non-monetary value transfer, not about coins functioning as *chalipin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to interpret the mishna in that manner, **from** the fact **that the latter clause** of that mishna **teaches: How so?** If **one exchanges an ox for a cow, or a donkey for an ox,** once this party acquires the animal that he is receiving, this party is obligated with regard to the item being exchanged for it. This clause apparently explains the previous clause, and employs the example of animals, not coins. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from it** that the reference in the mishna is to movable property, not to coins.

### Rav Nachman View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דְּאָמַר פֵּירֵי עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, אֶלָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר: כְּלִי – אִין, אֲבָל פֵּירֵי לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, מַאי ״כֵּיצַד״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: יֵשׁ דָּמִים שֶׁהֵן כַּחֲלִיפִין, כֵּיצַד? הֶחְלִיף דְּמֵי שׁוֹר בְּפָרָה אוֹ דְמֵי חֲמוֹר בְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet, who said: Produce effects** a transaction of **exchange. But according to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who said: A vessel, yes,** it effects a transaction of exchange, **but produce does not effect** a transaction of **exchange, what** is the meaning of the continuation of the mishna beginning with the question: **How so?** The Gemara answers that **this** is what the mishna **is saying: There is** a purchase with **money** where one acquires the purchase item without pulling it **that is like** a transaction of **exchange. How so?** It is in a case where **one exchanged** the **monetary** value of **an ox for a cow, or** the **monetary** value of **a donkey for an ox.** In this case, one sold his ox to another for an agreed sum of money, and after the buyer acquired the ox by pulling it, he then offered to give the seller his cow in exchange for the money that he owes him. In this case the cow is acquired without the seller having to pull it. Although this acquisition initially was to be an exchange, it is ultimately a purchase for money, as the second animal is acquired as a result of the forgiving of the monetary debt.

The Gemara then asks how Rav Nachman would understand this Mishna, given that Rav Nachman holds *peirot* cannot themselves serve as the instrument of *chalipin*. The *seifa* — which the revised reading understood to illustrate *chalipin* through commodities — would then be problematic for Rav Nachman. His answer, according to the Gemara, is that *yesh damim she'hein k'chalipin* — there is a category of value transfer that functions like *chalipin*. Specifically, what the Mishna teaches is that a person may transfer a *debt* — a monetary obligation owed to him by a third party — to another person, and that this transfer of the debt itself, combined with a symbolic act, is sufficient to constitute a legally effective acquisition. The *chiddush* is not that any commodity or coin can be lifted like a *sudar*, but that the conveyance of a legal claim to money can substitute for the physical delivery of money in certain transactional settings. The Mishna therefore has nothing to say about coins functioning as *chalipin*; it addresses the validity of debt-transfer as a mode of commercial exchange.

## Rav Nachman Rationale

### Torah & Rabbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן? סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה – גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר לוֹ: נִשְׂרְפוּ חִטֶּיךָ בָּעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rav Naḥman?** The Gemara explains: Rav Nachman **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says: By Torah law money effects acquisition,** i.e., when one pays money he acquires the item, even if he has not yet performed another act of acquisition. **And for what reason did** the Sages **say** that **pulling acquires** an item and money does not? This is a rabbinic **decree lest** the seller **say to** the buyer after receiving the money: **Your wheat was burned in the upper story.** If a fire breaks out or some other mishap occurs after a seller receives the money, he will not bother to save the goods in his house because they no longer belong to him, and the buyer may incur a loss.

The Gemara now probes the theoretical foundation of Rav Nachman's position. He is said to follow Rabbi Yochanan, who holds: *dvar Torah ma'os konos* — by Torah law, the transfer of money is sufficient to effect acquisition. The Rabbinic rule requiring *meshicha* — physical pulling or taking possession of the object — is not a Torah requirement but a Rabbinic enactment. The reason the Rabbis imposed this requirement is stated as a *gezeirah*: *shema yomar lo nisrefu chitecha b'aliyah* — lest the seller say to the buyer, "Your wheat was burned in the upper story," meaning, lest the seller have already received payment but, before the buyer takes possession, disclaim further responsibility when the goods are destroyed. To prevent such a scenario, the Rabbis required that ownership not transfer until *meshicha* — actual physical taking — has occurred.

### Uncommon Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִלְּתָא דִּשְׁכִיחָא – גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן. מִלְּתָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא – לָא גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages therefore decreed that acquisition takes effect only when a buyer pulls the item. The mishna allows a transaction that indicates that one can effect acquisition using only money because that case of the mishna as explained by Rav Naḥman is an uncommon occurrence. It is rare for one who has sold his animal in exchange for money to change his mind and request an animal from the purchaser instead. **And** it is only **with regard to a common matter** that **the Sages issued a decree,** whereas **with regard to an uncommon matter, the Sages did not issue a decree.** Consequently, the Sages did not apply their decree to this situation.

However, the Rabbis made their enactment only for common situations: *milta d'shchicha gazru bei Rabbanan, milta d'lo shchicha lo gazru bei Rabbanan*. For uncommon or atypical transactions — such as the transfer of a debt owed by one person to another, which is the mechanism Rav Nachman identifies as operative in the Mishna — the Rabbinic *gezeirah* does not apply. In those cases, the Torah's original rule remains in force: a symbolic act, without *meshicha*, suffices to complete the transfer.

### Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמַר מְשִׁיכָה מְפוֹרֶשֶׁת מִן הַתּוֹרָה, הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, מְתָרֵץ לַהּ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת. אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר: פֵּירֵי לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין וּמַטְבֵּעַ לָא קָנֵי, הֵיכִי מְתָרֵץ לַהּ? עַל כֻּרְחָךְ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת מְתָרֵץ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how is the mishna explained **according to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, who** disagrees with Rabbi Yoḥanan and **says** that **pulling** is **explicitly** stated **in the Torah?** Reish Lakish maintains that the acquisition of movable property cannot be performed with money by Torah law, and therefore there can be no distinction between common and uncommon cases. **This works out well if** Reish Lakish **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet,** who says that produce effects exchange. If so, he can **explain** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet. But if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who says** that **produce does not effect exchange and a coin does not effect acquisition** by Torah law or by rabbinic law, **how does he explain** the mishna? The Gemara answers: **Perforce** Reish Lakish **explains** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet.**

Resh Lakish takes a fundamentally different position: *meshicha meforasha min haTorah* — the requirement of *meshicha* to effect acquisition is derived explicitly from the Torah itself, not merely a Rabbinic enactment. On this view, money alone can never suffice under Torah law, and there is no Rabbinic *gezeirah* to be limited or circumscribed. The Gemara then asks how Resh Lakish would understand the Mishna. If he holds that *peirot* can serve as the instrument of *chalipin* — following the position of Rav Sheshet — then the *seifa*'s examples make sense on those terms. But if he follows Rav Nachman and holds that commodities cannot function as the instrument of *chalipin*, he would face the difficulty that neither money nor produce could explain the Mishna, and debt-transfer — Rav Nachman's solution — itself depends on a Rabbinic limitation that Resh Lakish's framework does not recognize. The Gemara leaves the tension with Resh Lakish's position as a difficulty to be navigated.

## Metaltelin Mishna

### Movables Acquire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כׇּל הַמִּטַּלְטְלִין קוֹנִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וַאֲפִילּוּ כִּיס מָלֵא מָעוֹת בְּכִיס מָלֵא מָעוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to those who exchange **all** forms of **movable property, each acquires** the property of **the other. And Reish Lakish says:** The expression: All forms of movable property, includes **even** a case where they exchange a money **pouch full of coins for** a money **pouch full of coins.** Apparently, coins effect exchange and are acquired by means of a transaction of exchange.

The Gemara introduces a final Mishna: *kol ha'metaltelin konin zeh et zeh* — any movable objects can acquire one another. That is, a barter transaction involving two physical movables is legally effective: when one party takes possession of the item being offered in exchange, the other party's item is simultaneously acquired by the counterparty.

On this Mishna, Resh Lakish comments: *va-afilu kis malei ma'ot be-kis malei ma'ot* — even a wallet full of money exchanged for a wallet full of money is encompassed within this rule. The wallets are *metaltelin*, movable objects, and when one party takes hold of the other's wallet, the acquisition is complete.

### Rashi Challenge

Rashi raises a pointed question on this statement. He argues that one is compelled to read Resh Lakish as referring to a *chalipin* transaction rather than a simple barter, because if it were a straightforward value-for-value exchange, money would not effect acquisition — the Rabbis having enacted that *ma'os lo kanu* in the ordinary course. Since an exchange of coins for coins cannot work as a simple monetary purchase, Resh Lakish must mean that one picks up the other's wallet as a *kinyan*, analogous to lifting the *sudar* in a *kinyan sudar*, thereby effecting a *chalipin*. This would imply that money can indeed function as the instrument of *chalipin* — both as the thing being transferred and as the operative object of transfer — a conclusion that seems to contradict everything established in the earlier discussion.

### Demonetization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמָא רַב אַחָא: בְּדִינָר אַנְ[יָ]קָא וַאֲנִיגְרָא, אֶחָד שֶׁפְּסָלַתּוּ מַלְכוּת, וְאֶחָד שֶׁפְּסָלַתּוּ מְדִינָה. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן פְּסָלַתּוּ מַלְכוּת – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא סָגֵי כְּלָל. אֲבָל פְּסָלַתּוּ מְדִינָה, דְּסָגֵי לֵיהּ בִּמְדִינָה אַחֲרִיתִי – אֵימָא אַכַּתִּי מַטְבֵּעַ הוּא וְאֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נִקְנֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן פְּסָלַתּוּ מְדִינָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא סָגֵי לֵיהּ לָא בְּצִנְעָא וְלָא בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא. אֲבָל פְּסָלַתּוּ מַלְכוּת, דְּסָגֵי לֵיהּ בְּצִנְעָא – אֵימָא אַכַּתִּי מַטְבֵּעַ הוּא, וְאֵין מַטְבֵּעַ נִקְנֶה בַּחֲלִיפִין. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa interpreted** that the exchange of coins is referring to the exchange of a money pouch filled **with *anka*** dinars **and** a money pouch filled **with *anigera* dinars. One** is a coin **that the kingdom invalidated,** as the king decreed that the coin will no longer be used, **and one** is a coin **that** the residents of **a province invalidated,** as they no longer use it as currency. **And** it is **necessary** to teach the *halakha* in both cases, **as, if** the *tanna* **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to a coin that **the kingdom invalidated,** I would have said it is not a coin **due to** the fact **that it does not circulate at all,** as the king banned its use. **But** with regard to a coin that the residents of **a province invalidated, which circulates in a different state, say** that **its** legal status **is still** that of **a coin, and money cannot be acquired by means of** a transaction of **exchange. And if** the *tanna* **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to a coin that the residents of **a province invalidated,** I would have said it is not a coin **due to** the fact **that** in that place **it neither circulates in private nor in public,** as the local residents do not use it as currency. **But** with regard to a coin that **the kingdom invalidated, which** could **circulate in private, say** that **its** legal status **is still** that of **a coin, and money cannot be acquired by means of** a transaction of **exchange.** Therefore, it was **necessary** to teach the *halakha* in both cases.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty. Rav Acha interprets the Mishna as referring to *dinar anaka* and *dinar nigra* — specific coin types — and specifies that in each case the coins had been demonetized: *echad she-paslo malchut ve-echad she-paslo medina* — one case where the sovereign government invalidated the currency, and one where a regional authority did so. Once a governmental authority declares that a coin is no longer legal tender, it ceases to function as money in any legal sense. It may still be minted and bear a royal insignia, but it is now merely a piece of metal — a commodity, like any other form of *metaltelin*. The exchange of two wallets of demonetized coins is therefore simply barter between two commodity-goods, not a transaction involving legal currency. There is consequently no tension with the principle that *ein matbea na'aseh chalipin*: these are no longer coins in the relevant legal sense.

### Two Demonetizations

The Gemara insists that both cases — *paslo malchut* and *paslo medina* — were necessary to include. Had the Gemara taught only the case where the empire (*malchut*) demonetized the currency, one might have thought this works only because the coins truly cannot circulate anywhere — they are universally rejected. But where only a regional authority (*medina*) demonetizes the coins, one might argue that the coins retain monetary status because they remain valid currency in neighboring regions; they can still be used as money elsewhere, and therefore should retain their character as *matbea* rather than becoming mere *metaltelin*, with the result that they should *not* be acquirable through *chalipin*. Conversely, had the Gemara taught only the case of *paslo medina*, one might have thought this works only because within that jurisdiction the coins cannot circulate at all — neither publicly nor privately. But when the empire demonetizes the coins, one might argue that they can still be used informally in private transactions — a kind of black-market currency — and therefore retain enough monetary character to preclude *chalipin*. Teaching both cases establishes that in either scenario — whether the invalidation is total and universal, or merely regional — the demonetized coins lose their status as legal currency and become ordinary movables subject to the standard rules of *metaltelin* acquisition.

## Final Synthesis

### Summary

After an extended *shakla ve-tarya*, the Gemara has arrived at a clear and consistent conclusion on two distinct questions. First, money cannot serve as the operative *instrument* of *chalipin* — a coin cannot be used the way a *sudar* is used to enact a symbolic transfer. Second, and more subtly, money also cannot be *acquired* through *chalipin* — it cannot serve as the object of transfer in such a transaction. Sources that appeared to challenge these conclusions — the *ma'aser sheni* Baraita, the money-changer case, the *Kiddushin* Mishna, and Resh Lakish's statement about wallets of coins — have each been reinterpreted. The *ma'aser sheni* case showed that no *chalipin* option was available when money was absent, confirming that money cannot be acquired this way. The money-changer case was resolved either by identifying the coins as unstamped commodity-metal (*prottotat*) or by classifying the transaction as a permissible form of loan under Rav Ashi's principle. The *Kiddushin* Mishna was shown to address the transfer of debt or appraised value through a *sudar*, not a coin functioning as instrument. And Resh Lakish's wallet case was explained as involving demonetized coins that are no longer currency in any legal sense. Throughout, the halakhic lines remain intact: *ein matbea na'aseh chalipin* — coinage has no standard role, whether as instrument or object, in the institution of *chalipin*.
