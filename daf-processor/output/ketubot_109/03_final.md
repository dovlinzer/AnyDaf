# Ketubot 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father Reneges Deal

### Setup & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפּוֹסֵק מָעוֹת לַחֲתָנוֹ, וּפָשַׁט לוֹ אֶת הָרֶגֶל — תֵּשֵׁב עַד שֶׁיַּלְבִּין רֹאשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to one who promises and **apportions money for his son-in-law** as a dowry, **and he went bankrupt,** and he now claims that he does not have the money to fulfill his financial obligations, the betrothed woman can be left to **sit** unwed in her father's house **until her head turns white.**

The daf opens at the very bottom of 108b, where the *mishna* introduces the first of *Admon*'s rulings. The case involves a father-in-law who made a financial agreement with his prospective son-in-law: marry my daughter and I will provide you with a sum of money in support. The Aramaic idiom *u'pashat lo es haregel* — literally, sticking out one's foot — captures the father-in-law's posture of reneging on the deal in a dismissive, almost contemptuous way. *Rashi* suggests the image may connote something like demanding that one clean the sole of his shoe, conveying disrespect, though the operative meaning is simply that the father-in-law is backing out.

A crucial detail: the couple had completed *kiddushin* but not *nisuin*. They are betrothed but not yet fully married. The son-in-law's response to being stiffed is stark — *teshev ad shetyalbin roshah*, let her sit until the hair on her head turns white. He will neither complete the marriage nor give her a *get*, leaving her in the position of an *agunah*. She cannot marry anyone else, and he exploits this leverage without paying what was promised.

*Tosfos* raises the question of whether the original financial agreement was legally binding at all. There is a principle — *hein hein ha-devarim ha-niknin ba-amira* — that an oral agreement can be binding when the marriage follows immediately upon it. *Tosfos* offers two readings: one, that the agreement here was not binding because some time elapsed between the agreement and the *kiddushin*, so the son-in-law cannot sue for the money; or two, that the agreement was indeed binding but would not take full effect until *nisuin* was consummated. Either way, the son-in-law finds himself unable or unwilling to pursue the money in court — perhaps the father-in-law has no assets, perhaps the litigation is not worth the trouble — and instead uses the *get* as a weapon. Whether or not the agreement was technically enforceable, the son-in-law's position is that his acquiescence to completing the marriage was conditioned on the support, and since that support is not forthcoming, he will not move forward. The result is a woman held hostage through no fault of her own.

### Admon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: אִילּוּ אֲנִי פָּסַקְתִּי לְעַצְמִי — אֵשֵׁב עַד שֶׁיַּלְבִּין רֹאשִׁי, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאַבָּא פָּסַק — מָה אֲנִי יְכוֹלָה לַעֲשׂוֹת? אוֹ כְּנוֹס, אוֹ פְּטוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Admon says** that **she can say: Had I apportioned** the money **myself** and broken my promise, I would agree to **sit until my head turns white.** However, **now that** my **father** was the one who **apportioned** the dowry, **what can I do? Either marry** me **or release** me by a bill of divorce.

*Admon* pushes back directly. The woman is permitted to say: *ilu ani pasakti l'atzmi* — if I had been the one who made this deal and I had reneged, then perhaps I would understand sitting here until my hair turns white, because the injury would have originated with me. But *abba pasak alai* — it was my father who made the agreement and my father who reneged. *Ma ani yechola la'asot* — what can I do about it? The conclusion is *o kenos o patur*: either complete the marriage or release me with a *get*. She cannot be made to suffer for her father's breach of contract.

This is a striking ruling. *Admon* is establishing that a woman cannot be held as an *agunah* as leverage for a financial grievance that is not her doing. The fact that the son-in-law was wronged by the father-in-law does not translate into a right to trap the daughter indefinitely.

### Wife's Own Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אַדְמוֹן וַחֲכָמִים עַל הַפּוֹסֵק מָעוֹת לַחֲתָנוֹ וּפָשַׁט לוֹ אֶת הָרֶגֶל, שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: "אַבָּא פָּסַק עָלַי, מָה אֲנִי יְכוֹלָה לַעֲשׂוֹת". עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל שֶׁפָּסְקָה הִיא עַל עַצְמָהּ. שֶׁחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: תֵּשֵׁב עַד שֶׁתַּלְבִּין רֹאשָׁהּ. אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר, יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: כִּסְבוּרָה אֲנִי שֶׁאַבָּא נוֹתֵן עָלַי, וְעַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֵין אַבָּא נוֹתֵן עָלַי — מָה אֲנִי יְכוֹלָה לַעֲשׂוֹת? אוֹ כְּנוֹס אוֹ פְּטוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said: Admon and the Rabbis did not disagree with regard to one who** promises and **apportions money for his son-in-law** as a dowry **and** subsequently **went bankrupt, that** the betrothed woman **can say:** My **father apportioned** money **for me; what can I do?** **With regard to what did they disagree?** It is **with regard to** a case **where she apportioned** money **for herself, as the Rabbis say: Let her sit until her head turns white,** as she did not fulfill her promise. However, **Admon says** that **she can say: I thought that** my **father would give** the money **for me, and now that** my **father is not giving** the money **for me, what can I do? Either marry** me **or release** me.

The *gemara* then raises a *beraita* attributed to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda that reframes where the actual dispute lies. According to this *beraita*, *Admon* and the *chachamim* do not actually disagree in the case described by the *mishna*, where the father made the deal and reneged. In that scenario, everyone agrees the woman can say *abba pasak alai* — she bears no responsibility — and the husband cannot hold her hostage.

The debate, says Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, is specifically *al she-paska hi al atzma* — when the woman herself made the financial commitment to the marriage. In that case, the *chachamim* hold that since she made the deal and she is the one who is reneging, the consequence falls on her and she may indeed sit until her hair turns white. *Admon*, however, says that even then she has a valid claim: *svura hayiti she-abba noten alai* — when I made that commitment, I assumed my father would be the one to write the check. Now that my father is not in fact providing the money, *ma ani yechola la'asot* — it is out of my hands. I made a promise I genuinely expected to keep; the non-fulfillment is beyond my control. Therefore, *o kenos o patur* — even in this case the husband must either marry her or release her.

### Rabban Gamliel's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן. תָּנָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בִּגְדוֹלָה, אֲבָל בִּקְטַנָּה — כּוֹפִין. כּוֹפִין לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לְאָב — אִיפְּכָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כּוֹפִין לַבַּעַל לִיתֵּן גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** And it is with regard to this case that **Rabban Gamliel said: I see** as correct **the statement of Admon,** as the betrothed woman has no money of her own, and she was clearly relying on her father to provide the dowry. § It is **taught: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in the case **of an adult** woman. **However,** with regard **to a minor** girl, the court **compels** payment. The Gemara asks: **Whom do they compel? If we say** that they coerce the **father** to pay, the *tanna* **should have stated the opposite.** It is more reasonable to suggest that the father is compelled to pay when an adult woman promises the money, as the promises of an adult are legally valid, whereas a minor is not legally competent and therefore her promises are of no consequence. **Rather, Rava said:** In the case of a minor, the court **compels the groom** either **to give** her **a bill of divorce** or to marry her.

*Rabban Gamliel* weighs in: *ro'eh ani et divrei Admon* — I see the force of *Admon*'s position. The *gemara* then addresses a *beraita* that asks: when does all of this apply? The answer is *gdola* — when she is an adult. But *aval b'ktana*, if she was a minor when she made the commitment, *kofin* — we actually compel. The *gemara* asks: *kofin le-mi*, compel whom? If the answer is compel the father to pay, that makes little sense — a minor's commitment is less binding than an adult's, so it would be illogical to apply greater force in that case. Rather, says *Rava*, *kofin l'ba'al liten get* — in the case of a minor, the husband is actually compelled to give a *get*.

The logic is that the entire debate only arises when an adult woman made the agreement and backed out. Even in that case, *Admon* says she cannot be held hostage. But if she was a minor when she made the commitment, her agreement carries no binding weight whatsoever, and even the *chachamim* would agree that she cannot be held. In that case, the husband is compelled to act — either to complete the marriage or to release her.

## Halakhic Standard

### Affirmed Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל "רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן" — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar said in the name of Ḥizkiyya: Anywhere** it is recorded **that Rabban Gamliel said: I see** as correct **the statement of Admon, the *halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion.

Having concluded the first group of *Admon*'s rulings, the *gemara* turns to the question of practical *halacha*. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Elazar says in the name of Chizkiya: *kol makom she-amar Rabban Gamliel ro'eh ani et divrei Admon halacha kemoto* — wherever *Rabban Gamliel* says "I see *Admon*'s position," we rule like him. This establishes a clear halakhic standard: affirmation by *Rabban Gamliel* is the criterion for ruling in accordance with *Admon*.

*Rava* then asks *Rav Nachman*: does this apply even to the *beraita*'s version of the case — the scenario where the woman herself made the deal and is reneging, which is the terrain of the *beraita* rather than the *mishna*? *Rav Nachman* responds: I did not say "wherever *Rabban Gamliel* says this in the *mishna*." I said *wherever* he says it — *be-chol makom she-amar Rabban Gamliel ka-amina*. Since *Rabban Gamliel* affirms *Admon* in the *beraita* as well, we rule like *Admon* there too. Even when the woman herself made the commitment and is backing out, she cannot be held hostage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: אֲפִילּוּ בְּבָרַיְיתָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי קָאָמְרִינַן בַּמִּשְׁנָה?! בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman:** Does this halakhic principle apply **even** when the debate between Admon and the Rabbis is recorded **in a *baraita*?** Rav Nachman **said to him: Did we say:** Anywhere it is recorded **in the Mishna? We said: Anywhere** it is recorded **that Rabban Gamliel said,** which means even in a *baraita*.

### חנן vs Admon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה: שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר חָנָן — הֲלָכָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר אַדְמוֹן — אֵין הֲלָכָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ. מַאי קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rabba bar Yirmeya said:** With regard to the **two statements that Ḥanan said,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him,** i.e., Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai (see 107b); however, with regard to the **seven statements that Admon said,** the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him,** i.e., Rabban Gamliel. The Gemara asks: **What is he saying?** What does this ruling mean?

The *gemara* then introduces a statement of Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rabbah bar Yirmeyah: regarding the two rulings of *Chanan*, *halacha kiyotzei vo* — we rule like him, and *kiyotzei vo*. The phrase *kiyotzei vo* is the subject of a dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosfos*. *Tosfos* understands it to mean that we not only rule like *Chanan* in the specific cases he addressed, but we extract the underlying principle and apply it to analogous cases. *Rashi*, by contrast, reads *kiyotzei vo* as referring to the one who affirmed the ruling — that is, since *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* affirmed *Chanan*'s two positions, we rule in accordance with that affirmation.

Regarding *Admon*'s seven rulings, Rabbi Zeira says *ein halacha kiyotzei vo*. On *Tosfos*'s reading, this means we do not extend *Admon*'s rulings to analogous cases. On *Rashi*'s reading, it means we do not rule in accordance with whoever affirmed him. The *gemara* immediately presses: if taken literally, this would seem to say we reject *Admon* entirely — but we know that cannot be right, since we just established from Rabbi Yitzchak bar Elazar that wherever *Rabban Gamliel* affirms *Admon* we rule like him.

### Partial Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר חָנָן — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ וְכַיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, וְשִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר אַדְמוֹן — אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ וְלֹא כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל "רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן" — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that **this is what** he **is saying:** With regard to the **two statements that Ḥanan said,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **and in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him, and** with regard to the **seven statements that Admon said,** the ***halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion **and** also **not in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him, but didn't Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar say in the name of Ḥizkiyya: Anywhere** it is recorded **that Rabban Gamliel said: I see** as correct **the statement of Admon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion.

The *gemara* works through the formulation carefully. An initial reading — *halacha kemoto u-kiyotzei vo* for *Chanan*, and *ein halacha kemoto* for *Admon* in all seven — is rejected because it contradicts the explicit ruling that we follow *Admon* in the cases where *Rabban Gamliel* affirmed him. Another reading — that *halacha kemoto* applies to all seven of *Admon*'s cases concretely, just not to analogous extensions — is also rejected for the same reason.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר חָנָן — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ וְכַיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר אַדְמוֹן — יֵשׁ מֵהֶן שֶׁהֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ וְכַיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן שֶׁאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ. בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, אִינָךְ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is what** Rabbi Zeira **is saying:** With regard to the **two statements that Ḥanan said,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **and is** also **in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him,** i.e., Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai. With regard to the **seven statements that Admon said, there are among** these rulings **those** in **which** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **and in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him, and there are among** these rulings **those** in **which** the ***halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion **but in accordance with** he who ruled **similarly to him.** **Anywhere** it is recorded **that Rabban Gamliel said: I see** as correct **the statement of Admon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion, whereas in those **other** cases, where Rabban Gamliel remained silent, indicating that he did not agree with him, the *halakha* is **not** in accordance with Admon.

The *gemara* settles on the following resolution: by *Chanan*, we rule like him in both of his rulings and we apply his principles to analogous cases as well — *halacha kemoto u-kiyotzei vo*. By *Admon*, the picture is mixed — *yesh mehem she-halacha kemoto ve-hayotzei vo*, some of his rulings we rule like him and apply to similar cases, *ve-yesh mehem she-ein halacha kemoto ve-hayotzei bo*, and some we do not rule like him even in the concrete case. The determinant is straightforward: *ve-chol makom she-amar Rabban Gamliel ro'eh ani et divrei Admon halacha kemoto* — wherever *Rabban Gamliel* affirmed him, we rule like him and extend the principle; in the other four rulings of *Admon*, we rule neither like him in the specific case nor in analogous ones. This final formulation simply restates what was established earlier, resolving the apparent contradiction by making clear that Rabbi Zeira's statement referred to the non-affirmed cases.

On *Tosfos*'s reading, the point being made is that for *Chanan* we abstract a general principle, whereas for the unaffirmed *Admon* rulings we do not even do that much. On *Rashi*'s reading, the structure is about the voice of affirmation — we follow *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* who affirmed *Chanan* in both cases, and we follow *Rabban Gamliel* where he affirmed *Admon*, but we do not follow *Admon* where no affirmation was given.

## Field & Witness

### Witness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹרֵר עַל הַשָּׂדֶה, וְהוּא חָתוּם עָלֶיהָ בְּעֵד — אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַשֵּׁנִי נוֹחַ לִי, וְהָרִאשׁוֹן קָשֶׁה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who contests** ownership **of a field,** claiming that a field under the control of someone else actually belongs to him, **and** the claimant himself **is signed as a witness on** the bill of sale to that other person, **Admon says:** His signature does not disprove his claim of ownership of the property, as it is possible that the claimant said to himself: **The second** person **is easier for me,** as I can reason with him, **but the first** owner, who sold the field to the current holder, **is more difficult** to deal with **than him.**

The *gemara* now transitions to *Admon*'s second ruling, which concerns a different sort of case entirely — one involving a claim of stolen property and the complications that arise from having signed as a witness on a document. The case: Reuven claims that Shimon stole his field. Reuven has evidence that the field was originally his — Shimon cannot produce a bill of sale, and Shimon does not have three years of *chazaka*. The absence of those would normally be sufficient for Reuven to press his claim successfully. There is, however, one complication: *ha-over al ha-sadeh ve-chatam aleha be-ed* — Reuven himself signed as a witness on the document by which Shimon sold that very field to a third party, Levi.

How can Reuven now claim the field was his if he was a witness to Shimon's sale of it? The implicit logic is that by signing as a witness, Reuven appears to have conceded that the field belonged to Shimon — otherwise, why would he have signed?

### Admon Excuses

The claimant might have been afraid to protest against the first one, who is perhaps violent, and therefore he was even willing to sign as a witness to transfer the field to the control of someone more amenable to his ensuing protest.

Reuven's can say: *ha-sheni no'ach li ve-ha-rishon kasheh himenu* — it is easier to litigate against Levi, the buyer, than against Shimon, the original thief. Shimon is a dangerous or powerful figure; Reuven may have had good reason not to confront him directly. By signing the document facilitating the sale from Shimon to Levi, Reuven may simply have been positioning himself to retrieve the field more conveniently from Levi — allowing the field to change hands to someone less threatening, and then pursuing his claim against the new holder. This is a fully articulated rationale for why a signature on a document of sale does not necessarily constitute a concession of ownership.

### Chachamim Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ. עֲשָׂאָהּ סִימָן לְאַחֵר — אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: He** has **lost his right** to contest ownership, as he signed a bill of sale that states that the field belongs to the present holder. If **he established** that field **as a marker for another** field, everyone agrees that **he** has **lost his right.** In other words, if the claimant wrote a document concerning another field and in that document he listed the first field as a boundary marker and described it as belonging to someone else, even Admon concedes that he has lost his right, as he had no reason to say it belonged to someone else other than his belief this was in fact the case.

The *chachamim* reject this entirely. Their position is *ibad et zechuto* — he has forfeited his rights. If Reuven signed as a witness to a document in which Shimon presented himself as the owner selling the field to Levi, then Reuven has implicitly conceded that the field was Shimon's. The *halacha* follows the *chachamim* here, not *Admon* — this is one of the four rulings of *Admon* that *Rabban Gamliel* did not affirm.

### Judge Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא עֵד, אֲבָל דַּיָּין — לֹא אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ. דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִין עַל הַשְּׁטָר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קְרָאוּהוּ. אֲבָל הַדַּיָּינִין חוֹתְמִים אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא קְרָאוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye said: They taught** this dispute **only** with regard to **a witness** who signed on a bill of sale. **However, a judge** who was contesting the ownership of a field and at the same time sat on a court that certified the bill of sale for that very tract of land **has not lost his right.** This is **as Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Witnesses do not sign a document unless they have read it,** which means that a witness cannot reasonably claim that he is certifying only part of document; it is assumed that he read and is aware of everything it contains. **However, judges do sign a document even though they have not read it,** as they are merely certifying the signatures without relating to the content of the document at all.

*Abaye* introduces an important distinction: *lo shanu ela ed* — this dispute, and the implicit concession, applies only when one signed as a *witness* to the document. *Aval dayan* — but if one served not as a witness but as a judge validating the signatures on the document, the rule is different. *Abaye* derives this from a teaching of Rabbi Chiyya: *ein ha-eidim chotmim al ha-shtar ela im ken kerauhu* — witnesses may not sign a document unless they have read it, because they are attesting to its content and must know what they are attesting to. But *aval ha-dayanim* — judges who are validating signatures do not need to have read the document. *Chotmim af al pi shelo kerauhu* — they may validate the signatures even without reading the full contents.

The analogy to a notary public is apt: a notary attests that the signatures are genuine, not that the contents of the document are true. Since a judge validating signatures need not have read — and may not have read — what the document says, his signature carries no implicit endorsement of the document's claims. Therefore, *aval dayan lo ibad et zechuto* — if Reuven served as a validating judge rather than as a substantive witness, he does not forfeit his right to claim the field.

### Boundary Marker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂאָהּ סִימָן לְאַחֵר. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְאַחֵר, אֲבָל לְעַצְמוֹ — לֹא אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that if someone **established** a field **as a marker for another** field, everyone agrees that he has lost his right to contest its ownership. **Abaye said: They taught** this **only** in a case where he stated this in a document unrelated to the field in question, which was written **for another** person. **However,** if he established it as a marker **for himself,** when he was buying a different field owned by the person who is in possession of the contested field, he has **not lost his right.**

A further distinction is introduced regarding a scenario where the field in question was not the field being sold but was mentioned only as a boundary marker. If Shimon sold Levi a neighboring field, Field B, and in that document described Field A — the field Reuven claims was stolen — as one of Field B's borders, then even *Admon* concedes that Reuven's signature on that document constitutes an implicit admission that Field A belongs to Shimon.

The reason is that in the original dispute — where Shimon was selling Field A itself to Levi — *Admon*'s rationale for Reuven's signature was that Reuven wanted the field to change to a less threatening holder. But here, Field A is not being transferred. It appears in the document only as a descriptive boundary marker. Reuven gains nothing from facilitating that transaction — there is no strategic reason for him to have signed a document that, in passing, describes Field A as belonging to Shimon. His signature therefore concedes Shimon's ownership, and *Abaye* rules *ibad et zechuto* — he has forfeited his claim.

## Coercion Defense

### Own Field Used

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר: אִי דְּלָא עֲבַדִי לֵיהּ הָכִי לָא הֲוָה מְזַבֵּין לַהּ נִיהֲלִי, מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר? אִיבְּעִי לִי לְמִימְסַר מוֹדָעָא — חַבְרָךְ חַבְרָא אִית לֵיהּ, וְחַבְרָא דְחַבְרָךְ חַבְרָא אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The reason is **that** the claimant can **say: Had I not done this** act of writing that the field belongs **to him, he would not have sold me this other** field. **What have you to say** against this argument? Will you say that **I should have put out a notice** and declared in the presence of witnesses that I am forced to write this way in the contract? I was concerned about doing this, as **your friend has a friend and your friend's friend has a friend.** In other words, it is hard to keep an announcement of this kind secret, and ultimately my notice would have become public knowledge and would have reached the owner of the field himself, and he would have refused to sell me the other tract of land.

*Abaye* continues with another variation: *lo shanu ela le-achar* — the previous analysis applies when Shimon is selling the field to someone else. But *aval le-atzmo* — what if Shimon is selling a different field directly to Reuven himself, and in the document of that sale Shimon uses Field A — the field Reuven claims was stolen — as a boundary marker? In that case, *lo ibad et zechuto* — Reuven does not forfeit his right to claim Field A.

Why not? Because Reuven can say: *de-amar i lo avdi lei hachi, hava lo mazbin li* — if I had not agreed to let him write the document that way, he would not have sold me the other field. Reuven needed to acquire Field B from Shimon. Shimon, who already took Field A from him, was insisting on describing Field A as his in the document of sale. Reuven had no realistic option but to go along with it in order to complete the transaction. His acquiescence to that language does not constitute a concession; it reflects economic compulsion.

### Moda'a Objection

The *gemara* objects: if Reuven felt genuinely coerced, *ibai lei le-mimsar moda'a* — he should have issued a *moda'a*, a formal protestation made before witnesses. The institution of *moda'a* allows someone who anticipates being coerced into signing a document or entering a transaction to declare beforehand to witnesses: "Know that whatever I am about to sign or agree to, I am doing so under duress, and my apparent agreement should not be taken as genuine consent." Had Reuven issued such a *moda'a*, he could later produce those witnesses to challenge the document. Why did he not do so?

### Secrecy Problem

Reuven's response: *chavrach chavra is lei, ve-chavra de-chavrach chavra is lei* — your friend has a friend, and your friend's friend has a friend. The *gemara*'s formulation is its version of the principle that secrets cannot be reliably kept. If Reuven had issued a *moda'a* to witnesses, the word would inevitably have reached Shimon, who would then have refused to proceed with the sale of Field B at all — and Reuven would have lost his only leverage to acquire anything. The impossibility of keeping a *moda'a* secret is itself sufficient justification for why Reuven did not pursue that course.

This is therefore a reasonable explanation for Reuven's conduct: he allowed the document to proceed as written because he had no viable alternative, and the concern about secrecy adequately accounts for why he did not protect himself through a *moda'a*. Given that reasonable explanation, his signing cannot be read as a concession that Field A belonged to Shimon.

## Orphans Case

### Initial Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּעֲשָׂאָהּ סִימָן לְאַחֵר, עַרְעַר, וּשְׁכֵיב, וְאוֹקֵים אַפּוֹטְרוֹפָּא. אֲתָא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֲשָׂאָהּ סִימָן לְאַחֵר אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** individual **who established** a field **as a marker for another** field, with ownership of the first field ascribed to someone else. Later he **contested** the ownership of the first field, claiming it as his own, **and** he subsequently **died. And** prior to his death he had **appointed a steward** to manage the properties of his orphans. **The steward came before Abaye.** Abaye **said to him:** The deceased **established** the field **as a marker for another, and** therefore **he has lost his right** to contest ownership of the land.

The *gemara* now brings a *ma'aseh*, a concrete case, that illustrates these principles. A man who had claimed that a field had been stolen from him used that same field as a boundary marker in another transaction. He then died — *e'ar ve-shachiv* — before being able to press his claim in court. The court appointed an *apotropas*, a guardian, to represent the interests of the orphans, his heirs, with respect to the contested field. When the *apotropas* appeared before *Abaye* and presented evidence that the field had in fact belonged to the deceased father, *Abaye* ruled against him: *asa siman le-achar ve-ibeid zechuto* — by allowing the field to be used as a boundary marker in another document, the father had implicitly conceded ownership and forfeited the right to claim it. The orphans, as heirs to their father's claim, inherited that forfeiture.

### Guardian Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: אִי הֲוָה אֲבוּהוֹן דְּיַתְמֵי קַיָּים, הֲוָה טָעֵין וְאָמַר: "תֶּלֶם אֶחָד עָשִׂיתִי לָךְ". אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אִם טָעַן וְאָמַר "תֶּלֶם אֶחָד עָשִׂיתִי לָךְ" — נֶאֱמָן. זִיל הַב לֵיהּ מִיהַת תֶּלֶם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The steward **said** to Abaye: **If the father of** these **orphans were alive he would have claimed and said: I established one furrow** for **you.** In other words, the marker I established by means of this field was not meant as a reference to the entire field but only to a furrow at the edge of the field. Abaye **said to him: You have spoken well, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: If he claimed and said: I established one furrow** for **you, he is deemed credible. Go** and **give him one furrow in any event,** as you freely admit that at least one furrow belongs to the one currently in control of the property.

The *apotropas* was not finished. He argued: *i havi avuhon de-yasmei kayam, havi to'en ve-amar telem echad asiti lach* — had the father still been alive, here is what he would have claimed. He would have said: I conceded only one furrow's worth of land — a thin strip — not the entire field. The logic is that when the father's field was described as the boundary of another field in the document, the literal implication is only that a narrow strip at the edge of his field — the actual boundary — belongs to the other party. There is no reason to read the use of a field as a boundary marker as a concession of the entire plot. The father was conceding the furrow that formed the edge, no more.

This is a sophisticated argument for minimizing the scope of the implicit admission. The field appears in the document as a boundary, not as a subject of sale. A boundary, by definition, is an edge — a line, not a mass. To say that Field A's western edge borders Field B does not require that all of Field A belongs to Field B's owner; it requires only that the very edge of Field A — perhaps a furrow's width — does.

### Rabbi Yochanan

*Abaye* acknowledged the force of this argument and cited Rabbi Yochanan: *im ta'an ve-amar telem echad asiti lach, ne'eman* — if the claimant says that all he conceded was one furrow, he is believed. On this basis, *abaye* ruled to award the field to the orphans — *shakluha ve-havu lehu le-yasmei* — with the qualification that the narrow strip be returned to the other party. The *apotropas*, however, pressed further: that strip happened to contain a dense stand of palm trees, *richba de-diklei*, making it quite valuable. He was reluctant to hand it back.

## Lost Path

### Path Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָלַךְ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְאָבְדָה דֶּרֶךְ שָׂדֵהוּ. אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵלֵךְ לוֹ בִּקְצָרָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִקְנֶה לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ בְּמֵאָה מָנֶה, אוֹ יִפְרַח בָּאֲוִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who went overseas and** in the meantime **the path** leading **to his field was lost,** e.g., the path he used to reach his land was taken over by the owner of the field through which it passed, so that its exact position is now unknown, **Admon says: Let him go** to his field **by the shortest** possible route. **And the Rabbis say: Let him buy himself a path** from an owner of a neighboring field at whatever price he can, even if it is **one hundred dinars [*maneh*], or let him fly through the air.**

The *gemara* moves to *Admon*'s third ruling, introduced in a new *mishna*. The scenario: *mi she-halach li-medinat ha-yam ve-avda derech sadehu* — a man went overseas, and while he was away, the path leading to his field was lost. The physical setting is important. Imagine a large agricultural district in which many fields are held by different owners. Nestled in the interior is Reuven's field, accessible only by passing through or alongside the surrounding fields. Reuven had an established right of access — a path — but after years away, no one can identify where it ran. Whether through genuine forgetting, neglect, or the self-interest of neighbors, the path has simply disappeared from collective memory.

### Admon Claims

*Admon omer yelech lo be-ketzara* — *Admon* rules that Reuven is entitled to be given the shortest available path through the surrounding property. The reasoning is that everyone knows Reuven is owed *some* path; the fact that the precise location has been forgotten does not extinguish the right. Since the right exists and the path must go somewhere, Reuven should receive the most efficient route — the shortest one. He cannot be asked to prove which specific path was his; it is enough that a path was his.

### Chachamim Harsh

The *chachamim* take a much harder line: *yikneh lo derech be-meah maneh, o yifrach ba-avir* — let him purchase a path for a hundred *maneh*, or fly through the air. In other words, if Reuven cannot identify which path was specifically his, he has no enforceable claim against any particular neighbor. Each neighbor can say "my field is not where your path was," and since Reuven cannot prove otherwise, he has no recourse. The neighbor is free to name any price for access, however exorbitant, and if Reuven cannot pay, he simply has no way in.

This is a striking ruling. As one listener in the shiur observed, it seems less like a legal principle and more like a neighbor squeezing a vulnerable party. And yet, the *gemara* takes it seriously as a *halachic* position.

### Ownership Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְרַבָּנַן, שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר אַדְמוֹן! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקִּיפוּהָ אַרְבָּעָה בְּנֵי אָדָם מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹתֶיהָ — אִי הָכִי, מַאי טַעְמָא דְאַדְמוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis** that he is not automatically entitled to a path leading to his field? After all, **Admon speaks well,** as it was established that he owned a path beforehand. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The mishna is dealing with a case **where** the field **was surrounded on four sides by** the property of **four different people.** Each of the four properties adjoining his field belonged to a different person, each of whom denied that his path cut through his land. Since he is unable to prove his claim with regard to each one of them, he has no choice but to buy himself a new path. The Gemara asks: **If so, what is the reason** for the opinion **of Admon?**

The *gemara* seeks to understand the logical basis of the dispute. *Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Rav*: the *chachamim*'s position makes sense in a case where *ke-gon she-hekifuha arba'ah b'nei adam me-arba ruchoteha* — four different individuals each own one of the four surrounding fields. In that scenario, any one of them brought to court can say: the path was never in my field — go prove it was. With four separate defendants each able to deflect responsibility to the others, Reuven has no effective legal recourse against any of them individually.

But then the *gemara* asks: *ei hachi, mai ta'ama de-Admon?* — if that's the scenario, then what is *Admon*'s basis for disagreeing? Even he should concede that Reuven cannot win. So the *gemara* concludes that the dispute must occupy a middle case.

### Rava's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאַרְבְּעָה דְּאָתוּ מִכֹּחַ אַרְבְּעָה, וְאַרְבְּעָה דְּאָתוּ מִכֹּחַ חַד — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמָצֵי מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּחַד דְּאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ אַרְבְּעָה. אַדְמוֹן סָבַר: מִכׇּל מָקוֹם דַּרְכָּא אִית לִי גַּבָּךְ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אִי שָׁתְקַתְּ — שָׁתְקַתְּ, וְאִי לָא — מַהְדַּרְנָא שְׁטָרָא לְמָרַיְיהוּ וְלָא מָצֵית לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** In a case **where** there are **four** owners of fields **who come by virtue of four** previous owners, **and** in a case where there are **four** owners **who come by virtue of one** previous owner of all four properties, **everyone agrees that they** may **put off** the claims of the owner of the field in the middle, as each can say: Your path did not cut through my property. **When they disagree** is in a case **where** there is **one** current owner of four fields **who comes by virtue of four** previous owners. Rava elaborates: **Admon holds** that the claimant can say to the landowner: **In any case, I have a path through your** property. Although he cannot prove where the path ran, it was certainly within the perimeter of the landowner's property that borders his own tract of land, and for this reason he selects the shortest route. **And the Rabbis hold** that the landowner can say to the man: **If you will stay silent,** then **stay silent** and we will compromise; **and if not, I will return the document** of each field **to its** previous **owner and you will not be able to negotiate with them,** as you do not know through which field your path passed.

*Rava* identifies that middle case. The debate is not about four separate owners who were always separate (*arba'ah de-atu mi-koach arba'ah*) — in that scenario, everyone agrees Reuven cannot successfully sue anyone. Nor is it simply about four fields originally held by the same owner. The key case is *be-chad de-atu mi-koach arba'ah*: originally, each of the four surrounding fields belonged to a different farmer. Then a single entity — *Rava* describes it as one person who acquired from four — came and purchased all four fields. Now they all belong to one owner.

*Admon*'s position: *mikol makom darchai ika gabech* — regardless of how those fields were originally held, the path exists somewhere within property that now belongs to you. I can bring a single litigant to court and demand my path. *Rabban* (the *chachamim*) respond: *ai shatakt shatakt, ve-ai lo, mehadarna shtra le-marihu* — if you leave this alone, we can work something out quietly; but if you insist on litigating, I will simply transfer the fields back to the four original owners, and then you will be back to square one — four separate defendants, each able to deflect responsibility. Since your rights originated in a situation where you could not have successfully forced any one person to provide the path, you gain no additional rights now merely because I happened to consolidate those fields.

The *gemara* acknowledges the strangeness of this argument — it has the character of an economic threat rather than a principled legal defense. One would not expect the court to take seriously the claim "I will undo a commercial transaction to disadvantage you in litigation." And sympathies here rest naturally with *Admon*. Yet this is how the *gemara* frames the *chachamim*'s position.

## Palm Tree Legacy

### Bequest Ignored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: דִּיקְלָא לִבְרַת. אֲזוּל יַתְמֵי פְּלוּג לְנִכְסֵי, לָא יְהַבוּ לַהּ דִּיקְלָא. סְבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ מַתְנִיתִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who said to** his heirs, in his will: I am leaving **a palm tree for my daughter. The orphans went** and **divided up the property** and **they did not give** the daughter **a palm tree** at all. **Rav Yosef thought to say that this is** exactly like the case of **the mishna,** as each heir can say to her: The tree designated for you is not in my portion but in that of a different heir. **Abaye said to him: Are** the cases **comparable?**

The *gemara* concludes with a case that echoes the structure of the lost-path problem in a different context. A man on his deathbed said to his children: *dikla le-brat* — give a palm tree to my daughter. He did not specify which palm tree. He then died. The male heirs proceeded to divide up the entire estate among themselves without setting aside a palm tree for their sister. Now she comes to court demanding what her father bequeathed.

The *gemara* initially considers comparing this to the lost-path case: just as each surrounding field owner could deflect responsibility to the next, perhaps each brother can say "your palm tree was in the other brother's portion, not mine." *Rav Yosef* is inclined toward this comparison.

### Half vs Whole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כֹּל חַד וְחַד מָצֵי מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ, הָכָא דִּיקְלָא גַּבַּיְיהוּ הוּא. מַאי תַּקַּנְתַּיְיהוּ? לִיתְּבוּ לַהּ דִּיקְלָא וְלֶיהְדְּרוּ וְלִיפַּלְגוּ מֵרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **There, each and every one** of the fields' owners **can put him off,** as in fact the path might not have passed through his property, whereas **here, the palm tree is in their** possession, i.e., they all share the obligation to give her a tree, and each is illegally holding on to a share of it. Abaye adds: **What is their remedy? Let them give her a palm tree and go back and divide** the inherited property once again **from the outset.** Since the palm tree will be taken from one of the shares, they must redistribute the estate afresh.

*Abaye* rejects the comparison. *Mi dami* — the cases are not alike. In the lost-path case, *kol chad ve-chad matzi madchei lei* — each owner can genuinely deflect, because the path was originally located in a specific field and no one knows which one. But here, *dikla gabaihu* — there was never any question that the estate as a whole owed her a palm tree. The obligation ran against the estate, not against any particular plot. The division of the estate among the brothers does not discharge that obligation; it only complicates how it is fulfilled. The solution is *leihavu lah dikla* — give her a palm tree — and then *ve-hadar ve-lipalgu me-reisha* — re-divide the estate from the beginning, with the bequest having been satisfied first. If the brothers feel that one of them is bearing a disproportionate cost by giving up a palm tree, that is a matter to resolve among themselves; but the sister's right cannot be extinguished simply because they divided without her.

### Halves Equal Whole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: דִּיקְלָא לִבְרַת. שְׁכֵיב וּשְׁבַיק תְּרֵי פַּלְגֵי דְּדִיקְלָא. יָתֵיב רַב אָשֵׁי וְקָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: מִי קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לִתְרֵי פַּלְגֵי דִּיקְלֵי דִּיקְלָא, אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמַר אֲבִימִי מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: קָרוּ אִינָשֵׁי לִתְרֵי פַּלְגֵי דִּיקְלֵי דִּיקְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar story: There was **a certain** man **who said to** his heirs, in his will: I am leaving **a palm tree for my daughter. He died and left behind two halves of palm trees,** as there had been two trees he had shared with different business partners. The heirs wished to give the girl these two halves, despite the fact that tending to them would involve considerably more trouble than taking care of a single tree. **Rav Ashi sat and posed a difficulty to** the proposal of the heirs. **Do people refer to two halves of a palm tree** as a whole **palm tree or not?** If the statement of the dying father can reasonably be interpreted as referring to these two halves she is at a disadvantage, as the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. Alternatively, if this is an unreasonable explanation of his wishes, they must give her a whole tree. Which alternative is correct? **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: Avimi of Hagronya said as follows in the name of Rava: People do** in fact **refer to two halves of a palm tree as** a whole **palm tree.** Consequently, the heirs may fulfill their father's dying wish by giving the girl the two halves they inherited.

The *gemara* then presents a related case. A man died with a similar bequest — *dikla le-brat*, a palm tree to his daughter — and it emerged that among the assets of the estate were two palm trees in which the deceased had a half-ownership interest in each. The heirs wished to discharge their obligation to the daughter by giving her the two half-interests — a full palm tree's worth, distributed across two trees — rather than one whole palm tree.

*Rav Ashi* was uncertain: *mi karu inshi le-trei palgei dikla dikla* — would people ordinarily refer to two half-palm-trees as "a palm tree"? The question is whether the deathbed bequest of *dikla* — a palm tree — can be satisfied by two partial interests, or whether the word *dikla* necessarily implies an undivided whole.

*Rav Mordechai* conveyed to *Rav Ashi* the ruling of *Abimi me-Hagronya* in the name of *Rava*: *karu inshi le-trei palgei dikla dikla* — yes, people do call two halves a palm tree. The ordinary usage of the term encompasses this configuration. Since the language of the bequest is not so precise as to demand a single, undivided tree, the heirs may satisfy the obligation with the two half-interests, and their obligation is thereby discharged.
