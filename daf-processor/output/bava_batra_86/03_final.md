# Bava Batra 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vessels & Price

### Intro & Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּירְקָן וְהִכְנִיסָן לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ; פָּסַק עַד שֶׁלֹּא מָדַד – אֵין שְׁנֵיהֶן יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶן, מָדַד עַד שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק – שְׁנֵיהֶן יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** But in a case where **he unloaded** the merchandise from them **and brought it into his house,** if **he fixed** a price **before he measured** the merchandise, **both** parties **are no** longer **able to renege on** the sale. If **he measured** the merchandise **before fixing** a price, **both of them are able to renege on** the sale.

Daf 86 opens eight lines into the page, completing a discussion carried over from the previous daf. The *baraita* under examination deals with donkey drivers or workers carrying loads into the buyer's house: at what point does the buyer acquire the merchandise? The broader question, which occupied the preceding discussion, concerns *keilo shel lokei'ach birshut mocher* — whether the buyer's vessels effect a *kinyan* while still in the seller's domain. The present *baraita* adds a further condition, one that at first glance seems puzzling: the sale is only final once the price has been set.

### Price & Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּמָר זוּטְרָא לְרָבִינָא: מִכְּדֵי "פֵּירְקָן" קָתָנֵי; מָה לִי פָּסַק וּמָה לִי לֹא פָּסַק? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פָּסַק – סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ, לֹא פָּסַק – לָא סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the matter itself, **Huna, son of Mar Zutra, said to Ravina: Since** the *baraita* **teaches: He unloaded them,** which indicates that unloading the merchandise constitutes the act of acquisition, **what** difference is there **to me** if **he fixed** a price, **and what** difference is there **to me** if **he did not fix** a price? Ravina **said to him:** If **he fixed** a price **he has made up his mind** to sell, and therefore the transaction can take place. If **he did not fix** a price, **he has not made up his mind** to sell and the transaction does not occur.

Huna bar Rav Sheshes poses this difficulty to Ravina. The *baraita* specifies that once the merchandise has been unloaded — *pirkan katani* — the goods are in the buyer's domain. Given that, why should it matter whether the price has been set? If the *kinyan* is taking effect, and the goods are no longer on the worker but in the buyer's possession, what difference does *psak* — price-setting — make?

Ravina's response is crisp: *pasak, samcha daatei; lo pasak, lo samcha daatei.* Once the price is fixed, the buyer's mind is settled and his intent to purchase is final. Without a set price, the seller might yet demand more, or the buyer might offer less — neither party has truly committed to the transaction. Even if the physical act of *kinyan* is underway, the absence of a settled price means the transaction lacks the mental finality required for *kinyan* to take hold.

### Rav & Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנֶה לוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי רְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as Rav and Shmuel both say: A person's vessel effects acquisition for him** of any item placed inside it, **in any place** that it is situated. **What is added** by the phrase: In any place? Does it **not** serve **to add** the **domain of the seller?**

Ravina then turns to Rav Ashi and invites him to consider a related source. Rav and Shmuel both say: *keilo shel adam koneh lo b'chol makom* — a person's vessel acquires for him in any location. The phrase *b'chol makom* seems to resolve the question directly: does it not come to include even the seller's domain? If so, *keilo shel lokei'ach* works even *birshut mocher* without any additional condition.

### Seller Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל קְנִי.
>
> **Translation:** There, it is referring to a specific case, **where** the seller **said to him: Go** and **acquire** it.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading. The phrase *b'chol makom* applies, but only where the seller has explicitly said *zil kani* — "go, take possession." This is a significant ruling. The reason the buyer's vessel does not automatically acquire in the seller's domain is that the merchandise is still perceived as being under the seller's control, effectively within his sphere. If, however, the seller actively relinquishes that control by telling the buyer to take possession now, the vessel of the buyer will work. The seller's utterance of *zil kani* is what removes the impediment. This is the *Gemara*'s conclusion regarding *keilo shel lokei'ach birshut mocher*: the buyer's vessel does not acquire in the seller's domain unless the seller grants explicit permission through the declaration *zil kani*.

## Meshicha Debate

### Chattels via Meshicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת – נִקְנִין בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, וְשֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת – אֵין נִקְנִין אֶלָּא בִּמְשִׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Kiddushin* 26a): **Property that is guaranteed,** i.e., land, **is acquired by** means of **money, or by** means of **a bill, or by taking possession** of it. **And** property **that does not have a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, **can be acquired only by** means of **pulling.**

The *Gemara* now pivots to a new topic, introduced by the phrase *tnan hatam* — "we learned elsewhere." The *Mishnah* in Kiddushin establishes that real property — *nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut* — is acquired through money, document, or *chazaka*, while *metaltelin*, chattel, is acquired only through *meshicha*, dragging. The *Mishnah*'s formulation seems to make *meshicha* the sole *kinyan* for all chattels.

### Hagbaha vs Meshicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא; בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא מַתְנוּ לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהַגְבִּיהַּ, אֲבָל דְּבָרִים שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לְהַגְבִּיהַּ – בְּהַגְבָּהָה אִין, בִּמְשִׁיכָה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** In Sura they taught this** following ***halakha* in the name of Rav Ḥisda,** while **in Pumbedita they taught it in the name of Rav Kahana, and some say in the name of Rava: They taught** that movable property is acquired by means of pulling **only** with regard to **items that are not typically lifted** due to their weight or for some other reason. **But** in the case of **items that are typically lifted,** then **yes,** they are acquired **by** means of **lifting,** but they are **not** acquired **by** means of **pulling.**

This universal rule, however, was significantly qualified by a teaching transmitted in the academies. In Sura it was taught in the name of Rav Chisda; in Pumbedita, in the name of Rav Kahana, with some attributing it to Rava. The teaching limits the applicability of *meshicha*: *lo shanu ela devarim she-ein darkan lehagbiya* — *meshicha* is effective only for objects not normally lifted, objects heavy enough that dragging is their natural mode of handling. For such objects, the act of dragging is the meaningful expression of taking possession. But *aval devarim she-darkan lehagbiya behagbahah in bemeshikhah lo* — for objects normally lifted, only *hagbahah*, lifting, constitutes a valid *kinyan*; *meshicha* does not suffice. The reasoning is that for a light, easily portable object, picking it up is the genuine demonstration of ownership. Dragging such an object carries none of that significance.

### Wallet Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב אַבָּיֵי וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא; אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה לְאַבָּיֵי: הַגּוֹנֵב כִּיס בְּשַׁבָּת – חַיָּיב, שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְחַיֵּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּבֹא לִידֵי אִיסּוּר שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye sat and related this *halakha*. Rav Adda bar Mattana raised an objection to Abaye** from a *baraita*: **One who steals a purse on Shabbat** is **liable** for theft. Based on the principle that one who is liable to receive two punishments receives only the greater of the two, in this case one might think that he should be exempt from paying for the theft, as the performance of a prohibited labor on Shabbat is punishable by death. The reason he is liable in this case, in apparent contradiction to that principle, is **that he already became liable for** the **theft** as soon as he lifted the purse. This occurred **before he came to** violate **the prohibition** of performing prohibited labor on **Shabbat** by carrying the purse into the public domain.

Abaye was in the midst of teaching this ruling when Rav Adda bar Matna raised an objection from a *baraita* concerning the principle of *kam lei bid'raba minei*. That principle holds that when a single act simultaneously incurs both a monetary liability and a criminal penalty — such as lashes or death — the heavier penalty overrides the monetary obligation, and the offender is exempt from financial restitution.

The *baraita* applies this principle to a case of stealing a wallet on *Shabbat*. If someone steals a wallet and carries it out into *reshut ha-rabim*, he is nevertheless *chayav* to pay for the theft, because the two liabilities do not coincide in time: *she-kvar nit-chayev bi-gneivah kodem she-ba lidei issur Shabbat*. The act of *gneivah* is defined by the moment of *kinyan*, which occurs when he lifts the wallet — *hagbahah* — while still inside the owner's house. The *chiyuv Shabbat*, the Shabbat violation, is only triggered when he crosses the threshold from the private domain into *reshut ha-rabim*. Since the two occur at different moments, *kam lei bid'raba minei* does not apply, and the thief must pay.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מְגָרֵר וְיוֹצֵא מְגָרֵר וְיוֹצֵא – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהֲרֵי אִיסּוּר שַׁבָּת וּגְנֵיבָה בָּאִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If he did not lift the purse but **was dragging** it on the ground **and exiting** the private domain, continuously **dragging and exiting,** he is **exempt, as the prohibition** of performing labor on **Shabbat and** the prohibition of **theft** are violated **simultaneously** the moment he drags the purse out of the owner's property into the public domain. Therefore, he receives only the greater punishment, death, for carrying on Shabbat.

However, if the thief was dragging the wallet — *haya mgarer ve-yotze* — sliding it along the ground from inside the house until it crossed into *reshut ha-rabim*, then he is *patur* from paying: *she-harei issur Shabbat u-gneivah ba'in ke-echad*, the Shabbat violation and the act of *gneivah* coincide. He has not performed *hagbahah* at any earlier point; it is only when the wallet crosses from the *reshut ha-yachid* into *reshut ha-rabim* that both the *meshicha* (completing the acquisition for *gneivah*) and the Shabbat violation occur simultaneously.

One might wonder how *meshicha* is possible in *reshut ha-rabim*, since *meshicha* requires a domain suitable for *kinyan*. The *Gemara* addresses this: the dragging occurred in the *simta*, the alleyway alongside the public thoroughfare, which is treated as *reshut ha-rabim* for Shabbat purposes but as a valid domain for *kinyan* purposes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי כִּיס – דְּבַר הַגְבָּהָה הוּא, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי קָנֵי בִּמְשִׁיכָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּמִיתְנָא. אֲנָא נָמֵי בְּמִיתְנָא קָא אָמֵינָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּמִידֵּי דְּבָעֵי מִיתְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Adda bar Mattana explains his objection: **But a purse is an item that can be lifted, and even so** it is apparent from the *baraita* that one **acquires** it **by** means of **pulling.** How then can it be stated that items that are typically lifted are not acquired by pulling? Abaye **said to him:** The *baraita* is referring to a case where the thief pulled the purse **with a rope.** Rav Adda bar Mattana thought that Abaye meant that the thief happened to drag it with a rope, and he responded: **I also state** my question even in a case where he pulled the purse **with a rope,** as it is still evident that one can acquire the purse by means of pulling instead of lifting. Abaye **said to him:** I meant that the *baraita* is referring to **an item that requires a rope.** It is a purse that is so large and heavy that it cannot be lifted and must be pulled, therefore it is acquired by means of pulling.

The challenge this presents to Abaye's teaching is pointed: *ve-ha kis de-bar hagbahah hu* — a wallet is precisely the kind of object that one normally lifts up, yet the *baraita* treats *meshicha* as an effective *kinyan* for it. Abaye responds: *be-mista* — he was speaking of a case involving a rope. Rav Adda counters that a rope changes nothing if the wallet itself is the kind of object normally lifted. Abaye then clarifies: *be-midi de-ba'ei mista* — he means a bag so large and heavy that it requires a rope to drag, not a simple wallet at all. This is admittedly a forced *okimta*; the plain reading of the *baraita* is clearly a wallet. But Abaye's position is that *meshicha* is effective only for things not naturally lifted, and the case must be read accordingly.

### Different Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיַּגְבִּיהֶנָּה, אוֹ עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֶנָּה מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ. אַלְמָא, מִידֵּי דְּבַר הַגְבָּהָה – אִי בָּעֵי בְּהַגְבָּהָה קָנֵי לֵיהּ, וְאִי בָּעֵי בִּמְשִׁיכָה קָנֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי – מִידֵּי דְּבַר הַגְבָּהָה, בְּהַגְבָּהָה; מִידֵּי דְּבַר מְשִׁיכָה, בִּמְשִׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection. **Come** and **hear:** If the merchandise is **in the domain of** the **seller,** the buyer **does not acquire** the merchandise **until he lifts it or until he removes it from the domain** of the seller. **Apparently,** with regard to **an item that can be lifted, if he** so **desires he acquires it by lifting, and if he** so **desires he acquires it by pulling. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This is not a proof, as it can be explained that the *tanna* **teaches it disjunctively,** i.e., the two options are referring to two different cases: In the case of **an item that can be lifted,** he acquires it **by lifting,** whereas with regard to regard to **an item that can be pulled,** he acquires it **by pulling.**

A second challenge comes from a different direction. The *baraita* dealing with acquisition in the seller's domain states: *birshut mocher lo kana ad sheyigbihena o ad sheyotziena meirshuto* — the buyer does not acquire until he either lifts the item or removes it from the seller's domain. This seems to present a single object that can be acquired through either *hagbahah* or *meshicha* — two *kinyanim* applicable to the same item — which would undermine the distinction between naturally-lifted objects (only *hagbahah*) and naturally-dragged objects (only *meshicha*).

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *letzitadin katani* — the *baraita* is not presenting two options for the same object. It is speaking in a bifurcated fashion: *meidi debar hagbaha behagbaha, meidi debar meshicha bemeshicha* — things naturally lifted are acquired by *hagbahah*, and things naturally dragged are acquired by *meshicha*. The two clauses of the *baraita* address two different kinds of objects.

The *Rashbam* notes that one might have resolved the difficulty differently: perhaps the *baraita* speaks of a naturally-dragged object, and the teaching is that even such an object can be acquired through *hagbahah*, since lifting is always a meaningful expression of ownership. The *Gemara* does not pursue this reading, but the *Rashbam* acknowledges its theoretical plausibility — particularly given the assumption that *hagbahah* always works. Regardless, the *Gemara*'s chosen answer stands: different objects, not different options for the same object.

### Produce Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, מָשַׁךְ וְלֹא מָדַד – קָנֵי. וְהָא פֵּירוֹת, דִּבְנֵי הַגְבָּהָה נִינְהוּ, וְקָתָנֵי דְּקָנֵי בִּמְשִׁיכָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna (84b) that even items that are usually lifted can be acquired by means of pulling: With regard to **one who sells produce to another,** if the buyer **pulled** the produce **but did not measure** it, **he has acquired** it. The Gemara explains the proof: **But produce can be lifted, and** yet the mishna **teaches that it is acquired by** means of **pulling.**

The *Gemara* now pivots to test this framework. The *Mishnah* discusses *hamocher perot lechavero* — one who sells produce to a friend — and states that *meshicha* effects the *kinyan* even before measuring. The *Gemara* observes that produce is the sort of thing one normally lifts, and yet the *Mishnah* says *meshicha* works. How does this square with the teaching that objects normally lifted require *hagbahah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּשְׁלִיפֵי רַבְרְבֵי. אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ פִּשְׁתָּן מֵחֲבֵירוֹ – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיְּטַלְטְלֶנּוּ מִמָּקוֹם זֶה לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר. אַטּוּ פִּשְׁתָּן בִּשְׁלִיפֵי רַבְרְבֵי מִי לָא עָבְדִי?! שָׁאנֵי פִּשְׁתָּן – דְּמִשְׁתְּמִיט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with large bundles** that are pulled from place to place and are not carried, due to their size. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the latter clause** of the mishna: **One who buys flax from another has not acquired** it **until he carries it from this place to another place,** i.e., it is acquired only though lifting and not through pulling. **Is that to say** that **flax is not prepared in large bundles?** The Gemara answers: Yes, **flax is different.** Unlike other produce, flax is not packed in large bundles, **as it** would **slip** from its place. Rather, it is packed in small bundles, and therefore flax is acquired specifically through lifting.

The *Gemara* resolves this with a contextual qualification: *hacha bemai askinan beshlifi ravrevi* — the *Mishnah* is dealing with large sacks, quantities so heavy that lifting is impractical. When it is a matter of a massive sack of produce, *meshicha* is the natural act, and therefore *meshicha* effects the *kinyan*.

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from the *seifa*, the latter clause of the *Mishnah*, which distinguishes produce from flax: *halokeach pishtan mechavero lo kana ad sheyitaltlenu mimmakom zeh lemmakom acher* — one who purchases flax does not acquire until he moves it from one place to another. If both the produce case and the flax case involve large sacks — as the *Gemara* just established for produce — the contrast is difficult to explain. The *Gemara* asks: *atu pishta bi-shifa rabba mi lo avdi?* Can one not make large bundles of flax as well?

The *Gemara* answers: *shani pishta de-mishtamet* — flax is different in character; it frays or slips apart and cannot be gathered into large, consolidated sacks the way grain can. Therefore flax comes only in small bundles that are natural to pick up. The *Rashbam* explains that because flax only comes in small quantities, it is necessarily of the kind that one normally lifts — and therefore *meshicha* does not apply. The phrase *motzi mi-makom zeh le-makom acher* in the *Mishnah*, which one might have read as describing a more complete form of *meshicha*, is here reinterpreted: it means *hagbahah*, lifting and relocating. The two clauses of the *Mishnah* are thus distinguishing not between simple *meshicha* and complete displacement, but between large bags of produce (acquired by *meshicha*) and small bundles of flax (which require *hagbahah*).

### Animal Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְסִירָה, וְהַדַּקָּה – בְּהַגְבָּהָה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה – בִּמְשִׁיכָה. וְהָא בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה, דְּבַר הַגְבָּהָה הִיא, וְקָתָנֵי דְּקָנֵי בִּמְשִׁיכָה! שָׁאנֵי בְּהֵמָה, דְּסָרְכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Kiddushin* 25b): **Large domesticated animals are acquired through passing** the animal's leash to the buyer, **and small** domesticated animals are acquired **through lifting;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. And the Rabbis say: Small domesticated animals** are acquired **through pulling.** Ravina explains the proof: **But small domesticated animals are** creatures **that can be lifted, and** yet the mishna **teaches that** one **acquires** them **through pulling.** Rav Ashi rejected this proof: **Domesticated animals are different, as they cling** to the ground and it is difficult to lift them. Therefore, the usual manner of moving animals is to pull them.

Ravina raises one further challenge to Rav Ashi. A *Mishnah* in Kiddushin states: *behema gassa nikneit bi-mesira ve-ha-dakka be-hagbah divrei Rabbi Meir* — a large animal such as a cow or horse is acquired through *mesira*, handing over the reins, while a small animal is acquired through *hagbahah*, lifting, according to Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. The *Chachamim*, however, say: *behema dakka bi-meshicha* — a small animal may be acquired through *meshicha*, dragging.

The challenge is clear: a small animal is something one can lift, yet according to the *Chachamim* it may be acquired through *meshicha*. The *Gemara* defends the framework: *shani behema de-sarcha* — an animal is different, because it clings to the ground. The rule that naturally-lifted objects require *hagbahah* and not *meshicha* applies to objects where lifting is the most natural and easy act. An animal that resists, braces, and is difficult to lift is not in that category — leading or dragging it is the more natural act. This may in fact be the very point of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim*: Rabbi Meir holds that even for a small animal, where *hagbahah* is possible, it is required; the *Chachamim* hold that since an animal naturally resists being lifted, *meshicha* suffices.

## Sale Finality

### Kor Lump Sum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: "כּוֹר בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ" – יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ בִּסְאָה הָאַחֲרוֹנָה. "כּוֹר בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים – סְאָה בְּסֶלַע אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ" – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another case with regard to sales. **Rav and Shmuel both say:** If a seller said to a buyer: **I am selling you one *kor*,** a measure equivalent to thirty *se'a*, of grain **for** the price of **thirty** *sela*, the seller **can renege on** the sale as long as the measuring vessel is not filled, **even when** only **the last *se'a*** has yet to be measured, because he had agreed to sell only a complete *kor*. By contrast, if the seller said: **I am selling you one *kor* for thirty** *sela* and each ***se'a*** is sold **for one *sela*,** he cannot completely renege on the sale in the middle of the transaction. This is because the buyer **acquires** each *se'a* **one** by **one** as it is measured, since the seller sold each *se'a* individually.

The *Gemara* turns to a new ruling of Rav and Shmuel, who both say: *kor bi-shloshim ani mocher lach* — if a seller says "I am selling you a *kor* for thirty dollars," stated as a lump sum, then *yachol lachzor bo afilu ba-se'ah achrona* — either party may retract even when twenty-nine of the thirty *se'ah* have been measured. A *kor* contains thirty *se'ah*. When the sale is framed as a single unit — a *kor* for thirty dollars — the unit of sale is the entire *kor*, and until the full thirty *se'ah* have been delivered, the object of the sale has not yet been fully transferred. The *kinyan* is not complete on any partial amount. This is analogous to selling an apple and a pear together for a dollar: acquiring the apple alone does not complete the purchase, since the deal was structured as a combined unit. As the *Rashbam* notes, this differs from a case where the object is fully identified but unmeasured — there, if the buyer knows what he is purchasing, measurement is merely administrative, and the sale stands. Here, the delivery of the full *kor* is itself constitutive of the transaction.

### Per-Unit Pricing

The contrast case follows. If the seller instead says *kor bishloshim se'ah besela ani mocher lecha* — "I am selling you a *kor*, thirty *se'ah* for a *sela* each" — then *rishon rishon kanah*: each *se'ah* as it is measured becomes a separate completed sale. By identifying the price per *se'ah*, the seller has made each *se'ah* a discrete unit of transaction. As each *se'ah* is measured into the buyer's vessel, it is acquired independently and the sale on that unit is final.

### Vessel Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִם הָיְתָה מִדָּה שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן קָנָה. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נִתְמַלְּאָה הַמִּדָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty from the *baraita* cited on 85a. **Come** and **hear: If the measuring** vessel **belonged to one of them,** the buyer **acquires** the items of sale **one** by **one. And** since this *halakha* is stated in general terms, it indicates that the buyer acquires each item as it is placed in the measuring vessel, **even though the measuring** vessel **was not filled.**

The *Gemara* now draws on a *baraita* that was introduced earlier in the tractate, dealing with *arba midot bemochrin* — four cases of sellers — and the role of the measuring vessel. The *baraita* established that when the measuring cup belongs to a third party, acquisition depends on the vessel being fully filled. But if the measuring cup belongs to the buyer, *im hayetah midah shel echad meihem rishon rishon kanah* — the buyer acquires each portion as it enters the vessel, even before the vessel is filled.

The *Gemara* then notes: *ve'af al gav dela nitmala hamidah* — it works even before the measuring vessel is full. This might seem to contradict the rule of Rav and Shmuel, which holds that a lump-sum *kor* sale is not final until the last *se'ah*. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension.

### Sub-Unit Pricing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: "הִין בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר סְלָעִים – לוֹג בְּסֶלַע אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ"; וְכִדְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: שְׁנָתוֹת הָיוּ בַּהִין, הָכָא נָמֵי – שְׁנָתוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the seller **said to him: I am selling you one *hin*,** a liquid measure equivalent to twelve *log*, **for twelve *sela*,** each ***log* for one *sela*. And** this is **in accordance with** an observation **that Rav Kahana says:** In the Temple **there were markings on the** vessel that measured ***hin*,** with which one could measure the different libations. **Here too, there were markings on the measuring** vessels, and since the measuring vessel indicates at which point each *log* had been filled, the buyer acquires it. This is comparable to the case of one who sells each *se'a* individually.

The *Gemara* explains that the *baraita* is dealing with a specific formulation: *kegon de'amar leh hin bishneim asar log besela ani mocher lecha* — where the seller said "I am selling you a *hin* for twelve *sela*, one *sela* per *log*." In this framing, each *log* has been priced as an independent unit. Therefore, as each *log* of the *hin* flows into the measuring cup and reaches each gradation mark, the buyer acquires that unit. The *Gemara* concludes by invoking a statement of Rav Kahana: just as Rav Kahana noted in a related context that measuring vessels had gradation marks — *shenatot hayu bahen* — so too here the measuring cup has marked lines. When the price has been specified per sub-unit, and the vessel bears gradations corresponding to those units, each level reached constitutes a separate completed sale. It is the combination of per-unit pricing and identifiable sub-units that transforms what would otherwise be a single reversible transaction into a series of individually finalized ones.
