# Nazir 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Klal U'Prat & Ribuy U'Mi…

### Intro: Hermeneutics

The entirety of today's daf — Nazir 35a — is devoted to the hermeneutic rules of *klal u'prat* and *ribuy u'miut*, and it is worth pausing at the outset to review what was established the previous day, including a correction to one point that was misstated.

### Klal U'Prat Rules

The foundational rules of the *klal u'prat* school — the school of Rabbi Yishmael — are as follows. When a verse presents a *klal* (a broad, general statement) followed by a *prat* (a specific, limiting statement), the operative principle is *klal u'prat*: we are left with only what is in the *prat*. Conversely, when a *prat* precedes a *klal*, the *klal* expands upon the *prat*, yielding everything — that structure is *prat u'klal*. When all three elements appear — *klal*, *prat*, and *klal* again — the result is anything similar to the *prat*. The same obtains for a *prat klal u'prat* structure: the final *prat* pulls back from the universality that a bare *prat u'klal* would have yielded, and the result is again anything similar to the *prat*.

One can observe an underlying logic: if one ended with *prat u'klal*, the *klal* would swallow everything. The final *prat*, coming after the *klal*, contracts the scope back to similarity with the *prat*. Thus in a *klal u'prat u'klal*, the second *klal* broadens; in a *prat klal u'prat*, the second *prat* narrows. Both structures resolve to the same conclusion — anything similar to the *prat* — though as the *gemara* will note toward the end of the daf, there is a subtle difference in how closely the similarity must obtain.

### Ribuy U'Miut Rules

The *ribuy u'miut* approach — associated with Rabbi Akiva — operates on a different logic. When a verse begins with a broad statement (*ribuy*) and then narrows (*miut*), the two terms together already generate an abstraction: we derive not the literal scope of the narrow term, but anything similar to it. This is because the reader is already prompted to ask: why did the verse begin broadly if it only meant the narrow? The answer is that the *miut* functions as an illustrative example, and the *ribuy* signals that the category is broader than the example alone.

When the order is reversed — *miut* followed by *ribuy* — the verse ends on the broad term. According to this school, that broad term would, standing alone, have included everything. But the initial *miut* exercises some limiting force, pulling back from true universality. The result is: everything except that which is most dissimilar. This is a distinct category, parallel to *prat u'klal* in structure but not equivalent in outcome.

An important observation follows from this: the *ribuy u'miut* school already generates two types of abstraction from a two-term structure — one yielding anything like the *miut*, one yielding everything except what is most unlike. The function of a third term in this system is therefore not self-evident, and the *gemara* will address this directly.

### Miut V'Ribuy Correction

The correction from the previous shiur concerns *miut v'ribuy*. When a verse starts with the narrow and ends with the broad, the ending broad term does not produce simple universality. The initial *miut* exercises a limiting force, and the result — everything except that which is most dissimilar — is a category without a precise parallel in the *klal u'prat* system. The *klal u'prat* school's *prat u'klal* yields truly everything; *miut v'ribuy* does not. That distinction will become critical later in the daf.

## Nazir Vine Case

### Rabbis' Dispute

The *gemara* opens its analysis with the question: *be-mai kamiflagi* — in what do Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbanan fundamentally disagree? The answer is that they employ different hermeneutic schools entirely. Rabbi Eliezer applies *ribuy u'miut*; the Rabbanan apply *klal u'prat*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Approach

Rabbi Eliezer reads the Nazir passage as follows. The verse opens with *miyayin v'shechar yazir* — a Nazir shall abstain from wine and *shechar* — which is a *miut*, a narrow term. It then continues *mikol asher ye'aseh migefen hayayin* — from anything made of the grapevine — which is a *ribuy*, a broad term. Rabbi Eliezer focuses on these two terms and treats the structure as *miut v'ribuy*. The *ribuy* at the end — *riba kol milei* — includes virtually everything. The *miut* at the opening excludes only that which is most dissimilar: *miut shevishta*, the wood of the vine. As a result, Rabbi Eliezer holds that grape leaves are included in the Nazir's prohibition.

### Rabbanan Approach

The Rabbanan read the same passage through the lens of *klal u'prat*. They identify three structural elements: *miyayin v'shechar yazir* is a *prat*; *mikol asher ye'aseh migefen hayayin* is a *klal*; and *mechartzanim v'ad zag lo yochal* — from the pits to the skin he shall not eat — is again a *prat*. This yields a *prat klal u'prat* structure, and the operative rule is *i attah dan ela k'ein haprat* — you may only derive what is similar to the *prat*. The question then becomes: what is the *prat*? What does it teach us?

### Pri U'Psolet Pri

The abstraction extracted from the *prat* is: *pri u'psolet pri* — fruit and spoiled fruit. The grape exemplifies *pri*: it is a fruit. Even an unripe grape qualifies as *pri*, because the verse explicitly mentions *anavim lachim v'yeveshim* — fresh and dried grapes — elsewhere. Vinegar (*chometz*) exemplifies *psolet pri* — a degraded or spoiled form of the fruit. From these two poles, the Rabbanan derive that the prohibition encompasses anything that is either a grape-type fruit (even unripe) or a spoiled grape product. What it does not encompass are leaves, which bear no resemblance to *pri* or *psolet pri*.

One might ask: should the *prat* not require a fully ripened, perfect fruit — *pri gamur* — and limit the prohibition to that? The *gemara* rejects this: if the prohibition extended only to fully developed grapes and their products, the hermeneutic would yield nothing beyond what is already explicit in the verses. Since *anavim lachim v'yeveshim* and *yayin v'chometz* are already stated explicitly, a *prat* that teaches us only those things contributes nothing. The point of the hermeneutic is to broaden the category by abstraction — to the general type, including its marginal cases.

### Third Term Function

This raises a pointed question: why did the verse need the final *prat* — *mechartzanim v'ad zag* — at all? If the verse had ended with *prat u'klal*, the *klal* would have expanded the scope to include everything, and no limiting principle would have applied. The final *prat* is structurally necessary: it creates the *prat klal u'prat* formation and thereby prevents the *klal* from being read as universal. Without it, the *klal* would have been *mosif al haprat* — simply additive upon the *prat* — and the Nazir's prohibition would have extended to everything derived from the vine. The third term teaches us not only its own content, but the governing hermeneutic of the entire passage.

### Final Prat Effect

The *gemara* articulates this explicitly: *kol makom she-atta motzei prat u'klal she-hayah besofo prat, i attah rashai le-moshcho u-leduno k'ein ha-prat* — wherever you find a *prat*, a *klal*, and then again a *prat* at the end, you may not simply follow the *klal* as though it were all-inclusive; you must draw your conclusions in the manner of the *prat*. The verse *ad sheyifrot lecha ha-katuv k'derech she-parat lecha be-Nazir mechartzanim v'ad zag* — until the text gives you a second *prat* in the manner it did in the Nazir passage — demonstrates this principle.

### V'Ad Zag Explained

Beyond its structural role in the hermeneutic, the phrase *v'ad zag* carries independent content, as Ravina explains: *l'atuyei de-beini beinei* — to include what is in between. Tosafot offers two readings of this. The straightforward reading is that *chartzanim* are the pits and *zag* is the skin, and what lies between them is the flesh of the grape itself — the *beini beinei*. Tosafot notes, however, that this is somewhat puzzling as a novel teaching, since the flesh of the grape is the most obvious part of the fruit. The *hava amina* would be that eating the flesh alone, having scraped it out while leaving the pits and skin, might constitute a *shinuy* — an unusual mode of eating — that could exempt one from liability. The phrase comes to negate this.

Tosafot's second reading is more inventive: *beini beinei* refers to the small, undeveloped grapes that grow between the larger, mature grapes on the cluster. These grapes are shaded, never develop properly, and will never ripen — unlike ordinary unripe grapes, which are merely premature, these are constitutionally incapable of ripening. One might have thought they fall outside the prohibition; the phrase *v'ad zag* comes to include them. Of the two explanations, the first is simpler, though it requires a somewhat strained *hava amina*. The second requires a more precise reading of *beini beinei* as referring to a specific viticultural reality.

## Third Term Resolution

### The Problem

Having established how the Rabbanan's *prat klal u'prat* hermeneutic operates, the *gemara* raises a difficulty involving Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, as we know from the Mishnah, darshens the phrase *mechartzanim v'ad zag* to teach that a Nazir is not liable for eating pits or skin alone unless he has consumed two pits or one skin — a specific *halacha* about the minimum quantities that constitute liability when eating these parts of the grape individually. The difficulty is this: for those in the *klal u'prat* camp, the phrase *mechartzanim v'ad zag* is indispensable as the final *prat* in the *prat klal u'prat* structure. But Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah has already deployed the phrase for a different *drasha* entirely. Where, then, does he obtain the final *prat* needed to construct the *prat klal u'prat*?

### Answer 1: Dependent

The *gemara*'s first answer is elegant: *savar lah k'Rabbi Eliezer de-darish mi'ut v'ribuy* — Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds like Rabbi Eliezer, who applies the *ribuy u'miut* hermeneutic. For Rabbi Eliezer, recall, the operative terms are only the first two — *miyayin v'shechar* and *mikol asher ye'aseh* — and he derives his conclusion from a *miut v'ribuy* analysis without needing a third term. Rabbi Eliezer has no structural use for *mechartzanim v'ad zag* in his hermeneutic, leaving it available for a separate *halacha*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah steps in to supply exactly that: the phrase teaches the *halacha* of two pits and one skin. The two opinions thus complement each other perfectly. Rabbi Eliezer's hermeneutic leaves the third term unoccupied; Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah puts it to use.

The cost of this answer, however, is that it renders the two disputes interdependent. Accepting it means that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds like Rabbi Eliezer, and that the question of how many pits and skins one must eat is linked to the question of which hermeneutic school one follows. We would prefer not to tie these two debates together in that way.

### Answer 2: Dual Use

The *gemara*'s second answer is more nuanced: *ve-iba'it eima k'rabbanan* — one could equally say that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds like the Rabbanan, maintaining the *prat klal u'prat* hermeneutic. The key insight is that the phrase *mechartzanim v'ad zag* can simultaneously serve two distinct functions without being double-counted, because the two functions operate on different levels: its content yields the *halacha* of two pits and one skin, while its placement — appearing after the *klal* — signals that it functions as the final *prat* in the *prat klal u'prat* structure.

### Syntax & Semantics

This distinction between placement and content is what allows the *gemara* to defend the dual function. The *gemara* asks: *l'mai hilchata katveih batar klala* — why is this phrase written after the *klal*? The only purpose its placement serves is to generate the *prat klal u'prat* hermeneutic. Its content — the mention of pits and skin — teaches the *halacha* of two pits and one skin.

But then the *gemara* presses further: could we not say the entire phrase is there only for the *klal u'prat* hermeneutic — that it mentions pits and skin simply because those needed to be prohibited, and the *drasha* of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah is illegitimate? The *gemara* answers by pointing to a grammatical anomaly in the text: *chartzanim* is written in the plural, while *zag* is written in the singular — *shnei chartzanim v'zag echad*. If the phrase existed solely to prohibit pits and skin and to anchor the hermeneutic, there would be no reason for this asymmetry; one would have expected either both in the plural or both in the singular. The fact that one appears in the plural and the other in the singular signals a further *drasha*: two pits and one skin. Placement teaches the hermeneutic; singular-versus-plural usage teaches the *halacha*. The two functions are thus derived from distinct features of the text and do not compete.

### Linguistic Defense

This analysis prompted a natural concern: is it not an act of interpretive violence to the text to simultaneously read the placement of a phrase as hermeneutically significant and its internal grammar as halakhically significant, while treating these as independent layers of meaning? The concern is legitimate, and it resonates with broader questions about the *gemara*'s hermeneutic freedom. It is worth noting that this is, after all, a principle of Rabbi Yishmael — *klal u'prat* — and Rabbi Yishmael is also the one who holds *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam*, that the Torah speaks in ordinary human language. Whether these two commitments sit easily together is itself a question, and the *gemara* does not pause to resolve it. The two functions of the phrase — structural and lexical — are defended on their own terms, and the analysis moves forward.

## Ribuy U'Miut Concession

### Three Terms Query

Having clarified how Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah can hold like either Rabbi Eliezer or the Rabbanan without contradiction, the *gemara* turns to a broader theoretical question. Rabbi Elazar — the *amora* who applies *miut v'ribuy* — is asked: *prat klal u'prat minalei* — where does he, as a member of the *ribuy u'miut* camp, get the principle of *prat klal u'prat*? In other words, when does the *ribuy u'miut* school ever make use of the three-term hermeneutic of *prat klal u'prat*? The question is pointed: if the *ribuy u'miut* camp operates on two-term structures and achieves all necessary abstractions with just *ribuy* and *miut*, what role does a third term ever play for them?

### Empirical Silence

Tosafot frames the issue in an illuminating way. The *drasha* of *klal prat u'klal* — or its mirror *prat klal u'prat* — appears constantly throughout the *gemara*. Yet we essentially never find a dissenting voice from the *ribuy u'miut* camp saying: wait, I read this passage differently; using *ribuy u'miut* I reach a broader conclusion, and I have a superfluous third term on my hands. The silence is conspicuous. If the two camps truly operated in parallel with strict consistency, we would expect robust disputes wherever a three-term structure appears. Instead, the *klal u'prat* analysis dominates almost without challenge.

### Concession Theory

Tosafot's inference from this silence is that the *ribuy u'miut* camp concedes the *prat klal u'prat* hermeneutic when circumstances make their own approach impractical or inapplicable. When a three-term structure appears and the *ribuy u'miut* analysis cannot be cleanly performed — whether because a third term has been assigned to another *drasha*, or because the structure does not lend itself to a two-term *ribuy u'miut* reading — the *ribuy u'miut* camp accepts the *klal u'prat* method without disputing it. This is an assumption of practical flexibility rather than doctrinal rigidity.

### Schools Consistency

This observation connects to a broader scholarly point. There is an important book on this subject — Y. J. Harris, *How Do We Know This?* — which examines the different hermeneutic schools and their consistency of application. In the *Midreshei Halacha* and in the Yerushalmi, the schools are applied with considerable discipline. A *tanna* affiliated with the school of Rabbi Yishmael consistently applies *klal u'prat*; one affiliated with the school of Rabbi Akiva consistently applies *ribuy u'miut*. Crossing between schools is not attested in those sources. The Bavli, by contrast, applies different methodologies at different times, seemingly without concern for consistency. The same *amora* may be cited as applying *miut v'ribuy* in one tractate and *klal u'prat u'klal* in another, and the Bavli does not experience this as a contradiction.

### Consistency Issue

This pragmatism does at times drive Tosafot to considerable effort. In some cases, Tosafot notes that Rabbi Eliezer appears to employ *miut v'ribuy* in one passage and *klal u'prat u'klal* in another, and attempts to resolve the inconsistency — sometimes proposing that the *ribuy u'miut* hermeneutic is used when it can be, and *klal u'prat* is used when the *ribuy u'miut* approach is unavailable for technical reasons. Whether these resolutions are fully satisfying is another question, but they reflect the Bavli's characteristic willingness to allow a single authority's hermeneutic identity to remain somewhat fluid.

## Prat Klal Source

### Guardian Example

Given this assumption that the *ribuy u'miut* camp does at times concede the *prat klal u'prat* hermeneutic, the *gemara* asks: from where does such a camp know this principle at all? Where is the *pasuk* that exemplifies or grounds it? Rabbi Abahu answers that the principle is derived from the passage concerning the obligations of guardians (*shomrim*): *v'chi yiten ish el rei'ehu chamor o shor o seh* — if a man gives his fellow a donkey, an ox, or a sheep — which is a *prat*; *v'chol behemah* — and any animal — which is a *klal*; *lishmor* — to watch — which Tosafot, working from a different textual reading, identifies as a second *prat*. The structure would then be *prat u'klal u'prat*, and the principle *i attah dan ela k'ein ha-prat* is derived from it.

### Korban Example

Rava offers a different *pasuk*, drawn from the laws of sacrifices: *v'im min hatzon korbano* — if from the flock his offering — where *min*, meaning "from," functions as a *prat* (indicating a subset, not the whole); *hatzon* — the flock — is a *klal*; and *k'vasim v'izim* — lambs and goats — is again a *prat*. This yields *prat klal u'prat*, and from it the principle is derived: *i attah dan ela k'ein ha-prat*.

### Critique of Examples

Both of these examples are, frankly, strained. In the guardian passage, it is not entirely clear how *lishmor* functions as a *prat* in any natural reading of the text, and Tosafot's reliance on an alternate textual tradition (*girsa*) to make it work signals the difficulty. In Rava's example from the sacrifices passage, one can ask: what exactly is the hermeneutic teaching us here? Sheep and goats are already mentioned explicitly. What is the *klal* — the word *hatzon* — adding that is then pulled back by the final *prat*? Tosafot suggests that one might have thought *k'vasim v'izim* was limited to one-year-old animals, and that *hatzon* comes to broaden the category to include older animals as well. But this is a somewhat forced reading, and the broader difficulty remains: demonstrating that a *pasuk* can be parsed as *prat klal u'prat* does not prove that this hermeneutic principle was learned from that *pasuk*, since one could equally have applied *ribuy u'miut* to the same text. The *gemara* seems to be illustrating rather than proving, and the examples serve as concrete anchors for the principle rather than logically decisive demonstrations of it.

### Objection & Defense

Rav Yehuda midiskarta challenges Rava's choice of the *pasuk* from the flock: why not derive the principle instead from the earlier passage concerning burnt offerings (*olah*) from cattle, which reads *min* — a *prat*; *ha-behemah* — animals in general — a *klal*; *bakar v'tzon* — cattle and sheep — a second *prat*? Rava responds that this earlier *pasuk* could not serve as the source, because it is defective as an illustration of the principle.

### Behemah vs. Chayah

The reason the cattle-offering *pasuk* fails is that the word *behemah* — animals — when read as a *klal*, might be understood to include *chayah*, wild animals. If that were so, then the initial *min* — the *miut* — would not have the power to exclude *chayah* from the scope of the *klal*, because the *klal* would have already encompassed it explicitly. One would then be unable to demonstrate how the *prat klal u'prat* structure limits the category to items similar to the *prat*. Rava's response to this objection is that the *pratim* — *bakar v'tzon*, cattle and sheep — make clear through the *prat klal u'prat* logic itself that *behemah* here means domesticated animals only, not wild ones. The *pratim* define the scope of the *klal*, and by the time one has completed the hermeneutic, it is evident that *behemah* does not include *chayah*. This, however, amounts to saying that the *pasuk* is self-referential in a circular way, and the overall impression is that these sourcing attempts are acknowledged to be weak. The *gemara* asserts the *prat klal u'prat* principle's validity through these examples without providing a clean, transparent demonstration.

## Maaser Sheni Source

### Maaser Passage

The *gemara* then turns to a passage that far more naturally and clearly exemplifies the *klal prat u'klal* structure: the laws of *Maaser Sheni*, the second tithe, in Devarim. This passage is taught in a *braisa* and is transparently organized as *klal*, *prat*, *klal* — making it an ideal illustration of the hermeneutic.

### First Klal

The verse opens: *v'nasata ha-kesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshekha* — you shall spend the money on whatever your heart desires. This sweeping statement is the *klal*: it is broad, open-ended, and inclusive of anything one might want.

### Prat Examples

The verse then specifies: *ba-bakar u-va-tzon u-va-yayin u-va-sheichar* — cattle, sheep, wine, and mead. These specific items are the *prat*: they are concrete, particular examples drawn from distinct categories of food and drink.

### Second Klal

The verse closes: *u-v'chol asher tish'alekha nafshekha* — and whatever your soul requests. This is the second *klal*, restoring the broad framing with which the verse began.

### Abstraction Rule

The hermeneutic yields: *klal u'prat u'klal, i attah dan ela k'ein ha-prat* — anything similar to the *prat*. The *braisa* specifies the abstracted characteristics: *pri m'pri* — something that is itself born or produced from another organism (live-born animals, which reproduce from one another) — and *gidulei karka* — produce that grows from the ground (wine, grain, fruit). These two characteristics capture what the examples in the *prat* have in common.

### Exclusions

From these two criteria, certain items are excluded. Water and salt, though fundamental to sustenance, do not grow from the ground in the relevant sense; they are extracted, not cultivated. Fish raise a more complex case: they do not give live birth in the same manner as land animals, and whether they qualify as *pri m'pri* is questionable. The *gemara*'s application of the *pri m'pri* criterion appears to track live birth as the operative feature, which is why birds — whose eggs are fertilized internally before being laid — are included, while fish — which typically fertilize externally — present a harder case. The *gidulei karka* requirement excludes items like water and salt still more clearly. The *Maaser Sheni* passage, with its natural and transparent three-part structure, serves as the paradigmatic demonstration of *klal u'prat u'klal* and the abstraction it yields.

## Klal vs. Prat Outer

### Outer Klal Function

With the mechanics of both structures established, the *gemara* turns to a comparative analysis that clarifies the precise function of the outer terms in each structure. In *klal u'prat u'klal*, the question is: what does the second *klal* actually accomplish? If the *prat* defines the category and the hermeneutic yields anything similar to the *prat*, one might think the initial *klal* would have been sufficient.

### Broadening Effect

The answer is that the second *klal* — the outer *klal* — broadens the scope of inclusion beyond the literal items of the *prat*. Without it, the structure would be *klal u'prat* alone, yielding only the *prat* itself. The second *klal* signals that the *prat* is illustrative rather than exhaustive and that the category extends to anything resembling it.

### Outer Prat Function

The parallel question arises for *prat klal u'prat*: what does the second *prat* accomplish? Without it, the structure would be *prat u'klal* — which yields everything, since a *klal* following a *prat* is universally expansive.

### Limiting Effect

The second *prat* — the outer *prat* in this structure — performs the opposite function: it contracts the scope. It prevents the *klal* from being read as universally inclusive and requires that whatever is derived must bear similarity to the *prat*.

### Common Result

Notably, both structures — *klal u'prat u'klal* and *prat klal u'prat* — arrive at the same conclusion: anything similar to the *prat*. Yet they reach it by opposite paths. In *klal u'prat u'klal*, the outer term is a *klal* that broadens; in *prat klal u'prat*, the outer term is a *prat* that narrows. The outer term's role is always to modify the tendency of the preceding two-term structure, whether by expanding or contracting.

## Similarity Requirements

### Single Aspect Suffices

Having established that both three-term structures yield "anything similar to the *prat*," the *gemara* now asks whether there is any practical difference between them. The answer is yes, and it concerns the degree of similarity required. When the majority of terms in the structure are broad terms — as in *klal u'prat u'klal*, where two *klalim* surround one *prat* — the broadening tendency dominates. In such a structure, it is sufficient for an item to resemble the *prat* in even a single characteristic in order to be included. The similarity threshold is low.

### Dual Aspect Required

By contrast, when the majority of terms are limiting terms — as in *prat klal u'prat*, where two *pratim* surround one *klal* — the narrowing tendency dominates. In such a structure, for an item to be included, it must resemble the *prat* in two characteristics. A single point of similarity does not suffice. Tosafot attempts to reconcile this rule with some of the earlier applications in the *gemara*, where the *prat klal u'prat* analysis of the Nazir passage seemed to require only a single dimension of similarity — grape-type fruit — rather than two. The reconciliation is not fully satisfying, and the tension is noted without full resolution. The broad principle, however, stands: more *pratim* means a higher threshold of similarity required; more *klalim* means a lower one.

## Prat U'Klal vs. Ribuy

### Both Seem Universal

The *gemara* now addresses a final comparative question: *prat u'klal* — a two-term structure ending with a *klal* — yields everything, since the *klal* swallows the *prat* and expands without limit. But *miut v'ribuy* — the corresponding two-term structure in the *ribuy u'miut* school, also ending with a broad term — likewise appears to yield everything: *ribuy ha-kol*. If both structures produce universal inclusion, what distinguishes them?

### Dissimilarity Limit

The critical difference is that *prat u'klal* is genuinely and completely universal: it includes everything without exception, because the *klal* at the end is read as absolute. *Miut v'ribuy*, by contrast, does not include everything. Even though it ends with a *ribuy*, the initial *miut* retains limiting force. The result is: everything except that which is most dissimilar to the *miut*. The most unlike item is excluded. The *ribuy u'miut* school does not simply fold the *miut* into the *ribuy* and discard it; the *miut* informs the boundaries of the *ribuy* by identifying a class of items too unlike to qualify.

### Application Example

Applied to the laws of the Nazir: under *prat u'klal*, one would include even vine leaves and the woody branches of the vine — everything connected to the vine in any way. Under *miut v'ribuy*, the most dissimilar item — the wood of the vine — is excluded. Leaves (*alim*) and vine tendrils (*lulafim*) fall in between: they are close enough to the vine's fruit-bearing parts to qualify under *miut v'ribuy*, but the wood is too dissimilar and is excluded. This contrast between near-universal *prat u'klal* and near-but-not-quite-universal *miut v'ribuy* defines the practical difference between the two systems at their broadest reach.

## Similarity Requirement…

### Vine Products

Returning to the Nazir context with this framework in place, the *gemara* now works through the specific vine products. Under *ribuy u'miut*, vine leaves and tendrils (*lulafim*) are included — they are similar enough to the fruit-bearing parts of the vine — while the wood is excluded as most dissimilar. Under *prat klal u'prat*, by contrast, both leaves and tendrils are excluded, because they do not share the relevant characteristic of being *pri* or *psolet pri*. The two hermeneutic methods thus produce meaningfully different practical outcomes for items at the margins of the vine's components.

### Leaves Contradiction

This generates a tension that is worth flagging directly. Earlier in the *gemara*, Rabbi Eliezer's *miut v'ribuy* analysis was presented as including grape leaves in the Nazir's prohibition. Now the *gemara* states that *miut v'ribuy* excludes the leaves (*alim lo*) while including the vine tendrils (*lulafim in*). Tosafot attempts to resolve this by distinguishing between edible and inedible leaves: edible leaves, being more similar to fruit, are included under *miut v'ribuy*, while inedible leaves are excluded as sufficiently dissimilar. The wood is the most dissimilar of all and is always excluded.

### Unresolved Issue

The difficulty with Tosafot's resolution is that the *gemara* uses the same term — *alim*, leaves — throughout, without distinguishing between edible and inedible varieties. The same word that was used earlier to exemplify what *miut v'ribuy* includes is now used to exemplify what it excludes. The terminology is not internally consistent, and Tosafot's distinction, while logically available, is imposed onto the text rather than drawn from it. This tension is noted and left unresolved. The broad outlines of the two systems remain clear even if this specific application resists clean harmonization.

## Why Klal Needed

### The Question

A fundamental question arises about the *klal u'prat* hermeneutic itself. If the rule of *klal u'prat* is *ein ba-klal ela mah sheba-prat* — the *klal* yields only what is in the *prat* — then what purpose does the *klal* serve at all? Why should the Torah write a broad, general statement if the *prat* that follows it immediately overrides it and reduces the scope to the specific items listed? The *klal* adds nothing to the legal outcome. Could the Torah not have simply stated the *pratim* directly?

### Rhetorical Answer

Tosafot addresses this directly: *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of ordinary human speech. People naturally speak in generalities before specifying particulars. One begins with a broad category and then identifies the examples. This is how human language works, and the Torah accommodates that natural rhetorical pattern even when, technically, the *klal* adds no independent legal content.

### Yishmael Principle

This answer is especially fitting given that the *klal u'prat* hermeneutic is associated with Rabbi Yishmael, who is also the author of the principle *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam*. The two principles cohere: Rabbi Yishmael's school reads the Torah as a text that employs the conventions of human rhetoric, and the presence of a *klal* before a *prat* is simply the Torah following the natural human pattern of general-to-specific speech. That the *klal* carries no independent legal weight is not a flaw or superfluity; it reflects the Torah's use of ordinary language.

### Structural Signal

There is, however, a further function that the *klal* performs, beyond mere stylistic convention. The presence of the *klal* signals to the reader that a hermeneutic is in play. Without the *klal*, one would have only a list of *pratim* — specific items stated without any categorical framing. When the *klal* appears, it announces that these *pratim* are examples of a broader category and that the relationship between the general and the specific must be interpreted. The *klal* thus teaches hermeneutics through structure: it marks the text as requiring interpretive engagement, tells the reader that the items listed are not exhaustive but illustrative, and invites the application of the *klal u'prat* rules to determine what else falls within or outside the category. Without the *klal*, the *prat* would be read as a closed list. With it, the *prat* becomes the defining example of an analytically open category, governed by the rules of similarity and abstraction that the hermeneutic tradition provides.
