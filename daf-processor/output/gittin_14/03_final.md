# Gittin 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shachiv Mera & Maamad

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ – הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, וְאָמַר: ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״ – כָּשֵׁר. בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וְלֹא בְּפָנַי נֶחְתַּם – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who brings a bill of divorce from a country overseas and says: "It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence" – it is valid. It was written in my presence but it was not signed in my presence – it is invalid.**

The *mishna* at the opening of our discussion presents a fundamental distinction: if a *shachiv mera* — someone on his deathbed — commands that money be distributed, that instruction takes effect even after his death. By contrast, a *get* or *shtar shichrur* cannot be delivered once the sender has died. The *gemara* seeks to understand the precise legal basis for this distinction, and in doing so it unpacks two separate explanations for the *mishna*'s ruling.

### Shachiv Mera Explanation

The first explanation invokes the principle of *divrei shachiv mera kkesuvon u'kmesuran damu* — the words of a person on his deathbed are considered as though they were already written, signed, and delivered. This is a rabbinic construct granting the deathbed bequest immediate legal force. Under this reading, the instruction to distribute the money works after death because *Chazal* endowed such expressions with a kind of automatic efficacy from the moment they are uttered.

### Maamad Shloshtan

The second explanation does not require the speaker to be ill at all. Rather, it invokes the principle of *maamad shloshtan* — the three of them being present. The scenario is this: Reuven owes me money, and rather than collecting it myself, I instruct Reuven in Shimon's presence to pay that money directly to Shimon. All three parties — the creditor, the debtor, and the intended recipient — are in the room together at the same time. Under the principle of *maamad shloshtan*, this instruction effects a legally binding transfer.

### Mechanism Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, תְּנֵהוּ לוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי״, בְּמַעֲמַד שְׁלָשְׁתָּן – קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says that Rav says: "I have one hundred dinars in your possession; give it to so-and-so," if this occurred in the presence of all three parties, that third person has acquired it.**

The *gemara*, however, presses on the underlying mechanism. When the subject is a concrete object — a *pikadon*, a deposit that Reuven is physically holding — one can understand how it might be transferred: the object is in Reuven's possession and can be re-designated for Shimon's benefit. But *maamad shloshtan* applies even to an abstract *milve*, a monetary debt. What is the legal mechanism by which a debt owed to one party can be redirected to a third party simply because all three are present in the room? The *gemara* proposes several possible mechanisms but ultimately rejects each of them, leaving the question open as it transitions to Mar Zutra's formulation.

## Mar Zutra's Ruling

### Mar Zutra Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: הָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּהִלְכְתָא בְּלָא טַעְמָא: חֲדָא – הָא; וְאִידַּךְ – דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַכּוֹתֵב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְאִשְׁתּוֹ – לֹא עֲשָׂאָהּ אֶלָּא אַפִּיטְרוֹפְּיָא; וְאִידַּךְ – דְּאָמַר רַב חֲנַנְיָה: הַמַּשִּׂיא אִשָּׁה לִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל בַּבַּיִת – קְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: These three matters were instituted by the Sages as a halakha without a reason: One is this case of an acquisition in the presence of all three parties. And another is that which Rav Yehuda says that Shmuel says: One who writes over all of his property to his wife has rendered her only a steward [*eppitrofeya*], but she does not become the owner. And another is that which Rav Ḥananya says: With regard to one who marries a woman to his eldest son, and the wedding takes place in his house, the son has acquired that house.**

The *gemara* on 14a, approximately six lines in, records the teaching of Mar Zutra: *hani tlat mili shavyinhu rabbanan k'hilchata belo ta'ama* — these three matters the Rabbis established as operative *halachos* without a stated reason. The phrase *belo ta'ama* — without a reason — requires careful unpacking, and it is precisely here that *Tosafot* offers its important interpretive intervention.

### Tosafot Interpretation

*Tosafot* clarifies that *belo ta'ama* does not mean there is no purpose or benefit in having these institutions. Rather, it means there is no clear legal *mechanism* — no satisfying technical explanation for *how* they work. The motivation for enacting each of them is apparent enough. As *Tosafot* puts it: *aval ta'am yesh lama tiknu chachamim maamad shloshtan* — there is indeed a reason why the Rabbis instituted *maamad shloshtan*, namely *shelo yitzatarech litroach vela'asot kinyanim* — so that one would not need to go to the trouble of executing formal *kinyanim* in every commercial transfer. It was a practical and efficient mechanism for commerce. What is lacking is not the rationale for the enactment but the internal jurisprudential logic — the *mechanics* — of how it achieves its result.

### Three Enactments

The *gemara* enumerates three rabbinic enactments that share this character. The first is *maamad shloshtan* itself, as described above. The second is the rule stated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: if a man writes all his property over to his wife — *hakosev kol nechasav le'ishto* — we do not take the document at face value as a complete transfer of ownership. Instead, *lo asah ella apotropa* — he has effectively designated her as an *apotropa*, a kind of executor or power of attorney, not as the outright owner of his estate. The third enactment concerns the case of a father who marries off his son and holds the wedding celebration in a house built or dedicated specifically for the son.

### Enactments Details

*Tosafot* explains the logic underlying each of the latter two enactments, even as it acknowledges they function without a fully transparent mechanism. Regarding the wife as *apotropa*: *mishum dela shavik livrei ve'yahiv le'achrini* — we assume a man would not wish to disinherit his children entirely by giving everything to his wife, since it is not the usual practice to pass one's estate to a spouse rather than to one's descendants. Therefore, however the document reads on its face, we interpret the gesture as one of honor and authority — *lachalek lah kavod velashavya apotropa* — rather than as a genuine conveyance of title. The intent, while not provable from the document alone, is presumed from the social logic of the situation.

The third case — the *chuppah* transaction — similarly turns on inferred *da'at*. If a father holds the wedding party for his adult son in a house set aside specifically for that purpose, emptying it of his own possessions and dedicating it to the occasion, we presume that he has expressed a genuine intent to transfer ownership of the house to the son. In all three cases, the Rabbis drew on their understanding of what a person in such a situation would plausibly intend and declared those consequences to follow as a matter of *halacha*, even without a formally airtight legal mechanism.

## Tosafot Questions

### Refusal Question

Once *maamad shloshtan* is understood to operate as a rabbinic enactment without a clear technical mechanism, *Tosafot* raises a pair of consequential questions. The first: what happens if one of the three parties refuses? In the standard scenario, Reuven owes me money, and I instruct him — in Shimon's presence — to pay that money to Shimon instead. But what if Reuven refuses? Does *maamad shloshtan* still work? *Tosafot* suggests it might, because the principle does not depend on Reuven's cooperation — *Chazal* granted the creditor the authority to redirect the debt regardless of the debtor's preferences. The point of *maamad shloshtan* is precisely that no formal *kinyan* or consent mechanism is required.

### Non-Jewish Parties

The second question *Tosafot* raises is whether *maamad shloshtan* applies when one or more of the three parties is not Jewish. This is less a question of mechanism than of the enactment's scope — did the Rabbis institute *maamad shloshtan* universally, or only in transactions among Jews? *Tosafot* raises the question without resolving it definitively, leaving it as a matter of *halachic* speculation.

## Saffron Transaction

### Saffron Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַב אַחָא בַּרְדְּלָא: קַבָּא דְּמוֹרִיקָא אִית לִי גַּבָּךְ; יַהֲבֵיהּ לִפְלוֹנִי. בְּאַפֵּיהּ קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ דְּלָא הָדַרְנָא בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Rav Aḥa Bardela: I have a kav of saffron [*morika*] with you that you owe me; give it to so-and-so. I say this to you in his presence so that I will not retract from this statement.**

The *gemara* now introduces a practical illustration of *maamad shloshtan* from Rav's own conduct. Rav said to Rav Acha Bardla: *kava de'morika* — a *kav* of *morika*, which the *gemara* identifies as saffron, a commodity of extraordinary value, comparable by weight to gold even in antiquity (though *Tosafot* on 47a in Gittin notes an alternative identification as safflower, a cheaper substitute used for producing yellow dye). Rav told Rav Acha Bardla: *is li gabach* — you are holding this for me, and I want you to give it to a certain *ploni*. He said: *be'apei ka'amina lach* — I am telling you this in that person's presence, *delo hadarna bi* — and I will not retract. The *gemara* notes the apparent implication: the phrasing suggests that Rav was invoking the social pressure of saying this in front of the intended recipient as a personal guarantee against backing out, which would imply that *legally* he retained the right to retract. Why else emphasize the presence of the third party as a bulwark against reversal?

### Repetition Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אָמַר רַב חֲדָא זִימְנָא; דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, תְּנֵהוּ לוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי״, בְּמַעֲמַד שְׁלָשְׁתָּן – קָנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But why is this statement of Rav recorded at all? Rav already said this halakha once. As Rav Huna says that Rav says: "I have one hundred dinars in your possession, give it to so-and-so," if this occurred in the presence of all three parties that third person has acquired it.**

The *gemara* then asks: *ha amar Rav chada zimna* — but Rav has already taught this principle! The *gemara* cites the previously stated ruling: *de'amar Rav Huna amar Rav — maneh li b'yadcha tnahu l'ploni b'ma'amad shloshtan kana* — if you instruct the holder of your money to give it to a third party in the presence of all three, the acquisition is effective. If Rav has already articulated this *halacha*, what does the saffron incident add?

### Small vs. Large Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי מַתָּנָה מְרוּבָּה, אֲבָל מַתָּנָה מוּעֶטֶת לָא לִיבְעֵי בְּפָנָיו, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **If the method of acquisition in the presence of all three parties was derived from that halakha alone, I would say that this matter, i.e., the need for the presence of the third party, applies only for the transfer of a large gift. However, in the case of a small gift one does not need to do this in the presence of the recipient. Instead, the giver can simply provide instructions and thereby transfer the gift to the other person, as there is no reason to think that he would retract. Therefore, Rav teaches us that this is not the case, as in all cases the transfer takes effect only in the presence of all three parties.**

The *gemara* answers: the earlier ruling might have been understood to apply only to a large gift — *matana meruba*, like a *maneh* — on the theory that the gravitas of a substantial transaction justifies the special efficacy of *maamad shloshtan*. One might have thought that for a small gift — *matana mu'etet* — the presence of all three parties is not required. Rav's conduct with the saffron teaches that *maamad shloshtan* applies equally to smaller transfers. *Rashi* frames the smaller gift as the saffron itself, though as one student pointed out, saffron by weight is comparably priced to gold, making it not obviously a trivial amount. *Rashi*'s point is apparently about the relative categorization within the *gemara*'s framework, where a *maneh* is the paradigmatic large gift. The *gemara*'s answer accordingly clarifies that the requirement of all three parties being present — *bifanav* — holds regardless of the magnitude of the transfer.

## Gardeners Case

### Account Settlement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ גִּינָּאֵי דַּעֲבֻיד חוּשְׁבָּנָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. פָּשׁ חֲמֵשׁ אִיסְתְּרֵי זוּזֵי גַּבֵּי חַד מִנַּיְיהוּ, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״יַהֲבִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְמָרֵי אַרְעָא״ – בְּאַפֵּי מָרֵי אַרְעָא, וּקְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There were certain gardeners who made a calculation of money owed to each other, and there remained with one of them five *isterei zuzei* more than he should have received. The others said to him: Give the money to the owner of the land, and they said this in the presence of the owner of the land, and the owner of the land acquired the money from him. In other words, the owner of the land performed a proper act of acquisition obligating the gardener to pay that amount.**

The *gemara* now turns to a vivid practical illustration. A group of gardeners — sharecroppers working land together — sat down to balance their accounts with one another after the growing season, tallying who had borrowed what and who owed what to whom. At the end of the accounting, five *istarei zuzei* — five units of a lesser coinage — remained outstanding, owed by one of them to the group collectively. The group decided that rather than collecting those five units among themselves, they would give the amount as a bonus to *marei ar'a*, the owner of the land. They declared this in the landowner's presence — *be'apei marei ar'a* — and the debtor even performed a *kinyan sudar*, a handkerchief transfer, to underscore his commitment. This constituted a classic *maamad shloshtan*: the group's claim against the debtor was redirected to a third party — the landowner — in the presence of all three parties.

### Mathematical Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אֲזַל עֲבַד חוּשְׁבָּנָא בֵּין דִּילֵיהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, לָא פָּשׁ גַּבֵּיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ultimately, that gardener went and performed a calculation on his own and concluded that nothing extra had been left over with him.**

Subsequently, the debtor reviewed the books and discovered that the original accounting had been mistaken. He did not owe the group any money at all. *Tosafot* explains that either the books themselves made this self-evident, or the other gardeners acknowledged the error upon examination — in any event, the mistake was not contested. The question then became: having already performed *maamad shloshtan* and having done a *kinyan sudar*, was he still obligated to pay the five units to the landowner?

### Nachman's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֶיעְבֵּיד לָךְ? חֲדָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב. וְעוֹד, הָא קְנוֹ מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **He came before Rav Naḥman to ask what to do. Rav Naḥman said to him: What can I do for you? The first issue is that Rav Huna said that Rav said that an acquisition in the presence of all three parties takes effect immediately, and therefore the owner of the land has already acquired the money. And additionally, he performed an actual act of acquisition and thereby acquired the money from you.**

The parties came before Rav Nachman, who ruled: *mai avid lachu* — there is nothing I can do for you, you must pay. His reasoning was twofold: first, the principle of *maamad shloshtan* itself — as stated by Rav Huna in the name of Rav — effects a binding transfer; second, and independently, the debtor had performed a *kinyan*, which creates its own binding obligation. You committed yourself, said Rav Nachman, and you cannot retract.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אַטּוּ הַאי, מִי קָאָמַר ״לָא יָהֲבִינָא״?! ״דְּלֵיכָּא גַּבַּאי״ קָאָמַר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, קִנְיָן בְּטָעוּת הוּא, וְכׇל קִנְיָן בְּטָעוּת חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: Is that to say that the gardener said: "I will not give"? In other words, he did not refuse to give a sum in which he was obligated. He actually said: "Which is not in my possession," as his colleagues told him to give five *isterei zuzei* that remained in his possession, and he now claims that this money actually belongs to him. Rav Naḥman said to him: If so, it is an acquisition performed in error, and any acquisition performed in error reverts back to the previous owner.**

Rava immediately challenged this ruling. The debtor, he pointed out, was not saying *lo yahvina* — I refuse to pay. He was saying *delika gabai* — I do not actually have those funds. He had agreed to transfer money he *believed* he owed; it turned out he owed nothing. This is not a case of refusing to honor *maamad shloshtan* or reneging on a *kinyan* — it is a case where the entire transaction was premised on a factual error. Upon hearing this clarification, Rav Nachman reconsidered: *im ken, kinyan bete'ut hu* — if that is the case, then this is a *kinyan* executed on the basis of a mistaken assumption, and *kol kinyan bete'ut chozer* — any *kinyan* rooted in a fundamental mistake is void. The case thus illustrates both the power of *maamad shloshtan* and its limits: it can only transfer what actually exists as a valid underlying obligation.

## Holich vs. Ten

### Holich Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לוֹ״, אָמַר רַב: חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ; וְאִם בָּא לַחְזוֹר – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, אִם בָּא לַחְזוֹר – חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated that if someone instructs an agent: "Deliver one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as I owe him that sum," Rav says: The person who sends him the money bears financial responsibility for their loss. If the one hundred dinars are lost by the agent, the sender must give another one hundred dinars to repay his debt. But if the sender seeks to retract this repayment after he has given it to the agent, he cannot retract it. And Shmuel says: Since the sender bears financial responsibility for their loss, this indicates that the one hundred dinars are considered to be in his domain, which means that if he seeks to retract he can retract it.**

With the analysis of *maamad shloshtan* complete, the *gemara* transitions to a distinct but related set of questions about the language used when instructing a messenger to deliver funds. Recall that the *gemara* earlier established the principle of *ten kezachi*: when I say *ten* — give — to a messenger, that word functions as an authorization for the messenger to take possession on behalf of the recipient, grounded in the principle of *zachin leadam shelo befanav* — one may acquire something on behalf of another person even in their absence. The question now is whether the word *holich* — deliver, or go bring — carries the same legal weight. Is *holich kezachi*?

This question is not merely semantic. Two distinct issues converge. First, does my use of the word *holich* indicate that I *want* the messenger to take possession on behalf of the recipient, or does it merely instruct conveyance, leaving possession in my hands until delivery? Second, even setting aside my intent, is it actually in the recipient's interest for the messenger to take full possession on his behalf? If so, *zachin leadam shelo befanav* would support it; if not, it would not.

### Rav's Position

Rav rules: *chayav be'achrayuso* — the sender remains responsible if the money is lost. The debt has not yet been paid. And yet — *ve'im ba lachazor eino chozer* — if the sender wishes to retract, he cannot. This is a striking intermediate position. The messenger holds the money in a way that prevents the sender from demanding it back, but the recipient has not yet acquired it sufficiently for the debt to be discharged. If the money is lost in transit, the debtor still owes the creditor. The messenger has taken a *partial* possession — enough to block retraction, not enough to complete payment.

### Shmuel Opposition

Shmuel disagrees. His position is that there is no logical space for such a middle state. *Mitoch shechayav be'achrayuso* — since the sender remains liable if the money is lost — it follows that the money is still legally his. And if it is still his, *iy ba lachazor chozer* — he retains the right to retract. You cannot simultaneously say the money is not yet the recipient's and also that the sender cannot take it back. Either the debt is paid and the sender is off the hook, or it is not paid and he retains full control. There is no halfway position.

### Middle Position Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ״ כִּ״זְכִי״ דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ״ לָאו כִּ״זְכִי״ דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say that they disagree about this issue, as one Sage, Rav, holds that saying: "Deliver, is like saying: "Acquire," and therefore the agent immediately acquires the money on behalf of the recipient. For this reason the sender cannot retract. And one Sage, Shmuel, holds that saying: "Deliver, is not like saying: "Acquire," and as the recipient has not yet acquired the money the sender can retract.**

The *gemara* explores whether the Rav-Shmuel dispute turns on the question of *holich kezachi* directly — that is, whether Rav holds *holich kezachi* and Shmuel holds *holich lav kezachi* — or whether the disagreement is at a deeper level. The *gemara* concludes that in fact both Rav and Shmuel may agree that *holich* functions like *zachi* in the sense that the sender is prepared to have the messenger represent the recipient's interest. The actual dispute, the *gemara* suggests, is whether a middle state of partial possession is legally coherent.

Rav's position is that such a middle state is possible: the messenger takes possession on the recipient's behalf in a limited fashion — sufficient to prevent retraction, insufficient to discharge the debt. This serves the recipient's interest optimally: he is protected from the sender changing his mind, while also protected from bearing the risk of loss in transit. Shmuel's counter — characterized by the *gemara* using the term *migo*, though not in its usual technical sense of a credibility argument — is that this intermediate state simply cannot exist as a matter of legal logic. If you have not yet paid the debt, the money remains yours, and you may reclaim it. Shmuel's *migo* here simply means: you cannot have it both ways. Partial possession is not a recognized category.

It is worth noting that within this framework, *ten* — "give" — has already been firmly established as *kezachi*: if I say *give* this money to Ploni, the messenger takes full possession on Ploni's behalf immediately. The ongoing question throughout this section of the *gemara* is specifically whether *holich* — the language of delivery rather than giving — carries the same force.

### Braita for Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: ״הוֹלֵךְ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לוֹ״; ״תֵּן מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לוֹ״; ״הוֹלֵךְ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי – פִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בְּיָדִי״; ״תֵּן מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי – פִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בְּיָדִי״ – חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, וְאִם בָּא לַחְזוֹר – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in a baraita in accordance with the opinion of Rav: If one said to another: "Deliver one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as I owe him that sum," or if he said: "Give one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as I owe him that sum," or if he said: "Deliver one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as they are a deposit he has in my possession," or: "Give one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as they are a deposit he has in my possession," in all these cases the sender bears financial responsibility for their loss, but if he seeks to retract he cannot retract.**

The *gemara* cites a *braita* in support of Rav's position. The *braita* states that whether one says *holich maneh l'ploni she'ani chayav lo* — deliver this *maneh* to Ploni whom I owe — or *ten maneh l'ploni she'ani chayav lo* — give this *maneh* to Ploni whom I owe — and whether the underlying asset is a monetary debt or a *pikadon* held on deposit, in all cases *chayav be'achrayut* — the sender remains responsible if anything is lost — *ve'im ba lachazor eino chozer* — and he cannot retract. This *braita* supports Rav's position that *holich* and *ten* are legally equivalent: both create the binding intermediate state Rav described.

## Pikadon Holding

### Pikadon Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּקָּדוֹן, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אֵין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁיְּהֵא פִּקְדוֹנוֹ בְּיַד אַחֵר! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כְּשֶׁהוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן.
>
> **Translation:** **In a case where the agent accepts upon himself to bring a deposit to its owners, let the sender, who is the bailee of the deposit, say to the agent: "It is not the will of the owner of the deposit that his deposit will be in the possession of another." Since this transfer is not in the interests of the owner of the deposit, the money should remain fully in the possession of the sender, and therefore he should be able to retract. Rabbi Zeira says: This is referring to a case where the sender, the bailee of the deposit, is established as a denier of financial obligations.**

The *braita*'s extension of this rule to the case of a *pikadon* — a concrete object held on deposit — raises a sharp question. When the *braita* says the sender cannot retract even in the case of a *pikadon*, this requires justification. In the monetary-debt scenario, one can argue that the messenger automatically represents the creditor's interest because it is beneficial to the creditor to have someone holding the money on his behalf. But in the case of a *pikadon* — say, a valuable silver watch that the debtor has been holding and now wishes to return through a messenger — can we truly say the messenger represents the recipient's interest? The recipient may well say: I did not appoint this messenger as my *shaliach*. I only trust the original holder. I would prefer the watch to remain where it is rather than have it pass through someone else's hands. In that case, is it not in the recipient's interest to say *ein retzono sheyehei pikdono beyadecha* — I do not want my deposit in your hands — and demand that the sender take it back and return it himself?

### Zeira Exception

Rav Zeira provides the answer: this concern is valid in the normal case, but the *braita* is speaking of an exceptional circumstance — *keshehuchzak kafran* — when the holder of the deposit has established himself as one who denies the deposit's existence. In such a case, the holder had been consistently lying, telling the owner that he had no such watch, denying all knowledge of the deposit whenever asked. Then his conscience impelled him to arrange for its return through a messenger. In this situation, it is manifestly in the owner's interest for the messenger to hold onto the *pikadon* and refuse to return it to the denying party. The concern that the recipient might not want a third party handling his property is overridden by the far greater concern that the *pikadon* would disappear again if returned to someone who has already demonstrated the willingness to deny its existence. Thus the *braita*'s ruling stands: even in the *pikadon* case, there are circumstances where the messenger's holding is clearly in the recipient's best interest, and in those circumstances the sender cannot retract.

## Sheshet Collection

### Collection Instructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת הֲוָה לֵיהּ אֲשַׁרְתָּא דְּסַרְבָּלֵי בְּמָחוֹזָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא: בַּהֲדֵי דְּאָתֵית, אַיְיתִינְהוּ נִיהֲלִי. אֲזַל, יַהֲבִינְהוּ לֵיהּ. אָמְרִי לְהוּ: נִיקְנֵי מִינָּךְ; אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet had a credit [*asharta*] of cloaks [*sarbelei*] in the city of Meḥoza, i.e., he had a set agreement with merchants that he would give them cloaks to sell in the city of Meḥoza and they would later reimburse him. Rav Sheshet said to Rav Yosef bar Ḥama: When you come here from Meḥoza, bring me the money. Rav Yosef went and brought the money to him. Those who gave him the money said to him: Let us make an acquisition for what you have received, so that if something happens to the money, you will be responsible. He said to them: Yes.**

The *gemara* now presents a series of practical cases that illuminate the dynamics of the *shaliach*'s role in debt collection. Rav Sheshet had sold *sarbale* — coats, as *Rashi* explains — in Machuza on credit, and he was seeking to collect from his debtors. Rav Yosef bar Chama happened to be traveling to Machuza, so Rav Sheshet asked him: on your way back, *aitinhu nihali* — please collect the money and bring it to me. This was an explicit appointment of a *shaliach* for collection — no principle of *zachin leadam shelo befanav* was even needed, since Rav Yosef was explicitly authorized.

### Kinyan Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אִישְׁתְּמִיט לְהוּ. כִּי אֲתָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ, דְּלָא שַׁוִּית נַפְשָׁךְ ״עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה״. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ; ״עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ultimately, he escaped them and did not perform the act of acquisition. When Rav Yosef came before Rav Sheshet and told him what had occurred, Rav Sheshet said to him: You did well, as you did not render yourself the subject of the verse: "The borrower is a slave to the lender" (Proverbs 22:7), by obligating yourself for no reason. In another version of this incident Rav Sheshet said to Rav Yosef: You did well, as the verse states: "The borrower is a slave to the lender," i.e., the merchants who borrowed from me must bear responsibility to ensure that the money will reach me.**

Rav Yosef went to the debtors, who paid him. But then the debtors asked: *nikni minach* — do a *kinyan* with us, a *kinyan sudar*, agreeing that we are now fully discharged and that if anything happens to the money you are now responsible. Rav Yosef said he would, but in the end *ishtamit lehu* — he evaded them, and never actually completed the *kinyan* that would have considered the debt paid upon handover to him. He thus preserved the optimal position for Rav Sheshet: he held the money, but the debtors remained liable until it was actually delivered.

### Sheshet's Praise

When Rav Yosef returned to Rav Sheshet, Rav Sheshet praised him: *shappir avadt* — you did well not to agree to assume that liability. The reasoning: *delo shaveit nafshach eved loveh le'ish malveh* — you should not have made yourself into a debtor to those debtors, you should not have placed yourself in their debt by agreeing to bear responsibility for the money. The *pasuk* teaches that a borrower is like a servant to a lender. Rav Sheshet's instruction was that a *shaliach* collecting a debt should not agree to take on the debtor's liability. The debtors remain in the position of obligation until the creditor himself receives the money. An alternative version of Rav Sheshet's praise makes the point from the creditor's side: those people are *my* servants until the debt is paid — why should you relieve them of that obligation?

## Goblet Case

### Goblet Retrieval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַחַי בְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה הֲוָה לֵיהּ אִיסְפְּקָא דְכַסְפָּא בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בַּר רַבִּי יַנַּאי וּלְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר כֵּיפַר: בַּהֲדֵי דְּאָתֵיתוּ, אַתְיוּהּ נִיהֲלִי. אֲזוּל, יַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥai, son of Rabbi Yoshiya, had a silver vessel [*ispeka*] in the city of Neharde'a. Rabbi Aḥai said to Rabbi Dostai bar Rabbi Yannai and to Rabbi Yosei bar Keifar: When you come from Neharde'a bring the vessel to me. They went to Neharde'a and the people who were in possession of the vessel gave it to them.**

A further illustration comes from the case of Rav Achai berabi Yoshiya, who had a silver goblet — an *ishpaka de-chaspa* — being held for him in Nahardea. He asked two rabbis, Rav Dostai berabi Yannai and Rav Yosef berabi Keifar, to retrieve it on their way back from Nahardea. They went, and the holders of the goblet gave it to them.

### Kinyan Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לְהוּ: נִקְנֵי מִינַּיְיכוּ; אָמְרִי לְהוּ: לָא. אָמְרִי לְהוּ: אַהְדְּרֵיהּ נִיהֲלַן. רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין; רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר כֵּיפַר אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **After handing over the vessel, those people said to them: Let us perform an act of acquisition with you, so that you will be responsible for the vessel until you reach Rabbi Aḥai. The agents said to them: No; we do not want to do this. They said to them: If so, return the vessel to us, as we do not wish to transfer it in such a manner that we retain responsibility. Rabbi Dostai, son of Rabbi Yannai, said to them: Yes, I am willing to return it. However, Rabbi Yosei bar Keifar said to them: No, as you do not have the right to retract in this situation.**

Then the holders had second thoughts and said: *nikni minaychu* — do a *kinyan* with us, confirming that we are now discharged and that responsibility for the goblet has passed to you. Rav Yosef berabi Keifar refused, correctly holding to the position we have been tracing: the messenger should not agree to release the holders from responsibility, as this would allow a middle state where he holds the goblet without the debt being considered repaid. The holders then demanded the goblet back. Rav Yosef refused to return it as well, correctly recognizing that he was entitled to hold it in the best interest of the owner without yielding either to the kinyan demand or to the retraction request. Rav Dostai berabi Yannai, however, agreed to return it.

### Pressure Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲווֹ קָא מְצַעֲרוּ לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי מָר הֵיכִי קָא עָבֵיד! אֲמַר לְהוּ: טָב רְמוֹ לֵיהּ. כִּי אֲתוֹ לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי מָר, לָא מִיסָּתְיֵיהּ דְּלָא סַיְּיעַן, אֶלָּא אֲמַר לְהוּ נָמֵי ״טָב רְמוֹ לֵיהּ״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי תֶּיעְבֵּד הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** **They tormented Rabbi Yosei bar Keifar to force him to agree to return the vessel. Rabbi Yosei bar Keifar said to Rabbi Dostai: See, my Master, what they are doing to me. Rabbi Dostai said to them: You are acting well; hit him. When these two agents came before Rabbi Aḥai, Rabbi Yosei bar Keifar said to him: See, my Master, not only did Rabbi Dostai not support me; rather, he even said to them: You are acting well; hit him. Rabbi Aḥai said to him: Why did you do this?**

Rav Yosef berabi Keifar then came to Rav Dostai and said: *chazi mar* — look what they are doing to me, they are beating me up. Remarkably, Rav Dostai responded: *tov* — good, let them beat you, you should give it back. When they later came before Rebbi Oshaya and Rav Yosef complained — *lo mistaya d'lo siyan ela amar lehu nami tov ramu lei* — not only did Rav Dostai fail to help me, he told them to keep beating me — Rav Dostai explained his reasoning. The people they were dealing with, he said, were men of enormous physical stature, with thunderous voices and terrifying names — Arda, Arta, and Pili Barish — and with connections to the government, horses and mules at their command, and the effective power to execute anyone they wished. *Iy harguvei l'Dustai, mi nasan l'Yannai abba bar kamosi* — if they had killed me, who would have given my father Yannai a replacement son?

### Practical Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנֵי אָדָם הַלָּלוּ קְרוֹבִים לַמַּלְכוּת הֵן? אָמַר לֵיהּ: הֵן. יֵשׁ לָהֶם סוּסִים וּפְרָדִים שֶׁרָצִים אַחֲרֵיהֶן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥai said to him: Are these people close to the government? Rabbi Dostai said to him: Yes. Rabbi Aḥai asked him: Do they have horses and mules that run after them, i.e., do they have servants to perform their bidding? Rabbi Dostai said to them: Yes. Rabbi Aḥai said to him: If so, you acted well, as the situation was entirely out of your control.**

The concluding exchange underscores the lesson. When Rav Dostai was asked whether these men had governmental connections, horses, and mules, and he confirmed that they did, the response was: *shappir avadt* — then you did the right thing, you should have given back the goblet. The principle being illustrated here is not only the legal rights of the *shaliach* but the practical wisdom that legal entitlements must be weighed against real-world circumstances. The *gemara* presents this with some humor, but the underlying point is serious: a *shaliach* operating under genuine physical threat is not bound to enforce his principal's interests at the cost of his life.

## Holich Kizachi

### Recipient Death Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הוֹלֵךְ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי״, וְהָלַךְ וּבִקְּשׁוֹ וְלֹא מְצָאוֹ – תָּנֵי חֲדָא: יַחְזְרוּ לַמְשַׁלֵּחַ; וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לְיוֹרְשֵׁי מִי שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּלְחוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to one who said to his agent: "Deliver one hundred dinars to so-and-so, and the agent went and searched for that person but did not find him, as he had died, it is taught in one baraita: The money should be returned to the sender. And it is taught in another baraita: The money should be given to the heirs of the one to whom it was sent.**

The *gemara* returns to the foundational question of *holich kezachi*. The scenario under consideration is as follows: a sender instructs a messenger — *holich maneh l'ploni* — deliver this *maneh* to Ploni. The messenger goes to find Ploni but cannot, because Ploni has died. The question is what happens to the money. Two *braitot* appear to contradict each other: one says *yachzeru lameshale'ach* — it goes back to the sender; the other says *le'yorshei mi shenishtalach lo* — it goes to Ploni's heirs.

### Holich Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ״ כִּ״זְכִי״, וּמָר סָבַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ״ לָאו כִּ״זְכִי״?
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say that they disagree about this, as one Sage, in the second *baraita*, holds that in the case of a gift, saying: "Deliver, is like saying: "Acquire," and consequently the agent took possession of the money on behalf of the one to whom it was sent. Therefore, the heirs inherit this item. And one Sage, in the first *baraita*, holds that in the case of a gift, saying: "Deliver, is not like saying: "Acquire," and therefore the agent must return the money to the sender.**

The simple reading of this dispute would be that it maps directly onto the question of *holich kezachi*: if *holich* is *kezachi*, then Ploni acquired the money the moment the sender said *holich*, and when Ploni died, his heirs inherited it; if *holich* is not *kezachi*, then Ploni never acquired it, and it returns to the sender. But the *gemara* does not accept this simple mapping.

### Memel Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״הוֹלֵךְ״ לָאו כִּ״זְכִי״; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא בְּבָרִיא, הָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel said: No; everyone agrees that in the case of a gift, saying: "Deliver, is not like saying: "Acquire, and the apparent contradiction between the two *baraitot* is not difficult: This *baraita*, which says that the agent must return the money to the sender, is dealing with a case where the giver was a healthy person when he issued the instructions. Such a gift is acquired by the recipient only once it reaches his possession, and this recipient died before the gift reached him. Conversely, that *baraita*, which says that he should give it to the heirs of the one to whom it was sent, deals with the instructions of a person on his deathbed. Since the gift of a dying person is immediately acquired by the recipient, this recipient acquired it straightaway, and therefore it must be given to his heirs.**

Abba bar Memel resolves the apparent contradiction by asserting: *uvechuli alma holich lav kezachi* — in fact, *everyone* holds that *holich* is not *kezachi*. This is a striking reversal of what the *gemara* had seemed to assume throughout the earlier discussion. If that is so, what is the case where the money goes to Ploni's heirs? Abba bar Memel explains: *velo kashya — ha b'bari, ha b'shachiv mera* — the two *braitot* are not in conflict at all. The case where the money goes to the heirs is a case of *shachiv mera* — where the sender was on his deathbed when he gave the instruction. A deathbed bequest takes effect immediately upon utterance, so Ploni owned the money the moment the *shachiv mera* spoke. When Ploni subsequently died, his heirs inherited what was already his. The case where the money returns to the sender is an ordinary, healthy person — a *bari* — instructing delivery, where *holich* is not *kezachi* and Ploni never acquired it.

### Alive vs. Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע; הָא – דְּאִיתֵיהּ לִמְקַבֵּל בִּשְׁעַת מַתַּן מָעוֹת, הָא – דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לִמְקַבֵּל בִּשְׁעַת מַתַּן מָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said: This *baraita* and that *baraita* are both dealing with a case where the giver was a person on his deathbed. Instead, the difference between the two sources is that this *baraita*, which states that he should give it to the recipient's heirs, is referring to a case where the recipient was alive at the time of the giving of the money. Consequently, when the sender gives the money to the agent the recipient immediately acquires it, and his heirs claim it in his stead. That *baraita*, which says that the agent must return the money to the sender, is referring to a case where the recipient was not alive at the time of the giving of the money, and consequently the agent could not acquire the money on his behalf.**

Rav Zevid offers an alternative resolution that also preserves the principle that *holich lav kezachi*. He maintains that both *braitot* address cases of *shachiv mera*, and that the distinction lies in the timing of Ploni's death relative to the moment of the gift. If Ploni was alive at the time the *shachiv mera* gave the instruction — *de'isa lemakabel b'sha'as matnaso* — then the deathbed bequest took effect with Ploni as its beneficiary, and when Ploni died, his heirs receive it. If Ploni was already dead at the moment of the instruction — *de'laisa lemakabel b'sha'as matnaso* — then the bequest never had a valid recipient and returns to the sender's estate.

### Pappa Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בְּבָרִיא; הָא – דְּמִית מְקַבֵּל בְּחַיֵּי נוֹתֵן, הָא – דְּמִית נוֹתֵן בְּחַיֵּי מְקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said yet another explanation of the *baraita*: This *baraita* and that *baraita* are both dealing with a healthy person who subsequently died. The difference is that this *baraita*, which said that the agent must return the money to the sender, is dealing with a case where the recipient died in the lifetime of the giver, and as he did not acquire the money himself his heirs do not take possession of it either. By contrast, that *baraita*, which states that the money should be given to the heirs of the recipient, is dealing with a case where the giver died in the lifetime of the recipient. Once he dies it is a mitzva to fulfill his wishes, and therefore the recipient, and his heirs after him, are entitled to the money.**

Rav Pappa offers a third resolution, one that introduces a distinct legal mechanism. He holds that both *braitot* address the case of a *bari* — a healthy sender. Since *holich lav kezachi*, Ploni does not acquire the money upon the instruction. But the *braita* that sends the money to Ploni's heirs is explained differently: what happened is that the sender *died* before the money was delivered. Once the sender dies, a special obligation arises — *mitzvah lekayeim divrei hameis* — there is a *mitzvah* upon the heirs to fulfill the expressed wishes of the deceased. This obligation impels delivery to Ploni's heirs because Ploni was still alive when the sender died and the obligation crystallized, even if Ploni subsequently died before delivery. The *braita* that returns the money to the sender reflects the scenario where Ploni died *before* the sender, meaning that when the sender died and *mitzvah lekayeim divrei hameis* came into force, there was no longer a living Ploni whose heirs could claim under it.

This is an important distinction from *shachiv mera*. A deathbed bequest automatically transfers title. *Mitzvah lekayeim divrei hameis* does not automatically transfer title; it imposes a moral and *halachic* obligation on the heirs to carry out what the deceased wanted. The practical results can be similar, but the mechanism differs. As one student raised: what happens in the *shachiv mera* case if Ploni dies before the *shachiv mera* himself dies? The *gemara*'s formulation — *de'isa lemakabel b'sha'as matnaso* — indicates that the recipient must be alive at the moment the bequest is made for the transfer to take effect, and *Rashi* notes that the *shaliach* acquires on the recipient's behalf immediately, with the heirs then inheriting from that acquisition. This is a matter that merits further examination, and we leave it as an open question.

### Holich Not Kizachi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בְּבָרִיא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי; וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara rejects this suggestion: No; it is possible that in a case where the giver was a healthy person when he appointed the agent everyone agrees that saying: Deliver, is not like saying: Acquire, and with what are we dealing here? We are dealing with a person on his deathbed who sent these one hundred dinars, and they disagree in the dispute between Rabbi Elazar and the Rabbis.**

The cumulative effect of Abba bar Memel, Rav Zevid, and Rav Pappa's positions is to drain the *holich kezachi* formulation of its assumed force. Throughout the earlier discussion, we had been proceeding on the assumption that *holich* was *kezachi* — that the word "deliver" authorized the messenger to take possession on the recipient's behalf immediately. Now the *gemara* systematically explains every case in which the recipient would receive the money as owing to a special mechanism — either the automatic efficacy of *shachiv mera*, or the *mitzvah* to fulfill the wishes of someone who died — rather than to *holich* carrying inherent *kezachi* force. The *gemara* thus closes the discussion of this section with the position that in an ordinary case — a healthy person instructing a messenger to deliver money to a healthy recipient — *holich lav kezachi*, and the recipient acquires nothing until the money actually reaches him.
