# Yevamot 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Single Witness Testimony

### Rabbinic Innovation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֵד אוֹמֵר ״מֵת״, וְנִשֵּׂאת, וּבָא אֶחָד וְאָמַר ״לֹא מֵת״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תֵּצֵא. עֵד אוֹמֵר ״מֵת״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא מֵת״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת, תֵּצֵא. שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״מֵת״, וְעֵד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא מֵת״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִשֵּׂאת, תִּנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **witness says:** The man **died, and** the wife **married** based on this testimony, **and one** other witness **came and said:** He did **not die, she** need **not leave** her new husband due to this testimony. However, if one **witness** comes and **says:** The husband **died, and two** witnesses **say:** He did **not die,** then **even though she married** based on the first witness **she must leave** her new husband. If **two** witnesses **say: He died, and** one **witness says:** He did **not die,** the testimony of the two witnesses is accepted, and **even though she did not** yet **marry, she may marry.**

Daf 118 is the last daf of the second-to-last *perek*, and we pick up with the *mishna* on the bottom of 117b. The broader context is the series of sugyot dealing with the specifics of believing testimony that a woman's husband has died. This represents a tremendous rabbinic innovation: the extension of legal credence to ordinarily disqualified testimony — from a single witness, and by extension from a woman — in order to permit a wife to remarry. As the preceding sugyot have established, this leniency applies not only to a single male witness but to a woman testifying about her own husband's death, and not to a woman testifying for her daughter-in-law, mother-in-law, or stepdaughter. The principle that emerges across all these cases is that any single qualified witness, including a woman, may testify to a husband's death and enable his wife to remarry.

### One vs. One/Two

The prior *mishna* addressed scenarios where testimony conflicts: one witness against one, or one against two. The *gemara* there gave greater weight to the testimony that the husband had died, such that even in the case of one against one, the side affirming death carries additional force — particularly when the testimony had already been accepted by *beit din* before it was contradicted. If it was accepted first and then contradicted, the contradiction cannot dislodge it. If the contradiction came simultaneously, or before formal acceptance, the competing claims may neutralize one another.

### Ulla vs. R. Nechemiah

Two underlying theoretical models govern how this weighting works. Ulla understands the rabbinic enactment as operating within the framework of actual *edut* — testimony proper — in which the single witness testifying to death is treated as if he or she were two witnesses, thereby achieving the weight of full legal testimony. Rabbi Nechemiah, by contrast, frames it not as testimony at all, but as a form of evidence, and accordingly the weight assigned on each side is assessed numerically. The *gemara* works to reconcile these two models throughout the preceding discussion.

## Co-wives Contradicti (II)

### Two Co-wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״מֵת״ וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״לֹא מֵת״. זוֹ שֶׁאוֹמֶרֶת ״מֵת״ — תִּנָּשֵׂא וְתִטּוֹל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, וְזוֹ שֶׁאוֹמֶרֶת ״לֹא מֵת״ — לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא וְלֹא תִּטּוֹל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If two women who were married to the same man come forward, and **one** of them **says** that the husband **died, and** the other **one says he did not die, the one who says he died may marry** on the basis of her own testimony, **and she takes** the money of **her marriage contract. And the one who said he did not die may not marry, and does not take** the money of **her marriage contract.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 117b introduces the following case: *achat omeret met ve-achat omeret lo met* — one co-wife says the husband died, and the other says he did not die. The ruling is that the co-wife who says he died may remarry and collect her *ketubah*, while the co-wife who says he did not die may neither remarry nor collect her *ketubah*. The scenario is vivid: they were traveling together, one returns saying he fell and died, and the other says the whole thing never happened.

### Co-wife Excluded

There are two distinct reasons why the co-wife who says "he did not die" cannot remarry. The first is obvious: she herself has declared that her husband is still alive, and one cannot remarry a living husband. The second reason — which the *gemara* will elaborate — is that even had she remained silent, a woman is not believed to testify on behalf of her co-wife (*tzaratah*). The concern is that the natural rivalry and animosity between co-wives, who share a husband, creates a presumption of bias sufficient to disqualify the co-wife's testimony entirely.

This raises a question, however. The earlier *mishna* suggested that when testimony is contradicted before it has been accepted in *beit din*, the contradicting testimony neutralizes it. If so, why doesn't the co-wife's denial neutralize the first wife's claim that he died — and thereby prevent the first wife from remarrying? The *gemara* will address this below: the *chiddush* of the *mishna* is precisely that we completely dismiss the co-wife's denial. Since she cannot testify for her co-wife to say that the husband died, she equally cannot testify for her co-wife to say that he did not die. Her statement is simply disregarded in its entirety.

## Died vs. Killed

### Died vs. Murdered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״מֵת״ וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״נֶהֱרַג״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וּמַכְחִישׁוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יִנָּשְׂאוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: הוֹאִיל וְזוֹ וָזוֹ מוֹדוֹת שֶׁאֵין קַיָּים — יִנָּשְׂאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** wife **says: He died** in a normal manner, **and** the other **one says: He was killed, Rabbi Meir says: Since they contradict one another, these** women **may not marry. Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon say: Since they both agree that he is not alive they may marry,** despite the fact that they dispute the circumstances of his demise.

The *mishna* continues with a related but distinct case: *achat omeret met ve-achat omeret neherag* — one co-wife says the husband died of natural causes, and the other says he was murdered. Both agree he is dead; they differ only on the manner of death.

### R. Meir Position

Rabbi Meir rules: *ho'il u-makchishot zo et zo harei elu lo yinase'u* — since they contradict one another, neither may remarry. This is a striking position. In the prior case, one wife said "he died" and the other said "he did not die" — and there, the second wife's claim was simply dismissed because a co-wife cannot testify against her co-wife. But here, Rabbi Meir treats the contradiction differently. Why?

Tosafot explains the distinction carefully. When one wife says "he did not die," that claim carries no independent weight — the rabbinic institution gave heightened credibility specifically to testimony that a husband *died*, not to testimony that he did not die. Therefore, the co-wife's denial cannot neutralize the first wife's affirmation. But when one wife says "he died" and the other says "he was murdered," both statements are in the affirmative about death — each co-wife is testifying about something she would herself be believed about under ordinary circumstances, namely, the death of her own husband. Each woman's testimony has a degree of independent weight, and precisely because they have weight, the fact that they describe the death in mutually incompatible ways creates a genuine contradiction sufficient to neutralize one another. That is Rabbi Meir's position.

### R. Yehuda & Shimon

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon disagree. In their view, both wives agree on the central fact — the husband is dead — and therefore both may remarry. The discrepancy in the details does not rise to the level of mutual neutralization. If you split the decision, as in the first case, you accept one and reject the other. Here, because both are affirming death, you either accept both or reject both. Since there is no reason to reject them outright, both may remarry.

## Co-wives Contradicti (II)

### Shimshon Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּאָמְרָה ״לֹא מֵת״, הָא אִישְׁתִּיקָא — תִּנָּשֵׂא? הָא אֵין צָרָה מְעִידָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that if one rival wife says their husband died, while the second maintains that he did not die, the one who says he did not die may not remarry. The Gemara infers: **The reason is that** the second rival wife **said** explicitly: **He did not die,** from which it may be inferred that if **she was silent** and said nothing, **she** is permitted to **marry** based on the testimony of the first. However, this conclusion is problematic, as there is a principle that **one rival wife may not testify on** behalf of **another,** and if so, how can she rely on the testimony of her rival wife?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא מֵת״ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָא מָיֵית, וְהָא דְּקָאָמְרָה ״לֹא מֵת״ — לְקַלְקוֹלַהּ לְצָרָה הִיא דְּקָמִיכַּוְּונָא, וְ״תָמוֹת נַפְשָׁהּ עִם פְּלִשְׁתִּים״ קָאָמְרָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this inference is incorrect, as **it was necessary for** the mishna to state: **He did not die,** as it might **enter your mind to say** that **this** man did in fact **die, and** with regard to **that** which **she says: He did not die, she intends to ruin her rival wife. And** as for herself **she says: Let her die with the Philistines.** This is a paraphrase of the verse: "Let me die with the Philistines" (Judges 16:30), which is a shorthand expression for one's desire to hurt his enemies even if he suffers the same fate himself. In this case it means that she is prepared to testify falsely that their husband did not die, so as to ruin her rival wife. Therefore the mishna **teaches us** that this is not the case, and she is not permitted to marry based on the testimony of her rival wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת מֵת כּוּ׳. וְלִיפְלוֹג רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּרֵישָׁא! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, בְּהָא אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר מוֹדֶה דְּכֹל ״לֹא מֵת״ בְּעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה לָאו הַכְחָשָׁה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that if **one** wife **says: He died,** and one says: He was killed, Rabbi Meir says that as they contradict one another, they may not remarry. The Gemara asks: **And let Rabbi Meir** also **disagree in the first clause,** when one witness says the husband died and the other claims he did not die. **Rabbi Elazar said:** This is **taught as** a tannaitic **dispute.** In other words, Rabbi Meir also disagreed with the first clause, **and** the unattributed opinion in the mishna **is** that of **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** can **even say** that the first clause is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as in this** case **even Rabbi Meir concedes that any** claim of: **He did not die, with regard to testimony** enabling **a woman** to remarry, **is not** considered **contradictory.**

The *gemara* now probes the reasoning behind the *mishna*'s ruling in the first case — that the co-wife cannot remarry. The *gemara* opens with a *diyuk*: *ta'ama d'amrah lo meit* — the reason the co-wife cannot marry is specifically because she said "he did not die." But this implies that had she remained silent, she might have been able to marry on the strength of the first wife's testimony. The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *v'ha ein tzarah me'idah l'chavertah* — a co-wife cannot testify for her co-wife! How could the co-wife have benefited from the first wife's testimony even in silence?

The *gemara* answers with a surprising twist. One might have thought that when the co-wife says "he did not die," she should actually be *more* believed than if she remained silent — and should even be permitted to remarry. The logic would run as follows: the husband is in fact dead, the second wife knows this too, and the only reason she says "he did not die" is to spite her co-wife and prevent her from remarrying. She is, in other words, willing to hurt herself — sacrificing her own ability to remarry — in order to damage the other wife. The *gemara* gives this attitude a memorable name: *tamus nafshi im Plishtim* — "let me die with the Philistines," the dying words of Shimshon. Just as Shimshon pulled down the hall to kill himself along with his enemies, so too the co-wife might say: I will forgo remarriage if it means my rival cannot remarry either.

If so, the *gemara* reasons, perhaps we should interpret the co-wife's denial not as genuine testimony that he is alive, but as an expression of spite — which would reinforce our assumption that he is dead and might even allow the denying wife to remarry. The *gemara* rejects this: *lo meis itztrichah lah* — the *mishna* needed to tell us that even in this case we do not reason this way. We cannot be certain the denying wife is lying to spite her rival; she might also be lying to implicate the first wife in a false claim. In any event, we do not go ahead and allow either wife to benefit from this chain of speculation.

### First Wife Marries

The genuine *chiddush* of the *mishna*, as the *gemara* makes clear, operates in the other direction. If a stranger — a non-co-wife — had contradicted the first wife's testimony that the husband died, and this contradiction came before the testimony was accepted in *beit din*, the two claims would neutralize and the first wife could not remarry. But here, the *chiddush* is that we completely dismiss the co-wife's denial — not only may the co-wife not remarry based on the first wife's testimony, but the co-wife's counter-testimony does not prevent the first wife from remarrying either. The co-wife's word is disregarded in its entirety. Just as she is not believed when she says "he died" (to benefit her rival), she is equally not believed when she says "he did not die" (to harm her rival). Either way, the testimony of the co-wife, directed toward the status of her rival, is simply voided.

## Died vs. Killed (II)

### Elazar vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: עֵד אוֹמֵר ״מֵת״, וְעֵד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא מֵת״, אִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״מֵת״ וְאִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״לֹא מֵת״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר — סְתָמָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קַשְׁיָא! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer. **We learned** in the mishna: If one **witness says: He died, and** one **witness says: He did not die,** or if one **woman says: He died, and** another **woman says: He did not die, she may not marry. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, the unattributed** statement in this mishna **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** that when a rebuttal contradicts the original testimony, one cannot rely upon the testimony that the husband died. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** this is **difficult.** The Gemara answers: Yes, this is **difficult.**

The *gemara* now examines Rabbi Meir's position — that in the case of "died" versus "killed," the testimonies neutralize one another — and asks: why does Rabbi Meir not also say the same in the first case, where one says "died" and the other says "did not die"? The implication is that if Rabbi Meir rules stringently in the "died/killed" case, he should rule stringently in the more obvious "died/didn't die" case as well. *Rav Elazar* responds: *bemachloket shenuya* — the first part of the *mishna*, which allows the first wife to remarry even over the co-wife's objection, is actually the position of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, not Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir would in fact also prohibit the first wife from remarrying in that case. So Rabbi Meir's position is internally consistent: in both the "died/didn't die" case and the "died/killed" case, the testimonies neutralize one another.

Rabbi Yochanan reads the *mishna* differently: *afilu teima Rabbi Meir* — even according to Rabbi Meir, there is a meaningful distinction between "died/didn't die" and "died/killed." In the former case, even Rabbi Meir would agree that the testimonies do not neutralize, because the claim "he did not die" in the context of testimony about a husband's death is treated as legally worthless.

### Co-wife Claim Void

Rabbi Yochanan's principle requires careful qualification. It is not that any claim of "he did not die" is always ignored — the end of the *mishna* states plainly that when one witness says "he died" and another says "he did not die," they do neutralize one another. So it cannot be a categorical rule that denials of death are void. The precise rule is this: a *co-wife's* claim that the husband did not die is voided, because just as a co-wife is not believed to say "he died" for the benefit of her rival, she is equally not believed to say "he did not die" to the detriment of her rival. The reasoning is the same in both directions — we do not trust any testimony the co-wife offers about her rival's husband's death or survival.

By contrast, when each wife says "he died," but one says "naturally" and the other says "by murder," each woman's testimony is accepted *for herself*. It is at that point — once each woman's individual testimony has been admitted for her own case — that the contradiction between the two accounts, both of which carry independent weight, creates the neutralization that Rabbi Meir invokes.

The *gemara* attempts to reconcile this analysis with the end of the *mishna*, which, when it says one woman says "died" and another says "did not die," could appear to include co-wives. According to Rabbi Elazar, that case in the *mishna* indeed reflects Rabbi Meir's position that co-wives can neutralize one another. According to Rabbi Yochanan, this case does not involve co-wives at all — and the difficulty for Rabbi Yochanan (*kashya*) is acknowledged. The overall upshot is clear: the co-wife's testimony, in any direction, is completely disregarded with respect to her rival.

## Woman Returns Testifies

### Classic Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלְכָה הִיא וּבַעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וּבָאָה וְאָמְרָה ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — תִּנָּשֵׂא וְתִטּוֹל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, וְצָרָתָהּ אֲסוּרָה. הָיְתָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a woman who went, she and her husband, overseas, and she comes and says: My husband died, she may marry, and she takes her marriage contract** based on her own testimony. **And it** remains **prohibited for her rival wife** to remarry, as a woman cannot testify on behalf of her rival wife. If the rival wife **was an Israelite woman** married **to a priest, she may** continue to **partake of *teruma*,** as she is not permitted to remarry, and therefore the presumption that the husband is still alive is maintained in relation to her. This is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon.**

The next *mishna* presents the archetypal case of overseas testimony: *ha-isha she-halcha hi u-va'alah limdinat ha-yam* — a woman who went with her husband overseas, and returned to testify that he died. The ruling is *tinasei ve-titol ketuvatah* — she may remarry and collect her *ketubah*. This is the foundational case of the entire rabbinic innovation.

### R. Tarfon & Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מוֹצִיאָתוֹ מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא אֲסוּרָה לִינָּשֵׂא, וַאֲסוּרָה מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: This is not** the **way to spare** someone **from transgression.** According to the opinion of Rabbi Tarfon, there is a concern that she might be eating *teruma* unlawfully. There is no remedy for this situation **unless it is prohibited** for the rival wife **to marry,** as she cannot rely on the testimony of her rival wife, **and it is** also **prohibited** for her **to partake of *teruma*,** lest the other woman was speaking the truth. In other words, the *halakha* is stringent on both counts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת חָמִי״ — תִּנָּשֵׂא וְתִטּוֹל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, וַחֲמוֹתָהּ אֲסוּרָה. הָיְתָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מוֹצִיאָ[תָ]הּ מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא אֲסוּרָה לִינָּשֵׂא וַאֲסוּרָה מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses a similar case. If a woman **said: My husband died and afterward my father-in-law died, she may marry and take her marriage contract, and it is prohibited** for **her mother-in-law** to remarry, as a woman may not testify on behalf of her mother-in-law. If the mother-in-law **was the daughter of an Israelite married to a priest, she may partake of *teruma*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says: This is not** the **way to spare** her **from transgression;** there is no remedy **unless it is prohibited** for the mother-in-law **to marry and** also **prohibited** for her **to partake of *teruma*.**

The *mishna* then adds: *ve-tzaratah asurah* — the co-wife is forbidden to remarry, since a woman's testimony about the death of a husband is not believed on behalf of her co-wife. The *mishna* then asks a further question about the co-wife's status: if the co-wife had previously been married to a *kohen*, may she continue to eat *terumah*? Rabbi Tarfon rules that she may — since we completely reject the co-wife's testimony, we do not treat it even as a *safek*, a doubt, and therefore the co-wife retains her presumed status as the wife of a living husband, allowing her to continue eating *terumah*. Rabbi Akiva rules more stringently: *ein zo derech motzi mi-yedei aveirah* — this approach does not protect her from sin. Since the testimony creates at least enough uncertainty to prevent her from remarrying, it creates enough uncertainty about eating *terumah* as well, and she must abstain from both. Rabbi Akiva's position is a *machmir* one precisely because treating the matter as a *safek* obligates caution on all sides.

### Father-in-law Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא — בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דְּצַעְרָא דְּגוּפַהּ. אֲבָל חֲמוֹת, דְּצַעְרָא מִילֵּי דְּעָלְמָא — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva with regard to two apparently identical cases. The Gemara explains: And it is **necessary** to state both of these examples. **As, had** the mishna **stated** the dispute only in **this first** case, I would have said: It is **in this** case that **Rabbi Tarfon said** the wife is suspected of lying, **because the suffering is physical,** i.e., she is jealous of her rival wife, with whom she shares her husband. **However,** with regard to **her mother-in-law, where the suffering is merely verbal** and not physical, one might **say** that **he concedes to Rabbi Akiva,** i.e., that she is not considered a liar, and therefore it should be prohibited for her mother-in-law to partake in *teruma*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא — בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** vice-versa: **Had** the mishna **stated** the dispute only **in this** second case, I would have said: It is **in this** case that **Rabbi Akiva said** that the mother-in-law must refrain from eating *teruma* in case the daughter-in-law is not lying, **but in this** other case of the rival wife, you might **say** that **he concedes to Rabbi Tarfon** that she may eat *teruma*, as a rival wife is certainly not telling the truth. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to state the dispute in both two cases.

A parallel case follows: *amra meit ba'ali ve-achar kach meit chami* — a woman who says "my husband died and then my father-in-law died." Ordinarily, a woman is not believed to testify on behalf of her *chamotah* (mother-in-law) or father-in-law, because we suspect the lingering animus of the in-law relationship. However, the *gemara* notes that once her husband has died, her father-in-law is no longer halachically her father-in-law — the relationship has dissolved. And a woman is believed to testify on behalf of an *ex*-father-in-law. So she is claiming: accept my testimony about my husband's death first, and then — since he is now my former father-in-law — accept my testimony about his death as well.

The *mishna* rules: *tinasei ve-titol ketuvatah*, she may remarry and collect her *ketubah*, but *chamotah asurah* — the mother-in-law's status is not changed by this testimony. Why not? Because since the entire chain of reasoning rests on the woman's own testimony, we are concerned that she has constructed the whole account to harm her mother-in-law. Her motivation to do so is real: if the mother-in-law is married to a *kohen* and we accept the testimony that her husband died, she can no longer eat *terumah*. So even if the woman is lying about her husband's death — which the *mishna* says she is believed about — she still achieves something negative against her mother-in-law. That concern alone is sufficient to reject the mother-in-law's portion of the testimony.

The ruling for the mother-in-law therefore mirrors that of the co-wife: Rabbi Tarfon says the testimony is completely rejected and the mother-in-law may continue eating *terumah*, while Rabbi Akiva says it is not enough to protect her from sin and she must treat the matter as a *safek*, refraining from both remarriage and *terumah*.

A question worth raising is why the co-wife should suffer this liminal status when the testimony might be entirely true and she has done nothing wrong. This is a genuine difficulty, and it underscores the tension the rabbis created when they expanded the category of believed testimony: they recognized the leniency could be abused and therefore imposed limits, but those limits sometimes fall on an innocent party caught in the wrong formal relationship. Rabbi Tarfon's approach — completely rejecting the testimony and restoring the co-wife's unambiguous prior status — has real appeal precisely because it avoids leaving an innocent person in an undefined halachic limbo.

### Both Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon.** The testimony of a woman who is suspected of harboring ill will toward another is entirely rejected with regard to that other person, and no credence is given to her account at all.

The *gemara* establishes that both cases — the co-wife and the mother-in-law — are necessary, and neither can be derived from the other. Had the *mishna* taught only the co-wife case, one might have thought Rabbi Tarfon completely rejects the testimony because *tzarat ha-gufa* — the co-wife's animosity is intensely personal, rooted in the intimate fact that the husband sleeps with her rival rather than with her. One might then think Rabbi Tarfon would concede to Rabbi Akiva in the case of the mother-in-law, where the animosity is of a more ordinary social variety — perhaps she badmouths her daughter-in-law to her son, a complaint common enough but far less visceral. Conversely, had the *mishna* taught only the mother-in-law case, one might think Rabbi Akiva concedes in the co-wife case, where the bias is so extreme that even he might agree we completely reject the testimony. By teaching both cases, the *mishna* makes clear that neither tanna modifies his position based on the type of animosity involved — each maintains his view across both scenarios.

The *gemara* then reports: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, halacha ke-Rabbi Tarfon* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Tarfon. The co-wife is not left in a state of ambiguity. The testimony is fully rejected, and she may go on living her life as before, without the paralysis of a *safek* she never created.

## One of Five Women

### Uncertainty Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קִידֵּשׁ אַחַת מֵחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים, וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ אֵי זוֹ קִידֵּשׁ, כׇּל אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״אוֹתִי קִידֵּשׁ״ — נוֹתֵן גֵּט לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת, וּמַנִּיחַ כְּתוּבָּה בֵּינֵיהֶן, וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In relation to the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva in the previous mishna, in which Rabbi Akiva states that one must avoid a possible transgression, the mishna cites two similar cases involving other topics. With regard to one who **betrothed one of five women, and he does not know which** of them **he betrothed,** and **each one** of them **says: He betrothed me,** if he does not want to marry any of them **he gives a bill of divorce to each and every one** of them so none will have the status of a woman with regard to whom there is uncertainty whether she is divorced. **And he leaves the marriage contract among them and departs.** The marriage contract remains in dispute between the women until they clarify which of them is entitled to the money. This is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon.**

The *mishna* now takes up what the *gemara* presents as a digression occasioned by the debate between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva. The case is: *kidesh achat mi-chamesh nashim ve-eino yodea eizo kidesh* — a man performed *kiddushin* with one of five women but does not know which one. Each woman claims he married her. This is not an impossible scenario given the realities of the time, when *kiddushin* and *nisuin* could be separated by years and the formal act of *kiddushin* might be the transfer of money to a father on behalf of a daughter, leaving uncertainty about which daughter was intended.

The *mishna* states that he must give a *get* to each one — since he cannot be certain which woman is his wife, he cannot simply live with any one of them or leave them all bound without resolution. As for the *ketubah*: *maniach ketubah beineihen u-mistalek* — he deposits the *ketubah* money among them and withdraws. He knows he is obligated to one of them but does not know which one, so he leaves the money for them to resolve.

### Hamotzi Framework

The underlying legal question is how to apply the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-re'ayah* — the burden of proof lies on the one seeking to extract from another. Each woman presents herself to him with a definite claim: you owe me a *ketubah*. He responds that he is uncertain. This is not precisely the case where a lender says "I lent you money" and the borrower denies it — there the certainty and uncertainty are on opposite sides. Nor is it quite the case where one says "I lent you money and I am not sure if you paid me back" — there the debtor concedes the debt. Here it is an intermediate case: he concedes the existence of an obligation to one of them, but not necessarily to the particular woman standing before him. This framing of the *hamotzi* analysis shapes the subsequent debate.

### R. Tarfon Position

Rabbi Tarfon says: he places the *ketubah* money among them and withdraws. He has discharged his definite obligation — to one of them — by depositing the full amount. They must resolve the distribution among themselves.

### R. Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה, עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן גֵּט וּכְתוּבָּה לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: This is not** the **way to spare** someone **from transgression,** as perhaps the woman he actually betrothed will not receive the money to which she is entitled. There is no remedy **unless he gives a bill of divorce and a marriage contract** payment **to each and every one.**

Rabbi Akiva rules: *ein zo derech motzi mi-yedei aveirah* — the same phrase we encountered above — this does not protect him from sin. He must give a *get* and a *ketubah* to each woman individually. The question is whether Rabbi Akiva is speaking in strictly legal terms — that a court would compel him to pay each one — or whether he is articulating a moral obligation that, while not technically enforceable through *dinei mamonot*, nonetheless constitutes a real spiritual and ethical debt. The parallel the *gemara* raises elsewhere is illuminating: one who places poison before another's animal is exempt *be-dinei adam* but liable *be-dinei shamayim* — exempt in human courts but obligated before the heavenly court. The language *ein zo derech motzi mi-yedei aveirah* suggests Rabbi Akiva may be operating in that register: you have a specific, concrete moral obligation that a *beit din* will seek to enforce, even if strict *dinei mamonot* would not technically mandate it.

## Theft from Five

### Theft Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּזַל אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה גָּזַל, כׇּל אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אוֹתִי גָּזַל״ — מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶן וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה, עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, in the case of one who stole** money **from one of five** people **and does not know from which** of them **he stole,** and **each one says: He stole from me, he leaves the stolen** money **among them and departs,** and they will decide among themselves how to distribute the money; this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says: This is not** the **way to spare** him **from transgression;** there is no remedy **unless he pays the stolen** money **to each and every one** of them. In this case, as he committed a transgression, he must ensure that the stolen money is restored to its proper owner.

A parallel case follows immediately: *gezel echad mi-chamishah ve-eini yodea me-eizeh gazal* — a man stole from one of five people and does not know which one. Each person claims he stole from them. Rabbi Tarfon rules that he deposits the stolen object among them and withdraws; Rabbi Akiva rules he must make full restitution to each one.

### Heightened Obligation

The obvious moral distinction between the two cases is that in the *kiddushin* scenario, the man did nothing inherently wrong — he entered into a marriage, lost track of which woman it was, and now must extract himself. In the theft scenario, he committed a transgression. One would therefore expect the obligation to make amends to be greater in the theft case. The *mishna* seems to present Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon as disagreeing in both scenarios equally — and it raises the question of whether the same analysis governs a morally neutral situation and a situation involving actual wrongdoing.

### R. Shimon b. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״קִידֵּשׁ״ — קָתָנֵי, ״בָּעַל״ — לָא קָתָנֵי. ״גָּזַל״ — קָתָנֵי, ״לָקַח״ — לָא קָתָנֵי. מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא תַּנָּא קַמָּא וְלָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers: It **is taught** in the mishna that **he betrothed** one of five women, whereas **it is not taught** that **he had sexual intercourse** with one of five women, as a different ruling applies in this case. Similarly, with regard to the second case, it **is taught** that **he stole** from one of five people, and **it is not taught** that **he purchased** an item from one of five people. If so, **whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **the first *tanna* and** it is also **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עַל שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ אַחַת מֵחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזוֹ קִידֵּשׁ, שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ כְּתוּבָּה בֵּינֵיהֶן וּמִסְתַּלֵּק. עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל שֶׁבָּעַל. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: מַנִּיחַ כְּתוּבָּה בֵּינֵיהֶן וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva did not dispute** the case **of** a man **who betrothed one of five women and he does not know which** of them **he betrothed,** as everyone agrees in this case **that he leaves** the money of **the marriage contract among them and departs. With regard to what did they disagree? With regard to** the case of one **who had sexual intercourse** with one of them for the purpose of betrothal. **Rabbi Tarfon says: He leaves the money among them and departs,** while **Rabbi Akiva says:** He does not fulfill his obligation **unless he pays each and every one of them.** Since he married in an inappropriate manner, the Sages penalized him by compelling him to pay all the women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עַל שֶׁלָּקַח מִקָּח מֵחֲמִשָּׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח — שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ דְּמֵי מִקָּח בֵּינֵיהֶן וּמִסְתַּלֵּק. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁגָּזַל מֵחֲמִשָּׁה, שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם גְּזֵילָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva did not dispute** the case **of** one **who purchased an item from** one of **five people and he does not know from which of them he purchased it.** Everyone agrees in this case **that he leaves the price of the purchase among them and departs. They disagree only with regard to** one **who stole from five people, as Rabbi Tarfon says: He leaves the stolen** money **among them and departs, and Rabbi Akiva says:** He does not fulfill his obligation **unless he pays the stolen** money **to each and every one** of them. In this case, as he committed a transgression, he must ensure that the stolen money is restored to its proper owner.

The *gemara* introduces a *braita* in which Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar clarifies the scope of the debate: *lo nechelku Rabbi Tarfon ve-Rabbi Akiva al she-kidesh achat mi-chamesh nashim ve-eino yodea eizo kidesh she-maniach ketubah beineihen u-mistalek* — Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva did not disagree at all in the case of simple *kiddushin* without a more heightened involvement. Both agree that he deposits the *ketubah* and withdraws. Similarly, they did not disagree in the case where someone purchased an item from one of five merchants and forgets which one — there, too, both agree he deposits the payment and withdraws. The debate, says Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, applies only in cases of heightened obligation: *kiddushin* accomplished through an act of intercourse (*kiddushei bi'ah*), and theft.

### Special Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר בְּ״קִידֵּשׁ״ וְ״לָקַח״ לָא פְּלִיגִי, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר פְּלִיגִי. מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said** that Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva **do not disagree with regard to** the cases of one who **betrothed and** one who **purchased,** one may learn **by inference that the first *tanna*,** with whom he disagrees, **holds** that **they do disagree** about the cases of betrothed and purchased, whereas with regard to one who had sexual intercourse and one who stole an item Rabbi Tarfon agrees with Rabbi Akiva. With this in mind, **who is** the author of the mishna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי תַּנָּא קַמָּא — לִיתְנֵי ״קִידֵּשׁ״ וְ״לָקַח״. אִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר — לִיתְנֵי ״בָּעַל״ וְ״גָזַל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the first *tanna*, let it teach** the cases of **betrothed and** also **purchased,** but not that of one who stole or one who had intercourse, as the first *tanna* maintains that Rabbi Tarfon agrees with Rabbi Akiva in those cases. **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, let it teach** the cases of one who **had sexual relations and** that of one who **stole.** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva disagree in the case of one who had sexual relations, not in the case of a man who betrothed one of five women. Likewise, he maintains that they disagree in the case of one who stole from five people, not one who purchased an item from five people.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, וּמַאי ״קִידֵּשׁ״ — קִידֵּשׁ בְּבִיאָה. תְּנָא ״קִידֵּשׁ״, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and what is** the meaning of the term: **Betrothed?** It means that **he betrothed** one of the women **by** means of **sexual intercourse.** And the mishna **taught** the term **betrothed to convey to you the far-reaching** nature of the stringent opinion **of Rabbi Akiva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיסּוּרָא דְּרַבָּנַן עֲבַד, קָנֵיס! תְּנָא ״גָּזַל״, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיסּוּרָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא עֲבַד, לָא קָנֵיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As, although** this man **performed** an act that violated **a prohibition** that applies **by rabbinic law,** i.e., he did not have licentious sexual relations with her but rather engaged in intercourse for the purpose of betrothal, which is in violation of a rabbinic prohibition that one may not betroth a woman by sexual intercourse *ab initio*, even so Rabbi Akiva **penalizes** him. And the mishna **taught** the case of one who **stole** so as **to convey to you the far-reaching** nature of the lenient opinion **of Rabbi Tarfon, as although he performed** an act that violated **a prohibition** that applies **by Torah law,** nevertheless **he does not penalize** him even in this case.

This creates a problem for how to read our *mishna*, which appears to present the debate in the cases of simple *kiddushin* (without *bi'ah*) and theft — one from each column. The *gemara* resolves: *le-olam Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — our *mishna* is in fact consistent with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's view. When our *mishna* says *kidesh*, it means *kiddushei bi'ah* — *kiddushin* effected through intercourse, which carries a special moral weight because, regardless of whether *kiddushei bi'ah* was halachically proper (and the rabbis disapproved of it, though it is valid), having been intimate with a woman and then walking away without ensuring she receives her *ketubah* creates a particular obligation. The *mishna* thus presents the two cases of heightened obligation — *kiddushei bi'ah* and theft — as the arena of the dispute.

Within those cases, moreover, the *gemara* identifies a further gradation. *Kiddushei bi'ah* involves only a rabbinic prohibition (*issur d'Rabbanan*), whereas theft is an *issur d'Oraita*. The *mishna* therefore teaches Rabbi Akiva's ruling in the lighter case — *kiddushei bi'ah* — to demonstrate the strength of his position: even where the transgression was only rabbinic in character, he imposes the full obligation to pay each woman. Conversely, the *mishna* teaches Rabbi Tarfon's ruling in the heavier case — theft — to demonstrate the strength of his position: even where there was a genuine biblical transgression, he does not require full individual restitution to each claimant.

The *gemara*'s closing word on Rabbi Akiva's position is *kanis* — a fine. This framing clarifies what Rabbi Akiva means: not that strict *dinei mamonot* technically obligates him to pay each woman, but that we impose a *knas* to ensure he fully discharges his responsibility to the one he actually wronged, since we cannot know which one that is.

## Son & Yibum Status

### Husband Then Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלְכָה הִיא וּבַעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וּבְנָהּ עִמָּהֶם, וּבָאת וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת בְּנִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. מֵת בְּנִי וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְחוֹשְׁשִׁין לִדְבָרֶיהָ, וְחוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a woman who went, she and her husband, overseas, and her son** was **with them, and** later **she came** back **and said: My husband died and afterward my son died, she is deemed credible.** It is permitted for her to remarry, and she is exempt from levirate marriage. The reason is that she had children when she left, and therefore she retains her presumptive status of one who is exempt from levirate marriage. However, if she said: **My son died and afterward my husband died, she is not deemed credible,** i.e., she may not enter into levirate marriage. **And** yet **we are concerned** and give some credence **to her statement,** in case she was actually widowed by a childless husband, and therefore **she performs *ḥalitza*** to exempt her from the levirate bond with her *yavam*, **and she does not enter into levirate marriage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נִיתַּן לִי בֵּן בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם״, וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בְּנִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. ״מֵת בַּעְלִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת בְּנִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְחוֹשְׁשִׁין לִדְבָרֶיהָ, וְחוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If she went with her childless husband and returned alone and testified: **A son was born to me overseas, and she** further **said: My son died and afterward my husband died, she is deemed credible** and may even enter into levirate marriage, as she was presumed to be childless when she left and consequently she retains that presumptive status. However, if she said: **My husband died and afterward my son died, she is not deemed credible** for the purpose of exempting her from levirate marriage, **but** the court is **concerned about her statement. And** therefore **she must perform *ḥalitza* and she does not enter into levirate marriage.**

The last *mishna* of the *perek* presents a series of cases involving a woman's testimony about events overseas that affect her *yibum* status. The *mishna* opens with the case we touched on earlier: *ha-isha she-halcha hi u-va'alah limdinat ha-yam u-vnah imahem* — a woman who departed with her husband and her son. Since she left with a son, she would not be obligated in *yibum* if her husband died. She returns and testifies: my husband died, and then my son died. In this configuration, she is believed — the only thing she is specially credited to testify about is her husband's death, and the two statements about the son cancel each other out. We assumed she would not be obligated in *yibum* when she left, and nothing she says about her son changes that. She may act accordingly.

### Son First Claim

Now consider a variation: she left with no son — so we assumed she *would* be obligated in *yibum*. She returns and says: I had a son born to me overseas, and my husband died, and then my son died. She is neutralizing her own claim: yes I had a son, but he died after my husband, so I am still not obligated in *yibum*. Since she is not trying to change her status in an advantageous direction — she is not claiming *yibum* rights or *yibum* exemption beyond what we already assumed — and her credibility extends to the statement about her husband's death, she is believed.

But if she says instead: my husband died, and then my son died — and she had left without a son — she is now claiming she is *exempt* from *yibum* based on a son whose existence and death she alone asserts. She is not believed to change her status in this way, because her special credibility pertains to her husband's death, not to the existence or death of a son. Nevertheless, *chosheshin li-dvareha* — we take her words into account enough to require caution. Since she claims exemption from *yibum*, we cannot permit *yibum* to proceed. The ruling: *choletzet ve-lo mityabemet* — she performs *chalitzah* but does not perform *yibum*.

### Brother-in-law Overseas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נִיתַּן לִי יָבָם בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם״, אָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת יְבָמִי״, ״יְבָמִי וְאַחַר כָּךְ בַּעְלִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If she said: **A *yavam* was born for me overseas,** i.e., when the family left the country her husband did not have a brother, and she claims that in the meantime a brother was born to her husband, and **she** also **said: My husband died and afterward my *yavam* died,** or: **My *yavam*** died **and afterward my husband** died, in either case **she is deemed credible.** This is because when she left she was not presumed to require levirate marriage, and the suggestion that her husband now has a brother is based solely on her testimony.

The more difficult case: she departed together with her husband and a known brother-in-law — *halchah hi u-va'alah ve-yavamah limdinat ha-yam* — meaning we know there is a *yibum* obligation should her husband die. She returns and testifies that both her husband and her brother-in-law have died — in either order. Since either order leads to the same result — no brother-in-law, no *yibum* — one might expect she is believed. But the *mishna* rules: *einah ne'emenet* — she is not believed.

### Scope Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלְכָה הִיא וּבַעְלָהּ וִיבָמָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, אָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יְבָמִי״, ״יְבָמִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בַּעְלִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת, שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה נֶאֱמֶנֶת לוֹמַר ״מֵת יְבָמִי״ שֶׁתִּנָּשֵׂא, וְלֹא ״מֵתָה אֲחוֹתָהּ״ שֶׁתִּכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ, וְאֵין הָאִישׁ נֶאֱמָן לוֹמַר ״מֵת אָחִי״ שֶׁיְּיַבֵּם אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְלֹא ״מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ״, שֶׁיִּשָּׂא אֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **she went, she and her husband and her *yavam*, overseas, and** upon her return she **said: My husband died and afterward my *yavam*** died, or: **My *yavam*** died **and afterward my husband** died, **she is not deemed credible, as a woman is not deemed credible** if she **says: My *yavam* died,** in order **that she may marry** another man. **And** she is **not** deemed credible if she says that **her sister died,** in order **that she may enter the house** of her sister's husband. **And a man is not deemed credible** if he **says: My brother died, so that he may enter into levirate marriage with his** brother's **wife, and** he is **not** deemed credible when he says that **his wife died,** in order **that** he **may marry** his wife's **sister.** The Sages accepted impaired testimony of this kind only when there was a concern about creating a situation of a deserted wife.

The reason is that she is testifying about her brother-in-law's death, which falls outside the scope of the special rabbinic credibility accorded to a wife. That credibility extends only to testimony about her own husband's death. The *mishna* articulates the principle broadly: *she-ein ishah ne'emenet lomar meit yavmi she-tinasei* — a woman is not believed to say "my brother-in-law died" so that she may remarry freely. Similarly, *ve-lo meitah achotah she-tikaneis le-veiso* — a woman is not believed to say "my sister died" so that she may marry her sister's husband. And *ve-ein ha-ish ne'eman lomar meit achi she-yiyabem et ishto* — a man is not believed to say "my brother died" in order to perform *yibum* with the brother's wife.

This last case is worth examining carefully. If a man returns and says his brother died, that statement is actually a form of testimony that a husband died — and one might think the sister-in-law could rely on it to remarry if there are children, in which case there is no *yibum* obligation. And indeed, in that context she might be able to. But she cannot rely on it to permit *yibum*, because his testimony in that case would *matir* an *issur* — it would be removing the prohibition of *eshet ach*, a brother's wife, which is otherwise biblically forbidden. The rabbinic credibility granted to single witnesses and wives applies only to permitting a woman to remarry as a widow, not to enabling an otherwise prohibited relationship.

The practical consequence for the woman in the brother-in-law case is severe: she testified that the brother-in-law died, she is not believed, and yet there is no brother-in-law present to perform either *yibum* or *chalitzah*. She is effectively an *agunah* — bound by a *yibum* obligation to a man whose whereabouts cannot be confirmed.

## Get to Absent Wife

### Get via Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: הַמְזַכֶּה גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ בִּמְקוֹם יָבָם, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּסָנְיָא לֵיהּ — זְכוּת הוּא לָהּ, וְזָכִין לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו. אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּזִימְנִין דְּרָחֲמָא לֵיהּ, חוֹב הוּא לָהּ, וְאֵין חָבִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** In the case of a man **who confers possession** of **a bill of divorce to his wife,** i.e., he appoints an agent to take the bill of divorce to his wife, **when** she has a potential ***yavam*, what is** the *halakha* if her husband dies before she is divorced? One might say that **since she hates** her *yavam*, receiving the bill of divorce is **for her benefit,** as this act renders her prohibited to him, **and** it is a principle that **one may act in a person's interest in his absence.** If so, as soon as the husband gives the bill of divorce to the agent she is divorced. **Or perhaps, since she sometimes loves** her *yavam*, **this** bill of divorce **is to her disadvantage, and one may not act against a person's interest in his absence.** Consequently, she is not divorced until the bill of divorce reaches her possession.

The *gemara* now introduces a question posed by Rava to Rav Nachman: *ha-mezakeh get le-ishto bimkom yavam mahu?* — if a husband wishes to give a *get* to his wife in a situation where there is a *yavam* (brother-in-law who would be obligated in *yibum*), but the wife is not present, can the husband appoint a third party to receive the *get* on the wife's behalf?

The scenario is one in which the husband wants to preempt the *yibum* obligation — perhaps he is terminally ill or departing for war and fears he will die, leaving his wife subject to *yibum* with a brother-in-law she does not want. He would like to give a *get* now so that upon his death the *yibum* obligation never arises. But the wife is not present to receive the *get* directly and has not appointed an agent to receive it. Can the husband effectively appoint a representative on his wife's behalf?

### Mezakeh Principle

The operative principle is *mezakeh* — one may acquire something on behalf of another person without their presence or knowledge, provided it is to that person's benefit (*zechus*). A husband can give his wife a *get* against her will, since once instituted, Cherem de-Rabbenu Gershom notwithstanding, *halacha* recognizes a unilateral *get*. But for the *get* to take effect, it must be received by the wife or her validly appointed agent. The question is whether the husband can himself appoint someone to represent the wife — treating this as an act of *mezakeh*, done on her behalf without her knowledge — given that a *get* would seemingly benefit her by freeing her from the *yibum* obligation.

### Benefit vs. Harm

The *gemara* frames the question as a genuine uncertainty. On one hand, *keivan d'sanya lah* — since we can assume she hates the brother-in-law, being freed from *yibum* is clearly to her benefit, and *zakin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* — one may acquire benefit for another without their presence. On the other hand, *dilma zimnin de-rachma lei* — perhaps there are times when a woman actually likes or loves her brother-in-law and would prefer *yibum* to remaining a widow. In that case, being divorced precludes the *yibum* she would have wanted, which makes the *get* a *chov* — a harm rather than a benefit. And *ein chavin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* — one cannot impose a harm on another person without their presence and consent.

The *gemara*'s framing is notable: it does not assume we can simply inquire about each woman's individual feelings. It is asking whether a general presumption can be established — can we rule categorically that a *get* in a *yibum* situation is always, or almost always, to the wife's benefit?

### Fighting Couple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: וְחוֹשְׁשִׁין לִדְבָרֶיהָ, וְחוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to** Rava that **we learned** an answer to this dilemma in the mishna: **And we are concerned about her statement, and she** must **perform *ḥalitza* and she does not enter into levirate marriage.** The mishna states that the court gives her statement some credence in either case, both when her testimony would exempt her from levirate marriage and when it would allow her to marry her *yavam*. This indicates that levirate marriage is considered neither in her interest nor a disadvantage for her. Rather, its classification is uncertain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: הַמְזַכֶּה גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ בִּמְקוֹם קְטָטָה, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לַהּ קְטָטָה בַּהֲדֵיהּ — זְכוּת הוּא לָהּ. אוֹ דִּלְמָא, נְיָחָא דְּגוּפָא עֲדִיף לַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טָב לְמֵיתַב טַן דּוּ מִלְּמֵיתַב אַרְמְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravina said to Rava:** With regard to one **who confers possession** of **a bill of divorce to his wife** through an agent in a situation **when there was a quarrel** between them, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains both sides of the question: **Since she has a quarrel with him,** the bill of divorce might be considered **for her benefit. Or perhaps,** her **physical comfort is preferable to her,** as she prefers to remain married despite the quarrel between her and her husband. **Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as Reish Lakish said:** There is a popular idiom among women: **It is better to sit as two [*tan du*] than to sit** lonely as a **widow,** i.e., a woman prefers the companionship of any husband over being alone.

The question is pressed further by Ravina, who asks Rava: *ha-mezakeh get le-ishah bimkom ketatah mahu?* — what if the husband and wife are in the midst of a serious conflict? They are fighting, tensions are high, perhaps legal proceedings have begun. Surely, one might argue, in that case the *get* is clearly to her benefit — she obviously wants out of the marriage. Or perhaps not: *nicha de-gufa adif lah* — even when a woman is fighting bitterly with her husband, the physical and emotional security of being married may still matter more to her than being free of him. Even in the midst of a quarrel, a woman may not want to be divorced.

### Two vs. Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: דְּשׁוּמְשְׁמָנָא גַּבְרָא, כּוּרְסְיַהּ בֵּי חָרָתָא רָמוּ לַהּ. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: דְּנַפָּצָא גַּבְרָא, תִּיקְרֵי בְּסִיפֵּי בָבָא וְתִיתִּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** a similar popular expression: One **whose husband is** small **as an ant, she places her seat among the noblewomen,** as she considers herself important merely by virtue of being married. **Rav Pappa said** a different maxim: One **whose husband is a wool comber [*naftza*],** a lowly occupation, **she calls him to sit** with her **at the entrance to the house,** to display herself as a married woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אוֹמֵר: דְּקוֹלְסָא גַּבְרָא, לָא בָּעֲיָא טְלָפְחֵי לְקִידְרָא. תָּנָא: וְכוּלָּן מְזַנּוֹת וְתוֹלוֹת בְּבַעְלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Ashi says:** One **whose husband sells cabbage heads [*kulsa*] does not require lentils for her pot.** She is so happy she is married that she does not mind even if he does not provide her with food. The Gemara comments: A Sage **taught: And all of these** women who appear so satisfied with their marriage, they all **commit adultery and attribute** the children **to their husbands.** This is another reason why they are so keen to be married. This shows that even when there are quarrels between a couple, the wife still prefers the status of a married woman, and therefore the bill of divorce is not considered in her interest.

The *gemara* invokes a famous statement of Reish Lakish: *tav lemetav tan du milemetav armalu* — better to sit as two than to sit as a widow. This is presented as a sociological assessment: women, as a general matter, prefer the state of being married — even to an imperfect husband — over the state of widowhood or singlehood. The statement carries enormous halachic weight and would later become the subject of a famous dispute between Rabbi Rackman and Rav Soloveitchik about whether changing social realities could alter this assumption. But in the *gemara* itself, it is offered as a presumption about women's preferences.

Several *amoraim* elaborate on this theme with increasingly pointed formulations. Abaye states: *deshumshmana gavra* — my husband could be as small as an ant, but as long as *kursiyei be-charta rami* — my seat is set among people of standing — I am content. A woman will accept a negligible husband if it means having the social standing of being married. Rav Pappa states: *denafsa gavra* — my husband could be a flax comber, but *tikrei be-seifei bava ve-teitiv* — as long as I am called to the door and given a place to sit, the dignity of being married suffices. Rav Ashi states: *dekulsa gavra* — my husband could be a cabbage head, but *lo ba'ei talfei le-kedira* — as long as I do not need to worry about putting food on the table myself, I will manage. The repeated motif is that women are willing to settle for husbands of very modest character and capacity rather than remain unmarried.

### Adultery Reversal

The *gemara*, however, does not end on that note. A *braita* is cited that dramatically reverses the valence of all that has been said: *ve-kullan mezanot ve-tolot be-ba'aleihen* — all of them commit adultery and attribute the resulting pregnancies to their husbands. What began as a description of women's willingness to endure and settle within marriage ends as an indictment: the very willingness to remain with inadequate husbands generates unfaithfulness, because the unhappiness they suppress does not simply disappear.

### Modern Issues

The *gemara*'s conclusion — that we cannot give a *get* to a wife without her presence even when she is fighting with her husband, because we cannot assume she would want it — flows from the assumption encoded in *tav lemetav tan du milemetav armalu*. And this has real and troubling contemporary consequences. The presumption that women prefer marriage to its absence, that they will endure rather than leave, has been invoked by *batei dinim* — particularly those identified as deeply committed to *shalom bayit* — to resist granting a *get* even in situations involving documented physical, emotional, or sexual abuse. "Try to work it out" becomes a halachic default rooted in this Talmudic assessment of women's preferences. What began as a sociological generalization in the *gemara* functions in practice as a structurally powerful obstacle for women seeking to leave dangerous or degrading marriages. Reading this *gemara* in full — ending not with the neat statement of Reish Lakish but with the *braita*'s unsettling reversal — should at minimum prompt honest reflection on how these assumptions shape rabbinic decision-making today.
