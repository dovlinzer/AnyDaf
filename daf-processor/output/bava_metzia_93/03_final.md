# Bava Metzia 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Workers' Rights: Stipula…

### Stipulation Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קוֹצֵץ אָדָם עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, עַל יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן דַּעַת. אֲבָל אֵינוֹ קוֹצֵץ עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A man can stipulate on his own behalf** that he receive a certain increase in his wages instead of eating the produce with which he works, and similarly, he can stipulate this **on behalf of his adult son or daughter, on behalf of his adult** Canaanite **slave or** Canaanite **maidservant, or on behalf of his wife,** with their agreement, **because they have** the basic level of mental **competence,** i.e., they are legally competent and can therefore waive their rights. **But he cannot stipulate** this **on behalf of his minor son or daughter, nor on behalf of his minor** Canaanite **slave or** Canaanite **maidservant, nor on behalf of his animal, as they do not** have the basic level of mental **competence.**

The daf opens by completing the *mishna*'s discussion of workers' rights to eat from the produce with which they labor. The *mishna* enumerates who may and may not waive these rights through stipulation. A person may stipulate on behalf of himself, his adult children, his wife (*al yedei ishto*), and his adult slaves — negotiating, in exchange for higher wages, that they will forgo the right to eat. The rationale is that all of these parties possess *da'at* — the intelligence, will, and legal capacity to make such an arrangement. They can waive their own rights, or implicitly authorize someone else to represent them in doing so.

### Da'at & Rights

By contrast, a person cannot stipulate away the eating rights of his minor children, his minor slaves, or his Canaanite slaves. The *mishna* specifies that this is *mipnei she-ein bahem da'at* — because they lack the recognized legal will to forgo their entitlement. The most natural explanation, developed earlier in the tractate, is *mi-shmaya hu zachah*: the right to eat is not conceived as a component of the worker's salary that the employer or parent controls. It is a distinct right granted directly by the *Torah*, and therefore cannot be negotiated away by anyone other than the right-holder himself — provided that right-holder has the requisite *da'at*.

### Animal Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And he cannot stipulate** this **on behalf of his animal, as they do not** have the basic level of mental **competence.**

The *mishna* concludes this category by stating that a person cannot stipulate that his animal will forgo its right to eat — that is, he cannot contract for higher rent while agreeing to muzzle the animal. The stated reason, again, is the absence of *da'at*. As *Rashi* notes, however, the animal's case rests on an independent foundation: the prohibition against muzzling an animal while it threshes is not a function of wage negotiations at all. Even an owner cannot muzzle his own animal. The *mishna* groups the animal together with the minors under the heading of lacking *da'at*, but in truth the prohibition against muzzling is categorically independent of any contractual framework.

## Fourth-Year & Containers

### Fourth-Year Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֲלִים לַעֲשׂוֹת בְּנֶטַע רְבָעִי שֶׁלּוֹ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ. אִם לֹא הוֹדִיעָן – פּוֹדֶה וּמַאֲכִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who hires a laborer to perform** labor **with his fourth-year fruit,** such laborers **may not eat** the fruit. And **if he did not inform them** beforehand that they were working with fourth-year fruit, he must **redeem** the fruit **and feed them.**

The *mishna* then addresses cases in which workers are hired to perform labor connected to *neta revai* — fourth-year plantings, whose fruit cannot be eaten outside of Jerusalem. If an employer hires workers to do such labor without disclosing this fact, the workers cannot eat (since the fruit is legally restricted), but the employer bears the consequence of his non-disclosure: *podeh u'ma'achilan* — he must redeem the fruit and allow them to eat from the redeemed value.

### Broken Containers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְפָּרְסוּ עִגּוּלָיו, נִתְפַּתְּחוּ חָבִיּוֹתָיו – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ. אִם לֹא הוֹדִיעָן – מְעַשֵּׂר וּמַאֲכִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** If **his** fig **cakes broke apart** and crumbled, so that they must be preserved again, or if **his barrels** of wine **opened** and he hired workers to reseal them, **these** laborers **may not eat,** as the work of the figs or wine had already been completed with regard to tithes, from which point a laborer may not eat them. And **if he did not inform them, he must tithe** the food **and feed them.**

The *mishna* introduces parallel cases involving pressed figs whose rounds (*igulav*) have broken and scattered, or barrels (*chaviyotav*) that were opened and required resealing. In both cases, the labor being performed — reconsolidating or resealing — occurs after *gmar melacha*, the point at which the produce became obligated in *ma'aser*. The workers therefore cannot eat directly from the produce. Yet if the employer failed to disclose the situation and the workers reasonably expected to be entitled to eat, the rule is *me'aser u'ma'achilan*: the employer must separate *ma'aser* and then permit them to eat from what remains. The *Gemara* had addressed in the prior session the question of how workers could plausibly have been unaware that *gmar melacha* had already passed, and provided various explanations.

## Fruit Watchers

### Shomrei Peirot Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת אוֹכְלִין מֵהִלְכוֹת מְדִינָה, אֲבָל לֹא מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds: **Watchmen of produce may eat** the produce of the field or vineyard **by local regulations,** i.e., in accordance with the ordinances accepted by the residents of that place, **but not by Torah law.**

The *mishna* introduces a new and significant category: *shomrei peirot* — those who guard fruit. The *mishna* states that *shomrei peirot ochlin me-hilchot medina aval lo min ha-Torah*: fruit watchers may eat by virtue of the prevailing practice (*hilchot medina*), but not by Torah right. The phrase *hilchot medina* is striking — the word *hilchot* ordinarily connotes religious law, yet here it functions to describe the standard commercial practices and customs of the land, echoing the principle of *ha'kol k'minhag ha'medina* introduced earlier in the tractate. The *Gemara* now turns to explain why fruit watchers lack a Torah-based entitlement to eat, and in doing so generates a dispute between Rav and Shmuel.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שׁוֹמְרֵי גַנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִין. אֲבָל שׁוֹמְרֵי גִיתּוֹת וַעֲרֵימוֹת – אוֹכְלִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה. קָסָבַר: מְשַׁמֵּר כְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna mentions **watchmen of produce. Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **watchmen of gardens and orchards,** in which the produce is still attached to the ground, and therefore the watchman would have no legal right to it were it not for the local custom. **But watchmen of winepresses and piles** of detached produce **may eat** from them **by Torah law,** as the decisive factor is whether or not the produce is attached to the ground. Evidently, Rav **maintains** that one **who safeguards is considered like one who performs labor,** and therefore he has the status of a laborer.

Rav states: *lo shanu ela shomrei gannot u'pardesim* — the rule that watchers eat only by communal practice applies specifically to those watching orchards and vegetable gardens, where the produce remains *mechubar la'aretz*, still attached to the ground. The reason for the limitation is straightforward: when produce is *mechubar*, a worker's Torah right to eat arises only at the moment of *gmar melacha*, the actual harvesting. A watcher who is not harvesting but merely guarding the attached produce is not at the right stage of the work cycle to trigger the Torah entitlement, and so he eats only by practice.

By contrast, Rav holds that *shomrei gatot va'areimot* — those watching wine in the vats or bundles of cut wheat — are entitled to eat *min ha-Torah*. Why? Because Rav maintains *mishamer k'oseh ma'aseh dami*: watching produce is legally equivalent to performing labor with it. Since this watching takes place after harvest and before *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* purposes — the very window in which workers are Biblically entitled to eat — the watcher has a full Torah right.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שׁוֹמְרֵי גִיתּוֹת וַעֲרֵימוֹת. אֲבָל שׁוֹמְרֵי גַנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִים – אֵינָן אוֹכְלִים לֹא מִן הַתּוֹרָה וְלֹא מֵהִלְכוֹת מְדִינָה. קָא סָבַר מְשַׁמֵּר לָאו כְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **Shmuel says** that the Sages **taught** the *halakha* of the mishna, that they may eat by local regulations, **only** with regard to **watchmen of winepresses and piles** of detached produce. **But watchmen of gardens and orchards may not eat, neither by Torah law nor by local regulations.** This shows that Shmuel **holds** that **one who safeguards is not considered like one who performs labor,** and therefore no watchman is entitled to eat by Torah law. In the particular case of detached produce, there is a local custom to allow a watchman of detached produce to eat from it.

Shmuel disagrees and takes the opposite position. He states *lo shanu ela shomrei gatot va'areimot* — the rule that watchers eat by communal practice applies only to those watching after the harvest, in the vats and at the threshing floor. But *shomrei gannot u'pardesim* — watchers of growing orchards and gardens — do not eat at all: *lo min ha-Torah v'lo mei-hilchot medinah*. And critically, Shmuel's reasoning extends further: even if a worker is performing real labor with pre-harvest produce — weeding the vegetable garden, for instance — he still has no right to eat before the time of harvest. Nobody eats before the harvesting stage, regardless of what labor they are performing.

The underlying principle for Shmuel is *mishamer lav k'oseh ma'aseh dami*: watching is not truly equivalent to performing labor with the produce. The *Torah* grants eating rights only to one who is actively engaged in labor with the produce itself. Guarding, however valuable and necessary, is an activity external to the produce — one protects it from without rather than working directly upon it. Therefore, even though a watcher of post-harvest produce is in the correct time window (after harvest, before *gmar melacha*), he does not eat *min ha-Torah*, only *me-hilchot medinah*.

### Timeline Diagram

To clarify the dispute, consider the stages of produce: first it is *mechubar* (attached to the ground), then it is harvested and becomes *taloosh*, and finally it reaches *gmar melacha* for purposes of *ma'aser*. The Torah right to eat exists in two moments: for *mechubar* produce, only at the instant of harvesting (*b'sha'at gmar melacha*); for *taloosh* produce, throughout the period from harvest until *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*.

According to Rav, a watcher watching the growing orchard or garden eats *me-hilchot medinah* (the act of watching is real labor, but the time is wrong — too early), and a watcher watching post-harvest produce eats *min ha-Torah* (real labor at the right time). According to Shmuel, a watcher of growing produce does not eat at all, and a watcher of post-harvest produce eats only *me-hilchot medinah* (right time, but watching is not real labor).

### Gemara Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הַמְשַׁמֵּר אֶת הַפָּרָה – מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְשַׁמֵּר לָאו כְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי – אַמַּאי מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יָזִיז בָּהּ אֵבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna raises an objection** to this reasoning from a *baraita*: **One who safeguards the** red **heifer** after it has been burned **renders** his **garments impure,** in accordance with *halakha* concerning all those who take part in the ritual of the red heifer. The Torah decrees that all those who take part in the ritual of the red heifer contract impurity (Numbers, chapter 19). It is therefore necessary to establish which people are considered to have taken part in this ritual. **And if you say** that **one who safeguards is not considered like one who performs labor, why** does he **render** his **garments impure?** He has not performed any labor. **Rabba bar Ulla said:** He does not render them impure due to his work as a watchman; rather, this is a rabbinic **decree, lest he move a limb** of the heifer.

The *Gemara* challenges Shmuel's position from two tannaitic sources. The first challenge comes from Rav Acha bar Rav Huna: *ha-shomer et ha-parah mitameh begadim* — one who guards the *parah adumah* from the moment of *shechita* through the reduction to ashes contracts impurity through his garments, which implies that guarding is indeed treated as a form of direct engagement with the object. If *mishamer lav k'oseh ma'aseh dami*, why does the guardian of the *parah adumah* become impure? Rava bar Ulla responds that this impurity is only rabbinic — a *gezeirah shema yaziz ba'evar*, a precautionary enactment lest the watcher come to actually move a limb of the animal, which would generate Biblical impurity. Biblical law does not treat watching the *parah adumah* as equivalent to labor with it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: הַמְשַׁמֵּר אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ מִקְשָׁאוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְמַלֵּא כְּרֵסוֹ מֵאֶחָד מֵהֶן, אֶלָּא מִכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד אוֹכֵל לְפִי חֶשְׁבּוֹן. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְשַׁמֵּר לָאו כְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי – אַמַּאי אוֹכֵל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raises an objection:** With regard to **one who safeguards four or five cucumber fields,** which contain various types of cucumbers and gourds belonging to different people, **this one may not fill his stomach from** any single **one of them. Rather,** he must eat **from each and every one by a proportionate** amount. **But if you say** that **one who safeguards is not considered like one who performs labor, why** is he allowed to **eat** at all?

The second challenge comes from Rav Kahana: *ha-mishamer arba'ah va'chamisha mitzpa'ot* — if one is watching four or five cucumber gardens belonging to different owners, he should not fill himself from one of them. Rather, *mikol echad v'echad ochel lefi cheshbon*, he should distribute his eating proportionally across all the gardens. The implication is that the watcher is eating while the cucumbers are still growing in the ground — which would support Rav's position that watching growing produce entitles one to eat. Abaye presses this point: if *mishamer lav k'oseh ma'aseh dami* as Shmuel holds, why does the watcher eat at all in this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: בַּעֲקוּרִין שָׁנוּ. עֲקוּרִין – וַהֲלֹא נִגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתָּן לְמַעֲשֵׂר? שֶׁלֹּא נִיטַּל פֵּיקֶס שֶׁלָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: They taught this** *halakha* **with regard to uprooted** cucumbers, concerning which even Shmuel agrees that a watchman may eat them by local regulations. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Uprooted? But** at that stage **hasn't their work** already **been completed with regard to tithes,** and therefore no laborer should be permitted to eat them? The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **when their blossom had not** yet **been removed.** Since the cucumbers still require work, they are not yet subject to tithes.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi resolves this: *b'akurei shanu* — the *mishna* is speaking of cucumbers that have already been pulled from the ground. They are already *taloosh*. The *Gemara* then asks: but once cucumbers are pulled, have they not reached *gmar melacha* and become obligated in *ma'aser*? The answer is *shelo nitlu piksa shelahem* — the blossom has not yet been removed, so *gmar melacha* has not yet been reached. Thus even in this case, Shmuel's framework is preserved: we are in the post-harvest, pre-*gmar melacha* window, the watcher eats *me-hilchot medinah*, and we are not dealing with watching produce that is still attached to the ground.

### Rav Ashi's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דִּתְנַן, וְאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה: הָעוֹשֶׂה בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר מְלָאכָה, וּבְתָלוּשׁ כּוּ׳. מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא דְּלָא קָא אָכֵיל מִן הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא מֵהִלְכוֹת מְדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: It stands to reason** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as we learned** in a mishna (87a): **And these** laborers **may eat by Torah law:** A laborer **who performs** labor **with** produce **attached to the ground at the time of the completion of** its **work,** e.g., harvesting produce; **and** a laborer who performs labor **with** produce **detached** from the ground before the completion of its work. The mishna's phrase: By Torah law, proves **by inference that** with regard to detached produce **there is one who does not eat by Torah law but by local regulations.**

Rav Ashi provides an elegant argument that Shmuel's position is the more logical fit with the *mishna*: *k'voseh d'Shmuel mistavra*. The earlier *mishna* opens by declaring that certain workers eat *min ha-Torah* — *ha'oseh b'mechubar l'karka b'sha'at gmar melacha*, one who works with attached produce at the harvesting stage, *u'vetalush ad shelo nigmerah melachto l'ma'aser*, and one who works with harvested produce until *gmar melacha*. The phrase *min ha-Torah* implies by inference (*michlal*) that some eat not *min ha-Torah* but only *me-hilchot medinah*. Later, the *mishna* states that the following do not eat: those working with *mechubar* produce before harvesting, and those working with *taloosh* produce after *gmar melacha*. The word "do not eat" in the *seifa*, argues Rav Ashi, must mean do not eat at all — not merely that they do not eat *min ha-Torah*. If it meant only the latter, the *seifa* would merely repeat what was implied by the *reisha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵינָן אוֹכְלִין. מַאי אֵינָן אוֹכְלִין? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁאֵין אוֹכְלִין מִן הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא מֵהִלְכוֹת מְדִינָה – הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא, אֶלָּא לָאו שֶׁאֵין אוֹכְלִין לֹא מִן הַתּוֹרָה וְלֹא מֵהִלְכוֹת מְדִינָה. וּמַאי נִיהוּ – עוֹשֶׂה בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין גְּמַר מְלָאכָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שׁוֹמְרֵי גַנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its proof: Now, **say the latter clause** of that mishna: **And these may not eat. What is** the meaning of: **May not eat? If we say** this means **that they may not eat by Torah law but by local regulations, this is** the same as **the first clause. Rather, is it not** correct to say that it means **they may not eat** at all, **neither by Torah law nor by local regulations? And who are** the people included in this list? They are **one who performs** labor **with** produce **attached to the ground at a time when it** has **not** reached **the completion of** its **work, and all the more so watchmen of gardens and orchards,** who do not perform any significant action.

This creates a problem for Rav. If working with attached produce before harvest means not eating at all, there is no room in Rav's framework for the category of eating *me-hilchot medinah* while watching growing produce. The *seifa* has closed off that scenario entirely. But for Shmuel, the framework works perfectly: the space for *hilchot medinah* is not a different time period, but a different mode of engagement — watching post-harvest produce at the right time but without performing real labor. That scenario falls neither into the *reisha*'s category of Torah entitlement nor into the *seifa*'s category of total non-entitlement, and it is precisely there that communal practice creates a right to eat.

### Shmuel Prevails

The conclusion is that Shmuel's position is the more logically coherent one. Watching (*mishmar*) is not equivalent to performing labor (*oseh ma'aseh*). The Torah entitles a worker to eat only when he is directly engaged in labor with the produce. A watcher, however essential his role, stands outside the produce rather than working upon it. His right to eat, when he has one at all, derives from the norms of the land — *hilchot medinah* — and not from any Biblical provision.

## Four Watchers

### Four Types Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִים הֵן: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר. שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נִשְׁבָּע עַל הַכֹּל, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are four** types of **bailees,** to whom different *halakhot* apply. They are as follows: **An unpaid bailee,** who receives no compensation for safeguarding the item; **and the borrower** of an item for his own use; **a paid bailee,** who is provided with a salary for watching over an item; **and a renter,** i.e., a bailee who pays a fee for the use of a vessel or animal. If the item was stolen, lost, or broken, or if the animal died in any manner, their *halakhot* are as follows: **An unpaid bailee takes an oath over every** outcome; whether the item was lost, stolen, or broken, or if the animal died, the unpaid bailee must take an oath that it happened as he described, and he is then exempt from payment. **The borrower** does not take an oath, but **pays** for **every** outcome, even in a circumstance beyond his control.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר נִשְׁבָּעִים עַל הַשְּׁבוּרָה וְעַל הַשְּׁבוּיָה וְעַל הַמֵּתָה, וּמְשַׁלְּמִין אֶת הָאֲבֵידָה וְאֶת הַגְּנֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *halakhot* of **a paid bailee and a renter** are the same: They **take an oath over an injured** animal, **over a captured** animal, **and over a dead** animal, attesting that the mishaps were caused by circumstances beyond their control, and they are exempt, **but they must pay** for **loss or theft.**

The *mishna* now opens the subject of *shomrim* — guardians — a topic that will occupy the remainder of the tractate. The *mishna* states: *arba shomrim hein* — there are four categories. The *shomer chinam*: one who watches for free, receiving no benefit. The *sho'el* (borrower): one who uses another's object without paying, receiving all the benefit. The *nosei sachar* (paid watcher): one who is compensated specifically for the act of guarding. And the *socher* (renter): one who pays for the use of an object and thus enters into a reciprocal exchange. What these four have in common is that each holds another's property and thus functions, in some sense, as a guardian.

Their liability levels vary. The *shomer chinam*, who provides a pure benefit to the owner with no reciprocal gain, has the lowest obligation: *nishba al hakol* — he swears regarding any loss that he was not negligent, and is thereby exempt. The *sho'el*, who receives all the benefit and gives nothing, has the highest obligation: *meshalem et hakol* — he pays for any loss, even those resulting from *ones*, an unpreventable accident. The *nosei sachar* and the *socher* occupy the middle tier. They swear and are exempt for true *ones* — events such as breaking, captivity, or death — but they pay for theft and loss (*gneiva va'aveida*), categories that, while not gross negligence, represent a failure to guard at the level their role demands.

### Author & Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מִי אִיכָּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ, מַאן תָּנָא שׂוֹכֵר כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר – רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who taught this mishna about **four** types of **bailees? Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: It is Rabbi Meir. Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Is there** any Sage **who does not** accept the *halakha* concerning **four** types of **bailees?** All of the Sages agree that the Torah spoke of these four types of bailees. Rav Naḥman **said to him: This is what I am saying to you,** i.e., I mean as follows: **Who is the *tanna*** who maintains that the *halakha* of **a renter** is **like** that of **a paid bailee? It is Rabbi Meir.**

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *man tana arba'ah shomrim* — who is the author of this *mishna*? Rav Nachman states in the name of Bar Abbuha that it is Rabbi Meir. Rava challenges Rav Nachman, pointing out that everyone agrees there are four types of *shomrim*. Rav Nachman clarifies: the real question is *man tana socher k'nosei sachar* — who holds that the *socher* is categorized together with the *shomer sachar* rather than the *shomer chinam*? The answer is Rabbi Meir, and this is the position the *mishna* adopts. Rava then notes that despite four distinct roles, there are only three legal categories (*dinehem shlosha*): total exemption for gross negligence only, liability for anything short of *ones*, and liability even for *ones*.

### Socher Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: שׂוֹכֵר כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֵּם? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר! רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אִיפְּכָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we hear** that **Rabbi Meir** said **the opposite, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a renter,** whose *halakha* is not stated in the Torah, **how does he pay,** i.e., in which cases is he liable to pay? **Rabbi Meir says:** He pays in the same cases **as an unpaid bailee; Rabbi Yehuda says** he pays in the same cases **as a paid bailee.** The Gemara explains: **Rabba bar Avuh teaches** this *baraita* in the **opposite** manner to the version here.

The *Gemara* immediately complicates the attribution to Rabbi Meir. A *braita* reports Rabbi Meir as saying the opposite: *socher keitzad meshalem — Rabbi Meir omer k'shomer chinam*, that the *socher* is liable only at the level of the *shomer chinam*, since his guardianship is incidental to his primary role as a user and renter. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds *k'shomer sachar*, that the *socher*'s reciprocal exchange relationship elevates his liability to that of the paid watcher. The *stam mishna* — whose anonymous formulation ordinarily follows Rabbi Meir — would thus seem to follow Rabbi Yehuda. The *Gemara* resolves this by reporting that Rabba bar Abbuha's version of the *braita* has the attributions reversed: Rabbi Meir holds the *socher* liable like a *shomer sachar*, consistent with our *mishna*, and Rabbi Yehuda holds like a *shomer chinam*. This resolution preserves the principle that the *stam mishna* reflects Rabbi Meir's view, and it is Rabbi Meir's position — that the *socher* bears the liability of a *shomer sachar* — that we rule by.

## Paid Watcher Standards

### River Sheep Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא רָעֲיָא דַּהֲוָה קָא רָעֵי חֵיוָתָא אַגּוּדָּא דִּנְהַר פָּפָּא. שְׂרִיג חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ וּנְפַלַת לְמַיָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה וּפַטְרֵיהּ. אָמַר: מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain shepherd who was herding animals on the bank of the Pappa River,** when **one of them slipped and fell into the water** and drowned. **He came before Rabba, and** Rabba **exempted him** from payment. Rabba **stated** the following reasoning in support of his ruling: **What could he have done?** A drowning of this kind is a circumstance beyond his control, and although a shepherd is a paid bailee he is exempt from liability in circumstances beyond his control.

Having established the framework of the four *shomrim* and their respective liability levels, the *Gemara* turns to the practical question of what standard of care is expected of a *shomer sachar*. The first case is stark: *hahu raya d'hava ka rai chivata aguda d'nahar pappa* — a shepherd was grazing animals along the bank of the river Pappa, and one of the sheep slipped and fell into the water. The case came before Rabba.

### Rabba's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נְטַר כִּדְנָטְרִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **He safeguarded them** in the manner **that people safeguard,** and he is not required to do anything more.

Rabba exempted the shepherd. His reasoning: *natri k'dnatri inshi* — he watched in the manner that normal people watch. This ruling is striking and deserves careful attention. One might assume that when the *Gemara* says a *shomer sachar* is liable for anything short of *ones*, it means liability whenever the loss was theoretically preventable — i.e., had the watcher exerted maximum effort, would the loss have been averted? Rabba rejects this framing entirely. The standard is not theoretical maximum prevention. The standard is contextual and norm-based: did the *shomer* watch in the way that people normally watch? If yes, any resulting loss is classified as *ones* from his perspective, and he is exempt.

This is a genuinely radical position. If the standard of care is purely contextual, then the line between negligence (*pshia*) and non-liability is not an objective threshold but a function of local norms and accepted practices. One might ask: if that is so, what distinguishes a *shomer chinam* from a *shomer sachar* at all? Perhaps *natri inshi* defines the baseline for a *shomer sachar*, while *shomer chinam* is only liable for something egregiously below that norm — gross and obvious negligence. Alternatively, there may be objective thresholds of *pshia* and *ones* at the extremes, with the norm-based standard (*natri inshi*) determining the boundary for the intermediate category of *shomer sachar* liability. Whatever the precise framework, Rabba's ruling establishes that there is no purely objective test of preventability.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, עָל לְמָתָא בְּעִידָּנָא דְּעָיְילִי אִינָשֵׁי – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָטוּר?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. גְּנָא פּוּרְתָּא בְּעִידָּנָא דְּגָנוּ אִינָשֵׁי – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָטוּר?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If that is so,** in a case where **he entered the city at a time when** other **people enter,** as shepherds normally do, when their animals are grazing in a quiet and safe place, and a theft occurred at that hour, **so too** will you say that he is **exempt?** Rabba **said to him: Yes.** Abaye raised a further difficulty: If **he slept a little at a time when people** generally **sleep, so too** is he **exempt?** Rabba **said to him: Yes.**

Abaye probes Rabba's principle by shifting from the degree of watching to its timing. He poses the following case: a shepherd left his flock and entered town for lunch (*b'idna d'ayli inshi*) — at the normal time when shepherds take their midday break — and during that time something happened to the animals. Is he exempt? Rabba says yes. What about an afternoon siesta (*b'idna deganu inshi*)? If that too is normal practice — if it is standard for shepherds to nap for a few minutes at that hour — yes, he is exempt as well.

This is even more striking than the initial ruling. The *shomer sachar* was, during those moments, performing no guarding whatsoever. Yet because the norm is that these breaks are taken, his role as *shomer* simply does not extend to those intervals. The scope of his obligation is defined not by continuous responsibility for the object, but by the customary practice of people in his position. The norm determines not only how one watches, but when one is obligated to watch at all.

### Tannaitic Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אֵלּוּ הֵם אוֹנָסִין שֶׁשּׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר פָּטוּר עֲלֵיהֶן – כְּגוֹן: ״וַתִּפֹּל שְׁבָא וַתִּקָּחֵם וְאֶת הַנְּעָרִים הִכּוּ לְפִי חָרֶב״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם בְּחַזָּנֵי מָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to him** from a *baraita*: **These are** the **circumstances beyond one's control for which a paid bailee is exempt: For example,** as it is stated in the verse: "The oxen were plowing, and the donkeys feeding beside them. **And the Sabeans made a raid and took them away, and they have slain the servants with the edge of the sword"** (Job 1:15). This teaches that only a robbery by an army is considered a circumstance beyond his control, but nothing less. Rabba **said to him: There** it is referring **to city watchmen,** i.e., professionals hired to watch over city property, who are exempt due to an occurrence on that scale, i.e., a military incursion.

The *Gemara* presses Rabba with a series of tannaitic sources that appear to demand an elevated standard of care from a paid watcher. The first source lists the paradigm of *ones* for a *shomer sachar*: the destruction visited upon the household of Job, where armies descended and took everything. If that is the archetype of *ones*, the implication is that anything less catastrophic does not qualify — suggesting an objectively high standard, not a norm-based one. Rabba responds: *hatam bechazanei mata* — that source speaks specifically of city guards, whose role demands constant, elevated vigilance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: עַד מָתַי שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר חַיָּיב לִשְׁמוֹר – עַד כְּדֵי ״הָיִיתִי בַיּוֹם אֲכָלַנִי חֹרֶב וְקֶרַח בַּלַּיְלָה״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם נָמֵי בְּחַזָּנֵי מָתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ חַזַּן מָתָא הֲוָה! דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְלָבָן: נְטַרִי לָךְ נְטִירוּתָא יַתִּירְתָּא כְּחַזָּנֵי מָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rabba from another *baraita*: **To what extent is a paid bailee obligated to safeguard?** He is obligated **to the extent** that Jacob said to Laban: **"Thus I was: In the day the drought consumed me, and the frost by night"** (Genesis 31:40). Rava **said to him: There too,** the *baraita* is speaking **of city watchmen,** whose responsibility extends further. Abaye **said to him: Is that to say** that **Jacob, our forefather,** whose statement is the source of this *halakha*, **was a city watchman?** Rava replied: It means **that** Jacob **said to Laban: I safeguarded for you an extra** level of **safeguarding, like** that of **city watchmen.**

A second source cites the famous lament of Jacob (*Yaakov*): *hayiti va'yom achalani chorev vekerach balaila* — "By day the heat consumed me, and the frost by night." This evokes a standard of unbroken, arduous attention, hardly consistent with afternoon naps and lunch breaks. Rabba again responds that this source concerns city guards, not ordinary shepherds. Abaye presses back: was *Yaakov Avinu* a city guard? He was manifestly a shepherd. Rabba answers: indeed he was — but Yaakov himself told Lavan *natarti lach netiruta yetirata*, I guarded for you with the vigilance of a city guard. Yaakov was going beyond what was required of him, performing an act of exceptional dedication. The *braita* does not extract from Yaakov's words a normative standard for shepherds generally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: רוֹעֶה שֶׁהָיָה רוֹעֶה וְהִנִּיחַ עֶדְרוֹ וּבָא לָעִיר, בָּא זְאֵב וְטָרַף, וּבָא אֲרִי וְדָרַס – אֵין אוֹמְרִים: אִילּוּ הָיָה שָׁם הָיָה מַצִּיל. אֶלָּא אוֹמְדִין אוֹתוֹ: אִם יָכוֹל לְהַצִּיל – חַיָּיב, אִם לָאו – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rabba from another *baraita*: With regard to **a shepherd who was herding** the animals of others, **and he left his flock and came to the town,** if in the meantime **a wolf came and tore** an animal to pieces, **or a lion came and trampled** one of his flock, **we do not say** definitively that **had he been there he would have rescued** them and therefore he is liable due to his absence. **Rather,** the court **estimates** with regard to **him: If he could** have **rescued** his animal by chasing a beast of this kind away, he is **liable,** as his departure from the scene was certainly a contributing factor to the damage. **If not,** he is **exempt** from liability.

A third tannaitic challenge involves a shepherd who left his flock, went to town, and during his absence a wolf or lion attacked. The *braita* states: *ein omrim ilu haya sham haya matzil* — we do not simply say that had he remained he would have saved the flock. Rather, we assess: *im yachol lehatzil chayav, v'im lav patur* — if he could have protected them had he been there, he is liable; if not, he is exempt. This seems to suggest that leaving the flock creates liability contingent on preventability — apparently cutting against Rabba's norm-based approach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו דְּעָל בְּעִידָּנָא דְּעָיְילִי אִינָשֵׁי? לָא, דְּעָל בְּעִידָּנָא דְּלָא עָיְילִי אִינָשֵׁי. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי פָּטוּר? תְּחִילָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס, חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **What, is** this *baraita* **not** referring to a case **when** the shepherd **enters** the town **at a time when** other **people** usually **enter?** If so, it presents a difficulty to the opinion of Rabba. Rabba responds: **No,** it is speaking **of one who enters at a time when** other **people do not** usually **enter.** Abaye retorts: **If so, why is he exempt** even if he could not have rescued the animal? This is a mishap that came about **initially through negligence and ultimately by accident,** and in a case of this kind he is **liable** due to his negligence.

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing: the shepherd in this case did not leave at the normal break time. He left at an unusual time (*b'idna d'lo ayli inshi*), which already constitutes a deviation from norms. But then the question arises: if his leaving was already irregular and thus a form of *pshia*, why is he ever exempt? The rule of *t'chilato bipshiah v'sofo b'ones* — when one begins with negligence and the loss is completed by an unforeseeable event, he is still liable. Why then does preventability matter?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּשְׁמַע קָל אַרְיָה וְעָל. אִי הָכִי, אוֹמְדִין אוֹתוֹ? מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד?! הָיָה לוֹ לְקַדֵּם בְּרוֹעִים וּבְמַקְלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: It is referring to one **who heard the sound of a lion** roaring **and entered** the city to save himself. In such a case, his actions were not initially negligent, but rather, it was a circumstance beyond his control. Abaye questions this response: **If so,** what is the relevance of the statement: The court **estimates** with regard to **him? What could he have done** to prevent an attack by a lion? Rabba replies: **He should have faced** the lion **with** other **shepherds and with sticks** to chase it away.

The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing a third scenario: the shepherd heard a lion roar and then departed. This is neither standard negligence nor a standard break — it is a response to a perceived threat, and what he should have done instead was organize the other shepherds and arm them with sticks (*hava lei l'kadumi b'roei u'b'maklot*). His failure to do so was a degree of negligence, but not gross negligence. Therefore, if the flock would have survived had he stayed, his partial negligence renders him liable; if the flock would have been lost regardless, his non-gross negligence does not suffice to hold him responsible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר? אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נָמֵי, דְּהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לְקַדֵּם בְּרוֹעִים וּבְמַקְלוֹת וְלֹא קִידֵּם – חַיָּיב. שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם בְּחִנָּם, שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר בְּשָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye asks: **If so, why specifically** state this *halakha* with regard to **a paid bailee?** The same would hold true **even** for **an unpaid bailee, as wasn't it** you, **Master, who said** that **an unpaid bailee who had** the option **of facing** an animal **with** other **shepherds and with sticks and did not face** it in this manner is **liable,** as he is considered negligent in his duty? Rabba answers: **An unpaid bailee** is liable only if he could have gathered together other shepherds to help him defend his animals **free of charge,** whereas **a paid bailee** is obligated to do so even **for payment.**

The *Gemara* then asks: if the liability here stems from this partial negligence, why does it apply only to a *shomer sachar*? Even a *shomer chinam* should presumably be obligated to rally the shepherds when he hears a lion coming. The distinction, the *Gemara* explains, is financial: a *shomer chinam* is not required to spend money out of pocket to hire protectors, while a *shomer sachar*, who is being paid for his guardianship, must actually hire helpers up to the full value of the flock. He then recovers those expenses from the owner (*dahadar shakil damei miba'al habayit*), but he is obligated to advance the funds. The owner would prefer to incur this expense and keep the flock — both to maintain the animals he is habituated to (*le'akushi dechiyuta*) and to avoid the labor of acquiring a replacement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה? עַד כְּדֵי דְמֵיהֶן. וְהֵיכָן מָצִינוּ בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁחַיָּיב בָּאוֹנָסִין? דַּהֲדַר שָׁקֵיל דְּמֵיהֶן מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And up to how much** must a paid bailee pay for this extra protection? The Gemara answers: **Up to** the sum **of the value** of the animals he is responsible to safeguard. The Gemara further asks: **But** in that case, it seems that a paid bailee must pay from his own pocket to protect the animals from marauding beasts; **where have we found with regard to a paid bailee that he is liable for circumstances beyond his control?** Everyone agrees that this loss was caused by circumstances beyond the bailee's control, and yet he must bear the expenses indirectly. The Gemara responds: The *halakha* is **that he may return and take the money** that he paid for these additional guards **from the owner.**

### Bar Adda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר אַדָּא סָבוֹלָאָה (הָוֵי) [הֲוָה] קָא מְעַבַּר חֵיוָתָא אַגַּמְלָא דְנַרֶשׁ. דְּחַפָה חֲדָא לַחֲבֶרְתַּהּ וּשְׁדָיתַהּ בְּמַיָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, חַיְּיבֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי הֲוָה לִי לְמֶעְבַּד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִבְּעִי לָךְ לְעַבּוֹרֵי חֲדָא חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Bar Adda the porter was transporting animals across the narrow bridge of Neresh** when **one** animal **pushed another and cast it into the water,** and it drowned. The case **came before Rav Pappa,** who **deemed him liable.** Bar Adda **said to him: What should I have done?** Rav Pappa **said to him: You should have transported them one by one.**

The *Gemara* introduces the case of Bar Adda Sabola, who was leading animals across the bridge of Narash. One animal pushed another off, and it fell into the water. The case came before Rav Papa, who held him liable. When Bar Adda protested — *mai hava li lema'avad*, what could I have done? — Rav Papa responded: *ibayei lach le'avuri chada chada*, you should have moved them single file. This is precisely the position that argues against Rabba. Bar Adda did not fall below the accepted norm; shepherds routinely move animals across bridges in groups. But Rav Papa holds that he did less than was maximally possible, and that is sufficient for liability.

### Rav Papa's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָדְעַתְּ בֵּיהּ בְּבַר אֲחָתָיךְ דְּמָצֵי (ל)מְעַבַּר חֲדָא חֲדָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבָר צְוַוחוּ קַמָּאֵי דְקַמָּךְ וְלָא אִיכָּא דְּאַשְׁגַּח בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The porter **said to him: Do you know of the son of your sister who can transport** them **one by one?** In other words, are you aware of anyone who can do such a thing? It is virtually impossible. Rav Pappa **said to him: The very earliest** scholars **before you already shouted** and complained about this, **but none paid attention to them.** Since you are an expert and were hired for this purpose, the responsibility is yours.

Bar Adda challenged Rav Papa: *yadit bah bevar achvotach dematzi lemiavad chada chada?* — do you really expect a fellow Jew to move animals single file across a bridge? This is an unrealistic demand, far outside the norm of professional shepherding. Rav Papa's reply is firm: *kevar shevikna kamchei dekamchit velo ika de'ashgach behu* — I have heard these complaints before and I am not moved by them. The norms of the profession may remain as they are, but so does the liability. If you want to avoid paying, you do what is maximally possible. The practical upshot of Rav Papa's position is nuanced: he does not abolish the norm, he simply refuses to let the norm serve as an exemption. You may continue to lead your animals in groups — but if one falls, you pay.

## Theft & Reimbursement

### Flax Theft Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְבוּ אַפְקֵיד כִּיתָּנָא בֵּי רוּנְיָא. אֲזַל שַׁבּוּ שַׁמְטֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ. לְסוֹף הוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, חַיְּיבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Aivu deposited flax** with a bailee in the **house of Ronya.** A robber called **Shabbu went and** forcibly **snatched** the flax **from him. Eventually the thief was identified** and caught. The matter **came before Rav Naḥman,** who **rendered** the bailee **liable.**

The *Gemara* now addresses a case involving a *shomer* whose charge was stolen by an armed thief. Aivo deposited some flax at the house of Runya, and the armed thief known as Shavu came and stole it. Eventually Shavu was apprehended and identified. The central question is who bears the burden of recovering the stolen property: must the *shomer* first pay the owner and then pursue the thief, or can the *shomer* step aside and let owner and thief settle matters directly?

### Rav Nachman Ruling

The case came before Rav Nachman, who ruled: the *shomer* pays the owner and then pursues the thief for recovery. Responsibility cannot simply be discharged by pointing to the thief; the *shomer's* obligation to the owner persists regardless.

### Free vs. Paid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּשְׁלַח רַב הוּנָא בַּר אָבִין: נִגְנְבָה בְּאוֹנֶס וְאַחַר כָּךְ הוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב, אִם שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם הוּא – רָצָה נִשְׁבָּע, רָצָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין. אִם שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר הוּא – עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין וְאֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As Rav Huna bar Avin sent** this ruling: In a case where an animal **was stolen in a circumstance beyond his control, and the thief was subsequently identified** and captured, **if** the bailee **is an unpaid bailee,** the following distinction applies: If **he wishes, he takes an oath** that he did not misappropriate the animal before it was stolen, and the owners must claim its value from the thief; if **he wishes, he enters into judgment with** the thief, by claiming the money directly from him. **If he is a paid bailee, he enters into judgment with** the thief **and does not take an oath.** Since the ruling of Rav Naḥman was in the case of an unpaid bailee, why did he have to deal with the thief?

Rav Huna bar Avin sent a ruling that illuminates the distinction between a *shomer chinam* and a *shomer sachar* in precisely this scenario. If flax was stolen in circumstances constituting *ones* — by an armed bandit — and the thief was subsequently identified: if the watcher is a *shomer chinam*, he has a choice. *Ratza nishba ratza oseh imo din* — he may either swear to the owner that he was not negligent (and the owner then pursues the thief directly), or he may pay the owner and then recover from the thief himself. A *shomer chinam*, whose liability never extended to *ones* in the first place, retains the option of simply exiting the picture via oath. But if the watcher is a *shomer sachar*, he has no such option: *oseh imo din ve'eino nishba*. Even though this was an *ones* and he bears no financial loss, he must pay the owner and recover from the thief. Being exempt from personal liability does not eliminate his ongoing responsibility as a custodian. The exemption from *ones* means he will not be out of pocket — but his relationship to the owner's property requires him to be the active party in resolving the loss.

### Negligence Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָתָם גַּבְרֵי דְּפַרְמוּסְקָא הֲווֹ קָיְימִי, דְּאִי רְמָא קָלָא הֲווֹ אָתוּ וּמַצְּלִין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The cases are not the same, as **there,** in the ruling of Rav Naḥman, **men from the government were standing there,** which means **that if he had raised his voice they would have come and rescued him.** Since it was his own negligence that caused the robbery, he must find a way to collect the money from the robber.

Rabba qualifies the case. The armed bandit scenario would ordinarily qualify as *ones* and exempt even a *shomer sachar* from any affirmative obligation. But Rabba notes that in the original case, there were *gavrei deparmustaka* — government officers, people of authority — nearby. Had the shepherd raised his voice, they would have come and rescued the flock. Since he failed to call for help that was readily available, what appeared to be *ones* was in fact negligence. Because the loss began with *pshia*, the *shomer* must pay the owner and then pursue the thief — and may not shelter behind the technical classification of *ones*.

## Ones Thresholds

### Ones Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זְאֵב אֶחָד – אֵינוֹ אוֹנֶס. שְׁנֵי זְאֵבִים – אוֹנֶס. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁעַת מִשְׁלַחַת זְאֵבִים, אַף זְאֵב אֶחָד אוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One wolf** that approaches a flock and attacks **is not** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control,** as the shepherd can drive it away, but an attack by **two wolves** is considered **a circumstance beyond one's control. Rabbi Yehuda says: At a time of wolf attacks,** when many wolves come out of hiding and pounce on animals at every corner, **even** an attack by **one wolf is** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control.**

The *mishna* now enumerates specific scenarios and classifies each as either *ones* (unpreventable accident, from which a *shomer sachar* is exempt) or not. The categories include wolves, dogs, predatory animals, bandits, and natural death.

### Wolf Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי כְלָבִים – אֵינוֹ אוֹנֶס. יָדוּעַ הַבַּבְלִי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מֵרוּחַ אַחַת – אֵינוֹ אוֹנֶס, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת – אוֹנֶס. הַלִּסְטִים – הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹנֶס. הָאֲרִי הַדּוֹב וְהַנָּמֵר וְהַבַּרְדְּלָס וְהַנָּחָשׁ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** An attack by **two dogs is not** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control. Yadua the Babylonian says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** If the two dogs came and attacked **from one direction it is not** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control,** but if they attacked **from two directions,** this is considered **a circumstance beyond one's control,** as the shepherd cannot protect his flock from both of them at once. If **bandits** came, **this is** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control.** Likewise, with regard to an attack by **a lion, a bear, a leopard, a cheetah, and a snake, these are** each considered **a circumstance beyond one's control.**

The *mishna* states: *zeev echad eino ones*, a single wolf is not *ones* — the shepherd should have been able to fend it off; *shenei zeevim ones*, two wolves constitute *ones* — he could not be expected to fight both simultaneously. Rabbi Yehuda adds: *bish'at mishlachat zeevim af zeev echad ones* — during a period when wolves are running wild and attacking aggressively, even a single wolf is classified as *ones*. The *Gemara* later confirms that the *braita* citing a single wolf as *ones* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view applied to the season of wolf-running, *rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* identifying the two sources as non-contradictory on this basis. On dogs: *shenei kelavim eino ones* — two dogs together is still not *ones*. Yadu'a HaBavli citing Rabbi Meir adds: *miruach achat eino ones, mishtei ruchot ones* — if the dogs approach from one direction, the shepherd can manage; if they come from two directions simultaneously, he cannot, and it is *ones*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁבָּאוּ מֵאֲלֵיהֶן. אֲבָל הוֹלִיכָן לִמְקוֹם גְּדוּדֵי חַיָּה וְלִסְטִים – אֵינוֹ אוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** **When** is an attack by one of the above considered beyond his control, which means that a paid bailee is exempt? It is **when** the dangerous beasts or bandits **came of their own accord** to the usual grazing spot. **But** if the shepherd **led** his flock **to a place of groups of beasts or bandits,** this **is not** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control,** as he is at fault.

### Bandit Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָתַנְיָא: זְאֵב אֶחָד – אוֹנֶס! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָהִיא בִּשְׁעַת מִשְׁלַחַת זְאֵבִים, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that an attack by **one wolf is** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: That** *baraita* is speaking **of a time of wolf attacks, and it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלִּסְטִים – הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹנֶס. אַמַּאי? לוֹקֵי גַּבְרָא לַהֲדֵי גַּבְרָא! אָמַר רַב: בְּלִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if **bandits came, this is** considered **a circumstance beyond one's control.** The Gemara asks: **Why? Let one man stand against another man.** The shepherd should defend his flock and fight the bandit, as that is what he was hired to do. **Rav said:** The mishna is referring **to armed bandits,** against whom the shepherd cannot reasonably defend the flock.

The *mishna* states that *halistim* — bandits — constitute *ones*. The *Gemara* asks: why? *Lokei gavra lahadei gavra* — it is one person against one person. Should not the shepherd be expected to stand and fight? Rav answers: *be-listim mezuyan* — the bandit is armed. Against an armed attacker, the shepherd is not expected to risk his life or physical wellbeing.

### Armed Shepherd

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין וְרוֹעֶה מְזוּיָּין, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן אוֹקֵי גַּבְרָא לַהֲדֵי גַּבְרָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: הַאי מָסַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ, וְהַאי לָא מָסַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ? מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּהַאי מָסַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ, וְהַאי לָא מָסַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the case involved **an armed bandit and an armed shepherd, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say, let one man stand** and fight **against another man, or perhaps this** bandit is willing to **risk his life and that** shepherd **is not** prepared to **risk his life?** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason that this one** will **risk his life and that one** would **not risk his life.** Consequently, the shepherd is not held responsible if he refuses to fight the bandit.

The *Gemara* then poses the question: what if both the shepherd and the bandit are armed equally — *listis mezuyan ve-ro'eh mezuyan*? Is the shepherd expected to fight? The *Gemara* resolves this with a principled distinction: *hai masar nafshai ve-hai lo masar nafshai* — the thief chose to risk his life when he set out to rob; the shepherd never agreed to such a risk. A *shomer* does not contractually commit to endangering himself. Therefore, even an equally armed shepherd is not required to fight, and the loss is classified as *ones*. One could, of course, stipulate otherwise by explicit contract — as when one hires trained armed guards with an explicit commitment to engage — but absent such agreement, the shepherd's baseline is that he need not put his life at risk.

### Negligent Disclosure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא: אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רוֹעֶה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּנָּבָא סַרְיָא! בְּדוּכְתָּא פְּלָנְיָא יְתִיבִינַן, כָּךְ וְכָךְ גַּבְרֵי אִיכָּא בַּהֲדַן, כָּךְ וְכָךְ כַּלְבֵי אִיכָּא בַּהֲדַן, כָּךְ וְכָךְ זוּקָתָא פְּסִיקָא לַן. וַאֲזַל וּשְׁקַל מִינֵּיהּ, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי הוֹלִיכָן לִמְקוֹם גְּדוּדֵי חַיָּה וְלִסְטִים.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to Rava:** What is the *halakha* if the **shepherd found** a known thief **and said to him: You dirty thief! In such and such a place we** shepherds **sit; we have such and such men with us; such and such dogs with us,** and **such and such slings [*zukata*] with us.** In other words, we are fully protected and you should not dare to take anything from us. If the thief subsequently **went and took** an animal **from him, what** is the *halakha*? Rava **said to** Abaye: It is as though **he has taken them to a place of groups of beasts and bandits,** as his taunting of the thief motivated the theft.

The final case of the daf is a memorable illustration of what converts an *ones* into negligence. Abaye poses: a shepherd encountered a known thief and, apparently to intimidate him, revealed the flock's precise location — *be-duchta planya yatbinan*, we are encamped over there at that spot on the river — along with detailed information about the number of guards, the number of dogs, and the number of slingshots available. The thief thanked him for the information and later came and stole the flock. What is the law? Rava responds without hesitation: *harei holichan le-makom gedudei chaya ve-listim* — this is equivalent to bringing the flock directly into the territory of wild animals or bandits. The shepherd effectively led the robber to his quarry. By volunteering this information, he converted what might otherwise have been an *ones* into an act of negligence, and he is therefore *chayav*.
