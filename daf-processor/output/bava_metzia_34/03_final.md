# Bava Metzia 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek 3: Shomrim Overview

### Perek 3 Structure

We begin the third *perek* at the top of 34a. Just as the first two *perakim* of the *masechet* were devoted to issues of *metzia* — finding lost objects — and yet the first *perek* rather than laying out basic parameters went immediately to special cases, so too this *perek*, rather than laying out the basic parameters of the situations under which *shomrim* are liable, compelled to take a *shevua*, or obligated to pay, begins immediately with special cases. The focus of the *perek* is the complex interplay between a *shomer*'s liability, the oath he might otherwise take, and the consequences when a thief is later apprehended.

### Mishnah: Payment vs. Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ בְּהֵמָה אוֹ כֵלִים, וְנִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ, שִׁילֵּם וְלֹא רָצָה לִישָּׁבַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: ״שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נִשְׁבָּע וְיוֹצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who deposits an animal or vessels with another,** who is acting as an unpaid bailee, **and they were stolen or they were lost,** and the bailee **paid** the owner the value of the deposit, **and did not wish to take an oath** that he did not misappropriate the item and that he was not negligent in safeguarding it, that will effect who keeps the deposit if it is found or returned. The bailee may also choose to take the oath, **as** the Sages **said: An unpaid bailee takes an oath, and he is** thereby **released** from the liability to pay the owner.

> נִמְצָא הַגַּנָּב – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. לְמִי מְשַׁלֵּם? לְמִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the thief is** later **found,** the thief **pays** the **double payment.** If the deposited item was a sheep or an ox and the thief **slaughtered or sold** it, **he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment. To whom does** the thief **pay?** He gives the payment **to the one who** had **the deposit in his** possession when it was stolen, i.e., the bailee. When the bailee paid the owner for the stolen item, the owner granted the rights to the item to the bailee. Therefore, the bailee is entitled to any payment the thief presents for the item, be it compensation for the item's value or a fine.

> נִשְׁבַּע וְלֹא רָצָה לְשַׁלֵּם, נִמְצָא הַגַּנָּב – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, לְמִי מְשַׁלֵּם? לְבַעַל הַפִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a bailee who **took an oath and did not wish to pay,** if **the thief is** then **found** and required to **pay** the **double payment,** or if he **slaughtered or sold** the animal and is required to **pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, to whom does** the thief **pay?** He gives the payment **to the owner of the deposit,** not the bailee.

The *mishnah* that anchors our discussion speaks about a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid watchman — whose object was stolen (*nignav*) and who is entitled to take an oath that he was not negligent and thereby be exempt from liability. Instead of taking that oath, however, he agrees to pay the owner. The *mishnah* then teaches that when the *ganav* is subsequently found, the *ganav* pays the *kefel* — the double payment — or, in the case of an animal that was slaughtered, four or five times the value, to the *shomer* rather than to the owner.

### Two Payment Scenarios

As we will see in the *gemara*, there are two distinct scenarios under which the *shomer* might agree to pay. In the first, he admits that he was negligent, and therefore says: "I will not swear falsely — I was negligent, and paying is my legal obligation." In the second, he maintains that he was not negligent but chooses to make the owner whole anyway: "Even though I am legally exempt, I would rather pay than take oaths — it was in my care and I feel responsible." In both scenarios, the *ganav* is ultimately found, and the question turns on who receives the *kefel*.

## Keifel to Shomer Logic

### Keifel Beneficiary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִתְנֵי בְּהֵמָה, וּלְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִתְנֵי כֵּלִים?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why does** the mishna need **to teach** the case of one who deposits **an animal, and why does** the mishna need **to teach** the case of one who deposits **vessels?** The mishna could have sufficed with a general *halakha* about one who deposits any item.

> צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי תְּנָא בְּהֵמָה – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהֵמָה הוּא דְּמַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישׁ טִירְחַהּ לְעַיּוֹלַהּ וּלְאַפּוֹקַהּ. אֲבָל כֵּלִים דְּלָא נְפִישׁ טִירְחַיְיהוּ, אֵימָא לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Both **are necessary, as, if** the mishna **taught** only the case of one who deposits **an animal, I would say:** It **is** only with regard to **an animal that** the owner agrees to **transfer** rights to **the double payment** to the bailee when the bailee pays for the stolen item. This is **due to** the fact **that the exertion** required to tend to the animal, **to bring** the animal **in and to take it out, is great.** Consequently, when it becomes clear that the bailee was not responsible for the theft of the animal but nevertheless compensated the owner, the owner waives his rights to any compensation the thief will pay. **But** in the case of **vessels, where the exertion** that is required to tend to the vessels **is not great, say that** the owner **does not transfer** to the bailee rights to the **double payment.**

The foundational question is striking: the object belonged to the owner, not to the *shomer*. The *shomer* is liable for the loss, but why should the *ganav* pay the *kefel* — or four and five — to the *shomer*? The most logical framing is that this represents a kind of rabbinic *takana*, undertaken for the benefit of all parties. The owner's primary concern is recovering the value of his object. By incentivizing *shomrim* to simply pay the owner and spare everyone the effort of litigation — rather than standing on their right to take a *shevua* — the system greases the wheels of recovery. If you are willing to pay the owner immediately and make things simple, you might actually receive a benefit from it: in a case of *gneiva*, you could ultimately receive the *kefel*, or even four or five.

### Takanah Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא כֵּלִים הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כֵּלִים הוּא דְּקָמַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא נְפִישׁ כְּפֵלַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה דְּכִי טָבַח וּמָכַר מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – אֵימָא לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the mishna **taught** only the case of one who deposits **vessels, I would say:** It **is** only with regard to **vessels that the owner transfers** rights to **the double payment** to the bailee when the bailee pays for the lost item. This is **due to** the fact **that double payment,** in **their** case, **is not substantial,** as that is the maximum payment that he could receive. **But** in the case of **an animal, where if** the thief **slaughtered or sold** it, **he pays the fourfold or fivefold payment,** which is substantial, **I** would **say that** the owner **does not transfer** the rights to the **double payment** to the bailee. Therefore, both cases are **necessary.**

Admittedly, it is not entirely clear how powerful a motive this is, given that it only applies in cases of *gneiva*. But the logic functions on two levels: it benefits the owner, who prefers a bird in hand over four or five in the bush, and it benefits the *beit din* by unclogging the courts — people do not have to drag cases into litigation when the *shomer* simply pays upfront. That framing would render this arrangement a straightforward *takana*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: וְהָא אֵין אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם! וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם, הָנֵי מִילֵּי כְּגוֹן פֵּירוֹת דֶּקֶל דַּעֲבִידִי דְּאָתוּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama objects to** the fundamental reasoning. How can the owner of the deposit transfer rights to the double payment to the bailee? **But isn't** there a principle that **one cannot transfer** to another **ownership of an entity that has not** yet **come into the world?** Since the thief was not yet liable to pay the double payment when the bailee paid the owner for the item, there was no way to transfer rights to that payment to another person. **And even according to Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a person** can **transfer** to another **ownership of an entity that has not** yet **come into the world, that statement** applies to items **such as the fruits of a date palm, which are likely to come** into being, as they grow on a regular basis.

However, the *stam* of the *gemara* at the beginning of the discussion we covered previously attempts to frame this not as a *takana* but rather as rooted in the psychology of the original owner — the owner himself implicitly wants to transfer the *kefel* to the *shomer* so that he can be paid his principal immediately. By situating the mechanism in the owner's own intent, the *gemara* creates a problem: how can one transfer *kefel* when it does not yet exist at the time of the transfer? If this were purely rabbinic, we might have more latitude. But if it is conceived as an implicit transaction between the two parties, what is the legal mechanism for transferring something that has not yet come into existence?

## Rava: Conditional Owners…

### Rava's Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא אָמַר רָבָא: נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לוֹ, לִכְשֶׁתִּגָּנֵב וְתִרְצֶה וּתְשַׁלְּמֵנִי הֲרֵי פָּרָתִי קְנוּיָה לְךָ מֵעַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: **It is as though** the owner **said to** the bailee at the time that he gave him the deposit: **When it will be stolen, and you will wish** to refrain from taking an oath, **and you will pay me** instead, ownership of **my cow is hereby transferred to you from this time,** and the cow is an entity that already came into the world. Since ownership of the cow is retroactively transferred to the bailee from the time of the deposit, any profits generated by the cow, e.g., the double payment, belong to the bailee.

*Rava* answers this question. Three lines from the top of 34a: *amar Rava* — *na'aseh ke'omer lo*, it is as though the owner is saying to the *shomer*, reflecting either what the owner truly intends or what the rabbis will impute to him in order to make this arrangement work: *lich'shetiganav vetirtzeh veteshalmeni* — if and when it gets stolen and you agree and you pay me — *harei parati knuya lach me'achshav* — this cow that I am giving you to watch is hereby yours as of this moment, contingent on that future event occurring. The condition is implicit: if the object is stolen and the *shomer* agrees to pay for it, then retroactively from the moment the object was entrusted, it will have been the *shomer*'s. This is what the owner truly thinks, or alternatively, this is what we as the rabbis assert the owner must be understood to be thinking, in order to make the mechanism work.

### Rabbi Zeira's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ גִּיזּוֹתֶיהָ וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ נָמֵי! אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא חוּץ מִגִּיזּוֹתֶיהָ וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לוֹ, חוּץ מִגִּיזּוֹתֶיהָ וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to this: If so,** then even the **fleece and offspring of** the animal that grew while it was in the bailee's possession should be the property of the bailee. **Why,** then, **is it taught** in a *baraita* that the bailee receives all profits generated by the animal **except for its fleece and its offspring? Rather, Rabbi Zeira says** that **it is as though** the owner **said to** the bailee: My animal is hereby transferred to you from this time **except for its fleece and its offspring.**

*Rabbi Zeira* challenges this: *ei hachi* — if that is truly the case, if the *shomer* retroactively owns the animal from the moment it was entrusted to him, then he should also be entitled to any shearings or offspring that accrued during that period. Consider a case where a sheep with wool is given to the *shomer*, or a pregnant cow. The *shomer* shears the sheep or the cow gives birth while in his care, and then the animal is stolen. The wool and the calves remain in the *shomer*'s possession — the *shomer* returns them to the owner along with compensation for the stolen animal. But if a *ganav* is later found, and we now say retroactively the *shomer* owned the animal from the time it was entrusted to him, does the *shomer* not also retroactively own those *gizos* and *velados* — the shearings and the offspring? Why then does a *braita* explicitly state *chutz migizeha vevaladeha* — that those are excluded?

### Zeira's Carve-Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי פְּסָקַאּ? סְתָמָא דְּמִלְּתָא שְׁבָחָא דְּאָתֵא מֵעָלְמָא, עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּמַקְנֵי. שְׁבָחָא דְּמִגּוּפַהּ, לָא עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּמַקְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **why** was this *halakha* stated in the mishna **without qualification?** Does every owner necessarily have that condition in mind? The Gemara answers: **Typically, profit that comes from elsewhere,** e.g., the double payment from a thief, which is difficult to anticipate, **a person is apt to transfer** to the bailee. But **profit that** originates **from the body** of the animal, which can be anticipated, **a person is not apt to transfer** to the bailee.

*Rabbi Zeira* therefore refines *Rava*'s formulation: *na'aseh ke'omer lo* — it is as though the owner says to the *shomer* — *chutz migizeha vevaladeha*. We must insert this exclusion into the implicit condition. The owner is understood to be saying: retroactively you will own it from the moment I gave it to you, except for any shearings or offspring — those remain mine. The ownership is carved out just enough to allow the *shomer* to receive *kefel*, and nothing more.

### External vs. Internal Be…

The *gemara* then asks: *u'mah paska* — what justifies splitting it in that particular way, that retroactive ownership applies for *kefel* purposes but not for *gizos* and *velados*? The answer is *stama d'milsa* — as a general rule: *shivcha d'asi me'alma avid inish d'aknoiyi* — people are willing to transfer an external benefit that comes from outside the animal itself, such as *kefel*, which is an unpredictable windfall that has nothing to do with the animal's intrinsic worth. *Shivcha d'migufah* — benefit that comes from the body of the animal itself — *lo avid inish d'aknoiyi* — people are not willing to transfer. Therefore, the *kefel*, which is not part of the animal itself and may never even materialize, is something the owner is presumed willing to cede to the *shomer*. The *gizos* and *velados*, which are products of the animal itself, are not. The implicit condition is thus carefully scoped to enable *kefel* and nothing else.

## Samuch Ligenivas Answer

### Moment-Before Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לוֹ, לִכְשֶׁתִּגָּנֵב וְתִרְצֶה וּתְשַׁלְּמֵנִי, סָמוּךְ לִגְנֵיבָתָהּ קְנוּיָה לָךְ. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rava says** his response differently. **It is as though** the owner **said to** the bailee at the time of the deposit: **When it will be stolen, and you will wish** to refrain from taking an oath, **and you will pay me** instead, ownership of my animal **is transferred to you adjacent to,** i.e., immediately before, **its theft.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the formulations in Rava's two responses?

There is an alternative answer — *ika d'amri amar Rava* — others report that *Rava* gave a different formulation. Here too the owner is understood to say *na'aseh ke'omer lo*, but the moment of ownership transfer is different: *samuch lignevasah knuya lach* — you will own it a moment before it gets stolen. This operates somewhat like a *matnas shechiv mera* — a gift that takes effect a moment before the giver's death. The owner transfers ownership to the *shomer* a moment before the theft occurs. At that instant the *shomer* owns it, the *ganav* then steals it from the *shomer*, and therefore the *shomer* is entitled to *kefel*.

### Avoids Zeira Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ קוּשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא. אִי נָמֵי דְּקָיְימָא בַּאֲגַם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to **the difficulty** raised **by Rabbi Zeira** with regard to fleece and offspring, which is irrelevant according to the second formulation. **Alternatively,** there is a difference in a case **where** the animal is **standing in a marsh [*ba'agam*]** at the time of its theft. Since the animal was not on the bailee's property at that time, he could not acquire it.

The first practical difference between this approach and *Rava*'s first answer is that this formulation entirely sidesteps *Rabbi Zeira*'s challenge about *gizos* and *velados*. Since ownership transfers only the moment before the theft — not retroactively from the time the object was entrusted — all shearings and offspring that accrued during the period of the *shomer*'s watch naturally belong to the owner. No special carve-out is needed.

### Agam Problem

However, this alternative answer has its own difficulty. *Ei nami d'kaima b'agam* — what if the animal is stolen while grazing in a swamp, not from the *shomer*'s property? In that case, even if we want to transfer ownership to the *shomer* at the moment before the theft, there is no mechanism by which he can take possession of it at that instant. He is not holding it, it is not on his property — there is no *ma'aseh kinyan* occurring at that second. He can retroactively own it from the moment it was handed to him two weeks earlier, because then it was in his hand and on his property. But he cannot acquire it the moment before it is stolen somewhere else. The problem with this *samuch ligenivas* answer, elegant as it is, is that it does not cover cases where the object was stolen from a location outside the *shomer*'s possession. Note that *agam* here simply refers to a location where the animal is not on the *shomer*'s property — it does not mean the animal is permanently inaccessible or lost to all. It means only that there is no *kinyan* possible at that moment.

### Tosafos's Tosafos

*Rashi* and *Tosafos* engage this difficulty. The question that arises — and which *Tosafos* explicitly raises — is: why not combine both approaches? Say *na'aseh ke'omer lo* that you will own it *me'achshav* — as of this moment — and the ownership terminates a moment before it is stolen. By adding *me'achshav*, the *kinyan* is grounded in the present moment when the animal is indeed in the *shomer*'s possession, and the ownership simply continues until immediately before the theft. This would obviate the need for any new act of acquisition at the moment before the theft, because the ownership begins now.

*Tosafos* raises this question explicitly — *teima, leneima na'aseh ke'omer lo lich'she'tignav vetishalmeni harei parasi knuyah lach me'achshav samuch lignevasah* — situating the transfer at two points: retroactively as of now, ending a moment before the theft. *Tosafos*'s answer is that even in that formulation, because ownership begins as of now, the *shomer* would be entitled to the *gizos* and *velados* that accrued during the interim, which reintroduces *Rabbi Zeira*'s challenge. So while the question is well posed, that combined approach does not in fact resolve all the difficulties.

## Shillem vs. Promise

### Mishnah's Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילֵּם וְלֹא רָצָה לִישָּׁבַע [וְכוּ׳]. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא ״שִׁילֵּם״ – שִׁילֵּם מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁילֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the bailee **paid** the owner **and did not wish to take an oath,** the thief pays the double payment to the bailee. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** When the mishna says: If the bailee **paid,** it does **not** mean that he **actually paid. Rather, once** the bailee **said: I hereby** choose to **pay, even if he did not** yet actually **pay,** he acquired the double payment and all other profits.

With the mechanism of ownership transfer established — however that mechanism is understood — the *gemara* turns to the conditions under which the *shomer* becomes entitled to *kefel*. The *mishnah* says *shilem ve-lo ratzah lishava* — he paid and did not want to take an oath. The language is *shilem* — he actually paid.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Rule

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *lo shilem shilem mamash* — when the *mishnah* says "paid," it does not mean literal payment. *Ela keivan she'amar hareni meshalem af al pi she'lo shilem* — once he says "I am going to pay," even though he has not yet paid, that suffices. This is a remarkable statement. The *shomer* need not actually transfer money; his verbal commitment is enough to entitle him to *kefel* when the *ganav* is found.

## Shillem vs. Promise (II)

### Mishnah Symmetry Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שִׁילֵּם וְלֹא רָצָה לִישָּׁבַע. שִׁילֵּם – אִין, לֹא שִׁילֵּם – לָא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: נִשְׁבַּע וְלֹא רָצָה לְשַׁלֵּם. טַעְמָא דְּלֹא רָצָה, הָא רָצָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁילֵּם. אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection from that which **we learned** in the mishna: If the bailee **paid** the owner **and did not wish to take an oath,** the thief pays the double payment to the bailee. The Gemara infers: If the bailee **paid, yes,** he acquires these rights; if the bailee **did not pay,** he does **not.** The Gemara answers: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: In the case of a bailee who **took an oath and did not wish to pay,** the thief pays the double payment to the owner. The Gemara infers: **The reason** that the thief pays the owner is **that** the bailee **did not wish** to pay. **But** if the bailee **wished** to pay, **although he did not** actually **pay,** he acquires the rights to the double payment. **Rather,** because the inference from the first clause and the inference from the latter clause are contradictory, **no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna.

Can this be derived from the *mishnah* itself? The *gemara* attempts to reason from the *mishnah*'s structure. The *reisha* says: *shilem ve-lo ratzah lishava* — he paid and didn't want to take an oath, implying: *shilem in, lo shilem lo* — only if he paid, not otherwise. But look at the *seifa*: *nishba ve-lo ratzah lishalem* — he took an oath and didn't want to pay. The *seifa*'s language is *lo ratzah* — he didn't want to pay. If the *seifa* were simply negating the *reisha*, it would say *nishba ve-lo shilem*, he swore and didn't pay. Instead, it says he didn't want to — implying that if he did want to (*ha ratzah*), even if he hadn't yet paid (*af al pi she'lo shilem*), he would get *kefel*. That would support *Rabbi Yochanan*.

### Ambiguity in Coverage

Nevertheless, the *gemara* concludes: *ela mei'ha leika lemishma mina* — from this *mishnah* no inference can be drawn, because the *mishnah* deals with two extremes and does not cover the excluded middle. The *reisha* and *seifa* are indeed symmetrical and elegant, but symmetry does not mean exhaustive coverage. One case states he paid and didn't swear; the other states he swore and didn't pay. The case where he neither swore nor paid but expressed willingness to pay — that is precisely the ambiguous middle ground that the *gemara* must analyze separately.

## Socher's Promise

### Socher Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר פָּרָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, וְנִגְנְבָה, וְאָמַר הַלָּה: ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם וְאֵינִי נִשְׁבָּע״, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַגַּנָּב – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל לַשּׂוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** In the case of **one who rents a cow from another, and it was stolen, and the other** party, the renter, **said: I hereby** choose to **pay and I will not take an oath, and the thief was located thereafter,** the thief **pays** the **double payment to the renter.** Apparently, once the renter chooses to pay, he acquires the rights to the double payment.

The *gemara* brings a *braita* to support *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position. *HaSocher para mechaveiro venigneva* — one who rented a cow and it was stolen. *Ve'amar halah harei ani meshalem ve'eini nishba* — and he says "I will pay and I will not take an oath." Note: he only says he will pay, he does not actually pay. *Ve'achar kach nimtza haganav* — and subsequently the *ganav* is found — *meshalem tashlumei kefel lasocher* — the *ganav* pays the *kefel* to the renter. The *braita* thus explicitly validates the position that agreeing to pay — without yet having paid — is sufficient.

### Socher's Legal Status

There is an additional layer of significance here. Unlike the *shomer chinam* who agreed to pay beyond his legal obligation, the *socher* — the renter — is actually legally obligated to pay in a case of *gneiva*. His only alternative to paying would have been to swear falsely — to claim, for instance, that the loss was an *onus*, an unavoidable accident, when in truth he knows it was stolen. He cannot honestly swear his way out of liability. So in the case of the *socher*, the wonderful thing he is doing is not paying beyond his legal obligation — he is simply doing what he is required to do and not lying under oath. One might think that for such a person no special incentive should be needed and certainly no *kefel* should be transferred. Yet the *braita* teaches that even for the *socher*, agreeing to pay — without yet having paid — entitles him to *kefel*.

### Socher as Proof

This *braita* thus proves two distinct *chiddushim* simultaneously: first, that actually paying is not required — a verbal agreement suffices; second, that even when the *shomer* is merely fulfilling his legal obligation rather than going beyond it, the *kefel* is still transferred to him. Both points emerge from the *socher* case, which serves as a proof for *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle.

## Changed Mind Cases

### Late Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: אָמַר אֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״, – הָא קָאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״. אֶלָּא אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments that it is **obvious** that if the bailee initially **said: I will not pay, and then said: I hereby** choose to **pay** rather than taking an oath, he is entitled to the double payment, as **didn't** he ultimately **say: I hereby choose to pay?** The Gemara raises a dilemma: **But** if he initially **said: I hereby** choose to **pay,**

The *gemara* proceeds to analyze a series of cases involving changed intentions and the timing of the *shomer*'s commitment. *Pishita amar eini meshalem vehazar ve'amar hareni meshalem* — a case where the *shomer* initially says he will not pay and will take an oath, and only later agrees to pay. One might have thought that *kefel* should be available only to one who makes everything simple from the beginning, not to someone who has been resisting for weeks and finally agrees after prolonged negotiation. The *gemara* considers this obvious: once he agrees to pay, regardless of the delay or earlier resistance, he is entitled to *kefel* if the *ganav* is subsequently found.

### Retraction Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״אֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן מִהְדָּר קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא בְּמִלְּתֵיהּ קָאֵי וְדַחוֹיֵי הוּא דְּקָא מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **and then said: I will not pay, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say he is retracting** his intention to pay and therefore has no right to the double payment? **Or, perhaps he stands** committed to **his** initial **statement and is** merely **postponing** payment to a later date, in which case he maintains rights to the double payment.

The more interesting case is the reverse: *amar hareni meshalem vehazar ve'amar eini meshalem* — he first agreed to pay, and then retracted and said he will not pay. The *gemara* asks: *mai?* What are we to make of this?

### Retraction Analysis

The *gemara* frames the question as a *safek*: *mi amrinan mehadar kahadar beih* — perhaps he has genuinely retracted and is no longer committed, in which case he should lose his entitlement to *kefel*; *o dilma bemilteih ka'ei, vedichuyei hu dekamidchei leih* — or perhaps he is still bound by his original statement and is merely pushing the owner off, saying he will not pay not because he actually intends to retract but because he does not have the funds on hand and is deflecting. This is a familiar dynamic — a debtor who owes money and, rather than admit he cannot pay at the moment, says he no longer intends to pay.

Interestingly, the *gemara* does not seem particularly concerned with the hardship this puts on the owner. Earlier, when the *shomer* first said he would not pay and later agreed, the *gemara* considered that case obvious — once he agrees, he gets *kefel*. Similarly here, if he is ultimately going to pay, it seems he retains his entitlement. The only genuine question is whether he has truly retracted. And if he has truly retracted, the *gemara* appears to imply that he may be entitled to do so — which makes *Rabbi Yochanan*'s rule an even more striking *chiddush*: a verbal agreement that can apparently be revoked still suffices to confer entitlement to *kefel*.

*Tosafos*, however, goes against the plain reading of the *gemara* here. In the *Tosafos* beginning *ve'chazar*, in the passage commencing with the word *gamar*, *Tosafos* argues: *vehacha dekamar mehadar kahadar bei lo sheyuchal lachzor* — it is not that he genuinely has the right to retract; *ela rotzeh hu lachzor af al pi sheino yachol* — he wants to retract even though he cannot. The question then is: if he is legally bound by his commitment, why does he lose *kefel* by attempting to retract? *Tosafos* answers that even though he is bound and cannot actually undo the obligation, his attempt to retract — which will require the owner to drag him to *beis din* to enforce the original commitment — costs him the *kefel*, because *lo makni lei kefela mishum d'ati levei dina* — the owner will not transfer *kefel* to someone who is making him work this hard to get his money back.

### Shomer Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״ וָמֵת, וְאָמְרוּ בָּנָיו ״אֵין אָנוּ מְשַׁלְּמִין״, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן מִהְדָּר קָא הָדְרִי בְּהוּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא בְּמִלְּתָא דַאֲבוּהוֹן קָיְימִי, וְדַחוֹיֵי הוּא דְּקָא מְדַחוּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another dilemma. If the bailee **said: I hereby** choose to **pay, and** then he **died** before paying, **and his sons said: We are not paying, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say they are retracting** their father's decision to pay, **or perhaps they stand** committed to fulfilling **their father's statement and are** merely **postponing** payment to a later date when they will be able to pay?

The *gemara* continues: *amar hareni meshalem umet* — the *shomer* committed to paying and then died. *Ve'amru banav ein anu meshallmim* — and the children say they will not pay. *Mai?* Is this a genuine retraction on behalf of the children, or are they simply the continuation of their father's commitment and merely delaying?

### Children Pay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלְּמוּ בָּנִים, מַאי? מָצֵי אָמַר לְהוּ: כִּי אַקְנַאי כְּפֵילָא – לַאֲבוּכוֹן דַּעֲבַד לִי נְיָיח נַפְשַׁאי, לְדִידְכוּ לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another dilemma. If the father died before he declared his willingness to pay and **the sons paid, what** is the *halakha*? **Can** the owner **say to them: When I transferred** rights to **the double payment,** I transferred them **to your father, who pleased me,** but **to you,** I did **not** transfer those rights? **Or perhaps** this case **is no different,** and the owner transfers rights to the double payment provided that he receives payment, and it does not matter whether it was the bailee or his sons who paid him.

A different scenario: the *shomer* died without having said anything, and the children simply paid on their own. *Shillmu banim, mai?* Here the *gemara* raises a new dimension: *matzei amri lehu ki akni kefela lavucha david li naichas ruach* — perhaps the owner can say to the children: I was willing to transfer *kefel* to your father because he did me a favor by watching the object; *ledichu* — but you never did any favor for me. This is not merely a rational business consideration about incentives. The *gemara* here introduces an interpersonal element — a sense of gratitude toward the original *shomer* — as a possible factor in whether the owner is understood to transfer *kefel*. Since we are situating the mechanism in the *da'as* of the owner rather than treating this purely as a *takana*, this kind of sentiment becomes legally relevant.

### Owner Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילֵּם לְבָנִים, מַאי? מָצוּ אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: כִּי אַקְנִי לָךְ אֲבוּנָא כְּפֵילָא – דַּעֲבַדְתְּ לֵיהּ נְיָיח נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֲבָל אֲנַן לְדִידַן לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא. שִׁלְּמוּ בָּנִים לְבָנִים, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** If the owner of the deposit died and the bailee **paid** the payment **to** the owner's **sons, what** is the *halakha*? **Can** the sons **say to** the bailee: **When our father transferred** rights to **the double payment to you,** it was because **you pleased him, but** as far as **we** are concerned, you did **not** please **us? Or perhaps it is no different,** and the bailee receives the double payment. Likewise, if both the owner and the bailee died, and **sons** of the bailee **paid the sons** of the owner, **what** is the *halakha*?

*Shillmu livanim* — what if the owner dies and the *shomer* pays the owner's heirs? From a purely business perspective, one would think the *shomer* deserves *kefel* all the more: the heirs may be unfamiliar with their father's affairs, and having the *shomer* step forward and pay immediately makes their lives considerably easier. Yet the same question arises in reverse: *ki akni kefela lavucha, aval anan ledidon lo yahavinan kefela* — perhaps the heirs of the owner will say: our father would have been willing to give you *kefel* because you did him a personal favor, but you never did anything for us. *O dilma lo shna* — or perhaps it makes no difference?

### Both Parties Dead

*Shamu banim livanim, mai?* Finally: both the original owner and the original *shomer* have died, and the heirs of the *shomer* paid the heirs of the owner. In this scenario, no party presently alive did any favor for any other party presently alive. The question is whether the entitlement to *kefel* is now purely a business matter — a straightforward incentive that runs with the obligation — or whether the absence of any personal relationship between the parties means it lapses entirely.

## Partial Payment Cases

### Half Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילֵּם מֶחֱצָה, מַאי? שָׁאַל שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת וְשִׁילֵּם אַחַת מֵהֶן, מַאי? שָׁאַל מִן הַשּׁוּתָּפִין וְשִׁילֵּם לְאֶחָד מֵהֶן, מַאי? שׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁשָּׁאֲלוּ וְשִׁילֵּם אֶחָד מֵהֶן, מַאי? שָׁאַל מִן הָאִשָּׁה וְשִׁילֵּם לְבַעְלָהּ, מַאי? אִשָּׁה שֶׁשָּׁאֲלָה וְשִׁילֵּם בַּעֲלָהּ, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises additional dilemmas: If the bailee **paid half** the value of the deposit before the thief was discovered, **what** is the *halakha*? If **one borrowed two cows,** and they were stolen, and **he paid** the value **of one of them, what** is the *halakha*? If one **borrowed** an item **from partners, and he paid one of them, what** is the *halakha*? In the case of **partners who borrowed** an item **and one of them paid, what** is the *halakha*? If **one borrowed** an item **from a woman and paid her husband, what** is the *halakha*? In the case of **a woman who borrowed** an item, **and her husband paid, what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: All of these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved **[*teiku*]**.

The *gemara* turns to cases of partial or split payment. Since *Rabbi Yochanan* has established that agreeing to pay is sufficient, the scenario here — as *Rashi* explains — must be one where the *shomer* originally declared: *hareni meshalem mechtzah ve'lo yoter* — I will pay half and no more. Because *amar* is generally sufficient, the case of paying half must refer to one who agreed to pay only half, not the full obligation.

### Two Cows

*Sha'al shtei parot ve'shileim achat meihen, mai?* One borrowed two cows, and paid in full for one while remaining uncommitted about the other. Do we treat these as two independent transactions, such that the full payment for the first cow entitles the *shomer* to *kefel* for that one? Or do we view it as a single transaction that has not been fully resolved?

### Partner Payment

*Shamu shutafin ve'shileim le'echad meihen, mai?* One borrowed from partners and paid one partner's full share. The partner was made whole, but the total object has not been fully paid for. Does the *shomer* receive at least half the *kefel*?

### Co-Borrower Pay

*Shutafin she'sha'alu ve'shileim echad meihen, mai?* Two partners borrowed together, and one of the borrowers paid his proportional share of the obligation. His personal liability has been fully discharged. Should he receive *kefel* for his portion?

### Husband-Wife

*Sha'alu min ha'ishah ve'shileim le'ba'alah, mai? O ishah she'sha'alah ve'shileim ba'alah, mai?* The *gemara* presents two scenarios involving a woman and her husband: one borrowed from a woman and paid her husband, and alternatively, a woman borrowed and her husband paid on her behalf. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* debate the precise configuration of these cases — in particular, whether the object involved was *nikhsei melog*, where the principal belongs to the wife and the husband is entitled to the produce — such that the question of who is truly the "owner" for purposes of *kefel* becomes genuinely complex.

### All Teiku

On every single one of these cases — partial payment, two cows, partners, co-borrowers, husband and wife in both configurations — the *gemara* concludes with *teiku*: these questions remain unresolved.

## Cases Receiving Keifel

### Pashati Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״פָּשַׁעְתִּי״, מַקְנֵה לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא. דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בִּגְנֵיבָה. שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״נִגְנְבָה״, מַקְנֵה לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא. דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בִּשְׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** In the case of **an unpaid bailee, once he stated: I was negligent,** thereby rendering himself liable to compensate the owner, the owner **transfers** rights to **the double payment to him, as, if** the unpaid bailee **wishes,** he can **exempt himself** from that liability **with** the claim of **theft.** Admitting negligence is tantamount to agreeing to pay rather than taking an oath. Likewise, in the case of **a paid bailee, once he stated: It was stolen,** the owner **transfers** rights to **the double payment to him, as, if** the paid bailee **wishes,** he can **exempt himself** from that liability **with** the claim that the animal was **maimed or died** due to circumstances beyond his control.

*Amar Rav Papa*: *shomer chinam keivan de'amar pashati makne lei kefela* — a *shomer chinam* who says *pashati*, I was negligent, immediately transfers entitlement to *kefel* to himself, even without explicitly saying "I will pay." By admitting negligence, he has effectively declared his legal obligation to pay, and that admission itself functions as the commitment. He could have claimed the object was stolen and been exempt; instead he was honest. That honesty, even though it amounts only to acknowledging what he is obligated to do, suffices.

### Honesty Incentive

Similarly, a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman — who admits *nigneva*, that it was stolen, acquires entitlement to *kefel*. The *shomer sachar* is liable for theft and loss; his admission of the truth is being rewarded to incentivize honesty over false oath-taking. For if he had wanted to extricate himself, *de'i ba'ei patar nafsheih bishvura u-metah* — he could have fabricated a claim that the animal fell off a cliff and died — an *onus* that would have exempted him. Because he had a way out and chose not to take it, the system rewards him with the possibility of *kefel*.

### Bifnim Question

The *gemara* raises the question of whether these cases are *bifnim* — within the scope of the *shomer*'s possession — or external to it. This question of where the loss occurred relative to the *shomer*'s *reshus* has implications for the mechanism of ownership transfer discussed earlier, but the *gemara* has not yet fully resolved it at this juncture.

### Shoal Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹאֵל שֶׁאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״ – לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, בְּמַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִפְטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ? בְּמֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה, מֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, in the case of **a borrower who says: I hereby** choose **to pay,** the owner **does not transfer** the rights to **the double payment to him.** The borrower's statement that he chooses to pay is ineffectual, as he is liable to pay even without it. Because the statement is ineffectual, it does not confer any rights. **With what** claim would a borrower be able **to exempt himself** from payment? It is only **with** the claim that the animal **died due to** ordinary **labor.** A case of an animal that **died due to** ordinary **labor is uncommon,** and this claim is therefore accepted only with corroborating proof.

The exception to all of this is the *shoel* — the borrower. *Shoel she'amar hareni meshalem lo makne lei kefela* — a borrower who says he will pay does not receive *kefel*. Why? *Be'mai havah lei lemiptar nafsheih?* What option did the *shoel* have for exempting himself? Only *mita machmat melachah* — death of the animal in the normal course of its use. The *shoel* is liable in virtually every case: lightning, disease, attack by predators — all fall on the *shoel*. The sole exception is when the animal dies as a natural consequence of the specific use for which it was borrowed, because the owner implicitly accepted that risk when lending it for that purpose.

### Rare False Claim

The *gemara*'s reasoning for excluding the *shoel* is that *mita machmat melachah* is simply not common enough to generate a meaningful incentive problem. The likelihood that someone will fabricate such a claim when it is false is small. We do not need to create an incentive to prevent the *shoel* from making that lie, because people are unlikely to resort to it. The other *shomrim* and the *socher* have more plausible false claims available to them, and the system therefore needs to incentivize their honesty. The *shoel* does not.

## Shoal Gets Keifel?

### Rav Papa Second View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שׁוֹאֵל נָמֵי כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״ – מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּמֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב זְבִיד: הָכִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שׁוֹאֵל עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? הוֹאִיל וְכׇל הֲנָאָה שֶׁלּוֹ, בְּדִיבּוּרָא לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rav Pappa said:** In the case of **a borrower, once he stated: I hereby** choose **to pay,** the owner **transfers** rights to **the double payment to him, as, if** a borrower **wishes,** he can **exempt himself** from that liability **with** the claim that the animal **died due to** ordinary **labor. Rav Zevid said to him** that **this** is what **Abaye said: A borrower** acquires the rights to the double payment **only when he** actually **pays** for the item. **What is the reason** for this? **Since all of the benefit** accrues **to** the borrower, as he enjoys the use of the item without payment, **the owner does not transfer** the rights to **the double payment to** the borrower **due to** his **statement** that he chooses to pay.

*Ika d'amri amar Rav Papa*: a second formulation attributed to *Rav Papa* holds that even a *shoel* gets *kefel* when he agrees to pay. The reasoning is that even a *shoel* could have made some other claim to extricate himself, and we want to encourage everyone to simply step up and pay as quickly as possible without considering their potential alternative arguments. Under this view, the incentive structure should apply universally.

### Abaye's Distinction

*Amar leh Rav Zvid hachi amar Abaye*: *Rav Zvid* reports *Abaye*'s position. *Shoel ad sheyeshalem* — the *shoel* will indeed receive *kefel*, but only when he actually pays, not merely when he agrees to. The *shoel* must go all the way and pay in full before he becomes entitled to *kefel*.

### Gratitude Factor

*Abaye* frames the reason not in terms of the *shoel*'s limited escape options, but in terms of the owner's disposition: *ho'il ve'chol hana'ah shelo* — since all the benefit of the original transaction ran to the *shoel*, the owner feels no particular gratitude toward him. The dynamic here is a meaningful departure from the purely rational incentive model. The owner agreed to the *shomer chinam* watching his object and feels grateful that the *shomer* bore the burden of safeguarding it, then steps up to pay. That gratitude inclines the owner to transfer *kefel*. By contrast, the *shoel* is the one who received all the benefit: the owner did him a favor by lending the object at no charge. In such a transaction, the owner has no reason to feel warmly predisposed toward the *shoel*, and that absence of gratitude means the owner is not inclined to transfer *kefel* merely on the basis of a promise. Only actual payment — which makes the owner's position immediately whole — is enough to overcome that reluctance.

### Tosafos Nafka Mina

*Tosafos* identifies the practical *nafka mina* between *Rav Papa*'s first formulation (the *shoel* has too few alternatives to warrant incentivizing) and *Abaye*'s formulation (the owner lacks gratitude toward the *shoel*). Suppose the *shoel* made a special stipulation at the outset, negotiating with the owner to limit his liability — for instance, saying he will only be liable for negligence and not for *gneiva* and *aveida*. In such a case, the *shoel* does have meaningful alternative claims available: he can say he was not negligent and thereby avoid paying. Under *Rav Papa*'s first reasoning, this *shoel* with limited liability should therefore receive *kefel* even for a mere promise to pay, because he faces the same incentive problem as other *shomrim*. Under *Abaye*'s reasoning, however, the owner was even more generous to this *shoel* — he not only lent the object but also agreed to reduce the *shoel*'s liability — making the owner even less predisposed to transfer *kefel* on the basis of a promise alone.

## Shoal Payment Braita

### Hikdim V'Shillem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב זְבִיד: הַשּׁוֹאֵל פָּרָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְנִגְנְבָה, וְקִידֵּם הַשּׁוֹאֵל וְשִׁילֵּם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַגַּנָּב – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל לַשּׁוֹאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Zevid:** In the case of **one who borrows a cow from another, and it was stolen, and the borrower went ahead and paid, and the thief was located thereafter,** the thief **pays** the **double payment to the borrower.** This indicates that the borrower receives the double payment only if he actually paid the owner for the item.

A *braita* supports *Rav Zvid*'s report of *Abaye*'s position: *hashoel parah mechaveiro venigneva vehikdim hashoel veshillem ve'achar kach nimtza haganav meshalem tashlumei kefel lashoel* — one who borrowed a cow and it was stolen, and the *shoel* stepped forward and paid, and the *ganav* was subsequently found — the *ganav* pays *kefel* to the *shoel*. The *braita* explicitly says *hikdim veshillem* — he stepped forward and actually paid — before the *shoel* is entitled to *kefel*.

### Socher-Shoal Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלִישָּׁנָא קַמָּא דְּרַב פָּפָּא – וַדַּאי לָא הָוְיָא תְּיוּבְתָּא. לְלִישָּׁנָא בָּתְרָא לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **According to the first version of** the statement of **Rav Pappa,** that the owner transfers rights to the double payment to the borrower only if he actually pays the owner, this *baraita* **certainly is not a conclusive refutation [*teyuvta*]** of the opinion of Rav Pappa, as the *baraita* corresponds to his opinion. **According to the latter version** of Rav Pappa's statement, that the owner transfers rights to the double payment to the borrower even if the borrower merely says that he chooses to pay, **shall we say** that this *baraita* **would be a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rav Pappa?

This stands in precise contrast to the earlier *braita* about the *socher*, which says *ve'amar* — and he said he would pay. The *socher* need only say; the *shoel* must step forward and pay. The juxtaposition seems deliberate and meaningful: the two *braitot* are applying different standards to the *socher* and the *shoel* respectively.

### Source Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב פָּפָּא: מִי אַלִּימָא מִמַּתְנִיתִין? דְּקָתָנֵי ״שִׁילֵּם״, וְאוֹקֵימְנָא, בְּאָמַר הָכָא נָמֵי בְּאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Pappa** could have **said to you: Is** the *baraita* **stronger than the mishna, which teaches** that the bailee receives the double payment if he already **paid** the owner, **and** nevertheless, **we established** that the mishna is referring **to** one who **states** his intent to pay but has not yet paid? **Here, too,** establish the *baraita* as referring **to** one who **states** his intent to pay but has not yet paid.

*Rav Papa* resists this conclusion. He argues: *mi alima mimisnisin* — is this *braita* any stronger than our *mishnah*? The *mishnah* says *shilem* — he paid — and yet we established, following *Rabbi Yochanan*, that *shilem* means agreeing to pay. Similarly, *hikdim veshillem* in the *braita* could mean he stepped forward and agreed to pay, not that he literally paid. The *gemara* objects: *hasam lo katani hikdim* — there, in the *mishnah*, it does not say *hikdim*, he stepped forward. Here the *braita* specifically says *hikdim veshillem* — the emphatic language of stepping forward and paying suggests actual payment, not merely a commitment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא קָתָנֵי ״קִידֵּם״, הָכָא קָתָנֵי ״קִידֵּם״! מַאי ״קִידֵּם״? קִידֵּם וְאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **Are** these cases **comparable? There,** in the mishna, it **is not taught** that he **went ahead** and paid. **Here,** in the *baraita*, it **is taught** that he **went ahead** and paid, which indicates that he actually paid and not merely that he agreed to pay. The Gemara responds: **What** is the meaning of **went ahead?** It means that **he went ahead and stated** his intent to pay, although he did not yet actually pay.

*Rav Papa* replies: *mai hikdim hikdim ve'amar* — even *hikdim veshillem* means no more than he stepped forward and declared his intention. The *gemara* then presses: if the *socher braita* says *amar* — he said — and the *shoel braita* says *hikdim veshillem* — he stepped forward and paid — and these two phrases are different, the contrast is legally significant. It appears we have two different standards. But perhaps the two *braitot* come from entirely different collections and were never intended to speak to each other; the language of *hikdim* in the *shoel braita* might be incidental, not contrastive.

### Tanaim Consultation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי שׂוֹכֵר ״וְאָמַר״, וְגַבֵּי שׁוֹאֵל ״קִידֵּם״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דַּוְקָא קָתָנֵי! מִידֵּי גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי תַּנְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But from** the fact **that** the *tanna* **teaches** the *halakha* in the *baraita* cited earlier **with regard to a renter** with the formulation: **And he said** that he would pay, **and** in the *baraita* **with regard to a borrower** the *tanna* uses the formulation: **Went ahead, conclude from it that** the *baraita* concerning a borrower **is teaching specifically** that he actually paid. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Are** these two *baraitot* **taught together** so that one can draw a conclusion based on a discrepancy in their formulation? Perhaps the *baraitot* are unrelated and are simply formulated in different styles.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁיְילִינְהוּ לְתַנָּאֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וּדְבֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְאָמְרִי: גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי תַּנְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The Sages **asked the *tanna'im* of the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Oshaya,** experts in *mishnayot* and *baraitot*, if these *baraitot* were formulated together. **And they said** that the *baraitot* **were taught together** as one long *baraita*, and therefore one can draw a conclusion based on a discrepancy in their formulation.

To resolve this, the *gemara* reports that a query was brought to the *tannaim* — the scholars who preserved the oral tradition of *braitot* — of the schools of *Rabbi Chiyya* and *Rabbi Oshaya*, who organized and systematized the *braitot*, particularly the *Tosefta*. The question put to them was whether these two *braitot* — the one about the *socher* and the one about the *shoel* — were taught side by side as part of the same collection. *Ve'amri gabi hadadi tanya* — and they confirmed: yes, these were taught together. The *braita* reads: *ha-socher she'amar* gets *kefel*; *ha-shoel hikdim veshillem* gets *kefel*. The contrast is explicit and intentional. The *shoel* must actually pay. This is a remarkable moment of textual research within the *gemara* itself — the Amoraim went to the repositories of *braita* tradition to establish the literary context of a ruling, recognizing that *braitot* learned atomistically, as individual units, could be misunderstood without their surrounding context.

## Rav Huna's Oath

### Oath Despite Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא עֵינָיו נָתַן בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says:** Even if the bailee declares his intention to pay for the deposit, the court **administers an oath to him that** the item was actually stolen or lost and **is not in his possession. What is the reason** for this oath? **We are concerned** that **perhaps he coveted** that item.

*Amar Rav Huna*: even when a *shomer* agrees to pay — or actually pays — and one would assume this relieves him of any further obligation to take a *shevua*, *Rav Huna* rules: *mashbinan lei shvu'ah she'einah birshuto* — we administer to him an oath that the object is not in his possession. The *shomer* may have believed that by agreeing to pay he was completely dispensing with any oath requirement. *Rav Huna* holds that the *beit din* must still be involved, and the *shomer* must still swear.

### Fraud Concern

The reason is *chashinan shema einav natan bah* — we are concerned that perhaps the *shomer* has set his eyes on the object. He is not being the great *tzaddik* one might imagine. Perhaps he genuinely wants the object for himself, and is fabricating the claim that it was lost or stolen, while agreeing to pay as a strategy to acquire it without taking a false oath about its whereabouts. By paying, he thinks he is getting out cleanly — he avoids swearing falsely about the loss, pays the owner's value, and keeps the object. *Rav Huna* says: this concern is live enough that even a *shomer* who agrees to pay must still swear that he is not in fact holding the object.

### Court Involvement

The implication is that despite all the incentive structure built around getting *shomrim* to pay quickly and spare everyone the litigation, *Rav Huna* insists that court involvement and an oath remain necessary to verify that the claimed loss actually occurred. The entire edifice of *kefel* and the encouragement of prompt payment does not displace the need for basic verification. This ruling thus qualifies the picture considerably: the *shomer* who pays may receive *kefel*, but he does not entirely escape the oversight of *beit din* or the responsibility to swear. We will take up the implications of *Rav Huna*'s position in the continuation.
