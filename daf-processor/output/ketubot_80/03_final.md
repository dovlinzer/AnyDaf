# Ketubot 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nichsei Milog Overview

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: אֲפִילּוּ גְּרוֹגֶרֶת אַחַת, וְהוּא שֶׁאֲכָלָהּ דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד. אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא, אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב: אֲפִילּוּ שִׁיגְרָא דְתַמְרֵי. בָּעֵי רַב בִּיבִי: חוּבְצָא דְתַמְרֵי מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And how much is** a **small** amount? **Rabbi Asi said:** It is **even one dried fig, provided he ate it in a dignified manner** befitting the owner of the produce and did not eat it by snatching the produce. It was **said by Rabbi Abba** that in **the school of Rav they say:** It is **even a cluster of dates** stuck together. **Rav Beivai asks:** If one ate **dough** made **of dates, what** is the *halakha*? Is this considered dignified consumption? The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The present daf continues from the middle of 79b, where the Gemara has been exploring the husband's entitlement to *peirot* — the usufruct — of *nichsei milog*, property that remains in the wife's legal title while the husband enjoys its income. The central tension throughout this discussion is the tension between protecting the wife's *keren* (principal) and honoring the husband's right to ongoing *peirot*.

### Tif & Principal

The Gemara had just dealt with the question of *tif* — the natural increment of a flock or the offspring of female slaves — and whether some portion of that increment must be reinvested to protect the principal, since animals, unlike land, can die. The Gemara had noted that even when an animal dies, some residual value remains in the form of leather and the like, though one must acknowledge that this offers rather minimal protection of the original principal.

### Chanania Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא אָכְלָה דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד מַאי? אָמַר עוּלָּא: פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ תְּרֵי אָמוֹרָאֵי בְּמַעְרְבָא, חַד אָמַר: בִּכְאִיסָּר, וְחַד אָמַר: בִּכְדִינָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **he did not eat it in a dignified manner, what** is the *halakha*? How much must he eat to be deemed a proper act of consumption? **Ulla said: Two *amora'im* in the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **disagree** about **this** matter. **One said:** He ate the amount **of an *issar*, and one said:** He ate the measure **of a dinar.**

The Gemara now rules: *amar Ravana bar Chiya amar Shmuel, halacha ke-Chanania* — the ruling follows Chanania, who held that whether the case involves female slaves or animals, the increment belongs to the husband as *peirot*. Chanania's position was *lo chashinan le-mita* — we are not concerned about the eventual death or loss of the principal. Since the risk to the principal is distant and for the time being the principal remains intact, we rule accordingly and the *tif* of the flock passes directly to the husband.

## Fruits & Principal Types

### Shevach Beit Aviha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דַּיָּינֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא: עֲבַד רַב יְהוּדָה עוֹבָדָא בַּחֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת. רַב יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲכָלָהּ עׇרְלָה, שְׁבִיעִית, וְכִלְאַיִם — הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The judges of Pumbedita say: Rav Yehuda took action** in a case **of a bundle of branches.** A husband took them from his wife's property and fed them to his animals, and Rav Yehuda ruled that this was treated as consumption of her property. The Gemara comments: **Rav Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning.** As **Rav Yehuda said:** If one took possession of a plot of land and **consumed** some of the produce of its trees that was forbidden due to the **prohibition against eating the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting** [***orla***] or produce of **the seventh year,** or a forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds, this** is considered **taking possession** of the land, as he was allowed to benefit from the permitted branches.

Notwithstanding the ruling in Chanania's favor, the Gemara immediately introduces an important qualification. *Amar Rav amar Rav Nachman*: although we rule like Chanania and the offspring of the animals or the children of the female slave are considered *peirot* belonging to the husband, *modeh Chanania she-im nitgarsha notenet damim ve-notaltan* — Chanania concedes that if the wife is divorced, she may pay for those offspring and take them back with her. The reason given is *mipnei shevach beit aviha* — on account of the dignity and status of her father's house. This is a concept we will encounter again in the next *mishnah*. The image here is evocative: a woman who enters a marriage bringing a flock and departs twenty years later with a much larger one. Even though during the marriage those offspring were legitimately the husband's *peirot*, her right, upon divorce, to pay their value and reclaim them reflects a recognized interest in maintaining the status associated with the property she originally brought in.

### Regular Fruit Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַמּוֹצִיא הוֹצָאוֹת עַל נִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ קְטַנָּה — כְּמוֹצִיא עַל נִכְסֵי אַחֵר דָּמֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא — עֲבַדוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִיפְסְדִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yaakov said** that **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **one who outlays expenditures for** the **property of his wife** who is a **minor girl** and was married off by her mother or brothers, he is **considered like one who outlays** expenditures **for the property of someone else.** Therefore, if she performed refusal upon reaching maturity, thereby annulling the marriage, he takes the value of the improvement. **What is the reason** for this? **The Sages enacted this ordinance in order that he should not** let her property **depreciate.** If he is not guaranteed reimbursement for his expenses if she refuses him as her husband, he will not attend to the upkeep of her property, causing its value to decline.

The Gemara continues with a new formulation from Rav Nachman: *hichnisah lo ez le-chalavah u-rachel le-gizatah ve-tarnegolet le-beitzatah ve-dekel le-peirotav* — if a woman brings into the marriage a goat for its milk, a ewe for its wool, a hen for its eggs, or a palm tree for its dates, the husband may continue eating the *peirot* — *ochel ve-holech ad she-tichleh ha-keren* — even until the principal is entirely consumed.

These cases are somewhat different from the earlier discussion of offspring, where the increment arises less frequently. Here we are speaking of regular, ongoing yield: milk, wool, eggs, dates. These are the classic model of *karka ve-peirot* — a principal that produces regular fruit — and accordingly there is no question that the husband is entitled to consume that fruit. Rashi notes that in all these cases some residual value remains even upon the animal's death, such as the hide, though he acknowledges that this is a rather thin form of principal protection.

### Non-Renewable Fruits

The Gemara now extends this logic in a direction that is genuinely striking. *Amar Rav Nachman: ayeila le-glimei — peira havei. Michlei va-azeil ad de-kalyah.* If she brings in a cloak, the husband's use of that garment is considered its *peirot*, and he may continue using it even as it wears away entirely. This is the same conceptual framework: even where the principal itself is being consumed in the course of producing the benefit, we classify that benefit as *peirot* and grant it to the husband.

A *baraita* reinforces and extends this principle further: *ha-melach ve-ha-chol harei zeh peirot* — salt and sand extracted from a mine are classified as *peirot*. These are non-renewable resources. When you dig sand from a pit or extract salt from a deposit, you are not generating something new — you are removing the *keren* itself. After extraction, nothing remains to replenish it. And yet the Sages classify this as *peirot*. Similarly, *gafrit* — sulfur — and what Rashi identifies as *alum* (used as a dye for clothing and soap) extracted from a mine fall into this category.

The *chiddush* here is considerable. If a woman brings into the marriage what amounts to oil fields, all of the oil extracted is classified as *peirot* going directly to the husband — even though once extracted, it is irreplaceable. After divorce, the well is no more productive than before; indeed, it is less so.

### Fruit vs. Principal

At this point the Gemara introduces the famous dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim*: *Rabbi Meir omer keren va-Chachamim omrim peirot* — Rabbi Meir classifies these extracted resources as principal, while the *Chachamim* classify them as *peirot*. This maps onto a broader debate about whether fruit still attached to the tree (*mechubarim*) is considered part of the *karka* or already reckoned as *peirot*.

The Gemara then introduces Rabbi Shimon's position: *Rabbi Shimon omer makom she-yifeh kocha* — Rabbi Shimon holds that where the husband's position is stronger at the time of marriage, it is weaker at the time of divorce, and vice versa for the wife. The Gemara objects that Rabbi Shimon's view is identical to that of the *Tanna Kamma*, since the *Tanna Kamma* also held that fruit attached to the tree at the time she enters the marriage is classified as *karka* and not *peirot*, consistent with the *Chachamim*'s position against Rabbi Meir. *Amar Rava: mechubarim be-sha'at yetzi'ah ika beinaihu* — the practical difference between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim* concerns fruit attached to the tree at the moment of divorce.

According to Rashi, the *Chachamim* hold that fruit which grew on the tree while the couple was married did so *bi-reshuto*, under his ownership, and therefore remains his. When the wife departs, she takes only the *karka* itself and must pay the husband for the value of fruit then attached to the tree. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that just as attached fruit is classified as *karka* when she enters, so too it is classified as *karka* when she exits — and she therefore takes it with her without payment. The *Chachamim* distinguish: entry and exit are not symmetric, because the fruit grew during his period of entitlement to *peirot*.

## Status vs. Income

### Old Slaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָפְלוּ לָהּ עֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת זְקֵנִים — יִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִלָּקַח מֵהֶן קַרְקַע, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ. נָפְלוּ לָהּ זֵיתִים וּגְפָנִים זְקֵנִים — יִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִלָּקַח בָּהֶן קַרְקַע, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **elderly slaves or maidservants were bequeathed to her, they are sold and land is acquired with them, and** the husband **consumes the produce** of the land. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: She** need **not sell** these slaves and maidservants, **because they are assets of her paternal family,** and it would be shameful to the family if they were sold to others. Likewise, if **old olive** trees **or grapevines were bequeathed to her, they are sold and land is acquired with them, and he consumes the produce. Rabbi Yehuda says: She** need **not sell** them, **because they are assets of her paternal family.**

The next *mishnah* takes up a new scenario. *Noflu lah avadim u-shfachos zekeinim* — she inherits elderly slaves. Old slaves produce little or no *peirot*; they may be more of a burden than a benefit. The *mishnah* rules that such slaves should be sold and the proceeds used to purchase land, from which the husband will then eat *peirot*: *yimachru ve-yilakach bahem karka ve-hu ochel peiros*. The husband, it appears, can compel this conversion.

However, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* dissents: *lo timkor mipnei she-hem shevach beit aviha* — she may not be compelled to sell them, because having servants, even infirm ones, contributes to her personal dignity and status. He cannot override that interest merely to secure a higher income for himself.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

The *mishnah* applies the same framework to old olive trees and grapevines: *noflu lah zeisim u-gefanim zekeinim* — she inherits aging orchards with diminished productivity. Again, the rule is that they should be sold and land purchased from the proceeds. But here *Rabbi Yehuda* objects: *lo timkor mipnei shevach beit aviha* — she may not be forced to sell, because even a vineyard or an olive grove, even one past its prime, confers status. Rabbi Yehuda thus goes further than Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: it is one thing to protect the status attached to a household of servants; Rabbi Yehuda holds that even the possession of an orchard, however unproductive, carries the same dignity interest.

### Own vs. Borrowed Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנָּפְלוּ בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁלָּהּ, אֲבָל בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלָּהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל תִּמְכּוֹר, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא כָלְיָא קַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Kahana said** that **Rav said:** This **dispute** concerning olive trees and grapevines is referring to a case **when they were bequeathed** to her **in her field,** as they are assets of her paternal family, and therefore Rabbi Yehuda rules that she need not sell them. **But** if she received them **in a field that is not hers, everyone agrees** that **she must sell** them **because** the **principal** will be **consumed.** Since these trees will not yield much produce, they will eventually be uprooted, and transitory property is not included in the category of assets of her paternal family.

The Gemara introduces a crucial distinction from Rav Kahana in the name of Rav: *machlokes she-noflu be-sadeh shelah* — the dispute between the *mishnah*'s ruling and the dissenting opinions applies only when the vines or olive trees are situated in land that she herself owns. In that case, the argument about *shevach beit aviha* is fully operative, and there is a genuine disagreement about whether the husband may compel a sale.

But *aval be-sadeh she-einah shelah* — if she owns only the trees and not the underlying land — *divrei ha-kol timkor*, everyone agrees she must sell. Why? *Mishum de-kalyah karna* — because in that case the principal itself is in the process of being destroyed. Old trees in a field one does not own will eventually die and leave nothing behind. The principal is actively at risk, and this transforms the analysis entirely.

This raises an interesting question: the risk to the principal is fundamentally *her* concern, since the principal belongs to her. Why should that risk be used to override *her* preference not to sell? Rashi, however, addresses this point from two directions simultaneously. The fact that the principal is at risk means *he* cannot object to a sale if *she* wants one, since she can invoke her interest in protecting her property. And the same fact drains the situation of any *shevach beit aviha*, since there is no status to be gained from trees on someone else's dying land — so she cannot invoke dignity to resist the sale either. Thus neither party can object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: הֲרֵי עֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת, דְּכִי שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלָּהּ דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי! אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלָּהּ, אֲבָל בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁלָּהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this: But** elderly **slaves and maidservants are considered like a field that is not hers,** as nothing will remain of the principal, **and** yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the first *tanna* **disagree** over this case. The Gemara retracts: **Rather,** if the above statement of Rav Kahana **was stated, it was stated as follows: Rav Kahana said** that **Rav said:** This **dispute** with regard to olive trees and vines is referring to when they are located **in a field that is not hers, but** if they are located **in her field, everyone agrees** that **she** need **not sell** them, **because** they are **assets of her paternal family.**

Rav Yosef raises an apparent difficulty: old slaves, even when infirm, are somewhat analogous to trees in another's field — the principal will eventually be consumed — yet the *mishnah* presents that as a case of genuine dispute (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel protecting the wife's status even there). The Gemara resolves this by inverting Rav Kahana's formulation: the dispute is actually in the case where the field is *not* hers, and even there one opinion holds that *shevach beit aviha* applies. But *aval be-sadeh shelo* — if she owns the land itself and the vines, so the land will remain even after the vines die — *divrei ha-kol lo timkor mipnei shevach beit aviha*: everyone agrees she cannot be compelled to sell, since the status interest is fully present and the principal, in the form of the land, is secure.

## Husband Expenses

### Investment Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא הוֹצָאוֹת עַל נִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ, הוֹצִיא הַרְבֵּה וְאָכַל קִימְעָא, קִימְעָא וְאָכַל הַרְבֵּה — מַה שֶּׁהוֹצִיא הוֹצִיא, וּמַה שֶּׁאָכַל אָכַל. הוֹצִיא וְלֹא אָכַל — יִשָּׁבַע כַּמָּה הוֹצִיא, וְיִטּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who pays expenditures for his wife's property** in an effort to improve it, if **he paid a large** amount in expenditures **and ate** only **a small** amount of produce before he divorced her, or if he paid **a small** amount in expenditures **and ate a large** quantity of produce, **that which he spent he has spent, and that which he ate he has eaten.** Therefore, none of it need be returned. However, if he **paid** expenditures for the property **and did not eat** any part of it, **he takes an oath** with regard to **how much he paid and** then **takes** his expenditures.

The next *mishnah* shifts to a different but related question: what happens when the husband actually spends money — *hotza'ot* — on the wife's *nichsei milog* property? *Ha-motzi hotza'ot al nichsei ishto* — the husband who makes out-of-pocket expenditures on his wife's property.

The *mishnah* establishes the baseline rule cleanly: *hotzi harbe ve-achal kima* — he invested a great deal and harvested very little, resulting in a net loss; or *kima ve-achal harbe* — he invested little and harvested a great deal, a significant net gain. In either case: *mah she-hotzi hotzi u-mah she-achal achal* — what he spent he spent, and what he ate he ate. He takes the risk and reaps the rewards. The investment is entirely his, the profit is entirely his, and the loss is entirely his. This makes straightforward sense: he bears the commercial risk and is entitled to the commercial upside.

### Hotzi V'Lo Achal

The more interesting case is: *hotzi ve-lo achal* — he invested but received no benefit whatsoever. The *mishnah* rules that in this case *yishava kama hotzi ve-yitol* — he may take an oath as to how much he invested and recover that amount.

One might initially read this as applying whenever he received no gross income — even during an ongoing marriage, simply because a bad harvest or blight left him with nothing. But Rashi explicitly interprets the scenario more narrowly: *lo hispik le-echol ad she-gerasha* — the reason he received no *achal* is that he was divorced before the harvest came in. Rashi is evidently concerned about manipulation: if we allowed a husband to recover his investment simply by choosing not to harvest — deliberately letting the produce rot so as to claim *hotzi ve-lo achal* — it would invite serious *hara'mah*, strategic deception. Similarly, it would allow a husband to take enormous risks with his investment knowing he could always recover his principal if the harvest disappointed. Rashi therefore limits the case to a genuine divorce scenario, where the husband literally could not eat because the marriage ended before the harvest.

### Minimum Benefit

The Gemara asks: *ve-kama achila* — what is the minimum amount of benefit that disqualifies the *hotzi ve-lo achal* claim? *Amar Rav Assi: afilu grogeret achat* — even a single pressed fig. But this applies only *ve-hu she-achalah derech kavod* — if he consumed it in a dignified manner, meaning it was a meaningful act of consumption, not merely an incidental snack.

*Amar Rabbi Abba amar Bei Rav: afilu shigra de-tamre* — even a pressed date suffices. The Gemara then records an unresolved question: *chuptza de-tamre mai?* — what about a mashed date? *Teiku* — no resolution.

The Gemara further asks: if he did not eat even the minimal amount *derech kavod*, what then? *Amar Ulla: peigi bah terei amoraei be-ma'arava* — two Amoraim in the Land of Israel dispute the minimum monetary threshold: one says the equivalent of an *isar*, the other a *dinar*. Either way, it is not a large sum. If that small amount passed through his hands from the property, he cannot recover his investment.

The judges of Pumbedita report that *Rav Yehuda* acted on a case involving *chazilei zemorot* — bundles of twigs taken from the field. Merely taking those twigs was considered sufficient benefit to deny the husband's recovery claim. This is consistent with Rav Yehuda's broader reasoning, as seen in the rule that consuming *orlah*, *shevi'it*, or *kila'im* produce from a field constitutes *chazakah* — establishing one's presence. Rashi there explains that "consuming" must refer to taking the wood or twigs (since the fruit itself is *assur be-hana'ah*), and Tosafot raises the question of whether even the wood of *kila'im* might be *assur*. But the analogy holds: just as taking twigs establishes one's presence for purposes of *chazakah* in Bava Batra, here too it counts as extracting benefit that bars recovery of the investment.

## Oath Requirement

### Minor Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַמּוֹצִיא הוֹצָאוֹת עַל נִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ קְטַנָּה — כְּמוֹצִיא עַל נִכְסֵי אַחֵר דָּמֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא — עֲבַדוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִיפְסְדִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yaakov said** that **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **one who outlays expenditures for** the **property of his wife** who is a **minor girl** and was married off by her mother or brothers, he is **considered like one who outlays** expenditures **for the property of someone else.** Therefore, if she performed refusal upon reaching maturity, thereby annulling the marriage, he takes the value of the improvement. **What is the reason** for this? **The Sages enacted this ordinance in order that he should not** let her property **depreciate.** If he is not guaranteed reimbursement for his expenses if she refuses him as her husband, he will not attend to the upkeep of her property, causing its value to decline.

The Gemara introduces a special case: *amar Rabbi Yaakov amar Rav Chisda: ha-motzi hotza'ot al nichsei ishto ketanah* — a husband who invests in the property of his minor wife (*ketanah*), specifically one who was married off by her mother and brothers and who therefore retains the right of *mi'un*, the ability to dissolve the marriage retroactively.

This creates a distinctive problem. The husband invests money in the field, the wife allows him to harvest just enough to trigger the *achal* threshold, and then she performs *mi'un* — which retroactively annuls the marriage as if it never existed. Retroactively, he was never her husband; he simply spent money improving a stranger's field. His investment may be entirely at her mercy.

The Gemara rules: *ke-motzi al nichsei acher dami* — it is treated as though he invested in a stranger's property. *Mai ta'ama?* — why? *Avdu bah rabanan takanta ki heichi de-la nifsadinehu* — the Rabbis enacted a protective ruling so that he should not act in ways that damage the property. The practical concern is obvious: if every dollar he invests is at risk of retroactive loss the moment she performs *mi'un*, he will minimize investment and maximize extraction, potentially at the expense of the principal. To induce prudent stewardship, the Rabbis protect his investment by treating him as a third-party investor with corresponding rights of recovery.

This also has a logical basis: since *mi'un* retroactively voids the marriage, at the time he was investing he was, retroactively speaking, a stranger. There is a famous position of Reb Chaim that explores just how far we look backwards after *mi'un* — whether we treat the period of the marriage as entirely void in retrospect or whether we recognize its status as it existed at the time. But for the purpose of this *halacha*, the Gemara treats it as investment in a stranger's field.

## Inheritance Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דִּנְפַלוּ לַהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי בֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אֲזַל גַּבְרָא אַפֵּיק שֵׁית מְאָה אַיְיתַי אַרְבַּע מְאָה. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָתֵי, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ חַד זוּזָא וּשְׁקַל מִנַּיְיהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַה שֶּׁהוֹצִיא — הוֹצִיא, וּמַה שֶּׁאָכַל — אָכַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who had four hundred dinars bequeathed to her** in **Bei Ḥozai,** a remote location in Babylonia. The **man,** her husband, **went** and **took** with him **six hundred** of his own dinars for travel expenses and **brought** back with him **four hundred. While he was coming** back **he required one dinar, which he took from** the money he had collected. **He came before Rabbi Ami** for a ruling. Rabbi Ami **said to him: That which he spent he has spent, and that which he ate he has eaten.** He has benefited from one dinar of her money and spent six hundred of his own, and neither amount can be claimed.

The Gemara now presents a vivid case. *D'naflu lah arba me'ah zuzei be-Khuza'i* — a woman had an inheritance of 400 *zuz* located in a distant city. The husband went there — *azal gavrah, afik shesh me'ah* — and spent 600 *zuz* on travel and retrieval expenses. When he arrived, the property turned out to be worth only 400 *zuz*, which he liquidated and brought back. On the return journey, he needed one *zuz* for incidental expenses — *itztarikh leh chada zuza* — and took it from the 400 he had recovered. The case came before Rav Ami.

### Rav Ami Ruling

*Amar leh Rav Ami: mah she-hotzi hotzi u-mah she-achal achal* — Rav Ami applied the standard *mishnah*'s rule: he invested (600 *zuz*), he ate (the 1 *zuz* spent en route), and the arrangement stands as is. Since he received some benefit, even minimal, he cannot recover his investment. The net loss of 200 *zuz* is his to absorb.

### Rabbis' Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקָאָכֵיל פֵּירָא, הָא קַרְנָא קָאָכֵיל וְהוֹצָאָה הִיא. אִם כֵּן, הָוֵה לֵיהּ הוֹצִיא וְלֹא אָכַל, יִשָּׁבַע כַּמָּה הוֹצִיא, וְיִטּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to Rabbi Ami: This applies** only **where** he **consumes** the **produce** of his wife's property, but **this** one **ate** from the **principal, and it is** merely **expenditures.** He replied: **If so, this is** a case of one who pays expenditures **and did not eat,** and the *halakha* is that in such a case **he takes an oath** with regard to **how much he paid and** then **takes** that amount.

The Rabbis objected to Rav Ami: *hanei milei heicha de-achal peirot* — that rule applies only when what he consumed was genuinely *peirot* produced by the investment. Here, what he retrieved was the *keren* itself — the principal. *Ha karna ka achil* — he was eating the principal, not *peirot*. The one *zuz* he spent was drawn not from the income of an ongoing investment but from the very corpus of the property he had retrieved. *Havei leh hotzi ve-lo achal* — this is properly classified as a case where he invested and received no *peirot*. *Ei hachi havei lei hotzi ve-lo achal yishava kama hotzi ve-yitol* — and therefore he may swear how much he invested and recover it.

### Value Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשָּׁבַע כַּמָּה הוֹצִיא וְיִטּוֹל. אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: וְהוּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ שֶׁבַח כְּנֶגֶד הוֹצָאָה. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁאִם הָיָה שֶׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הוֹצָאָה — נוֹטֵל אֶת הַהוֹצָאָה בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **He takes an oath** with regard to **how much he spent and takes** this sum. **Rabbi Asi said: And this** applies only **if there is enhancement** to the property **corresponding to** his **expense.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** was this stated? Is this a stringency for the husband that if the value of enhancement is less he may not reclaim all his expenses, or is it a leniency that if the value is greater he need not take an oath? **Abaye said:** It means **that if** the value of **enhancement was greater than** the **expense, he takes the expense without an oath.**

At this point a critical limitation must be noted. Can he recover the full 600 *zuz*? The Rosh addresses this explicitly and rules: *veyitol ad shiur shevach de-shaminan kama havu shavin arba me'ah zuzei de-shata ve-lo yoter* — he may recover only up to the actual increase in value of the property, not more. The wife cannot be made to suffer a loss as a result of his investment. Since the property was worth 400 *zuz*, he may recover at most 400 *zuz*. He cannot force her to pay 600 for a property worth only 400.

This limitation has implications beyond the specific case of divorce that Rashi describes. In this scenario, the couple does not appear to have been divorced at all; this is simply money that went into a hole and produced no *peirot*. Nevertheless, the husband can recover his investment — but only up to the actual increase in value he delivered to the property. He spent 600 *zuz* to give his wife access to a 400 *zuz* asset; he may therefore recover 400 *zuz*. The Rosh's principle ensures that the wife never pays more than the benefit she actually received.

## Oath Requirement

### Oath Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, אָתֵי לְאִיעָרוֹמֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה הוֹצָאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא הוֹצָאָה שִׁיעוּר שֶׁבַח, וּבִשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so, he will come to deceive,** as he can always say that he spent slightly less than the value of the enhancement and thereby receive this amount without having to take an oath. **Rather, Rava said:** It means **that if the expense was greater than the enhancement, he has** rights to reclaim the **expense only** up to the **amount of** the **enhancement,** but no more, **and** even this amount he can claim only **by an oath.**

The Gemara returns to the question of when an oath is required and what it accomplishes. *Amar Rav Assi: ve-hu she-yesh shevach ke-neged hotza'ah* — recovery through oath is available only where the actual increase in value is at least equivalent to the investment. This gives rise to two distinct scenarios: either the investment exceeded the increase (the case it comes to exclude from full recovery), or the increase exceeded the investment.

*Amar Abaye: she-im haya shevach yeter al ha-hotza'ah, notel et ha-hotza'ah belo shvu'a* — Abaye held that if the increase in value exceeds the investment, he need not take an oath; he simply claims his investment and is believed. Only when it is a break-even — investment equals increase — must he swear, since the wife leaves with nothing additional in her pocket.

*Amar leh Rava: im ken ati l'arumei* — Rava objected that this invites deception. If an oath is required only when investment equals increase, a husband will always claim he spent just under the increase, since below that threshold he is believed without a *shvu'ah*. The temptation to inflate the expense report is simply too great.

### Resolution

Rava's resolution stands as the final ruling: *Ela amar Rava: she-im hayta hotza'ah yetira al ha-shevach, ein lo ela hotza'ah shiur shevach u-veshvu'ah* — the ruling is as follows. He must always swear to collect. Moreover, if his investment exceeded the actual increase in value of the property, he is entitled only to recover up to the amount of the increase — not the full investment. *U-veshvu'ah* — and even then, always subject to an oath. This dual requirement — oath always required, recovery capped at actual increase — protects against both false claims and against making the wife bear losses that exceed the benefit she actually received from the investment.

## Husband Expenses

### Sharecroppers Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּעַל שֶׁהוֹרִיד אֲרִיסִין תַּחְתָּיו, מַהוּ? אַדַּעְתָּא דְבַעַל נָחֵית. אִיסְתַּלַּיק לֵיהּ בַּעַל, אִיסְתַּלַּיקוּ לְהוּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: אַדַּעְתָּא דְאַרְעָא נָחֵית, וְאַרְעָא כִּי קָיְימָא — לַאֲרִיסֵי קָיְימָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **a husband who engaged sharecroppers** to work his wife's property **in his stead, what is** the *halakha*? Does a sharecropper **begin work** on the land **with the intention** to work **for the husband,** so that if **the husband departs** the property, e.g., if he divorces his wife, **they** too **depart** as sharecroppers and do not receive their share of the profits from the land? **Or perhaps** a sharecropper **begins work with the intention** to work **the land, and the land, as it stands, stands** to be worked **by sharecroppers?** Since their involvement is directly with the land, it makes no difference who hired them, and they would stay on the land.

The Gemara raises a new question: *ba'al she-horid arisin tachtav mahu?* — what is the law when the husband places sharecroppers (*arisin*) on his wife's property? The scenario: the husband arranges for sharecroppers to work the land on the standard arrangement — they invest their labor and expenses, take half the harvest, and he takes the other half. But before the harvest comes in, he divorces his wife. The sharecroppers have invested in the land; the husband, having made no out-of-pocket expenditure himself (since his payment to them comes from the harvest), has received nothing. The wife now holds the improved land. What are the sharecroppers' rights — against whom, and for how much?

The Gemara presents two possible frameworks. *Ada'ata d'va'al nachit* — they entered into their arrangement with the husband. As Tosafot elaborates, presumably the sharecroppers were aware this was *nichsei milog* and understood that the husband's right to *peirot* was contingent on remaining married through the harvest. By taking the arrangement, they accepted that risk. If so, *histalek lei va'al histalku lehu* — when the husband is removed from the picture by divorce, they are removed with him. Their claim against the wife is zero.

*O dilma ada'ata de-ara necheis* — or perhaps their real relationship is not with the husband but with the land itself. There is something to an *aris* arrangement — sharecropping — that is distinctively tied to the land rather than to the owner. Unlike a day laborer (*po'el*) whose relationship is purely personal and contractual, the *aris* invests in the land and participates in its yield. On this view, their entitlement inheres in the land regardless of the marital status of its owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן: מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהַיּוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ וּנְטָעָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, שָׁמִין לוֹ, וְיָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Ḥanan objects to this** line of inquiry: In **what** way **is** this case **different from** that **of one who entered the field** of another **and planted it without permission?** In such a situation **one evaluates** his expenses **for him** and the value of his enhancement of the field, **and he is at a disadvantage.** Therefore, he always receives the smaller sum, whether it is equal to his expenses or the enhancement of the property. In this case too, even if the sharecroppers are viewed as unauthorized occupiers of the land, why shouldn't they be treated like one who entered another's field without permission and receive at least the smaller sum?

Rav Pappa bar Nachmani objects to the framing of this question entirely: even in the most unfavorable scenario, why should the sharecroppers receive *less* than a stranger who enters another's field without permission and plants it? Such a person is at minimum entitled to *yad al ha-tachtonah* — the lower hand — which is some form of recovery. So why are we considering zero as a possible outcome?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לֵיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּטָרַח, הָכָא אִיכָּא בַּעַל דְּטָרַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The two cases are not comparable: **There,** when one enters another's land, **there is no one** else **that** will **exert** himself for it, and therefore it is reasonable that the one who invested in this property should at least be compensated for the lesser value. However, **here, there is a husband who exerts** himself for the land. Since the sharecroppers act in his stead, they are entitled to remain on the land only as long as he is present.

The Gemara answers: *hatam leika inish de-tarach, hacha ika va'al de-tarach* — when a stranger enters an ownerless or neglected field, there is objectively no one who would have worked it, and his uninvited investment therefore confers a real benefit. But here, the wife can say: had the husband done it himself, I would owe him nothing — because his uninvited investment, ending without any *achal*, would be governed by *hotzi ve-lo achal* only in the context of divorce (per Rashi), and even there it would be capped by the increase in value. Since the husband could have worked the land himself, the sharecroppers' entry displaces no genuine gap; the wife suffered no loss by their working rather than the husband working.

### Land-Based Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: חָזֵינַן אִי בַּעַל אָרִיס הוּא — אִיסְתַּלַּק לֵיהּ בַּעַל, אִסְתַּלַּקוּ לְהוּ. אִי בַּעַל לָאו אָרִיס הוּא, אַרְעָא לַאֲרִיסֵי קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about** it, i.e., the original question? **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: We examine** the matter: **If** this **husband is a sharecropper** himself and possesses sufficient knowledge of working the land to perform the task himself, then when **the husband departs** from the property **they** too **depart,** as they are taking his place. **If** the **husband is not a sharecropper, the land is ready for sharecroppers,** as the husband would not have performed the work himself. Since the wife was in need of sharecroppers, they are not considered to have acted on behalf of the husband and do not forfeit their share.

*Amar Rav Huna breih de-Rav Yehoshua: chazina* — we examine the facts. *Ei va'al aris hu* — if the husband himself is capable of working the land as a sharecropper, then indeed *histalek lei va'al histalku lehu* — when the husband is gone, the sharecroppers' claim is gone with him, because the wife can say her husband would have worked it. Their deal was with the husband, and his removal ends their entitlement.

*Ei bela'av arisu* — but if the husband was not himself capable of working the land, then *ara le-arisuta kaima* — the land was, by its very nature, waiting for sharecroppers to come and work it. Someone had to do this work, and the natural arrangement is an *aris* relationship. In that case the sharecroppers are entitled not merely to their out-of-pocket investment but to the full *aris* arrangement, because that is what the land required. Presumably this also reflects that the husband made the actual deal with them, so their claim is grounded both in the nature of the land relationship and in the contractual arrangement.

## Transfer of Rights

### Selling Peirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּעַל שֶׁמָּכַר קַרְקַע לְפֵירוֹת, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן מַאי דְּקָנֵי (לַהּ) אַקְנִי, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כִּי תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן פֵּירוֹת לְבַעַל —
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to a **husband who sold** his wife's **land for produce,** i.e., rights to the produce were sold to one who agrees to work the land in exchange, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say: That which belongs to** the husband **he has transferred** to others, and therefore the sale of the produce is valid, **or perhaps** the principle is **that when the Sages instituted** that the **produce** goes **to the husband,**

A new *ibaya* opens: *ba'al she-machar karka l'peirot, mahu?* — may the husband sell his usufruct rights in his wife's property to a third party for a lump sum? The logic in favor seems straightforward: the husband legally holds a *kinyan l'peiros*, a recognized property right in the *peirot*. Ordinarily *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan ha-guf dami* — the right to *peirot* is treated like ownership of the body for many purposes. Why, then, should he be unable to transfer that right, receiving a lump sum in lieu of the ongoing annual yield?

The Gemara frames two competing rationales. *Mi amrinan mah d'kana akni* — does he sell what he owns, and therefore the sale stands? *O dilma ki tikinu lei rabanan peiros le-ba'al mishum revacha d'beisa* — or perhaps the Sages' grant of *peirot* to the husband was made specifically for the purpose of *revach ha-bayit*, providing a steady, ongoing income to the household? If that is the reason, then the right was given for a purpose and may not be redirected. A one-time lump sum deposited in a bank account — or spent on a luxury — is not the same as the regular annual income that the *peirot* arrangement is designed to provide. *Aval le-zabuni, lo* — it was not intended to be converted into a lump sale.

### Validity Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רְוַוח בֵּיתָא, אֲבָל לְזַבּוֹנֵי — לָא? יְהוּדָה מָר בַּר מָרִימָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה, עָשׂוּי. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** they did so **for** the **gain of the house,** as more food is available when he brings produce home, **but** in order for him **to sell** it they did **not** institute their decree? Two opinions were stated with regard to this issue: **Yehuda Mar bar Mareimar said in the name of Rava: What he did is done,** i.e., takes effect. **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava: He did not do anything.**

*Yehuda Mar Bar Marmar mishmei de-Rava amar: mah she-asah asui* — in the name of Rava, Yehuda Mar Bar Marmar held that *be-di'eved* such a sale is valid. *Rav Pappa amar mishmei de-Rava: lo asah ve-lo klum* — Rav Pappa, also in Rava's name, held that it does not work at all; the sale is entirely void. Rav Pappa notes that Yehuda Mar Bar Marmar's attribution to Rava was not stated explicitly but inferred from a case.

### Maidservant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא דִּיהוּדָה מָר בַּר מָרִימָר לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִתְּמַר, אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִתְּמַר. דְּהָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּעַיִּילָה לֵיהּ לְגַבְרַאּ תַּרְתֵּי אַמְהָתָא, אֲזַל גַּבְרָא נְסֵיב אִיתְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי, עַיֵּיל לַהּ חֲדָא מִנַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This** statement **of Yehuda Mar bar Mareimar was not stated explicitly** in Rava's name. **Rather,** it **was stated from an inference** based on an incident that occurred in **which a certain woman brought in for** her **husband two maidservants** as part of her dowry. The **man went** and **married another woman** in addition to the first. **He** subsequently **brought in to** the second wife **one of** the maidservants to attend to her needs.

The case from which Yehuda Mar Bar Marmar inferred: a woman brought two maidservants into the marriage. The husband, retaining the right to their service as *peirot*, then married a second wife and told her that a maidservant's service — drawn from his *peirot* rights over his first wife's property — would be at her disposal. The first wife cried out in protest: *tzavcha* — he was effectively transferring to the second wife the benefit he had in her maidservant. The case came before Rava, who did not intervene. The observer might infer that Rava held such a transfer to be valid.

### Revach Beisa Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, צְוַוחָה, לָא אַשְׁגַּח בַּהּ. מַאן דַּחֲזָא סָבַר מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי. וְלָא הִיא, מִשּׁוּם רְוַוח בֵּיתָא — וְהָא קָא רָוַוח.
>
> **Translation:** The first wife **came before Rava** and **cried** about the injustice done to her, but **Rava took no notice of her,** claiming she had no right to complain. **He who observed** this incident **thought** that Rava ruled this way **because he holds** that **what he did is done,** i.e., takes effect, and a husband may sell his wife's usufruct property and use its produce as he sees fit. **But that is not so,** as the Sages instituted the ordinance that a husband owns the rights to the produce of his wife's property **for the gain of the house, and** here **the house does gain** from his action, as the maidservant also performs work for the house.

But the Gemara clarifies: *ve-lo hi* — that inference is incorrect. *Mishum revach beisa* — Rava's real reason for not intervening was that even on his view, the transfer here remained within the framework of household benefit. The maidservant, though technically serving the second wife, was still performing domestic work within the household — cooking, cleaning, and so on — and this preserved the essential character of the *peirot* arrangement: an ongoing benefit to the *beisa*, the household. Selling the right externally for a lump sum is a categorically different act and would not be countenanced even by Rava.

## Prohibition Reasons

### Exploitation Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: בַּעַל שֶׁמָּכַר קַרְקַע לְפֵירוֹת — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תַּכְסִיף. רָבָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם רְוַוח בֵּיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **a husband who sold land for produce did not do anything.** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for this ruling? **Abaye said: We are concerned** that **perhaps** the land itself **will deteriorate** over time, as the purchaser has acquired only its produce and has no incentive to take proper care of the land. **Rava said:** This is **because** there is no **gain of the house** here.

The Gemara now states the final *halacha* explicitly: *ve-hilcheta ve-lo hi de-kol she-machar nechasav le-peiros lo asah ve-lo klum* — the ruling is that any sale of *peirot* rights is entirely void. The Gemara then explores the underlying rationale, since it matters practically.

*Abaye amar: chashinan shema tachsise* — Abaye's reason is concern that a third-party buyer will exploit the land and destroy the principal. The husband has a long-term stake in the marriage and in the property; he is incentivized to manage it prudently. A purchaser of the *peirot* rights, however, faces a different calculus: the husband could divorce his wife at any time, ending the purchaser's rights. Knowing this, such a purchaser will squeeze the maximum *peirot* from the land in the minimum time, without regard to the long-term health of the principal. The prohibition thus protects the wife's *keren*.

### Household Income

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ אַרְעָא דִּמְקָרְבָא לְמָתָא, אִי נָמֵי: בַּעַל אָרִיס הוּא. אִי נָמֵי, זוּזֵי וְקָא עָבֵיד בְּהוּ עִיסְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference between** these two explanations? The Gemara explains: **The practical difference between them** is, e.g., **land that is close to** the **town,** as one can check at any time whether the land is being cared for properly. **Alternatively,** the difference involves **a husband** who **is a sharecropper** and works the land himself but sold the rights of the produce to someone else. As a sharecropper, the husband retains part of the produce and will also ensure that the land does not deteriorate. **Alternatively,** the difference concerns a husband who receives **money** for the produce **and does business with it,** which provides gain for the house.

*Rava amar: mishum revach beisa* — Rava's reason is different. The *peirot* were granted to the husband not to protect the wife's principal but to ensure a regular, ongoing stream of income into the household. A lump-sum sale converts that ongoing yield into a one-time windfall, which serves neither the household's regular needs nor the purpose for which the arrangement was created. The prohibition, on Rava's account, is structural: the right to *peirot* is inherently bound up with the ongoing marital household and cannot be severed from it.

### Exceptions

The practical difference between Abaye and Rava becomes clear in a series of limiting cases. *Ika beinaihu ara de-mekarva le-masa* — if the land is close to the city, the husband can keep an eye on it and prevent a purchaser from exploiting it; Abaye's concern about destruction of the principal is diminished, and on his view the sale might be permitted. But on Rava's view, even in that case the sale would be prohibited, since it still converts ongoing income into a lump sum.

*I nami ba'al aris hu* — or if the husband himself is a skilled *aris*, knowledgeable in land management, he can monitor and control the third party's actions effectively, again addressing Abaye's concern but not Rava's. Finally, *zuzei ve-ka'avid behu iska* — if the asset is money rather than land and the husband invests that money in an *iska*, a structured business arrangement that generates a regular yield, this too addresses Abaye's concern (there is no land to destroy) and, notably, may also satisfy Rava's concern, since the *iska* arrangement generates an ongoing return rather than a one-time lump sum — preserving the essential character of *revach beisa*.
