# Avodah Zarah 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Kashering Methods

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ כְּלֵי תַּשְׁמִישׁ מִן הַגּוֹיִם, דְּבָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן — מַטְבִּילָן וְהֵן טְהוֹרִין. דְּבָרִים שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עַל יְדֵי צוֹנֵן, כְּגוֹן כּוֹסוֹת וְקִתּוֹנִיּוֹת וּצְלוֹחִיּוֹת — מַדִּיחָן וּמַטְבִּילָן וְהֵם טְהוֹרִין. דְּבָרִים שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עַל יְדֵי חַמִּין, כְּגוֹן הַיּוֹרוֹת הַקּוּמְקְמוֹסִין וּמְחַמֵּי חַמִּין — מַגְעִילָן וּמַטְבִּילָן וְהֵם טְהוֹרִין. דְּבָרִים שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר, כְּגוֹן הַשַּׁפּוּדִין וְהָאַסְכְּלָאוֹת — מְלַבְּנָן וּמַטְבִּילָן וְהֵן טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught: One who purchases utensils from the gentiles** must prepare them for use in the following manner: With regard to **items that** the gentile **did not use, one immerses them, and they are pure.** With regard to **items that** the gentile **used for cold** food or drink, **such as cups and jugs and flasks, one rinses them and immerses them, and they are pure.** With regard to **items that** the gentile **used for hot** food or drink, **such as** large **pots,** small **kettles [*hakumkamusun*], and samovars, one purges them** with boiling water **and immerses them, and they are pure.** With regard to **items that** the gentile **used with fire, such as spits and grills, one heats them until white-hot and immerses them, and they are pure.**

The *mishnah* had already established the general principle that vessels are kashered in accordance with the manner in which they were used. The *baraita* cited here elaborates on that principle with greater precision and, notably, brings in the element of *tevilah* as an integral part of the process. The *baraita* identifies four distinct categories of vessels, each requiring a different method of preparation before use.

### Unused Vessels

The first category concerns vessels purchased from non-Jews that were never actually used: *devarim shelo nishtamesh bahen*. For these, the *baraita* states that immersion alone suffices — *matbilan vehen tehorin*. This is a cleaner and more explicit articulation of the principle than what appeared in the *mishnah*.

### Cold-Use Vessels

The second category covers vessels used only for cold contents — glasses, plates, and similar items: *kegon kosot vekityonot utzluchiyot*. Here, one must rinse them and then immerse them — *madiachan umatbilan vehen tehorin*. The rinsing requirement echoes the earlier *mishnah* concerning a wine press: there remains a concern that some residual matter adheres to the inside of the vessel, so one must clean it thoroughly before *tevilah*.

### Hot-Water Vessels

The third category covers vessels used with hot water — pots, kettles, and similar implements: *kegon hayorot vehakumkumos umachamei chamim*. These require *hagala* — immersion in boiling water — followed by *tevilah* in a *mikveh*: *magilan umatbilan vehen tehorin*. The *baraita* thus treats *hagala* and *tevilah* as two sequential and complementary stages in rendering the vessel fit for use.

### Fire-Direct Vessels

The fourth category covers vessels used directly on an open flame — spits and grates: *kegon hashapud veha'askalot*. These require *libun*, heating them until white hot, followed by *tevilah*: *melabnan umatbilan vehen tehorin*.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַטְבִּיל וְשֶׁלֹּא יַגְעִיל וְשֶׁלֹּא יְלַבֵּן, תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אָסוּר, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **all** utensils **that one used before immersing and purging and heating** them **until white-hot, it is taught** in **one** *baraita* that the food prepared with them is **forbidden, and it is taught** in **another** *baraita* that the food prepared with them is **permitted.**

The *baraita* then addresses what happens *bediavad* — if one used such a vessel without first kashering it and without immersing it. One *baraita* rules that the food is forbidden; another *baraita* rules that it is permitted. The *gemara* notes that this is not a genuine contradiction: *lo kashya*.

### Noten Ta'am Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם — אָסוּר, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This contradiction is **not difficult. This** ruling, that the food prepared with such utensils is forbidden, **is according to the one who says** that if a forbidden substance **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture, the permitted food is **forbidden. That** ruling, that the food prepared with such utensils is permitted, **is according to the one who says** that if a forbidden substance **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it remains **permitted.** Here too, since the flavor of non-kosher food imparted by the utensil detracts from the flavor of the food, such food is permitted.

The resolution hinges on the debate regarding *noten ta'am lifgam* — whether a taste that has deteriorated and become unpleasant retains its forbidden status. The operative assumption is that any vessel purchased from a non-Jew will, by the time one uses it, be at least a day old, meaning the absorbed taste will already be *lifgam* — degraded. The *baraita* that permits the food *bediavad* follows the view that *noten ta'am lifgam mutar*, which is the ruling practice. The *baraita* that forbids it follows the opposing view that *noten ta'am lifgam assur*, that even a degraded forbidden taste renders the food prohibited.

## Toveling Status

### Hagala + Toveling

Having laid out the four categories, it is worth pausing on the *baraita*'s consistent pairing of *hagala* and *tevilah*. In each case, both steps are enumerated together as the full process of rendering a vessel fit for use.

### Toveling Prohibition

The *baraita*'s formulation of the *bediavad* case is instructive: it says that if one used the vessel without kashering it *and* without performing *tevilah*, that is the point of potential prohibition. This phrasing could suggest that even omitting *tevilah* alone — without any failure of kashering — suffices to create a prohibited situation, weaving the two requirements tightly together.

### Toveling Order

The practical *halachah* does not follow this strict reading. We rule that *tevilah* may be performed even prior to kashering, though this is itself a matter of debate among the *rishonim*. Similarly, the food is considered kosher *bediavad* even if the vessel was used before *tevilah*. Yet there remains a generally accepted position in *halachah* that it is forbidden to use a vessel before performing *tevilah*, even though the precise source of this prohibition is not entirely clear. There is certainly a *mitzvah* to immerse the vessel, but the nature and origin of the prohibition against using it without first doing so is not straightforwardly articulated in the *gemara*.

## Ben Yomo Principle

### Torah's Ben Yomo Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם — מוּתָּר, גִּיעוּלֵי גוֹיִם דְּאָסַר רַחֲמָנָא הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא קְדֵירָה בַּת יוֹמָאּ, דְּלָאו נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who says** that that if a forbidden substance **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture then the mixture is **permitted,** with regard to utensils of **gentiles** that require **purging, which the Merciful One renders forbidden** until they are purged, **how can you find these** circumstances when it is actually prohibited to use the utensils until they are purged? Since utensils impart a detrimental flavor to food that is cooked in them, there seems to be no reason not to use utensils of gentiles without purging them.
>
> **Rav Ḥiyya, son of Rav Huna, says: The Torah renders forbidden only a pot** that was used for cooking **that same day** by a gentile, **as** in such a case, **it does not impart flavor** to food cooked in it **to** the **detriment** of the mixture.

With the *noten ta'am lifgam* principle established as the basis for permitting *bediavad* use, a pointed question arises: if we rule that *noten ta'am lifgam mutar*, then when exactly did the Torah forbid non-Jewish vessels in the first place? If any vessel, by the time it is purchased and used, will already have deteriorated taste, what was the prohibition of *klei Midyan* all about? Rabbi Chiya son of Rav Huna answers: *lo asrah Torah ela kdeira bat yoma* — the Torah forbade it only in the case of a vessel that was used on that very day, a *ben yomo* vessel, *delav noten ta'am lifgam hu* — because within the first day the absorbed taste is still good and has not yet degraded.

### Bad Taste Rationale

This clarifies the *baraita* contradiction as well: the *baraita* that forbids the food *bediavad* is dealing specifically with a situation where someone used the vessel on the same day it had been used by a non-Jew — within the period in which the taste remains *taam l'shvach*, pleasantly detectable. The *baraita* that permits it is dealing with use after that period has elapsed.

The opposing view — that *noten ta'am lifgam assur* — holds that even on the first day the taste in a non-Jewish vessel is already degraded: *kdeira bat yoma nami mifgam pagma*. If that is so, then the Torah's prohibition of *klei Midyan* must apply even to a bad taste, meaning that school holds the Torah prohibits *noten ta'am lifgam* categorically.

### Rashi's Night Definition

Rashi's formulation of *ben yomo* is notable and deserves attention. Rashi writes: *bat yoma shebishlu bah ovdei kochavim hayom aval bishlu bah etmol kvar heifig ta'amo belinat laylah* — a *ben yomo* vessel is one in which the non-Jew cooked today, but if they cooked yesterday, the taste has already dissipated through the passage of the night. On this reading, it is not a strict 24-hour period that determines *ben yomo* status, but rather whether an intervening night has passed.

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam in a way that lends some support to Rashi's position, but there is another view that *ben yomo* means precisely 24 hours. Conceptually, the 24-hour reading is easier to understand: after a certain passage of time the absorbed taste changes in character. What is harder to understand is what specifically the passage of night accomplishes and why it should matter whether a vessel was used yesterday or today if fewer than 24 hours have elapsed. One way Tosafot attempts to explain the night-based view is that people did not cook at night, though how that fact causes the taste to deteriorate is not fully clear. Another possible approach is that *ben yomo* status is less about the physical reality of the taste and more about the vessel's halachic identity — a vessel used the same day has a much stronger character as an active *treif* vessel than one that belongs to a prior day's history. The standard practical definition remains 24 hours.

### Eino Ben Yomo Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לִישְׁתְּרֵי! גְּזֵירָה קְדֵירָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בַּת יוֹמָאּ מִשּׁוּם קְדֵירָה בַּת יוֹמָאּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **from that** point **onward** the pot **should be permitted,** as on the following day the taste of the non-kosher food imparted by the pot is already to the detriment of the food. The Gemara answers: There is a rabbinic **decree** that prohibits use of **a pot that** was **not** used by a gentile **that same day, due to** concern that one will use **a pot** used by a gentile **that same day.**

If the Torah only forbade *ben yomo* vessels, one might ask why we cannot simply use a vessel once it is *eino ben yomo* — older than 24 hours — without any kashering at all. The *gemara* answers: *gzeirah* — it is a rabbinic decree. Using an *eino ben yomo* vessel is forbidden *d'rabbanan*, a rabbinic fence against coming to use a *ben yomo* vessel: *gzeirah kdeira she'eina bat yoma mishum kdeira bat yoma*.

### Pottery Vessels

Some *rishonim* raised the question of whether at least *bediavad* it should be permissible to use pottery vessels — *klei cheres* — that are *eino ben yomo*, since pottery cannot be kashered at all and must be destroyed. Perhaps, they reasoned, the *bediavad* situation would justify leniency. However, the established text of the *gemara* is clear that pottery vessels cannot be used even when *eino ben yomo* and must be destroyed. The reason is not merely the rabbinic decree. Tosafot's framing is that the pottery vessel absorbed actual *treif* — it cooked real forbidden food. It therefore possesses the status of a genuine *treif* vessel, and the rabbinic decree against using it stands even at significant cost to the owner. The *gzeirah* is not relaxed for pottery.

### Post-Kasher Taste

Yet once food has been cooked in such a vessel *bediavad*, Tosafot explains why the food itself is permitted: when the taste finally exited the vessel into the food, the vessel was already *eino ben yomo* and the taste was therefore already *lifgam* — degraded. The vessel absorbed the *treif* taste when it was real and good, but the food only ever received the bad taste. That is why the food is permitted *bediavad* while the vessel itself retains its forbidden status. It is not a general *bediavad* leniency — if it were, we would apply it to pottery vessels themselves — but rather a consequence of the specific moment at which the taste transferred to the food.

## Spits/Grates Debate

### Libun vs Hagala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַב עַמְרָם לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, תְּנַן: הַשַּׁפּוּדִין וְהָאַסְכָּלָא — מְלַבְּנָן בָּאוּר, וְהָתַנְיָא גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים: הַשַּׁפּוּד וְהָאַסְכָּלָא — מַגְעִילָן בְּחַמִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram raises a contradiction before Rav Sheshet: We learned** in the mishna: With regard to **the spits and the grill, one must heat them until white-hot in the fire. But it is taught** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 97a) **with regard to sacrificial** meat: **The spit and the grill** that were used to roast sacrificial meat may not be used again after the time for eating that particular offering has passed, as the leftover taste of the offering in these utensils is forbidden, unless **one purges them in hot water.** Apparently, heating them until white-hot is unnecessary.

Rav Amram raised a contradiction to Rav Sheshes. Our *mishnah* states that a spit and a grate — *hashapud v'ha'askala* — used by non-Jews must be subjected to *libun*, white-hot heating, in order to be kashered. Yet a *mishnah* in Zevachim, dealing with *kodashim*, states that a spit and a grate used for sacrificial meat require only *hagala* — immersion in boiling water — to be kashered after the meat left in them becomes *nosar* (forbidden leftover sacrifice). Why should the method differ?

### Heteira vs Issura

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַמְרָם בְּרִי, מָה עִנְיַן קָדָשִׁים אֵצֶל גִּיעוּלֵי גוֹיִם? הָכָא הֶיתֵּירָא בָּלַע, הָתָם אִיסּוּרָא בָּלַע.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: Amram, my son,** what has the **matter** of **sacrificial** meat to do **with** vessels of **gentiles** that require **purging? Here,** in the case of sacrificial meat, the utensils **absorbed a permitted** food that subsequently became forbidden, and therefore purging in hot water is sufficient. **There,** in the case of utensils acquired from gentiles, the utensils **absorbed a forbidden** food, and so they must be heated until white-hot.

Rav Sheshes answered with a fundamental distinction: *mai inyan kodashim etzel ovdei kochavim?* — what comparison is there between sacrificial vessels and non-Jewish vessels? By *kodashim*, the vessel absorbed permitted, indeed sacred, food — *heteira bala*. By non-Jewish cooking, the vessel absorbed forbidden food — *issura bala*, *chazer*. This distinction, Rav Sheshes argues, justifies different kashering requirements. In fact, this is the final *halachah*: when something absorbed *heteira* — whether through cooking with *chametz* before Pesach, or through mixing *milchigs* and *fleishigs* — *hagala* suffices even for a vessel used directly on the fire. Only when the vessel absorbed actual *issur* directly is *libun* required.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: סוֹף סוֹף כִּי קָא פָלֵיט אִיסּוּרָא קָא פָלֵיט, אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי הַגְעָלָה? נָמֵי שְׁטִיפָה וּמְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** disagreed, **saying: Ultimately,** even in the case of sacrificial meat, **when it emits** flavor, **it emits a forbidden** flavor, so what difference does it make that it was permitted when it was absorbed? **Rather, Rava said: What** is meant by the **purging** stated with regard to sacrificial meat? It means that besides heating it until white-hot, **rinsing and scouring** it are **also** required, as is the *halakha* with regard to any utensil used with sacrificial meat, as the verse states: "It shall be scoured and rinsed in water" (Leviticus 6:21).

Rava initially challenged this distinction: *sof sof ki ka palit issura ka palit* — ultimately, when the taste is expelled, it is *nosar* taste that comes out. Since *nosar* is forbidden, the vessel is releasing *issur* at the moment of kashering, and therefore the kashering should need to be equally vigorous as for *treif* absorption. Rava further notes that it is indeed a *chiddush* that *chatas* taste, once it has become *nosar*, continues to have that forbidden status even when it is no longer physically present in the vessel but merely absorbed within the metal of the grate.

### Hagala Definition

Rava therefore proposed a different resolution: *mai hagala nami merika v'shtifa* — the word *hagala* in the *mishnah* in Zevachim does not refer to kashering through boiling water at all. Rather, it refers to *merika u'shtifa* — the ritual rinsing and washing required by the Torah after cooking a *korban* in a vessel, as the verse states *u'murak v'shutaf ba-mayim*. On this reading, the Zevachim *mishnah* is not describing a kashering procedure at all; it is describing a separate ritual obligation. The actual kashering of the spits and grates would still require *libun*, white-hot fire.

### Abaye Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בְּצוֹנֵן, הַגְעָלָה בְּחַמִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Are** rinsing and scouring **comparable** to purging? **Scouring and rinsing** are done **in cold** water, whereas **purging** is done **in hot** water. Therefore, this is not a valid interpretation of the term purging.

Abaye rejected this interpretation directly: *mi dami?* — *merika v'shtifa b'tzinuni*, Abaye holds that *merika* and *shtifa* refer to cold rinsing, inside and outside the vessel. *Hagala b'chamimin* — *hagala* means hot water. The two terms are simply not interchangeable. The word *hagala* cannot be stretched to mean cold rinsing.

### Both Sources Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ — תְּנָא הָכָא לִיבּוּן, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְהַגְעָלָה; תְּנָא הָתָם הַגְעָלָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְלִיבּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the Mishna employs the style of: **Its counterpart reveals about it** (see Job 36:33), as follows: The Mishna **taught here** that the spit and the grill require **heating until white-hot, and the same is true of purging,** which is also required. The Mishna **taught there** that **purging** the spit and the grill is necessary, **and the same is true of heating until white-hot.**

Abaye therefore offered his own resolution, citing the verse in Iyov: *yagid alav re'o* — let its companion teach about it. Each source teaches one half of the complete *halachah*. The *mishnah* here teaches *libun*, and the same applies to *hagala* — *hu hadin l'hagala*. The Zevachim *mishnah* teaches *hagala*, and the same applies to *libun* — *hu hadin l'libun*. Read together, they establish that both *hagala* and *libun* are required for direct-fire vessels. Each source teaches half and the two complete one another.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, לִתְנִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ בַּחֲדָא, וְלִיתְנֵי חֲדָא בְּאִידַּךְ אַחֲרִיתִי, וְלֵימָא: "יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ"!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so, let** the Mishna **teach all of** these requirements **in one** of the places, **and let it teach** only **one** of them **in the other, and** then **let us say** that the Mishna employs the style of: **Its counterpart reveals about it.** The principle that one mishna supplements the other can apply when all of the information is stated in one of the two places, but not when each has only part of it.

Rava pushed back on Abaye's resolution with a pointed structural objection: *ei hachi lisninhu l'chulhu b'chada* — if both requirements are real and necessary, why not state them together in one place? State the full *halachah* — both *hagala* and *libun* — in one context, and then the other *mishnah* need mention only one, which we would understand by reference to the fuller source. What is deeply problematic is if each *mishnah* states only its half of the *halachah* and neither source ever presents the complete requirement. Rava extends this criticism to both possible readings of Abaye: whether Abaye means that both *hagala* and *libun* are required together, or that either one independently suffices, the flaw remains — the complete *halachah* should appear in at least one place, and one could then point to the other for the partial statement.

## Daily Kashering

### Daily Hagala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: קָדָשִׁים הַיְינוּ טַעְמַיְיהוּ, כִּדְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל יוֹם וָיוֹם נַעֲשֶׂה גִּיעוּל לַחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that with regard to **sacrificial** meat, **this is** the **reason** that **its** utensils do not require heating until white-hot: It is **in accordance with that which Rav Naḥman** says that **Rabba bar Avuh says, as he says: Each and every day constitutes purging for the other** day, i.e., the previous day. Since the designated time for eating the meat of a peace-offering ends the day after it is sacrificed, using the utensils every day for an offering sacrificed on that day guarantees that the taste of every offering is expelled from the utensils before they become forbidden, by using them with fire on the following day. Therefore the utensils do not require special heating until white-hot in fire in order to become permitted for subsequent use.

Rava ultimately advances a different solution, grounded in a teaching of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha cited in Zevachim: *kol yom v'yom na'aseh gi'ul l'chavero* — every day's cooking constitutes a *gi'ul*, a kashering, for the previous day. The logic is straightforward: if one wishes to perform *hagala* on a *fleishig* pot used on Sunday, one would boil water in it. If on Monday one cooks vegetable soup in that same pot, the boiling vegetable soup accomplishes the same result. The *poskim* discuss whether this holds in practice — there are concerns about whether the water reaches a full rolling boil and other practical considerations — but the fundamental principle is sound.

In the context of the *Beis Hamikdash*, this means that the continuous cycle of cooking *korbanos* on the grates and spits serves as a constant kashering. Each day's cooking kashers the residue of the previous day. That is why the Zevachim *mishnah* requires only *hagala* and makes no mention of *libun*: the vessel is being kashered daily by the cooking itself.

### Shelamim Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח שְׁלָמִים, דְּכֵיוָן דִּלְשְׁנֵי יָמִים מִיתְאַכְלִי, מִקַּמֵּי דְּנִיהְוֵי נוֹתָר קָא הָוֵי גִּיעוּל. אֶלָּא חַטָּאת, כֵּיוָן דִּלְיוֹם וְלַיְלָה מִיתְאַכְלָא, כִּי מְבַשֵּׁל בַּהּ הָאִידָּנָא חַטָּאת — הָוֵי נוֹתָר, כִּי הָדַר מְבַשֵּׁל בַּהּ לִמְחַר אוֹ שְׁלָמִים אוֹ חַטָּאת, קָא פָלֵיט נוֹתָר דְּחַטָּאת דְּהָאִידָּנָא בְּחַטָּאת וּשְׁלָמִים דְּלִמְחַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: This **works out well** with regard to **peace-offerings, as, since** their meat **may be eaten over two days, purging is** achieved **before they become leftover** sacrificial meat, which is forbidden. **But** with regard to **a sin-offering, since** its meat **may be eaten** only **over** the course of **a day and a night, when one cooks** the meat of **a sin-offering with** the utensil **now, it becomes leftover** the next morning. And **when one cooks with it again the next day, whether** the meat of **a peace-offering or a sin-offering,** the utensil **expels the leftover** taste **of the sin-offering** sacrificed **now into** the meat of **the sin-offering or peace-offering** sacrificed **the next day,** and it should be forbidden.

The *gemara* works through how this principle operates in practice. Consider a *shelamim* offering cooked on Sunday. A *shelamim* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night — Sunday, Sunday night, and Monday. If on Monday one cooks another *shelamim* on the same grate, that cooking constitutes *hagala* for Sunday's *shelamim* while it is still *heter*: Sunday's *shelamim* has not yet become *nosar*. The Monday *shelamim* absorbs the taste of Sunday's *shelamim*, but since that taste is still *heter* at the moment of cooking, it presents no problem. The grate is kashered, and the Monday *shelamim* is not compromised.

### Chatas Complexity

A *chatas* offering is more complex, since it may be eaten only for a day and the following night — Sunday and Sunday night only. If one cooks another *korban* on the grate on Monday, Monday's cooking would expel Sunday's *chatas* taste, but by Monday that taste is already *nosar* — forbidden. The expelled taste of Sunday's *chatas* would thus contaminate whatever is cooked on Monday.

### Daily Alternation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּכִי מְבַשֵּׁל בַּהּ חַטָּאת הָאִידָּנָא, הָדַר מְבַשֵּׁל בַּהּ הָאִידָּנָא שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **It is not necessary** to purge the utensil by heating it until white-hot even if it was used for the meat of a sin-offering; it is possible to avoid such a requirement, **as when one cooks a sin-offering with** the utensil **now,** he can **then cook** the meat of **a peace-offering with** the same utensil **now,** i.e., on the same day, and the taste of the sin-offering is consequently expelled from the utensil on that same day.

The *gemara* proposes a specific cooking order that maintains the integrity of the system. Each day one must cook first a *chatas* and then, on the same day, a *shelamim*. The sequence works as follows. On Sunday, one first cooks a *chatas*, then immediately cooks a *shelamim* on the same grate. The *shelamim* cooking kashers the grate from Sunday's *chatas* while it is still *heter* — while Sunday's *chatas* may still be eaten. The meat of that *shelamim*, however, has now absorbed *chatas* taste, and since *chatas* must be consumed by Sunday night, the *shelamim* that absorbed it must also be finished by Sunday night. The grate now holds only Sunday's *shelamim* taste.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּחַטָּאת דְּלִמְחַר, וּשְׁלָמִים דְּאֶתְמוֹל, בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָא שָׁלֵים זִמְנַיְיהוּ, וַהֲדַר מְבַשֵּׁל שְׁלָמִים דְּלִמְחַר.
>
> **Translation:** Then, when the taste of the peace-offering is absorbed, it creates a situation **where the** designated **times for** eating the meat of **the sin-offering of the next day and** the meat of **the peace-offering of the previous day are complete simultaneously.** It is therefore possible to use the utensil for cooking the meat of a sin-offering the next day without having to purge it from the taste of the previous day's offerings. **And one can then cook** the meat of **a peace-offering** with the utensil **the next day,** thereby expelling from it the taste of the sin-offering of that day, and repeat this practice day after day.

On Monday, one cooks a *chatas*, which absorbs Sunday's *shelamim* taste from the grate. Sunday's *shelamim* may be eaten through Monday day, so at the moment of Monday's cooking, that taste is still *heter*. Monday's *chatas*, containing Sunday's *shelamim* taste, can be eaten through Monday, since both its own time and the *shelamim* taste's time expire together. Then on Monday, one again cooks a *shelamim*, kashering the grate from Monday's *chatas*, and the cycle repeats.

The Ritva, citing Tosafot, raises an important question about this arrangement: by cooking a *chatas* and then a *shelamim* on the same day, one is effectively restricting the time during which the *shelamim*'s meat can be eaten, since it has absorbed *chatas* taste. Under normal circumstances, a *shelamim* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night; once it absorbs *chatas* taste, its permissible eating period is shortened. Reducing the *zman achilah* of a *korban* — *mema'it zman achilah* — is itself ordinarily prohibited. The *gemara* does not resolve this concern here, but it stands as a significant question on the whole construction.

### Hagala Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַגְעָלָה נָמֵי לָא לִיבְעֵי! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **If so,** if each day the utensil expels the taste of the sacrificial meat that has not become leftover, then **purging** it with boiling water **is also unnecessary.** Why, then, does the mishna require purging with boiling water? The Gemara comments: This poses **a difficulty** to Rava's explanation.

Given Rava's answer that the daily cooking itself performs continuous *hagala*, the *gemara* raises the obvious follow-up: *ai hachi hagala nami lo libaei* — if daily cooking already constitutes *hagala*, why does the Zevachim *mishnah* mention *hagala* at all? It should simply say that regular use renders explicit kashering unnecessary. The *gemara* concedes: *kashya* — this is indeed difficult. The *gemara* does not resolve it with a clean answer, leaving the question open.

### Ease of Extraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הַאי קָרִיד, הַאי לָא קָרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that there is another resolution to the contradiction between *mishnayot*: In **this** case of a gentile's spit and grill, because it is a utensil that is not in continual use, it **becomes crusty** and must be heated until white-hot; but **that** utensil used for sacrificial meat is in continual use, so it **does not become crusty.**

Rav Pappa offers one response: *hai karid v'hai lo karid* — one vessel adheres more readily and the other less so. Because the Temple vessels were used in constant daily cooking, the absorbed taste is less deeply embedded — it remains closer to the surface, more easily expelled by *hagala*. A vessel used less frequently would require more vigorous kashering to extract the same taste. This explains why *hagala* suffices for Temple vessels even when it might not for other contexts. The explanation is acknowledged to be somewhat difficult, but it is the position offered.

### Heteira Bala Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כְּדַאֲמַרַן מֵעִיקָּרָא, הָכָא הֶתֵּירָא בָּלַע, הָכָא אִיסּוּרָא בָּלַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** the contradiction should be resolved **as we said from the outset,** as explained by Rav Sheshet: **There,** in the case of sacrificial meat, the utensils **absorbed a permitted** food that subsequently became forbidden, and therefore purging is sufficient. **Here,** in the case of utensils acquired from gentiles, the utensils **absorbed a forbidden** food, and so they must be heated until white-hot.

Rav Ashi provides the most satisfying resolution: *le'olam kede'amran mei'ikara* — really the original answer stands. *Hacha heteira bala, hacha issura bala* — by *kodashim* the vessel absorbed *heter*, by non-Jewish vessels it absorbed *issur*. That distinction justifies the difference in required kashering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא פָלֵיט אִיסּוּרָא קָא פָלֵיט, בְּעִידָּנָא דְּקָא פָלֵיט לָא אִיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **that which** poses **a difficulty for you, that at the time** that the utensil **expels** flavor, **it expels** the flavor of **a forbidden** food, the answer is that **at the time it expels** flavor, the **forbidden** food **is nonexistent in its substantive form.** Since the forbidden substance expelled from the utensil is not the forbidden food itself but only its flavor, it is treated leniently, and therefore the fact that it was permitted at the time it was absorbed in the utensil is taken into account.

One might object, as the *gemara* itself anticipates, that *be'idna dekapalit issura kapalit* — at the moment the taste is expelled during kashering, it is *nosar* taste that comes out, and *nosar* is *issur*. Should not *issur* exiting require equally vigorous kashering? Rav Ashi responds: *be'idna dekaplit lav issura be'einei* — when the taste comes out, it is not *issur be'ein*, not a physically present forbidden substance. It is only the residual taste of something that became *nosar*, and since it is not *be'ein*, it does not require the same degree of hechsher.

The *gemara* does not fully explain why *heteira bala* requires less, or why the *be'ein* distinction should matter mechanically. One conceptual approach is that kashering is less a physical process of extraction and more a matter of identity. A vessel that absorbed *issur* directly on fire acquires the identity of a *treif* grate. The only way to reverse that identity is through an equal and opposite process — *k'bolo kach polto*. But a vessel that absorbed *heter*, even if that *heter* subsequently became *nosar* within the metal, never acquired the identity of a *treif* vessel in the first place. Its identity was always one of permitted food. To reset that identity does not require an equal and opposite process; any meaningful procedure suffices. This framework also explains why it matters whether something is *be'ein* or not — since the vessel only ever carried a *heter* identity, the subsequent transition of that absorbed taste to *issur* status inside the metal does not convert the vessel's identity into that of a *treif* vessel.

The practical *halachah* that emerges from this sugya is significant: *heteira bala* — absorbed permitted taste — does not require *libun* even for vessels used directly on a fire. *Hagala* suffices. This applies, for example, to kashering chametz vessels before Pesach, or to separating *milchig* and *fleishig* vessels — *hagala* is always sufficient unless actual *issur* was absorbed.

## Hagala Methods

### Libun Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה מְלַבְּנָן? אָמַר רַבִּי מִנִּי: עַד שֶׁתַּשִּׁיר קְלִיפָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** § Returning to the mishna, the Gemara asks: **And how much does one heat** utensils to make **them white-hot? Rabbi Mani says: Until they shed their outer layer.**

Before concluding the sugya, the *gemara* turns to practical questions of kashering procedure. Rav Mani addresses the required extent of *libun*: *ad shetashir klipatan* — until the outer layer peels or flakes off, indicating that the vessel has reached the degree of white-hot heat required. This is what is referred to as *libun chamur* — the more intense form of *libun*. There is a lesser form, *libun kal*, which is required where *heteira bala* applies: a level of heat sufficient to ignite a piece of straw or paper tissue placed on the vessel's surface, but not necessarily reaching the point where the outer shell peels.

### Small Pot Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיצַד מַגְעִילָן? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יוֹרָה קְטַנָּה בְּתוֹךְ יוֹרָה גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And how does one purge** utensils with boiling water? **Rav Huna says:** One immerses **a small kettle inside a large kettle** of boiling water.

Rav Huna addresses the procedure for *hagala*: *yora ketana betoch yora gedola* — one immerses a small pot within a large pot filled with boiling water. This is the standard method: the smaller vessel must be fully submerged in boiling water in order for the *hagala* to be effective.

### Large Pot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרָה גְּדוֹלָה, מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא דּוּדָא דַּהֲוַאי בֵּי רַב עֲקַבְיָה, אַהְדַּר לַהּ גְּדָנְפָא דְּלַיְשָׁא אַפּוּמַּאּ, וּמַלְּיַוהּ מַיָּא וְאַרְתְּחַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does one do with **a large kettle?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer from an incident **involving a certain cauldron that was** in **the house of Rav Akavya** and required purging. **He surrounded it** with **a rim [*gedanfa*] of dough around its rim, and filled it with water and boiled it,** so that the water boiled along its rim.

The obvious question then arises: *yora gedola mai* — what does one do with a large pot that cannot fit inside another vessel?

The *gemara* relates that there was a large pot in the house of Rav Akiva. The solution employed was *ahadru lei gadanpa delisha apuma* — a rim was fashioned out of dough and attached to the mouth of the pot. The pot was then filled with water all the way to the top and beyond, and the water was brought to a boil. The mechanism here addresses a specific concern: when one cooks *treif* contents in a large pot, the boiling liquid can splatter and overflow, reaching the outer walls of the pot. To truly kasher the vessel, one must replicate that process — water must reach and overflow the outer walls as well. Simply boiling water inside the pot to a rolling boil will produce some splashing, but may not fully replicate the original splattering that affected the outer surface. By constructing a dough rim extension, one raises the effective walls of the pot and fills it with a correspondingly greater volume of water. When this water boils and overflows, it runs completely down the outer walls of the pot, fully replicating the original condition.

### Belo Kach Polto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן חַכִּים לְמֶעְבַּד כִּי הָא מִילְּתָא, אִי לָאו רַב עֲקַבְיָה דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּא הוּא? קָסָבַר: כְּבוֹלְעוֹ כָּךְ פּוֹלְטוֹ — מָה בּוֹלְעוֹ בְּנִצוֹצוֹת, אַף פּוֹלְטוֹ בְּנִצוֹצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Who** would be **clever** enough **to perform such an action if not Rav Akavya, as he is a great man. He maintains** that **as it absorbs it so it expels it; just as** the rim **absorbs** the forbidden substance **by small drops** of it that reach the rim, **so too it expels** the forbidden substance **by small drops** of boiling water that reach the rim.

Rava's comment on this solution is striking: *man chakim lemiavad ki hai milta ela Rabi Akiva, degavra raba hu* — who but Rav Akiva, a great man, would have thought of this? *Kasavar kebolo kach polto* — he grasped the principle of *k'bolo kach polto*, as it absorbed so it expels. *Ma bolo benitzotzos af polto benitzotzos* — just as the absorption occurred through the spattering of overflowing liquid, so too the expulsion must occur through the spattering of overflowing liquid. The *chiddush* here is twofold. First, the creative engineering of the dough rim. But second, and more conceptually significant, Rav Akiva recognized that there is no inherent need to immerse the outer wall of the pot in boiling water as such. The outer wall was not affected by immersion but by splatter. Therefore, what is needed is not that the outer wall be covered with water, but that boiling water run over it — replicating the original splatter. The dough rim accomplishes exactly this.

In contemporary practice, the solution often involves placing a boiling-hot stone or rock into a pot filled to the brim with water, causing the water to overflow without cooling. There are practical difficulties in doing this properly, and *poskim* address the details of how to ensure the water remains at a boil throughout.

## Knife Kashering

### Knife Friction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסַּכִּין — שָׁפָהּ וְהִיא טְהוֹרָה. אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: וְנוֹעֲצָהּ עֲשָׂרָה פְּעָמִים בַּקַּרְקַע. אֲמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וּבְקַרְקַע שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲבוּדָה. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: וּבְסַכִּין יָפָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ גּוּמּוֹת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: סַכִּין יָפָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ גּוּמּוֹת נוֹעֲצָהּ עֲשָׂרָה פְּעָמִים בְּקַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **the knife, one must polish it and it is** rendered **pure. Rav Ukva bar Ḥama says: And one must thrust it ten times into the ground. Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says: And** this must be done **in untilled earth,** i.e., hard earth. **Rav Kahana says: And** this applies **to a good knife that does not have notches,** so that the entire surface of the knife is scraped against the ground. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a good knife that does not have notches, one can thrust it ten times into the ground.**

The *gemara* turns next to the kashering of a knife. The ruling is that rubbing the knife with a rough material — *shafafa* — suffices to clean it for use, given that knife usage typically involves cold cutting and the knife does not genuinely absorb taste through cold contact. The friction removes whatever residue adheres to the surface.

### Ground Stabbing

Rav Ukva bar Chama adds that one may insert the knife ten times into hard ground — *no'atza esser pa'amim bakarka*. The firm resistance of the soil functions equivalently to rubbing with a coarse cloth; the friction against the blade's surface dislodges adhering residue.

### Good Knife

Rav Kahana adds an important qualification: *besakin yafa she'ein ba gumot* — this method applies only to a knife with a smooth, unblemished flat surface. If the flat of the blade contains pockmarks, crevices, or surface imperfections, rubbing and stabbing alone will not dislodge material lodged within those recesses. Such material requires more targeted cleaning, as mere surface friction cannot reach inside small cavities.

### Cold Use Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לֶאֱכוֹל בָּהּ צוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** This is sufficient **for** the purpose of **eating cold** food **with it.**

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua adds the critical limitation: *le'echol ba tzonen* — this entire procedure cleans the knife only for use with cold foods. The friction and ground-stabbing remove material adhering to the surface, but they do not address anything absorbed into the metal of the blade itself through heat. If a knife was used to cut hot *treif* meat and one wishes to use it with hot food, surface cleaning is entirely insufficient. Heat kashering would be required to extract what the metal absorbed. This method is thus specifically for the scenario where the knife's contact with *treif* was through cold cutting, meaning nothing penetrated the metal in the first place.

### Bati Bar Tuvia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּמָר יְהוּדָה וּבָאטִי בַּר טוֹבִי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּשַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא, אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ אֶתְרוֹגָא. פְּסַק אֲכַל, פְּסַק וְהַב לֵיהּ לְבָאטִי בַּר טוֹבִי, הֲדַר דָּצַהּ עַשְׂרָה זִימְנֵי בְּאַרְעָא, פְּסַק הַב לֵיהּ לְמָר יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ בָּאטִי בַּר טוֹבִי: וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא לָאו בַּר יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָר קִים לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ, וּמָר לָא קִים לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that** incident **involving Mar Yehuda,** an important personage of the house of the Exilarch, **and Bati bar Tuvi,** a wealthy man, who **were sitting before King Shapur,** the king of Persia. The king's servants **brought an *etrog* before them.** The king **cut** a slice and **ate** it, and then he **cut** a slice **and gave** it **to Bati bar Tuvi. He then stuck** the knife **ten times in the ground, cut** a slice, and **gave** it **to Mar Yehuda. Bati bar Tuvi said to him: And is that man,** referring to himself, **not Jewish?** King Shapur **said to him: I am certain of** that **master,** Mar Yehuda, that he is meticulous about *halakha*; **but I am not certain of** that **master,** referring to Bati bar Tuvi, that he is meticulous in this regard.

The *gemara* concludes knife kashering with a narrative that illustrates the principle in action. Mar Yehuda and Bati bar Tuvia were seated before Shavor Malka, the Persian king. An etrog was brought before them. The king cut a piece, ate it himself, then cut another piece and gave it to Bati bar Tuvia without any preparation. He then stabbed the knife ten times in the ground and cut a piece for Mar Yehuda. He was aware that Mar Yehuda would not eat food cut with a knife that had not been cleaned, and so he performed the ten-stab cleaning before cutting for him. It is worth noting that the etrog here does not appear to be treated as a *davar charif* — a sharp food that would draw absorbed taste from the knife — since the surface cleaning was deemed sufficient.

Bati bar Tuvia was taken aback: *lav bar Yisrael hu* — am I not Jewish? Why was the knife cleaned before cutting for Mar Yehuda but not before cutting for me? The king responded: *Mar kim li b'gaveih, Mar lo kim li b'gaveih* — I know that this Mar Yehuda is careful about these matters; about you, I am not certain. The fact that Bati bar Tuvia had accepted the first piece without objection served as some evidence of his position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִידְּכַר מַאי עֲבַדְתְּ בְּאוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that King Shapur **said to him: Remember what you did last night.** The Persian practice was to present a woman to each guest, with whom he would engage in intercourse. Mar Yehuda did not accept the woman who was sent to him, but Bati bar Tuvi did, and therefore he was not assumed to be meticulous with regard to eating kosher food.

A second version of the exchange records the king as saying: *idkar mai avadt be'orta* — remember what you did last night? Rashi explains that the Persian practice at such royal gatherings was to send female companions to the guests overnight. Mar Yehuda refused; Bati bar Tuvia accepted. The king's point was that he had drawn his own conclusions about Bati bar Tuvia's level of religious carefulness and therefore did not feel the need to be scrupulous on his behalf.

## Masechet Conclusion

### Final Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָדְרָן עֲלָךְ הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֵת הַפּוֹעֵל, וּסְלִיקָא לַהּ מַסֶּכֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you chapter "One who rents a worker" and this is the end of Tractate Avodah Zarah

This story carries resonances beyond the immediate *halachah* of knife cleaning. The willingness of a non-Jewish king to be sensitive to the *kashrus* needs of his Jewish guests, and indeed to take it upon himself to perform the requisite cleaning without being asked, presents a striking image. Conversely, the story raises the uncomfortable question — one the *gemara* takes seriously — of the Jewish guest who did not require that accommodation.

### Thematic Arc

There is a fitting coherence to the way *Masechet Avodah Zarah* closes. A *masechet* that opens with the fraught and complex relationship between Israel and the surrounding non-Jewish world — its hostility, its theological challenges, the dangers of too close an engagement — ends with questions of *kashrus* and the intimate daily interactions that define that relationship at the practical level. The final narrative captures this complexity in miniature: a non-Jewish king more attentive to Jewish law than one of the Jewish guests at the same table. The theme of perception, compartmentalization, and the non-Jewish world's understanding of Jewish practice — which appeared not long before in discussions about trusting a non-Jew not to tamper with wine — resurfaces here from a different angle. And with this, we conclude *Masechet Avodah Zarah*. *Hadran alach Hasocher Et Hapoel v'silka lach Masechet Avodah Zarah*.
