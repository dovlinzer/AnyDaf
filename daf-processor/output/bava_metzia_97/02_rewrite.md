# Bava Metzia 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Borrower Exemptions

### Opening & Context

The daf picks up near the top of 97a, continuing the discussion that has been unfolding across the previous pages. We have been transitioning through two distinct exemptions available to a *shoel*, a borrower. The first is *baalav imo* — that the owner of the borrowed object was simultaneously hired or asked to work alongside the borrower, which provides a full exemption from liability. The second is *meita machmat melachah* — that the animal or object died or broke as a direct result of normal, intended use.

### Meita Machmat Melachah

Two explanations are offered for why *meita machmat melachah* exempts the *shoel*. The first is that it actually reflects a degree of *pshia* on the part of the *mashil*, the one who lent the object or animal. If the animal cannot sustain a normal workload, the owner should not have lent it in the first place; the liability therefore reverts to the owner for having effectively guaranteed that the animal or object could withstand that level of use. The second explanation treats it as ordinary wear and tear — something entirely to be expected when one lends an item for use. If it breaks or dies as a result of that expected use, the outcome was foreseeable and the owner implicitly accepted it.

### Rav's Axe Ruling

Turning briefly to the case of Rav's ruling on the broken axe: Rav at first appeared to *pasken lifnim mishurat hadin*, beyond the strict letter of the law. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged this — *dina hachi?* Is that really the law? — and it seems the *halachah* follows them. As Rashi, drawing on Bava Kamma, explains, even where the *shoel* acted negligently and the breakage was not *meita machmat melachah*, he is liable only for the partial loss in value, not the full worth of the axe, because *bealim metaplim bineveilah* — the owner retains the broken object — and that is why Rav's initial ruling seemed difficult.

### Rav Pappa Bucket

The Gemara continues on the third line of 97a. A person borrowed a bucket from his friend; it broke. He came before Rav Pappa. Rav Pappa told him: bring witnesses that you did not misuse it — *ayiti sahadi dlo shinit bah* — and you will be exempt. This extends the principle of *meita machmat melachah* to inanimate objects. What is notable here is that the burden of proof falls squarely on the *shoel*. Given the *shoel*'s elevated level of liability, the default assumption when something breaks is negligence. Only once the *shoel* demonstrates proper use does the exemption apply.

### Shvua & Shoel

One might ask: why must the *shoel* bring witnesses? Could he not simply exempt himself through *shvuat hashomrim* — the oath that *shomrim* typically take, swearing that they were not negligent, did not misappropriate the object, and so on? In most *shomrim* cases, such an oath would suffice. Most *Rishonim* do in fact hold that a *shoel* may take the *shvua*, and the specific cases discussed here simply happen to involve witnesses as the available avenue of proof, with the *shvua* as an alternative where witnesses are unavailable. However, the *Ritva* cites an opinion — ultimately rejected — that a *shoel* differs fundamentally from other categories of *shomrim*. Given the *shoel*'s uniquely high level of liability, perhaps *shvuat hashomrim* is simply not available to him as a vehicle for exemption. While the majority of *Rishonim* do not accept this position, it illuminates the degree to which the *shoel* occupies a distinct and more demanding position within the *shomrim* framework.

## Cat Case

### Mice Attack Cat

The Gemara now introduces what is perhaps the most colorful case on the page. *Hahu gavra disha'al shunra michavreih* — a man borrowed a cat from his friend. This requires a brief note: in the world of *Chazal*, a cat is not a pet but a working animal whose job is to catch mice. But here the situation went badly wrong — *chavru aleih achbarei vekatluha* — the mice ganged up on the cat and killed it. The infestation was so severe that the sheer number of mice was able to overpower a single cat.

### Rav Ashi Question

*Yativ Rav Ashi vekamibaia leih ki hai gavna mai* — Rav Ashi sat and posed the question: how should one rule in such a case? Does it qualify as *meita machmat melachah*? After all, the cat was doing precisely what it was borrowed to do — catching mice. Yet one could argue that the *shoel* was a *poshea* in a sense, since the infestation was so extreme that a single cat was clearly insufficient; perhaps he should have arranged for multiple cats. So is this case comparable to *meita machmat melachah* or not?

Rav Mordechai answered on behalf of Rav Ashi, citing a tradition in the name of Avimi from Agronya, who reported in the name of Rava: *gavra denashei katluha, lo dina velo dayana* — a man who was killed by women has no claim and no judge. The saying captures the extreme rarity of the scenario. In an era when violent confrontations were generally determined by brute physical strength, a man overpowered and killed by women would represent something so far outside the ordinary that it would be nearly impossible to convince anyone it had occurred. So too here: mice overpowering and killing a cat is so extraordinary and so far beyond what a borrower could have anticipated or prevented that it falls squarely within the category of *meita machmat melachah*, and the *shoel* is exempt.

### Cat Overstuffed

The Gemara then presents an alternative version of the case. *Ika de-amri hachi achal achbarei tuva vechavil umit* — there are those who say the actual case was different: the cat did not die from the mice attacking it, but rather from overeating — it consumed so many mice that it was overwhelmed and died. Rav Ashi is described as sitting and pondering this version of the case — *yativ Rav Ashi veka maayan bah ki hai gavna mai* — and again asking: how should one rule? Rav Mordechai again cites the same ruling from Avimi in the name of Rava, applying the same phrase: *gavra denashei katluha*.

### Rashi Interpretation

What is striking is that Rashi understands the idiom differently in the context of this second case. Whereas in the first version the phrase evokes an unlikely violent defeat, in the second version — where the cat died from overindulgence — Rashi explains *denashei katluha* as *ba'al be'ilot harbeh umet*: a man who was excessively licentious and was debilitated or died from the consequences of his indulgence. The analogy shifts accordingly: just as such a fate, while self-inflicted, results from the very activity one is engaged in and exceeds all ordinary expectation, so too a cat dying from consuming an excessive quantity of mice is an outcome so unusual and so far outside what the *shoel* could have foreseen that it remains *meita machmat melachah*, and the exemption holds.

## Employment Loophole

### Rava's Loophole

The Gemara pivots to a clever but somewhat mischievous scenario introduced by Rava. *Hai man deba'ei lemish'al midi michavreih* — what if someone wants to borrow an object from a friend while avoiding any liability at all? The simplest method, Rava explains, is to invoke *baalav imo* not through a genuine employment arrangement, but through a minimal gesture: *neimei lei ashkayan maya* — just say to the owner, "go get me a glass of water." At the moment of borrowing, the owner is technically performing a service for the borrower and is therefore under his employ — *de'hava she'ila biv'alav* — and the borrowing falls under the exemption of *baalav imo*.

### Closing the Loophole

Rava immediately anticipates the obvious counter-strategy. *I pikeiach hu* — if the lender is shrewd enough to recognize the scheme, he will respond: *neimei lei shtayei bidisha vehadar ashkayan* — finish the threshing first, and only afterward will I go get you the glass of water. That is, the owner insists on completing the actual work — the task for which he is being hired — before the act of lending takes place. By sequencing the labor before the loan, the employment relationship is concluded by the time the borrowing commences, and the *baalav imo* exemption no longer applies. The loophole closes.

It is worth noting that the concept of "hiring" in this context does not require monetary compensation. *Baalav imo* can be established through any form of work, paid or unpaid — even asking someone to perform a small favor constitutes sufficient grounds, which is precisely what makes the loophole so easy to exploit and the counter-strategy so important.

## Public Servants

### Service Workers

The Gemara proceeds to a fascinating application of *baalav imo* rooted in the economic realities of the *Chazal* era. Rava rules: *dekari dardekei* — one who reads to children, i.e., a school teacher; *shatla*, a gardener; *tavcha*, a butcher or slaughterer; and *umana*, a bloodletter — *kuleihu be'idan avdeihu keshe'ila biv'alav damu* — all of these, during the time they are engaged in their work, are treated as if one has borrowed something from them in the presence of the owner. If you borrow any object from any of these individuals, it is automatically regarded as *she'ila biv'alav*, and the borrower is exempt from liability. The rationale is that each of these figures works in a service capacity for the general public, and in that sense, the borrower is always already, in some meaningful sense, their employer.

### Communal vs Individual

This ruling generates a significant *machloket Rishonim* about the precise mechanism at work. Rashi understands the exemption to operate on an individual basis: when you have a specific, direct business relationship with one of these professionals — for example, you have engaged the butcher to slaughter and process your animal — then for the duration of that processing work, you have effectively hired him, and *baalav imo* applies. The week of preparation that leads up to the moment you pay for a finished cut of meat means that the butcher was, in a contractual sense, working on your behalf through the entire process, even though your actual transaction lasted only a few minutes.

The Rif takes a broader, more communal view. These professionals are employed, in effect, by the community as a whole. The butcher provides kosher meat for the town; the teacher educates the community's children; the gardener maintains shared spaces. Since the community collectively sustains their livelihood — whether through taxes, communal funds, or simply by being the aggregate customer base — every member of the community is considered to have a share in their employment at all times. The practical difference between the two positions is significant: according to Rashi, the exemption applies only when you have an active, specific engagement with that professional; according to the Rif, the exemption is essentially perpetual for all community members.

## Teacher Authority

### Teacher vs Students

This question — who has employed whom — surfaces in a direct and somewhat amusing exchange. The students came before Rava and asked: *shaylan mar akpid* — if we were to borrow something from you, would you be upset? The implicit argument is that they have hired Rava to teach them, so any borrowing from him would automatically fall under *baalav imo*. Rava was not amused. *L'afku'ei mamonai ka ba'itu?* — are you trying to deprive me of my money? And then he turns the claim on its head: *aderaba, atun she'iltun li* — on the contrary, you are hired out to me. It is the students who are in service of the teacher, not the other way around. His proof: *d'ilu ana matzi l'ishtamuti l'chu m'masechta l'masechta, atun lo matzitu l'ishtamuti* — I can switch from one tractate to another as I see fit, but you cannot walk out on me. The teacher's authority to set the curriculum establishes that the teacher is the party in charge and the students are subordinate.

## Public Servants (II)

### Modern Cases

These questions about service professionals have obvious modern analogues. One might ask whether an internet service provider, for instance, occupies a similar communal function — employed perpetually by all subscribers in a way that would generate *baalav imo* implications. The same question applies to a *shul* rabbi. If the rabbi is considered a communal public servant in the same way as the school teacher or butcher of the Talmudic era, then borrowing anything from the rabbi would be essentially liability-free — *baalav imo*, always. Whether any of these modern cases actually meets the threshold is a question that would need to be taken to a *posek*, particularly since explicit contractual conditions and communal *minhag* can modify the default halachic rules. In matters of *mamonot*, the general principle *kol tnai she'b'mamon kayam* — that financial conditions stipulated by parties are binding — means that specific agreements can supersede the *Torah*'s default framework, even where *de'oraita* liability would otherwise apply.

## Teacher Authority (II)

### Curriculum Choice

The Gemara qualifies Rava's ruling with an *okimta*. Is it really always true that the teacher determines everything and is therefore not the hired party of the students? *V'lo hi* — not exactly.

The distinction hinges on *yomei d'challa*. The *challa* gatherings — the great assemblies held in the weeks before Nisan and Tishrei — involved a fixed curriculum that was set in advance. The topic to be taught was predetermined and could not be deviated from. In that context, it is the students, or more precisely the community that assembled with specific expectations, who have effectively obligated the teacher to a fixed program — and in that case, the students could reasonably claim to have hired the teacher.

### Fixed Curriculum

On regular days, however, when there is no fixed curriculum and the teacher retains the authority to choose which tractate to teach, it is the teacher who is in control. The *okimta* therefore resolves the apparent tension: Rava's claim that students are hired to him applies specifically to regular teaching days, while on *yomei d'challa*, where the curriculum is fixed, the dynamic may well be reversed.

### Daf Yomi Application

This distinction raises an intriguing question about *Daf Yomi* itself. The *Daf Yomi* curriculum is entirely fixed in advance, determined by an external calendar that neither the teacher nor the students can alter. It would seem to fit squarely within the *yomei d'challa* model: the students have, in a real sense, engaged the teacher to deliver a specific predetermined program. Under this analysis, in a *Daf Yomi* framework, the students may well be considered to have hired the teacher — not the reverse — which would have direct implications for the *baalav imo* analysis. The practical details of salary structure — whether a teacher is paid annually, whether a *ba'al koreh* is compensated for his week of preparation in addition to his Shabbat reading, how to classify summer months for a teacher on a ten-month contract — these are all live questions addressed extensively in *shut* literature dealing with public servants and communal employees.

## Donkey Loading

### Donkey Dies

The Gemara introduces a case involving Mar bar Rav Chanina. He had hired out donkeys in Bei Chozai. While the donkeys were being loaded, the owner came out — *nafak gaduda'ei* — to adjust the packages being placed on one of the animals. In the course of the loading, someone acted negligently and the donkey died. The case came before Rabba, who ruled that the loaders were *chayav* — liable.

### Supervision Question

The scholars challenged this ruling immediately: *pshia be'ba'alim hi* — this was a case of negligence while the owner was present, which should exempt the *shoel* or the hired party from liability, not impose it. Rabba was taken aback — *ichsif* — he was embarrassed. But then the matter was investigated further, and it emerged that the owner had not actually come out to participate in the loading. *Delmeisar tauna hee denafak* — he came out only to supervise, to watch that the packages were not loaded excessively, not to physically assist with the work. Rashi explains: *lemeisar lirot shelo yarbu be'masao* — to ensure the load was not too heavy. If the owner was present only in a supervisory role and not as an active participant in the labor, then it is not *pshia be'ba'alim*, and the exemption does not apply. This detail makes a profound practical difference: the line between an owner actively helping with the work and an owner standing nearby to watch is legally decisive, and the facts of the case must be established with precision.

### Theft Alternative

The Gemara then shifts to an alternative reading of the case. Perhaps the facts were different from what was initially assumed — not a *pshia* scenario at all, but rather a case where the load or the donkey was stolen: *ignavo u'meita* — it was taken, and then it died while in the thief's possession — *k'darkah be'ganav havai*, as is the way when an animal dies while being led by a thief. Under this reading, the question becomes whether *gneiva be'ba'alim* — theft occurring while the owner is present — generates the same exemption as *pshia be'ba'alim*. The investigators uncovered the same underlying fact: the owner had come out not to assist with the labor but merely to supervise the loading. This resolved why Rabba ruled *chayav*: the owner was not genuinely present as a working participant, and therefore neither *pshia be'ba'alim* nor *gneiva be'ba'alim* could generate the exemption.

As for the underlying *halachah* — whether *pshia be'ba'alim* exempts or obligates — this was itself a *machloket* treated earlier in the sugya, on 95b, derived from the analogy to *shomer chinam*. The prevailing view appears to be that *pshia be'ba'alim* is *patur*. For those who wish to pursue the issue further, the *Rambam* in the opening of *She'ila u'Pikadon* is an excellent place to begin.

## Mishnah Cow

### Mixed Days

The Gemara now introduces a new Mishnah, returning to the theme of borrowed and rented animals. *Hashoel et hapara* — one who borrowed a cow. The Mishnah sets up several variants: what if the cow was borrowed for half a day and rented for half a day? What if it was borrowed for one full day and rented for the next? What if one animal was rented and a different one was borrowed? In each scenario, *u'meita* — the animal died.

### Lender's Claim

The first case: the lender claims *she'ula meita be'sha'a she'hayata she'ula meita* — the cow died at the time it was borrowed, not during the rental period. This matters enormously, because a *shoel* bears liability even for unavoidable accidents — *ones* — while a *socher*, a renter, does not. The lender therefore has every incentive to insist the death occurred during the borrowing period, since that would maximize his recovery. The borrower, for his part, says *eini yodea* — I don't know when it died. The Mishnah rules: *chayav* — the borrower is liable. As Rashi notes in his commentary to the Gemara, this is a case of *bari veshema bari adif* — when one party makes a definite claim and the other says "I don't know," the certain claim prevails.

### Renter's Claim

The case then flips. *Hasocher omer sechura metah beyom she'hayata sechura* — the renter says the animal died while it was being rented, not while it was being borrowed. He claims the lower-liability period. The owner responds: *eini yodea* — I don't know. Here too, *bari veshema bari adif* applies, but now in the other direction: the renter's certain claim beats the owner's uncertainty, and the renter is *patur* — exempt.

### Direct Contradiction

When both parties make definite, conflicting claims — *bari vebari* — the Mishnah rules differently. *Yishava hasocher shesechura metah* — the renter must take an oath that the animal died during the rental period. This oath is striking. It is not *shvuat hashomrim* — the standard oath a *shomer* takes to exempt himself — because *shvuat hashomrim* operates as a declaration of non-negligence and non-misappropriation. This oath is about a factual matter: when did the animal die? It resembles in some ways the oath discussed at the opening of Bava Metzia — on 3a — where Rabbi Yochanan explains that such an oath is *takanat chachamim*, a rabbinic enactment to prevent people from casually seizing others' property. The mechanism of this oath and its precise categorization is a major discussion in *Tosafot*.

### Unknown Split

The final case: *zeh omer eini yodea vezeh omer eini yodea* — neither party knows when the animal died. The Mishnah rules *yachloku* — they divide the liability. When both claims are *shema*, neither party has a stronger position, and the equitable resolution is to split the obligation. In practical terms, this means the borrower-renter pays half of what the full liability would have been. This outcome makes intuitive sense: if the arrangement covered, say, two days — one borrowed and one rented — and neither party can establish during which period the death occurred, each scenario is equally plausible, and splitting the loss reflects that symmetry.

## Gemara Analysis

### Rav Nachman

The Gemara immediately asks whether the Mishnah contradicts the position of Rav Nachman. The issue is the *bari veshema* case — where the lender makes a definite claim and the borrower says *eini yodea*, and the borrower is ruled *chayav*. The Gemara cites a dispute among the *Amoraim*: *maneh li beyadcha vehalah omer eini yodea* — if someone says "you owe me a hundred dollars" and the other responds "I don't know," Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda say *chayav* — the uncertain party must pay. Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan say *patur* — he is exempt.

Rav Nachman's position, as clarified by the Gemara, is not that uncertainty is irrelevant, but that liability in such a case arises only *kegon sheyesh esek shvua beineihem* — where there is already a *shvua* engagement between the parties, a prior oath obligation that connects them. If no such *shvua* is in play, the uncertain party cannot be compelled to pay and is therefore exempt.

The question is: how then does the Mishnah rule the borrower *chayav* in a *bari veshema* case? According to Rav Nachman, there should be no liability unless some oath obligation already exists between the parties. Is this a *tiyuvta* — a refutation — of Rav Nachman's position?

### Gilgul Shvua

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction. *Hacha nami kegon sheyesh esek shvua beineihem* — here too, the Mishnah must be dealing with a case where there was already a *shvua* obligation between the *socher* and the *mashil*, unrelated to the question of when the animal died. Because such an obligation exists, the mechanism of *gilgul shvua* — the cascading oath obligation — comes into play. Once a party is obligated to take an oath on one matter, the opposing party can require that the oath extend to encompass other related disputes. So the existing oath obligation between these two parties cascades to include the question of when the animal died, obligating the *socher* to swear on that factual matter as well.

### Cannot Swear

The second step of the analysis is equally important. Once the *gilgul shvua* obligation is established — that the *socher* must now take an oath about whether the animal died during the borrowing or the rental period — he finds himself in an impossible position. His own claim is a *shema* claim: he says only "I don't know." A person cannot take a positive oath about something he admits he does not know. Since his claim is uncertain, he cannot truthfully swear to the fact, and therefore the oath cannot be taken.

This brings into play the rule of *mitoch she'eino yachol lehishava yeshalem* — because he cannot swear, he must pay. The inability to take the oath functions as a constructive refusal, and the legal consequence is monetary liability. It is not that *bari veshema* independently forces payment; rather, the *gilgul shvua* creates the oath obligation, the *shema* claim makes it impossible to fulfill, and the impossibility of fulfillment triggers payment.

### Modeh B'Miktzat

The Gemara illustrates this mechanism with a parallel case drawn from the laws of *modeh b'miktzat*. As Rabba articulates it: if someone claims "you owe me a hundred dollars" and the other responds "I only owe you fifty — and as for the other fifty, I don't know," this is a *modeh b'miktzat shema* kind of case. The partial admission of the fifty establishes the *shvua* obligation — a classic *modeh b'miktzat* triggers the requirement to take an oath on the disputed remainder. But since the claimant's position on the remaining fifty is itself a *shema* — he says he doesn't know — he cannot actually take the oath. *Mitoch she'eino yachol lishava meshallem* — because he cannot swear, he must pay the full amount.

This case functions as a precise parallel to the *socher*'s situation in the Mishnah: in both instances, the party is caught between a *gilgul shvua* obligation they cannot escape and an epistemically uncertain claim that prevents them from fulfilling it. The solution in both cases is the same — liability follows from the impossibility of taking the oath.
