# Bava Kamma 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatzi Nezek—Initial vs. Actual Breakage

### Tzeroros Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: דָּרְסָה עַל כְּלִי וְלֹא שְׁבָרַתּוּ, וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר וְנִשְׁבַּר, מַהוּ? בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלִינַן – וְגוּפֵיהּ הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא בָּתַר תְּבַר מָנָא אָזְלִינָא – וּצְרוֹרוֹת נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** If an animal **trod upon a vessel but did not break it and** the vessel then **rolled to another place and broke** there, **what is** the *halakha*? Rava elaborates: In determining one's liability for causing damage, **do we follow the initial** action that ultimately led to the damage **and this** case is regarded as damage caused by the animal **itself?** Accordingly, the owner would pay the full cost of the damage as he would in any case classified under the primary category of Trampling. **Or perhaps** do **we follow the breaking of the vessel and** therefore **this** case is regarded as similar to the case of **pebbles** propelled by the foot of an animal in the course of its walking, since the vessel was not broken by a direct action of the animal, but rather, as an indirect result of the animal's action?

The daf opens in the midst of the *sugya* of *chatzi nezek tzeroros*—the rule that an animal which causes damage not through direct bodily contact but through indirect force, such as pebbles or other objects kicked up in the course of walking, pays only half damages. The Gemara has already established that this principle is a *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai*, a tradition from Sinai. Sumchus, however, disagrees and holds that even in such cases the full amount is owed. With this backdrop established, the Gemara now proceeds to probe several dimensions of the *chatzi nezek tzeroros* rule.

### Rava's Vessel Question

Rava poses the first of his questions: if an animal stepped on a vessel but did not break it on contact, and the vessel rolled to another place and then shattered, how do we categorize the damage? Is the damage treated as *gufo*—caused by the body of the animal—because the animal's body made direct contact with the vessel at the outset and set in motion the sequence leading to the break? If so, the owner would owe full damages. Or do we follow the moment of actual breakage, *basar tevar mana*, when the vessel was no longer in contact with the animal, making it a case of *kocho*—indirect force—analogous to *tzeroros*, and therefore only half damages?

### Rabbah's Staff Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבָּה – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: זָרַק כְּלִי מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וּבָא אַחֵר וּשְׁבָרוֹ בְּמַקֵּל – פָּטוּר; דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: מָנָא תְּבִירָא תְּבַר! לְרַבָּה – פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, לְרָבָא – מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve** Rava's dilemma **from** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says:** If one **threw a vessel from a roof and another came and broke it with a stick** as it was falling, the one who hit it with the stick is **exempt** from all liability **as we say to him** that **he broke a broken vessel.** From the moment the vessel was tossed from the roof it was inevitable that it would break. Consequently, breaking it with a stick as it was falling had no real effect. Apparently, the initial action is the decisive factor in determining liability for damages. The same should be the *halakha* in a case where a vessel was trod upon by an animal but it did not break, and the vessel then rolled to another place and broke there. The liability should be determined based on the initial action that caused the damage. The Gemara rejects this proof: The *halakha* in this case **is clear to Rabba,** who ruled as he did, but it remains **a dilemma to Rava.** One cannot prove the opinion of one *amora* from the opinion of another.

The Gemara suggests deriving an answer from a ruling of Rabbah. Rabbah states that if one throws a vessel from a rooftop and another person strikes it with a staff before it hits the ground and shatters, the second person is exempt from liability. The rationale is that the second person can say: I broke something that was already in the process of breaking—*mana sevira tavar*. The implication seems to be that once the first actor put the vessel in motion toward destruction, the object is already considered effectively broken, and any subsequent breaking is treated as having been caused by the original act. This would support the view that one follows the initial act, *basar me'ikara*, and the animal's body would be deemed the operative cause throughout.

### Rabbah vs. Rava

The Gemara clarifies the distinction between Rabbah and Rava. For Rabbah, the principle *basar me'ikara* is self-evident, and that is precisely the basis of his ruling about the staff. But Rava does not accept this as a settled premise—he regards it as an open question requiring proof. Rava's inquiry is therefore not resolved by Rabbah's position, which simply reflects Rabbah's own settled view. The Gemara now proceeds to seek actual evidence.

## Hen & Rope Case

### Hidus Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִידּוּס – אֵינוֹ מוּעָד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the resolution of this dilemma from a *baraita*: With regard to **hopping,** a chicken **is not** deemed **forewarned. And some say that it is forewarned.**

The Gemara attempts to resolve Rava's question by examining a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: *hidus eino muad*—a chicken that hops is not considered *muad* (habituated), while others say it is. The unusual framing—*muad* versus *eino muad* rather than the expected language of full versus half damages—immediately invites scrutiny.

### Hidus as Tzeroros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִידּוּס סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא לָאו הִידּוּס וְהִתִּיז, וּבְהָא קָמִפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר: בָּתַר תְּבַר מְנָא אָזְלִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the formulation of the *baraita*: Does it **enter your mind** to raise the possibility that a chicken is not forewarned with regard to **hopping?** Hopping is the typical manner of movement for chickens. **Rather, is it not** that the reference in the *baraita* is to a case where the chicken was **hopping and** the hopping **propelled** the vessel to roll to another place, and the vessel was broken there? **And they disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage holds** that **we follow the initial** action that ultimately led to the damage, and since hopping is typical manner of movement for a chicken, its owner is responsible for any damage that resulted from its hopping. **And** one **Sage holds: We follow the breaking of the vessel,** and since there was an additional factor that broke the vessel, the owner of the chicken is not liable.

The Gemara dismisses the literal reading. Hopping is an entirely natural activity for a chicken, and there would be no basis for any debate if the damage were caused directly through hopping on an object. The Gemara therefore reinterprets: *hidusa v'hitiz*—the chicken was hopping and in doing so scattered pebbles or kicked up a vessel, and the damage was caused indirectly. This is a classic *tzeroros* scenario. One reading of the debate, consistent with Rava's question, would be: one authority holds *basar me'ikara*—you follow the initial act of the chicken's body, deeming it *gufo* and full damages, hence *muad*; while the other holds *basar tevar mana*—you follow the moment of breakage, which was indirect, rendering it *kocho* and half damages, hence *eino muad*. This would be a neat resolution.

### Tarnigolim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: תַּרְנְגוֹלִים שֶׁהָיוּ מְחַטְּטִין בְּחֶבֶל דְּלִי, וְנִפְסַק הַחֶבֶל וְנִשְׁבַּר הַדְּלִי – מְשַׁלְּמִין נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלִינַן! תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַחֶבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an additional attempt to resolve the dilemma from a *baraita*: In a case where **chickens were pecking at the rope** tied to **a bucket and the rope was severed and the bucket** fell and **broke,** the owner of the chickens **pays the full** cost of the **damage. Conclude from** the *baraita* that **we follow the initial** action that ultimately led to the damage in determining liability for damage. The Gemara rejects this conclusion, and one cannot adduce proof from the *baraita*. **Interpret** instead that the reference in the *baraita* is **to** the damage caused to **the rope,** while the owner pays only half the cost of the damage for breaking the bucket.

The Gemara then turns to another *baraita*: chickens were pecking at a rope holding a bucket (*tarnigolim she-hayu mechatetin be-chevel deli*). The rope snapped, the bucket fell, and shattered. The *baraita* rules that one pays the full amount for the bucket. This appears directly analogous to Rava's question: the chickens acted on the rope, the bucket broke upon hitting the ground, and yet full damages are assessed. The Gemara initially suggests this proves *basar me'ikara*—you follow the initial act.

### Proof of Initial Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חֶבֶל מְשׁוּנֶּה הוּא! דְּמָאוּס בְּלִישָׁה: וְהָא ״נִשְׁבַּר דְּלִי״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא סוֹמְכוֹס הִיא, דְּאָמַר: צְרוֹרוֹת – נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** eating **a rope atypical** behavior for a chicken, and therefore it should be classified within the primary category of Goring, not the primary category of Eating? Why then must the owner of the chicken pay the full cost of the damage? The Gemara answers: The reference in the *baraita* is to a case where the rope **was filthy [*ma'us*] with kneaded dough,** and in those circumstances it is typical for a chicken to peck at it. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *tanna* **teach** in the *baraita*: And the **bucket broke,** indicating that the dispute is with regard to damage done to the bucket, not to the rope? The Gemara explains: **Rather,** this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos, who says:** In the case of **pebbles** propelled by an animal, the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

The Gemara explores whether the ruling of full payment applies to the rope or to the bucket. If it refers to the rope, the *tzeroros* question is avoided. But the Gemara then objects: pecking a rope in good condition would be *meshuneh*—unusual—and would itself warrant reduced liability, not full payment. However, the *baraita* specifies that the rope was coated with dough and was therefore attractive to the chickens, rendering the pecking normal. The Gemara presses further: the *baraita* explicitly says the bucket broke, so clearly the payment for the bucket is what is at issue. This seems to establish *basar me'ikara* as the operative principle—the bucket's breakage is attributed to the initial act of pecking the rope.

### Sumchus Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סוֹמְכוֹס, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: נִיתַּז מִמֶּנּוּ שֶׁבֶר, וְנָפַל עַל כְּלִי אַחֵר וּשְׁבָרָהּ – עַל הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְעַל הָאַחֲרוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. וְאִי סוֹמְכוֹס, מִי אִית לֵיהּ חֲצִי נֶזֶק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos, say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **If a shard was propelled from** the bucket broken by a chicken **and it fell upon another vessel and broke it,** the owner of the chicken **pays the full** cost of the **damage for the first** vessel, **and half** the cost of **the damage for the second** one. **And if** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos, is he of** the opinion that one pays **half** the cost of **the damage** caused by pebbles?

The Gemara deflects: perhaps the ruling reflects the position of Sumchus, who holds that *tzeroros* always obligates full payment. On this reading, the bucket is treated as *kocho* of the chickens, and Sumchus obligates full damages for *kocho*. The Gemara then examines the continuation of the *baraita*: if pieces from the shattered bucket flew off and struck another vessel and broke it, one pays full for the bucket but only half for the second vessel. This works perfectly according to the Rabbanan, who hold that the pecking constitutes *gufo* with respect to the rope-and-bucket unit (*basar me'ikara*), while the flying shards constitute *tzeroros*, warranting only half damages. But if we are reading the *baraita* according to Sumchus, who holds that *kocho* obligates full payment, why would the second vessel receive only half? The answer proposed is that Sumchus himself distinguishes between *kocho* and *ko'ach kocho*: Sumchus holds that the animal's own indirect force (*kocho*) is close enough to the animal that full payment is owed, but a derivative of that indirect force—*ko'ach kocho*, the force of the force—is sufficiently removed that even Sumchus would concede only half damages.

### Rav Ashi Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ לְסוֹמְכוֹס בֵּין נֶזֶק כֹּחוֹ לְכֹחַ כֹּחוֹ; וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּבָעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: כֹּחַ כֹּחוֹ לְסוֹמְכוֹס, כְּכֹחוֹ דָּמֵי אוֹ לָאו כְּכֹחוֹ דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that there is **a difference, according to Sumakhos, between damage** resulting from **a force** generated by the animal's action, e.g., pebbles, **and** damage caused **by a force** generated by **a force** generated by **its** action, e.g., damage caused by the shard of the bucket, and in the latter case Sumakhos concedes that the owner pays half the cost of the damage, that would result in a difficulty. The Gemara asks: **But** what of **this dilemma raised by Rav Ashi: According to** the opinion of **Sumakhos,** what is the *halakha* with regard to damage caused **by a force** generated by **a force** generated by the animal's action? Is the status of that secondary force **like** that of damage caused by **a force** generated by **its** action, in which case the owner is liable, **or** is its status **not like** that of damage caused by **a force** generated by **its** action?

At precisely this point, Rav Ashi poses an independent question: according to Sumchus, is *ko'ach kocho* treated like *kocho* itself, obligating full payment, or is it treated differently, obligating only half? This question remains unresolved. The Gemara now observes that the *baraita* about the bucket could in principle resolve one of two unresolved questions—either Rava's question about *basar me'ikara*, or Rav Ashi's question about *ko'ach kocho* according to Sumchus—but one cannot prove both simultaneously.

### Resolve as Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ דְּלָאו כְּכֹחוֹ דָּמֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבָּנַן הִיא? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** Why did Rav Ashi state his dilemma; why did he not **resolve** the dilemma and conclude that according to Sumakhos its status is **not like** that of damage caused by **a force** generated by **its** action? Since Rav Ashi does not resolve the dilemma based on this *baraita*, apparently, Sumakhos does not distinguish between the two types of force. The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** is it **not** so that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **conclude from** the *baraita* **that we follow the initial** action that ultimately led to the damage when determining one's liability for the damage. Therefore, an owner is liable for any damage resulting from his animal's typical behavior, and he pays the full cost of the damage done to the bucket even if the damage took place in another location.

To avoid the *baraita* from resolving Rav Ashi's question about Sumchus, the Gemara concludes that the *baraita* must be read according to the Rabbanan, not Sumchus. On that reading, the full payment for the bucket is due to the principle of *basar me'ikara*—the initial act of pecking is treated as *gufo*, and the bucket's breakage is attributed directly to the chickens' bodies. The half payment for the shards-damaged vessel reflects the standard *tzeroros* rule of the Rabbanan. This reading would indeed resolve Rava's first question in favor of *basar me'ikara*.

### Alt: Continued Pushing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: דְּקָאָזֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said:** There is no proof from the *baraita*, as it could be explained that the reference in the *baraita* is to a case **where** the bucket is **continually** propelled **from** the impetus of the chicken, who caused the bucket to break. That is why the ruling in the *baraita* is that the owner of the chicken pays the full cost of the damage. The dilemma with regard to the case where an animal treads upon a vessel that rolls away and breaks elsewhere remains unresolved.

However, Rav Bibi bar Abaye offers yet another reading of the *baraita* that avoids proving anything either way. He suggests that the case is not one where the rope was pecked and the bucket fell and shattered on its own, but rather that the chickens continued actively pushing the bucket after it fell, causing the breakage through direct ongoing contact—*gufo*. This, of course, renders the *baraita* entirely unremarkable on the question at hand, and the Gemara acknowledges that this reading, while possible, strips the scenario of its analytical interest. The more natural and compelling reading of the bucket case does support *basar me'ikara* in line with Rava's implicit inclination—which is also consistent with Rabbah's position—but the Gemara ultimately leaves the question formally unresolved, having declined to commit to any single proof.

## Migufo vs. Me'aliya

### Migufo Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: חֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, אוֹ מֵעֲלִיָּיה מְשַׁלֵּם? מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם – דְּלָא אַשְׁכְּחַן חֲצִי נֶזֶק דִּמְשַׁלֵּם מֵעֲלִיָּיה, אוֹ דִלְמָא מֵעֲלִיָּיה מְשַׁלֵּם – דְּלָא אַשְׁכְּחַן כְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ דִּמְשַׁלֵּם מִגּוּפֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to one's liability to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** caused by **pebbles** inadvertently propelled by the foot of a walking animal, does the owner of the animal **pay** restitution exclusively **from** the **body** of the animal, just as one pays half the cost of the damage caused by an innocuous ox with regard to damage categorized under the primary category of Goring? **Or** does he **pay** restitution **from** his **superior**-**quality** land, as he does for other forms of damage? The Gemara elaborates: Does he **pay** restitution exclusively **from** the **body** of the animal, **as we do not find** any case where one **pays half** the cost of **the damage from** his **superior**-**quality** land? **Or perhaps he pays** restitution **from** his **superior**-**quality** land, **as we do not find** any case where an animal causes damage **in its** typical **manner where** the owner **pays** restitution exclusively **from** the **body** of his animal?

Rava's second question concerns the modality of payment for *chatzi nezek tzeroros*. Even granting that only half damages are owed, how is that half collected? Is payment extracted from the body of the animal itself (*migufo*), as is the case with *keren*—the category of intentional or unusual goring—where the owner's liability is capped at the value of the offending animal? Or is it paid out of the owner's own assets (*me'aliya*), as is standard for *regel*—damage caused in a natural, expected manner?

The two poles of the question reflect competing analogical frameworks. On one hand, one could argue: we have no precedent for half damages paid *me'aliya*—the only other instance of *chatzi nezek* is *keren* of a *tam* (an animal that has not yet established a pattern of goring), which is always paid *migufo*. Perhaps the half-payment structure carries with it the *migufo* mode as an inseparable feature. On the other hand, *tzeroros* is categorized as a *toldah* of *regel*—a derivative of the *regel* category—and *regel* always pays *me'aliya*. Since the damage occurs in a natural and habitual manner, perhaps the *me'aliya* mode is the appropriate one.

### Hidus Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִידּוּס – אֵינוֹ מוּעָד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה מוּעָד. הִידּוּס סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא לָאו הִידּוּס וְהִתִּיז, וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ מוּעָד – קָסָבַר מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מוּעָד – קָסָבַר מֵעֲלִיָּיה מְשַׁלֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita*: With regard to **hopping,** a chicken **is not** deemed **forewarned. And some say** that **it is forewarned.** The Gemara questions the formulation of the *baraita*: Does it **enter your mind** to raise the possibility that a chicken is not forewarned with regard to **hopping?** Hopping is the typical manner of movement for chickens. **Rather, is it not** that the reference in the *baraita* is to a case where the chicken was **hopping and propelled** pebbles that caused the damage? **And** it is **with regard to this** matter **that they disagree: The one who says** that the chicken **is not** deemed **forewarned holds that** its owner **pays** restitution **from** the **body** of the animal, as is the *halakha* when an innocuous animal gores, while **the one who says** that the chicken **is forewarned holds that he pays** restitution **from** his **superior**-**quality** land, as is the *halakha* when a forewarned animal causes damage. According to this analysis, Rava's dilemma is the subject of a tannaitic dispute.

The Gemara revisits the *baraita* of *hidus*—*hidus eino muad, v'yesh omrim harei zeh muad*. As established earlier, this must refer to a case where the chicken scattered pebbles (*tzeroros*). But the *baraita*'s choice of language—*muad* versus *eino muad*—rather than the expected *nezek shalem* versus *chatzi nezek*, is now given a fresh reading. Perhaps the debate is not about the amount of payment at all. Both sides may agree with the Rabbanan that *tzeroros* obligates half damages. What they are debating is the *form* of payment. The one who says *eino muad*—not *muad*—means that it is treated like a *tam* (*gufo*), and therefore pays *migufo*. The one who says *harei zeh muad* means it is treated like a *muad* (*me'aliya*), since the behavior is natural and habitual, and therefore pays out of pocket. The terminology of *muad* and *eino muad* maps directly onto the *me'aliya* versus *migufo* distinction.

### Inconclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּסוֹמְכוֹס וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** perhaps the dispute in the *baraita* is **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Sumakhos and the Rabbis** with regard to the legal status of damage caused by a force generated by the animal's action. It is with regard to the similarity of damage caused by an action performed by an animal's body to the case where the chicken was hopping and propelled pebbles that caused the damage that the *tanna'im* in the *baraita* **disagree.**

The Gemara rejects this reading as unprovable. The *baraita* may simply reflect the familiar dispute between Sumchus and the Rabbanan—full versus half damages—with *muad* meaning full payment and *eino muad* meaning half, nothing more. The second question of Rava thus remains unresolved.

## Ember in Grain

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֶּלֶב שֶׁנָּטַל חֲרָרָה וְהָלַךְ לְגָדִישׁ, וְאָכַל הַחֲרָרָה וְהִדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ – עַל הַחֲרָרָה מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְעַל הַגָּדִישׁ מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this dilemma from a mishna (21b): If **the dog took a loaf [*ḥarara*]** that was baking on hot coals **and went to a stack of grain** to eat the loaf, **and** the dog **ate the loaf and ignited the stack** of grain with one of the coals attached to the loaf, the owner of the dog **pays the full** cost of the **damage for** the damage to **the loaf,** as it is classified in the primary category of Eating, **and he pays half** the cost of **the damage for** the damage to **the stack** of grain. Rabbi Elazar says: He pays the full cost of the damage for the damage to the stack of grain as well.

The Gemara now introduces a *mishnah* from later in the tractate. A dog took a wafer (*harara*) that had been sitting on coals, with a burning ember clinging to it, and carried it to a haystack. The dog ate the wafer, and the ember set the haystack on fire. The ruling: for the wafer itself, full damages are owed (*nezek shalem*)—this is a standard case of *shen*, the animal consuming food for its own benefit. For the haystack, only half damages are owed. The reason, as the Gemara infers, is that the ember constitutes *tzeroros*—not the animal's body itself, but an indirect force analogous to scattered pebbles.

### Tosefta Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא – לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוְיָא לְהוּ צְרוֹרוֹת? וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק – מִגּוּפוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that the first *tanna* holds that the owner pays half the cost of the damage caused to the stack of grain? **Is it not due to** the fact **that it is** analogous to propelling **pebbles,** as the coal fell from the loaf held in its mouth and ignited the grain? **And** it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in that regard** that when the first *tanna* said that **he pays half** the cost of **the damage,** he pays restitution exclusively **from** proceeds of the sale of the **body** of his animal. Apparently, half the cost of the damage caused by pebbles is paid from the body of the animal that caused the damage.

A *Tosefta* commenting on this case states explicitly: *meshalem chatzi nezek migufo*—the half damages for the haystack are paid from the body of the animal. This appears to answer Rava's second question directly: *chatzi nezek tzeroros* is paid *migufo*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא?! לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מִגּוּפֵיהּ מִי אַשְׁכְּחַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **And can you understand** the *baraita* in that way? According to that understanding, with regard to Rabbi Elazar, who disagrees with the first *tanna* and holds that the owner of the dog pays the full cost of the damage caused to the grain, **do we find** anywhere that **according to Rabbi Elazar** one **pays** restitution exclusively **from** proceeds of the sale of the **body** of his animal?

But the Gemara immediately complicates the picture. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Eliezer holds that the payment for the haystack is full—*nezek shalem*. If the debate between the first opinion and Rabbi Eliezer is simply the dispute between the Rabbanan and Sumchus—half versus full, both treating the ember as *tzeroros*—then Rabbi Eliezer, following Sumchus, would owe the full amount. But if so, why would the *Tosefta* specify *migufo*? Sumchus holds *tzeroros* obligates full payment because he views *kocho* as equivalent to the animal's body—and if that is the basis of Rabbi Eliezer's full payment, there would be no reason to pay *migufo*; it would be paid *me'aliya* like any standard *regel*-type damage. The internal logic breaks down: you cannot have full payment on a Sumchus theory and *migufo* simultaneously.

### Meshuneh Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּשַׁנִּי בְּהָא גַּחֶלֶת, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, דְּאָמַר: מְשׁוּנֶּה קֶרֶן בַּחֲצַר הַנִּיזָּק, נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* can be explained in a case **where** the dog **acted atypically with this coal** with which he lit the stack of grain. Since the dog's behavior was atypical, the damage is classified under the primary category of Goring, not under the category of Eating. **And Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon, who says** in a mishna (24b): The *halakha* of **Goring in the courtyard of the injured** party **is atypical,** as there, the owner of the ox **pays the full** cost of the **damage,** in contrast to half the cost of the damage that he pays when it transpires in the public domain. Nevertheless, since it was an innocuous animal that caused the damage, restitution is paid from its body.

The Gemara concludes that the ember case cannot be one of *tzeroros* at all. Instead, it must be a case of *meshuneh*—unusual conduct—in which the dog directly placed the ember onto the grain. This unusual action does not belong to the *kocho/tzeroros* framework but rather to the *keren* framework, where an animal acts in an abnormal way. *Keren* in the form of *meshuneh* pays half damages, paid *migufo*, which is why the *Tosefta* specifies *migufo*.

### Rabbi Tarfon Position

Rabbi Eliezer's ruling of full damages is then explained through his alignment with Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Tarfon argues by *kal vachomer*: *shen* and *regel* are exempt in the public domain but pay full damages in the property of the injured party (*chatzer hanizak*). *Keren* pays half in the public domain. Certainly, then, *keren* in the property of the injured party should pay at least as much as *shen* and *regel* pay there—namely, the full amount. According to Rabbi Tarfon, *keren meshuneh* in *chatzer hanizak* obligates the full amount. Rabbi Eliezer, following this position, rules full damages for the dog's unusual act of placing an ember directly on the grain in what is effectively the domain of the injured party.

### Migufo Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; מַאי טַעְמָא מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – מִשּׁוּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that explanation: **But** that **is not so. What is the reason** that **you interpreted** that the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon?** It is **due to** the difficulty presented by the *halakha* that he pays **the full** cost of the **damage** exclusively from proceeds of the sale of the body of the animal, which would not be so according to Rabbi Elazar.

The Gemara presses: if Rabbi Eliezer follows Rabbi Tarfon and holds that the full amount is owed, why should it still be paid *migufo*? Rabbi Tarfon derives his ruling by analogy to *shen* and *regel*, which pay *me'aliya*. If we are upgrading *keren* to full payment by comparing it to *shen* and *regel*, perhaps we should likewise adopt the *me'aliya* payment mode. Why does *migufo* persist?

### Rabbi Yehuda Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר כְּסוֹמְכוֹס, דְּאָמַר: צְרוֹרוֹת – נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מְשַׁלֵּם; וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: צַד תַּמּוּת בִּמְקוֹמָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת. וְכִי קָתָנֵי מִגּוּפוֹ – אַצַּד תַּמּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: There is an alternative explanation. **Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Sumakhos, who says** that one **pays** the **full** cost of the **damage** caused by **pebbles, and** he also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** with regard to a forewarned ox: **The aspect of innocuousness,** which dictates that the owner pays half the cost of the damage from the body of the ox, **remains intact.** According to Rabbi Yehuda, the owner of a forewarned ox pays half the cost of the damage from the body of his ox and half the cost of the damage from his superior**-**quality land. **And** when the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teaches** that the owner pays restitution **from the body** of his animal, the reference is **to the aspect of innocuousness,** i.e., half the cost of the damage. He pays the other half from his superior**-**quality land as he would in the case of any forewarned animal.

The Gemara introduces an alternative explanation. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer holds simultaneously like Sumchus—that *tzeroros* obligates full payment—and like Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that when a *tam* becomes a *muad*, the first half of the full damages is still paid *migufo* (reflecting the animal's original *tam* status), while the second half is paid *me'aliya*. Rabbi Yehuda's model thus creates a hybrid in which full damages are owed but the first half is still extracted from the animal's body. Applied here: Rabbi Eliezer treats this as a *tzeroros* case with full payment per Sumchus, and follows Rabbi Yehuda's model so that at least the first half remains *migufo*. When the *Tosefta* says *chatzi nezek migufo*, it is referring to this first half.

### Rav Sama Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סַמָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבִינָא: אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, בְּתָם וְנַעֲשָׂה מוּעָד; בְּמוּעָד מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Samma, son of Rav Ashi, said to Ravina: Say that you heard** that **Rabbi Yehuda** stated that the aspect of innocuousness remains intact **with regard to** damage categorized under the primary category of Goring, where the ox was initially **innocuous and** then **became forewarned. In** the case of propelled pebbles, which is a subcategory of Trampling, where the ox is **forewarned from the outset, did you hear him** distinguish between the different parts of the payment? There is no aspect of innocuousness in the case of pebbles.

Rav Sama the son of Rav Ashi raises a pointed objection to Ravina: Rabbi Yehuda's hybrid model—half *migufo*, half *me'aliya*—makes sense only for an animal that *began* as a *tam* and subsequently became a *muad*. In that developmental sequence, the animal had an established identity as a *tam*, which paid half *migufo*, and the upgrade to *muad* adds a second layer of full payment in a different mode. But where an animal is treated as *muad* from the outset—or, in our case, where Sumchus treats *tzeroros* as obligating full payment from the start—there is no prior *tam* stage to anchor the *migufo* element. The hybrid model cannot be applied where there was never a period of half-from-the-body to begin with. This objection stands unanswered, leaving the *migufo* question of *chatzi nezek tzeroros* unresolved.

## Ha'ada'ah for Tzeroros

### Ha'ada'ah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – בָּעֵי רָבָא: יֵשׁ הַעֲדָאָה לִצְרוֹרוֹת, אוֹ אֵין הַעֲדָאָה לִצְרוֹרוֹת? לְקֶרֶן מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself,** cited earlier. **Rava raises** a **dilemma: Is there forewarning for** propelling **pebbles,** so that the owner would pay the full cost of the damage if he had been forewarned three times, **or is there no forewarning for** propelling **pebbles,** and he will always pay half the cost of the damage? The Gemara elaborates: **Do we liken** the case of propelled pebbles **to** damage categorized as **Goring?** Accordingly, when an innocuous ox causes damage by propelling pebbles its owner pays half the cost of the damage, and when a forewarned ox does so the owner pays the full cost of the damage. **Or perhaps, is it a subcategory** of the primary category **of Trampling,** which is deemed forewarned from the outset? Accordingly, the owner would always pay half the cost of the damage, as there is no change in status after the third warning.

Rava poses his third question: *yesh ha'ada'ah l'tzeroros o ein ha'ada'ah l'tzeroros*—does the mechanism of formal warning (*ha'ada'ah*) apply to *tzeroros*? By *ha'ada'ah* is meant the process whereby an animal that has gored three times is deemed *muad*, transforming the owner's liability from half to full damages. The question is whether an analogous process applies to *tzeroros*: if an animal has repeatedly scattered pebbles and caused damage, and the owner has been formally warned three times, does the animal's *tzeroros* liability escalate from half to full?

The question is genuinely difficult on both sides. One might argue that *tzeroros* is a *toldah* of *regel*, and *regel* is entirely natural—warning does not change the nature of natural behavior or elevate liability. Alternatively, one might reason that *chatzi nezek* follows the structural form of *keren*, and just as *keren* admits of escalation through *ha'ada'ah*, so too should *chatzi nezek tzeroros*. Or one might simply argue that *ha'ada'ah* transforms any repeated pattern into something analogous to intentional damage, regardless of category.

This question also provides a new lens through which to read the ember case. The Gemara proposes: what if the debate in the *baraita* about the ember reflects exactly this question? The scenario could be one in which the owner was warned three times about the animal's *tzeroros*-type behavior. The first opinion holds that *ein ha'ada'ah l'tzeroros*—warning does not elevate *tzeroros* to full—and the ruling is therefore *chatzi nezek migufo*, consistent with the standard *tzeroros* rule. Rabbi Eliezer holds *yesh ha'ada'ah l'tzeroros*—warning does escalate *tzeroros* to full—and he rules full payment. And since, per Rabbi Yehuda's model, the first half remains *migufo* even after escalation to full, we can account for the *Tosefta*'s specification of *migufo* alongside Rabbi Eliezer's full payment.

### Three-Time Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִידּוּס – אֵינוֹ מוּעָד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה מוּעָד. מַאי, לָאו כְּגוֹן דַּעֲבַד תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי – וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר יֵשׁ הַעֲדָאָה, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין הַעֲדָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the resolution of this dilemma from a *baraita*: With regard to **hopping,** a chicken **is not** deemed **forewarned. And some say that it is forewarned.** The Gemara elaborates: **What, is it not** referring to a case **where** the chicken has **done** so **three times? And** it is **with regard to this** that **they disagree:** One **Sage holds: There is forewarning for** pebbles, **and** one **Sage holds: There is no forewarning for** pebbles. According to this analysis, Rava's dilemma is the subject of a tannaitic dispute.

The Gemara now revisits the *baraita* of *hidus* under this new lens. The language *muad* versus *eino muad* fits this reading naturally and perhaps most elegantly. The scenario is one of repeated conduct—the chicken has been hopping and scattering pebbles three times. One authority says *harei zeh muad*: warning three times makes it *muad*, the liability escalates to full. The other says *eino muad*: no amount of warning converts *tzeroros* into full damages. The very terminology of the *baraita* directly tracks the question of *ha'ada'ah*.

### Single vs. Repeated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בְּחַד זִימְנָא, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּסוֹמְכוֹס וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this resolution: **No,** perhaps the reference in the *baraita* is to a case where the chicken hopped only **one time. And** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between **Sumakhos and the Rabbis,** concerning the compensation for damage caused by pebbles.

The Gemara once again declines to treat this as a resolution. The *baraita* may simply describe a single incident, and the debate may be nothing more than the familiar dispute between Sumchus and the Rabbanan—full versus half—with the language of *muad* and *eino muad* referring only to the amount of payment, not to any question of *ha'ada'ah*. Thus Rava's third question, like the first two, remains formally unresolved.

## Defecation Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁהֵטִילָה גְּלָלִים לְעִיסָּה, רַב יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the resolution to the dilemma from an amoraic dispute: If **an animal dropped excrement onto dough** and damaged it, **Rav Yehuda says:** The owner of the animal **pays the full** cost of the **damage, and Rabbi Elazar says:** He pays **half** the cost of **the damage.**

The Gemara turns to another *baraita* in an attempt to resolve the *ha'ada'ah* question. An animal defecated into dough (*behema she-hitila glalim l'isa*). Rabbi Yehuda rules full damages; Rabbi Eliezer rules half. The defecation is treated as a form of *tzeroros*—something issuing from the animal's body but not the body itself. The question is what drives the disagreement.

### Normal vs. Unusual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו כְּגוֹן דַּעֲבַד תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי – וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר יֵשׁ הַעֲדָאָה, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין הַעֲדָאָה? לָא, בְּחַד זִימְנָא, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּסוֹמְכוֹס וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי. וְהָא מְשׁוּנֶּה הוּא! דִּדְחִיק לֵיהּ עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** referring to a case **where** the animal has **done** so **three times? And** it is **with regard to this** that **they disagree,** as one **Sage holds: There is forewarning for** pebbles, **and** one **Sage holds: There is no forewarning for** pebbles. The Gemara rejects this resolution: **No,** perhaps the reference in the *baraita* is to a case where the animal dropped excrement only **one time. And** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between **Sumakhos and the Rabbis,** concerning compensation for damage caused by pebbles. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** this **atypical** behavior of the animal, and since all atypical behavior is classified within the category of Goring, meaning that the owner should be liable to pay only half the cost of the damage? The Gemara answers: The case is one **where** the animal **was forced** into a narrow place and had no alternative to relieving itself onto the dough; therefore, it does not constitute atypical behavior.

The Gemara considers whether the act of defecating into the dough is *meshuneh*. The Gemara concludes it is not: the animal was in a crowded situation (*dachika lei alma*) and had no choice, so the act was not unusual. With *meshuneh* ruled out, the case is a straightforward *tzeroros* scenario. The Gemara then asks: might the debate be one of *ha'ada'ah*—the animal defecated three times and was warned, and now Rabbi Yehuda says this warrants full payment? Once again, the Gemara dismisses the reading: the case could be one occurrence, and the debate reduces to Sumchus versus the Rabbanan.

### Pedagogical Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּלָלִים אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וּבָתַר גּוּפֵיהּ גְּרִירִין – כְּגוּפֵיהּ דָּמֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It was **necessary** for them to state their dispute in this case, as there is a novel element concerning **excrement. It** would **enter your mind to say: Since** the excrement **is drawn after** the body of the animal, its legal status is **like** that of damage caused by **its body,** not like pebbles propelled by the animal. Therefore, their stating the dispute with regard to excrement **teaches us** that its status is like that of propelling pebbles. Therefore, the dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar can be explained as parallel to the dispute between Sumakhos and the Rabbis with regard to pebbles.

One might ask why the Gemara bothers introducing this case at all if it merely restates the Sumchus-Rabbanan dispute. The Gemara explains: *glalim itzricha lei*—the defecation scenario was necessary to make a specific point. There was reason to think that the party who holds like the Rabbanan—and rules half for *tzeroros*—might concede full damages here. One could argue that since feces emerge directly from the animal's body (*basar gufa griri*), they should be viewed as an extension of the body itself rather than as *tzeroros*. The *baraita* therefore teaches that we do not adopt this reasoning: excrement is treated as *tzeroros* and not as the body of the animal.

### Sumchus vs. Rabbanan

Rabbi Yehuda's ruling of full damages reflects his alignment with Sumchus, who holds that *kocho*—and by extension all *tzeroros*-type damage—obligates full payment. Rabbi Eliezer's ruling of half reflects the position of the Rabbanan. The debate is squarely Sumchus versus the Rabbanan, and the defecation case adds the *chiddush* that even something as physically proximate to the animal as its own excrement does not elevate the liability beyond what the *tzeroros* framework dictates. The three questions of Rava—*basar me'ikara* versus *basar tevar mana*; *migufo* versus *me'aliya*; and *yesh ha'ada'ah* versus *ein ha'ada'ah*—remain open at the conclusion of the daf.
