# Nazir 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Tamei - Shaving

### Nazir Tamei Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְטִמֵּא רֹאשׁ נִזְרוֹ״, בְּטָהוֹר שֶׁנִּטְמָא הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, שֶׁהוּא טָעוּן הַעֲבָרַת שֵׂעָר וַהֲבָאַת צִיפֳּרִין. וְלִפְטוֹר אֶת הַנָּזִיר בְּקֶבֶר — שֶׁאֵין טָעוּן הַעֲבָרַת שֵׂעָר וַהֲבָאַת צִיפֳּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof to answer the question of whether one who vows naziriteship while in a cemetery must shave upon undergoing purification. **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he defiles his consecrated head,** he shall shave his head on the day of his purification, on the seventh day shall he shave it" (Numbers 6:9). The *baraita* explains: **The verse is speaking of a ritually pure** nazirite **who became impure, who requires hair removal and the bringing of birds** as offerings. And this verse comes **to exempt a nazirite** who vowed while **in the grave,** i.e., a cemetery, **that he does not require hair removal and the bringing of birds.**

The daf opens with a *tashma* two lines from the top of 18a, continuing the discussion of a *nazir tamei* — a *nazir* who accepted his *nezirut* while in a state of *tumah*, specifically *tumas meis*. Such a person cannot begin counting his thirty days of *nezirut* until he becomes *tahor*, a process that takes seven days. The question that has been driving this discussion concerns what happens on day seven: must this *nazir* shave his head, even though he does not bring *korbanot* on day eight?

The reason the *nazir tamei* does not bring *korbanot* on day eight is already established — the *mishna* and the *gemara* have consistently maintained that the *korbanot* associated with *tumas nazir* apply only when someone began *tahor* and subsequently became *tamei*. But that conclusion regarding *korbanot* does not automatically resolve the question of shaving. Shaving and the *korbanot* are performed on different days: the shaving is on day seven, explicitly connected in the *pesukim* to the *taharah* process, while the *korbanot* are on day eight. Perhaps the shaving of a *nazir* who is completing a *taharah* process serves an independent function — enabling the resumption of *nezirut* — and would therefore be required even when no *korbanot* are brought.

### Pesukim - Days 7 & 8

The relevant *pesukim* from *Parshas Naso* are worth reading carefully, as they clarify the structure of the inquiry. The Torah states: *v'chi yamut meis alav befesa pitom v'timei rosh nizro* — if a corpse dies upon him suddenly and the head of his *nezirut* becomes *tamei* — *v'gilach rosho b'yom taharato b'yom hashvi'i yegalhenu*, he shall shave his head on the day he becomes pure, which is day seven. Then, *u'vayom hashmini*, on day eight, he brings two turtledoves or two young pigeons to the *kohen* at the entrance of the *ohel moed*. One is a *chatas* and one is an *olah*. The *kohen* atones for him, *v'kidesh et rosho bayom hahu*, he sanctifies his head on that day — presumably day eight — and *v'hizir l'Hashem et yemei nizro*, he dedicates to God the days of his *nezirut*. Finally, *v'hevi keves ben shnato l'asham*, he brings a lamb as an *asham*, and *v'hayomim harishonim yiplu ki tamei nizro* — the earlier days are lost because his *nezirut* became *tamei*.

The structure thus shows clearly that the shaving is on day seven, tied directly to the *taharah* process, while the *korbanot* are on day eight. The *gemara*'s question therefore has genuine textual grounding: since the shaving is connected to *taharah* and not explicitly to the *korbanot*, perhaps it is required independently, even in the case of someone who began *tamei* and whose *taharah* process on day seven marks the beginning of his *nezirut* of *tahara*, even without any *korbanot*.

### Baraita - Tahor/Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה טָהוֹר שֶׁנִּטְמָא — טָעוּן הַעֲבָרַת שֵׂעָר וַהֲבָאַת צִיפֳּרִין, מִי שֶׁהָיָה טָמֵא מִתְּחִלָּה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָעוּן הַעֲבָרַת שֵׂעָר וַהֲבָאַת צִיפֳּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues its analysis of this *halakha*. For one might have explained differently: **And are** these **matters,** the ruling that such a nazirite is exempt, **not** inferred with an ***a fortiori* inference** to reach the opposite conclusion: **Just as a** nazirite who was **pure** from the outset and **who** subsequently **became impure requires hair removal and the bringing of birds,** if **one was impure from the outset,** as he took a vow of naziriteship while in a cemetery, **is it not logical that he should require hair removal and the bringing of birds?**

The *gemara* resolves this with a decisive *baraita*. The verse states *v'timei rosh nizro* — the head of his *nezirut* became *tamei* — and the *baraita* explains: *b'tahor she'nitma hakatuv midaber*, the verse is speaking about a *nazir tahor* who became *tamei*. It is precisely such a person who requires *ha'avarat se'ar v'hava'as tziporin* — the removal of hair and the bringing of the bird-offerings. And it concludes: *u'fatar et haNazir bakever* — this formulation exempts the *nazir* who accepted his *nezirut* in a graveyard, who does not require the shaving of the hair or the bringing of the birds.

The *baraita* then acknowledges that one might have constructed a *kal vachomer* in the other direction.

### Kal Vachomer Refuted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְטִמֵּא רֹאשׁ נִזְרוֹ״, בְּמִי שֶׁהָיָה טָהוֹר וְנִטְמָא הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, שֶׁיְּהֵא טָעוּן הַעֲבָרַת שֵׂעָר וַהֲבָאַת צִיפֳּרִין, וְלִפְטוֹר אֶת הַנָּזִיר בְּקֶבֶר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore **the verse states: "And he defiles his consecrated head,"** indicating that **the verse speaks** only **of one who was a pure** nazirite **and** later **became impure,** and **that** only **he requires hair removal and the bringing of birds. And** the verse serves **to exempt the nazirite** who vowed while **in** a place of **a grave,** who was ritually impure from the outset. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from this** that a nazirite who was ritually impure from the outset does not have to shave; this answers the question of Rav Ashi.

The *kal vachomer* would run as follows: if a *nazir tahor* who subsequently became *tamei* — someone who at least began on the right foot — requires the full process of shaving and bird-offerings, then how much more so should someone who accepted *nezirut* from the outset as *tamei*, having never properly begun, be required to undergo this process before commencing a *nezirut* of *tahara*? This is not a frivolous argument. The logic is that the harder it is to begin, the more rigorous the entry requirements should be.

To this the *baraita* responds: *talmud lomar v'timei rosh nizro* — the verse's very phrasing, describing someone whose head *became* *tamei* (implying it was previously *tahor*), confines the law to *b'mi shehayah tahor v'nitma hakatuv midaber*, one who was *tahor* and became *tamei*. The Torah is not making a logical point; it is limiting the law by description. Only that person requires the shaving and the birds.

This is a classic form of *midrash halacha*: the *derashah* is stated, and then the text steps back to explain why the *derashah* was necessary — because without it, one might have reasoned logically toward the opposite conclusion. Sometimes *midreshei halacha* present arguments that could cut in either direction, and the *pasuk* resolves the ambiguity. Here, only one direction of the *kal vachomer* is presented, but the structure is the same: logic might have led one way, and the *pasuk* redirects.

### Tamei - No Shaving

The conclusion is clear: the entire complex of rituals — *korbanot* and shaving alike — applies exclusively to someone who began *tahor*. A person who accepted *nezirut* while *tamei* simply undergoes the normal *taharah* process, and on day seven begins his *nezirut* of *tahara* without any shaving of the hair. *Shema mina.*

## When Restart Counting

### Tamei vs Tahor Alike

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא הָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין בֵּין טָמֵא שֶׁנָּזַר לְנָזִיר טָהוֹר שֶׁנִּטְמָא, אֶלָּא שֶׁטָּמֵא שֶׁנָּזַר — שְׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן, וְנָזִיר טָהוֹר שֶׁנִּטְמָא — אֵין שְׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן?
>
> **Translation:** § After resolving the question, the Gemara discusses additional *halakhot* involving a ritually impure person who took a vow of naziriteship. **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught this** *baraita* **that the Sages taught** in the previous discussion: **The difference between an impure** person **who took a vow of naziriteship and a pure nazirite who became ritually impure is only** the following *halakha*: **That** with regard to **an impure** person **who took a vow of naziriteship, his seventh** day of purification **counts** as part **of his tally** of the term of naziriteship. **But** with regard to **a pure nazirite who became impure, his seventh** day of purification **does not count** as part **of his tally.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a related question. It cites a teaching that distinguishes between two types of *nazir*: one who accepted *nezirut* while *tamei* (*tamei she'nazar*), and a *nazir tahor* who subsequently became *tamei* (*nazir she'nitma*). The only difference between them, according to this formulation, is that for the *tamei she'nazar*, his seventh day counts toward the new tally — since his *taharah* is complete on day seven, he begins counting from that point. By contrast, for the *nazir tahor she'nitma*, day seven does not count, because he is obligated to bring *korbanot* on day eight, and his new period of *nezirut* only begins on day eight, after the *korban* is brought.

Or so we have assumed throughout. The *gemara* now reveals that this position — that the *nazir tahor she'nitma* restarts only on day eight — is not unanimous.

### Two Tanaim Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: אֵין נְזִירוּת טׇהֳרָה חָלָה אֶלָּא עַד שְׁמִינִי. דְּאִי תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, הָאָמַר נְזִירוּת דְּטׇהֳרָה מִשְּׁבִיעִי הוּא דְּחָיְילָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Naziriteship of purity takes effect only from the eighth day.** When a nazirite becomes impure during his term, he begins to count his term of observing naziriteship in purity only on the day following his purification. **For if you say it** follows the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, didn't he say** that the **naziriteship of purity takes effect from the seventh** day of his purification process?

The *gemara* attributes the position we have been working with to *Rebbi* specifically. *Amar Rav Chisda Rebbi hi* — this is the opinion of *Rebbi*. The reason for saying so is that if the source were *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*, a different and previously unintroduced *tanna*, the picture would look quite different. We are now introduced to this dispute for the first time.

### R. Yose - Day 7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי וּמַאי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְקִדַּשׁ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״ — בְּיוֹם הֲבָאַת קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּיוֹם תִּגְלַחְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** the statement of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and what is** the statement of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** that Rav Ḥisda referred to? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a ritually impure nazirite who underwent the purification rite: **"And he shall sanctify his head on that day"** (Numbers 6:11). This means that he renews the sanctity of his hair growth, i.e., begins observing his naziriteship in purity, **on the day of the bringing of his offerings,** the eighth day of his purification. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** This means that he renews the sanctity of his hair growth **on the day of his shaving,** the seventh day of his purification.

*Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda* holds a fundamentally different position: *amar nezirut detahara mishvi'i chayla* — the *nezirut* of *tahara* takes effect from day seven, the very day the *nazir* becomes *tahor*. Even in the classic case of a *nazir tahor* who became *tamei*, even though the *korbanot* are brought on day eight, the resumption of his *nezirut* of *tahara* begins immediately upon becoming *tahor* on day seven. The *korban* obligation exists independently; the restarting of the count is tied to *taharah*, not to the *korban*.

The source for this dispute is a *baraita* interpreting the phrase *v'kidesh et rosho bayom hahu*.

### Pesuk - Day 7 Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: נָזִיר שֶׁנִּטְמָא טוּמְאוֹת הַרְבֵּה — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד, מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: נְזִירוּת טׇהֳרָה מִשְּׁבִיעִי חָיְילָא. וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי. (וּמַנִּי, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא) כֵּיוָן דְּלֹא יָצָא שָׁעָה הָרְאוּיָה לְהָבִיא קׇרְבָּן — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a related *halakha*: **And that which we learned** in a mishna (*Karetot* 9a): If **a nazirite became impure** by contracting **many** consecutive **impurities, he brings only one** set of **offerings. Who taught** that? **Rav Ḥisda said: It is Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** the **naziriteship of purity takes effect from the seventh** day of his purification process. **And you find this,** that he contracts ritual impurity several times consecutively, in a case **where he became ritually impure on the seventh** day **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the seventh** day. **And whose** opinion is it? **It is** that of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda. Since** the **appropriate time to bring an offering had not** yet **arrived,** as all agree that his offerings are only brought on the eighth day, if he became ritually impure a second time **he is obligated** to **bring only one** set of **offerings.**

Returning to the *pesukim*: the Torah describes the shaving on day seven (*v'gilach rosho b'yom taharato b'yom hashvi'i yegalhenu*), then on day eight the *korbanot* are brought (*u'vayom hashmini yavi shetei torim*), after which the *kohen* atones, and then: *v'kidesh et rosho bayom hahu* — he sanctifies his head on that day. At first read, "that day" plainly refers to day eight, the day being discussed. *Rebbi* reads it this way: *divrei Rebbi* — the day he brings his *korbanot*. *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda* reads it differently: *beyom tiglachto* — no, "that day" refers back to the day spoken of earlier, namely day seven, the day of shaving. He reads *bayom hahu* as a reference to the previously mentioned day, not the one currently under discussion.

This is admittedly not the *pshat* of the *pasuk*, and the *gemara* will address this. But before unpacking the precise derivation, it ties the dispute into a well-known *mishnah*.

### Multiple Tumos

The *mishnah* states: *nazir shenitma tumos harbe* — a *nazir* who became *tamei* many times — *eino mevi ela korban echad*, brings only one *korban*. This requires explanation, because intuitively, each separate act of becoming *tamei* after having been *tahor* ought to generate its own *korban* obligation. The question is: under what circumstances is it possible to have multiple episodes of *tumah* and *tahara* and yet incur only one *korban*?

*Rav Chisda* says: this *mishnah* reflects the opinion of *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*, for whom *nezirut detahara* begins on day seven. The scenario is constructed as follows.

### Day 7 Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי תֵּימָא רַבִּי הִיא, אִי דְּנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי (וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי) — כּוּלְּהוּ טוּמְאָה אֲרִיכְתָּא הִיא. וְאִי דְּנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי, וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי — הֲרֵי יָצְתָה שָׁעָה שֶׁרְאוּיָה לְהָבִיא קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **For if you say it** follows the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, how can this mishna be explained? **If** it deals with a nazirite **who became ritually impure on the seventh** day, **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the seventh** day, **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the seventh** day, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would hold that **all these** impurities **are** in fact one **long ritual impurity,** because he never reached the eighth day, which would renew his observance of naziriteship in ritual purity, so the mishna would not refer to this as a case of multiple impurities. **And if** the mishna is referring to a nazirite **who became ritually impure on the eighth** day **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the eighth** day, the **appropriate time to bring an offering had** already **arrived,** and if he becomes ritually impure at that stage, it is not viewed as a continuation of the previous ritual impurity, and he is obligated to bring another set of offerings.

The *nazir* became *tamei*, underwent the seven-day *taharah* process with the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes on days three and seven, became *tahor* on day seven, and then became *tamei* again — all on day seven itself. According to *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*, the moment he became *tahor* on day seven, his *nezirut detahara* restarted. He then became *tamei* again, meaning all the conditions for a second *korban* obligation would seem to be in place: he was genuinely a *nazir tahor* who became *tamei*. Yet the *mishnah* says only one *korban*. The explanation: *keivan de'lo yatza sha'ah re'uyah l'havi korban* — since the time fit for bringing a *korban* has not yet arrived. The *korban* is brought on day eight. On day seven, even after restarting *nezirut*, there is no *chiyuv korban* yet. Therefore, regardless of how many times he became *tahor* and *tamei* on day seven, only one *korban* is ever incurred.

The scenario is necessarily one where the *taharah* and subsequent *tumah* occur on day seven of one cycle, followed by another day-seven repetition of the same pattern. It is not literally the identical calendar day repeated, but successive cycles in which the action occurs on the seventh day before day eight's *korban* time arrives. The key structural point is: he entered a genuine state of *nezirut detahara*, became *tamei*, yet never reached day eight, and so the *korban* obligation from the first cycle was never yet triggered. Under this account, multiple such cycles yield only one *korban*.

## Rabbi - Day 8 View

### Why RY Yose Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? אָמַר קְרָא ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו מֵאֲשֶׁר חָטָא עַל הַנָּפֶשׁ״, וַהֲדַר ״וְקִדַּשׁ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ״. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִם כֵּן — לֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְקִדַּשׁ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda: **What is the reason for Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's ruling that he begins counting only from the eighth day? The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "And the priest shall prepare one for a sin-offering, and the other for a burnt-offering, **and make atonement for him, for he sinned by the soul"** (Numbers 6:11), **and** the same verse **again** states immediately afterward: **"And he shall sanctify his head."** This shows that the naziriteship of purity commences only after he has brought his offerings. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** says in response: **If so,** if the verse intended to teach us that his naziriteship starts only on the eighth day, **let the verse** merely **state: "And he shall sanctify his head,"** since that phrase addresses the bringing of his offerings, which is on the eighth.

The *gemara* now asks: why must we say this *mishnah* goes like *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*? Why not say it goes like *Rebbi*? To make it work for *Rebbi*, one would need to construct a scenario where the *nazir* genuinely restarted his *nezirut detahara* — which for *Rebbi* requires entering day eight — and then became *tamei* again, yet still without having yet incurred a *korban* obligation. Is there such a window?

The suggestion is: day eight has two parts — night and day. *Korbanot* are brought only during the day. But for *Rebbi*, who says the restart occurs simply when the time of the *korban* arrives, not when the *korban* is actually brought — perhaps entering the nighttime of day eight suffices to restart the counting, even though the *korban* itself cannot be brought until daybreak. If so, one could construct a scenario where the *nazir* enters night of day eight, restarts his *nezirut detahara*, and then becomes *tamei* before sunrise — before the time when he would be obligated in a *korban*. That would be multiple *tumot* and one *korban*, even for *Rebbi*.

The *gemara* presses on this: since *Rav Chisda* did not offer this solution and instead attributed the *mishnah* to *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*, it would appear he holds that the nighttime does not function as a separate window in the way this suggestion assumes. There is a deeper principle at stake here, which *Rav Adda bar Ahava* will articulate.

The underlying principle bears some unpacking. According to *Rebbi*, the reason the *nazir* restarts his count on day eight is that day eight is the *zman* — the time designated for bringing the *korban*. It is not that the act of bringing the *korban* itself triggers the restart; it is that day eight is recognized as the moment when this transition becomes operative, because it is the time of the *korban*. Now, what is the relationship between the *nezirut detahara* restart and the *korban* obligation? For *Rebbi*, they are tied to the same conceptual anchor: the *zman korban*, the time designated for the *korban*. This is the key insight that *Rav Adda bar Ahava* makes explicit.

The entire thrust of the proposed scenario — that night and day might be "out of sync" with respect to restart of *nezirut* and *chiyuv korban* — rests on the assumption that these two things could come apart. But they cannot, because for *Rebbi* both are functions of the same underlying concept: the time of the *korban*.

### Night/Day Sync

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָא בְּהָא תַּלְיָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ לֵילְיָא מְחוּסַּר זְמַן, אֵימַת מִיחְזֵי לְקׇרְבָּן — לְצַפְרָא, נְזִירוּת נָמֵי לָא חָיְילָא עַד צַפְרָא. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ לֵילְיָא אֵינוֹ מְחוּסַּר זְמַן — נְזִירוּת טׇהֳרָה חָיְילָא מֵאוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** This conclusion is incorrect, as even if Rav Ḥisda holds that the night is considered to be premature, he could not establish the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, as the offerings and his new term of naziriteship **depend on each other: If you say night is** considered **premature, when is** he **fit to** bring his **offerings?** Only **in the morning.** Accordingly, **naziriteship also does not take effect until the morning. And if you say night is not** considered **premature,** and he is already obligated in his offerings at night, in that case **naziriteship of purity takes effect from the evening,** which means that the ritual impurity he suffered at night renders him obligated to bring an additional set of offerings. Consequently, the mishna does not follow the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, whether night is considered premature or not, and nothing can be inferred from here with regard to Rav Ḥisda's opinion in that matter.

*Amar Rav Adda bar Ahava ha b'ha talya* — these two matters are bound together. The reason *nezirut detahara* restarts on day eight for *Rebbi* is precisely because day eight is considered the time of the *korban*. It follows inescapably that the two — restart of *nezirut* and *chiyuv korban* — track each other identically. They move together; they cannot come apart.

If one holds that nighttime is *mechusar zman* — that it is still considered lacking in time, meaning the *korban* time has not yet truly arrived — then on that view, neither the *korban* obligation nor the *nezirut* restart has kicked in at night. Both are deferred to the daytime. And if one holds that nighttime is not *mechusar zman* — that the time of the *korban* is already considered present at night — then both the *korban* obligation and the *nezirut* restart take effect at night. Either way, they are synchronized. The attempt to exploit a gap between them — where the *nezirut* restarts at night but the *korban* obligation only kicks in by day — is structurally impossible. *Ha b'ha talya*: they are interdependent. No arbitrage is available.

### R. Day 8 Refined

This brings *Rebbi*'s position into sharper focus. *Rebbi* holds that the *nazir* restarts his count on day eight — not because the *korban* was actually brought, but because day eight is the time designated for bringing the *korban*. Whether that time begins at nightfall or at sunrise depends on one's general position regarding the status of the night preceding a daytime *korban* — but in either case, the restart and the *chiyuv* are always in step with each other. The *nazir* can never find himself in a state where his *nezirut detahara* has restarted but he has not yet incurred the *chiyuv korban*. Consequently, for *Rebbi*, it is impossible to construct a scenario on day eight where multiple *tumot* yield only a single *korban* obligation in an interesting way. Any scenario where the *nazir* is *tamei* again on day eight is either trivial (he never truly restarted) or incorrect (he has already incurred the *korban* obligation and the second *tumah* generates a second obligation). Only *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda*, for whom the restart occurs on day seven while the *korban* time is not until day eight, can generate the genuine gap that makes the *mishnah*'s ruling non-trivial.

## Korban Requirement

### Multiple Tumos Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: נִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד. נִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי, וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי — מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. מַתְחִיל וּמוֹנֶה מִיָּד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:8) concerning **the** matter of a nazirite who contracted ritual impurity many times **itself:** If a nazirite became impure, and then **became impure on the seventh** day of his purification process, **and again became impure on the** following **seventh** day of his purification process, **he brings only one** set of **offerings.** If **he became impure on the eighth** day, **and again became impure on the** following **eighth** day, **he brings** a set of **offerings for each and every** time he became impure. Nevertheless, he **begins counting** his ritually pure naziriteship **immediately** on the eighth day, even if he has not yet brought his offerings. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

The *gemara* now returns to the *baraita* itself — introduced with the word *gufa* — and examines the full spectrum of opinions on when exactly the *nazir* restarts his count after becoming *tamei*. The text under discussion: *nitma beshvii v'chazar v'nitma beshvii*, became *tamei* on the seventh and again on the seventh — one *korban* only. *Nitma bishmini v'chazar v'nitma bishmini* — became *tamei* on the eighth and again on the eighth — a *korban* for each one. The debate within this *baraita* centers on the following: when does the counting restart?

*Matchil u'moneh miyad* — he begins counting immediately. This, following the *gra*'s emendation which removes the word *Eliezer*, is *divrei Rebbi*. And we now encounter the full formulation.

### Chachamim - Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד עַל הַכֹּל, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ. הֵבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ וְנִטְמָא, וְהֵבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ וְנִטְמָא — מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. הֵבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ וְלֹא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ — מוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** If he had not yet brought his offerings on the eighth day when he became impure, it is considered one long period of ritual impurity, and he brings **one** set of **offerings for all** the times he became impure, **until he has brought his sin-offering** at the end of his period of ritual impurity. It is only then that he can begin counting his next term of naziriteship. Therefore, if he **brought his sin-offering and** then **became impure, and** again **brought his sin-offering and** then again **became impure, he brings** a set of **offerings for each and every one. If he brought his sin-offering and did not** yet **bring his guilt-offering,** he begins to **count** his term of naziriteship in ritual purity.

The *chachamim* take a different position: *korban echad al hakol ad sheyavi chatato* — one *korban* covers everything, until he brings his *chatas*. Only once the *chatas* is brought does the counting restart. If he becomes *tamei* again after bringing his *chatas*, even before bringing the *olah* or the *asham*, that generates a new *korban* obligation — because the *chatas* already restarted the count.

To understand the structure of this debate: there are three *korbanot* involved — two birds (*chatas* and *olah*) and a lamb (*asham*). The *chachamim* maintain that the *chatas* is the pivotal *korban* for purposes of restarting the *nezirut*. The *olah*, as will be explained, is not central to any *taharah* or status-change process; it is universally understood to be a voluntary gift-offering (*doron be'alma*) and requires no special verse to exclude it from being a necessary prerequisite. The *chatas*, by contrast, is precisely the type of *korban* that marks a change of status, and the *chachamim* hold that without it, the *nezirut detahara* does not resume.

The practical formulation makes this clear. *Heivi chataso v'nitma* — he brought his *chatas* and became *tamei* — *heivi chataso v'nitma* — brought his *chatas* and became *tamei* again — *meivi korban al kol echad v'echad*. Each *tumah* after a *chatas* has been brought generates its own full obligation. Before the *chatas* is brought, no matter how many times the cycle recurs, it remains a single process with a single *korban*.

To summarize *Rebbi* versus *chachamim* cleanly: *Rebbi* says the restart occurs on day eight regardless of whether any *korban* was actually brought — the arrival of day eight's *zman korban* is what matters. The *chachamim* say the restart occurs only when the *chatas* is actually brought. A *nazir* who reached day eight but did not yet bring his *chatas* has not yet restarted his count. Additionally: *heivi chataso v'lo heivi ashamo moneh* — even if the *nazir* brought only his *chatas* and has not yet brought his *asham*, he begins counting. The *chatas* suffices; the *asham* is not required to trigger the restart.

### R. Yishmael - Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ עִיכְּבַתּוּ, כֵּן אֲשָׁמוֹ מְעַכְּבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: Just as** not bringing **his sin-offering precludes him** from starting his naziriteship in ritual purity, **so** too, not bringing **his guilt-offering precludes him** from starting his naziriteship in ritual purity, and if he became impure again before he brought his guilt-offering, he only brings one set of offerings for all his impurities.

*Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* disputes this final point. *Keshem shechatasо ikvato, kach ashamo me'akvo* — just as the *chatas* holds him back from restarting, so too does the *asham* hold him back. Both the *chatas* and the *asham* are the central *korbanot* of *taharah*-change processes — appearing in the *taharah* of the *metzora*, the *zavah*, and similar cases — and *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that both must be brought before the counting resumes. Only the *olah* is dispensable, because an *olah* is never structurally central to any transition of status.

Thus *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position is that the *nazir* must bring both the *chatas* and the *asham* before his *nezirut detahara* restarts. The *olah* he may defer; the other two he may not.

### Chachamim Pesuk Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אָמַר קְרָא ״וְקִדַּשׁ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו. וְרַבָּנַן: ״הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** After having quoted the *baraita*, the Gemara proceeds to analyze the three opinions: **Granted, according to Rabbi Eliezer,** his reason is that **the verse states: "And he shall sanctify his head on that day"** (Numbers 6:11), which indicates: **Even though he has not brought his offerings,** the eighth day determines the start of his ritually pure naziriteship. **And the Rabbis** agree that the phrase "on **that** day" is extra, and it teaches that he begins counting from that day **even though he has not brought his guilt-offering,** but he does not begin counting until after bringing his sin-offering.

The *gemara* now seeks the *pesuk*-based grounding for each of these positions. For *Rebbi*: *amar kra v'kidesh et rosho bayom hahu* — the verse says "on that day," which implies the day itself, independent of the *korbanot* being brought. *Af al pi shelo heivi korbanotav* — even without having brought the *korbanot*, the day itself is the trigger.

For the *chachamim*, the verse *bayom hahu* is also relevant, but it functions differently. They agree that the day has significance — specifically that it exempts the *olah* from being necessary. The phrase "on that day" tells them that the day matters, which they interpret as meaning: the *asham*, which comes later in the sequence, is not required. But the *chatas* — which is the essential *taharah korban* — is still required. *Olah lo baei miuta* — you need no special verse to exclude the *olah*; it is self-evidently non-central. So the *chachamim* read *bayom hahu* as limiting the requirement to exclude the *asham*, but insisting on the *chatas*.

A *baraita* provides the *chachamim*'s derivation: the verse states *v'hizir l'Hashem et yemei nizro* — he shall re-dedicate the days of his *nezirut* — and then, *v'hevi keves ben shnato l'asham* — and bring the lamb as an *asham*. The sequence is: rededicate, then bring the *asham*. This teaches that normally one might have assumed the *asham* was also a prerequisite, since *ashamot* throughout the Torah are generally central to *taharah* processes. Yet the order here — rededication first, *asham* afterward — shows that the *asham* is not a *me'akev*: *af al pi shelo hevi, hizir* — even without bringing the *asham*, the rededication of *nezirut* has already taken effect.

### R. Yishmael Pesuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, ״הָהוּא״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר לָךְ: ״הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא עוֹלָתוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן: עוֹלָה לָא בָּעֵי מִיעוּטָא — דּוֹרוֹן בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** according to **Rabbi Yishmael, why do I** need the phrase "on **that** day"? According to his opinion the issue depends on the offerings, not the day. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael could have **said to you:** The phrase "on **that** day" teaches that he can begin counting **even though he has not brought his burnt-offering,** as he agrees that not bringing the burnt-offering does not preclude him from counting his naziriteship. **And the Rabbis** respond to this: **A burnt-offering does not require a limitation** in the text to teach that its omission does not preclude the start of his naziriteship in ritual purity, since **it is merely a gift** and not part of his atonement process.

*Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* reads the same phrase differently. *V'hizir v'hevi* — "he shall rededicate and bring." *Eimatai hizir? Bi'zman shehevi* — when does he rededicate? When he has brought. The conjunction is not sequential (*and then bring*) but conditional (*when he has brought*). The rededication of *nezirut* occurs precisely at the moment that the full *korban* process — *chatas* and *asham* — is completed. The *olah* he concedes is not required, since *olah* is never central. But the two *korbanot* that define the transition of status must both be brought before counting may resume.

For *Rabbi Yishmael*, the phrase *bayom hahu* in the earlier verse serves only to exempt the *olah* — not to permit restarting before the *asham* is brought.

The *gemara* presses: if *Rabbi Yishmael* reads the sequence in this way, what does he do with the implication of *bayom hahu*, which seems to emphasize the day itself? The answer is that *bayom hahu* tells him even without the *olah* one may restart — but not without the *chatas* and *asham*. That is the work the phrase does for him.

The result is four distinct positions on when *nezirut detahara* restarts: *Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Yehuda* — day seven, when *taharah* is achieved; *Rebbi* — day eight, when the *zman korban* arrives, regardless of whether the *korban* is brought; *chachamim* — when the *chatas* is brought; and *Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* — when both *chatas* and *asham* are brought.

## Tosafos on Gufa

### Gufa Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהִזִּיר לַה׳ אֶת יְמֵי נִזְרוֹ וְהֵבִיא כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ לְאָשָׁם״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ שֶׁכׇּל אֲשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁהֵן מְעַכְּבִין, יָכוֹל אַף זֶה מְעַכְּבוֹ —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason of the Rabbis** for ruling that only refraining from bringing his sin-offering precludes the start of his new count of naziriteship? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a nazirite who became ritually impure: **"And he shall consecrate to the Lord the days of his naziriteship, and he shall bring a lamb in its first year for a guilt-offering"** (Numbers 6:12). **What does this teach?** Doesn't he wait to begin the days of his naziriteship until after he has brought all of his offerings? Rather, **since we have found that all guilt-offerings that are** mentioned **in the Torah are indispensable** for atonement, and before he has brought his guilt-offering he is forbidden from partaking of any sacred offerings, one **might** have thought that not having brought **this** guilt-offering **also precludes him** from counting his ritually pure naziriteship,

*Tosafos* on 18b notes a textual issue with this usage and takes the opportunity to explain precisely what *gufa* means and when it is legitimately used.

*Tosafos* states: *lo garsinan gufa* — we do not read *gufa* here — because throughout *Shas*, *gufa* is only used in a specific context: when the *gemara* has previously cited a *memra* or a *baraita* in passing — *agav grira* — as part of another discussion, in order to extract a particular point from it, and then returns to that same text to analyze it in greater depth. The word *gufa* signals: let us now examine the body of that text properly, rather than just the piece we used earlier.

Here, by contrast, the *gemara* is not returning to a previously cited *baraita* or *memra* of an *amora*. It is referring to its own *stam* language — the *shakla v'tarya* of the *gemara* itself. For that, *gufa* is not the appropriate term.

### Gufa vs Amar Mar

*Tosafos* further distinguishes *gufa* from *amar mar*. Both serve the function of returning to an earlier text, but they reflect different situations. *Gufa* applies when the full *baraita* or *memra* was already quoted in its entirety, but only in passing — to extract a small point — and the *gemara* now wants to engage with the whole text in depth. *Amar mar* applies when only a single line of a text was quoted in the earlier discussion, and the *gemara* now quotes the fuller text in order to address it systematically.

The distinction, then, is not merely grammatical but structural: *gufa* returns to a complete text that was cited instrumentally; *amar mar* returns to a text of which only a fragment was previously cited. In both cases the *gemara* is returning to source material it has touched earlier; the difference lies in how much of that material was already on the table.
