# Bava Metzia 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Public/Private Domain

### Intro & Topic Shift

We arrive today at the final *daf* of *Bava Metzia*, 119. The *perek* in which we now find ourselves represents something of a pivot: we have shifted from the earlier discussions concerning the ground floor and the upper story — who bears responsibility when floors give way or structures fail — toward the broader terrain of neighbor law: walls and trees falling into adjacent properties, and the rights of individuals operating in the *reshus harabim*.

As was observed at the close of the previous session, *reshus harabim* is not a simple, undifferentiated neutral space that everyone shares solely as a thoroughfare. There are *reshuyos harabim* that function as through-streets, and there are those that function more as plazas — spaces where individuals may legitimately conduct their own affairs: preparing plaster, taking delivery of materials, passing goods along. Yet all of this must be done in a manner that respects the public's right of access and does not create hazards for others.

### Two Key Questions

Two distinct questions animate this entire area of law, and they will continue as a dominant theme into the opening of *Bava Batra*. The first is the question of freedom to act: what degree of latitude does a person have when operating in his own domain, or in the public domain, in ways that may cause indirect harm to another? Must I conduct myself so as to prevent all indirect damage to my neighbor, or does he bear the responsibility to protect himself — *al hamazik leharchik et atzmo o al hanizak leharchik et atzmo*? The second question is a *Bava Kamma*-style inquiry: even if I do have the right to act in a certain way, am I *chayav* to pay if someone is actually damaged as a result?

### Operating in Reshus Hara…

These two levels of analysis do not necessarily travel together. One might hold that a person is permitted to empty refuse into the *reshus harabim* because it creates no sufficient hazard — yet still hold him liable if injury results. Conversely, one might hold that such an act is forbidden because of the potential hazard — yet exempt the actor from payment if injury does occur, on the grounds that the harm was indirect. The *Gemara* has presented instances running in both directions: *potchin bevibar birshus harabim*, where even a licensed activity may generate liability. And indeed, this last *perek* of *Bava Metzia*, which really ought to have been the opening *perek* of *Bava Batra*, sits precisely at the intersection of private domain and public domain, exploring both the scope of permitted action and the consequences of damage after the fact.

## Abiyi's Position

### Three Tannaim Agree

The *Gemara* at this point on 118b records the following statement: *amar Abaye, Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel v'Rabbi Shimon, kulhu sevira lehu: kol makom she'natnu lo chachamim reshus v'hizik, patur.* Abaye — who characteristically gathers disparate *Tannaic* opinions and draws them under a single conceptual umbrella — declares that these three *Tannaim* all share the same fundamental position: wherever the *chachamim* have granted permission to perform a given act, one is exempt from liability if damage results. This is, as Abaye frames it, the coherent and reasonable resolution of the two-level question: if we are permitting you to engage in this activity, that permission carries with it an exemption from liability. Not every *Tanna* holds this, but these three do.

### Rabbi Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda had de'amran* — Rabbi Yehuda is the source already cited in a preceding *braita*, which established that one who acts *birshus* — with license — is *patur* from liability. The case of *Chanukah* and other contexts involving Rabbi Yehuda were already adduced.

### Rabban Gamliel

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel de'tnan*: Rabban Gamliel's position is derived from a *mishnah*. The *mishnah* addresses one who has been hired to build a wall in the *reshus harabim*: one may not leave building materials strewn in the street indefinitely, but must take delivery and build promptly. The *mishnah* further states that even if the worker has been authorized and licensed to build in the *reshus harabim*, he would nonetheless be *chayav* if someone is damaged. To this, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel responds: *af metaken hu et melachto lifnei shloshim yom* — one may even spend the entire month preceding construction preparing the site and staging materials there. Rashi notes that Rabban Gamliel does not explicitly state an exemption from liability, but the *Gemara* reads this latitude as implying *patur*: the word *af*, "even," reads almost as an exclamatory rejoinder to the position that one would be *chayav* — if you are going to tell me a person is liable even when acting with full authorization, I am going to tell you that this same person may prepare his worksite for a full month in advance. The generosity of that permission signals an accompanying exemption from liability.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon's position is drawn from a *mishnah* in *Bava Batra* concerning a case where one person occupies the ground floor and another the upper story — a familiar configuration from earlier in the *perek*. The *mishnah* states: *haya maamido ba'aliya, tzarich she'yehei tachtav maaziva shlosha tfachim* — if one places a wood-burning *tanur* on the upper floor, there must be at least three *tefachim* of plaster beneath it, so that the heat does not break through to the floor below. For a *kira*, which burns less intensely, one *tefach* suffices. The *mishnah* then states: *v'im hizik, meshalem mah she-hizik* — even if one has complied with all of these specifications, if a fire nonetheless results and damages the other party's portion of the building, one must pay. Rabbi Shimon dissents: *lo amru kol hashiurim elalu ela she'im hizik patur mi'leshalem* — the entire purpose of these measurements was only to establish that if one adhered to them and damage nonetheless occurred, one is exempt from payment.

### Two Levels Analysis

One might read Rabbi Shimon's formulation extremely literally — as if to say the only purpose of a building code is to ensure that the damaged party will ultimately be compensated, and that if a person deposits the full value of the adjacent property in escrow, he may ignore the specifications entirely. That reading, however, seems unreasonable. The more natural reading is that Rabbi Shimon is making a rhetorical point: obviously one has an obligation to avoid causing harm regardless of one's willingness to pay. But once one has acted within the parameters the *chachamim* established, having conducted oneself in the prescribed manner confers an exemption from payment if something goes wrong despite that compliance. This, then, is the critical two-level structure: the question of what I am *permitted* to do, because my actions may indirectly harm others even when I act within my own domain, is analytically distinct from the question of whether I am *chayav* to pay after the fact. Abaye's contribution is to observe that for these three *Tannaim*, the two questions do track one another: freedom to act entails exemption from liability.

## Worker Chain Liability

### Individual Stage Liabili…

The *Gemara* now turns to a *braita* that addresses liability across a chain of workers — and here, I confess, the full analysis resists easy resolution. The *braita* describes a sequence: *hachatzav shemasar lesatat* — the quarrier, who removes the stone from the mountain, hands it to the *satat*, the one who cuts and smooths it to the correct dimensions. *Hasatat chayav*. If the stone then slips from the *satat*'s hand and causes damage, he is *chayav*. This is presumably a case of *adam hamazik*: it does not matter who owns the stone; if I am holding it and it falls and injures someone, I am liable, much as one who is asked to hold a knife and it slips from his hand is liable for any resulting injury.

The chain continues: *satat shemasar lechamar* — the cutter hands the stone to the *chamar*, the donkey driver, who transports it. *Hachamar chayav*. Then: *chamar shemasar lekatef* — the donkey driver hands it to the *katef*, the porter at the worksite. *Hakatef chayav*. Then *katef shemasar lebanai* — the porter hands it to the *banai*, the builder doing the actual construction. *Habanai chayav*. And finally: *banai shemasar leadrichal* — the builder hands it to the *adrichal*, the foreman. *Headrichal chayav*.

This is a remarkably concrete image of an ancient worksite, each stage of the process visible and named. At each stage, the person currently in physical custody of the stone is the one held liable if damage results — though the precise *halachic* category varies depending on the facts. If the stone falls off the *chamar*'s wagon without being dropped from his hand directly, this is no longer straightforwardly *adam hamazik* — one begins to wonder whether it is *eish* or another *av nezikin* altogether, and whose stone it is in the first place. If the stone belongs to the public — if the entire construction project is a communal commission — then individual workers lack ownership, and the question of which *av nezikin* applies becomes genuinely complex.

### Joint Final Stage

The final line of the *braita* is the most striking: *vehiniach even al hadimus v'hizika, kulan chayavin leshalem* — once the stone has been placed upon the building itself, on the top row of stones, if damage results, *all* of the workers are jointly liable. This is surprising. The quarrier who long ago removed the stone from the mountain must now pay? If we are looking for a specific act of negligence — someone who handled the stone badly — the question is who that person is. And if nobody was negligent and it is simply an *ones*, then presumably nobody should be liable at all. Yet the *braita* says all of them pay jointly.

### Kablanut Distinction

The *Gemara* immediately raises a contradiction from another *braita*: *acharon chayav v'kulan pturim* — only the last person in the chain is *chayav*, and all the others are exempt. This resembles the principle from the case of multiple *shomrim* watching a well: when responsibility passes from person to person, it is the last custodian who is *chayav*. The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya, kan biskirut, kan bekablanut* — there is no contradiction, because the cases differ in their employment structure. *Biskirut* — hired labor — means each worker has been engaged individually for his specific task. In that configuration, responsibility is personal and sequential: whoever currently holds the stone is the responsible party, and when custody passes, so does liability. The last person before the stone is placed is therefore the one *chayav*. *Bekablanut* — contract work — means that the entire crew has been hired collectively to complete the whole job. In that configuration, the group as a whole is conceptually engaged for the entire project, and so once the stone is placed and no individual can be identified as the responsible party, they all share corporate liability.

### Corporate Responsibility

Rashi makes this explicit: *b'kablanut, kulan chayavin, heicha delo naflu miyad echad mehen, de'al kulan lishmor she'tehei yoshevet yafe* — when the stone did not fall from the hand of any one of them specifically, the duty to ensure it was properly placed rested on all of them jointly. This is, as Rashi frames it, a shared obligation of oversight, not merely individual physical custody. Whether *miyad* is meant literally — if it can be shown to have fallen from someone's hands directly, it reverts to individual *adam hamazik* liability — or whether Rashi means simply that once no single individual's negligence can be isolated, the joint standard applies, is itself a question worth probing.

### Strict Liability Parallel

One way to make sense of this is to view the *kablanut* case not as an ordinary application of the *arba avot nezikin* but as something closer to what modern law would call strict liability: a recognition that certain activities — particularly large-scale construction in a public thoroughfare — are inherently hazardous to the public, and that therefore the ordinary calculus of individual fault gives way to collective responsibility. The public hazard created by a building project in the *reshus harabim* may warrant a different framework, one in which the contractor-entity as a whole assumes the risk regardless of which worker can be pinpointed as the proximate cause. This reading would treat the *braita* as a deliberate carve-out from the standard *Bava Kamma* categories — and it is, as noted, a substantial *chiddush*, since *halacha* does not ordinarily recognize anything resembling a corporate entity or shared *shutafus* of this kind.

## Two Gardens

### Case Setup

The *mishnah* now introduces a new scenario: *shtei ginos zo agav zo, v'hayerak baintayim* — two gardens, one above the other, with vegetation growing in the space between them. Reuven owns the upper parcel with its soil; Shimon owns the lower parcel with its airspace directly beneath the overhang. A vegetable is growing — presumably from wild seed — rooted in Reuven's earth but extending into Shimon's airspace. To whom does it belong?

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir rules: *shel elyon* — it belongs to the upper owner, Reuven, from whose ground it grows. Rabbi Meir supports this: *im yirtzeh elyon likach et afaro, ein kan yerak* — Reuven could simply excavate his soil entirely, and the vegetable would cease to exist. This demonstrates that Reuven controls the very substrate from which the vegetable draws its life. The vegetable is his.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda rules: *shel tachton* — it belongs to the lower owner, Shimon, in whose airspace it grows. He counters symmetrically: *im yirtzeh tachton lemalot et ginato, ein kan yerak* — Shimon could fill his airspace with earth, and the vegetable would equally be destroyed. Both parties control the fate of the vegetable.

Rabbi Meir acknowledges this symmetry but draws his conclusion differently: *me'achar she'shneihem yecholim limchot zeh al zeh, ro'im meiheichan yerek zeh chai* — since each has the power to prevent the vegetable's existence, we ask the decisive question: from where does the vegetable actually live? And the answer is: from the ground. The roots draw nutrients from Reuven's earth, and that is what sustains it. Therefore it is Reuven's.

One might note that the case is somewhat misleadingly framed if the vegetation grows above ground — a tomato, for example, on a vine — because in that case the physical presence of the desired produce is entirely in Shimon's airspace, not merely rooted in Reuven's earth. The *mishnah* does not draw this distinction, but the *Gemara* will address it shortly through Rava's intervention.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava clarifies the scope of the dispute: *be'ikaro kulei alma lo pligi de'elyon havei, ki pligi benofo* — regarding what grows below ground, all opinions agree that it belongs to Reuven. If it is a carrot, there is no disagreement; its edible portion is physically located in Reuven's earth and draws from Reuven's earth, and so it is unambiguously his. The dispute is specifically about *nofo* — the foliage, the above-ground portion, which is physically present in Shimon's airspace while drawing its life from Reuven's ground. That is the genuine contested case, and it is to that case that the competing principles of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda apply.

## Roots vs. Location

### Nof After Ikar

The *Gemara* now articulates the underlying principles. Rabbi Meir holds *shadi nof batar ikar* — one "throws" the canopy after the roots. Whatever grows from a root system is legally identified with the location of those roots, regardless of where the above-ground growth physically extends. The produce or foliage is deemed to belong to the same domain as the roots from which it springs.

This principle has broad application in the *Gemara*. A classic example: a tree whose roots are in *Chutz LaAretz* but whose trunk and branches extend into *Eretz Yisrael*, bearing fruit in *Eretz Yisrael*'s airspace. Does the fruit belong to *Eretz Yisrael* or to *Chutz LaAretz* for purposes of *ma'asrot* or similar obligations? Do we say *shadi nof batar ikar* and attribute the fruit to the location of the roots in *Chutz LaAretz*? Or do we say the fruit grew in *Eretz Yisrael*'s airspace and is therefore subject to *Eretz Yisrael*'s obligations? Another playful application: a fugitive fleeing to an *ir miklat* jumps into a tree — is he considered to have entered the city of refuge if the trunk is within its boundaries but his body is suspended at a height, or if the roots are within while he hangs in the tree over the border? The answer may well depend on what legal question is being asked.

In our case, the question is ownership. Rabbi Meir applies *shadi nof batar ikar* to conclude that since the roots are in Reuven's ground, the above-ground growth belongs to Reuven as well.

### Yehuda Rejects

Rabbi Yehuda rejects *shadi nof batar ikar* in this context. For Rabbi Yehuda, the above-ground foliage is physically present in Shimon's airspace — and Shimon owns that airspace. The produce grew there; it is his.

### Tree Braita

The *Gemara* then introduces a *braita* that is parallel in some respects, though Tosafot notes it is not an exact analogue. The case: one person owns a tree and another person owns the land in which that tree is planted — the tree-buyer has no land rights whatsoever. New growth sprouts from the tree's root system. *Hayotzei min hageza umin hasharashim, harei elu shel ba'al hakarka, divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that whether the new growth emerges from the trunk or from the roots, it belongs to the owner of the land. Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes: *min hageza, shel ba'al ha'ilan* — growth from the trunk belongs to the tree-owner, since it resembles the fruit and produce of the tree, which he owns; *min hasharashim, shel ba'al hakarka* — growth from the roots belongs to the land-owner, since the roots are part of the land.

The parallel to our *mishnah* is not perfect, as Tosafot observes: in the *braita*'s case, the tree-buyer has no air rights at all, whereas in our *mishnah*, Shimon does own his airspace. Still, a family resemblance is apparent: Rabbi Meir tends to root everything in the land — defaulting to the land-owner — while Rabbi Yehuda is more attentive to what grows above ground and to rights in the space above the earth.

### Orlah Parallel

A similar debate appears in the context of *orlah*. If a tree produces a new shoot — either from the trunk or from the roots — is it subject to the *orlah* prohibition as a new tree, or is it treated as a branch of the existing tree and therefore exempt? *Ilan hayotzei min hageza umin hasharashim chayav ba'orlah, divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that either way, it is a new tree subject to *orlah*. This reflects the same orientation: growth emerging from below, from the root system or trunk, is treated as a new entity growing from the ground. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: min hageza, patur; umin hasharashim, chayav* — from the trunk, it is like a branch or fruit of the existing tree and is exempt; from the roots, it is a new tree subject to *orlah*.

### Both Cases Needed

The *Gemara* asks: why is it necessary to state both disputes — the *orlah* case and the ownership case? *Tzrichi*. Were only the ownership case taught, one might say Rabbi Yehuda there awards the growth to the tree-owner as a leniency in a monetary matter — he prefers to recognize the tree-owner's rights — but in the realm of *issur*, where *orlah* is a prohibition, perhaps he would agree with Rabbi Meir and treat the growth as a new tree subject to *orlah*. Conversely, were only the *orlah* case taught, one might think Rabbi Meir is strict only because it is an *issur*, but in monetary law he might concede to Rabbi Yehuda. Both cases are therefore necessary, as each ruling might otherwise seem to be the product of its particular legal context rather than a consistent underlying principle.

## Access Determines

### Rabbi Shimon Rule

Rabbi Shimon's approach departs from both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda's conceptual frameworks and grounds itself in a physical and practical criterion: *kol she'ha'elyon yachol lifshot et yado velitol, harei zeh shelo, vehashe'ar shel tachton* — whatever the upper owner can reach out and take, it is his; the remainder belongs to the lower owner. Ownership is determined not by the abstract question of whether roots or airspace governs, but by the simple, immediate fact of physical access.

### Access Question

The school of Rabbi Yannai immediately qualifies this: access must be possible without endangering oneself — *ubvilvad shelo ye'anes*. One cannot say "if I dangled from a rope I could reach it" and thereby claim ownership. Unaided, reasonable physical access is the standard. A question then arises — attributed in one version to Rabbi Yannai, and in another to Rabbi Yirmiya: *magiya l'nofo v'eino magiya l'ikaro, magiya l'ikaro v'eino magiya l'nofo, mah?* What if Reuven can reach the above-ground foliage but not the underground root, or can reach the root but not the desired produce? What counts as the determinative "access" when the roots and the edible portion are in different locations?

### Teiqu

*Teiku* — the *Gemara* leaves this unresolved.

### Halacha Ruling

Notwithstanding the *teiku*, Efrayim Safra, the student of Reish Lakish, transmits a ruling in his teacher's name: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — the law follows Rabbi Shimon's physical access standard. This is perhaps somewhat surprising: of the three positions, Rabbi Shimon's feels the most pragmatic and the least conceptually grounded. The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda rests on a genuine and interesting theoretical question — does ownership of foliage follow the roots or the airspace? Rabbi Shimon sidesteps that question entirely in favor of a practical test. Yet Reish Lakish rules like him — perhaps precisely because, given the theoretical deadlock, pragmatism is the most workable resolution.

## Shabur Malka Story

### Praise of Shimon

The *Gemara* then reports: *va'amru kamei d'Shavor Malka* — they conveyed this ruling before *Shavor Malka*, and he responded: *Aperion namtei leRabbi Shimon* — words of praise and approbation for Rabbi Shimon.

### Rashi on Identity

Rashi addresses the identity of *Shavor Malka*. He writes: *li nireh Shavor Malka mamash, u'melech Paras haya, u'baki b'dinim* — in Rashi's view, this refers to the actual Persian king, *Shapur*, who was known to take an interest in law and had a close relationship with Shmuel. The *Gemara* elsewhere records conversations between *Shapur* and Shmuel. On Rashi's reading, someone shared Rabbi Shimon's position with this Persian king — not as a *halachic* ruling binding on him, but as an intellectual curiosity, a window into Jewish legal reasoning — and the king responded with admiration. Rashi notes an alternative reading: since Shmuel was so close to the real *Shavor Malka*, he was sometimes himself called by that name in the *Gemara*. But Rashi rejects this: *tmiha li* — it is difficult. If it were Shmuel, what would it mean to say "they told him" about a position in the *mishnah*? Would Shmuel not already know what Rabbi Shimon holds? Even granting that perhaps what they told Shmuel was specifically the ruling of Reish Lakish via Efrayim Safra, Shmuel could have praised Rabbi Shimon on his own merits without waiting to hear that Reish Lakish had ruled accordingly. And notably, it is Rabbi Shimon who is praised, not Reish Lakish. Rashi therefore maintains that *Shavor Malka* here refers to the Persian king himself.

### Afiryon Meaning

The word *aperion* — or in alternate textual traditions, *apria* — admits of several interpretations. Rashi reads the *yod-nun* suffix as possessive — "our" — and understands the phrase as: our favor, our acknowledgment of esteem, should go to Rabbi Shimon; *yikabel chen me'itanu al davar zeh*. The *Aruch*, however, preserves a different *girsa*: *hava lei apria leRabbi Shimon*, reading *apria* as a cognate for something like a bier or litter, and glossing it as a Persian expression equivalent to *yashar kocho* — well done, strength to him. Other versions read it as a term of fruitfulness, from the root of *ben porat Yosef* — may scholars and sages as wise as he flourish and multiply. The *psukim* also offer a suggestive resonance: *aperion asa lo ha'melech Shlomo* — the litter that King Solomon made for himself — though the *Gemara* does not explicitly invoke that verse here.

### Theological Note

There is something arresting about the fact that the final words of the entire tractate of *Bava Metzia* come from the mouth of a non-Jewish Persian king. Is there a message embedded in this editorial choice? *Dinei mamonot* — monetary law — is territory that secular legal systems occupy as well. Unlike prohibitions such as *lo talin pe'ulat sachir* or *ribbit*, which are unambiguously *mitzvot* grounded in *pesukim*, much of the commercial law of *Bava Metzia* and *Bava Batra* finds analogues in every civilized society. *Bava Batra*, which we are about to begin, is notable for having almost no *pasuk* to anchor its vast legal corpus; it is largely the *chachamim* developing a framework for interpersonal and commercial relations — not so much elaborating *mitzvot* as building a jurisprudence. How do we understand such law as *Torah*? Is it the same religious act to study a chapter on *nedunyon* or *shtarot* as it is to study *hilchos shechita* or *Shabbos*?

### Dine Mamon Status

The *Torah* itself seems to anticipate this question. *Rak am chacham v'navon ha'goy ha'gadol hazeh, ki mi goy gadol asher lo chukim umishpatim tzadikim* — the nations of the world will look upon Israel's statutes and judgments and recognize them as just. The endorsement of a foreign king for a *halachic* position is not presented as something embarrassing or incidental; it may be presented as a kind of vindication — that these laws, even when not tied to explicit *mitzvot*, carry an inherent justice recognizable to any thoughtful observer. The ending of *Bava Metzia* with such an endorsement may be a deliberate statement: that *dinei mamon* are not merely pragmatic rabbinic constructions, but laws whose inner logic reflects a genuine and universal *tzedek*.
