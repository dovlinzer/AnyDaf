# Meilah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Kedusha & Meilah

### Prev. Perek Review

The previous *perek* dealt extensively with questions about the stages of *kedusha*: once something is sanctified, *me'ilah* applies, and different stages — such as placement into a *keli* or *zerikat hadam* — affect what additional statuses become relevant. The new *perek* turns to examine cases where there is no *me'ilah*, because the sanctified item is no longer fit for the *mizbeach*.

### Pigul & Status

Before proceeding to these cases, it is worth recalling a foundational principle established at the very opening of the *masekhet*: once something is sanctified, even if it subsequently becomes unfit — for instance, through a thought of *pigul* or because the meat becomes *tamei* — *me'ilah* still applies. Indeed, it may apply for a longer duration than usual, since a *korban* that has become *pasul* never becomes *muttar lakohanim* the way normal meat does — at least regarding *kodshei kodashim*. The fact that it is *pasul* does not remove it from the category of *me'ilah*. Until something becomes *muttar* for *achila*, it remains subject to *me'ilah*. With this in mind, one can appreciate the significance of the cases to follow, where the Mishnah teaches that *me'ilah* does *not* apply.

## Chatat Shemetah

### Three Cases

The Mishnah at the bottom of 10b, which opens the new *perek*, presents the classic cases of a *chatat* that must be left to die — *chatat shemetah* — because it can no longer fulfill its function of *kapara*. Three standard examples are given: *vlad chatat* (the offspring of a *chatat*, since a *chatat* was a female animal and its offspring cannot serve the function of *kapara*); *tmurat chatat* (the *tmurah* of a *chatat*, since only the original animal can function for *kapara*, not its substitute); and *chatat she'metu be'aleha* (a *chatat* whose owners have died, so there is no longer any relevant *kapara* to be achieved). In all three cases, the ruling is *yamutu* — the animal must be allowed to die.

### Avra Shnata

Two additional cases of *chatat shemetah* depend more on circumstances. The first is *she'avar shnata ve'avda* — the animal became more than a year old, or it was lost and subsequently found to have a *mum*. The second scenario specifies that if *mishekippru haba'alim tamut* — if the owners have already achieved atonement through another offering — then the animal must also die, falling into the category of *chatat shemetah*.

### Unconditional vs. Condit…

One might ask: what distinguishes *avar shnata* from *nitkapper be'acher*? The critical difference is that the first two categories — *vlad chatat* and *tmurat chatat* — are inherently and permanently unfit for *kapara*, and the death of the owners likewise forecloses any possibility of *kapara* entirely. These are unconditional cases. *Avar shnata*, by contrast, is more like a *mum*: there is simply a problem that prevents bringing the animal in its current state, but one could wait until the animal actually develops a *mum* and then be *podeh* — redeem it. Similarly, if the animal was lost and then found with a *mum*, one could have been *podeh* it if the owners had not yet brought another *korban*. These later cases, therefore, represent scenarios where there was a genuine possibility of eventual use that was foreclosed by the owners having gone ahead and brought another offering. In both instances — the one that aged past its year, and the one that was lost — the operative factor is that the owners subsequently achieved *kapara* elsewhere, *nitkapper be'acher*, and only then does the animal fall into the *shemetah* category.

### Tmurah & Meilah Remain

The Mishnah at the top of 11a specifies the converse: if the owners have not yet achieved *kapara* — *ad shelo kipperu haba'alim* — the advice is *teira'eh ad shetistave ve'timacher*: wait for the animal to develop a *mum*, sell it, and use the proceeds to purchase another *korban*. In those cases, since the *kedusha* could eventually transfer to another viable *korban*, the animal *osa tmurah u'mo'alin bah* — it retains the capacity to make a *tmurah*, and *me'ilah* continues to apply. It should be noted that *me'ilah* does not require *kedushat korban* specifically; *kedushat damim* — where the value will ultimately be transferred into a *korban* — also falls within *me'ilah*. What *me'ilah* cannot tolerate is an animal that is entirely and definitively headed for destruction.

### Kodshei Hashem Removal

This brings us to the fundamental reason underlying the entire discussion. By contrast, in the cases where *yamutu* applies unconditionally, the ruling is *ve'lo nehenin ve'lo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit from the animal even rabbinically, and there is no *me'ilah*. Even a *korban* that has become *pasul* retains *me'ilah*; even *psulei hamukdashim* are treated with a certain sanctity within the framework of the *Beit HaMikdash*. But something that has been completely removed from the category of *kodshei Hashem* — condemned definitively to death with no possible redemptive use — exits the *me'ilah* framework entirely. As *Tosafot* on 12b puts it explicitly: *dekeivan de'azlei le'ibud lo shaikh behu me'ilah velo karinan behu kodshei Hashem* — since they are going to destruction, *me'ilah* is inapplicable and they are no longer called *kodshei Hashem*. The *mefareish* here notes that there may nevertheless be *me'ilah derabbanan* in such cases — but that question remains open for now. The principle is clear in either case: it is still rabbinically forbidden to derive benefit from such animals, even without *me'ilah*. One might ask what practical difference there is between a mere *issur derabbanan* of *hana'ah* and *me'ilah derabbanan*. Among the *nafka minot*: under *me'ilah*, even if there is no *korban* and no *chomesh*, one is at minimum required to restore the monetary value. Under a simple *issur hana'ah*, there is no such obligation.

### Gemara: Reisha vs. Seifa

The *Gemara*'s analysis of this *Mishnah* is brief and self-evident. It asks: *mai shna reisha de'lo kamaplig u'mai shna seifa de'kamaplig* — why does the first part list three cases without qualification, while the second part distinguishes based on whether *nitkapper be'acher* has occurred? The answer is that *reisha psika lei* — in the first three cases, there is simply no scenario where the circumstances could differ. *Vlad chatat* and *tmurat chatat* are never fit for *kapara* under any conditions; if the owners died, *nitkapper be'acher* becomes impossible. There is nothing to qualify. In the *seifa*, *lo psika lei* — the circumstances genuinely matter, and so the distinction must be spelled out.

### Meilah & Tmura Purposes

The *Gemara* then raises one further question: this same *Mishnah* appears in *Tmura* as well — why is it taught in both *masekhtot*? The answer is equally straightforward. The category of a *chatat* condemned to death carries two distinct *halachic* consequences: it cannot make a *tmurah*, and there is no *me'ilah*. *Tana hatam mishum tmurah* — it is taught there for the laws of *tmurah*; *tana hacha mishum me'ilah* — it is taught here for the laws of *me'ilah*.

One might wonder, as a practical matter, whether when the Mishnah advises waiting for the animal to develop a *mum* and then redeeming it, that represents the preferable course of action — since in the meantime the owners remain without *kapara*. The Mishnah does seem to present *teira'eh ad shetistave ve'timacher* as the prescribed path, implying that yes, waiting for the *mum* is indeed what one should do rather than immediately bringing another *korban*. This would mean the owner must continue without *kapara* while the animal waits, which is striking. It is a real tension the sources present without fully resolving.

## Nazirite Money

### Undetermined Money

The next *Mishnah* deals with a different category: *hamafrish ma'ot lineziruto* — one who sets aside a sum of money for his *nazir* offerings, without specifying which portion is designated for which of the three required *korbanot* (a *chatat*, an *olah*, and a *shelamim*). The ruling is *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit, and if one does, there is no *me'ilah*.

### Shelamim Default Reason

Why is there no *me'ilah*? Because *mipnei she'hen re'uyin lavo kullan shelamim* — in principle, the entire sum could have been used for a *shelamim*. One might initially have expected that since some portion of the money would necessarily have been designated for an *olah* or a *chatat* — offerings for which there is *me'ilah* — there ought to be at least partial *me'ilah* on that money. But since the owner never specified the allocation, one cannot say that any particular portion was certainly designated for an *olah* or *chatat*. He could theoretically have used the entire sum for a *shelamim* and then separately obtained funds for the other offerings. And since by a *shelamim* there is no *me'ilah* until after *zerikat hadam* — specifically on the *imurin* — undesignated *shelamim* money also carries no *me'ilah*.

This reflects a larger conceptual principle, already developed in the earlier *perek*, that the category of *kodshei kalim* is fundamentally different from *kodshei kodashim*. The distinction is not merely practical — it is not simply about what you are permitted to use. It is conceptual: *kodshei kodashim* is fully *kodshei Hashem*; *kodshei kalim*, by contrast, is characterized by *kodashim kalim mammon ba'alim* — in some sense it remains the property of its owners. This is why, for example, *me'ilah* does not apply to the meat of a *shelamim* before *zerikat hadam* even though that meat is *assur* to eat before that point — just as it is *assur* to eat the meat of *kodshei kodashim* before *zerikat hadam*, yet *me'ilah* applies in the latter case even before *zerikat hadam*. The meat of *kodshei kodashim* is fully *kodshei Hashem* and therefore falls under *me'ilah* until it becomes *muttar*; the meat of *kodshei kalim* is not fully *kodshei Hashem* and therefore only the *imurin*, after they have been designated, fall under *me'ilah*. This same conceptual framework explains why money set aside for a *shelamim* — or money that could be applied to a *shelamim* — carries no *me'ilah*.

### Death Becomes Olah

If the *nazir* dies before completing his *nezirut*, and he had left behind a pile of undesignated money (*ma'ot stumin*) set aside for his *nazir* offerings, the ruling is *yipelu linedavah* — the entire sum is applied to an *olah*. The reasoning, one might understand conceptually, is that a *chatat* cannot be brought because the owner is deceased; the *shelamim* of a *nazir* is somewhat distinctive to the ritual conclusion of *nezirut* itself; whereas an *olah* is simply a generic *stam olah*, with nothing that uniquely ties it to the living *nazir*'s ritual. The money therefore defaults to an *olah*.

### Explicit Money Allocation

If, however, the *nazir* had designated his money explicitly — separate piles for his *chatat*, his *olah*, and his *shelamim* — and then died, each pile is treated according to its designation.

### Chatat Money Status

The money designated for the *chatat* — *demei chatat yolichu l'Yam HaMelach* — is destroyed, effectively analogous to a *chatat she'metu be'aleha*. The same ruling follows: *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — no benefit, and no *me'ilah* — just as with the *chatat shemetah* discussed above.

### Olah Money Meilah

The money designated for the *olah* — *demei olah yavi'u olah* — is used to bring an *olah*. Unlike the *chatat* and *asham*, an *olah* does not require the specific atonement of its owner; sanctified property designated for an *olah* can be brought even after the owner's death. Consequently, *u'mo'alin bahen* — *me'ilah* applies to this money.

### Shelamim Post-Death

The most complex case is the money designated for the *shelamim*. In principle, a *shelamim* can be brought after its owner's death — unlike a *chatat* or *asham*, it does not require the owner to be present for *kapara*. So the ruling is *yavi'u shelamim* — a *shelamim* is brought. However, because this money was originally sanctified as the *shelamim* of a *nazir*, it retains a quasi-*shalmei nazir* character, resulting in a split *halakha*. On the one hand, *ne'echalin l'yom echad* — it is eaten only for one day, as befits *shalmei nazir*, rather than two days like an ordinary *shelamim*. On the other hand, *ve'ein te'unim lechem* — the bread accompanying *shalmei nazir* is not brought. The reason for this distinction is that the Torah specifies the bread is brought *al kapei hanazir* — on the hands of the *nazir* himself — making the bread intrinsically bound to the *nezirut* ritual and the living presence of the *nazir*. Without the *nazir*, and without the concluding rite of *nezirut*, there is no bread. Yet the *kedusha* of the *korban* itself is sufficient to impose the one-day eating restriction of *shalmei nazir*. It is a genuine split: quasi-*shalmei nazir* in some respects but not others.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

*Reish Lakish* challenges the earlier ruling regarding undesignated *nazir* money by asking: why does the *Mishnah* not teach similarly that one who designates money for *kinim* — bird offerings — incurs *lo nehenin velo mo'alin*, since the money could potentially be applied in a way that would involve a bird not presently fit for the *mizbeach*? This question leads directly into the next *Mishnah*'s discussion of birds.

---

## Bird Age Requirement (II)

### Tosafot: Young Bird Use

*Tosafot* reframes *Reish Lakish*'s challenge in a way that makes it more compelling. *Reish Lakish*'s question cannot simply mean that the money is unsubject to *me'ilah* because one could use it to buy an incorrectly aged bird — that would be an absurd argument, since one obviously should use the money correctly. Rather, *Tosafot* clarifies, *Reish Lakish* is asking specifically about the case of a *tor* that is too young. If one uses the money to buy a *tor she'lo higia zemano* — a dove that has not yet reached its proper age — one has not done anything wrong, because one could simply wait until the bird matures and then bring it. But at the moment of purchase, there would be no *me'ilah*, since a too-young *tor* itself has no *me'ilah*. So the question becomes: why not say that the undetermined *nazir* money also has no *me'ilah*, since it is at least possible that the money will be used to purchase a currently immature *tor* that will later become fit?

---

## Nazirite Money (II)

### Rava's Response

*Rava* responds: *amrah Torah bimostumin havei shelamim* — the Torah is entirely comfortable with all of the undesignated *nazir* money being used for a *shelamim*, but *amrah Torah hada* — does one really think the Torah intends for one to purchase birds that are *she'einam re'uyin lamizbeach*, not presently fit for the *mizbeach*? The point is that when one designates money for a *korban*, we assume that the money's status corresponds to its proper and immediate use. The money is designated to purchase something that can be offered right away, and consequently *me'ilah* applies to that money. There is an implicit assumption of immediate fitness, which forecloses the theoretical argument that the money might be used for an as-yet-immature bird.

---

## Bird Age Requirement (II)

### Tor vs. Benei Yonah

With that background, the *Mishnah* governing bird offerings can be appreciated. There are two types of bird *korbanot*: *torim* (doves), which must be adult, and *benei yonah* (young pigeons), which must be young. If one sanctified a *tor* that has not yet reached its proper age, or *benei yonah* that have passed their proper age, the ruling is *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — no benefit and no *me'ilah*. One might have expected that, as with an animal that has a *mum*, there should still be *me'ilah* even if the bird cannot presently be used. Why do birds differ?

### No Pidyon for Birds

The answer is that there is no *pidyon* for birds — no possibility of redeeming them and transferring their *kedusha* to another animal or sum of money, even if the bird has a disqualifying defect or is a *mechusar zman*. Since there is no mechanism by which the *kedusha* can ever be realized or transferred, the situation of a wrong-aged bird is structurally identical to the *chatat shemetah*: it is *holekh le'ibud*, heading toward inevitable destruction, completely removed from the category of *kodshei Hashem*. Therefore *lo nehenin velo mo'alin*.

## Blood (Dam) Paradox

### Dam Initially No Meilah

*Rabbi Shimon* introduces a striking principle: *hadam kal bitchila ve'chamur besofo* — blood is more lenient at the outset and more strict at the end. Conversely, *nesachim chomer bitchilatan ve'kal besofo* — the wine libations are more strict at the outset and more lenient at the end. The case of the blood is genuinely startling. *Dam bitchilato ein mo'alin bo* — initially, before the blood has served its function on the *mizbeach*, there is no *me'ilah* by the blood. This is counterintuitive to the point of paradox: if anything seems to be *kulo laHashem*, it is the blood of the *korban*. How can there be no *me'ilah*?

### Dam Flow & Sale

After the blood is applied to the *mizbeach*, the excess blood on the floor was washed through drainage channels in the *Beit HaMikdash*, flowing out into the Kidron Valley. The Temple administration would then sell this blood to farmers for use as fertilizer — a practical arrangement for dealing with the large quantities of blood produced daily. The ruling in the *Mishnah* is that once this blood reaches the Kidron and is subject to sale, if one uses it without purchasing it from the Temple, *mo'alin bo* — there is *me'ilah*. This is paradoxical: before the blood served its purpose, there is no *me'ilah*; after it has done its job and flowed out to the Kidron, there is.

### Dam Derabbanan Meilah

One critical qualification, not made explicit in the *Gemara* here but clear from other sources, is that even when the *Mishnah* says *mo'alin bo* regarding blood in the Kidron, this *me'ilah* is only *derabbanan*. The reason is the principle of *na'aseit mitzvato* — once the blood fulfilled its function on the *mizbeach*, the mitzvah was completed, and it is a foundational rule that *ein lecha davar she'na'aseit mitzvato she'mo'alin bo*: nothing for which the mitzvah has been performed remains subject to *me'ilah*. Therefore the *me'ilah* in the Kidron can only be rabbinic. What makes the case of *dam* so extraordinary, then, is that before *zrikat hadam* — when the blood is at its most powerful ritual moment, about to achieve *kapara* — there is no *me'ilah* at all, not even *derabbanan*. After *na'aseit mitzvato*, there is rabbinic *me'ilah*. The pattern is the inverse of everything one would expect.

### Rabbi Meir vs. Chachamim

A *baraita* records a dispute directly on this point. *Rabbi Meir* holds *mo'alin bedamim* — there is *me'ilah* by blood. The *Chachamim* hold *ein mo'alin* — there is no *me'ilah*. The *Gemara* then presents three distinct explanations for the position of those who exempt blood from *me'ilah*.

### Ula: Service to Owner

*Ula* derives the exemption from a verse: the blood was given to you upon the *mizbeach* to atone — *lachem*, for you, meaning it is performing a service on your behalf. This somehow removes it from the category of *kodshei Hashem*, and consequently there is no *me'ilah*. This rationale is difficult to internalize. The entire concept of blood as *korban* seems to be the paradigm of something given wholly to God — the soul of the animal, as it were, offered entirely to the divine. Yet here the *pasuk* is read to say that its service is rendered to the benefit of the owner, and that very fact removes it from full *kodshei Hashem* status. The irony is difficult to fully resolve.

### R. Yishmael: Atonement P…

The school of *Rabbi Yishmael* offers a formulation that is conceptually somewhat clearer: *l'chaparah netativo velo lime'ilah* — I gave it for atonement, not for *me'ilah*. The *pasuk* is read as the Torah explicitly exempting blood from *me'ilah* because its entire purpose is to help the person achieve atonement. It is there to assist, not to cause additional stumbling through the prohibition of *me'ilah*. One might immediately ask: why not say the same about the entire *korban*? If the blood is exempt from *me'ilah* because it serves the owner's atonement, surely the meat and the *imurin* also serve the *kapara* process? The question does not receive a direct answer, and the tension remains unresolved.

### R. Yochanan: Blood Soul

*Rabbi Yochanan* derives the rule from a different verse: *ki hadam hu banefesh yichaper* — because the blood is the soul, and through it atonement is achieved. He reads *hadam hu* as establishing a single, unchanging status for blood: *lefnei kaparah k'le'achar kaparah* — blood has the same status before *kapara* as after *kapara*. Since after *kapara* there is clearly no *me'ilah* — because of *na'aseit mitzvato* — the same must apply before *kapara*. The *Gemara* considers the reverse: perhaps one should read *hu* in the other direction, establishing that just as before *kapara* there is *me'ilah* (as one might expect from any sanctified item), so too after *kapara* there should be *me'ilah*? The *Gemara* dismisses this: *veki yesh lecha davar she'na'aseit mitzvato veyesh bo me'ilah*? Is there anything for which the mitzvah has been completed and *me'ilah* still applies? This violates a clear and established principle, and so the *pasuk* must be read as exempting *me'ilah* before rather than imposing it after.

### Exceptions to Na'aseh

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this confident assertion that nothing once its mitzvah is completed retains *me'ilah*. *Harei trumat hadeshen* — the ashes removed in the daily *trumat hadeshen* ritual. The *kohen* takes a scoop of ashes from the *mizbeach*, carries them down the ramp, and places them at the side of the ramp — and yet *me'ilah* continues to apply to those ashes even after the ritual is complete. This is distinct from the question debated in the preceding days regarding whether ashes left on the *mizbeach* still have outstanding *mitzvot*. Here the question is about the specific ashes to which the *trumat hadeshen* act was performed: once the *kohen* has placed them by the *mizbeach*, the mitzvah is done — and yet *me'ilah* continues. The derivation is from the verse *ve'samo eitzel hamizbeach*: the word *samo* implies the ashes must remain there and preserve their state, from which we learn that their *kedusha* — and *me'ilah* — persists. So here is a genuine exception to the principle that *na'aseit mitzvato* eliminates *me'ilah*.

A second exception: the *se'ir hamishtalach* — the scapegoat sent over the cliff on *Yom Kippur*. Even after the goat has been hurled over and its body shattered, the limbs remain *assur behana'ah*, retaining their *kedusha* and *me'ilah* status despite the fact that *na'aseit mitzvato*.

### Two Exceptions Principle

The *Gemara* resolves the tension by invoking the principle of *shnei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad ein melamdim*: when two verses create exceptions to a general rule, those exceptions do not become the rule — they remain limited to the specific cases from which they are derived. *Trumat hadeshen* and *se'ir hamishtalach* are two exceptions; from two exceptions one does not generalize, and therefore *dam* cannot claim to be a third exception.

The *Gemara* further considers: what if one holds that the limbs of the *se'ir hamishtalach* are in fact *muttar* after it goes over the cliff — i.e., that *na'aseit mitzvato* does apply and there is no continued *issur*? In that case, there would be only one exception (*trumat hadeshen*), and one exception normally does become the rule. The *Gemara* responds that in that case, the *bigdei kehuna* of the *Kohen Gadol* after *Yom Kippur* supply the second exception: those garments, by virtue of *ve'hinicham sham* — "he shall place them there" — may never be used again (though *Rabbi Dosa* holds that an ordinary *kohen* may use them during the year). And if *Rabbi Dosa*'s view eliminates the *bigdei kehuna* example as well, the *Gemara* supplies yet another: the *egla arufa*, whose neck is broken in the ritual and which thereafter remains *assur behana'ah* despite *na'aseit mitzvato*. The *egla arufa* is technically not a *kodshim* case and carries no *me'ilah*, but it demonstrates the broader principle — applicable beyond *me'ilah* — that *na'aseit mitzvato* does not always produce a status change. This broader concept encompasses *issur hana'ah* generally, as seen for instance in *chametz* after *sereifah*, where once burned it becomes *muttar behana'ah*: *na'aseit mitzvato* changes the status, even outside the *me'ilah* framework.

It is worth pausing to note that the *Gemara* has not provided a fully direct answer to the original question. The question was how we know that *dam* is exempt from *me'ilah* before *kapara*, given that we compare before and after via *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading of *hu*. The *Gemara* responded by establishing that *na'aseit mitzvato* always removes *me'ilah*, and that while there are exceptions, they cannot be generalized. But the underlying challenge remains: we are not asking to generalize the exceptions into a rule — we are asking merely why *dam* cannot be one additional exception. Saying there are no exceptions proves too much, and saying the exceptions cannot be generalized merely prevents *dam* from being treated as the norm. The *Gemara* proceeds on the working assumption that unless there is a specific derivation establishing a given item as an exception, it follows the general rule — and for *dam*, the general rule derived from *hu* is no *me'ilah* before *kapara*, while the anomalous rabbinic *me'ilah* after the blood flows to the Kidron is left without extended discussion, presumably because it was a well-known and practically common rabbinic enactment given the daily scale of the *Beit HaMikdash*'s operations.

## Nesachim (Wine)

### Wine: Start & End

The counterpart to *dam* is the *nesachim* — the wine libations. Here the pattern is the inverse: *nesachim bitchilatan mo'alin bahen* — initially there is *me'ilah* by the wine, since it is sanctified to be poured as a *korban*. *Yardu lashitin ein mo'alin bahen* — once the wine is poured down into the *shitin* (the channels and caverns within the *mizbeach*), there is no *me'ilah*. This is the standard application of *na'aseit mitzvato* — the mitzvah has been performed, and *me'ilah* ceases. There may still remain a question of *lo nehenin*, but there is certainly no *me'ilah*. The *nesachim* thus exemplify the normal *halachic* trajectory — *me'ilah* before, none after — while the *dam* exemplifies the paradoxical inverse.

### Lul & Wine Residue

The *Gemara* initially considers whether our *Mishnah* (which holds that there is no *me'ilah* after the wine is poured) is inconsistent with the opinion of *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok*. The *baraita* records that *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* described a *lul katan* — a small duct or passage — situated between the ramp (*keves*) and the *mizbeach*, at the western end of the ramp. Once every seventy years, *pirchei kehuna* — young *kohanim*, small enough to fit into this narrow passage — would enter and extract *yayin karush*, congealed wine that had accumulated inside the hollow of the *mizbeach*, which he describes as resembling rounds of pressed figs. They would then burn this congealed wine *bikdusha* — in a sacred manner, in a designated place — just as *psulei hamukdashim* are burned. The apparent implication is that this wine retains its *kedusha* even after having been poured, which would make it an exception to *na'aseit mitzvato* and seemingly at odds with the *Mishnah*'s ruling that there is no *me'ilah* after the wine descends.

### Burning Congealed Wine

The source for burning the congealed wine *bikdusha* is derived from the verse concerning *nesachim* — *bakodesh hasech nesech shechar laHashem* — which, through double language, is taken to indicate that the wine retains a sacred character throughout. *Ravina* derives it through a *gezeira shava* of *kodesh-kodesh*: the word *kodesh* here is linked to the verse *vesarafta et hanotar ba'esh lo ye'achel ki kodesh hu*, which discusses the burning of *notar*. Just as *notar* must be burned because of its *kedusha*, so too this wine must be burned because of its *kedusha*.

### Gemara Initial Assumption

The *Gemara*'s initial reading is that *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok*'s position — that the wine is burned *bikdusha* — is inconsistent with the *Mishnah*'s position that there is no *me'ilah* after pouring, since the requirement to burn implies continued *kedusha*, which would imply continued *me'ilah*.

### Tosafot: Before Floor

*Tosafot* proposes a resolution: the wine that *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* says must be burned *bikdusha* is wine that hit the floor of the *mizbeach*'s hollow. The floor of the *Beit HaMikdash* has its own sanctifying power, which imparts a renewed level of *kedusha* to the wine — at least *derabbanan*. Consequently, the *Mishnah*'s ruling of no *me'ilah* after pouring would apply specifically to wine caught *before* it strikes the floor. If the wine hits the floor of the *mizbeach*'s interior, it is re-sanctified and must be burned. This creates a somewhat unusual scenario in which *na'aseit mitzvato* first removes *me'ilah*, but contact with the *mizbeach* floor then re-ignites *kedusha* — an admittedly strange sequence.

### Burning Derabbanan Only

The *Gemara* ultimately offers an alternative resolution: *afilu tema Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* — even the *Mishnah* could accord with *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok*. The answer is *mi-d'rabbanan ve-kra asmakhta* — when *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* says to burn the wine *bikdusha*, he means only *derabbanan*. The verse he cites is merely an *asmakhta*, a rabbinic support. In truth, *na'aseit mitzvato* fully applies, and there is no Torah-level *kedusha* or *me'ilah* remaining. The rabbinic enactment to burn the wine — rather than use it or discard it informally — reflects appropriate reverence, and the *Gemara* is comfortable reading *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* as not actually claiming continued *me'ilah* but only a rabbinic requirement of proper disposal. Under this reading, there is no conflict with the *Mishnah*'s ruling, since the *Mishnah* speaks of *me'ilah* and *Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok* speaks only of rabbinic practice.
