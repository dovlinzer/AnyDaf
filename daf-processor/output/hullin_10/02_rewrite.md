# Hullin 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mayim Megulim

### Three Liquids Assur

The Gemara opens this section with a Mishnah cited from elsewhere: three types of liquids are forbidden if left exposed — *mayim v'yayin v'chalav*, water, wine, and milk — due to the concern that a snake may have drunk from them and left behind venom. The question then becomes: how long must the liquids have been left unattended before they become forbidden? The answer given is *k'dei she'yeitzei harachash mimakom karov v'yishteh* — enough time for a crawling creature, specifically a snake, to emerge from a nearby place and drink. The Gemara then asks how nearby is "nearby," and Rabbi Yitzchak son of Rav Yehuda answers: enough time for a snake to emerge from beneath the handle of a vessel, drink, and return to its hole. The logic is straightforward: you cannot simply measure the time for the snake to slither out and drink, because if it had only done that much, you would still see it upon turning back. Therefore, the time threshold must account for the full round trip — slither out, drink, and return.

### Snake Timeline

The Gemara's formulation — *ela yishteh veyachzor lechoro* — establishes that the operative concern is the complete sequence: emergence, drinking, and retreat. This is the window during which one must have left the liquid unattended for the prohibition to apply.

### Tosfos on Snakes

Tosfos cites the Yerushalmi, which takes an even stricter approach. The Yerushalmi mentions a variety of snake called a *shfiphon* that is as thin as a hair, and states that *reshus nitna lekarka lehibakeia lefanav* — permission is granted for the ground to crack open before it. This means that even if no visible hole is present near the vessel, one cannot confidently rule out the snake's access, since it can apparently find or create an entry point in the earth that is invisible to the observer. Consequently, the time threshold must account for this more expansive possibility, and the standard of concern is accordingly heightened.

### Ashkenazi Practice

Despite the explicit and early halakhic formulation of *mayim megulim* — appearing at the Tannaitic level with the language of *assur* and with detailed parameters — Tosfos in several places states that this halakha is not practiced among Ashkenazim. His reasoning is that the entire prohibition is grounded in a concern for *sakana*, specifically the prevalence of dangerous snakes, and that this environmental condition no longer applies in the communities where Ashkenazim live. This is why many are unfamiliar with the law that leaving one's drink unattended renders it forbidden. The Rambam, however, rules it straightforwardly, and the Shulchan Aruch similarly appears to rule it for Sefardim.

### Health-Based Laws

This situation closely parallels the halakha of *mayim acharonim*, where the Gemara requires washing one's hands at the end of a meal on account of the salt residue left on one's fingers — a concern rooted in the heavy salting used to preserve meat in the Talmudic era, compounded by the absence of forks, which were not introduced into European usage until roughly the 14th century. Here too Tosfos states that the practice is not required in his day, though his precise reasoning is less transparent given that salted meat preservation was still practiced in his time. The Rambam and Shulchan Aruch continue to rule *mayim acharonim* as obligatory. What is striking about the comparison between these two halakhot is that *mayim megulim* employs far stronger halakhic language — *assur*, with detailed Tannaitic parameters — and yet Tosfos sets it aside, just as he does with the Amoraic, and in some ways more modest, formulation of *mayim acharonim*. In both cases, the principle that underlies the halakha — a health concern — is treated by Tosfos as the operative factor that determines the law's ongoing applicability.

### Halacha Obsolescence

One might ask: who decides which halakhot have become obsolete, and by what authority? The general principle holds that even when the underlying reason for a halakha no longer applies, the halakha itself does not change. However, matters of health and *sakana* occupy a somewhat distinct category. The Rambam, for instance, readily sets aside Talmudic medical remedies — and even the Gemara's prohibition of eating fish and meat together, which also uses the language of *assur* — on the grounds that the scientific assumptions underlying those rulings were simply incorrect. So specifically in the domain of health, one can argue that such statements were never purely halakhic in the same sense as other prohibitions and are therefore more susceptible to revision based on changed circumstances.

### Rambam/SA Ruling

The Rambam's approach is nonetheless inconsistent, retaining some health-based halakhot while discarding others. The Rambam rules *mayim megulim* and *mayim acharonim* while dismissing certain Talmudic medical prescriptions — reflecting that even within this domain there is no single clean criterion.

### Tosfos Method

An important methodological point about Tosfos bears emphasis here, one that is sometimes misunderstood. Tosfos does not proceed by examining a halakha, reasoning that its underlying rationale no longer applies, and concluding that the community need not observe it. Rather, Tosfos almost invariably begins from the observed fact that the halakha was *not* being practiced in his community and then works backward to explain why this practice — or non-practice — might be correct. He is reconciling the fact on the ground with the Talmudic text, not abolishing laws from the top down. This is a characteristically Ashkenazic and community-oriented halakhic posture: assume the community's practice may be defensible and seek the justification, rather than assume the community is wrong. It is worth emphasizing that one cannot find a case in all of Shas where Tosfos says: "although the community observes this halakha, since the reason no longer applies we may abandon it." The directionality always runs the other way.

## Knife Nick After Slaught…

### Case Setup

The Gemara now turns to what becomes one of the central and most analytically rich cases of this section. *hashochet besakin venimtset peguma* — one slaughtered with a knife, having checked it beforehand, and upon completing the shechita discovered that the knife was nicked. The question is: when did the nick occur? Before the shechita, rendering the animal non-kosher, or after?

### Rav Huna Position

Rav Huna rules: *afilu shiber bah atzamot kol hayom pesula* — even if after the shechita the knife was used to chop bones the entire day, the shechita is invalid. The reasoning is *chaishinan shema ba'or nifgama* — we must be concerned that the nick occurred at the very beginning of the shechita, in the outer skin of the animal's neck, before the cut was completed. This is admittedly a worst-case scenario — it is far more plausible that a nick found after extensive bone-chopping was caused by the bones. Nevertheless, Rav Huna holds that once you observe that the status quo has changed — the knife, which was checked and known to be good, is now nicked — you must be concerned that the change occurred as early as possible, not as late as possible.

To appreciate the stakes here, it is important to distinguish this case from the cases discussed previously. Before, the principle of *chazakah* was invoked to say: we had a status quo and a doubt arose — we dismiss the doubt and maintain the status quo. Here, by contrast, the status quo has demonstrably ended. The knife was good; now it is not. The question is no longer whether a change occurred — it did — but *when*. And the question of when a chazakah ends, and whether one can assume it held as long as possible, is the crux of the dispute.

An instructive parallel is the case of a *sefer torah* being read and a letter found to be cracked mid-reading. The ruling is that only the last *aliyah* must be repeated, because we assume the crack occurred at the last possible moment and not during an earlier *aliyah*. This suggests that in some contexts the principle of extending the chazakah until the last possible moment does apply. Whether that principle governs the shechita case — where, crucially, the animal begins with a chazakah of being non-kosher — is precisely what Rav Huna and Rav Chisda dispute.

### Rav Chisda Position

Rav Chisda rules: *keshera* — the shechita is valid. His reasoning introduces a distinct halakhic principle: *etzem vadai pogem, o safek pogem safek lo pogem* — a bone will definitely cause a nick, while whether the animal's skin would cause a nick is a genuine doubt. This invokes the principle of *safek v'vadai*: *ein safek motzi miyedei vadai* — a possibility does not displace a certainty. If there is a definite cause to which the nick can be attributed, namely the bone-chopping that occurred after the shechita, then the speculative concern that it was caused by the skin during the shechita is insufficient to undermine the definite explanation. The Gemara notes that this is a strong argument: the bone-breaking definitively, or at minimum far more plausibly, accounts for the nick, and there is no reason to abandon that explanation in favor of a far-fetched alternative.

### Mikvah/Chatzitza Case

The Gemara records that Rava attempts to marshal support for Rav Huna from a parallel case: *taval v'ala v'nimtza alav davar chotzetz* — one immersed in a mikvah and upon emerging discovered a *chatzitza* on one's body. The ruling is that even if one worked with the very material that could have caused the *chatzitza* — for example, cement, which could easily lodge under fingernails — the entire day after the immersion, the *tevilah* is invalid unless one can state with certainty, *bari li*, that the obstruction was not present beforehand. The parallel to the knife case seems precise: in both instances, there is a post-facto discovery of a problem, and there is a plausible explanation attributing it to activity that followed the act in question, yet that explanation is insufficient.

### Competing Chazakos

The Gemara responds to this parallel by noting that in the *tevilah* case there are two competing *chazakot*. The person had a chazakah of not having a *chatzitza* on their body, but they also had a prior status of being *tamei*. The principle established earlier applies: *ha'amed tamei al chazakato* — maintain the person's status of *tum'ah* until you know with certainty that it changed. Any doubt, even a doubt supported by a plausible alternative explanation, is insufficient to change a status that begins as *tamei*. Similarly, the Gemara suggests, the animal begins with a chazakah of being non-kosher, and one cannot assume the shechita was valid unless there is complete certainty. Rav Huna is consistent: he applies this principle across both cases.

### External vs. Intrinsic

To defend Rav Chisda's position — which is lenient about the shechita but presumably would be strict in the *tevilah* case — the Gemara offers a formal distinction: *sakin itra'ei beheima lo itra'ei* — the doubt pertains to the knife, not to the animal. In the *tevilah* case the doubt concerns the person's own body, which is the very subject of the status change from *tamei* to *tahor*. Because the *chatzitza* doubt is intrinsic to the person himself, it cannot be set aside. In the shechita case, the locus of doubt is the knife — external to the animal — and therefore the animal's shechita can be evaluated independently: the act was performed, the *simanim* appeared correct, and the problematic question is an extrinsic one about the instrument.

### Tosfos Critique

Tosfos finds this distinction deeply unsatisfying. In what way does the formal location of the problem — knife versus animal — change the halakhic reality? If the knife was nicked, the shechita was invalid regardless of whether we call the doubt "external" or "intrinsic." The question of validity still reduces to whether the knife had a nick at the time of shechita, which is precisely the doubt. Tosfos therefore offers an alternative reading: in the knife case there is a *double* doubt. First, it is uncertain when the nick occurred. Second, even if it is assumed the knife had a nick, the nick might have been located on a part of the blade that did not actually contact the *simanim* during the cut — a long knife could conceivably be nicked at one point while the shechita was performed with a different portion of the blade. This double doubt, he argues, is what actually distinguishes the case from the *tevilah* scenario, which involves only a single doubt.

### Inspection Evidence

A cleaner explanation, however, may lie in the distinction between what can be directly inspected after the fact. In the shechita case, as the previous daf established, one examines the *simanim* — the trachea and esophagus — after the fact, and that direct inspection provides affirmative evidence that the cut appears to have been performed correctly. This post-facto examination constitutes meaningful evidence that the shechita was valid, giving substance to the claim that the knife was likely fine at the moment of the cut and the nick occurred later. In the mikvah case, by contrast, emerging wet from the water provides no equivalent evidence. One cannot inspect the immersion retroactively the way one can inspect the *simanim*. The person's being wet shows that they dunked, not that they dunked without a *chatzitza*. In this reading, the Gemara's phrase *harei shechuta l'fanecha* — here the slaughter is before you — refers precisely to this: the visible evidence of the *simanim* supports the presumption of validity. By contrast, *harei tavul l'fanecha* carries no such evidentiary weight. This explanation may in fact be embedded in the Gemara's own language, rendering the Gemara's distinction less purely formalistic than it at first appears.

## Bird Trachea Case

### Baraita on Trachea

The Gemara cites a Tannaitic case to probe the question further. *shachat es haveshet ve'achar kach nishmata hagargeres* — one slaughtered the esophagus of a bird and then found that the trachea had been displaced from its proper location. To be clear on the anatomy: *veshet* is the esophagus and *gargeres* is the trachea. For a bird, only one of the two *simanim* must be cut, but both must be in their correct anatomical position at the time of shechita, lest the cut constitute an *ikkur* — a dislodgment that invalidates the shechita. If the trachea was already displaced before the shechita, the shechita is invalid; if it moved afterward, it is valid. The case arose in practice — *zeh hayah ma'aseh* — and the ruling was issued: *kol safek bashechita pasul* — any doubt in shechita renders it invalid.

### Doubt Principle

The Gemara asks what the word "any" in *kol safek bashechita pasul* comes to include. The answer is that it includes even cases where there might be contextual reason to assume validity — the broad formulation sweeps in doubts that might otherwise seem resolvable. The implication seems to be that even in the knife case, where bone-breaking provides a plausible alternative explanation for the nick, the principle of *kol safek* would still invalidate the shechita, thereby supporting Rav Huna.

### Animal vs. Knife Doubts

The Gemara responds: *lo le'asuyei safek she-hasafek daras* — the broad principle of *kol safek* is directed at standard categories of doubt about the act itself, such as *shehiyah* (pausing mid-cut) or *derisah* (applying pressure rather than cutting), not at the specific case where one has a definite causal explanation attributable to something that occurred after the shechita. Furthermore, the Gemara reasserts its earlier distinction: *hasam israyei be'reisha bavehema* — in the trachea case, the locus of doubt is the animal itself, its own physical condition and the act performed upon it. *sakin israyei beheima lo israyei* — in the knife case, the doubt concerns the knife, which is external to the animal.

### Principle Agreement

At this point the Gemara clarifies that both Rav Huna and Rav Chisda actually agree on the general principle of *kol safek bashechita pasul*. Their dispute is not about whether doubts are generally disqualifying — both agree they are — but rather about whether the specific configuration of the knife case, particularly when there is bone-breaking to attribute the nick to, constitutes the kind of resolved situation that removes it from the category of genuine doubt. The Gemara's distinction between doubts about the animal and doubts about the knife is the formal mechanism by which that resolution is achieved, at least according to Rav Chisda.

## Resolution & Halacha

### Hilcheta Rav Huna

The Gemara renders its final ruling in two parts. *v'hilchata kevastei de-rav kahana* — the halakha follows Rav Huna when no bone-breaking took place. If one slaughtered an animal, found a nick in the knife afterward, and had not used the knife on bones in the interim, the shechita is invalid. The reasoning is that without a definite cause to attribute the nick to, the concern that the nick was present at the time of shechita — that it arose *ba'or*, in the skin — cannot be dismissed, and we rule stringently consistent with the animal's initial chazakah of being non-kosher.

### Hilcheta Rav Chisda

*V'hilchata kevastei de-Rav Chisda* — when bone-breaking did occur, the halakha follows Rav Chisda and the shechita is valid. The presence of bone-breaking constitutes the *vadai* to which the nick can be attributed, and the principle of *ein safek motzi miyedei vadai* operates to permit the animal. The Gemara then works through the full range of the debate between Rav Huna and Rav Chisda, clarifying what each position holds even in cases they did not explicitly address. Rav Chisda, understood as invoking the chazakah of the knife and assuming the problem occurred at the last possible moment, would logically attribute the nick to the spine at the conclusion of the very last shechita — meaning all animals in a sequence would be valid. Rav Huna, applying worst-case assumptions throughout, would say even the first animal is compromised. The Gemara resolves that in a case without bone-breaking we follow Rav Huna; with bone-breaking, Rav Chisda.

### Rav Yosef Case

The Gemara records a practical case: *havah uvda* — such an incident occurred, and Rav Yosef *taraf* — declared non-kosher — thirteen animals after a nick was found in the knife at the end. The Gemara asks: according to whose opinion did Rav Yosef rule? If it is like Rav Huna, then even the first animal is invalid, which would account for all thirteen. But if it is like Rav Chisda, then thirteen animals are too many to explain, since Rav Chisda should allow you to attribute the nick to the very last moment.

### Nick Attribution

The Gemara considers the possibility that Rav Yosef ruled like Rav Chisda but in the version where the nick is attributed not to the very last shechita but to the backbone of the first animal, meaning all subsequent animals after the first are invalid — which would yield twelve invalid animals, not thirteen, unless the first is also counted, arriving at the same number. The Gemara then entertains an alternative: *v'ee ba'is ayma l'olam k'Rav Huna* — actually, Rav Yosef ruled entirely like Rav Huna, and all thirteen animals are invalid including the first, because Rav Huna's position is that once the chazakah of the knife is undermined by visible evidence of a nick, the concern reaches all the way back. If Rav Chisda's leniency were truly operative, one would logically extend the best-case attribution to the very last shechita and declare all thirteen valid — since *micdei matlee taleenan*, if we are going to project a best-case scenario, why attribute the nick to the first spine rather than the last? The fact that Rav Yosef declared all thirteen invalid confirms he ruled like Rav Huna.

### Practical Solution

Rav Acha son of Rava reported to Rav Ashi that Rav Kahana's practice was *mashich bedikuta beyn kol chad v'chad* — to extend the checking of the knife between each and every animal. This is the practically prudent approach: if a nick is discovered, the damage is contained to at most the most recently slaughtered animal, rather than retroactively invalidating an entire sequence. By checking the knife after each shechita, the shochet limits the range of uncertainty and avoids the disaster of invalidating a large batch.

### Knife Inspection

The Gemara observes that this practice of intermediate checking resembles the standard pre-shechita inspection before a sage — raising the question of whether each intermediate inspection also requires showing the knife to a *chacham*. The Gemara responds to this question by invoking Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: *lo amru l'har'ot sakin l'chacham elah mipnei kvodo shel chacham* — the requirement to show the knife to a sage was never a halakhic prerequisite for valid shechita but was instituted for the honor of the sage, to acknowledge rabbinic authority and oversight over the process. Once that obligation is discharged — as when all the *shochtim* in a community present their knives to a newly arrived authority — the ongoing intermediate checking can be done by the shochet himself.

### Self-Checking

This is grounded in the principle *eid echad ne'eman b'issurin* — a single witness is trusted in matters of kashrut. Just as one trusts one's butcher or household members regarding the status of food, the shochet is trusted to inspect his own knife competently. Accordingly, after the initial formal presentation to the sage, the shochet may check his own knife between animals without requiring external validation, and that intermediate self-inspection is halakhically sufficient to establish the knife's status at each stage of the shechita process.

The practical upshot: we rule like Rav Huna in cases without bone-breaking, meaning a nick discovered at the end creates retroactive doubt about all animals slaughtered since the last verified inspection. The solution is to check the knife after each animal so that any problem is isolated. If one was chopping bones in the interim, we rule like Rav Chisda and the shechita is valid. Rashi records that this question was raised in practice and that his teacher Rabbeinu Yaakov ben Rabbi Yitzchak ruled the animal *assur* in a case involving a bird — consistent with the ruling like Rav Huna.

## Source of Chazaka

### Derivation Question

Having applied the principle of chazakah extensively, the Gemara now steps back and asks the foundational question: *mena hani mili d'amri rabanan okminhu a'chezkateh* — from where do the Rabbis derive the very principle of chazakah, that one establishes something upon its prior status and assumes that status to persist until one knows otherwise?

### Tzara'at Source

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: we learn it from the *pasuk* regarding *tzara'at* of a house. The Torah states *veyatza hakohen min habayit el pesach habayit vehisgir et habayit shivat yamim* — the kohen exits to the doorway of the house and seals it for seven days. The implicit question is: how can the kohen declare the house *tamei* if he is not directly observing the spot at the very moment of declaration? From the instant he saw the spot until the moment he steps outside and pronounces the declaration, perhaps the spot shrank below the minimum size required for *tum'ah*. The fact that the Torah enables this declaration at all demonstrates that we rely on chazakah — we assume the spot is still the same size it was when last observed, and the kohen acts on that assumption.

### Backward Walking

Rav Acha bar Yaakov challenges: perhaps the source tells us nothing about chazakah at all. Perhaps the kohen walks out of the house *backwards*, keeping his eyes on the spot the entire time, so that he is in fact directly observing it as he exits. If so, there is no gap between observation and declaration, and no need to invoke any principle of assuming status quo.

### Abaye's Answers

Abaye offers two responses. First, *yetziah derech achorav lo shemah yetziah* — walking backwards does not constitute a normal departure; when the Torah says the kohen "exits," it means he exits in the normal fashion, facing forward. Second, even if one were to allow backward walking, what happens when the spot is behind the door? If the door opens outward and the spot is on the wall behind it, the kohen cannot see the spot as he exits regardless of which direction he faces. One might suggest cutting a window into the door to allow a line of sight, but here Abaye invokes another ruling: *bayit afel ein potchin bo chalonot lir'ot et nigo* — for a dark house one does not open windows to see the spot, because the standard is *l'chol mareh einei hakohen*, what the kohen can see under natural conditions. The house is assessed as it naturally stands, not as artificially illuminated. By analogy, one would not cut a window into a door solely to maintain visual contact with a spot.

### Rava Response

Rava counters Abaye on both points. Regarding backward walking not constituting a valid exit: *kohen gadol b'Yom HaKippurim yochiach* — the Kohen Gadol on Yom Kippur proves the contrary. The Torah describes him as exiting the Holy of Holies (*veyatza*), and we know from the Mishnah that *yatza u'va lo derech knisato* — he exits the way he entered, which is understood to mean he walked backwards to avoid turning his back on the Holy of Holies. Yet the Torah uses the language of *yetziah* for this backward exit, demonstrating that backward departure is indeed called leaving.

Regarding the dark-house ruling: *hani mili heichah d'lo itchazek itchazek* — that rule applies before a spot has been established. When no spot has yet been formally identified, one does not open windows to try to find or better see one. But *aval heichah d'itchazek itchazek* — once a spot has been established and its size confirmed, it may be permissible to open a window to continue observing it as the kohen exits. So Rava maintains that the backward-walking scenario, supplemented by a window, remains a viable alternative interpretation of the verse.

### Baraita Rules

The Gemara resolves the back-and-forth by citing a Baraita that rules definitively against the backward-walking reading. The Baraita derives from the Torah's phrasing that the kohen must fully exit the house — *ad sheyeitzei min habayit kulo* — he may not stand under the lintel when making the declaration. Rather, *omed b'tzad hamashkof u'masgir* — he stands just outside, near the lintel, and seals the house from there.

### Post-Facto Sealing

Crucially, the Baraita continues: *u'minayin she'im halach l'soch beiso v'hisgir* — how do we know that if the kohen deviated from the procedure and went all the way home before declaring the house sealed, or even remained inside the house and declared it from within, *she'hisgir u'musgar* — the house is still considered sealed and has its halakhic status? From the phrase *vehisgir et habayit* — "and he shall seal the house" — *mikol makom*, regardless. This is the decisive proof: the kohen can be entirely at home, with no visual contact with the spot, and yet the declaration is valid. This is only possible if we apply chazakah — we assume the spot retained its qualifying size, precisely because we maintain the status quo until we know otherwise.

### Final Challenge

Rav Acha bar Yaakov makes one final attempt to deflect the proof, suggesting that perhaps the kohen received a relay of reports — *k'gon d'kaimi dara d'gavrei v'amri k'dikay kai* — a row of people standing between the house and the kohen's home, each confirming that the spot is still present, passing the information along so that the kohen acts on real-time knowledge rather than on presumption. Of course, by the time the information traveled down the chain of people, there would still be a moment of lag between the final observation and the declaration, but the Gemara treats this as the last serious objection before conceding the point.

### Chazaka Conclusion

In the end, the Gemara concludes: the principle of chazakah — assuming that an established status persists until one knows definitively otherwise — is derived from the *tzara'at* passage. The kohen can seal the house and declare it *tamei* based on his earlier observation of the spot because we presume things to remain as they were last known to be.

### Tosfos on Chazaka

Tosfos raises two further refinements. First, one might object that the *tzara'at* source only demonstrates chazakah being applied stringently — to render a house *tamei*. How do we know the principle also operates leniently, to permit something or preserve a status of *taharah*? Tosfos responds that once the principle is established as a mechanism for resolving uncertainty, it applies symmetrically to both strict and lenient outcomes; we do not distinguish between the two. He also notes that leniency is implicitly present even in the *tzara'at* case, since the kohen who declares the house sealed is thereby enabling the bringing of a *korban* at the conclusion of the process — a leniency of sorts, since permitting a *korban* to be brought by someone who is not a genuine *metzora* would itself be a problem. Second, Tosfos addresses the question of chazakah when there is a later *rei'uta* — an undermining development. If the kohen returns and finds the spot has shrunk below the minimum size, we do not retroactively assume it was already undersized when the house was sealed. Rather, we assume it remained at the qualifying size until it was observed to have shrunk, meaning the chazakah held for its full duration and only ended at the point of observed change. This maps directly onto the question about the nicked knife discussed at length earlier in the shiur.

With this, the principle of chazakah has been fully established and sourced. Tomorrow's daf will turn to the next foundational principle: *rov*.
