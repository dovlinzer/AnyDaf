# Bava Batra 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel on Shutaf

### Four Points Preview

The daf opens by situating us within Shmuel's broader statement concerning a *shutaf* — a partner in a jointly owned field. Shmuel makes several distinct pronouncements, and the Gemara spends this daf unpacking each in turn. The first point concerns the principle of *yored bireshut*: a partner who goes and works the jointly owned field is treated as though he has permission from his co-owner. The second point — which, on its face, seems almost impossible to understand — is that partners may testify for one another (*me'idim zeh l'zeh*) regarding each other's portion of the field. The third is that each partner is considered a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman — with respect to the other's portion. These three points, along with the underlying framework of *yored bireshut* established on the previous amud, form the architecture of the daf.

### Yored Bireshut

By way of review: the Gemara on the previous amud grappled with whether the phrase *shutaf k'yored bireshut dam* — a partner is like one who descends into the field with permission — was introduced solely to teach that a partner cannot establish a *chazakah*, adverse possession, against his co-owner. The Mishnah already establishes that point in some form, so the Gemara pressed: if that were the only intent, why not simply say *shutaf ein lo chazakah*? The answer is that the phrase carries additional legal implications beyond the question of *chazakah*. The Gemara now turns to exploring what those implications are.

---

## Yored Bireshut Explained

### Asuya Lita Analysis

The Gemara cites Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha, who explains that the phrase teaches that a partner who works the field receives *shevach ha-magia le-ktafayim* — the improvement of the field that "reaches the shoulders." It further specifies that this applies both to a field that is *asuyah le-ita* (suited and in need of the work performed) and to a field *she-einah asuyah le-ita ke-sadeh ha-asuyah le-ita* — one that is not ordinarily suited for that kind of work, yet is treated as if it were.

To appreciate this, one must first understand the baseline rules for someone who works another person's field without permission — a *yored l'sdeh chavero shelo bi-reshut*. Halachically, if a person enters a neighbor's field and does work that the field genuinely needed — *asuyah le-ita* — the owner must pay something resembling the standard contracting fee, what Tosafot calls the rate of a *kablan*, a fixed-fee contractor. This is the lower end of compensation. If, however, the work was not something the field normally called for, the owner pays only the lesser of the worker's expenses or the improvement to the field — an even more minimal reimbursement.

Against this backdrop, the *chiddush* of Shmuel's statement emerges. Tosafot understands *shevach ha-magia le-ktafayim* not as a description of produce that has reached shoulder height (though that is Rashi's and Rashbam's reading), but as a description of labor that required genuine effort — putting one's back, one's shoulders, into the work. The force of the ruling is then twofold.

### Sharecropper Rights

First, when the field genuinely needed the work (*asuyah le-ita*), the partner who performed it does not collect at the rate of a *kablan* — the flat-fee contractor rate that a stranger who worked the field without permission would receive. Instead, he collects as an *aris*, a sharecropper, taking a proportionate share of the benefit. The distinction is significant: a *kablan* receives a fixed sum and leaves; an *aris* is given explicit, ongoing authorization and receives a substantially higher percentage of the field's yield. Because the partner, by virtue of his co-ownership, is treated as having been implicitly authorized, he is compensated at the higher *aris* rate for his work on the co-owner's portion of the field — even though no express permission was ever given.

Second — and this is the more striking *chiddush* — even when the field was not suited for the particular work performed, *she-einah asuyah le-ita*, the partner is still compensated as though it were *asuyah le-ita*. In the case of a stranger who plants orange trees in a wheat field, the owner owes little. But in the case of a partner who does the same, the co-owner actually owes compensation at the standard rate.

### Harebrained Ideas

This last point is genuinely surprising. One might have expected that a joint owner would have no right to make unilateral decisions about the use of a shared field — that any such action would require agreement between the partners. Yet the *halacha* as read by Tosafot is that the partner's 50% stake grants him sufficient authority that even eccentric or idiosyncratic agricultural decisions bind the other partner financially. The practical consequence is almost comic: imagine a partner with unconventional ideas who, while the other partner is away, plants orange trees in a wheat field. Upon returning, the other partner finds herself obligated to pay her co-owner as though she had hired him to do precisely that. It seems to grant substantial unilateral authority to a *shutaf* — authority one would not have expected to find in the structure of a joint ownership.

## Me'idim Zeh L'Zeh

### Nogei'in B'Eidutan

Now the Gemara turns to Shmuel's second point, the one that seems most difficult to justify: partners may testify for one another — *me'idim zeh l'zeh*. To clarify what is meant: when two people are partners, they do not each own a distinct, physically demarcated portion of the field. They jointly own the entire field together. Neither owns "the north half" while the other owns "the south half." Ownership is undivided.

Now suppose a third party, David, comes forward claiming that the entire field is his and produces evidence to that effect. One partner — call him Dov — cannot testify on his own behalf, since he is a direct litigant. But it seems as though he could testify that the field belongs to his partner Bobby, joining together with another witness to rebuff David's claim. The problem is immediate: testifying to protect Bobby is testifying to protect himself, since every inch of the field is jointly held. If David takes any part of the field, Dov loses as well. As the Rashbam makes explicit, *kol zman she-lo chilku* — as long as the field has not been formally partitioned — any intrusion by David harms both partners equally. This is a textbook case of *nogei'in b'eidusan* — a witness who is personally vested in the very testimony he offers, which renders him halachically invalid.

### Kinyan and Divestment

The Gemara's initial response is to reframe the case: we are dealing with a situation where the testifying partner has already written over his ownership to the other. He forfeits his stake in the field, transfers it to Bobby, and then testifies on her behalf as a disinterested party.

But this raises an immediate technical problem. The Gemara cites a *Baraita*: one who says *din u-devarim ein li al sadeh zo* — I have no claim on this field — and adds *ein li esek bah* and *yadi mesuleket heimena*, has said nothing legally operative. These are declarations of a present state of affairs, and they are false — the speaker actually does own the field. You cannot disclaim ownership; you can only transfer it. Rashbam notes a possible alternative reading: the problem is not the phrasing but the tense — describing a current state (*I have no stake*) accomplishes nothing, whereas an operative declaration of change (*I hereby remove myself*) might be different in principle. Rashbam draws an analogy to *harei at mekudeshet li*, where the word *harei* transforms a descriptive statement into a performative act. Nevertheless, the Rashbam concludes that none of these formulations suffice — whether one says *I have no stake* or *I hereby have no stake*, the effect is the same: zero. Ownership transfers only through actual conveyance.

The Gemara therefore reformulates: we are dealing with a case where a proper *kinyan* was performed. Dov hands Bobby a handkerchief — a formal act of acquisition — and declares that he thereby transfers his ownership of the field to her. Now he no longer owns the field, and it would seem he can testify.

### Creditor Lien Issue

Yet the Gemara immediately objects: *harei ma'amida bifnei ba'al chovo* — by transferring the field to Bobby, Dov has placed it in front of his own creditor. How so? Suppose Dov owes Joe a thousand dollars, and Joe holds a lien on Dov's assets, including his half of the field. Once Dov transfers his half to Bobby, Joe's lien follows that property — he can collect the debt from the half now in Bobby's possession. But consider what would happen if David were to succeed in proving that the field was always his: in that case, the field would never have legitimately belonged to Dov, and Joe could not collect from it, since there would be nothing that meaningfully "went through" Dov. By testifying that the field is Bobby's — and therefore that it was legitimately Dov's before he transferred it — Dov preserves the ability of his creditor to collect from that very property. He remains a beneficiary of the testimony, and therefore *pasul*.

Crucially, this concern arises even when Dov has transferred the field *shelo be-achrayut* — without accepting any warranty obligation to reimburse Bobby if the field is later taken from her. Even without such a guarantee, the fact that the property passed through Dov's hands means that his creditor can still pursue it in Bobby's possession. Dov testifies to benefit himself precisely in that regard.

It is worth noting, parenthetically, that the Gemara is adopting a categorical rule here — we assume a person may have creditors and therefore may benefit from testimony that preserves a field's legal pedigree, even if in a specific case the witness could theoretically prove he has no outstanding debts. The Gemara does not entertain case-by-case demonstrations of this kind; the hypothetical possibility of benefit appears sufficient to disqualify.

### Two Types of Liability

The Gemara proposes a resolution: we are dealing with a case where Dov accepted *achrayut* upon himself when transferring the field to Bobby. But the Gemara immediately asks: *achrayut* for what? *Achrayut d'alma* — liability for an external claimant like David, who argues that the field was never Dov's to begin with — would make things worse, not better. If Dov promised to reimburse Bobby in the event that David takes the field, then Dov has an even stronger financial interest in defeating David's claim. That cannot be the kind of *achrayut* that resolves the problem.

The operative *achrayut* must therefore be *achrayut d'ati leh machmateh* — liability arising from Dov's own debts. Specifically: Dov accepts upon himself that if his creditor Joe were to seize the field from Bobby in order to satisfy a loan Dov owes, Dov will reimburse Bobby for that loss. This changes the calculus entirely. If Joe seizes the field from Bobby, Bobby can recover from Dov. If Dov benefits from Joe collecting the field — because his debt to Joe is now satisfied — he simultaneously incurs an equivalent new debt to Bobby. The net gain is zero. By accepting this form of *achrayut*, Dov has neutralized the financial benefit he would otherwise derive from the testimony, and he is now permitted to testify.

### Achrayut Permits Testimo…

The resulting *halacha* is as follows: Dov may perform a proper *kinyan* transferring his half of the field to Bobby, accept *achrayut* specifically for losses arising from his own debts (*achrayut d'ati leh machmateh*), and thereafter testify on Bobby's behalf against David's claim. Under these conditions, he neither benefits from defeating David (since that protects Bobby against *achrayut d'alma*, for which Dov accepted no liability), nor benefits from preserving the field's legal lineage (since any gain from a creditor collecting from Bobby would be offset by his obligation to reimburse her). The Gemara does not yet directly confront the concern that Dov is simply executing a temporary maneuver — transferring the field, testifying, and then reclaiming his stake from Bobby afterwards. That concern lurks behind the entire discussion and will be addressed through the cases that follow.

## Renouncing Stake Debate

### Mistaleik Question

The Gemara now presses the deeper question: even granting that the technical requirements have been met, when a partner removes himself from ownership of the field, is that removal genuine? The Rashbam explains that what the Gemara is probing is precisely the concern of a temporary arrangement — Dov transfers his stake, testifies, and then Bobby quietly transfers it back. The testimony may be entirely truthful, but the divestment is merely instrumental. Can we accept such a procedure, or do we say that *mistaleik nafshia mineih* — his removal of himself — is not a genuine *mistaleik*?

### Sefer Torah Case

The Gemara brings a compelling *Baraita* as evidence. The *Baraita* states: *bnei ir she-ganvu sefer Torah shelahen* — the people of a town whose Sefer Torah was stolen — *ein danin be-dayanei ota ha'ir v'ein mevi'in ra'ayah me'anshei ota ha'ir*. The townspeople cannot serve as judges in the case, and the townspeople cannot serve as witnesses. Every member of the community jointly owns the Sefer Torah and stands to benefit directly from its recovery. They are all *ba'alei din* in the matter.

The practical consequence is startling: the only people who witnessed the theft are the townspeople, and none of them can testify. The thief may well escape accountability. (A similar structural problem arose in the time of the *Rishonim* in *Ashkenaz*, where the only available witnesses to certain commercial transactions were women, who are *pasul* as witnesses under standard *hilchos eidus*. Communal practice eventually developed to treat women's statements not as formal testimony but as credible evidence — a parallel accommodation to the same underlying tension.)

Now, if renunciation of ownership were a reliable mechanism for restoring eligibility to testify, why not simply have two townspeople formally divest themselves of their ownership share in the Sefer Torah and then serve as witnesses or judges, while the remaining community members constitute the other roles? The Gemara raises exactly this: *yisalku be-trei minaihu u-ladun* — let two people step aside and adjudicate. The *Baraita*'s silence on this solution implies it would not work.

### Permanent Beneficiary

The reason it does not work is that ownership of the Sefer Torah is not what constitutes the problem. Even a townsperson who holds no monetary ownership stake in the Sefer Torah is a *beneficiary* of its presence in the community. The Sefer Torah enables *Kriyat HaTorah*, the regular Shabbos leining, the entire fabric of communal Torah life. One might argue that *mitzvot lav le-hanot nitenu* — fulfilling mitzvot is not considered a form of personal benefit. Nonetheless, the Gemara's position is that the ability to maintain regular communal Torah reading constitutes sufficient benefit to render every townsperson a *nogei'a* in the case, regardless of their monetary ownership status. A person cannot renounce a benefit they will continue to receive simply by virtue of living in the town and using its shul.

This is a different and more expansive category of disqualification than mere financial interest. The witness need not own the Sefer Torah or have a monetary stake in its return; the indirect, ongoing, communal benefit of having a functioning Sefer Torah in the town is enough. Renouncing one's property interest cannot cure what is not a property problem to begin with.

### Town Donation Case

The Gemara adduces a further proof. The case: someone declares a donation of a *maneh* — a substantial sum — to the people of his city for general civic purposes, and then denies having made the pledge. The townspeople are the only witnesses. The *Baraita* rules: *ein danin be-dayanei ota ha'ir v'ein mevi'in ra'ayah me'anshei ota ha'ir* — the townspeople may neither serve as judges nor as witnesses. Again, why not have two of them renounce their anticipated share of the donation and testify? The fact that the *Baraita* does not entertain this solution implies that such renunciation is not accepted.

### Public Goods Benefit

The Gemara's analysis of this case becomes more granular. It raises an intermediate puzzle about the version of this case that concerns a donation to the *aniyim* — the poor of the city. If the benefit flows exclusively to the poor, why are the judges of the city disqualified? The judges are not poor; they will not receive the funds. The Gemara therefore revises the formulation: the ruling applies to the *dayanei aniyei ota ha'ir* — the poor of the city cannot be judges, and the poor of the city cannot be witnesses, since they are the direct beneficiaries.

The Gemara then reintroduces the question of why the entire community is disqualified, not merely the poor. The answer: *u-va'aniyei d'ramu aleihu* — it is the responsibility of the entire community to provide for its poor. When a donor pledges a large sum for the poor, he alleviates a burden that falls on every resident. The entire community therefore benefits, which is why no one can testify or adjudicate.

### Fixed Contributions

One might think this concern could be addressed by individuals committing to pay their fixed share regardless of the donation. If Nissan and David declare they will continue contributing their usual amount as though the donation had never been pledged, they no longer benefit from it — can they then testify? The Gemara explores this. If contributions are *katzuv* — fixed in advance — then in principle it might seem that two individuals could formally commit to paying their full fixed share and thereby divest themselves of the benefit of the donation. But the Gemara offers two responses.

First, the immediate case is one where contributions are *lo katzuv* — not fixed in advance. The amount owed is determined dynamically, assessed according to communal need and individual means. When a large donation arrives, it reduces everyone's assessment proportionally. There is no fixed amount any individual can pre-commit to, and so there is no mechanism by which a person can remove themselves from the benefit pool.

Second, even where amounts are fixed, there is an indirect benefit: *keivan de-revach, revach* — once substantial funds have been received for the poor, there is no urgency in collecting from anyone. Even those who will ultimately pay their full share benefit from the breathing room — the immediate pressure is off. This residual benefit, the Gemara concludes, cannot be renounced and therefore disqualifies even those who commit to paying in full.

The conclusion that emerges from all of this is significant. The Gemara's general position — outside the special cases of *Sefer Torah* and communal donations — is that genuine divestment of a financial interest through formal *kinyan* does remove a person from the category of *nogei'a* and permits testimony. We are not categorically afraid that after winning the case the witness will reclaim his stake. It is specifically the Sefer Torah and communal donation cases where the benefit is irreducibly public, cannot be renounced, and therefore permanently disqualifies.

## Rishonim Solutions

### Tosafot Approach

Having worked through the Gemara's cases, the *Rishonim* raise a serious structural question. There is a well-established principle: *techilato bi-fsol ve-sofo be-kashrus pasul* — if a witness was disqualified at the time the relevant events occurred, he remains disqualified even if his situation subsequently changes. The classic example: I witness my brother-in-law borrow money. At the time, I am *pasul* as a witness because of our familial relationship. Later, I divorce my wife and the relationship is severed. I cannot testify about what I saw, because *techilato bi-fsol* — at the outset I was invalid.

Why, then, does the Gemara allow a partner who has divested his stake to subsequently testify? At the time he was present and aware of the facts bearing on the case, he was a full owner — clearly disqualified. His later divestment should be no more effective than my later divorce.

Tosafot addresses this by drawing a distinction between two types of disqualification: *beguf* and *be-mamon* — personal status versus financial interest. Being a brother-in-law is a *status* — it attaches to the person as a classificatory label. That label being present at the time of witnessing generates the *techilato bi-fsol* concern. Financial interest, by contrast, is not a status — it is a contingent, situational condition. At any given moment the question is simply: does this person currently stand to gain? If the financial interest has been genuinely eliminated through transfer, there is no residual label of *pasul* that carries forward. The disqualification was never about who the person *is*, only about what benefit he *stands to receive*, and once that benefit is gone, so is the disqualification.

### Ra'avad Framework

A more conceptually elegant approach is attributed to the Ra'avad, who draws a fundamental distinction between two different legal categories: *ba'al din* and *nogei'a be-edut*. A *ba'al din* is the direct litigant — the person whose money or property is immediately at stake in the litigation. A *nogei'a be-edut* (also *nogei'a ba-davar*) is not the litigant himself, but someone who will indirectly benefit from the outcome — a vested interest, but at one remove.

In the partnership cases, before divestment, the partner is a direct *ba'al din*: it is his field, his money on the line. After divestment but before the disqualifying concern about creditor liens is resolved, he is a *nogei'a be-edut*: no longer the direct litigant, but still indirectly benefiting. These are not merely two points on a spectrum — they are categorically different legal statuses.

The Ra'avad argues that *techilato bi-fsol* applies to *nogei'a be-edut*, not to *ba'al din*. The reason is this: a *ba'al din* is not a *pasul eid* — he is a *non-eid*. A litigant and a witness occupy mutually exclusive roles; the litigant is simply not functioning as a witness at all. Since the partner who was a *ba'al din* was never classified as a *pasul eid*, he never acquired the negative label that *techilato bi-fsol* would then perpetuate. Once he divests himself and is no longer a *ba'al din*, he steps into the role of witness for the first time — and at that moment, he is *kasher*.

The irony is striking: the person who had the larger stake — the direct owner, the *ba'al din* — may more readily transition into a valid witness upon divestment than someone who had a lesser, more indirect interest, since the latter was already functioning in the domain of testimony (as a *nogei'a be-edut*) and therefore accumulated a disqualifying history. The Ra'avad's approach is thoroughly formalistic rather than pragmatic, but it illuminates with precision the conceptual boundaries of *techilato bi-fsol* as applied to the testimony of interested parties.

## Shomer Sachar Zeh

### Watching Arrangement

The Gemara turns to Shmuel's third point: partners are *shomrei sachar zeh l'zeh* — each serves as a paid watchman for the other's portion of the field. The scenario is as follows: Bobby and Dov jointly own a field. Bobby is watching the field entirely on her own, while Dov is away attending to other matters. Bobby falls asleep, and thieves damage or destroy the field. Bobby obviously bears responsibility for her own half of the loss. But does she bear responsibility for Dov's half as well?

The answer is yes — she is a *shomer sachar* with respect to his portion, and therefore liable. A *shomer sachar* bears liability for negligence (including falling asleep on the job), unlike a *shomer chinam* who has a more limited liability. Since Bobby was watching the entire field, her negligence applies to the whole of it, and she owes Dov for his half of the loss.

The Gemara, however, raises an immediate objection: surely this is a case of *shemirah be-ba'alim* — watching being performed while the owner is simultaneously in the watchman's service. The principle of *shemirah be-ba'alim* exempts a watchman from all liability when the owner is actively working for him at the time the *shemirah* began. If Bobby is watching Dov's portion, and Dov is simultaneously watching Bobby's portion from New Jersey (as partners in a jointly owned field there), then at the very moment Bobby is watching for Dov, Dov is watching for Bobby. The mutual, simultaneous exchange of services should invoke *shemirah be-ba'alim* and exempt Bobby entirely.

### Alternating Watch Days

Rav Papa resolves this: the case is not one of simultaneous watching. Rather, the arrangement is *shemor li hayom ani eshmor lecha le-machar* — watch for me today, and I will watch for you tomorrow. On Bobby's watch day, Dov is genuinely off duty and is not providing any service to Bobby. He is not in her employ today. Tomorrow, when it is his turn to watch, Bobby will be off. The exchange is sequential, not simultaneous, and therefore *shemirah be-ba'alim* does not apply on any given day. Since the partners are compensating each other with their respective service — each paying for the other's watch day with their own watch day — each is classified as a *shomer sachar* when it is their turn to watch. Bobby's liability for Dov's loss on her watch day is therefore intact.

## Machar Lo Bayis Case

### Field Sale Problem

The Gemara now introduces a new *Baraita* that picks up the theme of *achrayut* and testimony in the context of sale. The *Baraita* states: if one sells a house or a field to another person (*machar lo bayis*, *machar lo sadeh*), the seller cannot testify on the buyer's behalf regarding that property — *mipnei she-achrayuso alav* — because the seller's liability remains attached to it. The assumption is that when a field is sold, the seller implicitly accepts *achrayut* — warranty liability — such that if the buyer later loses the field to a creditor or a claimant, the seller must reimburse him. This gives the seller a direct financial interest in any litigation that could disturb the buyer's title, and therefore disqualifies him as a witness.

### Movable Goods Contrast

The *Baraita* then offers a contrasting case: if one sells a cow (*machar lo parah*) or a garment (*machar lo tallis*) — movable goods — the seller *can* testify on the buyer's behalf. The reason given: *ein achrayuso alav* — there is no liability on him. At first glance this seems puzzling, because if the movable item turns out to have been stolen, the transaction is a *mekach ta'os* — a mistaken sale — and the seller would owe restitution even without any formal warranty. He cannot simply claim he had no liability; he sold something he didn't legitimately own.

### Difference Question

This generates the natural question with which the daf concludes: *mai shena reisha u-mai shena seifa* — what is the difference between the first case (field) and the latter case (movable goods)? In both cases, the seller stands to benefit from testimony that confirms the buyer's ownership, since in both cases a successful claim by the original owner would expose the seller to liability. Why does the Gemara treat these cases differently? The *Baraita* appears to assert a distinction, but the grounds for it are not yet clear. The Gemara's resolution of this question, which will require some creative interpretation of the underlying principles, is deferred to the following daf.
