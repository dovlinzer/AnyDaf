# Hullin 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol Habasar Intro

### Perek Overview

We begin a new *perek* — *mazal tov* to all — and embark on *Kol Habasar*, which will occupy us for approximately three weeks. The entirety of this *perek* is devoted to *basar b'chalav*, and we will accordingly be covering a great deal of practical *halakha*. We pick up at the bottom of 103b, where the new *perek* opens.

### Mishna: Meat/Milk Rule

The *mishnah* states: *kol habasar assur levashel bechalav chutz mibasar dagim vechagavim* — all meat is prohibited to cook with milk, with the exception of the meat of fish and grasshoppers. And further: *ve'assur leha'alos im hagevinah al hashulchan chutz mibasar dagim vechagavim* — it is also prohibited to bring meat together with cheese onto the same table, even where the prohibition is only rabbinic, again with the same exceptions of fish and grasshoppers.

The first clause reflects the biblical prohibition; the second extends the concern to table proximity, anticipating the later *mishnah* that forbids placing meat and milk — or meat and cheese — on the same table simultaneously. The fact that even these rabbinic extensions apply, yet fish and grasshoppers are explicitly exempted, is a point the *Gemara* will take up shortly.

### Table/Vows Extensions

The *mishnah* then addresses an entirely separate question — the definition of meat in the context of vows: *ha-noder min ha-basar mutar b'vasar dagim v'chagavim*. If a person takes a *neder* prohibiting himself from all meat, he is nevertheless permitted to eat the meat of fish and grasshoppers, because these are not considered meat even for the purposes of vows.

### Vows & Vernacular

Vows are governed by the vernacular — *lashon bnei adam* — and when an ordinary person says "meat," no one expects to receive fish for dinner, let alone grasshoppers. Hence, they are not included in such a vow. This observation about popular perception may also be hinting at the rationale behind the rabbinic extension of the *basar b'chalav* prohibition to birds. As we will see, it is nearly universally accepted that birds are not included in the Torah's prohibition, since the verse speaks of a kid goat cooked in its mother's milk, and birds have no mother's milk. Yet the prohibition is extended rabbinically. One reason may be precisely because birds are what people naturally understand as "meat." We do not typically think about the *basar b'chalav* prohibition in terms of the verse about the kid goat; we think of it in terms of meat and milk. And chicken reads as meat. If you told many people that chicken and milk is only rabbinic and not biblical, they might not believe you — because our conceptual framework is *basar b'chalav*, not the specific model of the biblical verse.

So the final clause of the *mishnah*, about vows and the vernacular definition of meat, may implicitly inform the first clause's extension of the prohibition to birds.

## Biblical vs Rabbinic

### Birds Extension Logic

The *mishnah* thus brings together two distinct lines of inquiry: the question of which categories of meat are included in the *basar b'chalav* prohibition, and the question of which categories of *eating together* are prohibited. The two are related but conceptually separate.

The extension to birds rests on the popular perception of what constitutes meat. Although the Torah's verse is anchored in the image of the kid goat and its mother's milk — a model that by definition cannot apply to birds — we have shifted our conceptual vocabulary to the more general category of *basar b'chalav*. Within that framework, poultry naturally falls inside the category of meat.

### Two Extensions

There are accordingly two areas of rabbinic extension at work in this *perek*. The first concerns the prohibited *actions*: beyond cooking meat and milk together (which is the biblical violation), the Rabbis prohibit eating them together even if not cooked, and even having them on the same table. The second concerns the prohibited *categories*: beyond the animals that are explicitly implicated by the biblical verse, the Rabbis extend the prohibition to birds, on account of their status in popular perception as meat.

### Mishna Structure

It is also worth noting that this *perek* departs from a structural pattern found in the preceding *mishnayot* — *Oto Ve'es Beno*, *Kisuy Hadam*, *Gid Hanasheh* — which each methodically address whether a given law applies to *chullin* or *mukdashin*, within the Land of Israel or outside, in the presence of the *Beis Hamikdash* or not. That formal checklist structure is dropped here, though it will resume later, by *Shiluach Haken*. The *Tosefta* preserves it; *Tosafot* notes the omission without fully resolving it. The most natural explanation is that the *mishnah* here has larger concerns to address and moves directly into the substantive content of the *basar b'chalav* laws.

## Birds Status Debate

### Birds Biblical Assumption

The *Gemara* opens with the assumption that *ha-of assur mi-d'Oraisa* — that fowl is biblically prohibited in the *basar b'chalav* framework. The question is: why would the *Gemara* assume this, given that the near-universal view is that birds cannot be included in a verse about a kid goat and its mother's milk?

### Tosafot Logic

*Tosafot* explains that the *Gemara*'s assumption is driven not merely by the fact that the *mishnah* includes birds without distinction, but specifically by the elaborate network of rabbinic safeguards the *mishnah* extends to all meat. If the eating itself of birds with milk is only rabbinic, why would we then layer an additional prohibition against even having them on the same table? It is difficult to understand such extensive safeguarding unless there is a biblical concern underlying it. The presence of these *gezeirot* suggests, at minimum, that the *mishnah* operates on the assumption of an underlying biblical prohibition with respect to birds.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

The *Gemara* therefore notes that the *mishnah* does not appear to reflect the view of Rabbi Akiva, who states explicitly in a later *mishnah*: *chaya v'of einam min ha-Torah* — neither wild animals nor birds are included in the Torah's *basar b'chalav* prohibition. The exclusion is based on the requirement that the prohibited meat pattern itself after the model of the *g'di* — the kid goat. Birds do not fit that model. While the *Gemara* attributes this position specifically to Rabbi Akiva, the exclusion of birds is in truth nearly universal.

### Vow Principle Applied

Now the *Gemara* turns to the final clause of the *mishnah* — the vow ruling — and notes a tension. The *mishnah*'s inclusion of birds in the vow against meat aligns with Rabbi Akiva's principle, stated in tractate *Nedarim*: *kol mili d'mimla alei shliach bar minei hu* — any item about which a messenger would need to check back with his sender is included within the periphery of the sender's category.

### Vegetables Vow Example

The principle is illustrated clearly in the case of one who vows against *yarak* — vegetables. The *mishnah* rules that he is permitted to eat gourds, since gourds are not what people ordinarily have in mind when they say "vegetables." Rabbi Akiva disagrees: he rules that gourds are included. The Rabbis challenge him — after all, if you send a messenger to buy vegetables and he returns saying "I couldn't find any vegetables, only gourds," that very formulation demonstrates that gourds are not considered vegetables. Rabbi Akiva responds that this is precisely his point: the messenger *does* ask about gourds — he checks in, he frames the question — which shows that gourds occupy the peripheral zone of the category. They are not clearly included, but they are not clearly excluded either. By contrast, no one would report back "I couldn't find vegetables, but I found legumes" — legumes (*kitniyot*) are entirely outside the category and no one would even consider checking about them. Rabbi Akiva's rule is thus: anything that prompts a check is included in the periphery of the vow category and is therefore prohibited.

Applying this to our *mishnah*: if someone is sent to buy meat (*basar*) for dinner, and the messenger returns saying he could only find chicken — Rabbi Akiva would hold that the messenger would check about this, since *basar* in the ordinary sense means *basar behema*. That very need to check places poultry within the peripheral zone of the meat category, and therefore birds are included in the vow. The end of our *mishnah*, then, aligns with Rabbi Akiva's position on vows.

It should be noted that according to Rabbi Akiva in *Nedarim*, even fish under most circumstances would be included in such a vow — the messenger might equally report back that he found salmon but not meat. The *Gemara* accommodates this by positing special circumstances in which the sender had made clear he did not want fish. But in any event, the inclusion of birds in the vow and the exclusion of fish in our *mishnah* is not a perfect fit even for Rabbi Akiva.

### Inconsistency Problem

This creates a striking internal tension in the *mishnah*. The opening clause — which includes birds in *basar b'chalav* with all its attendant safeguards — implies that birds are subject to a biblical prohibition, which runs contrary to Rabbi Akiva's explicit position. Yet the closing clause — which includes birds in the vow against meat — aligns with Rabbi Akiva's principle. The *mishnah* thus appears to reflect Rabbi Akiva in the *seifa* and the Rabbis in the *reisha*: *reisha rabanan vesifa Rabbi Akiva*.

## Attribution Resolution

### Rav Yosef Solution

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction simply: *Rebbi hi* — the *mishnah* was composed by Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi, and he is free to adopt different *tannaitic* opinions on different matters. These are conceptually unrelated questions — the laws of *basar b'chalav* and the laws of vows — and there is no inconsistency in following one authority on each.

### Hanasi's Positions

Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi holds like Rabbi Akiva when it comes to *nedarim* — vows go by vernacular perception, and since a messenger would check about chicken when sent for meat, chicken falls within the category of meat for vow purposes. But when it comes to *basar b'chalav*, Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi holds like the Rabbis, who maintain that birds are biblically prohibited.

### Tannaim Uncertainty

It must be acknowledged, however, that the position attributed here to the Rabbis — that birds are *de-Oraisa* prohibited in *basar b'chalav* — is exceedingly difficult to sustain as an independent *tannaitic* opinion. The *tannaim* we will encounter in later *mishnayot* exclude birds from the biblical prohibition essentially without dissent. The idea that birds could be included requires abstracting the verse so completely away from its plain meaning — the model of the kid goat and its mother's milk — that it becomes nearly unrecognizable. What the *Gemara* may effectively be doing is constructing this "Rabbis" position by inference from the *mishnah*'s own language and structure, rather than recovering a clearly attested *tannaitic* view.

## Rav Ashi Alternative

### All Meat/Some Biblical

Rav Ashi offers an entirely different reading. *Kula Rabbi Akiva hi* — the entire *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Akiva's position throughout, and it should be read as follows: *kol habasar assur levashel bechalav mehem midivrei Torah mehem midivrei sofrim* — all meat is prohibited from being cooked with milk, some categories biblically and some rabbinically. The *mishnah* is not asserting that all prohibited meat is equally biblical; it is asserting a unified prohibition that encompasses both biblical and rabbinic categories.

### Birds Rabbinic Only

On this reading, birds are rabbinically prohibited while *basar behema* is biblically prohibited. Fish and grasshoppers are neither: *she'einam lo midivrei Torah velo midivrei sofrim* — they are exempt from both biblical and rabbinic prohibition. The *mishnah*'s structure thus accommodates all of this within a single Akivan framework without internal contradiction.

## Multiple Safeguards Prob…

### Rav Yosef Challenge

This reading, however, immediately raises a new difficulty. If birds are only rabbinically prohibited from *basar b'chalav*, why does the *mishnah* extend an additional safeguard prohibiting them from even appearing at the same table as cheese? Rav Yosef presses this point: *shma mina basar of bechalav de'oraisa* — from the fact that such a safeguard exists, we can infer that birds must be biblically prohibited. For if the eating itself — *achila gufa* — is already a rabbinic *gezeirah* (since we are not speaking of birds cooked with milk, but of eating them in proximity), to then prohibit table proximity lest one come to eat them together seems like an unacceptable compounding of rabbinic safeguards.

### Safeguard Doubling

The *Gemara* responds by questioning the premise: *uman teima dela gazrinan gezeira ligezeira* — who says one may not make a safeguard upon a safeguard? The *Gemara* raises this as a genuine question rather than assuming the answer in either direction.

*Tosafot* observes that the *Gemara*'s position on *gezeirah ligezeirah* is inconsistent across the tractates. In several places the *Gemara* treats it as axiomatic that one may not double safeguards; in others it assumes the opposite without comment; and in still others the question is raised as uncertain. *Tosafot* concludes that there is no universal rule — the *Gemara* has an intuitive sense of when a given *gezeirah* is of a kind that admits doubling and when it does not, and no single standard can be extracted that explains all the cases. The prohibition of doubling safeguards, in other words, is not an absolute principle but a contextual one.

### Tosafot Safeguards

*Tosafot*'s discussion makes clear that we cannot simply assume the *Gemara* will always prohibit or always permit *gezeirah ligezeirah*. What matters is the nature and the realistic danger of the specific situation.

### Table Safeguard Question

To press the point, the *Gemara* adduces a comparison: *challat chutz la-aretz ne'echelet im ha-zar al ha-shulchan* — the separated *challah* of produce grown outside the Land of Israel may be eaten at the same table as a non-*kohen*. Outside the Land of Israel, *challah* is only a rabbinic obligation; and since we are all in a state of ritual impurity (*tumah*), even a biblical *challah* would be ritually impure and unavailable to a *kohen*. Given this double leniency, one might have thought we would prohibit the non-*kohen* from sitting at the table lest he inadvertently eat from the *kohen*'s *challah*. Yet we do not. This appears to demonstrate that we do not extend rabbinic safeguards yet further — that we do not make a *gezeirah ligezeirah* — even where the underlying concern is relatively clear. Why then should birds be treated more strictly?

## Abaye Slippery Slope

### Challah Comparison

Abaye answers by distinguishing the two cases at a fundamental level. He argues: *bishlama i ashmeinan challat chutz la-aretz be-aretz* — if the ruling had concerned *challah* from outside the Land of Israel while one is *in* the Land of Israel, and the *Gemara* had still not made a further safeguard, that would be a genuine proof that we do not double *gezeirot* — because there, at least, there would be some eventual path by which sitting at a table could lead to eating biblically-obligated *challah* from Eretz Yisrael. But as things stand, the ruling concerns *challah* from outside the Land while one remains outside the Land. There is no realistic prospect that sharing a table with a non-*kohen* will lead, in any reasonable chain of events, to eating *challat Eretz Yisrael*. We do not make a safeguard when the feared outcome is not meaningfully *in the offing* — it is not the absence of a rule against doubling *gezeirot* that explains the leniency, but the absence of any realistic danger that the case will lead anywhere prohibited.

With *basar b'chalav*, however, the calculus is entirely different. *De-sharat leh lemichal of u-gevinah, atei lemichal basar u-gevinah, u-michal basar be-chalav de-oraita* — if you permit someone to have chicken and cheese at the same table, he will naturally come to have beef and cheese at the same table; and from having them at the table, he will come to eat them together; and eating them together will readily shade into cooking them together, which is a biblical violation. The slope here is genuinely slippery — each step leads naturally to the next — and therefore a doubled safeguard is not only permissible but appropriate. Birds can thus be purely rabbinic and still warrant the additional prohibition of table proximity.

The underlying principle Abaye articulates is: a *gezeirah ligezeirah* is justified not as a matter of abstract principle but as a function of how naturally and readily one step leads to the next. Where the chain of causation is realistic and close at hand, we may stack safeguards. Where the feared outcome is remote and practically unreachable, we do not.

## Safeguards & Definitions

### Student Objections

A natural question arises: why would people not simply be taught the distinction? Chicken is one thing, beef another; one is rabbinic, one biblical. If people understood the boundary, the slope would not be slippery at all.

### Cultural Kashrus Complex…

The concern, it should be emphasized, is not about people who knowingly intend to transgress — such people would ignore *gezeirot* along with everything else. The concern is about well-intentioned people who confuse similar cases or who absorb the logic of one situation and apply it, perhaps unconsciously, to an adjacent one. And this is a realistic worry. Ask many people who carefully observe *basar b'chalav* whether chicken and milk is biblical or rabbinic, and a significant number will not know — because we do not think in terms of the verse about the kid goat, we think in terms of the category of meat and milk. Chicken sits inside that category in popular consciousness, and someone who sees chicken and cheese together at a table may reason: if that is permitted, why not this?

The difficulty of maintaining categorical distinctions in practice is not unique to this domain. The *Gemara* throughout *Shas* addresses the distinction between the *chaver* and the *am ha-aretz* — and the *am ha-aretz* is not defined as a person of bad faith, but as one who is not scrupulous about *tithes* and *tumah ve-tahara*. These were observant Jews who simply did not track all the fine distinctions. The rabbinic response was sometimes to flatten those distinctions — to impose uniform rules that are easier to maintain precisely because they do not require the practitioner to navigate subtle gradations. More stringent, perhaps, but more sustainable. The extension of *basar b'chalav* restrictions to poultry may reflect exactly this instinct: rather than require everyone to track which prohibitions are biblical and which rabbinic, we apply a uniform standard and avoid the slippage.

## Abaye Slippery Slope…

### Rav Sheshet Challenge

A further challenge to the table-proximity safeguard is raised. Even granting that birds and cheese cannot be at the same table, any such scenario involves cold food — *tzon u-mitzon hu*. There is no biblical prohibition at stake in this setting regardless of what happens. If you have steak and cheese at the same table, you cannot commit a biblical violation there and then; the biblical prohibition requires cooking. Even if the concern is that the person will eat them together, that too involves cold food and is itself only rabbinic. There seems to be no chain that leads from the table to a biblical violation, so why bother with the safeguard?

### Hot Pot Response

Abaye responds: *gezeirah shema ya'aleh be-ilfas rotei'ach* — there is a concern that one might bring a hot, boiling stew pot directly to the table. In that case, the food is genuinely hot and capable of cooking whatever falls into it. If the cheese is right there and a piece falls in, you have a biblical *basar b'chalav* violation right at the table.

The *Gemara* interjects: but surely what arrives at the table is not the pot from the stove but a serving vessel — a *kli sheni*, a second vessel — and a *kli sheni eino mevashel*, a second vessel cannot cook. Even if cheese were to fall in, the cooking threshold would not be reached. The *Gemara* answers: *ela gezeirah shema ya'aleh b'kli rishon* — the concern is specifically for those occasions when the original cooking pot is brought directly to the table, still boiling. That is a *kli rishon*, and there, dropping cheese into the pot would create a biblical *basar b'chalav* violation. It is not the common scenario, but it is a possible one, and that possibility suffices to justify the safeguard.

### Kli Sheni Discussion

This leads naturally to the question of what distinguishes a *kli rishon* from a *kli sheni* in *kashrus* contexts, and how this compares to *Shabbat*. Conceptually, one might expect *kashrus* to be stricter than *Shabbat* on this question, since *Shabbat*'s prohibition of cooking focuses on process — the act must resemble the classic form of cooking, which takes place on a flame or in a primary vessel. By contrast, *kashrus* concerns are oriented toward result: did the flavors become intermingled? That would suggest that any significant heat, regardless of vessel, should create a *kashrus* concern, even in a *kli sheni*.

In practice, however, the opposite stringency obtains. Because *Shabbat* violations are so weighty, authorities are reluctant to rely on the *kli sheni* exemption and often extend caution even to secondary and tertiary vessels. For *kashrus*, where the stakes are serious but not at the level of *Shabbat*, we are more willing to follow the *Gemara*'s lead and draw the line at *kli rishon*. This is an example of stringency shaped not by the underlying conceptual logic but by the relative severity of the domain.

## Birds/Cheese Dispute

### Mishna Dispute Rules

The next *mishnah* addresses the question of birds and cheese directly, and notably in a context of dispute: *of oleh im hagvinah al hashulchan, ve'eino ne'echal, divrei Beis Shammai* — birds may be brought together with cheese on the table but may not be eaten together, in the view of Beis Shammai. *U'Beis Hillel omrim, lo oleh velo ne'echal* — Beis Hillel holds that they may neither be brought to the table nor eaten together.

### Debate Indicates Rabbinic

The very existence of this debate is significant. Biblical prohibitions are not subject to this kind of halakhic dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. The fact that they disagree about birds and cheese strongly supports the view that the bird prohibition is rabbinic — had it been biblical, we would not expect it to be a matter of open debate.

### Rabbi Yosi Comment

Rabbi Yosi adds: *zu mikulei Beis Shammai u'mechumrei Beis Hillel* — this is one of the cases where Beis Shammai is lenient and Beis Hillel is strict. The *Gemara* will note that this attribution is not new information — it says precisely what the *Tanna Kamma* already said. The *mishnah*'s purpose in citing Rabbi Yosi by name is explained by the *Gemara* through the well-known principle: *kol ha-omer davar beshem omro meivi geula la-olam* — whoever quotes a teaching in the name of its originator brings redemption to the world, as the verse says: *va-tomer Esther la-melech beshem Mordechai* — Esther told the king in Mordechai's name, and that ultimately led to the redemption.

It is worth noting that Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi sometimes chose to render rulings *stam* — anonymously — to give them greater normative weight. Here, by contrast, he attributed the ruling to Rabbi Yosi explicitly. An additional reason may be that it is so unusual for Beis Shammai to be the lenient party that explicit attribution was necessary to prevent readers from assuming an error and reversing the positions.

## Safeguards & Definitio…

### Table Type Distinction

The *mishnah*'s ruling — that birds and cheese may not appear together on the table — is clarified by the *Gemara*: *be'eizeh shulchan amru, beshulchan she'ochel alav* — this applies to the table at which one actually eats. *Aval beshulchan shesoder alav es hatavshil* — but the table on which food is arranged, like a serving counter or buffet surface, is treated differently. There, one may place them side by side without concern: *noten zeh b'tzad zeh ve'eino choshesh*.

## Birds/Cheese Dispute…

### Table Type Clarification

This distinction is striking because intuitively one might expect the preparation surface to be the *more* dangerous location — the food there is being actively handled, often still hot, and the conditions for actual *bishul* of *basar b'chalav* are more readily present. Yet this is precisely where the restriction is *relaxed*. The *Gemara* clarifies that the prohibition applies to the eating table specifically.

### Rashi Explanations

Rashi explains the distinction on psychological grounds: at the eating table, one is engaged in conversation, relaxed, mentally occupied — exactly the conditions under which one's attention wanders and one might mindlessly reach across and combine prohibited items. By contrast, at the preparation surface, one is actively and consciously engaged in the task of arranging the food; the focused, purposeful activity of setting up a meal is not the setting in which inadvertent mixing occurs.

## Safeguards & Definitio…

### Extension Framework

Rashi's explanation is illuminating, but there may be a deeper conceptual point at work. Rashi himself uses a phrase in connection with the reason for *gezeirah ligezeirah* in this context that is suggestive: *ha'ala'ah zo achila* — bringing to the table, that is eating. It is possible to read this not merely as "bringing to the table easily becomes eating" but as a definitional claim: at the rabbinic level, having meat and milk at the same table *constitutes* a form of eating them together.

This reframing helps make sense of the entire architecture of these extensions. The *Gemara* and later *halakha* develop an elaborate series of restrictions: no cooking together (biblical), no eating together even cold (rabbinic), no sharing a table, waiting periods between eating one and then the other, hand-washing, mouth-rinsing. If all of these are understood as a chain of safeguards, each designed to prevent the next violation, the chain becomes unwieldy and the reasoning strained. But if we understand them instead as rabbinic *redefinitions* of what it means to eat meat and milk together, the structure becomes more coherent.

On this reading, the Rabbis did not simply say "don't eat chicken with milk or you might come to eat beef with milk." Rather, they conceptually expanded the definition of *basar b'chalav* eating to encompass a broader range of scenarios: eating them cold together, having them at the same table, eating one shortly after the other while the taste of the first remains. Each of these is, in the rabbinic imagination, a form of eating meat and milk together. That is why the distinction between an eating table and a preparation surface makes intuitive sense: at the eating table you *are eating* — and having meat and milk present at that table is, conceptually, eating them together. At the preparation surface you are not eating; you are working. The prohibition of *eating* them together does not apply there, regardless of the physical proximity.

## Meat After Cheese Rules

### Igra Baraita

*Tana Igra chamu-ei de-Rebbi Abba* — Igra, the father-in-law of Rabbi Abba, taught a *baraita*: *of ve-gevina ne'echalin be-afikoren* — bird and cheese may be eaten without any particular separation or ceremony between them. He both transmitted this as a tradition and provided its commentary: *belo netilas yadayim u-velo kinuach ha-peh* — without washing the hands and without rinsing or wiping the mouth.

As is well known, the rule after eating dairy before meat requires ensuring that no dairy residue remains — on one's hands or in one's mouth — before consuming meat. For ordinary meat, one washes the hands and cleans the mouth; for birds, because the prohibition is only rabbinic, one need not take even these steps, and one may eat cheese and then bird in immediate succession. The leniency reflects the rabbinic status of the bird prohibition.

The question of whether this applies to eating in order of milk first then meat, or the reverse, or both, is one we will address in the next session. For now we assume the simpler scenario: dairy first, then the meat.

### Rav Ashi Story

An illustrative story is then recounted. *Rebbi Yitzchak brei d'Rav Mesharshiya* visited the home of Rav Ashi. They brought him cheese and he ate it; then they brought him meat — ordinary *basar behema* — and he ate it as well. *V'lo mashi yadei* — he did not wash his hands between the two. Those present challenged him: but did not Igra teach that even in the case of birds and cheese one eats without concern — implying that by contrast, in the case of red meat and cheese, one must take precautions? Why, then, did Rabbi Yitzchak not wash his hands?

### Daytime Visibility

He replied: *hani mili b'leilya* — those precautions are necessary at night. *Aval b'yimama ha kachazina* — but during the day, I can inspect my hands directly. I saw that my hands were clean. One might ask what became of the concern about the mouth; presumably he had eaten bread or drunk something in the course of the meal that served the function of cleaning the mouth, though the text focuses on the hand issue alone.

### Wipe vs Rinse

A *baraita* then addresses the mouth-cleaning requirement directly: *Beis Shammai omrim mekane'ach u'Beis Hillel omrim medi'ach* — Beis Shammai says one wipes out the mouth, Beis Hillel says one rinses the mouth.

### Either/Or Interpretation

The *Gemara* proceeds to interpret this dispute carefully. One might initially read it as Beis Shammai requiring only wiping (not rinsing) and Beis Hillel requiring only rinsing (not wiping), each holding that their preferred method alone is necessary and sufficient. But this creates a difficulty: Rav Zeira will rule that wiping the mouth requires bread — a specific food that actually cleans the oral surfaces — and he rules this as normative *halakha* in accordance with Beis Hillel. If Beis Hillel held that rinsing (not wiping) was required, there would be no reason to specify the mechanics of wiping as the Hillel position.

Alternatively, one might read Beis Hillel as requiring *both* wiping and rinsing, with Beis Shammai requiring only wiping. But this would make Beis Hillel the stricter party — and would have to be included in the list of Beis Shammai leniencies and Beis Hillel stringencies, which it is not.

The *Gemara* therefore resolves: *mar machada u'mar machada v'lo pligi* — each authority is describing one sufficient option, and they do not actually disagree. Beis Shammai says you may wipe (and equally you could rinse); Beis Hillel says you may rinse (and equally you could wipe). Either method alone is sufficient; neither requires the other. The dispute is not prescriptive but descriptive — each school mentions what it considers the more natural or preferable approach, while both agree that either works.

## Mouth Cleaning Details

### Bread Requirements

Rav Zeira specifies: *ein kinuach hapeh ela b'pas* — the mouth is properly wiped only with bread. This refers to actual bread used to scrub the mouth, not a napkin.

### Bread Qualifications

The specification is refined: *v'hani mili b'dichti* — this applies to wheat bread. Barley bread does not work because it crumbles and fails to clean effectively. And even wheat bread qualifies only when it is cold — hot bread tears and crumbles similarly. Nor should it be too hard, as extremely hard bread will break apart rather than clean.

### Practical Food Rules

Having laid out all these qualifications, the *Gemara* concludes with the practical ruling: *v'hilchasa, b'chol mili havei kinuach* — in practice, any food suffices to clean the mouth. The only exceptions are flour (*kimcha*), dates (*tamri*), and vegetables (*yarka*), which are ineffective as cleansing agents. Any other food is acceptable.

## Summary & Preview

### Prohibitions So Far

To summarize where we stand: the *mishnah* prohibits having meat and dairy together on the table. The Amoraic discussion has introduced the additional requirements, traceable to a *baraita* attributed as far back as the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel debates, that even eating them sequentially requires ensuring that the hands are clean and the mouth has been wiped or rinsed. In the case of birds, because the prohibition is rabbinic, even these sequential requirements are relaxed.

### Order Direction Note

All of the scenarios discussed in today's *daf* — the table prohibition, the hand-washing, the mouth-cleaning — assume the same order: dairy first, meat second. The question of the reverse order, eating meat before dairy, and the famous question of waiting periods, will be taken up in the next session.
