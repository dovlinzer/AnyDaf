# Kiddushin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Position

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אַחַת מִכֶּם״ וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת? לְרָבָא קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, לְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא. אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, רָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where he said to them: One of you** is betrothed, **and it teaches** that **the sisters are not betrothed,** because with regard to the sisters the betrothal is not given to consummation? The Gemara comments: If so, the **first clause** of the mishna **is difficult for Rava,** while the **last clause is difficult for Abaye.** The Gemara presents a resolution: **Abaye explains** the last clause according **to his** line of **reasoning,** and **Rava explains** the first clause according **to his** line of **reasoning.**

The sugya continues from where we left off on 51b, in the middle of Rava's position regarding *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via* — a betrothal that cannot ultimately lead to consummation. Although *kiddushin* ordinarily precedes *nisuin* by a considerable period, Rava holds that if the *kiddushin* can never translate into a permissible marital relationship, it is not a valid *kiddushin* to begin with.

A clarification is necessary at the outset. As Tosafos already notes, there are prohibited relationships — *isurei lavin*, such as a *kohen* marrying a *gerusha* — where the couple is forbidden from having relations, yet the *kiddushin* takes effect regardless. Rava's principle is confined to *isurei kareis*: if the relationship would carry the penalty of *kareis*, everyone agrees the *kiddushin* does not take effect at all. The contested terrain is the case where the *kiddushin* itself would be valid but uncertainty about the identity of the woman makes consummation perpetually impossible.

### Two Sisters Case

The paradigmatic example is a man who betroths one of two sisters without knowing which one he has married. Rava's contention is that in such a case, neither woman requires a *get mi'safek*. Since he can never have relations with either — doing so might mean sleeping with his wife's sister — the *kiddushin* is fundamentally defective. The doubt itself prevents the *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via* from ever taking effect.

### Abaye's View

Abaye disagrees with Rava's position. Thus far, the evidence presented in the sugya has consistently cut against Rava, though Rava has managed to respond to each textual challenge. The Gemara now continues pressing the point.

## Mi Sheyeish Lo

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי כִּתֵּי בָנוֹת מִשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וְאָמַר: ״קִידַּשְׁתִּי אֶת בִּתִּי הַגְּדוֹלָה, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּגְּדוֹלוֹת וְאִם גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת, אִם קְטַנָּה שֶׁבַּגְּדוֹלוֹת שֶׁהִיא גְּדוֹלָה מִן הַגְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת״ – כּוּלָּן אֲסוּרוֹת, חוּץ מִקְּטַנָּה שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (64b): With regard to **one who has two groups of daughters from two women,** e.g., he has multiple daughters from his first wife, and after his first wife died he remarried and had multiple daughters with his second wife, **and he said: I betrothed my elder daughter** to someone, **but I do not know if** it was the **eldest of the older** group of daughters **or if** it was **the eldest of the younger** group of daughters, or **if** it was the **youngest of the older** group, **who is** nevertheless **older than the eldest of the younger** group; **all** the daughters **are prohibited** from marrying someone else without first receiving a bill of divorce, **except for the youngest of the younger** group, who is certainly not betrothed. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** This betrothal is not given to consummation, since each daughter could be the sister of his actual betrothed, and yet the betrothal is considered valid, as otherwise they would all be permitted to marry without requiring a bill of divorce.

The Gemara introduces a *braisa*: *mi sheyeish lo shtei kitei banot mi'shtei nashim* — a man who has two sets of daughters from two wives, each wife having borne him multiple daughters. The man declares that he married his older daughter — *kidashti et biti hagdola* — to a particular man, but he cannot remember which daughter he meant. Several possibilities present themselves: he may have meant the oldest among the children from his first wife, or the older one from his second wife's children, or even a younger daughter from the first wife who is nonetheless older than the oldest daughter of the second wife.

### Get Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּשֶׁהוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי: ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״אֵין יָדוּעַ״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a situation **where they were identified and later intermingled.** In other words, this mishna is not dealing with one who betrothed a daughter without specifying which one; rather, he initially stated which daughter he intended to betroth but subsequently forgot. The actual betrothal is given to consummation, so the daughters are betrothed due to the uncertainty. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** that he said: **I do not know,** in the first person and in the present tense, indicating that he did know who it was at one point but does not know now, **and it does not teach** that he said: **It is not known.** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from** the language of the mishna that we are indeed dealing with a case where the daughters were intermingled after they were identified.

The ruling — this is the position of Rabbi Meir — is that *kulan asurot*: all of these daughters are potentially married to this man and require a *get*, with the single exception of the youngest daughter of the second wife, *katanat sheba-ktanot*, whom the father could not possibly have meant when he said "my older daughter."

This case seems to pose a strong challenge to Rava. Here is a situation precisely analogous to marrying one of two sisters — the *choson* cannot have relations with any of the daughters because whichever one is truly his wife, the others are potentially her sisters. Yet the *kiddushin* is treated as taking effect *mi'safek*. If Rava were correct, none of them should require a *get* at all.

### Initial Knowledge

The Gemara deflects the proof: *hacha b'mai askinan — keshehukru u'levasof nisarvu*. The case is not one where the father never knew which daughter he intended. Rather, at the moment of *kiddushin* he knew precisely which daughter he had in mind, and only afterward did he forget. The *kiddushin* therefore took effect validly at the moment it was performed — when it was *mesura l'via* — and the subsequent confusion does not retroactively undo it.

### Linguistic Proof

The Gemara adds a textual confirmation: *deika nami d'katani eini yodea* — the *braisa* uses the first-person "I do not know," meaning the father currently does not know, rather than the impersonal *eino yaduia*, "it is not known" — which would indicate it was never known. This linguistic precision supports the interpretation that knowledge existed initially and was subsequently lost.

### Rabbi Yose Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: לָא מַחֵית אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמַחֵית אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** this; what novel *halakha* does it teach? The Gemara answers: The mishna serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei, who says: A person does not place himself** into a situation **of uncertainty,** and when he said elder daughter he must have meant the eldest of the older group, who is the absolute eldest. Rabbi Meir **teaches us that a person does place himself** into a situation **of uncertainty,** and the uncertainty applies to each of his daughters, except for the youngest of the youngest.

But if so, the Gemara asks, what is the *chiddush* of the *braisa*? If the father once knew and merely forgot, it seems obvious that all the daughters would require a *get*. The Gemara answers: *le'apokei mid'bei Rabbi Yose*. The *chiddush* is that Rabbi Meir disagrees with Rabbi Yose's position.

Rabbi Yose would reason that if a person says "my older daughter" and that is the only detail he can recall, we assume he meant it in the most unambiguous sense — the oldest of all his daughters. The reasoning is *lo machis inish nafshei li'sefeika*: a person does not allow himself to enter into such a state of doubt. If "older daughter" were ambiguous in your own mind, you would have been more precise in your formulation so you could remember. The very fact that you remember only "older daughter" implies you meant it to refer to one clearly identifiable person.

Rabbi Meir rejects this assumption. *Ka mashma lan demachis inish nafshei li'sefeika* — people do in fact allow themselves to use loose formulations and wind up in precisely this kind of doubt. Therefore, all the daughters remain candidates, and all require a *get* save the youngest of the youngest. That is the *chiddush*.

## One of Two Sisters

### Marriage Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִי שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ אַחַת מִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזוֹ קִידֵּשׁ – נוֹתֵן גֵּט לָזוֹ וְגֵט לָזוֹ. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּשֶׁהוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי: ״אֵינוֹ יָדוּעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Yevamot* 23b): In the case of **one who betrothed one of two sisters and he does not know which** one **he betrothed,** he must **give a bill of divorce to this** one **and a bill of divorce to that** one, due to the uncertainty. This betrothal is not given to consummation, so why do they require bills of divorce? The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is **when they were identified and later intermingled.** The actual betrothal was given to consummation, so the sisters are betrothed due to the uncertainty. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** the phrase: **He does not know,** indicating that he did know who it was at one point but now does not know, **and it does not teach** the phrase: **It is not known.**

The Gemara presents another challenge: *mi shekidesh achas mi'shtei achayot v'eino yodeia eizeh kidesh* — a man married one of two sisters but does not know which one he married. Again, we are invited to read this as a case where he never knew, which would make it a direct refutation of Rava.

### Get Solution

The ruling is *nosein get lazo v'get lazo* — he must give a *get* to each sister, since each one is in a state of *safek* betrothal to him. This appears once more to demonstrate that *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via* takes effect.

### Knew Then Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? סֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ: מֵת וְלוֹ אָח אֶחָד, חוֹלֵץ לִשְׁתֵּיהֶן. הָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנַיִם – אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ וְאֶחָד מְיַיבֵּם. אִם קָדְמוּ וְכָנְסוּ, אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָם מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** this; what novel *halakha* does it teach? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach **the latter clause** of that mishna: If this man **died and he had one brother,** the brother **performs *ḥalitza* with both of** the sisters, but he cannot perform levirate marriage with either because one of them is the sister of his *yevama*, who is forbidden to him by rabbinic law. If **he had two** brothers, **one** of them **performs *ḥalitza*** with one of the sisters but may not take her in levirate marriage, due to the possibility that she is the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond. **And** then **one takes** the other **in levirate marriage** if he so desires. If both brothers **married** the sisters **before** consulting the court, the court does **not remove them from** the marriage and they are permitted to remain married.

The Gemara again deflects: *hacha b'mai askinan — keshehukru levasof v'nisrefu*. Once more, the case involves a situation where the identity was known at the outset and subsequently forgotten. The linguistic indicator is the same: *eini yodea* rather than *eino yaduia*.

The Gemara then asks again: if that is so, what is the *chiddush*? The answer is: *seifa itzterichah lei* — the *braisa* is needed for what follows, namely the complex laws of *chalitza* and *yibum* that arise if the husband dies without the confusion having been resolved.

## Yibum/Chalitza

### Death Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּוְקָא מִיחְלָץ וַהֲדַר יַבּוֹמֵי, אֲבָל יַבּוֹמֵי וַהֲדַר מִיחְלָץ – לָא, דְּקָא פָּגַע בִּיבָמָה לַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the novelty of the latter clause: This is effective **specifically** if one of the two brothers first **performs *ḥalitza* and afterward** the other brother **performs levirate marriage, but** if one of the two brothers first **performs levirate marriage and afterward** the other brother **performs *ḥalitza*,** the levirate marriage would **not** take effect, **as he** is possibly **encountering a *yevama*** getting married **to** a member of the **public.** This is because it is possible that the woman he married was not his *yevama* but someone else's *yevama*, and until the other man's brother performs *ḥalitza* with her it is still prohibited for her to marry other men.

The *braisa* continues: if this man — who was married *mi'safek* to one of two sisters — dies, and he has one surviving brother, that brother must perform *chalitza* with both women, since one of them is his brother's widow and requires either *yibum* or *chalitza*.

### Multiple Brothers

If there were two brothers, the ruling is *echad choleitz v'echad miyabeim* — one brother performs *chalitza* and the other may perform *yibum*. Furthermore, if they both already went ahead and married both sisters — *im kadmu v'kanesu* — we do not remove them from the marriage.

### Achot Zekukato

Why can a single brother not simply perform *yibum* with one of the sisters directly, without first performing *chalitza* for the other? Because before *chalitza* is performed, both brothers maintain a *zikah* — a levirate bond — to both women. If a brother were to perform *yibum* with one sister while that *zikah* remains intact, he might be marrying *achot zekukato* — the sister of a woman to whom he is already quasi-bonded. That is prohibited.

The mechanics of two brothers work as follows. Suppose brother A performs *chalitza* for sister 1. This removes the *zikah* that both brothers held toward her. Brother B, who did not himself perform the *chalitza*, has no *achot chalutzato* issue. He is therefore free to perform *yibum* with sister 2. Since it was brother A who did the *chalitza*, sister 1 is *achot chalutzato* only with respect to brother A — not to brother B. Brother B's *yibum* with sister 2 is therefore unproblematic.

If, however, the brothers had not first done *chalitza* and both simply proceeded to marry the two sisters — *kinsu* — the fact that *achot zekukato* is only a *d'rabbanan* prohibition, combined with the fact that the *zikah* dissipates once the marriages are performed, means we allow the marriages to stand after the fact.

### Already Married

The principle established is: once they are already married, *ein motzi'in osam mi'yadam* — we do not remove them from their marriages.

### Proper Sequence

The proper sequence is therefore explicit: *davka michlatz v'hadar yabumi* — specifically, *chalitza* must precede *yibum*. If one brother does *yibum* first and only then the other does *chalitza*, it does not work, *d'ka paga b'achot zekukato* — because the brother doing *yibum* before the *chalitza* was performed would be engaging with the sister of a woman to whom he still has a *zikah*. This is the genuine *chiddush* of the passage — a *halacha* in the laws of *yibum*, not a ruling about *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via*.

## Two Men Two Sisters

### Mixed Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאֶחָד חוֹלֵץ לִשְׁתֵּיהֶן, וְהַשְּׁנַיִם – אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ וְאֶחָד מְיַבֵּם. אִם קָדְמוּ וְכָנְסוּ – אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **the one** brother **performs *ḥalitza* with both of them, and** of **the two** brothers, **one performs *ḥalitza*** with one of the sisters **and one,** if he so desires, **performs levirate marriage** with the other sister. **If they married** the widows **before** consulting the court, the court **does not remove them from** the marriage, and they are permitted to remain married.

The Gemara moves to yet another challenge: *shnayim shekidshu shtei achayot v'zeh eino yodeia eizo kiddesh v'zeh eino yodeia eizo kiddesh* — two men, who are not brothers, each married one of two sisters. Each man married one sister and each does not know which sister he married. Once again, the assumption is that this means the men never knew.

### Get Distribution

The ruling is that each man must give a *get* to both women. Man A might be married to Rachel or to Leah, so he must give a *get* to each. Similarly for Man B. Rachel and Leah cannot remarry until they have received a *get* from each man, since either one might be married to either man. Each man thus gives two *gittin*, one to each of the two women.

Once more the Gemara answers that this too is a case of *keshehukru levasof v'nisarvu* — initially known, subsequently forgotten. The *seifa itzterichah lei* — and the real novelty of the ruling emerges in the more complex scenarios that follow.

### Unequal Brothers

If both men die, and each leaves a surviving brother, each brother must perform *chalitza* for both women. But the *braisa* then introduces an asymmetric case: one man left one brother, and the other left two brothers. The single brother — let us call him B' — performs *chalitza* for both women and thereby removes himself entirely from the scene. Only once B' has stepped off entirely are the two brothers of the other man — A' and A'' — in a position analogous to the earlier case of two brothers with two sisters: one performs *chalitza* and one performs *yibum*.

### Sequence Req.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל יַבּוֹמֵי וַהֲדַר מִיחְלָץ – לָא, דְּקָא פָּגַע בִּיבָמָה לַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** but if one of the two brothers first **performs levirate marriage and afterward** the other brother **performs *ḥalitza*,** the levirate marriage would **not** take effect, **as he** is possibly **encountering a *yevama*** getting married **to** a member of the **public.**

The sequence is critical. B' must first perform *chalitza* for both women before A' and A'' can proceed with their *chalitza* and *yibum*. If A' and A'' were to act before B' performed his *chalitza*, one of the two women would be a *yevamah* — a levirate widow obligated to either B' — who has not yet released her. Marrying her before that *chalitza* is performed constitutes the prohibition of *yevamah la'shuk* — a levirate widow going out into the marketplace. Only after B' has performed *chalitza* for both women can the remaining two brothers proceed.

Once again, the Gemara notes: *aval yabumei v'hadar michlatz lo* — if the two brothers of the first man were to perform *yibum* and *chalitza* before B' removes himself, it would not work, *d'ka paga b'yevamah la'shuk* — because one of these women is still awaiting her *chalitza* from B'. The *halacha* being taught here is the required sequence in a complex *yibum* case — not a ruling about *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via*.

## Five/Five Case

### Non-Specific Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי טָבְיוֹמֵי: לְזֶה חֲמִשָּׁה בָּנִים וּלְזֶה חֲמִשָּׁה בָּנוֹת, וְאָמַר: ״אַחַת מִבְּנוֹתֶיךָ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לְאֶחָד מִבָּנַי״ – כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת צְרִיכָה חֲמִשָּׁה גִּיטִּין. מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם – כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת צְרִיכָה אַרְבָּעָה גִּיטִּין, וַחֲלִיצָה מֵאֶחָד מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara derives a proof: **Come** and **hear, as** the Sage **Tavyumei taught** the following *baraita*: If **this** man **had five sons and that** man **had five daughters, and** the first man **said: One of your daughters is betrothed to one of my sons, each and every one** of the daughters **requires five bills of divorce,** one from each of the sons, due to the uncertainty of who is betrothed to whom. If **one of the** sons **died** before giving his bill of divorce, **each and every one** of the daughters **requires four bills of divorce,** one from each of the surviving brothers, **and *ḥalitza* from one of them,** in case she was betrothed to the deceased son. The *ḥalitza* of one of the brothers suffices to release her from her levirate bond. According to Rava this betrothal should not be valid, as it is not given to consummation. Why, then, do they each require bills of divorce?

The Gemara now arrives at what it treats as decisive. It cites a teaching of *Tiviumi*: one man has five sons, another has five daughters. The father of the sons declares: *achas mi'bnotecha mekudeshet l'echad mi'banai* — one of your daughters is married to one of my sons.

### Get Requirements

The ruling: *kol achas v'achas tzrichah chamishah gittin* — each of the five daughters requires five *gittin*, one from each of the five sons. Since no one knows which daughter was married to which son, every daughter is in a state of *safek* betrothal to every son.

### Death Adjustment

If one of the sons dies, each daughter now requires four *gittin* from the surviving sons and *chalitza* from one of them — since she might have been betrothed to the deceased son, triggering levirate obligation. Crucially, she does not need *chalitza* from all four surviving brothers — any one brother performing *chalitza* suffices. One brother thus both gives a *get* (as he might be her husband) and performs *chalitza* (as he might be her deceased brother-in-law's *yavam*).

### Non-Specific Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי כְּשֶׁהוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ, הָא ״אַחַת מִבְּנוֹתֶיךָ לְאֶחָד מִבָּנַי״ קָתָנֵי, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא, תְּיוּבְתָּא. וְהִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי בְּיַעַ״ל קַגַ״ם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **here too** it is referring to a case **where they were identified and later intermingled,** that cannot be. This is as the *baraita* **teaches: One of your daughters to one of my sons,** indicating that the betrothal could never have been given to consummation, and yet it is a valid betrothal. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation** of the opinion **of Rava is a conclusive refutation.** The Gemara further notes: **And the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye** when he has a dispute with Rava **with regard to** six *halakhot*, as represented by the mnemonic ***yod*, *ayin*, *lamed*, *kuf*, *gimmel*, *mem*:** Despair that is not conscious [*yeush shelo mida'at*], conspiring witnesses [*eidim*] who are disqualified retroactively, a side post [*leḥi*] standing alone, betrothal [*kiddushin*] that is not given to consummation, revealing intent with a bill of divorce [*get*], and an apostate [*mumar*] who sins rebelliously. Although the *halakha* generally is in accordance with the opinion of Rava in his disputes with Abaye, in these six cases the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Abaye.

What makes this case decisive is the precise language of the declaration: *achas mi'bnotecha l'echad mi'banai* — one of your daughters to one of my sons. The statement is non-specific from the outset. There is no question of "initially knew and then forgot." The marriage was constituted in entirely unspecified terms — and it takes effect. This directly refutes Rava: even *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via*, formed when the identity of the parties was never specified and consummation could never take place with any particular woman, is nonetheless valid.

### Rava Refuted

The Gemara declares: *tiyuvta d'Rava tiyuvta* — Rava is definitively refuted. As a general rule in *Shas*, the formulation *tiyuvta d'peloni tiyuvta* signals a complete rejection of that position and a ruling against it. Only on rare occasions does the Gemara say *tiyuvta v'hilchasa* — a refutation followed nonetheless by a ruling in keeping with the refuted position — and that itself prompts the Gemara to ask how such a thing can be. Here, the rejection is unambiguous.

### YAL KGAM Acronym

The Gemara then states: *v'hilchata k'vosei d'Abaye b'Ya'al Kegam* — although we generally rule like Rava in disputes with Abaye, there are six cases where we rule like Abaye, and they are encoded in the acronym *Ya'al Kegam*.

The *yud* stands for *yeiush shelo mi'da'as* — the question treated in the second chapter of Bava Metzia of whether giving up hope of recovering a lost object, had the owner known it was lost, constitutes valid *yeiush* even if he did not consciously despair. The *ayin* stands for *eid zomem* — whether a witness who is rendered a *zomem* is disqualified retroactively from the time he gave his testimony, not merely from the time the counter-witnesses testified against him. The *lamed*, though there is some debate about this position, stands for *lechi ha'omed mei'eilav*. The *kuf* — the case before us — stands for *kiddushin she'eina mesura l'via*: we follow Abaye that such a *kiddushin* takes effect. The *gimmel* stands for *gilluy da'as b'get*, the question of whether implicit indications of intent are operative even without explicit statement — a matter discussed in the context of *gittin* and the principle of *devarim she'belev*. The *mem* stands for *mumar ochel neveilot l'tei'avon* — whether someone who transgresses a particular prohibition due to *yetzer hara* rather than as an ideological rejection of *halacha* is disqualified as a witness. That is Rashi's account of the six; Tosafos offers a slightly different list.

It is worth noting, as Tosafos observes at the conclusion of his discussion of *Ya'al Kegam*, that there are other cases where the ruling follows Abaye against Rava — such as *techilaso bi'fshia v'sofo b'ones* — that are not included in the acronym. Tosafos resolves this by citing the *Halachos Gedolos*, who suggests that the *hilchata* ruling in that case was not part of the original text of the Gemara but was appended by the *Rabbanan Savorai* or the *Geonim* after the *chatimas ha'Shas*. This is a broader phenomenon: certain *hilchata* rulings that appear embedded in the Aramaic of the Gemara were actually added by post-*siyum* editors whose glosses were subsequently absorbed into the received text. The fact that some rulings following Abaye are not in *Ya'al Kegam* is thus explained as those rulings having been formulated after the original editing of the Talmud.

One final structural observation: the father of the five daughters could marry off his daughters to one of the five sons because he has the legal right to betroth his daughters. But the father of the five sons — what authorizes him to participate? One must say that each son appointed his father as his *shliach*: whichever woman you choose for me, I accept.

## Five Women/Figs

### Marriage Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים. אָמַר רַב: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִמַּתְנִיתִין אַרְבַּע, וְנָקֵיט רַב בִּידֵיהּ תְּלָת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **An incident** occurred **involving five women,** and among them were two sisters. And one person gathered a basket of figs that were from their field, and the fruit was of the Sabbatical Year. And he said: You are hereby all betrothed to me with this basket, and one of them accepted it on behalf of all of them. And the Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed. **Rav says:** One can **learn four** *halakhot* **from the mishna.** The Gemara adds: **And Rav held three** of them **in his hand,** i.e., knew what they were, but he was uncertain about the fourth.

The Gemara now returns to the *ma'aseh* — the incident involving five women and the basket of figs that opens the mishnah. A man took figs and used them to attempt to betroth all five women simultaneously, with two sisters among the group. Rav declares: *shema mina mimatnisin arba* — our mishnah teaches four *halachos*. However, he adds: *v'nakad Rav b'yadei tlas* — he only has clear rulings for three of them, because the mishnah's teaching regarding the fourth remains ambiguous to him.

### Shemita Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּגָזֵל – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, אֲפִילּוּ בְּגָזֵל דִּידַהּ. מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי: שֶׁלָּהֶם הָיְתָה וְשֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה – טַעְמָא דִּשְׁבִיעִית, דְּהֶפְקֵר הוּא, הָא דִּשְׁאָר שְׁנֵי שָׁבוּעַ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav clarifies: **Learn from it** that with regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with produce of** the **Sabbatical** Year, **she is betrothed,** and you do not say that this produce can be used only for eating and not for any other purpose. **And learn from it** that if one **betroths** a woman **with a stolen item, even** if it was **her stolen item, she is not betrothed.** You do not say that her accepting from him an item that he stole from her indicates that she released him from his obligation to return it, rendering it his to use in order to betroth her. **From where** can these two *halakhot* be deduced? It is **from** the fact **that it teaches** that the produce **was from their** field, **and was of** the **Sabbatical** Year, which indicates: **The reason** it could be used for betrothal is that the produce was **of** the **Sabbatical** Year, **which** causes the figs to be **ownerless** property. **But** if it was one **of the other years of the seven-**year Sabbatical cycle, the betrothal would **not** take effect with stolen property, even if she accepted it for her betrothal.

The first *halacha*: *hamekadesh b'peiros shevi'is mekudeshes* — betrothal effected with the fruit of *shemita* is valid. The *chiddush*, as Rashi explains, lies in the prohibition against conducting business with *shemita* produce — *v'hayesa shabbat ha'aretz lachem le'ochla*, the Torah specifies the fruit is for eating. One might have thought that using *shemita* fruit as the medium of a transaction renders the *kiddushin* invalid, or that it constitutes prohibited commerce. The mishnah teaches that even so, the transaction is effective, and this use does not carry the characteristics of prohibited *s'chorah* with *shemita* fruit.

### No Stolen Goods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אִשָּׁה נַעֲשֵׂית שָׁלִיחַ לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית לָהּ צָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And learn from it: A woman can become an agent** to accept a betrothal **for another** woman, **and** this is so **even in a situation where she** thereby **becomes** the **rival wife of** the other, as in the case of the mishna.

The second *halacha*: *kidsha b'gezel eina mekudeshes* — betrothal with stolen property does not take effect. Since the man picked the women's figs and returned them as the medium of *kiddushin*, the only reason it worked was that it was *shemita* — when the fruit was *hefker* and he was legally entitled to take it. Had it not been *shemita*, returning their own figs to them would not have constituted valid *kesef kiddushin*, even though he stole the goods from the very women he wished to marry.

### Woman as Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ מַאי הִיא? קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִים לְבִיאָה. וְנִיחְשְׁבַהּ! מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי כְּאַבָּיֵי אִי כְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the other** *halakha*, the fourth one mentioned by Rav? It is the *halakha* of **betrothal that is not given to consummation.** The Gemara suggests: **And let** Rav also **count this** *halakha*. The Gemara explains: He does not do so, **because he is uncertain whether** the correct interpretation of the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Abaye,** who maintains that such a betrothal is valid, or **whether** the correct interpretation of the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rava,** which would mean it is not betrothal.

The third *halacha*: *ishah na'asis shliach la'chaverta* — a woman can serve as an agent for another woman to receive *kesef kiddushin* on her behalf, *afilu b'makom shena'asis lah tzara* — even in a case where the agency results in the woman becoming her co-wife. One might have thought that implicit consent to serve as *shliach* could not be assumed in such a circumstance, particularly since the woman accepted the *kiddushin* for herself at the same time without explicitly declaring herself an agent for the others. The mishnah teaches that she does serve as their agent nonetheless.

### Ambiguous Position

The fourth matter: the mishnah presumably has something to say about *kiddushin she'eino masur l'via*, since two of the women were sisters. However, Rav cannot determine what the mishnah is teaching on this point because the case admits two readings. If the man was attempting to marry both sisters simultaneously, then the mishnah's ruling that they are not married reflects the straightforward prohibition against marrying two sisters — not any principle about *kiddushin she'eino masur l'via*. In that reading, the mishnah would imply that ordinary *kiddushin she'eino masur l'via* — where only one sister was intended — would in fact work, consistent with Abaye. Alternatively, if the man was attempting to marry each sister separately without knowing which was which, then the sisters being unmarried would support Rava's position. Because the mishnah is susceptible to both readings, Rav cannot extract a clear ruling from it about *kiddushin she'eino masur l'via*.

### Rav's Inference

What Rav does extract — and what he presents as the mishnah's clear teaching — are the three *halachos* above. The fourth point remains, as far as Rav is concerned, unresolved.

## Gezel in Kiddushin

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא אַמְרַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי אָמַר רַב הָכִי? וְהוּא לָא אֲמַר?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּזַל וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵינָם יְכוֹלִים לְהַקְדִּישׁ, זֶה לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְזֶה לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ. הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִי אָמַר רַב כְּווֹתִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Zeira ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **he stated this *halakha* before Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who **said to him: Did Rav** actually **say so?** This indicates that Rabbi Yoḥanan disagreed with the statement of Rav that these *halakhot* can be learned from the mishna, which leads the Gemara to ask: **But didn't he** himself **say** so? **And didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** If one **robbed** another of an item **and** its **owner has not despaired** of recovering it, **neither of them can consecrate** the stolen item? **This** one, the robber, cannot consecrate it **because it is not his, and that** one, the owner, cannot consecrate it **because it is not in his possession.** By the same logic, one should not be able to betroth a woman with stolen property, as it is not his. The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yoḥanan was not objecting to Rav's opinion, but **this is** what **he said to him: Did Rav** actually **say in accordance with my** opinion?

When Rabbi Zeira ascended to *Eretz Yisrael* and related this discussion to Rabbi Yochanan — the inference that Rav drew from the mishnah about *gezel* — Rabbi Yochanan responded with evident interest: *mi amar Rav haki*? Did Rav really say this? The Gemara initially reads this as an expression of surprise that Rabbi Yochanan seemingly agrees. Rabbi Yochanan's own position is: *gazel v'lo ni'yashu ha'be'alim — shneihem einam yecholim l'hakdish* — if an object was stolen and the owners have not yet despaired of its recovery, neither the thief nor the owner can sanctify it.

The thief cannot sanctify it because it is not his. The owner cannot sanctify it — and here is the genuine *chiddush* of Rabbi Yochanan — because even though the owner retains legal title, the object is no longer under his control, and one cannot perform acts of sanctification over an object that is not in one's domain. By analogy, one cannot use an object to betroth a woman if one does not own it.

### Braita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּגָזֵל, בְּחָמָס, וּבִגְנֵיבָה, אוֹ שֶׁחָטַף סֶלַע מִיָּדָהּ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! הָתָם בְּגָזֵל דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Rav's opinion that one cannot betroth a woman with stolen property, from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:5): If one **betrothed her with** an item taken **in robbery, in a forced transaction, or in theft, or if he grabbed a *sela* from her hand and betrothed her** with it, **she is betrothed.** This indicates that one can betroth a woman with stolen property. The Gemara answers: **There** it is referring to **her stolen** property, and by accepting it from him as betrothal, she indicates that she has released him from his obligation to return it.

But the Gemara raises a challenge from a *braisa*: *kidsha b'gazel, b'chamas u'vigniva, o shechataph sela mi'yadah v'kidshah — mekudeshes* — if one betrothed a woman with stolen goods, with extorted money, or seized a coin from her hand and used it to betroth her, the betrothal is valid. This seems to directly contradict the principle that *kiddushin* with stolen property does not work.

### Stolen from Her

The Gemara resolves: *hasam b'gezel deidah* — that case involves property stolen from the woman herself. The reasoning is that when she accepts the object back from the thief in the context of a declaration of *kiddushin*, we interpret her acceptance as a waiver — she is *mochelet* the object to him, allowing him to use it as *kesef kiddushin*.

### Implicit Willingness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אוֹ שֶׁחָטַף סֶלַע מִשֶּׁלָּהּ – מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא בְּגָזֵל דְּעָלְמָא עָסְקִינַן! פָּירוּשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּגָזֵל, בְּחָמָס, וּבִגְנֵיבָה כֵּיצַד? כְּגוֹן שֶׁחָטַף סֶלַע מִיָּדָהּ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But from** the fact **that the last clause** of the *baraita* **teaches: Or if he grabbed a *sela* of hers, it may be inferred that** in **the first clause we are dealing with** property **stolen from others.** The Gemara explains: These are not two separate cases. Instead, the *tanna* **is explaining** his earlier statement: If one **betrothed** a woman **with** an item taken **in robbery, in a forced transaction, or in theft; how so? For example, if he grabbed a *sela* from her hand and betrothed her with it.**

The Gemara then examines this resolution more carefully. From the *braisa* itself, it appears that the opening clause — *kidsha b'gazel, b'chamas u'vigniva* — is stated in general terms, without specifying that the stolen goods belonged to the woman. Does this not indicate that even generically stolen goods can effect *kiddushin*? The Gemara answers: *perushei ka mefareish* — the end of the *braisa* is not adding a new case; it is explaining the opening clause. The meaning is: what kind of stolen goods are we speaking of? The case where he seized a coin from her own hand. Generic stolen goods from a third party do not work.

### Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מַתְנִיתִין, דְּגָזֵל דִּידַהּ, וְקָאָמַר רַב: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – דְּשַׁדֵּיךְ, הָא – דְּלָא שַׁדֵּיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this analysis: **But** doesn't **the mishna** deal **with** a case where the **stolen item** is **hers, and** yet **Rav says she is not betrothed.** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; this** case, in the *baraita*, is referring to a situation **where he had arranged** to betroth her beforehand, which indicates that she has released him from his obligation to return it, but **that** case, in the mishna, is referring to a situation **where he had not arranged** his marriage with her, so it is stolen property and she is not betrothed.

The Gemara now notes a remaining difficulty: our mishnah with the figs, as Rav interpreted it, establishes that even returning stolen goods from the woman herself does not effect *kiddushin* — the figs worked only because it was *shemita* and they were *hefker*. How then can the *braisa* say that seizing her coin and using it for *kiddushin* works? The answer: *ha d'shaddikh v'ha d'la shaddikh* — the distinction depends on whether *shidduchim*, a prior engagement process, had taken place. If the *kiddushin* is being performed in the context of an established betrothal arrangement, the woman's acceptance of her own goods back can be construed as an implicit waiver — she intends to let him have them so he can use them for the *kiddushin*. Without that prior context, the most natural reading of her taking back the coin is that she merely wants her property returned; there is no basis for inferring that she is extending a waiver. The mishnah's case of the figs took place without any such prior arrangement, which is why it would not have worked absent *shemita*.

## Ownership Disputes

### Woman Bathing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מָשְׁיָא כַּרְעַאּ בִּמְשִׁיכְלָא דְמַיָּא, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, חֲטַף זוּזֵי מֵחַבְרֵיהּ וּשְׁדָא לַהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: מִיקַּדְּשַׁתְּ לִי. אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר: לֵית דְּחָשׁ לְהָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: סְתַם גְּזֵילָה – יֵאוּשׁ בְּעָלִים הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who was washing her feet in a vessel of water. A certain man came** along, **grabbed** a few **dinars from another** person, **and threw** them **to her,** and **said to her: You are betrothed to me. That man** subsequently **came before Rava,** to inquire as to the status of the woman. Rava **said: There is not** anyone **who is concerned for this** opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, who said:** In **an ordinary** case of **robbery** the **owner has despaired** of recovering the stolen item, and it belongs to the robber. Rather, the assumption is that the owner has not despaired of recovering the stolen item. In this case, since the stolen dinars do not belong to the man, his betrothal is of no effect.

The Gemara now presents a series of practical cases testing the boundaries of *kiddushin* effected with another's property. In the first: a woman was washing her feet in a basin of water. A man came, seized a coin from someone else's hand, and threw it to her, declaring *mekudeshes li* — be married to me. The case came before Rava.

Rava ruled: *leis d'chash lah l'ha d'Rabbi Shimon* — we do not rule in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's position that ordinary *gezeila* — brazen robbery committed openly — creates a presumption of *yeiush ba'alim*, that the owners have despaired of recovering the stolen property. Rabbi Shimon's reasoning would be that the bold, public nature of the theft signals to the victim that recovery is hopeless, and thus *yeiush* is presumed. If *yeiush* had occurred, and the coin then passed to the woman — constituting *shinui reshuyot*, a change of domain — the combination of *yeiush* and *shinui reshuyot* might in principle allow the transaction to take effect, since the woman would acquire the coin cleanly and could not be required to return it.

The logic deserves elaboration: if there is *yeiush* followed by *shinui reshuyot*, the original owner's claim against the woman lapses, even though the thief still owes him compensation. In that case, the woman would be holding a coin she lawfully possesses, and the *kiddushin* might be valid. But since we do not follow Rabbi Shimon's presumption of *yeiush* in cases of *gezeila*, we cannot assume *yeiush* occurred, the coin remains fully stolen property, and the *kiddushin* does not take effect.

### Sharecropper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אֲרִיסָא דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּמוֹזָא דְשַׁמְכֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן אַחֲלָךְ? וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמוֹזָא, אֲבָל כִּישָׁא, מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא שְׁקַלִי כִּישָׁא, שְׁקֵיל אַתְּ כִּישָׁא, כִּישָׁא כִּי כִישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: The was **a certain sharecropper who betrothed** a woman **with a handful [*bemoza*] of onions [*deshamkhei*]** taken from the field where he worked. **He came before Rava** to ask about the status of the woman. Rava **said to him: Who relinquished** these onions **to you?** Since the owner did not allow you to take them, they are stolen property, and the woman is not betrothed. The Gemara comments: **And this matter applies** only **to a handful, but** if he took **a bundle** of onions and betrothed a woman with them, the sharecropper **can say to** the owner: **I took a bundle, you take a bundle; one bundle for** another **bundle.** Since in any case they divide the crop between them, it is not considered theft.

In the second case: a sharecropper betrothed a woman with a bundle of onions taken from the owner's field before the crop had been formally divided. He came before Rava. Rava said to him: *man achlah lach* — who was *mochel* to you, who permitted you to do this? The onions were not yet the sharecropper's to give. Although he would eventually be entitled to a percentage of the crop, that entitlement only crystallizes at the time of division.

### Bundled Crops

However, the Gemara qualifies this ruling: *v'hanei milei b'muza* — this applies only when the sharecropper took a random handful. If the produce had already been made into bundles — *kisha* — then one can say: *ana shakilna kisha, shakul at kisha* — I'll take one bundle, you take one bundle. Where produce has been gathered into units that lend themselves naturally to division, the sharecropper may perform an impromptu mini-division on the spot, taking one bundle as his share. That is regarded as a legally operative preliminary division rather than unauthorized taking. But to grab a handful before any such bundling has occurred is simply stealing from the owner's undivided crop.

### Beer Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא סְרָסְיָא דְּקַדֵּישׁ בִּפְרוּמָא דְשִׁיכְרָא. אֲתָא מָרֵיהּ דְּשִׁיכְרָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי לָא תִּיתֵּיב מֵהַאי חָרִיפָא? אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר: לֹא אָמְרוּ ״כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָפוֹת״, אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן תְּרוּמָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain brewer [*sarseya*]** who was making date beer for someone, **who betrothed** a woman **with sediment [*bifruma*] from the beer. The owner of the beer came** and **found him.** The owner **said to him: Why don't you give** her the betrothal **from this,** the **sharp** sediments that are of better quality than the kind you chose? The brewer **came before Rava** to ask whether the owner's comment indicated that he had relinquished his rights to the sediment, which would mean the woman is betrothed. Rava **said** to him: The Sages **said** that if the owner discovers that someone has taken something of his without permission and says: **Go to** and take **the** item of **better** quality, that it is a sign he agrees with the man's action **only with regard to *teruma* alone,** and you did not have the right to use the sediment.

The third case: a beer-maker — someone who received dates from an owner, fermented them into beer, and was entitled to a portion of the proceeds — betrothed a woman with a vessel belonging to the owner, which had been used for storing the beer and had absorbed its flavoring. The owner of the beer came, found him, and said: why did you give from that vessel? You should have given from a better one — apparently the more saturated vessels were more prized. The question was whether the owner's comment could be construed as retroactive consent to the act.

Rava ruled: *lo amru k'lach etzel yafot*, except *le'inyan truma bilvad* — the principle that saying "you should have taken the better ones" signals consent applies only in the context of *truma*, not here.

### Terumah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד אָמְרוּ: תּוֹרֵם שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה? הֲרֵי שֶׁיָּרַד לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ וְלִיקֵּט וְתָרַם שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, אִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לָאו – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the previous statement: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 1:5): **When did they say** that in the case where **one separates *teruma* without** the owner's **consent, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*?** The *baraita* clarifies: In a case where **there was** someone **who entered another's field and gathered** produce from it, **and separated *teruma* without** the owner's **permission, if** the owner is **concerned about** his actions and view it **as robbery, his *teruma* is not *teruma*, but if** he is **not** concerned, **his *teruma* is *teruma*.**

The operative *braisa*: if someone entered another person's field without permission and separated *truma* from the produce — *taram shelo bi'reshut* — when does the *truma* take effect? If the owner is distressed by it, treating it as theft, the *truma* is invalid. If not, it is valid. How do we determine his attitude? If the owner arrives, finds the situation, and says *k'lach etzel yafot* — you should have taken from the better produce — and there genuinely is better produce available, we take the statement at face value: he is indicating that he would have wanted better *truma* taken on his behalf, and this signals acceptance. If there is no better produce, the comment is read as biting sarcasm — his way of expressing displeasure at having his best grain consecrated without his consent — and the *truma* is invalid. And if the owner himself joins in the harvesting alongside the agent, the *truma* is valid regardless.

### Terumah vs. Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ אִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל אִם לָאו? הֲרֵי שֶׁבָּא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וּמְצָאוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָפוֹת״ אִם נִמְצְאוּ יָפוֹת מֵהֶם – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה. הָיוּ הַבְּעָלִים מְלַקְּטִים וּמוֹסִיפִים, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And from where would** the gatherer **know whether** he should be **concerned** that the owner objects and views it **as robbery or not?** If **the owner came and found him** separating *teruma* **and said to him: Go to** take **the** produce of **better** quality and separate *teruma* from that, then **if** produce of **better** quality **than** the produce he had separated **is found, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*,** since the owner is assumed to have been sincere and pleased that the other has separated *teruma* from his produce. **But if not, his *teruma* is not *teruma*,** as it may be assumed that the owner was angry at him and was speaking sarcastically. The *baraita* adds: If **the owners were gathering and adding** to the *teruma* he had separated, indicating that they agree to his act of separation, **either way,** whether or not better-quality produce was found, **his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*.**

Why does the "you should have taken the better ones" principle apply to *truma* but not to *kiddushin* with the owner's vessel? Because in the case of *truma*, the owner has an independent obligation to separate *truma* from his grain. When he speaks approvingly of what was done — provided the words can be read as genuine — we interpret them as indicating he ratifies the act, since it discharges an obligation he was in any event required to fulfill. By contrast, in the *kiddushin* case, no one obligated the beer-maker to use the owner's vessel to betroth a woman. Whatever the owner says in response to finding what happened, we cannot read it as endorsement. *Mishum kisufa de'avad* — the owner says what he says out of embarrassment for the beer-maker, not wishing to shame him publicly. It would be unreasonable to interpret social courtesy as legal consent.

### No Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם כִּיסּוּפָא הוּא דַּעֲבַד, וְאֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava concludes the explanation of his ruling: This *halakha* applies only to *teruma*, which is a mitzva that the owner must in any case perform. **But here,** in the case of the brewer who betrothed a woman with sediment from the beer, the owner **acts because of embarrassment,** and while he does not feel comfortable protesting, he did not in fact relinquish his rights to the sediment, **and she is not betrothed.**

The ruling: *v'eina mekudeshes* — the betrothal is invalid. The beer-maker used property that was not his and for which he had no authorization. Whatever the owner subsequently said cannot transform unauthorized use into permitted use, and therefore the *kiddushin* does not take effect.
