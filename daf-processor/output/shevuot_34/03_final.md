# Shevuot 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Braisah Overview

### Intro & Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״אוֹאִין״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אוֹאִין״; מָה לְהַלָּן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that the verse** with regard to an oath of testimony **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim? Rabbi Eliezer says:** Multiple instances of the term **"or" are stated here,** with regard to an oath of testimony: "And he hears the voice of an oath and he is a witness or he saw or he knew" (Leviticus 5:1), **and** multiple instances of the term **"or" are stated there,** with regard to an oath on a deposit: "And he deals falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of an outstanding loan, or of robbery, or he exploited his colleague, or he found a lost item" (Leviticus 5:21–22); **just as there,** the verse **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim, so too here,** the verse **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim.**

The daf opens in the middle of a long *braisah* that demonstrates how we know *shvuas ha'eidus* applies exclusively to monetary matters. The relevant verses state simply that he is a witness — *vehu eid o ra'ah o yada* — yet later in Vayikra, a separate *parsha* treats *shvuas hapikadon*, which concerns denying a monetary deposit or obligation owed to another: *o vitshumes yad o vegazel o ashak es amiso*. There the *korban* is an *asham*. The *braisah* draws a link between the two *parshot* and argues: just as *shvuas hapikadon* is entirely about monetary matters, so too *shvuas ha'eidus* is limited to monetary matters.

### O's Approach

The first method of linkage relies on the repeated use of *o* (*o, o, o*) — what the *Gemara* calls *o'in* — appearing in both passages, providing a textual parallel that ties them together.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֶאְשַׁם לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״ – יֵשׁ מֵאֵלֶּה שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, וְיֵשׁ מֵאֵלֶּה שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר. הָא כֵּיצַד? תְּבָעוֹ מָמוֹן – חַיָּיב, תְּבָעוֹ דָּבָר אַחֵר – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** that it is written with regard to an oath of testimony: **"And it shall be when he will be guilty of one of these"** (Leviticus 5:5). The term "of these" is a restrictive expression from which it is derived: **There are** some **of these** for **which he is liable and there are** some **of these** for **which he is exempt. How so?** If the plaintiff **demanded** testimony from the witness with regard to **a monetary** claim, the witness is **liable** for taking a false oath; if the plaintiff **demanded** testimony from the witness with regard to **another matter,** he is **exempt.**

Rabbi Akiva's approach derives from the phrase *achat me'eileh*, which introduces a distinction without specifying its content. He reads this as implying that while the *parsha* governs monetary cases, not all monetary cases are included.

### Rabbi Yose HaGlili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדָע״ – בְּעֵדוּת הַמִּתְקַיֶּימֶת בִּרְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה וּבִידִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** cites a different proof and **says:** The verse **states** with regard to an oath of testimony: **"And he is a witness or he saw or he knew"** (Leviticus 5:1). It is **with regard to testimony that is founded on sight,** i.e., direct observation, **without knowledge** of the matter, **or by means of** indirect **knowledge** of the matter **without sight** that **the verse is speaking.**

Rabbi Yose HaGlili focuses on the nature of the testimony described in *shvuas ha'eidus* — *vehu eid o ra'ah o yada* — testimony that can be established through knowledge without seeing or through seeing without knowledge. He argues that only monetary cases permit these hybrid forms of testimony, which is why the verse's formulation signals that the entire *parsha* is limited to money.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חִיֵּיב כָּאן וְחִיֵּיב בְּפִקָּדוֹן; מָה לְהַלָּן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן. וְעוֹד – קַל וָחוֹמֶר: מָה פִּקָּדוֹן – שֶׁעָשָׂה בּוֹ נָשִׁים כַּאֲנָשִׁים, קְרוֹבִים כִּרְחוֹקִים, פְּסוּלִים כִּכְשֵׁרִים; וְחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן;
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** cites a different proof and **says:** The Torah rendered one **liable** if he takes a false oath **here** with regard to testimony, **and** the Torah rendered one **liable** if he takes a false oath **with regard to a deposit; just as there,** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim, so too here,** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim. And furthermore,** one can infer this *halakha* by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference from the *halakha* of a deposit. **If** in the case of **a deposit, with regard to which** the Torah **rendered** the halakhic status **of women like** that of **men,** the halakhic status of **relatives like** that of **non-relatives,** and the halakhic status of **unfit** witnesses **like** that of those **fit** to testify, **and he is liable for each and every** oath if the plaintiff administered several oaths to him and he denied having the deposit in his possession, whether he took the oath **before a court or not before a court,** and despite the broad application of the *halakha*, the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim,** then in the case of an oath of **testimony** it is even more restricted.

Rabbi Shimon employs a *kal vachomer*: *shvuas hapikadon* has a broader scope of application — it encompasses men and women, those eligible to testify and those who are not — and yet it is limited to monetary matters. *Shvuas ha'eidus*, which has a narrower scope, should certainly be limited to monetary matters as well. It is in the middle of this *drasha* that the daf begins.

## Kal Vachomer Fails

### Kal Vachomer Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵדוּת שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהּ נָשִׁים כַּאֲנָשִׁים, קְרוֹבִים כִּרְחוֹקִים, פְּסוּלִין כִּכְשֵׁרִים; וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן?!
>
> **Translation:** **testimony with regard to which** the Torah **did not render** the halakhic status of **women like** that of **men,** the status of **relatives like** that of **non-relatives,** and the status of **unfit** witnesses **like** that of those **fit** to testify, **and he is liable** to bring **only one** sliding-scale offering if the plaintiff administered several oaths to him and he falsely denied knowledge of the matter **in the presence of a court, is it not right that** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim?**

Rabbi Shimon's *kal vachomer* is stated explicitly: if *shvuas hapikadon*, which does not treat women as men, does not treat relatives as non-relatives, does not treat the ineligible as the eligible, and limits liability to a single oath when the demand is made before *beis din* — if despite its narrower scope *shvuas hapikadon* is restricted to monetary matters, then *shvuas ha'eidus*, with its even narrower scope, certainly must be restricted the same way.

### Pikadon Narrower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְפִקָּדוֹן – שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מוּשְׁבָּע כַּנִּשְׁבָּע וּמֵזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג; תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדוּת – שֶׁכֵּן עָשָׂה בָּהּ מוּשְׁבָּע כַּנִּשְׁבָּע וּמֵזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this inference: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a deposit?** It is notable in **that with regard to** a deposit the Torah **did not render** the halakhic status of **one to whom an oath was administered** by others **like** that of **one who** himself **took an oath,** as one to whom an oath was administered by others is exempt; **and** the Torah did not render the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath **like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath. **Will you say** that the same is true **with regard to** an oath of **testimony, as in** that case the Torah **rendered** the halakhic status of **one to whom an oath was administered** by others **like** that of **one who** himself **took an oath; and** it rendered the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath **like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath, and one is liable to bring an offering in both instances?

The *braisah* immediately objects: *mah lapikadon sheken lo asa bo mushba k'nishba*. There are respects in which *shvuas hapikadon* is actually narrower than *shvuas ha'eidus*. First, *shvuas hapikadon* does not apply when the oath is imposed by another party — *mushba* — whereas *shvuas ha'eidus* applies even in such a case, as the Torah describes *v'shomea kol alah*, an oath imposed upon the witness. Second, *u'mezid k'shogeg*: *shvuas hapikadon*, like most *korbanos*, applies only when the violation is unintentional — *b'shogeg* — while *shvuas ha'eidus*, as derived from the absence of the word *v'ne'elam*, applies even when the transgression is willful — *b'mezid*. Since *shvuas hapikadon* is narrower in these respects, one cannot simply assume it is always the broader category from which to derive a *kal vachomer*.

### Edus Narrower

The *braisah* anticipates a counter-objection: *tomar b'edus sheken asa bo mushba k'nishba* — here, by contrast, is where *edus* is broader: it applies even when the oath is imposed, and it applies even in cases of *mezid*. The two categories thus each have domains of greater and lesser scope relative to the other.

### Need Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תֶּחֱטָא״–״תֶּחֱטָא״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה – נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״תֶּחֱטָא״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״תֶּחֱטָא״; מָה לְהַלָּן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states** the term **"shall sin"** with regard to an oath of testimony and states **"shall sin"** with regard to an oath on a deposit in order **to** derive **a verbal analogy. Here, it is stated** with regard to an oath of testimony: **"Shall sin"** (Leviticus 5:1), **and there, it is stated** with regard to an oath on a deposit: **"Shall sin"** (Leviticus 5:21). **Just as there,** concerning an oath on a deposit, the verse **is speaking only with regard to a monetary claim, so too here,** concerning an oath of testimony, the verse **is speaking only with regard to a monetary claim.**

Since the *kal vachomer* cannot stand — *shvuas hapikadon* is not uniformly broader, rendering the inferential chain invalid — the *braisah* concludes: *talmud lomar techeta techeta l'gezeirah shavah*. A *gezeirah shavah* is required.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Techeta Link

The word *techeta* appears in both relevant passages, providing the verbal link for the *gezeirah shavah*. This connects *shvuas ha'eidus* to *shvuas hapikadon* through shared textual language.

### Two Verses

The two verses in question are Leviticus 5:1 and 5:21. The first, governing *shvuas ha'eidus*, reads: *v'nefesh ki techeta v'shomea kol alah vehu eid o ra'ah o yada im lo yagid v'nasa avono*. The second, governing *shvuas hapikadon*, reads: *v'nefesh ki techeta u'ma'ala ma'al b'Hashem v'kichash b'amiso*. The linkage is not merely the single word *techeta* but the entire opening phrase *v'nefesh ki techeta*, which opens both passages identically. This stands in contrast to the earlier *o'in* approach, which linked the passages through structural parallelism; here the link is substantive and phraseological, and it is this verbal identity that Rabbi Shimon ultimately employs.

## Why Not Bitui?

### Challenge to O's

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: ״אוֹ״ ״אוֹ״ בִּיטּוּי יוֹכִיחוּ – שֶׁהֵן ״אוֹאִין״, וְיֵשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה, וְאֵין עִמָּהֶן כֹּהֵן – וּמְדַבְּרִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla objects to this:** The multiple instances of the term "or" in the verse: **"Or** if any one shall take an oath to clearly express with his lips to do evil **or** to do good" (Leviticus 5:4), which is written with regard to an oath on **an utterance, will prove** that one is liable even without a monetary claim, **as they are** multiple instances of the term **"or," and there is an oath in their** context, **and there is no priest in their** context, **and they are not speaking with regard to a monetary claim.**

Rabba bar Ulla raises a challenge against the first approach — the linkage via *o'in*. The *Gemara* had established that the *o'in* in *shvuas ha'eidus* parallel those in *shvuas hapikadon*, and that both passages share additional features: both involve a *shevuah*, and neither involves a *kohen* (excluding the case of *sotah*). But Rabba bar Ulla asks: why not link *shvuas ha'eidus* to *shvuas bitui* instead? After all, *shvuas bitui* also uses the disjunctive language and is also a *shevuah* without a *kohen*. And *shvuas bitui* — an oath about future conduct, introduced by *o nefesh ki tishava levatei visfa'taim lehara o lehitiv* — is not limited to monetary matters. If the linkage went to *shvuas bitui*, the conclusion about *shvuas ha'eidus* being restricted to money would not follow.

### More Comparisons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִפִּקָּדוֹן הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן ״תֶּחֱטָא״ מִ״תֶּחֱטָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **It stands to reason** that **he should have derived** the *halakha* with regard to an oath of testimony **from** an oath on **a deposit** and not from an oath on an utterance **due to** the verbal analogy between the terms **"shall sin"** and **"shall sin."**

The *Gemara* responds: it is more reasonable to derive from *shvuas hapikadon* — *mistavra mipikadon hava lei lemeilaf* — because these two passages share an additional bond beyond the *o'in* and the *shevuah*: the phrase *nefesh ki techeta*, which appears in both, provides an extra point of comparison not present between *shvuas ha'eidus* and *shvuas bitui*.

### Bitui vs Pikadon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה – מִבִּיטּוּי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן חַטָּאת מֵחַטָּאת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary, he should have derived** the *halakha* with regard to an oath of testimony **from** the *halakha* with regard to an oath on **an utterance, as** it is a derivation of one case for which one is liable to bring **a sin-offering** for taking a false oath **from** another case for which one is liable to bring **a sin-offering** for taking a false oath. This is in contrast to an oath on a deposit, for which one is liable to bring a guilt-offering for taking a false oath.

The *Gemara* then entertains a counter-argument in favor of linking to *shvuas bitui*: *adraba mibitui hava lei lemeilaf sheken chatat michatat*. The argument here is not about a shared word but about shared *halakha*: *shvuas ha'eidus* and *shvuas bitui* are literally in the same *parsha*, they share the same category of *korban* — a *chatat oleh veyored*, a sliding-scale sin-offering — and neither involves a *chatat* with a *chomesh*. Thus the thematic and legal affinity between *shvuas ha'eidus* and *shvuas bitui* is substantial. The two sides of the argument can now be laid out: linking to *shvuas hapikadon* offers the *v'nefesh ki techeta* verbal parallel; linking to *shvuas bitui* offers the shared *korban* type and shared *parsha*.

### Pikadon Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִפִּקָּדוֹן הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף – שֶׁכֵּן חֵטְא; בְּמֵזִיד; תַּבְעֵיהּ וְכַפְרֵיהּ; וְעַבְרֵיהּ. הָנָךְ נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it stands to reason** that **he should have derived** the *halakha* with regard to an oath of testimony **from** the *halakha* with regard to an oath on **a deposit, as** there are many elements common to both oaths, represented by the mnemonic: **Sin, intentionally, claimed** from **him, denied his** claim, **and his past.** There is a verbal analogy between them, as the term "shall sin" appears in both contexts. In both cases one is liable for taking a false oath intentionally. Additionally, in both cases there is a claim presented by one of the parties and denial of that claim by the one taking the oath. And both oaths relate to events that transpired in the past. The Gemara answers: **These** elements common to an oath on a deposit and misuse of consecrated property are more **numerous** than the elements common to an oath on a deposit and an oath on an utterance.

The *Gemara* resolves the competition by counting points of comparison. In favor of *shvuas hapikadon*: first, the shared phrase *v'nefesh ki techeta*; second, both apply even in cases of *mezid* — neither passage uses *v'ne'elam*, so both are not limited to inadvertent violation; third, both involve a *teviah u'kfirah*, a demand and a denial; and fourth, both are oriented toward past conduct. That gives four substantive points of comparison. Against this, the case for *shvuas bitui* offers three: the shared *chatat* type, the *oleh veyored* form, and the absence of *chomesh*. Since *haneich nefishin* — the *pikadon* side has more points — the linkage goes to *shvuas hapikadon*, and therefore *shvuas ha'eidus* is restricted to monetary matters.

## Rabbi Akiva's Limits

### Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיקוּם עִלָּאי מִשּׁוּם דִּי אִיבַּעְיָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** [This section discusses the practical difference between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva regarding the scope of exemptions from liability in false oath of testimony.]

Having established the general principle through multiple approaches, the *Gemara* now turns to Rabbi Akiva's specific contribution. Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* reads: *vehaya ki ye'asham le'achat me'eileh* — the verse states that one becomes liable for *one* of these. From this Rabbi Akiva derives that *yeish me'eileh shehu chayav v'yeish me'eileh shehu patur* — there are some cases within this category that incur liability and some that do not. The *braisah's* plain reading is that Rabbi Akiva is offering another proof that the *parsha* concerns money. But the *Gemara* reorients his position: Rabbi Akiva was actually making a narrower point — not merely confirming that the *parsha* is about money, but pointing out to the other *tanna'im* who were busy establishing that principle that *achat me'eileh* signals that not every monetary case is included.

### Land or Knas?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קַרְקַע – לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר חַיָּיבִין, לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The practical difference **between their** opinions is in the case of **one who administers an oath to witnesses** with regard to testimony involving **land. According to Rabbi Eliezer, they are liable** if they take a false oath. **According to Rabbi Akiva they are exempt** in that case, as it is excluded by the term "of these."

The *Gemara* asks: what is the distinction Rabbi Akiva draws, and where does it emerge from a debate with Rabbi Eliezer? The *Gemara* initially proposes that the debate concerns *edei karka* — testimony relating to land. There is a general principle that one does not take an oath regarding land. The question here is subtler: when witnesses are asked to testify about a claim that has implications for liens on land, does that implicate the land-oath principle? If the witnesses are not swearing about the land itself but about whether they possess certain testimony, it is unclear whether this falls under the *shvuas ha'eidus* framework at all.

### Edei Karka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר הָתָם: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קַרְקַע אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פְּטוּרִין – הָכָא מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says there** with regard to an oath on a deposit and an oath of testimony that in the case of **one who administers an oath to witnesses** with regard to testimony involving **land,** the witnesses **are exempt even according to Rabbi Eliezer, what** difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva?**

The *Gemara* proposes that Rabbi Eliezer holds the witnesses are liable — *chayavin* — while Rabbi Akiva holds they are exempt — *patur* — in the case of *mashbia edei karka*. However, the *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan elsewhere rules that even Rabbi Eliezer would agree that testimony relating to land is exempt — *afilu Rabbi Eliezer patur*. If that is so, then the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva must concern something else: *edei knas*. Witnesses summoned to testify about a penalty — a *knas* — may be exempt according to Rabbi Akiva's reading of *achat me'eileh*, since the *knas* context has features that distance it from the ordinary monetary framework, including the possibility that the liable party could preempt the witnesses by confessing directly and thereby avoiding the *knas* altogether.

## Rabbi Yose HaGlili

### Yedia/Reiah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדָע״ – בְּעֵדוּת הַמִּתְקַיֶּימֶת בִּרְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה וּבִידִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** cites a different proof and **says:** The verse **states** with regard to an oath of testimony: **"And he is a witness or he saw or he knew"** (Leviticus 5:1). It is **with regard to testimony that is founded on sight without knowledge** of the matter, **or by means of knowledge without sight,** that **the verse is speaking.**

Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's approach returns to the content of the verse itself: *vehu eid o ra'ah o yada*. He reads the disjunctive formulation — either he saw or he knew — as pointing to a category of testimony that operates even when seeing and knowing come apart. Two distinct sub-cases follow: *yedia belo re'iyah* — knowing without seeing — and *re'iyah belo yedia* — seeing without knowing.

### Monetary Examples

The *Gemara* provides examples to illustrate these concepts. What does *yedia belo re'iyah* look like in a monetary case? The witnesses heard Michael admit that he owes Dov a hundred dollars. They did not see the original loan; their knowledge derives entirely from the admission. In monetary matters, self-admission is legally operative, so such *yedia* suffices to establish the claim. *Re'iyah belo yedia* is actually the more striking case: witnesses saw money counted out from one party to another but do not know what it represented — a loan, a gift, repayment of a prior debt. Rashi explains that this testimony has value in monetary contexts because it may pressure the other party to admit the truth, or, as Tosafot elaborates later in the *Gemara*, because the defendant may have made statements that effectively committed him to a particular interpretation of the event, such that witnesses who corroborate the act itself resolve the dispute in the plaintiff's favor.

### Not Capital

Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's point is that this kind of testimony — where seeing and knowing come apart — simply does not arise in capital cases or cases of *malkus*. In those domains, witnesses must have both direct visual observation and knowledge of the legally relevant facts. Since the verse's formulation presupposes testimony of these distinctive hybrid types, it signals that the entire *parsha* concerns monetary matters.

## Rav Acha's Camel

### Rav Acha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַחָא? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: גָּמָל הָאוֹחֵר בֵּין הַגְּמַלִּים וְנִמְצָא גָּמָל הָרוּג בְּצִידּוֹ – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁזֶּה הֲרָגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Shall we say** that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili does not accept** the opinion **of Rabbi Aḥa? As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Aḥa says:** If there is **a rutting** male **camel** that is rampaging **among** other **camels and** then **a camel was found killed at its side, it is evident that this** rampaging camel **killed it,** and the owner must pay for the damage.

Rav Pappa asks Abaye whether Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position — that *yedia belo re'iyah* works only in monetary contexts — conflicts with a *braisah* of Rav Acha. That *braisah* states: if a camel was acting wild among other camels — either goring them or, according to one of Rashi's readings, in some other aggressive manner — and a dead camel was found beside it, *b'yaduah she-zeh harago*: it is established knowledge that this camel killed the other, and one may collect damages accordingly. This is a case of *yedia belo re'iyah* in a monetary context. The question is whether the same reasoning could extend to capital cases. The *Gemara* observes: *b'dinei nefashot nami mishkachas lah* — you can construct an analogous case in capital law. And it invokes the story of Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach.

### Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: אֶרְאֶה בְּנֶחָמָה אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי אֶחָד שֶׁרָץ אַחַר חֲבֵירוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה, וְרַצְתִּי אַחֲרָיו, וּמָצָאתִי סַיִיף בְּיָדוֹ וְדָם מְטַפְטֵף וְהָרוּג מְפַרְפֵּר; אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: רָשָׁע! מִי הֲרָגוֹ לָזֶה? אוֹ אֲנִי, אוֹ אַתָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Shataḥ said** in the form of an oath: **I will** not **see the consolation** of Israel **if I did not see one who was running after another into a ruin, and I ran after him and found a sword in his hand and blood dripping** from the sword, **and the slain** person **convulsing. I said to him: Wicked** one, **who killed this** person? It was **either me or you,** as there is no one else here.

The *braisah* records Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's account, prefaced with the oath-formula *ereh b'nechamah* — may I see the consolation of Jerusalem. He saw a man running after another into a desolate ruin. He ran after them. He found the pursuer holding a sword, blood dripping from it, and the victim thrashing on the ground in his death throes. Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach said: "Wicked one — who killed this man? It is either you or I, and I know it was not I." Yet he could do nothing, because the Torah requires *al pi shenayim eidim o sheloshah eidim yumos ha-meis* — conviction requires two witnesses. He declared that God would exact retribution, and, the *braisah* records, before they left that spot a snake bit the killer and he died. The clear implication of Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's lament is that had there been a second witness, the circumstantial chain of *yedia belo re'iyah* might have sufficed. This appears to concede that *yedia belo re'iyah* can function in capital cases.

### Concession/Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַחָא; בִּשְׁלָמָא יְדִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ; אֶלָּא רְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה – הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? מִי לָא בָּעֵי מִידָּע אִם גּוֹי הָרַג אוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל הָרַג, אִם אָדָם טְרֵפָה הָרַג אוֹ שָׁלֵם הָרַג?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even** if **you say** that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Aḥa** that one may rely on circumstantial evidence even in cases of capital law, one may nevertheless distinguish between cases of monetary law and cases of capital law. **Granted,** even in cases of capital law **you find** testimony based on **knowledge without sight, but how can you find** a case of **sight without knowledge? Don't** the witnesses **need to know if** the one whom he witnessed killing another **killed a gentile or he killed a Jew, if he killed one who has a wound that would have caused him to die within twelve months [*tereifa*] or he killed** one whose body is **intact?**

This is a striking *Gemara*. The *Gemara* seems to concede, at least provisionally, that *yedia belo re'iyah* might work even in capital cases. Tosafot raises the obvious difficulty: where was the *hatra'ah* — the required forewarning? Tosafot answers that perhaps the murderer was a *chaver*, a Torah scholar presumed to know the law and therefore not requiring formal warning. But the deeper problem is that the entire thrust of *shas* is that capital convictions require direct eyewitness observation of the act itself — no inferences, no circumstantial chains, no drawing of conclusions from what was not seen. The case Rav Acha presents involves a monetary dispute about a camel; it does not touch capital law, and it is therefore surprising that the *Gemara* treats it as potentially applicable to *dinei nefashot*. It must be emphasized that this theoretical concession does not reflect the *halakha*. The *Gemara* appears to be making a conditional argument: even granting for the sake of argument that *yedia belo re'iyah* might work in capital cases, there remains a distinction between *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* — and that distinction is *re'iyah belo yedia*. The *Gemara* pivots: *ela re'iyah belo yedia heichi mishkachas lah?* — you simply cannot construct a case of seeing without knowing in capital law.

## Mamonot vs Nefashot

### Critical Facts

Why can't *re'iyah belo yedia* function in capital cases? The *Gemara* explains: *mi lo ba'ei meida im oved kochavim harag o Yisrael harag* — the witnesses must know whether the victim was Jewish or not, since killing a non-Jew does not carry the death penalty. They must also know *im adam treifa harag o shalem harag* — whether the victim was a *treifa*, a person with a life-threatening internal condition, because killing such a person would render the killer technically exempt from execution. Tosafot notes that in practice one might assume the victim was healthy and Jewish — particularly in an insular community where those assumptions are virtually certain — and that explicit testimony about these facts is only required when there is actual reason for doubt. But the principle stands: in capital cases, partial visual testimony that leaves legally critical facts unestablished cannot sustain a conviction.

### Knas Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קְנָס – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Conclude from it** that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds** that in a case where **one administers an oath to witnesses** with regard to testimony involving **a fine,** the witnesses are **exempt** from liability for taking a false oath of testimony.

The *Gemara* then asks whether this reasoning applies equally to *knas* cases, and infers that Rabbi Yose HaGlili would hold witnesses about a *knas* exempt — *mashbia ed knas patur*. The logic runs as follows: even if we grant (provisionally) that *yedia belo re'iyah* might work even in capital cases, it is *re'iyah belo yedia* that definitively cannot function there. And *re'iyah belo yedia* fails in *knas* cases for the same reason it fails in capital cases: critical facts remain unknown. The *Gemara* asks: what would the witnesses need to know? *Nochris ba'al o bas Yisrael ba'al* — was the woman involved Jewish or not? *Besula ba'al o be'ula ba'al* — was she a virgin or not? These facts determine whether a *knas* applies at all. Since *re'iyah belo yedia* leaves such facts unresolved, it cannot carry the testimony in *knas* cases either.

### Examples

The *Gemara* accordingly concludes — reading the inference as a statement rather than a question — that Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds *knas* is also excluded from *shvuas ha'eidus*. The distinction that Rabbi Yose HaGlili draws between monetary cases and other domains thus excludes not only capital and *malkus* cases but *knas* cases as well, since *re'iyah belo yedia* cannot do the necessary legal work there.

## Monetary Scenarios

### Pillar Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְיָתֵיב רַב יְהוּדָה וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה מְנִיתִיךְ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי״, וְעֵדִים רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna was sitting before Rav Yehuda, and Rav Yehuda was sitting and he was raising a dilemma:** If one demands payment from another and claims: **I counted for you** and gave you **one hundred dinars in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so, and witnesses see him** counting the money **from outside, what** is the *halakha*? Is their testimony accepted?

Rav Hamnuna was sitting before Rav Yehuda, who posed the following hypothetical as a kind of examination: "Reuven says to Michael — *maneh maniti lach bifnei ploni u'ploni*, I counted out a hundred dollars to you in front of these two witnesses. The witnesses were standing outside watching through the window — *veha'eidim ro'in oso mibachutz* — and Michael, unaware he was being observed, denied the transaction." What happens when the witnesses are produced in court?

### Hamnuna's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא: וְהַלָּה מָה טוֹעֵן? אִי אָמַר: ״לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם״ – הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן! אִי אָמַר: ״אִין, שְׁקַלִי – וְדִידִי שְׁקַלִי״ – כִּי אָתוּ עֵדִים מַאי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna said to** Rav Yehuda: **And what does the other** person **claim** in response to the demand for repayment? **If he says:** These **matters never happened, he assumes the presumptive status of a denier** of the truth, as the witnesses testify that they saw the claimant counting the money and giving it to him. **If he says: Yes, I took** money from him, **but** it is **my** money that **I took,** then **when** the **witnesses come** and testify that they saw the claimant counting the money and giving it to him, **what of it?**

Rav Hamnuna's response: *ei amar lo hayu devarim me'olam* — if Michael says the entire event never took place, then *huchzak kafran* — Michael is now established as a liar. The witnesses saw the money being counted; Michael denied it happened at all. Rashi reads the consequence as follows: Michael can no longer be believed for purposes of taking a *shvuah*, which may ultimately affect the resolution of the monetary claim.

### Michael Admits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְנוּנָא אַתְּ? עוּל תָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Are you Hamnuna? Enter** and **come** into the study hall, as you make your teacher wiser.

But if Michael says *in shakli v'didi shakli* — yes, you counted out money, and it was simply mine that you were returning to me — then *ki atu edei mai mahani?* — what do the witnesses accomplish? They saw money change hands but they have no idea what it represented. Rav Hamnuna responds: *haimnu ul* — you understand it correctly, come take your seat in the *beit midrash*. The witnesses, in this scenario, do nothing. The *Gemara* thus has not yet satisfactorily explained how *re'iyah belo yedia* genuinely functions in monetary cases.

## Credibility Tests

### Location Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה מָנִיתִי לְךָ בְּצַד עַמּוּד זֶה״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֹא עָבַרְתִּי בְּצַד עַמּוּד זֶה״. אֲתוֹ תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי, אַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ דְּהִשְׁתִּין מַיִם בְּצַד עַמּוּד זֶה. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: There was **a certain** individual **who said to another: I counted for you** and gave you **one hundred dinars** as a loan **alongside this column.** The other person **said to him** in response: **I did not pass alongside this column. Two witnesses came and testified about him** that they saw **that he urinated alongside this column. Reish Lakish said: He assumes the presumptive status of a denier** of the truth, as the testimony of witnesses proves that he passed alongside the column.

The *Gemara* presents a further case. Someone says to his friend: *maneh maniti lach betzad amud zeh* — I counted out a hundred dollars to you by that pillar. The friend replies: *lo avarti betzad amud zeh* — I was never by that pillar. Witnesses then come forward and testify that they observed him urinating by that very pillar. Rav Yitzchak rules: *huchzak kafran* — he is established as a liar.

### Absolute Denial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי דִּינָא פָּרְסָאָה הוּא! מִי קָאָמַר ״מֵעוֹלָם״?! בְּעֵסֶק זֶה קָאָמַר לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman objects to this: That is a ruling** characteristic of **a Persian** court, not a reasonable ruling characteristic of a Jewish court. **Did** the respondent **say** that he **never** passed alongside the column? It was that he did not pass alongside the column **in** the context of **this matter** that **he said to him** that he did not pass the column; therefore, the testimony of the witnesses does not contradict his statement.

Rav Nachman objects: *hai dina parsa'ah hu* — that would be Persian justice, not *halakha*. When Michael said "I wasn't by that pillar," he meant: in the context of the transaction you're describing, I was not there. He did not mean he had never in his life stood near that pillar. The witnesses' testimony about his urinating there is therefore irrelevant to this dispute. The *Gemara* then presents a modified version of the case — *ikha de'amri* — in which Michael explicitly says *lo avarti betzad amud zeh me'olam*: I was never, ever by that pillar. In that version, Rav Nachman agrees: *huchzak kafran*. If Michael made an absolute, global denial of ever having been near that location, witnesses who place him there establish him as a liar.

### Irrelevant Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּלָא רַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּאִינִישׁ, עָבֵיד לַהּ וְלָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman:** There is no proof from here that he assumes the presumptive status of a denier, as **any matter that is not incumbent upon a person** to remember, **he performs it and it is not on his mind.** Therefore, when he denied ever passing alongside the column, it was because there was never any reason for him to remember that he had been there.

Rava challenges even the stricter version: *kol milta dela ramya aleih de'inish avid leih velav adaiteih* — anything that is not of significance to a person, he will do without registering it in his memory. Which pillar one happens to urinate by is entirely irrelevant to most people's lives. Michael could say with full sincerity that he was never at that pillar, not because he is lying, but because passing by that pillar simply did not register. One cannot therefore establish him as a *kafran* based on testimony about a detail he had no reason to track. Rava's objection illustrates the principle: only if the person made a denial about something he would necessarily have noticed and remembered can contradicting witnesses establish his dishonesty.

Tosafot, however, proposes a scenario in which *re'iyah belo yedia* does genuinely function as useful testimony. If Michael had said not merely "I wasn't there" but effectively: "if you can prove it happened, I concede I owe you" — then witnesses who corroborate the act of counting itself resolve the case entirely in the plaintiff's favor without needing to interpret what the money transfer meant. This reading, Tosafot notes, is not explicit in the *Gemara* but provides the most coherent account of why *re'iyah belo yedia* has any legal relevance at all in monetary matters.

## Rav Shiman's Kal

### Rav Shiman Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חִיֵּיב כָּאן וְחִיֵּיב בְּפִקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** cites a different proof and **says:** The Torah **rendered** one **liable** if he takes a false oath **here** with regard to testimony, **and** the Torah **rendered** one **liable** if he takes a false oath **with regard to a deposit.**

Rav Shiman states a direct *kal vachomer*: *chiyuv kan v'chiyuv bepikadon* — there is liability here (*shvuas ha'eidus*) and there is liability in *shvuas hapikadon*. *Shvuas hapikadon* has broader scope, yet it is limited to monetary matters; therefore *shvuas ha'eidus*, which is narrower, is also limited to monetary matters.

### Mocking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחֲכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא. מַאי חוּכָא? דְּקָתָנֵי: מָה לְפִקָּדוֹן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מוּשְׁבָּע כַּנִּשְׁבָּע, מֵזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג.
>
> **Translation:** **They mocked** this proof **in the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara asked: **What** is worthy of **mockery** in the statement of Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara explains that they mocked **that** which the *baraita* **teaches** in the continuation, rejecting the *a fortiori* inference suggested by Rabbi Shimon: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a deposit?** It is notable in **that with regard to** a deposit the Torah **did not render** the halakhic status of **one to whom an oath was administered** by others **like** that of **one who** himself **took an oath,** as one to whom an oath was administered by others is exempt; and the Torah did not render the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath **like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath.

*Machu alav bamaarava* — they laughed at this in Eretz Yisrael. The *Gemara* asks: why is this position mockable? The answer: *dekasani mah lipikadon sheken lo asa bo mushba k'nishba mezid k'shogeg* — the *braisah* itself already established that *shvuas hapikadon* is not uniformly broader. There are ways in which *shvuas hapikadon* is narrower: it does not apply when the oath is externally imposed (*mushba*), and it is limited to *shogeg*. Since Rav Shiman's *kal vachomer* assumes *shvuas hapikadon* is the broader category, it was mocked on the grounds that this assumption is demonstrably false.

### Self-Imposed Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי מוּשְׁבָּע מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ בְּעֵדוּת, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְנָא לֵיהּ – דִּגְמַר מִפִּקָּדוֹן; פִּקָּדוֹן נָמֵי – מוּשְׁבָּע מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים, נִגְמַר מֵעֵדוּת!
>
> **Translation:** This rejection is difficult: **Now,** with regard to the fact that **one who administered an oath to himself** is liable **in** the case of an oath of **testimony, from where** is it derived **according to Rabbi Shimon?** Rabbi Shimon **derives** it by means of a verbal analogy **from** an oath on **a deposit.** If so, based on the same verbal analogy, in the case of an oath on **a deposit too, let us derive from** the case of an oath of **testimony** the fact that one is liable for a false **oath** that **was administered by others.**

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: *mushba mipi atzmo b'eidus Rav Shiman minaleh?* — how does Rav Shiman even know that *shvuas ha'eidus* applies when the oath is self-imposed (*nishba*), not merely imposed by another? The verse describes *v'shomea kol alah*, an oath imposed upon the witness. The answer: Rav Shiman derives *nishba* via a straightforward *kal vachomer* — if you are liable when someone else imposes the oath upon you, you are certainly liable when you take it upon yourself. This *kal vachomer* operates internally within *shvuas ha'eidus* itself, and therefore does not require any cross-reference to *shvuas hapikadon*. The asymmetry thus stands: *shvuas ha'eidus* applies to both *mushba* and *nishba* (the latter by *kal vachomer*), while *shvuas hapikadon* applies only to *nishba*; and *shvuas ha'eidus* applies to *mezid* (from the absence of *v'ne'elam*), while *shvuas hapikadon* is limited to *shogeg*. In both respects *shvuas hapikadon* is the narrower category, and Rav Shiman's *kal vachomer* collapses.

## Meilah Comparison

### Mezid/Shogeg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב הוּנָא: וּמַאי חוּכָא? דִּלְמָא מֵזִיד דְּלָאו כַּשּׁוֹגֵג בְּפִקָּדוֹן, מִמְּעִילָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גָּמַר לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said to** the Sages: **And what** is worthy of **mockery** in that statement? **Perhaps** the fact **that** the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath **is not like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath **in** the case of **a deposit,** and it is **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse** of consecrated property that **Rabbi Shimon derived it.** Just as one is liable to bring a guilt-offering for the misuse of consecrated property only if he did so unwittingly, one is liable to bring a guilt-offering for a false oath on a deposit only if he unwittingly took the false oath.

Rav Huna intervenes to defend Rav Shiman and explain the mocking more precisely. He notes that the *kal vachomer* objection regarding *mushba k'nishba* is not the real problem — that can be handled by Rav Shiman's internal *kal vachomer*. The point on which the mocking is justified is the *mezid/shogeg* distinction. The question is: why does Rav Shiman assume *shvuas hapikadon* is limited to *shogeg*? After all, *shvuas hapikadon* also does not say *v'ne'elam*. By the same logic used for *shvuas ha'eidus* — absence of *v'ne'elam* implies *mezid* applies — one should conclude *shvuas hapikadon* also applies to *mezid*. Rav Huna answers: *dilma mezid d'lav shogeg b'pikadon mi'me'ilah Rabbi Shimon gamar lah* — Rabbi Shimon derives the restriction of *shvuas hapikadon* to *shogeg* from its comparison to *me'ilah*, the laws of trespass against consecrated property. *Me'ilah* incurs liability only in *shogeg*; since *shvuas hapikadon* is linked to *me'ilah* by the word *u'ma'ala ma'al b'Hashem*, it inherits *me'ilah*'s restriction. The word *me'ilah* thus actively limits *pikadon* to *shogeg*, just as the absence of *v'ne'elam* actively extends *shvuas ha'eidus* to *mezid*.

### Meilah Links

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִמְּעִילָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן מְעִילָה מִמְּעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **It stands to reason** that **he should have derived** it **from** the case of **misuse** of consecrated property, **as** that is a derivation of **misuse** written with regard to an oath on a deposit: "If any one shall sin and commits an act of misuse and dealt falsely with his colleague in a matter of deposit" (Leviticus 5:21), which is derived **from misuse** written with regard to misuse of consecrated property: "If any one commits an act of misuse and sinned unwittingly from items consecrated to the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15).

Rav Huna then elaborates the points of comparison between *shvuas hapikadon* and *me'ilah* that make this linkage compelling: first, both passages use the word *me'ilah*; second, in both cases the one who sins is deriving personal benefit — the one who denies the *pikadon* is withholding money that belongs to another, and the one who benefits from *hekdesh* is appropriating consecrated property for personal use; third, both incur a *korban* of fixed cost as opposed to a variable one; and fourth, the *korban* in both cases is an *asham* accompanied by a *chomesh*, a penalty of an added fifth. The Torah itself, it is worth noting, employs the word *me'ilah* — trespass — in both contexts: an act of trespass against God by appropriating *hekdesh*, and an act of trespass against God through false swearing about a deposit. The overlap in terminology and consequence is striking and provides a strong basis for the linkage.

### More Points

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִבֵּי רַב, אָמְרִי: הַיְינוּ חוּכָא – מִכְּדֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה גְּמִיר, לְמָה לֵיהּ דְּפָרֵיךְ: מָה לְפִקָּדוֹן שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מוּשְׁבָּע כַּנִּשְׁבָּע, מֵזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג?
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, came from the study hall of** their **teacher, they said: This is** what is worthy of **mockery: Now,** since ultimately **Rabbi Shimon derives** the *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy** between the term "shall sin" written with regard to an oath on a deposit and the term "shall sin" written with regard to an oath of testimony, **why** is it **that he refutes** the parallel between them by saying: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a deposit?** It is notable in **that with regard to** a deposit the Torah **did not render** the halakhic status of **one to whom an oath was administered** by others **like** that of **one who** himself **took an oath,** as one to whom an oath was administered by others is exempt; and the Torah did not render the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath **like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath.

Rav Pappa and Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua then arrive from their studies and offer the following assessment: the linkage from *shvuas hapikadon* to *me'ilah* is actually stronger than any alternative linkage one might propose. This counting of points — more points to *me'ilah* than to *eidus* — is what justifies Rabbi Shimon's decision to derive the *shogeg*-only restriction from *me'ilah* rather than concluding that *pikadon* also extends to *mezid* by parallel to *eidus*.

## Gezeirah Override

### Gezeirah Impact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חוּכָא? דִּלְמָא כִּי פָּרֵיךְ – מִקַּמֵּי דְּתֵיקוּם לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה; בָּתַר דְּקָמָא לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לָא פָּרֵיךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And what** is worthy of **mockery** in that statement? **Perhaps when** Rabbi Shimon **refuted** the parallel between the two oaths, it was **prior to the verbal analogy being established for him,** and the derivation was by means of a paradigm. **After the verbal analogy was established for him, he does not refute** the parallel and holds that in the case of an oath on a deposit one is liable to bring a guilt-offering for false oaths administered by others as well as for intentional false oaths.

The *Gemara* now raises the question that was, according to Rav Pappa and Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua, the actual reason for the mocking: once Rabbi Shimon ultimately falls back on a *gezeirah shavah* — *techeta techeta* — to establish the link between *shvuas ha'eidus* and *shvuas hapikadon*, why do the prior distinctions matter at all? Normally when a *gezeirah shavah* is accepted, it equalizes the two compared domains and would eliminate any asymmetries between them. Should the *gezeirah shavah* not import *mezid* into *pikadon* and *mushba* into *pikadon* as well? And conversely, should the restrictions of *pikadon* not be reimposed on *eidus*? The mocking was directed precisely at Rabbi Shimon's insistence on maintaining distinctions even after invoking a *gezeirah shavah*.

### Shimon's Stand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָאָמַר לְהוּ רָבָא בַּר אִיתַּי לְרַבָּנַן: מַאן תָּנָא שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן לֹא נִיתַּן זְדוֹנָהּ לְכַפָּרָה – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does Rabbi Shimon **not** refute the parallel between the two oaths? **But didn't Rava bar Ittai say to the Sages: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** with regard to **an oath on a deposit** that **atonement** by means of an offering **is not possible** for one who takes **an intentional** false oath? **It is Rabbi Shimon.** Apparently, Rabbi Shimon concludes that there remains a distinction between intentional and unwitting in the case of an oath on a deposit.

The *Gemara* confirms through Rava's testimony that Rabbi Shimon does indeed hold *shvuas hapikadon* is restricted to *shogeg* even after the *gezeirah shavah* is in place. *Man tana shvuat hapikadon lo nitna l'hitkaper b'mezid — Rabbi Shimon hi*. And this generates the question: if the *gezeirah shavah* links the two passages, why does it not extend *mezid* to *pikadon* as well?

### Rava Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא מֵזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג פָּרֵיךְ, דְּגָמַר לַהּ מִמְּעִילָה, דְּהָנָךְ נְפִישִׁין; אֲבָל מוּשְׁבָּע כַּנִּשְׁבָּע לָא פָּרֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Perhaps** with regard to the halakhic status of one who takes **an intentional** false oath being **like** that of one who takes **an unwitting** false oath, Rabbi Shimon **refutes** the parallel between the two oaths even after the verbal analogy is established for him, **as he derives** the *halakha* of an oath on a deposit **from** the *halakha* of **misuse** of consecrated property, where there is a distinction between intentional and unwitting, **as those** elements common to an oath on a deposit and the misuse of consecrated property are more **numerous** than the elements common to an oath on a deposit and an oath of testimony. **But he does not refute** the parallel between the two oaths with the claim that there is a distinction between them with regard to whether the halakhic status of **one to whom an oath was administered** by others is **like** that of **one who** himself **took an oath.** Once the verbal analogy was established for him, there is no longer a distinction between the two oaths in that regard.

Rava explains that Rabbi Shimon acknowledges the *gezeirah shavah* resolves certain distinctions. Specifically, the *mushba/nishba* distinction: after the *gezeirah shavah* equalizes the two passages, the *mushba* that applies to *eidus* is imported into *pikadon* as well. Rabbi Shimon concedes this point. But the *mezid/shogeg* distinction is different. The *kal vachomer* and *gezeirah shavah* cannot override an active textual signal. The word *u'ma'ala ma'al* in the *pikadon* passage ties it to *me'ilah*, and *me'ilah* is explicitly *shogeg*-only. This is not a default assumption or an inference from silence — it is a positive linkage that restricts *pikadon* to *shogeg* independent of any comparison to *eidus*.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָכִי כַּתְבַהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְעֵדוּת גַּבֵּי שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי וְגַבֵּי טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – דִּבְכוּלָּן נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״וְנֶעְלַם״, וְכָאן לֹא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״וְנֶעְלַם״; לְחַיֵּיב עַל הַמֵּזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **For that** reason, **the Merciful One writes** the verse with regard to an oath of **testimony near** the verse with regard to **an oath on an utterance and near** the verse with regard to **the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **as in all of** those passages **it is stated concerning them: "And it is hidden," and here,** with regard to an oath of testimony, **it is not stated concerning them: And it is hidden.** The Torah did so to underscore the contrast between them and **to deem liable** one who takes **an intentional** false oath of testimony **like** one who takes **an unwitting** false oath of testimony.

The *Gemara* articulates the final resolution: the absence of *v'ne'elam* in *shvuas ha'eidus* is itself a meaningful textual signal. The word *v'ne'elam* appears in every other parallel context — *shvuas bitui*, *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* — and its systematic absence from *shvuas ha'eidus* is not silence but active communication. It communicates *l'chayav al ha'mezid k'shogeg*: the *mezid* case is explicitly made equivalent to *shogeg* for purposes of liability. This positive signal that *eidus* applies to *mezid* cannot be reversed by the *gezeirah shavah*. Conversely, the word *me'ilah* in the *pikadon* passage provides the positive signal that *pikadon* is limited to *shogeg*; this positive restriction likewise cannot be overridden by the *gezeirah shavah*. The two signals operate in opposite directions and remain in force simultaneously. The result, as Rabbi Shimon holds: *shvuas ha'eidus* applies even in *mezid*, and *shvuas hapikadon* is limited to *shogeg* — even after the *gezeirah shavah* linking the two passages is accepted. The *rabanan* dispute this, holding that the *gezeirah shavah* overrides the *me'ilah* link and extends *pikadon* to *mezid* as well, but Rabbi Shimon insists the word *me'ilah* holds firm.
