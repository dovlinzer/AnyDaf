# Bekhorot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Accepting Chaveirim

### Three Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת — צָרִיךְ לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים, וּבָנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵּיתוֹ אֵינָן צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who comes to accept upon himself** a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status must accept** it **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*. But his children and** the **members of his household are not required to accept** the status of *ḥaver* separately **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*.**

The *gemara* picks up approximately twenty lines into 31b with a *baraisa*: *haba l'kabel divrei chaverus tzarich l'kabel bifnei shlosha chaveirim* — one who wishes to accept the obligations of *chaverus* and become a member of the *chaver* community must do so formally before three other *chaveirim*. The *chaverus* institution was concerned primarily with *taharos* and *trumos u'maasros*, and this formal acceptance before three *chaveirim* functions analogously to a *beis din*, constituting the recognized induction procedure of the group.

### Family Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵּיתוֹ אֵינָן צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף בָּנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה חָבֵר שֶׁקִּיבֵּל לְבֶן חָבֵר שֶׁקִּיבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **But his children and** the **members of his household are not required to accept** the status of *ḥaver* separately **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even his children and** the **members of his household must accept** the status of *ḥaver* **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*, because a *ḥaver*, who accepted it** himself in the presence of three others, **is not comparable to the son of a *ḥaver*,** who **accepted** that status only due to his father but did not accept it himself explicitly.**

The same *baraisa* continues: *u'banav u'vnei beiso ein tzrichim l'kabel bifnei shlosha chaveirim* — his children and members of his household do not require a formal acceptance before three *chaveirim*. The underlying assumption is that the father and husband, as *pater familias*, determines the religious practice of the household, and his acceptance therefore draws the family along with him. The *gemara* makes clear that *bnei beiso* includes his wife, his children, and his slaves.

Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel dissents: *af banav u'vnei beiso tzrichim l'kabel bifnei shlosha chaveirim* — even his children and household members must undergo a formal acceptance. His reasoning is *lefi she'eino domeh chaver shekibel l'ven chaver shekibel*: it is not the same thing for one to have accepted personally as for one's son merely to have been born into a family that accepted. The child's acceptance lacks the weight of personal responsibility.

Rashi notes that this final explanatory phrase is actually ambiguous and could be read according to the *tanna kamma* as well: precisely because the father's acceptance naturally draws the child along, no personal acceptance is required. But the more straightforward reading supports Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel's position.

### Talmid Chacham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת — צָרִיךְ לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם צָרִיךְ לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who comes to accept upon himself** a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status must accept** it **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*, and even a Torah scholar** who wishes to become a *ḥaver* **must accept** the status of *ḥaver* **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*.**

A second *baraisa* sharpens a critical point: *haba l'kabel divrei chaverus tzarich l'kabel bifnei gimmel chaveirim v'afilu talmid chacham tzarich l'kabel bifnei shlosha chaveirim* — even a Torah scholar must formally accept before three *chaveirim*. This passage is particularly revealing because it underscores that the institution of *chaverus* and the identity of the *talmidei chachamim* are genuinely distinct. The *chaveirim* are those scrupulous in *tumah v'taharah* and *trumos u'maasros*; the *talmidei chachamim* are those who spend their time in Torah study. There is considerable overlap, and *chullin b'taharah* is certainly a form of halachic scrupulousness, but they are fundamentally two different groupings. Hence, even if one is a *talmid chacham*, he cannot assume *chaver* membership without going through the formal process.

### Zaken v'Yoshev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָקֵן וְיוֹשֵׁב בִּישִׁיבָה — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים, שֶׁכְּבָר קִיבֵּל עָלָיו מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁיָּשַׁב.
>
> **Translation:** **An elder who sits** and studies Torah **in a yeshiva is not required to accept** the status of *ḥaver* **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*, as he already accepted it upon himself from the moment he sat** and dedicated himself to study Torah in yeshiva.

The *baraisa* provides an exception: *zaken v'yoshev b'yeshiva eino tzarich l'kabel bifnei shlosha shekvar kibel alav misha'ah sheyashav* — an elder sitting in a *yeshiva* need not accept before three, because he already implicitly accepted upon assuming his position. The precise meaning of *zaken v'yoshev b'yeshiva* is not entirely clear: does it refer to sitting on a *beis din*, to a communal leadership post, or to someone who literally delivers a *shiur* in a *yeshiva*? In any case, the Rambam in his *Mishneh Torah* does not elaborate on this category.

One might ask: if the acceptance is merely implicit, what becomes of the requirement for *bifnei sheloshah*? The answer is that *bifnei sheloshah* here is not analogous to the *beis din* of *geirus*, where a formal court effects a halachic change of status. Rather, it is the community's way of acknowledging and registering its members. Since the appointment of a *zaken v'yoshev b'yeshiva* is itself a public communal act, it functions as the requisite acknowledgment and no separate ceremony is needed.

### Two Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף תַּלְמִיד חָכָם אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵירִים, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁאֲחֵרִים מְקַבְּלִים לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Shaul says: Even a Torah scholar is not required to accept** the status of *ḥaver* **in the presence of three *ḥaverim*; and not only** does he have the status of *ḥaver* without an explicit declaration in the presence of three *ḥaverim*, **but others** can **accept** that they wish to become a *ḥaver* **in his presence.**

The *gemara* then records the view of Abba Shaul, who holds that a *talmid chacham* does not require a formal acceptance before three *chaveirim* at all — by definition, a *talmid chacham* is a *chaver*. This position represents the tendency to merge the two identities rather than maintain their distinction.

This is an important point for understanding how these terms evolved. Reading the *gemara* in most contexts, one might assume that *chaver* is simply a synonym for *talmid chacham*, set in contrast to *am ha'aretz*. The Rambam in his *Peirush HaMishnayos* goes so far as to say that *chaveirim* essentially means *talmidei chachamim*, since true fellowship is friendship grounded in shared Torah pursuit *leshem shamayim*. Yet the *gemara* here makes abundantly clear that these were originally two separate institutions with separate membership requirements, and the historical convergence of the two is precisely what the *gemara* next proceeds to address.

## Historical Merger

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּימֵי בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס נִשְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This mishna,** i.e., the ruling that a Torah scholar must declare his intent to become a *ḥaver* in the presence of three *ḥaverim*, **was taught in the days of the son of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus.**

Rabbi Yochanan provides a temporal anchor: *bimei beno shel Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus nishnes mishna zu* — the *mishnah* that treated *talmidei chachamim* and *chaveirim* as requiring separate formal acceptance was taught in the days of the son of Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus. The *gemara* then proceeds to explain through a narrative what that period looked like.

### Chanina Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אִיסְתַּפַּק לְהוּ מִילְּתָא בִּטְהָרוֹת, שַׁדַּרוּ רַבָּנַן לְגַבֵּי בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס, אֵזִילוּ אֱמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לְעַיֵּין בַּהּ. אַשְׁכְּחוּהּ דְּקָא טָעֵין טְהָרוֹת, אוֹתֵיב רַבָּנַן מִדִּידֵיהּ לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, וְקָאֵי אִיהוּ לְעַיּוֹנֵי בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei were uncertain about** a certain **matter of ritual purity. The Sages sent** a delegation of their students **to the son of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus** and told them to **go** and **tell him to examine** this matter. The students **found him while he was carrying** items that were ritually **pure.** The son of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus **seated Sages from his own** yeshiva **next to** the students who came to ask the question, because he did not trust these students to keep his items pure. **And he stood and examined** the matter.

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi — two of the foremost *tanna'im* and students of Rabbi Akiva — had a doubt in a matter relating to *taharos*. They sent emissaries to the son of Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus to seek his ruling. Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus was a *kohen*, and the choice of addressee is suggestive: who would know *taharos* better than a *kohen* who was also a *talmid chacham*? Indeed, even after the destruction of the *beis hamikdash*, it seems that *kohanim* may have retained some special acknowledgment in the realm of *taharos*. The emissaries arrived and told him to look into the matter.

### Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ אָמְרִי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אָבִיו שֶׁל זֶה בִּיזָּה תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, אַף הוּא מְבַזֶּה תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** The students returned and **came and told Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei** that the son of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus had treated them as though they had the status of *amei ha'aretz*. **Rabbi Yehuda said to them** in anger: **This one's father,** i.e., Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus, **degraded Torah scholars** by not trusting them with matters of ritual purity. And **he too,** the son of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus, **degrades Torah scholars.**

*Ashkuchuah de'ka tain taharos* — they found him occupied, watching over *taharos*, guarding certain food from becoming *tamei*, whether *trumah* or even *chullin b'taharah*. He was too occupied to attend to their question himself and sent one of his own students to go and answer the question on behalf of the visiting rabbis. He then sent his student *veka'ihu leyunei bah* — to look into the matter for them — while he continued his *taharos* work.

### Complaint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כְּבוֹד זָקֵן יְהֵא מוּנָּח בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, אֶלָּא מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נָהֲגוּ כֹּהֲנִים סִילְסוּל בְּעַצְמָן, שֶׁאֵין מוֹסְרִין אֶת הַטְּהָרוֹת לְכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said to him: Let the honor of the elder,** i.e., both the father and son, **be left in its place.** He did not act in this manner to degrade Torah scholars. **Rather, from the day the Temple was destroyed, the priests were accustomed to act with a higher standard for themselves,** and they decided **that they will not pass ritually pure** items **to any** other **person.** Therefore, the son of Rabbi Ḥanina, as a priest, acted appropriately.

The emissaries returned to Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda and reported what had transpired. Rabbi Yehuda responded sharply: *aviv shel zeh bizeh talmidei chachamim — af hu mevazeh talmidei chachamim* — his father denigrated *talmidei chachamim*, and so does he. Rashi notes that there is no actual record of the father having done so; Rabbi Yehuda was simply venting his anger. Tosafos, however, reads this more literally, suggesting that there may have been a genuine incident involving *taharos* in which the father similarly failed to show proper deference, and that Rashi's reading understates the complaint. In Tosafos's view, *al taharos k'mo she'asa b'no* — the father, like the son, prioritized his *taharos* work over responding to the *talmidei chachamim*.

There is a notable irony here. Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda were themselves offended, yet they had not gone personally — they sent students in their stead. The son of Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, in turn, could not leave his *taharos* and sent a student of his own. Yet the expectation is clearly that he should have responded in person to the senior *tanna'im*. This reveals the underlying assumption: the hierarchy places the acknowledged Torah sages above the *chaveirim*, and the greater *tanna'im* deserve a direct response.

Rabbi Yosi then responds more temperately to Rabbi Yehuda's outburst: *kvod zakein hein munachim bimkomon* — the honor of the elder should remain in its place; let us not speak ill of him.

### Priests' Status

The narrative does not explicitly continue with further text insertion here, as the analysis flows from the previous section.

The word *silsul* is suggestive. It appears in the famous *midrash* about Yosef — *misalsel bis'aro* — where it carries a connotation of self-preening and attending overmuch to one's appearance. *Silsul be-atzman* here therefore implies something like an excessive holding of oneself apart, a cultivated aloofness. While Rabbi Yosi says *kvod zakein* — let us respect him — the subtext seems to be: we understand what is going on, these *kohanim* are holding onto the vestiges of their former status through their scrupulousness in *taharah*, and while we will not denigrate them, we all understand that the true *kavod* belongs to the *talmidei chachamim*.

### Conflation

What this narrative illuminates is the historical moment when the two groups were still meaningfully distinct and even in tension. The *chaveirim* — and particularly those *kohanim* deeply invested in *taharos* — maintained a separate identity that could place them in conflict with the *talmidei chachamim*. It was precisely in this period, when that competition was live, that the *mishnah* was taught requiring even a *talmid chacham* to formally accept membership in the *chaverus* community. Later, as the Rambam reflects, the two identities effectively merged, and the *talmid chacham* came to be regarded as the paradigmatic *chaver* — rendering the separate formal acceptance no longer meaningful.

## Status Changes

### After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָבֵר שֶׁמֵּת — אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵּיתוֹ הֲרֵי הֵן בְּחֶזְקָתָן, עַד שֶׁיֵּחָשֵׁדוּ. וְכֵן חָצֵר שֶׁמּוֹכְרִין בָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת — הֲרֵי הִיא בְּחֶזְקָתָהּ, עַד שֶׁתִּיפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a *ḥaver* that died, his wife and children and members of his household retain their presumptive** status **until they are suspected** of engaging in inappropriate deeds. **And similarly,** in the case of **a courtyard in which one sells sky-blue dye, it retains its presumptive** status as a place in which fit sky-blue dye is sold **until it is disqualified** due to the merchant's unscrupulous behavior.

The *gemara* returns to questions of family membership. *Chaver she-meis — ishto u'vanav u'vnei beiso harei hein b'chezkasan ad she-yeichasheidu*: if a *chaver* dies, his wife, children, and household members retain their status as *chaveirim* in presumption, as long as they have not been found suspect. The rationale is that once the family has been raised with or habituated into the practice of *chaverus*, they are presumed to continue maintaining those standards even without the father-husband, unless there is specific evidence to the contrary.

### Market Analogy

The *gemara* draws a parallel: if there was a marketplace courtyard where *techeiles* was sold and it was known to be genuine, *harei hi b'chezkasah ad she-tipaseil* — we presume the vendors continue selling genuine *techeiles* unless something specific disqualifies them. This analogy is interesting in its own right. In the family case, the presumption of continuing behavior rests on upbringing and religious habituation. In the marketplace case, it rests on established commercial reputation. One might wonder what mechanism keeps the merchants honest in the absence of ongoing inspection — and the answer is likely the community of other vendors in the market who would not countenance fraudulent product being brought in alongside theirs.

### Woman Enters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵשֶׁת עַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְחָבֵר, וְכֵן בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְחָבֵר, וְכֵן עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁנִּמְכַּר לְחָבֵר — כּוּלָּן צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת בַּתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The** former **wife an *am ha'aretz* who** later **marries a *ḥaver*, and likewise the daughter of an *am ha'aretz* who marries a *ḥaver*, and likewise the slave of an *am ha'aretz* who is sold to a *ḥaver*, must all accept** upon themselves a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status.**

The *gemara* then addresses the case of transition from one social world to another. *Eshes am ha'aretz she-nisa'as l'chaver* — the wife of an *am ha'aretz* who marries a *chaver*, or *bito shel am ha'aretz she-nisa'as l'chaver* — the daughter of an *am ha'aretz* who marries a *chaver*, or the slave of an *am ha'aretz* who is sold to a *chaver*: *kulam tzrichin l'kabel divrei chaveirus bat'chila* — all of them must undergo a formal acceptance from the outset.

Why? The principle that the husband's acceptance encompasses the wife applies when the family was formed in the context of *chaverus* — the wife was present when the husband underwent his induction, and the children were born into that environment. Here, however, not only were these individuals previously habituated to different practices, but when the husband-father performed his original acceptance into *chaverus*, these people were not yet part of his household and therefore the acceptance did not extend to them. The combination of prior habituation to *am ha'aretz* standards and the absence at the original acceptance means they must accept formally for themselves.

### Woman Exits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת חָבֵר שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְעַם הָאָרֶץ, וְכֵן בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל חָבֵר שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְעַם הָאָרֶץ, וְכֵן עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל חָבֵר שֶׁנִּמְכַּר לְעַם הָאָרֶץ — אֵין צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת בַּתְּחִלָּה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף הֵן צְרִיכִין לְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶן דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **the** former **wife of a *ḥaver* who** later **marries an *am ha'aretz*, and likewise the daughter of a *ḥaver* who marries an *am ha'aretz*, and likewise the slave of a *ḥaver* who was sold to an *am ha'aretz*,** these people **need not accept** upon themselves a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status *ab initio*,** as each of them is already accustomed to behave as a *ḥaver*. **Rabbi Meir says: They too must accept** upon themselves a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status *ab initio*.**

The reverse case is then presented: *aval eshes chaver she-nisa'as l'am ha'aretz, v'chein bito shel chaver she-nisa'as l'am ha'aretz, v'chein avdo shel chaver she-nimkar l'am ha'aretz — ein tzrichin l'kabel divrei chaveirus bat'chila*. If the *chaver*'s wife, daughter, or slave now find themselves in the household of an *am ha'aretz*, they do not require a new formal acceptance to resume their *chaver* status. This is a striking ruling: not only has the husband-father who anchored their status departed, but they are now actively living in the household of an *am ha'aretz* where one would expect their practices to naturally follow the lower standard. Yet they retain their status.

Rashi addresses this difficulty by reading the case as applying *after* they have left the *am ha'aretz* household — the husband died or divorced, and now the question is whether they automatically revert to their prior *chaver* status. According to Rashi, the *chachamim* say yes, the prior status reasserts itself without the need for new formal acceptance.

### R. Meir Strict

Rabbi Meir disagrees: *af hein tzrichin l'kabel aleihem divrei chaveirus l'chatchila* — even these individuals, once they return from an *am ha'aretz* environment, must re-accept *chaverus* formally. Rabbi Meir's concern is that people's behavior naturally adapts to their surroundings, and the fact that they were previously inducted into *chaverus* does not guarantee that their practices survived the *am ha'aretz* household intact.

### Woman Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְחָבֵר וְהָיְתָה קוֹמַעַת לוֹ תְּפִילִּין עַל יָדוֹ, נִשֵּׂאת לְעַם הָאָרֶץ וְהָיְתָה קוֹשֶׁרֶת לוֹ קִשְׁרֵי מוֹכֵס עַל יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would** illustrate this point and **say in the name of Rabbi Meir:** There was **an incident involving a certain woman who married a *ḥaver* and would tie [*koma'at*] for him phylacteries on his hand,** and she later **married a tax collector and would tie for him tax seals on his hand,** which shows that her new husband had a great influence on her level of piety.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar reports in the name of Rabbi Meir a concrete illustration: there was a woman who had been married to a *chaver* and used to help him wrap his *tefillin*. She then married an *am ha'aretz* and found herself tying up his bundles of tax receipts — he was a tax collector. The very fact that Rabbi Meir invokes this example suggests that his concern is not merely about the formal status question post-divorce or widowhood, but about the natural gravitational pull of one's current domestic environment on one's religious practice. Even a *frum* woman who had internalized the standards of *chaverus* will drift toward the practice of the household she now inhabits.

The juxtaposition of *chaver* and tax collector is itself notable, as these two figures appear repeatedly throughout rabbinic literature as opposites. The tax collector's world — associated with the foreign government, commercial exploitation, and a general suspicion of dishonesty — stands in stark contrast to the world of *chaverus* and its scrupulous concerns.

## Backsliding

### Original View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, וְכוּלָּן שֶׁחָזְרוּ בָּהֶן אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן עוֹלָמִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **And** with regard to **all of them, when they retract** and return to being *amei ha'aretz*, **they are never accepted** as *ḥaverim*; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *gemara* cites a further *baraisa* — from the Tosefta of Demai — dealing with *chaveirim* who have lapsed entirely: *v'chulan she-chazru bahen ein mekabelin osan l'olam divrei Rabbi Meir* — if any of them backslid, we never accept them back, in the words of Rabbi Meir. The case is straightforwardly one of a *chaver* who formally accepted the responsibilities of *chaverus*, subsequently reverted to lower standards, and now seeks readmission. Rabbi Meir holds that the fact of backsliding itself — having once accepted and then having returned to prior behavior — demonstrates an instability that cannot be rehabilitated through renewed acceptance. Once you have shown that you naturally revert, you cannot be trusted again.

### R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָזְרוּ בְּמַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If **they retracted in private,** while in public they continue to act as *ḥaverim*, **they are not accepted,** but if they retract **in public [*befarheseya*], they are accepted,** as they can be trusted.

Rabbi Yehuda takes a more nuanced position: *chazu b'matmonis ein mekabelin osan b'farhesya mekabelin osan* — if the backsliding occurred in private while they publicly maintained the appearance of being *chaveirim*, we do not accept them back. If, however, the backsliding was public and widely known, we do accept them back. The reasoning behind this distinction is important. One who sinned in private while presenting a *chaver* face to the community has demonstrated not only lapsed standards but active duplicity — they cared about appearances more than about actual practice. Their subsequent claim to have genuinely changed is therefore highly suspect; perhaps they are once again performing for the community without inner transformation. But if the backsliding was public — if the person never hid it and simply said, in effect, this is how I am living right now — then their current declaration that they wish to return is more credible. We can believe their sincerity, even if we retain questions about their ability to sustain the commitment.

### Private Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִם עָשׂוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶן בְּמַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** another version of this discussion: If **they performed their actions** as *ḥaverim* even **in private,** when they initially accepted the status of *ḥaverim*, then **they are accepted** when they wish to return to that status. Conversely, if they performed their actions as *ḥaverim* only **in public, they are not accepted.**

An alternative reading is then offered: *im asu divreihem b'matmonis mekabelin osan* — if we have evidence that even during their lapsed period they privately maintained some of their higher practices, then we accept them back. According to this reading, rather than asking about whether the *sin* was public or private, we are asking about whether the effort at *teshuva* has been private. If someone is quietly trying even while they have not yet made a public declaration, that internal effort is compelling evidence of genuine will to return. Conversely, if all we see is public rehabilitation with nothing to indicate private sincerity, we are more skeptical. Tosafos adopts this reading.

### Always Accept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוּבוּ בָּנִים שׁוֹבָבִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa say: Both** in **this** case **and** in **that** case **they are accepted, as it is stated: "Return, you backsliding children;** I will heal your backslidings" (Jeremiah 3:22).**

Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha take the most inclusive position: *bein kach u'vein kach mekabelin osan* — regardless of all these distinctions, we accept them back, as the verse states *shuvu banim shovavim* — return, O backsliding sons. The doubled form *shovavim* suggests those who have returned and lapsed repeatedly, and yet the invitation remains open. The doors of *teshuva* are never closed.

### Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתוֹ הַזּוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak of the village of Akko says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **that pair,** Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa.**

Rav Yitzchak Ish Kfar Akko said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. It is worth pausing over the practical implications of this ruling. The risk of accepting a backslider back into *chaverus* is real but measured — they might handle someone else's *chullin* when they are not actually maintaining *chullin b'taharah*, or someone might rely on their word that *tevel* has been properly tithed. The *gemara* is prepared to accept that risk in the name of encouraging *teshuva* and leaving the community open. Whether the same principle applies when the risk of the person's unreliability is more severe — as in modern cases of ethical or criminal misconduct — is a question the *gemara* does not resolve, and it ought not be too quickly generalized.

### Tax Collector

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: חָבֵר וְנַעֲשָׂה גַּבַּאי — דּוֹחִין אוֹתוֹ מֵחֲבֵירָתוֹ, פֵּירַשׁ — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ. חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: פֵּירַשׁ — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Initially,** the Sages **would say** with regard to one who is **a *ḥaver* and became** a tax **collector** that **he is rejected from his** status as **a *ḥaver*.** Even if he later **resigns** from his position as a tax collector, **he is not accepted.** In later generations **they went back to saying** that if **he resigns** from his position as a tax collector **he is like any other person** and may be accepted once again as a *ḥaver*.

The *gemara* then applies these principles to the tax collector scenario. *Berishonah hayu omrim chaver she-na'asah gabbai dochakin oso meichavruso* — the original position was that a *chaver* who becomes a tax collector (*gabbai*, which Rashi equates with the *mochs*, a tax collector for the foreign government) is removed from *chaverus*. *Perash ein mekabelin oso* — and if he subsequently leaves the tax collecting post, we do not simply re-accept him.

The *gemara* then reports that the position changed: *chazru lomar perash harei hu k'chol adam* — they later ruled that if he left the tax collecting, he is like any other person and may undergo a new formal acceptance. This represents a middle path: he is not automatically re-instated, but neither is he permanently barred. He must simply go through the normal acceptance process as anyone would.

The *gemara* illustrates this with the case of Rav Huna bar Chiya, who was a figure of great scholarly standing — *itztricha lei sha'ata*, a phrase indicating that people sought him out for his expertise. Rabbah and Rav Yosef, who were among the greatest *talmidei chachamim* of their generation, came to learn Torah from him, accompanied by four hundred pairs of rabbinic students. When Rav Huna bar Chiya heard they were coming, he prepared four hundred chairs for the delegation.

## Partiality Concerns

### Three Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַבְּכוֹרוֹת אָדָם רוֹאֶה, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל עַצְמוֹ, וְרוֹאֶה אֶת קֳדָשָׁיו, וְאֶת מַעְשְׂרוֹתָיו, וְנִשְׁאָל עַל טׇהֳרוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may inspect all firstborns** for blemishes **except for his own** firstborns, as he has a vested interest. **And** despite his vested interest in these cases, one may **inspect his own sanctified animals** that he consecrated for the Temple, to determine whether they have blemishes and may be redeemed; **and** he may inspect **his** animal **tithes** to decide whether they are fit for sacrifice in the Temple; **and** one **may ask** himself **with regard to his ritually pure** foods, i.e., he may decide for himself whether or not they have become impure, and he is not suspected of claiming that impure food is actually pure.

The *mishnah* states that anyone may inspect firstborn animals for blemishes except for the animal's owner — in that case three judges are required. The *gemara* addresses why three judges are specifically required for *bechor*, and whether a single recognized expert (*mumcheh*) might suffice, drawing on Rav Chisda's statement in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that a single *mumcheh* can substitute for three in appropriate circumstances. The underlying concern is partiality: the owner of the *bechor* has a direct interest in its being declared blemished, since a blemished *bechor* may be eaten by the *kohen* rather than remaining in a restricted state. With a *beis din* of three, partiality is mitigated, though one might note that the research literature on committee decision-making suggests this is not always as effective as assumed — individual accountability is in some ways more potent than collective deliberation.

### Sanctified Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרוֹאֶה אֶת קֳדָשָׁיו, דְּאִי בָּעֵי מִיתְּשִׁיל עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** despite his vested interest, one may **inspect his own sanctified animals.** Why is it permitted for him to inspect them? The reason is **that if he wants** to avoid giving the animal to the Temple, he can **issue a request** from a halakhic authority to annul his oath. Consequently, he does not truly have a vested interest.

When it comes to other consecrated animals (*kodshim*), the owner may inspect his own animal because *ee baei misheil alei* — if he wished, he could simply request a *hatara* from a *chacham* and undo the sanctification. Since there is a legitimate avenue to extricate himself from the restriction without any ruling, we are not concerned that his inspection is driven by partiality.

### Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעְשְׂרוֹתָיו, דְּאִי בָּעֵי שָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא בְּכוּלֵּיהּ עֶדְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he may rule with regard to **his** own animal **tithes** to decide whether they are fit to be sacrificed in the Temple. The Gemara explains that the reason is **that if he wants** to avoid bringing the animals as offerings, he can **inflict a blemish upon his entire herd** before separating the tithe, which would render them all unfit for sacrifice in the Temple.

For *ma'aser*, one cannot undo the sanctification after the fact. Yet the *gemara* explains that we are not concerned about partiality: *de'ee baei shadi bei muma b'chulei edra* — if the owner truly intended to act dishonestly, he could have gone through his entire flock beforehand and placed a blemish on every animal, ensuring that whichever became the tenth would already be *pasul*. Since there was a way to obtain the desired result without any biased ruling, we are not concerned about partiality in the act of inspection itself.

### Taharat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִשְׁאָל עַל טׇהֳרוֹתָיו, דְּהָא חֲזוּ לֵיהּ בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, the *baraita* states: **And** one **may ask** himself **with regard to his ritually pure** foods, i.e., he may decide for himself whether or not they have become impure, and he is not suspected of claiming that impure food is actually pure. The Gemara explains that his vested interested in this case is not so great, **as** the food **is fit for him** to eat **in his days of impurity.**

For questions of *tumah v'taharah* regarding one's own food, the owner may answer questions about his own *tahara* because *d'chazi lei bimei tumaso* — even if the food were declared *tamei*, he could eat it during a period when he himself is *tamei*. The potential loss is therefore not absolute, which reduces the partiality concern.

### Tosafos

Tosafos raises a pointed challenge to this last reasoning. What if the food in question is *trumah teme'ah* — something that cannot be eaten even when one is *tamei*, so that the owner has a real and unmitigated interest in a *tahor* ruling? *V'chi lo yehei ne'eman* — do we seriously say that a rabbi cannot issue a *psak* about his own *terumah*? And more broadly: *u'vein b'shar kol issur v'heter* — is there any general principle that a *posek* cannot rule on matters in which he has a personal interest? Indeed, there is explicit *gemara* elsewhere — *hai tzurba d'rabbanan chazi lei l'nafshei* — that a *talmid chacham* may inspect things for himself.

This exposes a genuine *machlokes haposkim*. The present *gemara*, particularly given that all the cases involve changing an existing *chezkas issur* — the animal starts as a *bechor*, the produce as *ma'aser*, and a ruling is required to permit it — suggests that at minimum in those contexts the concern for partiality is heightened and the rule is that external oversight is preferred. The practical conclusion reached by *poskim* is that a *posek* may indeed rule for himself, as Tosafos indicates is well supported by the sources, but this *gemara* places that principle under real pressure.

## Blemished Animals

### Market Sales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כָּל פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, הֲנָאָתָן לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, נִמְכָּרִין בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁחָטִין בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁקָלִין בְּלִיטְרָא,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **all disqualified consecrated** animals that were disqualified for sacrifice due to blemishes and were redeemed, all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the Temple** treasury. In order to ensure that the Temple treasury will not suffer a loss, these animals **are sold in the butchers' market [*ba'itliz*] and slaughtered in the butchers' market,** where the demand is great and the price is consequently higher. **And** their meat **is weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** in the manner that non-sacred meat is sold.

The *gemara* opens a new *perek* with the *mishnah: kol pesulei hamukdashim* — all invalid consecrated animals, meaning animals originally designated as *korbanos* that subsequently acquired a *mum* and were redeemed. After redemption such an animal retains residual *kedushah* — it may not be shorn or worked — but it does regain a degree of ordinary status. The *mishnah* states that they may be sold in the butcher marketplace, *shechted* in the butcher marketplace, and weighed out by the pound.

One might have expected a concern that selling a formerly-consecrated animal in an ordinary meat market is a *bizayon kodshim* — a degradation of something that once possessed sanctity. The *mishnah* overrides this concern because in the case of ordinary *pesulei mukdashim*, the benefit flows to *hekdesh*: the animal will be redeemed, and the redemption value equals the animal's full market price. If the animal can be sold openly in the retail meat market and weighed by the pound, its value — and therefore the redemption sum flowing to *hekdesh* — is maximized. Restricting it to private wholesale sales would depress the redemption value, meaning *hekdesh* collects less. The concern for *kavod kodshim* is therefore set aside in favor of maximizing the return to *hekdesh*.

### Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁהֲנָיָיתָן לַבְּעָלִים. פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין — הֲנָיָיתָן לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** This is the *halakha* with regard to all consecrated animals **except for the firstborn** offering **and** an animal **tithe** offering. When these become blemished and their slaughter is permitted, they are sold and slaughtered only in the owner's house and are not weighed; rather, they are sold by estimate. The reason is **that** all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the owner,** i.e., the priest in the case of the firstborn and the owner in the case of the animal tithe offering. It is not permitted to treat disqualified consecrated animals as one treats non-sacred animals merely to guarantee that the owner will receive the optimal price. This is in contrast to **disqualified consecrated** animals, where all **benefit** accrued from **their** sale belongs **to the Temple** treasury, and therefore the animal is sold in the market to ensure that the optimal price is received.

This logic does not apply to *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah*. These animals are never redeemed; *hekdesh* does not receive their monetary value. The *bechor* belongs to the *kohen*, and the *ma'aser behemah* is eaten by the owner. Since these animals will never translate into redemption funds for *hekdesh* — *she'hana'ah labe'alim* — the justification for tolerating market sale disappears. Accordingly, greater restrictions apply: they may not be sold openly in the marketplace, and standard commercial weighing by the pound is prohibited.

### Balance Weighing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹקְלִין מָנָה כְּנֶגֶד מָנָה בַּבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** although the meat of the firstborn is not weighed and sold by the *litra*, nevertheless, if one has non-sacred meat weighing one hundred dinars, **one may weigh one portion** of non-sacred meat **against one portion** of the meat **of the firstborn,** because that is unlike the manner in which non-sacred meat is weighed.

A limited leniency does apply even to *bechor*: *shoklin mane keneged mane* — one may weigh out the meat of a *bechor* in a balanced manner, matching piece against piece. If a buyer brings a pound of meat from the market and wishes to purchase an equal weight of *bechor* meat, the seller may use a balance to equalize the portions. This is distinguished from placing standard pound weights on a scale — the classic commercial method — which is prohibited for *bechor*. The balance method is perceived as less businesslike and commercial; it is a way of measuring rather than a way of trading. The sale itself is entirely permitted; what is restricted is only the most explicitly mercantile mode of transacting it. The full working-out of these *halachos* will continue in the following *daf*.
