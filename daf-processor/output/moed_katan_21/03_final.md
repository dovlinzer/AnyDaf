# Moed Katan 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Seven Relatives

### Tanna's List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הָאָמוּר בְּפָרָשַׁת כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁכֹּהֵן מִיטַּמֵּא לָהֶן — אָבֵל מִתְאַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶן, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אִשְׁתּוֹ, אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, אָחִיו וַאֲחוֹתוֹ, בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: All** of the relatives **mentioned in the** Torah in the **passage** referring to **priests, for which a priest becomes impure, a mourner must mourn for them. And they are: His wife, his father, and his mother, his brother and his sister, his son, and his daughter.**

The *gemara* opens with a *tanna rabanan* — a *baraita* — that establishes which relatives create an obligation of *aveilus*. The source is the *parasha* of the *kohanim*: since a *kohen* is normally prohibited from becoming *tamei le'meis*, yet the Torah explicitly permits him to become *tamei* for his immediate relatives, those relatives serve as the paradigmatic category for whom the obligation of *aveilus* applies. As the *baraita* states: *kol ha'amur b'parshas kohanim shekohein mitama lahem, avel mitabel aleihem* — anyone mentioned in the *parasha* of the *kohanim* as one whom a *kohen* may become *tamei* for, one is also obligated to observe *aveilus* for.

The list that follows is the classic enumeration of seven relatives: *ishto* (wife — notably listed first, even though, as discussed on the previous *daf*, there is a debate between the Rambam and the Ra'avad as to whether the wife's inclusion is biblical or rabbinic; the Rambam's position, though startling, is that even though a *kohen* who becomes *tamei* for his wife violates a negative prohibition, the inclusion of the wife in the category is only rabbinic in origin), *aviv, imo, achiv, ve'achito, b'no u'vito* — father, mother, brother, sister, son, and daughter.

### Extensions of List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן: אָחִיו וַאֲחוֹתוֹ הַבְּתוּלָה מֵאִמּוֹ, וַאֲחוֹתוֹ נְשׂוּאָה, בֵּין מֵאָבִיו בֵּין מֵאִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **added** other relatives **to** this list: **His** maternal **brother and his unmarried sister from the same mother, and his married sister, whether from the same father or from the same mother.**

Beyond these seven, the *baraita* records extensions. First: *achiv ve'achoto habetulah me'imo* — a half-brother or half-sister via the mother. Since a patriarchal system defines siblinghood primarily through the father, the Rabbis extended the obligation to include maternal half-siblings as well. The qualifier *habetulah* — virgin — reflects the language of the verse concerning the *kohen*, where the sister is referred to in that way, and also points to the next addition: *achoto nesuah bein me'aviv u'vein me'imo* — a married sister, whether from the father's or mother's side. Even though in the classical family structure a married woman is considered to have joined her husband's family, the Rabbis continued to recognize her membership in her birth family and maintained the obligation of *aveilus* for her.

### 2nd Generation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמִּתְאַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶם — כָּךְ מִתְאַבֵּל עַל שְׁנִיִּים שֶׁלָּהֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ מִתְאַבֵּל אֶלָּא עַל בֶּן בְּנוֹ וְעַל אֲבִי אָבִיו. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל שֶׁמִּתְאַבֵּל עָלָיו, מִתְאַבֵּל עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as one mourns for them, so too he mourns for their** relatives' relatives, who are his **second-degree relatives.** That is to say, just as one is required to mourn over his close relatives, so too he is required to mourn over his relatives' close relatives, which are known as second-degree relatives. For example, if his father's father, his son's son, his brother's son, or the like passed away, he must join his relatives in their mourning; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: He mourns only over his son's son and his father's father,** but not over the other relatives of his relatives. **And the Rabbis say: Any** relative **over whom one would mourn** if that person died, **one mourns with him** when he is in mourning.

The *baraita* then turns to second-generation relatives: *k'shem shemitabel aleihem, kach mitabel al shniyim shelahem* — just as one observes *aveilus* for these immediate family members, so too for their relatives — meaning a spouse's parent, a spouse's child who is not one's own, and so forth. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees: *eino mitabel ela al ben b'no ve'al avi aviv* — the only second-generation relationship that creates *aveilus* is that of a grandson or a grandfather, where there is a direct, unmediated connection.

### Chachamim Position

The Sages (*chachamim*) offer a third formulation: *kol shemitabel alav mitabel imo* — anyone for whom you yourself would observe *aveilus* if they died, you observe *aveilus together with* that person when they are themselves sitting *shiva*. Thus, if a person's sister is sitting *shiva* for a child — a niece or nephew — that person would sit *shiva* alongside his or her sister.

At first glance this seems identical to the *tana kama*, but the *gemara* draws a sharp distinction: *ika beina'ihu imo babayis* — the difference lies in whether the person is present in the same house. According to the *tana kama*, the obligation flows from a direct relationship to the deceased. According to the *chachamim*, the basis is not the relationship to the one who died but the relationship to the one who is sitting *shiva*. It is an act of identification and respect for that person, not an independent *aveilus* obligation. Consequently, the observance is conditioned on the mourner's presence.

### Direct vs. Respect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ, וְכֵן אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: בְּאַפַּהּ — נְהוֹג אֲבִילוּתָא, בְּלָא אַפַּהּ — לָא תִּינְהוֹג אֲבִילוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like what Rav said to his son, Ḥiyya, and** it is **similarly** like what **Rav Huna said to his son Rabba,** when the latter's wife was in mourning: **In her presence practice mourning,** but **out of her presence do not practice mourning.**

This distinction between a direct obligation and a respect-based observance has significant practical implications. If the observance derives from solidarity with the one sitting *shiva* — not from a direct bond to the deceased — then it applies only *be-apei*, in that person's presence. Outside their presence, there is no obligation.

### Rav & Chiya Case

This is illustrated by the teaching that Rav said to Chiya's son, and Rav Huna said to Rabba's son: *be-appa nehog gabei lusa, velo appa lo tignog gabei lusa* — in your wife's presence, observe *aveilus* with her; not in her presence, do not observe *aveilus* with her. This would apply when one's wife is sitting *shiva* for a parent — a father-in-law or mother-in-law.

### Mar Ukva Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר עוּקְבָא שְׁכֵיב לֵיהּ בַּר חֲמוּהּ. סְבַר לְמֵיתַב עֲלֵיהּ שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים, עָל רַב הוּנָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: צוּדָנְיָיתָא בָּעֵית לְמֵיכַל?! לֹא אָמְרוּ לִכְבוֹד אִשְׁתּוֹ — אֶלָּא חָמִיו וַחֲמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **the son of Mar Ukva's father-in-law,** i.e., Mar Ukva's brother-in-law, **died,** and Mar Ukva **thought to sit** in mourning **over him for** the **seven- and thirty-**day periods of mourning. **Rav Huna went into his** house, **found him** observing the rites of mourning, and **said to him: Do you desire to eat mourners' food [*tzudaniyyata*]?** The Sages **said** that one should observe mourning **in honor of his wife only** when she is in mourning over the death of **his father-in-law or his mother-in-law.**

The case of Mar Ukva sharpens the scope of this principle. Mar Ukva's brother-in-law died — his mother-in-law's son. He considered observing a full *shiva* and *shloshim* for him. Rav Huna came to visit and challenged him: "Do you want to eat a mourner's meal?" — meaning, do you consider yourself a mourner? Rav Huna clarified: *lo amru lichvod ishto ela chamiv vechamoso* — when the Rabbis said that a husband observes *aveilus* together with his wife out of respect for her, that applies only for a father-in-law or mother-in-law, not for a brother-in-law.

### Spouse Duties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִי שֶׁמֵּת חָמִיו אוֹ חֲמוֹתוֹ — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לָכוֹף אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, אֶלָּא כּוֹפֶה מִטָּתוֹ, וְנוֹהֵג עִמָּהּ אֲבֵילוּת. וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁמֵּת חָמִיהָ אוֹ חֲמוֹתָהּ — אֵינָהּ רַשָּׁאָה לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, אֶלָּא כּוֹפָה מִטָּתָהּ וְנוֹהֶגֶת עִמּוֹ אֲבֵילוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One whose father-in-law or mother-in-law died may not force his wife to paint [*koḥelet*]** her eyelids **or put rouge [*pokeset***] on her face while she is in mourning. **Rather, he should overturn his bed, and observe** the rites of **mourning with her. And similarly, when her father-in-law or mother-in-law dies, she may not paint** her eyelids **or put rouge** on her face. **Rather, she should overturn her bed and practice** the rites of **mourning with him.**

This is elaborated in a *baraita*: if a man's father-in-law or mother-in-law died, *eino rashai lachofos ishto lihyos kocheles u-pokeses* — he may not compel his wife to put on makeup or adorn herself while she is sitting *shiva*. More than that, *ela kofeh mittaso venoheg immah aveilus* — he must overturn his bed and observe *aveilus* together with her. Symmetrically, when a woman's father-in-law or mother-in-law dies — when her husband is sitting *shiva* — she similarly may not put on makeup, and she too must overturn her bed and observe *aveilus* together with him.

### Contradiction Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לָכוֹף אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לִהְיוֹת כּוֹחֶלֶת וְלִהְיוֹת פּוֹקֶסֶת, בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ מוֹזֶגֶת לוֹ אֶת הַכּוֹס, וּמַצַּעַת לוֹ מִטָּה, ומַרְחֶצֶת לוֹ פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **Even though** the Sages **said that** a husband **may not force his wife to paint** her eyelids **or put rouge** on her face when she is in mourning, i.e., that she may not treat her mourning lightly, **actually, they said** that **she may pour his cup** of wine, **make his bed, and wash his face, hands, and feet,** as these activities are expressions of affection between husband and wife rather than unnecessary adornment or a belittlement of the mourning.

A second *baraita*, however, appears to qualify this: *be-emes amru mozeges lo es hakos umatzah lo es hamitah u-meracheset panav yadav veraglav* — in truth they said that she still performs her wifely duties of mixing and pouring drinks, spreading the bed, and washing his face, hands, and feet. This seems to contradict the first *baraita*, which required turning over the bed — how can she both spread the bed and overturn it?

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction, as Rabbeinu Gershom formulates it: *de-hacha tnai matza lo mittaso ve-hacha tnai kofeh mittaso* — one *baraita* says she spreads the bed for him, the other says he overturns the bed. The resolution: *ela lav shema mina kan be-chamiv ve-chamoso kan bishear kerovim* — the two cases differ based on whose relative died. When it is his father-in-law or mother-in-law who died — and he is the one with a full *aveilus* relationship — he overturns the bed; she assists with her domestic duties but cannot be compelled to apply makeup. When it is her *chamav* or *chamosa* who died — his parent, and he is sitting *shiva* — the situation and the bed obligations differ accordingly.

What emerges from this entire discussion is a three-layered structure. The broadest view holds that any relationship one generation removed creates a direct *aveilus* obligation. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar narrows this to only grandson and grandfather. The *chachamim* and the *baraita* preserve a concept of *be-apei* observance — solidarity with a spouse or relative who is sitting *shiva* — but limit it to two conditions: physical presence, and, in the case of the spouse, only for a parent-in-law. In practice, this solidarity *aveilus* is observed in a relatively limited way today, though one certainly conducts oneself with appropriate dignity and restraint in a *shiva* house even for an extended obligation of this kind.

Tosafot, on the question of *be-afei nahag aveilus*, clarifies that "in his presence" encompasses the full range of *aveilus* prohibitions — no shoes, no washing — and that "in his presence" may extend to anywhere within the same house, not merely when one is literally face-to-face.

## Keriah Practice

### Ameimar's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר שָׁכֵיב לֵיהּ בַּר בְּרֵיהּ, קְרַע עִילָּוֵיהּ. אֲתָא בְּרֵיהּ — קְרַע בְּאַפֵּיהּ. אִידְּכַר דִּמְיוּשָּׁב קְרַע, קָם קְרַע מְעוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** It was further related that **the son of Ameimar's son died,** and Ameimar **rent** his garment **over him. His son came** before him, and **he rent** his garments again **in the presence of his** son, as an expression of empathy with his son's pain and grief. Later, **he remembered** that when he rent his garments in his son's presence **he rent** them while **sitting,** and therefore **he stood up** and **rent** his garment again while **standing.**

Ameimar's grandson died. Ameimar performed *kriya* for him — presumably reflecting a position, either his own or the *tana kama*'s, that *aveilus* extends to a grandson. Ameimar's son then arrived and performed *kriya* in his father's presence, *ata brei kara be-apei* — the son doing *kriya* for his own son, which he was fully obligated to do. The preferred reading (that of Tosafot, over against Rabbeinu Gershom) is that it was the son, not Ameimar, who performed *kriya* at this point — the son doing *kriya* for his deceased child.

Seeing his son stand to perform *kriya*, Ameimar suddenly recalled: *itkar de-meyushav kara* — he realized that when he had done *kriya* the first time, he had done so while seated. Like a person who enters *shul* during *musaf* and suddenly realizes it is *Rosh Chodesh* and they forgot *ya'aleh ve-yavo*, Ameimar immediately rose and performed *kriya* again, this time standing: *kam kara me-umad*.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: קְרִיעָה דִּמְעוּמָּד מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּקׇם אִיּוֹב וַיִּקְרַע אֶת מְעִילוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, the Gemara reports that **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: From where do we** derive that **rending** must be done while **standing?** He responded: **As it is written** about the deaths of Job's sons: **"Then Job arose, and rent his coat"** (Job 1:20).

This moved the discussion from the question of *aveilus* for indirect relatives to the requirement that *keriah* be performed standing. Rav Ashi challenged Ameimar: *kriya de-me-umad minalan* — what is the source that *keriah* must be done standing? Ameimar cited the verse: *vayakom Iyov vayikra et me'ilo* — "And Job arose and rent his garment" (Job 1:20).

### Job Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״וְעָמַד וְאָמַר לֹא חָפַצְתִּי לְקַחְתָּהּ״, הָכָא נָמֵי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: בֵּין יוֹשֵׁב בֵּין עוֹמֵד בֵּין מוּטֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi challenged him: **However, if** that is **so,** one should be required to stand while performing the ritual through which the brother-in-law frees the *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*], as the verse states: **"And if he stands and he says: I do not wish to take her"** (Deuteronomy 25:8). **Here** must he **also** stand for the ceremony? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that *ḥalitza* may be performed **whether** the man is **sitting, or standing, or reclining?**

Rav Ashi pressed further: if standing is derived from the verse by Job, then by analogy, the verse about *chalitzah* — *ve-amad ve-amar lo chafatzti le-kachtah*, "and he shall stand and say, I do not desire to take her" — should likewise require standing. Yet a *baraita* teaches: *bein yoshev bein omed bein mutah* — whether sitting, standing, or reclining, *chalitzah* is valid. Ameimar replied: *hatam ktiv ve'amad ve'amar, hacha ktiv vayakom vayikra* — there the verse says "he shall stand and say," a prescriptive formulation, whereas here it says "and he arose and rent," which is narrative and descriptive.

### Chalitzah Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָא כְּתִיב ״וְיַעֲמֹד וְיֹאמַר״, הָכָא כְּתִיב ״וַיָּקׇם וַיִּקְרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: There,** in the case of *ḥalitza*, **it is not written: And he will stand and he will say,** which would imply an obligation to stand; whereas **here,** in the case of rending, **it is written: "Then** Job **arose, and rent** his coat" (Job 1:20).

One way of understanding the distinction is that a prescriptive "shall stand" in law, when it is ultimately not strictly required (as the *baraita* permits *chalitzah* even seated), is less compelling than a purely narrative "arose" read as implied normative practice. Another reading is that *ve-amad ve-amar* does not literally mean standing at all — it can mean "and he roused himself and said," without implying a standing posture — whereas *vayakom* more directly connotes rising to one's feet.

### King David Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מִנַּיִן לִקְרִיעָה שֶׁהִיא מְעוּמָּד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּקׇם אִיּוֹב וַיִּקְרַע״. דִּלְמָא מִילְּתָא יַתִּירְתָּא הוּא דַּעֲבַד? דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי — ״וַיָּגׇז אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: From where** is it derived **that rending must be** performed while **standing? As it is stated: "Then Job arose, and rent** his coat" (Job 1:20). The Gemara asks: **Perhaps he did something extra** beyond what is required, and actually there is no obligation to stand; **as, if you do not say** that he did more than what was required of him, then how do you explain the continuation of the verse: **"And he shaved his head"** (Job 1:20)? Is every mourner required to act in **this** manner **also** and shave his head?

Rami bar Chama proposed an alternative source: *vayakom hamelech vayikra et begadav* — "And the king arose and rent his garments" (2 Samuel 13:31), King David's *keriah* upon hearing of Amnon's death.

### David's Propriety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וַיָּקָם הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיִּקְרַע אֶת בְּגָדָיו״. וְדִלְמָא מִילְּתָא יַתִּירְתָּא עָבֵיד? דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי — ״וַיִּשְׁכַּב אָרְצָה״, הָכִי נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather,** this source must be rejected, and instead the *halakha* is derived **from here,** the verse that describes David's mourning over his son: **"Then the king arose, and rent his garments"** (II Samuel 13:31). The Gemara asks: But **perhaps he** too **did something extra** beyond what is required, and he was not actually obligated to stand; **as, if you do not say** that he did more than what was required of him, then how do you explain the continuation of the verse: **"And he lay on the earth"** (II Samuel 13:31)? Is every mourner required to act in **this** manner **also?**

But the *gemara* again raises the objection: *dilma milta yetirata hu de'avad* — perhaps David was simply acting in an excessive or supererogatory manner. And if the verse is not normative, one cannot derive a *halacha* from it. If David's actions do not constitute normative *aveilus* practice, then one cannot derive that *keriah* must be performed standing. To illustrate, the *gemara* asks: the same passage says *vayazon et rosho* — he shaved his head — does that mean an *avel* must shave his head? Obviously not.

### David's Lying Down

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יָשַׁב עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה, עַל גַּבֵּי כִּסֵּא, עַל גַּבֵּי אוּדְיָינִי גְּדוֹלָה, עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע — מִכּוּלָּן לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁלֹּא קִיֵּים כְּפִיַּית הַמִּטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If a mourner **sat on a bed, on a chair, on a large mortar [*udyanei*], or on the ground, he does not fulfill his obligation through any of them. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **because he did not fulfill** his obligation to **overturn his bed,** even if he lay on the ground. This indicates that there is no requirement whatsoever to lie on the ground.

Tosafot raises an additional dimension here. The verse says *vayishtachu artzah* — he prostrated himself on the ground — before the child of Bat Sheva had actually died; he was praying for the child's survival. Nevertheless, Tosafot notes that because the prophet had already declared *mot yamut* — the child will die — it may be that David treated the child *kemet*, as if already dead, and thereby performed practices of *aveilus* in anticipation. Though fascinating, the *gemara* itself does not develop this inference.

Returning to the objection about lying on the ground: the *gemara* cites a *baraita* that an *avel* may sit on a bed, a chair, or even a large overturned object such as a mortar (*machteshet*), and in fact, as Rabbi Yochanan taught, sitting directly on the ground is not considered fulfillment of *kfiyat hamita* — the overturning of the bed — because the authentic expression of *aveilus* is sitting on the lowly overturned bed itself, not simply on the earth. David's lying on the ground, therefore, cannot be cited as standard *hilchos aveilus*. The *gemara* then replies: *ke'ein artzah* — when the verse says he was on the ground, it is to be understood as being near the ground, i.e., on a low overturned bed, so that his actions were indeed normative *aveilus* — and since he then arose to perform *keriah*, that arising is the source for performing *keriah* standing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּעֵין אַרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** Ameimar **said to** Rav Ashi: The verse does not mean that David actually lay on the ground. Rather, it was **as if** he were **on the ground,** since he overturned his bed and brought it closer to the ground. If this is the case, then Ameimar acted correctly, and a mourner should rend his garments while standing.

The *gemara* thus presents two proofs — from Job and from David — each with difficulties, but offering the best available sources for the requirement that *keriah* be performed standing.

## Shiva Prohibitions

### Prohibition List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָבֵל אָסוּר בָּהֶן: אָסוּר בִּמְלָאכָה וּבִרְחִיצָה וּבְסִיכָה וּבְתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה וּבִנְעִילַת הַסַּנְדָּל. וְאָסוּר לִקְרוֹת בְּתוֹרָה וּבַנְּבִיאִים וּבַכְּתוּבִים, וְלִשְׁנוֹת בַּמִּשְׁנָה, בַּמִּדְרָשׁ וּבָהֲלָכוֹת, וּבַתַּלְמוּד וּבָאַגָּדוֹת. וְאִם הָיוּ רַבִּים צְרִיכִין לוֹ — אֵינוֹ נִמְנָע.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: These are the activities that a mourner is prohibited from** engaging **in: He is prohibited from working, and from bathing, and from anointing** himself with oil, **and from engaging in sexual relations, and from wearing shoes. And he is prohibited from reading in the Torah, and in the Prophets, and in the Writings, and from studying in the Mishna, in the midrash, and in the *halakhot*, and in the Talmud, and in the *aggadot*. But if the public needs him** to teach them these things, **he need not refrain** from doing so.

The *gemara* presents a *tanna rabanan* that enumerates, in distilled form, the prohibitions that apply to an *avel* during *shiva*: *assur bimelacha* — forbidden to do work; *ubirechitzah* — bathing; *ubissichah* — anointing; *uvitashmish hamitah* — conjugal relations; *ubine'ilas hassandal* — wearing shoes. The observant reader will immediately notice the parallel to *Yom Kippur*.

### Work Prohibition

The prohibition of *melacha* was addressed earlier in the tractate and will be elaborated further below.

### Physical Prohibitions

Bathing, anointing, shoes, and conjugal relations constitute the cluster of physical pleasures restricted during *shiva*, mirroring the five afflictions of *Yom Kippur*.

### Torah Learning

The *baraita* continues: *assur likros baTorah biNevi'im uvaKesuvim ulishnos baMishnah bamidrash bahalachos uv'aggados* — the *avel* is forbidden to read from Torah, Prophets, and Writings, or to study *Mishnah*, *midrash*, *halachos*, or *aggados*. At this point the list takes on a different character, paralleling not Yom Kippur but *Tisha B'Av*. This connection is not coincidental: the practices of *Tisha B'Av* are themselves drawn from *minhagei aveilus*. Torah study is an expression of joy, and even joy rooted in something as profound as learning is displaced during the week of *shiva*.

### Community Exception

The *baraita* qualifies this: *im rabbim tzrichin lo, eino nimna* — if the community needs the scholar, he does not hold himself back.

### Rabbi Yosei Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה וּמֵת בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, וְדָרַשׁ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **There was an incident that the son of Rabbi Yosei died in Tzippori, and** Rabbi Yosei **entered the study hall and expounded** there **for the entire day.**

This is illustrated by the case of Rabbi Yosei, whose son died in Tzippori. Despite his bereavement, he entered the *beit hamidrash* and expounded throughout the entire day.

### Rabbah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אִיתְּרַעָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא, סָבַר דְּלָא לְמִיפַּק לְפִירְקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אִם הָיוּ רַבִּים צְרִיכִין לוֹ — אֵינוֹ נִמְנָע. סְבַר לְאוֹקוֹמֵי אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: תַּנְיָא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲמִיד תּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **a calamity,** i.e., a death in the family, once **befell Rabba bar bar Ḥana,** and **he thought not to go out to** deliver **his exposition. Rabbi Ḥanina said to him:** Is it not taught that **if the public needs him** to teach them these things, **one need not refrain** from doing so? **He** then **thought to place an interpreter alongside him,** who would sound his words to the public, as was normally done for such an exposition. **Rav said to him: It is taught** in a *baraita* similarly: **Provided that he does not place the disseminator** alongside him.

A related case involves Rabbah bar bar Chana, for whom an *atra bei milsa* — a loss occurred. He considered not going out to give his *pirka* (public lecture). Rabbi Chanina told him: *im hayu rabbim tzrichin lo eino nimna* — if the masses need him, he should not hold back. Rabbah bar bar Chana then considered having his *amora* stand with him — the *amora* being the figure who would receive the lecture in a low voice and then announce it loudly to the assembled audience. However, Rav told him: a *baraita* teaches *uvillvad shelo ya'amid turgaman* — he may not appoint a *turgeman* or similarly an *amora*, because that would constitute too prominent and status-conferring a role for one who is in *aveilus*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? כִּי הָא דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וּמֵת בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַאי, וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. וְנִכְנַס רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא וְיָשַׁב בְּצִדּוֹ, וְלָחַשׁ הוּא לְרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא, וְרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא לְתוּרְגְּמָן, וְתוּרְגְּמָן הִשְׁמִיעַ לָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how,** then, should he **act** so that he can be heard? The Gemara answers: It is **like that which is taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident and the son of Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai died, and** Rabbi Yehuda **entered the study hall. And Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya** entered after him and **sat by his side.** Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai then **whispered** his lecture **to Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya, and Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya** whispered it **to the disseminator, and the disseminator sounded to the public** what had been told to him. In this way, it became known that Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai was in mourning.

The *gemara* therefore asks: in what manner should such a scholar proceed? The answer is provided by a *baraita* describing the case of Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai, whose son died. He entered the *beit hamidrash* and was accompanied by Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya — not a mere translator, but a rabbinic colleague — who sat beside him.

Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai whispered to Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya what he wished to teach, line by line. Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya then relayed it to the *turgeman*, and the *turgeman* announced it to the assembled public. The mechanism here is not a technical workaround. From an observer's perspective, the one who is visibly addressing the *turgeman* — and therefore appearing to lead the session — is Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya, not Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai. The status of public address and its attendant dignity are thereby deflected. The greater the remove from the scholar in *aveilus*, the less the association of formal status.

### Prohibition vs. Practice

Tosafot raises the important conceptual point that this list of prohibitions is not exhaustive. The *baraita* formulates itself as *elu devarim she'ha'avel assur bahen* — these are things the *avel* is forbidden to do. By contrast, it does not enumerate the active *practices* of *aveilus* — things the *avel* is *commanded* to do, such as *kfiyat hamita* (overturning the bed) and *atifat harosh* (covering the head). Those are positive *minhagei aveilus*, not prohibitions, and so they fall outside the *baraita*'s framing.

### List Incompleteness

Even within the category of prohibitions, the list is incomplete. Tosafot notes that *tefillin* and *she'eilas shalom* are not included. The reason is instructive: *tefillin* applies only on day one (as we shall see below), and *she'eilas shalom* differs between the first three days and the last four. Since these prohibitions are variable in scope across the *shiva* period, they do not fit neatly into a uniform list of across-the-board forbiddens. The *baraita* lists only those prohibitions that apply uniformly throughout. The incompleteness is thus principled, not accidental.

It is also worth noting here, in connection with Torah study at a *shiva* house, that Tosafot records the view of Rabbeinu Tam, who initially held that even the sections of *Tanakh* and topics permitted on *Tisha B'Av* — such as *Iyov*, *Kinot*, and the difficult passages of *Yirmiya* — are forbidden during *shiva*, since the *gemara* does not explicitly permit them as it does for *Tisha B'Av*. However, in his later years Rabbeinu Tam retracted this and permitted those sections. The *Yerushalmi* additionally permits an *avel* to study in an area outside his normal area of learning, since the unfamiliarity diminishes the pleasure.

## Tefillin Rules

### Three Days Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָבֵל אָסוּר בָּהֶן: אָסוּר בִּמְלָאכָה וּבִרְחִיצָה וּבְסִיכָה וּבְתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה וּבִנְעִילַת הַסַּנְדָּל. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָבֵל, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים — אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, מִשְּׁלִישִׁי וְאֵילָךְ, וּשְׁלִישִׁי בַּכְּלָל — מוּתָּר לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין. וְאִם בָּאוּ פָּנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת — אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵץ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: For **the first three days, a mourner is prohibited from donning phylacteries. From the third** day **and on, and including the third** day, **he is permitted to don phylacteries. And if new faces,** i.e., people who had not yet come to console him, **come, he need not remove** them, although these people may falsely conclude that he had also donned phylacteries during the first two days of his mourning; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

A *tanna rabanan* teaches: *avel gimmel yamim harishonim assur lehaniach tefillin* — an *avel* during the first three days may not don *tefillin*. This is striking: *tefillin* is a biblical *mitzvah*, and here the prohibition of *aveilus* overrides it. The framework of three days parallels the *mishnah*'s treatment of the first three days as a distinct and intensified phase of *aveilus*.

### Day Three Permission

The *baraita* continues: *u'mishlishi v'eilach v'shlishi bichlal, mutar lehaniach tefillin* — from day three onward, including day three itself, one is permitted to don *tefillin*. The formulation is unusual: the *baraita* says three days are forbidden yet permits them on day three itself. The conceptual resolution is *miktzat hayom kekulo*: the very beginning of day three — even its first moment — begins the permitted zone, such that in practice only two full days are actually excluded.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

The *baraita* adds a further detail from Rabbi Eliezer: *v'im ba'u panim chadashot, eino choletz* — if new visitors arrive at the *shiva* house after day three, the *avel* need not remove his *tefillin* out of concern that they will think he has been wearing them from the beginning of *aveilus*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the essential period is two days (with day three already within the permitted zone), once the *avel* has observed the core days without *tefillin*, he may continue wearing them without concern for what newcomers might think.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אָבֵל, שְׁנֵי יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין. מִשֵּׁנִי, וְשֵׁנִי בַּכְּלָל — מוּתָּר לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וְאִם בָּאוּ פָּנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת — חוֹלֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: For the first two days, a mourner is prohibited from donning** phylacteries. **From the second** day, **and including the second** day, **he is permitted to wear** phylacteries. **But if new faces come in** to console him, **he must remove** his phylacteries.

Rabbi Yehoshua takes a more lenient position on the duration: *b'shnei yamim harishonim assur lehaniach* — only the first two days are forbidden, and *mishani v'hasheni bikhlal, mutar lehaniach tefillin* — from day two onward, including day two, one may don *tefillin*. In practice this means only day one is truly excluded. However, Rabbi Yehoshua is more stringent about the presence of new visitors: *choletz* — if new people arrive and see the *avel* wearing *tefillin*, he must remove them, to avoid creating the impression that he has been wearing them throughout.

### Verse Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְּמוּ יְמֵי בְכִי אֵבֶל מֹשֶׁה״. אָמַר רַב עֵינָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַחֲרִיתָהּ כְּיוֹם מָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana said: What is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer? As it is written: "And the days of weeping and mourning for Moses ended"** (Deuteronomy 34:8). The plural term "days" implies a minimum of two, and it indicates that the main portion of the mourning period is the first two full days. **Rav Eina said: What is the reason of Rabbi Yehoshua? As it is written:** "And I will make it as the mourning for an only son, **and its end as a bitter day"** (Amos 8:10), i.e., a single "day."

What is the basis for two days versus one? Rav Chisda explains Rabbi Eliezer's position: the verse says *vayitmu yimei bekhi eivel Moshe* — "the days of weeping over Moses were completed" (Deuteronomy 34:8) — using the plural *yamim*, which indicates a minimum of two days. Hence the core of *aveilus* that can override *tefillin* spans two days.

Rav Eina explains Rabbi Yehoshua's position from a different verse: *v'acharitah k'yom mar* — "its aftermath is like a bitter day" (Amos 8:10) — in the singular. The essential bitterness — and therefore the essential period during which the *mitzvah* of *tefillin* is overridden — is only a single day.

### Override Limited

The *gemara*'s underlying assumption is clear: the power of *aveilus* to override a biblical *mitzvah* such as *tefillin* is not unlimited. It applies only to the *ikar* — the essential core — of *aveilus*. The debate is whether that core is two days or one.

### Day Two Gray Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בַּחֲלִיצָה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּהַנָּחָה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּשֵּׁנִי לְעוּלָּא, חוֹלֵץ אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵץ? תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר עוּלָּא: חוֹלֵץ וּמַנִּיחַ אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer with regard to** the **removal** of phylacteries. A mourner need not remove them when new people come in to console him. **And the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua with regard to the donning** of phylacteries, and so a mourner may already don phylacteries on the second day. **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: **According** to **Ulla,** who rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehoshua that a mourner may don phylacteries **on** his second **day** of mourning, is the mourner required to **remove** them if new people arrive on that day, **or** is he **not** required to **remove** them? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** what **Ulla said** explicitly: **One removes** his phylacteries when new people come to console him, **and he dons** them again when they leave, **even** if he must don and remove them **a hundred times.**

*Ulla* rules: *halakhah ke-Rabbi Eliezer b'chalitzah, v'halakhah ke-Rabbi Yehoshua b'hanachah* — we rule leniently in both directions. We follow Rabbi Eliezer that once *tefillin* are worn, they need not be removed for new arrivals; and we follow Rabbi Yehoshua that they may be donned after day one. This produces the most lenient combination. But a question arose: when *Ulla* said one does not remove *tefillin*, that ruling was stated in the context of the position that permits from day three onward. What happens on day two according to *Ulla*? *Ulla* himself answered: *choletz u-maniach afilu meiah pe'amim* — one removes and replaces even a hundred times. Day two is a gray zone: one may don *tefillin*, but must remove them whenever new, uninformed visitors arrive.

### Rava's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִנִּיחַ שׁוּב אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵץ. וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּתַנָּא דִּידַן דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Once he dons** his phylacteries, **he does not remove** them **again.** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it Rava** himself who **said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the *tanna*** of **our** mishna, who **said** that the most serious period of mourning is **three** days? How then does he rule in accordance with the *baraita* that a mourner may don phylacteries during these days?

Rava goes further: *kivan she-heniach shuv eino choletz* — once a person has put on *tefillin* (on day two), he need not remove them even if new visitors arrive. This is the most lenient of all positions.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **A mitzva is different,** and since it is a mitzva to put on phylacteries, the mourner is required to do so, even during the first three days.

The *gemara* notes a potential tension: Rava is also the one who rules that an *avel* must observe three days of *aveilus* before a *regel* can annul it — a seemingly strict position. How can he also be the most lenient about *tefillin*? The answer: *mitzvah shanei* — the performance of a *mitzvah* is different. The framework of three days as a conceptual minimum for *aveilus* is one matter; the question of how many days can override the biblical *mitzvah* of *tefillin* is another. On the latter, even Rava limits the override to day one.

In practice, we follow the most lenient positions throughout. The *avel* does not wear *tefillin* on the day of burial (day one of *aveilus*) until nightfall, and from the following morning onward wears *tefillin* without removal for new visitors.

## Work Details

### Three Day Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָבֵל, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אָסוּר בִּמְלָאכָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ עָנִי הַמִּתְפַּרְנֵס מִן הַצְּדָקָה. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — עוֹשֶׂה בְּצִינְעָא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. וְהָאִשָּׁה טוֹוָה בַּפֶּלֶךְ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **During the first three days** after his bereavement, **a mourner is prohibited from working, even if he is a poor person who is supported by charity. From this** point **forward, he may do** work **privately in his** own **home** if he needs to do so. **And** similarly **a woman may spin** thread **on a spindle in her** own **home** when she is mourning.

The *gemara* now turns to the prohibition of *melacha*. A *tanna rabanan* states: *avel gimmel yamim harishonim assur b'melacha* — for the first three days, an *avel* is prohibited from performing work.

### Poor Exception

This applies even in extreme circumstances: *v'afilu ani hamitparneis min hatzdakah* — even a poor person who subsists on communal charity, who might otherwise need to work to put food on the table and reduce his dependence on public funds, is prohibited from working during the first three days.

### Private Work Later

After those three days: *mikan v'eilach oseh betzina betoch beito* — a person may perform work privately within his home.

### Women's Work

Additionally: *v'ishah tova b'pelekh betoch beita* — a woman may spin with a spindle within her home. The precise rationale is not entirely clear: it may be that spinning or similar domestic tasks are treated as not quite rising to the level of formal *melacha*, or that performing them privately within the home places them in a different category once the first three days have passed.

### Work Ambiguity

The *gemara* here contains significant ambiguity. The statement *mikan v'eilach oseh betzina betoch beito* — after three days one may work privately in the home — could be read in two ways. Either it is a general rule for all *aveilim* that the prohibition of *melacha* applies only for the first three days, after which private work at home is permitted; or it is a continuation of the case of the *ani*, specifying that even the poor person, once past the first three days, may work privately. Tosafot discusses this question at length without reaching a definitive conclusion. Similarly, the question of whether others may perform work on behalf of the *avel* — for example, employees of a business the *avel* owns — is not clearly resolved in the *gemara* at this point.

## Visiting Rules

### Three Day Isolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָבֵל, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אֵינוֹ הוֹלֵךְ לְבֵית הָאֵבֶל, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ הוֹלֵךְ, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹם הַמְנַחֲמִין, אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם הַמִּתְנַחֲמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in another *baraita*: **A mourner during the first three days** after his bereavement **may not go to** another **mourner's house** to console him. **From this** point **forward, he may go, but he may not sit among the consolers, but rather in the place of those being consoled,** i.e., with the mourners in that house.

A further *tanna rabanan*: *avel gimmel yamim harishonim eino holech leveit ha'avel* — during the first three days, an *avel* may not go to another mourner's house. This should not be confused with a prohibition on leaving one's own home. What is prohibited is visiting another *avel*'s house — a context that would position the mourner as a consoler rather than one in need of consolation, and that is inappropriate during the most acute phase of *aveilus*.

### Later Visiting

From day four through seven: *holech ve'eino yoshev bimkom hamenachamim ela bimkom hamitnachamim* — the *avel* may go to another mourner's house but does not sit in the place of the consolers. Rather, he sits among those being consoled. Even during the later days of *shiva*, the *avel*'s primary identity remains that of a mourner, and the way he positions himself in another *shiva* house must reflect that.

## Sheilas Shalom

### Three Day Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָבֵל, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אָסוּר בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם. מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד שִׁבְעָה — מֵשִׁיב וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹאֵל. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, שׁוֹאֵל וּמֵשִׁיב כְּדַרְכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in yet another *baraita*: **A mourner, during the first three days** after his bereavement, **is prohibited from extending greetings** to others. **From the third** day **to the seventh** day, **he may respond** when other people address him, **but he may not extend greetings** to them. **From this** point **forward, he may extend greetings and respond in his** usual **manner.**

A *tanna rabanan* teaches: *avel gimmel yamim harishonim assur beshe'eilas shalom* — during the first three days, an *avel* may not engage in *she'eilas shalom* (asking after another's well-being). The scope of this prohibition will be unpacked below.

### Response Only

From day three through day seven: *meshiv ve'eino shoel* — the *avel* may respond if someone addresses him, but does not initiate.

### Normal After

After the *shiva*: *shoel u'meshiv kedarko* — the *avel* may both initiate and respond normally.

### Rabbi Akiva Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אָסוּר בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם? וְהָתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וּמֵתוּ בָּנָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, נִכְנְסוּ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהִסְפִּידוּם הֶסְפֵּד גָּדוֹל. בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתָן, עָמַד רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עַל סַפְסָל גָּדוֹל וְאָמַר: אַחֵינוּ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל שִׁמְעוּ! אֲפִילּוּ שְׁנֵי בָנִים חֲתָנִים — מְנוּחָם הוּא בִּשְׁבִיל כָּבוֹד שֶׁעֲשִׂיתֶם. וְאִם בִּשְׁבִיל עֲקִיבָא בָּאתֶם, הֲרֵי כַּמָּה עֲקִיבָא בַּשּׁוּק. אֶלָּא כָּךְ אֲמַרְתֶּם: תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהָיו בְּלִבּוֹ. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁשְּׂכַרְכֶם כָּפוּל, לְכוּ לְבָתֵּיכֶם לְשָׁלוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is he really **prohibited** from extending **greetings during the first three** days of mourning? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident and the sons of Rabbi Akiva died, and all the Jews entered to eulogize them with a great eulogy. When they were about to take leave, Rabbi Akiva stood on a large bench and said: Our brothers, the house of Israel, listen! Even had my two sons been bridegrooms, I would have been consoled on account of the honor you have shown** them. **If you came** to console **for the sake of Akiva, there are many Akivas in the marketplace** whom the Jews do all not come to console. **Rather,** certainly **this is what you said** to yourselves: **"The Torah of his God is in his heart"** (Psalms 37:31), and you wished to show your respect for the Torah. **All the more so is your reward doubled,** for you have consoled mourners and shown respect for the Torah. **Return** now **to your homes in peace.** This indicates that a mourner may greet other people even on the first day of his mourning.

The *gemara* immediately raises an apparent contradiction. A case is cited: when Rabbi Akiva's sons died — the plural is itself arresting — the entire Jewish people came, gave a great eulogy, and as they were departing, Rabbi Akiva mounted a large bench to be heard and addressed the crowd: *acheinu beit Yisrael, shemu* — our brothers of the house of Israel, hear me. He said: *afilu shenei banim chatanim menucham hu bishvil kavod she'asitem* — even though these were two sons who had been bridegrooms, I am consoled by the honor you have shown. And he concluded: *lechu levateichem leshalom* — go to your homes in peace (*leshalom*).

This last phrase is a form of *she'eilas shalom*, yet it was spoken during the first three days. The *gemara* answers: *kevod rabbim shani* — honoring the assembled public is different. When the multitude had come to honor Rabbi Akiva's *Torah*, he honored them in return; the context of *kevod rabbim* created an exception.

### Shalom Meaning

The case of Rabbi Akiva also illuminates what *she'eilas shalom* means. In Hebrew, *shalom* connotes not merely a greeting but *shleimut* — wholeness, completeness. To wish someone *shalom*, or to ask after their *shalom*, is to invoke a state of intactness that is simply not appropriate during *aveilus*. The *avel* is not in a state of *shleimut*; to ask after his *shalom* would be incongruous, and for him to wish it to others participates in a social register that is at odds with his condition. The distinction the *gemara* develops between "initiating" and "responding" — and between asking about one's own *shalom* versus another's — turns significantly on this semantic weight of the word *shalom*.

### Thirty Day Consolations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמּוֹצֵא אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אָבֵל, בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ, לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita* that states: **One who finds another in mourning during the** first **thirty days** of that person's bereavement **may** still **speak words of consolation to him, but he should not extend greetings toward him.** If he finds him **after thirty days, he may extend greetings toward him, and he should not speak words of consolation to him,** so as not to remind him of his pain.

The *gemara* then introduces a teaching from *Masechet Semachot*: if one encounters a friend who is an *avel* within the thirty days of mourning — after *shiva* but before the end of *shloshim* — *midaber imo tanchumin* — one may speak words of consolation — *ve'eino shoel b'shlomo* — but one does not ask after his *shalom*.

### After Thirty Days

After *shloshim*: *shoel b'shlomo ve'eino midaber imo tanchumin* — the reverse obtains. One asks after his well-being, the person having now re-entered normal society, but one does not speak words of *tanchumin*, because doing so risks reopening a wound that is beginning to heal.

### Avidi bar Avin's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הוּא שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹם אֲחֵרִים — שֶׁאֲחֵרִים שְׁרוּיִים בְּשָׁלוֹם, אֲחֵרִים אֵין שׁוֹאֲלִים בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ, שֶׁהוּא אֵינוֹ שָׁרוּי בְּשָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Idi bar Avin said:** The two *baraitot* refer to different situations: The first *baraita* is referring to the mourner **himself, who may extend greetings [*shalom*] to others** after the completion of his seven days of mourning, **as the others are at peace [*shalom*].** The second *baraita*, which speaks of a prohibition that lasts thirty days, is referring to **other people, who may not extend greetings to him, as he,** the mourner, **is not at peace.**

This raises a question: we said earlier that after *shiva* the *avel* may engage in *she'eilas shalom*, yet the *Masechet Semachot* says that during *shloshim* one does not ask after the *avel*'s *shalom*. Avidi bar Avin resolves this: *hu shoel b'shalom chaveirim shehem shruyim b'shalom* — during *shloshim*, the *avel* may ask after others' *shalom*, because they are in a state of *shleimut* and he is directing the inquiry toward their wholeness, not his own. What the *avel* may not do, and what others may not do to him, is ask after his own *shalom* — *she'hu eino sharui b'shalom* — because he is still not in a state of *shleimut*.

The distinction then is: social interaction (asking about others) — permitted after *shiva*; asking after the *avel*'s own *shalom* — not until after *shloshim*.

### Mourner's Shalom Ban

During the entire *shloshim*, others should not ask after the *avel*'s *shalom*, because he is not yet *sharui b'shalom*.

### Unaware Responder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי מֵשִׁיב, מִכְּלָל דְּשָׁיְילִינַן לֵיהּ? דְּלָא יָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But from** the fact **that it teaches** in a *baraita* that after the third day of his bereavement, the mourner may **respond** when other people address him, **by inference** others **may extend greetings to him.** The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case where people **did not know** that he was in mourning and unknowingly extended greetings to him.

This creates a further question. The *baraita* says that from day three through seven the *avel* "responds" — implying that someone is in fact asking after his *shalom*. But how is someone asking, if it's prohibited? The answer: *delo yad'ei* — people sometimes ask without being aware that the person is an *avel*, or without knowing they are not supposed to ask. In such cases, the *avel* may respond after the first three days.

### No Response Days 1-3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָתָם נָמֵי! הָתָם — מוֹדַע לְהוּ וְלָא מַהְדַּר לְהוּ, הָכָא — לָא צָרִיךְ לְאוֹדוֹעִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the *baraita* speaks of a case where the people who greeted him were unaware that he was in mourning, then **there also,** during the first three days of his bereavement, he should be permitted to respond when other people address him. The Gemara answers: **There,** during the first three days, **he must inform them** that he is in mourning **and not respond** to their greetings. **Here,** after the first three days, **he need not inform them** about his bereavement, but rather he may respond to their greetings.

But why not respond even during the first three days, if the questioner was simply unaware? The *gemara* answers: *hatam moda lehu velo mehadar lehu* — during the first three days, even if someone unknowingly says *shalom aleichem* or *ma shlomcha*, the *avel* must inform them: "I am an *avel*, I cannot respond." He does not respond in kind. After the first three days, however, the *avel* need not flag the situation — *lo tzarich lehod'inhu* — and may simply respond. The concern for the other person's discomfort is balanced against the *avel*'s own halakhic state, and the line is drawn at the boundary of the first three days.

### After 30 Days Ok

After *shloshim*, asking after the *avel*'s *shalom* is permitted; the *avel* has been restored to a state of fuller social participation.

### Parent Year Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמּוֹצֵא אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אָבֵל, בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ — מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין, וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ. לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ — שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין, אֲבָל מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita* that states: **One who finds another in mourning during** his **twelve months** of bereavement **may** still **speak words of consolation to him, but he should not extend greetings toward him.** If he finds him **after twelve months, he may extend greetings toward him, and he should not speak words of consolation to him. He may, however, speak to him indirectly,** i.e., he may say to him: May you be consoled, without mentioning the name of the deceased.

A further *baraita* from *Masechet Semachot* extends the framework to a full year: *hamotze et chaveiro avel betoch yod-bet chodesh, medaber imo tanchumin, ve'eino shoel b'shlomo* — if within the twelve months one encounters a friend who is in mourning, one speaks words of consolation and does not ask after his *shalom*. After the twelve months: *shoel b'shlomo ve'eino medaber imo tanchumin* — one asks after the *shalom* and does not speak *tanchumin*. The twelve-month framework applies to mourning for a father or mother; for other relatives, the framework closes at thirty days.

### Broken Leg Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הַמּוֹצֵא אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אָבֵל לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, וּמְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין, לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה רַגְלוֹ וְחָיְתָה, מְצָאוֹ רוֹפֵא וְאָמַר לוֹ: כְּלָךְ אֶצְלִי שֶׁאֲנִי שׁוֹבְרָהּ וַאֲרַפְּאֶנָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁתֵּדַע שֶׁסַּמְמָנִין שֶׁלִּי יָפִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir said: One who finds another in mourning after twelve months and speaks to him words of consolation, to what may** this situation **be likened? To a person who broke his leg and it healed,** and afterward a physician **found him and said to him: Come to me, for I will break it** a second time **and** then **I will heal it, so that you may know how good my medicines are** and how well they work. One who consoles his friend after so much time has passed acts in a similar fashion, stirring up an old wound and then trying to heal it. In any event, it appears that one must not extend greetings to a mourner during the entire twelve-month mourning period.

Rabbi Meir addresses the case of someone who, a year after a death, nevertheless launches into a full *shiva*-style consolation. *Le-ma hu domeh?* — to what is this compared? *Le-adam shenishbera raglo, ve-chayeta, u-matza'o rofe, ve-amar lo: bo etzli, she-ani shovera ve'erpa'ena, kedei she-teda she-sammanin sheli yaffin* — to a person whose leg broke, healed, and a doctor found him and said: come to me, I will break it and reset it so that you will see how effective my medicine is. The image is as vivid as it is painful. Paying an elaborate *shiva* call after a year has passed reopens a wound that has healed. The impulse may arise from genuine sentiment, but it is oriented around the consoler's own need to do something rather than around what the mourner actually needs at this stage. The *avel* has healed; there is no virtue in causing fresh pain.

### Indirect Consolations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּאָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, הָא בִּשְׁאָר קְרוֹבִים. הָתָם נָמֵי, יְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין מִן הַצַּד? אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. וּמַאי אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ תַּנְחוּמִין — כְּדַרְכּוֹ, אֲבָל מְדַבֵּר עִמּוֹ מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is not difficult. This** *baraita*, which says that one must not extend greetings to a mourner for twelve months, is referring to one who lost **his father or mother. That** *baraita*, which teaches that a mourner may be greeted after seven days, is referring to one who is in mourning over **other relatives.** The Gemara asks: **There too,** in the case of other relatives after thirty days, **let him speak words of consolation to him indirectly.** Why does the *baraita* say that after thirty days he should not speak words of consolation to him at all? The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so. And what** is meant by the words: **He should not speak words of consolation to him?** This means that he may not console him **in his** usual **manner, but he may speak to him indirectly.**

The *gemara*'s final word on this is a refinement. Once past the thirty-day threshold (or the year, for parents), one does not speak *tanchumin kedarko* — in the normal, direct way — but *medaber imo min hatzad* — makes some oblique allusion, acknowledges what occurred without turning it into a *shiva* call. The *gemara* confirms: *ein hachi nami* — yes, this applies even after *shloshim* for other relatives: the prohibition on full *tanchumin* means only *kedarko*, in the direct way, but some indirect, sideways acknowledgment remains appropriate and, indeed, in many circumstances, deeply appreciated.
