# Bava Metzia 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pruta Lost or Found

### Pruta Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ – תִּנָּתֵן לְמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ, דְּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ שַׁעוֹרֵי קָא מְשַׁעַר לַהּ כַּד מִכַּסֵּי לַהּ מָרַהּ וְקָאֵי, וּמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ לָא מִשְׁתַּעַר לֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one claimant provides **the measure of length of** a lost garment **and** the other provides **the measure of its width,** the garment **shall be given to** the claimant who provided the **measure of its length, as one can approximate the measure of its width when its owner dons** the garment **and stands, but the measure of its length cannot be approximated** in that manner.

The daf begins by picking up at the bottom of 27a, where the Gemara has been analyzing the various words employed in the *pesukim* dealing with *aveidah*. A prior discussion had noted that the word *שמלה* (*simla*), garment, teaches the principle of *simanim* and of *to'im* — that one returns a lost object when there are claimants and when there is some distinguishing mark. The Gemara had also raised the question of the *pruta* threshold: since an object worth less than a *pruta* need not be returned, from which word in the verse is this derived — from *asher tovad*, "that was lost" (indicating significance at the moment of loss), or from *u-metzata*, "and you found it" (indicating significance at the moment of finding)?

On the face of it, there appears to be no *nafka mina* between the two positions — it is merely a question of which textual emphasis one prefers. Rava, however, argues that a practical difference does exist: *pruta she-huzla ika beinayhu* — the case of an object that was worth a *pruta* at the time of loss but has since depreciated below a *pruta*. According to the view that derives the threshold from *asher tovad*, what matters is its value when it was lost, and since it was worth a *pruta* then, there is an obligation to return it. According to the view that derives it from *u-metzata*, what matters is its value when found — and since it is now worth less, there would be no obligation.

### Both Words Required

The Gemara, however, is not satisfied with this formulation. Even the view that derives the rule primarily from *asher tovad* cannot ignore the word *u-metzata*, which is equally present in the verse. Surely, the Gemara presses, one would need to satisfy both endpoints — and in the depreciation case, the finding-side requirement is not met. Similarly, the reverse case — an object worth less than a *pruta* at loss but more at finding — would fail on the losing-side for the view that derives from *u-metzata*, yet that view cannot simply disregard *asher tovad* either. The Gemara concludes that neither of these simple cases can serve as the *nafka mina*.

### Fluctuating Value

The Gemara therefore proposes a more complex scenario: *pruta she-hokra ve-huzla ve-hazara ve-hokra* — an object that was worth a *pruta* at the time of loss, then dropped below a *pruta* at some intermediate point, and then rose back above a *pruta* by the time it was found. Both endpoints are satisfied. The debate, then, is whether the intermediate dip matters.

The view deriving from *asher tovad* holds that the emphasis is on the moment of loss; the secondary requirement of *u-metzata* is satisfied by the final value, and the transient depreciation in between is irrelevant. The view deriving from *u-metzata*, by contrast, takes a broader reading of "finding": it requires *de'it bei shiur metzia mi-sha'as aveidah v'ad sha'as metzia* — that the object maintain a minimum worth throughout the entire period from loss to finding. The precise logic underlying each position remains somewhat difficult to pin down, but this is at least how Rava frames the possible *nafka mina* between the two readings.

## Simanim Source

### Simanim: Torah or Rabbis?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי גֵּט אִשָּׁה בְּסִימָנִים. אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מַהְדְּרִינַן, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן – כִּי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא בְּמָמוֹנָא, אֲבָל בְּאִיסּוּרָא – לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The practical difference is **with regard to returning the bill of divorce of a woman** that was lost by an agent before its delivery, **on the basis of distinguishing marks. If you say** that the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is **by Torah law, we return** the document and allow the agent to transmit it to the woman. **But if you say** that it is **by rabbinic law,** we do not return the document, because **when the Sages institute an ordinance,** it is only **with regard to monetary matters** they have the authority to declare property ownerless; **but with regard to ritual matters, the Sages do not institute an ordinance.** They lack the authority to abrogate the prohibitions by Torah law that are associated with a woman's marital status.

At the very end of 27a, the Gemara raises a fundamental question: *simanim d'Oraisa o d'Rabbanan?* Is the principle that one may return a lost object — or adjudicate ownership — on the basis of distinguishing marks (*simanim*) a Biblical rule, or only Rabbinic?

At the outset it is worth clarifying the taxonomy of *simanim*. There are effectively three categories. First, a description so generic that it does not even qualify as a *siman* at all — for example, identifying an object merely as "black" or "large," something that describes a vast proportion of similar items. Second, a standard *siman*: a mark that narrows the field significantly and renders ownership substantially more probable — something like identifying a garment by its color and style. Third, a *siman muvhak*: a mark so specific and unusual that virtually only the true owner could know it, such as a distinctive tear in a particular location. The universally agreed rule is that a *siman muvhak* has the force of Torah law. The debate about *simanim d'Oraisa o d'Rabbanan* concerns the middle category — the ordinary, reasonable *siman*.

### Simanim Scope

The practical *nafka mina* is stated at the top of 27b: *l'hadurei get isha b'simanim* — whether one may return a *get* to a woman based on her ability to provide *simanim*. In monetary matters, the Rabbis have broad latitude to assign ownership: *hefker beis din hefker*, and in general *Chazal* may regulate property rights. But matters of personal status and *issur* are different. If *simanim* are *d'Oraisa*, they carry evidentiary weight recognized by Torah law and may therefore permit a woman to be classified as divorced. If they are only *d'Rabbanan*, *Chazal* would not have had the authority to apply their enactment to an *issur* question — they could not permit a woman to remarry on the basis of a mechanism that the Torah itself does not recognize as valid evidence.

### Simla & Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף הַשִּׂמְלָה הָיְתָה בִּכְלַל כׇּל אֵלּוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצָאת לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ? וְלוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה שִׂמְלָה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לָהּ תּוֹבְעִין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לוֹ תּוֹבְעִין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. תַּנָּא: תּוֹבְעִין אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סִימָנִין כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the mishna: **The garment was also** included **in the generalization** that one must return **all of these** items. **And why did it emerge** from the generalization that is should be specified? **To draw an analogy to it and to say to you: What** is notable **about a garment?** It is notable **in that there are distinguishing marks concerning it and it has claimants** asserting ownership, and its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **So too** with regard to **any item concerning which there are distinguishing marks and it has claimants** asserting ownership, its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. Clearly, the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law. The Gemara rejects the proof: Perhaps it **was necessary for the *tanna*** to mention only the criterion of **claimants,** and the *tanna* **cited** the criterion **of distinguishing marks for no reason,** as by Torah law distinguishing marks are not a relevant factor.

The Gemara turns to proof. It quotes the Mishna: *af ha-simla haysa bi-khlal kol eileh* — the garment was already included in the general category of "any lost object." Why then was *simla* singled out by the verse? *L'hakish eleha* — to draw an analogy: just as a garment characteristically has *simanim* and has claimants still demanding it, so too any object with *simanim* and active claimants must be proclaimed and returned. This appears to be a straightforward derivation of *simanim* from a *pasuk*, indicating that *simanim* are *d'Oraisa*.

### Asmachta Theory

The Gemara deflects this proof: *tana tov'in itztericha lei, simanim kedi nasvah* — the real teaching of the *pasuk* was the requirement that claimants still be pressing their claim (i.e., that there has been no *ye'ush*). The mention of *simanim* is incidental — an *asmachta*, a Rabbinic principle attached to the verse rather than directly derived from it. What the Torah actually demands, on this reading, is *edim* — witnesses who can identify the object as belonging to a specific person. The *simanim* principle is a Rabbinic layer built on top of that underlying requirement.

## Simanim Testing

### Donkey Saddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמוֹר בְּסִימָנֵי אוּכָּף. אֵימָא ״בְּעֵדֵי אוּכָּף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the aforementioned statement: The obligation to return **a donkey** to its owner **on the basis of** the **distinguishing marks of the saddle** is derived based on the mention of the word "donkey" in the verse from Deuteronomy. Clearly, the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law. The Gemara rejects this proof: Emend the *baraita* and **say:** There is an obligation to return the donkey only **on the basis of witnesses** who testify with regard to the identity of the owner based on the fact that **the saddle** belongs to him, and not on the basis of distinguishing marks.

The Gemara continues to probe. A *braisa* cited the previous day had taught that one returns a donkey by providing *simanei ukaf* — identifying marks on the saddle. This too appears to derive from the Torah's mention of *chamor*, suggesting that *simanim* are *d'Oraisa*. Again the Gemara deflects: *eima be'adei ukaf* — perhaps the *braisa* should be understood as requiring witnesses to the saddle, not mere *simanim*.

### Inquiry Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְהָיָה עִמְּךָ עַד דְּרֹשׁ אָחִיךָ אֹתוֹ״. וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁיִּתְּנֶנּוּ לוֹ קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּדְרְשֶׁנּוּ?! אֶלָּא דׇּרְשֵׁהוּ אִם רַמַּאי הוּא אוֹ אֵינוֹ רַמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a mishna (28b): "And if your brother be not near you, and you know him not, then you shall bring it into your house, **and it shall be with you until your brother claims [*derosh*] it,** and you shall return it to him" (Deuteronomy 22:2). **Would it enter your mind that he would give** the lost item **to him before he claims it?** How can the finder return it if he does not know the identity of the owner? **Rather,** the verb *derosh* is not referring to the claim of the owner; it is referring to the scrutiny performed by the finder. **Scrutinize him [*darshehu*]** to determine **whether** the claimant **is a swindler** or **whether he is not a swindler.** Only then may you return the lost item to him.

Another *braisa* expounds the verse *vehaya imcha ad derosh achicha oso* — "it shall remain with you until your brother seeks it out." The *braisa* asks: would you really give it back before he seeks it? Of course not. So what is the verse teaching? *Darshehu im ramai hu o eino ramai* — you must investigate whether the claimant is telling the truth. The Gemara asks whether this does not indicate that the investigation proceeds by *simanim*. But the answer is equally available: *lo, be'edim* — no, it means he must bring witnesses. The *braisa* never mentions *simanim* at all, and the proof remains weak.

### Husband Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְעִידִין אֶלָּא עַל פַּרְצוּף הַפָּנִים עִם הַחוֹטֶם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ סִימָנִין בְּגוּפוֹ וּבְכֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a mishna (*Yevamot* 120a): **One testifies** that a man died, thereby permitting his wife to remarry, **only** if he can testify **about** seeing **the countenance [*partzuf* ] of the face with the nose,** as this allows one to identify the individual with certainty. **Although there are distinguishing marks on his body and on his garments,** which appear to indicate his identity, they cannot be used to identify the person.

The Gemara then pivots from *aveidah* to the personal-status angle directly. A *braisa* teaches: *ein me'idin ela al partzuf panim im ha-chotem* — one may not testify that a woman's husband has died unless the witnesses actually saw the face together with the nose, meaning they had genuine direct recognition of the deceased. Crucially, this is so *af al pi sheyesh simanim begufo u-vicheilav* — even if there are identifying marks on his body and his clothes. On its face, this *braisa* seems to flatly rule that *simanim* do not suffice for a *d'Oraisa* personal-status ruling.

### Face Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אָמְרִי: גּוּפוֹ – דְּאָרוֹךְ וְגוּץ. כֵּלָיו – דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its suggested proof: **Conclude from it** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is not by Torah law.** The Sages **say** in rejecting that proof: The distinguishing marks on **his body** mentioned in the mishna are non-specific distinguishing marks, e.g., **that** he was **tall or short,** and that is the reason that the distinguishing marks are ineffective in determining his identity. The distinguishing marks on **his** garments mentioned in the mishna are ineffective in determining his identity, **as we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan,** e.g., perhaps the husband loaned his clothes to the deceased.

The Gemara, however, resists this conclusion. It offers the rejoinder: *amri gufo de'aroch ve-gutz* — the "body marks" the *braisa* dismisses are merely generic descriptions like height and build. These are not really *simanim* in any meaningful sense, as they describe a great many people. So the *braisa* is not categorically excluding *simanim*; it is excluding the weakest, most generic descriptors. This is admittedly a strained reading of the *braisa*, which frames the issue as face recognition versus any bodily marks, but the Gemara works with it.

### Weak Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה, חֲמוֹר בְּסִימָנֵי אוּכָּף הֵיכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן? אָמְרִי: אוּכָּף, לָא שָׁאיְלִי אִינָשֵׁי אוּכָּפָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַקֵּב לֵיהּ לַחֲמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan, how do we return a donkey** to its owner **on the basis of** the **distinguishing marks of the saddle;** perhaps it was borrowed? The Sages **say** in response: **People do not** typically **borrow a saddle because** saddles that are not custom fit **wound the donkey.**

A further issue presents itself: even regarding the clothes, the Gemara says, one cannot rely on identifying them — *d'chaishinan l'she'ela* — because of concern that the items may have been lent to another person. Even if one could say "I recognize those clothes as Reuven's," the worry that Reuven may have lent them introduces doubt. And so the *braisa*'s dismissal of *simanei keilav* could be explained not as a categorical rejection of *simanim* but as reflecting the weak nature of those particular marks: *bichivrei u-vesumkei* — they amounted to nothing more than "he wore white clothes" or "he wore red clothes," which are entirely generic.

## Borrowed Items

### Donkey Lending

The section on donkey saddles introduces a new thread. If the reason that clothing-based *simanim* fail in the testimony context is the concern about lending (*she'ela*), then one must ask: does the same concern not apply to the donkey saddle case? One would be returning a donkey to whoever can identify the saddle — but what if the saddle was lent to a different donkey's owner?

### Non-Lent Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּכָּף לָא שָׁאיְלִי אִינָשֵׁי אוּכָּפָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַקֵּב לֵיהּ לַחֲמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **People do not** typically **borrow a saddle because** saddles that are not custom fit **wound the donkey.**

The Gemara responds: *ukaf lo sha'ali inshi* — people do not lend out their saddles. The reason is *mishum demiskav* — a saddle is worn in to fit a particular animal, and using it on a different animal would cause irritation and discomfort. It is therefore not the sort of item that gets lent out, and *simanim* on the saddle are reliable. This is the principle: the concern about lending applies to items that are commonly lent, but not to items that by their nature are not passed around. A saddle fits that latter category.

The Gemara then proceeds to examine a *braisa* concerning the *get*: if a *shaliach* who was carrying a *get* lost it and then found it *kashur be-kiso, be'arnaki, u-vetaba'at* — tied up together with his pouch, his wallet, or his ring — or found it among his personal belongings, then even after a prolonged period, the *get* is *kasher*. He can identify it by recognizing the objects it was associated with. This too involves using *simanim* in a personal-status context. Does this not prove *simanim d'Oraisa*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כֵּלָיו בְּחִיוָּרֵי וּבְסוּמְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: The distinguishing marks on **his garments** mentioned in the mishna are non-specific distinguishing marks, e.g., **where** the witness said that they were **white or red,** and that is the reason that the distinguishing marks are ineffective in determining his identity.

The Gemara challenges: but if we are worried about lending, why would recognition of the wallet or ring be reliable? The *shaliach* may have lent his wallet to someone, and that person's *get* ended up bundled with it. The Gemara's response identifies three items that are never lent: *kis ve'arnaki* — a pouch and a wallet — because of a concern that lending them away is a bad omen (*menachesh kiso*), a kind of squandering of one's fortune; and *taba'at* — a signet ring — because it could be used to forge one's documents. Since these items are simply not lent out, finding them bundled together with the *get* is meaningful evidence of its identity.

### Get & Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַיָא: מְצָאוֹ קָשׁוּר בְּכִיס אוֹ בְּאַרְנָקִי וּבְטַבַּעַת, אוֹ שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ בֵּין כֵּלָיו, אֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה – כָּשֵׁר. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה, כִּי מְצָאוֹ קָשׁוּר בְּכִיס אַמַּאי כָּשֵׁר? נֵיחוּשׁ לִשְׁאֵלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the previous answer with regard to the concern about the possibility of a loan. **But** there is **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the agent **found** the bill of divorce that he lost **bound to** his **pouch, or** his **purse, or** his signet **ring, or if he found it among his garments, even** if he found it **a long time** after he lost it, the distinguishing marks on those items are sufficient in order to identify the bill of divorce as the one that he lost, and it is **valid. And if it enters your mind** that **we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan, when he found** the bill of divorce **bound to** his **pouch, why is it valid? Let us be concerned about** the possibility of **a loan** and that perhaps the pouch and the bill of divorce belong to someone else.

Having established that these particular items carry reliable identifying weight because they are not lent, the Gemara uses this to explain the *braisa* about the *get* without necessarily proving anything about the general status of *simanim*. The recognition of the wallet or ring may be a case of near-direct identification rather than mere *simanim*, since these items are uniquely associated with their owner and never circulate to others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כִּיס וְאַרְנָקִי וְטַבַּעַת לָא מַשְׁאֲלִי אִינָשֵׁי. כִּיס וְאַרְנָקִי – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַמְּנִי, וְטַבַּעַת – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְזַיֵּיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: There is no concern in this case, as **people do not loan a pouch, a purse, or** a signet **ring** to another person. One does not loan his **pouch and** his **purse** to others **due to** the fact **that it portends** the loss of his good fortune. **And** one does not loan his signet **ring** to others **due to** the fact **that** it could be used to **forge** documents.

### Resolution

The Gemara steps back and acknowledges that the proofs attempted so far — both from *aveidah* texts and from personal-status texts — have not conclusively established whether *simanim* are *d'Oraisa* or *d'Rabbanan*. The *pesukim* appeared to derive *simanim* directly, but each time the Gemara could reinterpret the derivation as concerning *edim* or as an *asmachta*. The *braisa* about testimony for a deceased husband appeared to rule against *simanim*, but the Gemara could explain that as addressing only generic, low-quality marks or items that raise lending concerns. The question remains formally open.

## Bodily Marks

### Body Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְתַנָּאֵי: אֵין מְעִידִין עַל הַשּׁוּמָא, וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַהֲבַאי אוֹמֵר: מְעִידִין עַל הַשּׁוּמָא. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי? דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: סִימָנִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַהֲבַאי סָבַר: סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the dilemma whether the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law or by rabbinic law is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **One does not testify on the basis of a mole** on the body of the deceased to determine the identity of a man who died and permit his wife to remarry. **And Elazar ben Mahavai says: One testifies** to identify the corpse **on the basis of a mole. What, is it not with regard to this** matter **that they disagree; as the first *tanna* holds** that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by rabbinic law** and therefore, testimony concerning those marks cannot dissolve a marriage by Torah law; **and Elazar ben Mahavai holds** that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law.**

A *braisa* is now cited: *ein me'idin al ha-shuma* — one may not testify to identify a deceased person based on a bodily swelling or discoloration of the limbs. Elazar ben Mahabai disagrees: *me'idin al ha-shuma* — such a mark may serve as valid identification. Since this dispute concerns personal status — whether a woman's husband is confirmed as dead — it directly engages the question of what counts as sufficient evidence at a *d'Oraisa* level.

### Multiple Readings

The Gemara asks: should we not read this *tannaitic* dispute as being precisely about whether *simanim* are *d'Oraisa* or *d'Rabbanan* — the first *tanna* holding *d'Rabbanan* (and therefore it does not work for personal status) and Elazar ben Mahabai holding *d'Oraisa* (and therefore it does work)? Rava immediately resists this reading and offers alternative explanations, each of which keeps the underlying question of *simanim d'Oraisa* from being definitively resolved by this text.

### Feature Frequency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְהָכָא בְּשׁוּמָא מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ, וּמָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** That is not necessarily the crux of their dispute, as perhaps **everyone** agrees that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law, and here,** it is **with regard to** whether one needs to be concerned that **a mole is** often **found on one's contemporary,** i.e., one born under the same constellation, rendering it useless as a means of identification, that **they disagree. One Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **a mole is** often **found on one's contemporary** and therefore it is insufficient as a means of identification; **and one Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds that a mole is not** often **found on one's contemporary,** and therefore it is sufficient as a means of identification.

The first alternative: *d'kulei alma simanim d'Oraisa*, and the dispute here is about a different question entirely: *be-shuma metzuya b'vnei gilo kamiflagei* — whether this type of bodily characteristic is common among people of that person's demographic cohort. If the mark is prevalent among peers, it is not even a meaningful *siman*; it would be as generic as saying the man was of average height. One *tanna* holds the mark is common enough to disqualify it; Elazar ben Mahabai holds it is rare enough to qualify as a valid middle-level *siman*. Both could agree that *simanim d'Oraisa*.

### Post-Death Changes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ, וְהָכָא בְּסִימָנִין הָעֲשׂוּיִן לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: סִימָנִין עֲשׂוּיִם לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה. וּמָר סָבַר: סִימָנִין אֵין עֲשׂוּיִם לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that **everyone** agrees that **a mole is not** often **found on one's contemporary, and here** it is **with regard to** whether the appearance of **distinguishing marks** on the body **is apt to change after death** that **they disagree. One Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the appearance of **distinguishing marks is apt to change after death,** and that consequently they are insufficient as a means of identification; **and one Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds** that the appearance of **distinguishing marks is not apt to change after death,** and therefore, they are sufficient as a means of identification.

The second alternative: *d'kulei alma shuma eina b'vnei gilo* — everyone agrees the mark is uncommon enough to be meaningful. The dispute is instead about *simanim asuyin le-hishtanot le-achar mita* — whether bodily marks of this kind tend to change after death. If they do change, then even if the mark was distinctive during the man's life, it is no longer a reliable identifier once he has died. The *Chachamim* hold that such marks are prone to post-mortem change and therefore cannot be relied upon; Elazar ben Mahabai holds they do not change and remain valid. Both could again agree on the general question of *simanim d'Oraisa*.

### Exceptional Marks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְסִימָנִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְהָכָא בְּשׁוּמָא סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא לָאו סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that **everyone** agrees that **a mole is not apt to change after death, and** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is by rabbinic law, and here** it is **with regard to** whether **a mole is a clear-cut distinguishing mark** that **they disagree. One Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds** that **a mole is a clear-cut distinguishing mark** that can be relied upon without hesitation even in matters of Torah law, e.g., dissolving a marriage; **and one Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds that a mole is not a clear-cut distinguishing mark.** Since standard distinguishing marks are sufficient by rabbinic law, a marriage, which is in effect by Torah law, cannot be dissolved on the basis of a mole.

The third alternative: *d'kulei alma shuma eina asuya le-hishtanot le-achar mita* — everyone agrees there is no concern about post-mortem change — and yet everyone also agrees that ordinary *simanim* are only *d'Rabbanan*. The dispute then would be: *be-shuma siman muvhak kamiflagei* — is this particular bodily characteristic exceptional enough to be classified as a *siman muvhak*? A *siman muvhak*, by universal agreement, carries *d'Oraisa* weight. Elazar ben Mahabai holds that the *shuma* rises to the level of *siman muvhak* and therefore works for personal-status testimony; the *Chachamim* hold it is merely an average *siman*, which, if *d'Rabbanan*, would not suffice. All three readings of the dispute leave the underlying question — whether standard *simanim* are *d'Oraisa* or *d'Rabbanan* — unresolved. The debate among the *tannaim* can be fully explained on any of the possible underlying positions.

## Loser's Interest

### Finder's Incentive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הֵיכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן אֲבֵידְתָּא בְּסִימָנִין? דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְמוֹצֵא אֲבֵידָה דְּנַהְדַּר בְּסִימָנִין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּכִי אָבְדָה לֵיהּ לְדִידֵהּ נָמֵי נַהְדְּרוּ לֵיהּ בְּסִימָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: If you say** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is not by Torah law, how do we return a lost item** to the presumed owner **on the basis of distinguishing marks;** perhaps it will result in the return of property to one who was in fact not the owner? Rava answers: We return the lost item, **as** it is **satisfactory to the finder of a lost item to return** it **on the basis of distinguishing marks,** rather than exercise his right by Torah law to retain it, **so that when** an item **is lost from him** in the future, the finder **will return it to him on the basis of distinguishing marks as well.**

Rava now poses a broader question: *im timtzi lomar simanim lav d'Oraisa* — if we were to conclude that *simanim* are only Rabbinic, we must explain what motivated *Chazal* to institute the principle at all for *aveidah*. Why would they depart from the Torah's standard of requiring *edim* and allow *simanim* to suffice for returning a lost object? His initial answer is framed from the perspective of the finder: *d'nicha lei l'motzi aveida d'nahadra b'simanim* — it is in the interest of someone who finds a lost object that the system operates on *simanim*, because that same person may someday lose an object himself and want it returned to him via *simanim*.

### Owner's Perspective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְרָבָא: וְכִי אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה טוֹבָה לְעַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said to Rava: But can a person perform** an act that results in **benefit for himself with property that is not his?** The lost item belongs not to the finder but to the one who lost it. How can the finder waive the right of the true owner to the lost item so that he may recover his own lost item in the future?

Rav Safra challenges this immediately: *ve-chi adam oseh tovah le-atzmo b'mamon she-eino shelo?* How can the finder justify delivering property to Reuven — whose claim is supported only by *simanim* — on the grounds that it benefits his own future interests? The finder has no right to use someone else's property as the currency for securing his own future advantage. Perhaps the item should in fact go to Shimon, who might produce *edim*, and the finder cannot sacrifice that outcome for the sake of a general social policy that happens to benefit himself.

In light of this challenge, Rav Papa reformulates the argument entirely: *nicha lei l'ba'al aveida le-meiyav simanim* — the justification must be grounded in the perspective of the owner who lost the object, not the finder. It is the owner who is the potential loser if the *simanim* principle is misapplied. But remarkably, it is also the owner whose interests are *best served* by the institution of *simanim*.

### Owner's Advantage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְבַעַל אֲבֵידָה לְמֵיהַב סִימָנִין וּלְמִשְׁקְלֵיהּ. מִידָּע יָדַע דְּעֵדִים לֵית לֵיהּ, וּמֵימָר אָמַר: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא יָדְעִי סִימָנִין מוּבְהָקִים דִּידַהּ, וַאֲנָא יָהֵיבְנָא סִימָנִין מוּבְהָקִים דִּידַהּ וְשָׁקֵלְנָא לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we return the lost item, as it is **satisfactory to the owner of the lost item to** be able to **provide** a description using **distinguishing marks and** on that basis **take** possession of the item. **He knows that he has no witnesses** to testify to his ownership, **and he says: No one** else **knows** the **clear-cut distinguishing marks** that are on the item. **And I will provide** a description using the **clear-cut distinguishing marks, and** based on that information **I will take** possession of the item. Each owner gives his tacit agreement to the return of lost items on the basis of distinguishing marks, based on the belief that he is best able to identify them.

The reasoning is as follows. If the Torah requires *edim* to reclaim a lost object, the owner will very often be unable to retrieve it — people rarely have witnesses standing by who can identify their possessions. If, however, the system runs on *simanim*, the owner is actually in a strong position: *kulei alma lo yadi simanim muvhakim didi* — not everyone knows the fine details of my object the way I do. The true owner will be able to give more precise and accurate *simanim* than any impostor. So even though the *simanim* principle theoretically opens the door to a fraudulent claimant walking off with the object, in practice the owner is better positioned than anyone else to give the most specific and convincing marks. The real owner will prevail in the overwhelming majority of cases. Accordingly, even from the owner's perspective, a world with *simanim* is preferable to one requiring *edim*.

The phrase *simanim muvhakim* here is used somewhat loosely — even if *simanim d'Rabbanan*, the point is that the real owner will know the object better and be able to give more detailed marks than a stranger's guesswork. The principle is not about *siman muvhak* in the technical sense, but about the owner's informational advantage.

### Promissory Notes

This reasoning, however, runs into a serious difficulty. If the *takana* rests on the premise that it is in the owner's interest to have *simanim* available as a retrieval mechanism, there is one category of document where this logic fails entirely: *shtar chov* — a promissory note. The Mishna teaches (*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer*): if one finds three documents and all three name the same person as the borrower (*echad ha-loveh mi-shlosha yachzir la-loveh*) — Reuven borrowed from Shimon, Reuven from Levi, Reuven from Yehuda — the common denominator is the borrower, and one assumes the documents fell from Reuven, who had repaid his debts and retrieved them. Conversely, if the three documents show three different borrowers from a single lender (*shlosha she-lavu min ha-echad yachzir la-malveh*), the common denominator is the lender, and one returns the documents to him.

The difficulty is sharpest regarding the second case, where one returns the documents to the *malveh* on the basis of *simanim*. A *shtar chov* in the hands of the *malveh* is a weapon against the *loveh* — it can be used to collect the debt. It is therefore *never* in the *loveh*'s interest for a *shtar chov* to be easily returned to the *malveh*. The *loveh* would want the barriers to return to be as high as possible. So if the *takana* of *simanim* rests on the principle that all relevant parties benefit from the institution, this is a case where half the parties — the *lovim* — emphatically do not benefit. The justification collapses.

## Bundled Documents

### Lender Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד הַלֹּוֶה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה – יַחְזִיר לַלֹּוֶה, שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁלָּווּ מִן הָאֶחָד – יַחְזִיר לַמַּלְוֶה. נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** there is **that which we learned** in that mishna (20a): **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If one found three promissory notes relating to the loan of **one** debtor **who borrowed** money **from three** creditors, **he must return** the documents **to the debtor.** If one found three promissory notes relating to the loans of **three** debtors **who borrowed** money **from one** creditor, **he must return** the documents **to the creditor.** If one returns lost items on the basis of distinguishing marks due to the tacit agreement of the owners, is it **satisfactory to the debtor to** have the documents **returned to the creditor,** as doing so would enable the creditor to collect payment of the loan?

The case where three people borrowed from one lender is somewhat easier to explain in terms of *sevara* — pure logic. If one finds documents stating that Reuven lent to Shimon, Reuven lent to Levi, and Reuven lent to Yehuda, the near-certain inference is that these all fell from Reuven the lender, since documents recording one person's loans would naturally be in that person's possession. This is closer to a *siman muvhak* than to an ordinary *siman* — the force of the logic is so compelling that no special *takana* is needed.

### Borrower Denominator

Similarly, when all three documents name the same borrower, the inference that they fell from that borrower is compelling: a single person having borrowed from three different lenders and having retrieved the paid-up documents is the obvious explanation for the cluster. The Gemara frames both cases as *sevara* — the logical evidence is compelling regardless of the *loveh*'s preferences.

### Tied Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: מָצָא תַּכְרִיךְ שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת אוֹ אֲגוּדָּה שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** there is **that which we learned** in that mishna (20a): If one **found a roll of documents or a bundle of documents, he shall return** the documents to whomever describes the roll and the bundle, which serve as distinguishing marks. Would one say that **so too,** if one returns lost items on the basis of distinguishing marks due to the tacit agreement of the owners, it is **satisfactory to the debtor to** have the documents **returned to the creditor?**

But the Gemara then identifies a case that cannot be dismissed so easily. The Mishna teaches: *matza tachrich shel shtarot o aguda shel shtarot, harei ze yachzir* — if one finds a bundle of documents rolled or tied together, one returns them. In what circumstances? Clearly, it is returned to whomever can provide *simanim* identifying the bundle — perhaps by describing the string that tied them, the number of documents, or the like. This is a straightforward case of returning documents based on *simanim*. The person who identifies the bundle might well be the *malveh*, and the *loveh* would have no interest in making that easy. The question *nicha leh la-loveh le-ahadurei lei la-malveh?* is now pointed: on what basis does *Chazal* extend the *simanim* principle to this case?

### Rava's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה עִמְּךָ עַד דְּרֹשׁ אָחִיךָ אֹתוֹ״, וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁיִּתְּנֶנּוּ קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּדְרְשֶׁנּוּ?! אֶלָּא דׇּרְשֵׁהוּ אִם רַמַּאי הוּא אוֹ אֵינוֹ רַמַּאי. לָאו בְּסִימָנִין? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** Identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law, as it is written:** "And if your brother be not near you, and you know him not, then you shall bring it into your house, **and it shall be with you until your brother claims [*derosh*] it,** and you shall return it to him" (Deuteronomy 22:2). **Would it enter your mind that he would give** the lost item **to him before he claims it?** How can the finder return it if he does not know the identity of the owner? **Rather,** the verb *derosh* is not referring to the claim of the owner; it is referring to the scrutiny performed by the finder. **Scrutinize him [*darshehu*]** to determine **whether** the claimant **is a swindler** or **whether he is not a swindler.** Only then may you return the lost item to him. **What, is it not** that the one who claims the lost item proves that he is not a swindler **on the basis of distinguishing marks** that he provides? Rava affirms: **Conclude from it** that identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law.

The Gemara at this point quotes Rava's conclusion: *ella amar Rava simanim d'Oraisa* — Rava declares that he is persuaded. The fact that *Chazal* return a *shtar chov* on the basis of *simanim*, even in a case where the *loveh* would have no interest in it, proves that this is not a Rabbinic *takana* grounded in general social benefit. *Chazal* would not enact a *takana* that operates against the interests of one of the relevant parties. Since the return of a bundled *shtar chov* by *simanim* does operate against the *loveh*'s interests, it must be that *simanim* are *d'Oraisa* — and Torah law does not require justification in terms of whose interests are served.

Rava grounds this in the *pasuk*: *de-ksiv vehaya imcha derosh achicha oso* — "it shall remain with you until your brother seeks it out." As the *braisa* explains, this means you must investigate whether the claimant is truthful. Rava reads this as referring to investigation via *simanim*. The Gemara then interrupts: a moment ago Rava used *im timtzi lomar* — the conditional formulation suggesting he was not certain — yet he now presents *simanim d'Oraisa* as settled? The Gemara resolves this: the *pasuk* alone is not conclusive proof, since it might refer to *edim* rather than *simanim*. So Rava acknowledges that strict proof from the verse is not airtight — *mishum de-ika le-meimar kedei she-nisan simanim kedei she-nisan* — but he proceeds on the working assumption that *simanim d'Oraisa*, and explores its consequences.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. ״אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר״?! הָא פְּשִׁיט לֵיהּ סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר כִּדְשַׁנִּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** begins his statement and **says: If you say** that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law.** The Gemara interjects: **If you say? Didn't he** already **resolve** the dilemma and conclude that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law?** The Gemara answers: Rava phrased his statement conditionally **due to** the fact **that** although he holds that identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law, **one could** reject his conclusion and **say as we explained** previously (27b), that when the mishna states that the finder scrutinizes whether he is a swindler, he does so on the basis of witnesses and not on the basis of distinguishing marks.

## Evidence Hierarchy

### Equal Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָנִין וְסִימָנִין – יַנִּיחַ. סִימָנִין וְעֵדִים – יִנָּתֵן לְבַעַל הָעֵדִים. סִימָנִין וְסִימָנִין וְעֵד אֶחָד – עֵד אֶחָד כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וְיַנִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes Rava's interrupted statement: If you say that identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law, then in a case where an item is found and two people claim it as theirs, and one describes **distinguishing marks** on the item **and** the other describes **distinguishing marks** on the item, the finder **shall leave** it in his possession and not give it to either claimant. In a case where one person describes **distinguishing marks** on the item **and** the other brings two **witnesses** to support his claim of ownership, the item **shall be given to** the claimant **with witnesses.** In a case where one person describes **distinguishing marks** on the item **and** the other describes **distinguishing marks** on the item **and** brings **one witness** to support his claim of ownership, the **one witness is as one who is not** there, **and** the finder **shall leave** it in his possession. The testimony of a single witness has no legal standing in this case.

Even granting that *simanim* are *d'Oraisa*, there remains a hierarchy of evidence when competing claims are made. The first case: *simanim ve-simanim yaniach* — if Reuven provides *simanim* and Shimon provides *simanim* of equivalent weight, they neutralize one another and the object is held until the matter can be clarified further.

### Simanim vs. Witnesses

Even if *simanim* carry *d'Oraisa* force, *edim* are superior: *simanim ve-edim yinasen le-ba'al ha-edim* — you give the object to the party who has witnesses. Direct testimony from people who recognize the object beats out any description of distinguishing marks.

### One Witness

The next variation: *simanim ve-simanim ve-ed echad* — suppose both Reuven and Shimon give equivalent *simanim*, but Shimon also has a single witness who attests the object is his. One might think the single witness tips the balance. The Gemara rules: *ed echad ke-man de-leisa dami* — a single witness has no probative force in this type of dispute, and the result is the same as pure *simanim* versus *simanim*: *veyaniach*, the object is held.

### Weaving vs. Dropping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵדֵי אֲרִיגָה וְעֵדֵי נְפִילָה – תִּנָּתֵן לְעֵדֵי נְפִילָה, דְּאָמְרִינַן זַבּוֹנֵי זַבְּנַהּ וּמֵאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא נְפַל.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where one claimant to a found garment brings **witnesses** who testify that the garment was **woven** for him, **and** the other claimant brings **witnesses** who testify that the garment had **fallen** from him, the garment **shall be given to** the claimant whose **witnesses** testified that the garment had **fallen** from him, **as we say** that perhaps the one for whom it was woven **sold** the garment **and** it **fell from another person,** who is the current owner.

Suppose Reuven has witnesses that he wove the garment and Shimon has witnesses that Shimon dropped it: *tei-nasen l'edei nefila* — give it to Shimon, the one from whom it fell. The logic is: *zivuni zavneh u-minei d'achrina nefal* — Reuven may have woven it but then sold it on to someone else. The more recent chain of ownership defeats the earlier one. Witnesses to the most recent act of ownership or possession win.

### Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ – תִּנָּתֵן לְמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ, דְּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ שַׁעוֹרֵי קָא מְשַׁעַר לַהּ כַּד מִכַּסֵּי לַהּ מָרַהּ וְקָאֵי, וּמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ לָא מִשְׁתַּעַר לֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one claimant provides **the measure of its length of** a lost garment **and** the other provides **the measure of its width,** the garment **shall be given to** the claimant who provided the **measure of its length, as one can approximate the measure of its width when its owner dons** the garment **and stands, but the measure of its length cannot be approximated** in that manner. Therefore, it is a more clear-cut distinguishing characteristic.

*Midas arko u-midas rachvo*: if one person testifies to the length of a garment and the other to the width, *tei-nasen l'midas arko* — give it to the one who gave the length. The reason: *d'midas rachvo shiurei — kama shayer lah kad mikasei lah mareh* — the width of a garment can be estimated by someone who has seen it being worn. Looking at a person in a cloak, one can approximate their width. But the length is harder to estimate from observation without actually holding or examining the garment. The one who can give the harder-to-estimate dimension is more likely the true owner.

### Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ וּמִדַּת גַּמָּיו – יִנָּתֵן לְמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וְרׇחְבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If one claimant provides **the measure of its length and the measure of its width and** the other provides **the measure of its** gamma, its combined length and width, which together form the Greek letter **gamma,** but does not provide each measure individually, the item **shall be given to** the claimant who provided **the measure of its length and** the measure of **its width** separately.

*Midas arko u-midas rachvo u-midas gamav*: if one person gives the length and width separately and another gives a combined measurement (like a total perimeter or combined dimensions), *tei-nasen l'midas arko ve-rachvo* — the person who breaks down the measurement precisely wins. Greater precision is more indicative of genuine ownership.

### Weight Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וּמִדַּת רׇחְבּוֹ וּמִדַּת מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו – יִנָּתֵן לְמִדַּת מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If one claimant provides **the measure of its length and the measure of its width and** the other provides **the measure of its weight,** the item **shall be given to** the claimant who provided **the measure of its weight,** which, because it is more difficult to approximate, is a more clear-cut distinguishing characteristic.

*Midas arko u-midas rachvo u-midas mishkelotav*: one person gives length, one width, and one gives the weight of the garment. *Tei-nasen l'midas mishkelotav* — the testimony of weight prevails. Weight is the dimension least amenable to guessing from casual observation; one must have genuinely handled and weighed the object to know it. This is therefore the strongest indicator of true ownership, and the person who gives the weight beats out those who give only the physical dimensions.

## Get Simanim

### Recipient Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא אוֹמֵר סִימָנֵי הַגֵּט וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת סִימָנֵי הַגֵּט – יִנָּתֵן לָהּ. בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ וְרׇחְבּוֹ, דִּלְמָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ חֲזַיְתֵיהּ. אֶלָּא – נֶקֶב יֵשׁ בּוֹ בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: In a case where a bill of divorce is found and it is unclear whether it had been delivered to the wife, and the husband, who reconsidered, **states** the **distinguishing marks of the bill of divorce** and claims that he did not yet give it to his wife, **and** the wife, who wants to be divorced, **states** the **distinguishing marks of the bill of divorce** and claims that she already received it, the document **shall be given to** the wife. The Gemara asks: **With what** distinguishing mark did she describe the bill of divorce? **If we say** that she described it **with the measure of its height and its width,** that is not a clear-cut distinguishing mark; **perhaps while** her husband **was holding** the bill of divorce, **she saw it,** although he had not yet given it to her. **Rather,** it must be that she says that **there is a perforation alongside such and such letter** in the document, which she could know only if the bill of divorce had been in her hand.

The Gemara turns to the application of the *simanim* hierarchy to the *get* case, where the underlying principle has a particular logic of its own. Since the husband is the original owner of the *get* and the wife only gains knowledge of it upon actually receiving it, if both husband and wife provide *simanim* of the *get* and the wife's *simanim* are at least as strong, the object is returned to her: *hu omer simanei ha-get ve-hi omeret simanei ha-get, tei-nasen lah*. The reasoning is that she would not know the *simanim* unless she had actually received it, whereas he naturally knows the *simanim* since it originated with him. Her knowledge is therefore the more probative fact.

### Hole in Letter

The Gemara challenges: what *simanim* are being described? If the claim is based on *midas arko ve-rachvo* — the length and width of the *get* — this is something the wife might have observed while the husband was holding it, without ever having received it herself. The Gemara answers that the relevant case would be something like *nekev yesh lo b'tzad os pelonit* — she says there is a hole next to a particular letter in the fifth line of the document. That is the kind of detail only someone who had actually read or handled the document closely would know.

### String Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא אוֹמֵר סִימָנֵי הַחוּט, וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת סִימָנֵי הַחוּט – יִנָּתֵן לָהּ. בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּחִיוָּרָא וּבְסוּמָּקָא, וְדִלְמָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ חֲזַיְתֵיהּ? אֶלָּא בְּמִדַּת אׇרְכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the husband **states** the **distinguishing marks of the string** with which the bill of divorce is bound, **and she states** the **distinguishing marks of the string,** the document **shall be given to** the wife. The Gemara asks: **With what** distinguishing mark did she describe the string? **If we say** that she described it **by** saying that the string is **white or by** saying that it is **red,** this cannot be the mark based on which she proves her ownership, as **perhaps while** her husband **was holding** the bill of divorce, **she saw** the string. **Rather,** it must be that she stated **the measure of its length.** As the string was wrapped around the document, she would know its length only if the bill of divorce had been in her hand.

Similarly, if both husband and wife give *simanei ha-chut* — describing the string that tied the *get* — one must ask what exactly they describe. If it is merely the color (*bechivra u-vesumka*), she might have glimpsed the string while he was holding it, so this does not prove receipt. But if she specifies the length of the string — *ela be-midas arka* — that is a detail precise enough that the Gemara assumes she would not have gotten it right without actually having received the *get*.

### Storage Container

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא אוֹמֵר בַּחֲפִיסָה וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת בַּחֲפִיסָה – יִנָּתֵן לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִידָּע יָדְעָה דְּכֹל מָה דְּאִית לֵיהּ בַּחֲפִיסָה הוּא דְּמַנַּח לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the husband claims that the bill of divorce was not given to the wife and **states** that it was stored **in a case, and** the wife claims that she received the bill of divorce and **states** that it was stored **in a case,** the document **shall be given to** the husband. **What is the reason?** Identification of the document based on its storage cannot prove her ownership, as **she knows that he places any** valuable item **that he has** in his possession **in the case.**

The final case: *hu omer be-chafisa ve-hi omeret be-chafisa* — both husband and wife claim the *get* was stored in his carrying case. Here, *yinasen lo* — it is returned to the husband, not the wife. The reason: *meda yad'a de-chol midait lei be-chafisa hu de-manach lei* — she simply knows her husband's habits. She knows that he stores all his important documents in his carrying case. This does not prove she received the *get* at all; it only reflects her familiarity with his organizational customs. Since this knowledge could have been acquired without receipt, it does not carry the evidentiary weight needed to return the *get* to her.

The sugya thus concludes with a rich picture of the *simanim* question: the debate over whether *simanim* are *d'Oraisa* or *d'Rabbanan*; the three tiers of *simanim* — below threshold, standard, and *siman muvhak*; the competing justifications for the Rabbinic *takana* if *simanim* are not Biblical; Rava's ultimate conclusion that the return of *shtarei chov* by *simanim* forces us to regard them as *d'Oraisa*; and finally, the hierarchy governing competing claims — *simanim* versus *simanim*, *simanim* versus *edim*, and the logic of which dimension of identification most closely tracks genuine ownership.
