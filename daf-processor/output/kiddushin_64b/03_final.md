# Kiddushin 64b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hazakah & Mah Li Leshaker

### Mishnah's Basic Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא נֶאֱמָן לְהַתִּיר וְאֵין נֶאֱמָן לֶאֱסוֹר. נֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Apparently,** one can infer from the mishna that a husband **is deemed credible to render** his wife **permitted but he is not deemed credible to render** her **forbidden.** The Gemara suggests: Shall **we say** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan?**

The Gemara on 64b opens by completing its analysis of the Mishnah's rule that a husband is believed to free his wife from *yibbum* but not to bind her in it. The Mishnah had established this principle without fully explaining its rationale, and the Gemara — having already introduced the positions of Rabbi and Rabbi Natan in the preceding discussion — now presses toward a resolution that clarifies the underlying logic.

### Abaye on Hazakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: בִּשְׁעַת קִידּוּשִׁין אָמַר יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים, בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה אָמַר אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים, בִּשְׁעַת קִידּוּשִׁין אָמַר אֵין לוֹ אַחִים, בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה אָמַר יֵשׁ לוֹ אַחִים – נֶאֱמָן לְהַתִּיר וְאֵין נֶאֱמָן לֶאֱסוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אַף נֶאֱמָן לֶאֱסוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If someone **said at the time of** his **betrothal** that **he has children,** but **at the time of** his **death he said** that **he does not have children;** or if he **said at the time of betrothal** that **he does not have brothers,** and **at the time of death he said he has brothers,** in both cases he is **deemed credible to render** her **permitted,** i.e., to release her from the obligation of levirate marriage on the basis of his first statement, **but he is not deemed credible to render** her **forbidden** based on his last claim. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Natan says:** He is **deemed credible even to render** her **forbidden.**

Abaye explains the Mishnah's position as follows: the husband is believed only insofar as his statement reinforces the existing *hazakah* — the presumptive status of his wife. Since we have no established presumption that the man has brothers or that he lacks sons, we begin by presuming she is not obligated in *yibbum*. If on his deathbed he says, "I have children," he is saying something consistent with that presumption and he is believed. If, however, he says, "I have brothers," he is attempting to overturn the status quo and make her *asur* to the world — and for that, his unilateral claim carries no weight. As the Gemara puts it: *lav kol kemeinei de'asar la akol alma* — it is not within his power to forbid her to everyone.

The Mishnah's principle, then, is not precisely that he is believed to permit and not to forbid; it is that he is not believed to go against the existing *hazakah*.

### Rabbi Natan's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא מוּחְזָק לַן בְּאַחֵי וְלָא מוּחְזָק לַן בִּבְנֵי, דְּאָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מוּחְזָק לַן בִּבְנֵי וְלָא בְּאַחֵי אָמַר: "יֵשׁ לִי בָּנִים" – נֶאֱמָן, "יֵשׁ לִי אַחִים" – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ דְּאָסַר לַהּ אַכּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: The mishna** is referring to a case **where we** do not **have a presumptive status with regard to** possible **brothers** of his, **and** likewise **we** do **not have a presumptive status with regard to** his **children.** The court has no prior knowledge as to whether the dying man has brothers or children. **As we say: Since we** do **not** have **presumptive status with regard to either children or brothers,** if **he said: I have children,** he is **deemed credible,** whereas if he said: **I have brothers,** he is **not deemed credible.** Why? **It is not in his** power **to render her forbidden to everyone,** counter to the presumption that he has no brothers.

The *baraita* of Rabbi Natan presents a more complex scenario. There, it was established before the marriage that the husband did have brothers — there was a presumption (*hazakah*) that she would be obligated in *yibbum*. At the time of *kiddushin*, the husband said, "I have no brothers," which went against that presumption. On his deathbed, he retracted and said that he does have brothers. Rabbi Natan holds that in this case, the deathbed statement is believed and she is bound in *yibbum*.

### Mah Li Leshaker Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמוּחְזָק לַן בְּאַחֵי וְלָא מוּחְזָק לַן בִּבְנֵי, דְּאָמְרִינַן: "מָה לִי לְשַׁקֵּר": מַאי קָאָמַר, לְמִיפְטְרַהּ מִיָּבָם? מָצֵי אֲמַר: פָּטַרְנָא לִךְ בְּגִיטָּא.
>
> **Translation:** a case **where we have a presumptive status that** he has **brothers, but we do not have a presumptive status that** he has **children,** although no full-fledged testimony has been submitted for either claim. **As** in that case **we say** and apply the logic of: **Why would I lie?** In other words, if he were lying he would have stated a more credible claim or performed an action achieving the desired result. The Gemara explains: For **what** reason **is he saying** that he has children or that he has no brothers, if not **to exempt her from a *yavam*?** But if so, **he can say** to her instead: **I exempt you by** means of **a bill of divorce.** Consequently, it can be assumed that he is telling the truth when he says that he has children.

The first question is why we believed him at the time of *kiddushin* when he said he had no brothers — a statement that contradicted the prevailing *hazakah*. The answer is *mah li leshaker*: why would he lie? One might argue he was trying to induce her to marry him by assuring her she would never be subject to *yibbum*. But this reasoning fails, because if he simply wanted to make that promise, he could have told her he would give her a *get* that would take effect a moment before his death. Since he had an alternative means of achieving the same result without misrepresenting his family situation, we say there is no sufficient motive to lie — and therefore we believe him, even against the *hazakah*. This *mah li leshaker* functions much like *migo*: because he could have made a more modest claim and achieved the same benefit, the fact that he made the stronger claim is treated as equivalent to the testimony of witnesses.

This is, the Gemara acknowledges, a significant *chiddush*. Even where there was a pre-existing *hazakah* that she was obligated in *yibbum*, his statement at *kiddushin* was sufficient to override it.

### Rabbi's Perspective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי סָבַר: "מָה לִי לְשַׁקֵּר" כִּי עֵדִים דָּמֵי, וְאָתוּ עֵדִים עָקְרִי חֲזָקָה. וְרַבִּי נָתָן סָבַר: "מָה לִי לְשַׁקֵּר" כִּי חֲזָקָה דָּמֵי, לָא אַתְיָא חֲזָקָה וְעָקְרָה חֲזָקָה לִגְמָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In light of the above explanation, the Gemara clarifies the dispute in the *baraita*. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **maintains** that the claim of: **Why would I lie,** is **considered like** the testimony of **witnesses, and witnesses** can **come and uproot a presumption.** Therefore, he does not have the power to contradict his previous claim and say that he has no children and render her forbidden at the time of his death. **And Rabbi Natan maintains** that: **Why would I lie,** is **considered like a presumption,** and **a presumption cannot come and uproot** another **presumption entirely.** As a result, he is deemed credible even for the purpose of rendering her forbidden, i.e., if he says at the time of his death that he has no children, the earlier presumption that he had brothers, but no children, is reinstated.

Rabbi holds that this first *mah li leshaker* was so powerful that it is treated *k'eidim dami* — as equivalent to the testimony of witnesses. And just as witness testimony would completely uproot the *hazakah*, so too his statement at *kiddushin* entirely erased the prior presumption and established a new one: she is now presumed to be free from *yibbum*. With that new *hazakah* firmly in place, his later deathbed statement — that he does have brothers — is merely an attempt to make a woman who is presumed *mutar* into someone *asur*. And for that, there is no *mah li leshaker*: there is no obvious alternative means by which he could have accomplished that goal on his deathbed. He cannot claim he "could have" bound her another way. Perhaps he simply wishes, in his final moments, to cause her anguish. Without a compelling counter-motive, his statement has no special credibility, and since she already has the *hazakah* of being free, he cannot overturn it.

### Rabbi Natan Refined

Rabbi Natan disagrees with Rabbi's assessment of the first statement's power. He holds that *mah li leshaker* is only *k'chazakah dami* — as strong as a *hazakah*, not equivalent to witnesses. The first statement was sufficient to neutralize the pre-existing *hazakah* that she was obligated, but it did not completely erase that original presumption and replace it with a new one. The scales were tipped, but not so decisively. Consequently, when on his deathbed he goes back to reinforcing the original presumption that he has brothers, he is believed — because he is not creating a new *issur* from nothing; he is returning us to an underlying presumption that was only partially displaced.

### Unified Understanding

The Gemara uses this analysis to make an important clarifying point. Rabbi Natan's position had initially appeared to say that a husband is believed to make his wife obligated in *yibbum* — a sweeping power to bind her by his statement alone. The Gemara now shows that even Rabbi Natan would agree that a husband cannot, through a bare claim, take a woman who is presumed *mutar* and make her *asur*. Rabbi Natan only believes the husband when the statement reinforces a pre-existing *hazakah* that had been partially, but not fully, neutralized. Both Rabbi and Rabbi Natan concur that a husband's claim cannot independently create an obligation of *yibbum* where none was presumed to exist. Their dispute is narrow: how potently did the *mah li leshaker* at *kiddushin* shift the presumptive status, and can a deathbed retraction restore the original *hazakah*?

## Hamekadesh Et Bito

### Bogeret Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ סְתָם – אֵין הַבּוֹגְרוֹת בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who betroths his daughter** to a man **without specification,** i.e., without specifying which daughter he meant, **the grown women are not included** among those who might be betrothed, since he does not have the right to betroth them.

The Mishnah states: *hamekadesh et bito stam* — a man who betroths off his daughter without specifying which one. The rule is that *bogeret* daughters — those who are *gedolot*, over twelve and a half years old — are not included, because he has no legal authority to arrange their *kiddushin*. This seems obvious enough, and the Gemara will shortly ask what the *chiddush* could possibly be.

### Plural vs. Actual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קְטַנּוֹת בַּכְּלָל, שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִים לְבִיאָה, הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּשֶׁאֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא גְּדוֹלָה וּקְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** One can **learn from** the mishna **that betrothal that is not given to consummation,** i.e., where the husband may not consummate the marriage, in this case because each of the women might not be his wife but his wife's sister, **is** nevertheless considered **betrothal** to the extent that each of the women requires a bill of divorce to render her permitted to marry someone else. This contradicts the opinion of Rava that betrothal that is not given to consummation does not take effect at all. The Gemara rejects this: **Here we are dealing with** a case in **which there are only** two daughters, **an adult woman and a minor girl.**

Before reaching that question, the Gemara notes that the Mishnah uses the plural *bogrot*, which might suggest the man has multiple adult daughters. The Gemara clarifies: this is not a factual description of his family. The plural is idiomatic — it reflects how people generally speak in such cases. When people say that a father marrying off his daughter does not include the adult daughters, they express it in the plural as a category, not as a description of how many adult daughters this particular man actually has.

The real case of the Mishnah, the Gemara establishes, involves a father who has exactly one adult daughter and one underage daughter (*ketanah*). The point is not to address multiple adult daughters; it is to clarify which daughter is included in an unspecified betrothal. And the *chiddush*, the Gemara explains, lies in a specific circumstance: what if the adult daughter designated the father as her *shaliach* — her agent — to arrange her *kiddushin*? In that case, he would be legally empowered to marry off both daughters. Yet the Mishnah implies that even then, the unspecified betrothal is presumed to refer to the underage daughter, not the adult one.

### Shaliach & Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, בּוֹגְרוֹת מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּשַׁוִּיתֵיהּ שָׁלִיחַ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי מְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשֵׁי אַדַּעְתָּא דִידַהּ קָא מְקַבֵּל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ מִידֵּי דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ, וְעָבֵיד מִידֵּי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a different question: This *halakha* **is obvious,** as **what are grown women doing** in this context, i.e., how could it be suggested that a father could betroth to a man his daughter who is a grown woman? His authority extends only to his minor daughters. The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the adult daughter **appointed** her father as her **agent** to accept her betrothal, and he proceeds to betroth one of his daughters without specifying which one. **Lest you say** with regard to this situation that **when** he **accepts** the **betrothal he accepts** it **with** his adult daughter **in mind,** as he is her agent, the mishna **teaches us** that **a person does not put aside a matter from which he derives benefit,** i.e., the betrothal of his minor daughter, where he keeps the money, **and perform a matter from which he does not derive benefit,** i.e., the betrothal of his adult daughter, where she keeps the money.

When the adult daughter has made the father her *shaliach*, one might have supposed that since he is authorized to act for her, and since a marriage of an adult woman is more intuitive — we might presume he intended the adult daughter. The Gemara rejects this: *lo shavik inish midi d'is lei hana'ei minei v'avid midi d'leis lei hana'ei minei* — a person does not abandon something that benefits him in favor of something that does not. When a father marries off his *ketanah*, he retains the *kesef kiddushin* — the ring or money given by the *mekadesh* — for himself. When he acts as *shaliach* for his adult daughter, the *kesef kiddushin* goes to her. He therefore has a financial incentive to be marrying off the *ketanah* rather than the *bogeret*.

### Father's Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: "קִדּוּשַׁיי לָךְ"? אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ מִצְוָה דְּרַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ וְעָבֵיד מִצְוָה דְּלָא רַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Are we not dealing** even with a case **where** the daughter **says to him:** As reward for fulfilling your agency, **my betrothal** money shall belong **to you?** This would mean that the father derives benefit from the betrothal of his adult daughter as well. The Gemara counters: **Even so, a person does not put aside a mitzva that is imposed upon him,** i.e., the betrothal of his minor daughter, **to perform a mitzva that is not** directly **imposed upon him,** the betrothal of his adult daughter. Since she is of age, her betrothal is her own responsibility.

The Gemara presses further: what if even the adult daughter told the father that he may keep the *kesef kiddushin* for his efforts? Would we not then presume, absent any financial differential, that the *bogeret* is the intended bride? The Gemara answers that even so, the father's presumed intention remains the *ketanah*. The reason is not financial but moral and legal: *lo shavik inish mitzvah d'ramya alei v'avid mitzvah d'lo ramya alei* — a person does not set aside an obligation that rests upon him in order to fulfill one that does not.

The Torah places the responsibility for an underage daughter squarely on her father. In the world the Talmud presumes, a daughter moves directly from *beit aviha* — her father's home — to *beit ba'alah* — her husband's home. There is no normative period of independent adult life without either a father's or a husband's protection. Once a girl reaches *bogeret* status, she acquires her own legal standing and can manage her own affairs; she is, in a sense, no longer fully under her father's jurisdiction. The *ketanah*, however, is entirely his responsibility. He is obligated to ensure that she transitions properly from his household into a marriage. That obligation weighs on him and constitutes the priority: he will, as it were, take care of his own obligations first.

### Two Daughter Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי בָנוֹת מִשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וְאָמַר: "קִדַּשְׁתִּי אֶת בִּתִּי הַגְּדוֹלָה, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּגְּדוֹלוֹת, אוֹ גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת, אוֹ קְטַנָּה שֶׁבַּגְּדוֹלוֹת שֶׁהִיא גְּדוֹלָה מִן הַגְּדוֹלוֹת שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת" – כּוּלָּן אֲסוּרוֹת חוּץ מִן הַקְּטַנָּה שֶׁבַּקְּטַנּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן מוּתָּרוֹת, חוּץ מִן הַגְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּגְּדוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who has two groups** of **daughters from two women,** i.e., one group of daughters from each wife, **and he said: I betrothed my elder daughter** to someone **but I do not know if** I meant **the eldest of the older** group of daughters, **or the eldest of the younger** group of daughters, **or the youngest of the older** group, **who is** nevertheless **older than the eldest of the younger** group, **all** the daughters **are forbidden, except for the youngest of the younger** group. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yosei says:** Despite the uncertainty, **they are all permitted except for the eldest of the older** group, as one who says elder without further specification means the oldest of them all.

The Mishnah then addresses a more complex case. A man has two groups of daughters — one group from an earlier wife and a second group from a later wife. He says, "I betrothed my *gedolah* — my oldest," but then cannot remember precisely whom he meant. The ambiguity the Mishnah describes is striking: did he mean the oldest of the older group? The oldest of the younger group — who is the oldest within that set? Or the youngest of the older group — who is nonetheless older than the oldest of the younger group?

The Mishnah walks through the parallel case using the word *ketanah* — youngest — with the same structure.

### Rabbi Meir's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מַחֵית אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִסְפֵיקָא, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: לָא מַחֵית אִינִישׁ לִסְפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to these opinions: **Is this to say that Rabbi Meir maintains: A person places himself in** a situation of **uncertainty,** i.e., people issue statements and accept upon themselves obligations that are not clearly defined, **and Rabbi Yosei maintains** that **a person does not place himself in** a situation of **uncertainty,** as one has only well-defined situations in mind?

Rabbi Meir holds that all daughters who could in any sense be called *gedolah* are potentially betrothed and therefore *asur* — all, that is, except for the very youngest of the younger group. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that a person is willing to use a term loosely and allow themselves to enter into a state of ambiguity. If he remembers saying "*gedolah*," he may well have been using that label in a relative sense — anyone who is older than someone else might be called *gedolah*. As long as a daughter has someone younger than her, she can bear that label. Rabbi Meir does not expect precision in such a memory, and the result is that the uncertainty sweeps broadly.

### Rabbi Yosi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּהָא, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דִּ"קְטַנָּה" – לְהָךְ קְטַנָּה דְּכוּלְּהוּ קָרֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But in this** case of one who betroths his younger daughter, **say** that **he concedes to Rabbi Yosei** that a father **calls** only **that** one who is the **youngest of all: Younger,** as a father prefers to call his daughters: Elder, whenever possible.

Rabbi Yosi takes the opposite position: *kulan mutarot chutz min hagedolah shebagedolot* — they are all permitted except for the oldest of the oldest group. Rabbi Yosi reasons that a person does not get themselves into ambiguity (*lo machzik inish nafshei besafeka*). If what he remembers is the word *gedolah*, he must mean the most specific and unambiguous referent of that word — the daughter who is oldest in the clearest sense, the oldest of the oldest group. Anything less definite would not have registered in his memory under that label.

### Pesach Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיפְכָּא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ, דִּתְנַן: הַנּוֹדֵר "עַד הַפֶּסַח" – אָסוּר עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ, "עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא פֶּסַח" – אָסוּר עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא, "עַד פְּנֵי פֶסַח" – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אָסוּר עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If he said: **Until before Passover, Rabbi Meir says:** It is **forbidden** to him **until** Passover **arrives. Rabbi Yosei says:** It is forbidden to him **until it ends.** This indicates that Rabbi Meir holds that one does not put himself into a situation of uncertainty, as despite his mention of the presence of Passover, which might be referring to any of the days of the Festival, he does not mean one of the days of Passover itself but until the arrival of the Festival, whereas Rabbi Yosei claims that his words apply to each of the days of Passover, due to uncertainty. This is the opposite of the above interpretation of the mishna concerning a man who betroths his daughter.

The Gemara notes that there is an apparently parallel debate elsewhere. If someone takes a *neder* forbidding himself from something *ad pnei HaPesach* — until the "face" of *Pesach* — what does that mean? The Mishnah cited there states: Rabbi Meir says *asur ad sheyagia* — forbidden until *Pesach* arrives; Rabbi Yosi says *asur ad sheyeitzei* — forbidden until *Pesach* departs. But this seems to be the reverse of their positions in our Mishnah. Here, Rabbi Meir holds that the term can refer to many things and thus extends the prohibition broadly; there, Rabbi Meir seems to take the narrower reading, and Rabbi Yosi the broader one.

The Gemara initially suggests that the names may have been accidentally reversed in transmission — *muchlefet hashita* — and that a *baraita* records the positions in the correct order consistent with our Mishnah.

### Abaye's Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי בָנוֹת, אֲבָל בְּכַת אַחַת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל גְּדוֹלָה מַמָּשׁ, קְטַנָּה מַמָּשׁ, אֶמְצָעִית בִּשְׁמָהּ קָרֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei applies **to two groups of girls,** as Rabbi Meir maintains that all of them can be called: Elder, except for the youngest of the younger group. **But with regard to one group,** i.e., if all the daughters share the same mother, **everyone agrees** that when the father says: Elder, he means the **actual eldest** one, and when he says: Younger, he means the **actual youngest** daughter. As for the **middle** daughter, she is called neither elder nor younger; rather, **he calls her by her name.**

Abaye offers a fundamental clarification of the debate in our Mishnah. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi, he says, applies specifically to the case of *shtei kitei banot* — two distinct groups of daughters. The crucial feature of two groups is that membership in the older group itself confers the label *gedolah* on every daughter within it. A daughter in the older group can be called *gedolah* not because she is the oldest individual, but because she belongs to the *kat hagedolah* — the older cohort. This is what allows the ambiguity to extend beyond a single individual.

By contrast, if there is only one group of daughters — say, four daughters in a single family unit — then *gedolah* means the oldest and *ketanah* means the youngest, full stop. A middle daughter would be called by her name. The label *gedolah* or *ketanah* attaches unambiguously only to the extremes. In that case, there is no dispute: everyone agrees that *gedolah* means precisely the oldest, and *ketanah* precisely the youngest.

### Three Daughters Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אֶמְצָעִית שֶׁבְּכַת שְׁנִיָּה תִּשְׁתְּרֵי! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בְּשֶׁאֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא גְּדוֹלָה וּקְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Adda bar Mattana said to Abaye: If that is so, with regard to one who said: I betrothed my elder daughter, **the middle** daughter **of the** younger **second group should be permitted,** as he would have called her by name rather than referring to her as: The elder one. The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where there are only** two daughters, **an adult woman and a minor girl,** but no middle daughter.

Rav Adda bar Masna challenges Abaye: if the debate applies only when there are two groups, and within those groups the label *gedolah* attaches to everyone in the older cohort, then what if there are three daughters in the younger group rather than two? The middle one of the younger group would seem ambiguous — she could arguably be called *gedolah* relative to the youngest, but she cannot be the *gedolah* of the younger group in any straightforward sense. Should she then be *mutar*?

Abaye responds that the Mishnah is best read as presupposing exactly two daughters in the younger group. This is evident from the Mishnah's own language: it says that the father does not know whether he meant *gedolah shebakedolot*, *gedolah shebaketanot*, or *ketanah shebagedolot* — but says nothing about a middle daughter in either group. If there were three in the younger group, the Mishnah would have needed to mention the middle one as a case of ambiguity. Since it does not, the case must be that there are only two in each group.

### Pesach Resolution

Returning to the *Pesach* case, Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua asks Rava: if the entire debate in the Mishnah rests on the two-group structure — which allows the label *gedolah* to attach to an entire cohort rather than a single individual — why does the *Pesach* case generate a parallel dispute? The days of *Pesach* are all in one group; there are not two cohorts of days. According to Abaye, only one day should clearly be called the *pnei haPesach*, just as only one daughter in a single group can unambiguously be called *gedolah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם בְּלִישָּׁנָא דְעָלְמָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: "עַד פְּנֵי הַפֶּסַח" – עַד קַמֵּי פִיסְחָא, וּמָר סָבַר: עַד דְּמִיפְּנֵי פִּיסְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: There they disagree with regard to the general** usage of **language.** In other words, their dispute in that case does not concern the basic issue of whether or not one places himself in a position of uncertainty. Rather, they disagree over the way people speak. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the phrase: **Until before Passover,** means: **Until** just **before Passover, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that it means **until Passover passes** and ends.

The Gemara resolves this by clarifying that the *Pesach* case involves an entirely different type of dispute. The debate there is not about whether a person enters into ambiguity or remembers things loosely — it is a purely linguistic question about the meaning of the word *pnei*. One view (*mar savar*) holds that *pnei* means "before" — *ad kamei Pischa* — and so the prohibition lasts until *Pesach* begins. The other view holds that *pnei* derives from *mifnei* — to turn away or pass — and so the prohibition lasts until *Pesach* has passed. The two Amoraim are therefore debating the semantic content of a single word, not the psychological question of how loosely people use descriptive labels or whether they tolerate ambiguity. Once this distinction is clear, the apparent parallel with the daughters case dissolves entirely, and the positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi in our Mishnah stand on their own terms without needing to be reversed.

## He Said / She Said

### His Claim Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה: "קִדַּשְׁתִּיךְ" וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: "לֹא קִדַּשְׁתַּנִי" – הוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ וְהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who says to a woman: I betrothed you, and she says: You did not betroth me, he is forbidden to her relatives,** as his claim that he has betrothed her renders himself forbidden to her relatives. **And she is permitted to his relatives,** in accordance with her stance that she is not betrothed to him.

The Mishnah presents a series of cases in which the two parties to an alleged *kiddushin* give contradictory accounts. The first: a man says to a woman, *kidashtich* — "I betrothed you" — and she says, *lo kidashtani* — "you did not betroth me." The result is asymmetric. He is *asur* to her relatives — he cannot marry her mother, her daughter, her sister — because he is bound by his own statement. She, however, is entirely *muteret*: she is not bound by his statement and may marry whomever she wishes, including his own relatives. One person's claim cannot impose a status upon another person.

### Her Claim Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת "קִדַּשְׁתַּנִי" וְהוּא אוֹמֵר "לֹא קִדַּשְׁתִּיךְ" – הוּא מוּתָּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ וְהִיא אֲסוּרָה בִּקְרוֹבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If **she says: You betrothed me, and he says: I did not betroth you, he is permitted to her relatives and she is forbidden to his relatives** by the same reasoning.

The reverse: she says he married her, and he says he did not. Now she is *asurah* to his relatives — and, more immediately, she cannot marry any other man, since she is claiming she is already married. He, however, is *mutar* to her relatives, since he denies the marriage. She has bound herself by her own words; she has not bound him.

### Different Targets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "קִידַּשְׁתִּיךְ" וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת "לֹא קִידַּשְׁתָּ אֶלָּא בִּתִּי" – הוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹת גְּדוֹלָה וּגְדוֹלָה מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו. הוּא מוּתָּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹת קְטַנָּה וּקְטַנָּה מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If a man says to a woman: **I betrothed you, and she says: You betrothed only my daughter, he is forbidden to the relatives of the older** woman, the mother, whom he claims to have betrothed, **and the older** woman **is permitted to his relatives. He is permitted to the relatives of the younger** woman, the daughter, as he maintains that he did not betroth her, **and the younger** woman **is permitted to his relatives,** since her mother's statement is insufficient to render her forbidden.

The Mishnah then introduces cases in which the two parties do not even agree on who was betrothed. He says, "I betrothed you," and she says, "No — you betrothed my daughter." Each party is bound only by their own statement. He is *asur* to the mother's relatives (since he claims he married her), but *mutar* to the daughter's relatives (since he makes no such claim about her). The mother is *muteret* to his relatives (since she does not claim to be married to him), and the daughter is also *muteret* to his relatives, because a mother's claim about her daughter's betrothal does not bind the daughter.

### Mother vs. Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "קִדַּשְׁתִּי אֶת בִּתִּךְ" וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת "לֹא קִדַּשְׁתָּ אֶלָּא אוֹתִי" – הוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹת קְטַנָּה וּקְטַנָּה מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו. הוּא מוּתָּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹת גְּדוֹלָה וּגְדוֹלָה אֲסוּרָה בִּקְרוֹבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if he says: **I betrothed your daughter, and she,** the mother, **says: You betrothed only me, he is forbidden to the relatives of the younger** woman, **and the younger** woman **is permitted to his relatives; he is permitted to the relatives of the older** woman, **and the older** woman **is forbidden to his relatives.**

The final case reverses the parties: he says, "I betrothed your daughter," and she says, "No — you betrothed me." Again, each is bound only by their own claim. He is *asur* to the daughter's relatives, *mutar* to the mother's relatives. She is *asurah* to his relatives because she claims the marriage, and the daughter remains *muteret* to his relatives since she said nothing.

The underlying principle throughout is simple: *you are bound by your own statement, and your statement cannot bind another person*.

### Why Believe Whom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּגַבְרָא לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ וּמִיקְּרֵי אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **necessary** for the mishna to specify all these cases. The Gemara elaborates: **As, had** the mishna **taught us** the *halakha* only **with regard to himself,** i.e., the case where he claims to have betrothed the woman, one might have said that he is not deemed credible at all, **because a man does not care** if **he happens to say** that he betrothed a woman even if he did not do so, as he can betroth another woman.

The Gemara asks why these cases need to be stated explicitly rather than derived from each other. The answer illuminates the asymmetry between male and female claims. When a man says he married a woman, it costs him relatively little: his only immediate *issur* is with respect to her close relatives. He remains free to marry other women; no sweeping prohibition falls upon him. Since his statement is so inexpensive, *gavra lo ikhpat lei u'mikrei amar* — men will make such statements casually — and we have no strong reason to extend its legal effect to bind anyone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִיהִי, אֵימָא: אִי לָאו דְּקִים לַהּ בְּדִיבּוּרַהּ, לָא הֲוָת אָמְרָה, וְלִיתְּסַר אִיהוּ בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in a case where **she** claims to have been betrothed by him, one might **say** that **if her statement was not certain to her she would not have said** it. Since her claim that he betrothed her renders her forbidden to everyone else, it is likely that it is true, **and** therefore one might think that **he should** also **be forbidden to her relatives** on the basis of this assumption. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that this is not the case.

By contrast, when a woman says she is married to a man, she is making an extraordinarily consequential claim. She is effectively declaring herself *asurah* to every other man in the world. No one would make such a statement carelessly or frivolously. The inherent weight of her claim gives it credibility: *lo havi amra l'meisar nafsha b'krovateih* — she would not make that statement, imposing such a severe restriction upon herself, unless it were true. One might therefore argue that even the man should be bound — that at minimum he should not be permitted to marry her relatives, since she has given such a compelling self-incriminating statement. The Gemara concludes, however, that even this reasoning does not suffice to make something *asur* upon another person on the basis of one party's unilateral claim.

### Mother's No Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "קִידַּשְׁתִּיךְ" וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת וְכוּ'. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הִיתְּסַר אִיהוּ בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ וְהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הֵימְנֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְאָב, מִדְּרַבָּנַן הֵימְנוּהָ לְדִידַהּ וְתִיתְּסַר בְּרַתַּהּ בְּדִיבּוּרַהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, with regard to one who says: **I betrothed you, and she says:** You betrothed only my daughter, in which case he is forbidden to her relatives but she is permitted to his, the Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this as well?** The principle has already been established. The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to state this case too, as it might **enter your mind to say:** Since **by Torah law, the Merciful One deems credible a father** who claims to have betrothed his daughter to a particular person, perhaps the Sages **deem** a mother **credible by rabbinic law, and** therefore **her daughter should be forbidden** based **on her statement.** The mishna therefore **teaches us** that a mother is not believed with regard to her daughter.

The case of the mother who claims that her daughter was betrothed raises a natural question: the Torah empowers a father to betroth off his underage daughter, but grants no such authority to the mother. Might we nonetheless say that rabbinically, the mother should be believed enough to impose some restriction upon her daughter? The Gemara states that no such rabbinic authority exists — the mother's claim does not bind the daughter even rabbinically. The *chiddush* of the Mishnah in including this case is precisely to tell us that we do not extend the father's Torah-level power to the mother on a rabbinic basis.

### Pesach & Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: כּוֹפִין, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מְבַקְּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over how the court should proceed in practice with regard to the cases described in the mishna. **Rav says:** The court **forces** the man to give her a bill of divorce, **and Shmuel says:** The court **requests** that he give a bill of divorce.

The Gemara then addresses the practical consequence in the case where she claims to be married and he denies it. Shmuel holds: *mevakshin mimenu litten get* — we ask him, as a matter of propriety and compassion, to give her a *get*. She has put herself in a bind by her own statement; she can now not marry anyone else. Even though he is not halakhically compelled to do anything, it is proper to request that he give her a *get* and thereby free her from her self-imposed status.

### Request vs. Coercion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלָא: שְׁמַעְתָּתָא אַהֲדָדֵי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְבַקְּשִׁין מִמֶּנּוּ לִיתֵּן גֵּט. אָמַר רַב: אִם נָתַן גֵּט מֵעַצְמוֹ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִיתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara offers a different explanation: These ***halakhot* were stated together,** as follows: **Shmuel says** that the court **requests of him to give a bill of divorce. Rav says: If he gave a bill of divorce of his own accord,** without being asked to do so but merely in response to her claim, the court **forces him to give** her payment for her **marriage contract** as well. By giving her a bill of divorce of his own volition, he has effectively admitted that he betrothed her, despite the fact that he has not said so explicitly. Consequently, he must also provide her with payment for her marriage contract.

Rav's position initially appears to be that we *force* him to give a *get* — *kofin*. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between Rav and Shmuel by showing that they are not disputing but complementing one another. Shmuel's statement establishes that we request a *get* from him. Rav adds: *v'im natan get mei-atzmo* — if he gives the *get* on his own initiative, without being asked or pressured by the *beit din*, that act constitutes an implicit admission that he was in fact married to her. Having admitted the marriage through the voluntary *get*, we then *force* him to pay her *ketubah*.

The practical implication is notable: a man in this situation would be well advised to give the *get* only in response to the *beit din's* request, and not spontaneously. A voluntary *get*, given before any such request, reads as an admission of the marriage and opens him to a compelled *ketubah* payment. The Gemara presents this without editorializing, though the underlying logic is clear.

## Witnesses Requirement

### Rav Yehuda's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּעֵד אֶחָד – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְקִידּוּשָׁיו. בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים, מַאי? אִין וְלָא וְרַפְיָא בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **one who betroths** another **with,** i.e., in the presence of, **one witness, one** need **not be concerned that his betrothal** has taken effect. The students **raised a dilemma before Rav Yehuda:** If **both** the man and the woman **concede** that it was a betrothal, **what** is the *halakha*? Is the betrothal valid? Rav Yehuda did not provide a clear answer. He said: **Yes and no, and** the matter was **uncertain to him.**

The Gemara now turns to what is, in many respects, one of the most consequential *sugyot* in the entire tractate for contemporary *hilchos kiddushin*. Rav Yehuda states: *hamekadesh be'ed echad ein chosheshin lekiddushav* — if a man performs *kiddushin* with only one witness present, we have no concern that the *kiddushin* is binding. The marriage is without legal effect.

### Constitutive vs. Evident…

This rule is clear in its practical import, but its conceptual basis is ambiguous. Does the requirement for two witnesses mean that their presence is necessary to *prove* the *kiddushin* occurred — so that if everyone already agrees it happened, one witness might suffice? Or does it mean that the presence of two kosher witnesses is *constitutive* of the *kiddushin* itself — without them, the act simply does not take effect as a *kiddushin*, regardless of how confident everyone is that it occurred? The test case is clear: both parties fully admit that *kiddushin* took place, and one witness corroborates them. We have no factual doubt whatsoever. Is this *kiddushin* binding?

### Rav Yehuda Uncertain

Rav Yehuda himself, when asked this precise question, could not resolve it. He vacillated — *lo pashat b'yadei* — and left the question open. Whether two witnesses are required as a constitutive element of *kiddushin* or merely as an evidentiary mechanism for proving it remained, for him, genuinely uncertain.

### Shmuel's Clear Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּעֵד אֶחָד – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְקִידּוּשָׁיו, וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* discussed this point. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with one witness, one** need **not be concerned that his betrothal** has taken effect, **and** this is the *halakha* **even if both** parties **concede** that there was a betrothal.

Rav Nachman, citing Shmuel, provides an unambiguous answer: *hamekadesh be'ed echad ein chosheshin lekiddushav, va'afilu shneihem modim* — even if both parties fully admit to the *kiddushin*, without two witnesses present the *kiddushin* is not legally binding. Shmuel's position is that witnesses are not merely evidentiary — they are constitutive. Without them, the act of *kiddushin* does not take legal effect.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה "קִדַּשְׁתִּיךְ" וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת "לֹא קִדַּשְׁתַּנִי" – הוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ וְהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו. אִי דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים – אַמַּאי מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו? וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים – אַמַּאי אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ? אֶלָּא לָאו בְּעֵד אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman** from the mishna: With regard to **one who says to a woman: I betrothed you, and she says: You did not betroth me, he is forbidden to her relatives and she is permitted to his relatives.** Rava proceeds to analyze the exact circumstances of this case. **If** the case is one **where there are witnesses, why** is **she permitted to his relatives?** It is a full-fledged betrothal performed in the presence of witnesses. **And if there are no witnesses** at all, **why** is **he forbidden to her relatives** without any testimony to that effect? **Rather, is it not** referring **to** a case where there was **one witness?**

Rava challenges this from the very Mishnah we have been studying. The *he said / she said* cases in the Mishnah — which occupy a significant portion of the tractate — appear to operate without any mention of witnesses. A man says he betrothed a woman; she denies it; the Mishnah determines who is bound by what. If *kiddushin* requires two witnesses to be legally binding, the Mishnah's entire framework of claims and counter-claims should be irrelevant: without witnesses, no *kiddushin* occurred, end of discussion. The fact that the Mishnah engages extensively with whose statement binds whom seems to presuppose that *kiddushin* can be legally effective without two witnesses present and actively testifying.

### One Witness Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דַּאֲמַר לַהּ "קִידַּשְׁתִּיךְ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי", וְהָלְכוּ לָהֶם לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where he said to her: I betrothed you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so,** i.e., there were two witnesses, **but they went overseas** and there is no way of clarifying what really occurred. Consequently, there are only the conflicting accounts of the man and woman, and therefore he is prohibited from marrying her relatives while she is permitted to marry his.

The Gemara resolves Rava's challenge by reinterpreting the Mishnah's scenario. The cases in the Mishnah do not presume an absence of witnesses. Rather, the Gemara reads the Mishnah as operating in a case where the claimant asserts that witnesses were present at the time — *kidashtich bifnei ploni u'ploni* — but those witnesses are not currently available to testify. They have, as the Gemara puts it, gone overseas (*halchu lahem limdinat hayam*). The legal question is then not whether *kiddushin* requires witnesses, but rather: given that someone claims witnesses were present, how much do we credit that claim? Each party is bound by their own statement regarding an alleged act they themselves assert was properly witnessed, even though those witnesses cannot currently be produced.

### All Cases Presume Witness

This resolution has far-reaching implications. Every one of the Mishnah's cases — not just the current ones but all the earlier discussions about who is believed regarding marriage — must be understood as involving a claim that witnesses were present, even though those witnesses are not currently being called to testify. The Mishnah is not operating in a world without witnesses; it is operating in a world where witnesses existed at the time but are unavailable now. This is, the Gemara notes, a significant reinterpretation of the entire series of *mishnayos*, and it is precisely what Shmuel's position requires.

### Seclusion as Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְלָנָה עִמּוֹ בְּפוּנְדְּקִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised an objection to** the opinion of Rav Naḥman from a mishna (*Eduyyot* 4:7): If **one divorces his wife, and she** subsequently **lodged with him in an inn, Beit Shammai say: She does not require a second bill of divorce from him, and Beit Hillel say: She requires a second bill of divorce from him.**

The Gemara then raises a further challenge from the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding a man who divorced his wife and later spent the night with her at an inn. *Beit Hillel* holds that a second *get* is required — suggesting that the encounter may have constituted new *kiddushin*; *Beit Shammai* holds it does not. The Gemara initially considers whether this debate might hinge on the question of one witness versus two, with the positions of *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* mapping onto different views of evidentiary sufficiency.

### What Witnesses See

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלָא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא עֵדֵי יִחוּד וְלֵיכָּא עֵדֵי בִיאָה. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: לָא
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is clear that the mishna is not referring to when there is one witness, and **here we are dealing with** a case **where there are witnesses to** their **seclusion, but there are no witnesses to** their engaging in **intercourse.** The dispute is based on the implications of this seclusion. **Beit Shammai hold:** One does **not**

The Gemara immediately recognizes that this reading does not hold up under scrutiny. If witnesses directly observed them having intercourse, *Beit Shammai* would have no grounds to say no second *get* is needed. And if there were no witnesses at all, *Beit Hillel* would have no basis to require one. The debate cannot be about the number of witnesses. It must concern something else.

The Gemara resolves this: the case involves witnesses who observed the couple entering into *yichud* — seclusion together — but who did not directly witness any sexual act. The legal and factual question is whether *eidei yichud* — witnesses to seclusion — are treated as *eidei biah* — witnesses to intercourse — and thereby constitute evidence of a new act of *kiddushin*.

### Beis Shammai

*Beit Shammai* holds: *lo amrinan hein hein eidei yichud hein hein eidei biah* — we do not treat witnesses to seclusion as equivalent to witnesses to intercourse. Seclusion alone, without prior sexual history between the parties, does not allow us to presume that intercourse occurred, and therefore no new *kiddushin* is created.

### Beis Hillel

*Beit Hillel* holds the opposite: *amrinan hein hein eidei yichud hein hein eidei biah* — where the couple had previously been married and had engaged in intercourse, witnesses seeing them enter seclusion together are treated as if they had witnessed the sexual act itself, and this constitutes evidence of a new *kiddushin* requiring a new *get*.

### Consensus on Virgins

Both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree, however, that if the couple had been divorced after *eirusin* — betrothal without consummation — and had therefore never had sexual relations, then witnesses to seclusion are not treated as witnesses to intercourse, and no second *get* is required. The reasoning: *she'ein libo gas bah* — without the prior intimacy of a consummated marriage, we cannot presume that an incidental overnight encounter would naturally lead to intercourse. The debate between the two schools thus concerns not the requirement for witnesses, but the evidentiary and presumptive weight to be assigned to what those witnesses actually saw.
