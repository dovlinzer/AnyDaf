# Horayot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Framework

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין בֵּית דִּין חַיָּיבִין עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ בְּדָבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ, וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ עַל דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The court is not liable** to bring an offering for absence of awareness of the matter **unless they issue a ruling with regard to a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering. And likewise the anointed priest** is liable only for such a ruling. **Neither** is the court liable to bring a sin-offering **for idol worship unless** the judges **issue a ruling with regard to a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet*, and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

Daf 8 continues into the second *perek*, which deals not only with the *korban* of the *Beit Din HaGadol* when the people follow its incorrect ruling, but also with the *korban* of the *Kohen Gadol*. The *Kohen Gadol*'s situation is a striking one: his special *korban* is only triggered when he himself *paskens* incorrectly and then follows his own *psak*. If he simply commits an ordinary sin *b'shogeg*, he does not bring his special *korban* — a conceptually unusual idea. The *daf* also takes up the parallel cases of the *nasi* (the political head) and the special *korban* brought in the context of *avodah zarah*, where the *Beit Din HaGadol* brings both a *par l'olah* and a *se'ir l'chatas* when most of the *tzibbur* follows their erroneous ruling. In that latter case, there is a *machloket* between *Rebbe* and the *Chachamim* regarding whether the *Kohen Gadol* requires the same mechanism of *psak* and following, or whether he functions more like an ordinary individual.

### Mishna: Par Criteria

The *mishna* on 8a states: *ein Beit Din chayav ad she-yiru b'davar she-zidono karet v'shgegato chatas* — the *Beit Din* brings its special *korban*, the *par*, only when the ruling concerns a sin for which willful violation incurs *karet* and unintentional violation incurs a standard *chatas*. If the sin is of a category for which an ordinary individual would bring, say, an *asham vadai* or an *oleh v'yored*, the *Beit Din*'s special collective *korban* does not apply.

### Kohen Gadol & Nasi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ, וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ עַל דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise the anointed priest** is liable only for such a ruling. **Neither** is the court liable to bring a sin-offering **for idol worship unless** the judges **issue a ruling with regard to a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet*, and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *mishna* extends this principle: *v'chen hamashiach* — the *Kohen Gadol* likewise brings his special *korban* only for that category of sin which would ordinarily obligate an individual in a *chatas*. Similarly, *v'lo b'avodah zarah ad she-yiru b'davar she-zidono karet v'shgegato chatas* — even the special collective *korban* for *avodah zarah* (the *par l'olah* and *se'ir l'chatas* brought when the *Beit Din HaGadol*'s erroneous ruling is followed) applies only to a form of *avodah zarah* for which willful violation would incur *karet* and unintentional violation a *chatas*. By contrast, an act such as hugging or kissing an idol — which *Rashi* identifies as expressing fealty rather than constituting actual worship — carries only a simple *lav* and would not ordinarily require a sin offering. If the *Beit Din* ruled erroneously on such a matter and people followed, no special *korban* would be brought.

## Karet Limitation

### Avodah Zarah Karet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִנָּלַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the statement in the mishna that the court is liable only for a ruling with regard to a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet* and for whose unwitting violation one is liable to bring a sin-offering, the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*?

The *Gemara* now asks: *menalan* — what is the source for the requirement that the *Beit Din*'s special *korban* applies only to sins for which *karet* is the penalty for willful violation?

### Arayot Deriv.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״עָלֶיהָ״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״עָלֶיהָ״. מָה לְהַלָּן – דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כָּאן – דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The word **"that" is stated here** in the verse: "And the sin that they sinned" (Leviticus 4:14), with regard to the offering brought by the court for the transgression of the public based on their erroneous ruling, **and** the word **"that" is stated there** in the verse: "And you shall not take a woman to her sister, to be a rival to her, to uncover her nakedness, that is in her lifetime" (Leviticus 18:18), with regard to the prohibition against marrying two sisters. It is derived: **Just as there** it is **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering, so too here,** the reference is to **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

A *baraita* is cited: *Rebbe omer* — *Rebbe* derives this from a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *aleha*. In the *parsha* of *Beit Din HaGadol*, the verse uses the word *aleha* (*v'nodah hachatas asher chatu aleha*). The same word *aleha* appears in the *parsha* of *arayot* (*l'galot ervatah aleha*). Just as there — by *arayot* — the relevant sin is one for which willful violation incurs *karet* and unintentional violation incurs a *chatas*, so too here: the *Beit Din*'s *korban* applies only to such a category of sin. The *Gemara* notes that the *karet* dimension is clear from the *arayot* context; the requirement that unintentional violation produce a *chatas* is what links the two cases together. *Ashken tzibbur* — fine, that establishes the rule for the *Beit Din HaGadol*.

### Nasi Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן צִבּוּר, מָשִׁיחַ מְנָלַן? ״לְאַשְׁמַת הַעָם״, הֲרֵי מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר. נָשִׂיא? יָלֵיף ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״, כְּתִיב גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת מִכׇּל מִצְוֹת ה׳״, וּכְתִיב בְּצִבּוּר: ״וְעָשׂוּ אַחַת מִכׇּל מִצְוֹת״ – מָה צִבּוּר – דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף נָשִׂיא – דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **We found** a source for the court bringing an offering for a transgression of the general **public** based on their erroneous ruling. **From where do we** derive that this is the *halakha* for **an anointed** priest? The Gemara answers: It is derived from the verse: "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that the status of **an anointed** priest **is like** that of the transgression of the general **public.** The Gemara asks: From where is it derived that this is the *halakha* with regard to the **king?** The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **derives** a verbal analogy between the term **"commandments"** written with regard to a king and the term **"commandments"** written with regard to the general public. **It is written with regard to a king: "And he performed one of all the commandments of the Lord** his God that are not to be performed" (Leviticus 4:22), **and it is written with regard to** the general **public: "And performed one of all the commandments** of the Lord that are not to be performed" (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as** with regard to the general **public** there is liability only for **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering, so too,** in the case of **a king** there is liability only for **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *Gemara* then proceeds through each of the parties mentioned in *Perek Daled* of *Vayikra*. That chapter opens (*Perek 4, Pasuk 1*) with *nefesh ki techeta bishgaga mikol mitzvot Hashem* — introducing the general category of *chatas* — and then separately treats the *Kohen Gadol* (Pasuk 2 through 12), *Beit Din HaGadol* (Pasuk 13 through 21), the *nasi* (Pasuk 22 through 26), and finally the ordinary *yachid* (Pasuk 27 to the end of the chapter).

For the *Kohen Gadol*: the Torah says the *Kohen Gadol* sins *l'ashmat ha'am* — his sin is like the sin of the congregation. Therefore *harei mashiach k'tzibbur* — his *korban* follows the same rule as that of the *Beit Din HaGadol*, namely only for sins of *karet*.

For the *nasi*: *nasi yalif mitzvot mitzvot* — a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *mitzvot*. By the *nasi* the verse says *v'asah achat mikol mitzvot Hashem*, and by the *tzibbur* it similarly says *v'asu achat mikol mitzvot*. Just as the *tzibbur*'s *korban* — already established through the *aleha* link — applies only to sins of *karet* and *chatas*, so too the *nasi*: *af nasi davar shezidono karet ushgegato chatas*.

### Yachid Chatas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָחִיד? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״, וְיִלְמַד תַּחְתּוֹן מֵעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: From where is it derived that **an individual** is liable to bring a sin-offering only for a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*? The Gemara answers: **The verse** with regard to an individual **states: "And if one soul** from among the common people shall sin unwittingly" (Leviticus 4:27). The verse begins with the conjunction "and," represented by the letter *vav*. This indicates that **one shall derive** the *halakha* with regard to the **lower** verse, i.e., the verse written later in the passage, **from** that which is written in the **upper** verses, i.e., those written earlier. Just as in those verses earlier in the passage, addressing the public, priest, and king, there is liability only for a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*, so too, in the case of the individual addressed in this verse, there is liability only for a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*.

For the ordinary *yachid*: the *Gemara* notes that the verse introducing the *yachid*'s sin offering begins with *v'im nefesh achat techeta*, and the *vav* — *v'im* — links it to everything above. Since all the preceding categories were established to apply only to sins of *karet*, the ordinary *chatas* of an individual is likewise limited to such sins. This then works reciprocally: the individual *chatas* confirms that the class of sins punishable by *karet* is also the class for which an ordinary individual brings a *chatas* — and this is precisely the standard that all these special *korbanot* require.

The *Gemara* then raises an objection to this chain of reasoning. It works, the *Gemara* notes, according to *Rebbe*, who uses the word *aleha* in a *gezeirah shavah* to import *karet* from *arayot*. But the *Rabbanan* who require the *aleha* there to teach a separate *halacha* relating to *arayot* and *tzarot* and *yibum* — those *Rabbanan* cannot use that *aleha* for this purpose.

### AZ Yachid Se'ir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ. בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְפִי שֶׁיָּצְאָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָדוּן בְּעַצְמָהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין אֲפִילּוּ עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מֵעֵינֵי״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״מֵעֵינֵי״. מָה לְהַלָּן דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כָּאן דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Neither** is the court liable to bring a sin-offering **for idol worship unless** the judges **issue a ruling** with regard to a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*, and for whose unwitting violation one is liable to bring a sin-offering. The Gemara asks: **In** the case of **idol worship, from where do we** derive this *halakha*? It is derived **as the Sages taught: Due to** the fact **that idol worship left** the category of unwitting transgressions **to be discussed by itself** (see Numbers, chapter 15), one **might** have thought **that** the court and the priest **would be liable even** if it is **not** for **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.** The *baraita* continues: Therefore, the term **"from the eyes of" is stated here,** with regard to idol worship (Numbers 15:24), **and "from the eyes of" is stated there,** with regard to an unwitting communal sin-offering for all other mitzvot (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as there** the court is liable only for **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering, so too here,** the court is liable only for **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

The discussion turns to *avodah zarah*. There is a separate *parsha* — *Bamidbar Perek 15* — which deals with the *korbanot* for *avodah zarah*, and this *parsha* applies to different categories: the *ציbbur* (verses dealing with *me'einei ha'eida*), and the *yachid* (*v'im nefesh achat techeta bishgaga*, who brings a *se'irah*, a she-goat). The *Gemara* establishes that the *Kohen Gadol* and *nasi* are included in the individual's category of *nefesh achat*, so that all of them — the ordinary individual, the *Kohen Gadol*, and the *nasi* — bring a *se'ir* for *avodah zarah*.

How do we know that the *tzibbur*'s special *korban* for *avodah zarah* is limited to *karet*-level offenses? The *Gemara* uses a *me'einei–me'einei* link: the verse in the *avodah zarah* context of *Bamidbar* 15 says *v'ne'elam davar me'einei ha'eida*, using the word *me'einei*; the verse by the *Beit Din HaGadol*'s standard *korban* in *Vayikra* 4 likewise uses the word *me'einei*. The *me'einei–me'einei* link connects the *avodah zarah korban* of the *tzibbur* to the *Beit Din HaGadol*'s standard *korban*, which is already established as applying only to sins of *karet*. Thus: *mah lehalan davar shezidono karet v'shgegato chatas, af kan davar shezidono karet v'shgegato chatas*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן צִבּוּר. יָחִיד, נָשִׂיא, מָשִׁיחַ מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת״, אֶחָד יָחִיד וְאֶחָד נָשִׂיא וְאֶחָד מָשִׁיחַ כּוּלָּן בִּכְלַל ״נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת״ הֵן, וְיִלְמַד תַּחְתּוֹן מִן הָעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **We found** a source for the court bringing an offering for a transgression of the general **public** based on their erroneous ruling; but **from where do we** derive that this is the *halakha* for **an individual, a king,** and **an anointed** priest who engage in idol worship? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "And if one soul** sins unwittingly, then he shall offer a female goat of the first year as a sin-offering" (Numbers 15:27). And with regard to **an individual, a king, and an anointed** priest, **they all are included in the category of "one soul." And one shall derive** the *halakha* with regard to that which is written in the **lower** verse, i.e., the verses concerning an individual, a king, and an anointed priest, **from** that which is written in the **upper** verse, i.e., the verse concerning the transgression of the general public.

That resolves the *tzibbur*'s *avodah zarah* case. But how do we know the same for the *yachid*, the *nasi*, and the *Kohen Gadol* in the *avodah zarah* context? The *Gemara* answers: *amar kra v'im nefesh achas* — the verse *nefesh achas* groups all three together, and then *v'yelamed tachton min ha'elyon* — the lower section learns from the upper. Once *karet* is established for the *tzibbur*'s *avodah zarah korban* via the *me'einei–me'einei* link, the individual cases follow.

The *Gemara* then notes a difficulty: this entire chain only works for the *Rabbanan* who cannot use the *aleha* link, if there is an alternative way to introduce *karet* into the *avodah zarah parsha* itself — without depending on *arayot*. This alternative comes from *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, who taught his son from the verse *torah achas yihiyeh lachem la'oseh bishgaga* (Bamidbar 15:29). Immediately following the *avodah zarah* passages, the Torah says this "one Torah" for the one who sins inadvertently. Just before, it says *v'hanefesh asher ta'aseh b'yad ramah… v'nichretah hanefesh* — the one who acts presumptuously incurs *karet*. Once *karet* appears in this *parsha*, it can be propagated: just as *avodah zarah* done willfully incurs *karet*, unintentional *avodah zarah* incurs a *chatas*, and the *torah achas* generalizes this principle across all categories. *Huksha kol hatorah kullaha l'avodas kochavim* — the entire Torah is compared to *avodah zarah*: just as willful *avodah zarah* incurs *karet* and unintentional *avodah zarah* incurs a *chatas*, so too for every sin in this framework.

### Torah Achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִדְּמַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי לִבְרֵיהּ: ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה וְגוֹ׳״ – הוּקְּשָׁה כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כָּאן שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **They derive it from** that **which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught to his son** based on the verse: **"There shall be one Torah for you for the one who acts unwittingly. And the soul that performs with an upraised hand,** he blasphemes the Lord; and that soul shall be cut off [*venikhreta*] from among his people" (Numbers 15:29–30). The Sages understood the phrase "with an upraised hand" to be referring to idol worship (see *Sanhedrin* 99a). **The entire Torah is juxtaposed to idol worship: Just as idol worship** is a prohibition for **which one is liable for its intentional** violation to receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation to bring **a sin-offering, so too here,** for the entire Torah **one is liable for its intentional** violation to receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *torah achas* verse thus serves as a general principle unifying the entire structure: whatever sin is in this category — whether by an individual, the *nasi*, the *Kohen Gadol*, or the *Beit Din HaGadol* — it is only a sin for which *karet* applies to willful violation and a *chatas* applies to unintentional violation. And then, in the manner described above, the *ציbbur*'s rule can also be learned back upward from the individual cases once *karet* is anchored below: *tzibur menalan yalif elyon mitachton*.

### Rav Hilkiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּמַפֵּיק לַהּ לְ״עָלֶיהָ״ לִגְזֵרָה שָׁוָה, כְּדַאֲמַרַן. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן דְּמַפְּקִי לַהּ לְ״עָלֶיהָ״ לַעֲרָיוֹת וְצָרוֹת, דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת – מְנָא לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **This works out well according to the one who derives** the *halakha* by using the term **"that" for a verbal analogy** to teach that the court is liable only for a ruling with regard to a matter for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*, **in accordance with** that **which we stated. But according to the Rabbis, who derive** the *halakha* by using the term **"that" to** teach the *halakhot* of **those with whom relations are forbidden and rival wives** with regard to a *yevama* (see *Yevamot* 3b), **from where do they** derive the *halakha* of **a matter** for **whose intentional** violation one is liable to receive ***karet* and** for **whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering** in the case of the transgression of the general public?

The *Gemara* then asks: if *torah achas* is used to establish the *karet* standard, what was *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaching it to address? The answer is that *torah achas* is needed for a different purpose, as derived in a *baraita*: since we find that the Torah differentiates between a multitude and individuals in the case of intentional *avodah zarah* — a city that worships *avodah zarah* is executed by the sword and its property is destroyed, while an individual worshipper is stoned and their property is preserved — one might have thought (*yachol*) that there would similarly be distinct *korbanot* for unintentional *avodah zarah* by a city, as opposed to by ordinary individuals. The *torah achas* teaches otherwise: whether it is one individual or an entire city short of the full *tzibbur*, all bring the same *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חִלְקִיָּה מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא: טַעְמָא דְּלֹא חָלַק הַכָּתוּב. הָא חָלַק, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא נַחְלוֹק. מַאי נַיְיתֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥilkiya of Hagronya objects to it: The reason** that both individuals and the general public are liable to bring the same offering for unwitting idol worship is **that the verse did not distinguish** between them. **But had the verse distinguished** between them, **I would say: Let us distinguish** between individuals and the general public, and the general public would bring a different offering for their unwitting idol worship. **What** different offering **shall they bring?**

*Rav Hilkiya me-Hagronya* challenges this: what distinct *korban* could we possibly have expected a city to bring? Running through the possibilities — a *par* (already the *tzibbur*'s *korban* for ordinary sins), a *par l'olah* and *se'ir l'chatas* (already the *tzibbur*'s *korban* for *avodah zarah*), a *se'ir* (already the *nasi*'s *korban* for other sins), a *se'irah* (already the individual's *korban* for *avodah zarah*) — there is nothing distinctly left for a city's *korban*. So what exactly was the *hava amina* that needed to be ruled out?

### AZ Korbanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ? סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: צִבּוּר מַיְיתֵי פַּר לְעוֹלָה וְשָׂעִיר לְחַטָּאת, הָנֵי נַיְיתֵי אִיפְּכָא – פַּר לְחַטָּאת וְשָׂעִיר לְעוֹלָה. אִי נָמֵי: צָרִיךְ וְאֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Why** do you say that the verse that equates the offerings of the individuals and the public is **not necessary?** Were it not for the verse, it might **enter your mind to say:** The general **public brings a bull for a burnt-offering and a goat for a sin-offering, but this** majority of a city that unwittingly engaged in idol worship **shall bring the opposite: A bull for a sin-offering and a goat for a burnt-offering. Alternatively,** perhaps the majority of a city that unwittingly engaged in idol worship **must** bring an offering, **but it has no remedy,** because there is no unique offering that it can bring. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that there is no distinction between many people and individuals in this case.

The *Gemara* responds: the *hava amina* would have been that a city brings the same elements as the *tzibbur* — a *par l'olah* and a *se'ir l'chatas* — but in reversed order, bringing a *par l'chatas* and a *se'ir l'olah*. Or alternatively, we might simply not have known which *korban* they would bring, and the *torah achas* informs us that they bring exactly what an individual brings. Thus *ka mashma lan* — the *torah achas* teaches that even for a city committing *avodah zarah* inadvertently, the rule follows that of individuals.

In any case, the conclusion reached by all opinions is that all these *korbanot* — the *par v'se'ir* of the *tzibbur* for *avodah zarah*, the *se'ir* for the individual, *nasi*, and *Kohen Gadol* — are written in the context of *avodah zarah*, and the *karet* requirement is established and propagated throughout. The *Gemara* identifies the *avodah zarah* context of these verses through three independent derivations: (1) *kol hamitzvot ha'eileh* refers to the *mitzvah* that is equivalent to all *mitzvot*, which is *avodah zarah*; (2) the verse mentions both *asher diber Hashem el Moshe* and *asher tziva Hashem aleichem b'yad Moshe*, and the only *mitzvah* heard both directly from God and transmitted through *Moshe* is *lo yihiyeh lecha* — the prohibition of *avodah zarah*; (3) the verse mentions that God commanded *min hayom… l'doroteichem* — from the first day of commandment onward — implying the very first *mitzvah*. The *Gemara* does note, however, that this third derivation is weak: the first *mitzvot* were actually received at *Marah*, before *Har Sinai*. So the better explanations remain the first two. Ultimately, one way or another, *karet* is introduced into the *avodah zarah* context and propagated throughout all the linked cases.

## Chatas & Oleh V.

### Mikdash Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאֵין מְבִיאִים אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court is **not liable** to bring a bull as an unwitting communal sin-offering for issuing a ruling with regard **to a positive** mitzva **or a prohibition related to** the defiling of **the Temple** by one being there while ritually impure, or the defiling of its sacrificial foods by one partaking of them while ritually impure. There is a positive mitzva to remove impure people from the Temple, and there is a prohibition against entering the Temple while in a state of ritual impurity. **And one does not bring a provisional guilt-offering for a positive** mitzva **or a prohibition related to** the defiling of **the Temple** or its sacrificial foods.

The *Gemara* now introduces a second limiting principle. In addition to the *karet* requirement, all these special *korbanot* apply only when the underlying sin would normally obligate a *chatas kevuah* — a fixed-value sin offering. Where the sin ordinarily obligates a *chatas* that is an *oleh v'yored* — a variable-cost offering whose value shifts with the sinner's financial means — the *Beit Din HaGadol* does not bring its special *korban*. The *mishna* states: *ein chayavin al asei v'lo sa'asei sheb'mikdash* — the *Beit Din* is not liable for ruling on a positive or negative commandment relating to the *mikdash*. An example of a positive commandment would be expelling *temei'im* from the camp; a negative commandment would be the prohibition against entering the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah*.

### No AT Mikdash

An individual who is uncertain whether they ate *kodashim* while *tamei* — a case of *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* — would not bring an *asham taluy*, since *asham taluy* applies only where the underlying sin, if established, would require a *chatas kevuah*. An *oleh v'yored* does not support an *asham taluy*.

### Niddah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל חַיָּיבִין עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּנִּדָּה, וּמְבִיאִים אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּנִּדָּה. אֵיזוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבַּנִּדָּה? פְּרוֹשׁ מִן הַנִּדָּה. וּמִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה? אַל תָּבֹא עַל הַנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** the court **is liable** to bring a bull **for a positive** mitzva **or** for **a prohibition that is related to a menstruating woman, and one brings a provisional guilt-offering for a positive** mitzva **or** for **a prohibition that is related to a menstruating woman. What is** the **positive mitzva that is related to a menstruating woman? Distance** yourself **from the menstruating woman.** If a man engages in intercourse with a woman and she begins menstruating, he is required to separate from her. **And what is the prohibition? Do not engage in intercourse with** a woman whom you know is **a menstruating woman.**

The *mishna* then provides a positive contrast: *aval chayavin al asei v'lo sa'asei shebeniddah* — the *Beit Din HaGadol* does bring its *korban* for an erroneous ruling about positive and negative commandments relating to *niddah*. The positive commandment is *perosh min haniddah* — separating from a woman approaching the time of her period; the negative commandment is the prohibition against intercourse with a *niddah*. The case the *mishna* envisions is one where the *Beit Din* ruled incorrectly and people followed — and where, for instance, a couple was engaged in relations at the moment the woman became a *niddah*, so the positive commandment's violation led directly into the negative's violation. This is a case of *karet* with a *chatas kevuah*, and the *Beit Din*'s special *korban* applies. Similarly, an individual uncertain whether such a violation occurred would bring an *asham taluy*.

It is noteworthy that the *mishna* does not actually quote the *pesukim* for these *mitzvot*. One might have expected textual citations. There is also a broader conceptual question lurking here, about the relationship between *hilchot niddah* and the frameworks of *dinei arayot* versus *dinei tumah* — but that discussion is for another time.

### Ve'Ashem Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּצִבּוּר לָא מְחַיְּיבִי קׇרְבָּן בְּעָלְמָא, וְיָחִיד אָשָׁם תָּלוּי נָמֵי לָא? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: נֶאֱמַר ״וְאָשֵׁם״ בְּחַטָּאת וּבְאָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וְנֶאֱמַר ״וְאָשֵׁמוּ״ בְּצִבּוּר. מָה ״וְאָשֵׁם״ בְּיָחִיד – בְּחַטָּאת קְבוּעָה, אַף ״וְאָשֵׁמוּ״ בְּצִבּוּר – בְּחַטָּאת קְבוּעָה, וּמָה צִבּוּר בְּחַטָּאת קְבוּעָה, אַף אָשָׁם תָּלוּי נָמֵי אֵין בָּא אֶלָּא עַל סְפֵק חַטָּאת קְבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? That is, from where is it derived **that** the general **public is not liable** to bring any **offering at all** for a sin like the defiling of the Temple, which carries no liability for an individual to bring a fixed sin-offering for violating it unwittingly, **and** from where is it derived that **an individual too** is **not** liable to bring **a provisional guilt-offering** for the defiling of the Temple? **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says:** The term **"and he is guilty" is stated with regard to a sin-offering** (Leviticus 4:27) **and with regard to a provisional guilt-offering** (Leviticus 5:17), **and** the similar term: **"And they are guilty"** (Leviticus 4:13), **is stated with regard to** the general **public. Just as** the term **"and he is guilty"** that is stated **with regard to an individual** is **concerning** a transgression for which one is liable to bring **a fixed sin-offering,** as it is written there, **so too,** the term **"and they are guilty"** that is stated **with regard to** the general **public** is **concerning** a transgression for which one is liable to bring **a fixed sin-offering.** For the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering, not a fixed offering. **And just as with regard to** a transgression performed by the general **public** the liability is to bring **a fixed sin-offering, so too, a provisional guilt-offering comes only for uncertainty** with regard to a transgression that renders one obligated to bring **a fixed sin-offering.**

The *Gemara* asks: *minani milei* — what is the source that the *Beit Din*'s special *korban* and an individual's *asham taluy* apply only when the underlying sin is a *chatas kevuah*?

*Rabbi Yitzbar Avdimi* says: a *gezerah shavah* on the word *v'ashem*. In the passage of the ordinary individual *chatas* (*Vayikra* 4), the verse reads *v'im nefesh techeta v'astah achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashem* — it says *v'ashem*. In the passage of *asham taluy*, the verse reads *v'nefesh ki techeta v'astah achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'lo yada v'ashem* — also *v'ashem*. And in the passage of the *tzibbur*'s *korban*, the verse reads *v'asu achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashemu* — similarly *v'ashemu*.

The link between these three *v'ashem* formulations establishes a common requirement: just as the individual's *chatas* (the base case in *Perek 4 of Vayikra*) is a *chatas kevuah*, so too the *korban* of the *tzibbur* requires a *chatas kevuah* — *mah v'ashem b'yachid b'chatas kevuah, af v'ashemu b'tzibbur b'chatas kevuah*. And by the same logic, the *asham taluy*, which also uses *v'ashem*, is brought only in cases of doubt regarding what would otherwise be a *chatas kevuah* — *af asham taluy nami ein ba ela al safek chatas kevuah*.

### OV Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִי הָכִי, קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֶאְשַׁם לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״! דָּנִין (״אָשֵׁם״ מִן ״אָשֵׁמוּ״) [״אָשֵׁמוּ״ מִן ״אָשֵׁם״], וְאֵין דָּנִין ״אָשֵׁם״ מִן ״יֶאְשַׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** This is the source for the fact that there is no liability to bring a sin-offering if the general public defiles the Temple or its sacrificial foods, and individuals are not liable to bring a provisional guilt-offering for those transgressions. The Sages **say: If so,** and the derivation is based on that term, this is difficult, as **isn't it also written** with regard to **a sliding-scale offering: "And it shall be when he shall be guilty of one of these matters"** (Leviticus 5:5)? The Gemara responds: **One derives** the meaning of **"they are guilty" from** the meaning of **"he is guilty," but one does not derive** the meaning of **"he is guilty" from** the meaning of **"he shall be guilty,"** even though both are stated in the singular.

The *Gemara* raises a powerful objection: if *v'ashem* is what defines membership in this "club" of *chatas kevuah* cases, then note that the *oleh v'yored* passage — precisely the category you are trying to *exclude* — also uses the very same word *v'ashem*. The verse in the *oleh v'yored* passage (*Vayikra* 5) reads: *v'hayah ki ye'asham l'achat me'eileh* — here the word appears as *ye'asham* (with a *yud* prefix). More pointedly, the *Gemara* notes that the *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* case — the archetypal example of *oleh v'yored* that the *mishna* explicitly excludes — contains the exact word *v'ashem*: *v'hu tamei v'ashem*. The *Gemara* thus challenges: how can one use *v'ashem* to create a link that excludes *oleh v'yored* when *oleh v'yored* uses the identical term?

The *Gemara* attempts a first answer: *danin ashemu me'asham v'ein danin asham mi'ye'asham* — the forms *ashem* and *ashemu* are sufficiently similar to create a link between themselves, but the form *ye'asham* (with the *yud* prefix) is different enough to be excluded. The *Gemara* immediately rejects this: what practical difference does that distinction make? The very logic of *gezeirah shavah* permits linking words that are not identical — the *Gemara* cites the *baraita* from *d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* concerning *tzara'at*, where the *kohen* "returns" and "comes," and even though those are distinct words, *zu hi shiva zu hi vi'a* — the link is made because they refer to the same idea. And in any case, the *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* passage uses *v'ashem* — the exact word — so the form distinction cannot do the work needed.

### Double Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דָּנִין ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּ״מִצְוֹת ה׳״ מִן ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּ״מִצְוֹת ה׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי לְרַב פָּפָּא: וְנֵילַף ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּנְשִׂיאַת עָוֹן מִן ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּנְשִׂיאַת עָוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: One derives** the meaning of: **"And he is guilty," and: "The commandments of the Lord"** (Leviticus 5:17), which are written with regard to a provisional guilt-offering, **from** the meaning of: **"And they are guilty," and: "The commandments of the Lord"** (Leviticus 4:13), which are written with regard to the unwitting communal sin-offering. The phrase: The commandments of the Lord, is not written with regard to the sliding-scale offering. **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said to Rav Pappa: Let us derive** the meaning of: **"And he is guilty and bearing of iniquity"** (see Leviticus 5:1, 4), **from** the meaning of: **"And he is guilty and bearing of iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:17).

*Rav Papa* offers the resolution: *danin v'ashem umitzvot Hashem min v'ashem umitzvot Hashem* — the link is not based on *v'ashem* alone, but on a double link combining *v'ashem* and *mitzvot Hashem*. In the *tzibbur*'s passage (*Vayikra* 4:13), the verse says *mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashemu*. In the individual *chatas* passage (*Vayikra* 4:27), the verse says *achat mimitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashem*. Both passages contain both *mitzvot Hashem* and *v'ashem*. The *oleh v'yored* passages do not contain this double combination, and therefore they are not part of this exclusive club.

*Rav Shimi bar Ashi* raises a challenge to *Rav Papa*: by *oleh v'yored*, the text uses both *v'ashem* and *v'nasa avono* — a double link of its own. If the principle is that a double link creates a special category, then why not link the *asham taluy* to the *oleh v'yored* (via *v'ashem* and *v'nasa avono*) rather than to the *chatas kevuah* cases?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דָּנִין ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּ״מִצְוֹת ה׳ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה״ – מִ״וְאָשֵׁם וּמִצְוֹת ה׳ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה״. וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ שְׁמִיעַת קוֹל וּבִיטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם וְטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶם ״וְאָשֵׁם״ וּ״מִצְוֹת ה׳ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: One derives** the meaning of **"and he is guilty" and "the commandments of the Lord that shall not be done"** written with regard to a provisional guilt-offering, **from** the meaning of: **"And he is guilty," and: "The commandments of the Lord that shall not be done"** (Leviticus 4:27), written with regard to a fixed sin-offering. **And** the cases of **hearing of a voice,** i.e., an oath of testimony, **and a statement of the lips,** i.e., an oath on an utterance, **and the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, for which one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering, **shall not prove** otherwise, **as it is not stated concerning them: And he is guilty, and: The commandments of the Lord that shall not be done.**

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* provides the final answer: the decisive link is a triple combination — *v'ashem*, *mitzvot Hashem*, and *asher lo te'asenah* — all appearing together. Comparing the *tzibbur* verse (*v'asu achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashemu*) and the individual *chatas* verse (*v'im nefesh techeta v'astah achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah v'ashem*): these phrases are essentially identical, drawn from the same *parsha*, and constitute an unmistakably tight verbal parallel. The *oleh v'yored* and *asham taluy* passages lack this precise combination. The *Gemara* acknowledges, however, that it remains somewhat unclear at the end of the day precisely how *asham taluy* is linked to the *chatas kevuah* camp rather than to *oleh v'yored*; the primary derivation here is for the *korban* of the *tzibbur*, which is clearly linked to the *chatas kevuah* category by this multiple verbal parallel.

The *mishna*'s final formulation confirms: *ein chayavin al shemiat kol v'al bitui sefatayim v'al tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — these three are the classic cases of *oleh v'yored*, explicitly excluded from the *Beit Din*'s special *korban*. The inclusion of *niddah* as a case that does obligate the *Beit Din* — being a *chatas kevuah* carrying *karet* — is thus the positive counterpart demonstrating the rule.

## Nasi & Oleh V.

### Nasi Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל שְׁמִיעַת קוֹל, וְעַל בִּיטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם, וְעַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. וְהַנָּשִׂיא כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court **is not liable for** issuing a ruling with regard to **hearing of a voice,** i.e., a false oath of testimony taken by a witness who refuses to testify on behalf of a litigant who demands that he testify, **or for an utterance of the lips,** i.e., a false oath stated about some matter, **or for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. **And the** status of the **king is like** that of the court, and he is exempt from bringing an offering in those cases; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The next *mishna* introduces the *nasi*: *v'hanasi kayotzei bahem* — the *nasi* is like the *Beit Din* in this respect, and does not bring a *korban* for any of the three *oleh v'yored* categories. *Divrei Rabbi Yose HaGlili* — this is the position of *Rabbi Yose HaGlili*.

### R. Akiva Pos.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַנָּשִׂיא חַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן חוּץ מִשְּׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, שֶׁהַמֶּלֶךְ לֹא דָּן וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ [לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ].
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: The king is liable in all of** those cases **except for** the case of **hearing of a voice,** and even in that case, his exemption is not intrinsic to the mitzva. Rather, it is for technical reasons, **as the king neither judges** others **nor** do others **judge him; he does not testify nor** do others **testify against him.** Therefore, a demand that he testify is not applicable.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: *hanasi chayav bekulan chutz mishemiat hakol* — the *nasi* is liable for all the *oleh v'yored* categories, and does bring the variable *korban* for each of them, with one exception: *shemiat kol* — the case of being sworn to testify.

### Court Testimony

The reasoning for *Rabbi Akiva*'s exception is *shehamelech lo dan v'lo danin oto, lo me'id v'lo me'idin oto* — a king neither judges nor is judged, neither testifies nor is compelled to testify. The *oleh v'yored* case of *shemiat kol* involves being bound by an oath to provide testimony; since a king cannot be summoned to court to testify, that category simply does not apply to him, and he therefore brings no *korban* for it. But the other two categories — *bitui sefatayim* and *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* — do apply to him, and he brings the corresponding *oleh v'yored* like anyone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה כִי יֶאְשַׁם לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״, כֹּל שֶׁמִּתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחַת – מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן, וְשֶׁאֵין מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחַת – אֵין מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ulla said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili?** It is as **the verse states** with regard to a sliding-scale offering: **"And it shall be when he shall be guilty of one of these matters"** (Leviticus 5:5), indicating that **anyone who becomes liable in** every **one** of the instances for which one brings a sliding-scale offering **becomes liable in all of** those instances; **and anyone who does not become liable in** every **one** of the instances to bring a sliding-scale offering **does not become liable in any of** those instances. Since the king is exempt from taking an oath of testimony, he is exempt in the other cases as well.

The *Gemara* asks: why does *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* exclude the *nasi* from all three? One might suggest it is because the verse *v'hayah ki ye'asham l'achat me'eileh* — listing all three *oleh v'yored* obligations together — implies that liability for one entails liability for all: *kol shemis'chayev b'achat mis'chayev bekulan v'she'eino mis'chayev b'achat eino mis'chayev bekulan*. Since a *nasi* cannot be obligated in *shemiat kol*, he is excluded from the entire framework. The *Gemara* challenges this reading: perhaps *le'achat me'eileh* means that being obligated in even one of the three is sufficient to bring a *korban*, such that the *nasi* would still bring for the other two. The *Gemara* concedes that the exegetical basis from this verse is uncertain.

### Wealth Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי מֵהָכָא, דְּתַנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תַגִּיעַ יָדוֹ״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תַשִּׂיג יָדוֹ״ – מִי שֶׁבָּא לִידֵי עֲנִיּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת. יָצָא נָשִׂיא וּמָשִׁיחַ, שֶׁאֵין בָּאִין לִידֵי עֲנִיּוּת,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is from here, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yirmeya would say: It is stated: "And if his means suffice not"** (Leviticus 5:7), **and it is stated: "And if his means not suffice"** (Leviticus 5:11), indicating that the sliding-scale offering applies only to **one who can come to** a state of **poverty and wealth.** This serves to **exclude a king and an anointed** priest, **who cannot come to** a state of **poverty.**

The *Gemara* then establishes the *tanna*'s actual reasoning, citing a *baraita* from *Rabbi Meir*: the *oleh v'yored* passages use the language *im lo tasig yado* (if he cannot afford) — the entire framework of variable-cost offerings presupposes a person who could be either wealthy or poor. *Mi sheba lidei aniyut v'ashirut* — only someone who may experience both poverty and wealth is within the scope of *oleh v'yored*.

### Always Wealthy

*Yatza nasi umashiach* — the *nasi* and the *Kohen Gadol* are excluded because they never enter into poverty. *She'ein ba'in lidei aniyut* — they are structurally prevented from becoming poor.

### Nasi Elohav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשִׂיא – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת מִכׇּל מִצְוֹת ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו״. מִי שֶׁאֵין עַל גַּבָּיו אֶלָּא ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו. מָשִׁיחַ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו״, שֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו בְּנוֹי, בְּכֹחַ, בְּחָכְמָה וּבְעוֹשֶׁר. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אֵין לוֹ גַּדְּלֵהוּ מִשֶּׁל אֶחָיו? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו אֲשֶׁר יוּצַק עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״ – גַּדְּלֵהוּ מֵאֶחָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The **king** cannot become poor, **as it is written** concerning him: **"And he performed one of all the mitzvot of the Lord his God"** (Leviticus 4:22), referring to the king as **one who has only the Lord his God upon him.** He is greater than the entire nation and is not a poor person dependent on others. **An anointed** priest cannot become poor, **as it is written: "And the priest that is greatest among his brethren"** (Leviticus 21:10), meaning **that he is greater than his brethren in beauty, in power, in wisdom, and in wealth,** not a poor person. **Others say: From where** is it derived **that if** the High Priest **does not have** personal wealth, one should **make him great from** the property **of his brethren? The verse states: "And the priest that is greatest among his brethren upon whose head the anointing oil is poured"** (Leviticus 21:10), from which it is derived: **Make him great from** the property **of his brethren,** who will provide him with enough property to render him wealthy.

The textual source for the *nasi*: the verse describing his sin reads *v'asah achat mikol mitzvot Hashem elohav* — "the commandments of Hashem his God." The unusual phrase "his God" — as if God is specifically *his* — is interpreted to mean: *mi she'ein al gabav ela Hashem elohav* — he is a person over whom no human being stands in authority. This implies absolute primacy, not merely in political terms but in all respects, including wealth. The *nasi* must be the wealthiest person in the realm, such that no one stands above him even in material standing.

### KG Gadol

For the *Kohen Gadol*, the source is *v'hakohen hagadol me'echav* — the *Kohen Gadol* must be *gadol me'achav*: greater than his brethren in appearance, in strength, in wisdom, and in wealth. If a *Kohen Gadol* is appointed who does not possess sufficient wealth, *acherim* rule that his fellow *kohanim* must contribute to make him wealthy: *gadlohu mishel achav* — they elevate him from their own means, fulfilling the verse *v'hakohen hagadol me'achav*, make him greater from his brothers.

Thus the debate between *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* and *Rabbi Akiva* turns on this principle. *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* holds that since *nasi* and *Kohen Gadol* are always wealthy and can never be poor, the *oleh v'yored* framework — which presupposes the possibility of poverty — simply does not apply to them at all. *Rabbi Akiva*, by contrast, holds that they do fall within the *oleh v'yored* framework; the only exclusion is the *nasi*'s inability to be compelled to testify, which removes the *shemiat kol* category for him specifically.
