# Shabbat 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Reshuyot

### Reshut HaYachid

Before moving forward into new material, it is worth briefly reviewing the four *reshuyot* introduced in the *Tosefta* cited on the preceding *daf*, since the Gemara will occupy itself with their elaboration for another day and a half. The first domain, *reshut hayachid*, was defined as a space of four by four *tefachim* with walls ten *tefachim* high — and this holds even when the walls are technically below the enclosed space, as in the case of a post or a pit. There was a noted debate among the *Rishonim* as to whether three walls or four are required.

### Reshut HaRabim

The second domain, *reshut harabim*, was described as a major public square or thoroughfare, though the *Tosefta* itself does not specify exactly how major. Elsewhere we find an additional criterion of sixteen *amot* — approximately twenty-four feet — in width, and the question of what further factors are required becomes highly consequential. Some authorities maintain that the space must serve 600,000 people daily, or must be a major road running directly between cities; other criteria are brought as well. A related and critical debate between the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Yehuda concerns whether walls can surround a *reshut harabim*, or whether we say *ati rabim umvatli mechitza* — the public comes and negates the walls. If we accept the latter view, as we generally do, then an *eruv* cannot function in a genuine biblical *reshut harabim*. The definition of *reshut harabim* is therefore of enormous practical consequence: if we conclude that most contemporary situations constitute a *karmelit* rather than a *reshut harabim*, it both reduces the relevant prohibition to a rabbinic level and permits the construction of an *eruv*.

### Eiruv & Definition

The stakes, then, are twofold: first, whether a given space creates biblical liability or merely rabbinic; and second, whether an *eruv* is valid. These questions turn directly on how strictly or broadly one defines *reshut harabim*.

### Beit Satayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דְּאִית לַהּ מְחִיצּוֹת. וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קַרְפֵּף יוֹתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַּף לְדִירָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוֹר וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוֹרַיִים, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? — מְחִיצָה הִיא, אֶלָּא שֶׁמְחוּסֶּרֶת דָּיוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The valley discussed in the mishna in *Teharot* is unusual, as it refers to **a case where it has partitions** that are ten handbreadths high surrounding it. **And in accordance with that** which **Ulla said** that **Rav Yoḥanan said: An enclosure [*karpef*],** a large courtyard that is not contiguous with the house and does not serve a direct purpose for the house, that is **greater than** a field that produces a crop of **two *se'a*, that was not** originally **surrounded** by a fence **for** the purpose of **residence,** but with a partition to protect his belongings, **and even** if it is as large as a field that produces a crop of one ***kor*,** thirty times the size of a *se'a*, **and even two *kor*,** it is still considered a private domain. **And,** consequently, **one who throws** an object **into it** from the public domain on Shabbat **is liable. What is the reason** for this? **It is a partition** that surrounds the enclosure and its legal status is like that of a partition in every sense, **except that it is lacking residents.** Even though the Rabbis were stringent with regard to this enclosure because of the lack of residents and prohibited carrying in it as if it were a *karmelit*, that does not negate its primary legal status; by Torah law it is a full-fledged private domain. The same is true with regard to the aforementioned valley. The valley is a large area surrounded by partitions erected for the purpose of protection and thereby assumes private domain status.

One additional point from the prior discussion bears mentioning: even a fully walled enclosure may be treated rabbinically as a restricted domain for carrying if it exceeds a certain size — approximately 5,600 square feet, the measurement known as *beit satayim* — and is not used for habitation. What exactly "habitation" means in this context is itself a broad and somewhat abstract question.

## Karmelit Definition

### Karmelit Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכַּרְמְלִית. אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי לָאו כַּרְמְלִית נִינְהוּ? כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְקֶרֶן זָוִית הַסְּמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּזִימְנִין דְּדָחֲקִי בֵּיהּ רַבִּים וְעָיְילִי לְגַוַּהּ, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא נִיחָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתֵּיהּ, כִּי כַרְמְלִית דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In the *Tosefta,* the list of places whose legal status is that of a *karmelit* also includes ***karmelit.*** The Gemara asks: **Aren't they, all** the other places listed there, i.e., a sea, a valley, and a colonnade, **a *karmelit* too?** If so, what is this *karmelit* that is prominently mentioned here? The Gemara answers: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** addition of *karmelit* **was only necessary** in order to teach the case of **a corner adjacent to the public domain, where, although at times the multitudes push** their way **in and enter it, since its use is inconvenient it is considered a *karmelit*.**

The Gemara now turns to the third of the four *reshuyot* — the rabbinic domain, the *karmelit*. The *Tosefta* cited a list of examples falling into this category: an ocean, a valley, and the like, as well as a *karmelit* in the narrow sense, which Rashi identifies as a forest. The difficulty the Gemara raises is terminological: since all these cases belong to the class of *karmelit*, why use the word *karmelit* as one item among them? The answer is that *karmelit* has evolved from a proper noun — the name of a specific type of space — into an abstract category name. The Gemara therefore asks what the *Tosefta* adds by listing the term itself.

The response: *ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rabbi Yochanan* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael and transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — *lo nitzrecha ela le'keren zavit hasmucha lereshut harabim*: the novel case being added is a corner niche adjacent to the *reshut harabim*, which one might not have thought to classify as a *karmelit*.

### Keren Zavit

The *keren zavit* is a corner or intruding niche off the main *reshut harabim*. The Gemara notes that sometimes, when the main street becomes overcrowded, people will push into this corner — *de'avda gav dei de'dachki bei rabim ve'ayli legavei* — yet since it is not used in a convenient and direct manner, *keivan delo nicha tashmishtei*, it retains the status of a *karmelit* rather than a *reshut harabim*.

This introduces a theme that will recur throughout the sugya: the distinction between physical proximity to the *reshut harabim* and functional incorporation into it. The Artscroll edition provides a helpful illustration of the configuration: a main thoroughfare with a small niche or side street set off to the side — not a convenient passage, not part of the primary flow of traffic, and therefore not a *reshut harabim*. In fact, anything that fails to qualify as a *reshut harabim* is a *karmelit*, even if it is closely connected to the *reshut harabim*. This is the principle at work.

### Usage Criterion

An important conceptual distinction emerges here between *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim*. Ownership is irrelevant to both: a *reshut hayachid* can be owned by the public, and a *reshut harabim* can theoretically be privately owned but fully public in use. What does matter — and this is primary for *reshut harabim* in a way that it is not for *reshut hayachid* — is use. The physical criteria of *reshut hayachid* are structural: four by four *tefachim*, walls ten *tefachim* high. But the defining quality of *reshut harabim* is functional: how it is used, how easily it is traversed, and how directly it serves the public.

And so the question the Gemara continues to press is: for spaces that are adjacent to or partially connected with the *reshut harabim*, what threshold of convenient use must they meet before they are absorbed into the *reshut harabim*? That criterion — *nicha tashmishtei*, convenient or easy use — is going to be the organizing principle of the discussions that follow.

## Pillars & Stalls

### Between Pillars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֵּין הָעַמּוּדִין נִידּוֹן כְּכַרְמְלִית. מַאי טַעְמָא? אַף עַל גַּב דְּדָרְסִי בַּהּ רַבִּים, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מִסְתַּגִּי לְהוּ בְּהֶדְיָא — כְּכַרְמְלִית דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Between the pillars** alongside the public domain **is judged like a *karmelit*. What is the reason** for this? **Although the multitudes stride there, since they cannot walk** in it **in a direct manner,** uninterrupted, **it is considered like a *karmelit*.**

The Gemara brings another ruling of Rabbi Yochanan transmitted by Rav Dimi: *bein ha'amudim nidon ke'karmelit* — the space between the pillars is treated as a *karmelit*. This would seem to be what was meant in the *Tosefta* by the term *istavinah* (also rendered *istava* or *istov*), referring to a colonnaded walkway alongside the stores. Rashi clarifies that the *istova* referred specifically to the booths set up in front of the stores, while the pillars themselves formed a kind of colonnaded corridor through which people did walk.

One can visualize this as something akin to the covered colonnaded street of an ancient city — like the Cardo in Jerusalem — where rows of pillars defined a space alongside the storefronts, through which foot traffic moved but with considerably more obstruction than the open road. People do walk between the pillars, but they must navigate around them; it is not the direct, unimpeded flow of the main thoroughfare.

Why then is this space a *karmelit* and not a *reshut harabim*? The Gemara explains: *af al gav de'darsi bei rabbim* — even though the masses do in fact walk there — *keivan de'lo mistagi lehu behedya*, since they do not travel through it *behedya* — directly, straightforwardly, without obstruction or detour — it is like a *karmelit*. The word *behedya* carries the sense of directly, without circumlocution or circuitous maneuvering.

### Booth Area

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אִיצְטְבָא שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הָעַמּוּדִים נִידּוֹן כְּכַרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Yehuda said:** The same is true for **the bench that is before the pillars** upon which the merchants place their wares; it **is judged** to be **like a *karmelit*.**

Rav Zeira then cites Rav Yehuda: *itztava she'lifnei ha'amudim nidon ke'karmelit* — the booth area in front of the pillars is similarly a *karmelit*. This is notable because Rashi had identified the *istavinah* in the *Tosefta* with precisely these booths. In any case, the Gemara's logical structure is as follows: for one who holds that even the space between the pillars — where people actually walk — is a *karmelit*, it follows *kol shekein* that the booth area, where people merely stand and browse, is certainly a *karmelit*. But for one who holds only that the booth area is a *karmelit*, it might be that the space between the pillars, where movement does occur, would qualify as a *reshut harabim* — *d'nicha tashmishtei*, since it is more conveniently used for passage.

One might ask at this point: what kind of use is being measured? Is it specifically travel, or any type of use? A *reshut harabim* can be either a major road or a major public square, so gathering and standing might also in principle constitute the relevant use. But here, in the context of the colonnaded market, the Gemara's language throughout emphasizes movement and passage — *af al gav de'darsi bei rabbim*, the masses trample through it — suggesting that the primary criterion being assessed is convenience as a means of transit.

### Standing vs. Walking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: אֲבָל בֵּין הָעַמּוּדִין דְּזִימְנִין דְּדָרְסִי לֵיהּ רַבִּים, כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Another version** of that statement: **However, between the pillars where, at times, the multitudes stride there is considered like the public domain.**

The Gemara then presents an alternative version (*lishna achrina*) of Rav Zeira's statement, in which the distinction between the booth area and the space between the pillars is made explicit from the outset: the booth area is a *karmelit* because it is a place where people stand; the space between the pillars, in this version, may in fact be a *reshut harabim* because *zimnin de'darsi lah rabbim* — there are times when the masses actually walk through it. The difference between the two versions is subtle but meaningful: the first formulation frames the distinction in terms of degree of ease of use in the abstract, while the second frames it as a categorical distinction between a place of standing versus a place of walking. Whether *reshut harabim* requires specifically a space of travel, or whether any sufficiently convenient and common use qualifies, is a question the Gemara will return to shortly.

## Elevated Structures

### Levaina Zekufa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לְבֵינָה זְקוּפָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְזָרַק וְטָח בְּפָנֶיהָ — חַיָּיב. עַל גַּבָּהּ — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the question to what degree does the use of the multitudes determine whether a specific place is considered a public domain, the Gemara cites the *halakha* that **Rabba bar Sheila said** that **Rav Ḥisda said:** If **an upright brick** was placed **in the public domain and one threw** an object from a distance of four cubits **and he stuck** the object **to its side, he is liable** for throwing in the public domain. But if the object landed **atop** the brick, **he is not liable.** Because the multitudes do not step on the brick, it is not a full-fledged public domain.

Rava bar Shila cites Rav Chisda: *levaina zekufa birshus harabim* — a brick standing erect in the *reshut harabim* — *ve'zarak ve'tach b'faneha chayav, al gabah patur*. If one throws a sticky object and it adheres to the face of the brick, one is liable; if it lands on top, one is exempt. The Gemara here transitions from definitional questions about domains back to the act of carrying, but the domain analysis remains central. The sticky object in question — the *deveila*, a round of pressed figs or dates — adheres to the side of the brick while it is still within the airspace of the *reshut harabim*, which extends upward to ten *tefachim*. Since it rests, *nach*, within that airspace, one is liable. If it lands on top of the brick, however, we must examine whether the top of the brick is a *reshut harabim* or not.

### Three-Tefach Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: וְהוּא שֶׁגְּבוֹהָה שְׁלֹשָׁה, דְּלָא דָּרְסִי לַהּ רַבִּים. אֲבָל הִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא גְּבִיהֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה. וְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ הִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי, אֲבָל צוֹאָה לָא. וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ צוֹאָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was **Abaye and Rava, who both said: And that is** specifically when that brick **is** at least **three** handbreadths **high,** as then **the multitudes do not step on it,** and, therefore, even though the brick is standing in the public domain, it is considered an independent domain. However, **thorns and shrubs, even though they are not three handbreadths high,** are not considered part of the public domain. Since people do not walk on thorns, those areas cannot be considered part of the public domain. **And Ḥiyya bar Rav said: Even** the place where there are **thorns and shrubs** in the public domain, if they were low, the place is considered part of the public domain. **However,** a place in the public domain where there are **feces** is **not** considered part of the public domain, as people do not walk there. **And Rav Ashi said: Even** a place in the public domain where there are **feces** is considered part of the public domain, since ultimately people who are rushing to work do not take care to avoid it and will step on it.

Abaye and Rava jointly address this: *ve-hu she-gavoha shelosha* — it is only when the brick is higher than three *tefachim* that its top is not part of the *reshut harabim*, *de'lo darsi lei rabbim*, because people do not walk over it when it stands that high. When the brick is less than three *tefachim* high — about nine to ten inches off the ground — people will simply step on it and trample over it, and it is therefore considered part of the *reshut harabim* through its use, not merely through its structural proximity.

This is a striking point. The principle of *lavud* — the rabbinic rule that anything within three *tefachim* of another surface is considered connected to it — might have sufficed to explain why a low brick is part of the ground. Yet Abaye and Rava deliberately do not invoke *lavud* here. They say instead: because people walk on it. This is because, as the Gemara has been establishing, structural connection alone is insufficient for *reshut harabim*; it must also be used as such. Were everyone to walk around this low brick, even a structurally connected surface would not be *reshut harabim*.

### Obstruction Debate

The Gemara makes the principle still more explicit: *aval hizmei ve'higi* — thorn bushes or thistles — *af al gav de'la gevihai shelosha*, even if they are lower than three *tefachim*, they are not *reshut harabim*, because people walk around them to avoid the thorns. The structural proximity to the ground is irrelevant; the decisive factor is that the public does not use that space.

This generates a range of opinions. Abaye and Rava hold that only thorn bushes — not soft obstructions — cause people to walk around: thorn bushes people avoid; a brick they step over. Then comes the view that *ve'ki ata Rav amar afilu hizmei ve'higi* — even thorn bushes people will eventually trample through — and therefore they are still *reshut harabim*, but *aval tzoah lo*: excrement is different, because people will walk around it entirely. Rav Ashi goes further still, suggesting that even excrement does not remove a space from *reshut harabim*, either because people do walk through it anyway, or because — as another reading suggests — the relevant criterion is not immediate avoidance but permanence of obstruction, and excrement is not a permanent feature.

What emerges clearly from all these positions is that use, specifically current immediate use, governs domain status. The Gemara may be saying that even temporary circumstances — a pile of dung, a thorn bush — can dislodge a space from *reshut harabim* status, because the masses are not actually using it as such right now. Structural location within the *reshut harabim* is not enough.

A further question arises from the converse direction: what if a space is higher than three *tefachim* but is nonetheless actually used by the *rabim*? Must both conditions be met — structural proximity through *lavud* and actual use — or is use alone sufficient? The Gemara will address this explicitly in the coming *daf*, where it will discuss a platform approximately nine *tefachim* high, about shoulder height, which is conveniently used by the public for placing and retrieving objects. The Gemara will treat that platform as part of *reshut harabim*. This shows that being within three *tefachim* of the ground is not strictly necessary; what is necessary and sufficient is that the space be used by the *rabim* within the ten-*tefach* airspace. Usage is more foundational than structural proximity.

## Size & Height Rules

### Four-by-Four Min.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה דְּבֵי רַב שֵׁילָא: כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין כַּרְמְלִית פְּחוּתָה מֵאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba from the school of Rav Sheila said: When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: There is no *karmelit* less than four** handbreadths.

The Gemara now returns to transmissions from Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ein karmelit pechusah me'arba'ah* — a *karmelit* cannot be smaller than four by four *tefachim*. This is a significant ruling that defines the lower boundary of the *karmelit*. Most *karmeliyot* — open fields, valleys, oceans — are vast, and no minimum size question arises. But within the *reshut harabim*, where we find raised bricks, posts, small platforms, and ledges that are neither used by the *rabim* as part of the street nor walled off as a *reshut hayachid*, the question is: what are these small structures? If they are not *reshut harabim* and not *reshut hayachid*, the remaining options are *karmelit* or *makom patur*.

Rabbi Yochanan establishes that a space must be at least four by four *tefachim* — approximately a foot by a foot — to qualify as a *karmelit*. Anything smaller is a *makom patur*, an exempt space. This parallel to *reshut hayachid* is intentional: just as an object must rest on a surface of four by four *tefachim* to be considered to have been placed down in a domain, so too a space must have that minimum area to be classified as a domain at all. The practical application: a fire hydrant or a similarly narrow surface in the street, smaller than four by four *tefachim*, is a *makom patur*.

### Ten-Tefach Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וְתוֹפֶסֶת עַד עֲשָׂרָה. מַאי ״וְתוֹפֶסֶת עַד עֲשָׂרָה״? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִי אִיכָּא מְחִיצָה עֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּהָוֵי כַּרְמְלִית, וְאִי לָא — לָא הָוֵי כַּרְמְלִית, וְלָא? וְהָאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב: בַּיִת שֶׁאֵין בְּתוֹכוֹ עֲשָׂרָה וְקֵרוּיוֹ מַשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה, עַל גַּגּוֹ — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, בְּתוֹכוֹ — אֵין מִטַּלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet** added and **said: And** the *karmelit* **extends up to ten** handbreadths. With regard to the formulation of Rav Sheshet, the Gemara wondered: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **And extends up to ten? If you say that** it means **if there is a partition ten** handbreadths **high** surrounding it **then it is** considered **a *karmelit*, and if not, it is not** considered **a *karmelit*. And** is it **not** a *karmelit*? **Didn't Rav Giddel say** that **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said** that **Rav said: A house that does not have** walls **inside it** that are **ten** handbreadths **high, and** with **its roofing it reaches** a height of **ten** handbreadths above the ground; **on its roof, one may carry on all of it,** as its roof is a private domain in every sense, **and inside it, one may only carry four cubits,** as inside, the height is insufficient to render it a private domain, and it retains *karmelit* status? Apparently, even an area less than ten handbreadths high has the legal status of a *karmelit*.

Rav Sheshet then qualifies: *ve'tofeset ad asarah* — the *karmelit* "grasps" only up to ten *tefachim*.

What does *ve'tofeset ad asarah* mean? The Gemara raises a first reading: perhaps a *karmelit* requires walls of ten *tefachim*. But this immediately creates a problem, as the presence of ten-*tefach* walls would make the space a *reshut hayachid*, not a *karmelit*. Tosafot therefore offers a better reading — and it is this reading that fits the context — namely, that the *karmelit* may require ten *tefachim* of airspace above it. The question would then be: can a space with a low ceiling, say five *tefachim* high, be a *karmelit*? Perhaps a domain, to function as such, requires not only four by four in area but also a height of ten *tefachim*, just as *reshut hayachid* typically has walls of ten *tefachim* and *reshut harabim* has airspace up to ten *tefachim*.

### Doghouse Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי ״וְתוֹפֶסֶת עַד עֲשָׂרָה״ — דְּעַד עֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּהָוְיָא כַּרְמְלִית, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים לָא הָוְיָא כַּרְמְלִית. וְכִי הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, לָא תְּיהַוֵּי בְּמִילֵּי דְשַׁבְּתָא לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּאֵין רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה? וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נָעַץ קָנֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו, אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ מֵאָה אַמָּה — חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is** the meaning of Rav Sheshet's formulation: **And extends up to ten?** Apparently, **up to ten** handbreadths **is that which is** within the parameters of **a *karmelit*, and above ten** handbreadths **is not a *karmelit*. And as Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: Keen scholar [*shinnana*], do not be** involved with questions **in the matters of Shabbat above ten** handbreadths. The Gemara elaborates: With regard **to what *halakha*** and in the context of what issue did Shmuel make this statement? **If you say** his intention was that **there is no private domain above ten** handbreadths, **didn't Rav Ḥisda say:** One who **stuck a stick in the** ground of **the private domain and threw** an object from the public domain **and it landed atop it, even** if the stick was **a hundred cubits high, he is liable, since the private domain extends up to the sky?** Apparently, there is a private domain even above ten handbreadths.

The Gemara then challenges this possibility. It cites a ruling of Rav Giddel citing Rav Chiya bar Yosef citing Rav: *bayis she'ein besocho asarah ve'kiruyo mashlimo le'asarah* — a house whose interior is only nine *tefachim* high, but whose roof, one *tefach* thick, brings the outer wall to ten *tefachim* total — has the following status. *Al gago mutar letaltel bekulo*: on the roof, one may carry over the entire surface, because the outer walls are ten *tefachim* high, and we imagine them as extending upward, enclosing the rooftop space as a *reshut hayachid*. *Besokho, ein metaltelin bo ela be'arba ammos*: inside, one may carry only within four *amot* — the restriction applicable in a *reshut harabim* and equally in a *karmelit*. The interior, with walls only nine *tefachim* high from the inside, is not a *reshut hayachid*; not a *reshut harabim*; it is therefore a *karmelit*.

This case — which one might call the "doghouse" — demonstrates that a *karmelit* can exist even when the interior height does not reach ten *tefachim*. The *karmelit* here has an airspace of only nine *tefachim*, yet it is treated as a *karmelit*. This refutes the suggestion that a *karmelit* requires ten *tefachim* of interior height.

What then does *ve'tofeset ad asarah* mean? The Gemara resolves: it means that the *karmelit*'s airspace extends upward only until ten *tefachim* — *ve'ad yud hu de'havia karmelis, lema'ala miyud tefachim lo havei karmelis*. Just as *reshut harabim* has airspace only up to ten *tefachim* above the ground, so too the *karmelit*, parallel to *reshut harabim* in this respect. Above ten *tefachim*, the airspace of a *karmelit* becomes a *makom patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כַּרְמְלִית, דְּאֵין כַּרְמְלִית לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה. וְאַקִּילוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן מִקּוּלֵּי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּמִקּוּלֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. מִקּוּלֵּי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — דְּאִי אִיכָּא מָקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה הוּא דְּהָוְיָא כַּרְמְלִית, וְאִי לָא — מְקוֹם פָּטוּר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. מִקּוּלֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — דְּעַד עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים הוּא דְּהָוְיָא כַּרְמְלִית, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים לָא הָוְיָא כַּרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that Shmuel's statement was referring to **a *karmelit*; there is no *karmelit* above ten handbreadths. And,** if so, **the Sages were lenient with regard to** a *karmelit* and applied **some leniencies of the private domain and some leniencies of the public domain.** The Gemara elaborates: **Some leniencies of the private domain: That if there is an area of four** handbreadths, **then it is a *karmelit*, and if** there is **not** an area of four handbreadths, **it is merely an exempt domain. Some leniencies of the public domain: That until** a height of **ten handbreadths, it is a *karmelit,* above ten handbreadths is not a *karmelit*.**

This is confirmed by the teaching of Shmuel to Rav Yehuda — *shinena*, "sharp one" — *lo tehei bemilei de'Shabbata lema'la me'asara*: do not concern yourself with Shabbat carrying issues above ten *tefachim*. The Gemara considers two possible meanings of this instruction. Could he have meant that *reshut hayachid* does not extend higher than ten *tefachim*? This is contradicted by the ruling of Rav Chisda — which will be discussed momentarily — that *reshut hayachid* extends to the sky. Could he have meant that *reshut harabim* does not go above ten *tefachim*? That is already stated explicitly in a *Mishna* (*hazorek arba amot bekotel lema'la me'asara tefachim kezorek ba'avir, lemata me'asara tefachim kezorek ba'aretz*) and would not need to be transmitted specially to Rav Yehuda. Therefore, Shmuel must have been referring specifically to the *karmelit* — *ela akarmelit, de'ein karmelit lema'la me'asara* — teaching that the *karmelit*'s airspace similarly does not extend above ten *tefachim*.

### Karmelit Hybrid

The Gemara now summarizes the hybrid nature of the *karmelit*: *ve'akilu ba'rabbanan mikulei reshus hayachid u'mikulei reshus harabbim* — the Rabbis applied leniencies from both *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim* in defining the *karmelit*'s parameters.

From the leniencies of *reshut hayachid*: only if the space is four by four *tefachim* is it a *karmelit*; if smaller, it is merely a *makom patur*. From the leniencies of *reshut harabim*: the *karmelit*'s airspace extends only up to ten *tefachim*, not beyond.

Yet in terms of the actual restrictions on carrying, the *karmelit* is treated strictly on both ends: one may not carry from it to a *reshut hayachid*, nor from it to a *reshut harabim*, and within it one may carry only four *amot*. The *karmelit* is thus a hybrid in its definitional parameters, borrowing size and height thresholds from both major domains, while remaining a distinct and fully restricted rabbinic domain in practice.

To summarize the practical contours: a *makom patur* is a surface smaller than four by four *tefachim*, more than three *tefachim* off the ground (since within three it is absorbed into *reshut harabim* by use), and less than ten *tefachim* high (since at ten *tefachim* with sufficient area it could become a *reshut hayachid*). The classic *makom patur* is found in that zone between roughly nine inches and three feet off the ground, with a footprint smaller than a foot by a foot.

## Associated Spaces

### Pit Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב: בַּיִת שֶׁאֵין תּוֹכוֹ עֲשָׂרָה וְקֵרוּיוֹ מַשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה, עַל גַּגּוֹ — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, בְּתוֹכוֹ — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְאִם חָקַק בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה, — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? — הָוֵי חוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְחוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד כִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **To** the matter **itself:** It was mentioned above that **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said** that **Rav said: A house that does not have inside it** walls that are **ten** handbreadths **high, and** with **its roofing it reaches** a height of **ten** handbreadths above the ground; **on its roof, one may carry on all of it,** as its roof is a private domain in every sense, **and inside it, one may only carry four cubits,** as inside the height is insufficient to render it a private domain and it retains *karmelit* status. **Abaye said: And if he dug** out an area of **four by four** handbreadths **in** the floor of the house **and** in the place where the digging took place, its height to the ceiling **reaches ten** handbreadths, the house becomes a private domain, and **it is permitted to carry in the entire** house. **What is the reason** for this? Since the dug out area is a private domain, the rest of the house is ancillary to it, **and it assumes the** legal **status of the holes of the private domain, and the holes of the private domain,** although they lack the measure of a private domain, **are considered like the private domain** itself.

The Gemara returns *gufa* — to examine on its own terms the ruling of Rav Giddel that was cited earlier in passing. The "doghouse" scenario is revisited. Abaye adds: *ve'im chakak bo arba al arba ve'hishlimu le'asarah mutar letaltel bekulo* — if one dug a pit inside the structure, four by four *tefachim* in area, such that from the floor of the pit to the ceiling is ten *tefachim*, one may now carry throughout the entire interior of the house.

The logic: this pit, four by four *tefachim* with a height of ten *tefachim*, is a *reshut hayachid*. The walls of the house, though not immediately adjacent to the pit, surround it. The remaining floor space of the interior — the areas outside the pit, which themselves still lack ten *tefachim* of interior height — are now permitted for carrying as well. Why? *Havei chorei reshut hayachid* — they become like the "holes of a *reshut hayachid*."

### Holes in Private

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: חוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד כִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד דָּמוּ, חוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אַבָּיֵי אוֹמֵר כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמוּ. רָבָא אוֹמֵר: לָאו כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was stated:** Everyone agrees that **the holes of the private domain are** considered **like the private domain;** since they are subsumed within the private domain, they are judged to be like it. However, they disagreed with regard to **the holes of the public domain. Abaye says: They are considered** to be **like the public domain. And Rava says: They are not considered** to be **like the public domain;** they are either a *karmelit* or an exempt domain.

The principle of *chorei reshut hayachid* is that the niches and recesses associated with a *reshut hayachid* share its status. In Talmudic construction, stone walls typically had recesses — small niches used for storage. Such a niche, considered in isolation, does not meet the formal criteria of *reshut hayachid*: it is not four by four *tefachim* with walls ten *tefachim* high. Yet because it serves the *reshut hayachid* and is situated within its walls, it is considered an extension of the *reshut hayachid*.

Applied to the doghouse scenario: once the pit creates a genuine *reshut hayachid* within the structure, all the surrounding floor space, though itself lacking the necessary interior height, is treated as associated space — *chorei reshut hayachid* — and acquires the status of *reshut hayachid*. The entire interior thus becomes permitted for carrying.

Tosafot raises an objection from the laws of *sukkah*: we know that in the laws of *sukkah*, the *sechach* must be connected to the walls; a *sukkah* with *sechach* in the middle and walls far off to the side is invalid. Why then does *reshut hayachid* not similarly require the walls to be connected to the central space? Two answers suggest themselves. First, *sukkah* has an additional requirement of *sechach*, and the rule that *sechach* must be connected to the walls is a distinct criterion generated by the interaction of those two elements; the four-by-four pit here has no such roof requirement. Second, and more fundamentally, *reshut hayachid* is not a *house* — it is a *space enclosed by walls*. A *sukkah* is, in essence, an edifice, a dwelling, and as such its walls must actually constitute the walls of that dwelling. *Reshut hayachid* is defined purely as an area surrounded by walls, however distant. The walls enclose the space; they need not be architecturally connected to any particular point within it. This distinction — between a space defined by its enclosure and an edifice defined by its structure — appears to be the underlying conceptual difference.

### Holes in Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ חוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמוּ, מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְקֶרֶן זָוִית הַסְּמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְתִיהְוֵי כְּחוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים?! הָתָם לָא נִיחָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתֵּיהּ, הָכָא נִיחָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Abaye: According to you, who said** that the **holes of the public domain are considered like the public domain, in what** way **is it different from this** *halakha*? **As when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** addition of *karmelit* to the *Tosefta* **was only necessary** to teach the case of **a corner adjacent to the public domain. And,** according to your opinion, **let** this corner **be like the holes of the public domain,** and its legal status should be that of a public domain itself and not that of a *karmelit*. Abaye answered: There is a distinction between the cases. **There,** the corner, **its use is not convenient; here,** the holes of the public domain, **their use is convenient.** Since it is convenient to utilize the holes of the public domain, and they are in fact utilized, they are a public domain in every sense.

The Gemara now addresses *chorei reshut harabbim* — niches or holes in walls along the *reshut harabim* that face the street and are used by passersby. If *chorei reshut hayachid* share the status of *reshut hayachid*, do *chorei reshut harabbim* share the status of *reshut harabim*?

Abaye says yes: *ke'reshut harabbim dami* — holes in a wall along the *reshut harabim*, if used by the public, have the status of *reshut harabim*. Rava disagrees: *lav ke'reshut harabbim damu* — they do not.

Rava challenges Abaye: if holes in a public domain wall are *reshut harabim*, how does that square with the ruling — also transmitted by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — that the *keren zavit*, the corner niche alongside the *reshut harabim*, is a *karmelit*? People do sometimes push into that corner. If even that much use suffices to make something *reshut harabim* according to Abaye, then the *keren zavit* should also be *reshut harabim*. What is Abaye's response?

### Convenience Compare

*Hasam lo nicha tashmishtei, hacha nicha tashmishtei* — there, in the case of the corner niche, the use is not convenient; here, in the case of the holes in the wall, the use is convenient. The *keren zavit* requires one to turn off the main path, squeeze into a corner, move against the flow — it is not *nicha tashmishtei*. A niche in a wall at street level, by contrast, into which one can place or retrieve an object without breaking stride — that is *nicha tashmishtei*, convenient use, and Abaye therefore treats it as *reshut harabim*.

This is not a binary yes/no determination but a qualitative continuum. Every case must be assessed individually for the degree of convenience of its use. The contrast between Abaye and Rava is that Rava requires a very high threshold of use to assign *reshut harabim* status — so high that even a convenient wall niche does not meet it — while Abaye draws the line between genuinely inconvenient use (like the *keren zavit*) and genuinely convenient use (like a wall niche). This distinction cuts to the heart of what *reshut harabim* is: not merely a physical territory but a functionally defined space, and the primary question throughout is always how it is used.

## Wall Throw Cases

### Sticky Fig Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַזּוֹרֵק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בַּכּוֹתֶל, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאֲוִיר. לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאָרֶץ. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: מַאי כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאָרֶץ? וְהָא לָא נָח! וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, בִּדְבֵילָה שְׁמֵינָה שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raised an additional difficulty for Abaye's opinion: **We learned** in a mishna with regard to **one who throws** an object **four cubits** in the public domain, and the object came to rest **on a wall** standing in the public domain **above ten handbreadths** from the ground, it **is as if he were throwing** an object **in the air** and it never landed. If it came to rest **below ten handbreadths** off the ground, it **is as if he were throwing** an object **to the ground,** and he is liable. **And we discussed** this *halakha*: **What is** the reason that when the wall is not ten handbreadths high it is **as if he threw it to the ground?** The object **did not** come to **rest** on the wall, as presumably the object hit the wall and then fell to the ground. Since there was no act of placement, he did not perform the prohibited labor of carrying in the public domain. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **they learned** this mishna as referring to a case **when** he threw **a juicy cake of figs** that sticks to the wall and remains there.

The Gemara now returns *gufa* to the *Mishna* cited earlier (*hazorek arba amot bekotel lema'la me'asara tefachim kezorek ba'avir, lemata me'asara tefachim kezorek ba'aretz*) and its accompanying explanation by Rabbi Yochanan. The Gemara had asked: if one throws something at a wall and it hits below ten *tefachim*, it is like placing it on the ground — but *veha lo nach*, it did not come to rest! Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *be'dveila shemeinah shanu* — the case is one of a fat, sticky *dveila*, a round of pressed figs, that adheres to the side of the wall. The object does in fact come to rest — it sticks to the face of the wall within the ten-*tefach* airspace of the *reshut harabim*, and one is therefore liable.

### Why Not Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ חוֹרֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמוּ, לְמָה לִי לְאוֹקֻמַהּ בִּדְבֵילָה שְׁמֵינָה? לוֹקְמַהּ בִּצְרוֹר וְחֵפֶץ, וּדְנָח בְּחוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **And should it enter your mind** to say that **the holes of the public domain are considered like the public domain, why do I need to establish** the mishna as referring to the case **of a juicy cake of figs? Let us establish it** simply as referring to the case **of a** run-of-the-mill **stone or object, and** that it **came to rest in a hole.**

This raises an obvious question: why construct such an unusual scenario involving sticky figs? Rava poses this to Abaye: if you hold that *chorei reshut harabbim ke'reshut harabbim damu* — that holes in a wall of *reshut harabim* have the status of *reshut harabim* — then there is a far simpler scenario. *Lokmi be'tsror ve'cheifetz ve'danach be'chor* — simply discuss a rock or any ordinary object that was thrown and came to rest in a hole in the wall. No sticky figs required; the object lands in the hole and stays there. Why does Rabbi Yochanan need the peculiar case of the sticky fig?

### Carving Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין מְשַׁנֵּי לַהּ, שָׁאנֵי צְרוֹר וְחֵפֶץ דְּמִיהֲדַר וְאָתֵי. זִימְנִין מְשַׁנֵּי לַהּ, בְּכוֹתֶל דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חוֹר. מִמַּאי? — מִדְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: זָרַק לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאֲוִיר. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּכוֹתֶל דְּאִית בֵּיהּ חוֹר, אַמַּאי כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאֲוִיר? הָא נָח בְּחוֹר! וְכִי תֵּימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלֵית בְּהוּ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: זָרַק לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים וְהָלְכָה וְנָחָה בְּחוֹר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבָּנַן. דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי אֵין חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בְּכוֹתֶל דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חוֹר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Sometimes** Abaye **would answer** the question by saying that **a stone or object is different** from a juicy fig **in that they come back** when they are thrown and do not come to rest in the hole. Therefore, it was simpler to establish the mishna in the case of a fig. **And sometimes he would answer it** by saying that the mishna is referring to **a wall that has no hole. And from where** does he find support for this explanation? **From that which we learned in the first clause** of the mishna: **One who throws above ten handbreadths** from the ground, it **is as if he is throwing in the air** and it never landed. **And if it should enter your mind** to say that we are speaking here **about a wall that has a hole in it, why** should it be **as if he threw it in the air** and it never landed? **It rested in a hole,** and that hole is a private domain, as it is above ten handbreadths, and in that way the prohibited labor of carrying in was performed. **And if you say** that **the mishna** is referring to a case **where** holes **do not have** an area of at least **four by four** handbreadths, which is common for holes in the wall, and therefore the holes have exempt domain status, **didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya said: One who threw** an object **above ten handbreadths and** the object **went and came to rest in a hole of any size, we have arrived** in this matter **at the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis?** The decision whether or not there is a prohibition here depends on an analysis of that dispute. **Rabbi Meir holds** that in all cases where a certain minimum area is required for a specific *halakha* to take effect and the existing area is smaller, if, theoretically, circumstances would allow to carve out and create an area of the requisite size, **one** considers it as if he **carves out** the space **to complete** it**,** i.e., the space has the legal status as if it was actually enlarged. **And the Rabbis hold** that **one does not carve out** the space **to complete** it. Rather, the legal status of the area corresponds to its actual size. Consequently, according to Rabbi Meir, if an object landed in a small hole, one considers the area as if it were carved out to complete the hole to four by four handbreadths, and its legal status is like that of a private domain in every sense. **Rather, can we not conclude from** the mishna that maintains that one who throws an object onto a wall above ten handbreadths it is as if he threw it in the air, that it is referring to **a wall that has no hole in it,** and the possibility of carving out the space was never raised? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it.**

One answer: *zimnin meshani lei tsror ve'cheifetz miahdar ve'ati* — a rock or hard object thrown at a hole in a wall will typically bounce back. Getting a thrown object to land in a small hole and stay there is actually a less realistic scenario than a sticky fig adhering to the wall face. It is like a carnival game — it looks easy, but in practice the object bounces back. The sticky fig is actually the more natural case.

The second and more fundamental answer: *zimnin meshani lei be'kosel de'leis bei chor* — Rabbi Yochanan is speaking about a wall without holes. And the Gemara explains why a wall with holes cannot serve as the clean example we need. The *Mishna*'s first case is: if one throws something *higher* than ten *tefachim* and it hits the wall, it is like throwing in the air — *makom patur*, exempt. This case is structurally parallel to the case below ten *tefachim*. If we imagine a wall with holes, then a hole higher than ten *tefachim* would also need to be considered. But a hole in a wall higher than ten *tefachim*, if it is four by four *tefachim*, is a *reshut hayachid* — not a *makom patur*. One would be liable for landing something there, not exempt.

And even if the hole is smaller than four by four — which might seem to avoid the *reshut hayachid* classification — the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis applies. The Gemara cites: *zarak lema'la me'asara tefachim ve'halcha ve'nachah be'chor kol shehu banu le'machlokes Rabbi Meir ve'Rabbanan* — if one threw an object higher than ten *tefachim* and it came to rest in even a small hole, the case falls into the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis regarding *chokekin le'hashlim* — carving out to complete the requisite measurement.

The context of this dispute is a doorway with an arch: to be obligated in *mezuzah*, a doorway must be at least ten *tefachim* high with a width of four *tefachim* at that height. A classic arch narrows at the top; the width of four *tefachim* may not be maintained at the full height of ten *tefachim*. Rabbi Meir holds *chokekin le'hashlim* — we imaginarily "carve out" the stone to square off the arch, and if the resulting space would be ten by four, we treat it as if it already is. The *Chachamim* disagree: *ein chokekin le'hashlim* — we do not carve. Applied to a small hole in a wall: Rabbi Meir would say that if the surrounding stone could theoretically be removed to yield a four-by-four space, we treat the hole as a *reshut hayachid*. Therefore, even a small hole higher than ten *tefachim* would, according to Rabbi Meir, be a *reshut hayachid* — and the case of throwing above ten would not be a clean case of *makom patur*.

### No-Hole Assumption

*Ela lav shema mina de'kosel de'leis bei chor* — we must conclude that the *Mishna* and Rabbi Yochanan's explanation are dealing with a wall that has no holes at all. This is why the sticky fig is used: a wall without holes means nothing can land in a hole; the only way something can come to rest on the face of the wall is if it is sticky enough to adhere. And this also ensures that throwing above ten *tefachim* results in a clean *makom patur* — there are no holes that could constitute a *reshut hayachid* and complicate the analysis. *Shema mina* — this is indeed the correct inference.

Abaye's position is thus preserved: holes in a *reshut harabim* wall, when conveniently used, do have the status of *reshut harabim*. The Gemara's choice of the sticky fig case was not due to any problem with that principle, but rather to avoid the complications that holes above ten *tefachim* would introduce — complications involving *reshut hayachid* and the dispute of Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis.

## Flagpole Height

### Pole Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נָעַץ קָנֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו, אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ מֵאָה אַמָּה — חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again returns to the **matter** that was mentioned above in passing **itself [*gufa*]. Rav Ḥisda said:** One who **stuck a stick in the** ground of **the private domain, and** an object that he himself threw from the public domain **rested atop it, even** if that stick was **a hundred cubits high, he is liable.** The reason for this is **because the private domain rises up to the sky.**

The Gemara returns *gufa* to the ruling of Rav Chisda: *na'atz kaneh be'reshut hayachid ve'zarak ve'nach al gabav* — one thrust a pole into a *reshut hayachid* and threw an object that came to rest on top of the pole — *afilu gavoah me'ah amah chayav, mipnei she'reshut hayachid olah ad larakia* — even if the pole is a hundred *amot* high, one is liable, because *reshut hayachid* extends all the way to the sky. The walls of the *reshut hayachid*, by the principle that they extend upward without limit, encompass the entire column of airspace above them, however high. Something that comes to rest atop a flagpole standing within a *reshut hayachid* — even one surrounded merely by a low fence — has been deposited in *reshut hayachid*.

### Rebbi vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב חִסְדָּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי? דְּתַנְיָא: זָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבֵּי זִיז כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — רַבִּי מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים. אַלְמָא לָא בָּעֵינַן מָקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that when **Rav Ḥisda said** his statement, it was **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The *tanna'im* disagreed with regard to a similar issue, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: One who **threw** an object on Shabbat in the public domain, **and** the object **rested on a projection of any size, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems him liable and the Rabbis deem him exempt. Consequently,** only according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is there no need for the object to come to rest on an area of a specific size, and therefore the statement of Rav Ḥisda with regard to the stick can only be in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion.

The Gemara raises a question: does the ruling of Rav Chisda reflect the view of Rebbi alone, against the *Chachamim*? The problem is that the top of the flagpole is an extremely small surface — far less than four by four *tefachim* — and we have established that resting on a surface of four by four *tefachim* is required for the carrying act to be considered complete. The Gemara cites a *braita*: *zarak ve'nach al gabei ziz kol shehu — Rebbi mechayev ve'Chachamim potrin* — one threw something and it rested on a tiny protrusion: Rebbi holds one is liable; the *Chachamim* hold one is exempt. Presumably, Rebbi does not require the object to rest on a space of four by four *tefachim*, while the *Chachamim* do. Rav Chisda's ruling, which makes one liable for an object resting atop a thin pole, would seem to align with Rebbi.

Abaye's response will open *daf* 7b — and the question of whether four by four *tefachim* is required in *reshut hayachid* will be taken up there.
