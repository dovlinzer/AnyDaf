# Shabbat 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Awareness & Exemption

### Daf 102 Overview

Daf 102a marks the conclusion of the lengthy section on carrying (*hotza'ah*) that has dominated so much of Masechet Shabbat. With the turn of the page, we leave *hotza'ah* behind entirely. But first, the daf opens with a final *mishnah* and the *gemara*'s analysis of it, completing the perek on a set of nuanced principles governing awareness, intention, and exemption from a *korban*.

### Hazorek Venizkar

The *mishnah* presents the case of *hazorek venizkar leachar sheyatzta miyado* — someone who threw an object four *amot* in *reshut harabim* (or from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*) and remembered, after the object had already left his hand, that it was Shabbat — before the object came to rest. The general principle governing liability for a *korban* is that one must have been a *shogeg*, acting unwittingly, from the beginning to the end of the act. If a person remembered while the object was still in flight, it would seem that at the end of the *melacha* he was no longer in a state of unawareness — which ought to exempt him from a *korban*, even though he has certainly violated Shabbat. The *mishnah*, however, goes on to list additional grounds for exemption that make the act even less of a *melacha*.

### Object Doesn't Rest

These additional scenarios are: *kalta acher* — someone else grabbed the object from midair (making it at least *shnayim she'asu*, two people performing the act, or preventing it from landing where intended); *kalta kelev* — a dog jumped up and caught it in its mouth (raising a further question of whether a dog's mouth constitutes a valid *makom* of four *tefachim*); *o shenisrefa* — or the object burnt up in midair and never came to rest at all. In all these cases, *patur* — one is exempt, and the exemption flows from the fact that the *melacha* was not fully completed: the object did not come to rest properly, or a third party intervened, or the object was destroyed mid-flight.

The *mishnah* then introduces a second case: *zorek la'asot chabura* — one threw an object intending to wound a person or animal — *venizkar ad shelo na'asit chabura* — and remembered before the wound was actually inflicted. There, the *melacha* was completed (the wound was made), yet since the person was in a state of awareness when it was completed, he is *patur* from a *korban*. The simplest reading of the *mishnah*, which the *gemara* will eventually endorse, is that all these cases are listed with commas between them — each is an independent ground for exemption. One is exempt if one remembered mid-flight; or if someone grabbed it; or a dog grabbed it; or it burnt. Similarly in the *chabura* case, remembering before the wound is made suffices to exempt. The concluding *ze haklal* of the *mishnah* then articulates the general rule.

## Ze Haklal Rule

### Ze Haklal Principle

The *mishnah* concludes with *ze haklal*: *kol chayavei chatot einam chayavim ad shetehei techilatan vesofan shigaga* — all obligations of a *chattat* sin offering require that both the beginning and the end of the act be performed in a state of unawareness. This is the governing principle.

### Zadon/Shogeg Mix

The *mishnah* enumerates the combinations: if the beginning was *shogeg* and the end was *zadon* — meaning one was unwitting at the outset but became aware (remembered it was Shabbat) before the act concluded — one is *patur*. Similarly, if the beginning was *zadon* and the end was *shogeg* — one knew it was Shabbat when one began the act, but somehow forgot by the time it concluded — one is also *patur*. This latter scenario is harder to imagine: perhaps one threw the object knowing it was Shabbat, and during the long arc of flight forgot again. In either case, *paturin*. Only when *techilatan vesofan shigaga* — beginning and end both in a state of unawareness — does the obligation of a *korban* apply. The middle, as the *gemara* will clarify, is a separate question.

### Carrying vs. Wounding

It is worth noting that the *mishnah* actually addresses two distinct types of *melacha*. The cases involving the dog catching the object, someone else grabbing it, or the object burning — these are exemptions grounded in the incompleteness of the *melacha* itself. The cases involving awareness — whether the person remembered mid-flight in the carrying case, or remembered before the wound was inflicted in the *chabura* case — these are exemptions grounded in the person's state of mind, not in the nature of the act. The *ze haklal* addresses only the latter: when the act is fully performed, the person's state of awareness at beginning and end determines whether a *korban* is owed.

A question naturally arises about the *chabura* case: if someone throws a rock intending to wound another person and then remembers mid-flight — he is exempt from a *korban*, but what of civil liability? The principle of *tanna d'vei Chizkiyah* establishes that even *b'shogeg*, the category of an act that would be *chayav mita* overrides civil liability. But that discussion belongs properly to Bava Kamma and is not the focus here. The *gemara*'s concern is the narrow technical question of the *korban*, and it brackets the moral dimension of the act accordingly.

## Lichta U'Mashna

### Rav Kahana Challenge

The *gemara* now interrogates the *mishnah*'s structure. As initially read, the first clause of the *mishnah* appears to combine two conditions for exemption: the person both remembered mid-flight *and* a dog or person caught the object. The *gemara* asks: why are both needed? The *ze haklal* at the end of the *mishnah* establishes that remembering at the end should suffice on its own to exempt. Rav Kahana responds: *seifa atan l'lichta u'mashna* — the concluding clause of the *mishnah* (where awareness alone exempts) is not universally true. It applies only to a specific case — *lichta u'mashna*, an object tied to a rope.

### Control & Awareness

The underlying principle Rav Kahana is articulating is this: becoming aware exempts a person only when, at the moment of awareness, he has the ability to control or interrupt the act. If one has thrown an object and it is in mid-flight, the act is beyond one's control. One's change in mental state — remembering that it is Shabbat — has no effect on the physical reality of the object in the air. The act is proceeding independently of one's will. Therefore, the *gemara* argues, one's awareness in that moment does not redefine the quality of the act. The act remains effectively *shogeg* from start to finish, because one is no longer *doing* anything — the act is simply completing itself. In this reading, the first clause of the *mishnah* means: you threw the object, you remembered mid-flight, but since you cannot stop it, remembering does not help — and the only thing that exempts you is the external intervention of a dog or person catching it, or the object burning.

### Rope Example

When, then, does awareness during an in-progress act actually exempt? Only in the case of *lichta u'mashna* — Rashi explains that *mashna* is a rope and *lichta* is a piece of wood attached to the rope. If the object is tied to a rope one is holding, then upon becoming aware, one could in theory pull the object back. The knot is in one's hand (*ugdo b'yado hu*). One's awareness now meaningfully changes the quality of the act, because one is in a position to interrupt it. The *gemara* immediately points out, however, that if one is holding the rope, one is already exempt from the *melacha* of carrying regardless — since part of the object remains effectively in one's *reshut*, the act of carrying is never completed. The rope case therefore cannot illustrate an exemption through awareness in a carrying context.

### Wounding w/ Rope

The *gemara* therefore clarifies: the case of *lichta u'mashna* applies to the *melacha* of *chabura* — wounding. One threw a rock tied to a rope, intending to wound someone. The *melacha* of *chabura* is not carrying; it involves taking blood or inflicting injury, akin in category to *shechita* and *nefilat neshama*. Holding the rope is irrelevant to whether the wound was inflicted. But if upon becoming aware mid-throw one could have pulled the rope back and prevented the wound, then one's awareness genuinely changes the quality of the act — the final phase of the act is now attributed to one's knowing inaction rather than to *shigaga*. This is what makes one *patur*. On this reading, the *ze haklal* at the end of the *mishnah* — that awareness at beginning and end suffices — applies only in this narrow scenario: throwing with a rope in order to make a *chabura*, where one had the physical capacity to stop the act. In any other case of throwing, awareness mid-flight does not exempt.

## Meavir Case

### Carrying vs. Throwing

Rava objects to Rav Kahana's reading. The *ze haklal* at the end of the *mishnah* seems to follow directly from the *chabura* case and is phrased in terms of throwing (*zerika*). One cannot simply declare that the general rule switches mid-*mishnah* from throwing to carrying. The *gemara* itself acknowledges this difficulty — *ve-ha ze haklal d'ketani azrika ketani* — but Rava sets the objection aside and offers an alternative.

### Four Amot Example

Rava says: *ela amar Rava, b'meavir* — the case where becoming aware exempts you is not *zerika* but *meavir*, carrying. If one was walking four *amot* in *reshut harabim* carrying an object, and by the last two *amot* one remembered that it was Shabbat — one actively performed the final stage of the *melacha* in a state of full awareness. This is not a situation where one merely could have controlled the act; one actually *was doing* the act in a knowing state. In this case, the act genuinely ends *b'zadon*, and that exempts one from a *korban*. This stands in contrast to throwing, where once the object leaves one's hand, becoming aware changes nothing — one is no longer doing anything, and the physical completion of the act proceeds regardless of one's mental state.

### Simple Mishna Reading

Rava ultimately endorses the simple reading of the *mishnah*: *tarti katani* — the *mishnah* lists multiple independent cases separated by commas. Remembering in mid-flight is one ground for exemption. A person grabbing the object is another. A dog catching it is another. The object burning is another. Each suffices on its own. The *ze haklal* then generalizes: any case where beginning and end are both *shogeg* incurs a *korban*; if either the beginning or the end is *zadon*, one is *patur*. This is the plain reading — and as the *gemara* concludes, it is the correct one.

## Middle Awareness

### Two Conditions

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading of the opening of the *mishnah*, one that requires careful attention. He says *chisorei mechasra vehachi katani* — the *mishnah* is abbreviated and must be read with implied content. On his reading, the first clause means: one threw the object, one remembered mid-flight, *and* a dog caught it — and that combination exempts one. Why would both conditions be necessary?

### No Forgetting Again

Rav Ashi's answer is that the *mishnah* is speaking of a case where one remembered in the middle but then forgot again before the object landed. In that scenario — *shechazar veshachach* — one was momentarily aware during flight but returned to a state of *shigaga* by the time the act was completed. That middle-awareness does not help. The beginning was *shogeg*, the end was *shogeg*, and the momentary awareness in the middle is irrelevant. In that case, one needs a separate exemption — the dog catching it, or the object burning — to avoid liability. But if one did *not* forget again — *aval lo chazar veshachach* — if one remained aware through the landing, then *patur*, remembering alone suffices, because the end of the act was in a state of awareness.

### Beginning/End Rule

This reading yields a striking rule: the *halacha* looks only at two points — the beginning and the end of the act. The middle is legally irrelevant. If one was *shogeg* at the start and *shogeg* at the finish, one brings a *korban* — even if during the middle of carrying four *amot* one became momentarily aware. This is counterintuitive. One might have argued that awareness in the middle should define the quality of the act, particularly when, as in *meavir*, one was actively performing the *melacha* at that moment. One might equally have argued in the other direction — that only if one can control the act does awareness matter at all. The *gemara*'s conclusion, however, is neither of these: it defines *shigaga* purely by reference to the bookends of the act. The middle — even a middle during which one was actively aware and actively carrying — does not break the act. It is the beginning and the end, and only those two points, that determine liability.

## Split Awareness

### Rabba vs. Rava

The *gemara* now records a dispute (*itmar*) between Rabba and Rava regarding a more complex scenario. One carries an object four *amot* in *reshut harabim* in the following sequence: the first two *amot* are *b'shogeg*, the middle two *amot* (steps three and four) are *b'mezid* — one becomes aware during this stretch — and the final two *amot* are again *b'shogeg*. The question is: does the period of awareness during *amot* three and four suffice to exempt, given that by *amah* four one has already completed the minimal *melacha* of carrying four *amot*?

### Rabba's View

*Rabba amar patur* — Rabba says one is exempt. His reasoning invokes the position of Rabban Gamliel regarding *yediya l'chatzi shiur*. The case of Rabban Gamliel involves writing two letters of a word with awareness intervening between them: one writes *shin*, becomes aware, and then writes *mem* after forgetting again. Rabban Gamliel holds that awareness between two halves of a *melacha* does not break it — *ein yediya l'chatzi shiur* — because when the second letter was written one was again *shogeg*, and both letters were written *b'shogeg*. But here, Rabba argues, the situation is different. When one reached *amah* four — the moment at which the minimal *melacha* was complete — one was *b'mezid*. Even though one continued walking afterward in a state of *shigaga*, the act was minimally complete at a moment of awareness. Therefore, even according to Rabban Gamliel, one is *patur*.

### Meavir & Control

Rabba's position applies specifically to *meavir* — the act of carrying. When one is carrying, one has control over the act at every moment; one can stop. When one reached *amah* four in a state of awareness, one was capable of setting down the object and ending the act right there. The fact that the minimum *melacha* was executed in a state of knowing agency — even if one then continued unwittingly — makes the conclusion of the act *b'zadon*. This is what renders one *patur*.

### Rava's View

*Rava amar chayav* — Rava disagrees and says one is *chayav*. His reasoning draws on the position of the *rabbanan* who dispute Rabban Gamliel, holding *yesh yediya l'chatzi shiur*: awareness between the *shin* and the *mem* does break the act, because at that moment of awareness one could have stopped writing. If one could stop and did not, that awareness separates the two halves. But in the carrying case described, Rava argues, being aware at *amah* four does not help — *harei beyado* does not apply — because if the scenario involves *zerika* (throwing), one was never in a position to stop the act at *amah* four. The act proceeded past that point without one's active agency.

### Throwing/Carrying

The *gemara* resolves the apparent dispute by clarifying that Rabba and Rava are actually describing different cases. Rabba's ruling (*patur*) addresses *meavir* — carrying, where at *amah* four one could stop. Since the minimal *melacha* was completed at a point where one was aware and could have ended the act, that constitutes a *zadon* conclusion, and one is *patur*. Rava's ruling (*chayav*) addresses *zorek* — throwing, where one could not stop the act at *amah* four. Since stopping was not in one's power, awareness at that theoretical end-point has no legal significance. One looks only at the actual beginning and actual end of the throw — both *shogeg* — and one is *chayav*.

## Dog's Mouth

### Intent to Dog

Rava introduces a seemingly paradoxical ruling: *zarak v'nach befi kelev o befi kivshan, chayav* — if one threw an object and it landed in a dog's mouth or in a furnace, one is *chayav*. This appears to contradict the *mishnah*, which rules *patur* when a dog catches the object in flight.

### Intent Difference

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hatam delo mechaven, hacha dekamechaven* — the *mishnah* speaks of a case where one did not intend for the object to land in the dog's mouth; it was grabbed unexpectedly. Rava's case involves a situation where one deliberately threw the object toward the dog's mouth, intending it to land there. The legal significance of intention here is the principle of *machshava meshvia makom* — one's intent to place an object in a location endows that location with the status of a significant *makom*, even if the location would not ordinarily qualify as a *makom arba tefachim*. A dog's mouth is not four *tefachim* wide and would not normally constitute a valid place of rest. But when one specifically intends for the object to land there, one's intention confers on it the status of a meaningful place, and one is therefore *chayav*. In the *mishnah*'s case, where there was no such intention — the dog jumped up unexpectedly — the mouth was never designated as a *makom*, and the interception therefore exempts.

A related question arises: does it matter whether the dog jumped up to catch the object, versus simply having its mouth open and the object falling in passively? The distinction likely tracks the same axis of intention. If one intended the object to fall into the dog's open mouth, that intention confers significance on the location and creates liability, while the dog's passivity means the act was completed entirely through one's own energy, without the complicating factor of *shnayim she'asu*. If the dog actively jumped and caught it, that independent action may introduce the issue of whether the dog's intervention constitutes a form of shared performance of the *melacha*.

## One Eating/6 Offerings

### Multiple Chiyuvim

The *gemara* records a teaching: *yesh ochel achila achat v'chayav aleha arba chataot v'asham echad* — there is a case in which one eating can render a person liable for four *chataot* and one *asham*. The case: a *tamei* person eats *chelev* (forbidden fat) that is *nosar* (leftover *korban* meat past its time) from *hekdesh* (consecrated property) on *Yom Kippur*. The *chataot* are: (1) eating *chelev*, which carries *karet*; (2) eating *nosar*, which carries *karet*; (3) eating *hekdesh* while *tamei*, which carries *karet*; (4) eating on *Yom Kippur*, which carries *karet*. The *asham* is the *asham meilos* for the benefit derived from *hekdesh* — owed even independently of the *tumah* status. That gives five *korbanot* for one act of eating.

### Rabbi Meir Add-on

Rabbi Meir adds: *af im hayesa Shabbat vehotzia befiv, chayav* — if it was also Shabbat and the person was carrying the food in his mouth as he walked, one can add a sixth *korban* for the *melacha* of carrying (*hotza'ah*). The idea is that by swallowing the food, the descent into the stomach constitutes a *hanachah*, and if this occurred while crossing a *reshut harabim*, carrying is complete.

### Gemara Response

The *gemara* objects: *eino min hashem* — the carrying obligation does not arise from the same act, in the sense relevant here. The first five obligations all derive from the act of *eating* — they are each defined by the nature of eating. Carrying in one's mouth while walking is not defined by eating; it is defined by carrying. While both acts occur simultaneously and through the same physical motion, they are conceptually distinct. The earlier Sages therefore reject Rabbi Meir's addition: it cannot be counted in a list of *chiyuvim* that stem from a single act of eating.

The *rishonim* clarify Rabbi Meir's position: he does not merely mean that one incidentally carried the food in one's mouth. Rather, he holds that the swallowing of the food — its descent into the stomach — constitutes the *hanachah* of carrying, just as ink alighting on paper constitutes a *hanachah* even while one is walking, because the paper is the natural resting place of the ink. Similarly, the stomach is the natural resting place of food. The physical act of eating is thus simultaneously the physical act of carrying. Nevertheless, the *gemara* rules that conceptually these remain different *melachot*, and the sixth cannot be counted.

## Intent & Place

### Machshava

The *gemara* articulates the principle underlying Rava's ruling about the dog's mouth and the implicit logic behind the Rabbi Meir discussion: *machshava meshvia lei makom* — one's intention to place an object in a non-standard location confers upon that location the legal status of a significant place. Without intention, a location that does not meet the objective criteria — four *tefachim*, a normal resting place — is not halakhically significant. With intention, it becomes so.

### Applications

This principle applies in two directions in the *gemara* here. First, to the dog's mouth: when one intends to throw an object into the dog's mouth, that intention makes the mouth a *makom*, and one is *chayav*. Without that intention, the dog's mouth has no halakhic significance as a resting place, and the unexpected interception exempts. Second, to the carrying-in-one's-mouth case: *ein derech hotza'ah b'khach* — carrying in one's mouth is not the normal manner of carrying. Ordinarily this would render one exempt, since *melachot* must be performed in their typical fashion. But *keivan dekamichaven* — since one intends to carry the food this way — *machshavto mashveiah lei makom*: one's intention makes one's mouth a valid place for the purpose of the *melacha*. The *gemara* then applies this same logic back to the dog's mouth case: just as intention makes one's own mouth a valid *makom* for carrying, intention makes the dog's mouth a valid *makom* for resting. The two cases are structurally parallel, though they concern slightly different legal qualities — in one case the normality of the mode of carrying, in the other the adequacy of the resting place.

## End/New Section

### Hadran Alach

With this, we complete the perek — and with it, the extended treatment of *hotza'ah* that has defined so much of Masechet Shabbat until this point.

### Carrying Significance

The sheer volume of material devoted to carrying is worth pausing over. It occupies a remarkable proportion of the first hundred dapim of the masechet, dwarfing even the substantial treatment of *muktzah*. Two explanations suggest themselves. First, *hotza'ah* is what is sometimes called a *melacha geru'ah* — a degraded or non-creative *melacha*. Unlike plowing, weaving, or cooking, carrying does not transform or create; it merely moves. One might therefore have thought it less significant, and the *gemara* seems to elaborate on it precisely to establish its seriousness. Second, carrying was likely an area of active controversy with various sects — the *Tzedukim* and others — for whom its status as a *melacha* was less obvious than the creative *melachot*. The elaboration may reflect a conscious effort to define and defend the parameters of this less intuitive prohibition.

### Habona

We now turn to the second half of Masechet Shabbat, which opens with the *melacha* of *boneh* — building. The *Tosafot* notes the apparent discontinuity: *boneh* appears far down the list of the thirty-nine *melachot*, and it is not immediately obvious why the masechet jumps there directly from carrying. The *masechet* will in fact move between various *melachot* in an order that does not track the canonical sequence of the thirty-nine, and we will see in the coming dapim a similarly non-linear treatment.

## Building Minimum

### Kol Shehu

The *mishnah* asks: *habona kama yivneh veyechayev* — how much must one build in order to be *chayav*? The answer is stark: *habona kol shehu* — even the smallest amount. This stands in sharp contrast to the elaborate *shi'urim* required for other *melachot*. Carrying requires four *amot* in *reshut harabim*, writing requires two letters, and so on. Building requires nothing beyond the act itself.

### Other Acts

The *mishnah* then lists additional acts that fall within this framework: *vehamestatet* — one who chisels or smooths a stone; *vehamakeh bapatish* — one who strikes with a hammer; *uvema'atzad* — or with an adze (a tool with a flat, squared blade); *vehakodea kol shehu* — and one who drills even the smallest hole. All are *chayav*. These acts are all presented within the discussion of *boneh*, as activities that constitute or accompany the process of building.

### Mitkayem

The *mishnah* then interjects: *ze haklal, kol haoseh melacha umelachto mitkayemet, chayav* — the general rule is that anyone who performs a *melacha* that endures is *chayav*. The requirement of *mitkayem* — that the act must last — is well established in the laws of Shabbat. Writing one's name in frost on a window, for example, incurs no *korban*, because it does not last; it is *rabbinically* forbidden but one is *patur* from a *korban*. The surprising thing about this *ze haklal* is its placement — it appears mid-discussion of *boneh*, without transition, as if inserted rather than integrated. The *mishnah* never explicitly said that *boneh* requires *mitkayem*; it simply began describing acts of building and then suddenly announced this as the governing principle of all *melachot*.

Equally striking is the word *b'Shabbat*: *kol haoseh melacha umelachto mitkayemet b'Shabbat*. Does *b'Shabbat* qualify *mitkayem* — meaning the act must last through the duration of Shabbat — or does it qualify *melacha*, as Rashi reads it: *kol haoseh melacha b'Shabbat umelachto mitkayemet*, whoever performs a *melacha* on Shabbat such that it endures? Rashi's reading separates the words, making *b'Shabbat* the condition for the act rather than the duration of its endurance. The precise duration required for *mitkayem* is left undefined, and may well vary by *melacha* — a matter with real practical implications.

### Rabban Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: *af hamakeh bekurnas al hasadan beshaat melacha chayav* — even one who strikes the hammer on the anvil in the course of *melacha* is *chayav*, because it is like preparing the *melacha*. He is saying that one need not actually produce a completed object; participating in the activities that constitute the process of building — even a preparatory act like striking one's hammer against an anvil to shape or ready it — is itself sufficient for liability.

This opens an important conceptual question about *makeh b'patish* as it appears in the *mishnah*. Reading the *mishnah* as a whole, one might conclude that *makeh b'patish* is not a conceptually distinct category of *melacha* but simply one of the activities of *boneh* — as are chiseling, hammering a nail, and drilling. On this reading, *makeh b'patish* is context-dependent and activity-dependent: it is part of what builders do, and one need not complete a product. The *gemara* will, however, extract *makeh b'patish* into an independent and much broader category — one that functions as a catch-all for any act that completes or finalizes something, regardless of whether it involves construction at all.

## Kol Shehu Examples

### Kol Shehu Question

The *gemara* asks: *kol shehu l'mai chazi* — what can possibly be accomplished by the smallest conceivable act of building? What useful purpose can such a minimal act serve, and is there a parallel in the *mishkan* that would anchor this standard?

### Hole Example

Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *sheken ani chofer guma l'hatznia vah prutotav* — a poor person will dig a tiny hole to hide his pennies in. This constitutes a genuine act of building — not because it involves any structure, but because it creates a purposeful space. Even though digging sounds like *choresh* (plowing), if the purpose is storage rather than planting, the act is classified as *boneh*. The parallel in the *mishkan*: the artisans who sewed the curtains would dig small holes to store their needles.

### Tripod Example

Abaye objects to this example: *keivan d'mishtachlei lo avdi* — needles buried in the ground would rust; the *mishkan*'s craftsmen would not have done this. Instead, Abaye offers: *sheken ani oseh pitputei kirah ketanah lishpot aleha kdeirah* — a poor person makes a tiny tripod for a small pot, a minimal structure sufficient to support a miniature vessel. The parallel in the *mishkan*: those who cooked the dye-ingredients (*mevashlei samanim*) for coloring the curtains, if they needed only a small additional amount of dye, would construct a tiny tripod stand (*pitputei kirah ketanah*) and place a small pot on it (*yorah ketanah*) to produce the remaining dye. This is a genuine act of minimal construction with a real functional purpose.

This raises a question worth pausing on: why does the *gemara* need a scenario where the minimal act produces something fully usable? For other *melachot*, we do not require this. Two stitches are sufficient for liability in sewing even if the garment is nowhere near complete. The stitches represent meaningful progress. By analogy, one might expect that any contribution to a building project — laying a single brick, applying a small amount of mortar — should suffice for liability without needing to identify a case where the minimal act is itself complete. *Tosafot* suggests that the unusual *kol shehu* standard here specifically requires illustration, because such a minimal act of building is not self-evidently significant. The *gemara* therefore seeks a case where even the smallest act achieves something functionally complete — where *kol shehu* constitutes an entire act, not merely progress toward one.

### Filling Hole

Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers an alternative: *ein aniyut b'makom ashirut* — the Temple was not a place of poverty; one would not make teeny pots of dye there when ample supplies were available. His example instead: *sheken ba'al habayit sheyesh lo nekev b'birato v'satamo* — a wealthy householder who has a small hole in the wall of his estate fills it with a small amount of putty or plaster. That small act of filling is a complete and meaningful act of building — the hole is sealed, the structure is repaired. The parallel in the *mishkan*: a beam (*keresh*) that had been damaged by something like a wood-boring worm (*darna*) would be repaired by filling the hole with lead, sealing it. In both cases, the minimal act is itself a complete repair.

### Cornerstone

Shmuel rules: *hametzaded et ha'even, chayav* — if one positions a cornerstone, fitting it into its place in the ground without plaster, simply by maneuvering it into position and stabilizing it with dirt, one is *chayav* for *boneh*. This seemingly simple act — placing a large stone and setting it — constitutes building.

## Plaster Debate

### Stone/Plaster

A *braita* challenges Shmuel's ruling: *echad hanoten et ha'even v'echad hanoten et hatit, hanoten et hatit chayav* — if one person laid the stone and another laid the plaster, only the one who laid the plaster is *chayav*. The implication is that the stone alone is insufficient; the plaster is what constitutes *boneh*. How then can Shmuel say that placing a stone without plaster makes one *chayav*?

### Rabbi Yose View

The *gemara* turns to the end of the same *braita*: Rabbi Yose says *v'afilu he'ela v'hiniach al gabei dimos shel levanim, chayav* — even if one merely lifted a stone and set it on top of a row of bricks without any plaster, one is *chayav*. Rabbi Yose thus explicitly holds that no plaster is needed. The *gemara* understands that we do in fact rule in accordance with Rabbi Yose. The contradiction — plaster required versus not required — must therefore be resolved.

### Three Levels

The *gemara*'s resolution: *tlata binyana havei* — building a structure involves three distinct levels, and the requirements differ at each level. The bottom course (*tasa*), the foundation: here the stones must be worked into the ground by fitting them into dug-out positions and stabilizing them with earth (*afra*) — no plaster is used, but the process of fitting and stabilizing is what constitutes *boneh* at this level. The middle courses (*metzia*): here plaster (*tina*) is required, as the stones must be bonded together. The top course (*ila'ah*): here simply laying the stone flat without any mortar or plaster suffices — *b'hanachah b'alma*. The *braita*'s ruling that plaster is required refers to the middle rows; Rabbi Yose's ruling that no plaster is needed refers to the top row; and Shmuel's *metzaded* case involves the foundation course. With this resolution, all three positions cohere.

## Makeh B'Patish

### Mesatet

The *mishnah* listed *mesatet* — chiseling or smoothing a stone — among the acts subject to liability. The *gemara* now asks: *mishum mai mechayiv* — under which *melacha* category is one liable for chiseling a stone? This question is what introduces the distinction between *boneh* and *makeh b'patish* as competing, rather than overlapping, categories.

### Rav/Shmuel Stone

Rav says: *mishum boneh* — chiseling a stone is an act of building. Working on the stone is part of the construction process; it all falls under the broad umbrella of *boneh*. Shmuel says: *mishum makeh b'patish* — chiseling a stone is not yet building anything; one has only done something to the stone itself. That act, for Shmuel, falls into the category of *makeh b'patish* rather than *boneh*. Shmuel thus has a narrower conception of *boneh*: it requires that one actually incorporate something into a structure, not merely shape the materials beforehand.

### Chicken Coop

A second case is presented: *ha'oseh nekev b'luli shel tarnegolim* — one who makes a hole in a chicken coop for ventilation. Rav says: *mishum boneh* — one is completing the structure by providing it with air. Shmuel says: *mishum makeh b'patish*. The reason Shmuel does not classify this as *boneh* appears to be technical: Shmuel requires that a hole in a structure serve bidirectional airflow — air in and air out — to constitute the kind of opening that makes *boneh*. A hole made only for air to exit (or only for one direction) does not meet this standard, and so despite being made in a complete structure, it falls under *makeh b'patish* rather than *boneh* for Shmuel.

### Shovel Handle

A third case: *ha'oshufsah b'kufina d'mara* — one who fits a wooden handle into the socket of a hoe, attaching the two parts together. Rav says: *mishum boneh* — this is *binyan b'keilim*, building as applied to vessels. *Tosafot* notes that while the principle of *ein binyan b'keilim* — that the concept of building does not apply to vessels — is broadly accepted, Rav's position seems to be that when the act of assembly is tight, permanent, and substantial, it qualifies as *boneh* even for a vessel. Shmuel says: *mishum makeh b'patish*.

### Rav Broad View

The three cases together map out the full scope of Rav's position on *boneh*: it applies to acts done on raw stone (shaping materials for construction), to acts done to completed structures (making holes in a chicken coop), and even to acts done to vessels (attaching a handle to a tool). Rav's *boneh* is expansive. Shmuel's *boneh* is narrow, limited to acts that directly incorporate elements into a structure in the standard way; everything else he categorizes as *makeh b'patish*.

### Why Three Cases

The *gemara* explains why all three cases needed to be stated. Had only the stone case been given, one might have thought Rav called it *boneh* only because working with stone is the paradigmatic act of construction — *orech binyan bekach* — but that a chicken coop hole, which seems less classically structural, would prompt him to concede to Shmuel. Had only the chicken coop case been given, one might have thought Rav called it *boneh* there because it involves a real structure with airflow — *d'dami l'vinyan d'avdi l'avira* — but would concede for a shovel, which is not a structure at all. And had only the shovel case been given, one might have thought Shmuel restricted his disagreement to that case — a vessel plainly not resembling a building — but would agree with Rav in the stone and chicken coop cases. Each case is therefore necessary to demonstrate the full breadth of Rav's position and the full consistency of Shmuel's opposing view.

What emerges clearly from this discussion is that *makeh b'patish* is no longer merely one of the activities of *boneh*, as a surface reading of the *mishnah* might suggest. It has become a distinct and independent category — one that competes with *boneh* and absorbs acts that do not fit Shmuel's narrow definition of building. The implications of this distinction, and how far *makeh b'patish* extends as an independent *melacha*, will continue to unfold in the discussion that follows.
