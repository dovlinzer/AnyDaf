# Bava Metzia 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witnesses vs. Admission

### Rabbi Chiya's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קַל וָחוֹמֶר? וּמָה פִּיו שֶׁאֵין מְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן – מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁבוּעָה, עֵדִים שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעָה?
>
> **Translation:** And what is the explanation of this *a fortiori* inference? It should be explained as follows: If admitting to part of a claim by his own mouth, which does not render him liable to pay the money he admitted to owing, nevertheless obligates him to take an oath, with regard to the testimony of witnesses, which does render him liable to pay money, is it not logical that it should obligate him to take an oath with regard to the remaining sum?

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 3b, where the Gemara is examining the position of Rabbi Chiya. Rabbi Chiya maintains that just as a *modeh be-miktzat* — one who admits to part of a monetary claim — is required to take an oath on the remainder, the same obligation applies when witnesses partially substantiate the claim. That is, if the plaintiff claims a hundred and witnesses testify that the defendant owes fifty, the defendant must swear regarding the other fifty, even though he has neither admitted nor fully denied the claim. The Gemara acknowledges upfront that the internal logic driving the *shvua* in the admission case — the defendant's self-interested rationalization — is less present when the testimony comes from external witnesses. Nevertheless, Rabbi Chiya argues that the formal weight of witnesses is sufficient, and that argument rests on a *kal va-chomer*: since the evidentiary strength of witnesses exceeds that of a party's own admission, the oath requirement should apply at least as strongly.

### Power Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מָמוֹן״ – קְנָס. וּמָה פִּיו שֶׁאֵין מְחַיְּיבוֹ קְנָס, מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁבוּעָה! עֵדִים שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ קְנָס, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** What is the money that one is not liable to pay based on his own admission? It is referring to the payment of a fine [*kenas*]. In all cases where the Torah imposes a fine, if the defendant admits his liability voluntarily he is not required to pay it. Therefore, the *a fortiori* inference is as follows: If the admission of one's own mouth, which does not render him liable to pay a fine, nevertheless renders him liable to take an oath with regard to the part of the claim to which he did not admit, with regard to the testimony of witnesses, which does render him liable to pay a fine, is it not logical that it should render him liable to take an oath with regard to the remaining sum?

The basis for the *kal va-chomer* is a concrete legal asymmetry: witnesses can compel a defendant to pay a *knas* (a penal fine), whereas a party's own admission cannot. The principle *ein adam mesim atzmo rasha* — a person cannot render himself a *rasha* — precludes self-incriminating testimony in the domain of penalties, lashes, and capital punishment. In monetary matters, however, admission is fully operative; indeed, it obligates payment. The relevant question here is not the moral status of the statement but the comparative evidentiary force of witnesses versus admission across legal categories. In the domain of *knas*, witnesses are categorically stronger.

### Ein Adam Mesim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְפִיו – שֶׁכֵּן מְחַיְּיבוֹ קׇרְבָּן, תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדִים שֶׁאֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ קׇרְבָּן!
>
> **Translation:** What is notable about the admission of one's mouth? It is notable in that it renders him liable to bring an offering. One who admits that he transgressed a prohibition unwittingly is obligated to bring an offering for atonement. Would you say that the *halakhot* of admission apply with regard to the testimony of witnesses, which, in a case where they testify that one transgressed a prohibition, does not obligate him to bring an offering if he denies it?

The Gemara complicates this picture by pointing out that a party's admission can trigger the obligation to bring a *korban*, whereas witnesses cannot — since bringing a *korban* is bound up with the acceptance of guilt, and guilt cannot be imposed by external testimony. The Gemara responds that this follows the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that witnesses carry so sweeping a legal force that they can even impose the obligation to bring a *korban* without any personal acceptance of culpability. Under Rabbi Meir's view, witnesses are stronger than admission across the board.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: רַבִּי חִיָּיא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: עֵדִים מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ קׇרְבָּן מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** This is not difficult. Rabbi Ḥiyya holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who says that the testimony of witnesses renders him liable to bring an offering, based on an *a fortiori* inference.

The Gemara then raises *asham*: a party's admission obligates an *asham*, whereas witnesses do not. The Gemara dismisses this quickly — an *asham* is simply a type of *korban*, and since under Rabbi Meir witnesses can compel a *korban*, they can equally compel an *asham*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מָה לְפִיו – שֶׁכֵּן מְחַיְּיבוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ. הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, רַבִּי חִיָּיא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּמְחַיֵּיב לֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר – מְחַיֵּיב לֵיהּ חוֹמֶשׁ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, what is notable about the admission of one's mouth? It is notable in that it renders one who unlawfully possessed the money of another liable to pay an additional one-fifth of the value of that money when he returns it of his own accord (see Leviticus 5:20–26). By contrast, if witnesses testify that he unlawfully possessed the money of another, he is not obligated to add one-fifth to his payment. The Gemara answers: This is not difficult; Rabbi Ḥiyya holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir. Just as Rabbi Meir holds that the testimony of witnesses renders one liable to bring an offering due to an *a fortiori* inference, he also holds that the testimony of witnesses renders one liable to add one-fifth, via an *a fortiori* inference.

A more potent objection follows: admission obligates payment of a *chomesh* (an added fifth), as in the case of falsely denying a bailment under oath and later confessing — whereas witnesses who expose the lie do not trigger the *chomesh*. Here the Gemara invokes Rabbi Meir's position again: just as Rabbi Meir holds that witnesses can impose the *korban* obligation without personal acceptance of guilt, so too they can impose the *chomesh* obligation via a *kal va-chomer* from the *korban*. The logic is consistent: if the most personal expression of atonement — the *korban* — can be imposed externally by witnesses under Rabbi Meir, then so can the monetarily quantifiable *chomesh*. Thus Rabbi Chiya's position is understood as consistent with that of Rabbi Meir.

### Admission Irreducibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מָה לְפִיו – שֶׁכֵּן אֵינוֹ בְּהַכְחָשָׁה וּבַהֲזָמָה, תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדִים שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָן בְּהַכְחָשָׁה וּבַהֲזָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, what is notable about the admission of one's mouth? It is notable in that it is not subject to contradiction or to refutation as applies to the testimony of conspiring witnesses, as the testimony of witnesses cannot negate the admission of a litigant. Would you say the same *halakhot* with regard to witnesses, who are subject to contradiction and to refutation as conspiring witnesses? Evidently, the testimony of witnesses is weaker, in some aspects, than the admission of a litigant.

The Gemara, however, identifies a domain in which witnesses are unambiguously weaker than admission and which cannot be dissolved by appeal to Rabbi Meir. When a party admits to a monetary obligation, that admission is absolutely irreducible: no contrary evidence, no contradictory witnesses, and no proof that the circumstances described never occurred can undo it. The principle *hoda'at ba'al din ki-me'ah eidim dami* — a litigant's admission is equivalent to a hundred witnesses — is not really a statement about the factual weight of testimony. It reflects a deeper point: a monetary admission is not merely an assertion about what happened in the world; it functions as a present act of obligation, independent of the historical facts. If I say I owe David a hundred dollars, I owe David a hundred dollars, even if a thousand witnesses could prove I was elsewhere when the alleged loan was made.

Witnesses, by contrast, are subject to *hachshasha* (contradiction through counter-testimony) and *hazama* (formal refutation by proof that the witnesses were elsewhere). There is therefore at least one dimension in which witnesses are not stronger than admission. This means the *kal va-chomer* cannot operate as a blanket rule, and the obligation to swear in the case of partial witness testimony cannot be derived from it unconditionally.

## Kal VaChomer from One Witness

### One to Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָתְיָא מֵעֵד אֶחָד: וּמָה עֵד אֶחָד שֶׁאֵין מְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן, מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁבוּעָה – עֵדִים שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rabbi Ḥiyya's *a fortiori* inference is apparently derived from the *halakha* of the testimony of one witness. If the testimony of one witness, which does not render the defendant liable to pay money, obligates him to take an oath to contradict the testimony, is it not logical that the testimony of two witnesses, which render one liable to pay money, also obligates him to take an oath?

Since the comparison of two witnesses to self-admission is undermined by the irreducibility of admission, the Gemara pivots to a different *kal va-chomer*: from *eid echad* (a single witness) to two witnesses. The structure is as follows: a single witness, whose testimony cannot compel monetary payment, nonetheless obligates the defendant to take an oath to contradict him. If one witness — who cannot make the defendant pay — generates the obligation to swear, then two witnesses — who can make the defendant pay — should certainly generate the obligation to swear on the remainder. Two witnesses are in every respect stronger than one, so there is no basis for the asymmetry that troubled the prior comparison.

### Contradiction Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְעֵד אֶחָד שֶׁכֵּן עַל מָה שֶׁהוּא מֵעִיד הוּא נִשְׁבָּע, תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדִים – שֶׁעַל מַה שֶּׁכָּפַר הוּא נִשְׁבָּע!
>
> **Translation:** What is notable about the testimony of one witness? It is notable in that the defendant takes an oath with regard to the matter concerning which he testifies, not with regard to other claims raised by the claimant. Would you say the same with regard to two witnesses, in which case the defendant takes an oath with regard to the claim that he denies, and not with regard to the debt about which they testify?

The Gemara, however, identifies a fundamental structural difference between the two cases. When one witness says the defendant owes a hundred dollars and the defendant denies it, the *shvua* functions as a direct contradiction of the witness's testimony. The defendant swears against precisely what the witness asserts. But in the scenario under discussion — where two witnesses testify to fifty dollars and the defendant has already paid that fifty — the *shvua* would be taken on the *remaining* fifty, a claim on which the witnesses say nothing. The defendant is not swearing against the witnesses; he is swearing on something else entirely. The *shvua* in the *eid echad* case and the proposed *shvua* in the two-witness case operate on entirely different logic, and one cannot be used to prove the other.

### Gilgul Shvua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אָתֵי מִגִּלְגּוּל שְׁבוּעָה דְּעֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav Pappa said: Rabbi Ḥiyya's *a fortiori* inference is derived from the extension of an oath that the testimony of one witness obligates him to take. Once a defendant is obligated to take an oath, the plaintiff can demand that he take an oath with regard to other claims that he has against him as well.

At this point Rav Papa introduces the mechanism of *gilgul shvua* — the "rolling oath." The principle is that once a court compels a party to take an oath in one context, that oath can be extended to cover additional claims by the opposing party. The litigant, having already been placed under oath, is required to swear on any other disputed matter the plaintiff raises. This is why appearing before a court under oath is so consequential: the first oath can cascade into an obligation to swear on unrelated matters.

Rav Papa applies this to the *eid echad* scenario. The single witness generates an initial *shvua*. Once that *shvua* is in place, the plaintiff can use the *gilgul* mechanism to compel the defendant to swear on a separate, unrelated claim. Now compare this to the two-witness case: if two witnesses say the defendant owes fifty, and the defendant — who according to Rabbi Chiya must take an oath on the remainder — is in some sense already "under oath," could that lead, via *gilgul*, to an oath on the other fifty? The argument is that since one witness indirectly generates an oath through the *gilgul* mechanism on claims beyond his testimony, two witnesses — who are stronger — should at least be able to generate an oath on what remains of the very claim at issue.

### Gilgul Inapplicable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְגִלְגּוּל שְׁבוּעָה דְּעֵד אֶחָד, שֶׁכֵּן שְׁבוּעָה גּוֹרֶרֶת שְׁבוּעָה, תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדִים – דְּמָמוֹן קָא מְחַיְּיבִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: What is notable about the extension of an oath that is obligated by the testimony of one witness? It is notable in that one oath leads to another oath. The obligation to take the second oath is not caused directly by the witness. Would you say the same in the case of witnesses, whose testimony renders the defendant liable to pay money? Incurring liability to pay one part of the claim does not extend further and incur liability to pay the rest.

The Gemara rejects this reasoning sharply. The *gilgul shvua* in the *eid echad* case works because it piggybacks on an existing *shvua*: one oath drags another in its wake (*shvua goreret shvua*). But in the two-witness case, what is produced initially is not an oath but a monetary payment. The defendant pays fifty dollars. Payment does not function like an oath; it cannot serve as a platform for *gilgul*. The cascade requires an initial *shvua* as its foundation, and absent that, the mechanism does not engage. The Gemara's formulation is precise: *tomar be-eidim de-mamona kam-chaivei* — in the case of witnesses, what is being compelled is money, not an oath, and money cannot roll into further oath-taking the way an oath can.

## Three Cases Compared

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁעַל יְדֵי טַעֲנָה וּכְפִירָה הֵן בָּאִים, וְנִשְׁבָּע. אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא עֵדִים, שֶׁעַל יְדֵי טַעֲנָה וּכְפִירָה הֵם בָּאִים – וְנִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** And the derivation has reverted to its starting point. At this point, the *halakha* is derived from a combination of the two sources: The aspect of this case, admission, is not like the aspect of that case, the extension of an oath resulting from the testimony of one witness, and the aspect of that case is not like the aspect of this case. Their common denominator is that these cases come before the court with a claim and its denial, and the defendant is obligated to take an oath. I will also include in the obligation to take an oath the case of Rabbi Ḥiyya, where there is testimony of two witnesses about part of the debt, which comes before the court with a claim and its denial. And therefore the defendant is obligated to take an oath. This is the inference to which Rabbi Ḥiyya was referring.

The Gemara now steps back to construct the argument more carefully, using the classical method of *tzad ha-shaveh* — reasoning from a common denominator across two cases to establish a rule applicable to a third. The two established cases where a *shvua* is required are: (1) *eid echad*, where a single witness testifies to the full claim; and (2) *modeh be-miktzat*, where the defendant admits to part. The proposed third case is two witnesses who establish part of the claim. The Gemara sets up this triangular structure explicitly.

Consider the diagram: in the *eid echad* case, the defendant takes a *shvua* to directly contradict the witness's testimony. In the *modeh be-miktzat* case, the defendant takes a *shvua* on what he has not admitted — the rest of the claim. These are two distinct logical structures for how a *shvua* is generated. The defendant in the *eid echad* case swears against what is testified; the defendant in the *modeh be-miktzat* case swears on what he denies despite admitting to part. Each case has a feature the other lacks.

The Gemara proposes that neither case alone can serve as a proof for the two-witness scenario, but that their common denominator can. What is that common denominator? *Shel-ta'anat kefirah hen ba'in* — both cases arise in the context of a claim that has been at least partially substantiated, followed by a counter-claim. In both cases, the opposing party's position has received some evidentiary backing, and in both cases a *shvua* results. By analogy, when two witnesses substantiate part of a claim and the defendant denies the remainder, the same logic should apply: the claim has been substantiated, and the defendant should take an oath. This is Rabbi Chiya's argument.

### Distinguishing Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן. תֹּאמַר בְּעֵדִים שֶׁכֵּן הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן.
>
> **Translation:** What is notable about their common denominator, i.e., the common denominator shared by admission to part of a claim by the defendant and the extension of an oath resulting from the testimony of one witness? It is notable in that the defendant does not assume the presumptive status of one who falsely denies his debts. He has not been proven to be lying, so he is trusted to take an oath. Would you say the same with regard to the case of two witnesses who contradict the defendant's denial of the plaintiff's claim, where the defendant assumes the presumptive status of one who falsely denies his debts? In this case, he is no longer deemed trustworthy and his oath may not be credible.

The Gemara walks through why neither case can single-handedly establish the rule for the other, and why only the common denominator approach works. The *eid echad* case cannot prove the *modeh be-miktzat* structure, because in *eid echad* the *shvua* targets the testimony itself, not a separate remainder. The *modeh be-miktzat* case cannot prove the *eid echad* structure because admission cannot be contradicted, giving it a strength that testimony lacks. Yet the *eid echad* case has its own advantage over admission: it can be contradicted, and despite that vulnerability it still generates a *shvua*. These asymmetries create a perfect standoff in which neither case is simply superior to the other, which is exactly the condition (*lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh*) that validates the common denominator argument.

The abstraction the Gemara arrives at is clean: whenever there is something that substantiates the claim — whether that is a partial admission or the testimony of a single witness — the opposing party must take an oath. When two witnesses substantiate part of a claim, the same logic applies. The common shape in all these cases is that the plaintiff's claim has been given evidentiary footing, and so a *shvua* is warranted.

### Thief vs. Denier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְעֵדִים מִי הוּחְזַק כַּפְרָן! וְהָאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַכּוֹפֵר בְּמִלְוֶה – כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת. בְּפִיקָּדוֹן – פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** But in a case where his denial is contradicted by two witnesses, does he assume the presumptive status of one who falsely denies his debts? But doesn't Rav Idi bar Avin say that Rav Ḥisda says: One who denies a claim that he received a loan and is contradicted by witnesses is fit to bear witness in a different case. He does not assume the status of a confirmed liar, as perhaps he intended to return the money afterward and denied the claim only in order to buy time until he acquired the necessary funds to repay the loan. By contrast, if one denies receiving a deposit and witnesses testify that he is lying, he is disqualified from bearing witness in other cases, as in that case he has no reason to buy time and is clearly a robber. Therefore, Rabbi Ḥiyya's *a fortiori* inference stands.

The Gemara raises a more pointed challenge to the common denominator. In both established cases — *eid echad* and *modeh be-miktzat* — there is no direct evidence that the defendant is a thief or a deliberate liar. The defendant in the admission case has come forward voluntarily; the defendant in the *eid echad* case is contradicting a single, non-dispositive witness. In neither case has the defendant been proven to be dishonest. A *shvua* makes sense precisely because the defendant retains enough credibility to be bound by one.

But in the two-witness scenario under discussion, the defendant declared he owed nothing, and two witnesses established that he owes fifty. The witnesses have, in effect, proven him a liar — *huchzak kafran*. If he is a proven liar, why should we think a *shvua* will accomplish anything? Who are we kidding by imposing an oath on someone who has already demonstrated his willingness to deny what is true?

### Trustworthiness

The Gemara responds by drawing a critical distinction between a *kafran* in monetary matters and one in matters of *pikadon* (bailment of a concrete object). The Gemara cites the ruling of Rabbi Avin in the name of Rav Chisda: one who denies a monetary debt is still *kasher le-edut* — qualified to testify. He has not been disqualified. This is because the denial of a monetary obligation may not reflect an intent to steal; the defendant may simply be delaying payment because he lacks the funds at the moment, intending to pay eventually. He may be dishonest about the present state of the debt without being a *gazlan*. A *gazlan*, who has stolen a concrete object and denies possessing it, is *pasul le-edut* because his denial demonstrates a present intention to appropriate something belonging to another. By contrast, denying a monetary claim is not equivalent to theft in the same direct sense. The defendant who denies owing money and is later shown to owe it remains, technically, a reliable oath-taker. His *shvua* retains legal meaning.

This resolves the concern: the two-witness case does not render the defendant categorically untrustworthy in the way that would make a *shvua* meaningless. The claim has been substantiated, he is not disqualified as a swearer, and the common denominator argument holds. Rabbi Chiya's position is therefore sound: when witnesses partially substantiate a claim, the defendant takes an oath on the remainder.

## Tallit vs. Admission

### Asymmetry Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּקָאָמַר וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לְמַלְוֶה אִית לֵיהּ סָהֲדֵי, לְלֹוֶה לֵית לֵיהּ סָהֲדֵי דְּלָא מַסֵּיק לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, דְּאִי הֲווֹ לֵיהּ סָהֲדֵי לְלֹוֶה דְּלָא מַסֵּיק לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי – לָא בָּעֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְאִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי, הָכָא כִּי הֵיכִי דַּאֲנַן סָהֲדֵי בְּהַאי – אֲנַן סָהֲדֵי בְּהַאי, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי מִשְׁתַּבְעִי!
>
> **Translation:** But with regard to that which was stated: And the *tanna* of the mishna also taught a similar *halakha* to that of Rabbi Ḥiyya, there is a difficulty. Is the case of the mishna comparable to the *halakha* of Rabbi Ḥiyya? There, in the case of witnesses to a loan, the creditor has witnesses to support his claim that there was a loan while the debtor does not have witnesses to support his claim that he does not owe the creditor anything. As, if the debtor had witnesses to support his claim that he does not owe the creditor anything, Rabbi Ḥiyya would not require him to take an oath. By contrast, here, in the case of the mishna, just as it is clear to us that this claimant has a right to the garment, as he is holding it, so too, is it clear to us that that other claimant has a right to the garment, as he is also holding it. Yet nevertheless, in the mishna each party is required to take an oath.

Having established the theoretical soundness of Rabbi Chiya's ruling, the Gemara turns to his attempt to ground it in the *mishna* of *shenayim ochazin be-tallit* — two people holding a garment and each claiming it entirely. Rabbi Chiya sought to derive support for his ruling from this *mishna*, but the Gemara identifies a fundamental asymmetry that makes the comparison untenable.

In the classic *modeh be-miktzat* scenario underlying Rabbi Chiya's ruling: the plaintiff claims a hundred, has witnesses to fifty, and the defendant has no witnesses whatsoever. The plaintiff has evidentiary backing; the defendant has none. That asymmetry is precisely why the defendant must swear. But in the *tallit* case, the evidentiary footing is exactly equal on both sides. Each party holds half the garment; each party's possession of his half is just as strong a proof as the other's. Whatever evidence the plaintiff can marshal, the defendant has the mirror image.

### Holding Equals Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּתָפֵיס – אֲנַן סָהֲדֵי דְּמַאי דְּתָפֵיס הֵילָךְ הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי: יִשָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** And here, in the mishna, since each one grasps half the garment, it is clear to us that what one grasps is in his possession, just as if the other one had said to him: Here you are, I am giving it to you. And the mishna teaches that he takes an oath. Evidently, in a case where one denies part of a claim that is brought against him, and with regard to the rest of the claim he says to the claimant: Here you are, he is obligated to take an oath.

Rabbi Chiya had attempted to frame the *tallit* case as analogous to *modeh be-miktzat* by treating each party's holding as a kind of implicit admission: since I am holding half, it is as though I am conceding that half and contesting only the other half. That concession-through-possession is structurally similar to handing over the admitted portion, creating a *heilech*-like situation and leaving the remainder in dispute.

### Equal Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין! אָמַר לְךָ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַתְנִיתִין תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** But according to the opinion of Rav Sheshet, the mishna is difficult, as it seems to be a comparable case and yet an oath is required. The Gemara answers: Rav Sheshet could have said to you: The oath mentioned in the mishna is a rabbinic ordinance, which pertains specifically to that case. In general, a debtor who immediately hands over the money that he admits to owing is exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara flatly rejects this framing. The fact that I hold half cannot be treated as an admission or concession in my favor, because the same logic applies equally to my counterpart: his holding of half is equally evidence of his claim. *Ki heichi de-anan sahadi be-hai, anan sahadi be-hai* — the same way we have witnesses for this party's entitlement to half, we have witnesses for the other party's entitlement to half. There is no asymmetry, and therefore no *modeh be-miktzat* structure whatsoever.

### Different Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? אִין תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא. וּמִיהוּ, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא ״הֵילָךְ״ חַיָּיב – מְתַקְּנִי רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא ״הֵילָךְ״ פָּטוּר – מְתַקְּנִי רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה דְּלֵיתָא דִּכְווֹתַהּ בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** And how would the other *amora*, Rabbi Ḥiyya, respond to this assertion? Indeed, he would agree that it is a rabbinic ordinance. However, granted, if you say that by Torah law one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath, that explains why the Sages instituted the oath mentioned in the mishna, as it is similar to an oath administered by Torah law. But if you say that by Torah law one who says: Here you are, is exempt from taking an oath, would the Sages institute an oath that has no corresponding oath in Torah law? Clearly, there is a basis for the oath instituted by the Sages in Torah law, and that basis is the case where the defendant says: Here you are.

The conclusion is that the *mishna* of *shenayim ochazin* cannot be used to support the teaching about two witnesses and partial oath-taking. However, the Gemara clarifies that the *mishna* does support a different teaching of Rabbi Chiya — namely, the *heilech* ruling. It is to that teaching, and that debate, that the Gemara now turns.

## Heilech Question

### Rabbi Chiya on Heilech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אִיתְּמַר ״וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא״ – אַאִידַּךְ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אִיתְּמַר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״ וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא חֲמִישִּׁים זוּז, וְהֵילָךְ״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, when the phrase was stated: And the *tanna* of the mishna also taught a similar *halakha*, it was stated with regard to another statement of Rabbi Ḥiyya. As Rabbi Ḥiyya says: If one says to another: I have one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says in response: You have only fifty dinars in my possession, and here you are, handing him the money, he is obligated to take an oath that he does not owe the remainder.

Rabbi Chiya's ruling on *heilech* concerns the following case: the plaintiff claims a hundred; the defendant says he owes only fifty and immediately hands over the fifty (*ve-heilech* — "and here it is"). Does this constitute *modeh be-miktzat*, requiring an oath on the remaining fifty? Or does the immediate payment of the admitted amount remove it from the table entirely, leaving the parties in a posture of a simple full-denial case regarding the remainder?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא – ״הֵילָךְ״ נָמֵי כְּמוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** What is the reason? One who says: Here you are, while immediately giving the money, is also considered like one who admits to part of the claim. It cannot be reasoned that by immediately handing over the amount to which he admits, the defendant thereby reduces the claim by the fifty dinars that he denies owing, and he is consequently exempt from taking an oath like any defendant who denies the claim entirely.

Rabbi Chiya holds that even with *heilech*, it remains *modeh be-miktzat*, and the defendant must take an oath on the rest. The reasoning is that the moment of admission is what counts. The defendant admitted to fifty, and that admission established a partial concession. The fact that he immediately paid does not retroactively dissolve the admission's legal character; *ve-heilech nami ke-modeh be-miktzat ha-ta'ana dami* — *heilech* is also treated as a partial admission.

The Gemara sought to bring support for this from the *tallit* case: in *shenayim ochazin*, each party holds half — which is structurally similar to having already handed over half (*heilech*) — and yet both parties are required to swear. This suggests that even when the "admitted" portion is effectively already transferred, a *shvua* is still required on the remainder. As we have seen, however, this proof was undermined by the equal-and-opposite evidentiary structure of the *tallit* case, which is ultimately a *takkanat chachamim* rather than a biblical *modeh be-miktzat*.

### Rav Sheshet's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר ״הֵילָךְ פָּטוּר״. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״הֵילָךְ״, הָנֵי זוּזִי דְּקָא מוֹדֵי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ – כְּמַאן דְּנָקֵיט לְהוּ מַלְוֶה דָּמֵי. בְּאִינָךְ חֲמִשִּׁים הָא לָא מוֹדֵי, הִלְכָּךְ לֵיכָּא הוֹדָאַת מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** And Rav Sheshet says: One who says about part of the claim: Here you are, and denies the rest of the claim, is exempt from taking an oath about the rest. What is the reason? Since he said to him: Here you are, those dinars that he admitted to owing are considered as if the creditor has them in his possession already, and with regard to the other fifty dinars, the defendant did not admit to owing them. Therefore, there is no admission to part of the claim.

Rav Sheshet disagrees categorically. Once the defendant says *heilech* and hands over the fifty, that money is legally as if it already sits in the plaintiff's pocket. The admitted portion is off the table entirely. The only live dispute is now over the remaining fifty, on which the defendant is denying any obligation. That is a case of *kofer ha-kol* regarding fifty dollars, not *modeh be-miktzat* regarding a hundred. And a *kofer ha-kol* does not take a *shvua* under biblical law. Rav Sheshet's formulation is precise: *hanei zuzzei de-ka modeh be-gavaihu ke-man de-nakit lehu malveh dami* — the coins he admits to are as if already in the lender's possession — *ve-inach chamishim, ha lo modeh lei, ve-hava lei kofer ha-kol* — and on the other fifty he is a full denier. *Heilech patur.*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין! אָמַר לְךָ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַתְנִיתִין תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** But according to the opinion of Rav Sheshet, the mishna is difficult, as it seems to be a comparable case and yet an oath is required. The Gemara answers: Rav Sheshet could have said to you: The oath mentioned in the mishna is a rabbinic ordinance, which pertains specifically to that case. In general, a debtor who immediately hands over the money that he admits to owing is exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara then challenges Rav Sheshet from the *mishna* of *shenayim ochazin*: in the *tallit* case, each party's holding is structurally like *heilech* — effectively already in possession — yet both are required to swear. This seems to contradict Rav Sheshet's view.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? אִין תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא. וּמִיהוּ, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא ״הֵילָךְ״ חַיָּיב – מְתַקְּנִי רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא ״הֵילָךְ״ פָּטוּר – מְתַקְּנִי רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה דְּלֵיתָא דִּכְווֹתַהּ בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** And how would the other *amora*, Rabbi Ḥiyya, respond to this assertion? Indeed, he would agree that it is a rabbinic ordinance. However, granted, if you say that by Torah law one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath, that explains why the Sages instituted the oath mentioned in the mishna, as it is similar to an oath administered by Torah law. But if you say that by Torah law one who says: Here you are, is exempt from taking an oath, would the Sages institute an oath that has no corresponding oath in Torah law? Clearly, there is a basis for the oath instituted by the Sages in Torah law, and that basis is the case where the defendant says: Here you are.

Rav Sheshet responds that the *tallit* case is not a biblical *modeh be-miktzat* at all. It is a *takkanat chachamim*. The Rabbis enacted the oath requirement in that case either to discourage people from grabbing objects and claiming them as their own, or to create a more accurate evidentiary process. The *tallit* oath is an entirely rabbinic institution with no biblical *modeh be-miktzat* structure underlying it.

The Gemara presses further: if *heilech* is *chayav* biblically, then the rabbinic enactment of the *tallit* oath is modeled on a biblical precedent — the Rabbis made a *shvua* parallel to a *d'Oraita*. But if *heilech* is *patur* biblically, the Rabbis would be instituting a *shvua* for which there is no biblical analogue whatsoever. Would the Sages enact something that has no parallel in Torah law?

### Policy Argument

This is Rabbi Chiya's response to Rav Sheshet: the coherence of the rabbinic enactment in the *tallit* case presupposes that there is a biblical category — *modeh be-miktzat* with *heilech* — that generates an oath. If that biblical category did not exist, the Rabbis could not model the *tallit* oath on anything. The very existence of a rabbinic *shvua* in the *tallit* case argues for the existence of a biblical precedent, which means *heilech* must be *chayav* at the biblical level. Rabbi Chiya thus emerges with his original position intact, at least structurally, even if the *tallit mishna* itself cannot directly prove his case.

## Damaged Document Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְלָעִים, דִּינָרִין. מַלְוֶה אוֹמֵר חָמֵשׁ, וְלֹוֶה אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְהוֹדָה מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה – יִשָּׁבַע. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה, וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If it is written in a promissory note that one borrowed an unspecified amount of *sela'im*, or that one borrowed an unspecified amount of dinars, and the creditor says: I lent you five *sela*, and the debtor says: You lent me only three, in this case Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Since the debtor admitted to part of the claim, he takes an oath. Rabbi Akiva says: Since the wording of the note indicates only that he owes two dinars, the minimum plural amount, by admitting that he owes three he is merely the equivalent of one returning a lost item, and he is exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* involving a damaged *shtar*. The document records a loan in the currency *dinarim*, but the specific numeral indicating the amount has been obscured or worn away. What the document clearly preserves is the word for a denomination that minimally means two. The plaintiff claims the loan was for five; the defendant claims it was for three. The two-*dinar* minimum is established beyond dispute by the face of the document.

### Rabban Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Elazar rules that since the defendant admitted to more than the two-*dinar* minimum — that is, since she said three rather than two — she has thereby admitted to part of the plaintiff's claim, and she must take an oath on the rest. The admission to three (one above the undisputed minimum of two) constitutes *modeh be-miktzat*, and the *shvua* follows accordingly.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: one who admits to three is like one who *returns a lost object* (*meshiv aveidah*), and is therefore exempt. The principle of *meshiv aveidah* holds that when a person voluntarily returns something he could have kept without legal consequence, we do not reward that honesty by imposing further obligations on him. The defendant here could have simply said two — the minimum already established by the document — and denied everything beyond it without contradiction. By admitting to three, she volunteered information she could have withheld. To penalize her with an oath would be counterproductive: it would discourage honest admissions.

Whether Rabbi Akiva is making a *takanah* argument (societal policy) or a *migo* argument (since she could have said two, her admission of three should be trusted) is left somewhat open, but the practical outcome is the same: admitting to three is exempt.

### Two vs. Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר, הוֹאִיל וְהוֹדָה מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה – יִשָּׁבַע. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר שָׁלֹשׁ, הָא שְׁתַּיִם פָּטוּר, וְהַאי שְׁטָר דְּקָמוֹדֵי בֵּיהּ הֵילָךְ הוּא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֵילָךְ פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** In any event, the *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Since the debtor admitted to part of the claim, he takes an oath. This indicates that the reason that he is obligated to take an oath is specifically because he said that he owes three dinars, but had he admitted to owing only two, he would have been exempt from taking an oath. And concerning the minimal obligation recorded in this promissory note, to which he admits, which is two dinars, it is as though he said: Here you are. An obligation recorded in a promissory note is tantamount to an obligation concerning which the defendant says: Here you are. And therefore, conclude from it that one who says: Here you are, is exempt from taking an oath with regard to the part of the claim he denies.

The Gemara analyzes the relationship between the two positions. Rabban Shimon ben Elazar's statement says: *since she admitted to part, she must swear* — implying that admitting to three triggers *modeh be-miktzat*. The implicit inference is that had she said two, she would be *patur*. The Gemara asks: why should two be *patur*? Surely if I claim five and she admits to two, that is *modeh be-miktzat*?

The answer is that the two-*dinar* minimum is already established by the *shtar* before any admission is made. Saying two is not an admission of anything beyond what the document already proves. The two *dinarim* are effectively off the table before litigation even begins. Admitting to two is therefore structurally like *heilech* — the money was already effectively allocated — or more precisely, it is not an admission at all, since the *shtar* backs it up entirely. This is why two is *patur*.

## Technical Exemptions

### Shiud and Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ שְׁתַּיִם חַיָּיב, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי שָׁלֹשׁ, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הָוֵי וּפָטוּר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה הָוֵי, וְחַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: No, actually I will say to you that even if he admitted that he owes two dinars he is obligated to take an oath, and the reason that the *baraita* teaches the dispute specifically with regard to the case of three dinars is not to exclude a case where he admits that he owes only two, but rather it is to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who says that he is the equivalent of one returning a lost item, and he is therefore exempt. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches us that he is considered like one who admits to part of the claim, and he is obligated to take an oath.

Having established that saying two is *patur*, the Gemara offers two distinct technical reasons why. The first, simpler reason is that the *shtar* itself backs up the claim to two *dinarim*, so that the defendant's "admission" of two adds nothing — it merely confirms what the document already establishes. But the Gemara also introduces a more technical ground: *mishum de-havei lei shtar shibud karkaot ve-ain nishba'in al kfirat shibud karkaot* — because the *shtar* creates a lien (*shibud*) on the borrower's land, and one does not take an oath regarding the denial (or admission) of land liens.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְהוֹדָה מִקְצָת הַטְּעָנָה יִשָּׁבַע, ״אַף זֶה יִשָּׁבַע״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, the *baraita* should have been phrased differently. If the defendant is obligated to take an oath even in a case where he admits that he owes two dinars, rather than stating that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Since the debtor admitted to part of the claim, he takes an oath, the *baraita* should have stated: Even this one, who admits to owing three dinars, takes an oath, in addition to one who admits to owing two dinars.

The mechanics are as follows: a *shtar* of indebtedness creates a *shibud* on the debtor's land for the amount provable from the document. If the document is damaged and the only legible amount is two *dinarim*, then the *shibud* on land is for two *dinarim*, regardless of what the parties claim the true loan amount was. When the defendant admits to two, what she is admitting is precisely the two-*dinar* *shibud* — an obligation that is, in legal character, tied to land. And the established rule is that there is no *shvua* over matters relating to land: land cannot be stolen (*karka einah nigzelet*), and oaths do not apply to real-property obligations in the same way they apply to movable assets.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לְעוֹלָם שְׁתַּיִם פָּטוּר, וְהֵילָךְ חַיָּיב. וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּקָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, that explanation should be rejected. Actually, if he admits that he owes two dinars, he is exempt from taking an oath, but nevertheless, one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath. And the reason for this distinction is that here, the case is different, as the note supports him, i.e., it indicates that he owes two dinars. Therefore, he is exempt from taking an oath with regard to the rest.

Therefore, even if I claim a thousand and the defendant admits only to the two-*dinar* *shibud* provable by the *shtar*, her entire admission consists of a land-lien obligation. That admission does not constitute *modeh be-miktzat* for purposes of triggering a *shvua*, because the admission is entirely in the domain of land. Had she admitted to three, she would be admitting to one additional *dinar* beyond the *shibud* — a cash obligation untethered from land — and that one *dinar* admission, set against the remainder of the claim, would be a genuine *modeh be-miktzat*.

### Shtar's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁטָר שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת, וְאֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Alternatively, if he admits to owing two dinars he is exempt for a different reason: Because a promissory note creates a lien on the debtor's land, and there is a principle that one does not take an oath with regard to a debtor's denial of a debt that is secured with a lien on land. Oaths are administered only when one denies owing money or movable property.

The Gemara elaborates on the *shtar*'s role in this analysis. A *shtar* functions as testimony — it is equivalent to two witnesses for purposes of establishing the debt. When the *shtar* says *dinarim* without a numeral, it establishes the minimum (two) with the force of testimony. Before the parties have exchanged a word in court, the two-*dinar* obligation is already proven. The entire litigation concerns only the disputed amount above two. When the defendant confirms two, she confirms nothing that was not already proven; her "admission" adds no evidentiary weight. And for the *shibud karkaot* analysis, the *shtar*-backed amount creates a land lien of exactly that size, placing the admitted sum squarely within the exempted category.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמוֹתֵיב מִסֵּיפָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה וּפָטוּר. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר שָׁלֹשׁ, הָא שְׁתַּיִם חַיָּיב. וְהָא שְׁטָר, כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מוֹדֵי בֵּיהּ כְּ״הֵילָךְ״ דָּמֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״הֵילָךְ״ חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** There are those who raise an objection to Rav Sheshet's opinion from the latter clause of this *baraita*, which teaches that Rabbi Akiva says: He is merely the equivalent of one returning a lost item and is exempt from taking an oath. The Gemara infers: The reason he is exempt is that he said that he owes three dinars. But had he admitted to owing only two, he would have been obligated to take an oath. And concerning the minimal obligation recorded in this promissory note, to which he admits, which is two dinars, it is as though he said: Here you are. Learn from it that one who says: Here you are, is obligated to take an oath.

The Gemara notes that there are two versions of the *baraita*: one presented from the perspective of Rabban Shimon ben Elazar, and one from the perspective of Rabbi Akiva. The Gemara reconciles them by showing that both versions, properly understood, agree on one fundamental point: saying two is always *patur*. The debate between Rabban Shimon ben Elazar and Rabbi Akiva is specifically and only about the case of three. Rabban Shimon ben Elazar says three is *modeh be-miktzat* and requires an oath. Rabbi Akiva says three is *meshiv aveidah* and is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ שְׁתַּיִם נָמֵי פָּטוּר, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי שָׁלֹשׁ, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר מוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה הָוֵי – וְחַיָּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הָוֵי – וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: No, actually I will say to you that if he admits that he owes two dinars he is also exempt from taking an oath, and the reason that the *baraita* teaches the dispute specifically with regard to the case of three dinars is to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who says that he is considered one who admits to part of the claim and he is obligated to take an oath. Rabbi Akiva, therefore, teaches us that in his opinion, the defendant is the equivalent of one returning a lost item, and he is exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara confirms this reading by showing that the alternative interpretation — that Rabban Shimon ben Elazar would say two is also *chayav* — generates an internal contradiction in Rabbi Akiva's position. If two were *chayav*, then a knowing defendant would strategically say three instead to invoke the *meshiv aveidah* exemption. The court would in effect be rewarding deceptive behavior. Since we assume the Rabbis do not construct rules that can be easily gamed in this way, two must be *patur* under both views, and the entire debate is limited to the three case.

### Why Two Is Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ: שְׁתַּיִם חַיָּיב, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ הֵיכִי פָּטַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַאי אִעָרוֹמֵי קָא מַעֲרִים! סָבַר: אִי אָמֵינָא שְׁתַּיִם בָּעֵינָא אִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי, אֵימָא שָׁלֹשׁ דְּאֶהְוֵי כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה, וְאִיפָּטֵר. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שְׁתַּיִם נָמֵי פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: So too, it is reasonable to explain the *baraita* as just explained, as, if it enters your mind that one who admits that he owes two dinars is obligated to take an oath, how does Rabbi Akiva deem him exempt in a case where he admits that he owes three dinars? Perhaps this debtor is employing artifice, thinking: If I say that I owe two, I will be required to take an oath. Therefore, I will say that I owe three so that I will be considered equivalent to one returning a lost item and will be exempt from taking an oath. Rather, learn from it that even if he admits that he owes only two dinars, he is also exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara concludes with a convergence of three independent grounds for the *patur* of two: (1) *heilech* — admitting to two while the *shtar* already establishes two is like handing over money that was already allocated, leaving the remainder as a *kofer ha-kol*; (2) the *shtar* backs up the claim of two with full evidentiary force, so no *shvua* is needed to substantiate it; (3) the admitted two *dinarim* are entirely within the *shibud karkaot* — the land lien — and one does not take an oath on the denial or admission of land-lien obligations. Each of these reasons independently suffices to exempt the defendant who says two from any *shvua* obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּקָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא. אִי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁטָר שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת, וְאֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But this explanation poses a difficulty to the opinion of Rabbi Ḥiyya, that a defendant is obligated to take an oath in a case where he says: Here you are. In other words, Rabbi Ḥiyya's opinion is negated by the case of one who admits that he owes only two dinars, where he is exempt from taking an oath. The Gemara answers: The case there is different, as the note supports him. Therefore, he is not required to take an oath. Alternatively, he is exempt because a promissory note creates a lien on the debtor's land, and there is a principle that one does not take an oath with regard to a debtor's denial of a debt that is secured with a lien on land.

The daf concludes at this point, with the Gemara having established the convergent technical foundations for this exemption.
