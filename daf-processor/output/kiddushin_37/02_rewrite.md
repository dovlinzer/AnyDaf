# Kiddushin 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mitzvot Categori…

### Perek Theme

The *daf* before us is 37, and we pick up with a *mishna* appearing at the bottom of 36b. The broader theme of this *perek* is the construction of categories: first, the categorization of the various modes of acquisition (*kinyanim*), and now a categorization of *mitzvot* according to who and where they obligate. The *perek* calls to mind the *ain bein* passages in *Megilla*, which sweep across a wide scope of material. There may also be a linguistic thread — the *kol* construction, as in *kol mitzvas haben al ha'av* — but the dominant organizing principle seems to be simply the *perek*'s ambition to sort broad classes of things into coherent categories. It is, notably, the longest *perek* in *Shas*.

### Gender to Geography

Having covered the division of *mitzvot* between men and women, the *mishna* now transitions to a second axis of categorization: which *mitzvot* are geographically restricted to *Eretz Yisrael*, and which apply wherever one happens to be.

---

## Mitzvot Teluyot Ba'aretz

### Mishna Text

The *mishna* states: *kol mitzva she'hi teluya ba'aretz eina noheget ela ba'aretz* — any *mitzva* that is dependent on the land applies only in the land of Israel. *Ve'she'eina teluya ba'aretz noheget bein ba'aretz uvein bechutz la'aretz* — and what is not dependent on the land applies whether inside or outside of the land of Israel.

### Orlah & Kilayim

The *mishna* immediately carves out two exceptions: *chutz min ha'orlah vehakilayim* — *orlah* and *kilayim* apply even outside the land, despite being land-based *mitzvot*. *Orlah* refers to the prohibition on fruit during the first three years of a tree's planting; *kilayim* refers to the prohibition on crossbreeding seeds.

What is striking here is the relative legal weight of these two exceptions. *Orlah* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, while *kilayim* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is only *derabbanan*. Intuitively, one might have expected *kilayim* to carry the heavier extra-territorial force, since the prohibition on mixing species is not obviously tethered to the sanctity of the land — it parallels other forms of *kilayim* such as crossbreeding animals or mixing wool and linen. *Orlah*, by contrast, is closely linked in the *Torah* to *neta reva'i*, the fourth-year fruits brought to the *Beit Hamikdash*, and would seem on its face to belong to the category of *kedushat ha'aretz*. Yet the *halacha* inverts one's expectations: *orlah* outside the land carries the weight of *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, while *kilayim* is merely *derabbanan*.

The further implication of the *mishna's* phrasing is important. By explicitly listing *orlah* and *kilayim* — one of which is only *derabbanan* — as the exceptions that apply in *chutz la'aretz*, the *mishna* implies that everything else does not apply outside the land even at the *derabbanan* level. This is a significant point that will bear on later discussion.

### R. Eliezer on Chadash

*Rabbi Eliezer* adds: *af chadash* — even *chadash*, the prohibition on new grain before the *omer* is brought on the sixteenth of *Nissan*, applies outside the land of Israel. This position sets up the central dispute of the *sugya*.

---

## Teluyah Definition

### Peshat Proposal

The *gemara* opens by asking: *mai teluya u'mai she'eina teluya* — what does it mean for a *mitzva* to be "dependent" on the land, and what does it mean to be "not dependent"? The initial proposal is that *teluya* means that the relevant verse explicitly conditions the *mitzva* on entering the land — *kol mitzva sheketiv bah bi'ah* — while a *mitzva* that does not say "when you come into the land" is not so restricted.

### Tefillin Objection

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading: *va'harei tefillin u'peter chamor dekesiv bahen bi'ah* — both *tefillin* and *peter chamor* (the redemption of the firstborn donkey) are explicitly conditioned in the verse on entering the land, yet both apply equally inside and outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. The verse in *Shemos* 13 — *vehayah ki yeviacha Hashem el eretz hakenani* — introduces both the *mitzva* of the firstborn and, ultimately, *tefillin*, within the frame of the entry into the land. If the proposed criterion were correct, these *mitzvot* should not apply in *chutz la'aretz* — or at the very least, one might argue they apply only *derabbanan* outside the land. But the *mishna* forecloses even that escape: since *kilayim* is only *derabbanan* outside the land and yet is listed explicitly as an exception, the implication is that everything not listed does not apply even *derabbanan*. We know from the *gemara* itself that the Sages of *Bavel* wore *tefillin*, so *tefillin* plainly applies in *chutz la'aretz* at the full *d'oraita* level.

### Rashi's Answer

There is a famous *Rashi* on the *Torah* — picked up by the *Ramban* — which is relevant here. On the *pasuk* in the second *parasha* of *Shema* — *vesamtem es devarai eileh al levavchem ve'al nafshechem* — *Rashi* explains: *af acharei shetiglu heyu metzuyanim bemitzvot* — even after you go into exile, distinguish yourselves with *mitzvot*: put on *tefillin*, affix *mezuzot* — *kedei shelo yihyu lachem chadashim keshetachzoru* — so that these practices will not seem foreign to you when you return. *Rashi* then cites the verse *hatzivi lach tziyunim*.

The *Ramban* is struck by this: can one really say that *tefillin* and *mezuzot* are merely "practice" for the real performance back in the land? For the *Ramban*, this *Rashi* opens onto his broader theological position that even *mitzvot* that are *chovat haguf* — personal obligations — find their true and ideal fulfillment in *Eretz Yisrael*. The *Torah* does not envision Judaism as an abstract religion that happens to include, as an adjunct, a homeland; the *Torah* of *Devarim* imagines a nation living as a people in their land. *Galus* is called exile for a reason — it is not the natural condition. So even though it is true that *mitzvot ha-teluyot ba-guf* apply in *chutz la'aretz*, that is not the ideal mode of their performance.

Some have been so unsettled by *Rashi's* formulation — that *tefillin* and *mezuzot* are essentially preparatory exercises for return — that they have proposed emending the text of *Rashi* to read *terumos u'maasros* instead of *tefillin u'mezuzot*. But that emendation is not authentic; *Rashi* is drawing on the plain sense of the *pesukim*, which speak about going into exile and then say *vesamtem es devarai eileh...uktavtem al mezuzos beiseicha* — with *tefillin* and *mezuzah* explicitly in view, followed by *lema'an yirbu yemeichem al ha'adamah*.

What makes this *gemara* striking is that it presents as a mere *havah amina* what *Rashi* treats as the *peshat* of the *pesukim* — that *tefillin* might be restricted to *Eretz Yisrael*. The *gemara* ultimately rejects that reading, but the underlying intuition that all *mitzvot* find their home in *Eretz Yisrael* is very much present.

## Chovat Haguf & Karka

### Rav Yehuda's Principle

*Rav Yehuda* resolves the question by reframing it entirely. The distinction, he says, is not about how the verse is phrased, but about the nature of the *mitzva* itself. *Kol mitzvah she'hi chovat haguf noheget bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — any *mitzva* that is a personal obligation applies everywhere. *Chovat karka* — a land-related obligation — applies only in *Eretz Yisrael*, with the exceptions of *orlah* and *kilayim*. One need not search the *pesukim* for contextual cues; one simply looks at the reality of what the *mitzva* demands. Does it engage you as a person? Then it applies everywhere. Does it engage the earth and its produce? Then it is restricted to the land of Israel.

### Midrash Source

The *gemara* asks *minah hani milei* — what is the source? The *tanna'im* teach: *eileh hachukim vehamishpatim asher tishmerun la'asot ba'aretz* — these are the laws you shall observe to perform in the land. The *midrash* unpacks this: *eileh hamidrashot* — these are the *midrash*-derived teachings; *vehamishpatim eileh hadinim* — these are the civil laws; *asher tishmerun* — that you shall guard — *zo mishnah*, that refers to the learning and transmission of the laws; *va'asot* — to perform — *zu ma'aseh*, that is the actual practice. Then *ba'aretz* — in the land.

One might think from this that all *mitzvot* apply only in the land — *yakhol kol hamitzvot kulan lo yehu nohagot ela ba'aretz*. The *gemara* therefore brings the continuation of the verse: *kol hayamim asher atem chayim al ha'adamah* — all the days that you live on the land. There is a certain irony in the interpretive move here: the phrase *al ha'adamah* ties the text to the land, yet *Chazal* read *kol hayamim* — all times — to indicate that the obligation is universal. After the text has spoken broadly and narrowly, one must determine what falls into each category.

### Avodah Zarah Paradigm

The operative teaching that follows is: *tzei u'lemad* — go and look at the opening subject of that passage, which is *abed tave'dun et kol hamekomot asher avdu sham hagoyim et eloheihem* — you shall destroy all the places where the nations worshipped their idols. *Mah avodat kokhavim meyuchedet she'hi chovat haguf* — just as destroying *avodah zarah* is a personal obligation that *noheget bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — applies in all places — so too everything that is a *chovat haguf* applies everywhere.

The choice of *avodah zarah* as the paradigm is itself ironic on multiple levels. First, the very phrase *kol hayamim asher atem chayim al ha'adamah* that anchors the teaching in the land is immediately followed by a *mitzva* that is at once personal and, in its fullest expression, fundamentally territorial. The obligation to destroy *avodah zarah* in *Eretz Yisrael* is heightened — *chayav attah lishrosh achareiha*, one must uproot it completely, pursuing it even beneath the surface. The *pesukim* speak clearly about clearing the land so that it becomes one's own national home. The intuition that if one is in India and encounters an idol one is obligated to smash it does not emerge naturally from reading those *pesukim* in context.

Second, read *Masechet Avodah Zarah* in its entirety, and one would never arrive at the conclusion that there is a positive *mitzva* to destroy *avodah zarah* — because the historical realities of Jewish life under Roman rule required the wholesale practical suspension of that obligation. Scholars and academics have commented extensively on this. And yet the *gemara* here uses *avodah zarah* destruction as the quintessential model of a *chovat haguf* that applies everywhere. The double irony — of a text anchored in *al ha'adamah* teaching universal personal obligation, instantiated through a *mitzva* whose real scope is territorial and whose practical observance was historically bracketed — is difficult to miss.

## Chadash Dispute

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *tanna kamma* holds that *chadash* applies only in *Eretz Yisrael*. To understand this position, one must look at the relevant *pasuk*: *lechem vekali vekarmel lo tochlu ad etzem hayom hazeh...chukat olam ledoroteichem bechol moshvoteichem* — you shall not eat bread or parched grain or fresh grain until that very day...it is an eternal statute for your generations in all your dwellings. The phrase *bechol moshvoteichem* — in all your dwellings — might seem to extend the prohibition everywhere. The *tanna kamma* reads it otherwise: *moshav le'achar yerusha veyeshiva mashma* — *moshav* means after you have already inherited and settled the land. Thus *bechol moshvoteichem* means once you are settled in the land, not wherever you happen to dwell. On this reading, the prohibition was not even operative during the first fourteen years after entry — the years of conquest and division — because the land was not yet *moshav*, not yet truly settled.

What makes this practically significant is the agricultural reality of *chutz la'aretz*. In *Eretz Yisrael* there is essentially one wheat season, and the harvest comes naturally around the time the *omer* is brought; one would have to make a special effort to create a problem of *chadash*. But in the diaspora — in France, Germany, and elsewhere — grain was planted in spring, took root after the *omer*, and came to market around *Rosh Hashana*. For those who hold that *chadash* applies in *chutz la'aretz*, that grain would be forbidden for the following six months, until the next *omer*. This creates an acute practical challenge that the *rishonim* had to confront head-on.

### R. Eliezer Position

*Rabbi Eliezer* reads *bechol moshvoteichem* in its plain sense: *bechol makom she'atem yoshvim* — wherever you dwell. On this reading, *chadash* applies outside *Eretz Yisrael* just as it does within it. The *gemara* establishes that *Rabbi Eliezer* is being *machmir* — his position is the stricter one — and that the *tanna kamma* is the more lenient position, restricting *chadash* to the land.

## Chadash Dispute (II)

### Nesachim & Central Altar

*Abaye* identifies the *tanna kamma* as *Rabbi Yishmael*, and frames the entire dispute around a broader hermeneutical principle. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that whenever a verse combines both the language of *bi'ah* — entering the land — and *moshav* — dwelling — the combination restricts the obligation to *le'achar yerusha veyeshiva*, after the land has been inherited and settled. He derives this principle from the laws of *nesachim* — the wine, flour, and oil libations that accompany sacrifices. The relevant *pasuk* in *Shelach Lecha* reads *ki tavo'u el eretz moshvoteichem va'asitem isha l'Hashem* — when you come to the land of your dwellings and bring a fire-offering to God. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that individuals did not bring *nesachim* on a *bamat yachid* in the *midbar*; only the communal *bamah gedolah* had *nesachim*. Since the verse says *lachem* — in the plural, indicating communal offering at the *bamah gedolah* — and that was already operative in the *midbar*, the *chiddush* of the *pasuk* must be that individual *nesachim* will now be brought — but only once the land is settled. Given this, *moshvoteichem* cannot mean "wherever you dwell" in the sense of individual locations throughout the land; it must mean "after your settlement is complete." And this principle, says *Rabbi Yishmael*, generalizes: wherever the *Torah* combines *bi'ah* and *moshav*, it means only after the first fourteen years of conquest and settlement.

---

## R. Yishmael vs. Akiva

### R. Yishmael's Rule

*Rabbi Yishmael's* general hermeneutical principle is thus: *kol makom shene'emar bo moshav eino ela le'achar yerusha veyeshiva* — wherever the *Torah* uses the language of dwelling in combination with entering the land, it means only after the land has been inherited and settled. When the *pasuk* on *chadash* says both *ki tavo'u* — when you come — and *bechol moshvoteichem*, the combination limits the prohibition to *Eretz Yisrael* after settlement, not to wherever Jews may dwell.

### Shabbat Objection

*Rabbi Akiva* objects: *harei Shabbat shene'emar bah moshvot* — but *Shabbat* also uses the language of *moshvot*, and *Shabbat* applies everywhere, both inside and outside *Eretz Yisrael*. The *pasuk* reads *Shabbat hi l'Hashem bechol moshvoteichem* — it is a *Shabbat* to God in all your dwellings. Clearly, *moshvoteichem* there means wherever you dwell, not only in the land after settlement.

### Response on Shabbat

*Rabbi Yishmael* responds on two levels. First: *ana bi'ah u'moshav ka'amina* — my principle applies only when the verse contains both the language of entering and the language of dwelling. *Shabbat* mentions only *moshvot*, without *bi'ah*, and therefore does not fall under my rule. Second — and in any case — *Shabbat kal vachomer hi*: even if one were to argue from the word *moshvot* alone, we know *Shabbat* applies in *chutz la'aretz* from a *kal vachomer*: if *mitzvot* that are lighter apply outside the land, how much more so the weightier *mitzva* of *Shabbat*.

The *gemara* notes that *Rabbi Yishmael's* response is actually a compound reply — *chada ve'od ka'amar lei* — he is saying one thing and additionally another: first, that *Shabbat* is categorically irrelevant to his rule because it lacks the *bi'ah* language; second, that even if it were relevant, the *kal vachomer* would resolve it independently.

## Chadash Halakha Today

### Mishna Conflict

The question of how to rule practically on *chadash* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is complicated by a conflict between the *mishna* in our *masechta* and a *mishna* elsewhere. The *mishna* in *Kiddushin* presents the restriction of *chadash* to the land as a dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *tanna kamma*, suggesting we might simply rule like the *tanna kamma* — and therefore like *Rabbi Yishmael* — that *chadash* does not apply in *chutz la'aretz* even *derabbanan*. That would be the most straightforward and lenient resolution.

The problem is the last *mishna* in *Orlah*, which states unambiguously: *ha-chadash asur min ha-Torah bechol makom* — new grain is forbidden by *Torah* law everywhere. No dispute, no qualification. A number of *rishonim* attempt to rule like the *tanna kamma* in *Kiddushin* on the grounds that the position of *Rabbi Eliezer* there is a *da'as yachid*. Others argue that the *stam mishna* in *Orlah* — situated in the primary discussion of these issues — must be dispositive and cannot be overridden by a *da'as yachid* in *Kiddushin*, especially given the principle of *stam ve'achar kach machlokes*.

### Tosafot on Chadash

*Tosafot*, at the bottom of 36b, does not resolve the dispute between the two *mishnayot* but states plainly: *venira de-chadash noheig bizman hazeh* — it appears that the prohibition of *chadash* applies in our times, meaning in the diaspora. He does not elaborate on whether this follows *Rabbi Eliezer* or is derived from the *mishna* in *Orlah*. Instead, he proceeds directly to practical guidance.

### Rov & Safek Sefeika

*Tosafot* writes: *ul'kach yesh lehizaher she'im adam yode'a bevadai she-se'orim nizre'u achar zman hakravas ha-omer* — one should be careful that if one knows with certainty that grain was planted after the *omer* was brought, i.e., after the sixteenth of *Nissan*, one should not eat from it. However, *u'missafek ein le'asur kol ha-se'orim* — in cases of doubt, one should not prohibit all grain, *keivan d'rov se'orim nizre'u kodem tet-zayin b'Nisan* — since the majority of grain was planted before the sixteenth of *Nissan*, and one may therefore follow the *rov*.

*Tosafot* mentions *se'orim* — barley — specifically, likely because the most pressing practical challenge involved beer, which was pervasive in the diaspora communities and from which people could not easily abstain. The *issur* of *chadash* does not apply to benefit (*hana'ah*), but one may not eat it.

There is a certain irony in the structure of this *hetter*. *Chadash* is an *issur d'oraita*, and normally a *safek* in a *d'oraita* prohibition would not suffice to permit; here it is an assertion of *rov* that carries the weight. One must ask whether that assertion is truly accurate and whether the *rov* is *mevurrar* — established — or relies on an approximation. In practice, it seems that many *rishonim* began with the reality that communities could not avoid eating the grain, and worked backward to find justification.

Beyond *rov*, some *rishonim* developed a *safek sefeika*: perhaps the grain came from last year's supply, and even if from this year, perhaps it took root before the *omer*. Though one might argue that these two doubts collapse into a single question — was it *yashan* or *chadash* — the *rishonim* maintained this framework as a further basis for leniency in combination with other factors.

Additional arguments proposed by various *rishonim* include: ruling entirely like the *tanna kamma* that *chadash* does not apply in *chutz la'aretz*; arguing that *chadash* grown by non-Jews is exempt; and the position of the *Aruch Hashulchan* that grain of a second wheat season — which could never be fit for the *omer* — should never have been subject to the *chadash* prohibition in the first place, limiting the concern to the primary wheat season only. Against the exemption for non-Jewish-owned grain, the *Yerushalmi* itself closes the door: it explains why *challah* is not listed alongside *chadash* in the *mishna* — because *challah* does not apply to grain owned by non-Jews — implying that *chadash* as listed does apply to grain owned by non-Jews.

### Modern Context

The practical landscape has changed significantly in the modern era. For centuries, communities maintained the permissive practice because there was simply no way to know with certainty the origin of grain — whether it had taken root before or after the *omer*. The arguments from *rov* and *safek sefeika* were, at least in part, post-hoc rationalizations of a practice driven by practical necessity.

Today, however, detailed information is available about grain lots and their planting schedules. It is possible to determine, based on barcodes or lot information, precisely when a given batch of grain took root and whether it qualifies as *yashan* or *chadash*. The availability of this information raises serious *halachic* questions about whether one may continue to rely on *rov* or *safek sefeika* when the facts are in principle ascertainable. The most straightforward *halachic* resolution — and the one that avoids all of these complexities — would be to rule definitively like the *tanna kamma* against *Rabbi Eliezer*, holding that *chadash* does not apply in *chutz la'aretz* even *derabbanan*. Whether the *stam mishna* in *Orlah* forecloses that option remains the underlying difficulty.

## Orlah & Wine

### Orlah Problem

*Tosafot* raises an equally pressing question about *orlah* in the diaspora. Since *orlah* applies in *chutz la'aretz* as a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, and since virtually all vineyards propagate new vines by attaching shoots to existing root systems — a process that, if it constitutes a new planting, would make the new growth subject to the three-year *orlah* prohibition — how has it been historically permissible to drink wine from diaspora vineyards? *Tosafot* notes that one cannot rely on *rov* here as one might in other cases, because the majority of vines are indeed propagated this way.

### Mavrich Solution

*Tosafot* cites *Rabbeinu Shimshon* — the *Rash mi-Shantz* — who investigated the matter by actually speaking with farmers and viticulturists. What he found was that the process being used was not *markiv* — true grafting, where a shoot is severed and joined to a different root — but rather *mavrich* — layering, where a shoot is bent underground and trained to emerge elsewhere, while still remaining physically attached and connected to the original vine. Crucially, *she'einam chotchim ha-zmorah min ha-sadan* — they never actually sever the shoot from the trunk. It remains drawing nourishment from the old vine throughout.

The legal implication is decisive: *im kein hu yonek od min ha-zkeinah* — since it is still drawing from the old vine, it retains the legal status of the old plant. A shoot that is not severed is not a new planting; *orlah* does not commence.

### Yerushalmi Source

The *Yerushalmi* provides the source for this distinction: *ha-mavrich bizman she-yonek min ha-zkeinah patur min ha-orlah* — when the layered shoot still draws from the old vine, it is exempt from *orlah*. Once it is severed and no longer drawing, it becomes subject to the full *orlah* counting.

### Signs of Attachment

The *Yerushalmi* also provides a physical sign by which one may determine whether the layered vine remains attached. *Keshe-ha-zmorah yoneket min ha-zkeinah harei hem notim letzad acher* — when the shoot is still drawing from the old vine, the leaves turn away from it; *u-cheshe-posek harei hem notim letzad ha-zkeinah* — once it is severed and stops drawing, the leaves turn back toward the old vine. The mnemonic offered is charming: *de-hai man de-achil me-chavrei ba'ei le-istakulei bei* — one who eats from another person's table is a little embarrassed to look directly at them. So too, the shoot that is still feeding off the old vine averts its leaves. Whether this botanical observation fully maps onto modern viticulture is another question, but that is the tradition as *Tosafot* records it.

## Nesachim Debate

### R. Yishmael View

The *gemara* now clarifies the foundational disagreement between *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva* that drives the *chadash* dispute. *Rabbi Yishmael savar lo keruvu nesachim ba-midbar* — *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that individuals did not bring *nesachim* on a *bamat yachid* in the *midbar*. Only the communal *bamah gedolah* received *nesachim* during the wilderness period.

### Yishmael's Consequence

Given this starting point, the *chiddush* of the *pasuk* in *Shelach Lecha* — *ki tavo'u el eretz moshvoteichem* — is that upon entering the land, individuals will for the first time bring *nesachim* on their individual *bamot*. Since the *pasuk* is introducing this novel permission or obligation, *moshvoteichem* cannot mean "wherever you are currently located," because individuals were not bringing *nesachim* anywhere; the *pasuk* must mean after you have settled and conquered the land — *le'achar yerusha veyishiva*. The verse does not broaden the geographic scope of *nesachim*; it defers their onset until after settlement is complete.

### R. Akiva View

*Rabbi Akiva savar keruvu nesachim ba-midbar* — *Rabbi Akiva* holds the opposite: individuals already brought *nesachim* on a *bamat yachid* in the *midbar*. The communal obligation was already operative in the wilderness.

### Akiva's Consequence

If *nesachim* were already being brought individually in the *midbar*, the verse *ki tavo'u el eretz moshvoteichem* cannot be introducing something new — individuals were already bringing *nesachim*. The *chiddush* must therefore be something else: you will continue to bring *nesachim* even on an individual *bamat yachid* after you enter the land. And since it is telling you that individual *bamot* remain valid in the land, *moshvoteichem* must mean *bechol makom she'atem yoshvim* — wherever you dwell. For *Rabbi Akiva*, the word *moshvoteichem* naturally carries the broader geographic meaning, and it is from this foundation that he argues against *Rabbi Yishmael's* restrictive reading — including, by extension, on the question of *chadash*.
