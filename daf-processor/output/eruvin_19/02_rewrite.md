# Eruvin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psim: Extension or Planks

### Meir vs. Yehuda Gap

The *daf* under discussion is 19b, and the *gemara* is engaged with the topic of *pasi biraot* — the corner-posts (*psim*) erected around a well to create a permissible domain for drawing water on *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* establishes that according to Rabbi Meir, the gap between corners may be as large as ten *amot*, while according to Rabbi Yehuda, the permissible gap extends to thirteen and a third *amot*.

### Extend vs. Add Planks

Against this backdrop, *Abaye* posed a question to *Rava*: if instead of adding straight planks (*peshutim*) to fill a wider gap, one were to extend the corners themselves (*dyomdim*) to the equivalent measurement — what is the law according to Rabbi Meir?

*Rava* replied that the answer may be derived from the *mishnah* itself, which states *u-vilvad she-yarbeh ba-pasim* — "as long as one increases the *pasim*." The *gemara* initially reads this as referring to increasing the length of the corners, since the phrase *yarbeh ba-pasim* speaks of increasing *within* the *pasim*, suggesting extension of the existing corner-posts rather than adding separate straight sections. This reading would indicate that extending the corners is not only permitted but may in fact be what the *mishnah* requires.

However, the *gemara* then objects: if extending the corners is what is meant, the *mishnah* should have said *yarbeh pasim* — "increase *pasim*" — rather than *yarbeh ba-pasim*, which implies increasing something about the *pasim* themselves, i.e., their length. The *gemara* therefore emends the text: read it as *ad she-yarbeh pasim* — until one has more planks.

A second version of the exchange (*ika d'amri*) presents the proof in the opposite direction, where *Rava* attempts to demonstrate from *yarbeh ba-pasim* that extension of the corners is what is contemplated, since *yarbeh ba-pasim* most naturally means lengthening the *pasim* themselves. The *gemara* accepts this reading — *hachi nami mistabra* — because extending the corners does in fact make intuitive sense: the more the corners are extended, the greater the enclosed area, until only a single gap remains on each side.

### Yehuda: Extension

*Abaye* then posed the parallel question regarding Rabbi Yehuda: given that Rabbi Yehuda permits a gap of thirteen and a third *amot*, must one add straight planks (*peshutim*) to fill a larger gap, or may one instead extend the corners (*dyomdim*)?

*Rava* directed attention to the *mishnah*'s discussion of how far the walls of the enclosure may be from the well. The *mishnah* states that the minimum distance is enough for a cow to insert her head and most of her body (*rosho ve-rubo shel parah*), while regarding the maximum distance, the anonymous opinion holds that even an enclosure as large as a *kor* or two *korim* is permissible. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, limits the area to *beit sa'atayim* — 5,000 square *amot* — and prohibits anything larger.

The *chachamim* then challenge Rabbi Yehuda: does he not agree that other enclosed areas — a livestock pen, a storage yard, a courtyard — may be *reshut ha-yachid* even if they are five or ten *korim* in area? Rabbi Yehuda responds: *zo mechitza ve-eilu pasim* — those areas have proper walls, whereas these are merely corners.

### Wall vs. Corner Law

The *gemara* seizes on this formulation. If the only permissible method for Rabbi Yehuda were extending the corners, then when he distinguishes between a *mechitza* and *pasim*, he should have said *zo mechitza ve-zo mechitza* — calling both cases wall-configurations — since extended corners that surround a large area would almost entirely enclose it with wall-like coverage, leaving only minimal gaps on each side. Why then does he specifically call one a *mechitza* and the other *pasim*?

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that Rabbi Yehuda's formulation carries a specific halakhic meaning: *zo torat mechitza aleha u-firtzoteha be-eser* — a true *mechitza* is one whose gaps may not exceed ten *amot*, whereas *torat pasim* — the law of corner-posts — permits gaps up to thirteen and a third. The distinction is therefore not about the physical configuration of extension versus straight planks, but about the halakhic category governing the gaps. It does emerge, however, as a possibility — admittedly a difficult read — that for Rabbi Yehuda, extending the corners may be required rather than adding flat straightaways.

## Solid Objects/Diomed

### Hill as Diomed

*Abaye* posed a further question to *Rabba*: a hillside that rises ten *tefachim* within four *amot* of horizontal distance may count as a wall for purposes of *eruv* law. If such a hill stands at the corner of a well's enclosure, may it serve as a *diyumed* — a corner-post? The *diyumed* functions not merely as a wall but as a corner, and the question is whether one may theoretically imagine the hill "carved out" into a V-shape, leaving an *amah* of material extending in each direction, and thus treat it as a functioning corner-post.

### Square/Circular Stones

*Rabba* answered by citing a *tosefta*: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar rules that if there is a large square stone at the corner, one may view it as though it were carved out — *ro'in ota k'ilu techalek* — provided that after the imaginary carving there would remain an *amah* on each side. If so, it counts as a *diyumed*; if not, it does not.

Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, rules similarly regarding a circular stone: *ro'in ota k'ilu techakek ve-techalek* — one imagines both that it is rounded and then that it is divided — and if the result would leave an *amah* in each direction, it counts as a *diyumed*. This is a striking formulation. Why does a circular stone require two acts of theoretical carving while a square stone requires only one? The answer seems to be that a *diyumed* requires a sharp right-angle corner at the turning point. For a square stone, the corner already exists; one merely imagines the interior carved away. For a circular stone, the curve must first be squared off and then the interior carved out — two separate conceptual operations.

The *gemara* clarifies the nature of the dispute: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that one may envision a single theoretical transformation (*chad ro'in*) but not two (*trei ro'in*), while Rabbi Yishmael permits even two. Both, however, agree that a solid object may count as a *diyumed* if the theoretical carving would yield the requisite dimensions. The question raised by *Abaye* is thus resolved in the affirmative.

### Stick Fences/Lavud

*Abaye* then asked *Rabba* a further question: may a fence made of reeds or sticks, where each stick is within three *tefachim* of the next (thereby qualifying under the principle of *lavud*, which treats gaps of less than three *tefachim* as if closed), count as a *diyumed*? In other words, may one combine the leniency of *lavud* with the concept of *diyumed*?

*Rabba* replied by citing a *braita*: "If at the corner there was a tree, a wall, or a fence of reeds — it counts as a *diyumed*." The *gemara* queries whether the "fence of reeds" in the *braita* refers to reeds joined by *lavud* (sticks within three *tefachim* of one another) or to a fence woven tightly at the base (*gudrita dekani*), where the reeds are densely packed at the bottom and spread apart at the top — structurally analogous to a tree. *Rashi* explains that the *chiddush* of the woven fence, like that of the solid stone, is not that it constitutes a wall in the ordinary sense, but that one may envision it as if carved out in two directions.

The *gemara* concludes that in either reading, the three items listed — tree, wall, and reed fence — represent two types each, and both types qualify. The principle that emerges is that even complex multi-element configurations, including *lavud*, may combine with *diyumed* to constitute a valid corner-post. As a matter of halakhic instinct, there seems little reason to restrict *lavud*, which functions generally throughout the laws of *mechitza*, from combining with *diyumed* in this context.

## Courtyards & Eruv

### Single Courtyard

*Abaye* now asked *Rabba* about a courtyard that opens into the space between the *psim* (*bein ha-passim*). This space, bounded by the four corner-posts, constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid*, but it is not an inhabited area — it is merely the functional space around the well. The question is whether the residents of the courtyard may carry between the courtyard and the space between the *psim* and vice versa.

*Rabba* answered: it is permitted. Since nobody actually dwells in the space of *bein ha-passim*, there is no need to establish an *eruv* combining that space with the courtyard. The space serves a purely functional purpose, and the courtyard may use it freely.

### Two Courtyards Forbidden

*Abaye* then asked: what if there are two parallel courtyards, each opening into the space between the *psim*? *Rabba* replied: that is forbidden. Rav Huna elaborates and rules that even if the two courtyards made an *eruv* with one another, it remains forbidden — and this applies even where there is an opening (*pirtzah*) in the wall separating the two courtyards. His reasoning is as follows: those present in the space between the *psim* will not notice the break in the wall further back between the courtyards. Observing that people carry from one courtyard to the other via the shared space, they will assume that the *eruv* was made in the space of *bein ha-passim* itself — which is not a place where people reside and therefore not a valid location for the placement of an *eruv*. This appearance of impropriety (*maris ayin*) applies even when the two courtyards have technically combined through a proper opening.

### Rava Permits Eruv

*Rava* takes a more lenient position: if the courtyards did not make an *eruv* — meaning their wall remains intact — it is permissible. *Abaye* supports this view by citing a *braita*.

### Braita Eruv Rules

The *braita* states: if a single courtyard opens into the space between the *psim*, one may carry from the courtyard into that space and from that space into the courtyard. If two courtyards open into it, this is forbidden — but that prohibition applies only when they did not make an *eruv* together. If they did make an *eruv*, it is permitted.

This appears to directly contradict Rav Huna's position, which prohibits even when an *eruv* has been made. Rav Huna responds by reinterpreting the word *irvu* in the *braita*: it does not mean that they made an *eruv* in the usual sense, but rather that they merged physically — that there is a break in the back wall of the courtyard (*pirtzah*) visible from the outside, so that the entire configuration is perceived as one unified space. In that case, no observer would mistake the *eruv* as having been established in the space of the *psim*. It must be noted that this is a strained reading of the *braita*, and it also creates a certain internal tension: the concern throughout presupposes that observers in the space of *bein ha-passim* cannot see the break in the dividing wall deeper within the courtyards, yet at the same time we credit those same observers with noticing a break at the far back end of the courtyard.

## Water Drying/Returning

### Water Dries

*Abaye* posed another question to *Rava*: what happens if the water in the well dried up on *Shabbat*? The entire rabbinic dispensation of *pasi biraot* was enacted for the purpose of providing access to water. If the water disappears, does the *mechitza* status of the enclosure remain in force for the rest of *Shabbat*?

*Rava* answered unequivocally: *mayim ein kan, mechitza ein kan* — once there is no water, there is no *mechitza*. The fundamental justification for this leniency was the presence of water; when that condition ceases, the permission lapses.

This ruling stands in some tension with a general principle associated with *Abaye* himself — *Shabbat ho'il ve-hutrah hutrah* — that once a domain was legitimately constituted at the outset of *Shabbat*, incidental problems arising during *Shabbat* do not retroactively revoke that status. *Tosafot* addresses this tension and explains that *Abaye* concedes here precisely because the walls of *pasi biraot* are inherently deficient — they have large gaps and are mostly open, and the water was not a peripheral matter but the very basis of the entire dispensation. Where the enabling condition is so central to the construction of the leniency, its removal necessarily dissolves the leniency, even mid-*Shabbat*.

### Water Returns

*Rava* then raised a more complex scenario: the water dried up on *Shabbat* and then returned on *Shabbat*. What is the status of the enclosure once the water returns?

*Abaye* analyzed this in two steps. First, he stated that having previously established that drying up on *Shabbat* terminates the status, that much is clear. Second, he noted that the restoration of water on *Shabbat* should also be straightforward, because this would constitute a *mechitza* that came into being on *Shabbat* — and there is a *braita* establishing that any *mechitza*, however it came into being on *Shabbat* — whether through intentional action, inadvertent action, or circumstances entirely beyond one's control — is nevertheless a valid *mechitza* (*shmah mechitza*).

The *gemara* then invokes Rav Nachman's qualification of that *braita*: the ruling that a *mechitza* made on *Shabbat* is valid applies only with respect to liability for *hotza'ah* — throwing an object into such a space renders one *chayav* biblically. However, it does not permit *rabbinic* carrying within that space, since one should not benefit from something brought about through a violation of *Shabbat* (*ma'aseh Shabbat*). The *gemara* clarifies that Rav Nachman's restriction was stated specifically in the context of a *mechitza* made through deliberate violation (*meizid*). Where no violation occurred — as in the case of water naturally returning to a well — there is no concern of benefiting from forbidden labor, and the *mechitza* is fully valid for all purposes. Indeed, even if *Shabbat* had begun with the well completely dry and the water appeared only during *Shabbat*, the analysis would be the same: the enclosure is valid and carrying is permitted.

## Biblical Status/Rabbim

### Rav Nachman Distinction

With the discussion of *Abaye*'s questions concluded, the *gemara* introduces a statement: Rabbi Elazar rules that one who throws an object into the space of *pasi biraot* is *chayav* — liable for the biblical prohibition of carrying from *reshut ha-rabbim* to *reshut ha-yachid*. This implicitly confirms that the enclosure constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid* at the biblical level.

### Rabbi Eliezer Throws

The *gemara* objects: this seems obvious! If *pasi biraot* did not constitute a biblical *reshut ha-yachid*, how could the rabbis have permitted drawing water there in the first place? The fundamental structure of the laws of *eruv* requires that one first establish a biblical *reshut ha-yachid*; only then may one ask whether rabbinic carrying is also permitted within it. If the enclosure is not biblical, the entire rabbinic dispensation collapses.

The *gemara* therefore reframes the statement: Rabbi Elazar is not teaching about an enclosure around a well but about a person who constructed this same corner-post configuration (*k'ein pasi biraot*) in the middle of a *reshut ha-rabbim* — in an open public thoroughfare — and then threw an object into it. In that case, one might have thought the *reshut ha-rabbim* negates the effectiveness of the *mechitza*, rendering the configuration invalid. Rabbi Elazar teaches that this is not so: even in the middle of a street, this configuration is a *reshut ha-yachid*.

The *gemara* again objects: is this not also obvious? If this configuration were not effective in general as a *mechitza*, it could never work around a well either. The *gemara* therefore arrives at the true *chiddush*.

### Rabbim Non-Negation

The novelty is this: *af al gav d'ka bakei bah rabbim* — even though the general public (*rabbim*) passes through the space. If one constructed the corner-post configuration in Times Square, for instance, with major thoroughfares passing through two of the gaps and another road through the other two, one might think that the presence of a *reshut ha-rabbim* flowing through the gaps negates the wall entirely — that there is no *mechitza* to speak of when the public passes through unimpeded. Rabbi Elazar teaches that *rabbim* do not negate the *mechitza*: the enclosure remains a *reshut ha-yachid* even under these conditions.

This principle connects directly to a dispute that appears in a *mishnah* coming later in *Eruvin*. Rabbi Yehuda rules that if a *reshut ha-rabbim* — a major thoroughfare — passes through the gaps of a *pasi biraot* enclosure, the street must be redirected to the side (*yesakenu le-tzadin*); otherwise the *mechitza* is negated. The *chachamim* disagree and hold that no such redirection is required. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar are cited as stating jointly: *kan hodiucha kochan shel mechitzot* — "here they have shown you the power of walls." That is, the *chachamim*'s position demonstrates how robust a *mechitza* is: even with a *reshut ha-rabbim* passing directly through, the walls are not nullified.

The *gemara* notes that if Rabbi Elazar's ruling were only implicit in his commentary on the *chachamim*'s position in that *mishnah*, one might think he was merely explaining the *chachamim*'s view without personally endorsing it. The present statement establishes that Rabbi Elazar personally rules in accordance with the *chachamim* — that *rabbim* do not negate the *mechitza*.

An important qualification emerges from the *rishonim*: Rabbi Yochanan himself does *not* rule this way. When we reach that *mishnah*, Rabbi Yochanan will maintain that a *reshut ha-rabbim* does negate the *mechitza*. The joint formulation here (*Rabbi Yochanan ve-Rabbi Elazar damrei tavayhu*) refers only to their shared commentary that the *chachamim*'s position reflects great confidence in the power of walls; it does not mean both personally endorse that position as *halakha*. Rabbi Elazar's independent statement makes his personal ruling clear; Rabbi Yochanan's independent ruling is otherwise established.

## Head & Body Requirement

### Head & Body Rationale

The *gemara* now examines the requirement, stated in the *mishnah*, that the cow's head and the majority of its body (*rosho ve-rubo*) must be inside the enclosure for the watering to be permissible. To understand this requirement, the *gemara* draws a parallel from a *mishnah* in *Eruvin* (which appears later, on 99b): a person may not stand in *reshut ha-rabbim* and drink from a window opening into a house, nor may he stand inside and drink outside, unless his head and the majority of his body are in the same domain as the liquid he is drinking. The concern is that if only part of one's body is in the appropriate domain, one may inadvertently straighten up and carry the cup back across the *mechitza*. If, however, the head and the majority of the body are positioned where the drinking occurs, the risk of such inadvertent carrying is sufficiently mitigated.

The same logic applies to the cow at the well: the concern is that if only a small part of the cow is inside the enclosure, the cow may step outside and the person holding the vessel will follow, inadvertently carrying from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-rabbim*. When the majority of the animal is inside, this risk is deemed sufficiently controlled.

### Question: More Required?

The *gemara* then raises a more refined question: does this requirement of *rosho ve-rubo* apply across all circumstances, or might it vary depending on the degree of control the person exercising?

The *gemara* distinguishes two cases. Where the person is holding the vessel (*nakit mana*) but not holding the cow (*lo nakit lah*), there is no question that *rosho ve-rubo* of the cow is required inside the enclosure: the cow is free to move and the holder of the vessel is subject to the risk of following. But what if one is holding both the vessel and the cow (*nakit mana ve-nakit lah*)? In that case, the person has full physical control over the animal's movements. Perhaps the requirement of *rosho ve-rubo* applies only when the cow is free to move; when the person is in control, even less than *rosho ve-rubo* may suffice.

*Rabba* replied that the *mishnah*'s language resolves this: *u-vilvad she-tehei parah roshah ve-rubah mi-bifnim ve-shotah* — "provided that the cow's head and majority of its body are inside and drinking." The *gemara* then attempts to determine which scenario the *mishnah* is addressing.

### Trough Case Analysis

The *gemara* raises a *braita*: a person may not fill a vessel with water on *Shabbat* and hold it before his animal (*lo yemalei adam mayim ve-yiten be-Shabbat lifnei behemto*). He may, however, fill water and pour it into a trough (*avus*), and the animal will drink on its own. If the prohibition is categorical — one may never hold the vessel without also restraining the cow — then the *mishnah*'s case of *rosho ve-rubo* must be a scenario where the person *is* holding the cow (and the vessel), since holding the vessel alone is categorically forbidden.

However, *Abaye* qualifies the *braita* significantly: the prohibition against holding the vessel before the animal is not a categorical rule, but applies specifically to a case where the *avus* — the trough — is positioned with one end inside the *pasi biraot* enclosure and extends outward into the *reshut ha-rabbim*, yet the trough itself is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide, and thus constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid* in its own right. In such a case, what one might wish to do is carry the vessel over the airspace of the trough — which is *reshut ha-yachid* — directly to where the animal stands outside the enclosure. This the *braita* forbids.

Why? Not because the act itself would be biblically prohibited — but because of a rabbinic concern: the person may notice a crack or defect in the trough (*avus d'mikalkel*), descend into the *reshut ha-rabbim* to fix it, and bring the vessel down with him. After fixing it, he will then carry the vessel back up from the *reshut ha-rabbim* into the airspace of the trough, which is *reshut ha-yachid* — and that constitutes a biblical act of *hotza'ah*.

The *gemara* challenges even this: when the person originally descended, that was not a prohibited act since he did not *intend* to bring the vessel to *reshut ha-rabbim*; it was merely incidental movement. The principle articulated in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is that one who moves objects from corner to corner within a domain and then changes his mind and takes them outside is *patur* — exempt — because the initial *akira* (lifting) was not performed with the intent to remove them to another domain. So here too, the original descent was not an *akira* for *reshut ha-rabbim* purposes. The *gemara* responds: the concern is not for the descent but for what follows — once he is in *reshut ha-rabbim* fixing the trough, he sets the vessel down on the ground, and then when he picks it up again to bring it to the animal, *that* is a full *akira* from *reshut ha-rabbim* with intent to bring it to *reshut ha-yachid*, and that is the act from which the *gezeira* protects.

### Approach 1: Holding

The *gemara* thus presents a first approach: the *braita*'s prohibition of holding the vessel before the animal applies categorically in any normal scenario. Since it is never permitted to hold the vessel without restraining the cow, the *mishnah*'s ruling — that *rosho ve-rubo* is required — necessarily addresses the case where the person *is* holding the cow (and the vessel). In that case, *rosho ve-rubo* of the cow is both required and sufficient, even though the person maintains physical control.

### Approach 2: Not Holding

The second approach (*ika d'amri*) inverts the reading of the first question. According to this version, it may be taken as given that *rosho ve-rubo* suffices when one is holding both the vessel and the cow. The question is whether *rosho ve-rubo* suffices when one is *not* holding the cow — or whether a greater portion of the animal must be inside. The *mishnah* is then cited to show that *rosho ve-rubo* suffices in the case of not holding the cow. And thanks to *Abaye*'s qualification of the *braita*, the prohibition of holding the vessel without restraining the cow is not categorical — it applies only in the special case of the extended trough (*avus*). Outside that scenario, holding the vessel while the cow's head and majority of body are inside is perfectly valid, and *rosho ve-rubo* is sufficient.

The two approaches thus differ in their starting assumptions. The first assumes that holding the vessel without restraining the cow is generally forbidden, and therefore the *mishnah* must be addressing the case where one holds both, in which circumstance *rosho ve-rubo* is required but sufficient. The second assumes that holding the vessel without restraining the cow is generally permitted (the *braita*'s prohibition being limited to the *avus* case), and the *mishnah* addresses that ordinary case, ruling that *rosho ve-rubo* is sufficient even without holding the cow.

## Camel Distinction

### Camel Long Neck

The *gemara* then introduces a *braita* about feeding a camel: if the camel's head and majority of body (*rosho ve-rubo*) are inside the *pasi biraot* enclosure, one may engage in *obsin* — stuffing food into its mouth, the manner in which camels are typically fed, as the *gemara* in *Masechet Shabbat* discusses at length. The *gemara* reasons that *obsin* is analogous to holding both the vessel and the cow simultaneously, since one is actively engaged with the animal's mouth. Yet even in this case, *rosho ve-rubo* is required. This seems to refute the first approach's implicit suggestion that perhaps less than *rosho ve-rubo* would suffice when one holds both animal and vessel, since here one holds both and *rosho ve-rubo* is still demanded.

### Other Animals

Rav Acha bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Sheshes responds: a camel is different (*shani gamal*) because of its long neck. Even when one controls a camel physically, the neck is long enough that the animal can easily extend its head outside the domain. Consequently, *rosho ve-rubo* remains essential for a camel even when the feeder has full control. For an animal with a short neck — a cow, for instance — when the person physically restrains it, the degree of control is qualitatively greater, and it remains possible that less than *rosho ve-rubo* would suffice.

The *gemara* then introduces a second *braita*: for a generic *behema* (animal) with its head and majority of body inside, one may engage in *obsin*. Once again, the *gemara* reads this as equivalent to holding both animal and vessel, yet *rosho ve-rubo* is required. If a generic *behema* also requires *rosho ve-rubo* even when controlled, there is no ground for distinguishing camel from cow.

### Rabbi Eliezer Prohibition

The *gemara* resolves this by arguing that the two *braitot* were not composed together; the word *behema* in the second *braita* also refers to a camel. The *gemara* supports this by citing another *braita*: Rabbi Eliezer prohibits the use of a camel for this purpose altogether, precisely because of its long neck. The neck of a camel introduces a category of risk that cannot be adequately controlled, and therefore Rabbi Eliezer extends his concern to an absolute prohibition in the camel's case. This supports the reading that the halakhic difficulties raised by *rosho ve-rubo* in the *braitot* are specific to the camel's unique anatomy, leaving open the possibility that for other animals — where physical restraint provides full control — the requirements may be less demanding.
