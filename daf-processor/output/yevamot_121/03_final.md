# Yevamot 121 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Body ID & 3-Day Rule

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כָּל וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא לְקוּלָּא פְּלִיג, אוֹ לְחוּמְרָא פְּלִיג.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that one may testify to someone's death only when he saw the corpse within three days of the individual's death. However, **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava says: Not every** person, nor every place, nor every hour is identical. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Did Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava disagree** with the Rabbis with the intent **to** rule more **leniently** and hold that sometimes one may testify to the identity of one who died even if he did not see the body within three days of his death? **Or, did he disagree** with the intent **to** rule more **stringently** and hold that sometimes one may not testify even if he saw the body within three days of the individual's death?

The daf opens with a *mishna* near the top of 121 in which the question of identifying a drowned man's body is governed by a three-day standard: after three days the body may bloat beyond recognition, so the window for valid identification closes. Rabbi Yehuda dissents, arguing that not all people are the same and not all conditions are the same — no fixed rule can be applied uniformly across every case.

### Lenient or Strict?

The *gemara* asks: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, likula pligi o lechumra pligi?* — Is Rabbi Yehuda being lenient or strict relative to the *chachamim*? Tosafos notes that the obvious answer is neither, or rather both: his position is that there is no fixed rule. He is strict in the sense that sometimes even fewer than three days may be insufficient, and lenient in the sense that sometimes more than three days may still allow valid identification. The point is that circumstance determines the outcome, not an arbitrary standard.

### Proof Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּטָבַע בְּכַרְמֵי, וְאַסְּקוּהוּ אַבֵּי הֲדָיָא לְבָתַר תְּלָתָא יוֹמִין, וְאַנְסְבַהּ רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ. וְתוּ: הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּאטְבַע בְּדִגְלַת, וְאַסְּקוּהוּ אַגִּישְׁרָא דְּשַׁבִּיסְתָּנָא, וְאַנְסְבַהּ רָבָא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ אַפּוּמָּא דְשׁוֹשְׁבִינֵי לְבָתַר חַמְשָׁה יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and hear** a solution: **A certain man drowned in** a place called **Carmi, and they drew him out** of the water **near Bei Hedya after three days, and Rav Dimi from Neharde'a allowed his wife to marry. And furthermore, a certain man drowned in the Tigris** River, **and they drew him out** of the river **onto the Bridge of Shabistana, and Rava allowed his wife to marry based upon his friends' testimony,** although the body was seen only **five days after** death.

The *gemara* then brings two cases to demonstrate that Rabbi Yehuda's position at minimum permits leniency beyond the three-day limit. In the first, a man drowned in a place called Charmi, his body was recovered in a location called Chadya, and Rav Dimi of Nehardea permitted his wife to remarry — after three days. In the second, a man drowned in the Tigris, his body was recovered at the bridge of Shavistana, and Rava permitted his wife to remarry based on the testimony of the deceased's *shushvini* — his close companions — after five days. The *gemara* argues: if Rabbi Yehuda is being lenient, these rulings make sense as following his position. But if he is only being strict — tightening the standard so that even fewer than three days may be insufficient — then who are Rav Dimi and Rava following?

### Water Shrinkage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְקוּלָּא פְּלִיג, אִינְהוּ דַּעֲבוּד כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְחוּמְרָא פְּלִיג, אִינְהוּ דַּעֲבוּד כְּמַאן? שָׁאנֵי מַיָּא, דְּצָמְתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava **disagreed** with the intent **to** rule more **leniently, these** Sages **who acted** here, allowing these women to marry, acted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. But if you say he disagreed** with the intent **to** rule more **stringently, in accordance with whose** opinion did these Sages **act?** The Gemara answers: **Water is different, since it contracts** the body, preventing it from bloating and changing shape.

The *gemara* deflects: *shani maye d'tsamtei* — water is different, because it shrivels the body. Therefore, in drowning cases specifically, there is more time within which identification remains possible. This means Rav Dimi and Rava may actually be following the *chachamim* who maintain the three-day standard, while applying a recognized extension for the category of drowning — distinct from Rabbi Yehuda's case-by-case approach. The irony is not lost: Rabbi Yehuda's whole point is that you cannot make categorical rules, yet the *gemara* proposes that drowning constitutes its own formal category warranting extended time.

There is an apparent tension with a principle discussed previously — that *maya marzu maka*, water exacerbates a wound and makes even non-fatal injuries lethal. The *gemara* resolves this: that principle applies where there is an existing wound. Where there is no wound, *mitsmat tsamit* — the water will shrivel the body, permitting identification over a longer period.

### Timing of Exam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּכִי אַסְּקֵיהּ חַזְיֵיהּ בְּשַׁעְתֵּיהּ. אֲבָל אִישְׁתַּהִי, מִיתְפָּח תָּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this applies** only in a case **where they drew him out** of the water and **viewed him at that time. But** if the viewing **was delayed** for some time after the body was drawn from the water, it certainly **will have become** very **bloated,** making it impossible to positively identify.

A further qualification follows: this extended window applies only *dehachi aska chazi besha'atei* — if, when the body was brought out of the water, it was examined immediately. But *aval ishtahi*, if they waited before examining it, *mitpach tapach* — it will bloat once removed from the water and will no longer be recognizable. The *gemara* does not reach a firm conclusion about the precise scope of Rabbi Yehuda's disagreement, but it is clear that even the *chachamim's* three-day standard should not be read as an absolutely rigid rule.

## Edges of Water

### Definition of Edges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָפַל לְמַיִם, בֵּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן סוֹף, בֵּין שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁנָּפַל לְבוֹר הַגָּדוֹל, וְעָלָה לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a man **fell into the water** and did not come out, **whether** the body of water **has** a visible **end or does not have** a visible **end, his wife is prohibited** from remarrying. There is no absolute proof that the man died, as it is possible that he emerged from the water some distance away. **Rabbi Meir said: An incident** occurred **involving a certain** person **who fell into the Great Cistern and emerged** only **after three days.** This is evidence that sometimes one may survive a fall into water, even when everyone assumes he is dead.

The *mishna* now introduces one of the central distinctions in this chapter: *mayim she-yesh lahem sof* versus *mayim she-ein lahem sof* — water that has a visible edge versus water that does not. The formulation, *nafal lemayim bein she-yesh lahem sof bein she-ein lahem sof*, sets up the question of whether the wife of a drowning victim may remarry. The principle that emerges is counterintuitive at first glance: if the shores of the water are visible on all sides, the wife may remarry even without a body; if the shores cannot be seen, she may not. One might have thought that visible shores would give the man a better chance of escape and survival — which is precisely why the rule makes sense. If the shores are visible and he did not emerge, we can be confident he did not survive. If the water is boundless and the far edges cannot be seen, he may have emerged somewhere beyond sight.

A student raises a sharp point: a river would always seem to qualify as *ein lahem sof*, since one can never see its terminus. The *gemara* will bear this out.

### Rabbi Meir Position

### Rabbi Yosi's Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּסוֹמֵא שֶׁיָּרַד לִטְבּוֹל בִּמְעָרָה, וְיָרַד מוֹשְׁכוֹ אַחֲרָיו, וְשָׁהוּ כְּדֵי שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁם — וְהִשִּׂיאוּ אֶת נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶם. וְשׁוּב מַעֲשֶׂה בְּעַסְיָא, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשִּׁלְשְׁלוּהוּ לַיָּם, וְלֹא עָלְתָה בְּיָדָם אֶלָּא רַגְלוֹ, אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַעְלָה — תִּנָּשֵׂא. מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַטָּה — לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: An incident** occurred **involving a blind man who descended to immerse** for ritual purity **in a cave, and his guide descended after him,** and they disappeared there, **and they remained there long enough for their souls to have departed, and** the Sages **permitted their wives to marry** because they had disappeared into the water and not emerged. **And** there was **another incident in Asya in which they lowered a certain** man **into the sea** on a rope, **and** when they pulled the rope back to land **only his leg came up in their hands,** and they were not certain whether he was alive or dead. **The Sages said:** If his leg was cut **from the knee and above,** his wife **may marry,** as he did not survive such a wound; if his leg was cut only **from the knee and below, she may not marry.**

Rabbi Yosi disagrees, though characteristically he does not state a legal principle directly — he responds with stories. First, a blind man descended into a cave to immerse; another person apparently followed him down. The sages waited the time estimated to be sufficient for both men to die from lack of air or sustenance, and then permitted their wives to remarry. Second, in Asia, a man was lowered into the sea for deep-sea diving and only his leg was recovered.

### Severed Limb Rule

Regarding the recovered limb, the sages ruled: *min ha-arkuvah ule-ma'lah tinasei* — if the leg was severed above the knee, the wife may remarry, since a wound of that severity is certainly fatal. *Min ha-arkuvah ule-matah lo tinasei* — if it was severed below the knee, she may not. This case is somewhat distinct from the broader debate, since here physical evidence of a fatal wound exists; the question is merely whether the partial body is sufficient proof of death. The deeper disagreement between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi concerns whether one can declare death without any body at all, based solely on lethal circumstances.

### Baraita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָפַל לַמַּיִם, בֵּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף, בֵּין שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת, וְשֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** If a man **fell into the water, whether** the body of water **has** a visible **end or does not have** a visible **end, his wife is prohibited** from marrying; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** If he fell into a body of water **with** a visible **end, his wife is permitted** to marry, **but** if he fell into a body of water **with no end, his wife is prohibited** from marrying.

The *gemara* presents a *baraita* that makes the positions explicit: *nafal lemayim bein she-yesh lahem sof bein she-ein lahem sof ishto asurah divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir forbids in all cases. *Ve-chachamim omrim, mayim she-yesh lahem sof ishto muteret* — the *chachamim* permit the wife to remarry if the water has a visible edge. *Ve-she-ein lahem sof, ishto asurah* — if the water has no visible edge, she is forbidden, since the man might have emerged somewhere beyond the observer's sight.

### 4-Direction Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֹּל שֶׁעוֹמֵד וְרוֹאֶה מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** defining a body of **water with** a visible **end? Abaye said: Any** body of water **where one stands** in one place **and can see** the shore **in** all **four directions** is considered water with a visible end, since anyone emerging from the water would be seen. However, if the body of water is so large that it is impossible to see its shore on all sides, the individual may have emerged at a place where he could not be seen by others standing at the place where he fell in.

The *gemara* asks: what exactly constitutes *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*? Abaye defines it: *kol she-omed vero-eh me-arba ruchotav* — any body of water where one can stand and see its shores in all four directions. If even one direction is unobserved, one cannot rule out that the man emerged there.

## Rav Shila Story

### Wife Married

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּטְבַע בְּאַגְמָא דְסַמְקֵי, אַנְסְבַהּ רַב שֵׁילָא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לִשְׁמוּאֵל, תָּא נְשַׁמְּתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִשְׁלַח לֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain man who drowned in the lake in** a place called **Samkei. Rav Sheila allowed his wife to marry** based on the testimony of witnesses who saw that he entered the water and did not emerge. **Rav said to Shmuel: Come, let us excommunicate him** for having issued this ruling. Shmuel **said to him: Let us first send him** a message and clarify whether he had a sufficient reason to issue this ruling.

The *gemara* presents a case: a man drowned in the swamp of Samki. Rav Shila permitted the man's wife to remarry. Upon learning this, Rav said to Shmuel: *ta nishmateih* — let us excommunicate him, placing him in *shamta*, since this swamp appears to be a case of *ein lahem sof*, and Rav Shila had no authority to permit the wife.

### Shmuel's Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף, אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה אוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת? שְׁלַח לְהוּ: אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה. וְאַגְמָא דְסַמְקֵי — מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף, אוֹ מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף? שְׁלַח לְהוּ: מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף הוּא. וּמָר, מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** **They sent him** the following question: When a man disappears in **an endless body of water, is his wife a forbidden or a permitted** woman, i.e., may she remarry? **He sent** back **to them: His wife is forbidden.** They asked him further: **Is the lake of Samkei a body of water with** a visible **end or an endless body of water? He sent** a response **to them: It is an endless body of water,** since one cannot see the water's edge on every side. They then asked him: If so, **what is the reason** that **the Master,** i.e., Rav Sheila, **acted this way,** allowing the wife to remarry?

Shmuel counseled restraint: *nishlach lei bereisha* — let us first send him a message and investigate before acting. They sent Rav Shila two questions. First: in water that has no visible edge, is the wife permitted or forbidden? He responded: *ishto asurah* — she is forbidden. Second: is the swamp of Samki considered *yesh lahem sof* or *ein lahem sof*? Pressed, he acknowledged it was *ein lahem sof*. They then asked him directly: given these concessions, why did you permit her to remarry?

### Rav's Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיטְעָא טְעֵינָא, אֲנָא סְבַרִי כֵּיוָן דִּקְווּ וְקָיְימִי — כְּמַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף דָּמֵי, וְלָא הִיא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא גַּלֵּי — אֵימוֹר גַּלֵּי אַשְׁפְּלוּ[הּ].
>
> **Translation:** He answered them: **I erred** in my reasoning. **I thought: Since the waters are gathered and stagnant** and not flowing like a river, **they are considered as a body of water with** a visible **end. But that is not so. Since there are waves** in this body of water, **say that the waves carried him** away from our sight, allowing him to emerge without being seen.

Rav Shila admitted: *mita te'ina* — I made a mistake. *Ana savri, kivan d'ka-i v'kaimi k'mayim she-yesh lahem sof dami* — I assumed that since this is standing water — a swamp, with no moving current — even though the edges cannot be seen, it functions like *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*. My reasoning was that if the water is still, a person who survived would have surfaced near where he went under. There would be no current to carry him elsewhere.

This was, on its face, a very reasonable assumption. Indeed, the puzzle in the story is how exactly Rav Shila came to reverse himself. When asked whether the swamp was *yesh lahem sof* or *ein lahem sof*, he presumably should have answered *yesh lahem sof* — since that was his original reasoning. Somewhere in the questioning he recognized that his interlocutors did not approve of his ruling, and he adjusted his position accordingly.

### Waves Theory

The *gemara* explains why the reasoning fails: *kivan d'ika gali, eimar gali ashpluhu* — since there are waves, or currents, in the swamp, perhaps the currents carried him beneath the surface. Even in seemingly still water, wind alone acting on the surface can generate waves sufficient to carry a body. Therefore, even standing water without visible shores remains categorically *ein lahem sof*.

### No Shamta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי שְׁמוּאֵל עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב: ״לֹא יְאוּנֶּה לַצַּדִּיק כׇּל אָוֶן״. קָרֵי רַב עֲלֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: ״וּתְשׁוּעָה בְּרוֹב יוֹעֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** In light of this response, **Shmuel recited** this verse **about Rav: "No mischief shall befall the righteous"** (Proverbs 12:21). Since the righteous Rav waited and did not excommunicate Rav Sheila, he was prevented from causing him injustice. Rav Sheila had been mistaken and had not intentionally violated the decree of the Sages prohibiting a woman from remarrying on the basis of her husband having disappeared into an endless body of water. **Rav recited** this verse **about Shmuel: "But salvation** lies **in much counsel"** (Proverbs 11:14), since it was Shmuel's advice that caused Rav to wait.

Once it became clear that Rav Shila had acted in good faith, making an honest legal error rather than willfully defying accepted practice, the question of *shamta* fell away. *Shamta* is reserved for one who brazenly violates established bounds, not for a *posek* who reasons carefully and errs. Accordingly, Shmuel applied to Rav the verse *lo ye-uneh la-tzadik kol aven* — no harm comes to the righteous man — since Rav was spared from the improper act of excommunicating someone who had made an honest mistake. And Rav applied to Shmuel the verse *ve-teshuah b'rov yo-etz* — salvation comes from good counsel — crediting Shmuel's prudent advice for saving him from that error. Regarding the woman herself, the *gemara* will address this shortly: even in a case of *ein lahem sof* without a body, if the wife was in fact permitted and married, she may remain married *bedi'avad*.

## Against Rabbi Meir

### Rejected Meir

By this point in the sugya it has become clear that the *halacha* follows the *chachamim* against Rabbi Meir. In cases of *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*, even without a body, one may testify to the husband's death and the wife may remarry.

### Torah Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא דַּאֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה — הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּאִינִישׁ דְּעָלְמָא, אֲבָל צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן — לָא. אִי דִּסְלֵיק — קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ. וְלָא הִיא: לָא שְׁנָא אִינִישׁ דְּעָלְמָא, וְלָא שְׁנָא צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן — דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: That which the Sages said,** that if a man fell into **an endless body of water, his wife is prohibited** from remarrying, **applies** only **to an ordinary person** who is not well known and could slip away secretly and live in anonymity, hiding the fact that he survived. **But** it does **not** apply to **a Torah scholar,** because **if he would emerge** from the water, **publicity would be** generated and the news of his survival would spread. The Gemara rejects this: **That is not so. It is not different** for **an ordinary man and it is not different** for **a Torah scholar.** If a woman remarried based on testimony that her husband drowned in an endless body of water, **after the fact, yes,** she may remain married, but she may **not** remarry ***ab initio*.**

Rav Ashi introduces a further extension: *hada amru rabanan mayim she-ein lahem sof ishto asurah, hani mili be-inish de-alma* — when the *rabbanan* say that in boundless water the wife is forbidden, that applies to an ordinary person. *Aval tzurba me-rabanan* — but if the deceased was a Torah sage, then even in *ein lahem sof*, one may assume he is dead. The reasoning is: *e d'salik, kala is lei* — if he had survived and emerged somewhere, word would have spread, since he is a person of consequence. The absence of any such report is itself evidence of his death.

### No Fame Rule

The *gemara* rejects this: *velo hi, lo shna inish de-alma velo shna tzurba me-rabanan* — this distinction does not hold. Fame does not create a legal category that transforms the evidentiary standard. The underlying concern about boundless water applies equally to sages and ordinary men.

### Bedi'avad Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא: לָא שְׁנָא אִינִישׁ דְּעָלְמָא, וְלָא שְׁנָא צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן — דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **That is not so. It is not different** for **an ordinary man and it is not different** for **a Torah scholar.** If a woman remarried based on testimony that her husband drowned in an endless body of water, **after the fact, yes,** she may remain married, but she may **not** remarry ***ab initio*.**

However, the *gemara* now introduces, without extensive discussion, a principle that is not apparent in the *mishna* at all and is something of a surprise: *bedi'avad*, if the woman already remarried — even in a case of *ein lahem sof* without a recovered body — *le-chatchila lo*, she cannot be permitted to remarry at the outset, but if she did, she may remain with her new husband. This is a significant ruling that the *gemara* drops almost in passing, and it will resurface explicitly in the later discussion of the Chassa case.

## Shipwreck Stories

### Rav Dimi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם מְכַמְּרִין מִכְמוֹרִין בַּיַּרְדֵּן, וְנִכְנַס אֶחָד מֵהֶם לִמְחִילָּה שֶׁל דָּגִים, וְשָׁקְעָה חַמָּה וְלֹא רָאָה פִּתְחָהּ שֶׁל מְחִילָּה, וְשָׁהָה חֲבֵרוֹ כְּדֵי שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשׁוֹ, וּבָא וְהוֹדִיעַ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. לְמָחָר, זָרְחָה חַמָּה וְהִכִּיר פִּתְחָהּ שֶׁל מְחִילָּה, וּבָא וּמָצָא הֶסְפֵּד גָּדוֹל בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי: כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁאָמְרוּ: מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת, שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: An incident** occurred **involving two people** who **were fishing with nets on the Jordan** River, **and one of them entered a cave containing a** pond of **fish** next to the shore. Meanwhile, **the sun set and** the one who had entered the cave **could not see the cave's opening** and did not exit, so his friend thought he had drowned. **His friend waited long enough for his soul to have departed and came and notified** the man's **household** that he had drowned. **The following day the sun rose, and** the man in the cave **recognized the opening of the cave** and exited through it. **And he came and found profuse eulogizing in his house. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said** about this: **How great are the words of the Sages, who said:** If a man fell into **a body of water with** a visible **end, his wife is permitted** to marry, but in a case of water **with no end, his wife is prohibited** from marrying.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi — Rebbe Yehuda HaNasi — reports a story of two fishermen casting nets in the Jordan. One entered an underwater tunnel where fish were found and became trapped when night fell and he could no longer see the exit. His companion waited long enough to conclude the man had died and informed his household. The next morning, when light returned, the trapped fisherman found the opening and returned home to find a *hesped gadol* — a great eulogy — already underway in his house. Rashi explains that there were air pockets within the cavern that allowed him to survive.

### Gamliel's Praise

Rabbi concludes this account with the words *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim* — how great are the words of the sages. The *gemara* will explain his precise intent in what follows.

### Gamliel Shipwreck

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף נָמֵי, לֵיחוּשׁ לִמְחִילָּה שֶׁל דָּגִים! בְּמַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף מְחִילָּה שֶׁל דָּגִים לָא שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** even in the case of **a body of water with** a visible **end, let us also be concerned about a cave containing a** pond of **fish.** Even if the individual did not emerge for a long period of time, it is possible that he entered a cave and is still alive. The Gemara answers: **A cave containing a** pond of **fish is not common in a body of water with** a visible **end.**

The *gemara* raises an objection: if this cavern-survival is possible, should we not also be concerned about it even in cases of *yesh lahem sof*? The *gemara* responds: *mayim she-yesh lahem sof, mechila shel dagin lo shchichi* — in smaller, bounded bodies of water, such underwater caverns with air pockets are uncommon. The rule for *ein lahem sof* is thus justified by the greater range of unusual possibilities that vast bodies of water present, including both distance of emergence and subterranean air pockets.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בִּסְפִינָה, וְרָאִיתִי סְפִינָה אַחַת שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה. וְהָיִיתִי מִצְטַעֵר עַל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁבָּהּ, וּמַנּוּ — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וּכְשֶׁעָלִיתִי בַּיַּבָּשָׁה, בָּא וְיָשַׁב וְדָן לְפָנַי בַּהֲלָכָה. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: בְּנִי, מִי הֶעֶלְךָ? אָמַר לִי: דַּף שֶׁל סְפִינָה נִזְדַּמֵּן לִי, וְכׇל גַּל וְגַל שֶׁבָּא עָלַי — נִעְנַעְתִּי לוֹ רֹאשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* records Rabban Gamliel's testimony: **he was once sailing and saw another ship wrecked at sea. He was distressed in particular for the Torah sage aboard — Rabbi Akiva. When he later docked and Rabbi Akiva appeared before him to discuss *halacha*, Rabban Gamliel asked how he had survived. Rabbi Akiva told him that a plank from the shipwreck had come his way, and he had clung to it. Regarding each wave that approached, *naaneti lo roshi* — he bent his head so the wave passed over him rather than knocking him off.**

A *baraita* records Rabban Gamliel's testimony: he was once sailing and saw another ship wrecked at sea. He was distressed in particular for the Torah sage aboard — Rabbi Akiva. When he later docked and Rabbi Akiva appeared before him to discuss *halacha*, Rabban Gamliel asked how he had survived. Rabbi Akiva told him that a plank from the shipwreck had come his way, and he had clung to it. Regarding each wave that approached, *naaneti lo roshi* — he bent his head so the wave passed over him rather than knocking him off.

### Akiva's Survival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אִם יָבוֹאוּ רְשָׁעִים עַל אָדָם — יְנַעְנֵעַ לוֹ רֹאשׁוֹ. אָמַרְתִּי בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה: כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁאָמְרוּ: מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף — מוּתֶּרֶת, מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From here the Sages stated: If wicked people come upon a person, he should bend his head before them.** That is, he should temporarily restrain himself and not fight them, and he will therefore be saved. After this parenthetical statement, the Gemara returns to Rabban Gamliel's story. **I said at that moment: How great are the words of the Sages, who said:** If a man fell into **a body of water with** a visible **end,** his wife **is permitted** to remarry. But in a case of **an endless body of water,** his wife **is prohibited** from remarrying.

From this experience Rabbi Akiva derived a *mashal*: *im yavo-u resha-im al adam, yenane-a lo rosho* — when wicked people come against a person, bow the head, let the force pass. Discretion is preferable to a futile stand. Rabban Gamliel concluded: *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim she-amru mayim she-yesh lahem sof muteret mayim she-ein lahem sof asurah* — how great is the ruling that in boundless water the wife is forbidden, because he himself had witnessed a shipwreck in open ocean and yet the man survived, carried to safety on a plank, invisible to any observer.

### Akiva's 2nd

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בִּסְפִינָה, וְרָאִיתִי סְפִינָה אַחַת שֶׁמִּטָּרֶפֶת בַּיָּם, וְהָיִיתִי מִצְטַעֵר עַל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁבָּהּ, וּמַנּוּ — רַבִּי מֵאִיר. כְּשֶׁעָלִיתִי לִמְדִינַת קַפּוֹטְקְיָא, בָּא וְיָשַׁב וְדָן לְפָנַי בַּהֲלָכָה. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: בְּנִי, מִי הֶעֱלְךָ? אָמַר לִי: גַּל טְרָדַנִי לַחֲבֵרוֹ, וַחֲבֵרוֹ לַחֲבֵרוֹ, עַד שֶׁהֱקִיאַנִי לַיַּבָּשָׁה. אָמַרְתִּי בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה: כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁאָמְרוּ: מַיִם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת, מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Akiva said: Once I was traveling on a boat, and I saw a certain boat sinking at sea, and I was grieved over** the apparent death of **the Torah scholar who was on board. And who was it? Rabbi Meir. But when I disembarked at the province of Cappadocia, he came, and sat, and deliberated before me about *halakha*. I said to him: My son, who brought you up** from the water? **He said to me:** One **wave carried me to another, and that other** wave **to another, until** I reached the shore, and a wave **cast me up onto dry land. I said at that moment: How great are the words of the Sages, who said:** If a man fell into **a body of water with** a visible **end, his wife is permitted** to remarry. But in a case of **an endless body of water, his wife is prohibited** from remarrying.

A second *baraita* then presents the parallel account from Rabbi Akiva's own perspective. Rabbi Akiva reports that he was once at sea and saw another ship in distress, aboard which was a Torah sage — Rabbi Meir. When Rabbi Akiva later arrived at Cappadocia (*Kaftor*, identified with modern-day Turkey), Rabbi Meir appeared and wished to discuss *halacha*. Rabbi Akiva asked how he had survived. Rabbi Meir replied: *gal tardani le-chavero ve-chavero le-chavero, ad she-hikiani la-yabasha* — one wave cast me to the next, and the next to the next, until I was thrown ashore. Rabbi Akiva likewise concluded: *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim she-amru mayim she-yesh lahem sof isha muteret mayim she-ein lahem sof isha asurah* — since a person can survive in open water by being swept from wave to wave to a shore beyond sight, therefore we must forbid remarriage in *ein lahem sof*.

It is worth noting that this shipwreck — an event presumably of great consequence in Rabbi Akiva's life — appears here only as an aside. And as one might note, the stories have a somewhat formulaic quality: in each account, the teacher witnesses his student's ship in distress, the student survives through a remarkable means, and the teacher draws the same legal conclusion. Nevertheless, the Talmudic point they illustrate remains clear and vivid.

## Non-Water Cases

### Lions Pit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָפַל לְגוֹב אֲרָיוֹת — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו. לַחֲפוּרָה מְלֵאָה נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים — מְעִידִין עָלָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: אַף לַחֲפוּרָה מְלֵאָה נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו, חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא חַבָּר הוּא. וְתַנָּא קַמָּא, אַגַּב אִיצְצָא מַזְּקִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If an individual **fell into a lion's den, one may not testify about him** that he died, since the lions might not have killed him. If he fell **into a pit filled with snakes and scorpions, one may testify** about his death, since it is certain that they killed him. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: Even** if he fell **into a pit filled with snakes and scorpions, one may not testify about him** that he died, because **we are concerned** that **perhaps he is a charmer [*ḥabbar*]** of snakes and scorpions who knows magic or some stratagem to keep them from harming him. **And the first *tanna*** holds that we need not be concerned about this possibility, **as, due to the pressure** of his body falling on top of them, **they will harm him,** even if he could control them under other circumstances.

The *gemara* turns to cases that do not involve water. *Nafal le-gov arayot, ein me-idim alav* — if a man fell into a pit of lions, one cannot testify to his death, because we cannot be certain the lions attacked him. Rashi suggests the lions may not have been hungry, and indeed observation from wildlife contexts suggests that lions do not attack simply out of proximity.

### Snakes/Scorpions

*Le-chafura me-le-a nechashim va-akrabim, me-idim alav* — if he fell into a pit full of snakes and scorpions, the *tanna kamma* holds that one can testify to his death, since in such crowded conditions they will certainly attack him. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira disagrees: *af le-chafura me-le-a nechashim va-akrabim ein me-idim alav* — even there one cannot testify, since we must consider the possibility that *shema chaver hu* — perhaps the man is a snake charmer who knows how to handle them. The *tanna kamma* responds: *agav itzam mat-ke-lei* — even a snake charmer, if he is being pressed and crowded by them, will be bitten.

### Fiery Furnace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָפַל לְתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ — מְעִידִין עָלָיו. לְיוֹרָה מְלֵאָה יַיִן וָשֶׁמֶן — מְעִידִין עָלָיו. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אַחָא אָמְרוּ: שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן — מְעִידִין עָלָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַבְעִיר. שֶׁל יַיִן — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מְכַבֶּה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: תְּחִלָּתוֹ מְכַבֶּה וְסוֹפוֹ מַבְעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If one **fell into a blazing furnace, one may testify about him** that he has died. If he fell **into** a boiling **cauldron filled** with **wine or oil, one may** also **testify about him** that he has died. **In the name of Rabbi Aḥa, they said:** If he fell into a cauldron **of oil, one may testify about him** that he has died, **because this ignites** the fire even more. Through the force of his fall, oil will splash into the fire and increase the heat of the cauldron. However, if he fell into a cauldron **of wine, one may not testify about him** that he has died, **because** when wine splashes into the fire **it extinguishes** the fire. **They said to him: At first it** partially **extinguishes** the fire **but eventually it ignites** it even more, and therefore it can be assumed that he has died.

*Nafal le-toch kivshan ha-esh, me-idim alav* — if thrown into a fiery furnace, one can testify to his death. This scenario recalls the story of Chananya, Mishael, and Azaryah, and it is understood that survival in such circumstances would require open miracle.

### Boiling Liquids

If a man fell into a large vat, *yora*, filled with boiling wine or oil, a distinction is drawn. In the name of Rabbi Akiva: *shemen me-idim alav mipnei she-hu maveer* — in boiling oil one can testify to his death, since when he falls in, the oil splashes onto the fire and intensifies it. *Shel yayin, ein me-idim alav mipnei she-hu mechabeh* — but in boiling wine, one cannot testify to his death, since the wine splashes onto the fire and extinguishes it, potentially allowing escape. The sages respond to this: *techila hu mechabeh ve-sofo maveer* — it may initially douse the flame but the fire will reignite, so ultimately death can be assumed.

### Well Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁנָּפַל לַבּוֹר הַגָּדוֹל וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: אֵין מַזְכִּירִין מַעֲשֵׂה נִסִּים. מַאי מַעֲשֵׂה נִסִּים? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא אֲכַל וְלָא אִישְׁתִּי — וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְצוּמוּ עָלַי וְאַל תֹּאכְלוּ וְאַל תִּשְׁתּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that **Rabbi Meir said: An incident** occurred **involving a certain** individual **who fell into the Great Cistern** and emerged after three days. It **is taught** in a *baraita*: **They said to Rabbi Meir: One does not mention miraculous acts** to teach general *halakha*. The Gemara asks: **What** about that story defines it as an example of **miraculous acts? If we say** the fact **that he did not eat or drink** for three days and still survived is miraculous, **but isn't it written: "Fast for me, and neither eat nor drink** three days, night or day" (Esther 4:16), demonstrating that one may survive this experience naturally?

Rabbi Meir reasserts his position: *ma-aseh be-echad she-nafal le-vor gadol* — a man fell into a large well and survived for three days. Therefore, absent a confirmed body, one can never testify to death. The *baraita* records the sages' response: *ein mazkirin ma-aseh nissim* — one cannot cite miracles as legal precedent. Halachic standards must be calibrated to normal circumstances.

### Sleep & Ledges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא נָיֵים. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אִישַׁן שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ, וְיִישַׁן לְאַלְתַּר. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כִּיפִּין עַל גַּב כִּיפִּין הֲווֹ. וְרַבָּנַן — דְּשֵׁישָׁא הֲווֹ. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא מְסָרֵיךְ וְנָיֵים פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the miraculous element is **that he did not sleep** during those three days, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If one says: I hereby take on **an oath that I will not sleep for three days,** the court **flogs him** for taking an oath in vain, **and he may sleep immediately** because it is impossible to remain awake for three consecutive days. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Meir, what is the reason** this was not a miraculous incident? The Gemara answers that **Rav Kahana said: There were** several levels of **arches** built **on top of** other **arches** within the Great Cistern, and he supported himself on the arches and slept. **And the Rabbis** said that **they were** made **of marble** and it was impossible to hold onto them and sleep. **And Rabbi Meir** assumed that **it is inconceivable that he didn't grasp** some part of an arch for a short time **and sleep a little,** and therefore this incident does not qualify as a miracle.

The *gemara* asks what the miraculous element of the survival was. If it was merely that he went without food and water for three days — *tzomu alai ve-al tochlu ve-al tishtu shloshet yamim* — we know people can survive that long. Rather, the *gemara* suggests, the miracle is that he did not sleep. Rabbi Yochanan rules that a vow not to sleep for three days is a *shvuat shav* — an oath that cannot be kept — and the person is immediately lashed and permitted to sleep. Staying awake for three days is considered physiologically impossible and therefore miraculous.

Rabbi Meir's counter: Rav Kahana suggests that perhaps *kifin al gabi kifin havu* — there were ledges within the well on which he could have positioned himself and slept. The Rabbis respond that *de-shisha havu* — the ledges were smooth marble, offering no secure foothold for sleep. Rabbi Meir maintains it is conceivable to lean against a small indentation and rest. The discussion leaves unresolved exactly how any of this maps onto the water-survival question, but the broader point stands: miracles cannot serve as the basis for legal standards.

## Nechunya Story

### Daughter Saved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבִתּוֹ שֶׁל נְחוּנְיָא חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין שֶׁנָּפְלָה לְבוֹר הַגָּדוֹל, וּבָאוּ וְהוֹדִיעוּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא. שָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁלוֹם. שְׁנִיָּה, אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁלוֹם. שְׁלִישִׁית, אָמַר לָהֶם: עָלְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: An incident** occurred **involving the daughter of Neḥunya the well digger, who fell into the Great Cistern, and they came and notified Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa** and asked that he pray for her. After **the first hour, he said to them:** She is at **peace** and unharmed. After **the second** hour, **he said to them:** She is at **peace.** After **the third** hour **he said to them: She has ascended** from the cistern.

The *gemara* presents a well-known *aggadic* account: the daughter of Nechunya the well-digger — whose great *mitzvah* to the public was digging wells for the *olei regel*, those who ascended to Jerusalem for the pilgrims' festivals — fell into a large well. The people came and informed Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa. After the first hour he told them: *shalom*, she is fine. After the second hour: *shalom*. After the third hour — the point at which survival was considered no longer possible — he said: *alta*, she has been saved, she has come up.

### Ram & Old Man

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ: בִּתִּי, מִי הֶעֱלֵךְ? אָמְרָה לוֹ: זָכָר שֶׁל רְחֵלִים נִזְדַּמֵּן לִי, וְזָקֵן מַנְהִיגוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נָבִיא אַתָּה? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״לֹא נָבִיא אָנֹכִי וְלֹא בֶּן נָבִיא אָנֹכִי״, אֶלָּא: דָּבָר שֶׁהַצַּדִּיק מִתְעַסֵּק בּוֹ — יִכָּשֵׁל בּוֹ זַרְעוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** When she came to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa, **he said to her: My daughter, who pulled you up** from the cistern? **She said to him: A male sheep,** i.e., a ram, **happened by** and sensed **me** in that cistern, **and** there was **an old man leading him** who pulled me out. **They said** to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa: **Are you a prophet** with knowledge of what is happening far away? **He said to them,** using a figure of speech from the Bible: **"I am not a prophet, nor the son of a prophet am I"** (Amos 7:14). **Rather,** I reasoned as follows: Could it be that concerning **the endeavor that the righteous** Neḥunya **is engaged** in, i.e., digging for the benefit of the public, **his offspring would stumble upon** its fruits and thereby be killed? I therefore knew that God would certainly save her.

When asked how she survived, she reported: *zachar shel rachelim nizdamen li, ve-zaken manhigo* — a ram appeared for her, and an old man was leading it. Rashi identifies the old man as Avraham Avinu, and the ram as the ram of the Akeidah.

### Tzadik Logic

Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa's reasoning was not prophecy but inference: *ela davar she-ha-tzadik mit-asek bo, yikashel bo zar-o?* — could it be that the very thing this righteous man devoted his life to would be the instrument of his child's death? His whole life was spent ensuring the public had water. That his daughter would perish in a well was inconceivable to Rabbi Chanina. A *tzadik's* life's work cannot end in the undoing of his own household through that very work.

### Son's Thirst

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: אַף עַל פִּי כֵן מֵת בְּנוֹ בַּצָּמָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּסְבִיבָיו נִשְׂעֲרָה מְאֹד״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְדַקְדֵּק עִם סְבִיבָיו כְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said: Even so, the son** of Neḥunya the well digger **died of thirst,** and the merit of his father, who attended to the water supply, did not protect him, **as is stated: "And around Him it storms [*nisara*] mightily"** (Psalms 50:3), **which teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, is exacting with His surroundings,** i.e., the righteous who are close to Him, **up to a hairsbreadth [*sa'ara*],** so that even slight deviations can elicit severe punishment.

Rabbi Abba then introduces the jarring counterpoint: *af al pi chen, met b'no ba-tzama* — nevertheless, his son died of thirst. The man who spent his life making water available died having a son who perished for lack of it.

### Harsh Justice

The verse cited is *u-svivav nis-ara me-od* — around Him there is great storm — interpreted as *melamed she-ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu medakdek im svivav ke-chut ha-se-ara* — God exacts from those close to Him with the precision of a hair's breadth. Tosafos attempts to soften the juxtaposition, noting that at least it was not the same well Nechunya dug from which his son died. But the *gemara* does not allow the narrative to rest comfortably on the idea that the world is simply just or that *tzadikim* are shielded from tragedy. The juxtaposition is deliberate: yes, the daughter was saved, and yes, the son died. The world is not simply ordered by reward and punishment in predictable ways, and even those close to God are held to an exacting standard that brings suffering alongside blessing.

## Hearsay Evidence

### Women's Hearsay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֲפִילּוּ שָׁמַע מִן הַנָּשִׁים אוֹמְרוֹת ״מֵת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי״ — דַּיּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁמַע מִן הַתִּינוֹקוֹת אוֹמְרִים ״הֲרֵי אָנוּ הוֹלְכִין לִסְפּוֹד וְלִקְבּוֹר אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי״. בֵּין שֶׁהוּא מִתְכַּוֵּין, וּבֵין שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Even** if one **heard from the women,** who were **saying: So-and-so died, this is sufficient** in order to testify to his death. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even if one heard from the children,** who were **saying: We are going to eulogize and bury so-and-so,** that is also sufficient. Furthermore, one may rely upon someone mentioning that a man died, regardless of **whether** the speaker **intends** to testify and thereby allow the man's wife to remarry **or whether he does not intend** to offer formal testimony.

The *mishna* now broadens the categories of acceptable testimony in agunah cases in a striking direction: *afilu shama min ha-nashim omrot met ish ploni dayo* — even if one merely heard from women saying that so-and-so has died, that suffices. While earlier *mishnayot* had already indicated that women could testify in these contexts, the novelty here is not the gender of the witness but the informality of the communication. This is not a formal act of testimony — it is hearsay.

### Children Testify

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *afilu shama min ha-tinokot omrim harei anu holchim lispod u-likbor et ish ploni* — even if one hears from children saying, "we are going to eulogize and bury so-and-so," that suffices. Here the witness lacks legal standing on two grounds: children are not formal witnesses, and the statement is not presented as testimony.

### Intent Question

The *mishna* continues: *bein she-nitkaven bein she-eino mitkaven* — whether the speaker intended to testify or not. This raises the question of whether intentionality, which typically strengthens testimony, is here a factor at all.

### Jew vs. Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא אוֹמֵר: בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא מִתְכַּוֵּין, וּבְגוֹי אִם הָיָה מִתְכַּוֵּין — אֵין עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava says: With regard to a Jew** who offers this information, it may be relied upon **even if he intends** for his statement to be considered formal testimony. However, **with regard to a gentile, if he intended** to testify, **his testimony is not** considered valid **testimony.** His statement is relied upon only when he does not intend to state it as formal testimony.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava distinguishes between Jews and non-Jews. Regarding a non-Jew: *u-v'ovdei kochavim im haya mitkaven ein eduto edut* — if a non-Jew intentionally testified, it is not valid testimony. The concern is that an intentional non-Jewish declaration may be motivated by a desire to cause harm. Only when a non-Jew speaks innocently — *mesiach lefi tumo* — do we trust him, since there is no suspicion of an ulterior motive. Regarding a Jew, there are two variant readings in the text: one holds that even intentional testimony by a Jew is acceptable; the other, the inverse — that a Jew must testify formally and intentionally for his statement to carry weight, while mere passing remarks are insufficient.

## Intention Narrowed (II)

### Rav Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְגוֹי, אִם הָיָה מִתְכַּוֵּין וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לְהַתִּיר, אֲבָל נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהָעִיד — עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת. הֵיכִי יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בָּא לְבֵית דִּין וְאָמַר: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מֵת, הַשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ״ — זֶהוּ נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַתִּיר. ״מֵת״ סְתָם — זֶהוּ נִתְכַּוֵּין לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **With regard to a gentile, if he intended** to testify, his testimony is not considered valid testimony. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They taught** this **only** in a case **where he intended to permit** the woman to remarry through his testimony, **but** if **he** merely **intended to testify** about the man's death, **his testimony is** considered **testimony.** The Gemara asks: **How do we know** the intention of the gentile? **Rav Yosef said:** If **he came to the court and said: So-and-so died, allow his wife to marry, this is** an instance of **intending to permit** her to remarry. If he said simply: **He died, this is** an instance of merely **intending to testify.**

The *gemara* now works to narrow the category of *nitkaven* — intentional testimony — to a very thin slice. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *lo shanu ela she-nitkaven le-hatir* — the disqualification of a non-Jew's intentional testimony applies only if his explicit intent was to permit the woman to remarry. *Aval nitkaven le-ha-id, eduto edut* — if he came merely to testify, stating the fact of the man's death without specifically invoking the wife's status, his testimony is valid. The practical result is that *nitkaven le-hatir* — explicitly coming to court to say "permit this woman to remarry" — becomes an extremely narrow category. How often does someone phrase his testimony that way? In almost every realistic case the man will simply say that the husband is dead, and that is accepted.

The *gemara* asks: how would we even know which intent he had? Rav Yosef clarifies: *ba le-veit din ve-amar ish ploni met, hasi-u et ishto, zehu nitkaven le-hatir* — if he came to court and said, "so-and-so has died; you should permit his wife to remarry," that is *nitkaven le-hatir*, and it is rejected. *Meit stam* — if he simply said "so-and-so is dead" without mentioning the wife, that is *nitkaven le-ha-id*, and it is accepted.

### Reish vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לְהַתִּיר, אֲבָל נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהָעִיד — עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרַבִּי, שֶׁהִתִּירָם עִם שְׁמֹנִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה זְקֵנִים. אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לְהַתִּיר, אֲבָל נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהָעִיד — עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת. וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** This **was also stated** by other *amora'im*. **Reish Lakish said: They taught** this **only when one intended to permit** the woman to remarry, **but if he** merely **intended to testify** about the man's death, **his testimony is** considered valid **testimony. Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: Didn't such an incident occur involving** Rabbi **Oshaya the Distinguished, who permitted** women to marry based upon the testimony of gentiles while he was **with eighty-five Elders? He said to** the Elders: **They taught** that one may not rely upon a gentile's testimony **only when he intended to permit** the woman to remarry, **but if he** merely **intended to testify** about the man's death, **his testimony is** considered valid **testimony. But the Rabbis did not concur with him** on this, and they maintained that one may not rely upon the testimony of a gentile at all.

Reish Lakish states the same principle: only explicit intent to permit her remarriage disqualifies the testimony. Rabbi Yochanan objects, citing a tradition that this very position — "only *nitkaven le-hatir* is disqualified, but *nitkaven le-ha-id* is valid" — was contested by eighty-five elders, who rejected it. According to Rabbi Yochanan, any intentional testimony by a non-Jew is disqualified, regardless of how he frames it. The *gemara* then presses Rabbi Yochanan: if any intentional statement is invalid, what does *mesiach lefi tumo* look like? The answer: *bemesiach lefi tumo* — a person simply speaking without any formal purpose. The subsequent cases illustrate this.

## Innocent Speech

### Innocent Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: וּבְגוֹי, אִם הָיָה מִתְכַּוֵּין — אֵין עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? בְּמֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ. כִּי הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: ״מַאן אִיכָּא בֵּי חִיוַּאי, מַאן אִיכָּא בֵּי חִיוַּאי, שְׁכֵיב חִיוַּאי״, וְאַנְסְבַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לִדְבֵיתְהוּ. הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: ״וַוי לֵיהּ לְפָרָשָׁא זְרִיזָא דַּהֲוָה בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא דִּשְׁכֵיב״, וְאַנְסְבַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף, וְאִיתֵּימָא רָבָא, לִדְבֵיתְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But,** if that is so, what about **that** which **is taught** in **the mishna: With regard to a gentile, if he intended** to testify, **his testimony is not** considered valid **testimony,** which implies that if the gentile does not intend to testify, his statement may be relied upon? **How can you find** a case where one would rely on his statement? The Gemara answers: One may rely on a gentile's statement **when he speaks offhandedly,** without any intention to testify. **Like that** gentile **who was going around saying: Who is** from **the house of Ḥivvai; who is** from **the house of Ḥivvai? Ḥivvai has died. And** based upon this report, **Rav Yosef allowed** Ḥivvai's **wife to marry.** There was also **a certain** gentile **who was going around saying: Alas for the brave horseman who was in Pumbedita, for he is dead. And Rav Yosef, and some say Rava, allowed his wife to marry.**

The *gemara* presents examples of *mesiach lefi tumo*. In one case, a man was walking about lamenting: *vai lah le-parshata de-zeriza dahavei be-Pumbedita de-shachiv* — woe to the zealous soldier from Pumbedita who has died. He was simply bewailing someone's death. On the basis of this, Rav Yosef — or according to another version, Rava — permitted the dead man's wife to remarry.

### Chassa Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: ״מַאן אִיכָּא בֵּי חָסָא, טְבַע חָסָא״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָאֱלֹקִים! אֲכַלוּ כְּווֹרֵי לְחָסָא מִדִּיבּוּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אֲזַלָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְחָסָא וְאִינַּסְבָא, וְלָא אֲמַרוּ לָהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** There was also **a certain** gentile **who was going around saying: Who is** from **the house of Ḥasa? Ḥasa has drowned. Rav Naḥman said: By God! The fish have eaten Ḥasa.** The Gemara relates: **Due to Rav Naḥman's statement,** although he did not issue a court ruling permitting it, **Ḥasa's wife went and married, and no one said anything to her** to protest this action.

In another case, someone was going about asking: *man ika bei Chassa? tava Chassa* — who is from the family of Chassa? Chassa drowned. Rav Nachman, upon hearing this, exclaimed: *achlu kavrei le-Chassa* — the fish have eaten Chassa. What is striking here is that Rav Nachman's exclamation itself constitutes an implicit ruling: by saying the fish ate Chassa, he was accepting the testimony of this non-Jew speaking innocently, and implicitly ruling that Chassa should be presumed dead — without apparent concern that Chassa might have emerged on another shore. From this it follows that Rav Nachman was treating this as a case he was willing to rule upon.

## Bedi'avad Remarriage

### Stays Married

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הָא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה, הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְכַתְּחִלָּה, אֲבָל אִי נָסֵיב — לָא מַפְּקִינַן לַהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Learn** the following **from this** incident: **That which the Sages said,** that if a man fell into **an endless body of water, his wife is prohibited** from remarrying, **this applies** only ***ab initio*, but if** someone **married** her, **we do not take her away from him.**

The *gemara* uses the Chassa case to crystallize the *bedi'avad* principle. When the *rabbanan* say *mayim she-ein lahem sof isha asurah* — that in boundless water the wife is forbidden — this refers to *lechatchila*: at the outset, a *beit din* may not permit her to remarry in such circumstances. But that prohibition is not absolute.

### Self-Marriage

Chassa's wife took matters into her own hands: *azlah devinhu de-Chassa ve-insava* — she went and remarried on her own, without rabbinic sanction. She had not received a formal ruling; she overheard a scholar make a loose remark and acted unilaterally.

### No Divorce

Nevertheless, *velo amru lah velo midi* — no one said anything to her, before or after. The *gemara* did not require her to divorce her new husband. This is a powerful illustration of the *bedi'avad* principle: even where remarriage was not permitted prospectively, after the fact she is not compelled to undo it.

### Rav Nachman's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אַנְסְבַהּ רַב נַחְמָן לִדְבֵיתְהוּ, אֲמַר: חָסָא גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אִיתֵיהּ, אִם אִיתָא דִּסְלֵיק — קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ לְמִילְּתָא. וְלָא הִיא: לָא שְׁנָא גַּבְרָא רַבָּה, לָא שְׁנָא לָאו גַּבְרָא רַבָּה — דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Naḥman** actually issued a ruling and **allowed his wife to marry. He said: Ḥasa is a great man; if it was so that he emerged** from the water **the incident would have generated publicity.** Since nothing was heard from Ḥasa in a long while, it can be assumed that he died. The Gemara comments: **That is not so. It is not different** if he is **a great man** and it **is not different** if he is **not a great man.** If a woman remarried based on testimony that her husband drowned in an endless body of water, **after the fact, yes,** she may remain married, but she may **not** remarry ***ab initio*.**

Some say (*ika de-amri*) that Rav Nachman actually did formally permit her: *ansivah Rav Nachman lidevinhu* — Rav Nachman permitted his wife to remarry. His reasoning would then parallel Rav Ashi's earlier suggestion: *Chassa gavra rabba isei, im issa de-salik kala is lei le-milsa* — Chassa was a man of great stature, and had he survived and emerged elsewhere, word would have reached us. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *velo hi, lo shna gavra rabba velo shna lo gavra rabba* — fame creates no legal distinction. This is not a valid basis for a *lechatchila* ruling, neither for the famous nor for the ordinary man.

### Lechatchila/Bedi'avad

The final distinction stands: *lechatchila*, a *beit din* may not permit remarriage in *ein lahem sof* without a body. *Bedi'avad* — if she remarried, she remains married. This principle, barely hinted at in the *mishna*, emerges through the case of Chassa's wife as one of the significant practical *chiddushim* of the entire sugya. The *gemara's* sustained drive throughout the daf has been to expand the conditions under which a woman can be freed from her *agunah* status, not by overriding the rules but by carefully defining their contours and by establishing that post-facto remarriage, even in borderline cases, is not undone.
