# Yevamot 121 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Body ID & 3-Day Rule

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

The daf opens with a *mishna* near the top of 121 in which the question of identifying a drowned man's body is governed by a three-day standard: after three days the body may bloat beyond recognition, so the window for valid identification closes. Rabbi Yehuda dissents, arguing that not all people are the same and not all conditions are the same — no fixed rule can be applied uniformly across every case.

### Lenient or Strict?

The *gemara* asks: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, likula pligi o lechumra pligi?* — Is Rabbi Yehuda being lenient or strict relative to the *chachamim*? Tosafos notes that the obvious answer is neither, or rather both: his position is that there is no fixed rule. He is strict in the sense that sometimes even fewer than three days may be insufficient, and lenient in the sense that sometimes more than three days may still allow valid identification. The point is that circumstance determines the outcome, not an arbitrary standard.

### Proof Sources

The *gemara* then brings two cases to demonstrate that Rabbi Yehuda's position at minimum permits leniency beyond the three-day limit. In the first, a man drowned in a place called Charmi, his body was recovered in a location called Chadya, and Rav Dimi of Nehardea permitted his wife to remarry — after three days. In the second, a man drowned in the Tigris, his body was recovered at the bridge of Shavistana, and Rava permitted his wife to remarry based on the testimony of the deceased's *shushvini* — his close companions — after five days. The *gemara* argues: if Rabbi Yehuda is being lenient, these rulings make sense as following his position. But if he is only being strict — tightening the standard so that even fewer than three days may be insufficient — then who are Rav Dimi and Rava following?

### Water Shrinkage

The *gemara* deflects: *shani maye d'tsamtei* — water is different, because it shrivels the body. Therefore, in drowning cases specifically, there is more time within which identification remains possible. This means Rav Dimi and Rava may actually be following the *chachamim* who maintain the three-day standard, while applying a recognized extension for the category of drowning — distinct from Rabbi Yehuda's case-by-case approach. The irony is not lost: Rabbi Yehuda's whole point is that you cannot make categorical rules, yet the *gemara* proposes that drowning constitutes its own formal category warranting extended time.

There is an apparent tension with a principle discussed previously — that *maya marzu maka*, water exacerbates a wound and makes even non-fatal injuries lethal. The *gemara* resolves this: that principle applies where there is an existing wound. Where there is no wound, *mitsmat tsamit* — the water will shrivel the body, permitting identification over a longer period.

### Timing of Exam

A further qualification follows: this extended window applies only *dehachi aska chazi besha'atei* — if, when the body was brought out of the water, it was examined immediately. But *aval ishtahi*, if they waited before examining it, *mitpach tapach* — it will bloat once removed from the water and will no longer be recognizable. The *gemara* does not reach a firm conclusion about the precise scope of Rabbi Yehuda's disagreement, but it is clear that even the *chachamim's* three-day standard should not be read as an absolutely rigid rule.

## Edges of Water

### Definition of Edges

The *mishna* now introduces one of the central distinctions in this chapter: *mayim she-yesh lahem sof* versus *mayim she-ein lahem sof* — water that has a visible edge versus water that does not. The formulation, *nafal lemayim bein she-yesh lahem sof bein she-ein lahem sof*, sets up the question of whether the wife of a drowning victim may remarry. The principle that emerges is counterintuitive at first glance: if the shores of the water are visible on all sides, the wife may remarry even without a body; if the shores cannot be seen, she may not. One might have thought that visible shores would give the man a better chance of escape and survival — which is precisely why the rule makes sense. If the shores are visible and he did not emerge, we can be confident he did not survive. If the water is boundless and the far edges cannot be seen, he may have emerged somewhere beyond sight.

A student raises a sharp point: a river would always seem to qualify as *ein lahem sof*, since one can never see its terminus. The *gemara* will bear this out.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *mishna* opens with the position of Rabbi Meir, clarified by a *baraita*: *nafal lemayim bein she-yesh lahem sof bein she-ein lahem sof ishto asurah divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that in either case, without a body, the wife is forbidden. He supports this with a case: *ma'aseh be-echad she-nafal le-vor gadol ve-alah le-achar sheloshah yamim* — a certain person fell into a deep pit and emerged alive after three days. As long as there is no confirmed body, the man might have survived. Rabbi Meir's position is therefore absolute: no body, no testimony, no remarriage, regardless of the water's extent.

### Rabbi Yosi's Cases

Rabbi Yosi disagrees, though characteristically he does not state a legal principle directly — he responds with stories. First, a blind man descended into a cave to immerse; another person apparently followed him down. The sages waited the time estimated to be sufficient for both men to die from lack of air or sustenance, and then permitted their wives to remarry. Second, in Asia, a man was lowered into the sea for deep-sea diving and only his leg was recovered.

### Severed Limb Rule

Regarding the recovered limb, the sages ruled: *min ha-arkuvah ule-ma'lah tinasei* — if the leg was severed above the knee, the wife may remarry, since a wound of that severity is certainly fatal. *Min ha-arkuvah ule-matah lo tinasei* — if it was severed below the knee, she may not. This case is somewhat distinct from the broader debate, since here physical evidence of a fatal wound exists; the question is merely whether the partial body is sufficient proof of death. The deeper disagreement between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi concerns whether one can declare death without any body at all, based solely on lethal circumstances.

### Baraita Rule

The *gemara* presents a *baraita* that makes the positions explicit: *nafal lemayim bein she-yesh lahem sof bein she-ein lahem sof ishto asurah divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir forbids in all cases. *Ve-chachamim omrim, mayim she-yesh lahem sof ishto muteret* — the *chachamim* permit the wife to remarry if the water has a visible edge. *Ve-she-ein lahem sof, ishto asurah* — if the water has no visible edge, she is forbidden, since the man might have emerged somewhere beyond the observer's sight.

### 4-Direction Test

The *gemara* asks: what exactly constitutes *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*? Abaye defines it: *kol she-omed vero-eh me-arba ruchotav* — any body of water where one can stand and see its shores in all four directions. If even one direction is unobserved, one cannot rule out that the man emerged there.

## Rav Shila Story

### Wife Married

The *gemara* presents a case: a man drowned in the swamp of Samki. Rav Shila permitted the man's wife to remarry. Upon learning this, Rav said to Shmuel: *ta nishmateih* — let us excommunicate him, placing him in *shamta*, since this swamp appears to be a case of *ein lahem sof*, and Rav Shila had no authority to permit the wife.

### Shmuel's Advice

Shmuel counseled restraint: *nishlach lei bereisha* — let us first send him a message and investigate before acting. They sent Rav Shila two questions. First: in water that has no visible edge, is the wife permitted or forbidden? He responded: *ishto asurah* — she is forbidden. Second: is the swamp of Samki considered *yesh lahem sof* or *ein lahem sof*? Pressed, he acknowledged it was *ein lahem sof*. They then asked him directly: given these concessions, why did you permit her to remarry?

### Rav's Admission

Rav Shila admitted: *mita te'ina* — I made a mistake. *Ana savri, kivan d'ka-i v'kaimi k'mayim she-yesh lahem sof dami* — I assumed that since this is standing water — a swamp, with no moving current — even though the edges cannot be seen, it functions like *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*. My reasoning was that if the water is still, a person who survived would have surfaced near where he went under. There would be no current to carry him elsewhere.

This was, on its face, a very reasonable assumption. Indeed, the puzzle in the story is how exactly Rav Shila came to reverse himself. When asked whether the swamp was *yesh lahem sof* or *ein lahem sof*, he presumably should have answered *yesh lahem sof* — since that was his original reasoning. Somewhere in the questioning he recognized that his interlocutors did not approve of his ruling, and he adjusted his position accordingly.

### Waves Theory

The *gemara* explains why the reasoning fails: *kivan d'ika gali, eimar gali ashpluhu* — since there are waves, or currents, in the swamp, perhaps the currents carried him beneath the surface. Even in seemingly still water, wind alone acting on the surface can generate waves sufficient to carry a body. Therefore, even standing water without visible shores remains categorically *ein lahem sof*.

### No Shamta

Once it became clear that Rav Shila had acted in good faith, making an honest legal error rather than willfully defying accepted practice, the question of *shamta* fell away. *Shamta* is reserved for one who brazenly violates established bounds, not for a *posek* who reasons carefully and errs. Accordingly, Shmuel applied to Rav the verse *lo ye-uneh la-tzadik kol aven* — no harm comes to the righteous man — since Rav was spared from the improper act of excommunicating someone who had made an honest mistake. And Rav applied to Shmuel the verse *ve-teshuah b'rov yo-etz* — salvation comes from good counsel — crediting Shmuel's prudent advice for saving him from that error. Regarding the woman herself, the *gemara* will address this shortly: even in a case of *ein lahem sof* without a body, if the wife was in fact permitted and married, she may remain married *bedi'avad*.

## Against Rabbi Meir

### Rejected Meir

By this point in the sugya it has become clear that the *halacha* follows the *chachamim* against Rabbi Meir. In cases of *mayim she-yesh lahem sof*, even without a body, one may testify to the husband's death and the wife may remarry.

### Torah Sages

Rav Ashi introduces a further extension: *hada amru rabanan mayim she-ein lahem sof ishto asurah, hani mili be-inish de-alma* — when the *rabbanan* say that in boundless water the wife is forbidden, that applies to an ordinary person. *Aval tzurba me-rabanan* — but if the deceased was a Torah sage, then even in *ein lahem sof*, one may assume he is dead. The reasoning is: *e d'salik, kala is lei* — if he had survived and emerged somewhere, word would have spread, since he is a person of consequence. The absence of any such report is itself evidence of his death.

### No Fame Rule

The *gemara* rejects this: *velo hi, lo shna inish de-alma velo shna tzurba me-rabanan* — this distinction does not hold. Fame does not create a legal category that transforms the evidentiary standard. The underlying concern about boundless water applies equally to sages and ordinary men.

### Bedi'avad Rule

However, the *gemara* now introduces, without extensive discussion, a principle that is not apparent in the *mishna* at all and is something of a surprise: *bedi'avad*, if the woman already remarried — even in a case of *ein lahem sof* without a recovered body — *le-chatchila lo*, she cannot be permitted to remarry at the outset, but if she did, she may remain with her new husband. This is a significant ruling that the *gemara* drops almost in passing, and it will resurface explicitly in the later discussion of the Chassa case.

## Shipwreck Stories

### Rav Dimi Case

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi — Rebbe Yehuda HaNasi — reports a story of two fishermen casting nets in the Jordan. One entered an underwater tunnel where fish were found and became trapped when night fell and he could no longer see the exit. His companion waited long enough to conclude the man had died and informed his household. The next morning, when light returned, the trapped fisherman found the opening and returned home to find a *hesped gadol* — a great eulogy — already underway in his house. Rashi explains that there were air pockets within the cavern that allowed him to survive.

### Gamliel's Praise

Rabbi concludes this account with the words *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim* — how great are the words of the sages. The *gemara* will explain his precise intent in what follows.

### Gamliel Shipwreck

The *gemara* raises an objection: if this cavern-survival is possible, should we not also be concerned about it even in cases of *yesh lahem sof*? The *gemara* responds: *mayim she-yesh lahem sof, mechila shel dagin lo shchichi* — in smaller, bounded bodies of water, such underwater caverns with air pockets are uncommon. The rule for *ein lahem sof* is thus justified by the greater range of unusual possibilities that vast bodies of water present, including both distance of emergence and subterranean air pockets.

A *baraita* records Rabban Gamliel's testimony: he was once sailing and saw another ship wrecked at sea. He was distressed in particular for the Torah sage aboard — Rabbi Akiva. When he later docked and Rabbi Akiva appeared before him to discuss *halacha*, Rabban Gamliel asked how he had survived. Rabbi Akiva told him that a plank from the shipwreck had come his way, and he had clung to it. Regarding each wave that approached, *naaneti lo roshi* — he bent his head so the wave passed over him rather than knocking him off.

### Akiva's Survival

From this experience Rabbi Akiva derived a *mashal*: *im yavo-u resha-im al adam, yenane-a lo rosho* — when wicked people come against a person, bow the head, let the force pass. Discretion is preferable to a futile stand. Rabban Gamliel concluded: *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim she-amru mayim she-yesh lahem sof muteret mayim she-ein lahem sof asurah* — how great is the ruling that in boundless water the wife is forbidden, because he himself had witnessed a shipwreck in open ocean and yet the man survived, carried to safety on a plank, invisible to any observer.

### Akiva's 2nd

A second *baraita* then presents the parallel account from Rabbi Akiva's own perspective. Rabbi Akiva reports that he was once at sea and saw another ship in distress, aboard which was a Torah sage — Rabbi Meir. When Rabbi Akiva later arrived at Cappadocia (*Kaftor*, identified with modern-day Turkey), Rabbi Meir appeared and wished to discuss *halacha*. Rabbi Akiva asked how he had survived. Rabbi Meir replied: *gal tardani le-chavero ve-chavero le-chavero, ad she-hikiani la-yabasha* — one wave cast me to the next, and the next to the next, until I was thrown ashore. Rabbi Akiva likewise concluded: *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim she-amru mayim she-yesh lahem sof isha muteret mayim she-ein lahem sof isha asurah* — since a person can survive in open water by being swept from wave to wave to a shore beyond sight, therefore we must forbid remarriage in *ein lahem sof*.

It is worth noting that this shipwreck — an event presumably of great consequence in Rabbi Akiva's life — appears here only as an aside. And as one might note, the stories have a somewhat formulaic quality: in each account, the teacher witnesses his student's ship in distress, the student survives through a remarkable means, and the teacher draws the same legal conclusion. Nevertheless, the Talmudic point they illustrate remains clear and vivid.

## Non-Water Cases

### Lions Pit

The *gemara* turns to cases that do not involve water. *Nafal le-gov arayot, ein me-idim alav* — if a man fell into a pit of lions, one cannot testify to his death, because we cannot be certain the lions attacked him. Rashi suggests the lions may not have been hungry, and indeed observation from wildlife contexts suggests that lions do not attack simply out of proximity.

### Snakes/Scorpions

*Le-chafura me-le-a nechashim va-akrabim, me-idim alav* — if he fell into a pit full of snakes and scorpions, the *tanna kamma* holds that one can testify to his death, since in such crowded conditions they will certainly attack him. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira disagrees: *af le-chafura me-le-a nechashim va-akrabim ein me-idim alav* — even there one cannot testify, since we must consider the possibility that *shema chaver hu* — perhaps the man is a snake charmer who knows how to handle them. The *tanna kamma* responds: *agav itzam mat-ke-lei* — even a snake charmer, if he is being pressed and crowded by them, will be bitten.

### Fiery Furnace

*Nafal le-toch kivshan ha-esh, me-idim alav* — if thrown into a fiery furnace, one can testify to his death. This scenario recalls the story of Chananya, Mishael, and Azaryah, and it is understood that survival in such circumstances would require open miracle.

### Boiling Liquids

If a man fell into a large vat, *yora*, filled with boiling wine or oil, a distinction is drawn. In the name of Rabbi Akiva: *shemen me-idim alav mipnei she-hu maveer* — in boiling oil one can testify to his death, since when he falls in, the oil splashes onto the fire and intensifies it. *Shel yayin, ein me-idim alav mipnei she-hu mechabeh* — but in boiling wine, one cannot testify to his death, since the wine splashes onto the fire and extinguishes it, potentially allowing escape. The sages respond to this: *techila hu mechabeh ve-sofo maveer* — it may initially douse the flame but the fire will reignite, so ultimately death can be assumed.

### Well Miracle

Rabbi Meir reasserts his position: *ma-aseh be-echad she-nafal le-vor gadol* — a man fell into a large well and survived for three days. Therefore, absent a confirmed body, one can never testify to death. The *baraita* records the sages' response: *ein mazkirin ma-aseh nissim* — one cannot cite miracles as legal precedent. Halachic standards must be calibrated to normal circumstances.

### Sleep & Ledges

The *gemara* asks what the miraculous element of the survival was. If it was merely that he went without food and water for three days — *tzomu alai ve-al tochlu ve-al tishtu shloshet yamim* — we know people can survive that long. Rather, the *gemara* suggests, the miracle is that he did not sleep. Rabbi Yochanan rules that a vow not to sleep for three days is a *shvuat shav* — an oath that cannot be kept — and the person is immediately lashed and permitted to sleep. Staying awake for three days is considered physiologically impossible and therefore miraculous.

Rabbi Meir's counter: Rav Kahana suggests that perhaps *kifin al gabi kifin havu* — there were ledges within the well on which he could have positioned himself and slept. The Rabbis respond that *de-shisha havu* — the ledges were smooth marble, offering no secure foothold for sleep. Rabbi Meir maintains it is conceivable to lean against a small indentation and rest. The discussion leaves unresolved exactly how any of this maps onto the water-survival question, but the broader point stands: miracles cannot serve as the basis for legal standards.

## Nechunya Story

### Daughter Saved

The *gemara* presents a well-known *aggadic* account: the daughter of Nechunya the well-digger — whose great *mitzvah* to the public was digging wells for the *olei regel*, those who ascended to Jerusalem for the pilgrims' festivals — fell into a large well. The people came and informed Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa. After the first hour he told them: *shalom*, she is fine. After the second hour: *shalom*. After the third hour — the point at which survival was considered no longer possible — he said: *alta*, she has been saved, she has come up.

### Ram & Old Man

When asked how she survived, she reported: *zachar shel rachelim nizdamen li, ve-zaken manhigo* — a ram appeared for her, and an old man was leading it. Rashi identifies the old man as Avraham Avinu, and the ram as the ram of the Akeidah.

### Tzadik Logic

When people came to Rabbi Chanina and said *navi ata* — you must be a prophet, to have known she was saved — he replied: *lo navi anochi velo ben navi anochi*. His reasoning was not prophecy but inference: *ela davar she-ha-tzadik mit-asek bo, yikashel bo zar-o?* — could it be that the very thing this righteous man devoted his life to would be the instrument of his child's death? His whole life was spent ensuring the public had water. That his daughter would perish in a well was inconceivable to Rabbi Chanina. A *tzadik's* life's work cannot end in the undoing of his own household through that very work.

### Son's Thirst

Rabbi Abba then introduces the jarring counterpoint: *af al pi chen, met b'no ba-tzama* — nevertheless, his son died of thirst. The man who spent his life making water available died having a son who perished for lack of it.

### Harsh Justice

The verse cited is *u-svivav nis-ara me-od* — around Him there is great storm — interpreted as *melamed she-ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu medakdek im svivav ke-chut ha-se-ara* — God exacts from those close to Him with the precision of a hair's breadth. Tosafos attempts to soften the juxtaposition, noting that at least it was not the same well Nechunya dug from which his son died. But the *gemara* does not allow the narrative to rest comfortably on the idea that the world is simply just or that *tzadikim* are shielded from tragedy. The juxtaposition is deliberate: yes, the daughter was saved, and yes, the son died. The world is not simply ordered by reward and punishment in predictable ways, and even those close to God are held to an exacting standard that brings suffering alongside blessing.

## Hearsay Evidence

### Women's Hearsay

The *mishna* now broadens the categories of acceptable testimony in agunah cases in a striking direction: *afilu shama min ha-nashim omrot met ish ploni dayo* — even if one merely heard from women saying that so-and-so has died, that suffices. While earlier *mishnayot* had already indicated that women could testify in these contexts, the novelty here is not the gender of the witness but the informality of the communication. This is not a formal act of testimony — it is hearsay.

### Children Testify

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *afilu shama min ha-tinokot omrim harei anu holchim lispod u-likbor et ish ploni* — even if one hears from children saying, "we are going to eulogize and bury so-and-so," that suffices. Here the witness lacks legal standing on two grounds: children are not formal witnesses, and the statement is not presented as testimony.

### Intent Question

The *mishna* continues: *bein she-nitkaven bein she-eino mitkaven* — whether the speaker intended to testify or not. This raises the question of whether intentionality, which typically strengthens testimony, is here a factor at all.

### Jew vs. Non-Jew

Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava distinguishes between Jews and non-Jews. Regarding a non-Jew: *u-v'ovdei kochavim im haya mitkaven ein eduto edut* — if a non-Jew intentionally testified, it is not valid testimony. The concern is that an intentional non-Jewish declaration may be motivated by a desire to cause harm. Only when a non-Jew speaks innocently — *mesiach lefi tumo* — do we trust him, since there is no suspicion of an ulterior motive. Regarding a Jew, there are two variant readings in the text: one holds that even intentional testimony by a Jew is acceptable; the other, the inverse — that a Jew must testify formally and intentionally for his statement to carry weight, while mere passing remarks are insufficient.

## Intention Narrowed

### Rav Yehuda

The *gemara* now works to narrow the category of *nitkaven* — intentional testimony — to a very thin slice. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *lo shanu ela she-nitkaven le-hatir* — the disqualification of a non-Jew's intentional testimony applies only if his explicit intent was to permit the woman to remarry. *Aval nitkaven le-ha-id, eduto edut* — if he came merely to testify, stating the fact of the man's death without specifically invoking the wife's status, his testimony is valid. The practical result is that *nitkaven le-hatir* — explicitly coming to court to say "permit this woman to remarry" — becomes an extremely narrow category. How often does someone phrase his testimony that way? In almost every realistic case the man will simply say that the husband is dead, and that is accepted.

The *gemara* asks: how would we even know which intent he had? Rav Yosef clarifies: *ba le-veit din ve-amar ish ploni met, hasi-u et ishto, zehu nitkaven le-hatir* — if he came to court and said, "so-and-so has died; you should permit his wife to remarry," that is *nitkaven le-hatir*, and it is rejected. *Meit stam* — if he simply said "so-and-so is dead" without mentioning the wife, that is *nitkaven le-ha-id*, and it is accepted.

### Reish vs. Yochanan

Reish Lakish states the same principle: only explicit intent to permit her remarriage disqualifies the testimony. Rabbi Yochanan objects, citing a tradition that this very position — "only *nitkaven le-hatir* is disqualified, but *nitkaven le-ha-id* is valid" — was contested by eighty-five elders, who rejected it. According to Rabbi Yochanan, any intentional testimony by a non-Jew is disqualified, regardless of how he frames it. The *gemara* then presses Rabbi Yochanan: if any intentional statement is invalid, what does *mesiach lefi tumo* look like? The answer: *bemesiach lefi tumo* — a person simply speaking without any formal purpose. The subsequent cases illustrate this.

## Intention Narrowed (II)

### Innocent Speech

The *gemara* presents examples of *mesiach lefi tumo*. In one case, a man was walking about lamenting: *vai lah le-parshata de-zeriza dahavei be-Pumbedita de-shachiv* — woe to the zealous soldier from Pumbedita who has died. He was simply bewailing someone's death. On the basis of this, Rav Yosef — or according to another version, Rava — permitted the dead man's wife to remarry.

### Chassa Case

In another case, someone was going about asking: *man ika bei Chassa? tava Chassa* — who is from the family of Chassa? Chassa drowned. Rav Nachman, upon hearing this, exclaimed: *achlu kavrei le-Chassa* — the fish have eaten Chassa. What is striking here is that Rav Nachman's exclamation itself constitutes an implicit ruling: by saying the fish ate Chassa, he was accepting the testimony of this non-Jew speaking innocently, and implicitly ruling that Chassa should be presumed dead — without apparent concern that Chassa might have emerged on another shore. From this it follows that Rav Nachman was treating this as a case he was willing to rule upon.

## Bedi'avad Remarriage

### Stays Married

The *gemara* uses the Chassa case to crystallize the *bedi'avad* principle. When the *rabbanan* say *mayim she-ein lahem sof isha asurah* — that in boundless water the wife is forbidden — this refers to *lechatchila*: at the outset, a *beit din* may not permit her to remarry in such circumstances. But that prohibition is not absolute.

### Self-Marriage

Upon hearing Rav Nachman's remark that the fish had eaten Chassa, Chassa's wife took matters into her own hands: *azlah devinhu de-Chassa ve-insava* — she went and remarried on her own, without rabbinic sanction. She had not received a formal ruling; she overheard a scholar make a loose remark and acted unilaterally.

### No Divorce

Nevertheless, *velo amru lah velo midi* — no one said anything to her, before or after. The *gemara* did not require her to divorce her new husband. This is a powerful illustration of the *bedi'avad* principle: even where remarriage was not permitted prospectively, after the fact she is not compelled to undo it.

### Rav Nachman's Role

Some say (*ika de-amri*) that Rav Nachman actually did formally permit her: *ansivah Rav Nachman lidevinhu* — Rav Nachman permitted his wife to remarry. His reasoning would then parallel Rav Ashi's earlier suggestion: *Chassa gavra rabba isei, im issa de-salik kala is lei le-milsa* — Chassa was a man of great stature, and had he survived and emerged elsewhere, word would have reached us. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *velo hi, lo shna gavra rabba velo shna lo gavra rabba* — fame creates no legal distinction. This is not a valid basis for a *lechatchila* ruling, neither for the famous nor for the ordinary man.

### Lechatchila/Bedi'avad

The final distinction stands: *lechatchila*, a *beit din* may not permit remarriage in *ein lahem sof* without a body. *Bedi'avad* — if she remarried, she remains married. This principle, barely hinted at in the *mishna*, emerges through the case of Chassa's wife as one of the significant practical *chiddushim* of the entire sugya. The *gemara's* sustained drive throughout the daf has been to expand the conditions under which a woman can be freed from her *agunah* status, not by overriding the rules but by carefully defining their contours and by establishing that post-facto remarriage, even in borderline cases, is not undone.
