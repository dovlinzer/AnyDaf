# Kiddushin 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Torah vs. Marriage

### Tanu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה וְלִישָּׂא אִשָּׁה – יִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה. וְאִם אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ בְּלֹא אִשָּׁה – יִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If one has to decide whether **to study Torah or to marry a woman,** which should he do first? **He should study Torah and afterward marry a woman. And if it is impossible for him** to be **without a wife, he should marry a woman and then study Torah.**

The daf under discussion is daf 30, and we continue in the middle of a rich and multifaceted *mishnah* dealing with a father's obligations to his son. The preceding material covered *bris milah*, *pidyon haben*, and *talmud Torah*, and the shiur now turns to continue exploring *talmud Torah* and related themes.

The *gemara* on 29b opens with a *beraita* introduced by *tanu rabbanan*: between learning *Torah* and getting married — *lilmod Torah v'lisa isha* — which takes priority? This question is no longer about the parental obligation per se, but about competing priorities a young man faces, analogous to earlier questions such as whether one redeems oneself or one's son first, or whether one teaches oneself or one's son first.

### Torah First

The *beraita*'s answer is unambiguous: *yilmod Torah v'achar kach yisa isha* — first learn *Torah*, and only afterward get married. The reasoning is implicit but clear: once married, a man takes on a range of obligations and responsibilities that will inevitably compromise the time and energy available for *Torah* study. Marriage is a weighty undertaking, and one should therefore front-load one's *Torah* learning before assuming those burdens.

It should be noted that implicit in this statement is an assumption of a lifelong, ongoing responsibility to learn. The *gemara* had previously described the father's obligation to educate his son as a kind of foundational primary education — and indeed noted that if the father failed to teach, the son must teach himself. Yet the broader *ethos* of the rabbis is captured in *vehagita bo yomam valailah* — constant, perpetual engagement with *Torah*. The verse *v'shinnantam l'vanecha v'dibarta bam b'shivtecha b'vetecha u'v'lechtecha vaderech* gestures toward this unceasing obligation, even if its precise *halachic* parameters have not yet been fully articulated. The priority given to *Torah* over marriage reflects this assumption: one should be learning as much as possible, growing as much as possible, before taking on responsibilities that will curtail that growth.

### Exception

The *beraita* acknowledges a qualification: *v'im iy efshar lo b'lo isha* — if it is truly impossible for him to manage without a wife, whether for emotional reasons or, as the *gemara* will make explicit shortly, because of the pressure of sexual thoughts (*hirhurei aveira*), then *yisa isha v'achar yilmod Torah* — he may marry first and learn afterward. The hierarchy is not absolute. If a man genuinely needs to marry in order to function, that need takes precedence, but the ideal sequence remains *Torah* first. It is worth noting that the obligation to keep learning is ongoing regardless of when one marries.

## Shmuel vs. Yochanan

### Marriage First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה, נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is that one should **marry a woman and afterward study Torah.**

The *gemara* then records a striking ruling: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — the *halacha* is *nosei isha v'achar yilmod Torah*, marry first, then learn *Torah*. This is the direct opposite of the *beraita* just cited. The question immediately arises: how can Shmuel issue a ruling against an explicit *beraita*?

### Millstone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רֵיחַיִם בְּצַוָּארוֹ וְיַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה?! וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא – לַן, וְהָא – לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** How can one do this? With **a millstone** hanging **from his neck,** i.e., with the responsibility of providing for his family weighing upon him, can **he engage in Torah** study? The Gemara comments: **And** the *amora'im* **do not disagree; this is for us and that is for them.** In other words, one statement applies to the residents of Babylonia, whereas the other is referring to those living in Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, sides with the *beraita*, and expresses his position with a memorable image: *reichaim b'tzavaro v'ya'asok baTorah?* — you have a millstone around your neck and you think you can learn *Torah*? Once a man shoulders the *ol parnassah*, the yoke of family responsibilities and the need to earn a living, when will he ever find time to learn? Therefore, *Torah* must come first; marriage, with all its weight, must wait.

### V'lo Pligi

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *v'lo pligi* — they do not actually disagree. *Ha lan v'ha lahu* — this ruling applies to us, and that ruling applies to them. Shmuel's ruling is directed at the Jews of Babylonia, while Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is for the inhabitants of *Eretz Yisrael*. This resolution requires explanation, and *Rashi* provides it.

## Bavel vs. EY

### Rashi's Explanation

*Rashi*'s explanation is unquestionably the *pshat*. In Babylonia, scholars would leave home and travel to *Eretz Yisrael* to study *Torah*, and in doing so they would effectively abandon their wives and children for extended periods. The *gemara* at the beginning of *Gittin* records this phenomenon vividly: Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, writing from *Eretz Yisrael* to those in Babylonia whose husbands had come to learn and left their families behind, invokes the verse *vayitnu et hayeled b'zonah v'et hayaldah machru bayayin* — they have handed their children over to prostitution, as the abandoned families were left without support or supervision. The picture is stark: the pursuit of *Torah* in *Eretz Yisrael* sometimes came at an enormous cost to family life back in Babylonia.

### Babylonian Model

Beyond the geographic dynamic, there is a deeper conceptual difference in how the two communities understood the value of marriage itself. Many Babylonian *gemaras* describe the value of marriage in instrumental terms: marriage protects the husband from sin, provides an outlet for desire, frees him from domestic distraction so he can learn *Torah*, and supplies a kind of domestic infrastructure supporting his learning. In this framework, marriage serves the man's *Torah* study. It is thus consistent that in Babylonia the ruling would be to marry first: get married, satisfy your needs, and then go and learn *Torah* — even if that means eventually leaving your wife and household for extended periods.

### Monogamy/Polygamy

This difference in the conceptualization of marriage is also bound up with a broader sociological distinction. Babylonia, influenced by the surrounding Sassanid culture and still reflecting the biblical model, maintained polygamy, at least among the wealthy. *Eretz Yisrael*, shaped by Roman cultural norms, had moved toward monogamy. This historical difference has long-term consequences: Babylonian Jewish culture eventually flowed into *Sefarad*, where Jews lived among Muslims, and polygamy persisted in those communities. *Eretz Yisrael*'s culture flowed into *Ashkenaz*, embedded in Roman-Christian lands, where monogamy was the norm. This divergence is reflected in the *Yerushalmi*, which essentially assumes monogamy, while the *Bavli* assumes polygamy.

When marriage is understood as exclusively between one man and one woman — a genuine partnership — one thinks of it in terms of mutual obligation and intrinsic value. When the surrounding culture permits multiple wives, the perception of marriage shifts: a wife is more a member of the household than a full partner, and marriage is more easily framed in utilitarian terms.

### EY Model

In *Eretz Yisrael*, by contrast, the *gemara*s describe marriage in far more intrinsic terms. A man who is not married lacks *simcha*, lacks *shalom*, is in some fundamental sense incomplete. Marriage is a primary responsibility in itself, not merely a means to some other end. It follows that once a man in *Eretz Yisrael* marries, those obligations become paramount — *reichaim b'tzavaro*, as Rabbi Yochanan puts it. You cannot then simply leave your wife and go learn *Torah*. Therefore, the prescription for *Eretz Yisrael* is: learn your *Torah* first, because once you are married, that is your primary responsibility and it cannot be subordinated to learning. In Babylonia, by contrast, the attitude was more permissive: marry first, but one may still leave and devote long stretches of time to *Torah* study, since family obligations do not hold the same absolute priority.

*Rashi* reads the *gemara* this way straightforwardly. *Tosafot*, scandalized by what seems to be an endorsement of abandoning one's wife and children, attempts a complicated re-reading of the *gemara* — but *Rashi*'s reading is clearly the *pshat*. The tension this generates — between the all-consuming obligation of *talmud Torah* and a man's obligations to his family — remains unresolved in the *gemara* and is, of course, entirely alive today in communities where husbands learn in *kollel* while wives work and fathers are substantially absent from domestic life. The *Rambam* wrote sharply in his *Peirush HaMishnayot* on *Pirkei Avot* against making *Torah* into a *kardom lachpor bo* — a shovel to dig with — and against communal financial support for scholars. Yet the trade-off between *talmud Torah* and family responsibility is precisely what this *gemara* identifies as the defining difference between Babylonia and *Eretz Yisrael*.

## Marriage Age

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁתַּבַּח לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הוּנָא בִּדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא דְּאָדָם גָּדוֹל הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּשֶׁיָּבֹא לְיָדְךָ – הֲבִיאֵהוּ לְיָדִי. כִּי אֲתָא חַזְיֵיהּ דְּלָא פָּרֵיס סוּדָרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא פָּרֵיסַתְּ סוּדָרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּלָא נְסֵיבְנָא. אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ לְאַפֵּיהּ מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי דְּלָא חָזֵית לְהוּ לְאַפַּי עַד דְּנָסְבַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to marriage, the Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥisda would praise Rav Hamnuna to Rav Huna** by saying **that he is a great man.** Rav Huna **said to him: When he comes to you, send him to me. When** Rav Hamnuna **came** before him, Rav Huna **saw that he did not cover** his head with **a cloth,** as Torah scholars did. Rav Huna **said to him: What is the reason** that **you do not cover** your head **with a cloth?** Rav Hamnuna **said to him:** The reason is **that I am not married,** and it was not customary for unmarried men to cover their heads with a cloth. Rav Huna **turned his face away from him** in rebuke, and **he said to him: See** to it **that you do not see my face until you marry.**

The *gemara* continues within this general context of *talmud Torah* and marriage. The following story: *Rav Chisda* was in conversation with *Rav Huna*, singing the praises of *Rav Hamnuna* — *adam gadol hu*, a tremendous *talmid chacham*. *Rav Huna* replied: the next time he is with you, bring him to me. When *Rav Hamnuna* was presented to *Rav Huna*, *Rav Huna* noticed that he was not wearing a *sudra* — the kind of turban or head-covering worn by married men. He asked: *mai ta'ama lo parist sudra?* Why are you not wearing this? *Rav Hamnuna* replied: *de-lo nessivna* — because I am not yet married. At that, *Rav Huna* turned his face away and refused to look at him: *ad nessivta* — I will not look at you until you are married. The gesture was dramatic, even harsh.

### Ben Esrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וְלֹא נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה – כׇּל יָמָיו בַּעֲבֵירָה. בַּעֲבֵירָה סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כָּל יָמָיו בְּהִרְהוּר עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rav Huna** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says:** If one is **twenty years old and has not** yet **married a woman, all of his days** will be **in** a state of **sin** concerning sexual matters. The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** that he will be **in** a state of **sin** all of his days? **Rather, say** that this means the following: **All of his days** will be **in** a state of **thoughts of sin,** i.e., sexual thoughts. One who does not marry in his youth will become accustomed to thoughts of sexual matters, and the habit will remain with him the rest of his life.

The *gemara* explains *Rav Huna*'s severity: *Rav Huna l'ta'ameih* — this was consistent with his known position. He had stated that *ben esrim shana ve-lo nasa isha, kol yamav ba-aveira* — a man who reaches twenty without having married has spent all his days in sin. The *gemara* immediately qualifies this: literally in sin? No — *ella eima kol yamav b'hirhur aveira* — he has spent all his days besieged by sexual thoughts.

### God's Wait

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: עַד עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה יוֹשֵׁב הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וּמְצַפֶּה לָאָדָם מָתַי יִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ עֶשְׂרִים וְלֹא נָשָׂא, אוֹמֵר: תִּיפַּח עַצְמוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said, and similarly, the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Until** one reaches the age of **twenty years the Holy One, Blessed be He, sits and waits for a man,** saying: **When will he marry a woman? Once he reaches** the age of **twenty and has not married, He says: Let his bones swell,** i.e., he is cursed and God is no longer concerned about him.

*Rava* adds: *v'chen tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* — and the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught similarly. Until the age of twenty, God sits and waits — *nu*, when will this person get married? Once a person reaches twenty and has still not married, *amar tippach atzmotav* — let his bones be blasted! The parental quality of the image is striking: a kind of divine exasperation at a son who ought to have already acted.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי דַּעֲדִיפְנָא מֵחַבְרַאי, דִּנְסֵיבְנָא בְּשִׁיתְּסַר, וְאִי הֲוָה נָסֵיבְנָא בְּאַרְבֵּיסַר הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְשָׂטָן: גִּירָא בְּעֵינָיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The fact **that I am superior to my colleagues** is **because I married** at the age of **sixteen, and if I would have married at** the age of **fourteen,** I would have said to the Satan: An arrow in your eye.

*Rav Chisda* adds his own personal testimony: *hai de'adifna mikhavray, denasivna beshis'ar* — the reason I surpassed my colleagues in religious achievement is that I married at sixteen. Had I married at fourteen, *ha'amina lesatan girei be'einecha* — I could have said to the *satan*, arrows in your eyes! Marrying young eliminated the *yetzer hara* as a factor entirely.

This entire set of statements reflects a very much instrumental, utilitarian view of marriage: its function here is to provide an outlet for sexual desire, freeing the man to focus on *Torah* and *avodat Hashem*. There is no talk here of partnership, companionship, or intrinsic fulfillment — this is fully consistent with the Babylonian framework described earlier.

It is worth noting that the *Rambam* in *Hilchos Kiddushin* picks up on this *gemara* and gives it a striking *halachic* formulation. He states that from the age of seventeen onward, a man becomes obligated in the *mitzvah* of *peru u'revu*, and once he reaches twenty without having married, *harei ze over u'mevattel mitzvas asei* — he has actively negated a positive commandment. This is a remarkable claim. One would expect that as long as the man eventually has children, he has only postponed the *mitzvah* rather than negated it — *bittul* implies a definitive failure, not a delay. Yet the *Rambam* frames it in terms of *bittul*, hinging on the age of twenty established in our *gemara*. He does, however, create an exemption: if one is deeply engaged in *Torah* study and fears that marriage will derail his learning by imposing the necessity of earning a livelihood, then *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — being engaged in one *mitzvah* exempts one from another.

The *Rambam*'s use of the language *bittul mitzvas asei* in this context is puzzling in two respects. First, the *gemara* itself frames the concern in terms of *hirhurim*, not *bittul mitzvah*. Second, the standard model of *bittul* applies when one has definitively failed to perform something, not when one is merely pushing it off. That said, the *Rambam*'s position does carry practical implications: any community that does not actively push its young men to marry by age twenty is either not ruling in accordance with the *Rambam* or maintaining that the concept of *bittul mitzvah* is contingent on it being the *right time* — and that the appropriate time is itself part of the framing of when the *mitzvah* applies.

## Parental Guidance

### Age Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבִּי נָתָן בַּר אַמֵּי: אַדִּידָךְ עַל צַוְּארֵי דִּבְרָיךְ. מִשִּׁיתְּסַר וְעַד עֶשְׂרִים וְתַרְתֵּי וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: מִתַּמְנֵי סְרֵי עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabbi Natan bar Ami: While your hand is still on your son's neck,** i.e., while you still have authority and control over him, find him a wife. What is the appropriate age? **From sixteen until twenty-two, and some say from eighteen until twenty-four.**

The *gemara* then records a teaching of *Rava* addressed to *Rav Natan bar Ami*: *al tzavarei devareich* — literally "on the necks of your sons" — meaning, keep the pressure on, maintain the weight of encouragement on your son, *mi-shitz'ar ve-ad esrim ve-taryan* — from sixteen to twenty-two. The image of the weight on the neck deliberately echoes *reichaim b'tzavaro* from earlier — but here it is a productive pressure, parental effort to guide a son toward marriage.

### Two Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״חֲנֹךְ לַנַּעַר עַל פִּי דַרְכּוֹ״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, חַד אָמַר: מִשִּׁיתְּסַר וְעַד עֶשְׂרִים וְתַרְתֵּין, וְחַד אָמַר: מִתַּמְנֵי סְרֵי וְעַד עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** based on the verse: **"Train a child in the way that he should go"** (Proverbs 22:6). **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya** disagreed about the age in which the verse instructs the parent to educate his child: **One said** that the verse is referring to the ages **from sixteen until twenty-two, and one said** it is referring to the ages **from eighteen until twenty-four.** The dispute concerning the correct age for marriage and the dispute about educating a child are the same, as while a father still has a large measure of influence over his son, he must both teach him and find him a wife.

A second opinion is recorded: *ve-amre lei mi-tamne-isrei ad esrim ve-arba* — from eighteen to twenty-four. The pressure apparently should not start before sixteen or eighteen because the son would be too young, and should not continue beyond twenty-two or twenty-four because past a certain point it becomes counterproductive — the son stops listening and the relationship deteriorates. This is framed as a *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemiah, tied to the verse *chanoch la-na'ar al pi darko* — train the child according to his path. *Rashi* notes that while *chanoch* suggests a general sense of *chinuch* and upbringing, the ages given make clear that marriage is the primary subject. And indeed the next topic in the *mishnah* is a father's obligation to marry off his son.

## Father's Obligation

### How Far Teach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד הֵיכָן חַיָּיב אָדָם לְלַמֵּד אֶת בְּנוֹ תּוֹרָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן זְבוּלוּן בֶּן דָּן, שֶׁלִּימְּדוֹ אֲבִי אָבִיו מִקְרָא וּמִשְׁנָה וְתַלְמוּד הֲלָכוֹת וְאַגָּדוֹת. מֵיתִיבִי: לִמְּדוֹ מִקְרָא – אֵין מְלַמְּדוֹ מִשְׁנָה, וְאָמַר רָבָא: מִקְרָא – זוֹ תּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of a father's obligation to teach his son Torah. **To what** extent **is a person obligated to teach his son Torah? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** One should emulate the education of, **for example, Zevulun ben Dan,** a contemporary of Shmuel, **whose father's father taught him Bible, Mishna, Talmud, *halakhot*, and *aggadot*. The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If a father **taught** his son **Bible,** he is **not** required to **teach him Mishna. And Rava said** in explanation of this *baraita*: **Bible is** the **Torah,** not the Prophets or Writings, i.e., he is not required to teach him anything else, including Mishna.

Returning now to *talmud Torah* proper, the *gemara* asks: *ad hachan chayav adam le-lammed et beno Torah* — until what point is a person obligated to teach his son *Torah*? The question concerns not only the content of the education but also its generational extent.

### Zevulun Example

*Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Shmuel*: like *Zevulun ben Dan* — a person known at that time — whose grandfather (*avi aviv*) had taught him *mikra u-mishnah* — *Tanakh* and *mishnayot* — *ve-Talmud, halachos ve-aggados*, the entire range of learning. Everything. So on first reading, the obligation would seem to include teaching one's son the full breadth of *Torah*: the text of the written Torah, the received traditions, the analytical *shakla ve-tarya* of the *Talmud*, the *halachic* rulings, and the *aggadic* material.

### Mikra Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּזְבוּלוּן בֶּן דָּן, וְלֹא כִּזְבוּלוּן בֶּן דָּן. כִּזְבוּלוּן בֶּן דָּן – שֶׁלִּמְּדוֹ אֲבִי אָבִיו, וְלֹא כִּזְבוּלוּן בֶּן דָּן, דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם מִקְרָא מִשְׁנָה וְתַלְמוּד הֲלָכוֹת וְאַגָּדוֹת, וְאִילּוּ הָכָא – מִקְרָא לְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Shmuel's statement should be understood as follows: One should teach his son **like Zevulun ben Dan was taught** in certain aspects, **but not like Zevulun ben Dan** in other respects. One should teach his son **like Zevulun ben Dan** in **that his father's father taught him; but not like Zevulun ben Dan, as there** he was taught **Bible, Mishna, Talmud, *halakhot*, and *aggadot*, while here,** in this *baraita*, one is required to teach his son **Bible alone.**

The *gemara* immediately raises an objection from a *beraita*: *limmdo mikra ein melammdo mishnah* — once you have taught him *Tanakh*, you have no obligation to teach him even *Mishnah*. This is a significant *chiddush*. Your entire parental educational obligation is fulfilled with *mikra* alone. You are not even required to provide basic rabbinic literacy. The verse *v'shinnantam l'vanecha v'dibarta bam* refers, after all, to *mikra* — that is what existed when the verse was given.

### Rava's View

*Rava* then sharpens this further: *mikra* here means only the *Torah* — the Five Books — not even *Nevi'im u-Ketuvim*. That is the outer limit of the parental obligation. The *gemara* then reconciles the *Zevulun ben Dan* example: the point of invoking *Zevulun ben Dan* was not to define the *content* of education, but to establish its *generational* scope — specifically, that the obligation extends even to grandchildren, as we will see. The reading is thus *ke-Zevulun ben Dan v'lo ke-Zevulun ben Dan*: like *Zevulun ben Dan* in the sense that the obligation extends to grandchildren, but not like him in that the content is not everything — only *mikra*.

This limitation to *mikra* is genuinely surprising. The natural response is that what is being defined here is the parental obligation of primary education, which is distinct from and far narrower than a person's independent lifelong obligation of *talmud Torah*. When the *gemara* earlier said that if the father didn't teach, the son must teach himself, it appears to be referring to this foundational primary education. A person's own ongoing obligation to learn is clearly understood as far more expansive — *vehagita bo yomam valailah*, after all — even if the precise contours of that obligation remain to be worked out fully. It is also worth noting, as one participant observed, that *mikra* may have been the only content that was realistically accessible in written form, making it the only component of education every father could provide regardless of his own level of learning.

## Grandsons

### Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲבִי אָבִיו מִי מִיחַיַּיב? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְלִמַּדְתֶּם אֹתָם אֶת בְּנֵיכֶם״ – וְלֹא בְּנֵי בְּנֵיכֶם. וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ וּלִבְנֵי בָנֶיךָ״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: שֶׁכׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד אֶת בְּנוֹ תּוֹרָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ לִמְּדוֹ לוֹ וּלִבְנוֹ וּלְבֶן בְּנוֹ, עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is one's father's father obligated** to teach him Torah? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*, that the verse: **"And you shall teach them to your sons"** (Deuteronomy 11:19), indicates: **But not your sons' sons? And how do I realize,** i.e., understand, the meaning of the verse: **"But make them known to your sons and to your sons' sons"** (Deuteronomy 4:9)? This serves **to say to you** that **whoever teaches his son Torah, the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he taught him, and his son, and his son's son, until the end of all generations.**

The *gemara* now addresses whether the obligation to teach extends to grandchildren. A *beraita* argues that it does not: *ve-limadetem osam et beneichem* — you shall teach *your sons*, *ve-lo benei beneichem* — not your grandsons. On this reading, the obligation is one generation only.

### Generations

But then the *gemara* asks: how do we account for the verse *ve-hodastem l'vanecha u-le-venei vanecha* — you shall inform your sons and your sons' sons? One answer given is that the second verse is not a separate obligation but an expression of consequence: if you teach your son *Torah*, it is as though you have taught all future generations, because the chain of transmission continues. *Kol ha-melamed et beno Torah, ma'aleh alav ha-kasuv ke-ilu limdo lo u-le-veno u-le-ven beno ad sof kol ha-doros* — teaching your son effectively plants *Torah* in an unbroken chain unto all generations. This is a powerful idea: education has a compounding effect through time.

The *gemara* then resolves the tension by appealing to another *tanna* who reads the two verses differently: the verse *ve-limadetem* — teaching your sons — tells us your sons are obligated; the verse *ve-hodastem* — informing sons and sons' sons — extends the obligation to grandsons as well. The exclusion of daughters is derived separately: *beneichem ve-lo benoteichem*.

### Grandson = Sinai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד אֶת בֶּן בְּנוֹ תּוֹרָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ קִבְּלָהּ מֵהַר סִינַי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ וְלִבְנֵי בָנֶיךָ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״יוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַדְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹקֶיךָ בְּחֹרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Anyone who teaches his son's son Torah, the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he received it from Mount Sinai, as it is stated: "But make them known to your sons and to your sons' sons," and juxtaposed to it** is the phrase in the verse: **"The day when you stood before the Lord your God in Horeb"** (Deuteronomy 4:10), as Horeb is Mount Sinai.

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* then adds a remarkable statement: *kol ha-melamed et ben beno Torah, ma'aleh alav ha-kasuv ke-ilu kiblah mi-Har Sinai* — whoever teaches his grandson *Torah*, the verse treats him as though he received it directly from *Har Sinai*. The prooftext: *ve-hodastem l'vanecha u-le-venei vanecha* is placed immediately adjacent to *yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev* — the day you stood before God at *Sinai*.

The grammatical referent of "he received it from *Har Sinai*" is ambiguous — it could refer to the grandson, who by receiving *Torah* from his grandfather is connected to that unbroken chain stretching back to *Sinai*, or it could refer to the grandfather, who by teaching his grandchildren demonstrates that he understands Torah transmission as a multigenerational obligation rooted in *Sinai* itself. Teaching a grandchild does something that teaching a child does not: it shows that the passing down of *Torah* is not merely an individual parental act but a chain of responsibility that reaches back through history. The grandfather who teaches his grandchild places himself within that chain in a uniquely vivid way.

## Chiya Story

### Handkerchief

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי דְּשָׁדֵי דִּיסְנָא אַרֵישֵׁיהּ וְקָא מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ לְיָנוֹקָא לְבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי כּוּלֵּי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי זוּטַר מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ״ וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״יוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַדְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹקֶיךָ בְּחֹרֵב״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: Once **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba encountered Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** and saw **that he had placed** an inexpensive **covering on his head and brought** his **child to the synagogue** to study. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: What** is the reason for **all this** fuss, as you are in such a hurry that you do not have time to dress yourself properly? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said to him: Is it insignificant, that which is written: "But make them known to your sons," and juxtaposed to it** is the phrase in the verse that states: **"The day when you stood before the Lord your God in Horeb"?**

The *gemara* relates the following story. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* came upon *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — a sage of *Eretz Yisrael* — who had thrown a simple cloth (*disna*) over his head, not bothering to wear a proper *sudra*, and was hurrying to bring his young child to *beis knesses* — the school-house. People asked him: *mai kulei hai?* What is all this? Why did you rush out without properly dressing?

### Ve-Hodastem

He replied: *mi zutar mai deksiv — ve-hodastem l'vanecha*, immediately followed by *yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev?* Is it a small thing what the Torah says — that teaching your children is juxtaposed with the day you stood at *Sinai*? In other words: taking a child to school is not a trivial domestic errand. It is an act that participates in the grand tradition of *Torah* received at *Sinai* and transmitted through the generations. To delay it, or to depute it to someone else, or to pause to dress properly first — none of that is appropriate when the obligation is this weighty.

There is perhaps a subtle irony here as well. Recall that the *sudra* was the garment that identified a man as both a *talmid chacham* and a married man — its absence in the story of *Rav Hamnuna* was the marker of his unmarried state. Here, *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — himself clearly learned and presumably married — prioritizes the physical act of getting his child to school over the formality of proper dress. The contrast may be intentional: the outward markers of status are subordinated to the actual performance of parental educational obligation, which is itself the mark of genuine *talmud Torah* in *Eretz Yisrael*.

## Personal Practice

### Before Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לָא טָעֵים אוּמְצָא עַד דְּמַקְרֵי לְיָנוֹקָא וּמוֹסְפֵיהּ. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא לָא טָעֵים אוּמְצָא עַד דְּמַיְיתֵי לְיָנוֹקָא לְבֵית מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **From this** moment **onward, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba would not taste meat [*umtza*],** meaning he would not eat breakfast, **before he had read to** his **child and added to** the child's studies from the day before. Similarly, **Rabba bar Rav Huna would not taste meat before he had brought** his **child to the study hall.**

*Rav Safra* reports in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya*: from that point on, *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* would not taste even a bit of food in the morning until he had reviewed with a child the *pesukim* they had learned the previous day and added a new *pasuk* — *umosefei*. That is, his practice was to first fulfill the obligation of teaching a child *Torah* before attending to his own eating. Whether this refers specifically to his own child or to any child under his care is not entirely precise from the context, but the thrust is clear: the obligation of *talmud Torah* with children takes priority over one's personal needs and forms the first act of the day.

### Beis Midrash

Similarly, *Rabba bar Rav Huna* would not eat anything in the morning until he had brought the child to the *beis medrash*. The personal practice of these sages illustrates how seriously they understood the parental obligation of *talmud Torah* — not as a background duty but as the day's first priority.

## Shinnantam

### Three Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם״, אֶלָּא ״וְשִׁלַּשְׁתָּם״. לְעוֹלָם יְשַׁלֵּשׁ אָדָם שְׁנוֹתָיו: שְׁלִישׁ בַּמִּקְרָא, שְׁלִישׁ בַּמִּשְׁנָה, שְׁלִישׁ בַּתַּלְמוּד. מִי יוֹדֵעַ כַּמָּה חָיֵי? לָא צְרִיכָא לְיוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Safra says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And you shall teach them diligently [*veshinnantam*] to your sons"** (Deuteronomy 6:7)? **Do not read** this as **"*veshinnantam*,"** with the root *shin*, *nun*, *nun*, which indicates a repetition. **Rather,** read it as ***veshillashtam*,** with the root *shin*, *lamed*, *shin*, related to the word three, *shalosh*. This means that one must study, review, and study again, thereby dividing one's studies into three parts. In light of this statement, the Sages said that **a person should always divide his years into three** parts, as follows: **A third for Bible, a third for Mishna, and a third for Talmud.** The Gemara asks: How can a person divide his life this way? **Who knows the length of his life,** so that he can calculate how long a third will be? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary for** one's **days,** i.e., one should divide each day of his life in this manner.

*Rav Safra* also transmits a teaching in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya* based on the verse *v'shinnantam l'vanecha*: *al tikrei v'shinnantam ela v'shillashtam* — do not read it as *shinnantam* but as *shillashtam* — divide into three. The double *nun* in *shinnantam* signals not merely repetition but a tripling. From this: *le'olam yeshalesh adam shenotav* — a person should divide his years, or as the *gemara* clarifies, his days, into three: *shlish be-mikra, shlish be-mishnah, u-shlish be-Talmud* — one third to *Tanakh*, one third to *Mishnah*, and one third to *gemara*.

### Daily Division

The *gemara* objects: how can one divide one's *years* into thirds when one does not know how long one will live? The answer: *lo tzricha leyomei* — one should divide one's *days*, not one's lifetime. *Rashi* understands this as dividing the days of the week: two days to *mikra*, two days to *Mishnah*, two days to *gemara*, with *Shabbos* perhaps for *chazara* or rest.

*Tosafot* challenges *Rashi*'s reading: if dividing by weeks, you still face the same problem — what if you die in the middle of a week, before having completed all three sections? *Tosafot* therefore concludes that the division must be within each day itself: every single day, one third to each. This is the basis for the practice, codified by Rav Amram Gaon and preserved in some communities today, of reciting a passage of *mikra*, *Mishnah*, and *gemara* each morning before *pesukei dezimra* — a brief but daily *kiyum* of this tripartite obligation.

*Tosafot* then raises a further difficulty in the name of *Rabbeinu Tam*: in practice, the overwhelming focus of Jewish learning at every stage has been *gemara*. How is it possible to justify spending a full third of one's learning time on *mikra* and another third on *Mishnah*? *Rabbeinu Tam* answers by appeal to a principle stated in *Sanhedrin*: the Babylonian *Talmud* is called *belulah* — mixed — because it is interwoven with *Tanakh*, *Mishnah*, and *gemara* together. When you learn *Bavli*, you encounter *mishnayot*, *beraitot*, and occasional citations of *pesukim*. This admixture is what makes the *Bavli* a vehicle for satisfying all three components simultaneously.

### Rambam

The *Rambam* in *Hilchos Talmud Torah*, chapter 1, *halacha* 11, takes the *gemara*'s ruling very literally. He rules that one is obligated to divide one's study time into thirds — *shlish baTorah shebikhtav*, *shlish baTorah shebe'al peh*, and a third for independent analysis: *yavin veyaskil acharit davar mereshito* — understanding the conclusions from their premises, drawing inferences, applying the principles through which *Torah* is expounded. This last third is what is called *gemara*. The *Rambam* illustrates: if a person works at a trade three hours a day, he has nine hours available for learning. Of those nine, three go to *Torah shebikhtav*, three to *Torah sheba'al peh*, and three to analysis.

But the *Rambam* then substantially modifies this ruling: *bameh devarim amurim betechilat talmudo shel adam* — this applies at the beginning of a person's learning, when he is still building his foundational knowledge and skills. Once he has grown in *chochmah* and has mastered the written *Torah* and the received traditions, *yikra be'ittim mezumanim Torah shebikhtav vedivrei hashemuah kedei shelo yishkach* — he should review *Torah shebikhtav* and *Torah sheba'al peh* occasionally so as not to forget them — *v'yifneh kol yamav le-Gemara bilvad* — but all of his real learning should be devoted to *gemara*. This ultimately brings the *Rambam* very close to *Tosafot*'s conclusion: the mature scholar's obligation is primarily the study of *gemara*, with the tripartite division applying to the formative years of education. The irony, noted in the shiur, is that the *Rambam* himself famously described his own exhausting daily schedule as a court physician in the letter he wrote later in life — a schedule that left him almost no time for Torah study beyond an hour on *Shabbos*.

## Sofrim

### Why Sofrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרְאוּ רִאשׁוֹנִים ״סוֹפְרִים״, שֶׁהָיוּ סוֹפְרִים כׇּל הָאוֹתִיּוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: וָא״ו דְּ״גָחוֹן״– חֶצְיָין שֶׁל אוֹתִיּוֹת שֶׁל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה. ״דָּרֹשׁ דָּרַשׁ״ – חֶצְיָין שֶׁל תֵּיבוֹת. ״וְהִתְגַּלָּח״ – שֶׁל פְּסוּקִים. ״יְכַרְסְמֶנָּה חֲזִיר מִיָּעַר״ – עַיִ״ן דְּיַעַר חֶצְיָין שֶׁל תְּהִלִּים. ״וְהוּא רַחוּם יְכַפֵּר עָוֹן״ – חֶצְיוֹ דִּפְסוּקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** because they devoted so much time to the Bible, the **first** Sages **were called: Those who count [*soferim*],** because **they would count all the letters in the Torah, as they would say** that the letter ***vav*** in the word **"belly [*gaḥon*]"** (Leviticus 11:42) **is the midpoint of the letters in a Torah scroll.** The words: **"Diligently inquired [*darosh darash*]"** (Leviticus 10:16), **are the midpoint of the words** in a Torah scroll. And the verse that begins with: **"Then he shall be shaven"** (Leviticus 13:33), is the midpoint **of** the **verses.** Similarly, in the expression: **"The boar out of the wood [*miya'ar*] ravages it"** (Psalms 80:14), **the *ayin* in** the word wood **[*ya'ar*]** is the **midpoint of Psalms,** with regard to its number of letters. The verse: **"But He, being full of compassion, forgives iniquity"** (Psalms 78:38), **is the midpoint** of **verses** in the book of Psalms.

The *gemara* transitions with a seemingly abrupt *lefikach* — therefore — which *Rashi* acknowledges is a new independent topic. The early sages were called *sofrim* — *shehayu sofrim kol otiyot shebaTorah* — because they would count all of the letters in the *Torah*.

### Halfway Points

The *gemara* then lists several calculations attributed to these *sofrim*. The large *vav* in the word *gachon* — in the verse about creatures that go on their belly in *Vayikra* — is said to be the midpoint of the letters of the *sefer Torah*. The phrase *darosh darash* — which we just recently encountered in *Shemini* — is said to be the midpoint of the words of the *Torah*. The word *vehitgalach* is said to mark the midpoint of the *pesukim*. Turning to *Tehillim*: the *ayin* of *ya'ar* in the verse *yecharsmena chazir miyaar* is said to be the midpoint of the letters of *Tehillim*; the verse *vehu rachum yechapeir avon* marks the midpoint of its *pesukim*.

### Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: וָא״ו ״דְּגָחוֹן״, מֵהַאי גִּיסָא אוֹ מֵהַאי גִּיסָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נֵיתֵי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְנִימְנִינְהוּ, מִי לָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וּמְנָאוּם? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִינְהוּ בְּקִיאִי בַּחֲסֵירוֹת וִיתֵרוֹת, אֲנַן לָא בְּקִיאִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises a dilemma:** Does the ***vav* of** the word **"belly [*gaḥon*]"** belong **to this side or to this side?** Is it part of the first or second half of the Torah? The Sages **said to him: Let us bring a Torah scroll and count** the letters. **Didn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say** with regard to a different issue: **They did not move from there until they brought a Torah scroll and counted** the letters? Therefore we can do the same. Rav Yosef **said to them: They** were **experts** in the **deficient and plene** forms of words and therefore could count the letters precisely. **We are not experts** in this regard, and therefore we would be unable to resolve the question even if we were to count the letters.

*Rav Yosef* raises a question about the *vav* of *gachon*: is it on this side or that side of the midpoint — meaning, with an odd total number of letters, is the *vav* itself the exact middle, or does it fall just before or just after? His interlocutor suggests: let us bring a *sefer Torah* and count. *Rav Yosef* responds by citing another case where this was actually done — a case mentioned by *Rabbah bar bar Chanah* — suggesting it is at least possible to undertake such a count.

### Limited Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וְהִתְגַּלָּח״, מֵהַאי גִּיסָא אוֹ מֵהַאי גִּיסָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: פְּסוּקֵי מִיהָא לַיְתוֹ לִימְנִינְהוּ! בִּפְסוּקֵי נָמֵי לָא בְּקִיאִינַן, דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב אַחָא בַּר אַדָּא, אָמַר: בְּמַעְרְבָא פָּסְקִי לֵיהּ לְהַאי קְרָא לִתְלָתָא פְּסוּקֵי: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי בָּא אֵלֶיךָ בְּעַב הֶעָנָן״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Yosef raises a dilemma:** Does the midpoint of the verses in the Torah, which is **"then he shall be shaven,"** belong **to this side or to this side? Abaye said to him:** Even if we cannot count the letters, **we can at least bring** a Torah scroll **to count the verses.** Rav Yosef explained: **We are not experts about verses either, as when Rav Aḥa bar Adda came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said: In the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **they divide this** following **verse into three** separate **verses: "And the Lord said to Moses, behold I come to you in a thick cloud,** that the people may hear when I speak with you, and may also believe you forever; And Moses told the words of the people to the Lord" (Exodus 19:9). Perhaps there are other verses that we do not know how to divide properly.

But then the *gemara* deflates both the historical claims and the proposal: *inhu bki'ei b'chaseros v'yesiros, anan lo bki'inan* — the earlier generations had precise knowledge of whether words were written *malei* or *chaser* — fully spelled or defective — and could therefore count letters exactly. We, by the time of the *Amoraim*, no longer possess that precision. Our *sifrei Torah* may not be exactly correct in every *chaser* and *malei*, and therefore we cannot validate or replicate these counts. This admission by the *gemara* is significant: it explicitly concedes that even in the *Amoraic* period, the exact orthography of the *Torah* text was not perfectly known. The *baalei hamesorah*, the Masoretes — most famously the *Ben Asher* family — only completed the systematic cataloging of every letter, vowel, and cantillation mark around the fifth or sixth century, well after this period, in order to fix the text precisely. This uncertainty also dispenses with any naive confidence in Bible codes or letter-count-based exegesis: the *gemara* itself acknowledges that the precise letter count was not reliably known.

## Counting Pesukim

### Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמֵשֶׁת אֲלָפִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת וּשְׁמוֹנִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה פְּסוּקִים הָווּ פְּסוּקֵי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, יָתֵר עָלָיו תְּהִלִּים שְׁמוֹנָה, חָסֵר מִמֶּנּוּ דִּבְרֵי הַיָּמִים שְׁמוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Five thousand eight hundred and eighty-eight verses are the verses in a Torah scroll. Psalms has eight more** verses **than that,** and **Chronicles** has **eight fewer** verses **than that.**

The *gemara* similarly questions the count of *pesukim* in the *sefer Torah*. *Rav Yosef* asks about *vehitgalach* — the alleged midpoint of *pesukim* — whether it falls on this or that side of the divide. *Abaye* responds that for *pesukim*, unlike letters, we ought to be able to count: let us just do it. The *gemara*'s response is: *bpsukei nami lo bki'inan* — we are also not expert in counting *pesukim*.

A *beraita* records: the *sefer Torah* contains 5,888 *pesukim* — whereas our current count comes to approximately 5,845, a discrepancy of about forty. *Tehillim* has eight more *pesukim* than the *Torah*, and *Divrei HaYamim* has eight fewer.

### Regional Variations

The reason for uncertainty about *pesukim* is then made clear: *Rav Acha bar Adda* reported that in *Eretz Yisrael*, what we read as a single *pasuk* was divided into three separate *pesukim*. The example given is the verse *vayomer Hashem el Moshe hinei anochi ba eilecha be'av he'anan* — which could be divided at *he'anan*, then at *ba'avur yishma ha'am b'dibri imcha v'gam becha ya'aminu l'olam*, and finally at *vayageid Moshe et divrei ha'am el Hashem*. Different regional traditions of where *pasuk* divisions fall mean that even the count of *pesukim* cannot be determined with certainty. The divergence between Babylonian and *Eretz Yisrael* manuscripts was apparently significant enough to make such counts unreliable.

## Warrior Imagery

### Sharp Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מְחוּדָּדִים בְּפִיךְ, שֶׁאִם יִשְׁאַל לְךָ אָדָם דָּבָר אַל תְּגַמְגֵּם וְתֹאמַר לוֹ, אֶלָּא אֱמוֹר לוֹ מִיָּד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֹר לַחׇכְמָה אֲחֹתִי אָתְּ״ וְאוֹמֵר: ״קׇשְׁרֵם עַל אֶצְבְּעֹתֶיךָ כׇּתְבֵם עַל לוּחַ לִבֶּךָ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כְּחִצִּים בְּיַד גִּבּוֹר כֵּן בְּנֵי הַנְּעוּרִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״חִצֵּי גִבּוֹר שְׁנוּנִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״חִצֶּיךָ שְׁנוּנִים עַמִּים תַּחְתֶּיךָ יִפְּלוּ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אַשְׁרֵי הַגֶּבֶר אֲשֶׁר מִלֵּא אֶת אַשְׁפָּתוֹ מֵהֶם לֹא יֵבֹשׁוּ כִּי יְדַבְּרוּ אֶת אוֹיְבִים בַּשָּׁעַר״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"And you shall teach them diligently [*veshinnantam*]"** (Deuteronomy 6:7). The root *shin*, *nun*, *nun*, of *veshinnantam* should be understood as meaning sharp, i.e., **that matters of Torah should be sharp** and clear **in your mouth,** so **that if a person asks you something, do not stutter** in uncertainty **and say** an uncertain response **to him. Rather, answer him immediately, as it is stated:** **"Say to wisdom: You are my sister,** and call understanding your kinswoman" (Proverbs 7:4), which indicates that one should be as knowledgeable in the Torah as in the identity of his sister. **And it states: "Bind them upon your fingers, you shall write them upon the tablet of your heart"** (Proverbs 7:3). **And it states: "As arrows in the hand of a mighty man, so are the children of one's youth"** (Psalms 127:4). **And it states: "Sharp arrows of the mighty"** (Psalms 120:4). **And it states: "Your arrows are sharp, the peoples fall under you"** (Psalms 45:6). **And it states: "Happy is the man who has his quiver full of them; they shall not be put to shame when they speak with their enemies in the gate"** (Psalms 127:5).

The *gemara* returns to the verse *v'shinnantam l'vanecha* to derive a different teaching. *Tanu rabbanan*: *v'shinnantam l'vanecha* — the word *shinnantam* connotes sharpness, from *shinnun*, sharp. The teaching: *she-yihyu divrei Torah mechuddadim b'ficha* — the words of *Torah* should be sharp in your mouth. If someone asks you something, you should not hesitate or stumble — *al tegagaym ve-tomar lo*, do not mumble — but answer immediately, with fluency and confidence.

### Like Sister

The *gemara* cites the verse *emor la-chochmah achoti at* — say to wisdom: you are my sister. Your knowledge of *Torah* should be as intimate and immediate as your knowledge of your own sister — or, as an alternative *drasha*, as certain as the prohibition of your sister is to you. Either way, the image is of knowledge so internalized that it admits no hesitation.

### Arrows

A series of verses is then brought to expand on the theme of sharpness. *Koshrem al etzbe'otecha katvem al lu'ach libbecha* — bind them to your fingers, inscribe them on the chambers of your heart. *Torah* should be fully internalized, in the blood. Then: *ke-chitzim be-yad gibbor ken bnei ha-ne'urim* — like arrows in the hand of a warrior, so are the children of one's youth. *Rashi* interprets this as referring to one's learning — the *Torah* one acquires is one's "children," and what one learns while young is particularly sharp and durable. *Ve-omer chitzei gibbor shenunim* — the arrows of the warrior are sharp. *Ve-omer chitzeycha shenunim ammim tachteycha yippolu* — your arrows are sharp, nations fall beneath you. *Ve-omer ashrei ha-gever asher millei et aspato me-hem* — praised is the man who fills his quiver with these arrows, who fills himself with *Torah* knowledge that is sharp and ready.

### Enemies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֶת אוֹיְבִים בַּשָּׁעַר״? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אֲפִילּוּ הָאָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה בַּשָּׁעַר אֶחָד – נַעֲשִׂים אוֹיְבִים זֶה אֶת זֶה. וְאֵינָם זָזִים מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁנַּעֲשִׂים אוֹהֲבִים זֶה אֶת זֶה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת וָהֵב בְּסוּפָה״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּסוּפָה״ אֶלָּא ״בְּסוֹפָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"enemies in the gate"** with regard to Torah study? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: Even a father and his son,** or **a rabbi and his student, who are engaged in Torah** together **in one gate become enemies with each other** due to the intensity of their studies. **But they do not leave there until they love each other, as it is stated** in the verse discussing the places the Jewish people engaged in battle in the wilderness: "Therefore it is said in the book of the wars of the Lord, **Vahev in Suphah [*beSufa*],** and the valleys of Arnon" (Numbers 21:14). The word "*vahev*" is interpreted as related to the word for love, *ahava*. Additionally, **do not read** this as **"in Suphah [*beSufa*]"; rather,** read it as **"at its end [*besofa*],"** i.e., at the conclusion of their dispute they are beloved to each other.

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* answers: *afillu ha-av u-vno, ha-rav ve-talmido* — even father and son, even a master and his student, when they are *oskim ba-Torah be-sha'ar echad* — engaged in *Torah* in the same space — *na'asim oyvim zeh la-zeh*. They become enemies to one another: they argue, they contest, they push back. Anyone who has sat in a *beis medrash* during a heated *chavrusa* session recognizes this dynamic. The formality of deference to a father or teacher does not apply within the walls of *Torah* debate; there, one argues for the truth without deference.

### Love

And yet — *v'aynam zazim misham ad shenasim ohavim zeh la-zeh* — they do not leave that encounter until they have become lovers of one another. The argument is entirely *lishma* — for the sake of truth — and so when it is over, there is no residual bitterness, only affection. The prooftext: *et vahev b'sufah* — from the *sefer milchamot Hashem*, the Book of the Wars of God. *Al tikrei b'sufah ela b'sofah* — do not read it as "in a whirlwind" but as "at its end" — *vahev* is related to *ahavah*, love. At the end of the wars of *Torah*, love follows.

Several dimensions of this passage are worth remarking upon. First, there is the martial, warrior imagery attached throughout to *talmud Torah* — arrows, quivers, warriors, enemies. As scholars of rabbinic literature have observed, this seems to be a deliberate refashioning of an activity that might otherwise be coded as passive or non-masculine into something heroic and vigorous. The rabbis mobilize an entire armory of imagery to invest *Torah* study with aggression, strength, and martial valor. Second, notice how far we have traveled from the initial framing: the father's obligation to provide his son a *mikra* education, which was presented as a relatively limited parental duty, gives way, in these *aggadic* passages, to a far more totalizing vision — knowledge that must be perfectly sharp, arguments that are like arrows, devotion so complete that one becomes a warrior for *Torah*. The *gemara* does not fully reconcile these different framings, and how the limited parental obligation relates to the vast personal obligation remains an open question.

## Torah Remedy

### Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשַׂמְתֶּם״ – סַם תָּם, נִמְשְׁלָה תּוֹרָה כְּסַם חַיִּים. מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁהִכָּה אֶת בְּנוֹ מַכָּה גְּדוֹלָה וְהִנִּיחַ לוֹ רְטִיָּה עַל מַכָּתוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָרְטִיָּה זוֹ עַל מַכָּתְךָ אֱכוֹל מַה שֶּׁהֲנָאָתֶךָ, וּשְׁתֵה מַה שֶּׁהֲנָאָתֶךָ, וּרְחוֹץ בֵּין בְּחַמִּין בֵּין בְּצוֹנֵן וְאֵין אַתָּה מִתְיָירֵא. וְאִם אַתָּה מַעֲבִירָהּ הֲרֵי הִיא מַעֲלָה נוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: "And you shall place [*vesamtem*]** these words of Mine in your hearts" (Deuteronomy 11:18). Read this as though it stated ***sam tam*, a perfect elixir. The Torah is compared to an elixir of life.** There is **a parable that** illustrates this: **A person hit his son with a strong blow and placed a bandage on his wound. And he said to him: My son, as long as this bandage is on your wound** and is healing you, **eat what you enjoy and drink what you enjoy, and bathe in either hot water or cold water, and you do not need to be afraid,** as it will heal your wound. **But if you take it off,** the wound **will become gangrenous.**

The *gemara* closes with an *aggadic* passage of striking beauty and psychological acuity. The verse says *v'samtem et divrei eileh asher anochi m'tzavcha hayom* — the word *v'samtem* is read as *sam tam*, a complete remedy, a perfect antidote. *Nimshla Torah k'sam chayim* — the *Torah* is compared to a potion of life. A *mashal*: a man struck his son a great blow and then placed a bandage (*rtiya*) on the wound. He said to him: my son, as long as this bandage is on your wound, you may eat and drink whatever you wish, bathe in hot or cold water, and you need not fear any ill effect — this bandage will protect you against all of it. But if you remove the bandage, *harei hi ma'aleh numi* — the wound will fester and become infected.

### Tavlin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּךְ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אָמַר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: בָּנַי, בָּרָאתִי יֵצֶר הָרָע וּבָרָאתִי לוֹ תּוֹרָה תַּבְלִין. וְאִם אַתֶּם עוֹסְקִים בַּתּוֹרָה – אֵין אַתֶּם נִמְסָרִים בְּיָדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלוֹא אִם תֵּיטִיב שְׂאֵת״,
>
> **Translation:** **So** too **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Israel: My children, I created an evil inclination,** which is the wound, **and I created Torah as its antidote. If you are engaged in Torah** study **you will not be given over into the hand** of the evil inclination, **as it is stated: "If you do well, shall it not be lifted up?"** (Genesis 4:7). One who engages in Torah study lifts himself above the evil inclination.

God speaks to Israel in the same terms: *bni, barati yetzer hara* — I created the *yetzer hara*, which is the blow. *U-varati lo Torah tavlin* — but I also created the *Torah* as its *tavlin*, its antidote, its spice and remedy.

### Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵין אַתֶּם עוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה – אַתֶּם נִמְסָרִים בְּיָדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַפֶּתַח חַטָּאת רֹבֵץ״, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁכׇּל מַשָּׂאוֹ וּמַתָּנוֹ בְּךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶיךָ תְּשׁוּקָתוֹ״. וְאִם אַתָּה רוֹצֶה אַתָּה מוֹשֵׁל בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה תִּמְשׇׁל בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you do not engage in Torah** study, **you are given over to its power, as it is stated: "Sin crouches at the door"** (Genesis 4:7). **Moreover, all** of the evil inclination's **deliberations** will be **concerning you, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"And to you is its desire." And if you wish you shall rule over it, as it is stated** in the conclusion of the verse: **"But you may rule over it"** (Genesis 4:7).

*Ve-im atem oskim ba-Torah einchem nimsarim beyado* — if you are engaged in *Torah*, you will not be delivered into its power. The verse cited: *halo im teitiv se'eit* — if you do good, meaning if you learn *Torah*, you will carry yourself above it, you will conquer the *yetzer hara*. *Rashi* understands *se'eit* as implying elevation — you will be raised above it.

But if one does not engage in *Torah*: *atem nimsarim beyado* — you are delivered into the hands of the *yetzer hara*. *Lapetach chatat rovetz* — it crouches at the door, waiting. *V'lo od ella shekol masa u-matano bach* — and furthermore, its entire attention, its whole enterprise, is directed at you: scheming, working, seeking entry. *Ve'eilecha teshukatoi* — its desire is toward you. And yet — *ve-im ata rotze ata moshel bo, shene'emar ve'ata timshol bo* — if you so choose, you can be master over it.

The *gemara* will continue from this point, taking up the challenge of the *yetzer hara* and *Torah* as its antidote.
