# Shabbat 139 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Judicial Corruption

### Rabbi Yose ben Elisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן אֱלִישָׁע אוֹמֵר: אִם רָאִיתָ דּוֹר שֶׁצָּרוֹת רַבּוֹת בָּאוֹת עָלָיו — צֵא וּבְדוֹק בְּדַיָּינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. שֶׁכׇּל פּוּרְעָנוּת שֶׁבָּאָה לְעוֹלָם לֹא בָּאָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל דַּיָּינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁמְעוּ נָא זֹאת רָאשֵׁי בֵּית יַעֲקֹב וּקְצִינֵי בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל הַמְתַעֲבִים מִשְׁפָּט וְאֵת כׇּל הַיְשָׁרָה יְעַקֵּשׁוּ. בּוֹנֶה צִיּוֹן בְּדָמִים וִירוּשָׁלִַים בְּעַוְלָה. רָאשֶׁיהָ בְּשׁוֹחַד יִשְׁפּוֹטוּ וְכֹהֲנֶיהָ בִּמְחִיר יוֹרוּ וּנְבִיאֶיהָ בְּכֶסֶף יִקְסוֹמוּ וְעַל ה׳ יִשָּׁעֵנוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei ben Elisha says: If you see a generation that many troubles are befalling it, go and examine the judges of Israel.** Perhaps their sins are the cause, **as any calamity that comes to the world comes due to the judges of Israel** acting corruptly, **as it is stated: "Please hear this, heads of the house of Jacob, and officers of the house of Israel, who abhor justice and pervert all equity, who build up Zion with blood, and Jerusalem with iniquity. Their heads they judge for bribes, and their priests teach for hire, and their prophets divine for money; yet they lean upon the Lord,** saying: Is not the Lord in our midst? No evil shall befall us" (Micah 3:9–11).

The daf opens in the middle of an aggadic digression. A *baraita* is cited: *tanya, Rabbi Yose ben Elisha omer, im ra'ita dor she'tzarot rabot ba'ot alav, tze'i u'vdok b'dayanei Yisrael* — if you see a generation upon which many afflictions come, go and examine the judges of Israel. The leaders, the *dayanim*, bear the primary responsibility. Who exactly Rabbi Yose ben Elisha was is difficult to establish; he does not appear in standard biographical sources, and his statement is largely an elaboration and focusing of themes already present in the *pesukim* he cites. What he contributes is the insistence on a single, decisive point of accountability.

### Afflictions from Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְשָׁעִים הֵן, אֶלָּא שֶׁתָּלוּ בִּטְחוֹנָם בְּמִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם. לְפִיכָךְ, מֵבִיא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עֲלֵיהֶן שָׁלֹשׁ פּוּרְעָנִיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד שָׁלֹשׁ עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבְּיָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָכֵן בִּגְלַלְכֶם צִיּוֹן שָׂדֶה תֵחָרֵשׁ וִירוּשָׁלִַם עִיִּין תִּהְיֶה וְהַר הַבַּיִת לְבָמוֹת יָעַר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **They are wicked, but they placed their trust in the One Who spoke and the world came into being,** the Almighty. **Therefore, the Holy One, Blessed be He, brings upon them three calamities corresponding to the three transgressions** for which **they are responsible,** as **it is stated** in the following verse: **"Therefore, because of you, Zion shall be plowed as a field, and Jerusalem shall become heaps, and the Temple Mount as the high places of a forest"** (Micah 3:12).

The principle is stated starkly: *shekol puraniyot she'ba'ah l'olam lo ba'ah ela bishvil dayanei Yisrael* — all afflictions that come into the world come only because of the judges of Israel. The supporting verse is from Micah: *shimu na zot rashei beit yaakov u'ktzinei beit yisrael hamita'avim mishpat v'et kol hayeshara ye'akeshuv* — hear this, O heads of the house of Jacob and leaders of the house of Israel, who despise justice and pervert everything that is straight. The verse continues: *boneh tziyon b'damim v'yerushalayim b'avla* — building Zion in blood and Jerusalem in iniquity.

### Three Sins & Punishments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָתוֹ עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ שׁוֹפְטִים וְשׁוֹטְרִים רָעִים מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָשִׁיבָה יָדִי עָלַיִךְ וְאֶצְרוֹף כַּבּוֹר סִיגָיִךְ וְאָסִירָה כׇּל בְּדִילָיִךְ. וְאָשִׁיבָה שׁוֹפְטַיִךְ כְּבָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְיוֹעֲצַיִךְ כְּבַתְּחִלָּה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Holy One, Blessed be He, will not rest His Divine Presence on the Jewish people until evil judges and officers shall be eliminated from the Jewish people, as it is stated: "And I will turn My hand upon you, and I will purge away your dross as with lye, and I will remove all your alloy. And I will restore your judges as at first, and your counselors as at the beginning;** afterward you shall be called the city of righteousness, a faithful city" (Isaiah 1:25–26).

The *Gemara* identifies three distinct categories of sin and a corresponding set of three afflictions. The verse specifies: *rosheha b'shochad yishpotu* — its leaders judge through bribery; *v'chohaneha b'machir yoru* — its priests teach for pay; and *u'nevi'eha b'kesef yichsomu* — its prophets practice divination for money. The *kohanim* here are understood in their capacity as teachers and legal authorities — the forerunners of the rabbinic role — rather than in their Temple function. The phrase *yoru mishpatecha l'yaakov* underlies this reading. Everyone is for sale, and yet the verse concludes *v'al Hashem yisha'enu* — and they rely on God — expressing a kind of religious self-satisfaction that makes the corruption all the more severe. The *Gemara* notes that they were wicked, *ela she'talu bitchonam b'mi she'amar v'hayah ha'olam* — but they placed their trust in the Creator of the world. Whether this slightly mitigates the condemnation or compounds it by adding hypocrisy to corruption is left ambiguous by the *Gemara's* formulation.

Therefore, *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* brings three afflictions corresponding to the three sins — *gimel puraniyot keneged gimel aveirot she'beyadam* — as Rashi identifies them: the judges, the priests, and the prophets. The three afflictions are drawn from the continuation of Micah: *lachem biglalchem tziyon sadeh techaresh* — because of you, Zion will be plowed like a field; *yerushalayim iyin tihyeh* — Jerusalem will become a wasteland; *v'har habayit l'bamot yaar* — and the Temple Mount will be like a wooded height.

### Jerusalem's Restoration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: אֵין יְרוּשָׁלָיִם נִפְדֵּית אֶלָּא בִּצְדָקָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִיּוֹן בְּמִשְׁפָּט תִּפָּדֶה וְשָׁבֶיהָ בִּצְדָקָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Jerusalem will be redeemed only through righteousness, as it is stated: "Zion will be redeemed with justice and those who return to her with righteousness"** (Isaiah 1:27).

The *Gemara* continues: *v'ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu mashrei shechinato al Yisrael ad she'yichlu shoftim v'shotrim ra'im mi'Yisrael* — God will not cause His *Shechinah* to dwell upon Israel until evil judges and officers have ceased. The verse from Isaiah is then cited as the promise of restoration: *v'ashiva yadi alayich v'etzrof kabor sigayich* — I will return My hand upon you and purify you as with lye; *v'ashiva shoftayich k'varishona* — and I will restore your judges as at first. The verse concludes with the promise that Jerusalem will again be called *ir hatzedek* — the city of righteousness, *kirya ne'emana* — the faithful city. The entire arc, destruction and restoration alike, passes through the integrity or corruption of those who dispense justice.

## Redemption via Justice

### Ulla & Redemption

Directly in the wake of the passage about Jerusalem's restoration, the *Gemara* cites Ulla: *ein Yerushalayim nifdet ela bi-tzedakah* — Jerusalem will be redeemed only through *tzedakah*, or through justice. The supporting verse is Isaiah: *tziyon be-mishpat tipadeh v'shaveha bi-tzedakah* — Zion will be redeemed through justice, and those who return to her through righteousness. There is a textual variant in which the subject is *yisrael* rather than *yerushalayim*, and it is likewise somewhat unclear whether the reference is to the return from exile or to the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*. But the core point is that redemption comes through the embodiment and enactment of justice.

### Merit vs. Mercy

This principle connects to the broader and well-known tension in rabbinic thought about the nature of the messianic redemption. A *Gemara* elsewhere cites the verse *be'ita achishena* — I will hasten it in its time — and resolves the apparent contradiction by saying: *lo zakhu — be'ita; zakhu — achishena*. If Israel does not merit redemption it will come in its appointed time; if they merit it, it will be hastened. The question is the degree to which redemption comes through divine compassion versus through earned merit.

### Vilna Gaon's Blessing

This question had a practical liturgical consequence. The Vilna Gaon was *makpid* on account of this *Gemara* that in the third *bracha* of *bensching* one should not recite *boneh ve-rachamav Yerushalayim amen* — emphasizing God's compassion as the vehicle of Jerusalem's rebuilding — because *tziyon be-mishpat tipadeh*: Zion is redeemed through justice, not through mercy alone. The correct formulation, on his view, is therefore *boneh Yerushalayim amen*, without the word *ve-rachamav*. The emphasis of Ulla's statement, as the Gaon understood it, is less about the tension between divine justice and divine mercy and more about the necessity of Israel's own enactment of justice as the precondition for redemption.

## Chain of Corruption

### Rav Pappa's Pyramid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי בָּטְלִי יְהִירֵי — בָּטְלִי אַמְגּוּשֵׁי. אִי בָּטְלִי דַּיָּינֵי — בָּטְלִי גְּזִירְפָּטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: If the arrogant will cease** to exist, **the** Persian **fire priests will cease** to exist as well. **If the** deceitful **judges** will **cease** to exist, **the royal officers [*gazirpatei*]** and taskmasters **will cease** to exist.

The *Gemara* now presents an almost one-to-one correspondence between categories of sin and categories of resulting affliction. Rav Pappa says: *i batli yahiri, batli amgushi* — if the arrogant ones would cease, the *amgushi* would cease. Steinsaltz identifies the *amgushi* as Zoroastrian fire-priests in Persia. The verse cited is *v'etzrof kabor sigayich* — I will cleanse you as with lye of your impurities — where the word *sigayich* is read by Rashi as related to arrogance (*yisgeh*, to be lifted up; cf. also *gas*). The end of the verse, *v'asira kol bedilayich*, yields the next correspondence: the *bedilayich*, those who cause separation between Israel and God, are linked to the *amgushi* who headed a rival religion and periodically pressed Jews into service for their sacred fire. A second correspondence follows: *ibatlei dayanei, ibatlei gezirpati* — if the corrupted judges cease, the *gezirpati* (non-Jewish legal officers) will cease — drawn from the verse *hesir Hashem mishpatayich, pina oyvech*: God will remove your corrupt judgment, and your enemies will turn away.

### Broken Staff Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מַלַּאי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״שָׁבַר ה׳ מַטֵּה רְשָׁעִים שֵׁבֶט מוֹשְׁלִים״. ״שָׁבַר ה׳ מַטֵּה רְשָׁעִים״ — אֵלּוּ הַדַּיָּינִין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ מַקֵּל לְחַזָּנֵיהֶם. ״שֵׁבֶט מוֹשְׁלִים״ — אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבְּמִשְׁפְּחוֹת הַדַּיָּינִין. מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמְלַמְּדִים הִלְכוֹת צִיבּוּר לְדַיָּינֵי בּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Mallai said in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "The Lord has broken the staff of the wicked, the rod of the rulers"** (Isaiah 14:5)? He explains: **"The Lord has broken the staff of the wicked"; these are the judges who have become staffs for their attendants.** The attendants abuse people, and the judges provide the attendants with legal backing and moral support. **"The rod of the rulers"; these are the Torah scholars** who are members of **the families of the judges.** These Torah scholars assist their relatives, the judges, conceal their faults. **Mar Zutra said: These are the Torah scholars who teach communal *halakhot* to ignorant judges.** They teach ignorant judges just enough Torah and modes of conduct to prevent the people from realizing how ignorant they are, enabling them to maintain their positions.

A further teaching is brought in the name of Rabbi Milai citing Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon, on the verse *shavar Hashem mateh reshaim shevet moshlim* — God will break the staff of the wicked, the staff of rulers. A staff is not here a weapon for striking but a support, something one leans upon. *Mateh reshaim* — the staff of the wicked — *elu hadayanim* — these are the judges. But then: *shenasu makel lechazzaneihem* — they have become a stick of support for their officers. The point is that the corrupt judges bear not only the guilt of their own perverted rulings but also the guilt of enabling all those who work for them. The officers — the *chazzanim* — circulate and exploit the judges' authority: they say, in effect, if you want this summons served, if you want this ruling enforced, you will have to pay for it. The corruption spreads outward from the judges through all the attendant positions of authority that shelter beneath their institutional power.

### Talmidei Chachamim Role

The second half of the verse — *shevet moshlim*, the staff of rulers — is applied as follows: *elu talmidei chachamim shebemishpachot hadayanim* — these are the Torah scholars in the families of the judges. Rashi explains that these judges do not genuinely deserve their positions, but their family members who are learned teach them just enough Torah to maintain an appearance of competence, and defend them when they act improperly. This is a strikingly sharp indictment: it is ultimately the *talmidei chachamim* who are most to blame, because they prop up the unworthy judges, and the unworthy judges in turn prop up all those who perpetuate the corruption around them.

Mar Zutra offers a variant formulation: *elu talmidei chachamim she-melamdim hilchot tzibur le-dayanei bur* — these are Torah scholars who teach communal law to ignoramus judges. The point is not necessarily limited to family members; any learned person who provides just enough knowledge to give an incompetent judge a veneer of authority is complicit in the resulting injustice. There is a notable shift in the analysis here from wickedness to incompetence: the corruption begins with judges who are knowledgeable but venal, and then extends to judges who are simply unqualified but sustained in their positions by those who know better.

This naturally raises questions about nepotism and the practice of obtaining positions through family connections rather than merit, though the *Gemara* does not address that dimension explicitly. What it does say is that the responsibility of those who enable the unqualified — whether as relatives, tutors, or institutional supporters — is at least as grave as that of the incompetent judges themselves.

### Fingers & Tongues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן מַלַּאי מִשּׁוּם רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי כַפֵּיכֶם נְגֹאֲלוּ בַדָּם וְאֶצְבְּעוֹתֵיכֶם בֶּעָוֹן, שִׂפְתוֹתֵיכֶם דִּבְּרוּ שֶׁקֶר לְשׁוֹנְכֶם עַוְלָה תֶהְגֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Mallai said in the name of Reish Lakish: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "For your hands are defiled with blood, and your fingers with iniquity; your lips have spoken lies, your tongue utters wickedness"** (Isaiah 59:3)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי כַפֵּיכֶם נְגוֹאֲלוּ בַדָּם״ — אֵלּוּ הַדַּיָּינִין. ״וְאֶצְבְּעוֹתֵיכֶם בְּעָוֹן״ — אֵלּוּ סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין. ״שִׂפְתוֹתֵיכֶם דְּבָרוֹ שֶׁקֶר״ — אֵלּוּ עוֹרְכֵי הַדַּיָּינִין. ״לְשׁוֹנְכֶם עַוְלָה תֶּהְגֶּה״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלִי דִּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: **"For your hands are defiled with blood"; these are the judges** who take bribes in their hands. **"And your fingers with iniquity"; these are the scribes of the judges,** who write falsehood with their fingers. **"Your lips have spoken lies"; these are the legal advisors. "Your tongue utters wickedness"; these are the litigants** themselves.

The entire legal system is thus implicated from top to bottom. What is particularly striking about this passage is not merely the extent of the corruption but the precision with which it assigns responsibility. Beginning from the *talmidei chachamim* who enable unworthy judges, continuing through the judges themselves, their officers, their scribes, their lawyers, and finally the litigants — every participant is named and held accountable. The *Gemara* does not treat this as a purely historical or literary exercise on the *pesukim*; it moves between *tannaitic* and *amoraic* voices across generations, suggesting an enduring concern.

## Joseph & Aaron

### Joseph as Nazir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי מַלַּאי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִצְחָק מַגְדְּלָאָה: מִיּוֹם שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ יוֹסֵף מֵאֶחָיו לֹא טָעַם טַעַם יַיִן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּלְקׇדְקֹד נְזִיר אֶחָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Mallai said in the name of Rabbi Yitzḥak from Migdal: From the day that Joseph took leave from his brothers, he did not sample a taste of wine, as it is written:** "They shall be on the head of Joseph, **and on the crown of the head of he who was separated [*nezir*] from his brothers"** (Genesis 49:26). The language of the verse alludes to the fact that Joseph conducted himself like a nazirite and abstained from wine.

The *Gemara* now moves to three apparently unrelated statements, all attributed to Rav Milai in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak from Migdal. The first: *miyom sheparash Yosef me'echav, lo ta'am ta'am yayin* — from the day that Joseph was separated from his brothers, he did not taste wine. The proof-text is the phrase *ulekodkod nezir echav* — literally, to the crown of the head of the one separated from his brothers — which, read through the lens of *nezir*, suggests that Joseph adopted a *nazir*-like abstinence. This is combined with the well-known detail that he had long hair, and the inference is extended to the full *nazir* profile: no wine, no contact with the dead, and distinctive hair. The *Gemara* appears to understand this abstinence as a form of mourning — Joseph, torn from his family, separating himself from worldly pleasures in his grief.

### Brothers' Wine Abstention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: אַף הֵן לֹא טָעֲמוּ טַעַם יַיִן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁתּוּ וַיִּשְׁכְּרוּ עִמּוֹ״, מִכְּלָל דְּעַד הָאִידָּנָא לָא הֲוָה שִׁיכְרוּת. וְאִידַּךְ, שִׁיכְרוּת הוּא דְּלָא הֲוָה, שְׁתִיָּהּ מִיהָא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** Joseph's brothers **too did not sample the taste of wine** during the intervening period, due to their remorse, **as it is written: "And they drank and became drunk with him"** (Genesis 43:34). **By inference: Until now there was no drunkenness,** as they abstained from drinking. **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Mallai, holds: **It was drunkenness** of **which there was none; however, there was drinking** on the part of the brothers during the intervening years.

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina adds that the brothers likewise did not taste wine during all those years of separation. The proof-text is *vayishtu vayishkeru imo* — they drank and became drunk with him — with the inference being that the emphasis on *imo*, with him, implies that until that moment they had not been drinking at all: *michlal de'ad idna lo* — by implication, until now they had not. A dissenting voice objects that perhaps the verse simply indicates that they did not previously get *drunk*, while they did drink; the response is that *shtiya mi'hava* — drinking they did engage in — but *shichrus de'lo hava* — drunkenness had not occurred until that moment of reunion. One might also wonder, as the *MAHARSHA* does, whether on Joseph's side it was truly *nazir*-like abstinence or whether *eimas malchus* — awe of the ruler — kept the brothers from drinking. The *MAHARSHA* also raises a technical difficulty: *vayigalach vayichalef simlosav* — Joseph shaved and changed his garments — which complicates reading him as a literal *nazir*. The *Gemara* may therefore be using the term in a looser, evocative sense rather than asserting formal *nezirut*.

### Aaron & Heart Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר רַבִּי מַלַּאי: בִּשְׂכַר ״וְרָאֲךָ וְשָׂמַח בְּלִבּוֹ״, זָכָה לְחֹשֶׁן הַמִּשְׁפָּט עַל לִבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Mallai said:** It is stated in the verse: "And the anger of the Lord was kindled against Moses, and He said: Is there not Aaron your brother the Levite, I know that he can surely speak, and also behold, he is coming out to greet you, and he will see you and be glad in his heart" (Exodus 4:14). Rabbi Mallai taught that **as reward for** Aaron's lack of jealousy at seeing his brother Moses rise to greatness, as it is stated: **"And he will see you and be glad in his heart," he merited** to become the High Priest, and **for the breastplate of judgment** to rest **on his heart.**

The third statement of Rav Milai: *bischar de'ra'acha vesamach belibo* — in the merit that God foretold to Moses that Aaron would see him and rejoice in his heart — *zacha l'choshen hamishpat al libo* — Aaron merited that the *choshen mishpat* would rest upon his heart. The *MAHARSHA* notes that the verse does not merely say Aaron would appear to rejoice — the rejoicing was genuine and internal, free of any hidden jealousy. The reward is therefore fitting: the *choshen mishpat*, which rests on the heart, goes to one whose heart was truly pure. One might note a faint thematic resonance between this and the extended discussion of corrupt judges that preceded it — there, the corruption of the legal system is traced to the total absence of inner integrity; here, it is precisely inner integrity, the heart that genuinely rejoices, that earns the ultimate symbol of judgment placed over the heart. The *Gemara*, however, does not draw this connection explicitly, and the three statements of Rav Milai appear to have been assembled here simply because they share an author rather than because they share a theme.

## Responsa & Truth

### Three Questions to Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי בָּשְׁכָּר לְלֵוִי: כִּילָּה מַהוּ? כְּשׁוּתָא בְּכַרְמָא מַהוּ? מֵת בְּיוֹם טוֹב מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the laws of a canopy. **The inhabitants of** the town of **Bashkar sent to Levi: What is** the *halakha* with regard to spreading **a canopy** on Shabbat? Additionally, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to **hops in a vineyard?** Do they constitute a prohibited mixture of diverse kinds? Finally, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to one who **died on a Festival?** How can the people attend to his burial?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּאָזֵיל נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּלֵוִי. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב מְנַשְּׁיָא: אִי חַכִּימַתְּ, שְׁלַח לְהוּ. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: כִּילָּה — חָזַרְנוּ עַל כׇּל צִידֵּי כִּילָּה וְלֹא מָצִינוּ לָהּ צַד הֶיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As** the messenger **was going** with the question, **Levi died. Shmuel said to Rav Menashya: If you are wise** and able to respond, **send them** answers to their questions. **He sent them:** With regard to **a canopy, we reviewed all aspects of** the matter of the **canopy, and we did not find any permissible aspect.**

The *Gemara* now transitions back toward the laws of *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* through a narrative about three *sheilot*. The people of Bischar sent three questions to Levi: what is the law regarding a *kilah* (a canopy placed over a bed)? What about planting *kushuta* (hops, a vine-like plant) in a vineyard — is that *kilayim*? And what happens when someone dies on *Yom Tov*? As the messenger was traveling, *nach nafshih d'Levi* — Levi died. Shmuel then turned to Rav Menashia and said: *i chakimas, shalch lehu* — if you are capable, send them a response. This appears to have functioned as a kind of training exercise — here is a real question, let us see how you handle it.

### Canopy Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישְׁלַח לְהוּ כִּדְרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל! לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן בְּנֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him send them** that it can be permitted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rami bar Yeḥezkel.** The Gemara answers: He did not want to reveal that leniency to them, **because they are not** well versed **in Torah,** and they would not distinguish between permitted and prohibited methods of spreading the canopy.

Rav Menashia responded: regarding the *kilah*, *chazarna akol tzidi kila velo matzana lah tzad heter* — we have looked at every side of the canopy issue and cannot find any angle of leniency. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: but could he not have sent them the ruling of Rami bar Yechezkel, discussed on the preceding daf, regarding a drawstring arrangement that would permit it? The answer: *lefi she'einam bnei Torah* — because they were not *bnei Torah*. The concern was that if given a nuanced leniency, they would be unable to internalize the distinctions and would come to apply the permissive ruling even in circumstances where it does not apply.

### Kilayim Caution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשׁוּתָא בְּכַרְמָא — עִירְבּוּבָא. וְלִישְׁלַח לְהוּ כִּדְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן? דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשׁוּת, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. וְקַיְימָא לַן: כׇּל הַמֵּיקֵל בָּאָרֶץ, הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ! לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן בְּנֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** He also told them: **Hops in a vineyard are** a forbidden **mixture** of diverse kinds. The Gemara asks: **And let him send them** the message that it is permitted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon, as it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: With regard to **hops, Rabbi Tarfon says:** They do **not** constitute a prohibited mixture of **food crops in a vineyard, and the Rabbis say:** They constitute a forbidden mixture of **food crops in a vineyard. And we maintain** that **anyone who is lenient** with regard to the *halakhot* of diverse kinds **in Eretz** Yisrael, even if the *halakha* is not ruled in accordance with his opinion, **the *halakha* is** ruled **in accordance with his** opinion **outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael, where the *halakhot* of diverse kinds apply only by rabbinic law. The Gemara explains: He did not reveal this leniency to them, **because they were not** well versed in **Torah.**

Regarding *kushuta* in a vineyard, Rav Menashia replied: *irvuva* — it is a forbidden mixture. Again the *Gemara* asks: why not transmit the ruling of Rav Tarfon? A *baraita* records that Rav Tarfon holds *kushuta* is not *kilayim b'cherem*, while the *chachamim* hold that it is. The question seems to turn on whether *kushuta* — a vine-like plant — is classified as vegetation or as a tree, since only grain or vegetables planted in a vineyard create *kilayim*, not trees. The general principle *kol hameikel ba'aretz halacha kamoso b'chutz la'aretz* establishes that in the diaspora, where agricultural commandments are rabbinic rather than biblical (or at most *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai*), we rule leniently, following Rav Tarfon. So the lenient position was technically available. Again the answer: *lefi she'einam bnei Torah*.

### Rav Amram's Severity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרִיז רַב: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמִיזְרַע כְּשׁוּתָא בְּכַרְמָא — לִיזְרַע. רַב עַמְרָם חֲסִידָא מְנַגֵּיד עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the matter of hops in a vineyard, the Gemara relates that **Rav would announce: One who seeks to sow hops in a vineyard, let him sow.** In contrast, **Rav Amram Ḥasida** would **administer lashes for** sowing hops in a vineyard.

The contrast is sharpened by noting that Rav himself made a public announcement — *machriz Rav* — that anyone who wishes to plant *kushuta* in a vineyard may do so. He was evidently not concerned about misapplication. Yet Rav Amram the pious — *chasida* — would administer lashes to anyone who did it. Tosafot raises the obvious difficulty: how could Rav Amram give lashes if the *halacha* follows Rav Tarfon permitting it? The answer Tosafot gives is the same: *d'vimkomam lo hayu bnei Torah* — in their locality people could not be trusted with the knowledge, and so Rav Amram enforced the stricter position even through corporal punishment, out of concern for what irresponsible application of the leniency would lead to.

### Non-Jewish Child

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ פְּרוּטָה לְתִינוֹק גּוֹי וְזָרַע לֵיהּ. וְלִיתֵּן לֵיהּ לְתִינוֹק יִשְׂרָאֵל! אָתֵי לְמִיסְרַךְ: וְלִיתֵּן לֵיהּ לְגָדוֹל גּוֹי! אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Mesharshiya would give a *peruta* to a gentile child, and** the child would **sow** hops **for him.** The Gemara asks: **And let him give** the *peruta* **to a Jewish child,** who is also not obligated in mitzva observance. The Gemara answers: **He** may **come to continue** this habit and violate the prohibition as an adult. The Gemara asks: **And let him give** the *peruta* **to an adult gentile.** The Gemara answers: **He** may **come to confuse** him **with a Jew.**

Rav Mesharshiya chose a middle path: *yahiv lei pruta l'tinok nochri v'zara lei* — he gave a small coin to a non-Jewish child and had the child plant for him. He evidently accepted the *halacha* as technically permissible but was still uncomfortable doing it directly, preferring to have a non-Jew act in his place.

### Habituation Concern

These concerns give rise to two broad *halachic* questions that extend well beyond this particular case. The first is the scope of *amirah l'goi* — the prohibition of instructing a non-Jew to do something forbidden — outside the specific context of *Shabbos*. The second is the question of *chinuch* and habituation: under what circumstances does performing a technically permissible act create a risk of training undesirable behavior?

Tosafot raises a related parallel: when a *bris milah* falls on *Yom Kippur*, may one recite *kiddush* over a cup and have a child drink from it, given that it is a rare occurrence? And separately, may one say *shehecheyanu* on *Yom Kippur* over a cup and have a child drink, when this is done annually? Tosafot distinguishes the two cases precisely on the grounds of habituation: the rare case does not create a pattern, but the annual repetition of drinking from a *kos* on *Yom Kippur* risks accustoming the child in a way that could lead to inappropriate behavior when grown.

### Burial on Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת — שְׁלַח לְהוּ: מֵת לֹא יִתְעַסְּקוּ בּוֹ לֹא יְהוּדָאִין וְלֹא אַרְמָאִין, לֹא בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a person who **died** on a Festival, **he sent them** in response: If a person **died** on a Festival, **neither Jews nor Arameans,** i.e., gentiles, **should attend to his** burial, **neither on the first day of a Festival, nor on the second day of a Festival** observed in the Diaspora.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר שִׁילַת אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בְּבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא דְּמָעוֹן בְּיוֹם טוֹב הַסָּמוּךְ לַשַּׁבָּת, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מִלְּפָנֶיהָ אִי מִלְּאַחֲרֶיהָ, וַאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: יִתְעַסְּקוּן בֵּיהּ עַמְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rabbi Yehuda bar Sheilat say** that **Rabbi Asi said: There was an incident** that happened **in the synagogue** of the settlement of **Maon on a Festival adjacent to Shabbat,** and a person died, **and I do not know whether** the Festival occurred **before** Shabbat **or after** Shabbat. **And they came before Rabbi Yoḥanan, and he said to them: Let gentiles attend to his** burial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: מֵת בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן יִתְעַסְּקוּ בּוֹ עַמְמִין, בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי — יִתְעַסְּקוּ בּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבֵיצָה. לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן בְּנֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **Rava said:** If a person **died on the first day of a Festival, gentiles may attend to his** burial; **on the second day of a Festival** observed in the Diaspora, **Jews may attend to his** burial. **And** this is the *halakha* **even on the second day of the festival of Rosh HaShana, which is not so with regard to an egg.** With regard to an egg laid on the Festival, the two days of Rosh HaShana are considered one long day that cannot be disconnected. Therefore, in contrast to other two-day Festivals in the Diaspora, use of an egg laid on the first day of Rosh HaShana is prohibited on the second day. However, in deference to the dead, the Sages were lenient with regard to burial on the second day of Rosh HaShana. Why then did Rav Menashya prohibit the inhabitants of Bashkar from attending to the burial on a Festival? The Gemara answers: **Because they were not** well versed in **Torah.**

The third question was the most urgent: what happens when someone dies on *Yom Tov*? Rav Menashia replied that neither Jews nor non-Jews may involve themselves in burial — not on the first day of *Yom Tov* and not on the second. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this as inconsistent with established *halacha*. Rav Yehuda bar Shilas citing Rav Assi reports a case in the synagogue of Maon, where *Yom Tov* fell adjacent to *Shabbos* — he was uncertain whether *Yom Tov* came before or after *Shabbos*, but in either case the concern was that the deceased would go unburied for multiple days. The case came before Rabbi Yochanan, who ruled: *yisasku bo amamim* — let non-Jews handle the burial. Rava then states the full *halacha* explicitly: *meis b'Yom Tov rishon yisasku bo amamim* — if someone dies on the first day of *Yom Tov*, non-Jews bury him; *b'Yom Tov sheni yisasku bo Yisrael* — if on the second day, even Jews may bury him. This applies even to the second day of *Rosh Hashanah*, which ordinarily carries the full stringency of the first day — for burial purposes, Jews may act on the second day. This leniency does not carry over to *Beitzah* laws: an egg born on the first day remains forbidden on the second, even though burial is permitted on the second day.

Why, then, did Rav Menashia tell the people of Bischar that burial is forbidden on both days? Again: *lefi she'ein b'nei Torah* — they could not be trusted to properly distinguish between first day and second day, or between the conditions under which Jews may act and those under which only non-Jews may.

Rashi understands this entire discussion of first- and second-day burial as limited to the case where *Yom Tov* is adjacent to *Shabbos*, such that not burying extends the delay to multiple days. The accepted *halacha*, however, is that the distinction between first and second day applies regardless of adjacency to *Shabbos*. There is a further question about whether this *halacha* applies in the same way today, when bodies can be refrigerated and the urgency of immediate burial is reduced.

Tosafot adds a further consideration drawn from an otherwise unrelated *Gemara*: if non-Jews observe Jews performing labor on the second day of *Yom Tov*, they may press Jews into their service on other occasions, reasoning that Jews are evidently willing to work on *Yom Tov*. Rabbeinu Tam applied this logic to an actual case in which a deceased was brought outside the *techum* on *Yom Tov* and Jews rode out on horseback to participate in the burial. Rabbeinu Tam was angry even at those who merely walked. He went further and applied the principle of *bnei Torah* directly: just as the *Gemara* could not trust the people of Bischar with the full *halacha*, Rabbeinu Tam maintained that the community in question was likewise not *bnei Torah* and therefore the leniency could not be disclosed to them.

### Withholding Truth

This raises a genuine ethical tension that deserves direct attention. For many readers, withholding accurate *halachic* information strikes us as deeply paternalistic — people ask a *posek* a question and deserve to be treated as adults capable of receiving and applying the answer responsibly. At the same time, there is a practical reality: even the most well-intentioned person, once told that a *bedieved* ruling applies in a particular case, may come to treat that *bedieved* as a *l'chatchila*. The *bedieved* becomes the new normal. When a *posek* knows this will happen — when it is not a theoretical possibility but a predictable consequence — the question becomes sharper: is the person asking for *halachic* information, or are they asking for help remaining within the bounds of *halacha*? If the latter, then providing technically accurate information that will predictably cause them to cross those bounds may be a failure of *psak*, not a faithful execution of it.

Some *halachic* handbooks have addressed this by placing the nuanced distinctions in footnotes and presenting only the baseline ruling in the main text — on the theory that anyone who reads the footnotes has enough sophistication to handle the nuance. In Israel that solution is complicated by the fact that virtually everyone can read the footnotes. The deeper question of how much responsibility a *posek* bears for the downstream behavior of those he instructs is not settled by this *Gemara* but is genuinely raised by it.

It is worth noting one asymmetry among the three cases. The first two — the canopy and the *kilayim* — involve situations where withholding the lenient ruling simply means someone forgoes a convenience or plants elsewhere. The third case involves *kevod hameis*, the dignity of the dead, and leaving a body unburied for an extended period. The competing value is not merely inconvenience but a serious matter of human dignity. That asymmetry arguably makes the third case the most troubling application of the *bnei Torah* principle — though the *Gemara* applies the same reasoning uniformly across all three.

## Haarama & Scheming

### Sheet as Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: מִתְעַטֵּף אָדָם בְּכִילָּה וּבְכִסְכָּסֶיהָ וְיוֹצֵא לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gureya said: A person may wrap himself in a canopy and in its straps and go out to the public domain on Shabbat, and he need not be concerned** about violating the prohibited act of carrying out a burden.

Returning now to the laws of *Shabbos*, the *Gemara* cites Rav Avin bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria: *mis'atef adam bikila u-vikiskas'ha v'yotzei l'reshus harabim b'Shabbos* — a person may wrap himself in the canopy sheet together with its straps and go out into the *reshus harabim* on *Shabbos*. The canopy sheet cannot be used to construct a canopy on *Shabbos*, but it may be worn as a garment. When worn as a garment, the straps — which ordinarily function to attach the canopy to the bed — are considered secondary to the garment and do not constitute an independent object being carried. One can, in essence, wear a blanket into the public domain, and the incidental straps are subsumed into the act of wearing.

### Tzitzit vs. Straps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הַיּוֹצֵא בְּטַלִּית שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיֶּיצֶת כְּהִילְכָתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת! צִיצִית לְגַבֵּי טַלִּית — חֲשִׁיבִי, וְלָא בָּטְלִי. הָנֵי לָא חֲשִׁיבִי, וּבָטְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way is the *halakha* with regard to the straps **different from that** which **Rav Huna** said? **As Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: One who goes out** unwittingly to the public domain **on Shabbat with a** four-cornered **garment that does not have the requisite ritual fringes attached is liable** to bring **a sin-offering,** because the remaining fringes are not an integral part of the garment. Since they do not fulfill the mitzva, they are considered a burden that may not be carried into the public domain on Shabbat. The Gemara answers: There is a distinction between the cases. **Ritual fringes are significant relative to a garment and are not negated.** They are considered an independent entity that may not be carried out into the public domain. **These** straps of a canopy **are not significant and are negated.**

This leads immediately to a comparison with a ruling of Rav Huna citing Rav: one who goes out on *Shabbos* wearing a *tallis* that lacks *tzitzit* on all four corners — say, only two or three corners are fringed — is liable for a sin-offering, because the incomplete *tzitzit* are not performing a *mitzvah* and are therefore not incorporated into the *beged*, and so amount to carrying strings into the public domain. Why, then, are the canopy straps not similarly considered an independent object?

The answer: *tzitzis legabei tallis chashivi velo batli* — *tzitzit* in relation to the *tallis* are significant in their own right and are not nullified; *hani lo chashivi u-vatli* — these straps are not significant and are nullified to the garment. Rashi explains the significance of *tzitzit* in terms of their material value — *techeiles* dye was expensive. Tosafot offers a different account: *tzitzit* are significant because they are a *mitzvah* object with independent utility, and because if you will not use them on this *tallis* you will remove them and use them on another. They have a life beyond this particular garment. The straps, by contrast, exist only in relation to the canopy; no one salvages canopy straps for independent use. Because they have no independent significance, they are simply part of the *beged* when the sheet is worn as a garment.

A natural question arises: what about wearing a *tallis* at night, or while walking home, when there is no *mitzvah* obligation? The issue turns on whether the *tzitzit* are defined by their status as a *chefza* — an object of *mitzvah* — independently of whether a *kiyum hamitzvah* is currently occurring. This is genuinely debated, and the resolution seems to be that once *tzitzit* are properly attached, they are incorporated into the *beged* regardless of the time of day or the circumstances, since the *beged* with its *tzitzit* constitutes a single halachically defined object.

### Sieve Scheme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: מַעֲרִים אָדָם עַל הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב לִתְלוֹת בָּהּ רִמּוֹנִים, וְתוֹלֶה בָּהּ שְׁמָרִים. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וְהוּא דְּתָלָה בָּהּ רִמּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** According to the Rabbis, who prohibit suspending a strainer on a Festival, **a person** may nevertheless **employ artifice** and circumvent the prohibition against suspending **a strainer** by taking it **on a Festival** and **suspending it** for the purpose of **pomegranates in it,** which is permitted. Once the strainer is suspended, he may **suspend it** in order to filter **sediment** from the wine, as even the Rabbis hold that on a Festival it is permitted to do this straining through a strainer that is already suspended. **Rav Ashi said: And this** is only permitted provided **that he** actually **suspended pomegranates in it** before using it to strain wine. It must be obvious to all that he used the strainer in a permitted manner.

The *Gemara* now shifts from *ohel* back to the other topic of the *Mishnah* — using a *mishmeres* (sieve or strainer) to pour wine through on *Yom Tov*. The *Mishnah* recorded a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* about placing the strainer on a vessel on *Yom Tov*; the *halacha* follows the *chachamim* that it may not be placed. Rabbah bar Rav Huna then teaches: *maarim adam al hamishmeres b'Yom Tov litlos bah rimonim* — a person may scheme with regard to the strainer on *Yom Tov* by placing it over the vessel and declaring that he is using it as a rack upon which to hang pomegranates. Since the concern with the strainer is not *ohel* but rather *uvda d'chol* — a weekday-like activity suggestive of professional wine production — reframing the act as pomegranate-hanging removes the *uvda d'chol* character. Once the strainer is in place for that declared purpose, *d'tala bah shamrim* — he can then put wine lees into it and use it for filtering.

Rav Ashi tightens the ruling: *v'hu d'tala bah rimonim* — this only works if he actually hangs pomegranates in it. One cannot merely declare the intention; the alternative purpose must be genuinely realized, or the entire act will appear transparently pretextual.

### Garlic Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: מְטִילִין שֵׁכָר בַּמּוֹעֵד לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד, שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — אָסוּר, אֶחָד שֵׁכַר תְּמָרִים וְאֶחָד שֵׁכַר שְׂעוֹרִים. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן יָשָׁן — מַעֲרִים וְשׁוֹתֶה מִן הֶחָדָשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** that *halakha* **different from that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may begin brewing beer during** the intermediate days of **a Festival for the purpose of** using it on **the Festival.** If it is **not for the purpose of the Festival, it is prohibited.** This is the *halakha* **both** with regard to **date beer and** with regard to **barley beer.** And **even though they have old** beer, **one may employ artifice and** say that he wishes to **drink from the new,** and that he is making beer on the intermediate days of the Festival for that purpose. Apparently, it is permitted to employ artifice even without actively demonstrating that one is performing the action for a permitted purpose. This contradicts the opinion of Rav Ashi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא, הָכָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to beer, **the matter is not evident.** When people see someone starting to brew beer, they have no way of knowing whether or not he has beer at home, and consequently, whether or not the action itself is prohibited in that case. However, **here,** with regard to a strainer, **the matter is evident,** as people see him suspending a strainer for wine, which is prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: חֲזִי מָר הַאי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְרַב הוּנָא בֶּן רַבִּי חִיּוֹן שְׁמֵיהּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרַבִּי חִלְווֹן שְׁמֵיהּ, דְּשָׁקֵל בְּרָא דְתוּמָא וּמַנַּח בְּבַרְזָא דְּדַנָּא, וְאָמַר: לְאַצְּנוֹעֵיהּ קָמִיכַּוֵינָא. וְאָזֵיל וְנָאֵים בְּמַבָּרָא, וְעָבַר לְהַךְ גִּיסָא וְסָיַיר פִּירֵי, וְאָמַר: אֲנָא לְמֵינַם קָמִיכַּוֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of artifice, the Gemara relates that **the Rabbis said to Rav Ashi: Master, observe this Torah scholar, and Rav Huna ben Rabbi Ḥayon is his name, and some say** that **his name is Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Ḥalvan, who took a slice of garlic and placed it in the spout of a barrel, and said: I intend to store it.** He thereby stopped the spout on Shabbat. **And** similarly, **he went and slept in a ferry** on the river, and the ferryman sailed the ferry across the river, **and he** thereby **crossed to the other side and inspected the fruit** of his vineyard. He said: **I intend to sleep.** In this way, he crosses the river by boat on Shabbat, which is a prohibited activity.

The *Gemara* then reports a case involving a young *talmid chacham*, Rav Huna bar Chiya (some say Rav Huna bar Rav Chalvo), who engaged in two acts of *haarama* that drew the attention of the other rabbis. The first: *deshakil barda detuma u-manach devei barza dedanna* — he took a clove of garlic and placed it in the opening of a barrel's faucet, and then said *le'atnoei ka michavne* — I was placing my garlic in storage. In reality he seems to have been using the garlic to plug the faucet — an act that Rashi understands as a form of *metaken*, repairing or preparing — but he framed it as merely finding a convenient place to keep his garlic.

The second act: *v'azal v'nayam be'mabra* — he went and fell asleep on a ferry — and told the ferryman (*achissa*) nothing. The non-Jewish ferryman took him across the river. When he arrived at the other side, *vesiyar peirei* — he used the opportunity to inspect his fruit trees. And he said: *ana l'niyum ka michavne* — I intended only to take a nap; I cannot be held responsible for where the ferryman took me. The rabbis wanted Rav Ashi to do something about this young scholar who was deploying one scheme after another.

### Rav Ashi Allows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַעֲרָמָה קָאָמְרַתְּ? הַעֲרָמָה בִּדְרַבָּנַן הִיא, וְצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן לָא אָתֵי לְמִיעְבַּד לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to them: Are you speaking of artifice? This is artifice** employed to circumvent **a rabbinic** prohibition, **and a Torah scholar will not come to perform** the action ***ab initio*** without artifice. Therefore, there is no reason to prohibit him from doing so.

Rav Ashi's response was instructive: *harama ka'amartu? harama de'rabbanan hi* — are you talking about scheming? This is rabbinic-level scheming — none of these involve biblical prohibitions. Furthermore, *tzurba merabbanan lo asi leme'evad lechatchila* — a *talmid chacham* will not come to do these things in a straightforwardly prohibited manner. He knows enough to keep the surface appearance legitimate, and he will not slide from this kind of maneuvering into actual violation. Once again the *bnei Torah* distinction appears, but now in a permissive direction: precisely because this individual is a *talmid chacham*, *haarama* in the domain of rabbinic prohibitions is acceptable.

This raises the deeper question of why *haarama* is permissible at all. One way to understand it is as a safety valve in *halacha* — a recognition that law must balance its normative demands against genuine human needs, and that where the law is felt to be overly restrictive, providing a legitimate mechanism for relief is preferable to either frontally violating or creating impossible standards. That is how *lechatchila*, *bedieved*, and *sha'as had'chak* function; *haarama* occupies a similar space. But *haarama* carries a different valence than those categories, because it explicitly involves framing one's actions as something other than what they are — it teaches a method of pretense, of appearing to comply while achieving a different end. In this respect it is arguably more troubling than the other mechanisms. The *Gemara* does not seem troubled by this; it simply specifies the conditions under which *haarama* is permissible and leaves the conceptual question open. The recurring qualification — that only a *talmid chacham* may rely on it — serves as the limiting principle, though whether that limitation is wholly satisfying is a question the *Gemara* leaves for the reader.

## Filtering Liquids

### Water Through Lees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹתְנִין מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי הַשְּׁמָרִים בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּצּוֹלּוּ. וּמְסַנְּנִין אֶת הַיַּיִן בְּסוּדָרִין וּבִכְפִיפָה מִצְרִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may pour water over sediment** that is in a strainer on Shabbat **so that it will become clear** and clean. **And** similarly, **one may filter wine through cloths and through an Egyptian basket** made from palm leaves. Since these liquids are drinkable even without filtering, doing so does not violate the prohibition of selecting.

The *Mishnah* states: *notnin mayim al gabei shemarim bishvil sheyeitzolu* — one may pour water onto grape lees so that the water will pass through. This is not *borer*, because no mixture is being separated into its components. Water enters, passes through the lees, absorbs flavor, and exits on the other side. The analogy to a tea bag is apt: the liquid moves through, the solid remains, and nothing is being sorted. This is why using a tea bag on *Shabbos* does not raise a concern of *borer*.

### Non-Standard Filtering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנִין בֵּיצָה בִּמְסַנֶּנֶת שֶׁל חַרְדָּל, וְעוֹשִׂין אֵנוֹמֵלִין בְּשַׁבָּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּשַּׁבָּת בְּכוֹס, בְּיוֹם טוֹב — בְּלָגִין, וּבַמּוֹעֵד — בְּחָבִית. רַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר הַכֹּל לְפִי הָאוֹרְחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **one may place an egg in a mustard strainer** in order to separate the yolk from the egg-white, **and one may prepare *anumlin*,** a wine-based drink, **on Shabbat. Rabbi Yehuda says: On Shabbat** one may only make *anumlin* in a small **cup; on a Festival, in a** larger **vessel; and on the intermediate days of a Festival,** one may even prepare it **in a barrel. Rabbi Tzadok says:** There is no objective principle; rather, **it is all according to the** number of **guests;** if they are numerous, one may prepare a larger quantity of *anumlin*.

The *Mishnah* continues: *u'mesanenin et ha-yayin be-sudarim* — one may filter wine through a cloth. Even where wine has impurities, and a formal strainer placed on the vessel is prohibited (as discussed at length regarding *ohel* and *uvda d'chol*), one may use a non-standard vessel. Filtering through a cloth or through a wicker basket (*chfiffa mitrit*) does not carry the same concerns as using a designated strainer.

*V'notnin beitzah bi-mshenenet shel chardal* — one may pass an egg through a mustard sieve. Either the egg receives the yellow color from the mustard, or the egg yolk flows down and adds color to the mustard below. In either direction this is understood as a form of mixing or blending — adding and receiving color — rather than as *borer*, sorting one substance from another.

*V'osin anomilin b'Shabbos* — one may prepare *anomilin* (a honey-wine mixture) on *Shabbos*. Rabbi Yehuda specifies: on *Shabbos*, one may prepare it only in a single cup (*bekos*); on *Yom Tov*, in a *log* (a much larger measure); on *Chol HaMoed*, in a barrel. The scale of permitted preparation increases as the sanctity of the day decreases and the latitude to engage in food preparation expands. Rabbi Tzadok adds a practical principle: everything depends on the number of guests — the quantity is calibrated to genuine need rather than to the abstract status of the day.

### Clear Liquids Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר זְעֵירִי: נוֹתֵן אָדָם יַיִן צָלוּל וּמַיִם צְלוּלִין לְתוֹךְ הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בְּשַׁבָּת וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ, אֲבָל עֲכוּרִין — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ze'iri said: A person may place clear wine and clear water into a strainer on Shabbat, and he need not be concerned** over the prohibition of selecting, as the wine is drinkable even without this filtering. **However,** doing so with **murky** liquids, **no,** one may not strain them.

Zeiri then states a principle of considerable scope: *noten adam yayin tzalul u-mayim tzulalim letoch hamishmeres b'Shabbos v'eino choshesh* — one may pour clear wine and clear water into a strainer on *Shabbos* without concern. *Aval achurin, lo* — but if the liquid is cloudy, one may not. The principle being articulated is that if a liquid is already fit to drink as it is, filtering it does not constitute *borer*. This is the basis for permitting use of a water filter on *Shabbos*: since the water is drinkable without filtering, one who filters it is not separating the unfit from the fit but merely improving what was already fit.

### Pressing Season

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: טוֹרֵד אָדָם חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן יֵינָהּ וּשְׁמָרֶיהָ, וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בַּשַּׁבָּת וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ! תַּרְגְּמַהּ זְעֵירִי: בֵּין הַגִּיתּוֹת שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A person may stir a barrel of wine,** mixing **its wine and its sediment, and place it in a strainer on Shabbat, and he need not be concerned.** Apparently, it is permitted to place even murky wine in a strainer. **Ze'iri explained that** *baraita* and resolved the difficulty: **That was taught** with regard to wine **between the presses,** when the wine has yet to ferment and will remain murky even after filtering.

The *Gemara* challenges Zeiri's principle from a *baraita* citing Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *tored adam chavis shel yayin ve-yayin u-shemareha v'noten letoch hamishmeres b'Shabbos* — one may actively agitate a barrel, mix the wine with its settled lees, and then pour the whole thing through a strainer on *Shabbos*. This seems plainly to involve cloudy liquid — wine with lees mixed in is not clear, and presumably no one wants to drink wine mixed with lees. How can this be permitted? Zeiri himself resolves the difficulty: *targema Zeiri bein ha-gittos shanu* — this ruling applies during the pressing season, when wine has just been made and is not yet clear. During that period, people actually do drink the wine in its cloudy state — it is the way wine is customarily consumed at that time of year. Therefore, during the pressing season, the cloudy wine with lees is considered fit to drink as it is, and filtering it does not constitute *borer*.

### Drinkability Test

The *Gemara* thus arrives at the governing principle: if a liquid is drinkable in its current state — whether because it is already clear, or because it is the pressing season when cloudy wine is routinely consumed — filtering it on *Shabbos* does not violate *borer*. This is a remarkable ruling, because nothing analogous applies to solid food: we do not say that because you would have eaten the mixture without sorting it, you may now sort it. The distinction between liquids and solids in the laws of *borer* is a fundamental one, and its precise rationale is much discussed in the *rishonim* and *acharonim*.

The practical consequences are significant. A water filter is permitted on *Shabbos* because the water is drinkable without it. Orange juice with pulp may be strained on *Shabbos* according to Rav Moshe Feinstein, since most people are prepared to drink it with the pulp; Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach draws a distinction in this case, though the simpler position is the more lenient one. The limiting case is the person who will not drink unfiltered water due to concerns about, for example, microscopic organisms (*copepods*): if that person would refuse to drink the water unfiltered, then for them the water is not currently fit to drink, and they may not use a filter on *Shabbos* — filtered water must be prepared before *Shabbos* begins. Similarly, a pitcher with a built-in strainer to hold back ice: since most people would drink the water with ice, and ice and water are arguably the same substance, this does not raise a concern. The *Gemara* will continue to develop these principles in the passages that follow.
