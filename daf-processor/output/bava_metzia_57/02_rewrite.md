# Bava Metzia 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chadash & Omer

### Seeds in Land

Before advancing to the new material on 57a, a brief clarification of the previous day's discussion is in order. The *mishnah* enumerates categories excluded from the laws of *ona'ah* and *shemirah*, and yesterday's focus was on *karka* — land — and its associated *halachos* concerning *ona'ah* and *shevua*. The final case examined involved *chadash* and the *omer*, but it was misread. The correct scenario is as follows.

You cut your harvest before the second day of *Pesach*. Had you left the grain sitting in storage — in your cabinet or jars — the arrival of the second day of *Pesach* would render it permitted through the *omer*. Instead, you placed the grain back into the ground, where it has not yet taken root. The question then is: when the second day of *Pesach* arrives, does the *omer* permit this grain? Is the grain treated simply as grain-in-storage — in which case the *omer* would apply — or has placing it in the ground transformed its status, such that it is now treated as part of the land itself? If the latter, the *omer* would no longer have any bearing on it, and if one were to scoop it out of the ground and eat it immediately after the *omer*, there would be no *heter* for doing so.

To be clear: what grows *afterwards* is obviously a separate matter — that grain has not yet been subject to the *omer* at all. The question is specifically about the grain already in the ground at the time the *omer* arrives. Can one scoop it out and eat it? That was yesterday's case.

## Ona'ah vs Bitul Mekach

### Rabbi Ami's Question

Three lines from the top of 57a, the *Gemara* introduces the teaching of Rava in the name of Rav Chasa: *bei'i Rabbi Ami* — Rabbi Ami posed the following question. The *mishnah* established that *ona'ah* does not apply to land, slaves, *shtarot*, and *hekdesh*. But what about *bitul mekach*? Does the voiding of a sale apply to these categories or not?

To appreciate the question, one must recall the structure of *ona'ah*. The *Torah* prohibits overcharging, and the Rabbis determined — drawing on their own judgment, as the *Torah* entrusted such determinations to the Sages — that a one-sixth discrepancy constitutes the threshold for *ona'ah*. Beyond that, the assumption is that so great a disproportion between price and value amounts to a voided transaction. There is, as was noted in an earlier discussion, a certain irony here: the *greater* the overcharge, the more options the seller has, since he too may void the sale. This suggests conceptually that *bitul mekach* may belong to an entirely different legal category.

Thus the question: even if the *Torah*'s specific laws of *ona'ah* do not apply to land, slaves, *shtarot*, and *hekdesh*, perhaps *bitul mekach* — which is less a law of overcharging than a recognition that a grossly disproportionate transaction simply was never a valid sale — does apply. If I sell you the wrong thing, or a fundamentally different thing from what you bargained for, *bitul mekach* would seem to operate regardless of whether the technical *halachos* of *ona'ah* apply. The exemption from *ona'ah* might speak only to moderate overcharges, not to transactions so disproportionate that they were never genuinely agreed to.

Now, the difference between one-sixth and just-over-one-sixth is admittedly not dramatic. But perhaps if the overcharge is double the value — charging 200 for something worth 100 — there is no need to invoke the special *halachos* of *ona'ah* at all. That is simply a voided sale, objectively. The *Gemara* then answers the question: Rabbi Ami himself resolved it. The *Gemara* records that Rav Nachman stated, citing Rav Chasa, that Rabbi Ami concluded — *ona'ah ein lahem, bitul mekach yesh lahem* — the laws of *ona'ah* do not apply to these categories, but *bitul mekach* does. Only the specific *halachos* of overcharging are set aside; an outright voided sale based on gross disproportion remains operative.

### Tosafot on Ona'ah

Before proceeding, it is worth examining Tosafot's reinterpretation of the *Gemara*. Tosafot notes that it *appears* Rav Nachman agrees with Rav Chisda and Rav Pappa that *bitul mekach* applies to *karka*. However, Tosafot raises a difficulty from a later section of Bava Metzia — in the chapter of *ha-mekabbel* — where a case is described of selling for 200 what is worth 100, and Rav Nachman states explicitly: *ein ona'ah le-karka'os*, no *ona'ah* applies to land. The clear implication is that even so extreme an overcharge does not constitute *bitul mekach* with respect to *karka*. This appears to directly contradict the reading that Rav Nachman endorses *bitul mekach* for these categories.

Rabbeinu Tam resolves the difficulty with a significant reinterpretation: when the *Gemara* says *ein ona'ah*, it does not mean merely that a one-sixth overcharge is set aside. It means that the exemption from the laws of *ona'ah* extends all the way up to — and including — double the value. So if something is worth 100, charging up to 200 falls within the scope of *ein ona'ah*. Where does this limit of double come from? From the very case mentioned: *zavin be-reish ve-shavei be-me'ah*, sold for 200 and worth 100 — that is *palga*, half the price, i.e., the full doubling. More than double the value — that is what the *Gemara* means by *bitul mekach yesh lahem*. Only when the overcharge exceeds 100% does *bitul mekach* apply even to these categories.

This is admittedly not the straightforward reading of the *Gemara*, whose plain sense is that *bitul mekach* kicks in as soon as the overcharge exceeds one-sixth, just as with ordinary sales. But one can understand what motivates Rabbeinu Tam. It is hard to see how a one-fifth overcharge is so qualitatively different — so radical a departure from a legitimate transaction — that it would constitute *bitul mekach* applicable even to categories that are otherwise exempt from *ona'ah*. Doubling the price is a different matter; there the objective disproportion speaks for itself.

### Rav Nachman

The *Gemara* states clearly that the conclusion transmitted through Rav Chasa — in the name of Rabbi Ami — is *ona'ah ein lahem, bitul mekach yesh lahem*: these categories are exempt from the laws of *ona'ah*, but the voiding of the sale does apply to them.

### Yona & Yirmiya

The *Gemara* then introduces two parallel transmissions in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. Rav Yona stated this rule with reference to *hekdeshos*, and Rabbi Yirmiyah stated it with reference to *karka'os* — and both transmitted it in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ona'ah ein lahem, bitul mekach yesh lahem*. The plain reading of the *Gemara*, as noted, is that *bitul mekach* applies as soon as the overcharge exceeds one-sixth. According to Tosafot, only when the overcharge exceeds 100%.

## Land vs Hekdesh

### Bitul Mekach Scope

Having established that both Rav Yona and Rabbi Yirmiyah said the same thing in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — yet one applied it to *hekdeshos* and the other to *karka'os* — the *Gemara* asks whether this reflects a genuine disagreement. The two categories in the *mishnah* are conceptually quite distinct. One set — *karka*, *avadim*, *shtarot* — is defined by the type of object. The other — *hekdesh* — is not a type of object but a *status* of an object, and it is perhaps better understood as defining the counterparty in the transaction, derived from the verse *achiv o rei'ehu* — one is not doing business with a fellow person but with *hekdesh*.

The *Gemara* resolves the question by noting that the two opinions are not equivalent. The one who said *bitul mekach* applies to *hekdeshos* would certainly agree it applies *kol she-ken* to *karka'os* — the argument for *bitul mekach* is if anything stronger there. But the one who stated the rule only regarding *karka'os* may well hold that *hekdesh* is different — that even gross disproportion does not result in *bitul mekach* by *hekdesh*. Why? Because of Shmuel's teaching: *hekdesh shaveh maneh she-chillelo al shaveh perutah mechullal* — even if *hekdesh* worth a hundred *zuz* is redeemed onto something worth a single *perutah*, the redemption is valid. Since even a grossly disproportionate transfer works by *hekdesh*, there can be no *bitul mekach* — no voiding of the transaction — on grounds of disproportion.

### Rashi on Hekdesh

Rashi explains the rationale behind *hekdesh*'s exemption from *bitul mekach* as follows: no party can truly deceive *hekdesh*, because God is omniscient. When one overcharges a human seller or buyer, the deception operates on a person who could not have agreed had he known the true state of affairs — thus there was no genuine meeting of minds, and the sale is void. But with *hekdesh*, there is no other conscious party to be deceived. God knows what is being done, and therefore the premise that underlies *bitul mekach* — that the other side was misled into a transaction it would never have agreed to — does not apply.

### Kedusha & Pidyon

There is, however, a deeper way to understand why *hekdesh* is different, one that goes beyond Rashi's framing. The key question is whether one thinks of *hekdesh* primarily as an *ownership* entity — like any other institutional property-owner who happens to be the *Beit HaMikdash* — or as a *status*. If *hekdesh* is simply a property-owner, then dealing with its assets is formally similar to dealing with any other party, even if the *Gizbar* (Temple treasurer) rather than a private individual manages the account. If so, the *Gizbar* could theoretically be deceived, or could himself deceive others — which would put pressure on whether *bitul mekach* ought to apply.

But there is a different way to frame *hekdesh*: not as ownership, but as *kedusha* — a sanctified status embedded in the object. Under this view, my car donated to the *Beit HaMikdash* is not simply transferred into an institutional account; it is filled with *kedusha*. Misusing it triggers *me'ilah*. And when I "redeem" it, I am not buying it back from *hekdesh* for a *perutah*. I am *transferring the status of kedusha* from one object to another. That is why Shmuel's teaching makes sense: because I am not buying a thousand-dollar item for a penny, I am relocating *kedusha* from one address to another, and so long as the *kedusha* has a valid destination, the transaction is complete. It is not a *mekach* at all — it is a *pidyon*, a formal transfer of *kedusha* — and therefore the categories of *ona'ah* and *bitul mekach*, which apply to commercial transactions, simply do not engage.

Both framings may in fact be simultaneously true: *hekdesh* is owned by the *Beit HaMikdash* (the *ownership* dimension), and the object possesses *kedusha* (the *status* dimension). The *halacha* may depend on which dimension is operative in a given transaction. When the *Gizbar* picks up a donated car and sells it to a buyer, with the proceeds acquiring *kedusha* and the buyer getting the car — that looks much more like an ordinary *mekach u'memkar*, an ordinary sale. In that case, one might have expected *bitul mekach* to apply. The *Gemara* does not fully unpack all of these distinctions, but establishes the principle that *hekdesh* may well be categorically exempt from *bitul mekach* — whether because there is no party to be deceived (Rashi's framing), or because the transaction can be understood as a *pidyon*, a transfer of *kedusha*, rather than a commercial sale.

## Hekdesh Redemption

### Blemished Animals

The *Gemara* now introduces a *mishnah* from *Temurah*: if an animal designated as *hekdesh* has a blemish and is redeemed, it passes to *chulin* — non-sacred status — but *tzarich la'asot lo damim*, an accounting must be done. If the animal used in the redemption is worth more than the original consecrated animal, the extra constitutes an additional donation to the *Beit HaMikdash*. But if the redeeming animal is worth *less*, the difference must be made up. The formula *tzarich la'asot lo damim* demands that equivalent value be provided. The relevant background is that Rashi explains the language involved was not *zu tachat zu* (the language of *temurah*, of exchange) but *zu mechulelet al zu* — "let this be redeemed upon this" — which is the language of *pidyon*, allowing a *blemished* consecrated animal to be redeemed even upon another animal, not only upon money.

### Value Equivalence

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish disagree about the weight of this accounting requirement. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *yatza le-chulin devar Torah* — the redemption takes full effect at the biblical level as soon as any transfer is made, regardless of whether the values are equivalent. The requirement to supply the difference in value — *tzarich la'asot lo damim* — is only *mi-derabanan*, rabbinic. Reish Lakish disagrees: even *min ha-Torah*, the redemption is not complete until the full equivalent value is provided.

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge. If the case under dispute involves a discrepancy within the *ona'ah* range — one-sixth or less — one would never say that Reish Lakish requires making up that difference at a biblical level. For the *mishnah* itself establishes *hekdesh ein lo ona'ah* — the laws of *ona'ah* do not apply to *hekdesh*. If that means anything, it means that a discrepancy of up to one-sixth is entirely acceptable and creates no obligation of any kind. It would be absurd for Reish Lakish to demand biblical-level restitution in a case that the *mishnah* tells us doesn't trigger even the standard laws of *ona'ah*. (Tosafot adds parenthetically that if animal one was worth a thousand and animal two worth eight hundred, according to Reish Lakish, eight hundred of the original animal is redeemed on animal two, and the remaining two hundred must be supplemented separately — the redemption is partial, not entirely void.)

Therefore the *Gemara* concludes that the dispute must be about a discrepancy in the range of *bitul mekach* — more than one-sixth (or, according to Tosafot, more than double). The question then becomes: does the concept of *bitul mekach* apply to *hekdesh*, and if so, what are its consequences?

The difficulty now turns back on Rabbi Yochanan. Rav Yona transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that *hekdesh* does have *bitul mekach*. If that is so, how can Rabbi Yochanan also hold that the redemption works at the biblical level even when the disproportion is in the *bitul mekach* range — with only a rabbinic requirement to supply the difference? If there is *bitul mekach*, the transaction should not be fundamentally valid, either in full or in part.

At this stage, Tosafot raises an illuminating point. Normally, *bitul mekach* is designed to protect an aggrieved party from being locked into a grossly unfair transaction — especially once the other side has taken the object and disappeared. But with *hekdesh*, that concern does not arise in the same way. *Hekdesh* is always present; *kedusha* is always operative. If two hundred dollars' worth of the original cow remains unredeemed, the *me'ilah* prohibition remains in place on that portion. *Hekdesh* cannot be cheated and left with no recourse. Therefore, Tosafot suggests, *bitul mekach* by *hekdesh* need not mean the transaction is entirely void and must start from scratch. It can mean instead that the redemption is *incomplete* — it proceeds as far as the value supplied, and the remainder awaits further payment. The protections *bitul mekach* is designed to provide are achieved through the ongoing *me'ilah* liability, without needing to unwind the transaction entirely.

### Positions Reversed

Given this difficulty — that Rabbi Yochanan's position (as transmitted by Rav Yona) implies *bitul mekach* applies to *hekdesh*, yet Rabbi Yochanan apparently holds the redemption is fundamentally valid *de-oraisa* — the *Gemara* resolves the tension by reversing the attributions: *ve-ipoch*, switch the positions. What was attributed to Rabbi Yochanan is actually Reish Lakish's position, and vice versa. Under this reversal, it is Rabbi Yochanan who holds — consistent with Rav Yona's transmission — that *bitul mekach* applies to *hekdesh*, meaning the redemption is not complete until the full equivalent value is provided. And Reish Lakish holds there is no *bitul mekach* by *hekdesh*, so even a grossly disproportionate redemption is fundamentally valid *de-oraisa*.

The *Gemara* identifies the underlying substantive disagreement: *mar is lei de-Shmuel, mar leis lei de-Shmuel* — one authority holds of Shmuel's teaching and the other does not. Shmuel held that *hekdesh* worth a *maneh* that was redeemed on something worth a single *perutah* is validly redeemed. If one holds of this teaching, one holds there is no *bitul mekach* by *hekdesh* — even extreme disproportion does not invalidate the transaction. If one rejects Shmuel's teaching in this context, one holds that *bitul mekach* does apply, and an incomplete redemption remains incomplete until the balance is supplied. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish (now in their reversed positions) is thus a debate about whether to accept Shmuel's ruling.

### Intentionality

The *Gemara* then offers a second way of understanding the dispute: *ei ba'is eima*, perhaps both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish accept Shmuel's teaching in principle — that a *perutah* can redeem *hekdesh* worth a *maneh* — and their debate is not about whether Shmuel's rule holds, but about whether it applies *le-chatchila*.

One view holds that while the redemption works *be-di'eved*, it may not be done *le-chatchila*: one is not supposed to redeem a thousand-dollar sanctified object onto an eight-hundred-dollar animal while knowing the values differ. If one did so intending an equivalent exchange, the redemption is not complete until the balance is paid — this is understood as a *de-oraisa* *le-chatchila* obligation. The other view holds that even *le-chatchila*, a disproportionate redemption is permissible.

Tosafot objects to this framing on two grounds. First, there is a moral difficulty: what does it mean to say one may *le-chatchila* shortchange *hekdesh*? Second, the more lenient opinion already holds that *mi-derabanan* one must pay the remaining two hundred — so in what sense is *le-chatchila* being said to be permitted?

Tosafot therefore reinterprets *le-chatchila* not as a question of permission but as a question of *intentionality*. The distinction is between two mental frames: when someone *knowingly* redeems a thousand-dollar object onto a penny — fully aware of the disproportion, deliberately framing the act as a *pidyon*, a transfer of *kedusha* — the redemption works. That is Shmuel's case. But when someone exchanges cow-one for cow-two genuinely *intending* an equivalent exchange, thinking both animals are worth the same, then that person is not performing a *pidyon* — they are attempting a *mekach*, a commercial transaction. And when the transaction is framed as a *mekach*, the rules of equivalent exchange apply, and it is not complete until full value is provided.

This ties back to the fundamental conceptual framework. If one is consciously transferring *kedusha* — aware of the disproportion and choosing to frame the act as a *pidyon* — the *kedusha* transfers because that is all that *pidyon* requires. But if one is genuinely trying to do an equivalent exchange with *hekdesh*, treating it as a commercial counterparty, then *bitul mekach* may apply, and Rabbi Yochanan's position is that the exchange is not fully consummated until true equivalence is achieved. This is what the *Gemara* means, in Tosafot's reading, by the *le-chatchila/be-di'eved* distinction — not a question of what is permitted, but a question of what framework governs based on what the party was consciously trying to accomplish.

## Rav Chisda Stringency

### No Limit for Hekdesh

The *Gemara* now introduces yet another way of understanding the *mishnah*'s statement *ein lahem ona'ah* with respect to *hekdesh*, proposed by Rav Chisda. This interpretation is a striking one. Rav Chisda holds that *einam be-torat ona'ah* — they are not governed by the *laws* of *ona'ah*. But this does not mean they enjoy a *leniency*. It means *l'chumra*: that the protections of *ona'ah* do not apply because something far stricter governs. Even an overcharge of *less* than one-sixth must be returned when dealing with *hekdesh*. *De-afilu pachot mi-khdei ona'ah chozer* — even below the usual threshold, the difference must be repaid.

The practical asymmetry is stark. If *you* overcharge *hekdesh* even slightly, you must return the difference. The house always wins — *yad hekdesh al ha-elyona*. This reinterpretation means that the *mishnah*'s inclusion of *hekdesh* in the list of *ein lahem ona'ah* carries the opposite implication from the other items on the list. For *karka*, *avadim*, and *shtarot*, exclusion from *ona'ah* means the ordinary protections don't apply and parties may be overcharged without consequence. For *hekdesh*, exclusion from *ona'ah* means the ordinary ceiling of one-sixth doesn't apply as a *floor* — even a penny of overcharge must be returned. As was noted regarding similar exclusions in *Bava Kamma*, when the *Torah* says a law doesn't apply to *hekdesh*, it sometimes means the ordinary limitation is removed in *hekdesh*'s favor, not that *hekdesh* is disadvantaged.

This is admittedly a very difficult reading of the *mishnah*. The plain sense of *ein lahem ona'ah* as applied to *hekdesh* would suggest the same leniency as for the other categories on the list. But this reading is driven by the desire to protect *hekdesh*'s interests — a motivation that recurs throughout the *Gemara*'s treatment of this topic.

### Braita Comparison

A *braita* is then introduced: *ribbit ve-ona'ah le-hedyot, ve-ein ribbit ve-ona'ah le-hekdesh* — the laws of *ribbit* and *ona'ah* apply to a commoner but not to *hekdesh*. At first glance, this sounds identical to the *mishnah*, and the *Gemara* asks: is this *braita* any stronger a proof against Rav Chisda than the *mishnah* itself? The answer is no — *mi alima mi-matnitin*? — it is no more authoritative. Just as we reinterpreted the *mishnah* to mean not that *hekdesh* is exempt but that the ordinary *thresholds* don't apply (with even small amounts requiring restitution), we can reinterpret the *braita* in the same fashion: *ein ribbit ve-ona'ah* means the ordinary rules of *ribbit* and *ona'ah* — with their specific limitations and exemptions — don't apply to *hekdesh*, because *hekdesh* is governed by stricter rules.

### Obvious Strictness

The *Gemara* raises an objection to this reading. The *braita* concludes: *ve-zeh chomer be-hedyot mi-be-hekdesh* — and this is a stringency of the commoner over *hekdesh*. That phrase makes sense only if there is genuinely a way in which the laws as applied to commoners are *stricter* than those applied to *hekdesh*. Under the reinterpretation, however, *hekdesh* is in fact stricter in the domain of *ona'ah*, which would call for an additional clause: *ve-zeh chomer be-hekdesh mi-be-hedyot* — and this is a stringency of *hekdesh* over the commoner. Why doesn't the *braita* say that?

The *Gemara* responds: *hachi hashta* — this is no difficulty. The *braita*'s purpose is to highlight the one unusual case where the *commoner* is stricter. That *hekdesh* is generally stricter needs no statement — it is self-evident. But the *ribbit* case, where the ordinary laws apply to a commoner but not to *hekdesh*, represents the rare reversal, and that is worth singling out. To add a clause noting that *hekdesh* is stricter by *ona'ah* would be unremarkable — of course *hekdesh* is stricter. The *braita* is teaching us the one surprising exception where the commoner's law is more demanding, not cataloguing all the ways *hekdesh* is the harder standard.

This remains, however, a radical reinterpretation of both the *mishnah* and the *braita*. The plain sense of the text places *hekdesh* in the *leniency* column along with *karka*, *avadim*, and *shtarot*. The reinterpretation restructures the entire passage to protect *hekdesh*'s interests.

## Ribbit & Hekdesh

### Lending Scenario

The *Gemara* now asks: *ribbit de-hekdesh heichi dami* — what would be a case of *ribbit* applying or not applying to *hekdesh*? The hypothetical suggested is: what if the *gizbar* lent out a hundred dollars from *hekdesh* funds, to be repaid at a hundred and twenty?

This raises an immediate problem, however. The money in the Temple treasury carries *kedusha*. Using consecrated funds for a loan — placing them in private hands without any sanctified object receiving the *kedusha* in return — constitutes *me'ilah*, the prohibited misuse of consecrated property. There is nothing for the *kedusha* to transfer to in a loan transaction, unlike when *hekdesh* funds are used to purchase building materials (where the *kedusha* transfers to the purchased object) or to pay workers after the fact (where the *kedusha* conceptually transfers to the enhanced value of the work done). A simple cash loan leaves the *kedusha* nowhere to go.

### Gizbar Me'ilah

The *Gemara* therefore states: *ve-keivan de-ma'al gizbar, yatzu ma'osav le-chulin, de-havei lei hedyot* — once the *gizbar* commits *me'ilah* by lending out the funds, the money loses its *kedusha* and passes to *chulin* status. At that point it is effectively treated as ordinary (*hedyot*) money, and the normal laws of *ribbit* would apply to any subsequent loan transaction. The scenario dissolves the *hekdesh* status before any interest question arises.

One might ask: even after *me'ilah* removes the *kedusha*, doesn't the *Beit HaMikdash* still *own* the money? And if *hekdesh* is an ownership entity, might the laws applicable to *hekdesh*-owned property — including the *ribbit* exemption — continue to apply even absent *kedusha*? This is precisely the status-versus-ownership question that has run throughout this discussion. At this stage the *Gemara* assumes that once the money is lent out and taken possession of by the borrower, it simultaneously loses both its *kedusha* and its connection to *hekdesh* ownership. The borrower's act of acquisition severs both dimensions at once, and the remaining transaction is governed by ordinary *ribbit* law.

### Flour Contract

Rav Hoshaya offers a viable scenario for how *ribbit* could implicate *hekdesh* without a *me'ilah* problem. The case involves a supplier who contracts with *hekdesh* to supply flour for *menachos* at the going rate of four *se'ah* per unit of currency. The supplier is paid upfront. When the time comes to deliver, the price of flour has risen, so that the going rate is now three *se'ah* per unit. The supplier, having received payment at the four-per-unit rate, now delivers four thousand *se'ah* — but at current prices, *hekdesh*'s payment would have bought only three thousand. *Hekdesh* effectively receives more flour than its money was worth at the time of delivery. This raises a *ribbit*-like concern: is the supplier's delivery of extra units (relative to current market value) a form of prohibited interest on the advance payment received?

The *braita* cited rules that *yad hekdesh al ha-elyona* — *hekdesh* always gets the better rate. Whether prices rose or fell, the supplier must supply at whichever rate benefits *hekdesh*. This is precisely the context in which the question of *ribbit* applied or not applied to *hekdesh* is relevant — and this scenario involves no *me'ilah*, since the transaction concerned is a supply contract, not a cash loan.

### Building Stones

Rav Pappa offers a second scenario that directly illuminates the status-versus-ownership framework. He describes *avnei binyan ha-mesuros le-gizbar* — building stones that have been handed over to the *gizbar* but which have not yet been formally consecrated. This is precisely the case of *hekdesh* owning something without that thing possessing *kedusha*. As Shmuel taught: *bonin be-chol ve-achar kach makdishin* — one builds in ordinary (*chol*) status and only afterward consecrates the completed structure. This practice exists for good reason: a large stone sitting in a quarry awaiting use is likely to be casually leaned upon or used by passersby; if it already had *kedusha*, every such contact would constitute *me'ilah*. The solution is to donate the stone to the *Beit HaMikdash* without yet making it *kadosh*, allow it to be incorporated into the building, and then consecrate the completed structure.

In such a case, *hekdesh* owns the stones without their having any *kedusha*. The *gizbar* can therefore lend them out — effectively loan five stones and receive six in return — without any *me'ilah* issue, since there is no *kedusha* to be violated. And since the transaction is with *hekdesh*, the laws of *ribbit* may not apply. This neatly separates the two dimensions of *hekdesh*: ownership without sanctity, showing that both can exist independently.

## Other Exclusions

### Kefel Exclusions

The *Gemara* now works through a series of additional exclusions, moving at considerable speed. It states that there is no *kefel* — no double payment — for theft of *karka*, *avadim*, *shtarot*, or *hekdesh*. The source is derived hermeneutically. The relevant *pesukim* employ the structure of *klal u-frat u-klal* — a general statement, followed by specifics, followed by a return to the general. The specifics — *shor, chamor, seh, salma* — define the scope: *davar ha-mitaltel ve-gufo mamon*, movable property with intrinsic value. Anything that does not fit this profile is excluded. *Karka'os* are excluded because they are not movable; *avadim* are linked to *karka* by rabbinic tradition; *shtarot*, though physically movable, lack intrinsic value — their worth lies in the debt they evidence, not in the paper itself; and *hekdesh* is excluded by the word *rei'ehu* — "his fellow" — which excludes *hekdesh* as a counterparty.

The *Gemara* further notes that since *hekdesh* is excluded from *kefel*, it is likewise excluded from the four-and-five payment (*tashlumei arba'ah va-chamishah*) applicable to one who steals and slaughters or sells a *shor* or *seh*. The reasoning is straightforward: the four-and-five consists of the base value plus *kefel* plus the additional payment; once *kefel* is removed, only the base value and the additional payment remain — yielding three-and-four — a combination that has no independent *halachic* standing. Exclusion from *kefel* thus entails exclusion from four-and-five.

### Shomer Chinam

The *Gemara* next derives that a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid watchman — takes no *shevua* with respect to these categories. The derivation again proceeds by *klal u-frat u-klal*: the verse *ki yiten ish el rei'ehu* — "when a man gives to his fellow" — is a general; *kesef o kelim* — silver or utensils — is the specific; and a subsequent phrase returns to the general. The *prat* again defines the scope as movable property with intrinsic value. *Karka'os* are excluded as non-movable; *avadim* as linked to *karka*; *shtarot* as lacking intrinsic value; and *hekdesh* by the word *rei'ehu* — one gives to one's fellow, not to *hekdesh*.

### Shomer Sachar

The same analysis is applied to the *shomer sachar* — the paid watchman — who is likewise not obligated to pay for theft or loss of these categories. A third set of *pesukim* employs the same *klal-frat-klal* structure: *ki yiten ish el rei'ehu* as the general, *chamor o shor o seh* as the specific, *ve-chol beheimah lishmor* as the return to the general. The same derivations follow: *karka'os*, *avadim*, *shtarot*, and *hekdesh* are all excluded, each for the same reason as before.

### Negligence Liability

Before concluding, Tosafot on the *shomer chinam* raises a question that was touched upon in the previous day's discussion: if a *shomer chinam* or *shomer sachar* is negligent with respect to these excluded categories — *karka*, *avadim*, *shtarot*, or *hekdesh* — does liability for negligence attach?

Tosafot is inclined to hold that it does not. He argues that if even a *shomer sachar* who is negligent would be exempt, then *a fortiori* a *shomer chinam* would be as well. He cites a *Gemara* in support. However, this position is not without difficulty. The exemptions derived from the *pesukim* concern the specific liabilities of *shomrim* — the oath of the *shomer chinam*, the payment obligations of the *shomer sachar* for theft and loss. These are the formal *halachos* of the watchman relationship. Negligence, however, might be conceived differently: once a party undertakes a duty of care, a failure to exercise that care might constitute not a violation of *shomer* law but an act of *mazzik* — direct damage. If negligence is categorized as *mazzik* rather than as a *shomer* liability, the exclusions derived from the *pesukim* governing *shomrim* would not apply, and liability could still follow.

Tosafot raises this possibility and is inclined to resolve it in favor of exemption, but the question remains genuinely contested. All the *pesukim* establish is that these categories are excluded from *shevua* and from payment for *gneivah va-aveidah*. Whether a *shomer* who is negligent — and whose negligence might be analyzed as damage rather than as a watchman's failure — would pay from the category of *mazzik* is a distinct question that the derivations from the *pesukim* do not necessarily resolve.
