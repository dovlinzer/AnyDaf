# Nedarim 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Father-Husband

### Na'arah Status

Today's daf is 68. As with yesterday, there is relatively little *gemara* text here — much of the page is occupied by *Ran* — and the substantive *halachic* discussion follows closely in the spirit of what we saw on the preceding daf.

By way of review: the *mishnah* introduces the case of a girl who has not yet reached *bogeret* status, meaning she is still a *na'arah* — or in some circumstances even a *ketanah* whose *neder* is halakhically binding. Such a girl is considered, both physically and, more importantly, *halachically*, to be in her father's house. This remains true even after *kiddushin*, and continues until *nissu'in* — until the formal transfer at *chuppah* when she fully enters her husband's domain.

### Darsha Method

Nevertheless, *Chazal* read one of the *parashiyos* in the *Torah* that discusses the husband or father annulling a *neder* in a striking way. That *parasha* is understood to describe a case where the father and husband are *partners* in the act of *hafara* — specifically when the girl is a *na'arah murasah*, a betrothed minor. One might have expected that in this period, since she is still legally in her father's house, the father alone would hold the power of *hafara*. Yet *Chazal* read the *Torah* to teach that the husband participates as well, and that the two together annul the vow.

### Two Principles

Counterintuitively, however, even though the *parasha* the *gemara* draws upon speaks primarily of the husband, the father remains the primary and more central figure. Two important consequences flow from this. First, the *hafara* applies even to vows that predate the *kiddushin*, because it is fundamentally the father's authority that is operative. Second, if the girl has no father — if she is a *yesomah* with *kiddushin* — the husband alone cannot annul the *neder*. The father is indispensable.

## Two Categories

### Braita Reading

At the top of 68a, the *gemara* cites a *braita*: *tanya devei Rabbi Ishmael* — from the school of Rabbi Ishmael the following was taught. The *pasuk* reads: *bein ish l'ishto bein av l'vito b'neureha beis aviha* — "between a man and his wife, between a father and his daughter, in her youth in her father's house." The question is posed: the *Torah* has already stated *av l'vito* — why add *b'neureha beis aviha*? The plain reading of the *pasuk* would be that the section about the father applies when she is in his house, and the section about the husband applies after she marries. What, then, is the additional phrase teaching?

The *braita* answers: *mikan l'na'arah murasah she'aviha u'va'alah mefirin nedareha* — from here we derive the case of the *na'arah murasah*, whose father and husband together annul her vows. The *pasuk* is read not as describing two mutually exclusive scenarios but as identifying a single case that is simultaneously *bein ish l'ishto* and *bein av l'vito* — when she is a *na'arah* in her father's house who also has a husband. That is the *na'arah murasah*.

The *gemara* then asks: according to this *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Ishmael, what does he do with the other *pasuk* — *v'hayah tihyeh l'ish v'nedareha aleiha*? The answer is that he uses it for the other teaching of Rabbah, which we will encounter in approximately two *dafim*. That *drasha* addresses the case where one *arus* dies and she subsequently becomes betrothed to another: the double language of *v'hayah tihyeh* signals that the father's full authority over her — which he exercised before the first betrothal — is restored in the interval before the second.

### Inuy Nefesh

The *gemara* proceeds to ask: and what does Rabbah himself do with the *pasuk* of *bein ish l'ishto bein av l'vito*? The answer is that he uses it to derive a crucial *halacha* about the scope of the husband's power of *hafara*: what categories of vows can a husband annul?

The *Torah* repeatedly employs the phrase *le'esor issar al nafshah* — to make something forbidden upon herself — and the related *issar le'annot nefesh*, which refers to a type of self-denial or self-affliction. This is the same root used on *Yom Kippur* — *te'anu et nafshoteichem*. This explicit language establishes *inuy nefesh* — affliction-type vows — as one category the husband may annul.

### Devarim Shebeino

The second category is not stated explicitly but is derived. There are vows that do not rise to the threshold of *inuy nefesh* but that directly affect the dynamics of the husband-wife relationship. For example, she takes a vow that forbids her certain garments or modes of personal appearance. That may not constitute *inuy nefesh* in the strict sense, yet it plainly affects the marital relationship. For this second category — *devarim she'beino l'veinah*, matters between them — the *Torah*'s phrase *bein ish l'ishto* serves as the source. Thus the two categories the husband may annul are: *inuy nefesh* and *devarim she'beino l'veinah*.

### Ran vs. Rambam

An interesting question now arises: do these same two categories — and hence the same *limitations* — apply to the father as well? Or is the father's authority over his daughter absolute, unlimited by category?

The *Ran* cites the *Yerushalmi* explicitly. The *Yerushalmi* asks: we know that *inuy nefesh* vows fall under the husband's power, and that *devarim she'beino l'veinah* are derived from *bein ish l'ishto* — but what is the source for the father? The *Yerushalmi*'s answer is precise: just as the husband may only annul vows of *inuy nefesh* and *devarim she'beino l'veinah*, so too the father may only annul vows in those same two categories. The *Ran* states this explicitly.

The *Rambam*, however, takes a different position. In his view, the father's authority over his daughter is categorical and unlimited — he may annul any *neder* she takes, without restriction to these two categories. This is not made explicit in the *Bavli*, and the *Rambam* understands the father's control to be absolute. As the *Ran* notes, this is a direct *machloket Rishonim*, and it presumably reflects a deeper question about the extent of parental and spousal authority as understood by *Chazal*.

## Hafara Mechanics

### Question Setup

The *gemara* now raises a conceptual question about the mechanics of *hafara* in this partnership: *ba'al megez gayiz o miklesh kalesh?* When the husband — or, by extension, either partner — performs *hafara*, does he *cut off* half of the *neder*, or does he merely *weaken* it?

The *megez gayiz* position holds that each partner's act of *hafara* has an independent and concrete effect: each one cuts off his half. Under this view, if one partner does *hafara* and the other does not, something real has been accomplished — half the *neder* has been annulled, even if the other half remains. The *miklesh kalesh* position holds instead that the act of *hafara* by one party weakens the *neder* as a whole but does not sever a portion of it. The entire *neder* remains, just in a diminished state.

### Two Olives Case

The *gemara* illustrates the practical stakes with a concrete case. Suppose she took a vow not to eat two specific olives. The *arus* heard and performed *hafara*. She then ate both olives. If *megez gayiz* is correct — if he cut off half — then his *hafara* effectively released her from the prohibition on one of the two olives. She thus remained forbidden in one olive, ate it, and would be liable to *malkos*. But if *miklesh kalesh* is correct — if he only weakened the *neder* — then the entire prohibition remained in some attenuated form, and eating both olives would constitute a violation, but not one of sufficient weight to generate *malkos*.

### Student Challenge

One might raise a more fundamental objection: why assume that either partner's *hafara* has any independent effect at all? The structure of the partnership might suggest that nothing occurs until *both* parties act — like a system that requires two keys to be turned simultaneously before anything happens. If one key is turned without the other, nothing changes. This challenge is a serious one, and the *gemara* does not address it conceptually. Instead, it brings a *braita* as a proof-text and resolves the question from there.

## Father as Anchor

### Principle Statement

The *braita* opens by articulating the foundational principle: *met ha-ba'al nidrokna reshut la'av; met ha'av v'lo nidrokna reshut la'ba'al* — when the husband dies, his authority empties out to the father; when the father dies, his authority does not transfer to the husband. This is simply another formulation of the theme we have been developing: the father is the *anchor* of the *hafara* partnership. If the husband dies, full control reverts to the father — who was always the primary party anyway. But if the father dies, the husband is left without the essential partner, and his own rights cannot stand alone.

### Husband Dies Case

The *braita* now unpacks when it is that the husband's death empties his authority to the father. The answer: *bizman she-lo shama ha-ba'al kodem she-yamut* — when the husband had not heard the *neder* before he died, or when he heard and was silent, or when he heard and actively performed *hafara*.

### Three Scenarios

Three scenarios are thus identified where the husband did nothing problematic prior to dying. First, he never heard the *neder* at all. Second, he heard but died within that same day — meaning his silence never extended to a full period that would constitute *kiyum*. Third, he heard and actively performed *hafara*. In all three cases, he either did nothing or did nothing harmful, and the father may now take full control and annul the *neder*.

### Kiyum Problem

There are, however, two bad scenarios. The first is that the husband heard and performed an explicit *kiyum* — he actively confirmed the *neder*. The second is that he heard, remained silent for a full day's period, and then died — making his silence effectively a *kiyum* by inaction, a kind of pocket *kiyum*. In either of these cases, *ein yachol le-hafer* — even though he has now died and the father is the anchor, the husband has already put a stop to the possibility of *hafara*. That cannot be undone.

## Father Dies Case

### Father Hafara

The *braita* now turns to the reverse scenario: the father dies and the husband cannot annul, even when the father had already performed active *hafara*. The case: the father heard the *neder* and performed *hafara*, but the husband did not hear about it before the father died. One might reason that this case is particularly strong for the *neder* being annulled — the husband was not yet in the picture at all, the father alone acted, and the father did everything he was supposed to do. Perhaps the husband can now simply finalize what was all but completed?

The *Ran* notes that the *chidush* here is precisely to reject this reasoning. Despite the father having performed his *hafara*, despite the husband being entirely absent and peripheral, *met ha'av lo nidrokna* — the rights do not pass to the husband. The father is the anchor, and the *hafara* requires the father's presence all the way through. If the father dies, there is no completion. The husband cannot finish what the father began.

### New Vow

If she takes an entirely new *neder* after the father has died, the husband similarly cannot annul it — not until *nissu'in*. This is a point that underscores how thoroughly the husband's *hafara* authority is contingent on the father. Even if the two of them were the only parties involved, and she makes the *neder* after *kiddushin* but before *nissu'in*, he has no independent standing to annul. And even once they do marry and *nissu'in* occurs, he cannot annul retroactively a *neder* from before *nissu'in*, because retroactive *hafara* of prior vows is not within his power at that point.

### Partnership Required

The *braita* continues with a case that uses the explicit language of *shutafut*: *she'ein ha-ba'al meifer ela b'shutafut* — the husband can only annul as part of a *partnership* with the father. Here the father heard the *neder* and performed *hafara*, but the husband had not heard, and then the father died. Since the vow was taken while both the father and the husband were in the picture — even if the husband had not yet engaged with it — there remains a portion of the *hafara* that conceptually belongs to the husband. The father cannot supply that on his own after the husband's death, and certainly the husband cannot supply the father's portion. Without the *shutafut*, the *hafara* is incomplete.

## Father Takeover

### Scenario Setup

The *braita* now reaches its final and most complex case. The father heard the *neder* and performed *hafara*. The husband, however, died without ever having heard the *neder* at all — not just without having responded to it, but without even knowing it existed. This is an extreme case of the husband's non-involvement. One might argue that since he was never in the picture at all, the father's *hafara* should simply operate as though there were no husband — as the dominant and essentially exclusive party.

### Shammai v. Hillel

This case is disputed by *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*.

### Shammai View

*Beis Shammai* holds: *chozer ha-av u-meifer chelko shel ba'al* — the father goes back and annuls the husband's portion. Notably, even *Beis Shammai* does not say the *neder* is simply annulled automatically now that the husband is gone. Rather, there is still a distinct act required: the father must perform an additional *hafara* specifically for the portion that would have been the husband's. Since the *neder* was taken while there was a *arus*, there is a portion of it that had been under his purview. Even though he never acted on it, that portion was not dissolved by the father's initial *hafara*. Upon the husband's death, the father now assumes that right and can annul it with a further act.

### Rabbi Natan

Rabbi Natan clarifies: this is the position of *Beis Shammai*, not *Beis Hillel*.

### Hillel View

*Beis Hillel* says: *ein yachol lehafer* — the father cannot annul the husband's portion. According to *Beis Hillel*, when the groom was in the picture and died, the right that was his does not simply pass to the father. Nobody now steps into the groom's role, and consequently the *neder* cannot be fully annulled. The girl remains bound by it — which, as one might note, may in some cases actually be her preference.

## Shma Mina

### Conclusion

The *gemara* now returns to its original question about *hafara* mechanics and draws the answer directly from this *machloket*. *Shma mina leBeis Shammai megez gayiz, leBeis Hillel miklesh kalesh* — we may conclude from this debate: *Beis Shammai* holds *megez gayiz*, that each party's *hafara* cuts off his half; *Beis Hillel* holds *miklesh kalesh*, that each party's *hafara* merely weakens the *neder* as a whole. *Shma mina* — this is the resolution.

### Ran Explanation

The *Ran* explains what precisely is at stake in each position, and why the *macholoket* about *hafara* mechanics maps onto the *macholoket* about whether the father can take over the husband's portion. The key concept the *Ran* introduces is *yerusha* — inheritance or transfer of rights. The question of whether the father can step into the husband's role is modeled on whether a *right* can be transferred, which in turn depends on whether that right is sufficiently concrete and substantial to function like a transferable object.

### Shammai Logic

According to *Beis Shammai*, the father's initial *hafara* annulled precisely his half — *chelko bilvad*. The husband's half was entirely unaffected — *lo nigrah klal* — it remained completely intact. That means there is something whole and substantial that was the husband's: the full right to annul the *neder* with respect to his portion, equivalent to one of the two olives in the earlier example. Because that right is intact and concrete — *chashuv hu* — it can be transferred and inherited by the father when the husband dies.

### Hillel Logic

According to *Beis Hillel*, when the father performed *hafara*, he did not sever a clean half. Rather, he weakened the *neder* as a whole — *miklesh kalesh* — affecting both portions together. What the husband is left with is therefore not a full, intact right to annul his half, but merely the right to act on a vow that has already been weakened. That is not substantial enough to constitute a transferable object — *eino chashuv lehorisho la'av*. Furthermore, since the father is still alive when this weakening occurs, his own portion of the *hafara* has not yet fully taken effect either; the *neder* sits in a weakened state. A weakened right is all the more incapable of being transferred, and thus the father cannot inherit it.

The *Ran* articulates the underlying model clearly: we are using the framework of *yerusha* — of transmissible rights — and for that framework to apply, the thing being transferred must be sufficiently object-like. A half-annulment that is whole and concrete can be conceptualized as a thing and passed on. An attenuation of the whole, with no clean half belonging to anyone, cannot.
