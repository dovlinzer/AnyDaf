# Gittin 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

### Rabbah's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁטָר – דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה לֹא נִפְדָּתָה אוֹ חוּפְשָׁה לֹא נִתַּן לָהּ״ – מַקִּישׁ שְׁטָר לְכֶסֶף, מָה כֶּסֶף – בֵּין כּוּלּוֹ בֵּין חֶצְיוֹ, אַף שְׁטָר נָמֵי – בֵּין כּוּלּוֹ בֵּין חֶצְיוֹ; וְרַבָּנַן גָּמְרִי ״לָהּ״–״לָהּ״ מֵאִשָּׁה – מָה אִשָּׁה חֶצְיָהּ לֹא, אַף עֶבֶד נָמֵי חֶצְיוֹ לֹא; אֲבָל בְּכֶסֶף – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל קָנָה, פְּדוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ פְּדוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabba said: Their dispute pertains only to a case where the master emancipated him with a bill of manumission, as Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds:** In the context of a Jewish man who engages in sexual intercourse with a maidservant who had been designated to cohabit with a Hebrew slave, the Torah writes that neither the Jewish man nor the maidservant is liable to receive the death penalty, though the man is liable to bring a guilt-offering. If the woman had been emancipated, she would be a regular married woman, and they would incur the death penalty for their act of intercourse. In describing the woman in that case, the Torah writes: **"And not at all redeemed, nor was freedom given to her"** (Leviticus 19:20). This is referring to two methods of emancipating a slave: Redemption via money, in the phrase: "And not at all redeemed," and emancipation via a bill of manumission, in the phrase: "Nor was freedom given her." **The verse juxtaposes the freeing of a slave via a bill of manumission to the freeing of a slave via payment of money, to teach that just as with money the slave can free either all of him or half of him, for if he were to pay half of his value to the master he would be half-redeemed, so too, by receiving a bill of manumission either all of him or half of him can be emancipated. And the Rabbis derive their opinion, that a slave cannot acquire half of his freedom via a bill of manumission, from a verbal analogy of the word "her [*lah*]" written here: "Nor was freedom given to her [*lah*]" (Leviticus 19:20), and the word "her [*lah*]" written with regard to a man who divorces a woman: "And he writes her [*lah*] a scroll of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:1). Just as in the case of a woman, one is not able to divorce half of her with a bill of divorce, so too, in the case of a slave, one is not able to emancipate half of him with a bill of manumission. However, with regard to a slave who frees himself by giving money, everyone agrees that the slave acquires half of himself, and a female slave would also be partially redeemed but not fully redeemed.**

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 41b, in the midst of the debate between Rabbi and the Rabbanan over whether a slave can be half-freed. Two approaches had been proposed. Rabbah argued that the dispute concerns the *shtar* — the documentary act of freedom — which pertains to the slave's personal status. By contrast, with regard to *kessef*, monetary redemption, which concerns the proprietary dimension of the slave, all agree that a partial redemption is effective. The *shtar*, which transforms personal status, is precisely where Rabbi and the Rabbanan diverge: Rabbi holds it can work partially; the Rabbanan maintain it cannot.

### Ulla's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּכֶסֶף – דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה לֹא נִפְדָּתָה״ – פְּדוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ פְּדוּיָה; וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם; אֲבָל בִּשְׁטָר – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef said: The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis pertains only to a case where the slave was freed via money, for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds:** The *halakha* that a slave can be half-freed can be derived from the phrase: **"And not at all redeemed [*vehofde lo nifdata*]"** (Leviticus 19:20), indicating that she has been partially **redeemed but not** fully **redeemed. And the Rabbis hold: The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and the repetition of the verb in the verse is not to be used as a source for deriving a *halakha*. **However,** when the slave is half-emancipated **via a bill of manumission, everyone agrees that** the slave has **not acquired** himself and remains a full slave.

Ulla inverts this framework entirely, contending that a *shtar* obviously cannot take effect partially — the dispute, in his view, concerns *kessef*. The question of whether monetary redemption can be applied halfway is what divides Rabbi from the Rabbanan.

### Shifcha Canaanis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּכֶסֶף, בִּשְׁטָר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה לֹא נִפְדָּתָה אוֹ חוּפְשָׁה לֹא נִתַּן לָהּ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכָתַב לָהּ סֵפֶר כְּרִיתוּת״ – מָה לְהַלָּן בִּשְׁטָר, אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁטָר. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חֶצְיוֹ בְּכֶסֶף, אוֹ כּוּלּוֹ בִּשְׁטָר; חֶצְיוֹ בִּשְׁטָר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה לֹא נִפְדָּתָה אוֹ חוּפְשָׁה לֹא נִתַּן לָהּ״ – מַקִּישׁ שְׁטָר לְכֶסֶף, מָה כֶּסֶף בֵּין כּוּלּוֹ בֵּין חֶצְיוֹ, אַף שְׁטָר נָמֵי בֵּין כּוּלּוֹ בֵּין חֶצְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: **And I have derived only that she can be partially redeemed via money. From where do I derive that she can also be emancipated via a bill of manumission? The verse states: "And not at all redeemed, nor was freedom given to her"** (Leviticus 19:20), **and later on, in the case of divorce, it states: "And he writes her a scroll of severance"** (Deuteronomy 24:1). **Just as later on freedom is granted via a bill of divorce, so too here, a maidservant is freed via a bill of manumission. And I have derived only that half of him can be redeemed via money, as detailed above, or that all of him can be emancipated via a bill of manumission. From where do I derive that half of him can be emancipated via a bill of manumission? The verse states: "And not at all redeemed, nor was freedom given her," and thereby juxtaposes a bill of manumission to money. Just as a slave can be either fully redeemed or half-redeemed via money, so too, he can be either fully emancipated or half-emancipated via a bill of manumission.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* drawn from the *parsha* of a *shifcha kena'anis* — a case in which a man is liable for relations with a non-Jewish slave woman who had been partially designated to another. The *pasuk* reads: *vehispaday lo nifsasah o chuphshah lo nittan lah* — "she has not been redeemed and her freedom has not been given to her." Rather than reading this as an emphatic absolute, as one might read *nakei lo yenakeh* ("he shall surely not be cleansed"), the *gemara* reads it as indicating a partial state: *vehispaday* — she has been redeemed; *lo nifsasah* — she has not been redeemed. The resulting status is *piduyah ve'eina piduyah*: she is half-redeemed. Crucially, the *baraita* specifies that this partial redemption operates *bekessef uveshvei kessef* — through money or its equivalent. This establishes the principle that at least monetary redemption can function in a partial manner.

### Verses Analysis

From the *baraita*'s opening, we know only that partial freedom can be achieved through money — *ein li ela bekessef*. How do we know that a *shtar* can also effect freedom? The *baraita* derives this from the continuation of the same *pasuk*: *o chuphshah lo nittan lah*, "or her freedom has not been given to her." The *lah lah* comparison — a classic *gezerah shavah* linking the *lah* here to the *lah* in the verse *vechasav lah sefer kerisus*, which concerns a woman's bill of divorce — teaches that just as a woman is divorced by a writ, so too a slave is freed by a writ.

This yields the baseline: a slave can be freed both by money and by *shtar*. But the *baraita* goes further. From the monetary case we know partial freedom is achievable; but what of partial freedom by *shtar*? The verse contains both clauses — *vehispaday lo nifsasah* and *o chuphshah lo nittan lah* — and the *baraita* links them through *makish shtar lekessef*: the *shtar* is analogized to *kessef*. Just as monetary redemption can be total or partial, so too documentary freedom can be total or partial. This *baraita* is thus thoroughly consistent with Rabbi's position.

### Baraita Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב יוֹסֵף בָּתַר דְּאִיתּוֹתַב, הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּה – רֵישָׁא דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי?! אָמַר לָךְ רַבָּה: אִין, רֵישָׁא דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: רַבִּי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita* in light of the earlier opinions. **Granted, according to Rav Yosef after he was conclusively refuted,** who now holds that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis disagree both with regard to money and a bill of manumission, in accordance with **whose opinion is this baraita? It is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, for half of the slave is freed, whether via money or a bill of manumission. However, according to the opinion of Rabba, that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis disagree only with regard to a bill of manumission, but everyone agrees that the slave can redeem half of himself with money, do all agree with the statement of the first clause, but the last clause is in accordance with the opinion of only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? Rabba could have said to you: Yes, all agree with the statement of the first clause, and the last clause is in accordance with only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Rav Ashi said: This baraita is entirely in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.**

The *gemara* observes that according to Rav Yosef — who, after being challenged, conceded that the debate touches upon *shtar*, and then argued that it also extends to *kessef* — this *baraita* presents no difficulty: it is simply the view of Rabbi, while the Rabbanan would reject both halves of it.

The difficulty arises for Rabbah. According to Rabbah, partial redemption through *kessef* is a point of universal agreement, while the dispute concerns only *shtar*. If so, why does the *baraita* first establish the *kessef* rule as if it were an innovation, and then derive the *shtar* rule from it? Is the opening of the *baraita* *divrei hakol* while the conclusion is exclusively Rabbi's position?

Rava resolves this straightforwardly: yes, precisely that — *reisha divrei hakol, seifa Rebbi*. The first half, that partial monetary redemption is valid, is universally accepted. The second half, that a *shtar* can likewise effect partial freedom, represents Rabbi's contested position. The *baraita* naturally opens with the uncontested premise and then uses it as a platform for Rabbi's novel ruling.

Rav Ashi offers a slightly different formulation: the entire *baraita* is Rabbi's, and Rabbi himself is the one who opens with the agreed-upon *kessef* rule before extending it to *shtar*. There is nothing unusual about an author building from common ground toward his own distinctive position.

## Half-Slave Status

### Mishna Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבָּה – מוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּכֶסֶף, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל; אֶלָּא לְרַב יוֹסֵף – לֵימָא רַבִּי הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן?! אָמַר רָבִינָא: בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to the mishna that teaches: In the case of one who is a half-slave half-freeman, granted, according to the opinion of Rabba, he establishes the mishna as referring to a case where the slave was half-redeemed with money, and everyone agrees that this is effective. However, according to the opinion of Rav Yosef, shall we say that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and not in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis?** According to the opinion of Rav Yosef, the Rabbis hold that a slave cannot be half-free, regardless of whether he is freed via money or a bill of manumission. **Ravina said: The mishna is referring to a slave belonging to two partners, and everyone agrees that each one of them can emancipate his portion of the slave. Consequently, there could be a half-slave half-freeman even according to Rav Yosef's understanding of the opinion of the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* turns to the *mishna*, which addresses a person who is half-slave and half-free — *mi she-chetzyo eved ve-chetzyo ben chorin* — and the question of whether the master is compelled to free him so that he may marry a woman and have children. According to Rava, the *mishna* poses no difficulty: it can be attributed to *divrei hakol*, since the half-slave status arose through partial monetary redemption, which all agree is valid.

The difficulty falls on Rav Yosef. Having maintained that the Rabbanan reject not only partial *shtar* freedom but also partial monetary redemption, Rav Yosef faces a *mishna* premised on the very phenomenon the Rabbanan supposedly deny. Can the *mishna* really represent only Rabbi's minority view and not the Rabbanan?

### Joint Ownership

Ravina resolves the difficulty — as Rashi had already noted on the earlier *mishna* — by identifying the *mishna*'s case as one of joint ownership: the slave belongs to two masters, and one of them frees his portion entirely. Each master divests himself completely of his own half-ownership. Since the act of the freeing master is total with respect to what he owns, even the Rabbanan agree it is effective. The resulting anomaly — that the slave is half-owned by a second master and half-free — arises not from a deficient act of liberation but from an external constraint on the outcome.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחֲלוֹקֶת – בְּשֶׁשִּׁיחְרֵר חֶצְיוֹ וְהִנִּיחַ חֶצְיוֹ; אֲבָל שִׁיחְרֵר חֶצְיוֹ, וּמָכַר חֶצְיוֹ אוֹ נָתַן בְּמַתָּנָה חֶצְיוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּקָנָפֵיק מִינֵּיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses another dispute with regard to one who emancipates half of his slave. **Rabba said: The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis concerning whether the slave can be half-emancipated applies only when the master freed half of him and left the other half of him unaffected. However, if he freed half of him and sold the other half of him, or gave the other half of him as a gift to someone else, then, since the slave left him entirely, as the original master no longer owns any portion of the slave, everyone agrees that the slave has acquired half of his freedom.**

Rava now offers a more expansive formulation of the same principle: the dispute between Rabbi and the Rabbanan is confined to the case where a single master frees half and *retains* half — *shichrer chetzyo ve-hiniach chetzyo*. But if the master frees half and *sells* half, or gifts half to another person — *shichrer chetzyo u-machar chetzyo o nasan be-matanah chetzyo* — then, since he completely divests himself of the slave, *keivan de-nafik minei kulei*, even the Rabbanan concede that the act is effective, producing a half-slave half-free individual.

Rashi explains the underlying logic: the defect the Rabbanan identify in a partial *shtar* is not that it produces an intermediate status but that the act itself is incomplete — akin to a husband who divorces his wife while retaining some hold over her. The act of *shichrur* must mirror the totality of *gerushin*: a complete severance. Accordingly, even if the result is that the slave is now half-owned by someone else, what matters is that the master's own act of liberation was absolute. On this reading, simultaneously selling half and freeing half in a single moment also qualifies, since at the conclusion of the act the master retains nothing.

### Estate Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּבְכוּלּוֹ – לָא פְּלִיגִי?! וְהָתָנֵי חֲדָא: הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לִשְׁנֵי עֲבָדָיו – קָנוּ וּמְשַׁחְרְרִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ, הָאוֹמֵר: ״כׇּל נְכָסַי נְתוּנִין לִפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֲבָדַיי״ – אַף עַצְמָם לֹא קָנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And do they not disagree with regard to a case where he is released entirely? But it is taught in one baraita: In the case of one who writes his property to his two slaves, i.e., gives it to them via a document, they acquired the property and they free each other, because each one has ownership over half of the other slave. And it was taught in another baraita that in the case of one who says: All of my property is given to so-and-so and so-and-so my slaves, they did not acquire even themselves, and all the more so they did not acquire the property. Seemingly, the two baraitot contradict each other.**

Abaye challenges Rava's position with a *baraita*: one who writes over his entire estate to two of his slaves — *ha-kosev nechasav li-shnei avadav* — produces a situation in which each slave receives half the estate including half of the other slave. Since each is now half-free (by virtue of having received half of himself), each can own property and hold the other slave's half-ownership, and so *hem meshachrerim zeh es zeh* — they free one another by writing writs of liberation to each other. This *baraita* seems to validate Rava's view that full divestiture in a half-half arrangement works even according to the Rabbanan.

But a second *baraita* complicates matters: *ha-omer kol nechasai nesunim li-peloni u-peloni avadai* — one who says "all my property is given to Reuven and Shimon, my slaves" — *af atzman lo kanu*, they do not acquire even themselves. This sounds identical to the first case, yet the result is the opposite.

The *gemara* first proposes attributing the two *baraitot* to different authorities: the first to Rabbi, whose view permits partial *shichrur*; the second to the Rabbanan, who deny it. But the *gemara* then rejects this and argues that both *baraitot* can reflect the Rabbanan's view.

### Document Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו הָא רַבִּי, וְהָא רַבָּנַן?! לָא, אִידִי וְאִידִי רַבָּנַן; הָא דְּאָמַר כּוּלּוֹ, הָא דְּאָמַר חֲצִי חֲצִי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּשְׁטָר אֶחָד, כָּאן בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues his question: **What, is it not that the way to reconcile the baraitot is to say that this, the first baraita, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and that, the second baraita, is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis?** The Gemara offers a different reconciliation: **No, both this and that baraita are in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. This first baraita is discussing a case where one said that he is giving all of his property to each one of the slaves. Since he gave everything to both of them, they each acquire the property, including each other, emancipate each other, and divide the property between them. That second baraita is discussing a case where one said that he is giving half of his property to one slave and half of it to the other, so neither is fully emancipated. And if you wish, say instead that it is not difficult. Here, in the second baraita, where they did not acquire the property, this is a case where the master wrote everything in one document. The reason the slaves do not acquire themselves is that it is not possible to emancipate two slaves with one document. There, in the first baraita, which rules that they did acquire the property, the case is one where the master wrote the transfer of property in two documents.**

The resolution turns on two distinct variables. The first concerns the linguistic formula used: *kulhu* versus *chatzi chatzi*. When the master says to each slave "I give you *all* my property," each slave in principle receives the totality — including the full person of himself. Since, in one valid sequential arrangement, this would work (if you gave everything to one slave, he would own himself and be free), it also works simultaneously. But when the master says "I give you *half*" to each slave, this can never work sequentially: half-freeing a slave effects nothing in sequence, and therefore, by the same logic, it effects nothing simultaneously either. This distinction — articulated by Tosafot — underlies the difference between the two *baraitot*.

The second resolution (*ve-iba'it eima*) focuses on the number of documents: *kan bi-shtar echad kan bi-shnei shtarot*. A single *shtar* addressed jointly to two slaves is analogous to a single *get* issued to two wives simultaneously — a technical invalidity. Thus the case of *af atzman lo kanu* may involve a single document, which is defective for this reason alone, irrespective of the language used. Two separate *shtarot*, by contrast, each addressed to a single slave, would avoid this defect entirely — and according to Rava, if the formula is *kulhu* (all to each), even the Rabbanan would concede that it works.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁטָר אֶחָד, מַאי אִירְיָא חֲצִי חֲצִי? אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר כּוּלּוֹ נָמֵי לֹא קָנוּ! הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: אַף עַצְמָן לֹא קָנוּ, בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּשְׁטָר אֶחָד, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת – קָנוּ; וְאִם אָמַר חֲצִי חֲצִי – אַף בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת נָמֵי לָא קָנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the second baraita is referring to a case where he wrote everything in one document, why specifically mention that the slaves do not acquire themselves when he gave half to one and half to the other? Even if he said all of it as well, they did not acquire the property, as was taught explicitly in the beginning of the baraita. The Gemara answers: That is also what the tanna is saying: They did not acquire even themselves. In what case is this statement said? In a case where the master wrote everything in one document. But if he wrote it in two documents, they acquired the property. And if the master said that he gives half to one and half to the other, then even if he wrote it in two documents they also did not acquire the property.

The *gemara* ultimately preserves Rava's principle: the Rabbanan agree that full divestiture in a half-half arrangement is effective, provided the act of liberation meets the technical requirements of a valid *shtar shichrur* — one document per slave — and the formula used could function validly in sequence.

## Rav Ashi's View

### Terminology Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּקָא קָרֵי לְהוּ ״עֲבָדַיי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: The contradiction between the baraitot can be resolved by noting that it is different there, in the second baraita, because the master calls them: My slaves.** By writing: All of my property is given to so-and-so and so-and-so my slaves, he indicates that he does not intend to free them.

Rav Ashi proposes an entirely different explanation of why the second *baraita* — in which the master says "all my property is given to Reuven and Shimon, my slaves" — fails. The problem, he argues, is not structural or sequential: it is terminological. The master called them *avadai* — "my slaves" — thereby indicating that he conceived of them as remaining his slaves even as he transferred property to them. The word *avadai* reveals his intent: he did not mean to free them.

### Refram Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַפְרָם לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא ״עֲבָדַיי״ – שֶׁהָיוּ כְּבָר! מִי לָא תְּנַן: הַכּוֹתֵב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְעַבְדּוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת; שִׁיֵּיר קַרְקַע כָּל שֶׁהוּא – לֹא יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הוּא בֶּן חוֹרִין, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״כׇּל נְכָסַי נְתוּנִין לִפְלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי, חוּץ מֵאֶחָד מֵרִבּוֹא שֶׁבָּהֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rafram said to Rav Ashi: But perhaps when he says: My slaves, he is referring to those who were his slaves in the past. Didn't we learn in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:8): In the case of one who writes all of his property to his slave, i.e., gives it to him via a document, the slave has been emancipated. But if he reserved for himself even any amount of land, then he has not been emancipated. Rabbi Shimon says: Actually, the slave is a freeman until the master says the following: All of my property is given to so-and-so my slave except for one ten-thousandth of it, as in that case it is possible that the master meant to include the slave in the portion that he is not giving.**

Rafram challenges this immediately: perhaps *avadai* merely describes their prior status — "who have been my slaves until now" — and carries no implication about their ongoing servitude. To support this, Rafram points to the *mishna*: *ha-kosev kol nechasav le-avdo* — one who writes all his property to his slave, the slave *yatza le-cheirus*, goes free. The *mishna* uses the identical construction *avdo* — "his slave" — and yet that slave undeniably goes free. The formula therefore cannot be read as evidence of intent to retain ownership.

### Mishna Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר [״חוּץ מֵאֶחָד מֵרִבּוֹא שֶׁבָּהֶן״], הָא לָא אָמַר הָכִי – קָנֵי; אַמַּאי? וְהָא ״עֶבֶד״ קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא ״עַבְדִּי״ שֶׁהָיָה כְּבָר, הָכָא נָמֵי ״עֲבָדַיי״ שֶׁהָיוּ כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** Rafram continues his proof: **The reason the slave is not emancipated is specifically because the master said: Except for one ten-thousandth of it. But if he did not say this, then the slave acquires the property. According to your reasoning, why does he acquire the property? Didn't he call him a slave? Rather, it must be that when he called him: My slave, he meant that he was his slave in the past. Here also, when he said: My slaves, he meant: Those who were my slaves in the past.** Therefore, Rav Ashi's attempt to resolve the contradiction between the *baraitot* cannot be accepted.

The *gemara* confirms Rafram's objection. In the *mishna*, even when the text reads *le-avdo* — to his slave — the act is treated as a full *shichrur*. The term merely designates the person's prior status. The *gemara* therefore concludes that Rav Ashi's terminological argument fails: one cannot derive from the word *avadai* that the master intended to retain the slaves. The *mishna*'s use of *avdo* proves that such language does not negate the liberating effect of the transfer.

The consequence is that the terminological explanation is rejected, and the earlier resolutions — based on document structure or the *kulhu* versus *chatzi chatzi* distinction — remain the operative frameworks for understanding the two *baraitot*.

## Goring Damages

### Day-Based Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְגָחוֹ שׁוֹר; יוֹם שֶׁל רַבּוֹ – לְרַבּוֹ, יוֹם שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ – לְעַצְמוֹ. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה – יוֹם שֶׁל רַבּוֹ יִשָּׂא שִׁפְחָה, יוֹם שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ יִשָּׂא בַּת חוֹרִין! אִיסּוּרָא לָא קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § If a half-slave half-freeman, who works for himself and his master on alternating days, **was gored by an ox and damaged, then, if he was gored on the day of his master, the reimbursement for the damage caused is paid to his master. If it was on his own day, it is paid to the slave himself.** The Gemara asks: **However, if that is so, if he is viewed as entering and leaving a state of slavery each day, then on the day of his master he should be able to marry a maidservant and on his own day he should be able to marry a free woman.** However, the mishna states that he cannot marry anyone. The Gemara answers: **We are not saying that he is viewed as entering and leaving a state of slavery each day with regard to prohibitions.**

The *gemara* now raises a distinct and unresolved *ibba'ya* concerning the slave who is *mechusar get shichrur* — one who lacks only a *get shichrur* to become free. The paradigmatic case is a slave whom the master has declared *hefker* (ownerless), or one whom Beis Hillel now compels the master to free: the master no longer holds any monetary claim over the slave, yet the slave's personal status has not changed, because a formal *get shichrur* has not yet been delivered. The master retains, in some sense, the residual status of *adon* — enough standing to write and deliver the liberating document — but no proprietary claim.

Before Beis Hillel agreed with Beis Shammai and ruled that the half-slave must be fully freed, the *halacha* was that he works half his days for his master and half for himself. The *gemara* raises the question: if an ox gores this slave on a day designated for his master, does the master collect the damages? And on a day the slave works for himself, does the slave collect?

The initial premise — *yom shel rabo lerabo, yom shel atzmo le'atzmo* — would suggest that his identity, at least in monetary terms, alternates based on the day: on the master's day he is entirely the master's, and on his own day he is entirely his own.

### Status vs. Labor

The *gemara* immediately rejects any extension of this principle to matters of personal status. The suggestion that he might therefore be permitted to marry a free woman on his own days and a slave woman on his master's days is dismissed out of hand: *issura lo ka'amrinan* — we make no such ruling regarding his personal status, which remains fixed as half-slave, half-free regardless of the day. The day-based framework applies only to the question of whose earnings he generates, not to who he is.

### Calya Karna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הֵמִית מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין – נוֹתֵן חֲצִי קְנָס לְרַבּוֹ, וַחֲצִי כוֹפֶר לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. אַמַּאי? הָכִי נָמֵי לֵימָא: יוֹם שֶׁל רַבּוֹ – לְרַבּוֹ, יוֹם שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ – לְעַצְמוֹ! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּקָא כָלְיָא קַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and hear a proof with regard to this issue: When a Canaanite slave is killed by an ox, in addition to the ox being put to death, the owner of the ox pays a fixed penalty of thirty shekels to the slave's owner. If a freeman is killed by an ox, in addition to the ox being put to death, the freeman's value is paid to his heirs. If an ox killed a half-slave half-freeman, then the owner of the ox gives half of a penalty, i.e., fifteen shekels, to his master and half of a ransom, i.e., half of the value of the deceased, to his heirs. According to what was stated earlier, why is this the halakha? So too, in this case let us say: If the ox killed him on the day of his master, then the full penalty of thirty shekels is paid to his master, but if it killed him on his own day, then the full ransom is paid to the slave himself, i.e., his heirs. The Gemara answers: This is different, for the principal is consumed.** Since the slave is dead, even if the goring occurred on a day that he was working for himself, there is a permanent loss to the master, who is therefore entitled to be paid half of a penalty.

But even within the monetary domain, the day-based principle has limits. The *gemara* introduces the concept of *kalya karna* — destruction of the principal. If the slave is gored and killed, the *gemara* rules that the payment does not depend on whose day it was but is divided according to his permanent ownership structure: half a *knas* to the master and half a *kofer* to his heirs. This is because the death of the slave is not a question of whose earnings are lost on a given day — it is a permanent destruction of the property itself, and the question of who owns him is a permanent, fundamental one that must be assessed at the level of basic ownership. Since he is half-owned by himself and half by his master at all times, the payment is split accordingly.

The day-based rule, then, applies only to situations that are not *kalya karna* — not permanent damage. A temporary injury from which the slave will fully recover does not constitute a destruction of the principal. The slave will eventually return to full capacity, so the owners have not permanently lost anything; they have only lost his labor for the period of recovery. That lost labor is treated as earnings, and earnings go to whoever is entitled to them on the relevant day.

### Shevet Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְאַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ שֶׁבֶת גְּדוֹלָה, וְשֶׁבֶת קְטַנָּה – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶלָּא שִׁבְתּוֹ שֶׁבְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם; הַאי – שׁוֹר הוּא, וְשׁוֹר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא נֶזֶק! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כְּשֶׁהִכָּהוּ אָדָם. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: מֵימְרָא הִיא, וּמֵימְרָא לְרָבָא לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **This works out well according to the opinion of Abaye, who says: If one injures another and the injury causes him temporary paralysis, then he gives him the value of the major loss of livelihood, i.e., the decrease in his value, as measured by his price on the slave market, due to his temporary paralysis. And he also gives him the value of the minor loss of livelihood, i.e., the actual wages he lost during the time he was injured. This works out well, as in this case, the owner of the ox would have to pay the major loss of livelihood. However, according to the opinion of Rava, who says: He gives him only the value of his loss of livelihood for each and every day but does not have to pay for the decrease in his value because the slave will be healed and will return to his previous value, this case is one of an ox that injured the half-slave half-freeman. And an ox, i.e., the owner of an ox, pays only damage, as measured by his price on the slave market, but he does not pay for loss of livelihood at all. How then does Rava explain this case? If you wish, say that this is referring to a case where the slave was struck by a person, not an ox, so that the one who struck him must pay for the loss of livelihood. And if you wish, say instead: This halakha is merely a statement of an amora, and Rava is not bound to hold in accordance with a statement of an amora.**

Here the *gemara* surfaces a dispute between Abaye and Rava with significant implications for this framework.

Abaye holds that even in the case of a non-permanent injury, the payment takes the form of *shevet gedola ve-shevet ketana*. *Shevet gedola* — the major work-suspension payment — treats the temporary incapacity as a form of *nezek*: just as permanent injury is assessed by comparing the slave's market value before and after, so too is a temporary injury, even though he will recover. The comparison is made against the current condition of the slave while injured. *Shevet ketana* then covers the additional period of complete incapacitation — the time during which he cannot perform even minimal tasks. Under Abaye's view, an ox that gores a slave in a temporary injury would be liable for an assessable *nezek*, and this payment, since it concerns a valuation of the slave's current state rather than a permanent alteration of the principal, would go to whoever is entitled to his proceeds on that day.

Rava disagrees. In a case of non-permanent injury, there is no *nezek* to be assessed via the slave market. The injury produces no permanent diminution of value; once healed, the slave is worth exactly what he was before. The only payment owed is for *shevet* — time out of work — which is calculated day by day based on what work the slave is unable to perform relative to what he would otherwise do in his injured state. Crucially, *shevet* of this kind is a category of payment in cases of personal injury caused by a human being (*chavala*); an ox, however, only pays *nezek* — the actual, assessable cost of damage. Since under Rava's view a temporary injury produces no *nezek*, an ox that causes a non-permanent injury to a slave owes nothing at all under the laws of goring.

### Damage Split

This creates a tension in the *gemara*'s framework. We had said the day-based rule applies to non-permanent injuries — implying there is a payment from an ox in such cases that can then be allocated to the appropriate party. But according to Rava, an ox owes nothing for a non-permanent injury, because there is no *nezek* and an ox does not pay *shevet*. On what basis, then, would the day-based rule ever operate in an ox-goring scenario?

The *gemara* resolves this in two ways. First, *ee-ba'it eima keshe-hikahu adam*: perhaps the day-based rule applies not to a case of ox-goring at all, but to a case where the injury was caused by a human being. A human injurer does pay *shevet*, and if the injury is non-permanent, the *shevet* payment — being a function of lost labor rather than a destruction of the principal — goes to whoever was entitled to that labor on the relevant day. Second, *ee-ba'it eima memra hu*: the rule as stated is simply an independent teaching (*memra*), and Rava himself does not subscribe to it. It is not a *baraita* or *mishna* that demands his agreement.

The upshot is that permanent damage — whether death or lasting injury — is always allocated based on the slave's fundamental half-half ownership, split between master and slave. Temporary, non-permanent damage, if actionable at all, is allocated based on whose day it was, since it functions as a form of lost earnings rather than a diminution of the underlying property.

## Get Shichror Status

### Payment Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מְעוּכָּב גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר, יֵשׁ לוֹ קְנָס אוֹ אֵין לוֹ קְנָס? ״כֶּסֶף שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים יִתֵּן לַאדוֹנָיו״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְהַאי לָאו אָדוֹן הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּמְחוּסָּר גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר, אָדוֹן קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before the Sages: If a slave is detained in his master's possession only due to not having received a bill of manumission, but he is no longer obligated to perform labor for his master, e.g., in a case where his master declared him to be ownerless, does he render liable one whose ox kills him to pay the penalty of thirty shekels to his master, or does he not render one liable to pay the penalty? The Merciful One states in the Torah: "He shall give thirty shekels of silver to his master"** (Exodus 21:32), **and this is not a master, as in practice the slave has already been declared ownerless. Or perhaps, since he is lacking a bill of manumission, he is still called a master.**

The *gemara* now raises a distinct and unresolved *ibba'ya* concerning the slave who is *mechusar get shichrur* — one who lacks only a *get shichrur* to become free. The paradigmatic case is a slave whom the master has declared *hefker* (ownerless), or one whom Beis Hillel now compels the master to free: the master no longer holds any monetary claim over the slave, yet the slave's personal status has not changed, because a formal *get shichrur* has not yet been delivered. The master retains, in some sense, the residual status of *adon* — enough standing to write and deliver the liberating document — but no proprietary claim.

The question: if an ox gores this slave during the interim period, does the master collect the thirty-*shekel* *knas*? The Torah says *kesef shloshim shekalim yiten le-adonav* — the payment goes to "his master." Is this former master still an *adon* in the relevant sense? Or has he forfeited that status entirely along with his monetary ownership? Perhaps the *knas*, being a fixed fine rather than a payment for lost property, could be owed to whoever holds formal master-status — and the residual status needed to write a *get shichrur* might suffice. The *gemara* leaves the question open.

### Half-Slave Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הֵמִית מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין – נוֹתֵן חֲצִי קְנָס לְרַבּוֹ וַחֲצִי כוֹפֶר לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. מַאי, לָאו כְּמִשְׁנָה אַחֲרוֹנָה? לֹא, כְּמִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come and hear a proof for this based on what is taught in a baraita: If an ox killed one who is a half-slave half-freeman, then the owner of the ox gives half of a penalty, i.e., fifteen shekels, to the slave's master and half of a ransom, i.e., half of the value of the deceased, to his heirs. What, is the baraita not in accordance with the ultimate version of the mishna, after Beit Hillel conceded to Beit Shammai that the master is forced to emancipate the half-slave half-freeman, and he is merely lacking a bill of manumission? Despite this, the baraita states that the master receives the money of half of a penalty. The Gemara answers: No, this baraita is in accordance with the initial version of the mishna and in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, that the slave works for his master and himself on alternating days. He is still an actual slave, and that is why the master receives the sum of half of a penalty.**

The *gemara* attempts a proof from the earlier ruling: *hamamit mi she-chatzi eved ve-chatzi ben chorin nosei chatzi knas le-rabo ve-chatzi kofer le-yorshav* — an ox that kills a half-slave, half-free person pays half a *knas* to the master and half a *kofer* to the heirs. Perhaps this refers to the situation *after* Beis Hillel agreed with Beis Shammai — i.e., the master has effectively been compelled to free the slave and is now only awaiting the delivery of a *get shichrur*. If even in that situation the master collects half the *knas*, that would prove that the *mechusar get shichrur* retains enough master-status to collect *knas*.

The *gemara* rejects the proof: *lo, be-mishnah rishonah* — the ruling refers to the original situation (*mishnah rishonah*), before Beis Hillel reversed their position, when the half-slave arrangement was stable and the master fully owned his half. This case is not analogous to one in which the master has no monetary ownership at all.

### Blinding Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ וְסִימָא אֶת עֵינוֹ – יוֹצֵא בְּשִׁינּוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן דְּמֵי עֵינוֹ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ יֵשׁ לוֹ קְנָס, וּקְנָס לְרַבּוֹ – הַשְׁתָּא חָבְלִי בֵּיהּ אַחֲרִינֵי יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ לְרַבֵּיהּ, חָבֵל בֵּיהּ רַבֵּיהּ גּוּפֵיהּ, יָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come and hear a proof based on what is taught in a baraita: If the master knocked out the slave's tooth and then blinded his eye, then the slave is emancipated due to the loss of his tooth, as the verse states: "He shall let him go free for his tooth's sake" (Exodus 21:27). And his master gives him reimbursement for the value of his eye, as though he were a freeman. And if you say that a slave that did not yet receive a bill of manumission does render one whose ox kills him liable to pay the penalty, and the penalty is paid to his master, then one could ask: Now if other people injure him, then they give the payment to his master; if his master himself injures him, then should he give the payment for the slave's eye to him?**

A second attempted proof: if the master knocks out the slave's tooth and blinds his eye, the slave goes free — *yotzei be-shino*, *yotzei be-eino*. This creates a *mechusar shtar shichrur* scenario: the master no longer exercises monetary dominion, yet a *shtar shichrur* remains to be written. Now, if an ox were to gore the slave during this interval, and if the rule is that the master collects the *knas*, then by parity of reasoning, personal injury (*chavala*) payments should likewise flow to the master. But the *halacha* is that when the master himself injures the slave in this state, the payment goes to the slave. Does this not prove that in the *mechusar get shichrur* scenario the master does not collect — neither *knas* nor *chavala*?

### Limb Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ – דְּתַנְיָא: בְּכוּלָּן, עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Perhaps this baraita is in accordance with the one who said that when a slave is emancipated because his master knocked out his tooth he does not need a bill of manumission and is automatically emancipated the moment his tooth is knocked out, as it is taught in a baraita: For all of them, for all twenty-four limbs about which the Sages said that a slave is emancipated if his master damages one of them, a slave is emancipated by means of them, and he requires a bill of manumission from his master; this is the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Meir says: He does not require a bill of manumission. Rabbi Eliezer says: He requires it. Rabbi Tarfon says: He does not require it. Rabbi Akiva says: He requires it.**

This leads the *gemara* to cite a *baraita* cataloguing the dispute among *tannaim* on precisely this question: when a master destroys one of the slave's limbs, does the slave need a *get shichrur* to effect his freedom, or does he go free automatically?

### Freedom Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכְרִיעִין לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים, אוֹמְרִים: נִרְאִים דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן, הוֹאִיל וְתוֹרָה זִכְּתָה לוֹ, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – בִּשְׁאָר אֵבָרִים, הוֹאִיל וּקְנַס חֲכָמִים הוּא. קְנָס?! הָא קְרָאֵי קָא דָרְשִׁינַן! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִדְרַשׁ חֲכָמִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Those who rule before the Sages, i.e., those who attempt to rule by finding a middle ground between the different opinions, say as follows: The statement of Rabbi Tarfon, that the slave does not require a bill of manumission, seems correct in the case of a tooth or an eye, since the Torah transferred his freedom to him explicitly** (Exodus 21:26–27). **And the statement of Rabbi Akiva, that he does require a bill of manumission, seems correct in the case of other limbs, since it is a rabbinic penalty that he goes free. The Gemara questions the wording used: Is it a rabbinic penalty? Don't we interpret verses to conclude that a slave is emancipated when any of the twenty-four limbs are damaged by the master? Rather, say as follows: the statement of Rabbi Akiva seems correct in the case of other limbs, since it is an interpretation of the Sages,** and it is not written explicitly in the Torah. This question of the Gemara remains unresolved.

Rabbi Yishmael holds that in all such cases the slave goes free but requires a *get shichrur* from his master. Rabbi Meir holds he does not require one — freedom is automatic. Rabbi Eliezer says he does require one; Rabbi Tarfon says he does not; Rabbi Akiva says he does.

The *baraita* records that those who weighed the opinions before the sages (*ha-machri'in lifnei chachamim*) — Rashi notes he is uncertain of the precise referent — reached a compromise: *nir'im divrei Rabbi Tarfon be-shein va-ayin* — for tooth and eye, Rabbi Tarfon's position is compelling: the slave goes free without a *get shichrur*, since the Torah explicitly mandates his freedom in those cases. *Ve-divrei Rabbi Akiva bi-she'ar eivarim* — for other limbs, Rabbi Akiva's view is correct: a *get shichrur* is required, since the slave's freedom in those cases derives from rabbinic enactment (*knas chachamim*) rather than a direct biblical mandate.

The *gemara* objects: if it is *knas chachamim*, why call it a *knas*? It is derived from *pesukim*! The response: *ela eima ho'il u-midrash chachamim hu* — since it is known only through rabbinic *midrash* and not explicitly stated in the Torah, there is concern that the master might later find the slave in the marketplace and declare "you are still my slave — no limb-related freedom applies to you," since it is not as universally recognized as the explicit cases of tooth and eye. Tosafot (*Rabbeinu Tam*) makes the reasoning explicit: for tooth and eye, where the Torah states it openly, no one can plausibly deny it, and the slave needs no document for evidentiary protection; for other limbs, derived only by inference, the slave may be vulnerable to a master's bad-faith denial, and a *get shichrur* serves as his protective evidence.

### Unresolved Status

The *gemara* cannot complete its proof regarding the *mechusar get shichrur* because the tooth-and-eye case is disputed: according to several *tannaim*, that slave requires no *get shichrur* at all and goes free immediately, making the subsequent injury a case of injuring a free person entirely — not a case of *mechusar get shichrur*.

The fundamental question — whether a slave who is no longer the master's property but is still awaiting a *get shichrur* retains enough connection to the master for the master to collect *knas*, or *chavala*, on his behalf — remains *teku*, unresolved. The *gemara* leaves open what formal residue of master-status persists during that intermediate period and which monetary consequences, if any, attach to it. As the *gemara* closes the daf, the question of whether it is the *knas* (a fixed fine, perhaps more plausibly tied to formal status) or the *chavala* payment (a monetary loss that follows actual ownership) that the master might claim is left for further analysis.
