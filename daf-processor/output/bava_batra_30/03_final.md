# Bava Batra 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rashi & Chazaka

### Rashi Commentary

Two preliminary observations from yesterday's material deserve clarification before proceeding to today's *gemara*. The first concerns Rashi's commentary on Bava Batra. Research into this question reveals that Rashi did not produce three distinct versions of his commentary; rather, the prevailing assumption is that he continuously edited a single working commentary throughout his life, without issuing any official revised edition. What is clear regarding Bava Batra specifically is that the Rashbam, in several places, explicitly references notes or a draft of Rashi's commentary on this tractate that he incorporated into his own work. Beyond that, there is no direct evidence that Rashi ever completed a full commentary on Bava Batra. Grossman has argued that of course Rashi must have written one, and that it simply became lost — yet if it were lost, it was lost remarkably quickly. From this point in the tractate onward, Tosafot consistently cites the Rashbam; whenever Tosafot quotes Rashi, it is citing something he wrote in a different *masechta*. The implication of Rashi's complete absence from Tosafot's citations here is that he never wrote the commentary in the first place. The evidence remains largely circumstantial, but that is the weight of it.

### Chazaka Continuity

The second point revisits the question raised at the end of yesterday's shiur: must the *chazaka* of three years be continuous, and if so, what does that continuity require? Two positions emerged from the *gemara*. Tosafot reads the *gemara*'s conclusion as permitting an inference of continuity — if witnesses testify that someone was in the house, we assume, absent contrary evidence, that he was there day and night. The Rashbam, by contrast, reads the *gemara* as requiring direct testimony of that continuity.

### Rashbam Daily

The Rashbam's position on this point is stated explicitly in his commentary to 29b, approximately nine lines into the narrow-column text. He writes: *hilkach yavi hamachazik eidim shelo yatza min habayit kol shalosh shanim afilu layla achat* — "therefore the one establishing *chazaka* must bring witnesses that he did not leave the house for all three years, even for a single night." This is a striking requirement: according to the Rashbam, the holder's possession must function, in effect, like a kind of imprisonment in the property. The one concession the Rashbam offers is that if the contesting party does not actively demand such testimony — does not raise the question of whether the possessor was ever absent — there is no obligation to produce it. But if the challenge is raised, it can trigger an obligation to demonstrate presence for every single night over the full three-year period. From a practical standpoint, this creates a formidable difficulty: someone who wishes to forgo his deed and rely on *chazaka* instead could not leave for even one night without potentially invalidating the entire claim.

## Mechuza Stores

### Daytime Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַב הוּנָא בְּחַנְוָתָא דְמָחוֹזָא – דְּלִימָמָא עֲבִידָא, לְלֵילְיָא לָא עֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the requirement for continuous day and night use to establish the presumption of ownership, the Gemara comments: **And Rav Huna concedes with regard to a store** in the town **of Meḥoza, which** is generally **used in the day** and **is not** generally **used in the night,** that one is not required to use it in the night to establish the presumption of ownership.

The *gemara* continues, two lines from the top of 29b: *u'modeh Rav Huna b'chanuta d'Mechuza d'yimama avidi b'leilya lo avidi* — Rav Huna, who held that one must occupy a property day and night, concedes in the case of the stores of Mechuza that these are establishments used exclusively during the day; they have no nighttime presence. Accordingly, a *chazaka* on a storefront does not require nighttime occupation, because that simply is not how such a property is used.

### Unit Analysis

A question naturally arises: does this mean one needs only three years of daytime occupation, or must one accumulate the equivalent of three full years of day-and-night occupation — which would require six years of daytime use, analogous to fields that lie fallow in winter? Tosafot suggests that if the case involved a standard storefront used only during the day by everyone, the ruling would be obvious and not much of a *chidush*. Tosafot therefore reads the *gemara* as discussing a hybrid property — one that sometimes serves as a residence and is therefore occupied day and night, and sometimes serves as a storefront and is therefore used only during the day. Because for other people such a space functions as a residence, the failure to be present at night does not constitute a break in the *chazaka*; however, since daytime use alone is the mode of occupation, one would need six years of daytime presence to equal three full years of day-and-night occupation. The simpler reading of the *gemara*, though, is that this refers to a type of place inherently used only during the day, in which case three years of daytime presence suffices.

## Partnership Case

### Slave Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא וְרַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא זְבוּן הָהִיא אַמְּתָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. מָר אִישְׁתַּמַּשׁ בָּהּ רִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁלִישִׁית וַחֲמִישִׁית, וּמָר אִישְׁתַּמַּשׁ בָּהּ שְׁנִיָּה רְבִיעִית וְשִׁשִּׁית. נְפַק עַרְעָר עִילָּוַהּ,
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the requirement of continuous use to establish the presumption of ownership, the Gemara relates that **Rami bar Ḥama and Rav Ukva bar Ḥama purchased a maidservant together.** One **Sage made use of her** during the **first, third, and fifth** years, **and** one **Sage made use of her** during the **second, fourth, and sixth** years. **One** individual **who contested** their ownership **of** the maidservant **emerged** and claimed that he was the owner.

The *gemara* turns to a case involving Rami bar Chama and Rav Ukva bar Chama, who were apparently brothers. *Zavin hacha amsa behadi hadadi* — they purchased a maidservant in partnership. One brother used her the first, third, and fifth years; the other used her the second, fourth, and sixth years. A third party then appeared and challenged their ownership, demanding either a *shtar* or proof of *chazaka* — since slaves, like real property, require either a deed or three years of possession to establish ownership. The two brothers came before Rava.

### Alternation Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא עָבְדִיתוּ הָכִי – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תַּחְזְקוּ אַהֲדָדֵי; כִּי הֵיכִי דִּלְדִידְכוּ לָא הָוֵי חֲזָקָה, לְעָלְמָא נָמֵי לָא הָוֵי חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They came before Rava** for a judgment. **He said to them: What is the reason you acted like this,** making use of her in alternate years, **so that** each of **you would not establish** the **presumption** of ownership **concerning each other** by making use of her for three consecutive years? **Just as** this usage does **not** establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to you,** it does **not** establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to others,** i.e., the claimant in this case, **as well.**

Rava asked them directly: why did you alternate by year? The evident reason, he concluded, was to prevent either brother from accumulating three continuous years of use and thereby establishing a *chazaka* against the other. *Ki heichi delo techziku hadadi* — so that neither could claim ownership against his partner by virtue of uninterrupted possession. Rava then applied this logic symmetrically: just as the alternating use prevented either brother from establishing a *chazaka* against the other, it equally prevented them from establishing a *chazaka* against the original owner. Neither had three consecutive years of possession. *Lo havi chazaka le'ama nami lo havi chazaka* — the same arrangement that defeated their claims against each other defeats their claims against the claimant.

What the brothers would ideally want, of course, is to be treated as a single legal entity — a partnership, *shutfin* — whose collective use was continuous across all six years, thereby constituting three full years of joint *chazaka* sufficient to rebuff the original claimant.

### Partnership Doc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא כְּתוּב עִיטְרָא, אֲבָל כְּתוּב עִיטְרָא – קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And we said** this **only** in a case **where** it **was not written** in a **document** that they are partners and are dividing the use, **but** if it **was written** in a **document,** the document generates **publicity** that they own the maidservant as partners rather than individually, and they can jointly establish the presumption of ownership.

The *gemara* addresses this: *ve'amru lo kasav itra* — all of the above applies only if they did not write an *itra*, which here means something like a *shtar shutfus*, a formal partnership document. *Aval kasav itra kala it lei* — but if they did write such a document, it has "a voice." The Rashbam offers two explanations of what this means. The first, more extreme reading is that the very act of processing a formal document in court — one that defines the terms of their partnership and implicitly asserts their joint ownership of the slave — constitutes a provocation equivalent to three years of *chazaka*. The original owner, upon hearing that two people had drawn up a formal partnership agreement regarding his slave, should have objected immediately; his silence functions as the equivalent of acquiescence. The Rashbam does not favor this reading. His preferred explanation is more modest: the *itra* frames the legal character of their joint possession. Without it, the original owner could argue that the alternating use was insufficiently provocative — it did not look like the kind of ownership that demands a response. With the *itra*, however, the arrangement becomes publicly documented and legally defined. The original owner can no longer claim that the pattern of possession gave him no clear signal that his property was being claimed by others. *Kala it lei* — it has a voice; it was known, it was formal, and it was precisely the kind of assertion of ownership that obligates the original owner to protest. Their joint possession across all six years is thus framed as a unified *chazaka*, and his silence in the face of it is meaningful.

The obvious follow-up question is whether the *itra* itself must be physically available or extant. Presumably, testimony that such a document was written might suffice — though that remains an open question. What is required, in any case, is sufficient evidence to establish that the partnership was formally constituted and that their joint possession was publicly understood as such.

## Field Portions

### Beit Rova

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲכָלָהּ כּוּלָּהּ, חוּץ מִבֵּית רוֹבַע – קָנָה כּוּלָּהּ, חוּץ מִבֵּית רוֹבַע.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the requirement for full use of land to establish the presumption of ownership. **Rava says:** If one worked and **profited,** for three years, **from all of** the land **except for** the **area required for sowing a quarter-*kav* of seed [*beit rova*],** which he did not use, **he acquires all** of the field based on presumptive ownership **except for** that ***beit rova*.**

The *gemara* now shifts to a series of questions about the physical scope of *chazaka* — specifically, whether unused or unusable portions of a field are included in or excluded from a claimant's three-year possession. Rava states: *achlah kullah chutz mibeit rova* — if someone worked the entire field but left over a small corner, a *beit rova* (approximately 104 square *amos* — roughly 10 by 10 *amos*, a quite small area), the ruling is *kanah kullah chutz mibeit rova*: he acquires the whole except for that *beit rova*. The logic follows directly from the evidentiary framework of *chazaka*: just as one cannot infer from the absence of testimony about nighttime use that the possessor was present at night, one cannot infer from the use of 99% of a field that the possessor also possessed the remaining 1%. The field is not necessarily treated as an indivisible unit. Perhaps the original owner sold 99% and retained that small corner for himself. Since the possessor provided no provocation with respect to that corner — did nothing there that would have put the original owner on notice — he has no *chazaka* on it.

### Unsuitable Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּבַר זְרִיעָה הִיא, אֲבָל לָאו בַּר זְרִיעָה הִיא – קָנֵי לַהּ אַגַּב אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says: And we said** this ruling **only where** that *beit rova* **is suitable for planting, but** if **it is not suitable for planting, he acquires it by means of** the acquisition of the rest of the **land.**

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua qualifies this ruling: *v'lo amran ela de'bar zriah hi* — this applies only when the uncultivated corner is the kind of land that could have been planted. *Aval lav bar zriah hi* — but if the corner is inherently unsuitable for cultivation, say because it is rocky or too hard, then *kani lah agav ara*: it is acquired together with the rest of the field. Since there was no reasonable expectation that he would plant it, his failure to do so proves nothing. We presume it was all part of the same purchase, and his *chazaka* on the rest of the field encompasses this patch as well.

### Alternative Uses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, צוּנְמָא – בַּמֶּה יִקְנֶה? אֶלָּא בְּאוֹקוֹמֵי בָּהּ חֵיוָתָא וּמִשְׁטְחָא בַּהּ פֵּירֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי – אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאוֹקוֹמֵי בָּהּ חֵיוָתָא, אִי נָמֵי מִשְׁטְחָא בַּהּ פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye objects to this: If that is so, how could one acquire** an area that is only **rock** by establishing the presump-tion of ownership, as one does not plant rocky land? **Rather,** the presumption of ownership can be established **by standing animals on it or spreading produce on it. Here too, he should be required to stand animals on** the *beit rova* **or** to **spread produce on it.** If he did not profit from this small piece of land at all, he has not established the presumption of ownership with regard to it, and acquires only the rest of the field.

Rav Dimi bar Abaye challenges this exception. He asks: if a plot consists entirely of rock, how would one establish a *chazaka* on it at all? The answer is *ela be'okmi bah chayuta u'mishtacha bah peiri* — through other forms of use: keeping livestock there, or spreading fruit in the sun to dry. There are modes of using land that do not involve planting. The same applies here. Even if this small corner cannot be planted, the possessor could have stationed animals there or used it for drying produce. *Hacha nami be'okmi bah chayuta i nami mishtacha bah peiri* — the same options were available for this patch. Since he failed to use it in any of these ways, his non-use is not excused by the land's unsuitability. There were things he could have done and didn't, so he has no *chazaka* on that portion.

The underlying tension here is worth noting. The larger context — the fact that one is farming an entire field — might reasonably suggest that the whole field was purchased as a unit. Particularly for a very small and unusable patch embedded within a larger worked field, one might argue that the *chazaka* on the surrounding land creates a reasonable presumption covering the whole. Yet the *gemara* resists that inference, demanding instead some affirmative act even for the unusable portion. It is also worth noting the interesting use of the word *kani* — "he acquires" — in this passage, which echoes an older conceptual framework in which *chazaka* creates or transfers ownership rather than merely evidencing it. Those who think of *chazaka* primarily as creating ownership would naturally ask whether any act was performed that transferred the status of this small piece; those who prefer the evidentiary model ask whether any provocation was given with respect to it.

## House Division

### Inner Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי בֵּיתָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִינָּךְ זְבֵינְתֵּיהּ, וַאֲכַלִית שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא בִּשְׁכוּנֵי גַּוּוֹאֵי הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that there was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want,** i.e., what are you doing, **with this house** of mine? The other **said to him: I purchased it from you and I** worked and **profited** from **it** for the **years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. The claimant **said to him: I was among the distant settlements,** and was unaware that you were residing in my house, which is why I did not lodge a protest.

The *gemara* opens a series of case-law disputes with a recurring formulation: *hahu de'amar lei lechavreih, mai ba'it behai beita* — somebody said to his fellow, "What are you doing in my house?" This series of cases is striking in that it represents genuine case law embedded in the *gemara* — actual disputes brought before the *amoraim* for adjudication, though scholars debate how many of these are real cases versus illustrative scenarios.

In the first case, the man found in the house responds: *minach zevintah va'achalti shenei chazaka* — "I bought it from you and I have had three years of possession." The original owner counters: *ana beshikunei gavai havai* — "I was living in the inner chamber of the house." The claim, as Tosafot explains it, is not merely rhetorical. The original owner can bring evidence that he was residing in the back room throughout those three years. The question is how he was accessing the back room. If he was entering and exiting through a separate rear entrance with no presence in the front portion of the house, then one could argue that the person in the front had a legitimate *chazaka* on that part. But if the original owner was regularly passing through the front of the house, his ongoing presence would undermine the *chazaka* — it would suggest that the person in the front was perhaps a renter, a guest, or a hired hand rather than a new owner, since a true owner would not silently watch someone else take possession while he walked through daily.

The key question left implicit but resolved by Tosafot is that the original owner's claim implies he was entering through the back door and was not maintaining a regular visible presence in the front portion — which is precisely what the person in the front would need to disprove.

### Exclusive Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל בְּרוֹר אֲכִילְתָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The one residing in the house **came before Rav Naḥman** for a judgment. Rav Naḥman **said to him: Go clarify your profiting,** i.e., prove that you really resided there for three years, and then the case can be judged.

The case came before Rav Nachman, who ruled: *zil baror achilatcha* — go prove your consumption. That is, the *machzik* — the person asserting *chazaka* — must demonstrate that he used the property exclusively, without the original owner's presence. This is admittedly difficult to prove in the abstract, but Tosafot explains that the original owner's evidence of living in the back room now creates a sufficient challenge to shift the burden: the *machzik* must now have witnesses testify not only that he was present for three years, but that he was present alone — that the original owner's use of the property did not amount to an ongoing demonstration of ownership.

### Burden Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: הָכִי דִּינָא?! הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Naḥman: Is **this the** correct **judgment?** The *halakha* is that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** Therefore, the one who is attempting to take the house from the possessor should have to prove that the other did not reside in the house.

Rava challenges Rav Nachman's ruling: *hachi dina?* — is that really the law? *Hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — it is the one seeking to take away from another who bears the burden of proof. Rava's premise is that once a person establishes three years of *chazaka* in the front of the house, he becomes the *muchzak* — the one considered to be in legal possession. He has not necessarily proven ownership, but he is in possession, and that status means the original owner becomes the *motzi*, the one seeking to reclaim, and the burden of proof falls on him. Even though the original owner has raised a challenge — claiming he was in the back room — that challenge does not automatically dissolve the *muchzak* status of the person in the front. The burden remains on the original owner to affirmatively prove that his presence was such as to undermine the *chazaka*. This is, for Rava, one of the core functions of *chazaka*: beyond creating ownership or evidencing it, it creates *muchzakut* — a legal status of possession that shifts the burden of proof.

## Bar Sissan

### Name vs Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: כׇּל נִכְסֵי דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין – מְזַבֵּינָא לָךְ. הֲוַאי הָהִיא אַרְעָא דַּהֲוָה מִיקְּרֵי ״דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא לָאו דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין הִיא, וְאִיקְּרוֹיֵי הוּא דְּמִיקַּרְיָא ״דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **who said to another: I** am hereby **selling to you all** of the **property** that I own **of the house of bar Sisin. There was a certain** parcel of **land that was called: Of the house of bar Sisin.** The seller **said to** the buyer: **This** parcel of land that I own is **not** actually **of the house of bar Sisin, and it is** merely **called: Of the house of bar Sisin,** and it is not included in the sale.

The *gemara* then presents what appears at first glance to be a contradictory pair of rulings by Rava and Rav Nachman. The case, beginning at the top of 31a, involves someone who says to his fellow: *kol nichsei devei Bar Sissan mazbinna lach* — "I am selling you all the property of the house of Bar Sissan." As the Rashbam explains, the transaction is understood to mean: I purchased a portfolio of land from Bar Sissan, and I am now selling to you everything that I acquired from them. The name "house of Bar Sissan" carries an implicit understanding — the property that Bar Sissan recently owned and that I subsequently bought from them.

After the transaction is completed, a dispute arises over a particular plot of land. *Havei hahu ara de-havei mikrei devei Bar Sissan* — there was a certain parcel of land that was called "the house of Bar Sissan." The seller claims: *hai lav devei Bar Sissan hi* — this was not actually owned by Bar Sissan; it was never part of what I purchased from them. *Veyikrei hu de-mikrei devei Bar Sissan* — it merely goes by that name, perhaps because it was associated with Bar Sissan generations ago. When I said I was selling you "the house of Bar Sissan," I meant the portfolio I acquired from them, and this parcel was not among it.

### Buyer Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אוֹקְמָא בִּידָא דְלוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **They came before Rav Naḥman** for judgment, and **he established** the land **in the possession of the buyer.**

The case came before Rav Nachman. *Ve'okmah beyada delokayach* — he ruled that the buyer keeps the disputed parcel, because prima facie it appears to have been part of the portfolio being sold. The name matches, the seller was conveying everything associated with Bar Sissan, and the land in question bears that name.

### Seller Muchzak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: דִּינָא הָכִי?! הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Naḥman: Is **this the *halakha*?** Isn't the *halakha* that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant,** and the land should remain in the possession of the seller?

Rava challenged: *dina hachi? Hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — is that the law? The buyer is seeking to take it from the seller; the seller, as the original possessor, is the *muchzak*, and the burden of proof falls on the buyer to demonstrate that this parcel was indeed part of what was sold.

This appears to directly contradict the positions taken in the house case. There, Rav Nachman placed the burden on the *machzik* to prove exclusive use, while Rava said the *machzik* was the *muchzak* and the original owner bore the burden. Here, the positions seem reversed: Rav Nachman gives the property to the buyer while Rava insists the seller is the *muchzak*.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרָבָא אַדְּרָבָא, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן אַדְּרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: There is **a difficulty** from one statement **of Rava to** another statement **of Rava,** and there is also **a difficulty** from one statement **of Rav Naḥman to** another statement **of Rav Naḥman,** as in the first case, where the claimant states that he had been in a distant location, Rav Naḥman ruled in favor of the claimant, and Rava ruled in favor of the possessor; while in the second case, that of the property of bar Sisin, their opinions were reversed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרָבָא אַדְּרָבָא לָא קַשְׁיָא – הָתָם מוֹכֵר קָאֵי בְּנִכְסֵיהּ, הָכָא לוֹקֵחַ קָאֵי בְּנִיכְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The contradiction between one statement **of Rava and** another statement **of Rava** is **not difficult,** because **there,** in the case of the property of bar Sisin, the **seller** had been **established** as having the land **in his property,** which is why Rava rules in his favor. But **here,** in the case where the claimant states that he had been in a distant location, the **buyer** is **established** as having the house **in his property.**

The *gemara* resolves both apparent contradictions. *De-Rava ad-Rava lo kashya* — Rava is not contradicting himself. *Hatam mocher kay bi-nichsei hacha lokeach kay bi-nichsei* — the distinction is simply about who is actually in physical possession at the relevant moment. In the Bar Sissan case, the transaction was being contested immediately upon its completion. Until that very moment, the seller had owned and possessed all this land. The question was whether this specific parcel had just changed hands. Since the seller had been in possession until now, he is the *muchzak*, and the buyer who seeks to claim the parcel bears the burden of proof. In the house case, by contrast, the buyer had been living in the front of the house for three years. That three-year presence made him the *muchzak* — not the original owner. The burden of proof therefore shifted to the original owner. *Muchzakut* is not determined by who is the buyer or seller in the abstract; it is determined by who is actually in possession right now.

### Evidence Primacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַב נַחְמָן אַדְּרַב נַחְמָן נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא – כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין, וּמִיקַּרְיָא ״דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין״, עֲלֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ רַמְיָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי דְּלָאו דְּבֵי בַּר סִיסִין הִיא; אֲבָל הָכָא, לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא דְּנָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא – מִי לָא אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: קַיֵּים שְׁטָרָךְ וְקוּם בְּנִיכְסֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The contradiction between one statement **of Rav Naḥman and** the other statement **of Rav Naḥman** is **not difficult as well,** because there, **since** the seller **said to him:** I am hereby selling you all of the property that I own **of the house of bar Sisin, and** this parcel of land is **called: Of the house of bar Sisin,** it is **incumbent on him to reveal that** the parcel under dispute is **not of the house of bar Sisin. But here,** in the case where the claimant states that he had been in a distant location, it **should not be** any **different** from a case **where** the possessor is **holding a document** as evidence that he purchased the house. **Wouldn't we** then **say to him:** First **ratify your document, and** only then be **established in** the **property?** In this case as well, since his presumptive ownership is in place of a document, he needs to clarify the matter by means of witnesses.

The *gemara* explains Rav Nachman's consistency as follows: *kevan de'amar lei Devei Bar Sisan u'mekarya Devei Bar Sisan adideh ramya lei giluyei de'lav Devei Bar Sisan hi* — since the seller said "the property of Bar Sissan" and the disputed parcel is called "the property of Bar Sissan," the burden falls on the seller to demonstrate that it was not included. This is a fascinating formulation. Rav Nachman is not framing the question primarily in terms of *muchzakut* — legal status of possession — but in terms of what the evidence itself indicates. The prima facie reading of all available facts points in one direction: the land is called Bar Sissan, the seller sold Bar Sissan property, and the buyer is holding land called Bar Sissan. If the seller wants to argue against this convergence of evidence, he bears the burden of doing so. The buyer has, in effect, already brought a *ra'ayah* — the name itself is evidence. For the seller to counter that with "the name is incidental," he must disprove what the evidence naturally indicates.

This is the deeper principle Rav Nachman is applying. The *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* rule does not simply assign burden based on formal legal status; it is calibrated to the weight of the evidence. Once the buyer has presented what any reasonable observer would consider evidence in his favor, the seller cannot rest on a bare claim that the name is a coincidence. He must affirmatively prove it.

Returning to the house case, Rav Nachman's reasoning becomes clearer. The *gemara* points out that the situation is analogous to someone who holds a *shtar*: even a *shtar* does not end the inquiry — the opposing party can challenge the witnesses and require validation of the document. Similarly, a *chazaka* of three years is substantial evidence, but it does not automatically foreclose a reasonable counter-claim. When the original owner produces evidence that he was residing in the back room and regularly passing through the property, that is a reasonable challenge — it is not a far-fetched alternative interpretation, unlike claiming that "Bar Sissan" is merely a coincidental name. A reasonable counter-claim shifts the inquiry back: the *machzik* must now prove that his use was truly exclusive. Rav Nachman may not deny that *chazaka* creates *muchzakut* in some sense, but he treats the question of who bears the burden as responsive to how the evidence lines up, not merely to formal status. What remains less than fully explicit is whether Rav Nachman affirmatively endorses Rava's concept of *chazaka* as creating *muchzakut*, or merely declines to rely on it. He consistently frames the analysis in terms of *shtar* and the strength of evidence rather than in terms of possession-status — and that framing may itself be significant.

## Absence Case

### Distance Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי בֵּיתָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִינָּךְ זְבֵנְתֵּיהּ, וַאֲכַלִית שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא בִּשְׁכוּנֵי בָּרָאֵי הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that there was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want,** i.e., what are you doing, **with this house** of mine? The possessor **said to him: I purchased it from you and I** worked and **profited from it** for **the years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. The claimant **said to** him: **I was in** the **outer marketplaces,** and was unaware that you were residing in my house, and therefore did not lodge a protest, so your profiting does not establish the presumption of ownership.

The *gemara* presents another case in the same recurring form. Someone asks his fellow: *mai ba'it behai beita* — what are you doing in my house? The occupant responds: *minach zavinteih ve'achaltieh shenei chazaka* — I bought it from you and I have established three years of *chazaka*. The original owner counters: *ana beshuchnei barai havaite* — I was in an outer region, a distant territory, for those three years. This is the concept of *chazaka shelo befanav* — adverse possession in the absence of the owner. The principle is that *chazaka shelo befanav* is effective only when the owner is in a region where communication and travel are reasonably available, so that word of what is happening on his property could reach him. If someone is in one part of Judea and the property is elsewhere in Judea, we assume there is sufficient travel between those places that a proper *chazaka* would put the owner on notice. But if the owner is in a region entirely cut off from regular travel with the region of the property — as between Judea and the Galil, where regular movement is limited — the three years do not produce a valid *chazaka*. The owner here claims he was in a distant foreign territory, equivalent in practical terms to China, and had no way of receiving word of the adverse possession. The three years therefore cannot stand against him.

### Annual Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אִית לִי סָהֲדִי דְּכֹל שַׁתָּא הֲוָה אָתֵית תְּלָתִין יוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The possessor **said to him: But I have witnesses that every year you would come** here for **thirty days** and had an opportunity to know that I was residing in your house and to lodge a protest.

The occupant responds: *ve'ha it lei sa'adei de'chol shata havei asi tlashin yomei* — but I have witnesses that every year you returned for thirty days. This return, the occupant argues, gave the original owner ample opportunity to observe the adverse possession and to protest. His silence during those thirty days, repeated over three years, constitutes acquiescence.

### Preoccupation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּלָתִין יוֹמֵי – בְּשׁוּקַאי הֲוָה טְרִידְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The claimant **said to him: I was occupied with** my business in the **marketplaces** for those **thirty days.**

The owner counter-claims: *tlashin yomei be'shuka havei tridna* — during those thirty days I was occupied with my business. He was returning from abroad with his merchandise and selling it in the market; he was too preoccupied to take notice of what was happening on his land. The Rashbam considers two possible readings of this claim. One is that the owner admits he heard about the adverse possession but argues he was too busy to file a formal *macha'ah* — a protest before witnesses — during those thirty days. This is a weaker claim, since a *macha'ah* requires only telling two witnesses that the possessor is on his land without his consent, a minimal act. The other reading, which the Rashbam prefers, is that the owner claims he was so preoccupied that he genuinely never knew about it — not that he was too busy to respond, but that the information never reached him during those frantic thirty days of commerce.

### Reasonableness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּכֹל תְּלָתִין יוֹמֵי טְרִיד בְּשׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: A person is apt** to be **occupied** with business **in the marketplace for all** of **thirty days,** and accepted his claim.

Rava rules: *avid inish de'ka'azil tlashin yomei ve'tarid be'shuka* — it is entirely plausible that a person traveling thirty days with his goods, selling in the market, could be so preoccupied as to be genuinely unaware of what was happening on his land. This is a reasonable claim, and it undermines the *chazaka*. The burden of proof is relevant here as well: the original owner does not need to produce witnesses proving that every single spare moment was consumed by business. Once the larger context is established — that he was away for the year and returned only for intensive commercial activity — the claim of preoccupation is plausible on its face, and that plausibility is sufficient to cast doubt on the *chazaka* without requiring affirmative proof. It may also be noted that the case begins without a presumption of *chazaka*, since the owner's prolonged absence already raises questions about whether the conditions for *chazaka shelo befanav* were met. The thirty days of annual return, claimed by the occupant to create sufficient notice, is precisely what is being contested.

## Middleman

### Third-Party Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי אַרְעָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִפְּלָנְיָא זְבֵינְתַּהּ, דְּאָמַר לִי דְּזַבְנַהּ מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want with this land** of mine? The possessor **said to him: I purchased it from so-and-so, who told me that he purchased it from you.**

The next case: *hahu de'amar lei lechavrei, mai ba'it be'hai ara* — someone says to his fellow, what are you doing in this land? The occupant replies: *mi'planya zavnisah de'amar li de'zavnah minach* — I bought it from Plonya, who told me he bought it from you. Notice immediately that the occupant does not say *va'achalti shenei chazaka* — he makes no claim of three years of possession. The dispute is thus purely about the chain of title.

### Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ לָאו קָא מוֹדֵית דְּהַאי אַרְעָא דִּידִי הִיא, וְאַתְּ לָא זְבֵינְתַּהּ מִינַּאי? זִיל, לָאו בַּעַל דְּבָרִים דִּידִי אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The claimant **said to him: Don't you concede that this land is** formerly **mine, and** that **you** did **not purchase it from me? Go** away; **I am not legally answerable to you.**

The original owner responds: *at lav ka modit dehai ara didi hi* — you yourself have just admitted that this land was originally mine. You are not claiming to have bought it directly from me, and you are conceding that it was mine to begin with. *Lav ba'al devarim didi at* — you are not my litigant. You are making no claim directly against me; your claim runs through Plonya. And since you have implicitly conceded my original ownership without providing any proof that I ever transferred the property to Plonya, you have no standing.

### Implicit Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דִּינָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The claimant **stated the *halakha* to** the possessor, as this is a legitimate claim, and Rava accepted his claim.

Rava agrees: *dina ka'amar lei* — the original owner's argument is legally sound, and the occupant loses. The substance of the ruling warrants careful analysis. In a classic case where there is a known original owner, a middleman, and a current possessor who has three years of *chazaka*, the *halacha* generally provides for the current possessor to prevail — even without knowing for certain that the middleman legitimately purchased from the original owner — because the *gemara* steps in and makes that claim on behalf of the middleman (*taninan lelokayach*). This works because the three years of adverse possession itself provides evidence that the original owner was no longer asserting ownership, and his silence is treated as acquiescence. But there is a critical qualification: the middleman must have been present on the land for at least one day. Without any evidence that the middleman ever possessed the land, the claim that he had purchased it becomes too suspicious, and the possessor's *chazaka* cannot back up a legally hollow chain of title.

Here, the occupant lacks even three years of *chazaka*. Without that evidentiary foundation, his claim rests entirely on Plonya's assertion of ownership — and Plonya's claim is entirely unsupported. The occupant's own formulation of his claim implicitly acknowledges the original owner's title while failing to produce any evidence of a valid transfer. His admission cannot be rescued by a *migo* or *hapeah she'assar*, because we do believe him exactly as he states — and what he has stated is that he transacted with someone who claimed ownership without proof. That is insufficient to displace the original owner.

### Claim Support

This raises the fundamental principle underlying the entire series of cases: *chazaka* is only as strong as the *ta'anah* it supports. Adverse possession of three years silences the original owner's protest and shifts the burden of proof — but it functions as evidence backing a claim, not as a free-standing substitute for a claim. If the underlying claim is legally sound, the *chazaka* gives it force. If the underlying claim is defective — because the person from whom the occupant purchased was known to be a charlatan, or because there is simply no evidence that the middleman ever had any connection to the property — the *chazaka* cannot rescue it. The analogy is pointed: claiming to have bought property from someone who had no demonstrated ownership is no different in principle from claiming to have bought the Brooklyn Bridge from a stranger. The original owner of the Brooklyn Bridge remains its owner regardless of what the stranger claimed. The occupant has, without any intent to do so, proven only that he made a bad business deal.

## Gazlan Case

### Thief Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי אַרְעָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִפְּלָנְיָא זְבֵינְתַּהּ, וַאֲכַלְתִּיהָ שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלָנְיָא גַּזְלָנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want with this land** of mine? The possessor **said to him: I purchased it from so-and-so and** then **I** worked and **profited from it** for **the years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. The claimant **said to him: So-and-so is a robber** who robbed me of the field, and he did not have the authority to sell it to you.

The final case of the shiur: *hahu de'amar lei lechavrei, mai ba'it b'arai* — someone says to his fellow, what are you doing in my land? The occupant replies: *mi'planya zevina va'achaltieh shenei chazaka* — I bought it from Plonya and I have three years of *chazaka*. The original owner responds: *planya gazlana hu* — that Plonya is a known *gazlan*, a thief. The *halacha* is that a known *gazlan* cannot establish a *chazaka* — his circumstances of possession are interpreted differently from those of an ordinary person, since a *gazlan* who occupies land is understood to be doing so as a *gazlan*, not as an owner, and his presence therefore does not serve as a provocation to the original owner in the normal way. The original owner is claiming that because Plonya was a known *gazlan*, any *chazaka* built on Plonya's possession is invalid, and therefore the occupant's three years of use, derived from that possession, cannot stand.

### Advice Given

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אִית לִי סָהֲדִי דַּאֲתַאי אִימְּלַכִי בָּךְ, וַאֲמַרְתְּ לִי: ״זִיל זְבֹין״!
>
> **Translation:** The possessor **said to him: But I have witnesses that I came** and **consulted with you, and you said to me: Go purchase** the land, indicating that you conceded that he had the authority to sell it.

The occupant counters: *ve'ha ika sahadi d'atai imlech bach v'amrat li zil zevin* — but I have witnesses that I came to you and asked whether I should buy this land from Plonya, and you told me to go ahead and buy it. This is significant because if the original owner had genuinely considered the land stolen, he would not have advised the occupant to purchase it. His advice to buy therefore constitutes an implicit concession that Plonya was a legitimate owner — or at minimum, that the original owner did not consider himself the victim of a theft.

### Easier Target

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשֵּׁנִי נוֹחַ לִי; הָרִאשׁוֹן קָשֶׁה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The claimant **said to him:** The reason that I advised you to purchase it was because **the second** person, i.e., you, the possessor, **is amenable to me,** while **the first,** i.e., the purported thief, **is more difficult than he,** i.e., I prefer to litigate with you rather than with him.

The original owner has an explanation: *hasheni noach li harishon kasheh mimeni* — the second defendant is easier for me than the first. He advised the occupant to buy precisely because the occupant — an honest, law-abiding person — would be a far more manageable adversary in court than the *gazlan*. A *gazlan* is skilled at evading legal accountability; litigation against him is arduous. By encouraging the occupant to take possession, the original owner created a situation where he could pursue his legitimate claim against someone accessible and cooperative. This is, incidentally, a case implicating the prohibition of *lifnei iver lo titen michshol* — giving advice that serves one's own interest at another's expense — though whether the original owner actually transgressed that prohibition is a separate question.

### Words vs Deed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דִּינָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The claimant **stated the *halakha* to** the possessor, as this is a legitimate claim, and Rava accepted his claim.

Rava rules: *dina ka'amar lei* — the original owner's explanation is legally sound. It is entirely plausible that a person would give such advice for strategic self-interested reasons rather than as a concession of ownership. The advice he gave does not constitute an implicit admission.

### Admon Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן, כְּאַדְמוֹן? דִּתְנַן: הָעוֹרֵר עַל הַשָּׂדֶה וְחָתוּם עָלֶיהָ בְּעֵד – אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַשֵּׁנִי נוֹחַ לִי; הָרִאשׁוֹן קָשֶׁה הֵימֶנּוּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִיבֵּד אֶת זְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is Rava's statement? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Admon? As we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 109a): With regard to **one who contests** ownership **of a field,** claiming that a field possessed by someone else actually belongs to him, **and** the claimant himself **is signed as a witness on** the bill of sale of the field to that other person, **Admon says:** His signature does not disprove his claim of ownership of the property, as it is possible that the claimant said to himself: **The second** person **is amenable to me** to deal with, as I can reason with him, while **the first** owner, who sold the field to the current possessor, **is more difficult** to deal with **than he. And the Rabbis say: He lost his right** to contest, as he signed a bill of sale that states that the field belongs to the possessor. Rava's ruling appears to be in accordance with the individual opinion of Admon, and not with the opinion of the Rabbis.

The *gemara* then asks whose position this resembles — and suggests it sounds like that of Admon in the Mishnah. The relevant case there is: someone challenges another's possession of a field — *ha'orer al ha'sadeh* — but it emerges that he himself signed as a witness on the possessor's deed. Admon rules that the challenger can still say *hasheni noach li harishon kasheh mimenu* — I signed the document as a strategy; I wanted you as the possessor rather than the previous owner, who would be harder to deal with in court. My signature was not an admission of your legitimate title. The *chachamim* disagree: *ibad et zechuto* — by signing the document he forfeited his claim. Having put his name as a witness on a deed, he has clearly and implicitly testified to the legitimacy of the transaction, and he cannot now claim it was merely a legal maneuver.

The *gemara* asks: does Rava's ruling here follow Admon against the *chachamim*? The *gemara* answers: *afilu teima rabanan* — even the *chachamim* would agree in this case. *Hasam avid lei ma'aseh* — there, in the Admon case, the challenger performed an act: he physically signed his name on a document. Signing is not merely expressing an opinion; it is a formal legal act, an affirmation of testimony, and it carries the full implicit concession that the transaction it records is legitimate. The Rashbam does not favor this reading. His preferred explanation is more modest: the *itra* frames the legal character of their joint possession. Without it, the original owner could argue that the alternating use was insufficiently provocative — it did not look like the kind of ownership that demands a response. With the *itra*, however, the arrangement becomes publicly documented and legally defined. The original owner can no longer claim that the pattern of possession gave him no clear signal that his property was being claimed by others. Words of advice, by contrast — *hacha dibura* — are not in the same category. *Avid inish de'mikrei ve'amar* — a person may casually offer advice that is entirely self-interested, without any intention to make a legal concession. The act of signing a deed creates an admission that cannot be explained away; the act of giving advice does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן – הָתָם עֲבַד לֵיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה; אֲבָל הָכָא דִּבּוּרָא – עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּמִיקְּרֵי וְאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **You** may **even say** that Rava's ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis. There,** in the case of the mishna in tractate *Ketubot*, by signing the bill of sale the claimant **performed an action** indicating that the field was not his **for** the benefit of the possessor of the field, **but here,** in Rava's case, there was no action, only **speech,** and **a person is apt to casually say** statements, and he does not lose his right by virtue of this.
