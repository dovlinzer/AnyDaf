# Yevamot 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Brother's Wife After Con…

### Ger & Brother's Wife

The daf opens with a question that has occupied the preceding discussion: may a *ger* marry the wife of his biological brother after both have converted? The Gemara has been bringing various proofs, each of which it finds ways to deflect. The current attempt to resolve the matter comes from a *baraita* introduced with the formula *ta shema*.

The *baraita* opens with the case of *ger she-haysa lidato bi-kedushah ve-horato she-lo bi-kedushah* — a convert whose conception occurred before his mother's conversion but whose birth occurred after it. The ruling: *yesh lo she'ar ha-em, ve-ein lo she'ar ha-av* — there remains a prohibited-relative relationship on the mother's side, but not on the father's side. The practical illustration given is *nasa achoto min ha-em, yotzi* — if he marries a woman who is his maternal half-sister (biologically, though not legally, since she was born before their mother converted and the legal bond of kinship was severed), he must divorce her. But if she is only his paternal half-sister, *yekayem* — he may maintain the marriage. What is striking here is that despite the obvious biological incest, the paternal relation does not trouble us. The reason, as the preceding discussion has established, is that relationships on the mother's side are far more evidently perceived by others as genuine familial bonds, whereas paternal relationships among non-Jews are not perceived in the same way.

### Maternal vs. Paternal Si…

The *baraita* continues with a further distinction. *Achot ha-av min ha-em, yotzi* — if his father's sister is a maternal sibling of his father's (i.e., the father and the aunt share a mother), he must divorce her. But if the father and his sister are only paternal siblings, *yekayem* — the marriage stands. The same logic operating on the sibling level is extended one generation upward: whether the relation is problematic depends on whether the sibling bond runs through the mother or through the father.

Similarly, *achot ha-em min ha-em, yotzi* — a mother's maternal sister clearly falls under the prohibition. Then comes the interesting mirror case: *achot ha-em min ha-av* — a mother's sister who is related to his mother only through their shared father (a paternal sister). Here the mother is one link in the chain, but the sibling bond itself is paternal. *Rabbi Meir omer yotzi* — Rabbi Meir says he must still divorce, on the grounds that *kol ervah she-mishum she'ar ha-em yotzi, mishum ha-av yekayem*: any prohibited relation that involves the maternal line at any point requires dissolution. *Chachamim omrim yekayem* — the Sages, however, hold that what matters is the nature of the sibling bond, and a paternal sibling bond is not treated as a prohibited maternal relationship even when one generation removed through the mother.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot Debate

The underlying rationale for why the maternal side generates this prohibition while the paternal side does not is debated by *Rashi* and *Tosafot*. *Rashi* frames it as a *gezeirah*: if we allow this person to maintain a marriage to a woman who was his sister before conversion, we will come to allow him to marry a sister who was born after conversion — a woman who is legally his sister under Jewish law. *Tosafot* rejects the *gezeirah* framing and instead argues that the concern is one of appearance (*mar'it ayin*): because the maternal relationship is perceived by onlookers as a genuine sibling bond, allowing such a marriage looks inherently improper. According to *Tosafot*, there is no fear of a mistaken legal conclusion, merely of a disturbing social appearance. *Tosafot* notes that the practical implications of these two positions differ in certain cases, and takes the appearance-based rationale as more fundamental. The critical point for both opinions is that the concern is triggered specifically by the maternal relation, which is the one that registers publicly as a family bond.

### Father's Sibling Relatio…

The extension to the father's sibling relationship warrants brief clarification. Even though the case of *achot ha-av min ha-em* runs through the father — the ger's relation to this woman is via his father's sibling bond — *Rashi* explains that since the category remains one of sibling, and the sibling bond in question runs through a mother (the father and his sister share a mother), the same sensitivity applies. The logic is that we are still operating within the framework of maternal siblinghood, even if the pathway to the ger runs through his father. This is why Rabbi Meir's sweeping rule captures it: any *ervah* that reaches the convert through a maternal-sibling link requires divorce.

### Brother & Uncle's Wife P…

The *baraita* now turns directly to the very question the Gemara has been investigating. *U-mutar be-eshet achiv me-imo* — he is permitted to marry his maternal brother's wife. This is exactly our question, and the *baraita* answers it affirmatively. Even though he may not marry his maternal sister herself, he may marry his maternal brother's wife — the reasoning being that while the blood relation on the maternal side is perceived as genuine, the marital extension (his brother's wife) is one further degree removed, and we are correspondingly more lenient. *U-ve-eshet achi aviv* — he is likewise permitted to marry his uncle's wife, since again the bond is through marriage rather than blood. *Ve-she'ar kol arayot mutarot lo* — and all other formerly prohibited relations are now permitted to him. The Gemara specifies that *le-atuyei eshet ha-av* — this includes, for example, his father's wife, who is no longer legally related to him since his father was a non-Jew.

### Mother-Daughter Marriage…

A striking case follows: *nasa isha u-vittah* — if before his conversion he had been married to both a woman and her daughter, upon converting *kones achat u-motzi achat* — he may keep one of them but must divorce the other. This might seem puzzling: if both women converted along with him, they are no longer legally mother and daughter — *ger she-nitgayyer ke-katan she-nolad dami*, a convert is like a newborn with no prior legal relations. So why can he not keep both? The answer is that this is a case of blood relation — a biological mother and daughter — and the perception of that bond is so powerful that we do not permit him to remain married to both simultaneously, even after conversion. The *baraita* adds *lechatchila lo yichnos* — he should not enter such an arrangement even initially.

### Baraita on Brother's Wife

The Gemara now zeroes in on the section of the *baraita* relevant to its inquiry: *katani mihat u-mutar be-eshet achiv* — the *baraita* taught that he is permitted to marry his brother's wife. The Gemara asks: *mai lav de-nasvu ke-shehu ger* — does this not refer to the obvious case where his brother married this woman after the *ger* had already converted, making her legally his brother's wife while the *ger* and his brother share a genuine legal bond of siblinghood? If so, this *baraita* would directly answer our question in the permissive direction.

### Non-Jew Marriage Resolut…

The Gemara deflects: *lo, de-nasvu ke-shehu akum* — no, the *baraita* speaks of a woman his brother had married while still a non-Jew. In that case, the brother's conversion dissolves the marriage, meaning the woman was never legally his brother's wife under Jewish law. The Gemara has now deployed the same escape it used in every prior case: the *baraita* speaks only of a pre-conversion marriage.

### No Gezerah Concern

But then the Gemara adds: *mahu de-teima, ligzor ke-shehu akum atu ke-shehu ger* — one might have thought we should issue a decree prohibiting even a pre-conversion brother's-wife relationship lest it lead to permitting the post-conversion scenario. *Kamashma lan* — the *baraita* teaches us that we make no such decree. The practical upshot of the Gemara's entire discussion is thus: the evidence from all these cases, when taken together, clearly points to the conclusion that a *ger* marrying his biological brother's wife is permitted at least *mi-de-oraita*, and the evidence strongly suggests it is permitted *mi-de-rabbanan* as well — though the Gemara has not arrived at a definitive resolution.

### Lechatchila Restrictions

The Gemara returns to the *lechatchila* ruling in the *baraita*: *nasa isha u-vittah, kones achat u-motzi achat, lechatchila lo yichnos*. The Gemara objects: *hashtah mafik mafik, lechatchila mibaya?* — if he must divorce one of them even after the fact, is it necessary to add that he shouldn't do it *lechatchila*? The Gemara resolves this: the *lechatchila* clause does not refer to the mother-daughter case immediately preceding it, but rather to the earlier rulings — those cases where the Rabbis said he may maintain a marriage *bedi'avad*. On those, the *baraita* now adds: even though *bedi'avad* the marriage stands, one should not enter into such a situation *lechatchila*, even in something like a paternal sister case.

### Wife's Death & Mother-in…

The *baraita* concludes with: *metah ishto mutar be-chamato* — after his wife dies, he may marry her mother (the biological mother, now a convert and hence legally no longer his mother-in-law). *Ve-ika de-amrei asur be-chamato* — some versions state that even after the wife's death he may not marry her. The Gemara identifies these two positions: *chada ke-Rabbi Yishmael ve-chada ke-Rabbi Akiva*.

The stringent view follows Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that a man's mother-in-law remains fully prohibited even after his wife's death — *chamato le-achar mitah be-isura kayma*, the prohibition does not weaken. Accordingly, *le-gabei ger, gazru bei Rabbanan* — the Rabbis imposed the same rule on the *ger*, making no distinction for the post-death scenario. The lenient view follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds that after the wife's death the prohibition against the mother-in-law weakens — *kalash lei isura*. *Tosafot* notes that "weakened" for Rabbi Akiva still means a biblical prohibition carrying *karet*, but it is no longer a capital offense. Since there is some degree of weakening, and the prohibition applicable to the *ger* in any event is only *mi-de-rabbanan*, the Rabbis did not extend their decree to the post-death scenario according to this view. *Tosafot* concedes that the reasoning is not entirely obvious — why a non-capital prohibition should generate a more lenient rabbinic stance toward the *ger* — but accepts this as the Gemara's framework.

## Mixed Children Scenarios

### Five Women Setup

The Mishnah now introduces what is one of the more involved practical constructions in the tractate. *Chamesh nashim she-nisarvu vildoseihen* — five women whose newborn children became mixed up with one another. The scenario is that the women were all in the same location — a cave, or as one might modernize it, a darkened ward — and gave birth simultaneously, after which it became impossible to determine whose child was whose. The critical detail is that each of these five women also has an older child whose identity is certain. Thus there are two groups of sons: five young men of known identity, one per mother, and five young men of completely uncertain identity, any one of whom could be the son of any of the five mothers.

All five of the uncertain-identity sons grow up, marry women, and then die childless. This triggers the laws of *yibum* and *chalitzah*: the widows require either levirate marriage or release. The known brothers — one per mother — must step forward, but the problem is that nobody knows which of the five mixed sons is his brother.

### Rotation & Yibum

The Mishnah's ruling: *arba'ah choltzin le-achat ve-echad meyabbemet* — for each widow, four of the known brothers perform *chalitzah* and one performs *yibum*. The rotation proceeds so that for each successive widow, a different known brother is the one who performs *yibum*. The Gemara clarifies that this is not the only valid arrangement — one could in principle have a single man perform *yibum* with all five widows — but the Mishnah teaches that the rotating arrangement is also permitted and perhaps preferable, as each man may thereby happen to perform *yibum* with his own sister-in-law.

The Gemara makes explicit why the rotation is preferable: *dilma mistarmia le-didei* — perhaps by this arrangement each man's rightful sister-in-law will fall to him, maximizing the chance that each act of *yibum* is halakhically optimal. From a purely combinatorial standpoint, if one man performs *yibum* with all five, exactly one of the five acts of *yibum* will be with his genuine sister-in-law, while the remaining four are simply permitted marriages to unrelated women. With the rotation, one could in a favorable case have all five *yibum* acts performed by the correct men — though one could equally have none done correctly.

Regarding the widows' situation: it is worth noting that the Mishnah does not simply require all five known brothers to perform *chalitzah* and leave the women as *chalutzohs*. Rather, it actively structures a rotation to ensure each woman receives *yibum* as well, reflecting a concern either for the woman's interests or for the performance of the *mitzvah* of *yibum* itself.

### Chalitzah Before Yibum

The Gemara adds an important procedural requirement: *vedavka machlatz ve-hadar yibbumei* — specifically, the *chalitzah* from the four must precede the *yibum* by the fifth. One may not begin with *yibum*: *aval yibumei be-reisha la, de-kapaga bi-yevamah la-shuk* — because if *yibum* is performed first, the man might be having relations with a woman who is in fact not his sister-in-law but still requires *chalitzah* from someone else to be released. To cohabit with such a woman would constitute *yevamah la-shuk*, a violation of her *yibbum*-bond without proper resolution.

### Partial Brothers' Chalit…

The Mishnah continues: *miktzatan achin u-miktzatan she-ein achin* — some of the known brothers are full brothers to some of the mixed sons and mere maternal half-brothers to others. The ruling seems strange at first: *ha-achin choltzin ve-she-ein achin meyabbemin* — the brothers do *chalitzah* and the non-brothers do *yibum*. The Gemara asks: *mai ka-amar* — what could this possibly mean? A maternal half-brother has no obligation of *yibum* or *chalitzah* whatsoever.

### Paternal-Maternal Mix

Rav Safra explains: *miktzat achin min ha-av u-miktzat achin min ha-em* — the case is where some of the known brothers are only paternal half-brothers and some are only maternal half-brothers to the mixed sons. *Achin min ha-em choltzin ve-achin min ha-av meyabbemin* — the maternal half-brothers perform *chalitzah* and the paternal half-brothers perform *yibum*?

This too seems backwards. *Rashi* clarifies the underlying situation: suppose one of the known brothers is a paternal half-brother to one of the mixed sons and a maternal half-brother to another. He cannot perform *yibum*, because one of the widows might be the wife of his maternal half-brother — and cohabiting with his maternal half-brother's wife would constitute an *issur arayot* with no *mitzvah* of *yibum* attached. He must, however, perform *chalitzah* with all the widows, because he needs to free the wife of his paternal half-brother. The combined effect is: *chalitzah* for all, and no *yibum*.

### Kohanim Restrictions

*Miktzatan kohanim u-miktzatan she-ein kohanim* — some of the known brothers are *kohanim* and some are not. A *kohen* may not marry a *chalutzah*, but *chalitzah* must precede *yibum*. Therefore, once a widow has received *chalitzah* from others, the *kohen* may no longer perform *yibum* with her — she has become a *chalutzah*. Accordingly: *kohanim choltzin ve-she-ein kohanim meyabbemin* — the *kohanim* can only perform *chalitzah*, while the non-*kohanim* perform *yibum*. In this arrangement, a single non-*kohen* may end up performing *yibum* with more than one widow, since the equal one-to-one rotation of the Mishnah's first scenario no longer holds.

### All Chalitzah Cases

The Mishnah then presents the most restrictive configuration: *miktzatan kohanim u-miktzatan achin min ha-em* — some have the *kohen* restriction and others have the maternal-half-brother restriction, with no one in the group who has neither limitation. In such a case, *elu va-elu choltzin ve-lo meyabbemin* — every one of the known brothers can only perform *chalitzah*, and no *yibum* is performed at all. Neither category of men can perform *yibum*: those with a maternal half-brother among the mixed sons cannot, lest they commit an *issur arayot*, and the *kohanim* cannot, lest they marry a *chalutzah*.

## Doubtful Incest Relations

### Mother & Other Woman

The Gemara now presents a series of cases where a person may be obligated to perform *chalitzah* with a woman who is possibly his own close relative. These scenarios emerge from the same framework of mixed-up children.

*Yesh choletz le-imo mi-safek la-achoto mi-safek le-vito mi-safek* — there are cases in which someone may be required to perform *chalitzah* with his mother under uncertainty, with his sister under uncertainty, or with his daughter under uncertainty.

The case of the mother: *imo ve-ishah acheret, ve-lahen shenei zecharim, ve-chazru ve-yaldu shenei zecharim be-me'arah* — his mother and another woman each have sons whose identity is known, and then both women give birth to two sons in a cave, with the identities mixed. *U-va benah shel zo ve-nasa imah shel zo u-meitu* — one of the known sons married the other woman, and then both men die childless. Now the widow requires *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The obligation falls on the deceased husband's brother — but one of the mixed-identity sons is certainly the deceased's brother, while the other is the widow's son. Neither can perform *yibum*. But who does *chalitzah*? Since we do not know which of the two mixed-identity men is the brother, both must perform *chalitzah*. The result is *nimtza kol echad ve-echad choletz le-imo mi-safek* — at least one of the men is definitively performing *chalitzah* with his own mother, though under uncertainty.

### Brother-Sister Mix

The case of the sister: *imo ve-ishah acheret she-yaldu shtei nekevot be-machba* — his mother and another woman gave birth to two daughters in hiding, with identities mixed. *U-va'u acheihem shelo mi-ota ha-em ve-nasa'um* — their paternal half-brothers (sharing the same father but different mothers) came and married these two daughters. This is permitted: since the brothers share only a father and not a mother with these daughters, and the daughters' identities are entirely unknown, these men may marry them. The husbands then die childless.

Now the paternal half-brothers of the deceased must perform *chalitzah*. But two women must receive *chalitzah*, and one of the two women is the surviving brother's sister — the daughter born to his mother. He cannot perform *yibum* and must perform *chalitzah* with both. He is therefore definitively performing *chalitzah* with his sister, albeit under uncertainty as to which one she is. *Nimtza choletz achoto mi-safek*.

### Paternal Brothers & Daug…

The case of the daughter: *isha ve-ishah acheret she-yaldu shtei nekevot be-machba* — his wife and another woman gave birth to two daughters in a cave, identities mixed. *U-va'u acheihem ve-nasa'um* — the man's brothers came and married these daughters. A man is permitted to marry his niece, so no prohibition applies at the time of the marriages. The brothers then die childless. The surviving brother — the father of one of the girls — must now perform *chalitzah* with both widows, since one of them is his daughter. *Choletz le-vito mi-safek*.

*Rashi* works to make each of these cases as precise and structurally parallel as possible, so that in each scenario the person performing *chalitzah* is certainly doing so with his close relative — not merely possibly doing so — while the uncertainty lies only in *which* of the women that relative is. The Gemara confirms this reading and notes that the parallel constructions for each sub-case can be built either with one brother performing *chalitzah* to both women or with two brothers on either side, the result in each case being the same.

### Niece & Daughter

The subsection of the daughter case draws the section to a close. The Gemara confirms that in the scenario of *isha ve-ishah acheret*, his wife and another woman giving birth to daughters whose identities are mixed, and his brothers marrying those daughters — both daughters must receive *chalitzah* from the surviving brother. *Zeh choletz le-vito mi-safek ve-zeh choletz le-vito mi-safek* — each of the relevant men is performing *chalitzah* with his own possible daughter.

## Five Categories Scenario

### Slave Conversion Setup

The Gemara now introduces a celebrated *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Meir: *pe'amim she-molidin chamesh umot* — sometimes a man and a woman can produce children belonging to five distinct national-halachic categories. *Yisrael she-lakach eved ve-shifcha min ha-shuk* — a Jew purchased a male and female non-Jewish slave from the marketplace. *Ve-lahem shenei vanim* — they already had two biological children. These children are not themselves slaves: either because children of non-Jewish slaves owned by non-Jews were not automatically considered the property of a new Jewish owner, or — as *Rashi* discusses — because the parents were enslaved only after the children had already been born.

*Ve-nitgayyer echad mehem* — one of those two children converted. The result: *nimtza echad geir ve-echad oved kochavim* — one child is a *ger* and one is a non-Jew. *Hitbilu le-shem avdut* — the two parents were then immersed in a *mikveh* to become *avdei Kena'ani* of the Jewish owner. *Ve-nizkak zeh la-zeh* — they then cohabited. A child born at this point is a slave. So now there are three children: a *ger*, a non-Jew, and a slave.

### Mamzer from Eved

*Shichrar et ha-shifcha* — the master then freed the female slave. *U-va aleha ha-eved* — the male slave continued to cohabit with her. The child of a slave and a free woman, following the position of Rabbi Meir (who defines a *mamzer* broadly to include such a union), is a *mamzer*. Thus the fourth category is added: *harei kan geir ve-oved kochavim ve-eved u-mamzer*. The Gemara confirms this is specifically the *chiddush* of the case: *oved kochavim ve-eved ha-ba al bat Yisrael havei mamzer* — an *eved* who cohabits with a free woman produces a *mamzer*, at least according to this *baraita* and its underlying authority.

### Israeli-born Status

*Shichrar shenehem ve-hisiam zeh la-zeh* — the master then freed both the male slave and the woman and caused them to marry one another. The child born of a freed male slave and a freed female slave who are now legally married is a full-fledged Jew born in Israel — a *Yisrael*. Thus: *harei kan geir ve-oved kochavim ve-eved u-mamzer ve-Yisrael* — five distinct categories from one couple.

## Selling Father for Ketub…

### Mother's Ketubah Lien

Another playful *baraita*: *yesh mocher et aviv le-hagbot et ketuvas immo* — there is a case in which a man sells his own father in order to pay his mother's *ketubah*. *Keitzad?* — how?

*Yisrael she-lakach eved ve-shifcha min ha-shuk, ve-lahem ben* — a Jew bought a male and female slave who had a son. *Ve-shichrar et ha-shifcha ve-nesa'ah* — the master freed the female slave and married her. He now owns his new wife's former husband as a slave. *Ve-amar ve-chasav kol nechasai le-vni* — after marrying this woman, the master transferred all his property to her son — the son she had had with the slave. When the master dies, his wife (the freed slave, now his widow) wishes to collect her *ketubah*. The lien for the *ketubah* falls on her son, who now owns all the property — including his own biological father, who remains a slave. In order to pay off his mother's *ketubah*, the son sells his father.

### Slave Lien Chiddush

*Ma kamashma lan?* — what is the *chiddush*? *Kula Rabbi Meir hi* — this *baraita* follows Rabbi Meir throughout. *Ve-avda ke-metalteli, u-metalteli mishtabdi li-ketuvah* — the legal novelty is that Rabbi Meir holds that an *eved* has the status of movable property (*metalteli*), and Rabbi Meir further holds that movable property is subject to the lien of a *ketubah*. Alternatively, *ve-i ba'it eima, avda ke-mikarkei dami* — one might say the *chiddush* is that Rabbi Meir treats an *eved* as real property (*mikarka'i*), which is obviously subject to a *ketubah* lien. The point in either reading is a legal principle about *eved* and *ketubah* liens — not a moral lesson about selling one's father.

## Uncle-Nephew Mix

### Uncle-Nephew Setup

The Mishnah now presents a variation: *ha-isha she-nitarev valdah be-vlad kallatah* — a woman whose newborn child was mixed up with her daughter-in-law's newborn child. The result is a man and his nephew of uncertain identity — neither the uncle nor the nephew knows who he is. Both men grow up, marry, and die childless.

### Other Brothers Chalitzah

The Mishnah distinguishes between different categories of brothers who must now respond. *Bnei ha-kalah choltzin ve-lo meyabbemin* — the other sons of the daughter-in-law (i.e., the additional brothers of the mixed sons from that side) may only perform *chalitzah*, not *yibum*. The reason: *safek eshet achiv safek eshet achei aviv* — each widow might be a brother's wife (requiring *yibum*) or might be an uncle's wife (an *ervah*). Since the *ervah* possibility exists, *yibum* is prohibited.

### Flexible Chalitzah or Yi…

*Bnei ha-zekenah* — the other sons of the older woman (from the grandmother's line, so to speak) are in a more favorable position. For them, *o choltzin o meyabbemin* — they may perform either *chalitzah* or *yibum*. The reason: *safek eshet achiv ve-eshet ben achiv* — each widow is either a brother's wife or a nephew's wife. Both possibilities permit *yibum* or *chalitzah* without any *ervah* concern.

### Role Reversal

*Meitu ha-kesherim* — the Gemara specifies that when "the kosher ones" die, the reference is not that the others are disqualified (*pesulim*) in some way, but simply that those of certain identity (*ha-vadayim*) have now died. Rav Pappa accordingly emends the reading: *eima u-meitu ha-vadayim* — read it as "those of certain identity died." The situation then reverses: *ha-ta'arovot li-vnei ha-zekenah choltzin ve-lo meyabbemin*, while *li-vnei ha-kalah echad choletz ve-echad meyabem* — the analysis mirrors what was laid out above, with the widow categories flipped. As before, the Gemara emphasizes: *vedavka be-reisha chalitzah ve-hadar yibum, aval yibum be-reisha la* — the mandatory order is *chalitzah* first, then *yibum*, never the reverse.

## Kohen & Slave Child Mix

### Terumah Sharing

The final Mishnah of the daf introduces the case of *kohenet she-nitarev valdah be-vlad shifchatah* — a *kohen*'s wife whose newborn child was mixed up with her slave's newborn child. One of the two mixed children is a *kohen* and one is a *kohen*'s slave. Since the slave of a *kohen* eats *terumah*, both children may eat *terumah* — whichever one is the slave eats by virtue of his status as the slave of a *kohen*, and the other eats as the *kohen* himself. *Chelkei ke-echad ba-goren* — at the threshing floor they receive one combined portion, since regardless of which is which, together they are entitled to only a single family portion.

### Restrictions While Mixed

*Ve-einam mitam'im le-meitim* — they may not contract corpse-*tumah*, since one of them is a *kohen*. *Ve-einam nosim nashim* — they may not marry, since the *kohen* among them may only marry certain categories of women while the slave may not marry at all; the two categories of permitted women are mutually exclusive. *Bein kesherot bein pesulot* — this restriction holds regardless of whether the prospective wife is of fully valid lineage or not.

### After Mutual Freedom

*Higdilu ha-ta'arovot ve-shichreru zeh et zeh* — when they grew up, they freed one another: each declared that if the other is the slave and he himself is the master, he frees him. Now both are free men. *Nosim nashim re'uyot li-khunah* — they may now marry women who are fit to marry *kohanim*, since whoever the *kohen* among them is, he must marry appropriately, and the freed slave may also marry such women. *Ve-einam mitam'im le-meitim* — they still may not contract corpse-*tumah*, as one remains a *kohen*. *Ve-im nitme'u einam sofgin et ha-arba'im* — if they did contract *tumah*, they do not receive lashes, because lashes require certainty as to which man is the definite *kohen*.

### Terumah Selling Solution

*Ve-einam ochlim bi-terumah* — they may no longer eat *terumah*. Previously, even the slave was permitted because a *kohen*'s slave eats *terumah*. Now that both are free, neither can be presumed to be the *kohen*'s slave, and the uncertainty means neither may eat. *Ve-im achlu einam meshalmin keren ve-chomesh* — if they did eat *terumah*, they need not pay the principal and the fifth that a non-*kohen* who eats *terumah* must pay, since either of them might in fact be a *kohen* eating his own *terumah*.

*Ve-einam cholkim al ha-goren* — they cannot receive *terumah* at the threshing floor, since neither is certainly a *kohen* or a *kohen*'s slave. *U-mochrin et ha-terumah ve-ha-damim shelahen* — however, the *terumah* they separate from their own produce they need not give to anyone else. They may sell it and keep the money. The rationale is *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah*: the claimant bears the burden of proof. Anyone seeking to take their *terumah* would need to prove that they are not *kohanim*, and this cannot be established. They themselves, however, may not eat it, since they might equally not be *kohanim*.

*Ve-einam cholkim be-kodshei ha-mikdash* — they do not receive a share in the *kodshim* of the *Beit Hamikdash*, since one may not be a *kohen*. *Ve-ein notnim lahem kodshim* — one does not give them *korbanot* to sacrifice, nor — as will be seen — does one give them a *bechor*.

### Bechor Waiting for Mum

The Mishnah addresses *bechor*: if one of them has a firstborn animal, *yireh ad she-yista'ev* — it is allowed to graze until it develops a *mum* (a blemish). Once the *bechor* has a *mum*, the *kohen* may eat it outside the *Beit Hamikdash* without the act of *korban* being brought. Since one of the two men is a *kohen*, and since neither needs to hand the animal to another *kohen* (by virtue of *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah*), they wait out the *bechor*'s life until it is blemished and then eat it.

Regarding their own *korbanot*: *Rashi* addresses two possibilities. If they have a *bechor*, the procedure is as above. If they have an *olah* or *chatat* to bring, they may designate a *kohen* to perform the *avodah* on their behalf and declare that if they themselves are the *kohen*, they retain the right to the *or* (hide) or equivalent, since it may belong to them — but they cannot personally perform the *avodah* or eat from the *kodshim* portions.

### Double Stringencies

*Ve-notnim alav chomrei kohanim ve-chomrei Yisraelim* — they are treated with the stringencies of both *kohanim* and *Yisraelim*. This formulation encapsulates the entire preceding discussion: any stringency that attaches to *kohanim* applies because one of them may be the *kohen*, and any stringency that attaches to ordinary Israelites applies because one of them may be the non-*kohen*. The leniences of either status are unavailable to either man in their mixed condition.

## Terumah & Kohen Percepti…

### Rabbi Yehudah's Rule

The Mishnah's ruling that the two mixed children must go to the threshing floor together — *chelek ke-echad* — is now explained in light of a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yossi. *Ein cholkin terumah le-eved ela im kein rabo imo, divrei Rabbi Yehudah* — Rabbi Yehudah holds that one does not distribute *terumah* to a slave unless his master is present with him. The *baraita* requires the two of them to come together: *chelek ke-echad* means they must appear together to collect.

### Rabbi Yossi's Allowance

*Rabbi Yossi omer yachol she-yomar: im kohen ani, tnu li bishvil atzmi; ve-im eved le-kohen ani, tnu li bishvil rabbi* — Rabbi Yossi says the slave may appear alone and state: "If I am a *kohen*, give it to me on my own account; if I am a slave of a *kohen*, give it to me on behalf of my master." Under this reasoning, the slave's presence alone is sufficient, and there is no need for the master to accompany him.

### Perception-Based Halacha

The Gemara explains Rabbi Yehudah's position: *bi-mkomo shel Rabbi Yehudah hayu ma'alin mi-terumah le-yuchasin* — in Rabbi Yehudah's locale, the practice of collecting *terumah* was treated as evidence of *kohen* lineage. If a man came to the threshing floor and collected *terumah*, people would assume he was a *kohen* and would arrange marriages for him accordingly — marrying him off to women appropriate for *kohanim*.

Given this communal expectation, if a slave were to go alone to collect *terumah*, bystanders would assume he was a *kohen* and treat him as such in matters of marriage and lineage. Therefore, in Rabbi Yehudah's locale, the rule is that a slave may only collect *terumah* in the presence of his master, making the nature of the arrangement evident. The logic is circular in an instructive way: because only *kohanim* collect *terumah*, people infer that *kohen* status from *terumah* collection; and because people draw that inference, the halacha must protect against it by ensuring no slave collects alone. *Bi-mkomo shel Rabbi Yossi lo hayu ma'alin mi-terumah le-yuchasin* — in Rabbi Yossi's locale, no such assumption was made. People understood that one who collected *terumah* might be a slave doing so on his master's behalf, so no presumption of *kohen* lineage attached, and the slave could collect independently.

### Elazar's Testimony

A *baraita* relates: *amar Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, miyamai lo he'edeti ela eidut achat* — Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok said that in his entire life he gave only one testimony, and that testimony led to a result in which *he'elu eved li-khunah al pi* — a slave was elevated to *kohen* status on the basis of his words. He had testified that a certain man collected *terumah*, and on that basis the community treated the man as a *kohen* and arranged a marriage for him accordingly — only for it to emerge that the man was in fact a slave.

### Prevented Error

The Gemara objects: *ve-he'elu salka da'atak?* — could we truly think that such an error actually occurred and was consummated, particularly through the testimony of a *tzaddik* of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok's stature? *Hash-ta bi-vehemtan shel tzaddikim ein ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu meivi takala al yadam; tzaddikim atzmam lo kol she-kein* — if the Holy One does not allow even the animals of the righteous to stumble into transgression (as illustrated by the donkey of Pinchas ben Yair, which would not eat *tevel*), how much more would He not allow a *tzaddik* himself to cause such an outcome. Therefore: *ela eima, bikshu le-ha'alot eved li-khunah al pi* — emend the text to say that they were *about* to elevate a slave to *kohen* status on the basis of his testimony, but were prevented before the error was finalized.

### Locale Customs Difference

The Gemara then explains how such a near-error came about. *Chaza be-asrei de-Rabbi Yossi* — Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok witnessed the event in Rabbi Yossi's locale, where slaves routinely collected *terumah* at the threshing floor without their masters present, and where no inference of *kohen* lineage was drawn from such collection. He then testified about what he had seen — "I saw this man collect *terumah*" — but he gave this testimony in Rabbi Yehudah's locale, where the same act of collecting *terumah* was taken as definitive evidence of *kohen* status. The testimony was accurate as a description of events, but its halachic implications were entirely different depending on the locale's custom. In Rabbi Yehudah's locale, his truthful testimony nearly caused a slave to be enrolled as a *kohen* — and the Gemara's emendation ensures that the *tzaddik* was spared from having his words actually produce that outcome.
