# Shevuot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohanim Atonement

### Intro: Two Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁחַט אֶת שְׂעִיר הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר לָעָם – שֶׁאֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים מִתְכַּפְּרִין בּוֹ. וּבַמֶּה מִתְכַּפְּרִין? בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states with regard to the internal goat: **"He shall slaughter the goat sin-offering of the people"** (Leviticus 16:15); the term "of the people" excludes Aaron and the priests, and therefore indicates **that the priests do not achieve atonement through** that goat. **But** then, **through what do they achieve atonement?** Presumably, **through** the **bull of Aaron,** i.e., the bull of the High Priest.

The discussion at the close of the first chapter of Shevuot centers on a fundamental question: what atones for the *kohanim*? We know that *Yisrael* atones through the *seir*, the goat, but whether the *kohanim* are included in that atonement is the subject of a dispute between two *tanna'im*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that when the *seir* atones for *Yisrael* and the verse says *ba'ad asher la'am* — for the people — this phrase includes everyone, *kohanim* included. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that the *kohanim* achieve their *kapparah* through a different mechanism entirely.

### Sewage Metaphor

The underlying structure of the entire *kapparah* system on *Yom Kippur* can be visualized as a great purifying operation — all transgression flows toward one central point of cleansing, and if that final stage is not resolved, what flows back into the system remains impure and unusable. The entire enterprise depends on clarity about where each stream of *chata'im* is directed.

### Three Funnels

Concretely, the *par* — the bull — belongs to *Aharon*, and the people have the *seir*. But the precise boundary remains to be determined. Is the *par* exclusively for *Aharon* himself, given that the verse says *vichapper ba'ado* — he shall atone for him? Or does it extend to *Aharon*'s immediate family? And, as the *gemara* will demonstrate, will all the *kohanim* ultimately be folded into *Aharon*'s household? Pictured as funnels, *Aharon*'s transgressions flow clearly to the bull, the people's flow clearly to the *se'irim*, and the remaining *kohanim* stand in an uncertain middle position.

### Seir Dispute

The core question is therefore this: does the *seir* atone for *kohanim*, or only for *Yisrael*, and if the *kohanim* are excluded from the *seir*, where does their *kapparah* come from? This is precisely the dispute being worked through in the *baraita* and the subsequent *gemara* discussion.

## Baraita Analysis

### Asher La'am

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. בַּמָּה הֵם מִתְכַּפְּרִין? מוּטָב שֶׁיִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן, שֶׁהֲרֵי הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ אֵצֶל בֵּיתוֹ; וְאַל יִתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים, שֶׁהֲרֵי לֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **for** their **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. **Through which** means, then, **do they achieve atonement** for this? Is it through the internal goat or the bull of Aaron? It is **better** to say **that they achieve atonement through the bull of Aaron, as** in any event **with regard to his household an exception was made to its rule** that it atones only for Aaron. **And** one should **not** say that **they achieve atonement through the goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary, **as it is not** found that with regard to Aaron's household **an exception was made to its rule.**

The *baraita* opens with the verse from Vayikra 16:15: *veshachat et se'ir hachattat asher la'am* — he shall slaughter the goat of the sin offering that is for the people. The phrase *asher la'am* is understood as a *mi'ut*, an exclusionary formulation, which excludes *Aharon* the High Priest and all the *kohanim* from the atonement provided by this *seir*. The *gemara* then draws the direct conclusion: *she'ein hakohanim mitkaperin bo* — the *kohanim* have no *kapparah* through this goat.

### Two Pathways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר – הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בֵּית אַהֲרֹן בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is your wish to say** that this reasoning can be refuted, one can bring another proof, as **it states: "House of Aaron, bless the Lord,"** which is referring to all priests and not just to Aaron's immediate household, and so it is reasonable that Aaron's bull should atone for them.

The *baraita* then immediately affirms the alternative: *ubame mitkaperin? beparo shel Aharon* — and through what are they atoned? Through Aaron's bull. Two pathways are thus established: the *seir* for the people, the *par* for the *kohanim*. But if the analysis were this clean, the discussion would end here.

### Gap Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יִתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן; שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – מֵעַתָּה אֵין לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים״ – מָצִינוּ לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **they would** also **not achieve atonement through** the **bull of Aaron, as it is already stated:** "Aaron shall bring near **his own** bull sin-offering" (Leviticus 16:6), which indicates that it atones only for Aaron's transgressions, not for the transgressions of others. And if **that is so,** then **they do not have** any means through which to achieve **atonement.** But **when** the verse **states: "And he shall effect atonement…upon the priests"** (Leviticus 16:33), **we have** clearly **found that they** do **have** a means of achieving **atonement.**

A problem immediately surfaces. About the bull, the Torah says *asher lo* — which is to him, to *Aharon* alone. If the *par* belongs specifically to *Aharon*, then there is a gap in the system: the people are covered by the *seir*, *Aharon* is covered by the *par*, but the rest of the *kohanim* fall between the two. The *baraita* raises this possibility directly: *yachol lo yitkapru b'paro shel Aharon she'harei kvar ne'emar asher lo — me'ata ein lahem kapparah*. Perhaps the other *kohanim* have no atonement at all.

### Vayikra 16:29

This concern is resolved by an explicit verse. At the close of the *Achrei Mot* passage read on *Yom Kippur*, the Torah enumerates all those who receive atonement: *v'chiper et mikdash hakodesh v'et ohel moed v'et hamizbe'ach yichaper, v'al hakohanim v'al kol am hakahal yichaper* — he shall atone for the sanctuary, the Tent of Meeting, and the altar, and for the *kohanim* and for the entire assembled people. The Torah states explicitly, *meforash*, that the *kohanim* receive atonement. The question then becomes not whether they are atoned for, but through which mechanism: *bameh hem mitkaprin?* The *baraita* resolves this by reasoning that it is preferable for the *kohanim* to be atoned through *Aharon*'s bull — because that offering was already expanded from the narrow *asher lo* to include his household via the phrase *asher lo u'v'veito* — rather than through the *seir* that was performed inside, whose exclusionary language was never opened to include anyone beyond the people.

### Beit Aharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״אִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא ״בֵּיתוֹ״ כְּתִיב – כּוּלָּן קְרוּיִים ״בֵּיתוֹ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּית אַהֲרֹן בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳, יִרְאֵי ה׳ בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the last part of the *baraita*: **And what** possible refutation is the *baraita* referring to when it says: **If it is your wish to say** that this reasoning can be refuted? The Gemara explains: **And if you would say** of the preceding proof that it is incorrect to suggest that all priests achieve atonement from the bull of Aaron, as with regard to it the term: **"His household"** (Leviticus 16:6), **is written,** which suggests that it atones only for his immediate family, then this can be refuted, as **all of** the priests **are** collectively **referred to as his household,** as is evident from **that** which **is stated: "House of Aaron, bless the Lord;** house of Levi, bless the Lord, **those who fear the Lord, bless the Lord."**

Should any doubt remain, the *baraita* adduces a proof from Tehillim: *beit Aharon barchu et Hashem, beit ha-Levi barchu et Hashem, yirei Hashem barchu et Hashem*. The verse divides the nation into three categories — *Beit Aharon*, *Beit Ha-Levi*, and *Yirei Hashem* — without ever mentioning *kohanim* as a distinct category. The conclusion is that the *kohanim* are subsumed within *Beit Aharon*. Since the *par* is brought for *Aharon* and his household, and the *kohanim* constitute that household, the *kohanim* are atoned through the bull.

## Levi'im Question

### Tosafot Aside

A pointed question is raised by *Tosafot* on the following page: if the system accounts for the atonement of the people through the *se'irim* and for *Aharon* and his household — i.e., the *kohanim* — through the *par*, where does that leave the *Levi'im*? The location of the *Levi'im* within this system of atonement is, if anything, more difficult than the question of the *kohanim*. One might suggest that the *Levi'im* are included with *Yisrael* under *asher la'am*, and indeed that appears to be *Tosafot*'s conclusion — but *Tosafot* treats it as a genuine question, particularly given that the *gemara* here is focused specifically on *ha'am* and *hakohanim* without explicitly addressing the *Levi'im*. There is something almost poignant about the position implied: the *Levi'im* share a measure of the *kohanim*'s prestige but, when it comes to atonement, travel with the people rather than with their priestly brethren.

## Attribution

### Not Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת שְׂעִיר הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר לָעָם״ – שֶׁאֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים מִתְכַּפְּרִין בּוֹ. וּבַמֶּה מִתְכַּפְּרִין? בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן. יָכוֹל לֹא יִתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן; שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – מֵעַתָּה אֵין לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים״ – מָצִינוּ לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה. בַּמָּה הֵם מִתְכַּפְּרִין? מוּטָב שֶׁיִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּפָרוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן – שֶׁהֲרֵי הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ אֵצֶל בֵּיתוֹ, וְאַל יִתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים – שֶׁלֹּא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ אֵצֶל בֵּיתוֹ. וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר – הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בֵּית אַהֲרֹן בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳, בֵּית הַלֵּוִי בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳, יִרְאֵי ה׳ בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳״. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה; דְּאִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָאָמַר: ״כֹּהֲנִים יֵשׁ לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that which the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the internal goat: **"He shall slaughter the goat sin-offering of the people"** (Leviticus 16:15); the term "of the people" excludes Aaron and the priests, and therefore indicates **that the priests do not achieve atonement through** that goat. **But** then, **through what do they achieve atonement?** Presumably, **through** the **bull of Aaron,** i.e., the bull of the High Priest. One **might** have thought that **they would** also **not achieve atonement through** the **bull of Aaron, as it is already stated:** "Aaron shall bring near **his own** bull sin-offering" (Leviticus 16:6), which indicates that it atones only for Aaron's transgressions, not for the transgressions of others. And if **that is so,** then **they do not have** any means through which to achieve **atonement.** But **when** the verse **states: "And he shall effect atonement…upon the priests"** (Leviticus 16:33), **we have** clearly **found that they** do **have** a means of achieving **atonement.** **Through which** means then **do they achieve atonement?** It is **better** to say **that they achieve atonement through the bull of Aaron, as** in any event **with regard to his household an exception was made to its rule** that it atones only for Aaron. **And** one should **not** say that **they achieve atonement through the goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary, **as it is not** found that with regard to Aaron's **household an exception was made to its rule.** **And if it is your wish to say** that this reasoning can be refuted, one can cite another proof, as the verse **states:** "House of Israel, bless the Lord; **house of Aaron, bless the Lord; house of Levi, bless the Lord; those who fear the Lord, bless the Lord"** (Psalms 135:19–20). It is apparent from this verse that "house of Aaron" is referring to all priests and not just to Aaron's immediate household, and so it is reasonable that Aaron's bull should atone for them. **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this *baraita*? **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** It **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as, if** one suggests that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** one can counter: **Doesn't he say** that the **priests have** a means of **atonement through** the **scapegoat.**

The *gemara* now asks *man tanna* — who is the *tanna* responsible for this *baraita*? The initial response, attributed to Rabbi Yirmeyah, is that it cannot be Rabbi Yehuda. The reason is straightforward: Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *kohanim* do receive *kapparah* through the *seir hamishtalei'ach* — *d'ei Rabbi Yehuda amar kohanim yesh lahem kapparah b'seir hamishtalei'ach* — and the *baraita* we have just studied categorically denies this.

### Rava: Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַנִּי? רָבָא אָמַר: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: ״כֹּהֲנִים אֵין לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **But** then, **whose** opinion is expressed? **Rava says:** It **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that the **priests do not have** a means of achieving **atonement through** the **scapegoat.**

Rava responds that the *baraita* must reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon — *Rabbi Shimon hi d'amar kohanim ein lahem kapparah b'seir hamishtalei'ach*. Since Rabbi Shimon holds that the *kohanim* are not atoned through the scapegoat, he is the natural author of a *baraita* that attributes their *kapparah* entirely to the bull of *Aharon*.

### Abaye: Yehuda Too

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מֵעַתָּה אֵין לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים״, מָצִינוּ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת; וּכְמוֹ דְּמָצִינוּ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת, כָּךְ יֵשׁ לָהֶם כַּפָּרָה
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and **this** is what it **is saying:** If **that is so,** that the priests do not achieve atonement through the bull of Aaron, then **they do not have** any means through which to achieve **atonement for** the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. **When** the verse **states: "And he shall effect atonement…upon the priests," we have** clearly **found that they** do **have** a means of **atonement for other transgressions. And** it follows that **just as we have found that they have** a means of **atonement for other transgressions,** as Israelites do, **so** too, **they** must also **have** a means of **atonement** [for the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods].

Abaye demurs. He argues that the *baraita* can in fact be reconciled with Rabbi Yehuda's position, provided one narrows the *baraita*'s scope appropriately.

## Abaye Harmonizes

### Narrow Scope

The *baraita* is not making a sweeping claim that the *kohanim* lack any *kapparah* through the *seir* for all transgressions. Rather, it is saying specifically that for the category of *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — the impurity of the temple and the holy things — the *kohanim* do not derive *kapparah* from the *seir*. This is consistent with Rabbi Yehuda, who can still hold that for other transgressions the *seir* atones for everyone including *kohanim*.

### Par Fits Better

The *baraita*'s logic then follows: since the *par* already has an opening — via the phrase *asher lo u'v'veito*, his household — to include the *kohanim*, it is the more appropriate vehicle for their atonement in the category of *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*, while the *seir*, whose exclusionary language was never broadened, cannot serve this function for them.

### Vayikra Cited

Abaye then cites Vayikra 16:29 — the same verse establishing explicit *kapparah* for the *kohanim* — to support his reading of Rabbi Yehuda. Just as the *kohanim* are included in atonement for other transgressions, so too must they have atonement for *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*, and the *par* is the mechanism that provides it.

## Ownership Logic

### Exclusion Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא בִּדְעַם – דְּלָא מִכַּפְּרִי, דְּלָא קָא חָסְרִי בֵּיהּ מָמוֹנָא, דִּכְתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר לָעָם״; בִּדְאַהֲרֹן נָמֵי לָא קָא חָסְרִי בֵּיהּ מָמוֹנָא! וְקָאָמַר: כּוּלָּן קְרוּיִים ״בֵּיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** If both mentions of the phrase "his own" are necessary to teach about the ownership of the bull, how can the above *baraita* suggest that the phrase indicates that the bull atones only for the High Priest's transgressions? The Gemara explains: **This is what** is **difficult for the *tanna*** of the *baraita*: **What is different about** the goat **of the people that** explains why **it does not atone** for the priests? The difference is **that** the priests **did not forfeit** any **money over** the purchase of **it.** Therefore, it does not atone for the priests, but only for the Israelites, **as it is written** with regard to the internal goat: **"Of the people." With regard to** the bull **of Aaron as well,** the priests **do not forfeit** any **money over** the purchase of **it,** so it follows that they should not achieve atonement through it. **And** therefore, to explain why they do achieve atonement, the *baraita* **states** that **all of** the priests **are** collectively **referred to as: His household.**

The *baraita*'s underlying logic depends on an important principle concerning ownership and financial responsibility. The *seir* is marked by explicit exclusionary language — *asher la'am* — which removes the non-Israelite population from its atoning scope. The *par* carries a household clause — *asher lo u'v'veito* — which, by contrast, opens it up.

### Money and Kapparah

The governing principle is that only those who contribute financially to a sacrifice receive atonement from it. This is a striking idea: *kapparah* is not a free-floating benefit that attaches to all who happen to benefit from a sacrifice; it flows specifically to those who bear fiscal responsibility for it.

### Household Clause

Applying this principle: the *seir* is purchased from the public funds of *Yisrael*, and therefore it atones for *Yisrael*. The *kohanim* do not contribute to the purchase of the *seir* and therefore receive no *kapparah* from it. But the *par*, the *baraita* argues, is different. The verse says *asher lo u'v'veito* — his own, and his household. This phrase simultaneously establishes that *Aharon* must pay for the *par* from his own funds, and that the atonement of the *par* extends to his household.

### Proof Restated

The *Tehillim* verse is then invoked again to establish the identity of that household: *beit Aharon barchu et Hashem, beit ha-Levi barchu et Hashem, yirei Hashem barchu et Hashem*. The three categories of the verse — *Beit Aharon*, *Beit Ha-Levi*, and *Yirei Hashem* — encompass the entire nation, and the *kohanim*, who appear nowhere else in the verse, must be subsumed within *Beit Aharon*. *Kulam keruyim beito* — they are all called his household.

## Necessity Query

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״אֲשֶׁר לָעָם״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ דְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: מִדְּעַם לֶיהֱוֵי! הָהוּא מִ״וּמֵאֵת עֲדַת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions some of the expositions of the *baraita*: **And** with regard to **this** phrase: "Goat **of the people"** (Leviticus 16:15), **does it come to** teach **that** which the *baraita* teaches, i.e., that the priests do not achieve atonement through it? But **that** phrase **is necessary** to teach **that the Merciful One states** that the goat **must be** purchased with funds collected **from** the **people.** The Gemara refutes this: **That** requirement **is derived from** the verse: **"And from the assembly of the children of Israel** he shall take two goats" (Leviticus 16:5).

Having established the function of *asher la'am*, the *gemara* pauses to ask whether this exclusionary phrase was even necessary. Could one not derive the people-only rule from a different verse entirely, rendering *asher la'am* redundant?

### Tum'at Adat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא מֵבִיא, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And** with regard to **this** phrase: **"His own** bull sin-offering" (Leviticus 16:6), **does it come to** teach **that** which the *baraita* teaches, i.e., that it atones only for Aaron's transgressions, not for the transgressions of others? But **that** phrase **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The High Priest **brings,** i.e., purchases, the bull **from his own** funds, **but he does not bring it from** funds collected **from the public.**

The response is that *asher la'am* does not merely exclude the *kohanim* from the *seir*'s atonement — it teaches a distinct law: that the *seir* atones specifically for *tum'at adat bnei Yisrael*, the impurity of the community of Israel as a community. The phrase carries its own substantive teaching and cannot be derived from any other source. The *gemara* points to Vayikra 16:5 — *vahaya asher lo, hahu tum'at adat Yisrael* — as the verse from which the impurity of the community is learned, distinguishing its function from that of *asher la'am*.

## Asher Lo Triple

### Aaron Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יָבִיא מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר, שֶׁאֵין הַצִּבּוּר מִתְכַּפְּרִין בּוֹ; אֲבָל יָבִיא מִשֶּׁל אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים, שֶׁאֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים מִתְכַּפְּרִין בּוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״. יָכוֹל לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא כָּשֵׁר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר שׁוּב ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – הַכָּתוּב שָׁנָה עָלָיו לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **he does not bring it from** funds collected **from the public because the public does not achieve atonement through it, but he may bring** it **from** funds belonging to **his brethren the priests because his brethren the priests achieve atonement through it.** To counter this, **the verse states: "His own** bull sin-offering," to indicate that he must purchase it using only his own funds. One **might** have thought that **he should not bring** it from others' funds, **but** nevertheless, **if he did** it would still be **valid.** To counter this, **the verse again states "his own." The verse repeats** the phrase **to render** the requirement **essential.**

The *baraita* draws an inference from the phrase *asher lo* in connection with the *par*: the bull atones only for *Aharon*'s own transgressions and not for the transgressions of others. This is the starting premise before the discussion of the household clause.

### Dual Function

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty. If the phrase *asher lo* is needed to establish the ownership requirement — that *Aharon* must bring the *par* from his own funds, *mishelo hu mevi v'eino mevi mishel tzibur* — then it seems that the phrase is already "used up" for that purpose. How can the same phrase simultaneously teach a scope limitation, restricting the *par*'s atonement to *Aharon* alone? A phrase cannot carry two distinct teachings, or can it?

### Repetition Logic

The answer lies in the fact that in the relevant section of Vayikra, the phrase *asher lo* appears not once but three times. Each repetition adds a layer of meaning. The first occurrence establishes that the bull atones for *Aharon* himself. The second, in conjunction with *u'v'veito*, opens the atonement to his household — i.e., the *kohanim*. The third emphasizes the ownership requirement: *Aharon* cannot bring the *par* from communal funds. This is discussed at length in the context of Tractate Yoma, where the triple repetition of *asher lo* is carefully parsed. The multiplicity of the phrase allows it to carry all three teachings simultaneously without collision.

### Reconciliation

The resulting picture, then, is that the exclusionary force of *asher lo* — which might have been read to confine atonement to *Aharon* alone — is counterbalanced by the household clause and supported by the *Tehillim* verse establishing the *kohanim* as *Beit Aharon*. Ownership language and scope language coexist, each anchored in a distinct occurrence of the phrase.

## Financial Difference

### Seir Failure

The *baraita* now poses the underlying difficulty directly: what explains the *seir*'s failure to atone for the *kohanim*?

### No Contribution

The answer returns to the principle of financial participation: *ma shna de'am delo mechaper delo ka chasri bei mamona d'chtiv asher la'am* — what is different about the goat of the people is that the *kohanim* did not forfeit any money for its purchase. Since the *seir* is funded by *Yisrael*, only *Yisrael* receives its *kapparah*.

### Inverse Logic

This immediately generates a symmetrical problem for the *par*. If the *kohanim* do not contribute money toward the *par* — since it is funded by *Aharon* from his own resources — then by the same logic, the *kohanim* should not receive atonement from it. Why should the *par* atone for those who did not pay for it?

### Rescue Clause

The rescue comes from the phrase *asher lo u'v'veito*. The Torah requires that the *par* be *Aharon*'s own, purchased from his personal funds — and it is this requirement of personal financial responsibility that justifies extending the *par*'s atoning power to his household. The household expansion is not gratuitous; it is precisely calibrated to the ownership structure the Torah establishes. *Aharon* pays; his household benefits. The *kohanim*, as members of *Beit Aharon*, are included.

## Shimon Framework

### Parallel Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב תְּרֵי וִידּוּיִן וְדַם הַפָּר, חַד כְּנֶגֶד שָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים, וְחַד כְּנֶגֶד שָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ, וְחַד כְּנֶגֶד שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ;
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the priests do not achieve atonement through the scapegoat, **that is** why **it is written** in the Torah that **two confessions** are to be recited over the bull **and** that the **blood of the bull** is to be presented inside the Sanctuary: Of these three forms of atonement, **one corresponds to** the atonement provided by the **goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary, **one corresponds to** the atonement provided by the **goat whose** blood presentation is **performed outside** the Sanctuary, **and one corresponds to** the atonement provided by **the scapegoat.**

With the mechanics of the *baraita* now fully explained, the *gemara* turns to examine how Rabbi Shimon's framework accounts for the same set of concerns. Rabbi Shimon holds that there are two *viduyim* — confessions — and the blood of the *par*, and these parallel three *se'irim* for the people. His enumeration proceeds as follows: one *seir* that is sacrificed inside, one *seir* that is sacrificed outside, and one *seir hamishtalei'ach* — the scapegoat. These three *se'irim* correspond to the people's atonement, while the two confessions and the *par* blood address the atonement of *Aharon* and his household.

### Shimon Logic

The reason Rabbi Shimon requires two confessions is precisely that, in his view, the *seir* provides no *kapparah* for the *kohanim*. The *seir* cannot be counted in any enumeration relevant to the *kohanim*'s atonement. Therefore, the two *viduyim* must each carry independent weight within the structure of *Aharon*'s *par*.

## Yehuda Reconciles

### One Confession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, תְּרֵי וִידּוּיִן וְדַם הַפָּר לְמָה לִי? בְּחַד וִידּוּי וְדָמוֹ סַגְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that the priests do achieve atonement through the scapegoat, **why do I** need the **two confessions** recited over the bull **and** the **blood of the bull** to be presented inside the Sanctuary? **One confession** over the bull **and its blood** being presented inside the Sanctuary **would be sufficient.**

The *gemara* then asks how Rabbi Yehuda can operate with only one *viduy* and one blood application when it would seem that one of each should be sufficient.

### Two Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד לוֹ, וְאֶחָד לְבֵיתוֹ. כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כָּךְ הִיא מִדַּת הַדִּין נוֹהֶגֶת: מוּטָב יָבוֹא זַכַּאי וִיכַפֵּר עַל הַחַיָּיב, וְאַל יָבוֹא חַיָּיב וִיכַפֵּר עַל הַחַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Two confessions are necessary, **one for** the High Priest **himself, and one for his household,** i.e., the priests, **as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The High Priest must first confess his own transgressions and only afterward those of the priests, because **that is** how **the attribute of justice functions:** It is **better that the innocent come and atone for the guilty, than** that **the guilty come and atone for the guilty.** When the High Priest confesses the transgressions of those in his house, it is better that he already be considered innocent, having confessed and been absolved of his own transgressions.

The answer is that the two mentions within the *par* ritual — *asher lo* and *asher lo u'v'veito* — are split between *Aharon* himself and his household respectively. One mention of *asher lo* covers *Aharon*'s personal atonement, and the second, with the addition of *u'v'veito*, covers the *kohanim* who constitute his household. Two confessions are thus necessitated by the Torah's own bifurcated language: *echad lo v'echad le'veito*.

## Order Principle

### Justice Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ שְׁבוּעוֹת שְׁתַּיִם
>
> **Translation:** [Concluding formula indicating the end of Tractate Shevuot, Chapter One]

Completing the first chapter, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael that articulates a general principle of justice: *mutav yavo zakai v'yechaper al hachayav v'al yavo chayav v'yechaper al hachayav* — it is preferable that the innocent one come and atone for the guilty, rather than that the guilty one come and atone for the guilty.

### Aaron First

This principle explains the ordering of the *Yom Kippur* service itself. *Aharon* must first achieve his own atonement — becoming, in the language of the *baraita*, a *zakai*, one who is clean — before he can function as the agent of atonement for others. Only once he has been purified through the *par* can he proceed to atone for the *kohanim* and, ultimately, for all of *Yisrael*.

### Chapter One Done

*Hadran alach Shevuot shtayim*. The first chapter of Shevuot is complete.

## Chapter Two Open

### Shtayim Arba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יְדִיעוֹת הַטּוּמְאָה שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע: נִטְמָא וְיָדַע, וְנֶעֶלְמָה מִמֶּנּוּ הַטּוּמְאָה וְזָכוּר אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ;
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to cases of **awareness of the defiling** of the Temple by entering it while one is ritually impure, or defiling its sacrificial foods by partaking of them while one is ritually impure, there are **two** types **that are** actually **four.** How so? If **one became ritually impure and he was aware** that he was impure, **but** afterward his **impurity was hidden from him, though he remembered** that he was partaking of **sacrificial** food, which is forbidden to one who is in a state of ritual impurity; this is one of the four types of awareness of impurity.

The second chapter opens with the famous *mishna* that employs the structural formula *shtayim shehein arba* — two that are four — across four domains: *shavuot*, *yediot hatuma*, *yetziot ha-Shabbat*, and *mar'ot nega'im*. The laws of *Shabbat* under this formula will be addressed in Tractate Shabbat; the laws of *nega'im* in their own tractate; the laws of *shavuot* will be treated in the later chapters of this tractate. The present chapter takes up *yediot hatuma* — the awareness states connected to ritual impurity.

### Tumah Yedi'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְזָכוּר אֶת הַטּוּמְאָה; נֶעֶלְמוּ מִמֶּנּוּ זֶה וָזֶה; וְאָכַל אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְלֹא יָדַע, וּמִשֶּׁאָכַל יָדַע – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** If the fact that he was partaking of **sacrificial** food **was hidden from him, though he remembered the ritual impurity** that he had contracted; this is the second of the four types of awareness of impurity. And the same *halakha* applies if both **this and that were hidden from him,** both the fact that he was impure and the fact that he was partaking of sacrificial food. In all these cases, if **he partook of the sacrificial** food **and was unaware** either that he was impure, or that the food was sacrificial food, or both, **and after he partook of** it **he became aware** of that which he had forgotten, **he is** required to bring **a sliding-scale** offering. In this type of offering, the sinner sacrifices an animal, bird, or meal-offering, depending on his financial status.

The *mishna* teaches: *nitma v'yada v'ne'elmah mimenu hatuma v'zachar et hakodesh, ne'elam mimenu hakodesh v'zachar et hatuma, ne'elmu mimenu zeh v'zeh v'achal et hakodesh v'lo yada umishe'achal yada, harei zeh be'oleh v'yored*. The structure is: a person became impure and knew it; then the awareness of the impurity escaped him but he retained awareness of the *kodesh* — option one; or the awareness of the *kodesh* escaped him but he retained awareness of the *tumah* — option two; or both awarenesses escaped him entirely, he ate the *kodesh* without knowing, and only afterwards became aware — this is the case that obligates a *korban oleh v'yored*. The two primary states of awareness thus generate four combinatorial permutations — two that are four.

### Mapping Options

The *mishna* identifies two variables: awareness of *tumah* and awareness of *kodesh* or *mikdash*. In each case the person may know or not know, and these two binary variables combine to produce the four states the *mishna* enumerates. In the cases where liability for a *korban oleh v'yored* arises, there must have been a *he'alem* — a lapse of awareness — at some point, and the lapse must be accompanied by the transgression. Working through the precise mapping of which combinations produce liability and which do not is the central analytic task of the chapter.

## Expand Sanctity

### Five Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הַנִּכְנָס לָעֲזָרָה וְאֶחָד הַנִּכְנָס לְתוֹסֶפֶת הָעֲזָרָה, שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הָעִיר וְעַל הָעֲזָרוֹת אֶלָּא בְּמֶלֶךְ וְנָבִיא וְאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים וְסַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד, וּבִשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת וּבְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** As for the boundaries of the Temple with regard to the *halakhot* of impurity, **the same** *halakha* applies to **one who enters the** area that was part of the original Temple **courtyard and** to **one who enters the** later **addition to the** Temple **courtyard,** because the additional section is sanctified with the full sanctity of the Temple courtyard. The mishna notes: **As, additions can be made to the city** of Jerusalem **or to the** Temple **courtyards only by** a special body comprising the **king, a prophet, the *Urim VeTummim*, and the Sanhedrin of seventy-one** judges, **and with two thanks-offerings and with** a special **song.** Once the addition to the courtyard is made by this body and this process, it is given the full sanctity of the original courtyard area.

The *mishna* introduces a seemingly parenthetical discussion: what is the law if a person entered not the *azarah* proper but the *tosefet ha'azarah* — an area added to the *azarah* through a formal expansion process? The *mishna* specifies that one cannot simply expand the holy area by administrative decision. The process requires: a *melech*, a *navi*, *urim v'tumim*, a *sanhedrin* of seventy-one, two *todot* — thanksgiving bread offerings — song, the *beit din* proceeding in procession, the two *todot* following, and all of *Yisrael* behind them.

### Adding Holiness

The reason for this elaborate requirement points to a profound question: how does one add holiness to a place? Holiness is not a civic designation that can be conferred by a board resolution or a municipal decree. A place acquires *kedushah* only through a specific, legally constituted ritual process involving the full weight of national religious authority.

### Inner Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית דִּין מְהַלְּכִין, וּשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת אַחֲרֵיהֶן, וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֲרֵיהֶם. הַפְּנִימִית נֶאֱכֶלֶת, וְהַחִיצוֹנָה נִשְׂרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna provides certain details of the consecration ceremony. **And the court** would **move** forward, **and two thanks-offerings** would be brought **after them, and all of the Jewish people** would follow **behind them.** When they would reach the end of the place that they desired to consecrate, **the inner** thanks-offering would **be eaten and the outer one** would **be burned.**

The *mishna* further specifies that within the expansion ritual, the inner fence offering is eaten and the outer fence offering is burned. These correspond to distinct stages of the ceremony that will be elaborated upon later in the *gemara*'s discussion.

### No Ritual No Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשֵׂית בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ – הַנִּכְנָס לְשָׁם אֵין חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **any** addition to the Temple **that was not made with all these** ceremonial procedures, **one who enters there** while ritually impure **is not liable** to bring an offering if his entry was unwitting, nor to be punished with *karet*, excision from the World-to-Come, if his entry was intentional.

The *mishna* then draws the legal consequence directly relevant to the chapter's topic: *v'chol shelo na'aseta b'chol elu, hanichnas lesham ein chayav aleha* — if all these conditions were not fulfilled, one who enters such an area incurs no liability. Without the formal expansion ritual, the place has no *kedushah* in the legally operative sense, and therefore entering it while impure generates no obligation for a *korban*.

## Temple Entry Cases

### In Court Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא בָּעֲזָרָה, וְנֶעֶלְמָה מִמֶּנּוּ טוּמְאָה וְזָכוּר אֶת הַמִּקְדָּשׁ; נֶעְלַם הֵימֶנּוּ מִקְדָּשׁ וְזָכוּר הַטּוּמְאָה; נֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ זֶה וָזֶה; וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁהָה בִּכְדֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה אוֹ בָּא לוֹ בָּאֲרֻוכָּה – חַיָּיב. בַּקְּצָרָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The first part of the mishna discussed one who became ritually impure before entering the Temple. The mishna proceeds to consider a case involving one who was ritually pure when he entered the Temple but **who became impure** while **in the** Temple **courtyard, and** afterward, his **impurity was hidden from him** but **he remembered** that he was standing in **the Temple,** or the fact that he was standing in **the Temple was hidden from him** but **he remembered** his **impurity,** or both **this** fact **and that** fact **were hidden from him.** In all these cases, if **he bowed** down, **or he tarried** in the Temple courtyard **long enough to bow down** even though he did not actually bow, **or he went out** by way of **a longer** route when he could have taken a shorter route, he is **liable** to bring a sliding-scale offering. But if he left the Temple via **the shortest** way, he is **exempt.**

The *mishna* presents a distinct scenario: a person who was pure upon entering the *azarah* but became impure while already inside. The *mishna* maps through the same *he'alem* variations: the *tumah* was hidden from him but he remembered the *mikdash*; or the *mikdash* was hidden from him but he remembered the *tumah*; or both were hidden from him.

### Tarrying Liability

In all these cases, liability hinges on what the person did after becoming aware. If he performed *hishtachavah* — an act of worship — or lingered in the *azarah* for the time it would take to perform *hishtachavah*, or chose the longer route out of the temple upon becoming aware of his *tumah*, he is liable. If he immediately took the short route out, *biktzarah patur* — he is exempt. The governing principle is that once awareness returns, the person must act with alacrity. Delay — even in the form of choosing a longer exit route — constitutes the transgression.

### Oleh Ve-Yored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** mitzva that the ritually impure must be sent out of the Temple **is the positive mitzva concerning the Temple for which,** as is taught elsewhere in the Mishna (*Horayot* 8b), the Sanhedrin **is not liable** to bring an offering for an erroneous ruling. A communal bull sin-offering is brought because of the unwitting transgression of a prohibition involving an action by the Jewish people resulting from an erroneous halakhic decision handed down by the Sanhedrin. But if the Sanhedrin mistakenly ruled that one who became impure while in the Temple may leave by way of a longer route, they do not bring this offering, as it is brought only for an erroneous ruling on a matter that requires the bringing of a fixed sin-offering, and not a sliding-scale offering, for its unwitting violation.

This category of transgression — entering or tarrying in the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah* — obligates the *korban oleh v'yored*, the sliding-scale offering described in Vayikra 5 whose cost adjusts to the financial means of the transgressor. The *mishna* also notes the specific rule concerning *mitzvas aseh she'bamikdash* — that an impure person must be sent out of the temple is itself a positive commandment — and addresses the question of whether an erroneous ruling by the *Sanhedrin* regarding this law would generate the communal bull offering discussed in Tractate Horayot. The *mishna*'s conclusion is that the *Sanhedrin* does not bring such an offering for this particular category.

## Niddah Scenario

### Niddah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבַּנִּדָּה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ? הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה וְאָמְרָה לוֹ ״נִטְמֵאתִי״, וּפֵירַשׁ מִיָּד – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיְּצִיאָתוֹ הֲנָאָה לוֹ כְּבִיאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And which is the positive mitzva with regard to a menstruating woman for which,** as is taught in *Horayot* there, the Sanhedrin is **liable** to bring a bull offering for an erroneous ruling? If a man **was engaging in intercourse with a ritually pure woman, and** during the course of their act of intercourse **she** experienced menstrual bleeding and **said to him: I have become impure, and** unwittingly **he immediately withdrew** from her and did not wait until his penis became flaccid, he is **liable** to bring a sin-offering for engaging in intercourse with a menstruating woman, **because his withdrawal** from her **is as pleasant to him as his entry.** If the Sanhedrin mistakenly ruled that one may withdraw immediately, they bring a bull offering for their erroneous ruling.

The *mishna* then pivots to a strikingly different scenario: a man and woman were engaged in marital relations, both in a state of purity, when the woman experienced the onset of menstrual bleeding and informed him — *nitma'ti*.

### Opposite Rule

This case operates under rules that are precisely the inverse of the temple scenario. In the *mikdash* case, once a person realizes he is *tamei*, he must depart immediately; any delay renders him liable. In the *niddah* case, the opposite holds: if the man immediately withdraws upon being informed, he is liable. Lingering is not the transgression; premature withdrawal is.

### Pleasure Logic

The *mishna* explains the reasoning: *mipnei sheyetziato hana'ah lo keviato* — because his withdrawal is itself a form of physical pleasure comparable to entry. Since the withdrawal carries its own pleasure, the Torah does not demand immediate cessation; the person is expected to wait until the act concludes naturally. Were he to withdraw immediately upon notification, the deliberate pleasure he would derive from that withdrawal would itself constitute a transgression.

### Bull Offering

If the *Sanhedrin* erroneously ruled that immediate withdrawal upon such notification is permitted, they bring a communal bull offering for their erroneous ruling — this is the specific *chatas* associated with the *Sanhedrin*'s *shgaga* that produces communal liability, as discussed in Tractate Horayot.

## Three View Dispute

### Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״הַשֶּׁרֶץ״ – ״וְנֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ״, עַל הֶעְלֵם שֶׁרֶץ חַיָּיב, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to the sliding-scale offering the verse states: "Or if a person touches any impure thing, whether it is the carcass of a non-kosher undomesticated animal, or the carcass of a non-kosher domesticated animal, or the carcass of a non-kosher **creeping animal, and it is hidden from him"** (Leviticus 5:2). A precise reading of this verse indicates that in a case where one has **a lapse of awareness** that he had contracted ritual impurity by touching **a creeping animal,** he is **liable** to bring a sliding-scale offering for having defiled the Temple or the sacrificial food, **but he is not liable** to bring such an offering in a case where he has **a lapse of awareness** that he is entering the **Temple** or partaking of sacrificial food.

The *mishna* concludes with three competing positions on which states of unawareness — *he'alem* — generate liability for the *korban oleh v'yored* in the context of entering the *mikdash* while *tamei*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that liability attaches only when the person was unaware of the *tumah*, that is, when the *sheretz* or impure substance was *ne'elam mimenu* — hidden from his awareness. One who was fully aware of his *tumah* but unaware that he was standing in the *mikdash* is, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, not liable.

### Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״וְנֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ וְהוּא טָמֵא״ – עַל הֶעְלֵם טוּמְאָה חַיָּיב, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Akiva says:** The verse states: **"And it is hidden from him, so that he is impure"** (Leviticus 5:2), thereby teaching that in a case when one has **a lapse of awareness** that he had contracted **ritual impurity,** he is **liable** to bring a sliding-scale offering, **but** one **is not liable** to bring such an offering in a case when he has **a lapse of awareness** that he is entering the **Temple** or partaking of sacrificial food.

Rabbi Akiva states the same rule in slightly different language: *v'ne'elam mimenu v'hu tamei* — the operative *he'alem* is that of the *tumah* itself. Like Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Akiva holds that unawareness of the *mikdash* alone does not create liability for this offering. Only unawareness of the *tumah* does.

### Yishmael View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: ״וְנֶעְלַם״ ״וְנֶעְלַם״ שְׁתֵּי פְּעָמִים, לְחַיֵּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם טוּמְאָה וְעַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says:** The verse states: **"And it is hidden** from him" (Leviticus 5:2), and it states: **"And it is hidden** from him" (Leviticus 5:3), **twice,** in order **to render one liable** to bring a sliding-scale offering both in a case where one has **a lapse of awareness** that he had contracted **ritual impurity and** in a case where one has **a lapse of awareness** that he is entering the **Temple.**

Rabbi Yishmael disagrees. He argues that the word *ne'elam* appears twice in the relevant verses in Vayikra 5 — *v'ne'elam v'ne'elam, shtei pe'amim* — and from this double usage he derives that one is liable for both types of unawareness: *lechayev al he'elem tumah v'al he'elem mikdash*. Both the lapse of awareness about the *tumah* and the lapse of awareness about the *mikdash* independently generate the obligation to bring the *korban oleh v'yored*.

## Math Breakdown

### Papa Counts Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע?! שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ הָוְיָין – יְדִיעוֹת הַטּוּמְאָה תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף, יְדִיעוֹת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף, יְדִיעוֹת מִקְדָּשׁ תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the *halakhot* concerning awareness of ritual impurity are two that are further subdivided into four. **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** Are these actually **two** states of awareness **that are** subdivided into **four?** As the mishna lists them, they seem to **be two that are** subdivided into **six: Awareness of the impurity** at the **beginning and** at the **end, awareness of the sacrificial** food at the **beginning and** at the **end,** and **awareness of** the **Temple** at the **beginning and** at the **end.**

The *gemara* opens with Rav Papa addressing Abaye on the mathematical structure underlying the *mishna*'s formula of *shtayim shehein arba*. The two basic binary variables are: does the person know or not know about the *tumah*, and does the person know or not know about the *mikdash* or *kodshim*? Each variable has two states, yielding two combinations that are four.

### Papa Counts Six

Rav Papa then refines his own count. He argues that the correct number is not four but six. The reasoning is that each type of awareness — *yediot hatumah*, *yediot hakodesh*, *yediot mikdash* — has two temporal dimensions: awareness at the beginning of the relevant episode and awareness at the end. Two temporal states times three categories of awareness yields six: *yediot hatumah techilah v'sof, yediot hakodesh techilah v'sof, yediot mikdash techilah v'sof*.

### Abaye: Eight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תַּמְנֵי הָוְיָין – דְּהָא אִיכָּא טוּמְאָה דְּקוֹדֶשׁ וְטוּמְאָה דְּמִקְדָּשׁ תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye answered him: **According to your reasoning,** that you count all the various cases listed in the mishna, **there are eight** states of awareness, **as there is** also awareness of the **impurity** in connection **with** eating the **sacrificial** food, at the beginning and at the end, **and** awareness of the **impurity** in connection **with** entering the **Temple,** at the **beginning and** at the **end.** The mishna mentions awareness of the impurity both in the first clause, which discusses partaking of sacrificial food, and in the second clause, which discusses entering the Temple.

Abaye counters that by Rav Papa's own logic, the correct count should be eight, not six. The *mishna* mentions awareness of *tumah* in two distinct relational contexts — in connection with eating *kodshim* and in connection with entering the *mikdash*. If these are counted as separate awareness categories, each with beginning and end states, the total reaches eight: *deha'ika tumah d'kodesh v'tumah d'mikdash techilah v'sof*.

### Compression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, שֵׁם טוּמְאָה אַחַת הִיא. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם שֵׁית הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa refutes this: **This** is **not difficult,** as the status of **ritual impurity** carries **one name** in both cases: The person was aware that he had contracted ritual impurity and then it became hidden from him, and there is no reason to distinguish between impurity in connection with partaking of sacrificial food and impurity in connection with entering the Temple. Accordingly, Rav Pappa's first question remains: **In any case there are six** states of awareness.

The *gemara* responds that this objection is not difficult: *shem tumah achat hi mikol makom, sheit havyan* — the status of *tumah* carries a single name regardless of the context in which it is operative. Since the *tumah* is one status whether it pertains to *kodshim* or to *mikdash*, the two apparently separate categories can be compressed into one, and the count returns to six.

### Papa Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְעוֹלָם תַּמְנֵי הָוְיָין, אַרְבְּעֵי קַמָּיָיתָא, דְּלָא מַיְיתַן לֵיהּ לִידֵי קׇרְבָּן – לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב; אַרְבְּעֵה בָּתְרָיָיתָא, דְּמַיְיתַן לֵיהּ לִידֵי קׇרְבָּן – קָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** in answer to his own question: **Actually, there are eight** states of awareness, two of the impurity in connection with partaking of sacrificial food, two of the impurity in connection with entering the Temple, two of awareness of the sacrificial food, and two of awareness of the Temple, each pair having one awareness at the beginning and one at the end. But **the first four** states of awareness at the beginning **do not** in themselves **bring** the unwitting transgressor **to** liability to bring **an offering,** as if he does not reach awareness at the end, he will not have known that he transgressed. Therefore, the *tanna* **does not count** them. But the *tanna* **does count the last four** states of awareness, **which bring** the unwitting transgressor **to** the liability to bring **an offering.**

Rav Papa ultimately affirms that eight is in fact the correct count, and he introduces a new framing: there are four states and four states. The first four states of awareness — those in which there is awareness at the beginning of the episode — do not generate liability for a *korban*, and are counted in the *mishna*'s enumeration because they are novel to this legal domain; in all other areas of Torah where a *shegagah* offering is required, there is no condition of prior awareness. The last four states — those in which awareness returns at the end — do generate liability. An alternative formulation inverts the logic: the four initial states are counted because they are not found elsewhere in Torah, while the concluding four, which are standard sin-offering cases found throughout Torah, are left uncounted. Either way, the total count of eight is affirmed, split between states that do and states that do not produce the *korban*.

## Sheratzim Halakha

### Papa Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: נֶעֶלְמוּ מִמֶּנּוּ הִלְכוֹת טוּמְאָה, מַהוּ? הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא יָדַע אִי שֶׁרֶץ טָמֵא אִי צְפַרְדֵּעַ טָמֵא – זִיל קְרִי בֵּי רַב הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The *amora'im* try to define the awareness of the impurity mentioned in the mishna. **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If **the *halakhot* of impurity became hidden from him, what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable to bring a sliding-scale offering in such a situation? The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the case under discussion? **If we say that he did not know whether a creeping animal is impure** or pure **or whether a frog is impure** or pure, this **is** a topic that you could **go learn in a children's school.** As these matters are explicitly recorded in the Torah, they can never be considered hidden.

Rav Papa poses a further question: *ne'elmu mimenu hilchot tumah, mahu?* — what is the law if a person's unawareness is not about whether he was *tamei* or whether he was in the *mikdash*, but rather about the *halakha* of *tumah* itself, specifically regarding *sheratzim*?

### Basic Knowledge

The initial response is that such a case seems impossible. Anyone who does not know that a *sheretz* is *tamei* should simply go and learn — *zil krei b'rav*. This is foundational knowledge, the kind that is taught at the earliest stages of Jewish education. Ignorance of so basic a fact cannot constitute a *he'alem* in the legally operative sense.

### Shiurim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּיָדַע בְּטוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ; וּכְגוֹן דִּנְגַע בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, וְלָא יָדַע כַּעֲדָשָׁה אִי מְטַמֵּא אִי לָא מְטַמֵּא. מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּיָדַע דִּמְטַמֵּא שֶׁרֶץ בָּעוֹלָם – יְדִיעָה הִיא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּכַעֲדָשָׁה לָא יָדַע אִי מְטַמֵּא אִי לָא מְטַמֵּא – הַעֲלָמָה הִיא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Actually,** Rav Pappa must have been asking about a case **where he knew** the essential *halakha* with regard to **the ritual impurity of a creeping animal, and** it is a case **where he touched** a portion of the animal that was **of a lentil-bulk and he did not know** the *halakha* concerning **whether** a portion that was **of a lentil-bulk renders** a person **impure or does not render** him **impure. What** is the *halakha* in such a case? The Gemara explains the two possibilities: Does one say that **since he knows generally that a creeping animal renders** a person **impure, it is awareness? Or perhaps** one says that **since he does not know whether** a portion that was **of a lentil-bulk renders** a person **impure or does not render** a person **impure, it is** considered **hidden** from him. The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The question is then refined: the case is not one where the person does not know that *sheratzim* are impure, but rather where he knew that a *sheretz* renders one impure yet did not know whether a piece the size of a lentil — *k'adasha* — suffices to create *tumah* or not. The *shiurim* — the precise measurements that determine halachic liability — are a matter of learning, and one can plausibly be ignorant of them even while knowing the general principle. Does such ignorance of the *shiurim* constitute a *he'alem* sufficient to generate liability for the *korban oleh v'yored*?

### Teiku Stalemate

The *gemara* cannot resolve this question. *Keivan d'yada d'metame sheretz, ba'olam yedi'ah hi* — since he knew that *sheretz* renders one impure, that may constitute sufficient awareness, making any further ignorance about *shiurim* legally irrelevant. Or, *keivan d'k'adasha lo yada i metame i lo metame, ha'alamah hi* — since he did not know whether the specific measurement creates *tumah*, that ignorance may qualify as a genuine *he'alem*. The *gemara* reaches no conclusion: *teiku*.

### Temple Ignorance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בֶּן בָּבֶל שֶׁעָלָה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְנֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ מְקוֹם מִקְדָּשׁ – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma** concerning the awareness of the Temple: If **a Babylonian** or a resident of another country **ascended to Eretz Yisrael, and the site of the Temple was hidden from him,** so that he unwittingly entered into the Temple in a state of ritual impurity, **what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable to bring a sliding-scale offering to atone for his offense, or not?

The broader discussion continues with the question of how someone could fail to know he was inside the *mikdash* — the scenario underlying the *he'alem mikdash* category. The *gemara* considers: perhaps he came from Bavel and simply was unaware of where the boundaries of the temple precinct lay, or perhaps he came from Eretz Yisrael but the precise demarcation of the *azarah* was unclear to him.

### Knowledge System

The entire discussion of awareness, unawareness, and the various combinations of *yedi'ah* and *he'alem* ultimately serves the same purpose as the atonement system mapped at the opening of the daf: to clarify with precision what one knows and what one does not know, so that the appropriate mechanism of *kapparah* can be applied. The goal of the system, as throughout the first chapter, is to trace *tumah* to *taharah* — to move from whatever state of impurity or unknowing one stands in, toward clarity and atonement.
