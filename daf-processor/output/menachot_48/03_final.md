# Menachot 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Lambs, Two Loaves

### Braita Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁחַט אַרְבָּעָה כְּבָשִׂים עַל שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת – מוֹשֵׁךְ שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶן, וְזוֹרֵק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one slaughtered four sheep** for *Shavuot*, rather than the required two, accompanied **by two loaves,** he **draws two of** the sheep out of the four **and sprinkles their blood not for the sake** of the sheep of *Shavuot*. He then sprinkles the blood of the other sheep for the sake of the sheep of *Shavuot*.

The daf opens with a *braita* taught by Rabbi Chanina Tirsa in the presence of Rabbi Yochanan. The case presented is the inverse of what the Gemara had been discussing previously: whereas the earlier discussion concerned two *kivsei atzeret* paired with four *challot*, here we have four *kevasim* paired with only two *challot*. The *braita* thus begins: *shachat arba kevasim al shtei challot* — he slaughtered four lambs alongside two loaves.

### Two Valid, Two Invalid

The structural problem is immediately apparent. Only two of the four lambs can function as *kivsei atzeret*, since there are only two loaves to pair with them. The other two lambs, however many they may be, cannot achieve the status of *kivsei atzeret* without their accompanying bread. If one were to perform *zrikas hadam* on all four *lishmo* — with the intent of *kivsei atzeret* — the latter two would become *kivsei atzeret* without *lechem*, which renders them *pasul*. On the other hand, if the two surplus lambs are instead treated as *shlamim*, they are entirely valid.

### Making Two Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כָּךְ, הִפְסַדְתָּ אֶת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As, if you do not say** to do **this,** but rather require him to first sprinkle the blood of two of the sheep for their own sake, then **you have caused the loss** of **the latter** two sheep. Since they were previously fit to have their blood sprinkled on the altar for the sake of the sheep of *Shavuot*, and were disqualified from this status when the blood of the other two sheep was sprinkled for that purpose, they are no longer fit to have their blood sprinkled even for the sake of a different offering.

The *braita*'s solution is *mash'neh shnayim mehem be-zorek hadam she-lo lishmo*: deliberately perform *zrikas hadam* of two of the lambs *she-lo lishmo*, thereby converting them into *shlamim*. This we have already seen: a *korban* from *kivsei atzeret* upon which *zrikas hadam* is performed *she-lo lishmo* loses its status as *kivsei atzeret* but is valid as a *shlamim*. With two thus converted, the remaining two are proper *kivsei atzeret* paired with their bread, and those two become valid as *shalmei Yisrael*. The *braita* adds: *ve-im ee ata omer kach* — if you do not proceed this way, and instead you perform *zrikas hadam lishmo* on all four, then two of them will necessarily be *pasul* as *kivsei atzeret* without *lechem*.

### Alternative Problem

A natural question arises: why not simply perform *zrikas hadam lishmo* on the first two and *she-lo lishmo* on the last two, rather than pre-selecting which ones to designate *she-lo lishmo*? The answer depends on an important technical distinction. The *shechita* was already performed, and the question is now about *zrikas hadam*. If one were to throw the blood of the first two *lishmo*, those become the valid *kivsei atzeret*, and the remaining two are then potentially left without *lechem*.

### Rashi's Dichuy

Rashi raises the concern of *dichuy*. Once the first two have been designated as the valid *kivsei atzeret* via *zorek hadam lishmo*, the last two — which had also been candidates (*ra'uy*) — may now be considered *nidcheh*, rejected. A *korban* in the state of *ra'uy ve-nidcheh* is *pasul*, and if that is the case, performing *zrikas hadam she-lo lishmo* on them afterward would not help. Rashi acknowledges some ambiguity here, since the earlier Gemara was not entirely clear that *dichuy* would in fact apply in this manner. Nevertheless, the preferred approach is to select two lambs proactively to be rendered *she-lo lishmo* from the outset, before any others have been validated, so that the situation is clear.

### Simultaneity & Zika

This raises a further question. Is not the *shechita* itself already significant? Does the act of *shechita lishmo* of the first two not already establish a *zika* — a legal bond — between those lambs and the *lechem*, thereby automatically leaving the remaining two without any connection to the bread? The answer is that the *shechita* does not definitively establish which lambs are paired with the bread; it is the *zrikas hadam* that *koveia* the final relationship. Nonetheless, if the *shechitot* were performed sequentially rather than simultaneously, one might argue that even the *shechita* of the first lambs creates a *zika* that preemptively excludes the others. This is a cogent point, and it may well be that the *braita* presupposes a scenario of simultaneous *shechita* — two *kohanim* slaughtering at the same time — so that all four remain equal candidates throughout, and the pre-selection of two *she-lo lishmo* effectively resolves the situation cleanly.

## Chata Kedei Shetizkeh

### Omer Lo Leadam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְכִי אוֹמֵר לוֹ לְאָדָם ״עֲמוֹד וַחֲטָא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּזְכֶּה״?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Rabbi Ḥanina Tirata: **And does** the court **say to a person: Arise and sin in order that you may gain?** Is it proper for the priest to sprinkle the blood of the first pair not for their own sake so that the second pair will remain fit?

Rabbi Yochanan's response to the *braita* is sharp: *vechi omer lo leadam amod vachata bishvil shetizkeh?* — do we instruct a person to go and sin in order that there be a benefit? This is a variation on the familiar principle of *chata kedei sheyizkeh chavercha*, though here it is not exactly a case of sinning for the sake of another person. The question is more precise: do we allow a violation of the *mikdash*'s procedural norms — performing *zrikas hadam she-lo lishmo* — in order to achieve a net gain?

### Bread Analogy

A classic illustration of this type of question involves bread being baked in an oven on *Shabbat*. If the bread has not yet finished baking, one has not yet violated *chillul Shabbat d'oraitta*. A bystander who wishes to prevent the violation might remove the bread before it finishes baking, but this itself constitutes an *issur d'rabbanan* — removing baking bread from an oven on *Shabbat* is a *chochma ve'eina melacha*, rabbinically prohibited. May one commit an *issur d'rabbanan* to prevent another's *chillul Shabbat d'oraitta*? The *chata kedei sheyizkeh chavercha* question encompasses a complex set of variables: the magnitude of each party's violation, whether the original actor is acting *bemezid* or *beshogeg*, and whether rescuing a *mezid* is even warranted.

### Sin vs. Procedure

The present case, however, is not quite the same as that scenario. As Tosafot essentially makes the same point: when one performs *zrikas hadam she-lo lishmo*, it is not obviously a personal sin in the conventional sense. The *Rambam* speaks of a positive obligation to perform every act of a *chattat* or *shelamim* according to its proper procedure, but whether violating that procedure constitutes a personal transgression in the ordinary sense is not entirely clear. The better framing, then, is not "do we permit sinning to benefit a neighbor," but rather: do we permit a violation of the *mikdash*'s procedural norms — departing from the established way the *mikdash* operates — for the sake of a net gain? If one performs *zrikas hadam she-lo lishmo*, two *korbanot* are salvaged as *kivsei atzeret* and the remaining two continue as *shlamim*. If one does nothing wrong and proceeds entirely *lishmo*, or if one is simply passive and allows events to unfold, two *korbanot* are lost. Is that trade-off warranted?

### Mixed Limbs Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: אֵבְרֵי חַטָּאת שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בְּאֵבְרֵי עוֹלָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יֻתְּנוּ לְמַעְלָה, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת בְּשַׂר חַטָּאת לְמַעְלָה כְּאִילּוּ הִיא עֵצִים, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן וְיֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 77a) that *tanna'im* disagree concerning this matter? The mishna teaches: In the case of **the limbs of a sin offering,** whose flesh is eaten by priests and may not be burned on the altar, **that were intermingled with the limbs of a burnt offering,** which are burned on the altar, **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priest **shall place** all the limbs **above,** on the altar, **and I view the flesh** of the limbs **of the sin offering above** on the altar **as though they are** pieces of **wood** burned on the altar, not an offering. **And the Rabbis say:** One should wait until **the form of** all the intermingled limbs **decays and they will** all **go out to the place of burning** in the Temple courtyard, where all disqualified offerings of the most sacred order are burned.

A parallel case from the *mishna* sharpens the question considerably. The case is *eivarei chattat shenitarvu be'eivarei olah*: the limbs of a *chattat* became mixed with the limbs of an *olah*. Both *korbanot* were validly slaughtered and the blood properly thrown. The problem arises in completing the service: the limbs of the *olah* must be burned on the *mizbeiach*, while the limbs of the *chattat* must be eaten by the *kohanim*. Since they are now mixed and indistinguishable, eating everything is out of the question — one may not eat the meat of an *olah*, which would constitute *me'ila* or equivalent violation. Simply doing nothing means waiting for everything to become *pasul belinah* overnight and then burning it all. The third option is to burn everything on the *mizbeiach*, treating the *chattat* limbs as firewood. This is what Rabbi Eliezer permits: *yitnu lemalah* — place it all on the *mizbeiach* — and consider the *chattat* limbs as wood, not as an *haktarah* of the *chattat* itself. Still, this involves actively disposing of *chattat* limbs incorrectly in order to salvage the *olah*. The *chachamim* disagree. They rule *te'aver tzuratan* — let their form deteriorate overnight, let everything become *pasul belinah*, and burn it all. Better to be entirely passive and lose everything than to actively do something procedurally wrong, even in service of a net gain. This debate maps precisely onto Rabbi Yochanan's objection: the *chachamim* reject the very logic of doing one thing wrong to salvage another.

## Same Category Principle

### Same Category Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲמוֹד וַחֲטָא בְּחַטָּאת בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּזְכֶּה בְּחַטָּאת – אָמְרִינַן, עֲמוֹד וַחֲטָא בְּחַטָּאת בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּזְכֶּה בְּעוֹלָה – לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina Tirata answered Rabbi Yoḥanan: **We do say: Arise and sin with a sin offering in order that you may gain with regard to a sin offering,** since it is the same type of offering. Similarly, one may sin with regard to the sheep of *Shavuot* in order to gain with regard to the other sheep brought for the same offering. **We do not say: Arise and sin with a sin offering in order that you may gain with regard to a burnt offering.** Therefore, the Rabbis prohibit burning the limbs of the sin offering on the altar in order to allow for the burning of the limbs of the burnt offering.

The Gemara presses further. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *braita*'s proposed solution — performing *zrikas hadam she-lo lishmo* on two lambs to salvage the others — involves instructing someone to violate procedure for a net gain. The Gemara asks: does Rabbi Yochanan's principle apply equally to cases within a single category? The answer is: *amod vechatei bechatas bishvil shetiskei bechatas amrinan* — if the violation and the benefit both fall within the category of *chattat*, we might indeed permit such a trade-off.

### Cross-Category Forbidden

However, *amod vechatei bechatas bishvil shetiskei be'olah lo amrinan* — to sin with a *chattat* in order to benefit an *olah* is a different matter entirely. We do not permit cross-category violations. When the violation occurs in one ledger column and the gain appears in another, we cannot simply take a step back and declare a net gain. The violation in the *chattat* column cannot be bracketed by pointing to a gain in the *olah* column. They remain separate, and the loss on each side is real.

### Shlamim Category

What then is the *tanna*'s case in the *braita*? All four lambs are intended as *kivsei atzeret*; when two are converted *she-lo lishmo* they become *shlamim*; the remaining two are valid *kivsei atzeret* which themselves ultimately function as a species of *shalmei Yisrael*. The entire operation takes place within the *shlamim* column. There is a procedural violation — *zovcham shelo lishmam* — but within that same category there is a net gain: two *korbanot* that would otherwise have been *pasul* or burned are instead eaten, their *emurim* offered, the *avodah* completed. When the calculation is performed entirely within a single category, perhaps one may bracket the violation and recognize the overall gain. That is the logic the Gemara entertains for the *tanna*'s position, and it is a logic Rabbi Yochanan contests.

### Shabbat Zrikas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיְתָה שַׁבָּת, לֹא יִזְרוֹק. וְאִם זָרַק – הוּרְצָה לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִין לָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan continued: **And if** the Festival **was** on **Shabbat, one may not sprinkle** the blood, the sacrificial portions may not be burned on the altar, and the meat may not be eaten. This is because the improper slaughter of the sheep disqualified them as communal offerings, whereas individual offerings may not be sacrificed on Shabbat. **But if** the priest nevertheless **sprinkled** the blood of these sheep on Shabbat, the offering **is accepted** in that it is permitted **to burn** its sacrificial **portions** on the altar **in the evening,** after the conclusion of Shabbat, and then the meat may be eaten.

The Gemara now tests the same-category principle against a new case involving *Shabbat*. The scenario: *kivsei atzeret* were slaughtered on what was thought to be *Shavuot*, but it emerged that *Shavuot* had actually fallen the day before. The lambs were slaughtered — already a *chillul Shabbat* — but what should be done about the blood? If nothing is done, the blood will become *pasul belinah* by nightfall. If *zrikas hadam* is performed, the blood of what is now merely a *shlamim* is thrown on *Shabbat* — but there is no *melacha* involved in throwing blood on a *mizbeiach*. The prohibition is not one of *melacha* but of the *mikdash*'s own rule against bringing on *Shabbat* a *korban* that is not designated for *Shabbat*. Should one violate that *mikdash* rule — throw the blood on *Shabbat* to salvage the *korban* — so that by *Motzei Shabbat* one may burn the *emurim* and eat the meat? The *braita* rules: *lo yizrok* — do not throw the blood. And if one did throw it, *hurtza* — it is accepted, and one may burn the *emurim* that evening. The Gemara asks why one should not act, given that the violation and the benefit appear to fall within the same sphere. The Gemara answers: the violation occurs on *Shabbat*, while the benefit — eating the meat and burning the *emurim* — accrues only *Motzei Shabbat*, in *chol*. That is already a cross-category situation: *amod vechatei biShabbat kidei shetizke bechol lo amrinan*. The benefit does not accrue on *Shabbat* itself; it accrues afterward. We will not countenance a *Shabbat* violation for a gain that materializes in a different time-domain.

### Passive Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְתַרְתֵּי מִילֵּי לָא אָמַר? וְהָתְנַן: חָבִית שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה בְּגַת הָעֶלְיוֹנָה, וּבַתַּחְתּוֹנָה חוּלִּין טְמֵאִין – מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שֶׁאִם יָכוֹל לְהַצִּיל מִמֶּנָּה רְבִיעִית בְּטׇהֳרָה יַצִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that **with regard to two** separate **matters** the court does **not say** that one should sin with regard to one in order to gain with regard to other? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 8:9): In the case of **a barrel of** wine that is ***teruma* that broke in the upper** section of a **winepress, and in the lower** section of the winepress there is **non-sacred, impure** wine, and the wine that is *teruma* will flow into the lower press and become impure, **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua** both **concede that if one is able to rescue** even **a quarter-***log* **of** the wine that is *teruma* in a pure vessel so that it retains its **ritual purity, he** should **rescue** it, even if, in the process, the rest of the wine that is *teruma* will mix with the non-sacred wine. This will cause the owner a financial loss, because the wine that is *teruma* will become impure, causing the entire mixture to become prohibited for consumption.

Having established the same-category principle, the Gemara raises one final challenge from the laws of *terumah*. A barrel of *terumah* wine breaks in the upper vat of a wine press while the lower vat contains *chulin tamei* wine. As the *terumah* pours downward, it will become *terumah teme'ah* upon contact with the *tamei chulin* below, rendering the entire contents forbidden. One might wish to intercept the *terumah* by catching it in available *tamei* vessels — actively rendering it *tamei* in one's hands — thereby preventing it from mixing with the *chulin* and preserving the *chulin*. Is one permitted to actively be *metamei* the *terumah* in order to salvage the *chulin*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: תֵּרֵד וְתִטַּמֵּא, וְאַל יְטַמְּאֶנָּה בַּיָּד, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אַף יְטַמְּאֶנָּה בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **But if not,** i.e., one cannot save any of the wine that is *teruma*, e.g., if one does not have any pure vessels in which to collect it, **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The *teruma* wine should be allowed to **descend and become impure** on its own, ruining the non-sacred wine in the lower press, **but one** should **not render it impure through** his direct **action** by catching it in an impure vessel, even though catching it would prevent the wine that is *teruma* from mixing with his impure, non-sacred wine. **And Rabbi Yehoshua says:** Since the wine that is *teruma* will become impure in any event, **one** may **even render it impure through** his direct **action** in order to save his non-sacred wine. This indicates that according to Rabbi Yehoshua it is permitted to sin with regard to one matter, i.e., the wine that is *teruma*, in order to gain with regard to another matter, i.e., the non-sacred wine.

The *mishna* records that both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree that if one can salvage a *revi'it* of the *terumah* in a pure state, one is obligated to do so and may not deliberately render it *tamei*. If no *terumah* can be salvaged — the *terumah* is lost regardless — Rabbi Eliezer rules *tered vetitame ve'al yetame'enah beyad*: let it descend and become *tamei* on its own; do not actively render it *tamei* with your own hands. Rabbi Yehoshua rules *af yetame'enah beyad*: actively catch it in *tamei* vessels to save the *chulin*. The Gemara considers whether Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling represents the cross-category trade-off that the Gemara has been debating — rendering *terumah tamei* to salvage *chulin* — and whether this contradicts the principle that we do not permit cross-category violations. The Gemara answers: *shani hatam delivei tumah ka'azla* — that case is different because the *terumah* was already heading toward *tumah* regardless. Rabbi Yehoshua's leniency is not a paradigmatic case of introducing a new violation to achieve a gain; the *terumah* was going to become *tamei* no matter what. The marginal harm of catching it in one's hands is minimal. In contrast, the blood of the *chattat* or the blood of the *shlamim* on *Shabbat* was not fated to become invalid — those are cases where one introduces a novel procedural violation, and for those we do not apply the trade-off logic across categories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּלְטוּמְאָה קָא אָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It **is different there,** in the case of the wine, **because** the wine that is *teruma* **is going to** become **impure** in any event. Consequently, his action is not considered a sin, and this is not a case of sinning with regard to one matter in order to gain in another.

The Gemara leaves the matter without a definitive resolution regarding the same-category case. Its conclusion is that cross-category trade-offs are clearly impermissible, but whether the same-category argument — which the *tanna* of the *braita* apparently accepted — is ultimately valid remains open.

## Wrong-Age Lambs

### Shelo K'Mitzvatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק, תָּנֵי: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתָן – פְּסוּלִין, וּתְעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן, וְיֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the sheep of *Shavuot*. **When Rav Yitzḥak came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he reported traditions that he learned in Eretz Yisrael, and **he taught** a *baraita*: With regard to a case of the two **sheep** of ***Shavuot* where one slaughtered them not in accordance with their mitzva,** e.g., he slaughtered them for the sake of a different offering, they are **disqualified; and** they should be left overnight until **their form decays** and they attain the status of leftover sacrificial meat, **and** then **they are brought out to the place** designated **for burning.**

The Gemara now transitions to a new topic: the *halachot* of *kivsei atzeret* themselves, setting aside for the moment the relationship between the lambs and the loaves. When Rav Yitzchak came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he taught a *braita*: *hakevasim sheshachtan shelo k'mitzvatan psulin* — if the lambs of *atzeret* were slaughtered not in accordance with their commandment, they are *pasul*. Furthermore, they require *ibbur tzura* — one does not burn them immediately; one waits until they become *pasul belinah* overnight — and then *veyatze l'veit hasreifa*, they are burned. The question turns on the phrase *shelo k'mitzvatan*. What precisely was done incorrectly?

Rashi's first reading is that it refers to *shelo lishmo* — an incorrect *kavana* at the moment of *shechita*. However, this reading creates an immediate tension, since the entire earlier Gemara proceeds on the assumption that *shelo lishmo* still produces a valid *korban* as a *shlamim*. This is, after all, one of the foundational rules from the opening of *Zevachim*: *shechita shelo lishmo* renders a *korban* valid for every species except a *chattat* and a *pesach*, though it does not fulfill the owner's obligation. If this *braita* declares such a *korban pasul*, it would be arguing against the most basic premises of the entire tractate.

Rashi therefore offers a second explanation, which Tosafot also strongly endorses and which fits far better with the continuation of the Gemara: *shelo k'mitzvatan* means the lambs were the wrong age. *Kivsei atzeret* are required to be one year old — *bnei shana* — and the *braita* addresses a case where two-year-old animals were brought. The phrase *sheshachtan*, Rashi acknowledges, is slightly awkward for this reading since the problem with the animal's age arises at *hakdasha*, before *shechita*. Nevertheless, this second reading governs the subsequent discussion.

The underlying question is stark: if an animal that was meant to be *kivsei atzeret* is too old to qualify, does it remain *pasul* entirely — or can we salvage it as a generic *shlamim*, since a two-year-old animal is perfectly valid for an ordinary *shlamim*? Rav Yitzchak's *braita* declares it *pasul*. The question is whether that ruling can be sustained.

### Rav Yitzchak Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מָר, דְּמַקֵּישׁ לְהוּ לְחַטָּאת – תָּנֵי פְּסוּלִין, תָּנָא דְּבֵי לֵוִי, דְּגָמַר שַׁלְמֵי חוֹבָה מִשַׁלְמֵי נְדָבָה – תָּנֵי כְּשֵׁרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** to Rav Yitzḥak: The **Master,** i.e., Rav Yitzḥak, **who compares** the sheep of *Shavuot* **to a sin offering** because they are juxtaposed in a verse (see Leviticus 23:19), **teaches:** The sheep are **disqualified,** like a sin offering that was slaughtered not for its own sake. By contrast, the ***tanna* of the school of Levi, who derives** the *halakha* with regard to **an obligatory peace offering,** e.g., the two sheep of *Shavuot*, **from** the *halakha* concerning **a voluntary peace offering, teaches** that the two sheep remain **valid** offerings, just as a voluntary peace offering remains valid even if it is slaughtered for the sake of a different offering.

Rav Nachman explains Rav Yitzchak's reasoning. The *pasuk* dealing with *kivsei atzeret* juxtaposes them with a *chattat*: *se'ir izim echad l'chattat ushnei kvasim bnei shana l'zevach shlamim* — one goat for a *chattat*, and two yearling lambs as a *zevach shlamim*. This juxtaposition — *makish lehu l'chattat* — invites us to apply to *kivsei atzeret* the same stringencies that govern a *chattat*. Just as any variation in a *chattat* renders it *pasul* — and a *chattat* that has passed its year (*chattat she'avra shnata*) is among the *chataot hameitot*, the category of *chataot* that must die — so too any variation in *kivsei atzeret* renders them *pasul*, with no possibility of salvaging them as *shlamim*.

### Tana D'Bei Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: וּשְׁאָר שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתָן – כְּשֵׁרִין, וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה, וְנֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה, וְאֵין טְעוּנִין לֹא לֶחֶם וְלֹא זְרוֹעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Levi teaches: And** with regard to the **other peace offerings of a nazirite that one slaughtered not in accordance with their mitzva,** they are **valid** offerings like voluntary peace offerings, **but they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner** to bring the required nazirite peace offerings. **And** these offerings **are eaten for** only **one day and one night,** in accordance with the *halakha* concerning the peace offerings of nazirite, and not for two days and one night like voluntary peace offerings. They **require neither bread nor** the **foreleg,** unlike the required peace offering of a nazirite.

Against this position stands the *tana d'bei Levi*, who draws an entirely different inference from the same *pasuk*. Since the *kivsei atzeret* are explicitly called *shlamim* in the *parsha*, the *tana d'bei Levi* reasons that we should evaluate them in the context of general *shlamim*. The principle is *degamar shalmei chova mishalmei nedava*: we understand an obligatory *shlamim* in light of a voluntary *shlamim*. The result is that even if the lambs were too old, the *korban* remains *kasher*, since a two-year-old animal is valid for a free-will *shlamim*. The *tana d'bei Levi* can thus rule: *tani ksheirin* — even a wrong-age animal is valid.

This position finds explicit support in a *tosefta* in *Nazir*. The *korban* of a *nazir* involves an analogous structure — *shalmei nazir* are accompanied by bread, just as *kivsei atzeret* are, and the same juxtaposition with *chattat* appears in the *parsha* of *nazir* as well. The *tosefta* states: *v'shear shalmei Nazir sheshachtan shelo k'mitzvatan... vene'echalim leyom valayla ve'ein te'unim lo lechem velo zeroa* — if the *shalmei nazir* were slaughtered not in accordance with their commandment, they remain valid and are eaten for a day and a night, but they do not require the accompanying bread, the *zeroa*, or the other special elements of *shalmei nazir*. They are no longer *shalmei nazir*; they are ordinary *shlamim*. The *korban* is salvaged. That the *tana d'bei Levi* would apply the same logic to *kivsei atzeret* follows naturally.

Rashi notes a further dimension to this leniency. Even if someone made a *neder* to bring a one-year-old *shlamim* and instead brought a two-year-old, the *korban* would still be valid as a *shlamim*, even though the owner's obligation is unfulfilled. This establishes that the *halacha* can tolerate an age discrepancy in *shlamim* — the *korban* stands even when it does not match the owner's obligation — which supports the *tana d'bei Levi*'s position that the wrong-age *kivsei atzeret* can be salvaged similarly.

### Asham Age Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָשָׁם בֶּן שָׁנָה וְהֵבִיא בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם, בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם וְהֵבִיא בֶּן שָׁנָה – פְּסוּלִין, וּתְעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן וְיֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵפָה. אֲבָל עוֹלַת נָזִיר, וְעוֹלַת יוֹלֶדֶת, וְעוֹלַת מְצוֹרָע, שֶׁהָיוּ בְּנֵי שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, וּשְׁחָטָן – כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Yitzḥak from that which was taught in a *baraita*: In a case where one is obligated to sacrifice as **a guilt offering** an animal **in its** first **year,** which the Torah calls a lamb, **and** instead he **brought** an animal **in its second** year, which is considered a ram; or if he is obligated to sacrifice as a guilt offering an animal **in its second** year **and he brought** an animal **in its** first **year;** the offerings are **disqualified.** They are to be left overnight until **their form decays, and are brought out to the place** designated for **burning. But** in the case of **the burnt offering of a nazirite,** i.e., the lamb that is sacrificed when he completes his naziriteship; **or the burnt offering of a woman after childbirth,** i.e., the lamb she sacrifices on the forty-first day after giving birth to a son or on the eighty-first day after giving birth to a daughter; **or the burnt offering of a leper,** i.e., the lamb that is sacrificed after he is purified; in all of these cases if the animals **were in their second year** instead, **and one slaughtered them,** the offerings are **valid.**

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that appears to challenge Rav Yitzchak directly. The *braita* states: *asham ben shana vehevi ben shtayim* — if one owed a one-year-old *asham* and brought a two-year-old, or owed a two-year-old *asham* and brought a one-year-old, they are *psulim* and must undergo *ibbur tzura* before being burned. This applies specifically to *asham*. But the *braita* continues: if one brought a wrong-age animal as an obligatory *olah* — *olat nazir*, *olat yoledet*, or *olat metzora* — and the animals were two years old when they should have been one year old, they are *kasher*. The ruling is then generalized: *kol hakasher be'olat nedava kasher be'olat chova* — anything valid for a free-will *olah* is valid for an obligatory *olah*.

This *braita* seems to directly disprove Rav Yitzchak. It demonstrates that a wrong-age obligatory *korban* can still be valid — so why should wrong-age *kivsei atzeret* be entirely *pasul*? The Gemara dispenses with the challenge efficiently: *hai tanna tanna devei Levi hu* — this is simply a *baraita* from the school of Levi, representing one side of an already-established *tannaitic* dispute. The *braita* proves nothing new; it merely confirms that the *tana d'bei Levi* and Rav Yitzchak are in disagreement.

### Olat Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר בְּעוֹלַת נְדָבָה – כָּשֵׁר בְּעוֹלַת חוֹבָה, וְכׇל הַפָּסוּל בְּחַטָּאת – פָּסוּל בְּאָשָׁם, חוּץ מִשֶּׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **The principle of the matter** is: **Any** animal **that is valid as a voluntary burnt offering is** also **valid as an obligatory burnt offering, and any** animal **that is disqualified as a sin offering is** also **disqualified as a guilt offering, except for** an offering that was sacrificed **not for its own sake,** which is disqualified in the case of a sin offering but not a guilt offering. This demonstrates that the *halakhot* of obligatory burnt offerings are derived from those of voluntary burnt offerings, despite the fact that the burnt offering of a nazirite is juxtaposed to the sin offering of a nazirite (see Numbers 6:14) and the burnt offering of a leper is juxtaposed to the sin offering of a leper (see Leviticus 14:19). Similarly, the *halakha* pertaining to the sheep of *Shavuot*, which are obligatory peace offerings, should be derived from the *halakha* pertaining to voluntary peace offerings, and not from the *halakha* pertaining to a sin offering as Rav Yitzḥak holds.

The *braita*'s principle — that anything valid for a free-will *olah* is valid for an obligatory *olah* — rests on a conceptual foundation that runs through this entire discussion. When an obligatory *korban* of a given type is brought with the wrong specifications, the question is whether the *korban* can be reconfigured as the free-will version of the same type. For *olah* and *shlamim*, such free-will versions exist and permit a range of ages. This makes it possible to say: even though you did not fulfill your obligation by bringing a two-year-old animal when a one-year-old was required, the *korban* itself is still valid as a *korban* — it functions as a *nedavah* of that type even if it fails to satisfy the *chovah*.

### Asham Unique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאָר שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתָן – כְּשֵׁרִין, וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה. שְׁחָטָן מְחוּסַּר זְמַן בִּבְעָלִים, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ בְּנֵי שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּשְׁחָטָן – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered them** when the **time had not yet** arrived **for** their **owners** to sacrifice these offerings, **or they were in their second year** instead of their first year **and one slaughtered them,** they are **disqualified.**

The case of *asham* is categorically different. An *asham* has no *nedavah* form — there is no such thing as a free-will *asham*. One brings an *asham* only when obligated to do so. Therefore, if the wrong-age animal is brought for an *asham*, there is no conceptual "floor" to fall back on — no free-will version of the *asham* into which the *korban* could be reconfigured. One cannot say "treat it as a *stam* voluntary *asham*," because no such animal exists. The *korban* is therefore entirely *pasul*. This is also why a *chattat she'avra shnata* is among the *chataot hameitot*: the *chattat* similarly has no *nedavah* form, and therefore no wrong-age variation can be salvaged.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיגְמַר מִשְּׁלָמִים? שְׁלָמִים מִשְּׁלָמִים – גָּמַר, אָשָׁם מִשְּׁלָמִים – לָא גָּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But if it is so** that Levi derives the *halakhot* of an obligatory offering from those of a voluntary one, **let him derive** the *halakha* of the guilt offering **from** that of the **peace offering,** in which case the guilt offerings should be valid even if they were in their second year. The Gemara answers: Levi **derives** the *halakha* concerning an obligatory **peace offering from** the *halakha* concerning a voluntary **peace offering,** but **he does not derive** the *halakha* concerning **a guilt offering from** the *halakha* concerning **a peace offering.**

The *braita* cited earlier makes this explicit: *chutz mishelo lishmo* — the only exception is *shelo lishmo*. If an *asham* is slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, the *korban* remains valid even for *asham*, unlike the analogous rule for *chattat*. But if the animal is the wrong age, there is no path to validity, because no free-will *asham* exists. This asymmetry between *asham* and *olah* or *shlamim* is the linchpin of the distinction.

### Shimi's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי גָּמַר שְׁלָמִים מִשְׁלָמִים, לִיגְמַר נָמֵי אָשָׁם מֵאָשָׁם: אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע מֵאֲשַׁם גְּזֵילוֹת וַאֲשַׁם מְעִילוֹת, אוֹ אֲשַׁם גְּזֵילוֹת וַאֲשַׁם מְעִילוֹת מֵאֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע. אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ מִדָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ, וְאֵין דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ מִדָּבָר שֶׁבְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further challenges the statement of Rav Naḥman: **But if** Levi **derives** the *halakha* of an obligatory **peace offering from** that of a voluntary **peace offering, let him** similarly **derive** the *halakha* of one **guilt offering from** that of another **guilt offering.** He should derive that **the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper** are valid even if the animal is in its second year **from** the *halakha* concerning **a guilt offering for robbery and a guilt offering for misuse** of consecrated property, which are supposed to be a ram in its second year. **Or,** if one brought a lamb in its first year as **a guilt offering for robbery or a guilt offering for misuse** of consecrated property, Levi should derive that it is valid **from** the *halakha* concerning the **guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper,** which are lambs in their first year. **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: One can derive** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** e.g., the sheep of *Shavuot* that were slaughtered not for their own sake, **from** the *halakha* with regard to another **item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** e.g., a voluntary peace offering slaughtered not for its own sake. **But one cannot derive** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** e.g., the guilt offering of a nazirite or a leper that is sacrificed when it is in its second year, **from** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **in its valid manner,** e.g., a guilt offering for robbery or for misuse of consecrated property that is sacrificed when it is in its second year.

The Gemara then asks: why not learn from an *asham* to an *asham*? We have two-year-old *ashamos* — *asham gezelot* and *asham me'ilot* — and we have one-year-old *ashamos* — *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*. Why not say that since both one-year and two-year *ashamos* exist, one can salvage a wrong-age *asham* by analogy with the correctly-aged version of the other category? Shimi bar Ashi formulates the answer as a general rule: *donin davar she-lo be-hachshero mi-davar she-lo be-hachshero ve'ein donin davar she-lo be-hachshero mi-davar she-be-hachshero* — we may learn an incorrect case from another incorrect case, but we may not learn an incorrect case from a case that is fundamentally proper.

The rule operates as follows. When we permit a wrong-age *olah* to function as a valid free-will *olah*, we are drawing an analogy to the case of *nedavah* where someone vowed to bring a one-year-old *olah* and instead brought a two-year-old — even that case, where the person did something incorrect relative to their *neder*, produces a valid *korban*. That is a case of *shelo be-hachshero* — incorrectly executed — that still generates a valid outcome. We can therefore learn from it that our obligatory wrong-age *olah* is also valid. But for *asham*, the only analogous cases are *ashamos* brought in their correct form — *asham gezelot* as a two-year-old is *be-hachshero*, that is precisely how it is supposed to be. We cannot learn from a correctly brought two-year-old *asham* to validate an incorrectly brought two-year-old *asham* for a *chovah* that requires one year. There is no parallel case of an incorrect *asham* that was nevertheless accepted. The analogy simply does not hold.

## Shelo Lishmo Meaning

### Kavashim Eilim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשׁוּם אֵילִים – כְּשֵׁרִין, וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: עָלוּ וְעָלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another discussion concerning the sheep of *Shavuot*. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana taught** a *baraita* **before Rav:** In a case of **the** two **sheep of *Shavuot* where one slaughtered them for the sake of rams** and not for their own sake, **they are valid** offerings, **but they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner,** i.e., the community, to sacrifice these offerings. **Rav said to** Rabba bar bar Ḥana: That is not so; rather, the sheep **certainly satisfy** the obligation of the community.

The Gemara now opens what is, in many respects, the most fundamental question of the entire laws of *shelo lishmo* — one that arguably should have been raised at the very beginning of *Zevachim*, in the first *daf* where *shelo lishmo* was discussed. What precisely does *shelo lishmo* mean? Does it mean that the *kohen* knew the *korban*'s identity but deliberately attempted to redefine it — slaughtering a *shlamim* consciously intending it as an *olah*? Or does it also encompass an honest mistake — the *kohen* genuinely believed the animal was something other than what it was? The Gemara poses this question through a specific case: *Kivsei Atzeret sheshachtan lishum Eilim kesherim* — if one slaughtered the lambs of *atzeret* thinking they were rams (*eilim*), Rabbah bar bar Chana taught before Rav that such *korbanot* are valid, though the owners do not fulfill their obligation.

This is a striking formulation. We have never previously encountered a *pesul* based on thinking the animal is the wrong species, as distinct from thinking the *korban* is the wrong type of *offering*. The familiar cases of *shelo lishmo* concern *kavana* about the *zevach* — intending an *olah* when one is slaughtering a *shlamim*, or intending the wrong *ba'al*. Here the *kavana* concerns the animal's physical identity — the *kohen* believes these are rams, not lambs. Rabbah bar bar Chana rules this creates a *pesul* equivalent to *shelo lishmo*, such that the owners do not fulfill their obligation. Rav responds with a dismissive *alu va'alu* — this is simply not a problem.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין, הַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁפִּיגְּלוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ מְזִידִין – חַיָּיבִין, הָא שׁוֹגְגִין – פְּטוּרִין, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: פִּיגּוּלָן פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We raise an objection to our** own ruling concerning this ***halakha*** from that which was taught in a mishna (*Gittin* 54a): In a case of **priests who disqualified** an offering **through improper intention in the Temple,** by expressing, while sacrificing the offering, the intention to eat it after the appropriate time, if they did so **intentionally, they are liable** to pay the value of the offering to its owner, who must now bring another offering. It follows, **therefore,** that if the priests did so **unintentionally they are exempt. And** it **is taught concerning this** case in a *baraita*: Even though they slaughtered it with improper intent unintentionally, **their** act of **improper intention** renders the offering ***piggul*,** despite the fact that they are exempt from paying damages.

Rava enters the discussion with a general principle: *akira b'ta'us lo havei akira* — an unintentional uprooting of an object's definition, an honest mistake about its identity, does not constitute a genuine redefinition. The implication for all of *Zevachim* is vast. If a *kohen* genuinely believed the animal was an *olah* and slaughtered it with that *kavana*, but in fact it was a *shlamim*, the *korban* according to Rava would be entirely *kasher*, not merely valid in the diminished sense of *lo alu l'be'alim l'shem chova*. The mistake simply does not register as a redefining *machshava*. A truly honest mistake leaves the *korban*'s status entirely unaffected.

### Rav's Response

Rav's *alu va'alu* is now understood against this backdrop. The animals are lambs; the *korban* is being offered correctly. Whatever is going on in the *kohen*'s head about rams is irrelevant, because the *kohen* is not consciously attempting to change anything. The animals are what they are; the *avodah* proceeds on that basis.

### Honest Mistake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרַב בִּכְסָבוּר אֵילִים, וּשְׁחָטָן לְשׁוּם כְּבָשִׂים, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָשִׂים לְשׁוּם כְּבָשִׂים נִשְׁחֲטוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: The statement of Rav is reasonable in** a case where **one thought** that they were **rams** when he slaughtered them, **and** nevertheless **slaughtered them for the sake of lambs.** In such a case, it makes sense that they satisfy the community's obligation **since** the **lambs were slaughtered for the sake of lambs.**

Rav Chisda moves to clarify — and significantly limit — what Rav's ruling covers. *Mistavra milta d'Rav bechasavur Eilim v'shachtan lishum Kevashim* — Rav's ruling that it is *kasher* applies specifically to the case where the *kohen* thought the animals were rams but then slaughtered them as lambs. That is: the *kohen* is told these are *kivsei atzeret* but thinks someone made an error and brought rams by mistake. When the *kohen* then performs *shechita*, he says to himself: "These should have been lambs — I will slaughter them as lambs, treating them as the lambs they ought to have been." In this case, the animal is a lamb, the *kohen*'s intent at the moment of *shechita* is to treat it as a lamb, and the only "mistake" is a mistaken belief about the animal's species that has already been corrected in the *kohen*'s own *kavana*. There is no effective *shelo lishmo* at all.

### Akira B'Taos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל כְּסָבוּר אֵילִים, וּשְׁחָטָן לְשׁוּם אֵילִים – לָא. עֲקִירָה בְּטָעוּת הָוְיָא עֲקִירָה; וְרַבָּה אָמַר: עֲקִירָה בְּטָעוּת לָא הָוְיָא עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in a case where **he thought** that they were **rams** when he slaughtered them, **and** therefore **slaughtered them for the sake of rams,** they do **not** satisfy the community's obligation, even though they were actually lambs. This is due to the fact that the **erroneous uprooting** of the status of an offering **constitutes uprooting,** despite the fact that it was done in error. **But Rabba said:** The **erroneous uprooting** of the status of an offering **does not constitute uprooting.**

However, *aval kasavur Eilim v'shachtan lishum Eilim* — if the *kohen* thought they were rams and slaughtered them as rams, this is *lo kasher*. Why? *Akira b'taut havi akira* — even an honest mistake that redefines the animal's status is still a redefining act. If the *kohen* genuinely thought these were rams and his *shechita* was performed with the intent of slaughtering rams, he has effectively declared: "These are rams, and I am slaughtering rams." Even though he is wrong, even though it was an honest error, the act of mentally categorizing them as rams — and performing *shechita* with that identification — constitutes an *akira*, a displacement of their true status. This position — that *akira b'taut havei akira* — stands in tension with Rava's view that *akira b'ta'us lo havei akira*.

### Pigul Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָדַע דְּחַטָּאת הִיא, וְקָא מְחַשֵּׁב בַּהּ לְשׁוּם שְׁלָמִים – הַאי שׁוֹגְגִין?! מְזִידִין הָווּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** in which this is the *halakha*? **If we say** that it is referring to a case where the priest **knew that** the offering **was a sin offering,** which is eaten for only one day and one night, **and** nevertheless **he intended** the offering to be **for the sake of a peace offering,** which is eaten for two days and one night, and thereby his intention was that it be eaten after its appropriate time, is **this** considered **an unintentional** act by the priests? Certainly **they were** acting **intentionally.**

The Gemara now presses the question further through the lens of *pigul*. A *mishna* in *Gittin* records: if *kohanim* rendered *korbanot* invalid through *pigul*, those who acted *mezidin* must compensate the owner, while *shogegin* are exempt from payment. Yet a *baraita* adds: *pigulam pigul* — even the *shogeg* has made *pigul*. How can one create *pigul* unintentionally? The Gemara considers the case where a *kohen* thought the *korban* was a *shlamim* — valid for two days — when in fact it was a *chattat*, valid only for one day. Thinking it was a *shlamim*, the *kohen* entertained the intent to eat it the next day, which is the classic *machshava* of *pigul*. But this intent arose from an honest mistake about the *korban*'s identity. If *akira b'ta'us lo havei akira*, how can this honest mistake generate *pigul*? The Gemara thus appears to demonstrate that an honest mistake does still have halachic effect — which would support the position that *akira b'ta'us havei akira*.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דִּכְסָבוּר שְׁלָמִים הוּא, וְקָא מְחַשֵּׁב בַּהּ לְשׁוּם שְׁלָמִים, וּבְאוֹמֵר ״מוּתָּר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where the offering was a sin offering, which is eaten for only one day and one night, and the priest **thought that it was a peace offering and** therefore **he intended** the offering to be **for the sake of a peace offering,** which is eaten for two days and one night, and thereby his intention was that it be eaten after its appropriate time? **And** with regard to this case the *tanna* **taught: Their** act of **improper intention** renders the offering ***piggul*. Apparently,** this proves that the **erroneous uprooting** of the status of an offering **constitutes uprooting,** contrary to the ruling of Rabba.

Abaye responds: the case of the *shogeg* who does not pay is not one of mistaken identity at all. Rather, the *kohen* knew exactly what the *korban* was — he knew it was a *chattat* — but did not realize that his intent to eat it the next day would constitute *pigul* and invalidate the *korban*. His *shogeggut* was in the *violation*, not in the *korban*'s identity. This preserves Rava's position: *akira b'ta'us lo havei akira* means that a genuine mistake about the *korban*'s identity has no effect. A mistake about whether one's thought constitutes a violation is an entirely different matter. A *kohen* who consciously entertains an out-of-time eating intent — even while mistakenly believing this is harmless — has still performed a real *pigul machshava*. But a *kohen* who simply misidentifies the animal has not performed any real mental act of redefinition.

### Machshava Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, כׇּל מְנָחוֹת שֶׁנִּקְמְצוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, וְעָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה. שֶׁאֵין הַמְּנָחוֹת דּוֹמוֹת לַזְּבָחִים, שֶׁהַקּוֹמֵץ מַחֲבַת לְשׁוּם מַרְחֶשֶׁת – מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ שֶׁהִיא מַחֲבַת, חֲרֵבָה לְשׁוּם בְּלוּלָה – מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ שֶׁהִיא חֲרֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** to Rabba's opinion that erroneous uprooting of the status of an offering does not constitute uprooting. A *baraita* teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says: All meal offerings** from which **the handful was removed not for their sake are** entirely **valid, and have** even **satisfied the obligation of the owner,** unlike animal offerings slaughtered not for their sake, which do not satisfy the obligation of the owner. This is **because meal offerings are not similar to animal offerings. As,** in the case of **one who removes a handful** from a meal offering prepared in a shallow **pan for the sake of** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan, its mode of preparation proves** that **it** is a shallow-**pan** meal offering and not a deep-pan meal offering. Since a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan is hard and one prepared in a deep pan is soft, his intention is plainly false. Similarly, if one removes a handful from **a dry** meal offering, the meal offering of a sinner, which has no oil, **for the sake of** a meal offering **mixed** with oil, his intention is plainly false, as **its mode of preparation proves** that **it** is **a dry** meal offering.

Rav Zeira then challenges from a *mishna* involving Rabbi Shimon's ruling about *menachot*. Rabbi Shimon holds that for *menachot*, the physical reality of the *korban* overrides *machshava*: *kol menachos shenikmetzu shelo lishmon kshairos* — all *menachot* whose *kometz* was taken with incorrect intent are valid and the owners fulfill their obligation. The reason, Rabbi Shimon explains, is that *menachot* are not like animal *korbanot*: *shechita achas l'kulan, kabala achas l'kulan, zrika achas l'kulan* — with animal sacrifices, every animal undergoes the same external acts, making *machshava* the only distinguishing factor. But with *menachot*, the physical reality is self-evident: if the vessel contains the quantity of oil appropriate for a *machvas*, the physical reality declares it a *machvas* — *ma'aseha mochiachin aleha*; if the *mincha* is a *chariva*, dry without oil, physical reality declares it so. Therefore *machshava* cannot override what is physically manifest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בִּזְבָחִים אֵינוֹ כֵּן, שְׁחִיטָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן, קַבָּלָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן, זְרִיקָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to animal offerings** this **is not so.** There is **one** mode of **slaughter for all of** the offerings, **one** mode of **collection** of the blood **for all of them,** and **one** mode of **sprinkling for all of them.** Since the only factor that distinguishes between one type of offering and another is the intention of the individuals involved in its sacrifice, their intention is significant. Consequently, if one of the sacrificial rites is performed for the sake of the wrong type of offering, the offering does not satisfy its owner's obligation.

How does this intersect with the debate about *akira b'ta'us*? Rav Zeira argues that Rabbi Shimon's ruling must concern an honest mistake. If the *kohen* knew it was a *machvas* and deliberately tried to redefine it as a *marcheshes*, why would Rabbi Shimon excuse the *machshava*? Even Rabbi Shimon would agree that willful redefinition matters. The only reading that makes sense for Rabbi Shimon's leniency is that the *kohen* made an honest mistake and thought the *korban* was a *marcheshes*. And Rabbi Shimon says: even that honest mistake is overridden by the physical reality. This implies that absent such physical evidence, *akira b'ta'us* would be a genuine *akira* — an honest mistake would still constitute a redefinition. That would contradict Rava.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָדַע דְּמַחֲבַת הִיא, וְקָא קָמֵיץ לַהּ לְשׁוּם מַרְחֶשֶׁת – כִּי מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין, מַאי הָוֵי? הָא מִיעְקָר קָא עָקַיר לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** to which Rabbi Shimon is referring? **If we say** that he is referring to a case **where** the priest **knew that** the meal offering **was** prepared in a shallow **pan, and** nevertheless **he removed a handful** from it **for the sake of** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan,** then although **its mode of preparation proves** that it was prepared in a shallow pan, **what of it?** In any case **he is** consciously **uprooting** its status as a shallow-pan meal offering, changing it to a deep-pan meal offering by means of his intention when he removes the handful.

Abaye responds to preserve Rava's position: *le'olam d'yada d'machvas hi v'ka kametz lah l'shum marcheshes* — Rabbi Shimon's case actually involves a *kohen* who knows it is a *machvas* and deliberately attempts to treat it as a *marcheshes*. The *kohen*'s *machshava* is willful, not mistaken. And even so, Rabbi Shimon rules the *korban* is valid. Why? Because the physical reality is so demonstrably clear — *ma'aseha mochiachin aleha* — that even a willful attempt to redefine it cannot take effect. The vessel, the oil, the form of the *mincha* — all of these shout what it actually is. Rava then articulates this as a general principle: *machshava d'lo minkara pasal Rachmana, machshava d'minkara lo pasal Rachmana* — a *machshava* that is not externally evident, where nothing about the physical reality contradicts the intent, the Torah treats as operative and potentially invalidating. A *machshava* that is obviously contradicted by the physical reality — where the *machshava* does not register as credible against the evidence of the world — the Torah does not treat as operative. Applied to *menachot*, the *machshava* is always contradicted by the physical form of the *korban*, and therefore never invalidates. Applied to animal *korbanot*, the *machshava* is never independently visible, and therefore always carries weight — whether the intent is willful or mistaken.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דִּכְסָבוּר מַרְחֶשֶׁת הִיא, וְקָא קָמֵיץ לָהּ לְשׁוּם מַרְחֶשֶׁת, וּטְעָה, דְּהָכָא הוּא דְּמַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ, הָא בְּעָלְמָא עֲקִירָה בְּטָעוּת הָוְיָא עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where** the priest **thought it was** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan and** therefore **removed a handful for the sake of** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan, but he was mistaken,** as it was actually a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan? **It is** specifically **here,** in this case, that Rabbi Shimon rules that the offering remains valid, **since its mode of preparation proves** that **it** is a shallow-pan meal offering, which indicates **that in general, erroneous uprooting** of the status of an offering **constitutes uprooting,** contrary the ruling of Rabba.
>
> **Abaye said to** Rabbi Zeira, in response to his objection: This objection to Rabba's opinion is inconclusive, because one can explain that the *baraita* is **actually** referring to a case **where** the priest **knew that** the meal offering **was** prepared in a shallow **pan, and** nevertheless **he removed a handful for the sake of** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan.**
>
> **And** with regard to **that which you said** in your question: Although **its mode of preparation proves** that **it** is prepared in a shallow pan, **what of it,** i.e., since he is consciously uprooting its identity, how can it satisfy its owner's obligation? Abaye answers that **Rava** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rava says: The Merciful One disqualifies** an offering due to improper **intent that is not recognizably** false, i.e., when the physical properties of the offering itself do not prove that the intent is mistaken. **The Merciful One does not disqualify** an offering due to improper **intent that is recognizably** false. Consequently, if one removes a handful from a shallow-pan meal offering for the sake of a deep-pan meal offering, even if he did so consciously, the offering remains valid and satisfies the owner's obligation.

This leaves the Gemara at its closing point — and it is a genuinely open one. Throughout the many chapters of *Zevachim* in which *shelo lishmo* was discussed, the assumption was that a *kohen* who performs *zrikas hadam* of a *shlamim* with the intent of an *olah* has done something that invalidates the *korban* for the owner's *chovah*. But the Gemara has now surfaced the question of whether that assumed rule applies only when the *kohen* knew what the *korban* was and willfully redefined it, or whether it applies equally when the *kohen* was genuinely mistaken. The debate between Rav Chisda and Rava — *akira b'ta'us havei akira* versus *akira b'ta'us lo havei akira* — runs through the entirety of *Zevachim* without resolution, and the Gemara leaves us here, at the end of these pages, with a question that reaches all the way back to the very beginning.
