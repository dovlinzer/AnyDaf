# Pesachim 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bitul & Bedika Intro

### Rashi: Bitul & Tashbitu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַבּוֹדֵק צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: One who searches** for leaven **must render** all his leaven **null and void,** cognitively and verbally.

The opening discussion of our *daf* centers on a foundational principle stated earlier in the *masechet*: *habodek tzarich sheyevatel* — one who performs the search for *chametz* must also perform *bitul*, nullification. To understand why, it is worth surveying the range of opinions about how *bitul* functions in the first place.

In several places the *gemara* states that *bitul b'alma sagi* — mere *bitul* suffices. Rashi explains this by grounding *bitul* in the biblical command of *tashbitu*: the Torah's instruction to destroy *chametz* is fulfilled, according to Rashi, by the psychological act of negation. When one genuinely regards one's *chametz* as nothing — as worthless, as non-existent from one's own perspective — that mental act constitutes a fulfillment of *tashbitu*. The implication follows naturally: once *tashbitu* has been performed in this manner, one cannot be in violation of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh*, even though, technically speaking, one might still be said to "own" the *chametz* in some sense.

### Tosafot: Hefker Model

Tosafot finds this approach unsatisfying. For Tosafot, the only way to avoid transgressing *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh* is to ensure that the *chametz* is genuinely no longer one's own. The *chametz* must cease to belong to the person. Accordingly, Tosafot understands *tashbitu* as operating essentially as a form of *hefker* — a declaration of ownerlessness. When one performs *bitul*, one is in effect saying: this is not mine, I renounce it, it is hereby *hefker*. This position does create certain difficulties — there is a view that *hefker* requires three people present to take effect — but that is the thrust of Tosafot's position.

### Ran: Torah Non-Ownership

The Ran offers what might be characterized as a synthesis, or at least a significant variation on the Tosafot approach. He agrees that *bitul* works because the *chametz* is rendered not one's own — but the mechanism by which this occurs is not an act of *hefker*. Rather, the Ran points to a *gemara* learned earlier in the *masechet* which establishes that the Torah itself does not ascribe ownership of *chametz* to a person, at least in a meaningful sense. The underlying reasoning is that *chametz* during Pesach is *asur b'hana'ah* — forbidden to derive benefit from — and objects from which no benefit may be derived are, in principle, not owned in any legally cognizable sense. This is a broader question in halakha: do *issurei hana'ah* admit of *ba'alut*, of ownership at all? The straightforward reading of the *gemara* is that they do not.

And yet, the *gemara* also says that if one was holding *chametz* at the moment it became forbidden, one is liable. How does the Ran reconcile this? He explains that if a person actively possesses or implicitly holds onto *chametz* — if he has not in any way detached himself from it — then even though technically he does not "own" it, the Torah holds him responsible as if he did. The transgression of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh* reflects that continued, implicit possession.

*Bitul*, then, on the Ran's view, works as follows. Since *chametz* will automatically become *asur b'hana'ah* and thus not truly "owned," all one needs to do is indicate — at the moment *yom tov* begins — that one has no interest in it. By signaling that detachment, one ensures that the automatic non-ownership operates in one's favor. One is not making an active *hefker*; rather, one is simply removing the one factor — implicit possessive intent — that would have caused one to be held responsible despite the technical non-ownership. The moment one withdraws one's hands, so to speak, one is no longer responsible.

### Why Bedika After Bitul

Having established that *bitul* in principle suffices to avoid *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh*, a natural question arises: why does the *gemara* institute *bedika* at all? This question is more pointed than it might first appear. The first Rashi on the *mishna* explains that one performs *bedika* precisely in order not to transgress *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh* — one doesn't want *chametz* remaining in the house. Tosafot challenges this: the *gemara* itself says *bitul* suffices to avoid that violation, so once *bitul* is performed, Rashi's reason for *bedika* collapses.

The real answer to Rashi is that, from the *mishna*'s perspective, he is entirely correct. Scanning the *mishnayot*, one finds that *bitul* is almost never mentioned as a general practice — the sole exception is the case of someone on his way to bring a *korban Pesach* or to perform a *bris milah* for his son who suddenly realizes he left *chametz* at home. In that emergency scenario, the *mishna* permits *bitul* as a fallback. Beyond that, the *mishnayot* treat *bedika* as the primary, and indeed only, obligation. *Bitul* is a *bedi'eved* option for when circumstances prevent one from doing it properly.

It was Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav — *amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav, habodek tzarich sheyevatel* — who introduced the now-standard practice of combining *bedika* with *bitul* as belt-and-suspenders. The logic is: if somehow one missed a piece of *chametz* during the *bedika*, the *bitul* covers it. Once this belt-and-suspenders principle is established, Tosafot's question emerges with full force: if the suspenders (*bitul*) are already in place, why do we still need the belt (*bedika*)? Tosafot gives two reasons. First, even if one is not technically in violation of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh*, having *chametz* around the house creates a real risk of accidentally eating it. *Chametz* is not like *chazir*, which one knows at all times not to eat; *chametz* is food one normally consumes, and one might simply forget that it is Pesach. Second, beyond that practical concern, the Torah's language of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatzeh* demonstrates that the Torah itself objects intrinsically to the presence of *chametz* — not merely because one might eat it, but because there is something inherently problematic about its being there at all. The rabbis therefore maintained the *bedika* requirement even after *bitul* was introduced.

### Bal Yera'eh Approaches

These two reasons Tosafot offers for *bedika* — the safeguard against accidental eating, and the intrinsic objection to the presence of *chametz* — actually map onto two distinct approaches to understanding the *bal yera'eh u'val yimatzeh* prohibition itself. One might ask: why does the Torah state *bal yera'eh u'val yimatzeh* at all? For every other prohibition, the Torah simply says: don't eat this. Why, with *chametz*, does it add: don't even have it visible, don't even have it found?

One approach holds that this is a safeguard. *Chametz* is so common, so familiar as a food, that simply prohibiting its consumption is insufficient — people will forget, they will slip. *Bal yera'eh u'val yimatzeh* therefore operates as a hedge around the eating prohibition. On this view, Tosafot's first reason for *bedika* — the concern about accidental eating — precisely parallels the Torah's concern.

The second approach inverts the logic entirely. The focus is not on *chametz* as a food that must not be consumed. The focus is on Pesach as *Chag HaMatzot* — the Festival of Matzot. On this reading, the very presence of *chametz* is antithetical to the nature of the *chag*. It is a reenactment of the primal experience of *Bnei Yisrael* leaving Egypt with no *chametz* whatsoever. The prohibition on eating *chametz* is therefore a consequence of this more fundamental reality, not the other way around. And so the very presence of *chametz*, even when there is no risk of eating it, is itself a violation of what the *chag* demands. This parallels Tosafot's second reason: the rabbis objected intrinsically to *chametz* in the house, not only because one might come to eat it.

This interpretive duality is further reflected in the distinction between the two *pesukim* underlying *bal yera'eh u'val yimatzeh*. The *gemara* — which was the subject of the previous *daf* — treats *lo yimatzeh b'vateichem* and *lo yera'eh lecha b'chol gevulecha* as a unified prohibition. But the *pshat* of the two verses reflects the two different concerns independently. *Lo yimatzeh b'vateichem* carries no limit of *lecha* — it is not about my ownership or my seeing it; it is about the possibility that *chametz* could be uncovered and found in my home, regardless of whose it is or whether it is buried. Proximity creates risk. By contrast, *lo yera'eh lecha b'chol gevulecha* — out in the wider world beyond one's home — the concern shifts to one's personal connection: it is mine (*lecha*) and I am encountering it. Even absent any risk of eating, my being connected to *chametz* in the public domain is itself the problem. So the two reasons Tosafot gives for *bedika* may indeed correspond to the two different emphases of the Torah's language, which the *gemara* has unified into one *issur*.

### Da'ateih Iluyei Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגֵּמָ׳: וּמִי תֵּימָא, דִּילְמָא מִשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ לְבַטְּלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 6b, line 15) **The Gemara** asks: **And let him nullify it when he finds it.**

The *gemara* justifies the combined practice of *bedika* and *bitul* by noting: if one searches but does not also perform *bitul*, one might find a loaf of *chametz* on Pesach and *da'ateih illuyeih* — one's mind will fix upon it — and one will thereby transgress. The question is what exactly *da'ateih illuyeih* means, and here Rashi and Tosafot diverge significantly.

For Tosafot, *da'ateih illuyeih* does not require any special affirmative act of wanting to keep the *chametz*. It simply means the opposite of *perurin* — crumbs. The *gemara* elsewhere establishes that crumbs are automatically *batel*, considered insignificant by default. *Da'ateih illuyeih* is merely saying: unlike crumbs, a proper loaf is not automatically *batel*. If you missed it during *bedika* and did not do *bitul*, that loaf is still fully significant and you have transgressed by having it there. No new act of positive desire is needed.

Rashi, however, makes the term carry more weight. For Rashi, *da'ateih illuyeih* implies an active desire — one finds the loaf and actively wants it, fixes one's intention upon it. This connects to the Ran's framework in an interesting way. One who performed *bedika* has been actively searching to rid the home of *chametz*, and that very act of searching signals a general detachment from all *chametz* in the house. The *chametz* was technically never one's own to begin with, and one was actively trying to eliminate it. So one exists in a grey zone: not having pushed the *chametz* actively away through *bitul*, but also clearly not possessing it with any affirmative intent. Perhaps, in that grey zone, if upon discovery one immediately acts to destroy the *chametz*, one would be safe — because it was never truly one's own, and one never intended to keep it. The moment one *wants* the loaf, however — that is an act of possession. And that, according to Rashi's understanding of *da'ateih illuyeih*, is what triggers the transgression. *Bitul* prevents this by preemptively eliminating even the possibility of such possessive intent arising.

## Bitul Timing Rules

### Fourth/Fifth Hour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיבַטְּלֵיהּ בְּאַרְבַּע, וְנִיבַטְּלֵיהּ בְּחָמֵשׁ? כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו זְמַן אִיסּוּרָא הוּא וְלָאו זְמַן בִּיעוּרָא הוּא, דִּילְמָא פָּשַׁע וְלָא מְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 6b, line 17) If so, **let him render** the leaven **null and void during the fourth hour or let him render it null and void during the fifth hour** of the fourteenth of Nisan. Why is he required to do so when he searches for leaven on the evening of the fourteenth? The Gemara answers: **Since** the fourth hour **is neither the time** of the **prohibition** of the leaven **nor the time** of its **removal,** it is a nondescript point in time. There is concern that **perhaps he will be negligent and will not render it null and void,** and the leaven will remain in his possession.

The *gemara* now raises a seemingly simple question: given that *bitul* must accompany *bedika*, why not simply defer the *bitul* to the daytime on *erev Pesach* — say, the fourth or fifth hour of the day? Why connect it to the nighttime *bedika* at all?

The *gemara* answers: *keivan d'lav zman issura hu v'lav zman biyura hu* — the fourth and fifth hours are neither the time when *chametz* becomes forbidden nor the recognized time of its destruction. If *bitul* is not anchored to a defined, meaningful moment in the *chametz*-removal process, *dilma pasha v'lo mivatel lei* — one will be negligent and fail to perform it. The rabbis understood human psychology well: attaching *bitul* to the *bedika* ritual gives it a clear moment and ensures it will be performed. A diffuse obligation with no defined hook will be forgotten.

### Sixth Hour Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיבַטְּלֵיהּ בְּשֵׁית! כֵּיוָן דְּאִיסּוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן עִילָּוֵיהּ — כִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא דָּמְיָא, וְלָאו בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ קָיְימָא, וְלָא מָצֵי מְבַטֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 1) The Gemara asks: **But let him render** the leaven **null and void during the sixth** hour, when he burns it. The Gemara answers: **Since** there is **a rabbinic prohibition** that takes effect **on** the leaven, as it is prohibited to derive benefit from it after the fifth hour, its legal status **is like** that of leaven prohibited **by Torah law, and** therefore **it is not in his possession and he** is **unable to nullify** it.

The *gemara* then considers whether *bitul* should instead be performed at the sixth hour — 11 o'clock on a standard day — when *chametz* becomes rabbinically prohibited. This moment has clarity and significance; surely it would be an appropriate time for *bitul*? The *gemara* answers: *keivan d'issura d'rabbanan it lei, k'd'oraisa damya* — since the rabbinic prohibition has taken effect, it operates functionally like the biblical prohibition in this context. Once *chametz* is rabbinically forbidden, it is *lav birshutei kayma* — it is no longer in one's legal domain — and therefore *v'lo matzi mevatel lei*, one can no longer perform *bitul* over it. Just as one cannot perform *bitul* once it is 12 o'clock and the biblical prohibition has set in, so too one cannot perform *bitul* at 11 o'clock once the rabbinic prohibition has set in.

### Kiddushin Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ מִשֵּׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת וּלְמַעְלָה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּחִיטֵּי קוּרְדִּנְיָתָא — אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְקִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 2) **Rav Giddel said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **a man who betroths** a woman on the fourteenth of Nisan **from** the beginning of **the sixth hour and onward, even** if he does so **with wheat from the mountains [*kurdanaita*],** which is particularly hard and there is no certainty that it will ferment even if water falls on it, nevertheless, as it is possible that the wheat leavened, its legal status is that of leaven. Consequently, it is prohibited to derive benefit from this wheat, which is legally worthless. Therefore, if a man gives the wheat to a woman for the purpose of betrothal, **one** need **not** be **concerned that** it is **a betrothal.**

The *gemara* grounds this principle in a teaching: *d'amar Rav Gidel amar Rav Chiya bar Yosef amar Rav, hamekadesh mishesh sha'os ul'ma'la afilu b'chitei kordneisa ein choshashin l'kidushin*. If one betroths a woman from the sixth hour onward using *chitei kordneisa* — a variety of hard wheat from a certain region that will not ferment even if water touches it, and thus constitutes only rabbinic *chametz* (*chametz nuksha*) — there is no concern whatsoever for the validity of the *kiddushin*. The wheat is *asur b'hana'ah* and therefore effectively worthless; one cannot effect a betrothal with it.

The structure of the analogy is instructive. The case involves the biblical time period — from midday onward — but rabbinic *chametz*. The parallel case for our purposes involves biblical *chametz* but the rabbinic time period — from the sixth hour, meaning 11 o'clock. In both configurations, the *chametz* is rendered legally without value, and therefore *bitul* — like *kiddushin* — cannot take effect.

Rashi, it should be noted, attempts to read *mishesh sha'os ul'ma'la* as meaning from 11 o'clock onward — that is, from the beginning of the sixth hour rather than from its end at midday. Almost all the *Rishonim* disagree, maintaining that the phrase always means from midday onward. The analogy therefore, in their view, involves *chametz nuksha* at the biblical time, standing in for biblical *chametz* at the rabbinic time period. What motivates Rashi's reading is the very need to make the analogy work directly, without the transposition — a reading that the other *Rishonim* find linguistically unpersuasive.

This raises a broader question about rabbinic authority over *d'oraisa* statuses. One could argue that the rabbis are not directly declaring a *d'oraisa* status to be altered; rather, since in practice no benefit may be derived from the *chametz* within the normative framework of rabbinic law, it is effectively worthless in practice, and therefore worthless for *kiddushin* purposes as well — regardless of whether the disqualification is rabbinic or biblical in origin. A parallel question arises regarding a witness who is disqualified only rabbinically: does a *kiddushin* in which he served as witness have *d'oraisa* validity, or does the practical reality of his non-acceptance in rabbinic courts undermine even the *d'oraisa* status of the *kiddushin*? That, too, is debated, and the structure of the question is the same.

### Braita: Yom Tov Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְנִזְכַּר שֶׁיֵּשׁ חָמֵץ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, מְבַטְּלוֹ בְּלִבּוֹ, אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 3) **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If one **was sitting in the study hall and he remembered that there is leavened bread in his house,** he should **render it null and void in his heart, both on Shabbat and on the Festival.**

The *gemara* now faces a challenge from a *braita*. The *braita* states: *ha-yoshev b'veis ha-midrash v'nizkar she-yesh chametz b'soch beiso, mevatlo b'libo* — if one is sitting in the *beis midrash* and recalls that there is *chametz* in one's home, one may simply perform *bitul* in one's heart. And this applies equally to *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*.

The *gemara* accepts the *Shabbat* case without difficulty — that refers to when the fourteenth of Nisan falls on *Shabbat*, so one performed *bedika* on Thursday night and now finds oneself on *erev Pesach* which is *Shabbat*, with *chametz* one forgot to eliminate. *Yom Tov*, however, is startling: if it is *Yom Tov*, then Pesach has already begun. This appears to directly contradict the principle just established — *bitar issura*, once the prohibition has taken effect, *bitul* is no longer possible. Yet the *braita* seems to permit *bitul* even after Pesach has commenced.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הָכָא בְּתַלְמִיד יוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ עָסְקִינַן, וְנִזְכַּר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִיסָּה מְגוּלְגֶּלֶת בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וּמִתְיָירֵא שֶׁמָּא תַּחֲמִיץ. קָדֵים וּמְבַטֵּיל לַיהּ מִיקַּמֵּי דְּתַחְמִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 4) **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: Here we are dealing with a student sitting before his teacher, and he remembers that there is kneaded dough in his house, and he is afraid lest it leaven** before he can return home to warn the members of his household. Since the dough has not yet leavened and is not yet prohibited, he can take **earlier** action **and render it null and void before it becomes leavened.**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov resolves the difficulty: *hacha b'issos ha-yoshev lifnei rabbo askinan* — the *braita* is speaking of a specific case. The person sitting in the *beis midrash* is a student sitting before his teacher, and what he recalls is not that he left finished *chametz* behind, but that he left behind *issos megulgalos* — dough that had not yet been put in the oven. On *Yom Tov* one could have baked it; the student is sitting before his teacher and realizes he left the dough, and *u-misyara shema tachmitz* — he fears it will become *chametz*. But it has not yet become *chametz*. Therefore, *kodem hu u-mevatlah mikamei de-tachmitz* — he can perform *bitul* before it becomes *chametz*, because at this moment it is still not forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בְּתוֹךְ בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 5) The Gemara comments: The language of the *baraita* **is also precise** in accordance with this explanation, **as** the *baraita* **teaches:** If one **was sitting in the study hall.** This indicates that the dough has not yet risen, and the problem is that he cannot arrive home in time to prevent it from rising. However, if it had already become leavened, rendering it null and void will not remedy the situation even if he were home. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** proof that Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov's interpretation is correct.

The *gemara* finds support for this reading in the very language of the *braita*: it says *ha-yoshev* — the one who is *sitting* in the *beis midrash*. The specific scenario of sitting before one's teacher explains why he cannot simply leave and resolve the problem directly. The implication is that if he could leave, he would have done so and prevented the dough from becoming *chametz* in the first place. *Shema mina* — this is a valid inference.

The overall picture that emerges from this section is that the *gemara*'s introduction of *bitul* as a standard accompaniment to *bedika* represents a significant shift from the *mishna*'s framework. Where the *mishna* treated *bedika* as the primary obligation and *bitul* as an emergency fallback, the *gemara* transforms the picture: *bitul* becomes a standard and important practice, with careful rules governing when it can and cannot be performed.

## Moldy Bread Case

### Bread Basket Rov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הַפַּת שֶׁעִיפְּשָׁה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָבְתָה מַצָּה — מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 6) **Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** With regard to a vessel that contains several loaves in which there was **bread that became moldy,** and it is not evident whether it is leaven or *matza*, **once there was more *matza*** than leaven in the vessel, it is **permitted.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a case brought by Rabbah bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *ha-pach she-ipash* — a bread basket in which a moldy piece of bread is found. *Keivan she-rubah matzah, muteres* — if the majority of use of that basket has been for *matzah*, the moldy bread is permitted.

Some preliminary clarification is in order. This discussion presupposes a world in which *matzah* looked very different from the flat, cracker-like *matzah* we know today. Sefardi *matzah* gives a better approximation — something closer to a pita. When a piece of bread goes moldy in a basket, it can be impossible to determine at that stage of decomposition whether the original item was a flat *matzah* or a regular loaf of *chametz*. In that state of uncertainty, the *gemara* says one may rely on *rov* — majority usage — and the bread is *muttar*. This is a striking ruling: on Pesach, where we might expect extreme stringency, the *gemara* is prepared to apply ordinary halakhic decision-making principles rather than simply requiring one to discard the item.

### Jerusalem Money Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא תְּנַן: מָעוֹת שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ לִפְנֵי סוֹחֲרֵי בְהֵמָה — לְעוֹלָם מַעֲשֵׂר. בְּהַר הַבַּיִת — חוּלִּין. בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, בִּשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל — מַעֲשֵׂר, בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה — חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, lines 8-9) **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **coins that were found before animal merchants** in Jerusalem, **they are always** assumed to be money of the second **tithe,** as most of the animals purchased in Jerusalem were bought with that money. This *halakha* applies both during a Festival and throughout the year, as people would purchase animals for meat with their second-tithe money, and it can therefore be assumed that these coins have the status of second tithe. However, if the money was found **on the Temple Mount** it is **non-sacred** money, even during a Festival. If the money was found elsewhere **in Jerusalem during the Festival,** when many people came to Jerusalem with their second-tithe money, the coins are presumed to be second-**tithe** money. However, if the coins were found **during the rest of the year,** it is **non-sacred** money.

The *gemara* immediately questions this approach. Why rely on *rov* at all? Even if the majority of usage was for *chametz*, surely we should rather follow *batar basra* — the most recent use. If the basket was last used for *matzah*, that should be the operative assumption. Isn't that the stronger principle?

The *gemara* supports this intuition by citing a *mishna* concerning money found near the animal-sellers in Jerusalem. Money found in front of those who sell animals for slaughter in Jerusalem must always be assumed to be *ma'aser sheni* money — regardless of the time of year — because the predominant use of funds in that vicinity is *ma'aser sheni* money, which pilgrims brought to spend, often on meat or *shelamim* sacrifices. Money found on the Temple Mount itself (*Har HaBayis*) is assumed to be *chulin* — ordinary money — because people did not typically bring animals there for purchase; Tosafot adds that even if one might carry money there, that location does not generate the same presumption of *ma'aser sheni* usage.

The more interesting case is money found in the streets of Jerusalem generally. *B'sha'as ha-regel* — during the festival period — such money is assumed to be *ma'aser sheni*, because the entire city is filled with pilgrims and the majority of currency circulating is *ma'aser sheni* money. *B'she'ar yemos ha-shana* — the rest of the year — such money is assumed to be *chulin*. But why? One might think that even after the *regel*, there could still be leftover *ma'aser sheni* money that had been in circulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שְׁמַעְיָה בַּר זֵירָא: מַאי טַעְמָא — הוֹאִיל וְשׁוּקֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם עֲשׂוּיִין לְהִתְכַּבֵּד בְּכׇל יוֹם. אַלְמָא אָמְרִינַן: קַמָּאֵי קַמָּאֵי אָזְלִי לֵיהּ, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 10) **Rav Shemaya bar Zeira said: What is the reason** that during the rest of the year the coins are considered non-sacred money, even on the day after the Festival? **Since the markets of Jerusalem tend to be cleaned every day,** any money left there would already have been found by the street cleaners. Consequently, any coins found there were left there recently. **Apparently, we say** that **the first ones are gone and these** objects **are later ones.**

Rav Shmaya bar Zeira explains: *ho'il u-shukei Yerushalayim asuyim lehiskaibed bechol yom* — the marketplaces of Jerusalem were swept daily. Therefore one can always assume that the money found on the ground fell recently — within the last day. After the *regel*, the most recent money circulating would be ordinary *chulin* money, so that is what one assumes. The principle at work here is *kamai kamai azlu lei, v'hanei achrinei ninhu* — the earlier money has been swept away, and what remains is from the most recent usage.

The *gemara* then asks: why not apply this same principle to the bread basket? Even if one used the basket for *chametz* in the weeks prior, that older *chametz* usage is gone, and the most recent use was for *matzah*. Why rely on *rov* rather than *batar basra*?

### Mold Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּעִיפּוּשָׁהּ מוֹכִיחַ עִילָּוָיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 11) It is **different here, as the mold proves about** the loaf that it is leaven, as food does not become moldy unless it has been sitting for a long time.

The *gemara* answers: *lo, shani hacha d'ipusha mochach iluyei* — no, here it is different, because the mold is itself evidence. If the bread is moldy, it is clearly not from the last few days. Therefore the principle of *azlinan batar batra* cannot be invoked, since the mold demonstrates that this is not a recent item.

The *gemara* then pursues this further. If the mold proves the bread is old, then one might say the *rov* is also irrelevant: the mold demonstrates the bread is from a time before Pesach, when *chametz* was certainly being used — so what does it help that the majority use was *matzah* if the item demonstrably predates the *matzah* period?

### Fresh Baking Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לָא תֵּימָא שֶׁרָבְתָה מַצָּה, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁרַבּוּ יְמֵי מַצָּה עִילָּוָיהּ. אִי הָכִי, פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּעִיפּוּשָׁהּ מְרוּבֶּה. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּעִיפּוּשָׁהּ מְרוּבֶּה — אִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דְּוַדַּאי חָמֵץ מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כֵּיוָן שֶׁרַבּוּ יְמֵי מַצָּה עִילָּוָיהּ, אָמְרִינַן: כׇּל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא נַהֲמָא חַמִּימָא אֲפָה, וּשְׁדָא עִילָּוָיהּ וְעָפְשָׁא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, lines 11-13) **Rabba said: Do not say there was more *matza*** than leaven in the vessel; **rather, say that several days of** eating ***matza* have passed over** the vessel. In other words, several days of the Festival, during which *matza* is consumed, have passed. Therefore, it is more likely that the moldy loaf is *matza*. The Gemara asks: **If so,** it is **obvious** that the moldy loaf is *matza*, not leaven. The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary** to teach this *halakha* with regard to a situation where **its mold is extensive. Lest you say: Since its mold is extensive the matter is revealed that it is certainly leavened bread,** therefore Rav **teaches us** that one cannot be entirely sure that this is the case. The Gemara explains the reason for the uncertainty. **Since several days of** eating ***matza* have passed over** the vessel, **we say: Each and every day he baked warm loaves, which he placed upon** the previous days' *matza*, causing **it to grow moldier.** Therefore, it is possible that even though only a brief time has passed, the *matza* has grown very moldy, due to the moisture and heat inside the vessel.

Rava resolves this by reinterpreting the case. The operative factor is not merely that the majority use of the basket was *matzah*, but rather that *rabu yemei matzah iluyeih* — most of Pesach has already passed. Given that most of Pesach has elapsed, one can account for the heavy mold not by assuming the bread is from before Pesach, but by applying the following reasoning: *amrinan kol yoma v'yoma nami chamimei afu* — each day fresh *matzah* was baked and placed in the basket, piling on top of the small remaining piece, and the accumulation of fresh, warm bread on top is what caused the mold to develop so extensively. In other words, the mold is explained not by the item's pre-Pesach origin but by the continuous process of fresh *matzah* being added daily throughout Pesach.

The *gemara* presses: if it is merely very moldy, one might have thought — *keivan d'ipusha meruba iglai milta d'vadai chametz mealya hu* — that such extensive mold proves definitively that this is pre-Pesach *chametz*. The *gemara* teaches: no, *keivan sherabu yemei matzah iluyeih*, since most of Pesach has passed, we can account for the mold through the daily baking theory, and therefore the bread remains permitted.

### Practical Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר בָּתְרָא? וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תֵּיבָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהּ מְעוֹת חוּלִּין וּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר, אִם רוֹב חוּלִּין — חוּלִּין, אִם רוֹב מַעֲשֵׂר — מַעֲשֵׂר. וְאַמַּאי? לֵיזִיל בָּתַר בָּתְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 14) In regard to the aforementioned principle, the Gemara asks: **And do we,** in general, **follow the last** item in determining the identity of the item in question? **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says:** With regard to **a box** that people **used for** both **non-sacred coins and** second-**tithe coins, if the majority** of its use was for **non-sacred** money, the coins are considered **non-sacred. If the majority** of its use was for second-**tithe** coins, the coins are considered second-**tithe** money. The Gemara asks: **But why** is this so? **Let us follow** the **last** item placed in the box.

The underlying conceptual issue is significant. One might have assumed that in the context of Pesach — where we are extraordinarily careful about even the slightest possibility of *chametz* — we would never rely on *rov* or *batar basra* to permit something that could conceivably be *chametz*. Yet the *gemara* shows no such hesitation. Standard halakhic tools of probability and presumption apply. This reflects an important baseline: the *gemara* does not treat Pesach *chametz* as categorically different from other halakhic uncertainties when it comes to applying evidentiary principles. The stringency that characterizes our contemporary practice — where any possibility of *chametz* leads to immediate disposal — is not the *gemara*'s default posture.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהּ מְעוֹת חוּלִּין וּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵין יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן בַּסּוֹף. רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהּ צִיבּוּרִין צִיבּוּרִין. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח בְּגוּמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 15) **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** people **used** the box **for** both **non-sacred coins and** second-**tithe coins, and he does not know which** of the two kinds of money was placed there **last.** **Rav Zevid said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where he used** one part of the box for **piles** of non-sacred coins and another part of the box for **piles** of second-tithe coins. In this case, there was no definitive most recent use of the box, as a coin may have moved from one side of the box to the other. **Rav Pappa said:** We are dealing with a case **where** the coin **was found in a hole** in the box. The concern is that this coin might not be of the type last placed into the box. Instead, it is possible that this coin remained from a previous use and was not removed because it was obscured in the hole.

The principle of *azlinan batar batra* — follow the most recent use — and *rov* each have their domain. When the most recent use is clear, one follows it. When it is not clear (because one does not remember which use was most recent, or because one used the vessel for both simultaneously, or because the item found was in a crevice that would have escaped any emptying), one follows *rov*. This emerges from the *gemara*'s further analysis of the money-box case, where Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda rules by *rov* rather than *batar basra*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains this is where one does not remember which use was most recent; Rav Zevid explains it as a case where both uses occurred simultaneously; Rav Pappa explains it as a case where the coin was found in a crevice, meaning it could not be assumed to have come from the most recent deposit.

## Brachot on Bedika

### Bracha Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַבּוֹדֵק צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 16) **Rav Yehuda said: One who searches** for leaven **must recite a blessing.**

The *gemara* returns to the ritual of *bedika* itself with the statement of Rav Yehuda: *ha-bodek tzarich she-yivarech* — one who performs the search for *chametz* must recite a blessing. The *mishna* discusses using candlelight for *bedika*, but the *mishna* does not cast *bedika* as a formal ritual with a required blessing. It is the *gemara* — and specifically this ruling of Rav Yehuda — that transforms *bedika* into a ritualized act demanding a *bracha*. Once *bitul* has been introduced as a standard accompaniment to *bedika*, the *bedika* itself takes on greater ritual significance, and that significance demands a blessing.

### Le-Va'er vs. Al Biyur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? רַב פַּפֵּי אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: (אוֹמֵר) ״לְבַעֵר חָמֵץ״. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״עַל בִּיעוּר חָמֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7a, line 16) **What blessing does he recite,** i.e., what is the correct formula of the blessing? **Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava** that one **recites:** Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and has commanded us **to remove leavened bread. Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava:** One should recite: **Concerning the removal of leavened bread.**

The *gemara* then poses the natural follow-up: *mai mivarech* — what blessing does one recite? Here there is a dispute between two of Rava's students. Rav Pappi, citing Rava, says: *le-va'er chametz* — "who has commanded us to destroy *chametz*." Rav Pappa, also citing Rava, says: *al bi'ur chametz* — "upon the destruction of *chametz*."

Both agree on the conceptual content — this blessing relates to the *d'oraisa* mitzva of *bi'ur chametz*, of *tashbitu*. The dispute is purely linguistic: should the blessing use the infinitive *le-va'er* (to destroy), or the prepositional phrase *al bi'ur* (upon the destroying)? The *gemara* frames this as follows. *Be-le-va'er kulei alma lo peligi d'vadai le-haba mashma* — regarding *le-va'er*, all agree it unambiguously refers to the future, to something about to be done. The phrase "to destroy," formulated as an infinitive, clearly speaks of an imminent action. *Ki peligi be-al bi'ur* — the dispute is specifically about *al bi'ur chametz*. One view holds that *al* intrinsically implies a past action — "upon the destroying" means "in response to destroying that has already occurred" — and therefore using *al* before the act is linguistically problematic. The other view holds that *al* can equally refer to the future and poses no difficulty.

### Al for Future Tense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּ״לְבַעֵר״ — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּוַדַּאי לְהַבָּא מַשְׁמַע. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּ״עַל בִּיעוּר״. מָר סָבַר: מֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע, וּמָר סָבַר: לְהַבָּא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, lines 1) **With regard to** the formula: **To remove, everyone agrees that** it **certainly refers to the future.** This formulation undoubtedly indicates that the person reciting the blessing is about to begin fulfilling the mitzva of removing leaven, and it is therefore an appropriate blessing. **Where they disagree is with regard to** the formula: **Concerning the elimination** of leaven. One **Sage,** Rav Pappi, **maintains** that **it is referring to** an act that was performed **previously.** Since this formula is referring to the removal of leaven as a task already completed, it would be more appropriate for a blessing recited after performance of that mitzva was completed. **And** the other **Sage,** Rav Pappa, **maintains** that this expression **refers to the future.**

The *gemara* now proceeds to test whether *al* can legitimately refer to a future action, by examining instances where *al* appears in established blessings that clearly precede the act they accompany.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״בָּרוּךְ ... אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל הַמִּילָה״!
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 2) The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Pappi's opinion from the formula of the blessing recited just prior to circumcision: **Blessed** are You…**Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and has commanded us concerning circumcision.** Apparently this expression indeed is referring to a future act.

The first example: *baruch asher kiddishanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al ha-mila* — the blessing recited at *mila*, which uses the *al* formulation. Is this not a case of *al* referring to a future act? The *gemara* deflects the proof: in the context of *mila*, using *le-mul* (to circumcise) would be problematic, because "You have commanded *me* to circumcise" implies a personal obligation to perform the act — yet it is typically the *mohel* who performs *mila*, not the father. *Al ha-mila* is therefore a more general formulation — "upon the *mila*" — which does not imply personal agency. The *al* formulation was chosen not because *al* refers to the future but because the personal *le* formulation was unavailable. The *gemara* then asks: what would the father say if he himself performs the *mila*? The answer: in that case, one could say *la-mul*, and presumably that would be correct. This opens the possibility that the blessing's formulation varies based on circumstance — a fluidity that was characteristic of the Tannaitic period, before blessings were fully standardized.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, הֵיכִי נֵימָא? נֵימָא ״לָמוּל״ — לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָאו אִיהוּ מָהֵיל? אֲבִי הַבֵּן מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אִין הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 3) The Gemara rejects this contention: That is no proof, as **what** alternative formula **can we recite there?** If **we say:** He, Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and commanded us **to circumcise, is there no alternative to he** himself, i.e., the boy's father, **circumcising** his son? The father is commanded to circumcise his son, and he may appoint one who is not commanded to circumcise his son to act in his stead. Therefore, the more general formula of the blessing is recited: About the circumcision. The Gemara raises a difficulty: In a case where **the child's father** himself circumcises his son, **what can be said?** The Gemara responds: **Yes, it is indeed so.** If the father himself performs the circumcision he in fact recites the blessing: And has commanded us to circumcise.

Tosafot draws an important observation from this exchange: the very fact that the *gemara* permits *al ha-mila* when there is a need, even though *al* might suggest the past, proves that everyone agrees *al* is capable of referring to the future. Otherwise, it would never have been permitted even in cases of necessity. What the position opposed to *al* maintains is not that *al* is incapable of future reference, but that *le* is more naturally forward-looking, and therefore *le* is preferable when available.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״בָּרוּךְ ... אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל הַשְּׁחִיטָה״! הָתָם נָמֵי, הֵיכִי נֵימָא? נֵימָא ״לִשְׁחוֹט״ — לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָאו אִיהוּ שָׁחֵט?
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 4) The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Pappi's opinion. The blessing recited over ritual slaughter is: **Blessed**…**Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and has commanded us concerning slaughtering.** This blessing likewise indicates that this formula is appropriate prior to an action. The Gemara again rejects this claim: **There too, what** alternative formula **can we recite?** If **we say:** Who has commanded us **to slaughter, is there no alternative to his slaughtering** the animal? There is no mitzva to slaughter an animal. It is merely the necessary preparation before one may eat meat. Therefore, the more general formula of the blessing is recited: Concerning slaughtering.

The second example: *baruch asher kiddishanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al ha-shechita* — the blessing upon *shechita*, which also uses *al*. Again the *gemara* deflects: *shechita* as a command is not personal to any particular individual — the *shochet* is not uniquely commanded to perform it, others could do so — and therefore a *le-shechot* formulation would be inappropriate. *Al ha-shechita* is the general, non-personal formulation required by the circumstances. The *gemara* asks about one who brings a *korban Pesach* and could personally be said to be commanded to perform the *shechita* — and again the answer is that in such a case, *li-shchot* would be appropriate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח וְקׇדָשִׁים מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אִין הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 5) The Gemara raises a difficulty: If so, with regard to the slaughter of the **Paschal lamb and** other **consecrated** animals, **what can be said?** One is indeed commanded to slaughter these animals. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** When slaughtering the Paschal lamb or any other offering, one recites: Who has commanded us to slaughter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָעוֹשֶׂה לוּלָב לְעַצְמוֹ, מְבָרֵךְ: ״שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה״. נְטָלוֹ לָצֵאת בּוֹ, אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל נְטִילַת לוּלָב״! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאַגְבְּהֵהּ נְפַק בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 6) The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Pappi's opinion from the *Tosefta*: **One who prepares a *lulav* for himself recites the blessing: Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time.** When **he takes it to fulfill with it** the obligation to take the *lulav*, **he says: Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and has commanded us concerning the taking of the *lulav*.** Although he has yet to perform the mitzva, he does not recite the formula: To take. The Gemara answers: **It is different there, as at the time when he lifts** the *lulav* before he recites the blessing, he already **fulfilled** his obligation by Torah law. Consequently, the formula: Concerning the taking, is indeed more appropriate for an action that he has already performed.

The third example concerns the blessing upon taking the *lulav*: *al netilat lulav*. Here the *gemara* offers a different explanation for the use of *al*. *B'idana d'agbei nafik bei* — at the moment one lifts the *lulav*, one has already fulfilled the obligation. The act of lifting constitutes fulfillment. Therefore the blessing upon taking the *lulav* can be said to come, in practice, after the moment of fulfillment, and thus *al* — which suggests response to something that has occurred — is actually apt. Tosafot qualifies this: one cannot truly say the obligation is completely fulfilled the moment the *lulav* is lifted, because then making a *birchat ha-mitzva* after fulfillment would be improper. Rather, the continued holding and shaking of the *lulav* constitutes an ongoing *ma'aseh mitzva*, so even once the initial act of lifting has been performed, the person is still in the midst of the mitzva. The *al* formulation reflects the slightly-after-the-fact character of the blessing relative to the initial moment of fulfillment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי: ״לָצֵאת בּוֹ״? ״יָצָא בּוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא: ״לֵישֵׁב בַּסּוּכָּה״, תְּנָא רֵישָׁא נָמֵי ״לָצֵאת בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 7) The Gemara raises an objection: **If so,** the statement that he takes it **to fulfill** his obligation **with it** is imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have** said that he took the *lulav* **with which** he already **fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so;** the *tanna* should have formulated the *halakha* in that manner. **But due to** the fact **that he wants to teach the latter clause** of the *baraita*: One who comes **to sit in the *sukka*, he likewise taught** in **the first clause: To fulfill** his obligation **with it.** This phrase maintains the consistency of the language of the *Tosefta*, even though it is imprecise with regard to the *halakha* of *lulav*.

The *gemara* further notes that the *braita* formulating the *lulav* blessing uses the language *latzet bo* — "to go out with it" — rather than *yatza bo* — "having gone out with it." This might suggest the act has not yet occurred. The *gemara* explains the choice of *latzet* as a function of literary parallelism with the *succah* blessing that follows: the *succah* case involves sitting in the *succah* to fulfill the obligation, using the formulation *leishev ba-succah*, a clearly future-oriented *le* formulation. To maintain parallel structure, the *braita* uses *latzet* for *lulav* as well, even though in fact the *lulav* obligation is already being fulfilled at the moment of lifting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּה לְעַצְמוֹ, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ ... שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה״. נִכְנַס לֵישֵׁב בָּהּ, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ ... אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לֵישֵׁב בַּסּוּכָּה״. וְהִלְכְתָא: ״עַל בִּיעוּר חָמֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 8) **As it teaches in the latter clause** of this *baraita*: **One who erects a *sukka* for himself recites: Blessed are You, God, Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time.** When **he enters to sit in** the *sukka* **he says: Blessed**…**Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and has commanded us to sit in the *sukka*.** In summary, no conclusive proof has been found for either side of this debate. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that one should recite: **Concerning the removal of leaven,** as that expression is referring to the future as well.

### Halakha: Al Biyur

The *gemara* rules: *v'hilchasa al biyur chametz* — the *halacha* follows the view of *al bi'ur chametz*. This ruling, combined with the discussion above, effectively endorses the position that *al* poses no difficulty when used in reference to a future act. Rashi concludes from this that all the distinctions drawn in the preceding discussion — whether the father performing *mila* himself should say *la-mul* rather than *al ha-mila*, whether the one bringing a *korban Pesach* should say *li-shchot* — dissolve. Now that the *gemara* has affirmed *al bi'ur chametz* as the standard and non-problematic formulation, there is no longer a need to craft special formulations for edge cases.

This leaves unresolved, however, a broader question: why do some blessings use *le* and others use *al*? The Ri (cited by Tosafot) attempted to identify a consistent principle governing when each formulation is used, and could not. The Ran devotes a substantial discussion to surveying all *birchat ha-mitzva* formulations and mapping them against the *le* vs. *al* distinction. One significant factor is personal obligation: when the blessing-maker is specifically and personally commanded to perform the act, *le* is natural. When the obligation is more general or the person is not the uniquely mandated agent, *al* fits better. Another possible factor — which does not perfectly account for all cases — is whether the blessing focuses on the act itself or on an end result to be achieved. *Al bi'ur chametz* and *al ha-mila* focus on an outcome: the *chametz* being gone, the child being circumcised. *Leishev ba-succah* and *lehaniach tefillin* focus more directly on the act of performance. This distinction captures something real even if it does not fully resolve every case.

One additional observation from Tosafot concerns the blessing *lehachniso bi-vriso shel Avraham Avinu* — "to bring him into the covenant of Abraham our father" — which uses a *le* formulation. One might think this blessing, being future-oriented (*le-haba mashma*), must be recited before the *mila*. Tosafot argues otherwise: *lehachniso bi-vriso shel Avraham Avinu* is not a *birchat ha-mitzva* on the act of *mila* — it is a *birchat ha-shevach*, a blessing of praise by the father, expressing gratitude that the Jewish people have been commanded this covenant. A *birchat ha-shevach* need not precede the act; it responds to the experience. Therefore this blessing comes after the *mila*, notwithstanding its *le* formulation.

Tosafot further notes that the question of whether *birchat eirusin* precedes or follows the *kiddushin* is a *machloket Rishonim*. The text of that blessing — *asar lanu et ha-arayot v'hitir lanu et ha-arusos* — reads much more as a *birchat ha-shevach* than as a standard *birchat ha-mitzva*. If it is fundamentally a *shevach*, it should follow; if it is a *birchat ha-mitzva*, it should precede. Contemporary practice uniformly recites it before the ring is given, but the underlying question reflects a genuine dispute about the category of that blessing.

## Brachot on Mitzvot

### Bracha Before Mitzva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא, מֵעִיקָּרָא בָּעִינַן לְבָרוֹכֵי, מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת מְבָרֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹבֵר לַעֲשִׂיָּיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 9) The Gemara poses a question: **In any event,** it is clear from the previous discussion that **everyone agrees** that **one is required to recite a blessing prior to** performing a mitzva. **From where do we** derive this principle? It is **as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **all the mitzvot, one recites a blessing over them prior to [*over*] their performance.**

The *gemara* now establishes the universal principle underlying the entire preceding discussion: *kol ha-mitzvot mevarech aleihen over la'asiyastan* — for all *mitzvot*, the blessing is recited prior to performing them. This statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel is itself taken as a given, but the *gemara* pauses to ask how we know that *over la'asiyastan* means *prior to* doing them, rather than during or after.

### Over L'Asiyatan Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״עוֹבֵר״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַקְדּוֹמֵי הוּא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיָּרׇץ אֲחִימַעַץ דֶּרֶךְ הַכִּכָּר וַיַּעֲבֹר אֶת הַכּוּשִׁי״. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְהוּא עָבַר לִפְנֵיהֶם״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״וַיַּעֲבֹר מַלְכָּם לִפְנֵיהֶם וַה׳ בְּרֹאשָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 10) The Gemara asks: **From where may it be inferred that** the word ***over* is the language of priority? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said that the verse said: "And Ahimaaz ran by the way of the plain, and overran [*vaya'avor*] the Cushite"** (II Samuel 18:23), i.e., Ahimaaz overtook the Cushite. **Abaye said:** It is derived **from here: "And he passed [*avar*] before them"** (Genesis 33:3). **And if you wish, say** instead that the proof is **from here: "And their king passed [*vaya'avor*] before them and God at their head"** (Micah 2:13).

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains through *pesukim*. The root *avar* — to pass — is demonstrated from biblical usage to mean going in front of, preceding. The verse recounting *Achimaatz* running by way of the plain (*vayaratz Achima'atz derech ha-kikar*) and passing the *Kushi* — *vaya'avor et ha-Kushi* — shows that *avar* here means he went ahead, he preceded. Abaye adds: *v'hu over lifnehem* — "and he passed before them," meaning he went in front of them. A further source: *vaya'avor malkam lifnehem v'Hashem berosham* — "their king passed before them, and God at their head" — which again means the king went ahead of the people. Through these prooftexts, the *gemara* demonstrates that *over la'asiyastan* means to precede the act of performance.

The underlying logic of this rule, while not stated explicitly by the *gemara*, connects to the nature of *birchat ha-mitzva*. The blessing functions to frame the act — to identify, label, and direct consciousness toward the mitzva about to be performed. As such, it must precede the act, much as framing a picture necessarily precedes placing the picture in the frame. This is in contrast to *birchat ha-shevach*, which responds to experience and therefore comes after, and *birchat ha-nehenin*, which serves a different function altogether.

### Three Blessing Types

The *gemara*'s discussion illuminates a fundamental taxonomy of blessings. *Birchat ha-mitzva* — the blessing on performance of a commandment — always precedes the act. Its function is to frame the performance, and by reciting it, one in effect declares: I am about to perform a mitzva. *Birchat ha-nehenin* — the blessing recited before deriving benefit, such as eating or drinking — also precedes, but for a different reason: one must acknowledge the divine source of one's pleasure before being permitted to enjoy it. *Birchat ha-shevach* — the blessing of praise, such as *asher yatzar* after using the bathroom, or *borei me'orei ha-eish* at *Havdalah* — comes after, because it is a response to an experience, not a preparation for one.

The example of *borei me'orei ha-eish* is instructive. Everyone visually demonstrates the practice by holding their hands to the flame and looking at them before reciting the blessing. But this is in fact the wrong order — or at least, not what the *gemara* requires. The *gemara* states *ad she-ye'osu l'oro* — one must first benefit from the light, and only then recite the blessing. *Borei me'orei ha-eish* is a *birchat ha-shevach* responding to seeing fire; it comes after the experience of benefit, not before. If one is going to perform this act of looking at one's fingernails, one should see the reflection of the light before making the blessing, not after.

### Tevilah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּי רַב אָמְרִי: חוּץ מִן הַטְּבִילָה וְשׁוֹפָר. בִּשְׁלָמָא טְבִילָה — דְּאַכַּתִּי גַּבְרָא לָא חֲזֵי. אֶלָּא שׁוֹפָר מַאי טַעְמָא? וְכִי תֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּילְמָא מִיקַּלְקְלָא תְּקִיעָה. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ שְׁחִיטָה וּמִילָה נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 11) **In the school of Rav they say:** One recites a blessing prior to performing all mitzvot, **except for** the ritual **immersion** after a nocturnal emission **and** the blowing of the ***shofar*.** The Gemara elaborates: **Granted** one does not recite a blessing prior to **immersion, as** this **man** who has not yet immersed **is still unfit** to recite a blessing because he is ritually impure. **However,** with regard to **a *shofar*, what is the reason** that one does not recite a blessing before sounding the *shofar*? **And lest you say** the reason is **due to** a concern **lest the sounding** of the *shofar* emerge **flawed,** and the blessing will be in vain, **if so,** one should not recite a blessing **even** prior to ritual **slaughter and circumcision,** as in those cases too one might fail to perform the action in the requisite manner.

The *gemara* notes an exception to the rule of *bracha* before the act. In the school of Rav they taught: *chutz min ha-tvila v'shofar* — the exceptions are *tvila* (immersion in a *mikvah*) and *shofar*, in which the blessing is recited afterward. The *gemara* immediately accepts *tvila* but challenges *shofar*: *shofar mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for making the blessing after *shofar*? If the reason is that one might blow improperly and then the blessing would have been made in vain — *v'chi teima mishum dilma mekalkel tekiya* — then the same concern applies to *shechita* and *mila*, where one could also err. The *gemara* therefore concludes with Rav Chisda: *chutz min ha-tvila bivad* — the only exception is *tvila*. *Shofar* is removed from the list.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: חוּץ מִן הַטְּבִילָה בִּלְבַד אִיתְּמַר. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: טָבַל וְעָלָה, בַּעֲלִיָּיתוֹ אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ ... אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל הַטְּבִילָה״.
>
> **Translation:** (From Pesachim 7b, line 12) **Rather, Rav Ḥisda said: Except for** prior to **immersion alone was stated,** due to the aforementioned reason. The Gemara adds: **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who **immersed** for ritual purification after a nocturnal emission **and emerged, as he emerges he recites: Blessed**…**Who has made us holy through His mitzvot and commanded us concerning immersion.**

### Tevilah Explanations

The reason *tvila* is an exception is stated as: *d'achti gavra lo chazi* — the person is not yet fit to recite the blessing. Why not? Two explanations are offered.

Rashi explains that the *braita* speaks of a *ger* — a convert — who immerses as part of the conversion process. Before immersion is complete, the *ger* has not yet become part of *Klal Yisrael* and is not yet obligated in *mitzvot*. One who is not yet commanded cannot recite *asher kiddishanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu* — "who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us." The blessing can only be recited after one has emerged from the water and entered the covenant.

Tosafot disagrees and explains the exception differently: it refers to *takanas Ezra*, the ordinance that a man who was a *ba'al keri* — who had experienced a seminal emission — must immerse before being permitted to recite blessings or study Torah. For such a man, he cannot recite the blessing before immersing because he is specifically prohibited from doing so in his current state. The blessing therefore must wait until after he emerges.

The practical question that follows from this debate is significant for women. Neither a *ger* nor a *ba'al keri* account for a woman immersing in a *mikvah* after her period of *niddah*. If the exception for *tvila* is exclusively due to one of those two specific circumstances, then a woman immersing for *tahara* purposes would not fall into the exception and should recite the blessing *before* immersion. Some *Rishonim* hold precisely this. Others hold that once the *gemara* establishes that for *tvila* the blessing is recited after, this becomes the rule regardless of the specific reason, and it applies to all cases of *tvila*. The resulting difference in practice is well known: *Sefardi* women recite the blessing before immersion; *Ashkenazi* women immerse, emerge, recite the blessing, and then immerse again — thereby covering both possibilities while ensuring the blessing is in close proximity to the act of immersion regardless of which timing is correct.
