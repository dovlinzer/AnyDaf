# Yevamot 42b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lo Pelug Principle

### Virgins vs Non-Virgins

The *daf* begins in the middle of a discussion about the three-month waiting period that a woman must observe before remarrying, so that it can be determined whether she is pregnant from her previous husband. The *mishna* states that this requirement applies equally whether the woman is a *betula* — a virgin whose first marriage was her only marriage — or a *be'ula*, a non-virgin, and regardless of whether either the prior or the forthcoming relationship is *erusin* or *nissuin*. The logic behind applying the wait even where pregnancy is clearly impossible is *lo pelug*: the Rabbis did not wish to introduce distinctions that might lead to confusion or abuse. However, the *mishna* then records that both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yossi disagree, holding that if either the prior marriage or the new relationship is merely *erusin* — a stage that has not yet involved marital relations — there is no need to impose the three-month wait.

The *Gemara* opens with a textual question: since virgins are by definition women whose only prior relationship was *erusin*, why does the *mishna* list "virgins" and "those who were only *arusa*" as if they were two separate categories? Similarly, being a *be'ula* is equivalent to having been *nesua*. The *Gemara* answers through *Rav Yehuda*, who explains that the *mishna* is not presenting two independent categories but rather is clarifying a single one: women who were either divorced or widowed whether from *erusin* or *nissuin*. The mention of *erusin* and *nissuin* is therefore explanatory of the earlier distinction between *betulot* and *be'ulot*, not an additional case.

### Divorced/Widowed Categor…

With this reading established, the discussion moves to a practical ruling. Rabbi Eliezer had missed the session in the *beis medrash* and sought out Rabbi Assi to learn what had been said. Rabbi Assi reported that Rabbi Yochanan had ruled: *halacha k'Rabbi Yossi* — the law follows Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Yossi's position, shared in substance with Rabbi Yehuda, is that the three-month wait is necessary only when the woman was *nesua* to her prior husband and is entering *nissuin* with the new one, meaning there was opportunity for marital relations at both ends. If either the prior or the new relationship is only *erusin*, no such wait is required.

### R. Yochanan's Ruling

This ruling immediately raises a methodological problem. Rabbi Yochanan is known for the principle that *halacha k'stam mishna* — the law follows the anonymous, unattributed position in the *Mishna*. But our *mishna* opens with an anonymous *stam* voice demanding the three-month wait in all cases. How, then, can Rabbi Yochanan rule against that *stam*? The *Gemara* responds that the *stam* voice in this case should not be treated as the standard authoritative *stam*, because it is *yachid* — it reflects only a single opinion — as the *Gemara* is about to demonstrate from a *braisa*.

### Non-Pregnant Cases

The *braisa* presents a series of circumstances in which the woman is presumptively not pregnant: she had been habitually at her father's house away from her husband (*redufa levei aviha*); there had been ongoing conflict (*ka'as*) between the spouses, making relations unlikely; the husband had been imprisoned; he was elderly to the point of near-infertility; he was ill; she herself was ill; she miscarried after the husband's death; she was infertile (*akara*); she was postmenopausal (*zekeina*); she was a minor (*ketana*); she was an *ailonis*, a woman constitutionally incapable of bearing children; or for any other reason she could not carry a pregnancy. In all of these cases, Rabbi Meir rules that she still must wait three months. The *lo pelug* of our *mishna* is therefore the position of Rabbi Meir alone.

Rabbi Yehuda — or in some versions, Rabbi Yossi, as both draw distinctions based on the circumstances — permits *erusin* immediately even following a prior *nissuin*, and in these demonstrably non-pregnant cases permits even *nissuin* immediately. This confirms that the anonymous *stam* voice is effectively *Rabbi Meir's* individual opinion, and therefore Rabbi Yochanan was entitled to rule against it in favor of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yossi.

### R. Yochanan Reversal

However, Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba then reports that Rabbi Yochanan subsequently reversed himself — *chazru bo Rabbi Yochanan* — and returned to demanding the three-month wait in all cases without distinction. Rav Yosef clarifies that the reversal was prompted by the *braisa* of the *Kerem b'Yavneh*, the tradition preserved from the sages who gathered at Yavneh following the destruction of the Temple. Rabbi Yishmael, the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, testified: *shamati mipi chachamim b'Kerem b'Yavneh* — "I heard from the sages in the *Kerem b'Yavneh* that all women must wait three months." No distinctions are made, regardless of whether pregnancy was possible. This *Kerem b'Yavneh* tradition evidently carried sufficient weight to cause Rabbi Yochanan to reverse his earlier ruling.

## Stam vs Machloket

### Contradiction Raised

Rabbi Yirmiya then sends Rabbi Zerika to raise the following contradiction before Rabbi Abbahu: on the one hand, Rabbi Yochanan ruled *halacha k'Rabbi Yossi*; on the other, Rabbi Yochanan is the very authority behind the principle of *halacha k'stam mishna*. And our *mishna* states explicitly, in anonymous voice, that *kol hanashim lo yinaseu v'lo yisarsu* — all women may neither marry nor become betrothed — until the three-month waiting period has elapsed, whether they are virgins or non-virgins.

Rabbi Abbahu's response is pointed: the one who posed this question *lo chash l'kimchei* — is not careful about the quality of his thinking. This is not a standard *stam mishna*. A *stam mishna* that obligates us is one where the anonymous position stands uncontested within the *mishna* itself. Here, the *mishna* immediately follows the *stam* with a recorded debate — Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yossi disagree. This configuration is called *stam v'achar kach machlokes*, and in such a case, *ein halacha k'stam* — we are not obligated to rule according to the *stam*. One may note that this does not necessarily mean we rule *against* the *stam*, only that we are not bound by it.

### Stam Then Machloket

The *Gemara* then articulates the general principle through the name of Rav Papa, with some attributing it to Rabbi Yochanan himself: *machlokes v'achar kach stam — halacha k'stam*. If the debate appears first in the *Mishna* and the anonymous position follows it, we rule according to the *stam*. But *stam v'achar kach machlokes — ein halacha k'stam*. When the *stam* comes first and the debate follows, we do not rule according to the *stam*.

This hermeneutical framework has broad applications. It is worth noting that comparable interpretive questions arise in the *Shulchan Aruch* as well — when the *Shulchan Aruch* states a ruling and then records *v'yeish omrim*, there is an entire developed methodology for determining which view carries practical weight depending on the order and structure of the presentation.

One might observe that, as the *Gemara* already noted, the *stam* of our *mishna* represents the opinion of Rabbi Meir — since the anonymous voice in the *Mishna* is generally attributed to Rabbi Meir. This makes it no surprise that it stands in tension with the views of other *tanna'im*. That said, even the principle of *stam mishna* does not typically concern itself with who authored the *stam* but with whether it is formally presented as uncontested — and here it clearly is not.

### R. Abbahu's Cases

Rabbi Abbahu expands the discussion to enumerate four configurations. His attendant Rabbi Nachum posed a series of questions while accompanying him, and Rabbi Abbahu addressed each in turn. First: *machlokes v'achar stam* — *halacha k'stam*, we follow the *stam*. Second: *stam v'achar machlokes* — *ein halacha k'stam*, we do not follow the *stam*. Third: *stam b'masnisin, machlokes b'braisa* — the *Mishna* presents it anonymously but a *braisa* records it as a debate. Rabbi Abbahu rules *halacha k'stam*: since the anonymous position stands uncontested in the *Mishna*, the *braisa's* recording of a debate does not dislodge it. Fourth: *machlokes b'masnisin, stam b'braisa* — the *Mishna* records a debate, but a *braisa* presents the matter anonymously. Here Rabbi Abbahu replies: *v'chi Rebbi lo shna'ah, Rebbi Chiyya minalo* — if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did not include this as a *stam* in the *Mishna*, on what authority does Rabbi Chiyya present it as one? The *Mishna* carries ultimate authority, and the *braisa's* anonymous formulation does not override the *Mishna's* explicit recording of a dispute.

The overall logic of these cases is intuitive: the *stam* carries authority when it represents the formal, uncontested position in the highest-level source. Any configuration that diminishes either the formality or the source level weakens its claim to be the definitive ruling.

### Combs Mishna

At this point Rabbi Abbahu's questioner challenges the principle with a *mishna* that is taught as *stam* yet, as everyone knows, is not followed in practice. The *mishna* in question concerns combs used for processing fiber. A flax comb (*masreik shel pishtan*) whose teeth have been removed: if two teeth remain adjacent to one another, the implement is *tamei* because it retains functional utility; if only one tooth remains, it is *tahor* since a single tooth is useless for combing. As for the individual teeth that break off one by one, they are themselves *tamei* because a single tooth can serve as a toothpick. Now regarding a wool comb (*shel tzemer*): if every other tooth has been removed such that no two remain adjacent, the comb is *tahor*. If three teeth remain together in one location, it is *tamei*. Further, if the outer tooth is one of a cluster of three, it is *tahor* — the outer teeth being less functionally critical. If two adjacent teeth broke off still connected at their base, they can serve as tweezers and are *tamei*. If one tooth broke off and was modified to serve as a lamp-wick cleaner or for stretching material, it is *tamei*.

This final ruling — that a single modified tooth is *tamei* — is presented in the *Mishna* as a *stam*. Yet Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are known to both declare *zu einah mishna* — this ruling is not authoritative and we do not decide the law accordingly.

The question is pressing: here is a *stam mishna* that established authorities explicitly set aside. How can one maintain that *halacha k'stam mishna* as a binding principle?

### Combs Resolution

Rabbi Abbahu answers: *bar minei d'hi* — put that case aside entirely. It is a known exception, and both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish explicitly identified it as one. The *Gemara* then proceeds to explain *why* this *mishna* is treated as an exception, and the answer turns on internal problems within the *mishna* itself. Rav Huna bar Minyumi in the name of Rav Idi reports that the *reisha* contradicts the *seifa*: the opening of the *mishna* dealing with the wool comb states that when teeth are missing with a gap between remaining ones (*achas mi-beino*), the comb is *tahor*, implying that two adjacent teeth (*shtayim b'makom echad*) would render it *tamei* — yet the later clause specifies *three* together (*shalosh tamei*), implying that two alone would not suffice. The *Gemara* raises several possible resolutions of this apparent internal contradiction — perhaps the cases involve outer versus inner teeth, or teeth that break off with the base of the comb attached versus those that break off cleanly, or thick teeth versus thin teeth — and concludes that none of these distinctions are entirely forced. The *mishna* is not demonstrably self-contradictory.

The final answer is therefore textual rather than logical: those who are precise about the *girsa* — the exact text — note that their manuscripts conclude the *mishna* with the phrase *zu divrei Rabbi Shimon*, identifying the final ruling as specifically the opinion of Rabbi Shimon rather than an anonymous *stam*. There is therefore no *stam* ruling here to begin with, and the challenge to the principle dissolves. As the *Gemara* acknowledges, this was a rather extraordinary digression — the example chosen to challenge the principle of *stam mishna* turns out to involve the textual apparatus of combs, a matter of limited practical consequence. Whether the ruling about the modified tooth affects any of the other cases in the *mishna* is left unexplored.

## Three Months & Erusin

### Day Ninety Question

The *Gemara* returns to the three-month waiting period and records a series of practical rulings. Rabbi Yoshiyah bar Avin sent a ruling that *me'arsin toch gimel* — one may perform *erusin* within the three months — following the lenient view of Rabbi Yehuda that *erusin* is not problematic because it does not yet place the woman in a context of marital relations. He adds that this was also the practice and teaching of Rabbi Elazar. Additionally, a tradition in the name of Rabbi Chanina HaGadol holds that *rubo shel rishon, v'rubo shel shlishi, v'emtza'i v'hashalem* — the majority of the first month, the majority of the third month, and the entirety of the middle month would be sufficient. This formulation is striking, as it implies that something less than three complete months may suffice — perhaps reflecting a view that seven full months are required to establish paternity, in which case slightly more than two months of waiting would be adequate. Neither *Rashi* nor *Tosafot* address this point directly.

*Tosafot* suggests that this leniency in counting applies only when the waiting woman is an *arusa*, not a *nesua*. On that reading, the ruling is less radical: it is not that such a woman need not wait at all, only that the three months may be calculated with some flexibility.

*Ameimar* went further and permitted performing *erusin* on day ninety itself, without requiring the couple to wait until day ninety-one. Rav Ashi challenges this from a ruling attributed to both Rava and Shmuel, who state that the three-month wait requires a full three months, exclusive of the day of the husband's death and exclusive of the day of the *erusin* itself — meaning the *erusin* must take place on day ninety-one at the earliest.

### Nursing Women Case

The *Gemara* resolves this challenge by clarifying that the ruling of Rava and Shmuel was not stated in the context of the three-month wait at all. It was stated regarding the waiting period before remarriage for a woman who is nursing her infant — *l'inyan meneket*. Rava and Shmuel had said that a nursing mother must wait twenty-four months before remarrying, exclusive of the day the child was born and exclusive of the day of the *erusin*, placing the *erusin* on the day after the twenty-four months conclude. The version cited by Rav Ashi had confused the two contexts: it was not three months and day ninety-one, but twenty-four months and the day following.

A subsequent case reinforces this: there is a report that a man arranged a *seudas erusin* on day ninety and Rava compelled him to cancel the feast and postpone. The *Gemara* clarifies that this too involved *seudas nissuin*, not *seudas erusin* — and for *nissuin*, the stricter count indeed applies.

### Lo Pelug Conclusion

The final ruling (*hilchasa*) is that a nursing woman must wait twenty-four months, exclusive of the day of birth and the day of *erusin*. For the three-month wait applicable to all other remarrying women, it is likewise exclusive of the day of death and exclusive of the day of *erusin*. The more significant conclusion, however, is substantive: the *halacha* follows *lo pelug*, and even *erusin* requires the full three-month wait. This represents Rabbi Yochanan's final position following his reversal based on the *Kerem b'Yavneh* tradition.

This conclusion raises a broader question about the applicability of *lo pelug* in the contemporary context. The *lo pelug* rationale does not express a genuine belief that a postmenopausal woman or a minor might be pregnant. Rather, it reflects a concern that if we begin introducing distinctions, borderline cases will escape the necessary scrutiny. But if that is the logic, then the underlying concern — which is determining pregnancy status — could in principle be satisfied by a modern pregnancy test. On this analysis, *lo pelug* requires *some* form of determination in all cases; it does not necessarily require that the determination take the form of waiting three months. That said, whether pregnancy tests may substitute for the three-month wait is a *machlokes haposkim*, and on the strict reading, one would require even an elderly woman to demonstrate non-pregnancy before proceeding.

## Widow's Aveilus Issue

### R. Yossi Exception

The *mishna* records that Rabbi Yossi permits women to perform *erusin* immediately — *chutz min ha'almanah mipnei ha'eibul* — except for a widow who is in *aveilus*. The logic is straightforward: if the woman was *nesua* to her first husband and he died, she is in *aveilus* for the first month. Even though the new relationship involves only *erusin*, and therefore poses no concern about determining paternity, she is still mourning the prior husband and may not yet enter even a betrothal relationship.

The background assumption is that a woman who was *nesua* to her first husband and is now entering *erusin* with a new partner does not need the three-month wait — because the new relationship is only *erusin*, which involves no marital relations. Yet she cannot proceed even with this limited step during the first month, because she is in personal *aveilus*.

### Rav Chisda Challenge

Rav Chisda challenges this on *kal v'chomer* grounds. He argues: during the period when it is forbidden to launder one's garments — that is, during the first week of personal *aveilus*, when the laws of mourning are most severe — it is still permitted to perform *erusin*. How much more so during the subsequent weeks of the month, when laundering is again permitted, should *erusin* certainly be allowed? If the severity of the prohibition does not extend to blocking *erusin* even at its most stringent point, how can Rabbi Yossi claim there is any prohibition at all?

### Tisha B'Av Precedent

The *kal v'chomer* draws on a *Mishna* and *braisa* relating to the nine-day period preceding *Tisha B'Av*. The *Mishna* states that during the week in which *Tisha B'Av* falls (*shavua she'chal bo Tisha B'Av*), it is forbidden to take a haircut or launder clothing. A *braisa* records that during the period before this week, the people limit business dealings, building, and planting — yet they may still perform *kiddushin*, though not *nissuin*, and may not hold a *seudas erusin*. The *Gemara* understands *Shmuel's* principle — that one may perform *kiddushin* even close to *Tisha B'Av* lest another man get there first (*shema yekadmenu acher*) — as the basis for this leniency.

The preliminary *braisa* seems to speak of the period before the *shavua she'chal bo*, meaning even before the most restricted week. The *Gemara* considers whether the permission to perform *kiddushin* extends into the *shavua she'chal bo* itself, when laundering is forbidden. Rava constructs a *kal v'chomer*: if during the nine days when business dealings are restricted one may still perform *kiddushin*, how much more so during the less restricted days of personal *aveilus* when business dealings are permitted should *kiddushin* be allowed?

### Reinterpreting R. Yossi

In order to accommodate the evident logic that *erusin* should not be prohibited during personal *aveilus*, the *Gemara* undertakes a significant reinterpretation of Rabbi Yossi's ruling. Rather than reading him as saying *kol hanashim yisarsu chutz min ha'almanah* — all women may perform *erusin* except for the widow — the *Gemara* proposes reading: *kol hanashim yinaseu* — all women may perform *nissuin*. What Rabbi Yossi objects to is not *erusin* during *aveilus*, but *nissuin* during *aveilus*. *Erusin*, he agrees, is unproblematic.

On this revised reading, the structure of the case changes entirely. The prior relationship was *erusin* — which is why there is no concern about determining pregnancy — and the new step being contemplated is *nissuin*. Rabbi Yossi says: ordinarily this *nissuin* is fine (since the prior stage was only *erusin*, there is no issue of confusing lineage), but if the woman is an *almanah*, her first month of *aveilus* blocks even *nissuin*.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: but if Rabbi Yossi is talking about a woman whose prior relationship was *erusin*, and she is now proceeding to *nissuin* — why should there be any concern about determining pregnancy at all? Since the prior stage was *erusin*, there were no marital relations, and she cannot be pregnant. The *Gemara* answers: *lais lei* — Rabbi Yossi is simply not concerned about determining pregnancy in this case. He does not hold the position of *lo pelug* in this context.

Alternatively, the *Gemara* considers: perhaps Rabbi Yossi does accept the concern about pregnancy but is dealing with a different configuration — specifically, women who were previously *arusot* and are now divorced (*arusot gerushot*) and wish to proceed to *nissuin*. In this reading, there is no *aveilus* concern because a *gerusha* is not a widow; the exception is only an *almanah* — a woman who was previously *arusa* and whose *arus* died. In that case, one might ask: does such a woman have *aveilus* at all?

The *Gemara* then asks: if Rabbi Yossi is now only talking about an *arusa* who became an *almanah* proceeding to *nissuin* — what is the difference between his position and Rabbi Yehuda's? The *Gemara* answers: the distinction lies in the case of a *nesua* seeking to perform *erusin*. Rabbi Yehuda permits *erusin* as well — as long as *one* of the two stages is *erusin*, the waiting period is unnecessary. Rabbi Yossi, on the revised reading, addresses only the case where the *first* stage is *erusin* and the *second* step is *nissuin*; he does not explicitly permit *erusin* for a woman who was already *nesua*.

### Almanah Question

This generates the next question. According to the revised reading of Rabbi Yossi — where he is discussing an *arusa* who is an *almanah* proceeding to *nissuin* — does such a woman even have *aveilus*? The *Gemara* cites a *braisa* taught by Rabbi Chiyya bar Ami: a man's wife who is only his *arusa* — they have not yet begun living together — *lo onen*, he is not an *onen* for her if she dies; *v'lo mitame lah*, if he is a *kohen* he does not become *tamei* on her account; *v'chen hi*, and similarly she is not an *onenes* for him and does not defile herself for him; *mes hu — govah kesuvashah*, if he dies she is still entitled to collect her *kesuvah*, since the basic obligation of the *kesuvah* attaches from the time of *erusin* even though the ongoing marital obligations do not yet apply; *meitah — eino yorshah*, but if she dies he does not inherit her. The clear implication of this *braisa* is that there is no *aveilus* obligation for an *arusa*.

This brings the discussion full circle. Everyone agrees there is no *aveilus* for an *arusa* relationship. If the deceased was the woman's husband in a full *nissuin* relationship, then there is *aveilus*, but then in order to remove the three-month concern, the *new* relationship must be only *erusin* — and we are back to the original question: why should *erusin* be prohibited during *aveilus*?

The *Gemara* concludes that this question reflects a genuine *machlokes tanna'im*. A *braisa* addressing the nine-day period states: from *Rosh Chodesh Av* until *Tisha B'Av*, people limit business dealings, building, planting, *erusin*, and *nissuin*. This represents a *tanna* who holds that *erusin* is indeed restricted during mourning-adjacent periods. The same *braisa* then states that during the *shavua she'chal bo Tisha B'Av* it is forbidden to launder and to take a haircut, with one opinion extending these restrictions to the entire month. Rabbi Yossi may be following this stricter *tanna* who restricts *erusin*.

### New vs Old Aveilus

Rav Ashi raises one final challenge. How do we know that the *braisa*'s prohibition of *le'ares* during the nine days refers to the actual act of *kiddushin* rather than merely to holding a *seudas erusin*? The *Gemara* answers: the same question could be raised regarding *nissuin* — and we know that the prohibition on *nissuin* during this period refers to the act itself, not merely to the feast. The distinction is: *nissuin* without a feast still constitutes a full marriage and carries its own inherent *simcha*; but *erusin* without a feast carries no *simcha* at all. Therefore, if there is any prohibition of *erusin* in such a context, it must apply to the act itself.

This brings us back to the starting point without resolution through the *Tisha B'Av* material alone. The final answer given by Rav Ashi is conceptual: *shani aveilus chadasha me'aveilus yeshana* — a fresh personal *aveilus* is categorically different from the ancient, commemorative mourning of *Tisha B'Av*. And *shani aveilus derabbim me'aveilus deyachid* — the communal mourning of *Tisha B'Av* is different from the individual's personal *aveilus*. Whatever evidence one brings from the *Tisha B'Av* period — whether permissive or restrictive — is not directly transferable to personal *aveilus*. Rabbi Yossi is therefore entirely entitled to hold that in a fresh, personal *aveilus*, even the act of *kiddushin* is prohibited, regardless of what one rules about the analogous period before *Tisha B'Av*. And if Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, the dispute is rooted in precisely this question: whether the communal, commemorative mourning of *Tisha B'Av* is strict enough to serve as a relevant precedent for individual personal *aveilus*. On the simple reading, the *mishna* is restored: Rabbi Yossi prohibits *erusin* for a widow during the month of *aveilus*, and the permissive cases discussed throughout the *sugya* do not override this.

## Next Mishna Preview

### Four Brothers Case

The *daf* concludes with a preview of the next *mishna*. Four brothers are married to four women, and all four brothers die, leaving a fifth surviving brother. The *mishna* states that if the eldest brother wishes to perform *yibum* with all four widows, the prerogative is his — the responsibility for *yibum* devolves primarily upon the eldest, and he may choose to marry all four.

### Two Brothers Case

The *mishna* then turns to a man who was married to two women and dies. Performing either *bi'ah* or *chalitzah* with one of them automatically exempts the other — as has been established from the beginning of the tractate.

### Kasher/Pasul Mix

Finally, the *mishna* addresses the case where of the two wives, one is *kashera* — eligible for marriage to a *kohen* — and one is *pesulah*, already disqualified by virtue of a prior divorce. If the surviving brother intends to perform *chalitzah*, he should do so with the *pesulah*, so as not to add a further disqualification to the *kashera*. If he intends to perform *yibum*, he should do *yibum* with the *kashera*. Whether this means he performs *yibum* exclusively with the *kashera*, or whether after performing *yibum* with the *kashera* he may also marry the *pesulah*, will be taken up in the *Gemara*.
