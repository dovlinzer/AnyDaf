# Yevamot 112b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Deaf & Hearing

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ שֶׁנָּשָׂא פִּקַּחַת וּפִקֵּחַ שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֵרֶשֶׁת, אִם רָצָה לְהוֹצִיא — יוֹצִיא, וְאִם רָצָה לְקַיֵּים — יְקַיֵּים. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהוּא כּוֹנֵס בִּרְמִיזָה — כָּךְ הוּא מוֹצִיא בִּרְמִיזָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a deaf-mute who married** a halakhically **competent woman, and** a halakhically **competent man who married a deaf-mute: If** either man **wants** to divorce his wife, **he may divorce** her, **and if he wants to maintain** her as his wife, **he may maintain** her. The reason why a deaf-mute man can divorce his wife is that **just as he marries** her **by intimation,** i.e., his marriage is not performed by explicit speech, as deaf-mutes rely on gestures, **so too, he divorces** her **by intimation.**

The present *daf* opens a new *perek* centered on the *cheresh* — the deaf-mute — and his or her status in the laws of marriage and divorce. The *mishnah* that occupies this *amud* has already been encountered in fragments throughout the preceding chapters; accordingly, the treatment here proceeds with relative dispatch, building on ground already covered while sharpening the principles that will drive the *gemara's* subsequent analysis.

### Cheresh & Pikeach

The *mishnah* opens with two cases: *cheresh she-nasa pikeachet* — a deaf man who married a hearing woman — and *pikeach she-nasa chereshet* — a hearing man who married a deaf woman. In both instances the *mishnah* rules *im ratzah le-hotzi, yotzi; ve-im ratzah le-kayem, yekayem*: if he wishes to divorce, he may do so, and if he wishes to maintain the marriage, he may do that as well.

The underlying principle is that where one party to a marriage is deaf, there is no *kiddushin d'Oraita*. Since a *cheresh* is not classified as a *bar da'at* — a legally capable agent — the *kiddushin* is purely rabbinic. But precisely because the marriage was rabbinic, the Rabbis could equally authorize a rabbinic divorce. The *mishnah* adds: *kesheim she-hu kones bi-remizah, kach hu motzi bi-remizah* — the same way he contracted the marriage through gesturing, he may also dissolve it through gesturing. Whether or not the full verbal and written form of a *get* can be executed by a deaf man, the expression of will through gesture suffices within the rabbinic framework that established the marriage in the first place.

### Divorce Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּקֵּחַ שֶׁנָּשָׂא פִּקַּחַת וְנִתְחָרְשָׁה, אִם רָצָה — יוֹצִיא, וְאִם רָצָה — יְקַיֵּים. נִשְׁתַּטֵּית — לֹא יוֹצִיא. נִתְחָרֵשׁ הוּא אוֹ נִשְׁתַּטָּה — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיאָהּ עוֹלָמִית.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, in the case of a halakhically **competent man who married a** halakhically **competent woman, and she** later **became a deaf-mute: If he wants** to divorce his wife, **he may divorce** her, as a wife does not have to have intellectual capacity to receive a bill of divorce, **and if he wants** to maintain her as his wife, **he may maintain** her. **If she became an imbecile, he may not divorce** her, i.e., a bill of divorce is ineffective in this case. If **he became a deaf-mute or an imbecile** after they were married, **he may never divorce her,** as he does not have the legal competence to give a bill of divorce.

The *mishnah* then distinguishes two further situations. First: *pikeach she-nasa pikeachet ve-nischershah* — a hearing man married a hearing woman, and she subsequently became deaf. This is *kiddushin d'Oraita*, contracted when both parties possessed full legal capacity. The rule is that *im ratzah yotzi ve-im ratzah yekayem* — he may still divorce her if he chooses, because the *get* is a unilateral act of the husband, and as long as he is hearing and retains his *da'at*, his act of divorcing is valid regardless of her diminished status. The *gemara* will explain explicitly that the woman need not be a *bar da'at* for the divorce to take effect, since she is the passive recipient of the *get*, not its author.

However, *nishtateit lo yotzi* — if she became mentally ill (*shotah*) rather than merely deaf, he may not divorce her. The Rabbis instituted this protection to prevent her from being abandoned in a state of vulnerability, with no capacity to protect herself.

Conversely, *nitcharesh hu o nishtateh* — if the husband himself became deaf or mentally ill — then *eino motzi olamit*, he can never divorce her. He is the active party who must give the *get*, and since he is no longer a *bar da'at*, he lacks the legal capacity to do so, even if he is physically capable of the act.

## Rabbinic Kiddushin

### Ben Nuri Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְחָרְשָׁה יוֹצְאָה, וְהָאִישׁ שֶׁנִּתְחָרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: For what** reason is the *halakha* that in the case of **the woman who becomes a deaf-mute,** her husband **may divorce her, but** in the case of **the man who becomes a deaf-mute, he may not divorce** his wife?

At this point Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri raises a pointed question: *mipnei mah ha-ishah she-nischershah yotzah, ve-ha-ish she-nitcharesh eino motzi?* Why, when the *woman* becomes deaf after a biblical marriage, may she be divorced, yet when the *man* becomes deaf, he cannot give a *get*?

### Man vs Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה הָאִישׁ הַמְגָרֵשׁ לָאִשָּׁה הַמִּתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה יוֹצְאָה לִרְצוֹנָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִרְצוֹנָהּ, וְהָאִישׁ אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא אֶלָּא לִרְצוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: The man who divorces** his wife **is not similar to the woman who is divorced, as the woman is divorced** whether **she is willing or unwilling.** Since the woman's consent is not required, she may be divorced even if she is a deaf-mute. **And,** conversely, **the man divorces** his wife **only willingly,** and therefore the bill of divorce of a deaf-mute, who is not legally competent, is ineffective.

The response cuts to the structural asymmetry of Jewish divorce law: *eino domeh ha-ish ha-megaresh le-ishah ha-mitgareshet*. The man who divorces is not comparable to the woman who is divorced. *She-ishah yotzah li-rtzionah ve-she-lo li-rtzionah* — a woman is divorced with or without her consent. She is not the legal actor in the transaction; she need not be a *bar da'at*. The husband, however, *eino motzi ela li-rtzono* — acts only through his own volitional, legally cognizant will. He must therefore be a *bar da'at*, and if he has lost that status, the *get* cannot issue from him.

### Gudgada Test.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ שֶׁהִיא יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף זוֹ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgada testified with regard to a female deaf-mute whose father married her off** when she was a minor, which means her marriage was valid by Torah law, **that she can be divorced with a bill of divorce** even when she matures and is no longer under her father's authority, despite the fact that she is not legally competent. **They said to him: This** woman, **too,** has **a similar** status. In other words, a woman who possessed all her faculties and later became a deaf-mute is comparable to a minor whose marriage was valid by Torah law and later, when she matured and was no longer under the authority of her father, received a bill of divorce. Both of these women can receive a bill of divorce, in accordance with the principle stated in the previous paragraph.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada then offers a formal testimony: *he'id al ha-chereshet she-hisi'ah aviha she-hi yotzah be-get* — that a deaf-mute woman whose father arranged her marriage while she was a minor may be divorced by *get*. This is a case of *kiddushin d'Oraita* — the father's act of marriage on behalf of his minor daughter is biblically valid — yet the woman is deaf, and the husband can still divorce her. The Rabbis respond to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri that this is precisely analogous to the case of a hearing woman who becomes deaf: *af zo ke-yotzei bah*, our case is just like it. *Tosafot* notes that the Rabbis actually summoned Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada to the *beis midrash* specifically in order to bring this proof.

## Two Families

### Deaf Bros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִין חֵרְשִׁין נְשׂוּאִין לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת, אוֹ לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת פִּקְּחוֹת, אוֹ לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת אַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת וְאַחַת פִּקַּחַת. אוֹ שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת נְשׂוּאוֹת לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין פִּקְחִין, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין חֵרְשִׁין, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם. וְאִם הָיוּ נׇכְרִיּוֹת — יִכְנוֹסוּ, וְאִם רָצוּ לְהוֹצִיא — יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In a case where there were **two deaf-mute brothers married to two deaf-mute sisters or to two** halakhically **competent sisters, or to two sisters, one** of whom was **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent; or** in a case where there were **two deaf-mute sisters married to two** halakhically **competent brothers or to two deaf-mute brothers or to two brothers, one** of whom was **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent,** all **these** women **are exempt from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage.** Each of them is forbidden to her *yavam* because he is married to her sister. **And if they were unrelated women,** i.e., the women are not sisters, the men **may marry** them in levirate marriage, **and if they want to divorce** them later, **they may divorce** them.

The *mishnah* now proceeds to the intricate permutations of two brothers married to two sisters where one or more parties are deaf. The governing principle is introduced at the outset: when two deaf brothers are married to two sisters — whether those sisters are both hearing, both deaf, or one of each — all of the marriages are rabbinic, since the deaf brothers render each union merely *d'rabbanan*. Accordingly, *harei eilu peturot min ha-chalitzah u-min ha-yibum* — if one brother dies, the surviving widow is exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibum*, because the operative prohibition is *achot ishah* (wife's sister), and since both marriages function on the same rabbinic plane, neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah* applies.

However, *im hayu nochriyot yichnasu* — if the women were unrelated to each other, the surviving brother could perform *yibum*, and *im ratzu le-hotzi, yotziu* — he could afterward give a *get* if he wished, since the whole framework is rabbinic. What cannot occur in any of these cases is *chalitzah*, because *chalitzah* requires full legal agency on both sides, and at minimum one party is always a *cheresh*.

### Mixed Pairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים, אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ, נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת פִּקְּחוֹת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל הַפִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל הַפִּקַּחַת — תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **two brothers, one** of whom is a **deaf-mute and** other **one** halakhically **competent,** were **married to two** halakhically **competent sisters,** and **the deaf-mute married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do?** His brother's wife is **released** without levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, **due to** the prohibition with regard to **a wife's sister.**

The *mishnah* turns to the case of *shenei achim, echad cheresh ve-echad pikeach, nesuim li-shtei achayot pikechot* — one deaf brother and one hearing brother, each married to one of two hearing sisters. The two marriages therefore occupy different legal planes: the hearing brother has a *kiddushin d'Oraita*, the deaf brother only a *kiddushin d'rabbanan*.

If the deaf brother dies (*met cheresh ba'al ha-pikachet*), what must the hearing brother do? His *d'Oraita* marriage makes the widow his wife's sister in the fullest biblical sense; *teitzei mishum achot ishah* — she simply goes free on account of the prohibition of a wife's sister. Straightforward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל הַפִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — מוֹצִיא אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו אֲסוּרָה לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** If the halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the deaf-mute** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do? He divorces his wife with a bill of divorce,** as his wife's sister came before him for levirate marriage by Torah law, and the legal status of her marriage and her levirate marriage is higher than his own marriage, which applies only by rabbinic law. **And his brother's wife is forbidden** to him **forever,** and there is no remedy for her. He cannot marry her, as by rabbinic law she is the sister of his ex-wife, nor can he exempt her by means of *ḥalitza*, as he is a deaf-mute.

But if the hearing brother dies (*met pikeach ba'al ha-pikachet*), matters become complicated. The deaf brother is now the surviving *yavam*. His brother's widow is his wife's sister, but his own marriage to his wife is only rabbinic. He cannot perform *yibum* — that would be sexual relations with his ex-wife's sister even after he divorces her. He cannot perform *chalitzah* — he is a *cheresh* and lacks the legal capacity to do so. The *mishnah* rules: *motzi ishto be-get ve-eshet achiv asurah lo le-olam* — he must divorce his wife, and his brother's wife is forbidden to him forever. The deaf brother is entirely stuck.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים פִּקְחִין נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, אַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת וְאַחַת פִּקַּחַת. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **two** halakhically **competent brothers** were **married to two sisters, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent, and the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do?** The deaf-mute sister is **released due to** the prohibition with regard to **a wife's sister.** If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute** sister **do? He divorces his wife with a bill of divorce,** as the halakhically competent sister came before him for levirate marriage, and the status of her levirate bond is higher than the status of his marriage to his wife, a deaf-mute. **And he** releases **his brother's wife,** who is not a deaf-mute, **by means of *ḥalitza*,** as they are both legally competent and can therefore perform *ḥalitza*.

The parallel permutation — two hearing brothers, one married to a deaf woman and one to a hearing woman — yields a symmetric analysis. When the hearing brother married to the hearing woman dies (*met pikeach ba'al ha-pikachet*), the surviving hearing brother who was married to the deaf woman faces his wife's sister. His own marriage is only rabbinic (because his wife was deaf), so *teitzei mishum achot ishah* does not resolve the situation cleanly by itself; he must divorce his wife. But because *he* is hearing, he can perform *chalitzah* with his brother's widow — *ve-eshet achiv be-chalitzah*. This stands in contrast to the previous case, where the deaf brother was incapable of performing *chalitzah* at all.

### Stuck Deaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים, אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ, נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, אַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת וְאַחַת פִּקַּחַת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת — מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו אֲסוּרָה לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** If **two brothers, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent,** were **married to two sisters, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent, and the deaf-mute** brother who was **married to the deaf-mute woman died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman do?** The deaf-mute woman is **released due to** the prohibition with regard to **a wife's sister.** If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman died, what should the deaf-mute** brother **married to the deaf-mute woman do? He divorces his wife with a bill of divorce,** which is as valid as their original marriage. **And his brother's wife is forbidden** to him **forever.** There is no remedy for her, as he may not consummate levirate marriage with her because she is the sister of his ex-wife by rabbinic law, and he cannot perform *chalitzah* with her either, as he is a deaf-mute.

The *mishnah* then presents the case where both the surviving brother and the woman involved are deaf: *shenei achim, echad cheresh ve-echad pikeach, nesuim li-shtei achayot, achat chereshet ve-achat pikachet*, where the deaf man was married to the deaf woman. If the hearing brother and hearing wife's marriage ends and the deaf man with the deaf wife survives, the result once again is *motzi ishto be-get ve-eshet achiv asurah lo le-olam*. He must divorce his wife because living with her would render him bound to his deceased brother's wife as *achot zekukato*. Yet he cannot perform *chalitzah* because he is a *cheresh*, and he cannot perform *yibum* because that would mean consummating a union with his now ex-wife's sister. She is permanently forbidden to him.

This series of permutations yields an important contrasting principle: when a *cheresh* enters into *kiddushin* of his own accord, the marriage is rabbinic and he can likewise be divorced rabbinically. But when a *cheresh* falls into a *yibum* obligation — as will be shown — the *yibum* itself is a biblical act, since it carries over the *kiddushin* of the deceased hearing brother. In that case, the *cheresh* who performs *yibum* cannot subsequently give a *get*, because the bond is now *d'Oraita* and he is not a *bar da'at* who can dissolve it.

## Yibum Cases

### Deaf Yibum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים, אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ, נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת פִּקְּחוֹת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — אוֹ חוֹלֵץ אוֹ מְיַיבֵּם. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — כּוֹנֵס וְאֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** If **two brothers, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent,** were **married to two unrelated,** halakhically **competent women, and the deaf-mute married to the** halakhically **competent woman died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman do?** He **either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.** If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman died, what should the deaf-mute** brother **married to the** other halakhically **competent woman do?** He cannot perform *ḥalitza* with her, as he is a deaf-mute. Rather, **he marries** her, **and he may never divorce** her, as sexual intercourse between a *yavam* and his *yevama* creates a valid marriage that cannot be broken by the bill of divorce of a deaf-mute.

Having completed the cases involving two sisters, the *mishnah* turns to two brothers married to two unrelated women, where one brother is deaf and one is hearing. The critical case: *met pikeach ba'al ha-pikachet* — the hearing brother dies. What must the deaf brother do? His brother was married to this woman by *kiddushin d'Oraita*, and there is accordingly a biblical *mitzvah* of *yibum*. The women are unrelated, so there is no prohibition of *achot ishah*. One might think the deaf brother has free choice. But: *kones ve-eino motzi le-olam*. He can only perform *yibum*, and once he has done so, he can never give a *get*. He cannot perform *chalitzah* because he is a *cheresh*. His only avenue is *yibum*. And since the *yibum* is a biblical act that creates a *d'Oraita* bond — even though the man performing it is a *cheresh* — he cannot subsequently dissolve it, because he lacks the *da'at* to author a valid *get*. The woman, equally, has no recourse to *chalitzah*. The situation is: *yibum* and no divorce, ever.

This stands in deliberate contrast to the first *mishnah* in the *perek*: when a *cheresh* marries, the marriage is rabbinic and so is the divorce. When a *cheresh* performs *yibum* on a *d'Oraita* bond, the *yibum* is binding *d'Oraita* and no divorce is possible.

### Hear/Deaf Yib.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים פִּקְחִין נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת, אַחַת פִּקַּחַת וְאַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — כּוֹנֵס, וְאִם רָצָה לְהוֹצִיא — יוֹצִיא. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת — אוֹ חוֹלֵץ אוֹ מְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** If **two** halakhically **competent brothers** were **married to two unrelated women, one** of whom is halakhically **competent and** the other **one a deaf-mute, and the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute** woman **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman do?** The brother cannot perform *ḥalitza* with her, as she is a deaf-mute. Rather, **he marries** the deaf-mute, **and if he wishes to divorce** her, **he may** subsequently **divorce** her with a bill of divorce. If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent sister died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute do? Either he performs *ḥalitza* or he enters into levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* addresses the reverse: a hearing brother was married to a deaf woman, and that brother dies. The surviving hearing brother must reckon with his *yibum* obligation toward a deaf woman. Since *she* is deaf, she cannot participate in *chalitzah* — and accordingly, *chalitzah* is not an option. He performs *yibum*. But — because *he* is hearing — *ve-im ratzah le-hotzi, yotzi*: he can give her a *get* afterward. This returns to the foundational principle of the *perek*: the woman need not be a *bar da'at* for a *get* to be valid, so long as the man is hearing.

### All Combos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים, אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ, נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת, אַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת וְאַחַת פִּקַּחַת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת — כּוֹנֵס, וְאִם רָצָה לְהוֹצִיא — יוֹצִיא. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת — כּוֹנֵס וְאֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** If **two brothers, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent,** were **married to two unrelated women, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent, and the deaf-mute** brother who was **married to the deaf-mute woman died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman do? He may marry her** if he desires the deaf-mute woman, **and if he wishes** afterward **to divorce** her, **he may divorce** her. If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent woman died, what should the deaf-mute** brother **married to the deaf-mute woman do? He marries** his *yevama* **and may never divorce her,** as he does not have the legal capacity to end a valid marriage.

The final permutation: *shenei achim, echad cheresh ve-echad pikeach, nesuim li-shtei nochriyot, achat chereshet ve-achat pikachet*, where the deaf man is married to the deaf woman and the hearing man to the hearing woman. If the deaf man dies (*met cheresh be-chereshet*), what does the hearing brother do? He has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* with the deaf woman, but it is a rabbinic obligation because his brother's marriage was only *d'rabbanan*. Since *chalitzah* is unavailable (she is deaf), he performs *yibum* — *kones ve-im ratzah le-hotzi, yotzi* — and he can give a *get* afterward because he is hearing. If instead the hearing brother dies (*met pikeach be-pikachet*), what does the deaf brother do? He faces a *d'Oraita* *yibum* obligation with an unrelated woman. He cannot perform *chalitzah*; he can only do *yibum*. But now, since the *yibum* is *d'Oraita* and he is a *cheresh*, he cannot give a *get*: *kones ve-eino motzi le-olam*.

The comprehensive takeaway from all these permutations: if a *cheresh* performs *yibum* on a *d'Oraita* bond, he is locked in — *yibum* only, no divorce. If the woman is the *chereshet* and the man is hearing, the *yibum* can be followed by a *get*, because the man's *bar da'at* status is what governs.

## Deaf vs Shoteh

### Rami Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מַאי שְׁנָא חֵרֵשׁ וְחֵרֶשֶׁת דְּתַקִּינוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין, וּמַאי שְׁנָא דְּשׁוֹטֶה וְשׁוֹטָה דְּלָא תַּקִּינוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין? דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן שֶׁנָּשְׂאוּ נָשִׁים וּמֵתוּ — נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּבּוּם!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rami bar Ḥama said: What is the difference** between the case of **a deaf-mute man and a deaf-mute woman, that the Sages enacted** rabbinic **marriage for them** despite their condition, **and** the case of **an imbecilic man and an imbecilic woman, that the Sages did not enact marriage for them? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an imbecilic man and a minor** who **married women and died, their wives are exempt from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage.** This indicates that the marriages of an imbecile and a minor are of no significance.

Having established the entire rabbinic framework of marriage and divorce for the deaf, the *gemara* now steps back and asks the foundational institutional question. Rami bar Chama poses it sharply: *mai shna cheresh ve-chereshet de-takinu lehu rabanan nisuin, u-mai shna shoteh ve-shotah de-lo takinu lehu rabanan nisuin?* What distinguishes the deaf person, for whom the Rabbis established a rabbinic institution of marriage, from the mentally ill person, for whom they did not? How do we even know the Rabbis did not establish *nisuin* for a *shoteh*? The source is a *baraita*: *shoteh ve-katan she-nasu nashim u-metu, nesheihem peturot min ha-chalitzah u-min ha-yibum* — the wives of a mentally ill man and a minor who die are entirely exempt from *chalitzah* and *yibum*, without even rabbinic recognition of the prior bond.

### Inst. Stands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ וְחֵרֶשֶׁת, דְּקָיְימָא תַּקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן — תַּקִּינוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין. שׁוֹטֶה וְשׁוֹטָה, דְּלָא קָיְימָא תַּקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן, דְּאֵין אָדָם דָּר עִם נָחָשׁ בִּכְפִיפָה אַחַת — לָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In the case of **a deaf-mute man and a deaf-mute woman, where the ordinance of the Sages can be fulfilled,** i.e., these marriages can be maintained, **the Sages enacted marriage for them.** By contrast, with regard to **an imbecilic man and an imbecilic woman, where the ordinance of the Sages cannot be fulfilled, as** one cannot live with an imbecilic partner, in accordance with the well-known saying: **A person cannot reside in a basket,** i.e., in close quarters, **with a snake, the Sages did not enact marriage for them.**

The *gemara* answers by drawing a distinction based on institutional viability. *Cheresh ve-chereshet de-kayma takanta de-rabbanan, takinu lehu rabanan nisuin* — in the case of the deaf, the rabbinic institution can stand, and therefore the Rabbis established it. *Shoteh ve-shotah de-lo kayma takanta de-rabbanan* — in the case of the mentally ill, the institution cannot stand. Why? *De-ein adam dar im nachash be-kefifah achat* — because a person cannot live together with a snake in a basket. Marriage to a mentally unstable person is inherently untenable; any such institution would constantly collapse, and it is therefore pointless to establish it.

### Minor Diff.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא קָטָן דְּלָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין, וְחֵרֵשׁ תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין? חֵרֵשׁ, דְּלָא אָתֵי לִכְלַל נִשּׂוּאִין — תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין, קָטָן, דְּאָתֵי לִכְלַל נִשּׂוּאִין — לָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the difference** between **a minor, that the Sages did not enact marriage** for him, **and a deaf-mute,** that **the Sages did enact marriage for him?** The Gemara explains: In the case of **a deaf-mute, as he will not reach the stage of** eligibility for **marriage** by Torah law, **the Sages enacted marriage** for him. Conversely, with regard to **a minor, as he will** eventually **reach the stage of** eligibility for **marriage** by Torah law when he matures, **the Sages did not enact marriage** for him.

One might then ask: a minor is also not a *bar da'at* — why did the Rabbis not establish rabbinic *kiddushin* for a minor boy? The *gemara* answers: a *cheresh* will never naturally arrive at the state of being capable of biblical marriage — *de-lo ati li-khlal nisuin* — and so if the Rabbis do not create an institution for him, he will never be able to marry at all. Therefore *takinu rabanan nisuin*. A minor boy, however, will grow up — *de-ati li-khlal nisuin* — and there is simply no need to create such an institution. Let him wait.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי קְטַנָּה, דְּאָתְיָא לִכְלַל נִשּׂוּאִין, וְתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן נִשּׂוּאִין! הָתָם — שֶׁלֹּא יִנְהֲגוּ בָּהּ מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** there is the case of **a minor girl, who will reach the stage of** eligibility for **marriage** by Torah law someday, **and** yet **the Sages enacted marriage** for her, as her mother and brothers may marry her off by rabbinic law. The Gemara answers: **There** the Sages issued their decree for a different reason, so **that** people should **not treat her in the manner of ownerless** property. If she marries, her husband will watch over her; if not, she might be treated disrespectfully.

This prompts the inverse question: a *ketanah* (minor girl) will also eventually be capable of biblical marriage — why then did the Rabbis establish an institution of *kiddushin* for her? The answer: *shelo yinhagu bah minhag hefker* — so that she should not be sexually taken advantage of. A young girl without a father to protect her is vulnerable to exploitation, and the Rabbis therefore instituted rabbinic *kiddushin* to provide her a measure of protection and social standing.

## Miun & Terumah

### Deaf Mi'un

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא קְטַנָּה דִּמְמָאֲנָה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא חֵרֶשֶׁת דְּלָא מְמָאֲנָה? דְּאִם כֵּן, מִימַּנְעִי וְלָא נָסְבִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the difference** between **a minor girl, that she may perform refusal,** i.e., she can retroactively nullify her marriage by means of a declaration of refusal of her husband, **and a female deaf-mute, that she cannot perform refusal,** as the Sages did not establish the option of refusal in her case? Since the marriage of a deaf-mute woman also applies by rabbinic law, why didn't the Sages establish refusal in her case as well? The Gemara answers: The reason is **that if so,** i.e., had the Sages enacted refusal for a female deaf-mute, others **would refrain from marrying her** at all, as she can issue a declaration of refusal indefinitely, whereas in the case of a minor there is a time limit with regard to her option of a refusal.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of comparative questions, each asking why the *ketanah* and the *chereshet* — both subjects of rabbinic *kiddushin* — are treated differently with respect to specific *halachot*. The first: *u-mai shna ketanah de-mema'enet, u-mai shna chereshet de-lo mema'ana?* Why may a minor girl exercise *mi'un* (the right of refusal that dissolves her rabbinic marriage without a *get*), while a deaf woman may not? The *gemara* answers: *im ken, mimnei ve-lo nasbi lah* — if we were to allow a deaf woman to perform *mi'un* throughout the entire duration of her marriage, no one would ever agree to marry her. A rabbinic marriage subject to perpetual revocation at her unilateral discretion offers no meaningful commitment; it would serve as a permanent disincentive. A *ketanah*'s right of *mi'un*, by contrast, is temporally limited to the period of her minority, after which the marriage becomes fully binding — and so the concern does not apply in the same way. (It should be noted that if a *ketanah* also happens to be a *chereshet*, she may still exercise *mi'un* on account of her minority, though her status as a *chereshet* does not independently grant her that right.)

### Terumah Gez.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא קְטַנָּה דְּאָכְלָה בִּתְרוּמָה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא חֵרֶשֶׁת דְּלָא אָכְלָה בִּתְרוּמָה? דִּתְנַן: הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט, וְעַל קְטַנָּה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן שֶׁאוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאִילּוּ חֵרֶשֶׁת לָא אָכְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further asks: **What is the difference** between **a minor girl, that she may partake of *teruma*** when she is married to a priest, **and a deaf-mute woman, that she may not partake of *teruma*** when she is married to a priest, despite the fact that both of their marriages apply by rabbinic law? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 55b): **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgada testified with regard to a female deaf-mute whose father married her off, that she can be divorced with a bill of divorce. And** he testified **with regard to a minor girl, daughter of an non-priest who was married to a priest, that she may partake of *teruma*, whereas a deaf-mute woman,** it may be inferred, **may not partake** of *teruma*.

The second question: *u-mai shna ketanah de-achlah bi-terumah, u-mai shna chereshet de-lo achlah bi-terumah?* Within the institution of rabbinic *kiddushin*, a minor girl married to a *kohen* may eat *terumah*, but a deaf woman in the same position may not. The source for this distinction is the very testimony of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada quoted earlier in the *mishnah*, which implies that a *ketanah* married to a *kohen* eats *terumah*, but says nothing comparable for a *chereshet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִיל חֵרֵשׁ בְּחֵרֶשֶׁת. וְלֵיכוֹל, קָטָן אוֹכֵל נְבֵלוֹת הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The reason that a deaf-mute woman may not partake of *teruma*, even if she is married to a halakhically competent priest, is due to a rabbinic **decree lest a deaf-mute** priest likewise **feed** *teruma* **to** his **deaf-mute** wife. The Gemara asks: **And** which prohibition would that violate? **Let him feed** her, as **he is** equivalent to **a minor** who **eats** meat from **unslaughtered animals.** This is referring to the *halakha* that there is no obligation to prevent minors from committing transgressions. Since a deaf-mute, who is not legally competent, has the status of a minor, the same reasoning should apply in this case. Consequently, the court should be under no obligation to prevent this deaf-mute woman from eating *teruma* unlawfully.

Why the difference? The *gemara* answers: *gezeirah shema yashia cheresh be-chereshet* — a rabbinic decree lest a deaf *kohen* marry a deaf woman, and the extension of *terumah* rights to her lead to the following dangerous permutation: a deaf *kohen* married to a hearing woman, whose *kiddushin* is only *d'rabbanan*, and that hearing, fully obligated woman will eat *terumah*. The *gemara* initially objects: *katan ochel neveilot hu* — just as we are not concerned when a minor or someone who is not obligated in *mitzvot* eats something rabbinically prohibited, why not extend the same leniency? The answer carries the objection one step further.

### Slippery Slope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּזֵירָה דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאוֹכֹלַהּ בִּתְרוּמָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is a rabbinic **decree,** as **perhaps he will come to feed her *teruma*** that applies **by Torah** law.

The deeper concern is expressed as *gezeirah dilma atya le-meichal be-terumah d'Oraita* — once a hearing woman married to a deaf *kohen* begins eating any form of *terumah*, the concern is that she will slide into eating *terumah d'Oraita*, violating a biblical prohibition. She is, after all, a fully obligated adult, and the line between rabbinic and biblical *terumah* can easily become blurred in practice.

One might ask: why don't we raise the same concern about the *ketanah* case? Why are we not afraid that permitting a minor girl married to a *kohen* to eat *terumah* will lead, by mirror image, to permitting an adult woman married to a minor *kohen* boy to eat *terumah*? Rashi explains that in the case of the *cheresh*, any variation of the case — whether she is deaf or he is deaf — involves the same rabbinic *kiddushin* framework, and it is easy to confuse the two directions. With a *ketanah*, the social reality was that it was invariably young girls who were married early, not young boys — and moreover, there is no institution of rabbinic *kiddushin* for a minor *boy*, so the mirror-image case simply does not exist. The *ketanah* concern therefore never arises.

## Ketubbah Status

### Ket. Compare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא קְטַנָּה דְּאִית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא חֵרֶשֶׁת דְּלֵית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה? דְּאִם כֵּן — מִימַּנְעִי וְלָא נָסְבִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And what is the difference** between **a minor girl, that she has a marriage contract, and a deaf-mute woman, that she does not have a marriage contract?** The Gemara answers: The reason is **that if so,** if the husband of a deaf-mute would be obligated to give her a marriage contract, men **would refrain from marrying her** at all.

The *gemara* now asks a third comparative question: *u-mai shna ketanah de-is lah ketubbah, u-mai shna chereshet de-leis lah ketubbah?* Why does the rabbinic institution of *kiddushin* for a minor girl include a *ketubbah*, while the rabbinic institution for a deaf woman does not? The answer: *de-im ken, mimnei ve-lo nasbi lah* — because if a *ketubbah* obligation were attached to marriage with a deaf woman, it would deter men from marrying her. Since she is already a difficult match in the social reality that the *gemara* is addressing, adding a financial obligation to the prospective husband would create a further disincentive. Waiving the *ketubbah* makes the marriage more accessible to her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְטַנָּה מְנָלַן דְּאִית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה? דִּתְנַן: הַמְמָאֶנֶת, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּיה, וְאַיְילוֹנִית — אֵין לָהֶן כְּתוּבָּה. אֲבָל יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט וּקְטַנָּה — יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And a minor girl, from where do we** derive that **she has a marriage contract? As we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 67a): With regard to a minor **who refuses** her husband and leaves him, **and** likewise a woman who is a **secondary** forbidden **relative** prohibited by rabbinic law, **and a sexually underdeveloped woman** who is incapable of bearing children, these women **have no marriage contract.** The Gemara infers: **However,** any other woman who can be **divorced by** means of **a bill of divorce, and** this includes **a minor girl, is entitled to a marriage contract.**

How do we know a *ketanah* has a *ketubbah*? The *mishnah* elsewhere teaches: *ha-mema'enet, ve-ha-sheniyah, ve-ha-ailonit* — the girl who exercises *mi'un*, the woman in a secondarily prohibited relationship, and the *ailonit* (a woman who never developed typical physical characteristics, presumed to be a *mekach ta'ut*) — *ein lahen ketubbah*. The negative implication is instructive: *ha-mema'enet* has no *ketubbah* specifically because she exercised *mi'un*, dissolving the marriage retroactively. But *aval yotzah be-get ketanah yesh lah ketubbah* — were a minor girl to be divorced via *get* rather than through *mi'un*, she would have a *ketubbah*. The right to a *ketubbah* is part of the rabbinic institution of *kiddushei ketanah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחֵרֶשֶׁת מְנָלַן דְּלֵית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה? דְּתַנְיָא: חֵרֵשׁ וְשׁוֹטֶה שֶׁנָּשְׂאוּ פִּקְּחוֹת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּתְפַּקֵּחַ הַחֵרֵשׁ, וְנִשְׁתַּפָּה הַשּׁוֹטֶה — אֵין לָהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם כְּלוּם. רָצוּ לְקַיְּימָן — יֵשׁ לָהֶם כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And a deaf-mute woman, from where do we** derive that **she has no marriage contract? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A deaf-mute and an imbecile who married** halakhically **competent women, even if the deaf-mute** subsequently **regained his senses, and the imbecile regained his competence,** their wives **have no** claim of **anything against them,** even if their wives received marriage contracts from them. However, **if** the men **wish to maintain** these women as their wives after they became fully competent, they **have a marriage contract** from that point onward.

How do we know a *chereshet* has no *ketubbah*? A *baraita* teaches: *cheresh ve-shoteh she-nasu pikechot* — a deaf man or a mentally ill man who married hearing women — *afilu nispakeach ha-cheresh ve-nishta'peh ha-shoteh, ein lahem aleihem kelum* — even if they recovered and became hearing or mentally competent during the marriage, there is no *ketubbah* obligation. The institution of rabbinic *kiddushin* for a *cheresh* did not include a *ketubbah*. And though there might be a question whether even a voluntary *ketubbah* would be binding — since a *cheresh* is not a *bar da'at* — if they ultimately decide to continue the marriage after recovery, *ratzu le-kayyeman yesh lahem ketubbah*: now that the marriage is *d'Oraita*, the full rabbinic institution of *ketubbah* kicks in automatically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפִקֵּחַ שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֵרֶשֶׁת אוֹ שׁוֹטָה, אֲפִילּוּ כָּתַב לָהּ מֵאָה מָנֶה — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ קַיֶּימֶת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרָצָה לִזּוֹק בִּנְכָסָיו. טַעְמָא דְּרָצָה, הָא לֹא רָצָה — אֵין לָהּ, דְּאִם כֵּן מִימַּנְעִי וְלָא נָסְבִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in the case of **a** halakhically **competent man who married a deaf-mute or an imbecile,** and he decided to write a marriage contract for her, **even if he wrote for her one hundred dinars her marriage contract is valid, because he wanted to harm his** own **property.** In other words, as he acted willingly, despite the lack of obligation to do so, this is comparable to one who chooses to harm himself and give away property in any other manner; it is his prerogative. The Gemara infers: **The reason** for this *halakha* is **that he wanted** to write her a marriage contract, from which it may be inferred that if **he does not want** to write one, **she** will **not have** a marriage contract. The logic is as stated above, **that if so,** men **would refrain from marrying her.**

The *baraita* then states the contrasting rule: *pikeach she-nasa chereshet o shotah, afilu kasav lah me'ah maneh, ketubbatah kayyemet* — a hearing man who married a deaf woman or a mentally ill woman, even if he wrote her a *ketubbah* of one hundred *maneh*, it is fully binding. Because he is a *bar da'at*, his voluntary undertaking of financial obligation is legally enforceable. *Mipnei she-ratzah li-zakhot be-nechasav* — he chose to encumber his own property, and he is entitled to do so. There is no rabbinic institution of *ketubbah* in this case, but his personal commitment stands.

This distinction — between the deaf man's inability to undertake binding financial obligations on behalf of the institution, and the hearing man's full capacity to do so voluntarily — raises a genuine difficulty: if the Rabbis trust the *cheresh* enough to establish the institution of *kiddushin* for him, why is it unreasonable to also require the normal *ketubbah* protection for the woman who enters into that marriage? The *gemara* does not resolve this cleanly. The apparent logic is that since a *cheresh* is not a *bar da'at*, imposing a standard financial obligation on him is of uncertain propriety; but this does not fully explain why the woman should bear the cost of that uncertainty by forfeiting her normal protection. The answer based on *de-im ken mimnei ve-lo nasbi lah* explains the policy choice, not the underlying principle.

### Marriage Incentive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, פִּקַּחַת לְחֵרֵשׁ לִיתַקֵּן לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה, דְּאִם כֵּן מִימַּנְעִי וְלָא מִינַּסְבִי! יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁהָאִישׁ רוֹצֶה לִישָּׂא, אִשָּׁה רוֹצָה לְהִנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** in a case of **a** halakhically **competent woman** who was married **to a deaf-mute, let the Sages enact a marriage contract for her, for if so,** i.e., if women would not receive marriage contracts in this situation, they **would refrain from marrying** deaf-mute men entirely. The Gemara answers: **More than the man wants to get married, a woman wants to be married.** Consequently, women will not be too discriminating with regard to marriage with a deaf-mute, even if they are not entitled to a marriage contract.

The *gemara* then raises the question from the opposite direction: given that we waive the *ketubbah* for a deaf woman so that men will be willing to marry her, why not also waive it — or create an especially attractive *ketubbah* — to incentivize women to marry a deaf man? The *gemara* responds with the famous principle: *yoter mi-she-ish rotzeh li-sa, ishah rotzah le-hinasei* — more than a man wishes to marry, a woman wishes to be married. Therefore, men require greater inducement to marry than women do. Waiving the *ketubbah* requirement is necessary to bring men to marry a deaf woman; no such waiver or inducement is necessary to bring women to marry, even a deaf man.

This principle reflects a perceived societal reality — that marriage for women has historically been harder to secure than for men — and the *gemara* is legislating accordingly. The asymmetry remains imperfect: the principle explains why no *extra* incentive is needed for women to marry a deaf man, but it does not satisfactorily explain why the woman who does marry him should be denied the ordinary *ketubbah* protection. This gap is apparent in the discussion and does not receive a fully satisfying answer.

### Malkio Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא חֵרֵשׁ דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַב מַלְכִּיּוֹ, אַנְסְבֵיהּ אִיתְּתָא וּכְתַב לַהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי מִנִּכְסֵיהּ. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן חַכִּים כְּרַב מַלְכִּיּוֹ, דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא. קָסָבַר: אִילּוּ רָצָה שִׁפְחָה לְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ מִי לָא זָבְנִינַן לֵיהּ? כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן הָכָא, דְּאִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain deaf-mute** man **who was in the neighborhood of Rav Malkiyyu.** Rav Malkiyyu **married him to a woman, and he wrote four-hundred dinars for her from the property** of the deaf-mute as her marriage contract. **Rava said: Who is as wise as Rav Malkiyyu, as he is a great man** who found a way to achieve a desirable result by giving her a marriage contract, despite the fact that the deaf-mute was not obligated to do so. Rav Malkiyyu **reasoned** as follows: **If** that deaf-mute man **wanted a maidservant to attend to him, would we not acquire one for him? All the more so here, as there are two** advantages, for she will attend to his needs both as a maidservant and as a wife.

An illustrative episode: there was a *cheresh* who lived in the neighborhood of Rav Malkio, and he married a woman and wrote her a *ketubbah* of four hundred *zuzim* — a substantial sum. The *gemara* records Rava's response: *man chacim ke-Rav Malkio, de-gavra rabba hu* — who is as wise as Rav Malkio, for he is a great man. The basis for this ruling is as follows: *ilu ratza shifcha le-shamshei mi lo zavninan lei* — if this deaf man wanted to buy a slave woman to attend to his domestic needs, we would certainly permit him to engage in that transaction and regard it as legally binding. A *cheresh* is not altogether barred from financial transactions necessary for his livelihood; we recognize such transactions as at least functionally valid. *Kol sheken hacha* — how much more so in the case of marriage, where the woman will care for his household and he is entering the full domestic arrangement of married life. Rav Malkio permitted the *ketubbah* on this reasoning, treating the deaf man's voluntary financial commitment as binding in the context of a transaction necessary and beneficial for his functioning life.

This episode resonates with the earlier question: if we recognize a *cheresh* as capable of such transactions when the practical necessity is clear, why does the rabbinic institution not include a mandatory *ketubbah*? The Rav Malkio ruling shows that *at minimum*, a *cheresh* can voluntarily undertake one — which brings the policy gap into sharper relief.

## Bar Da'at Status

### Asham Talui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵשֶׁת חֵרֵשׁ — אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Shmuel said:** If a man had unwitting relations with **the wife of a deaf-mute,** i.e., not knowing that she was married, **he is not obligated to** bring **a guilt-offering for uncertainty on her account.** He is not liable to bring an offering that is brought in cases where one is unsure whether he committed a sin that requires a sin-offering. A man who unwittingly has relations with a married woman must bring a sin-offering, whereas if the woman was doubtfully married, he brings a guilt-offering for uncertainty. However, the marriage of a deaf-mute is not even categorized as a doubtful marriage.

Having exhausted the comparative analysis of the rabbinic institution, the *gemara* turns to a deeper question: to what extent is the *cheresh* definitionally excluded from the category of *bar da'at*, and to what extent might his status be one of doubt? This question has significant implications for the *d'Oraita* dimension of his marriage and for the broader *halachic* framework.

Chiya bar Ashi citing Shmuel states: *eshet cheresh ein chayavin aleha asham talui* — if a man commits adultery with the wife of a deaf man, he does not incur the obligation to bring a *korban asham talui*, the guilt offering for an uncertain transgression. An *asham talui* is brought when one may have violated a *d'Oraita* prohibition. The fact that no *asham talui* is required suggests that the marriage of a *cheresh* has no *d'Oraita* dimension whatsoever — it is purely rabbinic, and adultery with his wife carries no biblical weight.

### Terumah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: חֲמִשָּׁה לֹא יִתְרוֹמוּ, וְאִם תָּרְמוּ — אֵין תְּרוּמָתָן תְּרוּמָה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה, וְקָטָן, וְהַתּוֹרֵם אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְגוֹי שֶׁתָּרַם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Let us say that** the mishna (*Terumot* 1:1) **supports** Shmuel's opinion: There are **five** categories of people who **may not separate *teruma*** *ab initio*, **and if they separated *teruma*, their *teruma* is not** considered ***teruma*. They are: A deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, and one who separates *teruma*** from produce **that is not his, and a gentile who separated *teruma*** from the produce **of a Jew even with the Jew's permission.** In this last case, **his *teruma* is not considered *teruma*,** because a gentile cannot be appointed as an agent to separate *teruma*, and all the more so he cannot separate *teruma* on his own. This shows that the actions of a deaf-mute have no effect, and are not even considered of doubtful validity.

To reinforce this, the *gemara* invokes a well-known ruling: *chamishshah lo yitremu, ve-im taramu ein trumatam terumah* — five categories of persons cannot take *terumah*, and if they did, their act is entirely void. These five are: a *cheresh*, a *shoteh*, a *katan*, one who separates from produce that does not belong to him, and a non-Jew acting on behalf of a Jew even with permission. The formulation *ein trumatam terumah* — their act of separation has no effect whatsoever — implies that a *cheresh* has no biblical standing at all. If even a rabbinic effect could be attributed to his action, we might expect the produce to be treated as doubtful *terumah*. The categorical nullity suggests complete exclusion from the domain of *bar da'at*.

### Eliezer Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תְּרוּמַת חֵרֵשׁ לֹא תֵּצֵא לְחוּלִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא סָפֵק. אִי סְבִירָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אָשָׁם תָּלוּי נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is no proof, as with regard to *teruma*, Shmuel **said** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: The *teruma* of a deaf-mute is not released into a non-sacred** status, **because it is uncertain.** Rabbi Elazar does not maintain that the actions of a deaf-mute have no consequence whatsoever. The Gemara asks: **If** Shmuel **maintains, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** that a deaf-mute is competent, **let him also obligate** a man who has relations with the wife of a deaf-mute **to** bring **a guilt-offering for uncertainty.**

Yet the *gemara* immediately introduces a complication: *hu de-amar ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — perhaps Shmuel's statement reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer specifically. For Rabbi Yitzchak teaches in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *trumat cheresh lo teitzei le-chulin, mipnei she-hu safek* — the *terumah* of a deaf person does not revert to the status of ordinary produce (*chulin*), because his status is one of doubt. Rabbi Eliezer thus holds that a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at* — a person of uncertain legal standing — not a definitive non-agent. His act of *terumah* separation is therefore not entirely void; it creates a state of *safek terumah*.

If Shmuel holds like Rabbi Eliezer, then one might expect that sleeping with the wife of a *cheresh* would incur *asham talui*, since the marriage may have *d'Oraita* status. Why then did Shmuel say no *asham talui* is required?

### Safek Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵינַן חֲתִיכָה מִשְׁתֵּי חֲתִיכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We require one piece from two pieces.** Shmuel maintains that one is not liable to bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty in every case where there is doubt whether or not there was a transgression, and where one would be liable to bring a sin-offering if it were certain that there was a transgression. Rather, a guilt-offering for uncertainty is brought if, for example, one had two pieces of meat before him, one of which was definitely forbidden while the other was permitted, but he does not know with certainty which one he ate. However, when the doubt involves a single item or action, which may or may not have been prohibited, in that situation one does not bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty. In the case discussed here, the doubt concerning the wife of a deaf-mute does not involve a choice between an action that is prohibited and one that is permitted. Rather, it depends on the status of the woman's marriage.

The *gemara* resolves this by reference to the technical conditions for *asham talui*. The classical case requiring *asham talui* is *chaticha mi-shtei chatichot* — one piece from two pieces: two objects are before me, one forbidden and one permitted, and I ate one without knowing which. There is a concrete object that embodies the prohibition. Shmuel may follow Rabbi Eliezer in treating the *cheresh* as a *safek bar da'at*, but he may disagree about the scope of *asham talui*, holding that the obligation requires a concrete doubtful object — not a mere abstract uncertainty about a person's legal category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חֲתִיכָה מִשְׁתֵּי חֲתִיכוֹת? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כּוֹי — חַיָּיבִין עַל חֶלְבּוֹ אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Elazar need** a case involving **one piece from two pieces** to render one liable to bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard to **a *koy*,** a kosher animal with characteristics of both a domesticated animal and a non-domesticated animal, **one is obligated to** bring **a guilt-offering for uncertainty for** eating **its forbidden fat.** Certain fats, which are permitted in the case of a wild animal, are prohibited if they are from a domesticated animal, and one who partakes of them is liable to bring a sin-offering. Since a *koy* is of uncertain status, one must bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty for eating its fat. This shows that Rabbi Elazar maintains that one brings a guilt-offering for uncertainty even for a doubt involving one item or action.

The *gemara* then notes that Rabbi Eliezer himself does not appear to require the *chaticha mi-shtei chatichot* formulation in all cases — for Rabbi Eliezer holds that one who eats the *chelev* (forbidden fat) of a *koy* (a creature of ambiguous species, neither clearly *behemah* nor *chayah*) is liable for *asham talui*, even though there is no second piece from which the forbidden one was distinguished. If Rabbi Eliezer applies *asham talui* to categorical doubts in that case, why not here? The resolution is that Shmuel adopts Rabbi Eliezer's position on *cheresh* status but not on the extension of *asham talui* to categorical doubts. He holds Rabbi Eliezer in one matter (*safek bar da'at*) and disputes him in another (*asham talui* requirements): *savar lah ke-Rabbi Eliezer be-chada, u-fligei leh be-chada*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar in one** matter, the status of a deaf-mute, **and disagrees with him in one** other **matter,** the *halakha* of a guilt-offering for uncertainty.

## Eliezer Logic

### Da'ata Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּחֵרֵשׁ דַּעְתָּא קְלִישְׁתָּא הוּא, וּמִיהוּ מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי דַּעְתָּא צִילּוּתָא
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raised a dilemma: What is the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar?** One possibility is that it **is obvious to him that the mind of a deaf-mute is weak. However, he is uncertain as to whether his mind is clear.** In other words, although a deaf-mute is weaker intellectually than an average person, and he does not understand everything, nevertheless he is aware of what he is doing with regard to certain undertakings.

Rav Ashi now asks a penetrating analytic question about the very basis of Rabbi Eliezer's position. *Mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer?* What is Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning? One option: *mi-fpashat peshita leh de-cheresh da'ata kelisha hu* — it is clear to him that a *cheresh* has attenuated *da'at*, diminished mental capacity. But reduced intelligence alone does not remove one from the category of *bar da'at*; people vary in intellectual capacity without losing legal standing.

The more precise question is whether Rabbi Eliezer's doubt concerns *da'ata tzelula* — clear, lucid thinking — rather than intelligence per se. There is an important distinction: someone may be of lower intellectual capacity but still think clearly and reason consistently; such a person remains a *bar da'at*. But if a person is unable to think with clarity — if his reasoning process itself is compromised — that is what removes him from the legal category. Rabbi Eliezer may be asking precisely this: does a *cheresh* think clearly, or does he not?

### Category vs Case

The question then bifurcates into two entirely different framings. The first possibility: *ul'olam chada da'ata hu* — the *cheresh* always has the same mental state; it is stable and consistent. We simply do not know which side of the line it falls on. Is the entire category of *cheresh* consistently clear-thinking or not? If this is the framing, then Rabbi Eliezer's doubt is a category-level uncertainty: all *chereshim* are in the same situation, and we simply cannot determine their collective classification.

The second possibility: *itin chalim ve-itin shoteh* — sometimes he is clear and sometimes he is not. Under this framing, the doubt is not about the category but about the individual — and more specifically, about the individual at any given moment. A particular *cheresh* may be lucid when he performs *kiddushin* and not lucid when he attempts to give a *get*, or vice versa.

### Divorce Outcome

The practical difference between these two framings is decisive in one key case: *le-hotzi ishto be-get* — can a *cheresh* divorce his wife? If the doubt is a category-level question — all *chereshim* are in the same stable state, we just don't know which — then *ki-kiddushin kach geirushin*: whatever the status of the *kiddushin* was, the *get* would have the same status. If the *kiddushin* was *d'Oraita*, the *get* is valid *d'Oraita*; if rabbinic, the *get* is rabbinic. Since his mental state has not changed, there is no reason to question the *get* that would not equally apply to the *kiddushin*. A *get* would therefore be possible.

But if the doubt is case-by-case — *itin chalim ve-itin shoteh* — then *kiddushin matzi mikadesh*: the *kiddushin* is effective because at the moment of *kiddushin* he may have been lucid, and a *kiddushin* effected in a lucid moment stands. But *geirushin lo matzi megaresh*: he cannot give a valid *get*, because at the moment of the *get* he may not be lucid — and you cannot know. The *kiddushin* is binding (either by *d'Oraita* or at least *d'rabbanan*), but a *get* given in a moment of non-lucidity would be invalid. Since there is no way to verify his state of mind at the time of the *get*, he cannot divorce.

### Teiku Conclusion

The *gemara* leaves the question unresolved: *teiku*. The doubt about Rabbi Eliezer's precise reasoning — whether the uncertainty is categorical or case-by-case — and its implications for the possibility of a *cheresh* giving a *get*, remain open.

## Modern Implications

### Geonim Develop.

What is striking, and what the *gemara* itself has now foregrounded, is that Rabbi Eliezer's position represents a serious *halachic* alternative to the categorical exclusion of the *cheresh*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that a *cheresh* is a *safek bar da'at*, and the *gemara* engages this position extensively. Some among the *Geonim* appear to have ruled in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer, and their rulings seem to indicate not only that the *cheresh* is a doubt-category but that where evidence exists in a particular case that an individual functions as a *bar da'at*, that individual may actually be treated as such.

### Changing Status

This has profound implications once circumstances change. The *gemara's* entire treatment of *cheresh* assumes a social context in which deaf individuals lack formal education, cannot communicate through language, and give every external appearance of a person unable to function as a full legal agent. Once that context shifts — once schools for the deaf develop, sign language emerges as a rich communicative medium, and deaf individuals demonstrably engage in sophisticated reasoning and deliberate decision-making — the case for treating them as *safek bar da'at* in Rabbi Eliezer's sense, or even as full *b'nei da'at*, becomes considerably stronger. The determination is then case-by-case: does this individual, in light of his background, education, and demonstrated capacity, function as a *bar da'at*? This trajectory, traceable from Rabbi Eliezer's *safek* through the *Geonim* and into the *halachic* literature of the last several centuries, reflects one of the more remarkable instances in which the underlying category of *p'sak* evolves as the factual reality it was designed to address fundamentally changes.
