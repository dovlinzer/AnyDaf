# Ketubot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blister Case

### Intro: Sex on Shabbat

The sugya on daf 7b continues the discussion of *bi'ah rishonah* — first-time sexual relations — on Shabbat, focusing on the concern that breaking the hymen may constitute a prohibited act. The Gemara picks up toward the bottom of 6b with the case of *hamefis mursa b'Shabbos*: one who lances a blister on Shabbat.

### Blister Halakha

The ruling is as follows: *im la'asos lah peh* — if the person's intent is to create an opening in the blister — *chayav*, he is liable. But *im l'hotzi mimenu leicha* — if his intent is merely to extract the pus — *patur*, he is exempt. The question that naturally arises is: for what exactly is one liable when making an opening? This is not an act of wounding in the conventional sense.

### Rashi: Opening = Boneh

Rashi explains: *im niskavin la'asos lah peh, shemiskayvein l'yafosah, lihayos lah pesach zeh l'yom achar, chayav shehu mesakein pesach, v'chayav mishum boneh* — if one intends to create an opening so that the blister will have a fixed aperture going forward, this constitutes fixing an opening, and he is liable on account of *boneh*, the prohibited labor of building. Rashi cites the verse describing the creation of Chava — *vayiven es hatzela* — as the prototype for this category, the same source he invoked earlier in the discussion about first-time intercourse. The parallel is noted, though it remains conceptually curious why creating a small drainage opening in a blister should rise to the level of *boneh*.

### Pakid vs. Akir

The Gemara then turns to the second half of the ruling — the exemption when one intends only to extract the pus — and considers how this relates to the earlier discussion about blood released during first intercourse. The Gemara's answer is: *hasam pakid v'akir, hacha pakid v'lo akir* — in the blister case, the pus is both stored and already fully uprooted from the flesh, contained entirely outside the skin. In the case of the hymen, the blood may be stored behind it, but it is not yet uprooted from within the skin. When the hymen breaks, blood is still being released from within the skin — it is bleeding in the full sense.

This answer is somewhat puzzling. One might have expected the Gemara simply to say that the blister case involves pus, not blood, and that the prohibition of *chavala* — wounding — is fundamentally defined by the extraction of blood. The Gemara's distinction between *pakid v'akir* and *pakid v'lo akir* seems to assume that the pus in the blister may contain some blood, which is why the answer focuses on degrees of uprootedness rather than on the substance itself. One might also ask what the Gemara's question was in the first place: the physical question of whether hymenal blood is truly comparable to blood drawn through a wound is not obviously answered by a case about pus in a blister. The Gemara's conclusion is simply that the blister case involves material already fully outside the skin, while the hymenal case does not.

### Pattur Aval Mutar

A further point arises regarding the category of *patur* in the blister case. As established at the beginning of Maseches Shabbos, *patur* on Shabbat ordinarily means *patur aval assur* — exempt from liability but still forbidden. There are, however, three exceptions where *patur* means *patur aval mutar* — exempt and actually permitted. This blister case is one of them, on account of the element of *tza'ar*, physical suffering, involved. Rashi confirms this reading.

## Amoraim Debate

### Rav Ami Permits

With that digression resolved, the Gemara returns to the central question of first-time intercourse on Shabbat. *Rav Ami sharah l'meiva batchilah b'Shabbos* — Rav Ami permitted *bi'ah rishonah* on Shabbat. The debate among the *Tannaim* and earlier *Amoraim* on this question evidently continued into later generations. Upon hearing Rav Ami's ruling, the rabbis of the academy challenged him: *ha lo kesiva kesuva* — but the *ketuva* has not yet been written! The assumption behind this objection is that if the wedding took place on Friday afternoon or Friday night, there would not have been time to write the *ketuva* before Shabbat. And a husband and wife are not permitted to live together without a *ketuva* — indeed, the Gemara will later characterize such an arrangement as resembling *be'as znus*, because without the protections of the *ketuva* the wife lacks the commitment and security that sanctify the relationship and guard against capricious divorce.

### Ketuva & Collateral

Rav Ami's response was: *atfuso matalten* — in the case under discussion, the woman had been given movable property as collateral to hold until the *ketuva* could be formally written. This ensures that even before the document is drawn up, she has concrete security for her eventual collection in the event of divorce. This exchange signals a broader theme of Maseches Ketubot: it is not merely the act of *nisuin* itself — the *chuppah*, the *sheva brachot* — that constitutes a fully sanctioned marriage. The protections of the *ketuva* are themselves a constitutive element of a committed marital relationship, providing reciprocal obligations and security for both parties.

### Other Authorities

The Gemara then records additional authorities who permitted *bi'ah rishonah* in similar circumstances. *Rav Zvid sharah l'meiva betechila b'Shabbos* — Rav Zvid likewise permitted it, and some report that he personally did so at his own wedding. *Rav Yehuda sharah l'meiva betechila b'Yom Tov* — Rav Yehuda permitted it on Yom Tov.

### Rav Pappi vs. Pappa

At this point the Gemara records a significant dispute between two authorities, each transmitting a ruling in the name of Rava. Rav Pappi in the name of Rava held: *lo teima b'Yom Tov desharei ha b'Shabbos assur* — do not suppose that Rava made a distinction between Yom Tov and Shabbat. Rather, *hu hadin d'afilu b'Shabbos nami sharei* — the same ruling applies even on Shabbat. The case simply happened to arise on Yom Tov. Rav Pappa in the name of Rava, however, held precisely the opposite: *b'Yom Tov sharei b'Shabbos assur* — Rava specifically permitted *bi'ah rishonah* on Yom Tov but not on Shabbat. The question is immediately apparent: whether the issue is *petach* or *chavala*, the physical concern is identical on Yom Tov and Shabbat. What could be the basis for a distinction?

### Mitoch & Shaveh

Rav Pappa explains to Rav Pappi: *mitoch shehutra chabura letzorech hutra nami shelo letzorech* — since wounding (*chavala*) is permitted on Yom Tov for a purpose, namely *shechitah* for food as taught at the beginning of Maseches Beitzah, it is therefore permitted even in a context that is not directly related to food preparation. The concern about *bi'ah rishonah*, on this view, is primarily a concern of *chavala* rather than *petach*, and on Yom Tov a specific leniency applies through the principle of *mitoch*.

Rav Pappa then challenges this reasoning: *aval ela me'ata mutar la'asos mugmar b'Yom Tov* — if *mitoch* works that broadly, one should similarly be permitted to burn incense on Yom Tov, since fire (*havara*) is permitted on Yom Tov for cooking, and by *mitoch shehutra havara letzorech hutra nami shelo letzorech*, burning for any purpose should follow. The implicit premise of the objection is that burning incense is in fact *assur* on Yom Tov. The answer given is: *alecha amar kra, ach asher ye'achel lechol nefesh* — the verse says "only that which shall be eaten by every soul," indicating that only something that is *davar hashaveh lechol nefesh* — universally beneficial, something that every person appreciates — may be performed on Yom Tov outside of direct food preparation. Burning incense benefits some people and repels others; it does not meet this threshold.

This Gemara is a central *sugya* for understanding the two distinct models of Yom Tov leniency. One model, the blanket *mitoch*, tends to apply to fire-making and carrying — two *melachot* that are somewhat conceptually marginal: carrying is described by the *Rishonim* as a *melacha geru'a* because it is not inherently creative, and fire, though the one *melacha* explicitly mentioned in the Torah (*lo teva'aru bechol moshvoteichem beyom haShabbat*), has an opinion among the *Tannaim* that one is not *chayav misa* for it on Shabbat. By contrast, other *melachot* require something analogous to the permitted act of eating — something *shaveh lechol nefesh*, a pleasure or need that is both universal and bodily. This Gemara insists on this second model. Under this model, the distinction between sex and burning incense is clear: sexual relations are a basic human need and pleasure, universal in scope, whereas burning incense is not.

### Sex vs. Incense

Tosafot elaborates on the *mitoch* principle, explaining: *perush l'tzorecho shel nefesh, ubilvad sheyihiyeh tzorech hana'at hayom o tzorech kiyum mitzva b'Yom Tov kigone hotza'at katan u'l'molo v'Sefer Torah likros bo* — it must serve some legitimate purpose, whether for personal enjoyment or for fulfilling a *mitzva* on Yom Tov, such as carrying a child to circumcise him or carrying a *Sefer Torah* for reading. Tosafot does not require *shaveh lechol nefesh* for the *mitoch* of carrying. This reinforces the point that the two models operate differently.

The Gemara's resolution — that *bi'ah rishonah* on Yom Tov is permitted because *chavala* in that context is *shaveh lechol nefesh* while incense-burning is not — is itself worth examining. It is true that sexual relations in general are a universal human pleasure. One might raise the point that first-time intercourse, from the woman's perspective particularly, is not the pleasurable experience the category implies, and that the *chavala* component is not itself pleasurable at all. The Gemara, it must be said, is approaching this entirely from the man's perspective. Nevertheless, the principle being invoked is that within the context of a pleasure that is universally *shaveh lechol nefesh*, a *chavala* that is necessary to enable that pleasure is subsumed under the leniency.

### Deer Case

The Gemara then presents a further challenge: *nizdamen lo tzvi b'Yom Tov, ho'il v'eino shaveh lechol nefesh, hachi nami d'assur l'mishchatei* — would you say one is forbidden to slaughter a deer on Yom Tov because venison is a rare delicacy, not something universally enjoyed? The response is: *ana davar hatzarich lekhol nefesh ka'amina* — the rule of *shaveh lechol nefesh* applies to things that are *necessary* for all people, not merely enjoyed by all. The underlying logic seems to be that since deer is food, it falls under the general category of eating, and the leniency for food preparation on Yom Tov does not require that every individual enjoy that particular food. The *shaveh lechol nefesh* requirement operates to extend Yom Tov leniency to things that are *not* food-related, by analogy to food; once something is food, the ordinary rules of Yom Tov cooking apply regardless of the specific delicacy involved.

## R. Yochanan & Lore

### Hora'ah Halakha

The Gemara records: *hora Rabbi Yochanan b'Tzaidan, assur lavo betechila b'Shabbat* — Rabbi Yochanan ruled in Tzidon that *bi'ah rishonah* on Shabbat is forbidden. The Gemara immediately questions whether a ruling to forbid can properly be called a *hora'ah* at all. *Umi ika hora'ah le'issur* — is there such a thing as authoritative adjudication in the direction of prohibition?

### Matir Authority

Rashi's explanation is illuminating. He writes: *mi kari issur hora'ah mishum dechol adam matzi lehachmir, she'afilu min hasafek she'ein lo barur lo hu ba ve'oser* — calling something forbidden does not demonstrate rabbinic authority because anyone can be strict, and even one who is uncertain about the law can simply declare something forbidden without any real knowledge or tradition behind the ruling. *Aval hamatir somech al shmu'ato o sevara chochmato vehi hora'ah* — but one who rules permissively must be relying either on a received tradition or on the strength of his own scholarly judgment, and that is what constitutes genuine *hora'ah*. The ability to say *mutar* is the true mark of judicial authority.

This principle is illustrated by a famous anecdote involving the Noda BiYehuda. A *shochet* from a neighboring town would make the long journey to ask his *treifa* questions, but eventually stopped coming. When he resumed, he explained that he had found a simpler method: upon reading the verse *uvasar basadeh treifa lo socheilu lakelev tashlichun oto*, he realized he could simply throw questionable meat to the dog — if the dog ate it, it was *treifa*; if not, it was kosher. The Noda BiYehuda asked why he had then returned. The *shochet* replied: "The dog was too much of a *machmir*." The point is well taken: being strict requires no expertise.

### Rebbe Examples

The Gemara offers two examples of *hora'ah le'issur* to complicate this principle. First: *horu Beis Hillel shetei nezirut sheva shanim acherot* — Beis Hillel ruled that Heleni HaMalka, having fulfilled her vow of *nezirut* in *chutz l'aretz*, had to repeat the full seven years upon entering Eretz Yisrael. While this ruling is strict, it is a genuine *chiddush* — the ruling that her vow did not count requires real knowledge. Second: *chut hashidra shenifsak berubo, divrei Rabbi; Rabbi Yaakov omer afilu nikev; hora Rebbe ke-Rabbi Yaakov* — Rebbe first ruled that the spinal cord must be severed in its majority to render an animal a *treifa*, then reversed himself and ruled, like Rabbi Yaakov, that even perforation suffices. The latter case is more directly relevant because Rebbe's reversal could be understood as mere stringency rather than authoritative ruling. The Gemara accepts that such language can be used even for a strict ruling. *Amar Rav Huna: ein halacha ke-Rabbi Yaakov* — and in fact we do not rule like Rabbi Yaakov; the cord must be severed in its majority.

### R. Yochanan Forbidden

The Gemara then presents the full account. Rabbi Abbahu reports: *sha'al Rabbi Yishmael ben Yaakov d'min Tzur et Rabbi Yochanan b'Tzaidan, ve-ana shemi'ah* — Rabbi Yishmael the son of Yaakov from Tzur asked Rabbi Yochanan in Tzidon whether *bi'ah rishonah* on Shabbat is permitted, and Rabbi Abbahu overheard the response. *Ve-amar lei assur* — Rabbi Yochanan ruled it forbidden. Nevertheless: *ve-hilchata b'tzlachot mutar liv'ol bitchilah b'Shabbat* — the final *halakha* is that it is permitted.

It is worth noting, as Tosafot observes, that Rabbi Yochanan is an *Amora* of Eretz Yisrael, while most of the *Amoraim* who ruled permissively throughout this *sugya* appear to be Babylonian. The *hilcheta* here follows the Babylonian *Amoraim*. Tosafot at the end of this passage adds: *ve-hilchata mutar liv'ol bitchilah b'Shabbat, v'lo chayshinan shema yishchot ben of* — we do not also worry that the groom will come to perform *shechitah* for the wedding meal and accidentally violate another *melacha* in the process. *V'aha samchinan l'me'evad seuda b'Shabbat* — and it is on this basis that the Ashkenazic custom developed to marry on Friday afternoon and hold the wedding *seuda* on Shabbat, a practice that also had the practical benefit of allowing the costs of Shabbat hospitality to serve double duty as the wedding meal.

## Sheva Brachot

### Rav Huna Teaching

The *sugya* now transitions from *bi'ah rishonah* to the laws of *birchat chatanim* — the wedding blessings. *Amar Rabbi Chelbo amar Rav Huna amar Rabbi Abba bar Zavda amar Rav: achas betulah v'achas almanah, te'unah bracha* — whether the bride is a *betulah* or an *almanah* (meaning a woman marrying for the second time), a *bracha* is required. The term *birchat chatanim* is the language the Gemara uses throughout; it does not use the phrase *sheva brachot*, in part because the Geonic period later added the *borei pri hagefen* to bring the count to seven, and the Gemara's set consists of six blessings. The *Mishnah* in Megillah establishes that *birchat chatanim* requires a *minyan* of ten: *ein birchat chatanim pachot m'asarah*.

The teaching uses the striking formulation *te'unah bracha* — she requires a *bracha*, literally, the *bracha* is incumbent upon her. This language, centering the woman as the subject to whom the blessing attaches, will become conceptually significant as the discussion develops.

### Duration Rules

The Gemara challenges Rav Huna's ruling by citing a contradictory statement attributed to him: *almanah einah te'unah bracha* — for a widow, no *bracha* is required. The Gemara resolves this with the distinction: *kan b'bachur shenasa almanah, kan b'alman shenasa almanah* — in one case a bachelor married a widow, in the other a widower married a widow. The principle that emerges is that if for either member of the couple this is a first marriage, the full seven days of *sheva brachot* are observed; if for both it is a second marriage, only one day is required.

It is also important to note the conceptual distinction between the *brachot* recited under the *chuppah* and the *brachot* recited at the meals during the week. The *Rambam* rules that if the *sheva brachot* were not recited at the time of *nisuin*, they may be recited many days later — but one cannot make up for *sheva brachot* missed at the meal by reciting them at a different time within the week. The *brachot* under the *chuppah* are attached to the act of *nisuin* itself, while the meal *brachot* belong to the ongoing celebration.

The Gemara further addresses a *braita* that teaches: *she'kedu chachamim al takanas benos Yisrael she'yesame'ach imah gimel yamim* — the Sages enacted that a husband should rejoice with his wife for three days, which was achieved by the practice of marrying an *almanah* on Thursday, yielding Thursday, Friday, and Shabbat together. The Gemara asks: if it is a first marriage for him, do you not say seven days of *sheva brachot*? And if it is a second marriage for him, do you not say only one day? What then is the meaning of "three days"? The Gemara offers two resolutions: either it is a case of a second marriage for both, where the *bracha* requirement is one day but *simcha* continues for three; or it is a first marriage for him, where *brachot* extend for seven days but the three days refers to a separate rabbinic expectation of sustained *simcha* and companionship.

### Gemara Resolution

The Gemara then raises a further challenge from a *braita*: *mevarchin b'betulah sheva ule'almanah yom echad* — we bless for seven days for a *betulah* and one day for an *almanah*. The *braita* makes no mention of the man's status. Does this not imply that the distinction is entirely based on the woman's status — a widow who marries a bachelor would still receive only one day?

The Gemara rejects this: *lo, kan b'alman shenasa almanah* — the *braita* refers only to the case where the man is also a widower. If a bachelor marries a widow, the ruling would be seven days. The *braita*'s formulation is *milta psikta katani* — it states things in absolute terms: a *betulah* never has fewer than seven days, and an *almanah* never has fewer than one day, but sometimes an *almanah* also receives seven. The Gemara's answer is admittedly not entirely satisfying, as the *braita* does seem to ground the distinction entirely in the woman's status.

### Status Shift

This observation leads to an important conceptual observation. The original framing of these laws — much like the *ketuva* system itself — orients entirely around the woman's status: is she a *betulah* or not, is her *ketuva* 200 *zuz* or 100 *zuz*? In that framework, the degree of communal celebration is entirely a function of what type of woman is being married. The Gemara's reframing, by introducing the man's prior marital status as the determining variable, represents a significant shift: the duration of *sheva brachot* now reflects the *simcha* of the couple as a couple — the degree to which this is a new beginning for either of them — rather than the personal status of the bride alone.

This distinction carries conceptual weight. The *sheva brachot* are a communal celebration, which is why a *minyan* is required. The extent to which the community continues to celebrate corresponds to the degree of novelty and joy for both parties. If for both it is a second marriage, the communal celebration is more abbreviated. If for either it is a first, the full week of celebration is warranted. The three days of *simcha* in the *braita*, by contrast, belong to the couple themselves — a period of time spent together at the outset of their shared life, regardless of prior marital history.

## Minyan Requirement

### Minyan Sources

Having established that *birchat chatanim* requires a *minyan*, the Gemara now examines the sources for this requirement. The *Mishnah* in Megillah states it as a rule alongside other rabbinic acts requiring ten men, but what is the textual or exegetical basis?

### Ruth 4 Source

*Amar Rav Nachman amar lei Huna bar Nasan: tana, minayin l'virchat chatanim b'asarah? Shene'emar, vayikach asarah anashim mizeknei ha'ir vayomer shevu po vayeisheivu* — Rav Nachman, citing a tradition from Huna bar Nasan, grounds the requirement in Ruth 4:2, where Boaz took ten elders of the city and said, "Sit here," and they sat. The blessings given to Boaz and Ruth by the assembled elders and people at the gate serve as the prototype for *birchat chatanim*.

### Makor Imagery

Rabbi Abbahu derives the *minyan* requirement from a different verse: *Bemakhelos barchu Elokim Hashem mimakor Yisrael* — "In the assemblies, bless God, the Lord, from the *makor* of Israel" (Psalms 68:27). *Makor* here points to matters connected with marriage and reproduction, and *bemakhelos* implies a gathering — hence a *minyan* of ten.

### Makor Meaning

The Gemara asks what each authority does with the verse cited by the other. Rav Nachman uses the verse from Psalms for a different *drasha* altogether, as cited in a *braita*: *haya Rabbi Meir omer minayin she'afilu ubarim she-bimei imanim amru shira al ha-yam* — Rabbi Meir asks how we know that even fetuses in their mothers' wombs burst forth in *shira* at the crossing of the *Yam Suf*. The answer is from this very verse: *Hashem mimakor Yisrael* — praise arose from the *makor*, the womb, meaning from within the bodies of the Israelite women. On this reading, *makor* means womb, the source of fetal life.

Rabbi Abbahu responds: *im ken limra kra mibeten* — if the verse meant only to indicate the womb, it should have used the word *beten*. The use of *makor* instead points to something more specific: *al iskei makor* — related to matters of the *makor* itself. And *makor*, as is clear from the verse *es mekora hera* used in the context of marital law, refers not to the womb but to the genitals themselves.

This is a striking and theologically significant point. If *birchat chatanim* finds its scriptural anchor in the verse *Hashem mimakor Yisrael*, and *makor* there refers to the genitals rather than the womb, then the blessings are oriented toward the sexual union of husband and wife rather than toward fertility and children. Indeed, a close reading of the text of the *sheva brachot* bears this out: there is no explicit reference to children in the blessings themselves. The *brachot* speak of the creation of *Adam* and *Chava*, of the joining of *chatan* and *kallah*, of the joy each finds with the other — but not explicitly of procreation. The implicit theme of *birchat chatanim*, understood in light of this *drasha*, is the sanctification of the sexual union itself, set in the context of creation and covenant. The *bracha* of *birchat eirusin* makes this even more explicit: *assar lanu es ha-arusos ve-hittir lanu es ha-nessuos* — He forbade for us the *arusos* and permitted for us the *nessuos* — the blessing is directly about the permissibility of marital sex.

### Pirsum Requirement

The Gemara then returns to the question of what Rav Nachman does with the verse in Ruth 4. On the surface, the ten elders there were needed to formalize the legal transaction — the redemption and the *chalitzah*-like proceeding — not to recite *brachot*. Rabbi Abbahu therefore says that what Rav Nachman needs the verse for is a different *drasha* entirely: *lemidrash amoni velo amonit moavi velo moavit* — to publicize the ruling that the prohibition on Moabites and Ammonites applies only to males, not females, and this is why Boaz needed elders present before he could marry Ruth.

The Gemara then asks: if the ten were needed for the *drasha*, why specifically elders rather than any ten people? And if needed for the *bracha*, why elders rather than any *minyan*? The Gemara's answer is: *lefirsumei milta* — to publicize the matter. The *zekeinim* serve as a *beis din* for the legal ruling; the ten are needed for *pirsum*, public announcement. This is illustrated by the story of Shmuel who told Rav Chanan: *puk veaisi li bei asarah, ve-ima lach b'anpaihu: hamizakeh le'ubar kanah* — go gather ten people, and I will declare in their presence that a transfer of property to a fetus is legally effective. The need for ten here is specifically to publicize an innovative ruling. (The Gemara adds parenthetically: *ve-hilchata hamizakeh le'ubar lo kanah* — the final ruling does not, in fact, follow this view.)

## Brachot Location

### Chatanim Brachot

A *braita* teaches: *mevarchin birchat chatanim beveis chatanim* — the *birchat chatanim* is recited in the house of the *chatanim*, meaning at the wedding venue or in the context of the *nisuin* itself.

### R. Yehuda Eirusin

Rabbi Yehuda, however, says: *af beveis ha'irusin mevarchin osah* — one also recites the blessings at the *eirusin*, the betrothal. This is not merely a question of physical location but of timing: Rabbi Yehuda holds that *birchat chatanim* — what we would understand as the blessings associated with *nisuin* — is recited not only at the time of *nisuin* but already at *kidushin*, which could precede the *nisuin* by months or years.

### Abaye Explanation

Abaye explains: *uviYehuda shanu, mipnei shemisyached imah* — this applies specifically in Yehuda, where the practice was that after *kidushin*, even before *nisuin*, the couple would spend time alone together in the bride's father's house in order to grow comfortable with one another. Since seclusion could lead to sexual relations — not as an explicit intention but as a natural result — special rules applied there.

### Rashi: Kallah Forbidden

Rashi explains the necessity for *birchat chatanim* at this stage as follows: *kallah belo beracha asurah le-ba'alah keniddah* — a bride without having had *birchat chatanim* is forbidden to her husband like a *niddah*. On this view, the *sheva brachot* function as a *matir* — they are what renders marital sex permissible. Without them, the sexual act is forbidden. Therefore, if the couple in Yehuda might have sexual relations during the period of *eirusin*, the blessings must be recited beforehand to avoid a forbidden act.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot's explanation differs somewhat. Tosafot writes: *u-lefi she-pe'amim ba aleha shelo leshem chuppah, osim beracha kedei shetehei kallah be-beracha* — since on occasion the husband would come to her not in the context of the formal *chuppah*, they recite the *bracha* so that the *kallah* should have the *bracha* in place. Tosafot does not go so far as Rashi in saying she is forbidden like a *niddah* without it; the concern is simply that there should be a *bracha* before any sexual act occurs.

### Sex vs. Joy

This discussion raises a deeper conceptual question about the nature of *sheva brachot*. The blessings speak of *simcha*, of *chatan* and *kallah* rejoicing together, of *menucha* and *bayit ne'eman* — the language is that of marital joy and communal celebration. Yet here, the Gemara seems to root the necessity of the blessings specifically in the potential for sexual relations. The language of *te'unah bracha* — she requires a *bracha* — and the comparison to *niddah* point toward an understanding of *birchat chatanim* as a *matir*, a ritual permission-granting act, rather than simply a communal celebration. A parallel might be drawn to *birchat hanehnin* — blessings recited before deriving physical pleasure — where the act of blessing frames and sanctifies a physical experience rather than merely marking a social occasion. Whether *birchat chatanim* functions primarily as such a *matir* for marital sex, or primarily as a celebration of the couple's new life together, is a question the Gemara gestures toward without fully resolving.

### Two Brachot

A final *braita* clarifies: *mevarchin birchat chatanim b'veis chatanim u'virchat eirusin b'veis ha'eirusin* — the *birchat chatanim* is recited at the *nisuin*, and a separate *birchat eirusin* is recited at the *eirusin*. This is striking, because while *birchat chatanim* appears in the *Mishnah* in Megillah and has clear Tannaitic grounding, the idea of a distinct *birchat eirusin* has no explicit earlier source. The *Gemara* will open tomorrow's discussion with precisely this question — what is the *birchat eirusin*, and where does it come from?
