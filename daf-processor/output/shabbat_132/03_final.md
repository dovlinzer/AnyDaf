# Shabbat 132 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Machshirin & Mitzvot

### Machshirin Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכֵּן אִם עָבַר זְמַנָּהּ בָּטְלָה. אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל בְּשַׂר עׇרְלָתוֹ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** in each, **as if its time passed, it is void,** unlike the mitzva of circumcision, which can be fulfilled at a later date if the child is not circumcised on the eighth day. **Rather, this is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer, as the verse says: "And on the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised"** (Leviticus 12:3), indicating that he is circumcised on the eighth day **even** if it falls **on Shabbat.**

The daf begins five lines from the top, at the double dot (*ad kan*). The Gemara has established Rabbi Eliezer's sweeping position: even *machshirin* — preparatory actions for *mitzvot* — may be performed on Shabbat, and this is true even for *mitzvot* that would not themselves override Shabbat under ordinary circumstances, such as *shofar* or *sukkah*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, if the *mitzvah* itself overrides Shabbat, then its preparatory acts do as well, and if it does not, the preparatory acts still may. This is quite a bold and broad position.

### Rabbis Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ אֶלָּא בְּמַכְשִׁירֵי מִילָה, אֲבָל מִילָה גּוּפַהּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל דּוֹחָה שַׁבָּת, מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara departs from the facilitators of circumcision to the *halakha* of circumcision itself and asks: **The Rabbis only disagree with** Rabbi Eliezer **with regard to actions that facilitate circumcision,** which, in their view, do not override Shabbat; **however,** with regard to **circumcision itself, everyone agrees** that it **overrides Shabbat. From where do we** derive this *halakha*?

The Rabbis dispute Rabbi Eliezer specifically on the question of *machshirei milah* — the preparatory acts for circumcision. The Gemara states: *ad kan lo pligi Rabbanan alei ela bemachshirei milah* — the Rabbis only disagree with Rabbi Eliezer regarding preparatory actions for *milah*.

### Milah Consensus

However, on the core question, there is no dispute: *aval milah gufa divrei hakol docha Shabbat* — the *milah* itself, everyone agrees, overrides Shabbat. This gives rise to the obvious question: *minalan* — from where do we know this?

## Milah Source: Halacha

### Halacha L'Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר עוּלָּא: הֲלָכָה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק הֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** This is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, but there is no biblical basis for it. **And so too, Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** It is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

Ula states that the source is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — an oral tradition going back to Moses at Sinai. Rabbi Yitzchak concurs. One might think this settles the matter, but the Gemara is not satisfied.

### Kal Vachomer Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מִנַּיִין לְפִיקּוּחַ נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: מָה מִילָה שֶׁהִיא אַחַת מֵאֵיבָרָיו שֶׁל אָדָם דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת — קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְפִיקּוּחַ נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in the *Tosefta*: **From where** is it derived **that saving a life overrides Shabbat? Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says** it is derived from the mitzva of circumcision: **Just as circumcision, which** pertains to only **one of a person's limbs, overrides Shabbat,** all the more so it is an ***a fortiori* inference** that **saving a life,** which is a mitzva that pertains to the entire person, **overrides Shabbat.**

The Gemara challenges this position by citing a well-known *braita* — found also in the Tosefta and quoted in Yoma — regarding *pikuach nefesh* overriding Shabbat. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah there argues: if *milah*, which involves only one of a person's limbs, overrides Shabbat, then *kal vachomer* — all the more so — saving an entire life should override Shabbat. This *braita* takes for granted that *milah* overrides Shabbat and uses it as the premise for a *kal vachomer*.

The problem this creates is pointed:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ הֲלָכָה — קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵהֲלָכָה מִי אָתֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: עֲקִיבָא, עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה (מְטַמֵּא) הֲלָכָה, וּרְבִיעִית דָּם קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וְאֵין דָּנִין קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵהֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it should enter your mind** to say that circumcision may be performed on Shabbat based on a ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **is an *a fortiori* inference derived from a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai? **Wasn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita* that an *a fortiori* inference cannot be derived from a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai? Rabbi Akiva sought to derive that a nazirite who comes into contact with a quarter *log* of blood from a corpse becomes ritually impure and is required to shave his hair. He sought to do this based on an *a fortiori* inference from the *halakha* of the bone from a dead person the size of a grain of barley, as he had a received tradition that a nazirite is required to shave his hair due to that contact. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said to him: Akiva,** the *halakha* that **a bone the size of a** grain of **barley transmits ritual impurity is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and** you would derive from it that **a quarter** of a *log* **of blood** transmits ritual impurity based upon **an *a fortiori* inference, and one does not derive an *a fortiori* inference from a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai. The *Tosefta* explicitly states that Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya himself derived an *a fortiori* inference from the *halakha* of circumcision on Shabbat. Clearly, then, it is derived from the Torah itself and not from a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

*v'i salka deitach halacha* — if we say that *milah*'s override of Shabbat is known only through *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, then Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah cannot derive a *kal vachomer* from it. A *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is, by its nature, an isolated ruling that does not necessarily integrate into the broader logical system of *halacha*. It stands alone. One cannot abstract from it and apply the standard forms of legal inference.

### Bone & Blood Case

The Gemara illustrates this principle with a debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva wished to argue by *kal vachomer* that a *revi'is* of blood from a corpse — which causes *tumah* even under the same roof — should also be a *tumas meis* that obligates a *nazir* to shave and that a *kohen* is prohibited from contracting. His argument ran as follows: an *etzem kese'orah* — a bone the size of a barley grain — causes *tumah* that requires a *nazir* to shave, even though it only conveys *tumah* by contact, not under a roof. A *revi'is* of blood, which is stronger in that it conveys *tumah* even under a shared roof, should certainly obligate a *nazir* to shave. To this, Rabbi Eliezer replied: the rule that an *etzem kese'orah* causes *tumah* is itself a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. And *ein danin kal vachomer me'halacha* — one cannot derive a *kal vachomer* from a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### No Kal Vachomer

It follows that if *milah*'s override of Shabbat rests on *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's *kal vachomer* regarding *pikuach nefesh* would be untenable. The Gemara therefore concludes that the source must lie elsewhere, and it proceeds to explore alternative derivations.

## Gezeirah Shavah Os

### Os Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָתְיָא ״אוֹת״ ״אוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Elazar said:** This *halakha* is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the word **sign** that appears with regard to circumcision: "And you shall be circumcised in the flesh of your foreskin; and it shall be a sign of the covenant between Me and you" (Genesis 17:11), and **sign** that appears with regard to Shabbat: "However, you shall keep My *Shabbatot*, for it is a sign between Me and you throughout your generations" (Exodus 31:13). From this verbal analogy, it is derived that circumcision, which is a sign, may be performed even on Shabbat, which is itself a sign.

Rabbi Elazar proposes a *gezeirah shavah* based on the word *os* — sign. Shabbat is called *ki os hi beini uveineichem* — "for it is a sign between Me and you." *Milah* is similarly called *v'hayah l'os bris* — "and it shall be a sign of the covenant." Through this verbal analogy, we derive that *milah*, which is an *os*, is performed even on Shabbat, which is an *os*.

### Tefillin Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּפִילִּין דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״אוֹת״, לִידְחֵי שַׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if** what you say is **so, phylacteries, with regard to which** the term **sign is** also **written:** "And it shall be for a sign on your hand and for frontlets between your eyes" (Exodus 13:16), **should** also **override Shabbat,** and they should be donned on that day.

The Gemara objects immediately: *tefillin* is also called an *os* — *v'hayu l'os al yadecha*, "and they shall be a sign upon your hand." If the *gezeirah shavah* from *os* teaches that *milah* overrides Shabbat, should we not conclude that *tefillin* likewise overrides Shabbat? The objection is pointed. There is a certain irony here as well: the very reason we do not wear *tefillin* on Shabbat is that Shabbat itself is already an *os*, making the *tefillin* — also an *os* — superfluous. Yet here we are suggesting that the shared status of *os* teaches the opposite: that the one *os* is performed on the day of the other *os*. The Gemara sets this approach aside and tries another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָתְיָא ״בְּרִית״ ״בְּרִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this principle is **derived** by means of a different verbal analogy from the word **covenant** that appears with regard to circumcision: "And you shall be circumcised in the flesh of your foreskin; and it shall be a sign of the covenant between Me and you" (Genesis 17:11), and the word **covenant** that appears with regard to Shabbat: "The children of Israel shall keep the Shabbat, to observe the Shabbat throughout their generations for a perpetual covenant" (Exodus 31:16).

The Gemara then shifts to a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *bris* — covenant. *Milah* is called a *bris* repeatedly — *es brisi heifir* — and Shabbat is called a *bris*: *l'dorosam bris olam*. The *gezeirah shavah* would teach that *milah*, which is a *bris*, is performed on Shabbat, which is also a *bris*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּדוֹל, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בְּרִית״, לִידְחֵי שַׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If this is so, then the circumcision of **an adult** should also be permitted on Shabbat and it should not be limited to a child on the eighth day, **as** the term **covenant is written with regard to him** as well, as it applies to any Jewish male not yet circumcised. Therefore, **let** his circumcision **override Shabbat.** The *halakha*, however, is that only circumcision at its proper time on the eighth day overrides Shabbat.

But this too raises a difficulty: the only place the Torah explicitly uses the language of *bris* in the context of *milah* is in the verse about an adult male who has willfully refused circumcision — *v'arel zachar asher lo yimol besar orlaso es brisi heifir*. That verse concerns a *gadol*, an adult who has chosen not to circumcise himself. An infant would not be subject to *kares* and would not be implied by this verse. We would therefore know only that an adult's *milah* — which would never override Shabbat since it would never fall on the eighth day — is called a *bris*, and the infant's *milah* remains unaddressed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָתְיָא ״דּוֹרוֹת״ ״דּוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this *halakha* **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the word **generations** that appears with regard to Shabbat: "Throughout their generations for a perpetual covenant" (Exodus 31:16), and the word **generations** that appears with regard to circumcision: "And I shall establish My covenant between Me and you, and between your seed after you throughout their generations, for an everlasting covenant" (Genesis 17:7).

The Gemara tries yet again with the word *doros* — generations. Shabbat is described as *l'dorosam bris olam* — "for their generations, an eternal covenant." And the verse about infant *milah* reads *yimol lachem kol zachar l'doroseichem* — "be circumcised, every male, for your generations." Perhaps the shared term *doros* teaches that *milah* is performed on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִיצִית, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״דּוֹרוֹת״, לִידְחֵי שַׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **let ritual fringes** too, **with regard to which** the term **generations is** also **written, override Shabbat,** and it should be permitted to affix ritual fringes to a garment on Shabbat.

But the Gemara raises the same objection: *tzitzis* is also described with the word *doros*, so should *tzitzis* also override Shabbat?

### Three Terms Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דָּנִין ״אוֹת״ ״בְּרִית״ וְ״דוֹרוֹת״ מֵ״אוֹת״ ״בְּרִית״ וְ״דוֹרוֹת״ — לְאַפּוֹקֵי הָנָךְ, דְּחַד חַד הוּא דִּכְתִיב בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This *halakha* is derived not from one common word alone, but **one derives** it based upon the three words **sign, covenant, and generations** that appear with regard to circumcision, **from sign, covenant, and generations** that appear with regard to Shabbat, **to the exclusion of these,** i.e., ritual fringes and phylacteries, **that with regard to each of them, one** of these **is written** but not all three words together.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves all these difficulties with an elegant answer: *danin os bris v'doros* — we derive the rule by applying all three terms together. Shabbat is described with *os*, *bris*, and *doros* — all three. *Milah* is similarly described with all three terms. No other *mitzvah* shares all three terms with Shabbat: *tefillin* has *os* but not *bris* and *doros*; *tzitzis* has *doros* but not the others in the same configuration. It is this convergence of all three terms that establishes *milah* uniquely as the *mitzvah* performed even on Shabbat.

One cannot help but notice the significance of these three terms — *bris*, *doros*, *os* — in the context of Jewish identity. Shabbat observance and *bris milah* have historically been the twin markers of communal belonging, and it is striking that the Gemara identifies precisely these three shared terms — covenant, generations, sign — as the textual basis for their interrelationship.

## Bayom Derivation

### Bayom Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״בַּיּוֹם״. ״בַּיּוֹם״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The verse says:** "And **on the** eighth day…shall be circumcised" (Leviticus 12:3), which means that the child is circumcised **on the** eighth **day** whenever it occurs, **even on Shabbat.**

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different derivation altogether. The verse states *bayom hashmini* — "on the eighth day" — and Rabbi Yochanan reads the word *bayom* as teaching that *milah* is performed on that day, meaning specifically on that day, *afilu b'Shabbos* — even if it falls on Shabbat.

### Korban Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה מְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״בַּיּוֹם״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּדָחוּ שַׁבָּת?! הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ בַּיּוֹם וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: But if** what you say is **so,** then, with regard to **those lacking atonement,** such as a *zav* or a healed leper, who must after their immersion still bring an atonement offering in order to complete their purification process, **with regard to whom** the term **on the day is** also **written,** as in the verse: "And on the eighth day he shall take two he-lambs without blemish, and one ewe-lamb of the first year without blemish" (Leviticus 14:10), sacrificing their atonement offerings **should also override Shabbat.** Rabbi Yoḥanan responded: **That** verse **is necessary** to teach that the sacrifice must be brought **during the day and not at night.**

Reish Lakish challenges this. The word *bayom* appears in the context of *mechusrei kapparah* — those who require a *korban* to complete their purification process — where the verse similarly says *bayom hashvi'i* or *bayom hashmini*. Should we conclude from those verses as well that such *korbanot* may be brought on Shabbat?

### Bayom Daytime

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי נָמֵי, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ בַּיּוֹם וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה! הָהוּא מִ״בֶּן שְׁמֹנַת יָמִים״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish asked: **This** verse with regard to the mitzva of circumcision **is also necessary** to teach that circumcision must be performed **during the day and not at night.** Rabbi Yoḥanan replied: **That** is derived **from** a different verse, which states: "And **he that is eight days old** shall be circumcised among you throughout your generations" (Genesis 17:12). That circumcision must take place during the day is derived from that verse.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds: in that case, *bayom* teaches something else entirely — *bayom v'lo b'lailah* — that the *korban* is brought by day and not by night. The Gemara asks whether the same answer applies to *milah* — perhaps *bayom* there too teaches only daytime, not Shabbat override. The Gemara responds: by *milah*, the daytime requirement is derived from a different source — *ben shemonas yamim*, where the word *yamim* (days, plural) implies daytime. With the daytime requirement covered by that word, the additional *bayom* is free to teach *afilu b'Shabbos* — even on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי נָמֵי, מִ״בְּיוֹם צַוּוֹתוֹ״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish says: **That** matter, that the atonement offering must be sacrificed during the day, can **also** be derived **from** a different verse, as it is stated: "This is the law of the burnt-offering, of the meal-offering, and of the sin-offering, and of the guilt-offering, and of the consecration-offering, and of the sacrifice of the peace-offerings; which the Lord commanded Moses at Mount Sinai **on the day He commanded** the children of Israel to present their offerings to the Lord in the wilderness of Sinai" (Leviticus 7:37–38), and from here **it is derived** that all offerings are sacrificed by day and not at night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל גַּב דְּנָפְקָא מִ״בְּיוֹם צַוּוֹתוֹ״, אִצְטְרִיכָא. סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּדַלּוּת, בַּלַּיְלָה נָמֵי לַיְתֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Although** this *halakha* **is derived from: "On the day He commanded,"** an additional source **is necessary** for those lacking atonement. **It might have entered your mind to say** that **since the Torah shows him mercy** by allowing him **to bring** an offering **of poverty,** as if one cannot afford to sacrifice the regular atonement offering, the Torah enables him to sacrifice a less costly one, **let him also bring it at night,** as perhaps the Torah shows him mercy and allows him to hasten his atonement. Therefore, **it teaches us** that he too must bring his offering only by day and not at night.

But there remains the question about *mechusrei kapparah*. The Gemara explains: by *korbanot* generally, we know that sacrificial service is performed during the day from the verse *b'yom tzavaso* — "on the day He commanded." One might have thought that since the Torah was unusually lenient with *mechusrei kapparah* — allowing them to bring progressively less expensive offerings if they lack funds — perhaps all the standard rules are relaxed for them, and they might even bring their *korban* at night. The verse therefore says *bayom* to confirm that the normal rules apply: daytime only, not nighttime. It is this concern, rather than any Shabbat override, that the word *bayom* addresses in the context of *mechusrei kapparah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יְהֵא זָר כָּשֵׁר בָּהֶן, וִיהֵא אוֹנֵן כָּשֵׁר בָּהֶן! הָא אַהְדְּרֵיהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina strongly objects to this** reasoning: **But if** what you say is **so,** that the Torah has compassion on a person lacking atonement and is lenient with regard to the *halakhot* of the atonement offering, **a non-priest should be fit** to sacrifice **them, and** similarly, a priest who is **an acute mourner,** i.e., one whose relative died that same day and has not yet been buried, **should be fit to** sacrifice **them.** The Gemara answers: **The verse has restored this.** The additional verse that teaches that even one lacking atonement must sacrifice during the day, also teaches that the Torah was lenient with regard to this offering only in the ways explicitly stated in the Torah.

Rav Avina adds: could we truly have entertained the notion that all rules collapse for *mechusrei kapparah*? Would we have allowed a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — to bring their *korban*? Would we have allowed an *onen* — someone in acute mourning — to bring it? Clearly not. The *bayom* restores the normal framework of rules; once the Torah reasserts that it must be done by day, we understand that all other standard requirements remain intact as well. The conclusion is therefore that *bayom* in the context of *mechusrei kapparah* is not about Shabbat override, while *bayom* in the context of *milah* is free to teach precisely that.

### Shmini Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא: ״שְׁמִינִי״. ״שְׁמִינִי״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** There is a different proof from the Torah that circumcision is performed even on Shabbat, for **the verse said:** "On the **eighth** day," underscoring that circumcision is performed specifically on the **eighth** day and indicating that it is performed **even on Shabbat.**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers another approach: the word *shmini* — eighth — is written once in the phrase *uven shemonas yamim* and appears a second time in the Scriptural formulation, and this redundancy teaches that *milah* is performed on the eighth day *afilu b'Shabbos* — even on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״שְׁמִינִי״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁבִיעִי! שְׁבִיעִי מִ״בֶּן שְׁמֹנַת יָמִים״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This** usage of the term **eighth is necessary to exclude** the **seventh** day, i.e., a child may not be circumcised before the eighth day. The Gemara answers: The fact that one may not circumcise on the **seventh** day **is derived** from a different verse, as it is stated: "And **he that is eight days old** shall be circumcised among you throughout your generations" (Genesis 17:12).

The Gemara questions this: the word *shmini* is needed to exclude a seven-day-old infant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁבִיעִי, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי תְּשִׁיעִי. דְּאִי מֵחַד, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שְׁבִיעִי הוּא דְּלָא מְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ, אֲבָל מִשְּׁמִינִי וְאֵילָךְ זִמְנֵיהּ הוּא! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: Both verses are **still necessary, one to exclude** the **seventh** day **and one to exclude** the **ninth** day. **As if** it were derived **from one** verse alone, **I would have said: It is** on the **seventh** day that one may not circumcise, since **the time** to circumcise this child **has not** yet **arrived** and the obligation of circumcision is not yet in effect; **however, from** the **eighth** day **and onward is its time,** and therefore it is permissible to postpone a circumcision until the ninth day. No answer was found to this question, and the Gemara concludes: **Rather,** the derivation **is clear according to Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

But then the Gemara points out that the phrase *ben shemonas yamim* already tells us the minimum age of eight days; the restatement of *shmini* then teaches that the eighth day specifically — and not a later day — is the proper time, excluding a nine or ten-day-old. Between the two, there is no remaining redundancy to teach a Shabbat override.

### Bayom Preferred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּדְלָא כְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: ״שְׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״מְחַלְּלֶיהָ מוֹת יוּמָת״ — בִּשְׁאָר מְלָאכוֹת חוּץ מִמִּילָה. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מִילָה, וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״שְׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״ — חוּץ מִשַּׁבָּת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם״ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov,** as the *tanna* interprets the phrase: "On the **eighth** day **he shall be circumcised"** to mean that the circumcision must be performed **even on Shabbat. And how do I fulfill** the prohibition against performing prohibited labor explicit in the Torah in the verse: "And you shall guard the Shabbat, for it is holy to you; **he who desecrates it shall surely die"** (Exodus 31:14)? That is referring **to other prohibited labors besides circumcision.** The *tanna* questions his previous statement: **Or perhaps that is not** the case, and the prohibition of performing prohibited labor on Shabbat includes **even circumcision, and,** on the contrary, **how do I fulfill** the verse: "On the **eighth** day **he shall be circumcised"?** It applies when the eighth day is any day **other than Shabbat. The verse states: "On the day,"** meaning on that very day when he turns eight days old, **even on Shabbat.** The *tanna* of this *baraita* rejects Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov's proof and accepts Rabbi Yoḥanan's assertion that the phrase "On the day" conclusively establishes that circumcision is performed even on Shabbat.

The Gemara therefore concludes: *ela mivarta k'Rabbi Yochanan* — the preferable approach is that of Rabbi Yochanan, who derives the Shabbat override from the word *bayom*. A *braita* is cited in explicit support of Rabbi Yochanan's position and against that of Rav Acha bar Yaakov.

## Braita & Kal Vachomer

### Braita Milah

The *braita* states: *shmini yimol afilu b'Shabbos* — on the eighth day one circumcises, even on Shabbat.

### Braita Resolution

The *braita* then addresses the apparent tension with the verse *mechaleleha mot yumos* — those who violate Shabbat are put to death — and resolves it by explaining that this applies to all other *melachot*, except for *milah*. The word *bayom* then confirms that *milah* is performed even on Shabbat.

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי תַּנָּא מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי קָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ, וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this *baraita*, **Rava said: Initially, what** did **this *tanna*** find **acceptable, and ultimately, what** did **he** find **difficult?** Initially he suggested that: "On the eighth day he shall be circumcised" is a valid source for the fact that circumcision overrides Shabbat, but ultimately, he deemed that difficult and turned to an alternative source, yet provided no reason, neither for his initial statement nor for his second statement.

This raises an important methodological observation. The *braita* frames its discussion in terms of competing textual possibilities — perhaps the verse means one thing, perhaps another — and ultimately resolves the question by citing the word *bayom*. But Rava asks: what is the underlying logical tension that the *braita* is articulating? What actual reasoning would have suggested that *milah* overrides Shabbat, and what reasoning would have pushed back against that conclusion? The *braita* as written merely toggles between textual possibilities without explaining the substantive arguments.

This reflects a meaningful distinction between the style of *Midreshei Halacha* — which tend to present competing textual readings without deeper argumentation — and the Gemara's characteristic desire to find the substantive reasons behind each position. Once the *braita* says "you could have read it this way, or perhaps that way," the Gemara wants to know: what are the actual arguments pulling in each direction?

### Kal Vachomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״שְׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״מְחַלְּלֶיהָ מוֹת יוּמָת״ — בִּשְׁאָר מְלָאכוֹת חוּץ מִמִּילָה, אֲבָל מִילָה דָּחֲיָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? — קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא: וּמָה צָרַעַת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה,
>
> **Translation:** Rather, we can explain that **this is what he is saying:** "On the **eighth** day **he shall be circumcised"** applies **even on Shabbat. And how do I fulfill: "He who desecrates it shall surely die"?** That is referring to the **other prohibited labors besides circumcision; however, circumcision overrides** Shabbat. **What is the reason** for this? **It is** derived by means of **an *a fortiori* inference: Just as leprosy, which overrides the** Temple **service,** as a priest who is a leper may not serve in the Temple and it is prohibited to cut off the symptoms of leprosy,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַעֲבוֹדָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת — מִילָה דּוֹחָה אוֹתָהּ. שַׁבָּת שֶׁנִּדְחֵית מִפְּנֵי הָעֲבוֹדָה, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּהֵא מִילָה דּוֹחָה אוֹתָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **and** the Temple **service overrides Shabbat,** as Shabbat offerings are sacrificed at their appointed time, and nevertheless **circumcision overrides** leprosy, i.e., if there were symptoms of leprosy on the foreskin of the baby, one circumcises the child even though he thereby violates the prohibition to cut off symptoms of leprosy; therefore, with regard to **Shabbat, which is overridden by the** Temple **service, is it not logical that circumcision,** which is so stringent that it overrides leprosy, **overrides** Shabbat as well? This was the *tanna's* reasoning at the outset.

The Gemara reconstructs the argument as follows. One might have reasoned by *kal vachomer*: *u'ma tzara'at she'docha et ha'avodah, ve'avodah docha et haShabbos* — if the prohibition against cutting off *tzara'at* overrides the *avodah* (a *kohen* afflicted with *tzara'at* on his foreskin, or a person with *tzara'at*, may not cut it off even to enable performance of the Temple service or eating the *Korban Pesach*), and the *avodah* in turn overrides Shabbat (communal sacrificial service is performed on Shabbat), then by the transitive principle, the prohibition of cutting off *tzara'at* is more weighty than Shabbat. Since *milah* overrides the prohibition of *tzara'at* — one circumcises even when *tzara'at* is present on the foreskin — it follows that *milah* overrides *tzara'at*, *tzara'at* outweighs *avodah*, *avodah* outweighs Shabbat, and therefore *milah* is the most weighty of all and overrides Shabbat. A compelling chain of reasoning.

### Tzara'at Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״אוֹ אֵינוֹ״ דְּקָאָמַר — הֲדַר אָמַר: וּמִמַּאי דְּצָרַעַת חֲמוּרָה? דִילְמָא שַׁבָּת חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהּ עוֹנָשִׁין וְאַזְהָרוֹת הַרְבֵּה. אִי נָמֵי: וּמִמַּאי מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲמִירָא צָרַעַת הִיא, דִּילְמָא מִשּׁוּם גַּבְרָא הוּא דְּלָא חֲזֵי. וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״שְׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״ — חוּץ מִשַּׁבָּת. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם״ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** was the reason the *tanna* **said: Or perhaps,** and questioned his previous statement? **He reconsidered** and **said: And from where** do we know **that leprosy is** more **stringent** than Shabbat? **Perhaps Shabbat is** more **stringent, as it includes** the severe **punishments** of *karet* and execution by stoning, **and numerous warnings** pertaining to it throughout the Torah. **Alternatively, and from where** do we know that the reason the Temple service does not override the prohibition of leprosy is specifically **because leprosy is** more **stringent** than the Temple service? **Perhaps** the Temple service does not override the prohibition of leprosy **because a man** afflicted with leprosy **is unfit** to perform the Temple service and not due to the stringency of the prohibition to remove symptoms of leprosy from one's body. **And** if so, **how do I establish** the verse: "And on the **eighth** day the flesh of his foreskin **shall be circumcised"** (Leviticus 12:3)? That it applies to days **other than Shabbat.** Consequently, the *tanna* cited additional proof from that which **the Torah states: "On the day,"** indicating that circumcision is performed **even on Shabbat.**

The Gemara then reconstructs the pushback: *hadar amar u'mi yeimar d'tzara'at chamura* — but who says that *tzara'at* is actually weightier than Shabbat? *Dilma mishum gavra hu d'lo chazi* — perhaps the reason we do not cut off the *tzara'at* to enable *avodah* has nothing to do with *tzara'at* being more weighty than *avodah* or Shabbat. The real reason is simply that even if one were to cut off the *tzara'at*, the person would remain *tamei* that day. He would need to immerse in a *mikvah*, and only after sunset would he be *tahor* and eligible to perform *avodah*. Cutting off the *tzara'at* would not help him perform today's *avodah* at all. The prohibition against cutting off *tzara'at* is therefore not overriding the *avodah* because it is weightier — it is simply that the two are not in the kind of productive conflict that would trigger any override. There is no immediate *avodah* that the cut would enable. The whole transitive argument therefore collapses: one cannot conclude that *tzara'at* is weightier than Shabbat, and so the *kal vachomer* that *milah* should override Shabbat fails to be airtight. The *braita* therefore concludes that the word *bayom* is what clinches the ruling — and without that verse, the matter would be genuinely uncertain.

This is where Rashi notes that according to the view of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, the tradition was simply known, and the derivations are being worked out after the fact. As the Gemara makes clear in Yoma in the parallel discussion of *pikuach nefesh*, the Sages produced many derivations for what they already knew intuitively. By the time of the Maccabees, *pikuach nefesh* overriding Shabbat was already a practical reality — the soldiers initially refused to fight on Shabbat, were slaughtered, and then intuited that God must want them to defend themselves. Only later does the Gemara ask *minayin* — from where do we derive it? — and produce a range of textual proofs. The same dynamic applies here: *milah* overriding Shabbat was taken for granted; the derivations are the Sages working backwards to anchor in text what was already known by tradition.

## Milah & Tzara'at

### Milah Overrides Tzara'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִילָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַצָּרַעַת, בֵּין בִּזְמַנָּהּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ. יוֹם טוֹב אֵינָהּ דּוֹחָה אֶלָּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Circumcision overrides leprosy.** The foreskin is cut even if it has symptoms of leprosy on it, despite the fact that there is a Torah prohibition to cut off symptoms of leprosy. This is the *halakha* **both** when the circumcision takes place **at its** appointed **time,** on the eighth day, or **when it is not** performed **at its** appointed **time** but after the eighth day. However, circumcision **overrides a Festival only** when performed **at its** appointed **time.**

The Gemara now turns to a closely related but distinct question. A *braita* teaches: *milah docha es hatzara'as, bein bizmano bein shelo bizmano* — *milah* overrides *tzara'at* whether or not it is performed on the eighth day, i.e., whether or not it is performed at the prescribed time. By contrast, *milah* overrides *Yom Tov* — and, needless to say, Shabbat — only when it falls on the eighth day, *bizmano bilvad*. But with respect to *tzara'at*, any *milah* at any time overrides the prohibition against cutting off *tzara'at*.

### Besar Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יִמּוֹל בְּשַׂר עׇרְלָתוֹ״, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם בַּהֶרֶת, יָקוֹץ. וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״הִשָּׁמֶר בְּנֶגַע הַצָּרַעַת״ — בִּשְׁאָר מְקוֹמוֹת חוּץ מִמִּילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And on the eighth day **the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised"** (Leviticus 12:3), since this verse is stated in general terms, it teaches that **even though there is a bright white** leprous **spot there, he should cut it. And how do I establish** the verse: **"Take care with regard to the plague of leprosy** to take great care and to perform in accordance with all that the priests, the Levites, instruct you; as I commanded them you shall take care to perform" (Deuteronomy 24:8)? Does usage of the term "take care" indicate that there is a negative mitzva that prohibits cutting off symptoms of leprosy? We establish this prohibition as applying **in other places, other than** the place of a **circumcision.**

The Gemara asks: *minalan* — from where do we know this? The *braita* derives it from the word *besar* — flesh — in the verse *yimol besar orlaso*. The Torah could simply have written *yimol orlaso* — "circumcise his foreskin" — but it says *yimol besar orlaso* — "circumcise the flesh of his foreskin." This extra word teaches that *milah* is performed even when there is *tzara'at* — a *baheret* — on the site: *af al pi sheyesh sham baheres, yikotz* — even so, one cuts it off.

### Braita Logic

The *braita* then presents its deliberative structure: one might have thought that the prohibition *hishamer benega hatzara'as* — "guard yourself regarding the *nega* of *tzara'at*," understood as a prohibition against cutting off *tzara'at* — applies everywhere, including on the foreskin, so that *milah* would not override it. Alternatively, one might argue that *milah* does override it and the prohibition applies only elsewhere on the body. The *braita* concludes: the extra word *besar* teaches that *milah* is performed even in the presence of *tzara'at*.

### Rava Analyzes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי תַּנָּא מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי נִיחָא לֵיהּ וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Initially, what** did **this *tanna*** find **acceptable, and ultimately, what** did **he** find **difficult?** At first he assumed that the mitzva of circumcision is more stringent, but he ultimately rejected this assumption with no explanation.

As before, Rava asks what the underlying reasoning is on each side of this debate — the *braita* presents the options without explaining the substantive arguments.

### Kal Vachomer Tzara'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״יִמּוֹל בְּשַׂר עׇרְלָתוֹ״ — וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ [שָׁם] בַּהֶרֶת, וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״הִשָּׁמֶר בְּנֶגַע הַצָּרַעַת״ — בִּשְׁאָר מְקוֹמוֹת חוּץ מִמִּילָה. אֲבָל מִילָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַצָּרַעַת, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָתְיָא מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה שַׁבָּת חֲמוּרָה, מִילָה דּוֹחָה אוֹתָהּ, צָרַעַת לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **this is what he is saying:** Initially he held that the phrase: **The flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised** teaches that it is permitted to remove the foreskin **even though there is a bright white spot** there. **And how do I validate** the following verse: **Take care with regard to the plague of leprosy?** It applies **in other places, aside from** the place of **circumcision, but circumcision overrides leprosy. What is the reason** for this? **It is derived by** means of **an *a fortiori* inference: Just as Shabbat** is **stringent** and nevertheless **circumcision overrides it, all the more so** that circumcision overrides **leprosy,** which is less stringent than Shabbat.

The initial argument in favor of performing *milah* despite *tzara'at* would rest on a *kal vachomer*: *u'ma Shabbos chamura milah docha oslah, tzara'as lo kol she'ken* — Shabbat is weighty, yet *milah* overrides it; *tzara'at* is surely less weighty than Shabbat, so *milah* should certainly override *tzara'at* as well. If asked whether Shabbat or *tzara'at* is the weightier prohibition, virtually everyone would say Shabbat. It follows that *milah*'s override of the lesser prohibition of *tzara'at* would seem to require no special derivation.

### Tzara'at Counterarg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״אוֹ אֵינוֹ״ דְּקָאָמַר — הֲדַר קָאָמַר: מִמַּאי דְּשַׁבָּת חֲמִירָא? דִילְמָא צָרַעַת חֲמִירָא, שֶׁכֵּן דּוֹחָה אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה, וַעֲבוֹדָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּשָׂר״ — וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם בַּהֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** was the reason for the term **or perhaps that** the *tanna* **is saying** to question his previous statement? **He reconsidered** and **said: From where** do we know **that Shabbat is** more **stringent? Perhaps leprosy is** more **stringent, as** leprosy **overrides the** Temple **service,** as stated earlier, **and the** Temple **service overrides Shabbat.** Therefore, **the verse states** the additional word **flesh,** to teach that the foreskin is removed **even though there is a bright white** leprous **spot there.**

The pushback: *mi'mai de-Shabbos chamira? dilma tzara'at chamira* — what makes you confident that Shabbat is weightier than *tzara'at*? Perhaps it is *tzara'at* that is weightier. The very same transitive argument applies here: *tzara'at* overrides *avodah* (one does not cut off *tzara'at* to enable performance of *avodah*), *avodah* overrides Shabbat — so perhaps *tzara'at* is weightier than Shabbat, and *milah*, while it overrides Shabbat, does not necessarily override *tzara'at*. Since the argument is genuinely two-sided, we cannot resolve it by *kal vachomer* alone, and the word *besar* is required to establish that *milah* does in fact override *tzara'at*.

## Aseh vs. Lo Taase

### Aseh Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא. מִילָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַצָּרַעַת, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְדָחֵי לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **another version** of Rava's comments. Initially the *tanna* thought that **circumcision overrides leprosy. What is the reason** for this? He relied on the principle **that a positive** mitzva **comes and overrides a negative** mitzva.

The Gemara now presents an alternative approach — the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*: a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition. *Milah* is a *mitzvas asei*, and the prohibition against cutting off *tzara'at* is derived from *hishamer* — a *lo ta'aseh*. Accordingly, the positive commandment of *milah* should override the negative prohibition of *tzara'at* as a matter of straightforward principle, without requiring any *kal vachomer* from Shabbat.

### Objection to Aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״אוֹ אֵינוֹ״ דְּקָאָמַר — הֲדַר קָאָמַר: אֵימַר דְּאָמְרִינַן דְּאָתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְדָחֵי אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה גְּרֵידָא, הַאי עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה הוּא. וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״יִמּוֹל בְּשַׂר עׇרְלָתוֹ״ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בַּהֶרֶת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּשָׂר״ — וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם בַּהֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** was the reason the *tanna* **is saying: Or perhaps** to question his previous statement? **He reconsidered** and **said: Say that we say that a positive** mitzva **comes and overrides a negative** mitzva when there is **a negative** mitzva **alone.** However, **this** cutting off leprosy **is** prohibited by both **a positive** mitzva **and a negative** mitzva. **And how do I establish** the verse: **The flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised?** It applies only **when there is no bright white spot on** the foreskin. Therefore, **the verse states** the additional word **flesh** in order to emphasize that the foreskin is removed, **even though there is a bright white spot there.**

The objection: *asei docheh lo ta'aseh greda* — the principle of a positive commandment overriding a negative one applies only when the negative stands alone. The prohibition of cutting off *tzara'at* is not a standalone *lo ta'aseh*. It is accompanied by a positive obligation as well — *lishmor v'la'asos*, "guard and perform," which is understood as an *asei* component to the laws of *tzara'at*. When the opposing prohibition involves both a positive and a negative obligation, the simple hierarchy of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* does not apply.

### Braita Resolves

The *braita* therefore concludes: the word *besar* is the necessary textual anchor. Without it, neither the *kal vachomer* from Shabbat nor the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* would be sufficient to establish that *milah* overrides *tzara'at*. With the word *besar*, the override is explicit.

## Common Denominator

### Besar in Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח גָּדוֹל, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בָּשָׂר״. קָטָן נָמֵי, כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בָּשָׂר״. בֵּינוֹנִי מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the derivation from the word flesh: This **works out well** with regard to the circumcision of **an adult** who has not yet been circumcised, **as** the word **flesh is written with regard to** adults in the verse: "And an uncircumcised male who will not circumcise the flesh of his foreskin, that soul shall be cut off from its people; My covenant he has broken" (Genesis 17:14). Similarly, it works out well with regard to **a minor** who is circumcised on the eighth day, as the superfluous word **flesh is also written** with regard to him. However, with regard to a person at an **intermediate** stage of life, i.e., a child who was not circumcised on the eighth day but has not yet reached majority, **from where do we derive** that his circumcision overrides leprosy? The Torah explicitly mandates his circumcision: "Circumcise for yourselves every male" (Genesis 17:10). However, that verse does not employ the term flesh.

A further difficulty now arises. The word *besar* appears in two specific contexts: by the infant — *uven shemonas yamim yimol besar orlaso* — and by the adult — *asher lo yimol besar orlaso*. The adult gets *kares* for willful non-circumcision, and only an adult is described in those terms. A minor who is older than eight days — a *beinoni* — is neither an eight-day-old infant nor yet an adult subject to *kares*. The word *besar* is not explicitly written in connection with his *milah*. One might therefore argue that only the infant's and the adult's *milah* override *tzara'at* — the infant because of the weight of the eighth-day commandment, and the adult because of the severity of *kares* — but the *beinoni*, lacking either of those special weights, might not override *tzara'at*.

### Abaye's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָתְיָא מִבֵּינַיָּא: מִגָּדוֹל לָא אָתְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת. מִקָּטָן לָא אָתְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן מִילָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן — שֶׁכֵּן נִימּוֹלִין וְדוֹחִין אֶת הַצָּרַעַת, אַף כֹּל שֶׁנִּימּוֹלִין — דּוֹחִין אֶת הַצָּרַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It is derived from a combination** of the two sources about the status of a child at the intermediate stage, from the common denominator between an eight-day-old and one who reached majority. **From an adult** alone, the *halakha* with regard to an intermediate child **cannot be derived, as** an adult **is punishable by *karet*** if he fails to circumcise himself, but an intermediate child is not punishable by *karet*. Likewise, **from** the case of **an** eight-day-old **child,** the case of an intermediate child **cannot be derived; since** the **circumcision at its time** overrides Shabbat it may also override leprosy. However, the **common denominator** between an eight-day-old and an adult **is that they are circumcised and** their circumcision **overrides leprosy. So too, all who are circumcised,** including those in the intermediate stage, **override leprosy.**

Abaye resolves this with the principle of *tzad hashaveh* — the common denominator. One cannot learn simply from the *gadol* that *milah* overrides *tzara'at*, since the *gadol* has the distinctive weight of *kares*. One cannot learn simply from the *katan* of eight days, since that *milah* has the distinctive weight of being performed at the prescribed time. But what they share in common — the very act of *milah* — is that each overrides *tzara'at*. From this common denominator, *Abaye* concludes: *af kol shehamol docheh es hatzara'at* — every circumcision overrides *tzara'at*, including that of the *beinoni*.

## Tzara'at Not Weightier

### Chain Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְרָבָא: מִמַּאי דְּשַׁבָּת חֲמִירָא? דִּילְמָא צָרַעַת חֲמִירָא, שֶׁכֵּן דּוֹחָה אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה, וַעֲבוֹדָה דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת! הָתָם, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲמִירָא צָרַעַת, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּגַבְרָא הוּא דְּלָא חֲזִי. אַמַּאי: וְיָקוֹץ בַּהַרְתּוֹ וְיַעֲבוֹד! מְחוּסָּר טְבִילָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said to Rava: From where** do we know that **Shabbat is** more **stringent? Perhaps leprosy is** more **stringent, as** leprosy **overrides the** Temple **service, and the** Temple **service overrides Shabbat.** Rava answered: **There,** when leprosy overrides the Temple service, **it is not because leprosy is** more **stringent; rather,** it is **because the man** afflicted with leprosy **is unfit** for the Temple service. Rav Safra asked: **Why** is he unfit? **Let him cut off his bright white** leprous **spot and serve.** Rava answered: He would remain unfit to serve, as **he is lacking immersion.** In order to purify himself for service in the Temple, he must immerse himself and wait until the following day. In the meantime he is unfit.

The Gemara returns to press the transitive argument once more. Rav Safra asks Rava: *mimai de-Shabbos chamira? dilma tzara'at chamira* — what makes you think Shabbat is weightier than *tzara'at*? *Sheken dacha avodah, ve'avodah dacha Shabbos* — *tzara'at* overrides *avodah*, and *avodah* overrides Shabbat, so perhaps *tzara'at* is the weightiest of the three?

The Gemara answers: *hatam lav mishum dechamira tzara'at* — the reason we do not cut off *tzara'at* to enable *avodah* is not because *tzara'at* is weightier. *Ella mishum degavra hu delo chazi* — it is because even after cutting off the *tzara'at*, the person would not be fit to perform *avodah* that same day. He would be *mechusar tevillah* — lacking immersion — and would need to go to the *mikvah* first. Only after sunset following the immersion would he become *tahor* and eligible for *avodah*. Cutting off the *tzara'at* therefore provides no practical benefit for today's *avodah*. The prohibition against cutting *tzara'at* is not demonstrating that it is more weighty than *avodah*; it simply means there is nothing to be gained today.

### Direct Confrontation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח נְגָעִים טְמֵאִים. נְגָעִים טְהוֹרִים, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra raised a difficulty: **It works out well** if we are referring to **impure** symptoms of **leprosy,** as even one who removes them must immerse afterward. However, with regard to **pure** symptoms of **leprosy,** there is a prohibition to cut off the symptoms even though there is no impurity. They have the legal status of blemishes that invalidate a priest from serving until it is cured. Once the bright white spot is removed, he may immediately serve in the Temple without immersion. **What is there to say** in that case?

The Gemara then raises a sharper version of the challenge: what of *nega'im tehorim* — *tzara'at* lesions that are *tahor* and do not convey *tumah*? In such a case, cutting off the *tzara'at* would not leave the person *tamei*; he would be eligible to perform *avodah* immediately. Yet even there the prohibition against cutting it off applies. Does this not prove that the prohibition of *tzara'at* is indeed weightier than *avodah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הֵיכָא אָמְרִינַן דְּאָתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְדָחֵי לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — כְּגוֹן מִילָה בְּצָרַעַת, אִי נָמֵי צִיצִית וְכִלְאַיִם, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּמִתְעֲקַר לָאו — קָא מוֹקֵים עֲשֵׂה. הָכָא, בְּעִידָּנָא דְּמִתְעֲקַר לְלָאו — לָא קָא מוֹקֵים עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said** that this is the reason that leprosy overrides the Temple service: **Where do we say that a positive** mitzva **overrides a negative** mitzva? It is in cases **like circumcision in** a case of **leprosy,** or **alternatively, ritual fringes and diverse kinds** of wool and linen, **as at the time the negative** mitzva **is uprooted,** the **positive** mitzva **is fulfilled** in the very same action, e.g., when the ritual fringes are woolen and will be attached to a linen garment, a prohibited mixture is created. However, **here,** in the case of a person afflicted with pure symptoms of leprosy cutting off his symptoms to enable his involvement in the Temple service, it is different, **at the time the negative** mitzva **is uprooted,** the **positive** mitzva **is not** yet **fulfilled,** as cutting off the symptoms is only a preliminary action that enables him to serve. In that case, the positive mitzva does not override the negative one.

Rav Ashi addresses this with a fundamental principle: *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* — the rule that a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition — applies only when the two directly confront each other at the very moment of violation. *Be'idna de'mit'akar lav, kamukim aseh* — at the precise moment the prohibition is being transgressed, the positive commandment must simultaneously be fulfilled.

The classic examples are *milah* overriding *tzara'at*, where the act of cutting the *tzara'at* is simultaneously the act of performing the *milah*; and *tzitzis* overriding *kilayim*, where attaching the woolen *tzitzis* to a linen garment involves the *kilayim* prohibition at the very moment the *mitzvas asei* of *tzitzis* is fulfilled. In both cases, violating the prohibition and fulfilling the positive commandment are a single act.

By contrast, when someone cuts off *tzara'at* in order to later perform *avodah*, the two acts are not simultaneous. Cutting off the *tzara'at* is not itself the performance of *avodah*. Therefore, the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* cannot be invoked to permit cutting off even a *tahor tzara'at* for the sake of *avodah*.

### No Weightier Status

The conclusion follows: the fact that *tzara'at* is not cut off even to enable *avodah* proves nothing about the relative weight of the two prohibitions. It reflects only the absence of direct confrontation, not any hierarchy of severity. *Tzara'at* is therefore not demonstrably weightier than Shabbat, and the transitive argument that would have undermined the *kal vachomer* is itself undermined on both grounds: the *mechusar tevillah* answer (even after cutting, the person cannot perform *avodah* today) and Rav Ashi's answer (the two do not directly confront each other in any case).

## Tannaim Debate

### Rabbi Yoshiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״בָּשָׂר״ וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם בַּהֶרֶת — ״יִמּוֹל״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, שַׁבָּת חֲמוּרָה — דּוֹחָה, צָרַעַת — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **is** a dispute of ***tanna'im*, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: The term **flesh** comes to teach that **even though there is a bright white spot there, one should circumcise;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says: There is no need** for this derivation from the word flesh in the verse. Rather, the same law can be derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference: If circumcision **overrides Shabbat,** which is **stringent, all the more so** that it overrides **leprosy.**

The Gemara concludes by noting that the underlying dispute between Rava and Rav Safra reflects a *Tannaitic* disagreement, as a *braita* on daf 83b records. Rabbi Yoshiya derives from the word *besar* that *milah* overrides *tzara'at* even when *tzara'at* is present — specifically relying on the textual derivation, since the logical arguments on each side are insufficiently decisive.

### Rabbi Yonatan

Rabbi Yonatan argues that no explicit derivation is necessary for the case of eighth-day *milah*: since eighth-day *milah* overrides Shabbat, which is the weightier prohibition, it certainly overrides *tzara'at* — *Shabbat chamura docha tzara'at lo kol she'ken*. For Rabbi Yonatan, the *kal vachomer* is sufficient for the eighth-day case, and the word *besar* comes to address something else entirely — namely, that *milah* outside the prescribed eighth-day time (*shelo bizmano*) also overrides *tzara'at*, a case that could not be derived from the *kal vachomer* alone. This neatly accounts for the use of the word *besar* even in Rabbi Yonatan's view.

The sugya thus covers substantial ground: the question of where *milah* overriding Shabbat is derived (*halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, various *gezeirot shavot*, and *bayom*), the parallel question of *milah* overriding *tzara'at* (derived from *besar* and extended via *tzad hashaveh* to all cases of *milah*), and the structural question of why the transitive chain of *tzara'at–avodah–Shabbat* does not establish *tzara'at* as weightier than Shabbat, with Rav Ashi's direct-confrontation principle serving as the capstone of the discussion.
