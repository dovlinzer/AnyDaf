# Shevuot 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Witnesses & Denial

### Intro & Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּפַר אֶחָד וְהוֹדָה אֶחָד כּוּ׳. הַשְׁתָּא בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ קָא כָפְרִי – אָמְרַתְּ הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב וְהַשֵּׁנִי פָּטוּר; כָּפַר אֶחָד וְהוֹדָה אֶחָד מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **one** of the two witnesses **denied** knowledge of the incident, **and** the other **one admitted** that he had knowledge and proceeded to testify, **the one who denies** knowledge of the incident is **liable.** The Gemara asks: Why was it necessary to teach this *halakha* in the mishna? **Now** that in the previous case, where the potential witnesses **both** denied knowledge of the matter **one after the other, you said: The first is liable and the second is exempt,** in a case where **one denied** knowledge **and one admitted** that he had knowledge and proceeded to testify, **is** it **necessary** to mention that the one who denied knowledge is liable?

The daf picks up at the bottom of 32b, continuing the treatment of *shevuat edut*. Several principles have already been established. The *kefira* — the denial of knowledge of testimony — must take place in *beit din*, the place where the *edut* is actually relevant; the *shevua* itself, however, may have been taken outside of *beit din*, and the denial inside *beit din* still renders one *chayav*. A further requirement is that the *edut* being denied must be the kind capable of extracting or protecting money. This means it must be the testimony of *shnei edim*, since only two witnesses carry the legal weight to compel payment.

The complication, of course, is how one arrives at *shnei edim* when each person takes a *shevua* individually. Either they take their *shevuot* simultaneously or within *toch kedei dibbur* of one another, or the first witness to take the *shevua* undermines the sufficiency of the remaining testimony — he has, as it were, destroyed the *edut*, and thus bears the *chiyuv*. By the time the second witness takes his *shevua*, the remaining testimony is only that of *ed echad*, which is insufficient to compel payment.

There was, to be sure, a significant position advanced in an earlier *Gemara* — that of Rabbi Elazar BeRabbi Shimon — who held that even an *ed echad* is *chayav*. Two approaches were entertained: first, that *ed echad* might itself obligate payment; second, and the one the *Gemara* settled on, that even if *ed echad* only obligates a *shevua*, since the defendant might choose not to take that *shevua* and thereby trigger a monetary loss, the testimony carries sufficient potential weight to fall under *shevuat edut*. But we proceed according to the *Chachamim*, who hold that *ed echad* is not *chayav*. The upshot is that *chiyuv* falls either on both witnesses when they take their *shevuot* together, or on the first of the two when they act sequentially.

### Simultaneous vs Sequential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁכָּפְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן, וְחָזַר אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְהוֹדָה בְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר; וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר כְּדִיבּוּר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where both of them denied** knowledge of the matter, **and one of them retracted** his denial **and admitted** to knowledge of the matter **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase. **And this teaches us that** the halakhic status of a pause or retraction **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase **is like** that of continuous **speech.**

The *Mishna* states: *kafar echad ve-hoda echad* — one witness denied and one admitted. The *Gemara* raises the obvious question. If we have already established that when both deny sequentially the first is *chayav* and the second is *patur* — even though the second also denied — because the first had already nullified the *edut*, then surely it is self-evident that if one denies and one admits, the one who denied is *chayav* and the one who admitted is *patur*. What does the *Mishna* need to add?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzericha*. The *Mishna* is not describing the simple case. Rather, it addresses a scenario in which they *both* denied — *she-kaferu shneihem* — and then one of them retracted his denial *be-toch kedei dibbur*.

### Toch Kedei Dibbur

This picks up directly on the earlier discussion of *toch kedei dibbur* — the principle that acts performed within the brief span of completing a phrase (*shalom alecha rabbi*) are considered part of a single continuous speech act, and that a retraction made within this window remains legally effective. So: suppose Charlie denies first, making him the one who bears the *chiyuv* for the *korban*; David then denies, but at that point his denial carries no legal weight since Charlie's denial has already nullified the *edut*. If Charlie then retracts his denial within *toch kedei dibbur*, Charlie's denial is undone, and David is now left as the operative denier — even though he took the *shevua* second. The *Gemara* formulates this: *toch kedei dibbur ke-dibbur dami* — what falls within the time of speech is treated as a single continuous act.

### Rav Chisda & R.Y.G.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּמוֹקֵי לַהּ לְהָהוּא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – רֵישָׁא אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וְסֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר כְּדִיבּוּר דָּמֵי. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – רֵישָׁא תּוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר, סֵיפָא תּוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר! תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rav Ḥisda, who interprets** the previous case in the mishna in which the two witnesses denied knowledge of the incident as one, and they are liable **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** a different *halakha* may be learned from each clause in the mishna. It is learned from **the first clause** that it is **possible** for two events **to coincide precisely, and the latter clause was necessary to teach us that** the halakhic status of a pause or retraction **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase **is like** that of continuous **speech. But according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** the *halakha* of: **Within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase, is learned from **the first clause,** and the *halakha* of: **Within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase, is learned from **the latter clause. Why do I** need **two** clauses to teach the same *halakha*?

The *Gemara* now explains why this is significant given the structure of the *Mishna*. According to Rav Chisda, the *reisha* of the *Mishna* — both witnesses denying simultaneously — was interpreted as following Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who holds that it is possible for two acts to occur literally at the same time, or at least to be treated *halakhically* as simultaneous. On that reading, the *reisha* had not yet introduced the concept of *toch kedei dibbur* at all. The *seifa* of the *Mishna* is therefore needed to introduce *toch kedei dibbur* as its own operative principle — teaching that a retraction within that window is legally effective and can shift the *chiyuv* from one witness to the other.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan, however, disagrees. He holds that strict simultaneity is legally impossible, and therefore the *reisha* of the *Mishna* — which describes both witnesses as *chayav* when they deny together — must already be speaking of *toch kedei dibbur*. If the *reisha* already invokes *toch kedei dibbur*, then the *seifa* seems to repeat the same principle. As the *Gemara* frames it: *reisha toch kedei dibbur, seifa toch kedei dibbur — tarti lamah li?* Why do you need both halves of the *Mishna* if each one is teaching the same thing?

The *seifa*, after all, says that one denied and one admitted — of course the one who denied is *chayav*. The only novelty would be if the one who ultimately admitted had initially denied and then retracted *be-toch kedei dibbur*. But that principle has already been introduced in the *reisha*. So what does the *seifa* add?

### Daisy-Chaining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי כְּפִירָה וּכְפִירָה, אֲבָל כְּפִירָה וְהוֹדָאָה – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Even according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, both clauses are necessary. **Lest you say** that **this statement:** The halakhic status of a pause or retraction within the time required for speaking a short phrase is like that of continuous speech, applies only in a case where the first statement is **a denial and** the second statement is **a denial; but** in a case where the first statement is **a denial and** the second statement is **an admission, say** that **no,** its halakhic status is not like that of continuous speech and one cannot admit knowledge after having denied it. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that even in a case where he retracts the denial and admits his knowledge, its halakhic status is not like that of continuous speech.

The *Gemara* explains the mechanism that makes this question necessary. The principle of *toch kedei dibbur* had been understood as operating in a "daisy-chaining" fashion: witness one speaks and finishes; witness two begins within one or two seconds of witness one's conclusion. That brief overlap causes witness two's entire speech to be treated as a continuation of witness one's speech-act. And as long as that continuity holds, witness one retains the ability to retract — even after witness two has begun speaking, because it is all treated as one extended act.

## Deny & Admit Mix

### Contradiction Case

But this raises the question: perhaps that daisy-chaining logic applies only when both witnesses are saying essentially the same thing — *kefira u-kefira*, both denying. One might argue that *toch kedei dibbur* creates a unified speech-act only when the two speakers are delivering a coherent, consistent message. If witness one says, effectively, "I don't know of any testimony," and witness two says, "I do know testimony," they are contradicting each other. Perhaps in that case the second statement breaks the chain and the framework of *toch kedei dibbur* does not link them into a single act.

This is precisely the teaching of the *seifa*: *mah de-teima hani mili kefira u-kefira* — one might think this rule holds only when both are denying — *aval kefira ve-hoda'ah* — but when one denies and one admits, the linkage does not apply. *Ka-mashma lan* — the *Mishna* therefore teaches that it does apply even in that case. Even when the two witnesses end up delivering contradictory messages, the *toch kedei dibbur* principle still operates: the retraction is valid, and whoever is left standing as the denier bears the *chiyuv*.

### Redundancy Question

One might note that the case described in the *Mishna* is somewhat more nuanced than a pure case of one denying and one admitting from the outset. In the actual scenario, both witnesses initially denied, and it is only within the *toch kedei dibbur* window that one of them retracted and switched from denial to admission. Since they began by saying the same thing, the initial combination of their speech acts was already established; the retraction then shifts things. The *Gemara*'s point, however, is that the *Mishna* is teaching that even when the witnesses ultimately land in contradictory positions — one left denying, one having admitted — the framework of *toch kedei dibbur* remains intact, and the witness who retracted is not treated as having caused a break in the chain.

## Two Groups Case

### First Group Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים, כָּפְרָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ כָּפְרָה הַשְּׁנִיָּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁנִיָּה תִּתְחַיֵּיב – דְּכָפְרָה לַהּ רִאשׁוֹנָה; אֶלָּא רִאשׁוֹנָה אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **there were two sets of witnesses** that took the oath of testimony, and **the first** set **denied** knowledge of the matter **and thereafter, the second** set **denied** knowledge of the matter, both sets of witnesses are liable. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the **second** set **will be held liable, as** since **the first** set **denied** knowledge of the matter, the ability of the plaintiff to collect his monetary claim is dependent exclusively on the second set, and their denial caused his loss. **But why** is the **first** set liable?

The *Gemara* continues to the next clause: *hayu shtei kitei eidin* — there were two groups of witnesses. The *Mishna* states that if both groups denied the testimony sequentially, both are *chayav*. The *Gemara* will interpret this *Mishna* quite differently from what its plain meaning seems to convey.

On the surface, the *Mishna* appears to be teaching that if you deny someone a testimony that was *sufficient* for their case — even if that testimony was not *necessary*, because another group of witnesses could also have supplied it — you are still *chayav*. Denying sufficient testimony is enough to create a *chiyuv* even when the claimant had alternative avenues. That seems to be the straightforward message.

But the *Gemara* resists this reading. It asks: granted, the second group is *chayav*, since after the first group denied, the entire case rested on the second group's testimony. *Bishlama shniya titchayiv* — the second group's *chiyuv* is clear. But why is the first group *chayav*? The second group was still present and available — *haka kayma shniya*, the second group is standing right there at the top of 33a. If the testimony of the first group is redundant, their denial did not deprive the claimant of anything; he could simply turn to the second group. The *Gemara* thus rejects the *pshat* of the *Mishna* and insists that mere sufficiency without necessity does not create a *chiyuv*.

### Pasul Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָיְימָא שְׁנִיָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **But** the **second** set of witnesses still **stands** available to testify, so what loss did the first set cause the plaintiff when they denied knowledge of the matter?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׁנִיָּה בִּשְׁעַת כְּפִירַת רִאשׁוֹנָה קְרוֹבִין בִּנְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן, וּנְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן גּוֹסְסוֹת; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רוֹב גּוֹסְסִים לְמִיתָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא שְׁכֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the two witnesses in the **second** set **were relatives through their wives at the time of the denial of the first** set of witnesses. The two witnesses in the second set were married to two sisters, and brothers-in-law are unfit to serve as witnesses together. Therefore, the claim of the plaintiff is entirely dependent upon the testimony of the first set of witnesses. **And** the sisters who were **wives** of the witnesses in the second set **were moribund. Lest you say** that since, presumably, the **majority** of individuals who are **moribund will** actually **die** very soon, and therefore these two witnesses are fit to testify together, therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that since **now in any event,** the wives **had not** yet **died,** their husbands remain unfit to testify together.

Ravina resolves the difficulty: the case in the *Mishna* must be one in which the second group of witnesses was actually *pasul* at the time the first group denied. Therefore the first group's testimony was not merely sufficient but was, at that moment, the *only* valid testimony available — the second group could not have served as an alternative.

### Wives' Deaths

The specific example Ravina gives is that the two witnesses in the second group were married to sisters. Two witnesses who are brothers-in-law — related through their wives who are sisters — are disqualified from testifying together in the same case. Their wives were also, at the time of the first group's denial, *gossot* — on their deathbeds. This introduces the principle of *rov gosesin le-mita*: most dying people will die soon. One might have thought that since the wives were expected to die imminently, the second group should be considered effectively *kasher*, on the anticipation that they would shortly become eligible. The *Gemara* rejects this: *hashta miha la shchiv* — right now the wives have not died. Whatever the statistical likelihood, the second group remains *pasul* at this moment, and therefore the first group's testimony was the only valid testimony available. Their denial was a meaningful deprivation, and they are *chayav*.

The *Gemara*'s reinterpretation of the *Mishna* is striking. The *Mishna* itself appears to be saying that redundancy is irrelevant — you are *chayav* for denying testimony that was sufficient even if not necessary. The *Gemara* insists the *Mishna* must be read as a case where the second group was *pasul* — because otherwise there would be no *chiyuv* for the first group. This is a significant departure from the plain sense of the text.

### Tosafot Solution

*Tosafot* finds a middle ground. On the phrase *haka kayma shniya* — Ravina's answer — *Tosafot* notes that the *Mishna* may in fact be read according to its plain meaning, but with a nuance. *Tosafot* distinguishes between alternative witnesses who are immediately present and accessible, and alternative witnesses who exist but are not accessible at the moment — perhaps in a different town. If the second group of witnesses is right there, physically present and available, then the first group's denial is truly redundant and they are *patur*. But if the second set of witnesses exists somewhere else and is not immediately accessible to the claimant, then the first group's denial is a meaningful deprivation, and they are *chayav*.

This reading preserves something of the *Mishna*'s plain meaning while accommodating the *Gemara*'s concern about pure redundancy. The *chiyuv* depends not on a binary of necessary versus sufficient, but on the practical accessibility of the alternative testimony. When the second group is right there before the court, you are exempt; when they are unavailable even if they technically exist, your denial has deprived the claimant of something real.

## Money Requirement

### Pikadon Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָךְ עֵדוּת״ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that I have in the possession of so-and-so a deposit, and** an outstanding **loan, and a stolen item, and a lost item,** and they lied in reply: On our **oath we do not know** any **testimony on your behalf, they are liable** for taking **only one** false oath of testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לְךָ בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה״ – חַיָּיבִין עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** But if they lied in reply: On our **oath we do not know that you have in the possession of so-and-so a deposit, and** an outstanding **loan, and a stolen item, and a lost item, they are liable for each and every one** of the components of the claim. It is as though they took a separate oath with regard to each of the details of the claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וְתָעִידוּ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי פִּקָּדוֹן חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת״ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify that I have in the possession of so-and-so a deposit of wheat, and barley, and spelt,** and they lied in reply: On our **oath we do not know** any **testimony on your** behalf, **they are liable** for taking **only one** false oath of testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לְךָ בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״ – חַיָּיבִין עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** But if they lied in reply: On our **oath we do not know** any **testimony on your** behalf **that you have in the possession of so-and-so wheat, and barley, and spelt, they are liable for each and every one** of the components of the claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי נֶזֶק, וַחֲצִי נֶזֶק, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶּפֶל, וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְשֶׁאָנַס אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אֶת בִּתִּי, וּפִתָּה אֶת בִּתִּי, וְשֶׁהִכַּנִי בְּנִי, וְשֶׁחָבַל בִּי חֲבֵירִי, וְשֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת גְּדִישִׁי בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that I have in the possession of so-and-so** an outstanding payment for **damage; or** an outstanding payment for **half the damage,** which the owner pays for damage caused by his innocuous ox goring another animal; **or** with regard to an outstanding **payment of double** the principal that a thief must pay the owner of the stolen item; **or** with regard to an outstanding **payment of four or five** times the principal that a thief pays when he stole a sheep or an ox, respectively, and then slaughtered or sold it; **or** in a case where the plaintiff said: I administer an oath to you concerning your refusal to testify if you do not come and testify **that so-and-so raped my daughter; or, he seduced my daughter; or, that my son struck me; or, that another injured me; or, that he set my stack of grain on fire on Yom Kippur;** if in any of these cases the witnesses took an oath falsely denying any knowledge of the matter on behalf of the plaintiff, **these** witnesses **are liable** for taking a false oath of testimony.

The *Gemara* now directly borrows the language of the *shevuat pikadon* passage and restates it in the context of *shevuat edut*: "I impose an oath on you — if you do not come and testify for me that so-and-so has of mine a *pikadon*, or *tsumet yad*, or *gezel*, or *aveidah*." These are the four categories drawn from the *pasuk*. The witnesses then say: "*shevuah she-ein anu yodim lecha edut*" — we swear we have no testimony for you. If they made this generic denial in one *shevua*, they are *chayav* only one *korban*, *ein chayavin ela achas*, even if the denial technically covered all four categories.

But if they enumerated the objects specifically — *shevuah she-ein anu yodim she-yesh lecha be-yad ploni pikadon u-tsumet yad u-gezel va-aveidah* — naming each category, then they are *chayav al kol achat ve-achat*, a separate *korban* for each. This parallels the earlier discussion of *shevuat bitui*, where objects separately named are treated as separate *shevuot* even if only one word of *shevua* was uttered.

The *Mishna* then extends this to the sub-case of a single category with multiple objects. If I say: come and testify that so-and-so holds a *pikadon* for me consisting of wheat, barley, and spelt — *chitin use'orin ve-kusmin* — and they say generically we know no testimony, they are *chayav* only one *korban*. But if they named each item — wheat and barley and spelt — then they are *chayav* separately for each.

### Generic vs Specific

The crucial distinction is between a generic denial and a specific one. A generic *shevua* covering multiple categories or multiple objects produces a single *chiyuv*. Once the denier specifies each object or category explicitly, the law treats each denial as a separate *shevua* carrying its own *chiyuv*. This mirrors the principle seen in *shevuat bitui*, where separate enumeration creates separate *chiyuvim* regardless of whether the oath was formally repeated.

One might ask about the case of multiple *korbanot* in the same breath — that comes from separately naming categories, analogous to *shevuat bitui* where multiple named items generate separate obligations even under one formal oath.

### Knas Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי נֶזֶק, וַחֲצִי נֶזֶק, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶּפֶל, וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְשֶׁאָנַס אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אֶת בִּתִּי, וּפִתָּה אֶת בִּתִּי, וְשֶׁהִכַּנִי בְּנִי, וְשֶׁחָבַל בִּי חֲבֵירִי, וְשֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת גְּדִישִׁי בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the plaintiff said to the witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my behalf that I have in the possession of so-and-so** an outstanding payment for **damage; or** an outstanding payment for **half the damage,** which the owner pays for damage caused by his innocuous ox goring another animal; **or** with regard to an outstanding **payment of double** the principal that a thief must pay the owner of the stolen item; **or** with regard to an outstanding **payment of four or five** times the principal that a thief pays when he stole a sheep or an ox, respectively, and then slaughtered or sold it; **or** in a case where the plaintiff said: I administer an oath to you concerning your refusal to testify if you do not come and testify **that so-and-so raped my daughter; or, he seduced my daughter; or, that my son struck me; or, that another injured me; or, that he set my stack of grain on fire on Yom Kippur;** if in any of these cases the witnesses took an oath falsely denying any knowledge of the matter on behalf of the plaintiff, **these** witnesses **are liable** for taking a false oath of testimony.

The *Mishna* then introduces a set of cases where the monetary obligation in question is not straightforward compensation but takes the form of a *knas* — a penalty or fine. The cases listed are:

*Nezek* — full damages — which is ordinary *mamon*. *Chatzi nezek* — half damages — which in the context of a *tam* ox may be considered a *knas*. *Tashlumei kefel* — double payment imposed on a thief — which is certainly a *knas*. *Tashlumei arba'ah va-chamisha* — four or five times payment when the thief also slaughtered or sold the stolen animal — again a *knas*. *She-anas ish ploni et biti u-fita et biti* — that a certain man raped or seduced my daughter — with a *knas* of fifty *shekel*. *She-hikani bni* — my son struck me — without *chavalah* (wounding), because if it were wounding that would be *chayav misa* and *kim leih*. And finally: *she-chaval bi chaveri ve-hidlik gdishi be-Yom HaKippurim* — my friend wounded me or burned my haystack on *Yom HaKippurim*. The author of this *Mishna* holds that for a *chayav karet* — unlike a *chayav misa* — there is still a monetary obligation and *kim leih* does not apply. The *Mishna* states that in all these cases the witnesses are *chayavin* — the *knas* element does not exempt them, *knas* being treated as *mamon* for these purposes.

## Knas Testimony

### Knas Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קְנָס, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In a case where **one administers an oath to witnesses concerning a fine** if they do not come and testify that one performed an action that renders him liable to pay a sum fixed by the Torah as penalty for that action, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it like any other case of an oath administered to witnesses in monetary matters or not?

The *Gemara* raises the question: *mashpia edei knas mahu* — when a person adjures witnesses to testify that another owes him a *knas*, and they deny, are they *chayav* a *korban*? On the surface this seems to be exactly what the *Mishna* just taught. But the *Gemara* will scrutinize whether the *Mishna* actually establishes this.

### R.E.B.R.S. View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּאָמַר: יָבוֹאוּ עֵדִים וְיָעִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refines the dilemma: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, do not raise the dilemma, as he says** in a case where one performed an action in the presence of witnesses for which he is liable to pay a fine, and before the witnesses testified, he admitted his liability, even though the principle is: One who admits that he is liable to pay a fine is exempt from paying the fine, **the witnesses shall come and testify** and render the one who performed the action liable to pay the fine. In his opinion, payment of the fine is dependent upon their testimony, and therefore if they lie, they are liable for taking a false oath of testimony.

First the *Gemara* clarifies: *aliba de-Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon lo tiba'ei lach* — according to Rabbi Elazar BeRabbi Shimon, this is not a question at all. The relevant rule here is that one who admits to a *knas* is exempt from paying it — the principle that there is no self-incrimination for a punitive fine. The question, then, is whether such an admission permanently neutralizes the obligation, or merely fails to activate it. Rabbi Elazar BeRabbi Shimon holds that admission merely fails to activate: it does not get one off the hook if witnesses subsequently appear. In his view, witnesses to a *knas* can always render the person liable even after he has admitted. Therefore their testimony is at all times capable of compelling payment, and when they deny it they are straightforwardly *chayav shevuat ha-edut*.

### Rabbanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **as they say:** One who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt,** even if **afterward witnesses came** and testified to his liability. The case in the mishna is one where the plaintiff demanded that the witnesses testify on his behalf before the defendant admitted knowledge of the fine.

The question is acute only *aliba de-Rabbanan* — according to the Rabbis. The Rabbis hold *modeh be-knas ve-achar kach ba'u edim patur* — if the defendant admitted to the *knas* and witnesses subsequently came, the witnesses cannot override the admission; the defendant remains exempt. The difficulty is then the following: when I adjure the witnesses to testify that Michael owes me a *knas*, they might argue that their testimony would not necessarily have secured the money for me — because Michael could have stepped forward and admitted the *knas* himself, preempting the witnesses and rendering himself exempt from payment. Since the outcome depended on whether Michael would confess before they testified, their testimony was not a certain cause of my recovery.

### Definitive Testimony

This argument has a familiar ring. It echoes the earlier debate regarding *gorem le-mamon*: is testimony that might lead to money but does not definitively compel it sufficient to generate a *chiyuv* for *shevuat edut*? The Rabbis had ruled there that *gorem le-mamon lav ke-mamon dami* — causing a potential monetary outcome is not equivalent to a direct monetary outcome — and that therefore witnesses are not *chayav* unless their testimony would definitively have resulted in payment.

The *Gemara* notes, however, that there is a crucial distinction between the *knas* scenario and the earlier *gorem le-mamon* cases. In those earlier cases — the single witness whose testimony needed to be combined with the defendant's refusal to take a *shevua*, or the *sotah* case where the testimony needed to be combined with the wife's refusal to drink the water — the testimony was genuinely just one piece of a multi-part causal chain. Something positive had to happen in addition to the testimony for the money to flow.

In the *knas* case, the testimony alone will secure the money provided the status quo holds — provided Michael does nothing. The witnesses do not need anything additional to happen. The only thing that could preempt their testimony is an active intervention by Michael: he would have to run to *beit din* and confess before they testify. So perhaps the correct framing is the present status: *hashta miha lo odei* — right now Michael has not admitted. At this moment, the testimony is fully operative. Should we not define the legal significance of the testimony by the status quo at the time it was withheld?

This is the unresolved question: does the mere theoretical possibility that Michael could preempt the testimony by confessing first render the testimony non-definitive — like *gorem le-mamon* — and therefore insufficient to generate a *chiyuv*? Or do we assess the testimony by the status quo, in which it would have been decisive, and hold the witnesses *chayav*?

### False Report Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי שֶׁהוֹצִיא אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֵׁם רַע עַל בִּתִּי״ – חַיָּיבִין. הוֹדָה מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita* to resolve the dilemma: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify on my** behalf **that so-and-so slandered my daughter.** In such a case, the witnesses are **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony. If the accused **admitted on his own** that he slandered the young woman, he is **exempt** from paying the fine. The only payment in the case of a husband who falsely accuses his bride of adultery is payment of a fine, and yet the *baraita* says that the witnesses are liable for taking a false oath of testimony.

A *braita* is cited: one adjures witnesses to come and testify that a certain person spread a false report — *shem ra* — about his daughter. This is a pure *knas* case. If they denied the testimony, *chayavin* — they are *chayav*. But if the defendant admitted on his own — *hoda mipi atzmo* — then *patur* — they would be exempt. This *braita* appears to address precisely the open question. Before the defendant's admission, the witnesses are *chayav*; after, they are exempt. The implication is that even though it was theoretically possible at any point for the defendant to admit and preempt the witnesses, so long as he has not actually done so the witnesses' denial is treated as a meaningful deprivation and they are *chayav*.

### Braita Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: יָבוֹאוּ עֵדִים וְיָעִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says:** Even if the one who is liable to pay the fine admits his liability, **the witnesses shall come and testify** and render the one who performed the action liable to pay the fine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר, אֲתָאן לְרַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: If that man **admitted on his own** that he slandered the young woman, he is **exempt** from paying the fine. Ostensibly, in this **we arrive at** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that if the one who is liable admits his liability before the witnesses testify, he is exempt from paying the fine, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ הוֹדָה מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ דְּפָטוּר, אֶלָּא הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים כְּלָל וְהוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The *baraita* in **its entirety is** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, and this** is what the *tanna* **is saying** in the latter clause: **You find** a case of one who **admitted** liability **on his own that** he is **exempt** from paying the fine, **only** in a case **where there are no witnesses at all and he admitted** liability **on his own.** In a case where there are witnesses, he is liable to pay the fine on the basis of their testimony even if he admitted liability.

The *Gemara* objects: *ha mani Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon hi* — this *braita* follows Rabbi Elazar BeRabbi Shimon, who holds that witnesses to a *knas* can render the person liable even after admission. Under his view, the witnesses are always *chayav* when they deny, since their testimony would be effective even after the defendant admitted. The *seifa* of the *braita* — *hoda mipi atzmo patur* — is then interpreted not as the admission neutralizing later witnesses, but as the specific case where the defendant admitted when there were no witnesses at all. In the absence of any witnesses, the admission alone settles the matter; but as long as witnesses exist, they can come and testify even after an admission.

The *Gemara* acknowledges this is a forced reading of the *braita*, which in its plain sense does seem to describe exactly the scenario the question was probing: that prior to admission the witnesses are *chayav*, and after admission they are *patur*, suggesting that the *chiyuv* is assessed at the time of the denial, by the status quo, and that the theoretical possibility of a later preemptive admission does not retroactively negate it. But the *Gemara* will not allow this to resolve the question definitively, attributing the *braita* instead to Rabbi Elazar BeRabbi Shimon. The question according to the Rabbis thus remains open.

## Non-Money Cases

### Mishna Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם, אִם לֹא תָבוֹאוּ וְתָעִידוּ שֶׁאֲנִי כֹּהֵן, שֶׁאֲנִי לֵוִי, שֶׁאֵינִי בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה, שֶׁאֵינִי בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, שֶׁאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן, שֶׁאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי לֵוִי, שֶׁאֵינוֹ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה, שֶׁאֵינוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where the plaintiff said to two witnesses: **I administer an oath to you** concerning your refusal to testify **if you do not come and testify that I am a priest,** or **that I am a Levite,** or **that I am not the son of** a priest and **a divorced woman,** or **that I am not the son of** a priest and **a *ḥalutza*,** or **that so-and-so is a priest,** or **that so-and-so is a Levite,** or **that he is not the son of** a priest and **a divorced woman,** or **that he is not the son of** a priest and **a *ḥalutza*;** in all these cases the witnesses are exempt from liability for taking a false oath of testimony, because these do not involve monetary claims.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאָנַס אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אֶת בִּתּוֹ, וּפִתָּה אֶת בִּתּוֹ, וְשֶׁחָבַל בִּי בְּנִי, וְשֶׁחָבַל בִּי חֲבֵירִי, שֶׁהִדְלִיק גְּדִישִׁי בְּשַׁבָּת״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if the plaintiff said to them: I administer an oath to you concerning your refusal to testify if you do not come and testify **that so-and-so raped his daughter, or** that **he seduced his daughter, or that my son injured me, or that another** person **wounded me** on Shabbat, or **that he set my stack of grain on fire on Shabbat;** in all these cases **these** witnesses **are exempt,** as each case is punishable with the death penalty, and therefore they are cases that do not involve monetary payment.

The next *Mishna* addresses non-monetary testimony — cases where the witnesses are *patur* because the *edut* being withheld does not concern a monetary claim. "I impose an oath on you — come and testify for me that I am a *kohein*, that I am a *levi*, that I am not the son of a *grusha* or a *chalutzah*." These are status questions. Or: "Come and testify that so-and-so is a *kohein*, that so-and-so is not the son of a *grusha* or *chalutzah*." Or: "Come and testify that a certain man raped so-and-so's daughter or seduced so-and-so's daughter." Or: "That my son wounded me." And: "That my friend wounded me, or burned my haystack on *Shabbat*." In all these cases the witnesses are *paturim*.

The *chiddush* of this *Mishna* is layered. The case of someone demanding testimony about their own *kohein* status is genuinely novel: it directly affects that person, there is arguably monetary consequence to it (access to *teruma*, *ma'aser*), and yet it is *patur* because the deprivation is indirect, not a direct monetary claim. The cases involving third parties — testify that Reuven is a *kohein*, that Reuven raped Shimon's daughter — are more straightforward, because the basic rule of *shevuat edut* requires that the demand be made by the *tove'a* personally, the person to whom the testimony is relevant.

### Third Party Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן, דְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי לֵוִי; הָא ״מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיַד פְּלוֹנִי״ – חַיָּיבִין; הָא קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁמְעוּ מִפִּי הַתּוֹבֵעַ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers: **The reason** that the witnesses are exempt is that the plaintiff demanded that they testify **that so-and-so is a priest** or **that so-and-so is a Levite,** which are claims that do not involve monetary payment; **but** were the plaintiff to say: I administer an oath to you concerning your refusal to testify if you do not come and testify that **one hundred dinars** belonging **to so-and-so are in the possession of** another **so-and-so,** they would be **liable. But isn't it taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna (35a): The witnesses are exempt **until they hear** a demand to come and testify directly **from the mouth of the plaintiff,** and here the individual administering the oath is not the plaintiff.

The *Gemara* focuses on the third-party cases. If the *Mishna* states that witnesses who are asked to testify about *ish ploni kohein* — that a certain *other* person is a *kohein* — are *patur*, this might seem obvious since the demand was not made by the party to whom it pertains. The *Gemara* notes, however, that there is a specific *chiddush* here: the case is one where the demand was made through a *harsha'ah* — a power of attorney. One might have thought that if the person to whom the testimony is relevant granted another a formal *harsha'ah*, that agent could make the demand in the principal's name and the witnesses would be *chayav*.

### Qi'yum Leih

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁנִיָּה תִּתְחַיֵּיב – דְּכָפְרָה לַהּ רִאשׁוֹנָה; אֶלָּא רִאשׁוֹנָה אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** the **second** set **will be held liable, as** since **the first** set **denied** knowledge of the matter, the ability of the plaintiff to collect his monetary claim is dependent exclusively on the second set, and their denial caused his loss. **But why** is the **first** set liable?

The cases at the end of the *Mishna* — *she-chaval bi bni* (my son wounded me) and *she-chaval bi chaveri ve-hidlik gdishi be-Shabbat* (my friend wounded me or burned my haystack on *Shabbat*) — are *patur* for a different reason: *kim leih*. Where the perpetrator is subject to a capital or lashes penalty, the monetary obligation is subsumed under the greater penalty. In these cases the witnesses' testimony about the monetary dimension is therefore not testimony about operative *mamon* and generates no *chiyuv*. The *Yom HaKippurim* case in the previous *Mishna* excluded precisely this reasoning: the author there held that a *chayav karet* offense does not trigger *kim leih*, and hence the monetary obligation remains operative.

### Power of Attorney

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּבָא בְּהַרְשָׁאָה. וְהָאָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: לָא כָּתְבִינַן אוֹרַכְתָּא אַמִּטַּלְטְלִי! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּכַפְרֵיהּ, אֲבָל לָא כַּפְרֵיהּ – כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** The reference here is to a case **where** the individual administering the oath **comes with authorization** to demand that the witnesses testify on his behalf. The Gemara asks: **But didn't** the Sages **of Neharde'a say: We do not write an authorization document concerning movable property?** The Gemara answers: **This statement** that one does not write authorization for movable property applies only in a case **where** the respondent already **denied** the claim against him. **But** in a case where the respondent **did not** yet **deny** the claim against him, **we write** authorization even for movable property.

The mechanism of *harsha'ah* as understood in *halakha* deserves attention. When Dov wishes to empower Michael to sue on his behalf and to compel testimony on his behalf, the legal structure involves something between *shlichus* and *hakna'ah* — a combination of agency and transfer. Dov effectively says: that hundred dollars Reuven owes me — I am treating it as though I have transferred it to you. Michael can then sue Reuven in his own name and demand testimony from witnesses as though it were his own claim. When Michael makes the demand of the witnesses, it is as if Dov is demanding it, and the witnesses are *chayav*.

But when the subject matter is a status question — whether Dov is a *kohein* — there is no money to transfer. Dov cannot render Michael the one to whom his *kohein* status is relevant. There is no legal fiction by which Michael acquires Dov's status for purposes of the lawsuit. Therefore the *harsha'ah* does not function, Michael is not treated as Dov, and when he demands the testimony of the witnesses it is not a demand by the *tove'a*, and they are *patur*.

### Harsha'ah Limits

A further constraint on *harsha'ah* applies even in monetary cases. The *Amora'im* of *Nehardea* held that one does not write a *harsha'ah* on *metaltelin* — movable property — once the defendant has denied owing it. The reasoning flows from the legal structure of *harsha'ah*: for the fiction to work, Dov must be able to say plausibly, "I am giving Michael the hundred dollars Reuven owes me." If Reuven has publicly denied the debt, the money is as if inaccessible — one cannot transfer what one has no current grasp of. But if Reuven has not yet denied the debt in court, the money still exists in a legally accessible sense, and Dov may write a *harsha'ah* empowering Michael.

By contrast, if the subject matter is land, the fiction is more easily sustained: the land is visible and present, Dov's ownership claim is tangible, and even after Reuven's denial the court can speak of Dov's land in a concrete sense. Hence *harsha'ah* on land remains valid even after denial.

*Rabbeinu Tam* rules as a matter of practical *halakha* that we do write a *harsha'ah* even on *metaltelin* even after denial. He explains that the reason for the Nehardean restriction was only because such a transfer *mechezi ke-shikra* — looks like a farce, since one appears to be giving something one arguably does not have access to. But *Rabbeinu Tam* holds that we do not in practice concern ourselves with things merely looking like a legal fiction, since the entire device of *harsha'ah* is a legal fiction to begin with. This represents a meaningful *halakhic* adaptation to the commercial realities of the medieval period.

## Money Derivation

### R. Eliezer Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״אוֹאִין״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אוֹאִין״; מָה לְהַלָּן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that the verse** with regard to an oath of testimony **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim? Rabbi Eliezer says:** Multiple instances of the term **"or" are stated here,** with regard to an oath of testimony: "And he hears the voice of an oath and he is a witness or he saw or he knew" (Leviticus 5:1), **and** multiple instances of the term **"or" are stated there,** with regard to an oath on a deposit: "And he deals falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of an outstanding loan, or of robbery, or he exploited his colleague, or he found a lost item" (Leviticus 5:21–22); **just as there,** the verse **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim, so too here,** the verse **is speaking only about** cases involving **a monetary claim.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *tannaitic* derivations of the requirement that *shevuat edut* must concern monetary subject matter. Four separate approaches are presented.

Rabbi Eliezer derives it through a *gezerah shavah* of the word *o* — the repeated "or" connectives found in both *parashiyot*. In *shevuat edut* the *pasuk* reads *ve-hu ed o ra'ah o yada* — multiple *o*'s. In *shevuat pikadon* the *pasuk* reads *o be-tsumet yad o be-gezel o ashak et amito* — again multiple *o*'s. Since the same grammatical pattern appears in both contexts and *shevuat pikadon* concerns exclusively monetary matters, *ne'emar kan o ve-ne'emar le-halan o mah le-halan eino medaber ela bi-tvi'at mamon af kan eino medaber ela bi-tvi'at mamon* — just as the *pikadon* case speaks only of monetary claims, so too *shevuat edut*.

### Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹאִין״ דְּרוֹצֵחַ יוֹכִיחוּ – שֶׁהֵן ״אוֹאִין״, וּמְדַבְּרִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The multiple instances of the term **"or" that** are stated with regard to **a murderer:** "And if he shoved him with hatred or cast upon him any vessel without lying in wait or in enmity smote him with his hand" (Numbers 35:20–21), **will prove** that multiple instances of the term "or" are unrelated to limiting the application of the *halakha* to cases involving a monetary claim, **as** these **are** multiple instances of the term **"or"** in verses **that are not speaking about** cases involving **a monetary claim.**

The *Gemara* challenges this derivation. The same connective *o* appears in the case of murder — *o be-eivah hikahu o hishlich alav bi-tzdiya* — and murder is obviously not about monetary claims. Should we then learn from murder that *shevuat edut* can apply to non-monetary testimony?

The answer distinguishes the cases: *danim o she-yesh imahem shevuah me-o she-yesh imahem shevuah* — we derive one *shevua* context from another *shevua* context. Both *shevuat edut* and *shevuat pikadon* involve oaths, and they appear adjacent in the Torah. We do not derive from the murder passage, which has nothing to do with oaths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין ״אוֹאִין״ שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה מֵ״אוֹאִין״ שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה, וְאַל יוֹכִיחוּ ״אוֹאִין״ דְּרוֹצֵחַ – שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One infers** multiple instances of the term **"or" with which there is an oath** administered, i.e., an oath of testimony, **from** multiple instances of the term **"or" with which there is an oath** administered, i.e., an oath on a deposit, **and** the multiple instances of the term **"or" that** are stated with regard to **a murderer will not prove** anything to the contrary, **as there is no oath in their** context.

A second challenge: the connective *o* also appears in the *sotah* passage — *o avar alav ruach kinah* — and *sotah* involves an oath (the waters of *sotah*) yet concerns something entirely non-monetary. Should we then learn that *shevuat edut* applies even non-monetarily? The answer here: *danim o she-yesh imahem shevuah ve-ein imahem kohein* — we compare oath cases that do not involve a *kohein*. The *sotah* case uniquely involves a *kohein* who administers the oath. Therefore *shevuat edut* and *shevuat pikadon* — both oaths not administered by a *kohein* — are compared to each other, not to *sotah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹאִין״ דְּסוֹטָה יוֹכִיחוּ – שֶׁהֵן ״אוֹאִין״, וְיֵשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה, וּמְדַבְּרִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The multiple instances of the term **"or" that** are stated with regard to **a *sota*** in the Torah: "Or if the spirit of jealousy came upon him…or a man upon whom a spirit of jealousy came" (Numbers 5:14, 30), **will prove** that multiple instances of the term are unrelated to a monetary claim, **as** these **are** multiple instances of the term **"or," and there is an oath** administered by the priest to the *sota* **in their** context, **and** the verse **is speaking about** cases **not** involving **a monetary claim.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין ״אוֹאִין״ שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵין עִמָּהֶן כֹּהֵן, מֵ״אוֹאִין״ שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵין עִמָּהֶן כֹּהֵן; וְאַל יוֹכִיחוּ ״אוֹאִין״ דְּרוֹצֵחַ – שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה; וְאַל יוֹכִיחוּ ״אוֹאִין״ דְּסוֹטָה – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהֶן שְׁבוּעָה, יֵשׁ עִמָּהֶן כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **One infers** multiple instances of the term **"or" with which there is an oath** administered **and with which there is no priest** administering it, i.e., the case of an oath of testimony, **from** multiple instances of the term **"or" with which there is an oath** administered **and with which there is no priest** administering it, i.e., an oath on a deposit, **and** the multiple instances of the term **"or" that** are stated with regard to **a murderer will not prove** anything to the contrary, **as there is no oath in their** context, **and** the multiple instances of the term **"or" that** are stated with regard to **a *sota* will not prove** anything to the contrary, **as even though there is an oath in their** context, **there is a priest** administering the oath **in their** context. This is the source from which Rabbi Eliezer proves that liability for an oath of testimony is limited to cases involving a monetary claim.

### R. Akiva Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֶאְשַׁם לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״ – יֵשׁ מֵאֵלֶּה שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, וְיֵשׁ מֵאֵלֶּה שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר. הָא כֵּיצַד? תְּבָעוֹ מָמוֹן – חַיָּיב, דָּבָר אַחֵר – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** that it is written with regard to an oath of testimony: **"And it shall be when he will be guilty of one of these"** (Leviticus 5:5). The term "of these" is a restrictive expression from which it is derived: **There are** some **of these** for **which he is liable and there are** some **of these** for **which he is exempt. How so?** If the plaintiff **demanded** testimony from the witness with regard to **a monetary** claim, the witness is **liable** for taking a false oath; if the plaintiff demanded testimony from the witness with regard to **another matter,** he is **exempt.**

Rabbi Akiva's approach is different. He derives from the phrase at the end of the *shevuat edut* passage — *ve-hayah ki yisham le-achat me-eleh* — that one is *chayav* from "some of these" situations, implying not all. *Yesh me-eleh she-hu chayav ve-yesh me-eleh she-hu patur* — in some cases of this type you are *chayav*, in others *patur*. Rabbi Akiva does not derive the specific content requirement — monetary vs. non-monetary — directly from the *pasuk*; he derives only that not all testimony cases generate a *chiyuv*, and he then rules that the dividing line is monetary versus non-monetary. The *Gemara* will note that this leaves open the question of how we know precisely where to draw the line, since the *pasuk* only tells him that there is a line somewhere.

### R.Y.H. Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדָע״ – בְּעֵדוּת הַמִּתְקַיֶּימֶת בִּרְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה וּבִידִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** cites a different proof and **says:** The verse **states** with regard to an oath of testimony: **"And he is a witness or he saw or he knew"** (Leviticus 5:1). It is **with regard to testimony that is founded on sight,** i.e., direct observation, **without knowledge** of the matter, **or by means of** indirect **knowledge** of the matter **without sight** that **the verse is speaking.**

Rabbi Yose HaGlili takes a fundamentally different approach, deriving the monetary requirement from the phrase *ve-hu ed o ra'ah o yada* — he is a witness, or he saw, or he knew. This phrasing, he argues, encompasses testimony of a certain distinctive character: *edut ha-mitkayemet be-re'iah belo yedi'ah* — testimony that consists of seeing something without knowing its legal significance — and *o be-yedi'ah belo re'iah* — or knowing something without having directly witnessed it. These two categories of testimony, he argues, exist only in monetary cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה – כֵּיצַד? ״מָנֶה מָנִיתִי לְךָ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי״, ״יָבוֹאוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְיָעִידוּ״ – זוֹ הִיא רְאִיָּה בְּלֹא יְדִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Sight without knowledge, how so?** It is a case where the creditor claims: **I counted one hundred dinars for you before so-and-so and so-and-so,** and the debtor responds: **Let so-and-so and so-and-so come and testify. This is** a case of **sight without knowledge,** as the witnesses do not know whether the money was counted as a loan, a deposit, or repayment of a loan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְדִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה – כֵּיצַד? ״מָנֶה הוֹדֵיתָה לִי בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי״, ״יָבוֹאוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְיָעִידוּ״ – זוֹ הִיא יְדִיעָה בְּלֹא רְאִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Knowledge without sight, how so?** It is a case where the creditor claims: **You admitted to** owing **me one hundred dinars before so-and-so and so-and-so,** and the debtor replied: **Let so-and-so and so-and-so come and testify. This is** a case of **knowledge** of the debt **without sight** of the loan taking place. Testimony based on sight without knowledge or based on knowledge without sight is possible only in cases involving a monetary claim.

*Re'iah belo yedi'ah* — seeing without knowing — the *Gemara* illustrates as follows: you watched as someone counted out a hundred dollars and handed it to Michael. You witnessed the transaction but do not know whether it was a gift, a repayment, or a loan. I tell you: come to court and testify to what you saw. In the monetary context, your testimony is relevant: Michael may then be pressured to admit that the transaction was a loan. The court can work with testimony about a transaction even without knowing the parties' intent at the time, because subsequent admissions can supply the missing context. In a capital case, by contrast, partial observation without knowledge of legal significance is generally not usable testimony, because courts require objective legal determinations, not testimony that relies on subsequent admission.

*Yedi'ah belo re'iah* — knowing without seeing — is illustrated by: Michael, you admitted to me in front of Charlie and David that you owed me a hundred dollars. I now adjure Charlie and David to come and testify. They did not observe the original transaction; they merely heard Michael's admission. In a monetary case, this constitutes valid testimony — they know about the obligation through Michael's admission even if they never saw the loan. In a capital context, knowledge without direct observation of the act is not valid testimony for the charge itself.

Since both forms of testimony — seeing without knowing, and knowing without seeing — occur only in monetary contexts, *shevuat edut* is thereby limited to those contexts.

### R. Shimon Kal V.C.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חִיֵּיב כָּאן וְחִיֵּיב בְּפִקָּדוֹן; מָה לְהַלָּן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** cites a different proof and **says:** The Torah rendered one **liable** if he takes a false oath **here** with regard to testimony, **and** the Torah rendered one **liable** if he takes a false oath **with regard to a deposit; just as there,** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim, so too here,** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד – קַל וָחוֹמֶר: מָה פִּקָּדוֹן – שֶׁעָשָׂה בּוֹ נָשִׁים כַּאֲנָשִׁים, קְרוֹבִים כִּרְחוֹקִים, פְּסוּלִים כִּכְשֵׁרִים; וְחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן; עֵדוּת שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהּ נָשִׁים כַּאֲנָשִׁים, קְרוֹבִים כִּרְחוֹקִים, פְּסוּלִין כִּכְשֵׁרִים; וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּתְבִיעַת מָמוֹן?!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** one can infer this *halakha* by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference from the *halakha* of a deposit. **If** in the case of **a deposit, with regard to which** the Torah **rendered** the halakhic status **of women like** that of **men,** the halakhic status of **relatives like** that of **non-relatives,** and the halakhic status of **unfit** witnesses **like** that of those **fit** to testify, **and** he is **liable for each and every** oath if the plaintiff administered several oaths to him and he denied having the deposit in his possession, whether he took the oath **before a court or not before a court,** and despite the broad application of the *halakha*, the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim,** then in the case of an oath of **testimony with regard to which** the Torah **did not render** the halakhic status of **women like** that of **men,** the status of **relatives like** that of **non-relatives,** and the status of **unfit** witnesses **like** that of those **fit** to testify, **and he is liable** to bring **only one** sliding-scale offering if the plaintiff administered several oaths to him and he falsely denied knowledge of the matter **in the presence of a court, is it not right that** the verse **is speaking** of liability **only in** cases involving **a monetary claim?**

The final approach belongs to Rabbi Shimon, who constructs a *kal va-chomer*. He begins with a comparison — somewhat analogous to Rabbi Eliezer's *o-o* link — establishing that *shevuat edut* and *shevuat pikadon* are compared, and that *shevuat pikadon* is monetary. He then builds a *kal va-chomer*: *shevuat pikadon* is broader in scope — it applies to women as it applies to men, *nashim ke-anashim*; to relatives as to non-relatives, *kerovim ke-rechokim*; to those who are otherwise unfit witnesses as to those who are fit, *pesulim ke-kasherim*; and one is *chayav* multiple times for *shevuat pikadon* even outside of *beit din*. And yet, despite all this breadth, *shevuat pikadon* speaks only of monetary matters.

*Shevuat edut*, by contrast, is far more restricted in its application: it does not apply to women as it does to men; it does not apply to relatives as to non-relatives; it does not apply to those who are unfit for testimony; and one is *chayav* only once and only in the context of *beit din*. *Eino din she-lo yehei medaber ela bi-tvi'at mamon* — it stands to reason that this more restricted category of testimony likewise — and indeed all the more so — speaks only of monetary matters. The *kal va-chomer* thus reinforces and derives the same conclusion: the subject matter of *shevuat edut* is limited to monetary claims.
