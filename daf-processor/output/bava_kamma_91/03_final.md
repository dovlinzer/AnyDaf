# Bava Kamma 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Murder Weapon Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וְהִכָּה אִישׁ אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בְּאֶבֶן אוֹ בְאֶגְרֹף" – שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים, אַף כֹּל שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים; פְּרָט לְשֶׁיָּצְתָה מִתַּחַת יַד הָעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In the case of what is stated in the Torah concerning injuries: "And if men contend, **and one smite the other with a stone, or with his fist"** (Exodus 21:18), **Shimon HaTimni says: Just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that it was the fist used to strike, as a fist remains attached to the assailant, **so too,** a ruling can be issued in the case of **any** item **that is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that this was the item used to strike? This serves to **exclude** a case **where** the stone that injured **left the possession of the witnesses** and is not available to be inspected by the court to assess if it is capable of causing the purported injury.

### Direct Inspection View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וְכִי בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין הִכָּהוּ – שֶׁיּוֹדְעִין כַּמָּה הִכָּהוּ, וְעַל מָה הִכָּהוּ – אִם עַל שׁוֹקוֹ אוֹ צִיפַּר נַפְשׁוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: But** is it the case that in all incidents of injury the assailant **struck him in the presence of the court,** so **that they know** exactly **how** hard **he struck him and on what** part of the body **he struck him,** e.g., **if** he struck him **on his thigh** or **if** he struck him on **the lobe of his heart?** Rather, the court relies on witnesses to testify about the injury. Therefore, the witnesses should be able to testify about the item used as well.

### Witnesses Suffice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הֲרֵי שֶׁדָּחַף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג אוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַבִּירָה וָמֵת – בֵּית דִּין הוֹלְכִין אֵצֶל בִּירָה, אוֹ בִירָה הוֹלֶכֶת אֵצֶל בֵּית דִּין? וְעוֹד, אִם נָפְלָה, חוֹזֵר וּבוֹנֶה?!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** if **there** was one **who pushed another from the top of the roof or from the top of a building, and** the one who was pushed **died** as a result of the fall, do the members of **the court go to the building** to inspect how high it is, **or** does the **building go to the court?** Obviously neither, and the court relies upon the testimony of witnesses who state how tall the building is. **And furthermore, if** the building **collapsed,** must the court **rebuild it** in order to assess its height? The witnesses should then be able to testify about the item used as well.

Shimon HaTeimani reasons as follows: just as the *beit din* could directly inspect the man's fist — which is always present and available for examination — so too must they be able to directly inspect any weapon used to kill. The logic is that if direct inspection of the fist is the standard, then whatever object serves as the instrument of killing must likewise be physically examined by the court itself.

Rabbi Akiva rejects this position on practical grounds. One could never expect the *beit din* to observe all the circumstances under which a murder was committed. Since the court relies on *edim* — witnesses — for everything else regarding the act, it may similarly rely on the witnesses' testimony regarding the murder weapon. The *beit din* need not see it directly.

## Ox Murder & Injury

### Shor Tam & Mu'ad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר תָּם שֶׁהֵמִית וְהִזִּיק – דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְאֵין דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת. מוּעָד שֶׁהֵמִית וְהִזִּיק – דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְחוֹזְרִין וְדָנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. קָדְמוּ וְדָנוּהוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – אֵין חוֹזְרִין וְדָנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In the case of **an innocuous ox that killed** a person **and** subsequently went and **caused damage,** the court **judges it** as a case of **capital law** and the ox is killed, **and** the court **does not judge it** as a case of **monetary law,** despite the damage that it caused. By contrast, in the case of **a forewarned** ox **that killed** a person **and** subsequently went and **caused damage,** the court **judges it** as a case of **monetary law,** and the owner is liable to pay for the damage it caused, **and** then the court **goes back and judges it** again as a case of **capital law,** and the ox is killed. But if the court **proceeded and judged it** first as a case of **capital law,** the court **does not go back and judge it** again as a case of **monetary law,** since it has already been sentenced to be killed.

The *gemara* then introduces a *baraita* that, in some respects, belongs thematically in the earlier portions of Bava Kamma but is brought here for its connection to the preceding discussion. The case: an ox has both murdered a person and caused injury to another. If the ox is a *shor tam* — an ox without prior warning — the injured party will simply not be able to collect, because a *shor tam*'s liability for damage is paid only from its own body (*mi-gufo*), and the ox itself will become a *shor haniskal*, sentenced to execution, rendering it worthless for collection purposes. In such a case, only the *shor haniskal* judgment needs to be carried out, and the injured party loses his claim.

However, if the ox is a *shor mu'ad* — a forewarned ox, whose owner pays cash for damages — one might think the order of adjudication is irrelevant. Yet the *baraita* insists that even here, it is essential to first adjudicate the payment case and only afterward adjudicate the *shor haniskal* case. Reversing the order, the *baraita* says, prevents the injured party from collecting.

### Reversed Order Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי קָדְמוּ וְדָנוּהוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת מַאי הָוֵי? לִיהְדַּר וְלִידַיְינֵיהּ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if they proceeded and judged it** first as a case of **capital law, what of it? Let them go back and judge it** again **also** as a case of **monetary** law.

This prompts the *gemara*'s question: why should reversing the order prevent collection? Once the ox is declared a *shor haniskal*, it will be stoned — but the owner's obligation to pay cash for the injury of a *shor mu'ad* remains. The cash obligation does not depend on the ox's continued existence. So what precisely is lost by adjudicating the *shor haniskal* first?

### Rava's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רָבָא: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב, דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: "מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים". אַלְמָא בָּעֵינַן אוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי דִינָא; וְהָא, כֵּיוָן דִּגְמַר דִּינֵיהּ לִקְטָלָא – לָא מְשַׁהֵינַן לֵיהּ לְאוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי דִינָא, וְלָא מְעַנֵּינַן לְדִינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I found the Sages of the school of Rav who were sitting and saying** to explain this: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon HaTimni, who says** that **just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** to attest that this was the fist used to strike, so too, a ruling can be issued in the case of any item that is submitted to the assembly of judges to assess its ability to injure and to the witnesses to attest that this was the item used to strike. Apparently, we require the evaluation of the court** in order to render him liable to pay for the damage. **And** with regard to **this** ox that killed, **once the verdict** was given that it to be put **to death, we do not delay** the implementation of the verdict in order to wait **for the evaluation of the court, and we do not delay its judgment.** Therefore, the monetary case is not judged.

Rava reports that he found the scholars of the *beit midrash* explaining this *baraita* as reflecting the position of Shimon HaTeimani. According to Shimon HaTeimani, just as the *beit din* must directly inspect the murder weapon, so too must it directly inspect the object that caused the injury — and this applies not only when a person inflicts injury but even when an ox does so. The court must physically bring the ox before it and determine: could this ox, under ordinary circumstances, have caused the kind of damage it did? The question is not whether it in fact caused the damage — that is established — but whether such damage was to be expected from this ox.

This is the key link. Once the ox has already been declared a *shor haniskal*, you cannot ask the court to hold off on executing it for a week while it handles the separate injury case. And that is why, according to Shimon HaTeimani, the order is critical: the injury case must be tried first, before the *shor haniskal* judgment is entered, so that the *beit din* can still directly examine the ox.

## Order & Payment Logic

### Immediate Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּרַח. אִי בָּרַח, כִּי לֹא דָּנוּהוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – הֵיכִי דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בְּלֹא בְּעָלִים? דְּקַבֵּיל סָהֲדִי וּבָרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And I,** Rava, **said to** the Sages of the school of Rav: **Even** if **you say** that this *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who does not require assessment by the court, one could explain that **here we are dealing with** a case **where** the owner of the ox **fled,** and the court cannot judge his case in his absence and cannot render him liable to pay for the damage caused by his ox. The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* is discussing a case where the owner **fled,** then in the case **where they did not** yet **judge it** as a case of **capital law, how does** the court **judge it** as a case of **monetary law without** the **owner** of the ox being present? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the court **accepted** the testimony of the **witnesses** in the presence of the owner, **and** he subsequently **fled.**

The *gemara* explores a further dimension of why the order matters. Once an ox has been declared a *shor haniskal*, it must be executed without delay. One cannot say: "Let us defer the execution for a week so that the injured party's case can also be heard and the *beit din* can inspect the ox." That deferral is simply not permitted.

### Plowing First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, מֵהֵיכָא מִשְׁתַּלַּם – מֵרִידְיָא; אִי הָכִי, תָּם – נְדַיְינֵיהּ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בְּרֵישָׁא וְנִשְׁתַּלַּם מֵרִידְיָא, וַהֲדַר נְדַיְינֵיהּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, from where** are the damages **paid** in a situation where the owner fled, as he is not here to pay the damages? The Gemara answers: They are paid **from plowing.** The ox is rented out to plow fields, and the fees generated are used to pay the damages. Afterward, the ox is judged for the killing as a case of capital law. The Gemara asks: **If so,** with regard to **an innocuous** ox, why did the *baraita* rule that the court does not judge it as a case of monetary law? The court **should judge it** as a case of **monetary law first, and** the damages **will be paid from** fees generated from renting out the animal for **plowing, and then** the court **should judge it** as a case of **capital law.**

The *gemara* therefore proposes that the proper order creates a window of opportunity: if the injury case is adjudicated first, the injured party can be paid by using the ox — putting it to work plowing his fields — and only after the debt has been recouped through that use is the *shor haniskal* judgment carried out.

### Postponement Affliction

What makes postponing the execution impermissible? The *gemara* draws a striking parallel to capital cases involving human beings: just as it is considered a form of anguish and affliction to postpone the execution of a person who has already been sentenced to death, so too it is considered affliction to the ox to defer its execution. This is a remarkably formal application of a human legal concept to an animal. The ox presumably has no awareness that such an order has been issued; the *gemara* itself acknowledges the irony. Yet the formalism serves an important purpose: the entire legal apparatus surrounding the *shor haniskal* — including the deliberative process, the witnesses, the court proceedings — mirrors the procedures for capital cases involving human beings. The Torah was radical in insisting that only the ox, and not the owner or his children, is punished, and by treating the process with the full gravity of a capital case, the legal system underscores that this is no trivial matter.

It follows that once the ox is declared a *shor haniskal*, one cannot allow the injured party to use it for plowing during the waiting period — because keeping it alive to plow constitutes the very postponement that is forbidden.

### Owner Absence

Rava himself then offers an alternative explanation that does not depend on Shimon HaTeimani's position at all, making the ruling consistent even with Rabbi Akiva's view. He posits the following scenario: witnesses were accepted to testify that the ox both murdered and injured — and then the owner fled. One cannot adjudicate a case in the owner's absence; the hearing of witnesses must take place with the owner present, and that has already occurred. But the problem now is different: the owner has absconded with all his assets. The only remaining asset from which any recovery is possible is the ox itself.

The *gemara* presses: if the owner has fled, the problem is not the order of adjudication — it is simply that the owner is not present. How does the order of the cases resolve that? The *gemara* clarifies: the witnesses testified and were received, and then the owner fled. The procedural validity of the testimony is not in question. The operative issue is that there are no other assets from which to collect. This forces the question of which case to adjudicate first, because everything turns on what happens to the ox.

### Payment from Ox

If the injury case is adjudicated first, the injured party can be paid by having the ox plow his field until the debt is satisfied — then the *shor haniskal* judgment proceeds. If the *shor haniskal* judgment is entered first, the ox immediately must be executed, and the injured party is left with nothing to collect from.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: רִידְיָיא – עֲלִיָּיה דְּמָרֵהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, said** in answer to this last question: **That is to say** that the money gained from **plowing is** considered like the **superior-quality** property **of its owner.** In other words, that money is like the rest of the owner's estate, and is not considered to be part of the value of the ox itself. Since payment for damage caused by an innocuous ox is taken only from the value of the ox itself, it follows that money received from renting out the ox to plow cannot be used to pay damages.

The *gemara* then raises an apparent objection: if payment via the ox's plowing works for a *shor mu'ad*, why doesn't the same solution apply to a *shor tam*? By a *shor tam*, the *baraita* states that it makes no difference what order the cases are tried — the injured party cannot collect regardless. But seemingly, the same trick should work: let the injured party use the ox to plow before it is stoned, and collect his payment that way, even though a *shor tam*'s liability is limited to the ox's body (*mi-gufo*). After all, he would be getting paid from the ox's body — through the benefit generated by the ox's labor.

## Plowing Benefit Debate

### Plowing Not Body

This is where Rav Mari bar Rav Kahana enters with a crucial analytical point. He derives from the *gemara*'s discussion: *radya alya de-mari hu* — the benefit derived from the ox's plowing is not considered the ox's body. It is considered an asset belonging to the owner. Therefore, collecting payment through the ox's plowing is not equivalent to collecting *mi-gufo*, from the body of the ox itself.

### Shor Tam Exemption

This distinction explains why the solution does not work for a *shor tam*. A *shor tam*'s liability is specifically *mi-gufo* — from the physical body of the ox, meaning the proceeds from its sale. The benefit derived from putting the ox to work does not count as the body of the ox; it counts as the owner's other assets. Since the *shor tam* regime limits recovery strictly to the ox's body, and the owner has no other accessible assets, the injured party simply cannot be paid. No reordering of the cases can solve this.

### Shor Mu'ad Solution

By contrast, a *shor mu'ad*'s owner is liable in cash from his general assets. The benefit derived from the ox's plowing is treated as the owner's asset — and since the *shor mu'ad* owner pays from his assets, using the plowing benefit as the medium of payment is perfectly valid. Accordingly, adjudicating the injury case first, allowing the injured party to work the ox until the debt is covered, and only then proceeding to the *shor haniskal* judgment, is a workable and legally sound arrangement.

## Object Assessment

### Question Formulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: יֵשׁ אוֹמֶד לִנְזָקִין, אוֹ אֵין אוֹמֶד לִנְזָקִין?
>
> **Translation:** § In connection with the ruling of the *baraita* that the court must assess whether the death can be attributed directly to the action of the assailant, the Gemara notes: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Is there an evaluation** performed **with regard to damage** as well, to determine whether the act was sufficient to inflict that damage, **or** is there **no evaluation** performed **with regard to damage?**

The *gemara* now raises a new question that flows naturally from everything discussed: *yesh omed l'nezikin o ein omed l'nezikin* — when damage is caused, do we assess whether it was reasonable to expect that this damage would occur? In other words, does the same *umdena* — assessment of the capacity of the object — that applies in murder cases also apply in cases of civil damages?

### Murder Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: לִקְטָלָא הוּא דְּאָמְדִינַן – בְּהָכִי נָפְקָא נְשָׁמָה, בְּהָכִי לָא נָפְקָא נְשָׁמָה; אֲבָל לִנְזָקִין – כֹּל דְּהוּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Do we say** that specifically in a case **of killing is where we perform an evaluation?** The assessment is to determine whether **a soul departs with this** assault and the assailant will be held liable or whether **a soul doesn't depart with this** assault and the death is attributed to other factors. **But** in a case of **damage,** perhaps we say that he is liable for **whatever** caused the damage. **Or perhaps** there **is no difference** between the two, and with regard to damage as well, the court evaluates whether the act was sufficient to inflict that damage, and if it was not, he will be exempt.

The framework for murder is clear. The *pasuk* in Bamidbar says explicitly: *asher yamut bo hika'u yamut* — if he smote him with a wood weapon that could kill him, then he shall be put to death. The Torah itself requires that the weapon have the capacity to kill; this is not a *drash* but close to the *pshat* of the verses. If a person was in a fight and struck another with a stone that would not, under normal circumstances, have been lethal — yet in this instance the victim died — the assailant cannot be executed, because the act is characterized as a freak occurrence rather than a genuine act of murder.

This is striking enough in murder. In order for execution to follow, it is not sufficient that the killer intended to kill and that the victim in fact died. The weapon must have had the capacity. Consider the following scenario: a man tries to kill his victim, takes a rock, strikes him, and the victim dies. If *beit din* determines that the rock should not, under ordinary circumstances, have produced that result, the killer goes free — not because he lacked intent, and not because the victim survived, but because the causal mechanism was, legally speaking, anomalous.

The question posed by the *gemara* is whether this principle extends to *nezikin*, civil damages. Imagine striking someone's valuable object with a stone in a way that, under normal circumstances, should not have damaged it — yet damage results. Does the person escape liability on the grounds that the causation was aberrant? The *gemara* frames the question as potentially factual rather than purely legal: perhaps the very nature of damage is that almost anything can cause it, so there is no meaningful standard of "capacity to cause damage" in the first place. Or perhaps — *lo shana* — it is no different from murder, and we do assess.

### Well Depth Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה בּוֹר – שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית, עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים; אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית, עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. הָיוּ פְּחוּתִין מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. הוּזַּק בּוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **Come** and **hear** a proof from what was taught in a mishna (50b) concerning the *halakhot* of a pit: **Just as a pit that has sufficient** depth **to cause death** when falling into it is at least **ten handbreadths** deep, **so too, any** other excavations **that have sufficient** depth **to cause death** may be no less than **ten handbreadths.** If any of the types of excavations **were less than ten handbreadths** deep, **and an ox or a donkey fell into** one of **them and died,** the digger of the excavation is **exempt.** But if it **was injured in it** but did not die he is **liable** to pay damages.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the laws of the *bor* — the pit or well. It is established that a well ten *tefachim* deep can kill an animal that falls in, and the owner is liable for the death. If the well is less than ten *tefachim*, the owner is exempt for the death but liable for any injury the animal sustains. The *gemara* initially reads this as teaching that for any depth — even a single *tefach* — there is liability for *nezikin*, suggesting that no assessment of adequacy is performed: *ein omdin l'nezikin*, there is no capacity assessment for damages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה קָא חָשֵׁיב – וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מִטֶּפַח וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה – מִיתָה לֵיכָּא, נְזָקִין אִיכָּא? אַלְמָא לִנְזָקִין – כֹּל דְּהוּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין אוֹמְדִין לִנְזָקִין!
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** the case that the mishna **is counting** the handbreadths from **below to above, and this** is what it **is saying: From** one **handbreadth** deep **until ten** handbreadths deep, **there is no** liability for **death** but **there is** liability for **damage? Apparently, with regard to damage** he is liable for **whatever** caused the damage, even a fall into an excavation having the depth of one handbreadth. **Learn from** the mishna that the court **does not** perform **an evaluation with regard to damage.**

The *gemara* rejects this reading. The proper reading, it says, moves from the higher depth downward. Ten *tefachim* produces liability for death. Just below ten — say, 9.9 *tefachim* — produces liability for injury but not death. This does not imply that any depth, no matter how shallow, produces liability for injury. It may still be that for a very shallow well, one must assess whether that depth was capable of producing the particular injury that resulted. The proof fails to establish *ein omed l'nezikin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, מִמַּעְלָה לְמַטָּה קָא חָשֵׁיב; וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: עֲשָׂרָה – מִיתָה אִיכָּא; פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה פּוּרְתָּא – נְזָקִין אִיתָא, מִיתָה לֵיכָּא. וּלְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ יֵשׁ אוֹמֶד לִנְזָקִין, וְכֹל מִידֵּי וּמִידֵּי – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמִיתַּזְקָה בֵּיהּ בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the mishna **is counting from above to below, and this** is what it **is saying:** If the excavation is **ten** handbreadths deep then **there is** liability for **death.** If the excavation is **slightly less than ten, there is** liability for **damage** but **there is no** liability for **death. And actually, I will say to you** that **there is an evaluation** performed **for damage, and** for **each and every** circumstance, **we require that** there be conditions **whereby** the injured party **could** reasonably **be damaged by** whatever caused the damage.

### Eye Ear Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִכָּהוּ עַל עֵינוֹ וְסִימְּאוֹ, עַל אׇזְנוֹ וְחֵירְשׁוֹ – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. כְּנֶגֶד עֵינוֹ – וְאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה, כְּנֶגֶד אׇזְנוֹ – וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ, אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵינַן אוּמְדָּנָא – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּה:ּ יֵשׁ אוּמְדָּנָא לִנְזָקִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **Come** and **hear** a proof from what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:26): If a slave owner **struck** his Canaanite slave **on his eye and blinded him,** or **on his ear and deafened him, the slave is emancipated by means of these** wounds. If he struck **near his eye and** as a result **he does not see,** or **near his ear and he does not hear, the slave is not emancipated by means of these** wounds. **What is the reason** that the slave is not emancipated? **Isn't it due to** the fact **that we require an evaluation** of the damage, and in such a case the assumption is that the owner's action did not suffice to cause the injury? **And learn from** the *baraita* that **there is an evaluation** performed **with regard to damage.**

The *gemara* tries again with a case from the laws of the *eved Kena'ani* — the Canaanite slave. If a master strikes his slave directly in the eye and blinds him, or directly in the ear and deafens him, the slave goes free — for the Torah grants freedom to a slave whose limb is knocked out. The *pasuk* uses the language of *ve-shichatah* — "destroyed it" — which the *gemara* understands to include functional destruction, even where the organ itself remains physically intact.

The interesting case is the following: if the master struck the wall *next* to the slave's eye — without actually touching the slave — and the resulting shock caused the slave to lose his sight; or if he struck next to the slave's ear and the slave lost his hearing — in these cases the slave does not go free. The *gemara* asks: what is the reason? It suggests that it must be because *ba'inan umdena* — we require an assessment, and in this case the act of striking near the body (without contact) was not, under normal circumstances, the kind of thing that should have produced blindness or deafness. Therefore, it counts as a freak occurrence, and there is no legal consequence.

This would seem to prove that *yesh omed l'nezikin* — we do assess in damage cases.

### Direct vs Indirect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: הוּא דְּאַבְעֵית נַפְשֵׁיהּ. כִּדְתַנְיָא: הַמַּבְעִית אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם, וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. כֵּיצַד? תָּקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵירְשׁוֹ – פָּטוּר, אֲחָזוֹ וְתָקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵירְשׁוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No;** the slave is not emancipated, **because we say** that it is possible that it was the slave **who frightened himself,** resulting in his blindness or deafness, but it was not the actual strike that caused it. An injury can result from being frightened, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:16): **One who frightens another** and thereby causes him injury is **exempt according to human laws but liable according to the laws of Heaven. How so?** If **one shouted into** another's **ear and deafened him,** he is **exempt** according to human laws but liable according to the laws of Heaven. But if **he held him and shouted into his ear and deafened him,** he is **liable** according to human laws as well, as he physically took hold of him.

The *gemara* rejects this inference as well. The reason the slave does not go free, it explains, is not because of an *umdena* about the object's capacity. It is because *hu d'avise nafshiah* — he frightened himself; he brought it upon himself. When there is no direct physical contact, the legal definition of "causing damage" is simply not satisfied. What happened to the slave was not produced by an act done *to* him; it was his own physical reaction to something done *near* him.

This is a formal legal category, not a factual assessment about likelihood. The *gemara* makes this explicit through a *baraita*: one who frightens his fellow is exempt from human courts but liable in the heavenly court. *Taka be'ozno ve-cheirshah* — if he shouted directly into his ear and caused deafness, he is exempt; *achazo ve-taka be'ozno ve-cheirshah* — if he held the person while shouting into his ear and deaffened him, he is liable. The difference is purely one of physical contact. If there is contact, the act is legally defined as an injury he inflicted. If there is no contact, the act is not legally classified as an injury done to the victim — regardless of how predictably it might cause harm.

This distinction is formally bizarre but halachically consequential. The *gemara* is not saying that blowing out someone's eardrum by yelling at close range is morally innocent; it is saying that without the formal criterion of physical contact, the legal category of *nezikin* does not attach.

### Medical Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים אוֹמְדִין אוֹתוֹ, וְנוֹתְנִין לוֹ מִיָּד. רִיפּוּי וָשֶׁבֶת – עַד שֶׁיִּתְרַפֵּא. אֲמָדוּהוּ, וְהָיָה מִתְנַוְנֶה וְהוֹלֵךְ – אֵין נוֹתְנִין לוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמוֹ שֶׁאֲמָדוּהוּ. אֲמָדוּהוּ וְהִבְרִיא – נוֹתְנִין לוֹ כׇּל מַה שֶּׁאֲמָדוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **Come** and **hear** a proof from what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:3): With regard to the **five types of indemnity** that one who injures another must pay, the court **evaluates** the victim as to the value of the damage that he suffered, **and** the assailant **gives** this payment **to him immediately.** Payment for **medical costs and loss of livelihood** is evaluated from the moment of injury **until he is healed.** If the court **evaluated** that a certain sum would be needed to pay for **his** medical costs and loss of livelihood, **and** subsequently the victim's condition began **progressively deteriorating** and the sum proved to be inadequate, **they give him** payment **only as they had evaluated him. They evaluated him** as requiring a certain sum for medical costs and loss of livelihood **and he healed** sooner than expected, **they** nevertheless **give him** the **entire** sum **as they had evaluated him.**

The *gemara* makes yet another attempt to resolve the *umdena* question. It cites a ruling that the five heads of damages — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *sheves*, and *boshet* — are assessed and paid immediately upon the occurrence of the injury. The notable cases are *ripui* and *sheves*: one does not know in advance how long the recuperation period will last or how large the medical bills will be. Nevertheless, *beit din* assesses the expected cost at the time of judgment and pays that amount immediately.

Furthermore: if the assessment predicted a certain recovery period and the victim recovered more slowly — his bad luck, he receives no more than the assessed amount. If he recovered more quickly — his good luck, he pockets the full assessed amount. Rava adds that if someone was assessed to be incapacitated for a full day and recovered in the afternoon and was able to return to work, he still receives payment for the full day's *sheves*, because the injury itself was judged to be worth a day's lost work; the quicker recovery was a gift from heaven, not a revision of that assessment.

The *gemara* considers whether this proves *yesh omed l'nezikin*: we assess what is reasonable and the victim receives only what the injury should have cost. The *gemara* rejects this proof, however, because there are two entirely different kinds of assessment at issue. One kind of assessment — which is never in dispute — is determining *how much* damage was done once liability is established: what are the medical costs, how long is the recovery, what is the loss of income? Everyone agrees that this assessment takes place. The *umdena* question is entirely different: it asks whether we assess the *object* that caused the damage to determine whether it even had the *capacity* to produce the harm. That second question — *le-omed ḥeftza* — is the one that remains unresolved after all these attempted proofs.

## Assessment Conclusion

### Object Inspection Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים, אַף כֹּל מְיוּחָד שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יֵשׁ אוֹמֶד לִנְזָקִין! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta, Sanhedrin* 12:3): **Shimon HaTimni says: Just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that this was the fist used to strike, **so too,** a ruling can be issued in the case of **any** item **that is submitted to the assembly** of judges to identify its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that this was the item used to strike. **Learn from** the *baraita* that **there is an evaluation** performed **with regard to damage.** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from** the *baraita* that this is so.

The resolution finally arrives from Shimon HaTeimani's own ruling. The *pasuk* about the stone and the fist — *be-even o be-egrof* — is, as noted at the outset, a *pasuk* about injury, not murder. When Shimon HaTeimani says that the *beit din* must directly inspect the weapon — just as it could inspect the fist — and Rabbi Akiva says reliance on witnesses suffices — both of them are speaking about the injury context. The very debate between Shimon HaTeimani and Rabbi Akiva presupposes that *yesh omed l'nezikin*: all agree that an assessment of the object's capacity must be made in injury cases. The only dispute is whether the *beit din* must make that assessment directly, by physically examining the object, or whether the witnesses' testimony about the object is sufficient.

*Shema mina* — this is the conclusion: *yesh omed l'nezikin*. Everyone agrees that in cases of civil damage, just as in murder, an assessment is made of whether the object that caused the harm had the capacity to do so. The debate between Shimon HaTeimani and Rabbi Akiva is solely about who performs that assessment — the *beit din* itself or the *edim*. According to Shimon HaTeimani, the court must see the weapon directly, which is why the ox must be brought before *beit din* for examination in injury cases. According to Rabbi Akiva, the witnesses' account suffices.

It follows that if someone throws a stone at another's property, causes damage, and *beit din* concludes that under normal circumstances that stone should not have caused that result — the perpetrator goes free. This is genuinely surprising in the context of civil law, especially given the principle of *adam mu'ad l'olam* — that a person is always considered forewarned for his own acts and bears liability regardless of negligence or intent. Evidently, the absence of reasonable capacity in the instrument overrides even that principle and breaks the causal chain entirely.

## Spitting & Shame

### Spit Contact Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָקַק – וְהִגִּיעַ בּוֹ הָרוֹק, וְהֶעֱבִיר כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בּוֹ, אֲבָל בְּבִגְדוֹ – לָא. וְנִיהְוֵי כִּי בִיֵּישׁ בִּדְבָרִים!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches (90a): If he **spat** at him **and** his **spittle reached him, or** if **he removed** another's cloak, he must give the injured party four hundred dinars. **Rav Pappa says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where the spittle reached **him. But** if the spittle landed **on his clothing** without touching him, he is **not** required to pay him. The Gemara asks: Why not? **Let it be like** one who **humiliated** another **with words.**

The *mishnah* states that if someone spat at another person and the spit reached (*higi'a bo ha-rok*) — that is, it physically made contact with the person — the spitter is liable for *boshet*, the *mishnah* specifying a payment of 400 *zuz* or the like. The *gemara* now refines this: *lo shanu ela bo* — this applies only if the spit actually struck the person. But *aval be-vigdo* — if it only hit his garments — there is no *boshet* payment.

### Verbal vs Physical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: בִּיְּישׁוֹ בִּדְבָרִים – פָּטוּר מִכְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say in the name of Rabbi Yosei bar Avin: That is to say** that if **he humiliated** another **with words** alone, he is **exempt from** paying **anything,** although he will be held accountable by Heaven for his sin.

This raises an important structural question about *boshet*. The *gemara* asks: if spit hitting the clothing rather than the body generates no liability, is that any different from shaming someone with words alone? And indeed, *amri b'ma'arava mishemei de-Rabbi Yossi bar Avin* — they said in the west in the name of Rabbi Yossi bar Avin: this demonstrates that *biyush bidvarim patur mi-klum* — verbal shaming, with no accompanying physical act, carries no legal liability whatsoever.

This is a striking feature of *hilchot nezikin*. Shaming someone purely through words — publicly mentioning a private matter, calling someone a name in *reshut ha-rabbim* — may be a severe prohibition (the *gemara* elsewhere likens it to murder), but it is not a tort for which damages are paid. The comparison to *motzi shem ra* — defamation, which in American law generates a cause of action for slander or libel — is instructive: in *halacha*, even serious reputational harm caused by words does not give rise to compensable damages. There may be elements that fall under *garmi* or *grama*, but the basic structure does not provide compensation for verbal shaming and slander.

The logic that emerges is parallel to what we saw with *nezikin* from non-contact acts: just as one is not liable for injury unless there was actual physical contact with the victim's body, one is not liable for *boshet* unless one performed a physical act directed at the person. Removing a woman's head covering, for instance, creates liability even though no skin was touched: the covering was on her body, and removing it is legally treated as doing something to her body. But spitting on her clothes is merely affecting her clothing, not her person — it is categorically closer to verbal shaming, and accordingly carries no liability.

One additional point must be noted: *boshet* is not limited to cases where physical injury also occurred. Spitting on someone, pulling off a head covering — these cause no physical harm whatsoever, yet they constitute independent torts for which full damages are owed. The requirement for *kavanah* — intent — applies here as well: the act must be done with the intention to cause embarrassment or to cause harm. But the point is that *boshet* stands entirely on its own as a cause of action, independent of injury.

## Honor & Payment

### Amounts Min or Max

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל לְפִי כְבוֹדוֹ [וְכוּ׳]. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְקוּלָּא קָאָמַר, אוֹ לְחוּמְרָא קָאָמַר? לְקוּלָּא קָאָמַר – דְּאִיכָּא עָנִי דְּלָא בָּעֵי לְמִשְׁקַל כּוּלֵּי הַאי, אוֹ דִלְמָא לְחוּמְרָא קָאָמַר – דְּאִיכָּא עָשִׁיר דְּבָעֵי לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ טְפֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches with regard to the assessment of payment for humiliation caused to another: It is **all** evaluated **in accordance with** the **honor of** the one who was humiliated. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does **the first *tanna*** in the mishna **say** his opinion **as a leniency, or** does **he say** his opinion **as a stringency?** The Gemara explains the two possibilities: **He says** his opinion **as a leniency,** teaching **that there is a poor** person, **who doesn't need to take so much** payment for humiliation, as detailed in the fixed sums of the mishna. **Or perhaps he says** his opinion **as a stringency,** teaching **that there is a rich** person **whom** the assailant **needs to give him more** than those fixed sums.

The *mishnah* provides specific figures for *boshet* payments — 200 *zuz*, 400 *zuz*, and so on — and then adds *ha-kol lefi kevodo*, that all amounts vary according to the honor of the person involved. The *gemara* now asks how to read the relationship between these two elements. Does the *tanna kamma* intend the listed amounts as maximums — meaning that based on a person's lesser honor, one could pay less — or as minimums — meaning that based on a person's greater honor, one would pay more?

### Akiva Full Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲפִילּוּ עֲנִיִּים שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל – רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ הֵן בְּנֵי חוֹרִין שֶׁיָּרְדוּ מִנִּכְסֵיהֶם, שֶׁהֵם בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְקוּלָּא קָאָמַר! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof **from that** which **Rabbi Akiva says** in the mishna: **Even** with regard to the **poor of the Jewish people, they are viewed as though they were freemen who lost their property** and were impoverished, and their humiliation is calculated according to this status, **as they are the sons of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob,** and are all of prominent lineage. **Conclude from** Rabbi Akiva's statement that **the first *tanna* says** his opinion **as a leniency,** meaning that while the first *tanna* states the principle that payment for humiliation is in accordance with the honor of the one who was humiliated, and therefore a poor person will receive less than the fixed sums listed in the mishna, Rabbi Akiva responds that even a poor man is deserving of those fixed sums by virtue of his esteemed lineage. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the mishna that this is so.

The *gemara* resolves this from Rabbi Akiva's formulation. Rabbi Akiva says that even the poor of Israel are to be regarded as if they are free people who have lost their wealth, as sons of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and are therefore entitled to the full amount. *Shema mina*: since Rabbi Akiva feels compelled to insist on the full payment for the poor, it is clear that the *tanna kamma* had been using the listed amounts as maximums, permitting reductions based on lower social standing. Rabbi Akiva disputes this and rules that everyone receives the maximum.

Rabbi Akiva's position reflects a principled egalitarianism in the law of *boshet*: the shame of being spat upon or having one's covering removed is not diminished by poverty. Every person, regardless of station, is heir to a dignity that demands full recompense when violated.

### Hair Covering Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁפָּרַע רֹאשׁ הָאִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, בָּאת לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְחִיְּיבוֹ לִיתֵּן לָהּ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, תֵּן לִי זְמַן. וְנָתַן לוֹ זְמַן. שְׁמָרָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת עַל פֶּתַח חֲצֵירָהּ, וְשָׁבַר אֶת הַכַּד בְּפָנֶיהָ, וּבוֹ כְּאִיסָּר שֶׁמֶן. גִּילְּתָה אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ, וְהָיְתָה מְטַפַּחַת וּמַנַּחַת יָדָהּ עַל רֹאשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates: **And an incident** occurred **involving one who uncovered the head of a woman in the marketplace,** and the woman **came before Rabbi Akiva** to request that he render the assailant liable to pay for the humiliation that she suffered, **and** Rabbi Akiva **rendered** the assailant **liable to give her four hundred dinars.** The man **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **My teacher, give me time** to pay the penalty, **and** Rabbi Akiva **gave him time. The man then** **waited for her** until **she was standing by the opening of her courtyard, and he broke a jug in front of her, and** there was the value of **about an *issar* of oil inside** the jug. The woman then **exposed her** own **head and she was wetting [*metapaḥat*]** her hand in the oil, **and placing her hand on her head** to make use of the oil.

The *mishnah* concludes with a story illustrating Rabbi Akiva's ruling. A man watched for a woman to appear at the entrance of her courtyard, went and removed her head covering in a public space, and then was instructed by Rabbi Akiva to pay 400 *zuz*. The man asked for time to pay, and time was granted — but he then took advantage of the delay to perform a trick that arguably implicated the woman's own cooperation, apparently attempting to undermine the claim. Rabbi Akiva dismissed the maneuver and imposed full liability.

## Payment Timing Rules

### No Time Injury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי יָהֲבִינַן זְמַן? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֵין נוֹתְנִין זְמַן לַחֲבָלוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna relates: **And an incident** occurred **involving one who uncovered the head of a woman** in the marketplace. The woman came before Rabbi Akiva to request that he assess payment for the humiliation that she suffered, and Rabbi Akiva obligated the assailant to give her four hundred dinars. The man said to Rabbi Akiva: My teacher, give me time to pay the penalty, and Rabbi Akiva gave him time. The Gemara asks: **But do we give time** for an assailant to pay compensation? **But doesn't Rabbi Ḥanina say:** The court **does not give** one **time** to provide payment **for injuries,** and he must pay compensation immediately.

This story raises a procedural question. The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Chanina rules: *ein notnin zman l'chavalot* — one does not grant time to pay when the debt is for *chavalah*, physical injury. Why then was time granted in the *boshet* case?

### Financial Loss Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ זְמַן – לַחֲבָלָה, דְּחַסְּרֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא; אֲבָל לְבוֹשֶׁת, דְּלָא חַסְּרֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא – יָהֲבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When we do not give him time** to pay **compensation** it is specifically **with regard to injury, as he caused a monetary loss** by inflicting physical damage on the victim. **But with regard to humiliation, where he did not cause him a monetary loss, we do give** him time to pay compensation.

The answer lies in the nature of the loss. When someone causes a physical injury, there is an immediate and concrete financial diminishment: medical costs accumulate, income is lost, the body itself loses value. Granting time for payment in such cases means the victim suffers ongoing financial harm while waiting. But *boshet* — shame — does not generate a financial deficit in the same sense. The victim is not out of pocket in any direct way; the 400 *zuz* is a fine for the violation of dignity. Since no ongoing financial loss accrues from the delay, it is appropriate to grant time.

## Honor & Payment (II)

### Time to Pay

Rabbi Akiva's response in the *mishnah* thus concerns the *boshet* case specifically: he confirms that for an act of shaming without physical injury, the court may indeed grant time for payment, since the payment is punitive-compensatory in character rather than a direct reimbursement of quantifiable loss.

## Self-Injury Allowed

### Embarrass Not Wound

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: צָלַלְתָּ בְּמַיִם אַדִּירִים וְהֶעֱלִיתָ חֶרֶס בְּיָדְךָ, אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לַחְבֹּל בְּעַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates that the man then **waited for her** until **she was standing by the opening of her courtyard,** and caused her to uncover her own head. Rabbi Akiva said that although she caused injury to herself, which is not permitted, this does not negate the man's requirement to pay her compensation. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva said to him: You plunged into mighty waters and you raised** nothing more than worthless **earthenware in your hand,** since it is **permitted for a person to injure himself.** This contradicts the mishna's account of Rabbi Akiva's opinion.

The *gemara* now confronts a tension. The *mishnah* states that *ha-chovel b'atzmo af al pi she-eino rashai patur* — one who injures himself, even though it is not permitted, is exempt from paying himself. This implies that self-injury is prohibited. Yet a *baraita* cited earlier quotes Rabbi Akiva as saying *adam rashai l'chavel b'atzmo* — a person is permitted to injure himself. How are these reconciled?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בַּחֲבָלָה, כָּאן בְּבוֹשֶׁת. וְהָא מַתְנִיתִין בְּבוֹשֶׁת הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי: הַחוֹבֵל בְּעַצְמוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאִי – פָּטוּר! הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִבַּעְיָא בּוֹשֶׁת, דְּאָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת עַצְמוֹ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חֲבָלָה, דְּאֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ – אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בּוֹ, חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rava said:** It is **not difficult. Here,** in the mishna, where it states that one may not injure himself, it is stated **with regard to** inflicting actual physical **injury,** while **there,** in the *baraita*, where it states that it is permitted for one to injure himself, it is stated **with regard to** causing **humiliation** to oneself. The Gemara asks: **But** the ruling of Rabbi Akiva in **the mishna is** stated **with regard to** causing **humiliation, and it teaches:** With regard to **one who injures himself, although it is not permitted for him** to do so, he is nevertheless **exempt** from any sort of penalty, indicating that the prohibition is in effect even with regard to humiliation. The Gemara answers: **This is** what Rabbi Akiva **said to** the man: **It is not necessary** to say with regard to **humiliation, where it is permitted for a person to humiliate himself,** that someone else who humiliated him is liable. **But even** with regard to **injury, where it is not permitted for a person to injure himself, others who injured him are liable.**

Rava resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, kan b'chavalah, kan b'boshet* — there is no difficulty; one source speaks of physical wounding, the other of embarrassing oneself. Wounding oneself is not permitted; embarrassing oneself is permitted. The *mishnah* says you may not wound yourself. The *baraita* says you may embarrass yourself if you choose.

The *gemara* presses: but the *mishnah* explicitly addresses *chavalah* — *ha-chovel b'atzmo*. How does that speak to embarrassment? The *gemara* explains: Rabbi Akiva in the *baraita* was making a rhetorical point with a *kal va-chomer* structure. *Lo mibaya boshet* — it goes without saying that one may embarrass oneself. *Ella afilu chavalah* — but even when it comes to physical wounding, which one is not permitted to do to oneself, *acherim she-chavlu bo chayavin* — others who wound that person are still fully liable. The *mishnah*'s point is not that self-inflicted wounding has no consequences for others; it is that self-inflicted wounding carries no personal payment obligation against oneself. The prohibition on self-wounding stands, but it does not generate a compensable claim.

### Oath to Injure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ? וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל נִשְׁבַּע לְהָרַע בְּעַצְמוֹ – וְלֹא הֵרַע, יְהֵא פָּטוּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵטִיב" – מָה הֲטָבָה רְשׁוּת, אַף הֲרָעָה רְשׁוּת; אָבִיא נִשְׁבָּע לְהָרַע בְּעַצְמוֹ וְלֹא הֵרַע!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses whether it is permitted to injure oneself. **And is a person not permitted to injure himself? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that if one **takes an oath to do evil to himself and did not do evil he will be exempt** from bringing an offering for having transgressed this oath. Therefore, **the verse states:** "Or if anyone swear clearly with his lips **to do evil or to do good"** (Leviticus 5:4), which teaches that **just as** taking an oath to **do good** for which one is liable is referring to **an optional** activity, as opposed to taking an oath to perform a mitzva, **so too,** taking an oath to **do evil** is referring to **an optional** activity, as opposed to taking an oath to transgress. **I** can therefore **include** within the category of one who is liable if he transgressed his oath the person who **takes an oath to do evil to himself and did not do evil**. It is clear from this *baraita* that doing evil to oneself is permitted.

The *gemara* then challenges the prohibition on self-wounding from a different direction. A *baraita* in *hilchot shevuot* teaches: if someone swears to do harm to himself — *le-hara le-atzmo* — and then does not follow through, might he be exempt because the oath was never binding in the first place? An oath to violate a prohibition is non-binding. The *baraita* answers: *talmud lomar le-hara o le-heitiv* — the verse teaches "to do evil or to do good." Just as one has permission to do good to oneself, so one has permission to do harm to oneself — and therefore an oath to do harm to oneself is entirely binding. The oath is not dismissed as an oath on a prohibited act.

This *baraita* seems to state unambiguously that harming oneself is permitted, making such an oath valid. The *baraita* contrasts this with taking an oath to harm someone else — *ekeh ploni ve-eftza mocho* — which is not binding because one has no permission to harm others. The implication is stark: self-harm is in a different legal category from harming others precisely because it is permitted.

### Fasting Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּ"אֵשֵׁב בְּתַעֲנִית".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel says:** The ruling of the *baraita* is not referring to one who takes an oath to injure himself but is stated **with regard to** one who takes an oath stating: **I will sit in** observance of **a fast,** which it is permitted to do.

Shmuel offers a resolution: *be-yoshev be-ta'anit* — the *baraita* about oaths refers to sitting in a fast, not to physical wounding. One may swear to fast, and that oath is binding because one has permission to deprive oneself of food. The *baraita*'s language of "harming oneself" means self-deprivation — fasting or similar acts — not actual physical injury.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי הֲרָעַת אֲחֵרִים – לְהֹשִׁיבָם בְּתַעֲנִית; אֲחֵרִים מִי מוֹתֵיב לְהוּ בְּתַעֲנִיתָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Earlier in this same *baraita*, it states that one who takes an oath to do evil to others is not liable for violating his oath if he does not do evil, as it is prohibited to do evil to others. If the *baraita* is referring to one who takes an oath to fast, then **in the corresponding** situation **in the context of doing evil to others,** the *baraita* must also then be referring to one who takes an oath **to have** others **sit in** observance of **a fast.** The Gemara asks: **Can** one compel **others** to **sit in** observance of **a fast?**

The *gemara* challenges this interpretation. The *baraita* draws a parallel: an oath to harm others is not binding, and the case given is *ekeh ploni ve-eftza mocho* — a violent physical assault. If harming others means violent assault, then harming oneself, the counterpart to which the oath *is* binding, must similarly refer to physical injury, not mere fasting. Forced starvation of another — *de-madeik lehu be-androna*, locking them in a room — is the analogous act on the other side. The *gemara* suggests that could work technically, but the parallel is strained: the case of harming another is plainly one of physical violence, and its counterpart of harming oneself should be the same.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין; דִּמְהַדַּק לְהוּ בְּאִנְדְּרוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes;** it is possible, as by preventing others from accessing food he can impose a fast on them, e.g., in a situation **where he confined them in a room.**

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ הֲרָעַת אֲחֵרִים? "אַכֶּה פְּלוֹנִי וְאֶפְצַע אֶת מוֹחוֹ"!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the assertion that this is the case of the *baraita*: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **What is** considered taking an oath to **do evil** to **others?** It is considered such if one takes an oath stating: **I will strike so-and-so and I will injure his brain.** Accordingly, in the corresponding situation of doing evil to oneself it is necessary to explain that the *baraita* is referring to causing injury as well, and the inference that this is permitted remains.

Unable to reconcile the sources through reinterpretation, the *gemara* concludes: *ela tanna'i hi* — it is a dispute among *tannaim*. Some *tannaim* hold that a person is permitted to wound himself; others hold that he is not. As Tosafot points out, the two statements attributed to Rabbi Akiva — one permitting self-injury and one prohibiting it — may themselves represent two different *tannaim* who each report Rabbi Akiva's position differently: *trei tanna'ei aliba de-Rabbi Akiva*. What is clear is that the *Rabbi Akiva* of our *mishnah* holds that self-wounding is not permitted, while the *baraita* reflects a position that it is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא; דְּאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְאִיכָּא מַאן דְּאָמַר: אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that this **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as there is** a *tanna* **who says** that **it is not permitted for a person to injure himself, and there is** a *tanna* **who says** that **it is permitted for a person to injure himself.**

## Self-Injury Sources

### Suicide Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאִי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ? אִילֵּימָא הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא – דְּתַנְיָא: "וְאַךְ אֶת דִּמְכֶם לְנַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם אֶדְרֹשׁ" – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מִיַּד נַפְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם אֶדְרֹשׁ אֶת דִּמְכֶם;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna* that you heard that says: It is not permitted for a person to injure himself? If we say** that **it is this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And surely your blood of your souls will I require"** (Genesis 9:5), and **Rabbi Elazar says: From the hand of your souls,** i.e., from yourself, **will I require your blood,** meaning one is liable even for taking his own life, that is not a correct inference.

Now that the *gemara* has established a *tannaic* dispute on self-injury, it identifies the specific *tanna* whose view underlies the prohibition. The *baraita* cites: *ve-ach es dimchem le-nafshoteichem edrosh* — "and your blood for your souls I will seek." Rabbi Elazar reads this as: I will hold your souls accountable for the spilling of your blood — that is, for suicide. This verse is the basis for the prohibition of taking one's own life.

### Bal Tashchit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא קְטָלָא שָׁאנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **But perhaps killing is different.** While Rabbi Elazar holds that it is prohibited to take one's own life, it cannot be inferred from here that he holds that one is liable for injuring himself.

The *gemara* presses: is the prohibition of self-wounding really derived from the suicide verse? Suicide is actual killing — *ketala shani* — and surely everyone agrees that is forbidden. The question is physical wounding that falls short of death. For permanent injury — cutting off a limb — the prohibition might rest on *bal tashchit*, the prohibition against wasteful destruction derived from the verse commanding that fruit-bearing trees not be cut down during a siege.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא – דְּתַנְיָא: מְקָרְעִין עַל הַמֵּת, וְלֹא מִדַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהַמְקָרֵעַ עַל הַמֵּת יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי – לוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תַּשְׁחִית". וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן גּוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, it is** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may rend** garments in anguish **over one who died, and** it is **not** considered **of the ways of the Amorites,** but a Jewish custom. **Rabbi Elazar says: I heard that one who rends** his garments **excessively over one who died is flogged for** having transgressed the prohibition of: **Do not destroy** (see Deuteronomy 20:19). The Gemara suggests: **And all the more so** it is the case that according to Rabbi Elazar one who injures **his body** in anguish transgresses this prohibition.

Rabbi Elazar states that one who tears his garments excessively in mourning for the dead receives lashes for violating *bal tashchit*. By *kal va-chomer*, if *bal tashchit* applies to property, it certainly applies to one's own body — *kol sheken gufo*.

### Non-Permanent Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא בְּגָדִים שָׁאנֵי, דִּפְסֵידָא דְּלָא הָדַר הוּא! כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קָרֵי לְהוּ לְמָאנֵיהּ "מְכַבְּדוֹתַי", וְרַב חִסְדָּא כַּד הֲוָה מְסַגֵּי בֵּינֵי הִיזְמֵי וְהִגֵּא – מְדַלֵּי לְהוּ לְמָאנֵיהּ, אָמַר: זֶה מַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה, וְזֶה אֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **But perhaps garments are different,** in **that** tearing them **is a loss that is irreversible, like that** practice **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who would **refer to his garments as: My honor, and** like that practice of **Rav Ḥisda,** who, **when he would walk among thorns and shrubs,** would **raise his clothing** despite the fact that his skin would get scratched by the thorns. **He said** in explanation of his actions: **This** flesh **will heal** if scratched, **but that** garment **will not heal** if torn. Similarly, perhaps it is prohibited to rend one's garments, but it is permitted to injure oneself.

The *gemara* tests the scope of *bal tashchit* as a basis for prohibiting self-injury. It suggests that *bal tashchit* may apply to garments but not necessarily to the body — because clothes do not heal (*pesida de-lo hadra*), whereas the body does. The example given: Rabbi Yochanan called his garments *mechabduta* — the things that honor him — indicating that he treated his clothing with great care as a vehicle of dignity. Rav Chisda, when walking among thorns and thistles, would lift his cloak to spare it from tearing, even at the cost of scraping his ankles and shins. His reasoning: *ze ma'ale arucha ve-ze eino ma'ale arucha* — the skin heals, the garment does not. The body's capacity to recover distinguishes it from inanimate objects for the purpose of *bal tashchit*. A non-permanent injury to the body, therefore, may not fall under *bal tashchit* at all, leaving the question of its basis unresolved.

### Affliction Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא – דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר בְּרַבִּי, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו מֵאֲשֶׁר חָטָא עַל הַנָּפֶשׁ"? וְכִי בְּאֵיזֶה נֶפֶשׁ חָטָא זֶה? אֶלָּא שֶׁצִּיעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִן הַיַּיִן. וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וְחוֹמֶר – וּמָה זֶה, שֶׁלֹּא צִיעֵר עַצְמוֹ אֶלָּא מִן הַיַּיִן, נִקְרָא חוֹטֵא; הַמְצַעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִכׇּל דָּבָר, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it is this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar the Distinguished said: What** is the meaning when **the verse states** with regard to a nazirite: **"And he shall atone for him for sinning by the soul"** (Numbers 6:11)? **And with which soul did this** person **sin** by becoming a nazirite? **Rather,** in **that he afflicted himself** by abstaining **from wine** he is considered to have sinned with his own soul, and he must bring a sin-offering for the naziriteship itself, for causing his body to suffer. **And are** these **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*: And just as this** person **who afflicted himself** by abstaining **only from wine is** nevertheless **called a sinner, one who afflicts himself** by abstaining **from everything,** through fasting or other acts of mortification, **all the more so** is he described as a sinner? Consequently, Rabbi Elazar HaKappar holds that one may not harm himself in any manner.

The *gemara* then turns to Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's teaching. The Torah requires a *nazir* who became *tamei* to bring a *korban* and atone for having sinned against the *nefesh* — the soul. Rabbi Elazar HaKappar reads this broadly: the *nazir* sinned by afflicting himself — by withholding from himself the pleasure of wine. And by *kal va-chomer*: if a *nazir* who deprived himself only of wine is called a sinner, how much more so one who afflicts himself in any other way.

This is a notably weak foundation for a prohibition of self-injury. First, it is not a direct prohibition but rather a characterization — *nikra chotei*, he is *called* a sinner. Second, and more critically, the *kal va-chomer* seems to reach far beyond physical wounding: if skipping wine makes one a sinner, does voluntary fasting also constitute sin? The *gemara* itself seems aware that there is no obvious qualitative line between accepting an unnecessary *ta'anit* and causing oneself some minor physical pain.

### Rambam Wounding

The Rambam, when he codifies the prohibition of self-injury, addresses it specifically in terms of *chavalah* — wounding that draws blood. He rules that a person is forbidden to wound another person, and is similarly forbidden to wound himself. He adds a further prohibition: even striking another person without causing a wound violates a biblical command, derived from the verse *lo yosif lehakoto* — the prohibition against administering excessive lashes. Since even the court-appointed administrator of lashes is restricted by this verse, the principle extends to any person: one may not strike another. What is not fully clear from the Rambam, however, is the specific biblical basis for the self-wounding prohibition in particular — whether it stems from *lo yosif lehakoto*, from the *bal tashchit* principle, from the *nazir* verse, or from some other source.

### Basis Unclear

The *gemara* leaves the precise basis for the prohibition of self-injury unresolved. The suicide prohibition is clear and separately grounded. The prohibition of permanent injury finds support in *bal tashchit*, at least as applied to objects, though the extension to the body is contested. Non-permanent self-injury appears to rest on the *nazir*-based *kal va-chomer* of Rabbi Elazar HaKappar, which is itself a less-than-precise foundation. The Rambam appears to absorb the question into the broader prohibition on striking — but that verse is generally understood to apply to others, not oneself. The result is a prohibition that is practically clear in its core applications but theoretically elusive in its precise source — a fitting conclusion to a *sugya* that has navigated between the formal and the intuitive across multiple areas of *hilchot nezikin*.
