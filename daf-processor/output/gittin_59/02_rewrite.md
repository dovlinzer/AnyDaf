# Gittin 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gido bar Re'ili

### Land Tax Arrangement

The daf opens in the middle of 58b with the case of Gido bar Re'ili, situated within the broader discussion of *sikrikon* — the legal framework governing property seized under threat of life and the various *takkanot* enacted to address it. By way of review: the first stage of the *takana* required a purchaser to acquire the property from both the *sikrikon* and the original owner, and in the correct order, so that the original owner would not feel coerced into a sale. The second stage, enacted later, permitted purchase from the *sikrikon* alone after twelve months, on the assumption that the owner had by then relinquished hope of recovering the land.

The case of Gido bar Re'ili unfolds as follows. A valley's land had been abandoned by its owners — perhaps they had left the region — yet the community as a whole remained liable for the land tax. To resolve this, the community arranged for Gido bar Re'ili to work the land in the owners' absence: he would keep the produce and pay the tax on their behalf. It was a reasonable arrangement for all parties.

### Prepayment Problem

The difficulty arose when Gido bar Re'ili, perhaps overeager, prepaid the land tax for three years in advance, presumably hoping to secure three years of produce. However, after only the first year, the original owners returned and reclaimed their land. Their position was entirely reasonable: you paid the tax and kept the produce for the first year while we were away — fair enough. But from this point forward, *anan yavnan va'anan achlinan* — we will pay the tax and we will take the produce. It is, after all, our land.

### Rav Pappa's Solution

The matter came before Rav Pappa, who considered writing Gido bar Re'ili a *turfa* — a writ of the *beis din* authorizing him to collect his prepaid taxes from the people of the valley. The reasoning was that the valley community had, in effect, appointed him to this role, and having paid out money on their behalf, he ought to be able to recoup it from them.

### Rav Huna Rejects

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua objected to Rav Pappa's proposed remedy: *im ken asita sikrikon* — you have turned this into a case of *sikrikon*. The analogy Rashi draws, though admittedly somewhat difficult, is as follows. In the *sikrikon* scenario, a purchaser buys land that originally belonged to someone else, and the *takana* protects him from losing out simply because the land had previously belonged to the original owner. Here, Rav Pappa would be treating the valley people as though they had some right to grant Gido bar Re'ili a claim over the land — as if, now that the original owners have returned, Gido bar Re'ili is guaranteed not to lose out on his investment, analogous to the protection afforded by the *sikrikon takana*. But this analogy fails entirely. There was no seizure of land under threat of life, no *sikrikon* in any meaningful sense, and therefore no foundational presumption that this person had a right to buy the land such that the owners' return cannot be held against him.

### Sikrikon Analogy

The response of Rav Huna makes clear that the *sikrikon* framework cannot be extended by analogy to situations that lack its defining features. The *sikrikon* scenario involves a coercive seizure; the arrangements by the valley community involved no such thing. They simply needed someone to manage the land and pay the tax. There is no underlying structure that would generate a protected expectation of the kind that the *sikrikon takana* was designed to address.

### Heniah Maotav

Rather, Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua rules: *heniah maotav al keren hatzvi* — he placed his money on the horns of a deer. In other words, he effectively threw his money away. Nobody asked him to prepay three years of taxes in advance. He has no fundamental right, simply because the owners abandoned the land, to assume some protected status analogous to a *sikrikon* purchaser such that he will not lose out. One might ask: since those taxes were already paid to the government, shouldn't the returning owners at least reimburse him, since he saved them that expense? Rashi seems to suggest that the government would actually double-collect from the returning owners — a jarring result, but that appears to be how Rashi reads the case. More broadly, *heniah maotav al keren hatzvi* can apply even where there may be some moral responsibility to repay — the Gemara is addressing the question of legal obligation, and legally, Gido bar Re'ili is not owed anything. He acted unilaterally and must bear the consequences.

## Sikrikon Takana

### First Takana

The Gemara now returns to the *sikrikon* framework more directly, reviewing its two stages. The first *takana* — *mishna rishona* — required that a purchaser acquire the property from both the *sikrikon* and the original owner, and in the proper sequence, so the original owner would not feel compelled.

### Second Takana

The second *takana*, enacted by a later *beis din*, held that if the property sat in the hands of the *sikrikon* for twelve months and the original owner did not repurchase it, anyone could go ahead and buy it from the *sikrikon* — on the implicit assumption that after twelve months the owner had fully relinquished his expectation of recovery. However, the purchaser must still pay the original owners a *revi'a* — a quarter — because he acquired the property at a discount.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The question then becomes: how exactly is this *revi'a* calculated? The Gemara presents a dispute between Rav and Shmuel on the matter.

### Quarter Calculations

Rav holds *revi'a bemaot* — a quarter of the purchase price. So if the purchaser paid one hundred for the land, which was really worth one hundred and twenty-five, he pays the original owners twenty-five — that is, a quarter of what he paid, representing the discount he received. *Revi'a bekarka*, in Rav's formulation, would mean that equivalent amount expressed in land: since the land is worth one hundred and twenty-five and the purchaser paid one hundred, returning a portion of the land worth twenty-five actually means returning a fifth of the land — hence the apparent paradox where *revi'a bekarka* in practice means a fifth of the land.

Shmuel, by contrast, holds *revi'a bekarka shehen shalish bemaot* — a quarter of the land, which works out to a third of the purchase price. In Shmuel's view, the discount was larger: the purchaser paid one hundred for land worth approximately one hundred and thirty-three, so he must pay the owners thirty-three — a full third of what he paid. In terms of land, thirty-three out of one hundred and thirty-three is one quarter, hence the phrase *revi'a bekarka*. The underlying dispute is simply whether the discount was one-fifth of the actual value (*nechi chumsha zavin*) or one-quarter of the actual value (*nechi revi'a zavin*).

A *braisa* is then cited: *zu mishna rishona* — after twelve months, anyone may purchase, *aval nosein l'baalim revi'a bekarka o revi'a bemaot*, and the owners may choose whether they want their portion in land or in cash. The *braisa* concludes with the phrase *revi'a bemaot*, which appears to support Rav's position — that a quarter of the purchase price, i.e., twenty-five out of one hundred, is owed to the owners.

### Rav Ashi Resolves

Rav Ashi reconciles the *braisa* with Shmuel's position as follows. Under Shmuel's formulation, the purchaser pays the *sikrikon* one hundred and then pays the owners thirty-three. Out of the total amount expended — one hundred and thirty-three — the owners received thirty-three. That is precisely a quarter of the total money spent. So when the *braisa* says *revi'a bemaot*, it can be read in Shmuel's terms as well: the owners receive a quarter of all the money the purchaser spent in total, counting both the payment to the *sikrikon* and the payment to the owners. For Rav, the quarter is of the purchase price alone; for Shmuel, the quarter is of the total expenditure. Both positions can be described as *revi'a bemaot*, resolving the apparent tension.

## Rav in Beit Din

### Rav Voted First

Rav now makes a striking personal disclosure: *ana hava b'minyana d'vei Rebbi* — I was present in the *beis din* that Rebbi convened, and *minai didi manu b'reisha* — they asked for my vote first. This is itself a remarkable datum, because Rav is the great transitional figure between the *tannaim* and *amoraim* — *Rav tanna hu u-falig* — and here he testifies that he participated in Rebbi's court when the vote on the *sikrikon* was taken.

### Voting Order Rules

The Gemara notes what appears to be a procedural irregularity. The *mishna* teaches: *dinei mamonot v'ha-taharot v'ha-tumos matchilin min ha-gadol* — in monetary cases and matters of *tumah* and *taharah*, the court begins with the most senior member voting first. The rationale is honor and respect for the senior authority. In *dinei nefashot* — capital cases — the procedure is reversed: *matchilin min ha-tzad*, voting begins from the side, from a more junior member. Two reasons are given for this: first, in a capital case where someone's life is at stake, you want every judge to feel maximally free to voice an independent opinion, without being overawed by the senior authority's position. Second, Rashi cites a *drasha* from the *pasuk* *lo sa'aneh al rav* — one may not simply acquiesce to a senior authority's ruling in *dinei nefashot*. So in capital cases, starting from the junior members protects the integrity of deliberation.

Since the vote on *sikrikon* was clearly not a capital case, Rebbi himself should have voted first. How, then, did Rav — presumably not the most senior member — cast the first vote?

### Rebbi's Humility

The Gemara answers, in the name of Rabbah son of Rabbah (or, some say, Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas): *shani minyana d'vei Rebbi* — Rebbi's court was different. *D'kulei maminu lei min hatzad* — in all matters, they always began from the side, not from Rebbi. Tosafot explains this in light of the famous last *mishna* of *Sotah*: *batul anavah* — when Rebbi died, humility ceased. Rebbi was so humble that even in cases where he was entitled by right to vote first, he voluntarily stepped back and allowed others to begin deliberations. He was *mochel* on his prerogative, and the result — more open, more honest deliberation — was in every respect beneficial. This is why Rav could have voted first in a non-capital case: Rebbi chose, as a matter of *anavah*, to let others lead.

## Torah & Status

### Rebbi Unique

The implicit reference to Rebbi's *anavah* — and the account of his convening a *beis din* — leads the Gemara into an extended digression. Rabbah son of Rabbah (or Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas) states: *mi-yemos Moshe v'ad Rebbi lo matzinu Torah u'gedulah bemakom echad* — from the time of Moshe Rabbeinu until Rebbi, we never find Torah greatness and worldly greatness (*gedula*, which Rashi explains as wealth, though one might also read it as broader societal authority and stature) combined in a single person. The claim is not that there were no great Torah scholars, nor that there were no figures of great status or wealth — but that the two were never unified in one individual to the degree they were in Rebbi. One might wonder whether this implies Rebbi was the greatest Torah scholar of his time in an absolute sense, or whether it is more a claim about the extraordinary degree of *both* qualities simultaneously residing in one person. The Gemara proceeds to test this claim historically.

### Early Examples

The Gemara raises a series of counterexamples. *Havei Yehoshua* — was Yehoshua not both a great Torah scholar (as Moshe's foremost student) and the political leader of Israel? The response: *havei Elazar* — Elazar was also present, a great Torah authority in his own right as Aharon's student, and therefore Yehoshua was not unchallenged in his combination of roles. The Gemara then asks: after Yehoshua's death, was not Elazar unrivaled? *Havei Pinchas* — Pinchas was also there. And when Elazar died and Pinchas remained, was he not uniquely combining these roles? *Havah zekenim* — the elders remained as political and religious authorities alongside him, a point reinforced by the repeated motif in *Sefer Shoftim* of *ein melech b'Yisrael* — suggesting that no single figure held unchallenged authority of both kinds.

A question worth raising in this context: this entire sugya seems to run counter to the principle of *yeridas hadoros* — the decline of generations. The response is that the claim is always relative: in any given generation, the question is whether one person towers above all others in both dimensions simultaneously, not whether they surpass figures of previous ages.

### Royal & Sage

The Gemara continues through the monarchic period. *V'ha Shaul* — Shaul was a king and, according to rabbinic tradition, a great Torah scholar. Response: *havah Shmuel*, who was the supreme religious authority of the age. But when Shmuel died while Shaul was still alive, was Shaul not then unrivaled? *Kulei shani ka'amina* — the claim is about someone who held this status throughout his entire career, not merely for a brief window.

*V'ha David* — David was a king and his Torah scholarship is well attested throughout *Masechet Brachos* and elsewhere. Response: *havah Ira haYairi*, who is, admittedly, a name one would not otherwise encounter, but whom rabbinic tradition regards as a great Torah scholar. This response feels somewhat weaker — Ira haYairi had no real authority comparable to David's — and Rashi limits his description simply to *gadol baTorah*, without addressing the *gedula* dimension. Again the Gemara responds that he did not hold this dual status throughout his entire career. The same pattern plays out with Shlomo: *havah Shimi ben Gera* — and when Shlomo had him killed, the response is again that Shlomo was not uncontested his entire reign.

*V'ha Chizkiyahu* — Chizkiyahu was a king and a great Torah scholar. Response: *havah Shevna*, a sage of his time. Shevna was killed, but again: *kulei shani ka'amina*, the whole-career qualification applies. Notably, the Gemara invokes Shevna rather than Yeshayahu as the counterweight to Chizkiyahu — why Yeshayahu is not cited here is unclear.

*V'ha Ezra* — Ezra HaSofer was both political leader and supreme Torah authority of the return from Babylonian exile. Response: *havah Nechemya ben Chilkiya*. With this, all the historical challenges have been answered.

### Later Period

The Gemara then adds the parallel claim for the later period: *mi-yemos Rebbi v'ad Rav Ashi lo matzinu Torah u'gedula bimkom echad* — from Rebbi's time until Rav Ashi, the same combination was not seen, until Rav Ashi, who was evidently a man of both great Torah stature and great wealth or societal standing. The Gemara anticipates a challenge: *v'ha havah Huna bar Nasan*, who also seemed to combine both qualities. The response: *d'mikaf havah kaif lei l'Rav Ashi* — Huna bar Nasan actually submitted himself to Rav Ashi's authority, and therefore he cannot serve as a genuine counterexample to Rav Ashi's unique status.

## Cheresh Status

### Definition Context

The Gemara now returns to the *mishnah*'s enumeration of rabbinic *takkanot*, moving from the *sikrikon* framework toward a broader set of measures addressing individuals with diminished legal capacity. The phrase *tikkun olam* has by this point largely receded from the discussion — we are now in the realm of rabbinic enactments addressing changing social circumstances and specific populations, though the *mishnah* we are about to examine is, in scope, as broad as some of the classic *tikkun olam* provisions.

The *cheresh* — the deaf-mute — is, as a matter of Torah law, not considered a *bar daas*, a person of full legal competency. As such, he cannot enter into legally binding transactions: buying and selling, *kiddushin*, *geirushin*, and so forth. He cannot discharge others' obligations such as blowing the *shofar*, and is not personally *chayav b'mitzvot* — his status is comparable in this respect to that of a minor.

That said, it is important to note that the Gemara does contain an opinion that regards the *cheresh* as having a meaningful degree of *daas*, or at least holds that competency ought to be assessed on a case-by-case basis. Even those who classify him as lacking full *daas* treat him differently from a *shoteh* — a person categorized as mentally ill in a more fundamental sense, perhaps unable to properly interpret reality. The *cheresh* is understood as diminished in *daas* but not entirely devoid of it, and this intermediate status is precisely what makes *takkanot* on his behalf possible.

An important historical observation is worth making here. One might wonder how frequently a person would be both deaf and mute. The answer is that in the pre-modern world, virtually every deaf person fell into this category — because individuals who cannot hear are generally unable to modulate their speech, and without education or training they could not speak intelligibly. The only exceptions were those who had been born hearing and later lost their hearing. As a result, nearly every deaf person was treated halachically as a *cheresh*. In recent centuries, as schools for the deaf developed and it became undeniable that deaf individuals can communicate with full intelligence through sign language, many *poskim* have revisited this categorization. Some have argued that a person communicating through sign language does not fall into the category of *ilemet* (mute) at all, and therefore is not a *cheresh* in the halachic sense. These developments lie beyond the scope of the Gemara, but they are essential background for understanding why the Gemara's enactments were designed as they were: the *cheresh* was understood to possess real, if imperfect, *daas*, enough to justify accommodating him in the normal functioning of society.

### Gesture Transactions

The *mishnah* states: *cheresh romez v'nirmas* — a *cheresh* can engage in buying and selling through gestures. If he points to an item he wants and holds out money, the transaction is valid at the rabbinic level, even though *d'Oraita* it would not be binding. This was enacted as a *takanna* precisely because of this intermediate status of the *cheresh*: he is not like a *shoteh*, and it would be untenable to deny him the ability to engage in basic commercial transactions.

### Ben Beteira

*U'ven Beteira omer kofetz v'nikpatz* — Ben Beteira holds that even jumping — an action less precisely expressive than hand gestures — constitutes a sufficient signal. The *chachamim*, by contrast, apparently regarded jumping as too ambiguous to be reliably interpreted as communicating transactional intent.

### Moveable Only

All of this applies only to *mitaltelin* — moveable property. The *takanna* was not extended to the purchase of land (*karka*). Two reasons converge: first, there was not the same pressing necessity, since the *takanna* for *mitaltelin* was grounded in the need for the *cheresh* to be able to function in everyday life — to buy basic provisions. Land transactions are qualitatively different in nature and not part of ordinary daily commerce. Second, land is far too weighty a matter, and the Rabbis were not willing to institute a *takanna* extending such capacity to a person who is not *d'Oraita* a full *bar daas*.

### Get Standards

The Gemara now addresses the question of the *get* — the divorce document. Rav Nachman explains: the dispute between Ben Beteira and the *chachamim* pertains to *mitaltelin*, as the *mishnah* explicitly states. When it comes to a *get*, by contrast, *divrei hakol b'remiza* — all agree that a proper hand gesture is required; jumping would not suffice.

The case of a *get* for a *cheresh* applies specifically when the man was already a *cheresh* when he entered into *kiddushin*. Since the *kiddushin* of a *cheresh* is only rabbinically valid — it is a rabbinic enactment parallel to the one permitting him to engage in transactions — the resulting marriage is dissolved through a corresponding rabbinic *get*. The *cheresh* husband can signal to the *sofer* his intention to write a *get* for his wife, and this suffices. But even in this context, which involves only rabbinic *kiddushin* and rabbinic divorce, the standard for gesture is higher than for *mitaltelin*: everyone requires clear *remiza*, proper hand signaling.

One might have thought the opposite: since the *get* is only undoing a rabbinic institution, perhaps the standards would be *lower*, and even jumping would suffice. The Gemara therefore clarifies that this is not the case — *mishnah* addresses *mitaltelin*, and for *get* the standards are uniformly higher, with everyone requiring *remiza*.

A second version (*ika d'amri*) of Rav Nachman's statement holds that the dispute between Ben Beteira and the *chachamim* applies equally to *mitaltelin* and to *gittin*. Under this reading, the *mishnah* mentioning *mitaltelin* simply addresses that context, but the dispute carries over to the *get* case as well, with Ben Beteira maintaining that jumping suffices even there.

## Pe'utot Minors

### Age Range

The *mishnah* also states that *pe'utot* — young children — may buy and sell *mitaltelin*, and such transactions are rabbinically valid. The Gemara asks: *v'ad kama* — until what age, or rather, down to what age? Rav Yehuda indicated to his son Rav Yitzchak: approximately six or seven years old. Rav Kahana says seven or eight. A *baraita* teaches nine or ten.

The Gemara resolves the apparent disagreement: *v'lo pligi* — these do not actually dispute one another. *Kol chad v'chad l'fi chorfei* — it depends on the sharpness of the individual child. There is no fixed biological age; what matters is whether the child has reached a sufficient level of understanding — knowing the value of money, understanding what a transaction means, being able to manage change appropriately. The different age ranges reflect the range of children's development rather than competing fixed thresholds.

### Rationale

Why was this *takanna* enacted? Rav Abba bar Yaakov said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mishum k'dei chayyav* — because of his livelihood, so that he can function in society. In earlier periods, children as young as six or seven might be apprenticed or otherwise fending for themselves to a considerable degree. The inability to execute any valid commercial transaction would be practically untenable.

The Gemara at this point also brings a passage in which the same *baal memra* — Rav Abba bar Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — interprets the word *maltechah* (from 2 Kings 10:22: *vayomer la'asher al hamaltechah hotzei levush l'chol ovdei hab'aal*) as *davar hanimlal v'nimtach* — something spun and stretched by hand, i.e., a fine linen garment. This is connected to an account of Bunaya ben Nunaya sending Rebbi several types of extraordinarily fine fabrics — *sivni v'chumes* (folded to the size of a nut or half a nut), *salsala u'malmala* (folded to the size of a pistachio or half a pistachio) — with *malmala* receiving the same etymology: *davar hanimlal v'nimtach*, spun and stretched linen. The passage is cited here because of the shared *baal memra*, not because of topical relevance to the *pe'utot* laws.

### Overpayment Limits

If a *pe'utat* overpays, does the standard law of *ona'ah* apply? Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Zeira: *ad shtus k'gadol* — up to a sixth, as with an adult. The transaction stands but the excess must be returned; an overpayment exceeding a sixth would void the transaction, just as it would for a competent adult.

### Gift Validity

Abaye raises a further question: *matnaso mah* — what is the status of a gift given by a *pe'utat*? The *takanna* was enacted to allow children to participate in normal commercial life; a gift is outside that rationale, and there is greater opportunity for exploitation. Can a minor give a valid gift?

Rav Yeimar holds that it is not a valid gift; Mar bar Rav Ashi holds that it is. When the question was transmitted to Rav Mordechai for his view, the names were accidentally reversed — it was reported to him that Mar bar Rav Ashi held gifts were invalid and Rav Yeimar held they were valid, precisely the opposite of what each had actually said.

Rav Mordechai responded: go tell the son of my master — meaning Mar bar Rav Ashi, whose father Rav Ashi was Rav Mordechai's *rebbe* — don't you remember the incident? When Rav Ashi was walking up the steps to the *beis hamidrash* — one foot on the ground, one foot on the step — we asked him: *matnaso mah*, what is the law regarding a *pe'utat*'s gift? And he told us: *matnaso matnah* — it is a valid gift. This applies whether it is a gift of a *shechiv mera* (given on a deathbed), a *matnas bari* (given while healthy), a large gift, or a small gift — all are valid.

This of course confirms what Mar bar Rav Ashi had actually said — that gifts are valid — rather than the reversed version that had been reported to Rav Mordechai. Tosafot notes that the Gemara does not specify whether this ruling applies to *mitaltelin* only or also to *karka*; given the context — the entire discussion of *pe'utot* concerns *mitaltelin* — it is reasonable to assume only moveable property is intended, since there was no *takanna* extending transactional capacity to land.

## Darchei Shalom

### Aliyot Order

The *mishnah* now shifts to a new organizing principle: *mipnei darchei shalom* — for the sake of peaceful relations and social order. Where *tikkun olam* addressed deep systemic inequities, *darchei shalom* addresses the more quotidian need to establish conventions that minimize conflict. Having a recognized way of doing things — an acknowledged order or sequence — preempts many disputes before they arise. The opening example is the *aliyot* order: a *kohen* reads first, then a *levi*, then a *Yisrael*.

There is something worth pausing over in this institution. The authentic *halacha* of *krias hatorah* is that the most accomplished Torah scholar present should receive the first *aliyah*. Torah study is the great equalizer — as the Gemara elsewhere teaches, a *mamzer talmid chacham* takes precedence over a *kohen gadol am ha'aretz*. The institution of *krias hatorah* in principle reflects this: *hakol olim l'minyan shivah*, even a woman, even a minor. Torah is accessible to all, and greatness in Torah supersedes hierarchies of birth.

Yet this very egalitarianism creates a practical problem: precisely because greatness in Torah is a matter of achievement rather than birth, there is no predefined, unambiguous hierarchy. Disputes over who is the greater *talmid chacham* are endemic. The flip side of equal access is endless contest over rank. By instituting the *kohen-levi-Yisrael* sequence *mipnei darchei shalom*, the Rabbis introduced a clear, uncontested ordering that resolves at least the initial hierarchy of honors, even at the cost of some of the pure meritocracy that *krias hatorah* in principle demands.

### Torah Equalizer

As noted, while the *kohen-levi-Yisrael* order serves *darchei shalom*, the Torah itself is a domain of equal access. The priority of status applies in matters of *kavod*, but the very existence of Torah as the measure of worth means that a *talmid chacham* who is a *mamzer* takes precedence in Torah honor over a *kohen gadol* who is an *am ha'aretz*. This reflects a broader principle running through rabbinic Judaism: the shift from a *beis hamikdash*-centered hierarchy (which is about birth status, tribe, priestly lineage) to a Torah-centered hierarchy (which is about achievement and learning). The institution of *krias hatorah* lives in the tension between these two frameworks, and the *darchei shalom* ordering of *kohen-levi-Yisrael* is one resolution of that tension.

### Eiruv & Wells

Two further applications of *darchei shalom* follow. First: *eiruvin b'vayis yashan mipnei darchei shalom* — when an *eiruv chatzeros* has customarily been stored in a particular house, one should continue placing it there rather than shifting it to a different house. This preserves an established expectation and avoids disputes over whose home is used as the locus of the *eiruv*.

Second: *bor she'haya karov l'ama, mitmaleh rishon mipnei darchei shalom* — if a well was situated near an irrigation canal, the owner of the well closest to the water source has the first right to fill his well from the canal. In times of scarcity, when everyone wishes to store water in their wells before the canal level drops, having a recognized principle of priority by proximity prevents conflict.

### Traps Ownership

The *mishnah* states: *metzudot chayah v'ofot v'dagim yesh bahem mishum gezel mipnei darchei shalom* — traps set for wild animals, birds, or fish are protected *mipnei darchei shalom*, such that if another person comes upon the trap before the trapper returns and takes the animal, it is considered a form of theft. Strictly speaking, the trapper has not yet performed a formal *kinyan* — he has not physically taken possession of the animal, and depending on the circumstances, the trap may not function as a *kinyan kli* that transfers ownership automatically. Nevertheless, the Rabbis declared that one who takes from another's trap commits a form of *gezel*. The reason goes beyond mere conflict avoidance: the very institution of trapping as a livelihood depends on trappers being able to rely on ownership of their catch.

Rabbi Yose says: *gezel gamur* — it is real theft. The Gemara will explain that Rabbi Yose means rabbinic theft — the Rabbis enacted that taking from the trap constitutes genuine *gezel* rabbinically — rather than merely establishing a convention. The difference between the *tanna kama* and Rabbi Yose is thus not whether the trapper has a right, but whether the Rabbis articulated that right as a true *gezel* or merely as a *darchei shalom* convention.

### Lost Property

*Metziat cheresh shoteh v'katan yesh bahem mishum gezel mipnei darchei shalom* — lost objects found by a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or minor are treated as their property for purposes of *darchei shalom*, even though these individuals lack the full legal capacity to effect a *kinyan* through finding. Taking from them what they have found is treated as *gezel*. Rabbi Yose again: *gezel gamur* — real rabbinic theft.

### Olive Knocker

*Ani hamenakef berosh hazayit* — a poor person who has climbed to the top of an olive tree and knocked olives down has not yet formally acquired them, since he has not yet performed *hagbahah* or another recognized *kinyan*. If someone passes by and takes the olives lying below, *ma shetachtav gezel mipnei darchei shalom* — what is beneath him is considered stolen *mipnei darchei shalom*. Rabbi Yose: *gezel gamur*. The pattern is consistent throughout this section.

### Non-Jewish Poor

*Ein memachin b'yad aniyei ovdei kochavim beleket shichcha u'peah mipnei darchei shalom* — one does not prevent non-Jewish poor from collecting *leket*, *shichcha*, and *peah* from the fields, for the sake of *darchei shalom*. It is important to situate this ruling within the context of what *darchei shalom* has meant throughout this *mishnah*. It is not simply about cultivating good relations with non-Jewish neighbors for self-interested reasons. *Darchei shalom* here expresses a value of peaceful, well-ordered society in which people's rights are respected and the vulnerable are not excluded. This reading is supported by the Rambam's use of the verse *deracheha darchei noam v'chol netivoteha shalom* in a related context — all the ways of the Torah are ways of pleasantness and peace.

## Kohen Honor

### Biblical Sources

The Gemara now asks: *mina hani milei* — what is the biblical source for giving *kohanim* the first *aliyah*? Three sources are offered. Rav Mattana cites: *vayichtov Moshe et hatorah hazot vayitnah el hakohanim bnei Levi* — Moshe wrote the Torah and gave it to the *kohanim bnei Levi*. The Gemara asks the obvious: we already know that *kohanim* are *bnei Levi* — why state both? The answer: to teach precedence — first *kohanim*, then *levi*. This functions as an *asmachta* for the ordering in *krias hatorah*.

Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha cites: *venigshu hakohanim bnei Levi* — and the *kohanim bnei Levi* shall draw near. Again, the doubling of the description teaches the order of precedence. Rav Ashi cites: *bnei Amram Aharon uMoshe vayibadel Aharon le'hakdisho kodesh kodashim* — Aharon was separated and sanctified as *kodesh kodashim*, above even Moshe, who was a *levi*. This establishes that the *kohen* occupies a higher level of *kedushah* than the *levi*.

### Honor Rights

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba derives the principle from the *pasuk* *ve'kidashto* — you shall sanctify him — applied to the *kohen*, *lekhol davar shebikdusha*: in every matter of sanctity or honor. This manifests in three specific honors: *liftoach rishon* — he opens first (gives the first speech or *drashah*); *ulvarekh rishon* — he leads *birkat hamazon*; and *ulitol manah yafah b'rishon* — at a meal, he has first choice of the food. These are matters of *kavod* in social settings, not financial matters like dividing an estate.

### De'oraisa Status

This sets up a striking exchange between Abaye and Rav Yosef. If *ve'kidashto* establishes a *d'Oraita* obligation to honor *kohanim* in these ways, then the *kohen*'s precedence in *krias hatorah* is not merely a rabbinic convention for *darchei shalom* — it reflects a *d'Oraita* principle. Abaye asks Rav Yosef directly: *mipnei darchei shalom, d'Oraita?*

Rav Yosef responds: *d'Oraita u'mipnei darchei shalom* — it is *d'Oraita*, and the Torah itself commands it *mipnei darchei shalom*. This is a puzzling formulation: the Torah's command to honor *kohanim* is rooted in their elevated *kedushah* and the recognition of their special role, not in *darchei shalom* as such.

Abaye pushes back sharply: *kol hatorah kulah nami mipnei darchei shalom* — if you use that reasoning, then every *d'Oraita* law is *darchei shalom*, since *de'chtiv deracheha darchei noam v'chol netivoteha shalom* — the entire Torah is characterized as ways of pleasantness and peace. This is an extraordinarily powerful statement. If every Torah obligation can be described as serving *shalom*, then *darchei shalom* becomes a vacuous explanation. Abaye's point is that one cannot collapse the specific category of *darchei shalom* into the general theological claim that Torah promotes peace.

Abaye therefore offers an alternative synthesis, citing what the *mar* — Rabbah bar Nachmani — taught. A *baraita* states: if two people are eating together, one waits for the other before beginning; with three, one need not wait. The one who recited *ha-motzi* has first access to the shared dishes, but may yield his priority to his *rebbe* or to someone of greater stature if he chooses. And on this Rabbah taught: *lo shanu ela b'seudah* — the option to waive one's *kavod* applies at a private meal. *Aval b'veit haknesset lo, de'atu le'intzuyei* — but not in *shul*, because it leads to fights.

### No Waiving

The synthesis now becomes clear. The *d'Oraita* obligation of *ve'kidashto* establishes that a *kohen* must be honored in settings involving *kedushah* — and *krias hatorah* in *shul* is such a setting. That honor manifests as receiving the first *aliyah*. However, were the *kohen* permitted to waive this honor at will — to say, I am *mochel*, let my friend take the first *aliyah* — the result would be precisely the competitive争 claiming of honor that the *darchei shalom* ordering was designed to prevent. People would lobby to be chosen, others would feel slighted for not being chosen, and communal harmony would break down. The rabbinic layer, then, is not the *kohen*'s honor itself — that is *d'Oraita* — but the prohibition on the *kohen*'s waiving that honor in *shul*. This prohibition is the *darchei shalom* element: it protects the institution by ensuring it remains stable and non-negotiable.

### Day Distinction

Rav Mattana adds a further refinement: the prohibition on the *kohen* waiving his *kavod* in *shul* applies specifically on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* — *d'shechichi rabim*, when there is a large crowd in *shul*. On Mondays and Thursdays, when attendance is smaller, the *kohen* may be *mochel* on his *kavod* without generating the *darchei shalom* concerns that would arise in a full public setting.

Here the daf concludes. The discussion of *kohanim* and *aliyot* and the interplay between *d'Oraita* honor and rabbinic *darchei shalom* protections will continue in the following daf.
