# Sukkah 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ezrat Nashim & Mechitzah

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמוֹצָאֵי יוֹם טוֹב כּוּ׳. מַאי תִּיקּוּן גָּדוֹל? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: חֲלָקָה הָיְתָה בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה. וְהִקִּיפוּהָ גְּזוּזְטְרָא, וְהִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ נָשִׁים יוֹשְׁבוֹת מִלְמַעְלָה וַאֲנָשִׁים מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** At the conclusion of the first Festival day, etc., the priests and the Levites descended from the Israelites' courtyard to the Women's Courtyard, where they would introduce a significant repair. The Gemara asks: What is this significant repair? Rabbi Elazar said that it is like that which we learned: The walls of the Women's Courtyard were smooth, without protrusions, initially. Subsequently, they affixed protrusions to the wall surrounding the Women's Courtyard. Each year thereafter, for the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water, they placed wooden planks on these projections and surrounded the courtyard with a balcony [*gezuztra*]. And they instituted that the women should sit above and the men below.

Daf 52 covers two major topics. The first is a *gemara* that has been used classically — and in more recent periods with particular intensity — as the primary basis for the requirement of a *mechitzah* in a *shul*. The remainder of the *daf* constitutes an extended *agadata* centered on the concept of *yetzer hara*, touching on its nature, its names, its strategies, and ultimately its messianic defeat.

### Tikun Gadol

The *gemara* begins seven lines from the bottom of 51b. The *mishnah* states that on the night following *Yom Tov*, they would enter the *Ezrat Nashim* and perform a *tikun gadol* — a great fixing-up — in preparation for the *Simchat Beit Hashoeva* celebrations, which would take place at night and continue into the following morning when the water was drawn from the well.

The *gemara* asks: *mai tikun gadol* — what exactly was this great arrangement? Rabbi Eleazar answers by citing a *mishnah* in *Middot*: originally, the *azara* — the *Ezrat Nashim* — was flat and smooth. Then they surrounded it with a *gazustra* and instituted that women should sit above and men below. This, it turns out, was the first balcony arrangement in Jewish communal life.

### Gazustra Interpretation

There is an interesting debate, based on the *Rambam* in *Peirush Hamishnayot* and others, about precisely what the *gazustra* was and what *milmala umilmata* — above and below — means in practice. One might ask: if the arrangement involved women sitting above, that clearly suggests a balcony. But the *mishnah* is contrasting this with the fact that the floor had previously been smooth and flat. Building a balcony does not make the floor unflat — you could support it with poles — so the description seems to be pointing at something else.

*Rashi* explains that the walls had previously been smooth, and they then fashioned protrusions from the walls to support a balcony — the *gazustra* being these wall-projections. Steinsaltz notes that the word derives from a Greek term for a projection from the side of a building upon which boards are laid, which aligns with *Rashi*'s reading. Others, however — and the *Rambam* in *Peirush Hamishnayot* seems to lean in this direction — suggest that it was less a suspended balcony and more a raised platform on the floor, behind which women would stand. Why a raised platform rather than a balcony? Perhaps so that women standing behind a barrier could still see what was happening. In any case, the plain meaning of *milmala umilmata* most naturally points to a balcony arrangement.

### Separation Progression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ נָשִׁים מִבִּפְנִים וַאֲנָשִׁים מִבַּחוּץ, וְהָיוּ בָּאִים לִידֵי קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ נָשִׁים יוֹשְׁבוֹת מִבַּחוּץ וַאֲנָשִׁים מִבִּפְנִים, וַעֲדַיִין הָיוּ בָּאִין לִידֵי קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ נָשִׁים יוֹשְׁבוֹת מִלְּמַעְלָה וַאֲנָשִׁים מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in the *Tosefta*: Initially, women would stand on the inside of the Women's Courtyard, closer to the Sanctuary to the west, and the men were on the outside in the courtyard and on the rampart. And they would come to conduct themselves with inappropriate levity in each other's company, as the men needed to enter closer to the altar when the offerings were being sacrificed and as a result they would mingle with the women. Therefore, the Sages instituted that the women should sit on the outside and the men on the inside, and still they would come to conduct themselves with inappropriate levity. Therefore, they instituted in the interest of complete separation that the women would sit above and the men below.

The *gemara* then cites a *Tana* teaching that describes the progression of separation arrangements. Originally, *barishona hayu nashim mibifnim veanashim mibachutz* — women were on the inside and men on the outside. *Rashi* suggests that "outside" might mean outside the *Ezrat Nashim* entirely, on the *Har Habayit* itself. But *vehayu baim lidei kalut rosh* — this arrangement led to levity, to inappropriate mixing and heightened sexual tension. So they reversed it: women outside, men inside. Yet *ve'adayin hayu baim lidei kalut rosh* — even that still led to frivolity. Finally, they instituted that women sit entirely above and men below, which completely prevented interaction.

### Authority Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עֲבִיד הָכִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״הַכֹּל בִּכְתָב מִיַּד ה׳ עָלַי הִשְׂכִּיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How could one do so, i.e., alter the structure of the Temple? But isn't it written with regard to the Temple: "All this I give you in writing, as the Lord has made me wise by His hand upon me, even all the works of this pattern" (I Chronicles 28:19), meaning that all the structural plans of the Temple were divinely inspired; how could the Sages institute changes?

This raises a striking question: *heichi avid hachi* — how could they do this? Does not the verse say *hakol bichsav miyad Hashem alai hiskil* — that everything about the construction of the *Beit Hamikdash* was revealed from God's hand, as King David declared when transmitting the plans for the *Beit Hamikdash*? Admittedly, this question carries several complications: that verse refers to the *Beit Rishon*, whereas this is the *Beit Sheni*; the *Beit Sheni* already differed substantially from the *Beit Rishon* in many respects; and the *Ezrat Nashim* is not technically part of the *Beit Hamikdash* proper. But whatever the precise contours of the question, the *gemara* proceeds to provide an answer.

### Kal Vachomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: קְרָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ וּדְרוּשׁ — ״וְסָפְדָה הָאָרֶץ מִשְׁפָּחוֹת מִשְׁפָּחוֹת לְבָד מִשְׁפַּחַת בֵּית דָּוִד לְבָד וּנְשֵׁיהֶם לְבָד״. אָמְרוּ: וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּהֶסְפֵּד וְאֵין יֵצֶר הָרָע שׁוֹלֵט בָּהֶם — אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה אֲנָשִׁים לְבַד וְנָשִׁים לְבַד. עַכְשָׁיו, שֶׁעֲסוּקִין בְּשִׂמְחָה וְיֵצֶר הָרָע שׁוֹלֵט בָּהֶם — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav said: They found a verse, and interpreted it homiletically — "The land will eulogize, each family separately; the family of the house of David separately, and their women separately, the family of the house of Nathan separately, and their women separately" (Zechariah 12:12). This indicates that at the end of days a great eulogy will be organized during which men and women will be separate. They said: And are these matters not inferred *a fortiori*? If in the future, at the end of days referred to in this prophecy, when people are involved in a great eulogy and consequently the evil inclination does not dominate them, as typically during mourning inappropriate thoughts and conduct are less likely, and nevertheless the Torah says: Men separately and women separately; then now that they are involved in the Celebration of the Drawing of the Water, and as such the evil inclination dominates them, since celebration lends itself to levity, all the more so should men and women be separate.

Rav answers: *kra ashkechu v'darash* — they found a *pasuk* and explicated it to justify the modification. The verse is from *Zechariah*, in a prophecy about the war of Armageddon: *veSafda ha'aretz mishpachot mishpachot levad* — the land will eulogize, family by family, separately. And specifically: *mishpachat beit David levad u'nesheihem levad* — the family of the house of David separately, and their wives separately. Within each family, men and women mourn apart.

From this, the Sages constructed a *kal v'chomer*: *u'mah le'atid lavo she'oskim b'hesped* — in the future time, when everyone is occupied with mourning, *v'ein yetzer hara sholet bahem* — and the *yetzer hara* does not have dominion over them. This could mean simply that at a time of tragedy and grief, sexual desire naturally subsides. But *Rashi* adds a second dimension: since this is a messianic vision, it is a time in history when the *yetzer hara* will have been eliminated entirely. And yet, *amra Torah anashim levad v'nashim levad* — even then, the Torah requires men and women to be separate.

*Al achat kama v'kama* — how much more so in the present, when *she'oskim b'simcha* — people are engaged in joy and festivity, which heightens desire and leads to frivolity, and when *yetzer hara sholet bahem* — the *yetzer hara* is very much operative. This was their justification for implementing the separation.

### Modern Mechitzah Practice

This *gemara* has become, in halachic literature of recent centuries, one of the primary textual bases for the requirement of a *mechitzah* between men and women during *davening*. It is worth noting something striking: there is no explicit statement anywhere in the *gemara* that a *mechitzah* is required during *davening*, nor any explicit statement that men and women were mixed during *davening*, nor any explicit statement that they were separated. The historical evidence for the practice dates from the time of the *Geonim* onward, where separate seating appears simply to have been taken for granted and required no justification. The need to articulate and defend the requirement arose later.

The issue first became contested in the period of the *Chatam Sofer*, even before the Reform movement had introduced mixed seating. Mixed seating appears, in fact, to have originated in America — the Albany congregation being a commonly cited early example, rather than in early European Reform communities. When the *Chatam Sofer* addressed the topic — initially in the context of a question about a mixed choir to be presented before the Czar in Saint Petersburg, which led into a broader discussion of *kol b'isha erva* and the separation of the sexes — this *gemara* became a central source.

There are, however, real difficulties in using this *gemara* as the proof-text for a *mechitzah* during *davening*. The argument cuts in two opposing directions simultaneously. On one hand, it seems too narrow: the concern here is with an atmosphere of festivity and frivolity — *Simchat Beit Hashoeva* — which might suggest the requirement applies only in such contexts, not during the solemnity of prayer. But then the *gemara* itself counters this by constructing the *kal v'chomer* from the scene of mourning, when there is no such frivolity and when the *yetzer hara* is absent — and still men and women are separate. That makes the argument too broad: it would require separation at all times and in all contexts. How one derives specifically the middle position — that separation is required during *davening* but not universally — is genuinely unclear from this *gemara* alone.

The question of *what kind* of separation is also illuminated by this source. The *gemara* presents two models: the balcony and the side-by-side separation. If a balcony ultimately suffices — and that is indeed what was instituted in the end — then the concern is with intermingling and physical contact, not with visual separation, since from a balcony one can see perfectly well. If, on the other hand, the relevant model is the side-by-side division with a wall, then perhaps sight itself is the concern. The plain sense of this *gemara* strongly underscores the intermingling concern, and this has significant implications for how one understands the nature and purpose of *mechitzah* requirements derived from this source.

### Yetzer Hara Development

Before proceeding further into the *sugya*, it is worth pausing on the association already present in the *gemara* between *kalut rosh* — levity — and *yetzer hara* in the context of the mixing of men and women. This raises a broader question about what exactly *yetzer hara* means and how it has been understood across rabbinic periods. A careful reading of the literature suggests that our instinctive contemporary understanding — that *yetzer hara* is an internalized psychological drive, often primarily associated with sexual desire, to be contrasted with the *yetzer hatov* — may not reflect the earlier, Tannaitic conception.

The phrase *yetzer hara sholet bahem* — the *yetzer hara* has dominion over them — is itself ambiguous. Is *sholet* describing an internal dominion, or something more like a demonic external force that exercises power over us from outside? The earlier understanding may have been closer to the latter: an external enticement toward evil, not specifically associated with appetitive desire, akin to a demonic power that tempts human beings to sin. The shift toward the internalized, psychological model — the *yetzer hara* as one's own drives and inclinations, powerful energies that can be channeled for good or ill — appears to develop over time.

As this image from the prophecy of *Kayin* illustrates — *lappetach chatat roveitz, ve-atah timshol bo* — the *yetzer hara* is depicted crouching at the entrance, an external, almost animal force, yet one that the human being is capable of mastering. As we read through the rest of the *daf*, this interplay between the external and internal models will be visible throughout.

## Mashiach ben Yosef vs Yetzer Hara

### Eulogy Identity Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא הֶסְפֵּידָא מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי דּוֹסָא וְרַבָּנַן. חַד אָמַר: עַל מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן יוֹסֵף שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג, וְחַד אָמַר: עַל יֵצֶר הָרַע שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the eulogy at the end of days, the Gemara asks: For what is the nature of this eulogy? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Dosa and the Rabbis disagree concerning this matter. One said that this eulogy is for Messiah ben Yosef who was killed in the war of Gog from the land of Magog prior to the ultimate redemption with the coming of Messiah ben David. And one said that this eulogy is for the evil inclination that was killed.

The *gemara* now identifies the subject of the mourning described in *Zechariah*. *Peligei bah Rabbi Dosa veRabbanan* — Rabbi Dosa and the Rabbis debate whose eulogy this is. One view says: *mashiach ben Yosef sheneharg* — it is the mourning over *Mashiach ben Yosef* who was killed. The concept of *Mashiach ben Yosef* does not appear explicitly in any single canonical source, but it is a well-developed Rabbinic understanding. Based on the verse *vehayah beit Yosef eish uveit Yaakov lehavah vehayah Eisav lekash* — the house of *Yosef* compared to a fire — the Rabbis understand that the leader who will go to war against the enemies of Israel in the eschatological battle will be from the house of *Yosef*, a figure known as *Mashiach ben Yosef*. He will die in that war, after which *Mashiach ben David*, the ultimate *Mashiach*, will arrive. It is *Mashiach ben Yosef*'s death that triggers the mourning.

The alternative view: *ve-chad amar al yetzer hara sheneharg* — the mourning is over the *yetzer hara*, which will be killed in the messianic era.

### Logical Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עַל מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן יוֹסֵף שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִבִּיטוּ אֵלַי אֵת אֲשֶׁר דָּקָרוּ וְסָפְדוּ עָלָיו כְּמִסְפֵּד עַל הַיָּחִיד״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עַל יֵצֶר הָרַע שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג — הַאי הֶסְפֵּידָא בָּעֵי לְמֶעְבַּד? שִׂמְחָה בָּעֵי לְמֶעְבַּד! אַמַּאי בָּכוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Granted, according to the one who said that the lament is for Messiah ben Yosef who was killed, this would be the meaning of that which is written in that context: "And they shall look unto Me because they have thrust him through; and they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for his only son" (Zechariah 12:10). However, according to the one who said that the eulogy is for the evil inclination that was killed, does one need to conduct a eulogy for this? On the contrary, one should conduct a celebration. Why, then, did they cry?

This second view immediately encounters a devastating logical objection. *Bishlama le-man d'amar Mashiach ben Yosef sheneharg* — the view that it refers to *Mashiach ben Yosef* works well: *hayinu dechtiv vehibitu elai et asher dakaru* — and they will look toward me, the one who was pierced, *ve-safdu alav ke-misped al ha-yachid* — and they will mourn over him like the mourning over an only child. That makes sense: a heroic leader falls in battle, and the people mourn him deeply.

But *ella le-man d'amar al yetzer hara sheneharg* — if it refers to the *yetzer hara* being destroyed — *hai speda ba'ei le-me'ebad?* Why would you hold a eulogy? *Simcha ba'ei le-me'ebad!* You should be having a celebration! And beyond the question of the eulogy: *amai bachu?* Why were they crying?

### Mourning Perspectives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְדָרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, מְבִיאוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיֵצֶר הָרָע, וְשׁוֹחֲטוֹ בִּפְנֵי הַצַּדִּיקִים וּבִפְנֵי הָרְשָׁעִים. צַדִּיקִים נִדְמֶה לָהֶם כְּהַר גָּבוֹהַּ, וּרְשָׁעִים נִדְמֶה לָהֶם כְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה. הַלָּלוּ בּוֹכִין וְהַלָּלוּ בּוֹכִין. צַדִּיקִים בּוֹכִין וְאוֹמְרִים: הֵיאַךְ יָכוֹלְנוּ לִכְבּוֹשׁ הַר גָּבוֹהַּ כָּזֶה! וּרְשָׁעִים בּוֹכִין וְאוֹמְרִים: הֵיאַךְ לֹא יָכוֹלְנוּ לִכְבּוֹשׁ אֶת חוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה הַזֶּה! וְאַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא תָּמֵהַּ עִמָּהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת כִּי יִפָּלֵא בְּעֵינֵי שְׁאֵרִית הָעָם הַזֶּה בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם גַּם בְּעֵינַי יִפָּלֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This can be understood as Rabbi Yehuda taught: In the future, at the end of days, God will bring the evil inclination and slaughter it in the presence of the righteous and in the presence of the wicked. For the righteous the evil inclination appears to them as a high mountain, and for the wicked it appears to them as a mere strand of hair. These weep and those weep. The righteous weep and say: How were we able to overcome so high a mountain? And the wicked weep and say: How were we unable to overcome this strand of hair? And even the Holy One, Blessed be He, will wonder with them, as it is stated with regard to the eulogy: "So says the Lord of hosts: If it be wondrous in the eyes of the remnant of this people in those days, it should also be wondrous in My eyes" (Zechariah 8:6).

The answer comes from the teaching of Rabbi Yehuda. *Le-atid lavo*, in the future time, *mevi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-yetzer hara* — God will bring the *yetzer hara* and *ve-shochato bifnei ha-tzaddikim uvifnei ha-resha'im* — slaughter it before the righteous and before the wicked. *Tzaddikim nidmeh lahem ke-har gavoha* — to the righteous it appears like an enormous mountain; *ve-resha'im nidmeh lahem ke-chut hase'ara* — to the wicked it appears like a single thread of hair. *Halalu bochim ve-halalu bochim* — these cry and those cry.

The righteous cry and say: *heich yacholnu lichbosh har gavoha kaze* — how did we ever manage to conquer something as immense as this mountain? The wicked cry and say: *heich lo yacholnu lichbosh et chut hase'ara haze* — how could we not have overcome something as trivial as this thread?

### Mountain & Hair

The *Maharsha* makes a brilliant observation about the imagery of the mountain and the hair. *Har Seir* — Mount Seir — is the paradigmatic home of *Eisav*, who represents both the human enemies of Israel and, allegorically, the *yetzer hara* itself. *Har* is the mountain; *se'ar* is hair. The same root thus encapsulates both images simultaneously, making the choice of metaphors anything but arbitrary.

### Righteous Tears

Why exactly the righteous are weeping is not immediately obvious. *Rashi* explains: they are crying as they recall how difficult the struggle with the *yetzer hara* was throughout their lives. It is a weeping over past suffering and difficulty — the enormity of what they had to overcome.

### Lost Opportunity

The *Maharsha* offers a different reading. He notes that the plain sense of the text seems to suggest the righteous are crying from joy — and indeed that would make sense. But if it is joy, it is not a *hesped*, a eulogy. So perhaps, he suggests, the righteous are crying because they are now losing the opportunity for religious struggle — the struggle through which they earned their reward. Now that the *yetzer hara* is gone, the arena of striving and merit has closed. That loss is genuinely mourned.

There remains a further puzzle: why does the *yetzer hara* appear to the righteous as a mountain and to the wicked as a thread? Intuitively, one would expect the reverse. The image seems to suggest that in some sense, the truth is revealed: for the wicked, who always complained that resisting temptation was impossibly hard, it becomes clear that it was never more than a thread — it only needed a small act of will to begin redirecting the course of one's life. Once a person begins making incremental improvements, each step is manageable, and looking back one realizes the path was not as impossible as it seemed. Like someone who has never run before who trains steadily and eventually completes a marathon — while in the training, no single step feels like climbing a mountain; only in retrospect does the enormity become apparent.

For the righteous, the reverse is true: they did not experience the *yetzer hara* as an overwhelming obstacle because they were constantly exercising the right spiritual muscles, making incremental progress. But when the scales finally fall from their eyes, they see that what they conquered was, in fact, a mountain of immense proportions.

### God's Astonishment

*Ve-af ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu tameh imahem* — and even God is astonished with them. As the verse says: *ki yipaleh be-einei she'erit ha-am haze ba-yamim ha-heim, gam be-einai yipaleh* — if it will be wondrous in the eyes of this people in those days, even in My eyes it will be wondrous. Whether God's astonishment is directed at the *resha'im* — how could you not have managed this? — or at the *tzaddikim* — how did you manage to accomplish that much? — is left open.

## Spider Web to Wagon Cord

### Rav Assi Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: יֵצֶר הָרָע בַּתְּחִילָּה דּוֹמֶה לְחוּט שֶׁל בּוּכְיָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף דּוֹמֶה כַּעֲבוֹתוֹת הָעֲגָלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי מוֹשְׁכֵי הֶעָוֹן בְּחַבְלֵי הַשָּׁוְא וְכַעֲבוֹת הָעֲגָלָה חַטָּאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the evil inclination and the battle against it, the Gemara cites that which Rav Asi said: Initially, when it begins to entice someone, the evil inclination is like a strand of a spider's web [*bukhya*]; and ultimately it is like the thick ropes of a wagon, as it is stated: "Woe unto them that draw iniquity with cords of vanity, and sin as if it were with a wagon rope" (Isaiah 5:18). Initially, the enticement is almost imperceptible, like a thin strand; however, after one sins, it is like wagon ropes tied tightly around him.

*Amar Rav Assi*: *yetzer hara batchila domeh le-chut shel buchya* — the *yetzer hara* originally resembles a spider web. This resonates directly with the imagery of the *chut hase'ara* discussed above: the initial temptation is barely perceptible, almost nothing.

### Accumulation Metaphor

But *u-vasof domeh ka-avotot ha-agala* — by the end, it resembles the thick cords used to drag a wagon. The dynamic is familiar: small temptations, if yielded to, build upon one another. Each capitulation makes the next easier, each sin strengthens the habit, until what began as a barely-visible thread has become an almost unbreakable cord. The pattern of addiction — the incremental strengthening of destructive habits until they feel impossible to escape — is precisely what is being described here with remarkable psychological acuity.

### Yeshayahu Source

*She-ne'emar: hoy moshchei he-avon be-chavlei ha-shava* — woe to those who drag sin upon themselves with ropes of nothingness: *u-cha-avotot ha-agala chata'ah* — and like the cords of a wagon, they bring sin upon themselves. It begins from nothing, *chavlei ha-shava*, ropes of emptiness, and ends with the thickness of wagon cords.

## Mashiach ben David

### God's Offer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן דָּוִד שֶׁעָתִיד לְהִגָּלוֹת בִּמְהֵרָה בְּיָמֵינוּ, אוֹמֵר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: שְׁאַל מִמֶּנִּי דָּבָר וְאֶתֵּן לָךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲסַפְּרָה אֶל חוֹק וְגוֹ׳ אֲנִי הַיּוֹם יְלִדְתִּיךָ. שְׁאַל מִמֶּנִּי וְאֶתְּנָה גוֹיִם נַחֲלָתֶךָ״. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁרָאָה מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן יוֹסֵף שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג, אוֹמֵר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! אֵינִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִמְּךָ אֶלָּא חַיִּים. אוֹמֵר לוֹ: חַיִּים, עַד שֶׁלֹּא אָמַרְתָּ כְּבָר הִתְנַבֵּא עָלֶיךָ דָּוִד אָבִיךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חַיִּים שָׁאַל מִמְּךָ נָתַתָּה לוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: To Messiah ben David, who is destined to be revealed swiftly in our time, the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: Ask of Me anything and I will give you whatever you wish, as it is stated: "I will tell of the decree; the Lord said unto me: You are My son, this day have I begotten you, ask of Me, and I will give the nations for your inheritance, and the ends of the earth for your possession" (Psalms 2:7–8). Once the Messiah ben David saw Messiah ben Yosef, who was killed, he says to the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, I ask of you only life; that I will not suffer the same fate. The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to him: Life? Even before you stated this request, your father, David, already prophesied about you with regard to this matter precisely, as it is stated: "He asked life of You, You gave it to him; even length of days for ever and ever" (Psalms 21:5).

*Tanu rabanan*: *Mashiach ben David she-atid lehitgalot bimheira be-yameinu* — *Mashiach ben David*, who will be revealed speedily in our days. The *gemara* invokes this title — rather than simply *Mashiach* — because the discussion has been distinguishing between *Mashiach ben Yosef* and *Mashiach ben David*. At the moment of revelation, *omer lo ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu: she-al mimeni davar ve-eten lach* — God says to him: ask me for anything and I will give it to you. The source is the opening of *Tehillim Perek 2*, which in context is understood as a messianic psalm: *asapra el chok, ani ha-yom yeladeticha* — I will tell of the decree, I have today given birth to you — and then: *she-al mimeni ve-etnah goyim nachalatecha* — ask of me and I will give the nations as your inheritance.

### Mashiach's Request

But then — *ve-keivan she-ra'ah Mashiach ben Yosef she-neherag* — when *Mashiach ben David* sees that *Mashiach ben Yosef* has been killed in battle, his response is striking. *Amar lefanav: Ribono shel Olam, eini mevakesh mimcha ela chayim* — Master of the Universe, I ask nothing of you but life. He does not ask to conquer nations, to inherit the earth, to be given worldly dominion. In the face of death and war and the killing of *Mashiach ben Yosef*, he asks only for *chayim*. Whether he is asking for his own life — not to die as *Mashiach ben Yosef* did — or for life in a more universal sense, a world without death, perhaps anticipating *techiyat hameitim*, is not entirely clear. But the choice is deliberate: he turns away from conquest and toward life.

### God's Reply

*Omer lo*: *chayim? Ad she-lo amarta kvar hitnabei alecha David avicha* — life? Before you even asked, your father David already prophesied that this would be given to you. *She-ne'emar: chayim sha-al mimcha natata lo* — he asked of You life, and You gave it to him. The *mizmor* referenced is one that speaks of God's gifts to the king, here read as referring to *Mashiach*. The request was already granted before it was made.

## Seven Names of Yetzer Hara

### Seven Names Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי עַוִּירָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: שִׁבְעָה שֵׁמוֹת יֵשׁ לוֹ לְיֵצֶר הָרָע. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא קְרָאוֹ ״רַע״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יֵצֶר לֵב הָאָדָם רַע מִנְּעוּרָיו״. מֹשֶׁה קְרָאוֹ ״עָרֵל״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמַלְתֶּם אֵת עׇרְלַת לְבַבְכֶם״. דָּוִד קְרָאוֹ ״טָמֵא״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֵב טָהוֹר בְּרָא לִי אֱלֹהִים״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Avira, and some say Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, taught: The evil inclination has seven names. The Holy One, Blessed be He, called it evil, as it is stated: "For the inclination of a man's heart is evil from his youth" (Genesis 8:21). Moses called it uncircumcised, as it is stated: "And circumcise the foreskin of your hearts" (Deuteronomy 10:16). David called it impure, as it is stated: "Create for me a pure heart, O God" (Psalms 51:12); by inference, there is an impure heart that is the evil inclination.

*Darash Rav Avira ve-iteima Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the *yetzer hara* has seven names. Each name was given by a different figure — God, Moses, David, Solomon, Isaiah, Ezekiel, and Joel — and each reflects a different aspect of what the *yetzer hara* is and how it operates.

### God's Name: Evil

God called it *ra* — evil. *She-ne'emar: ki yetzer lev ha-adam ra mine-urav* — for the inclination of the human heart is evil from its youth.

### Moses: Uncircumcised

Moses called it *arel* — uncircumcised. *She-ne'emar: u-maltem et orlat levavchem* — you shall circumcise the foreskin of your hearts. The *yetzer hara* is the spiritual foreskin that covers and constricts the heart.

### David: Impure

David called it *tamei* — impure. *She-ne'emar: lev tahor bera li Elohim* — create for me a pure heart, O God. *Miklal de-ika tamei* — the implication being that there is also an impure heart.

### Solomon: Enemy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹמֹה קְרָאוֹ ״שׂוֹנֵא״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם רָעֵב שֹׂנַאֲךָ הַאֲכִילֵהוּ לָחֶם וְאִם צָמֵא הַשְׁקֵהוּ מָיִם כִּי גֶחָלִים אַתָּה חוֹתֶה עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וַה׳ יְשַׁלֶּם לָךְ״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״יְשַׁלֶּם לָךְ״, אֶלָּא ״יַשְׁלִימֶנּוּ לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** Solomon called it enemy, as it is stated: "If your enemy is hungry, give him bread to eat, and if he is thirsty, give him water to drink; for you will heap coals of fire upon his head, and the Lord will reward you" (Proverbs 25:21–22). Do not read it as: And the Lord will reward you [*yeshalem lakh*]; rather read it as: And the Lord will reconcile it to you [*yashlimenu lakh*]. God will cause the evil inclination to love you and no longer seek to entice you to sin.

Solomon called it *sonei* — hater, enemy. *She-ne'emar: im ra-ev sonecha he-achilehu lechem ve-im tzamei hashkehu mayim* — if your enemy is hungry, feed him bread; if he is thirsty, give him water; *ki gechalim ata choteh al rosho* — for in doing so you are heaping coals on his head. *Al tikrei yeshalem lach ela yashlimenu lach* — do not read it as God repaying you, but as God making peace between you and it. *Rashi* explains: this is being read not as literally about human enemies, but about the *yetzer hara* as your enemy. And what does it mean to feed it bread and water? It means feeding it *Torah*. If the *yetzer hara* is hungry and demands to be fed with sin, feed it instead with *Torah* — and you will be able to redirect and ultimately make peace with it.

### Isaiah: Stumbling Block

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְשַׁעְיָה קְרָאוֹ ״מִכְשׁוֹל״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סוֹלּוּ סוֹלּוּ פַּנּוּ דָרֶךְ הָרִימוּ מִכְשׁוֹל מִדֶּרֶךְ עַמִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** Isaiah called it a stumbling block, as it is stated: "And He will say: Cast you up, cast you up, clear the way, take up the stumbling block out of the way of My people" (Isaiah 57:14).

Isaiah called it *michshol* — a stumbling block. *She-ne'emar: solu solu panu derech, harimu michshol miderech ami* — clear, clear the way, remove the stumbling block from the path of my people.

### Ezekiel: Stone Heart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְחֶזְקֵאל קְרָאוֹ ״אֶבֶן״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַהֲסִרֹתִי אֶת לֵב הָאֶבֶן מִבְּשַׂרְכֶם וְנָתַתִּי לָכֶם לֵב בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** Ezekiel called it stone, as it is stated: "And I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you a heart of flesh" (Ezekiel 36:26).

Ezekiel called it *even* — stone. *She-ne'emar: va-hasiroti et lev ha-even mi-vsarchem ve-natati lachem lev basar* — I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh.

### Joel: Hidden One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹאֵל קְרָאוֹ ״צְפוֹנִי״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶת הַצְּפוֹנִי אַרְחִיק מֵעֲלֵיכֶם״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאֶת הַצְּפוֹנִי אַרְחִיק מֵעֲלֵיכֶם״ — זֶה יֵצֶר הָרָע, שֶׁצָּפוּן וְעוֹמֵד בְּלִבּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** Joel called it hidden one, as it says: "But I will remove the northern one [*hatzefoni*] far off from you" (Joel 2:20). The Sages taught concerning the verse: "But I will remove the northern one [*hatzefoni*] far off from you," that this is referring to the evil inclination. And why is the evil inclination referred to as *tzefoni*? It is due to the fact that it is always hidden [*tzafun*] in the heart of man.

Joel called it *tzefoni* — the northern one, or the hidden one. *She-ne'emar: ve-et ha-tzefoni archik me-aleichem* — I will distance the hidden one from you.

It is worth noting, in light of the earlier discussion about the internal versus external conception of *yetzer hara*, that the seven names are not uniform in their orientation. The uncircumcised heart, the stone heart, the pure and impure heart — these are deeply internal images. The stumbling block and the enemy are external. The "evil" and the "hidden one" occupy an ambiguous middle ground. The tension between externalized and internalized understandings of the *yetzer hara* is built into the very language the tradition uses to name it.

## Assault on Beit Hamikdash

### Joel Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִדַּחְתִּיו אֶל אֶרֶץ צִיָּה וּשְׁמָמָה״ — לִמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין בְּנֵי אָדָם מְצוּיִין לְהִתְגָּרוֹת בָּהֶן. ״אֶת פָּנָיו אֶל הַיָּם הַקַּדְמוֹנִי״ — שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו בְּמִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן וְהֶחְרִיבוֹ, וְהָרַג תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבּוֹ. ״וְסוֹפוֹ אֶל הַיָּם הָאַחֲרוֹן״ — שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי וְהֶחְרִיבוֹ, וְהָרַג תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבּוֹ. ״וְעָלָה בׇאְשׁוֹ וְתַעַל צַחֲנָתוֹ״ — שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, וּמִתְגָּרֶה בְּשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. ״כִּי הִגְדִּיל לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וּבְתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים יוֹתֵר מִכּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues interpreting the verse in the book of Joel. "And will drive it to a land barren and desolate" (Joel 2:20), where there are no people for the evil inclination to incite. And what damage does the evil inclination cause? "With its face toward the eastern [*hakadmoni*] sea" (Joel 2:20), as it set its eyes on the First [***mukdam***] Temple and destroyed it, and killed the Torah scholars that were in it; "and its end toward the western [*ha'aḥaron*] sea" (Joel 2:20), as it set its eyes on the Second [***aḥaron***] Temple and destroyed it, and killed the Torah scholars that were in it; "its foulness may come up, and its ill odor may come up" (Joel 2:20), as it forsakes the nations of the world and incites the enemies of the Jewish people: In this context, the term the nations is a euphemism for the Jewish people. The evil inclination seeks to corrupt the Jews more than it does the members of any other nation. "Because it has done greatly" (Joel 2:20): Abaye said: And it provokes Torah scholars more than it provokes everyone else.

*Tanu rabanan* — a Rabbinic teaching expands on Joel's verse. *Ve-et ha-tzefoni archik me-aleichem* — this is the *yetzer hara*, *she-tzafun ve-omed be-libo shel adam* — that is hidden and stands in the heart of a person. Here the image is explicitly internal: the *yetzer hara* is concealed within.

### Dispersal Metaphor

But then the verse continues: *ve-hidachtiv el eretz tziya u-shemama* — I will thrust it away to a barren and desolate land, *li-mkom she-ein bnei adam metzuyim le-hitgarot bahen* — to a place without people, so that it cannot entice or provoke anyone. This shifts back to an external image: the *yetzer hara* is taken out from inside the person and cast away to a desolate place where it has no one to tempt. The passage thus moves fluidly between the internal and external models, without resolving the tension between them.

### Beit Hamikdash Assault

The *gemara* then reads further into the verse. *Et panav el ha-yam ha-kadmoni* — his face toward the eastern sea. The word *yam* — sea — is interpreted as referring to the *Beit Hamikdash*, since just as God gathers all the waters, all people gather to the *Beit Hamikdash*. And *kadmon* — first, or eastern — refers to the *Beit Hamikdash Rishon*, the first Temple. *She-natan einav be-Mikdash rishon ve-hecherivo* — the *yetzer hara* turned its gaze toward the first *Beit Hamikdash* and destroyed it, *ve-harag talmidei chachamim she-bo* — and killed the Torah scholars within it.

*Ve-sofo el ha-yam ha-acharon* — and its end toward the western sea: *she-natan einav be-Mikdash sheni ve-hecherivo* — it also trained its gaze on the second *Beit Hamikdash* and destroyed it, *ve-harag talmidei chachamim she-bo* — and killed the Torah scholars within it. The pairing of the destruction of the *batei hamikdash* with the killing of *talmidei chachamim* is notably consistent and pointed.

### Enmity to Scholars

*Ve-ala va-asho ve-ta-al tzachanato* — and its stench rises. *She-maniach umot ha-olam u-mitgare be-son-eihem shel Yisrael* — it abandons the other nations and focuses its attacks on the Jewish people. *Ki higdil la-asot* — for it has acted greatly, hugely.

*Amar Abaye*: and specifically, *be-talmidei chachamim yoter mi-kulam* — the *yetzer hara* targets Torah scholars more than anyone else. The mention of *talmidei chachamim* has appeared twice already in the description of the *Beit Hamikdash*'s destruction; Abaye now makes this into an explicit principle.

### Abaye's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאַבָּיֵי שַׁמְעֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקָאָמַר לְהַהִיא אִתְּתָא: נַקְדֵּים וְנֵיזִיל בְּאוֹרְחָא. אֲמַר: אֵיזִיל אַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ מֵאִיסּוּרָא. אֲזַל בָּתְרַיְיהוּ תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי בְּאַגְמָא. כִּי הֲווֹ פָּרְשִׁי מֵהֲדָדֵי, שַׁמְעִינְהוּ דְּקָא אָמְרִי: אוֹרְחִין רַחִיקָא, וְצַוְותִּין בְּסִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara illustrates that point. It is like this incident, as Abaye once heard a certain man say to a certain woman: Let us rise early and go on the road. Upon hearing this, Abaye said to himself: I will go and accompany them and prevent them from violating the prohibition that they certainly intend to violate. He went after them for a distance of three parasangs in a marsh among the reeds, while they walked on the road, and they did not engage in any wrongful activity. When they were taking leave of each other, he heard that they were saying: We traveled a long distance together, and the company was pleasant company.

The *gemara* illustrates this with a famous story. Abaye once overheard a man saying to a woman: *nakdim ve-nizil be-orcha* — let us get up early and travel the road together. Abaye, suspecting an assignation in the making, decided to follow them discreetly to prevent anything improper from happening. He followed them for three *parsaot* — a considerable distance, perhaps eight miles or more — through a marshy field. When they reached a crossroads and parted ways, he heard them say to one another: *orcha rachika ve-tzavta basuma* — the road was long but the company was so pleasant. They had simply been walking companions, and nothing improper had occurred.

### Elder's Consolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִי מַאן דְּסָנֵי לִי הֲוָה, לָא הֲוָה מָצֵי לְאוֹקוֹמֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אֲזַל תְּלָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּעִיבּוּרָא דְּדָשָׁא וּמִצְטַעֵר. אֲתָא הָהוּא סָבָא, תְּנָא לֵיהּ: כׇּל הַגָּדוֹל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ — יִצְרוֹ גָּדוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: In that situation, if instead of that man it had been one whom I hate, a euphemism for himself, he would not have been able to restrain himself from sinning. After becoming aware of so great a shortcoming he went and leaned against the doorpost, thinking and feeling regret. A certain Elder came and taught him: Anyone who is greater than another, his evil inclination is greater than his. Therefore, Abaye should not feel regret, as his realization is a consequence of his greatness.

At this, Abaye said: if that had been himself — he speaks euphemistically, referring to himself as "the one who hates me" — *lo havay matzi le-okmei nafshay* — he would not have been able to withstand the temptation. He had assumed himself to be at a high level of religious and moral attainment, and now he realized that in those same circumstances, he would have been overcome. One might also note that the very act of following them — that sustained attention, traveling eight miles behind a couple — suggests something about where his own thoughts had already gone.

Abaye went and leaned against a doorpost in anguish, *u-mitztayer* — utterly despondent. *Ata hahu saba* — a certain elder came to him and taught him: *kol ha-gadol me-chavero yitzro gadol himenu* — the greater a person is than his fellow, the greater his *yetzer* is.

This principle invites the same internal/external tension noted throughout the *daf*. Read in the internalized, psychological register — and recalling the *midrash* that *Rashi* cites on *ve-hineh tov me'od zu yetzer hara* — the *yetzer hara* is understood as a powerful drive that, when properly channeled, propels great achievement. If it had not been for the *yetzer hara*, no one would build a house, no one would marry, no one would accomplish anything of consequence. The same ambition and desire that can lead a person astray can, when redirected, lead to monumental good. The *Ramban* reads *tov me'od* as meaning that creation is mostly good, and the *yetzer hara* represents the small part that is not — but the *midrash* seems to intend something more: that even the *yetzer hara* is part of what is *tov me'od*. From this perspective, *kol ha-gadol me-chavero yitzro gadol himenu* is straightforwardly true: the greatest people often have the most powerful drives, and what makes them great is precisely the way they have harnessed those energies.

There is, however, a real danger in this reading: it can serve as a license. "I have such a great *yetzer hara*, therefore I may be excused for giving in to it" is an obvious corruption of the principle.

Read through the externalized lens — where the *yetzer hara* abandoned the *umot ha-olam* to focus on Israel, where it destroyed the *batei hamikdash*, where it is an almost demonic force in the world — *kol ha-gadol me-chavero yitzro gadol himenu* means something quite different: the metaphysical force of evil is specifically deployed against those who are doing the most good, and the greater the person, the more targeted they become. These are two genuinely distinct theological and psychological frameworks, and the *gemara* moves between them without announcing the transition.

## Daily Struggle

### Daily Growth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יִצְרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו בְּכׇל יוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רַק רַע כׇּל הַיּוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A person's inclination overcomes him each day, as it is stated: "Only evil all day" (Genesis 6:5). All day long his thoughts and desires are for evil.

*Amar Rav Yitzchak*: *yitzro shel adam*, the *yetzer* of a person — note the shift in language, from the *yetzer hara* as a distinct entity to *yitzro shel adam*, "his *yetzer*," possessive and personal — *mitgaber alav be-chol yom* — grows stronger over him every day. *She-ne'emar: rak ra kol ha-yom* — it is only evil the whole day. The *yetzer* does not plateau; it gains strength daily, losing no momentum.

### Seeking Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: יִצְרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו בְּכׇל יוֹם וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לַהֲמִיתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צוֹפֶה רָשָׁע לַצַּדִּיק וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לַהֲמִיתוֹ״, וְאִלְמָלֵא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁעוֹזֵר לוֹ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ לֹא יַעַזְבֶנּוּ בְיָדוֹ וְלֹא יַרְשִׁיעֶנּוּ בְּהִשָּׁפְטוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: A person's evil inclination overcomes him each day and seeks to kill him, as it stated: "The wicked watches the righteous and seeks to kill him" (Psalms 37:32); the wicked here is referring to the wickedness inside one's heart. And if not for the Holy One, Blessed be He, Who assists him with the good inclination, he would not overcome it, as it is stated: "The Lord will not leave him in his hand, nor suffer him to be condemned when he is judged" (Psalms 37:33).

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *yitzro shel adam mitgaber alav be-chol yom u-mevakesh lahamito* — not only does it grow stronger each day, it seeks to kill him — to bring him into spiritual death, destruction of the soul. *She-ne'emar: tzofeh rasha la-tzaddik u-mevakesh lahamito* — the wicked one watches for the righteous and seeks to kill him. *Ve-ilmalei ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu she-ozer lo, eino yachol lo* — were it not for God helping him, a person would not have the strength to withstand it on his own. *She-ne'emar: Hashem lo ya-azvennu ve-yado ve-lo yarshi-ennu be-hishafeto* — God will not abandon him into its hands and will not let him be condemned through its judgment.

### Beit Midrash Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אִם פָּגַע בְּךָ מְנֻוּוֹל זֶה — מׇשְׁכֵהוּ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. אִם אֶבֶן הוּא — נִימּוֹחַ, אִם בַּרְזֶל הוּא מִתְפּוֹצֵץ. אִם אֶבֶן הוּא נִימּוֹחַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״, וּכְתִיב: ״אֲבָנִים שָׁחֲקוּ מַיִם״. אִם בַּרְזֶל הוּא מִתְפּוֹצֵץ, דִּכְתִיב: ״הֲלׂא כֹה דְבָרִי כָּאֵשׁ נְאֻם ה׳ וּכְפַטִּישׁ יְפוֹצֵץ סָלַע״.
>
> **Translation:** The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: If this scoundrel, the evil inclination, accosted you, seeking to tempt you to sin, drag it to the study hall and study Torah. If it is like a stone, it will be dissolved by the Torah. If it is like iron, it will be shattered. The Gemara elaborates: If it is like stone, it will be dissolved, as it is written: "Ho, everyone who is thirsty, come you for the water" (Isaiah 55:1), water in this context meaning Torah; and it is written: "Stones were worn by water" (Job 14:19). If it is like iron, it will be shattered, as it is written: "Is not My word like fire, says the Lord; and like a hammer that shatters rock" (Jeremiah 23:29).

*Tana devei Rabbi Yishmael*: *im paga becha menuval zeh* — if this disgusting one accosts you, *mashcheihu le-veit ha-midrash* — drag it into the *beit midrash*. When the *yetzer hara* has seized hold of you, bring it with you into the *beit midrash*, make it your study partner — meaning: learn *Torah*. That is the remedy.

*She-im even hu nimoach* — if it is as hard as stone, *Torah* will dissolve it like water, as the verse says: *hoy kol tzamei lechu la-mayim* — come, all who are thirsty, to the water — and elsewhere: *avanim shachaku mayim* — stones are worn away by water. *Ve-im barzel hu mitpotzetz* — and if it is like iron, *Torah* will shatter it. *She-ne'emar: halo koh devarai ka-esh ne-um Hashem u-che-fatish yefotzetz sela* — are not My words like fire, says God, and like a hammer shattering rock?

A subtle difficulty arises here that both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* address. The verse as cited introduces an apparent tension: if iron shatters, the natural image would be of something breaking the iron. But in the verse, it is the rock that is shattered by the hammer, not the iron. *Rashi* resolves this by focusing on the first half of the verse: God's word is like fire — and fire melts iron, causes it to spark and dissolve. The image for the iron, then, is not the hammer and rock but the fire. *Tosafot* suggests reading the verse differently — that the hammer itself is what *yefotzetz sela*, shatters the rock — but this is admittedly a strained reading. In any case, the *gemara*'s point stands: bring the *yetzer hara* into the *beit midrash* and engage it with *Torah*, whether as melting water against stone or as divine fire against iron.

## Seduction & Testimony

### Dual Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: יֵצֶר הָרָע מְסִיתוֹ לָאָדָם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וּמֵעִיד עָלָיו לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מְפַנֵּק מִנּוֹעַר עַבְדּוֹ וְאַחֲרִיתוֹ יִהְיֶה מָנוֹן״ — שֶׁכֵּן בְּאַטְבַּ״‎ח שֶׁל רַבִּי חִיָּיא, קוֹרִין לְסָהֲדָה מָנוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The evil inclination incites a person to sin in this world, and then testifies against him in the next world, as it is stated: "He that delicately brings up his servant from a child shall have him become a master [*manon*] at the last" (Proverbs 29:21). Initially, in one's youth, the evil inclination, which should have been enslaved to him, takes control of him and causes him to sin. Then, ultimately, that same evil inclination becomes his *manon*. *Manon* means witness, as in Rabbi Ḥiyya's coded alphabet in which ***alef*** and ***tet*** and ***beit*** and ***ḥet*,** etc., are interchanged. Witness [***sahada***] is called *manon*. The letters *mem* and *samekh*, *nun* and *heh*, and *vav* and *dalet* are interchanged with other letters.

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: *yetzer hara mesito la-adam ba-olam ha-zeh* — the *yetzer hara* seduces a person in this world, *u-me-id alav le-olam ha-ba* — and will testify against him in the world to come. What seems in this world like your companion, your indulgent friend who encourages you to give in to desire, will in the end turn against you entirely and stand as a witness for the prosecution.

### Servant Parable

*She-ne'emar: me-faneik mi-no-ar avdo, ve-acharito yihyeh manon* — one who indulges his servant from youth, in the end he will be a *manon*. The *yetzer hara* is here called your servant — *Rashi* explains that it is ultimately subject to your control — but it has been indulging you and encouraging indulgence since you were young. And in the end, *manon*: which, the *gemara* explains, equals *sahada*, witness, through the letter-substitution cipher known as *atbach* of Rabbi Chiya. In *atbach*, letters are substituted according to a systematic pairing of the alphabet folded over itself and incorporating the *sofit* forms. Through this cipher, the letters of *manon* yield *sahad-a* — witness. The *yetzer hara* was always taking notes.

## Passerby to Master

### External vs Internal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא רָמֵי כְּתִיב: ״כִּי רוּחַ זְנוּנִים הִתְעָה״. וּכְתִיב: ״בְּקִרְבָּם״! בַּתְּחִלָּה הִתְעָם, וּלְבַסּוֹף בְּקִרְבָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna raised a contradiction between two verses. It is written: "For the spirit of harlotry caused them to err" (Hosea 4:12), indicating that this spirit was a temporary phenomenon and not an integral part of their persona. And it is also written: "For the spirit of harlotry is within them" (Hosea 5:4), indicating that it is an integral part of their persona. The Gemara explains: Initially, it causes them to err from without, and ultimately, it is from within them.

*Rav Huna rami ksiv* — Rav Huna raises a contradiction between two verses. One verse says *ki ruach znunim hit-am* — a licentious spirit led them astray, suggesting an external force. Another says *be-kirbam* — in their midst, suggesting something internal. How are these reconciled?

### Three Stages

*Bitchila hit-am* — initially it merely led them astray; it was an external force that occasionally deflected them from the right path. *Aval be-sof be-kirbam* — but in the end it became *within* them, embedded inside, a regular and internalized presence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בַּתְּחִלָּה קְרָאוֹ ״הֵלֶךְ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ ״אוֹרֵחַ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ ״אִישׁ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹא הֵלֶךְ לְאִישׁ הֶעָשִׁיר וַיַּחְמוֹל לָקַחַת מִצֹּאנוֹ וּמִבְּקָרוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת לָאוֹרֵחַ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח אֶת כִּבְשַׂת הָאִישׁ הָרָשׁ וַיַּעֲשֶׂהָ לָאִישׁ הַבָּא אֵלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: Initially, the verse called the evil inclination a traveler coming from afar. Subsequently, the verse calls it a guest, as one welcomes it. Ultimately, the verse calls it man, indicating significance, as it became the homeowner. As it is stated in the parable of the poor man's lamb that Nathan the prophet said to David: "And there came a traveler to the rich man, and he was reluctant to take of his own flock and of his own herd, to prepare for the guest" (II Samuel 12:4). And it is written in the same verse: "And he took the poor man's lamb, and prepared it for the man that was come to him." In other words, the evil inclination that began as a traveler gradually rose in prominence.

*Amar Rava*: this mirrors the progression described elsewhere about the spiderweb and the thick cords. *Bitchila kara-o holech* — at first it was called a passerby, merely passing through and occasionally creating temptation. *U-ve-sof kara-o oreach* — then it became a guest, a more regular visitor who stays awhile. *U-ve-sof kara-o ish* — and in the end it became the *ba-al ha-bayit*, the master of the house.

### Natan's Parable

This is illustrated by the *mashal* that *Natan* told David to rebuke him for the sin involving *Bat Sheva*. *Vayavo helech le-ish he-ashir* — a passerby came to the wealthy man — the man who had enormous flocks and yet, when a visitor needed to be fed, he took instead the one beloved lamb of his poor neighbor. The wealthy man's sheep are David's wives; the one lamb is *Bat Sheva*, wife of *Uriah*. The passerby who needs feeding — the one whose need David chose to satisfy at another's expense — is David's own *yetzer hara*. What began as a passing desire became a guest, and then became the master of the house.

## Sexual Desire

### Appetite Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵבֶר קָטָן יֵשׁ לוֹ לָאָדָם, מַרְעִיבוֹ — שָׂבֵעַ, מַשְׂבִּיעוֹ — רָעֵב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כְּמַרְעִיתָם וַיִּשְׂבָּעוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A man has a small organ used in sexual relations. If he starves the organ, and does not overindulge, it is satiated; however, if he satiates the organ and overindulges in sexual relations, it is starving, and desires more, as it is stated: "When they were fed, they became full, they were filled, and their heart was exalted; therefore have they forgotten Me" (Hosea 13:6).

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *eiver katan*, a small limb, the *gemara* now turning explicitly to sexual desire. *Yesh lo le-adam* — a person has this limb. *Mar-ivo savea* — if he starves it, he is satisfied; *u-mashi-vo ra-ev* — if he satiates it, he is always hungry. Feeding the desire whets it further; the more one gives in, the more insistent the appetite becomes. *She-ne'emar: ke-mar-isam va-yisba-u* — as they grazed, so they were satisfied (here read against its grain to make the point). A degree of abstinence, counter-intuitively, is what actually brings satisfaction; indulgence only deepens the hunger.

## Four Regretted Creations

### Four Entities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר אַחָא, אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב: אַרְבָּעָה מִתְחָרֵט עֲלֵיהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁבְּרָאָם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: גָּלוּת, כַּשְׂדִּים, וְיִשְׁמְעֵאלִים, וְיֵצֶר הָרָע. גָּלוּת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעַתָּה מַה לִּי פֹה נְאֻם ה׳ כִּי לֻקַּח עַמִּי חִנָּם וְגוֹ׳״. כַּשְׂדִּים, דִּכְתִיב: ״הֵן אֶרֶץ כַּשְׂדִּים זֶה הָעָם לֹא הָיָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥana bar Aḥa said that the Sages in the school of Rav say: There are four creations that the Holy One, Blessed be He, created, yet He, as it were, regrets that He created them, as they do more harm than good. And these are they: Exile, Chaldeans, and Ishmaelites, and the evil inclination. Exile, as it is written: "Now therefore, for what am I here, says the Lord, seeing that My people is taken away for naught" (Isaiah 52:5). God Himself is asking: For what am I here? Chaldeans, as it is written: "Behold the land of the Chaldeans, this is the people that was not" (Isaiah 23:13), meaning, if only they never were.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan bar Abba amrei bei Rav*: *arba-ah mischarat aleihem ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu she-bera-am* — four entities God, as it were, regrets having created. Not in the literal sense of divine error, but in the sense that they produce enormous evil in the world. *Ve-eilu hein*: exile, the Chaldeans, the Ishmaelites, and the *yetzer hara*.

### Three Nations

Regarding exile: *v-atah mah li foh ne-um Hashem, ki lukach ami chinam* — what am I doing here, says God? My people were taken for nothing. The exile was purposeless devastation. Regarding the Chaldeans: *hein eretz Kasdim zeh ha-am lo hayah* — this is a nation that should not have been. Regarding the Ishmaelites: *yishlayu ohalim le-shodedim, u-btuchos le-margizei El* — tranquil tents for highway robbers, security for those who provoke God — *la-asher heivi Eloah be-yado*, that God brought into existence through His own hand. The regret is that God created them, and yet they have become a source of violence and evil in the world.

### Yetzer Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׁמְעֵאלִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִשְׁלָיוּ אֹהָלִים לְשׁוֹדְדִים וּבַטּוּחוֹת לְמַרְגִּיזֵי אֵל לַאֲשֶׁר הֵבִיא אֱלוֹהַּ בְּיָדוֹ״. יֵצֶר הָרָע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר הֲרֵעֹתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** Ishmaelites, as it is written: "The tents of robbers prosper, and they that provoke God are secure, in whatsoever God brings with His hand" (Job 12:6). God brought upon Himself these Arabs that dwell in the deserts in tents. The evil inclination, as it is written: "On that day, says the Lord, will I assemble her that is lame, and I will gather her that is driven away, and her that I corrupted" (Micah 4:6). God is saying that He created the evil inclination that led the people to sin and to be cast into exile.

And finally: *yetzer hara dichsiv, va-asher hareiti* — and that which I have made evil. This is read as God expressing something like regret over creating the *yetzer hara*, acknowledging that it has led human beings into destruction.

### Three Verses Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִלְמָלֵא שָׁלֹשׁ מִקְרָאוֹת הַלָּלוּ, נִתְמוֹטְטוּ רַגְלֵיהֶם שֶׁל שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. חַד, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר הֲרֵעֹתִי״. וְחַד, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנֵּה כַחוֹמֶר בְּיַד הַיּוֹצֵר כֵּן אַתֶּם וְגוֹ׳״. וְאִידַּךְ: ״וַהֲסִרֹתִי אֶת לֵב הָאֶבֶן מִבְּשַׂרְכֶם וְנָתַתִּי לָכֶם לֵב בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Were it not for these three verses that indicate that God controls people's hearts, the legs of the enemies of the Jewish people, a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves, would have collapsed, unable to withstand the repercussions of their sins. One, as it is written: "And her that I corrupted," indicating God's regret for doing so. And one, as it is written: "Behold, as the clay in the potter's hand, so are you in My hand, O house of Israel" (Jeremiah 18:6). And the other verse: "And I will take away the heart of stone out of your flesh, and I will give you a heart of flesh" (Ezekiel 36:26), indicating that the matter is not solely in human hands, but in the hands of God as well.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *ilmalei shalosh mikra-ot halalu, nismotetu ragleihem shel son-eihem shel Yisrael* — were it not for the following three verses, the legs of the Jewish people (spoken of euphemistically as "their enemies") would have given way entirely under the weight of their sins.

The first verse: *va-asher hareiti* — God acknowledges responsibility for creating the *yetzer hara*, and thus bears part of the burden for the sins it causes. The second: *hine kachomer be-yad ha-yotzer kein atem be-yadi ne-um Hashem* — as clay in the hand of the potter, so are you in my hand — meaning that God has the power to strengthen us and reshape us; the fact that we have sometimes failed is not entirely on our own account. The third: *va-hasiroti et lev ha-even mi-vsarchem ve-natati lachem lev basar* — God has placed this stone heart within us, and God alone can replace it with a heart of flesh. Together, these verses provide a kind of theological scaffolding: we are not absolved of responsibility, but we are also not entirely abandoned to it. We were created this way, and God could have made it otherwise. *Rav Pappa* adds a fourth verse: *ve-et ruchi eten be-kirbechem* — I will place my spirit within you — which again promises divine assistance in the struggle against the *yetzer hara*.

### Rav Pappa's Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר, אַף מֵהַאי נָמֵי: ״וְאֶת רוּחִי אֶתֵּן בְּקִרְבְּכֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said: It is derived from this verse as well: "And I will put My spirit within you, and cause you to walk in My statutes, and you shall keep My ordinances, and do them" (Ezekiel 36:27).

## Craftsmen & Horns

### Four Craftsmen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּרְאֵנִי ה׳ אַרְבָּעָה חָרָשִׁים״, מַאן נִינְהוּ אַרְבָּעָה חָרָשִׁים? אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן דָּוִד, וּמָשִׁיחַ בֶּן יוֹסֵף, וְאֵלִיָּהוּ, וְכֹהֵן צֶדֶק.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the end of days, the Gemara cites another verse and interprets it homiletically. It is stated: "The Lord then showed me four craftsmen" (Zechariah 2:3). Who are these four craftsmen? Rav Ḥana bar Bizna said that Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida said: They are Messiah ben David, Messiah ben Yosef, Elijah, and the righteous High Priest, who will serve in the Messianic era.

The *gemara* now turns to a vision in *Zechariah*: *vayar-eni Hashem arba-ah charashim* — God showed me four craftsmen. *Amar Rav Chana bar Bizna amar Rabbi Shimon Chasida*: the four craftsmen are *Mashiach ben David, Mashiach ben Yosef, Eliyahu*, and *Kohen Tzedek*. *Rashi* explains: *Mashiach ben David* and *Mashiach ben Yosef* will build the *Beit Hamikdash*, hence they are craftsmen. *Eliyahu* built the altar on *Har Carmel* for his confrontation with the *neviei ha-Ba-al*. And *Kohen Tzedek* is read as *Malki-Tzedek*, son of *Noach*, who helped build the *tevah*.

### Rav Sheshet's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי אֵלֶּה הַקְּרָנוֹת אֲשֶׁר זֵרוּ אֶת יְהוּדָה״, הָנֵי לְשׁוּבָה אָתוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet raised an objection: If so, if that is the identity of the four craftsmen, then that which is written in the previous verse: "And he said to me: These are the horns that scattered Judea" (Zechariah 2:4), is difficult; these four in the first verse are coming for their enemies, and are not redeemers.

*Masiv Rav Sheshet*: if these are good figures, what is meant by the next verse — *vayomer elai eleh ha-kranot asher zeru et Yehudah* — these are the horns that scattered Judah? How can these same four craftsmen be identified as the ones who scattered Judah?

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, שְׁפֵיל לְסֵיפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא: ״וְיָבוֹאוּ אֵלֶּה לְהַחֲרִיד אוֹתָם לְיַדּוֹת אֶת קַרְנוֹת הַגּוֹיִם הַנּוֹשְׂאִים קֶרֶן אֶל אֶרֶץ יְהוּדָה לְזָרוֹתָהּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥana said to Rav Sheshet: Go to the end of the verse: "These then are come to frighten them, to cast down the horns of the nations, which lifted up their horn against the land of Judah to scatter it." This indicates that the horns refer to the nations that exiled the Jewish people and that the four craftsmen will hurl those horns aside.

*Shafil le-seifa di-kra* — look at the end of the verse: *vayavo-u eleh le-hacharid otam lidot et karnot ha-goyim ha-nosim keren el eretz Yehudah le-zarosah* — these came to terrify them and to cast down the horns of the nations who lifted their horn against the land of Judah to scatter it. The verse contains two different "these": the first refers to the horns that scattered Judah, and the second refers to the four craftsmen who have come to destroy those horns. The craftsmen are not the scatterers; they are the destroyers of the scatterers.

### Rav Chana's Modesty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּהֲדֵי חָנָא בְּאַגָּדְתָּא לְמָה לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet said to him: Why should I disagree with Rav Ḥana in matters of ***aggada,** where he is more expert than I, and I cannot prevail?

*Be-ha Chana be-agadeta la matzisai* — I should have known better than to try to compete with Chana in *agadeta*, says one of the Sages. The comment is somewhat humorous, since the resolution was actually a matter of careful reading of the *pesukim* — closer to *peshat* than to *agadeta* — but the point about intellectual humility in the face of a colleague's superior mastery stands.

## Shepherds & Leaders

### Seven Shepherds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיָה זֶה שָׁלוֹם אַשּׁוּר כִּי יָבֹא בְאַרְצֵנוּ וְכִי יִדְרוֹךְ בְּאַרְמְנוֹתֵינוּ וַהֲקֵמֹנוּ עָלָיו שִׁבְעָה רוֹעִים וּשְׁמֹנָה נְסִיכֵי אָדָם״. מַאן נִינְהוּ ״שִׁבְעָה רוֹעִים״? דָּוִד בָּאֶמְצַע, אָדָם שֵׁת וּמְתוּשֶׁלַח מִימִינוֹ, אַבְרָהָם יַעֲקֹב וּמֹשֶׁה בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ. וּמַאן נִינְהוּ ״שְׁמֹנָה נְסִיכֵי אָדָם״? יִשַׁי, וְשָׁאוּל, וּשְׁמוּאֵל, עָמוֹס, וּצְפַנְיָה, צִדְקִיָּה, וּמָשִׁיחַ, וְאֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues homiletically interpreting verses that relate to the end of days. It is stated: "And this shall be peace: When the Assyrian shall come into our land, and when he shall tread in our palaces, then shall we raise against him seven shepherds, and eight princes among men" (Micah 5:4). The Gemara asks: Who are these seven shepherds? The Gemara explains: David is in the middle; Adam, Seth, and Methuselah are to his right; Abraham, Jacob, and Moses are to his left. And who are the eight princes among men? They are Yishai, Saul, Samuel, Amos, Zephania, Zedekiah, Messiah, and Elijah.

*Ve-hayah zeh shalom Ashur ki yavo be-artzeinu* — this will be peace when Ashur comes against our land — *va-hakimonu alav shiv-ah ro-im u-shmonah nesichei adam* — we will raise up against them seven shepherds and eight leaders of people, who will ultimately take over the land of Ashur. *Mani nihu shiv-ah ro-im?* — who are the seven shepherds? *David ba-emtza* — David in the center. *Adam, Shet, u-Mesushalach mi-yimino* — Adam, Seth, and Methuselah on his right. *Avraham, Yaakov, u-Moshe bi-smolo* — Abraham, Jacob, and Moses on his left. The *Maharsha* notes that most of these figures were, in fact, literally shepherds as well as spiritual leaders, which gives the choice of imagery an additional layer of resonance. The absence of Isaac from the list is not addressed.

### Eight Leaders

*U-mani nihu shmonah nesichei adam?* — who are the eight leaders? *Yishai ve-Shaul u-Shmuel, Amos ve-Tzefaniah, Tzidkiyahu, u-Mashiach ve-Eliyahu*. What precisely connects these eight figures to one another is left for further study.

### Anti-Christian Notes

It is noted that the aggadic material at the end of this *daf*, concerning the messianic figures and their roles, reflects a polemical anti-Christian dimension, as is often found in *agadeta* of this period. The full explication of those dimensions would require engagement with the relevant patristic parallels, but the pattern is characteristic of much late Amoraic aggadic literature on messianic themes.
