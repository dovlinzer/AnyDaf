# Sanhedrin 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pshara & Kinyan

### Pshara: No Kinyan Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל דַּיָּין שֶׁדָּן דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ, מַשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Any judge who judges a judgment** according to **absolute truth** [*emet*] **causes the Divine Presence to rest among Israel**

The daf opens with a discussion that was already underway, examining the relationship between *pshara* (compromise) and *din* (strict judgment), specifically whether *pshara* constitutes a form of binding arbitration — a type of *din* — or merely a contractual agreement between the parties. This question is intimately connected to the debate about whether *pshara* requires one judge or three: if it is a form of *din*, it may well require three, as *din* does; if it is merely a contractual mechanism, one might suffice.

Now Rav Ashi draws an inference. *Amar Rav Ashi shma minah pshara eina tzricha kinyan* — from this very debate between those who require three judges and those who require only one, we may infer that *pshara* does not require a *kinyan* (a formal act of acquisition or obligation, such as a *kinyan sudar*, the handkerchief exchange). The reasoning runs as follows: *di salka daitach tzricha kinyan* — were you to maintain that *pshara* requires a *kinyan*, then the position that three judges are necessary would be incomprehensible. If the parties are binding themselves through a formal *kinyan* to accept whatever decision emerges, then two witnesses would suffice — there is no need for a judicial panel of three. The very existence of an opinion requiring three judges indicates that we are not operating within a framework of contractual self-obligation, but within something more akin to a judicial proceeding.

### Three Judges & Kinyan

The Gemara's inference seems compelling — yet it then reaches a surprising conclusion. Despite the apparent logic that a *kinyan* would render three judges unnecessary, the Gemara rules: *v'hilchata pshara tzricha kinyan* — the *halacha* is that *pshara* does in fact require a *kinyan*. This is unexpected. If a *kinyan* is performed, why would anyone require three judges? The parties have already obligated themselves to the outcome through the formal act of *kinyan*; whatever one judge decides ought to be binding.

### Tosafot: Legitimacy

Tosafot addresses this directly. Their language is precise: *v'ha d'lo sagi bitrei mishum d'havei k'kinyan b'ta'us d'lo b'ki b'pshara shapir* — the reason three are nonetheless required is that a *kinyan* executed toward an irresponsible or illegitimate *pshara* would be akin to a *kinyan ba-ta'ut*, a *kinyan* made in error, and therefore not truly binding. The parties are not agreeing in the abstract to accept whatever random words emerge from a single individual; they are committing themselves to a genuine, well-reasoned compromise. For such a commitment to be meaningful, the *pshara* itself must be arrived at responsibly and with appropriate deliberation. Three judges provide precisely that legitimacy. Fewer than three raises the concern that the process was insufficiently rigorous to justify binding both parties to a decision that departs from strict law.

### Baraita: Three & Gmar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִגְמַר הַדִּין, אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִבְצוֹעַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Once **the** verdict of the **judgment has been issued,** it **is not permitted** for **you to mediate** a dispute.

The Gemara then cites a *baraita*: *k'shem she'adin b'shlosha kach bitzua b'shlosha* — just as *din* requires three judges, so too does *bitzua* (the act of compromise/binding arbitration). This establishes that *pshara* or *bitzua* functions as a parallel process to *din* in its procedural requirements. However, once *gmar din* — the finalized judgment — has been rendered, *bitzua* is no longer permitted: *nigmar hadin i ata rashai livtzo'a*. What constitutes *gmar din* will be defined later; Rashi's formulation is that it occurs when the court explicitly declares *ish ploni atah zakai, ish ploni atah chayav* — "so-and-so, you are exempt; so-and-so, you are liable." Once those words are spoken, compromise is foreclosed.

## R. Eliezer: Assur

### Assur Livtzoa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סרמ״ש בנק״ש סִימָן. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אָסוּר לִבְצוֹעַ, וְכׇל הַבּוֹצֵעַ – הֲרֵי זֶה חוֹטֵא, וְכׇל הַמְבָרֵךְ אֶת הַבּוֹצֵעַ – הֲרֵי זֶה מְנָאֵץ. וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״בֹּצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **a mnemonic** device alluding to the names of *tanna'im* in the coming discussion: ***Samekh*, *reish*, *mem*, *shin*; *beit*, *nun*, *kuf*, *shin*.** The *Tosefta* cites several statements of *tanna'im* related to compromise and the term *botze'a*. **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, says:** It is **prohibited to mediate** a dispute; **and anyone who mediates [*habotze'a*]** a dispute **is a sinner; and anyone who blesses the mediator is cursing** God. **And of this, it is stated:** "And **the covetous [*botze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord"** (Psalms 10:3).

With the procedural framework in place, the Gemara now turns to a fundamental dispute among the Tannaim about whether *pshara* is desirable at all. The tension at its core is between *emet* (truth) and *shalom* (peace). Strict *din* may yield the most legally correct outcome, but it does not always leave all parties at peace. *Pshara* aims at *shalom* but may require some concession from strict *emet*. Which is to be preferred — and at what stage of the proceedings?

Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yossi HaGlili takes the most stringent position: *assur livtzoa* — it is forbidden to engage in *bitzua*. Not only is it forbidden, but *vechol habotzeia harei zeh chotei* — one who does so is considered a sinner, perverting truth. Moreover, *vechol hamevarech et habotzeia harei zeh mena'etz* — one who praises the judge who engaged in *bitzua* is blaspheming God. The prooftext is *botzeia berech ni'etz Hashem* — "the *botze'a* who blesses desecrates God." On its plain meaning, *botze'a* refers to one who takes ill-gotten gains; here it is read as the one who compromises between litigants, and one who blesses such a person thereby desecrates the divine name. The underlying principle is *ela yikov hadin et hahar* — let judgment pierce the mountain. No matter how formidable the obstacle, justice must prevail. *Ki hamishpat lelokim hu* — the judgment belongs to God, and to compromise it is to pervert God's own mandate.

### Moshe vs. Aharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יִקּוֹב הַדִּין אֶת הָהָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַמִּשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהִים הוּא״. וְכֵן מֹשֶׁה הָיָה אוֹמֵר: יִקּוֹב הַדִּין אֶת הָהָר. אֲבָל אַהֲרֹן אוֹהֵב שָׁלוֹם וְרוֹדֵף שָׁלוֹם, וּמֵשִׂים שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תּוֹרַת אֱמֶת הָיְתָה בְּפִיהוּ וְעַוְלָה לֹא נִמְצָא בִשְׂפָתָיו בְּשָׁלוֹם וּבְמִישׁוֹר הָלַךְ אִתִּי וְרַבִּים הֵשִׁיב מֵעָוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the judge must assure that **the** true **judgment will** prevail at all costs and metaphorically **pierce the mountain, as it is stated: "For the judgment is God's"** (Deuteronomy 1:17). **And similarly, Moses would say: Let the judgment pierce the mountain. But** by contrast, **Aaron,** whose role was not that of a judge, was **a lover of peace and a pursuer of peace, and** he **would apply peace between** one **person and the other, as it is stated: "The law of truth was in his mouth, and unrighteousness was not found in his lips; he walked with Me in peace and uprightness, and turned many away from iniquity"** (Malachi 2:6).

To illustrate the contrast, the Gemara juxtaposes Moshe and Aharon. Moshe embodies the principle of *yikov hadin et hahar* — uncompromising, piercing justice, brooking no deviation from the law that descended from Sinai. Aharon, by contrast, is characterized as *ohev shalom verodef shalom umesim shalom bein adam lechavero* — a lover of peace, a pursuer of peace, one who made peace between people. The verse in his praise reads: *torat emet hayta bepihu ve'avla lo nimtza bisfatav* — "a teaching of truth was in his mouth and perversion was not found on his lips." He walked in peace and on the straight path, *verabim heshiv me'avon* — and he turned many away from sin.

This sounds thoroughly praiseworthy of Aharon — yet Rabbi Eliezer has just declared *bitzua* to be forbidden. Is there a contradiction?

### Rashi: Before Din

Rashi resolves this cleanly. The prohibition of *assur livtzoa* applies specifically once the litigants have brought their case before the judges: *mi shebau ledin assur ladayanim livtzoa* — once they have come to the court of justice, the judges are obligated to administer justice. What Aharon did was entirely different: he would pursue peace *before* people came to him for *din*. Rashi makes this explicit: *vekeivan shehaya shomeia machloket beineihem kodem sheyavou lefanav ledin haya rodef achareihem umetil shalom beineihem* — when he would hear of a dispute between people, before they came before him for judgment, he would pursue them and make peace between them. There is no tension, then, between Aharon's conduct and Rabbi Eliezer's ruling: pre-litigation peacemaking is praiseworthy; perverting the judicial process once it has begun is forbidden.

### Stolen Goods & Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל סְאָה שֶׁל חִטִּים, וּטְחָנָהּ וַאֲפָאָהּ וְהִפְרִישׁ מִמֶּנָּה חַלָּה, כֵּיצַד מְבָרֵךְ? אֵין זֶה מְבָרֵךְ אֶלָּא מְנָאֵץ. וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּבֹצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* cites several other interpretations of the above-mentioned verse from Psalms. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If **one stole a *se'a* of wheat and ground it and baked it and separated *ḥalla* from it,** i.e., separated the portion of the dough that must be given to the priests, **how can he** possibly **recite the blessing** on the mitzva of *ḥalla*? **He is not blessing; rather,** he is **cursing** God. **And of this** offense **it is stated: "And the covetous [*uvotze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord,"** interpreted homiletically as: And whoever blesses upon breaking [*botze'a*] the bread despises the Lord.

The Gemara now digresses, picking up on the *botze'a berech* theme in a different context. Rabbi Eliezer teaches: if someone steals a measure of grain, grinds it, bakes it, and then separates *challah* from it — how can such a person recite a blessing? *Ein zeh mevarech ela mena'etz* — this is not a blessing but a desecration. Invoking God's name over the fruit of theft is a case of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah* — a mitzvah performed through the means of a sin. Here too, *botzeia berech ni'etz Hashem* applies: the one who blesses over ill-gotten gains desecrates God.

### R. Meir & Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״בּוֹצֵעַ״ אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד יְהוּדָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוּדָה אֶל אֶחָיו מַה בֶּצַע כִּי נַהֲרֹג אֶת אָחִינוּ״. וְכׇל הַמְבָרֵךְ אֶת יְהוּדָה – הֲרֵי זֶה מְנָאֵץ, וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּבֹצַעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** The term ***botze'a*** employed in that verse **was stated only with regard to Judah, as it is stated: "And Judah said to his brothers: What profit [*betza*] is it if we slay our brother** and conceal his blood? Come, and let us sell him to the Ishmaelites" (Genesis 37:26–27). **And** consequently, **anyone who blesses Judah** for this act **is cursing** God, **and of this it is stated: "And the covetous [*uvotze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord,"** interpreted homiletically as: "And whoever blesses the profiteer [*botze'a*] despises the Lord."

Rabbi Meir extends the *botze'a* teaching in yet another direction: *lo ne'emar botzeia ela keneged Yehuda* — the *botze'a* verse was said specifically in reference to Yehuda's role in the sale of Yosef. The verse reads: *vayomer Yehuda el echav mah betza ki naharog et achinu* — "And Yehuda said to his brothers, what profit is there that we should kill our brother?" He proposed selling Yosef to the Ishmaelites rather than killing him, and the brothers listened. Some have praised Yehuda for thereby saving Yosef's life. Rabbi Meir rejects this framing entirely: Yehuda was in a position of influence and could have convinced the brothers to return Yosef outright. By settling for the half-measure of selling him — preventing murder while still enabling enslavement and exile — Yehuda acted as a *botze'a*. *Vechol hamevarech et Yehuda harei zeh mena'etz* — praising Yehuda for this is blaspheming God. And the verse confirms it: *uvotzeia berech ni'etz Hashem*.

## R. Yehoshua: Mitzvah

### Emes Umishpat Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: מִצְוָה לִבְצוֹעַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֶת וּמִשְׁפַּט שָׁלוֹם שִׁפְטוּ בְּשַׁעֲרֵיכֶם״. וַהֲלֹא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מִשְׁפָּט – אֵין שָׁלוֹם, וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם – אֵין מִשְׁפָּט? אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִשְׁפָּט שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שָׁלוֹם? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה בִּיצּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** It is **a mitzva to mediate** a dispute, **as it is stated: "Execute the judgment of truth and peace in your gates"** (Zechariah 8:16). **Is it not that in the place where there is** strict **judgment there is no** true **peace, and in a place where there is** true **peace, there is no** strict **judgment? Rather, which is the judgment that has peace within it? You must say: This is mediation,** as both sides are satisfied with the result.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha takes the diametrically opposite position: *mitzvah livtzoa* — it is a *mitzvah* to engage in *bitzua*, and this applies even after the litigants have come before the *beit din*. His prooftext is: *emes umishpat shalom shiftu besh'areichem* — "truth and a judgment of peace you shall judge in your gates." Even at the gates of the court, the verse instructs judges to seek out a judgment that incorporates peace.

### Judgment with Peace

But here lies a paradox, which the Gemara articulates: *vehalo bimkom sheyesh mishpat ein shalom, bimkom sheyesh shalom ein mishpat* — where there is strict judgment, there is no peace, and where there is peace there is no strict judgment. These are in tension by their very nature. So how can the verse call for a "judgment of peace"? The resolution: *ela eizehu mishpat sheyesh bo shalom hevei omer ze bitzua* — what is the kind of judgment that contains within it peace? That is *bitzua*. *Bitzua* is not a departure from judgment but a form of it — one that applies law with sufficient flexibility to attend to context, to the needs of both parties, and to the production of a genuinely satisfactory and peaceful outcome. This reading also bears on the earlier debate about whether *bitzua* requires one or three judges: if *bitzua* is indeed a form of *mishpat*, it has an inherent dignity and structure that demands the full judicial apparatus.

### David's Mishpat & Tzedaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּדָוִד הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְהִי דָוִד עֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה״. וַהֲלֹא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מִשְׁפָּט – אֵין צְדָקָה, וּצְדָקָה – אֵין מִשְׁפָּט? אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִשְׁפָּט שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ צְדָקָה? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה בִּיצּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, with regard to David, it says: "And David executed justice and charity** to all his people" (II Samuel 8:15). **And is it not** that **wherever there is** strict **justice, there is no charity, and** wherever there is **charity, there is no** strict **justice? Rather, which is the justice that has within it charity? You must say: This is mediation.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha adduces a further proof from David: *vayehi David oseh mishpat utzedakah lechol amo* — "And David did *mishpat* and *tzedakah* for all his people." But the same paradox applies: *vehalo kol makom sheyesh mishpat ein tzedakah, vechol makom sheyesh tzedakah ein mishpat* — where there is *mishpat* there is no *tzedakah* (charity or generosity), and where there is *tzedakah* there is no *mishpat*. If a wealthy creditor is legally entitled to the full sum and the court rules in his favor, the poor debtor is left penniless — that is *mishpat*, but it is not *tzedakah*. If the judge shows leniency to the poor debtor, that may be *tzedakah*, but it is not strict *mishpat*.

The Gemara, however, shifts interpretive tracks here, reading *tzedakah* not as a metaphorical dimension of *mishpat* but in its more literal, rabbinic sense — charity in the form of personal financial giving. On this reading, David would administer strict justice — declaring the innocent exempt and the liable party guilty — and then, if a poor person had suffered a financial loss as a result of the judgment going against him, David would personally pay him out of his own pocket. *Ze mishpat utzedakah*: *mishpat* for the one who prevailed in law, and *tzedakah* for the one who lost but was made whole through David's personal largesse. This reading places the *baraita* in alignment with the position of *assur livtzoa* — David did not compromise the judgment itself, but supplemented it from his personal resources to achieve a just outcome for all.

### Judgment as Tzedakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה: מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה – שֶׁהֶחְזִיר לוֹ מָמוֹן, וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה – שֶׁשִּׁילֵּם לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. וְכֵן בְּדָוִד הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְהִי דָוִד עֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה לְכׇל עַמּוֹ״. מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה – שֶׁהֶחְזִיר לוֹ אֶת מָמוֹנוֹ, וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה – שֶׁשִּׁילֵּם לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is **justice for this** one **and charity for that** one: It is **justice for this** one, **because** the judge **restored** his **money to him; and** it is **charity for that** poor person, **because** the judge **paid for him from his** own **house. And similarly, with regard to David, it says: "And David executed justice and charity to all his people"** (II Samuel 8:15). He executed **justice for this** one, **because** he **restored his money to him, and charity for that** one, **because** he **paid for him from his** own **house.**

Yet this reading raises an objection: the verse says David did *mishpat utzedakah lechol amo* — for *all* his people. If *tzedakah* here means personal charitable payment to the losing poor party, this would apply only to a subset of cases — those where the losing party happened to be poor. *Rebbi* resolves this by offering an alternative reading that does not require David to dip into his own pocket at all: *af al pi shelo shilem mitoch beito zehu mishpat utzedakah*. How so? *Mishpat laze shehechziru lo mamono* — it is *mishpat* for the party who won and had his money restored, *utzedakah laze shehotzi gzeila mitachat yado* — and it is *tzedakah* for the party who lost, because the judgment freed him from holding on to money that was not rightfully his. By compelling him to return the ill-gotten funds, the court performed a kindness for him — it restored his integrity and released him from the weight of wrongful possession. On this reading, every judgment, regardless of the financial circumstances of the parties, constitutes both *mishpat* and *tzedakah* — for every winner and every loser alike. This is how *lechol amo* is satisfied.

## R. Shimon: Conditional

### Before Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁבָּאוּ לְפָנֶיךָ לַדִּין, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן, אוֹ מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאִי אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן דִּין נוֹטֶה – אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״. מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה – אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן וְלִפְנֵי הִתְגַּלַּע הָרִיב נְטוֹשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** If **two** litigants **come before you for a judgment, before you hear their** respective **statements** and claims; **or after you hear their statements but you do not** yet **know where the judgment is leaning,** meaning that it is not yet clear to the judge which party is in the right, **you** are **permitted to say to them: Go out and mediate.** But **after you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning, you are not permitted to say to them: Go out and mediate, as it is stated: "The beginning of strife is as when one releases water; therefore leave off contention before the quarrel breaks out"** (Proverbs 17:14). Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya interprets the verse to mean: **Before** the resolution of **the contention is revealed, you can cast it off. Once** the resolution of **the contention is revealed, you cannot cast it off.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya offers a position that mediates between the two poles. When two litigants come before a judge — before he has heard their claims and counter-claims, or even after he has heard them but has not yet formed a sense of which way the judgment will lean (*v'ee ata yodea leheichan hadin noteh*) — *ata rashai lomar lahem tze'u uvitz'u*: the judge may tell them to go and work out a compromise. The language here is notable: the judge is not conducting the *bitzua* himself as a form of *din berachamim* but suggesting that the parties go and reach their own compromise. This positions *bitzua* more as a contractual resolution between the parties than as a judicial act.

### After Clarity: Forbidden

Once, however, the judge has heard the claims and formed a clear impression of which way the judgment is leaning — *leheichan hadin noteh* — he may no longer suggest compromise. The prooftext is *poter mayim reishit madon* — "releasing water is the beginning of contention." Rashi reads this as: if you wish to exempt yourself, to withdraw from the judgment, you may do so *reishit madon* — at the beginning, before the dispute has been fully exposed and revealed. *Kodem shenitgala hariv ata yachol lintoshov* — before it has become revealed you may withdraw, but *mishenitgala hariv ee ata yachol lintoshov* — once it has become revealed, you may not.

There is a meaningful distinction between this position and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's. For Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, suggesting that parties seek their own compromise is a form of the judge stepping away — exempting himself from administering *din*. Once the judge's judgment is engaged — once he knows where it is heading — he cannot step away without perverting justice. The image is not of *bitzua* as a superior form of *mishpat* but as an exit from the judicial obligation, one that is only permissible before that obligation is fully activated.

This yields three distinct positions: Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yossi HaGlili forbids *bitzua* once the parties appear before the court. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha permits — indeed encourages — offering *bitzua* even well into the proceedings. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya permits suggestion of *bitzua* until the judge discerns which way the judgment leans, at which point it is forbidden.

## Recusal Before Clarity

### Recusal Early Stage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַדִּין, אֶחָד רַךְ וְאֶחָד קָשֶׁה, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶם, אוֹ מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאֵין אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן דִּין נוֹטֶה – אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶם: ״אֵין אֲנִי נִזְקָק לָכֶם״, שֶׁמָּא נִתְחַיֵּיב חָזָק וְנִמְצָא חָזָק רוֹדְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says:** If **two** litigants **come for a judgment,** and **one** is **flexible** and agreeable **and one** is **rigid** and contentious, **before you hear their** respective **statements, or after you hear their statements but you do not** yet **know where the judgment is leaning,** it **is permitted** for **you to say to them: I will not submit to** your request to judge **you.** The judge may refuse the case out of fear that **perhaps the strong** and contentious one will **be** found **liable, and** it will **turn out** that **the strong** one will **pursue** the judge with intent to harm **him.**

Reish Lakish (some versions say Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lakish) introduces a related scenario. Two litigants come to court — one soft-spoken, one aggressive and intimidating. The judge is concerned: if the aggressive party is found liable, he may come after the judge. Reish Lakish rules: *ad shelo tishma divreihen o mishetishma divreihen v'ee ata yodea leheichan hadin noteh ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem* — before hearing their claims, or after hearing them but without yet knowing which way the judgment will lean, the judge may recuse himself. He may say: I am not available to deal with you.

This parallels Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's position on *bitzua*: before the judicial obligation is fully activated — before *leheichan hadin noteh* is known — the judge retains some latitude. The threat of intimidation from a firm and potentially dangerous litigant is acknowledged as a human reality. Indeed, the Torah's command of *lo taguru mipnei ish* — do not be afraid of any person — already implies that the temptation to fear exists. The *chidush* of Reish Lakish is that before the judgment's direction is clear, the judge may act on that fear by recusing rather than by staying and being corrupted.

### No Recusal Late Stage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה – אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״אֵינִי נִזְקָק לָכֶם״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Once you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning, you may not say to them: I will not submit to** your request to judge **you, as it is stated: "You shall not be afraid before any man"** (Deuteronomy 1:17).

Once the judge knows which way the judgment is leaning, however, recusal is forbidden. *Mishetishma divreihen v'ata yodea leheichan hadin noteh ee ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem* — at that stage, the judge cannot withdraw. The prooftext is now invoked in full force: *lo taguru mipnei ish ki hamishpat lelokim hu* — do not fear any man, for the judgment belongs to God. When the judge is already in possession of where justice lies, his obligation to see it through is absolute. The verse does not merely permit courage — it mandates it.

## Obligations to Justice

### Student's Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ וְרָאָה זְכוּת לֶעָנִי וְחוֹבָה לֶעָשִׁיר, מִנַּיִין שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתּוֹק? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ״. רַבִּי חָנִין אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּכְנִיס דְּבָרֶיךָ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: From where** is it derived **that a student who is sitting before his teacher and** he **sees** a point of **merit for a poor person or liability for a wealthy person, from where** is it derived **that he should not be silent? As it is stated: "You shall not be afraid before any man";** he should fear neither his teacher nor the wealthy litigant. **Rabbi Ḥanin says:** The verse intimates: **Do not suppress your statement** of opinion **due to** any **person.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha now adds a dimension that extends the obligation of speaking truth beyond the judges themselves. The verse *lo taguru mipnei ish* is applied to a student sitting before his teacher during court proceedings. If that student perceives a *z'chut* (a merit or legal argument) that favors the poor party, or a fault with the rich party's position, he cannot remain silent — *minayin shelo yishtok* — and the verse commands him to speak. *Lo taguru* is read here not in the sense of fearing but in the sense of withholding, gathering in: *lishana dekinnushei* — the language of gathering in and holding back. Do not gather your words inside yourself out of fear of your teacher or of the powerful party before the court.

### Speaker's Obligation

The Gemara continues the thought, and Rabbi Chanina broadens this further: *lo tachnis devarecha mipnei ish* — do not hold your words back because of any person. This applies even to one who is not a judge and not a student in any formal capacity. If you observe a judicial proceeding and perceive something relevant to justice, you bear an obligation to speak. The scope of the obligation, then, extends from the judges themselves outward to students, observers, and bystanders.

### Witness Awareness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהוּ עֵדִים יוֹדְעִים אֶת מִי הֵן מְעִידִין, וְלִפְנֵי מִי הֵן מְעִידִין, וּמִי עָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָמְדוּ שְׁנֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר לָהֶם הָרִיב לִפְנֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The witnesses should know about whom they are testifying, and before Whom they are testifying, and Who will ultimately exact payment from them, as it is stated: "Then both the men, between whom the controversy is, shall stand before the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 19:17).

The Gemara turns to witnesses. They too must internalize who they are testifying about, before whom they testify, and who will ultimately exact payment from them: *v'hayu edim yod'im et mi hem me'idim v'lifnei mi hem me'idim u'mi atid lipara mehem*. Testifying before a human court is testifying before God. And who are they testifying about — to some degree, as we shall see, also God.

### Witnesses Before God

Testifying before a human court is testifying before God. The verse is cited: *v'amdu shnei ha'anashim asher lahem hariv lifnei Hashem* — "and the two men who have the dispute shall stand before God." This is read as referring not merely to the litigants but to the witnesses, who present themselves before God. Rashi's comment on *mi hem me'idim* is striking: false testimony is *ke'ilu hen me'idim b'Hakadosh Baruch Hu she'matrichin oto lehachazir mamon l'ba'alo* — as if they are testifying against God Himself, forcing God to work harder to restore money to its rightful owner through other means. By corrupting the judicial process, false witnesses shift the burden of justice back onto the divine. In that sense, they are testifying against — and burdening — God.

### Judge Awareness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהוּ הַדַּיָּינִין יוֹדְעִין אֶת מִי הֵן דָּנִין, וְלִפְנֵי מִי הֵן דָּנִין, וּמִי עָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל״. וְכֵן בִּיהוֹשָׁפָט הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל הַשֹּׁפְטִים רְאוּ מָה אַתֶּם עֹשִׂים כִּי לֹא לְאָדָם תִּשְׁפְּטוּ כִּי אִם לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the judges should know whom they are judging, and before Whom they are judging, and Who will ultimately exact payment from them, as it is stated: "God stands in the congregation of God; in the midst of the judges He judges"** (Psalms 82:1). **And similarly, with regard to Jehoshaphat it says: "And he said to the judges: Consider what you do; for you judge not for man, but for the Lord"** (II Chronicles 19:6).

The same framework applies to judges. *V'hayu hadayanim yod'im et mi hen danin v'lifnei mi hen danin u'mi atid lipara mehem* — judges must know who they are judging, before whom, and who will exact from them. The prooftext: *Elokim nitzav ba'adat El* — God stands in the assembly of the divine, meaning God is present among the judges. The precedent from Yehoshaphat reinforces this: *r'u mah atem osim ki lo l'adam tishpotu ki l'Hashem* — see what you are doing, for you judge not for man but for God.

### Judge's Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר הַדַּיָּין: מָה לִי בְּצַעַר הַזֶּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עִמָּכֶם בִּדְבַר מִשְׁפָּט״. אֵין לוֹ לַדַּיָּין אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁעֵינָיו רוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** And **lest the judge say: What** value is there **for me with this suffering?** Why should I engage in such a burdensome and difficult task? **The verse states: "He is with you in giving judgment,"** from which it is derived that in rendering his decision, **a judge has only that which his eyes can assess.** He is enjoined to render the best judgment possible based on the information he has available, and he is not accountable for anything else.

One might think that this overwhelming weight of divine accountability would paralyze a judge. Lest the judge recoil and say *mah li b'tzar zeh* — why do I need this anguish? — the verse adds: *imachem bidvar mishpat* — God is with you in the matter of justice. And the corollary: *ein lo l'dayan ela mah she'einav ro'ot* — a judge is only accountable for what his own eyes can assess. If the judge proceeds with integrity, rigor, and honesty, the obligation is fulfilled. The outcome is not entirely in his hands; the process is. This principle is the basis for Rav Moshe Feinstein's approach to *psak halacha*: if the process is undertaken with integrity, whatever conclusion emerges is legitimate. *Lo bashamayim hi* — the Torah is not in heaven; it is entrusted to human judgment exercised with good faith.

## Gmar Din & Halacha

### Definition of Gmar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי גְּמַר דִּין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה חַיָּיב, אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Earlier, the *Tosefta* stated that once the verdict has been issued, it is not permitted for the judge to arrange a compromise. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **a verdict,** i.e., what is the formal action that signifies the conclusion of the case? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is when the judge says: **So-and-so, you are liable; so-and-so, you are exonerated.**

Returning to the earlier point that once *gmar din* is reached *bitzua* is universally forbidden, the Gemara now defines *gmar din* precisely. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: *ish ploni atah chayav, ish ploni atah zakai* — when the court explicitly declares "so-and-so, you are liable" or "so-and-so, you are exempt," that is *gmar din*. At that moment, the judgment is final and compromise becomes irrelevant.

### Halacha: Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה. אִינִי? וְהָא רַב הוּנָא תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲוָה! כִּי הֲוָה אָתוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא אָמַר לְהוּ: אִי דִּינָא בָּעֵיתוּ, אִי פְּשָׁרָה בָּעֵיתוּ? מַאי ״מִצְוָה״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** who said it is a mitzva to mediate a dispute. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? And was it** not that **Rav Huna was a student of Rav,** and **when** litigants **would come before Rav Huna he would say to them: Do you want** a strict **judgment,** or **do you want a compromise?** Evidently, Rav's student Rav Huna did not hold that it is a mitzva to specifically arrange a compromise. The Gemara clarifies: **What** does **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** mean **that he says** it is **a mitzva?**

The Gemara then rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who held *mitzvah livtzo'a*, that engaging in *bitzua* is a *mitzvah*. The evidence is Rav Huna, who was a student of Rav. When litigants came before Rav Huna, he would say to them: *i dina ba'itu o pshara ba'itu* — do you want me to adjudicate strictly according to law, or do you want *pshara*? He would offer both options. This practice reflected the ruling in favor of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha.

### Mitzvah: Inform Options

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה לְמֵימְרָא לְהוּ: אִי דִּינָא בָּעֵיתוּ, אִי פְּשָׁרָה בָּעֵיתוּ? הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִצְוָה: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה סָבַר מִצְוָה, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** He means that it is **a mitzva to say to them: Do you want** a strict **judgment,** or **do you want a compromise?** The Gemara objects: Since this opinion **is** the same as that of **the first *tanna*,** who also allows compromise, it is redundant to teach it. The Gemara answers: There is a difference **between** them with regard to the question of whether it is **a mitzva** to arrange a compromise. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa holds** that it is **a mitzva** to offer them the option of compromise, and **the first *tanna* holds** that it is merely **permitted.**

But does "it is a *mitzvah*" mean the judge should actively promote *bitzua*? The Gemara clarifies: *mitzva lemeimar lehu i dina ba'itu pshar ba'itu* — the *mitzvah* consists in informing the litigants of both options equally, as Rav Huna did. It is not a mandate to encourage compromise specifically, but to present it as an equally legitimate path alongside strict *din*. The judge lays out the choice without tilting the litigants toward one option or the other.

### Mitzvah: Inform Options

If Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha merely requires that the judge present *bitzua* as an option, how is this different from the *Tanna Kamma* who implied that *bitzua* is available but never said the judge must affirmatively offer it? The Gemara's answer: *ika beinaihu mitzvah* — that is precisely the difference. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that *bitzua* is *reshut* — permissible if the parties seek it, but the judge need not proactively raise it. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha holds it is *mitzvah* — the judge must affirmatively inform the parties that this option exists. The word *mitzvah* here signals not enthusiasm for *bitzua* as the superior outcome, but an active obligation to make it known as a real possibility.

### Difference: Proactive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה, אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: If so, the opinion of the first *tanna* **is** the same as that **of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya.** The Gemara answers that there is a difference **between them** with regard to the principle: **After you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning,** it **is not permitted** for **you to say to them: Go out and mediate.** In that instance, the first *tanna* holds that it is still not too late to suggest mediation.

One further question: if the *Tanna Kamma* permits *bitzua* before *gmar din* and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya permits it before *leheichan hadin noteh*, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha holds it is a *mitzvah* — what exactly separates Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya from the *Tanna Kamma*? The Gemara answers: *ika beinaihu mishetishma divreihem v'atah yode'a leheichan hadin noteh ee atah rashai lomar lahem tze'u uvitz'u* — the point of difference is precisely this: once the judge knows which way the judgment is leaning, Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya forbids even mentioning *bitzua*, while the *Tanna Kamma* permits it up until actual *gmar din*. The positions, in sum, cascade as follows: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha — *mitzvah* to offer *bitzua* even after hearing the parties and knowing the direction; *Tanna Kamma* — permissible to allow *bitzua* if sought, up through just before *gmar din*; Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya — *bitzua* may be suggested only until *leheichan hadin noteh* is known; Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yossi HaGlili — forbidden from the moment the litigants appear before the court.

## R. Tanchum: Eigel

### Botze'a = Eigel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי: לֹא נֶאֱמַר מִקְרָא זֶה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד מַעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא אַהֲרֹן וַיִּבֶן מִזְבֵּחַ לְפָנָיו״. מָה רָאָה? אָמַר רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רָאָה חוּר שֶׁזָּבוּחַ לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the various Sages who offered interpretations of the verse: "And the covetous blesses himself, though he despises the Lord" (Psalms 10:3), **disagree with** the explanation **of Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai. As Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai says: This verse was stated only with regard to the incident of the** Golden **Calf, as it is stated: "And Aaron saw this, and he built [*vayyiven*] an altar [*mizbe'aḥ*] before it…**and said: Tomorrow shall be a feast to the Lord" (Exodus 32:5). **What** did Aaron **see? Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: He saw Hur, who** had been appointed together with Aaron by Moses to lead the people during Moses' absence (see Exodus 24:14), **slaughtered before him,** as he had protested the plan to fashion a calf and had been murdered by the people as a result. The verse is therefore interpreted not as: Aaron built an altar before the calf, but rather: He understood [*vayyaven*] from the slaughter [*mizavuaḥ*] before his own eyes; and he then called for a feast.

The Gemara now introduces a dissenting interpretation of the *botze'a berech* verse. Rabbi Tanchum bar Chanilai argues: *lo ne'emar mikra zeh ela keneged ma'aseh ha'eigel* — this verse was not said about *pshara* at all, but specifically about the episode of the Golden Calf, where Aharon made a *pshara* — a compromise — with his own conscience. The *botze'a* who blessed and thereby desecrated God was Aharon himself.

### Aharon's Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אִי לָא שָׁמַעְנָא לְהוּ הַשְׁתָּא, עָבְדוּ לִי כְּדַעֲבַדוּ בְּחוּר, וּמִיקַּיַים בִּי ״אִם יֵהָרֵג בְּמִקְדַּשׁ ה׳ כֹּהֵן וְנָבִיא״, וְלָא הָוְיָא לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא לְעוֹלָם. מוּטָב דְּלִיעְבְּדוּ לְעֵגֶל, אֶפְשָׁר הָוְיָא לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא בִּתְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Aaron **said** to himself: **If I do not listen to them now, they will do to me as they did to Hur,** and the verse: **"Shall the priest and the prophet be slain in the sanctuary of the Lord?"** (Lamentations 2:20), **will be fulfilled through me, and they will never have a remedy** for such a sin. It is **better** for them **to worship the calf,** as it is **possible they will have a remedy through repentance.** Nevertheless, according to Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai, whoever praises Aaron for this compromise is provoking God.

The Gemara presents Aharon's reasoning. When the people demanded a golden calf, Aharon looked and saw that Chur had been slaughtered before him — *ra'ah Chur shezavuach lefanav*. He understood: *ilo shamana lehu hashta avdu li kide'avdu beChur* — if I do not comply, they will do to me what they did to Chur. But his reasoning was more than simple self-preservation. He feared the compounding horror of *im yehareg bemikdash Hashem kohen venavi* — if a *kohen* and *navi* were to be murdered, the sin would be of an entirely different and irremediable order. *Mutav deya'avdu la'egel* — better that they engage in idol worship around a calf, *efshar havi lehu takanah betshuvah* — for that there can be a path of *teshuvah*. The sin of murdering a *kohen* and *navi* would be *velo havi lehu takanah le'olam* — without remedy. So Aharon compromised: he enabled the *eigel* to prevent something far worse. He told himself he was not acting out of fear for his own life but out of concern to channel the people's energy toward a remediable sin rather than a catastrophic one.

### Critique of Justification

The Gemara does not entirely accept this rationalization. Aharon built the calf and blessed the proceedings — *vayar Aharon vayiven mizbe'ach lefanav* — and the Gemara's implicit critique is that whatever his motivations, his self-justification may have been precisely that: a self-justification. He may genuinely have believed he was preventing a greater sin, but perhaps he was also, on some level, making peace with his own fear by dressing it in more noble rationale. The connection to Yehuda's half-measure in the sale of Yosef is not far in the background — the question of whether a compromise that averts the worst is genuinely praiseworthy, or whether it is a *botze'a* that blesses its own accommodation.

## Stages of Conflict

### Torah Study Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״, מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? כִּדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אֵין תְּחִילַּת דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם נִידּוֹן אֶלָּא עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **those *tanna'im*** who did not interpret the verse: **"The beginning of strife is as when one releases water"** (Proverbs 17:14), with regard to compromise, **what do they derive from** this verse? They understand the verse **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna says: The beginning of a person's judgment** after he dies is that he is **judged only concerning matters of Torah, as it is stated: "The beginning of strife is as when one releases water."**

The *Tanna'im* who reject the reading of *poter mayim reishit madon* as a license for early-stage recusal or *bitzua* must find an alternative application for the verse. The Gemara tells us they read it in line with Rav Hamnuna.

### Rav Hamnuna's Reading

*Damar Rav Hamnuna: ein techilat dino shel adam nadon ela al divrei Torah* — the first thing on which a person is judged is *divrei Torah*, whether he was *mevatel* from Torah study. *Poter mayim reishit madon*: releasing from *mayim* — and Torah is compared to water, as the verse says *hoy kol tzamei lechu lamayim* — is the very beginning of all judgment against a person. The failure to engage with Torah is not a minor sin tucked away in the middle of the divine accounting; it is the first question asked.

### Cracked Hose Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַאי תִּיגְרָא דָּמְיָא לְצִינּוֹרָא דְּבִידְקָא (דְּמַיָּא), כֵּיוָן דְּרָוַוח – רָוַוח.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this verse, **Rav Huna says: This quarrel** between people is **comparable to** a split in **a hose** caused by **a burst of water,** emptying into a field; **once** the split in the hose **widens, it widens** even more and can no longer be repaired. To save the field, the hose must be repaired as soon as it splits. The same is true with regard to a quarrel; it must be stopped as soon as it begins.

Rav Huna offers an image for the dynamics of interpersonal conflict: *hai tigra damya letzinora debitka damya* — a quarrel is like a cracked pipe. *Keivan deravach ravach* — once the crack widens, it continues to widen. A conflict that is not addressed early will expand and deepen until it becomes irreparable. The implication is that early intervention — whether through *pshara*, through making peace before the *beit din* on the model of Aharon, or through any other means — is critical precisely because the window of opportunity closes rapidly.

### Bridge Board Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי קַשִּׁישָׁא אָמַר: דָּמֵי לְגוּדָּא דְּגַמְלָא, כֵּיוָן דְּקָם – קָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye the Elder** makes a similar point with a different metaphor, and **says:** A quarrel is **comparable to a board in** a wooden **bridge. Once** it **has stood** in its place and been stabilized, it continues to **stand** and becomes ever more rigid and stable. Consequently, the best time to address and end the dispute is at the very beginning.

Abaye Kishisha offers a contrasting image: *dami leguda degamla* — a quarrel is also like a board of a bridge. *Keivan dekam kam* — once it is set in place, it holds firm. Once a dispute has become entrenched, it is as stable in its hostility as a bridge board is in its position. Reconciliation becomes progressively harder with time. Together, the two images reinforce the same practical message from different angles: the cracked pipe speaks to how quickly conflict escalates if unchecked, while the bridge board speaks to how stubbornly it persists once established.

### Folk Wisdom & Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְעִי וּשְׁתֵּי שֶׁבַע זְמִירוֹת הוּא, סִימָן. הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: טוּבֵיהּ דְּשָׁמַע וְאָדֵישׁ, חַלְפוּהּ בִּישָׁתֵיהּ מְאָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״ – רֵישׁ מְאָה דִּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the previous discussion, the Gemara recounts several incidents in which passersby recited popular proverbs. ***Shimi ushti*, *sheva zemirot hu*** is **a mnemonic** device for these incidents. There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked: It is good** for a person **who hears** statements said against him **and** yet **remains silent,** as **a hundred misfortunes pass him by** as a result. Upon hearing this, **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"The beginning of strife is as when one releases water"** (Proverbs 17:14). The words "beginning [*poter*] of strife [*reishit madon*]" allude to: **The beginning of one hundred litigations [*reish me'a dinei*].** Troubles are avoided if one overlooks and excuses [*poter*] an offense.

The Gemara at this point collects a series of aphorisms — folk sayings from oral tradition — each accompanied by a corroborating verse that Shmuel identifies for Rav Yehuda. The rhetorical pattern is consistent throughout: someone was going around repeating a saying, and Shmuel would demonstrate to Rav Yehuda that the saying is already encoded in Scripture — *kra ktiv*, it is written in a verse.

### Ignoring Insults

One aphorism notes that a person is not struck down for the first transgression, or the second, or the third — seeming impunity can breed complacency. Shmuel cites the verse: *al shlosha pishei Adam v'al arba'a lo ashivenu* — on three transgressions and on four I will not hold back; beyond a certain threshold, judgment arrives. The impunity is not permanent.

A third: for a person of peace, seven pits are set out and he navigates them all; for the wicked, a single pit will suffice to bring him down. Shmuel cites: *ki sheva yipol tzaddik v'kam* — a righteous person will fall seven times and rise; *u'resha'im yikashlu b'ra'ah* — and the wicked will stumble in evil.

### Losing Party Joy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: דְּאָזֵיל מִבֵּי דִינָא שְׁקִל גְּלִימָא – לִיזַמַּר זְמָר וְלֵיזִיל בְּאוֹרְחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְגַם כׇּל הָעָם הַזֶּה עַל מְקֹמוֹ יָבֹא בְשָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked:** With regard to one **who goes from the court,** and his **cloak** has been **taken** from him in the course of the proceedings, i.e., he lost all his money due to a ruling against him, **let him sing a song and go** happily **on the way.** Although he lost the case, he has benefited from justice being served. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** with regard to Yitro's advice for judiciary reforms that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"And all these people shall also go to their place in peace"** (Exodus 18:23). If justice is served, all the litigants, not only those who emerge victorious, can leave in peace.

A fourth: someone going from court having had his cloak taken from him in judgment should sing a song on his way — because he is no longer holding ill-gotten goods. This returns to the theme from the discussion of David's *mishpat utzedakah*: even the losing party has had a kindness done for him by being freed from wrongful possession. Shmuel cites: *v'gam kol ha'am hazeh al mekomo yavo v'shalom* — all this people shall go to their place in peace. Good judgment, even when its verdict stings, produces peace.

### Lazy Woman & Careless Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: הִיא נָיְימָא, וְדִיקּוּלָא שָׁפֵיל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״בַּעֲצַלְתַּיִם יִמַּךְ הַמְּקָרֶה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked:** If a woman is carrying a basket on her head, when **she is dozing the reed basket falls. Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"By laziness the rafters sink in;** and through idleness of the hands the house leaks" (Ecclesiastes 10:18).

A fifth: if a woman carrying a basket on her head nods off, the basket falls — inattention and laziness lead to loss and collapse. Shmuel cites: *ba'atzaltayim yemach hamikreh* — through laziness the roof-beam warps and sinks.

### Betrayal by Trusted Friend

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: גַּבְרָא דִּרְחִיצְנָא עֲלֵיהּ, אַדְיֵיהּ לִגְזִיזֵיהּ וְקָם. אָמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״גַּם אִישׁ שְׁלוֹמִי אֲשֶׁר בָּטַחְתִּי בוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked: The man upon whom I relied** has **lifted his fist [*ligzizeih*] and stood** against me. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"Indeed, my own familiar friend, in whom I trusted,** who did eat of my bread, has lifted up his heel against me" (Psalms 41:10).

A sixth: the man I relied upon raised his fist against me — the pain of betrayal by someone trusted. Shmuel cites: *v'gam ki ish shlomi asher batachti vo* — even the man of my peace, in whom I placed my trust, lifted against me.

### Love vs. Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: כִּי רְחִימְתִּין הֲוָה עַזִּיזָא, אַפּוּתְיָא דְּסַפְסֵירָא שְׁכִיבַן. הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא עַזִּיזָא רְחִימְתִּין, פּוּרְיָא בַּר שִׁיתִּין גַּרְמִידֵי לָא סַגִּי לַן. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְנוֹעַדְתִּי לְךָ שָׁם וְדִבַּרְתִּי אִתְּךָ מֵעַל הַכַּפֹּרֶת״, וְתַנְיָא: אָרוֹן תִּשְׁעָה וְכַפּוֹרֶת טֶפַח, הֲרֵי כָּאן עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying** about his marriage **as he walked: When our love was strong, we** could have **slept on** a bed that was **the width of a sword. Now that our love is not strong, a bed of sixty cubits** is **not sufficient for us. Rav Huna said: Verses** are **written** that convey these sentiments. **Initially, it was written: "I will meet with you there and I will speak with you from above the Ark Cover"** (Exodus 25:22), **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: The **Ark** of the Covenant was itself **nine** handbreadths high, **and** the **Ark Cover** was one **handbreadth** thick. **There** is a total height of **ten** handbreadths **here.** At first, when God had great affection for Israel, the Divine Presence was revealed within the confines of this limited space.

The final aphorism in the series is the most evocative: *ki rechimtin havai aziza a'putya d'safsira shchivan* — when our love was strong, we could sleep on the blade of a sword. In the early days of love, poverty is no obstacle; the bond itself sustains everything. *Hashta d'lo aziza rechimtin, purya bar shitin garmida lo sagi lan* — now that our love has weakened, a bed of sixty *amot* is not enough for us. Physical comfort and material wealth cannot substitute for genuine closeness.

### God's Presence & Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״וְהַבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה הַמֶּלֶךְ שְׁלֹמֹה לַה׳ שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה אׇרְכּוֹ וְעֶשְׂרִים רׇחְבּוֹ וּשְׁלֹשִׁים אַמָּה קוֹמָתוֹ״. וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הַשָּׁמַיִם כִּסְאִי וְהָאָרֶץ הֲדֹם רַגְלָי אֵיזֶה בַיִת אֲשֶׁר תִּבְנוּ לִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written: "And the house that King Solomon built for the Lord, its length was sixty cubits, and its breadth twenty cubits, and its height thirty cubits"** (I Kings 6:2). **And at the end,** when Israel sinned, the whole of the space of the Temple was not expansive enough for the Divine Presence to rest within it, as **it is written: "Thus says the Lord: The heaven is My throne, and the earth is My footstool; where is the house that you may build for Me?** And where is the place that may be My resting place?" (Isaiah 66:1). In times of discord, the Temple is an insufficient resting place for the Divine Presence.

Rav Huna extends this teaching into theology. *Me'ikara ksiv v'noadeti lecha sham v'dibarti itcha me'al hakaporet* — originally God said He would speak from above the *kapporet* of the Ark, which stood ten *tefachim* from the ground. God's presence was concentrated and immediate, dwelling within the intimate space of ten *tefachim*, right at human height. The *baraita* corroborates: the *aron* was nine *tefachim* tall, the *kapporet* one *tefach*, totaling ten — and God was right there.

Then came the *Beit HaMikdash* of Shlomo: *vehabayit asher bana Shlomo la'Hashem shishim ama orko, ve'esrim rochvo, u'shloshim ama komato* — sixty *amot* long, twenty wide, thirty tall. Already God's presence required a far more expansive structure. And finally, when Israel sinned: *koh amar Hashem hashamayim kisi veha'aretz hadom raglai eizeh bayit asher tivnu li* — the heavens are My throne and the earth My footstool; what house could you build for Me? God withdrew entirely; not even a thirty-*amah* ceiling could contain the *Shechina* that had been estranged by sin. The progression is devastating: from ten *tefachim* of intimate closeness, to a thirty-*amah* building, to the infinite expanse of heaven and earth — and God declaring that no human structure could any longer serve as His home.

## Lo Taguru Etymology

### Taguru = Gather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּכַנּוֹשֵׁי הוּא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיַיִן לֹא תִשְׁתֶּה וְלֹא תֶאֱגֹר״. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״תָּכִין בַּקַּיִץ לַחְמָהּ אָגְרָה בַקָּצִיר מַאֲכָלָהּ״. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״אֹגֵר בַּקַּיִץ בֵּן מַשְׂכִּיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to analyzing the *Tosefta*. **From where may** it **be inferred that this** expression: **"You shall not be afraid [*taguru*]"** (Deuteronomy 1:17), **is a term for gathering in,** so that the term may be interpreted to mean that a judge may not keep his ruling to himself? **Rav Naḥman said: The verse states:** "You shalt plant vineyards and dress them, **but you shall neither drink of the wine, nor gather [*te'egor*]"** (Deuteronomy 28:39). **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says** it is derived **from here: "She provides her bread in the summer, and gathers [*agra*] her food in the harvest"** (Proverbs 6:8). **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says** it is derived **from here: "A wise son gathers [*oger*] in the summer"** (Proverbs 10:5).

The Gemara circles back to the word *taguru* in the verse *lo taguru mipnei ish*, the command not to withhold one's words or testimony out of fear. The question is the derivation: *mai mashma dehai lo taguru lishana dekinnushei hu* — what is the evidence that *taguru* means to gather in, to withhold? The prooftext: *veyayin lo tishteh velo te'egor* — wine you shall not drink and you shall not gather in. The root *aleph-gimel-reish* clearly means to accumulate or gather, and this confirms that *lo taguru mipnei ish* means do not gather in your words before any man — do not hold them back.

### Ant's Gathering

Rav Acha bar Yaakov cites the verse about the industrious ant: *tachin bakayitz lachma, agra bakatzir ma'achala* — it prepares its bread in the summer, it *gathers* (*agra*) its food in the harvest. The root again is *ayin-gimel-reish*, the same sense of gathering and accumulating, confirming that *oger* means to gather up and store.

### Wise Son Gathering

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika cites a third verse: *oger bakayitz ben maskil* — the wise son gathers in the summer. Here the word *oger* is explicit and unmistakable in its meaning. From all three prooftexts, the linguistic case is closed: *taguru* in *lo taguru mipnei ish* means to gather in, to hold back, to withhold. The command is therefore not merely about personal courage in the face of fear — it is a prohibition against the act of withholding one's words or testimony when justice demands that they be spoken.
