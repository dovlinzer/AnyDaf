# Zevachim 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine Burning Debate

### Shmuel's Wine Ruling

We pick up at the bottom of 92a–b, returning to the ongoing discussion about which offerings take precedence and the question of whether one may bring an independent voluntary offering of oil. That debate among the *tanna'im* — between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Tarfon — also touches on what form such an offering takes: is it like a *mincha*, where a *kometz* is removed and the *kohanim* eat the remainder, or like an *olah* where everything is burned, or like *nesachim* where everything is poured on the *mizbe'ach*? That question was connected to whether the offering is one *log* (on the model of the *mincha*) or three *log* (on the model of the wine of the *minchat nesachim*, which according to Rabbi Akiva could also be brought as a separate *korban* and poured entirely on the altar — though not on the fire, but down a funnel).

The Gemara now introduces a position of Shmuel. About ten lines from the bottom of the page, following the marker, we read: *amar Shmuel hamitnadev yayin mevi umazippo gabei ishim* — Shmuel says that one who brings a voluntary offering of wine brings it and burns it upon the fire. Until now all the *tanna'im* seemed to hold that wine is poured down the *sfalim*, the funnels, just as is done with the wine of the *minchat nesachim* and with the water on Sukkot. Shmuel, however, rules that a separate *korban* of wine is burned on the fire, just as oil is.

What is his reason? The verse states: *v'yayin takriv lanesech chatzi hahin isheh rei'ach nichoach l'Hashem* — "and wine you shall bring as a libation, half a *hin*, a fire-offering, a pleasing aroma to God." The verse uses the word *isheh*, a fire-offering, in connection with the wine. Shmuel is not disagreeing with the established practice that *yein nesakhim* accompanying a *korban* goes down the *sfalim* — everyone agrees on that. But to the extent that we derive from elsewhere (such as the word *ezrach* or related inferences) that there exists an independent *korban* of wine, Shmuel reads the word *isheh* in that context literally: it goes on the fire.

### Fire Extinguishing Issue

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty. If you pour wine on the fire, you will extinguish it. The Torah states *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach* — a constant fire shall burn on the altar — and from this we derive an implicit prohibition against extinguishing the altar fire. So how can Shmuel permit pouring wine onto it? *V'ha ka mechabi chibuyei* — surely you are extinguishing it?

### Constant Fire Principle

The Gemara's initial answer is: *chibuyei b'miktzat lo shmei chibui* — partial extinguishing does not constitute extinguishing in the legally significant sense. As long as some fire remains and the *esh tamid* continues, there is no violation.

The Gemara challenges this: *V'ha amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha hamorid gachelet mei-al gabei hamizbe'ach v'chibah chayav* — one who removes a single coal from the *mizbe'ach* and extinguishes it is liable. The implication is that even extinguishing one coal is a violation, not only extinguishing the entire fire.

The Gemara clarifies: that ruling refers to a scenario where that coal was the *only* fire on the *mizbe'ach* — so extinguishing it amounted to extinguishing the entire fire.

A second answer is then offered: *kibuy de-mitzvah shani* — extinguishing that occurs in the context of fulfilling a *mitzvah* is different. If one places the prescribed parts of a *korban* on the fire and in doing so inadvertently extinguishes a coal, that is part of the *mitzvah* itself. When performing the commanded act of placing offerings on the fire, one need not be concerned that it may cause a small portion of the fire to go out, provided some fire remains.

### Trumas Ha-Deshen

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a *braita*: *Tana Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — once the Torah commands one to perform *trumas ha-deshen*, the removal of ashes, one might think that in the process it would be permitted to extinguish some of the flame. The *braita* teaches that no, one may not extinguish. But *trumas ha-deshen* is also a *mitzvah*. Why is the principle of *kibuy de-mitzvah* inapplicable there?

### Mitzvah Context

The Gemara distinguishes: *shani hasam efshar de-yasiv ve-natar* — there, one has an alternative, one can simply wait. The Gemara envisions a scenario where the only place from which to take ashes is a spot where the fire still burns, and one might think that as with a campfire, one could stomp it out, turn it to ash, and then take it. Yet one is told that this is impermissible — and the reason is that waiting is an available option. In our case of the wine, however, there is no alternative: if the *mitzvah* requires placing wine on the fire, and that necessarily produces some degree of extinguishing, there is no way around it, and one proceeds. The Gemara thus may be refining the principle: it is not merely *mitzvah* context that permits the extinguishing, but *mitzvah* context combined with the absence of any alternative.

## Shmuel vs. Tannaim

### Contradictory Braisas

The Gemara then presses harder with a direct *ta shema*: *yayin k'divrei Rabbi Akiva l'sfalim* — a *braita* states that for Rabbi Akiva the wine goes down the funnels. A further *braita* explicitly reasons through the question: *yayin nesech l'sfalim* — wine should go down the funnels. Perhaps it should go on the fire? The *braita* answers: *amarta lo yechabeh* — we say no, because doing so would violate the prohibition of extinguishing. So these *braisot* directly contradict Shmuel's ruling.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya ha Rabbi Yehuda ha Rabbi Shimon* — there is no difficulty; the *braisot* follow Rabbi Yehuda, while Shmuel holds like Rabbi Shimon. All the *braisot* that route the wine down the funnels do so because they acknowledge the verse uses the word *isheh* but are driven away from its literal meaning by the concern about extinguishing the fire. The question then becomes: what is driving these *braisot* to be concerned about extinguishing, and what permits Shmuel not to be?

### Intention in Violation

This opens into the broader dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon regarding *eino miskavein* — an unintended act. The classic example: if one drags a bench across a flower bed on *Shabbat*, intending only to move the bench, but in doing so may create a furrow in the ground — has one violated *Shabbat*? Now, if one knows the furrow will definitely result, that raises the separate issue of *psik reisha*; but if it is not a necessary consequence, Rabbi Shimon holds that since the furrow was incidental and unintended, even if it results, one bears no responsibility. The act is defined by one's intention, and since one was not trying to create the furrow, one is not halachically connected to it. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that while one would not violate if no furrow resulted — the question is only about being held responsible if the furrow does result — if in fact the furrow was made, one cannot be free of responsibility. Rabbi Yehuda does not allow intention to fully disconnect one from the physical consequences of one's act.

Shmuel, the Gemara explains, holds like Rabbi Shimon. Therefore, Shmuel says: sprinkle the wine on the fire — it will not necessarily extinguish the flame, and if it does, you were not intending it and it was not a guaranteed consequence, so there is no violation. The *braisot* that redirect the wine to the funnels hold like Rabbi Yehuda: even an unintended extinguishing counts as a violation, and therefore they cannot permit placing wine on the fire.

Two important conclusions emerge here. First, the Gemara has now abandoned the earlier resolution that *kibuy de-mitzvah* explains why extinguishing is permitted. If *mitzvah* context were the operative answer, those *braisot* would have no reason to redirect the wine to the funnels. The correct explanation is *eino miskavein* — it is entirely a question of whether unintended consequences constitute a violation. Second, regarding *psik reisha*: the Gemara does not raise the issue here at all. Rashi suggests that in practice wine can be sprinkled in small drops that may evaporate before reaching the flame, so it is not a *psik reisha*. But the plain reading of the Gemara is that even if extinguishing were a guaranteed consequence, the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon addresses exactly that. The Gemara's silence on *psik reisha* implies that according to this *sugya*, even a guaranteed unintended consequence may not constitute a violation according to Rabbi Shimon — a striking position.

### Purpose vs. Consequence

The Gemara now raises what appears to be a contradiction within Shmuel's own rulings. It has been established that Shmuel holds like Rabbi Shimon on *eino miskavein*. But elsewhere, *amar Shmuel mechabin gachelet shel matechet birshut harabim bishvil shelo yizoku ba rabim* — Shmuel rules that one may extinguish a metal ember found in a public domain on *Shabbat* so that members of the public not be harmed. Why only a metal ember? Because extinguishing a metal-based fire does not constitute a biblical violation of *kibbuy*. The reason: the biblical *melacha* of extinguishing is defined by its productive result — the creation of charcoal. The only act of extinguishing that produces a new, desired end product (charcoal) is extinguishing a wood-based fire. Extinguishing a metal ember produces no such end product; you simply stop the metal from burning. Therefore it is only a rabbinic prohibition, and in the face of harm to the public, that rabbinic prohibition is set aside. However, Shmuel adds — as stated on 42a — that one may *not* do this for a wood-based fire, since there the prohibition is biblical: one has produced charcoal.

This is where the concept of *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* enters. Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda debate not only *eino miskavein* but also *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — an act performed intentionally but not for the purpose that defines that *melacha* halachically. When one extinguishes a fire to prevent harm to the public, one is intentionally extinguishing — this is not *eino miskavein* — but one's goal is not the production of charcoal, which is what *halacha* defines as the point of the act of extinguishing. That is *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*. Rabbi Shimon holds that such an act is only a rabbinic violation; Rabbi Yehuda holds it is biblical. Another example: if one digs a hole to obtain dirt, one is intentionally digging, but one's goal is the dirt, not the creation of the hole — one would be equally content to get dirt from a sandbox. The hole is not the objective; that is *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*.

### Two Positions

The Gemara asks: if Shmuel holds like Rabbi Shimon, why does he prohibit extinguishing a wood-based fire even for the sake of public safety? Even that should be only a rabbinic violation according to Rabbi Shimon, who holds *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* is not a biblical offense. The Gemara's answer is that Shmuel maintains two independent positions: *bidvar she'eino miskaven savar lah k'Rabbi Shimon* — regarding unintended acts, he holds like Rabbi Shimon, and an unintended consequence is no violation; *uv'melacha she'eina tzricha legufa savar lah k'Rabbi Yehuda* — regarding an act done intentionally but for the wrong purpose, he holds like Rabbi Yehuda, and such an act is a biblical violation.

Tosafot notes that the Gemara's *hava amina* — that these two positions should go together — is not unreasonable, since both concepts revolve around how intention defines and connects one to an act. In one case the question is whether one was trying to do the act at all; in the other, whether one was doing it for its halachically defined purpose. Rabbi Shimon in both instances is more lenient because he gives greater weight to intention in defining the act. Rabbi Yehuda in both is stricter because he is less willing to allow intention to sever one's responsibility for the physical reality that resulted. Tosafot also points out that *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* is a *Shabbat* concept, while *eino miskavein* applies *kol haTorah kulah* — across all areas of *halacha* — as this very *sugya* demonstrates by applying it to the fire of the *mizbe'ach*.

## Invalid Nesachim

### Rav Huna Ruling

Following the *hadran* concluding the previous chapter, we move to a brief but important ruling of Rav Huna: *nesachim shenitmu ose lahen maaracha bifnei atzman v'sorfan* — if liquids designated for the altar (wine, oil, or the like) became *tamei*, one creates a separate fire for them and burns them within the *azara*.

### Temple Burning Rules

This ruling requires some explanation. There is a known distinction between *parim hanisrafim* — certain *korbanot* whose blood was brought into the inner sanctuary, which are burned *outside* the *Beit HaMikdash* — and offerings that became *pasul* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, which are burned *inside*. The general principle is *bakodesh ba'esh tisaref*. Rav Huna holds that invalid *nesachim* follow this latter rule and are burned inside. The *chiddush*, however, is that they are not burned together with other *pasul* items on the standard fire, but on a separate fire created specifically for them.

### Rashi's Reasoning

Why a separate fire? Rashi explains that the reason is related to visibility. When *pasul korbanot* are burned, it is important that it be apparent that what is being burned is a *pasul* item. If liquid offerings are burned together with other materials on the same fire, no one would notice that liquids are being burned — they would simply be absorbed or dispersed. A separate fire makes the burning of these liquids visible and publicly recognizable. This may also connect to the earlier concern about liquids extinguishing other fires: handling them separately avoids that problem.

A *braita* confirms this: *hadam v'hashemen v'hamenachot v'hanesachim shenitmu ose lahen maaracha bifnei atzman v'sorfan* — the blood, oil, *menachot*, and *nesachim* that became *tamei* are burned on a separate fire. It is interesting that *menachot* appear in this list alongside liquids; though not a liquid, a *mincha* is not meat and would not remain as a solid piece burning on a fire the way animal parts do — it disperses, which presents similar concerns of visibility.

### Public Announcement

The Gemara then records a striking detail: Shmuel instructed Rav Chana of Baghdad (*Rav Chana Bagdata'ah*), saying *aiti li bei asara* — bring me ten people together — and he wished to announce publicly in front of ten people: *nesachim shenitmu ose lahen maaracha bifnei atzman v'sorfan*. This is a peculiar choice of *halacha* to publicize. The principle of burning *pasul* items inside the *Beit HaMikdash* is well established; the particular detail about a separate fire is not so radical a ruling. Yet Shmuel considered it important enough to gather a *minyan* and issue a public announcement. The Gemara does not explain why.

## Dam Chatas Intro

### Scriptural Source

We now begin the chapter of *dam chatas*, which concerns special laws pertaining to the blood of a *chatat*. The chapter opens with a *pasuk*: *asher yizeh midama al habeged* — if the blood of a *chatat* spatters onto a garment — *techaves bemakom kadosh* — the garment must be laundered in a holy place; one may not take it outside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Extending to All

The *Mishnah* immediately engages in *midrash halacha*. It observes that although the *pshat* of the *psukim* appears to be discussing only *chatat* offerings that are eaten — *bimkom kadosh te'achel*, eaten in a holy place, followed by *asher yizeh midama al habeged techaves bimkom kadosh* — nevertheless the law of laundering applies equally to both *chatat* offerings that are eaten and to inner *chata'ot* that are burned. The source for this extension is the phrase *Torat HaChatas*, the Torah of the *chatat* — *torah achat l'chol hachata'ot* — one teaching applies to all *chata'ot*.

### Invalid Blood

The *Mishnah* then qualifies: *chatas pesulah ein damah ta'un kibus* — if a *chatat* was invalid, its blood does not require laundering. The derivation is from the word *asher yizeh* — "which is sprinkled" — implying that laundering is required only for blood that is fit to be sprinkled. Blood that is invalid and not eligible for sprinkling does not carry this *din*. This applies regardless of whether the *chatat* had a moment when it was valid — for example, blood that was caught in a *kli sharet* and was eligible to be thrown, but then was left overnight (*shelana*), became *tamei*, or was taken outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — or whether it was never valid from the outset, as when the *chatat* was slaughtered with intent for the wrong time (*chutz lizmanah*), wrong place (*chutz limkomah*), or the blood was caught by invalid persons or not received in a *kli sharet*. In all these cases, the blood does not obligate laundering.

## Bird Sin-Offering

### Inclusion Question

The Gemara opens with a challenge against the *Mishnah*: *ve'ee torah achas l'chol chata'ot* — if the phrase *torah achat* extends the *din* of laundering to all *chata'ot* — *afilu chatas ha'of nami* — then it should also apply to a bird *chatat*. Yet a *braita* explicitly asks whether the blood of a bird *chatat* should require laundering and then teaches that it does not.

### Limiting Word Zot

The *braita* excludes bird *chatat* through a limiting word: *talmud lomar zot* — the verse says "this," which is a word of exclusion, narrowing the scope of the *din*. The Gemara thus faces a classic tension: the word *torah* includes; the word *zot* excludes. Among the candidates for inclusion and exclusion are the inner *chatat* and the bird *chatat*. The question is: what is the basis for choosing to include one and exclude the other?

### Reish Lakish View

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, offers a different approach to the exclusion of bird *chatat*: the verse states *tishachet bimkom kadosh* — it shall be *slaughtered* in a holy place — indicating that the Torah is speaking only of offerings that undergo *shechita*, which excludes birds, whose method is *melikah*, not *shechita*.

### The Problem

The Gemara immediately challenges this: by the same logic, the *pshat* of the *psukim* also limits the law to things that are eaten — *bimkom kadosh te'achel* — which would exclude inner *chata'ot*. If *tishachet* limits us to non-birds, then *te'achel* limits us to eaten offerings and equally excludes inner *chata'ot*. There is no textual basis within the *pshat* to include one and exclude the other; both fall outside the literal scope of the verse. The inclusion of inner *chatat* and exclusion of bird *chatat* must therefore be argued on other grounds.

### Comparison Points

The Gemara's answer is that while both the word *torah* includes and the word *zot* excludes, when we must choose what to include and what to exclude, we look at which candidate has more points of comparison to the outer *chatat* — the subject of the verse in its plain meaning. The outer *chatat* is: a *behemah*, slaughtered via *shechita*, in the northern part of the *azara* (*tzafon*), with the blood received in a *kli sharet*, applied to the *keren* of the *mizbe'ach*, and the innards placed on the fire (*ishim*). We then ask: which candidate is more similar to this — the inner *chatat* or the bird *chatat*?

### Seven Similarities

The Gemara enumerates the points of comparison between the outer *chatat* and the inner *chatat*: *sheken behemah* — both are *behemot*; *shechitah* — both are slaughtered; *tzafon* — both require the northern section; *kabbalas kli* — both require reception of blood in a *kli sharet*; *keren* — both involve the horn of the *mizbe'ach*; *etzba* — both involve the finger; *ishim* — both involve placing parts on the fire. Seven points of similarity, against which the only difference is that one is eaten and one is burned internally. By contrast, the bird *chatat* differs in every one of these respects: it is an *of*, not a *behemah*; its method is *melikah*, not *shechita*; it does not require *tzafon* but may be performed anywhere (*kol makom*); there is no *kli sharet*; its blood goes on the lower part of the *mizbe'ach*, not the *keren*; and nothing is placed on the fire since it is entirely eaten.

The Gemara then asks whether we should reverse the choice: perhaps we should include the bird *chatat* and exclude the outer *chatat*, since the outer *chatat* and the bird *chatat* share the features of being eaten (*achilah*) and going on the outside (*chutz*). The Gemara dismisses this: *hanoch nefishet* — there is no contest, the inner *chatat* has far more points of comparison and therefore is the correct candidate for inclusion.

### Rav Yosef View

Rav Yosef offers a different derivation. The verse says *yochlena* — "he shall eat it." This word focuses the verse on eaten offerings specifically. Rav Yosef reads the word *zot* as coming to exclude something *within* the category of eaten offerings — namely the bird *chatat*, which is also eaten. The word *yochlena* is not merely incidental phrasing; it zeroes in on what we are discussing, and within that category the word *zot* makes a distinction. The Gemara asks: if *yochlena* does the work, why is *zot* needed? The answer: without *zot*, one would say *yochlena* is simply the way the verse speaks, not a term of exclusion (*orchay d'kra*). It is *zot* that signals something is being excluded, while *yochlena* identifies *which* category of things is being narrowed — things that are eaten — thus pointing to the bird *chatat* as the excluded item.

### Rava's Approach

Rava takes a different starting point entirely. Rava observes that the verse, in the context of the laundering *din*, uses the word *yizeh* — which is the terminology of *hazaa*, sprinkling. This word is most naturally associated with the inner *chatat*, where blood is sprinkled multiple times in the sanctuary — *achat achat v'achat* — rather than with the outer *chatat*, where blood is placed with a finger on the *keren*. Therefore, says Rava, the verse does not begin with the outer *chatat* as its subject and then extend to the inner; rather, the verse's primary subject is the inner *chatat*, and from there it extends to include the outer. In other words, Rava reverses the starting point: instead of beginning with the outer and including the inner, we begin with the inner and include the outer.

This has implications for how one reads the *Mishnah*, which states *af al pi she'ein hakatuv ela bene'achlot* — "although the verse speaks only of eaten offerings." Rava says: that language in the *Mishnah* applies to the passage dealing with *merika ushtifa* — the cleansing of the pot used to cook the *chatat* — where the *pshat* does speak of an eaten offering. But regarding the *din* of laundering, triggered by the word *yizeh*, the verse's subject is the inner *chatat*. A *braita* confirms this reading: "although the verse speaks only of eaten offerings" is limited to the context of *merika ushtifa*, while for *kibus* the verse uses *asher yizeh*, placing the inner *chatat* at the center.

### Rava Challenge

The Gemara challenges Rava: if we begin from the inner *chatat*, we still face the question of why we extend to include the outer and not the bird. The word *torah* includes, the word *zot* excludes, and if we have begun from the inner *chatat*, why not include the bird, which shares the feature of *hazaa* — the very term that, by Rava's own reasoning, placed the inner *chatat* at the center of the verse? The Gemara answers: *hani nefishen* — there are still far more points of comparison between the inner *chatat* and the outer *chatat* than between the inner *chatat* and the bird. What is more, if Rava began from the inner *chatat*, the bird *chatat* actually presents a stronger challenge here than it did under the previous approaches: Rava's very reason for starting with the inner *chatat* was the word *yizeh*, the language of sprinkling — but the bird *chatat*'s blood is also squirted, not placed with a finger, and is equally described by the language of *hazaa*. The Gemara nevertheless concludes: the sheer number of comparative points between inner and outer *behemah chatat* still outweighs the single shared feature of sprinkling with the bird. Thus Rava's approach, for all its reordering, ultimately produces the same conclusion — and the rhetorical question hangs in the air of what Rava's approach actually adds.

## Unresolved Questions

### Blood in Neck

Rav Avin raises a question that shifts the discussion away from laundering and toward the rules of the bird *chatat* service itself: *chatat of she-hichnis dama bifnim mah?* — if one performed *melikah* on a bird *chatat*, held one's hand against the throat so the blood would not yet squirt out, and then carried the bird into the *heikhal* — what is the law?

As established earlier, if the blood of a *chatat* is brought inside the *heikhal* it becomes invalid — the offering is disqualified and must be burned. That rule normally applies when blood is carried inside in a *kli sharet*. Here, the bird's blood has not yet been discharged into any vessel — it remains within the bird's neck. The question is whether to treat the bird's neck as the functional equivalent of a *kli sharet*, such that carrying the bird into the *heikhal* would constitute bringing blood inside and thereby invalidate the offering — or whether, since the blood is still within the body of the animal, it is more analogous to the neck of a *behemah* whose blood has not yet been separately received, and therefore does not yet count as blood being brought inside.

### Neck as Vessel

The Gemara frames the two sides of the question precisely: *savar kikli sharet dami* — perhaps, since there is normally no *kli sharet* in the bird *chatat* service, the neck of the bird *is* the *kli sharet*, and therefore the blood is already "in a vessel" and bringing it into the *heikhal* should invalidate — *umifsal* — or perhaps we treat it as *k'tzavar beheima midamei*, comparable to the neck of a *behemah*, where the blood has simply not yet emerged as a discrete halachic entity, and the act does not constitute bringing blood inside.

The Gemara suggests a possible resolution from a verse: *midama amar Rachmana* — the Torah speaks of "its blood" being brought inside, *v'lo b'sara* — and not "its flesh." While the blood remains inside the neck and has not been separated from the flesh, one is not bringing *dam*, blood, inside — one is bringing the body of the animal. This would suggest that the act does not invalidate. The same reasoning applies by analogy: just as we would say that walking a cow into the *heikhal* with its throat cut but the blood retained does not constitute bringing blood inside, so too with the bird.

### Thrashing Braisa

A *braita* is adduced: *pirkesa v'nikhnesa lifnim v'hazra kesheira* — if after *melikah* the bird thrashed about in its death throes, rolled into the *heikhal*, and then rolled out, the offering remains *kesheira*. The Gemara observes that the implicit reasoning is that the bird moved itself — there was no human act of *bringing* the blood inside *l'khaper*, with intent toward atonement, which is part of what triggers the invalidation. The *braita* might therefore be read to imply that had one *intentionally* brought the bird inside, it would have been *pesulah*.

### Intentional vs. Unintent…

The Gemara immediately rejects this implication by analogy. A similar *braita* about an animal *chatat* teaches: *pirkesa v'yatzah l'darom v'hazra kesheira* — if the animal thrashed after *shechita* and moved to the south (outside the *tzafon* area), and then returned, it remains valid. No one would suggest that if a person intentionally moved the blood to the south it would invalidate — there is simply no invalidating act in taking blood southward. The *braita* records the case of thrashing not to contrast it with intentional action, but to teach something else.

### Outside Courtyard

What is the *chiddush* of the thrashing *braita*? It is that even if the bird inadvertently moved *outside the azara* altogether — not merely to the south inside the *azara* but actually out of the *azara* — that does not invalidate. The lesson is about the absence of invalidation when the animal exits the *azara* on its own. The implication about intentional bringing into the *heikhal* remains unresolved; the thrashing case does not speak to it.

### Spilled Blood

Rav Avin raises a second question: *nishpach al haritzpah ve-asafah mahu?* — if after *melikah* the blood of a bird *chatat* squirted not onto the *mizbe'ach* but onto the floor — perhaps the *kohen* was distracted and missed — can one gather it up from the floor and apply it to the *mizbe'ach*?

### Direct vs. Vessel

This question depends on a prior *halacha* for animal *korbanot*: if blood was received in a *kli sharet* and then spilled, it may be gathered and reapplied; if it was never received in a *kli sharet*, it cannot. But the bird *chatat* has no *kli sharet*. So the question resolves into a deeper structural question about the nature of the bird *chatat* service: does the Torah's dispensing with a *kli sharet* mean that the body of the bird itself functions as the *kli sharet* — such that the blood was already "received," and if it spills it may be gathered — or does the Torah's omission of a *kli sharet* reflect a fundamentally different mode of service, in which the *mitzvah* is the direct application of blood from the bird's neck straight onto the *mizbe'ach*, and any deviation from that path is not the *mitzvah*?

### Rava Argument

Rava attempts to resolve this from the earlier *braita* about laundering. The *braita* asked whether blood of a bird *chatat* requires laundering and excluded it via the word *zot*. But if it is true that the Torah requires direct neck-to-altar application, and anything else invalidates the blood, then there would have been no need to exclude the bird *chatat* from laundering through *zot*. Whenever blood splatters on a garment — rather than going directly onto the *mizbe'ach* — it would already be *pasul* blood, and *pasul* blood does not require laundering. The exclusion would be unnecessary. The very fact that *zot* was needed to exclude bird *chatat* from laundering implies that bird blood is *not* automatically invalidated when it takes an indirect path — and therefore the possibility of gathering spilled blood from the floor and using it is a live one.

### Air Space Principle

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua responds: the case addressed by the *braita* is *bemadbi'a kli betzavara* — the vessel was pressed directly against the neck of the bird, so the blood hit the garment without passing through any air space. The existing *halacha* is that blood that passes over the air space of a vessel is considered to have entered that vessel — *ba-avir kli mifsal* — and would thereby be invalidated. In the *braita*'s case, since there was no air space, the blood was not invalidated before striking the garment. It was therefore still valid blood at the moment of impact, and one might have thought laundering would be required — hence the need for *zot* to exclude it. This does not tell us anything about the structural question of whether direct application is required; it tells us only that the *braita* is discussing a narrow scenario where the question of prior invalidation through air space does not arise.

### Unresolved Nature

The question of the fundamental nature of bird *chatat* blood application thus remains unresolved: does the Torah require direct squirting from neck to *mizbe'ach*, making any other mode of application an invalid deviation — or does the Torah simply dispense with the *kli sharet* requirement, leaving one free to apply the blood by other means and permitting the gathering of spilled blood? The consequences are significant: whether spilled blood may be gathered, whether a *kli sharet* could theoretically be used, and whether the bird's neck itself functions as a *kli sharet* for all attendant purposes. We leave these questions as *teku* — unresolved — and will continue from here.
