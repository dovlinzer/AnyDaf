# Pesachim 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women & Korban Pesach

### Perek HaIsha Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית בַּעְלָהּ, שָׁחַט עָלֶיהָ בַּעְלָהּ וְשָׁחַט עָלֶיהָ אָבִיהָ — תֹּאכַל מִשֶּׁל בַּעְלָהּ. הָלְכָה רֶגֶל רִאשׁוֹן לַעֲשׂוֹת בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ, שָׁחַט עָלֶיהָ אָבִיהָ וְשָׁחַט עָלֶיהָ בַּעְלָהּ — תֹּאכַל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהִיא רוֹצָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A woman, when she is** living **in her husband's house,** if **her husband slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **on her behalf and her father** also **slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **on her behalf, she** should **eat from her husband's** lamb because it is assumed that the wife intended to be included in her husband's group. However, if, as was often customary, **she went on the first Festival** following her marriage **to observe** the Festival **in her father's house,** then, if both **her husband slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **on her behalf and her father** also **slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **on her behalf, she** may **eat in** whichever **place she wishes,** since it is not obvious with whose group she intended to be included.

We begin now the eighth *perek*, known as *HaIsha*, opening at the top of 87a. One topic that has not yet received focused treatment — though it has been alluded to in passing — is women's obligation in the *korban Pesach*. The present *mishna* opens precisely on this question, though it approaches it obliquely: since we have been discussing the *chavura*, the group that participates together in the *korban Pesach*, this *mishna* begins by asking which group a particular woman belongs to — a woman who is simultaneously a wife and a daughter. But the discussion will eventually extend to the broader question of women's obligation in the *korban Pesach* itself.

### Woman in Husband's House

The *mishna* opens: *haisha bizman shehi beveis ba'alah* — when a woman is in her husband's house, and both her husband (*shachat aleha ba'alah*) and her father (*shachat aleha aviha*) each slaughter the *korban Pesach* with her in mind, then *tochal mishel ba'alah* — we assume she wishes to be with her husband, and she eats from his *korban*.

This raises an immediate and important question beyond the matter of assumed preference: what exactly constitutes membership in a *chavura* for the *korban Pesach*? There is no indication in the *mishna* that she contributed money, or that any formal act of *kinyan* was performed. It sounds as though her inclusion is determined purely by intent — what was in her head, or what others assumed was in her head. One might ask whether this is an application of *zachin leadam shelo bifanav*, the principle that one may acquire a benefit on behalf of another without their explicit presence. That is certainly possible. But it is not obvious that a formal act of *kinyan* was performed at all. This remains something to explore: what precisely constitutes membership in the *chavura*?

### Woman Visits Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ עָלָיו אַפּוֹטְרוֹפְּסִין — יֹאכַל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה. עֶבֶד שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין לֹא יֹאכַל מִשֶּׁל שְׁנֵיהֶן. מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין לֹא יֹאכַל מִשֶּׁל רַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **an orphan** with multiple guardians, if each of his **guardians [*apotropsin*] slaughtered** a Paschal lamb **on his behalf,** intending that he be included in their group, he may **eat in** whichever **place he wishes. A slave** jointly owned **by two partners** may **not eat from** the lamb of **either of them,** unless it was stipulated beforehand from whose lamb he will partake. **One who is half slave and half free man may not eat from his master's** lamb. It is assumed that the master did not intend to allow this person's free half to partake of the lamb, and therefore the master did not slaughter the lamb with him in mind. Consequently, the half slave is not included among those registered for his master's offering unless he was explicitly included.

A more complex case: *halcha regel rishon la'asos beveis aviha* — if on the first *Pesach* she went to her father's house, and both her father and her husband slaughtered the *korban Pesach* with her in mind, then *tochal bimakom shehi rotza* — she eats where she wishes.

Several ambiguities press upon this case. First, what is meant by *regel rishon* — is it the very *Pesach* during which she went to her father's house? Or does it refer to the following year, based on a pattern established when she spent the first *yom tov* after marriage at her father's home? Moreover, the *regel rishon* in question need not have been *Pesach* at all — perhaps she went to her father's house on *Sukkos*, thereby indicating a general preference for spending *yom tov* with her parents, and the question then arises for the following *Pesach*. The *mishna* is not clear on any of these points.

What is clear is that the *mishna* allows her preference to be determinative, and the *gemara* will raise the question of *breira* — retroactive determination — in connection with this ruling.

### Orphan & Trustees

A parallel case involves an orphan whose estate is managed by *apitropsin* — trustees or executors — and each of the two trustees slaughtered a *korban Pesach* intending to include the orphan in his group. Since the two *korbanot* are distinct, *yochal bimakom shehu rotze* — the orphan eats where he wishes. Again, the question immediately arises: does this not require a determination at the time of *shechita*? The *gemara* will address this.

### Slave Two Partners

The case of an *eved shel shenei shutfin* — a slave jointly owned by two partners — is categorically different. Here, *lo yochal mishel sheneihem*: the slave may not eat from either *korban*, and this is true even if one partner and the slave himself explicitly express their preference that the slave eat with one particular master. Since the slave belongs equally to the other partner, nothing may be done without both partners' agreement. If both partners agree, of course, the matter is straightforward — but explicit stated preference alone is insufficient without joint consent.

### Half-Slave Status

Finally, the case of *mi shechetzio eved vechetzio ben chorin* — someone who is half slave and half free, typically the result of one of two partners having emancipated his share. Such a person may not eat with his master, because we cannot take for granted that the master wishes to include him: this person is no longer fully his slave, and so the ordinary assumption that a master includes his slave in his *korban* no longer applies. Whether this person eats his own *korban* independently, or is dependent on his master's initiative, remains among the questions the *gemara* will resolve.

## Breira & Shechita

### Breira Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה? מַאי רוֹצָה — בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that in certain cases one partakes of the lamb of whichever group he desires. One's inclusion in a group requires that he be registered with that group from the outset, before the lamb is slaughtered. The Gemara suggests: **You learn from it** that **there is retroactive clarification.** One's ultimate decision as to which group he wishes to be part of retroactively indicates that, from the outset, he was registered in that group. This is problematic, as no halakhic conclusion has been reached in the matter of retroactive clarification. The Gemara therefore rejects this suggestion: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: She may eat in whichever place **she wishes?** It is referring to a case where a woman has already expressed her choice before **the time of slaughter.** Therefore, this case does not relate to the principle of retroactive clarification, and no conclusion concerning it may be drawn from it.

*Shema mina* — the *gemara* infers from this entire *mishna* that it operates on the principle of *yesh breira*: retroactive determination. If the woman's, or the orphan's, expressed preference after the fact can determine their group membership retroactively, then *breira* — the idea that a later choice reveals what the status was from the beginning — must be operative. Otherwise, once the *shechita* has passed, it would be too late; one cannot retroactively join a group.

### Tosafot Breira Timing

The *gemara* deflects this inference: *mai rotza beshat shechita* — what the *mishna* means by "where she wishes" is that she had already expressed her preference at the moment of *shechita*. *Tosafot* spells this out precisely: *davka beirura beshat shechita* — the expression of preference must be specifically at the time of *shechita*. If no preference was expressed and we hold *ein breira*, then *eino ochel lo mishel zeh velo mishel zeh* — she cannot eat from either, because she is not definitively a member of either group, yet she cannot eat from both, since one may only eat from a *korban Pesach* one is a member of.

### Text vs. Doctrine

This resolution, while logically coherent, is clearly a difficult reading of the *mishna*. The simple sense of *tochal bimakom shehi rotza* is that she decides after the *shechita*, and her subsequent choice retroactively determines her membership — which is precisely the principle of *breira*. The *gemara*'s reading, in which she had already chosen at the moment of *shechita*, is strained. This is not unusual: when there is a *machloket* over a principle, the *gemara* works to show how each side can accommodate the evidence against it, even at the cost of a forced reading. Though it should be noted that *breira* is itself a dispute among *tanna'im*, so it would not have been troubling to attribute this *mishna* to the *tanna* who holds *yesh breira*.

## First vs. Later Holidays

### Remini Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: אִשָּׁה, רֶגֶל הָרִאשׁוֹן אוֹכֶלֶת מִשֶּׁל אָבִיהָ. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, רוֹצָה — אוֹכֶלֶת מִשֶּׁל אָבִיהָ, רוֹצָה — מִשֶּׁל בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** between the ruling in the mishna and a parallel ruling in a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: **A woman,** on **the first Festival** following her marriage, **eats from her father's** Paschal lamb. **From here on,** if **she wishes, she eats from her father's** lamb, and if **she wishes,** she eats **from her husband's** lamb. Whereas the mishna teaches that after the first Festival, she eats exclusively with her husband, the *baraita* teaches that she may continue to choose.

The *gemara* raises a *remini* — a contradiction from a *braita*. Whereas our *mishna* treats the woman's presence in her husband's home as the default assumption and raises doubt only when she went to her father's house on the *regel rishon*, the *braita* says the opposite: *isha regel harishon ochelet mishel aviha* — on the first *regel*, one assumes that she wants to be at her father's house, with no need for any expressed preference.

### Father First Holiday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן, בִּרְדוּפָה לֵילֵךְ. כָּאן, בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ רְדוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **not difficult. There,** the *baraita* is referring **to** the case of a woman who eagerly hurries as one **pursued to go** to her father's house. It is therefore reasonable that, even after the first year of her marriage, she wishes to be included in her father's group. However, **here** the mishna is referring **to** the case of a woman who **does not** eagerly hurry as one **pursued to go** to her father's house, and it is therefore presumed she wishes to be included in her husband's group.

The *braita* thus establishes that the first *yom tov* after marriage is assumed to be spent at the father's house — a presumption so strong that even if she did not actively go there, we assume that is where she wants to be. This aligns with a well-known social practice, attested here as a long-standing reality: that the first *yom tov* of a newly married couple is spent at the wife's parents' home.

### Later Holidays Open

After the first *regel*, the *braita* says: *rotza ochelet mishel aviha, rotza ochelet mishel ba'alah* — she eats where she wishes, and it is an open question. This stands in contrast to our *mishna*, which seems to treat the husband's household as the ordinary default. The two sources appear to reflect different baseline assumptions about where a wife wishes to celebrate *yom tov*.

### Lo Kashya Women Types

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult. *Kan be-redufa leilech, kan be-she-eino redufa*: one teaching refers to a woman who tends to run back to her father's house (*redufa*), while the other refers to a woman who does not have that inclination. The presumptions differ depending on the type of woman. As the *pasuk* says, *az hayiti be-einav kemotzet shalom* — then I was in his eyes like one who finds peace. Rabbi Yochanan interprets this strikingly: *ke-kallah she-nimtza'at sheleimah be-veit chameha* — like a bride who is found wholesome and beloved in her father-in-law's house, and because she is so beloved there, *rotza leilech le-hashbi'ach be-veit aviha* — she wants to go back to her father's house to report how warmly she has been received. This is a notable twist: one would have expected that the woman returning to her parents is doing so because things are difficult with the in-laws. Rabbi Yochanan inverts this — it is precisely because she is so loved in her husband's home that she wants to share the news with her own family.

## Shir HaShirim Interp.

### Ishi vs. Bali

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא נְאֻם ה׳ תִּקְרְאִי אִישִׁי וְלֹא תִקְרְאִי לִי עוֹד בַּעְלִי״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּכַלָּה בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ, וְלֹא כְּכַלָּה בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is written: "And it shall be at that day, says the Lord, that you shall call Me: My Husband, and shall call Me no more: My Master"** (Hosea 2:18), of which **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** She shall be **like a bride in her father-in-law's house,** where she experiences a close relationship with her husband. **And** she shall **not** be **like a bride** still in the betrothal period and living **in her father's house,** during which time her relationship with her husband has still not developed.

The *gemara* introduces a new *pasuk* — one that the mention of *kallah* and the dynamics of marriage seem to have called to mind. The verse from *Hoshea* reads: *ve-haya ba-yom ha-hu neum Hashem tikrei Ishi ve-lo tikrei li od Ba'ali* — on that day you will call me *Ishi* (my husband) and no longer call me *Ba'ali* (my master). The plain sense of the verse is that the name *Baal* will be expunged because of its association with idolatrous worship. But the verse also carries a resonance between intimacy and ownership: *ba'ali* carries connotations of possession, dominion, and a certain coarseness of relationship, whereas *ishi* evokes equivalence and closeness.

Rabbi Yochanan interprets the verse: *ke-kallah be-veit chameha ve-lo ke-kallah be-veit aviha* — like a bride in her father-in-law's house, not like a bride in her father's house. The implication is that in her father-in-law's house she has arrived at the fullness of the relationship — the intimacy of *ishi* — whereas in her father's house she was still in the status of *arusah*, the betrothed, not yet having entered fully into the husband's domain. As a betrothed woman, *ba'ali* would not yet have applied; only in the father-in-law's house does the relationship achieve its complete form. The precise connection between the two parts of Rabbi Yochanan's statement remains somewhat elusive, and if a better interpretation is available, it should be offered.

### Sister Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָחוֹת לָנוּ קְטַנָּה וְשָׁדַיִם אֵין לָהּ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ עֵילָם, שֶׁזָּכְתָה לִלְמוֹד, וְלֹא זָכְתָה לְלַמֵּד.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the verse from Song of Songs cited previously, the Gemara homiletically interprets an adjacent verse: **"We have a little sister, and she has no breasts"** (Song of Songs 8:8). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This is** an allusion to the Jewish community of **Eilam, which was privileged to study** Torah and become Torah scholars, but **was not privileged to teach** and influence the masses.

The *gemara* continues with *Shir HaShirim*. The verse reads: *achot lanu ketanah ve-shadayim ein lah* — we have a little sister and she has no breasts. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *zu Eilam* — this refers to the region of Eilam, near Bavel, where Daniel went into *galut*. Eilam *zachatah lilmod ve-lo zachatah l'lamed* — it merited to learn Torah but did not merit to produce teachers of Torah. The "breasts" (*shadayim*) represent Torah in its nurturing capacity: Eilam absorbed Torah but did not nourish others from it. It is worth noting that in its famous *Shir HaShirim* commentary, *Artscroll* translates the plain sense in ways that quietly incorporate the allegorical meaning — rendering *shadayim ein lah* as essentially "she has not yet reached spiritual maturity."

### Wall Imagery Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה״ — זוֹ תּוֹרָה, ״וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים. וְרָבָא אָמַר: ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה״ — זוֹ כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets another verse: **"I am a wall and my breasts are like towers"** (Song of Songs 8:10). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: "I am a wall"; this is** a reference to the **Torah. "And my breasts are like towers"; these are the Torah scholars,** who, by disseminating their Torah and influencing the masses protect them like watchtowers. **And Rava said: "I am a wall"; this is the Congregation of Israel. "And my breasts are like towers"; these are the synagogues and study halls** in which the Congregation of Israel is nurtured by the Torah, from which it draws its spiritual strength.

The verse continues: *ani chomah ve-shadai ke-migdalot* — I am a wall and my breasts are like towers. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *ani chomah zu Torah* — the wall is the Torah. *Ve-shadai ke-migdalot elu talmidei chachamim* — the towers are the Torah sages. As Rashi explains, the image here is not nurturing but protective: *she-meginim al ha-dor* — they protect the generation, as towers protect a city. Rava offers a broader reading: *ani chomah zu Knesset Yisrael* — the wall is the Jewish people itself, and *shadai ke-migdalot elu batei knesiyot u-vatei midrashot* — the towers are the synagogues and houses of study. This reading is more inclusive, encompassing the entire people and its institutions rather than Torah study alone.

### Sons Daughters Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנֵינוּ כִּנְטִעִים מְגֻדָּלִים בִּנְעוּרֵיהֶם בְּנוֹתֵינוּ כְזָוִיֹּת מְחֻטָּבוֹת תַּבְנִית הֵיכָל״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zutra bar Toviya said** that **Rav said** a homiletic interpretation of another verse in praise of Israel: **What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "We whose sons are as plants grown up in their youth; whose daughters are as corner pillars carved after the fashion of a palace"** (Psalms 144:12)?

Rav Zutra bar Tuvia said in the name of Rav: what is meant by the verse *asher baneinu ke-neti'im megudalim bi-neureihem* — our sons are like young shoots growing in their youth — *binoteinu ke-zaviyot mechutavot tavnit heichal* — our daughters are like hewn cornerstones, fashioned in the image of the *heichal*?

### Interpretation of Sons and Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנֵינוּ כִּנְטִעִים״ — אֵלּוּ בַּחוּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא טָעֲמוּ טַעַם חֵטְא. ״בְּנוֹתֵינוּ כְזָוִיֹּת״ — אֵלּוּ בְּתוּלוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאוֹגְדוֹת פִּתְחֵיהֶן לְבַעֲלֵיהֶן. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּמָלְאוּ כַּמִּזְרָק כְּזָוִיֹּת מִזְבֵּחַ״, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״מְזָוֵינוּ מְלֵאִים מְפִיקִים מִזַּן אֶל זַן״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets each phrase of this verse: **"We whose sons are as plants"** indicates they are healthy and undamaged; **these are the young men of Israel who have not tasted the taste of sin. "Whose daughters are as corner pillars"** indicates that they are filled and sealed; **these are the virgins of Israel, who bind** and seal **their openings** exclusively **for their husbands. And similarly,** another verse demonstrates that a corner refers to something full: **It is stated: "And they shall be filled like the basins, like the corners of the altar"** (Zechariah 9:15). **If you wish, say** an alternative support for this idea **from here: "Our corners are full, affording all manner of store"** (Psalms 144:13).

*Asher baneinu ke-neti'im* — these are the young men of Israel *shelo ta'amu ta'am chet* — who have not yet had the taste of sin, referring in context to the domain of sexual transgression, including *zera le-vatalah* and *hirhurim*. They are still like young saplings, innocent of the sins that come with maturity. *Binoteinu ke-zaviyot* — the daughters, like cornerstones, *elu betulot Yisrael she-ogdot pitcheihen le-ba'aleihen* — these are the virgins of Israel who guard their openings for their husbands, protecting their virginity until marriage.

### Bloodline Symbol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מְחֻטָּבוֹת תַּבְנִית הֵיכָל״ — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מַעֲלֶה עֲלֵיהֶן הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ נִבְנָה הֵיכָל בִּימֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns and interprets the final phrase of the verse: **"Carved after the fashion of a palace"; the verse ascribes to both these and those,** the young men and women who vigilantly preserve their modesty, **as though the Sanctuary were built in their days.**

How does the word *zaviyot* convey this? The *gemara* draws a comparison to the verse: *u-mal'u ke-mizrak ke-zaviyot mizbe'ach* — and they will be filled like the *mizrak*, like the corners of the altar. The *mizrak* is the bowl that catches the blood of the sacrifice at the corners of the altar. *Zaviyot* in both places involves the retention of blood — and so it evokes the retention of blood that signifies virginity. This is the plain sense of the interpretation. Rashi also suggests a second reading: that it refers to women who are meticulous about *hilchot niddah*, ensuring that marital intimacy does not occur during the forbidden period — *sochfot pitcheihen le-ba'aleihen* — meaning that it is the women who maintain this boundary even when the husband might urge otherwise.

A further verse is offered: *meizaveinu melei'im mefikkim mizan el zan* — our storehouses are full, giving forth produce upon produce. The word *mezavveinu* echoes *ke-zavit*, and *mizan el zan* may carry a play on *znut* — sexual desire. The picture is one of abundant desire that is nevertheless mastered.

### Desire Mastery

This is concluded by the *gemara* at the phrase *mechutavot tavnit heichal* — hewn in the image of the *heichal*, specifically of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* states that both the men and the women who achieve mastery over their desires are recognized by Scripture as if the *Beit HaMikdash* has been built in their days. This is the culminating point of the digression: the mastery of desire — whether in the form of young men untainted by sin or young women who guard their fidelity — is the spiritual equivalent of the construction of the *Mikdash*.

## Hoshea Narrative

### Four Prophets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דְּבַר ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הָיָה אֶל הוֹשֵׁעַ וְגוֹ׳ בִּימֵי עֻזִּיָּהוּ יוֹתָם אָחָז יְחִזְקִיָּה מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה״. בְּפֶרֶק אֶחָד נִתְנַבְּאוּ אַרְבָּעָה נְבִיאִים, וְגָדוֹל שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן הוֹשֵׁעַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּחִלַּת דִּבֶּר ה׳ בְּהוֹשֵׁעַ״. וְכִי בְּהוֹשֵׁעַ דִּבֵּר תְּחִלָּה? וַהֲלֹא מִמֹּשֶׁה עַד הוֹשֵׁעַ כַּמָּה נְבִיאִים! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּחִלָּה לְאַרְבָּעָה נְבִיאִים שֶׁנִּתְנַבְּאוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַפֶּרֶק, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הוֹשֵׁעַ, יְשַׁעְיָה, עָמוֹס וּמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the verse from Hosea, the Gemara interprets additional verses there: **"The word of the Lord that came unto Hosea,** the son of Beeri, **in the days of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah, kings of Judea,** and in the days of Jeroboam the son of Joash, king of Israel" (Hosea 1:1). The Sages explained: **Four prophets prophesied in one era and the oldest of them was Hosea, as it is stated: "When the Lord spoke at first with Hosea"** (Hosea 1:2), indicating that Hosea was the first of these prophets. If not, the question arises: **And** was it **with Hosea that** the Lord **spoke first** of all the prophets? **Weren't there several prophets** who lived and prophesied during the period **from Moses until Hosea?** Rather, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He was **the first of the four prophets who prophesied during that period, and these are they: Hosea, Isaiah, Amos, and Micah.**

Returning to the verse in *Hoshea* — *devar Hashem asher hayah el Hoshea ben Be'eri bimei Uziyahu Yotam Achaz Chizkiyahu malchei Yehudah* — the *gemara* notes that this opening formula parallels that of *Yeshayahu*: *chazon Yeshayahu ben Amotz asher chazah bimei Uziyahu Yotam Achaz Chizkiyahu*. The *gemara* observes: *be-ferek echad nitnab'u arba'ah nevi'im* — four prophets prophesied in the same period: *Hoshea, Yeshayah, Amos*, and *Michah*. *Tosafot* notes that *Michah* does not overlap perfectly with all four kings, but the approximation is close enough. Among these four, says the *gemara*, *ve-gadol she-be-chulam Hoshea* — the greatest was *Hoshea*, which is striking given that *Yeshayahu* fills over sixty chapters, while *Hoshea* is a relatively brief book. The proof text: *techilat devar Hashem be-Hoshea* — the beginning of God's word was with *Hoshea*. Rabbi Yochanan interprets this not as a chronological claim but as a hierarchical one: *techilah le-arba'ah nevi'im she-nitnab'u be-oto ferek* — *Hoshea* was the first, i.e., the greatest, among the four who prophesied in that era.

### God Complains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהוֹשֵׁעַ: בָּנֶיךָ חָטְאוּ. וְהָיָה לוֹ לוֹמַר: בָּנֶיךָ הֵם, בְּנֵי חֲנוּנֶיךָ הֵם, בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, גַּלְגֵּל רַחֲמֶיךָ עֲלֵיהֶן. לֹא דַּיּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא אָמַר כָּךְ, אֶלָּא אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, כׇּל הָעוֹלָם שֶׁלְּךָ הוּא, הַעֲבִירֵם בְּאוּמָּה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Hosea: Your sons,** the Jewish people, **have sinned.** Hosea **should have said** to God in response: But **they are Your sons; they are** the **sons of Your beloved ones,** the **sons of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Extend Your mercy over them. Not only did he fail to say that, but** instead **he said before Him: Master of the Universe, the entire world is Yours;** since Israel has sinned, **exchange them for another nation.**

Now the *gemara* reconstructs the backstory to *Hoshea*'s command to take a wife of prostitution. This is all implied rather than stated in the text, and *Tosafot* makes the point explicitly: *mim-she-amar lo likahat eishet zenunim darish she-mistama kach amar lo ve-kach heishiv* — from the fact that God commanded him to take a prostitute wife, we infer that there must have been a prior exchange that the *gemara* now fills in.

God said to *Hoshea*: *banecha chata'u* — your children have sinned. The expected response: *ve-hayah lo lomar* — he should have said, *banecha hen* — they are Your children, *benei chanunecha hen* — the children of Your beloved, *bnei Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov* — the children of the Patriarchs. Roll Your compassion upon them, God. Recognize that they are Yours.

### Hoshea Dismisses

Instead, *Hoshea* responded: *Ribbono shel olam, kol ha-olam shelcha hu* — Master of the Universe, the whole world is Yours. If the Jewish people have sinned, exchange them for another nation. This is presented as a radical failure of advocacy — the precise opposite of what a prophet was called to do.

### Prostitute Wife Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה לְזָקֵן זֶה? אוֹמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ וְקַח אִשָּׁה זוֹנָה וְהוֹלֵיד לְךָ בָּנִים זְנוּנִים, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹמַר לוֹ שַׁלְּחָהּ מֵעַל פָּנֶיךָ. אִם הוּא יָכוֹל לִשְׁלוֹחַ — אַף אֲנִי אֲשַׁלַּח אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הוֹשֵׁעַ לֵךְ קַח לְךָ אֵשֶׁת זְנוּנִים וְיַלְדֵי זְנוּנִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ וַיִּקַּח אֶת גֹּמֶר בַּת דִּבְלָיִם״,
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: What shall I do to this Elder** who does not know how to defend Israel? **I will say to him: Go and take a prostitute and bear for yourself children of prostitution. And after that I will say to him: Send her away from before you. If he is able to send** her away, **I will also send** away **the Jewish people.** This deliberation provides the background of the opening prophecy in Hosea, **as it is stated: "The Lord said to Hosea: Go, take for yourself a wife of prostitution and children of prostitution"** (Hosea 1:2). **And** then **it is written: "So he went and took Gomer the daughter of Diblaim"** (Hosea 1:3), and the Sages interpreted her name homiletically.

God's response — *mah e'eseh le-zakein zeh* — what shall I do with this elder? The lesson had to be taught through experience. God instructed *Hoshea*: *lech ve-kach ishah zonah ve-holid lecha bnei zenunim* — go and take a prostitute wife and have children from her, children whose paternity is uncertain. *Ve-achar kach amar lo: shalcheha me-al panecha* — and afterward, divorce her. *Im hu yachol lishloa'ch* — let us see if he is able to. If you think it is so easy to cast off a people, let us see whether you can cast off your own wife and children.

### Gomer Name Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גּוֹמֶר״, אָמַר רַב: שֶׁהַכֹּל גּוֹמְרִים בָּהּ. ״בַּת דִּבְלָיִם״, דִּבָּה רָעָה בַּת דִּבָּה רָעָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שֶׁמְּתוּקָה בְּפִי הַכֹּל כִּדְבֵלָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: שֶׁהַכֹּל דָּשִׁין בָּהּ כִּדְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Gomer"; Rav said** she was so called **because everyone would finish [*gomerim*]** having relations with her and satisfy their desires **with her. "The daughter of Diblaim";** the name Diblaim can be taken as the dual form of the word *dibba*, ill repute. It suggests that she was a woman of **ill repute, daughter of** a woman of **ill repute. And Shmuel said:** The name Diblaim is the plural of the word *deveila*, a cake of pressed figs, indicating that **she was as sweet as a cake of pressed figs,** and therefore everyone used her services. **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** based on a similar derivation, **said** the name signifies **that everyone would tread [*dashin*] upon her,** a euphemism for sexual relations, **like a cake of pressed figs.**

*Va-yelech va-yikach et Gomer bat Divlayim* — he went and took a woman named *Gomer bat Divlayim*. The *gemara* now interprets these names. *Gomer*: Rav says *she-hakol gomrin bah* — all men finish with her, meaning all men have had relations with her. *Bat Divlayim*: *dibah ra'ah bat dibah ra'ah* — she has a wicked reputation, the daughter of a woman with a wicked reputation. *Shmuel* says: *she-metukah be-fi hakol ke-devilah* — she is as sweet to everyone as pressed dried figs — with an obvious sexual connotation. Rabbi Yochanan says: *she-hakol dushin bah ke-devilah* — all trample upon her like figs being trodden during their preparation — a coarse image, using *dash*, a word associated with threshing and also with sexual intercourse. These are among the more explicit passages in *aggadah*, and one does wonder about the all-male setting in which these interpretations developed and were transmitted.

A more elevated alternative is offered: Rabbi Yehudah says *Gomer* alludes to the time — *she-bikshu ligmor mamon Yisrael* — when the nations sought to finish off the wealth of Israel, as the *bayit rishon* period drew to its close and Israel was constantly besieged and looted. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *baze-zu ve-gamru* — not merely sought, but actually did so. As the verse says, *ki ibdam melech Aram va-yasimem ke-afar la-dush* — for the king of Aram had destroyed them and made them like dust for trampling.

### Children Names Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״גּוֹמֶר״, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שֶׁבִּקְּשׁוּ לְגַמֵּר מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיָמֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בָּזְזוּ וְגָמְרוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אִבְּדָם מֶלֶךְ אֲרָם וַיְשִׂמֵם כֶּעָפָר לָדֻשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** with regard to the name **Gomer, Rav Yehuda said:** The name can be understood as deriving from the root *gamar*, to finish. It alludes to the fact that the gentiles **sought to finish the money of the Jewish people in her days. Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** They did not just seek to do so, but were successful. **They plundered and finished** it, **as it is stated: "For the king of Aram destroyed them and made them like the dust in threshing"** (II Kings 13:7).

*Va-tahar va-teled lo ben* — she conceived and bore a son. God commanded *Hoshea*: *kera shemo Yizre'el* — call his name *Yizre'el*, recalling the blood of *Navot*'s vineyard and the sin of the house of *Achav*: *ki od me'at u-fakadti et d'mei Yizre'el al beit Yehu* — I will visit the blood of *Yizre'el* upon the house of *Yehu*. *Ve-hishbatti mamlechut beit Yisrael* — I will cause the sovereignty of the house of Israel to cease.

She conceived again and bore a daughter. *Kera shemah Lo Ruchama* — call her name *Lo Ruchama*, for *ki lo osif od arachem et beit Yisrael* — I will no longer have compassion on the house of Israel. The closing phrase of that verse, *ki naso esa lahem*, is difficult — literally it seems to speak of lifting or forgiving, and Rashi reads it rhetorically: do you think I will still forgive them?

Then a third child, a son: *kera shemo Lo Ami* — call his name *Lo Ami*, for *ki atem lo ami* — you are not my nation — *ve-anochi lo ehye lachem* — and I will no longer be for you.

### God Demands Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ [לוֹ] שְׁנֵי בָנִים וּבַת אַחַת, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהוֹשֵׁעַ: לֹא הָיָה לְךָ לִלְמוֹד מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבָּךְ? שֶׁכֵּיוָן שֶׁדִּבַּרְתִּי עִמּוֹ פֵּירַשׁ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, אַף אַתָּה בְּדוֹל עַצְמְךָ מִמֶּנָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם יֵשׁ לִי בָּנִים מִמֶּנָּה וְאֵין אֲנִי יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאָהּ וְלֹא לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **After two sons and one daughter had been born to him, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Hosea: Shouldn't you have learned from** the example of **your master Moses, who, once I spoke with him, separated from** his **wife? You too, separate yourself from** your wife. **He said to Him: Master of the Universe, I have sons from her and I am unable to dismiss her or to divorce her.**

After these two sons and a daughter were born, God said to *Hoshea*: *lo hayah lecha lilmod mi-Moshe rabecha* — should you not have learned from your teacher Moses? *She-keivan she-dibarti imo peirash min ha-ishah* — from the moment I spoke with him, he separated himself from his wife. *Af ata bdal atzmecha mimenah* — you too should now separate yourself from this woman. Divorce her.

### Israel Eternal Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: וּמָה אַתָּה שֶׁאִשְׁתְּךָ זוֹנָה וּבָנֶיךָ [בְּנֵי] זְנוּנִים, וְאֵין אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ אִם שֶׁלְּךָ הֵן אִם שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים הֵן — כָּךְ, יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהֵן בָּנַי, בְּנֵי בְּחוּנַי בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, אֶחָד מֵאַרְבָּעָה קִנְיָנִין שֶׁקָּנִיתִי בְּעוֹלָמִי.
>
> **Translation:** In response to Hosea's show of loyalty to his family, **the Holy One, Blessed be He,** rebuked him and **said to him: Just as you, whose wife is a prostitute and your children** from her **are children of prostitution, and you do not** even **know if they are yours** or **if they are** children **of other** men, despite this, you are still attached to them and will not forsake them, **so** too, I am still attached to **the Jewish people, who are My sons, the sons of My** faithful who withstood **ordeals, the sons of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.** They are so special that they are **one of the four acquisitions that I acquired in My world.**

*Hoshea* replied: *Ribbono shel olam, yesh li banim mimenah, ve-eini yachol lehotzi'ah ve-lo le-garshah* — I have children from her; I cannot expel her, nor can I divorce her. Note that expulsion and formal divorce are treated as two separate acts.

God responded: *u-mah ata she-ishtecha zonah u-vanecha bnei zenunim, ve-ei ata yode'a im shelcha heim im shel acherim heim* — you, whose wife is a prostitute and whose children may not even be yours — you cannot bring yourself to divorce her. Yet you told Me to simply exchange the Jewish people for another nation? *She-hein banai, bnei bechunai, bnei Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov* — they are My children, the children of My chosen ones, the children of the Patriarchs. They are *echad me-arba'ah kinyanim she-kaniti be-olami* — one of four special acquisitions I have in this world. Torah — *Hashem kanani reishit darko*. Heaven and earth — *koneh shamayim va-aretz* (and it should be noted that *koneh* in biblical Hebrew can also mean "to create," as in *kaniti ish* at the birth of *Kayin*). The *Beit HaMikdash* — *har zeh kanah yeminecha*. And Israel — *am zu kanita*. *Ve-ata amarta ha'avireim be-umah acheret* — and you told Me to exchange them for another nation?

### Hoshea's Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּדַע שֶׁחָטָא, עָמַד לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל עַצְמוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: עַד שֶׁאַתָּה מְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל עַצְמְךָ — בַּקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁגָּזַרְתִּי עֲלֵיהֶם שָׁלֹשׁ גְּזֵירוֹת בַּעֲבוּרֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Once** Hosea **realized that he had sinned, he got up to request** that God have **compassion upon him** for having spoken ill of the Jewish people. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Before you request compassion upon yourself,** first **request compassion upon the Jewish people, since I have** already **decreed upon them three** harsh **decrees on your account,** in response to your condemnation of them.

*Keivan she-yada she-chata* — once *Hoshea* understood that he had sinned, *amad le-vakesh rachamim al atzmo* — he stood to seek compassion for himself. This is noted pointedly: even now, his concern was for himself alone.

### Compassion for Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד וּבִקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים וּבִטֵּל גְּזֵירָה, וְהִתְחִיל לְבָרְכָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה מִסְפַּר בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּחוֹל הַיָּם וְגוֹ׳ וְהָיָה בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם לֹא עַמִּי אַתֶּם יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם בְּנֵי אֵל חָי וְנִקְבְּצוּ בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יַחְדָּו וְגוֹ׳ וּזְרַעְתִּיהָ לִּי בָּאָרֶץ וְרִחַמְתִּי אֶת לֹא רֻחָמָה וְאָמַרְתִּי לְלֹא עַמִּי עַמִּי אַתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Hosea **stood and requested compassion** upon the Jewish people **and nullified** the **decree.** God responded **and began to bless them, as it is stated: "Yet the number of the children of Israel shall be as the sand of the sea,** which cannot be measured nor numbered. **And it will be that instead of that which was said to them: You are not My people, it shall be said to them: You are the children of the living God. And the children of Judea and the children of Israel shall be gathered together"** (Hosea 2:1). **And I will sow her to Me in the land; and I will have compassion upon her that had not received compassion; and I will say to them that were not My people: You are My people"** (Hosea 2:25).

God said to him: *ad she-ata me-vakesh rachamim al atzmecha, bakesh rachamim al Yisrael* — before you worry about forgiveness for yourself, ask for compassion for Israel. That had been his obligation from the very beginning. *She-gazarti aleihem shalosh gezeirot ba'avurecha* — because of your sin, the three harsh edicts — the names *Yizre'el*, *Lo Ruchama*, *Lo Ami* — were issued. It is a striking theological move: all of the negative prophecies are attributed not to God's will but to *Hoshea*'s failure. There is a *yom kippur* resonance here — not merely seeking personal *mechilah*, but taking genuine responsibility for everything one has caused.

*Amad u-vikeish rachamim* — and so *Hoshea* stood and finally asked compassion for the Jewish people. *U-vatlah gezeirah* — the edict was nullified. The Jews were still destined for *galut*, but the harsh terms — *Lo Ami*, *Lo Ruchama* — were reversed. *Ve-hitchil le-varchem* — and God began to bless them through the mouth of *Hoshea*: *ve-hayah mispar bnei Yisrael ke-chol ha-yam* — they will be as the sand of the sea. *Ve-hayah bi-makom asher ye'amer lahem lo ammi atem, ye'amer lahem bnei El chai* — instead of being called "not My nation," they will be called "children of the living God." *Ve-nikbezu bnei Yehudah u-vnei Yisrael yachdav* — and Judah and Israel will be gathered together, reversing the rupture signaled by *Yizre'el*. *Ve-zartiha li va-aretz* — I will plant them in the land. *Ve-richamti et Lo Ruchama* — I will have compassion on *Lo Ruchama*. *Ve-amarti le-Lo Ami ammi atah* — and to those I called "not my nation," I will say: you are my nation.

What makes this *gemara* so theologically powerful is not merely that the negative prophecies are reversed — it is that they are revealed to have never been God's true intention. It is not simply that there was a period of *Lo Ami* that gives way to *Ami*; rather, God's whole point from the beginning was that He would never divorce Himself from the Jewish people, that they are always His children. The harsh decrees were entirely a lesson for *Hoshea*, and nothing that God ever willed for Israel. The poetic and literary flexibility of the *gemara*'s reading shifts the theology entirely. Furthermore, by making *Hoshea* the source of all the negative prophecies, the *gemara* extends a kind of theological vindication to the people: even the children born with a stigma of *bnei zenunim* — the metaphorical standing of Israel during this period — will be purified and reclaimed. Their identity as *bnei Yisrael* is not diminished by sin or punishment, because God's love and commitment never wavered.

## Commentary Authority

### Rabbinate Burden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹי לָהּ לָרַבָּנוּת שֶׁמְּקַבֶּרֶת אֶת בְּעָלֶיהָ. שֶׁאֵין לָךְ כׇּל נָבִיא וְנָבִיא שֶׁלֹּא קִיפֵּחַ אַרְבָּעָה מְלָכִים בְּיָמָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֲזוֹן יְשַׁעְיָהוּ בֶן אָמוֹץ אֲשֶׁר חָזָה עַל יְהוּדָה וִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Woe to authority, which** shortens the life and **buries its holders.** This is evident from the fact **that you don't have any prophet who did not outlast four kings in his lifetime,** as the kings' positions of authority caused them to die young. A prophet outliving four kings is demonstrated in the opening verses of Hosea, and similarly, **as it is stated** with regard to Isaiah: **"The vision of Isaiah, the son of Amoz, which he saw concerning Judea and Jerusalem** in the days of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah, kings of Judea" (Isaiah 1:1).

Rabbi Yochanan says: *oi lo le-rabbanut she-mekaveret et ba'aleha* — woe to positions of authority, for they bury those who hold them. The proof: *she-ein lecha kol navi ve-navi shelo kippe'ach arba'ah melachim be-yamav* — every prophet in this group outlived four kings. As the verse says, *chazon Yeshayahu ben Amotz asher chazah al Yehudah vi-Yerushalayim bimei Uziyahu Yotam Achaz Chizkiyahu malchei Yehudah* — so all four of these prophets outlived four successive kings. Authority takes its toll.

### Yerovam Accepts Amos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה יָרׇבְעָם בֶּן יוֹאָשׁ מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהִמָּנוֹת עִם מַלְכֵי יְהוּדָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא קִבֵּל לָשׁוֹן הָרַע עַל עָמוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Due to what** reason was the less than righteous **Jeroboam, son of Joash, king of Israel, privileged to be counted** in the verse together **with the** righteous **kings of Judea?** It is **due to** the fact **that he did not accept slander about Amos.**

Rabbi Yochanan then asks: *mipnei mah zachah Yerovam ben Yoash melech Yisrael le-himanot im malchei Yehudah* — why did *Yerovam ben Yoash*, a king of Israel, merit to be listed among the kings of Judah in the opening verse of *Hoshea*? *Mipnei shelo kibel lashon hara al Amos* — because he did not accept the *lashon hara* that was spoken against *Amos* the prophet. This is derived from the account in which *Amatzyah*, the idolatrous priest of *Beit El*, sent word to *Yerovam* that *Amos* had prophesied *bacherev yamut Yerovam* — that *Yerovam* would die by the sword. *Yerovam* responded (as supplied by the *gemara*): *chas ve-shalom* that the righteous *Amos* should have meant such a thing — and if he truly prophesied this, then *mah e'eseh lo, she-shechinah amrah lo* — what can I do? God told him so. We never find that *Yerovam* acted against *Amos*, and from his inclusion in the list we infer that he rejected the accusation entirely.

### Compassion in Anger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת כַּעְסוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא זוֹכֵר אֶת הָרַחֲמִים. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא אוֹסִיף עוֹד אֲרַחֵם אֶת בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״כִּי נָשֹׂא אֶשָּׂא לָהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Even at** the **time of** the **anger of the Holy One, Blessed be He, He remembers the** attribute of **compassion, as it is stated: "For I will no more have compassion upon the house of Israel"** (Hosea 1:6). Even when implementing His attribute of justice, God still mentions His attribute of compassion. **Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Ḥanina said** that this is also indicated **from here,** from the continuation of the verse, which states: **"That I should bear them,"** indicating that God promised to eventually bear Israel's sins and pardon them.

Rabbi Elazar says: *afilu bi-she'at ka'aso shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu, zocher et ha-rachamim* — even at the moment of God's anger, He remembers compassion. The proof: when God says *ki lo osif od arachem et beit Yisrael* — I will no longer have compassion on the house of Israel — the very phrasing invokes compassion even in its negation. When the verse says God will no longer have compassion, He could have said He would bring fierce anger; instead, He speaks in the language of compassion even while withdrawing it. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina draws the same point from the phrase *ki naso esa lahem* — even if read rhetorically as "will I forgive them?," the fact that forgiveness appears in the text at all, even as a rhetorical question within a moment of judgment, demonstrates that compassion is never entirely absent.

### Exile & Converts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא הִגְלָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְבֵין הָאוּמּוֹת אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתּוֹסְפוּ עֲלֵיהֶם גֵּרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּזְרַעְתִּיהָ לִּי בָּאָרֶץ״. כְּלוּם אָדָם זוֹרֵעַ סְאָה אֶלָּא לְהַכְנִיס כַּמָּה כּוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, exiled Israel among the nations only so that converts would join them, as it is stated: "And I will sow her to Me in the land"** (Hosea 2:25). **Does a person sow a *se'a*** of grain for any reason other **than to bring in several *kor*** of grain during the harvest? So too, the exile is to enable converts from the nations to join the Jewish people.

Rabbi Elazar further says: *lo higla Hakadosh Baruch Hu et Yisrael le-vein ha-umot ela ke-dei she-yitvasfu aleihem gerim* — the only reason God exiled Israel among the nations was so that converts would join them, so that Israel scattered across the world would bring knowledge of God to the peoples and that people would come to convert. This is a striking repositioning of *galut*: it is no longer merely punishment for sin, but is revealed as part of a divine plan oriented toward the messianic future, toward the universal recognition of God. Moreover, it reflects a profoundly positive attitude toward *gerim* — indeed, it places the value of *gerus* at the very center of the purpose of Jewish history.

The verse cited is *ve-zartiha li va-aretz* — I will plant them to Me in the land. The *gemara* reads: *adam zoreia se'ah ella le-hachnis kama korin* — one plants a small measure of grain in order to harvest many barrels. So too, the Jewish people are planted in the lands of exile in order to bring in a harvest many times larger. It is worth noting the irony: in its plain sense, the verse speaks of planting Israel back in the land of Israel as part of the ultimate redemption. The *gemara* reads it as the planting of Jewish communities in the lands of exile as seeds for that very redemption.

### Nations Converted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְרִחַמְתִּי אֶת לֹא רֻחָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that this idea may be derived **from here: "And I will have compassion upon her that had not received compassion;** and I will say to them that were not My people: You are My people" (Hosea 2:25). Even those who were initially "not My people," i.e., gentiles, will convert and become part of the Jewish nation.

Rabbi Yochanan extends this further, citing the verse *ve-richamti et Lo Ruchama ve-amarti le-Lo Ami ammi atah*: the final words — *le-Lo Ami ammi atah* — are read not only as the restoration of Israel's relationship with God, but as God's declaration to those who were never part of the nation in the first place: *you too shall be my people*. This is the vision of *gerus* as inseparable from the purpose of *galut*.

## Scattering Kindness

### Scattering Kindness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״צִדְקַת פִּרְזוֹנוֹ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״, צְדָקָה עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁפִּזְּרָן לְבֵין הָאוּמּוֹת. וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲנַן מְעַלִּינַן מִינַּיְיכוּ, כְּתִיב בְּכוּ: ״כִּי שֵׁשֶׁת חֳדָשִׁים יָשַׁב שָׁם וְגוֹ׳״, וְאִלּוּ אֲנַן אִיתִינְכוּ גַּבַּן כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי וְלָא קָא עָבְדִינַן לְכוּ מִידֵּי. אָמַר לוֹ: רְצוֹנְךָ יִטָּפֵל לָךְ תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The righteous acts of His rulers [*pirzono*] in Israel"** (Judges 5:11)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, performed a charitable deed toward Israel** in **that He scattered them [*pizran*] among the nations;** had He exiled them to one place, they could have all been destroyed at once. **And this** concept **is that which a certain apostate said to Rabbi Ḥanina: We** gentiles **are superior to you** Jews in that we have patience. **It is written of you: "For** Joab and all Israel **remained there six months** until he had cut off every male in Edom" (I Kings 11:16), **whereas we,** although **you have been with us for several years, are not doing anything to you. He said to him:** With **your consent, let one student deal with your** assertion and answer you.

Rabbi Oshia adds from the verse *tzidkat pirzono be-Yisrael* — rendering *pirzono* as *pizron*, scattering: *tzedakah asa Hakadosh Baruch Hu be-Yisrael she-pizran bein ha-umot* — God performed an act of righteousness and kindness for Israel by scattering them among the nations. This goes beyond the point about converts: the scattering itself, regardless of the missionary dimension, was an act of divine grace.

## Heretic Debate

### Heretic Boasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי, דְּאָמַר: שְׁלֹשָׁה חָזְרוּ לְמַטָּעָתָן, אֵלּוּ הֵן: יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֶּסֶף מִצְרַיִם, וּכְתַב לוּחוֹת. יִשְׂרָאֵל — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. כֶּסֶף מִצְרַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בַּשָּׁנָה הַחֲמִישִׁית לַמֶּלֶךְ רְחַבְעָם עָלָה שִׁישַׁק מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְגוֹ׳״. כְּתַב הַלּוּחוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֲשַׁבְּרֵם לְעֵינֵיכֶם״, תָּנָא: לוּחוֹת נִשְׁבְּרוּ וְאוֹתִיּוֹת פּוֹרְחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** expressed in the statement of **Rabbi Alexandri, who said:** There are **three** that **returned to their** points of **origin,** and **these are they: The Jewish people, the money of Egypt, and the writing** on the **Tablets** of the Covenant. **The Jewish people; that which we** just **said,** they returned to Babylonia. **The money of Egypt; as it is written: "And it came to pass in the fifth year of King Rehoboam, that Shishak, king of Egypt, came up against Jerusalem;** and he took away the treasures of the house of the Lord and the treasures of the king's house; he took everything" (I Kings 14:25–26). **The writing on the Tablets** of the Covenant; **as it is written:** "And I took hold of the two tablets, and cast them out of my two hands, **and broke them before your eyes"** (Deuteronomy 9:17). And **it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **The tablets were broken and the letters are flying** and returning to their point of origin.

A *min* — likely a Jewish Christian in this context — said to Rabbi Chanina: *anan mallei mineichu* — we are better than you. His evidence: the verse says that *David* sat in *Edom* for six months (*ki sheishet chodashim yashav sham*) and wiped out many of the *Edomim*. You, when you had power, used it to destroy. Whereas we — *itnechu gaban* — you have been living among us for many years, *ve-lo avdinan lachu midi* — and we have not done anything to you.

### Chanina Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְפַּל לֵיהּ רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא יָדְעִיתוּ הֵיכִי תַּעְבְּדוּ, תְּכַלִּינַן כּוּלְּהוּ — לֵיתַנְהוּ גַּבַּיְיכוּ. מַאי דְּאִיכָּא גַּבַּיְיכוּ, קָרֵי לְכוּ מַלְכוּתָא קְטִיעֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּפָּא דְרוֹמָאֵי! בְּהָא נָחֲתִינַן וּבְהָא סָלְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya dealt with his** assertion and **said to him:** This is not a sign of your righteousness but is simply **because you do not know how to do** it, to destroy us. If you seek **to destroy all of** the Jewish people, you cannot because **they are not** all **with you** in your kingdom. If you destroy only **those** Jews **who are with you** in your kingdom, **you will be called a severed kingdom** for murdering part of its own population. The apostate **said to him:** I swear by **Gappa,** god **of the Romans, with this** problem **we lie down and with this** problem **we rise up,** for we are constantly struggling with the dilemma of how to eliminate the Jewish people.

Rabbi Chanina replied: if you want, I will have a student respond to this on my behalf — treating the question as too elementary to require his personal attention. Rabbi Oshia then took the case and said: do you know why you have not eliminated us? *Mishum de-lo yadeitu heichi ta'avdu* — because you do not know how to do it. If you were to destroy all of us — *tahlinu kulhu* — we are not all concentrated among you. We are scattered. And if you want to destroy *ma de-ika gabaichu*, those of us who are among you — *kari lachu malchuta ktiasta* — you would be called a murderous kingdom. You hold back not from virtue, but from reputation.

### Scattered Safe

This ties back directly to Rabbi Oshia's earlier point about scattering as divine kindness: the very fact that Israel is scattered prevents their total annihilation. What from one angle looks like the vulnerability of exile is, from another, the guarantee of survival. The concentration of Jewish communities in any single location is itself the danger; the dispersion is the protection. The passage has obvious resonances that do not need to be made explicit.

## Why Babylon Exile

### Roman Cruelty Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אֱלֹהִים הֵבִין דַּרְכָּהּ וְהוּא יָדַע אֶת מְקוֹמָהּ״, יוֹדֵעַ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לְקַבֵּל גְּזֵירוֹת שֶׁל רוֹמִיִּים, לְפִיכָךְ הִגְלָה אוֹתָם לְבָבֶל. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא הִגְלָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְבָבֶל אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעֲמוּקָּה כִּשְׁאוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִיַּד שְׁאוֹל אֶפְדֵּם מִמָּוֶת אֶגְאָלֵם״. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקָּרוֹב לְשׁוֹנָם לִלְשׁוֹן תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "God understands its ways and He knows its place"** (Job 28:23)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, knows the Jewish people, who are unable to withstand the** harsh **decrees of the Romans. Therefore, He exiled them to Babylonia,** whose people are less cruel. **And Rabbi Elazar said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, exiled Israel to Babylonia only due to** the fact **it is** a land **as deep as the netherworld,** i.e., it is a land of plains and valleys, which alludes to that **which is stated: "I shall ransom them from the power of the netherworld, I shall redeem them from death"** (Hosea 13:14). **Rabbi Ḥanina said: It is due to** the fact **that their language,** Aramaic, **is similar to the language of the Torah,** which enables the Jews who live there to study Torah.

Rabbi Chanina explains: *yodea Hakadosh Baruch Hu et Yisrael she-einam yecholim lekabel gezerot achzariyot* — God knows that Israel could not endure the cruel decrees of *Edom*, meaning Rome. Had Israel remained in the land under Roman rule, the persecution would have been unendurable. *Le-fikach higlam le-Vavel* — therefore He exiled them to *Bavel*, where the conditions were far more tolerable.

### Babylon Deep Sheol

Rabbi Elazar adds: *lo higla Hakadosh Baruch Hu et Yisrael le-Vavel ela mipnei she-amukah ke-sheol* — God exiled Israel to *Bavel* because it is as deep as *sheol*, the underworld. The verse says *mi-yad sheol efdem, mi-mavet eg'alem* — from the hand of *sheol* I will redeem them, from death I will save them. Rashi understands this to mean that God would redeem them more swiftly precisely because they were in such a depth. There is something of a playfulness here: God decreed that they be in *sheol* and then declared He would redeem them from *sheol* — the decree creates the very occasion for the redemption.

### Aramaic Affinity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׁיגְּרָן לְבֵית אִמָּן. מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁכָּעַס עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ, לְהֵיכָן מְשַׁגְּרָהּ — לְבֵית אִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is due to** the fact **that He sent them to their mother's house,** i.e., the birthplace of the forefathers of the Jewish people, who lived in Aram-Nahara'im, which is in Babylonia. This is **comparable to a man who is angry at his wife; to where does he send her?** He sends her **to her mother's house.**

Rabbi Chanina says: *mipnei she-karov leshonam li-leshon toratan* — because the Aramaic language of *Bavel* is close to the language of their Torah. If exile was inevitable, at least *Bavel* provided linguistic and cultural kinship that made the preservation of Torah possible. This does not explain why *Bavel* over *Eretz Yisrael*, but if a destination outside the land was to be chosen, *Bavel*'s linguistic proximity made it the fitting choice.

### Mother's House Return

Rabbi Yochanan says: *mipnei she-shigram le-veit imam* — they were being sent back to their mother's house, as Abraham originated from *Aram Naharaim*, which is *Bavel*. The connection to the *mashal* offered at the beginning of the *daf* is clear: *mashal le-adam she-ka'as al ishto, le-heich meshagrah, le-veit imah* — when a man is angry with his wife, where does he send her? To her mother's house. And so God, as it were, in His anger with Israel, sent them back to the ancestral home.

### Three Returns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי, דְּאָמַר: שְׁלֹשָׁה חָזְרוּ לְמַטָּעָתָן, אֵלּוּ הֵן: יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֶּסֶף מִצְרַיִם, וּכְתַב לוּחוֹת. יִשְׂרָאֵל — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. כֶּסֶף מִצְרַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בַּשָּׁנָה הַחֲמִישִׁית לַמֶּלֶךְ רְחַבְעָם עָלָה שִׁישַׁק מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְגוֹ׳״. כְּתַב הַלּוּחוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֲשַׁבְּרֵם לְעֵינֵיכֶם״, תָּנָא: לוּחוֹת נִשְׁבְּרוּ וְאוֹתִיּוֹת פּוֹרְחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** expressed in the statement of **Rabbi Alexandri, who said:** There are **three** that **returned to their** points of **origin,** and **these are they: The Jewish people, the money of Egypt, and the writing** on the **Tablets** of the Covenant. **The Jewish people; that which we** just **said,** they returned to Babylonia. **The money of Egypt; as it is written: "And it came to pass in the fifth year of King Rehoboam, that Shishak, king of Egypt, came up against Jerusalem;** and he took away the treasures of the house of the Lord and the treasures of the king's house; he took everything" (I Kings 14:25–26). **The writing on the Tablets** of the Covenant; **as it is written:** "And I took hold of the two tablets, and cast them out of my two hands, **and broke them before your eyes"** (Deuteronomy 9:17). And **it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **The tablets were broken and the letters are flying** and returning to their point of origin.

Rabbi Alexandri adds: *sheloshah chazru le-mata'atan* — three things returned to their origin. *Yisrael* — the Jewish people, who returned to *Bavel*, the land of Abraham. *Kesef Mitzrayim* — the money of Egypt that Israel took at the Exodus: *va-yehi ba-shanah ha-chamishit le-melech Rechavam alah Shishak melech Mitzrayim al Yerushalayim* — in the fifth year of King *Rechavam*, *Shishak* king of Egypt came up to Jerusalem and looted it, taking back the wealth that had left Egypt centuries before. And *ketav ha-luchot* — the script of the tablets: *va-ashabrem le-eineichem* — I broke them before your eyes. The *tanna* teaches: *ha-luchot nishberu ve-ha-otiyot porchot* — the tablets were broken, but the letters flew back to God, returning to their divine source. They were never destroyed; they returned to where they came from.
