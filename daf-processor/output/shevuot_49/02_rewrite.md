# Shevuot 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ta'anat Shema & Gilgul S…

### Ta'anat Shema Principle

We are picking up a piece from the *mishnah* that begins with *ve'elu nishba'in* — "and these take oaths." If one returns to the *mishnah* on 45a, one finds a list of people about whom it is stated: *ve'elu nishba'in shelo bita'anah: hashutafin, veha'arisin, veha'apotropin, veh'ishah ha-noset ve-ha-notenet betoch habayit u-ven habayit* — these take oaths even without an explicit claim: partners, sharecroppers, trustees, the woman who manages the household affairs, and the member of the household. The *mishnah* then adds: *amar lo, mah atah toenani, retzoni shetishava li, chayav* — if one says to another, "What is your claim against me? I simply want you to take an oath," he is obligated to do so.

Upon first reading, this is striking. We come from a tradition that understands words to carry the weight of creation itself, and that *shvuot* are not taken lightly — as the entirety of this *masechet* makes abundantly clear. How can one simply demand that another person swear an oath? The *gemara* on 48b raises this very question: *atu beshuftani askinan?* — are we dealing with fools who swear for nothing? Rather, the *gemara* explains, *hachi ka'amar*: *ve'elu nishba'in shelo bita'anat bari, ela bita'anat shema* — these people take oaths not on the basis of a definitive claim, but on the basis of a *ta'anat shema*, a claim of doubt.

What is the underlying situation? A person has an ongoing business relationship with another, and while there is no clear proof that anything is amiss — no *bari*, no explicit evidence — there is a nagging sense that something in the business is lagging or missing. The owner cannot point to a specific wrong, but the doubt is real. It is precisely in this context that he may demand an oath from those individuals who are so regularly in contact with his property that they occupy a position of potential temptation. The *gemara* explains this with the phrase *mishum d'moru bah hateira* — because these people tend to give themselves a kind of implicit *heter*, a permission to compensate themselves. A partner might say: "My contribution to this enterprise far exceeds yours; I am simply taking what is rightfully mine." The person hasn't yet actually stolen anything, but the mental path toward rationalizing a wrong is open — and it is because of that possibility that the owner may demand a *shvua* to resolve the uncertainty.

### Partners & Threshold

*Amar Rav Yosef bar Minyumi amar Rav Nachman: vehu sheyesh ta'anah beneihem shtei kesef* — this rule applies only when there is a claim between them of at least *shtei kesef*, two silver coins. This threshold was the subject of a dispute between Rav and Shmuel that was already addressed back on 39a. The question is where precisely the line falls. Rav holds that the relevant threshold involves *shtei kesef* plus a *pruta* — that is, above two silver coins. Shmuel holds that the *kfira*, the denial of a claim, can be as little as one *pruta*, with *shtei kesef* being the amount of the claim itself. Put differently: is the threshold line drawn on the inside of the *shtei kesef* or on the outside? Both opinions agree that some minimum quantum of disputed value must exist before an oath can be demanded — the debate concerns precisely where that minimum begins.

The *gemara* then notes: *chalku hashutafim veha'arisin* — once the partners or sharecroppers have divided their shared property and each has taken his portion, that relationship is closed, and neither can demand an oath from the other on account of that prior partnership.

### Gilgul Shvua

This leads the *gemara* to raise the question of *gilgul shvua* — the extension, or rolling, of an oath from one situation into another. If one party already has a legitimate basis to impose an oath upon the other for one matter, may that oath be extended to cover an additional, separate claim as well? We know this principle applies in a *deoraita* context. The question raised here is whether one may be *megalgel shvua* in a *derabbanan* context as well.

### Partnership & Loan Case

To address this, the *gemara* offers a proof: *ta shema* — come and hear. *Lavah heymenu erev shvi'it, ul'motzaei shvi'it naaseh lo shutaf* — if person A borrowed money from person B right before the Sabbatical year, and at the conclusion of *shmitta* they became partners or sharecroppers — in that case, *ein megalgelin*, one cannot extend the oath. The reason: *de'atya shvi'it afkateh* — the *shmitta* year arrived and cancelled the loan. Since the underlying obligation that would have grounded the original *shvua* has been annulled by *shmitta*, there is no valid oath to extend.

One might then infer that in the rest of the years of the seven-year cycle, *megalgelin* — such extension is permitted. But the *gemara* cautions against reading the case this way and instead reverses the scenario. The operative case in which extension *is* permitted is the reverse: *naaseh lo shutaf o aris erev shvi'it, ul'motzaei shvi'it lavah heymenu megalgelin* — if they first became partners or sharecroppers before the *shviit*, and only at the conclusion of the Sabbatical year was there a loan, then the borrower incurs an oath obligation by Torah law, and that *shvua* may be extended. The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina megalgelin bederabbanan, shema mina* — we may infer from this that *gilgul* applies even in a rabbinic context.

### Rav Huna vs. Chisda

Having established the principle, the *gemara* now records a dispute between *amora'im* about the scope of *gilgul*. *Amar Rav Huna: lakol megalgelin, chutz misachir she'ein megalgelin* — we may extend an oath onto any matter, with one exception: the *sachir*, the hired worker, for whom we do not extend. *Rav Chisda amar: lakol ein megalgelin, chutz misachir demegalgelin* — for all situations we do not extend, except for the hired worker, for whom we do. *Mai beinaihu? Ika beinaihu liftu'ach lo* — the practical difference between them concerns the concept of *liftu'ach lo*, the question of how the court may open an additional oath in a given situation.

Some background is essential here. The *sachir* occupies a privileged position in *halachah* because the asymmetry between employer and worker is recognized: a *ba'al habayit* may employ hundreds of workers in the field and may simply not recall whether a particular individual received payment on a given Friday. The worker, by contrast, is unlikely to forget whether he was paid. One of the foundational assumptions running through this entire *masechet* — and one that deserves extended reflection — is that people are more afraid of taking a false oath than they are of committing a financial wrong. That assumption undergirds the entire institution of the *shvua* as a legal mechanism: we believe that imposing an oath upon a person who has wronged another will function as an effective instrument of truth precisely because the wrongdoer fears the oath more than the underlying transgression. It is on the basis of this reasoning that we extend credibility to the *sachir* who claims he was not paid.

The practical difference between Rav Huna and Rav Chisda concerns the procedural question of *liftu'ach lo* — which party may open the additional oath in court, and in which direction. Rav Huna holds that extension is generally permitted except in the case of the *sachir*, while Rav Chisda holds that extension is generally not permitted but is specifically available for the *sachir* — with the practical difference emerging in how the court constructs the oath procedure in that particular case.

### Shmitta Meshemetet

The final section of this chapter addresses a related question: *vehashvi'it mashmetet* — does the Sabbatical year cancel oaths just as it cancels monetary debts? *Mena hanei milei?* — from where do we derive this? *Amar Rav Gidal amar Rav: de'amar kra* — Rav Gidal citing Rav anchors the ruling in a *pasuk*. The verse in Deuteronomy 15:2 reads *veze d'var hashmitah* — "and this is the matter of the *shmitta*." The word *davar* is seemingly superfluous; the verse could simply have said *veze hashmita*. From the inclusion of *davar* — which also means speech or utterance — we derive that *dibbur mashmet*, even a verbal commitment, a *shvua*, is annulled by the *shmitta* year. With that, the *gemara* concludes: *hadran alach kol hanishba'in*.

## Four Guards Overview

### Four Categories

The final chapter of *Masechet Shevuot* — the eighth and last — contains a lengthy *mishnah* structured almost like a poem, followed by *gemara* to unpack it. The *mishnah* opens: *arba'ah shomrim hen* — there are four categories of guardians. This typology was already treated extensively in *Nezikin*, and it appears here again in the context of *shevuot*. The four are: *shomer chinam*, *hasho'el*, *nose sachar*, and *hasocher* — the unpaid guard, the borrower, the paid guard, and the renter.

The *shomer chinam* receives no compensation — he is simply doing a favor. The *sho'el* borrows an item. The *nose sachar* is actively paid to guard. The *socher* rents the object in exchange for money. Each carries a different level of liability, and the *mishnah* will explore what happens when each takes an oath about the fate of the deposited object.

The *mishnah* then lays out the general framework: *shomer chinam nishba al hakol* — the unpaid guard may take an oath regarding any outcome and is exempt from payment. *Vehasho'el meshalem et hakol* — the borrower must pay for everything. *Nose sachar vehasocher nishba'im al hashevurah ve'al hashevuyah ve'al hametah* — the paid guard and the renter take oaths regarding wounding, captivity, and death — *u'meshalmim et ha'avedah ve'et haganeyvah* — and they pay for loss and theft.

The logic maps onto five categories of mishap: *met, nishbar, nishba, avad, nignav* — death, wounding, captivity, loss, and theft. The first three — death, wounding, captivity — are considered *ones*, events beyond the guardian's control. The last two — loss and theft — imply a failure of watchfulness, a breach of the very duty for which the guardian was engaged.

## Shomer Chinam Cases (II)

### Death Claim (Multiple)

The *mishnah* now delivers its "poetry" — a rhythmic, repetitive structure that cycles through every possible combination of claim and reality. The owner asks: *heichan shori?* — where is my ox? The *shomer chinam* answers: *amar lo met* — it died. Then the *mishnah* recounts: *vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav o avad* — and in reality it died, or it was wounded, or it was captured, or it was stolen, or it was lost. The same structure is repeated for each possible claim: *nishbar vehu shemet o nishbar o nignav o avad*; *nishbah vehu shemet o nishbar o nignav o avad*; *nignav vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o avad*; *avad vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav*. In every configuration: *mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — patur*.

The *shomer chinam* is exempt regardless. Whatever he says happened, and whatever actually happened, he bears no financial liability for any of these outcomes — because his was an unpaid guardianship and he is not obligated to pay for any of these contingencies. One might object: even if the financial outcome is the same, he has still sworn falsely — he claimed the animal died when it was actually stolen. The *gemara* will address this tension directly.

## Four Guards Overview (II)

### Monetary Outcome Rule

Before returning to the full poetic sequence, the *mishnah* states its organizing principle. A brief but critical rule shapes all that follows: *zeh haklal* — this is the overarching principle: *kol hamishtaneh mechova lechova, umipatur lepatur, umipatur lechova — patur; mechova lepatur — chayav*.

If one moves from one obligation to another obligation, from one exemption to another exemption, or from an exemption to an obligation — he is *patur*, exempt from the consequences of a false oath. But if one moves from an obligation to an exemption — that is, if he takes an oath designed to *lighten* his financial burden — he is *chayav*, liable. The principle, simply stated: if the oath does not change the monetary outcome, there are no consequences; if it does change the outcome — specifically, if the swearer is trying to walk away from a liability he actually carries — then the false oath carries halachic weight.

The question immediately arises: even if the monetary outcome is identical, the person has still sworn falsely before God. Which *shvua* is being invoked when the *mishnah* says he is *patur*? Are we speaking of the *shevuat shomrim* — the guardian's oath — or of a *shevuat bitui* — a false expression? Is he truly exempt from both, or only from one? The *gemara* will address this at length.

## Shomer Chinam Cases (II)

### Ignorance Claim

The second case: *heichan shori? Amar lo, eini yodea mah atah sach* — where is my ox? The *shomer chinam* replies: I have no idea what you are talking about. *Vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav o avad — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — patur*. Whatever actually happened to the animal, the *shomer chinam* who says "I don't know what you're talking about" is still *patur* — because he remains a *shomer chinam*, bearing no financial liability for any of these outcomes.

### False Theft Claim

The third case: *heichan shori? Amar lo, avad* — where is my ox? It was lost. He takes the oath. *Ve'edim meidim oto she'achalo* — and witnesses testify that he himself ate the ox. Now we have left the realm of competing accounts and entered the realm of *mezid*, of willful wrongdoing — the *shomer* himself consumed the deposited animal. *Meshalem et hakeren* — he must pay the value of the ox.

But here the *mishnah* introduces a remarkable distinction: *hodeh me'atzmo — meshalem keren ve'chomesh ve'asham* — if he admits on his own, he pays the principal, plus a fifth, and brings a *korban asham*. One might wonder: why does the admission of guilt result in a *greater* financial obligation — the addition of *chomesh* and *asham* — while being exposed by witnesses results in only the principal? The answer is that when a person genuinely admits wrongdoing, he opens a pathway to *teshuva*, and the *halacha* provides him the mechanism to complete that process: the *chomesh* and the *asham*. When witnesses expose him and he does not come forward on his own, the financial obligation is still there, but the *teshuva* process remains unresolved and incomplete.

### Public Accusation

The fourth case: *amar lo, nignav* — the *shomer chinam* claims the animal was stolen, takes the oath, *ve'edim meidim oto sheg'navo* — and witnesses testify that he himself stole it. The *shomer* was, under the guise of guardianship, himself the thief. *Meshalem tashlumei kefel* — he pays double. *Tashlumei kefel* is a *knas*, a penalty specifically for the thief who denies his theft under oath while holding the stolen object. Again, *hodeh me'atzmo — meshalem keren ve'chomesh ve'asham* — if he confesses on his own, he pays principal, a fifth, and brings the *asham* offering.

### Partial Admission

The fifth case involves not the *shomer* but the accused thief directly: *amar l'echad bashuk, heichan shori she'ganavta?* — the owner confronts someone in the marketplace and says, where is my ox that you stole? The accused denies it; witnesses testify that he did steal. *Meshalem tashlumei kefel*. If he then *tavach umachar* — slaughtered it and sold it — *meshalem tashlumei arba'ah ve'chamishah* — he pays four or five times the value, the penalty for a thief who not only took the animal but appropriated it entirely, making commercial use of it. However: *ra'ah edim she'mimashmeshim u'va'im, amar ganavti aval lo tavachti ve'lo macharti* — if he sees witnesses approaching and says, "I did steal it, but I did not slaughter it and I did not sell it," *eino meshalem ela keren* — he pays only the principal, since his partial admission removes the basis for the higher penalties.

## Sho'el Cases

### Death/Loss Claims

The *mishnah* now turns to the *sho'el*, the borrower. *Amar l'sho'el, heichan shori?* — where is my ox? The *mishnah* again cycles through the full rhythm: *amar lo met, vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav o avad; nishbar vehu shemet o nishbar o nignav o avad; nignav vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o avad; avad vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — patur*. In every combination of claim and reality, the *sho'el* is *patur* — exempt from the consequences of a false oath.

Why? Because the *sho'el* is liable for *everything* regardless. He claimed the animal died when it was actually stolen — but he would have to pay in either case. Since his financial obligation is constant no matter what account he gives, the discrepancy between his claim and reality has no monetary consequence. There is therefore no *shvua* that functions to his advantage and no *shevuat shomrim* that carries penalties.

### Ignorance Admission

The second case for the *sho'el*: *heichan shori? Amar lo, eini yodea mah atah sach* — I don't know what you are talking about. *Vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah o nignav o ne'evad — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — chayav*. Here the *sho'el* is *chayav* — liable. The distinction is subtle but crucial. When the *sho'el* gives a particular account — "it died," "it was stolen" — he is offering a specific story. Perhaps the timing of his account is inconvenient; perhaps the animal died at midnight under circumstances he would rather not explain. He tells one story when the truth was slightly different, but the financial obligation remains unchanged either way. That is harmless in terms of the oath's legal weight. But when he says *eini yodea mah atah sach* — I have no idea what you're talking about — he is behaving as if he never had the animal at all. This is a qualitatively different kind of denial, and it is this that renders him *chayav*.

## Paid Guard/Renter Cases

### Liability Categories

The *mishnah* now addresses the *nose sachar* and the *socher* together. *Amar lenosei sachar vehasocher, heichan shori?* The pattern repeats: *amar lo met, vehu shenishbar o nishbah* — he says it died, but in reality it was wounded or captured; *nishbar, vehu shemet o nishbar*; *nignav, vehu avad*; *avad, vehu shenignav — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — patur*.

To understand the *mishnah*'s ruling, one must recall the fundamental distinction in their liability structure. The *nose sachar* and *socher* are exempt from liability for death, wounding, and captivity — *ones*, events beyond their control — but they are *obligated* to pay for loss and theft, since those are precisely the contingencies their payment is meant to guard against. The *mishnah* accordingly divides their world into two categories: on one side, *met*, *nishbar*, and *nishbah* — death, wounding, captivity — for which they take an oath but do not pay; on the other side, *avad* and *nignav* — loss and theft — for which they pay without oath.

### Category Crossing

The principle governing when the *nose sachar* or *socher* is *patur* and when *chayav* is therefore one of category: as long as the false claim stays within the same liability category, there is no monetary consequence and hence no oath penalty. *Patur*. But if the false claim crosses from one category to the other — if the *shomer* says the animal died when it was actually stolen, thereby claiming exemption from an obligation he actually carries — that is when the oath produces a change in financial outcome.

*Met o nishbar o nishbah, vehu shenignav o avad — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — chayav*. If he says death or wounding or captivity but the reality is loss or theft, he is crossing from the exempt category to the obligated category, and so he is *chayav*. *Avad o nignav, vehu shemet o nishbar o nishbah — mashbi'acha ani ve'amar amen — patur*. If he goes the other direction — saying it was stolen when it actually died — he is claiming responsibility for something he is not actually obligated for. The *mishnah* says: he is *patur*, and the *gemara* will explain that we do not allow him to add an obligation upon himself either.

---

## The Klal Rule

### Klal Statement

The *mishnah* now articulates the governing principle behind all the preceding cases: *zeh haklal — kol hamishtaneh mechova lechova, umipatur lepatur, umipatur lechova — patur; mechova lepatur — chayav*. This is the overarching rule: one who changes from one obligation to another obligation, from one exemption to another exemption, or from exemption to obligation — is *patur*. Only one who moves from obligation to exemption — who takes a false oath in order to relieve himself of a financial burden he genuinely carries — is *chayav*.

### Lighten vs. Strengthen

The *mishnah* then restates this principle in a second formulation: *kol hanishba lehakel al atzmo — chayav; lehachmir al atzmo — patur*. One who swears in order to lighten his own burden is liable; one who swears to add to his own burden is exempt. The *halacha* does not allow a person to use an oath to evade a legitimate obligation. But it equally does not require the law to treat a self-imposed additional burden as legally operative — the oath that purports to strengthen one's liability beyond what the law demands is not recognized as consequential in this context.

## Attribution & Disputes

### Rabbi Meir Attribution

The *gemara* opens with a question: *man tana arba'ah shomrim?* — who is the *tana* responsible for this doctrine of four guardians? *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha: Rabbi Meir hi* — this is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rava immediately challenges Rav Nachman: *mi ika tana deleit lei arba'ah shomrim?* — is there any *tana* who does not hold by four guardians? This typology is fundamental and was established throughout *Nezikin* — what is there to attribute? Rav Nachman clarifies: the real question is not whether there are four guardians, but rather *man tana de'amar socher kenosei sachar dami* — who is the *tana* that holds the *socher*, the renter, has the same halachic status as the *nose sachar*, the paid guard? Our *mishnah* effectively groups them together. *Amar Rabbah bar Avuha: Rabbi Meir hi* — this is Rabbi Meir's position.

But the *gemara* immediately complicates this: *ve-ha Rabbi Meir ipcha sham'inan lei* — but we have heard Rabbi Meir say the opposite! In another *mishnah* (*detnan: socher keitzad meshalem — Rabbi Meir omer keshomer chinam, Rabbi Yehuda omer kenosei sachar*), Rabbi Meir holds that the *socher* is like the *shomer chinam*, while Rabbi Yehuda holds he is like the *nose sachar*. So which *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Meir's true position? The answer: *Rabbah bar Avuha ipcha tani* — Rabbah bar Avuha's version of the *girsa* is the inverse, and under that reading, our *mishnah* — which groups *socher* with *nose sachar* — is indeed consistent with Rabbi Meir.

A further question arises: *hanei arba'ah havu, tlata havu* — if the *socher* and the *nose sachar* share the same liability rules, are there really four guardians here, or effectively only three? *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak amar: arba'ah shomrim vedineihen sheloshah* — there are indeed four guardians as distinct categories of persons, but for the purposes of the laws of *shevuot* as presented in this *masechet*, there are only three distinct legal rulings.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The *gemara* now returns to the question that was implicitly raised during the reading of the *mishnah* itself. *Amar Rav: bekulan peturin mishevuat shomrim ve'chayavin mishum shevuat bitui* — Rav says that all these guardians are exempt from the consequences of the *shevuat shomrim*, the guardian's oath, but they are nonetheless liable for bringing a sin offering on account of *shevuat bitui*, for having made a false verbal declaration. *Ve'Shmuel omer: af peturin mishum shevuat bitui* — Shmuel says they are even exempt from the consequences of *shevuat bitui*, because there was no change in monetary outcome.

### Past vs. Future Oaths

*Bemai kamiflagi?* — on what exactly do Rav and Shmuel disagree? *Shmuel savar leita bilehara uleheitiv; ve-Rav savar ita belav vehen*. Shmuel holds that the category of *shevuat bitui* — the oath of expression — applies only to future undertakings, things one commits to do or refrain from doing. The verse in *Vayikra* speaks of *lehara o leheitiv* — doing harm or doing good — which implies future action. An oath about what happened to an ox in the past — whether it died, was stolen, or was lost — is not the kind of oath *shevuat bitui* is designed to govern. Rav, however, holds that *shevuat bitui* applies equally to both negative and positive statements, including declarations about the past — *belav vehen*, yes or no, I ate or I didn't eat, the animal died or it didn't die. Under Rav's view, a false declaration about past events is still a *shevuat bitui* and still incurs liability.

### Stone/Sea Case

The *gemara* notes that this exact dispute between Rav and Shmuel has already been recorded once before, on 25a, in the case of *shvua she'zarak ploni tsror la-yam* — one who swears "so-and-so threw a stone into the sea," or swears that he did not. Rav holds that if this statement is false, the swearer is liable — since this is a statement that can be made in either the positive or negative form, *belav vehen*. Shmuel holds he is exempt — since this is a statement that involves no future undertaking, it falls outside the category of *shevuat bitui*. The *gemara* explains why it is nevertheless necessary to record the dispute in both contexts — the case of the stone thrown into the sea and the case of the guardians — despite their apparent identity. Each case adds something the other does not. In the case of the stone, one might have thought Rav holds the swearer liable specifically because the person swore entirely on his own initiative, *menafshei*, of his own accord. One might then have said: but in a court-administered oath, Rav would concede to Shmuel. Conversely, one might have supposed that Shmuel's exemption applies specifically to court-administered oaths, while in the case of a self-initiated oath he concedes to Rav. Since each case might be distinguished, both must be recorded.

### Court vs. Self Oaths

This brings the *gemara* to a principle of Rav Ami: *kol shevuah she'hadayanim mashbi'in otah, ein chayavin aleha mishum shevuat bitui* — any oath that is administered by the court judges does not carry liability under the category of *shevuat bitui*. This is a striking ruling deserving extended examination. The basis for it is derived from the verse in *Vayikra* chapter 5, *o nefesh ki tishava levate bisefatayim* — "or if a soul swears, expressing with its lips." The word *ki* in this verse, as Reish Lakish explains, functions in one of four possible senses: *ei, dilma, ela, deha* — if, perhaps, rather, or as. Here, the *ki* indicates that the liability for *shevuat bitui* is predicated on the person swearing *me'atzmo*, of his own volition. An oath that the court imposes does not carry the full weight of *shevuat bitui* because it is not the spontaneous expression of the individual's own speech — it is a judicially administered mechanism, and the verse's requirement of personal initiative is not satisfied.

## Rabbi Elazar & Conclusion

### Rabbi Elazar Rule

Rabbi Elazar states a position that synthesizes much of what has been discussed: *kulam peturim mishevuat shomrim ve'chayavim mishum shevuat bitui* — all four categories of guardians are exempt from the *shevuat shomrim* but are liable under *shevuat bitui*. This is consistent with Rav's position, and it applies across all the cases surveyed in the *mishnah*.

### Theft/Loss Exception

There is, however, an exception: *chutz misachir she-lo yodea mah atah sach shel hashoel* — except for the *sho'el* who says "I don't know what you are talking about." That case, as established earlier, renders the *sho'el chayav* in a different way. Additionally, Rabbi Elazar addresses the specific situations of *gneivah ve'aveidah* — theft and loss — for the *nose sachar* and the *socher*. In those cases, *shehu chayav, sheharei kaparah mamon* — the guardian is liable, because his *kaparah*, his atonement, comes through monetary payment. Since the *nose sachar* and *socher* bear financial responsibility for theft and loss, that monetary obligation cannot simply be sidestepped by the mechanics of oath exemption; the obligation to settle accounts monetarily stands, and the swearer cannot walk away.

### Completion

*Hadran alach arba shomrim, ve'silka lah Masechet Shevuot.* We have reached the end of *Masechet Shevuot* — a *masechet* that began with the hidden awareness of *tum'ah* in the sanctuary, moved through the mysteries of the oaths of expression, the oaths of vain utterance, and the oath of testimony, and concludes here with the intricate legal choreography of the four guardians and the conditions under which a false declaration before God carries halachic consequence. If we leave with more questions than we had when we began, that is precisely as it should be. *Hadran alach*.
