# Zevachim 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inner Altar Sanctifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי מְקַדֵּשׁ פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: The inner altar,** i.e., the golden altar inside the Sanctuary, **sanctifies disqualified** offerings such that if they ascended onto it, they do not descend.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

The sugya opens with a statement attributed to Rabbi Eliezer near the beginning of 27b, just before the lines widen: *mizbeach hapnimi mekadesh psulim* — the inner altar sanctifies invalid offerings. This principle emerges in the context of a broader discussion about what happens when the *dam* of a sacrifice is applied to the wrong part or the wrong altar. As established in the preceding analysis, the question is whether misplaced blood accomplishes anything at all, or whether it accomplishes just enough to permit the limbs that have already been placed on the altar to remain there.

The specific application here is striking. If a *kohen* took the blood designated for the outer altar and instead brought it to the inner altar — an act that itself *invalidates* the blood — can the limbs placed on the outer altar subsequently remain? Rabbi Eliezer holds that yes, even this invalid application to the inner altar is sufficient to allow the limbs to remain. Crucially, this does not mean the *korban* has been validly offered, that anyone has fulfilled their obligation, or that the meat is permitted for consumption. It means only that the blood has reached *some* altar, and that threshold suffices to prevent the limbs from being removed.

### Blood Location Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: הַנִּיתָּנִין בְּפָנִים כּוּ׳! אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי דָּם – דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ; אֲבָל קוֹמֶץ, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this statement **teaching us? We** already **learn** this in the above *baraita*: If one placed the blood **that is** to be **placed inside** the Sanctuary outside on the external altar, or if one placed the blood that is to be placed outside the Sanctuary inside it, on the golden altar, the offering does not descend. The Gemara responds: **If** one were to learn the *halakha* only **from there, I would say: This matter** applies only to **blood** mistakenly placed on the golden altar, **as it is fit to** be placed on that altar in certain contexts, i.e., the blood of the bull and goat sin offerings on Yom Kippur; **but** with regard to **a handful** from a meal offering, **which is not fit to** be placed on the golden altar in any context, **I will say** that it is **not** sanctified when placed on it. Rabbi Eliezer therefore **teaches us** that even a handful from a meal offering does not descend from it.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *mai kamashma lan tanina* — what novelty is Rabbi Eliezer introducing? This same point appears to be covered by the earlier *baraisa* attributed to Rabbi Shimon, which lists scenarios including inner blood applied to the outer altar and outer blood applied to the inner altar, in all of which the limbs may remain. The Gemara assumes Rabbi Yehuda concurs with these scenarios, since his dissent in that *baraisa* is limited specifically to the case where the blood was spilled entirely — only there does he require the limbs to be removed. So what *chiddush* does Rabbi Eliezer add?

The Gemara answers: *ei mehatam havah amina hani mili dam* — had we learned only from that earlier source, we might have restricted the principle to blood. Blood, after all, sometimes belongs on the inner altar, as on Yom Kippur. One could argue that blood placed on the inner altar retains some residual legitimacy because blood has a legitimate relationship with that surface. But what about a *komets* — the handful of a *mincha* offering — placed on the inner altar? A *komets* has no business there whatsoever. The verse states explicitly: *lo ta'alu alav ketoret zarah ve'olah u'mincha* — do not offer upon it foreign incense, an *olah*, or a *mincha*. Given this explicit prohibition, one might have thought that placing a *komets* on the inner altar accomplishes nothing, leaving it subject to removal. Therefore, *ka mashma lan*: even the *komets* on the inner altar is sanctified to the degree that it may remain and continue to burn — and this despite the fact that leaving it there appears to continue the violation of the *lo ta'aleh alav* prohibition. The *chiddush* is that *lechatchila lo ta'aleh alav* — one may not do this in the first place — but *bedi'eved*, once it is there, it may remain.

### Ketoret Zarah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: קְטֹרֶת זָרָה (שעלה) [שֶׁעָלְתָה] לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – תֵּרֵד; שֶׁאֵין לְךָ מְקַדֵּשׁ פְּסוּלִין אֶלָּא מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן בְּרָאוּי לוֹ. חִיצוֹן אִין, פְּנִימִי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **strange incense,** i.e., incense that it is prohibited to burn on the golden altar, **ascended onto the altar, it shall descend, as only the external altar sanctifies disqualified** offerings that are **suited for it.** One can infer that the **external** altar **does** sanctify disqualified offerings, but the **inner** altar does **not.**

The Gemara now raises a challenge from a *baraisa*: *ketoret zarah she'alah legabei mizbeach tered* — foreign incense that was placed on the altar must be brought down. This is precisely analogous to the case of Nadav and Avihu, who brought incense they were not licensed to offer. If the *ketoret zarah* must be removed, why should the *komets* of a *mincha* be permitted to remain? Both appear in the same verse of prohibition.

The immediate reading of this *baraisa* seems to say: only the outer altar sanctifies invalid items; the inner altar does not. Perhaps the inner altar works for blood — since blood sometimes legitimately belongs there — but not for *mincha* or *ketoret*. This reading would directly contradict the position that the inner altar sanctifies even things not fit for it.

### Outer Altar Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּרֵיץ הָכִי: קְטֹרֶת זָרָה שֶׁעָלְתָה לְמִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן – תֵּרֵד; שֶׁאֵין מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן מְקַדֵּשׁ פְּסוּלִין אֶלָּא הָרָאוּי לוֹ, וְהַפְּנִימִי בֵּין רָאוּי לוֹ בֵּין שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי רִצְפָּה, וְהַאי כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Answer like this:** The *baraita* means that if **strange incense ascended onto the external altar, it shall descend, as the external altar sanctifies only disqualified** offerings **that are suited for it. But the inner** altar sanctifies everything, **whether it is suited for it or it is not suited for it. What is the reason** for this? **This,** the external altar, is considered part of the **floor,** since it is fixed to the floor of the Temple, **and that,** the inner altar, is considered **a service vessel** with a higher level of sanctity.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by rereading the *baraisa*. The correct reading is: *ketoret zarah she'alah lemizbeach hachitzon tered* — it is *ketoret zarah* placed on the *outer* altar that must be removed. Why? *She'ein mizbeach hachitzon mekadesh pesulim ela hara'ui lo* — the outer altar sanctifies only those things that are fit for it. *Ketoret* is not fit for the outer altar; therefore it does not become sanctified there and must be taken down.

This reading, far from limiting the inner altar, actually leaves it untouched by the *baraisa* entirely. The *baraisa* says nothing about the inner altar's inability to sanctify *ketoret* or *mincha*; it says only that the outer altar cannot sanctify *ketoret zarah*.

### Inner vs Outer Power

The conclusion is the inverse of the initial impression: *ve'hapenimi bein ra'ui lo bein she'ein ra'ui lo* — the inner altar sanctifies both things that are fit for it and things that are not fit for it. The inner altar is actually *more* powerful than the outer one, not less.

What accounts for this? *Hai ritzpa v'hai kli shares* — the outer altar has the status of the floor of the *Beis HaMikdash*, something fixed and immovable, while the inner altar carries the additional status of a *kli shares*, a sacred vessel. While one might intuitively assume the permanent structure of the *Mikdash* would be more sanctified than its vessels, the Gemara holds otherwise: the *kli shares* status of the inner altar confers a higher degree of sanctity. As a result, even explicit violations of *lo ta'aleh alav ketoret zarah ve'olah u'mincha* — once accomplished — do not require removal. The inner altar's sanctity is so great that it absorbs even these misplaced items. This is a remarkable *chiddush*: the inner altar is not weaker than the outer one with respect to sanctifying invalid items; it is stronger, and its power extends even to things that flagrantly do not belong upon it.

## Piggul & Chutz

### Machsheves Chutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לִזְרוֹק דָּמוֹ בַּחוּץ אוֹ מִקְצָת דָּמוֹ בַּחוּץ, לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרָיו בַּחוּץ אוֹ מִקְצָת אֵימוּרָיו בַּחוּץ, לֶאֱכוֹל בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּחוּץ אוֹ כְּזַיִת מִבְּשָׂרוֹ בַּחוּץ, אוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מֵעוֹר הָאַלְיָה בַּחוּץ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who slaughters the offering** with intent **to sprinkle its blood outside** the Temple **or** to sprinkle **part of its blood outside** the Temple, **to burn its sacrificial portions outside** the Temple **or** to burn **part of its sacrificial portions outside** the Temple, **to partake of its meat outside** the Temple **or** to partake of **an olive-bulk of its meat outside** the Temple, **or to partake of an olive-bulk of the skin of the tail outside** the Temple, in all of these cases the offering is **disqualified, and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of it.

Having concluded the extended digression into the laws of blood placement and altar sanctity, the Gemara now arrives at the *mishna* that introduces the thought-based invalidities of *piggul* — a subject the *masechet* has been circling for many pages and will continue to develop throughout. The *mishna* opens: *hashochet et hazevach lizrok damo bachutz* — if one slaughters a sacrifice with the intent to throw its blood outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Like the invalidity of *shelo lishmo*, the thought invalidates beginning already at *shechita*. The *mishna* then enumerates parallel cases: even if one intends to throw only part of the blood outside; to burn the *imurim* outside; to burn only part of them; to eat the meat outside; to eat even a single *kezayis* of the meat outside; or even to eat a *kezayis* of the skin of the fat tail outside. In all these cases, the *mishna* rules *pasul ve'ein bo karet* — the sacrifice is invalid but carries no *karet* penalty for one who subsequently eats from it.

### Chutz Limkomo

This category — intending to perform an act in the wrong *place* — is *chutz limkomo*. It is the counterpart to true *piggul* but distinct from it: the invalidity arises not from a thought about the wrong *time* but about the wrong *location*. Because it is not genuine *piggul*, it does not carry *karet*.

Before proceeding to the stronger form of invalidity, it is important to appreciate what these thoughts have in common. The *mishna* is careful to list only thoughts whose content relates to acts of eating: either the eating of the meat by a person, or the "eating" of the altar, meaning the *dam* placed upon it and the fats and limbs burned upon it. A thought about the wrong time or place only invalidates when it concerns one of these four *avodos* — *shechita*, *kabala*, *holacha*, *zerика* — and when its content pertains to an act of eating. This principle will prove critical to the debates about the fat tail skin that follow.

### Piggul Proper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִזְרוֹק דָּמוֹ לְמָחָר, מִקְצָת דָּמוֹ לְמָחָר, לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרָיו לְמָחָר אוֹ מִקְצָת אֵימוּרָיו לְמָחָר, לֶאֱכוֹל בְּשָׂרוֹ לְמָחָר אוֹ כְּזַיִת מִבְּשָׂרוֹ לְמָחָר, אוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מֵעוֹר הָאַלְיָה לְמָחָר – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** But if one had intent **to sprinkle its blood the next day** or **part of its blood the next day, to burn its sacrificial portions the next day or** to burn **part of its sacrificial portions the next day, to partake of its meat the next day or** to partake of **an olive-bulk of its meat the next day, or to partake of an olive-bulk of the skin of the tail the next day,** the offering is ***piggul*, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.**

The second half of the *mishna* presents the stronger category. If one slaughtered with intent to throw the blood *tomorrow* (*lemachar*), or some of the blood tomorrow, or to burn the *imurim* tomorrow, or some of them, or to eat the meat tomorrow, or a *kezayis* of the meat tomorrow, or even a *kezayis* of the skin of the fat tail tomorrow — in all these cases the ruling is *piggul ve'chayavim alav karet*. This is true *piggul*, the wrong-time invalidity, and carries the full *karet* penalty for one who eats the meat afterward.

It is worth noting one nuance regarding the word *lemachar*: in the case of a *shlamim*, which is permitted for eating over two days, the wrong-time thought would need to contemplate eating on the *third* day, not merely the next day. The very paradigm case of *piggul* in the Torah involves a *shlamim*, precisely because its extended permitted eating period makes "the third day" the relevant threshold.

## Fat Tail Skin Debate

### Samuel's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ: עוֹר אַלְיָה – כְּאַלְיָה דָּמֵי; וְהָא קָא מְחַשֵּׁב מֵאֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ לְאָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The students **assumed** that **the skin of the tail** **is considered as** though it were part of the **tail** itself. The tail of a sheep sacrificed as a peace offering is burned on the altar rather than eaten. **But** if so, one who slaughters the sheep with intent to consume the skin of its tail the next day **has intent** to shift its consumption **from consumption by** the **altar,** i.e., burning the offering, **to** consumption by **a person.** Since intent to consume part of an offering beyond its designated time renders an offering *piggul* only if that part is intended for human consumption, why does the mishna rule that such an offering is *piggul*?

The Gemara's immediate focus is the repeated appearance of *or ha'alya* — the skin of the fat tail — in both halves of the *mishna*. *Savruha or alya k'alya dami* — the Gemara initially assumes that the skin of the fat tail is like the fat tail itself, meaning it is categorized as something burned on the altar rather than something eaten by a person. On this reading, the skin goes onto the *mizbeach* along with the fat.

### Challenge to Samuel

This assumption immediately generates a difficulty. If the skin of the fat tail is like the fat tail — an item that belongs to the altar's "eating" — then why does thinking about eating it at the wrong time or wrong place invalidate the sacrifice? Doing so would constitute *kamachashev me'achilat mizbeach l'achilat adam* — taking something belonging to the altar's consumption and redirecting the thought toward human consumption. Generally, such a cross-category thought is not a valid *piggul* thought. The thought must concern eating what is meant to be eaten by a person (at the wrong time/place) or burning what is meant to be burned (at the wrong time/place). Transplanting an item across these categories should not invalidate.

### Rav Huna's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: עוֹר אַלְיָה – לָאו כְּאַלְיָה דָּמֵי. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא? ״חֶלְבּוֹ הָאַלְיָה״ – וְלֹא עוֹר הָאַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says: The skin of** the **tail is not considered as** though it were the **tail** itself. Unlike the tail itself, its skin is consumed. Consequently, both this mishna and the next can be understood in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **Rava said: What is the reasoning of Rav Huna?** As the verse states: "And he shall present of the sacrifice of peace offerings an offering made by fire unto the Lord: **The fat thereof, the fat tail"** (Leviticus 3:9), indicating that the priest must offer the fat of the tail on the altar, **but not the skin of the tail.**

The Gemara answers: *amar Shmuel ha meni Rabbi Eliezer hi* — Shmuel explains that the *mishna* follows the minority opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *machashev me'achilat mizbeach l'achilat adam u'me'achilat adam l'achilat mizbeach* — a cross-category thought *does* invalidate in both directions. A thought to burn something that should be eaten, or to eat something that should be burned, is still a *piggul*-type thought according to Rabbi Eliezer.

The source for Rabbi Eliezer's position is a *mishna* cited by the Gemara: if one slaughtered intending *le'echol davar she'ein darko le'echol* — to eat something not normally eaten (such as the blood or forbidden fat) — or *lehaktir davar she'ein darko lehaktir* — to burn something not normally burned — the ruling of the *rabbanan* is that such a sacrifice is *kasher* and the *piggul* invalidity does not apply. But *Rabbi Eliezer posel* — Rabbi Eliezer invalidates even in those cross-category cases.

This resolution, however, creates a striking internal problem within our *mishna* itself. Immediately following the cases of the fat tail skin, the next *mishna* (which the Gemara notes will be read on 29b) states: *zeh haklal* — this is the rule: *kol hashochait... le'echol davar shedarko le'echol ul'haktir davar shedarko l'haktir* — only thoughts about eating what is normally eaten or burning what is normally burned invalidate. This formulation is explicitly the *rabbanan's* position, not Rabbi Eliezer's. So the *reisha* of our *mishna* — which includes the fat tail skin case — would represent Rabbi Eliezer, while the immediately following *mishna* beginning with *zeh haklal* articulates the rabbanan's directly opposing rule. The Gemara acknowledges this tension and provides a weak but accepted resolution: *ein* — yes, the *mishna* is internally divided, the *reisha* following Rabbi Eliezer and the *zeh haklal* reflecting the rabbanan. The fact that the *mishna* presents these sequentially without flagging the discontinuity is noted as unusual, particularly since *zeh haklal* typically functions as a summary of the immediately preceding material.

Rav Huna offers a cleaner solution that avoids attributing the *mishna* to Rabbi Eliezer at all: *or alya lav k'alya dami* — the skin of the fat tail is *not* like the fat tail. The skin is categorically part of the meat that a person is permitted to eat, not part of what goes onto the *mizbeach*. On this reading, when one thinks about eating the skin of the fat tail at the wrong time or place, there is no cross-category problem whatsoever — one is simply thinking about eating human-consumable meat at the wrong time or place, which is a straightforward *piggul* or *chutz limkomo* thought.

The textual basis for Rav Huna's position is the verse *chelbo ha'alya temimah* — which specifies the *fat* of the complete fat tail. The possessive "its fat" indicates that only the *chelev* of the *alya* goes on the altar, *v'lo or ha'alya* — not the skin surrounding it.

### Rav Chisda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם עוֹר הָאַלְיָה כְּאַלְיָה דָּמֵי; וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּאַלְיָה שֶׁל גְּדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Actually, the skin of the tail is considered as** though it were the **tail** itself, and it is burned on the altar. **And here,** in the mishna, **we are dealing with the tail of a kid,** which is not burned on the altar but is consumed. Accordingly, intent to consume it outside its designated time renders it *piggul*.

Rav Chisda takes yet a third approach: *le'olam or ha'alya k'alya dami* — the skin of the fat tail is indeed like the fat tail itself and is burned on the *mizbeach*. Yet he too avoids attributing the *mishna* to Rabbi Eliezer. His solution is: *v'hacha b'mai askinan b'alya shel gdi* — the *mishna* is not discussing a sheep's fat tail at all, but rather the tail of a goat. A goat, unlike a sheep, does not have a fat tail; its tail is simply an ordinary tail whose flesh is consumed. Therefore, when the *mishna* speaks of the skin of the "fat tail," it refers to the skin of the goat's ordinary tail, which is human-consumable meat — and thinking about eating it at the wrong time is a standard *piggul* thought, fully within the rabbanan's framework.

### Rejecting Goat Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כְּרַב חִסְדָּא – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמְרִי? מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – עוֹר אַלְיָה כְּאַלְיָה דְּמֵי?! תְּנֵינָא: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁעוֹרוֹתֵיהֶן כִּבְשָׂרָן – עוֹר שֶׁתַּחַת הָאַלְיָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that Shmuel and Rav Huna **do not say as Rav Ḥisda** says, that the mishna is referring to the tail of a kid? The Gemara responds that they reason: According to Rav Ḥisda, **what is** the mishna **teaching us** by referring specifically to the skin of the tail? Does it mean to teach simply that the **skin of** the **tail is considered** edible, **like** the **tail** itself? **We** already **learn** this in another mishna (*Ḥullin* 122a): **These** are the entities **whose skin** has a halakhic status **like** that of **their meat,** since it is soft and edible: The **skin beneath the tail.**

The Gemara notes, however, that this reading faces an inherent conceptual awkwardness: calling the goat's tail *alya shel gdi* — the "fat tail" of a goat — makes no sense, since a goat has no fat tail. Referring to it in these terms is as strange as speaking of the "fifth leg" of a goat. Nevertheless, Rav Chisda maintains this reading.

The Gemara then asks why Shmuel and Rav Chisda — both of whom hold that the skin is like the fat tail — did not adopt Rav Huna's position. The answer: *or alya ka'alya dami kamashma lehu* — both are committed to the view that the skin is categorically equivalent to the fat tail. Rav Huna's solution was not available to them.

Conversely, the Gemara asks why Shmuel did not follow Rav Chisda's goat reading. The answer: if the *mishna* is talking about the tail of a goat, then the only *chiddush* it would be conveying is that the skin of a goat's tail counts as edible — i.e., that it has the status of meat rather than bone or hoof. But that point is already implicit from the laws of *tumah*.

### Edibility Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא – אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה – דְּרַכִּיךְ מִצְטְרֵף. אֲבָל הָכָא, אֵימָא ״לְמׇשְׁחָה״ – לִגְדוּלָּה, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים אוֹכְלִים; וְלָא עֲבִידִי מְלָכִים דְּאָכְלִי הָכִי, אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rav Ḥisda,** how would he respond? The Gemara answers: The reference here to the skin of the tail **was necessary,** as given only the mishna in tractate *Ḥullin*, it might **enter your mind to say: This matter,** the equation between the skin of the tail and the tail itself, applies only **with regard to the matter of ritual impurity, as** the skin of the tail is **soft** and edible, and it is therefore **counted** as part of the tail. **But here,** with regard to the matter of the Temple service, **I will say** that the verse states with regard to the gifts to which members of the priesthood are entitled: **"As a consecrated portion"** (Numbers 18:8), **to** indicate that they must be eaten in **greatness, in the way that the kings eat. And** since **kings do not generally eat** the skin of the tail, **I will say** that it is **not** considered an eaten portion of the offering. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that it is nevertheless considered like the tail itself.

This observation leads naturally to the *baraisa* dealing with *tumah*: *v'eilu she'oroteihen k'visran* — the following are cases where the skin of an animal is treated like its flesh, rendering it susceptible to *tumah*, since only edible matter contracts *tumah*. Among the items listed is the skin beneath the fat tail. If the *mishna* were limited to the goat case, its entire teaching would be that such skin is edible — but this is already derived from the *tumah* laws.

Rav Chisda responds: the *mishna* is still necessary. One might have thought that the skin counts as edible for *tumah* only because *d'rachich mitztaref* — it is soft and contiguous with the flesh, making it sufficiently flesh-like to combine with flesh for *tumah* purposes even if it is not truly edible on its own. But for the laws of *piggul*, the standard is higher. The *kohanim* must eat *korbanot* *l'moshchah*, *k'derech shehamelachim ochlim* — in a dignified manner, as kings eat. Would a king eat the skin of a goat's tail? Perhaps not, as it might be considered too difficult or undignified to consume. Therefore *eima lo* — one might have thought such skin is too lowly to qualify as "eating" for *piggul* purposes, and the *mishna* teaches us *ka mashma lan* that it does qualify.

## Tosefta: Olah Case

### Tosefta Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הָעוֹלָה לְהַקְטִיר כְּזַיִת מֵעוֹר שֶׁתַּחַת הָאַלְיָה; חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת; חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who slaughters a burnt offering** with intent **to burn an olive-bulk of** the **skin beneath the tail outside its** designated **area** renders the offering **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of the offering. If his intent is to burn it **beyond its** designated **time,** it is rendered ***piggul*, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating **it.** This is the opinion of the Rabbis.

The Gemara now cites a *Tosefta* that bears directly on the question: *hashochet et ha'olah lehaktir kezayit me'or shetachat ha'alya* — if one slaughtered a burnt offering (*olah*) with intent to burn a *kezayis* of the skin beneath the fat tail outside its proper place (*chutz limkomo*), the sacrifice is *pasul ve'ein bo karet*; if the intent was *chutz lizmano*, it is *piggul ve'chayavin alav karet*. This Tosefta aligns directly with our *mishna* and speaks explicitly of the skin beneath the fat tail.

### Edible Parts List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ אֲבֵלִים אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ כְּפַר עִיכּוּס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֶחָד עוֹר בֵּית הַפְּרָסוֹת בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה, וְאֶחָד עוֹר הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁל עֵגֶל הָרַךְ, וְאֶחָד עוֹר שֶׁתַּחַת הָאַלְיָה, וְכׇל שֶׁמָּנוּ חֲכָמִים גַּבֵּי טוּמְאָה ״וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁעוֹרוֹתֵיהֶן כִּבְשָׂרָן״ – לְהָבִיא עוֹר שֶׁל בֵּית הַבּוֹשֶׁת; חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת; חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Elazar ben Yehuda of Avelim says in the name of Rabbi Ya'akov, and so Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda of Kefar Ikos would say in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Whether** the **hide of the hooves** of **small livestock, or** the **skin of the head of a young calf, or** the **skin beneath the tail, or any** of the skins **that the Sages listed with regard to ritual impurity** under the heading: **These** are the entities **whose skin** has a halakhic status **like** that of **their meat,** which means **to include the skin of the womb,** if one has intent to burn one of them **outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it,** and if one's intent is to burn it **beyond its** designated **time,** he renders it ***piggul*, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.**

The Tosefta continues with a broader statement from Elazar ben Yehuda ish Evelim in the name of Rabbi Yaakov, and similarly from Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda ish Kfar Ichus in the name of Rabbi Shimon. They enumerate multiple types of skin treated as flesh: the skin of the area around the hooves (*or beit haparsot*) of small animals; the skin of the skull of a young calf; the skin beneath the fat tail; and, in general, everything listed by the Sages in the context of *tumah* as cases where skin is like flesh — including the skin of the genital area (*or beit haboshet*). In all these cases, the rule is *chutz limkomo pasul ve'ein bo karet, chutz lizmano piggul ve'chayavin alav karet*. The Tosefta thus confirms that thoughts about eating any of these skins, at the wrong time or wrong place, carry the full *piggul* or *chutz limkomo* consequences.

### Olah Constraint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹלָה אִין, אֲבָל זֶבַח לָא; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב הוּנָא – הַיְינוּ דְקָתָנֵי ״עוֹלָה״; אֶלָּא לְרַב חִסְדָּא – מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״עוֹלָה״? לִיתְנֵי ״זֶבַח״!
>
> **Translation:** One can infer from the *baraita* that only with regard to **a burnt offering, yes,** the skin of the tail is burned on the altar like the tail itself. **But** with regard to another **offering,** this is **not** the *halakha*. **Granted, according to Rav Huna,** who holds that the skin of the tail of a peace offering is not considered as though it were the tail itself, and it is eaten rather than burned, **this is** the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches** about **a burnt offering** specifically, as all portions of a burnt offering are burned, even those that are eaten in the case of other offerings. **But according to Rav Ḥisda,** who holds that the skin of the tail is burned together with the tail, **why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** about **a burnt offering?** If the skin of the tail is always considered as though it were the tail itself, **let** the *tanna* **teach** about any **offering.**

However, the Gemara points out a critical feature of the Tosefta's language: the case is introduced as *hashochet et ha'olah* — one who slaughters an *olah* — suggesting that this law applies specifically to a burnt offering and not to other sacrifices (*olah in aval zevach lo*). This is puzzling: if the skin beneath the fat tail is considered edible, why should the rule be limited to an *olah*?

The Gemara explains: *bishlama l'Rav Huna hainu d'katani olah* — for Rav Huna, this limitation makes perfect sense. Rav Huna holds that the skin of the fat tail is *not* burned on the altar together with the fat tail of a regular sacrifice; rather, it is consumed by people. Therefore, a thought about *burning* the skin at the wrong time or place only arises as a *piggul*-type concern in the case of an *olah*, where everything is burned on the altar. In an *olah*, even the skin beneath the fat tail is consigned to the fire; therefore, a thought about burning it in the wrong time or place is meaningful. But in other sacrifices, since the skin is eaten rather than burned, a thought about *burning* it would not arise in the relevant way.

The implicit difficulty with this analysis is that once we are dealing with an *olah* — where everything is burned — the reason to single out the skin beneath the fat tail (as opposed to hooves or other parts) must be to establish that this skin has the status of "meat" and not "hoof," thereby making a *piggul* thought about it meaningful. That appears to be the *chiddush*.

### Challenge to Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב חִסְדָּא: אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, בְּאַלְיָה שֶׁל גְּדִי; וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, תְּנִי: ״זֶבַח״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda** could have **said to you: If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is referring **to the tail of a kid,** which is never burned on the altar except in the case of a burnt offering. **And if you wish, say** instead that one should emend the text of the *baraita* to **teach:** One who slaughters any **offering,** etc.

For Rav Chisda, however, the *olah* limitation is difficult. Rav Chisda holds that the skin of the fat tail is burned on the *mizbeach* together with the fat tail in *every* sacrifice, not only in an *olah*. If so, why does the Tosefta specifically invoke the *olah* case? Any sacrifice involving the fat tail would generate the same question.

Rav Chisda offers two responses. First: *i ba'is eima b'alya shel gdi* — one can say the Tosefta is speaking of a goat's tail, in which case the *olah* limitation makes sense: only in the case of an *olah* would the goat's tail go onto the altar entirely; in other sacrifices the tail would be consumed. Second: *i ba'is eima tani zevach* — alternatively, the limitation to *olah* in our text of the Tosefta may simply be a textual variant, and the Tosefta in fact applies to all sacrifices. Tosafot notes that this second resolution is actually supported by their own textual tradition of the *Tosefta*, which does not limit the ruling to an *olah*.

## Piggul Sources

### Long Pesuk Tzav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if one intends to consume the meat or sprinkle the blood outside its designated area, the offering is **disqualified, and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of it. If one intends to do so beyond its designated time, the offering is rendered *piggul*, and one is liable to receive *karet* for burning or partaking of it. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Shmuel says: Two verses are written.**

Turning from the particular laws of the fat tail skin, the Gemara now asks: *mina hanei mili* — what is the scriptural basis for the entire framework of *piggul* and *chutz limkomo*, including the differentiation that one carries *karet* and the other does not? *Amar Shmuel trei kraei ktivi* — Shmuel states that there are two relevant verses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רַבָּה: ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ – זֶהוּ חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are** these verses? **Rabba said:** Shmuel is referring to two phrases from the verse: "And if any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten on the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be credited to he who offers it; it shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18). The Sages taught that this verse is referring not to offerings actually eaten on the third day, but to one who performs one of the sacrificial rites with intent to eat the offering beyond its designated time. Now, when the verse states: "And if any of the flesh…be at all eaten on the **third** day," **this is** referring to intent to consume the meat **beyond its** designated **time.**

The first is the long verse in Vayikra 7:18 (*parshat Tzav*), which reads:

> וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי לֹא יֵרָצֶה הַמַּקְרִיב אֹתוֹ לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב לוֹ פִּגּוּל יִהְיֶה וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ עֲוֹנָהּ תִּשָּׂא

This verse contains multiple key phrases. The reference to eating on the third day points to *chutz lizmano* — the wrong-time category. The word *lo yeratze* — it will not be accepted — is significant: once the blood is on the altar, eating from the meat cannot retroactively invalidate the offering. The acceptance or rejection of a *korban* is determined entirely by the blood. Therefore, the verse cannot be speaking of actually eating on the third day; it must be referring to thinking about eating on the third day. This is reinforced by *lo yeichashev* — "it will not be reckoned for him" — which the Gemara reads as *machashava*, thought. The word *piggul yihyeh* seems redundant after all the foregoing, and therefore carries additional information: it introduces the category of *chutz limkomo* as well.

### Raba's Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פִּגּוּל״ – זֶהוּ חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ. ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם; זֶהוּ חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וּלְמַעוֹטֵי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse states: "It shall be ***piggul*," this is** referring to one who has intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area.** And when the verse states: **"And the soul that eats of it** shall bear his iniquity," i.e., shall be liable to receive *karet*, "of it" indicates that only **one** of the above disqualifications carries the penalty of *karet* for one who partakes of it, **but not two** of them. And **this** included case **is** intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time, to the exclusion of** intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area,** which carries no such penalty.

*Amar Rava*: *shlishi zehu chutz lizmano* — the third-day reference is the source for *chutz lizmano*. *Piggul zehu chutz limkomo* — the apparently superfluous word *piggul* in the verse points to the second category, *chutz limkomo*. Strikingly, the word *piggul* — which gives its name to the wrong-time invalidity — is here being used as the source for the wrong-*place* invalidity. The Torah's own terminology is being deployed against its apparent meaning, in service of the exegetical need to source both categories from this verse.

### Karet Allocation

The verse concludes: *ve'hanefesh ha'ochelet mimenu avonah tisa* — the person who eats from it bears their sin. The word *mimenu* — "from it" — implies a single referent. Since both *chutz lizmano* and *chutz limkomo* have been read into the verse, the *karet* consequence (*avonah tisa*) applies to only one of them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ״ – זֶהוּ חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וּלְמַעוֹטֵי חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ! מִסְתַּבְּרָא חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ עֲדִיף – דִּפְתַח בֵּיהּ. אַדְּרַבָּה! חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ עֲדִיף – דִּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** the opposite, that when the verse states: **"And the soul that eats of it** shall bear his iniquity," **this is** referring to intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area, to the exclusion of** intent to consume it **beyond its** designated **time?** The Gemara responds: **It stands to reason** that intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time** is the **preferable** candidate to carry the punishment of *karet*, **as** the verse **opened with it.** The Gemara rejects this: **On the contrary,** intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area** is **preferable, as** the clause that teaches liability for *karet* is **adjacent to it.**

The Gemara asks: how do we know it is *chutz lizmano* that carries *karet* and not *chutz limkomo*? Perhaps *mimenu* refers to the *chutz limkomo* case. Several arguments are advanced. First: *mistabra chutz lizmano adif d'patach bei* — it stands to reason that *chutz lizmano* is the weightier category, since the verse opens with it. Counterargument: *adaraba chutz limkomo adif d'samich lei* — on the contrary, *chutz limkomo* is the most recently mentioned category and *mimenu* naturally refers back to the most proximate referent. The Gemara cannot resolve this from the verse alone.

### Short Pesuk Kedoshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי [אָמַר רַב], סָמֵיךְ אַדְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא: כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ בְּפָרָשַׁת ״קְדֹשִׁים תִּהְיוּ״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים בְּיוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי״;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: When Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi came,** he said that **Rav says:** Shmuel was actually referring to two separate verses. And he **relied on that which the *tanna* taught:** The verse is seemingly redundant **when it states:** "And if it be eaten at all on the third day, it is *piggul*; it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 19:7), **in the** Torah **portion** that begins: **"You shall be holy"** (Leviticus 19:2), **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this, **since it is already stated: "And if any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten on the third day**…it shall be *piggul*" (Leviticus 7:18).

This leads to the second verse: Vayikra 19:7 (*parshat Kedoshim*):

> וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי פִּגּוּל הוּא לֹא יֵרָצֶה

This is the short verse. It shares key language with the Tzav verse — *bayom hashlishi*, *piggul hu*, *lo yeratzeh* — but it omits the *karet* clause entirely. The Gemara, citing a *tanna* quoted by Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi in the name of Rav, proposes a complete reassignment: the long verse in Tzav is entirely about *chutz lizmano*, and the short verse in Kedoshim is entirely about *chutz limkomo*. Since the Kedoshim verse does not mention *karet*, this elegantly explains why *chutz limkomo* carries no *karet*: the verse that introduces it simply does not include that penalty. *She'ein talmud lomar* — there would be no need for the Kedoshim verse if both categories were already covered by the Tzav verse. Therefore: *im eino inyan lechutz lizmano tnehu inyan lechutz limkomo* — since the Kedoshim verse is redundant for *chutz lizmano*, apply it to *chutz limkomo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, תְּנֵהוּ לְעִנְיַן חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ; וּמִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי נוֹתָר: ״וְאֹכְלָיו עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָּׂא״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ מִכָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **if** the verse **is not** referring **to the matter** of intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time, apply it to the matter of** intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area. And** as for the punishment of *karet*, **the Merciful One restricted** it to the prohibition of ***notar*,** i.e., actually partaking of the offering beyond its designated time, as the verse states: **"But whoever eats it shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 19:8). This indicates that one is liable to receive *karet* for partaking of *notar*, **to the exclusion of** intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area.**

The next verse in Kedoshim (19:8) then reads:

> וְאֹכְלָיו עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָּׂא כִּי אֶת קֹדֶשׁ ה' חִלֵּל וְנִכְרְתָה

This verse follows the *chutz limkomo* verse and mentions *karet* (*ve'nichreta*). However, the Gemara argues, this *karet* applies not to *chutz limkomo* but to *nosar* — leftover meat that was not eaten in time. The *peshat* of the passage is indeed about *nosar*, and since the verse says *lo yeratzeh* (which, as established, signals that we are dealing with intention rather than actual eating), it must be that the *chutz limkomo* is embedded in it while the *karet* clause attaches to *nosar* alone. *Umi'et Rachmana gabi nosar ve'ochlav avono yisa lemi'utei chutz limkomo* — the Torah excludes *chutz limkomo* from *karet* through the *nosar* verse's formulation *ve'ochlav avono yisa*, which singles out one category (nosar) as bearing *karet* to the exclusion of the other (*chutz limkomo*).

## Nosar Connection

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ״ – זֶהוּ חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וּלְמַעוֹטֵי נוֹתָר מִכָּרֵת! מִסְתַּבְּרָא נוֹתָר הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְאוֹקוֹמֵי בְּכָרֵת – לְמִגְמַר ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ בְּז״ב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** the opposite, that when the verse states: **"And the soul that eats of it** shall bear his iniquity," **this is** referring to intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area, and** the verse serves **to exclude *nosar* from** the penalty of ***karet*?** The Gemara responds: **It stands to reason** that ***nosar* should be interpreted as** carrying the penalty of ***karet*,** because this would allow one **to derive** by way of verbal analogy between **"iniquity"** and **"iniquity"** stated with regard **to** the case of intent to consume the meat **beyond its** designated **time,** that the punishment of *karet* applies to the latter as well. The verbal analogy would be apt **since** one prohibition **is similar to** the other **in** that they share features that form the acronym ***zayin*, *beit*:** Both concern the offering's designated time [*zeman*], and both are applicable to an offering sacrificed on a private altar [*bama*].

The Gemara, however, immediately questions the allocation: why say that *ve'ochlav avono yisa* refers to *nosar* and not to *chutz limkomo*? Maybe *chutz limkomo* is the one that gets *karet* while *nosar* is excluded. *Amar Abaye ki asa Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi*, citing a teaching by Rav: the resolution comes from a *gezeirah shavah* taught by Rabbi Yochanan — *atya kodesh kodesh*. The phrase *ki et kodesh Hashem chillel* in Kedoshim 19:8 contains the word *kodesh*, and another verse about *nosar* — in Shemot 29 — reads *hanosar ba'esh lo ye'achel ki kodesh hu*. Since both verses use the word *kodesh* in the context of *karet* consequences for eating sanctified matter, we establish a *gezeirah shavah* linking them. This proves that the *karet* in Kedoshim 19:8 refers to *nosar*, not to *chutz limkomo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, תָּנֵי זַבְדִּי בַּר לֵוִי: אַתְיָא ״קֹדֶשׁ״–״קֹדֶשׁ״; כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״אֶת קֹדֶשׁ ה׳ חִלֵּל וְנִכְרְתָה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְשָׂרַפְתָּ אֶת הַנּוֹתָר בָּאֵשׁ וְגוֹ׳״; מָה לְהַלָּן נוֹתָר, אַף כָּאן נוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Zavdi bar Levi taught: It is derived** that the prohibition of *notar* carries the penalty of *karet* by way of verbal analogy between **"sacred"** and **"sacred." It is written here:** "But whoever eats it shall bear his iniquity, because **he has profaned the sacred item of the Lord; and** that soul **shall be cut off** from his people" (Leviticus 19:8). **And it is written there:** "And if any of the flesh of the consecration, or of the bread, remain until the morning, **then you shall burn the remainder [*notar*] with fire;** it shall not be eaten, because it is sacred" (Exodus 29:34). **Just as** the verse **there** is referring to ***notar*, so too,** the verse **here** is referring to ***notar*.**

### Karet Only Nosar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי נוֹתָר ״וְאֹכְלָיו עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָּׂא״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ מִכָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** consequently, when **the Merciful One restricted** the penalty of *karet* **to** the prohibition of eating ***notar*,** as the verse states: **"But whoever eats it shall bear his iniquity,"** this restriction serves **to exclude** the case of intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area from** the penalty of ***karet*.**

The logic is thus: the long Tzav verse is the source for *chutz lizmano*, and *karet* applies to eating from a sacrifice rendered *piggul* through that wrong-time thought. The short Kedoshim verse is the source for *chutz limkomo*, and it carries no *karet*. The following verse in Kedoshim does mention *karet*, but via the *gezeirah shavah* of *kodesh-kodesh* we know it refers to *nosar* — actual leftover meat — not to the *chutz limkomo* invalidity embedded in the same context.

### Zman & Bamah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ עֲדִיף – דִּפְתַח בֵּיהּ. אַדְּרַבָּה! חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ עֲדִיף – דִּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כּוּלְּהוּ מִקְּרָא אֲרִיכָא אָתַיִין; דִּכְתִיב: ״הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד אֲכִילַת אָדָם וְאֶחָד אֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** it stands to reason that **the long** verse is referring **to** intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area, and** that the verse that mentions the **third** day **in** the Torah portion that begins: **"You shall be holy,"** is referring **to** intent to consume it **beyond its** designated **time.** This is **due to** the fact **that** the latter intent is **similar to** *notar* in that both prohibitions share the features mentioned above that form the acronym: *Zayin*, *beit*, and it is therefore reasonable that the Torah **juxtaposed** the two in adjacent verses. **And** if so, when the verse states with regard to *notar*: "But whoever eats it shall bear his iniquity," this **excludes** intent to consume the offering beyond its designated time from the penalty of *karet*.

But the Gemara still needs to justify why *ve'ochlav avono yisa* attaches to *nosar* rather than to *chutz limkomo*, given the *gezeirah shavah*. The deeper argument is: *mistabra nosar havei leh le'okmei be'karet lemimar avon avon mi'chutz lizmano de'dami leh be'zav*. The *zav* here is an acronym: *zayin* for *zman* and *vet* for *bamah*. Both *chutz lizmano* and *nosar* share two defining characteristics — they are both time-related concepts (wrong *time* of intent vs. actually leaving meat past its permitted time), and both apply on a *bamah* (a private altar). These shared features make it appropriate to derive *karet* for *nosar* through comparison with *chutz lizmano*: the word *avon* in the *nosar* verse is learned from the *avon* in the *chutz lizmano* context. *Chutz limkomo*, by contrast, is place-based and does not apply on a *bamah*, so it shares neither of these features with *nosar*.

### Juxtaposition Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חָזֵית דִּקְרָא אֲרִיכָא – בְּחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וּ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ דְּפָרָשַׁת ״קְדֹשִׁים תִּהְיוּ״ – חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ? אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the initial assumption that the statement of Shmuel is referring to two verses: **And what did you see** that led you to conclude **that the long verse** (Leviticus 7:18) is referring **to** intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time, and** that the verse that mentions the **third** day (Leviticus 19:7) **in the** Torah **portion** that begins: **"You shall be holy,"** is referring to intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area? I** might just as well **reverse** the two.

The Gemara offers a counterargument: *adaraba aricha be'chutz limkomo* — perhaps the long verse in Tzav is actually about *chutz limkomo*, and the short Kedoshim verse is about *chutz lizmano*. In that case, the *karet* of the following verse would be applied via the acronym *mikdash* — *mem* for *machshava* (both are thought-based invalidities), *kof* for *ktzat* (both invalidate through a thought about even a portion), *dalet* for *dam* (both involve thoughts about blood), and *shin* for *shlishi* (both relate to the third day). These points of comparison between *chutz limkomo* and *nosar* are numerous — arguably more numerous than the *zav* comparison between *chutz lizmano* and *nosar*. Furthermore, it makes narrative sense for *chutz lizmano* to be juxtaposed to the *nosar* verse: they are conceptually similar (both time-related), and the juxtaposition would serve to compare and distinguish them — confirming that while both involve time considerations, only *nosar* (actual leftover meat) generates *karet* from the subsequent verse, not the mere thought of eating at the wrong time.

### Inconclusive End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא אֲרִיכָא בְּחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – דְּגָמַר ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ מִנּוֹתָר, דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ בְּז״ב. אַדְּרַבָּה! אֲרִיכָא בְּחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וּ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ דִּ״קְדֹשִׁים תִּהְיוּ״ בְּחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – מִשּׁוּם דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ, סַמְכֵיהּ וְקָא מְמַעֵט לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כּוּלְּהוּ מִקְּרָא אֲרִיכָא אָתַיִין; דִּכְתִיב: ״הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד אֲכִילַת אָדָם וְאֶחָד אֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It stands to reason** that **the long** verse is referring **to** intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time, as** this allows for one to **derive** by way of verbal analogy between **"iniquity"** and **"iniquity" from *notar*** that an offering sacrificed with such intent carries the penalty of *karet*. The verbal analogy is apt **since** one prohibition **is similar to** the other **in** that both share the features mentioned above that form the acronym: ***Zayin*, *beit*.

**Rather, Rava says:** One must accept the original interpretation of Shmuel's statement, that **all of** these *halakhot* with regard to intent to consume the offering beyond its designated time or outside its designated area **are derived from the long verse. As it is written:** "If any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten on the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be credited to he who offers it; it shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18). When the verse states: **"Be at all eaten [*he'akhol ye'akhel*],"** using a doubled verb, **the verse speaks of** intent with regard to **two consumptions, one consumption by a person, and one consumption by** the **altar,** i.e., burning the offering. Improper intent with regard to either of these acts renders the offering *piggul*.

The Gemara concludes that neither side can definitively prove its case. The question of which verse is the *chutz lizmano* verse and which is the *chutz limkomo* verse cannot be resolved through the arguments advanced. The shiur ends on this unresolved note — with the understanding that the following day will return to learn everything out from the first verse and demonstrate how both categories, and the differentiation in *karet*, are derived from that single source.
