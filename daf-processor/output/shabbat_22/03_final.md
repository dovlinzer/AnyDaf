# Shabbat 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Megillat Ta'anit Sources

### Megillat Ta'anit

Today's daf is 22a, and we continue our discussion of Chanukah. Before turning to the Gemara proper, it is worth pausing to examine the source most often associated with the Chanukah narrative: *Megillat Ta'anit*. A recent academic volume by Vered Noam has brought serious scholarly attention to this text, which is, in fact, remarkably brief. The entire entry for Kislev reads as follows: *Bi-tlata be-Kislev itnussun timusa min darta, bi-shiva yom tava, va-esrim va-achad yom Har Gerizim, u-va-esrim ve-chamitza pucha Chanukah yomin tamnaya de-lo le-mispade, ba-esrim u-temanya ne-itosei* — and then the text moves on to Tevet. That is it. A few words for each of the four or five days per month on which a *hesped* is prohibited, with no further elaboration whatsoever.

### Textual Variants

Very early on, a gloss on *Megillat Ta'anit* appeared — known in scholarly terminology as a *scholion* (plural: *scholia*) — which provided historical background for each of the holidays listed in the text. This gloss, composed probably at the end of the Tannaitic period and the beginning of the Amoraic period, exists in two significantly different versions: one that appears to derive from Eretz Yisrael and one from Bavel. What we possess today as the standard scholion on *Megillat Ta'anit* is in fact a hybrid of these two early versions, with heavy influence from the Talmud Bavli and extensive grafts from it. Neither of the two original manuscript families contains the story of the oil miracle. The oil miracle, as a historical-textual matter, originates exclusively in the Talmud Bavli, and from there it made its way into the later hybrid recension of the scholion.

### Eight Days Debate

Both early versions of the scholion do attempt to explain why Chanukah is celebrated for eight days, but they do so without any reference to oil. Their reasoning is as follows: when the *Chanukat HaMishkan* was inaugurated it lasted seven days, and similarly the *Chanukat HaMizbeach* in the time of Shlomo lasted seven days — so why eight days here? The scholion explains that they were occupied for eight days rededicating the *mizbeach*, remaking a *menorah*, and attending to the lighting of the *menorah*. One version adds that because all the *kelim* were *tamei*, they had to construct a new *menorah* — the tradition recorded also in the Bavli, that their initial *menorah* was fashioned from their spears covered with lead. But nowhere in either of these early versions is there any mention of the oil being *tamei* or requiring purification. That element belongs entirely to the Bavli.

### Oil Miracle & Presence

The Maharal is troubled by the prominence the Bavli gives to the oil miracle. His fundamental answer is that while the primary miracle of Chanukah was of course the military victory over the Greeks and the rededication of the *Mikdash*, the miracle of the oil served to demonstrate that it was God who was part of that entire process — that it was about God's presence, the *Shechinah*, once again dwelling with *Bnei Yisrael*. This is a point that the *aggadata* we will encounter shortly makes vivid and explicit. Understanding this theological dimension helps clarify why the Bavli places such emphasis on the oil miracle and why it became the defining narrative of the holiday.

### Mehadrin Levels

This framework also informs the discussion of *mehadrin min hamehadrin*. Some want to explain that even if *tahor* oil was not strictly necessary — since *tumah hutra betzibbur* — or even if *tahor* oil could have been obtained by other means, the fact that they went out of their way to perform the lighting *betaharah*, even when it was not strictly required, reflects an act of *hiddur*, of striving for the best possible fulfillment. And mirroring that striving, as the Maharal explains, God went out of His way to demonstrate His involvement and His presence. The miracle of the oil is thus a *nes* that reflects divine reciprocity, and it is in this spirit that the levels of *mehadrin* and *mehadrin min hamehadrin* take on their deeper meaning.

## Bava Kamma Parallels

### Overloaded Camel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: גֵּץ הַיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת הַפַּטִּישׁ וְיָצָא וְהִזִּיק — חַיָּיב. גָּמָל שֶׁטָּעוּן פִּשְׁתָּן וְהוּא עוֹבֵר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְנִכְנְסָה פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַחֲנוּת וְדָלְקָה בְּנֵרוֹ שֶׁל חֶנְוָנִי וְהִדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה — בַּעַל הַגָּמָל חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** We learned there in a mishna with regard to damages: In the case of a spark that emerges from under a hammer, and went out of the artisan's workshop, and caused damage, the one who struck the hammer is liable. Similarly, in the case of a camel that is laden with flax and it passed through the public domain, and its flax entered into a store, and caught fire from the storekeeper's lamp, and set fire to the building, the camel owner is liable. Since his flax entered into another's domain, which he had no permission to enter, all the damages were caused due to his negligence.

The Gemara now cites a *Mishnah* from tractate Bava Kamma: *Gitz hayotzei mitachat hapatish veyatza vehizik, chayav* — if a spark flew out from under a hammer and caused damage, the blacksmith is liable. This *Mishnah* is discussing *esh*, fire, one of the four categories of damage caused by one's property. The *Mishnah* continues: if a camel laden with flax was walking through the *reshut harabim* — the public domain — and the overloaded flax extended beyond the permissible width and entered a storekeeper's shop, where it caught fire from the storekeeper's candle, and that fire then spread and ignited a large building, the owner of the camel is liable. Not only is the storekeeper exempt, but the camel owner, by overloading his animal and causing it to exceed the parameters of the *reshut harabim*, essentially added fuel to the flame and is therefore responsible for all resulting damage.

### Storekeeper Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִנִּיחַ חֶנְוָנִי אֶת נֵרוֹ מִבְּחוּץ — חֶנְוָנִי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** However, if the storekeeper placed his lamp outside the store and it set fire to the flax, the storekeeper is liable, as he placed the lamp outside his domain where he had no right to place it.

*Hiniach hachanvani nero mibachutz* — if the storekeeper had placed his candle on the outside of his store, in the space that normally belongs to those traversing the *reshut harabim*, then the storekeeper is liable for any damage caused, since it is now effectively his fire.

### Chanukah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda says: If the flax was set on fire by the storekeeper's Hanukkah lamp that he placed outside the entrance to his store, he is not liable, as in that case, it is permitted for the storekeeper to place his lamp outside.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer: be-ner Chanukah patur* — Rabbi Yehuda rules that if it was a Chanukah candle, the storekeeper is exempt. The reasoning is that one is entitled to keep Chanukah candles outside, and those traversing the *reshut harabim* during Chanukah must take appropriate care to keep their loads pulled in. There may also be a broader principle at work — that performing a *mitzvah* confers a degree of exemption from certain liability in such cases. These are questions that belong to a full treatment of Bava Kamma.

One notes that Chanukah appears only this once in the *Mishnayot* — three words, *be-ner Chanukah patur*, as a parenthetical statement — and that there is no tractate dedicated to Chanukah the way there is a tractate *Megillah* devoted to Purim. The Ramban suggests this may reflect rabbinic unease with the Chashmonaim — not regarding the *nes Chanukah* itself, but for their subsequent seizure of political authority, which properly belonged to *Beit David*, in violation of the principle that *kehunah* and religious authority must be separated from political rule. Whatever one makes of that explanation, the singular and brief appearance of Chanukah in the Mishnah is certainly worth noting.

### Ten Tefachim Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבָּה: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת נֵר חֲנוּכָּה מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחָהּ בְּתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina said in the name of Rabba: That is to say that it is a mitzva to place the Hanukkah lamp within ten handbreadths of the ground.

Ravina, citing Rava — or according to some readings, in the name of Rava — draws an inference from Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: *zot omeret ner Chanukah mitzvah lehannicha betoch asara* — this teaches that it is a *mitzvah* to place the Chanukah candle within ten *tefachim* of the ground. The reasoning: if there were no upper limit on the permitted height, then the storekeeper should have been told that since he is allowed to keep it in the *reshut harabim*, he ought to place it higher than even a camel with its rider, thereby putting it out of anyone's way entirely. The fact that Rabbi Yehuda grants the exemption without requiring such a height implies that one is *not* required — or indeed permitted — to place it that high.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא, אִי מַיְטְּרְחָא לֵיהּ טוּבָא אָתֵי לְאִימְּנוֹעֵי מִמִּצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** And perhaps one is also permitted to place the Hanukkah lamp above ten handbreadths, and the reason Rabbi Yehuda exempted the storekeeper was due to concern for the observance of the mitzva of kindling Hanukkah lights. He held that if you burden one excessively, he will come to refrain from performing the mitzva of kindling Hanukkah lights.

The Gemara pushes back: perhaps the argument fails because *im matracha lei tuvah ati le-imnu-ei mimitzvah* — if we imposed too great a burden on people, they would simply refrain from performing the *mitzvah* altogether. It is therefore not that one cannot place it higher; rather, we accommodate people by allowing a convenient placement, and once that right has been granted, the responsibility shifts to those in the *reshut harabim* to take due care. This reflects the broader principle in Bava Kamma about how competing rights in the public domain are adjudicated: generally, the person exercising a recognized right is not the negligent party, and the one who fails to account for that right bears the liability. Fire, however, operates somewhat differently, which is why this case requires more careful analysis.

## Height Restrictions

### Twenty Amot Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵר שֶׁל חֲנוּכָּה שֶׁהִנִּיחָה לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה — פְּסוּלָה, כְּסוּכָּה וּכְמָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** A Hanukkah lamp that one placed above twenty cubits is invalid, just as a sukka whose roofing is more than twenty cubits high, and just as an alleyway whose beam, its symbolic fourth partition in order to place an eiruv, is more than twenty cubits high, are invalid.

The Gemara records that Rav Kahana taught in the name of Rav Natan bar Minyomi, who expounded in the name of Rabbi Tanchum, on daf 22a: *ner shel Chanukah shehinicho lemala me'esrim amah pesula* — a Chanukah candle placed higher than twenty *amah* is invalid. The two standard height thresholds in *halacha* are ten *tefachim* — roughly three feet — and twenty *amah* — roughly thirty feet. The question of whether the candle must be placed within ten *tefachim* was raised above. Here we learn that twenty *amah* is an absolute upper limit.

### Sukkah & Mavoi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְתִיב: דְּאִיתַנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וִיהִי כְמִשְׁמַע הָעוֹלָם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר וַיְדַבֵּר אֱלֹהִים וַיֹּאמֶר, וַדַּאי הָעוֹלָם לִיכְנַס בִּזְמַן הַלַּיִל לְעִומַת הָעוֹלָם לִיכְנַס בִּזְמַן הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The reason offered is as with a sukkah and as with a mavoi. In both of those domains, something situated higher than twenty amah is treated as non-functional because it is not naturally visible from ground level.

The reason offered is *kesukkah u'kemavoi* — as with a *sukkah* and as with a *mavoi*. In both of those domains, something situated higher than twenty *amah* is treated as non-functional because it is not naturally visible from ground level. A *sukkah* whose *sechach* is higher than twenty *amah* is invalid according to one explanation because one will not naturally look up and notice the *sechach*; one senses the walls but not the defining feature above. Similarly, the beam (*korah*) that marks the fourth side of a *mavoi* — the opening that has no wall — must be visible to serve its function as a *heker*, a sign indicating the permissible boundary. If placed above twenty *amah*, it will not be seen and cannot function as such. The same logic applies to *ner Chanukah*: *pirsumei nisa* requires that the candle actually be seen by those passing in the *reshut harabim*, and above twenty *amah* that condition cannot be met.

This ruling raises obvious practical questions for those who live on upper floors. The Gemara noted earlier that one who *dar be'aliyah* places the candle in the window closest to the *reshut harabim*; in Talmudic times, upper floors were generally not more than thirty feet off the ground. Contemporary multi-story dwellings present a genuine *she'elah*, though the possibility of fulfilling the *mitzvah* for the members of one's household — without the requirement of public visibility — remains available, as does the fact that wide streets and greater distances can sometimes allow for meaningful visibility at greater heights.

### Yosef's Pit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, דָּרֵשׁ רַב נָתָן בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב תַּנְחוּם: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״וְהַבּוֹר רֵק אֵין בּוֹ מָיִם״? מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהַבּוֹר רֵק״ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מָיִם? אֶלָּא מַה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֵין בּוֹ מָיִם״ — מַיִם אֵין בּוֹ, אֲבָל נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים יֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The same chain of tradition — Rav Kahana citing Rav Natan bar Minyomi in the name of Rabbi Tanchum — also offers a derash on a verse in Parashat Vayeshev, the parasha always read during Chanukah: "And they took him, and cast him into the pit; and the pit was empty, there was no water in it" (Genesis 37:24). By inference from that which is stated: And the pit was empty, don't I know that there was no water in it? Rather, why does the verse say: There was no water in it? The verse comes to emphasize and teach that there was no water in it, but there were snakes and scorpions in it.

The same chain of tradition — Rav Kahana citing Rav Natan bar Minyomi in the name of Rabbi Tanchum — also offers a *derash* on a verse in *Parashat Vayeshev*, the *parasha* always read during Chanukah: *ve-habor reik ein bo mayim* — the pit was empty, there was no water. The Torah already tells us it was empty; surely that implies there was no water. So why add *ein bo mayim*? The Gemara answers: *mayim ein bo, aval nechasim va'akrabim yesh bo* — there was no water, but there were snakes and scorpions. The *Torah Temimah* connects this to the twenty-*amah* principle: perhaps the pit was deeper than twenty *amah*, which is why Reuven, who intended to rescue Yosef, did not perceive the snakes and scorpions within it — they were too far below to be visible. Just as things beyond twenty *amah* upward are invisible to the eye, so too things deeper than twenty *amah* downward cannot be seen. The application to *ner Chanukah* is apparent: visibility is bounded in both directions. The *derash* itself is admittedly somewhat *ikkar chaseir min hasefer* — the twenty *amah* is not mentioned explicitly in the verse — but the principle it illustrates is clear.

## Doorway Placement

### Tefach Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחָהּ בְּטֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said: It is a mitzva to place the Hanukkah lamp within the handbreadth adjacent to the entrance.

Rabbah rules: *ner Chanukah mitzvah lehannichah be-tefach hasemucha le-fetach* — the proper placement of the Chanukah candle is within a *tefach* of the doorway, on the outside. If placed too far from one's doorway, passersby will not associate it with one's home and will not recognize it as a *ner Chanukah*. The *tefach* in question is approximately three to four inches — the candle must be quite close to the doorpost or door frame, so that its association with one's residence is unambiguous.

### Right vs Left

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא מַנַּח לֵיהּ? רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: מִיָּמִין רַב שְׁמוּאֵל מִדִּפְתִּי אָמַר: מִשְּׂמֹאל. וְהִילְכְתָא מִשְּׂמֹאל, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא נֵר חֲנוּכָּה מִשְּׂמֹאל וּמְזוּזָה מִיָּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** And where, on which side, does he place it? There is a difference of opinion: Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said: On the right side of the entrance. Rav Shmuel from Difti said: On the left. And the *halakha* is to place it on the left so that the Hanukkah lamp will be on the left and the *mezuza* on the right. One who enters the house will be surrounded by mitzvot.

Which side of the doorway? *Rav Acha bereih de-Rabba amar mi-yamin* — on the right, meaning the right side as one faces inward from outside. *Rav Shmuel mi-Diphti amar mi-smol* — on the left. The *hilchata*, the ruling, is *mi-smol* — on the left — *kedei she-tehei ner Chanukah mi-smol u-mezuzah mi-yamin*: so that the Chanukah candle is on the left and the *mezuzah* is on the right, surrounding the entrant with *mitzvot* on both sides. Presumably the opinion that favored the right held that the right always takes priority and thus both should be on the right; the ruling, however, is that separating them — one on each side — is preferable. This is also the accepted practice.

## Counting Money Ban

### Money Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לְהַרְצוֹת מָעוֹת כְּנֶגֶד נֵר חֲנוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda said that Rav Asi said that Rav said: It is prohibited to count money opposite a Hanukkah light.

Rav Yehuda, citing Rav Assi — though there is a variant reading that attributes this to Rav or to Rav Yirmeyah — states: *assur le-hartzos ma'os keneged ner Chanukah* — it is forbidden to count money in the presence of the Chanukah lights.

### Kedushah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: וְכִי נֵר קְדוּשָּׁה יֵשׁ בָּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** When I said this halakha before Shmuel, he said to me: Does the Hanukkah light have sanctity that would prohibit one from using its light?

When this ruling was reported before Shmuel, he objected: *ve-chi ner kedushah yesh bah?* — does the Chanukah candle possess *kedushah*? If an object is sanctified to the *Beit HaMikdash*, deriving *hana'ah* from it is forbidden. But a Chanukah candle is not such an object — it is used for a *mitzvah*, but it carries no formal *kedushah*. One would not say, for example, that a person cannot fidget with their *tzitzit* because doing so treats a sacred object improperly. Why then should one be prohibited from counting money by the light of *ner Chanukah*?

### Blood Covering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְכִי דָּם קְדוּשָּׁה יֵשׁ בּוֹ? דְּתַנְיָא ״וְשָׁפַךְ … וְכִסָּה״ — בַּמֶּה שֶׁשָּׁפַךְ יְכַסֶּה. שֶׁלֹּא יְכַסֶּנּוּ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִצְוֹת בְּזוּיוֹת עָלָיו. הָכָא נָמֵי, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִצְוֹת בְּזוּיוֹת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef strongly objected to this question: What kind of question is that; does the blood of a slaughtered undomesticated animal or fowl have sanctity? As it was taught in a baraita that the Sages interpreted the verse: "He shall spill its blood and cover it with dust" (Leviticus 17:13): With that which he spilled, he shall cover. Just as a person spills the blood of a slaughtered animal with his hand, so too, he is obligated to cover the blood with this hand and not cover it with his foot. The reason is so that mitzvot will not be contemptible to him. Here too, one should treat the Hanukkah lights as if they were sacred and refrain from utilizing them for other purposes, so that mitzvot will not be contemptible to him.

Rav Yosef responds sharply: *hachi dami kedushah yesh bo?* — is *kedushah* really the only basis for such a restriction? Consider the *mitzvah* of *kisuy hadam*: when one slaughters a *chayah* or a bird, the blood must be covered with earth. The verse says *veshafach vechisah* — one spills the blood and covers it. *Bamah sheshafach yechaseh* — the act of covering follows directly from the act of spilling, meaning one uses one's hand or a shovel. But *shelo yechasenu beregel* — one may not kick the dirt over with one's foot, which would be the most natural and effortless gesture. Why? *Shelo yehyu mitzvot buzuyot alav* — so that *mitzvot* should not be degraded in one's eyes. Kicking dirt over something is dismissive; it signals disregard. Therefore the act must be done respectfully. *Hacha nami* — here too, *shelo yehyu mitzvot buzuyot alav*: counting money by the Chanukah candle is not a question of *kedushah*, but of respect. The *mitzvah* must not be treated as a backdrop for mundane activity.

### Reconciling Sources

There is considerable discussion among the *Rishonim* about how to understand this passage and how it relates to the earlier *sugya* on whether one may *hishtamesh le-orah* — derive benefit from the light of *ner Chanukah*. The two discussions appear side by side without the Gemara explicitly connecting them, and the *Rishonim* disagree sharply about how to integrate them.

The *Ba'al HaMaor* takes the following approach. The earlier discussion — whether one may derive benefit from the light — operated according to the view that there is a quasi-*kedushah* to the Chanukah candle, at least in a rabbinic sense: since the *mitzvah* is modeled on the lighting of the *Menorah* in the *Mikdash*, the rabbis treated the candle as if it had borrowed a dimension of the *kedushah* of the *Menorah's* light, and on that basis one should not derive *hana'ah* from it. That was the concern of the earlier passage. The present passage, however, operates on the assumption that there is no such *kedushah* — and therefore generally speaking one may make use of the light. The distinct concern here is that counting money, a particularly mundane and undignified act, constitutes *bizuy mitzvah* — denigration of the *mitzvah* — and is therefore prohibited. According to the *Ba'al HaMaor*, the *halacha* is thus: no formal prohibition on using the light for legitimate purposes, but using it for crass mundane activity like counting money is denigrating and is forbidden.

The Ramban inverts the analysis. He argues that the earlier prohibition on *hishtamesh le-orah* — according to Rashi — was concerned with sustained use of the candle's light for personal purposes, since this would undermine *pirsumei nisa* by making it appear as though the candle was lit for practical illumination. Brief and incidental use, such as glancing at a coin for a moment, should not raise the same concern. It ought to be permitted. Yet the Gemara forbids even this. The Ramban therefore concludes that the answer invoking *bizuy mitzvah* is not merely identifying a denigrating act — after all, counting coins is hardly comparable in its crudeness to kicking dirt over blood — but rather establishing a legal framework: in order to institutionalize the prohibition against denigrating *mitzvah* objects, the rabbis treat the candle *as if* it has *kedushah*, which then generates a blanket prohibition on any use whatsoever. The invocation of *kedushah* is thus not a description of the candle's intrinsic status but a legal mechanism for enforcing *bizuy mitzvah* prevention.

In practical terms: according to the *Ba'al HaMaor*, the prohibition is specifically against degrading, mundane uses, while permissible uses — including fulfillment of other *mitzvot* or even having one's Shabbat meal by its light — remain allowed. According to the Ramban, the prohibition is absolute during the time the candle is burning, precisely because we institutionalize the *bizuy* concern by treating it as *kedushah*-bearing — and therefore any use is forbidden.

## Sukkah Decoration Use

### Using Decorations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַהוּ לְהִסְתַּפֵּק מִנּוֹיֵי סוּכָּה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ אָסוּר לְהַרְצוֹת מָעוֹת כְּנֶגֶד נֵר חֲנוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that they raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: What is the halakha with regard to using decorations of a sukka all seven days of the festival of Sukkot? He said to them: They already said in a similar vein that it is prohibited to count money opposite the Hanukkah light, which proves that one may not use the object of a mitzva for another purpose.

The Gemara now raises a related question directed to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *noi sukkah mahu lehistapek mimeno kol shiva?* — may one make use of the decorations of the *sukkah* during the seven days of Sukkot? Specifically, if one has hung edible fruits and other foods as *noi sukkah*, may one pluck them down and eat them if one is hungry? The response given was: just as one may not count money by *ner Chanukah*, so too one may not divert *noi sukkah* from its purpose.

### Tanna vs Amora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מָרֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם! תָּלֵי תַּנְיָא בִּדְלָא תַּנְיָא. סוּכָּה תַּנְיָא, חֲנוּכָּה לָא תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef replied in surprise: Master of Abraham! He makes that which was taught dependent upon that which was not taught. As, with regard to sukka, the prohibition to enjoy use of its decorations was taught in a baraita, and the prohibition to enjoy use of the Hanukkah lights was not taught in a baraita at all.

Rav Yosef objects vigorously: *mari d'Avraham! tali tanya bidla tanya* — God of Abraham! You are making a *Tannaitic* source dependent on a non-*Tannaitic* source. The ruling about *noi sukkah* is derived from a *Baraita* — a *Tannaitic* source. The ruling about not counting money by *ner Chanukah* is an *Amoraic* ruling. One cannot reason from the Chanukah case to establish the *sukkah* case; if anything, the direction of reasoning should be the reverse — we know the Chanukah ruling from the *sukkah* precedent, not the *sukkah* ruling from Chanukah.

### Baraita Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: סִכְּכָהּ כְּהִלְכָתָהּ וְעִיטְּרָהּ בִּקְרָמִים וּבִסְדִינִין הַמְצוּיָּירִין, וְתָלָה בָּהּ אֱגוֹזִים, אֲפַרְסְקִין, שְׁקֵדִים וְרִמּוֹנִים, וּפַרְכִּילֵי עֲנָבִים וַעֲטָרוֹת שֶׁל שִׁבֳּלִים, יֵינוֹת, (שֶׁל) שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת — אָסוּר לְהִסְתַּפֵּק מֵהֶן עַד מוֹצָאֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג.
>
> **Translation:** As it was taught in a Tosefta in tractate Sukka: With regard to one who roofed the sukka in accordance with its halakhic requirements, and decorated it with colorful curtains and sheets, and hung in it ornamental nuts, peaches, almonds, and pomegranates, and grape branches, and wreaths of stalks of grain, wines, oils, and vessels full of flour, it is prohibited to use them until the conclusion of the last day of the Festival.

The *Baraita* reads as follows: *sichachah kehilchasah* — if one laid *sechach* properly — *ve'itra bekarmeyim, uvesadinin, umetzuyarin* — and then beautified it with colored sheets and decorated fabrics — *vetalah bah egozin, afarsekin, shkedim, verimonim, ufarkilei anavim, ve'ataros shel shibolin, yeinos, shemanim* — and hung from the *sechach* nuts, peaches, almonds, pomegranates, clusters of grapes, wreaths of grain, and containers of wine and oil — *assur lehistapek mehen kol shiva* — it is forbidden to make use of any of these throughout the seven days — *ad motzei Yom Tov ha'acharon shel Chag* — until after the conclusion of Shemini Atzeret. Since one may not use them during *bein hashmashos* at the end of Sukkot lest Sukkot still be in effect, one also cannot use them on Shemini Atzeret.

### Stipulation Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הִתְנָה עֲלֵיהֶן — הַכֹּל לְפִי תְּנָאוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** And if one stipulated at the outset that despite designating these items as noi sukkah one was not fully devoting them for that purpose and reserves the right to use them at any time — the stipulation is effective.

However, *ve'im hisna aleihen* — if one stipulated at the outset that despite designating these items as *noi sukkah* one was not fully devoting them for that purpose and reserves the right to use them at any time — *hakol lefi tena'o* — the stipulation is effective. The details of how such a *tenai* operates need not detain us here; the basic teaching is that the prohibition without a *tenai* is complete, but the restriction can be overridden by a prior declaration.

### Blood Root

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲבוּהוֹן דְּכוּלְּהוּ דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef said: The paradigm of them all is blood.

Rav Yosef then draws together the threads of this discussion. *Avohon dekulhu dam* — the root, the father of all these rulings, is the case of blood. From the requirement to cover blood properly — and specifically from the prohibition against kicking dirt over it with one's foot — we derive the fundamental principle that *mitzvot* must be treated with dignity. From that root principle flows the teaching about *ner Chanukah* and the teaching about *noi sukkah*. It is worth noting that these three cases are not precisely parallel: kicking dirt over blood is an affirmatively crude act; counting money by a candle is mundane and dismissive; and diverting *noi sukkah* from its *mitzvah* purpose actually removes it from its function entirely — something not true in the other cases. The *Rishonim* are well aware of this complexity.

The *sukkah* case is in fact further distinguished by additional legal features. The *sechach* and *noi sechach* are *muktza* on *Yom Tov*; even an object that falls off the *sukkah* may not be handled. Moreover, a verse — *chag hasukkot ta'aseh lecha* — is interpreted to mean that *shem shamayim*, God's name, descends upon the *sechach*, conferring upon it a dimension of genuine *kedushah*. And according to several *Gemarot*, this *kedushah* extends to the *noi sukkah* as well. The *Rishonim* therefore wrestle with whether the prohibition on *noi sukkah* is about *bizuy mitzvah* or about intrinsic *kedushah* — or some combination of both. The Ramban's answer, as noted earlier, is that the mechanism of *kedushah* is itself the vehicle for institutionalizing *bizuy mitzvah* prevention.

What unites all these cases — *ner Chanukah*, *noi sukkah*, *lulav*, *esrog*, *sechach*, *tzitzit* — is that during the time they are actively fulfilling their *mitzvah* function, they are subject either to *bizuy mitzvah* restrictions or to something approaching *kedushah*. Once the *mitzvah* period ends, however, that status lapses. This is the category of *tashmishei kedushah* — objects used *for* a *mitzvah*, as distinct from objects upon which *shem Hashem* is written, such as *tefillin* and *mezuzah*. A *lulav* after Sukkot, a *ner Chanukah* after it has burned out — these may be used for other purposes or respectfully discarded. The defining characteristic is temporal: the restrictions apply *during* the *mitzvah* period, and there they apply in two dimensions: do not divert the object from its *mitzvah* purpose, and do not use it in a manner that is denigrating. Once the *mitzvah* time has passed, neither constraint applies.

## Candle-to-Candle Lighting

### Rav's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב אָמַר אֵין מַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר מַדְלִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in a dispute between amoraim that Rav said: One may not light from one Hanukkah lamp to another lamp. And Shmuel said: One may light in that manner.

The Gemara now addresses a related question: *Rav amar ein madlikin mi-ner l'ner* — Rav rules that one may not light one Chanukah candle from another. This has obvious implications for the function of the *shamash*: before, the *shamash* was discussed in the context of making it clear that one is not deriving personal benefit from the Chanukah lights. Here a separate concern arises — if one may not light from one Chanukah candle to the next, one needs another implement to accomplish the lighting.

### Shmuel's Allowance

*Shmuel amar madlikin* — Shmuel rules that one may light from one candle to the next. Once the first candle is lit, it may serve to kindle the others.

### Three Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר אֵין מַתִּירִין צִיצִית מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד. רַב אָמַר אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְרִירָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְרִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional disputes between Rav and Shmuel. Rav said: One may not untie ritual fringes from one garment in order to affix them to another garment. And Shmuel said: One may untie them from one garment and affix them to another garment. And Rav said: The halakha is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon in the case of dragging, as Rabbi Shimon permitted dragging objects on Shabbat, even if, as a result, a furrow would be dug in the ground. Shmuel said that the halakha is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon in the case of dragging.

The Gemara presents these positions as part of a cluster of three debates between Rav and Shmuel in which the general rule — that we follow Rav in disputes with Shmuel — is overridden, and the *halacha* follows Shmuel in all three. Abaye testifies: *kol mili d'mar Rabba avid k'Rav, l'bar mihani tlata d'avid k'Shmuel* — in all matters, Rabba, Abaye's teacher, ruled like Rav, except for these three cases where he sided with Shmuel.

The first is the case before us: *madlikin mi-ner l'ner* — one may light from one Chanukah candle to another. The second: *matirin mi-beged l'beged* — one may remove *tzitzit* from one garment and transfer it to another, even while the first garment remains usable. Rav had prohibited this on the grounds that one is removing the *mitzvah* status from the first garment even if the goal is to enable a *mitzvah* on another garment. Shmuel permits it, and Rabba — and by extension the *halacha* — sides with Shmuel. The third: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon b'gerira* — we rule like Rabbi Shimon regarding dragging furniture on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: גּוֹרֵר אָדָם מִטָּה כִּסֵּא וְסַפְסָל — וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לַעֲשׂוֹת חָרִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** As it was taught in a baraita, Rabbi Shimon says: A person may drag a bed, chair, and bench on the ground, as long as he does not intend to make a furrow in the ground.

This third dispute requires context. The *Mishnah* discusses dragging a bed, chair, or bench across a dirt floor on Shabbat. Doing so might inadvertently create a furrow, which would constitute a *melacha* — either the beginning of plowing or an act of construction. Rabbi Shimon holds: *gorer adam mita kisse v'safsal, u'bilvad shelo yitkaven la'asot haritz* — one may drag them, provided one does not intend to create a furrow. This is the principle of *aino mitkaven* — an unintended act that may or may not occur does not render the original action prohibited. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, holding that since the furrow might result, the action is forbidden. Rav ruled like Rabbi Yehuda; Shmuel ruled like Rabbi Shimon, and the *halacha* follows Shmuel. The practical importance of this ruling can scarcely be overstated: if *aino mitkaven* were prohibited, the restrictions of Shabbat would be vastly more burdensome — one could not walk through mud, snow, or soft earth, among countless other everyday situations.

## Rav's Reasoning

### Bizuy Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: טַעְמָא דְרַב מִשּׁוּם בִּיזּוּי מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages sat before Rav Adda bar Ahava, and he sat and said: The reason for the opinion of Rav, who prohibited lighting from one Hanukkah lamp to another, is due to contempt for the mitzva.

Why does Rav prohibit lighting one Chanukah candle from another? A rabbinic student sitting before Rav Adda bar Ahava offered the explanation: *ta'ama d'Rav mishum bizuy mitzvah* — the reason of Rav is *bizuy mitzvah*, the lighting being insufficiently respectful toward the *mitzvah*. This might seem puzzling — why would using one Chanukah candle to light another be disrespectful?

### Machish Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא תְּצִיתוּ לֵיהּ, טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַב מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַכְחִישׁ מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** He said to them: Do not listen to him, as the reason for Rav's opinion is because he thereby weakens the mitzva.

Rav Adda bar Ahava rejected this explanation sharply, telling the other rabbis present: *lo titzitu lei* — do not pay attention to him. *Ta'ama d'Rav mishum d'ka machish mitzvah* — the reason of Rav is that it looks as though one is *depleting* the *mitzvah*. Literally the candle loses nothing, but as Rashi explains, the visual impression of moving flame from one candle to another creates an appearance as though one is somehow drawing away from the first candle and diminishing it. The concern is the visual impression of depletion, not actual depletion.

### Direct vs Intermediate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּקָא מַדְלֵיק מִשְּׁרָגָא לִשְׁרָגָא: מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בִּיזּוּי מִצְוָה — מִשְּׁרָגָא לִשְׁרָגָא מַדְלֵיק. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אַכְחוֹשֵׁי מִצְוָה — מִשְּׁרָגָא לִשְׁרָגָא נָמֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** What is the practical difference between them? The practical difference between them is in a case where he lights directly from lamp to lamp, without using a wood chip or another lamp to light the second lamp. According to the one who said that Rav's reason is due to contempt for the mitzva, directly from lamp to lamp he may even light ab initio, as, by lighting another Hanukkah lamp, he does not thereby demean the sanctity of the mitzva because the second lamp is also a mitzva. According to the one who said that Rav's reason is because he weakens the mitzva, lighting directly from lamp to lamp is also prohibited, as ultimately, he utilizes the mitzva lamp for a task that he could have accomplished with a non-sacred lamp.

The Gemara then identifies the practical difference between these two explanations. *Ika beinaihu d'ka madlik mishraga l'shraga* — the case in which one goes directly from one candle to the other. If the reason for Rav's prohibition is *bizuy mitzvah*, then going directly from one Chanukah candle to the next is actually *permitted* — for there is no disrespect when one *mitzvah* object is used directly to perform another *mitzvah* act. What would be denigrating is using an intermediary — a match or a sliver of wood — because the immediate act of applying the match to a burning Chanukah candle is not itself an act of *mitzvah*; it is a preliminary action, and using a Chanukah candle to fuel a mundane preliminary step is *bizuy mitzvah*. But if one goes directly, the burning candle is immediately and entirely in service of a *mitzvah* act.

If, however, the concern is *machish mitzvah* — the appearance of depleting the *mitzvah* — then even going directly from one candle to the other is forbidden, because the visual impression of drawing light away from one candle and giving it to another is precisely the problem regardless of whether an intermediary is used. In that case, neither direct nor indirect lighting from one Chanukah candle to another is permitted, and one must use a *shamash* instead.

## Ma'aser Sheni Analogy

### Coin Counterweight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אַוְיָא: סֶלַע שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי אֵין שׁוֹקְלִין כְּנֶגְדּוֹ דִּנָרֵי זָהָב, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְחַלֵּל עָלָיו מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Avya raised an objection from that which was taught in a Tosefta: A sela of the second tithe, one may not weigh gold dinars with it in order to determine their precise weight. And doing so is prohibited even if he is weighing the coin in order to redeem other second-tithe produce with it, as one may not derive benefit from tithe money.

Rav Avya raises a challenge from the laws of *ma'aser sheni*. In the third and sixth years of the sabbatical cycle, a *ma'aser sheni* — second tithe — would have to be brought to Jerusalem. Since transporting large quantities of produce could be difficult, one could redeem the *ma'aser sheni* onto coins, which then acquired *kedushat ma'aser sheni*, and bring those coins to Jerusalem instead. Now: *ein shoklin kenegdo dinarei zahav* — one may not use such a coin as a counterweight on a balance scale to measure out gold *dinarim*. That would be using an object of genuine *kedushah* for a mundane purpose.

More strikingly, the prohibition extends even to a case in which the weighed gold coins will themselves be used to redeem other *ma'aser sheni*: *ve'afilu lechalel alav ma'aser sheni acher* — even if the purpose of the weighing is to redeem additional *ma'aser sheni*, the use of the *ma'aser sheni* coin as a counterweight is forbidden. This case is closely analogous to the question of lighting *ner Chanukah* through an intermediary: in both situations, the object of concern is being used as an intermediate step toward a valid and even laudable purpose, but the intermediate use itself is problematic.

Rav Avya's argument is as follows: if Rav and Shmuel debate only about going directly from one candle to the next — with both agreeing that using an intermediary is forbidden — then the *ma'aser sheni* case presents no contradiction, since there too using an intermediary toward a *mitzvah* goal is forbidden. But if Shmuel permits even the use of an intermediary — a sliver of wood placed between the two Chanukah candles — then why is the analogous case of the *ma'aser sheni* coin forbidden even when the gold coins will be used for a *mitzvah* purpose?

### Risk of Failure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא לֹא יְכַוֵּין מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו וְקָא מַפֵּיק לְהוּ לְחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said: This is not difficult, as in the case of weighing tithe money the Sages prohibited doing so as a decree lest the weights not be precisely equal. One will discover that the weight of the gold dinars is not equal to the weight of the sela that he used to weigh them, and he will reconsider and render them unsanctified, i.e., they will maintain their original, non-sacred status.

Rava answers: *gezira shema lo yechawein mishkolosav vechamapki lehu lechullin* — it is a rabbinic decree against the possibility that the weights may not come out correctly. If one places gold coins on the scale and they do not equal the needed amount, one may abandon the whole enterprise and put those coins to entirely non-*mitzvah* use. Because there is a realistic probability that the process will fail and the coins will never actually go toward *ma'aser sheni* redemption, we cannot rely on the stated intention. The intermediary in that case is too unreliable.

In the case of the *shamash* or a sliver of wood used to light the next Chanukah candle, while in theory the match might blow out, the likelihood that the process will succeed is high enough that we do not impose a comparable decree. Rava's answer thus distinguishes the two cases on the basis of the reliability of the intended outcome, and in doing so leaves open the possibility that Shmuel does indeed permit lighting through an intermediary — a question that remains somewhat unresolved.

## Temple Menorah

### Testimony & Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״מִחוּץ לְפָרוֹכֶת הָעֵדוּת יַעֲרוֹךְ״, וְכִי לְאוֹרָהּ הוּא צָרִיךְ? וַהֲלֹא כׇּל אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָלְכוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר לָא הָלְכוּ אֶלָּא לְאוֹרוֹ! אֶלָּא עֵדוּת הִיא לְבָאֵי עוֹלָם שֶׁהַשְּׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet raised an objection from that which was taught in a baraita. With regard to the Temple candelabrum, it is stated: "Outside the veil of the testimony, in the Tent of Meeting, shall Aaron order it from evening to morning before the Lord continually; it shall be a statute forever throughout your generations" (Leviticus 24:3). It must be understood: And does God require its light for illumination at night? Didn't the children of Israel, all forty years that they walked in the wilderness, walk exclusively by His light, the pillar of fire? Rather, the lighting of the candelabrum is testimony to mankind that the Divine Presence rests among Israel.

The Gemara introduces the *Menorah* into the discussion of *ner Chanukah*, at least by way of analogy. A verse states: *michutz laparoches ha'eidus yaaroch* — outside the curtain of the *Edut*, Aaron shall arrange the lamps. The Gemara asks: does God need the light of the *Menorah*? And further: for forty years in the desert, *Bnei Yisrael* walked by God's light — they needed God's light, not the other way around. So why light the *Menorah*? *Ela edut hi l'va'ei olam shehashchina shara b'Yisrael* — it is testimony to all who enter the world that the *Shechinah* dwells within Israel.

This is a striking passage, and it connects directly to the theological dimension of Chanukah developed earlier. The *Chazal* understood the *Menorah's* light as a symbol of God's presence. When the Chashmonaim rededicated the *Mikdash*, the deeper significance of that act was not merely the physical cleaning and restoration, but the removal of the spiritual *tumah* introduced by the Greeks and their idols — and the re-establishment of the *Mikdash* as a place where *Shechinah* could once again dwell. When the *Mishkan* was first dedicated, fire descended from heaven as a sign of divine acceptance. At Chanukah, the sign was different: the miracle of the oil. To understand what the *Menorah's* light represents — testimony to divine presence — is to understand why that particular *nes* was the vehicle for divine confirmation that the rededication had succeeded. It was not simply a wondrous event; it was God demonstrating that He was once again dwelling His presence in the *Mikdash*. This, as the Maharal explains, is the inner meaning of *nes Chanukah*, and the Gemara here, while not spelling out that connection explicitly, provides the theological foundation for it.

### Ner Maaravi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עֵדוּת? אָמַר רַב: זוֹ נֵר מַעֲרָבִי שֶׁנּוֹתֵן בָּהּ שֶׁמֶן כְּמִדַּת חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ, וּמִמֶּנָּה הָיָה מַדְלִיק וּבָהּ הָיָה מְסַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** What is this testimony? Rav said: That is the westernmost lamp in the candelabrum in which the measure of oil placed was the same measure of oil as was placed in the other lamps, and nevertheless he would light the others from it each day and with it he would conclude, i.e., the westernmost lamp would continue burning throughout the day after all the others were extinguished.

Rav then identifies the specific form this testimony took: *zo ner maaravi* — it is the western lamp. The *ner maaravi* was one branch of the *Menorah* — most commonly understood to be the central lamp, oriented toward the *Kodesh HaKodashim* — and it received the same measure of oil as all the other lamps. Yet two things distinguished it: first, *u-mimena hayah madlik* — they would light the other lamps from it; and second, *u-vah hayah mesayim* — it was the last to be cleaned out and refilled.

The miracle was this: each night, all the lamps were filled with oil and lit. By morning, some or all of the outer lamps would have gone out — particularly on summer nights when there was less darkness to fill. The *ner maaravi*, however, burned on. By a continuous daily miracle, it lasted a full twenty-four hours, still burning when nighttime arrived again. During the day, the other lamps would be cleaned out and refilled; the *ner maaravi* was left burning until the end of the day, at which point it alone was cleaned out, given new oil and a new wick. Then that newly prepared *ner maaravi* was used to light all the other lamps, and the cycle began again. This daily miracle was the testimony — the ongoing demonstration that the *Shechinah* dwelt within Israel.

It should be noted that a certain *Baraita* records that this miracle did not persist for the entire period of the *Mikdash*; there were times when the miracle ceased and the fire of the *mizbeach* had to be used to relight the *Menorah*. But during the period when it obtained, it constituted a daily, visible sign of divine presence.

### Long Wicks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא כֵּיוָן דִּקְבִיעִי נֵרוֹת, לָא סַגְיָא דְּלָא מִשְׁקָל וְאַדְלוֹקֵי. קַשְׁיָא בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בִּזּוּי מִצְוָה, וּבֵין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אַכְחוֹשֵׁי מִצְוָה!
>
> **Translation:** But isn't it true that here, in the Temple, since the lamps were fixed in the candelabrum, it was impossible to light directly from lamp to lamp? There was no alternative to taking a wood chip and lighting the rest of the lamps from the westernmost lamp. Consequently, it is difficult both according to the one who said that one may not light from lamp to lamp due to contempt for the mitzva and according to the one who said that one may not light from lamp to lamp due to weakening the mitzva.

The relevance of the *ner maaravi* to our *halachic* discussion about lighting *ner Chanukah* from one candle to the next is this: the practice in the *Mikdash* was to light the other lamps *from* the *ner maaravi*, which would seem to permit the very act Rav prohibits — using one consecrated lamp to light another. Rav Pappa resolves the difficulty: *targumah b'fitilot ha'aruchot* — they used very long wicks. The wick of the *ner maaravi* was extended all the way to the adjacent lamp and lit directly, without any separation. This resolves the case according to the view that direct lighting is permitted — one need not say there was any violation of the rule.

## Menorah Resolution

### Lighting vs Placing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב פָּפָּא בִּפְתִילוֹת אֲרוּכּוֹת. סוֹף סוֹף, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אַכְחוֹשֵׁי מִצְוָה קַשְׁיָא! — קַשְׁיָא. מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: חָזֵינַן, אִי הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה — מַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר. וְאִי הַנָּחָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה — אֵין מַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa explained that it need not necessarily be understood that way. Rather, there were long wicks in the candelabrum, which made it possible to reach and light directly from one lamp to another. However, ultimately, according to the one who said that one may not light from lamp to lamp due to weakening the mitzva, it is difficult. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, the question remains difficult. In summary, the Gemara asks: What is the halakhic conclusion reached about this matter in terms of lighting from lamp to lamp? Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yehoshua, said: We see; if the halakha is in accordance with the opinion of the one who said that kindling the Hanukkah light accomplishes the mitzva and the rest is secondary, one may light from lamp to lamp. The lighting itself is the essence of the mitzva of Hanukkah lights. And if the halakha is in accordance with the opinion of the one who said that placing the lit lamp in a suitable place accomplishes the mitzva, then one may not light from lamp to lamp. According to that opinion, lighting is simply an auxiliary action that facilitates the fulfillment of the essence of the mitzva, which is placing the lamp in a place where its light can be seen by the public.

The question raised regarding the *ner maaravi* — that the *Menorah* lamps were lit from each other — remains difficult according to at least one position. The Gemara therefore introduces a broader conceptual debate that reframes the entire question. Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua asks: *ee hadlakah oseh mitzvah, madlikin mi-ner l'ner* — if the act of *mitzvah* is fulfilled at the moment of lighting, then lighting from one candle to the next is permitted, because the act is itself a *ma'aseh mitzvah* and cannot be called *bizuy mitzvah* or *machish mitzvah*. *Ee hanacha oseh mitzvah, ein madlikin mi-ner l'ner* — but if the *mitzvah* is fulfilled not through the act of lighting but through the act of placing the candle in its proper position, then the act of lighting is merely preparatory, and using one Chanukah candle to kindle another is an inappropriate use of a *mitzvah* object for a non-*mitzvah* act.

## Mitzvah Essence

### Held Candle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה, אוֹ הַנָּחָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הָיָה תָּפוּשׂ נֵר חֲנוּכָּה וְעוֹמֵד — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** After the issue of whether lighting accomplishes the mitzva or placing accomplishes the mitzva was raised in the context of the previous discussion, the Gemara cites the discussion in its entirety. As a dilemma was raised before the Sages: In the case of the Hanukkah light, does lighting accomplish the mitzva, and placing the lit lamp is simply a continuation of that action, or does placing the kindled lamp accomplish the mitzva, and lighting is simply a practical necessity that facilitates placing the lamp? The Gemara suggests: Come and hear a solution to this dilemma from that which Rava said: One who was holding a burning Hanukkah lamp in his hand and standing, he did nothing in terms of fulfilling the mitzva.

The Gemara now brings cases to test which of the two positions is correct. Rava rules: *haya tofus ner Chanukah v'omed lo asah v'lo klum* — if one held the Chanukah candle in one's hand while lighting it, and remained standing with it lit in hand, one has not fulfilled the *mitzvah*. At first glance, this seems to support *hanacha*: the candle was lit but never placed down, and therefore the *mitzvah* was not achieved.

### Flashlight Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַנָּחָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה! הָתָם, הָרוֹאֶה אוֹמֵר לְצוֹרְכּוֹ הוּא דְּנָקֵיט לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Conclude from this that placing accomplishes the mitzva. Until he sets the lamp down in its appropriate place, he did not fulfill the mitzva. The Gemara rejects this: There, they said that he did not fulfill his obligation for a different reason. One who sees it will say that he is not holding the lamp in order to fulfill the mitzva, but he is holding it for his own needs. Since holding the lamp can mislead onlookers, he does not fulfill the mitzva in that manner.

The Gemara immediately rejects that inference. *Hasam haro'eh omer l'tzarcho hu d'nakit lei* — there, the problem is that an observer would say: he is holding it for his own purposes. A person who carries a lit candle around in their hand appears to be using it as a flashlight — moving through a dark space by its light. Such a candle does not broadcast *pirsumei nisa*; its purpose is ambiguous at best. The disqualification therefore has nothing to do with whether *hanacha* is the operative *mitzvah* act; it is because the act of lighting, while held, is not a meaningful act of *mitzvah* lighting at all, since it cannot serve the purpose of *pirsumei nisa* in that configuration.

### Indoor Lighting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הִדְלִיקָהּ בִּפְנִים וְהוֹצִיאָהּ — לֹא עָשָׂה כְּלוּם. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה — הַדְלָקָה בִּמְקוֹמוֹ בָּעֵינַן, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא עָשָׂה כְּלוּם. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ הַנָּחָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה, אַמַּאי לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם? הָתָם נָמֵי: הָרוֹאֶה הוּא אוֹמֵר לְצוֹרְכּוֹ הוּא דְּאַדְלְקַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Come and hear another resolution for this dilemma from that which Rava said: One who lights the Hanukkah lamp inside the house and then takes it out and places it at the entrance to his house did nothing in terms of fulfilling the mitzva. Granted, if you say that lighting accomplishes the mitzva it is understandable, as lighting in its place is required. That is why Rava ruled that he did nothing in terms of fulfilling the mitzva. However, if you say that placing accomplishes the mitzva, why did Rava rule that he did nothing? Didn't he set it down in its appropriate place? The Gemara answers: There too, even though he subsequently brought it outside, one who sees him lighting inside will say to himself that he is lighting the lamp for his own needs and not in fulfillment of the mitzva.

The Gemara tries again. Rava rules: *hidlikah bifnim v'hotzia lo asah v'lo klum* — if one lit the candle indoors and then carried it outside to the doorway — recall that the *mitzvah* requires placing the candle at the outer doorway — one has not fulfilled the *mitzvah*. This seems to support *hadlakah*: if *hanacha* were the operative act, then placing the lit candle outside after having lit it inside should be perfectly valid — one performed the lighting (preparatory), and then performed the placing (the *mitzvah*). Yet the ruling is that one has not fulfilled the obligation. *Ee amrat bishlama hadlakah oseh mitzvah, hadlakah bimkomah bainan* — if the *mitzvah* is the act of lighting, this is readily explained: the lighting must occur at the proper location, and lighting indoors does not fulfill that requirement.

But then *ela ee amrat hanacha oseh mitzvah, amai lo asah v'lo klum?* — if *hanacha* is the operative act, why is this invalid? One lit it inside and then placed it outside — the *hanacha* occurred in the right place. The Gemara answers: *hasam nami haro'eh omer l'tzarcho hu d'adlakah* — there too, the problem is the observer. When one walks from indoors to outdoors carrying a lit candle, the passerby sees a person carrying a light and naturally assumes it is being used as a flashlight. The movement itself — the carrying — confuses the purpose and undermines *pirsumei nisa*. Accordingly, the lighting must occur outdoors and the placing must follow immediately and in place, so that neither the act of lighting nor the subsequent positioning of the candle can be mistaken for ordinary domestic use of a light source.
