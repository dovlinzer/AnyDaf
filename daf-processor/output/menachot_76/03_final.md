# Menachot 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rubbings & Poundings

### 300 & 500 Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת טְעוּנוֹת שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת שִׁיפָה וַחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת בְּעִיטָה, שִׁיפָה וּבְעִיטָה בַּחִיטִּין. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בַּבָּצֵק. כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת בָּאוֹת עֶשֶׂר עֶשֶׂר, חוּץ מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים וַחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהֵם בָּאוֹת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם בָּאוֹת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, חוּץ מֵחַלּוֹת תּוֹדָה וּנְזִירוּת שֶׁהֵן בָּאוֹת עֶשֶׂר עֶשֶׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the meal offerings require rubbing three hundred** times **and striking five hundred** times with one's fist or palm. **Rubbing and striking** are performed **on the wheat** kernels to remove their husks prior to grinding them into flour. **And Rabbi Yosei says:** They are performed **on the dough** to ensure a smooth product. **All of the meal offerings come** as **ten** loaves or **ten** wafers from each one tenth of an ephah of flour, **except for the shewbread and the griddle-cake** offering **of the High Priest, which come** as **twelve** loaves or wafers; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: They all come** as **twelve** loaves **except for** the four types of **loaves** that accompany **the thanks offering and** the two types of loaves that accompany the peace offering of **naziriteship, which come** as **ten each.**

The daf opens with the Mishnah's statement: *Kol hamenachot te'unot shalosh me'ot shifa vachamesh me'ot be'ita* — all of the menachot require 300 rubbings and 500 poundings. These are striking numbers. The rubbings are presumably performed on the wheat kernels in order to loosen the shells and bran, while the poundings — Rashi understands the term *be'ita* as striking with the fist — are performed as part of the physical preparation of the offering. The word *be'ita* is related to the word for kicking with a leg, but Rashi, based on context, applies it to pounding with the fist.

### Tanna Kamma vs. R. Yosi

*[Continued from above]*

The Mishnah continues: *shifa u've'ita bechitim* — the rubbing and the pounding were both performed with the wheat. It is not entirely clear how pounding the wheat kernels differs from grinding them; one might have expected the process to involve a millstone rather than a mortar-and-pestle style pounding. The suggestion is that the pounding loosens the grain and prepares it for subsequent processing. Whatever the precise physical mechanics, the Tanna Kamma locates both actions at the stage of the wheat.

### Ten vs. Twelve Loaves

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *babatze'ik* — it was with the dough. For Rabbi Yosi, the pounding in particular makes considerably more sense as an action performed on dough, since one rolls and pounds dough as a natural part of its preparation. The rubbing of dough is less intuitive but still plausible.

### Alternating Pattern

*[Continued from above]*

What is especially striking about this dispute is its epistemological character. Neither the Tanna Kamma nor Rabbi Yosi appears to be basing himself on a *pasuk* or on a first-hand memory of how the procedure was actually performed. They had inherited a tradition — the earlier, apodictic Mishnah statement that all menachot require 300 rubbings and 500 poundings — and they are attempting to reconstruct its context. Was that tradition speaking about the wheat or the dough? That is the question they are debating.

This reflects a broader phenomenon well attested in Mishnaic literature. Scholars have observed that the later Tannaim were frequently the inheritors of earlier, terse Mishnah traditions and that what appears in a Mishnah as a tannaitic dispute is often, in reality, commentary and interpretation of an older, undisputed statement. A clear parallel is the first Mishnah in Gittin, where the global statement about bringing a *get* from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael* is followed by several Tannaim each explaining what *chutz* means — each interpreting the original line rather than independently framing a new debate. The same structure is present here: the apodictic statement — 300 rubbings and 500 poundings — was the tradition, and the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yosi are each explaining what context that tradition referred to.

### Back & Forth Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: שָׁף אַחַת, בּוֹעֵט שְׁתַּיִם. שָׁף שְׁתַּיִם, בּוֹעֵט שָׁלֹשׁ. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אַמְטוֹיֵי וְאֵתוֹיֵי חַד, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַמְטוֹיֵי וְאֵתוֹיֵי תְּרֵי? מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita* that the rubbing and striking are done in the following manner: He **rubs once** and **strikes twice.** Then he **rubs twice** and **strikes three** times. This sequence is repeated one hundred times, so that he rubs three hundred times and strikes five hundred times. **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma** with regard to the rubbing: Is the rubbing of the hand **back and forth** over the surface of the item considered **one** rubbing, **or** is **perhaps** rubbing **back and forth** considered **two** distinct rubbings? **What** is the correct count? The Gemara states: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

A Braita then explains how one practically maintains the three-to-five ratio of rubbings to poundings. Rather than performing all 300 rubbings followed by all 500 poundings, the practitioner alternates: *shaf achat bo'et shtayim* — one rubbing followed by two poundings; then *shaf shtayim bo'et shalosh* — two rubbings followed by three poundings. In this way, the ratio of three to five is preserved throughout the process. According to the Tanna Kamma, this alternating sequence is how one would loosen the shell and bran from the wheat kernels.

### R. Yosi on Dough

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Yirmiya raises a question about how to count the rubbings. When one performs a rubbing, the natural motion is back and forth. Does that back-and-forth movement count as one rubbing or as two? — *amtuyei ve'atuyei chad*, back and forth is one, *o dilma amtuyei ve'atuyei trei*, back and forth is two? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. This question recurs in other contexts in the Gemara where actions performed back and forth must be counted a fixed number of times.

### Mixed Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיפָה וּבְעִיטָה בַּחִיטִּין, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בַּבָּצֵק. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּבָּצֵק וְלֹא בַּחִיטִּין, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַף בַּבָּצֵק? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: שִׁיפָה וּבְעִיטָה בַּחִיטִּין, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שִׁיפָה וּבְעִיטָה בַּבָּצֵק.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rubbing and striking** are performed **on the wheat** kernels, while **Rabbi Yosei says:** They are performed **on the dough.** The Gemara comments: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does Rabbi Yosei mean that these actions are performed **on the dough and not on the wheat** kernels? **Or perhaps** he means that they are performed not only on the kernels but **also on the dough.** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rubbing and striking** are performed **on the wheat** kernels. **Rabbi Yosei says: Rubbing and striking** are performed **on the dough.**

The Gemara also raises the question of whether Rabbi Yosi intends to say that the rubbing and pounding were performed *only* with the dough and not at all with the wheat, or whether he agrees that they were also performed with the wheat and is merely adding that they were performed with the dough as well. A Braita resolves this: Rabbi Yosi's repetition of the verbs *shifa u've'ita* in his statement — rather than simply saying "also with the dough" — implies that the rubbing and pounding belonged to the dough alone, not to the wheat.

However, the left-hand column of the standard printed edition notes a different *girsa*: *shifa bechitim u've'ita babatze'ik* — the rubbing is with the wheat, and the pounding is with the dough. A note indicates *vechein mashma baRambam* — this is also the implication of the Rambam. This third reading is arguably the most logically intuitive of all: rubbing is a natural action to perform on wheat kernels to separate the shell, while pounding is a natural action to perform on dough during its preparation. Yet notably, no one derives their position from a *pasuk* or from any remembered practice; all positions are reconstructions of a shared older tradition.

## Model Selection Debate

### Paradigm Question

*[Continued from above]*

The Mishnah continues: *kol hamenachot ba'ot eser eser chutz milechem hapanim* — all of the menachot come in ten loaves, except for the *Lechem HaPanim*, which explicitly comprised twelve loaves arranged in two columns of six on the shulchan, and the chavitei Kohen Gadol — the daily minchat chavitin that the Kohen Gadol brought half in the morning and half in the afternoon — which also come in twelve. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### R. Yehuda: Ten

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Meir takes the opposite position: *kulam ba'ot shtem esrei* — all of the menachot come in twelve loaves, except for the chalot Toda and the breads accompanying the Nazir offering, which come in ten. The dispute is, in essence, about which is the general rule and which is the exception: is the paradigm ten loaves with twelve as the anomaly, or is the paradigm twelve with ten as the anomaly?

### Mincha & Toda Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת בָּאוֹת כּוּ׳. שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, **all of the meal offerings come** as ten loaves or wafers, except for the shewbread and the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which come as **twelve** loaves or wafers. The Gemara examines the sources for these exceptions: With regard to **the shewbread, it is written explicitly of it:** "And you shall take fine flour and bake twelve cakes of it" (Leviticus 24:5).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – אָתְיָא ״חֻקָּה״ ״חֻקָּה״ מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** That the **griddle-cake** offering **of the High Priest** is also offered as twelve units is **derived from** a verbal analogy of the word "obligation," written in the context of the verse discussing the **shewbread.** With regard to the griddle-cake offering, the verse states: "And the anointed priest…shall offer it; it is an **obligation** forever" (Leviticus 6:15), and the verse states with regard to the twelve loaves of the shewbread: "And it shall be for Aaron and his sons…a perpetual **obligation"** (Leviticus 24:9).

The Gemara establishes first the basis for the chavitei Kohen Gadol being twelve. By the *Lechem HaPanim* the Torah uses the word *chok*: *kodesh kodashim hu lo me'ishei Hashem chok olam*. By the chavitei Kohen Gadol it says: *chok olam la-Hashem kalil tuktir*. The shared term *chok* constitutes a *gezeirah shavah* — *atya *chukah chukah* millechem hapanim* — from which we derive that just as the *Lechem HaPanim* came in twelve, so too the chavitei Kohen Gadol come in twelve.

### Lechem Hapanim Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת דְּבָאוֹת עֶשֶׂר עֶשֶׂר, מְנָלַן? גָּמַר מִלַּחְמֵי תוֹדָה – מָה לְהַלָּן עֶשֶׂר, אַף כָּאן עֶשֶׂר. וְלֵילַף מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים – מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, אַף כָּאן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Yehuda, who states in the mishna with regard to **all the meal offerings that they come** as **ten** loaves or **ten** wafers, **from where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that he **derives** it **from the loaves of the thanks offering. Just as there,** there are **ten** loaves, **so too here,** with regard to all other meal offerings, there are **ten** loaves. The Gemara challenges: **But let him derive it from the shewbread: Just as there,** there are **twelve** loaves, **so too here,** with regard to all other meal offerings, there must be **twelve.**

Having established twelve for the *Lechem HaPanim* and, through the *gezeirah shavah*, for the chavitei Kohen Gadol, the Gemara asks: *kol hamenachot de'ba'ot eser eser minalan* — what is the source for the general rule of ten? *Gamar milachmei Todah* — we derive it from the breads of the Todah offering. *Mah le'halan eser af kan eser* — just as each type of bread there came in ten loaves, so too here each type of bread comes in ten.

The source that lachmei Toda themselves came in ten is derived from the Torah's language. The Torah describes four types of bread accompanying the Todah — three types of matzah and one type of chametz — and says that one of each type is to be given to the Kohen as a *terumah*, a gift. The term *terumah* appears elsewhere in the context of *terumas ma'aser*, where the Levites give a tenth of their tithe to the Kohen, and there the Torah explicitly calls that tenth a *terumah*. From this, Chazal understood that the *terumah* separated from the Todah breads likewise represents one-tenth — meaning each type must have comprised ten loaves in order for one of them to constitute a *terumah*. Once the lachmei Toda are established as coming in ten, that number serves as the paradigm for all other menachot.

### Mincha & Lechem Comparis…

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara raises an obvious question: we have two available models — the lachmei Toda at ten and the *Lechem HaPanim* at twelve. Why does Rabbi Yehuda choose the lachmei Toda as the paradigm and treat twelve as the exception, rather than making the *Lechem HaPanim* the rule and treating ten as the exception? *Mah le'halan shteim esrei af kan shteim esrei* — why not derive that everything comes in twelve?

### Kohen Gadol Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִלַּחְמֵי תוֹדָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן יָחִיד, הַמִּתְנַדֵּב, שֶׁמֶן, נִפְסָל, שֶׁלֹּא בְּשַׁבָּת, וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It **stands to reason** that Rabbi Yehuda **should derive** the *halakha* of the other meal offerings **from the loaves of the thanks offering, as** the other meal offerings resemble the loaves of the thanks offering and differ from the shewbread in several respects: They are offered by **an individual** and not by the public; they are brought by **one who donates** and not as obligatory offerings; **oil** is used in their preparation; they are **disqualified** when left overnight, whereas, by contrast, the rite of the shewbread demands that it be left on the Table for eight days; they are **not** brought **on the Sabbath,** unlike the shewbread, whose rite is performed on the Sabbath; **and** they are **not** brought **in** a state of **ritual impurity,** unlike the shewbread, which is brought even in a state of impurity.

The Gemara answers: *mistavra milachmei Todah havei leih lemilaf* — it is more logical to derive from the lachmei Toda. The points of comparison between a normal mincha and the lachmei Toda, and the ways in which they differ from the *Lechem HaPanim*, are as follows. *Sheken yachid* — both a normal mincha and lachmei Toda come as an individual's korban, whereas the *Lechem HaPanim* is a communal offering of the tzibbur. *Hamitnadeiv* — both come through a voluntary vow, whereas the *Lechem HaPanim* is an obligatory offering. *Shemen* — both have oil, whereas the *Lechem HaPanim* is baked without oil and is plain bread. *Shelo b'Shabbat* — neither overrides Shabbat, whereas the *Lechem HaPanim*, being a communal and fixed-time offering, does. *Shelo b'tumah* — neither is brought in a state of tumah, again reflecting that both are personal and not fixed-time offerings, in contrast to the *Lechem HaPanim*.

### Kohen Gadol Similarities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּלְבוֹנָה, מַצָּה, וָעֶצֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **On the contrary,** Rabbi Yehuda **should derive** the *halakha* of the other meal offerings **from the shewbread, as** they resemble the shewbread in several respects: They are **consecrated** property of the most sacred order, whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are of lesser sanctity; **and** they are brought with **frankincense,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are not; they are brought only as **unleavened bread,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering include some leavened bread; **and** each is brought as an offering by **itself,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are brought together with an animal sacrifice.

The Gemara counters: *adrabah*, on the contrary — *milechem hapanim havei lei lemilaf* — one should derive from the *Lechem HaPanim* instead. Here are the ways in which a normal mincha resembles the *Lechem HaPanim* and differs from the lachmei Toda. *Sheken hekdesh* — both a normal mincha and the *Lechem HaPanim* are *kodesh kodashim*, eaten by the Kohanim within the *Beit HaMikdash*. The lachmei Toda, by contrast, accompanies a Todah animal which is a shelamim — *kodashim kalim* — and is therefore itself eaten as *kodashim kalim*, outside the Mikdash in Yerushalayim. This is the one case in which a bread brought to the *Beit HaMikdash* has the status of *kodashim kalim*. *Levonah* — both a normal mincha and the *Lechem HaPanim* have levonah, whereas the lachmei Toda, accompanying the animal offering, has none. *Matzah* — both are entirely matzah, whereas ten of the forty Todah loaves are chametz. *Ve'etzem* — both come as independent offerings in their own right, whereas the lachmei Toda is merely an accompaniment to the animal; the animal is the primary korban.

### Chosen Model Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **These** analogies between other meal offerings and the loaves of the thanks offering **are** more **numerous** than the analogies between other meal offerings and the shewbread. Therefore, the *halakha* of other meal offerings is derived from the loaves of the thanks offering.

The Gemara resolves the tension: *hanehu nefishin* — the first list has more points of comparison. The lachmei Toda model is supported by five points while the *Lechem HaPanim* model has four, and therefore the lachmei Toda serves as the paradigm.

### R. Meir Counters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סְבִירָא לַן דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב, נֵילַף מֵחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל: מָה לְהַלָּן – שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, אַף כָּאן – שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara posited previously that according to Rabbi Yehuda, a verbal analogy teaches that, like the shewbread, the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest is brought as twelve units. The Gemara asks: **And if we hold** that **a matter learned through a verbal analogy may subsequently teach as a paradigm, let us derive** the *halakha* of the other meal offerings **from the griddle-cake** offering **of the High Priest: Just as there,** the meal **offering** comprises **twelve** units, **so too here,** other meal offerings should consist of **twelve** units.

The Gemara now raises a more sophisticated challenge. The chavitei Kohen Gadol were learned from the *Lechem HaPanim* via the *gezeirah shavah* of *chukah-chukah*. The question is whether *davar halamed bigezeirah shavah chozer umelamed bibinyan av* — can something that was itself derived through a *gezeirah shavah* then be used as a paradigm to teach other cases through binyan av? If yes, then one need not use the *Lechem HaPanim* directly; one can use the chavitei Kohen Gadol — which is an individual's offering rather than a communal one — as the model, and its many points of comparison with a normal mincha would seem to make it a superior paradigm.

### R. Meir's Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִלַּחְמֵי תוֹדָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁהִתְנַדֵּב חֲצָאִין;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It **stands to reason** that Rabbi Yehuda **should derive** their *halakha* **from** the *halakha* of **the loaves of the thanks offering, as** the other meal offerings resemble the loaves of the thanks offering and differ from the griddle-cake offering in several respects: They are brought by **an ordinary** person and not by the High Priest; they are brought by **one who donates** and not as obligatory offerings; and they are only brought whole, while the High Priest's griddle-cake offering is brought in **halves,** half in the morning and half in the afternoon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיגּוּל, שֶׁלֹּא בְּשַׁבָּת, וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, they are disqualified **as *piggul*,** i.e., when they are sacrificed with the intent to be consumed after their appointed time, unlike the griddle-cake offering, which is not eaten at all; they are **not** brought **on the Sabbath,** unlike the griddle-cake offering; **and** they are **not** brought **in** a state of **ritual impurity,** whereas the griddle-cake offering is brought by the High Priest even while impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מֵחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן עִשָּׂרוֹן, כְּלִי הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּלְבוֹנָה;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **On the contrary,** Rabbi Yehuda **should have derived** the *halakha* of the other meal offerings **from the High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, **as** the other meal offerings resemble the griddle-cake offering in several respects: They both contain **a tenth** of an ephah of fine flour, whereas the loaves of the thanks offering contain several tenths of an ephah; they are consecrated when placed in a service **vessel,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are consecrated when the thanks offering is slaughtered; they are **consecrated** property of the most sacred order, whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are of lesser sanctity; **and** they are brought with **frankincense,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַצָּה וָעֶצֶם, הַגָּשָׁה וְאִישִּׁים, וְהָנֵי נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, they are brought only of **unleavened bread,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering include some leavened bread; **and** each is brought as an offering **itself,** whereas the loaves of the thanks offering are brought together with an animal sacrifice; part of their rite is **bringing** them **near** the southwest corner of the altar, unlike the loaves of the thanks offering; **and** parts or all of them are placed on **the fires of the altar,** unlike the loaves of the thanks offering. The Gemara comments: **And** indeed, **these** similarities between other meal offerings and the griddle-cake offering **are** more **numerous** than the similarities between other meal offerings and the loaves of the thanks offering. Therefore, it seems more appropriate to derive the number of loaves from the griddle-cake offering.

The Gemara spells out why the chavitei Kohen Gadol might be a better model than the lachmei Toda. *Sheken issaron* — both the chavitei Kohen Gadol and a normal mincha consist of a single issaron of flour, whereas a Todah brings a much larger quantity. *Kli* — both attain their full kedushah through a kli sharet, a sacred vessel, whereas the lachmei Toda attain their full kedushah only through the slaughtering of the accompanying animal, since the Todah is a shelamim and the slaughter of the animal functions as the act of haktrava for the bread. *Hekdesh* — both are *kodesh kodashim*, whereas the lachmei Toda is *kodashim kalim*. *Levonah* — both have levonah, as they are brought as independent offerings. *Matzah* — both are entirely matzah. *Ve'atzmam* — both come as independent offerings on their own account. *Hagasha* — both require the act of hagasha, touching the mincha to the mizbeach, a requirement that does not apply to lachmei Toda which merely accompanies an animal. *Ve'ishim* — portions of both are actually burned on the altar's fire, again unlike the lachmei Toda. This is a considerably longer list than what the lachmei Toda model offered, and the Gemara acknowledges: *vehanach nefishen* — these points are more numerous.

A separate dimension of comparison introduced here concerns *pigul*. Both a normal mincha and lachmei Toda are susceptible to pigul — the mincha through an improper thought during the taking of the kometz, and the lachmei Toda through an improper thought during the sprinkling of the blood of the accompanying animal, which serves as the *matir* for the bread. The chavitei Kohen Gadol, however, is entirely burned on the altar — it has no *matir* in the sense of one act permitting a separate item — and therefore the mechanism of pigul does not apply to it. Similarly, both a normal mincha and lachmei Toda are personal, non-fixed-time offerings and therefore do not override Shabbat or tumah, while the chavitei Kohen Gadol does.

### Shmuel's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶדְיוֹט מֵהֶדְיוֹט עֲדִיף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda **prefers** to learn the *halakha* of the other meal offerings, which are brought by **an ordinary** person, **from** the loaves of the thanks offering, which are brought by **an ordinary** person.

Despite the longer list of comparisons favoring the chavitei Kohen Gadol as the paradigm, the Gemara concludes: *hedyot mehedyot adif leh* — the fact that both the lachmei Toda and a normal mincha are lay offerings, brought by ordinary Israelites rather than by the Kohen Gadol, is a more fundamental and persuasive point of comparison. Even a longer list of technical similarities cannot outweigh the categorical difference between a korban of a *hedyot* and a korban of the Kohen Gadol. It is preferable to derive the rule for a lay offering from another lay offering. This is Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Teruma Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן בָּאוֹת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה. מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּדָבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב – יָלֵיף מֵחֲבִיתֵּי (דכהן) [כֹהֵן] גָּדוֹל, דְּהָנֵי נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Meir says: They all come** as **twelve** loaves. The Gemara explains: **What does** he **hold? If he holds that a matter learned through a verbal analogy** is **subsequently** used to **teach as a paradigm,** he **derives** the number of loaves in other meal offerings **from the High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, which was itself derived from a verbal analogy, **as these** similarities between other meal offerings and the griddle-cake offering enumerated above **are** more **numerous.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּדָבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב – יָלֵיף מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים, הֶקְדֵּשׁ מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ עֲדִיף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **if he holds that a matter learned through a verbal analogy is not subsequently** used to **teach as a paradigm,** then he **derives** the number of loaves in other meal offerings **from** the twelve loaves of **the shewbread.** This is because Rabbi Meir **prefers** to derive the status of the other meal offerings, which are **consecrated** property of the highest order, **from** the shewbread, which is also **consecrated** property of the highest order, in contrast to the loaves of the thanks offering, which are of lesser sanctity.

The Gemara now turns to Rabbi Meir, who holds that the general rule is twelve. *Mai kasavar* — what does he maintain? Two possibilities are presented. If Rabbi Meir holds *davar halamed bigezeirah shavah chozer umelamed bibinyan av* — that something derived via *gezeirah shavah* can subsequently be used as a binyan av — then he would derive his rule of twelve from the chavitei Kohen Gadol, using that as his paradigm and relying on the longer list of points of comparison established above. If, however, he holds that something derived via *gezeirah shavah* cannot be used as a binyan av, then he cannot use the chavitei Kohen Gadol as his model, since it was itself derived from the *Lechem HaPanim* through the *chukah-chukah* *gezeirah shavah*. In that case, Rabbi Meir would derive his rule of twelve directly from the *Lechem HaPanim*. His rationale: *hekdesh mehekdesh adif lei* — just as Rabbi Yehuda argues that it is preferable to derive the rule for a lay offering from another lay offering (*hedyot mehedyot*), Rabbi Meir argues the mirror principle: it is preferable to derive the rule for a korban that is *kodesh kodashim* from another korban that is *kodesh kodashim*. A normal mincha is *kodesh kodashim*; the *Lechem HaPanim* is *kodesh kodashim*; this categorical kinship outweighs the advantage the lachmei Toda model offers through the *hedyot* comparison, since the lachmei Toda are *kodashim kalim*.

### Separation Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִלַּחְמֵי תוֹדָה וּנְזִירוּת, שֶׁהֵן בָּאוֹת עֶשֶׂר עֶשֶׂר. לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Meir says: They all come as twelve loaves **except for** the **loaves** of the **thanks offering and** the loaves that accompany the guilt offering of **naziriteship, which come** as **ten each.** The Gemara explains the exceptions: With regard to **the loaves of the thanks offering, it is written explicitly of them** that these loaves are brought in units of ten, based on a verbal analogy: With regard to the thanks offering, the verse states: "And of it he shall present one out of each offering for a gift [*teruma*] to the Lord" (Leviticus 7:14), and with regard to *teruma* of the tithe it is stated: "You shall set apart of it a gift [*teruma*] for the Lord, a tithe of the tithe" (Numbers 18:26). Just as the *teruma* of the tithe consists of one portion of ten, so too, the loaves of the thanks offering are brought in units of ten (see 77b).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְזִירוּת – דְּאָמַר מָר: ״שְׁלָמָיו״ – לְרַבּוֹת שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** That ten loaves accompany the guilt offering of **naziriteship** is derived from a verse, **as the Master says:** The verse describing the thanks offering states: "With cakes of leavened bread he shall present his offering with the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanksgiving" (Leviticus 7:13). The term **"his peace offerings"** serves **to include** the loaves of the **peace offering of the nazirite.** Just as each of the four varieties of loaves accompanying the thanks offering are brought in units of ten loaves, so too, the peace offering of the nazirite comprises ten loaves.

Having established twelve as the general rule, Rabbi Meir acknowledges the two explicit exceptions. *Chutz milachmei Toda u'nezirut de'atyan eser eser* — the breads of the Todah and of the Nazir come in ten. For the lachmei Toda, *be'hedya ketiv bei* — it is as if written explicitly, through the derivation from the word *terumah* that we have already seen. The Gemara notes with characteristic irony that calling it *be'hedya* ("explicitly written") is somewhat generous, given that the number ten is derived from the word *terumah* by way of comparison to *terumas ma'aser* — but since this is an established *drasha*, it is treated as though it were explicit. As for nezirut, the Gemara explains: we learn the laws of the Nazir's breads from the lachmei Toda because the Torah uses the superfluous phrase *al zevach todat shelamav* when describing the Todah. The word *shelamim* is redundant — it could have simply said *al zevach hatodah* — and so *lerabot shlamei nazir*, the word *shelamim* is there to include the shelamim of a Nazir. From this inclusion we derive that many of the laws of the Todah apply to the Nazir's offering, including the number of loaves: ten.

## Number Not Meakev

### Baraitot on Meakev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁאֲפָאָן אַרְבַּע חַלּוֹת – יָצָא. וְהָא בָּעֵינַן אַרְבָּעִים לְמִצְוָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The loaves of the thanks offering consist of ten of each of the following four types: Leavened loaves, unleavened wafers, standard unleavened loaves, and unleavened loaves made from flour mixed with water and oil. With regard to these loaves, **Rav Tovi bar Kisna says** that **Shmuel says:** In the case of **loaves of** the **thanks offering that one baked as** only **four loaves,** one of each of the four types, where each loaf is the size of ten loaves, he has **fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara asks: **But** do **we** not **require forty** loaves? The Gemara answers: Forty loaves are brought in order **to** fulfill the **mitzva** in the optimal fashion, but he has nevertheless fulfilled his obligation with four loaves, one of each type.

Having established the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir regarding the required number of loaves, the Gemara turns to the question of *bedi'avad* — what is the status after the fact when the prescribed number was not achieved? Rav Tuvi bar Kisna said in the name of Shmuel: *lachmei Toda she'afan arba chalot yatza* — if one baked the breads of the Todah as four loaves — one of each of the four types — rather than the required ten of each type, one has fulfilled the obligation after the fact. The full quantity of flour is used but shaped into one enormous loaf of each variety rather than ten. The number ten is *le'chat'chila*; *bedi'avad* it is not indispensable.

### Toda Number Meakev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵי אַפְרוֹשֵׁי תְּרוּמָה מִינַּיְיהוּ! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמַפְרֵישׁ מִכֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא – ״אֶחָד״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פָּרוּס, דְּאַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ בְּלֵישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **But** an individual who brings the loaves as an offering **is required to separate *teruma* from them** by designating one loaf of each type to be given to the priests, which cannot be done where only one loaf of each type exists. **And if you would say that he** may **separate** a tenth-sized piece **from each and every** loaf, that is difficult: Doesn't **the Merciful One state:** "And of it he shall present **one** out of each offering for a gift [*teruma*] to the Lord," which teaches **that he may not take sliced** bread, but rather a whole loaf? The Gemara answers: This is a case **where he separated it while kneading,** so that it is not considered separating a slice.

A difficulty arises immediately. The Torah requires that one complete loaf from each of the four types be given to the Kohen as *terumah*. If one has only one loaf of each type, how can one give the Kohen a complete loaf while still retaining the remaining loaves for oneself? One cannot give him a broken-off piece, because the Torah's use of the word *echad* — one — is understood to require a complete, unbroken loaf: *shelo yitol paros*. And one cannot give the Kohen the entire loaf of that type, because that would eliminate the character of *terumah*, which by definition involves separating one portion from the rest.

### R. Huna on One Loaf

*[Continued from above]*

The resolution offered is: *d'afrishinhu b'lisha* — one separates the Kohen's portion during the kneading of the dough, before baking. When preparing each type, one sets aside a portion of the dough at the kneading stage and bakes it separately. This separate piece then constitutes the complete loaf given to the Kohen, while the main mass of dough is baked as the owner's portion. The result is that one does not in fact end up with exactly four loaves, since one has baked the Kohen's portions separately; one ends up with eight distinct pieces. But the essential point — that the Kohen receives an intact, complete portion — is preserved.

### R. Papa Counters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁרִיבָּה בְּמִדַּת חַלָּתָן, אוֹ שֶׁמִּיעֵט בְּמִדַּת חַלָּתָן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, חוּץ מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים, וַחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה וּנְזִירוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Shmuel from a *baraita*: **All the meal offerings where** one **increased the measure of their loaves or where** one **decreased the measure of their loaves** are nevertheless **fit, except for the** twelve loaves of **shewbread, and the** twelve loaves of the **High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, **and** the ten **loaves** of each variety for the **thanks offering and** the ten loaves that accompany the guilt offering of **naziriteship.** Evidently, no fewer than forty loaves are fit for the thanks offering, in contradiction to the statement of Shmuel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁרִיבָּה בְּמִדַּת חַלָּתָן, אוֹ שֶׁמִּיעֵט בְּמִדַּת חַלָּתָן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, חוּץ מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים וַחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה וּנְזִירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All the meal offerings where** one **increased the measure of their loaves or where** one **decreased the measure of their loaves** are nevertheless **fit, except for** the twelve loaves of **the shewbread and** the twelve loaves of **the High Priest's griddle-cake** offering. **And some say: Even the** ten **loaves** of each variety **of the thanks offering and** the ten loaves that accompany the guilt offering of **naziriteship** are fit only when brought in their prescribed measures. Shmuel agrees with the first *tanna*, according to whom the loaves of the thanks offering and the loaves of naziriteship are fit even when not offered in the proper quantities.

The Gemara now cites a Braita with a broader ruling: *kol heminachot sheriba bemidat challatan o shemi'eit bemidat challatan kesherot* — any of the menachot for which one made more than the prescribed number of loaves, or fewer, is still valid. The prescribed number is a *mitzvah* — it is the *le'chat'chila* ideal — but it is not *me'akev* for most menachot. The exceptions are the *Lechem HaPanim* and the chavitei Kohen Gadol, for which twelve is indispensable. This is presumably because the *chukah* language of the *gezeirah shavah* implies a strict requirement, and in the case of the *Lechem HaPanim* in particular, the physical arrangement of twelve loaves on the shulchan cannot be achieved with any other number.

### Omer Ratio

*[Continued from above]*

The Braita adds two further exceptions: *ve'yesh omrim af lachmei Toda u'nezirus* — some say that even the breads of the Todah and the Nazir must be ten, and this too is *me'akev*. The basis for treating the ten as *me'akev* in these cases — given that the number is derived from *terumah* rather than from an explicit *chukah* formulation — is a matter of some difficulty. It may be that since the number is derived from an explicit textual comparison rather than purely by analogy, it is treated as though the Torah stated it directly.

### Shtei Halechem Ratio

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara explains Shmuel's ruling — that one is *yotzei* with only four loaves of lachmei Toda — by noting that Shmuel follows a Tanna who does not include the lachmei Toda and nezirut among the *me'akev* exceptions. The Braita in question reads: *kol heminachot sheriba bemidat challatan o shemi'eit bemidat challatan kesherot — chutz milechem hapanim vachavitei Kohen Gadol* — excluding only the *Lechem HaPanim* and the chavitei Kohen Gadol, but not the lachmei Toda. Shmuel aligns with this Tanna and therefore rules that four loaves of lachmei Toda are valid after the fact.

### Lechem Hapanim Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה (שאפה) [שֶׁאֲפָאָהּ] חַלָּה אַחַת – יָצָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? (מַצּוֹת) ״מַצָּת״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says:** With regard to an oven-**baked meal offering that** one **baked** as only **one loaf,** although he did not bring the mandated quantity, he has **fulfilled** his obligation. **What is the reason?** It is that the verse states with regard to the oven-baked meal offering: "And when you bring a meal offering baked in the oven, it shall be unleavened loaves of fine flour" (Leviticus 2:4). The term **"unleavened loaves [*matzot*]" is written** without the letter *vav*, which is generally used with regard to the plural form. Therefore, it may be read in the singular as **unleavened loaf [*matzat*],** which teaches that even one unleavened loaf is fit.

Rav Huna offers a more restricted ruling: *mincha ma'afeh she'afah challah achat yatza* — if one baked the oven-baked mincha as a single loaf rather than the prescribed ten, one has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation. His basis is a textual observation: the word in the Torah is *matzat* — spelled without the letter *vav* — which can be read as a singular form, indicating that *bedi'avad* even one *matzah* suffices. Rav Huna limits this ruling to the *mincha ma'afeh* specifically, based on this *pasuk*, and is not prepared to extend it to all menachot on the basis of the broader Braita.

### Ratio Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב ״מַצָּת״, הָא כְּתִיב ״מַצּוֹת״ – לָא? וְהָא גַּבֵּי לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה דִּכְתִיב ״מַצּוֹת״, וְאָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁאֲפָאָן אַרְבַּע חַלּוֹת – יָצָא! הָהִיא פְּלִיגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** derivation: **The reason** Rav Huna gives is **that it is written** in the singular form of ***matzat*. But** if it were **written: *Matzot*,** would this **not** be the *halakha*? **But** what about the verse **concerning the loaves of the thanks offering, where it is written "*matzot*"** with a *vav*, indicating the plural form, in the verse: "Then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanksgiving unleavened loaves mixed with oil" (Leviticus 7:12), **and** yet **Rav Tovi bar Kisna said** that **Shmuel said:** In the case of **loaves of the thanks offering that were baked as four loaves,** such that each of the four varieties of loaves is baked as only one loaf, he has **fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara answers: **That** statement of Shmuel **disagrees** with the opinion of Rav Huna, according to whom four loaves are not fit for the thanks offering.

Rav Pappa challenges this line of reasoning. If the logic is that *matzat* without a *vav* permits a reduced number, then by the breads of the Todah the Torah writes *matzot* — with a *vav* — and yet Shmuel rules that even four loaves are valid there. The presence of the full plural spelling *matzot* would, on Rav Huna's reasoning, imply that the full number is required, not that a reduced number is acceptable. This constitutes a challenge to Shmuel's ruling from Rav Huna's logic: if spelling matters in one direction, it ought to matter in the other. Tosafot reconciles this by suggesting that once Rav Huna derives *bedi'avad* validity from the defective spelling in *mincha ma'afeh*, one may extrapolate that principle broadly to all menachot — and from that extrapolation one could support Shmuel's position as well.

## Wheat-to-Flour Ratios

### Quality Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹמֶר הָיָה בָּא עִשָּׂרוֹן מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם שְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרוֹנִים מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרוֹנִים מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע סְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The *omer* offering,** i.e., the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan, **would come** from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour sifted **from three *se'a*** of barley. **The two loaves** would come from **two-tenths** of an ephah of flour sifted **from three *se'a*** of wheat. **The shewbread** would come from **twenty-four tenths** of an ephah of flour sifted **from twenty-four *se'a*** of wheat.

The Mishnah now addresses the quantities of wheat required for the three major communal flour-based offerings. The Omer requires one issaron of flour — approximately a five-pound bag of flour by rough modern equivalence — and the starting wheat for this is three *se'in*. Three *se'in* constitute one *eifah*, and one *eifah* equals ten *issaronim* (the issaron being one-tenth of an eifah, as the Torah states: *asirit ha'eifah*). The ratio of starting wheat to finished flour is therefore ten to one.

### Sieve Numbers

*[Continued from above]*

The *shtei halechem* requires two *esronim* of flour — one issaron per loaf — also from three *se'in* of wheat. Since three *se'in* yield two *esronim*, the ratio here is five to one, exactly half that of the Omer.

### R. Shimon Opinion

*[Continued from above]*

The *Lechem HaPanim* requires 24 *esronim* — two *esronim* per loaf for each of the twelve loaves, as the Torah states explicitly: *shtei esronim yihyeh hachalah ha'achat* — from 24 *se'in* of wheat. Since 24 *se'in* is eight *eifot*, which is 80 *issaronim*, the ratio of starting wheat to finished flour is approximately 80 to 24, or roughly three and one-third to one. The ratio thus decreases progressively: ten to one for the Omer, five to one for the *shtei halechem*, and approximately three and one-third to one for the *Lechem HaPanim*.

### Alternating Sieves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּמֵחָדָשׁ אָתֵי, וּמִשְּׂעוֹרִין אָתֵי, עִשָּׂרוֹן מוּבְחָר לָא אָתֵי אֶלָּא מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that according to the mishna, the relatively large amount of three *se'a* of barley is necessary to yield a single tenth of an ephah of flour for the *omer* offering? The Gemara answers: **Since** it **comes from** the fresh **new** crop, which contains a large amount of refuse, **and** it **comes from barley,** which is coarser than wheat, **a choice tenth** of an ephah of flour **comes only from** a minimum of **three *se'a*** of barley.

The Gemara explains why the ratios differ. For the Omer: *keivan d'michadsha ati* — it comes from newly harvested grain, *u'mise'orin ati* — and from barley. Two factors combine to require a high ratio. First, newly harvested grain that has not yet dried out retains considerably more bran and chaff, requiring proportionally more starting material to produce a given quantity of fine flour. Second, barley itself has substantially more *psolet* — dross, hulls, and waste — than wheat. For these two reasons, *issaron muvchar lo ati ela mishalosh se'in* — a fine, high-quality issaron of solet cannot be obtained from less than three *se'in*, yielding the ten-to-one ratio.

### Sieve Purposes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם שְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין – כֵּיוָן דְּמֵחִיטִּין אָתְיָין, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמֵחָדָשׁ אָתְיָין, שְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת אָתוּ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why does the mishna rule that **the two loaves** would come from **two-tenths** of an ephah of flour sifted **from three *se'a*** of wheat, whereas only a tenth of an ephah is used from the same amount of grain for the *omer*? The Gemara answers: **Since** the loaves **come from wheat,** which is of superior quality to barley, **although** they **come from** the **new** crop and require sifting, the grains are clean enough that **two-tenths** of an ephah **come from** the sifting of **three *se'a*** of grain.

The *shtei halechem* comes from newly harvested grain as well — these are the two loaves of the new wheat crop brought on Shavuot — but it is wheat rather than barley. The two factors therefore partially cancel: newly harvested grain increases the ratio, but wheat rather than barley cuts it in half. *Af al gav d'michadsha atyan* — although they come from new grain, *keivan d'michitin atyan* — since they come from wheat, the ratio is halved, and two *esronim* can be obtained from three *se'in*.

### Progressive Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה סְאִין – מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּמֵחִיטִּין אָתוּ, וּמִיָּשָׁן אָתוּ, עִשָּׂרוֹן מוּבְחָר אָתֵי מִסְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that **the shewbread** would come from **twenty-four tenths** of an ephah of flour sifted **from twenty-four *se'a*** of wheat. **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers: **Since** they **come from wheat, and** they **come from** the **old** crop, which contains relatively little refuse, the grains are so clean that **a choice tenth** of an ephah of flour **comes from one *se'a*** of grain, and twenty-four tenths of an ephah come from twenty-four *se'a*.

The *Lechem HaPanim* benefits from two favorable factors simultaneously. *Keivan d'michitin atu* — it comes from wheat, and *umiyashan atu* — it comes from aged, dried grain. The *Lechem HaPanim*, unlike the Omer or *shtei halechem*, need not come from the newly harvested crop; it can be brought from whatever wheat is available at any time of year, and older grain that has dried out yields a much higher proportion of fine flour. With both favorable factors in play, *issaron muvchar ati mise'ah* — a fine issaron can be obtained from a single *se'ah*, yielding the lowest ratio of approximately three and one-third to one.

### Buy Flour

*[Continued from above]*

An important clarification follows: one should not infer from the higher starting ratio of the Omer that the standard of flour quality required for the Omer is higher. The end product — a fine issaron of solet — is the same in all cases. The higher ratio simply reflects the inferior starting material: more wheat must be processed to produce the same quality of finished flour. The quality of the result is identical; what differs is how much raw material is needed to achieve it.

### Buy Wheat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁרִיבָּה בְּמִדַּת עֶשְׂרוֹנָן, אוֹ שֶׁמִּיעֵט בְּמִדַּת עִשָּׂרוֹן – פְּסוּלוֹת. רִיבָּה בְּמִדַּת סְאִין שֶׁלָּהֶן, אוֹ שֶׁמִּיעֵט בְּמִדַּת סְאִין שֶׁלָּהֶן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **All the meal offerings where** one **increased the measure of their tenth** of an ephah of flour by collecting it with a vessel larger than a tenth of an ephah, **or where** one **decreased the measure of** their **tenth** of an ephah of flour by collecting it with a vessel smaller than a tenth of an ephah, **are disqualified.** If he **increased the measure of their *se'a*** of grain **or decreased the measure of their *se'a*** of grain, e.g., if he used two or four *se'a* instead of three, but sifted until he was left with the correct measure of a tenth of an ephah of flour, they are **fit** offerings. This is because the number of *se'a* mentioned in the mishna is necessary only for the optimal fulfillment of the mitzva, but is not indispensable.

A Braita makes this distinction explicit and gives it halachic force: *kol hemenachot sheriba bemidat isronam o shemi'eit bemidat isronam pesulot* — any mincha for which the quantity of finished flour was more or less than the prescribed issaron is invalid. The issaron is *me'akev*. However, *riba bemidat se'im shelahen o shemi'eit bemidat se'im shelahen kesherot* — if one started with more or less wheat than the prescribed three *se'in* but still arrived at the correct issaron of fine flour, the mincha is valid. The ratios of starting wheat to flour are provided as practical guidance for achieving the correct quantity of solet, not as independent requirements. What must be precise is the finished product; the process of arriving there carries latitude.

## Sifting Process

### Lechem Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹמֶר הָיָה מְנוּפֶּה בִּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה נָפָה, שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם בִּשְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה, וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיָה לָהֶן קִצְבָה, אֶלָּא סוֹלֶת מְנוּפָּה כָּל צׇרְכָּהּ הָיָה מֵבִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ סֹלֶת וְאָפִיתָ אֹתָהּ״, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא מְנוּפָּה כָּל צׇרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The flour of **the *omer* was sifted with thirteen sifters,** each finer than its predecessor, and the flour that emerged from the final sifter was sacrificed. The flour of **the two loaves** was sifted **with twelve** sifters, **and** the flour of **the shewbread** was sifted **with eleven** sifters. **Rabbi Shimon says: They have no fixed** number of sifters; **rather,** it was **fine flour** that was **completely sifted** that **one would bring** for all of these offerings, **as it is stated: "And you shall take fine flour and bake it"** (Leviticus 24:5), indicating that one does not fulfill his obligation **until** the flour **will be completely sifted.**

The final Mishnah of the daf addresses the sifting process. The Omer was sifted through thirteen sieves. The *shtei halechem* was sifted through twelve. The *Lechem HaPanim* through eleven. The numbers decrease as the ratio of starting material to finished flour decreases — which is intuitive, since the inferior starting material of the Omer (new barley) requires more processing to achieve the same quality of solet. What is somewhat striking is that the differences between the three offerings are each only one sieve, even as the differences in the starting wheat ratios are quite dramatic. The Gemara does not offer an explicit explanation of this; the numbers appear to carry a degree of symbolic or conventional character.

One may also note that the Mishnah specifies numbers for these three communal offerings but does not prescribe a fixed number of sieves for an ordinary personal mincha. This reflects a broader pattern: the communal offerings were subject to precise, standardized procedures, whereas the personal mincha was held to a standard of *solet* — fine flour — without the same degree of procedural specification.

### Expense Principle

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the fixed numbers entirely: *lo haya lahen kitzva ella solet menufa *kol tzorcha* haya mevi* — there was no fixed number of sieves; rather, one would bring finely sifted flour sifted as much as necessary. His source is the verse *ve'lakachta solet ve'afita otah* — you shall take solet and you shall bake it. Rabbi Shimon reads a play on *ve'afita* — connecting it to the concept of being well-sifted (*menufa*) — to indicate that what matters is achieving sufficiently fine flour, not the specific number of sieves used. This is arguably the more logically coherent position: if the goal is *solet*, then one sieves until *solet* is achieved, whatever number of passes that requires.

### Shimon b Elazar Sieves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּדַּקָּה בְּגַסָּה, בְּדַּקָּה בְּגַסָּה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה נָפוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ וְזוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ, עֶלְיוֹנָה קוֹלֶטֶת סוּבִּין, תַּחְתּוֹנָה קוֹלֶטֶת סוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: When a fine sifter was used, the small, dust-like particles emerged and the fine flour was caught in the sifter, and when a coarse sifter was used, the fine flour emerged and the bran was caught in the sifter. With regard to the sifting of the flour, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The sifting would begin **in** a sifter of **slight** holes, and the flour that remained would then be sifted **in** a sifter of **large** holes. The flour that emerged would again be sifted **in** a sifter with **slight** holes that were not quite as small as the first one with small holes, and again **in** a sifter of **large** holes that were not quite as large as the first one with large holes. All the sifting would proceed in this manner. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: There were thirteen sifters in the Temple, this** one **above,** i.e., preceding, **that** one, **and this** one **above that** one. The **highest** sifter would **collect bran,** parts of the kernel aside from the actual pure flour, and the **lowest** sifter would **collect fine flour.**

A Braita describes the order of sifting: *b'daka b'gasa b'daka b'gasa* — one alternates between a fine-holed sieve and a coarse-holed sieve. The two types of sieves serve opposite purposes. The fine-holed sieve is designed to allow small particles — sand, dust, and fine debris — to pass through while retaining the grain; in this way, the fine *psolet* falls out below. The coarse-holed sieve is designed to allow the grain to pass through while retaining large particles such as bran above. By alternating the two types and progressively adjusting the sizes of the holes — the fine holes becoming progressively finer and the coarse holes becoming progressively coarser — more and more of the waste material is removed at each stage until only pure solet remains.

### Torah Saves Money

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — a distinct figure from Rabbi Shimon of the Mishnah — describes a different arrangement: *shlosha asar nefot hayu bamikdash, zu lemala mizu vezu lemala mizu* — there were thirteen sieves in the Temple, each one arranged above the other, *elyona kolelet subin vetachtona kolelet solet* — the topmost sieve catches the bran, and the bottommost catches the solet. On Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's reading, rather than alternating fine and coarse sieves, the thirteen sieves are arranged in a descending series from largest holes to smallest, so that the grain passes progressively through ever-finer sieving, with the bran caught at the top and the finest solet collected at the bottom. This is a single hierarchical system rather than an alternating back-and-forth process.

### Torah: Mammon Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיָה לָהֶן קִצְבָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״סֹלֶת וְאָפִיתָ אֹתָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּקַּחַת סוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in contrast to the first *tanna*, **Rabbi Shimon says: They have no fixed** number of sifters; but the flour would be sifted as many times as was necessary, based on the verse: "And you shall take fine flour, and bake it." In addition, **the Sages taught:** When the verse states with regard to the shewbread: "And you shall take **fine flour and bake it,"** this **teaches that fine flour is acquired** after it has been sifted for the baking of the shewbread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ חִיטִּין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. יָכוֹל אַף בִּשְׁאָר מְנָחוֹת כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֹתָהּ״, מִפְּנֵי הַחִיסָּחוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that even wheat** kernels may be purchased before they are ground and sifted? **The verse states: "And you shall take,"** indicating that the grain should be taken **in any case** and in any state. One **might** have thought that it is **so even for other meal offerings.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And you shall take fine flour and bake **it,"** indicating that the shewbread alone may be acquired as kernels **because** of **the sparing [*haḥissaḥon*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי ״מִפְּנֵי הַחִיסָּחוֹן״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. הֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִשְׁקִיתָ אֶת הָעֵדָה וְאֶת בְּעִירָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: What is** the meaning of: **Because** of **the sparing? Rabbi Elazar says: The Torah spared the money of the Jewish people.** Due to the large quantity of grain needed for the shewbread every week, purchasing sifted fine flour would be a substantial expense. The Gemara explains: **Where is the allusion** to this principle? It is found in a verse, **as it is written** that when the Jewish people were thirsty in the wilderness in the aftermath of Miriam's death, God instructed Moses: "And speak to the rock before their eyes, so that it will give forth its water; and you shall bring forth to them water out of the rock; **so you shall give drink for the congregation and for their cattle"** (Numbers 20:8). Evidently, the miracle of extracting water from the rock was performed even for the purpose of providing water for the livestock.

A Braita addresses the question of how the flour was obtained. The verse *ve'lakachta solet ve'afita otah* — you shall take solet and bake it — teaches that *solet* may be purchased directly from the marketplace. One need not produce the flour oneself from scratch; purchasing pre-sifted flour from a vendor is permitted. Furthermore, *u'minayin she'afilu chitin* — how do we know one may even purchase raw wheat rather than flour? *Talmud lomar ve'lakachta* — the word *ve'lakachta*, "you shall take," indicates that one may acquire the grain in whatever form it comes, even as unprocessed wheat.

However, *yachol af bish'ar menachot ken* — one might have thought that this latitude to purchase raw wheat applies to all menachot. The Braita implies that for other menachot it does not apply — for them one must purchase flour rather than unprocessed wheat — and the reason offered is *mipnei hachisachon*, because of the financial saving. The Gemara asks: *mai mipnei hachisachon* — what does this mean?

### Rabbi Elazar: The Torah Is Concerned for the Property of Israel — Source from Vehishkita

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Elazar explains: *haTorah chasah al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is concerned about the financial expenditure of Israel. Specially sifted and processed flour, of the quality required for the Mikdash, carries a significant premium — particularly for the *Lechem HaPanim*, which required 24 *esronim* of solet and was brought every week. Purchasing that quantity of premium flour from the marketplace week after week would be enormously expensive. Therefore, the Torah permits purchasing raw wheat for the *Lechem HaPanim* so that the processing can be done in-house, reducing the cost. The permission to purchase wheat rather than finished flour is specifically calibrated to cases where the financial burden would otherwise be prohibitive.

The principle *haTorah chasah al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is solicitous of the financial resources of Israel — is a significant and recurring principle in halacha. Its source, as cited here, is the verse *ve'hishkita et ha'eda ve'et be'iram* — and you shall give the congregation and their animals to drink — where concern for communal resources is explicit in the Divine instruction to Moshe. The principle plays an important practical role across many areas of halacha, frequently supporting more lenient rulings where strict application would impose unnecessary financial hardship.
