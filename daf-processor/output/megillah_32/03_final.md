# Megillah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Elders vs. Youth

### Demolish Don't Build

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִם יֹאמְרוּ לָךְ זְקֵנִים ״סְתוֹר״ וִילָדִים ״בְּנֵה״ — סְתוֹר וְאַל תִּבְנֶה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּתִירַת זְקֵנִים בִּנְיָן, וּבִנְיַן נְעָרִים סְתִירָה. וְסִימָן לַדָּבָר — רְחַבְעָם בֶּן שְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: If old men say to you: Demolish, and children** say to you: **Build,** then **demolish and do not build, because the demolishing of old men is** ultimately as constructive as **building,** despite the fact that it appears destructive, **and the building of children is** as destructive as **demolishing. An indication of this matter** is **Rehoboam, son of Solomon.** He ignored the advice of the Elders and did not lower himself before his people, which ultimately led to the people rebelling against him.

The daf opens with a *baraita* citing Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *im yomru lecha zekenim setor veyeladim b'nei, setor v'al tivneh* — if elders tell you to demolish and youth tell you to build, demolish and do not build. Even when the counsel of elders sounds counterintuitive or injudicious, one should listen to them over the seemingly constructive advice of the young.

### Rehoboam Example

The reason is given directly: *mipnei shestirat zekenim binyan, ubinyan nearim stirah* — because the demolition advised by elders is ultimately constructive, and the construction advised by youth is ultimately destructive. The proof offered is Rehoboam son of Solomon. As is well known, Rehoboam consulted the elders, who counseled leniency toward the people. He then turned to his young advisors, who told him that such leniency would make him appear weak and that he must be harsh. He followed the young, was harsh with the people, and the result was the permanent splitting of the land of Israel. The *baraita* thus appears here in connection with an earlier citation of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar encountered on the preceding daf.

## Weekly Reading

### Reading Continuity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָקוֹם שֶׁמַּפְסִיקִין בַּשַּׁבָּת שַׁחֲרִית — שָׁם קוֹרִין בְּמִנְחָה, בְּמִנְחָה — שָׁם קוֹרִין בְּשֵׁנִי, בְּשֵׁנִי — שָׁם קוֹרִין בַּחֲמִישִׁי, בַּחֲמִישִׁי — שָׁם קוֹרִין לַשַּׁבָּת הַבָּאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **the place** in the Torah **where** the congregation **concludes** the reading **on Shabbat morning,** it is from **there** that **they** continue to **read in the afternoon** service on Shabbat. Where they conclude **in the afternoon** service on Shabbat, from **there they** continue to **read on Monday** morning. Where they conclude **on Monday,** from **there they** continue to **read on Thursday** morning. Where they conclude **on Thursday,** from **there they** continue to **read on the coming Shabbat.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

A *baraita* now establishes the continuity of the Torah reading cycle across the days of the week. The place where one finishes the *Shabbat* morning reading is where one begins at *Shabbat Mincha*; where one finishes at *Mincha* is where one begins on Monday; where one finishes on Monday is where one begins on Thursday; and where one finishes on Thursday is where one begins the following *Shabbat*.

### Meir vs. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מָקוֹם שֶׁמַּפְסִיקִין בַּשַּׁבָּת שַׁחֲרִית — שָׁם קוֹרִין בְּמִנְחָה, וּבַשֵּׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי, וְלַשַּׁבָּת הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **the place** in the Torah **where they conclude** the reading **on Shabbat morning,** it is from **there** that **they** continue to **read in the afternoon** service on Shabbat. **And** from that same place **they** continue to **read on Monday** morning, **and on Thursday** morning, **and on the coming Shabbat.**

This is the view of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *makom shemapshikim b'Shabbat Shacharit, sham korin b'Mincha u'v'Sheni u'v'Chamishi u'l'Shabbat haba'ah* — the very same *aliyah* where one left off on *Shabbat* morning is what is read again at *Shabbat Mincha*, again on Monday, again on Thursday, and again at the start of the following *Shabbat*. The practical consequence is that everyone who attends only on *Shabbat* will hear that first *aliyah* of the new *parasha* — which presumably explains why so many people know the opening *aliyah* of every *parasha* so well.

### Halacha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הֲלָכָה, מָקוֹם שֶׁמַּפְסִיקִין בַּשַּׁבָּת שַׁחֲרִית — שָׁם קוֹרִין בְּמִנְחָה, וּבְשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי, וְלַשַּׁבָּת הַבָּאָה. וְלֵימָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! מִשּׁוּם דְּאָפְכִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Rabbi Zeira said:** The ***halakha*** is that with regard to **the place where they conclude** the reading **on Shabbat morning,** it is from **there** that **they** continue to **read in the afternoon** service on Shabbat. **And** from that same place **they** continue to **read on Monday** morning, **and on Thursday** morning, **and on the coming Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: If so, **let him** simply **say: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Why did he have to explicitly state the whole *halakha*? **Because there are those who reversed** the names in the *baraita* and attributed the position of Rabbi Yehuda to Rabbi Meir, and vice versa.

Rabbi Zeira rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — the law follows Rabbi Yehuda, and he states the ruling by describing the practice in full rather than simply attributing it to Rabbi Yehuda by name. The reason given is *mishum d'apchi lehu* — some authorities reverse which position belongs to Rabbi Meir and which to Rabbi Yehuda. To avoid any confusion, the *psak halacha* is stated substantively rather than by attribution.

## Torah Handling

### Yehuda's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פּוֹתֵחַ וְרוֹאֶה, גּוֹלֵל וּמְבָרֵךְ, וְחוֹזֵר וּפוֹתֵחַ וְקוֹרֵא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: פּוֹתֵחַ וְרוֹאֶה וּמְבָרֵךְ וְקוֹרֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When a person reads from the Torah, **he should open** the scroll **and see** the place from where he will read, **furl** it so that it is closed, **and recite the blessing, and** then **he should again open** the scroll, **and read.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda said: He should open** the scroll, **and see** the place from where he will read, **and,** without closing it again, **he should recite the blessing, and read.**

A new *baraita* addresses the procedure for opening and reading from a *Sefer Torah*. The *baraita* records that one opens the scroll, looks to find the place, closes it, recites the *bracha*, then opens it again and reads — this is the view recorded first. Rabbi Yehuda, however, rules: *poteach v'ro'eh u'mevarech v'koreh* — one opens, looks, recites the *bracha*, and reads, without any intervening closing. Rabbi Yehuda's approach is straightforward and avoids any interruption between the *bracha* and the reading.

### Meir's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? כִּדְעוּלָּא. דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ הַקּוֹרֵא בַּתּוֹרָה לֹא יְסַיֵּיעַ לַמְתוּרְגְּמָן — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ תַּרְגּוּם כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה. הָכָא נָמֵי: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ בְּרָכוֹת כְּתוּבִין בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for Rabbi Meir's** opinion that the blessing is not recited over an open scroll? The Gemara answers: His reasoning is **in accordance with** the statement **of Ulla, as Ulla said: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that **one who reads from the Torah should not assist the translator,** but rather the translation should be exclusively said by the translator? **In order that** people **should not say** that **the translation is written in the Torah. Here too,** the scroll should be closed when reciting the blessings, **in order that** people **should not say** that **the blessings are written in the Torah.**

The question arises: what is the reasoning behind the first opinion — the one that calls for closing the scroll before the *bracha*? The *gemara* answers by appeal to a statement of Ulla: *mipnei ma amru hakoreh baTorah lo yesaye'a l'meturgaman* — the reason the Sages said that the Torah reader may not assist the *meturgaman* when the latter stumbles is *kedei shelo yomru targum katuv baTorah* — so that people should not conclude that the *targum* is actually written in the Torah. If the reader is producing Aramaic words while the scroll is open, onlookers might believe those words appear in the text. The same logic applies here: *hacha nami kedei shelo yomru brachos ktuvos baTorah* — we close the scroll before the *bracha* so that observers should not think the blessings are written within the *Sefer Torah*. The reason for looking at the scroll beforehand is presumably so that after reciting the *bracha* one knows precisely where to read from and need not search around again — though in practice this does not always prevent a moment of searching when the scroll is reopened.

### Yehuda's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: תַּרְגּוּם — אִיכָּא לְמִיטְעֵי, בְּרָכוֹת — לֵיכָּא לְמִיטְעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda** is not concerned about this, as he claims that with regard to the **translation, there is** the possibility of people **erring** in this way, but with regard to **the blessings, there is no** concern about people **erring.** People will realize the blessings are not actually part of the Torah's text because they are recited by each person who reads.

Rabbi Yehuda's counter-argument distinguishes the two cases. Regarding the *targum*, he acknowledges *targum ika l'michtei* — there is genuine room for error, since there are Aramaic words present in the Torah itself, and people might plausibly mistake the *meturgaman*'s words for words being read from the text. But *brachos leika l'michtei* — no one is going to think that *Baruch Atah Hashem Elokeinu Melech Ha'olam asher kideshanu* is written in the *Torah*. The concern simply does not exist for *brachos*. Indeed, in the contemporary practice where every person called for an *aliyah* recites both the opening and closing *bracha*, the implausibility of mistaking those blessings for Torah text is even greater.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הֲלָכָה, פּוֹתֵחַ וְרוֹאֶה וּמְבָרֵךְ וְקוֹרֵא. וְלֵימָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! מִשּׁוּם דְּאָפְכִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Mattana said:** The ***halakha*** is that **he should open** the scroll, **see** the place from which he will read, **and,** without closing it again, **he should recite the blessing and read.** The Gemara asks: If so, **let him** simply **say: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Why did he have to explicitly state the whole *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **Because there are those who reversed** the names in the *baraita* and attributed the position of Rabbi Yehuda to Rabbi Meir and vice versa. In order to avoid any lack of clarity, Rabbi Zeira stated the *halakha* explicitly.

Rabbi Zeira rules: *halacha k'poteach v'ro'eh u'mevarech v'koreh* — the law follows the view of opening, looking, blessing, and reading without closing in between, in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda. Again, rather than simply citing Rabbi Yehuda by name, the ruling is stated in full due to the concern that authorities may have reversed which position belongs to which *Tanna*.

This ruling corresponds to the *Sephardi psak*. The question arises as to why *Ashkenazim* practice differently, closing the scroll before the opening *bracha*. The answer lies in *Tosafot*. *Tosafot* writes: *lechatchilah hu golelo v'sotmo kodem sheyivarech kedei shelo yomru hamon shehabrachos ktuvos bo* — *lechatchilah*, one should close the scroll before the *bracha* out of concern for widespread ignorance, which was the concern of Rabbi Meir. However, *b'diavad ein lachush* — after the fact, there is no need for concern, since people are not so ignorant today as to genuinely think the *brachos* are written in the *Torah*. Regarding the closing *bracha* after the reading, *Tosafot* adds that even according to Rabbi Yehuda one must definitely close the scroll before reciting it. The explanation may be that for the opening *bracha* there is a stronger concern about *hefsek* between the *bracha* and the reading, whereas for the closing *bracha* that concern is less pressing, making it more straightforward to require closing beforehand.

What emerges from *Tosafot* is that although the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda and there is room to say the scroll should *davka* remain open so as to avoid *hefsek*, *Tosafot* ultimately rules *lechatchilah* in accordance with Rabbi Meir's concern — reflecting a greater sensitivity to widespread ignorance than the *gemara* itself apparently felt necessary to address.

## Torah Sanctity

### No Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הַלּוּחוֹת וְהַבִּימוֹת — אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Mattana said: The boards,** i.e., the empty margins of a Torah scroll, **and the platforms** from which the Torah is read **do not have any sanctity.**

Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: *haluchos v'habimos ein bahem mishum kedusha* — the tablets and platforms have no sanctity. The statement is dense and requires unpacking: what precisely are these *luchos* and *bimos*?

### Rashi Explains

Rashi expresses uncertainty about the meaning of *luchos*, writing *lo yadati ma hen* — I do not know what these tablets are. He then suggests that some interpret them as the covers of a codex (*sefer* in book form rather than scroll). This would mean that although the covers house a sanctified text, they themselves carry no *kedusha*. This raises an interesting question in its own right, since it implies a discussion of whether a codex-form *Sefer Torah* carries *kedusha* at all — but at minimum, the covers of such a book would not. As for *bima*, Rashi identifies it as the platform constructed for the king during *Parshat Hamelech*, as described in Tractate Sotah, noting this as the closest analogy he could find to a *bima* connected to *kriyat haTorah*. By extension one might apply the same principle to an ordinary raised reading platform in a synagogue: such a structure, even if used for Torah reading, carries no sanctity of its own.

### Tosafot View

*Tosafot* and Rabbeinu Chananel read the passage very differently. Citing the *Aruch*, *Tosafot* explains *luchos* and *bimos* as the blank spaces of the *Sefer Torah* itself — the margins above and below the text and between the columns. These vertical white spaces are the *luchos* (tablets), while the horizontal spaces function as the *bima* (platform). This would make the statement a declaration that the blank margins of the scroll carry no independent *kedusha*.

One might immediately object: surely the margins of a *Sefer Torah* do have *kedusha*! *Tosafot* resolves this by clarifying that the *gemara* is speaking of margins that have been cut away from the scroll. Once removed, *ein bahem mishum kedusha* — they no longer carry sanctity. This is how *Tosafot* and Rabbeinu Chananel understand the ruling.

## Rolling Technique

### Seam Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁפַטְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַגּוֹלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה — צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּעֲמִידֶנּוּ עַל הַתֶּפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shefatya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When one furls a Torah scroll, he needs to position it** so that it closes **on the seam** between two sheets of parchment. Once closed, the seam should be between the two rolls of the scroll, so that if it is mishandled or overly tightened, it will come apart along the seam and not be torn across the writing.

Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *ha-golel Sefer Torah tzarich she-ya'amidenu al ha-tefer* — the one rolling the *Sefer Torah* must position the scroll so that the seam between parchment panels lies in the middle as the scroll is being tightened. This requirement applies not only to the act of rolling but, according to some readings, to *hagbaha* as well.

### Tearing Prevention

The reason is to prevent tearing at vulnerable points. When one rolls and tightens the scroll, pressure is applied outward from the center. If the seam is positioned in the middle, any tearing that might result from this tension will occur at the seam rather than through the body of the parchment — the seam being the weaker point, just as one would prefer a garment to tear at its seam rather than through the fabric itself. This same logic extends to *hagbaha*: when the scroll is lifted, the poles exert lateral pulling force, and having the seam centered similarly directs any stress to the appropriate place. In practice, one rarely if ever hears of the actual *klaf* tearing during *hagbaha*, but the principle is stated nonetheless.

### Outside-Inside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁפַטְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַגּוֹלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה — גּוֹלְלוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, וְאֵין גּוֹלְלוֹ מִבִּפְנִים. וּכְשֶׁהוּא מְהַדְּקוֹ — מְהַדְּקוֹ מִבִּפְנִים, וְאֵינוֹ מְהַדְּקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shefatya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When one rolls a Torah scroll** from one section to another, **he should roll it from the outside,** i.e., he should position the scroll so the two rollers are parallel to him and then roll the scroll by rotating the roller farthest away from him by rotating it toward himself, **and he should not roll it from the inside,** by rotating the roller nearest to him away from himself. If one does this and loses control, the scroll could roll away from him onto the floor. **And when one tightens** the scroll, after he has found the new section, **he should tighten it from the inside,** by rotating the roller nearest to him, **and not from the outside,** by rotating the roller furthest away from him, in order not to extend his arms over the text of the Torah and obscure the view of the community, for it is a mitzva for them to be able to see the text.

Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan further teaches: *ha-golel Sefer Torah gollo mibachutz v'ein gollo mibifnim* — when rolling the *Sefer Torah*, one rolls from the outside, not from the inside; *u'cheshehu mehadko, mehadko mibifnim v'eino mehadko mibachutz* — when tightening, one tightens from the inside, not the outside.

Rashi explains this as guidance for a single person handling the scroll on his lap without a table. *Mibachutz* — the outside — means the side of the scroll farther from the body. As one rolls, one controls the far pole, drawing it toward oneself, while the near pole remains stable against the body. This allows the person to maintain control and prevent the scroll from falling. If one tried instead to keep the far pole fixed and roll the near pole away, it would tip over. When it comes to tightening at the end, Rashi explains that *mibifnim* means one should not stretch one's arm over the *kesav* and close the scroll in a way that covers the writing, since this would obstruct the view of the text. Rather, one closes it from the nearer side so that the *kesav* remains visible to those present.

### Tosafot Gartel

*Tosafot* reads the passage quite differently. For *Tosafot*, *mehadko mibifnim* refers to the placement of the *gartel* knot: when tying the belt around the closed *Sefer Torah*, one should make the knot in the front, not in the back. The reason is twofold. First, it keeps the scroll better secured. Second, and more significant: when the *Sefer Torah* is next taken out, if the knot is in the back, one must turn the scroll upside down to undo it, which is not *kavod* to the *Sefer Torah*. By placing the knot in front, one can lay the Torah flat and untie it with ease and dignity. From this understanding of *mehadko*, *Tosafot* derives that *gollo mibachutz* means that the person performing *gelila* stands behind the scroll as it is being rolled — not directly in front of it — so that the *kesav* remains visible to the congregation as the scroll is being closed.

## Gelila Honor

### Ten Readers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁפַטְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁקָּרְאוּ בַּתּוֹרָה, הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶם גּוֹלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה. הַגּוֹלְלוֹ נוֹטֵל שְׂכַר כּוּלָּן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁקָּרְאוּ בַּתּוֹרָה, הַגּוֹלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה קִיבֵּל שְׂכַר כּוּלָּן. שְׂכַר כּוּלָּן סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: קִיבֵּל שָׂכָר כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shefatya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **ten** people **read from the Torah, the greatest among them should furl the Torah scroll,** for this is the most distinguished honor. **And the one who furls it takes the reward of all of them, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** If **ten** people **read from the Torah, the one who furls it receives the reward of all of them.** The Gemara asks: **Can it enter your mind** to say that he actually receives **the reward of all of them?** Why should all the others forfeit their reward? **Rather, say** instead: **He receives a reward equivalent to** that of **all of them.**

A new teaching of Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *asara shekaru baTorah, hagadol shebahem golel* — if ten people have read from the Torah, the greatest among them performs *gelila*, because the one who performs *gelila* receives credit equivalent to all the others. Rashi clarifies that *asara shekaru* does not necessarily mean ten people each received an *aliyah*; it means ten people gathered for the Torah reading.

This is a striking ruling, since in contemporary practice *hagbah u'gelila* tend to be treated as honors given to those who have not received a major *aliyah*, or to those with the physical strength for *hagbaha*. Some suggest that in the *gemara*'s context, *golel* also encompassed the one who lifted the scroll — that is, the roles of *magbi'ah* and *golel* were combined. This would make better sense of the significance assigned to the honor: the person who lifts the *Sefer Torah* and displays it to the congregation, enabling everyone to see the actual writing — *v'zos haTorah* — performs an act of tremendous *zechut*. It is the power of showing the text that they have been hearing, making the Torah tangible and present. This may be the better framework for understanding why *gelila* is here treated as the greatest honor among the ten.

### Completion Credit

*Tosafot* and other sources cite an alternative formulation in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and there are discussions in other passages about the one who completes the reading receiving special merit. However, the Sefaria text provided focuses on Rabbi Yochanan's statement as above. Later discussions about *mashlim* — the one who completes — appear in different contexts and traditions. For this daf's teaching as presented in Sefaria, the primary statement is the one about the greatest among the ten performing *gelila* and receiving reward equivalent to all.

### Credit Equivalent

The *gemara* itself raises an objection to the formulation *kibel sachar kulam* — he receives all of their credit. Does this mean the others receive no credit and he takes it from them? The *gemara* corrects: *ela eima kibel sachar k'neged kulam* — he receives credit *equivalent* to all of their credit combined. The others retain their own *schar* in full.

## Bas Kol Usage

### Permitted Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁפַטְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁמִּשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בְּבַת קוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאׇזְנֶיךָ תִּשְׁמַעְנָה דָבָר מֵאַחֲרֶיךָ לֵאמֹר״. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי, דְּשָׁמַע קָל גַּבְרָא בְּמָתָא, וְקָל אִיתְּתָא בְּדַבְרָא. וְהוּא דְּאָמַר: ״הֵין הֵין״ וְהוּא דְּאָמַר: ״לָאו לָאו״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shefatya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If one was deliberating about whether to do a certain action, and a Divine Voice indicated what he should do, **from where** is it derived **that one may make use of a Divine Voice** and rely upon it? **As it is stated: "And your ears shall hear a word behind you saying:** This is the way, walk in it" (Isaiah 30:21). The Gemara comments: **This applies** only **when one heard a male voice in the city,** which is unusual, for men are usually found in the fields, **or** when one heard **a female voice in the fields,** for women are generally not found there. Since the voice is unusual, one need not doubt it and may rely upon it. **And that** applies **when** the voice repeated its message and **said: Yes, yes. And that** also applies **when** the voice **said: No, no.**

Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *minayin she-mishtamshin b'bas kol* — from where do we know that one is permitted to make use of a *bas kol* as a sign when facing a decision? The question concerns, for example, a person who is deliberating over a course of action — a business matter, a significant choice — and hears a voice say yes or no.

### Unusual Sound

Rashi explains that this is not considered *nichush* — divination — and one may indeed use it as a *siman*. However, the *gemara* qualifies this: *v'hani milei d'shama kol gavra b'mata* — this applies only when the sound is unusual, such as a man's voice heard in a city where the men would typically be out working in the fields, leaving women at home; *o it-ta b'davra* — or a woman's voice heard out in the field, where one would expect to find only men. The unusual character of the sound is what elevates it to the status of a meaningful sign. Whether the *gemara* imagines a genuinely disembodied heavenly voice — in which case the unusual quality would confirm that it is not a human voice at all — or whether it envisions an actual person's voice that is simply unexpected in its context, is not entirely clear from the text. A further requirement: *v'hu d'amar hin hin, v'hu d'amar lav lav* — it must be a double affirmation: yes, yes, or no, no. A single utterance is insufficient; only a repeated statement may be used as a *siman*.

### Tosafot Source

*Tosafot* traces the source to the Yerushalmi, which cites the verse *lo senachashu v'lo se'onenu* — do not divine and do not engage in sorcery — and then draws a distinction: *af al pi she'ein nichush, yesh siman* — even though divination is forbidden, one may still use hints and signs. There is thus a principled halakhic distinction between forbidden divination and the permitted use of a *siman*. This calls to mind the joke about the doctor who keeps a good-luck charm in his office: asked how a man of science could believe in such a superstition, he replies that he has heard it works even for those who don't believe in it — a wry illustration of the human instinct to seek signs, which the *gemara* here treats with careful legal precision.

## Trop & Melody

### Without Trop/Melody

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁפַטְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַקּוֹרֵא בְּלֹא נְעִימָה וְשׁוֹנֶה בְּלֹא זִמְרָה, עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְגַם אֲנִי נָתַתִּי לָהֶם חוּקִּים לֹא טוֹבִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shefatya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Concerning anyone who reads** from the Torah **without a melody or studies** the Mishna **without a song, the verse states: "So too I gave them statutes that were not good,** and judgments whereby they should not live" (Ezekiel 20:25), as one who studies Torah through song demonstrates that he is fond of his learning. Furthermore, the tune helps him remember what he has learned.

Rav Shefatya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hakoreh belo ne'imah v'hashoneh belo zimrah* — one who reads the Torah without *trop* (cantillation), or who learns *Mishnah* without a *nigun* (melody). *Tosafot* explains the latter: in an oral culture where *mishnayos* were transmitted by heart, learning them with a melody made them far more memorable and reliable. Anyone who once learned a large amount of Torah text with the cantillation will confirm this — whatever was learned with *trop* tends to be retained. The *trop* also serves the additional function of clarifying punctuation and the division of *pesukim*.

### Proof Text

> The verse cited is **"So too I gave them statutes that were not good, and judgments whereby they should not live"** (Ezekiel 20:25).

Regarding such a person, the verse says: *vegam ani nasati lahem chukim lo tovim, umishpatim lo yichyu bahem* — I have given them statutes that are not good and laws by which they shall not live. The verse is understood here not as God's declaration but as a *makkah* — an indictment — of those who have received the Torah but transmitted it improperly, rendering the *chukim* into something *lo tovim*.

### Abaye Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא יָדַע לְבַסּוֹמֵי קָלָא — ״מִשְׁפָּטִים לֹא יִחְיוּ בָּהֶם״ קָרֵית בֵּיהּ?! אֶלָּא כִּדְרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא, דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַיּוֹשְׁבִים בְּעִיר אַחַת וְאֵין נוֹחִין זֶה אֶת זֶה בַּהֲלָכָה — עֲלֵיהֶם הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְגַם אֲנִי נָתַתִּי לָהֶם חוּקִּים לֹא טוֹבִים וּמִשְׁפָּטִים לֹא יִחְיוּ בָּהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye strongly objects to this:** Just **because one does not know** how **to make** his **voice pleasant, you read concerning him: "And judgments whereby they should not live"? Rather,** the verse should be understood **in accordance** with the statement of **Rav Mesharshiyya, who said: Concerning two Torah scholars who dwell in the same city and are not pleasant to one other in** matters of ***halakha*,** and they quarrel and stir up controversy, **the verse states: "So too I gave them statutes that were not good, and judgments whereby they should not live."**

Abaye challenges this reading. His objection is that understanding the verse as a reference to poor vocal quality — someone who simply does not have a pleasant voice — does not justify the severity of *umishpatim lo yichyu bahem*. Saying the laws will be *lo tovim*, perhaps unpleasant to hear, is one thing; saying people will not live by them is something far more serious. Abaye therefore redirects to a teaching of Rav Masharshiya: *shnei talmidei chachamim hayoshvim b'ir achas v'ein nochim zeh lazeh b'halacha* — two Torah scholars who dwell in the same city and do not cooperate pleasantly with one another in matters of *halacha*. The *Maharsha* reads this as meaning they do not study together, each learning in isolation without *chavrusa* partnership, without the give-and-take of discussion and critique.

### Chavrusa Learning

It is regarding such scholars that the verse applies: *vegam ani nasati lahem chukim lo tovim, umishpatim lo yichyu bahem* — because without pushback, critique, and genuine discussion, one cannot arrive at correct legal conclusions. The laws will be perverted; they will not live by them. This connects to other *gemaros* that speak in similarly strong terms, such as *cherev al habadim v'no'alu* — a sword against those who learn in isolation rather than *b'chavrusa*. Only through genuine intellectual partnership is *halacha* properly preserved and transmitted.

## Improper Handling

### Naked Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי פַּרְנָךְ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הָאוֹחֵז סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עָרוֹם, נִקְבָּר עָרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Parnakh said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Anyone who holds onto an uncovered Torah scroll,** i.e., holds the parchment directly without anything intervening, **will be** punished by being **buried uncovered.**

Rav Parnach in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol ha'ochez Sefer Torah arom* — anyone who holds a *Sefer Torah* naked — meaning the scroll itself is naked, that is, the person is touching the *klaf* directly rather than handling it through the cover, the poles, or the *atzei chayim* — such a person is buried naked, *nikbar arom*.

### Abaye Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָרוֹם סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: נִקְבָּר עָרוֹם בְּלֹא מִצְוֹת. בְּלֹא מִצְוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נִקְבָּר עָרוֹם בְּלֹא אוֹתָהּ מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can it enter your mind to say** that he will actually be buried **uncovered?** Why should he be buried in such a disgraceful manner? **Rather, say** that **he will be buried** metaphorically **uncovered,** i.e., **without** the merit of having performed **mitzvot.** The Gemara again asks: **Can it enter your mind to say** that he will be buried **without** the merit of having performed **mitzvot?** Why should he forfeit the merit of all the mitzvot that he performed during his lifetime? **Rather, Abaye said: He will be buried** metaphorically **uncovered,** i.e., **without** the merit of **that mitzva.** He forfeits the reward of the mitzva he performed while holding directly onto the parchment.

The *gemara* immediately questions whether this is to be taken literally. Is touching the *klaf* directly so grave a sin that one is buried without shrouds? The *gemara* then escalates: perhaps it means one is buried *belo mitzvos* — stripped of all *mitzvos*? But that too seems disproportionate. Abaye resolves it: *nikbar arom belo osah mitzvah* — one is buried without that particular *mitzvah*; one forfeits the merit of that specific act.

### Tosafot Interpretation

*Tosafot* acknowledges that this ruling, taken at face value, seems obvious: if one performs a *mitzvah* improperly, of course one does not receive *schar* for it. The novelty, *Tosafot* explains, lies in a broader interpretation offered by the Riva: *belo osah mitzvah she'asah b'osah sha'ah* — one loses the credit not merely for the act of holding the scroll, but for whatever other *mitzvah* was being performed in that context. Thus, for example, if one was holding the *Sefer Torah* directly on the *klaf* and reading from it, *ein lo schar min hakriya* — one forfeits the merit of the reading itself. The improper handling undermines the merit of whatever was done in that context — whether *gelila*, reading, or any other act performed while touching the scroll directly.

## Laws by Season

### Roll Covers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי רַבָּה: מוּטָב תִּיגָּלֵל הַמִּטְפַּחַת וְאַל יִגָּלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai, son of Rabbi Yannai the Elder, said in the name of Rabbi Yannai the Great: It is proper** that **the cloth** cover of a Torah scroll **be rolled** around the scroll, while holding the scroll stationary, **and one should not roll the Torah scroll** itself in the cloth in order to cover it.

Rabbi Yannai the son of Rabbi Yannai the elder, citing in the name of Rabbi Yannai the elder — notably, here we see the *Sephardi minhag* of children bearing the same name as their father, and in this case grandfather, father, and son all bearing the name Yannai — teaches: *mutav sheyigollel hamatpachos v'al yigollel Sefer Torah* — better to roll the *matpachos* (coverings) than to roll the *Sefer Torah* itself.

### Rolling Order

Rashi explains: one should complete the rolling of the *Sefer Torah* first and only then place the cover over it. One should not put the cover on first and then do the tightening after the cover is already in place. The rolling is done to the scroll itself before it is wrapped.

### Festival Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְדַבֵּר מֹשֶׁה אֶת מוֹעֲדֵי ה׳ אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, מִצְוָתָן שֶׁיִּהְיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתָן כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד בִּזְמַנּוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מֹשֶׁה תִּיקֵּן לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיְּהוּ שׁוֹאֲלִין וְדוֹרְשִׁין בְּעִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם. הִלְכוֹת פֶּסַח — בַּפֶּסַח, הִלְכוֹת עֲצֶרֶת — בָּעֲצֶרֶת, הִלְכוֹת חַג — בֶּחָג.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: The verse **"And Moses declared to the children of Israel the appointed seasons of the Lord"** (Leviticus 23:44) indicates that part of **the mitzva** of the Festivals is **that they should read** the portion relating to them, **each one in its** appointed **time. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Moses enacted for the Jewish people that they should make** halakhic **inquiries and expound upon the matter of the day.** They should occupy themselves with **the *halakhot* of Passover on Passover,** with **the *halakhot* of *Shavuot* on *Shavuot*, and** with the ***halakhot* of *Sukkot* on *Sukkot*.**

The daf closes with the verse: *vayedabber Moshe es mo'adei Hashem el Bnei Yisrael* — and Moses spoke to the children of Israel of the appointed times of God — from which the *gemara* derives that the *mitzvah* of each *moed* includes reading the appropriate *parasha* at its proper time. The *baraita* then builds upon this: *Moshe tikken lahem leYisrael sheyihyu sho'alim vedorshim b'inyano shel yom* — Moses established that Israel should ask and discuss the laws appropriate to the day: *Hilchos Pesach bePesach, Hilchos Atzeres beAtzeres, Hilchos Chag beChag* — each set of laws in its proper season. There is, of course, a separate tradition requiring study *shloshim yom kodem hachag* — thirty days before the festival — which concerns the practical preparation needed well in advance. What is described here is something different and, in some ways, more profound.

### Ritual Learning

The study of each festival's laws at the time of the festival is not merely instrumental — not simply a matter of knowing what to do tonight at the *Seder*. It is itself a way of *mekayem* the nature of the day, an act through which one embraces and inhabits the meaning of the *moed*. Learning *Hilchos Pesach* on *Pesach* is what one does on *Pesach*; it is a form of ritual engagement with the holiday in its own right.

### Talmud Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ בְּנֵי הָעִיר וּסְלִיקָא לַהּ מַסֶּכֶת מְגִילָּה
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you chapter "The people of the city" and this is the completion of Tractate Megillah.

It is worth pausing to notice what the *masechet* has done here at its conclusion. An entire *perek* — one might say the whole of *Maseches Megillah* — has been devoted to the primacy of scripture: the reading of the *Megillah*, the sanctity of the *Beis Knesses*, the *Sefer Torah*, *kriyat haTorah*, all of this. The *masechet* even opened with the teaching that *talmud Torah* is set aside in favor of *mikra Megillah*. And yet at the very last line, when the *gemara* speaks of what one does on *Yom Tov*, the answer it gives is not *koreh* — one reads — but *sho'alim vedorshim* — one learns the *halachos*. Not reading from the Torah, but *talmud Torah*. The *masechet* that began by prioritizing *mikra* ends by returning to the dominant *rabbinic* theme: it is the *talmud Torah* of each season, the learning of its laws and their meaning, that stands at the center.

Rashi, concluding with characteristic precision, explains the *d'rashah* on the verse: *vayedabber* is superfluous — surely Moses conveyed all the commandments to Israel — and its presence teaches *shehayah medabber immahem hilchos kol mo'ed umo'ed bizmano*, that Moses would speak to them the laws of each *moed* in its proper time. And then, because it is the end of a *masechet*, both Rashi and *Tosafot* allow themselves a small flourish. Rashi closes with *bizmanam ulehodia chukei ha'Elokim vesorosav, vekibbelu vekiyemu schar hamitzvah aleihem v'al beneihem baze uvava* — they accepted it upon themselves and their children for this world and the next. *Tosafot*, in its final lines discussing the one who handles the *Sefer Torah* properly through its wrappings, concludes with the words *orech yamim bimina, bismola osher vachavod* — length of days is in its right hand, in its left are wealth and honor. A *masechet* concluded; a *siyum* well earned.
