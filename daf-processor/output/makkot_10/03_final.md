# Makkot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blind Person Debate

### Intro: Blind Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַסּוֹמֵא אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: גּוֹלֶה. הַשּׂוֹנֵא אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַשּׂוֹנֵא נֶהֱרָג, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוּעָד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹלֶה וְיֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לְדַעַת הָרַג – אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה, וְשֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת הָרַג – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A blind person** who unintentionally murdered another **is not exiled;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** He is **exiled. The enemy** of the victim **is not exiled,** as presumably it was not a completely unintentional act. **Rabbi Yosei says:** Not only is **an enemy** not exiled, but he **is executed** by the court, **because** his halakhic status is **like** that of one who is **forewarned** by witnesses not to perform the action, as presumably he performed the action intentionally. **Rabbi Shimon says: There is an enemy who is exiled and there is an enemy who is not exiled. This is the principle:** In **any** case **where** an observer **could say he killed knowingly,** where circumstances lead to the assumption that it was an intentional act, the enemy **is not exiled,** even if he claims that he acted unintentionally. **And** if it is clear that **he killed unknowingly,** as circumstances indicate that he acted unintentionally, **he is exiled,** even though the victim is his enemy.

The daf opens in the middle of a tannaitic dispute regarding the culpability of a blind person who unintentionally caused a death. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a blind person does not go into exile, while Rabbi Meir holds that he does. One might initially frame this debate as a question about degree of responsibility — how much awareness and caution can we attribute to a blind person navigating the world? — but the Gemara anchors the disagreement firmly in hermeneutical analysis of the relevant verses rather than in any general theory of responsibility.

### Hermeneutics: Exclude/Include

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – פְּרָט לְסוֹמֵא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos the dispute in the mishna between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir with regard to a blind person, **the Sages taught:** It is written that one is exiled to a city of refuge if he killed another **"without seeing"** (Numbers 35:23), indicating that the reference is to one who has the capacity to see. This serves **to exclude a blind person;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** On the contrary, the term **"without seeing"** serves **to include a blind person.**

The Gemara introduces a *baraita*: the phrase *belo re'ot*, "without seeing," implies that the killer could have seen his victim but failed to do so — which, according to Rabbi Yehuda, excludes (*prat l'suma*) a blind person for whom there was never any possibility of seeing at all. Rabbi Meir reads the same phrase in the opposite direction: *belo re'ot* comes specifically to include (*l'rabbot et hasuma*) even someone who cannot see.

### R. Yehuda: 'Without Seeing'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יָבֹא אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בַיַּעַר״ – אֲפִילּוּ סוֹמֵא. אֲתָא ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״, מַעֲטֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is **as it is written** with regard to those who are exiled: **"And as one who goes with his neighbor into the forest"** (Deuteronomy 19:5), which is stated in general terms, applying **even** to **a blind person.** The phrase **"without seeing" comes** and **excludes** a blind person from this *halakha*.

The Gemara then asks: what is Rabbi Yehuda's textual basis? The verse states, "and one who comes with his friend into the forest" — which encompasses even a blind person. Then follows the phrase *belo re'ot*, "without seeing," which implies that the killer could have seen, thereby excluding the blind person for whom sight was never possible. On this reading, Rabbi Yehuda's interpretation is internally coherent and flows naturally from the text.

### R. Meir: Two Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – לְמַעֵט, ״בִּבְלִי דַּעַת״ – לְמַעֵט, הָוֵי מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט, וְאֵין מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir** interprets the verses differently: **"Without seeing"** serves **to exclude** a blind person, and **"without knowledge"** (Deuteronomy 19:4) also serves **to exclude** a blind person. **This is** an example of **a restrictive expression following a restrictive expression, and** there is a hermeneutical principle that **a restrictive expression following a restrictive expression** serves **only to amplify** the *halakha* and include additional cases. In this case, it includes a blind person in the *halakhot* of exile as well.

Rabbi Meir's position requires more explanation. He concedes that *belo re'ot* carries an exclusionary force. However, there is a second verse, *bivli da'at*, "without awareness," which similarly comes to exclude — implying someone who could have been aware, as opposed to someone who could not have been. Now there are two exclusionary phrases, each pointing toward limiting the class of those who go into exile. The Gemara applies the classic rabbinic principle: *mi'ut achar mi'ut* — a double exclusion functions not as exclusion but as inclusion. Since both phrases converge in excluding the blind person, their combined effect is to include him. This is admittedly a striking piece of hermeneutics; the obvious rejoinder is simply that if the intention were to include, the Torah could have remained silent on both counts. The interpretive move is recognized as somewhat counterintuitive, even if the underlying legal position need not be.

### R. Yehuda: Intent Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? ״בִּבְלִי דַּעַת״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to that derivation? The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yehuda says that the phrase **"without knowledge" comes to exclude one with intent,** e.g., one who had intent to kill an animal and who killed a person, from the *halakha* of exile. Rabbi Yehuda interprets the two restrictive expressions as excluding two unrelated cases from the *halakha* of exile, and therefore the principle of: A restrictive expression following a restrictive expression serves only to amplify, is not relevant in this case.

Rabbi Yehuda, having already used *belo re'ot* for his exclusion of the blind person, must account for the second phrase, *bivli da'at*. He uses it to exclude intentional killing — not outright premeditated murder, but rather a case in which someone intended to kill one thing and inadvertently killed a person, such as aiming at an animal and striking a human being. That class of actor, who was in some sense aiming and choosing, falls outside the category of accidental homicide warranting exile.

## R. Yossi: Hater

### R. Yossi: Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר הַשּׂוֹנֵא נֶהֱרָג כּוּ׳. וְהָא לָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ! מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָבֵר אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ הַתְרָאָה, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה הַתְרָאָה אֶלָּא לְהַבְחִין בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yosei says: An enemy is executed** because his halakhic status is like that of one who is forewarned. The Gemara asks: **But** why is an enemy executed? The witness **did not** actually **forewarn him,** and courts administer corporal punishment only to a defendant who was forewarned. The Gemara answers: **The mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: One** who is **devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot [*ḥaver*] does not require forewarning** for the court to administer corporal punishment to him, **because** fundamentally the obligation to issue **forewarning was established only to distinguish between unwitting and intentional** actions. The presumptive status of a *ḥaver* is that of one who knows the *halakha*; therefore, his action is presumed to have been intentional.

The Mishna had established that a *soneh* — someone who bore a known enmity toward the victim — does not go into exile, grounding this in the verse *ve-hu lo soneh lo mitmol shilshom*, "and he was not his enemy from yesterday or the day before." The underlying logic is that when an enemy kills someone even under apparently accidental circumstances, we suspect the act was intentional. Rabbi Yosei now goes a step further: the *soneh* is actually executed.

### Responsibility Levels

The reasoning reflects a broader structural principle in these laws. *Galut* — exile — occupies a precise middle position on the spectrum of culpability. Someone too far removed from responsibility, such as the blind person in Rabbi Yehuda's view, does not deserve exile because he bears no meaningful fault. Someone too far toward culpability — such as the *soneh* — also does not receive *galut*, not because he is innocent but because he is too suspect to merit the protective *kapara* of exile. We cannot execute him for lack of absolute proof, but we will not grant him the benefit of the city of refuge either. Rabbi Yosei, however, holds that the presumption of intent is strong enough to execute him outright.

### Warning Problem

A significant difficulty immediately presents itself: where is the *hasro'ah*, the formal warning required before capital punishment can be administered? The witnesses may have observed what looked like an accident — a rope breaking, a barrel slipping — and even if they find the circumstances suspicious given the defendant's known enmity toward the deceased, the formal technical requirement of warning someone immediately before his act seems to be entirely missing. The Gemara addresses this.

## R. Yossi b. R. Yehuda

### Chaver: No Warning

The Gemara resolves the problem by attributing the position of Rabbi Yosei to the broader ruling of Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda: a *chaver* — a learned person who is expert in the law — does not require *hasro'ah*. The *baraita* states this explicitly: *chaver eino tzarich hasro'ah*.

### Purpose of Warning

The reason is that *hasro'ah* serves a single essential function: to distinguish between *shogeg* and *meizid*, between one who acts without knowledge and one who acts in deliberate defiance. The formal warning achieves this by ensuring, first, that the person is fully aware of what he is about to do and, second, that he knows it is forbidden. The Gemara in Sanhedrin elaborates that *hasro'ah* does even more — it creates the reality that the person is acting in direct rejection of legal authority, and by accepting the consequences (*hitir atzmo l'misa*), he licenses the court to execute him. But Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the sole indispensable function of warning is to establish willful intent, and nothing more.

### Hater: No Warning

Combining the two elements: a *soneh* who is also a *chaver* satisfies both requirements without formal warning. As a *chaver*, he knows the law and therefore knows his act is forbidden. As a *soneh*, we presume he is not acting by accident. Together, these facts substitute for the *hasro'ah*. The court may proceed to execution.

It must be said that this remains a striking conclusion. Even sworn enemies sometimes have genuine accidents, and the move from presumption to execution is a significant one. The question of how a *soneh* is established in the first place — what proof suffices to label someone a formal enemy of the deceased — is itself an open question, though it may be that this status was already presumed and on record prior to the incident. Whether witnesses observing an apparently accidental event can honestly attest to the intentionality of the act, or whether we are relying on a prior presumption to supply that element, is a tension that runs more naturally into a discussion of Sanhedrin than of Makkot.

## R. Shimon: Context

### Shimon: May/May Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹלֶה וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא: כֵּיצַד אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן יֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹלֶה וְיֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה? נִפְסַק – גּוֹלֶה, נִשְׁמַט – אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says: There is an enemy who is exiled** and there is an enemy who is not exiled. The Gemara adds: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: In **what** circumstances **did Rabbi Shimon say** that **there is an enemy who is exiled and there is an enemy who is not exiled?** He said that in a case where the rope **snapped** and the object attached to the rope fell and killed a person, he is **exiled,** as that appears to be an accident. But if an object **was displaced** from his hands, **he is not exiled,** as presumably he loosened his grip until it fell.

Rabbi Shimon takes what might be called a contextual or circumstantial approach. He holds that a *soneh* sometimes goes into exile and sometimes does not, depending on whether the particular circumstances of the killing make it possible to say (*yachol lomar*) that the act was intentional. This is an important nuance: Rabbi Shimon does not simply rule categorically based on the status of the parties, but requires an evaluation of the act itself.

### Rope Break vs. Slip

A *baraita* provides the concrete illustration. *Nifsak goleh* — if the rope broke, he goes into exile. *Nishmat eino goleh* — if it slipped out of his hands, he does not go into exile. The intuition is that if the rope broke (*nifsak*), the most natural explanation is that it was an accident, and even a *soneh* should receive *galut*. If it slipped out of his hands (*nishmat*), the circumstances lend themselves to the possibility that the release was intentional — he let go deliberately — and this opens up enough of a reasonable suspicion, even if not more likely than the innocent interpretation, to deny him exile.

### Contradiction: Two Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁמֵט מַחְצָלוֹ מִיָּדוֹ. קַשְׁיָא נִפְסַק אַנִּפְסַק, קַשְׁיָא נִשְׁמַט אַנִּשְׁמַט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: One is never exiled unless his trowel** with which he was working **was displaced from his hand?** As Rabbi Shimon stated that *halakha* without distinguishing between friend and enemy, therefore, the apparent contradiction between a case where the rope **snapped,** according to the first *baraita*, **and** a case where the rope **snapped,** according to the second *baraita*, is **difficult.** And the apparent contradiction between a case where the object **was displaced,** according to the first *baraita*, **and** a case where the object **was displaced,** according to the second *baraita*, is **difficult.**

A second *baraita*, however, appears to say the exact opposite: Rabbi Shimon holds that a person never goes into exile unless the tool slips (*nishmat*) from his hand. This would make the slipping case the one that leads to exile and the breaking case the one that does not — a direct reversal of the first *baraita*. The Gemara acknowledges the contradiction sharply: *kashya nifsak anifsak, kashya nishmat anishmat*.

### Resolution: Friend/Enemy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְסַק אַנִּפְסַק לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּאוֹהֵב, וְהָא בְּשׂוֹנֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The apparent contradiction between a case where the rope **snapped** according to the first *baraita* **and** a case where the rope **snapped** according to the second is **not difficult. This** case in the second *baraita* is referring **to a friend** of the victim, and presumably if the rope snapped it is considered a case of circumstances beyond his control and he is exempt from exile, **and that** case in the first *baraita* is referring **to an enemy** of the victim. In that case, although the presumption is that the act was not intentional, due to his enmity toward the victim, it is also not assumed to be the result of circumstances that were completely beyond his control. Therefore, he is exiled.

The Gemara resolves the first contradiction — *nishmat* versus *nishmat* — by distinguishing between the nature of the relationship. Where the actor is a *soneh*, the slipping (*nishmat*) case results in no exile because we suspect intentionality. Where the actor is an *ohev*, a friend, the slipping case does result in exile because there is no such suspicion and the act looks purely accidental. This resolution is clean and consistent.

### Rabbi/Rabbanan Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁמַט אַנִּשְׁמַט לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי, וְהָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The apparent contradiction between a case where the object **was displaced** according to the first *baraita* **and** a case where the object **was displaced** according to the second *baraita* is **not difficult,** as **this** case in the first *baraita* is the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who ruled in a mishna (7b) that if the blade of one's ax was displaced from its handle and flew through the air and killed a person, he is exempt from exile, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **and that** case in the second *baraita* is the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who rule that in that case, the person wielding the ax is exiled, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

The second contradiction — *nifsak* versus *nifsak* — is resolved differently. The Gemara invokes the dispute between Rabbi and Rabbanan regarding the axe-head case. Rabbanan hold that even when the actor is left holding part of the instrument while another part flies off and kills someone, exile is warranted. Rabbi holds that in such a case — where the actor retains a portion — there is no exile; the *goleh* case is specifically where, like a splinter, no part of the instrument remains in the actor's hand. Accordingly, one *baraita* presents the *nifsak* case from the perspective of Rabbanan (exile), and the other presents it from the perspective of Rabbi (no exile). Rabbi Shimon in the two *baraitot* is thus being read as operating according to two different tannaitic frameworks, which is itself somewhat unsettling — one might ask why a single *amora* would present Rabbi Shimon's ruling according to two competing tannaitic opinions rather than resolving the matter. The Gemara leaves this as its answer without further elaboration.

A note on the underlying dispute: Rabbi himself reads the Torah's paradigm case not as the axe-head flying off (*hashel habarzel min ha'etz* meaning the iron flies from the wood), but as the axe causing a chip of wood to fly off from the wood being chopped. The *Torah* case and the *Rabbanan* case are thus in dispute over the very reading of the scriptural paradigm.

## Cities of Refuge

### Six Cities Total

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לְהֵיכָן גּוֹלִין? לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט, לְשָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן וּלְשָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵת שְׁלֹשׁ הֶעָרִים תִּתְּנוּ מֵעֵבֶר לַיַּרְדֵּן וְאֵת שְׁלֹשׁ הֶעָרִים תִּתְּנוּ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן וְגוֹ׳״. עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִבְחֲרוּ שָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא הָיוּ שָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן קוֹלְטוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שֵׁשׁ עָרֵי מִקְלָט תִּהְיֶינָה״ – עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שֶׁשְׁתָּן קוֹלְטוֹת כְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **To where are** the unintentional murderers **exiled?** They are exiled **to cities of refuge, to three** cities **that** were **in** the east **bank of the Jordan and to three cities that** were **in the land of Canaan,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **as it is stated: "Three cities shall you give beyond the Jordan and three cities shall you give in the land of Canaan;** they shall be cities of refuge" (Numbers 35:14). The mishna comments: **Until** the **three** cities of refuge **that** were **in Eretz Yisrael were selected,** an unintentional murderer **would not be admitted** to the **three that** were **in** the east **bank of the Jordan,** even though the latter three were already selected by Moses (see Deuteronomy 4:41), **as it is stated: "Six cities of refuge shall they be"** (Numbers 35:13), from which it is derived that they do not become cities of refuge **until** all **six of them admit** unintentional murderers **as one.**

The Gemara now turns to the *arei miklat* — the cities of refuge. The Mishna establishes that there were three on each side of the Jordan: three designated by Moshe in the Transjordan and three designated by Yehoshua in the land of Canaan, as the verse states: "You shall give three cities across the Jordan and three cities you shall give in the land of Canaan."

### Simultaneous Operation

A key operational principle follows: the three cities in the Transjordan did not function as operative *arei miklat* until the three in the land of Israel had been designated as well, a process that occurred only after fourteen years of conquest and apportionment. The verse states *sheish arei miklat tihyena* — there should be six — and the Gemara derives: *ad sheyiyu sheshtan koltot ke'achas*, until all six are operating simultaneously.

### Roads Between Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְכֻוּוֹנוֹת לָהֶן דְּרָכִים מִזּוֹ לָזוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תָּכִין לְךָ הַדֶּרֶךְ וְשִׁלַּשְׁתָּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And roads were aligned for them from this** city, i.e., all cities, **to that** city, i.e., they would pave and straighten the access roads to the cities of refuge, **as it is stated: "Prepare for you the road, and divide** the borders of your land, which the Lord your God causes you to inherit, into three parts, that every murderer may flee there" (Deuteronomy 19:3).

Highways were constructed connecting the cities to one another. This is somewhat surprising — one might expect the roads to lead from the general population toward the cities, since a fugitive fleeing from anywhere in the land needs to reach a city quickly. The focus on roads between the cities themselves, rather than roads leading to them, is not entirely clear, though it may be that establishing a network of clear roads connecting the six created a kind of major route structure that made access from adjacent regions more direct. The verse *tachin lecha haderech v'shilashta* — "you shall prepare the road and divide into thirds" — is cited as the source.

### Talmidei Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹסְרִין לָהֶן שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמָּא יַהַרְגֶנּוּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וִידַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף הוּא מְדַבֵּר עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֶה דְּבַר הָרֹצֵחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the court **would provide** the unintentional murderers fleeing to a city of refuge with **two Torah scholars,** due to the concern that **perhaps** the blood redeemer, i.e., a relative of the murder victim seeking to avenge his death, **will** seek to **kill him in transit, and** in that case **they,** the scholars, **will talk to** the blood redeemer and dissuade him from killing the unintentional murderer. **Rabbi Meir says:** The unintentional murderer **also speaks [*medabber*] on his own behalf** to dissuade the blood redeemer, **as it is stated: "And this is the matter [*devar*] of the murderer,** who shall flee there and live" (Deuteronomy 19:4), indicating that the murderer himself speaks.

Two *talmidei chachamim* were dispatched to accompany the person fleeing to the city of refuge. The concern was that the *goel hadam* — the blood avenger — might pursue the fugitive en route and kill him before he reached safety. The *talmidei chachamim* were to speak to the *goel hadam* and dissuade him from violence. One might note that two scholars seem a curious choice for what amounts to a security detail; presumably their authority and ability to invoke the law carried more weight than physical deterrence. The system reflects a broader effort to bring what had been a practice of vigilante justice under institutional and rabbinic control — a dynamic that has an analogue in the *sotah* procedure as treated in Gemara Sotah, where the court similarly inserts itself by having *talmidei chachamim* accompany the husband.

### R. Meir: Self-Defense

Rabbi Meir holds that the fugitive himself may speak to the *goel hadam* and argue his own case, citing the verse *vezeh d'var harotzeach* — "and this is the matter of the killer" — as indicating that the killer himself has a voice in the proceeding. The practical implication is somewhat unclear. The *tanna kamma* does not appear to prohibit self-advocacy; the difference may be that Rabbi Meir dispenses with the need for accompanying *talmidei chachamim* entirely, believing that the fugitive's own words suffice, while others may hold that self-advocacy would be counterproductive or that the *talmidei chachamim* serve an independent institutional function regardless.

### Initial Custody Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּתְּחִלָּה, אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד מַקְדִּימִין לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט וּבֵית דִּין שׁוֹלְחִין וּמְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ מִשָּׁם. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה בְּבֵית דִּין – הֲרָגוּהוּ, וְשֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה – פְּטָרוּהוּ. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב גָּלוּת – מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיבוּ אֹתוֹ הָעֵדָה אֶל עִיר מִקְלָטוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says: Initially, either** one who killed another **unintentionally or** one who killed another **intentionally** would **hurry** and flee **to the cities of refuge, and the court** in his city would **send** for him **and** would **bring him from there** to stand trial. For **one who was** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty **in court** for intentional murder, the court would **execute him, and** for **one who was not** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty, e.g., if they deemed that the death occurred due to circumstances beyond his control, **they** would **free him.** For **one who was** found **liable to be exiled,** the court would **restore him to his place** in the city of refuge, **as it is stated:** "And the congregation shall judge between the murderer and the blood redeemer…**and the congregation shall restore him to his city of refuge,** that he fled there" (Numbers 35:24–25), indicating that he had been in a city of refuge before his trial.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda introduces a general procedural rule: at the outset (*batchila*), whether the homicide appears to be *shogeg* or *meizid*, all such individuals are first sent to the city of refuge. Rashi clarifies that *batchila* here does not denote an earlier historical practice that was later changed; rather, it describes the first step in the process — regardless of the suspect's claims of innocence, the initial response is to direct everyone to the *ir miklat* in order to control the situation. Afterward, the *beit din* summons the person and tries the case.

### Three Outcomes

From the city of refuge, three outcomes are possible. One found guilty of intentional murder (*mechuyav mita*) is executed. One who is fully exonerated (*lo nischayev mita*) is released entirely. And one who committed a genuine *shogeg* killing (*nischayev galut*) is returned — in the sense of formally confirmed — to the city of refuge. The verse *v'heshivu oso ha'eidah el ir miklato* is cited as the source for this last category.

## Geographic Distribution

### Named Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁלֹשׁ עָרִים הִבְדִּיל מֹשֶׁה בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן וּכְנֶגְדָּן הִבְדִּיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, וּמְכֻוּוֹנוֹת הָיוּ כְּמִין שְׁתֵּי שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁבַּכֶּרֶם: חֶבְרוֹן בְּהַר יְהוּדָה כְּנֶגֶד בֶּצֶר בַּמִּדְבָּר, שְׁכֶם בְּהַר אֶפְרַיִם כְּנֶגֶד רָמוֹת בַּגִּלְעָד, קֶדֶשׁ בְּהַר נַפְתָּלִי כְּנֶגֶד גּוֹלָן בַּבָּשָׁן. ״וְשִׁלַּשְׁתָּ״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ מְשׁוּלָּשִׁין, שֶׁיְּהֵא מִדָּרוֹם לְחֶבְרוֹן כְּמֵחֶבְרוֹן לִשְׁכֶם, וּמֵחֶבְרוֹן לִשְׁכֶם כְּמִשְּׁכֶם לְקֶדֶשׁ, וּמִשְּׁכֶם לְקֶדֶשׁ כְּמִקֶּדֶשׁ לַצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: Moses designated three cities** of refuge **in the** east **bank of the Jordan, and corresponding to them, Joshua designated** three cities of refuge **in the land of Canaan. And the cities were aligned like two rows** of vines **in a vineyard:** In Eretz Yisrael there was **Hebron in Mount Judea, corresponding to Bezer in the wilderness; Shechem in Mount Ephraim, corresponding to Ramoth in the Gilead;** and **Kadesh in Mount Naphtali, corresponding to Golan in the Bashan.** From the term **"And you shall divide [*veshilashta*]"** (Deuteronomy 19:3), it is derived **that** the three cities in Eretz Yisrael will serve as **three [*meshulashin*]** lines of demarcation dividing the length of the land into four equal parts, in a manner **that** the distance **from the southern** border of Eretz Yisrael **to Hebron,** the southernmost city of refuge, **will be like** the distance **from Hebron to Shechem, and** the distance **from Hebron to Shechem** will be **like** the distance **from Shechem to Kadesh, and** the distance **from Shechem to Kadesh** will be **like** the distance **from Kadesh to the northern** border.

The *baraita* lists the six cities by name. On the west bank of the Jordan, in the land of Israel: Hebron in Judah, Shechem in Mount Ephraim, and Kedesh in Naftali. On the east bank: Betzer in the wilderness, Ramous in Gilead, and Golan in Bashan.

### Parallel Arrangement

The cities were arranged in parallel, like rows in a vineyard (*k'min shtei shurot shebakarem*), such that the cities on one side of the Jordan corresponded directly to those on the other. This implies not only that they were matched pairs but that each set was arrayed in a straight line north to south, so that Chevron corresponded to Betzer, Shechem to Ramous, and Kedesh to Golan.

### Equal Spacing

The verse's instruction *ushilashta* — "you shall divide into thirds" — is understood to require equal spacing: the distance from the southern border to Chevron equals the distance from Chevron to Shechem, which equals the distance from Shechem to Kedesh, which equals the distance from Kedesh to the northern border. This technically divides the land into four equal segments with three evenly spaced cities — hence the word "thirds" refers to the three intervals created by three points, even though the land is effectively quartered.

### Gilead Murderers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן תְּלָת, בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל תְּלָת?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּגִלְעָד שְׁכִיחִי רוֹצְחִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״גִּלְעָד קִרְיַת פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן עֲקֻבָּה מִדָּם״, מַאי ״עֲקוּבָּה מִדָּם״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹקְבִין לַהֲרוֹג נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the distribution of the cities: Why were there **three** cities designated **on** the east **bank of the Jordan,** where two and a half tribes resided, and **three** cities designated **in Eretz Yisrael,** where more than nine tribes resided? **Abaye said: In Gilead,** which is located on the east bank of the Jordan, **murderers are common.** Therefore, a greater number of cities of refuge per capita were required there, **as it is written: "Gilead is a city of those who work iniquity; it is covered [*akuba*] with blood"** (Hosea 6:8). **What** is the meaning of: **Covered [*akuba*] with blood? Rabbi Elazar says:** It means **that they would set an ambush [*okevin*] to kill people.**

A geographic asymmetry presents itself: three cities serve the two and a half tribes of Transjordan, while three also serve the much more populous west bank. Why the equal distribution? Abaye explains: *be-Gilad shechichi rotzchim* — murderers were prevalent in Gilead. The verse supports this: *Gilead kiryat poalei aven, akuva mi-dam* — Gilead is a city of evildoers, steeped in blood. Rabbi Elazar adds that *akuva mi-dam* means they would *plot* to kill (*oklavim laharog nefashot*), not merely act in passion. The higher frequency of homicides in the Transjordan justified having three cities serve that smaller population.

### Shechem Murderers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וּמֵהַאי גִּיסָא דִּמְרַחֲקִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא מְצִיעָאֵי דִּמְקָרְבִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁכֶם נָמֵי שְׁכִיחִי רוֹצְחִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּכְחַכֵּי אִישׁ גְּדוּדִים חֶבֶר כֹּהֲנִים, דֶּרֶךְ יְרַצְּחוּ שֶׁכְמָה וְגוֹ׳״, מַאי ״חֶבֶר כֹּהֲנִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁהָיוּ מִתְחַבְּרִין לַהֲרוֹג נְפָשׁוֹת, כְּכֹהֲנִים הַלָּלוּ שֶׁמִּתְחַבְּרִין לַחְלוֹק תְּרוּמוֹת בְּבֵית הַגֳּרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about the cities of refuge closest to the border on **this** southern **side** of the country **and from** the border on **that** northern **side** of the country, **that are distanced** one-quarter of the length of Eretz Yisrael from the border, **and what is different** about the city of refuge in the **middle** of the country, **which is** relatively **close** to any potential murderers? The maximum distance that one would need to travel to reach the middle city is one-half the distance from the northern and southern borders to their respective cities of refuge. **Abaye said: Murderers are also common in Shechem, as it is written: "And as troops of robbers wait for a man, so does the band of priests; they murder in the way toward Shechem,** yes, they commit enormity" (Hosea 6:9). **What** is the meaning of **"the band of priests"? Rabbi Elazar says:** It means **that** the people **would band together to kill people, like those priests who band together to distribute *teruma*** among themselves **in the granaries.**

Another problem: even within the land of Israel itself, the arrangement is not fully equitable. The cities in the north and south are farther apart, meaning that an inhabitant of the peripheral regions faces a longer maximum journey than someone in the center, where Shechem sits. The average distance to an *ir miklat* for someone in the middle of the land is correspondingly shorter than for someone at the edges. Abaye answers again: *be-Shechem nami shechichi rotzchim* — Shechem too had a disproportionately high rate of murderers, justifying placing a city of refuge there at the center of the land. The verse from Hoshea is cited: "As troops of robbers wait for a man, so does the band of priests — they murder in the way toward Shechem." The reference to priests (*kohanim*) is explained by Rabbi Elazar: just as *kohanim* band together to go to the threshing floors to collect *terumah*, so the people of that region would band together to commit murder. The explanation is admittedly somewhat elliptical, but the association of Shechem with violence is established through the verse.

### Six vs. Forty-Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַעֲלֵיהֶם תִּתְּנוּ אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁתַּיִם עִיר״! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַלָּלוּ, קוֹלְטוֹת בֵּין לְדַעַת בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת. הַלָּלוּ – לְדַעַת קוֹלְטוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אֵינָן קוֹלְטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are there no more** cities of refuge beyond these six? **But isn't it written:** "And the cities that you shall give to the Levites: The six cities of refuge you shall give for the murderer to flee there, **and beyond them you shall give forty-two cities"** (Numbers 35:6), indicating that the status of all the Levite cities is that of cities of refuge? **Abaye said:** With regard to **these** six cities designated specifically for this purpose, unintentional murderers in need of refuge are **admitted** there **whether** they entered the cities **deliberately,** aware that they are cities of refuge, or **whether** they entered **inadvertently.** By contrast, with regard to **those** forty-two Levite cities, unintentional murderers are **admitted** only if they entered the cities **deliberately,** but if they entered the cities **inadvertently,** they are **not admitted** to the cities.

The Gemara notes that the Torah also commands the addition of 42 cities of refuge beyond the primary six (the cities of the Levites designated to function as secondary *arei miklat*). Abaye distinguishes between them: the six primary cities provide sanctuary whether the fugitive arrived there intentionally seeking refuge or merely happened to be present (*bein le-daat bein she-lo le-daat*). The 42 secondary cities provide legal sanctuary only if the fugitive went there specifically for the purpose of seeking refuge (*le-daat*); incidental presence does not protect.

## Hebron & Kadesh

### Hebron: Suburbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחֶבְרוֹן עִיר מִקְלָט הֲוַא[י]? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְּנוּ לְכָלֵב אֶת חֶבְרוֹן כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר מֹשֶׁה״! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פַּרְווֹדֶהָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת שְׂדֵה הָעִיר וְאֶת חֲצֵרֶיהָ נָתְנוּ לְכָלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is Hebron a city of refuge? But isn't it written: "And they gave Hebron to Caleb, as Moses had spoken"** (Judges 1:20)? This indicates that Hebron belonged to Caleb from the tribe of Judah, and it was not a Levite city. **Abaye said: Its suburbs [*parvadaha*]** were given to Caleb; the city itself was a city of priests, **as it is written** in the context of the distribution of the Levite cities: "And they gave them Kiryat Arba…which is Hebron…**and the field of the city and its courtyards they gave to Caleb, son of Jephunneh"** (Joshua 21:11–12).

The Gemara raises a textual difficulty: is Hebron really an *ir miklat*? The verse states that Caleb was given Hebron, as Moshe had promised him — which seems inconsistent with it being a public city. Abaye resolves this: what was given to Caleb was the *suburb* (*parvara*) of the city, its surrounding fields and outlying areas. The verse itself says, "the fields of the city and its courtyards they gave to Caleb ben Yefuneh" — meaning only the surrounding land, not the city itself, which retained its status as a public *ir miklat*.

### Kadesh: Village

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקֶדֶשׁ עִיר מִקְלָט הֲוַי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְעָרֵי מִבְצָר הַצִּדִּים צֵר וְחַמַּת רַקַּת וְכִנָּרֶת וְגוֹ׳ וְקֶדֶשׁ וְאֶדְרֶעִי וְעֵין חָצוֹר״, וְתַנְיָא: עָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן לֹא טִירִין קְטַנִּים וְלֹא כְּרַכִּים גְּדוֹלִים אֶלָּא עֲיָירוֹת בֵּינוֹנִיּוֹת! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תַּרְתֵּי קֶדֶשׁ הֲוַאי. (אָמַר) רַב אָשֵׁי [אָמַר]: כְּגוֹן סְלֵיקוּם וְאַקְרָא דִּסְלֵיקוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And is Kadesh a city of refuge? But isn't it written: "And the fortified cities were Ziddim Zer, and Hammath, Rakkath, and Chinnereth…and Kedesh and Edrei and En Hazor"** (Joshua 19:35–37), **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **these cities** of refuge, **one does not establish them** in **small settlements [*tirin*] or** in **large cities; rather,** one establishes them in **intermediate**-sized **towns?** Apparently, Kadesh was a large, fortified city. **Rav Yosef said: There were two** cities named **Kedesh,** and the one listed among the fortified cities in the book of Joshua is not the one that was a city of refuge. **Rav Ashi said:** The listing of Kadesh among the fortified cities is not difficult, as it is **similar to** the two adjacent yet separate cities of **Selikum and the fortification [*ve'akra*] of Selikum.** Likewise, there was the fortified city of Kadesh, mentioned in Joshua, and the city itself, which was an intermediate city that served as city of refuge.

A parallel question is raised about Kadesh: can it be an *ir miklat* if the Torah elsewhere lists it among the fortified cities (*arei mivtzar*)? A *baraita* rules that *arei miklat* must be medium-sized towns — not small villages and not large cities. A major fortified city would fall into the prohibited category of *kerachim gedolim*. Two answers are offered. Rav Yosef says there were simply two separate places named Kadesh — one the fortified city, the other the *ir miklat*. Rav Ashi offers a different resolution, analogous to the Hebron answer: just as there is a city called Selikum and a separate place called "the *akra* of Selikum" (i.e., the fortified citadel of Selikum), so too there is Kadesh the large fortified city and Kadesh the town nearby, which is the *ir miklat*. On this reading, the two Kadeshes are not entirely separate localities but a city and its smaller adjacent community.

## Requirements & Rules

### Town Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: עָרִים הַלָּלוּ, אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן לֹא טִירִין קְטַנִּים וְלֹא כְּרַכִּין גְּדוֹלִים, אֶלָּא עֲיָירוֹת בֵּינוֹנִיּוֹת. וְאֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם מַיִם, וְאִם אֵין שָׁם מַיִם – מְבִיאִין לָהֶם מַיִם. וְאֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שְׁווֹקִים, וְאֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם אוּכְלוּסִין. נִתְמַעֲטוּ אוּכְלוּסֵיהֶן – מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶן. נִתְמַעֲטוּ דִּיּוּרֵיהֶן – מְבִיאִין לָהֶם כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself,** and it cites the complete *baraita*: With regard to **these cities** of refuge, **one does not establish them** in **small settlements or** in **large cities; rather,** one establishes them in **intermediate-**sized **towns. And one establishes them only in a place** where **water** is available, **and if there is no water** available **there,** as there is no spring accessible from the city, **one brings them water** by digging a canal. **And one establishes them only in a place** where there are **markets, and one establishes them only in a populated place,** where there are many people who regularly frequent the town. If the **population** of the surrounding areas **diminishes, one adds to it.** If the number of **residents** in the city of refuge itself **diminishes, one brings** new residents to the city, among them **priests, Levites, and Israelites.**

Returning to the *baraita* itself: *arei miklat* must be medium-sized towns — not too small and not too large. Extremes in either direction would be inappropriate.

### Water & Markets

The cities must be settled only where there is water; if natural water is unavailable, water must be brought in artificially. They must also have markets (*shevakim*), since the inhabitants require access to food and provisions.

### Population Needs

The cities must be placed where there is an *ochlusin* — a surrounding population — so that they are not isolated. Rashi explains that this requirement serves a security function: an isolated *ir miklat* would be vulnerable to attack by a *goel hadam* and others; the presence of surrounding towns reduces that vulnerability. If the internal population of the city itself (*diyureihem*) declines, it must be replenished — by whatever means necessary — and the repopulated city must include *kohanim*, *Leviyim*, and *Yisraelim* in a proper social diversity.

### Weapon Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹכְרִין בָּהֶן לֹא כְּלֵי זַיִין, וְלֹא כְּלֵי מְצוּדָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאֵין פּוֹרְסִין בְּתוֹכָן מְצוּדוֹת, וְאֵין מַפְשִׁילִין לְתוֹכָן חֲבָלִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא רֶגֶל גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם מְצוּיָה שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And one may not sell weapons or hunting tools** in the cities of refuge, to prevent the blood redeemer from gaining access to means that he could exploit to kill the unintentional murderer who fled to the city of refuge; this is **the statement of Rabbi Neḥemya. And the Rabbis permit** selling weapons and hunting tools. **And** Rabbi Neḥemya and the Rabbis **agree that one may not spread nets in** the cities of refuge, **nor may they braid [*mafshilin*] ropes in** those cities, **so that the foot of the blood redeemer will not be found there.** If the blood redeemer were to enter the city of refuge to purchase nets or ropes, he is apt to encounter the murderer and kill him.

Weapons (*klei zayin*) may not be sold in the *ir miklat*, nor may trapping implements (*klei metzudah*). The reason is self-evident: the city of refuge must not become a place where a *goel hadam* can conveniently acquire the means to kill the fugitive he is pursuing.

### Nets & Ropes

Rabbi Nechemia's position extends the prohibition to trapping implements generally. The *chachamim* permit selling trapping implements but agree that nets (*metzudot*) may not be spread out within the city, and ropes (*chavalim*) may not be laid or strung there either — *kedei shelo tehei regel Goel HaDam metzuyah* — so as not to encourage the *goel hadam* to frequent the city on the pretense of conducting his craft, as he might find occasion to use such things against the fugitive. The precise distinction between what the *chachamim* permit and what they prohibit remains somewhat unclear even after the Gemara's explanation.

### Verse: 'And Live'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי קְרָא? ״וְנָס אֶל אַחַת מִן הֶעָרִים הָאֵל וָחָי״ – עֲבֵיד לֵיהּ מִידֵּי דְּתֶהְוֵי לֵיהּ חִיּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What is the verse** from which these matters are derived? It is written: **"And he shall flee to one of these cities and live"** (Deuteronomy 4:42), meaning: **Perform some** actions **for** the unintentional murderer so **that** life in the city of refuge **will be** conducive to **living for him.** All these steps are taken to facilitate that objective.

Rabbi Yitzchak derives from the verse *vanas el achat he'arim ha'eleh vachai* — "he shall flee to one of these cities and live" — that the city of refuge must genuinely sustain life: *avid lei midei d'tehavi lei chiyuta*, one must provide the fugitive with everything necessary for his subsistence once he arrives.

### Student-Teacher Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: תַּלְמִיד שֶׁגָּלָה – מַגְּלִין רַבּוֹ עִמּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָחַי״ – עֲבֵיד לֵיהּ מִידֵּי דְּתֶהְוֵי לֵיהּ חִיּוּתָא. אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירָא: מִכָּאן שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁנֶה אָדָם לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught:** In the case of **a student who was exiled, his teacher is exiled** to the city of refuge **with him,** so that the student can continue studying Torah with him there, **as it is stated:** "And he shall flee to one of these cities **and live,"** from which it is derived: **Perform some** actions **for** the unintentional murderer so **that** life in the city **will be** conducive to **living for him.** Since Torah study is an integral component of his life, arrangements must be made to ensure continuity in that facet of his existence. **Rabbi Zeira says: From here** one learns **that a person should not teach a student who is not fit,** as that may result in the teacher following the student into exile.

A *baraita* rules: if a student goes into exile, his *rebbe* goes with him — derived from the same word *vachai*, "and live," understood as encompassing spiritual life. Without Torah study, the student cannot truly live; without his teacher, he cannot learn. The obligation that the teacher accompany his exiled student into the city of refuge is an application of this principle. This is admittedly a striking imposition on the teacher, who is uprooted from his home and community and, presumably, from his other students — for the sake of one student who accidentally killed someone. The Gemara does not pause to address this tension but moves forward with the ruling.

Rabbi Zeira draws an additional lesson: one should not teach a student who is unworthy (*talmid she'eino hagun*). The implicit assumption is that when someone accidentally causes a death, some degree of moral or spiritual deficiency is involved — whether because divine providence arranges such accidents specifically as punishment for negligence in the sanctity of human life, or because a person of truly refined character would be so vigilant that such accidents would not occur. The *rebbe*'s fate of exile is thus the consequence of having accepted such a student.

## Torah as Sanctuary

### Torah Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָרַב שֶׁגָּלָה – מַגְּלִין יְשִׁיבָתוֹ עִמּוֹ. אִינִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה שֶׁהֵן קוֹלְטִין? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת בֶּצֶר בַּמִּדְבָּר וְגוֹ׳״, [וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ:] ״וְזֹאת הַתּוֹרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **a teacher** of Torah **who was exiled, his school is exiled with him.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so** that a teacher of Torah is exiled? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **say: From where** is it derived **that matters of Torah provide refuge,** i.e., that the blood redeemer may not harm one who is engaged in Torah? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "Bezer in the wilderness,** in the flatlands, for the Reubenites; and Ramoth in Gilead, for the Gadites; and Golan in Bashan, for the Manassites" (Deuteronomy 4:43), in the list of cities of refuge designated by Moses, **and it is written thereafter: "And this is the Torah"** (Deuteronomy 4:44). Based on that juxtaposition it is derived that the status of Torah is like that of a city of refuge.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: how do we know that words of Torah provide sanctuary (*koltin*)? From the juxtaposition of the verse citing *Betzer bamidbar* (one of the *arei miklat*) with the immediately following verse *vezot haTorah* — "and this is the Torah." The Torah itself thus serves as an *ir miklat*.

### Active Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא – בְּעִידָּנָא דְּעָסֵיק בַּהּ, הָא – בְּעִידָּנָא דְּלָא עָסֵיק בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, that the status of Torah is like that of a city of refuge, is referring to Torah **at the time that one is engaged in its** study, and **that** statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, that the teacher of Torah must take his school to the city of refuge, is referring to the teacher of Torah **at the time that he is not engaged in its** study. His mere presence in a city of refuge provides him with continuous protection.

The Gemara qualifies this: there is no contradiction between a ruling that Torah provides sanctuary and the requirement to actually go to a city of refuge. The resolution is that Torah provides its protection only *b'idna d'asik bah* — during the time when one is actively engaged in study. The moment one stops, the protection lapses. This naturally raises a question: if Torah provides sanctuary for a *talmid chacham*, does he still need to go to an *ir miklat*? The Gemara's focus here is on the protection aspect alone. But *galut* serves not only as protection but as *kapara* and punishment — which Torah study, however potent, does not substitute for. The Gemara does not resolve this tension explicitly in this passage.

### David Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, מַאי קוֹלְטִין – מִמַּלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת. כִּי הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא הֲוָה יָתֵיב וְגָרֵיס בְּבֵי רַב, וְלָא הֲוָה קָא יָכֵיל שְׁלִיחָא [דְּמַלְאֲכָא דְּמוֹתָא] לְמִיקְרַב לְגַבֵּיהּ, דְּלָא הֲוָה שָׁתֵיק פּוּמֵּיהּ מִגִּירְסָא, סְלֵיק וִיתֵיב אַאַרְזָא דְּבֵי רַב, פְּקַע אַרְזָא וּשְׁתֵיק, וִיכֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say: What** is the meaning of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that matters of Torah **provide refuge?** It means protection, but not for an unintentional murderer from the blood redeemer; rather, it means protection **from the Angel of Death.** This is **as** it was in **this** incident **where Rav Ḥisda was sitting and studying in the study hall of Rav and the agent of the Angel of Death was unable to approach him** and take his life **because his mouth was not silent from his study** for even a moment. The agent **ascended and sat on the cedar** tree **of the study hall of Rav. The cedar** tree **broke and Rav Ḥisda was** momentarily **silent,** startled by the sudden noise, **and** the agent of the Angel of Death **overcame him.** Apparently, matters of Torah provide protection from the Angel of Death only when one is actively engaged in their study.

The protection afforded by active Torah study is illustrated by the story of David — or, more precisely, by an analogous account involving Rav Chisda. The *malach hamavet*, the angel of death, could not approach him because the words of Torah never left his lips (*d'la hava pasik pumieh migirsa*). The angel of death then went and sat in the cedar tree of Rav's study house. When the tree split — startling Rav Chisda into a moment of silence — the angel was able to claim him. The story dramatizes the principle that Torah protection is contingent on continuous, uninterrupted study; the moment it ceases, one is exposed. Notably, Rabbi Yochanan's teaching about *koltin* is clarified to refer not to protection from the *goel hadam* but from the *malach hamavet* himself: *koltin* means the Torah protects from death.

## Merits & Rewards

### Reuben: First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה רְאוּבֵן לִימָּנוֹת בְּהַצָּלָה תְּחִלָּה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פָּתַח בְּהַצָּלָה תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע רְאוּבֵן וַיַּצִּלֵהוּ מִיָּדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai says: For what** reason **was Reuben privileged to be enumerated first in the rescue,** as the first city of refuge listed is Bezer (see Deuteronomy 4:43), which is located in the tribal portion of Reuben? It is **due to** the fact **that he began the rescue** of Joseph **first, as it is stated: "And Reuben heard and delivered him from their hands"** (Genesis 37:21).

Rabbi Tanchum bar Chanilai asks: why did Reuben merit being the first mentioned in connection with the *hatzalah* — the rescue provided by the *arei miklat*, whose first-cited cities were those in the territory of Reuben? Because Reuben was the first to attempt rescue: *Vayishma Reuven Vayatzileihu Miyadam* — Reuben heard and saved Yosef from his brothers' hands. He did not succeed in the end, but he was the one who spoke up and tried to prevent the killing. That merit was rewarded by making the Transjordanian cities — situated in his territory — the first mentioned in the Torah's discussion of refuge.

### Sun Shines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אָז יַבְדִּיל מֹשֶׁה שָׁלֹשׁ עָרִים בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן מִזְרְחָה [שָׁמֶשׁ]״? אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: הַזְרַח שֶׁמֶשׁ לָרוֹצְחִים. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אָמַר לוֹ: הִזְרַחְתָּ שֶׁמֶשׁ לָרוֹצְחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Simlai taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Then Moses separated three cities beyond the Jordan, to the east of the sun [*mizreḥa shamesh*]"** (Deuteronomy 4:41)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: Shine the sun [*hazraḥ shemesh*] for murderers,** i.e., provide them with the hope of rescue. **Some say** that God **said to** Moses: In designating these cities of refuge **you have shined the sun for murderers.**

Rabbi Simlai expounds the verse: *Az yavdil Moshe shalosh arim b'ever haYarden mizrach shamesh* — "Then Moshe set apart three cities across the Jordan toward the sunrise." God said to Moshe: *mizrach shamesh lerotzchin* — let the sun shine for the murderers, meaning, ensure that even those fleeing to the cities of refuge have what they need. The term *rotzchin* — murderers — is striking given that these are people who committed unintentional homicide, but the word is used in the verse (*vezeh d'var harotzeach*) without distinction. An alternative reading treats *mizrach shamesh* as a statement of approbation: you, Moshe, have caused the sun to shine for those who needed it.

### Love of Mitzvos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי סִימַאי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אֹהֵב כֶּסֶף לֹא יִשְׂבַּע כֶּסֶף וּמִי אֹהֵב בֶּהָמוֹן לֹא תְבוּאָה״? ״אֹהֵב כֶּסֶף לֹא יִשְׂבַּע כֶּסֶף״ – זֶה מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, שֶׁהָיָה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין שָׁלֹשׁ עָרִים שֶׁבְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן קוֹלְטוֹת עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִבְחֲרוּ שָׁלֹשׁ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, וְאָמַר: מִצְוָה שֶׁבָּאָה לְיָדִי אֲקַיְּימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, **Rabbi Simai taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He who loves silver shall not be satisfied with silver; nor he who loves abundance with increase"** (Ecclesiastes 5:9)? **"He who loves silver shall not be satisfied with silver"; this** is a reference to **Moses our teacher,** whose love of mitzvot was so great **that** although **he knew that** an unintentional murderer **would not be admitted to the three cities** of refuge **that were in** the east **bank of the Jordan until** the **three** cities of refuge **that** were **in the land of Canaan were selected, and** that his designation of cities of refuge would have no practical ramifications in his lifetime, **he** nevertheless **said:** When there is **a mitzva that has come my way, I will fulfill it.**

Rabbi Simlai then expounds the verse from Kohelet: *ohev kesef lo yisba kesef* — one who loves money will never have enough. *Kesef* is read not as money but as *mitzvos*. Moshe knew that the three Transjordanian cities would not go into effect until the three in the land of Israel were designated — a milestone he would never live to see. He could have reasoned that there was no point in designating cities that would not yet function. Instead, he acted on the principle *mitzvah sheba'ah leyadi akaymena* — a *mitzvah* that comes to hand, I will perform, even if I will not live to see it take effect. This is the *ohev kesef* — one who loves *mitzvos* so much that he can never have enough, and seizes every opportunity regardless of whether he will see the fruit.

### Teaching Masses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִי אֹהֵב בֶּהָמוֹן לֹא תְבוּאָה״ – לְמִי נָאֶה לְלַמֵּד בְּהָמוֹן? מִי שֶׁכׇּל תְּבוּאָה שֶׁלּוֹ. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״מַאי דִּכְתִיב מִי יְמַלֵּל גְּבוּרוֹת ה׳ יַשְׁמִיעַ כׇּל תְּהִלָּתוֹ״? לְמִי נָאֶה (לְלַמֵּד) [לְמַלֵּל] גְּבוּרוֹת ה׳ – מִי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַשְׁמִיעַ כׇּל תְּהִלָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The next phrase in that verse: **"Nor he who loves abundance with increase,"** is also interpreted as referring to Torah: **For whom is it fitting to teach an abundance** of people? **One** for **whom all** its **increase belongs to him,** i.e., one who knows all the content of the Torah is worthy of teaching it in public. **And that is** identical to that **which Rabbi Elazar says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Who can express the mighty acts of God, or make all His praise heard"** (Psalms 106:2)? **For whom is it fitting to express the mighty acts of God?** It is **one who can make all His praise heard.** One who knows only part of it is unfit to teach the multitudes.

The verse continues: *u'mi ohev be'hamon lo tevuah* — and one who loves with the masses will not have grain. The word *tevuah* (grain) functions as a metaphor for Torah knowledge. Rabbi Simlai reads the phrase to mean: who is worthy to teach Torah to the masses? Only the one who has all the grain — i.e., comprehensive Torah knowledge. Rabbi Elazar draws on the verse *mi yemallel gevurot Hashem yashmia kol tehillato* — who can speak of the mighty deeds of God and proclaim all His praise? Only one who can truly *yashmia kol tehillato*, who can give voice to the fullness of God's glory through comprehensive mastery of Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי, אָמַר: ״מִי אֹהֵב בֶּהָמוֹן לוֹ תְּבוּאָה״ – כׇּל הָאוֹהֵב (לִמְלַמֵּד) בֶּהָמוֹן – לוֹ תְבוּאָה. יְהַבוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן עֵינַיְיהוּ בְּרָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** say, **and some say Rabba bar Mari says,** that the passage **"nor he who loves abundance with increase"** means **whoever loves** a Torah scholar **who teaches in** the presence of **an abundance** of people, **to him shall be increase,** i.e., sons who are Torah scholars. The Gemara relates: When they heard that interpretation, **the Sages cast their eyes upon Rava, son of Rabba,** who loved Torah scholars who disseminate Torah, and he was blessed with sons who were Torah scholars.

The *rabbanan* — and Rabbah bar Mari — reverse the equation: *mi ohev be'hamon lo tevuah* is read with *lo* spelled with a *vav*, meaning "he has grain." Anyone who loves those who study Torah — i.e., who loves *talmidei chachamim* — will himself merit Torah. Rashi specifies: *haTorah mechazeret alav v'al zaro* — the Torah will pursue him and his children. The Aramaic formulation makes this concrete: *hai man derachim rabbanan havu lei banin rabbanan* — one who loves Torah scholars will have children who are Torah scholars. The Rabbis then cast their eyes toward Rabbah the son of Rav, who exemplified this love and whose children were indeed Torah scholars.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָנֵי רַבָּנַן מְהֵמְנֵי וְסָפְרֵי).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for the ensuing interpretations of the second part of the verse cited earlier (Ecclesiastes 5:9): **Rabbis; *mehemni*,** i.e., the dispute with Rabbi Neḥemya; **and** the dispute with regard to Torah **scrolls** sewn with threads of flax.

A further reading of the same verse: anyone who loves to learn Torah with the masses — in a large *beit medrash*, amidst the *kol Torah* of a community — will be granted Torah. This shifts the emphasis from loving *talmidei chachamim* to the act of communal learning itself. And *Ravina* reads it from the perspective of the teacher: anyone who loves to teach the masses (*lelamed be'hamon*) will not err (*lo to'eh*). This is then connected to the famous teaching: much Torah did I learn from my teachers, more from my colleagues, and the most from my students.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כׇּל הָאוֹהֵב לִלְמוֹד בֶּהָמוֹן – לוֹ תְּבוּאָה. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״חֶרֶב אֶל הַבַּדִּים וְנֹאָלוּ״? חֶרֶב עַל צַוְּארֵי שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין וְעוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה בַּד בְּבַד, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּטַּפְּשִׁין – כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְנֹאָלוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אֲשֶׁר נוֹאַלְנוּ״. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁחוֹטְאִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲשֶׁר חָטָאנוּ״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״נוֹאֲלוּ שָׂרֵי צֹעַן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Anyone who loves to study in abundance,** i.e., with many colleagues, **to him shall be increase,** i.e., he will succeed in his studies. **And that is** parallel to that **which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "A sword is upon the *baddim*, *veno'alu*"** (Jeremiah 50:36)? It is fitting that **a sword** be placed **on the necks of the enemies of Torah scholars,** a euphemism for Torah scholars, **who sit and engage in** the study of **the Torah individually [*bad bevad*]. Moreover, they grow foolish** through individual study, as **it is written here: *Veno'alu*, and it is written there: "For we have been foolish [*no'alnu*]"** (Numbers 12:11). **Moreover, they** thereby **sin, as it is stated** immediately thereafter: **"And for we have sinned." And if you wish, say** that **from here** it is derived that *no'alu* means sinned: **"The ministers of Zoan have sinned [*no'alu*]"** (Isaiah 19:13).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כׇּל הָאוֹהֵב לְלַמֵּד בֶּהָמוֹן – לוֹ תְּבוּאָה. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: הַרְבֵּה תּוֹרָה לָמַדְתִּי מֵרַבּוֹתַי, וּמֵחֲבֵירַי יוֹתֵר מֵהֶם, וּמִתַּלְמִידַי יוֹתֵר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** that there is a different interpretation of the verse cited earlier (Ecclesiastes 5:9): **Anyone who loves to teach in abundance,** before the multitudes, **to him shall be increase,** as his Torah knowledge is enhanced through those lectures. **And that is** the parallel to that **which Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Much Torah have I studied from my teachers, and** I have learned **more from my colleagues** than **from them, and** I have learned **more from my students** than **from all of them.**

## Community Learning

### Love Community

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״עֹמְדוֹת הָיוּ רַגְלֵינוּ בִּשְׁעָרַיִךְ יְרוּשָׁלִָם״? מִי גָּרַם לְרַגְלֵינוּ שֶׁיַּעַמְדוּ בַּמִּלְחָמָה – שַׁעֲרֵי יְרוּשָׁלַםִ, שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹסְקִים בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the virtue of Torah study, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Our feet were standing in your gates, Jerusalem"** (Psalms 122:2)? **What caused our feet to withstand** the enemies **in war?** It is **the gates of Jerusalem, where they were engaged in Torah** study. He interprets the term "in your gates" to mean: Because of your gates, the place of justice and Torah.

The one who loves to be among the masses engaged in Torah — who seeks out large gatherings of learners — is described as deserving of Torah knowledge.

### Sin: Solitary

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanina expounds the verse: *cherev al habadim veno'alu* — a sword upon the *badim* and they shall grow foolish. He reads *badim* as a reference to *talmidei chachamim* who sit and learn Torah *bad be-vad* — each one alone, isolated from one another. Three consequences follow from solitary study. First, such scholars will grow weary and their learning will be ineffective. The word *veno'alu* is linked to the root of *noalnu* — "we have been foolish" — appearing in two other verses. Second, the learning itself is undermined: the scholar becomes dull from isolation. Third — and most sharply — it is an outright *cheit*, a sin: *shechot'in*, they actually transgress, as implied by the juxtaposition *asher noalnu va'asher chatanu*.

### Gates of Study

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi expounds: *omdot hayu ragleinu bish'arayich Yerushalayim* — our feet were standing in your gates, Jerusalem. What enabled our legs to stand firm in the battles of Torah? The gates of Jerusalem — meaning the places where masses gathered, the markets and public spaces — because it was there that Torah was studied communally, in the thick of human life. It was that communal, public engagement with Torah that gave the people their spiritual strength.

### David's Joy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׁיר הַמַּעֲלוֹת לְדָוִד שָׂמַחְתִּי בְּאֹמְרִים לִי בֵּית ה׳ נֵלֵךְ״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: ״מָתַי יָמוּת זָקֵן זֶה, וְיָבֹא שְׁלֹמֹה בְּנוֹ וְיִבְנֶה בֵּית הַבְּחִירָה וְנַעֲלֶה לָרֶגֶל?״, וְשָׂמַחְתִּי. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: ״כִּי טוֹב יוֹם בַּחֲצֵרֶיךָ מֵאָלֶף״ – טוֹב לִי יוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁאַתָּה עוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה לְפָנַי, מֵאֶלֶף עוֹלוֹת שֶׁעָתִיד שְׁלֹמֹה בִּנְךָ לְהַקְרִיב לְפָנַי עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "A song of the ascents to David: I rejoiced when they said to me, let us go to the house of God"** (Psalms 122:1)? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, I heard people who were saying** in reference to me: **When will this old man die, and Solomon his son will come** and succeed him **and build the Temple and we will ascend** there **for the pilgrimage Festival?** It was common knowledge that the Temple would be constructed by David's successor. David continued: **And** despite my pain that I am not privileged to build the Temple, **I rejoiced. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: "For better is one day in your courtyard than one thousand"** (Psalms 84:11), meaning, **I prefer one day** during **which you engage in** the study of **Torah before Me than** the **one thousand burnt-offerings that your son Solomon is destined to sacrifice before Me upon the altar** (see I Kings 3:4).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi expounds a final verse: *shir hama'alos le-David samachti be'omrim li beit Hashem neilech* — I rejoiced when they said to me, let us go to the house of God. David said before the Holy One: I heard people saying, when will this old man die so that his son Shlomo can come and build the Temple and we can be *oleh le-regel*? And I rejoiced. Rather than being wounded that they wished for his death, David heard only the desire to draw close to God — that they wanted to make the pilgrimage, that they wanted the Temple built.

God responded to David: *ki tov yom bachatzeirecha me'alef* — one day in Your courtyards is better than a thousand. The verse is read: *tov li yom echad she'ata osek ba-Torah lefanai me'alef olot she'asid Shelomo bincha lehakriv al gabei hamizbe'ach* — more precious to Me is one day that you sit and learn Torah before Me than the thousand sacrifices that your son Shlomo will one day offer on the altar. Whether this was meant to reassure David that his contribution was not diminished — or simply as a statement of the supreme value of Torah — the message is unmistakable: a single day of Torah study outweighs a thousand sacrifices. And here the daf concludes.
