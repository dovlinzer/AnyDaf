# Bava Batra 146 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shoshbinut & Reciprocity

### Shoshbinut Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, כְּלָלָא דְשׁוֹשְׁבִינוּתָא: הֲוָה בְּמָתָא – אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵיתֵא. שְׁמַע קָל טַבְלָא – אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵיתֵא. לָא שְׁמַע קָל טַבְלָא – אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאוֹדוֹעֵיהּ; תַּרְעוֹמֶת אִית לֵיהּ, שַׁלּוֹמֵי מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Kahana said: The principle with regard to** the reciprocation of **gifts of groomsmen** is: If the recipient **was in town** when his groomsman wed, **he should have come** to the wedding, and even if he did not come, he is obligated to send the gifts of groomsmen. If he was not in town, but **he** was nearby and he **heard the sound of the drum** announcing the wedding, **he should have come.** If **he** was far away and **did not hear the sound of the drum,** the betrothed man **should have informed him.** If he did not inform him, the recipient **has a grievance** against the betrothed man because he did not inform him about the wedding, but **he** still **repays** the gifts of groomsmen.

The daf opens, picking up on 145b approximately eight lines down, with a discussion of the institution of *shoshbinut* — the practice whereby designated groomsmen bring gifts to a wedding and, in turn, are owed a reciprocal gift when their own weddings take place. This is not merely a social custom but a genuine legal obligation: the recipient of a *shoshbin* gift incurs a real debt to repay it when the roles are reversed.

### Havi be-mata Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָהֲגוּ בְּנֵי גְנָנָא, עַד זוּזָא – אַיְּיתֵא בְּכַפֵּיהּ אַכְלֵיהּ בִּכְרֵסֵיהּ. עַד אַרְבְּעָה – מְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – אִינִישׁ אִינִישׁ כַּחֲשִׁיבוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, since he did not partake of the wedding feast, he is entitled to deduct a sum from the reciprocal gift. The Gemara asks: **And up to how much** can he deduct? **Abaye said:** The **members of a wedding** feast **were accustomed** to deduct according to the following principle: If the gift of groomsmen that the reciprocal giver received was **up to a dinar,** he now pays nothing, because what a person **brings in his hand he consumes in his stomach.** If the gift of groomsmen was **up to four** dinars, he now **pays half. From that** sum **onward, each person** deducts **according to his prominence,** i.e., in accordance with the outlay that would have been required to honor the reciprocal giver properly, had he participated in the wedding feast.

Rav Kahana states the general rule — *klal de-shoshbinuta* — governing what happens when a person who once received a *shoshbin* gift was, for some reason, absent from the giver's wedding. The underlying assumption had been that one would be invited to the other's wedding and repay the gift in that context, but Rav Kahana addresses the complications that arise when this does not occur.

If one was *havi be-mata* — present in the town at the time of the wedding — then *iba-ei lei lemeisei*: one should have come. The wedding was a known community event, and absence is one's own fault. The obligation to repay the *shoshbin* gift therefore stands in full. Similarly, if one was not in town but heard the wedding drum — *shama kol tavla* — the public announcement that a wedding was taking place, the same rule applies: *iba-ei lei lemeisei*, one had sufficient notice and should have attended. Failure to do so does not exempt one from the reciprocal obligation.

### Lo Shama Kol Tavla

The real question arises in the case of *lo shama kol tavla*: one was out of town and received no notification whatsoever. Here, one might reasonably argue: "You didn't invite me, you didn't inform me — why should I be obligated to repay a gift in the context of a wedding from which I was effectively excluded?" The claim seems compelling, rooted in the very logic of friendship and conviviality that underlies *shoshbinut* in the first place.

Surprisingly, however, this is not the *halacha*. The Gemara acknowledges that yes, *iba-ei lei lo-odeiyei* — the groom should have informed him, and *tarumas it lei* — the uninvited groomsman has a legitimate complaint. Yet despite this valid grievance, *shalumei meshalem*: he is still obligated to repay the gift. The fact that he had a right to be informed, and was not, gives him grounds for complaint but does not dissolve the underlying debt.

### Zuza Deduction Rule

The Gemara then raises a practical refinement. When one was not at the wedding and had a good reason — specifically in the case where one was not informed — must one repay the full value of the original gift? After all, had one attended, one would have enjoyed a meal and *smorg* at the groom's expense. The host provides food for the guest, and that value was already factored into the original gift's social context.

Abaye teaches: *nahagu benei ganuna* — the practice among wedding participants was as follows. If the *shoshbin* gift was worth up to one *zuza*, the principle *aisei be-kapei, achlei be-charsei* applies — what you bring with your hands, you eat with your stomach — meaning the food one would have received is presumed to be worth at least as much as the gift, and therefore nothing need be repaid. If the gift was worth up to four *zuza*, half is repaid, on the assumption that roughly half the gift's value would have been offset by the meal one would have enjoyed. Beyond four *zuza*, the *Rashbam* indicates the calculation proceeds according to the individual's status and the presumed quality of the feast — *inish inish k'chashivusei* — each person according to their standing.

This framework strikes a deliberately commercial tone. An institution rooted in friendship and communal celebration is here subjected to precise monetary accounting — yet that, it seems, is the nature of what this legal institution ultimately became.

### Equivalence Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עָשָׂה עִמּוֹ בְּפוּמְבֵּי, וּבִקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ בְּצִנְעָא – יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: בְּפוּמְבֵּי אֶעֱשֶׂה עִמְּךָ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ עִמִּי. עָשָׂה עִמּוֹ בִּבְתוּלָה, וּבִקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ בְּאַלְמָנָה – יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: בִּבְתוּלָה אֶעֱשֶׂה עִמְּךָ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ עִמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 10:9): If one **served** as groomsman **for** his friend **at a public [*pumbei*]** wedding, **and** when the groomsman himself wed, he **requested** that his friend reciprocate **by serving** as a groomsman **for him at a private** wedding, the friend **can say to him: I** am willing to reciprocate and **serve** as groomsman **for you** only **at a public** wedding, where the rejoicing is greater, **in the manner you served** as a groomsman **for me.** If one **served** as a groomsman **for** his friend **when** he married **a virgin, and** he **requested** that his friend reciprocate **by serving** as a groomsman **for him when** he marries **a widow,** the friend **can say to him: I shall serve** as a groomsman **for you** only **when** you marry **a virgin, in the manner you served** as a groomsman **for me.**

A *Tanna de-Bei* teaching elaborates on a further dimension of *shoshbinut* obligations: the requirement of equivalence between the two weddings. If one threw a grand public wedding — *bifumbi*, with hundreds of guests and a lavish feast — and the other now wishes to hold a small, private affair, the first may say: *bifumbi esseh imach kederach she'asita imi* — I will fulfill my *shoshbin* obligation at a wedding comparable to the one you gave for me. If the other's wedding does not rise to that level, there is no legal obligation to serve as *shoshbin* or to give the equivalent gift.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה עִמּוֹ בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וּבִקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה – יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: לִכְשֶׁתִּשָּׂא אִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת, אֶעֱשֶׂה עִמְּךָ. עָשָׂה עִמּוֹ בְּאַחַת, וּבִקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁתַּיִם – יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: בְּאַחַת אֶעֱשֶׂה עִמְּךָ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ עִמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** If one **served** as a groomsman **for** his friend **when** he married **a second** wife, **and** he **requested** that his friend reciprocate **by serving** as a groomsman **for him when** he marries **a first** wife, the friend **can say to him: When you marry another woman I will serve** as a groomsman **for you.** If one **served** as a groomsman **for** his friend **when** he married **one** woman, **and** he **requested** that his friend reciprocate **by serving** as a groomsman **for him when** he marries **two** women, the friend **can say to him: I will serve** as a groomsman **for you when** you marry **one** woman, **in the manner you served** as a groomsman **for me.**

Similarly, if one's wedding was for a *betulah* — a first marriage — and the other now marries a widow, one may say: *bivtulah esseh imach kederach she'asita imi* — my obligation applies to a first wedding, not a second. The reverse situation also generates an asymmetry: if one's own wedding was a second marriage and the other's is a first, one may say: *lichshe'tissa ishah acheret esseh imach* — when you have your second marriage, then I will fulfill my obligation, not now. And if one married one woman and the other now seeks to marry two women simultaneously — the plain reading of *asa imo be'achat ubikesh la'asot imo bishtayim* — one may declare the obligation inapplicable to that scenario as well.

It is worth noting the practical context of these rulings. They are not predicated on the assumption that people would routinely refuse to participate in a friend's wedding under such circumstances. Rather, when the matter arrives before a court and becomes a question of legal liability — of actually suing to recover or compel a gift — the technical obligation applies only where there is genuine equivalence. That is the narrower claim these teachings are making.

## Scholars & Wealth Metaphor

### Otzar Nechasim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עַתִּיר נִכְסִין עַתִּיר פּוּמְבֵּי – זֶה הוּא בַּעַל הַגָּדוֹת. עַתִּיר סְלָעִים עַתִּיר תְּקוֹעַ – זֶהוּ בַּעַל פִּלְפּוּל. עַתִּיר מְשַׁח עַתִּיר כְּמָס – זֶהוּ בַּעַל שְׁמוּעוֹת. הַכֹּל צְרִיכִין לְמָרֵי חִטַּיָּא – תַּלְמוּד.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There are different types of Torah scholars. There is a scholar who is **wealthy** in figurative **property** and **wealthy** in **public** renown; **this is the master of homiletics.** There is a scholar who is **wealthy** in **coins** and **wealthy** in **houses; this is the master of dialectics.** There is one who is **wealthy** in **oil** and **wealthy** in **hidden** stores; **this is the master of** halakhic **traditions. Everyone is dependent on the owner of wheat;** this is the master of **Talmud,** who understands the reasons behind the rulings and traditions.

At this point the Gemara transitions, somewhat unexpectedly, into an extended *aggadic* passage employing metaphors of wealth to characterize different types of Torah scholars. The connection to the preceding discussion of *shoshbinut* and its varying levels of obligation may be thematic — different types of celebration, different levels of social standing, different magnitudes of wealth — but the passage stands largely on its own terms as a celebrated *aggadic* teaching.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: *otzar nechasim otzar pumbi* — one who is wealthy in property and whose wealth is publicly known, visible to all in the form of land and flocks and assets that everyone can observe. The *Rashbam* reads this as referring to landed property and livestock. This describes, the Gemara teaches, *zehu baal aggadot* — the scholar who excels at *aggadah*, at popular preaching and inspiring public discourse. His Torah is as visible and accessible as publicly displayed wealth: hundreds or thousands come to hear his beautifully crafted sermons, and the full richness of his knowledge is on open display for all.

### Atir Sela'im Case

*Atir sela'im atir takua* — wealthy in coins, and *takua*, a term the *Rashbam* explains with some uncertainty, possibly referring to houses or, given that *Takua* was a town known for its oil, to oil. But setting aside the precise meaning of *takua*, the emphasis here falls on the coins: liquid wealth that is not publicly visible, that reveals itself only when someone comes to the money changer and transacts with him. This is *ze baal pilpul* — the scholar of profound analytic ability. *Pilpul* today carries a sometimes pejorative connotation, but in the Gemara's idiom it refers to genuine analytical depth and incisiveness. This scholar does not deliver crowd-pleasing homilies; he is not the one whose Torah is on public display. But sit down with him in learning, and the full depth of what he possesses becomes immediately apparent — like the coin changer who reveals his resources only when the moment of exchange arrives.

### Atir Mashach Case

*Atir mashach atir kamas* — wealthy in stored goods. *Mashach*, the *Rashbam* suggests, may refer to things that are measured out, and he reads it as something akin to grain stored in a storehouse; *kamas* more clearly means stockpiled goods. Together the image is of someone with vast reserves that are not circulating, not generating new wealth, but available to draw upon when needed. This is *zehu baal shmuot* — the scholar of wide factual knowledge, a walking encyclopedia of traditions and received teachings. Unlike the *ba'al pilpul*, this scholar is not generating new analytical insight; unlike the *ba'al aggadah*, this scholar's wares are not on public display. But his stored knowledge is indispensable.

Here the Gemara interpolates what appears to be a parallel teaching from elsewhere: *hakol tzrichim lemarei chittaya* — everybody needs the master of the wheat. This echoes the famous Talmudic question of which scholar type is preferable, the *oker harim* — the one who uproots mountains with analytical prowess — or the *Sinai*, the one who received the Torah at Sinai, as it were, possessing the comprehensive factual knowledge of traditions. The Gemara's answer: *hakol tzrichim lemarei chittaya*, everyone needs the one who has the wheat. The image is apt: *agirsa ligros*, grinding grain, is the analytic work of breaking down knowledge, crushing and developing it into something usable — but none of that can happen without the raw grain to begin with. Facts and received traditions are the necessary foundation before any analysis can proceed. The *ba'al shmuot* may not be generating new wealth, his knowledge may not be growing or producing, but it is the most essential starting point.

### Poor Days Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״? זֶה בַּעַל תַּלְמוּד. ״וְטוֹב לֵב מִשְׁתֶּה תָמִיד״ – זֶה בַּעַל מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "All the days of the poor are terrible;** and for the good-hearted it is always a feast" (Proverbs 15:15)? "All the days of the poor are terrible"; **this** is referring to the **master of Talmud,** who is wearied by the difficulty of his Talmud study. **"And for the good-hearted it is always a feast"; this** is referring to the **master of Mishna,** who recites the *mishnayot* by rote and is not wearied thereby.

Rav Zeira says in the name of Rav: what is meant by the verse in Mishlei, *kol yimei ani ra'im* — "all the days of a poor person are evil"? This refers to *ze ba'al Gemara* — the scholar who dedicates himself to Talmudic analysis. He is constantly struggling, banging his head against the wall of a difficult passage, wrestling with the analytical demands of *gemara* study. And *v'tov lev mishteh tamid* — "and the good-hearted is always at a feast" — that refers to *ze ba'al Mishnah*, the scholar who occupies himself only with memorizing the received traditions without the burden of analysis. His life is comparatively easy: he learns what the teachings are, he has no need to think too hard, and so his heart is always at ease, his learning always like a pleasant feast.

### Rava's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִיפְּכָא. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מַסִּיעַ אֲבָנִים יֵעָצֵב בָּהֶם, בּוֹקֵעַ עֵצִים יִסָּכֶן בָּם״? ״מַסִּיעַ אֲבָנִים יֵעָצֵב בָּהֶן״ – אֵלּוּ בַּעְלֵי מִשְׁנָה. ״בּוֹקֵעַ עֵצִים יִסָּכֶן בָּם״ – אֵלּוּ בַּעְלֵי תַלְמוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The opposite** is true. **And this is** consistent with that **which Rav Mesharshiyya says in the name of Rava: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "He who quarries stones shall be hurt by them; and he that chops wood shall be warmed thereby"** (Ecclesiastes 10:9). **"He who quarries stones shall be hurt by them"; these are the masters of Mishna.** They exert themselves to memorize the *mishnayot*, but since one cannot reach practical conclusions from the mishna, they are comparable to one who carries a heavy load without benefiting from it. **"He that chops wood shall be warmed thereby"; these are the masters of Talmud,** who attain the benefit of their exertions in the form of practical conclusions.

Rava reverses this entirely: *amar Rava ipcha* — it is precisely the opposite. The *ba'al Gemara*, who labors through the analysis, ultimately arrives at the *halacha* — he knows what to do, and the satisfaction of reaching a conclusion, of knowing how to act, makes his life *v'tov lev mishteh tamid*. The *ba'al Mishnah*, on the other hand, for all his stored knowledge, cannot reliably arrive at the proper *halacha* on his own, and so *kol yimei ani ra'im* — all his days are troubled. He carries the weight of accumulated knowledge without the analytical tools to render it actionable.

### Stone & Wood Metaphor

This position, Rava adds, underlies the teaching of Rav Mesharshaya in Rava's name, drawing on another verse. *Massa avanim ye'atzev bahem* — "one who carries stones will be anguished by them" — these are *ba'alei Mishnah*. They carry vast weight, all this accumulated knowledge, but it causes anguish because ultimately it does not lead them to knowing the correct *halacha*. And *bokei'a etzim yissachen bam* — "one who chops wood will be warmed by it" — these are *ba'alei Gemara*. Chopping, grinding, breaking down: the analytic act generates energy, warmth, and ultimately illuminates the *halacha*. The contrast is clean and unambiguous in Rava's reading.

### Alternative Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״ – זֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה רָעָה. ״וְטוֹב לֵב מִשְׁתֶּה תָמִיד״ – זֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה. רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״ – זֶה אִיסְטְנִיס. ״וְטוֹב לֵב מִשְׁתֶּה תָמִיד״ – זֶה שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other interpretations of this verse. **Rabbi Ḥanina says: "All the days of the poor are terrible"; this** is referring to one **who has a wicked wife. "And for the good-hearted it is always a feast"; this** is referring to one **who has a good wife. Rabbi Yannai says: "All the days of the poor are terrible"; this** is referring to **one who is delicate [*istenis*]** and overly sensitive, because he constantly encounters unpleasant situations. **"And for the good-hearted it is always a feast"; this** is referring to one **who is relaxed** and not particular with regard to his food or his surroundings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״ – זֶה רַחְמָן. ״וְטוֹב לֵב מִשְׁתֶּה תָמִיד״ – זֶה אַכְזָרִי. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״ – זֶה שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ קְצָרָה. ״וְטוֹב לֵב מִשְׁתֶּה תָמִיד״ – זֶה שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ רְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: "All the days of the poor are terrible"; this** is referring to **an empathetic person,** because he is constantly affected by the suffering in the world. **"And for the good-hearted it is always a feast"; this** is referring to **a cruel person,** who is not pained by the suffering of others. **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: "All the days of the poor are terrible"; this** is referring to a person **of impatient disposition. "And for the good-hearted it is always a feast"; this** is referring to a person **of patient disposition.**

The Gemara then offers several alternative interpretations of the same verse from Mishlei that have nothing to do with Torah study typologies at all, exploring instead various dimensions of human experience and disposition.

Rabbi Chanina reads *kol yimei ani ra'im* as referring to a man with a bad wife — *ze she'yesh lo isha ra'ah* — and *v'tov lev mishteh tamid* as the man blessed with a good wife. Rabbi Yannai reads the "poor person" as an *istenis* — an extremely finicky individual for whom nothing is ever quite right, who is always disturbed by some small imperfection, who finds nothing in life quite satisfactory. The *v'tov lev mishteh tamid* is correspondingly the *she'da'ato yaffa* — the easygoing soul, the person of broad and relaxed temperament for whom minor disorder causes no distress. Rabbi Yochanan offers the striking, almost ironic, reading that *kol yimei ani ra'im* refers to the *rachman* — the excessively compassionate person, someone too empathic and too open, who lacks the self-protective filter needed to get through life without being overwhelmed by the pain of others. And *v'tov lev mishteh tamid* is, in this reading, *ze achzari* — the cruel person, or at least the emotionally insulated one, who goes through life unbothered by others' suffering. The point is not an endorsement of cruelty but a recognition that the unguarded heart pays a price — while still acknowledging the need for a balance that allows one to genuinely help and empathize.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi reads the verse in terms of *da'ato ketzarah* versus *da'ato rechavah* — the narrow versus the broad spirit. The narrow-spirited person, always measuring, always chafing at what others have that they lack, lives in constant resentment and perceived injustice. The broad-spirited person, generous in outlook, genuinely happy for others' good fortune, lives in a kind of permanent contentment. The distinction maps broadly onto the contrast between the pessimist and the optimist, each going through life with a fundamentally different orientation.

### Poor Man's Misery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים״?! וְהָא אִיכָּא שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים! כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שִׁנּוּי וֶסֶת – תְּחִלַּת חוֹלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** Is it so that **all the days of the poor are terrible? But aren't there *Shabbatot* and Festivals,** when even the poor enjoy their meals and rest? Rather, explain this **in accordance with** the statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says: A change of regimen [*veset*]** causes **the onset of** intestinal **disease,** and as a result the poor suffer even from a change for the good.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב בְּסֵפֶר בֶּן סִירָא: כׇּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים. בֶּן סִירָא אוֹמֵר: אַף לֵילוֹת. בִּשְׁפַל גַּגִּים גַּגּוֹ, מִמְּטַר גַּגִּים לְגַגּוֹ. בְּרוּם הָרִים כַּרְמוֹ, מֵעֲפַר כַּרְמוֹ לִכְרָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** It **is written in the book of ben Sira: All the days of the poor are terrible. Ben Sira says: The nights as well. His roof is at the lowest point of the roofs; the rain of roofs go onto his roof. His vineyard is at the height of the mountains; the soil of his vineyard goes onto other vineyards.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, however, raises an objection against the original formulation: *kol yimei ani ra'im* — surely not *all* the days? What about *Shabbasos* and *Yomim Tovim*, when even the poor person receives food from the charitable community and enjoys something of a respite? He answers this with a teaching from Shmuel: *shinui vest techilas choli* — a change in one's dietary routine is itself the beginning of illness. Even the comparative richness of a Shabbos meal, for someone accustomed to deprivation, may cause physical distress through the disruption of bodily norms. So even those days offer no true relief.

The Gemara closes this digression by quoting from *Sefer Ben Sira* — one of the *Sefarim Chitzonim*, yet occasionally cited by the Gemara — which extends the verse further. Not only are the poor person's days evil, but *af leilos* — even the nights are evil. Why? *Bishfal gagin gago*: the poor person's roof is lower than those of his neighbors, and so *mimatar gagin l'gago* — all the rainwater from the surrounding rooftops runs down onto his and creates perpetual leaks. And *b'rum harim karmo me'afar karmo l'charamim* — his vineyard sits at the top of the mountain, so whatever good topsoil he has trickles down to the vineyards below him. Even his nighttime sleep brings no reprieve, while the wealthy rest peacefully. It is a vivid and affecting close to what was, structurally, an unexpected digression from the legal sugyot on either side of it.

## Sabalonot Gifts

### Meal Finalizes Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ סִבְלוֹנוֹת לְבֵית חָמִיו; שָׁלַח שָׁם מֵאָה מָנֶה, וְאָכַל שָׁם סְעוּדַת חָתָן אֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר – אֵינָן נִגְבִּין. לֹא אָכַל שָׁם סְעוּדַת חָתָן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נִגְבִּין. שָׁלַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת מְרוּבִּין שֶׁיַּחְזְרוּ עִמָּהּ לְבֵית בַּעְלָהּ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נִגְבִּין. סִבְלוֹנוֹת מוּעָטִין שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ – אֵין נִגְבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who sends presents [*sivlonot*] to his father-in-law's house** following his betrothal, even if **he sent there** the sum of **ten thousand dinars and** subsequently **ate there a groom's feast even** worth the value **of** a single **dinar,** if for any reason the marriage is not effected, the presents **are not collected** in return by the formerly betrothed man. If he **did not eat a groom's feast there,** the presents **are collected,** as they were not an unconditional gift. If he **sent many presents** with the stipulation **that they return with her to her husband's house,** i.e., to his own house, after the wedding, **these are collected** if the marriage is not effected. If he sent **a few presents for her to use while in her father's house, they are not collected.**

The Gemara now moves to the Mishnah governing *sabalonot* — the traditional gifts sent from the groom to the bride and her family after *kiddushin* (betrothal) but before *nissu'in* (marriage). The Mishnah teaches: *ha'shole'ach savlonot l'veit chamiv* — one who sends gifts to his father-in-law's house, worth as much as one hundred *maneh*, and then goes there and participates in a *seudat chatan*, a celebratory meal at the father-in-law's house — even if that meal cost only one *dinar* — then *afillu b'dinar ein nigbin*: even so, if the marriage subsequently does not reach *nissu'in* — whether through death, divorce, or some other rupture — the gifts cannot be reclaimed. The participation in a celebratory meal, however modest, is treated as an act of finalization: the groom has, in effect, committed to the gift unconditionally, and it passes fully to the bride and her family.

### No Meal, Gift Recovers

If, however, *lo achal seudat chatan* — they did not hold that meal — then *harei elu nigbin*: in the event the *nissu'in* does not take place, the gifts may be recovered. Without that moment of celebratory consummation, the *sabalonot* retain a provisional character; the *kiddushin* has occurred, but the incompleteness of the relationship means the gifts have not yet passed irrevocably.

### Large vs Small Gifts

The Mishnah then draws a further distinction regarding large versus small *sabalonot*. *Shalach savlonot merubim she'yachzeru ima l'veit ba'ala* — if the gifts sent were substantial, understood by their nature to be brought back with the bride when she moves to the husband's house at *nissu'in*, then *harei elu nigbin* — they may be recovered if *nissu'in* does not occur. *Savlonot muatim she'tishtamesh bahen b'veit aviha* — but if the gifts were small, of the sort understood to be used up while the bride is still in her father's house before *nissu'in*, then *einam nigbin* — they are not recovered.

Much remains ambiguous at this point in the Mishnah's formulation. Do the large versus small distinctions apply only when there was no *seudat chatan*, or do they operate independently? Is the Mishnah speaking of explicit stated intentions, or of implied presumptions based on quantity? What of intermediate amounts? The Gemara will attempt to resolve these ambiguities.

### Dinar Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: דַּוְקָא דִּינָר, אֲבָל פָּחוֹת מִדִּינָר – לָא. פְּשִׁיטָא, דִּינָר תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִדִּינָר, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי דִּינָר – אוֹרְחָא דְּמִילְּתָא קָתָנֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if the betrothed man ate food worth even a single dinar at his father-in-law's house, the presents are not returned. **Rava says:** This applies **specifically** to the value of **a dinar, but** if he ate food worth **less than** the value of **a dinar,** it is **not** so. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious,** as **we learned** the *halakha* in the mishna with reference to the value of **a dinar?** The Gemara answers: It is stated **lest you say** that **the same is true even** in the case of **less than a dinar, and** the reason **that** the mishna **teaches** the *halakha* with reference to **a dinar** is that it **teaches the matter in the manner in which it** typically occurs, and one does not usually eat less than that. Rava therefore **teaches us** that the formulation of the mishna is precise; the mishna is not referring to the value of less than a dinar.

Rava rules: *davka dinar* — the Mishnah's ruling that the gifts are finalized applies specifically to a meal worth a full *dinar*; *aval pachot mi'dinar lo* — anything less than a *dinar*, even if a *seudat chatan* of sorts took place, does not finalize the gifts, and they may still be recovered if *nissu'in* does not occur.

The Gemara challenges this: *pshita dinar tnan* — the Mishnah explicitly said *dinar*, so this seems obvious. The Gemara responds that one might have thought the Mishnah used *dinar* only as a conventional way of speaking to contrast with the enormous sum of one hundred *maneh*, and that even less than a *dinar* would suffice. Rava therefore teaches that *davka* — the *dinar* threshold is precise and not merely illustrative.

### Eating vs Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָכַל״ תְּנַן; שָׁתָה מַאי? ״הוּא״ תְּנַן; שְׁלוּחוֹ מַאי? ״שָׁם״ תְּנַן; שִׁגֵּר לוֹ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **We learned** the *halakha* in the mishna with regard to a betrothed man who **ate** at his father-in-law's house. **What** is the *halakha* if **he drank** there? **We learned** the *halakha* in the mishna with regard to a case where **he,** the betrothed, ate there. **What** is the *halakha* if **his agent** ate there? **We learned** the *halakha* in the mishna with regard to a case where the betrothed man ate **there,** at his father-in-law's house. **What** is the *halakha* if his father-in-law **sent him** a feast to his home?

The Gemara then examines the Mishnah's language more closely. The Mishnah says *achal* — he ate. What if he only drank — *shata mai*? Does drinking alone at the father-in-law's house constitute sufficient participation to finalize the gifts? Furthermore: the Mishnah seems to presume that the groom himself ate — but what if a representative, a *shlucho*, ate on his behalf? And: the Mishnah says he ate *sham*, there at the father-in-law's house — what if food was sent to him and he ate it at his own house, a kind of *shagar lo*? None of these questions are resolved directly; the Gemara turns instead to a narrative that sheds light on at least some of them.

### Hot Water Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁשִּׁגֵּר לְבֵית חָמִיו מֵאָה קְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְשֶׁל כַּדֵּי שֶׁמֶן וְשֶׁל כְּלֵי כֶסֶף וְשֶׁל כְּלֵי זָהָב וְשֶׁל כְּלִי מֵילָת; וְרָכַב בְּשִׂמְחָתוֹ, וְהָלַךְ וְעָמַד עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית חָמִיו; וְהוֹצִיאוּ כּוֹס שֶׁל חַמִּין וְשָׁתָה, וּמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a proof concerning one these matters, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** There was **an incident involving one man who sent to his father-in-law's house one hundred wagons** full **of jars of wine and of jars of oil, and** one hundred wagons full **of silver vessels, and** one hundred wagons full **of gold vessels, and** one hundred wagons full **of garments of fine wool [*milat*]. And he rode** over **in his** state of **joy and went and stood by the entrance to his father-in-law's house. And they brought out** to him **a cup of hot** drink **and he drank** it, **and he** subsequently **died.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ הֲלָכָה הֶעֱלָה רַבִּי אַחָא שַׂר הַבִּירָה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים לְאוּשָׁא, וְאָמְרוּ: סִבְלוֹנוֹת הָעֲשׂוּיִן לִיבְלוֹת – אֵין נִגְבִּין, וְשֶׁאֵין עֲשׂוּיִן לִיבְלוֹת – נִגְבִּין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The question arose as to whether the presents must be returned to the betrothed's heirs. **And Rabbi Aḥa Sar HaBira raised this *halakha* before the Sages in Usha, and they said: Presents that are typically consumed are not collected, and** those **that are not typically consumed are collected.** One can **conclude from** this incident that **even** if the betrothed only **drank,** some of the presents cannot be reclaimed.

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel: there was a man who sent to his father-in-law's house one hundred flasks of wine and of oil, silver and gold vessels, silk garments — an extraordinarily lavish array of *sabalonot* — and then rode joyfully toward the house. He arrived and stood at the doorway of his father-in-law's house, and before entering, they brought him a cup of hot water (*kos shel chamin*), which he drank — and he died right there on the threshold.

This case — the *halacha* of which was brought by Rav Acha Sarbiya before the sages in Usha, the seat of the Sanhedrin — prompted the following ruling: *savlonot ha'asuyot livlot ein nigbin* — *sabalonot* that are of the type normally consumed or worn out are not recovered; *she'einan asuyot livlot nigbin* — those that are not of that type are recovered.

This is a crucial reconceptualization. The distinction shifts from simple quantity (large or small) to the inherent nature of the gift: will it naturally be consumed before *nissu'in*, or was it always intended to last and return with the bride at *nissu'in*? Food, clothing that would be worn out, even money that would be spent — these are *asuyot livlot*, made to be used up, and once the gift is finalized (as it apparently was here through the cup of hot water), they do not return. A pearl necklace or gold vessels — *einan asuyot livlot* — those were always expected to come back into the marriage at *nissu'in*, and so even when gifts are otherwise finalized, these always remain subject to return if *nissu'in* does not occur.

### Eshuyot Livlot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁתָה. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִדִּינָר? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּלָא שָׁחֲקִי לֵיהּ מַרְגָּנִיתָא דְּשָׁוְיָא אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי, וְאַשְׁקְיֻהּ. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ אֲפִילּוּ שִׁגְּרוּ לוֹ? דִּלְמָא כׇּל פֶּתַח בֵּית חָמִיו – כְּבֵית חָמִיו דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can you learn from this** incident that the presents cannot be claimed **even** if he ate or drank **less than** the value of **a dinar,** since a cup of hot drink is not worth a dinar? **Rav Ashi said: Who shall say to us that they did not grind a pearl worth one thousand dinars and serve it to him to drink?** The Gemara asks: **Can you learn from this** incident that **even** if **they sent** the feast **to him** the presents cannot be claimed, as he drank at the entrance and did not enter inside? The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Perhaps the entire entrance of his father-in-law's house is considered as his father-in-law's house.**

This ruling also clarifies the end of the Mishnah, reconceptualizing it from a distinction between large and small into a distinction between *asuyot livlot* and *einan asuyot livlot*. And crucially, it applies this distinction even when the gift has been finalized by a meal: even after the *seudat chatan*, the items that were expected to return — the *einan asuyot livlot* — still revert to the groom's family if *nissu'in* does not take place. The finalization accomplished by the meal applies only to those gifts that were always destined to remain in the bride's father's house.

The story of the cup of hot water also seems to indicate that even *drinking* — not eating a full meal — can constitute sufficient participation to finalize the *asuyot livlot* category of gifts. And the question of whether the finalization requires eating at the father-in-law's house specifically is at least partially addressed: the man died *at the doorway*, and the ruling treated this as sufficient, though the Gemara notes — *dilma kol petach beit chamich kebeit chamich dami* — perhaps the immediate doorway of one's father-in-law's house is halachically equivalent to being inside, without resolving the question of what would apply further afield.

The Gemara draws inferences: *shma mina afilu shata* — we can infer that even drinking (not eating) suffices to finalize the gifts. And apparently *shma mina afilu pachot mi'dinar* — this seems to contradict Rava's requirement of a full *dinar*, since the cup of hot water was presumably worth far less than a *dinar*.

### Pearl Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּשׁ? שֶׁבַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאִי אִיתַנְהוּ – לְדִידֵיהּ הָדְרִי, בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ שְׁבוּח; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִי אָבְדִי אוֹ מִגַּנְבִי – בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ, בִּרְשׁוּתָא דִּידַהּ שְׁבוּח? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Rava states that if the betrothed man ate less than the value of a dinar at his father-in-law's house, and the marriage is not effected, he can collect the presents he sent. **What is** the *halakha* concerning **whether he must divide** the value of the presents, and claim only part of the value, in proportion to the amount he ate? And **what is** the *halakha* concerning **the enhancement of** the value of **the presents?** Does one say that **since** the *halakha* is that **if they are** extant **they are returned to him,** therefore **they were enhanced under his ownership? Or perhaps, since** the *halakha* is that **if they are lost or stolen,** the betrothed woman's family is **required to repay him,** therefore **they were enhanced under her ownership.** The Gemara concludes: These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi deflects this challenge: *man leima lan dela shachki lei marganita deshvya alfa zuzei ve'ashkeih* — who says they did not grind a pearl worth a thousand *zuza* into the drink? Perhaps what he consumed was worth far more than a *dinar*. The *Rashbam* suggests this might reflect a belief that ground pearl had medicinal properties. The argument strikes one as intentionally far-fetched — the grinding of an enormously valuable pearl into a cup of water seems absurd — yet Rav Ashi's point may be precisely that the *dinar* requirement, if insisted upon, can never be conclusively disproven, since one can never know exactly what went into the drink. The practical upshot is that the Gemara appears unable to cleanly maintain the *dinar* requirement in the face of this narrative, though it does not formally abandon it.

The Gemara similarly considers whether *shgar lo* — sending him food from the house for him to eat elsewhere — would be sufficient, and tentatively suggests that the doorway of the father-in-law's house may be treated as equivalent to the interior, leaving the more distant case unresolved.

### Gift Appreciation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: סִבְלוֹנוֹת הָעֲשׂוּיִן לִיבְלוֹת, וְלֹא בָּלוּ, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: וְזוֹ הֲלָכָה הֶעֱלָה רַבִּי אַחָא שַׂר הַבִּירָה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים בְּאוּשָׁא, וְאָמְרוּ: סִבְלוֹנוֹת הָעֲשׂוּיִן לִיבְלוֹת – אֵין נִגְבִּין, וְשֶׁאֵין עֲשׂוּיִן לִיבְלוֹת – נִגְבִּין. מַאי, לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא בָּלוּ? לָא, דְּבָלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **presents that are typically consumed, but were not consumed?** The Gemara replies: **Come** and **hear** a proof: **And Rabbi Aḥa Sar HaBira raised this *halakha* before the Sages at Usha, and they said: Presents that are typically consumed are not collected, and** those **that are not typically consumed are collected. What, is it not** referring to presents that are typically consumed **even though they were not consumed?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the mishna is referring to presents **that were** actually **consumed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: סִבְלוֹנוֹת מוּעָטִין שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, וְהִיא בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ – אֵין נִגְבִּין! תַּרְגְּמַהּ רָבָא: בְּיֵיבָא וּסְבַכְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If he sent a **few presents for her to use while she is in her father's house,** they **are not collected.** This indicates that they cannot be collected in any event, irrespective of whether they were used or not. **Rava interpreted** the mishna as referring **to a snood or hairnet,** which are insignificant items that the betrothed man sends without any intention of later collecting them.

A further set of questions now confronts the Gemara. *Ibaya lehu: mahu sheyisholash* — what is the rule if a full *dinar*'s worth was not consumed, say only half a *dinar*? Do the gifts become proportionally finalized — half finalized, half recoverable? This is a strange implication of the *dinar* requirement and the Gemara leaves it unresolved.

*Shevach savlonot mahu* — what is the *din* if the *sabalonot* appreciated in value during the period between *kiddushin* and *nissu'in*, in a case where the gifts are ultimately to be returned? Who owns the increase in value?

The Gemara frames this as a conceptual question about the underlying nature of the transaction. *Keivan de'inhu ledidei hadri birshutei shevach* — since these gifts ultimately revert to the groom, does that mean they were always effectively his property, and therefore the appreciation belongs to him? Or, *keivan de'i avdu o ignavu ba'ei shalumei lei* — since if the gifts were lost or stolen during this period, the bride's family would have had to compensate the groom for them, does that indicate that the gifts were in fact in the bride's *reshut* (legal domain), and therefore the appreciation belongs to her? The Gemara answers: *teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

This is a genuinely pivotal conceptual question, and closely parallels a discussion treated on the previous daf regarding the *kesef kiddushin* — the betrothal gift — and its status if the wedding does not proceed. There, too, the underlying question is whether these gifts constitute an absolute transfer of ownership with a contingent obligation of repayment, or something more like a conditional gift, a *matana betnai*. The Gemara there — *letivuyei nitnu*, "they were given to beautify her" — seemed to indicate that the *kiddushin* gifts are absolute gifts, with the repayment obligation existing alongside the transfer rather than negating it. The same conceptual tension applies here to *sabalonot*, and the *teiku* leaves it unresolved.

Rava then raises yet another question: *savlonot ha'asuyin livalot velo balu mahu* — what if the gifts belonged to the *asuyot livlot* category — food, wearable clothing, consumables — but in fact were not consumed? They are still intact. Do they now revert to the groom's family, since they are actually present and recoverable?

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the earlier ruling of Usha, which spoke of gifts that are *ha'asuyot livlot* — made to be consumed — without specifying that they were actually consumed. This seems to support the view that the category designation, not the actual fact of consumption, is determinative. But the Gemara pushes back: perhaps *d'balu* is what was meant — that they are things that have in fact been consumed. The Mishnah's language — *savlonot muatim she'tishtamesh bahen b'veit aviha* — items used up in the father's house — also does not clearly resolve it. Rava interprets the Mishnah narrowly: *targama Rava beivei v'savchei* — the Mishnah was speaking only of trivial items like a kerchief or scarf, and for more substantial items that happen still to be intact, the matter may be different. This reading is admittedly far from the plain meaning of the Mishnah, but it is at least what the Gemara offers.

### Atzeret Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁשִּׁגֵּר לְבֵית חָמִיו יַיִן חָדָשׁ וְשֶׁמֶן חָדָשׁ וּכְלֵי פִּשְׁתָּן חָדָשׁ בַּעֲצֶרֶת. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חֲשִׁיבוּתָא דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּאִי טָעֵין, טַעַנְתֵּיהּ טַעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving one man who sent new wine, and new oil, and new linen garments to his father-in-law's house at the** time of ***Shavuot*.** The Gemara asks: **What** is this incident **teaching us?** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that it **teaches us the importance,** i.e., the greatness, **of Eretz Yisrael,** where there is new wine, oil, and linen already available at the time of *Shavuot*. **And if you wish, say** instead that it teaches **that if** the betrothed man **claims** that he sent these items at the time of *Shavuot*, **his claim is** a plausible **claim,** and there is no reason to question it.

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: there was a man who sent to his father-in-law's house new wine, new oil, and new flax garments on *Atzeres* (Shavuos). The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is this story meant to teach us? Two possible answers are offered. One is simply a point of praise for the Land of Israel: *chashivus d'Eretz Yisrael kamashma lan* — in Eretz Yisrael, the agricultural bounty is so abundant that new wine, oil, and fine linen are already available and ready to send as gifts even at the early date of *Atzeres*, far sooner than in the Diaspora. Alternatively, the story establishes evidentiary credibility: if someone claims in court that he sent wine, oil, and linen as *sabalonot* at *Shavuos* time, one should not automatically dismiss this as implausible — the story proves it actually happened and is therefore not inherently a false claim, even if it raises questions of burden of proof.

### Impaired Smell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁאָמְרוּ לוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ תּוֹתְרָנִית הִיא, וְנִכְנַס אַחֲרֶיהָ לְחוּרְבָּה לְבוֹדְקָהּ, אָמַר לָהּ: רֵיחַ צְנוֹן אֲנִי מֵרִיחַ בַּגָּלִיל. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מִן יָהֵיב לַן מִכּוֹתָבוֹת דִּירִיחוֹ וְאָכַלְנָא בֵּיהּ. נְפַל עֲלַהּ חוּרְבָּה, וּמֵתָה. אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נִכְנַס אַחֲרֶיהָ אֶלָּא לְבוֹדְקָהּ, מֵתָה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving one man who was told** that **his wife,** i.e., his betrothed, **was one whose sense of smell was impaired, and he followed her into a ruin,** carrying a date with him, **to check her** to see if she could correctly identify the smell. **He said to her: I smell** the **scent of radish in the Galilee. She said to him: Who will give us of the dates of Jericho that I shall eat them,** hinting that she smelled the date he had brought with him. **The ruin collapsed upon her and she died. The Sages said: Since he went into** the ruin **after her only to check her** sense of smell, and not for the purpose of consummating their marriage, if **she dies, he does not inherit** from **her,** as the marriage was not effected, and a man does not inherit from his betrothed.

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: there was a man who was told *ishto tutranis hi* — his wife's sense of smell is impaired. Following the *Rashbam* and *Tosafot*, this is understood to refer to a situation after *kiddushin* but before *nissu'in*; the impaired sense of smell would constitute a *mum* — a blemish — with potential legal consequences regarding *get*, *ketubbah*, and the validity of the marriage, questions elaborated in Kesubos and Kiddushin.

To investigate the claim, the man followed his wife into a deserted area — *nichnas achareiha l'churba l'vodkah*. He said to her: *rei'ach tzonan ani meri'ach baGalil* — I can smell radishes from the Galilee, apparently either concealing a radish or a date beneath his garment and gauging her response. She replied that if only she could have the pressed dates of Jericho to eat — the city known for its dates — she would delight in them together with the radishes. According to the *Rashbam*, it was apparent from her response that she knew what he was about.

Whatever the result of this informal investigation, what followed was that the ruin collapsed upon her and she died — *nafal aleiha churba umesa*. The husband sought to inherit her estate, which as a general principle a husband can do only after *nissu'in*, not after *kiddushin* alone. He argued that he had brought her onto his property and that she had entered it for the purpose of *nissu'in*, which under halachic principles can constitute *chuppah* and thus effect *nissu'in*.

The sages, however, ruled against him: *ho'il v'lo nichnas achareiha ella l'vodkah* — since his entire purpose in bringing her there was to test her and to investigate whether to proceed with the marriage, this entry could not possibly be characterized as entry onto his property for the purpose of *nissu'in*. It was, if anything, an act of doubt and scrutiny, the very opposite of a consummating intention. Therefore, no *nissu'in* had taken place, and he could not inherit her.

## Conditional Return

### Ravin's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִבְלוֹנוֹת מוּעָטִין שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ וְכוּ׳. יָתֵיב רָבִין סָבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: בֵּין שֶׁמֵּתָה הִיא וּבֵין שֶׁמֵּת הוּא, הָדַר הוּא – סִבְלוֹנוֹת הָדְרִי, מַאֲכָל וּמִשְׁתֶּה לָא הָדַר. הָדְרָא בָּהּ אִיהִי – הָדְרָא אֲפִילּוּ כִּישָּׁא דְיַרְקָא. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְשָׁמִין לָהֶן דְּמֵי בָשָׂר בְּזוֹל. עַד כַּמָּה ״בְּזוֹל״? עַד תִּילְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he sent **a few presents for her to use while in her father's house,** they are not collected. **Ravin the Elder was sitting before Rav Pappa and he was sitting and saying: Whether she died, or whether he died,** or whether **he retracted** his agreement to the betrothal and divorced her, the **presents return** to the betrothed man or his heirs, but **food and drink do not return.** If **she retracted** her agreement to the betrothal and requested a divorce, **even a bundle of vegetables returns** to the betrothed man. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** When the presents are returned, the court **appraises for** the betrothed woman's family the sum that they must repay for any meat he gave her, according **to a reduced** assessment of the **value of** the **meat** and not according to the price the betrothed man paid. **How much** less is the **reduced** assessment? **Up to one-third** less than he paid.

Ravin the Elder, sitting before Rav Pappa, teaches the following comprehensive rule governing when *sabalonot* are returned. In all cases where the gifts are to be returned — whether because they are *einan asuyot livlot* or because no *seudat chatan* took place — the return applies across all scenarios of the marriage's dissolution: *bein shemesa hi ubein shemes hu* — whether she died or he died, and *hadar hu* — or he was the one who called off the *nissu'in*. In all of these cases: *savlonot hadrei* — the *sabalonot* that are returnable are returned; *ma'achal umishteh lo hadrei* — food and drink are not returned.

To be precise about the scope of this rule: it describes the situation where the groom did eat *b'veit chamiv* — at the father-in-law's house. In that case, the *asuyot livlot* category — the best example being food consumed — is finalized and does not return. The *einan asuyot livlot* — items expected to come back with the bride at *nissu'in* — always return, regardless of who died or who called off the wedding, so long as the items are still present.

### Woman Backs Out

The *chidush* comes now. *Hadra ba ee hi* — if she is the one who calls off the *nissu'in*, who backs out on her own initiative, then the equation changes dramatically. In that case, *hadra afilu kishara d'yarka* — even a bundle of vegetables must be returned. Even food that was consumed at the *seudat chatan*, even items entirely within the *asuyot livlot* category — everything must be repaid, because it was her decision to walk away. The finalization ordinarily accomplished by the *seudat chatan* is effectively nullified when the cancellation is her unilateral choice.

### Cheap Meat Valuation

However, Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: even in this case — where she bears responsibility and must repay even consumed food — *shamin lahen d'mei basar b'zol*, the repayment is calculated not at the price the groom paid for the food but at the price of inexpensive meat. *Ad kama — ad tilsa*: figure it at approximately one-third below what was actually paid.

This reflects a general halachic principle: when someone consumes another's food without having stolen it, under a reasonable assumption that they were entitled to it, and it subsequently turns out the entitlement was absent, the measure of unjust enrichment is calculated not at the market price of what was consumed but at what the consumer would themselves have spent on equivalent food. The luxury steaks provided at the celebration are not necessarily what the bride's family would have purchased for themselves, and so when computing what they owe back, we assess at the standard of inexpensive rather than premium food. That is the final *chidush* of the sugya: even when she is fully obligated to repay everything, the valuation is adjusted downward in keeping with this general principle of benefit calculation.
