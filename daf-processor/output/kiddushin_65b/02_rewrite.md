# Kiddushin 65b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witnesses & Kiddushin

### One Witness & Agreement

The central question animating this sugya is whether witnesses play a merely evidentiary role in *kiddushin* — that is, whether they are needed only to prove that the betrothal occurred — or whether they are constitutive of the act itself, such that without witnesses, the *kiddushin* simply did not take place. A further dimension of this question concerns whether one witness might suffice when both parties acknowledge that the *kiddushin* occurred. If the couple themselves agree, one might reason that the evidentiary function is already satisfied by their mutual admission, rendering a second witness superfluous.

### Rav & Rabbah Rulings

The *gemara* opens with a clear and emphatic ruling. In the name of Rav, Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta states: if a man performs *kiddushin* with only one witness, *ein chosheshin lekidushav* — there is no concern whatsoever that any *kiddushin* has taken place. Crucially, this applies *va'afilu shenehem modim* — even if both parties agree that the betrothal occurred. The mutual admission of husband and wife cannot substitute for the requirement of two witnesses.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna then repeats this ruling, attributing it to *Bei Dina Rabba* — "the great court." The *gemara* asks: who is this great court? The answer is Rav himself. An alternative version of the tradition has Rabbah bar Rav Huna citing Rav directly, in which case *Bei Dina Rabba* refers to Rebbi. In either formulation, an authority of the highest stature stands behind this position, and its force extends even to the rabbinic level — that is, it is not merely a *de'oraita* invalidation. This matters enormously in practice, because any requirement of a *get* carries grave consequences for the woman, and the rabbis were deeply reluctant to impose one without firm grounding.

### Two Men, Woman Bundle

A challenge is raised from the following scenario: two men arrive from overseas together with a woman and a bundle of belongings. Each man claims the woman as his wife, the other man as his slave, and the bundle as his property. The woman, for her part, claims both men as her slaves and the bundle as hers. The ruling is that she requires two *gittin* and may collect her *ketubah* from the bundle.

The immediate question is why she should need a *get* from either man, given that he is merely making a claim without witnessed proof. The *gemara* investigates: if each man had witnesses to his marriage, her counterclaim would carry no weight against their testimony. It therefore concludes that the case must involve one witness per claimant — and since one witness for one man is effectively cancelled by one witness for the other, we are back to the position of mutual, unwitnessed claims. *Ela le-mishrei le-alma de-kulei alma lo peligi de-sharya* — to permit her to the general public, everyone agrees she is free. The requirement of two *gittin*, then, is not a genuine *halachic* demand but a practical mechanism: only if one of the men voluntarily gives a *get* would she be entitled to collect her *ketubah* from the contested bundle. The *chiddush* is that of Rabbi Meir, who holds that movable property (*metaltelin*) is subject to the *ketubah* lien. The case thus does not prove that one witness effects *kiddushin*; rather, the absence of any objective proof means that no *get* is truly required, and the question reduces entirely to the disposition of the assets.

A final practical note: the *gemara* is very reluctant, absent clear necessity, to declare that a woman requires a *get*, given the devastating consequences in practice — the contemporary reality of *agunah* being the most acute expression of that concern.

## Proof vs. Constitutive

### Kahana vs. Pappa

The *gemara* now asks *mai havya alah* — what is the bottom line of all this analysis? Two positions emerge. Rav Kahana rules *ein chosheshin lekiddushin* — there is no concern at all; *kiddushin* performed with a single witness is entirely without effect. Rav Pappa rules *chosheshin lekiddushin* — he is still somewhat concerned, perhaps at the level of rabbinic stringency.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

Rav Ashi presses Rav Kahana: what is the source for his confident ruling that a single witness effects no *kiddushin* whatsoever? The most natural source would be a *gezeirah shava*: the Torah uses the word *davar* in the phrase *al pi shenei edim yakum davar* — testimony is established by two witnesses — and the same word *davar* appears in the phrase *ki matza bah ervat davar*, which concerns marital and personal status matters. From this verbal analogy, one might derive that the two-witness requirement applies to *davar she-be-erva* — matters of personal status — just as it applies to monetary matters.

But here the challenge bites: by monetary matters, the rule is *hoda'at ba'al din keme'ah edim dami* — a litigant's own admission is equivalent to a hundred witnesses. The debt exists whether or not there were witnesses present; the witnesses serve only to prove it in court. If the need for two witnesses in *kiddushin* is derived from the monetary domain, why not draw the same inference — that if both parties admit the *kiddushin* occurred, their admission should function like witnesses, and the *kiddushin* should be valid?

### Money vs. Status

The answer given is fundamental: *hatam lo kachaiv l'achrinei hacha kachaiv l'achrinei* — there is a crucial difference. When a borrower admits a debt, he harms only himself. His admission is therefore fully effective. But when a husband and wife admit to a *kiddushin*, they are harming others. The husband's admission restricts the wife vis-à-vis all other men; the wife's admission restricts the husband from her relatives; and other men in the world are now prohibited from marrying her under pain of adultery. Matters of personal status have societal ramifications that reach far beyond the admitting parties, and therefore personal admission — *hoda'at ba'al din* — cannot substitute for objective, external testimony.

Yet one might observe that this argument proves only that admission is insufficient as proof; it does not yet prove that witnesses are constitutive — that without them, the act did not occur at all. The gap between "we require a higher standard of proof" and "without witnesses, nothing happened" is a substantial one, and it is a point that troubles the *acharonim*. The *gemara* itself, they note, never fully bridges that gap.

### Edei Beirur Kiyum

This brings us to the conceptual heart of the discussion: the distinction between *edei beirur* and *edei kiyum*. *Edei beirur* — proof witnesses — serve only to establish in the eyes of the court that something happened. If everyone is honest and the fact is admitted, witnesses of this type are redundant. *Edei kiyum* — constitutive witnesses — do something altogether different: they make the act happen. Without their presence, the act is a legal nullity, regardless of what the parties intended or admit.

By monetary matters, witnesses are *edei beirur*. The obligation exists independently of whether anyone witnessed it; the witnesses merely allow us to enforce it. By *kiddushin* and *gittin*, witnesses are *edei kiyum* — without them, the marriage or divorce did not occur.

There is, however, a further debate among the *acharonim* about precisely how to conceptualize the *edei kiyum* function. One view holds that witnesses are still fundamentally evidentiary, but because *kiddushin* is *chav l'achrinei* — it impacts broader society — a society that cannot verify its occurrence cannot recognize it. In the absence of proof, the act did not, as a social-legal matter, happen. A second view holds that the witnesses do something categorically different: they formalize and give gravity to the act. Just as one cannot be executed without witnesses and *hatra'ah*, the weight and solemnity of *kiddushin* demands the formal presence of witnesses as a constitutive element of the act.

The *nafka mina* between these two views is whether witnesses hidden behind a bush — unknown to the bride and groom — would suffice. If witnesses are fundamentally there to prove that the act occurred, then hidden witnesses accomplish exactly that. If, however, witnesses must be part of formalizing the act and impressing its gravity upon the parties, their concealment might be fatal. The *Ktzot* in *Choshen Mishpat* engages this question extensively, drawing on a *machloket Rishonim* about precisely this scenario.

### Property Division

Mar Zutra and Rav Adda Sava, the sons of Rav Mari bar Issur, divided their inherited property between themselves without witnesses. They came before Rav Ashi with a question: does *al pi shnei edim yakum davar* — the Torah's requirement of two witnesses — mean that the division is simply invalid without witnesses? Or is the entire function of witnesses in monetary matters only to prevent subsequent denial, and since neither party intends to back out, the division stands?

### Abaye Liars Principle

Abaye answered them with the formulation that became the classic expression of the *edei beirur* principle: *lo ivru sahadei ella leshakrei* — witnesses were created (*nivre'u*) only for liars. The entire purpose of witnesses in monetary matters is to prevent dishonest parties from denying what occurred. In a world of honest parties who both affirm what happened, witnesses add nothing and are not required to validate the act. This is *edei beirur* in its purest form. And the implicit contrast with *kiddushin* and *gittin* could not be sharper: there, the witnesses are *edei kiyum*, and their absence means the act never happened, honest parties notwithstanding.

One might draw an interesting parallel to *malkut* and capital punishment, which similarly require both witnesses and *hatra'ah*. There, too, *hoda'at ba'al din* does not work. Whether the witnesses and *hatra'ah* in those cases are there to establish guilt evidentiary or to constitute the particular character of a defiant, warned transgression is a parallel conceptual question — one that mirrors the *edei beirur/edei kiyum* debate and is left here as a worthy avenue for further study.

## One Witness & Silence

### Forbidden Fat Case

Abaye now introduces a series of cases in which a single witness is believed when the person he testifies against remains silent. The first: one witness says to a person, *achalta cheilev* — you ate forbidden fat (*chelev*) inadvertently. The person says nothing. The *gemara* rules: *ne'eman* — the witness is believed, and the person would be obligated to bring a *korban*. A supporting *baraita* confirms this: if the accused says *lo achalti* — I did not eat it — he is exempt. The *ta'ama de'amar lo* — the reason is because he denied it. But *ha ishtik meheiman* — if he was silent, the witness is believed and the *korban* is required.

### Grain Purity Case

Abaye continues: one witness says to a person, *nitme'u taharotecha* — your grain has become *tamei*. The person is silent. The witness is believed, and the grain may not be eaten in a state of *tahara*. Again, a parallel *baraita* is cited: if one witness says the grain became *tamei* and the owner contradicts him, the grain is *tahor*. The reason is because he said otherwise; had he been silent, the single witness would have been believed.

### Ox Bestiality Case

A third case: one witness says *shorcha nirva* — your ox was used in an act of bestiality. The owner is silent. The witness is believed, and the ox is disqualified from being brought as a *korban*. Note that without two witnesses, the ox would not be stoned — the capital penalty for the animal requires two witnesses — but the prohibition on bringing it as a *korban* is triggered even by a single witness coupled with the owner's silence. A *baraita* confirms: animals used in sin or that killed a person *al pi ed echad* — based on one witness — or *al pi ba'alim* — based on the owner's admission — are disqualified from the altar. The *gemara* asks: what does *al pi ed echad* mean if the owner already admitted? It must mean the owner was silent, and silence functions as admission.

### Three Cases Analysis

The *gemara* then performs a *tzrichuta* — explaining why all three cases needed to be stated. In the *chelev* case, silence is clearly an admission: *dei lav dekimlei benafshei de-avad* — if he truly did not know he had eaten the forbidden fat, he would not remain silent, because the consequence of his silence is that he must bring a *korban*. And no one would bring a *korban* they were not certain they were obligated to bring, since that would constitute *chullin ba-azarah* — bringing non-sacred animals into the Temple precincts in a sacred context. His silence thus unmistakably signals acknowledgment.

In the grain case, one might think the silence is not admission — perhaps he reasons he will simply eat the grain on a day when he is *tamei* and suffer no loss, making his silence meaningless. The case therefore needed to be stated to show that since he does lose access to the grain on days when he is *tahor*, his silence is indeed an admission.

In the ox case, one might think silence means nothing — perhaps he never intended to bring the ox as a *korban* anyway, so he sustains no real loss by remaining silent. That case also needed to be stated.

### Tosafot Silence Distinct…

This *tzrichuta* leads Tosafot to a crucial interpretive conclusion: the operative legal mechanism in all three cases is not that the single witness is believed in his own right, but that the owner's silence is read as an admission. The witness functions as the prompt for an admission; it is the admission itself that carries legal weight. A critical *nafka mina* follows: if the owner says *eini yode'a* — I don't know — this is not silence, and therefore not admission, and the witness would not be believed. Similarly, if the witness testifies about something that occurred in the owner's absence — where silence cannot possibly constitute acknowledgment — the witness would likewise not be believed.

Tosafot also raises a broader question: given the principle of *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin* — a single witness is ordinarily believed regarding matters of prohibited status — why do these cases require the owner's silence at all? The answer seems to lie in the fact that all three cases involve a direct economic consequence to the owner of the property in question. This is qualitatively different from the general rule about *issurin*, where the witness is speaking about the status of an item without directly threatening the owner's assets. When a witness's testimony would force the owner to incur a loss — sacrificing an animal, discarding grain, losing the use of an ox — additional indicia of reliability are required, and the owner's own silence provides that assurance.

## Personal Status & Witnes…

### Wife Adultery Case

The *gemara* now poses the question that the preceding analysis has been building toward: *ishto zinta be-ed echad veshotek* — a single witness tells a man that his wife committed adultery, and the husband remains silent. What is the law?

To understand the stakes, a preliminary distinction must be drawn. If the husband himself declares that his wife committed adultery, the consequence is that he is bound by his own statement and must divorce her — but since he is exercising what is always his unilateral right to divorce, this is not considered *chav l'achrinei* in the relevant sense. He must, however, pay the *ketubah*, since there is no objective proof of the transgression. Now: what if a single witness makes this claim and the husband says nothing?

The single witness cannot be believed in the ordinary sense for matters of personal status. The principle of *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin* does not apply to *davar she-be-erva* — matters concerning marital and sexual status — which require two witnesses even for purposes of proof, not merely for the constitutive function discussed above.

### Abaye Rava Debate

Abaye rules *ne'eman* — the witness is believed, and the husband would be required to divorce his wife. Rava rules *eino ne'eman* — *havay davar shebe'ervah ve'ein davar shebe'ervah pachos mishnayim*: this is a matter of *ervah*, and matters of *ervah* require a minimum of two witnesses.

Tosafot explains the debate in terms of the distinction developed in the preceding section. Abaye reads the husband's silence as an admission. Since the husband is always personally bound by his own admission regarding his wife's fidelity, and since his silence functions as admission, the result is that he must divorce her — not because the external witness is believed as a formal matter of *davar she-be-erva*, but because the husband himself has constructively admitted the fact. Rava, by contrast, holds that the legal mechanism here is not silence-as-admission but the credibility of the external witness, and that credibility is simply insufficient for *davar she-be-erva*. Without two witnesses, the testimony cannot establish the wife's transgression, and the husband has no obligation to act.

### Blind Tanna Story

Abaye adduces a compelling source for his position. He notes: *dehava samya dehava mesader masneisa kamei deMar Shmuel* — there was a blind man who served as Shmuel's *tanna*, memorizing and reciting the Tannaitic traditions in the *yeshiva*. One day this man was running late. Shmuel sent a messenger to fetch him. The messenger took one route to his house while the *tanna* arrived by another, so they did not meet. In the meantime, the messenger — having arrived at the *tanna*'s house in his absence — witnessed the man's wife engaged in an act of infidelity. When the messenger returned to Shmuel and reported what he had seen, the *tanna* came before Shmuel and asked: must I divorce my wife?

This case is structurally revealing. The husband was not present and therefore could not have been silent in any meaningful sense. There is no question of *shtikat ke-hoda'ah* here. The entire question is about the weight of a single, unopposed witness in a matter of personal status, absent any admission. Shmuel ruled: *im meheman lach* — if you trust him, *zil apkah* — go divorce her; *va'i lo lo tipik* — if not, do not divorce her.

Abaye reads *im meheman lach* as meaning: if you know him to be reliable in the sense of not being a *gazlan* — that is, if there is no specific reason to doubt him — then you treat an unopposed single witness as sufficient to compel divorce. This would be a striking ruling: even absent any admission, an unopposed *ed echad* can operate in a matter of *davar she-be-erva*.

### Rava Rebuttal

Rava reads Shmuel's ruling entirely differently. *Im meheman lach ke-bei trei zil apkah* — if you trust him *as much as* two witnesses, then you must divorce her. This is not about the ordinary credibility of a single witness in the absence of contradiction. It is about a special and exceptional personal trust, equivalent to ironclad certainty, that this specific person would never lie. In the absence of that extraordinary trust, the testimony of a single witness in a matter of *davar she-be-erva* carries no legal weight whatsoever. The woman's marital status remains unchanged, and no divorce is required.

## King Yannai Narrative

### Yannai Feast Scheme

Abaye now seeks a further proof-text, this time from a striking historical *baraita* — a passage that has a parallel in Josephus and has attracted the attention of historians. The *baraita* recounts: King Yannai went to the region of Kochlit in the wilderness and conquered sixty cities. Upon his triumphant return, he was filled with great joy and summoned all the sages of Israel. He said to them: our ancestors, when they were engaged in building the *Beit HaMikdash*, ate salted foods (*meluchim*) in their poverty — let us likewise eat salted foods in memory of them. In a striking contrast, these humble foods were served upon golden tables. There is a *mishibud lige'ulah* quality to the moment — a deliberate juxtaposition of former deprivation and present glory.

At the feast, one man was present who is described in almost biblical literary language as a *latz*, a mocker — evil and scheming. His name was Elazar ben Po'ira. He approached King Yannai and said: *libam shel Perushim alecha* — the hearts of the Pharisees, meaning the sages, are against you. They do not support you.

### Yehuda Challenge

Yannai asked what he should do to test this claim. Elazar advised him: wear the *tzitz* — the golden headplate of the *Kohen Gadol* — and observe the reaction of the sages. (The *gemara* notes in passing the debate between Rashi and Tosafot about whether a *Kohen Gadol* is permitted to wear *bigdei kehunah* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and whether the *tzitz* — which the Torah describes as to be worn *tamid*, always — carries a special dispensation to be worn continuously. Neither question is the *halachic* focus here.)

One elder at the feast, named Yehuda ben Gedidya, spoke up — not very astutely, as it turned out: *rav lecha keter malchut* — the crown of kingship is sufficient for you, Yannai. *Hanach keter kehunah lezar'o shel Aharon* — leave the crown of *kehunah* for the descendants of Aaron. The reason he offered was that there had been a rumor that Yannai's mother had been taken captive at Modi'im. If true, this would have disqualified her from her *kohen* husband, rendering Yannai a *chalal* — someone of compromised priestly lineage — and unfit to serve as *Kohen Gadol*.

### Mother Captivity Rumor

The *baraita* states: *vayivukash hadavar velo nimtza* — the matter was investigated and nothing was found to substantiate it. The rumor had not been established. Nevertheless, Yehuda ben Gedidya's remark caused enormous offense, and *vayibadlu chachmei Yisrael beza'am* — the sages of Israel were separated, driven out in the king's fury. Perhaps their silence at Yehuda's outburst was read as complicity.

### Sages Destruction

Elazar ben Po'ira then pressed his advantage, saying to Yannai: if this were merely an ordinary person (*hedyot she-be-Yisrael*), what was done to Yehuda ben Gedidya would be appropriate. But you are a king and *Kohen Gadol* — is this the treatment you deserve? (The parallel in Josephus fills in the gap: the sages had imposed lashes on Yehuda ben Gedidya for *motzi shem ra*, and Elazar was arguing that this trivial penalty demonstrated the sages' fundamental disrespect for the king's dignity.)

When Yannai asked what he should do, Elazar replied: *romsem* — destroy them. Yannai asked: but what will become of the Torah? Elazar answered: *harei keruchah umunachat bekeren zavit* — it is rolled up and sitting in the corner. Any person who wishes to study may come and study. The Torah scroll is still there; kill the sages and the Torah survives.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak observes: *miyad nizreka bo apikorsus* — immediately a spirit of *apikorsus* took hold of Yannai. What was his *apikorsus*? That he should have responded: *tinach Torah shebikhtav, Torah she-be-al peh mai* — granted, we still have the Written Torah; but what of the Oral Torah? Who will transmit it? By remaining silent at Elazar's answer, Yannai implicitly accepted the premise that the Written Torah alone suffices — a denial, in effect, of the entire tradition of *Torah she-be-al peh*. It is worth noting that *apikorsus* in the *gemara* in Sanhedrin is specifically defined as *mevazeh talmidei chachamim* — contempt for the sages and their authority — which makes its deployment here particularly pointed.

### Shimon Restoration

*Miyad* — immediately — *vayahargu kol chachmei Yisrael* — all the sages of Israel were killed. *Vehaya ha-olam mishtomem* — the world was desolate and bereft. This continued until Shimon ben Shatach — who had been hidden away, reportedly because he was the brother of Yannai's wife — emerged and *hechzir et ha-Torah le-yoshnah*, restored the Torah to its former state. Some connect this restoration to the tradition that Shimon ben Shatach was instrumental in establishing primary Jewish education, ensuring that all children would be educated from a young age — a fitting counterpoint to the catastrophic loss described here.

### Evidence Analysis

The *gemara* now asks: what exactly was the evidentiary situation regarding the rumor about Yannai's mother? The *baraita* says it was investigated and dismissed. But what kind of investigation was this?

If two witnesses had originally testified that she was taken captive, and then two witnesses contradicted them, the result should be a standoff — *mai chazit disomkhat ahanei somkhat ahanei* — why do we credit one pair over the other? Rashi suggests that even in a two-against-two standoff, Yannai's status might remain in doubt, because while the mother had a *chezkat kashrut* going in her favor, Yannai himself — as the child whose status is the operative question — may never have had an established presumption of priestly fitness to fall back on. But this is itself a difficult position, since our intuition is that two witnesses against two witnesses should simply neutralize one another.

Abaye's reading of the story is that the evidence was one witness against one witness or one witness unopposed — and since an unopposed single witness is believed even in matters of *davar she-be-erva*, the rumor initially gained traction until it was directly contradicted.

## Unopposed Witness Conclu…

### Rava Position

Rava rejects Abaye's reading of the story entirely. He maintains *le'olam trei vetrei* — it was in fact two against two. The reason the claim was dismissed rather than left in doubt is explained by reference to the principle of *edei hazamah*: the second pair of witnesses did not simply testify to a different version of events, but rather told the first pair *atem einkem yecholim* — you could not have been there to see what you claim, because you were elsewhere with us. When witnesses are subjected to *hazamah* — a direct challenge to their physical presence at the scene of the alleged event — their testimony is not merely contradicted but completely annulled. Therefore the original two witnesses were entirely discredited, and nothing remained of the accusation.

Alternatively, Rava offers the explanation of Rabbi Yitzchak: *shifcha hachnisu tachteha* — a maidservant was substituted for Yannai's mother in captivity. The second pair of witnesses did not contradict the first pair but completed the account: yes, she was taken, but she was subsequently rescued and a maidservant was swapped in her place. In this reading, the testimony of the second pair does not cancel the first but resolves the situation into a positive outcome.

### Abaye Position

The debate concludes in a state of tension. Abaye holds the bold position that an unopposed single witness is believed even in personal status matters — *davar she-be-erva* — such that a husband confronted with such testimony and remaining silent would be compelled to divorce his wife. Rava holds that for *davar she-be-erva*, two witnesses are always required, whether for the constitutive function that creates the act of *kiddushin* or *gittin*, or for the evidentiary function of proving that a personal-status fact occurred. A single witness, even one who goes wholly unopposed, cannot satisfy that threshold. The *halacha* will follow Rava — as it nearly always does against Abaye — and the requirement of two witnesses for *davar she-be-erva* stands in full force. The discussion continues on the following *amud*.
