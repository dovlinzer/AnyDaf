# Taanit 13b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bathing Rules: Aveil

### Intro: Taanit vs Aveil

The sugya at the bottom of 13b continues a discussion already well underway: the distinction between the bathing restrictions that apply on *Tisha B'Av* and those that apply during *avelut*. The operative question is whether cold water is forbidden only for an *aveil* or also for someone observing a *ta'anit*, and conversely whether the prohibition for a *ta'anit* reaches no further than hot water. Both possibilities remain theoretically on the table as the *Gemara* proceeds.

### Taanit Exceptions

The *Gemara* opens with a *braita* that establishes two permitted exceptions to the general *ta'anit* restrictions. First, regarding the prohibition of *melacha*: when the rabbis said that work is forbidden on the more serious communal fast days, they said so only for the daytime — at night, before the fast, it remains permitted. Second, regarding *ne'ilat hasandal*, the prohibition against wearing shoes: when they said one may not wear shoes, they meant only within the city. On the road — *baderech* — it is permitted. The straightforward explanation for the travel exemption is the practical impossibility of demanding that someone journey barefoot. There is also, however, a distinctly public dimension to many of these practices. Closing bathhouses, closing shops, going unshod in public — all of these carry a communal, visible character. The shoe prohibition, even as it applies to the private *aveil*, expresses a kind of withdrawal from normal civic life; within the city that public dimension is operative, while travel removes the person from that context. The *braita* then adds the practical application: as one leaves the city, one puts on the shoe; the moment one enters the city, one removes it.

### Hands, Face, Feet

The *braita* continues: when the rabbis said that bathing is forbidden, they meant only bathing the entire body. But washing the face, hands, and feet — *panav yadav v'raglav* — remains permitted. This represents the minimal level of hygiene needed for basic well-being and dignity, and so it is carved out as an exception even on a full fast day.

### Menudeh and Aveil

The *braita* then states: *vechen atah motzei bimnudeh uve'avel* — the same rules and the same allowances found in the *ta'anit* context apply equally to a person in *niduy* (*menudeh*) and to an *aveil*. It is worth pausing on the pairing of *menudeh* and *aveil* here, because it echoes a formulation from the *Mishnah* studied earlier. The *Mishnah*, describing the posture of the community once all the fasts have passed without rain, says the people conduct themselves *kevnei adam hanezofim lamakom* — like people who have been rejected by God. The *menudeh* is precisely the worldly, social counterpart of that theological status. Just as God has rejected the community, the community formally excludes the *menudeh*. What the *Mishnah* calls *nezufim lamakom* finds its halakhic parallel in the institution of *niduy*. Many of the individual practices associated with *niduy* — not merely the communal dimension — parallel the practices of *avelut*. This is instructive on a deeper level. *Avelut* simultaneously expresses the grief of personal loss, the profound sense of being cut off from ordinary life, and the inability to re-integrate into the communal fabric. The *menudeh* occupies a structurally similar place: excluded, unable to engage normally with community and with God. The pairing in the *braita* thus resonates meaningfully with the *mishna*'s *nezufim lamakom*.

### Braita Proof

This identification becomes directly relevant to the cold-water question. The *Gemara* asks: *mai lav akuleihu* — when the *braita* equates the rules for *menudeh* and *aveil* with those of the *ta'anit*, does it not mean to equate all of them, including the bathing? And specifically, when discussing bathing with the allowance for face, hands, and feet — what type of water are we talking about? If we are talking about hot water, then permitting face, hands, and feet would be impossible to maintain, given Rav Sheshet's ruling that an *aveil* may not even dip a finger into hot water. For the allowance to be coherent, it must refer to cold water. This in turn implies that cold water is what is generally forbidden — not only for an *aveil* but, since the *braita* equates the two, for a *ta'anit* as well.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet's position is stated starkly: *aveil assur lehoshit etzba'o bechamim* — an *aveil* may not insert even a single finger into hot water. This is an extreme stringency and the *Gemara* treats it as unassailable, unwilling to carve out any exceptions within the category of hot water for an *aveil*. The implication is that whatever allowance does exist for an *aveil* in the domain of bathing must be operating exclusively in the domain of cold water.

### Gemara's Resolution

The *Gemara* pushes back on what appeared to be an airtight proof. It rejects the inference that the *braita* equates *ta'anit* and *avelut* with respect to bathing. Rather — *ashe'ara ka'ei* — the equation only concerns the rest of the listed practices, not the bathing. The problem with this resolution is apparent. Of the three cases listed in the *braita* — *melacha*, shoes, and bathing — the *melacha* exception (permitted at night) does not actually apply to an *aveil*, whose prohibition of *melacha* is not confined to daytime. So the equation is not even accurate for all of "the rest." *Rashi* and *Tosafot* attempt to invoke *sichah* (anointing) as a fourth item that could be equated, but anointing was not mentioned in the *braita* at all. The resolution is, as the *Gemara* itself seems to sense, strained. The simple reading of the *braita* is that *avelut* and *ta'anit* are being compared on the question of bathing. The cumulative force of the evidence — the *braita* here, and the earlier discussion about the problem of going to the *mikveh* on *Tisha B'Av*, which strongly suggests that cold water is forbidden — pushes toward the conclusion that cold water is *assur* whether for a *ta'anit* or for an *aveil*. The *Gemara*, however, continues to maneuver, keeping alive the possibility that a *ta'anit* forbids only hot water while *avelut* extends the prohibition to cold water.

## Cold Water Evidence

### Rabi Yosi Story

The *Gemara* introduces a story: Rabi Abba HaKohen citing Rabi Yosi HaKohen reported that there was an incident in which the children of Rabi Yosi ben Rabi Chanina all died — a terrible tragedy, likely from some disease or plague — and Rabi Yosi ben Rabi Chanina bathed in cold water throughout the entire *shiva*. This appears to be direct evidence that cold water is permitted for an *aveil*, apparently contradicting the position being developed.

### Multiple Mournings

The *Gemara* deflects the proof immediately: *hatam keshetakfuhu avalav havay* — that was a special case where multiple mourning periods had piled up one after another. A *braita* is cited in support: if a person's *aveluyot* come in rapid succession — *takfuhu avalav zeh achar zeh*, Heaven forbid — certain leniencies are granted. If his hair has grown heavy from weeks of not cutting, he may lighten it with a razor. If his clothes are filthy, he may launder them in water. Rav Chisda specifies the limits precisely: a razor, not scissors; water without soap or cleansing agents such as sand (*neter* or *chol*). By analogy, someone in such exceptional circumstances might be permitted to bathe in cold water, but this in no way represents the general rule. Indeed, the very framing of the story — emphasizing the exceptional nature of overlapping mournings — underscores that an *aveil* ordinarily may not bathe in cold water.

### Hair & Clothes Care

The *braita* and Rav Chisda's gloss articulate a carefully calibrated set of allowances. The razor but not scissors distinction reflects a principle of minimal intervention: enough relief to prevent the person from being incapacitated by physical discomfort, but not so much as to constitute normal grooming. Similarly, water without detergents permits basic laundering without the comfort of clean, fresh-smelling clothes that full laundering would produce. These calibrations reveal the underlying logic: *avelut* restrictions are designed to impose a real degree of withdrawal from normal bodily care, with targeted relief only where the situation has become literally unmanageable.

### Rava's Position

At this point, Rava stakes out a strikingly different position: *avel mutar lirchotz betzonen kol shiva* — an *aveil* is permitted to bathe in cold water for all seven days of mourning. This directly contradicts the trajectory of the discussion as it had been developing, and the *Gemara* notes the challenge this poses against the evidence considered thus far.

### Meat & Wine Analogy

Rava grounds his position in a pointed analogy: *midi dehavay abisra vechamra* — the same principle that governs meat and wine. The argument is that *avelut* is not fundamentally about physical suffering, *tzaar*. If it were, meat and wine would be forbidden for an *aveil* — and yet they are not. An *aveil* is permitted to eat meat and drink wine throughout the *shiva*. Therefore, the logic of denying cold water on grounds of imposing *tzaar* collapses: the practices of *avelut* must be serving some other purpose. Rather than expressing physical self-affliction, *avelut* might be understood as a total withdrawal from normal engagement with society — a focus so complete that ordinary distraction falls away — without necessarily requiring the positive infliction of pain. If that is the framework, then the relevant prohibition is hot water, which is a genuine indulgence, not cold water, which is not. This is a genuinely fascinating position, and the *Gemara* will now test it against the available evidence.

## Bogeret Proof

### Bogeret Concern

The *Gemara* challenges Rava's position with a *meitivei* from a *braita*: *ein habogeret resha'ah lenabbel atzmah bimei avel aviha* — a *bogeret*, a young woman of mature age who has not yet married, may not make herself repulsive during the *shiva* for her father. The term *bogeret* designates a girl who has passed the age of twelve and a half; she is at marriageable age and still unmarried. The *braita* then adds that the *na'ara* — the younger girl, not yet twelve and a half but already obligated in *avelut* — is permitted or in fact required to conduct herself in the standard manner of *avelut*, which would include all the normal forms of self-neglect. The implication is that a special dispensation is being extended to the *bogeret*: because she needs to present herself as marriageable, and *shiva* visits bring many potential matches through the house, she cannot afford to make herself look disheveled or repulsive in the ways that *avelut* ordinarily demands. It is also worth noting, as was pointed out, that the heightened concern for the *bogeret*'s marriage prospects at this specific moment is likely sharpened by the fact that her father has just died — he was presumably the one who would have been arranging and facilitating her match.

### Hot Water Analysis

The *Gemara* now investigates what exactly this dispensation for the *bogeret* involves. If it concerns bathing in hot water — meaning the *braita* is saying the *bogeret* may not deprive herself of hot water bathing as other *aveilim* do — this is immediately untenable. Rav Chisda's ruling stands: an *aveil* may not insert even a finger into hot water. Given the extreme nature of that prohibition, the *Gemara* is unwilling to entertain the possibility that any leniency would be extended to the *bogeret* with respect to hot water. Surely the *braita* cannot be saying that.

### Cold Water Exception

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ela lav betzonen* — the *braita* must be discussing cold water. The general rule is that cold water is forbidden for an *aveil*; the *bogeret* is granted a special exception to this rule precisely because of her marriage-related circumstances. Hot water remains entirely off the table for all *aveilim* without exception. Cold water is what is normally forbidden, and it is only cold water in which the *bogeret* is exceptionally permitted to bathe. This appears to be a strong proof against Rava's position that cold water is generally permitted.

### Makeup Alternative

The *Gemara* deflects the proof once more, proposing that the *braita* may not be discussing water bathing at all. Rather, it may be discussing *kichul u'firchus* — eye shadow, rouge, and the various cosmetic preparations used to enhance appearance. On this reading, the *braita* says the *bogeret* may not deprive herself of makeup during *shiva*, while the *na'ara* is either obligated or has no such exception. Makeup, it should be noted, is not obviously *assur* during *avelut* in the first place; the *braita* may simply be addressing the question of whether an *aveil* has leeway to practice makeup *bifnei ish* in the context of receiving visitors. In any case, under this interpretation, the proof against Rava dissolves, since the *braita* says nothing about cold water at all.

## Tzaar vs Indulgence

### Logical Parallel

The *Gemara* now turns to examine the underlying logical structure of the debate with greater precision. If one accepts, as the second version of Rava's position holds, that cold water is forbidden for an *aveil*, then a question arises from the meat-and-wine argument used by the first version of Rava: if the basis for forbidding cold water is *tzaar*, why should meat and wine be permitted? The logic cuts both ways. If *avelut* is grounded in physical *tzaar*, then cold water should be *assur*, but so should meat and wine. If meat and wine are permitted, then apparently *avelut* is not about *tzaar*, in which case cold water should also be permitted.

### Anxiety Exception

The *Gemara* resolves this tension with a sophisticated move. It accepts the premise that *avelut* is, in principle, grounded in *tzaar* — meaning the prohibition of cold water makes sense within that framework. But it then explains why meat and wine are nonetheless permitted despite this underlying principle: *hasam letachuchei pachtei hu de'avid* — there, the person eats and drinks in order to abate his fear, to relieve his anxiety. In other words, meat and wine serve a functional purpose that overrides the general principle of *tzaar*: they provide the *aveil* with a measure of relief from the overwhelming emotional burden of mourning. Without that relief, the person may not be able to cope at all.

This is a genuinely important idea in its own right. It parallels other exceptions we have already seen in the laws of *avelut* — the allowance to wear shoes when traveling outside the city, the leniencies granted when multiple *aveluyot* pile up in succession. In each case, there is an acknowledgment that the principle must yield where it would become literally unreasonable to maintain. The *Gemara* is saying that within the framework of *avelut*, which does rest on an idea of *tzaar*, the *aveil*'s emotional and psychological needs create a carve-out for meat and wine, even though the underlying principle would logically forbid them. The parallel to *aninuth* is worth noting: an *onein* is forbidden in *basar veyayin*, and there is a view among the *Rishonim* that *aninuth* and *avelut* are not entirely distinct categories but rather the same underlying framework with different exceptions applicable at different stages. By *avelut*, we make the exception for meat and wine; by *aninuth*, we do not, because the *onein* must remain focused entirely on the burial arrangements.

### Tzaar Assumption

The *Gemara*'s acceptance of the logical parallel between cold water and meat and wine has an important structural implication: it confirms that the *Gemara* is treating *tzaar* as the operative framework for *avelut* restrictions. Both cold water and meat and wine are being measured on the same axis — one of physical deprivation and indulgence. Hot water is an indulgence and is thus forbidden; cold water, though less of an indulgence, still represents a form of bodily comfort that falls on the prohibited side of the line when *tzaar* is the standard. Meat and wine should also be prohibited on this logic, but a targeted exception is created for them because the *aveil* needs them to function.

One might have argued an entirely different framework: that *avelut* restrictions have nothing to do with *tzaar* as such, but rather with the idea of withdrawal from society and from normal, engaged life. Under that reading, cold water would not necessarily be *assur* — it is not an expression of worldly engagement in the way that elaborate grooming or festive eating might be. But the *Gemara*, by framing the debate the way it does and accepting the premise linking cold water to meat and wine, seems to be settling on the *tzaar* framework as the operative one, even while acknowledging that the picture is complicated by necessary exceptions.

## Final Bathing Rules

### Aveil Bathing

The *Gemara* now draws its formal conclusions. The *halacha* is as the thrust of the entire discussion had been trending: an *aveil* is forbidden in cold water throughout the seven days of *shiva*. However, the allowance for face, hands, and feet — *panav yadav v'raglav* — does apply, but only with cold water. Given that even dipping a finger in hot water is forbidden for an *aveil* (following Rav Chisda and Rav Sheshet), the allowance for face, hands, and feet is exclusively in the domain of cold water.

### Sichah Anointing

Regarding *sichah* — anointing the body with oil — the conclusion is that an *aveil* is forbidden even in the smallest amount, *afilu kol shehu assur*. The analogy is roughly to hot water: anointing with oil was understood as a genuine bodily indulgence, even more so in ancient times when it served significant hygienic and aesthetic functions. Because it falls into the category of indulgence rather than basic necessity, no minimum threshold is permitted. There is, however, a critical exception: if the anointing is done *l'haavir es hazuhama* — to remove stench — it is permitted. Since people regularly used oil for deodorant purposes in the absence of other means, this exception was both practically important and logically coherent within the *tzaar* framework: the goal of *avelut* is *tzaar* and withdrawal, not the imposition of social offense.

### Tisha B'Av Parallel

A parallel set of allowances exists for *Tisha B'Av*. As one would expect from the comparison that has been running throughout the sugya, *Tisha B'Av* bathing restrictions track the *ta'anit* side of the distinction: hot water is what is fundamentally forbidden, but exceptions exist where water is needed not for the pleasure of bathing but for the direct removal of dirt or grime. Where the intent is genuine cleanliness rather than bodily indulgence, certain allowances apply. This concludes a *sugya* that has illuminated the comparative structure of *ta'anit* and *avelut* restrictions, the question of whether *avelut* practices are grounded in *tzaar* or in some other conceptual framework, and the relationship between the *menudeh*, the *aveil*, and the *nezufim lamakom* of the *Mishnah*.

## Aneinu Placement

### Individual Aneinu

The *Gemara* shifts — without any transitional signal — to an entirely new topic: the placement of the *Aneinu* prayer within the *Shemoneh Esrei* on fast days. The question is posed directly: *tzlusa d'taanisa heichi madkrinan* — where do we mention the prayer of the fast in the *Shemoneh Esrei*?

Rav Yehuda told his son Rav Yitzchak, and expounded: an individual (*yachid*) who has accepted a personal fast upon himself must still recite the special fast-day *tefillah* — the *Aneinu* — even though this is not a communal fast. The question is where: *bein Goel l'Rofei* — between the blessing of *Goel Yisrael* and the blessing of *Rofei cholei amo Yisrael*.

This is, of course, the familiar practice of the *shliach tzibur* on communal fast days: a standalone *bracha* of *Aneinu* inserted between *Goel* and *Rofei* in the *chazarat hashatz*. It is worth asking why this particular location was chosen. The *Shemoneh Esrei* has a notable structural shift midway through its middle blessings: the earlier ones — *Da'as*, *Slichah*, *Geulah*, *Refuah*, *Birkat Hashanim* — address primarily personal needs, while the later ones — gathering the exiles, loving justice, against informers — shift toward national concerns. The blessing of *Goel Yisrael* belongs in the more personal register: *re'eh b'onyeinu* — see our affliction, and redeem us from it. It is not *Geulah* in the grand national-historical sense, but rather a plea for God to perceive and respond to individual distress. *Aneinu* — whose very name means "answer us" — belongs thematically with this category of appeal. The dominant themes of the *Aneinu* prayer confirm this: *ki b'tzarah gedolah anachnu*, *al tefen el risheinu*, *al tasteir panecha mimenu*, *heyeh na karov leshav'atenu*, *she'atah ha'oneh b'eit tzarah podeh umatzil* — the overwhelming focus is on God's responsiveness and closeness in a moment of distress, not on formal *kapparah* or *slichah*. The prayer would not fit naturally after *Selach* lanu, since the theme of divine forgiveness is not its primary emphasis; it fits after *Goel* precisely because *Geulah* at this level is about God seeing our *tzarah* and answering.

It is also worth observing that the *Aneinu* prayer is formulated in the plural: *aneinu Hashem aneinu*, *ki b'tzarah gedolah anachnu*. This raises a natural question when it is being said by a private individual. The *Gemara* will address this implicitly in the resolution to follow, but the plural formulation is worth noting as an indication that the prayer was originally conceived for a communal context — which is precisely why the question of how a *yachid* uses it deserves examination.

The question of who the *yachid* is in this context is not entirely simple. It might refer to the pious individual who voluntarily takes on a fast as a personal religious act. Or it might refer to the *yechidim* of our *Mishnah* — the more spiritually sensitive individuals who begin fasting ahead of the community in response to a drought. Whether or not this *yachid* is still situationally connected to the communal rain-crisis context, or whether the discussion applies equally to any individual voluntary fast, the *Gemara* does not here resolve.

### Individual Challenge

Rav Yitzchak challenges the proposed placement directly: *vachi yachid koveia brachah le'atzmo*? — can an individual simply institute a new independent blessing for himself? The liturgy is fixed. An individual may insert language into an existing blessing at a designated point, but one does not simply add a new standalone *bracha* to the *Shemoneh Esrei* as a private person. Only a *tzibur* — or more precisely, the rabbinic institution acting on behalf of the community — has the authority to establish a new *bracha* in the liturgy.

### Resolution: Shomea Tefil…

Rav Yitzchak therefore offers a revised ruling: the *yachid* inserts *Aneinu* within *Shomea Tefillah*, the sixteenth blessing of the *Shemoneh Esrei* that serves as a general receptacle for personal petitions. Rav Sheshet concurs: *vekhen amar Rav Sheshet — b'Shomea Tefillah*. This is indeed our practice: in one's private *Shemoneh Esrei* on a fast day, even on *Tisha B'Av*, *Aneinu* is not said as a standalone *bracha* but is inserted within *Shomea Tefillah* without its closing formula (*chatimah*).

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a *braita*: *ein bein yachid litzibur ella shezeh mitpallel shmoneh esrei vezeh mitpallel tisha esrei* — the only difference between a *yachid* and a *tzibur* on a fast day is that this one prays 18 and that one prays 19. If the 19 refers to the *shliach tzibur* adding a standalone *Aneinu bracha*, then the text makes sense. But the *Gemara* notes that this cannot quite be right, because on the later, more serious fast days the *shliach tzibur* recites 24 blessings — not 19. If the *braita* is contrasting 18 with 19, it cannot be talking about the *shliach tzibur* on those later fasts.

The *Gemara* therefore reinterprets the *braita*: the contrast is not between a literally private individual and the *shliach tzibur*, but between two types of individual fasting — a *yachid* who has accepted a lighter *ta'anit yachid* (daytime only, no extra *inuyim*) versus a *yachid* who has accepted a heavier *ta'anit tzibur*-style fast (beginning the night before, with the full range of *inuyim*). The "19" refers to the latter: a private individual keeping the heavy-style fast does say *Aneinu* as an additional blessing — making his *Shemoneh Esrei* 19 rather than 18. This would appear to support the original position of Rav Yehuda, proving that a *yachid* can indeed add a standalone *bracha*.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference, however: *le'olam eima lach shliach tzibur* — it is the *shliach tzibur* after all. As for why 19 rather than 24: on the first three lighter communal fasts, the *shliach tzibur* says *Aneinu* as a standalone *bracha*, bringing the total to 19. The 24-*bracha* liturgy applies only to the final seven, more severe fasts. A *yachid*, under this reading, never makes a standalone *bracha* of *Aneinu* in his private *Shemoneh Esrei* regardless of which type of fast he is keeping.

The *Gemara* then addresses the question of the middle three fasts — do they warrant the 24-*bracha* liturgy of the later fasts, or are they treated like the earlier ones with only 19? A *braita* states there is no difference between the middle three and the last seven except for the *matri'in* (sounding blasts, presumably *shofar*) and the closing of the stores. This implies that the 24 *brachot* apply equally to the middle. The *Gemara* pushes back: the *braita* is an incomplete list, since it also omits the bringing of the *aron* into the street — which is clearly reserved for the last seven. One resolution proposed is that things done publicly (*beferahesya*) were omitted while things done indoors (*betzin'a*) were included — though this is admittedly a somewhat strained distinction, since *shofar* blasts and communal prayer are not exactly private. The stronger resolution offered later is that the *aron* is described in detail in a later *perek* (chapter) specifically as a feature of the final seven fasts, and the same is true of the 24-*bracha* liturgy — both are mentioned in that later *perek* as distinctive to the final seven. Therefore neither should be inferred from the equation made here between the middle three and the last seven.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is that the 24-*bracha* liturgy belongs exclusively to the final seven fasts, where it appears together with the *matri'in* and the bringing out of the *aron* — all of which represent a qualitative escalation in the communal response. The final ruling on the placement of *Aneinu* is stated by Rav Shmuel bar Sasartai and confirmed by Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *bein Goel l'Rofei* — for the *shliach tzibur*. And Rav Ashi in the name of Rav Yannai the son of Rav Yishmael rules: *b'Shomea Tefillah*. The *halacha* follows: *v'hilchata b'Shomea Tefillah*. In one's private *Shemoneh Esrei*, whether on a *ta'anit yachid* or a *ta'anit tzibur*, one inserts *Aneinu* in *Shomea Tefillah*. In the *chazarat hashatz* of the *shliach tzibur*, for the lighter communal fasts, it appears as a standalone *bracha* between *Goel* and *Rofei*, bringing the total to 19. For the final seven, it becomes part of the full 24-*bracha* liturgy.

## Pregnant Women Fasting

### Braita 1

The *Gemara* pivots once more, now to the question of pregnant and nursing women (*ubarot umeinikos*) and their obligation to fast on the communal fast days for rain. The first *braita* states: pregnant and nursing women fast during the early fasts (*rishonot*) but not during the later fasts (*acharonot*). The reasoning seems to be that by the time the later and more extended fasts arrive, the burden would be too great for women in those conditions to bear.

### Braita 2

A second *braita* contradicts this entirely: pregnant and nursing women fast during the later fasts (*acharonot*) but not during the early fasts (*rishonot*). By this view, the early fasts are too light to impose on these women — they are not yet serious enough to demand their participation — while the gravity of the later fasts brings them within its scope.

### Braita 3

A third *braita* contradicts both: pregnant and nursing women do not fast during the early fasts or the later fasts — *lo b'rishonot velo b'acharonot*. All three *beraitot* thus appear to be in direct contradiction: one says A and not B; one says B and not A; one says neither A nor B.

### Rav Ashi's Solution

Rav Ashi resolves all three with a single elegant observation: *nekut amtzeita biyadech d'metartzan kulhu* — take hold of the middle fasts and everything fits. His insight is that the terms *rishonot* (early/first) and *acharonot* (later/last) are relative, not absolute. Using the three-stage structure of the fast calendar — early, middle, and late — all three *beraitot* can be reconciled without changing any text.

The middle fasts are the ones where pregnant and nursing women do fast: heavy enough to be meaningful and obligating, but not so protracted as to become unbearable. With this as the reference point, the *braita* that says they fast during the *rishonot* and not the *acharonot* is using *rishonot* to mean the earlier of two groups — here, the middle fasts in relation to the final seven. The *braita* that says they fast during the *acharonot* and not the *rishonot* is using *acharonot* to mean the later of two groups — again the middle fasts, this time in relation to the initial three. And the *braita* that says they do not fast in either *rishonot* or *acharonot* means literally the first three and the last seven — leaving the middle as the window during which they do fast. The apparent contradictions dissolve entirely once one recognizes that first and last are relative terms pointing toward the same middle ground.

It should be noted that there is a tradition associated with the *Rambam* that pregnant and nursing women fast only during the final seven fasts — a position that appears to run against the *Gemara*'s conclusion here. One possible resolution is that the *Rambam*'s position takes *amtzeita* to mean not the middle set of fasts, but rather the middle of the sequence in some other sense — though this remains a matter requiring further analysis.
