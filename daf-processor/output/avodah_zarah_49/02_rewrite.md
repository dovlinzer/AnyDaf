# Avodah Zarah 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zeh ve-Zeh Gorem Debate

### Intro: Tree Fertilizer

The daf under discussion is 49 of Avodah Zarah, and the governing issue carries over from earlier discussion. The *mishnah* had addressed the prohibition of deriving benefit from an *asherah* tree — a tree worshipped as *avodah zarah*. One of the cases raised is planting beneath such a tree during winter, when its shade provides no benefit, but the droppings from the tree may serve as fertilizer. Rabbi Yosef held this to be problematic; the *chakhamim* did not. The question driving the *gemara* is what exactly underlies this dispute.

The framework that emerges is the principle of *zeh ve-zeh gorem*: when two contributing factors together bring about an effect, is one considered to have derived forbidden benefit from the prohibited element? Multiple things contribute to a plant's growth — sunlight, water, nutrients from the soil — but *Tosafot* makes it clear that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* applies specifically when both factors are contributing equally to one particular mechanism of growth. In our case, both the nutrients from the ground and those from the tree are contributing to the plant's growth. The question is whether that constitutes forbidden benefit from the *avodah zarah*, given that it was not exclusively coming from the tree.

### Offset Benefit Theory

The *gemara's* initial theory is that in fact everyone holds *zeh ve-zeh gorem* to be a problem — that is, both Rabbi Yosef and the *chakhamim* agree that when a forbidden and a permitted source contribute equally to some benefit, that benefit is nonetheless forbidden. According to this reading, the reason the *chakhamim* do not prohibit planting beneath the tree in winter is not that they disagree about *zeh ve-zeh gorem* as a principle, but rather that they take a macro view of the situation: the benefit derived from the tree's fertilizing droppings is offset by the detriment caused by the tree's shade. Looking at the net result, there is no real benefit. In principle, therefore, everyone still agrees that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is problematic; the *chakhamim* permit planting in winter simply because the benefit is neutralized.

### Contradiction in R. Yosef

This theory, however, runs into difficulty. The *gemara* notes that from another source, Rabbi Yosef appears to hold that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is actually *mutar*. That source involves the case of grafting a branch from an *orlah* tree: Rabbi Yosef had ruled that if one grafted such a branch — where both the *orlah* branch and the host tree contribute directly to the growth of the resulting fruit, making it a classic case of *zeh ve-zeh gorem* — the fruit is nonetheless permitted *bedi'avad*. This ruling indicates that Rabbi Yosef considers *zeh ve-zeh gorem* permissible, which contradicts the *gemara's* current theory that everyone holds it to be forbidden.

A separate source involving grinding up an idol and scattering it, where Rabbi Yosef was not concerned that the dust might contribute to a plant's growth, pointed in the same direction. The *gemara* therefore finds itself unable to maintain its original position that all *amoraim* and *tanna'im* agree *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is forbidden.

### Dual Braita Cases

To move the analysis forward, the *gemara* turns to two *baraita* cases. The first involves a field fertilized with dung of *avodah kokhavim* — whether that means dung produced by an animal worshipped as *avodah zarah*, or some other form of offering associated with idol worship need not be resolved. The second involves a cow fattened with some type of forbidden fodder connected to *avodah zarah* — *kashinim*, some grain-like feed. In both cases, the field or the animal has been improved by forbidden input alongside permitted input — a classic *zeh ve-zeh gorem* scenario.

One *baraita* rules that the field may be planted and the cow may be slaughtered — presumably reflecting the lenient position that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* presents no problem. A second *baraita* takes the opposite view. The *gemara* therefore concludes that this indeed is a debate about *zeh ve-zeh gorem* — specifically, in a case of *avodah zarah* — and the question is which *tanna'im* stand on which side.

### R. Yosef vs Chakhamim

The *gemara* proposes that this debate is between Rabbi Yosef and the *chakhamim*: Rabbi Yosef holds *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is permissible, which explains his rulings in the *orlah* case and the grinding case, while the *chakhamim* hold it is forbidden. This would also explain the dispute in our *mishnah* about the *asherah* tree in winter. The one remaining difficulty is why Rabbi Yosef appears to take the strict position in that very *mishnah*, saying the vegetables in winter are forbidden. But the *gemara* will return to resolve that.

### R. Eliezer Alternative

The *gemara* initially resists this reading and attempts to maintain that everyone agrees *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is forbidden, suggesting instead that the debate in the *baraita* about the field and the cow belongs not to Rabbi Yosef and the *chakhamim*, but to Rabbi Eliezer and *Rabbanan*. The proposal is that there is a known debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* that concerns *zeh ve-zeh gorem* — and the *gemara* now turns to identify precisely where that debate appears.

## Sourdough (Se'or) Case

### Sourdough Mishnah

The first candidate for a clear *tannaitic* debate over *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is the case of *se'or* — sourdough starter — and this case is particularly timely given its relevance to *Pesach*. As a preliminary note, *zeh ve-zeh gorem* has practical implications for *Pesach* in other contexts as well, including the common practice of purchasing milk and eggs before *Pesach* begins, due to concerns that animals consuming grain on *Pesach* might render the resulting dairy or eggs problematic — a kind of *zeh ve-zeh gorem* concern, though the practical turnover time makes its plausibility uncertain.

The *mishnah* presents the following scenario: there is sourdough of *chulin* — ordinary, permitted bread — and sourdough of *terumah*, which is the priestly portion and subject to restrictions. Both have fallen into a batch of dough. Crucially, neither portion alone contains enough to cause fermentation; only together, combined, do they have sufficient quantity to ferment the dough. This is therefore a textbook case of *zeh ve-zeh gorem* — both are necessary, each alone insufficient, and they work in concert.

### R. Eliezer: Last One

Rabbi Eliezer's position is: *achar ha'achrona ani ba* — I follow the last one. If the sourdoughs fell in sequentially, one after the other, the critical question is which one caused the fermentation. His reasoning is that the first sourdough, having fallen in without being able to cause fermentation on its own, was effectively *batel* — nullified — by the time the second one arrived. Once nullified, it is past history. The second one is therefore the operative cause, and the status of the bread depends entirely on whether that last addition was the *issur* or the *heter*. If the *terumah* fell first and the *chulin* completed the fermentation, the bread is permitted; if the *terumah* fell last, it is forbidden — because the decisive act was performed by the *issur*.

This reasoning is intuitive: the concept of *makeh b'patish*, the final decisive blow that completes an act, captures the sense that causation attaches to the last contributing element. What is past is past.

### Chakhamim: Joint Action

The *chakhamim* take a fundamentally different view: *bein shenafal issur l'chatchilah bein shenafal issur l'vasof* — whether the *issur* fell first or last, it is not forbidden unless it alone contained sufficient quantity to cause fermentation. The *chakhamim* step back from the sequential narrative and look at the situation holistically. If neither sourdough alone was capable of fermenting the dough, then one cannot say the *issur* "caused" the fermentation — it was jointly caused. Since it is *zeh ve-zeh gorem*, neither is the definitive cause, and therefore the fermentation is not attributed to the *issur*, and the dough remains permitted. The *chakhamim* thus clearly hold *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is *mutar*.

### Abaye's Reading

Abaye steps in to clarify what Rabbi Eliezer actually holds in the case where both sourdoughs are present simultaneously. His reading is restrictive: *lo shanu ela shekadam v'silek et ha'issur* — Rabbi Eliezer's leniency applies only when the *issur* fell in first and was subsequently removed before the *heter* fell in. In that case, the *issur* is genuinely past history — it was removed — and the *heter* completed the fermentation alone. That is why Rabbi Eliezer permits it.

But if the *issur* was not removed — if both are still present when fermentation occurs — Abaye says Rabbi Eliezer would consider it forbidden. His reasoning is that in such a case, both are operating jointly at the moment of fermentation. You cannot ignore the *issur* as "past history" if it is still physically there and actively participating. According to Abaye, then, Rabbi Eliezer holds *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is *assur* — he would forbid it whenever both are simultaneously present and contributing. The debate with the *chakhamim* is precisely that: the *chakhamim* say *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is *mutar*; Rabbi Eliezer says it is *assur*.

### Counter-Interpretation

The *gemara* does not accept that Abaye's reading is definitive. It challenges: who says we must read Rabbi Eliezer the way Abaye does? Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer also holds *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is *mutar*, and his ruling about following "the last one" has nothing to do with simultaneous contribution. On this alternative reading, if both sourdoughs fell simultaneously, Rabbi Eliezer would agree with the *chakhamim* and permit it. His only strictness is in the sequential case: when one fell after the other, he focuses on the last one as the operative cause, without looking at the joint picture. If the *issur* came last, it is forbidden on that basis — not because *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is generally a problem, but because in sequential cases he treats the last as solely responsible.

On this reading, *igmar b'issura assura, v'igmar b'hetera muttarin* — whether you removed the first or not — if the *heter* completed it, it is permitted; if the *issur* completed it, it is forbidden. The leniency or strictness follows only from the identity of the last element, not from any principle about simultaneous contribution.

### Limitation to Terumah

The *gemara* acknowledges that this entire discussion is a case of *terumah*, not *avodah zarah*. The prohibition in play is about *bittul* — whether fermentation caused jointly prevents nullification — not directly about deriving benefit from a forbidden object. There is thus a question whether conclusions drawn from the *se'or* case translate cleanly to the very different context of *avodah zarah*, where perhaps one must be stricter. The *gemara* therefore sets this case aside and turns to a more apposite source.

## Wood & Oven Case

### Oven Firing Scenario

The *gemara* identifies a more fitting case: Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* as they appear in the next *mishnah*, which deals with wood from an *asherah* tree used to fire an oven. The *mishnah* reads: *natal mimena etzim* — you took wood from an *asherah* tree; *assura behana'ah* — it is forbidden to derive benefit from it. *Hisik bah et hatannur* — you went ahead and used this wood to fire an oven. This case involves a specific aggravating feature: firing a new oven is not merely cooking over a fire, it is a structural act. The heat from the initial firing hardens and strengthens the oven walls, creating a permanent physical improvement. From this point on, every time the oven is used, the benefit of its structural integrity is indirectly traceable to that initial *asherah* wood.

This is why the case raises a *zeh ve-zeh gorem* problem of a particularly enduring kind. Even a week later, when one bakes bread in the oven using ordinary permitted wood, the bread is being cooked partly by the current fire and partly by the heat stored and released by the oven walls — walls that owe their strength to the *asherah* wood. In a traditional brick oven, this is not merely theoretical: the bread is cooked primarily by the radiant heat the bricks absorbed from the fire and then release, not directly by the flame itself. So the oven structure itself is a genuine contributor to the bread's cooking, and that structure was formed through the *asherah* wood.

### New vs. Old Oven

The *mishnah* draws a distinction: *chadash yutatz, yashan yutzan* — if it is a new oven, it must be broken, because the *asherah* wood left a permanent strengthening and the oven cannot be remediated merely by cooling. If it is an old oven — one already fully formed — then the wood merely contributed heat, not structural improvement, and one need only let the oven cool off; after that, no further benefit from the *asherah* wood accrues.

In either scenario, however, a *zeh ve-zeh gorem* problem exists once bread is baked: in the old oven scenario, the *asherah* wood is the forbidden source of the current heat, while the preexisting oven structure is the permitted contributor; in the new oven scenario (where the oven was not properly broken), the opposite pairing holds — the current permitted fire contributes heat, but the oven's very existence is the product of the initial forbidden firing.

### Bread Prohibition

Given this *zeh ve-zeh gorem* structure, the *mishnah* rules: *afa bo et hapas assura behana'ah* — if you baked bread in this oven, you cannot derive benefit from the bread. The *mishnah* thus assumes that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is forbidden in the context of *avodah zarah*. This is also a *chiddush* in a different dimension: one might have thought that using the forbidden wood to fire the oven already exhausted the transgression — I used it, I should not have, but now it is done. The ruling rejects this: even the indirect benefit of eating bread baked in an oven whose strength derives from *asherah* wood remains forbidden, even though the wood itself no longer exists.

### Mixed Bread Rule

The *mishnah* continues: *nitarvah ba'acherim* — if this loaf of bread became mixed with other loaves, *kullan asurot behana'ah* — all of them are forbidden. The reason is that by *avodah zarah*, we do not apply *bittul*. In ordinary circumstances, a forbidden object mixed into a permitted majority may be nullified. But the principle that one does not perform *bittul* for *avodah zarah* means the entire mixture is forbidden.

### R. Eliezer's Leniency

Rabbi Eliezer offers a partial remedy: *yolich hana'ah l'yam hamelach* — take the value of the benefit and cast it into the sea. His argument is that in principle, this loaf really should be *batel* in the mixture; we are simply strict and do not allow *bittul* for *avodah zarah*. If the practical concern is that one must not net any benefit from the forbidden item, then throwing out money equivalent to the loaf's value eliminates the net benefit. One has effectively not profited from the *avodah zarah*, and on that basis the remaining loaves may be used.

It should be noted that Rabbi Eliezer is not permitting the original identified loaf — if one knows which loaf it is, it must be discarded. The *pidyon* mechanism applies only to the situation where the loaf cannot be identified within the mixture.

### Chakhamim Reject

The *chakhamim* reject this entirely: *ein pidyon l'avodah zarah* — there is no redemption for *avodah zarah*. Their objection is that *pidyon* is a concept applicable to *hekdesh*, sacred objects that can have their sanctity transferred to money. *Avodah zarah* is not *hekdesh*, and its prohibition cannot be redeemed by a monetary substitution. More fundamentally, the *chakhamim* insist that when one actually eats the bread, one is actually deriving benefit from the bread at that moment — the fact that a dollar was thrown into the sea does not change the reality of that benefit.

## Final Resolution

### R. Yosef Critique

The *gemara* now reassembles all the evidence and offers its final framework. The key move is to return to the original suggestion — that the debate is between Rabbi Yosef and the *chakhamim* — and to explain the apparent inconsistency in Rabbi Yosef's positions. Recall that Rabbi Yosef appeared to say *assur* in one place and *mutar* in another regarding *zeh ve-zeh gorem* by *avodah zarah*. The *gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi Yosi l'divreicham d'Rabbanan ka'amar lehu* — when Rabbi Yosef argued for the strict position, he was not expressing his own view. He was arguing *ad hominem*, critiquing the *chakhamim* on the basis of their own stated principles.

Rabbi Yosef's actual position is: *l'didi zeh ve-zeh gorem mutar* — as far as I am concerned, *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is permissible, even in *avodah zarah*. That is why he ruled leniently in the *orlah* grafting case, leniently in the grinding and scattering case, and leniently regarding the *asherah* droppings. When he appeared to take a strict position regarding the vegetables in winter, he was saying: *I* think it is permitted, but *you* — the *chakhamim* — who hold *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is forbidden should logically be forbidding this case as well. It was a rhetorical challenge to their consistency, not an expression of his own ruling.

### Rabbi Yosef Lenient

With this framework, everything aligns. Rabbi Yosef holds *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is *mutar*, which explains all his lenient rulings. The *chakhamim* hold it is *assur*, which explains why they are strict in other contexts. The *mishnah* about the *asherah* droppings is now explained as Rabbi Yosef pressing the *chakhamim* on their own principles, not adopting their view. And the *chakhamim* respond, as Rav Mari in the name of Rav Kahana explained, that they are not required to be strict about the winter case because the shade of the tree offsets the benefit of the fertilizer — making it, from their perspective, a case where even by their own principles there is no net forbidden benefit.

### Halacha: R. Yosef

The *gemara* delivers the decisive ruling: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — we rule like Rabbi Yosef, that *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is permissible even in the context of *avodah zarah*. This is a striking ruling given how much of the preceding discussion seemed to point in the opposite direction, with the *mishnah* about the oven and the bread assuming *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is forbidden. Nevertheless, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosef's lenient position.

### Fertilized Field

A practical case immediately follows. There was a certain garden — *hahu ginnasa d'izdabal b'zivla d'avodat kochavim* — a field fertilized with dung of *avodah zarah*, precisely the scenario debated throughout this section. Rav Ammi sent the question to Rav Yosef: may the field be used? The exact stage of the question is not entirely clear — whether it concerned planting the field, using what has already grown, or both; there are meaningful distinctions at different stages of the process. But Rav Yosef replied with the ruling just established: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — we rule like Rabbi Yosei; *zeh ve-zeh gorem* is permissible even in *avodah zarah*, and therefore the field may be used.

## Weaver's Pick Case

### Pick Mishnah

The *mishnah* now turns to a parallel scenario: *natal mimena karkur* — you took wood from an *asherah* tree and fashioned from it a weaver's pick. *Assurin behana'ah* — the pick is forbidden for benefit. *Arag bo et habeged* — you then used this pick to weave a garment. *Assur behana'ah* — the garment is forbidden for benefit.

### Garment Prohibition

This case carries a double *chiddush*. First, the garment is now forbidden not because it is the *asherah* wood itself, but because benefit was derived from the forbidden pick in producing it. The one wearing the garment is indirectly deriving benefit from the pick, and that pick traces back to the *asherah* tree. This is a very attenuated chain of causation. One might have argued that using the pick to weave the garment already exhausted the transgression — I used the forbidden pick, I should not have, but having done so, the garment is now just a garment. The *mishnah* rejects this: even the indirect benefit of wearing a garment woven by a forbidden pick is prohibited. The forbidden object need not still exist; the chain of benefit runs through it nonetheless.

### Achar Ba'Achar

The *mishnah* then extends further: *nitarev ba'acherim, v'acherim ba'acherim* — if this garment became mixed with other garments, and then those garments became mixed with yet other garments, *kullan asurim behana'ah* — all are forbidden. This is *achar ba'achar*: one degree of removal mixed with another degree of removal. The strictness is remarkable. Consider the distance from the original *asherah* wood: the wood became a pick, the pick wove a garment, that garment mixed into a pile, and one garment from that pile mixed into yet another pile. Even at that second remove, the ruling is that everything is forbidden. The ראשונים debate exactly how to read *acherim ba'acherim* — whether it means a single garment from the first mixture entered a second mixture, or the entire first mixture entered a second — but in any reading, we are speaking of a striking degree of remoteness from the original forbidden object. This reflects the unusual severity of *avodah zarah* prohibitions: we do not apply *bittul*, and the chain of *hana'ah* extends further than one might expect.

### R. Eliezer Leniency

Rabbi Eliezer applies the same remedy as before: *yolich hana'ah l'yam hamelach* — in the case where the garment is unknown among many, take the monetary value of the benefit and cast it into the sea. The *chakhamim* again reject this: *ein pidyon l'avodah zarah*.

### Why Both Cases

The *gemara* explains why both cases — the bread and the garment — needed to be stated, as each addresses a conceptually distinct scenario. In the bread case, the forbidden object — the *asherah* wood — was destroyed in the process of baking the bread; it no longer exists when the bread is eaten. The benefit is therefore maximally indirect: the firewood is gone, yet we say its contribution to the baking creates a problem. One might have thought that since the forbidden object was destroyed, Rabbi Eliezer's mechanism of paying off the value is particularly compelling — there is something almost fictitious about the continuing prohibition when the source no longer exists, so flushing the dollar seems reasonable.

In the pick case, by contrast, the forbidden object — the pick — still exists when the garment is being worn. The connection to the *asherah* is more concrete and immediate. One might therefore have thought Rabbi Eliezer would not extend his leniency here, since there is still something tangible to point to as the ongoing source of forbidden benefit. Each case thus teaches something the other would not: you need both to establish the full scope of Rabbi Eliezer's leniency and the *chakhamim's* rejection of it.

## Indirect Benefit Debate

### Bread vs. Wine

Rav Ada bar Ahava introduces an important distinction. The ruling that one may perform *pidyon* — the monetary remedy of casting the benefit's value into the sea — applies only to bread: *lo shanu ella pat*. The bread, as emphasized, is not the forbidden object itself. It is a secondary product: something produced or affected by the forbidden item, not the forbidden item per se. Since the *assur* element is indirect — the bread is merely the product of baking in an oven fired with or strengthened by *asherah* wood — the *pidyon* mechanism has some foothold. We are addressing indirect benefit, and one can argue that throwing out the monetary equivalent neutralizes the net gain.

But *aval chavit* — a barrel of *yayin nesech*, forbidden wine — presents a fundamentally different case. There the barrel itself is the forbidden object. When it gets mixed with other barrels of wine, the mixture contains the actual *issur*, not merely a product of it. In that case, Rav Ada bar Ahava holds, the *pidyon* mechanism does not apply — one cannot simply throw money into the sea and then benefit from the entire mixture.

### Halacha: R. Eliezer

The *halacha* is ruled like Rabbi Eliezer: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer* — in the case of the garment/pick and analogous situations involving indirect benefit, the *pidyon* mechanism is valid. If the forbidden item became mixed into a larger group, one may cast the value of the forbidden item's benefit into the sea and then use the mixture. This ruling is transmitted through multiple authorities: Rabbi Hiyya the son of Rabbah bar Nachmani in the name of Rav Chisda in the name of Zeiri.

### Wine Barrel Case

A practical case: a certain man had a barrel of *yayin nesech* that became mixed into his other wine. He came before Rav Chisda with the question. Rav Chisda told him: *shkol arba zuzei ve-shadei be-nahara ve-nishtrei lach* — take four *zuz*, throw them into the river, and it will be permitted to you. Two features of this ruling are striking. First, Rav Chisda is applying the *pidyon* principle even to *chavit* — to the forbidden object itself, not merely to indirect benefit — apparently disagreeing with Rav Ada bar Ahava's distinction. Second, and perhaps more surprising, the four *zuz* presumably do not represent the full market value of a barrel of wine. There is therefore an additional *chiddush*: the payment need not be a precise monetary equivalent; a more nominal sum suffices as the formal act of "not benefiting."

The practical context of Rav Chisda's ruling is also clarified: this applies to a case where one is not drinking the wine directly — one might be feeding it to non-Jewish workers or deriving some other non-drinking benefit. This limitation connects to the next point.

### Rashi on Benefit

Rashi clarifies a critical conceptual distinction that the discussion might otherwise obscure. The *pidyon* mechanism — throwing money into the sea — operates on the *hana'ah* plane, the plane of benefit. The concern being addressed is: did one net a financial or practical benefit from the *avodah zarah*? If money was thrown out equivalent to (or symbolically representing) that benefit, one can argue the net gain is zero, and the benefit prohibition is satisfied.

But this operates entirely separately from the eating prohibition. Even if the *pidyon* successfully neutralizes the *hana'ah* concern, it does not permit one to eat something that is forbidden to eat. These are two distinct prohibitions with distinct standards. The money thrown into the sea solves the problem on the benefit dimension — one's bank account has not improved as a result of the *avodah zarah* — but it does nothing for the eating dimension. Rashi makes this explicit in his commentary: do not conflate the two. So in the wine case, *pidyon* may allow one to derive benefit from the barrel in a non-drinking context, but one could not proceed to drink the wine on the grounds that the *pidyon* was performed.

### Discount Sale Method

An alternative mechanism is available that avoids the debate entirely. If one takes all one hundred and one barrels — one hundred *heter* and one *yayin nesech* — and sells the entire lot to a non-Jew at a discount, charging only for the one hundred permitted barrels and effectively giving the forbidden barrel for free, this is universally permitted. The reasoning is that if one did not charge for the forbidden barrel, one received no benefit from it; the price received corresponds entirely to the *heter* barrels. There was no *hana'ah* in the first place — not because it was redeemed after the fact, but because one never converted the forbidden item into money at all. This approach, Rashi notes, is uncontroversial and does not depend on the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chakhamim*.

## Bittul of Idols

### Personal Use Bittul

The *mishnah* now addresses the mechanics of *bittul* — nullification — of *avodah zarah*, specifically the *asherah* tree. Only a non-Jew can perform *bittul* on *avodah zarah*; what acts constitute *bittul*? The *mishnah* specifies: *kirsam ve-zireid* — if a non-Jew broke off some kindling from the tree, or *natal mimenu makel* — took a branch to use as a walking stick, or even *afilu ala* — took a single leaf. *Harei ze batla* — this constitutes *bittul* of the tree. The criterion is that the non-Jew is taking something from the tree for his own personal use. That act demonstrates he does not treat the tree as a deity; he is using it instrumentally, for himself. A non-Jew who worshipped the tree can also nullify it, since the capacity for *bittul* rests with non-Jews alone.

*Shifa letzorkah* — if, however, the non-Jew shaved or pruned the tree for the sake of the tree itself — to improve its health, to help it grow better — *asurah*: this is not *bittul*. Two conditions are required: a physical removal from the tree, and the act being done for the non-Jew's own benefit. If done for the tree's benefit, the first condition may be met but the second is not, and it therefore fails as an act of *bittul*. *Shelo letzorkah* — if not for the tree's sake — *muteret*, it is permitted, because the non-Jew is again taking it for his own purposes.

### Shaving Debate

The *gemara* raises the question: *otan sheva'in mah tehei aleihen* — what is the status of the shavings themselves? If the non-Jew shaved the tree for its own benefit — so the tree itself remains forbidden — are the shavings that were removed also forbidden? Or might the act of separation itself constitute an implicit *bittul* at least on the shavings, even if not on the tree?

A debate is recorded: *chad amar assurim* — one opinion holds the shavings remain forbidden; *ve-chad amar mutarim* — the other holds they are permitted. The *gemara* reports that the *baraita* aligns with the lenient view.

### Baraita Resolution

The *baraita* provides a clear ruling: *oved kochavim she-shifa avodat kochavim* — a non-Jew who shaved an idol — *letzorko*, for his own purposes: *the idol and the shavings are both permitted*, because this was a full act of *bittul*. *Letzorkah*, for the sake of the idol: *hi asurah* — the idol remains forbidden — *ve-shva'eha mutarin* — but the shavings are permitted. The logic is that even when the primary act of *bittul* does not extend to the whole tree, the removal of the shavings from the tree constitutes an implicit *bittul* at least on those shavings: they have been separated, and the non-Jew's act — whatever its intent regarding the tree — at minimum disengages those particular pieces from the idol's status.

### Jew Cannot Nullify

*Yisrael she-shifa avodat kochavim* — a Jew who shaved the idol — *bein letzorkah bein letzorko* — whether for the idol's sake or for his own sake — *hi ve-shva'eha assurim* — both the idol and the shavings remain forbidden. A Jew has no capacity to perform *bittul* on *avodah zarah* at all. The act of *bittul* is exclusively within the domain of the non-Jew, as it is the non-Jew's attitude toward the idol that gives it its status. When a Jew interacts with the idol, regardless of purpose, no change of status occurs. Everything remains as it was.

## Broken Idol Debate

### Rav: Each Splinter

The *gemara* now takes up the case of an idol that broke — *avodat kochavim she-nishtabra* — whether by falling on its own or being broken by external force. Does the breaking itself constitute a kind of automatic *bittul*, given that the idol is no longer in its recognizable form? Or does each piece of the broken idol retain its forbidden status and require an explicit, conscious act of *bittul* by a non-Jew?

Rav's position is the strict one: *tzarich levatel kol kisam ve-kisam* — a non-Jew must perform *bittul* on every single splinter and chip. The broken state does not automatically nullify; each piece of the original idol carries the prohibition forward and individually requires a non-Jew's conscious act of renunciation.

### Shmuel: Natural State

Shmuel's position initially appears to say the opposite — but in an unexpected direction. As first presented, it sounds as though *bittul* can only occur when the idol is in its natural state, which would make it harder to achieve *bittul* on a broken idol, not easier. The *gemara* therefore immediately objects: *adaraba* — this is backwards. If the idol is intact and growing normally, how does one perform *bittul*? It is in its full divine status; that is precisely when *bittul* is most needed and most consequential.

The *gemara* therefore amends Shmuel's statement: *ein avodat kochavim tzricha levatel ella derech gedilasa* — an idol requires *bittul* only when it is in its natural, intact state. Once an idol has broken and is no longer in its natural form, no conscious act of *bittul* is required; its broken state automatically removes the prohibition. The rationale is that an idol in a broken or shattered state is not something non-Jews would worship; it no longer holds the status of a deity in practice.

### Worship Potential

The *gemara* considers whether the debate between Rav and Shmuel hinges on an empirical question: *d'mar savar ovdin lishvarim u-mar savar ein ovdin lishvarim* — one holds that non-Jews continue to worship the broken pieces of their idols, the other holds they do not. If the broken pieces are still objects of worship, they retain their forbidden status and require *bittul*; if no one worships them in that state, perhaps they automatically shed their status.

The *gemara* suggests this framing may be inaccurate, however, because the debate may actually concern something more specific.

### Splinters Debate

The *gemara* reframes: perhaps everyone agrees that non-Jews would still worship large broken pieces — *ovdin lishvarim*. The debate concerns *shivrei shevarim* — the chips and dust that broke off from the broken pieces. These tiny fragments are so far removed from the original idol that even if the broken idol retains its worship-potential, these infinitesimal remnants may not. *Mar savar shivrei shevarim asurim* — one holds even these remain forbidden; *u-mar savar shivrei shevarim mutarim* — the other holds they are permitted.

Alternatively, the *gemara* offers another reading: perhaps everyone agrees that *shivrei shevarim* — true tiny chips — are permitted. The debate concerns a different case: an idol made of parts that can be assembled and disassembled — one that fell apart into many pieces, but where even a non-expert (*hedyot*) could reassemble them. One position holds: *kivan d'hedyot yachol lehachzira lo batila* — since a layperson could put it back together, it has not genuinely lost its identity as an idol; the pieces remain forbidden even though they look like *shivrei shevarim*. The idol is notionally still whole, just temporarily in pieces — like components that could be snapped back together.

### Natural State Rule

The opposing position formulates the rule: *ein avodat kochavim batila ela derech gedilasa* — an idol only requires *bittul*, and only retains the need for *bittul*, when it is in its natural state. *Ha lav gedilasa hi ve-ein tzarich levatel* — once it is no longer in its natural state, even if the pieces could theoretically be reassembled, since it is not presently in its natural, intact form, no *bittul* is required; it is automatically considered nullified.

This debate touches on a deeper question: is *bittul* fundamentally a conscious act — something that changes the legal status of the idol by a deliberate act of renunciation by a non-Jew — or is it a response to reality? If *bittul* is about changing status through conscious act, then a broken idol needs a non-Jew to consciously renounce each piece. But if *bittul* is really a recognition that the object is no longer in a state anyone would worship, then reality itself can accomplish what the conscious act otherwise would — and an idol that is not in its natural, worshipped state is automatically *batel*, without any non-Jew needing to do a thing.

*Hadran alach kol hatzelamim.*
