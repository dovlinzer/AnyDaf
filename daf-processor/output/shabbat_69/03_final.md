# Shabbat 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tinok Shenishba

### Munbaz Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹנְבַּז פּוֹטֵר. וְכָךְ הָיָה מוֹנְבַּז דָּן לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הוֹאִיל וּמֵזִיד קָרוּי ״חוֹטֵא״ וְשׁוֹגֵג קָרוּי ״חוֹטֵא״, מָה מֵזִיד שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה — אַף שׁוֹגֵג שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֲרֵינִי מוֹסִיף עַל דְּבָרֶיךָ: אִי מָה מֵזִיד שֶׁהָיְתָה הַיְּדִיעָה בִּשְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה — אַף שׁוֹגֵג שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה בִּשְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה! אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁהוֹסַפְתָּ. אָמַר לוֹ: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, אֵין זֶה קָרוּי שׁוֹגֵג אֶלָּא מֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Munbaz deems him exempt.** And Munbaz deliberated before Rabbi Akiva as follows: **Since one who commits a transgression intentionally is called a sinner and one who commits a transgression unwittingly is called a sinner, just as one who commits the transgression intentionally is liable for punishment only in a case where he had prior knowledge that it was prohibited, so too, one who commits the transgression unwittingly is liable to bring a sin-offering only in a case where he had prior knowledge.** However, the action of one who had no prior knowledge at all is not considered unwitting; rather, it has the same legal status as an action performed due to circumstances beyond one's control, and he is completely exempt. **Rabbi Akiva said to him: I will elaborate upon your statement** and follow your reasoning to its logical conclusion and thereby test the validity of your reasoning. **If so, just as one who commits the transgression intentionally is liable for punishment only in a case where he had the awareness that he was sinning at the time that he performed the action, so too, with regard to one who commits the transgression unwittingly, say that he is only liable to bring a sin-offering in a case where he had awareness that he was sinning at the time that he performed the action.** If that is the case, it is no longer an unwitting transgression. Munbaz **said to him: Yes,** there is nothing unusual about that. In my opinion it is correct **and all the more so** now that **you have elaborated** upon my statement. Awareness at the time that one is performing the action is one of the criteria of my definition of an unwitting transgression, as will be explained below. Rabbi Akiva **said to him: According to your statement,** since while performing the action one is aware that it is prohibited, his action **is not called unwitting; rather,** it is a full-fledged **intentional** transgression.

The daf begins at the *ela Munbaz* passage, six lines down. To situate the discussion: there is a debate between Abaye, Rava, and Shmuel on one side and Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on the other regarding a *tinok shenishba* — someone who never knew they were Jewish — who violates *Shabbat*. Rava and Shmuel hold that such a person must still bring one *korban* for all violations. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish hold that no *korban* is brought at all, since there is no fault and no guilt. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish's position is, in a sense, the intuitive one — there is simply no culpability. Rava and Shmuel appear to operate from the premise that a *korban* is not exclusively a mechanism of personal guilt but functions to cleanse the very existence of the sin; accordingly, even a faultless actor must bring it. In the end, the *Rambam* rules in accordance with Rava and Shmuel.

This broader debate is paralleled by a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Munbaz regarding the *tinok shenishba*. Rabbi Akiva pressed Munbaz with a *reductio ad absurdum*: Munbaz's position seemed to require that just as the *meizid* — the willful violator — possesses knowledge and awareness, so too the *shogeg* must have had some initial awareness. Rabbi Akiva pushed the argument to its logical extreme and said: if that is your principle, then make it fully like a *meizid* and require awareness even at the moment of committing the sin. And Munbaz simply accepted this, saying: yes, why not?

### Munbaz Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מוֹנְבַּז, שְׁגָגָה בְּמַאי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁשָּׁגַג בְּקׇרְבָּן. וְרַבָּנַן: שִׁגְגַת קׇרְבָּן לֹא שְׁמָהּ שְׁגָגָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, according to Munbaz, who holds that included in the category of an unwitting sinner is one who at the time of action was aware that it was prohibited; if he were fully aware, in what sense was his action unwitting?** The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where he was unwitting with regard to the sacrifice.** He was aware that he was committing a transgression for which one is liable to be punished with *karet* when performed intentionally; however, he was unaware that he would be liable to bring a sin-offering if he performed the transgression unwittingly. Since he was not aware of all punishments and forms of atonement associated with that transgression, he is considered an unwitting sinner and is liable to bring a sin-offering. The Gemara asks: **And what do the Rabbis who disagree with Munbaz hold? They hold: Unwitting with regard to a sacrifice is not considered unwitting.**

This acceptance generates the *Gemara*'s question: *v'leMunbaz shegaga b'mai* — according to Munbaz, where exactly is the error? If Munbaz includes within the obligation of a *korban chatat* even someone who is fully aware of the prohibition at the time of the sin, what constitutes the element of *shegaga* — of mistake — that distinguishes this from an outright *meizid*?

The *Gemara* answers: *kegon sheshagag b'korban* — the error lies in the *korban* itself. He knew he was prohibited; he knew the act was punishable by *karet* or *mitah* in a court with witnesses. But he did not know that this was the type of sin that incurs an obligation to bring a *korban*. And that, according to Munbaz, is sufficient error to classify him as a *shogeg* and obligate him to bring the *korban*.

This is admittedly a somewhat circular position. The only reason he is considered a *shogeg* vis-à-vis the *korban* is precisely because Munbaz rules that such a case counts as *shogeg* and thus obligates a *korban*. Perhaps more charitably, it simply means that he lacked awareness that this category of sin comes with a *korban* attached, and that degree of ignorance — limited as it is — is sufficient for the *korban* obligation. It is a notably extreme and expansive position regarding how much awareness one can possess and still be eligible to bring a *korban*.

### Rabbanan Shogeg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, שְׁגָגָה בְּמַאי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁגַג בְּכָרֵת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֵזִיד בְּלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And in the opinion of the Rabbis, lack of awareness with regard to what aspects of the prohibition renders the action unwitting?** There are two possibilities: either the person must be unaware of the prohibition itself, or the person need only be unaware of the severe punishment (*karet*). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is an unwitting transgression since he was unwitting with regard to the fact that the punishment for his transgression is *karet*, even though he was aware that his action was in violation of a Torah prohibition, and he performed the transgression intentionally.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the mainstream position — the *rabbanan* — and asks: *verabbanan shegaga b'mai*? In an ordinary case where one brings a *korban*, what must the person have been unaware of?

In general, there are two paradigmatic forms of *shegaga*. In the case of, say, eating forbidden fat: either one does not know that *chelev* is prohibited in principle, or one knows the prohibition but is mistaken about the facts — one thought this piece of meat was a steak and did not realize it was *chelev*. There is an error in law and an error in fact; these are the two classic types of *shogeg*. By *Shabbat* there is an analogous structure: one either does not know that this *melacha* is forbidden, or one forgot that today is *Shabbat* — which is analogous to the factual error, like mistaking *chelev* for steak.

But the *Gemara* now probes whether there are additional scenarios of error that still qualify as *shegaga* sufficient to obligate a *korban*. And the *Gemara* is clear that for the *rabbanan*, an error limited to the *korban* alone — *shegagat korban lo shema shegaga* — is not a real error. If one is aware of everything else, aware that the act is prohibited, aware that it is punishable by *karet* or worse, there is ample reason not to commit the act. Being unaware of one minor subsidiary consequence does not render the willful violation anything less than willful.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan's position is striking: *keivan sheshagag b'karet af al pi sheheizid b'lav* — once one has erred regarding the *karet*, even though one was deliberate in violating the *lav*, a *korban* may be brought.

This is a remarkable idea. One can know that what one is doing is biblically prohibited — that it violates a negative commandment — yet if one does not know that it is punishable by *karet*, one may still bring a *korban*. The reasoning, as will emerge, is that a *korban* is only brought for prohibitions punishable by *karet*; there are only thirty-six such sins. If someone knows they are transgressing a *lav* but genuinely believes it is no more severe than an ordinary prohibition — thinking, for example, that eating *chametz* on *Pesach* is no worse than eating *chazir* or *neveilah* — then according to Rabbi Yochanan, such a person brings a *korban*. Not knowing how weighty the sin was, not knowing it was deserving of *karet*, is sufficient. One was intentional in the act, but not with full awareness of its gravity, and that partial ignorance is enough to render the act atone-able and the person eligible for a *korban*.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוֹג בְּלָאו וְכָרֵת. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ — אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה בִּשְׁגָגָה וְאָשֵׁם״ — עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוֹג בְּלָאו וְכָרֵת שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish said that according to the Rabbis it is not considered unwitting until he was unwitting with regard to both the prohibition and *karet*, i.e., he was unaware that his action was prohibited by Torah law. Rava said: What is the reason for the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish? The verse said: "And if one soul shall sin by mistake from the common people, by performing one of God's commandments that may not be done, and he becomes guilty," indicating that it is not considered unwitting until he was unwitting with regard to the prohibition and its concomitant *karet*.**

Reish Lakish says what one would expect from the normative logic of *shogeg*: one must be fully in error. To bring a *korban*, one must think that what one is doing is permitted. One must err both regarding the prohibition itself and regarding the *karet*. If one knows that what one is doing constitutes a violation of a biblical prohibition, one cannot be called a *shogeg* for purposes of the *korban* — regardless of whether one knew about the *karet* or not.

That said, there is a nuance even within Reish Lakish's position. Take *Shabbat*: *Shabbat* carries both a positive commandment — *tishbos*, to rest — and a negative prohibition, *lo ta'aseh melacha*. Someone raised, say, in a non-Jewish household who was taught to think of *Shabbat* as a day of rest — a positive obligation — but never understood it as a punishable prohibition, would know they are supposed to rest but not know they are violating a *lav*. For Reish Lakish, that would still constitute sufficient *shegaga*. Even Reish Lakish does not require one to think the act is completely neutral. What he requires is that one not know it constitutes a violation of a negative prohibition. So long as one lacks awareness of the *lav*, one may still bring a *korban* even according to the stricter view.

The *Gemara* notes, and rules, that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan — a position that is, in its way, quite generous: even someone who knowingly violates a prohibition but is unaware of its gravity is considered enough in error, and enough outside the category of a fully willful sinner, to be entitled to bring a *korban* of atonement.

One might also note that for Reish Lakish, there may be a gap: a person who knows the prohibition but not the *karet* cannot bring a *korban* since Reish Lakish requires full ignorance of the *lav*, yet it is unclear whether such a person would receive *karet* either — since intuitively *karet* picks up where the *korban* ends. The *Gemara* does not explore this question directly.

### Scriptural Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הַאי קְרָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ — פְּרָט לִמְשׁוּמָּד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה בִּשְׁגָגָה וְאָשֵׁם״. הַשָּׁב מִידִיעָתוֹ — מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ, לֹא שָׁב מִידִיעָתוֹ — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Yoḥanan do with that verse** cited as proof **by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish?** The Gemara answers: **He needs it for that which was taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase: **"From the common people"** (Leviticus 4:27) teaches that only some sinners, not all, bring sacrifices for their unwitting sins. It comes **to exclude an apostate.** When an apostate sins unwittingly, he is under no obligation to bring a sin-offering even after he repents. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: This halakha is derived from the phrase in that verse: "That may not be done, and he becomes guilty." One who repents due to his awareness,** i.e., one who repents as soon as he becomes aware that he performed a transgression, **brings a sacrifice for his unwitting transgression. However, one who does not repent due to his awareness** that he sinned, e.g., an apostate who continues to sin even after he becomes aware that he committed a transgression, does not **bring an offering for his unwitting action.**

How does Rabbi Yochanan handle this verse, which seems to anchor the error specifically in the prohibition itself? He needs it for another purpose — for what is taught in a *baraita*: the phrase *me'am ha'aretz*, "from among the people of the land," in the relevant passage comes to exclude a *mumar* — an apostate who has fundamentally rejected the entire system — from bringing a *korban*. Now, one might wonder: obviously a *mumar* cannot bring a *korban*, since they are acting as a *meizid*. But the *Gemara* constructs a case: the *mumar* today happens to eat what he thinks is a steak, not knowing it is *chelev*. Today's act, considered in isolation, was accidental. Even so, since this person is a *mumar* — prepared to sin willfully — even if they later do *teshuva*, they cannot bring a *korban* for that act. The verse *me'am ha'aretz* categorically excludes the *mumar*.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, speaking in the name of Rabbi Shimon, derives the same point differently — not from *me'am ha'aretz* but from the phrase *asher lo te'asena ve'ashem* itself, and specifically from a positive formulation: *hashav mi'yediato mevi korban al shigagaso* — one brings a *korban* only if, had they been made aware, they would have refrained. *Lo shav mi'yediato eino mevi korban al shigagaso* — if even with full knowledge they would have gone ahead and done it, no *korban* is brought. In the *mumar* case, even if the act today was technically accidental, since this person was prepared to sin willfully, we cannot say they would have stopped had they known. Therefore, no *korban*.

The two derivations arrive at a very similar conclusion. One approaches it categorically — the person is labeled a *mumar* and excluded as a type. The other focuses on the specific act — asking whether *this* act is the kind for which, had awareness been present, the person would have desisted. There is a *Gemara* elsewhere that attempts to find a case where these two approaches would diverge, but in practice they converge on the same principle: a *korban* is only brought when the person, had they known, would not have sinned.

### Tosafot Challenge

Tosafot raises the obvious question: once we accept the condition of *shav mi'yediato* — that one would have refrained had one known — we must examine how Rabbi Yochanan satisfies this condition in the case where one *does* know the prohibition but not the *karet*.

Rabbi Yochanan must be saying two things simultaneously. First, not knowing the *karet* is enough to prevent one from being categorized as a fully willful sinner — it is a sufficient degree of error to entitle the person to bring a *korban*. Second — and this is what Tosafot highlights — we must also assume that this same person, had they been told the full severity, would indeed have refrained. That is, even though one was willfully transgressing a *lav*, one must have been doing so with the implicit assumption that it was a relatively minor infraction. Had one known it was deserving of *karet*, one would have stopped. The very fact that one was unaware of the severity provides the basis for saying: yes, this person fits the *shav mi'yediato* criterion, because it was the ignorance of the gravity that made the difference.

This is admittedly demanding as an argument. It is worth noting the further tension that arises with Munbaz's position in relation to *shav mi'yediato*: if the only error is regarding the *korban* itself — one knew it was prohibited, knew it was punishable by *karet*, but simply did not know that a *korban* was also involved — it is genuinely difficult to understand how that satisfies the *shav mi'yediato* standard. Can one seriously argue that awareness of the additional consequence of a *korban* would have made the decisive difference? This is a real question, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it explicitly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אַחַת. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: מִנְיָנָא לְמָה לִי? וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָן כּוּלָּן בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — בִּזְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof from what **we learned** in a mishna: The number of **primary categories of prohibited labors** on Shabbat is **forty-less-one,** which the mishna proceeds to list. **And we discussed** this mishna: **Why do I need this tally of forty-less-one?** Isn't merely listing the prohibited labors sufficient? **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The tally was included to teach **that if he performed all of the prohibited labors in the course of one lapse of awareness during which he was unaware of the prohibition involved, he is liable for each and every one.** Therefore, the mishna indicated that one could conceivably be liable to bring thirty-nine sin-offerings. Under what circumstances **can you find** a case where one would be liable for unwittingly violating all thirty-nine labors? It must be in a case where **with regard to Shabbat** his actions were **intentional,** as he was aware that it was Shabbat; **and** with regard to the prohibited **labors** his actions were **unwitting,** as he was unaware that these labors are prohibited on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* following this passage also addresses the concept of *tinok shenishba* in a broader cultural and halachic context. Beyond the narrow question of whether a *korban* is brought, the idea of a *tinok shenishba* — someone who, due to their upbringing, cannot be held accountable for failing to observe the law — becomes a powerful concept of inclusion. The Rambam used it as a basis for a more forgiving approach toward the Karaites. The *Beit Yosef*, author of the *Shulchan Aruch*, followed suit. And in the post-Enlightenment era, with the vast majority of Jews raised in communities that do not consider themselves obligated in *halacha*, the category provides a framework for understanding diminished culpability. One must note, however, that the concept carries an inherent tension: it is, in a sense, patronizing — it achieves inclusion precisely by declaring that a person cannot be held responsible for their own beliefs. Still, as a concept addressing how far awareness and accountability can be expected to reach, *tinok shenishba* remains among the most consequential ideas in *halachic* thought.

## Karet vs Korban

### Abaye Shvuat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּשְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי, שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוֹג בְּלָאו שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Everyone agrees with regard to an oath on a statement,** a case where one swore to prohibit or to obligate himself to perform an action, **that the halakha is as follows: If he violates his oath he is only liable to bring an offering if he was unwitting with regard to its prohibition,** i.e., he was unaware that it is prohibited by Torah law to violate an oath.

Abaye now introduces a statement about *shvuat bituy* — the *korban* brought for making a false oath. The *Torah* creates a special *korban oleh v'yored* for one who swears falsely: either about the past (swearing one did or did not do something when the reverse is true) or about the future (swearing one will or will not do something and then violating the oath). This is a *korban* that applies even though *shvuat bituy* is not punishable by *karet* — it is a *lav* without *karet*. Abaye states: *hakol modim b'shvuat bituy she'ein chayavin aleha korban ad sheyishgog b'lav shebah* — everyone agrees that for a false oath one is not obligated in a *korban* unless the error was about the *lav* itself. An error only regarding the *karet* — which in any case does not exist here — is insufficient.

### Karet Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל מוֹדִים״, מַאן — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. פְּשִׁיטָא, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא כָּרֵת, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלֵיכָּא כָּרֵת — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** To **whose** opinion is Abaye referring in the phrase: **Everyone agrees? Certainly, it is the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan** with regard to the opinion of the Rabbis in their dispute with Munbaz. Even though Rabbi Yoḥanan generally holds that the fact that one is unwitting with regard to *karet* is sufficient to render his action unwitting, the case of an oath is different. The Gemara asks: In the case of an oath, **it is obvious** that he would agree. **When Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that one need not be unwitting with regard to the prohibition, it is in a case **where there is a prohibition punishable by *karet*; however, here, where there is no punishment of *karet*, Rabbi Yoḥanan would not** say so.

The *Gemara* interjects: *peshita* — isn't this obvious? Of course Rabbi Yochanan would agree here. When Rabbi Yochanan says an error regarding the *karet* suffices, he means in cases where there *is* a *karet* to err about. Here, with *shvuat bituy*, there is no *karet* at all, so there is nothing to make an error about except the prohibition itself. What else could the *shegaga* consist of?

### Karet Specific

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְחַיָּיב קׇרְבָּן — חִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דִּבְכָל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן לָאו דְּמַיְיתֵי עֲלֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן, וְהָכָא מַיְיתֵי — כִּי שָׁגַג בְּקׇרְבָּן נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It might enter your mind to say the following: Since the obligation to bring an offering in the case of the oath is a novel halakha, as throughout the whole Torah in its entirety we do not find a prohibition for whose unwitting violation one is liable to bring an offering and for whose intentional violation is not punishable by *karet*; and here, one** is liable to bring an offering for its unwitting violation, I might have said **that if he was unwitting,** i.e., unaware that he would be obligated, with regard to **the offering, let him be liable also** according to the Rabbis, who disagree with Munbaz.

The *Gemara* responds: no, it is not so obvious. One might have thought — *ho'il v'chiyuv korban chiddush hu* — since the very existence of a *korban* for a false oath is itself a *chiddush*. In the entire *Torah*, we do not find a simple prohibition without *karet* that incurs a *korban chatat* — a *korban* is normally the consequence of *karet*-level sins. Yet here, the *Torah* demands a *korban* even for a *lav* without *karet*. I might have thought that Rabbi Yochanan, precisely because the *Torah* is so demanding in requiring a *korban* in this case, would shift his position and allow even an error regarding the *korban* itself to suffice — effectively adopting Munbaz's position for this exceptional case. *Kama shema lan* that he does not. Rabbi Yochanan remains consistent: outside of Munbaz, no one holds that an error solely about the *korban* constitutes sufficient *shegaga*. Even in this case, Rabbi Yochanan maintains that one must err about the *lav*.

### Shvuat Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזוֹהִי שִׁגְגַת שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי לְשֶׁעָבַר — שֶׁאִם אָמַר ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁשְּׁבוּעָה זוֹ אֲסוּרָה, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן אוֹ לֹא״ — חַיָּיב! הָא מַנִּי? — מוֹנְבַּז הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, Abaye **teaches us** that this is not so. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **What is an unwitting violation of an oath on a statement relating to the past?** What is an example of one who unwittingly swore falsely with regard to an incident that occurred in the past? It cannot be a case where he forgot the incident, as in that case he is exempt from bringing an offering. It is a case **where if he said: I know that taking this false oath is prohibited, but I do not know whether or not one is liable to bring an offering for** swearing falsely, he is **liable** to bring an offering for an unwitting transgression. Apparently, with regard to an oath on a statement, unwitting with regard to the sacrifice renders the action unwitting. The Gemara rejects this: In accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** mishna? **It is** the opinion of **Munbaz.**

The *Gemara* now challenges with a *baraita*: *eizo hi shegagat shvuat bituy l'shavar* — what constitutes a *shegaga* in the case of a false oath about the past? The most intuitive answer would be: one forgot — one swore one had not eaten the apple, genuinely believing it to be true, when in fact one had. But the *Gemara* notes that in such a case, where at the moment of the oath one genuinely believes one is swearing truthfully, this is actually closer to *ones* — compelled sin, or at minimum not a false oath in any meaningful sense. The nature of an oath is such that a factual error in one's sincere belief does not constitute a transgression. 

So what does a *shegaga* in *shvuat bituy* look like? The *baraita* provides: *yodea ani sheshvua asura, aval eini yodea im chayav b'korban o lo* — I know that swearing falsely is prohibited, but I did not know one becomes obligated in a *korban* for it. And the *baraita* says one is *chayav*. This seems to vindicate Munbaz — error regarding only the *korban* suffices! The *Gemara* responds: *ha mani Munbaz hee* — this *baraita* follows Munbaz. It does not represent the normative position. According to the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Yochanan, one must genuinely think the act is permitted, or — in cases involving *karet* — not know of the *karet*; and in cases of *shvuat bituy* where there is no *karet*, one must genuinely believe the false oath is permissible.

### Terumah Abaye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּתְרוּמָה שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ חוֹמֶשׁ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוֹג בְּלָאו שֶׁבָּהּ. ״הַכֹּל מוֹדִים״ מַאן? — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. פְּשִׁיטָא! כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא כָּרֵת, הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא כָּרֵת לָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִיתָה בִּמְקוֹם כָּרֵת עוֹמֶדֶת, וְכִי שָׁגַג בְּמִיתָה נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said: Everyone agrees** with regard to ***teruma* that one is only liable to add a payment of one-fifth** the value of the *teruma* **for** eating **it unwittingly if he is unwitting with regard to its prohibition.** The Gemara asks: To **whose** opinion is Abaye referring in the phrase: **Everyone agrees? Certainly, it is the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Even though, in general, he holds that unwitting with regard to *karet* is sufficient to render the action unwitting, the case of *teruma* is different. The Gemara asks: In the case of *teruma*, **it is obvious** that he would agree. **When Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that one need not be unwitting with regard to the prohibition, it is in a case **where there is a prohibition punishable by *karet*; however, here, where there is no punishment of *karet*, Rabbi Yoḥanan would not** say so. The Gemara answers that nonetheless Abaye introduced a novel element: **Lest you say** that since one who intentionally eats *teruma* is subject to death at the hand of Heaven, perhaps **death stands in place of *karet*. And where he was unwitting with regard to** the punishment of **death** for this sin, **he should also be liable** to pay the added fifth as one who performed the transgression unwittingly because his case is analogous to one who is considered unwitting due to lack of awareness of *karet*.

Abaye now states a parallel rule regarding *terumah*: *hakol modim b'terumah she'ein chayav aleha chomesh ad sheyishgog b'lav shebah* — everyone agrees that regarding *terumah*, one is not obligated in the *chomesh* until one has erred about the prohibition itself. The background: if a non-*kohen* eats *terumah* accidentally, he must return what he ate and add a *chomesh* — one fifth — which itself becomes *terumah*. The question is what constitutes *shogeg* here.

The easy case of *shogeg* is always a factual error — one did not know this food was *terumah*. The more complex question is the legal-error case. *Terumah* is not punishable by *karet*; it is punishable by *mita b'yedei shamayim* — heavenly death — which sounds more severe but appears to occupy a lower rung in the *halachic* hierarchy than *karet*.

Abaye says everyone agrees — meaning even Rabbi Yochanan — that one must err about the *lav* itself. The *Gemara* asks: is this not obvious? Rabbi Yochanan only allows an error about *karet* to suffice when *karet* is present. Here there is no *karet*, only *mita b'yedei shamayim*, so of course Rabbi Yochanan would require an error in the prohibition. The *Gemara* responds: not so obvious — one might have thought that *mita b'yedei shamayim* stands in the place of *karet* here, and that erring about the *mita* would similarly suffice as *shegaga*. Abaye teaches us that this is not the case.

### Rava Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִיתָה בִּמְקוֹם כָּרֵת עוֹמֶדֶת וְחוֹמֶשׁ בִּמְקוֹם קׇרְבָּן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Indeed, **death stands in place of *karet* and the** added **one-fifth stands in place of a sacrifice.** One who is unwitting with regard to death at the hand of Heaven and the added fifth has the same legal status as one who is unwitting with regard to *karet* and an offering.

Rava disagrees with Abaye. Rava says: *mita b'makom karet omedet, v'chomesh b'makom korban ka'i* — *mita b'yedei shamayim* stands in the place of *karet*, and *chomesh* stands in the place of a *korban*. The structure is mutatis mutandis. Just as by the *korban* the *karet* is the key constituent — the *korban* is the mechanism of atonement for a *karet*-level sin, and erring about the *karet* is sufficient *shegaga* — so too here, the *chomesh* is the mechanism of consequence for a sin involving *mita b'yedei shamayim*, and erring about the *mita* is likewise sufficient.

Rava's formulation is revealing. He does not argue that *mita b'yedei shamayim* is so weighty that awareness of it would surely have deterred the person — which would be one way to ground the *shav mi'yediato* argument. Rather, Rava says something more technically precise: *mita* does for *chomesh* exactly what *karet* does for *korban*. The relationship is structural. The *mita* is the constituent element upon which the *chomesh* obligation hinges, just as *karet* is the constituent element upon which the *korban* obligation hinges. The error must occur precisely regarding that constituent element, whatever it happens to be in a given framework. This framing actually illuminates the underlying logic of Rabbi Yochanan's position more clearly: it is not merely about the subjective gravity of the sin in the person's mind, but about the technical relationship between the severity-marker (*karet* or *mita*) and the consequence-mechanism (*korban* or *chomesh*).

The rule of the *Gemara* in practice follows Rabbi Yochanan — and this is in a sense a deeply forgiving ruling, for it means that someone who knew they were violating a prohibition yet simply did not appreciate that it rose to the level of *karet* is treated as sufficiently non-willful to be entitled to bring a *korban*.

## Lost in Wilderness

### Lost Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ אוֹ בַּמִּדְבָּר וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵימָתַי שַׁבָּת, מוֹנֶה שִׁשָּׁה יָמִים וּמְשַׁמֵּר יוֹם אֶחָד. חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁמֵּר יוֹם אֶחָד, וּמוֹנֶה שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: One who was walking along the way or in the desert, and he does not know when Shabbat occurs, he counts six days** from the day that he realized that he lost track of Shabbat **and observes one day** as Shabbat. **Ḥiyya bar Rav says: He** first **observes one day** as Shabbat and then **he counts six** weekdays.

The *Gemara* now turns to a very different and more accessible case: what if someone has literally lost track of which day of the week it is? This is not a hypothetical — one could become lost in the wilderness and lose track of the days entirely. What must such a person do regarding *Shabbat*?

### Six vs One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי — מָר סָבַר כִּבְרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, וּמָר סָבַר כְּאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what do they disagree?** One **Sage,** Rav Huna, **held:** It is **like the creation of the world,** weekdays followed by Shabbat. **And** one **Sage,** Ḥiyya bar Rav, **held:** It is **like Adam, the first** man, who was created on the sixth day. He observed Shabbat followed by the six days of the week.

Rav Huna says: *moneh shisha yamim umeshamer yom echad* — one counts six days and then observes the seventh. That is, the first day one becomes aware of the disorientation is treated as the equivalent of Sunday; one counts forward, and the seventh day is kept as *Shabbat*.

Chiyya bar Rav says the opposite: *meshamer yom echad umoneh shisha* — one starts by observing one day immediately (treating the next full day as *Shabbat*), and then counts six days after it. This position is explained by Rashi as analogous to *Adam HaRishon*, who was created in the middle of the week, found himself in the middle without a clear starting point, and yet observed the *Shabbat* that followed his first full day of existence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵימָתַי שַׁבָּת מְשַׁמֵּר יוֹם אֶחָד לְשִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Ḥiyya bar Rav from a *baraita*: If a person **was walking along the way and does not know when Shabbat occurs, he observes one day for** every **six.**

The *Gemara* asks: *b'mai kamiflagi* — what is the underlying dispute? One holds *kevriyaso shel olam*: we follow the pattern of creation — six days of work, then *Shabbat*. The other holds *ke-Adam HaRishon*: since one finds oneself in the middle without knowing which day it is, one is like *Adam HaRishon*, and the immediate next day becomes *Shabbat*.

From a religious and experiential standpoint, the six-then-one approach seems more natural — building toward *Shabbat* rather than beginning with it. But the *Gemara* proceeds to a *baraita*: *meshamer yom echad leshisha* — one observes one day for six. This language suggests the six have already passed, implying one counts six and then keeps the seventh — a *tiyuvta* against Chiyya bar Rav. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, מוֹנֶה שִׁשָּׁה וּמְשַׁמֵּר יוֹם אֶחָד?! לָא, מְשַׁמֵּר יוֹם אֶחָד וּמוֹנֶה שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** What, does this **not mean that he counts six and** then **observes one day** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Huna? The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it could also mean that **he observes one day and** then **counts six.**

The *Gemara* confirms: *tiyuvta deChiyya Bar Rav tiyuvta*.

### Rava Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּכָל יוֹם וָיוֹם עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ כְּדֵי פַרְנָסָתוֹ [בַּר מֵהָהוּא יוֹמָא]. וְהָהוּא יוֹמָא לֵימוּת?! דְּעָבֵיד מֵאֶתְמוֹל שְׁתֵּי פַרְנָסוֹת. וְדִילְמָא מֵאֶתְמוֹל שַׁבָּת הֲוַאי! אֶלָּא כָּל יוֹם וָיוֹם עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ פַּרְנָסָתוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ הָהוּא יוֹמָא. וְהָהוּא יוֹמָא בְּמַאי מִינְּכַר לֵיהּ? בְּקִידּוּשָׁא וְאַבְדָּלְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The person who lost track of Shabbat and treats one day a week as Shabbat, **each day he makes** enough food **to sustain himself, except for that day** which he designated as Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **And on that day let him die?** Rather, it means **that the day before he makes twice** the amount of food that he prepared on the other days **to sustain** him for that day and the following day. The Gemara asks: **And perhaps the day before was** actually **Shabbat?** In that case, not only did he perform labor on Shabbat, but he also performed labor on Shabbat in preparation for a weekday. Rather, on **each and every day he makes** enough food **to sustain himself** for that day, **including** on **that day** that he designated as Shabbat. And if you ask: **And how is that day** which he designated as Shabbat **distinguishable** from the rest? It is distinguishable **by** means of **the *kiddush* and the *havdala*** that he recites on that day.

The more pressing question remains: how can one do *melacha* on any of the six days, given that each of those days is a *safek Shabbat* — a doubt about whether it might be *Shabbat*? Each carries a *safek* of a biblical prohibition.

Rava answers: *bechol yom v'yom oseh lo k'dei parnasato* — every single day one does only the minimum amount of work necessary for survival. And on the one day designated as *Shabbat*, one does no *melacha* at all. The *Gemara* pushes back: *v'hahu yoma leimus* — will one then starve on that day? Rava responds: one does slightly extra work on the day *before* the designated *Shabbat*, so as to have food ready for the following day — analogous to the double portion of *man* collected on *erev Shabbat* in the wilderness.

But then the *Gemara* asks: perhaps today is itself already *Shabbat*, and doing two days' worth of work today is not justified by *pikuach nefesh* — one can survive today on one day's minimum, and tomorrow's needs can be addressed tomorrow with *pikuach nefesh* as justification then. The *Gemara* concedes the point. The revised answer: every day, including the designated *Shabbat*, one does only the minimum work necessary for that day's survival. One never does extra. *Pikuach nefesh* permits only what is required for immediate survival.

What then distinguishes the designated *Shabbat* day from the others? *Bekidusha v'avdalta* — one makes *Kiddush* and *Havdalah*. Even verbally, even without wine or *besamim*, one sets the day apart and demarcates its beginning and end. This preserves the qualitative distinctiveness of *Shabbat* even when the practical content of the day — the minimum work for survival — is the same as any other.

### Partial Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הָיָה מַכִּיר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם שֶׁיָּצָא בּוֹ, עוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁבָּת לָא נָפֵיק, בְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא [נָמֵי] לָא נָפֵיק. וְהַאי, אִי נָמֵי בְּחַמְשָׁה בְּשַׁבְּתָא נָפֵיק — לִישְׁתְּרֵי לֵיהּ לְמֶיעְבַּד מְלָאכָה תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן זִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּח שְׁיָּירְתָּא וּמִקְּרֵי וְנָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If he had partial knowledge of the day on which he left,** i.e., he does not recall what day of the week it was but he does recall the number of days that passed since he left, every week **he can perform labor throughout the day** of his departure, since he certainly did not leave his house on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: That is **obvious,** and what novel element was introduced here? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say, since he did not leave on Shabbat, he also did not leave on Friday, and this** person, **even if he left on Thursday, should be permitted to perform labor** for **two days,** the eighth day and the ninth day from his departure, the same day of the week that he left and the following day. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that **at times one finds a convoy and happens to leave** on a journey even on Friday. Therefore, he is not permitted to perform labor on the day of the week following the day of his departure.

Rava then addresses the case where one has partial knowledge that allows elimination of certain days. *Im haya makir miktzat hayom sheyatza bo* — if one knows something about the day one departed, one may do as much *melacha* as desired on the day that is thereby eliminated from suspicion.

Rashi explains the case as follows: one knows that one has been traveling for three days. One has forgotten which day of the week one departed, but one knows the number of days elapsed. This means one can identify that whichever day of the week corresponds to the departure day was not *Shabbat* — since one cannot depart on *Shabbat*. That day of the week, recurring each week, is entirely free of the *Shabbat* concern and one may labor freely on it: *oseh melacha kol hayom kulo*.

The *Gemara* asks: is this not obvious? One might have thought that in addition to knowing one did not leave on *Shabbat*, one could also assume one did not leave on a Friday — who goes traveling on *erev Shabbat*? If so, perhaps two days per week could be eliminated. The *Gemara* rejects this: *zimnin d'meshkach shayarta umakrei v'nafik* — sometimes a caravan presents itself and one departs opportunistically, even on a Friday. But one will never knowingly depart on *Shabbat* itself. Therefore only the one day corresponding to one's departure is eliminated with certainty.

### Tosafot Walking

Tosafot raises an additional dimension: what about walking? *Aval l'haleich yachol kol mah sheyirtzeh* — one may walk as much as one wishes each day, including on the designated *Shabbat*, without concern for *tchumin*. Why? Because if one cannot walk, *lo yagia l'olam l'yishuv* — one will never reach civilization. Furthermore, even on the day designated as *Shabbat*, one may continue traveling, since the purpose is to get back to civilization — an ongoing *pikuach nefesh* consideration.

This raises a broader point worth noting. The *Gemara*'s framework imagines that one does just enough each day to survive today. But reality is often more complex: the work done today — carrying food and supplies, for instance — may not be serving an immediate need but rather preventing a future *pikuach nefesh*. Carrying one's canteen today does not save one's life today (one can drink without carrying); it saves one's life several days from now. The answer is that all such activity falls within the broad category of *pikuach nefesh*: if one does not carry and prepare, one will eventually run out of food and die before reaching civilization. This parallels a case where a doctor must travel on *Shabbat* to reach a patient whose life-threatening condition will materialize on Sunday — the *pikuach nefesh* is future-oriented, but the *Shabbat* violation to address it is permitted now. Tosafot alludes to this when addressing the walking question.

## Multiple Shabbatot

### One vs Many

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת: מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: ״וְשָׁמְרוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת שַׁבְּתֹתַי תִּשְׁמֹרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that there is a difference in *halakha* between **one who knows the essence of Shabbat** and one who does not know it. The Gemara asks: **From where** in the Torah **are these matters** derived? **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Two verses are written.** One states: **"And the children of Israel observed the Shabbat,** to perform the Shabbat through their generations, an everlasting covenant" (Exodus 31:16). **And it is written: "And you shall observe My *Shabbatot*** and you shall revere My Sanctuary, I am God" (Leviticus 26:2).

The *Gemara* concludes with the question of how many *korbanot* one brings when one violates multiple *Shabbatot*. The principle has already been stated: if one forgot that *Shabbat* exists altogether — *shikhach ikar Shabbat* — one brings only a single *korban* for all violations across all weeks. But if one knew that *Shabbat* exists and forgot which day it was — *yodeia ikar Shabbat* — one brings a separate *korban* for each *Shabbat* that was violated. The question is: from where do we derive this distinction?

### Two Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? ״וְשָׁמְרוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת״ — שְׁמִירָה אַחַת לְשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה. ״וְאֶת שַׁבְּתֹתַי תִּשְׁמֹרוּ״ — שְׁמִירָה אַחַת לְכׇל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **How** is it that Shabbat is in the singular in one verse, while in the other it is in the plural [*Shabbatot*]? It should be understood as follows: **"And the children of Israel observed the Shabbat": One observance for multiple *Shabbatot*.** If one commits several transgressions, in certain cases he is only liable to bring one sacrifice. **"And you shall observe My *Shabbatot*": One observance for each and every Shabbat.** In certain cases, one is liable to bring a sin-offering for each time that he unwittingly desecrated Shabbat.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, says there are two *pesukim*. The first: *veshamru venei Yisrael et haShabbat* — the children of Israel shall observe *the Shabbat*, in the singular. The second: *ve'et Shabbetotai tishmoru* — my *Shabbatot*, in the plural, you shall observe.

The derivation: *veshamru venei Yisrael et haShabbat* — one observance for many *Shabbatot*. This teaches that in certain circumstances all the *Shabbatot* are treated as a single unit, generating only one *korban*. *Ve'et Shabbetotai tishmoru* — each *Shabbat* is treated separately. This teaches that in other circumstances each *Shabbat* is independently significant and requires its own *korban*. As Rashi puts it: *lo masar katuv ela lachachamim* — the *Torah* left the interpretive assignment to the Sages, who applied the singular verse to the case of forgetting the existence of *Shabbat* entirely and the plural verse to the case of forgetting which day it was.

### Rabbi Yitzhak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא: ״וְשָׁמְרוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת״ — שְׁמִירָה אַחַת לְכׇל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת. ״וְאֶת שַׁבְּתֹתַי תִּשְׁמֹרוּ״ — שְׁמִירָה אַחַת לְשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak strongly objects: On the contrary, the opposite is reasonable. "And the children of Israel observed the Shabbat": One observance for each and every Shabbat. "And you shall observe My *Shabbatot*": One observance for multiple *Shabbatot*.** In any case, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak also holds that the *halakha* of our mishna is derived from comparing and contrasting these two verses.

Rabbi Yitzhak objects — not to the conclusion but to the assignment: *adaraba ipkha mistabra* — on the contrary, the verses should be understood in the opposite way. *Veshamru venei Yisrael et haShabbat* — "the Shabbat" in the singular — should mean: one observance for each individual *Shabbat*, treating each *Shabbat* as a separate unit. Therefore, each separately violated *Shabbat* incurs its own *korban*. And *ve'et Shabbetotai tishmoru* — "my *Shabbatot*" in the plural — should mean: one observance covering many *Shabbatot*, so that forgetting about *Shabbat* entirely is treated as one global lapse, generating only a single *korban*.

The result is identical to Rav Nachman's: forgetting the existence of *Shabbat* yields one *korban*, forgetting which day is *Shabbat* yields one *korban* per *Shabbat* violated. The only difference between Rabbi Yitzhak and Rav Nachman is which verse is assigned to which case. Both readings are equally valid derivations — the *Torah* provides two verses, one evoking singularity and one evoking plurality, and the Sages interpret them to capture both halachic realities. The *Gemara* will continue tomorrow with the question of forgetting about individual *melachot*.
