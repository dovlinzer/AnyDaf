# Nedarim 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Unawareness of Hafara

### Existence of Hafara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ נְדָרִים, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְפִירִין״, יָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a man's wife or daughter took a vow and he failed to nullify the vow on the day he heard it, but afterward he said: **I know that there are vows, but I don't know that there are those who** can **nullify** them, i.e., he was unaware of the possibility of nullifying vows, **he can nullify** the vow of his wife or his daughter on the day he learned that he can nullify vows.

The *daf* for today is 88, and we pick up at the bottom of 87b with a new *mishna*. We remain in the domain of *hafara* — the husband's power to annul his wife's vows — and the *mishna* we now encounter introduces a nuanced question about what it means to truly "hear" a vow.

The *mishna* presents the following case: a husband says, *yodea ani sheyesh nedarim* — I know that there is such a thing as a halachic *neder* — but *eini yodea sheyesh mefirim* — I did not know that there exists such a thing as *hafara*, or that anyone is empowered to act as a *meifer*, an annulling authority. Perhaps he assumed that *hafara* was an ancient biblical institution no longer in practice. Perhaps he simply never considered that such a mechanism was available to him at all. In such a case, the *mishna* rules *yafeh* — he may still annul the vow. The reason is that the day on which the vow was made is not considered *yom shamo*, the halachically operative "day of hearing." Since he was entirely unaware that *hafara* was relevant to his situation, he was not truly "hearing" the *neder* in any meaningful sense. It is only the day he realizes that he possesses this power that becomes his *yom shamo*, and that is the day he may perform *hafara*.

### Yom Shamo Defined

This raises an important conceptual question: one might argue that *yom shamo* should be defined purely in terms of the physical act of hearing — one either heard or did not. If we begin to sever the physical act of hearing from its practical significance and meaning, perhaps neither day qualifies as *yom shamo* and the husband finds himself unable to annul at all. The *mishna* rejects this approach, at least in the case where a person was entirely outside the *parsha* — entirely unaware that *hafara* existed or applied to him. In such a case, the day genuine awareness arrives is the operative *yom shamo*, and *hafara* is valid on that day.

### Relevance Anecdote

The underlying principle is that if something is not perceived as relevant to you, it does not register — even if it is technically within your sensory range. If a person does not know that commercial vehicles are prohibited on certain roadways, a sign reading "no commercial vehicles" simply does not register as one passes it. And if one does not know that a mechanism of *hafara* exists or applies to him, the words of the *neder* he overhears do not truly constitute hearing in the sense the Torah has in mind. *Shmi'ah* — the kind of hearing that activates the husband's right and obligation to respond — means a meaningful, cognizant apprehension of what one has heard and its implications.

## Partial Hearing Debate

### Specific Neder Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְפִירִין, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁזֶּה נֶדֶר״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָפֵר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, he said: **I know there are those who** can **nullify** vows, **but** I refrained from nullifying the vow that I heard because **I do not know that this** is considered **a vow, Rabbi Meir says he cannot nullify** the vow at this point, **but the Rabbis say** that even in this case **he can nullify** the vow on the day that he learned of his mistake.

The more complex and analytically rich case appears at the end of the *mishna*: *yodea ani sheyesh mefirim aval eini yodea shezeh neder* — I know that I possess the power of *hafara*, and I know that the world of *nedarim* is in principle my concern, but I did not realize that this particular utterance of my wife's constituted a *neder*. Perhaps he thought a certain key word was required and it was absent; perhaps he believed this topic fell outside the category of *devarim shebeino leveinah*. In any case, he was not entirely outside the *parsha* — he knew he was someone who could be a *meifer* — but he misjudged the specific details that would make this vow operative.

Is this sufficient to constitute *yom shamo*? The *mishna* records a dispute: *Rabbi Meir omer lo yafer* — Rabbi Meir says he cannot annul — while *Chachamim omrim yafer* — the Sages say he may.

### Ran's Explanations

The *Ran* offers two distinct explanations of this dispute, and they represent genuinely different readings of the underlying halachic positions.

### Realization as Hearing

The simpler explanation — which the *Ran* presents second but which flows most naturally from the *reisha* of the *mishna* — begins with the position of the *Chachamim*. The *Chachamim* hold that the husband may perform *hafara*, meaning: on the day he realizes that this utterance was in fact a halachic *neder*, that day becomes his *yom shamo*, and he may annul just as in the first case of the *mishna*. When the pieces come together and full awareness is achieved, that constitutes *yom shamo*.

Rabbi Meir, on this reading, disagrees. He holds that since the husband already knew he was within the *parsha* — he knew *hafara* was relevant to him as a general matter — that initial day of hearing already constituted *yom shamo*. One could therefore not retroactively designate the later day of fuller comprehension as *yom shamo*. The question then becomes whether Rabbi Meir would permit *hafara* on the original day of hearing itself. In principle, yes — if, for example, his wife urged him to perform *hafara* even though he himself was uncertain whether it was relevant, and he complied, that would presumably be valid according to Rabbi Meir. The operative point is that the day one knew one was in the *parsha* is *yom shamo*, and no later day can serve as a substitute.

### Two Half-Hearings

The first and more striking explanation the *Ran* offers presents Rabbi Meir as holding a far more restrictive position: the husband cannot annul either that day or the next. The reasoning is as follows. On the day of hearing, he knew he was within the *parsha*, but he lacked the specific knowledge that this was a *neder*. On the later day when he realizes it was a *neder*, too much time has passed — the clock of *yom shamo* already began ticking when he first heard and recognized his general capacity. Neither half constitutes a complete *shmi'ah*: the first half is sufficient to preclude the later day from serving as *yom shamo*, but it is not itself sufficient to permit *hafara*, because the hearing was incomplete. The second half completes the knowledge but falls outside the operative window. Rabbi Meir thus splits the hearing into two partial *shmi'ahs*, neither of which individually suffices.

According to this reading, the *Chachamim* disagree and hold that on the day the husband first heard — even with incomplete knowledge of the specifics — that constitutes adequate *yom shamo*, and he may perform *hafara* on that day if he chooses. The partial initial hearing, according to the *Chachamim*, is sufficient. In summary, under the second *Ran* explanation: Rabbi Meir holds that partial hearing is never sufficient — two halves do not make a whole — while the *Chachamim* hold that the initial partial hearing is adequate for *hafara* on that day itself.

## Blind Person Parallel

### B'lo Ra'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״, פְּרָט לַסּוֹמֵא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following *baraita*: With regard to one who kills unintentionally, the verse states: **"Without seeing"** (Numbers 35:23), which serves **to exclude a blind person** from the category of those who are exiled to a city of refuge due to having killed unintentionally, as the verse indicates that it was only in this instance that he did not see, but he is generally able to see. A blind person who kills another unintentionally is considered a victim of circumstances beyond his control. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says** the verse serves **to include a blind person** in the category of those who are exiled, as he too does not see. This shows that Rabbi Meir does not distinguish between different kinds of lack of knowledge, whereas the mishna suggests that he does accept such a distinction. The opposite is true of Rabbi Yehuda, who, unless it is otherwise indicated, is assumed to be Rabbi Meir's disputant in all places.

Having laid out the *mishna*'s debate, the *Gemara* now poses a challenge from an entirely different area of halacha: the laws of accidental manslaughter and *galus*, exile to a city of refuge. The verse describes a case where one person kills another *b'lo ra'ot* — without seeing. The scenario is the standard one: a person is chopping wood in a forest, the blade flies off the handle, and someone is killed. The verse's phrase *b'lo ra'ot* generates a dispute about the status of a *suma*, a blind person, who by definition always acts without seeing.

The *Gemara* cites the following: *b'lo ra'ot prat l'suma*, divrei Rabbi Yehuda — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *b'lo ra'ot* excludes a blind person. The logic is that *b'lo ra'ot* means one who could have seen but did not; a blind person, who can never see, is in a categorically different situation and is exempt from *galus* entirely. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, holds *l'rabot et hasuma* — that *b'lo ra'ot* includes the blind person. Now, the *Gemara* perceives a structural parallel to our *mishna*: the debate about the blind person concerns whether partial awareness — a kind of awareness-without-seeing — suffices to place someone within the legal category at hand. This maps, the *Gemara* suggests, onto the debate about partial hearing in the context of *hafara*. Yet something striking emerges: the positions appear to be reversed.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: גַּבֵּי רוֹצֵחַ כְּתִיב ״וַאֲשֶׁר יָבֹא אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בַיַּעַר״ — כׇּל דְּבַר מֵיעַל לְיַעַר, וְסּוֹמֵא נָמֵי בַּר מֵיעַל לְיַעַר הוּא. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא — מִ״יַּעַר״ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ פְּרָט לְסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda maintains** that **with regard to** the exile of an unintentional **killer it is written: "And a man who goes into the forest with his neighbor** to hew wood" (Deuteronomy 19:5), which serves to include **anyone** who **is capable of entering a forest, and a blind person is also capable of entering a forest. And if you say** that the phrase **"without seeing"** serves **to include a blind person,** this **is** already **derived from** the word **"forest,"** as he too can enter a forest. **Rather, learn from it** that the phrase **"without seeing"** serves **to exclude a blind person** from the category of unintentional killers who are exiled to a city of refuge.

The *Gemara* explains Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning as follows. The verse in the context of *galus* also contains the phrase *v'asher yavo et re'ehu baya'ar* — and one who comes upon his friend in the forest — regarding which the *Gemara* observes: *kol d'var me'ilei laya'ar hu* — any person who enters a forest is included. A blind person is perfectly capable of entering a forest, so the verse begins by assuming a blind person is included in the law of *galus*. When we then encounter *b'lo ra'ot*, there is no need to use it to include the blind person — that has already been accomplished. The only remaining function of *b'lo ra'ot* is therefore to exclude the blind person. This is the principle of *ein ribui achar ribui ela lema'et*: a second inclusion, when the item is already included, must function as an exclusion.

### Rabbi Meir's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כְּתִיב ״בִּבְלִי דַעַת״ — כׇּל דְּבַר מִידָּע, וְסּוֹמֵא לָאו בַּר מִידָּע הוּא. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ פְּרָט לַסּוֹמֵא — מִ״בְּלִי דַעַת״ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabbi Meir maintains: It is written** in that same section: "One who strikes his neighbor **without knowledge"** (Deuteronomy 19:4), which indicates that the *halakha* applies to **anyone who is capable of knowing** the precise location of the people standing about him, **but a blind person is not capable of knowing** this. **And if you say** that the phrase **"without seeing"** serves **to exclude a blind person,** this **is** already **derived from** the words **"without knowledge." Rather, learn from it** that the phrase **"without seeing"** serves **to include a blind person** in the *halakha* of exile, not to exclude him.

Rabbi Meir proceeds from the opposite starting point. A different verse in the passage uses the phrase *b'li da'at* — without awareness — and Rabbi Meir reads this as referring to someone who is *bar meda*, capable of full awareness. A blind person, he argues, is not fully capable of such awareness — *suma lav bar meda hu* — and so the passage, read from the outset, implies that a blind person is excluded. When the verse then says *b'lo ra'ot*, there is nothing left for it to do by way of exclusion — the blind person is already excluded. Therefore, this phrase must function to include the blind person. This is the principle of *ein miyut achar miyut ela lerabos*: a second exclusion, when the item is already excluded, must function as an inclusion.

### Partial Awareness Drive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא מֵעִנְיָנֵיהּ דִּקְרָא וְהָכָא מֵעִנְיָנֵיהּ דִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There is no contradiction here, as the dispute with regard to an unintentional killing is based on divergent interpretations of the verse. **Here,** the ruling follows **from the context of the verse, and there,** the ruling follows **from the context of the verse.**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes with *Rava*'s statement: *hacha me'inyana de'kra v'hacha me'inyana de'kra* — in each case, the outcome is driven by the specific language of the verse, and therefore the positions in the *galus* dispute need not be consistent with one's general view on partial awareness. What appears to be a reversal of positions is in fact the result of each *tanna* being driven by different textual starting points.

The *Ran*, however, goes further and offers a deeper reading of how positions on partial awareness actually *drive* the textual interpretations rather than being independent of them. The *Ran* aligns the *galus* dispute with the second explanation he gave of our *mishna*. According to that reading, Rabbi Meir holds that partial awareness — a half-hearing — is never sufficient. This is his general orienting principle. When Rabbi Meir approaches the *parsha* of *galus*, he therefore begins by assuming that a blind person, who has only partial awareness, is excluded, since his default approach is that the partial does not count. The extra verse then has no exclusionary work to do and must therefore include the blind person. Rabbi Yehuda, who is presumably the position of the *Chachamim* in our *mishna*, begins from the opposite principle: partial awareness is meaningful and sufficient. Accordingly, he reads the *parsha* of *galus* as initially including the blind person, since his default is that partial suffices. The extra verse then has no inclusionary work left and must therefore exclude.

On this reading, the positions are not merely reversed by textual accident. Rather, the deeper halachic intuition about whether partial awareness suffices shapes how each *tanna* reads the verse, and the resulting textual conclusion comes out in the opposite direction from where each would land in the context of *hafara*. The *Ran* is careful to observe that this is specifically true under the second reading of the *mishna*'s dispute — the reading in which Rabbi Meir holds that two half-hearings, each insufficient alone, leave the husband unable to perform *hafara* at all.

## Neder/Son-in-Law Property

### Vow Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּדִּיר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲתָנוֹ, וְהוּא רוֹצֶה לָתֵת לְבִתּוֹ מָעוֹת, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: הֲרֵי הַמָּעוֹת הָאֵלּוּ נְתוּנִין לָךְ בְּמַתָּנָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא לְבַעְלִיךְ רְשׁוּת בָּהֶן, אֶלָּא מָה שֶׁאַתְּ נוֹשֵׂאת וְנוֹתֶנֶת בְּפִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who vows** that **benefit** from him is forbidden **to his son-in-law, but he** nevertheless **wishes to give his daughter,** i.e., the wife of that same son-in-law, **money,** then, though he cannot do so directly, as anything acquired by a woman belongs to her husband, **he** should **say to her: This money is hereby given to you as a gift, provided that your husband has no rights to it,** but the gift includes **only that which you pick up and place in your mouth.**

We now turn to the next *mishna*. The case involves *hamadir hana'ah mechasno* — a father-in-law who takes a vow prohibiting his son-in-law from deriving any benefit from him. Having done so, he nonetheless wishes to give money to his daughter. The question the *mishna* grapples with is how the father-in-law can give property to his daughter in a way that does not benefit his son-in-law.

### Automatic Transfer

The difficulty is rooted in the principle of *kol shekansah isha kanah ba'alah* — whatever a wife acquires, her husband acquires. If the father simply gives money to his daughter, the husband immediately acquires it, and the father-in-law has thereby indirectly caused his son-in-law to benefit from his property, in violation of his vow. The father therefore faces a challenge: how does one give a gift to a married daughter without the benefit flowing through to the son-in-law?

## Blind Person Parallel…

The *Gemara*'s resolution through *Rava* — that in each domain the verse operates according to its own logic — applies specifically to the question of how the blind person debate in the laws of manslaughter maps onto the partial hearing debate in the laws of *hafara*, and the analysis belongs with the *galus* discussion above rather than being reintroduced here. What the context of the son-in-law *mishna* requires is a separate analysis of how the wife's acquisition operates and whether the father's stipulation can interrupt the automatic transfer to the husband.

---

## Neder/Son-in-Law Prope…

### Noseies V'Notenes

The *mishna* provides a solution: the father must give the money with the stipulation *mah she'at noseies v'notenet l'ficha* — "whatever you take and give over into your mouth." This phrasing goes beyond a simple declaration that the husband has no rights; it defines the moment of the daughter's acquisition in a specific and constrained way.

### Stipulation Fails

The *Ran* explains why a simple stipulation — saying merely that the husband shall have no rights over the gift — is insufficient according to the *mishna*. The reason lies in the principle of *yad isha k'yad ba'alah*, which the *mishna* is understood to follow in accordance with the position of Rabbi Meir. If the wife's hand is halachically equivalent to her husband's hand — that is, if she is not a legally independent acquisitional entity but rather an extension of him — then a stipulation excluding the husband's rights is logically incoherent. One cannot give something to a person's hand while simultaneously excluding that person from ownership. If her hand *is* his hand, the transfer goes to him directly and there is no mechanism to interrupt it.

The *mishna*'s solution, as the *Ran* explains it, is that the father is not truly transferring ownership to his daughter at all. The money remains, in effect, the father's. What he is granting is something far more limited: the right to consume. As she is in the act of placing the food or its equivalent into her mouth — and only at that moment — he permits her to benefit from it. At that instant of consumption, the benefit accrues to her directly, and there is no opportunity for it to pass through her hand into her husband's. Whether this applies only to consumables or extends to non-consumable goods such as clothing is a question the *Ran* considers separately: one might argue that a non-consumable is possessed at the moment she takes hold of it and could then pass to the husband; alternatively, one could argue that even then the item remains the father's, which she is merely permitted to use, and it never enters the chain of acquisition that leads to the husband.

## Rav vs Shmuel

### Yad Isha Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לַהּ ״מָה שֶׁאַתְּ נוֹשֵׂאת וְנוֹתֶנֶת בְּפִיךְ״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״מַה שֶּׁתִּרְצִי עֲשִׂי״, קְנָה יָתְהוֹן בַּעַל. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״מַה שֶּׁתִּרְצִי עֲשִׂי״ — לָא קְנָה יָתְהוֹן בַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav said** that **they taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where he** actually **said to her: That which you pick up and place in your mouth** is yours. **But** if **he said: Do as you please** with the money, his stipulation is of no effect, and **the husband acquires** the money. **And Shmuel says** that **even** if **he said: Do as you please** with the money, **the husband does not acquire it.**

At this point the *Gemara* sets out the conceptual distinction underlying the dispute between Rav and Shmuel, who debate whether the *mishna*'s stringent formulation is in fact required as a matter of *halacha*.

The conceptual foundation of the entire discussion turns on the precise meaning of *kol shekansah isha kanah ba'alah* versus the stronger formulation of *yad isha k'yad ba'alah*. These two principles are not equivalent. The first formulation describes an automatic transfer: the wife possesses property, but it immediately flows to the husband. One can visualize this as automatic email forwarding — the item enters her account and is instantly redirected to his. Under this model, she does have her own *yad*, her own acquisitional capacity; it is simply that her account is configured to forward everything to her husband's. If this is correct, perhaps one could interrupt the forwarding — perhaps the father can stipulate that what he deposits in her account shall not be forwarded.

The second and stronger formulation — *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* — holds something more radical. Her hand is not merely connected to his; it *is* his. She has no independent acquisitional standing at all. There is no separate account that then forwards; there is only his account. Under this model, no stipulation can change the outcome, because there is no interstitial stage of her ownership in which to intervene. The only approach that can work is the one the *mishna* describes: eliminating the transfer entirely by ensuring she never "acquires" in the standard sense, but only benefits at the precise moment of consumption.

Rav holds in accordance with this stronger model — and the *Gemara* will note that this aligns with Rabbi Meir's position — and therefore requires the full formulation of *noseies v'notenet l'ficha*. Shmuel holds the more moderate position and therefore maintains that a simple stipulation against the husband's rights suffices.

## Neder/Son-in-Law Prope…

### Money vs Food

One further question requires attention: why does the *mishna* specify that the father gives his daughter *money* rather than food directly? The *Ran* explains that food is a categorically different case. The husband is obligated to feed his wife. If a third party directly provides food to the wife, that provision satisfies the husband's existing obligation to feed her. The property, in other words, is discharging what is already his debt. Since what he owes her is her food, that food belongs to her by virtue of his obligation and does not carry with it any residual right for him to claim benefit. The father's gift of food would therefore be free of the problem entirely — the husband never acquires rights over what satisfies his own obligation to her.

The indirect benefit to the husband from not having to spend money on food himself is, the *Ran* notes, an instance of *mavriach ari* — warding off a lion, preventing a loss — which is not halachically treated as conferring direct benefit on the person in question. Therefore the father wishing to give food would face no difficulty at all. It is specifically money — and, by extension, non-food property — that presents the problem addressed by the *mishna*, because money given to the wife flows through her hand into the husband's in a way that food designated for her sustenance does not.

---

## Rav vs Shmuel…

### Rav: Both Phrases

The *Gemara* now presents Rav's position directly. *Lo shanu ela de-amar lah mah she-at noset ve-notenet be-ficha* — Rav taught that the *mishna*'s permission applies only if the father explicitly uses the formulation of "what you place into your mouth." *Aval amar mah she-tirtsi asi* — but if he says "do with it as you wish," even though this is functionally equivalent to saying "your husband has no rights over it," in that case *kana bahen baal* — the husband acquires it nonetheless. According to Rav, a simple declaration that the husband has no rights is legally ineffective. The only mechanism that prevents the husband from acquiring is the construction in the *mishna* whereby the wife does not fully acquire until the moment of consumption.

### Shmuel: Stipulation Only

Shmuel disagrees. *Afilu amar mah she-tirtsi asi* — even if the father merely says "do with it as you wish," conveying that it is entirely hers and the husband has no rights, *lo kana bahen baal* — the husband does not acquire it. Shmuel holds the more moderate view of *kol shekansah isha kanah ba'alah* — the automatic transfer model — and maintains that since the wife does possess her own acquisitional capacity, it is possible to interrupt the forwarding mechanism through explicit stipulation. Under Shmuel's position, the *mishna*'s formulation of *noseies v'notenet l'ficha* is not legally required; a straightforward condition that the husband shall have no rights is sufficient.

The *Ran* is careful to note that Shmuel is not disputing the internal logic of the *mishna* itself. Rather, Shmuel holds that the *mishna* reflects the position of Rabbi Meir — who applies *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* in its stronger sense — but that as a matter of practical *halacha*, we do not rule like Rabbi Meir on this point. Rav, by contrast, holds that the *mishna*'s stringent position is the operative *halacha*.

## Shitur Mevo'ot Example

### Shitur Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: כֵּיצַד מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בְּמָבוֹי? מַנִּיחַ אֶת הֶחָבִית וְאוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה לְכׇל בְּנֵי מָבוֹי. וּמְזַכֶּה לָהֶן עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים, וְעַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, וְעַל יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira continues: **And raise a contradiction** from an unattributed mishna (*Eiruvin* 73b), which presumably follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir: **How does one merge** the courtyards that open **into an alleyway** in order to permit its residents to carry on Shabbat from one courtyard to another in the same alley, if a person wishes to act on behalf of all the residents of the alleyway? **He places a barrel** filled with his own food or wine **and says: This is for all the residents of the alleyway.** For this gift to be acquired by the others, someone must accept it on their behalf, **and** the *tanna* therefore teaches that **he may transfer possession to them** even **by means of his Hebrew slave or maidservant,** whom he does not own, **and** likewise **by means of his adult son or daughter, and** similarly **by means of his wife.** These people may acquire the *eiruv* food on behalf of all the residents of the alleyway.

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge to Rav's position from a different context entirely: the laws of *shituf mevo'ot*. To understand the challenge, a brief description of the institution is necessary. In a shared alleyway — a *mavoi* — all the courtyards whose residents use that alleyway must establish a form of joint ownership in a single food item in order for the alleyway to be treated as a unified domain and carrying within it to be permitted on Shabbat. This is the *shituf mevo'ot*. Each courtyard is independently populated by residents of separate households, and each household is legally distinct. In order to unify all of these distinct entities into a single corporate-like domain, they all become joint owners of a single communal food item — typically a barrel of wine.

The practical question is how to effect this joint ownership efficiently. Rather than going door to door collecting a contribution from each household, a simpler method is available: one person takes the barrel and declares *harei zeh lechol bnei hamavoi* — this is hereby transferred to all the residents of the *mavoi*. This person confers ownership on everyone. But this act of conferring ownership requires a third party — someone who is not the owner — to take possession of the barrel and hold it on behalf of all the other residents. The owner cannot transfer something to himself; the acquisition must pass through an independent hand.

### Wife as Third Party

The *mishna* governing this process — a *stam mishna*, presumed to reflect the position of Rabbi Meir — lists the parties who may serve in this role. *Umezakeh lahen al yedei avdo veshifchato ha'ivrim* — one may effect the transfer through one's Hebrew male or female slaves. *Ve'al yedei beno uvito hagedolim* — through one's adult sons or daughters. *Ve'al yedei ishto* — and through one's wife.

The rationale for including adult children and Hebrew slaves is that even though they exist in a close relationship with the owner, they are halachically recognized as distinct legal persons capable of independent acquisition. The mention of the wife in this list is what creates the difficulty.

The community's *eruv* is handled in practice in a manner consistent with this principle: a rabbi takes a box of matzahs — which remain usable over an extended period — gives them to a designated individual, and says that this person is taking possession on behalf of all members of the community who reside in the relevant domain. The wife is listed as one who can perform this function for the *shituf mevo'ot*.

### Rabbi Meir Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ קְנָה יָתְהוֹן בַּעַלַהּ, עֵירוּב לָא נָפֵיק מֵרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּבַעַל!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira states the contradiction: **And if you say** that a woman's **husband acquires** anything given to her, **the *eiruv*** food **has** consequently **not left the husband's domain** when he gives it to his wife, for anything she acquires belongs to him. Rather, it can be seen from here that Rabbi Meir does not extend his principle from a slave to a married woman, in opposition of the ruling of Rav.

Here lies the tension. If Rabbi Meir holds *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* — that the wife's acquisitional capacity is simply an extension of the husband's — then giving the barrel to one's wife is no different from giving it from one's right hand to one's left. There is no independent legal entity receiving it; it simply remains in the husband's domain. *Ve'i amart kneyah shon ba'alah* — and if you say that the husband invariably acquires whatever comes into his wife's hand, with no possibility of stipulating otherwise — then *eruv lo nafik mereshuteih deba'al*: the *eruv* barrel never leaves the husband's domain, and he cannot use her to effect the transfer to the rest of the *mavoi*. How then can the *stam mishna* — which follows Rabbi Meir — include the wife as a valid agent for this *kinyan*?

### Rava's Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר יַד אִשָּׁה כְּיַד בַּעְלָהּ, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְעִנְיַן שִׁיתּוּף, דְּכֵיוָן דִּלְזַכּוֹת לַאֲחֵרִים הוּא מִיַּד בַּעְלָהּ — זָכְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: **Even though Rabbi Meir said** that in general **the hand of a woman is like the hand of her husband,** in accordance with the ruling of Rav, **Rabbi Meir** nevertheless **concedes with regard to the merging** of alleyways **that since it** is her aim **to acquire** the *eiruv* food **for others from the hand of her husband,** and not to acquire it for herself, **she can acquire** it from him for this purpose.

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *af al gav de'amar Rabbi Meir yad isha k'yad ba'alah, modeh Rabbi Meir le'inyan shituf, dekeivan d'lezakot l'acherim hu, miyad ba'alah zachyah* — even though Rabbi Meir holds that the wife's hand is like her husband's hand, he concedes in the case of *shituf* that she can serve as the acquisitional agent, because here the transfer is from the husband's hand and is intended to confer ownership upon others. In this circumstance, the wife successfully represents an entity distinct from the husband.

The *Ran* and *Tosafos* both emphasize that the critical element here is *miyad ba'alah* — that it is the husband himself who is initiating the transfer. The reasoning is as follows. The principle of *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* is not a metaphysical description of what a wife is; it is an imposed legal structure. Unlike the case of a non-Hebrew slave — whose status as the master's property is fundamental and cannot be severed by act of will — a wife retains full legal personhood within *halacha*. She can be a litigant, own property under certain conditions, and is in no way a chattel. The characterization of her *yad* as equivalent to her husband's is therefore a legal construct that was instituted and that, importantly, can be removed. The only party empowered to sever that connection is the husband himself. He can, by an act of explicit intention, declare that he is releasing his claim and that she is to function as a genuinely independent entity for purposes of this transaction.

A third party could not accomplish this. If someone other than the husband places an item in the wife's hands and says "this is for other people," the *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* structure is not disrupted — it takes what is going into her hand and redirects it to the husband. Only the husband, as the one whose relationship with her generates that legal structure, can choose to step outside it.

This is to be distinguished from reading the *Gemara*'s language as emphasizing *lezakot l'acherim* — the fact that the transfer is destined for others. On an alternative reading, one might argue that when a wife takes possession for the sake of parties other than herself, we view her as standing in for those third parties rather than as an extension of her husband, and therefore the *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* structure simply does not apply. Under this reading, the critical variable would be the destination of the property rather than the identity of the giver. However, the *Ran* and *Tosafos* both reject this reading and emphasize that it is specifically *miyad ba'alah* — the husband's active, explicit participation as the giver — that breaks the structural equivalence.

## Wife Standing Limit

### Wife Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֵלּוּ שֶׁזָּכִין לָהֶן: [עַל יְדֵי] בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, וְעַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין זָכִין לָהֶן: עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְעַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַכְּנַעֲנִים, וְאִשְׁתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised an objection to Rav Ashi** from the following *baraita*: **These are** the people **who can acquire** *eiruv* food **on behalf of** others: The *eiruv* food can be acquired **by means of his adult son or daughter, and** by means of **his Hebrew slave or maidservant. And these are** the people **who cannot acquire** an *eiruv* **on behalf of** others: The *eiruv* food cannot be acquired **by means of his minor son or daughter, or** by means of **his Canaanite slave or maidservant, or** by means of **his wife.** This indicates that a married woman does not have an independent right of acquisition to acquire the *eiruv* food on behalf of others, in opposition to the ruling of the mishna.

The *Gemara* now identifies a further complication. *Aesivay Ravina l'Rav Ashi* — Ravina posed to Rav Ashi a challenge from another Tannaic source: a *baraita* that explicitly enumerates the parties who can and cannot serve as the acquisitional agent for the *shituf mevo'ot*. On the permissible side, it lists adult children and Hebrew slaves — precisely as in the *stam mishna*. On the impermissible side — *elu she-ein zachin lahen* — it lists minor children, Canaanite slaves, and *v'ishto* — the wife. This creates a direct contradiction: one Tannaic source explicitly permits the wife to serve in this role and another explicitly prohibits it.

### Property Interest Requir…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ חָצֵר בְּאוֹתוֹ מָבוֹי עָסְקִינַן, דְּמִגּוֹ דְּזָכְיָא לְנַפְשַׁהּ — זָכְיָא לְאַחֲרִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** In **the mishna** in *Eiruvin*, **we are dealing with** a woman **who possesses a courtyard** of her own **in that alleyway,** i.e., it is a case where the husband had earlier stipulated that she should have property of her own, to which he renounces all his rights. **As, since she acquires** the *eiruv* food **for herself** by virtue of the courtyard that she owns in that alleyway, **she** likewise **acquires** it **for others.**

Rav Ashi resolves the contradiction. The *stam mishna* that permits use of the wife does not apply across the board. *Matnitin besheyesh lah chatzer be'oto mavoi askinan* — the *mishna* refers specifically to a case where the wife herself owns a house in that very *mavoi*. *Demigo dezachyah l'nafshah zachyah l'achrinei* — since she has independent standing to acquire for herself in this context, she can also acquire on behalf of others.

### House Ownership

The logic of Rav Ashi's answer is as follows. Even when the husband is willing to sever the *yad isha k'yad ba'alah* structure, and even when he is the one giving explicitly — as Rava established — Rabbi Meir still requires that the wife have some form of genuine independent legal standing in the relevant context in order for her acquisition to be recognized as belonging to a distinct entity. If she is simply his wife with no independent connection to the *mavoi*, she remains, even in the best of circumstances, primarily an extension of him and cannot serve as a genuinely independent *kinyan* vessel. But if she independently owns a house in that *mavoi*, she is already recognized as her own legal presence within the shared domain. That pre-existing standing — *miggo dezachyah l'nafshah*, since she is capable of acquiring for herself in this setting — enables her to acquire on behalf of others as well.

It bears noting that for the wife to independently own a house in the *mavoi* in a manner that is not subject to the husband's rights, it would likely have to have been brought into the marriage under conditions that explicitly excluded the husband's claims — perhaps as a prenuptial arrangement. Once inside the marriage, any newly acquired property would, according to Rabbi Meir's strong position, pass immediately to the husband's domain.

A further observation emerges from this entire discussion: the transfers being analyzed here appear to concern not merely the husband's right of *ochel peiros* — his entitlement to the usufruct of his wife's property while she retains title — but full transfer of ownership itself. The *shituf mevo'ot* question is about who holds title to the barrel of wine, not about who profits from it. This suggests that the halachic principle under discussion operates at the level of full ownership, a point that would require further elaboration beyond what this *Gemara* explores directly.

## Final Halacha

### Shmuel Ruling

The *Gemara* concludes with the question of practical *halacha*: do we rule like Rav or like Shmuel? The general principle is *halacha k'Shmuel b'dinei mamonot* — we follow Shmuel in monetary law. Although the dispute arises here in the context of *issur* — the prohibitions of *nedarim* — the *Ran* makes clear that the underlying debate is fundamentally one of *dinei mamonot*, concerning the nature of the wife's acquisitional relationship with her husband. Accordingly, the *Ran* cites the ruling of the *Nagid* Rabbeinu Shmuel HaLevi in the name of Rav Amram Gaon that the *halacha* follows Shmuel: *halacha k'Shmuel*. Furthermore, the *Ran* observes that Rav aligns with Rabbi Meir and Shmuel with the *Rabbanan* — the *Chachamim* — and that the general tendency of the *sugya* runs in Shmuel's direction. The practical consequence is that a father who gives his daughter a gift with an explicit condition that her husband shall have no rights over it — *al menat she'ein l'ba'alech reshut bahen* — that stipulation alone is halachically sufficient, and the elaborate formulation of *noseies v'notenet l'ficha* is not required.

### Alternative Positions

The *Ran* notes, however, that not all *poskim* accept this conclusion. There are authorities who rule like Rav and require the stricter formulation of the *mishna*. The precise scope of the debate — including how to read exactly what "do with it as you wish" implies and whether there are intermediate positions — is subject to further discussion among the *rishonim*. The *Ran* acknowledges these alternative views without fully resolving every dimension of the question.
