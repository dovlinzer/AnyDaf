# Bava Batra 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Migo vs. Chazaka

### Early Payment Claim

The *daf* opens by building on a conclusion established in the preceding discussion: there is a *chazaka* that people do not pay back a loan within the designated loan period — *chazaka lo avid inish deparei bego zimnei*. Ordinarily, the rule of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* governs debt disputes, placing the burden of proof on the claimant. But when a debtor claims he repaid within the loan period, this *chazaka* overrides his claim and he is not believed.

The *Gemara* now poses a new question: *tava'o achar zman ve'amar lo peraticha betoch zemani mahu?* — what is the *halacha* if the creditor makes his demand after the thirty days have elapsed, but the debtor responds not by saying he paid after the due date, but rather that he had already paid during the loan period, while the loan was not yet due? Had the debtor simply said he paid yesterday — that is, after the due date — he would clearly be believed, since that is a standard case of *kofer hakol* in which the rule of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* applies and he would at most be required to take a *shevuat hesset*.

### Migo Application

The question, then, is whether a *migo* can operate here. Since the debtor could have made the more advantageous claim — "I paid you after the loan came due" — and would have been believed, does that give him a *migo* to be believed even when he instead claims he paid earlier, during the loan period? In terms of *migo* logic, there is essentially no incentive to lie: it makes no practical difference to the debtor whether he says he paid last week or yesterday. Moreover, he is the one currently *muchzak* in the money. And yet the *Gemara* raises the counter-possibility: when a *migo* collides with a *chazaka* of this strength — the near-certain behavioral presumption that people simply do not repay within the loan period — perhaps even a *migo*, and even *muchzak* status, cannot overcome it.

### Wall Building Case

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this question by examining the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* teaches *bechezkat shenatan ad sheyavi re'aya shelo natan* — when a wall collapsed and both neighbors share an obligation to rebuild up to *arba amot*, and one party claims he already paid his share, he is presumed to have paid unless the other brings proof that he did not. The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario?

If the builder demanded payment after the wall was fully built and the debtor said he paid after construction was complete, that would be an obvious case of *kofer hakol* and there is no novelty in the *mishnah* believing him. *Ela lav* — the *mishnah* must therefore be addressing a more striking scenario: that the debtor claims he paid *during* the construction, while the wall was still being built — *de'amar lo peraticha betoch zemani*. And the *mishnah* believes him. If so, the *mishnah* would constitute a proof that *migo* does function even against this *chazaka*: since he could have said he paid after the wall was finished and been believed, that *migo* is enough to credit his claim of earlier payment.

The *Gemara* rejects this proof: *shani hacha dekol shafa veshafa zman hu* — the wall-building case is different, because here every brick laid creates a new obligation. The debt is not a single lump sum due at a fixed endpoint; it accrues continuously throughout construction. Every expenditure of labor and material generates an immediate obligation to reimburse, so there is no true *toch zman* — no period during which repayment would be premature. Since the debt is, in a sense, already owed at every moment, a claim of earlier payment is far more plausible, and the *mishnah*'s ruling does not bear on the question of whether *migo* can override a *chazaka* in the classic loan context.

### Higher Wall Analysis

The *Gemara* then attempts a proof from the second clause of the *mishnah*, which addresses the case of a wall built higher than *arba amot*. Beyond that height, there is no obligation arising from *hezek re'iyah*, so the neighbor cannot be compelled to contribute. However, the *mishnah* states that if the neighbor then builds a parallel wall — *samach lo kotel acher* — he demonstrates that he intends to make use of the additional height, and he becomes obligated to pay for it. In this case, the *mishnah* rules *bechezka shelo natan* — he is presumed not to have paid, and is not believed unless he brings proof.

The *Gemara* applies the same analytical framework: *ilima detav'o le'achar zmano ve'amar lo paraticha bizmani*, if he claimed he paid after the additional wall was complete, why would he not be believed? That would be an obvious *kofer hakol*. So *ela lav* — it must be that the *mishnah* is addressing the case where he claims he paid during construction, *de'amar paraticha betoch zmani*, and that is why he is not believed — *alma bemakom chazaka lo amrinan mah li lishaker* — we do not say *migo* against the *chazaka*.

The *Gemara* rejects this proof as well. The reason the debtor is not believed in the case of the higher wall has nothing to do with the *migo*-versus-*chazaka* question. Rather, *shani hacha de'amar mar mi yeimar demichayvu li rabanan* — the neighbor may simply not know that building a parallel wall obligates him to share the cost of the additional height. Since this is not common knowledge, the presumption is that he assumed he owed nothing and would not pay until a *beit din* ruled him obligated. Even once the obligation arises, he is presumed to be holding out until formally adjudicated. This explains the distinction between the first *arba amot* — where the obligation is well-known — and the higher portion of the wall. The *mishnah*'s ruling in this case therefore proves nothing about the *migo*-versus-*chazaka* problem.

### Baraita Evidence

Rav Acha the son of Rava then presents a *baraita* as a potential proof: *maneh li beyadecha* — if Reuven says to Shimon, "You owe me a hundred dinars," and Shimon admits it, and then the following day Reuven demands repayment — *im amar netativ lach patur*, if Shimon says he already paid, he is exempt. But if Shimon says *ein lacha beyadi*, there is nothing owed to you, he is liable, since this contradicts his own admission of the previous day.

The *Gemara* proposes an interpretation to make this *baraita* relevant: *mai lav netativ lach de'amar lei paraticha bizmani* — "I paid you" means he paid after the debt came due, and *ein lacha beyadi* means not a denial that he ever borrowed, but a claim that the debt never reached maturity because he had already prepaid it — *paraticha betoch zamni*. On this reading, the *baraita* would teach that this claim is not believed — proving that *migo* does not work against the *chazaka*.

### Denial Interpretation

The *Gemara* rejects this interpretation as well: *mai ein lacha beyadi lo hayu devarim me'olam* — the phrase *ein lacha beyadi* means simply a complete denial that there was ever a debt, not a claim of prepayment. And of course, if he is contradicting his own admission of the previous day, it is obvious he is not believed — there is no *chiddush* in that ruling.

The *Gemara* invokes the principle *de'amar mar kol ha'omer lo livisi ke'omer lo parati dami* — one who denies having borrowed at all is treated as if he declared he never repaid. *Tosafos* raises the question of why this principle is even needed here, given that the contradiction with yesterday's admission is self-evident. *Tosafos* explains: the principle is invoked to prevent Shimon from reinterpreting his own words — from claiming that when he said "I never borrowed," he really meant "I had already paid." The *Gemara* forecloses that interpretation; his denial is taken at face value. The *halacha* of *migo* against the *chazaka* of early repayment therefore remains unresolved.

## Shared Wall Rights

### Rav Huna Position

Returning now to the *mishnah*, the *Gemara* presents a dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman regarding what it means when one neighbor builds a partial parallel wall. Rav Huna states: *samach lepalga samach lekula* — if the neighbor built a parallel wall for only part of the height or only part of the length, we treat it as though he intends to use the entire wall. So if Reuven built a wall ten *amot* high and Shimon built a parallel wall only eight *amot* high, or built it along only half the length, Rav Huna holds Shimon liable for the full height and full length of Reuven's wall. The rationale is that by building any portion of a parallel wall, Shimon signals his intention to use the whole thing.

### Rav Nachman Position

Rav Nachman takes the more intuitive position: *lemai desamach samach lemai delo samach lo samach* — Shimon is obligated only for the portion he actually built parallel to. If he went up eight *amot*, he pays for eight *amot*; if he built along fifty feet, he pays for fifty feet. He does not become liable for anything he did not explicitly parallel.

### Edge Curling Exception

Both *Amoraim* agree on one exception. Rav Huna concedes — *modeh Rav Huna bekarna de-alufta* — that if Shimon curled the edge at the top or the end of his partial construction, this is a clear signal that he is not planning to extend further. As *Tosafos* explains, the curl at the edge demonstrates finality, and in such a case even Rav Huna would not obligate Shimon for the unbuilt portions.

Conversely, Rav Nachman agrees — *u-modeh Rav Nachman be-afriza u-be-kivasa de-kshurei* — that if Shimon took steps clearly indicating that he intends to continue building upward, such as laying a beam across the top in preparation for constructing a second story, then even Rav Nachman would hold him liable for the full height. The *afriza* is the preparatory beam laid across the top of the wall, and the *kivasa de-kshurei* refers to the channel or socket prepared to receive the crossbeams from the adjacent wall. These structural preparations make unmistakably clear that Shimon intends to use the entire height, and Rav Nachman would obligate him accordingly.

## Beam Holes Proof

### Holes as Proof

The *Gemara* now takes up a related question. Rav Huna states: *bei kivei lo havei chazaka* — holes made in the wall for crossbeams do not constitute a *chazaka*. Here the term *chazaka* refers not to the three-year *chazaka* of *Perek* 3, which involves a claim of purchase and requires three years of undisturbed possession, but rather to the type of *chazaka* in which a party's silence or acquiescence serves as evidence that rights were granted or a debt was settled. It is a much broader use of the term.

The case is as follows: Shimon built the shared wall to a height of, say, ten *amot*, and within the wall he made holes — and even inserted wooden paddings, *himlei*, within those holes — positioned precisely where Reuven would need to insert crossbeams if he were to build a parallel wall. Reuven argues: the fact that Shimon prepared these holes for me is proof that I had already paid him for my share of the extra height; otherwise, why would Shimon have accommodated me in this way?

### Anticipation Defense

The *Gemara* explains why this argument fails. *De'amar lei amina de-lechi payastei li lo lishat-ei ashitai* — Shimon can respond: "I anticipated that you would eventually build your wall and need to insert your crossbeams, and I made the holes in advance rather than waiting for you to pay me and then having to cut new holes into an already-completed wall, which would damage it." The preparation of the holes was prudent construction practice, not evidence of a prior payment. Reuven's claim that the holes prove he paid is therefore not accepted, and Rav Huna's ruling stands: *bei kivei lo havei chazaka*.

## Use Chazaka

### Use Rights Principle

Rav Nachman now turns to a different application of *chazaka* — establishing ongoing use rights through unprotested activity. The scenario is straightforward: Reuven's wall stands entirely within his own property, along the border. Shimon builds his own wall in his field and rests a crossbeam atop Reuven's wall. Reuven says nothing. Does Reuven's silence constitute an acquiescence that gives Shimon established rights to continue using the wall?

This raises a significant question debated among the *Rishonim*: is the silence alone sufficient to establish *chazaka*, or must the silence be backed by an explicit claim on Shimon's part that Reuven had formally granted him the rights — whether through sale, gift, or agreement? This distinction has far-reaching implications for questions involving tenants and neighbors.

### Light/Heavy Beams

Rav Nachman rules: *ahzik l'hurdi lo ahzik l'kshuri* — if Shimon established a *chazaka* by resting thin planks on Reuven's wall, that does not give him the right to rest heavy beams on it. Reuven can say: "I was tolerant of the light planks; I am not granting you permission to impose heavy beams on my wall." The converse, however, is the case: *l'kshuri ahzik l'hurdi* — if Shimon had established a *chazaka* with heavy beams and Reuven acquiesced, then implicitly Shimon also has rights to use the wall for light planks. Reuven's silence in the face of the greater imposition carries with it acceptance of the lesser one.

### Rav Yosef View

Rav Yosef disagrees: *ahzik l'hurdi ahzik l'kshuri* — even establishing a *chazaka* with thin planks gives Shimon rights to heavy beams as well, since the difference in burden on Reuven's wall is not so substantial as to require a separate grant of permission. A second version of the *Gemara* reports that Rav Nachman himself ultimately agreed that the rule operates symmetrically in both directions.

### Drainage Case

An analogous dispute is applied to the case of roof drainage. Rav Nachman states: *ahzik l'nitfi ahzik l'shifchi* — if Shimon established a *chazaka* for rain dripping off his roof in a dispersed pattern onto Reuven's field (*nitfi*), that also gives him the right to channel water through a drainage pipe (*shifchi*), since piped drainage is more concentrated but arguably more controlled. The reverse, however, does not follow: *ahzik l'shifchi lo ahzik l'nitfi* — a *chazaka* established through a drainage pipe does not give him rights to have water run off the roof indiscriminately in all directions across Reuven's field, which is a broader and less predictable form of intrusion.

Rav Nachman adds a further qualification: *aval l'tzrifa d'urbani lo* — even if *nitfi* establishes a *chazaka*, it does not extend to a steeply angled roof that channels rain in heavy concentrated streams, since that represents a significantly more severe incursion than ordinary dripping. Rav Yosef, however, rules: *afilu tzrifa d'urbani* — even the angled roof is included, and he in fact issued a practical ruling to that effect.

## Tenant Rights

### Base Extensions

Rav Nachman cites Rabba bar Avuha: *hamaskir bayis l'chavero b'bira gedola* — one who rents a room in a large building — the tenant may use the wall protrusions and the walls that extend beyond the immediate boundaries of his room, up to *arba amot*: *mishtamesh b'zizay u'b'kotlei ad arba amos*. These extensions are considered implicitly included in the rental. Similarly, *u'v'ovi hakosel bimkom shenahagu* — the thickness of the wall, which Rashi understands as the top surface of the wall, is also available for the tenant's use, where local custom supports it.

### Garden/Alley Rights

Beyond these basics, the *Gemara* records a dispute. The base ruling states *aval b'tarbitz apadni lo* — the tenant does not have rights to the garden in front of the building. Rav Nachman himself, however, disagrees: *afilu b'tarbitz apadni* — even the forecourt garden is included in the tenant's rights. As for the back alley behind the buildings — *aval rechova she-achorei ha-batim lo* — the initial ruling excludes this entirely. Rava extends the tenant's rights further still: *afilu rechova she-achorei ha-batim* — even the back alley is included.

## Chazaka Time Limit

### Thirty-Day Rule

Ravina now addresses the time threshold required to establish a *chazaka* of use. The case is a beam of a *sukkah* — here meaning a hut or temporary structure, not necessarily a *sukkah* of the *mitzvah* — resting on a neighbor's wall. *Ad taltin yomin lo hovei chazaka* — up to thirty days of unchallenged use does not establish a *chazaka*, since the owner may simply be tolerating it for a short period without intending to cede any rights. *Basar taltin yomin hovei chazaka* — beyond thirty days of silence, however, a *chazaka* is established.

It bears noting that this ruling, coming immediately after the discussions of *chazaka* of a few days and specifying thirty days rather than three years, makes clear that we are not dealing with the *chazaka* of purchase from *Perek* 3. This is an entirely distinct mechanism: the establishment of usage rights through unprotested activity.

### Sukkot Exception

An exception applies during *Sukkot*: if the structure is an actual *sukkah* of the *mitzvah*, the threshold is shortened. *Ad shiva yomin lo hovei chazaka* — up to seven days, the neighbor's silence is expected, since obviously he will tolerate a *sukkah* during the *chag*. *Basar shiva yomin hovei chazaka* — but if after the festival is over the *sukkah* remains and the neighbor still says nothing, a *chazaka* is established, since the normal time to dismantle it has passed. *Tosafos* notes that "seven days" should practically be understood as eight days — or nine for communities that observe *Simchat Torah* as well — since one does not dismantle the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Plastering Rule

A further rule: *v'ei chivreh b'tina l'altar hovei chazaka* — if the crossbeam was plastered into the wall and the neighbor saw this and said nothing, a *chazaka* is established immediately, without waiting for thirty days or seven. The permanence of the plastering and the neighbor's failure to react at that moment constitutes immediate establishment of rights. The *Rishonim* point to the word *la'altar* — "immediately" — as further proof that the thirty-day and seven-day rules discussed here are entirely separate from the three-year *chazaka* of purchase.

## Cross-Street Houses

### Property Setup

Abaye introduces a case involving two houses on opposite sides of a *reshut harabbim* — a public thoroughfare. The problem of *hezek re'iyah* arises between them, since each owner can see into the other's property. Since the public street separates the two properties, one cannot simply build a shared wall down the middle and divide the cost. The solution presented is: *zeh oseh makeh l'chatzi gago vezeh oseh makeh l'chatzi gago* — each party builds a parapet along half of his own roof, positioned so that they do not align with each other but rather overlap: *vezeh shelo keneged zeh umadif*. The overlap ensures that there is no angle of sight through the gap between them.

### Public vs. Private

The *Gemara* asks: *mai irya birshut harabbim* — why specify a public thoroughfare? Wouldn't the same problem and solution apply if the two houses were separated by a private street? The answer is that the *reshut harabbim* is the genuine *chiddush*. One might have thought that Reuven could argue: Shimon, you would have built a parapet anyway to shield yourself from the people passing below in the public domain, so my viewing you is irrelevant to your need for a wall — do not make me pay for half. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that this argument fails.

### Visibility Arguments

Shimon has compelling responses. First: *rabbim beyomama chazi li baleila lo chazi li* — the people in the street can see me in the daytime but not at night; I would not bother building for them alone. But Reuven can see me equally by day and by night, so I am building this wall on account of him. Second: *rabbim ki kyamina chazi li ki yasivna lo chazu li* — the public, being below, can only see me when I am standing; when I sit, they cannot see me. But Reuven, at the same height, sees me whether I am standing or sitting. Third: *rabbim ki me'aynu chazu li ki lo me'aynu lo chazu li* — the passersby must make a deliberate effort to look up intently; I would not be especially bothered by that. But Reuven sees me incidentally and without effort. On all three grounds, Shimon demonstrates that his need for a wall is driven specifically by Reuven's *hezek re'iyah*, not by the public's presence, and Reuven is therefore obligated to do his half.

### Half-Wall Method

The ruling itself — *zeh oseh makeh l'chatzi gago vezeh oseh makeh l'chatzi gago vezeh shelo keneged zeh umadif* — might seem obvious, and the *Gemara* acknowledges this: *peshita*. Each party builds half, and the overlap is also self-evidently necessary to prevent diagonal sightlines.

### Full Completion Question

The *Gemara* clarifies that the *chiddush* applies to a case where one of the parties went ahead and already completed his half: *lo tzericha dekadim chad mineihu ve'avid*. One might think that once one party has built his portion, the other can say: take your expenses from me and simply finish building the entire wall yourself — you're already there, just continue.

### Equal Obligation

The *Gemara* rejects this: *ka mashma lan d'amar lei at mai ta'ama lo avadat mishum demitra'e ashitech ana nami mitre'ina ashiti*. The party who has already built can respond: "Why have you not built your half? Because you do not want to impose the weight of a parapet on your own structure. Precisely the same consideration applies to me — I do not want to bear that weight either. The fact that I have already built my half does not change the fundamental principle: you are responsible for your half and I am responsible for mine." Each party must build his own portion on his own side. Prior completion by one party does not shift the obligation to complete the entire wall onto him, and the other party cannot buy his way out of building his own half.
