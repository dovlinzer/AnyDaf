# Pesachim 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chametz during Shechita

### Lo Tishchat Al Chametz

The daf opens by wrapping up the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* — the prohibition against *shechting* the *Korban Pesach*, or *korbanot* generally, while one still owns *chametz*. The scriptural basis is the verse *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi v'lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker*, which prohibits both the slaughter while *chametz* is present and the leaving over of the fats.

### Who Transgresses

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* from the bottom of 63b: *ha-shochet et ha-Pesach al ha-chametz over b'lo ta'aseh* — one who *shechts* the *Pesach* while *chametz* is owned transgresses a negative prohibition. Rabbi Yehuda extends this to include even the *tamid* on *erev Pesach*, while the Sages respond that the prohibition applies only to the *Korban Pesach* alone. The *baraita* then specifies whose *chametz* is relevant: that of the *shochet*, the *zorek*, or any member of the *chaburah*. If some stranger owns *chametz* at the other end of the world, that is not the group's concern. As for which act triggers the transgression, the *baraita* states: *v'echad ha-shochet v'echad ha-zorek v'echad ha-malik v'echad ha-mazeh chayav* — whether one performs *shechita*, *zerika*, *melika*, or the sprinkling of the blood in the context of a bird offering, one is liable. However, the *kometz* of a *minchah* does not trigger the prohibition — the verse says *al dam*, which excludes a *minchah* — and, according to this *baraita*, neither does *haktarah*.

### Melika vs. Haktarah

This *baraita* stands in tension with the earlier discussion. The earlier source had excluded *melika* but included *haktarah*; this *baraita* does the reverse, including *melika* and excluding *haktarah*. The Gemara observes: *kashya melika a-melika kashya haktara a-haktara* — there is a contradiction regarding *melika* and a contradiction regarding *haktarah*. But before resolving the inter-*baraita* conflict, the Gemara notes that this *baraita* is already internally problematic. It begins by declaring *lo amru ela b'Pesach bilvad* — the prohibition applies only to the *Korban Pesach* — yet immediately proceeds to discuss *melika*, which is the method of slaughter for birds, not an act performed in connection with the *Korban Pesach* at all. The Gemara answers: *ela ha v'ha Rabbi Shimon* — both *baraitot* reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon.

### Erev Pesach vs. Pesach

The resolution turns on Rabbi Shimon's distinctive position as stated in the *mishnah*: on the fourteenth, only the *Korban Pesach* triggers the prohibition — not the *tamid* and not any other *korban*. On *Pesach* itself, from the fifteenth through the end of *Yom Tov* including *Chol HaMoed*, any *korban* triggers the prohibition. With this in mind, the *melika* contradiction dissolves: *kan b'arba'ah asar kan b'cholo shel moed* — one *baraita* speaks of *erev Pesach*, where only the *Korban Pesach* is relevant, and the other speaks of *Chol HaMoed*, when all *korbanot* including bird offerings — which involve *melika* — are included. The first *baraita*'s statement that it applies only to the *Korban Pesach* addresses the question of which type of *korban* triggers liability on the fourteenth, while the second *baraita*'s enumeration of acts, including *melika*, concerns what acts trigger liability during the *chag* itself. The two *baraitot* are not addressing the same question, which explains why their conclusions appear to diverge. As for the *haktarah* contradiction — one *baraita* includes it and one excludes it — the Gemara resolves this simply as a debate among *tannaim*: *de'ika de-makish haktara li-shechita ve-ika de-man de-lo makish*. Whether the act of burning the *emurim* is included in the prohibition is a *machloket*, depending on whether one draws the analogy between *haktarah* and *shechita* from the second half of the verse, *v'lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker*.

### Zevach vs. Zivchay

Rabbi Yehuda's inclusion of the *tamid* rests on his reading of *mai zivchi* — "my sacrifice" — as referring to the sacrifice uniquely associated with the *mizbe'ach* at all times, which is the *tamid*: *olat tamid asuyah be-Har Sinai*. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, derives his position from the fact that the verse *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* appears twice in the Torah — once in *Mishpatim* and again following the episode of the *eigel*, when the *Aseret HaDibrot* and portions of *Mishpatim* are repeated. Rabbi Shimon reads the two appearances together: *kari beih zevach zivchay* — by moving the *yud* from one occurrence to the other, one instance is read as *zevach* (singular) and the other as *zivchay* (plural). *Zevach* — singular — refers to the single *korban* that matters on *erev Pesach*, namely the *Korban Pesach*. *Zivchay* — plural — refers to all *korbanot* brought during the *chag* itself. The Torah's deliberate doubling of the verse, rather than simply writing *zivchay* outright, signals a partition: *b'zman d'ika zevach lo mechayev a-zivchay* — when the singular *zevach* is in force (*erev Pesach*), one is liable only for that one; *b'zman d'leika zevach mechayev a-zivchay* — when that singular *zevach* is no longer in force (the *chag* itself), one becomes liable for all *korbanot*.

### Pesach Shelo Lishmo

Rabbi Shimon's position generates a further ruling: *u-b'moed lishmo patur* — if one *shechts* a *Korban Pesach* on the *moed* in its proper name as a *Pesach*, one is exempt from the prohibition. The reason is that a *Korban Pesach* slaughtered *lishmo* as a *Pesach* after the fourteenth is *pasul*, because *Pesach she-lo bi-zmano* is invalid. And Rabbi Shimon holds that *shechitah she-einah re'uyah lo shemai shechitah* — an act of *shechita* that is inherently unfit does not even register as a valid *shechita*, and therefore carries no liability. Conversely, if one *shechts* the animal *shelo lishmo* — designating it as a *shelamim* — it becomes a valid *zevach* and one would be liable.

This leads to a question: why does the *mishnah* say one needs to actively slaughter it *shelo lishmo* in order to make it a *shelamim*? The principle is that a *Pesach* in the rest of the year automatically becomes a *shelamim* — *pesach bi-sh'ar yemot ha-shanah shelamim havei*. If so, why is an active change of designation necessary? The Gemara entertains the inference that even after the fourteenth, a *Pesach* retains a submerged status as a *Pesach* and requires active uprooting — *akira* — to convert it. Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda reports that this question was resolved by the group in discussion: the ruling applies specifically to a case where the owners were *tumei meitim* and were therefore deferred to *Pesach Sheni*. In that case, the animal still genuinely awaits its proper time — the fourteenth of *Iyar* — and so it does not automatically convert to a *shelamim* during *Chol HaMoed*. An active *akira* is required. But where the owners have already fulfilled their obligation and this animal is simply a leftover, it automatically converts.

*Tosafot* reads the Gemara's question somewhat differently. According to *Tosafot*, the question is not merely why an active change is needed, but why *shechting* it *lishmo* as a *Pesach* would make it *pasul* at all. If after the fourteenth it is simply a *shelamim*, then *shechting* it in the name of a *Pesach* should merely render it a *shelamim shelo lishmo*, which is *kasher*. The only reason it would be *pasul* is if the *korban* retains some underlying *Pesach* identity that can be reawakened — so that *shechting* it *lishmo* as a *Pesach* actually does something, and that something is invalid. The Gemara's answer — that this is a special case involving animals held over for *Pesach Sheni* — explains why that dormant *Pesach* status persists and can be reactivated.

## Three Groups & Shechita

### Three Groups

The Gemara now turns to the *mishnah* describing the procedure of bringing the *Korban Pesach*, a welcome shift from abstract prohibition analysis to vivid Temple reality. *Ha-Pesach nishchat bi-shlosh kitot* — the *Pesach* is slaughtered in three groups. The scriptural basis is the verse *ve-shachatu oto kol kehal adat Yisrael bein ha-arbayim* — the three terms *kehal*, *edah*, and *Yisrael* each indicate a distinct group, and so the *Korban Pesach* is brought in three separate rotations throughout the afternoon of the fourteenth.

### Locking the Azarah

*Nichnesah kat rishonah* — the first group enters. *Nitmal'ah ha-azarah* — the *azarah* fills with those bringing their *korban*. At that point, *na'alu daltot ha-azarah* — the doors of the *azarah* are locked, signaling that the space is at capacity and the rest must wait for the next round. The *kohanim* then sounded *tekiah teruah tekiah* — a set of three *shofar* blasts — to mark the beginning of the service.

### Gold & Silver Bowls

*Ha-kohanim omdim shurot shurot* — the *kohanim* stand in rows. *U-veyadeihem bezichei chesef u-vezichei zahav* — in their hands are bowls of silver and bowls of gold. A row of silver bowls is entirely silver; a row of gold bowls is entirely gold. *Velo hayu me'uravim* — the two types are not intermingled within a row. And all of these bowls, *velo hayu la-bezichim shulayim*, have no base — they are rounded at the bottom and cannot be set down. The reason: *shema yanichum ve-yikaresh ha-dam* — lest the *kohanim* put a bowl down and forget it, allowing the blood to congeal and rendering *zrikat ha-dam* impossible. By making the bowls impossible to set down, the procedure ensures the blood is always in motion.

### Shechita & Blood Pass

The physical arrangement was a conveyor of sorts: *shachat Yisrael ve-kibel ha-Kohen* — a *Yisrael* performed the *shechita*, and a *Kohen* received the blood in a bowl. The Gemara will clarify that the *mishnah* is not saying a *Yisrael* must perform the *shechita* to the exclusion of a *Kohen*, but rather affirms that *shechita* by a non-*Kohen* is valid. However, the *Tosefta* quoted by *Tosafot* — *Yisrael shochtim et pischeihem u-Leviyim korim et ha-Hallel* — does suggest that in practice the owners themselves would perform the *shechita* while the *Leviyim* recited *Hallel*. The *mishnah* continues: *nosno l'chavero ve-chavero l'chavero* — the first *Kohen* passes the bowl to the next, who passes it to the one behind him. *U-mekabel et ha-male u-machazir et ha-reikan* — as one receives the full bowl coming toward the *mizbe'ach*, he simultaneously returns an empty bowl going back toward the point of *shechita*. This is a bucket brigade — a long line of *kohanim* passing blood forward and empty vessels backward in continuous motion, engaging as many *kohanim* as possible in the sacred service.

### Zrika Location

*Ha-Kohen ha-karov etzel ha-mizbe'ach zorko zrika achat keneged ha-yesod* — the *Kohen* nearest the *mizbe'ach* throws one *zrika* against the wall of the *mizbe'ach* opposite the base. This specification is meaningful because the *yesod* — the protruding base — did not run along all four sides. The *yesod* protruded only along the northern and western walls, not the southern and eastern ones. For a standard *korban*, blood is thrown at the two corners where two sides with a *yesod* meet — *shtayim she-hen arba*, two throws covering four walls. For the *Korban Pesach*, a single throw suffices, but it must be against one of the walls from which blood can run down to the *yesod*. The blood is thrown against the wall itself, and from there it runs down to the protruding base below.

### Hallel Recitation

While the *shechita* is underway, *Hallel* is being recited — as the *Tosefta* states, the *Leviyim* read *Hallel* while *Yisrael* perform the *shechita*. If the *Leviyim* completed *Hallel* and the group had still not finished bringing all their *korbanot*, they began *Hallel* a second time; if that too was completed, they began a third time. *Af al pi she-lo shilshelu mi-yemeihem* — in all of history, it never came to a third recitation. Rabbi Yehuda adds an observation about the third group in particular: *mi-yemeihem shel kat shlishit lo higu le-Ahavti ki yishma Hashem* — the third group was so small that they never even reached the passage *Ahavti ki yishma Hashem* in a single recitation of *Hallel*.

## Shabbat Pesach Procedure

### Flaying on Shabbat

*Ke-ma'aseihu be-chol kach ma'aseihu be-Shabbat* — the same procedure performed on a weekday is performed on *Shabbat*. One difference, however, involved the rinsing of the *azarah*. On a weekday, the *kohanim* would dam up the water channel flowing through the *azarah*, causing it to overflow and wash away the blood from the floor, after which the floor was squeegeed into the drainage holes. On *Shabbat* they would do this as well, *ella she-ha-kohanim madichin et ha-azarah she-lo birtson chachamim* — but the *chachamim* objected to this practice on *Shabbat*. The grounds for the objection will be discussed in the Gemara.

### Hooks & Poles

*Keitzad tolin u-mafshitin* — how did they suspend and flay the *Korban Pesach*? *Unkelyot shel barzel hayu kevi'im ba-ktalim u-va-amudim* — iron hooks were fixed into the walls and posts of the *Beit HaMitbachayim*, the slaughterhouse area of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the carcasses were hung from these hooks for *hafshata* (flaying). The *emurim* needed to be removed and burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and since this constituted the *avodah* of the *korban* rather than mere meat preparation, the *hafshata* was permitted even on *Shabbat*. For those who could not find an available hook, *maklot dakkim ve-chalakim hayu sham* — thin, smooth poles were kept on hand. *Meniach al ktefo ve-al ktef chaveiro* — one person would put the pole on his shoulder and his friend's shoulder, creating a human post from which the carcass was suspended for flaying.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Ruling

Rabbi Eliezer rules that when the fourteenth falls on *Shabbat*, even the smooth poles should not be used, and instead one uses human arms directly: *meniach yado al ktef chaveiro ve-yad chaveiro al ktefo* — placing one arm on his companion's shoulder and his companion's arm on his, forming a living crossbeam from which to suspend and flay the offering. The apparent concern is something akin to *muktzah* regarding the poles, even in the midst of all the other *Shabbat*-permitted *avodah*. The precise mechanics of how the carcass could be adequately supported by arms rather than poles remain somewhat difficult to picture given the weight of the animal.

### Innards & First Group

*Kar'o* — they split the carcass. *Hotzi et emurav* — the innards and fats were removed. *Netano ba-magis* — placed in a pan. *Hiktiru al gabbe ha-mizbe'ach* — and burned on the *mizbe'ach*. This completed the *avodah* of that group.

### Groups' Locations

On a weekday, after completing their *korbanot*, the first group simply went home. On *Shabbat*, however, carrying is prohibited and one cannot leave *Yerushalayim* with the carcass until nightfall. The *mishnah* therefore describes where each group waited. *Yatz'ta kat rishonah ve-yashvah lah b'Har HaBayit* — the first group went out and sat on *Har HaBayit*, which was enclosed by the outer wall. *Shniyah ba-cheil* — the second group waited in the *cheil*, the narrow corridor between the outer *soreg* barrier and the *azarah* walls. *Ha-shlishit bi-mkomah omedet* — the third group remained standing in the *azarah* itself. *Chashecha* — when darkness fell, they all went out and roasted their *Korban Pesach* at their lodgings in *Yerushalayim*.

## Minimum Pesach Size

### 30 per Group Rule

Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *ein ha-Pesach nishchat ela bi-shlosh kitot shel shloshim shloshim bnei adam* — the *Pesach* may not be slaughtered unless there are three groups of thirty people each. The reasoning derives from the same verse: the three terms *kehal*, *edah*, and *Yisrael* each imply a minimum quorum of ten, yielding thirty. Rabbi Yitzchak is uncertain whether the Torah means thirty people must be present simultaneously, or whether it requires three separate groups of ten brought in sequence. *Hilkhakh ba'inan shalosh kitot shel shloshim shloshim bnei adam* — to satisfy both possibilities, he requires three groups of thirty each. However, the Gemara points out that this need not mean ninety people altogether. *Chamishim nami sagi* — fifty suffice. The first group of thirty enters and performs the *shechita*, satisfying the requirement of thirty at one time. Then ten of them leave and ten newcomers enter, forming a second group of ten at a new time — but the twenty remaining from the first group still make up a group of thirty alongside them. Then another ten of those original thirty exit and the final ten newcomers enter, again reaching thirty with the lingering members. This overlapping arrangement covers both possibilities with only fifty individuals.

### Miracle vs. Manual Lock

The *mishnah* says the doors of the *azarah* were locked once the first group entered and filled the space. A dispute between Abaye and Rava concerns the nature of this locking. Abaye holds *ninalu* — the doors locked themselves, miraculously, of their own accord. The procedure was to allow people to continue entering for as long as they could, and one relied on the miracle that the doors would seal themselves when capacity was reached: *d'samchinan a-nisa*. Rava holds *no'alin* — human beings actively monitored the incoming crowd and locked the doors manually: *v'lo samchinan a-nisa*.

### Akavya Story

This same debate is applied to the story of Akavya ben Mahalalel. In a separate context in the Gemara, there is a claim that Akavya was placed in *niduy* after he disputed a ruling of Shemaya and Avtaliyon regarding whether a freed slave woman would undergo the *Sotah* process. Rabbi Yehuda responds: *chas v'shalom she-Akavya ben Mahalalel nitnade* — God forbid that Akavya ben Mahalalel was ever put in *niduy* — for *she-ein azarah nin'elet al kol adam b'Yisrael* — when the doors of the *azarah* were sealed on *erev Pesach* with all of Israel packed within, there was not to be found among all those people anyone equal *b'chokhmah u-v'yirat cheit* to Akavya ben Mahalalel. The phrase *azarah nin'elet* is thus read as an expression of the totality of Israel assembled.

Abaye and Rava each explain this phrase in accordance with their respective positions: Abaye reads *nin'elet* as the doors locking themselves — in that miraculous moment when the *azarah* sealed itself, there was no one within equal to Akavya. Rava reads *no'alim otah* — when they locked the *azarah* actively, among all those assembled there was no one his equal. The *masorah* does not decisively favor one reading over the other.

## Agrippas' Census

### Crushing Incident

The Gemara states: *me'olam lo nitma'eikh adam ba-azarah* — no one was ever crushed in the *azarah* despite the extraordinary crowding. The one exception: *chutz mi-Pesach echad she-hayah bi-ymei Hillel* — except for one *Pesach* in the time of Hillel, when an elderly person was crushed. That event was remembered and the *Pesach* of that year was called *Pesach Me'uchin* — the crushed *Pesach*. The Gemara notes that the report of this single exception actually strengthens the claim that it otherwise never happened.

### Kidney Count

Another *baraita* reports that King Agrippas — one of the kings at the close of the *Bayit Sheni* period — wished to gain a sense of the vast numbers of the Jewish people he ruled. He instructed the *Kohen Gadol* to count the *Pesachim* as a means of census. *Natlu kulya mi-kol echad* — a kidney was taken from each *Pesach* offering as a marker. *Ve-nimtze'u sham shishim ribo zugei kelyot* — six hundred thousand pairs of kidneys were found, meaning 1.2 million *Pesachim*. Since each *Pesach* was shared by at minimum ten people, this implies a population of twelve million — *kiflayim ke-yotzei Mitzrayim*, double those who left Egypt. The *baraita* notes that this count excluded those who were *tamei* or who were on a distant journey and therefore did not bring a *Pesach*. That year's gathering was called *Pesach Me'uvin* — the thick or dense *Pesach* — in contrast to the *Pesach Me'uchin* of Hillel's day.

### Capacity Analysis

This figure invites scrutiny. The *azarah* in front of the *mizbe'ach* — the *ezrat Yisrael* — was 135 *amot* long by 11 *amot* wide, yielding approximately 1,400 to 1,500 square *amot*. Even allowing two or three people per square *amah* in extremely crowded conditions, the space could hold at most several thousand people at a time. With three groups rotating, the total might reach fifteen thousand individuals at the outside. The figure of twelve million adult males is plainly beyond any physical capacity the *azarah* could accommodate — even accounting for the well-known principle of *omdim tzefufim u-mishtachavim b'revach*. The story is clearly not intended as a precise census.

### Kidney Marking

The Gemara raises a technical difficulty with Agrippas' method. The kidney must be burned on the *mizbe'ach* — *ha-ketiv haktarah*. How could it be removed and used as a counting token? The Gemara answers: *de-hadar maktru lehu* — after the count, they were returned and burned. But a further difficulty arises: the verse *ve-hiktiram* implies all the *emurim* of a single *korban* must be burned together, not separately. And another source says *she-kulan k'achat* — all together. How then can a kidney be temporarily separated and later returned? The Gemara resolves: *ella tefisa b'alma* — the kidney was not actually removed, but merely grasped and held up as a marker, *de-shakil mineihu*, while another indicator was used for the count, and then the kidney was immediately released back with the rest of the *korban*'s *emurim*.

## Practical Details

### Bowls Without Bases

A *baraita* clarifies that the practice of making bowls without a base was not unique to the blood-collection procedure of the *Korban Pesach* but was universal in the *Mikdash*: *kol ha-bazichin she-ba-Mikdash lo hayu lahem shulayim* — all the bowls in the *Mikdash* had no base, always for the same reason of preventing blood from congealing if set down. The sole exception: *chutz mi-vazichei levonah shel lechem ha-panim* — the spoons used to hold the *levonah* (frankincense) placed on the *shulchan* alongside the *lechem ha-panim*. Those needed to lie flat on the *shulchan* for an entire week. If they had no base, they would have to lean against the bread, and *v'yipresu ha-lechem* — they would crack the delicately made loaves.

### Aesthetic Arrangement

The question of why the gold and silver bowls are not intermingled within a single row is addressed by the Gemara. One might suggest that the concern is a kind of inequitable exchange — a *Kohen* might hand back a gold bowl and receive a silver one, or hand back one worth two hundred *zuz* and receive one worth only a hundred. But the Gemara dismisses this: that imbalance is always possible regardless of arrangement, since any two bowls of the same material can differ in value. Rather, *ella de-hachi shapir tefei* — the reason is simply aesthetic. A row of uniform silver and a row of uniform gold is more beautiful and dignified than a mixed arrangement.

### Transport Without Feet

The *mishnah*'s description of the bucket-brigade procedure — *nosno l'chavero ve-chavero l'chavero* — raises a significant *halachic* question. One of the *avodot* of blood service is *holacha*, the act of transporting the blood to the *mizbe'ach*. But in this procedure, no individual *Kohen* actually walks anywhere. Each stands in place and merely passes the bowl to the next person. Does *holacha she-lo be-regel* — transport without the use of one's feet — constitute a valid *holacha*? The Gemara offers the possibility that *dilma dahani purta* — perhaps the last *Kohen* in the line, the one adjacent to the *mizbe'ach*, took a small step before performing the *zrika*, which would satisfy the requirement of *holacha* through actual movement.

### Honor in Procedure

But if the question of *holacha* is resolved so simply, what is the *mishnah*'s purpose in elaborating the entire bucket-brigade procedure at all? The Gemara answers: *ha kamashma lan b'rov am hadrat melech* — it teaches us the principle that in the presence of a multitude, there is glory to the King. The point of involving so many *kohanim* in the passage of the blood is not to solve a *halachic* problem of transport but to maximize the honor and beauty of the sacred service by engaging as many participants as possible. The greater the number of *kohanim* involved in even a single act of *avodah*, the more magnificent the event becomes.
