# Pesachim 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chametz during Shechita

### Lo Tishchat Al Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חֲמֵצְךָ דָּם זִבְחִי וְלֹא יָלִין חֵלֶב חָגִי עַד בֹקֶר
>
> **Translation:** You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread, nor shall the fat of My festival offering be left overnight until morning.

The daf opens by wrapping up the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* — the prohibition against *shechting* the *Korban Pesach*, or *korbanot* generally, while one still owns *chametz*. The scriptural basis is the verse *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi v'lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker*, which prohibits both the slaughter while *chametz* is present and the leaving over of the fats.

### Who Transgresses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל הֶחָמֵץ — עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא לַשּׁוֹחֵט אוֹ לַזּוֹרֵק אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה. הָיָה לְאֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם — אֵין זָקוּק לוֹ. וְאֶחָד הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְאֶחָד הַזּוֹרֵק וְאֶחָד הַמַּקְטִיר — חַיָּיב. אֲבָל הַמּוֹלֵק אֶת הָעוֹף בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **One who slaughters the Paschal lamb with leavened bread** in his possession **transgresses a negative commandment. When? When** the leaven belongs **to the slaughterer, or to the one who sprinkles** the blood, **or to one of the members of the group.** If leaven belonged **to someone at the end of the world, he is not bound to him,** meaning that the slaughterer need not take him into account. **And** whether **he slaughters** the animal, **or he sprinkles** the blood, **or he burns** the fats, he is **liable. But one who pinches** the neck of **a bird,** which is not a Paschal offering but a burnt-offering or sin-offering, if he does so with leaven in his possession **on the fourteenth** of Nisan, after the prohibition against owning leaven has taken effect, **he does not transgress anything.**

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* from the bottom of 63b: *ha-shochet et ha-Pesach al ha-chametz over b'lo ta'aseh* — one who *shechts* the *Pesach* while *chametz* is owned transgresses a negative prohibition. Rabbi Yehuda extends this to include even the *tamid* on *erev Pesach*, while the Sages respond that the prohibition applies only to the *Korban Pesach* alone. The *baraita* then specifies whose *chametz* is relevant: that of the *shochet*, the *zorek*, or any member of the *chaburah*. If some stranger owns *chametz* at the other end of the world, that is not the group's concern. As for which act triggers the transgression, the *baraita* states: *v'echad ha-shochet v'echad ha-zorek v'echad ha-malik v'echad ha-mazeh chayav* — whether one performs *shechita*, *zerika*, *melika*, or the sprinkling of the blood in the context of a bird offering, one is liable. However, the *kometz* of a *minchah* does not trigger the prohibition — the verse says *al dam*, which excludes a *minchah* — and, according to this *baraita*, neither does *haktarah*.

### Melika vs. Haktarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא מְלִיקָה אַמְּלִיקָה, קַשְׁיָא הַקְטָרָה אַהַקְטָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** There seems to be **a contradiction between** the ruling in the first *baraita* with regard to **pinching** and the ruling in the second *baraita* with regard to **pinching.** In addition, there seems to be **a contradiction** between the ruling in the first *baraita* with regard to **burning** and the ruling in the second *baraita* with regard to **burning.** The first *baraita* rules that one who burns the sacrificial parts is included in the prohibition but that one who pinches the neck of a bird-offering is not, and the second *baraita* says the opposite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. מְלִיקָה אַמְּלִיקָה לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, כָּאן — בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say instead that both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* express the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** And **there is no contradiction between** the ruling in the first *baraita* about **pinching** and the ruling in the second *baraita* about **pinching: Here,** where it says that one who pinches does not transgress, it is referring **to the fourteenth** of Nisan, while **there,** where it says that one who pinches does transgress, it is talking about **the intermediate days of the festival** of Passover.

This *baraita* stands in tension with the earlier discussion. The earlier source had excluded *melika* but included *haktarah*; this *baraita* does the reverse, including *melika* and excluding *haktarah*. The Gemara observes: *kashya melika a-melika kashya haktara a-haktara* — there is a contradiction regarding *melika* and a contradiction regarding *haktarah*. But before resolving the inter-*baraita* conflict, the Gemara notes that this *baraita* is already internally problematic. It begins by declaring *lo amru ela b'Pesach bilvad* — the prohibition applies only to the *Korban Pesach* — yet immediately proceeds to discuss *melika*, which is the method of slaughter for birds, not an act performed in connection with the *Korban Pesach* at all. The Gemara answers: *ela ha v'ha Rabbi Shimon* — both *baraitot* reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon.

### Erev Pesach vs. Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקְטָרָה אַהַקְטָרָה נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא — תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּאִיכָּא דְּמַקֵּישׁ הַקְטָרָה לִשְׁחִיטָה, וְאִיכָּא מַאן דְּלָא מַקֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** There is also **no contradiction between** the ruling in the first *baraita* about **burning** and the ruling in the second *baraita* about **burning,** as the matter **is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon: **There is one who compares burning to slaughtering,** as Rav Pappa did, **and there is one who does not compare** them.

The resolution turns on Rabbi Shimon's distinctive position as stated in the *mishnah*: on the fourteenth, only the *Korban Pesach* triggers the prohibition — not the *tamid* and not any other *korban*. On *Pesach* itself, from the fifteenth through the end of *Yom Tov* including *Chol HaMoed*, any *korban* triggers the prohibition. With this in mind, the *melika* contradiction dissolves: *kan b'arba'ah asar kan b'cholo shel moed* — one *baraita* speaks of *erev Pesach*, where only the *Korban Pesach* is relevant, and the other speaks of *Chol HaMoed*, when all *korbanot* including bird offerings — which involve *melika* — are included. The first *baraita*'s statement that it applies only to the *Korban Pesach* addresses the question of which type of *korban* triggers liability on the fourteenth, while the second *baraita*'s enumeration of acts, including *melika*, concerns what acts trigger liability during the *chag* itself. The two *baraitot* are not addressing the same question, which explains why their conclusions appear to diverge. As for the *haktarah* contradiction — one *baraita* includes it and one excludes it — the Gemara resolves this simply as a debate among *tannaim*: *de'ika de-makish haktara li-shechita ve-ika de-man de-lo makish*. Whether the act of burning the *emurim* is included in the prohibition is a *machloket*, depending on whether one draws the analogy between *haktarah* and *shechita* from the second half of the verse, *v'lo yalin chelev chagi ad boker*.

### Zevach vs. Zivchay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַתָּמִיד וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר לָךְ: ״זִבְחִי״ — זֶבַח הַמְיוּחָד לִי, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? תָּמִיד.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One is liable **even for** slaughtering **the daily** afternoon **offering** on the eve of Passover with leaven in his possession. **What is the reason for Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion? The Gemara answers: **He** could have **said to you** that the verse states: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread" (Exodus 34:25). **"My sacrifice"** indicates **the sacrifice that is designated to Me; and what is it?** This is referring to **the daily offering,** which is a burnt-offering brought each day as part of the Temple service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַפֶּסַח בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דִּכְתִיב ״זִבְחִי״ ״זִבְחִי״ תְּרֵי זִמְנֵי, קְרִי בֵּיהּ: ״זֶבַח, זְבָחַיי״.
>
> **Translation:** It was further stated in the mishna that **Rabbi Shimon says: On the fourteenth** of Nisan one is liable for sacrificing **the Paschal lamb** with leaven in his possession, but he is not liable for sacrificing other offerings with leaven; however, during the Festival one is liable for sacrificing any offering with leaven in his possession. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for Rabbi Shimon's** opinion? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "My sacrifice [*zivḥi*]," "My sacrifice [*zivḥi*]," two times,** as first it is written: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice [*zivḥi*] with leavened bread" (Exodus 23:18) and then it is written again: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice [*zivḥi*] with leavened bread" (Exodus 34:25). If the two instances of the word *zivḥi* are combined, their letters may be rearranged to **read: A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*],** and: **My sacrifices [*zevaḥai*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא פַּלְגִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא מֵהֲדָדֵי וְלָא כְּתַב ״זְבָחַיי״? לְמֵימַר: בִּזְמַן דְּאִיכָּא זֶבַח — לָא מִחַיַּיב אַזְּבָחַיי. בִּזְמַן דְּלֵיכָּא זֶבַח — מִחַיַּיב אַזְּבָחַיי.
>
> **Translation:** **To** teach **what *halakha* does the Merciful One divide them** from one another **and not** explicitly **write: My sacrifices?** It was **to say** that **at the time when there is a sacrifice,** meaning at the time of the Paschal lamb, as mentioned in the continuation of the verse, **one is not liable for My sacrifices.** On the eve of Passover, when the Paschal lamb is brought, there is no liability for offering other sacrifices with leaven. However, **at a time when there is no sacrifice,** during the festival of Passover, **one is liable** for slaughtering any of **My sacrifices** with leaven in his possession.

Rabbi Yehuda's inclusion of the *tamid* rests on his reading of *mai zivchi* — "my sacrifice" — as referring to the sacrifice uniquely associated with the *mizbe'ach* at all times, which is the *tamid*: *olat tamid asuyah be-Har Sinai*. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, derives his position from the fact that the verse *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* appears twice in the Torah — once in *Mishpatim* and again following the episode of the *eigel*, when the *Aseret HaDibrot* and portions of *Mishpatim* are repeated. Rabbi Shimon reads the two appearances together: *kari beih zevach zivchay* — by moving the *yud* from one occurrence to the other, one instance is read as *zevach* (singular) and the other as *zivchay* (plural). *Zevach* — singular — refers to the single *korban* that matters on *erev Pesach*, namely the *Korban Pesach*. *Zivchay* — plural — refers to all *korbanot* brought during the *chag* itself. The Torah's deliberate doubling of the verse, rather than simply writing *zivchay* outright, signals a partition: *b'zman d'ika zevach lo mechayev a-zivchay* — when the singular *zevach* is in force (*erev Pesach*), one is liable only for that one; *b'zman d'leika zevach mechayev a-zivchay* — when that singular *zevach* is no longer in force (the *chag* itself), one becomes liable for all *korbanot*.

### Pesach Shelo Lishmo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַמּוֹעֵד לִשְׁמוֹ פָּטוּר וְכוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, הָא סְתָמָא — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that according to Rabbi Shimon, if one sacrificed a Paschal lamb **during** the intermediate days of **the Festival for its** own **purpose** as a Paschal lamb, **one is exempt** from liability if he had leaven in his possession during the time of the slaughter, as it is an invalid offering. However, if he sacrificed it for a different purpose, he is liable, because a Paschal lamb that is slaughtered at a time other than Passover eve is valid as a peace-offering. The Gemara analyzes this ruling: **The reason** he is liable is **that** he slaughtered it explicitly **for a different purpose; but** had he slaughtered it **without** specific **intent** he would be **exempt,** because the offering would be disqualified. Unless he specifically states otherwise, the animal retains its status as a Paschal lamb and is subsequently disqualified because it is not the proper time to sacrifice a Paschal lamb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה שְׁלָמִים הָוֵי! שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Why** must he expressly state that he is sacrificing it as a different offering? Isn't **a Paschal lamb** sacrificed **during the rest of the days of the year** presumed to be **a peace-offering?** There should be no need to state explicitly that it is a different offering. **Learn from this that a Paschal lamb** sacrificed **on the rest of the days of the year requires uprooting,** i.e., one must explicitly declare that he is sacrificing the offering as a peace-offering, and not as a Paschal lamb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמְדָּא: נִזְרְקָה מִפִּי חֲבוּרָה, וְאָמְרוּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעָלִים טְמֵאֵי מֵת, וְנִדְחִין לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, דִּסְתָמֵיהּ לְשׁוּם פֶּסַח קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda said:** A group of scholars were studying this issue and **it emerged from the group,** that is, one of the scholars made a statement with which the rest of them agreed, and it was subsequently forgotten who had made the statement, **and they said** as follows: We are dealing here with a case **where the owners** of the offering **were ritually impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse** during the first Passover, **and they were deferred to the second Passover.** A person who was ritually impure or on a distant journey and therefore failed to bring the Paschal lamb at its proper time on the fourteenth of Nisan must compensate by bringing the offering on the fourteenth of Iyyar. In such a situation, the offering **presumably stands** to be sacrificed **as a Paschal lamb,** and it does not automatically become a peace-offering unless its owners explicitly declare it as such. However, this would not be true of other Paschal lambs after the time to offer the Paschal lamb has passed.

Rabbi Shimon's position generates a further ruling: *u-b'moed lishmo patur* — if one *shechts* a *Korban Pesach* on the *moed* in its proper name as a *Pesach*, one is exempt from the prohibition. The reason is that a *Korban Pesach* slaughtered *lishmo* as a *Pesach* after the fourteenth is *pasul*, because *Pesach she-lo bi-zmano* is invalid. And Rabbi Shimon holds that *shechitah she-einah re'uyah lo shemai shechitah* — an act of *shechita* that is inherently unfit does not even register as a valid *shechita*, and therefore carries no liability. Conversely, if one *shechts* the animal *shelo lishmo* — designating it as a *shelamim* — it becomes a valid *zevach* and one would be liable.

This leads to a question: why does the *mishnah* say one needs to actively slaughter it *shelo lishmo* in order to make it a *shelamim*? The principle is that a *Pesach* in the rest of the year automatically becomes a *shelamim* — *pesach bi-sh'ar yemot ha-shanah shelamim havei*. If so, why is an active change of designation necessary? The Gemara entertains the inference that even after the fourteenth, a *Pesach* retains a submerged status as a *Pesach* and requires active uprooting — *akira* — to convert it. Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda reports that this question was resolved by the group in discussion: the ruling applies specifically to a case where the owners were *tumei meitim* and were therefore deferred to *Pesach Sheni*. In that case, the animal still genuinely awaits its proper time — the fourteenth of *Iyar* — and so it does not automatically convert to a *shelamim* during *Chol HaMoed*. An active *akira* is required. But where the owners have already fulfilled their obligation and this animal is simply a leftover, it automatically converts.

*Tosafot* reads the Gemara's question somewhat differently. According to *Tosafot*, the question is not merely why an active change is needed, but why *shechting* it *lishmo* as a *Pesach* would make it *pasul* at all. If after the fourteenth it is simply a *shelamim*, then *shechting* it in the name of a *Pesach* should merely render it a *shelamim shelo lishmo*, which is *kasher*. The only reason it would be *pasul* is if the *korban* retains some underlying *Pesach* identity that can be reawakened — so that *shechting* it *lishmo* as a *Pesach* actually does something, and that something is invalid. The Gemara's answer — that this is a special case involving animals held over for *Pesach Sheni* — explains why that dormant *Pesach* status persists and can be reactivated.

## Three Groups & Shechita

### Three Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח נִשְׁחַט בְּשָׁלֹשׁ כִּתּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחֲטוּ אוֹתוֹ כֹּל קְהַל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, קָהָל, וְעֵדָה, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל. נִכְנְסָה כַּת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, נִתְמַלְּאָה הָעֲזָרָה, נָעֲלוּ דַּלְתוֹת הָעֲזָרָה, תָּקְעוּ הֵרִיעוּ וְתָקְעוּ,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The Paschal lamb was slaughtered in three groups,** meaning those bringing the offering were divided into three separate sets, **as it is stated: "And the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall slaughter it** in the afternoon" (Exodus 12:6). The verse is interpreted as referring to three groups: **Assembly, congregation, and Israel.** The procedure for sacrificing the offering was as follows: **The first group** of people sacrificing the offering **entered,** and when **the Temple courtyard became filled** with them **they closed the doors of the Temple courtyard. They sounded uninterrupted, broken, and uninterrupted** trumpet **blasts,** as was done while sacrificing any offering.

The Gemara now turns to the *mishnah* describing the procedure of bringing the *Korban Pesach*, a welcome shift from abstract prohibition analysis to vivid Temple reality. *Ha-Pesach nishchat bi-shlosh kitot* — the *Pesach* is slaughtered in three groups. The scriptural basis is the verse *ve-shachatu oto kol kehal adat Yisrael bein ha-arbayim* — the three terms *kehal*, *edah*, and *Yisrael* each indicate a distinct group, and so the *Korban Pesach* is brought in three separate rotations throughout the afternoon of the fourteenth.

### Locking the Azarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּהֲנִים עוֹמְדִים שׁוּרוֹת שׁוּרוֹת, וּבִידֵיהֶם בְּזִיכִי כֶסֶף וּבְזִיכֵי זָהָב. שׁוּרָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ כֶּסֶף כֶּסֶף, וְשׁוּרָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ זָהָב זָהָב, לֹא הָיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין. וְלֹא הָיוּ לַבָּזִיכִין שׁוּלַיִים, שֶׁמָּא יַנִּיחוּם וְיִקָּרֵשׁ הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **The priests stood in rows** from the place of slaughter to the altar, **and in their hands** they held **bowls [*bezikhin*] of silver and bowls of gold** in order to receive the blood of the offerings. There was **a row entirely** composed of priests holding **silver** bowls, **and a row entirely** composed of priests holding **gold** bowls, as the gold and silver bowls **were not mixed** in the same row. **The bowls did not have** flat **bases** that would allow them to be put down, out of concern that **perhaps** the priests would **set them down** and forget about them **and** in the meantime **the blood would congeal** and become disqualified for sprinkling on the altar.

*Nichnesah kat rishonah* — the first group enters. *Nitmal'ah ha-azarah* — the *azarah* fills with those bringing their *korban*. At that point, *na'alu daltot ha-azarah* — the doors of the *azarah* are locked, signaling that the space is at capacity and the rest must wait for the next round. The *kohanim* then sounded *tekiah teruah tekiah* — a set of three *shofar* blasts — to mark the beginning of the service.

### Gold & Silver Bowls

*Ha-kohanim omdim shurot shurot* — the *kohanim* stand in rows. *U-veyadeihem bezichei chesef u-vezichei zahav* — in their hands are bowls of silver and bowls of gold. A row of silver bowls is entirely silver; a row of gold bowls is entirely gold. *Velo hayu me'uravim* — the two types are not intermingled within a row. And all of these bowls, *velo hayu la-bezichim shulayim*, have no base — they are rounded at the bottom and cannot be set down. The reason: *shema yanichum ve-yikaresh ha-dam* — lest the *kohanim* put a bowl down and forget it, allowing the blood to congeal and rendering *zrikat ha-dam* impossible. By making the bowls impossible to set down, the procedure ensures the blood is always in motion.

### Shechita & Blood Pass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְקִבֵּל הַכֹּהֵן. נוֹתְנוֹ לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וַחֲבֵירוֹ לַחֲבֵירוֹ. וּמְקַבֵּל אֶת הַמָּלֵא, וּמַחֲזִיר אֶת הָרֵיקָן. כֹּהֵן הַקָּרוֹב אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ זוֹרְקוֹ זְרִיקָה אַחַת כְּנֶגֶד הַיְּסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **An Israelite would slaughter** the sacrifice, **and a priest would receive** the blood and immediately **hand it to another** priest standing next to him, **and the other** priest would pass it **to another.** Each priest would **receive a full** bowl of blood from the priest next to him **and return** to him **an empty** bowl being passed in the opposite direction, the contents of which had already been sprinkled on the altar. The **priest who was closest to the altar would sprinkle a single sprinkling** of blood **against the base** of the altar, i.e., against the north and west sides of the altar, where there was a base.

The physical arrangement was a conveyor of sorts: *shachat Yisrael ve-kibel ha-Kohen* — a *Yisrael* performed the *shechita*, and a *Kohen* received the blood in a bowl. The Gemara will clarify that the *mishnah* is not saying a *Yisrael* must perform the *shechita* to the exclusion of a *Kohen*, but rather affirms that *shechita* by a non-*Kohen* is valid. However, the *Tosefta* quoted by *Tosafot* — *Yisrael shochtim et pischeihem u-Leviyim korim et ha-Hallel* — does suggest that in practice the owners themselves would perform the *shechita* while the *Leviyim* recited *Hallel*. The *mishnah* continues: *nosno l'chavero ve-chavero l'chavero* — the first *Kohen* passes the bowl to the next, who passes it to the one behind him. *U-mekabel et ha-male u-machazir et ha-reikan* — as one receives the full bowl coming toward the *mizbe'ach*, he simultaneously returns an empty bowl going back toward the point of *shechita*. This is a bucket brigade — a long line of *kohanim* passing blood forward and empty vessels backward in continuous motion, engaging as many *kohanim* as possible in the sacred service.

### Zrika Location

*Ha-Kohen ha-karov etzel ha-mizbe'ach zorko zrika achat keneged ha-yesod* — the *Kohen* nearest the *mizbe'ach* throws one *zrika* against the wall of the *mizbe'ach* opposite the base. This specification is meaningful because the *yesod* — the protruding base — did not run along all four sides. The *yesod* protruded only along the northern and western walls, not the southern and eastern ones. For a standard *korban*, blood is thrown at the two corners where two sides with a *yesod* meet — *shtayim she-hen arba*, two throws covering four walls. For the *Korban Pesach*, a single throw suffices, but it must be against one of the walls from which blood can run down to the *yesod*. The blood is thrown against the wall itself, and from there it runs down to the protruding base below.

### Hallel Recitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה כַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה, וְנִכְנְסָה כַּת שְׁנִיָּה. יָצְתָה שְׁנִיָּה, נִכְנְסָה שְׁלִישִׁית. כְּמַעֲשֵׂה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה כָּךְ מַעֲשֵׂה הַשְּׁנִיָּה וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית. קָרְאוּ אֶת הַהַלֵּל. אִם גָּמְרוּ — שָׁנוּ, וְאִם שָׁנוּ — שִׁלֵּשׁוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁלְּשׁוּ מִימֵיהֶם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִימֵיהֶם שֶׁל כַּת שְׁלִישִׁית לֹא הִגִּיעוּ לְ״אָהַבְתִּי כִּי יִשְׁמַע ה׳״, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעַמָּהּ מוּעָטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The first group exited** upon completion of the rite, **and the second group entered; the second group left** upon completion of its rite, **and the third group entered. As it was done by the first** group, **so was it done by the second and third** groups. All the people standing in the Temple courtyard while the Paschal lambs were being slaughtered would **recite *hallel*. If they finished** reciting it before all the offerings were slaughtered, **they** recited it **a second time, and if they** finished reciting it **a second time, they** recited it **a third time, although** in practice **they never** recited it **a third time,** as the priests worked efficiently and finished the rite before this became necessary. **Rabbi Yehuda says: The third group never reached** even once the opening verse of the fourth chapter of *hallel*: **"I love that the Lord hears** the voice of my supplications" (Psalms 116:1), **because its people were few** and the slaughtering of all the offerings was completed during the recitation of the first three chapters.

While the *shechita* is underway, *Hallel* is being recited — as the *Tosefta* states, the *Leviyim* read *Hallel* while *Yisrael* perform the *shechita*. If the *Leviyim* completed *Hallel* and the group had still not finished bringing all their *korbanot*, they began *Hallel* a second time; if that too was completed, they began a third time. *Af al pi she-lo shilshelu mi-yemeihem* — in all of history, it never came to a third recitation. Rabbi Yehuda adds an observation about the third group in particular: *mi-yemeihem shel kat shlishit lo higu le-Ahavti ki yishma Hashem* — the third group was so small that they never even reached the passage *Ahavti ki yishma Hashem* in a single recitation of *Hallel*.

## Shabbat Pesach Procedure

### Flaying on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַעֲשֵׂהוּ בַּחוֹל כָּךְ מַעֲשֵׂהוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים מְדִיחִים אֶת הָעֲזָרָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was done during the week, so was it done on Shabbat; only that** on Shabbat **the priests would rinse the Temple courtyard,** cleaning away the blood, **contrary to the wishes of the Sages,** as the priests did not want to veer from the weekday procedure in this regard.

*Ke-ma'aseihu be-chol kach ma'aseihu be-Shabbat* — the same procedure performed on a weekday is performed on *Shabbat*. One difference, however, involved the rinsing of the *azarah*. On a weekday, the *kohanim* would dam up the water channel flowing through the *azarah*, causing it to overflow and wash away the blood from the floor, after which the floor was squeegeed into the drainage holes. On *Shabbat* they would do this as well, *ella she-ha-kohanim madichin et ha-azarah she-lo birtson chachamim* — but the *chachamim* objected to this practice on *Shabbat*. The grounds for the objection will be discussed in the Gemara.

### Hooks & Poles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד תּוֹלִין וּמַפְשִׁיטִין? אוּנְקְלָיוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל הָיוּ קְבוּעִים בַּכְּתָלִים וּבָעַמּוּדִים שֶׁבָּהֶן תּוֹלִין וּמַפְשִׁיטִין. כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם לִתְלוֹת וּלְהַפְשִׁיט — מַקְלוֹת דַּקִּים וַחֲלָקִים הָיוּ שָׁם, מַנִּיחַ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ וְעַל כֶּתֶף חֲבֵירוֹ, וְתוֹלֶה וּמַפְשִׁיט.
>
> **Translation:** **How would one suspend and flay** the Paschal lamb in the Temple? **Iron hooks [*unkelayot*] were secured into the walls and pillars, and upon them one would suspend** the offering **and flay** it. If **anyone lacked a place** among the hooks in the Temple courtyard **to suspend and flay** the offering, **there were thin, smooth rods there,** which **he would place on his** own **shoulder and on another's shoulder, and** from it **he would suspend** the offering and flay it.

*Keitzad tolin u-mafshitin* — how did they suspend and flay the *Korban Pesach*? *Unkelyot shel barzel hayu kevi'im ba-ktalim u-va-amudim* — iron hooks were fixed into the walls and posts of the *Beit HaMitbachayim*, the slaughterhouse area of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the carcasses were hung from these hooks for *hafshata* (flaying). The *emurim* needed to be removed and burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and since this constituted the *avodah* of the *korban* rather than mere meat preparation, the *hafshata* was permitted even on *Shabbat*. For those who could not find an available hook, *maklot dakkim ve-chalakim hayu sham* — thin, smooth poles were kept on hand. *Meniach al ktefo ve-al ktef chaveiro* — one person would put the pole on his shoulder and his friend's shoulder, creating a human post from which the carcass was suspended for flaying.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת, מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עַל כֶּתֶף חֲבֵירוֹ, וְיַד חֲבֵירוֹ עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ, וְתוֹלֶה וּמַפְשִׁיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** When **the fourteenth** of Nisan **occurred on Shabbat,** when moving the rods is prohibited (Rambam), **he would rest his hand on another's shoulder and the other's hand on his** own **shoulder and suspend** the offering **and flay** it.

Rabbi Eliezer rules that when the fourteenth falls on *Shabbat*, even the smooth poles should not be used, and instead one uses human arms directly: *meniach yado al ktef chaveiro ve-yad chaveiro al ktefo* — placing one arm on his companion's shoulder and his companion's arm on his, forming a living crossbeam from which to suspend and flay the offering. The apparent concern is something akin to *muktzah* regarding the poles, even in the midst of all the other *Shabbat*-permitted *avodah*. The precise mechanics of how the carcass could be adequately supported by arms rather than poles remain somewhat difficult to picture given the weight of the animal.

### Innards & First Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָעוֹ, וְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵימוּרָיו. נְתָנוֹ בְּמָגֵיס, וְהִקְטִירָן עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **He would tear** open the flesh of the offering **and remove its sacrificial parts,** i.e., the fats and other parts offered on the altar. **He would place** the sacrificial parts **in a large basin [*mageis*] and burn them on the altar.**

*Kar'o* — they split the carcass. *Hotzi et emurav* — the innards and fats were removed. *Netano ba-magis* — placed in a pan. *Hiktiru al gabbe ha-mizbe'ach* — and burned on the *mizbe'ach*. This completed the *avodah* of that group.

### Groups' Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה כַּת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ בְּהַר הַבַּיִת. שְׁנִיָּה בַּחֵיל, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית בִּמְקוֹמָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת. חָשֵׁיכָה — יָצְאוּ וְצָלוּ אֶת פִּסְחֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If this took place on Shabbat, when carrying is prohibited, **the first group would exit and remain on the Temple Mount; the second** group would remain within **the rampart,** which was an area outside the women's courtyard; **and the third** group **would stand in its place** in the Temple. They would wait there until nightfall, and as soon as **it became dark, they would** all **go out and roast their Paschal lambs,** everyone in his own place.

On a weekday, after completing their *korbanot*, the first group simply went home. On *Shabbat*, however, carrying is prohibited and one cannot leave *Yerushalayim* with the carcass until nightfall. The *mishnah* therefore describes where each group waited. *Yatz'ta kat rishonah ve-yashvah lah b'Har HaBayit* — the first group went out and sat on *Har HaBayit*, which was enclosed by the outer wall. *Shniyah ba-cheil* — the second group waited in the *cheil*, the narrow corridor between the outer *soreg* barrier and the *azarah* walls. *Ha-shlishit bi-mkomah omedet* — the third group remained standing in the *azarah* itself. *Chashecha* — when darkness fell, they all went out and roasted their *Korban Pesach* at their lodgings in *Yerushalayim*.

## Minimum Pesach Size

### 30 per Group Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין הַפֶּסַח נִשְׁחָט אֶלָּא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ כִּתּוֹת שֶׁל שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁלֹשִׁים בְּנֵי אָדָם. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״קָהָל״ וְ״עֵדָה״ וְ״יִשְׂרָאֵל״. מְסַפְּקָא לַן אִי בְּבַת אַחַת אִי בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: The Paschal lamb is slaughtered only in three groups of** at least **thirty people** each. **What is the reason** for this rule? The verse says: **Assembly, congregation, and Israel,** and each of these terms refers to a group of no fewer than ten people. **We are uncertain** as to whether this means that we need three groups of ten people **at the same time** or **one after another.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ בָּעֵינַן שָׁלֹשׁ כִּתּוֹת שֶׁל שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁלֹשִׁים בְּנֵי אָדָם. דְּאִי בְּבַת אַחַת — הָא אִיכָּא, וְאִי בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה — הָא אִיכָּא, הִלְכָּךְ בְּחַמְשִׁין נָמֵי סַגִּיא, דְּעָיְילִי תְּלָתִין וְעָבְדִי, עָיְילִי עַשְׂרָה וְנָפְקִי עַשְׂרָה, עָיְילִי עַשְׂרָה וְנָפְקִי עַשְׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** in order to satisfy both possible interpretations, **we require three groups of thirty people** each. **As, if** you say we need all thirty **at the same time,** we **have** that, **and if** we need them **one after another,** we **have** that as well. **Therefore,** in pressing circumstances when there are not enough people present, **even fifty** people **suffice.** How so? **Thirty enter and perform** the necessary rite, **ten** others **enter and ten** of the original group **leave** so that those present are considered a new group, **and** then **ten** others **enter and ten** more **leave** so that those present now comprise a third group. In this way the Paschal lamb is slaughtered in three groups of thirty people each, although the total number of people involved is only fifty.

Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *ein ha-Pesach nishchat ela bi-shlosh kitot shel shloshim shloshim bnei adam* — the *Pesach* may not be slaughtered unless there are three groups of thirty people each. The reasoning derives from the same verse: the three terms *kehal*, *edah*, and *Yisrael* each imply a minimum quorum of ten, yielding thirty. Rabbi Yitzchak is uncertain whether the Torah means thirty people must be present simultaneously, or whether it requires three separate groups of ten brought in sequence. *Hilkhakh ba'inan shalosh kitot shel shloshim shloshim bnei adam* — to satisfy both possibilities, he requires three groups of thirty each. However, the Gemara points out that this need not mean ninety people altogether. *Chamishim nami sagi* — fifty suffice. The first group of thirty enters and performs the *shechita*, satisfying the requirement of thirty at one time. Then ten of them leave and ten newcomers enter, forming a second group of ten at a new time — but the twenty remaining from the first group still make up a group of thirty alongside them. Then another ten of those original thirty exit and the final ten newcomers enter, again reaching thirty with the lingering members. This overlapping arrangement covers both possibilities with only fifty individuals.

### Miracle vs. Manual Lock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנְסָה כַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה וְכוּ׳. אִיתְּמַר, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: נִנְעֲלוּ תְּנַן. רָבָא אָמַר: נוֹעֲלִין תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **the first group entered,** after which they closed the doors to the Temple courtyard. **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed about the precise wording of the mishna. **Abaye said: We learned** in the mishna that the doors of the Temple courtyard miraculously **closed by themselves. Rava said: We learned** in the mishna **that** people **would close** the doors of the Temple courtyard at the appropriate time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ לְמִסְמַךְ אַנִּיסָּא. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: נִנְעֲלוּ תְּנַן, כַּמָּה דַּעֲיַילוּ מְעַלּוּ, וְסָמְכִינַן אַנִּיסָּא. רָבָא אָמַר: נוֹעֲלִין תְּנַן, וְלָא סָמְכִינַן אַנִּיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the practical difference **between them? The** practical difference **between them is** with regard to **whether we rely on a miracle. Abaye said: We learned** in the mishna **that the doors closed by themselves; as many** people **as entered, entered, and we rely on a miracle** to close the doors so that an excessive number of people not enter and thus create a danger (Rabbeinu Ḥananel). **Rava said: We learned** in the mishna **that** people would **close** the doors, **and we do not rely on a miracle** to ensure that the courtyard not become overly crowded.

The *mishnah* says the doors of the *azarah* were locked once the first group entered and filled the space. A dispute between Abaye and Rava concerns the nature of this locking. Abaye holds *ninalu* — the doors locked themselves, miraculously, of their own accord. The procedure was to allow people to continue entering for as long as they could, and one relied on the miracle that the doors would seal themselves when capacity was reached: *d'samchinan a-nisa*. Rava holds *no'alin* — human beings actively monitored the incoming crowd and locked the doors manually: *v'lo samchinan a-nisa*.

### Akavya Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם שֶׁעֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל נִתְנַדָּה, שֶׁאֵין עֲזָרָה נִנְעֶלֶת עַל כׇּל אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּחׇכְמָה וּבְיִרְאַת חֵטְא כַּעֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל. אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: אֵין בָּעֲזָרָה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּנְעֲלָה עַל כׇּל אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּחׇכְמָה וּבְיִרְאַת חֵטְא כַּעֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל: רָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: אֵין בָּעֲזָרָה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹעֲלִין אוֹתָהּ עַל כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּחׇכְמָה וּבְיִרְאַת חֵטְא כַּעֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And that which we learned** elsewhere in a mishna with regard to the ban placed upon Akavya ben Mahalalel for having spoken harshly about Shemaya and Avtalyon, that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Heaven forbid that Akavya ben Mahalalel was banned;** it must have been someone else, as even when the entire Jewish people would come to Jerusalem for the Festival, **the Temple courtyard would not close on any man from Israel** as full of **wisdom and fear of sin as Akavya ben Mahalalel; Abaye** can **explain** this statement **according to his opinion, and Rava** can also **explain** it **according to his opinion. Abaye** can **explain** it **according to his opinion** as follows: **No man from Israel was in the Temple courtyard when it closed by itself** who was as full of **wisdom and fear of sin as Akavya ben Mahalalel. Rava,** too, can **explain** it **according to his opinion** as follows: **No** man **from Israel was in the Temple courtyard when they closed it** who was as full of **wisdom and fear of sin as Akavya ben Mahalalel.**

This same debate is applied to the story of Akavya ben Mahalalel. In a separate context in the Gemara, there is a claim that Akavya was placed in *niduy* after he disputed a ruling of Shemaya and Avtaliyon regarding whether a freed slave woman would undergo the *Sotah* process. Rabbi Yehuda responds: *chas v'shalom she-Akavya ben Mahalalel nitnade* — God forbid that Akavya ben Mahalalel was ever put in *niduy* — for *she-ein azarah nin'elet al kol adam b'Yisrael* — when the doors of the *azarah* were sealed on *erev Pesach* with all of Israel packed within, there was not to be found among all those people anyone equal *b'chokhmah u-v'yirat cheit* to Akavya ben Mahalalel. The phrase *azarah nin'elet* is thus read as an expression of the totality of Israel assembled.

Abaye and Rava each explain this phrase in accordance with their respective positions: Abaye reads *nin'elet* as the doors locking themselves — in that miraculous moment when the *azarah* sealed itself, there was no one within equal to Akavya. Rava reads *no'alim otah* — when they locked the *azarah* actively, among all those assembled there was no one his equal. The *masorah* does not decisively favor one reading over the other.

## Agrippas' Census

### Crushing Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מֵעוֹלָם לָא נִתְמַעֵךְ אָדָם בָּעֲזָרָה, חוּץ מִפֶּסַח אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה בִּימֵי הִלֵּל, שֶׁנִּתְמַעֵךְ בּוֹ זָקֵן אֶחָד, וְהָיוּ קוֹרְאִין אוֹתוֹ ״פֶּסַח מְעוּכִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: No one was ever crushed** by the great throngs of people **in the Temple courtyard, except for one Passover in the days of Hillel when an old man was crushed, and they called** that Passover **the Passover of the crushed.**

The Gemara states: *me'olam lo nitma'eikh adam ba-azarah* — no one was ever crushed in the *azarah* despite the extraordinary crowding. The one exception: *chutz mi-Pesach echad she-hayah bi-ymei Hillel* — except for one *Pesach* in the time of Hillel, when an elderly person was crushed. That event was remembered and the *Pesach* of that year was called *Pesach Me'uchin* — the crushed *Pesach*. The Gemara notes that the report of this single exception actually strengthens the claim that it otherwise never happened.

### Kidney Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פַּעַם אַחַת בִּיקֵּשׁ אַגְרִיפַּס הַמֶּלֶךְ לִיתֵּן עֵינָיו בְּאוּכְלוּסֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל: תֵּן עֵינֶיךָ בַּפְּסָחִים. נָטַל כּוּלְיָא מִכׇּל אֶחָד, וְנִמְצְאוּ שָׁם שִׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא זוּגֵי כְלָיוֹת כִּפְלַיִם כְּיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם. חוּץ מִטָּמֵא וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּדֶרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה. וְאֵין לָךְ כׇּל פֶּסַח וּפֶסַח שֶׁלֹּא נִמְנוּ עָלָיו יוֹתֵר מֵעֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם. וְהָיוּ קוֹרְאִין אוֹתוֹ ״פֶּסַח מְעוּבִּין״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Once, King Agrippa wished to set his eyes on the multitudes [*ukhlosin*] of Israel** to know how many they were. He **said to the High Priest: Set your eyes on the Paschal lambs;** count how many animals are brought in order to approximate the number of people. The High Priest **took a kidney from each one,** as the kidneys are burned on the altar, **and six hundred thousand pairs of kidneys were found there, double** the number of **those who left Egypt.** This did not reflect the sum total of the Jewish people, as it **excluded those who were ritually impure or at a great distance,** who did not come to offer the sacrifice. Furthermore, this was a count of the Paschal lambs and not of the people, **and there was not a single Paschal lamb that did not have more than ten people registered for it. They called that** Passover **the Passover of the crowded,** due to the large number of people.

Another *baraita* reports that King Agrippas — one of the kings at the close of the *Bayit Sheni* period — wished to gain a sense of the vast numbers of the Jewish people he ruled. He instructed the *Kohen Gadol* to count the *Pesachim* as a means of census. *Natlu kulya mi-kol echad* — a kidney was taken from each *Pesach* offering as a marker. *Ve-nimtze'u sham shishim ribo zugei kelyot* — six hundred thousand pairs of kidneys were found, meaning 1.2 million *Pesachim*. Since each *Pesach* was shared by at minimum ten people, this implies a population of twelve million — *kiflayim ke-yotzei Mitzrayim*, double those who left Egypt. The *baraita* notes that this count excluded those who were *tamei* or who were on a distant journey and therefore did not bring a *Pesach*. That year's gathering was called *Pesach Me'uvin* — the thick or dense *Pesach* — in contrast to the *Pesach Me'uchin* of Hillel's day.

### Capacity Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל כּוּלְיָא? הָא בָּעֵי אַקְטוּרַהּ! דַּהֲדַר מַקְטֵיר לְהוּ. וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְטִירוֹ״, שֶׁלֹּא יְעָרֵב חֲלָבָיו שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions one of the details of this story: How could the High Priest **take a kidney?** Didn't **he have to burn it** on the altar? The Gemara answers: **He** first took the kidneys for the count, and **subsequently he burned them.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't it written: "And** the priest **shall burn it** on the altar; it is the food of the offering made by fire to the Lord" (Leviticus 3:11)? The singular "it" apparently indicates that **he must not mix** the **fats of this** sacrifice **with** those of **another;** rather, he must burn each set separately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּהֲדַר מַקְטֵיר לְהוּ חֲדָא חֲדָא. וְהָתַנְיָא ״וְהִקְטִירָם״, שֶׁיְּהֵא כּוּלּוֹ כְּאֶחָד! אֶלָּא: תְּפִיסָה בְּעָלְמָא, דְּשָׁקֵיל מִינַּיְיהוּ עַד דְּיָהֲבִין לֵיהּ מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He subsequently burned them one by one** and not all together. The Gemara asks further: **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the plural "them" in the verse: "And the priest **shall burn them** upon the altar; it is the food of the offering made by fire for a satisfying aroma; all the fat is the Lord's" (Leviticus 3:16) indicates **that all** the sacrificial parts of a sacrifice must be offered **at the same time? Rather,** it must be that when the High Priest took a kidney for counting, it was **merely** momentary **seizure;** that is, **he took it from them until they brought him something else** with which to keep track of the numbers, and it was this other item that was counted afterward.

This figure invites scrutiny. The *azarah* in front of the *mizbe'ach* — the *ezrat Yisrael* — was 135 *amot* long by 11 *amot* wide, yielding approximately 1,400 to 1,500 square *amot*. Even allowing two or three people per square *amah* in extremely crowded conditions, the space could hold at most several thousand people at a time. With three groups rotating, the total might reach fifteen thousand individuals at the outside. The figure of twelve million adult males is plainly beyond any physical capacity the *azarah* could accommodate — even accounting for the well-known principle of *omdim tzefufim u-mishtachavim b'revach*. The story is clearly not intended as a precise census.

### Kidney Marking

The Gemara raises a technical difficulty with Agrippas' method. The kidney must be burned on the *mizbe'ach* — *ha-ketiv haktarah*. How could it be removed and used as a counting token? The Gemara answers: *de-hadar maktru lehu* — after the count, they were returned and burned. But a further difficulty arises: the verse *ve-hiktiram* implies all the *emurim* of a single *korban* must be burned together, not separately. And another source says *she-kulan k'achat* — all together. How then can a kidney be temporarily separated and later returned? The Gemara resolves: *ella tefisa b'alma* — the kidney was not actually removed, but merely grasped and held up as a marker, *de-shakil mineihu*, while another indicator was used for the count, and then the kidney was immediately released back with the rest of the *korban*'s *emurim*.

## Practical Details

### Bowls Without Bases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַבָּזִיכִין שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא הָיוּ לָהֶן שׁוּלַיִים, חוּץ מִבְּזִיכֵי לְבוֹנָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים — שֶׁמָּא יַנִּיחוּם וְיִפְרוֹס הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that states: **None of the bowls in the Temple had** flat **bases** for the same reason, so that they should not be put down, which would allow the blood to congeal. This was **with the exception of the bowls of frankincense** that would be placed on **the showbread,** which did have flat bases. They could not have sharp bottoms out of concern that **perhaps** the priests **would rest them on the bread and the bread would break.** The showbread had an intricate and delicate shape, and a bowl with a sharp bottom could pierce or break the bread.

A *baraita* clarifies that the practice of making bowls without a base was not unique to the blood-collection procedure of the *Korban Pesach* but was universal in the *Mikdash*: *kol ha-bazichin she-ba-Mikdash lo hayu lahem shulayim* — all the bowls in the *Mikdash* had no base, always for the same reason of preventing blood from congealing if set down. The sole exception: *chutz mi-vazichei levonah shel lechem ha-panim* — the spoons used to hold the *levonah* (frankincense) placed on the *shulchan* alongside the *lechem ha-panim*. Those needed to lie flat on the *shulchan* for an entire week. If they had no base, they would have to lean against the bread, and *v'yipresu ha-lechem* — they would crack the delicately made loaves.

### Aesthetic Arrangement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוּרָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ כֶּסֶף כֶּסֶף, וְשׁוּרָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ זָהָב זָהָב, לֹא הָיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין. וְלֹא הָיוּ לַבָּזִיכִין שׁוּלַיִים, שֶׁמָּא יַנִּיחוּם וְיִקָּרֵשׁ הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** [See section on Gold & Silver Bowls]

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא דִּילְמָא שָׁקְלִי דְּדַהֲבָא וּמְעַיְּילִי דְּכַסְפָּא — הָכִי נָמֵי, דִּילְמָא שָׁקְלִי בַּר מָאתַן וּמְעַיְּילִי בַּר מְאָה! אֶלָּא, דְּהָכִי שַׁפִּיר טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that there was no intermingling of gold and silver bowls? **If you say** that it was due to concern that **perhaps** a priest **would take a gold** bowl to keep for himself **and** then **return a silver** one in its place, the solution described in the mishna does not alleviate this concern. **Here, too,** in a row where everyone is holding gold bowls, there is concern that **perhaps** a priest **would take a two-hundred-**dinar bowl, keep it for himself, **and** then **return a** one-hundred-dinar bowl in its place (Rid). **Rather,** the reason is **that this** arrangement, where all the bowls in each row are of the same color, **is** aesthetically **more attractive.**

The question of why the gold and silver bowls are not intermingled within a single row is addressed by the Gemara. One might suggest that the concern is a kind of inequitable exchange — a *Kohen* might hand back a gold bowl and receive a silver one, or hand back one worth two hundred *zuz* and receive one worth only a hundred. But the Gemara dismisses this: that imbalance is always possible regardless of arrangement, since any two bowls of the same material can differ in value. Rather, *ella de-hachi shapir tefei* — the reason is simply aesthetic. A row of uniform silver and a row of uniform gold is more beautiful and dignified than a mixed arrangement.

### Transport Without Feet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט יִשְׂרָאֵל וְקִבֵּל הַכֹּהֵן וְכוּ׳. לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָאו יִשְׂרָאֵל?! הִיא גּוּפָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דִּשְׁחִיטָה בְּזָר כְּשֵׁירָה. וְקִבֵּל הַכֹּהֵן. הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, מִקַּבָּלָה וְאֵילָךְ מִצְוַת כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **An Israelite would slaughter** the offering **and a priest would receive** the blood and pass it to other priests. The Gemara asks: **Is it not sufficient** if someone who is **not an Israelite** slaughters the offering? Must the ritual slaughter be performed specifically by an Israelite, and not by a priest or a Levite? The Gemara answers: The mishna **teaches us this** *halakha* **itself, that** even if the **slaughter** is performed **by a non-priest** it **is valid.** And that which was stated in the mishna that **the priest receives** the blood **comes to teach us that from receiving and onward** the rite is **a commandment** cast **upon the priesthood,** and a non-priest may not perform it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹתְנוֹ לַחֲבֵירוֹ. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ הוֹלָכָה שֶׁלֹּא בָּרֶגֶל — הָוְיָא הוֹלָכָה! דִּילְמָא הוּא נָיֵיד פּוּרְתָּא. וְאֶלָּא מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן: ״בְּרׇב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** It was also taught in the mishna that the priest would **pass** the bowl of blood **to another** priest. The Gemara suggests: **Learn from this that carrying without walking,** i.e., transporting the blood to the altar by passing it from hand to hand without actually walking with it to the altar, **is** considered a valid act of **carrying** the blood of a sacrifice to the altar, one of the four rites involved in the offering of a sacrifice. This would resolve the same unanswered question in tractate *Zevaḥim*. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Perhaps** the priest **would move a little** with his feet as he passed the bowl to the next priest, in order to fulfill the requirement to walk with the blood to the altar. **Rather, what does this** account of how they transported the blood to the altar **teach us?** The Gemara answers: **It teaches us** that the priests were arranged in rows in order to increase the number of people involved in the rite and fulfill the principle that **"in the multitude of people is the king's glory"** (Proverbs 14:28).

The *mishnah*'s description of the bucket-brigade procedure — *nosno l'chavero ve-chavero l'chavero* — raises a significant *halachic* question. One of the *avodot* of blood service is *holacha*, the act of transporting the blood to the *mizbe'ach*. But in this procedure, no individual *Kohen* actually walks anywhere. Each stands in place and merely passes the bowl to the next person. Does *holacha she-lo be-regel* — transport without the use of one's feet — constitute a valid *holacha*? The Gemara offers the possibility that *dilma dahani purta* — perhaps the last *Kohen* in the line, the one adjacent to the *mizbe'ach*, took a small step before performing the *zrika*, which would satisfy the requirement of *holacha* through actual movement.

### Honor in Procedure

But if the question of *holacha* is resolved so simply, what is the *mishnah*'s purpose in elaborating the entire bucket-brigade procedure at all? The Gemara answers: *ha kamashma lan b'rov am hadrat melech* — it teaches us the principle that in the presence of a multitude, there is glory to the King. The point of involving so many *kohanim* in the passage of the blood is not to solve a *halachic* problem of transport but to maximize the honor and beauty of the sacred service by engaging as many participants as possible. The greater the number of *kohanim* involved in even a single act of *avodah*, the more magnificent the event becomes.
