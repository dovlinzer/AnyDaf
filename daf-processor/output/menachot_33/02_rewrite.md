# Menachot 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sefer Torah Status

### Bed & Sefer Torah

The daf opens with a brief but striking account reported by Rabbi Chelbo. He relates that he observed Rav Huna sitting on a bed upon which a *Sefer Torah* was resting. Rather than simply remaining on the bed, Rav Huna upturned a jug on the ground, placed the *Sefer Torah* upon it, and only then was willing to continue sitting on the bed. The operative principle, the Gemara explains, is that Rav Huna held it to be forbidden to sit on a bed upon which a *Sefer Torah* is resting — *asur leishev agabei mita sheSefer Torah munach aleha*. This ruling reflects a distinct dimension of the *Sefer Torah*'s sanctity, one that does not appear to extend to other sacred books.

### Rabba bar bar Chana

This position, however, conflicts with a ruling transmitted by Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, who permits sitting on such a bed — *mutar leishev agabei mita sheSefer Torah munach aleha*. A seemingly powerful counter-proof is raised: there is a well-known story of Rabbi Eliezer who, while sitting on a bed, suddenly remembered that a *Sefer Torah* was lying on it, and slipped off immediately as though he had been bitten by a snake. Surely this demonstrates that one may not sit on a bed together with a *Sefer Torah*?

### Gemara's Correction

The Gemara does not accept this challenge. It corrects the story: the *Sefer Torah* was not on the bed at all. Rabbi Eliezer was sitting on the bed and realized that someone had left the *Sefer Torah* on the ground — *agabei karka* — and it was in response to that indignity that he leapt off the bed and sat on the ground himself. The story, then, does not speak to the question of sitting on the same bed as a *Sefer Torah*; it speaks rather to the impropriety of leaving a *Sefer Torah* on the ground while one sits elevated above it.

### Kissei Hakodesh

This exchange introduces, without elaborating, the broader framework of *kissei hakodesh* — the elevated status of sacred writings. The Gemara here is not placing this ruling in a wider comparative context, and it does not explore how the ruling would apply to *tefillin*, *mezuzot*, or other *sfarim*. Those questions are discussed elsewhere in the Gemara and among the *rishonim* and *poskim*, but not here. What the Gemara does establish is that the ruling pertains specifically to the unique sanctity of a *Sefer Torah* — *kedushas Sefer Torah* — and not to sacred writings generically. Whether the *Sefer Torah* in question was housed in a protective covering, whether a *chumash* printed today would carry an equivalent status, and how all of this maps onto a broader taxonomy of *kedushat sfarim*, are questions left open by this sugya.

## Mezuzah Writing

### Igeret vs. Sefer

The Gemara now shifts its focus toward the laws of *mezuzah* in particular. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: if one wrote the *mezuzah* like a letter — *katva igeret* — it is invalid, *pesulah*. Rashi understands this to refer specifically to the *mezuzah*. The general question being raised is what kind of document the *mezuzah* must resemble: a *sefer*, a formal sacred book, rather than an *igeret*, an informal letter.

### Precision & Spelling

What exactly does *igeret* mean, and why would that form render the *mezuzah* invalid? The Gemara does not spell this out in detail, but Rashi and Tosafot offer explanations. One possibility is that *igeret* refers to writing without *sirtut* — the scored lines that are required in the writing of a *mezuzah*, as discussed earlier in the Gemara. Another possibility is that it refers to a lack of precision in spelling. When one writes an informal letter, one does not necessarily attend to the exact orthographic form of every word, particularly where variant spellings are customary or unnoticed. If the *mezuzah* was written with acceptable spelling variants that nonetheless deviate from the precise form in which those words appear in the Torah, the *mezuzah* would be invalid. The point is not about the width of the columns — indeed, the Gemara has already established that even two words to a line is acceptable in a *mezuzah* — but about the exacting standard of spelling required.

### Gezeirah Shavah

The Gemara invokes a *gezeirah shavah* to derive that the *mezuzah* must be written as a *sefer* and not as an *igeret*. The reasoning would be that while *mezuzah* is not explicitly described in the Torah with the word *sefer*, a verse elsewhere — such as *vekasav lah sefer kerisut* in the context of a *get*, or a verse concerning the writing of *sefer* by *Amalek* or by *Yehoshua* — contains that language, and by linking the verbal context of *uchtavtem al mezuzot beitecha* to a verse that uses the word *sefer*, one derives that the *mezuzah* must have the character of a *sefer*. The Gemara itself is notably vague here: it does not specify which word the *gezeirah shavah* is built upon, and the argument raises its own difficulty — why do we know the source any better than we know the *mezuzah* verse itself? Tosafot, for his part, rejects the suggestion that this is derived from a *get*, since there is no independent source that a *get* requires *sirtut*, which would make the derivation circular.

## Mezuzah Placement

### On Stick Invalid

The Gemara turns to the question of where the *mezuzah* must be physically affixed. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: if one suspended the *mezuzah* on a stick — *telaah bemakel* — it is invalid. The apparent practice being addressed is that of travelers who would carry a portable *mezuzah* mounted on a stick, which they would prop near a doorway when staying somewhere that lacked a *mezuzah*. Such an arrangement, the Gemara rules, is invalid. The reason is grounded in the verse: *bisharecha* — in your gates. The *mezuzah* must be affixed within the doorpost itself, not merely positioned near it.

A *baraita* corroborates this ruling. If one suspended the *mezuzah* on a stick, or placed it behind the door — *achar hadelet* — it is described as involving danger with no *mitzvah* fulfilled. The term *sakanah*, danger, here admits of two readings. Rashi understands the danger to be that once the *mezuzah* is not fulfilling its *mitzvah*, it no longer serves to protect the house — a reading that reflects the assumption that the *mezuzah* has a kind of protective function. Tosafot offers a more straightforward explanation: the danger is purely physical. A *mezuzah* improperly placed — say, sticking out awkwardly in a spot where one might walk — poses the risk of injury. This reading is strongly preferable. The question of the *mezuzah*'s protective role is one the Gemara handles with considerable subtlety, as we will see further on.

The question of *churei hadelet* — placing the *mezuzah* behind the door — refers to a situation where the door, when open, swings back to conceal the doorpost. In such a case, the *mezuzah* would be hidden and thus not properly affixed in the meaningful sense. The *sakanah* of Tosafot's reading in such a case would apply if the *mezuzah* were somehow protruding in a way that could cause someone turning a corner to strike their head against it, though admittedly the danger in that specific case is less obvious.

### Munbaz's Inns

A passage regarding the household of King Munbaz illustrates one form of *mezuzah* practice observed beyond the strict letter of the obligation. The members of his royal retinue would affix *mezuzot* in the inns where they lodged — *fundekaoteihen* — even though they were not technically obligated, as they did not own or rent the premises long-term. They did so as a *zecher lemezuzah*, a remembrance of the obligation, so as not to dwell in a place without any *mezuzah* at all.

This is a significant detail. The Gemara does not say they did this because of *sakanah* or for protection. It says they did it as a *zecher*, firmly grounding the practice in a *halacha mitzvah* framework rather than in a superstitious or apotropaic one. The parallel to *batei kenesiot* and *batei midrash*, which are also not technically obligated but for which the custom is to affix a *mezuzah* nonetheless, is instructive in the same direction.

### Door Thickness

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: the *mitzvah* is to place the *mezuzah* within the actual interior space of the doorframe — *betoch chalalo shel petach* — not on the front or the back face of the doorpost. The Gemara initially responds that this is obvious from the verse *bisharecha*, but then explains why one might have thought otherwise. Since Rava had ruled that the *mitzvah* is to place the *mezuzah* in the *tefach* of the doorframe's thickness closest to the *reshut harabim* — the public domain — one might have supposed that even farther outward would be better, and thus placing it on the exterior face of the doorpost entirely would be optimal. The Gemara rejects this: the requirement remains that the *mezuzah* be *besharecha*, inside the gate, within the thickness of the frame itself, not on its outer surface facing the street.

### One Parchment

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: if one wrote the *mezuzah* on two parchments — *al shenei dapin* — it is invalid. A question arises as to what *dapin* means here. Earlier in the tractate, *daf* referred to a column within a *Sefer Torah*; here it most naturally means two separate pieces of parchment. The Gemara works through a *baraita* that addresses the case of writing on two columns and placing them in two separate doorposts — *bishnei sipim* — and rules it invalid, while permitting placement in a single doorpost. The Gemara resolves the apparent tension by clarifying that when the *baraita* says "two doorposts" it means parchment *fit* to be placed in two doorposts — i.e., two separate pieces of parchment — not literally two separate doorposts with a single piece of parchment cut across two columns. The upshot is that the *Shema* and *Vehaya Im Shamoa* must both appear on a single, continuous piece of parchment. A *mezuzah* composed of two physically separate pieces of *klaf* sewn or otherwise joined together is invalid.

### Door Hinge

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: with respect to *mezuzah*, one should follow the *heker tzir* — the location of the door hinge. The Gemara asks what *heker tzir* means. Rav Adda explains it refers to the *afta*, the socket or hole in which the door's pivot sits.

Rashi's explanation clarifies the practical application. The rule that a *mezuzah* is affixed on the right-hand side as one enters a doorway is well known, but in certain cases the question of which direction constitutes "entering" is ambiguous. For a doorway leading from the street into a house, the direction of entry is obvious. But for an interior door between two rooms, the question may be genuinely unclear. The Gemara specifies that such a door should arise between, for example, the men's section and the women's section of a house — *bei gavrei* and *bei nashei* — and the direction of primary travel is determined by where the hinge is located.

The key point is not whether the hinge is on the left or the right of the door, but rather which *side* of the door it faces. A door hinge is placed on the interior side to prevent anyone outside from being able to remove the door by lifting the hinge pin. The side of the door that does *not* show the hinge is considered the exterior — the side from which one approaches. The assumed direction of travel is therefore from the side where the hinge is hidden toward the side where the hinge is visible. Whatever is the right-hand side when moving in that direction is where the *mezuzah* is affixed.

The Gemara observes, almost in passing, that by framing this rule as it does, it presupposes the principle of affixing the *mezuzah* on the right-hand side of entry — a rule the Gemara has not formally introduced, but simply treats as background.

The Gemara also acknowledges a broader question lurking here. In the time of the Gemara, it was common for a *bayis* to refer to a single room, and entire families often lived in one such room. Whether the modern practice of requiring a *mezuzah* on every interior room reflects the Gemara's own assumed context is not entirely clear. One *rishon* held that individual interior rooms within a house did not require *mezuzot* separately. The *halacha* as universally practiced today does require *mezuzot* on all rooms, and there are *gemarot* in this sugya that speak of individual rooms — but the degree to which the Gemara's cases map directly onto our contemporary multi-room dwellings is worth noting as an academic observation.

### Resh Galuta

When the *Resh Galuta* — the secular head of the Jewish community in Babylonian exile — built a new home, he sent to Rav Nachman asking him to arrange for the *mezuzot* to be affixed. Rav Nachman's response was: first hang the doors, then place the *mezuzot* — *tali dashei bereisha*.

Rashi explains that Rav Nachman needed there to be a physical doorframe in place before the *mezuzah* could be affixed; placing the *mezuzah* in the carved-out space first, before the frame was erected, would raise questions about the requirement for a physical doorframe. Tosafot offers a different explanation: Rav Nachman needed to see where the hinges were placed in order to determine the direction of primary passage between the rooms, and thereby to know on which side the *mezuzah* should be affixed. Until the doors were actually hung with their hinges, that determination could not be made.

## Angle Debate

### Nagar Invalid

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: if one made the *mezuzah* like a *nagar* — a peg or nail — it is invalid. According to Rashi's illustration, this refers to placing the *mezuzah* horizontally into the doorframe rather than vertically; one has, as it were, driven it in like a nail, and this is *pesulah*.

### Rabbi's House

The Gemara immediately challenges this ruling. When Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that all of the *mezuzot* in Rebbe's house were made like a *nagar* — *kemin nagar havei avidun* — and were evidently considered valid. Parenthetically, the Gemara notes at this point that the small door Rebbe used to pass from his private quarters into his *beis medrash* did not have a *mezuzah* — a point that will be addressed in the next section.

### Horizontal vs Vertical

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: the two cases are not comparable. In one case the *mezuzah* was placed like a *sichta* — a peg driven horizontally — which is invalid. In the other case it was placed like an *istavira*, which the Gemara identifies as the joint connecting the hip to the thigh. The question is what *istavira* means in terms of actual placement.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi offers two explanations. On the first reading, the *istavira* — the hip-thigh joint — is vertical when one is standing, and therefore placing the *mezuzah* like an *istavira* simply means placing it vertically. The invalid placement is horizontal, and the valid placement is vertical. On Rashi's second reading, the joint creates a right angle when one is seated, suggesting a bent or angled form — one that, practically speaking, would require carving a strangely shaped recess in the doorframe.

Tosafot offers a third reading: *istavira* refers to a diagonal placement. This is neither horizontal nor fully vertical but at an angle, which is geometrically natural and could correspond to a diagonal cut in the doorframe.

More significantly, Tosafot argues with the very premise of Rashi's ruling about what is *lechatchila*. Rashi holds that the *nagar* which is invalid is horizontal, and the proper placement is vertical. Tosafot reads the Gemara in precisely the opposite direction: the *nagar* that is invalid is vertical, and the proper placement is horizontal. Tosafot grounds this in the practice of how *klafim* are stored. While we are accustomed to *Sifrei Torah* being stored upright in the *aron*, there are traditions in which *Sifrei Torah* were stored lying flat, and that was considered the more dignified position — the parchment is settled and at rest rather than held awkwardly upright. Tosafot extends this logic to the *klaf* in *tefillin* as well, asking whether the *klaf* should be placed standing or lying flat. Tosafot then cites Rabbeinu Tam, who notes that when the *Sefer Torah* stands on the *bima* everyone rises, but when the *shaliach tzibbur* lays it down, everyone sits — suggesting that the lying-down position is the natural resting state, and thus the more respectful one for storage.

Given this direct contradiction between Rashi and Tosafot, Tosafot himself proposes a resolution: perhaps what Rebbe's house exemplified was the diagonal placement — the *istavira* understood as an angle. If so, both Rashi (who requires vertical) and Rabbeinu Tam (who requires horizontal) might agree that a true compromise at the diagonal satisfies both opinions. This is the origin of the *minhag* to place the *mezuzah* at an angle. Nevertheless, there were those who followed Rashi outright — the Vilna Gaon among them — and in Yerushalayim it is common to see *mezuzot* placed completely straight vertically, following the *minhagei haGra*. In practice, even those who follow the diagonal do not place it at a full 45-degree angle; rather, the *mezuzah* is placed primarily vertically with a modest tilt of perhaps 20 to 30 degrees.

### Doorbolt Analysis

Daniel Sperber, in the first volume of his *Minhagei Yisrael*, brings archaeological evidence that illuminates this entire discussion. Before turning to *minhagim* broadly, Sperber's primary scholarly focus was on the physical realia of the Talmudic period, using archaeological findings to reconstruct what Talmudic-era objects and structures actually looked like. He argues that the *nagar* referred to in the Gemara was not simply a generic nail or peg but specifically the doorbolt — the horizontal bar that secured the door from the inside.

Sperber shows, with diagrams of ancient Israelite homes from archaeological excavations, that doorframes were constructed with a deliberate asymmetry: one side was a single continuous column of stone, while the other side was composed of two stones with a deliberate gap between them. This gap served as the slot into which the doorbolt could be inserted to lock the door from the inside. Crucially, he notes that in many of these structures, the bolt-hole was positioned approximately one-third from the top of the doorframe — precisely the region where a *mezuzah* would be placed. The Gemara's ruling, then, makes immediate physical sense: do not place your *mezuzah* in the hole you carved for your doorbolt. Sperber also reports that angled bolt-holes have been found archaeologically, corresponding to Tosafot's reading of the house of Rebbe, but no purely horizontal placements are attested. Sperber's analysis supports Rashi's basic understanding that what is invalid is horizontal placement, and that the diagonal configuration attributed to Rebbe's house reflects a real architectural feature.

### Ashkenazi Angle

The practice of placing the *mezuzah* at an angle is specifically an *Ashkenazic* custom. In *Sefard* the practice of placing the *mezuzah* straight vertically was apparently unproblematic. There is a folk tradition — for which no clear documentary evidence has been cited — that the angled placement developed during the period of the Crusades. According to this account, Crusaders who entered Jewish spaces would take a piece of wood and strike the vertically placed *mezuzah* sideways, turning it into the shape of a cross. In response, Jews began placing the *mezuzah* at an angle to prevent this from being easily done. This may be legend, and it deserves further investigation, but it is worth noting as a proposed historical explanation for the specifically *Ashkenazic* emphasis on the diagonal.

## Secondary Doors

### Rabbi's Door

Returning to the parenthetical point raised earlier: the small door that Rebbe used to pass from his private quarters directly into his *beis medrash* did not have a *mezuzah*. The Gemara implies that not every doorway requires a *mezuzah* — the opening must constitute a meaningful and significant passageway. Rebbe's private entrance to his *beis medrash* was not the primary access point; it was a small, personal door used exclusively by Rebbe himself to move between his rooms. The main entrance to the *beis medrash* was used by all the students. Since this small door represented only Rebbe's private passage rather than a genuine communal entryway, it did not require a *mezuzah*.

### Rav Huna's Door

The Gemara challenges this with a counter-example: there was a door that Rav Huna used to enter his *beis medrash*, and that door did have a *mezuzah*. How can this be reconciled with the case of Rebbe's door? The Gemara answers: Rav Huna's door was *ragil* — commonly used, either by Rav Huna himself on a frequent basis or by others as well. It thus constituted a genuine passageway rather than a private, minor opening. The precise quantitative threshold between a *ragil* door and one that is not is not spelled out.

### Tosafot's Reading

Tosafot reads the passage somewhat differently. Examining the language of the Gemara carefully — *petach habayit she'hayu yotzim mimenu l'Beis Medrash* — Tosafot argues that the reason Rav Huna's door required a *mezuzah* was not simply because it was a *beis medrash* door, but because it was a *house* door. It was the door through which one exited a private residence on the way to the *beis medrash*. Tosafot adds explicitly: a door to a *beis medrash* itself — *petach Beis haMidrash atzmo* — does not require a *mezuzah*, because a *beis medrash* is not a place where people live. This is, in fact, the answer to the famous question attributed to Korach against Moshe: does a house full of *sefarim* require a *mezuzah*? The answer, *al pi din*, is no — a *beis midrash* or *beis knesses* where no one actually lives and sleeps does not require a *mezuzah*. The universal *minhag* is to affix one anyway, on the basis of custom alone.

### Common Usage

The principle articulated by the Gemara at this point — *halach achar haragil*, go by what is commonly used — serves as the general rule for *mezuzah* in cases involving multiple doorways. Rashi explains: if a single room has two doorways leading into the same adjacent space, and one of those doorways is the primary one used while the other is secondary, only the primary doorway requires a *mezuzah*. The underlying logic is that the *mezuzah* obligation attaches to a meaningful passageway between two spaces, not merely to any physical opening. If the primary passage from room A to room B already has a *mezuzah*, a secondary, less-used door between those same two spaces does not independently require one. The parameters of what qualifies as primary versus secondary remain somewhat fluid, but the governing principle is clear: follow the common usage.

## Height Placement

### Top Third

Rav Zeira said in the name of Rav, and Rav Matna said in the name of Shmuel: the *mitzvah* is to place the *mezuzah* at the beginning of the upper third of the doorframe — *bit'chilat shelish ha'elyon*. This means the *mezuzah* should be positioned so that its lower edge begins at the point two-thirds of the way up the doorframe, with the *mezuzah* itself occupying part of the upper third.

### Rav Huna's Range

Rav Huna disagrees, or at least broadens the acceptable range significantly. He states: the *mezuzah* should be raised at least one *tefach* from the ground and kept at least one *tefach* below the top of the doorframe, and within that range the entire doorway is valid for placing the *mezuzah* — *kol hapetach kulo kasher limezuzah*. These two positions are not necessarily in direct conflict; the statement about the top third was phrased as *mitzvah lehaniach* — the ideal placement — suggesting that even according to the view requiring the top third, placement elsewhere within Rav Huna's range may be acceptable *bedieved*.

### Rabbi Yose's Top

A *baraita* presents the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda — essentially identical to Rav Huna's formulation: one *tefach* from the ground, one *tefach* from the ceiling, and anywhere in between is valid. Rabbi Yose, however, derives a more stringent rule from a *gezeirah shavah* between *ukshartem* — the binding of *tefillin* — and *uchtavtem* — the writing of *mezuzah*. Just as *tefillin* are bound on the upper part of the body, whether the head or the upper arm, so too the *mezuzah* must be placed at the top. This suggests not merely the upper third but placement as high as possible within the doorframe, short of the top *tefach*.

### Floor vs Ceiling

The Gemara asks: given Rav Huna's ruling that the entire range is valid, he is clearly going like Rabbi Yehuda. But according to whom does Shmuel — who specifies the top third — rule? Not like Rabbi Yehuda, since Rabbi Yehuda allows the entire range; and not like Rabbi Yose, since Rabbi Yose would require placement at the very top. Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan resolves this: Shmuel does in fact rule like Rabbi Yose, and when Shmuel says *bit'chilat shelish ha'elyon*, he does not mean the top third is the ideal target — he means it is the lowest permissible point. One should not go below the top third, but within the top third, placement higher is better. The practical implication is that the *mezuzah* should be placed as high as possible, at least no lower than where the upper third begins.

### High Arches

Tosafot notes a passage in the Yerushalmi that addresses the case of a very high doorway. When the doorframe is so tall that the top third is far above reach, the *mezuzah* should be placed at shoulder height — *k'neged ktefav*. Tosafot observes that this position diverges from our own Talmud's conclusion. The practical *halacha* as we rule it follows the top third as the operative zone, without explicitly endorsing the view that higher within the top third is always better. In cases of very high arches, such as those found in ancient structures in Yerushalayim, the practice of placing the *mezuzah* at shoulder height reflects the Yerushalmi's approach.

## Protection Purpose

### Closest to Outside

Rava — the same ruling cited earlier in the context of the door's thickness — states: the *mitzvah* is to place the *mezuzah* in the *tefach* of the doorframe's thickness closest to the *reshut harabim* — *b'tefach hasamuch lereshut harabim* — i.e., the outermost portion of the frame's depth. Two reasons are offered for this preference.

### Immediate Encounter

The Rabbis explain: *kedei she'yifga b'mezuzah miyad* — so that a person immediately encounters the *mezuzah* upon entering the house. This rationale is grounded in the psychological and religious dimension of the *mezuzah*: the moment one crosses the threshold, the first thing one meets is the *mezuzah*, reinforcing awareness of God and the obligations it embodies.

### House Protection

Rav Chanina of Sura offers a different reason: *ki heichi d'tinaterei* — so that the *mezuzah* protects the house. The idea here is that if the *mezuzah*'s protective function is to guard the household, it should be positioned at the outermost boundary of the home, so that the protection begins as far outward as possible. This reasoning has a quasi-mechanical character: the precise location of the *mezuzah* within the doorframe's depth determines the boundary of the zone it protects.

One might ask: why not place the *mezuzah* entirely on the exterior wall if maximal outward protection is the goal? The answer, as the Gemara has already established, is that *bisharecha* requires the *mezuzah* to be within the doorframe, inside the gate — not on the external face of the wall. Given that constraint, the outermost viable position is the *tefach* closest to the public domain.

### God's Protection

The Gemara then shifts registers entirely with a statement by Rabbi Chanina: *bo ure'e shelo kemidat Hakadosh Baruch Hu midat basar vadam*. Come and see that the way of the Holy One Blessed be He is not like the way of flesh and blood. When a human king sits in his inner chamber, his guards stand on the outside protecting him from external threat. God's way is the reverse: *avadav yoshvim mibifnim vehu meshamer mibachutz* — His servants dwell inside, and He protects them from the outside. The proof-text is from Tehillim: *Hashem shomrecha, Hashem tzilcha al yad yeminecha*.

### Superstition Downplay

This shift is not incidental. The Gemara has presented two competing explanations for why the *mezuzah* should be placed at the outermost *tefach*: one psychological and one protective. The protective explanation — that the *mezuzah* itself guards the house from the outside — carries with it the potential to slide into a view of the *mezuzah* as a charm or amulet. The Gemara does not follow that path. Instead, it almost immediately pivots to a theological statement in which the protection comes not from the *mezuzah* as a magical object but from God, who stands watch over His servants from the outside.

This appears to be a deliberate strategy. There is ample archaeological evidence that *kameot* and magical amulets were widespread in Jewish practice throughout the *bayis sheni* period and beyond, and it is evident that many people associated the *mezuzah* with apotropaic power. The Gemara, however, consistently handles *mezuzah* through a *halachic* lens. Even Rashi's reading of the *baraita* — that a *mezuzah* placed behind the door creates *sakanah* because it no longer protects the house — reflects the protective view. But the Gemara does not elaborate on that view, and by concluding with *Hashem shomrecha*, it redirects: any protection associated with the *mezuzah* is ultimately God's protection of those who are mindful of His presence, not the operation of a physical charm. The Rambam, characteristically, makes this explicit: the protection comes from the heightened religious awareness generated by encountering the *mezuzah* each time one enters and exits, which orients the person toward God — not from any power inherent in the object itself.

## Depth & Multiple Doors

### Burial Depth

Rav Yosef the son of Rava taught in the name of Rava: if one embedded the *mezuzah* more than a *tefach* deep into the doorframe — *he'emikah tefach pesulah* — it is invalid. Once the *mezuzah* is buried beyond a *tefach* into the thickness or substance of the frame, it is no longer considered properly affixed as part of the doorframe itself.

### Adding Stone

A *baraita* is brought as a potential support. The *baraita* addresses two related cases: placing the *mezuzah* in the *patzin* — the doorframe — or subsequently attaching a stone or block — *malben* — over the area where the *mezuzah* was placed, effectively burying it deeper. The *baraita* states that if under these circumstances there is a *tefach* of doorframe below the *mezuzah*, a separate *mezuzah* is required; if not, no separate *mezuzah* is needed.

### Doors Behind Doors

The Gemara, however, declines to accept this as a direct proof for the rule about depth of embedding. It reinterprets the *baraita*: the case being discussed there is not a standard doorframe in which a *mezuzah* has been buried too deep, but rather a case of *petach she'be'churei hadelet* — a door situated behind another door. This refers specifically to a configuration in which two doors meet at a perpendicular angle, so that the opening of one door gives directly onto the space of the other. The question in such a case is whether these count as one doorway or two, and consequently whether one or two *mezuzot* are required.

### Tefach Separation

The determining criterion, the Gemara explains, is whether the two perpendicular doorways are separated from one another by a *tefach*. If the space between them is at least a *tefach*, they are counted as two distinct doorways, each requiring its own *mezuzah*. If the separation is less than a *tefach*, they are treated as a single continuous doorway and only one *mezuzah* is required. The Gemara confirms that the end of the *baraita* states this explicitly: *petach she'achorei hadelet* — the opening behind the door — requires a separate *mezuzah* if there is a *tefach* separating it from the primary door, and does not require one if there is not. This rule is entirely independent of the preceding question about how deeply the *mezuzah* is embedded into the doorframe.

## Tasseh Principle

### Reed Case

A *baraita* presents a final case concerning the construction of the doorframe itself and the manner in which the *mezuzah* is inserted into it. If the doorframe is made of reeds — *malben shel kanim* — the procedure is as follows: one cuts open a single reed and inserts the *mezuzah* into it — *chotech shefoferet umenichah*. Whether the reeds run horizontally or vertically determines which reed is to be cut, but assuming the standard vertical orientation, the *mezuzah* is placed inside one of the vertical reeds, which now serves as the *mezuzah*'s housing within the frame. This in itself is unremarkable.

### Frame First

Rav Acha the son of Rava adds a critical qualification: the ruling applies only when the doorframe was first erected and *subsequently* the reed was cut open and the *mezuzah* inserted — *she'he'emido uvasof chatcho*. In that sequence, the act of cutting and placing is an active, deliberate deed performed upon an already-existing doorframe, and the *mezuzah* is properly made in its place.

### Tasseh v'Lo

If, however, one first cut the reed and placed the *mezuzah* inside it, and *then* erected the doorframe — *chatach v'heniach uvasof he'emido* — the *mezuzah* is invalid. The principle invoked is *ta'aseh v'lo min he'asui*: you must actively make or do, not simply allow something to come into being automatically. This principle is most familiar from the laws of *sukkot*, where the verse *chag hasukkot ta'aseh lecha* teaches that one must actively construct the *sukkah* — a *sukkah* that forms itself automatically, such as by digging a hollow in a haystack, is invalid because no act of making was performed. Here, if the doorframe is erected with the *mezuzah* already inside the reed, the *mezuzah* has not been actively affixed to an existing doorframe; instead, the doorframe and *mezuzah* came into their combined state simultaneously, without any discrete act of placing the *mezuzah* upon an already-standing frame.

Notably, the Torah does not use the word *ta'aseh* in the context of *mezuzah* — the relevant verse reads *uchtavtem al mezuzot beitecha*. Nevertheless, the Gemara applies the principle of *ta'aseh v'lo min he'asui* here, understanding it as a general *halachic* requirement: the *mezuzah* must be written upon an existing doorpost and then actively placed, not come to be in place as a byproduct of the doorframe's construction.
