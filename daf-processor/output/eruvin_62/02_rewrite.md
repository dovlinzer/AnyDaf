# Eruvin 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv Chatzeros Setup

### Perek Overview

The new *perek* marks a transition in the tractate from *eruv techumin* to *eruv chatzeros*, and with it comes an entirely new set of questions: who may participate in the eruv, who renders the courtyard problematic, and what sense of community is being constructed through the institution of *eruv chatzeros* itself. The *perek* addresses the full and fascinating issue of participation, exclusion, and the social reality created when Jews attempt to establish a unified domain in a shared courtyard.

### Mishnah Problem

The opening *mishnah* establishes that if a non-Jew — or someone who does not accept the *eruv*, such as a *Kuti* or *Tzaduki* — resides in the courtyard, that person renders the courtyard forbidden to the Jewish residents. Strikingly, the *mishnah* offers no solution whatsoever; it does not suggest any mechanism by which the problem might be remedied. The non-Jew's presence simply creates a prohibition, and the *mishnah* leaves matters there.

## Abaye's Challenge

### Scene Setup

The *gemara* opens on 62a with a vignette: *Abaye bar Avin* was sitting together with *Rav Chanina bar Avin* — brothers — and *Abaye* was sitting alongside them. The brothers were engaged in a discussion about the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov concerning the effect of non-Jewish residents on the courtyard.

### Meir vs. Eliezer

The dispute under discussion is as follows. Rabbi Meir holds that the presence of non-Jews in the courtyard creates a problem regardless of how many Jews reside there — whether one Jew or two. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, by contrast, holds that the non-Jews pose a problem only when there are two or more Jews in the courtyard. If there is only one Jew — even if he shares the courtyard with ten non-Jews or ten *Kutim* — he may carry freely: from the courtyard into his house, from his house into the courtyard, and even from the courtyard into the non-Jews' houses and back, all without an *eruv*.

### Abaye's Logic

Abaye bar Avin and Rav Chanina bar Avin were reasoning as follows: *bishlama* Rabbi Meir — we understand Rabbi Meir. He holds *diras akum shema dira*: the domicile of a non-Jew is legally considered a domicile. His presence in the courtyard registers as a distinct domain, and since he is not part of the unified community, he disrupts the shared space. Therefore, *velo shna chad velo shna terei* — it makes no difference whether there is one Jew or two; the non-Jew's presence is always problematic.

But what does Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov hold? *Mai kasavar?* If he holds *diras akum shema dira* — that the non-Jew's domicile counts — then *afilu chad nami nitzur*: even with one Jew it should be forbidden. And if he holds *lo shema dira* — that the non-Jew's domicile is legally irrelevant — then *afilu terei nami lo nitzur*: even with two Jews it should be permitted. What, then, is the significance of the distinction between one Jew and two? Make up your mind: either the non-Jew is relevant or he is not.

## Gezeirah Framework

### Non-Jews Not Relevant

Abaye responds by challenging the brothers' foundational assumption about Rabbi Meir. They had assumed that Rabbi Meir's position — that non-Jews are always problematic regardless of how many Jews reside in the courtyard — rests on the principle that *diras akum shema dira*. Abaye rejects this. He says: and does Rabbi Meir truly hold that the non-Jew's domicile is a domicile? The *gemara* then invokes a *baraita* — which will be quoted in full later in the *daf* — stating: *chatzeros shel oved kochavim harei hu ke-diras shel behema*. The courtyard of a non-Jew is like a stable.

This is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, a deeply striking formulation. Steinsaltz suggests that the point is simply that the non-Jew's courtyard is not relevant for *halachic* purposes — that it does not carry the legal weight of a residential domain. Even granting that interpretive charity, the metaphor remains jarring. But the *halachic* point is clear: according to Rabbi Meir, as expressed in this *baraita*, the non-Jew's presence has no effect. It is not as though another person is living in the courtyard for purposes of *hilchot eruvin*; they are simply not part of the Jewish community being legally constituted.

And yet Rabbi Meir says they create a problem. How can one hold simultaneously that the non-Jew's domicile is irrelevant and that their presence makes it forbidden to carry? The resolution, Abaye explains, is that this is not a formal, principled *halachic* problem. It is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic decree — and a matter of policy.

*De-kulei alma diras akum lo shemei dirah* — everyone agrees, Abaye says, that a non-Jew's domicile is not considered a domicile for these purposes. *Hecha be-gezeirah shema yilmedu mima'asav kamiflegi* — the entire dispute about whether and when non-Jews make the courtyard problematic is a dispute about a rabbinic decree, enacted out of concern that Jews not become accustomed to living among non-Jews.

### Rabbinical Policy

This is quite a remarkable reading of the *mishnah*. The entire structure of the laws governing non-Jews in the courtyard — the prohibition on carrying, the demand for *sechirut*, the various distinctions between one Jew and two — is not rooted in any formal *halachic* category about domain or residence. It is social policy, enacted through the vehicle of *hilchot eruvin*, designed to make it difficult for Jews to establish themselves in mixed courtyards. The rabbis chose *Shabbat* as the point of pressure: they would not tell you what to do during the week, but they would make carrying on *Shabbat* difficult if you chose to live among non-Jews, thereby creating a counter-incentive.

### Community Coherence

This raises a broader point about what *eruvin* actually does. The modern scholar Charlotte Fonrobert has written extensively about *eruvin* and its role in creating coherent Jewish communities — not merely as a matter of architecture or *hilchot reshuyot*, but as a social institution that shapes urban patterns. When an *eruv* is established, property values shift, Jewish families concentrate, and a dense communal reality takes shape. The *eruv* creates a space; people want to carry on *Shabbat*; and so they come together within that space.

One can observe this phenomenon in cities around the world. The contrast is instructive: in Paris, for instance, where no *eruv* exists, Jews are dispersed throughout the city, and the formerly dense Jewish neighborhood that existed before the Second World War — when there was an *eruv* — has dissipated. The *eruv* creates community in a very concrete and measurable sense.

With this in mind, the *gemara*'s explanation of the *mishnah* takes on additional resonance. The rabbis, by making *eruv* more difficult to establish in the presence of non-Jews, were not merely regulating *Shabbat* carrying; they were shaping the social geography of Jewish life, encouraging a concentrated and coherent Jewish community rather than a diffuse, mixed one. The *mishnah*'s silence about any solution — it offers no mechanism for overcoming the non-Jew's presence — may itself reflect this intention. Perhaps, at the level of the *mishnah*, there was no solution at all.

## Eliezer's Logic

### Safety Rationale

Given this *gezeirah* framework, the *gemara* now works out how each *tanna* applies it. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds: *kivan de-oichal imach chashud ashfichut damim* — since a non-Jew who eats with you may be suspected of murderous intent. This reflects the reality of the period in which the *mishnah* was written, when Roman persecution was a genuine and documented danger, and when concern about the intentions of non-Jewish neighbors was not merely theoretical.

Against this background, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reasons: *trei d'shchichi d'dayri* — two Jews might realistically choose to live together in a courtyard shared with non-Jews, because together they feel less vulnerable. Two Jews would feel secure enough to remain in such a situation. *Gazru behu* — therefore the rabbis made a decree applying to that scenario.

### One Jew Rare

*Chad lo shchiach* — but one Jew living alone in a courtyard full of non-Jews? That is rare. Who would do that? And because that scenario is sufficiently uncommon, *lo gazru bei rabbanan* — the rabbis did not extend the decree to cover it. Therefore, one Jew among non-Jews may carry freely; as soon as there are two Jews, the decree takes effect.

This explanation is admittedly strange. The rabbis are effectively sending a signal — even if unintentionally — that one Jew may live in such a situation but two may not. It is a paradoxical message: we will restrict the more common case and leave the rarer one unrestricted. One might also ask: what about the opposite scenario? If ninety-nine Jews live in a courtyard with one non-Jew, is the non-Jew still problematic for all of them? These difficulties with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position remain, and the *gemara* does not fully resolve them. Nevertheless, what is significant is the underlying framework: this is fundamentally a *takkana* — a policy determination — not a principled ruling about the nature of domains.

## Meir's Position

### Meir's Case

Rabbi Meir's position is now explained within the same *gezeirah* framework. He holds: *zimnin d'mikri v'dayri* — sometimes it happens that one Jew ends up living among non-Jews. The scenario occurs, even if infrequently. And therefore *v'amru rabbanan ein eruv mo'il bimkom oved kochavim* — the rabbis declared that an *eruv* will not be effective in the presence of non-Jews, even though in principle, as established, the non-Jew's domicile is not a domicile. *V'ein bitul reshut mo'il bimkom oved kochavim* — and similarly, even the mechanism of *bitul reshut* — waiving one's rights in the domain — will not solve the problem. Both potential remedies are closed off.

### Sechirut Solution

The one and only remedy, according to this approach, is *sechirut* — rental. *Ad sheyaskir* — only if the Jew rents the non-Jew's domain can the problem be resolved. The *mishnah*, which mentions no solution at all, may reflect a position that nothing works. The *gemara*, however, introduces *sechirut* as a theoretical solution — while simultaneously explaining why it remains practically difficult.

## Rental Problem

### Fear of Rights

*V'oved kochavim lo mugar* — the non-Jew will not want to rent. Why? *Mai taama?* The *gemara* proposes: *ei neima mishum d'savar dilma ati l'achzukei b'reshuso* — perhaps he fears that if he rents his space to the Jew every *Shabbat*, the Jew will eventually come to claim real, ongoing rights in the courtyard. What begins as a formal religious arrangement might be leveraged into a permanent claim of possession. The non-Jew will therefore be reluctant to cooperate.

### Bria vs. Reua'a

But this explanation, the *gemara* notes, works only *haniicha l'man d'amar sechirut bria ba'inan* — if we require a "healthy rental," one with genuine legal substance. *Ela l'man d'amar sechirut reu'a ba'inan* — but if all we require is a "shaky rental," a pro forma arrangement with no real transfer of rights, *mai icha lemeimar* — why would the non-Jew resist? If the entire transaction is obviously a ritual with no practical legal consequences, what does he stand to lose?

The *gemara* here introduces a debate between *Rav Chisda* and *Rav Sheshet*. Rav Chisda holds *sechirut bria* — you need a healthy rental. Rav Sheshet holds *sechirut reu'a* — a shaky rental suffices. The question of what precisely distinguishes the two positions will be explored shortly. The parallel to *mechiras chametz* is apt: how real is the transaction, and which legal system — Jewish or non-Jewish — governs its validity when the two parties operate under different frameworks?

## Bnei Noach Laws

### Pruta Threshold

The *gemara* initially considers whether the distinction between *bria* and *reu'a* concerns the monetary amount involved: perhaps *bria* means giving at least a *pruta* — the minimum halachically recognized coin — and *reu'a* means giving something worth less than a *pruta*. The *gemara* immediately challenges this interpretation with a pointed question: *mi ika l'man d'amar me-akum pachos mishavei pruta lo?* — is there anyone who would say that when dealing with a non-Jew, less than a *pruta* is legally insignificant?

This question is genuinely surprising, since the standard assumption throughout *halachah* is that less than a *pruta* is legally meaningless. Yet the *gemara* asserts that when non-Jews are involved, even less than a *pruta* carries legal weight.

The basis for this is a teaching transmitted by Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *she-sochrim min ha-akum afilu b'pachos mishavei pruta* — one may rent from a non-Jew even with an amount less than a *pruta*. And the conceptual foundation for this principle is supplied by another statement in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: *ben Noach neherag al pachos mishavei pruta* — a *ben Noach* is liable to capital punishment for theft of less than a *pruta*.

### Pruta for Rental

This invokes the broader framework of *sheva mitzvot Bnei Noach*. In that framework, punishment for any violation is in principle *chayav misa* — there are no graduated penalties as there are in Jewish law (restitution, *malkot*, and so on); the single category of liability is capital. But beyond the severity of punishment, the *gemara* is drawing attention to a distinctive structural feature of the *Bnei Noach* laws: the concept of *shiurim* — minimum thresholds — does not apply to them. Any amount of theft constitutes *gezel*. Any portion of *eiver min hachai* constitutes a violation. Any degree of life — including a fetus — is considered life for purposes of the prohibition against *retzicha*.

There is a pattern here: the minimum thresholds that Jewish law carefully calibrates do not operate in the *Bnei Noach* framework. A *kezayis* of *eiver min hachai*, a *pruta*'s worth of *gezel* — these minimum measures are Jewish legal constructs that do not transfer. One might speculate that the *Bnei Noach* laws function on a more categorical level: all stealing is prohibited, full stop, without the nuanced gradations that Jewish jurisprudence developed. The consequence — that liability is triggered by amounts that would be irrelevant under Jewish law — could be understood as reflecting a deliberate policy of categorical simplicity, even if that simplicity carries its own complications regarding the relative stringency of the two systems.

The *gemara* in Sanhedrin does make the assertion that nothing should be permitted to a non-Jew that is forbidden to a Jew. The *rishonim* engage seriously with this principle alongside the apparent discrepancies in threshold and punishment, and the tension is genuine. But the operative point here is that since less than a *pruta* is legally significant in transactions involving non-Jews — because the non-Jew is *makpid* even on the smallest amount — such a minimal payment constitutes a valid rental even by rigorous standards.

### Pruta Debate

This point is explained differently by Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi's explanation, interestingly, inverts the common stereotype: it is not Jews but non-Jews who are *makpid* on the smallest amounts of money. Jews, by contrast, are accustomed to being *mochel* — to forgoing — trivial sums. Therefore, from the non-Jew's perspective as the recipient, even the most minimal payment is genuinely meaningful and sufficient to finalize a transaction. Since the non-Jew takes even a fraction of a *pruta* seriously, such a payment constitutes a valid consideration for the rental.

Tosafot offers a different reading of *v'lo nitan le-hishavon* — the phrase stating that there is no requirement to return the stolen amount. Rashi understands this as a positive commandment: Jews have a *mitzvah* to return stolen objects, while non-Jews do not. Tosafot reads it differently: it means that if a non-Jew steals less than a *pruta* from a Jew, there is no obligation of return — because from the Jew's perspective, less than a *pruta* is not *makpid*-worthy, and the loss is not legally cognizable.

## Bria/Reua'a Detail

### Rashi: Use Rights

Having established that the distinction between *bria* and *reu'a* is not about the amount paid, the *gemara* turns to what it actually means. *Ela bria bimuharki ve-aburgini, re'uah belo muharki ve-aburgini.* Rashi explains that *muharki* and *aburgini* refer to chairs and benches. A *sechirut bria* — a healthy rental — means that the Jew, having rented the space, may fill the entire courtyard with his furniture and effectively exercise physical dominion over it on *Shabbat*; the non-Jew cannot object, because he has rented out his domain. A *sechirut reu'a* — a shaky rental — means the Jew acquires no practical use rights at all; the transaction is purely formal, and he cannot do anything on *Shabbat* he could not have done the day before.

### Tosafot: Registration

Tosafot and the *Aruch* explain these terms differently. *Muharki* and *aburgini* refer to official registration — the degree to which the transaction is formally documented before authorities, something like notarization. A *bria* rental is one that is officially recorded and legally formalized; a *reu'a* rental is informal, unregistered, and legally thin. One might ask whether any rental arrangement between Jews requires such formal documentation — a standard Jewish contract requires witnesses, not notarial records. The question of why a non-Jew's involvement would change this requirement is a fair one, and the answer likely lies in the intent to make the transaction practically difficult.

### Witchcraft Fear

This brings the *gemara* back to its original question: if the rental is purely a *reu'a* — a pro forma ritual with no real transfer of rights — why would the non-Jew refuse to cooperate? The answer: *chashish akum lechshafim* — the non-Jew fears witchcraft, and therefore *velo mogir* — he will not rent.

What does this mean? The *gemara* has a kind of self-awareness here: non-Jews observe Jews performing strange rituals — wires on telephone poles, formal rental agreements for spaces they do not actually use, mysterious weekly transactions — and they do not understand what is happening. The concern about witchcraft reflects the non-Jew's perception of an incomprehensible religious practice. If the entire transaction is obviously not a real rental, then from the non-Jew's perspective it is something else entirely — some form of ritual manipulation of his space, perhaps intended to give Jews mystical power over it. He wants no part of it. The *gemara*'s observation captures something true: if the transaction cannot be explained as a straightforward commercial arrangement, it looks, from the outside, like something uncanny.

## Non-Jew Stable

### Stable Metaphor

The *gemara* now turns to the *baraita* invoked earlier: *chatzeros shel oved kochavim harei hu ke-dir shel behema* — the courtyard of a non-Jew is like a stable. The *halachic* content of this statement, bracketing the troubling metaphor, is that the non-Jew's presence does not create a legally distinct domain for purposes of *hilchot eruvin*. It is not considered a separate *reshut* requiring unification.

### No Jew Present

Therefore, the *baraita* continues, *mutar lehachnis ulehotzi min hachatzer labatim umin habatim lachatzer* — it is permitted to carry from the courtyard into the houses and from the houses into the courtyard. The scenario envisioned here is one in which no Jew is living in the courtyard. A Jew may be visiting his non-Jewish neighbors on *Shabbat* — he is present, but not resident. In that case, since the non-Jews' presence has no *halachic* status as creating a distinct domain, the entire space is treated as a unified area, and carrying is freely permitted.

The underlying principle, again, is that in the absence of any Jew establishing residence in this courtyard, the *takkana* designed to discourage such living arrangements has no occasion to operate. The non-Jews' houses do not, in and of themselves, create the *eruv*-related problems that Jewish houses would create. They are, for these purposes, treated as though they were not separate residential units at all.

### Jew Arrives

The situation changes entirely the moment a Jew moves in. *V'im yesh sham Yisrael echad, oser* — if one Jew takes up residence, carrying becomes forbidden. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, as noted, holds that the problem arises only when there are two Jewish residents: *l'olam eino oser ad she'yehu shnei Yisraelim osrim zeh al zeh* — it is never forbidden until there are two Jews who forbid one another.

What is striking about this formulation is how dramatically it illustrates the *takkana* nature of the prohibition. In principle, the non-Jews' presence is entirely irrelevant. The courtyard remains freely usable. The moment a Jew arrives — not because of any change in the non-Jews' status, not because of any new *halachic* reality regarding domains — the entire courtyard becomes forbidden. The prohibition is triggered by the Jew, and its rationale is entirely rooted in the rabbinic policy of discouraging Jewish residence among non-Jews.

## Mishnah Harmony

### Contradiction

The *gemara* now raises a textual difficulty. The *mishnah* states: *hadar im akkum b'chatzer harei zeh oser alav* — one who lives with a non-Jew in the courtyard: the non-Jew renders it forbidden to him. This formulation suggests that it is the non-Jew who imposes the restriction on the Jew. But the *baraita* states: *v'im yesh sham Yisrael echad, oser* — if there is a Jew there, he makes it forbidden. This formulation suggests the opposite: it is the Jew who imposes the restriction, presumably on the ability to carry into the non-Jews' houses. Who is making it forbidden to whom?

### Present vs. Absent

The resolution: *lo kashya — ha d'isa, ha d'leisa*. The two cases differ based on whether the non-Jew is actually present. When the non-Jew is living in the courtyard — *d'isa*, he is there — it is the non-Jew who makes it forbidden to the Jew: the Jew cannot carry freely from his house to the courtyard and back, because of the *gezeirah* against living among non-Jews. When the non-Jew is absent — *d'leisa*, he is not there — it is the Jew who imposes a restriction: specifically, the Jew's presence makes it forbidden to carry into the non-Jews' houses.

## Presence Matters

### Jews Always

The *gemara* now draws out the full implications of this distinction. By Jews, the rule is different: a Jewish resident creates a *halachic* problem even when he is not home. If five Jews live in a courtyard and four of them go away for *Shabbat*, the absent four still require their rights to be addressed in the *eruv*. Jewish residents create a real, formal *halachic* issue — a genuine problem of *reshuyot* — and therefore even their absence does not eliminate the need to include them.

### Non-Jews When Home

By non-Jews, the situation is different. Since the entire problem is rooted in a *gezeirah* — a policy concern about the reality of Jews living among non-Jews — the problem only applies when the non-Jews are actually present. *De-chi ita assar, de-chi leita lo assar* — when he is home, the decree applies; when he is not home, it does not. The concern that motivated the decree — the reality of cohabitation in a mixed courtyard — does not exist when the non-Jew is simply away. Therefore the rabbis did not extend the decree to that scenario.

### Left House Case

A challenge to this principle: does the *mishnah* not teach that one who leaves his house and goes to spend *Shabbat* in another city — *ha-maniach et beito ve-halach lo lishbot be-ir acheret* — is still considered to make the courtyard forbidden? And the case of *echad akum ve-echad Yisrael oser divrei Rabbi Meir* — the *mishnah* seems to indicate that even an absent non-Jew continues to impose a restriction. If so, how can we say that a non-Jew who is not present creates no problem?

### Same-Day Return

The *gemara* resolves this: *hatam de-ati beyomei* — that case concerns a situation where the non-Jew has not gone far and might realistically return on the same day. Since there is a genuine possibility that he will be present during *Shabbat*, the rabbis treated his status as though he were still there. But if he has genuinely gone away and cannot return that day, the decree does not apply and he does not render the courtyard forbidden.

## Rulings Summary

### Shmuel Rules

Having established the theoretical framework, the *gemara* turns to *pesak*. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halachah ke-Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Shmuel that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. This is a leniency: the problem arises only with two Jewish residents, not one.

## Paskening Before Rebbe

### Abaye Asks

Abaye poses a question to Rav Yosef: *kaima lan mishnat Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov kav ve-naki* — it is a standing principle that the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is like a sifted measure of grain: compact, precise, and authoritative. Furthermore, Rav Yehuda explicitly rules in the name of Shmuel that the *halachah* follows him. One might add that the principle *halachah ke-divrei ha-meikel be-eruv* — in matters of *eruvin*, we follow the lenient opinion — points in the same direction. Given all this, Abaye asks: *mahu le-oruyei bimkom rabbo* — is it permitted to render this ruling in the presence of one's *rebbe*?

## Rulings Summary…

### Minhag View

Before turning to Rav Yosef's response, the *gemara* notes two additional positions. Rav Huna says *minhag* — accepted practice is in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. According to Rashi, this means that we do not make a public pronouncement about it, but if anyone asks a *shaila*, that is how we *pasken*. The *minhag* is operative but not loudly proclaimed.

### People Practice

Rabbi Yochanan formulates it differently: *nahagu* ke-Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — people practice in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. This is a weaker endorsement: Rabbi Yochanan might personally prefer a different *psak*, but since the practice has become widespread, he does not intervene to overturn it. If asked directly for a ruling, he would give a stricter answer; but he accepts the established practice as a fait accompli.

## Paskening Before Rebbe…

### Yosef Answers

Rav Yosef responds to Abaye's question with a vivid example. *Afilu be'ata be-kutcha ba'u minei mi-Rav Chisda kol shenei de-Rav Huna ve-lo ori* — even the question of whether one may eat an egg dipped in a milk-based condiment (*kutcha*), the students would pose to Rav Chisda throughout all the years that Rav Huna was alive, and Rav Chisda would refuse to rule. Even so obvious and elementary a question — one that any child would know the answer to — Rav Chisda would not answer in the presence of his *rebbe* Rav Huna. The implication is unambiguous: when your *rebbe* is present, you do not issue rulings, regardless of how self-evident the answer may be.

### Written Law

Rav Yaakov bar Abba then poses a further challenge to Abaye: *kegon Megillat Taanit* — what about a case involving *Megillat Taanit*? This is a book that lists the days on which fasting and eulogies are prohibited. If someone asks: may I fast today, or may I deliver a *hesped* today — and the answer is written explicitly in the book, right there on the shelf (*de-ketiva u-mancha*) — is stating that answer considered *paskening*? Is it not merely reading from a text? And yet *mahu le-oruyei be-atra de-rabbeih* — is it permitted even to do that in the locale of one's *rebbe*, even if not in his immediate physical presence?

## Tosafot Qualifies

### Prevent Sin

Tosafot qualifies the sweeping prohibition against *paskening* before one's *rebbe* in several important ways. The first qualification: one may rule that something is *assur* in order to prevent an actual transgression. If a person asks a question and there is a real possibility that, without receiving an answer, he will proceed to do something forbidden, the student may step in and declare the act *assur*. The duty to prevent an *issur* from being committed overrides the prohibition against *paskening* before the *rebbe*.

### Hypothetical OK

Tosafot's second qualification: if the question is not a practical *shaila* — if the person is not asking for a ruling to govern his immediate conduct but is raising the matter as a hypothetical, as part of *talmud Torah* discussion — then responding is permitted. The prohibition against *paskening* applies to actual *pesak halachah*; academic engagement with *halachic* questions, even in the form of "how would you rule in such a case," does not cross that line. Tosafot acknowledges that many people frame practical questions as hypotheticals, but the principle stands: a genuinely theoretical discussion is not *paskening*.

### Novel Issue

Tosafot's third qualification pushes against what the *gemara* seems to say on its face: even in cases of actual *pesak*, the prohibition applies only when there is a real *chiddush* involved — a novel point of law or interpretation. If the answer is genuinely and completely obvious, it is not really *paskening*. Tosafot reads the cases cited in the *gemara* not as examples of self-evident law but as cases that contain a genuine *chiddush*.

### Examples

On this reading, the case of the egg in *kutcha* is not the trivial question it appears to be. What was actually asked, Tosafot argues, was a more complex case: a chicken was slaughtered (*shecht*) and an egg was found still inside it. The question — is that egg *fleishig*? — involves a genuine *chiddush*, not an obvious answer. Similarly, the question about *Megillat Taanit* involved some non-obvious *halachic* nuance about the scope or application of that document's provisions. In both cases, the *chiddush* is what makes it a genuine act of *paskening* — and therefore something that Tosafot holds must involve a *chiddush* in order to be prohibited before one's *rebbe*. Tomorrow's discussion will continue to explore the precise contours of this prohibition: what exactly the concern of *kvod rav* entails, how it applies when so much of *halachah* is now codified in writing, and where exactly the line falls between *talmud Torah* and *pesak*.
