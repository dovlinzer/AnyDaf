# Bava Batra 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cistern & Plaster

### Mazik vs. Nizak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל הַמַּזִּיק לְהַרְחִיק אֶת עַצְמוֹ
>
> **Translation:** upon the one causing the damage to distance himself

The daf opens by continuing the discussion of activities that cannot be conducted near a neighbor's structure when doing so would threaten or damage it. By way of orientation, two important points emerge from the preceding Gemara. First, reading the *mishnah* and related sources according to their plain meaning (*p'shat*), one may conduct activities right up to a boundary line so long as nothing on the other side is threatened — the restriction only applies when a neighbor's property stands to be damaged. Second, and more broadly, the daf returns to the ongoing debate between *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Yose regarding who bears the burden of distancing.

The *Rabbanan* maintain *al ha-mazik le-har'chik et atzmo* — it is upon the one causing the damage to distance himself. He must limit his activities in his own yard or field because they may indirectly impact his neighbor. Rabbi Yose, by contrast, grants the occupant wide latitude: one may do as one pleases in one's own domain, and if the neighbor does not wish to sustain damage, it is upon him to move his property — *al ha-nizak le-har'chik et atzmo*. The sole exception Rabbi Yose acknowledges is direct damage, though precisely what falls into that category remains to be worked out as the chapter unfolds.

### Machmetzan & Nadyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא נִבְרֶכֶת הַכּוֹבְסִין וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מִן הַמַּחְמְצָן, אֲבָל מִן הַנַּדְיָין – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Nor** may one set up **a launderer's pond** near his neighbor's wall unless he distances it three handbreadths from the wall. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: They taught this only with regard to a soaking pond,** in which soiled clothes are left to soak for several days. **But** in the case **of a washing pond [*hanadyan*],** where clothes are actively cleaned, **four cubits** are required.

The *mishnah* states: *v'lo ya'aseh et ha-kovsin* — one may not make a laundry pit adjacent to a neighbor's cistern. The seepage of water from such a pit, and the digging involved in its construction, can compromise the neighboring cistern.

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *lo shanu ela min ha-machmetzan* — the rule stated in the *mishnah* (a distance of three *tefachim*) applies only to the *machmetzan*, the soaking pit. As Rashi explains, this was a vat in which garments were submerged for a day or two in a mixture of water and dog excrement, which served as a kind of detergent to loosen dirt and stains. *Aval min ha-nadyan* — but from the *nadyan*, the rinsing pit where garments were vigorously scrubbed and beaten in relatively clean water, *arba amot*, four *amot* of distance are required.

This is initially counterintuitive: the rinsing pit seems far less noxious than the soaking pit, yet it requires greater distance. Rashi resolves this: *mitokh she-ha-mayim nitazin le-merachok* — because the vigorous scrubbing and beating of garments causes water to splatter far distances. The laundering process was evidently not the gentle hand-washing one might picture; garments were beaten against stone surfaces with force, sending water spraying considerable distances.

### Mishnah vs. Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: נִבְרֶכֶת הַכּוֹבְסִין – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים! אֶלָּא לָאו – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּדְרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** That opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **A launderer's pond** must be kept **four cubits** from one's neighbor's wall. **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that one must keep a distance of only **three handbreadths? Rather,** must one **not conclude from** the *baraita* that the statement of **Rav Naḥman** is correct?

The Gemara notes a difficulty: *tanya nami hachi* — a *braita* likewise states that the laundry pit must be four *amot* from the cistern, while our *mishnah* says only three *tefachim*. This apparent contradiction is resolved precisely through the distinction between the *machmetzan* and the *nadyan*: the *mishnah*'s three *tefachim* refers to the soaking pit, while the *braita*'s four *amot* refers to the rinsing pit. Some versions of the Gemara open directly with this contradiction and then arrive at Rav Nachman's resolution. Either way, the conclusion is the same. Indeed, Rav Chiya bar Abba and Rav Avia transmit this distinction explicitly in the text of the *mishnah* itself, with the word *machmetzan* appearing there directly.

### And vs. Or Plaster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָד בְּסִיד. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״וְסָד בְּסִיד״ תְּנַן, אוֹ דִילְמָא ״אוֹ סָד בְּסִיד״ תְּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who digs a pit must distance it three handbreadths from another's property and **plaster** it **with lime. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What is the precise wording of the mishna? **Did we learn: And plasters with lime,** meaning that the walls must be plastered with lime in addition to distancing the pit three handbreadths, **or perhaps we learned: Or plasters with lime,** i.e., one may plaster the walls with lime instead of digging the pit at a distance of three handbreadths.

The *mishnah* states that beyond distancing three *tefachim*, one must also apply plaster — *v'sid b'sid*. The Gemara raises the question: *v'sid b'sid tnan o sid b'sid tnan* — does the *mishnah* require both the distance and the plaster, or does it offer a choice between them?

The Gemara initially suggests this is obvious: the *mishnah* must mean "and" — both are required. The argument runs as follows. Later in the *mishnah*, when discussing damage not to a cistern but to a wall — the *gefet* (residue from the olive press), excrement, salt, and so on — the requirement is either three *tefachim* or plastering, one or the other. If the rule for the cistern were also an "or" formulation, the *mishnah* should have combined all the cases into a single list. The fact that two separate lists appear implies that the legal requirements differ: the first category (cistern) demands both distance and plaster, while the second (wall) demands only one or the other.

The Gemara pushes back: perhaps the two lists are separated not because of different legal requirements but simply because they involve different categories of damage — *reisha heizeika d'matuna*, the first involves seepage and moisture, while *seifa heizeika d'havla*, the second involves heat. Two categories of harm, two separate lists — yet the underlying rule might still be the same.

### Soft Ground Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סֶלַע הַבָּא בְּיָדַיִם – זֶה חוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹ מִכָּאן, וְזֶה חוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹ מִכָּאן; זֶה מַרְחִיק שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים וְסָד בְּסִיד, וָזֶה מַרְחִיק שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים וְסָד בְּסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **rock that** is so soft it **crumbles in** one's **hands, this one digs his pit from here,** on his property, **and that one digs his pit from there. This one distances** his pit **three handbreadths and plasters with lime, and that one distances** his pit **three handbreadths and plasters with lime.**

The Gemara seeks to resolve the question from a statement of Rabbi Yehuda regarding a case of *sela ha-ba b'yadayim* — ground so soft it can be dug with one's hands. In such a case, if two neighbors each dig a cistern from opposite sides, each must distance three *tefachim* and also plaster — *zeh marchik gimel tefachim v'sid b'sid v'zeh marchik gimel tefachim v'sid b'sid* — so that a total of six *tefachim* separates the two cisterns, with plaster applied on both sides. Here the "and" formulation is unmistakable.

One might infer from this that the "and" requirement applies specifically to soft ground, while hard ground requires only one of the two precautions. The Gemara rejects this: *hu hadin d'af al gav d'lav haba b'yadayim nami sid b'sid* — even with hard, firm ground, both distance and plaster are required. The *chiddush* of Rabbi Yehuda's statement is not that these precautions apply in soft ground; rather, it is that even in soft ground, three *tefachim* suffice. One might have thought that such unstable soil, which allows water to seep more easily, would demand even greater distance. The ruling tells us that three *tefachim* along with plastering is sufficient even there.

The Gemara does not reach a completely clean resolution, but its clear tendency is that the first list — things that damage a cistern — requires both distance and plaster together, while the second list — things that damage a wall — requires only one or the other.

## Heat Damage

### Heat-Giving Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַגֶּפֶת וְאֶת הַזֶּבֶל וְאֶת הַמֶּלַח וְאֶת הַסְּלָעִים וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one must distance the solid residue** of produce that has been pressed free of its oil, **and** animal **manure, and salt, and lime, and rocks** three handbreadths from the wall of another, or plaster its receptacle with lime.

The *mishnah* turns to a second category: *marchikin et ha-gefet v'et ha-zavel v'et ha-melach v'et ha-sela'im* — one must keep at a distance of three *tefachim* from a neighbor's wall the *gefet* (residue from the olive press), excrement, salt, and rocks. The concern here is not moisture but heat: these substances generate heat as they decompose or by their nature, and sustained heat near a wall causes structural damage.

### Two Lists Compared

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בַּמֶּה טוֹמְנִין וּבַמָּה אֵין טוֹמְנִין? אֵין טוֹמְנִין לֹא בַּגֶּפֶת, וְלֹא בַּזֶּבֶל, וְלֹא בַּמֶּלַח, וְלֹא בַּסִּיד, וְלֹא בַּחוֹל – בֵּין לַחִין בֵּין יְבֵשִׁין. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי סְלָעִים וְלָא קָתָנֵי חוֹל, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָתָנֵי חוֹל וְלָא קָתָנֵי סְלָעִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shabbat* 47b): **With what** substances **may one insulate** a pot of cooked food on Shabbat eve, **and with what** substances **may one not insulate** it? **One may insulate** the pot **neither with the solid residue** of produce that has been pressed free of its oil, **nor with manure, nor with salt, nor with lime, nor with sand, whether** those materials are **moist** or **whether** they are **dry.** All of these materials spontaneously generate heat when piled up for an extended period of time. Therefore, they add heat to the pot they insulate. The Gemara asks: **What is different here that** the mishna **teaches** the *halakha* in the case of **rocks and it does not teach** the *halakha* in the case of **sand, and what is different there that it teaches** the *halakha* in the case of **sand and it does not teach** the *halakha* in the case of **rocks?**

The Gemara now draws a comparison with a parallel discussion in tractate *Shabbat*, in the laws of *hatmana* — insulating hot foods before *Shabbat*. The relevant *halacha* is as follows. One may insulate hot dishes before *Shabbat* with materials that merely preserve heat. However, one may not insulate with substances that actively add heat. The paradigmatic case is ashes: submerging a pot in hot ashes continues to add heat, and the Rabbis prohibited this lest one come to stir the ashes, which could lead to kindling a fire. This prohibition was extended to all heat-adding substances, even those from which no fire could be created — organic materials that generate heat as they decompose, such as excrement and *gefet*.

The two lists — things that damage a wall and things forbidden for *hatmana* — are nearly identical, but not quite. Each list contains one item absent from the other. The wall list includes rocks; the *Shabbat* list does not. The *Shabbat* list includes sand; the wall list does not. The Gemara asks: *mai shna hacha de-katni sela'im v'lo katni chol, u-mai shna hatam de-katni chol v'lo katni sela'im* — why does each list include precisely the item the other lacks?

### Rocks Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַטְמִין בִּסְלָעִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי דַּרְכָּן שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַטְמִין בְּגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר וּלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן?! דְּתַנְיָא: טוֹמְנִין בְּגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר, וּבְצִיפֵּי צֶמֶר, וּבִלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן, וּבְמוֹכִין, וְאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** There is a practical reason for this difference. Rocks are not mentioned there **because it is not customary for people to insulate** food **with rocks. Abaye said to him: And is it customary for people to insulate** food **with wool fleece and tabs of purple wool? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may insulate** food **with wool fleece; with combed wool clumps,** which are unwoven; **with tabs of purple wool; and with** swatches of **soft material; but** one **may not move them** on Shabbat because they are set-aside [*muktze*].

Rav Yosef offers an initial answer: *lefi she-ein darkan shel bnei adam le-hatmin be-sela'im* — it is not the normal practice to insulate food in rocks, and so they were simply not included in the *Shabbat* list.

Abaye challenges this: *ve-chi darkan shel bnei adam le-hatmin be-gizei tzemer u-ve-leshonot shel argaman?* And is it really the normal practice to insulate food in wool shearings or in strands of royal purple thread? A *braita* explicitly lists *gizei tzemer* (wool shearings), *tzipei tzemer* (combed wool), *leshonot shel argaman* (strands of purple thread), and *mochin* (rags) as items permitted for *hatmana*. These are hardly everyday insulation materials, yet they appear in the list. If unusual materials are still listed, "not normally done" cannot explain the omission of rocks.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ״; תְּנָא הָכָא סְלָעִים – וְהוּא הַדִּין לְחוֹל, תְּנָא הָתָם חוֹל – וְהוּא הַדִּין לִסְלָעִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ״, לִיתְנִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ בַּחֲדָא, וְלִיתְנֵי חֲדָא מִנַּיְיהוּ בְּאִידַּךְ – וְהוּא הַדִּין לְאִידַּךְ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the *tanna* follows the biblical aphorism in the verse that states: **"Its neighbor tells about him"** (Job 36:33), i.e., one example is mentioned and the same applies to the other case. **He taught** the *halakha* in the case of **rocks here and the same is true of sand; he taught** the *halakha* in the case of **sand there and the same is true of rocks. Rava said to** Abaye: **If** this is correct, that **"its neighbor tells about him," let him teach** the *halakha* of **all of** these examples **in one** case, **and let him teach** the *halakha* of just **one in the other** case, **and** it can be said that **the same is true** with regard **to the others.**

Abaye therefore proposes a different approach: *yagid alav re'o* — a phrase drawn from the book of Iyov, meaning "his companion will tell about it." The literary device here is that neither list is complete; each informs the other. Whatever appears on one list is implicitly included in the other.

Rava objects to this approach. If the intent were simply to have each list supplement the other, the Gemara should have made one list fully comprehensive and let a single item from the other list cover the remainder. Having two parallel lists of five items each, differing only in the final entry, is a clumsy way to accomplish this — *litninehu le-chulhu be-chada*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא קָתָנֵי סְלָעִים – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְשַׁתְּכִי לַהּ לִקְדֵרָה; הָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא קָתָנֵי חוֹל – מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵחַמִּימֵי חָיִים וּמִקָּרִירֵי קָרֵיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: There, this is** the **reason that** the *tanna* **does not teach** the *halakha* in the case of **rocks: Because they break,** i.e., scratch, **the pot,** and consequently people do not use them for insulating food at all. **Here, this is** the **reason that** the *tanna* **does not teach** the *halakha* in the case of **sand: Because it heats hot** items **and cools cold** items, and therefore it does not cause any damage to the wall.

Rava therefore offers a precise explanation for why rocks appear on the wall list but not the *Shabbat* list: *mishum de-mashtchi lah le-kdeira* — rocks damage the pot. Rashi offers two readings of this phrase: either the rocks physically scrape and scratch the pot as it rests among them, or they cause the pot to rust. In either case, rocks are specifically contraindicated for use in insulation because they cause affirmative harm to the vessel, which explains their complete absence from the *Shabbat* discussion.

### Tosafot Practice

Tosafot is not satisfied with Rashi's explanation on its own. Tosafot agrees that *mashtchi le-kdeira* refers to rusting rather than breaking, but notes that this only explains why rocks are an especially poor choice — it does not explain the absence from the list differently from the mere "not normally done" reasoning that Abaye already rejected.

Tosafot offers a further interpretation: *de-lo shechi'cha hi klal, shari le-hatmin bah, u-ve-milta de-lo shechi'cha lo gazru bah Rabanan* — since the practice is not at all common, the Rabbinic prohibition against *hatmana* in heat-adding substances simply never applied to rocks in the first place. Where something is so unusual as to be practically non-existent, the Rabbis never enacted a decree against it.

Tosafot then draws a remarkable practical consequence: *ve-khen minhagenu she-anu nohagim le-hatmin be-sela'im she-korin kilos* — it is the Ashkenazic practice to actually insulate pots in a certain type of rock (referred to as *kilos*, possibly a form of flint or volcanic stone) before *Shabbat*. The reasoning forms an interesting loop: because it was not normally done, no rabbinic prohibition was enacted; because no prohibition was enacted, it is in fact permissible to do it; and therefore they actually do it.

### Sand Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: חוֹל! הָתָם בִּמְתוּנָא. תַּנָּא דִּידַן נָמֵי לִיתְנֵי, וְלוֹקְמֵיהּ בִּמְתוּנָא! הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ אַמַּת הַמַּיִם. אַטּוּ מִי לָא קָתָנֵי אַמַּת הַמַּיִם – וְקָתָנֵי נִבְרֶכֶת הַכּוֹבְסִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But Rabbi Oshaya taught** in a *baraita* that one must distance **sand** from his neighbor's wall. The Gemara answers: **There,** it is referring **to damp** sand, which must be kept at a distance due to its moisture. The Gemara challenges: **Let the *tanna* of our** mishna **also teach** the *halakha* in the case of sand **and we will interpret it** as referring **to damp** sand. The Gemara answers: This *tanna* **already taught** the case of **a water channel,** which is a source of dampness, and therefore there was no need to mention damp sand as well. The Gemara rejects this answer: That is incorrect, as **is that to say** that the mishna includes only one example of a source of dampness? **Doesn't** the mishna **teach** the case of **a water channel? And** yet **it** also **teaches** the example of **a launderer's pond.** This demonstrates that the mishna teaches many cases, despite the similarity between them, and therefore it should have mentioned the *halakha* in the case of sand as well.

Returning to the second discrepancy, Rava explains why sand does not appear on the wall list: *mishum de-mechameme chame u-mekarere kare* — sand does not generate its own heat. Rather, it functions as a perfect thermal insulator in both directions: it keeps hot things hot and cold things cold. If you embed a hot object in sand, it preserves the heat; if you embed a cold object, it keeps it cold. Sand does not independently produce heat directed at a wall, which is why it poses no concern for wall damage and was therefore omitted from that list.

Rabbi Oshaya does, however, include sand in his version of the wall list — but the Gemara explains that his concern is *de-matuna*, moisture rather than heat. Sand retains and seeps moisture, which can damage a wall's foundation. The Gemara then asks whether sand, if it is a moisture concern, should appear in the earlier list of things to keep away from a neighbor's *bor* (cistern). The Gemara answers that the moisture-related list already includes the irrigation canal (*amat ha-mayim*) and the laundry pit (*nivrechet ha-kovsin*), and each of those needed to be stated independently: had only the irrigation canal been listed, one might have thought the continuously present, fixed canal is uniquely problematic but the intermittent laundry pit is not; had only the laundry pit been stated, one might have thought that only still, standing water is dangerous but not a flowing canal. Each case was necessary, and sand is encompassed within them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ צְרִיכִי; דְּאִי תְּנָא אַמַּת הַמַּיִם – מִשּׁוּם דִּקְבִיעָא, אֲבָל נִבְרֶכֶת הַכּוֹבְסִין, דְּלָא קְבִיעָא – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי תְּנָא נִבְרֶכֶת הַכּוֹבְסִין – מִשּׁוּם דִּקְווּ וְקָיְימִי, אֲבָל אַמַּת הַמַּיִם – לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Both of **those** particular examples **are necessary, as, had** the *tanna* **taught** only the case of **a water channel,** one would have claimed that a distance must be kept **because it is fixed,** i.e., water constantly passes through it. **But** with regard to **a launderer's pond, which is not fixed,** as it sometimes holds water and sometimes does not, one might **say** that one need **not** distance it from his neighbor's property. **And** conversely, **had** the *tanna* **taught** only the case of **a launderer's pond,** one might have said that this must be distanced **because** its water **is fixed and standing** in one location and therefore leaks out. **But** with regard to **a water channel,** one might say distancing it is **not** required. Consequently, **it is necessary** to state both examples. By contrast, including the *halakha* in the case of sand would not add any novel understanding.

## Seeds & Plowing

### Seeds Plowing Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַזְּרָעִים וְאֶת הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One must distance seeds,** i.e., one may not plant seeds, **and** one may not operate **the plow,** and one must eliminate urine, three handbreadths from the wall of another.

The *mishnah* adds: *marchikin et ha-zera'im v'et ha-machresha* — seeds and plowing must likewise be kept three *tefachim* from a neighbor's wall. Here the concern is neither moisture nor heat but damage to the ground structure that supports the wall.

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְרָעִים – תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם מַחֲרֵישָׁה! בְּמַפּוֹלֶת יָד. מַחֲרֵישָׁה – וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם זְרָעִים! בְּחוֹרֵשׁ לְאִילָנוֹת. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם מַיָּא! תַּנָּא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל קָאֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״לִמְטַר הַשָּׁמַיִם תִּשְׁתֶּה מָּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to mention **seeds?** Let him **derive this** requirement to distance the seeds **due to** the requirement to distance **a plow,** as in any event the ground must be plowed before it can be sown? The Gemara answers: This is referring to planting **with a single hand motion,** which is performed without plowing. The Gemara further challenges: The mishna teaches that one must distance **a plow; but** let him **derive this** requirement to distance a plow **due to** the requirement to distance the **seeds,** as plowing is preparation for planting. The Gemara answers: This is referring **to one who plows** to prepare the ground **for trees.** The Gemara challenges: **But** if so, let him **derive this** requirement to distance a plow due to the requirement to distance the **water.** If there are trees, there must be a water channel to irrigate them, and arranging one's field in such a manner should be prohibited for that reason. The Gemara answers: **The *tanna* is referring to Eretz Yisrael,** concerning **which it is written: "And drinks water as the rain of heaven comes down"** (Deuteronomy 11:11). In Eretz Yisrael, water channels were not needed.

The Gemara questions the apparent redundancy. *Zera'im tipok lei mishum machresha* — why mention seeds separately? If one cannot plow near the wall, one obviously cannot plant seeds there either, since plowing is a prerequisite for seeding. The Gemara answers: *be-mapolet yad* — seeds can be sown by hand, without plowing. A small vegetable garden can be planted by hand-casting seeds without any prior plowing.

Conversely, *machresha tipok lei mishum zera'im* — once we prohibit seeds, why prohibit plowing? Who plows without intending to seed? The Gemara answers: *be-choresh le-ilanot* — one might plow the area near a wall for the benefit of trees already growing there, to loosen and aerate the soil, without any intention of seeding. Each prohibition is therefore independently necessary.

A further question: *v'teipok lei mishum mayim* — since planting a garden implies watering it, and watering near a wall is already prohibited as a moisture concern, why does the *mishnah* need to separately prohibit seeds? The Gemara responds: *tana be-Eretz Yisrael ka'i, dich'tiv limtar ha-shamayim tishteh mayim* — the *mishnah* is written in the context of the Land of Israel, where agriculture depends on rainfall rather than direct irrigation. In Israel one may plant without needing to actively water, relying on rain. Therefore seeds alone, without watering, constitute an independent concern that must be stated.

### Mavrich Vineyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִצְדָדִין קָא מִשְׁתָּרְשִׁי?! וְהָא תְּנַן: הַמַּבְרִיךְ אֶת הַגֶּפֶן בָּאָרֶץ, אִם אֵין עַל גַּבָּהּ עָפָר שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים – לֹא יָבִיא זֶרַע עָלֶיהָ; וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: אֲבָל זוֹרֵעַ אֶת הַצְּדָדִין אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **take root to the sides,** i.e., the growing roots spread sideways and cause damage to walls? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 7:1): With regard to **one who bends** a branch of **a grapevine into the ground** so that it strikes roots and produces a new vine, **if it does not have three handbreadths of earth over it he may not plant a seed above** it, as he would thereby transgress the prohibition of diverse kinds? **And it is taught with regard to** this mishna: **But one may plant on either side** of that bent branch. This indicates that there is no concern that the roots of the seeds will spread out sideways.

One might assume the concern with seeds near a wall is that their roots spread laterally, growing outward and eventually undermining the wall's foundation. The Gemara challenges this assumption: vegetable roots do not spread laterally; they grow vertically downward.

Evidence comes from the law of *ha-mavrich et ha-gefen ba-aretz* — the practice of training a vine underground (*mavrich*), where an offshoot is buried and made to emerge at a distance, functioning as a separate vine without being severed. The relevant *halacha* is: if there is not three *tefachim* of soil covering the underground vine, one may not plant seeds directly above it. But one may plant on either side of the underground vine's path. This demonstrates that we do not fear lateral root spread, for if we did, we would prohibit planting to the sides as well. Clearly, seed roots are assumed to go straight down, not outward.

### Ground Damage Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חַגָּא בְּשֵׁם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּחֲלִידִין אֶת הַקַּרְקַע וּמַעֲלִין עָפָר תִּיחוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥagga says in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** The issue here is not that the roots will spread out sideways and reach the wall. Rather, it is prohibited to plant seeds near one's neighbor's property **because they break up the ground and cause loose soil to rise up,** which damages the foundation of the wall.

If lateral root spread is not the concern, why must seeds be kept from a wall? Rabbi Chaga in the name of Rabbi Yosi explains: *mipnei she-machlidin et ha-karka u-ma'alin afar tichuach* — because seeds cause the ground itself to deteriorate and loosen. The planting process — not the roots spreading sideways — destabilizes the soil directly where the seeds are planted, causes the earth to become soft and crumbly, and in this way undermines the structural integrity of the ground supporting the wall.

## Urine & Walls

### Urine Permitted?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶת מֵי רַגְלַיִם מִן הַכּוֹתֶל – שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: מוּתָּר לָאָדָם לְהַשְׁתִּין מַיִם בְּצַד כּוֹתְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִכְרַתִּי לְאַחְאָב מַשְׁתִּין בְּקִיר, וְעָצוּר וְעָזוּב בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And urine** must be kept at a distance of **three handbreadths from the wall** of one's neighbor. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** It **is permitted** for **a person to urinate alongside the wall of another, as it is written: "And I will cut off from Ahab those who urinate against the wall, and him that is shut up and him that is left at large in Israel"** (I Kings 21:21). As the verse employs the term "those who urinate against a wall" to mean males, it seems that urinating against a wall was a common practice.

The *mishnah* states that *mey raglayim* (urine) must be kept three *tefachim* from a wall. Against this, Rabbah bar bar Channa ruled: *mutar le-adam le-hashtin mayim be-tzad kotlo shel chavero* — a man may urinate against his neighbor's wall without special permission. His proof is a verse from *Melachim*: *ve-hichrati le-Achav mashtin be-kir* — the phrase *mashtin be-kir* (one who urinates against a wall) is used to describe men, and since it describes them in what appears to be a matter-of-fact manner, it reflects an entirely common and accepted practice.

One might ask whether the permissibility here is limited to cases of urgency — the Gemara elsewhere discusses that withholding oneself when one needs to relieve oneself violates *bal teshaktzu*, and grants latitude in public situations on grounds of urgency. What is striking in Rabbah bar bar Channa's ruling is that urgency is not mentioned at all. The formulation sounds like an unqualified permissive ruling, applicable even when there is no urgency.

Why would a man specifically urinate against a wall? Presumably for the sake of privacy — pressing against a wall prevents exposure from a distance in a way that standing in an open field does not.

### Collected vs. Incidental

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: וְאֶת מֵי רַגְלַיִם מִן הַכּוֹתֶל – שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים! הָתָם בְּשׁוֹפְכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **urine** must be kept a distance of **three handbreadths from the wall?** The Gemara answers: **There,** the mishna is referring to urine **that is poured** from a chamber pot, as opposed to urine that is passed from the body.

The apparent conflict with the *mishnah* is resolved as follows: *hatam be-shofchin* — the *mishnah*'s requirement of three *tefachim* distance refers to collected urine, that is, when one gathers and disposes of substantial quantities of urine near a wall. This is qualitatively different from the incidental urination of an individual, which occurs occasionally and in small amounts. Only the collected, accumulated disposal constitutes a sustained moisture threat to the wall.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ אָדָם מַיִם בְּצַד כּוֹתְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים! הָתָם נָמֵי בְּשׁוֹפְכִין. תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא יַשְׁתִּין אָדָם מַיִם בְּצַד כּוֹתְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּכוֹתֶל לְבֵינִים, אֲבָל בְּכוֹתֶל אֲבָנִים – בִּכְדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַזִּיק. וְכַמָּה? טֶפַח. וְשֶׁל צוּנְמָא – מוּתָּר. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **A person may not pour water at the side of the wall of another unless he distances** the water **three handbreadths from it.** If pouring water is prohibited, then all the more so should urination be prohibited. The Gemara explains: **There too,** it is referring to urine **that is poured** from a chamber pot. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from a *baraita*: **A person may not urinate alongside the wall of another unless he distances** himself **three handbreadths from it. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **in** the case of **a brick wall. But in** the case of **a stone wall,** one must distance himself **enough so that it does not cause damage. And how far** must he distance himself? One **handbreadth. And** if there is **hard rock** present, it is **permitted** to urinate there. The Gemara comments: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba bar bar Ḥana is a conclusive refutation,** and his ruling is rejected.

A *braita* poses a more direct challenge: *lo yashtin adam mayim be-tzad kotlo shel chavero ela im ken hirchik mimenu gimel tefachim* — a man should not urinate against his friend's wall unless he distances himself three *tefachim*. This is explicitly about individual urination, not collected disposal.

The *braita* then distinguishes between wall materials. *Bameh devarim amurim be-kotel levenim* — this applies to a brick wall, which the urine will damage. *Aval be-kotel avanim* — but for a stone wall, *kedai she-lo yazik* — one need only stay far enough to avoid causing direct damage, which works out to one *tefach*. Rashi clarifies that the concern with a stone wall is not damage to the stones themselves (which urine would not harm) but rather damage to the ground at the wall's base, which could undermine its stability. If the wall is built directly on rock with no soil at its base, *mutar* — there is no concern at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה – קְרָא קָאָמַר! הָתָם הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִידֵּי דְּדַרְכֵּיהּ לְאַישְׁתּוֹנֵי בְּקִיר לָא שָׁבֵיקְנָא לֵיהּ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? כַּלְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But Rabba bar bar Ḥana stated a verse** in support of his opinion; how can the *baraita* rule counter to what is written in a verse? The Gemara answers: **This** is what it **is saying there,** i.e., this is the meaning of that verse: **I will not even leave** Ahab **something whose manner is to urinate against a wall. And what is that? A dog.** According to this interpretation, the verse is not referring to people at all.

The Gemara concludes: *tyuvta de-Rabba bar bar Channa tyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of Rabbah bar bar Channa's permissive ruling. He had cited the verse from *Melachim* as proof of universal permissibility. The Gemara reinterprets: *afilu midi de-darkei le-ishtunei be-kir, lo shafkina lei* — even something that naturally urinates against a wall will not be spared from destruction in the context of that verse. *U-mai nihu? Kalba* — the reference is to a dog, not a man. Thus *mashtin be-kir* in the verse refers to dogs, not human beings urinating against walls as a normative practice. Whatever latitude exists in cases of genuine urgency, as discussed elsewhere, the standard rule remains that one may not urinate against a neighbor's wall without maintaining the required distance.

## Tumah & Windows

### Wafer in Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: רָקִיק אֵינוֹ מְמַעֵט בַּחַלּוֹן. מַאי אִירְיָא רָקִיק? אֲפִילּוּ עָבֶה נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Tovi bar Kisna says** that **Shmuel says: A wafer does not reduce** the dimensions **of a window.** When a corpse, or a significant part thereof, is in one room, its impurity can spread to a room adjacent to it if there is a window of a certain size between the rooms. Shmuel states that when one puts a wafer in a window, the wafer is not considered an obstruction, so the size of the opening as relevant to this *halakha* remains the same. The Gemara asks: **Why** discuss **specifically** this case? Why does Shmuel teach this *halakha* with regard to **a wafer? Even a thick** chunk of bread **also** does not reduce the dimensions of a window.

The Gemara now shifts to an apparently unrelated topic — one that will eventually circle back to our *mishnah*, though the connection will only become clear later. The discussion concerns the laws of *tumah* and the spread of ritual impurity through openings.

The governing principle is as follows. When a corpse is present under a roof, the *tumah* spreads throughout the enclosed space beneath that roof. If two adjacent houses share a wall with a window between them, and that window measures at least one *tefach* by one *tefach* (approximately three inches square), the two spaces are considered connected and the *tumah* spreads through the window into the second house as well, rendering everything inside it *tamei*.

The question arises: what if the window opening is partially filled by an object placed within it, reducing the clear opening to less than one *tefach* by one *tefach*? Does this "shrink" the window halachically, preventing the *tumah* from passing through?

Rav Tuvi bar Kisna said in the name of Shmuel: *rakik eino mema'et ba-chalon* — a *rakik* (a thin wafer, a flat piece of baked food) placed in a window does not reduce the effective size of the opening and does not prevent *tumah* from spreading through.

### Wafer Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא עָבֶה – כֵּיוָן דְּאִיחֲזִי לֵיהּ, לָא מְבַטֵּיל לֵיהּ; אֲבָל רָקִיק, דְּמִמְּאִיס – אֵימָא בַּטּוֹלֵי מְבַטֵּיל לֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Shmuel **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to state this *halakha* with regard to **thick** bread. **Since it is fit for him** to consume, **he does not nullify it,** i.e., he does not plan to leave it in the window for an extended amount of time. **But** in the case of **a wafer, which** becomes **disgusting** when placed in a window, **I might say** that **he does nullify it** and it becomes part of the house, thereby reducing the size of the window. To counter this, Shmuel **teaches us** that even a wafer is not nullified, as it can be used to feed animals, as one is not particular about their food. Therefore, the wafer does not become part of the window in which it is placed.

The Gemara asks: *mai irya rakik* — why specifically discuss a wafer? *Afilu ava nami* — even a thick loaf of bread or substantial piece of food placed in the window clearly would not shrink the opening. Why? *Keivan de-khazei lei, lo mivatel lei* — because it is clearly visible, you will not forget it is there; you will take it out and eat it. It has not been treated as a permanent structural element. Of course it doesn't reduce the window.

The *chiddush* therefore concerns specifically the thin wafer. *De-mais* — a wafer is unappealing; sitting in a window it collects dirt and dust and becomes disgusting. One might have thought: *eima bitulei mevatel lei* — you will simply leave the wafer there permanently, having effectively incorporated it into the structure of the window. And if so, perhaps it does reduce the opening. The ruling of Shmuel tells us this is not the case — the wafer does not reduce the window even when one might have thought it would be left in place permanently.

### Susceptibility Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה – אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ בִּפְנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה! שֶׁנִּילֹשׁ בְּמֵי פֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And** let him **derive this** *halakha* from the fact that a wafer is **an item that is susceptible to ritual impurity, and any item that is susceptible to impurity does not serve as a barrier against** the spread of **impurity.** The Gemara explains: This is referring to a wafer **that was kneaded in fruit juice,** which is not one of the liquids that renders food susceptible to ritual impurity, and therefore the wafer is not susceptible to ritual impurity. Consequently, one might have thought that it serves as a barrier before ritual impurity and reduces the dimensions of the window.

Before even reaching the question of whether something has been permanently incorporated into the window, the Gemara identifies a more fundamental reason why a wafer cannot reduce the opening: *tipok lei d'havei davar she-mekabel tumah* — a wafer is food, and as such it is susceptible to *tumah*. And the principle is: *kol davar she-mekabel tumah eino chotzetz bifnei ha-tumah* — anything that can itself become *tamei* cannot serve as a barrier to the spread of *tumah*.

Even a thick stale loaf of bread, placed intentionally in the window as a permanent seal, would not block *tumah* from passing through — not because you would remove it, but because it is itself *mekabel tumah*.

This leads the Gemara to reframe Shmuel's ruling: *she-nilosh be-mei peirot* — the *rakik* in question was made with fruit juice rather than with one of the seven liquids that render food susceptible to *tumah*. The seven liquids are: *yayin* (wine), *devash* (honey), *shemen* (oil), *chalav* (milk), *tal* (dew), *dam* (blood), and *mayim* (water) — a mnemonic often used to recall them. Fruit juices other than wine and oil do not confer susceptibility. A wafer kneaded exclusively with, say, apple juice, and never subsequently exposed to any of the seven liquids, is not *mekabel tumah* and therefore could in principle serve as a barrier — if properly incorporated into the window structure. It is regarding this wafer that Shmuel rules it still does not reduce the window, because it has not been treated as a permanent fixture.

### Straw & Figs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: קוּפָּה מְלֵאָה תֶּבֶן, וְחָבִית מְלֵאָה גְּרוֹגְרוֹת, הַמּוּנָּחִים בַּחַלּוֹן – רוֹאִין, כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ יִנָּטְלוּ וִיכוֹלִין תֶּבֶן וּגְרוֹגְרוֹת לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן – חוֹצְצִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין חוֹצְצִין. וְהָא תֶּבֶן חֲזֵי לִבְהֶמְתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Shmuel's opinion from a mishna (*Oholot* 6:2): With regard to **a basket** that is **full of straw, or** an earthenware **barrel full of dried figs, which are placed in a window,** one **considers: If the straw or dried figs would stand on their own were** the basket or barrel **removed,** then **they would serve as a barrier against** the spread of impurity. **But if** they would **not** stand on their own **they would not serve as a barrier.** The Gemara explains the objection: **But** why should the straw or dried figs serve as a barrier? Even **straw** that can stand on its own **is fit for** feeding to **one's animal** and will likely be removed from the opening, which means it should not be considered part of the window.

The Gemara now challenges the emerging principle — that only something permanently left in place can reduce a window opening — from a *braita*: *kupah m'lei'ah teven, v'chavit m'lei'ah grogerot ha-munachim ba-chalon* — a box filled with straw or a barrel filled with pressed figs placed in a window. The rule stated is *ro'in* — one assesses the situation: if the container were removed and the straw or figs could remain in place independently, and they have not been rendered susceptible to *tumah*, then *chotzetzim* — they block the *tumah*. If not, they do not.

The problem is that both straw and pressed figs would normally be taken out and used. Straw is animal fodder; pressed figs are food. If we require intent to leave something permanently in place for it to reduce the window, neither of these should qualify — yet the *braita* seems to say they can.

The Gemara works through each case carefully. Regarding the straw: one might say it can be fed to animals — but it has thorns, making it unsuitable for fodder. One might say it can be used to make plaster or mortar — but it is moist. One might say moist straw can still be added to a large fire — but *hasakah gedolah lo shechi'ach*, maintaining a very large fire is not common. The Gemara thus reinterprets the case as one where the straw is effectively useless and will in fact be left in place.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסַרְיָא. חֲזֵי לְטִינָא! דְּאִית בֵּיהּ קוֹצֵי. חֲזֵי לְהַסָּקָה! בִּמְתוּנָא. חֲזֵי לְהֶסֵּק גָּדוֹל! הֶסֵּק גָּדוֹל לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to rotted** straw, which is unfit for animal consumption. The Gemara asks: But **it is fit for** use in the making of **clay** for bricks. The Gemara answers: This is referring to straw **that has thorns in it** and therefore is not fit for making bricks. The Gemara challenges: Even so, it is **fit for kindling** a fire. The Gemara answers that this is referring **to wet** straw. The Gemara responds: Nevertheless, it is **fit for kindling a large** fire. If one builds a large fire, wet straw will dry and become ignitable. The Gemara answers: **A large fire is not common,** and therefore, in all likelihood, the straw will remain in the window.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּרוֹגְרוֹת – הָא חֲזוּ לֵיהּ! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשֶׁהִתְרִיפוּ. וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בְּשֶׁהִתְרִיפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: But **dried figs are fit for him** to consume, and he will certainly remove them. Consequently, they should not be considered fixed in their place. **Shmuel says:** This is referring to a case **where** the figs **became worm infested [*beshehitrifu*]. And so Rabba bar Avuh teaches:** This is referring to a case **where** the figs **became worm infested.**

Regarding the pressed figs, Shmuel himself states — and Rabba bar bar Avuha teaches the same — *be-she-hitrifu*: the figs have become worm-eaten and spoiled. No one intends to eat them; they will remain in the window. Again, the case is reread as one where permanent placement was always the intent or practical reality.

The Gemara's consistent approach is thus confirmed: only something that one plans to leave permanently in place — whether consciously or by virtue of the object's worthlessness — can reduce the effective size of a window opening and thereby prevent *tumah* from passing through.

### Intent to Leave

The Gemara makes explicit what has been implicit throughout: the governing principle is *bitulei mevatel lei* — whether the owner has nullified the object's independent status by treating it as a fixed element of the structure. A *rakik* that is merely set in a window, even if thin and unappealing, has not been treated this way and therefore cannot reduce the opening. Only when the circumstances are such that the object genuinely will remain — because it is spoiled, useless, or otherwise permanently committed to that location — does it acquire the status of a structural fill.

### Metal Barrel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי חָבִית, הֵיכִי דָּמְיָא? אִי דְּפוּמָּא לְבַר – הִיא גּוּפַהּ תֵּיחוּץ, דְּהָא כְּלִי חֶרֶס אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ! אֶלָּא דְּפוּמָּא לְגָאו. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּפוּמָּא לְבַר, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּחָבִית שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the** exact **circumstances of this barrel** that held the dried figs? **If** this is referring to a case **where** its **opening faced outward,** i.e., not toward the source of the ritual impurity, **let** the barrel **itself serve as a barrier.** It should not be susceptible to impurity in this case, **as an earthenware vessel does not contract impurity if its exterior** is exposed to impurity. **Rather,** one must say **that its opening faces inward,** and it is rendered impure because the impurity enters through its opening. **And if you wish, say** instead that **actually its opening faces outward, and with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with a metal barrel,** which does contract impurity through its exterior.

The Gemara returns to the barrel of pressed figs and probes the case more carefully. When the Gemara hears "barrel," it envisions a pottery vessel, not a wooden one. The question becomes: in what configuration is the barrel positioned in the window?

*I de-fumah le-var* — if the opening of the barrel faces away from the house containing the *tumah*, then *hi gufah tachutz* — the barrel itself should block the *tumah* from passing through. The reason: *kli cheres eino mitamei mi-gabo* — a pottery vessel does not become *tamei* through its outer side; *tumah* enters pottery vessels only through their openings. If the opening faces away from the *tumah*, the barrel cannot become *tamei* and therefore itself functions as a valid barrier. There would be no need to look at the food inside.

The Gemara therefore concludes the case must be *ela de-fumah le-gav* — the opening faces toward the house containing the *tumah*. In this configuration, the barrel's opening is exposed to the *tumah*, the barrel becomes *tamei* itself, and therefore cannot serve as a barrier. The blocking function must fall entirely on the pressed figs inside — which, as established, can block *tumah* only if (a) they have not been rendered susceptible to *tumah*, (b) they can stand independently without the container, and (c) they are intended to remain in place permanently.

Alternatively, the Gemara offers a second reading: *le-olam de-fumah le-var* — perhaps the opening does face away from the *tumah*, but the barrel is made of metal rather than pottery. A metal vessel becomes *tamei* regardless of which side faces the *tumah* — metal vessels are *mekabel tumah* from any direction. Therefore the barrel itself cannot block *tumah* regardless of its orientation, and again the burden falls on the food inside.

The full structure of the ruling is thus: the barrel cannot itself serve as a barrier because it is *mekabel tumah*; the food inside can only serve as a barrier if it is not itself *mekabel tumah* and if it can stand independently and if it is treated as permanently in place. All of these conditions must be met, and the Gemara will continue to explore the implications tomorrow — with the promise that this elaborate discussion of *tumah* and windows will eventually connect back to the *mishnah* from which we began.
