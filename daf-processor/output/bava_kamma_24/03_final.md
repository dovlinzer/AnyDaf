# Bava Kamma 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inciting Animals

### Dog/Snake Attack Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שִׁיסָּה בּוֹ אֶת הַכֶּלֶב, שִׁיסָּה בּוֹ אֶת הַנָּחָשׁ – פָּטוּר. מַאן פָּטוּר – מְשַׁסֶּה פָּטוּר, וְחַיָּיב בַּעַל כֶּלֶב; וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ כַּחֲצַר הַמַּזִּיק דָּמֵי, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵי יְדָךְ בְּפוּמֵּיהּ דְּכַלְבַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: If **he incited a dog against** another person, or if **he incited a snake against** another person, he is **exempt** (*Sanhedrin* 66b). The Gemara clarifies: **Who** is **exempt? The one who incited** the dog is **exempt, but** the **owner of** the **dog** is **liable. And if you say** that the mouth of the animal is **like the courtyard of the one liable for the damage, let** the owner of the dog **say to** the injured party: **What does your hand want,** i.e., what is it doing, **in my dog's mouth,** which is my domain?

The *gemara* opens with a *tashma* — an attempt to bring a proof — appearing approximately ten lines into 23b. The passage reads: *shissa bo et ha-kelev, shissa bo et ha-nachash, patur* — if a person incites a dog or a snake to attack a third party, that person is *patur*, entirely exempt from liability. The case involves someone who encourages another's animal to attack a bystander; the inciter is not the owner, and the owner is not the one who actively caused the attack. Because liability appears to be split between two different parties, the question immediately arises: who, precisely, is *patur*?

### Liability Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: פָּטוּר אַף מְשַׁסֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna could be understood a different way. **Say** that **even the one who incited** the dog is **exempt,** as the dog is not his property, but the dog owner is also exempt because his dog's mouth has a legal status similar to that of the dog owner's courtyard.

The *gemara* poses the question directly: *man patur* — who is the one who is *patur*? The initial reading suggests that the inciter (*mashse*) is *patur*, and the owner of the dog (*ba'al ha-kelev*) is *chayav* — after all, it was the owner's animal that caused the damage. If a dog is already *mu'ad*, the owner would owe *nezek shalem*. This reading makes straightforward sense: the owner's animal attacked someone, so the owner bears responsibility.

### Open Mouth Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּאַפְּקֵיהּ לְנִיבֵיהּ וְסַרְטֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the case under discussion is one where the dog **extended his canine teeth and scratched the** victim, but the victim's hand was not inside his mouth, and therefore this incident cannot be treated as if it took place in the domain of the owner of the dog.

However, the *gemara* introduces a counterpoint: *ve-ee bayit eima de-afkalan niveiha ve-sarteh* — an alternative reading in which the dog bared and thrust its teeth outward. This *iba'is eima* proposes that when a dog opens its mouth and attacks, the teeth are no longer entirely within the dog's body in a way that could be considered the owner's private domain. The relevance of this point connects to the broader question being debated in the *sugya*: is the mouth of an animal considered *kechatzer ha-mazik* — like the property of the person causing the damage? If so, then just as one is *patur* for damage caused in the *mazik*'s own domain, the owner might similarly be *patur* when the dog bites, since the mouth is treated as his domain. The *iba'is eima* offers a way to maintain the owner's *chiyuv* even according to that view: if the dog lunged outward with open jaws, the mouth is no longer functioning as a contained domain.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi explains the *iba'is eima* precisely: *she-hotzi shinav ve-sarto de-lo hichnis yado le-toch piv* — the dog jumped out and attacked rather than the person inserting their hand into the dog's mouth. This distinction matters because the entire exemption under the *kechatzer ha-mazik* theory rests on the victim having, in effect, entered the *mazik*'s domain voluntarily. When the dog attacks outward with an open mouth, that framework no longer applies, and the *ba'al ha-kelev* becomes *chayav*. Importantly, as one might observe, the *iba'is eima* is not simply another answer to the same question — it is an alternative explanation of the same ruling, representing a genuine *machlokes* between the two readings of the *braisa*.

## Snake Bite Liability

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִשִּׁיךְ בּוֹ אֶת הַנָּחָשׁ – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים. וְאָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר יַעֲקֹב: כְּשֶׁתִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר; לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֶרֶס נָחָשׁ – בֵּין שִׁינָּיו הוּא עוֹמֵד, לְפִיכָךְ מַכִּישׁ בְּסַיִיף, וְנָחָשׁ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a solution based on the continuation of the above mishna: If **one caused the snake to bite** a person by bringing the fangs of the snake to the victim's body, and the snake killed him, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the one who instigated the attack **liable** to receive the death penalty **and the Rabbis exempt** him. **And Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says** in explanation of the dispute: When you analyze the matter **you will find that according to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda, the venom of a snake is** deemed to be constantly **present between its fangs,** and therefore when the snake is made to strike the victim it is not actively engaged in any action. **Consequently,** the person handling the snake is liable, similar to one who **strikes** a victim **with a sword,** while the **snake** itself is **exempt** as it has not done anything.

The *gemara* brings a second *tashma* concerning a case of directing a snake to bite someone. *Rabbi Yehuda mechayev* — Rabbi Yehuda holds the person *chayav* — while the *chachamim* hold the person *patur*. Rav Acha bar Yaakov explains Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning: *eres nachash bein shinav hu omed* — the venom of a snake sits lodged within its fangs at all times. According to this view, a snake is already a preloaded lethal weapon; its venom does not need to be voluntarily expelled. Consequently, one who takes a snake and has it bite another person is treated as if they struck someone with a poisoned blade, and the *halacha* is that such a person is executed by the sword (*machish be-sayaf*) for having committed an act of killing. The snake itself is *patur*, since the act of biting was entirely in the control of the human who deployed it.

### Chachamim's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, אֶרֶס נָחָשׁ – מֵעַצְמוֹ מִקִּיא, לְפִיכָךְ נָחָשׁ בִּסְקִילָה, וּמַכִּישׁ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** And **according to the statement of the Rabbis, the venom of a snake** is not present between its fangs but rather the snake must **secrete** it **of its own volition. Consequently,** the **snake** receives the penalty of **stoning** like any animal that kills a person, while the one who instigated the attack **and striking** with it is **exempt.**

The *chachamim* dispute this understanding of snake physiology and, accordingly, the legal conclusion. In their view, *eres nachash me-atzmo makya* — the venom of the snake is expelled by the snake's own volition. The snake decides whether to inject its venom when it bites, and therefore one who forces a snake to bite another person can never be certain that the bite will be lethal. Because it is never *vadai* — certain — that the snake will release venom, one cannot be held criminally liable for murder. The snake, having caused a death through its own volitional act, is executed by *skila*, but the person who wielded it is *patur* from capital liability. This raises the pointed question of whether such a person could at least be liable for *nezikin* — civil damages — but the *gemara* does not fully resolve it in this context, and the question of *kim lei be-drabba minei* (that the more severe penalty absorbs lesser liability) complicates any definitive answer.

### Capital Crime Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ פִּי פָרָה כַּחֲצַר הַמַּזִּיק דָּמֵי, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵי יְדָךְ בְּפוּמָּא דְחִיוַּואי? לְעִנְיַן קְטָלָא לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **the mouth of the cow** is **like the courtyard of the one responsible for the damage, let** the owner of the snake metaphorically **say to** the injured party: **What is your hand doing in my snake's mouth?** The Gemara answers: **We do not say** this principle **with regard to matters relating to** the **death** penalty; therefore, one who kills another is always liable, regardless of whether it occurred within the domain of the killer or within the domain of the victim.

The *gemara* raises an apparent difficulty: if we say that the mouth of the animal is *kechatzer ha-mazik*, why would the owner of the snake be *chayav* at all, even according to Rabbi Yehuda? The *gemara* answers: *le-inyan katla lo amrinan* — we do not apply the *kechatzer ha-mazik* principle in cases of capital crimes. That rule, which would permit the owner to say "why did you put your hand into the mouth of my animal," operates only in the realm of civil damages.

### Unauthorized Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא? דְּתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲצַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וּנְגָחוֹ שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וּמֵת – הַשּׁוֹר בִּסְקִילָה, וּבְעָלִים פְּטוּרִים מִן הַכּוֹפֶר. בְּעָלִים פְּטוּרִין מִן הַכּוֹפֶר מַאי טַעְמָא – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: בִּרְשׁוּתִי מַאי בָּעֵית; שׁוֹרוֹ נָמֵי, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בִּרְשׁוּתִי! אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן קְטָלָא לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say** this? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 5:13): With regard to **one who enters the courtyard of a property owner without** his **permission, and the property owner's ox gores** the intruder **and** he **dies, the ox** receives the penalty of **stoning while** the **owner** is **exempt from** having to pay **ransom.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** that the **owner** is **exempt from** paying **ransom?** He is exempt **because he can** metaphorically **say to** the victim: **What do you want in my domain,** i.e., why were you there without permission? Since he entered the premises without authorization, he bears responsibility for his own fate. But if that is so, the same applies to **his ox as well,** and **let** the owner of the ox **say to** the victim: **What do you want in my domain?** Rather, it must be that **we do not say** that this principle applies **with regard to matters relating to** the **death** penalty. Consequently, these cases do not prove that the mouth of the cow is like the courtyard of the victim within the scope of the *halakhot* of damages.

A *braisa* is cited to support this distinction: if someone enters another's *chatzer* without permission and is gored to death by the owner's ox, the *halacha* is that the ox is executed by *skila* — because it caused a death — but the owner is *patur min ha-kofer*, exempt from the monetary penalty of *kofer* that would normally accompany a killing by a *mu'ad* animal. The owner's defense is: *be-reshuti mai bayis* — what were you doing in my property? However, this same argument cannot save the ox from execution. *Le-inyan katla lo amrinan* — for the purposes of capital punishment of the animal, we do not extend the "it was my domain" argument. The animal must be destroyed regardless of whether the victim had permission to be on the premises.

## Rav Yosef's Goats

### Goat Damage & Fence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ עִיזֵּי דְּבֵי תַרְבּוּ דַּהֲווֹ מַפְסְדִי לֵיהּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְאַבָּיֵי: זִיל אֵימָא לְהוּ לְמָרַיְיהוּ דְּלַיצְנְעִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי אֵיזִיל? דְּאִי אָזֵילְנָא, אָמְרִי לִי: לִגְדּוֹר מָר גְּדֵירָא בְּאַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There were **these goats,** belonging to **the Tarbu family, that would** frequently **damage Rav Yosef's** property. Rav Yosef **said to Abaye: Go** and **tell their owners that they must keep** their goats enclosed within their own property. Abaye **said to** Rav Yosef in response: **Why should I go? If I go** and deliver this message, **they will say to me: Let** the **Master put a fence up around his property** so that the goats will not be able to get in there.

The *gemara* presents a vivid practical case. There were goats belonging to a family called Beit Tarbu (*hanehu izi de-vei tarbu*) that were causing ongoing damage to Rav Yosef's property. Rav Yosef instructed Abaye to go and tell the owners to confine their animals. Abaye pushed back: why must you issue a formal demand? The owners will simply say that the goats went and did as goats do, and that they bear no additional liability. Furthermore, Abaye raises the *halachic* challenge: if you are going to require owners to fence in their animals, then how can you ever have a case of liability for *shen* — damage caused by grazing? If an animal is always required to be locked up, and an animal escapes, the escape itself is *ones*, an unforeseeable circumstance, and the owner would have a defense. The *gemara* responds that *shen* liability can be found in a case where the animal dug under the fence (*ke-she-chatrah*), but this opens further questions about the scope of the owner's responsibility.

### Public Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרֵיז רַב יוֹסֵף וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה: דְּסָלְקִין לְעֵילָּא וּדְנָחֲתִין לְתַחְתָּאה – הָנֵי עִיזֵּי דְשׁוּקָא דְּמַפְסְדִי, מַתְרֵינַן בְּמָרַיְיהוּ תְּרֵי וּתְלָתָא זִמְנִין. אִי צָיֵית – צָיֵית. וְאִי לָא – אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: תִּיב אַמְּסַחְתָּא וְקַבֵּל זוּזָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rav Yosef, and some say** it was **Rabba, would announce: Those that ascend** to Eretz Yisrael from Babylonia, bringing with them the rulings of the Babylonian Sages, **as well as those that descend** to Babylonia from Eretz Yisrael, bringing the rulings of the sages of Eretz Yisrael with them, all agree to the following *halakha*: With regard to **these goats** that loiter **in the market** until they are slaughtered and in the meantime **cause damage** to others, **we warn their owner two or three times. If** the owner of the goats **heeds** the warning and safeguards his animals from damaging others, then **he heeds** it and no further action is necessary. **But if not, we say to him:** Go, **sit next to the butcher shop and take your money,** meaning we instruct him to slaughter his goats immediately. Since he in any event intends to slaughter them, the court can force him to do so immediately. Otherwise, he cannot be forced to slaughter them as a preventive measure.

Rav Yosef — or possibly Rava, as the *gemara* offers an alternative attribution — issued a public proclamation directed both to those traveling up to *Eretz Yisrael* and those coming down to *Bavel*, covering both communities. The announcement concerned goats taken to the marketplace (*hanei izei de-shuka*) that were causing damage. Rav Yosef declared that he was treating the owners as though they had already been warned two and three times (*trei u-tlata zimnei*), effectively establishing *mu'ad* status for these animals by public decree. He was not addressing any single owner individually; rather, he was using his authority to put all goat-herders on notice that their animals had achieved a presumptive status of *mu'ad* with respect to marketplace damage.

### Forced Slaughter

Beyond the proclamation, Rav Yosef went further: if the owners did not comply, he declared that they would be told to go and slaughter (*shecht*) their animals immediately. This meant selling the animals before the market day, when there would be fewer buyers and the price would be lower, causing the owners a real financial loss. This was a deliberate legislative measure — Rav Yosef was personally affected by the damage, and he used rabbinic authority to impose real economic consequences on those who refused to control their animals. The episode reflects the broader concern documented throughout *Bava Batra* as well, where *Chazal* expressed strong opposition to raising *behema daka* — small livestock such as goats and sheep — in *Eretz Yisrael*, precisely because such animals tend to wander and graze on others' property, which amounts to *gezel*.

## Tam vs. Muad

### Mishna Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵיזֶהוּ תָּם וְאֵיזֶהוּ מוּעָד? מוּעָד – כֹּל שֶׁהֵעִידוּ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, וְתָם – מִשֶּׁיַּחְזוֹר בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מוּעָד – שֶׁהֵעִידוּ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּעָמִים, וְתָם – כֹּל שֶׁיְּהוּ הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מְמַשְׁמְשִׁין בּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ נוֹגֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Which** type of ox **is** deemed **innocuous and which** is deemed **forewarned?** An ox is deemed **forewarned** in **any** case **where** witnesses **testified about it** that it gored on **three** different **days. And** it reverts back to its previous **innocuous** status **from when it reverses its** behavior and refrains from goring for **three** consecutive **days;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** It is deemed **forewarned** in any case **where** witnesses **testified** that **it** gored **three times,** regardless of the number of days on which this behavior occurred. **And** it reverts back to its previous **innocuous** status in **any** case **where children pet it** and play with it **and it does not gore** them.

The next *mishna* takes up a foundational question: *eizehu tam ve-eizehu mu'ad* — what constitutes a *tam* (innocent) animal and what constitutes a *mu'ad* (forewarned dangerous) animal? The *mishna* establishes that any animal about which testimony was given on three separate days (*kol she-he'idu bo shlosha yamim*) becomes *mu'ad*. Conversely, a *mu'ad* animal can revert to *tam* status (*ve-tam mi-she-yachzor bo shlosha yamim*) — after three days of not goring. This reversion cannot be based on mere passivity; it must involve some circumstance that would normally provoke the animal, where the animal nonetheless demonstrates restraint. This is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״תְּמֹל״ – חַד; ״מִתְּמֹל״ – תְּרֵי; ״שִׁלְשֹׁם״ – תְּלָתָא; ״וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ בְּעָלָיו״ – אֲתָאן לִנְגִיחָה רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** that an ox is deemed forewarned only if it gored on three separate days? **Abaye said:** It states in the verse: "Or if it is known that the ox was a goring ox from yesterday and the day before yesterday, and its owner has not secured it" (Exodus 21:36). This leads to the following inference: **"Yesterday"** indicates **one** day, **"from yesterday"** indicates **two** days, **"the day before yesterday"** indicates **three** days, **"and its owner has not secured it";** here **we arrive at a fourth** incident of **goring,** for which the owner pays the full amount of damages.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'amei de-Rabbi Yehuda* — what is the reasoning behind Rabbi Yehuda's position that three separate days are required? Abaye explains by parsing the *pasuk*: *temol* — yesterday, represents one day; *mi-temol* — meaning not merely yesterday but the day before, represents a second day; and *shilshom* — the day before that — represents a third day. The key textual detail is that *shilshom* means two days before today, which corresponds to three distinct calendar days. And *ve-lo yishmerenu ba'alav* — "and its owner did not guard it" — introduces the owner's responsibility only *after* those three days of testimony, meaning it is the fourth goring that triggers full *chiyuv* as a *mu'ad*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״תְּמֹל–מִתְּמֹל״ – חַד; ״שִׁלְשֹׁם״ – תְּרֵי; ״וְלָא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ״ הָאִידָּנָא – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: "Yesterday"** and **"from yesterday"** are not to be expounded separately. Rather, the inference from this phrase is that "from yesterday" indicates **one** day, **"the day before yesterday"** indicates **two** days, **"and its owner has not secured it"** indicates **now,** the third incident of goring, as it is **liable** as a forewarned animal for the third goring.

Rava disagrees with Abaye's parsing: in Rava's reading, *temol mi-temol* together count as a single unit of one day, and *shilshom* provides the second. The phrase *ve-lo yishmerenu* then brings the owner into liability at the third goring rather than the fourth. This is the classic tension between Abaye and Rava: is the third occurrence itself the point of *chiyuv*, or does *chiyuv* begin only with the fourth, after three completed warnings?

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: רִיחֵק נְגִיחוֹתָיו – חַיָּיב; קֵירַב נְגִיחוֹתָיו לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זָבָה תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁרִיחֲקָה רְאִיּוֹתֶיהָ – טְמֵאָה, קֵירְבָה רְאִיּוֹתֶיהָ – טְהוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** for the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who does not determine the forewarned status of an ox by the number of days on which the animal gored but simply by the number of times? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir said:** When the ox **performs its gorings at intervals,** its owner is **liable;** if it **performs its gorings successively,** is it **not all the more so** that its owner is liable? **They said to** Rabbi Meir: The *halakha* with regard to **a woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*] will prove** that your *a fortiori* inference is invalid: If **her sightings occur at intervals,** i.e., if she sees a flow of uterine blood on three consecutive days, she becomes **ritually impure;** while if **her sightings were consecutive,** for example if all three occurred on the same day, she remains **pure.**

Rabbi Meir takes a different approach entirely: *mu'ad mi-she-he'idu bo shlosha pe'amim* — an animal becomes *mu'ad* after being testified against three times, without requiring that those occurrences span three separate days. And for reversion to *tam* status, Rabbi Meir holds that it suffices for children to be playing around the animal without it goring — even if that happens in a single day.

Rabbi Meir's reasoning is presented as a *kal va-chomer*: *richak negichotav chayav* — if the gorings were spread out over multiple days, he is certainly *chayav*; *kirav negichotav* — if they were even closer together, is that not *kal she-ken*, all the more so? The *chachamim* challenge this with the case of *zava*: a woman whose three irregular flows occur on a single day does not achieve the halachic status of *zava gedola* and is not required to bring a *korban*. *Zava* requires three separate days. If Rabbi Meir's *kal va-chomer* were correct, one would expect *zava* to apply even more stringently when the occurrences are clustered; yet it does not.

### Zav/Zava Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְזֹאת תִּהְיֶה טֻמְאָתוֹ בְּזוֹבוֹ״ – תָּלָה הַכָּתוּב אֶת הַזָּב בִּרְאִיּוֹת, וְאֶת הַזָּבָה בְּיָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **said to them:** The case of the *zava* does not disprove my opinion, because the verse **states** in reference to the parallel *halakha* of a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]: **"And this shall be his ritual impurity when he has a discharge"** (Leviticus 15:3). The word "this" emphasizes that in this matter the *halakha* requires following the instructions of the verse precisely as they were recorded, and in this case **the verse associates** the impurity of **the *zav* with** the number of **sightings** of discharges the man observed, **and** it associates **the** impurity of the ***zava* with** the number of **days** during which she experienced sightings of blood, as it says: "Many days" (Leviticus 15:25). By contrast, with regard to the goring ox, the *a fortiori* inference remains in place.

Rabbi Meir responds by distinguishing between *zav* and *zava*. He cites the *pasuk*: *ve-zot tihyeh tum'ato be-zovo* — this shall be his impurity in his discharge — and argues that the Torah explicitly distinguished between the two cases. For *zav* — a man with an irregular discharge — the count is by *re'iyot*, individual occurrences, meaning three times even on one day can qualify. For *zava* — a woman — the count is by *yamim*, separate days. The word *ve-zot* functions as a *mi'ut*, an exclusion, to separate the two regimes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״וְזֹאת״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי זָבָה מֵרְאִיּוֹת? אֵימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי זָב מִיָּמִים! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַזָּב אֶת זוֹבוֹ לַזָּכָר וְלַנְּקֵבָה״ – מַקִּישׁ זָכָר לִנְקֵבָה; מָה נְקֵבָה בְּיָמִים, אַף זָכָר בְּיָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this interpretation: **From where** is it determined **that this** additional phrase: **"And this,"** serves **to exclude a *zava* from** having her status determined by individual **sightings** of blood, associating it instead with the number of days on which she experienced bleeding? **Say** instead that it serves **to exclude** the ***zav* from** having his status determined by sightings on separate **days,** indicating that he will be rendered a *zav* only if he experienced three discharges on a single day and not on separate days. The Gemara answers: **The verse states** elsewhere: **"And one who has a discharge, whether it be a man or a woman"** (Leviticus 15:33). The verse **juxtaposes** the ritual impurity of **a male to** that of **a female** to teach that **just as** the **female's** ritual impurity is caused by sightings on multiple **days, so too** the ritual impurity of **a male** can be caused by sightings on multiple **days.**

The *gemara* then asks: why apply the *mi'ut* to exclude *zava* from the *re'iyot*-based count, rather than to exclude *zav* from the *yamim*-based count? The answer is drawn from a subsequent *pasuk* that compares *zav* and *zava* as equivalent — *la-zachar ve-la-nekeva* — which creates a *hekesh* equating male and female. The *gemara* reasons that the *hekesh* applies in the context where the exclusion is made: where the text deals with *re'iyot*, the *mi'ut* excludes *zava* from that regime, and where the text deals with *yamim*, the *hekesh* brings *zav* in to require three separate days as well. Thus *zav* can achieve his status through three occurrences even on one day, but *zava* requires three consecutive days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַקֵּישׁ נְקֵבָה לְזָכָר – מָה זָכָר בִּרְאִיּוֹת, אַף נְקֵבָה בִּרְאִיּוֹת! הָא מַיעֵט רַחְמָנָא ״וְזֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The derivation from the verse could just as easily lead to the opposite conclusion. **But let** him **compare** the **female to** the **male; just as a male** becomes ritually impure **based on** the number of **sightings,** even if they all occur on the same day, **so too** should **a female** become ritually impure **based on** the number of **sightings,** even on the same day. The Gemara answers: **But the Merciful One excluded** this possibility by making use of the term **"and this."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ? מִסְתַּבְּרָא, קָאֵי בִּרְאִיּוֹת – מְמַעֵט רְאִיּוֹת; קָאֵי בִּרְאִיּוֹת – מְמַעֵט יָמִים?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** to make you exclude associating the ritual impurity of women with the number of sightings, rather than excluding associating the ritual impurity of men with the number of days? The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that this is so, as the context of the verse **deals with sightings,** and therefore an additional phrase serves to **exclude sightings,** but can you say that in a context where it **deals with sightings it excludes days?** The exclusionary term "and this" appears in the section discussing the *halakhot* of the *zav*, in the context of which it mentions sightings and not days, and therefore when it excludes something from these *halakhot* and limits them to specific cases, it will exclude it from being associated with sightings.

The *Rambam* in *Sefer Ha-Mitzvot* reflects precisely this distinction: he counts the *korbanot* for *zav* and *zava* as two separate *mitzvot*, because their underlying laws differ substantially, whereas the *korban* for *metzora* is listed as a single *mitzva* applying equally to men and women, since the laws of *tzara'at* apply identically to both.

### Mixed Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ מוּעָד – כֹּל שֶׁהֵעִידוּ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים; וְתָם – שֶׁיְּהוּ הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מְמַשְׁמְשִׁין בּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ נוֹגֵחַ; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מוּעָד – כֹּל שֶׁהֵעִידוּ בּוֹ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים אֶלָּא לַחֲזָרָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the subject of classifying an ox as innocuous or forewarned: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Which** type of ox **is** deemed **forewarned? Any** animal **about which** witnesses **testified** that it gored on **three days** is forewarned. **And** it reverts to its former **innocuous** status **if children pet it and** nevertheless **it does not gore;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Shimon says: A forewarned** ox is **any** ox **about which** witnesses **testified** that it gored **three times, and** the Sages **spoke** about **three days only with regard to reversals,** meaning that in order for an ox to revert to innocuous status, the ox must refrain from goring on three separate days. Each of these additional views on this subject combine aspects of both of the opinions mentioned in the mishna, those of Rabbi Yehuda and those of Rabbi Meir.

A *braisa* then presents the opinions of Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yose's position is a hybrid: his *reisha* mirrors Rabbi Yehuda in requiring three separate days for *mu'ad* status, but his *seifa* mirrors Rabbi Meir in that the standard for reverting to *tam* status is whether children can play with the animal without it goring. Rabbi Shimon holds that *mu'ad* status requires three occurrences (*shlosha pe'amim*) like Rabbi Meir, but limits the three-*pe'amim* rule to *chazara* — reversion to *tam* status — while applying different criteria to becoming *mu'ad* in the first place.

### Rav Nachman's Psak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמוּעָד, שֶׁהֲרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מוֹדֶה לוֹ; וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּתָם, שֶׁהֲרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מוֹדֶה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda concerning** the criteria for deeming an animal **forewarned, as Rabbi Yosei concedes to his** opinion on this issue, **and** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir concerning** the criteria for reverting the status of an animal to **innocuous, as Rabbi Yosei concedes to his** opinion on that issue.

Rav Nachman rules, citing Rabba bar Avuha: *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda be-mu'ad*, because Rabbi Yose agrees with Rabbi Yehuda on that point, making it two against one. *Ve-halakha ke-Rabbi Meir be-tam*, because Rabbi Yose agrees with Rabbi Meir on the reversion standard, again two against one. In effect, Rav Nachman is *paskening* like Rabbi Yose, who happens to align with different *tanna'im* on each half of the question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְלֵימָא מָר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמוּעָד, שֶׁהֲרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה לוֹ; וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּתָם, שֶׁהֲרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה לוֹ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סְבִירָא לִי, דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נִימּוּקוֹ עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: And let the Master say** the reverse, that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir concerning** the criteria for deeming an animal **forewarned, as Rabbi Shimon concedes to his** opinion on this issue **and** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda concerning** the criteria for reverting the status of an ox to **innocuous, as Rabbi Shimon concedes to his** opinion on that issue. Rav Naḥman **replied to** Rava: **I hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as Rabbi Yosei's analysis [*nimmuko*] is with him,** i.e., it is sound.

Rava challenges this: one could equally argue that Rabbi Shimon agrees with Rabbi Meir for *mu'ad*, making it two against two for that side, and that Rabbi Shimon agrees with Rabbi Yehuda for *tam*, making it two against two the other way — leaving the *psak* in the opposite direction. Rav Nachman's response is direct: *ana ke-Rabbi Yose sevira li* — I hold like Rabbi Yose because *Rabbi Yose nimuko imo*, Rabbi Yose always has well-grounded, well-developed reasoning. This invokes a general principle in the *klalei ha-psak*: when Rabbi Yose argues a position, his reasoning is typically the most fully articulated. It is worth noting the contrast with Rabbi Meir, about whom *Chazal* understood that his arguments were often so intellectually sophisticated that his colleagues could not always follow them — he was, in a sense, too creative for his own good.

## Muad Status Question

### Muad Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים״ דְּקָתָנֵי – לְיַיעוֹדֵי תּוֹרָא, אוֹ לְיַיעוֹדֵי גַּבְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised** before the Sages: The **three days that** the mishna **teaches** in the context of Rabbi Yehuda's opinion with regard to the testimony needed to assign the status of forewarned to an ox, are they needed in order **to render the ox forewarned, or** are they needed in order **to forewarn the man** who owns the ox, to inform him that he needs to take precautions to keep it from causing further harm?

With the *psak* established, the *gemara* opens a new *she'eila*: the three days that the *mishna* requires — are they *le-i'udei tora* or *le-i'udei gavra*? That is, are the three days of testimony meant to establish a legal *status* for the animal itself — that this ox is now a known *mu'ad* — or are they meant to inform the owner, placing on him a personal obligation to act, so that his subsequent failure to contain the animal generates full liability?

### Single-Day Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? דְּאָתוּ תְּלָתָא כִּיתֵּי סָהֲדִי בְּחַד יוֹמָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְיַיעוֹדֵי תּוֹרָא, מִיַּיעַד; וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ לְיַיעוֹדֵי גַּבְרָא, לָא מִיַּיעַד – מֵימָר אָמַר: הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּקָמַסְהֲדוּ בִּי. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** between the two possibilities? The Gemara answers: The practical difference is in a case **where three groups of witnesses came** to the court **on one day,** each testifying about a separate instance of goring caused by the ox on three separate days. **If you say** that their testimony serves to **render the ox forewarned** by determining that it gored on three separate days, then in this case the animal **has been classified** as **forewarned** on the basis of their testimony. **But if you say** that their testimony serves to **forewarn the man,** the ox **has not been classified** as **forewarned** on the basis of this testimony since the owner heard all of the testimony on a single day. This enables **him to say:** It is only **now** that they **testified against me;** and therefore his ox will not be deemed forewarned until he has been issued warnings over the course of three separate days. **What,** then, is the solution to the dilemma raised above?

The *nafka mina* is clarifying. At first the question may seem obscure, but the *gemara* focuses it with a concrete case.

The nafka mina is: what if three separate groups of witnesses came to court regarding the same ox, all on the same day? The ox gored three times in rapid succession, and three independent pairs of witnesses testified accordingly, all on that single day. If the requirement is *le-i'udei tora* — about establishing the ox's status — then the ox is indeed *mu'ad*, because three independent acts of testimony have been rendered. But if the requirement is *le-i'udei gavra* — about notifying the owner with enough time to act — then the owner can truthfully say: *hashta hu de-kamsa'adi bi* — only now are they informing me. He had no meaningful opportunity to respond to the information before the next goring occurred.

### Beit Din Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין הַשּׁוֹר נַעֲשֶׂה מוּעָד, עַד שֶׁיָּעִידוּ בּוֹ בִּפְנֵי בְעָלִים וּבִפְנֵי בֵית דִּין. הֵעִידוּ בּוֹ בִּפְנֵי בֵית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בְעָלִים, בִּפְנֵי בְעָלִים וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵית דִּין – אֵינוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה מוּעָד, עַד שֶׁיָּעִידוּ בּוֹ בִּפְנֵי בֵית דִּין וּבִפְנֵי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:3): **An ox does not become forewarned until** witnesses **testify against it in the presence of** its **owner and in the presence of a court.** If **they testified against it in the presence of a court but not in the presence of** its **owner,** or **in the presence of** its **owner but not in the presence of a court, it does not become forewarned until they testify against it in the presence of a court and in the presence of** its **owner.**

A *tashma* is brought: the ox cannot become *mu'ad* until the witnesses testify both in the presence of the owner and in the presence of *beit din* simultaneously. If testimony was given before *beit din* but without the owner present, or before the owner but not in court, it does not count toward establishing *mu'ad* status.

### Multiple Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶעִידוּהוּ שְׁנַיִם בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, וּשְׁנַיִם בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית – הֲרֵי כָּאן שָׁלֹשׁ עֵדִיּוֹת, וְהֵן עֵדוּת אַחַת לַהֲזָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, if **two** witnesses **testified against** the ox **concerning** its **first** incident of goring, **and two** other witnesses testified against it **concerning** the **second** incident, **and two** other witnesses testified against it **concerning** the **third** incident, **there are three** separate **testimonies here, but** they **are** considered as **one with regard to rendering** their statements as **conspiring testimony.**

The *braisa* continues with a more complex scenario: two witnesses came the first time, two more the second time, and two more the third time — six witnesses in three separate groups. The *braisa* notes that this constitutes three distinct *eduyot*, and that if any one group is later disqualified, it invalidates only that group's testimony while leaving the others intact. However, for the purpose of *hazamah* — if the witnesses are found to be *edim zomemim* — all six are treated as a single unit: *edut achat la-hazamah*.

### Third Group Hazama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצֵאת כַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה זוֹמֶמֶת – הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁתֵּי עֵדִיּוֹת, וְהוּא פָּטוּר וְהֵן פְּטוּרִים. נִמְצֵאת כַּת שְׁנִיָּה זוֹמֶמֶת – הֲרֵי כָּאן עֵדוּת אַחַת, וְהוּא פָּטוּר וְהֵן פְּטוּרִים. נִמְצֵאת כַּת שְׁלִישִׁית זוֹמֶמֶת – כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, if **the first set** of witnesses **is determined to be conspiring** witnesses, **there are two testimonies** remaining **here** that claim that the ox gored, and the owner must pay for half the cost of the damage in each case. **But** the owner of the ox is **exempt** from having to pay the full cost of the damage, **and** the conspiring witnesses are **exempt** from paying the owner what he would have had to pay were their testimony accepted and his ox deemed forewarned, as they testified only concerning the first incident of goring, and the ox would not have been deemed forewarned on the basis of this testimony alone. And similarly, if **the second set** of witnesses was also **determined to be conspiring** witnesses, **there is one testimony** remaining **here** that claims that the ox gored, the third. At this point too, the owner of the ox is **exempt** from having to pay the full cost of the damage **and** the conspiring witnesses are **exempt** from having to pay the owner the full cost of the damage. If **the third group** of witnesses was also **determined to be conspiring witnesses, all** the witnesses become **liable.**

The *braisa* then explores what happens when each successive group is found to be *zomemim*. If the first group is disqualified, there remain only two valid testimonies, the animal is not *mu'ad*, and both the defendant and the disqualified witnesses are *patur*. If the second group is disqualified, there remains only one valid testimony, and again both are *patur*. But if the third group is found to be *zomemim*, then *kulan chayavim* — all of them are liable. The logic is that it is only the third group whose testimony creates the *mu'ad* status, and therefore only at the moment of the third group's testimony does the *hazamah* have real monetary consequence — shifting liability from *chatzi nezek* to *nezek shalem*. The *edim zomemim* must pay what they sought to impose: the additional exposure that comes with *mu'ad* status.

The question emerges regarding how the six witnesses split liability. The *braisa*'s formulation that they function as *edut achat la-hazamah* might suggest they all split it equally. But the *gemara*'s *shakla ve-tarya* points toward the conclusion that only the third group bears sole responsibility, since it is their testimony alone that completes the *mu'ad* determination, while the first two groups' testimony, standing alone, achieved nothing.

### Rav Kahana's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְיַיעוֹדֵי תּוֹרָא – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְיַיעוֹדֵי גַּבְרָא, לֵימְרוּ הָנָךְ קַמָּאֵי: אֲנַן מִי הֲוָה יָדְעִינַן דְּבָתַר תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי – אָתוּ הָנֵי וּמְיַיעֲדִי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **If you say** that the purpose of the testimony is **to render the ox forewarned,** the *halakha* presented in this *baraita* **works out well,** as according to this opinion it can be said that all of the witnesses came on the same day at the behest of the alleged victim of the third incident, and therefore they were all certainly aware of each other's testimony. Consequently, the first set of witnesses shares in the responsibility for the testimony of the last group, as they are all co-witnesses in the effort to have the ox rendered forewarned. **But if you say** that the witnesses came **to forewarn the man,** to caution him to safeguard his animal from causing harm, then they must have come on three separate days. And if so, **let these first** witnesses **say: Did we know that after three days these** other witnesses **were going to come to testify about** the animal? Therefore, it must be that the case in the *baraita* is one where the three groups of witnesses all came on the same day, and if that is so, the purpose of the testimony must be to render the ox forewarned.

Rav Kahana challenges the *le-i'udei tora* interpretation sharply. Rav Kahana's objection: even if you say that it is all about establishing the ox's status, how can you make the last group of witnesses liable as *edim zomemim*? They arrived at court having seen an ox gore another animal, and they came to testify that the owner should pay *chatzi nezek* — they believed they were dealing with a *shor tam*. They had no way of knowing that two prior groups had already testified before them, rendering their testimony the decisive third strike. *Anan le-chayuvei gavra palga nizka asinan* — we came only to make him pay *chatzi nezek*, they could say. How can they be held responsible for creating *mu'ad* status if they were unaware that their testimony was the one that achieved it?

This challenge applies even if the *mu'ad* determination is purely about the animal's status and not the owner's notification. Even within the *le-i'udei tora* framework, the final witnesses acted without any knowledge of what their testimony would accomplish. Rav Kahana's point is that for *edim zomemim* liability to attach, the witnesses must have had some awareness of the consequences their false testimony would create.

### Conspiracy Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּשֶׁבָּאוּ רְצוּפִים. רָבִינָא אָמַר: בְּמַכִּירִין בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר, וְאֵין מַכִּירִין אֶת הַשּׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It must be that the case concerns a situation **where** the different groups of witnesses **were** observed **signaling** to each other, and therefore it is clear that they were aware of each other's testimony and that they came to the court for this purpose. **Rav Ashi said:** The case is one **where they** all **came in succession,** one following the other, and therefore it is clear that the latter witnesses are aware of the former. **Ravina said:** In this case the witnesses said that they were **acquainted with the owner of the** goring **ox but** they were **not familiar with the ox** itself. This indicates that they were coming to court to render the ox forewarned, as the witnesses are not able to identify the goring ox and they would not be able to hold its owner liable to pay for half the cost of the damage, as this payment is paid only from the proceeds of the sale of the animal. Therefore, the only purpose of their testimony would be to hold the owner liable to pay the full cost of the damage for the third incident, as this payment is made from the owner's superior-quality land.

Rav Ashi offers a resolution: *ke-she-ba'u retzufim* — the case of *edim zomemim* liability applies when the witnesses arrived one right after another in rapid sequence. Consecutive appearances in court serve as circumstantial evidence of a coordinated conspiracy. Ravina offers a different indicator: the case involves witnesses who could identify the owner of the ox (*makirin ba'al ha-shor*) but could not identify the specific ox (*ve-ein makirin et ha-shor*). Such witnesses came and said, in effect, "your ox gored someone" — but had they been asked to pick the ox out of a herd, they would not have been able to do so. This is evidence that they were fabricating testimony. Both of these markers — consecutive appearance or inability to identify the animal — can establish the conspiracy that would make the *edim zomemim* framework applicable.

## Friend's Animal Incitement

### Third-Party Attack

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הַמְשַׁסֶּה כַּלְבּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ בַּחֲבֵירוֹ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised** before the Sages: In the case of **one who incites another's dog against another,** i.e., a third person, and he is injured, **what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* now takes up a related *she'eila*: *ha-mshaseh kalbo shel chaveiro be-chaveiro mahu* — what is the law when someone incites his friend's dog to attack a third party? The inciter is not the owner; he has used another person's animal as a weapon against someone else entirely.

### Owner's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשַׁסֶּה – וַדַּאי פָּטוּר; בַּעַל כֶּלֶב – מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא – מַאי עָבֵידְנָא לֵיהּ? אוֹ דִלְמָא, אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּיָדְעַתְּ בְּכַלְבָּךְ דִּמְשַׁסֵּי לֵיהּ וּמִשְׁתַּסֵּי, לָא אִבְּעִי לָךְ לְאַשְׁהוֹיֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **The one who incited** the animal against him is **certainly exempt,** since he did not cause the damage directly and neither did his property; but **what** is the *halakha* with regard to the **owner of the dog?** The Gemara explains the different sides of the question: **Do we say** that the owner of the dog **can say to** the injured party: **What did I do to** the dog? **Or perhaps we say to him: Since you were aware that if others incite your dog it is prone to** being **affected** by the **incitement** and attacking, **you ought not to have kept it** in your possession. Since you did, you are liable for the damage and the injuries it causes.

One possibility is that the owner can claim: *ana mai avidna leh* — what did I do? I was not present, I did not direct my animal, I am not responsible for what someone else caused my dog to do.

The counter-argument is that the owner may nonetheless bear responsibility: *keivan de-yadata be-kalbecha de-mashseh leh u-mishtaseh* — since you knew that others were inciting your dog and that the dog was in fact being incited and attacking, *lo iba lach le-ashuyei* — you should not have kept the dog around. That is, once an owner is on notice that his animal is being used as a weapon by others, his failure to take protective measures may itself constitute culpable negligence.

### Tam Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: וְתָם – שֶׁיְּהוּ הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מְמַשְׁמְשִׁין בּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹגֵחַ. הָא נוֹגֵחַ – חַיָּיב! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, מִי קָתָנֵי: נָגַח חַיָּיב? דִּלְמָא הָא נָגַח – לָא הָוֵי תָּם; וּבְהָהִיא נְגִיחָה לָא מִחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Come** and **hear** a solution from the *baraita*: **And** an ox reverts to its former **innocuous** status **if children pet it and** nevertheless **it does not gore.** It can be inferred from this that **if it gores** when the children pet it, the owner would be **liable** to pay for the damage. An ox that gores because children pet it can be likened to one that gores because it was incited to do so, and nevertheless the mishna holds the owner liable. **Abaye said: Is it taught** in the *baraita* that if **it gored** the owner is **liable? Perhaps** that is not what the *baraita* means. It merely means that **if it gored it** does **not** revert to **innocuous** status; but the owner is **not liable for** any of the damage caused by **that goring** because the children incited the animal to do so, and therefore nothing can be conclusively demonstrated from the *baraita*.

An attempt is made to resolve this from the *mishna*'s standard for reverting an animal to *tam* status: *be-tam she-hayu tinokot me-shamshin bo ve-eino noge'ach* — if children were playing around the animal and it did not gore, the animal is *tam*. The implicit inference is: *ha nogei'ach chayav* — if it did gore when the children were playing around it, that act of goring creates *chiyuv*. Since children playing around an animal constitutes a form of incitement, this might serve as a precedent for holding the owner liable when someone else incites his dog.

### Abaye Response

Abaye rejects this inference. *Mi ke-tani nagach chayav?* — did the *mishna* actually say that if it gored in that circumstance it is *chayav*? All the *mishna* says is that if the animal did not gore when children played with it, that is the standard for reverting to *tam* status. If it did gore, the consequence is simply that it does not revert to *tam* — *dilma ha nagach lo havi tam u-be-hai negicha lo mechayev* — but for that particular act of goring it is not *chayav*, because it was provoked by the children. One cannot prove anything about the incited-dog case from this *mishna*.

## Tashma Proof

### Braita Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שִׁיסָּה בּוֹ אֶת הַכֶּלֶב, שִׁיסָּה בּוֹ נָחָשׁ – פָּטוּר. מַאי, לָאו פָּטוּר מְשַׁסֶּה, וְחַיָּיב בַּעַל כֶּלֶב? לָא; אֵימָא: פָּטוּר אַף מְשַׁסֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to prove this point from another source: **Come** and **hear** a solution from a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 76b): If one **incited a dog against another** person, or **incited a snake against another** person, he is **exempt. What, is it not that the one who incited** the dog against him is **exempt, but** the **owner of the dog** is **liable?** The Gemara rejects this: **No, say** that **even the one who incited** the animal against him is **exempt,** as well as the owner.

The *gemara* returns to the original *tashma*: *shissa bo et ha-kelev, shissa bo et ha-nachash, patur* — one who incites a dog or a snake to attack is *patur*.

### First Inference

The first possible reading of this *braisa*: *patur ha-mashse ve-chayav ba'al ha-kelev* — the inciter is *patur* but the owner of the dog is *chayav*. This is the straightforward reading that exonerates the inciter while placing responsibility on the animal's owner.

### Counter-Inference

The alternative reading is: *eima patur af mashse* — perhaps the *braisa* means that the owner is obviously *patur* (because the mouth of the animal is *kechatzer ha-mazik*, and one can ask why the victim put their hand into the dog's mouth), and even the inciter is *patur* — the less obvious case. On this reading, both parties are exempt.

### Rava's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: הַמְשַׁסֶּה כַּלְבּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ בַּחֲבֵירוֹ – חַיָּיב; שִׁיסָּהוּ הוּא בְּעַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? כׇּל הַמְשַׁנֶּה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְשִׁינָּה בּוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Even **if you say** that one who **incites the dog of another against** yet **another** person is **liable,** nevertheless if **he incited the dog against himself,** i.e., the dog bit the one who was inciting it to attack, he is **exempt. What is the reason** for this? Due to the principle that with regard to **anyone who deviates** from normative behavior in his actions, **if another came along** afterward **and deviates** from the norm **with regard to** the action the first has done and thereby causes damage to him, the one who causes the damage is **exempt** from liability. Since this individual deviated from the normative behavior and incited the dog against himself, the owner of the dog is not responsible for any damage the dog causes to him as a result, although generally speaking any time a dog bites it is in and of itself a deviation from its typical behavior and something for which its owner would normally be held liable.

Rava sharpens the analysis: *im timtzi lomar ha-mshaseh kalbo shel chaveiro be-chaveiro chayav* — if you say that one who incites another's dog against a third party is *chayav*, then *shisseihu hu be-atzmo patur* — if the person incited the dog against himself, the owner is *patur*. That is, if the victim was himself responsible for provoking the attack by engaging with the animal, the owner cannot be held liable.

### Change Principle

The underlying principle is: *kol ha-meshaneh, u-va acher ve-shinah bo, patur* — if one person introduces a change in the normal situation, and then another person comes and introduces a further change, the second party is *patur*. In the context of the dog: the inciter altered the normal situation by provoking the animal, and then the dog itself responded — one change layered upon another. Once the initial change is introduced by someone other than the owner, the owner's responsibility is displaced. This principle will recur shortly in the discussion of the two cows in the public domain.

## Cows Kicking Case

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: אִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ כְּווֹתָיךְ – דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אַחַת רְבוּצָה וְאַחַת מְהַלֶּכֶת, וּבִעֲטָה מְהַלֶּכֶת בִּרְבוּצָה – פְּטוּרָה. רְבוּצָה בִּמְהַלֶּכֶת – חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: It was stated in the name of Reish Lakish in accordance with your** opinion, that if anyone deviates from normative behavior in his actions, if another came along afterward and deviates from the norm with regard to the action the first has done and thereby causes damage to him, the one who causes the damage is exempt from liability. **As Reish Lakish says:** If there were **two cows in the public domain, one prone** in the street **and** the other **one walking,** if the **walking** cow **kicked** the **prone** cow, its owner is **exempt** from liability. But if the **prone** cow kicked the **walking** cow, its owner is **liable.** The rationale for this is that since it is typical behavior for cows to walk in the public domain and the prone cow deviated from this behavior, even if the walking cow also acted atypically and kicked the prone cow, the owner is exempt from liability.

Rav Papa brings a *mimra* from Reish Lakish that aligns with Rava's earlier analysis. Reish Lakish states: if two cows are in *reshut ha-rabim* and one is crouching (*revutza*) while the other is walking (*mahalechet*, and the walking one kicks the crouching one, the walking cow is *ptura* — exempt. The reason is that the walking cow is simply doing what cows do in the public thoroughfare; this is the normal and permitted use of *reshut ha-rabim*, and it falls under the category of *regel*, ordinary walking damage. But if the crouching cow kicked the walking cow — *revutza be-mahalechet* — the crouching cow is *chayavet*, liable. By kicking from a resting position, the crouching cow departed from normal behavior (*kol ha-meshaneh chayav*).

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא – בְּהָהִיא חַיּוֹבֵי מְחַיֵּיבְנָא, דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: כִּי אִית לָךְ רְשׁוּתָא – לְסַגּוֹיֵי עֲלַי; לְבַעוֹטֵי בִּי לֵית לָךְ רְשׁוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said** back **to** Rav Pappa: That is not in accordance with my opinion, as **I would have held** the owner of the walking cow **liable in that** case, **because** in my opinion **we say to him** on behalf of the prone cow: It is true **that you have the right to tread on me** in the public domain but **you do not have the right to kick me,** and therefore Rava's and Reish Lakish's opinions are not exactly the same.

Rava challenges Reish Lakish's ruling on the walking cow. Even if a cow has the right to be walking through *reshut ha-rabim*, that does not include the right to kick other animals. Kicking is not part of the normal walking motion; it is an independent act of damage. Therefore, Rava argues, even the walking cow should be *chayav*.

### Rights Analysis

The distinction Rava draws is conceptually precise: *ki it lach reshuta lisgu'ei alai, limva'at bi leit lach reshuta* — you have rights to walk past me in the street, but you do not have rights to kick me while doing so. The right of transit in *reshut ha-rabim* covers walking, not striking. Kicking is a *tolada* of *keren* — analogous to a horn-type damage — not a *tolada* of *regel*, since it is not the ordinary and inevitable consequence of the animal's movement through public space.

## Property Domain Effects

### Mishna Payment Rates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר הַמַּזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק, כֵּיצַד? נָגַח, נָגַף, נָשַׁךְ, רָבַץ, בָּעַט; בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And what is** the case of the **ox that causes damage** while **on the property of the injured** party, mentioned in an earlier mishna (15b) that listed animals that are forewarned? If the animal **gored, pushed, bit, squatted** upon, or **kicked** another animal **in the public domain,** the owner is liable to **pay half** the cost of **the damage** if the ox was innocuous, but if it acted while **on the property of the injured** party, **Rabbi Tarfon says:** He must pay the **full** cost of **the damage, and the Rabbis say:** He must pay **half** the cost of **the damage,** as in any other case classified as Goring.

The next *mishna* addresses the rates of payment owed when an animal causes damage in the *reshut ha-nizak* — the victim's private property. The *mishna* enumerates the relevant types of damage: *nagach, nagaf, nashach, ravatz, ba'at* — goring, pushing, biting, sitting on something, or kicking — and notes that when such damage occurs in *reshut ha-rabim*, the owner of a *tam* animal pays *chatzi nezek*.

### Rabbi Tarfon vs. Chachamim

The *machlokes* is over what happens when a *tam* animal causes *keren* damage in the *reshut ha-nizak*. Rabbi Tarfon holds that the owner pays *nezek shalem* — full damages — because the logic that reduces liability to *chatzi nezek* in *reshut ha-rabim* does not apply when the animal has trespassed onto the victim's property. The *chachamim* hold that *keren tam* always pays only *chatzi nezek*, regardless of domain — it is simply the *Torah*'s rule.

### Rabbi Tarfon's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: וּמָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁהֵקֵל עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר; הֶחְמִיר עֲלֵיהֶן בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – לְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם; מְקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר עַל הַקֶּרֶן – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, לְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק; אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנַּחְמִיר עָלָיו בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – לְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said to the** Rabbis: **If in a place where** the Torah **was lenient with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and with regard to Trampling,** specifically **in the public domain, as** the owner **is exempt** from liability, nevertheless the Torah was **strict with regard to these** forms of damage if they occurred **on the property of the injured** party, requiring him **to pay** the **full** cost of **the damage,** then in **a place where** the Torah **was strict with regard to** cases of damage classified as **Goring,** specifically **in the public domain,** requiring the owner **to pay for half** the cost of **the damage, is it not right that we** should **be strict with regard to this** form of damage if it occurs **on the property of the injured** party to likewise require the owner of the animal **to pay** the **full** cost of **the damage?**

Rabbi Tarfon makes a *kal va-chomer* argument. *Shein* and *regel* are entirely *patur* in *reshut ha-rabim*, yet in *reshut ha-nizak* they pay *nezek shalem*. *Keren* in *reshut ha-rabim* — where *shein* and *regel* are *patur* — still requires the owner to pay *chatzi nezek*. Therefore, in *reshut ha-nizak* — where *shein* and *regel* pay *nezek shalem* — should *keren* not pay *nezek shalem* as well?

### Chachamim Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין – לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן. מָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חֲצִי נֶזֶק, אַף בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** Although there is an *a fortiori* inference being applied here, still **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source,** meaning that the *halakha* cannot be stricter with the inference than it is with the case that serves as the source of the inference. Therefore, **just as** one is liable to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** classified as Goring **in the public domain, so too,** for damage classified as Goring **on the property of the injured** party he is liable to pay only **half** the cost of **the damage.**

The *chachamim* apply *dayyo la-ba min ha-din lihyot ka-nidon* — it is sufficient for the conclusion of a *kal va-chomer* to equal its premise, not to exceed it. *Mah bi-reshut ha-rabim chatzi nezek, af bi-reshut ha-nizak chatzi nezek* — just as *keren tam* pays *chatzi nezek* in the public domain, so too it pays *chatzi nezek* in the victim's domain. The *kal va-chomer* cannot generate a more stringent outcome than the rule from which it derives.

### Rabbi Tarfon Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָדוּן קֶרֶן מִקֶּרֶן, אֲנִי אָדוּן קֶרֶן מֵרֶגֶל – וּמָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁהֵקֵל עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – הֶחְמִיר בַּקֶּרֶן; מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל, בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנַּחְמִיר בַּקֶּרֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said to them: I will not derive an inference** with regard to **Goring from** a different case of **Goring. I will** instead **derive an inference** with regard to **Goring from Trampling: And if in a place where** the Torah **was lenient with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** specifically **in the public domain,** as the owner is exempt from liability, nevertheless the Torah was **strict with regard to** damage classified as **Goring,** requiring him to pay half the cost of the damage, then in **a place where** the Torah **was strict with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** specifically **on the property of the injured** party as the animal's owner is obligated to pay the full cost of the damage, **is it not right that we** should **be** equally **strict with regard to** damage classified as **Goring** and require payment of the full cost of the damage in this case as well?

Rabbi Tarfon responds that he was not comparing *keren* to *keren* — he was comparing *keren* to *regel*. His *kal va-chomer* is constructed from *regel*, not from *keren* itself: *u-mah bi-mkom she-heikel al ha-shein ve-al ha-regel bi-reshut ha-rabim, hechmir be-keren, makom she-hechmir al ha-shein ve-al ha-regel bi-reshut ha-nizak, eino din she-nachmir be-keren* — in the place where *shein* and *regel* are treated leniently (i.e., *patur* in *reshut ha-rabim*), *keren* is treated stringently (*chatzi nezek*); in the place where *shein* and *regel* are treated stringently (*nezek shalem* in *reshut ha-nizak*), should *keren* not also be treated stringently, paying *nezek shalem*? The *chachamim* apply *dayyo* once more and hold firm: *chatzi nezek* for *keren tam* applies in all domains.

## Tosafot Analysis

### Counting Gorings

With the *mishna*'s dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and the *chachamim* established, the shiur turns to a *Tosafot* on 24a that addresses the question of how gorings are counted toward establishing and triggering *mu'ad* liability. *Tosafot* raises the question: when exactly does the owner become *chayav* to pay *nezek shalem* as a *mu'ad* owner? Is it on the third goring, or only on the fourth? The *Tosafot* notes the view that the answer might be the fifth goring, and explains why.

### Tosafot's Position

*Tosafot* argues: *i le-i'udei gavra lo she-yehei mu'ad la-avor be-hatra'ot, de-im ken lo haya mechayev ad negichah shel chamishit* — if *mu'ad* is about the owner having been warned (*le-i'udei gavra*) and then violating that warning, one would expect *chiyuv* not until the fifth goring. The reasoning: the first goring generates the first warning; the second goring is a violation of the first warning and generates the second; the third goring violates the second warning and generates the third. At that point, the owner has been warned three times. But the fourth goring is the first one that violates all three accumulated warnings — and yet, that fourth goring is itself also the occasion of being told "this has now happened a fourth time." So the violation of the third warning, strictly speaking, would only become actionable at the fifth goring. 

*Tosafot* uses this reductio to reject the *le-i'udei gavra* framework: the rule cannot be about warning, because that would produce an absurd and unsupported result. Instead, *Tosafot* maintains that *mu'ad* is a *status* — it is about what kind of animal this has proven itself to be, not about whether the owner has violated a specific admonition. The gorings establish the status; the owner's consequent liability follows automatically from that status.

### Contradicting Tosafot

Yet, notably, another *Tosafot* in *Bava Batra* reaches precisely the opposite conclusion — the one that the *Tosafot* on 24a was rejecting. That *Tosafot* in *Bava Batra* holds that the owner does not become fully *chayav* until the fifth goring, because only at that point has he genuinely violated the complete set of three warnings. This represents the *le-i'udei gavra* position in full, and it directly contradicts the reasoning of *Tosafot* here. It is a striking internal tension within *Tosafot* across the two *masekhtot*, leaving the two approaches to *mu'ad* status — as a quality of the animal versus as a violation of the owner's informed duty — in genuine dispute, even within the *Rishonim*.
