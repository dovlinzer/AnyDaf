# Gittin 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Kereach

### Structure & Definition

The daf opens at the bottom of 81b with a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan concerning the *get kereach* — the "bald get," which originated from the *get mekushar* developed for *kohanim* to slow the process of divorcing their wives. In the *get mekushar*, the scribe writes several lines of text and folds the document over a blank space, with witnesses signing on the outer panel. This accordion-like structure is then repeated — fold upon fold, each sewn shut, each bearing a witness signature on its panel. A minimum of three such folds with three signatures is required for the *get* to be valid.

The *get kereach* arises when a husband prepared many such folds but failed to obtain signatures on all of them. He may have created ten folds but secured only nine signatures, for example. The *braita* treats this as a serious defect — according to Rabbi Meir, the woman must leave the marriage and the offspring is a *mamzer*, the gravest of consequences.

### Validity Requirements

Despite the severity of this defect, the *halacha* allows the blank spots to be filled in with *psulei edim* — disqualified witnesses — perhaps even a *karov*, a relative. The underlying rule is that the basic minimum of witnesses that the Rabbis required must themselves be *kasher*, but beyond that core, the remaining folds may be completed with otherwise disqualified signatories. The question raised at the close of the previous session was whether, when only two valid signatures exist — which suffices on a Torah level — the third must also be *kasher*, or whether the third may be filled in by a *karov* or other *pasul* witness.

### Disqualified Witnesses

The *gemara* is operating with the principle that even disqualified witnesses may fill the remaining blank spots in a *get kereach*, provided the foundational two or three valid signatures are in place. What requires further clarification is the scope of this leniency.

### One Disqualified Maximum

Rabbi Yochanan states explicitly: *lo huchsharu bo ela ed echad karov bilvad* — only one disqualified witness is permitted. All the other folds must bear the signatures of *kasher edim*. The permission to use a *pasul* extends to one slot only, not two or more.

### Validation Logic

The reason is elegant and practical. When *beis din* comes to validate a *get* bearing, say, ten signatures, it will seek out people who can identify the handwriting and confirm the signatures. Any three confirmations will suffice. If more than one *pasul* witness had signed, there is a real danger that *beis din* might validate the *get* on the basis of two *pasul* signatures — leaving only one valid witness among the three. Since we cannot guarantee which three of the ten signatures will be brought forward for validation, allowing two *pasulim* to sign creates an unacceptable risk that the woman will be permitted to remarry on the strength of invalid testimony. Restricting the *pasul* signature to one ensures that any three signatures that *beis din* selects will include at least two *kasher* witnesses.

### Abaye's Proof

Abaye adds a corroboration from a careful reading of the *braita* itself: *matniten nami dika* — the *braita* can be read to support this. The *braita* lists its cases as "seven folds and six witnesses, six folds and five witnesses, five folds and four witnesses," always speaking of a shortfall of exactly one witness. It never envisions filling in more than one missing signature. *Shma mina*, Abaye concludes: this confirms that only one *pasul* signature is ever contemplated.

One might ask whether it matters where in the sequence the *pasul* witness signs — must he be at the beginning, middle, or end? The answer flows from the same *braita*. Since it never specifies a location for the *pasul* signature, we infer that he may sign in any fold. As the *gemara* puts it: *midlo kavah lei makom* — because no particular location was specified, the *pasul* may sign anywhere — at the beginning, middle, or end.

### Non-Consecutive Validati…

A second inference follows from the same logic: *beis din* need not validate three consecutive signatures. If *ratsufin* — consecutive signatures — were required, one could simply mandate that the *pasul* witness sign only in a position immediately following two *kasher* witnesses. In that arrangement, any sequence of three consecutive signatures would necessarily include two valid ones. Since the *gemara* instead limits the *pasul* to a single occurrence regardless of position, it must be that *beis din* may validate any three signatures it chooses, not only consecutive ones. This is the rule of *mikol tlasa d'mekaymin* — validation may be drawn from any three confirmable signatures in the document.

### Rashi's Interpretation

Rashi's reading of this passage is remarkable — remarkable enough that at the end of his commentary he acknowledges it openly. His explanation is as follows: one might have supposed that a *pasul* could sign freely, so long as he was always preceded by two *kasher* witnesses. Under such an arrangement — *kasher*, *kasher*, *pasul*; *kasher*, *kasher*, *pasul* — the sequence would be repeatedly anchored in valid testimony, and a rule requiring consecutive validation would make the arrangement safe no matter how many *psulim* signed. Since the *gemara* rejects that approach and insists on only a single *pasul* signature, it necessarily follows that validation is not limited to consecutive groupings. Any three signatures may be used, and therefore even one *pasul* among ten signatories poses a risk if a second *pasul* is also present.

At the end of his commentary, Rashi writes with characteristic directness: *k'ein zu ikara shel sugya zu v'lo shimatiha ken* — "This, in my view, is the true meaning of this passage; that was not the tradition I received. But the version I previously had, from my teacher's written commentary, did not seem correct to me — and so I am presenting what I believe to be the proper explanation."

### Rabbi Ammi Case

The practical upshot is illustrated by a case that came before Rabbi Ammi. When presented with a *get kereach* bearing a blank spot, he ruled: *tzei v'hashleima lav afilu min hashuk* — go and complete it, even with someone from the marketplace, meaning even a slave could fill in the last missing signature. This reflects the ruling attributed to Ben Nanos, that one *pasul* signature — even that of a slave — suffices to complete the document.

One might have supposed that allowing a *pasul* as the *first* signer would set the wrong tone, or that there is something structurally significant about where in the sequence he appears. That intuition is understandable, but the *gemara* gives it no footing. What saves the document from the principle of *nimtza echad mehem karov o pasul* — that discovering a *pasul* among the signatories invalidates the group — is the context: it is understood that this particular signature serves a distinct purpose and is not part of the core witnessing group. The *Rama* applies a parallel logic to the practice in his time of passing a *ketuba* around for relatives and disqualified persons to sign after the two valid witnesses, as an honor to the *chatan* and *kallah*. Because the context makes clear that these additional signatories are functioning differently, they do not invalidate the *ketuba*.

## Conditional Divorce

### Exclusionary Get

With the conclusion of this *perek*, the *gemara* transitions to a new and altogether different set of questions. The new *perek* opens with *Hamgaresh et ishto* — a case in which a man divorces his wife and adds: *harei at muteres l'chol adam ela l'ploni* — behold, you are permitted to every man except Frank. He excludes one specific individual from the divorce's effect. Is this a valid *get*?

### R. Eliezer vs. Chachamim

Rabbi Eliezer rules that it is valid (*matir*), while the *Chachamim* forbid it — meaning they do not recognize it as a valid *get* at all. The core objection of the *Chachamim* is that a *get* must constitute *kritut* — a complete severance — and a *get* that leaves the woman still bound to even one person in the world fails that standard.

### Remedial Procedure

If the *get* as given is invalid, what should the husband do? The *mishna* prescribes: *yitlena mimena veyachzor veyitnena lah* — he takes the *get* back from her and gives it to her again, this time saying *harei at muteret lechol adam* without restriction. The underlying principle is that an invalid *act* of delivery does not consume the *get* itself as an instrument. The *cheftza* — the physical document — remains a valid instrument, and can be re-delivered with proper language.

However, if the exclusionary language was written *into the body of the get* — *katvo betocho* — then even if it is subsequently erased, the *get* is *pasul*. The witnesses signed a document that was from the outset an invalid *krisus*, and that defect cannot be undone. The distinction is between a problem with the *act* of delivery, which is remediable, and a problem with the *instrument* itself, which is not.

### Exclusion vs. Condition

At this point the *gemara* poses its fundamental analytical question: *hai ela — chutz o al menat?* What is the nature of the exclusion expressed by the word *ela* ("except")? Does it mean *chutz* — that the *get* takes effect for the whole world with the actual exception of Reuven, so that vis-à-vis Reuven she remains a married woman — or does it mean *al menat* — on the condition that she not marry or have relations with Reuven, so that the *get* takes full conceptual effect for everyone, but is retroactively annulled if she violates the condition?

The practical consequences are sharply different. Under the *chutz* reading: she sleeps with Shimon — not adultery; she sleeps with Reuven — adultery, because vis-à-vis Reuven she is still *eishet ish*. Under the *al menat* reading: she sleeps with Shimon and subsequently sleeps with Reuven. Once she has violated the condition, the *get* is retroactively void — which means her prior relations with Shimon were also adulterous.

### Practical Consequences

Beyond the question of adultery, the framing has implications for the *kehunah* status of the *get* as well. As one interlocutor notes, there is a meaningful difference between excluding a *kohen* from the *get* and excluding an ordinary Israelite. Ordinarily, a *kohen* already faces an *issur lav* against marrying a *grusha*; but if the husband explicitly says she is forbidden to that *kohen*, the act would constitute adultery, not merely an *issur kehunah*. Moreover, the question of whether the *issur kehunah* applies even to relations outside of marriage — a matter of dispute between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad* — gives the exclusion additional force in the case of a *kohen*.

One might also ask about excluding someone she could never marry regardless — say, a *chayav kares* relative, whose *kiddushin* would not take effect at all. In that case, excluding that person from the *get* would arguably have no legal consequence. The case of a *kohen*, by contrast, clearly does have a profound effect, as it transforms what would have been an *issur kehunah* into outright adultery.

### Word Ela Meaning

So which reading is correct — *chutz* or *al menat*? The *gemara* now argues that the plain meaning of *ela* is *chutz*. If the two are equivalent, the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* is most naturally cast as a dispute over whether *chutz* — actual exclusion — can produce a valid *get*. *Chutz* is obviously the problematic case: how can a *get* that is not effective vis-à-vis one person in the world qualify as *kritut*? If the dispute were only about *al menat*, it would be far less surprising, since any ordinary conditional transaction works, and Rabbi Eliezer would simply be applying standard rules of *tenai*. The *chiddush* would be minimal.

To confirm the meaning of *ela*, the *gemara* invokes a proof from an entirely different domain.

### Negaim Proof

Ravina brings the following *braita*: *kol habatim mitamim b'negaim ela shel ovdei kochavim* — all houses can become *tamei* through *nega tzaraas*, except the houses of non-Jews. The word *ela* there is structurally identical to the *ela* in our *mishna*. The question: does *ela* here mean "with the exception of" (*chutz*), or "on the condition that" (*al menat*)? If *ela* meant *al menat*, the sentence would read: all Jewish houses are susceptible to *negaim* only on the condition that the houses of non-Jews are not susceptible. And if the condition were violated — if non-Jewish houses could become *tamei* — then Jewish houses would not become *tamei*. This is obviously absurd. Who is one making this condition with? And further, the condition cannot be violated in any case, since a separate *braita* derives from the verse *v'nasati nega tzaraas b'veit eretz achuzaschem* — "I will place a plague of *tzaraas* in a house in the land of your inheritance" — that only property held as inheritance is susceptible; houses of non-Jews, even within *Eretz Yisrael*, are never susceptible to *negaim*. The *al menat* reading is thus completely incoherent in that context. *Ela* must mean *chutz* — "with the exception of." *Shma mina*, so too in our *mishna*.

This produces a substantial *chiddush* for Rabbi Eliezer's position: he holds that a *get* can take effect even when it is not effective vis-à-vis a particular person. The *get* is, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, valid as a document of divorce even though it leaves an island of continued marriage to one individual.

### R. Yose Braita

The *gemara* then notes that our *mishna*, now established as framing the dispute in terms of *chutz*, does not accord with a *braita* of Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehudah. That *braita* frames the dispute differently: Rabbi Yose states that Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* do not disagree at all about *chutz*. If a man says *harei at muteret l'chol adam chutz miploni* — you are permitted to everyone except so-and-so — everyone agrees she is not divorced. *Al mah nechleku?* — about what do they disagree? About *al menat*: if a man says *harei at muteret l'chol adam al menat she-lo tinassei l'ploni* — you are permitted to everyone on the condition that you not marry so-and-so. There, Rabbi Eliezer says the *get* is valid, while the *Chachamim* forbid it.

On Rabbi Eliezer's view in this *braita*, the *get* functions like any standard conditional transaction. The conceptual effect of the *get* is total — she is completely divorced from everyone — and the condition is simply an external restriction, like any other *tenai*. The *Chachamim* respond: any ordinary condition (*kol tenai d'alma*) works because the conceptual act is not compromised. But here, even if the *get* is conceptually complete, it does not in practice make her *muteret l'chol adam*. A *get* must produce actual freedom to marry anyone, and if in practice she is barred from one person, that practical limitation renders it an inadequate *kritut* — *hacha shiyyer lah b'get*.

*Tosafot* raises a further nuance: even if the condition is formulated as prohibiting both marriage and intercourse with the excluded person — making the exclusion as total in practice as the *chutz* formulation — Rabbi Eliezer would still say the *get* is valid. The reason: Rabbi Eliezer looks at the conceptual structure. The *get* itself is one hundred percent effective; the condition is an external layer. The *Chachamim* look at practical reality. If she cannot in practice be with that person, the *get* has not produced full *kritut*.

This is a genuine and deep *machloket*. The question is whether the effectiveness of a legal instrument is measured by its conceptual structure or by its practical outcome. One analogy: if one sells another the fruits of an olive tree — clearly the whole purpose of the transaction — but conditions the sale on the buyer not consuming ten percent of the fruits, has the sale accomplished what a sale of this type is supposed to accomplish? The *Chachamim* say no; Rabbi Eliezer says yes.

### Rav Yannai Verse

Returning now to the *mishna* as established — that the dispute concerns *chutz* — the *gemara* asks: how does Rabbi Eliezer justify his position? How can a *get* that explicitly leaves the woman married to one person constitute *kritut*?

Rav Yannai, citing an unnamed elder, points to the verse: *v'yatza mibeito v'halcha v'hayta l'ish acher* — she leaves his house and goes and becomes the wife of another man. Rav Yannai derives: *afilu lo hutrah ela l'ish echad harei zu megureshes* — even if the *get* makes her permissible to marry only one man, this constitutes a valid divorce. The Torah does not say the *get* must make her *muteret l'chol adam*; it says she goes and marries *ish acher* — another man. So long as some door opens, the *get* has done its work.

This is a striking *gemara*. It challenges the intuition that a *get* must fully sever. And it raises a further difficulty: what is the nature of the remaining marriage? Can a man be married to a woman vis-à-vis himself but not vis-à-vis the rest of the world? Is marriage a binary status, or can it be partial?

### Chachamim Response

The *Chachamim* respond to the verse: *l'chol ish v'ish* — *ish acher* does not mean some particular man she now has license to marry. It means any man she chooses. A *get* must open the door to the entire world; it must make her *muteret l'chol adam* in the full sense. If it cannot do that, it fails as *kritut*.

It is noted that the language of *sefer kritut* has not been invoked explicitly, but it is implicit throughout — it is clearly what drives the objection, and it is why Rabbi Eliezer must resort to a *pasuk* to defend his position.

### R. Yochanan Verse

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different Scriptural basis for Rabbi Eliezer's position. The verse in *Vayikra* regarding *kohanim* reads: *v'isha grusha me'isha lo yikachu* — a woman divorced from her husband, a *kohen* may not take. Why does the verse say "divorced from her husband" rather than simply "a divorced woman"? Rabbi Yochanan derives: *afilu lo nitgarsha ela me'isha nifsela min hakehuna* — even if a woman was only "divorced from her husband" — meaning she was given a *get* with the declaration *harei at megureshes mimeni v'i at muteret l'shum adam*, you are divorced from me but not permitted to anyone else — she is nonetheless *pasul l'kehunah*.

This is a remarkable *drasha*. Normally, *reiach haget* — the "scent of a *get*," the taint of divorce-like circumstances — is a rabbinic concept: certain types of *gittin* that are close to valid create a *derabanan* prohibition for *kohanim*. Rabbi Yochanan's *drasha* suggests that *reiach haget* can operate *mid'oraita*. Even a *get* that divorces her from her husband while permitting her to no one — which everyone agrees is not a valid *get* — creates a Torah-level disqualification from *kehunah*.

As *Tosafot* and Rashi explain, the *nafka mina* in such a case would be, for instance, whether the husband's brother could perform *yibum* if the husband subsequently died. It is *pashut* that he could, since the *get* was entirely ineffective. But the *chiddush* is the *kehunah* disqualification. And from this, the *gemara* builds the following argument: if even a *get* that excludes the entire world — which is obviously void — still creates a Torah-level *kehunah* problem, then a *get* that excludes only one person, while leaving her permissible to everyone else, might actually be a real *get*. If it were so hopelessly invalid that no concept of divorce were in play at all, the Torah would not have bothered to create a *kehunah* disqualification for it. The Torah's response implies there is something real here.

The *Chachamim* counter: *issur kehunah shani* — *kehunah* law is different. The *kehunah* disqualification reflects only *reiach haget* considerations and carries no implication about the *get*'s underlying validity. No inference about what constitutes a valid *get* can be drawn from it.

## Polyandry Scenarios

### Partial Kiddushin Questi…

Rabbi Abba now poses a question that pushes the sugya into still more remarkable territory: if Rabbi Eliezer allows a *get* that is only partially effective — that divorces the wife vis-à-vis everyone except Reuven — then what is the status of *kiddushin* given with a similar exclusion? If one can end up in a reality where a woman is "a little divorced," can one create the reverse reality at the outset — a "partial marriage"?

The question is asked *tiba'ei leRabbi Eliezer tiba'ei leRabanan* — both against the view of Rabbi Eliezer and against that of the *Rabanan*.

### Kiddushin-Gerushin Link

The *gemara* identifies the nexus between *kiddushin* and *gerushin* in the verse *v'yatza v'hayta* — the verse links the process of leaving one marriage to the process of entering another. If by *gerushin* the Torah tolerates a partial dissolution, then by *kiddushin* as well, one might argue, a partial acquisition is possible.

### Two Men Scenario

The *gemara* raises the possibility — now fully explicit — that two men could each perform *kiddushin* on the same woman, each excluding the other from his stipulation. Reuven says *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miShimon*, and Shimon says *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miReuven*. Neither man's *kiddushin* forecloses the other's. The result, within Rabbi Eliezer's framework, would be a woman simultaneously married to two men — a form of polyandry created not through violation but through structured exclusion.

### Abaye's Resolution

Abaye resolves the question: *bein leRabbi Eliezer bein leRabanan ba'inan v'yatza v'hayta* — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* agree that *kiddushin* is linked to *gerushin* through *v'yatza v'hayta*. The implication cuts in both directions: the same way Rabbi Eliezer tolerates a partial *get*, he would also allow partial *kiddushin*. And by the same token, for the *Chachamim*, if a partial *get* is invalid, a partial *kiddushin* is equally unavailing. The *kiddushin* and *gerushin* regimes are structurally symmetric.

The *chiddush* for Rabbi Eliezer's position is now fully stated: one may say to a woman *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miReuven*, and thereby create a partial marriage that coexists with Reuven's access to her.

### Two Brothers Case

The *gemara* now works through the strange case of two brothers. Reuven says *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miShimon*, and Shimon responds *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miReuven*. Each brother excludes the other from his *kiddushin*, allowing both relationships to coexist. In principle — within this framework — she would be married to both simultaneously. (It is worth noting that the Torah's prohibitions address a man married to two sisters, not a woman married to two brothers, so there is no Biblical *issur* on that dimension — though the questions of adultery, *onah*, and inheritance that would attend such an arrangement are substantial, and the *gemara* does not engage them here.)

### Yibum Problem

To test whether these dual *kiddushin* are actually legally effective, the *gemara* raises the *yibum* scenario. If both Reuven and Shimon die, is their third brother Levi obligated in *yibum* through both of them? This would constitute *zikas shenei yevamim* — the *zika* of two *yevamim* — which is already a category the *gemara* in *Yevamos* discusses in its rabbinic form. By way of background: if Reuven dies and Shimon performs *ma'amar* — a rabbinic-level act resembling *kiddushin* for *yibum* — but then dies before completing *yibum*, Levi's obligation runs through both brothers, creating a *zikas shenei yevamim* at the rabbinic level. The *gemara* here asks whether the same phenomenon can arise on a Torah level, through the dual *kiddushin* we have just described.

### Shimon Ineffective

The *gemara* answers that this does not produce *zikas shenei yevamim*, because Shimon's *kiddushin* was ineffective. When Reuven married her and said *chutz miShimon*, she became forbidden to the entire world except Shimon. When Shimon then came and said *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miReuven*, his act made no change to her legal status whatsoever. Before his *kiddushin*, she was forbidden to everyone in the world except Shimon. After his *kiddushin*, she remained forbidden to everyone in the world except Shimon. His act did not alter whom she was permitted to marry or with whom she was forbidden. A legal act that changes nothing in the world has no legal effect. *Kidushei d'Reuven ahannu, kidushei d'Shimon lo ahannu* — Reuven's *kiddushin* was effective; Shimon's was not.

The *gemara* thus uses the *yibum* question as a test case: not to show that she is married to two men, but to show that in the scenario as described, the second *kiddushin* simply does not take hold.

### Effective Marriage Case

There is, however, a scenario in which both *kiddushin* would be effective. Suppose Reuven said *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miShimon v'Frank* — you are married to me, except that Shimon and Frank remain accessible to you. She is now forbidden to everyone in the world except Shimon and Frank. Shimon now says *harei at mekudeshes li chutz miReuven* — but makes no exception for Frank. Shimon's *kiddushin* does something: it closes the door to Frank. Before Shimon's *kiddushin*, Frank was accessible; after it, Frank is excluded. Shimon's act changed her status in a meaningful way. In this case, she is genuinely married to both Reuven and Shimon simultaneously — each marriage having had a distinct legal effect.

The *gemara* also frames the *eshet shenei meitim* case — a woman who would truly be classified as the wife of two deceased men for purposes of *yibum* — as follows: Reuven marries her with *chutz miShimon*, and then Shimon comes and marries her *stam*, without any exclusion. Shimon's *stam kiddushin* has the effect of closing the door to Reuven — whom Reuven's own *kiddushin* had left accessible. *Kidushei d'Reuven ahannu lemisra achulei alma* — Reuven's *kiddushin* made her forbidden to everyone except Shimon. *Kidushei d'Shimon ahannu lemisra a'Reuven* — Shimon's *kiddushin* then made her forbidden to Reuven as well. The resulting state: she is bound to Reuven as a married woman but cannot have relations with him (Shimon's *kiddushin* made her *asura* to him), while she is also bound to Shimon but with Reuven still technically present as her husband. When both die, she is classified as *eshet shenei meitim*, the wife of two deceased men.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question in the name of Rabbeinu Yitzchak: if Shimon's *kiddushin* makes her *asura* to Reuven, why doesn't that act knock Reuven's *kiddushin* out altogether? Should not Shimon's act, which purports to override Reuven's rights, dissolve the first marriage? The *gemara* apparently holds that both marriages coexist: she is married to Reuven, and Shimon's *kiddushin* forbids her to Reuven — a configuration that is strange on its face, but that this *sugya* apparently accepts as coherent within Rabbi Eliezer's framework.

It is worth noting that this scenario has been cited in discussions of *hafka'at kiddushin* — retroactive annulment of *kiddushin* — in particular the *agunah* context. Whether the model could ever be implemented practically is a separate matter entirely.

### Simultaneous Marriage

Abaye raises a final question in this vein: if the two brothers' *kiddushin* scenarios are being entertained, what exactly is the woman's legal status when both men are alive and both *kiddushin* are effective? The *gemara* does not resolve who she can be with — it treats the *yibum* and *agunah* implications as the operative test cases, not the question of whom she may actually have relations with. The *gemara*'s concern throughout is the formal legal question of whose wife she is "on paper," not an adjudication of permitted relations. Whether any of this is practically relevant is left aside; the *gemara* is working through the implications of Rabbi Eliezer's position to their logical conclusion.

## Ambiguous Language

### Gap-Filling Scenario

With the *kiddushin* discussion concluded, the *gemara* turns to a series of cases involving a husband who partially fills the gap in an exclusionary *get*. The scenario: the husband said *harei at muteres l'chol adam chutz miReuven v'Shimon* — you are permitted to everyone except Reuven and Shimon. He has excluded both men. He then added — before delivering the *get* — the words *leReuven v'Shimon*. He handed her the *get* with both statements included. What do those closing words mean?

### Two Interpretations

The *gemara* poses the fundamental ambiguity: *mi amrinan mah d'asar shara?* — do we say that by saying *leReuven v'Shimon* he reversed his own exclusion, making her now permitted to Reuven and Shimon as well as everyone else, filling in the gap he had created? Or do we say *mah d'asar shara u'mah d'shara asar* — he completely inverted the earlier statement, so that now she is permitted only to Reuven and Shimon and forbidden to everyone else, having reversed the original formulation entirely?

### Single Name Case

The *gemara* then subdivides the question. If, under the first reading, saying *leReuven v'Shimon* fills the gap and permits her to everyone: what if he said only *leReuven*? Does *leReuven* mean he was speaking elliptically — naming Reuven because Reuven came first in his original list, but intending to restore permission to both Reuven and Shimon? *Lehu hadin leShimon* — and the same naturally applies to Shimon. Or does *leReuven* mean *leReuven davka* — exclusively Reuven, leaving Shimon still excluded?

### Shimon Only Case

If he said only *leShimon* — the reverse: does *leShimon* mean he is restoring permission to both men, choosing Shimon's name because it was the last word of his original exclusion (*mishum desalik mineih* — it was the word most recently in his mouth), and he intends by implication to fill in all the gaps? Or does *leShimon* mean only Shimon is now permitted, while Reuven remains excluded?

### Even For Case

Finally, Rav Ashi raises an additional layer: suppose the husband said *af leShimon* — "even Shimon." Does *af* here refer backward to Reuven — meaning you are permitted to Reuven and *even* Shimon, so both are now permitted? Or does *af* refer forward to the rest of the world — meaning you are permitted to everyone in the world and even to Shimon, but Reuven remains excluded?

### Rav Ashi Question

*Teiku* — all of these questions are left unresolved. The *gemara* does not provide answers to any of the cases Rav Ashi raises. It is worth noting that these questions can be read according to either Rabbi Eliezer or the *Chachamim*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the operative question is whether she remains *asura* to one of the excluded men even after the follow-up statement — which affects whether she can actually marry those individuals. According to the *Chachamim*, the follow-up statement's meaning is critical to whether the *get* is valid at all: if it successfully fills all the gaps and makes her *muteret l'chol adam*, the *get* becomes *kasher*; if any exclusion remains, it is *pasul*.

The *daf* closes on these open questions, but the deeper issue running through the entire *perek* remains: what is the nature of marriage and divorce? Is either a binary status, or can one be partially married and partially divorced? Can a legal instrument that is not one hundred percent effective still be a valid *get* or a valid *kiddushin*? These are the questions Rabbi Eliezer forces us to confront — and they will continue into the next *daf*.
