# Kiddushin 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabba vs. Rav Yosef

### Two Versions

The *gemara* on this daf centers on a dispute between Rabba and Rav Yosef concerning a man who attempted *kiddushin* by giving a woman silk (*shiraim*), and whether the silk required a formal appraisal — *tzrichi shuma* — before the *kiddushin* could be valid. Rabba held that it did not; Rav Yosef held that it did. Crucially, the *gemara* presents two distinct versions of this debate, and they differ not only in outcome but in the underlying conceptual question each version raises.

In the first version, the man claimed a specific value for the silk — say, it was worth fifty — and it turned out to be exactly that amount, but no expert had verified this before the exchange. Rabba held that since the silk was in fact worth what he said, she is married. Rav Yosef countered that silk is not something a layperson can readily assess, and therefore even if it happened to be worth what he claimed, the woman could not have been genuinely confident (*samcha da'atah*) of its value at the time of acceptance. That absence of confident reliance is a deficiency in the *da'as* required for *kiddushin* to take effect.

The second version is conceptually richer. Here, the man makes no value claim at all — he simply says he is marrying her with whatever the silk is worth, which is certainly at least a *perutah*, but no specific amount is asserted. One can readily understand why Rabba would not require an assessment in this case, since no representation about value was made. Yet Rav Yosef holds that even here, an assessment is required. Understanding why brings us to the heart of the debate.

### Rav Yosef on Kayatz

Rav Yosef's position rests on a fundamental principle about how *shaveh kessef* — things of monetary value — function in halachic transactions. The principle that *shaveh kessef harei hu k'kessef*, that items of value may serve as money, is well established and applies across many contexts. We know a woman may be betrothed with *kessef* either from the verse concerning the sale of a daughter as an *amah ivriyah*, or from the purchase of Ephron's field by Avraham, where the text specifies payment that functioned as currency.

But Rav Yosef insists that the equation of *shaveh kessef* with *kessef* is not a blanket substitution — it means *shaveh kessef* must *function like* money. And the defining feature of money is that it has a fixed, determined value. The principle he articulates at the opening of daf 8a is: *ma kessef d'kaitz, af shaveh kessef nami d'kaitz* — just as money has a fixed value, so too *shaveh kessef* must have a fixed value. Silk whose value has not been established — which might be worth anywhere from ten to a hundred — cannot function as money, because money by definition is determinate. Therefore, without prior assessment that fixes the value, the silk cannot serve as *kessef kiddushin*.

### Eved Ivri Source

Rav Yosef then offers a *talmudic* source for this position from a *braita* dealing with the redemption of a *eved ivri*. The verse states *mikessef miknaso* — that a Hebrew slave is purchased and redeemed by means of money — and the *braita* derives from this that redemption works only with *kessef* and not with *tevuah* (grain) or *keilim* (vessels).

The *gemara* then interrogates this *braita*: in what circumstances does it exclude grain and vessels? If we say they simply cannot effect the transaction at all, that seems to contradict the well-established principle that *shaveh kessef* functions as *kessef*, learned from the word *geulato* — "his redemption" — which is general and inclusive. If on the other hand we say they are excluded only when they lack the value of a *perutah*, then the exclusion has nothing to do with being grain or vessels specifically — the same would apply to money itself. So why mention *tevuah* and *keilim* at all?

The *gemara* concludes: we must be dealing with grain and vessels that *do* have the value of a *perutah*, and yet they still fail — *kivan delo kaitz lehu* — because their values are indeterminate. This is Rav Yosef's proof: the *braita* is teaching that even objects of value fail when their value is not fixed. The principle of *kaitz* is therefore derived from the *eved ivri* context and applied to *kiddushin* as well.

### Rabba's Response

Rabba responds with a fundamentally different reading of the *braita*. In his view, the exclusion of *tevuah* and *keilim* has nothing to do with indeterminacy of value. Rather, the *braita* is teaching that the transaction must be structured *betorat kessef* — as an act of monetary payment — and not *betorat chaliphin*, not as an act of symbolic exchange. *Chaliphin*, in the relevant sense here, refers to the procedure of kinyan sudra, the handkerchief-type acquisition: one takes an object of significance — not for its monetary value but as a symbolic act of finalization — and that effects the transaction. The *braita*, says Rabba, is simply excluding framing the acquisition of an *eved ivri* as an act of *chaliphin* rather than as a monetary purchase.

The *gemara* pushes back: this reading works for *keilim*, which are paradigmatically used for *chaliphin*, but what about *tevuah* — grain? Rav Nachman rules that food items (*peirot*) cannot function as *chaliphin* at all. So the *braita's* exclusion of *tevuah* cannot be read as excluding *chaliphin* performed with grain, since that isn't a recognized form of *chaliphin* in the first place.

Cornered, Rabba retreats to the position that indeed the case is where *tevuah* and *keilim* lack the value of a *perutah*. But then, as the *gemara* objects, why specify those categories? Rabba answers with a *lo mibaya* construction: it goes without saying that actual *kessef* less than a *perutah* does not effect a transaction. The *chiddush* of the *braita* is that even *tevuah* and *keilim* — which one might think could function differently, since their value is direct and experiential (one eats the grain, one uses the vessel) — even those fail when they fall below a *perutah* in monetary value. *Kamashma lan* that in the end, value must be monetary value, not merely functional value.

This illuminates the deeper structure of the disagreement. For Rav Yosef, *shaveh kessef* functions as *kessef* precisely because it is quasi-money — it stands in for money, and therefore it must exhibit money's defining characteristic of determinacy. For Rabba, *shaveh kessef* functions not as a substitute for money but as an object of value in its own right. The underlying logic of the transaction is that something of value changes hands, not that something money-like changes hands. That is why the requirement of *kaitz* — a fixed, determined value — does not apply.

### Pidyon Ha-Ben

Rav Yosef then brings a second source from a *braita* concerning *pidyon ha-ben*. The *braita* states: if a man says to a *kohen*, "I am giving you this calf for my son's *pidyon*," or "this *tallis* for my son's *pidyon*" — without specifying a value — *lo amar klum*, it accomplishes nothing. But if he says, "I am giving you this calf valued at five *sela'im* for my son's *pidyon*," or "this *tallis* valued at five *sela'im*," then *bno paduy* — the son is redeemed.

The *gemara* asks: in what case are we dealing? If the object is actually not worth five *sela'im* in either scenario, then in the second case, merely saying the value does not make it so — *kol k'minei*, he has no power to magically assign worth beyond the item's true value. So it must be that in both cases the item actually is worth five *sela'im*. And yet when he fails to state a value, it does not work. *Ve-keivan de-lo kaitz* — since no fixed value was declared, the *pidyon* is invalid. This, says Rav Yosef, confirms the *kaitz* requirement.

Rabba responds with a different reading: the two cases differ not in what the item is actually worth, but in whether the *kohen* is willing to accept it at that valuation. *Lo le-olam de-lo shavi* — in fact, the item is not intrinsically worth five *sela'im*. *Ve-kheivan de-kibel lei kohen iluyei* — but the *kohen* was willing to evaluate it at five *sela'im* for himself. This is Rabba's *chiddush*: the question is not about standardized market value or official assessment. It is about whether this recipient — the *kohen* — subjectively values it at the required amount, and whether that subjective valuation is credible.

This is illustrated by the case of Rav Kahana, who — at the time of performing a *pidyon ha-ben* — took a *sudra* (handkerchief) and said that to him, personally, it was worth five *sela'im*. Tosafot notes, interestingly, that Rav Kahana may not himself have been a *kohen* but was perhaps married to the daughter of a *kohen*, implying that a *kohen's* daughter may also receive the *pidyon* payment.

Mar bar Rav Ashi adds a critical qualification: this principle — that a person may declare a subjective valuation — applies only to someone like Rav Kahana, who was a *gavra raba*, a man of stature, for whom it was genuinely undignified to go about without a head covering. One can understand how he would truly be willing to pay five *sela'im* for a *sudra* under those circumstances. But if a normal person, with no particular pressing need, simply asserts that a trivial object is worth five *sela'im*, the law applies the principle of *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — his claimed valuation is simply discounted, because no reasonable person under those circumstances would pay that amount. A further illustration is the case of Mar bar Rav Ashi himself, who paid thirteen for a *sudra* worth ten, because it was of a quality appropriate to his standing and genuinely difficult to find.

The contrast between Rabba and Rav Yosef thus runs deeper than the question of formal assessment. Rav Yosef, who requires *shaveh kessef* to function as money, demands that the value be *kaitz* — fixed — in a way that reflects the objective determinacy of currency. Rabba, who holds that *shaveh kessef* functions as an object of value on its own terms, is comfortable with subjective valuation, provided it is a genuinely credible one. For Rabba, it need not fit the mold of money; it need only represent authentic value.

### Tosafot on Shiraim

The *gemara* concludes with the ruling: *vehilchesa shiraim lo tzrichi shuma* — silk does not require appraisal, and we rule like Rabba. Tosafot raises the well-known question: given that we generally rule like Rabba over Rav Yosef, why does the *gemara* need to state the ruling explicitly? This question also touches on which version of the debate the ruling addresses — the first version, concerning *smichat da'at* when a value is claimed, or the second version, concerning whether any *kaitz* is required at all.

Rabbeinu Tam offers a subtle and important distinction. The ruling that *shiraim* do not require *shuma* is specifically calibrated to the nature of silk: its value is roughly knowable — *shumasan yadu'a ketzat* — and the variance in how people assess it is not so wide as to undermine the woman's confidence. Silk is not the kind of item about which one can be dramatically mistaken.

But other items, such as precious stones and pearls, are a different matter entirely. With diamonds, the variance in valuation can be enormous — one must know not only whether the stone is genuine but also its cut, clarity, color, and carat weight, factors that can shift the price by orders of magnitude. In that case, *tzarich shuma mishum de-lo samcha da'atah* — an appraisal is required because the woman may be relying on a grossly inflated estimate of the stone's worth, and her *smichat da'at* is therefore compromised. Rabbeinu Tam's point thus operates on the terrain of the first version of the debate, focusing on the woman's subjective confidence rather than the abstract requirement of *kaitz*.

This distinction has direct practical implications. Tosafot states: *lefikach nahagu olam lekadesh betaba'at she-ein bah even* — and this is why the universal practice developed to perform *kiddushin* with a plain ring that contains no precious stone. A plain gold ring is valued by weight, and gold's weight-based valuation admits of no significant variance. The woman may not know the precise sum, but there is no concern about dramatic misjudgment, and therefore *smichat da'at* is not at issue and no assessment is required.

This also explains the practice at the *chuppah* whereby the rabbi confirms with the witnesses that the ring is worth at least a *perutah*, and asks the *chassan* accordingly. This is in part a way of framing the *kiddushin* so that the ring is not represented as worth any specific substantial amount — thereby removing the concern about *smichat da'at* entirely. As for common variations in ring shape or design — a square rather than a round band, an artistic cut — these do not generate meaningful indeterminacy in value, since anyone can verify the price through ordinary channels. A ring made by Picasso would present an entirely different problem.

## Maneh & Dinar

### Maneh Stam

Rabbi Elazar rules: if a man says to a woman, "Marry me with a *maneh*" — a hundred — and then hands her a single *dinar*, she is nonetheless betrothed, and he must eventually complete the payment. The reasoning, as the *gemara* explains, is that when he said *maneh* and immediately handed her a *dinar*, the context clarifies his intent: he was not proposing that a hundred coins constitute the *kessef kiddushin*. Rather, he was framing the full hundred as a *tenai* — a condition — while the *dinar* in her hand is the actual *kessef* of the *kiddushin*. The *gemara* invokes Rav Huna's ruling in the name of Rav: *kol ha-omer al menat, k'omer me-achshav dami* — one who says "on the condition" is regarded as having said "from now," meaning the *kiddushin* takes effect immediately with the *dinar*, subject to the condition of eventual full payment.

### Maneh Zu

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a *braita*: if a man says "Marry me with a *maneh*" and begins counting out the coins one by one, and either party wishes to retract — even when only the last coin remains — the right to retract is in hand, and the *kiddushin* has not yet taken effect. This implies that when a man says *maneh*, the full hundred is the *kessef kiddushin*, not merely a *tenai*, and the transaction is incomplete until the entire sum is delivered.

The *gemara* resolves this by proposing that the *braita* deals with the case of *maneh zu* — "this hundred," where the man pointed to a specific stack of coins. The *seifa* of the *braita* reinforces this: if he said *hitkadshi li be-maneh zu* — "marry me with this particular hundred" — and it turned out that one coin was missing, or one was copper (*dinar shel nechoshet*) rather than silver, the *kiddushin* is invalid. If, however, one coin was merely *dinar ra* — legitimate currency but in poor condition, one that storekeepers might decline but which would still be accepted at a bank — the *kiddushin* is valid, though he must replace the damaged coin.

### Clarification by Action

The *gemara* ultimately confirms that both the *reisha* and the *seifa* of the *braita* concern *maneh zu* — a specific, identified sum. The *seifa* clarifies and extends the *reisha* rather than introducing a new case. This reading preserves Rabbi Elazar's ruling intact: when a man says *maneh stam* — an unspecified hundred — and immediately hands over a smaller amount, the action itself clarifies that the hundred is a *tenai* and the smaller sum is the *kessef kiddushin*.

The *gemara* does briefly consider the possibility that the *reisha* was *maneh stam* and the *seifa* was introduced to clarify it, but settles on the simpler reading: the *seifa* is not merely clarificatory — it introduces a genuine *chiddush*. Even within the framework of *maneh stam*, there is a meaningful difference between the *reisha* and *seifa*. In the *reisha* (read as *maneh stam* where the full hundred is indeed the *kessef kiddushin*), if one coin is defective, it can be replaced with another — since he committed to *a* hundred, not *this* hundred. But in the *seifa*, where he identified a specific stack as the *kessef kiddushin*, the entire identified amount must be as represented; replacing a defective coin after the fact does not remedy the failed *kiddushin*.

### Dinar Ra

The *gemara's* distinction between *dinar ra* and *dinar shel nechoshet* raises a further question. A *dinar shel nechoshet* — a counterfeit or base-metal coin representing itself as silver — is simply not what was promised, and the *kiddushin* fails. But a *dinar ra* is genuine currency, even if worn or damaged; it has not lost its identity as legal tender. Rav Papa clarifies: the case of *dinar ra* that still validates the *kiddushin* is one where the coin *nafik al yedei hadach* — it circulates, albeit with some difficulty. It is not refused categorically; it may require some insistence, but it is still recognized. The woman received what was promised in substance, even if not in ideal condition, and therefore the *kiddushin* holds — with the obligation to exchange the coin for a better one.

## Meshkon Issues

### Meshkon Not Kessef

Rav Nachman rules: if a man says to a woman "Marry me with a *maneh*" and places his watch before her as *mashkon* — collateral — she is not married. The principle he states is *maneh ein kan, mashkon ein kan*: the hundred is not here, and the *mashkon* is not here as *kessef kiddushin*. The *mashkon* was not tendered as the *kessef* of the transaction but as a pledge of future payment, much as the collateral in the story of Yehuda and Tamar functioned as an assurance of a promised gift. An object given as collateral, however valuable, is not the *kessef kiddushin* — and since the *maneh* itself was never given, the *kiddushin* did not take effect.

### Kedisha B'Meshkon

Rava challenges Rav Nachman from a *braita* that states: *kiddeshah be-mashkon mekudeshet* — if a man betroths a woman with *mashkon*, she is betrothed. The *gemara* immediately distinguishes: that *braita* refers to *mashkon de-acherim* — collateral belonging to someone else — and its validity derives from the principle of Rav Yitzchak.

### Creditor Owns

Rav Yitzchak teaches: *minayin le-va'al chov she-koneh mashkon* — from where do we know that a creditor acquires ownership of collateral he holds? From the verse *ul'cha tihiyeh tzedakah* — "and it shall be righteousness unto you" — said in the context of returning a garment taken as *mashkon* each day or night. If the creditor did not own the collateral, asks Rav Yitzchak, in what sense is returning it an act of righteousness? One can always perform an act of kindness in returning something one lawfully holds without owning it. The inference of *tzedakah* implies that he is giving back something *his own* — which means the creditor holds genuine ownership in the *mashkon*.

Tosafot raises a technical objection: even without ownership, returning the garment so the debtor can sleep in it is an act of righteousness on its face. Tosafot then introduces an alternative explanation for the derivation, noting that the verse continues *ve-shachav be-salmato u-verachecha* — "he will sleep in his garment and bless you." If the creditor does not own the *mashkon*, then the blessing the poor man gives in return for use of his own garment would constitute *ribit devarim* — verbal usury — a form of benefit accruing to the lender from the loan. If, however, the creditor owns the *mashkon* and is genuinely returning something of his own, the blessing is a response to a gift, not to the loan, and there is no *ribit*. Tosafot adds a further interpretation: *tzedakah* technically requires that one give from one's own — *ha-noten mishelo* — and the verse's use of that term implies ownership.

The practical upshot of Rav Yitzchak's principle in the present context is that when Reuven holds Shimon's watch as collateral for a loan, Reuven has genuine ownership — or at least a form of ownership — in that watch. If Reuven then gives Shimon's watch to a woman as *kessef kiddushin*, he is giving her something he owns. Tosafot notes a potential distinction between collateral taken at the moment of the loan versus collateral taken subsequently, but in either case, from the principle in one situation, ownership can be inferred in the other as well.

The question of what the woman actually receives in such a transaction is worth pausing over. She now holds something Reuven owned. But practically speaking, can she use the watch? She cannot — it remains Shimon's watch in most practical senses. Does Reuven transfer to her the right to collect the debt if unpaid? That is unclear. The *gemara* does not resolve precisely what the nature of her acquisition is; perhaps it is sufficient that something of value, which he owned, was transferred to her possession, even without her accruing direct use-benefit from it.

### Priti Without Payment

The *gemara* then recounts the case of the sons of Rav Huna bar Avin, who were purchasing a maidservant with *priti* — cash — but did not have the money on hand at the moment of the transaction. They placed a piece of silver (*naska*) before the seller as a placeholder. Later the maidservant's value rose, and the seller sought to undo the sale, arguing it had never been finalized. The case came before Rav Ami, who ruled: *priti ein kan, naska ein kan* — the money is not here, and the silver piece is not here as purchase money. Parallel to *kiddushin*, what was placed before the seller was collateral, not the purchase price. Since the purchase money was never actually paid, the sale was not final.

Tosafot notes an interesting wrinkle: unlike a case of debt, where *mashkon* makes halachic sense as security for a sum already owed, here the sale had not yet been concluded and no debt yet existed. What legal function did the *naska* serve? Tosafot explores whether the *mashkon* can operate in a pre-debt context — and whether the seller could force the buyers to complete the purchase or forfeit the collateral — but leaves the question partially open. The *gemara's* ruling stands regardless: the *naska* was not the *kessef* of the transaction and therefore the acquisition was incomplete.

## Acceptance Issues

### Destruction

A *braita* teaches: if a man says "Marry me with this hundred" and hands her the money, and she takes it and throws it into the ocean or into a fire or otherwise destroys it — *einah mekudeshet*, she is not married. The *gemara* initially considers whether simply throwing it back in front of him would suffice as a rejection — she is clearly saying *lo be'ina*, I do not want it — and therefore the *braita's* need to specify destruction seems odd.

The *gemara* resolves this with a *lo mibaya* formulation: it goes without saying that throwing it back in front of him rejects the *kiddushin*. The *chiddush* of the *braita* concerns the case of destruction, where one might have reasoned differently. When she destroys the money, she is destroying what could be *his* money — since if she rejected the *kiddushin*, it was never hers. That means she will owe him reimbursement. Faced with that liability, we might have supposed that she in fact accepted the *kiddushin* — she took it as her own and then destroyed her own money as a test of his temperament (*savra ebdakia le-hai gavra i ritcha hu o lo*). *Kamashma lan* — the *braita* teaches that despite this reasoning, we read her act as rejection, and she is not betrothed.

### Sheliach Cases

A subsequent *braita* presents the case of a man who offers a woman *kiddushin* money and she responds: "Give it to my father" or "give it to your father." In that case — *einah mekudeshet* — she is not married. The *gemara* explains this as mocking rejection: she is saying, in effect, you want to waste your money, go give it to someone else. Even though her father would presumably hold the money for her, or might return it to her, we do not read her response as acceptance of the *kiddushin*.

However, if she says *al menat she-yekablum li* — on the condition that they accept it for me — she *is* betrothed, because in that formulation she is appointing them as her *shaliach* (agent) and the acceptance on her behalf effects the *kiddushin*.

The *braita* specifies both "give it to my father" and "give it to your father" to teach distinct lessons. The case of "my father" reveals the strength of the *reisha*: even though her father is a natural recipient who might hold the money for her, we still read the unqualified directive as rejection. The case of "your father" reveals the strength of the *seifa*: even directing the money to *his* father, if framed with the explicit condition that he accept it on her behalf, constitutes valid agency and the *kiddushin* takes effect.

### Ploni Distinction

The *braita* then presents a parallel case with *ploni* — a random third party. If she says "give it to *ploni*" without qualification, she is not married. If she adds *al menat she-yekablum li*, she is. The *gemara* explains why both cases are necessary, since each teaches something the other does not.

Had the *braita* only stated the case of father: we would have understood that the *al menat* formula works with family members — *samcha da'atah aliyei*, she trusts them to act on her behalf — but might have doubted whether it works with a stranger, since there is less reason to trust that *ploni* will faithfully execute her agency. And had the *braita* only stated the case of *ploni*: we would have understood that the unqualified directive to a stranger is a rejection — *de-lo mekarva da'atah legabei*, she has no close relationship with him — but might have misread a directive to her own father as an acceptance, reasoning that she feels close enough to her father to be directing a gift through him as an expression of her agreeing to the *kiddushin*. The *braita* therefore teaches both: the unqualified directive to father is still rejection; the qualified directive to *ploni* is still valid agency.

Rashi makes explicit what underlies this: *einah mekudeshet ve-einah doma le-ten maneh le-ploni ve-ikadesh lach* — this is unlike the case where *she* initiates by saying "give a hundred to so-and-so and I will be betrothed to you," because in that earlier case *hi ka'amra lei me-ikara* — she was the initiator. Here, *meshatei hi bo* — she is mocking him. The principle of *areiv* from the earlier *gemara* — where *kiddushin* can be effected by a payment directed to a third party — operates on the condition that the structure of the transaction was set up at her initiative, not his.

### Sela on Rock

Another *braita*: the man says "Marry me with this hundred" and she responds, "Put it on that rock." If the rock does not belong to her — *einah mekudeshet*. If the rock *does* belong to her — *mekudeshet*. Rashi explains that when she owns the rock, she acquires the money through *kinyan chatzer* — the halachic principle that one's property effects acquisition on one's behalf.

Rav Bibi raises the question: what if the rock belongs to *both* of them jointly? *Teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

Tosafot takes issue with Rashi's explanation. If the mechanism were simply *kinyan chatzer*, the question of a jointly-owned rock would be simple: partners cannot effect *kinyan chatzer* for one another in jointly-held property, so the answer should be obvious. Tosafot therefore proposes a different framework: the determinative factor is *smichat da'at* — whether she has confident reliance that she will be able to retrieve the money. When the rock is hers, she has that confidence. When the rock is not hers, she does not. And when the rock is jointly owned, it is genuinely unclear whether her *smichat da'at* is sufficient — hence the unresolved *teiku*. This is admittedly a striking approach, since the woman never actually takes possession of the money. The *gemara* throughout this section seems less concerned with perfecting a formal act of *kinyan* than with identifying what constitutes sufficient acceptance in the context of *kiddushin* — a question that remains to be clarified through the accumulation of these cases.

### Dog Case

A final *braita*: the man says "Marry me with this loaf of bread" and she responds "Give it to the dog." If the dog is not hers — *einah mekudeshet*. If the dog belongs to her — *mekudeshet*. The *gemara* leaves open whether the mechanism here is that she acquires the loaf through her dog (an extension of *kinyan chatzer*), or whether the *kessef kiddushin* is not the loaf itself but the benefit she receives from having her dog fed at his expense. This ambiguity is meaningful: across all these cases, the *gemara* does not fully resolve what precisely constitutes the act of *kiddushin* — formal acquisition, receipt of benefit, or some combination. As with *Gittin*, the question of whether what is required is a genuine act of giving and receiving, or merely placing something in a domain associated with the recipient, remains to be worked out through the full range of cases.
