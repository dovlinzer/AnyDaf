# Kiddushin 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabba vs. Rav Yosef

### Two Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בְּכֹל דְּהוּ נָמֵי פְּלִיגִי. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכֶסֶף, מָה כֶּסֶף דְּקִיץ – אַף שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף נָמֵי דְּקִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **even** in a case where he says to her: Be betrothed to me with these silk garments, **whatever** they are worth, the *amora'im* **disagree** with regard to the *halakha*. **Rav Yosef said:** An item **worth money is like money** in every way. **Just as money is set,** i.e., it has a clearly defined value, **so too,** an item **worth money** must be **set,** i.e., it must have a clearly defined value.

The *gemara* on this daf centers on a dispute between Rabba and Rav Yosef concerning a man who attempted *kiddushin* by giving a woman silk (*shiraim*), and whether the silk required a formal appraisal — *tzrichi shuma* — before the *kiddushin* could be valid. Rabba held that it did not; Rav Yosef held that it did. Crucially, the *gemara* presents two distinct versions of this debate, and they differ not only in outcome but in the underlying conceptual question each version raises.

In the first version, the man claimed a specific value for the silk — say, it was worth fifty — and it turned out to be exactly that amount, but no expert had verified this before the exchange. Rabba held that since the silk was in fact worth what he said, she is married. Rav Yosef countered that silk is not something a layperson can readily assess, and therefore even if it happened to be worth what he claimed, the woman could not have been genuinely confident (*samcha da'atah*) of its value at the time of acceptance. That absence of confident reliance is a deficiency in the *da'as* required for *kiddushin* to take effect.

The second version is conceptually richer. Here, the man makes no value claim at all — he simply says he is marrying her with whatever the silk is worth, which is certainly at least a *perutah*, but no specific amount is asserted. One can readily understand why Rabba would not require an assessment in this case, since no representation about value was made. Yet Rav Yosef holds that even here, an assessment is required. Understanding why brings us to the heart of the debate.

### Rav Yosef on Kayatz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״ – בְּכֶסֶף הוּא נִקְנֶה, וְאֵינוֹ נִקְנֶה בִּתְבוּאָה וְכֵלִים. הַאי תְּבוּאָה וְכֵלִים הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא מַקְנוּ בְּהוּ כְּלָל, ״יָשִׁיב גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – לְרַבּוֹת שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף כְּכֶסֶף; וְאִי דְּלֵית בְּהוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, מַאי אִירְיָא תְּבוּאָה וְכֵלִים, אֲפִילּוּ כֶּסֶף נָמֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאִית בְּהוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא קַיְיצִי לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: From where do I say** this opinion? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to redeeming a Hebrew slave: "He shall give back the price of his redemption **out of the money that he was bought for"** (Leviticus 25:51), which indicates: **He is acquired** specifically **through money and he is not acquired through grain or vessels. What are the circumstances** of **these grains and vessels? If we say that** a Hebrew slave **cannot be acquired through them at all,** as money alone may be used, **the Merciful One states: "He shall give back the price of his redemption,"** which serves **to include** all modes of repayment as valid equivalents of money, i.e., an item **worth money** is the same **as money.** There is no requirement to use money in particular; it is also possible to use items with monetary value. **And if** you say **that they do not have the value of one *peruta*, why** mention **specifically grain and vessels? Even money** that is not worth one *peruta* cannot be used for acquiring a slave. **Rather, is it not** speaking here about a case **where** the grain and vessels **do have the value of one *peruta*, but since they lack a set** value, **no,** a Hebrew slave cannot be redeemed with them?

Rav Yosef's position rests on a fundamental principle about how *shaveh kessef* — things of monetary value — function in halachic transactions. The principle that *shaveh kessef harei hu k'kessef*, that items of value may serve as money, is well established and applies across many contexts. We know a woman may be betrothed with *kessef* either from the verse concerning the sale of a daughter as an *amah ivriyah*, or from the purchase of Ephron's field by Avraham, where the text specifies payment that functioned as currency.

But Rav Yosef insists that the equation of *shaveh kessef* with *kessef* is not a blanket substitution — it means *shaveh kessef* must *function like* money. And the defining feature of money is that it has a fixed, determined value. The principle he articulates at the opening of daf 8a is: *ma kessef d'kaitz, af shaveh kessef nami d'kaitz* — just as money has a fixed value, so too *shaveh kessef* must have a fixed value. Silk whose value has not been established — which might be worth anywhere from ten to a hundred — cannot function as money, because money by definition is determinate. Therefore, without prior assessment that fixes the value, the silk cannot serve as *kessef kiddushin*.

### Eved Ivri Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: בְּתוֹרַת כֶּסֶף הוּא נִקְנֶה, וְאֵין נִקְנֶה בְּתוֹרַת תְּבוּאָה וְכֵלִים, וּמַאי נִינְהוּ – חֲלִיפִין. וּלְרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר: פֵּירוֹת לָא עָבְדִי חֲלִיפִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם דְּלֵית בְּהוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ ״מַאי אִירְיָא תְּבוּאָה, וְכֵלִים אֲפִילּוּ כֶּסֶף נָמֵי!״, לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא כֶּסֶף, דְּאִי אִית בֵּיהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא, אֲבָל תְּבוּאָה וְכֵלִים, אֵימָא מִדִּמְקָרְבָא הֲנָאָתַיְיהוּ, גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabba, who maintains that appraisal is not necessary, would respond: The *tanna* is referring to an item worth one *peruta*, but he is not teaching that one cannot redeem a slave with items whose value is not set. Rather, **this is what he is saying:** A Hebrew slave **is acquired through the mode of money, and he is not acquired through the mode of grain or vessels. And what is this** mode particular to the acquisition of grain and vessels? This is referring to symbolic **exchange.** A slave cannot be acquired through the mode of acquisition of symbolic exchange. **And** this explanation is valid only according to those who say that grain can be acquired through the mode of exchange; **but according to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who said** that **produce cannot effect** symbolic **exchange,** as this mode of acquisition applies only to vessels, **what can be said?** Why does the *tanna* mention grain if grain cannot be used in symbolic exchange? **Rather,** the Gemara rejects this explanation in favor of the following: **Actually,** this is referring to a case where the grain and utensils **do not have the value of one *peruta*. And as for that which you said: Why** mention **specifically grain and vessels; even money** that is not worth one *peruta* cannot acquire **either,** one could say that the *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** The Gemara elaborates: **It is not necessary** to state with regard to **money that if it has the value of one *peruta*, yes,** one effects acquisition with it, and **if not,** then **no,** one cannot effect acquisition with it. **But** with regard to **grain and vessels,** one might **say that** as **their benefit is** readily **available,** i.e., one can enjoy them immediately in their current state, perhaps the slave **decides and transfers** ownership of **himself** to the master by even less than the worth of one *peruta*. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that an article less than the value of one *peruta* cannot effect acquisition notwithstanding the above reasoning.

Rav Yosef then offers a *talmudic* source for this position from a *braita* dealing with the redemption of a *eved ivri*. The verse states *mikessef miknaso* — that a Hebrew slave is purchased and redeemed by means of money — and the *braita* derives from this that redemption works only with *kessef* and not with *tevuah* (grain) or *keilim* (vessels).

The *gemara* then interrogates this *braita*: in what circumstances does it exclude grain and vessels? If we say they simply cannot effect the transaction at all, that seems to contradict the well-established principle that *shaveh kessef* functions as *kessef*, learned from the word *geulato* — "his redemption" — which is general and inclusive. If on the other hand we say they are excluded only when they lack the value of a *perutah*, then the exclusion has nothing to do with being grain or vessels specifically — the same would apply to money itself. So why mention *tevuah* and *keilim* at all?

The *gemara* concludes: we must be dealing with grain and vessels that *do* have the value of a *perutah*, and yet they still fail — *kivan delo kaitz lehu* — because their values are indeterminate. This is Rav Yosef's proof: the *braita* is teaching that even objects of value fail when their value is not fixed. The principle of *kaitz* is therefore derived from the *eved ivri* context and applied to *kiddushin* as well.

### Rabba's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״עֵגֶל זֶה לְפִדְיוֹן בְּנִי״, ״טַלִּית זֹה לְפִדְיוֹן בְּנִי״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״עֵגֶל זֶה בְּחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים לְפִדְיוֹן בְּנִי״, ״טַלִּית זוֹ בְּחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים לְפִדְיוֹן בְּנִי״ – בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי. הַאי פִּדְיוֹן, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא שָׁוֵי, כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּשָׁוֵי, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא קַיְיצִי – לָא? לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא שָׁוֵי, וּכְגוֹן דְּקַבֵּיל כֹּהֵן עִילָּוֵיהּ. כִּי הָא דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא שָׁקֵיל סוּדָרָא מִבֵּי פִּדְיוֹן הַבֵּן, אָמַר (לֵיהּ): לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: From where do I say** that betrothal can be effected only with an item with a clearly defined value? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bekhorot* 6:4), that if one says to a priest: **This calf** should be **for the redemption of my** firstborn **son,** or: **This cloak** should be **for the redemption of my** firstborn **son,** then **he has said nothing.** But if he said: **This calf** worth the value **of five *sela*** should be **for the redemption of my** firstborn **son,** or: **This cloak** worth the value **of five *sela*** should be **for the redemption of my** firstborn **son,** then **his son is redeemed. What are the circumstances** with regard to **this redemption** with a calf or a cloak that has no clearly defined value? **If we say that they are not worth** five *sela*, **is it in his** power to give a priest less than the established amount? Why would it even be considered that perhaps the son is redeemed? **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where **even though they are worth** this amount, one **may not** redeem with them in the case of the first clause **since their value is not set?** This shows that there is a difference between an item that has a set value and one that does not. **No; actually,** this is referring to a case **where** the calf or cloak **is not worth** five *sela*, **and** the latter clause is referring to a case **where the priest accepted upon himself** to value the items as though they were worth this amount, which is why the son is redeemed. **This is like this** incident in **which Rav Kahana,** who was a priest, **took a cloth [*sudara*] from the house** of a man obligated to perform **the redemption of** his firstborn **son.** Rav Kahana **said to** the man: **For me, I view** this cloth as though it were worth **five *sela*.**

Rabba responds with a fundamentally different reading of the *braita*. In his view, the exclusion of *tevuah* and *keilim* has nothing to do with indeterminacy of value. Rather, the *braita* is teaching that the transaction must be structured *betorat kessef* — as an act of monetary payment — and not *betorat chaliphin*, not as an act of symbolic exchange. *Chaliphin*, in the relevant sense here, refers to the procedure of kinyan sudra, the handkerchief-type acquisition: one takes an object of significance — not for its monetary value but as a symbolic act of finalization — and that effects the transaction. The *braita*, says Rabba, is simply excluding framing the acquisition of an *eved ivri* as an act of *chaliphin* rather than as a monetary purchase.

The *gemara* pushes back: this reading works for *keilim*, which are paradigmatically used for *chaliphin*, but what about *tevuah* — grain? Rav Nachman rules that food items (*peirot*) cannot function as *chaliphin* at all. So the *braita's* exclusion of *tevuah* cannot be read as excluding *chaliphin* performed with grain, since that isn't a recognized form of *chaliphin* in the first place.

Cornered, Rabba retreats to the position that indeed the case is where *tevuah* and *keilim* lack the value of a *perutah*. But then, as the *gemara* objects, why specify those categories? Rabba answers with a *lo mibaya* construction: it goes without saying that actual *kessef* less than a *perutah* does not effect a transaction. The *chiddush* of the *braita* is that even *tevuah* and *keilim* — which one might think could function differently, since their value is direct and experiential (one eats the grain, one uses the vessel) — even those fail when they fall below a *perutah* in monetary value. *Kamashma lan* that in the end, value must be monetary value, not merely functional value.

### Further Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן רַב כָּהֲנָא, דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא וּמִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ סוּדָרָא אַרֵישֵׁיהּ, אֲבָל כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – לָא. כִּי הָא דְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי זְבַן סוּדָרָא מֵאִימֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא מִקּוּבֵּי שָׁוֵי עַשְׂרָה בִּתְלֵיסַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We said** that it is possible to redeem one's son in this manner **only** when the priest is an individual **such as Rav Kahana, who is a great man and is required** to wear **a cloth on his head.** It was common practice for important people to wear a scarf on their heads. **But** with regard to **everyone** else, i.e., those who do not wear these cloths and cannot say it is worth that amount to them, **no,** they may not perform the redemption of the firstborn son in this manner. The Gemara cites a proof that a distinguished individual who need a cloth will pay a large amount for one. **This is like this** incident in **which Mar bar Rav Ashi bought a cloth from Rabba's mother from Kovei for thirteen** dinars, despite the fact that it was **worth ten,** because he needed a cloth.

This illuminates the deeper structure of the disagreement. For Rav Yosef, *shaveh kessef* functions as *kessef* precisely because it is quasi-money — it stands in for money, and therefore it must exhibit money's defining characteristic of determinacy. For Rabba, *shaveh kessef* functions not as a substitute for money but as an object of value in its own right. The underlying logic of the transaction is that something of value changes hands, not that something money-like changes hands. That is why the requirement of *kaitz* — a fixed, determined value — does not apply.

### Tosafot on Shiraim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: שִׁירָאֵי לָא צְרִיכִי שׁוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *halakha*** is: With regard to **silk** garments that are worth more than one *peruta*, **appraisal** is **not necessary** before a woman can be betrothed with them.

The *gemara* concludes with the ruling: *vehilchesa shiraim lo tzrichi shuma* — silk does not require appraisal, and we rule like Rabba. Tosafot raises the well-known question: given that we generally rule like Rabba over Rav Yosef, why does the *gemara* need to state the ruling explicitly? This question also touches on which version of the debate the ruling addresses — the first version, concerning *smichat da'at* when a value is claimed, or the second version, concerning whether any *kaitz* is required at all.

Rabbeinu Tam offers a subtle and important distinction. The ruling that *shiraim* do not require *shuma* is specifically calibrated to the nature of silk: its value is roughly knowable — *shumasan yadu'a ketzat* — and the variance in how people assess it is not so wide as to undermine the woman's confidence. Silk is not the kind of item about which one can be dramatically mistaken.

But other items, such as precious stones and pearls, are a different matter entirely. With diamonds, the variance in valuation can be enormous — one must know not only whether the stone is genuine but also its cut, clarity, color, and carat weight, factors that can shift the price by orders of magnitude. In that case, *tzarich shuma mishum de-lo samcha da'atah* — an appraisal is required because the woman may be relying on a grossly inflated estimate of the stone's worth, and her *smichat da'at* is therefore compromised. Rabbeinu Tam's point thus operates on the terrain of the first version of the debate, focusing on the woman's subjective confidence rather than the abstract requirement of *kaitz*.

This distinction has direct practical implications. Tosafot states: *lefikach nahagu olam lekadesh betaba'at she-ein bah even* — and this is why the universal practice developed to perform *kiddushin* with a plain ring that contains no precious stone. A plain gold ring is valued by weight, and gold's weight-based valuation admits of no significant variance. The woman may not know the precise sum, but there is no concern about dramatic misjudgment, and therefore *smichat da'at* is not at issue and no assessment is required.

This also explains the practice at the *chuppah* whereby the rabbi confirms with the witnesses that the ring is worth at least a *perutah*, and asks the *chassan* accordingly. This is in part a way of framing the *kiddushin* so that the ring is not represented as worth any specific substantial amount — thereby removing the concern about *smichat da'at* entirely. As for common variations in ring shape or design — a square rather than a round band, an artistic cut — these do not generate meaningful indeterminacy in value, since anyone can verify the price through ordinary channels. A ring made by Picasso would present an entirely different problem.

## Maneh & Dinar

### Maneh Stam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״, וְנָתַן לָהּ דִּינָר – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְיַשְׁלִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר לַהּ ״מָנֶה״ וְיָהֵב לַהּ דִּינָר, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לַהּ: ״עַל מְנָת״ דָּמֵי. וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars, and he gave her** one **dinar** out of the one hundred, **she is betrothed** immediately **and he must** subsequently **complete** the payment of the remainder of the amount he promised her. **What is the reason** for this? **Since he said to her:** Be betrothed to me with **one hundred dinars, and** yet **he gave her** only one **dinar, he is like one who said to her:** Be betrothed to me **on the condition** that I will give you one hundred dinars. In other words, he betroths her now with one dinar on the condition he will pay the remaining ninety-nine in the future. The betrothal therefore takes effect immediately, and he owes her the rest of the one hundred dinars. **And** this is in accordance with the opinion that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **anyone who states** a provision employing the phrase: **On the condition, it is** tantamount to his **stipulating** that the agreement take effect retroactively **from now.** An agreement stipulated by means of the phrase: On the condition, takes effect immediately. This is not like an ordinary agreement, which takes effect only after the condition has been fulfilled.

Rabbi Elazar rules: if a man says to a woman, "Marry me with a *maneh*" — a hundred — and then hands her a single *dinar*, she is nonetheless betrothed, and he must eventually complete the payment. The reasoning, as the *gemara* explains, is that when he said *maneh* and immediately handed her a *dinar*, the context clarifies his intent: he was not proposing that a hundred coins constitute the *kessef kiddushin*. Rather, he was framing the full hundred as a *tenai* — a condition — while the *dinar* in her hand is the actual *kessef* of the *kiddushin*. The *gemara* invokes Rav Huna's ruling in the name of Rav: *kol ha-omer al menat, k'omer me-achshav dami* — one who says "on the condition" is regarded as having said "from now," meaning the *kiddushin* takes effect immediately with the *dinar*, subject to the condition of eventual full payment.

### Maneh Zu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״, וְהָיָה מוֹנֶה וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְרָצָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן לַחֲזוֹר אֲפִילּוּ בַּדִּינָר הָאַחֲרוֹן – הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאָמַר ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״. הָא מִדְּסֵיפָא ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״, רֵישָׁא בְּמָנֶה סְתָם, דְּקָא תָנֵי סֵיפָא: אָמַר לָהּ ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה זֶה״ וְנִמְצָא מָנֶה חָסֵר דִּינָר, אוֹ דִּינָר שֶׁל נְחֹשֶׁת – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. דִּינָר רַע – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְיַחְלִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:10). If a man says to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars, and** while **he was counting** the money, **one of them wanted to retract** the betrothal, **it is in the power** of either of them to do so **even when** only **the last** dinar remains to be given. This indicates that the betrothal does not take effect until the entire one hundred dinars has been paid. **With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where he says** to the woman: Be betrothed to me **with this one hundred dinars.** In this case the entire one hundred dinars, not just the first dinar, constitutes the betrothal money. **As it is taught in the latter clause** of that *baraita*, if **he said** to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with this one hundred dinars, and it was found to be one hundred dinars less a dinar, or** if it included **a dinar of copper** instead of silver, **she is not betrothed.** If one of the dinars was found to be **a flawed dinar,** i.e., it was a silver dinar but it was so worn that it would not be universally accepted, **she is betrothed, and he must exchange** that dinar for a different one.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a *braita*: if a man says "Marry me with a *maneh*" and begins counting out the coins one by one, and either party wishes to retract — even when only the last coin remains — the right to retract is in hand, and the *kiddushin* has not yet taken effect. This implies that when a man says *maneh*, the full hundred is the *kessef kiddushin*, not merely a *tenai*, and the transaction is incomplete until the entire sum is delivered.

The *gemara* resolves this by proposing that the *braita* deals with the case of *maneh zu* — "this hundred," where the man pointed to a specific stack of coins. The *seifa* of the *braita* reinforces this: if he said *hitkadshi li be-maneh zu* — "marry me with this particular hundred" — and it turned out that one coin was missing, or one was copper (*dinar shel nechoshet*) rather than silver, the *kiddushin* is invalid. If, however, one coin was merely *dinar ra* — legitimate currency but in poor condition, one that storekeepers might decline but which would still be accepted at a bank — the *kiddushin* is valid, though he must replace the damaged coin.

### Clarification by Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא דְּאָמַר ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״, וּפָרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ: רָצָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן לַחֲזוֹר, אֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר הָאַחֲרוֹן – הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ. כֵּיצַד – כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר לַהּ ״בְּמָנֶה זוֹ״. וְהָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ רֵישָׁא בְּמָנֶה סְתָם, הַשְׁתָּא בְּמָנֶה סְתָם לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״ מִיבַּעְיָא? אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא לָא אִירְיָא – תְּנָא סֵיפָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי רֵישָׁא, שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר רֵישָׁא ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״, אֲבָל בְּמָנֶה סְתָם הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין, תְּנָא סֵיפָא ״בְּמָנֶה זֶה״, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא בְּמָנֶה סְתָם, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מוֹנֶה וְהוֹלֵךְ שָׁאנֵי, דְּדַעְתַּהּ אַכּוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **No;** one can say that the **first clause and the latter clause** are both referring to a case **where he said: With this one hundred dinars, and** the latter clause **is explaining** the first clause, as follows: If **one of them wishes to retract the** betrothal, **it is in the power** of either of them to do so **even when** only **the last** dinar remains to be given. **How so?** This is referring to a case **where he said to her: With this one hundred dinars.** **And so too, it is reasonable** to explain the *baraita* this way, **as, if it enters your mind** that **the first clause** is referring **to an unspecified one hundred dinars,** consider the following: **Now** if **it is not a betrothal in** a case involving **an unspecified one hundred dinars,** and she can retract her agreement to the betrothal, **is** it **necessary** to say that she can retract it when he says: **With this one hundred dinars?** If that were the correct interpretation of the first clause, the ruling in the last clause would be unnecessary. **If it is due to that** reason, **there is no** conclusive **argument,** i.e., the proof of the argument is inconclusive. The reason is that one could say that the *tanna* **taught the latter clause to reveal** the meaning of **the first clause.** The function of the latter clause is not to teach a novel *halakha*, but to preempt a mistaken reading of the first clause. The latter clause was stated so **that you should not** mistakenly **say that the first clause** is referring only to a situation when he said: **With this one hundred dinars, but in** a case of **an unspecified one hundred dinars, it** would be **a betrothal.** Therefore, the *tanna* **taught** in **the latter clause: With this one hundred dinars,** which teaches **by inference that the first clause** is referring **to an unspecified one hundred dinars, and even so it is not a betrothal.** Although this proof is rejected, the Gemara remains with its explanation that the first clause is referring to a case where he said to her: With this one hundred dinars. **Rav Ashi said:** It is unnecessary to explain that the first clause of the *baraita* is referring to a case where he said: With this one hundred dinars, as a situation where he **was counting** the money **is different.** The reason is **that she has the entirety** of the one hundred dinars **in mind,** and therefore she will not be content with some of the money. His continuous action indicates that they both expect that he will give her the entire one hundred dinars, and consequently both parties can retract their agreement until he finishes counting.

The *gemara* ultimately confirms that both the *reisha* and the *seifa* of the *braita* concern *maneh zu* — a specific, identified sum. The *seifa* clarifies and extends the *reisha* rather than introducing a new case. This reading preserves Rabbi Elazar's ruling intact: when a man says *maneh stam* — an unspecified hundred — and immediately hands over a smaller amount, the action itself clarifies that the hundred is a *tenai* and the smaller sum is the *kessef kiddushin*.

The *gemara* does briefly consider the possibility that the *reisha* was *maneh stam* and the *seifa* was introduced to clarify it, but settles on the simpler reading: the *seifa* is not merely clarificatory — it introduces a genuine *chiddush*. Even within the framework of *maneh stam*, there is a meaningful difference between the *reisha* and *seifa*. In the *reisha* (read as *maneh stam* where the full hundred is indeed the *kessef kiddushin*), if one coin is defective, it can be replaced with another — since he committed to *a* hundred, not *this* hundred. But in the *seifa*, where he identified a specific stack as the *kessef kiddushin*, the entire identified amount must be as represented; replacing a defective coin after the fact does not remedy the failed *kiddushin*.

### Dinar Ra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי דִּינָר שֶׁל נְחֹשֶׁת, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָדְעָה בֵּיהּ – הָא סְבַרָה וְקַבִּלָה! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלַיְיהּ בְּלֵילְיָא, אִי נָמֵי דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח לַיהּ בֵּינֵי זוּזֵי. הַאי דִּינָר רַע הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא נָפֵיק – הַיְינוּ דִּינָר שֶׁל נְחֹשֶׁת! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן דְּנָפֵיק עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances of this copper dinar** mentioned here? **If she knows that it** is a copper dinar, **she knew and accepted** this coin as a dinar. If so, she cannot later retract her betrothal. The Gemara answers: **No;** it is **necessary** in a case **where he gave her** this dinar **at night** and at the time she did not see that it was copper. **Alternatively, it was found among** the other **dinars,** and she did not notice that one of them one was copper. **What are the circumstances of this flawed dinar,** also mentioned in the *baraita*? **If it cannot be spent,** i.e., it cannot pass as a silver dinar, **this is** exactly like **a copper dinar,** as it too is not worth a full dinar. **Rav Pappa said:** This is referring to a case **where** the dinar **can be spent with difficulty,** i.e., it is difficult, but not impossible, to find someone who will accept it.

The *gemara's* distinction between *dinar ra* and *dinar shel nechoshet* raises a further question. A *dinar shel nechoshet* — a counterfeit or base-metal coin representing itself as silver — is simply not what was promised, and the *kiddushin* fails. But a *dinar ra* is genuine currency, even if worn or damaged; it has not lost its identity as legal tender. Rav Papa clarifies: the case of *dinar ra* that still validates the *kiddushin* is one where the coin *nafik al yedei hadach* — it circulates, albeit with some difficulty. It is not refused categorically; it may require some insistence, but it is still recognized. The woman received what was promised in substance, even if not in ideal condition, and therefore the *kiddushin* holds — with the obligation to exchange the coin for a better one.

## Meshkon Issues

### Meshkon Not Kessef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אָמַר לַהּ ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״, וְהִנִּיחַ לָהּ מַשְׁכּוֹן עֲלֵיהֶ[ם] – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, מָנֶה אֵין כָּאן – מַשְׁכּוֹן אֵין כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** If **one said to** a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars, and** instead of giving it to her **he gave her collateral for** the money, **she is not betrothed. There is no one hundred dinars here,** as he has not yet given her one hundred dinars, and **there is no collateral here,** since this collateral is not a gift but merely a security. He has therefore not given her anything.

Rav Nachman rules: if a man says to a woman "Marry me with a *maneh*" and places his watch before her as *mashkon* — collateral — she is not married. The principle he states is *maneh ein kan, mashkon ein kan*: the hundred is not here, and the *mashkon* is not here as *kessef kiddushin*. The *mashkon* was not tendered as the *kessef* of the transaction but as a pledge of future payment, much as the collateral in the story of Yehuda and Tamar functioned as an assurance of a promised gift. An object given as collateral, however valuable, is not the *kessef kiddushin* — and since the *maneh* itself was never given, the *kiddushin* did not take effect.

### Kedisha B'Meshkon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: קִידְּשָׁה בְּמַשְׁכּוֹן מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! הָתָם בְּמַשְׁכּוֹן דַּאֲחֵרִים וְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from the following *baraita*: If **he betrothed her with collateral, she is betrothed.** The Gemara answers: **There,** it is referring **to collateral** belonging **to other** people, which was in the possession of the man who betrothed her, **and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak.**

Rava challenges Rav Nachman from a *braita* that states: *kiddeshah be-mashkon mekudeshet* — if a man betroths a woman with *mashkon*, she is betrothed. The *gemara* immediately distinguishes: that *braita* refers to *mashkon de-acherim* — collateral belonging to someone else — and its validity derives from the principle of Rav Yitzchak.

### Creditor Owns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״ – אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה, צְדָקָה מִנַּיִן? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral,** i.e., that the individual in possession of the collateral has the actual rights to it? **As it is stated:** "You shall surely restore him the pledge when the sun goes down that he may sleep in his garment, and bless you; **and it shall be righteousness to you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13). **If** the creditor **does not acquire** the pledge or collateral, **from where** does this considering his return of the collateral as **righteousness** stem? The creditor is not giving an item belonging to him; why is this considered a righteous act? Rather, **from here** it is derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** to a certain extent.

Rav Yitzchak teaches: *minayin le-va'al chov she-koneh mashkon* — from where do we know that a creditor acquires ownership of collateral he holds? From the verse *ul'cha tihiyeh tzedakah* — "and it shall be righteousness unto you" — said in the context of returning a garment taken as *mashkon* each day or night. If the creditor did not own the collateral, asks Rav Yitzchak, in what sense is returning it an act of righteousness? One can always perform an act of kindness in returning something one lawfully holds without owning it. The inference of *tzedakah* implies that he is giving back something *his own* — which means the creditor holds genuine ownership in the *mashkon*.

Tosafot raises a technical objection: even without ownership, returning the garment so the debtor can sleep in it is an act of righteousness on its face. Tosafot then introduces an alternative explanation for the derivation, noting that the verse continues *ve-shachav be-salmato u-verachecha* — "he will sleep in his garment and bless you." If the creditor does not own the *mashkon*, then the blessing the poor man gives in return for use of his own garment would constitute *ribit devarim* — verbal usury — a form of benefit accruing to the lender from the loan. If, however, the creditor owns the *mashkon* and is genuinely returning something of his own, the blessing is a response to a gift, not to the loan, and there is no *ribit*. Tosafot adds a further interpretation: *tzedakah* technically requires that one give from one's own — *ha-noten mishelo* — and the verse's use of that term implies ownership.

The practical upshot of Rav Yitzchak's principle in the present context is that when Reuven holds Shimon's watch as collateral for a loan, Reuven has genuine ownership — or at least a form of ownership — in that watch. If Reuven then gives Shimon's watch to a woman as *kessef kiddushin*, he is giving her something he owns. Tosafot notes a potential distinction between collateral taken at the moment of the loan versus collateral taken subsequently, but in either case, from the principle in one situation, ownership can be inferred in the other as well.

The question of what the woman actually receives in such a transaction is worth pausing over. She now holds something Reuven owned. But practically speaking, can she use the watch? She cannot — it remains Shimon's watch in most practical senses. Does Reuven transfer to her the right to collect the debt if unpaid? That is unclear. The *gemara* does not resolve precisely what the nature of her acquisition is; perhaps it is sufficient that something of value, which he owned, was transferred to her possession, even without her accruing direct use-benefit from it.

### Priti Without Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי רַב הוּנָא בַּר אָבִין זְבוּן הָהִיא אַמְתָּא בִּפְרִיטֵי, לָא הֲווֹ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, אוֹתִיבוּ נְסָכָא עֲלֵיהּ. לְסוֹף אִיַּיקַּר אַמְתָּא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: פְּרִיטֵי אֵין כָּאן, נְסָכָא אֵין כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The sons of Rav Huna bar Avin bought a certain maidservant** on the condition that they would pay **with** copper ***perutot*. They did not have** the money at the time, and therefore **they gave a piece of silver [*naskha*] for her** as collateral. **Ultimately,** the price of the **maidservant increased** and the sellers wanted to cancel the sale. **They came before Rabbi Ami** for his ruling and **he said to them: There are no *perutot* here,** and **there is no piece of silver here** either. There was no valid act of acquisition at all because they did not actually give the money, and the collateral does not transfer ownership.

The *gemara* then recounts the case of the sons of Rav Huna bar Avin, who were purchasing a maidservant with *priti* — cash — but did not have the money on hand at the moment of the transaction. They placed a piece of silver (*naska*) before the seller as a placeholder. Later the maidservant's value rose, and the seller sought to undo the sale, arguing it had never been finalized. The case came before Rav Ami, who ruled: *priti ein kan, naska ein kan* — the money is not here, and the silver piece is not here as purchase money. Parallel to *kiddushin*, what was placed before the seller was collateral, not the purchase price. Since the purchase money was never actually paid, the sale was not final.

Tosafot notes an interesting wrinkle: unlike a case of debt, where *mashkon* makes halachic sense as security for a sum already owed, here the sale had not yet been concluded and no debt yet existed. What legal function did the *naska* serve? Tosafot explores whether the *mashkon* can operate in a pre-debt context — and whether the seller could force the buyers to complete the purchase or forfeit the collateral — but leaves the question partially open. The *gemara's* ruling stands regardless: the *naska* was not the *kessef* of the transaction and therefore the acquisition was incomplete.

## Acceptance Issues

### Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״, נְטָלַתּוּ וּזְרָקַתּוּ לַיָּם אוֹ לָאוּר אוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר הָאָבֵד – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. הָא שְׁדֵיתִינְהוּ קַמֵּיהּ – הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין? הָא קָאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: שְׁקֵיל, לָא בָּעֵינָא! לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָא אָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא שְׁדֵיתִינְהוּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּלָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל זְרָקַתּוּ לַיָּם אוֹ לָאוּר אֵימָא כֵּיוָן דְּמִיחַיְּיבָא בְּהוּ, קַדּוֹשֵׁי קַדִּישׁ נַפְשָׁהּ, וְהָא דְּקָא עָבְדָא הָכִי, סָבְרָא: אֶיבְדְּקֵיהּ לְהַאי גַּבְרָא אִי רַתְחָנָא הוּא אוֹ לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:9): If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars,** and **she took it** from his hand **and threw it into the sea, or into the fire, or into anything that destroys, she is not betrothed.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this ruling: The *baraita* indicates that she is not betrothed only if the one hundred dinars are destroyed, **but** if she **threw the coins before him** but did not destroy them, **that is a betrothal.** Why should this be? By throwing the coins at him **she is** effectively **saying to him: Take** this; **I do not want** your betrothal. **The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the *baraita* **is speaking** using the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to state in a case where **she throws** the money **before him that it is not a betrothal,** as her action indicates that she does not want to be betrothed. **But** when **she throws it into the sea or into the fire,** one might **say** that **since she is obligated** to pay **for** the money he gave her, perhaps **she betrothed herself** with the money she received. **And as** for the reason why **she did this** act of destroying the money, **she thought: I will test this man** to see **if he is** an individual of **an angry** temperament **or not.** Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that even in that case it is not a betrothal.

A *braita* teaches: if a man says "Marry me with this hundred" and hands her the money, and she takes it and throws it into the ocean or into a fire or otherwise destroys it — *einah mekudeshet*, she is not married. The *gemara* initially considers whether simply throwing it back in front of him would suffice as a rejection — she is clearly saying *lo be'ina*, I do not want it — and therefore the *braita's* need to specify destruction seems odd.

The *gemara* resolves this with a *lo mibaya* formulation: it goes without saying that throwing it back in front of him rejects the *kiddushin*. The *chiddush* of the *braita* concerns the case of destruction, where one might have reasoned differently. When she destroys the money, she is destroying what could be *his* money — since if she rejected the *kiddushin*, it was never hers. That means she will owe him reimbursement. Faced with that liability, we might have supposed that she in fact accepted the *kiddushin* — she took it as her own and then destroyed her own money as a test of his temperament (*savra ebdakia le-hai gavra i ritcha hu o lo*). *Kamashma lan* — the *braita* teaches that despite this reasoning, we read her act as rejection, and she is not betrothed.

### Sheliach Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״ – ״תְּנֵם, לְאַבָּא וּלְאָבִיךָ״ – אֵינָהּ ״מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיְּקַבְּלוּם לִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. תְּנָא אַבָּא לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחַ דְּרֵישָׁא, תְּנָא אָבִיךָ לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחַ דְּסֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:9): If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars,** and she said to him: **Give** the money **to** my **father,** or: Give the money **to your father, she is not betrothed.** If she said: Give the money to my father, or Give the money to your father **on the condition that he accepts them for me, she is betrothed.** The Gemara comments: The *baraita* **taught** the case where she said: Give the money to my **father, to convey the far-reaching nature of** the *halakha* of **the first clause.** If she simply said that he should give the money to anybody else, even her own father, whom it can be assumed she wants to benefit, she is still not betrothed. And the *baraita* **taught** the case where she said: Give the money to **your father, to convey the far-reaching nature of** the *halakha* of **the latter clause.** If she said that someone else should receive the money on her behalf, it is a valid betrothal even if she said the money should be given to his father.

A subsequent *braita* presents the case of a man who offers a woman *kiddushin* money and she responds: "Give it to my father" or "give it to your father." In that case — *einah mekudeshet* — she is not married. The *gemara* explains this as mocking rejection: she is saying, in effect, you want to waste your money, go give it to someone else. Even though her father would presumably hold the money for her, or might return it to her, we do not read her response as acceptance of the *kiddushin*.

However, if she says *al menat she-yekablum li* — on the condition that they accept it for me — she *is* betrothed, because in that formulation she is appointing them as her *shaliach* (agent) and the acceptance on her behalf effects the *kiddushin*.

The *braita* specifies both "give it to my father" and "give it to your father" to teach distinct lessons. The case of "my father" reveals the strength of the *reisha*: even though her father is a natural recipient who might hold the money for her, we still read the unqualified directive as rejection. The case of "your father" reveals the strength of the *seifa*: even directing the money to *his* father, if framed with the explicit condition that he accept it on her behalf, constitutes valid agency and the *kiddushin* takes effect.

### Ploni Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״ – ״תְּנֵם לִפְלוֹנִי״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיְּקַבְּלֵם לִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן ״אַבָּא וְאָבִיךְ״, הָתָם הוּא דְּכִי אָמְרָה ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיְּקַבְּלוּם לִי״ – הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין, דְּסָמְכָה דַּעְתַּהּ עִילָּ[וַ]יְהוּ, סָבְרָה: עָבְדִין לִי שְׁלִיחוּתַאי. אֲבָל פְּלוֹנִי – לָא, וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן פְּלוֹנִי, הָכָא הוּא דְּכִי אַמְרַהּ ״תְּנֵם לִפְלוֹנִי״ – לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, דְּלָא מְקָרְבָא דַּעְתַּהּ לְגַבֵּיהּ לְמִיתְּבֵהּ לֵיהּ בְּמַתָּנָה, אֲבָל ״אַבָּא וְאָבִיךְ״, דִּמְקָרְבָא דַּעְתַּהּ לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, אֵימָא בְּמַתָּנָה יַהֲבִיתֵיהּ נִיהֲלַיְיהוּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** If he said to her: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars,** and she said to him: **Give them to so-and-so, she is not betrothed.** But if she said to him: **On the condition that he accepts them for me, she is betrothed.** The Gemara comments: **It is necessary** to issue this ruling, despite the fact that it is apparently identical to the previous *halakha*. **This is because if** the *tanna* had **taught us** only the case where she said: **Give** the money **to** my **father,** or: Give the money **to your father,** one would say that **it is** in that case **there, when she said: On the condition that** one of these relatives **accepts them for me,** that **it is a betrothal, as she relies on them** and she **thinks: They will do my bidding for me** and will hold this money on my behalf. **But** if she says: Give it to **so-and-so,** someone who is not related to either of them, **no,** the betrothal is not valid. **And** conversely, **if** the *tanna* had **taught us** only the case of **so-and-so,** one might say: **It is here, when she said: Give it to so-and-so,** that **it is not a betrothal,** as **she is not close to him** and is not interested in **giving him** this money **as a gift. But** if she said: **Give** the money **to** my **father,** or: Give the money **to your father, to whom she is close,** one might **say** that **she gave it to them as a gift,** i.e., she accepted the sum as her betrothal money and decided to give it as a gift. If so, she should be betrothed. **It was** therefore **necessary** for the *tanna* to issue both rulings and clarify that she is not betrothed in either case.

The *braita* then presents a parallel case with *ploni* — a random third party. If she says "give it to *ploni*" without qualification, she is not married. If she adds *al menat she-yekablum li*, she is. The *gemara* explains why both cases are necessary, since each teaches something the other does not.

Had the *braita* only stated the case of father: we would have understood that the *al menat* formula works with family members — *samcha da'atah aliyei*, she trusts them to act on her behalf — but might have doubted whether it works with a stranger, since there is less reason to trust that *ploni* will faithfully execute her agency. And had the *braita* only stated the case of *ploni*: we would have understood that the unqualified directive to a stranger is a rejection — *de-lo mekarva da'atah legabei*, she has no close relationship with him — but might have misread a directive to her own father as an acceptance, reasoning that she feels close enough to her father to be directing a gift through him as an expression of her agreeing to the *kiddushin*. The *braita* therefore teaches both: the unqualified directive to father is still rejection; the qualified directive to *ploni* is still valid agency.

Rashi makes explicit what underlies this: *einah mekudeshet ve-einah doma le-ten maneh le-ploni ve-ikadesh lach* — this is unlike the case where *she* initiates by saying "give a hundred to so-and-so and I will be betrothed to you," because in that earlier case *hi ka'amra lei me-ikara* — she was the initiator. Here, *meshatei hi bo* — she is mocking him. The principle of *areiv* from the earlier *gemara* — where *kiddushin* can be effected by a payment directed to a third party — operates on the condition that the structure of the transaction was set up at her initiative, not his.

### Sela on Rock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה״ – ״תְּנֵם עַל גַּבֵּי סֶלַע״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם הָיָה סֶלַע שֶׁלָּהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. בָּעֵי רַב בִּיבִי: סֶלַע שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars,** and she said to him: **Place them on a rock, she is not betrothed. And if** the **rock was hers, she is betrothed. Rav Beivai raises a dilemma:** If the **rock was** the property **of both of them, what is** the *halakha*? No answer was found, and the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Another *braita*: the man says "Marry me with this hundred" and she responds, "Put it on that rock." If the rock does not belong to her — *einah mekudeshet*. If the rock *does* belong to her — *mekudeshet*. Rashi explains that when she owns the rock, she acquires the money through *kinyan chatzer* — the halachic principle that one's property effects acquisition on one's behalf.

Rav Bibi raises the question: what if the rock belongs to *both* of them jointly? *Teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

Tosafot takes issue with Rashi's explanation. If the mechanism were simply *kinyan chatzer*, the question of a jointly-owned rock would be simple: partners cannot effect *kinyan chatzer* for one another in jointly-held property, so the answer should be obvious. Tosafot therefore proposes a different framework: the determinative factor is *smichat da'at* — whether she has confident reliance that she will be able to retrieve the money. When the rock is hers, she has that confidence. When the rock is not hers, she does not. And when the rock is jointly owned, it is genuinely unclear whether her *smichat da'at* is sufficient — hence the unresolved *teiku*. This is admittedly a striking approach, since the woman never actually takes possession of the money. The *gemara* throughout this section seems less concerned with perfecting a formal act of *kinyan* than with identifying what constitutes sufficient acceptance in the context of *kiddushin* — a question that remains to be clarified through the accumulation of these cases.

### Dog Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּכִכָּר״ – ״תְּנֵהוּ לְכֶלֶב״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם הָיָה כֶּלֶב שֶׁלָּהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a similar case. If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with a loaf** of bread, and she said to him: **Give it to a dog, she is not betrothed. And if** this **dog was hers, she is betrothed.**

A final *braita*: the man says "Marry me with this loaf of bread" and she responds "Give it to the dog." If the dog is not hers — *einah mekudeshet*. If the dog belongs to her — *mekudeshet*. The *gemara* leaves open whether the mechanism here is that she acquires the loaf through her dog (an extension of *kinyan chatzer*), or whether the *kessef kiddushin* is not the loaf itself but the benefit she receives from having her dog fed at his expense. This ambiguity is meaningful: across all these cases, the *gemara* does not fully resolve what precisely constitutes the act of *kiddushin* — formal acquisition, receipt of benefit, or some combination. As with *Gittin*, the question of whether what is required is a genuine act of giving and receiving, or merely placing something in a domain associated with the recipient, remains to be worked out through the full range of cases.
