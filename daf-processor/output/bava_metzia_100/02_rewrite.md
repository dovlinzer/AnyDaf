# Bava Metzia 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exchange & Offspring

### Opening & BK Parallel

Today's daf is Bava Metzia 100. The opening of this amud invites a comparison to a parallel *sugya* in Bava Kamma, a connection that will prove illuminating as we proceed. We begin with a new *mishna* that at first seems somewhat out of place within this *perek*, yet it touches on fundamental principles of monetary law — principles far-reaching enough that, as we approach the end of the tractate, we find ourselves circling back to ideas encountered at the very beginning of Bava Metzia.

### Cow-Donkey Exchange

The *mishna* opens with the case of *hamachlif para b'chamor* — two parties engaged in *chalipin*, a barter exchange of a cow for a donkey. Rashi notes that the fact that this is a *chalipin* transaction rather than a standard sale is legally significant, as we shall see. The case turns on the fact that the cow gives birth — *ve'yalda* — and the question arises: did the birth occur before or after the exchange was effected? The seller claims the birth took place while the animal was still in his possession; the buyer claims it occurred after the *kinyan* was completed.

### Slave Birth Dispute

The *mishna* presents a parallel case: someone sold a female slave (*shifchato*), and she gave birth. Here too, one party says the birth occurred before the sale — *ad shelo macharti* — while the other insists it happened after the acquisition — *misheli lachachti*. The same factual uncertainty underlies both disputes: was this offspring born in the seller's domain or the buyer's?

### BK Goring Case

This is precisely why the Bava Kamma parallel comes to mind. There, the case involves a bull that gored a pregnant cow — *shor shenagach hapara ve'nimtza ubar b'tzidda* — and the question is identical in structure: did the cow give birth before or after the goring? The factual uncertainty generating the monetary dispute is the same type of *mamon hamutal b'safek*, a doubtful monetary claim.

### Doubtful Case Split

In both cases, our *mishna* rules *yachloku* — they split the value. The offspring, whether calf or child slave, is divided between the two parties since the matter cannot be resolved. Rashi raises the question of why the *mishna* specifically frames the case as *chalipin*; the *gemara* will need to address this. One reason is that *chalipin* allows a *kinyan* to be effected even when the animal is not physically present before the parties — transferring the donkey effects acquisition of the cow. The *mishna* could alternatively have employed a *kinyan agav*, a transfer of movable property by means of land, but we deal with the case as the *mishna* presents it.

## Two Items Claims

### Two Slaves/Fields

The *mishna* continues with a new cluster of cases. Someone possessed two slaves, one adult and one child — *echad gadol ve'echad katan* — or similarly two fields, one large and one small. A sale takes place, and a dispute arises as to which item was sold.

### Bari v. Shema

In the first sub-case, the buyer claims he bought the large slave or field, while the seller says he does not know which he sold. The rule is *zacha bagadol* — the buyer's claim prevails. The principle at work is *bari veshema bari adif*: a certain, firm claim (*bari*) overrides a doubtful one (*shema*). The same logic operates in the reverse scenario: if the seller says he sold only the smaller item, and the buyer says he does not know which he purchased, the buyer can claim only the smaller one. As the Tosafot HaRosh observes, these cases up to this point merely restate principles already familiar to us; the real novelty of the *mishna* lies in the final case.

### Bari v. Bari Split

The final case poses the greatest difficulty: *ze omer gadol ve'ze omer katan* — the seller claims he sold the small item, the buyer insists he purchased the large one. Both parties make firm, certain claims that contradict one another entirely.

### Seller's Oath

In this situation, the *mishna* rules *yishava hamocher shehakatan machar* — the seller must take an oath that he sold the small one. This ruling is striking. We might have expected the *mishna* simply to say *yachloku*, or to invoke *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* and demand proof from the buyer. Instead, it prescribes a *shevu'ah*. The *gemara* will have to work hard to explain what kind of oath this is. We are accustomed to the *shevu'ah* of *modeh bemiktzat* — admitting part of a claim — but here we have two different slaves of different sizes. It is not obvious how admitting to the smaller slave constitutes a partial admission of a claim about the larger one. And a student might well raise the point already present in the *mishna* in *Kiddushin*: why not simply say *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*? That question is well taken, and it is indeed the *gemara*'s first move, as we shall see.

## Splitting Challenge

### Chazaka Question

The *gemara* focuses its initial challenge on the final clause of the *mishna*, the case where neither party knows what occurred — *ze omer eini yodea ve'ze omer eini yodea* — and the ruling is *yachloku*. The *gemara* asks: *amaya yachloku*? Why should we split at all? The more natural resolution would be *v'lechezi bereshut man kayma* — we should simply determine in whose domain the animal or slave currently stands. This is the principle of *chazakat mamon*: whoever holds or controls the property has a presumptive claim over it, and the party seeking to dislodge that control bears the burden of proof — *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*.

### Ownerless Area

Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin citing Shmuel resolves this with an *okimta*: the cow is standing in a swamp — *d'omedet b'agam* — a desolate, ownerless area, not in anyone's private domain. Israel was once heavily swampy terrain, before the large-scale planting of eucalyptus trees. Similarly, *shifcha nami d'kayma b'simta* — the slave woman is standing in a public alleyway. In either case, the property is not in anyone's *reshut*, and therefore *chazakat mamon* cannot be invoked. There is no presumption of control that would tip the scales toward either party.

### Original Owner

A second suggestion is then raised: *v'nikma a'chazaka d'mara kamma* — perhaps we should resolve the doubt by reverting to the original owner's presumption, *chazakat mara kamma*. We know with certainty who previously owned the animal or slave; perhaps until we can demonstrate otherwise, ownership remains with that person, and the claimant bears the burden of proof. This concept will receive extensive treatment in Bava Batra. Like *chazakat mamon*, this is a form of *status quo* reasoning — things remain as they were until proven otherwise. Here too the counter-argument applies: *v'havay idach hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the buyer making the claim would have to produce proof. The *gemara* ultimately rejects this avenue as well, and we will see why shortly.

---

## Sumchus Debate

### Sumchus Position

The *gemara* concludes that the *mishna*'s ruling of *yachloku* reflects the position of Sumchus: *ha mani, Sumchus hi*. Sumchus holds that *mamon hamutal b'safek cholkin b'lo shevu'ah* — whenever there is a genuine monetary doubt, the parties divide the contested value without requiring either of them to take an oath. This opinion of Sumchus features prominently toward the end of Bava Kamma in numerous cases. We cannot hold like Sumchus universally, however, because the *rabanan* disagree, maintaining *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the claimant must produce proof.

### Rabbanan Position

The difficulty with attributing our *mishna* to Sumchus is that we prefer to rule like the *rabanan*, and furthermore, a significant internal tension arises: *eimur d'amar Sumchus b'shema v'shema* — one might say Sumchus applies his principle only where both parties express doubt, where each says *eini yodea*. But does Sumchus apply *yachloku* even in a case of *bari v'bari*, where both parties make firm, certain claims?

### Sumchus Bari/Bari

Rabba bar Rav Huna answers: yes — *in, amar Sumchus afilu b'bari v'bari*. Sumchus holds that even when both parties claim certainty and those claims directly contradict one another, we split the value. This is a more radical rendering of Sumchus's position: rather than waiting for proof that can never come, we simply divide the money in half, even when each party insists with full confidence on their version of events.

---

## Splitting Challenge…

### Pregnant Chazaka

Tosafot raises a third possible *chazakah* that the *gemara* might have employed rather than splitting: the *chazakah* that the cow was pregnant. If we know the cow has just given birth, perhaps we should assume it was pregnant until the very recent past — *chazakat me'uberet*. However, Tosafot rejects this suggestion because *chazakat mamon* is a stronger *chazakah* than a *chazakah* rooted in physical or biological reality. The *chazakah* of being pregnant is a *chazakah* about the factual state of the animal's body; it is not a *halachic* presumption about legal status or ownership. Several *rishonim* and *acharonim* engage with this question: how do we categorize *chazakat mara kamma* by comparison — is it a monetary-legal *chazakah* or merely a factual one? This remains a *machlokes acharonim* about the nature of *chazakat mara kamma*.

---

## Sumchus Debate…

### Rava Limits Sumchus

Rava disagrees with Rabba bar Rav Huna's expansive reading of Sumchus. Rava maintains: *le'olam ki amar Sumchus shema v'shema, aval bari v'bari lo amar* — Sumchus only says to split in a case of *shema v'shema*, where both parties express genuine uncertainty. In a case of *bari v'bari*, Sumchus does not apply. Rava therefore proposes re-reading the *mishna*: the opening cases should be understood as *shema v'shema* cases, not the *bari v'bari* cases they appear to be on the surface. The *gemara* then notes a structural point: the final clause of the *mishna*, which explicitly says *ze omer eini yodea ve'ze omer eini yodea*, serves, according to Rava, to illuminate the *reisha* — demonstrating that the opening cases too are *shema v'shema*. According to Rabba bar Rav Huna, by contrast, the final clause serves the opposite purpose: since Sumchus says *yachloku* even in *bari v'bari*, the *seifa* clarifies that the *reisha* is specifically a *bari v'bari* case, showing that Sumchus applies his principle even there — *afilu hachi yachloku*. Each reading has its internal coherence, but Rabba bar Rav Huna must adopt the more radical position that Sumchus extends *yachloku* even to cases of competing certain claims.

## Final Clause

### Doubt Resolution Methods

It is worth stepping back to observe the broader landscape of how the *halacha* resolves monetary uncertainty across *Shas*. There are at least five or six distinct frameworks: Sumchus's *yachloku b'lo shevu'ah*, the *rabanan*'s *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, the *yachloku im shevu'ah* of the first *mishna* in Bava Metzia, *kol d'alim gvar* (whoever prevails keeps it), and *yehei munach ad sheyavo Eliyahu* (leave it in escrow until the matter can be resolved). These approaches differ along a significant axis: how actively *beit din* is adjudicating versus stepping back from a decision it cannot confidently make. The question of *din* versus *peshara* — hard justice versus accommodation — will arise again when we reach the beginning of Sanhedrin, where the relative merits of strict judgment and mediation are debated directly.

---

## Oath on Slaves

### Different Items

Returning to the case of *bari v'bari* — where the seller says he sold the small slave and the buyer says he bought the large one — the *mishna* rules that the seller must take an oath. The *gemara* now presses the obvious difficulty: *ma she'ta'ano lo hoda lo, ma shehoda lo lo ta'ano* — what was claimed (the large slave) is not what was admitted (the small slave), and what was admitted is not what was claimed. The classic *shevu'at modeh bemiktzat* requires that the defendant admit to *part of the very claim* being made. Here, the seller is admitting to a different item entirely, not a fraction of the same item. Furthermore, there is the problem of *heilach* — if the seller simply says "here, take the small slave," he is in effect tendering the item directly, which according to one opinion (that of Rabbi Chiyya) exempts him from taking any oath at all, since he is not withholding anything. And there is yet a third difficulty: *ein nishba'in al ha'avadim* — slaves are legally equated to real estate (*avadim hukshu l'karkaot*), and there is no *shevu'ah* for real estate claims. All three problems must be resolved before we can make sense of the *mishna*'s ruling.

---

## Oath on Slaves…

### Rav's Value Theory

Rav proposes an *okimta*: *beta'ano dmei* — the dispute was never about the slaves themselves but about their monetary value. Specifically, Rav reconstructs the case as involving a *shaliach* who was sent with funds to purchase a slave. The sender (*meshale'ach*) says he provided money sufficient to buy an adult slave; the *shaliach* returns with a smaller sum, claiming that is all that remains. The dispute is about the cash — the price of an adult slave versus the price of a child slave — which is a classic *modeh bemiktzat* monetary dispute. No slave was actually purchased in this reading; the parties are disputing the amount of money that passed between them.

### Shmuel's Accessories

Shmuel offers a different reconstruction: *beta'ano ksut eved gadol, ksut eved katan* — the dispute is about the clothing of an adult slave versus the clothing of a child slave, or alternatively *omrei sadeh gedola, omrei sadeh ktana* — the sheaves of grain on a large field versus those on a small field. Since clothing and sheaves are *metaltelin*, movable property, a *shevu'ah* can attach to them. The *gemara* immediately objects: even here, *ma she'ta'ano lo hoda lo* — if you claim an adult-sized garment and the other party admits only to a child-sized garment, these are two different garments, not a partial admission of the same claim.

### Uncut Cloth Theory

Rav Pappa resolves this: *b'yalfei* — the garment was still on the roll, a single bolt of uncut cloth. The purchase was of a slave along with a shirt to be cut from that bolt; since it had not yet been cut, it remains one undivided object. An admission regarding the smaller portion of the same uncut cloth *is* a partial admission of the same claim, satisfying the requirement of *modeh bemiktzat hata'anah*. The same logic can apply to the field: the small field is a section of the larger one.

---

## Uniqueness Problem

### Redundancy Objection

Rav Sheshet raises a methodological objection to Rabbi Oshaya's reading: *zokekin ata lishmi'inan?* Is the entire purpose of this *mishna* merely to teach us the principle of *zokekin* — *gilgul shevu'ah*, the rolling of an oath from *metaltelin* to *karka*? But we already have a *mishna* in Kiddushin that states explicitly: *zokekin hanichasim she'ein lahem achrayut et hanichasim she'yesh lahem achrayut lishhava aleihem* — movable property can generate an oath obligation that extends to real property. If that principle is already taught in Kiddushin, why does Bava Metzia need to teach it again?

### Rabbi Meir Slaves

Rav Sheshet therefore proposes a new reading: *ha mani, Rabbi Meir hi* — the *mishna* reflects Rabbi Meir's minority opinion, which holds *avda k'metaltelin dami* — a slave is considered legally equivalent to movable property, not to real estate. According to Rabbi Meir on this view, there is no bar to a *shevu'ah* on the slave itself, and the *mishna* is teaching something new: that even a slave can be the subject of *shevu'at modeh bemiktzat*.

### Rabban Gamliel

But even on Rav Sheshet's reading there remains the problem of *ma she'ta'ano lo hoda lo* — admitting to a small slave when a large one was claimed. To resolve this, Rav Sheshet adds that the *mishna* also follows Rabban Gamliel's position: *ta'ano chitim v'hoda lo se'orim, Rabban Gamliel mechayev* — if one claims wheat and the other admits to barley, Rabban Gamliel considers this *modeh bemiktzat*, since he assesses the admission in terms of monetary equivalence rather than object identity. The *chachamim* disagree and rule *patur*, since wheat and barley are distinct commodities. The *mishna* on Rav Sheshet's reading therefore combines Rabbi Meir's position on slaves as *metaltelin*, Rabban Gamliel's position on cross-commodity admissions, and the view that *heilach* does not exempt — a very narrow confluence of minority opinions.

---

## Rabbi Meir Status

### Both or Slaves Only?

The *gemara* now interrogates Rabbi Meir's actual position. Is it Rabbi Meir who holds that slaves are treated as *metaltelin* for oath purposes, or the reverse? The question turns on a *mishna* about theft: if someone steals an animal or slaves and they age during the period of the theft, what is owed upon return? The *mishna* states *meshalem kesh'at hagezeilah* — one pays the value at the time of the theft. Rabbi Meir says regarding slaves: *harei shelcha lefanecha* — you may simply return the slave as is.

### Meir-Land Agreement

At first glance this ruling about *harei shelcha lefanecha* does not obviously bear on whether Rabbi Meir treats slaves as *metaltelin* or *karka*. However, Rabba bar Avuha proposed switching the attributions: perhaps it is Rabbi Meir who holds *meshalem kesh'at hagezeilah*, and the *chachamim* who say *harei shelcha lefanecha* regarding slaves. From this it might appear that Rabbi Meir equates slaves to real estate — since *harei shelcha lefanecha* is clearly applicable to real estate, which retains its identity unchanged. But the *gemara* probes further: perhaps *makshinan karka l'eved* — perhaps the equation runs in the other direction, so that just as one takes an oath for slaves, one might also take an oath for real estate? The *gemara* dismisses this: one cannot extract from the *heikesh* that real estate becomes subject to oaths; the equation between slaves and land does not work in that direction.

### Baraita Oath Dispute

A *baraita* then makes Rabbi Meir's position explicit. The *baraita* presents a case closely parallel to our *mishna* — exchange of a cow for a donkey, birth of offspring, disputed ownership — and states: *divrei Rabbi Meir*, this is Rabbi Meir's ruling. The general principle stated is *kol hanishba'in sheba'Torah nishba'in v'lo meshalmin* — all Torah-mandated oaths function such that taking the oath exempts from payment. The *chachamim* respond directly: *ein nishba'in lo al ha'avadim v'lo al hakarka'ot* — we take no oath for slaves or for real estate. The *gemara* notes that from the *chachamim*'s formulation — addressing both slaves and real estate — it appears Rabbi Meir holds that one *does* take an oath for both. But the *gemara* cautions: perhaps Rabbi Meir agrees with the *chachamim* about real estate and only disagrees about slaves. The subsequent analysis attempts to adjudicate this, and it becomes clear through a *baraita* about *tenai* that Rabbi Meir agrees to the *chachamim* regarding real estate — *odei lei b'karkaot* — and his *machloket* is specifically about slaves.

### Grapes Status

A further illustration of Rabbi Meir's nuanced position comes from the case of *asarah gefanim te'unot masarti lach* — someone who claims he sold ten grapevines full of grapes (*omed livtzir*, ready for harvest) while the other party says only five were sold. Rabbi Meir holds *yesh devarim shehem k'karka v'einam k'karka* — there are things that are in some respects like real estate and in other respects not, and the *chachamim* do not agree with him about these cases.

### Hybrid Status

As the Tosafot HaRosh points out, for certain purposes — such as *Hilchot Shabbat* — even Rabbi Meir would agree the grapes are subject to the prohibition of harvesting as an act of *melacha*; he does not say they are *kevtzirut damyan* (treated as already harvested) for that purpose. Similarly, with respect to a creditor's lien, the *chachamim* would agree with Rabbi Meir that a creditor cannot seize grapes ready for harvest as part of the field's lien. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* thus applies only in the specific context of monetary claims and oaths, where the status of attached produce ready for harvest is in question. Different *halachic* domains apply different criteria, which is part of what makes the overall question of *avadim hukshu l'karkaot* so complex throughout *Shas*.

## Rabbi Oshaya Solution

### Back to Oshaya

Since Rav Sheshet's elaborate construction — reading the *mishna* as reflecting simultaneously Rabbi Meir's treatment of slaves as *metaltelin*, Rabban Gamliel's cross-commodity *modeh bemiktzat*, and the position that *heilach* does not exempt — has now been undermined, the *gemara* returns to Rabbi Oshaya's simpler explanation. The decisive point is that Rabbi Meir does *not* hold slaves are like *metaltelin*; the *beraitot* and *mishnayot* surveyed indicate that Rabbi Meir agrees with the *chachamim* that slaves are like *karka* for oath purposes, and his *machloket* with them concerns only specific boundary cases. With no *tanna* available who treats slaves straightforwardly as *metaltelin*, Rav Sheshet's reading collapses, and Rabbi Oshaya's *okimta* must stand.

### Accessory Oath

Rabbi Oshaya had proposed: *kegon she'ta'ano eved b'ksuto v'sadeh b'omreha* — the claim was for the slave together with the garment he wore, or for the field together with the sheaves of grain upon it. The shirt or the sheaves are *metaltelin* and can generate a *shevu'at modeh bemiktzat*. The *gemara* had objected that the garment admitted to (child-sized) is a different garment from the one claimed (adult-sized), which Rav Pappa resolved: *b'yalfei* — the cloth was still on the roll, a single undivided piece from which shirts of either size would be cut, so the admission and the claim concern the same object.

### Minor Items Effect

Rav Sheshet's question — that this teaches nothing new beyond what Kiddushin already established about *zokekin* — is answered directly: the *mishna* comes to teach that even an item as *tafel* (ancillary and minor in value) as the slave's shirt or the field's produce is sufficient to generate a *shevu'at modeh bemiktzat* on the primary, far more valuable purchase. One might have thought that when a buyer acquires a slave, the slave's clothing is so incidental to the transaction — the slave obviously comes wearing a shirt — that it should not be treated as a distinct *metaltelin* claim capable of triggering oath liability. The *mishna* teaches *kama mashmalon*: even this *tafel* item is sufficient to create the *shevu'ah* and, through *gilgul shevu'ah*, to require an oath on the slave or field as well.

---

## Final Clause…

### Both Ignorant Split

The final clause of the *mishna* returns to the case of *ze omer eini yodea ve'ze omer eini yodea yachloku* — where neither party knows what occurred and they divide the contested value. The *gemara* confirms: *ha mani, Sumchus hi* — this reflects Sumchus's position, *de'amar mamon hamutal b'safek cholkin* — whenever there is genuine monetary doubt, the parties split without an oath.

### Sumchus Doubtful Split

The *gemara* then revisits the penultimate clause — where both parties say *birshuti*, making firm claims, and the *mocher* must take an oath — and applies it to Rabba bar Rav Huna's expansive reading of Sumchus. According to Rabba bar Rav Huna, Sumchus says *yachloku* even in *bari v'bari*; so if he would say that in the stronger case of competing certain claims, why does the *mishna* need to tell us he says *yachloku* in the weaker case of *shema v'shema*? The answer is that Sumchus concedes to the *rabanan* in a case where a *shevu'ah de'oraita* applies — specifically *shevu'at modeh bemiktzat* — and the *mishna* comes to teach that even Sumchus, in the specific *bari v'bari* case with the *shevu'ah de'oraita* present (where the slave's hand had been injured so that *heilach* cannot be claimed), endorses a result that goes beyond simple *yachloku*. The final clause of *shema v'shema* then demonstrates that in a pure case of uncertainty, Sumchus's baseline principle operates without qualification.

### Sumchus-Rabbanan Contrast

The overarching structure of this entire *sugya* is the recurring contrast between Sumchus and the *rabanan* — a contrast that appears across many tractates throughout *Shas*. The dispute between them is not merely technical; it reflects a fundamental disagreement about how *beit din* should respond to irreducible factual uncertainty in monetary matters. In the famous opening case of this *masechet* — *shnayim ochzin b'tallit*, two claimants each holding a garment — that same tension was already operative. There the *mishna*'s ruling of *yachloku im shevu'ah* was motivated more by the *rabanan*'s framework of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* than by Sumchus's *cholkin b'lo shevu'ah*, even as Sumchus hovered in the background as the theoretical alternative. As we reach the end of Bava Metzia, that contrast remains very much alive, shaping the resolution of disputes wherever the facts cannot be determined with certainty.
