# Chagigah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'ilah & Hair's Breadth

### Me'ilah Intro

The daf for today is 11, with which we will, God willing, finish the first *perek* and move on to the second. We pick up with the question of in what way *me'ilah* is one of the things described as a mountain suspended by a hair's breadth. Rather than underscoring the absence of a good *pasuk* to base certain laws on, the *gemara* here emphasizes that some of these laws seem to be suspended on very little in terms of rational basis. The challenge is to identify which of them truly exemplify that quality.

### Beam Case

The *gemara* opens with the phrase *ela misefa* — "rather, from the end" — at three lines from the top of 11a. We have already discussed the *teshuvah* concerning somebody who takes a beam from *hekdesh* and does not commit *me'ilah* by that act alone, but does commit *me'ilah* when he hands it to his friend. That seems arbitrary: if you take it from *hekdesh*, why have you not yet transgressed? The *gemara* resolves this by noting that perhaps the person was himself the *gizbar*, the Temple treasurer, and therefore until he actually transfers it to someone else he has not truly removed it from *hekdesh*. So the real difficulty, says the *gemara*, comes not from that case but from the end of the *mishnah*.

### Building Beam

The *mishnah* states: *bena'ah betoch beito harei zeh lo maal ad sheyadur tachteha bishveh prutah* — if someone takes this beam from *hekdesh* and builds it into his house, he still does not commit *me'ilah* until he actually lives under it and derives a *prutah*'s worth of benefit. The difficulty is plain: why is the act of building the beam into the house not itself an act of misappropriation? *Shinuy kanah* — transforming the object — is ordinarily a mode of acquiring ownership. So asks the *gemara*, *mai irya dar mai irya lo dar* — what difference does it make whether or not he actually lived under it? The *gemara* itself identifies this as the paradigm case: *haino k'harim hamitlin besa'ara* — this is indeed like a mountain suspended by a hair. The "hair" here is of the rational variety, not necessarily a textual one.

### Rava's Explanation

The *gemara* pushes back: *umai kushya*, what exactly is the difficulty? Perhaps we can explain the case according to *Rava*'s reading. *De'amar Rava kegon sheheniachah pi aruba* — Rava explains that the person did not nail the beam in but rather rested it like *sechach* across a skylight. In that case, no *shinuy* has yet occurred. *Ei dar bah in* — only if he actually lives under it and benefits from it has he committed *me'ilah*; *ei lo dar bah lo* — otherwise, he has done nothing actionable, having merely rested the beam on top of the skylight.

The *gemara* then returns to *Rava*'s earlier explanation of a different case that seemed equally difficult to rationalize: where the *baal habayit* sent a *shaliach* to spend money and then realized mid-transaction that he had mistakenly given him *hekdesh* funds, thereby effectively revoking the agency. At that moment the *shaliach* is no longer his representative, yet the *shaliach* commits *me'ilah* despite being entirely unaware of any problem. The *gemara* presses: *hatam mida yadi d'ika zuzei d'Hekdesh* — in the standard case of *me'ilah*, the person knows he has sanctified funds in the house; *iba'ei lei le'iyuni* — he is obliged to pay attention, and if he accidentally uses the wrong funds, his unawareness does not absolve him because he was negligent. But here — *hacha mi yada* — how could the poor *shaliach* have known? Every time someone hands you money to spend, are you supposed to inquire whether it might be *hekdesh*? He had no basis for suspicion, yet he has committed *me'ilah* because of the sender's mistake and a mid-course change of heart. *Haino k'harim hamitlin besa'ara* — this is why it qualifies as a mountain on a hair's breadth. And notably, what makes this case so striking is not merely the absence of a supporting verse — it is that the law is not rationally defensible either. Had the *gemara* been able to point to an explicit verse, one could accept the result however counterintuitive. But when the law is both irrational and textually thin, its connection to any biblical source is shown to be genuinely fragile.

## Harim Hatluim

### Definition

Following the discussion of *me'ilah*, the *gemara* presents what appears to be a clarification of the category *harim hatluim besearah* — mountains suspended by a hair. The next line introduces the principle: *mikra muat vehalachot merubot* — a very small number of verses and a very large number of laws that grow from them. The "hair" of the metaphor, in other words, often refers to the thinness of textual support rather than strictly rational support.

### Few Verses Many Laws

A *braita* expands on the list in the *mishnah*, which had mentioned *chagigah*, *me'ilah*, and *shabbos* as examples of *harim hatluim besearah*. The *braita* adds *negaim ve'ohalos* — the laws of scaled disease and the *tumah* transmitted through shared overhead space — as further instances of *mikra muat vehalachot merubot*: few verses and an enormous body of laws.

### Negaim Opposite

This characterization immediately raises a difficulty. It is hard to say that *negaim* has few verses — *Parshat Tazria u'Metzora* is substantial. The *gemara* presses: *negaim mikra muat* — *negaim* has a small number of verses? *Negaim mikra merubah hu* — on the contrary, *negaim* has many verses! One cannot accurately say it is a case of few verses and many laws, even if the laws are disproportionately elaborate relative to the verses. *Amar Rav Pappa hachi ka'amar* — Rav Pappa explains that what the *braita* actually means is the reverse: *negaim mikra merubah vehalachot muatot* — *negaim* has many verses and, relatively speaking, few *halachot*. This too is initially surprising — there is an entire *masechet* devoted to *negaim*. But the *gemara*'s point seems to be proportionality: we normally expect *halachot* to proliferate far beyond the biblical verses that anchor them (consider, for example, that a single verse about *get* generates an entire *masechet* of *Gittin*). By contrast, *negaim* is roughly proportional — its legal elaborations track its verses in a way that is atypical. *Ohalos*, on the other hand, fits the standard pattern precisely: *mikra muat vehalachot merubot* — the verses about *tumah* transmitted through shared overhead space are relatively few, yet the resulting *halachot* are vast.

### Practical Application

One might ask: what is the practical value of distinguishing these two categories? The *gemara* poses exactly this question — *umai nafka mina* — and answers with a guide to research. *Ei mistapka lach milta benegaim* — if you have a question about *negaim*, *ayein bekrayei* — go look at the verses, because the verses themselves will carry you most of the way. The *pesukim* are rich enough and clear enough that a careful reading of them provides genuine halachic orientation. *Ve'ei mistapka lach milta be'ohalos* — but if your question is about *ohalos*, *ayein be'matnitin* — go look in the *mishnayot*, because the verses will leave you entirely at sea. The operative knowledge lives in the oral tradition.

### Avodah Zarah Tangent

This principle — that in some areas one looks to the text while in others the *mishnayot* are the primary resource — calls to mind a striking parallel from *Masechet Avodah Zarah*. In all the *mishnayot* and *gemara* of *Avodah Zarah*, one finds complete silence on a topic that is in fact a recurring theme throughout *Sefer Devarim*: the obligation to destroy *avodah zarah*. All of the many concerns about *avodah zarah* are represented in *Shas* — its prohibition of benefit, the laws relating to non-Jews and idolatry, and much else — but the *mitzvah* of actively destroying *avodah zarah*, which receives verse after verse in *Devarim*, has essentially no rabbinic legal elaboration whatsoever. Scholars have pointed out the obvious reason: if one is living in *Eretz Yisrael* under Roman rule, surrounded by statues at every turn, one does not publicly elaborate a *mitzvah* to destroy them, lest someone take it into his head to act on it.

When a student once pressed the claim that surely *somewhere* the *gemara* must discuss this, the response was to open the *Sefer HaChinuch* to that *mitzvah* in *Devarim*. After presenting the *mitzvah*, the *Sefer HaChinuch* arrives at the section *mi-dinei ha-mitzvah* — the legal details of the *mitzvah* — and says simply: *mi-dinei ha-mitzvah kulam meforashim ba-katuv*. All the laws are explained in the text itself. There are no *gemarot* to cite because there are none. This is the limiting case of *ayein bekra* — an area where there is essentially no *Torah she'be'al peh* at all, and everything is in the verses.

## Three Categories

### Three Categories Intro

The *gemara* now turns to the third category identified in the *mishnah*: *dinim*, *avodot*, *taharot*, and *arayot*, which are described as *yesh lahem mah she'yismochu* — they have something to support themselves on. This rounds out the three-tier structure: laws like *heter nedarim* fly in mid-air with nothing supporting them; the *harim hatluim besearah* have a thin thread of support; and this final category has genuine grounding in the biblical text. Yet even here, *Rashi* notes that the phrase *yesh al mah she'yismochu* — they have *what* to lean on — does not sound like a claim of full, explicit support. It sounds like adequate but not unambiguous support. Accordingly, the *gemara* will challenge each item on the list, asking: what do you mean this merely "has support"? Isn't it fully explicit?

### Dinim & Eye/Eye

The *gemara* opens with *dinim* — laws and judgments, meaning *dinei mammonot*. The obvious response is *michtav ketivan* — it's written out! *Parshat Mishpatim* is an entire *parasha* of civil law. The *gemara* concedes the point but says: *lo nitzrecha ela le'khid'rabbi* — the category is needed specifically for what *Rabbi* derives. *Rabbi* addresses the verse *nefesh tachat nafesh* — a soul for a soul — in the context of a person who, intending to strike his fellow, accidentally strikes a pregnant woman, who subsequently dies. The verse says *v'natata nefesh tachat nafesh* — you shall give a soul for a soul. The question is whether this means actual execution or monetary compensation.

*Rabbi* argues: *ne'emar netina lemata v'ne'emar netina lemala* — the same verb of "giving" appears in both places. Above, the verse speaks of a monetary fine paid through the court for the miscarriage; therefore, *ma netina ha'amura lemala mamon af kan mamon* — just as the payment for the miscarriage is monetary, so too the "soul for a soul" means a monetary payment, not execution. This is not the plain reading of the verse. Indeed, some Bible scholars have argued — pointing to parallels in the Code of Hammurabi — that "eye for an eye, tooth for a tooth" was a stock phrase in the ancient Near East connoting equal justice rather than literal retaliation. Whatever the philological history, the *gemara*'s point is clear: the derivation of monetary compensation from a verse that reads "a soul for a soul" is not self-evident, and the law therefore has something to lean on but is not fully supported.

### Avodot Blood Catching

For *avodot* — the Temple service — the initial challenge is even stronger: *michtav k'siva* — it is written in detail! The first half of *Vayikra* is devoted almost entirely to the laws of sacrifice, and if anything has explicit biblical detail, it is the *avoda* in the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *gemara* responds: *lo nitzrecha ela l'halachos hadam* — the category is needed specifically for the laws concerning the blood service, and in particular for the act of catching the blood, *kabalat hadam*, which is not so explicitly mandated.

The *braita* derives: *v'hikrivu bnei Aharon* — "and the sons of Aharon shall bring [the blood] close." The Rabbis read this as referring to *kabalat hadam*, the catching of the blood in a basin. Yet the Torah uses here the language of *holacha*, conveying — *v'afka Rachmana bilshon holacha* — because elsewhere the same verb *v'hikriv* is used for the fat and the limbs in the sense of bringing them to the altar. The *gemara* notes that *holacha* is in fact an *avoda she'efshar l'vatla* — a service that could theoretically be omitted by slaughtering the animal directly adjacent to the *mizbeach*, catching the blood, and immediately throwing it, without ever performing the act of conveyance. One might have thought that because *holacha* is dispensable it lacks the full legal status of an *avoda* — that, for instance, a disqualifying intent of *pigul* formed during *holacha* would be inconsequential. The *gemara* teaches that this is not so: *holacha* is as much of an *avoda* as *kabalat hadam*, and the Torah's use of the conveying-language when discussing blood catching is precisely to teach that the act of conveyance is halachically equivalent to the act of catching. None of this is obvious from the verses, which is why the category of *avodot* belongs on the list of things that have support but are not fully explicit.

### Taharot Shiurim

For *taharot* — purity laws — the same initial challenge applies: *michtav ketivan*, these are extensively written. The laws of *tumah v'tahara* dominate large sections of the Torah, and many of their details are indeed fairly clear. The *gemara* responds: *lo nitzrecha ela l'shiur mikva delo ktiv* — the category is needed specifically for the minimum size of a *mikva*, which is not written explicitly.

The *braita* derives: *verachatz b'saro bamayim* — "and he shall wash his flesh in water." This is understood to mean immersion in *mei mikva*. The verse then says *et kol b'saro* — all of his flesh — which is taken to mean *mayim shekol gufo oleh bahen*, water that can contain his entire body. From this the Rabbis quantify: *ama al ama berum shalosh amot* — a space of approximately one-and-a-half by one-and-a-half by four-and-a-half feet — and this equals *arbaim se'ah*, forty *se'ah*, the minimum measure for a valid *mikva*. The specific figure of forty *se'ah* is not written anywhere; it follows from measurement and estimation, which is precisely why it falls into the category of "having support but not fully explicit."

The *gemara* then turns to *tumah* — becoming *tamei* — with the same initial challenge. The response: *lo nitzrecha ela k'adasha min hasheretz* — the category is needed specifically for the *shiur* of a lentil bean's worth of a rodent as the minimum amount that conveys *tumah*. This is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — not derivable from the text at all — which is precisely the *gemara*'s point.

The *braita* explains the derivation. The verse says *kol hanogea bahem* — anyone who touches "them," which might imply the entire carcass must be present. Another verse says *asher yipol mehem el tocho* — "of them" falls into a vessel, suggesting even a small portion suffices. The *gemara* reconciles: *ad sheyagia bemiktzato shehu kechulo* — a portion is sufficient, but it must be representative of the whole. The Rabbis then determine: *shiru chachamim k'adasha* — a lentil bean is the representative minimum, *sheken chomet*, because a particular type of rodent is born at precisely that size. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* adds that the lentil bean corresponds to the tail of a lizard — *keznav haleta'ah* — which, even after being severed, continues to move, demonstrating that it represents something of the whole organism. All of this is not found in the verse itself.

### Arayot Daughter

For *arayot* — forbidden sexual relations — the challenge is perhaps most pointed of all. If anything in the Torah is detailed, it is the laws of *giluy arayot* in the second half of *Vayikra*. Indeed, one might note that everything on the *gemara*'s list — *dinim* (*Parshat Mishpatim*), *avodot* (the first half of *Vayikra*), *taharot* and *arayot* (the second half of *Vayikra* along with other sections) — are precisely the areas that receive the most detailed biblical treatment. The *gemara* chooses to read the *mishnah* as saying that even these areas are only "somewhat supported," and the implication is significant: even the sections of the Torah that appear most fully elaborated cannot be read without the mediation of *Torah she'be'al peh*. The *chachamim* are making a claim that their interpretive authority extends not to the textually sparse areas alone but to the very places where one might think the text speaks for itself.

The *gemara* says: *nitzrecha l'vito me'anusato* — the category is needed for the case of a man's biological daughter from a woman he raped, on 11b. The Torah explicitly forbids *isha u'vitah* — a woman and her daughter — but that formulation refers specifically to the daughter of a man's wife, whether or not she is his biological daughter. When the Torah refers to biological descendants without reference to a man's wife, it mentions only granddaughter: *ervat bat bincha u'bat bitecha* — the nakedness of your son's daughter or your daughter's daughter. A man's biological daughter who is not his wife's daughter thus appears to go unmentioned, and a special derivation is required to establish the prohibition.

*De'amar Rava amar li Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi atya hena hena atya zima zima* — Rava, citing Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi, derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* from the words *hena hena* and *zima zima*, connecting the case of granddaughter to the case of wife-and-daughter so as to include the biological daughter in the prohibition. There are in fact two such derivations — one establishing the prohibition and one establishing the punishment — corresponding to the two textual parallels. The law is there, but it requires this kind of indirect derivation, which is exactly what "having support without being fully explicit" means.

### Hen Hen Gufei

With that, the *mishnah* closes by declaring: *hen hen gufei Torah* — these are the very essence, the body, of Torah. This final statement, taken in isolation, sounds as if it is elevating only the last category — the well-supported laws — as the true substance of Torah, while the earlier categories of mountains-on-a-hair and laws-in-mid-air are somehow less essential. This would be a dangerous conclusion.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *hani in hanach lo* — only this last category is real Torah, and not the earlier ones? *Ela eima hen ve'hen gufei Torah* — no: read it as "these and they are the essence of Torah." Everything in the *mishnah*, whether in the first category, the middle category, or the last, is equally the essence of Torah. The laws that fly in mid-air with no textual support, the mountains suspended by a hair, and the fully elaborated biblical laws — all are *gufei Torah*. This is the statement on which the first *perek* ends.

*Hadran alach kol ha'chayavin.*

## Transition Perek 2

### Mishnah Intro

Before turning to the second *perek*, it is worth asking what this entire *mishnah* — with its typology of halachic support — is doing in *Masechet Chagigah* at all. *Chagigah* is mentioned on the list, but it is hardly the most prominent example, and the discussion ranges far beyond it. Albeck, in his edition of the *mishnah*, suggests an answer: these two *mishnayot* — the one just concluded and the one we are about to read — together form a commentary on the previous *mishnah* of this *perek*, which described missing the opportunity to bring one's *korbanot* on the *regel* as a *me'uvat lo yukhal litkon*, a perversion that cannot be corrected, and which concluded with the view that this phrase applies specifically to a *talmid chacham she'piresh min haTorah* — a Torah scholar who has abandoned the Torah. These two *mishnayot*, on this reading, identify where that danger leads: on one side, a scholar who separates from Torah might come to question the mountains on a hair, the laws that have weak textual support; on the other side, he might enter the dangerous areas of inquiry described in the second *perek* without the guardrails that make such study safe. So the list of halachic categories and the restrictions on esoteric inquiry are both answers to the same question: what goes wrong when a scholar breaks from the tradition?

### Four Areas

The second *perek* opens: *ein dorshin ba'arayot be'shlosha ve'lo be'maaseh bereishit be'shnayim ve'lo be'merkavah be'yachid ela im ken hayah chacham u'mevin mi'da'ato* — one does not explicate *arayot* with three students; one does not discuss *maaseh bereishit* with two; one does not teach *maaseh merkavah* even to a single individual, unless that individual is wise and can work out most of it on his own.

*Kol hamistakel b'arba'ah devarim* — anyone who gazes into four areas: *mah l'ma'alah mah l'matah* — what is above and what is below — and *mah l'fanim mah l'achor* — what is in front and what is behind. Whether "in front" and "behind" refer to spatial directions or to before and after time — before creation and at the end of time — is a question the *gemara* will address. Anyone who explores these four areas without proper standing, *v'chol shelo chas al kvod kono* — who has not protected the honor of his Creator — *ratui lo shelo ba l'olam*, it would have been fitting for such a person never to have come into the world at all.

What precisely are the kinds of concerns animating these restrictions? The Artscroll *mishnah* notes that the Gnostics were particularly focused on exactly these areas: they sought to explain the inner dynamics of creation and also, apparently, attacked the integrity of the *avot* by pointing to their apparent transgressions of *issurei arayot* — *Yaakov* married two sisters, and there are other such critiques. The *mishnah* may thus be responding to a specific intellectual environment in which such inquiries were the mark of the highest-level religious initiate, and the Rabbis are inverting that hierarchy entirely: these are not the pinnacle of religious pursuit; they are the areas of maximum danger to the honor of the Creator.

## Arayot Students

### Student Count

The *gemara* now addresses the clause *ein dorshin ba'arayot be'shlosha* and raises a question about how to count. Initially the *gemara* assumes that the number three includes the teacher. This creates an absurdity when we reach the clause *ve'lo be'merkavah be'yachid* — if one includes the teacher and thus cannot teach *merkavah* even to one student alone, then essentially no one can ever teach *maaseh merkavah* at all, since there would be no permissible arrangement. The *gemara* resolves this: *hachi ka'amar* — the *mishnah* means the following: *ein dorshin ba'arayot lo be'shloshah* — not to three students; *ve'lo be'maaseh bereishit lishnayim* — not to two students; *ve'lo be'merkavah l'yachid* — not even to one individual student, unless the student can largely work it out himself. The number in each case counts students, not the total group including the teacher.

### Verse Attempts

The *gemara* then asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the source for the specific limit of three students for *arayot*? The first attempt: *ish ish el kol shear besaro* — "each man to all his close relatives" — contains the double *ish ish*, which could be read as two people, and *shear besaro* as a third, making three. And *lo tikrevu l'galot ervah* — "you shall not come close to uncover *ervah*" — is then read as: do not expose *arayot* before three. This is a homiletic reading in which *l'galot* (to uncover, to reveal) is applied to the act of teaching.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: the same *ish ish* formulation appears elsewhere — *ish ish ki yekalel elohav* (any man who curses his God) and *ve'ish ish asher yiten mizaro l'Molech* (any man who gives his child to *Molech*). Would we similarly restrict teaching laws of blasphemy or *avodah zarah* to groups of two? The *gemara* responds that in those verses, *ish ish* carries a different *derasha* altogether: *lerabot et hanochrim* — to include non-Jews, who are also bound by these prohibitions as part of the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*. So *ish ish* in those contexts means "any person," and the same must be true in the *arayot* context. That removes the textual basis for the restriction.

A second attempt: *ushmartem et mishmarti* — "you shall observe my safeguard" — said in the context of *arayot*. *Ushmartem* is plural, suggesting two people; *mishmarti* is singular, adding one more — three. And *l'vilti asot mechukkot hato'evot* — not to act according to the practices of those peoples — implies that if you have three people you must be careful not to let the teaching go astray. The *gemara* again challenges: *ushmartem* appears in many contexts — *ushmartem et haShabbat*, *ushmartem et hamitzvot*, *ushmartem et mishmeret hakodesh*. Would we apply the same numeric restriction there? This verse too cannot serve as the basis.

### Subtlety Focus

*Ella amar Rav Ashi* — Rav Ashi offers the definitive resolution. *Mai ein dorshin ba'arayot be'shlosha* — what does the *mishnah*'s restriction actually mean? *Ein dorshin be'sitrei arayot* — it does not mean that one may not teach the straightforward *halachot* of *arayot* at all (which would be perverse, since people need to know those laws in order to observe them). Rather, the restriction applies specifically to the subtle, hidden, non-obvious dimensions of the laws — the *sitrei*, the covered-over aspects. And indeed, *l'galot* now reads quite naturally: it is specifically what is *galui*, revealed but not obvious, that must not be disclosed to a group of three.

### Sexual Desire Psychology

*Mai ta'ama* — but why? Here the *gemara* abandons the search for a verse and says simply: *svara hu* — it is a matter of rational understanding. *Bei terei ki yasvi kamei ravaihu* — if there are two students sitting before their *rebbe*, *chad shakil v'tari bahadi rabai* — one will be engaged in direct conversation with the teacher, *v'idach matzli udneih l'gemara* — and the other will simply be listening, attentive to every word. But *ba'telasa* — with three students — *chad shakil v'tari bahadi rabai v'hanach terei shaklu v'taru bahadi hadadi* — one engages the teacher while the other two will fall into a side conversation of their own. They will mishear what is being taught.

One might argue this risk applies to any halachic topic. The *gemara* addresses this directly: *arayot shani* — the laws of forbidden relations are different. *De'amar mar gezel v'arayot nafsho mechmadtan u'mitavah lahen* — when it comes to theft and to sexual desire, a person's soul craves these and lusts after them. The implication is that subconscious desire can distort perception: even a well-intentioned student may, without awareness, mishear a subtle halachic point if his desire is already engaged in that direction.

But why does the same logic not apply to the laws of theft? Here the *gemara* draws a sharp psychological distinction. *Arayot bein befanav bein shelo befanav nafish yitzreih* — sexual desire is present whether or not the object of desire is before the person. In a classroom, the student is not confronted with the object of his desire, yet that desire remains active. *Gezel lo — befanav nafish yitzreih shelo befanav lo nafish yitzreih* — the desire to possess, to take what is not one's own, is primarily activated by confrontation with the desired object. Away from it, the desire is largely dormant. In a study session, the student is not looking at the Porsche he covets; the relevant desire simply is not functioning at that moment. Sexual desire, by contrast, operates independently of the immediate presence of its object. Therefore, specifically in the area of *arayot*, one must be maximally careful that even subtle points are understood precisely, and that requires limiting the group to no more than two students at a time.

## Maaseh Bereishit

### Creation Verses

Having addressed the restriction on *arayot*, the *mishnah* turns to *maaseh bereishit* — the acts of creation — which may be taught to only one student at a time. *Mina hanei mili* — where is this derived? The *braita* cites: *ki she'al na l'yamim rishonim* — "if you will but ask of earlier days." *Yachid sho'el* — an individual may ask about the earlier days; *v'ein shnayim sho'alin* — but two may not. One cannot be taught *maaseh bereishit* in a group even of two students.

### Spatial Boundaries

The *braita* further delimits what may be asked even by a single individual. *Yakhol yishal adam kodem shenivra olam* — might one ask about what preceded the world's creation? *Talmud lomar* — the verse says: from the day that God created man on the earth. This limits the scope: one may inquire from the moment of creation, but not about what, if anything, preceded it. Conversely, might one think that inquiry is restricted to the period since *Adam* was created, excluding the first five days? *Talmud lomar l'yamim rishonim asher hayu lefanecha* — "the earlier days that were before you" permits inquiry back to the beginning of creation, before human beings.

So the temporal scope of legitimate inquiry runs from the beginning of creation through the present. And what are the spatial limits? *Ulmiktzeh hashamayim v'ad ketzeh hashamayim* — from one end of the heaven to the other. *Ata sho'el mimiktzeh hashamayim v'ad ketzeh hashamayim* — you may ask about everything within that span, *ve'ee ata sho'el mah l'ma'alah mah l'matah mah lifnim mah l'achor* — but you may not ask what is above the heavens, below the earth, before creation, or after the end. These questions — what lies outside the known universe, what preceded creation, what follows its end — are inadmissible. The analogy to modern cosmology is apt: the question of what lies "outside" curved, finite space is not an empirically admissible question, and the *gemara* takes a structurally parallel position with respect to theological inquiry.

### Adam Size Midrash

The *gemara* notes that once we have established the verse *l'min hayom asher bara Elokim adam al ha'aretz u'lemiktzeh hashamayim* as the source for the legitimate spatial boundaries of inquiry, the temporal verse — which limits questions to starting from the day of creation — might seem redundant. The *gemara* responds that the second verse carries an independent teaching, attributed to Rabbi Elazar: *adam harishon min ha'aretz ad larakia* — the first human being was of immense stature, spanning from the earth to the heavens. The verse is read as: "God created man *on the earth* [and he reached] *to the edge of the heavens*."

*V'kevan she'sarach heniach Hakadosh Baruch Hu yado alav u'mi'ato* — once *Adam* sinned with the *Etz HaDa'at*, God placed His hand upon him and diminished him. The verse from Psalms is cited: *achor vakedem tzartani* — front and back You have formed me — and *vatashet alai kapecha* — and You placed Your hand upon me. The reading: there was an original, vast form, and after the sin a diminished one, with the divine hand as the instrument of that reduction. This is clearly capable of being understood as a metaphor for the greatness of the original human potential and its curtailment through sin.

### Alternative Reading

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — Rav Yehuda further reports in the name of Rav: *adam harishon misof ha'olam v'ad sofo haya* — Adam did not merely span from earth to heaven vertically; laid horizontally, he would have stretched from one edge of the world to the other. The same verse is cited: *l'min hayom asher bara Elokim adam al ha'aretz u'lemiktzeh hashamayim v'ad ketzeh hashamayim* — from when God created man on the earth, from one edge of the heavens to the other. Again, once he sinned, *heniach Hakadosh Baruch Hu yado alav u'mi'ato*, God placed His hand on him and diminished him, *vatashet alai kapecha*.

This raises an apparent contradiction: one tradition says Adam spanned from earth to heaven (vertical), and the other says from one edge of the world to the other (horizontal). The *gemara* resolves: *idi v'eidi chad shiura hu* — these describe the same magnitude. The distance from the earth to the heavens is equal to the distance from one edge of the heavens to the other. There is no contradiction; both statements describe the same extraordinary original stature of the first human being.
