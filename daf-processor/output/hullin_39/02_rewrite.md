# Hullin 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Non-Jew Intent

### Mishnah Overview

The daf begins at the third line from the top, at the last word of the line, with the word *itmar*. Before the *gemara* introduces its new case, it is worth reviewing the dispute that animated the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* addresses a scenario in which a *shochet* slaughters an animal on behalf of a non-Jew, and the question arises whether we must be concerned that the non-Jew intends to use the animal for *avodah zarah*. There are in fact two sub-questions embedded here: first, whether we are required to suspect that the non-Jew harbors such an intent at all; and second, even granting that he does, whether that intent would have any bearing on the status of the slaughtered animal.

Rabbi Elazar is concerned about both questions. The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, are not concerned with either — either because we do not assume the non-Jew is thinking about *avodah zarah* in the first place, or because even if he is, the thought of a bystander is simply irrelevant to the *shechitah*. Only the act and intent of the one actually performing the *shechitah* defines it. As the *gemara* elsewhere states: *zeh mechashev ve-zeh oved lo aminan* — we do not say that this one is thinking while the other one is doing the *avodah*, and thereby treat the *shechitah* as though it were performed for idolatry.

### Censorship History

A brief historical note is in order regarding the terminology of our *mishnah*. The text as it appears in our printed editions reads *hashochet l'oved kochavim* — one who slaughters for an idolater — and Rabbi Elazar's statement is rendered as *stam machsheves oved kochavim l'avodas kochavim*, the standard thought of an idolater is directed toward idolatry. This terminology, however, is the product of medieval self-censorship. During the Middle Ages, Jewish apostates brought the attention of Church authorities to passages in the *gemara* that spoke disparagingly of non-Jews. In response, Jewish communities systematically replaced every instance of *goy* or *nochri* — neutral terms simply denoting a non-Jew — with *oved kochavim* or *akum*, terms meaning "star worshiper" or "idolater," so that it could be argued these passages referred only to ancient pagans and not to contemporary Christians. The original text of the *mishnah*, however, read *hashochet l'nochri* — one who slaughters for a *nochri* — and Rabbi Elazar's statement was *stam machsheves nochri l'avodas kochavim*: the standard thought of a non-Jew, in Rabbi Elazar's view, is directed toward *avodah zarah*. The implication is significant: Rabbi Elazar's suspicion extended to all non-Jews categorically, not only to confirmed idolaters.

### Core Question

The fundamental issue underlying the *mishnah* debate is whether the mental state of someone other than the *shochet* can define or invalidate the act of *shechitah*. According to the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosi, the answer is no — the *shechitah* is defined exclusively by the one performing it. The thoughts of the non-Jewish owner, however sinister, cannot redefine an otherwise valid act of slaughter.

## Shochet's Intent

### New Scenario Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a substantially different case. The *mishnah* dealt with a scenario in which a different person — the non-Jewish owner — was harboring the idolatrous thought, and the *shochet* himself was performing a standard *shechitah*. The new case presented with the word *itmar* shifts the picture: now it is the *shochet* himself who holds the intent. Specifically: *hashochet es habeheimah l'izrok dama l'avodas kochavim* — one who slaughters an animal with the plan to take its blood afterward and sprinkle it on the altar of an idol — or, alternatively, *l'haktir chelba l'avodas kochavim*, to burn its fat as an offering to *avodah zarah*. In this case we know exactly what the *shochet* is thinking, because it is the *shochet* himself who harbors the intent. And critically, this is a Jew, not a non-Jew.

### Shechitah vs Future Use

The conceptual difficulty here requires careful attention. There are two distinct acts being contemplated. The first is the *shechitah* itself, performed in a slaughterhouse in the normal way — the animal is not being slaughtered in front of an idol, and the act of slaughter is not itself conceived as a ritual of worship. The second is a planned future act: the *shochet* intends to collect the blood and bring it to the temple of a pagan deity, there to perform a *zrikas hadam* — a sprinkling of the blood — as an act of idolatrous service.

The question is whether the planned future use of the blood retroactively colors or redefines the present act of *shechitah*. We know that if the *shechitah* itself were performed in front of an idol as an act of ritual worship, it would constitute *avodah zarah*. We know that when the blood is actually sprinkled at the idol's altar, that act will itself be *avodah zarah*. But the *shechitah* in the slaughterhouse — performed for the purpose of obtaining the blood for that future sprinkling — does that act become an act of idolatrous service? Does the future plan redefine the current *shechitah*?

### Rabbi Yochanan

*Rabbi Yochanan amar psulah* — Rabbi Yochanan rules that the animal is *pasul*. It is considered as though it were slaughtered for *avodah zarah*, and something slaughtered or offered to an idol is called *zivchei meitim* and is forbidden in *hana'ah* — forbidden to derive any benefit from. Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning: *machshavin me'avodah l'avodah* — thoughts during one ritual act carry significance with respect to another ritual act — *v'afinan chutz mipnim*, and we learn this principle from inside the *beis haMikdash* and apply it outside. As Rashi explains, this is precisely the model of *pigul*: you slaughter the animal intending to sprinkle the blood or eat the meat at the wrong time; you are performing one *avodah* while planning for another. That is the classic *pigul* scenario. Rabbi Yochanan maintains that this model can be extended — both beyond *pigul* specifically and beyond the *beis haMikdash* entirely — to cases of *avodah zarah*.

### Reish Lakish

*Reish Lakish amar mutar* — Reish Lakish rules that the meat is permitted. One has plans for *avodah zarah*, but one has not yet performed an act of *avodah zarah*, and therefore the meat is unaffected. His reasoning: *ein machshavin me'avodah l'avodah* — thought on one *avodah* does not affect another — *d'lo gaminan chutz mipnim*, because we do not extrapolate principles from inside the *beis haMikdash* to apply outside it. The model of *pigul* is confined to its own domain and cannot be imported wholesale into cases of *avodah zarah* in the world at large.

It bears emphasis that the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is framed entirely around the status of the meat, not around the question of personal culpability for worshipping *avodah zarah*. One could separately analyze whether the *shochet* has committed the transgression of *avodah zarah* — whether he has already "worshipped" by harboring and acting upon such plans — but that question of personal liability is not the present focus. The *gemara* here is concerned solely with whether the act has defined the meat as *tikroves Avodah Zarah*.

## Pigul Model

### Pigul Definition

Rabbi Yochanan's position rests on the model of *pigul*. *pigul* in the *beis haMikdash* arises when a *kohen* performs one of the four *avodot* — *shechitah*, *kabala*, *holacha*, or *zerika* — with the intention of performing a subsequent *avodah* at the wrong time, or of eating the meat outside its permitted time or location. The paradigmatic case: one performs the *shechitah* while planning to sprinkle the blood or consume the meat beyond the allotted time. The crucial feature is that you are doing one *avodah* while planning a future *avodah* improperly. Everyone understands this to be the structure of *pigul*. The question Rabbi Yochanan poses is whether this structure can be extracted from the *beis haMikdash* context and applied elsewhere.

### Learning Outside Temple

Rabbi Yochanan answers yes — the principle that thought during one *avodah* can render invalid based on plans for another *avodah* is exportable. Reish Lakish says no — *d'lo gaminan chutz mipnim*, we do not learn outside from inside. This, as a student perceptively noted, is in substance the same debate that animates the *mishnah* itself, where the *chachamim* in effect reject Rabbi Eliezer's willingness to apply *beis haMikdash* logic to the world outside. Whatever model underlies *pigul* — whether it is the power of *kavanah* to change status, or the redefinition of the present act — the rules governing *pigul* might simply be different from those governing *avodah zarah*.

This objection retains its force, though it becomes somewhat more tractable if Rabbi Yochanan is understood to be arguing only about meat status and not about personal transgression. If we are not asking what constitutes a full act of *avodah zarah* sufficient to render one liable, but merely asking what categories of thought can affect the status of slaughtered meat, then there is at least a plausible common ground: within the domain of acts that partake of sacrificial ritual — whether *kodshim* in the *beis haMikdash* or idolatrous sacrifice outside it — intention plays a structuring role in determining the status of what is being done. *avodah zarah* is, after all, its own form of sacrificial ritual, even if it is not *kodshim* and the actor is not a *kohen*. And it is precisely because *avodah zarah* has this sacrificial character that Rabbi Yochanan is willing to invoke the *pigul* framework here, while he would never apply it to, say, someone writing a *sefer torah* who plans to write a future divine name *shelo lishma*.

### Intent Redefines Act

This brings us to a deeper conceptual question that lies at the heart of Rabbi Yochanan's position. When we speak of *kavanah* — intention — playing a role in *halacha*, there are two fundamentally different ways to understand what is happening. The first possibility is that *kavanah* redefines the act itself: because you are planning to sprinkle the blood for *avodah zarah* as you perform the *shechitah*, what you are doing right now is itself an act of idolatrous service — it is part of a process of serving the idol, and that redefines the present act as *avodah zarah*. The second possibility is that *kavanah* has its own independent power to change the status of the meat, distinct from any redefinition of the act itself.

These two readings have significant implications. If *kavanah* redefines the act, then Rabbi Yochanan could equally say that the *shochet* has already committed the transgression of *avodah zarah* and is liable. If *kavanah* merely changes meat status without redefining the act, then the meat is affected but the personal transgression may not yet have occurred.

### Tosafot: Act Redefined

Tosafot comes down clearly on the side of the first reading — that *kavanah* redefines the act. In the *tosafot* beginning *reish lakish*, three lines before the word *ela* that opens the next large *tosafot*, Tosafot writes: *dehacha afilu yaduah vaday shelo yizrok* — even if we know with certainty that in the end the *shochet* will never actually succeed in sprinkling the blood, he will never complete that planned act of *avodah zarah* — nevertheless, *dechashiv avodat kochavim bishchita zo mah sheshochet al menat lizrok* — since his plan was to slaughter in order to sprinkle, the *shechitah* itself is counted as an act of *avodah zarah*. It is not the eventual completion of the blood-sprinkling that makes the meat forbidden; it is the fact that the current *shechitah*, defined by the *shochet*'s present intent, is itself an act of service to the idol.

This reading makes Rabbi Yochanan's position both more coherent and more far-reaching. On this account, the *shochet* has not merely contaminated the meat's status — he has performed an act of *avodah zarah* in the very act of *shechitah*, simply by virtue of what he intends to do with the blood. This is a striking position, but it flows naturally from treating the *pigul* model as one of act-redefinition: just as *pigul* thought transforms an otherwise valid *shechitah* into a disqualified one by assigning it a new character through the *shochet*'s future-directed intent, so too here the future-directed idolatrous intent transforms the *shechitah* into an act of idol worship.

## Shechitah L'Shem Intent

### Proper Intent, Wrong Fut…

The *gemara* now notes *ve'azdu leta'ameihu* — they follow their respective lines of reasoning — and introduces a parallel dispute. The case: a *kohen* performs *shechitah* on a *korban olah* properly, with the correct *lishma* intent — this is an *olah* and he is slaughtering it as an *olah*. However, as he performs the *shechitah* he says to himself: when I get around to sprinkling the blood, I am going to sprinkle it *leshem chatas* — with the intent appropriate for a *chatas*, not an *olah*. He has the right intent now, during *shechitah*, but plans a wrong intent for a future *avodah*. Does that future plan of *shelo lishma* affect the present?

### Parallel Opinions

*Rabbi Yochanan amar psulah* — it is invalid. *reish lakish amar kesheira* — it is kosher. The structure of the debate is identical to the *avodah zarah* case. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *machshavin me'avodah l'avodah* — thought from one *avodah* affects another — *v'gamrinan m'machsheves pigul*: we derive this from the *pigul* paradigm. If *pigul* teaches us that planning a wrong act at a future *avodah* invalidates the present one, then the same should be true when the future wrong intent is *shelo lishma* rather than a *pigul*-type wrong-time intent. Reish Lakish holds that *ein machshavin me'avodah l'avodah v'lo gamrinan m'machsheves pigul* — *pigul* is its own category and cannot be extended.

### Extending Pigul Model

What this debate makes vivid is the full extent of Rabbi Yochanan's position. He begins with the *pigul* paradigm and takes it in two successive directions. First, he extends it within the *beis haMikdash* to non-*pigul* intent problems — a plan of *shelo lishma* at a future *avodah*, which is a different type of disqualifying thought altogether. Second, as we saw in the first case, he extends it entirely outside the *beis haMikdash* to the realm of *avodah zarah*. Each step is itself a *chiddush*, and Reish Lakish rejects both.

## Why Two Cases Needed

### Chutz M'Penim Issue

The *gemara* now explains why it was necessary to record both disputes rather than stating one and deriving the other. *u'tzricha* — both are needed.

### Excusing Reish Lakish

*D'ei itmar b'ha* — had the *gemara* recorded only the debate about *avodah zarah* and the intent to sprinkle blood for idols, we would have readily understood why Reish Lakish disagrees. *b'ha ka'amar reish lakish mishum d'chutz mipnim lo yalfinan* — in that case, Reish Lakish's rejection is grounded in the most obvious objection: how can you learn principles from the *beis haMikdash* and apply them to *avodah zarah* outside it? *chutz mipnim lo yalfinan* — we do not extrapolate outside from inside. The two domains are categorically different. But we might then have thought: *aval pnim pnim* — when both cases are within the *beis haMikdash*, when you are merely dealing with two different disqualifying thoughts both pertaining to *korbanos*, *eima modi lei l'Rabbi Yochanan* — perhaps Reish Lakish would concede to Rabbi Yochanan. Without the second case, we would not know that Reish Lakish maintains his position even within the *beis haMikdash* itself.

### Excusing Yochanan

Conversely, *v'ei itmar b'ha* — had the *gemara* recorded only the debate about *shelo lishma* within the *beis haMikdash*, we would have understood why Rabbi Yochanan holds the *shechitah* invalid: he is drawing on the *pigul* model and applying it to a closely analogous case of wrong intent, still within *kodshim*. But we might then have thought: *aval b'ha* — when it comes to the *avodah zarah* case, which has nothing to do with a *korban* at all, *eima modi lei l'Reish Lakish* — perhaps Rabbi Yochanan would concede to Reish Lakish that thoughts are irrelevant there. Without recording the first dispute, we would not know that Rabbi Yochanan extends his principle even to *avodah zarah* outside the *beis haMikdash*. Hence, *tzricha* — both cases are necessary.

## Kal V'Chomer Argument

### Kal V'Chomer Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a *kal va'chomer* posed by Rav Yosef, directed at the logic underlying the *mishnah*. Rav Yosef argues as follows: *u-mah be-makom she-machshava poselet be-mukdashin* — in the realm of *korbanos*, where thoughts do have the power to invalidate — *ein ha-kol holech ela achar ha-oved* — nevertheless, it is only the thoughts of the one performing the *avodah*, the *shochet*, that matter. The owner's thoughts are irrelevant even in the more stringent realm of *kodshim*. *makom she-ein machshava poselet be-chullin* — in non-sacrificial contexts, where thoughts have even less power to invalidate — *eino din she-lo yehei ha-kol holech ela achar ha-shochet?* — how much more so should it be that only the *shochet*'s thoughts matter, and not the thoughts of the non-Jewish owner?

### Thoughts Invalidate Deba…

The *gemara* then pauses to interrogate the assumption built into Rav Yosef's *kal va'chomer* — namely, that thoughts do not invalidate in *chullin*. *mai ein machshava poselet be-chullin?* — what exactly does Rav Yosef mean when he says thoughts do not invalidate by non-sanctified meat? If he means thoughts *never* invalidate in *chullin*, that cannot be right: *ela zoveach le'avodah zarah de-mitasra heichi mashkachat lah?* — how would we ever find a case of slaughtering for *avodah zarah* in which the meat becomes forbidden? The entire category of *tikroves Avodah Zarah* — things offered to an idol, which are forbidden in *hana'ah* — presupposes that thought or intent can affect the status of meat outside the *beis haMikdash*. So thoughts clearly do sometimes invalidate in *chullin*.

The more intuitive reading of Rav Yosef's statement would be that thoughts invalidate in a *much wider range* of scenarios in *kodshim* — *pigul*, *shelo lishma*, *shelo lishem ba'alim*, and more — while outside the *beis haMikdash*, only a thought of *avodah zarah* has this power. This would straightforwardly explain what Rav Yosef means by saying thoughts are more potent *bifnim* than *bachutz*. The *gemara*, however, does not take this path.

### Avoda L'Avoda Reread

Instead, the *gemara* chooses to read Rav Yosef's statement as follows: *ela peshita me'avodah l'avodah* — what Rav Yosef means is that thoughts from one *avodah* to another do not invalidate in *chullin*. The restatement of the *kal va'chomer* then becomes: *u-mah be-makom she-machshava poselet be-mukdashin me'avodah l'avodah* — in *kodshim*, where thoughts even across different *avodot* have the power to invalidate, as in *pigul* — *ein ha-kol holech ela achar ha-oved* — even there only the *shochet*'s thoughts matter. *makom she-ein machshava poselet be-chullin me'avodah l'avodah* — where, outside the *beis haMikdash*, a thought about a future *avodah* does not invalidate — *eino din she-lo yehei ha-kol holech ela achar ha-shochet?*

On this reading, Rav Yosef is specifically saying that *avoda l'avoda* — thought at one *avodah* about another — does not carry weight outside the *beis haMikdash*. And this is, as the *gemara* then points out, a direct contradiction of Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that *avoda l'avoda* does matter even outside the *beis haMikdash* when the future *avodah* is *avodah zarah*.

The *gemara* therefore announces: *pnim kasha l'Reish Lakish* — the implication that *avoda l'avoda* works *inside* the *beis haMikdash* across the board contradicts Reish Lakish, who holds it applies only in *pigul* and not in other intent-problems; *chutz kasha l'Rabbi Yochanan* — the implication that *avoda l'avoda* does not work outside the *beis haMikdash* contradicts Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that it does work even outside.

The *gemara* then attempts to resolve the difficulty against Reish Lakish: *ha mikmi deshama l'Rabbi Yochanan, ha l'basar deshama l'Rabbi Yochanan* — one statement was before he heard this teaching from Rabbi Yochanan and one after. This is, frankly, a strange answer. Tosafot already notes the difficulty: *temah mai teretz hu zeh she-tiretz d'amar Reish Lakish kodem she-lamad hamishna* — what kind of resolution is this, that Reish Lakish held this opinion before he had learned the *mishnah*? In an oral tradition in which *mishnayot* are memorized before one begins formulating opinions, this is difficult to accept. And even if Reish Lakish eventually reversed himself upon learning the *mishnah*, there is no explicit indication of that reversal anywhere in the sources. The *gemara*'s resolution on this point remains highly strained. The more pressing problem — *chutz kasha l'Rabbi Yochanan* — is then addressed by Rav Sheshet, to which we now turn.

## Four vs Two Avodot

### Rav Sheshet Solution

*Hu mosiv lah v'hu mifarek lah* — Rav Sheshet raised the question and resolved it. *b'arba avodot* — the resolution lies in the distinction between the four *avodot* and the two. The *gemara* now rereads Rav Yosef's *kal va'chomer* entirely differently: it is not about *avoda l'avoda* at all. Rather, Rav Yosef is saying that *machshava* is more potent *bifnim* than *bachutz* because in the *beis haMikdash* an improper thought can affect the status of a *korban* at any of the four *avodot* — *shechitah*, *kabala*, *holacha*, and *zerika* — whereas outside the *beis haMikdash*, in the realm of *avodah zarah*, thought only affects the status of the animal at two of them.

### Which Avodot Matter

The two that matter outside the *beis haMikdash* are *shechitah* and *zerika*. These are derived from *pesukim*: *zivchei meitim* — things slaughtered for idols — grounds the relevance of *shechitah*, and a *pasuk* in *tehillim* — *bal asich niskeihem midam* — establishes the significance of blood-sprinkling. *kabala* and *holacha*, the receiving and transporting of the blood, are treated as secondary acts. They are part of the process but are not themselves acts of "offering" in the sense that would define the animal or blood as having been presented to the idol. Performing *kabala* or *holacha* for *avodah zarah* may render one personally liable for worshipping an idol, but it does not transform the animal into *tikroves Avodah Zarah*. The animal's status as a forbidden offering is established only through *shechitah* or *zerika*.

### Sin vs. Meat Status

This distinction produces a striking result: one can commit the sin of *avodah zarah* at any of the four *avodot* — performing even *kabala* or *holacha* with idolatrous intent makes one liable — yet only two of those four acts affect the status of the meat or blood as a forbidden offering. Personal culpability and the object's halachic status do not perfectly coincide. This also clarifies an otherwise puzzling point about *haktara* — the burning of the fat on an altar. While this act would indeed make what is placed on the altar into *tikroves Avodah Zarah*, it does not render the entire animal forbidden. Only *shechitah* — which, as an act performed on the whole animal, can define the whole animal as an offering — has the power to make the entire *beheimah* forbidden. When the fat is burned on an idol's altar, the fat itself is forbidden in *hana'ah*, but the meat is not. Similarly, Tosafot notes based on a later *gemara* that even *zerika* makes only the blood itself forbidden, not the rest of the animal. The only path to forbidding the entire animal is *shechitah lishm Avodah Zarah*.

## Supporting Braita

### Braita Case

*Tanya k'vassay d'Rabbi Yochanan* — a *braita* is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's position. The *braita* states: *hashochet es habeheimah lizrok damah l'avodas kochavim ul'haktir chelba l'avodas kochavim* — one who slaughters an animal with the intent to sprinkle its blood for *avodah zarah* or to burn its fat for *avodah zarah* — *harei elu zivchei meitim*, these animals are *zivchei meitim* — forbidden in benefit. This exactly parallels the case Rabbi Yochanan discussed and confirms his ruling.

### Fat vs. Whole Animal

A remarkable implication emerges from the *braita*: the intent to burn the fat for *avodah zarah* is listed as a basis for the ruling, yet as we established, actually burning the fat for *avodah zarah* would make only the fat forbidden, not the whole animal. If the *shochet* succeeded in his plan and burned the fat on an idol's altar, the meat would remain permitted. Yet his *intent* during *shechitah* to do so makes the entire animal forbidden. How is that coherent?

### Redefining Act

The answer comes directly back to the question of how *kavanah* functions. If we say that *kavanah* redefines the act, then the explanation is this: because the *shochet* intended during *shechitah* to burn the fat for *avodah zarah*, the *shechitah* itself is redefined as an act of *avodah zarah* — it is now a *shechitah l'shem Avodah Zarah*, and that is what makes the whole animal forbidden. It is not the eventual act of fat-burning that would have produced this result, but the present act of *shechitah* as transformed by the *shochet*'s intent. The fact that actually burning the fat would have forbidden only the fat is beside the point — because what the intent accomplishes is not the equivalent of a successful fat-burning, but rather the equivalent of a full *shechitah l'Avodah Zarah*. This is precisely the force of Tosafot's position noted earlier: intent during *shechitah* redefines the *shechitah* itself as an idolatrous act.

## Caesarea Case

### Historical Context

The *gemara* now transitions from a general *braita* to a specific historical incident. The case occurred in Caesarea — *zeh hayah ma'aseh b'Kesari* — and it is worth pausing on what this tells us about the period. *avodah zarah* was rampant in the Land of Israel during the Talmudic era, and syncretism — the blending of outside religious practices with Jewish life — affected a not insignificant portion of the population. The mosaics and archaeological remains from ancient synagogues testify to the presence of images of sun gods and other pagan motifs. The case in Caesarea reflects this historical reality: a Jewish *shochet* who, it turned out, harbored plans for *avodah zarah* with his animal.

### The Actual Case

The specific case is: *shachat v'achar kach chishev aleha* — he slaughtered the animal, and only afterward declared his intent to use its blood or fat for *avodah zarah*. The *shechitah* itself was performed without any expressed idolatrous purpose. The question is whether his later declaration of intent should lead us to retroactively question what he was thinking at the time of *shechitah*. *zeh hayah ma'aseh b'kesari v'lo amru bo lo issur v'lo hetter* — this case occurred in Caesarea and the authorities were unable to render a clear ruling of either prohibition or permission.

### Rav Chisda Resolution

Rav Chisda explains the paralysis: *lo amru bo issur mishum kvod rabbanan* — they did not declare it forbidden out of respect for the position of the Rabbis. The Rabbis in the *mishnah* hold that we do not assume a non-Jew's thoughts are directed toward *avodah zarah* without clear evidence at the time. By analogy, even here, where the Jew later articulated an idolatrous intent, we give the benefit of the doubt and decline to assume he was thinking about *avodah zarah* at the moment of *shechitah*. *lo hetter mishum kvodo shel rabbi eliezer* — but they also did not declare it permitted, out of respect for Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer, who in the *mishnah* suspects that a non-Jew always intends *avodah zarah*, would presumably also suspect a Jew who later articulated such intent, and would hold that his later declaration reveals his earlier state of mind.

The case between the Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer does not map cleanly onto this scenario, however, and this is what genuinely paralyzed the authorities — not merely institutional deference. The Rabbis' leniency in the *mishnah* rested on there being no evidence of idolatrous intent; here there is at least some evidence, albeit post-hoc. Rabbi Eliezer's stringency in the *mishnah* concerned non-Jews specifically, about whom he maintains a categorical suspicion; here the actor is a Jew, about whom no such categorical suspicion normally applies. Each position in the *mishnah* thus suggests both a reason to be more lenient and a reason to be more stringent in the Caesarea case, and a clean ruling in either direction would implicitly take a side in the underlying dispute in a context where the dispute was not directly decided.

---

## Later Intent Problem

### Revealing Past Thoughts

The *gemara* presses further: *mimai* — how do you know that the positions of the Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer would translate into this case? One could argue the Caesarea case is actually *more* stringent than the *mishnah* case, or alternatively *less* stringent. On the one hand, *dilma ad kan lo ka'amri rabbanan hatam ela d'lo shamainan d'chashiv* — perhaps the Rabbis were lenient in the *mishnah* only because we never heard the actor articulate an idolatrous intent; but here, *deshaminan dechashiv* — we did hear him articulate it. And one might invoke *hochiach sofo al techilato* — his later statement reveals his earlier state of mind — to say that the Rabbis too would regard the meat as forbidden in this case.

On the other hand, *ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Eliezer hatam ela gabei ovdei kochavim* — perhaps Rabbi Eliezer's suspicion in the *mishnah* was specifically about non-Jews, *destama machshevet ovdei kochavim la'avoda zara* — because there is a categorical presumption that a non-Jew's thoughts are directed toward idolatry. *aval yisrael* — but by a Jew, who is presumed to be observant, even if we later see he harbors idolatrous plans, *hochiach sofo al techilato lo amrinan* — Rabbi Eliezer too might not apply the principle of "end reveals beginning." The Caesarea case is thus genuinely different from the *mishnah*'s case on two independent axes: the actor is a Jew (potentially more lenient) and the intent was explicitly articulated, albeit afterward (potentially more stringent). The combination of these factors renders the ruling indeterminate, and the authorities were accordingly unable to rule.

### End Reveals Beginning

The *gemara* then introduces a different resolution of the paralysis. *ela amar rav shizbi* — Rav Shizvi offers a different explanation of why the Caesarea authorities could not rule permissible: *lo amru bo hetter mishum kvodo deRabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — they were not prepared to permit the meat out of respect for the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The specific principle at stake is *hochiach sofo al techilato* — what someone does or says at the end retroactively illuminates what they were intending at the beginning. Rav Shizvi now goes in search of the relevant Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel ruling.

### Get Writing Case

*Hai Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel* — which Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel articulates this principle? *ilimah rabbi shimon ben gamliel deGittin* — perhaps it is the one cited in *gittin*. The *gemara* in *gittin* addresses the following *mishnah*: a healthy person — *ha-bari* — says to witnesses, *kitvu get le'ishti* — write a *get* for my wife. Normally, telling witnesses to "write" a *get* appoints them as agents only for the writing, not for the delivery; if the husband wants them to deliver it too, he must explicitly say *kitvu u-tnu*. The exception is when context makes clear the husband fears he is about to die — if he is being led to execution, going on a dangerous journey, or is gravely ill — in which case we understand that *kitvu* implicitly includes *tnu*. But when the person is healthy and there is no such context, saying *kitvu* might just be the husband playing with his wife — perhaps taunting her, or, in Rashi's more restrained reading, falsely reassuring her that a *get* is coming when he has no real intention of delivering it.

### Get and Roof Case

The *mishnah* then recounts: *u-ma'aseh be-vari* — there was an actual case of a healthy person who said *kitvu get le'ishti*, and then *ve'ala le-gag ve-nafal va-met* — he went up to a roof, fell, and died. Did he commit suicide? And if so, should we retroactively understand his *kitvu* instruction as including *tnu*? For if he was planning suicide, he was obviously worried about leaving his wife in the position of a *yevama* — bound to his brother through *yibum* or *chalitzah* — and his instruction to write the *get* was clearly meant to include delivery.

*Amar Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: im mei'atzmo nafal, harei zeh get* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if he threw himself off the roof intentionally, then it is a valid *get*; the suicide reveals that the husband had plans of self-destruction even when he gave his instructions, and therefore *kitvu* did implicitly mean *kitvu u-tnu*. *v'im ha-ruach dachaso, eino get* — if the wind blew him off, it was an accident, and we cannot retroactively read his instructions as including delivery. *im hochiach sofo al techilato* — if what came at the end proves something about the beginning, we reinterpret the beginning accordingly.

---

## Limits of Hindsight

### Only When Signals Present

The *gemara*, however, qualifies the scope of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's principle. *dilma shani hasam deka'amar kisvu* — in the *gittin* case, there was an earlier *act* — the instruction *kitvu get* — which can now be reinterpreted in light of the suicide. The instruction to write the *get* is an actual signal that can plausibly be understood differently once we know he was suicidal. The suicide reveals the meaning of the earlier instruction. The principle of *hochiach sofo al techilato* operates specifically when there is an earlier act or statement that can serve as a meaningful signal of intent — one that, in hindsight, admits of a reinterpretation consistent with the later behavior.

### Shechitah Not Signal

In the Caesarea case, by contrast, there is no such signal. The *shochet* simply performed a standard *shechitah*. There is nothing about the act of *shechitah* itself that serves as a signal of idolatrous intent. The later declaration that he planned to use the blood for *avodah zarah* cannot reach back and reinterpret the *shechitah* as a step in an idolatrous process, because the *shechitah* was not the kind of act that signals such a plan. Absent an earlier signal that can be retroactively decoded by the later behavior, *hochiach sofo al techilato* does not apply. The principle thus does not support ruling the Caesarea meat forbidden based on Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's *gittin* ruling.

### Ravina Proposal

*Ela amar Ravina* — Ravina therefore identifies a different Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel ruling, one whose application to the Caesarea case does not depend on the presence of an earlier signal. *mishum kvodo deRabban shimon ben gamliel dehacha* — out of respect for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel as expressed in the following *braita* in *bava batra*.

## Gift Refusal Case

### Slaves Bequest

The *braita* in *bava batra*: *ha-kosev nechasav le'acherin ve-hayu bahen avadim* — one who, presumably on his deathbed, gives all his property to others, and among his property are slaves. *ve-amar halah i efshi bahen* — the recipient says, "I do not want them." Slaves entail significant responsibility and obligation, so a recipient might reasonably decline. The question: has the recipient taken possession of the slaves or not? If the recipient is a *kohen*, this matters greatly — slaves owned by a *kohen* may eat *terumah*, and if the slaves have been legally transferred to the *kohen*-recipient, they may eat *terumah*; if they have not, they may not.

### Tanna Kamma View

The first opinion in the *braita* rules: *harei elu ochlin bi-terumah* — they may eat *terumah*. The recipient's declaration that he does not want the slaves is insufficient to undo the transfer. The *gemara* then clarifies the precise scope of this dispute. If the recipient was yelling from the very beginning — *betzoaveach mei'ikara* — objecting from the moment the bequest was announced, everyone agrees he did not take possession; one cannot force a gift on someone who has never consented. Similarly, if the recipient initially accepted the gift in silence and only later objected — *be-shotek u-ve-sof tzavach* — everyone agrees he has already taken possession and his subsequent objection is too late. The dispute arises in an intermediate case: *she-zichu lo al yedei acher* — someone else took possession of the *shtar* of transfer on the recipient's behalf, and the recipient himself was silent during this process, and only later began to object.

---

## Gift Refusal Case (II)

### Rabban Shimon View

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *keivan she-amar halah i efshi bahen, kevar zachu bahen yorshim* — since the recipient has now said he does not want them, the heirs of the donor take them instead. The gift was never truly transferred. *hochiach sofo al techilato* — his end behavior reveals that his beginning silence was not genuine consent.

### Consent Principle

The reasoning: the recipient was silent when someone else took the *shtar* on his behalf because he was thinking, *ki lo atei le-yadi amai etzvach* — "it has not yet really come into my hands; I will wait until they press me to actually accept it and then I will tell them I don't want it." His silence, therefore, was never meaningful consent — it was merely the silence of someone who expected another opportunity to decline. And now that he has declined, we see that the silence was never acceptance. His end behavior proves that his earlier silence was not the consent the *tanna kamma* took it to be.

This is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's broader application of *hochiach sofo al techilato* — and crucially, unlike the *gittin* case, it does not require a specific earlier signal. It simply requires that the later behavior be credibly connected to the earlier period, such that the later declaration illuminates what was really happening earlier. Applied to the Caesarea case: the *shochet*'s later declaration of idolatrous intent raises — according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's broader logic — the question of whether his *shechitah* was really performed with innocent intent. Out of respect for this position, the Caesarea authorities were not prepared to rule that the meat was definitively permitted.

---

## Final Ruling

### Ruling: Rabbi Yosi

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi. The operative rule is that the thoughts of the owner — as distinct from the *shochet* — are irrelevant to the status of the meat. Only the intent of the one actually performing the *shechitah* can define or invalidate it.

### Arab Traders Case

The practical application of this ruling arises from the case of *hanu tayei* — certain Arab traders — *de-atu l'Tzikunya* — who came to a place called Tzikunya. *yehavu dakhrei le-tavchei yisrael* — they gave their rams to Jewish *shochtim*. The Arabs explicitly told the Jewish butchers: *dama ve-tarba l'didan* — give us the blood and the fat; *maskha u-visra l'didkhu* — you can keep the skin and the meat. The intent of the non-Jewish owners to use the blood and fat for *avodah zarah* was explicit. The question of whether this affected the status of the meat was sent to Rav Yosef, and he replied with the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — the meat is unaffected.

### Application

The non-Jewish owners' idolatrous plans, however explicit, do not reach into the act of *shechitah* performed by the Jewish *shochet* and alter its status. The *shochet* was slaughtering the animals as ordinary meat; the fact that the owners intended to take the fat and blood for *avodah zarah* afterward is their affair and does not define the *shechitah*.

## Partial Ownership

### Ownership Threshold

*Amar lei Rav Aha berei d'Rav Avya le-Rav Ashi* — Rav Aha asks Rav Ashi: according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the owner's intent does matter, how much ownership is required before that intent becomes relevant? Rashi's premise here is that a genuine partial owner's idolatrous intent would, according to Rabbi Eliezer, affect the status of the entire animal — even if the other partial owner has entirely innocent intentions. But the question is where to draw the line.

### Pre-Purchase Intent

The scenario posed: *yahvei zuzei le-tavach Yisrael* — a non-Jew gives the Jewish butcher some money in advance, saying that after the *shechitah* he wants to purchase a pound of the meat. Does this pre-purchase agreement make him enough of an "owner" at the time of *shechitah* that his idolatrous intent would affect the animal's status?

### Real Partners Only

The answer: *chazinan* — it depends. *ee alma hu* — if this non-Jew is a powerful person, one who can compel the *shochet* to honor the agreement — *d'lo matzi madchei lei*, the *shochet* has no way of refusing him — then *assur*, it is forbidden. In that case the non-Jew has genuine effective ownership in the animal; the *shochet* cannot simply decline to sell him his pound of meat. But *ve-ee lav* — if the non-Jew is not powerful enough to compel compliance — *amar lei: reishach ve-tura* — the *shochet* can effectively tell him to take his business elsewhere — then the meat is permitted. Since the *shochet* retains the ability to refuse the sale, the non-Jew was never truly a partner in the animal, and his idolatrous plans have no bearing on its status. Real partnership triggers relevance under Rabbi Eliezer; a conditional or refusable arrangement does not rise to that level.
