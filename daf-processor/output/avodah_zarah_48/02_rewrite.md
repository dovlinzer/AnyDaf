# Avodah Zarah 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Houses/Stones Recap

### Previous Stages

The present daf opens by returning briefly to the *mishnayot* at the close of the previous *amud*, which addressed the case of a house or stone made to serve *avodah zarah*. The governing principle across those cases is that such objects pass through distinct stages of prohibition depending on what was done to them and whether they were actually worshipped.

### Made for Idolatry

The fundamental rule is that once a house or stone was made for *avodah zarah*, it becomes forbidden immediately, even without any act of worship having yet taken place. The *gemara* on the preceding *amud* clarified this point, but it also forced *Rashi* and *Tosafot* and other *rishonim* to conclude that the *mishnah* must be referring to cases in which the object was actually worshipped — either independently or alongside the idol placed within it — since otherwise the language of immediate prohibition is difficult to sustain.

### Added Materials

Where only something supplementary was added to the original structure — plaster applied to a stone, for instance — only the added material becomes forbidden. The addition does not redefine the underlying object. If the house or stone has not yet been worshipped, then only what was newly affixed takes on the forbidden character; the original structure itself remains permitted.

### Tashmish

A related consideration involves *tashmish*: what happens when an idol is placed in or on an object? While the idol is there, the object serves as a *tashmish* of *avodah zarah* and is therefore forbidden. Once the idol is removed, however, and assuming the object itself was never worshipped, it reverts to being permitted. The complicating question — one that the *gemara* does not resolve with clean edges — is what happens if the idol was actually worshipped while resting on or in the object. At that point, the *tashmish* relationship may be strong enough to render the object permanently forbidden even after the idol's removal. The general contours of the *gemara*'s approach presuppose that the object has not yet itself been worshipped, but many of the parameters remain far from black and white.

## Three Asherot

### Three Categories

The *mishnah* at the top of 48a turns to a new subject: the *asherah* tree. The *mishnah* is organized around three distinct categories, continuing the structural preference, common throughout this tractate, for presenting cases in groups of three.

### Planted for Idolatry

The first case: a tree that was planted from the outset for the purpose of *avodah zarah* — *ilan she-nata'o mi-techilah leshem avodat kochavim* — is forbidden from the moment of planting, even before any act of worship takes place. This parallels precisely the rule regarding a house or stone made for idolatry. Just as those become forbidden the moment they are fashioned for that purpose, so too the tree planted with that intention is forbidden immediately.

### Pruned Tree

The second case involves a tree that was pruned — *gido ufaslo* — in connection with idol worship, after which new growth emerged in the pruned area. According to *Rashi* and *Tosafot*, this case, like the others, proceeds from the principle that an idol object is forbidden from the moment it is fashioned, even without worship. Here, what was pruned and then regrew is analogous to the plaster applied to a stone: *notel mah she-hechlif*, one removes what regrew and that suffices. The pruner did not create the entire tree, and so the tree as a whole does not become forbidden. Only what grew in consequence of the pruning — that which the person effectively brought into being — takes on the forbidden character. Importantly, all of this presupposes that the tree has not yet been worshipped. The *gemara* will address the question of what happens once worship has occurred.

### Idol Underneath

The third case is a direct parallel to the earlier cases of the house and the platform. If an idol was placed beneath a tree — *he'mid tachteyha avodat kochavim* — without the tree itself being worshipped, then once the idol is removed, *harei zeh mutar*, the tree is permitted. While the idol is present, the tree functions as a *tashmish*, providing shade and shelter, and is thus forbidden during that period. But since the tree itself was not the object of worship, its status is entirely contingent on the idol's presence and resolves once the idol is taken away.

## Grafting Question

### Grafting Required

The *gemara* cites a teaching from the school of *Rabbi Yannai*: *ve-hu she-hivrich ve-hirkiv be-gufo shel ilan* — the middle case of the *mishnah* requires not merely that the tree was pruned and then regrew, but that an actual branch was grafted onto the body of the tree. On this reading, simple pruning followed by natural regrowth would not suffice to render the new growth forbidden; genuine grafting is required.

### Bittul Context

The *gemara* immediately resists this reading: but our *mishnah* says *gido ufaslo*, pruning alone — it does not require grafting. Accordingly, the *gemara* reframes *Rabbi Yannai*'s statement: *le-inyan bittul itmar*, it was said not in the context of what becomes forbidden, but in the context of *bittul* — specifically, what a non-Jew must do in order to render the tree permitted again. In that context, *Rabbi Yannai* is telling us that even if a branch was grafted onto the body of the tree, it does not redefine the tree as a whole.

### Doesn't Redefine

This principle is directly parallel to the rule discussed on the preceding *amud* regarding plaster that is pressed into the crevices of a stone. Even in that case, the addition does not redefine the entire stone; it is only the plaster itself that is affected, and its removal suffices. Similarly here: *ki natal mah she-hechlif shapir dami*, remove what was grafted on and the rest of the tree is fine. One might have thought — *mahu de-teima, keivan de-hivrich ve-hirkiv be-gufo shel ilan keman de-nata'o mi-techilla dami* — that since you grafted onto the actual body of the tree, it is as though you planted the whole tree afresh, and therefore the entire tree should become forbidden. *Ka mashma lan*: that is not so. The grafting affects only what was grafted, not the underlying tree.

## Shmuel Position

### Shmuel's View

*Shmuel* now introduces a new position: *ha-mishtachaveh le-ilan, toseftia asurah* — one who bows down to a tree renders its additional growth forbidden, though not the tree itself. Since the tree is rooted in the ground and participates in the landscape in the same way a mountaintop does, it cannot itself be made forbidden through an act of worship. But what grows afterward — the *tosefet*, the additional growth — does become forbidden. This intermediate position will shortly be connected to the opinion of *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Rabbi Elazar's Challenge

*Rabbi Elazar* challenges *Shmuel* from the *mishnah*: the *mishnah* specifies *gido ufaslo* — that the tree was pruned — as a necessary condition for new growth to become forbidden. This implies that *gido pislo in*, *lo gido pislo lo*: only if there was pruning does the new growth become a problem; without pruning, even new growth is not forbidden. This seems to contradict *Shmuel*, who holds that mere worship — without any pruning — suffices to render new growth forbidden. The straightforward response would be that the *mishnah* is describing a scenario in which no worship took place, only pruning — an answer *Tosafot* notes the *gemara* could have offered but does not. Instead, the *gemara* presses further, suggesting that the *mishnah* may indeed be discussing a case in which worship occurred, and asking why pruning is still needed.

### Attribution

*Shmuel*'s position is attributed by the *gemara* to *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*: *Shmuel de-amar ke-Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*. According to this attribution, the *mishnah*'s requirement of pruning reflects the view of the *rabbanan*, who hold that worshipping a tree does not render anything forbidden — not even subsequent growth — since such a tree is considered part of the natural landscape. According to the *rabbanan*, therefore, pruning is necessary, and even then only what regrew is affected. *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*, by contrast — and on his view, *Shmuel* — holds that worshipping a tree that was at least initially planted by a person renders the subsequent growth forbidden, even without pruning. Our *mishnah*, which requires pruning, would then be going according to the *rabbanan*. *Rashi* notes that the formula *amar lach Shmuel* — "*Shmuel* would say to you" — is the *talmud* speaking on *Shmuel*'s behalf, not *Shmuel* himself directly, and it is worth keeping that distinction in mind.

---

## Rav Ashi Reframes

### Base vs. Growth

*Rav Ashi* now proposes a fundamentally different reading of the entire dispute between *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabbanan*: *mimay d'Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbanan b'tosefta peligi* — what basis is there for assuming that their debate concerns whether worship renders new growth forbidden? Perhaps both agree on that point.

### All Agree New

*Rav Ashi* proposes: *dilma tosefta d'virei hakol assur* — perhaps everyone agrees that the new growth subsequent to worship is forbidden. The new growth postdates the act of worship and is, in some sense, a product of the worshipper's relationship to the tree; it is not pre-existing. On this reading, the debate between *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabbanan* shifts entirely.

### Yossi vs. Rabbis

*Uve'ikaro peligi*: the dispute concerns the pre-existing base of the tree — *ikar ha-ilan* — the part that was already growing before the act of worship. *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* holds that worshipping a tree makes the entire tree forbidden, base and all. The *rabbanan* hold that the base remains permitted; only what grew after worship becomes forbidden. This is a striking reassignment: what was previously characterized as *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*'s position now becomes the *rabbanan*'s position — everyone moves up one notch.

### Scriptural Proofs

The dispute finds support in two *pesukim*. *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* draws on the verse *ve-asheireihem tisrefun ba-esh* — "burn their *asherot* with fire" — which *Rashi* understands as encompassing both trees planted initially for idolatry and trees worshipped after planting, requiring total destruction in both cases. The *rabbanan* derive from *ve-asheireihem tigadun* — "hew down their *asherot*" — a verb that implies chopping down the growth while leaving the base, suggesting the base remains permissible. The tree referred to, on *Rav Ashi*'s revised reading, is *ilan she-neta'o ul-vasof avdo*: a tree that was planted and then later worshipped — the *ikar* of which, according to the *rabbanan*, is still permitted.

## Both Positions Work

### Accommodating Both

With *Rav Ashi*'s reframing in place, the *gemara* faces an immediate difficulty: if both *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabbanan* agree that new growth after worship is forbidden, then who is the author of our *mishnah*, which seems to require actual pruning before any growth becomes forbidden? *Im ken gidu pislo man tana lei* — according to whom would the *mishnah* require pruning at all? If the *rabbanan* hold that worship alone makes subsequent growth forbidden, the *mishnah* ought not to require pruning; and if *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* holds that worship makes the entire tree forbidden without pruning, the *mishnah* again makes no sense as stated. This was precisely why the earlier reading — where the *rabbanan* held that even new growth is not forbidden by worship alone — had force: it allowed *Shmuel* to follow *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* and gave the *mishnah* a clear *tanna* to rely on. The *gemara* now says: *ibayit eima Rabbanan, ve-ibayit eima Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* — I can make the *mishnah* work on either reading.

### Yossi's Position

To make the *mishnah* work according to *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*: *ki amar Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda be-lo gido pisulo ikar ilan assur* — when does he rule that even the base of the tree becomes forbidden? *Bistama* — in the ordinary case where someone simply worshipped the tree. In that case, with no act of pruning, the entire tree is forbidden, base and all. But if one did prune it first and then worshipped it, *galya da'atei* — the act of pruning reveals the worshipper's intention: *de-b'tosefta nicha lei, be-ikara lo nicha lei* — the focus is on the new growth, not on the base. Thus, paradoxically, pruning actually limits the scope of the prohibition rather than expanding it. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* according to *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* is not that you need to prune in order to create a prohibition; rather, the *chiddush* is that when one does prune, only the new growth becomes forbidden, whereas had there been no pruning and simply worship, the entire tree would have been forbidden.

### Rabbis' Position

To make the *mishnah* work according to the *rabbanan*: the *rabbanan* hold that worship alone renders subsequent new growth forbidden regardless of pruning. So why does the *mishnah* specify pruning at all? *Itzricha lei* — it is still necessary to state the case, because one might have thought that pruning makes things worse: *salka da'atach amina, keivan de-avid lei ma'aseh be-gufei, ikar ilan nami litzor* — since one performed a direct physical act upon the tree body itself, perhaps the *rabbanan* would say the entire tree becomes forbidden in that case, even though ordinarily they limit the prohibition to new growth. *Ka mashma lan*: the *mishnah* teaches that even pruning does not render the base forbidden — the pruning does not make things worse. The *chiddush* here is thus the inverse of the *chiddush* for *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda*: for the *rabbanan*, the *mishnah* is telling you that an act performed upon the tree's body does not escalate the prohibition to encompass the base.

The conclusion is that while the original framing of this debate — in which the *rabbanan* held that worship renders nothing forbidden — had the advantage of giving *Shmuel* a majority opinion to rely on rather than a lone view, *Rav Ashi*'s reframing is equally viable. Both positions can accommodate our *mishnah*, though they do so in structurally opposite ways.

## What is Asherah?

### Mishnah Structure

The next *mishnah* addresses a question that turns out to be more contested than it might initially appear: *eizu hi asherah* — what exactly constitutes an *asherah*? The *gemara* notes that the preceding *mishnah* already presented three cases, and clarifies: *shtayim le-divrei hakol ve-achat machleket Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabbanan* — the first two cases, in which the tree itself was the object of worship, are undisputed by all opinions. The third case — an idol placed beneath the tree without the tree itself being worshipped — is the subject of a dispute between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *rabbanan*.

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rabbi Shimon* holds: *kol she-ovdin oto* — an *asherah* is only a tree that is itself worshipped. The mere presence of an idol beneath a tree, without direct worship of the tree, does not make it an *asherah*. On *Rabbi Shimon*'s view, the *Torah*'s command to destroy *asherot* applies only where the tree itself is the object of veneration.

### Tzidon Story

*Rabbi Shimon*'s position is illustrated by a famous episode: in the city of Tzidon there was a tree that people would worship. Underneath it they found a pile of rubble — *gal*. *Rabbi Shimon* told them: examine the pile. They did, and found an idol — *tzurah* — buried within it. *Rabbi Shimon* then ruled: *hoil u-le-tzurah hein ovdim, natir lahen et ha-ilan* — since they were evidently worshipping the idol, not the tree, the tree itself is permitted. What had appeared to be worship of the tree was in fact directed at the hidden idol beneath it, and therefore the tree is no different from the third case of the preceding *mishnah*: once the idol is removed, the tree is *mutar*. *Rabbi Shimon*'s implicit position seems to be that the *Torah* only truly obligates destruction of an *asherah* when the tree itself is the object of worship — a case that does not arise as easily as one might think.

### Sages' View

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold: *kol she-yesh tachteyha avodat kochavim* — any tree beneath which an idol has been placed qualifies as an *asherah*, regardless of whether the tree itself was worshipped. This reading has some tension with the preceding *mishnah*, which implied that once the idol is removed, the tree is simply permitted — a position that sits more naturally with *Rabbi Shimon*'s view. The *gemara* does not fully resolve this tension, but it strongly suggests that the preceding *mishnah*, with its ruling that the tree becomes *mutar* upon removal of the idol, is authored in accordance with *Rabbi Shimon*.

## Identifying Asherah

### Rav's Criterion

Having defined *asherah* in principle, the *gemara* turns to the practical question of how one identifies a tree as an *asherah* in the absence of direct evidence. *Rav* provides a criterion: *kol she-komarim yoshvin tachteyha ve-ein to'amin mi-peiroteyha* — any tree beneath which priests regularly sit and from whose fruit they do not eat. Both signs together point to the tree's sacred status in the pagan cult: the priests frequent it as a holy site and yet abstain from its fruit, a combination that signals *asherah*.

### Shmuel's Extension

*Shmuel* extends the definition further: *afilu amru hani tamrei le-vei nitzrafi assur* — even if one hears the priests say that these dates from this tree are designated for a particular idol (*le-vei nitzrafi*, which *Rashi* identifies as the name of an idol), intending to press them into beer and drink that beer on their festival day, that suffices to make the tree forbidden. This is a striking and seemingly expansive ruling. The tree is not itself being worshipped; its fruit is not being offered as *tikrovet avodah zarah* in any direct sense — it is being processed into beer for a festive ritual occasion. That would not seem to constitute even a *tashmish* of *avodah zarah* in the direct sense. The *Rambam* (*Hilchot Avodah Zarah* 8:4) frames this as evidence that the tree is in fact worshipped: the practice of using the fruit in this way is itself the custom — *chok* — associated with an *asherah*, and thus serves as a reliable indicator that this tree is indeed one that the pagans revere.

### Sign Not Cause

The *Rambam*'s framing helps clarify the logical structure of both *Rav*'s and *Shmuel*'s criteria. These are *simanim* — signs — not *sibot*, causes. Neither the priests sitting beneath the tree nor the use of its fruit for cultic beer is itself what renders the tree forbidden. Rather, each practice is an indicator that this tree is in fact worshipped, and it is the underlying worship that produces the prohibition. The elders of *Pumbedita* are quoted as ruling that the *halacha* follows *Shmuel* on this point.

## Benefit Prohibition

### Shade Prohibition

The following *mishnah* addresses the prohibition of *hana'ah* — deriving benefit — from an *asherah*: *lo yeshev be-tzilah*, one may not sit in its shade. Notably, the *mishnah* adds: *ve-im yashav tahor* — if one did sit in its shade, one remains *tahor*, ritually pure. This introduces a distinction between the prohibition of *hana'ah* on the one hand and the *tumah* status generated by the *asherah* on the other. These are not coextensive. Furthermore, the *mishnah* rules *lo ya'avor tachteha ve-im avar tamei* — do not pass directly beneath it, and if one did, one is *tamei*.

### Types of Shade

The *gemara* unpacks the relationship between the *mishnah*'s two clauses — *lo yeshev be-tzilah* and *lo ya'avor tachteha* — by distinguishing between two kinds of shade. Directly beneath the tree, when the sun is overhead, there is *tzel komato*: the shadow cast that is equivalent in extent to the height of the tree itself. When the sun is at an angle, the shadow extends far beyond the tree's height; this extended shadow is called *tzel tzilah*, the shade of its shade. The *gemara* debates which of these two zones the *mishnah*'s ruling emphasizes. *Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* says the *mishnah*'s statement *lo yeshev be-tzilah* was necessary only to address *tzel komato* — even there it is forbidden — implying that if one does sit in *tzel komato*, one is still *tahor*, because one is not directly under the canopy of the tree. An alternative reading reverses the emphasis: the *chiddush* is that even *tzel tzilah* is forbidden to sit in, though of course one remains *tahor* there as well.

### Benefit vs. Tumah

The *gemara* resolves the two readings by clarifying the underlying principle: the prohibition of *hana'ah* applies in both zones — whether one sits in *tzel komato* or in *tzel tzilah*, one has derived benefit from the *asherah* and has violated the prohibition. The *tumah* dimension, however, applies only when one passes directly beneath the tree — *tachteha* — not in either of the two extended shade zones. Thus: sitting in *tzel tzilah* is a violation of *hana'ah*, but one remains *tahor*; sitting in *tzel komato* is likewise a violation, but one remains *tahor*; only passing directly underneath the canopy of the tree generates *tumah*.

### Impurity Source

The *gemara* asks: on what grounds does an *asherah* generate *tumah* at all? The *asherah* itself is *avodah zarah*, and *avodah zarah* is treated as a source of ritual impurity akin to a *sheretz* or a *niddah* — which would not generate *tumah* under a shared *ohel*. The answer: *ee efshar de-lika tikrovet avodah zarah* — it is impossible that a tree which was worshipped has not had offerings made to it and deposited at its base. Those offerings constitute *tikrovet avodah zarah*, which is treated as *tamei ke-met*, impure like a corpse. When one stands directly beneath the tree, the *tumah* of those buried offerings beneath the ground spreads upward and over the person, rendering him *tamei* just as one who stands in the same enclosure as a corpse. This is the position of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*: *minayin le-tikrovet avodah zarah she-metammei be-ohel* — how do we know that offerings to *avodah zarah* generate *ohel*-*tumah*? The verse states *va-yitzamedu le-va'al pe'or va-yochlu zivchei meitim* — "they ate the sacrifices of the dead." Just as a *meit* generates *tumah* under a shared roof, so does *tikrovet avodah zarah*.

## Walking Rules

### Public Domain

*Haysah gozelet et ha-rabbim ve-avar tachteyha tahor*: if the *asherah*'s branches overhang *reshut ha-rabbim* — the public domain — then passing beneath that overhanging section leaves one *tahor*, and moreover it is apparently not even prohibited as *hana'ah*. The *gemara*'s reasoning is that the *rabbanan* have no authority to impose a status of prohibition on space that belongs to the public. The *asherah* has no right to define the *halachic* character of what it overhangs into public territory.

### Forced Passage

The *gemara* then raises a new question about an *asherah* that extends over the public domain: the *mishnah* said *ve-avar* — if one passed — implying after the fact. May one pass *lechatchila*? *Rav Yitzchak bar Elazar* in the name of *Chizkiyah* says: *over* — one may pass *lechatchila*. *Rabbi Yochanan* says: *im avar* — only after the fact.

### Alternative Paths

*Ve-lo peligi* — they do not actually disagree: *ha de-ika darcha achrina, ha de-leika darcha achrina*. The distinction is whether an equivalent alternative route exists. If an equivalent path is available, one should not pass beneath the *asherah*, even in the public domain — that is *Rabbi Yochanan*'s case. If no equivalent alternative exists, passing beneath it is fully permitted *lechatchila* — that is *Rav Yitzchak bar Elazar*'s case. *Tosafot* clarifies that "another path" means a roughly equivalent route; it cannot mean any conceivable detour, for then there would never be a case of no alternative, since one can always travel around the long way.

### Rav Sheshet Story

The *gemara* illustrates the stakes of this distinction with an episode involving *Rav Sheshet*, who was blind. When passing through a public space with an *asherah* overhanging it, *Rav Sheshet* instructed his attendant: *ki matis le-hasam, arhitani* — when you reach that spot, rush me through quickly. The *gemara* asks: what was the case? If there was no equivalent alternative path, passing is entirely permitted — why the need to rush? If there was an equivalent alternative, even rushing through does not help — he should simply take the other path. The *gemara* resolves: there was in fact no equivalent alternative, and he was therefore permitted to pass. But *adam chashuv shani* — a person of *Rav Sheshet*'s stature ought not be seen lingering beneath an object of *avodah zarah*, even where doing so would be technically permitted.

## Planting Under

### Vegetables Allowed

The *mishnah* now considers what may be planted beneath an *asherah* tree, and the analysis turns on what constitutes benefit derived from the tree. *Zara tachteyha yerakot bi-ymot ha-geshamim* — vegetables may be planted beneath an *asherah* during the rainy season. During that period the shade the tree provides is actually unhelpful — if anything, it blocks rain and reduces the light the plants need. Since the shade is not beneficial in the rainy season, planting there does not constitute *hana'ah*.

### Lettuce Forbidden

*Ve-ha-chazirin lo bi-ymot ha-chamah ve-lo bi-ymot ha-geshamim* — but lettuce (*chazirin*) may not be planted beneath the tree in any season, because shade is always beneficial for lettuce, even during the rainy months. Since the *asherah*'s shade always helps it grow, planting lettuce there always constitutes *hana'ah* from the *asherah*.

### Rabbi Yossi

*Rabbi Yossi* is more stringent: *af lo yerakot bi-ymot ha-geshamim mipnei she-ha-novia nosheret aleihem ve-havay lahen le-zevel* — even in the rainy season, ordinary vegetables may not be planted beneath the tree, because the leaves and debris that fall from it onto the ground serve as fertilizer, providing benefit to the crops regardless of whether the shade itself is helpful.

### Two Factors

This disagreement prompts the *gemara* to frame the dispute as a case of *zeh v'zeh gorem*: two contributing factors, one permitted and one forbidden, both producing a single result. In the case of vegetables planted beneath the *asherah* in the rainy season, the soil itself provides nourishment — a permitted factor — and the fallen leaves from the *asherah* also provide nourishment — a forbidden factor. The *rabbanan* apparently hold that where two factors jointly contribute, the benefit from the forbidden factor is not treated as *hana'ah*. *Rabbi Yossi* holds the opposite.

---

## Zeh v'Zeh Gorem

### Two Sources

The *gemara* takes up the *zeh v'zeh gorem* debate directly, treating it as the structural key to the dispute in the *mishnah*. *Rabbi Yossi* forbids the vegetables in the rainy season because the forbidden leaves are contributing to the growth. The *rabbanan* permit it because they regard *zeh v'zeh gorem* as *mutar*.

### Orlah Contradiction

The *gemara* then notes a striking problem: from another *mishnah*, it appears that *Rabbi Yossi* takes the opposite position. There, *Rabbi Yossi* rules: *notin yichur shel orlah ve-ein notin egoz shel orlah* — one may plant a sapling of an *orlah* tree, since the sapling itself is not forbidden, but one may not plant the nut of an *orlah* tree, since the nut is *peri* and is forbidden in benefit. And *Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Rav* that *Rabbi Yossi* agrees that if one nevertheless did plant it and then grafted it, the resulting fruit is *mutar* — because at that point it is a case of *zeh v'zeh gorem*, the forbidden *orlah* branch contributing alongside the permitted tree, and *Rabbi Yossi* permits this. So in the *orlah* case, *Rabbi Yossi* seems to say *zeh v'zeh gorem* is *mutar*, while in the *asherah* case he seems to say it is *assur*.

### Indirect Benefit

The *gemara* resolves *Rabbi Yossi*'s apparent self-contradiction: *d'ka'azil le-ibud mattir* — when one is actively destroying the idol by grinding it up and casting it away, the chain of events that might lead to someone eventually deriving benefit from what was scattered is so attenuated that *Rabbi Yossi* is not concerned. *Tosafot* explains this means the scenario is simply too far-fetched — material thrown into the sea or scattered to the wind reaching someone as fertilizer is not a realistic prospect. *Hacha de-lo ka'azil le-ibud* — but in the case of the *asherah* tree, the leaves are falling directly beneath it onto the ground right there, with no such attenuation, and so *Rabbi Yossi* considers it a real and present *hana'ah*.

### Offset Principle

The *rabbanan*'s position, however, remains to be explained. If the *rabbanan* accept that *zeh v'zeh gorem* is generally *assur* — as their position in the case of grinding up an idol seems to suggest — why do they permit planting vegetables beneath the *asherah* in the rainy season, where the fallen leaves do provide benefit? The *gemara* resolves this by citing a principle from *Rav Mari the son of Rav Kahana*: *mah de-mashbiach be-ur pogem be-vasar* — what the fire improves in one respect damages in another. Applied here: *mashbiach be-nivia, pogem be-vasar* — what is gained from the fallen leaves as fertilizer is offset by what is lost through the shade, which harms the vegetables. Viewing the situation in the macro, the vegetables receive no net benefit from the *asherah*. The *rabbanan* are therefore not troubled by the *zeh v'zeh gorem* analysis in this case. This reasoning is admittedly striking: one might argue that at any given moment when the sun is not shining, the shade causes no harm, yet the fallen leaves still provide benefit — so why is each such moment not independently forbidden? The *gemara*'s answer operates on the macroscopic level rather than dissecting individual instants of benefit.

## Evidence Debate

### Orlah Grafted

The *gemara* now probes whether *Rabbi Yossi* truly holds *zeh v'zeh gorem assur*. The evidence that he does not — that he in fact permits it — comes from the *orlah* case. *Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Rav* that *Rabbi Yossi* permits the fruit of a tree grown from a forbidden *orlah* nut, if the tree was subsequently grafted onto a permitted tree. A *baraita* confirms this: *modeh Rabbi Yossi she-im nata ve-hivrich ve-hirkiv mutar*. Now, *Rashi* understands the *zeh v'zeh gorem* here as the combination of the *orlah* nut (which initiated growth) and the soil (which nourished it). *Tosafot* disagrees: that cannot constitute *zeh v'zeh gorem*, since the contributions of the soil and the nut operate on entirely different planes — the nut is the source of the tree, while the soil merely provides nutrients. For *Tosafot*, the case requires actual grafting: a branch from the *orlah*-derived tree is grafted onto a permitted tree, and both the *orlah* branch and the permitted tree contribute to the fruit that subsequently grows. That is a genuine *zeh v'zeh gorem*, where both factors contribute on the same plane to the same outcome, and *Rabbi Yossi* rules the fruit is *mutar*.

### Contradictory Sources

The *gemara* then cites two contradictory *baraitot*. One *baraita* teaches: *sadeh tizarei, parah tishacheit* — a field that was fertilized with forbidden fertilizer (from an animal that had been worshipped or from *tikrovet avodah zarah*) may be planted, and a cow fattened on forbidden fodder may be slaughtered and eaten. The other *baraita* rules: *sadeh tivor u-farah tirazeh* — the field must lie fallow until it recovers its natural fertility without the benefit of the forbidden fertilizer, and the cow must be allowed to become lean again, losing the weight gained from the forbidden fodder. These two *baraitot* seem to represent a direct dispute about *zeh v'zeh gorem*: one permits deriving benefit when both forbidden and permitted factors have contributed to a result, the other forbids it.

### Position Clarification

One might naturally read these two *baraitot* as the dispute of *Rabbi Yossi* and the *rabbanan*, tracking directly onto the debate we have been analyzing. But the *gemara* resists this: *lo ha-Rabbi Eliezer ve-ha-Rabbanan* — it is not *Rabbi Yossi* and the *rabbanan* who are the authors of these two *baraitot*, but rather *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan*.

### Transition Forward

This clarification raises a new question: if the debate in these *baraitot* is between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan*, then who holds that *zeh v'zeh gorem* is genuinely *mutar* in a straightforward sense — not as a consequence of offset or attenuation, but as a basic principle? That is the question the *gemara* will carry forward into the next discussion. The *gemara* pauses here, having established that the *Rabbi Yossi*-*rabbanan* dispute does not cleanly resolve the *zeh v'zeh gorem* question, and that a different set of authorities — *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan* — must be examined to locate the true *machlokes* on this principle.
