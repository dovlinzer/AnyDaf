# Kiddushin 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maaser Sheni & Hekdesh

### Maaser Sheni Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר הוֹאִיל וּסְתַם לַן תְּנָא כְּוָתֵיהּ, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ הוֹאִיל וּסְתַם לַן תְּנָא כְּוָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir with regard to** second **tithe** in that it is considered consecrated, **as** the ***tanna* taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with his** opinion. **And** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to consecrated** property, **as** the ***tanna* taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with his** opinion.

We resume in the middle of 54b, having just established the ruling that we follow *Rabbi Meir* on the question of *maaser sheni*. The position is that *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah* — God's money — and not the private property of the individual. The basis for this ruling is a *stam mishna* reflecting the position of *Beit Hillel*, which holds that one does not distribute the gifts of the poor — *peret u'olelot* — from *neta reva'i*, since *neta reva'i* shares the laws of *maaser sheni*. The logic is that if it is truly God's money, one cannot use it to betroth a woman, because one cannot effect *kiddushin* with property that does not genuinely belong to oneself.

### Me'ilah Distinctions

When it comes to *hekdesh* and *me'ilah*, however, the ruling follows *Rabbi Yehuda*. The many *mishnayot* in Masechet Me'ilah demonstrate that when one uses *hekdesh* funds, one transgresses *me'ilah b'shogeg* — unwittingly — and does not transgress *me'ilah b'mezid* — intentionally. *Rabbi Meir* takes precisely the reverse position: *me'ilah* can occur *b'mezid*, and moreover, when it comes to monetary transactions involving *hekdesh* funds, *me'ilah* does not occur *b'shogeg*, because had one known one was transacting with sacred funds, one would not have wanted the transaction at all, and the transaction is therefore void. The *stam mishna* in Me'ilah goes against *Rabbi Meir* and follows *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Stam Mishna Logic

Thus the general picture is: by *maaser sheni*, the *stam mishna* reflects *Rabbi Meir*; by *me'ilah*, the *stam mishna* reflects *Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Three Mishnayot Challenge

### Redeeming Maaser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, מַאי הִיא – דִּתְנַן: כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ וְאֵין לוֹ בִּיעוּר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this statement: **What is** the unattributed mishna that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir with regard to** second **tithe?** It is **as we learned** (*Pe'a* 7:6): It is prohibited to eat or derive benefit from fruit during the first three years after the tree is planted. The fruit of the fourth year is to be taken to Jerusalem and eaten there. With regard to **a vineyard** in its **fourth year, Beit Shammai say: It does not have** the *halakha* of adding **one-fifth,** i.e., if the owner himself redeems the fruit to bring money to Jerusalem to spend on food there, as one may also do with second tithe, he does not add one-fifth to its value but redeems it for its true value. **And it** also **does not have** the *halakha* of **disposal,** i.e., there is no obligation to dispose of it on the eve of Passover of the fourth year of the Sabbatical cycle, when all tithes that have not yet been separated must be disposed of. **And Beit Hillel say: It does have** the *halakha* of adding one-fifth and the *halakha* of disposal.

The *gemara* now raises a series of challenges. Having said we rule like *Rabbi Meir* that *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah*, the *gemara* cites the following *mishna* with the words *v'ha tnan* — but do we not teach? The *mishna* states: *HaPodeh Maaser Sheni shelo* — one who redeems his own *maaser sheni* — adds a fifth to its value. This applies *bein mishelo bein shenitan lo b'matanah* — whether the produce is his own or whether it was given to him as a gift, and he now wishes to redeem it rather than carry the fruit to Jerusalem.

### Gift-Giving Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים כְּמַעֲשֵׂר, כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לְהוּ? אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אַמַּאי כּוּלּוֹ לַגַּת? הָאָמַר מַעֲשֵׂר מָמוֹן הֶדְיוֹט הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify the opinions of Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai with regard to the obligation to leave *peret* and *olelot* from a fourth-year vineyard. **And** with regard to **Beit Hillel,** who **say** that the *halakha* of fourth-year produce is **like** the *halakha* of second **tithe, in accordance with whose** opinion **do they hold** with regard to second tithe? **If** they hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, why** does **all of it** go **to the winepress? Didn't** Rabbi Yehuda **say** that second **tithe is** itself **common,** i.e., non-sacred, **property,** and the owner must therefore leave *peret* and *olelot*? **Rather, is it not** the case that they hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who says that second tithe is consecrated property? The mishna aligning the opinion of Beit Hillel with that of Rabbi Meir is tantamount to there being an unattributed mishna in accordance with Rabbi Meir's opinion, as the *halakha* is generally in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel.

The difficulty is immediately apparent: if someone can give their *maaser sheni* to another person, and that other person genuinely owns it to the point of being required to add a fifth upon redemption, it sounds very much as though *maaser sheni* belongs to its owner as private property, transferable at will. The *gemara* asks: *mani* — who is the author of this *mishna*? If it is *Rabbi Meir*, how can one give it as a gift? *V'amar maaser mamon gavoah hu* — he holds it is God's money. One may have the right to bring it to Jerusalem and eat it there, but it is not one's property to transfer to someone else.

### Tevel Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן דְּיָהֵיב נִיהֲלֵיהּ בְּטִיבְלֵיהּ, וְקָסָבַר: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, actually** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and with what are we dealing here?** It is not a situation where one gave second tithe itself as a gift, but **where he gave him** his entire crop **in its** state of being **untithed produce.** Consequently, the portion he gave him included tithes that had not yet been separated, **and he holds** that **gifts that have not been separated are not considered as though they have been separated.** One does not categorize the untithed produce as a mixture of regular produce and tithes, but as a non-sacred category in and of itself. Since the second tithe has not yet been separated, the produce has no sanctity, and he can give it as a gift.

The *gemara* answers: *la, l'olam Rabbi Meir* — no, the *mishna* can still reflect *Rabbi Meir*'s view. *V'hacha b'mai askinan k'de'yahiv lei b'tevel* — what are we dealing with? A case in which the original owner gave the produce to the recipient while it was still *tevel* — before *trumot u'maasrot* had been separated. The giver said, in effect, here is some of my crop; take it, and you handle the separation of tithes. The *maaser sheni* thus never belonged to the first person in its final sanctified form; it was given as *tevel*, and it was the recipient who rendered it *maaser sheni* through his own act of separation.

One might press the point: even at the stage of *tevel*, the future *maaser sheni* already exists within the produce in potential — why does that not prevent transfer? The *gemara* responds that this *tanna* holds the position of *matnas shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu d'mi'an* — that gifts not yet separated are treated as though they already have been. On this view, before separation, the grain is simply *tevel*, an undifferentiated whole; the future *maaser sheni* does not have independent status yet, and therefore the entire *tevel* belongs to the owner to transfer. Once transferred, the recipient separates the tithe, and it is his own *maaser sheni* that emerges from his own produce. The *mishna* is therefore entirely consistent with *Rabbi Meir*.

On the related question of why *me'ilah* does not apply to *maaser sheni*: *me'ilah* applies only to things sanctified to the *beit hamikdash* — a *korban*, or something dedicated to *bedek habayis*. *Maaser sheni* has *kedushah*, but it is *kedushah* of a different type — the obligation to eat the produce in Jerusalem. It is a categorically distinct form of sanctity.

### Neta Reva'i Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַפּוֹדֶה נֶטַע רְבָעִי שֶׁלּוֹ – מוֹסִיף עָלָיו חֲמִישִׁיתוֹ, בֵּין מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, בֵּין שֶׁנִּיתַּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה. מַנִּי? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר – מִי מָצֵי יָהֵיב לֵיהּ? וְהָא גָּמְרִי ״קֹדֶשׁ״ ״קֹדֶשׁ״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its line of inquiry: **Come** and **hear** another unattributed mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda (*Ma'aser Sheni* 4:6): With regard to **one who redeems his** own **fourth-year produce, he adds to it its one-fifth, whether** the produce was **from his** crop or was **given to him as a gift.** The Gemara inquires: In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **If we say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, can one give** produce of the fourth year **to** another as a gift? **But didn't he derive** that one must add one-fifth from the verbal analogy of **"holy"** for fourth-year fruit from **"holy" from** second **tithe?** This would mean that fourth-year fruit, like second tithe, is property of the Most High and cannot be given as a gift. **Rather, is it not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?**

The *gemara* then attempts again: *ta shma* — come hear. Another *mishna* teaches: *hapodeh neta reva'i shelo* — one who redeems his own *neta reva'i*, his fourth-year fruit — adds a fifth. This applies *bein mishelo bein shenitan lo b'matanah*, whether the fruit is his own or was given to him as a gift. The implication is that ownership of *neta reva'i* can genuinely be transferred between individuals.

Critically, the *tevel* answer does not apply here. Fourth-year fruit does not exist as *tevel* and then become *neta reva'i* through an act of separation. Everything that grows in the fourth year of the vine or tree immediately has the status of *neta reva'i* — or *kerem reva'i* — which *Beit Hillel* treats as equivalent to *maaser sheni*. There is no prior stage at which the fruit is merely *tevel* that can be transferred before acquiring its sacred status. Yet the *mishna* states that it can be given as a gift and genuinely acquired by the recipient. This appears to be an unambiguous *stam mishna* like *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds *maaser sheni* is *mamon hedyot*.

### Smadar Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן דְּיָהֵיב כְּשֶׁהוּא סְמָדַר. וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: סְמָדַר אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פֶּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and with what are we dealing here?** It is not a situation where one gave fully grown fourth-year fruit but **where he gave him** the fruit **when it was a bud. And this is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says: A bud** of a fruit **is forbidden** as *orla*, **because it is** already considered **a fruit.** Rabbi Meir, by contrast, holds that a fruit bud is not considered fruit. Therefore, the *halakhot* of *orla* and fourth-year fruit apply only to fruit that has grown, whereas beforehand it can be given as a gift.

The *gemara* again insists: *la, l'olam Rabbi Meir* — the *mishna* can be explained as reflecting *Rabbi Meir*'s position. *V'hacha b'mai askinan kegon d'yahiv lei k'shehu smadar* — we are dealing with a case in which the owner transferred the vines to the recipient while the grapes were still in the unripe *smadar* stage, before they had developed into actual fruit. Since they had not yet become a *pri*, they had not yet acquired the status of *neta reva'i*. While they remained *smadar*, they were still ordinary property, and the owner could transfer them freely. The recipient then came to own the vines at the point the grapes ripened into *neta reva'i*, and so it is his own *neta reva'i* that he redeems. This reading diverges from the position of *Rabbi Yosi*, who holds that even at the *smadar* stage the fruit is already considered a *pri* and therefore already *orlah*-relevant — the present explanation cannot proceed according to *Rabbi Yosi*.

The irony is not lost: the natural reading of both *mishnayot* supports *Rabbi Yehuda*. These explanations are genuine *kvetches*. Nevertheless, because a *stam mishna* like *Rabbi Meir* exists, the *gemara* prefers to reinterpret those *mishnayot* as compatible with *Rabbi Meir* rather than acknowledge two *stamim* in favor of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Meshichah Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָשַׁךְ הֵימֶנּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר בְּסֶלַע וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִפְדּוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁעָמַד בִּשְׁתַּיִם – נוֹתֵן סֶלַע, וּמִשְׂתַּכֵּר בְּסֶלַע, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁלּוֹ. מַנִּי? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אַמַּאי מִשְׂתַּכֵּר בְּסֶלַע? ״וְנָתַן אֶת הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its line of inquiry: **Come** and **hear** another unattributed mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda (*Ma'aser Sheni* 4:6): If one was selling second-tithe produce, and the buyer **pulled tithe worth a *sela* from him,** thereby acquiring it, **and did not manage to redeem it until** its value **stood at two** *sela*, and he had not yet paid the seller, the buyer **gives a *sela*** to the seller, **and he** thereby **gains a *sela* and the second tithe is his,** since he acquired it when he pulled it and the price was fixed at that point. The Gemara inquires: In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **If we say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, why does** the buyer **gain a *sela*? The Merciful One states** in the Torah with regard to the redemption of consecrated property: **And he will give the money and it will be assured to him** (see Leviticus 27:19), from which it is derived that one acquires consecrated property only after paying money. This means that the value of the second tithe increased before he acquired it. **Rather, is it not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that second tithe is common property, and it is acquired from the moment he pulled it?

The *gemara* tries a third time. Another *mishna* in *maaser sheni* states: *mashach heimenu maaser b'selah* — someone pulled *maaser sheni* into his possession at the agreed price of one *selah*, performing *meshichah*, the formal act of acquisition. But *velo hispik lifstoso ad she-amad bish'tayim* — before he got around to completing the redemption by handing over the money, the market value of the produce doubled. The *mishna* rules *noten selah umistaker besela* — he pays the original agreed price and profits the difference. *U'maaser sheni shelo* — and the *maaser sheni* is his.

The underlying mechanics require some analysis. The act that determines the purchase price is the *meshichah*: once he performed *meshichah*, the transaction was legally finalized at the price agreed upon at that moment. Even though the money has not yet changed hands — and handing over the money would constitute the act of *pidyon*, redemption — the sale price was set at *meshichah*, and any subsequent change in market value does not alter his obligation. He pays the original *selah*, and the *kedushah* of the *maaser sheni* transfers onto that money.

### Meshichah Logic

Now the *gemara* identifies the problem. *Mani* — who is this *mishna* going like? If it is *Rabbi Meir*, there is a fundamental difficulty. *Rabbi Meir* holds *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah*. If so, there can be no *mechira*, no sale. The transaction cannot be treated as a commercial exchange finalized by *meshichah*, because *meshichah* effects acquisition only when ownership can genuinely be transferred. If it is God's money and not the seller's private property, *meshichah* is meaningless. What can happen is only *pidyon* — redemption — and *pidyon* takes effect at the moment the money is handed over, by the verse *v'nasan es hakesef vekam lo*, one gives the money and it is established. If that is the operative moment, then when the money is handed over and the value has risen to twenty, the redemption must be calculated at the current value of twenty. The fact that *meshichah* finalizes the transaction and locks in the lower price demonstrates that this is a genuine sale, treating *maaser sheni* as *mamon hedyot*. This is, apparently, an unambiguous *stam mishna* like *Rabbi Yehuda*.

The *gemara* concedes: *ella l'olam Rabbi Yehuda* — fine, this *mishna* is *Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Eduyos Authority

### Two vs One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְהָכָא חַד סְתָמָא, וְהָכָא תְּרֵי סְתָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara confirms: **Actually,** this mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Nevertheless, Rav Naḥman's statement that the *halakha* with regard to second tithe is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir is still correct, **as here** there is **one unattributed** mishna that is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, while **here** there are **two unattributed** *mishnayot* that indicate that Beit Hillel's opinion is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, the one in tractate *Ma'aser Sheni*, and one in tractate *Eduyyot* (4:2).

At this point the *gemara* recalibrates. *Ve'hacha chad stama ve'hacha trei stami* — here we have one anonymous *mishna* like *Rabbi Yehuda*, and elsewhere we have two *stam mishnayot* like *Rabbi Meir*. The first two *mishnayot* — the *tevel* case and the *smadar* case — were reinterpreted through strained but technically possible readings; the *meshichah* case is conceded to *Rabbi Yehuda*. But the *mishna* about *Beit Hillel* and *peret u'olelot* from *neta reva'i* appears both in Masechet Ma'aser Sheni and again in Masechet Eduyot, giving us two *stamim* aligned with *Rabbi Meir* against one with *Rabbi Yehuda*. We rule by the majority.

### Eduyos Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סְתָמָא דַּוְקָא, מָה לִי חַד סְתָמָא מָה לִי תְּרֵי סְתָמֵי?! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן בִּבְחִירָתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if an unattributed** mishna is stated that way **specifically** to teach the *halakha*, **what** difference is there **to me** if it is **one unattributed** mishna or **two unattributed** *mishnayot*? The Gemara suggests an alternate reasoning. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, since we learned** in a mishna in the **preferred** tractate, *Eduyyot*, **in accordance with his** opinion. Since the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with all of the *mishnayot* in *Eduyyot*, the fact that the opinion of Beit Hillel in that tractate is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir means that the *halakha* should be decided accordingly.

The *gemara* then raises a sharper objection: *mai li chad stama mai li trei stamei* — what difference does it make whether a *stam* appears once or twice? If a *stam mishna* carries authority because it represents *Rebbe*'s final ruling, why should mere repetition amplify that authority? The same *mishna* appearing in two places should not count as two separate votes.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* therefore offers the true basis for the ruling: *ho'il ve'isnan bi'vchirasa kevasei* — since we have a *stam mishna* like *Rabbi Meir* in the chosen *masechet*, in the *masechet* of choice. That *masechet* is Eduyot. The *mishnayot* of Masechet Eduyot carry greater weight than *mishnayot* elsewhere, because Eduyot is a tractate of authentic, carefully preserved testimonies and traditions. A *stam mishna* in Eduyot therefore carries special authority that tips the balance in favor of *Rabbi Meir*.

### Authority Tension

There is a striking irony embedded in this ruling. Masechet Eduyot was taught, according to the *gemara*, precisely when the doors of the *beit midrash* were opened to all — when *Rabban Gamliel* was replaced as head and a more inclusive policy was adopted. The *mishna* records that on that day the benches had to be multiplied to accommodate the flood of new students. Even ordinary craftsmen — weavers, for instance — came forward and transmitted authentic traditions they had preserved. The very tractate whose *stamim* carry the highest halachic weight was compiled under maximally populist circumstances, admitting testimony from people who would not normally have been considered *talmidei chachamim*. Yet what they transmitted carries more authority, not less. This tension — between elitist and populist sources of authority — runs deep through the structure of *halacha*, and echoes in the ongoing question of how much weight to give communal practice against canonical sources, or canonical sources against communal practice.

This is why we rule that *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah*, following *Rabbi Meir*, even though the natural reading of several *stam mishnayot* supports *Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Wandering Animal

### Animal Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת מִירוּשָׁלַיִם לְמִגְדַּל עֵדֶר וּכְמִדָּתָהּ לְכׇל רוּחַ, זְכָרִים – עוֹלוֹת, נְקֵבוֹת – זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of the desacralizing of consecrated property. **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shekalim* 20a): If there was **an animal** fit for the altar **that was found** straying, **from Jerusalem** and as far **as Migdal Eder, and** similarly if it was found **within that distance** from Jerusalem **in any** other **direction,** it is presumed that the animal came from Jerusalem. Most of the animals in Jerusalem were designated for offerings, and presumably this one was as well. **Males** are presumed to be **burnt-offerings,** as only males can be brought as burnt-offerings. **Females** are presumed to be **peace-offerings,** as it is permitted to bring a female peace-offering.

We now turn to a new topic introduced at the opening of 55a, drawn from a *mishna* in Shekalim. The *mishna* teaches: *behemah shenimtzes miYerushalayim leMigdal Eder* — an animal found wandering in the area between Jerusalem and Migdal Eder, approximately fifteen kilometers south of the city, and within a comparable radius in every direction. *Zcharim olos* — if it is a male, we presume it is an *olah*. *Nekeivos zivchei shelamim* — if it is a female, we presume it is a *shelamim*. The reasoning behind these presumptions is that in the vicinity of Jerusalem, where animals were brought constantly for *korbanot*, and where private individuals generally did not raise livestock within the city itself, a wandering animal is most likely a sacrificial animal.

### Male Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא זְכָרִים, עוֹלוֹת הוּא דְּהָווּ, זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים לָא הָווּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הָכָא בָּבָא לָחוּב בִּדְמֵיהֶן עָסְקִינַן, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא עוֹלוֹת, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּמֵזִיד מִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **But are males** only **burnt-offerings and not** also **peace-offerings?** As peace-offerings can be male or female, a male animal that was found might have been designated as a peace-offering. **Rabbi Oshaya said:** We are not speaking **here** of bringing the animals themselves as offerings. Rather, **we are dealing with one who comes to obligate** himself to consecrate **their value.** The finder wants to consecrate the value of the animal in case it had been designated as an offering, thereby redeeming the animal and desacralizing it, **and this is** what the mishna is **saying:** In the case of males **we are concerned** that **perhaps** they are **burnt-offerings,** so the money must be consecrated for the purpose of burnt-offerings as well as peace-offerings. **And this is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: Consecrated** property **is desacralized** if it is misused **intentionally.** Therefore, he may redeem the animal and must use the money to purchase both a peace-offering and a burnt-offering, thereby avoiding all uncertainty.

The *gemara* immediately interrupts with a question: it is true that all *olos* are male, but it does not follow that all males are *olos*. A *shelamim* can also be male. Why, then, when we find a male animal, do we not also consider the possibility that it might be a *shelamim*?

### Monetary Obligation

*Rabbi Oshaya* explains: *hacha bavo lachov bidmeihem askinan* — what the *mishna* is dealing with is not simple identification of the animal's status, but a situation in which a *tzaddik*, a conscientious person who found this animal, wishes to ensure that the *kedushah* is properly handled and is willing to incur personal monetary obligation to do so. The male has the possibility of being an *olah*, and the female can only be a *shelamim*. The question is what practically to do with such an animal — and that is what the *mishna* and the subsequent *gemara* discussion are working out.

## Me'ilah B'Mezid

### Rashi Dual Dedication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף מִי מִתְחַלֵּל? וְהָתְנַן: אֵין מוֹעֵל אַחַר מוֹעֵל בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** can something that has **inherent sanctity,** such as an animal that has been designated as an offering, **be desacralized? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 19b): **There is no misuse after misuse with regard to consecrated** property? This means that if one misuses consecrated property, the item is immediately desacralized and the prohibition of misuse no longer applies to it. This is so **apart from** the case of the misuse of **an animal** designated as an offering **and** apart **from** the case of the misuse of **service vessels alone.** These do not become desacralized when misused, because they have inherent sanctity.

*Rabbi Oshaya*'s resolution of the *mishna* invokes the position of *Rabbi Meir*: *Rabbi Meir hi de'amar hekdesh b'mezid mitchalel* — it is *Rabbi Meir* who holds that *hekdesh* can be desacralized and transferred to *chullin* even through an intentional act. On *Rashi*'s reading, the procedure works as follows. Since the male animal might be either an *olah* or a *shelamim*, the *tzaddik* takes two separate piles of money, each equal to the animal's value. He declares: if this animal is an *olah*, I am transferring its *kedushah* to pile A, and I am simultaneously dedicating pile B as a *shelamim* as a voluntary gift; if this animal is a *shelamim*, I am transferring its *kedushah* to pile B, and I am simultaneously dedicating pile A as an *olah* as a voluntary gift. The result is that one pile carries the transferred *kedushah* and the other carries his own voluntary dedication — one is an *olah* and one is a *shelamim* — and the animal itself is redeemed. Both the *olah* and *shelamim* obligations are thus fulfilled, covering whichever the animal actually was.

### Tosafot Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** That mishna, from tractate *Me'ila*, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** whereas **this** mishna, from tractate *Shekalim*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

*Tosafot* reads the mechanism differently, and while this reading is probably closer to the *pshat* of the *gemara*, it is striking enough that one can understand why *Rashi* sought an alternative. According to *Tosafot*, the *tzaddik* — precisely because he wishes to properly resolve the animal's status — takes the animal for himself. This is an act of *me'ilah*. He is fully aware of what he is doing; it is *b'mezid*. According to *Rabbi Meir*, *hekdesh b'mezid mitchalel* — this intentional act of *me'ilah* removes the *kedushah* from the animal and renders it *chullin*. The *tzaddik* then contributes both an *olah* and a *shelamim* to the *beit hamikdash* from his own funds, thus ensuring that the *beit hamikdash* receives its due regardless. He has, in effect, exposed himself to the transgression of *me'ilah* in order to resolve the animal's problematic status — hence the phrase *bavo lachov bidmeihem*, coming to incur an obligation commensurate with their value.

### Kedusha Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נִשְׁמַע לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – לָאו אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּשׁוֹגֵג מִתְחַלֵּל וּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף לָא מִתַּחֲלָא. לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּמֵזִיד מִתְחַלֵּל קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף לָא מִתַּחֲלָא! הָתָם לָא קָא מְכַוֵּין לְאַפּוֹקִינְהוּ לְחוּלִּין. הָכָא – קָא מְכַוֵּין לְאַפּוֹקִינְהוּ לְחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us hear,** i.e., infer, **from** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir: Didn't Rabbi Yehuda say** that **consecrated** property **is desacralized** if used **unwittingly, and** yet those items that have **inherent sanctity are not desacralized.** According **to Rabbi Meir as well, although consecrated** property **is desacralized** if used **intentionally,** items that have **inherent sanctity** should **not be desacralized.** The Gemara answers that there is a difference between the two opinions. **There,** in the case underlying Rabbi Yehuda's opinion, the one who misuses the consecrated item unwittingly **does not intend to withdraw it to a non-sacred** state but merely to use it, which is why an item that has inherent sanctity is not desacralized; whereas **here,** in the case underlying Rabbi Meir's opinion, one who acts intentionally **does intend to withdraw it to a non-sacred** state, so even an item that has inherent sanctity can be desacralized.

The *gemara* raises the obvious difficulty: even granting *Rabbi Meir*'s position that *me'ilah b'mezid* can remove *kedushah*, that principle was articulated with respect to *hekdesh* funds — money or property owned by the *beit hamikdash*, which is *bedek habayis*. *Bedek habayis* is essentially the *beit hamikdash*'s bank account: the institution owns the asset and can dispose of it as it sees fit. It is understandable that an act of misappropriation could sever that ownership relationship and render the object *chullin*.

But an animal dedicated as a *korban* has *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity. Its *kedushah* does not consist merely in being owned by the *beit hamikdash*; rather, the *korban* itself, in its very identity, is sacred. Why would an act of *me'ilah*, even *b'mezid*, have the power to remove a *kedushah* that defines the object's essential nature? The question becomes: does *me'ilah b'mezid* extend to *kedushat haguf*?

## Kedushat HaGuf

### Multiple Me'ilot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? הָיָה רוֹכֵב עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וְרָכַב, בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וְרָכַב – כּוּלָּם מָעֲלוּ. הָיָה שׁוֹתֶה בְּכוֹס שֶׁל זָהָב, בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וְשָׁתָה, בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וְשָׁתָה – כּוּלָּם מָעֲלוּ! הָהִיא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** That mishna continues: **How** so? If someone **was riding on** a consecrated **animal,** and **another came** after him **and** also **rode** on it, and yet **another came and rode** on it, **they have all misused** consecrated property. Similarly, if one **was drinking from a golden cup** used in the Temple service, and **another came and drank** from it, and yet **another came and drank** from it, **they have all misused** consecrated property. The Gemara asks: As this indicates that an item that has inherent sanctity is never desacralized, how can it be redeemed? The Gemara answers: **That** mishna, from tractate *Me'ila*, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** whereas **this** mishna, from tractate *Shekalim*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

The *gemara* brings a *mishna* that bears directly on this question. The *mishna* states: *ein moel achar moel b'mukkdashim ella b'vehema u've'klei shares bilvad* — one cannot do multiple acts of *me'ilah* on the same sanctified object, except in the case of an animal (*korban*) or the sacred vessels (*klei shares*). The reason one generally cannot transgress *me'ilah* twice on the same object is that once *me'ilah* has been committed, the object exits *kedushah* and becomes *chullin* — there is nothing sacred left to misuse a second time. The exception for animals and *klei shares* demonstrates that these objects, possessing *kedushat haguf*, retain their *kedushah* even after an act of *me'ilah*. Multiple individuals can ride upon a *korban* animal or drink from a sacred golden goblet, and each one commits *me'ilah*, because the *kedushah* of these objects is not extinguished by being misused.

### Survival Question

This *mishna* poses a sharp problem. If *kedushat haguf* survives even *me'ilah*, how can *me'ilah b'mezid* be said to remove the *kedushah* from this wandering animal? The very *mishna* just cited teaches that *kedushat haguf* persists through *me'ilah*.

### Strength of Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים, בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? – אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב שְׁמֵיהּ: קַל וָחוֹמֶר: קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים מִתְחַלְּלִים – קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Oshaya's explanation of the mishna: **Say that you have heard Rabbi Meir** express the opinion that one can desacralize a consecrated item **in** the case of **offerings of the most sacred order,** but **did you hear him** express this opinion **in** the case of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., a peace-offering? If Rabbi Meir holds that offerings of lesser sanctity cannot be desacralized, the explanation of Rabbi Oshaya would be insufficient, as it does not account for the possibility that the animal is a peace-offering. **One of the Sages, and his name was Rabbi Ya'akov, said to him:** This is learned by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference: If **offerings of the most sacred order** can **be desacralized,** is it **not all the more so** the case that this *halakha* would apply to **offerings of lesser sanctity?**

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the positions of *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir*. *Rav amar Rabbi Yehuda hekdesh b'shogeg mitchalel kedushat haguf lo mitchalel* — *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that *hekdesh* goes out to *chullin* only *b'shogeg*, not *b'mezid*, and moreover, *kedushat haguf* never goes out to *chullin* through *me'ilah* at all. One then asks: why should *Rabbi Meir* disagree on this latter point? Even if *Rabbi Meir* extends the effect of *me'ilah* to *b'mezid*, why should that change the rule regarding *kedushat haguf*?

The *gemara* answers with a crucial conceptual distinction. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *me'ilah* operates only *b'shogeg*. An inadvertent misuse is, inherently, a weak act — one did not intend it, did not consciously strive to appropriate or to desacralize. Because the act is weak, it can sever a relationship of ownership (*kedushat damim*, the *beit hamikdash*'s proprietary claim), but it cannot penetrate and negate a *kedushah* that defines the object's very identity. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the *mishna*'s rule that *kedushat haguf* survives *me'ilah* is entirely coherent: *me'ilah b'shogeg* is simply not a strong enough act to dislodge something so fundamental.

But according to *Rabbi Meir*, *me'ilah* can occur *b'mezid* — and *me'ilah b'mezid* is qualitatively different. When a person consciously, deliberately intends to remove an object from the domain of the *beit hamikdash* and take it into his own possession — *hacha ka michaven la'apukeia lechullin*, here he is intending to bring it out to *chullin* — that is an act of a fundamentally stronger character. It is, in a sense, the most aggressive possible form of appropriation. And according to *Rabbi Meir*, an act of that strength is sufficient to negate even *kedushat haguf*. The intuition is compelling: if any act is going to change the status of something intrinsically holy, it would be a maximally intentional, deliberate effort to desacralize it, not an accidental stumble.

The *gemara* further develops this: *amar lei hahu me'rabbanan, Rabbi Yaakov shemei, kal vachomer* — one of the rabbis, a figure named *Rabbi Yaakov*, argued from a *kal vachomer*: if *kodshei kodashim*, the most holy things, can be brought out to *chullin* through *me'ilah b'mezid* according to *Rabbi Meir*, then *kodashim kalim* — things of lesser sanctity, like a *shelamim*, which the owner himself will eat — should certainly be susceptible to losing their *kedushah*, since their sanctity is of a lower order and therefore more easily displaced. One might have thought the reverse: that a lower level of *kedushah* is more resistant to removal because it is less fragile. But the *kal vachomer* presses the other direction — lesser *kedushah* yields more readily, not less.

This is confirmed in a *baraita*: *amar Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Yaakov amar Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina, haya Rabbi Meir omer* — *Rabbi Meir* used to say: *hekdesh b'mezid mitchalel, b'shogeg ein mitchalel, echad kodshei kodashim v'echad kodashim kalim* — *hekdesh* goes out to *chullin* through intentional *me'ilah*, not through inadvertent *me'ilah*, and this applies equally to *kodshei kodashim* and to *kodashim kalim*. The *kal vachomer* is then stated explicitly.

### Sin Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּמֵזִיד – מִתְחַלֵּל, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – אֵין מִתְחַלֵּל, אֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים וְאֶחָד קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים. קַל וָחוֹמֶר: קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים מִתְחַלְּלִים – קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated: Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Akiva, says** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** that **Rabbi Meir would say: Consecrated** property **is desacralized** when misused **intentionally,** but it **is not desacralized** when misused **unwittingly.** This applies **both** to **offerings of the most sacred order and** to **offerings of lesser sanctity,** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference: If **offerings of the most sacred order** can **be desacralized,** is it **not all the more so** the case that this *halakha* would apply to **offerings of lesser sanctity?**

*Rabbi Yochanan* was astounded by the reading of *Rabbi Oshaya*: *V'chi omrim lo l'adam amod v'chata bishvil shetizkeh* — does one tell a person, "Go and sin so that things will work out properly with this animal"? Could the *mishna* actually be instructing someone to commit *me'ilah* deliberately as a matter of course?

## Blemish Solution

### Male Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְכִי אוֹמְרִים לוֹ לָאָדָם: עֲמוֹד וַחֲטָא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּזְכֶּה? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַמְתִּין לַהּ עַד שֶׁתּוּמַם, וּמַיְיתֵי שְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת וּמַתְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan wondered about** Rabbi Oshaya's explanation of the mishna: **And does one say to a person: Arise and sin in order that you may gain?** Even if consecrated property can be desacralized intentionally, one is not allowed to redeem an animal designated to be an offering. **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna does not refer to redeeming an unblemished animal but to a case where the one who found the animal **waits for it until it develops a blemish.** At that point it would no longer have inherent sanctity but merely sanctity that inheres in its value, so the animal may be redeemed. **And he brings two** unblemished **animals** of the same value, **and stipulates** that if the animal he found was a burnt-offering, the first animal should be a burnt-offering in its stead while the other should be a voluntary peace-offering. And if the animal he found was a peace-offering, the second animal should be a peace-offering in its stead and the first should be a voluntary burnt-offering. After doing so he may eat the animal he found.

*Rabbi Yochanan* therefore offers an entirely different reading of the *mishna*, free of any intentional transgression. *Mamtin lo ad sheyista'eiv* — one simply waits until the animal develops a *mum*, a blemish. Once it has a *mum*, it can be redeemed without restriction. Having redeemed the blemished animal, one then brings two substitute animals and makes a conditional declaration: if this wandering animal is an *olah*, I am transferring its *kedushah* to animal A, and I am voluntarily dedicating animal B as a *shelamim*; if it is a *shelamim*, I am transferring its *kedushah* to animal B, and I am voluntarily dedicating animal A as an *olah*. One then brings both animals. The *tzaddik* is out the cost of two animals, but no transgression has occurred.

### Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: זְכָרִים – עוֹלוֹת. דִּילְמָא תּוֹדָה הִיא? דְּמַיְיתֵי נָמֵי תּוֹדָה. וְהָא בָּעֲיָא לֶחֶם! דְּמַיְיתֵי נָמֵי לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to clarify the mishna. **The Master says** (55a): **Males** are presumed to be **burnt-offerings,** as only males may be brought as burnt-offerings. The Gemara asks: Why should a male necessarily be a burnt-offering; **perhaps it is a thanks-offering,** which can also be brought from a male animal? The Gemara answers: The mishna means **that** he must **also bring** a third animal and make a similar stipulation, that it should be either **a thanks-offering** in its stead or a voluntary one. The Gemara asks: **But** if he brings a third animal as a thanks-offering, doesn't a thanks-offering **require** the bringing of **bread** as well? The Gemara answers: **He brings bread as well.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges: why only two animals? *Dilma todah hi* — perhaps the animal is a *todah*, a thanksgiving offering, which is a subspecies of *shelamim* that can be brought by a male or female animal. One would then need a third animal to cover this possibility. *D'meisei nami todah* — fine, bring three animals. But *v'ha ba'ya lechem* — a *todah* requires bread loaves to accompany it. *D'meisei nami lechem* — bring the bread as well. If the animal turns out to be a *todah*, the *kedushah* transferred to the corresponding animal plus its bread; if not, the extra animal and bread were a voluntary *todah* dedication.

### Age Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא אָשָׁם הוּא? אָשָׁם בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, וְאִישְׁתְּכַח בֶּן שָׁנָה. וְדִילְמָא אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע הוּא, אֲשַׁם נָזִיר הוּא? לָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it is a guilt-offering,** which is also brought only from male animals? The Gemara answers: The animal for **a guilt-offering** is **in its second year, and** the mishna is referring to a case where **an** animal **in its first year was found.** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it is the guilt-offering of a leper** or **the guilt-offering of a nazirite,** which are brought from animals in their first year? The Gemara answers: Lepers and nazirites are **not common,** and there is no need to be concerned about that possibility.

The *gemara* then asks: *v'dilma asham hu* — perhaps it is an *asham*, a guilt offering, which is male, presents a more serious problem because an *asham* cannot be brought as a voluntary *nedavah*. The *gemara* answers: *asham ben shtei shanim* — an *asham* must be two years old. Since the case in the *mishna* involves a one-year-old animal, the possibility of *asham* is excluded by age. The *gemara* further raises the possibility of *asham metzora* or *asham nazir*, both of which require one-year animals, but these are dismissed as uncommon enough to set aside — *lo shchiach*.

### Pesach and Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא פֶּסַח הוּא? פֶּסַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ, מִזְהָר זְהִירִי בֵּיהּ, וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ – שְׁלָמִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it is a Paschal offering,** which is also brought from male animals? The Gemara answers: That is not likely, because with regard to **a Paschal offering,** if it is up to or **in its time** to be slaughtered, the owners **carefully guard it** to prevent it from going missing. **And** if it is a Paschal offering **that** is **not** offered **in its** proper **time,** but was left over and is offered later, **it** has the same *halakha* **as a peace-offering.**

*V'dilma pesach hu* — perhaps it is a *pesach* offering, which is also one year and male. The *gemara* answers that *pesach bizmano*, in its proper season, *miz'harei zehirei bei* — people are careful with their *pesach* animals, and one need not be concerned about a stray *pesach* wandering unattended. *V'shelo bizmano* — and if we are past the time of *pesach*, a *pesach* that was not offered in its time reverts to the status of a *shelamim*, and *shelamim* is already covered.

*V'dilma bechor u'maaser ninhu* — perhaps it is a *bechor* or *maaser beheimah*, both of which are male and one year old. The *gemara* answers: *g'mai hilchasa l'michli'inhu b'muman* — the established *halacha* for both *bechor* and *maaser* is that once they develop a *mum*, the owners may eat them outside the *beit hamikdash* without further ceremony. Since one is already waiting for a *mum*, this scenario is automatically handled: if the animal turns out to be a *bechor* or *maaser*, the owner simply eats it once the *mum* appears. *Hacha nami b'muman mi'achli* — here too, one eats it upon the *mum*.

### Female Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָ חַטָּאת הִיא? חַטָּאת בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ, וְאִישְׁתְּכַח בַּת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים. וְדִילְמָא חַטָּאת שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ! לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it is a sin-offering,** as it is permitted to bring a female sin-offering? The Gemara answers: The animal for **a sin-offering** is always **in its first year, and** the mishna is referring to a case where **an** animal **in its second year was found.** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps** it was **a sin-offering whose year had passed** without having been sacrificed? The *halakha* in such a case is that the animal is left to die. The Gemara answers: Such a case is **not common,** and there is no need to be concerned about such a possibility.

The *gemara* turns to the second part of the *mishna*: *nekeiva zivchei shelamim* — if it is a female, it is treated as a *shelamim*. The *gemara* initially questions why one cannot simply bring it directly — but the problem is *dilma todah hi*, it might be a *todah*, which is also female. Fine, one brings a *todah* as well and covers the bread requirement. But then: *v'dilma chatas hi* — perhaps it is a *chatat*, which is also female and which cannot be brought as a voluntary *nedavah*. If this animal might be a *chatat*, one cannot simply bring it as a *shelamim*.

The *gemara* notes that a *chatat* must be in its first year of life — *chatas bas shnata* — and accordingly, if the found female animal is two years old, the *chatat* possibility is eliminated. But if it is one year old, *ishtakach bas shnata mai* — what then?

### Cell Confinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשְׁתְּכַח בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ, מַאי? תַּנְיָא, חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן חֲכִינַאי אוֹמֵר: עֵז בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ לְחַטָּאת. לְחַטָּאת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּחַטָּאת – כּוֹנְסָהּ לְכִיפָּה וְהִיא מֵתָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This is the case if the animal was in its second year, but **what** is the *halakha* if it **was found** when it was **in its first year?** The Gemara answers: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai says:** If one found **a goat in its first year,** it is brought **as a sin-offering.** The Gemara asks: **Can it enter your mind** that it is brought **as a sin**-**offering?** How can he sacrifice the animal as a sin-offering due to a mere possibility that it had been designated as a sin-offering? One cannot bring a voluntary sin-offering. **Rather, Abaye says:** He treats it **as** though it were **a sin-offering,** i.e., he **conveys it into a cell and it dies on its own.** Since it might be a lost sin-offering, it must be left to die.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in which *Chananya ben Chachinai* teaches that a one-year-old female goat — *ez bas shnata l'chatas* — evokes the verse's language for a *chatat*. The *gemara* immediately asks: but perhaps it is not a *chatat* at all — perhaps it is a *shelamim*? *Ella amar Abaye: k'chatas* — *Abaye* answers that it must be treated as a *chatat*. What does that mean in practice? *Konas'la l'chippa v'hi mesa mei'aleha* — one confines it in a cell and allows it to die on its own. There is no offering one can bring for it, since one cannot voluntarily bring a *chatat* that does not correspond to a specific obligation. Any other *kedushah* the animal might have — if it were a *shelamim* — can be transferred conditionally to substitute animals and dealt with through *olah* and *todah* dedications, allowing those to proceed, while the animal itself is left in confinement until it dies.
