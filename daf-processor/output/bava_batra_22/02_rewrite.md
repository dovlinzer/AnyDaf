# Bava Batra 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hasagat Gvul Overview

### Hasagat Gvul Definition

Today's daf is 22, and we find ourselves in the midst of a fascinating discussion of a category familiar to many: *hasagat gvul*, encroaching upon another person's territory of *parnassa*. This is at its core an interesting policy question — a tension between protectionism and the free market. On one hand, there is a natural impulse to protect certain individuals from devastating competition; on the other hand, one must ask how such protection ultimately serves the general population. That is the overarching issue the *Gemara* has raised.

### Rav Huna's Mill Rule

The position that opens the discussion is that of Rav Huna, in the name of Rav: if someone already has a *reychayim* — a mill — operating in their *mavoi*, one cannot open up a competing mill in that same *mavoi*. The reasoning is straightforward: the original owner was already there, and the newcomer is unfairly encroaching upon his livelihood.

### Chachamim vs R' Yehuda

The *Gemara* then asks how this ruling fits with the dispute between the *Chachamim* and R' Yehuda recorded elsewhere. The case there involves two merchants: if one of them distributes free candy or nuts to children in order to draw all the business to himself, R' Yehuda holds this to be impermissible, since the merchant is not competing on the genuine merits of his goods but is simply using an enticement. The *Chachamim* disagree, ruling that the other merchant is equally free to adopt the same strategy.

The *Gemara* concludes that the two cases are not directly comparable. According to the *Chachamim*, they may well be prepared to protect a business owner from a competitor who moves in fresh — yet in the case of the candy distribution, both merchants were already present and operating, and either one could freely replicate the other's approach. Conversely, one could argue the other way as well: even R' Yehuda, who seeks to protect against unfair business practices, might permit the opening of a competing store outright, since just as the *Gemara* on the preceding daf observed *kinat sofrim tarbe chochma* — competition among Torah teachers sharpens everyone — so too competition among business owners can improve quality and reduce prices. The *Gemara* is thus indicating that the two issues operate along genuinely different axes.

## Property Rights Framework

### Customers as Property

One illuminating way of understanding the entire *hasagat gvul* framework is through the lens of property rights. When the *Gemara* speaks of *hasagat gvul*, the very term evokes moving a boundary marker — literally encroaching on what belongs to another. The question, then, is: when does one *own* something one expects to receive? If a business owner is sufficiently certain to receive a customer's patronage, that anticipated income can be conceptualized as something already in his possession. To divert it is therefore not merely unfair competition — it is akin to theft; one is taking something that already, in a meaningful sense, belongs to the other person.

This property-rights framing may also explain why the *Gemara* does not extend R' Yehuda's position to cover the case of opening a competing store in the *mavoi*. If R' Yehuda's concern is fundamentally one of theft, then even the candy-distribution case could be framed as a kind of taking — and *kal vachomer*, the problem would apply even more acutely when a competitor has not yet opened at all.

### Fishnet Case

This framework connects naturally to a question the *Gemara* raised earlier: how does the *hasagat gvul* discussion fit with the case of spreading a fishnet near a location another fisherman has already staked out? The *Gemara* noted that the staked-out case may be different precisely because the fisherman has drawn the fish to that spot — the fish are, in a sense, already in his hands. This raises the broader question of how much we treat customers as something already belonging to the business owner. The fish case represents a genuine, tangible property claim: ultimately, the fish will be physically captured. Customers, of course, are not taken home in a net — but the conceptual question of whether we frame the situation as a policy concern or as an already-established ownership claim remains the animating tension throughout the discussion. As for the precise measure of *kimlo ritzas hadag* and the limit of *ad parsa*, the *Gemara* itself does not resolve it with precision.

## Baraita Stores Rule

### Stores Next Door

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge — *meisvi* — from a *baraita*: *oseh adam chanut b'tzad chanuto shel chaveiro* — a person may open a store immediately adjacent to his fellow's store; *u'merchatz betzad merchatzato shel chaveiro* — and similarly a bathhouse immediately next to another's bathhouse. *Ve'eino yachol limchot beyado* — the established owner cannot prevent him. Why? Because one can simply say: *atah oseh betoch shelcha va'ani oseh betoch sheli* — you do as you wish on your property and I do as I wish on mine.

This formulation resonates with earlier discussions about physical damage caused indirectly — the principle that one may do as one wishes on one's own property even when the consequences spill over onto a neighbor's interests. If the result of exercising that freedom happens to be that business is diverted from a competitor, that is not the established owner's concern. On the other hand, there are contexts — such as a *chatzer* whose residents collectively wish to maintain residential rather than commercial character — where zoning-like agreements among co-owners can restrict what is permissible. That scenario represents a communal property interest distinct from the simple neighbor-vs.-neighbor dispute.

### Mavoi vs Property

The distinction between *mavoi* rules and ordinary neighbor conflicts is therefore significant. When the *mavoi* as a whole collectively holds an interest in a space — as a semi-public, collectively owned domain — its residents can exercise certain controls over who operates within it. But where the dispute is simply between two neighbors each acting within their own private property, the default rule is that one cannot prevent the other from opening a competing business.

### Competition Benefits

The economic dimension of this permission deserves attention. It might seem intuitive that having multiple competing businesses in a single location would harm each of them — the lone kosher restaurant on a given block would appear to benefit from having no competitor nearby. Yet economists have found that when competing businesses concentrate in the same location, they can collectively create a destination, driving more overall traffic to the area so that all benefit. The *shuk* of *Machane Yehudah* is a vivid illustration: blocks of sandal vendors, blocks of butchers — all thriving precisely because the concentration makes the entire area a destination. Whether this observation holds depends on circumstances, of course — if the available customer base is too limited to sustain two businesses, opening a competitor may simply ensure that both fail. The size and density of the community, the degree of differentiation between offerings, and the overall market are all relevant parameters that the *Gemara* leaves to practical judgment.

## Tanna'im Debate

### Tannaim Dispute

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between Rav Huna's ruling and the permissive *baraita* by noting that it is in fact a debate among *Tannaim* — *tanai*. The *baraita* elaborates: *kofin benei mavo'ot zeh et zeh shelo lehoshiv beneihem lo chayat velo bursaki velo melamed tinokot* — the residents of a *mavoi* may collectively compel one another not to install among them a tailor, a tanner, or a schoolteacher. The underlying concern, as Rashi explains, is specifically about preventing outsiders from opening businesses within the *mavoi*. The collective ownership of the *mavoi* allows its residents to exclude those who have no inherent right to be there, just as one may exclude strangers from private property. *Ul'shcheno eino kofeh'u* — but one cannot exclude a neighbor who actually resides in the *mavoi* from opening such a business.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *af lshcheno kofeh'u* — one may indeed compel even a neighbor, a fellow resident of the *mavoi*, to refrain from opening a competing business. The *Gemara* reads this dispute as addressing not the foot-traffic concern of the *Mishnah* but the question of commercial competition: can a *mavoi* resident be stopped from opening a competing enterprise by another resident who shares the same right to be there?

### Outsiders vs Residents

Rav Huna breih de-Rav Yehoshua clarifies what he considers obvious: *bar mata bar mata achrita matzi me'akev* — a resident of one town can certainly prevent a resident of a different town from opening a competing business, since the *mavoi* belongs, collectively, to the townspeople, and an outsider simply has no standing there.

### Same Tax Rule

However, there is an important qualification. *Ve'ei shayich becharga dehacha lo matzi me'akev* — if both parties pay the same tax to the same municipal authority, the outsider is not truly foreign and cannot be excluded on that basis alone. As the *Rishonim* explain, this applies to cases where the outsider is not encroaching on anyone's private courtyard or *mavoi* but is operating from a genuinely public space — a pushcart in the middle of the street, for instance. In that case, since he is effectively a fellow member of the same civic community, he cannot be prevented from conducting business in the public domain.

### Cross-Mavoi Question

A further question remains unresolved. Rav Huna breih de-Rav Yehoshua established that once someone has a right to be in a *mavoi*, they may open a competing business there and cannot be stopped — *bar mavoi l'bar mavoi d'nafsheih lo matzi m'akev*. But what of the case of *bar mavoi l'bar mavoi achrina* — a person who lives in one *mavoi* and wishes to open a competing store in a different *mavoi* within the same town? Both parties pay the same taxes and both belong to the same town. The question becomes: can the residents of this *mavoi* exclude someone from another *mavoi* who wishes to operate here?

The irony is that proximity ordinarily suggests greater competition — the closer one is, the more directly one encroaches. Yet the legal logic runs in the opposite direction: the more one "belongs" to a space, the more one's right to operate there is protected. Can the residents of one *mavoi* assert a quasi-ownership interest strong enough to exclude a fellow townsman from another *mavoi* who wishes to open a shop in their domain? The *Gemara* leaves this without resolution — *teiku*.

## Educational Overrides

### Schools Exception

Rav Yosef now observes that even Rav Huna, who takes the strictest position on protecting existing businesses from competition, agrees in one domain: *b'makrei dardakei d'lo matzi me'akev* — one cannot prevent a competing schoolteacher from opening up even where one would ordinarily be able to block a competing business. Competition in education is not suppressed even by those most committed to protecting commercial incumbents.

### Kinat Sofrim

The reason given is the principle invoked by the *Gemara* itself: *d'amar mar kinat sofrim tarbeh chochmah* — the competitive spirit among Torah scholars increases wisdom. When it comes to Torah education, the value of achieving the highest possible quality of instruction is so paramount that it overrides any concern for protecting the incumbent teacher's livelihood. The same logic that might, in a commercial context, tip the balance toward protecting an established business is decisively outweighed, in the educational sphere, by the overriding communal interest in Torah learning. This is structurally analogous to the *Mishnah*'s ruling that while residents of a *chatzer* may block businesses that generate disruptive foot traffic, they may not block a school — education is simply a value of a different order.

### Ezra's Takkanah

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak similarly observes that Rav Huna concedes a second override: *berochlin hamachzirim ba'ayarot dalo matzi me'akev* — the itinerant merchants known as *rochlin* who travel from town to town cannot be prevented from selling their wares, including fragrances and jewelry, even if local establishments carry similar goods. The reason is that this category of commerce is protected by one of Ezra's foundational *takanot*: *Ezra tiken lahem l'Yisrael sheyehu rochlin machzirim ba'ayarot kedei sheyehu tachshitim metzuyan livnot Yisrael* — Ezra instituted that itinerant merchants should circulate through the towns so that jewelry and adornments would be readily available to Jewish women. The protection of women's access to these goods constitutes the same kind of overriding communal concern as Torah education, and it therefore supersedes the normal protections afforded to local businesses.

### Rochlin Restrictions

Nevertheless, this dispensation has its limits. *Le'ahaduri* — merely passing through and selling as one goes — is permitted. *Aval le'akvei* — but establishing a permanent presence in the town — is not. An itinerant merchant who decides to park in a given town indefinitely and set up shop there has crossed from the protected category of the traveling *rochel* into the category of an outside competitor, and the town's residents can prevent him.

There is, however, an exception. *Ve'ee tzurba me'rabanan hee* — if the merchant is a *talmid chacham* — *afilu le'akvei nami* — he may establish himself permanently even if he comes from outside the town. The *Gemara* cites the ruling of Rava, who permitted Rav Yoshiya and Rav Ovadiah to establish shops in a town where they were not residents, contrary to the standard *halacha*. The reason: *keivan derabanan ninhu atu letirdumei migirsaihu* — since they are Torah scholars, requiring them to travel constantly from town to town would severely curtail their opportunity to learn Torah. The interest in sustaining the quality of their *talmud Torah* is sufficient to grant them this advantage.

### Rava's Exception

This ruling raises a pointed question: does not such an arrangement turn Torah into a *kardom lachpor bo*, a tool for personal financial gain? Rashi addresses this candidly. His comment reads *dalo kehilchata* — this is not strictly according to the law — but *ela lifnim mishurat hadin* — it is rather an application of the principle of *lifnim mishurat hadin*. What makes this a striking application of the principle is that *lifnim mishurat hadin* ordinarily describes a person voluntarily surrendering his own entitlements for the benefit of another. Here, the ruling effectively obligates the townspeople to surrender their legitimate right to exclude outside competitors — they are being asked to act *lifnim mishurat hadin* toward these rabbis, accepting a concession for the sake of Torah. One is, in effect, *paskening lifnim mishurat hadin* for the town.

## Market Day Rules

### Basket Sellers Case

The *Gemara* turns to a related scenario. Outside sellers of wicker baskets — *dikulei* — brought their wares to Babylonia from elsewhere. The townspeople of Mechuza sought to prevent them from selling, on the grounds that they were competing with local businesses. Since everyone agrees — whether following Rav Huna or otherwise — that outsiders can be excluded from encroaching on local commerce, the claim of the townspeople appeared straightforward. Yet Ravina permitted the outside sellers to proceed. The reason lies in the phrase *me'alma atu ule'alma lizbenu* — they came from the general population and therefore they may sell to the general population. The key is that on a day when customers from the surrounding region have gathered, the sellers are not specifically targeting the local townspeople's customer base; they are serving the broader, transient population that has assembled precisely because it is a market day.

### Market Day Limits

The *Gemara* makes the scope of this permission precise. *Vehanei milei beyoma d'shuka* — this applies only on the market day itself, when the town becomes a regional hub and its customer base is no longer exclusively local. *Aval belo yoma d'shuka lo* — on any other day, the outside sellers are genuinely displacing local business, and the townspeople may prevent them.

Even on a market day, the permission is limited in a second way. *Ella lizbonei beshuka* — the outside merchants may sell only in the marketplace itself. *Aval le'ahadurei lo* — they may not go door to door. This is coherent: the market is the space that belongs, in a meaningful sense, to the regional public on that day; the streets and homes of the town remain, in some sense, the private domain of its residents.

### Tosafot Moneylenders

Tosafot applies this principle to a significant practical context. The relevant phrase in the *Gemara* — *me'alma atu ule'alma lizbenu* — implies a permission to serve the broader outside population that comes to the market. Tosafot reasons that on a market day, an outside moneylender may lend money to non-Jews and to non-residents who have come from elsewhere specifically for the market: *lehalvos livne adam habaim mekomos sham leshuk ulenochrim deato mealma*. However, *aval livne makom lo* — he may not lend to the permanent residents of that town. Furthermore, *vedavka beshuka aval adure lo* — his lending activity must be confined to the marketplace; he may not pursue clients door to door, nor operate outside the designated market space.

This is a notably narrow reading of the *Gemara*. Where the plain sense of *le'alma lizbenu* might be taken to mean simply that on a market day one may sell to anyone who comes, Tosafot understands it to mean that the outside seller is limited to those who are themselves outsiders — people who, like the seller, have come from elsewhere. Whether Rashi would read the *Gemara* with equal restrictiveness is less clear from his commentary.

## Wool Merchants Case

### Town's Right

The *Gemara* continues with a parallel case. Outside wool merchants brought their wool to the riverbank — *amlorai de'aisu imra lefum nahara* — and the townspeople sought to block them from selling. Rav Kahana's initial ruling was unequivocal: *dina hu demaakvi alaichu* — the townspeople are legally correct; they have a right to prevent outside merchants from selling and competing with local business.

### Debt Collection Claim

The outside merchants, however, responded: *islan ashrai* — we have outstanding debts to collect here; local buyers have purchased goods from us on credit and have not yet paid. They were not, therefore, simply arriving to undercut local merchants — they had a legitimate prior claim on the town that required their presence.

### Minimal Livelihood Rule

Rav Kahana crafted a balanced response. *Zilu zivnu shir chyusaichu* — you may sell, but only in an amount sufficient to cover your basic living expenses while you are here, nothing more. *Ad deakrisu ashraichu vesilu* — sell only until you have collected your outstanding debts, and then depart. This ruling reflects the principle that the rights of the townspeople, while real, are not absolute. Just as a *talmid chacham* may be granted special access because his Torah learning requires it, so too a merchant with legitimate prior obligations cannot simply be turned away — he must be given a minimum foothold sufficient to discharge those obligations. The right to collect what is owed to you, and to sustain yourself while doing so, limits the exclusionary power of the local community, even if it does not override it entirely.

## Rav Dimi's Figs

### Figs Import

Rav Dimi of Nehardea brought a shipment of pressed dried figs by boat. The Resh Galuta instructed Rava: *puk chazi* — go find out and examine him. *Ee tzorba merabanan hu nakit lei shuka* — if he is a genuine *talmid chacham*, secure for him the right to sell in the market. This invokes exactly the special dispensation discussed above — the privilege extended to Torah scholars to operate commercially even as outsiders. Rashi goes further: *nakeit lei shuka* in his reading means *hachriz she-lo yimkor ish ba-ir grogros ela hu* — an announcement should be made that no one else in the city may sell dried figs, granting Rav Dimi an exclusive monopoly.

Rava delegated the task of examining Rav Dimi to his student Rav Adda bar Ahava, using the expression *puk taamei lei be-kankanei* — go smell his barrel, i.e., taste his wine. The idiom means: go examine his interior, probe the quality of his scholarship.

### Elephant Basket Test

Rav Adda bar Ahava went and posed his question — the most demanding one he could devise. He asked: if an elephant swallows an Egyptian wicker basket (*kfifa mitzrit*) and excretes it through the intestinal tract, what is the *halakhic* status of the object? The underlying issue, as Tosafot explains, is whether the resulting material could be fashioned into a vessel: a vessel made of wicker is susceptible to *tumah*, while a vessel made of excrement is not. What is the status of wicker that has passed through an elephant's digestive system? *Lo hava bideih* — Rav Dimi did not have the answer at hand.

### Rav Adda's Rejection

Having failed the examination, Rav Dimi asked his examiner: *Mar nihu Rava?* — Are you Rava? He was new to the town and did not know that Rav Adda was Rava's student rather than Rava himself. Rav Adda's response was physical: *tapach lei be-sandaleih* — he kicked him on his sandal. Rashi clarifies that this means he kicked Rav Dimi on his foot, not that he struck him with his sandal. He then said: *bein didi le-Rava ika tuva* — between me and Rava there is a very great gap. But the implication was clear: between Rav Dimi and Rav Adda, there was an equally large gap in the other direction. *Ana ravach ve-Rava raba de-ravach* — I am your rebbe, and Rava is your rebbe's rebbe. *Lo nakeitu lei shuka* — Rav Dimi was denied the monopoly right to sell in the market.

### Figs Spoil

The consequences were serious: *psadu grogros dideih* — all of his figs spoiled. Rav Dimi went to Rav Yosef and reported what had been done to him. Rav Yosef responded with a pointed invocation: *man de-la sheya le-unisa de-malka de-Edom lo nish-ha le-unisach* — He who did not withhold avenging the affliction of the king of Edom will not withhold avenging your affliction. The proof-text was from Amos: *koh amar Hashem al shlosha pishei Moav ve-al arbaah lo ashivenu al sarpo atzmot melech Edom la-sid* — thus said God: for three sins of Moab and for four I will not relent — because they burned the bones of the king of Edom into lime. Even the honor of a foreign king, desecrated after his death, was avenged by God. Rav Yosef was invoking the same principle on behalf of Rav Dimi.

## Rav Adda's Death

### Death Aftermath

*Nach nafshei de-Rav Ada bar Abba* — Rav Adda bar Abba died. What follows is a remarkable narrative: multiple leading scholars each stepped forward to claim responsibility for his death, and the *Gemara*'s account of their self-recriminations opens a window into the web of interpersonal tensions, competitive dynamics, and moral scrupulousness that characterized life in the great Babylonian academies.

### Rav Yosef's Blame

Rav Yosef said: *ana nishtei* — I am the one who brought this about, because *d'ana latyei* — I cursed him. The language he had used — invoking God's avenging of honor — was understood as a curse, and he took responsibility for its effect.

### Rav Dimi's Blame

Rav Dimi of Nehardea likewise said: *ana nishtei* — I am to blame, *d'afsi grogaros didi* — because it was through bringing my figs that the entire chain of events was set in motion. This reasoning is poignant: the one who was wronged — who had his figs spoiled, was humiliated in the examination, and was denied his commercial right — felt responsible for the death of the man who had wronged him, on the grounds that the entire episode would never have occurred had he not come with his figs. This reflects a kind of moral accounting that goes beyond simple causation: even the remote cause of a chain of events can weigh upon the conscience, particularly when the chain ends in tragedy.

### Abaye's Blame

Abaye said: *ana nishtei* — I am to blame, because *d'amru lehu l'rabbanan ad d'gamrimitu garmi bei Abaye tu achlu bisra shamna bei Rava* — Rav Adda used to tell Abaye's students: "Rather than chewing bones in Abaye's *yeshiva*, come eat fine, fat meat in Rava's *yeshiva*." He was actively recruiting away from Abaye's study hall, denigrating the quality of Abaye's Torah by contrast with Rava's. Abaye felt that since he was aware of this behavior and had said nothing to Rav Adda about it, he bore responsibility for the consequences — he should have rebuked him, and his failure to do so made him, in some sense, complicit. There may also be a subtler subtext here: several of those claiming responsibility are, in effect, describing ways in which Rav Adda had himself acted improperly — and yet each narrator frames it as his own failing. The moral posture throughout is one of self-examination rather than external attribution.

### Rava's Blame

Rava also said: *ana anishteia* — I brought about his death. The incident he described is this: when Rava's attendant went to the butcher to purchase meat — *lemishkal umtza* — Rav Adda was there and declared to the butchers: *ana shakilna bisra mikamei shamtei de-Rava* — I should receive my meat before Rava's attendant, because *ana adifna minei* — I am more important than he. Even if the claim was only that he outranked the attendant rather than Rava himself, the act of asserting precedence over Rava's representative was implicitly a statement about his own standing relative to Rava. Rava felt that knowing of this incident and failing to speak to Rav Adda about it made him culpable.

### Rav Nachman's Blame

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak said: *ana enishteia* — I am to blame. He explained: *de-Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak reish kalla hava* — he was the head of the *kalla*, responsible for delivering the major *shiur*. His practice — *kol yoma mikamei deniul le-kalla* — was that before each *shiur* he would review the material with Rav Adda bar Ahava as a preparation partner, *marei badeih Rav Ada bar Ahava leshamta vehadar ayeil le-kalla* — running through the *sugya* together, sharpening his thoughts, before entering to give the *shiur*. Rav Adda was, in effect, his indispensable *chavruta* for this purpose.

### Coffin Wish

On one particular day — *hahu yoma* — Rav Pappa and Rav Huna breih de-Rav Yehoshua detained Rav Adda bar Ahava because they had missed the conclusion of a tractate. Rashi identifies this as the *siyum* of Masechet Bechorot. They asked Rav Adda to relay to them all of Rava's *chidushim* on the final perek: *eima lan hanei shamata de-ma'aser behema heichi amrinhu Rava*. He did so at length — *amar lehu hachi amar Rava vehachi amar Rava* — and the learning extended so that *naga leih le-Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* — it grew late for Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak. His *chavruta* had not appeared and he had not had the opportunity to prepare.

When the assembled students urged Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak to rise and begin: *kum denaga lan lama yativ mar* — he replied: *yativna veka mintarna le-arseih de-Rav Ada bar Ahava* — I am sitting here waiting to see the coffin of Rav Adda bar Ahava. The words were heavy with fury: where are you? Come and help me, I need you to have prepared with me before *shiur*.

*Ada-hachi nafak kula denach nafshieh de-Rav Ada bar Ahava* — and at that very moment, word went out that Rav Adda bar Ahava had died. *U-mistabra de-Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak anshieh* — the *Gemara* concludes that it is most plausible that it was Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak who brought about his death, precisely because he had explicitly said that he was waiting for the coffin — a statement far more direct than Rav Yosef's invocation of divine justice — and the temporal sequence was as close as could be imagined.

It is worth noting, in connection with the examination Rav Adda had posed, that Tosafot points out the elephant-basket question is itself one the *Gemara* leaves unresolved elsewhere. One might ask, then, what kind of fair test that was. Tosafot addresses this: *ve'yesh lomar de'mikol makom hava lei le'atuyei hanach re'ayot de'maiti hatam ve'dachi lehu* — even if the question has no definitive answer, Rav Dimi should at least have marshaled the relevant arguments and evidence and engaged with them. Simply saying "I don't know" — without any attempt to reason through the problem — was itself the failing. A *talmid chacham* can be evaluated not only by whether he knows an answer but by how he thinks when confronted with a question he has not seen before.

## Wall Construction

### Wall Distance Rule

The *Gemara* transitions to the new *Mishnah*: *mi she'haya kotlo samuch le'kotel chaveiro lo yismoch lo kotel acher ela im kein hirchik mimenu arba amot* — if one person's wall is situated near another's wall, one may not build a second wall up against it unless one sets it at least four *amot* away. The concern is that if two walls are closer than four *amot* to each other, no passageway remains between them — and as the *Gemara* will explain, that passageway matters.

### Window Privacy

The *Mishnah* continues with *ha'chalonot* — the case of windows on a boundary wall. The rule is: *bein milma'ala bein milmata bein kenegdan arba amot* — whether the neighbor's window is above, below, or directly across, a wall may not be built within four *amot* of it. The concern here is *hezek re'iyah* — the damage caused by being able to see into another's windows. If the new wall is only slightly higher than the window, one could stand on it and look in. If it is only slightly lower, the same problem applies. To fully protect against this, the wall must be four *amot* away in any direction that would permit such a line of sight. The *Mishnah* permits building a wall six feet away even if it substantially reduces the neighbor's access to sunlight — a limitation that seems relatively minimal, particularly if the wall is subsequently built to a great height, though the *Mishnah* accepts this as the operative threshold.

### New Wall Reading

The *Mishnah*'s phrasing, however, presents a difficulty. *Mi she'haya* implies an existing wall — the phrasing seems to describe a situation already in place. How did the wall get there if it was not permitted to be built that close? Rav Yehudah offers a reading: *ha'ba lismoch lo yismoch ela im kein hirchik mimenu arba amot* — read the *Mishnah* as addressing a person who is planning to build: if you want to build a wall next to your neighbor's wall, do not do so unless you leave four *amot* of clearance. The present tense of *mi she'haya* is simply a description of the intended scenario, not of an already-completed wall.

### Wall Fell Reading

Rava objects: *ve'ha mi she'haya kotlo samuch le'kotel chaveiro katani* — but the *Mishnah* says *she'haya*, which grammatically implies that the wall already existed. *Ela amar Rava hachi katani* — Rava therefore offers an alternative reading: *mi she'haya kotlo samuch le'kotel chaveiro berichuk arba amot* — originally the wall was built properly, at a distance of four *amot* from the neighbor's wall, which was permitted. *Ve'nafal* — and then it fell. Now the owner wishes to rebuild it. *Lo yismoch lo kotel acher ela im kein hirchik mimenu arba amot* — when rebuilding, one may not place the new wall within four *amot* of the neighbor's wall; the same distance must be maintained. In either reading, the practical *halacha* is the same: four *amot* of clearance is required.

### Trampling Benefit

*Mai ta'ama?* — what is the reason for this distance requirement, given that these are simply two garden or courtyard walls and no outsiders are obviously affected? The *Gemara* answers: *dechasha dehacha ma'ale lehacha* — the trampling foot traffic on this side is beneficial to that side. If four *amot* of space are maintained between the two walls, a usable passageway is created through which people walk. As people walk alongside a wall, the ground is compressed and hardened, which reinforces the foundation and structural integrity of the wall. Whoever already has a wall therefore acquires a benefit from the passage of foot traffic alongside it. If the neighbor now builds a second wall so close that no one can pass through, that benefit is eliminated. The law therefore protects the existing wall-owner's acquired interest in that passage: you may not build so close to my wall that you deprive me of the foot traffic that is beneficial to it.
