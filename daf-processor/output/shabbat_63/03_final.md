# Shabbat 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Weapons as Ornaments

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יֵצֵא הָאִישׁ לֹא בְּסַיִיף וְלֹא בְּקֶשֶׁת וְלֹא בִּתְרִיס וְלֹא בְּאַלָּה וְלֹא בְּרוֹמַח. וְאִם יָצָא — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Just as it is prohibited for a woman to carry out certain items unique to a woman into the public domain, the Sages said that **a man may neither go out** on Shabbat **with a sword, nor with a bow, nor with a shield [*teris*], nor with an *alla*, nor with a spear. And if he** unwittingly **went out** with one of these weapons to the public domain **he is liable to bring a sin-offering.**

The Mishnah on 63a addresses what a man may not carry — or, more precisely, what he may not wear — on Shabbat, such that doing so constitutes the biblical prohibition of carrying in the public domain. The Mishnah states: *lo yetzei ha'ish lo besayif* — a man may not go out with a sword; *v'lo bekeshet* — nor with a bow; *v'lo betris* — nor with a shield; *v'lo be'alah* — nor with an *alah*, which the Gemara will identify as either a type of spear or a mace with a thick head; *v'lo beromach* — nor with a spear. If he does go out wearing any of these items, *chayav chatat* — he is liable to bring a sin-offering, because this is considered carrying, not wearing.

### R. Eliezer's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: תַּכְשִׁיטִין הֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: These** weapons **are ornaments for him;** just as a man is permitted to go out into the public domain with other ornaments, he is permitted to go out with weapons.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer tachshitin hein lo* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that these military items qualify as *tachshitin*, a form of adornment. People do dress in military attire — think of a royal military guard — wearing a sword not because they expect to go into battle that day, but because it is a particular way of presenting oneself. In that sense, it functions as an ornament.

### Sages' Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָן אֶלָּא לִגְנַאי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִתְּתוּ חַרְבוֹתָם לְאִתִּים וַחֲנִיתוֹתֵיהֶם לְמַזְמֵרוֹת וְלֹא יִשָּׂא גוֹי אֶל גּוֹי חֶרֶב וְלֹא יִלְמְדוּ עוֹד מִלְחָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: They are nothing other than reprehensible** and in the future they will be eliminated, **as it is written: "And they shall beat their swords into plowshares and their spears into pruning hooks; nation will not raise sword against nation, neither will they learn war anymore"** (Isaiah 2:4).

The Sages disagree: *v'chachamim omrim einam ela lignai* — these items are not ornamental but reprehensible. The proof text is the vision of Isaiah: *v'chititu charvotam l'ittim v'chanitoteihem l'mazmerot* — they will beat their swords into plowshares and their spears into pruning shears — *v'lo yisa goy el goy cherev v'lo yilmedu od milchamah* — one nation will not lift up a sword against another, and they will no longer learn war. War is not an ideal; it is a profoundly negative phenomenon. Therefore, the Sages refuse to recognize weapons as *tachshitin*.

### Tachshitin Concept

What exactly is *tachshitin*? It is not merely a category of things that are aesthetically pleasing. As established throughout these earlier discussions — involving bandages, *kame'ot*, and various other items worn on the body — *tachshitin* describes something that is fundamentally connected to the body, worn on the body rather than merely carried on it. A sword in its scabbard, worn on one's person not out of imminent military necessity but because one appreciates the way it looks, would in an objective description seem to fall into the category of something being worn, not carried.

What makes this sugya remarkable is the Sages' method of excluding weapons from *tachshitin* status. In earlier discussions, the question was framed in terms of societal norms — for instance, Rabbi Meir's view that a gold tiara is not considered "worn" because most people do not wear it. Here, however, the Sages do not invoke a societal norm at all. They invoke a religious and value-based judgment: because war is morally negative and because the prophetic vision is of a world without war, we refuse — regardless of how society treats such items — to recognize them as clothing. The introduction of values as a determinant of what counts as *levisha* is striking and worth dwelling on. It is not that weapons are aesthetically unpleasant; it is that we decline to confer upon them the status of dignified attire.

### Modern Applications

One might ask whether this discussion has any bearing on soldiers carrying weapons on Shabbat in the modern State of Israel. The primary halachic argument in that context is one of *pikuach nefesh*, not *tachshitin*. Rav Goren devoted an entire volume to the laws of Shabbat in the army, and the operative principle throughout is the preservation of life. This distinction is illustrated by a case in which soldiers wished to walk off-base to attend a synagogue on Shabbat: the ruling was that they could leave their weapons on base, because in that specific context the *pikuach nefesh* justification did not apply. The sword-as-*tachshit* argument, therefore, does not appear to be the operative frame in modern military halachah.

## War Symbolism Debate

### Weapon Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי בְּ׳אַלָּה׳ — קוּלְפָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the term ***alla*?** It means **club [*kulpa*].**

The Gemara begins by asking: *mai ba'ala* — what exactly is the *alah* mentioned in the Mishnah? The answer given is *kulpa*. The commentary explains: *peirush shevet shemakin bo verosho av* — it is a staff with a thick head, which points toward something like a mace. Steinsaltz identifies it from a Persian source as a club or lance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר תַּכְשִׁיטִין הֵן לוֹ. תַּנְיָא: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּתַכְשִׁיטִין הֵן לוֹ, מִפְּנֵי מָה הֵן בְּטֵלִין לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ? אָמַר לָהֶן: לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן צְרִיכִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יִשָּׂא גוֹי אֶל גּוֹי חֶרֶב״. וְתֶהֱוֵי לְנוֹי בְּעָלְמָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַשְּׁרָגָא בְּטִיהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says: These** weapons **are ornaments for him. It was taught** in a *baraita* that elaborates on this subject: The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Eliezer: And since,** in your opinion, **they are ornaments for him, why are they** to be **eliminated in the messianic era? He said to them: They will not be needed** anymore, **as it is stated: "Nation will not raise sword against nation"** (Isaiah 2:4). The Gemara asks: **And let the weapons be merely for ornamental** purposes, even though they will not be needed for war. **Abaye said:** It is **just as in** the case of **a candle in the afternoon.** Since its light is not needed, it serves no ornamental purpose. Weapons, too; when not needed for war, they serve no ornamental purpose either.

The Gemara then returns to the central dispute: *tachshitin hein lo* — Rabbi Eliezer's position that weapons are ornamental. A *baraita* records that the Sages challenged him: *vechi me'achar detachshitin hein lo mipnei mah hein beteilim le'atid lavo lemot haMashiach* — if they are ornaments, why will they be abolished in Messianic times? Why does the prophecy not simply say there will be no war — why does it say the swords themselves will be beaten into plowshares?

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *lefi she'einan tzrichin* — because they will no longer serve a purpose. When there is no war, weapons become pointless. The Gemara immediately pushes back: *vesehavei lenoy be'alma* — but if they look nice, they still look nice, regardless of whether they serve a martial purpose! Abaye responds with the principle *midi dehavei ashraga betihara* — like a candle at midday: something that serves no function in context becomes superfluous and ridiculous. The ornamental quality of weapons is not merely aesthetic; it is rooted in what they symbolize — valor, courage, might. In a world where war is no longer a reality, that symbolism dissolves, and with it the quality that made them ornamental in the first place.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אֶלָּא שִׁיעְבּוּד גָּלֻיוֹת בִּלְבַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא יֶחְדַּל אֶבְיוֹן מִקֶּרֶב הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this *baraita* **disagrees with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as Shmuel said: The only difference between this world and the messianic era is subjugation of the exiles** to other kingdoms, from which the Jewish people will be released. However, in other respects, the world will remain as it is, **as it is written: "Because the poor will not cease from within the land"** (Deuteronomy 15:11). Society will not change, and wars will continue to be waged.

Rivka raises a sharp point: the Mishnah itself already cited the same *pasuk* about beating swords into plowshares. The Gemara, however, is making a fundamentally different kind of argument. The Mishnah's use of the verse is a value argument: the prophetic vision of a world without war tells us that war is a negative symbol and therefore not to be embraced as a positive, ornamental one. The Gemara, by contrast, converts it into a technical question: if weapons are jewelry, why won't you still wear them as jewelry *l'atid lavo*? Rabbi Eliezer's answer — because they will have no function — is also technical. The Mishnah's argument is the stronger one; the Gemara is working on a different register.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, אֲבָל לְעוֹלָם הַבָּא: ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** However, **this** *baraita* **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who disagrees with Shmuel. **As Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: All of the prophets only prophesied with regard to the messianic era; however, with regard to the World-to-Come** it was stated: **"No eye sees, God, except You,** that which He will do for he that waits for Him" (Isaiah 64:3). What will be in the World-to-Come cannot be depicted even by means of prophecy.

The Gemara then notes: *u'pleigi d'Shmuel* — both the Sages and Rabbi Eliezer, who agree there will be no war in the future, disagree with Shmuel. For Shmuel holds: *ein bein ha'olam hazeh limot haMashiach ela shibud galuyot bilvad* — the only difference between this world and Messianic times is the subjugation of exile; politically, Israel will once again be self-governing, but otherwise the world will remain essentially as it is. Even economically: *ki lo yechdal evyon mikerev ha'aretz* — the poor will never cease from the land, even in Messianic times. It is not going to be a miraculous transformation.

This then supports the position of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: *kol hanvi'im lo nitnabu ela limot haMashiach* — all the prophets prophesied only about Messianic times. The great visions of transformation — no war, the wolf lying with the lamb — those are the descriptions of *yemot haMashiach*, not of *olam haba*. As for *olam haba*: *ayin lo ra'ata Elokim zulatcha* — no eye has seen it; we cannot begin to fathom it. The *masye* — the support for Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba — comes from the Mishnah's assumption that there will be no war in Messianic times, consistent with the view that all the great prophetic visions belong to the Messianic era rather than to some even more remote eschatological period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּתַכְשִׁיטִין הֵן לוֹ, מִפְּנֵי מָה הֵן בְּטֵלִין לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ? אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אֵינָן בְּטֵלִין. הַיְינוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** the disagreement between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis was different. **They said to Rabbi Eliezer: Since** in your opinion **they are ornaments for him**, **why will they be eliminated in the messianic era? He said to them: Even in the messianic era they will not be eliminated. And that is** in accordance with **that which Shmuel** stated that the world will remain fundamentally the same, **and** he **disagrees with Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba.**

The Gemara then presents an alternate version, *ika d'amri*: in this reading, Rabbi Eliezer responds to the Sages' challenge by saying *af limot haMashiach einan beteilim* — weapons will not be abolished even in Messianic times. The verse about beating swords into plowshares refers to *olam haba*, not to Messianic times — and on this reading, Rabbi Eliezer sides with Shmuel, who holds that Messianic times will be essentially continuous with the present world. This second version, the Gemara notes, argues on Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba, who places the great prophetic transformations within Messianic times itself.

It is worth reflecting on what is at stake in this debate more broadly. The Rambam strongly emphasized Shmuel's position in *Mishneh Torah*, writing *amru chachamim ein bein olam hazeh liyemot haMashiach ela shibud malchuyot bilvad* — as though this were the consensus view rather than one position among several. In *Peirush HaMishnayot* and elsewhere, the Rambam expressed deep frustration with the obsessive focus people placed on the details of Messianic times and *techiyat hameitim* — will a person rise with missing limbs, will he rise in his clothes, and so on. His emphasis was always on the world of *mitzvot* as the project of building a more just society, not on accumulating metaphysical rewards. To that end, he underscored Shmuel's more naturalistic vision. As Dr. David Berger has noted in a striking article, however, there is an irony in this: by insisting that Messianic times need not look miraculous, the Rambam's framework made it easier for messianic claimants to go unchallenged. The argument that "the world looks the same as before" is not available as a refutation if the Rambam himself said it would look the same. This dynamic played out in the case of Shabbetai Zvi and his followers, among Religious Zionists who understand the present era as *reishit tzmihat ge'ulateinu*, and in Chabad's claims regarding the Rebbe. The irony is considerable.

There is a further dimension to the *tachshitin* debate that deserves attention. The well-known idea that *Shabbat* is *me'ein olam haba* — a foretaste of the world to come — is relevant here: if something is so antithetical to the ideal of *olam haba*, can we embrace it as appropriate dress for Shabbat? One might wish to frame the Mishnah's rejection of weapons as *tachshitin* in precisely these terms. However, the Gemara's distinction between *yemot haMashiach* and *olam haba* complicates matters. We describe Shabbat as *me'ein olam haba*, not *me'ein yemot haMashiach* — and even Rabbi Eliezer, who agrees there will be no war in *olam haba*, might concede that point while maintaining that in this world, and possibly even in Messianic times, weapons carry genuine positive symbolic weight. The halachah, on this view, is this-worldly even when it draws on value judgments; and those value judgments must be grounded in this-worldly realities rather than utopian visions of a realm we can barely fathom.

### Biblical Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְרַב אַוְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב דִּימִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְרַב אַוְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּאָמַר תַּכְשִׁיטִין הֵן לוֹ — דִּכְתִיב: ״חֲגוֹר חַרְבְּךָ עַל יָרֵךְ גִּבּוֹר הוֹדְךָ וַהֲדָרֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Dimi, and some say** it was **to Rav Avya, and some say Rav Yosef said to Rav Dimi, and some say** it was **to Rav Avya, and some say Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What is the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer who said: These** weapons **are ornaments for him? As it is written: "Gird your sword upon your thigh, mighty one, your glory and your splendor"** (Psalms 45:4), indicating that a sword is considered an ornament.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer* — what is Rabbi Eliezer's actual textual basis for holding that weapons are *tachshitin*? The answer: *de-chtiv chagur charbecha al yerech gibor hodecha va-hadarkha* — gird your sword on your thigh, O hero — your glory and your beauty. The *pasuk* explicitly says that a warrior wearing his sword thereby acquires glory and beauty. So at least in this world, weapons can be positive symbols.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא לְמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: הַאי בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּתִיב! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מִקְרָא יוֹצֵא מִידֵי פְּשׁוּטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that some time later **Rav Kahana said to Mar, son of Rav Huna:** Is that really a proof? **This** verse **is written in reference to matters of Torah** and should be interpreted as a metaphor. **He said to him:** Nevertheless, **a verse does not depart from its literal meaning,** although there may be additional homiletical interpretations.

Rav Kahana challenges this proof: *hai b'divrei Torah chtiv* — that verse is speaking about Torah, not about literal weapons. It is a metaphor — keeping the sword of Torah always at one's side, ready to deploy.

### Literal Meaning Principle

Rav Kahana's interlocutor responds: *ein mikra yotzei midei peshuto* — a verse never entirely departs from its plain meaning. Even if the metaphorical reading is valid, the plain meaning retains its force. And crucially, a metaphor has no rhetorical power unless the image it invokes carries genuine weight in the real world. If wearing a sword were not, in fact, a source of glory for a warrior, the metaphor would be empty. One might think of the Artscroll approach to *Shir HaShirim*, where the metaphorical reading is read directly into the text — but as our Gemara implies, the metaphor presupposes the reality it maps onto.

### Learning Methodology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כַּד הֲוֵינָא בַּר תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי שְׁנִין וַהֲוָה גְּמִירְנָא לֵיהּ לְכוּלֵּיהּ תַּלְמוּדָא, וְלָא הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא דְּאֵין מִקְרָא יוֹצֵא מִידֵּי פְּשׁוּטוֹ עַד הַשְׁתָּא. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? דְּלִיגְמַר אִינִישׁ וַהֲדַר לִיסְבַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said** about this: **When I was eighteen years old and** had already **learned the entire Talmud, and** yet **I did not know that a verse does not depart from its literal meaning until now.** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Kahana **teaching us** with that statement? The Gemara answers: He comes to teach **that a person should** first **learn and then understand** the rationale.

Rav Kahana then shares a striking personal disclosure: *kad havina bar tamnei srei shanin* — when I was eighteen years old, *vehavah gmirna leh lechulei haShas* — I had already learned all of Shas (by which is meant all of *shisha sidrei mishna* and *Torah she-be'al peh* generally). *Beram velo havah yadana de-ein mikra yotzei midei peshuto* — yet I did not yet know that *ein mikra yotzei midei peshuto*, that the plain meaning of a verse retains its standing even alongside a derash.

This is understandable. Someone immersed in the world of *Torah she-be'al peh* — where the halachic reading of a *pasuk* is definitively established — may come to feel that no other reading is legitimate. The *talmud lomar* at the end of this anecdote is pointed: *deligmar inish vehadar lisbor* — one should first learn all the material, and only then begin to analyze it. The Gemara does not criticize Rav Kahana for the sequence of his learning; it holds it up as a principle. Breadth — *beki'ut* — must precede depth — *iyun*. You cannot analyze what you do not yet know exists. And yet even after knowing everything, there are still insights that emerge only later, in the process of reflection. This tension between *beki'ut* and *iyun* is itself a perennial question in Torah education, and there are *yeshivot* today, particularly in Israel, that deliberately structure their curricula along precisely these lines: years devoted to *Tanakh*, then years to *Mishnah* — breadth first, analysis later.

## Torah Study Models

### Sharpening Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמְחַדְּדִין זֶה לְזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַצְלִיחַ לָהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַהֲדָרְךָ צְלַח״. אַל תִּקְרֵי: ״וַהֲדָרְךָ״, אֶלָּא ״וַחֲדָדְךָ״. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁעוֹלִין לִגְדוּלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צְלַח רְכַב״. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל דְּבַר אֱמֶת״. יָכוֹל אִם הֵגֵיס דַּעְתּוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעַנְוָה צֶדֶק״. וְאִם עוֹשִׂין כֵּן זוֹכִין לַתּוֹרָה שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בְּיָמִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתוֹרְךָ נוֹרָאוֹת יְמִינֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Rabbi Elazar said: Two Torah scholars who sharpen one another in *halakha*; the Holy One, Blessed be He,** ensures **success for them, as it is written: "And in your majesty [*vahadarkha*] prosper,** ride on, in behalf of truth and meekness and righteousness; and let your right hand teach you tremendous things" (Psalms 45:5). The Sages said:  **Do not read "and your majesty [*vahadarkha*]," rather,** by changing some of the vocalization and the letters, read it as **and He will sharpen you [*veḥidedkha*],** and ultimately you will be successful.  **Moreover, they** who act in that manner will **rise to prominence, as it is written: "Prosper, ride on."**  **I might have thought even** if one engages in the study of Torah **not for its own sake;** therefore, **the verse states: "On behalf of truth."**  **I might have thought** that one would be rewarded with prosperity even **if he became arrogant;** therefore, **the verse states: "Meekness** and **righteousness."**  **And if they do so** in the proper manner **they merit the Torah that was given with the right hand** of the Holy One, Blessed be He, **as it is written: "And let your right hand teach you tremendous things"** (Psalms 45:5).

Since the *pasuk* about girding the sword has been established as a metaphor for Torah study, the Gemara now develops that metaphor in several directions, each offering a distinct model of *talmud Torah*. Rabbi Yirmiyah said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *shenei talmidei chachamim ha-mechadedim zeh la-zeh be-halakhah* — two Torah scholars who sharpen one another in *halakhah*, debating back and forth, each one sharpening the other the way running one blade against another produces a keener edge — *Hakadosh Baruch Hu matzliach lahem*, God causes them to succeed. The proof text continues from the same *pasuk*: *va-hadarkha tz'lach* — in your glory, succeed. On the reading *al tikrei va-hadarkha ela ve-chadadkha* — do not read "your glory" but "your sharpening" — we get: sharpen one another and you will succeed.

Not only that: *ela she-olim li-gdula* — they rise to greatness. The condition, however, is *al devar emet* — the approach must be truthful. And if success breeds pride, the *pasuk* adds *ve-anvah tzedek* — humility and righteousness are part of the formula. Only then does one *zocheh le-Torah she-nittnah be-yamin* — merit the Torah given in God's right hand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: זוֹכִין לִדְבָרִים שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרוּ בִּימִינָהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה. דְּאָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַאי דִכְתִיב ״אֹרֶךְ יָמִים בִּימִינָהּ בִּשְׂמֹאלָהּ עֹשֶׁר וְכָבוֹד״ — אֶלָּא בִּימִינָהּ אֹרֶךְ יָמִים אִיכָּא, עֹשֶׁר וְכָבוֹד לֵיכָּא?! אֶלָּא: לַמַּיְימִינִין בָּהּ — אֹרֶךְ יָמִים אִיכָּא, וְכָל שֶׁכֵּן עוֹשֶׁר וְכָבוֹד. לַמַּשְׂמְאִילִים בָּהּ — עוֹשֶׁר וְכָבוֹד אִיכָּא, אוֹרֶךְ יָמִים לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: They are rewarded with the matters stated with regard to the right** hand **of the Torah. As Rava bar Rav Sheila said and some say Rav Yosef bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Sheshet said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written, "Length of days is in her right hand and in her left hand are riches and honor"** (Proverbs 3:16)? Is that to say, **however,** that **in her right** there **is length of days, but there are not riches and honor? Rather,** it means: **Those who relate to it with the** skilled **right** hand, i.e., who study Torah for its own sake and with proper intentions, **there is length of days and all the more so riches and honor** for them. Whereas, **those who relate to it with the** unskilled **left** hand, **there are riches and honor; there is not length of days.**

Rabbi Nachman bar Yitzchak adds: one merits *dvarim shene'emru b'yemina shel Torah* — the things spoken of on the right side of the Torah. This is explained via the *pasuk*: *orech yamim b'yemina b'smola osher v'chavod* — long life in the right hand; in the left hand, wealth and honor. The apparent paradox — why does the right hand, which is superior, offer only long life while the left adds wealth and honor? — is resolved: *l'mayminim bah*, those who approach Torah in the right way, *orech yamim ika v'chol shekein osher v'chavod* — first they get long life, and of course wealth and honor come with that. *Lam'smilin bah*, those who do it on the "left," *osher v'chavod ika orech yamim lika* — they may get wealth and honor, but not the fullness of long life.

Rashi suggests two readings of *masmilin bah*: either those who learn without sufficient self-sacrifice, or — his alternative — those who learn *shelo lishma*, not for its own sake. This opens onto the well-known distinction in Tosafot between two kinds of *shelo lishma*: the kind that leads to *lishma* — where the motivation is impure but not antithetical to Torah — and the kind that is genuinely destructive, about which the Gemara says *na'asit lo sam mita* and *mutav lo shelo nivra*. The *shelo lishma* that we ordinarily say is acceptable — the normal human mixture of motivations, looking smart, being respected — is the kind that *mitoch shelo lishma ba lishma*, and that is likely what is meant here in the context of *osher v'chavod*.

### Cooperative Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַנּוֹחִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַקְשִׁיב לָהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז נִדְבְּרוּ יִרְאֵי ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״ — אֵין דִּבּוּר אֶלָּא נַחַת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַדְבֵּר עַמִּים תַּחְתֵּינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Two Torah scholars who are agreeable to each other** when engaging **in** discussions of ***halakha*, the Holy One, Blessed be He, listens to them, as it is stated: "Then they that feared the Lord spoke [*nidberu*]** one with another; and the Lord hearkened, and heard, and a book of remembrance was written before Him, for them that fear the Lord, and that think upon His name" (Malachi 3:16). **And** the term **speech** [***dibbur***] **means nothing other than calm, as it is stated: "He subdues [*yadber*] people under us"** (Psalms 47:4). He will cause the nations to submit to the Jewish people leading to a period of calm. Here too the term *dibbur* indicates calm and agreeability.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, as cited by Rabbi Abba, presents a second model: *shenei talmidei chachamim ha-nochim zeh la-zeh be-halakhah* — two Torah scholars who are *nochim* to one another in *halakhah*. Rashi offers two readings of *nochim*: pleasant with one another, or leading one another. Not the aggressive sharpening of the previous model, but a cooperative, guiding mode of study. The reward here is different: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu makshiv lahen* — God listens to them. The proof is from *az nidberu yirei Hashem* — those who feared God spoke to one another, and then God listened.

*Ein dibur ela nachat* — the word *dibur* here connotes pleasantness or leading. Rashi's preferred reading is "to lead," citing *yadber amim tachteinu* — He will lead nations beneath us. The reading of pleasantness is the more natural one given the context of cooperative study, but the verse's plain sense leans toward leading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וּלְחוֹשְׁבֵי שְׁמוֹ״? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֲפִילּוּ חִישֵּׁב לַעֲשׂוֹת מִצְוָה, וְנֶאֱנַס וְלֹא עֲשָׂאָהּ, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the phrase in that verse: **"And that think upon His name"? Rabbi Ami said: Even if one** merely **planned to perform a mitzva, and** ultimately **due to circumstances beyond his control did not perform** that mitzva, **the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he performed it.**

The *pasuk* continues *u'lchoshvei shmo* — and those who consider His name. Rabbi Ami derives from here: *afilu choshev la'asot mitzvah v'ne'enas v'lo asah ma'ala alav hakatuv k'ilu asah* — even one who intended to perform a *mitzvah* but was prevented by circumstances beyond his control is credited as though he had done it. This is a parenthetical derived from the *pasuk* quoted, not the main line of the *talmud Torah* discussion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: גָּדוֹל הַמַּלְוֶה יוֹתֵר מִן הָעוֹשֶׂה צְדָקָה. וּמֵטִיל בַּכִּיס, יוֹתֵר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: One who loans** another **money is greater than one who gives** him **charity. And the one who places** money **into** a common **purse,** i.e., one who enters into a partnership with a needy person, **is the greatest of them all,** since in that case the needy person is not embarrassed when receiving the assistance.

Two further *aggadic* statements appear here in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, via the same chain of transmission. First: *gadol hamalveh yoteir min oseh tzedakah* — lending money is greater than giving outright charity, and *umatil bekis yoteir mikulan* — investing in someone's livelihood or business is greater still. Rashi explains that lending and investing better preserve the recipient's dignity while also building self-sufficiency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אִם תַּלְמִיד חָכָם נוֹקֵם וְנוֹטֵר כְּנָחָשׁ הוּא — חׇגְרֵיהוּ עַל מׇתְנֶיךָ. אִם עַם הָאָרֶץ הוּא חָסִיד — אַל תָּדוּר בִּשְׁכוּנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** Even **if a Torah scholar is as vengeful and begrudging as a snake, wrap him** tightly around **your waist,** i.e., keep him close, because you will benefit from his Torah. On the other hand, even **if an *am ha'aretz* is righteous, do not dwell in his neighborhood,** as his righteousness does not compensate for the fact that he is ignorant.

Second: *im talmid chacham nokeim venoteir nuchshehu benachash hu* — if a Torah sage is vindictive and bears grudges, he is as bad as a snake, *chagreiho al matnecha* — yet still bind him at your loins. The image of binding at the loins is again, deliberately, the image of the sword. A sword can be dangerous and destructive, yet it still serves; similarly, even a difficult Torah sage has much to offer — though one might also note: try to keep the sword in its scabbard. Conversely: *im am ha'aretz hu chasid al tadur bishchunato* — if an ignorant person is pious, do not live in his neighborhood. Rashi explains: unlearned piety can lead to errors in practice, however sincere the motivation.

### Self-Study Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמַּקְשִׁיבִים זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שׁוֹמֵעַ לְקוֹלָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַיּוֹשֶׁבֶת בַּגַּנִּים חֲבֵרִים מַקְשִׁיבִים לְקוֹלֵךְ הַשְׁמִיעִנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Two Torah scholars who listen to each other in** the discussion of ***halakha*, the Holy One, Blessed be He, hears their voice, as it is stated: "You who dwell in gardens, the companions heed your voice, cause me to hear it"** (Song of Songs 8:13).

The Gemara then presents a third model: *shenei talmidei chachamim ha-madgilim zeh la-zeh be-halakhah* — two Torah scholars who are *madgil* one another in *halakhah*. What does *madgil* mean? Rashi understands it as helping one another in the absence of a teacher; Tosafot understands it as actually leading one another into mistakes — not intentionally, but as an inherent risk of learning without proper guidance. The reward here is striking: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu ohavan* — God loves them. Even if mistakes are made, even in the absence of a *rebbe*, the act of coming together to learn is beloved. The proof text: *ve-diglo alay ahavah* — his banner over me is love.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִם אֵין עוֹשִׂין כֵּן — גּוֹרְמִים לַשְּׁכִינָה שֶׁמִּסְתַּלֶּקֶת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּרַח דּוֹדִי וּדְמֵה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if they do not do so,** i.e., they do not listen to each other, **they cause the Divine Presence to depart from** among **Israel, as it is stated** in the following verse: **"Run away, my beloved, and be like** a gazelle or a young hart upon the mountains of spices" (Song of Songs 8:14).

Rava qualifies this: *ve-hu di-yade tzurta di-shma'ta* — it applies only if they at least know the basic shape of the *sugya*, the contours of the discussion. If they are completely at sea, the principle does not apply. And additionally: *ve-hu di-let le-hu rabba be-mata le-migmar mineh* — only if there is in fact no available teacher. If a proper teacher could be found and one chooses instead to muddle through on one's own, no credit is given for the resulting errors.

The contrast between the three models is worth pausing over. The sharpening model produces the most impressive individual achievement — great *talmidei chachamim*, Torah given in God's right hand. The cooperative and listening models produce something qualitatively different: the *Shechinah* is present, God listens, and the withdrawal of that presence — *gormim la-Shechinah she-tistalek mi-Yisrael* — is the consequence of its absence. The self-study model produces simply: God loves you, even with your mistakes. These are three different visions of what Torah study is for, and three different measures of its success.

### Aggadic Digressions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמְגַדֵּל כֶּלֶב רַע בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ מוֹנֵעַ חֶסֶד מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַמָּס
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said, and some say Rav Asi said** that **Reish Lakish said, and some say Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: Anyone who raises an evil dog within his home prevents kindness from** entering **into his home,** since poor people will hesitate to enter his house. As it is alluded to in the verse: **"To him that is afflicted [*lamas*],**

Two *aggadic* statements follow, attributed to Rabbi Chanina bar Idi and Rabbi Assi (or Rabbi Chanina): *kol ha'oseh mitzvah k'ma'amarah ein mevasrim oto b'sorot ra'ot* — one who performs a *mitzvah* properly will not receive bad tidings, derived from *shomer mitzvah lo yeda davar ra*. And — strikingly — *afilu Hakadosh Baruch Hu gozer gzeira hu mevatla* — even a divine decree can be nullified by one who keeps the *mitzvot* faithfully. This is derived from *ba'asher devar melech shilton u-mi yomar lo mah ta'aseh* — who can tell the king what to do? — juxtaposed with *shomer mitzvah lo yeda davar ra*: the one who can push back against the king is the *shomer mitzvah*.

### Bad Dog Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מֵרֵעֵהוּ חָסֶד״ — שֶׁכֵּן בְּלָשׁוֹן יְוָנִית קוֹרִין לְכֶלֶב ״לַמָּס״. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אַף פּוֹרֵק מִמֶּנּוּ יִרְאַת שָׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְיִרְאַת שַׁדַּי יַעֲזוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** **kindness from his friend** and awe of the Almighty will leave" (Job 6:14), **since in the Greek language they call a dog *lamas*. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** One who keeps an evil dog in his home **even divests himself of fear of Heaven, as it is stated** at the end of that verse: **"And awe of the Almighty will leave."**

A statement in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish — attributed variously to Rav Kahana, Rav Assi, or Rabbi Abba — declares: *kol megadel kelev ra betoch beito monei'a chesed mitoch beito* — one who raises a bad dog in one's home withholds *chesed* from one's home, because no one wishes to visit, and the opportunities for *hachnasat orchim* and charity are foreclosed. The proof text: *lamas merei'ehu chesed* — he withholds *chesed* from his neighbor. In Greek, the Gemara notes, a dog is called *lamas*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds: such a person also casts off the yoke of heaven — *ve-yirat Shaddai ya'azov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הַהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּעֲיַילָא לְהָהוּא בֵּיתָא לְמֵיפָא. נְבַח בָּהּ כַּלְבָּא, אִיתְעֲקַר וַלְדַּהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ מָרֵי דְבֵיתָא לָא תִּידְחֲלִי, דִּשְׁקִילִי נִיבֵיהּ וּשְׁקִילִין טוּפְרֵיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: שְׁקִילָא טֵיבוּתָיךְ וְשַׁדְיָא אַחִיזְרֵי, כְּבָר נָד וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A certain** pregnant **woman who entered to** use the oven in a **certain house to bake, the dog** in that house **barked at her, and her fetus was displaced. The owner of the house said to her: Do not be afraid because his teeth have been removed and his claws have been removed. She said to him: Take your goodness and throw it on the thorns.** Your encouragement is useless as the **fetus has already been displaced** and will certainly die.

The Gemara then illustrates this with a narrative: a woman entered a house to bake bread, using what was presumably a communal oven. *Navach bah kalba* — the dog barked at her and frightened her terribly. *It'akeir vlada* — she suffered a miscarriage. The owner of the house, hearing the commotion, came and said: do not be afraid, the dog's fangs have been removed and its claws have been trimmed. She replied: *shkol tivusach v'shadi achizrei* — take your kindness and throw it on the thorns. *Kvar nad vlad* — the fetus has already been lost. A powerful illustration that even the bark of a bad dog, with no bite at all, can cause devastating harm.

This narrative, while appearing within the string of *aggadic* statements in the name of Reish Lakish, connects back thematically to the larger discussion. The aggressive, militaristic image of Torah study — *milchamtah shel Torah* — has real costs. The verbal aggression and harsh confrontation that can accompany the sharpening model is not without danger, even if it produces intellectual greatness. The tension between that model and the more cooperative, peaceful vision is not resolved; it is left standing, precisely as the Mishnah left standing the competing visions of whether strength and valor are positive or negative symbols.

### Torah and Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי דִכְתִיב: ״שְׂמַח בָּחוּר בְּיַלְדוּתֶךָ וִיטִיבְךָ לִבְּךָ בִּימֵי בְחוּרוֹתֶיךָ וְהַלֵּךְ בְּדַרְכֵי לִבְּךָ וּבְמַרְאֵה עֵינֶיךָ וְדָע כִּי עַל כׇּל אֵלֶּה יְבִיאֲךָ הָאֱלֹהִים בַּמִּשְׁפָּט״ — עַד כָּאן דִּבְרֵי יֵצֶר הָרָע, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ דִּבְרֵי יֵצֶר טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, **Rav Huna said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "Rejoice young man in your youth, and let your heart cheer you in the days of your youth, and walk in the ways of your heart and in the sight of your eyes; but know that for all these things God will bring you to judgment"** (Ecclesiastes 11:9)? **Until here,** "the sight of your eyes," these are **the words of the evil inclination; from here on,** "but know that, etc.," these are **the words of the good inclination.**

Rav Huna cites a verse from Kohelet: *samach bachur beyaldutcha veyetivcha libcha bimei bechurotecha veholech bedarchei libcha ubemarei eineicha* — rejoice, O young man, in your youth, and let your heart be good to you in the days of your youth, walk in the ways of your heart and the sight of your eyes — *ki al kol eleh yevicha ha'Elokim bamishpat* — for all these things God will bring you to judgment. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between "go enjoy yourself" and "you will be judged" as follows: *ad kan divrei yetzer hara* — up to the word *veda* is the voice of the *yetzer hara*, telling you to enjoy yourself; *mikan ve'eilach divrei yetzer tov* — the reminder of divine judgment is the voice of the *yetzer tov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: עַד כָּאן לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לְמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said: Until here,** the verse refers **to matters of Torah.** One is provided the opportunity to study and involve himself in the Torah and rejoice in its innovations; **from here on,** "but know that, etc.," **it refers to good deeds.** One will ultimately stand trial for that which he studied and did not implement.

Reish Lakish reads the verse differently: *ad kan divrei Torah mikan ve'eilach lema'asim tovim* — the first part is about rejoicing in Torah study; the second is about translating that Torah into good deeds. Torah is not only about becoming a great *talmid chacham*; it must produce *ma'asim tovim*, a more just and righteous way of living in the world. Here, once again, the image is of joy in learning rather than of swords clashing. And the measure of success is not intellectual dominance but the quality of one's actions. These two competing visions — the aggressive sharpening model and the joyful, action-oriented one — animate the entire sugya, just as the Mishnah opened with the unresolved question of whether symbols of strength belong in our world at all.

## Leg Ornaments & Purity

### Biryas Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בִּירִית טְהוֹרָה וְיוֹצְאִין בָּהּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to women's ornaments, they added that **a garter** placed on her leg to hold up stockings **is pure** and cannot become ritually impure as a utensil, **and she may** even **go out with it on Shabbat.**

The Gemara now returns to the final portion of the Mishnah: *biryas tehorah* — a *biryas* is *tahor*, ritually pure. The *biryas* is some kind of clasp or ring used to hold up leg coverings — stockings or the lower portion of a garment — and one may go out wearing it on Shabbat. It is *tahor* because it is not itself a garment or ornament; it merely holds up other clothing. And one may wear it on Shabbat because there is no concern that one will remove it and carry it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כְּבָלִים — טְמֵאִים וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** However, **ankle chains,** which were also women's ornaments, can become ritually **impure, and she may not go out with them on Shabbat.**

*Kevalim tme'im* — *kevalim* are ritually impure — and one may not go out with them on Shabbat, and we will presently learn what they are and why they differ from the *biryas*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בִּירִית זוֹ אֶצְעָדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: Garter; that is a bracelet** worn on the arm.

Rav Yehuda explains: *biryas zu etzada* — the *biryas* is like a *etzada*, a bracelet, for the leg.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: בִּירִית טְהוֹרָה וְיוֹצְאָה בָּהּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאִילּוּ אֶצְעָדָה טְמֵאָה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection:** It is stated here that **a garter is pure and** a woman **may go out with it on Shabbat, while a bracelet is ritually impure.** It is mentioned explicitly in the verse enumerating the spoils of the war with the Midianites: "And we have brought the Lord's offering, what every man has gotten, of jewels of gold, armlets, and bracelets, signet rings, earrings, and girdles, to make atonement for our souls before the Lord" (Numbers 31:50). Earlier in that chapter it is written with regard to the spoils: "Purify yourselves on the third day and on the seventh day both you and your captives" (Numbers 31:19). Apparently, a bracelet can become ritually impure.

Rav Yosef challenges this: the Mishnah says the *biryas* is *tahor*, but a bracelet — *etzada* — is *tamei*, as we know from the narratives of Midian and elsewhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכִי קָאָמַר: בִּירִית תַּחַת אֶצְעָדָה עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is what** Rav Yehuda **is saying: A garter** on the leg **is in place of a bracelet** on the arm. It goes around the leg to hold a stocking in place just as a bracelet goes around the arm. However, unlike a bracelet, a garter cannot become ritually impure because it is not an ornament. It simply holds up the stocking.

The Gemara resolves this: *hachi ka'amar biryas tachat etzada omedet* — the *biryas* functions in relation to the leg the way a bracelet does in relation to the arm. It is not literally a bracelet, but rather some ring or band around the leg whose purpose is to hold up the leg covering. That functional, secondary purpose — serving other garments rather than directly adorning the body — is what renders it *tahor* and what distinguishes it from an ornament proper.

### Kevalim Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* יָתֵיב רָבִין וְרַב הוּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יִרְמְיָה, וְיָתֵיב רַב יִרְמְיָה וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם. וְיָתֵיב רָבִין וְקָאָמַר: — בִּירִית בְּאַחַת, כְּבָלִים בִּשְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabin and Rav Huna were sitting before Rav Yirmeya. And Rav Yirmeya was sitting and dozing** as the two students conversed. **And Rabin sat and said:** The difference between a garter and ankle chains is that **a garter is** worn **on one** leg, **and ankle chains** are worn **on two** legs.

Ravin and Rav Huna were sitting before Rabbi Yirmiyah, who had dozed off. While their teacher slept, the students engaged the question. Ravin proposed: *biryas be'achat, kevalim beshtayim* — the *biryas* involves one band, the *kevalim* involve two, and that is why *kevalim* are *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא: אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ בִּשְׁתַּיִם, וּמְטִילִין שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת בֵּינֵיהֶן וְנַעֲשׂוּ כְּבָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said to him: These** garters **and those** ankle chains **are** both worn **on two** legs. **And** when she wears garters on both legs **they place a chain between them, and they become** vessels with the legal status equal to that of **ankle chains.**

Rav Huna corrected him: *elu ve'elu beshtayim* — both the *biryas* and the *kevalim* involve two bands, one on each leg. What distinguishes the *kevalim* is something else entirely: *umatil shalshelet beineihem* — a chain is strung between the two bands, connecting them, *venasu kevalim* — and that is what makes it *kevalim*. The *kevalim*, then, are not merely two leg-bands but a contraption: two rings around the legs connected by a chain running between them.

### Historical Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְשַׁלְשֶׁלֶת שֶׁבּוֹ מְשַׁוְּיָא לֵיהּ מָנָא?
>
> **Translation:** And Ravin asked: **And** does **the chain that** is connected to **it render it a vessel?** If a garter without a chain is not considered a vessel, why would the addition of a chain render it a vessel that can become ritually impure?

This raises an obvious question: why would anyone wear such a thing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכָא נָמֵי קָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשְׁפָּחָה אַחַת הָיְתָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ פְּסִיעוֹתֵיהֶן גַּסּוֹת וְהָיוּ בְּתוּלוֹתֵיהֶן נוֹשְׁרוֹת, עָשׂוּ לָהֶן כְּבָלִים וְהֵטִילוּ שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת בֵּינֵיהֶן, שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ פְּסִיעוֹתֵיהֶן גַּסּוֹת וְלֹא הָיוּ בְּתוּלוֹתֵיהֶן נוֹשְׁרוֹת. אִיתְּעַר בְּהוּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, אֲמַר לְהוּ: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** He said to him: **Here, too,** the **chain is performing a** purposeful **action,** as **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: There was one family in Jerusalem whose** daughters' **strides were lengthy, and** as a result **their hymen** membranes **would fall** away. In order to solve this problem they made them ankle chains **and they hung a chain between them so that their strides would not be** so **large and,** indeed, **their hymen** membranes **would no** longer **fall** away. Meanwhile, **Rabbi Yirmeya awoke from their** voices **and said to them: Correct, and Rabbi Yoḥanan said likewise.**

The Gemara supplies an account. Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mishpacha achat hayeta b'Yerushalayim she-hayu pisioteihen gasot* — there was a family in Jerusalem whose members took excessively wide strides, *ve-hayu betuloteihen noshrot* — and as a result their young women would rupture their hymens. In response, *asu lahem kavalayim ve-hetilu sharsheret beineihem shelo yehu pisioteihen gasot* — they fashioned these banded chains to restrict their stride, thereby preventing the rupture.

The *kevalim* thus originated as a functional device for controlling gait — a modesty measure, in fact. What began as a family practice apparently became more widespread. Curiously, the Mishnah's prohibition is directed at men, even though the narrative concerns women. It appears that at some point this form of leg restraint was adopted more broadly by those who wished to walk with a more restricted step.

### Functional Vessel

Since the *kevalim* serve a specific bodily function — adjusting one's stride — the chain that connects the two bands is considered a *kli*, a functional vessel, which renders the whole apparatus *tamei*. The Gemara asks: *u'shalshelet shebo mashavei leih mana* — does the chain truly make it a vessel? On what basis? The Gemara considers the case of an instrument that produces sound as an analogy — *lemashmia kol bichli matchot shehu tamei* — but distinguishes it: a musical instrument is used actively to produce something. Here, the chain simply hangs between the legs. The Gemara affirms nonetheless: *kaavid maaseh* — it does perform an action, namely, restricting the stride. And because it functions purposively in this way, it qualifies as a *kli* and is therefore *tamei*.

Rabbi Yirmiyah, waking up from his doze, heard the students' exchange and said: *yishar kochacha* — well done, that is precisely correct. And that is the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan as well. Because the *kevalim* are a genuine functional vessel rather than merely decorative, they are *tamei*, and because there is no urgency to keep them on — unlike, say, a clasp that prevents one's clothing from falling — there is concern one might remove them in public, and one therefore may not wear them on Shabbat.

## Purity of Ornaments

### Tzitz Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין לְאָרִיג כׇּל שֶׁהוּא שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — מִצִּיץ.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of ritual impurity, the Gemara relates: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** is it derived **that a woven fabric of any size** can **become** ritually impure? It is derived **from the frontplate [*tzitz*]** of the High Priest, which is considered a vessel despite its small size.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *minayin l'arig kol shehu shehu tamei* — how do we know that a woven item of any size, however small, is *tamei*? *Mitzitz* — from the *tzitz*, the golden frontplate worn by the High Priest.

### Tzitz Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְצִיץ אָרִיג הוּא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: צִיץ כְּמִין טַס שֶׁל זָהָב, וְרוֹחַב שְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת, וּמוּקָּף מֵאוֹזֶן לְאוֹזֶן, וְכָתוּב עָלָיו בִּשְׁתֵּי שִׁיטִין: ״יוֹד הֵא״ לְמַעְלָה, וְ״קוֹדֶשׁ לָמֶד״ לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And is the frontplate a woven fabric? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The frontplate is** made **like a kind of** smooth **plate of gold, and** its **width is two fingerbreadths, and it encircles** the forehead **from ear to ear. And on it is written in two lines: *Yod heh*,** i.e., the Tetragrammaton, **above, and *kodesh lamed*,** i.e., sacred to, **below.** Thus, the words: Sacred to God, were written on the frontplate.

Abaye immediately challenges: *vetzitz arig hu* — is the *tzitz* woven? A *baraita* describes it as *kemin tas shel zahav* — a plate of gold, two finger-widths wide, running from ear to ear, with the inscription written on two lines: the divine name on the upper line and *Kodesh La-* below.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲנִי רְאִיתִיו בְּרוֹמִי וְכָתוּב עָלָיו ״קֹדֶשׁ לַה׳״ בְּשִׁיטָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: I saw it** in the Caesar's treasury **in the city of Rome and Sacred to God was written on one line.** In any case, since the frontplate is a gold plate, how can it serve as a source for ritual impurity in fabrics?

Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose adds: *ani raitiv b'Romi* — I saw the *tzitz* in Rome, where it had been taken after the destruction of the Temple. He reports that *Kodesh LaHashem* was written on a single line, not two — a minority position in tension with the *baraita*, though his testimony is direct and firsthand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי סְלֵיק רַב דִּימִי לִנְהַרְדְּעָא שְׁלַח לְהוּ: דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתִּי לָכֶם טָעוּת הֵם בְּיָדִי, בְּרַם כָּךְ אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין לְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — מִצִּיץ. וּמִנַּיִין לְאָרִיג כׇּל שֶׁהוּא שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא: — מֵ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi ascended to Neharde'a, he sent to** the yeshiva students: **The statements I said to you** with regard to woven fabrics of any size becoming ritually impure regardless of their size, **they are my mistake. However, this** is what **they said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: From where** is it derived **that an ornament of any size** can become **ritually impure?** It is derived **from the frontplate. And from where** is it derived **that a woven fabric of any size** can become **ritually impure?** It is derived **from** the verse: **"Or a garment** or leather or sack; any vessel with which any work is done must be put into water and it shall be unclean until evening, then it shall be clean" (Leviticus 11:32). From the extraneous phrase, "or a garment," it is derived that any garment, regardless how small, falls into this category.

Subsequently, Rav Dimi sent word from Nahardea: *devarim she'amarti lachem taut hem beyadi* — what I previously told you was a mistake. His original claim — that a woven item of any size is *tamei*, derived from the *tzitz* — was erroneous. *Beram kach amru mishum Rabbi Yochanan* — rather, what they said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is this: *uminayin letachshit kol shehu shehu tamei* — how do we know that an ornament of any size is *tamei*? *Mi'tzitz* — from the *tzitz*. Since the *tzitz* is an ornamental object worn by the High Priest, it establishes that ornaments of any size are susceptible to *tumah*.

### Inclusive Disjunction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָרִיג כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא, וְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא. אָרִיג וְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא. מוּסָף שַׂק עַל הַבֶּגֶד שֶׁטָּמֵא מִשּׁוּם אָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A woven fabric of any size** can become **ritually impure, and an ornament of any size** can become **ritually impure.** An object that is half **woven** fabric **and** half **ornament** of **any size** can become **ritually impure.** And **a sack is added** to the category of **garment; it** too **is ritually impure due to woven fabric.**

The remaining question is the source for the *tumah* of small woven items. *Uminayin le'arig kol shehu shehu tamei* — how do we know even a small woven thing is *tamei*? *Me'o beged* — from the *vav* of *o beged*, "or a garment," which appears in a *pasuk* listing categories of items susceptible to *tumah*. The *vav* — understood as the inclusive "or" — functions to broaden the category, encompassing even items of minimal size.

One might raise a difficulty: throughout many preceding *dappim* we have been discussing minimum sizes for garments — three fingers by three fingers, three handbreadths by three handbreadths. Tosafot addresses this directly: the distinction turns on whether the item was initially made at a larger size or initially fashioned to be small. A remnant of what was once a larger garment retains significance only if it meets the minimum size threshold. But an item initially crafted to function as a garment — however tiny — retains its *beged* status from the moment of its creation, regardless of size. That, according to Tosafot, is what the *o beged* derivation establishes.

The Gemara concludes with a *baraita*: *arig kol shehu tamei* — even a minimal woven item is *tamei*; *vetachshit kol shehu tamei* — even a minimal ornament is *tamei*; *arig vetachshit kol shehu tamei* — and a combination of woven and ornamental, even at minimal size, is *tamei*. This discussion of *tumah ve-taharah* will continue into the next *daf*.
