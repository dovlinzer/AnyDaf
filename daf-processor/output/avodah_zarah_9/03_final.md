# Avodah Zarah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sanhedrin's Exile

### Sanhedrin Moves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה עַד לֹא חָרַב הַבַּיִת גָּלְתָה סַנְהֶדְרִין וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ בַּחֲנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Forty years before the Temple was destroyed, the Sanhedrin was exiled** from the Chamber of Hewn Stone **and sat in the store** near the Temple Mount.

The *gemara* on daf 8b picks up the thread of a *baraita* concerning the period leading up to the destruction of the *beit hamikdash*. The *baraita* states: *arba'im shana ad lo charav habayit galta Sanhedrin veyashva la bakhanut* — forty years before the *beit hamikdash* was destroyed, the *sanhedrin* exiled itself and took up residence in the *khanut*. Although the word *khanut* suggests a shop or market stall, both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* understand it as referring to a different location on *Har HaBayit* — no longer situated within the *beit hamikdash* itself, but still within the broader Temple precinct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: לוֹמַר, שֶׁלֹּא דָּנוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is it necessary to know where the Sanhedrin would convene? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said:** It is necessary in order **to say that they no** longer **judged cases of fines.**

The question immediately arises: *lema'i hilcheta?* — for what legal consequence does this matter? Rav Yitzchak bar Adimi answers: once the *sanhedrin* relocated, they were no longer able to adjudicate *dinei knasot*, monetary fines. The reason is that imposing fines is not merely a matter of civil restitution; it represents the *beit din* exercising coercive punitive authority, which requires both *smicha* — rabbinic ordination traceable in an unbroken chain back to *Moshe Rabbeinu* — and the physical presence of the *sanhedrin* within the *beit hamikdash*. The moment they moved, even a short distance, the legal prerequisite was no longer met.

### Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרַם זָכוּר אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ לַטּוֹב, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא הוּא נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Indeed [*beram*], that man will be remembered favorably, and Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is his name, as had it not been for him the laws of fines would have been forgotten from** among **the Jewish people.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this framing. If the *sanhedrin* stopped adjudicating *dinei knasot* forty years before the *churban* — approximately the year 30 of the common era — how do we account for the famous account cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav? The *gemara* quotes: *baram zachur oto ha'ish letov veRabbi Yehuda ben Bava shemo* — let that man be remembered for good; Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is his name — *she'ilmalehu nishtachachu dinei knasot miYisrael* — for were it not for him, *dinei knasot* would have been forgotten from Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בָּטְלוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁגָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת הַרְשָׁעָה שְׁמָד עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל: כָּל הַסּוֹמֵךְ יֵהָרֵג, וְכׇל הַנִּסְמָךְ יֵהָרֵג, וְעִיר שֶׁסּוֹמְכִין בָּהּ תֵּחָרֵב, וּתְחוּם שֶׁסּוֹמְכִין בּוֹ יֵעָקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** his intention was to say that **the laws of fines would have ceased** to be implemented **from** among **the Jewish people,** as they would not have been able to adjudicate cases involving these *halakhot* due to a lack of ordained judges. This is **because** at one time **the wicked kingdom** of Rome **issued decrees of religious persecution against the Jewish people** with the aim of abolishing the chain of ordination and the authority of the Sages. They said that **anyone who ordains** judges **will be killed, and anyone who is ordained will be killed, and the city in which they ordain** the judges **will be destroyed, and** the areas around **the boundary** of the city **in which they ordain** judges **will be uprooted.** These measures were intended to discourage the Sages from performing or receiving ordination due to fear for the welfare of the local population.

The *gemara* presses: *ligresinu!* — could one not simply have learned the laws academically and preserved them? The answer is that the concern was not theoretical knowledge but practical application: *butlu dinei knasot miYisrael* — the actual practice of adjudicating fines would have ceased entirely. The reason was that the Roman government had issued a savage decree: whoever grants *smicha* shall be executed; whoever receives it shall be executed; the city in which it takes place shall be destroyed; and even the surrounding *tchum* shall be uprooted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא? הָלַךְ וְיָשַׁב בֵּין שְׁנֵי הָרִים גְּדוֹלִים, וּבֵין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת, בֵּין שְׁנֵי תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת, בֵּין אוּשָׁא לִשְׁפַרְעָם, וְסָמַךְ שָׁם חֲמִשָּׁה זְקֵנִים: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ, וְרַב אַוְיָא מוֹסִיף: אַף רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Rabbi **Yehuda ben Bava do? He went and sat between two large mountains, and between two large cities,** and **between two Shabbat boundaries: Between Usha and Shefaram,** i.e., in a desolate place that was not associated with any particular city so that he would not endanger anyone not directly involved, **and there he ordained five Elders,** namely: **Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Yosei, and Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua. And Rav Avya adds** that **Rabbi Neḥemya** was **also** among those ordained.

In the face of this, Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava acted with extraordinary courage. He went and situated himself between two large mountains and two large cities — specifically between Usha and Shefaram — placing himself outside the *tchum Shabbat* of either city. The intent was clear: he was not in any city, so no city could be destroyed; he was not in any *tchum*, so no *tchum* could be uprooted; and the sheer scale of the surrounding mountains and cities meant that any Roman reprisal would require leveling enormous, inhabited terrain. There, in that carefully chosen interstitial space, Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava conferred *smicha* on five *zkenim*: Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua — the great students of Rabbi Akiva, the leading *tannaim* of their generation, active around the year 130–150 of the common era. Rav Avimi adds that Rabbi Nechemia was also among those ordained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִכִּירוּ בָּהֶם אוֹיְבִים, אָמַר לָהֶם: בָּנַי, רוּצוּ! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רַבִּי, וְאַתָּה מָה תְּהֵא עָלֶיךָ? אָמַר לָהֶם: הֲרֵינִי מוּטָל לִפְנֵיהֶם כְּאֶבֶן שֶׁאֵין לָהּ הוֹפְכִין. אָמְרוּ: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁנָּעֲצוּ לְגוּפוֹ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת לוּלְנִיאוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, וַעֲשָׂאוּהוּ לְגוּפוֹ כִּכְבָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When** their **enemies discovered them,** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava **said to** the newly ordained rabbis: **My sons, run** for your lives. **They said to him:** Our **teacher, and what will be with you?** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava was elderly and unable to run. He **said to them:** In any case, **I am cast before them like a stone that cannot be overturned;** even if you attempt to assist me I will not be able to escape due to my frailty, but if you do not escape without me you will also be killed. People **said** about this incident: The Roman soldiers **did not move from there until they had inserted three hundred iron spears [*lulniot*] into his body, making his body** appear **like a sieve** pierced with many holes.

When the Romans became aware of what was happening, Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava told his students: *banai, rutzu* — my sons, flee. When they asked what would become of him, he answered that he would remain, immovable as a stone that cannot be overturned. He stood between his students and their pursuers, shielding them from the Roman assault. The *gemara* recounts that the Romans did not leave the spot *ad shena'atzu legufo gimel me'ot lulaniyot shel barzel* — until they had pierced his body with three hundred iron spears, making it *kichvarah* — like a sieve.

This episode, which took place roughly 150 years after the *sanhedrin* supposedly ceased adjudicating *dinei knasot*, demonstrates that the transmission of *smicha* — which enables *dinei knasot* — continued well after the forty-year mark before the *churban*. The original claim is therefore difficult to maintain as stated.

### Murderers & Capacity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לָא תֵּימָא דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא דָּנוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** in explanation: **Do not say** that after the Sanhedrin was exiled from the Chamber of Hewn Stone they no longer judged cases of **fines; rather,** emend the statement to say **that they no** longer **judged** cases of **capital law,** as a court does not have the authority to hear capital cases when the Sanhedrin is not sitting in the Chamber of Hewn Stone.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the difficulty: *lo teima dinei knasot* — the *baraita* does not mean that the *sanhedrin* stopped adjudicating fines. Rather, *ela shelo danu dinei nefashot* — it means they ceased adjudicating capital crimes. This is not merely a question of *smicha*. While *smicha* is indeed required for any exercise of the *beit din*'s punitive power — whether *knas*, *malkut*, or *mita* — capital cases carry an additional requirement: the *sanhedrin hagadol* must be physically present within the *beit hamikdash* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דִּנְפִישִׁי לְהוּ רוֹצְחִין וְלָא יָכְלִי לְמֵידַן, אֲמַרוּ: מוּטָב נִגְלֵי מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיחַיְּיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that the members of the Sanhedrin ceased to meet in their proper place and thereby ended the adjudication of capital cases? **Once they saw that the murderers were so numerous and they were not able to judge** them and punish them with death, **they said:** It is **better that we should be exiled** from the Chamber of Hewn Stone and move **from place to place, so that** offenders **will not be** deemed **liable** to receive the death penalty in a time period when the court does not carry out their sentences.

Why did the *sanhedrin* remove itself? The *gemara* explains: *keivan d'chazu denafishu lehu rotzchin velo yachlu lemidan* — when they saw that murderers had multiplied beyond their capacity to judge, they reasoned: *mutav nigli mimakom lemakom ki heichi d'lo litchayvu* — better to go into exile than to remain in a legal position of responsibility they could no longer fulfill.

The precise meaning of *d'lo litchayvu* invites scrutiny. *Rashi* reads it as: so that the murderers should not be held liable for the death penalty — since without a properly situated *sanhedrin* there can be no formal capital judgment. One might, however, prefer to read it as the *beit din* itself not being held accountable: better to place oneself in a position of legal incapacity than to remain formally obligated to judge while visibly powerless to do so. The distinction matters. In the latter reading, the *sanhedrin*'s relocation was an act of institutional self-protection — removing themselves from a situation where their impotence would be conspicuous and their failure to act publicly evident.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עַל פִּי הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יַגִּידוּ לְךָ מִן הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַמָּקוֹם גּוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why a court may not adjudicate capital cases once the Sanhedrin has left the Chamber of Hewn Stone. **As it is written: "And you shall do according to the tenor of the sentence, which they shall declare to you from that place"** (Deuteronomy 17:10). This verse **teaches that** it is **the place** where the Sanhedrin resides that **causes** the judgment to take place. In other words, if the Sanhedrin has abandoned its proper place, the Chamber of Hewn Stone, all courts must cease judging capital cases.

The *gemara* supports the ruling with a *pasuk*: *ve'asita al pi hadavar asher yagidu lecha min hamakom hahu* — you shall act according to what they instruct you from that place — teaching that *hamakom goreim*, the place itself is a constitutive requirement for the authority to judge capital cases.

## Chronology Overview

### Kingdom Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַלְכוּת פָּרַס בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאַרְבַּע שָׁנָה, מַלְכוּת יָוָן בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים שָׁנָה, מַלְכוּת חַשְׁמוֹנַאי בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת מֵאָה וְשָׁלֹשׁ, מַלְכוּת בֵּית הוֹרְדוֹס מֵאָה וְשָׁלֹשׁ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ צֵא וַחֲשׁוֹב כַּמָּה שָׁנִים אַחַר חוּרְבַּן הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Persian Empire,** which ruled the world before the construction of the Temple, continued to do so for **34 years in the presence of the Temple,** i.e., after the Temple was built; **the Greek Empire** ruled for **180 years in the presence of the Temple; the Hasmonean dynasty** ruled for **103** years **in the presence of the Temple;** and **the Herodian dynasty** likewise ruled for **103** years. **From this** point **forward,** you can **go and calculate** the date on which an event occurred by **how many years** it happened **after the destruction of the Temple.**

The *gemara* now turns to rabbinic chronology, working through the succession of foreign powers that ruled over Israel during the period of the Second *beit hamikdash*. The *baraita* records: *malchus Paras bifnei habayit shloshim ve'arba shanah* — the Persian kingdom held sway during the era of the *beit hamikdash* for 34 years. This reflects the rabbinic tradition that the Second *beit hamikdash*, rebuilt under Cyrus (*Koresh*), was constructed under Persian governance for only 34 years before the rise of Greek dominion. *Malchus Yavan bifnei habayit me'ah ushmonim shanah* — the Greek kingdom ruled during that era for 180 years. After that came 103 years of *Chashmonai* rule, followed by the Herodian period of 103 years under Roman suzerainty — together yielding the classical rabbinic figure that the Second *beit hamikdash* stood for 420 years in total.

It should be noted that secular historical reckoning places the duration of the Second Temple period at approximately 586 years, a discrepancy of over 160 years from the rabbinic count. The sources of this gap have been debated extensively; for the purposes of the *gemara*'s internal calculations, the 420-year figure is the working assumption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא מָאתַן וְשֵׁית הֲווֹ, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא עֶשְׂרִין וְשֵׁית שְׁנִין קָמוּ בְּהֵימָנוּתַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלָא אִישְׁתַּעְבַּדוּ בְּהוּ, וְאַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ כְּשֶׁפָּשְׁטָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** According to this statement of Rabbi Yosei, **evidently** when the Greek rule over the Jewish people ended and the Roman rule began **it was 206** years before the Second Temple was destroyed, i.e., the two sets of one hundred and three years of the Hasmonean dynasty and the Herodian dynasty. **But you said** that when the Roman Empire stretched forth over Israel and ruled over them **it was 180** years before the Temple was destroyed. This is not a contradiction, but **rather,** it proves that for **26 years** the Romans **stood faithfully with the Jewish people,** honoring their agreement, **and did not subjugate them. And therefore,** Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **does not count these** 26 years among the total years in **which the evil** Roman **Empire stretched forth over Israel** and ruled over them.

The *gemara* notes that from the beginning of Greek rule through the Herodian period amounts to 206 years before the *churban*. Yet an earlier statement held that the Romans took control only 180 years before the *churban*. The discrepancy — 206 versus 180 — is 26 years, and this becomes the subject of the next discussion.

### Roman Delay Period

The *gemara* resolves the 26-year discrepancy with reference to a tradition that the Romans had entered into a pact with the Jewish people to act jointly against the Greeks. During the first 26 years of what would otherwise be counted as Roman dominion, the Romans honored this agreement and did not subjugate Israel: *kamu be'heimanuteihu bahadi Yisrael velo ishtabdu vehu* — they stood by their word with Israel and did not impose dominion over them. Therefore, when the earlier statement says the wicked government subjugated Israel 180 years before the *churban*, it counts only from the point at which Rome actually imposed its rule, not from the formal inception of Roman regional power. Those first 26 years — whether understood as a period of Roman good faith or, more simply, as a period of genuine *Chashmonai* self-rule and autonomy — are accordingly excluded from the reckoning of Roman subjugation.

## Three Dating Systems

### Dating Systems Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי טָעֵי הַאי תַּנָּא, וְלָא יָדַע פְּרָטֵי כַּמָּה הֲוָה — לִישַׁיְּילֵיהּ לְסָפְרָא כַּמָּה כָּתֵיב, וְנִיטַפֵּי עֲלַיְיהוּ עֶשְׂרִין שְׁנִין, וּמַשְׁכַּח לֵיהּ לְחוּמְרֵיהּ, וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״זֶה לִּי עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי בְּבֵיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the above *baraita*, the Gemara relates that **Rav Pappa said: If this *tanna*,** i.e., one who dates years in reference to the destruction of the Temple, **forgot and did not know the details** of the date with regard to exactly **how much** time had passed since the destruction of the Temple, e.g., he remembered the century but could not recall the exact decade and year, **let him ask a scribe** who writes official documents **how many** years **he writes** when he dates the documents. The dating system of scribes uses as its starting point the beginning of the Greek rule, 380 years before the destruction of the Temple. **And let him add 20 years to** the number, **and** in this manner **he** will **find his number [*leḥumreih*]. And your mnemonic** to remember this is the verse: **"These twenty years have I been in your house"** (Genesis 31:41).

Rav Pappa introduces the practical concern that motivates this entire discussion: *i ta'ei tanna velo yada paratei kama havei lish'alei lesafra kama kesiv* — if a *tanna* becomes confused and does not know the *pratim*, the last two digits of the year count, he should go and ask a *sofer*, a scribe, what year he writes in his documents.

This presupposes an understanding of three distinct dating systems in use among the Jewish people. The most familiar to a modern reader is *beriyas olam* — counting years from the creation of the world — which is the system used in contemporary *ketubot* and *gittin*, typically accompanied by the phrase *livriyas olam leminyan she'anu monin* to specify which count is meant. A second system counted years from the *churban habayit*. A third system — arguably the most widespread in practice — was called *minyan shtarot*, the standard dating system used by *sofrim* for legal documents.

### Minyan Shtarot

*Minyan shtarot* began 380 years before the *churban habayit*, placing its year zero in the middle of the period of Greek dominion — specifically, as will be clarified, corresponding to the rise of the Seleucid Empire. This means that the year of the *churban habayit* was year 380 in *minyan shtarot* reckoning, and any year after the *churban* adds to that baseline.

This system was the standard employed by *sofrim* throughout the ancient Jewish world. The scribe writing a legal document would not count from the *churban* or from creation; he would write the year according to *minyan shtarot*, which had been the universal civic standard across the ancient Near East for centuries.

### Conversion Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי טָעֵי סָפְרָא — נְשַׁיְּילֵיהּ לְתַנָּא כַּמָּה חָשֵׁיב, וּנְבַצַּר מִינַּיְיהוּ עֶשְׂרִין שְׁנִין, וּמַשְׁכַּח לֵיהּ לְחוּמְרֵיהּ. וְסִימָנָיךְ: סָפְרָא בְּצִירָא, תַּנָּא תּוֹסְפָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **if a scribe forgot** the exact year, **let him ask** the ***tanna* how much** time **he calculates** has passed since the Temple's destruction, **and he should deduct from** the number **twenty years, and** in this manner **he** will **find his number. And your mnemonic** to remember that the *tanna* adds twenty to the date of the scribe, whereas the scribe deducts twenty from the number of the *tanna*, is as follows: **A scribe deducts,** while **a *tanna* adds on [*tosfa'a*].** This is a play on the phrase *tanna tosfa'a*, which also means to teach the *Tosefta*, the practice of a *tanna*.

The *gemara* provides the conversion formula between *minyan shtarot* and the *churban habayit* count. If a *tanna* — meaning someone using the *churban*-based reckoning — is uncertain of his last two digits, he should ask a *sofer* what the current *minyan shtarot* year is, and then add 20. Conversely, if a *sofer* using *minyan shtarot* is uncertain of his last two digits, he should ask the *tanna* his count and subtract 20.

To see why the adjustment is 20: the *churban habayit* corresponds to year 380 in *minyan shtarot*. Any subsequent year, expressed in *minyan shtarot*, will therefore exceed the *churban*-based count by 380. Since the hundreds are assumed known and only the last two digits are in question, the practical arithmetic reduces to adding or subtracting 20 — because 380 ends in 80, and adding 80 to any two-digit number and rounding for the century is equivalent to subtracting 20 (or, looking at it the other way, the *churban*-based count will lag the *minyan shtarot* by 80 in the tens and units, which when working modulo 100 is the same as being 20 ahead). The *gemara* provides a mnemonic for why one adds 20 when converting from *minyan shtarot* to *churban*-based dating: *ze li esrim shana beveitecha* — this is twenty years I have been in your house — a phrase with resonances of Israel reckoning its years within the house of the nations.

The corresponding mnemonic for the reverse — a *sofer* subtracting 20 — is *safra betzira*, the scribe reduces, with *Rashi* suggesting this evokes the *sofer*'s practice of writing words in their *chaser* form, without full spelling. The *tanna*, on the other hand, adds — *tanna tosfa* — as a *tanna* teaches a *tosefta*, implying augmentation.

### Seleucid Origin

One might reasonably ask: why should Jews date their documents from the rise of a Greek or Hellenistic empire? The answer is simply that this was the standard practice of the ancient world, and Jewish communities lived within that world. The Seleucid Empire rose to prominence around 312 BCE. The *beit hamikdash* was destroyed in 70 CE, which is indeed approximately 382 years after 312 BCE — consistent with the *gemara*'s figure of 380, given the rough approximations of rabbinic chronology. The rabbinic text frames the origin as the beginning of Greek world dominion, understanding the first six years of Greek rule as a period before that dominion became truly universal — a point the *gemara* will elaborate further.

The parallel to the modern situation is striking and the *gemara* itself seems aware of it. Just as contemporary Jews speak of dates in terms of the common era — itself derived from a non-Jewish religious reckoning — the Jews of the Talmudic period calibrated their documents to the dominant civic calendar of their world. The tractate of *avodah zara*, which is devoted to demarcating the boundaries between Israel and the surrounding culture, thus contains within it a remarkable acknowledgment: the very numbering of years in Jewish legal documents reflected immersion in the non-Jewish world. The *sofrim* of Babylonia continued using *minyan shtarot* through approximately the 9th century; the Jewish community of Egypt used it until the 14th century; and the Jews of Yemen dated their documents by *minyan shtarot* into the 20th century.

## Six-Thousand-Year Cycle

### Tanna D'vei Eliyahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ: שֵׁשֶׁת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה הָוֵי הָעוֹלָם, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים תּוֹהוּ, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים תּוֹרָה, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, בַּעֲוֹנוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁרַבּוּ יָצְאוּ מֵהֶן מַה שֶּׁיָּצְאוּ מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** In relation to the discussion on the calculation of years, the Gemara states that one of the Sages **of the school of Eliyahu taught: The world is** destined to exist for **six thousand years.** For **two thousand years** the world was **waste,** as the Torah had not yet been given. The next set of **two thousand years** are the time period of the **Torah.** The last set of **two thousand years** are the period designated for **the days of the Messiah,** but **due to our many sins** there are **those** years **that have been taken from them,** i.e., such and such years have already passed and **have been taken from** the two thousand years that are designated for the Messiah, and the Messiah has not yet arrived. Whenever a *tanna* taught this *baraita*, he would insert the number of years that was correct for his time.

The *gemara* now cites a famous cosmological teaching: *Tanna D'vei Eliyahu* — from the school of Eliyahu — *sheshet alfei shana havei alma* — the world endures for six thousand years. This framework maps the six days of creation onto six millennia of human history, with the seventh thousand year corresponding to *Shabbat* — the era of *olam haba*. The Ramban's commentary on the first chapter of *Bereishit* develops this correspondence in detail, matching the symbolism of each day of creation to the character of its corresponding millennium.

The six thousand years are divided as follows: *shnei alafim tohu* — the first two thousand years are *tohu*, void and waste, evoking the *tohu vavohu* of the primordial creation, a period before *Torah* and before Avraham's transformative influence. *Shnei alafim Torah* — the next two thousand years are the era of *Torah*. *Ushnei alafim yemot HaMashiach* — the final two thousand years constitute the era of the *mashiach*, the period in which redemption is possible and expected. *Uva'avonoteinu sherabu, yatzo mehen ma she-yatzo* — but because of our many sins, the *mashiach*-era has already extended far beyond its opening threshold without the redemption's arrival.

### Torah Era Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים תּוֹרָה מֵאֵימַת? אִי נֵימָא מִמַּתַּן תּוֹרָה עַד הַשְׁתָּא, לֵיכָּא כּוּלֵּי הַאי, דְּכִי מְעַיְּינַתְּ בְּהוּ תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי פְּרָטֵי דְּהַאי אַלְפָּא הוּא דַּהֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **two thousand years** of the time period of the **Torah, from when** are they counted? **If we say** that they started **from the giving of the Torah until now,** then **there is not** enough time for **all of these** years. **As, when you examine** the calculations, it is evident that from the creation of the world until the giving of the Torah **there were two thousand** years plus **a part of this** third **thousand,** as the Torah was given 2,448 years after the creation of the world. This would mean that the time period of the Torah encroaches upon the days of the Messiah.

This raises an immediate question: *shnei alafim Torah me'eimat* — when does the two-thousand-year era of *Torah* begin? The obvious answer would be from *matan Torah* at *Har Sinai*, but this creates a problem. *Matan Torah* occurred in the year 2448 from the creation of the world — not at year 2000. If the *Torah* era began only at year 2448, then the middle two thousand years are badly misaligned with the framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִ״וְּאֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר עָשׂוּ בְחָרָן״, וּגְמִירִי דְּאַבְרָהָם בְּהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא בַּר חַמְשִׁין וְתַרְתֵּי הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the two-thousand-year time period of the Torah is counted **from** the time when it is stated about Abraham and Sarah: **"And the souls that they had gotten in Haran"** (Genesis 12:5), which is interpreted by the Sages as referring to the men and women who were brought closer to the Torah by Abraham and Sarah. Therefore, it was at this point that the Torah began to spread throughout the world. **And it is learned** as a tradition **that at that time Abraham was fifty-two** years old.

The *gemara* therefore says: *ela min hanefesh asher asu veCharan* — rather, the *Torah* era begins from "the souls they made in Charan" (Bereishit 12:5), which the tradition understands as referring to Avraham's activity of bringing people close to God and teaching them monotheism. And there is a received tradition that *Avraham be'hee sha'ata bar chamsin vetartei havei* — Avraham was 52 years old at that time. Since Avraham was born in the year 1948 from creation, the year 2000 corresponds precisely to the moment when Avraham, at age 52, was still in Charan, preaching the existence of God and drawing people toward belief — even before God spoke to him at the age of 75 with the command of *lech lecha*. The two-thousand-year era of *Torah* therefore begins not from *matan Torah* but from this first moment of Avraham's public monotheistic mission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה בָּצְרָן מִדְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא? אַרְבַּע מֵאָה וְאַרְבָּעִים וּתְמָנְיָא שְׁנִין הָוְיָין, כִּי מְעַיְּינַתְּ בֵּיהּ מֵ״הַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר עָשׂוּ בְחָרָן״ עַד מַתַּן תּוֹרָה — אַרְבַּע מְאָה וְאַרְבָּעִים וּתְמָנְיָא שְׁנִין הָוְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **How many** years are **missing from** the two thousand year period of the Messiah, if the period of the Torah **that is taught by the *tanna*** is counted from the giving of the Torah? **There are 448** missing **years. When you examine** the calculation **from** the time when it is stated about Abraham and Sarah: **"The souls that they had gotten in Haran," until** the time of **the giving of the Torah, there are 448 years.**

This reading reframes the standard chronology: *matan Torah* was not the inauguration of the *Torah* era but its continuation, arriving 448 years into a process that began with Avraham in Charan. The *gemara* notes that if one were to count from *matan Torah*, one would be short by 448 years — *arba me'ah ve'arba'im vetamnya shanin* — confirming that the *tanna*'s calculation only works if we start the clock at Avraham's age 52.

### Year 4000 Calculation

Working through the rabbinic chronology: from the year 2000 (Avraham in Charan) to the birth of Yitzchak at Avraham's age 100 is 48 years, bringing us to year 2048. The tradition holds that the 400 years of exile foretold to Avraham (*ki ger yihyeh zaracha*) is counted from the birth of Yitzchak, since Yitzchak was already a stranger in a land not his own from birth. From Yitzchak's birth the count runs to the Exodus: 400 years, reaching year 2448 — the year of *yetziat Mitzrayim* and, shortly after, *matan Torah* at *Har Sinai*. (The intermediate figure of 210 years marks the descent of Yaakov and his family to Egypt, with the full 400-year reckoning encompassing a longer sweep.) The First *beit hamikdash* was built 480 years after the Exodus, at approximately year 2928. It stood for 410 years, was destroyed, and after 70 years of exile the Second *beit hamikdash* was built — its destruction in the year 3828 from creation. Adding 172 years brings us to year 4000 — which corresponds to approximately 172 CE, the period marking the close of the *tannaitic* era and the transition to the *amoraim*. The *gemara* presents this as the natural terminus of the *Torah* era and the beginning of the two-thousand-year *mashiach* period.

## Olam/Shtarot Conversion

### Add Forty-Eight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי טָעֵי תַּנָּא וְלָא יָדַע פְּרָטֵיהּ כַּמָּה הָוֵי — לִישַׁיְּילֵיהּ לְסָפְרָא כַּמָּה כְּתִיב, וְנִיטַפֵּי עֲלַיְיהוּ אַרְבְּעִין וְתַמְנֵי, וּמַשְׁכַּח לֵיהּ לְחוּמְרֵיהּ, וְסִימָנָיךְ:
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this *baraita* as well, **Rav Pappa said: If a *tanna*** meant to state that at his time such and such years were already lost from the two thousand years that are designated for the days of the Messiah, but he **forgot and did not know the details** of exactly **how much** time had passed, **let him ask a scribe how many** years **he writes** when he dates documents. **And let him add forty-eight to** the number, correcting for the century, **and** in this manner **he** will **find his number. And your mnemonic** for this is the verse:

Rav Pappa states: *i ta'ei tanna velo yada pratei kama hava, lish'alei lesafra* — if someone counting from *beriyas olam* (or, more precisely, from the year 4000, which is effectively the same system with the leading four thousand dropped) does not know his last two digits, let him ask the *sofer* who is counting *leminyan shtarot*. He should then take the *sofer*'s last two digits and *venitfei alaiyhu arba vetamne* — add 48 — and he will have his result.

The arithmetic behind this is as follows. The *churban habayit* was year 380 in *minyan shtarot* and year 3828 in *beriyas olam* — or, equivalently, year 172 after the year 4000 baseline. Since the year 4000 baseline corresponds to year 552 in *minyan shtarot* (380 plus 172 — wait: 380 is the *minyan shtarot* year of the *churban*, and the year 4000 is 172 years after the *churban*, so the year 4000 is *minyan shtarot* year 552), to convert from *minyan shtarot* to the 4000-based count, one must subtract 552 — or, working only with the last two digits and modulo 100, add 48. The formula thus emerges: take the last two digits of the *minyan shtarot* year and add 48 to obtain the corresponding last two digits in the *beriyas olam* / year-4000 count.

### Mnemonic: 48 Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה עִיר״. וְאִי טָעֵי סָפְרָא — נְשַׁיְּילֵיהּ לְתַנָּא כַּמָּה קָתָנֵי, וְנִיבַצַּר מִינַּיְיהוּ אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה, וּמַשְׁכַּח לֵיהּ לְחוּמְרֵיהּ, וְסִימָנָיךְ: סָפְרָא בְּצִירָא, תַּנָּא תּוֹסְפָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Forty-eight cities"** (Numbers 35:7). Similarly, **if a scribe forgot** the exact year, **let him ask the *tanna* how many** years does **he teach** have already been lost from the two thousand years that are designated for the days of the Messiah. **And he should deduct forty-eight** years **from** the number, correcting for the century, **and** in this manner **he** will **find his number. And your mnemonic** to remember that the *tanna* must add to the date of the scribe, while the scribe must deduct from the number of the *tanna*, is again: **A scribe deducts,** while **a *tanna* adds on [*tosfa'a*].**

The *gemara* supplies a mnemonic for the number 48: *arba'im ushmonah ir* — forty-eight cities. This refers to the forty-eight cities of the *Levi'im*, the priestly cities distributed to the tribe of Levi throughout the land of Israel as enumerated in *Bamidbar*. The association is simply numerical — the same figure of 48 appears in the *Torah* in a memorable context, anchoring it in memory.

### Subtract Forty-Eight

The reverse conversion works correspondingly: *ve'i ta'ei safra*, if the *sofer* using *minyan shtarot* forgets his last two digits, he should obtain the *beriyas olam* / year-4000 count and *venivtzar mineih arba'im ushmoneh* — subtract 48 — and he will recover his *minyan shtarot* position. The mnemonic again: *safra betzira*, the *sofer* reduces — just as a *sofer* writes in the abbreviated *chaser* spelling — while *tanna tosfa*, the *tanna* adds, as the *tanna* teaches an expansive *tosefta*.

To summarize the full set of conversion relationships: to move from *minyan shtarot* to *churban*-based dating, add 20. To move from *minyan shtarot* to *beriyas olam* / year-4000 dating, add 48. The inverse operations subtract those same values. All three dating systems are thus interconvertible, provided one knows which system is in use and can perform the appropriate adjustment to the last two digits.

## Shmita Cycle Calculation

### Add One, Divide by 7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הַאי מַאן דְּלָא יָדַע כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי בְּשָׁבוּעַ הוּא עוֹמֵד — נִיטַּפֵּי חַד שַׁתָּא, וְנִחְשׁוֹב כְּלָלֵי בְּיוֹבְלֵי, וּפְרָטֵי בְּשָׁבוּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** In the case of **one who does not know which year of** the seven-year **Sabbatical** cycle **he stands** in, **he should add one year** to the years that have passed since the destruction of the Temple in order to begin at the start of a Sabbatical cycle. **And he should calculate the general** years, i.e., the centuries, **as Jubilee** cycles, as every Jubilee concludes seven completed Sabbatical cycles of forty-nine years, **and the details as Sabbatical** cycles, i.e., he should divide the remaining years into seven-year Sabbatical cycles.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua turns to an adjacent problem: given a year expressed as years since the *churban habayit*, how does one determine which year of the *shmita* cycle one is in? The key datum is that the *churban habayit* itself fell in year 1 of a *shmita* cycle. This establishes the anchor from which all subsequent *shmita* calculations flow.

The basic algorithm is: take the number of years elapsed since the *churban*, add 1 (because the *churban* year itself was year 1, so the following year was year 2), and divide by 7. The remainder gives the position within the current *shmita* cycle.

### Hundred Extraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִשְׁקֹל מִמֵּאָה תְּרֵי, וְנִשְׁדֵּי אַפְּרָטֵי, וְנַחְשׁוֹבִינְהוּ לִפְרָטֵי בְּשָׁבוּעֵי, וְיִדַּע כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי בְּשָׁבוּעַ, וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״כִּי זֶה שְׁנָתַיִם הָרָעָב בְּקֶרֶב הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **But** as the Jubilee Year itself is counted as one year of a Sabbatical cycle, **he should take, from** every **hundred** years that passed, **two** years, **and add it to the details,** i.e., the remaining decades and single years. **And** then **he should calculate** with **the details** by dividing them **into Sabbatical** cycles. **And** from the remainder **he will know how many years in the** current **Sabbatical** cycle have passed. **And your mnemonic** that two years must be deducted from every century is the following verse: **"For these two years the famine has been in the land"** (Genesis 45:6).

The *gemara* offers a computational shorthand suited to an era without Arabic numerals and long division. Rather than dividing the full number by 7, one can: extract all the hundreds; account for two additional years for every hundred extracted; add those extra years to the remaining sub-hundred number; and then divide that manageable remainder by 7.

To illustrate: suppose it is 518 years after the *churban*. Add 1: now 519. Extract the hundreds: 500, with a remainder of 19. For every hundred, add two — five hundreds yield an additional 10 — giving 29. Divide 29 by 7: four full cycles of 7, with a remainder of 1. The current year is therefore year 1 of the *shmita* cycle.

The reason this works is rooted in the structure of the *yovel* cycle. The *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the 50th year of the *yovel* itself counts as year 1 of the subsequent *shmita* cycle, rather than like the *chachamim*, who hold that the 50th year stands alone and year 1 begins anew afterward. Under Rabbi Yehuda's position, every century spans exactly two *yovel* periods of 50 years each, but because the *yovel* year doubles as the first year of the next cycle, each century of 100 years actually corresponds to 100 years minus 2 — meaning that for each century removed from the count, two years have already been "used up" within the *yovel* structure and must be added back into the sub-hundred remainder before dividing by 7.

A student might rightly observe: if we are ruling like Rabbi Yehuda and the *yovel* year serves as year 1 of the next cycle, the entire structure is simply divisible by 7, and one could simply take the year-after-*churban* number, add 1, and divide by 7 directly. This is correct, and the *gemara*'s algorithm is simply an easier method for mental arithmetic when only Roman-style numerals were available and extracting small remainders by hand was simpler than performing full long division.

### Famine Mnemonic

The *gemara* provides a mnemonic for the critical step of adding two for every hundred removed: *ki zeh shenatayim haraav bekerev haaretz* — "for these two years has the famine been in the land" (Bereishit 45:6). The number two in that verse serves as the memory hook for the two-year adjustment per hundred years in the *shmita* calculation.

## Messiah Timeline

### Rabbi Chanina's 400 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַחַר אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת לְחוּרְבַּן הַבַּיִת, אִם יֹאמַר לְךָ אָדָם: קַח שָׂדֶה שָׁוֶה אֶלֶף דִּינָרִים בְּדִינָר אֶחָד — לֹא תִּקַּח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says: After** the year **400 from the destruction of the Temple, if a person says to you: Purchase a field** that is **worth one thousand dinars for one dinar, do not purchase** it.

Rabbi Chanina teaches: *achar arba me'ot leChurban HaBayit, im yomar lecha adam kach sadeh shaveh elef dinarin bedinar, lo tikach* — after 400 years from the destruction of the *beit hamikdash*, if someone offers you a field worth a thousand *dinarim* for a single *dinar*, do not take it. The reasoning is that by 400 years after the *churban*, the *mashiach* should certainly have arrived, and investing in property outside the land of Israel at that point would be pointless — one would shortly be returning to Israel with the redemption.

This teaching flows naturally from the cosmological framework established earlier. If the year 4000 from creation — the onset of the *mashiach* era — fell approximately 172 years after the *churban*, then 400 years after the *churban* places us roughly 228 years into the two-thousand-year *mashiach* period. The implication is not that *mashiach* will come precisely at the 400-year mark, but that 400 years into a period designated for messianic redemption, investment predicated on continued exile outside Israel is unreasonable.

### Baraita's 4231

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אַחַר אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים וּמָאתַיִם וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְאַחַת שָׁנָה לִבְרִיאַת עוֹלָם, אִם יֹאמַר לְךָ אָדָם: קַח לָךְ שָׂדֶה שָׁוֶה אֶלֶף דִּינָרִים בְּדִינָר אֶחָד — אַל תִּקַּח. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ תְּלָת שְׁנִין, דְּמַתְנִיתָא טָפְיָא תְּלָת שְׁנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught in a *baraita*: After** the year **4,231 from the creation of the world, if a person says to you: Purchase a field** that is **worth one thousand dinars for one dinar, do not purchase** it. The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** these two dates? The Gemara answers: There **is** a difference **between them** of **three years, as the *baraita* adds three years.** The year 400 from the destruction of the Temple corresponds to the year 4,228 from the creation of the world.

A *baraita* formulates the same teaching differently: *achar arba'at alafim uma'tayim ushloshim ve'achat shanah l'olam* — after 4231 years from creation, if someone offers you a field worth a thousand *dinarim* for a single *dinar*, do not take it. The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai beinayhu* — what is the difference between these two formulations?

The answer: *ika beinayhu tlas shanin* — there are three years between them. Four hundred years after the *churban*, given that the year 4000 corresponds to 172 years after the *churban*, is the year 4228 from creation. The *baraita* says 4231. The two traditions thus differ by exactly three years. Both agree, in substance, that *mashiach* should arrive at approximately the year 4228–4231 from creation — corresponding to the late 4th or early 5th century of the common era, a period that saw the collapse of Roman imperial order, the rise of Byzantine Christianity, and the subsequent emergence of Islam: a moment of profound civilizational transformation within which the Talmudic sages might well have expected divine intervention in history. *Umasnisa tefei tlas shani* — the *baraita*, counting from *beriyas olam*, came out three years later than Rabbi Chanina's *churban*-based count.
