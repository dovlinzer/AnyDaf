# Ketubot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiyum Shtarot & Judges

### Peh She'Asar Intro

The daf opens three lines from the bottom of 21b, with a teaching transmitted in the name of Rabbi Abba, who said it in the name of Rav Huna, who said it in the name of Rav. Before turning to that teaching directly, it is worth noting the broader context. The overarching theme of this section of *Ketubot* is *peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir* — the principle that the same mouth which creates a prohibition is believed to remove it. One of the central examples in the *mishnah* we have been working through involves witnesses who testify that a signature on a document is their own, but then introduce circumstances that would effectively invalidate it. That discussion has led us into a broader treatment of *kiyum shtaros* — the formal validation of documents by a *beis din* — and the rules governing contradictory testimony. The material we are about to address continues squarely within that world.

Before proceeding, it is worth briefly revisiting a question that arose in the previous session: whether it matters that a text is written before it is accurate, provided the signatories wait until it becomes accurate before signing. The concern is one of *me-chazi ke-mezuyaf* — the appearance of forgery. If the document declares "the three of us recognize this signature" but at the time of writing there are not yet three who have concurred, can it be pre-written in anticipation of the eventual signing? One practical echo of this question involves the *kesubah*, where some have the custom not to complete the *kuf* of *ve-kanina* until after the *kinyan* has been made, so that the text should not assert something as done that has not yet occurred. And yet, as was pointed out, the rest of the *kesubah* is also not yet true at the time of writing — it attests to a marriage that has not yet taken place, since *Ashkenazim* sign before the wedding. What makes the document valid is ultimately its signing, not its writing, and so the preoccupation with that single letter is puzzling. But this is an interesting tension and was the issue left at the end of the previous session.

### Three Judges, Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ לְקַיֵּים אֶת הַשְּׁטָר, וְקָרָא עַרְעָר עַל אֶחָד מֵהֶן. עַד שֶׁלֹּא חָתְמוּ — מְעִידִין עָלָיו וְחוֹתֵם. מִשֶּׁחָתְמוּ — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו וְחוֹתֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said that Rav Huna said that Rav said:** In the case of **three** judges **who convened** as a tribunal **to ratify a document, and** a person **raised a challenge** with regard to the fitness **of one of them** to serve as a judge, thereby preventing ratification of the document, **as long as they did not** yet **sign** the ratification, the other two judges **may testify about** the acceptability of the judge whose fitness was challenged, and **he** then **signs** the ratification. However, **once they signed** the ratification, **they may no** longer **testify about his** fitness **and** thereby enable him to **sign** the ratification. Once they sign, their testimony is no longer impartial because there is a conflict of interest as they seek to avoid being associated with a tribunal tainted by an unfit judge.

The teaching of Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav Huna in the name of Rav runs as follows: when three judges convene to validate a document — *sheloshah she-yashvu le-kayem es ha-shtar* — and a challenge (*irer*) arises against one of them questioning his validity, the following distinction applies. *Ad she-lo chasmu* — before they have signed the document — the other two may testify on behalf of the third. The reasoning is that at this stage they are not *nogeia badavar*, not a party to the matter. Their own standing has not yet been formally committed to the document, and so if the third turns out to be invalid, they can simply ask him to step down and bring in a replacement. There is no self-interest compelling their testimony. *Vechasim* — they may then go ahead and sign. But *mishechasmu* — if they have already signed the document — they can no longer testify on his behalf. At that point, their testimony in support of the third judge is tantamount to testifying in support of their own document, and they are considered *nogeia be-eidus*, partial witnesses whose testimony cannot be credited.

### Challenge Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַרְעָר דְּמַאי? אִי עַרְעָר דְּגַזְלָנוּתָא — תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ. אִי עַרְעָר דִּפְגַם מִשְׁפָּחָה, גִּלּוּי מִלְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ עַרְעָר דְּגַזְלָנוּתָא, וְקָאָמְרִי הָנֵי: יָדְעִינַן בֵּיהּ דַּעֲבַד תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: With regard to **a challenge of what** sort was this *halakha* stated? **If** it was **a challenge** based on an allegation **of theft,** the witnesses who testified that he is unfit and the judges who testified that he is fit **are two** witnesses **and two** witnesses who contradict them, and in that case, the allegation of theft is not completely eliminated. **If** it was **a challenge** based on an allegation of **flawed lineage,** e.g., he is a Canaanite slave and therefore unfit to serve as a judge, that **is a mere revealing of a matter** that will ultimately be revealed in any case and does not require actual testimony. Therefore, there is no conflict of interest preventing the judges from asserting his fitness after they signed. The Gemara concludes: **Actually, I will say to you** that it was **a challenge** based on an allegation **of theft, and these** judges **say: We know about him that he repented** and is now fit to serve as a judge. In that case, their testimony does not contradict the original testimony that he was guilty of theft.

The *gemara* now asks: what type of challenge are we dealing with? *Ei irur de-gazlanusa* — if the challenge is one of *gazlanusa*, meaning that two witnesses are asserting that this third judge is a *gazlan*, a thief, and therefore disqualified as a witness and a *dayan* — then the situation is *trei u-verei ninhu*, two against two. If two are saying he is a *gazlan* and the other two members of the *beis din* are countering that he is not, that would ordinarily be a standoff of equal testimony. Why would the second two be credited?

One might ask whether the word *irer* here implies formal testimony at all, or merely a rumor. Other *gemaros* assume that *irer* refers to actual testimony by two people, and that is the assumption adopted here.

### Giluy Milta Doctrine

The *gemara* therefore turns to a second type of challenge: *irur de-pgam mishpacha*, a challenge to his personal status — for example, a claim that he is a slave, which would disqualify him. In such a case, the *gemara* says: *giluy milsa be-alma hu*. This is not true testimony in the conventional sense; it is merely disclosure of a matter. The reasoning, as *Rashi* explains, is that a person's lineage and personal status are facts that are ultimately going to become known. They are not the kind of thing that is exclusively within the knowledge of two individuals. Other people know, there are records, the matter will be discovered. Because of this, the two *beis din* members who declare him valid are not regarded as offering formal *eidus* — they are merely providing information that is itself susceptible to public corroboration.

*Rashi* articulates this clearly: even after they have signed the document — *mishechasmu* — why can they not testify that he is not a slave? The concern would be *nogeia be-eidus*. But here, he writes, this is a matter that requires investigation and is of the type that will come to light on its own. They are not truly testifying; they are *megalei davar be-alma* — merely disclosing a fact. And therefore the concern about partiality does not apply in the same way.

There is, however, a difficulty worth noting. The principle of *giluy milsa be-alma* seems to work symmetrically. Those who are claiming he is a slave are also disclosing a matter of public record — their claim is equally subject to the logic that it will become known. It is not immediately obvious why this logic tilts the scale in favor of those declaring him valid rather than those declaring him disqualified. *Rashi*'s formulation does not fully resolve this asymmetry, and one is left wondering why the doctrine of *giluy milsa* gives greater weight to the validators rather than the challengers. The point that emerges from *Rashi* is perhaps more modest: the concern of *nogeia be-eidus* does not bar their testimony in either direction, since the whole inquiry is not truly *eidus* in the formal sense. Whether that fully resolves the asymmetry is less certain.

A further question was raised: if *giluy milsa* applies to claims about personal status, why did the *gemara* earlier not apply the same logic to witnesses whose signatures appear on a document but who were claimed to be *ketanim* at the time of signing? That too is a question of personal status — how old were they then? — and one might think it is equally a *giluy milsa*. The answer offered is that at the time the document was signed, the question of whether they were of age is a historical question that may no longer be determinable. Now they may be middle-aged; their parents may be gone; the evidence may simply not exist. The *giluy milsa* doctrine presupposes that the matter can indeed be disclosed and investigated. If the facts are no longer recoverable, the doctrine loses its force.

### Thief vs. Repentance

The *gemara* resolves the case differently. *Le-olam eima lach irur de-gazlanusa* — in fact, the challenge is one of *gazlanusa*, theft. The reason the other two are credited is that they are not contradicting the challengers. Rather, they are saying: *yadeinan bei de-avad teshuvah* — we know that he performed *teshuvah*. They concede that he stole, but they add that he has since repented, and therefore his *kashrus* as a *dayan* has been restored.

This opens an important and, sadly, recurring discussion. There are two distinct types of *teshuvah*. One is *teshuvah* between a person and God — an internal process of regret and resolve, which may be fully sincere and which one hopes God will accept. But that is not the *teshuvah* relevant to restoring a person's communal standing or halakhic *kashrus* as a witness or *dayan*. For that, a far more concrete standard applies, as developed at length in *Sanhedrin*: restitution of what was taken, and concrete, observable evidence that the person has genuinely changed. Expressions of regret, or returning to public religious observance, are not sufficient. This distinction has obvious relevance whenever questions arise about whether a figure who has acted improperly may be rehabilitated and trusted with a position of authority.

In the present case, the second two witnesses are not contradicting the first two — they affirm the theft — but they add the critical fact of *aved teshuvah*. The testimonies are therefore not in direct conflict.

### Before vs. After Signing

The overall principle is restated: *ad she-lo chasmu*, before signing, testimony about the third judge's validity may be heard; *mishechasmu*, after signing, it may not, because by then the two signing judges are *nogeia be-eidus* in an unambiguous sense.

## Judge Dies Before Signing

### Declaration of Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הָא מִלְּתָא מֵרַבִּי אַבָּא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, וְאִי לָאו רַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן עַכּוֹ, שְׁכַחְתַּהּ: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ לְקַיֵּים אֶת הַשְּׁטָר וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם — צְרִיכִין לְמִיכְתַּב: ״בְּמוֹתַב תְּלָתָא הֲוֵינָא, וְחַד לֵיתוֹהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: This matter, I heard from Rabbi Abba, and if it was not for Rabbi Abba of Akko, I would have forgotten it.** With regard to a case of **three** judges **who convened** as a tribunal **to ratify a document, and one of them died** before signing the ratification, the surviving judges **must** emend the standard formula of the ratification and **write: We were** convened **in a session of three** judges, **and one** of the judges **is no longer** alive. In that way it is clear that although only two judges signed, the document was ratified by three judges.

Rabbi Zeira introduces the next ruling, noting that he had heard it from Rabbi Abba — and that had Rabbi Abba of Akko not reminded him, he would have forgotten it. The teaching itself echoes the theme we encountered in an earlier *sugya* about a witness who had forgotten testimony and was reminded of what he had always known.

The case is as follows: *gimmel she-yashvu le-kayem es ha-shtar* — three sat together as a *beis din* to validate a document — *u-meis echad meihem* — and one of them died before they signed. The three had already deliberated and reached their conclusion, but only two now remain to sign. The ruling is that they must write explicitly on the document: *be-moshav tlasa havina* — we were a sitting of three — *ve-chad laytohei* — and one of us is no longer present. The echo of *ve-echad einenu* — "and one is not" — the brothers speaking of Joseph — is deliberate and striking. The surviving two must note that the third has died, explaining why only two signatures appear. This is necessary because otherwise observers will assume that only two judges validated the document — which would undermine the requirement that a *kiyum ha-shtar* rest on a *beis din* of three.

### Official Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: וְאִי כְּתַב בֵּיהּ: שְׁטָרָא דְּנַן נְפַק לִקְדָמַנָא בֵּי דִינָא — תּוּ לָא צְרִךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: And if** the judges **wrote in** the ratification: **This document was produced before us, the court;** they **no longer need** to add that they were three judges, as, when unmodified, the term court connotes a tribunal of three judges.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative formulation. If the document contains the phrase *shtar denan nafak le-kadmana bei dina* — "this document was brought before us, the *beis din*" — followed by their signatures, that is sufficient. The statement that they were a *beis din* is understood to imply they were three, and so no explicit mention of the number is required.

### Chatzuf Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא בֵּית דִּין חָצוּף הוּא, וּכְדִשְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁדָּנוּ, דִּינֵיהֶם דִּין, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּקְרָא בֵּית דִּין חָצוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And perhaps it was an impudent court, and** that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as Shmuel said: Two** judges **who** convened a tribunal and **judged, their verdict is a** binding **verdict; however,** because they contravened the rabbinic ordinance mandating that a court be comprised of three judges, **they are called an impudent court.** Therefore, writing: Before us, the court, does not rule out the possibility that the document was ratified by fewer than three judges.

The *gemara* challenges this: perhaps it was a *beis din de-Chatzpa* — a brazen *beis din* of only two? The reference is to Shmuel's ruling that *shnayim she-danu dineihem din* — if two sat in judgment, their ruling stands after the fact — except that they are called a *beis din chatzuf*, an insolent court. So if Shmuel's view is that two can validate in principle, perhaps the term *beis din* on the document does not guarantee three were present. The mere use of the title may not be enough.

The *gemara* responds: the document does not merely say *beis din*; it says *bei dina de-Rabbana Ashi* — the *beis din* of Rav Ashi, the *rav* of Mata Mechasya. That institutional affiliation implies that all proper procedures were observed, and no one would imagine that the official court of Rav Ashi would operate with only two judges.

### Technical Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא רַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב אָשֵׁי כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ! דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״וְאָמַר לַנָא רַבַּנָא אָשֵׁי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And perhaps the Rabbis of the court of Rav Ashi hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel** and they convened an impudent court, whose ruling is binding. The Gemara answers: It is clear, **as it is written in** the ratification: Before us, the court of our teacher Rav Ashi, **and our** teacher **Rav Ashi said to us** how to ratify the document.

But the *gemara* presses further: perhaps even the rabbis of Rav Ashi's court followed Shmuel's view and were comfortable with two? The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the document contains yet a further phrase: *ve-amar lana Rabbana Ashi* — "and Rav Ashi commanded us" to sit as a *beis din*. Rav Ashi himself would never have directed a *beis din* to sit with fewer than three, and therefore this phrase conclusively establishes that three were present.

There is an irony worth noting: the discussion began by saying that if you write *beis din* you need not specify the number three explicitly. By the time it concludes, the required language is quite elaborate — the institutional affiliation, the invocation of Rav Ashi's authority, his specific directive — which, as the *gemara* essentially concedes, amounts to proving three were present in a roundabout way. One might as well have simply written that there were three. But this elaborate language reflects what appears to be the actual technical formulation that was in use in the Geonic courts.

## Peh She'Asar Principle

### Personal Statements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ הָיִיתִי וּגְרוּשָׁה אֲנִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. שֶׁהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר, הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר. וְאִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: ״גְּרוּשָׁה אֲנִי״, אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. אָמְרָה: ״נִשְׁבֵּיתִי, וּטְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. שֶׁהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר, הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר. וְאִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁנִּשְׁבֵּית, וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: ״טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְאִם מִשֶּׁנִּשֵּׂאת בָּאוּ עֵדִים — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who said: I was a married woman and** now **I am a divorcée, she is deemed credible** and permitted to remarry, **as the mouth that prohibited** and established that she was married **is the mouth that permitted,** and established that she is divorced. **However, if there are witnesses that she was a married woman, and she says: I am a divorcée, she is not deemed credible.** Similarly, with regard to a woman who **said: I was taken captive but I am pure,** as I was not raped in captivity, **she is deemed credible** and permitted to marry a priest, **as the mouth that prohibited** and established that she was taken captive **is the mouth that permitted** and established that she was not defiled. **But if there are witnesses that she was taken captive, and she says: I am pure, she is not deemed credible. And if witnesses came after she married, this** woman need **not leave** her husband.

Having completed the discussion of *kiyum shtaros* and the rules of documentary validation, the *gemara* now returns to the broader principle of *peh she-asar* and its intersection with matters of personal status. The *mishnah* presents two cases in which a woman's own statements determine whether she is permitted or forbidden.

### Woman's Marital Status

The first case: *ha-ishah she-amra eshet ish hayiti, u-gerushah ani* — a woman who declared that she was married, and then stated that she is now divorced. She is believed — *ne'emenes* — since *ha-peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir*: the same mouth that made her forbidden to all other men by declaring she was married is the mouth that is now saying she is divorced. However, *ve-im yesh eidim she-haitah eshet ish* — if there are independent witnesses who established that she was married — *ve-hi omeret gerushah ani* — and she merely claims to be divorced — *einah ne'emenes*, she is not believed. Once her married status is independently attested, her own word alone cannot dissolve it.

### Captive Woman Case

The second case involves a woman taken captive. The general presumption is that a captive woman was violated, which would render a *kohen*'s wife forbidden to her husband. Yet if the woman herself says *shiviti ve-tehorah ani* — I was taken captive but was not raped — she is believed, again on the basis of *peh she-asar*: we only know of her captivity through her own words, and therefore her own words are also credited regarding the conditions of that captivity. Again, if there are independent witnesses to the captivity, *einah ne'emenes* — she is no longer believed about the consequences.

The *mishnah* then adds: *ve-im mi-she-nises bau eidim* — if, after she married a second man, witnesses came and established that she had been married previously — *harei zu lo tetzei*, she does not have to divorce her current husband. This is a significant ruling, and one that extends the discussion of *peh she-asar* into territory with real consequences for an established marriage.

One might ask whether the captivity case can properly be governed by *peh she-asar* at all. After all, the *gemara* elsewhere raises the possibility that when there is a strong countervailing presumption — as there is that a captive woman was violated — we might not credit the second part of the statement. *Rabbi Meir*'s position regarding witnesses who claim they were *ketanim* at the time of signing was understood by some as reflecting exactly that: even granting *peh she-asar* in principle, a counter-presumption can override the second half of the statement. But the *mishnah* here does not adopt that stringency for the captive woman case. The distinction, as *Rashi* explains *peh she-asar*, is between a case where the person volunteers the information without any prompting — which is the core of *peh she-asar* — versus *migo*, where a person has a strategic alternative available. *Tosafot* disagrees and maintains that *peh she-asar* can apply even when the person is responding to a challenge, but distinguishes it from *migo* in that there is no concrete piece of evidence — no *shor shachut lefanecha* — that must be explained away. These distinctions matter for determining when a counter-presumption can override the mechanism.

### Source of Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מִנַּיִן לְהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה לְאִשָּׁה״. ״לָאִישׁ״ — אָסְרָה, ״הַזֶּה״ — הִתִּירָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Asi said: From where in the Torah** is the principle: **The mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted,** derived? It is derived **as it is stated: "I gave my daughter to this man [*la'ish hazeh*] as a wife"** (Deuteronomy 22:16). When the father said that he married her off **"to the man [*la'ish*]"** without revealing his identity, **he rendered her forbidden** to all men. When he then says **"this [*hazeh*],"** thereby identifying the man to whom he married her off, **he renders her permitted** to her husband.

After several *daf* of applying *peh she-asar* to a range of cases, the *gemara* now finally raises the foundational question: *minayin le-ha-peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir* — what is the source of this principle? Perhaps particularly in a case of personal status, where the stakes are an *issur* rather than merely a monetary question, we require a stronger basis than mere logic.

Rabbi Assi cites a *pasuk*: *es biti natati la-ish ha-zeh le-isha* — I gave my daughter to this man as a wife. The reading is: *la-ish* — "to a man" — the father creates a prohibition on his daughter vis-à-vis all other men; *ha-zeh* — "this man" — he then specifies who is permitted. So the same mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted.

### Sevara Over Scripture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי קְרָא? סְבָרָא הִיא: הוּא אַסְרַהּ, וְהוּא שָׁרֵי לַהּ! אֶלָּא כִּי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא, לְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לָאָב שֶׁנֶּאֱמָן לֶאֱסוֹר אֶת בִּתּוֹ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ״. ״הַזֶּה״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to derive this from **the verse? It is** based on **logic: He rendered her forbidden and he rendered her permitted. Rather, where this verse is necessary, is** in order **to** derive the *halakha* **that Rav Huna said** that **Rav said, as Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: From where in the Torah** is it derived that **a father is deemed credible to render his daughter forbidden?** It is derived **as it is stated: "I gave my daughter to the man [*la'ish*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:16). The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the subsequent term **"this [*hazeh*]"?**

The *gemara* immediately pushes back: *lamah li kra? sevara hu* — why do you need a *pasuk* for this? It is simple logic. *Hu asar, hu hetir* — he is the one who created the prohibition, he is the one who removes it. If you are going to believe one part of a person's statement, you should believe the whole of it.

There is a point worth noting about the *derashah* itself. In the *pasuk* about *motzi shem ra*, nobody is actually debating whether the daughter was married — that is conceded. The father is there to defend her honor, not to establish the fact of her marriage. Using this *pasuk* to derive that a father's testimony creates a marital bond is a significant inferential leap from the context in which those words appear.

Ultimately, the *gemara* accepts that *peh she-asar* rests on *sevara* — logical reasoning — not on scriptural necessity. Everything that has been discussed for the past several sessions regarding *peh she-asar* is thus grounded in *sevara*, which is in keeping with how it has largely been understood throughout.

### Biblical Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא, לְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לָאָב שֶׁנֶּאֱמָן לֶאֱסוֹר אֶת בִּתּוֹ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ״. הַזֶּה לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי רַבִּי יוֹנָה. דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי יוֹנָה: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה״. ״הַזֶּה״ — וְלֹא לַיָּבָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, where this verse is necessary, is in order to derive the *halakha* that Rav Huna said that Rav said, as Rav Huna said that Rav said: From where in the Torah is it derived that a father is deemed credible to render his daughter forbidden? It is derived as it is stated: "I gave my daughter to the man [*la'ish*]" (Deuteronomy 22:16). The Gemara asks: Why do I need the subsequent term "this [*hazeh*]"? The verse is necessary in order to derive the *halakha* that **Rabbi Yona taught; as Rabbi Yona taught** in a *baraita* that in the verse: **"I gave my daughter to this man,"** written in the context of a husband slandering his wife, **"this"** is written to infer: The *halakhot* in this passage apply to a man who slanders his wife and **not to the *yavam*,** in the case of levirate marriage.

If *peh she-asar* does not require the *pasuk*, the *gemara* asks: *lamah li kra?* What then does the verse teach? *Ki itzterich le-chide-Rav Huna* — it is needed for a separate teaching of Rav Huna. For Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: *minayin le-av she-ne'eman le-esor es bito min ha-Torah* — how do we know from the Torah that a father is believed to establish that he has married off his daughter, such that she would be treated as an *eshet ish* as a result? The answer: *shene'emar, es biti natati la-ish ha-zeh* — as it says, "I gave my daughter to this man." This, the *gemara* holds, establishes that the father's declaration carries legal weight to create her marital status, quite apart from the *peh she-asar* principle itself.

The *gemara* then asks: *ha-zeh lamah li?* — why is the word *hazeh* needed? Now that the *pasuk* is used to establish the father's credibility and not to derive *peh she-asar*, what work does *hazeh* do? The answer comes from what Rabbi Yona teaches in a *baraisa*: *es biti natati la-ish ha-zeh* — to this man, *ve-lo le-yavam* — and not to the brother-in-law. In the context of *motzi shem ra*, if it is the brother-in-law who spreads the false report about the woman's virginity, he is not subject to the laws of *motzi shem ra*, because those laws apply specifically to the husband. *Hazeh* — this specific man — is what distinguishes the husband from others, including the *yavam*.

## Retracting Statements

### Marriage Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה ״אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ אֲנִי״, וְחָזְרָה וְאָמְרָה ״פְּנוּיָה אֲנִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְהָא שַׁוְּויַהּ לְנַפְשַׁהּ חֲתִיכָה דְּאִיסּוּרָא? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּתְנָה אַמַּתְלָא לִדְבָרֶיהָ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אָמְרָה אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ אֲנִי, וְחָזְרָה וְאָמְרָה: פְּנוּיָה אֲנִי — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְאִם נָתְנָה אַמַּתְלָא לִדְבָרֶיהָ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to **the woman who said: I am a married woman, and then said: I am unmarried,** that **she is deemed credible.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't she render herself an entity of prohibition?** When she said that she was married she rendered herself forbidden to all men. How then can she abrogate the prohibition? The Gemara answers that **Rava bar Rav Huna said:** It is referring to a case **where she provided a rationale [*amatla*] for her initial statement** and explained why she said that she was a married woman. **That was also taught** in a *baraita* with regard to **the woman who said: I am a married woman, and then said: I am unmarried,** that **she is not deemed credible. And if she provided a rationale for her initial statement, she is deemed credible.**

The *gemara* now turns from *peh she-asar* to a related but distinct question: can a person retract an earlier statement about personal status if they provide a reasonable explanation? The starting point is a *baraisa*: *ha-ishah she-amra eshet ish ani ve-chazra ve-amra penuyah ani* — a woman who went around saying she was married and then changed her statement and said she is single. In principle, *einah ne'emenes* — she is not believed. But *im nasna amatla le-devareha* — if she provides a reasonable explanation for why she originally said she was married — *ne'emenes* — she is believed.

### Forbidden Status

The *gemara* challenges the very idea that she could be believed to retract. *Ve-shavya le-nafshah chaticha de-issura* — she has made herself a forbidden object. The principle of *shavya le-nafshah chaticha de-issura* holds that when someone makes a declaration that renders themselves forbidden, we accept that declaration as binding against them. We accept statements that restrict a person — we do not accept statements that benefit them — and therefore she would seem to be locked into her earlier declaration that she was married.

### Amtla Explanation

The resolution comes from Rav, as transmitted by Rav Huna: the key is *k'gon she-nasna amatla le-devareha* — when she provides a reasonable explanation for her original claim. A person is believed to retract an earlier statement, even a statement that bound them in terms of personal status, if they can explain coherently why they said what they said initially. This is confirmed by a *baraisa* that restates the same rule and immediately follows it with a concrete historical illustration.

### Woman's Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁהָיְתָה גְּדוֹלָה בְּנוֹי, וְקָפְצוּ עָלֶיהָ בְּנֵי אָדָם לְקַדְּשָׁהּ. וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: ״מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת אֲנִי״. לְיָמִים עָמְדָה וְקִידְּשָׁה אֶת עַצְמָהּ. אָמְרוּ לָהּ חֲכָמִים: מָה רָאִית לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן? אָמְרָה לָהֶם: בַּתְּחִלָּה שֶׁבָּאוּ עַלַי אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינָם מְהוּגָּנִים, אָמַרְתִּי: ״מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת אֲנִי״, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁבָּאוּ עַלַי אֲנָשִׁים מְהוּגָּנִים, עָמַדְתִּי וְקִדַּשְׁתִּי אֶת עַצְמִי. וְזוֹ הֲלָכָה הֶעֱלָה רַב אַחָא שַׂר הַבִּירָה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים בְּאוּשָׁא, וְאָמְרוּ: אִם נָתְנָה אַמַּתְלָא לִדְבָרֶיהָ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And there was also an incident involving an important woman who was outstanding in beauty, and** many **men were clamoring to betroth her. And she said to them: I am** already **betrothed. Sometime later she arose and betrothed herself** to a man. **The Sages said to her: What did you see** that led you **to do so? She said to them: Initially, when unscrupulous people approached me** seeking to marry me, **I said: I am betrothed. Now that scrupulous people approached me, I arose and betrothed myself** to one of them. **And** the Gemara notes: **This *halakha* was raised by Rav Aḥa Sar HaBira before the Sages in Usha,** the seat of the Sanhedrin, **and** the Sages **said: If she provided a rationale for her statement, she is deemed credible.**

The story is told of a woman *gedolah be-noi* — a woman of exceptional beauty — who had suitors constantly pressing her for marriage. She began telling them *mekudeshes ani* — I am married. *Li-yamim* — after some time — *amda ve-kidshah atzmah* — she went ahead and contracted a proper marriage. The *chachamim* asked her: what made you do this — first you said you were married, and now you have married? She replied: *be-tchilah she-ba-u elai anashim she-einam hagunim, amarti mekudeshes ani* — at first, when men who were unsuitable were pressing their suits, I said I was married to deter them. *Achshav she-ba-u elai anashim hagunim, kidashti es atzmi* — now that suitable men came forward, I went ahead and married.

One might wonder why the proper suitors would even approach a woman who had been telling everyone she was married. Perhaps she had moved, or the right suitors came from elsewhere. In any case, the *gemara* does not censure her behavior in the slightest. The *halacha* of *midvar sheker tirchak* applies in the context of *beis din*, not to ordinary interpersonal dealings, and *muter lishanot mipnei ha-shalom* is well established. What matters halakhically is whether her explanation is reasonable, and here it clearly is. She is believed. This ruling was presented by Rav Acha Sar Ha-Bira before the *chachamim* in Usha, who affirmed: *im nasna amatla le-devareha, ne'emenes*.

### Niddah Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל מֵרַב: אָמְרָה ״טְמֵאָה אֲנִי״, וְחָזְרָה וְאָמְרָה ״טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף בְּזוֹ, אִם נָתְנָה אַמַּתְלָא לִדְבָרֶיהָ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. תְּנָא מִינֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעִים זִימְנִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא עֲבַד שְׁמוּאֵל עוֹבָדָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel raised a dilemma before Rav:** If a woman **said** to her husband: **I am ritually impure** as I am menstruating, **and then said: I am pure, what is** the *halakha*? Is she permitted based on her latter statement, or did she render herself an entity of prohibition with her first statement and therefore remains forbidden? Rav **said to him: Even in that** case, **if she provided a rationale for her statement, she is deemed credible.** The Gemara relates: Shmuel **learned** this *halakha* **from him forty times** to ensure that he would not forget it, **and even so,** when confronted with a similar situation, **Shmuel did not** rely on that lenient ruling and did not **take action with regard to himself** and his wife.

Shmuel asked Rav the following: *amra temei-ah ani ve-chazra ve-amra tehorah ani* — a woman told her husband she is a *niddah*, and then she retracted and said she is actually *tehorah*. What is the law? Rav replied: *af be-zo, im nasna amatla le-devareha ne'emenes* — even in this case, if she provides a reasonable explanation, she is believed. Shmuel reportedly asked this forty times and received the same answer forty times.

*Ve-afilu hachi lo avad Shmuel uvda be-nafshei* — and yet, despite this, Shmuel was not prepared to rule on this for himself. *Tosafot* quotes a *Yerushalmi* that gives the backstory: Shmuel sought to be intimate with his wife, who told him she was a *niddah*. The next day she said she was *tehorah*. Shmuel pointed out that one cannot change from *niddah* to *tehorah* in a single day. She explained: *etmol lo havi bi cheila ki ha-hi sha-ata* — yesterday I did not have the strength at that moment. Shmuel then went and asked Rav, who confirmed that with a reasonable explanation she is believed.

This case is a greater *chiddush* than the marriage case in several respects. First, the *amatla* here — exhaustion, not wanting to have relations at that particular time — requires the woman to have essentially fabricated a *niddah* status, which carries significant consequences extending well beyond a single night. Second, the possibility that she genuinely became a *niddah* is real — women sometimes have unexpected breakthrough bleeding — which introduces grounds for suspicion about the retraction rather than the original declaration. Third, this arises in an ongoing marital context, not a one-time social claim, and so its implications play out differently over time. One can understand why Shmuel resisted applying the ruling to himself, even after hearing it confirmed repeatedly.

Some sociologists who have studied the practice of *hilchos niddah* in traditional communities have noted that the laws can function in an empowering way for women, providing a halakhic basis for saying no to a husband at times when social dynamics would otherwise make refusal very difficult. The *sugya* here makes that dimension explicit in a striking way: the *gemara* records, without censure, that a woman may use the declaration of *niddah* status for that purpose and then retract it the next day if she provides a reasonable explanation. The question of what then happens to the credibility of future *niddah* declarations — and whether that is part of why Shmuel resisted — is left implicit but is clearly in the background of the discussion.

## Contradictory Witnesses

### Death Testimony Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״מֵת״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא מֵת״. שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא, וְאִם נִשֵּׂאת — לֹא תֵּצֵא. רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: תֵּצֵא. אָמַר רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי: אֵימָתַי אֲנִי אוֹמֵר תֵּצֵא, בִּזְמַן שֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִשֵּׂאת. אֲבָל נִשֵּׂאת וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים — לֹא תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that if **two** witnesses **say:** The husband **died, and two** witnesses **say: He did not die;** or if **two** witnesses **say:** This woman **was divorced, and two** witnesses **say: She was not divorced, this** woman **may not remarry** as there is no unequivocal testimony that she is unmarried. **And if she remarried,** she need **not leave** her husband. **Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei says:** She must **leave** her husband. And **Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei said: When do I say** that **she must leave** her husband? It is in a case **where witnesses came** to testify that she is still married **and she remarried thereafter. However, if she remarried and the witnesses came thereafter,** she need **not leave** her husband based on the uncertainty created by contradictory witnesses.

The *gemara* now moves to a series of cases involving contradictory testimony about a woman's status — specifically, whether her husband has died or whether she has been divorced — and what the consequences are when witnesses are in conflict. A *baraisa* teaches: *shnayim omrim meis ve-shnayim omrim lo meis* — two witnesses say the husband died and two witnesses say he did not die. *Harei zu lo tinasei* — the woman may not marry another man. With contradictory testimony canceling out, she retains her status as a married woman. *Ve-im nises, lo tetzei* — but if she went ahead and married, she does not have to divorce from the second marriage. This is the position of the *tanna kamma*.

### Divorce Testimony Rule

Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yossi adds a parallel case with a different outcome: *shnayim omrim nitgarshah ve-shnayim omrim lo nitgarshah* — two say she was divorced, two say she was not. *Harei zu lo tinasei* — again, she may not marry. But *ve-im nises, tetzei* — if she married, she must divorce from the second marriage. Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yossi himself then clarifies the timing: when he rules that she must leave, he means specifically when the witnesses came and contradicted each other *before* she married the second man. If she married first and the contradicting witnesses came afterward, *lo tetzei* — even he would agree she need not divorce.

### Marrying Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ, הַבָּא עָלֶיהָ בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי קָאֵי! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְאֶחָד מֵעֵדֶיהָ. הִיא גּוּפַהּ בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי קָיְימָא! בְּאוֹמֶרֶת: ״בָּרִי לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **After all, they are two** witnesses testifying that she is married **and two** witnesses testifying that she is not; how then can the ruling be that after remarrying she need not leave her husband? In that case, **one who engages in intercourse with her stands liable** to bring **a guilt-offering for uncertainty.** For any prohibition whose certain violation renders one liable to bring a sin-offering, its uncertain violation renders one liable to bring a guilt-offering for uncertainty. **Rav Sheshet said:** This is a case **where she married one of her witnesses** who testified that she is unmarried. As far as he is concerned, there is no uncertainty. The Gemara asks: **She herself stands liable** to bring **a guilt-offering for uncertainty,** as she has no independent knowledge whether her husband died. The Gemara answers that this is a case **where she says: It is clear to me** that he died.

The *gemara* asks how the *tanna kamma* can rule that if she married she need not divorce, given that the testimony is neutralized and she retains a *chezkas eshet ish*. Any man who had relations with her under such circumstances would be liable for an *asham talui* — the suspensive guilt-offering brought for doubtful violations of *issur kares* — and yet we are tolerating the marriage after the fact?

Rav Sheshet resolves this: the case is specifically where she married one of the witnesses who testified that the husband had died — *nises le-echad me-eideha*. That witness is saying, in effect: I personally know the husband is dead. From the perspective of the broader community and *beis din*, there is a contradiction and a *safek*. But from his own perspective, there is no *safek*. He knows what he saw.

### Beit Din Authority

This leads to a broader observation about the limits of *beis din* authority. We tend to think of *beis din* as overseeing all halakhic matters — but there is a meaningful distinction here. *Beis din* cannot impose *malkot* in a case of *safek*, and bringing a *korban asham* or *korban chatat* for personal *kapara* is not something *beis din* can compel. If the individual says "I know I am not sinning" and *beis din* cannot demonstrate otherwise — if all *beis din* has is a *safek*, not a *vaday* to the contrary — then the matter is between that person and God. *Beis din* will not force a divorce in such a case. This stands in contrast to other situations — such as where a wife has committed adultery and wishes to remain with her husband — where *beis din* does intervene actively to break up the marriage, because there the *issur* is objectively established.

### One vs. One Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים: ״מֵת״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים: ״לֹא מֵת״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא, וְאִם נִשֵּׂאת — לֹא תֵּצֵא. שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא, וְאִם נִשֵּׂאת — תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that if **two** witnesses **say:** The husband **died, and two** witnesses **say: He did not die, this** woman **may not remarry. And if she remarried** she need **not leave** her husband. If **two** witnesses **say:** This woman **was divorced, and two** witnesses **say: She was not divorced, this** woman **may not remarry. And if she remarried** she must **leave** her husband.

Abaye offers a different resolution of the entire *baraisa*. He says: *targema be-eid echad* — read the case not as two against two but as one against one. One witness says the husband died; one witness says he did not. Now, by the special rule of *Yevamos*, when one witness testifies that a husband died, the *rabbonim* gave that witness the weight of two — *heimnuhu rabbanan ke-vei trei*. This is based on the rule of Rabbi Ulla: whenever the Torah or the rabbis believe one witness, that witness is treated as two. So the one who says the husband died carries the weight of two, while the one who says he did not die carries only the weight of one. *Ein devarav shel echad bi-mekom shenayim* — one cannot override two. So, in the death case, one against one effectively becomes two against one in favor of death, and she is believed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּרְגְּמַהּ בְּעֵד אֶחָד. עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״מֵת״, הֵימְנוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּבֵי תְרֵי, וְכִדְעוּלָּא. דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר ״לֹא מֵת״ — הָוֵה לֵיהּ חַד, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause** of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, where, if she remarried, she need not leave her husband, **and what is the different** in **the second clause,** where, if she remarried, she must leave her husband? **Abaye said: Interpret** Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement **in** a case where each testimony was given by **one witness.** If **one witness says:** The husband **died, the Sages** instituted an ordinance and **accorded him credibility like** that of **two** witnesses in order to enable his wife to remarry. **And** that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ulla, as Ulla said: Wherever the Torah deemed one witness credible,** his legal status **there** is that of **two** witnesses. **And** the legal status of **that** second witness **who says: He did not die, is** that of **one** witness, **and the statement of one** witness has **no** validity **in a place** where there is the testimony of **two** witnesses. Therefore, she need not leave her husband.

If so, asks the *gemara*, why can she not marry *le-chatchila*? If the testimony in favor of death prevails, she should simply be permitted to remarry outright. The *gemara* answers by invoking Rabbi Assi's principle: *haser mimcha ikshus peh ve-lazus sefasayim harchek mimcha* — remove from yourself perversion of speech and keep scandalous rumor far from you. Even if halakhically the testimony resolves in her favor, the appearance of the situation — one witness against another — is such that it will generate *lashon ha-ra* and suspicion. Therefore, *le-chatchila* she should not marry, but *bedi-avad* she need not divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי! מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב אַסִּי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: ״הָסֵר מִמְּךָ עִקְּשׁוּת פֶּה וּלְזוּת שְׂפָתַיִם הַרְחֵק מִמֶּךָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this is a case of the testimony of two witnesses against the testimony of one witness, it should be permitted for her to remarry **even *ab initio*.** The Gemara answers that she may not remarry *ab initio* **due to** the opinion of **Rav Asi, as Rav Asi said** that in any case of uncertainty, the verse **"Remove from you a crooked mouth, and perverse lips put far from you"** (Proverbs 4:24) applies. Although it is not a strictly prohibited action, it is, nevertheless, inappropriate.

In the parallel case of *gerushin*, however — one witness says she was divorced, one says she was not — both witnesses are conceding that she was a married woman. The one who says she was divorced is then just a single witness to the act of divorce, and *ein devarav shel echad bi-mekom shenayim*. There is effectively no valid testimony of divorce. In that case, even *bedi-avad*, if she married, she must divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא, עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״, וְעֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ קָמַסְהֲדִי. וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר נִתְגָּרְשָׁה — הָוֵה לֵיהּ חַד, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** In **the latter clause** of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, where **one witness says:** This woman **was divorced, and one witness says: She was not divorced, both of them are testifying** that she was **a married woman, and that** witness **who says: She was divorced, is one** witness, **and the statement of one** witness has **no** validity **in a place** where there is the testimony of **two** witnesses. Therefore, even if she remarried she must leave her husband.

### Two vs. Two Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ. וְרָאָה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי בְּגֵרוּשִׁין, וְלֹא רָאָה בְּמִיתָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִיתָה — אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה מַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ, גֵּרוּשִׁין — יְכוֹלָה מַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** explained the difference between the two cases in Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement and **said: Actually** it is a case where **they are two** witnesses testifying that she is married **and two** testifying that she is not, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan saw the statement of Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei** as correct **in** the case of **divorce but did not see** it as correct **in** the case of **death. What is the reason** that he distinguishes between the two cases? In the case of **death,** if ultimately her husband returns alive, **she is unable to deny** that **he** is alive. Therefore, the woman's decision to remarry is credible, as if she were not certain that he was dead, she would not have remarried. However, in the case of **divorce,** if her husband returns and claims that he did not divorce her, **she is able to deny his** claim. Therefore, her decision to remarry is suspect, and the Sages penalized her and established that she must leave her husband.

Rava disagrees with Abaye and maintains that the case is indeed two against two — *terei ve-terei ninhu* — in both the death case and the divorce case. The difference in outcome must therefore be explained differently. Rava says that Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yossi was persuaded by the argument that *bedi-avad* she must leave only in the case of *gerushin*, but not in the case of *misah*.

### Death vs. Divorce

The distinction Rava draws is as follows: *misah einah yecholah le-hachchishot* — death cannot be contradicted. If witnesses say the husband died and the woman remarries on that basis, and the husband then appears alive, she cannot simply say "I knew he was alive all along." The risk she took was total and irreversible. A woman therefore takes tremendous care before acting on testimony of death, and if she does act, we may credit that she had genuine certainty. In the death case, therefore, the *tanna kamma* rules that *bedi-avad lo tetzei* — she need not divorce.

But *gerushin yecholah le-hachchishot* — divorce can always be contradicted. If the husband appears and says he never gave a *get*, she could claim he is wrong. The risk is far smaller, and so the inclination to act on possibly insufficient testimony is much greater. We therefore do not extend the same degree of trust to her judgment in the *gerushin* case, and even *bedi-avad*, she must divorce.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חֲצִיפָה כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וְהָאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה לְבַעְלָהּ ״גֵּרַשְׁתַּנִי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. חֲזָקָה אֵין אִשָּׁה מְעִיזָּה פָּנֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי בַּעְלָהּ! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים דְּקָא מְסַיְּיעִי לַהּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים דְּקָא מְסַיְּיעִי לַהּ — מְעִיזָּה וּמְעִיזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** the woman **impudent** to **that extent,** that she would lie in the presence of her husband and claim that he divorced her? **But didn't Rav Hamnuna say** with regard to **a woman who said to her husband: You divorced me, she is deemed credible,** as there is a **presumption that a woman is not insolent in the presence of her husband?** The Gemara answers: **This** statement that she is not insolent **applies only** in a case **where there are no witnesses who are supporting her; however,** in a case **where there are witnesses who are supporting her, she would certainly be insolent.**

The *gemara* asks: but would she really be so *chatzuf* — so brazen — as to falsely claim she was divorced with the husband potentially able to deny it? The answer is provided by Rav Hamnuna's ruling: *ha-ishah she-amerah le-va-alah gerashtani, ne'emenes* — a woman who says to her own husband "you divorced me" is believed. Why? *Chazakah ein ishah me-izah paneha bifnei ba-alah* — a woman would not be so brazen as to say this to her husband's face without it being true. Should this not mean we trust her in the *gerushin* case as well?

The *gemara* responds: *hani mili heicha de-leika sahadei* — that principle applies only where there are no witnesses at all. *Aval heicha de-ika sahadei de-kamsayiah lah* — but where there are witnesses supporting her claim — *me-aza u-me-aza*, she is quite willing to be brazen. The presence of supporting witnesses is itself what emboldens her, removing the deterrent effect that would otherwise make us trust her. Therefore, when there are two witnesses saying she was divorced alongside two saying she was not, the logic of Rav Hamnuna does not apply, and even *bedi-avad* she must divorce.

In sum: the *machlokes* between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yossi, and the further split between *misah* and *gerushin*, resolves as follows. When it is two against two and the testimony concerns death, *bedi-avad* she may remain married, because the very gravity of the situation ensures she acted with sufficient confidence. When the testimony concerns a *get*, even *bedi-avad* she must divorce, because the softness of the risk makes her willingness to act an insufficient guarantee of the truth of her claim.
