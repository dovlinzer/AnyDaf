# Kiddushin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketana Shelo L'Da'as Avi…

### Father Travels Abroad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְהָלַךְ אָבִיהָ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְעָמְדָה וְנִישֵּׂאת, אָמַר רַב: אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה. רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, שֶׁמָּא יָבוֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת זָרָה אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה לְמַפְרֵעַ. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְחַשׁ לַהּ רַב לְהָא דְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** If a minor **became betrothed with her father's consent, and her father left for overseas, and she went ahead and got married** of her own accord in her father's absence, **Rav says:** If she is an Israelite who married a priest, **she may partake of *teruma* until her father comes and protests,** explicitly stating that he does not agree to the marriage. **Rav Asi said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Perhaps her father will come and protest, and it will be found retroactively that a non-priest has partaken of *teruma*.** The Gemara relates: **There was an incident** of this kind, **and Rav was concerned** for this opinion **of Rav Asi** and did not allow a girl in this situation to partake of *teruma*.

The *gemara* on 45b deals with the case of a *ketana* whose *kiddushin* was performed with her father's knowledge — whether the father himself arranged the marriage or whether she entered into it herself with his clear approval. The central question concerns what happens next: *vehalach aviha limedinas hayam* — the father subsequently travels to a distant country — and then *ve'amda venises* — she proceeds directly to *chuppah* without the father's knowledge or consent. Do we presume that since the *kiddushin* was *leda'as aviha*, he would naturally be pleased that the *nisuin* took place whenever it did? Or do we say that the *chuppah* without his explicit approval is problematic?

This raises a deeper conceptual question about the nature of *nisuin* as a *kinyan*. On one hand, one might argue that *chuppah* is less a new act of *kinyan* and more the translation of an already-established legal status into a factual living arrangement, and therefore requires less *da'as* than an entirely new *kinyan*. On the other hand, *nisuin* is precisely the moment when a *ketana* or *na'arah* leaves her father's *reshus* and enters her husband's *reshus*. Even if in the case of an adult woman *nisuin* requires somewhat less formal *da'as*, here one cannot imagine that she can transition out of the father's control without his approval. Several *nafka minas* attend this question: can she eat *terumah* if her husband is a *kohen* — since a wife of a *kohen* may eat *terumah* only once she is a *nesu'ah* — and does her husband inherit her estate if she dies?

### Rav vs. Rav Assi

The *gemara* records the following dispute. *Amar Rav ocheles biterumah* — Rav holds that she is presumed to be a *nesu'ah* and may eat *terumah* if her husband is a *kohen*, and this presumption holds *ad sheyavo aviha veyimcheh* — unless and until the father returns and objects. If the father protests, retroactively she will not have been a *nesu'ah*, at least for so long as she remains a *ketana* or *na'arah* under his authority.

Rav Assi disagrees: *eina ocheles*, she may not eat *terumah*, *shema yavo aviha veyimcheh* — because we are concerned the father may object when he returns, and if he does, it will turn out retroactively that she was a non-*kohen*'s wife eating *terumah*, a *zar* eating *terumah l'mafre'a*. The question is whether we begin from a presumption that the father will likely approve, or whether we begin from a posture of concern.

The *gemara* records that there was an actual incident — *hava uvda v'chash lah Rav l'ha d'Rav Assi* — and Rav was *machmir* in practice like the position of Rav Assi. Some *Rishonim* understand that Rav did not actually rule like Rav Assi; he simply adopted the stringent position as a practical matter, while maintaining that *min hadin* — strictly speaking — she is permitted.

### Okki Mamona B'Chezkas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: וּמוֹדֶה רַב שֶׁאִם מֵתָה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ. אוֹקִי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: And Rav concedes that if** the minor girl **dies,** the husband **does not inherit** from **her,** because of the principle: **Establish money in the possession of its owner.** Since the validity of her marriage is a matter of uncertainty, as the father might protest it, the money remains with her previous inheritors.

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak reports a concession that qualifies Rav's position significantly: *u'modeh Rav she'im mesa eina yorshah* — Rav concedes that if she were to die, her husband does not inherit her. Even though Rav considers her *nesu'ah* enough to permit the eating of *terumah*, the *nisuin* is not sufficiently established to transfer her estate to the husband upon her death. The reason is *oki mamona b'chezkas mareih* — money, or property, remains in the possession of its original owner under conditions of doubt. Since it is uncertain whether there is *nisuin*, we do not remove property from her estate and transfer it to him.

This creates an apparent tension: if Rav concedes enough doubt about the *nisuin* that he will not allow the property to transfer, how does he permit her to eat *terumah* on the basis that she is a *nesu'ah*?

### Terumah vs. Yerusha

The resolution offered by Rashi and Tosafot is grounded in the distinction between *mide'oraisa* and *miderabbanan*. According to the Bavli, from the moment of *kiddushin* the wife of a *kohen* may eat *terumah* by Torah law — she does not need to be a *nesu'ah* for this. The requirement that specifically a *nesu'ah* may eat *terumah* — to the exclusion of an *arusin* — is a *miderabbanan* enactment. Accordingly, both Rav and Rav Assi agree that her status as a *nesu'ah* is a *safek*. When it comes to the Rabbinic question of *terumah*, Rav is willing to be lenient. When it comes to the Biblical matter of *yerusha*, where property can only be transferred with certainty, he is not.

Tosafot adds a further point: one might ask why Rav Assi is concerned about this *miderabbanan* restriction at all, given that the practical rationale behind the Rabbinic enactment — namely that an *arusin* who lives separately might bring *terumah* back to her father's non-*kohen* household — does not apply here, since she is living with her husband. Tosafot answers that the Rabbis made the enactment without drawing internal distinctions — *lo plug* — and therefore even in this case the formal rule applies.

## Father Present, Silent

### Kiddushin Yes, Nisuin No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה לְדַעַת וְנִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וְאָבִיהָ כָּאן, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another dispute between the Sages: If a minor **became betrothed with** her father's **consent, and she married without** his **consent, and her father is here,** i.e., present, **Rav Huna said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may partake** of *teruma*.

The *gemara* now turns to a variation: the *kiddushin* was *leda'as aviha* — her father knew of and approved the *kiddushin* — but the *nisuin* took place *shelo leda'as aviha*, and the father is present, *v'aviha kan*. Presumably the father found out after the fact and said nothing. What is the legal status of his silence?

### Silence: Approval or Obj…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר אוֹכֶלֶת – הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא אִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, הַאי דְּאִישְׁתִּיק – מִירְתָּח רָתַח. רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַב אַסִּי דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, הָתָם הוּא דְּשֶׁמָּא יָבוֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה, אֲבָל הָכָא מִדִּשְׁתֵיק – אִיתְנוֹחֵי אִיתְנְחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **Rav Huna said she may not partake** of *teruma*, **and even according to Rav, who said** that if her father is overseas **she may partake** of *teruma*, this is so only **there,** in the case **where the father is not present. But here, where the father is present,** he does not consent to the marriage; the reason **that he was silent** in **this** situation and refrained from protesting is that he was **so angry** that he did not wish even to speak to her. Conversely, **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may partake** of *teruma*, **and even according to Rav Asi, who said** that **she may not partake** of *teruma*, it is **there** that there is a concern **that perhaps her father will come and protest. But here, from** the fact **that he was silent** the assumption is that **he is comfortable** with the marriage.

Rav Huna says: *eina ocheles* — she may not eat *terumah*. Even accepting Rav's position that when the father is in a distant country we presume he would approve, *hasam hu d'lo isei l'av* — that is only because the father is not present, and in his absence we may assume he will be satisfied when he learns of it. But here, *d'isei l'av* — the father is present. He heard about the *nisuin* and said nothing. *Hai de'ishtik mirtzach ratach* — his silence reflects smoldering anger; a man too upset to speak should not be understood as having acquiesced.

Rav Yirmeya bar Abba disagrees: *ocheles*. Even granting Rav Assi's position that when the father is far away we are concerned he will object — *hasam hu d'shema yavo aviha veyimcheh* — that is because there is an open question whether he will protest. But here, *de'ishtik, isnuche ishnach leh* — he heard and said nothing, which we interpret as acceptance. The principle of *shtika k'hoda'ah* — that silence in the face of a known fact constitutes a form of assent — supports this reading. Normally, we would expect an opposed party to say something.

This leads to a striking analytical observation. The position that makes intuitive sense is Rav Yirmeya bar Abba's: if one begins from a presumption of concern (Rav Assi's approach), silence that follows upon hearing changes the calculus, since a person who genuinely objects would speak up. What is harder to understand is the reverse switch in Rav Huna's reasoning: if one begins from a presumption that the father will be fine with it (Rav's approach), how does the fact that he heard and said nothing make things *worse* than when he was simply absent? The idea that silence signals rage rather than approval seems strained when the baseline presumption is benign.

### Both Acts Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְנִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וְאָבִיהָ כָּאן, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת, רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. אָמַר עוּלָּא: הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא ״כַּחֹמֶץ לַשִּׁנַּיִם וְכֶעָשָׁן לָעֵינָיִם״. הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם דְּקִידּוּשֵׁי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אָמְרַתְּ לָא אָכְלָה, הָכָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another case: If a minor **became betrothed without the consent of her father, and married without** his **consent, and her father is here, Rav Huna said: She may partake** of *teruma*. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Ulla said: This** opinion **of Rav Huna,** that in this case she may partake of *teruma*, is irritating **"as vinegar to the teeth, and as smoke to the eyes"** (Proverbs 10:26), as it contradicts his earlier ruling. **Now** consider: **And what** about **there,** i.e., in a case where her betrothal was with her father's consent, **when** there is at least **betrothal by Torah law, you said she may not partake** of *teruma* in case he did not consent to the marriage. Is it **not all the more so** the case that **here,** i.e., where even the betrothal was performed without her father's consent, she should not be permitted to partake of *teruma*?

The *gemara* now advances to a more extreme scenario: *niskadsha shelo leda'as aviha venisas shelo leda'as aviha v'aviha kan* — both the *kiddushin* and the *nisuin* were performed without the father's knowledge, and the father is present. Again, the assumption is that once he found out, he was silent. Here Rav Huna says *ocheles* — she may eat — while Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says *eina ocheles*. The positions are now reversed from the previous case.

Ulla is sharply critical of Rav Huna's ruling here. He invokes the verse: *k'chometz l'shinayim u'k'ashan l'einayim* — like vinegar to the teeth and smoke to the eyes — to convey how intolerable this position is. The argument is stark: in the *immediately preceding case*, where the *kiddushin* was definitely valid and performed with his knowledge, and only the *nisuin* was done without him — yet Rav Huna there said the father's silence signals objection and she *cannot* eat — how can it be that here, where the father was not even present for the *kiddushin*, his silence now somehow signals approval?

### Nachas B'Ma'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא – הוֹאִיל וְנַעֲשָׂה בָּהּ מַעֲשֵׂה יְתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason of Rav Huna? Since an action was done with her** that renders her like **an orphan in the lifetime of her father,** she is considered married and may partake of *teruma*. The fact that her father ignored both her betrothal and her marriage indicates that he has waived his rights to control whom she marries. She is therefore considered like an orphan, whose marriage is valid with her own consent.

Rava defends Rav Huna with a different rationale. *Mai ta'ama d'Rav Huna? Ho'v'na'aseh b'ma'aseh yatom b'chayei av* — this girl has been conducting herself as though she has no father. Two major events in her life — *kiddushin* and *nisuin* — have occurred without his knowledge or involvement, and he has reacted to neither. In such a pattern of behavior, where the daughter consistently acts autonomously and the father consistently fails to assert his authority, we interpret the cumulative passivity as an implicit relinquishment of control. He has essentially made his peace and decided to let her make her own decisions.

The contrast with the previous case is now coherent: when the *kiddushin* was firmly established under his auspices and authority, and only the *nisuin* was done without him, he has never done anything to signal that he was stepping back from his paternal role. His silence regarding the *nisuin* alone is therefore reasonably read as resistance. But when he allowed *both* acts to occur without reaction, the pattern itself speaks — and we conclude he has, in effect, yielded.

## Father's Direct Response

### Bein Hi U'Vein Aviha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קְטַנָּה שֶׁנִּתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, אָמַר רַב: בֵּין הִיא וּבֵין אָבִיהָ יְכוֹלִין לְעַכֵּב. וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אָבִיהָ וְלֹא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disputed the following issue: In the case of **a minor girl who became betrothed without her father's consent, Rav says: Either she or her father is able to prevent** the betrothal from taking effect. **And Rav Asi says: Her father** can prevent it **but not she,** since she initially agreed.

The *gemara* now returns to the original case: *ketana sheniskadsha shelo leda'as aviha* — a *ketana* who entered into *kiddushin* without her father's knowledge. This was the primary focus of the previous *sugya*, and the conclusion of Rav and Shmuel was that there is a *chashash kiddushin* — one is concerned that *kiddushin* may have taken effect — on the possibility that the father will retroactively approve, *shema yisratzeh ha'av*. What was never clarified there, however, is: what happens when the father actually responds?

Rav now addresses this directly: *bein hi uvein aviha yecholim limchos* — both the daughter and the father can prevent the *kiddushin* from taking effect, or conversely can ratify it. This is, on the surface, somewhat surprising. We already know that if the father objects, there is no concern — that is obvious. The new information is that *she* can also object. But how? She was the one who accepted the *kiddushin*. She is a minor, so what does her objection mean?

Rashi explains that her objection is only effective if it occurs *before* the father finds out. She accepted the money; the father will hear of it tomorrow; but if before he does she retracts, she has cut off the possibility of ratification before it could crystallize. The act of acceptance is undermined by her subsequent retraction before the father's knowledge can validate it.

Tosafot offers a broader reading: she may be able to object even *after* the father approves, on the theory that the father's approval is premised on her also being agreeable. However, Tosafot is vague on the outer limits of this — does she have an indefinite window to object? — and this reading is admittedly difficult, since she was the one who originally accepted the *kiddushin*.

### Shtika vs. Micha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר אוֹכֶלֶת – הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא אִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְאָב, הַאי דְּאִישְׁתִּיק – מִירְתָּח רָתַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **Rav Huna said she may not partake** of *teruma*, **and even according to Rav, who said** that if her father is overseas **she may partake** of *teruma*, this is so only **there,** in the case **where the father is not present. But here, where the father is present,** he does not consent to the marriage; the reason **that he was silent** in **this** situation and refrained from protesting is that he was **so angry** that he did not wish even to speak to her.

Tosafot clarifies the relationship between this ruling and everything that came before. When Rav earlier said there is a *chashash* that the father will approve — *chaishinan shema yisratzeh ha'av* — that ruling was specifically in a case of *stam*, where the father heard and was silent, *sheshama v'shatak*. The entire preceding *sugya* was about how to interpret paternal silence. But where the father explicitly objects — *micha, gila b'da'ato* — he has disclosed his position and the *kiddushin* is certainly void. Similarly, if he explicitly approves, it is certainly valid. The debate about silence is thus the operative center of the preceding discussion, which now becomes clear in retrospect.

### Daughter's Miun

The right of the daughter to object constitutes a form of *miun* — refusal — in the context of a *ketana*. It is a different mechanism than ordinary *miun*, since according to Rav there is at least a *chashash kiddushin d'oraisa*. According to Rav Assi, however, only the father can object — *aviha v'lo hi* — which is the more standard position. The father's authority over his minor daughter's marriage is the default; the daughter's power of objection is the exceptional ruling that requires derivation.

## Seduction & Torah

### Midrash on Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב אַסִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ חִיָּיא בַּר רַב לְרַב אַסִּי: ״אִם מָאֵן יְמָאֵן אָבִיהָ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיהָ, הִיא עַצְמָהּ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם מָאֵן יְמָאֵן״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna raised an objection to Rav Asi, and some say** it was **Ḥiyya bar Rav** who raised an objection **to Rav Asi:** The *baraita* states in the case of a seduced woman: **"If her father refuses** to give her to him, he shall pay money according to the dowry of virgins" (Exodus 22:16). The Sages expounded: **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* that **her father** can refuse to give her to the seducer in marriage; **from where** do I derive that **she herself** can also refuse? **The verse states: "If he refuses [*ma'en yema'en*]."** The repetition of the verb indicates that the right of refusal exists **in any case.** Despite the fact that she was seduced, she can change her mind and say that she does not want to marry him.

Rav Huna (or, in another version, Chiyya bar Rav) presents a challenge to Rav Assi from the Biblical passage dealing with *mefuta* — the case of a man who seduces a *na'arah*. The verse states *im ma'ein yema'ein aviha lesitta lo* — if the father absolutely refuses to give her to him, the seducer must pay fifty *kesef*. The *gemara* asks: from where do we know that *she* can also refuse? The answer derived by *midrash* is from the doubled language of *ma'ein yema'ein* — a doubling that implies both the father's refusal and hers. The underlying assumption of the question is that the Torah case involves a *kiddushin* of sorts — the seduction was *leshum ishus*, for the purpose of marriage, and the act of intimacy constituted *kiddushei bi'ah*, with the father's knowledge now a condition of validity. This is precisely analogous to the cases we have been discussing.

### Pita Shelo L'Shum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ רַב: לָא תֵּיזְלוּ בָּתַר אִיפְּכָא. יָכֵול לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי לְכוּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁפִּיתָּהּ שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת. פִּיתָּהּ שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת קְרָא בָּעֵי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לוֹמַר שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם קְנָס כִּמְפוּתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to those** who tried to offer support for his opinion: **Do not go after the opposite,** i.e., do not cite a proof from a source that could be understood in the opposite manner. Rav Asi **can answer your** argument by claiming that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he** initially **seduced her not for the sake of marriage.** Their initial act of intercourse was not performed in order to effect betrothal, so they are not betrothed. The Gemara questions this: In the case of **a seduction** that is **not for the sake of marriage,** is **a verse required** to teach that she can refuse to marry him? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** It serves **to say that** if she refuses to marry him despite her father's consent to the match, the seducer nevertheless **pays the fine like** a standard case of **a seduced woman.** The obligation to pay the fine is not only for a case where her father refuses to let her marry the seducer.

Rav deflects the challenge: *lo teizil basar ipcha* — do not seek complications. *Yacholni lshinuyei lach lchula* — I can answer you in a way that resolves everything. Specifically: *kegon shepita shelo lshum ishus* — the seduction in the Biblical case was not for the purpose of marriage. The man simply seduced her for the sake of intimacy, not as an act of *kiddushin*. Accordingly, what the Torah is addressing when it says the father can refuse is not refusal of *kiddushin*, but refusal of the proposed marriage after the fact — since of course the father controls whom his daughter may marry, the case tells us the legal consequence of that refusal, namely that the seducer pays fifty *kesef*.

### Maher Yimarena

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּתְנֵינָא: ״מָהֹר יִמְהָרֶנָּה לּוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״ – שֶׁצְּרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ קִידּוּשִׁין. וְאִם פִּיתָּהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת, קִידּוּשִׁין לְמָה לִי? אָמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: צְרִיכָה קִידּוּשִׁין לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to him: If so,** if the *baraita* is discussing a case where intercourse was not performed to effect betrothal, **this is what we learned** in a *baraita* with regard to a seduced woman: **"He shall pay a dowry for her to be his wife"** (Exodus 22:15). This teaches **that she requires betrothal from him. And if** the verse is speaking of one who **seduced her for the sake of marriage, why do I** need **betrothal?** The act of intercourse can serve as the betrothal. Rather, the *baraita* must be referring to a case where he seduced her not for the sake of marriage. **Abaye said to him:** Even if he seduced her for the sake of marriage, **she requires** an additional **betrothal with her father's consent,** since she is a minor and became betrothed without it.

Rav Ashi then interjects: *ei hachi hainu dtanyana, maher yimarena lo l'isha shetricha heimenu kiddushin* — this reading actually makes good sense of the verse that follows, which says *maher yimarena lo l'isha*, meaning he must now marry her. The implication is that he still needs to perform *kiddushin*, which confirms that no *kiddushin* had yet taken place — the seduction was indeed *shelo lshum ishus*. Some *Rishonim* read *ei hachi hainu dtanyana* not as a sharp question but as an expression of approval — "ah, now it fits perfectly" — though others understand it as a genuine challenge.

### Tzricha Kiddushin L'Da'as

The *gemara* then raises the alternative reading: *ve'ei pita lshum ishus kiddushin lama li* — what if the seduction *was* for the purpose of marriage? In that case, the act of intimacy constituted *kiddushei bi'ah*. What then does *maher yimarena lo l'isha* mean? Abaye answers: *tzricha kiddushin leda'as aviha* — the verse teaches that even if the father chooses not to object and allows the marriage to proceed, a new act of *kiddushin* must be performed, this time with the father's knowledge and proper approval from the outset. This is admittedly strange — if the act of seduction already constituted *kiddushin* and the father ratifies it, why is another *kiddushin* required? The answer seems to be that the Torah is insisting on a fully proper, formal process even where the prior act has a degree of legal standing.

### Miun & Knas

The case of the daughter's refusal now requires closer attention. On the reading that the seduction was *shelo lshum ishus*, if the father objects and she does not marry the seducer, he pays fifty *kesef*. But the verse also implies she can refuse. What does her refusal accomplish, given that she is a *ketana* or *na'arah* still under her father's authority?

Tosafot addresses this in two ways. The first answer is that her *miun* — her refusal — does not prevent the marriage from taking place if the father wants it to proceed. *D'keivan d'avia rotzeh she'tehei ishto, eino yachola l'akeiv* — if the father wishes to marry her off, she cannot block it. Rather, what her refusal accomplishes is to trigger the payment of *knas*: just as the father's refusal causes the seducer to pay, so too her refusal, even if the marriage ultimately proceeds, causes the payment. This leads to what seems like an odd outcome: could the father and daughter coordinate — the father consents to the marriage, the daughter refuses, they collect the *knas* and she marries the man anyway?

Tosafot's second and better answer is that her refusal is operative specifically in the case where the father has died before the matter is resolved. *D'hashta mehani miun didah l'akeiv she'lo tehei ishto* — in the absence of a father, her refusal is what prevents the marriage from taking effect. This is more coherent: once there is no father, she is the one in whose hands the decision rests, and her *miun* then has full legal force. The general question that is never fully resolved, however, is what it means to speak of the daughter's right of refusal when a father is present and she is still a *ketana* or *na'arah* — under normal circumstances, she simply has no such right.

## Multiple Objects

### Separate Statements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בִּתְמָרָה זוֹ״, ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בָּזוֹ״ אִם יֵשׁ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ״ אִם יֵשׁ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בְּכוּלָּן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. הָיְתָה אוֹכֶלֶת רִאשׁוֹנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to a woman: Be betrothed to me with this date,** and adds: **Be betrothed to me with that** one, then **if one of** the dates **is worth one *peruta* she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed,** since he mentioned betrothal in connection with each date. But if he said: Be betrothed to me **with this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one, then even **if all of them** together **are worth one *peruta* she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed.** If he gave her dates with the intention of betrothing her with them, and **she was eating** them **one by one** as she received them, **she is not betrothed unless one of them is worth one *peruta*.**

The *gemara* turns to a *Mishnah* dealing with the case of a normal adult woman entering into *kiddushin*. If a man says to a woman *hiskadshi li b'tmara zo, hiskadshi li b'zo* — "be betrothed to me with this date, be betrothed to me with this date" — giving her separate objects with a separate declaration for each, the rule is: *im yesh b'achas meihen shaveh prutah mekudeshes* — if any one of them was individually worth a *perutah*, she is *mekudeshes*; if not, she is not, even if all of them combined would equal a *perutah*. Since each declaration constitutes a separate act of *kiddushin*, the objects cannot be combined.

### Combined Statements

If, however, he said *b'zo u'v'zo u'v'zo* — "with this and this and this" — without repeating the declaration between each object, then *im yesh shaveh prutah b'kulam mekudeshes* — as long as they collectively amount to a *perutah*, she is *mekudeshes*. Since the single declaration covers all the objects together, they combine into one act.

### Eating While Giving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי״ ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי״? אָמַר רַבָּה: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר שְׁבוּעָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that it is only if he says: **Be betrothed to me, be betrothed to me,** in connection with each date that each act of betrothal is considered distinct? **Rabba says: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** in the context of the laws of deposit oaths: He is never liable to bring more than one offering **unless he will state** an expression of **an oath to each and every one.**

A third case: *haysa ocheles rishona rishona* — if she was eating each date as he placed it in front of her, *einah mekudeshes ad she'tehei b'achas meihen shaveh prutah* — she is not *mekudeshes* unless one of the objects is individually worth a *perutah*. The reason for this rule is the focus of the subsequent *gemara*.

The *gemara* first asks who the *tana* is who holds that saying *hiskadshi, hiskadshi* between each object is what breaks the statements into discrete acts. Rabbah answers that it is Rabbi Shimon, who holds the same principle in the context of *shvuos*: if one says "a *shvuah* I do not owe you, and a *shvuah* I do not owe you," those are separate oaths with separate *korbanos* for false swearing. But if one says "a *shvuah* I do not owe you or you or you," that is one oath. The same logic applies here.

### Areisha L'Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לְעוֹלָם אַרֵישָׁא, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא מַנַּחַת, דְּאִי אִיכָּא שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא. אֲבָל אוֹכֶלֶת, הוֹאִיל וּמִיקָּרְבָא הֲנָיָיתַהּ, אֵימָא גָּמְרָה וּמַקְנְיָא נַפְשַׁהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel both said: Actually,** the case of the woman eating the dates one by one **is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, where one betrothed the woman with each date separately, **and it is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** The mishna stated, for stylistic reasons, a *halakha* that it did not need to state, and should be understood as follows: **It is not necessary** to state the *halakha* in the case of a woman who **placed** the dates down, **that if** one of them **is worth one *peruta*, yes,** she is betrothed, and **if not,** she is **not** betrothed. **But** in a case where she was **eating** them, one might think that **since her benefit is immediate, say that she has decided to transfer herself** to him by means of that date, although it is worth less than one *peruta*. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that she is nevertheless not betrothed.

The *gemara* then asks on which clause of the *Mishnah* the eating case applies. If we say it is the first clause — the case of separate *hiskadshi* declarations — the point of mentioning eating is unclear. In that case even without eating, if one of the dates was worth a *perutah*, she would be *mekudeshes*, and if not, not. Why add the detail of eating? Rav and Shmuel both say *le'olam areisha* — it goes on the first clause — and the *Mishnah* employs the rhetorical device of *lo mibaya*: it goes without saying that if the dates are simply lying before her, the rule is that each needs to be worth a *perutah*; the *chidush* is that even when she is eating them eagerly, demonstrating immediate enjoyment — *ho'il u'mekarva hana'atah* — one might have thought that her enthusiasm itself signals acceptance even for less than a *perutah*. The *Mishnah* teaches that this is not so: a *perutah* of objective value is still required. Tosafot notes that this is a relatively weak *chidush*, since the earlier *gemara* made clear that *kiddushin* requires an objectively recognized *perutah*, not a subjective sense of value.

### Aseifa L'Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אַסֵּיפָא, וּמַאי ״עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said** a different explanation: **Actually,** the case of the woman eating the dates one by one **is referring to the latter clause** of the mishna, where he betrothed her by giving her all of the dates. **And what** is the meaning of: **Unless one of them is worth one *peruta*?** It means: **Unless the last** of them **is worth one *peruta*.** But if one of the other dates is worth one *peruta* she is not betrothed, since they are considered a loan until all the dates have been given.

Rabbi Ami offers the more natural reading: *le'olam aseifa* — it goes on the last clause, the combined-statement case. The *Mishnah*'s rule that "one of them must be worth a *perutah*" means specifically the *last* one. Here is the logic: when a man says "with this and this and this," the act of *kiddushin* is not complete until the final object is given. By that time, she has already eaten the preceding dates. Those earlier dates no longer exist as concrete objects; they have been consumed. Consequently, the man cannot "marry her" with those dates in their current non-existent form — to do so would be *mekadesh b'milveh*, marrying with a debt, which is invalid. However, the last date, which has not yet been eaten at the moment the statement concludes and the act is completed, is present before her. If that last date is worth a *perutah*, the *kiddushin* takes effect on the basis of that concrete object.

### Shma Mina Tlat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה, דַּעְתַּהּ אַפְּרוּטָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מָעוֹת בְּעָלְמָא – חוֹזְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Conclude three** conclusions **from** this statement **of Rabbi Ami: Conclude from it** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed,** since she is not betrothed with a date that she has already eaten. **And conclude from it** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **by** forgiving **a loan and** giving her **one *peruta*, her mind** is focused **on the *peruta*** although the debt is far larger, and she will be betrothed. In the latter case, although the value of the dates she ate was more than the value of the last date, which was merely worth one *peruta*, she is nevertheless betrothed. **And conclude from it** that **generally, money** that was given for a betrothal that did not take effect is **returned.** It is not viewed as a gift, but as a loan that must be repaid. This is evident from the fact that all the dates except the last one are considered a loan that must be repaid.

Rava then draws three inferences from Rabbi Ami's explanation. *Shma mina hamekadesh b'milveh einah mekudeshes* — first, it is confirmed that one who performs *kiddushin* using a debt (a *milveh*) accomplishes nothing, since Rabbi Ami does not challenge the premise of the question but rather redirects which date is operative.

*U'shma mina hamekadesh b'milveh u'prutah da'atah a'prutah* — second, if a man says "marry me with the hundred dollars you owe me and with this additional penny," does it work? One might argue that since he framed it as both the debt and the coin, and the debt portion is invalid for *kiddushin*, perhaps the coin does not stand alone either. Rava infers from Rabbi Ami's ruling that in such a case, both he and she are understood to be focusing on the concrete *perutah* before them — *da'atah a'prutah* — and the *milveh* component drops away as legally inoperative, leaving the *perutah* as the effective *kesef kiddushin*.

*Shma mina ma'os be'alma chozrin* — third, the very fact that we describe the earlier consumed dates as a *milveh* implies that money given as *kesef kiddushin* that does not result in valid *kiddushin* must be returned. If the money were simply a *matanah* — a gift — there would be no reason to call it a *milveh* at all.

### Mkvadesh B'Milveh

Tosafot raises a question on this third inference: is Rava not simply reading his assumption back out of his conclusion? The question *u'milveh hi* was posed as a challenge, and now we are deriving from Rabbi Ami's non-response that yes, it is a *milveh*. But what if the money was treated as a *matanah*? Tosafot argues that the distinction is legally significant: if the money given during the sequential date-giving is understood as a *matanah* regardless of whether *kiddushin* ultimately takes effect, then she owns each date the moment she receives it. In that case, there is no conceptual gap between the early dates and the later ones — she already owns them, and there is no "debt" relationship to speak of. Accordingly, the entire combined statement could be viewed as one integrated act, and the collective value might suffice for *kiddushin*. The only way to explain why the *Mishnah* requires one specific date to be worth a *perutah* by itself is through the *milveh* framework: since those earlier dates might need to be returned if *kiddushin* fails, they are conceptually debt rather than concrete present property, and *kiddushin* cannot rest upon them.

## Invalid Kiddushin Cases

### Sister Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֲחוֹתוֹ, רַב אָמַר: מָעוֹת חוֹזְרִים. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מָעוֹת מַתָּנָה. רַב אָמַר: מָעוֹת חוֹזְרִים – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לְשׁוּם פִּקָּדוֹן. וְלֵימָא לַהּ לְשׁוּם פִּקָּדוֹן! סָבַר לָא מְקַבְּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** In the case of **one who betroths his sister** by giving her money, **Rav says: The money** of the betrothal must be **returned** by his sister, as this betrothal does not take effect. **And Shmuel says:** This **money** is considered to be **a gift** that she may keep. The Gemara clarifies their respective opinions. **Rav says: The money** must be **returned,** since **a person knows that betrothal does not take effect with his sister, and he decided to give** the money to her **for the sake of a deposit.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And let him** explicitly **say to her** that he is giving her the money **for the sake of a deposit.** The Gemara answers: **He thought she would not accept** it from him.

The *gemara* now presents a clearer case: *hamekadesh achoto* — a man who "betroths" his sister, declaring *harei at mekudeshes li b'tabaat zu*. This obviously cannot take effect. The question is: does she return the ring?

### Money Returns

Rav says *ma'os chozrin* — the money must be returned. The reasoning is *adam yodea she'ein kiddushin topsin b'achoso, v'gamar v'nasan lshum pikadon* — he knows perfectly well that *kiddushin* cannot take effect with his sister, so his act of giving the ring is to be understood as a deposit, as placing the object in her hands for safekeeping. The *gemara* asks: if that is what he intended, why not simply say so explicitly? The answer is *savar lo mikabla* — he was concerned she would refuse to accept it if framed that way, so the *kiddushin* language was a pretext to get the ring into her hands. Since the underlying intention was a deposit, it must be returned.

### Money as Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר: מָעוֹת מַתָּנָה – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לְשׁוּם מַתָּנָה. וְנֵימָא לַהּ לְשׁוּם מַתָּנָה! סָבַר: כְּסִיפָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel maintains:** The **money** is considered to be **a gift,** because **a person knows that betrothal does not take effect with his sister, and he decided to give** the money to her **for the sake of a gift.** The Gemara again raises a difficulty: **And let him** explicitly **say to her** that he is giving it to her **for the sake of a gift.** The Gemara answers: He **thought it would be embarrassing to her** and she would refuse to accept the money. He therefore attempted to give it to her by an alternative method.

Shmuel disagrees: *ma'os matanah* — the money is a gift. *Adam yodea she'ein kiddushin topsin b'achoso, v'gamar v'nasan lshum matanah* — knowing that no *kiddushin* can take effect, his act of giving must be interpreted as an outright gift. The *gemara* similarly asks: if so, why not say explicitly it is a gift? *Savar ksifa lei milsa* — it would be embarrassing. Framing it as a gift is socially awkward, whereas the *kiddushin* framing allows the ring to pass into her possession without the discomfort of one party explicitly giving the other a gift in this context. The practical effect is that she keeps it.

## Challah & Terumah Cases

### Challah Flour Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבִינָא: הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַלָּתוֹ קֶמַח – אֵינָהּ חַלָּה, וְגָזֵל בְּיַד כֹּהֵן. וְאַמַּאי גָּזֵל בְּיַד כֹּהֵן? נֵימָא: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁים חַלָּה קֶמַח, וְנָתַן לְשֵׁם מַתָּנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raises an objection** from a mishna (*Ḥalla* 2:5): In the case of **one who separates his *ḥalla*,** the portion of dough that must be given to a priest, from **flour,** before it has been made into dough, the portion he has separated **is not *ḥalla*.** Since the Torah states: "Of the first of your dough you shall set apart" (Numbers 15:20), *ḥalla* can be separated only from dough. **And** if the priest fails to return the flour it is considered **stolen property in the priest's possession.** Ravina asks: **And why** is it **stolen property in the priest's possession?** In this case too, **let us say** as Shmuel does: **A person knows that one cannot separate *ḥalla*** from **flour, and he gave** the flour **for the sake of a gift.**

Ravina challenges Shmuel's position with a parallel case: *hamfrish chalato kemach* — one who separates *challah* while the grain is still flour rather than dough. This is invalid; *challah* cannot be separated until the dough stage. *Eino challah* — it does not take effect as *challah*. And yet, if the flour was given to a *kohen*, we say *v'gezel b'yad kohen* — it is theft in the *kohen*'s possession; the *kohen* must return it. But why, according to Shmuel?

### Confusion Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּנָפֵק חוּרְבָּה מִינַּהּ – זִימְנִין דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן פָּחוֹת מֵחָמֵשׁ רְבָעִים קֶמַח, וְהַאי אָלֵישׁ לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְקָסָבַר נִתַּקְּנָה עִיסָּתוֹ, וְאָתֵי לְמֵיכְלַהּ בְּטִיבְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There it is different.** The priest must return the flour **because** otherwise a **ruinous** situation may **emerge from it.** How so? **Sometimes the priest has,** on his own, **less than five-fourths** of a *kav* of **flour,** i.e., he has less than the amount necessitating the separation of *ḥalla*, **and** he also has **this** flour, which gives him a total of more than five-fourths of a *kav*, the amount necessitating the separation of *ḥalla*. **He will knead** all **this** flour **together and** will **think his dough has been made ready** with regard to *ḥalla*, since he did not have enough of his own flour to require the separation of *ḥalla* and he had added flour that had been separated as *ḥalla* to it. **And he will** then **come to eat it in its untithed** state, as the flour he received was not in fact *ḥalla*. Therefore, the Sages required him to return the flour.

The *gemara* applies Shmuel's logic: everyone knows that *challah* cannot be separated from flour, so why not say the person gave it as a *matanah*, and let the *kohen* keep it? The *gemara* answers: *shani hasam d'nafi churbah minei* — this case is different, because allowing the *kohen* to keep the flour would lead to a serious problem. *Zimnin d'is lei l'kohen pachos m'chamesh rivayim kemach* — there may come a time when the *kohen* has slightly less than the minimum quantity of flour obligated in *challah*. He then takes the flour you gave him — which he believes is already *challah* — and mixes it with his own supply. Now the combined quantity exceeds the threshold and is genuinely obligated in *challah*, but he thinks no *challah* need be separated because he believes a portion of the mixture already is *challah*. *V'asi l'meichal b'tevel* — he will eat the bread when it is still *tevel*, since true *challah* was never separated.

The *gemara* then challenges this reasoning: you just said that everyone knows you do not separate *challah* from flour — so why would the *kohen* be confused? The answer distinguishes between knowing the rule and understanding its legal consequence. People know that one is *not supposed to* separate *challah* from flour, and they assume the rationale is *mishum tircha dekohen* — so as not to burden the *kohen* with having to convert flour to dough himself. But they do not know with certainty what happens *bedi'avad* if it was done anyway. They may think it worked after the fact, especially since the *kohen* is *mochel* on his own *tircha*. Therefore, if we allow him to keep the flour without requiring return, people will conclude it really did work, leading to the error described.

### Hydroponic Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיהְוֵי תְּרוּמָה, וְלֹא תֵּאָכֵל עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא עָלֶיהָ חַלָּה מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר. מִי לָא תְּנַן: מִן הַנָּקוּב עַל שֶׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב – תְּרוּמָה, וְלֹא תֵּאָכֵל עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וּמַעֲשֵׂר מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question based on a comparison to a similar case: **But** why must the priest go to the effort of returning the flour to its owner? **Let** the separated flour **be** considered ***teruma*,** i.e., *ḥalla*, **and it should not be eaten** by the priest **until he removes *ḥalla* for it from somewhere else,** in order to make it ready to be eaten. **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Demai* 5:10): If one separated *teruma* **from** produce grown in **a perforated** pot, which is obligated in *terumot* and tithes by Torah law, **for** produce that had grown in **a non-perforated** pot, which is not obligated in *terumot* and tithes according to Torah law; although the separating of *teruma* did not take effect, and the putative *teruma* is still untithed produce, it is considered to be ***teruma*** and remains in the possession of the priest, **and it may not be eaten until he removes *teruma* and tithes for it from somewhere else.** The same *halakha* should apply in the case of the *ḥalla*.

The *gemara* proposes an alternative solution: rather than requiring the *kohen* to return the flour, perhaps declare it simultaneously *challah* and not-*challah* — allow him to keep it but require him to separate *challah* again from another source before eating. A precedent exists for this kind of dual status. The *gemara* cites a case: *min hanakuv al she'eino nakuv* — one takes *terumah* from produce grown in a pot with a hole (which is biblically obligated) on behalf of produce grown in a pot without a hole (hydroponic produce, which is not fully biblically obligated). The *terumah* separated in this way has an ambiguous status. The ruling is: *terumah* — it is treated with the stringency of *terumah* — *velo tochal ad sheyotzi alei terumah uma'aser mimmakom acher* — but it cannot be eaten until additional *terumah* and *ma'aser* are separated from another source. The *kohen* keeps the produce but must separate again. Why not do the same with the flour?

### Bitrei Mani vs. Echad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּתְרֵי מָנֵי תְּצָאֵית, בְּחַד מָנָא לָא תְּצָאֵית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to things that are **in two** separate **containers,** the priest **will listen.** Since the priest is well aware of the difference between a perforated and a non-perforated pot, he will accept the ruling of the Sages to separate *teruma* an additional time. With regard to something that is **in one container, he will not listen.** He does not see any difference between receiving flour or dough, and he will not accept the ruling of the Sages to separate *ḥalla* an additional time. Consequently, they required him to return the flour.

The *gemara* answers with a distinction of considerable practical subtlety. *Bitrei mani tzyat, b'chad mana lo tzyat* — with two vessels they will understand; with one vessel they will not. The hydroponics case involves produce from two conceptually distinct categories — pots with holes and pots without holes are recognizably different situations. When told that the *kohen* can keep the produce but must separate again, the recipient intuitively grasps that there is something unusual about the provenance of this particular produce, and the dual treatment makes sense to him.

### Return vs. Re-Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁים חַלָּה קֶמַח! יוֹדֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ. יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁין חַלָּה קֶמַח, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ, דְּסָבַר טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא דְכֹהֵן, טִירְחָא דְכֹהֵן קַבְּלַהּ עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** that **a person knows that one may not separate *ḥalla*** from **flour?** The Gemara answers: **He knows** the *halakha* **but he does not know** the reason for the *halakha*. **He knows that one cannot separate *ḥalla*** from **flour, but he does not know** the reason, **as he thinks: What** is **the reason** one may not separate *ḥalla* from flour? It is **due to the labor of the priest.** And as far as **the labor of the priest** goes, he **has accepted** that task upon **himself.**

But in the flour case, everything is *chad mana* — one vessel, one undifferentiated mass. If we tell the *kohen* he can keep the flour but must separate *challah* again, he will not understand why. As far as he can see, the flour he holds is *challah*, full stop — and the instruction to separate again will strike him as incomprehensible. He will not internalize the message that the original separation was invalid; he will simply think the requirement to re-separate is an inexplicable imposition. Under these conditions, the confusion is not adequately addressed by requiring re-separation. The only solution that genuinely communicates the invalidity of the original act is insisting on return. Therefore, in the flour case, the *kohen* must return the flour; in the hydroponic case, he may keep the produce but must separate again.
