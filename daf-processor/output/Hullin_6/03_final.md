# Chullin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Samaritan Shechita & Ido…

### Dovecote Idol at Har Ger…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי טַעְמָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן? כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, שַׁדְּרֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְאֵתוֹיֵי חַמְרָא מִבֵּי כוּתָאֵי, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ סַכִּין בְּלֹעֶךָ אִם בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ אָתָּה״, הָלַךְ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר וְסִפֵּר דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְגָזַר עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **the Sages,** Rabban Gamliel and his court, **issued a decree** rendering it prohibited to eat from the slaughter of Samaritans? The Gemara answers: It is **like that** case **involving Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** in which **Rabbi Meir dispatched him to bring wine from the area of the Samaritans. A certain elder found him** and **said to him: "And put a knife to your throat, if you are a man given to appetite"** (Proverbs 23:2),as a warning to distance himself from them and not to drink their wine, because they were not reliable. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar went and related** those **matters before Rabbi Meir, and** Rabbi Meir **issued a decree against them.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דְּמוּת יוֹנָה מָצְאוּ לָהֶן בְּרֹאשׁ הַר גְּרִיזִים, שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹבְדִין אוֹתָהּ. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא, וְגָזַר רוּבָּא אַטּוּ מִיעוּטָא. וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית דִּינוֹ נָמֵי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that the Samaritans are deemed unreliable? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: At the peak of Mount Gerizim they found the image of a dove, which** the Samaritan residents of Mount Gerizim **would worship; and Rabbi Meir** issued the decree **according to his** line of **reasoning that he takes the minority into consideration, and** therefore, despite the fact that the majority of Samaritans did not live on Mount Gerizim, **he issued a decree** rendering meat slaughtered by **the majority** forbidden **due to the minority** that worshipped that idol. **And Rabban Gamliel and his court also hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

We begin on 6a, four lines from the top, with the question of the shechita of a Kuti. By the time of Rabban Gamliel, such shechita had been forbidden even when performed under direct observation, and the Gemara now seeks to understand why.

The Gemara asks: u'mai ta'ama gazru bo rabbanan — what was the reason the Rabbis issued this ruling against Kuti shechita? The answer is introduced through the following episode. Rabbi Meir sent Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — shadreih Rabbi Meir *le'asuyei chamra mi'beit kuta'ei* — to bring wine from the house of Kutim, either because wine had been stored there or for purposes of purchase. While on this errand, a certain elder found him — ashkecheih hahu saba. Tosafot notes that when the Talmud refers anonymously to "a certain elder" in this manner, it may well be a reference to Eliyahu HaNavi. In any event, the elder is someone whose words carry great weight. The elder quoted the pasuk: vesamta sakin beloecha im *ba'al nefesh* ata — "you should put a knife in your throat if you are a person of soul" — effectively warning Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar that he should not be drinking this wine.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar then returned and told Rabbi Meir what had transpired — ve'siper devarim lifnei Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir heard the account and, presumably after some investigation, issued an edict — *vegazar leih* — forbidding the wine of Kutim. The logic here is important: the prohibition was not grounded in any technical deficiency in how the Kutim handled the wine. Rather, the ruling treated them as non-Jews. And once their wine is forbidden on those grounds, it becomes clear why their shechita is likewise invalid: even if technically performed and directly observed, we are treating Kutim as non-Jews, and that categorically excludes them — as was established in yesterday's daf — from performing valid shechita.

### Rabbi Meir's Minority Co…

The Gemara continues to press the explanation. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak provides the factual basis: demut yona matzu lahen berosh Har Grizim — they found a form, some type of statue of a dove, at the summit of Har Gerizim, the Samaritans' holy mountain — she'hayu ovdin otah — and they worshipped it. The Samaritans, in other words, had become idolaters.

The Gemara then adds: Rabbi Meir letamei — Rabbi Meir acts in accordance with his characteristic reasoning — dechayesh lemi'uta — he is concerned even for minority cases. This principle appears elsewhere in Rabbi Meir's rulings. One classic example involves yibbum: if the deceased husband's brother seeks to perform yibbum with a minor widow, Rabbi Meir objects on the grounds that she might eventually turn out to be sterile, in which case she would not be subject to yibbum, and the marriage would constitute a violation of the prohibition of *eishet achiv*. Even though only a small minority of women are sterile, Rabbi Meir requires that we account for the possibility.

Applying this same principle to the Samaritans: even if only a subset of them — say, those living near Har Gerizim — worshipped the idol, Rabbi Meir concluded that we must be concerned about all of them. Hence: vegazar ruba atu mi'uta — he extended the prohibition to the majority on account of the minority. All Samaritans were to be treated as suspected idolaters. It bears noting, however, that this does not necessarily mean Rabbi Meir considered them non-Jews. As was discussed yesterday, there is an intermediate category — a Jewish idolater — who is treated as a non-Jew for purposes of halacha without literally being assigned the status of a non-Jew. So what Rabbi Meir may have been saying is that Samaritans are Jewish idolaters, and on that basis their wine is forbidden.

### Rabban Gamliel's Extensi…

Rabban Gamliel u'beit dino — k'Rabbi Meir svira lehu — agreed with Rabbi Meir's underlying reasoning and extended it to shechita as well. This extension represents a genuine progression in the logic. The prohibition on wine is specifically linked to idolatry concerns — yayin nesech flows directly from the worry that wine was used in idolatrous worship. But Rabban Gamliel then reasoned: if we are already treating Kutim as idolaters, that designation is not confined to laws relating directly to idolatry. Rather, as we saw yesterday, being classified as an idolater constitutes a categorical exclusion across the board — including shechita, which has nothing to do with idolatry per se. So the stages are: they are suspected idolaters, therefore their wine is forbidden; and because they are categorically excluded as idolaters, their shechita is likewise invalid.

Importantly, as we have already seen, this ruling was not immediately or universally accepted. There were Amoraim who resisted it, likely because the idolatrous practice was demonstrably limited to a subset of the Samaritan population and did not apply to all Kutim equally.

## Knife in Your Mouth

### Verse Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשָׁטֵיהּ דִּקְרָא בְּמַאי כְּתִיב? בְּתַלְמִיד הַיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: ״כִּי תֵשֵׁב לִלְחוֹם אֶת מוֹשֵׁל בִּין תָּבִין אֶת אֲשֶׁר לְפָנֶיךָ וְשַׂמְתָּ סַכִּין בְּלֹעֶךָ אִם בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ אָתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: As to **the plain** meaning **of** that **verse:** "And put a knife to your throat, if you are a man given to appetite," **with regard to what** matter is **it written?** The Gemara answers: It is written **with regard to a student who is sitting before his teacher,** as he must consider his words carefully. **As Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** a *baraita* interpreting the verses: **"When you sit to eat with a ruler, consider well [*bin tavin*] him that is before you; and put a knife to your throat, if you are a man given to appetite"** (Proverbs 23:1–2).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יוֹדֵעַ תַּלְמִיד בְּרַבּוֹ שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לְהַחֲזִיר לוֹ טַעַם – ״בִּין״, וְאִם לָאו – ״תָּבִין אֶת אֲשֶׁר לְפָנֶיךָ וְשַׂמְתָּ סַכִּין בְּלֹעֶךָ״, ״אִם בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ אָתָּה״ – פְּרוֹשׁ הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* explains the verse: **If a student knows about his teacher that he knows to respond to him** with **a reasoned** answer, **seek wisdom [*bin*]** from him. **And if** the student believes that the teacher is **not** capable of doing so, **understand [*tavin*] who is sitting before you, and put a knife to your throat** and refrain from embarrassing him with questions that he cannot answer. And **if you are a man given to appetite** and you seek an answer to your question, **distance yourself from him.**

Having cited the pasuk vesamta sakin beloecha im *ba'al nefesh* ata in the context of warning against Kuti wine, the Gemara pauses to ask parenthetically: *pashte dikra bemai* ktiv? — to what does the simple sense of that verse actually refer? Because in the story above it was deployed as a warning against drinking problematic wine, but surely that is not its original context.

The Gemara answers: betalmid hayoshev lifnei rabbo — the verse speaks of a student sitting before his teacher. A Baraita taught by Rebbi Chiya elaborates on the full pasuk: ki teshev lachom et moshel — when you sit down to contend with a ruler — bin tavin et asher lefanecha — understand what is before you — vesamta sakin beloecha im *ba'al nefesh* ata — and put a knife in your throat if you are a person of soul.

The Gemara interprets: im yodea talmid birabbo *sheyodea lehachzir lo ta'am* — if a student knows that his teacher is capable of answering his questions and offering him substantive responses — bin — then he should go ahead and ask. *ve'im lav* — but if he knows his teacher cannot adequately answer him — tavin et asher lefanecha — he should understand his situation and refrain from asking questions that will only frustrate him and embarrass the teacher. Vesamta sakin beloecha — put a knife in your throat — meaning, hold your tongue and do not ask. Im *ba'al nefesh* ata — if you are a person of real intellectual drive and desire to learn — *perosh mimenu* — then leave him and find another teacher. But do not subject the teacher to questions he cannot answer.

The real plain meaning of the verse, by contrast, is a more general counsel about knowing your audience: if you are going to dispute with a person of power, exercise discretion, because the stakes are high.

### Pshat Terminology

This discussion presents an opportunity to clarify a point about terminology. The Gemara uses the phrase pashte dikra — the pshat of the verse. We commonly translate pshat as the "simple sense" of the verse, but that is not actually what the word means. *pshat means* the official or accepted interpretation of the verse. This distinction is illuminated by the Talmudic phrase *pshuto kemashmao* — the pshat is what it sounds like. If pshat already meant "the simple sense," then *pshuto kemashmao* would be tautological. Rather, pshat denotes the authoritative reading, and mashmao — what it sounds like — is a separate claim being made about that reading. So when the Gemara here asks *pashte dikra bemai* ktiv, it is asking: what is our accepted way of understanding this verse? — not merely what the words superficially seem to mean.

## Later Acceptance of Ban

### Rabbi Isaac's Wine Incid…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן יוֹסֵף שַׁדְּרֵיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לְאֵתוֹיֵי חַמְרָא מִבֵּי כוּתָאֵי, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵית כָּאן שׁוֹמְרֵי תוֹרָה. הָלַךְ רַבִּי יִצְחָק וְסִפֵּר דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, וְהָלַךְ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְסִפֵּר דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, וְלֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאוּם גּוֹיִם גְּמוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu dispatched Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Yosef to bring wine from the area of the Samaritans. A certain elder found him** and **said to him:** The people **here** are **not keepers of the Torah. Rabbi Yitzḥak went and related the matters before Rabbi Abbahu, and Rabbi Abbahu went and related the matters before Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi, and they did not move from there until they rendered** the Samaritans **full-fledged gentiles.**

We now return to the question of Kuti wine and shechita, because as we have seen, not everyone was prepared to accept the new status assigned to the Kutim. A second incident is now recorded that is strikingly parallel to the first. Rebbe Yitzchak ben Yosef was sent by Rebbe Abbahu — shadrei Rebbe Abbahu la'asuyei chamra mibei kusai — to bring wine from the Kutim. Again, an elder found him: ashkachay hahu saba — and said: *leis kan shomrei Torah* — it seems that people here are not keeping Torah. The reference was presumably not to the Kutim themselves but to those who were going to purchase wine from them, people who, if they truly cared about halacha, should not have been doing so.

Rebbe Yitzchak did not complete the errand. He returned — azal Rebbe Yitzchak vesiper devarim lifnei Rebbe Abbahu — and reported what the elder had told him. The episode then cascaded upward: ve'azal Rebbe Abbahu vesiper devarim lifnei Rebbe Ami — Rebbe Abbahu went and told Rebbe Ami. The fact that the account of one elder's remark set off such a significant chain of consultation is itself notable, which is presumably why Tosafot entertains the possibility that this "elder," like the one in the earlier story, may have been Eliyahu HaNavi.

### Ami & Assi's Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִי לִשְׁחִיטָה וְיֵין נֶסֶךְ, מֵהָתָם גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן! אִינְהוּ גְּזוּר וְלָא קַבִּלוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ, אֲתוֹ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי גְּזַרוּ וְקַבִּלוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** matters did those Sages render them full-fledged gentiles? **If it was to** prohibit eating from their **slaughter and** to render their wine as **wine** used for **a libation** in idol worship, these prohibitions were issued previously. **From there,** from the generations of Rabbi Meir and Rabban Gamliel, **the Sages issued a decree** prohibiting **them.** The Gemara answers: **They issued a decree, and** the people **did not accept** it **from them. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi came** and **issued a decree, and** the people **accepted** it **from them.**

The consultation ultimately reached Rebbe Ami and Rebbe Assi. V'lo zazu misham — they did not move from that place — ad she'asa'um ovdei kochavim gemurim — until they had declared Kutim to be full-fledged idolaters, with all the halachic consequences that entails: their wine, their shechita, everything.

The Gemara then asks: lemei — for what halacha precisely did they need to make this declaration? Ilima shechita v'yein nesech — if it is about their slaughter and their wine — that had already been addressed by Rabbi Meir and Rabban Gamliel. The Gemara's answer: inhu gazru v'lo kiblu minayhu — they made the ruling but it was not accepted by their generation. Atu Rabbi Ami v'Rabbi Assi — Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi came along — gazru v'kiblu minayhu — and when they reiterated the ruling, it was finally accepted. So this was not a more expansive ruling but rather a reaffirmation of an existing one, now successfully implemented.

### Rashi on Habit & Change

This raises an obvious question: why was the ruling of Rabbi Meir and Rabban Gamliel — authorities far greater than Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi — not accepted in their own time, while Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi succeeded? Rashi addresses this directly with a fascinating sociological observation. Lo she'elu gedolim mehem — it is not that Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi were greater authorities — ela shebdoro shel Meir lo ratzu lekabel mehem lifrosh min haKutim lefi shehayu regilim bahem: in the generation of Rabbi Meir, people were already deeply habituated to trusting Kutim and treating them with full Jewish status. They were not prepared to reverse their well-entrenched behaviors and attitudes.

By the time of Rabbi Ami, says Rashi — ubdoro shel Rabbi Ami hayah efshar lifrosh — it had become possible to enforce the separation. People were increasingly aware that Kutim could not be trusted and were beginning to view them differently. It was therefore finally possible for the ruling to take hold. The lesson here is a striking one about the relationship between correct halachic rulings and the social conditions required for their acceptance: even if a ruling is halachically sound, its implementation may have to await a moment when the community is psychologically and culturally ready to receive it.

### Gerizim Temple Context

Steinsaltz offers a complementary historical perspective. Apparently, a temple was actually built on Har Gerizim in the wake of the Bar Kokhba rebellion, representing a concrete and visible intensification of Samaritan idolatrous practice. This may well be what stands behind the Gemara's account of discovering the dovecote idol — the Dositheana's worship at Har Gerizim may be connected to this actual construction. The timing aligns with the generation of Rabbi Meir, explaining why the campaign against Kuti wine and shechita began precisely then. As for why acceptance awaited the generation of Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi, Rashi's sociological explanation remains the more compelling answer — though the historical confirmation of actual idolatrous worship on Har Gerizim lends the initial ruling its firm empirical footing.

## Idolaters vs. Non-Jews

### Status Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי גּוֹיִם גְּמוּרִין? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת וְלִיתֵּן רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **full-fledged gentiles? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It means that the halakhic status of a Samaritan is like that of a gentile with regard **to renouncing** his **domain** in a jointly-owned courtyard on Shabbat **and to transferring** his **domain** in the courtyard to residents of that courtyard.

The Gemara declares that Kutim were made ke-ovdei kochavim gemurim — like full-fledged ovdei kochavim. The phrase ovdei kochavim, however, is ambiguous: it can mean either non-Jews or idolaters. Given that up to this point the assumption has been that Kutim were geirei emet — genuine converts — the most coherent reading would be that they are now classified as Jewish idolaters. A Jew who is oved avodah zarah is categorically excluded from performing shechita and his wine is forbidden, as was established yesterday. This accounts for all the practical consequences without requiring us to strip them of Jewish status entirely.

### Rambam on Tumah

There is, however, a striking indication in a Rambam in one location in the Peirush HaMishnayot that the matter may go further. There is a Mishnah in Niddah that deals with the bloodstains of Kuti women — the stains of niddah, menstrual bleeding. The Rambam there rules that such stains do not convey tumah, because *chazal made* Kutim like ovdei kochavim. This is remarkable on several levels.

The laws of niddah tumah apply to Jewish women, not to non-Jewish women — but they do apply even to a Jewish woman who is an idolater. The exclusion entailed by being classified as an idolater pertains to functional matters like shechita and wine; it does not reassign a person's fundamental halachic identity for purposes of tumah. So when the Rambam says their bloodstains do not convey tumah, he appears to be treating Kutim not merely as Jewish idolaters but as actual non-Jews.

What makes this even more striking is the direction of the ruling: here the Rambam is being meikel — lenient. In every other context, treating Kutim as ovdei kochavim was a stringency: don't drink their wine, don't eat their meat. But to say that their niddah bloodstains are tahor because they are not Jewish — that is a leniency. It is genuinely difficult to understand how, if they were truly Jewish, their status could be so comprehensively transformed. The Rambam's ruling raises the serious question of whether the Rabbinic declaration meant that Kutim were treated as idolaters or actually reclassified as non-Jews — and the Rambam's position in Niddah seems to point toward the latter.

## Eruv & Reshus

### Eruv Mechanics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי גּוֹיִם גְּמוּרִין? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת וְלִיתֵּן רְשׁוּת. וּכְדִתְנַיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד מְשַׁמֵּר שַׁבַּתּוֹ בַּשּׁוּק, מְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת וְנוֹתֵן רְשׁוּת, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּר שַׁבַּתּוֹ בַּשּׁוּק, אֵינוֹ מְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת וְנוֹתֵן רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **full-fledged gentiles? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It means that the halakhic status of a Samaritan is like that of a gentile with regard **to renouncing** his **domain** in a jointly-owned courtyard on Shabbat **and to transferring** his **domain** in the courtyard to residents of that courtyard. **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a Jewish transgressor** who nevertheless **observes his Shabbat in the marketplace,** i.e., in public, if he failed to establish a joining of houses in a courtyard before Shabbat, his halakhic status is that of an observant Jew, and he may **renounce** his **domain** in the courtyard **and transfer** his **domain** in the courtyard. **But** a transgressor **who does not observe his Shabbat in the marketplace** may **neither renounce** his **domain** in the courtyard **nor transfer** his **domain** in the courtyard.

Having established that Kutim are treated as full ovdei kochavim, the Gemara asks: mai ovdei kochavim gemurim — in what halachic area does this designation matter beyond shechita and wine, which were already addressed earlier? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers: l'vattel reshus u'litten reshus — with respect to annulling and giving one's domain in the context of eruv.

Some clarification of the mechanics is helpful here. What is commonly called an eruv — the string and posts surrounding a neighborhood — is technically a tzurat hapesach, a virtual doorway that creates a notional enclosure. The actual eruv, in the strict sense, refers to eruv chatzerot: the joining of separate domestic domains. Because each household owns its own property, the space within the enclosure is subject to *chiluk reshuyot* — a division of domains — which prevents it from functioning as a single private domain. To address this, residents join together in a communal share of food, typically a box of matzot held in one household, establishing a fictive joint ownership. Since everyone now has a share in that one household, the entire space is treated as a single unified domain.

### Annulling vs. Renting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּת וּמְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת, וּבְגוֹי עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** This is **because** the Sages **said:** Only **a Jew** may verbally **transfer** rights in his **domain or renounce** his rights in his **domain,** but **with regard to a gentile,** the other residents cannot establish a joining of courtyards **unless** the residents of the courtyard **lease** his domain from him. The halakhic status of one who publicly desecrates Shabbat is that of a gentile.

What happens if someone forgets to participate in the communal box of matzot and Shabbos arrives? If the person is a Jew, the solution is straightforward: he can verbally declare that he is annulling his domain — reshusi mevutela lach — or transferring it to others. *ve'ein tzarich lizkos* — no formal act of acquisition is required; the verbal declaration alone suffices, and the eruv is thereby preserved.

For a non-Jew, this mechanism is unavailable. A non-Jew is not part of the collective framework that makes this verbal fiction operative. Accordingly, if there is a non-Jew in the shared space, one cannot simply have him declare his domain annulled. Instead, his domain must actually be rented from him — a real transaction, not merely a verbal declaration — in order to incorporate his space into the shared domain.

### Samaritans in Eruv

The practical question this raises for the modern eruv — how to address non-Jewish-owned property within the enclosure, whether through municipal agreements, the doctrine of eminent domain, or other mechanisms — is a separate and complex matter. The relevant principle established here is that a Kuti, now that he has been declared ke-ovdei kochavim gemurim, falls into the same category as a non-Jew for purposes of eruv. His verbal annulment of reshus is ineffective, and his domain must actually be rented from him. Notice, however, that this does not technically require that he be a non-Jew — only that he be treated like one. The same rule applies, as the Baraita will demonstrate, to a Jewish public violator of Shabbos. It is a matter of halachic status, not necessarily of ethnic or religious identity.

## Baraita: Mumar & Shabbat

### Mumar Respecting Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״רְשׁוּתִי קְנוּיָה לָךְ״ ״רְשׁוּתִי מְבוּטֶּלֶת לָךְ״ – קָנָה, וְאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִזְכּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does a Jew transfer or renounce his domain? If a Jew **says to** his neighbor: **My domain is transferred to you** or **my domain is renounced to you,** his neighbor **has acquired** his domain, **and it is not necessary** for him **to grant** it to his neighbor by means of one of the standard modes of acquisition.

The Baraita cited earlier in the Sefaria indicates the case of a non-observant Jew who keeps Shabbos in public — Yisrael mumar *meshameir Shabatto bashuk* — such a person retains full Jewish halachic status. Accordingly: *mevattel reshus v'notein reshus* — he can annul his domain or give it, and his verbal declaration on Shabbos is effective for eruv purposes.

### Public Shabbat Violator

She'eino *meshameir Shabatto bashuk* — one who does not keep Shabbos in public, meaning a mechalel Shabbos b'farhesia, a public *shabbos violator* — eino *mevattel reshus v'notein reshus*. His verbal annulment is ineffective. To incorporate his space into an eruv, his domain must be rented from him, exactly as one would have to rent from a non-Jew.

### Jewish Verbal Annulment

The Baraita articulates the general principle that Yisrael notein reshus u'mevattel reshus — a Jew who forgot to participate in the eruv can verbally give or annul his domain on Shabbos itself. Reshusi knuya lach, reshusi mevutela lach — my domain is acquired by you, my domain is nullified — and that verbal act alone is sufficient; v'ein tzarich lizkos, no physical act of acquisition is needed.

The conclusion that follows is that not only a non-Jew, but also a public *shabbos violator* — and now, by extension, a Kuti — requires actual rental rather than verbal annulment. The eruv must treat the Kuti's domain as it would treat that of a non-Jew or a mechalel Shabbos b'farhesia.

## Inn Incident & Tzadikim

### Eggs & Wine Mixture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַב אַסִּי אִיקְּלַעוּ לְפוּנְדְּקָא דְּיָאֵי, אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ בֵּיצִים הַמְצוּמָּקוֹת בְּיַיִן. רַבִּי זֵירָא לָא אֲכַל, וְרַב אַסִּי אֲכַל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַב אַסִּי: וְלָא חָיֵישׁ מָר לְתַעֲרוֹבֶת דְּמַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו אַדַּעְתַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara revisits the statement that the righteous would not experience mishaps. **Rabbi Zeira and Rav Asi happened** to come **to the inn of** the town of **Ya'ei.** In the inn, **they brought before** these Sages **eggs that shriveled** after being cooked **in wine. Rabbi Zeira did not eat** the eggs, **and Rav Asi ate** them. **Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Asi: And is the Master not concerned about** the possibility that the dish is **a mixture** containing wine that is **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*]?** Rav Asi **said to him:** It did **not** enter **my mind.**

The Gemara now shifts to a different narrative, returning to the theme — which appeared earlier in the tractate — of whether God allows tzadikim to accidentally eat forbidden food. Rabbi Zeira and Rav Assi came to an inn — *ikla'u lefundaka deYa'i*, in a place called Ya'i. There, eggs that had been cooked together with wine were brought before them — aisu lekameihu beitzim hametzumakos beyayin. Rabbi Zeira did not eat. Rav Assi ate.

Afterward, Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Assi: *velo chayish mar leta'aroves demai*? — were you not concerned about the mixture of demai? The category of demai refers to agricultural produce purchased from an am ha'aretz, a Jew who is not learned or careful in the observance of halachic requirements. Because such a person may not have taken terumot u'ma'asrot — tithes and priestly portions — from his produce, one must suspect that the product remains tevel, with the separations not yet made. Demai is this doubtful status. The eggs themselves presented no problem, but the wine mixed with them presumably came from an am ha'aretz innkeeper, and might well have been demai. How, then, could Rav Assi have eaten it?

Rav Assi replied: lav adaitai — I simply was not thinking about it. I saw eggs and did not focus on the wine mixed in.

### Tzadikim Protected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֶפְשָׁר גָּזְרוּ עַל הַתַּעֲרוֹבֶת דְּמַאי, וּמִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא דְּרַב אַסִּי לְמֵיכַל אִיסּוּרָא? הַשְׁתָּא בְּהֶמְתָּן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים אֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵבִיא תַּקָּלָה עַל יָדָן, צַדִּיקִים עַצְמָן לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** to himself: Is it **possible** that the Sages **issued a decree on a mixture** containing ***demai* and the matter eventuated that Rav Asi ate forbidden** food? **Now,** since **even** with regard to **the animals of the righteous, the Holy One, Blessed be He, does not generate mishaps through them,** is it **not all the more so** true that **the righteous themselves** would not experience mishaps?

Rabbi Zeira found this answer difficult to accept. He said to himself: efshar gazru al ta'aroves demai — is it really possible that the Rabbis forbade something merely because it is mixed with demai? Ve'istaiya milsa deRav Assi lemichal issura — and it would have come to pass that Rav Assi ate something forbidden? He could not accept this. Even granting that Rav Assi had simply not been thinking, Rabbi Zeira could not countenance the idea that a tzadik of that stature would have inadvertently eaten even a rabbinic prohibition — for that is all demai is, a rabbinic concern.

His reasoning was drawn from a well-known principle: hashta behemton shel tzadikim ein hakadosh baruch hu meivi takala al yadam — if the Holy One, blessed be He, does not cause even the animals of tzadikim to stumble into transgression — *tzadikim atzman lo kol shekein*? — certainly He would not allow the tzadikim themselves to do so. Rabbi Zeira thus went out — nafik — to investigate whether there is in fact any prohibition on a ta'aroves of demai.

## Demai Mixtures

### Raw Ingredients Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַק רַבִּי זֵירָא דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דִּתְנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ יַיִן לָתֵת לְתוֹךְ הַמּוּרְיָיס אוֹ לְתוֹךְ הָאֲלוּנְתִּית, כַּרְשִׁינִין לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן טְחִינִין, עֲדָשִׁים לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן רְסִיסִין – חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם דְּמַאי, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר מִשּׁוּם וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira emerged, analyzed, and found** that no mishap was generated through Rav Asi, **as we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Demai* 1:24): In the case of **one who purchases wine to place into fish gravy [*hamorayes*] or into *aluntit*,** a beverage in which wine is mixed, or one who purchases **vetch to prepare grist from it,** or **lentils to prepare groats from it,** if it is uncertain whether what he purchased is tithed, e.g., he bought it from one who is unreliable with regard to tithes [*am ha'aretz*], **one is obligated** to tithe it, **due to** the fact that it is ***demai*. And needless to say,** if it is certain that what he purchased is not tithed, he is obligated to tithe it **due to** the fact that it is **certain** that it is untithed produce.

Rabbi Zeira investigated and found the following Baraita: halokei'ach yayin lases lesoch hamoryas o lesoch ha'aluntis — one who purchases wine from an am ha'aretz, intending to put it into moryas, some type of prepared dish, or aluntis, a mixture of wine and oil and other ingredients. Or o karshinim la'asos mehen tchinim — one who buys karshinim, apparently some type of leek, intending to make a prepared dish from it. O adashim la'asos mehen resisin — lentils to make a different type of mixture. In all of these cases, the person is purchasing a raw agricultural product of demai status, with the intention of mixing it into a dish rather than eating it directly.

The Baraita rules: *chayav mishum demai* — one is nonetheless obligated to take terumot u'ma'asrot on account of the demai laws. *ve'ein tzarich lomar mishum vadai* — and needless to say, if the seller explicitly states that separations were not made, one must certainly take. The obligation is triggered by the initial encounter with the raw product, and the fact that one intends to mix it does not exempt one.

### Prepared Dish Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵן עַצְמָן מוּתָּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן תַּעֲרוֹבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And they themselves,** the gravy, *aluntit*, grist, and groats that one purchased from an *am ha'aretz*, **are permitted, because they are a mixture.** Since only one element of the mixture must be tithed, the food is permitted.

However, the key ruling follows: *vehein atzman mutarin* — if, however, one encounters the product not as a raw ingredient but as a prepared dish in which the demai has already been incorporated, one does not need to take terumot u'ma'asrot. Mipnei shehein ta'aroves — because it is a mixture. This distinction is crucial: the obligation is created at the moment one first confronts the original product in its raw state. If one never encounters it in that form — if it arrives already embedded in a dish — the obligation never crystallizes.

### First Encounter Rule

This Baraita was precisely what Rabbi Zeira needed. The case at the inn was exactly analogous: the eggs and wine were presented to them already combined as a prepared dish. Rav Assi never encountered the wine as a standalone raw ingredient; he encountered it only as part of the mixture. The Baraita says that in such a case, there is no obligation and no prohibition. Rabbi Zeira could now rest easy: Rav Assi had not eaten anything forbidden, since the rabbinic concern of demai does not apply when one first encounters the product as a prepared mixture. His confidence in divine protection of the righteous was vindicated — not overridden — by the halacha.

The Gemara then presses the question: v'lo gazru al ta'aroves demai? — is it truly the case that the Rabbis placed no prohibition on a mixture of demai? The Gemara raises a Baraita that appears to complicate this: hanosenes leshchentah issa le'ofos — a woman who is a chaver, someone scrupulous in terumot u'ma'asrot, gives dough to her neighbor, an am ha'aretz, to bake. Or *ukdeirah levashel* — a pot to cook. Einah chosheshas l'se'or vetavlin shebah — she need not be concerned about the sourdough or spices in it, *lo mishum shevi'is velo mishum ma'aser* — neither for shevi'is nor for *ma'aser laws*. Meaning, she does not suspect that the neighbor will swap the product, so she can assume she gets back what she gave.

Ve'im amrah lah asi li mishelach — but if she explicitly tells her neighbor: please bake using your own dough, please add your own spices — then *chosheshas l'se'or vetavlin shebah mishum shevi'is umishum ma'aser*. Now she must be concerned, because the neighbor's own products may be shevi'is produce or may lack terumot u'ma'asrot.

The apparent difficulty is this: in the case where the neighbor adds her own spices or sourdough, the resulting food is still a mixture — the majority of the stew is not problematic. Why should a minority admixture of demai spices or sourdough create an obligation?

## Sourdough & Spices

### Additive vs. Dominant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לָהּ: ״עֲשִׂי לִי מִשֶּׁלִּיכִי״, חוֹשֵׁשׁ לִשְׂאוֹר וְתַבְלִין שֶׁבָּהּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיעִית וּמִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂר. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּקָאָמַר לַהּ ״עֲשִׂי לִי מִשֶּׁלִּיכִי״, כְּמַאן דְּעָרֵיב בְּיָדַיִם דְּמֵי. רַפְרָם אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי שְׂאוֹר וְתַבְלִין, דִּלְטַעְמָא עֲבִיד, וְטַעְמָא לָא בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he says to her: Prepare** the dough or the food **for me with your own** leaven and spices, **he** must be **concerned about the leaven and the spices that are in** the dough and the pot respectively, **with regard to** the possibility that they are **Sabbatical** Year produce **and with regard to** the possibility that **tithe** was not separated, even though it is a mixture containing *demai*. It is **different there, as, since he said to her: Prepare** the dough or the food **for me from your own,** it is **like one who mixed it by** direct **action. Rafram said: Leaven and spices are different, as** each of them **is made for** the purpose of adding **taste** to the mixture, **and taste is not nullified** in a mixture.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty in two ways. The first answer is: shani hassam — there it is different — keivan d'amrah lah asi li mishelach — since you explicitly told the neighbor to add her own ingredients — *keman d'arvah b'yadayim dami* — it is as though you yourself mixed them in with your own hands. And if you personally add an ingredient, you have encountered the original product in its raw state at the moment of incorporation. The exemption for ta'aroves applies only when one first encounters the product already embedded in a prepared dish. Here, the explicit instruction to add the ingredient is tantamount to personally handling the raw product, and so the exemption does not apply.

Rafram offers a second, independent answer: shani se'or vetavlin d'leta'ama avid — sourdough and spices are different because they are added for taste and leavening, and as such they are not merely incidental additives. Sourdough makes the bread rise; spices define the character of a dish. In these cases, the problematic ingredient is not simply one of many components — it is dominant. When the Rabbis said that a ta'aroves of demai is not a problem, they meant a case where the demai item is genuinely subordinate and not the defining element. V'ta'ama lo batil — something that gives the dish its essential taste or function is not nullified. This is not the ordinary law of bittul — which would require sixty times the volume — but rather a distinct principle: if the demai ingredient is dominant by taste or function, it is not considered a mere mixture, and the exemption does not apply.

## Swapping Food Risk

### Mother-in-Law Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא גָּזְרוּ עַל תַּעֲרוֹבֶת דְּמַאי? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹתֵן לִשְׁכֶנְתּוֹ עִיסָּה לֶאֱפוֹת, וּקְדֵירָה לְבַשֵּׁל – אֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ לִשְׂאוֹר וְתַבְלִין שֶׁבָּהּ, לֹא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיעִית וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that the Sages **did not issue a decree on a mixture** containing ***demai*? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who gives his neighbor,** who is an *am ha'aretz*, **dough to bake** and gives her leaven for the dough to rise, **or** gives her **a pot** of food and the spices **to cook** in it, he need **not be concerned about the leaven and the spices that are in** the dough and the pot respectively, that perhaps she replaced them with her own, **neither with regard to** the possibility that they are **Sabbatical** Year produce **nor with regard to** the possibility that **tithe** was not separated.

The Gemara raises a further question: v'chilufei lo chashinan — is it truly the case that we are not concerned that someone will swap the food entrusted to them? The Baraita above stated that a woman who gives her neighbor dough to bake need not suspect that the neighbor will exchange it for her own. But a Mishnah appears to complicate this. Ha-noten lachamoto ma'asser et shehu noten lah ve'et shehu notel himmenah — a man whose mother-in-law is an am ha'aretz, not careful in terumot u'ma'asrot, who gives her something to cook or bake, must take ma'aser both from what he gives her and from what he gets back.

Why must he separate from what he gives her? Because if she swaps the food on him, the product he has given her — which he has not yet separated from — will be eaten by her as tevel. He would be party to causing her to eat something non-kosher, and therefore he must take terumot u'ma'asrot from what he gives her before handing it over. And why must he separate from what he gets back? Because she may have swapped it, and what he receives may be her own food, which has not had terumot u'ma'asrot taken. So the concern about swapping cuts in both directions.

### Embarrassment Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְתַנְיָא טַעְמָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: רוֹצֶה הִיא בְּתַקָּנַת בִּתָּהּ וּבוֹשָׁה מֵחֲתָנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There, the reason** is **like it is taught** explicitly in that mishna, that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** The mother-in-law **desires her daughter's well-being** and wants to ensure that she eats quality food, **and is reticent** to tell **her son-in-law** that she replaced the ingredients that spoiled.

This Mishnah appears to contradict the earlier principle that we do not suspect food-swapping as a general matter. The Gemara resolves the tension: hatam kedei tanya ta'ama — there, the reason is as taught in a Baraita. Amar Rabbi Yehuda: rotzah hi betakanat bittah u'bushah mechamiah — the mother-in-law wants her daughter's household to be well-served, and she is embarrassed before her son-in-law. These family dynamics create a specific and identifiable motivation for swapping. If the food burns or is ruined, the mother-in-law will not hand back the ruined dish. Out of embarrassment, she will prepare a fresh dish using her own ingredients and present that instead. The swap is not motivated by dishonesty but by the desire not to disappoint her son-in-law.

It is precisely this particular social dynamic — the combination of wanting to benefit one's daughter and fearing the son-in-law's disapproval — that creates the reasonable suspicion of swapping in this case. As a general rule, when one entrusts food to a neighbor or acquaintance, no such specific motivation exists, and we do not suspect that they will exchange it. The mother-in-law case is the exception, not the rule, defined by the Baraita's explicit psychological explanation.
