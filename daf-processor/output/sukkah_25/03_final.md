# Sukkah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Dwelling & Exempt…

### Overview & Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלוּחֵי מִצְוָה — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַסּוּכָּה. חוֹלִין וּמְשַׁמְּשֵׁיהֶן — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַסּוּכָּה. אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין עֲרַאי חוּץ לַסּוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Those on the path** to perform **a mitzva are exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*.** The **ill and their caretakers are exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*. One** may **eat and drink** in the framework of a **casual** meal **outside the *sukka*.**

Daf 25 marks a significant transition in the second *perek* of *Masechet Sukkah*. Having spent the preceding folios working through exactly what a *sukkah* is supposed to look like and how it is supposed to be constructed, the *gemara* now turns to the parameters of the *mitzvah* of dwelling within it. The *mishnah* that opens this daf moves from form to function, from the laws of construction to the laws of sitting.

### Why Start w/ Exemptions

There is something structurally notable about how this *mishnah* approaches its subject. Just as the tractate's opening *mishnah* led with what *invalidates* a *sukkah* rather than what constitutes one, so too here the *mishnah* does not begin by laying out the positive parameters of the *mitzvah*. It begins instead with the exemptions. One might have expected the *masechet*, having worked through all the construction details, to now arrive at a clear articulation of the obligation itself — and only thereafter proceed to its exceptions. Instead it does the reverse. This inversion is worth dwelling on, and the reason for it turns out to be conceptually significant.

### Enumeration of Exemptions

The *mishnah* enumerates three categories of exemption. *Shluchei mitzvah peturim min ha-sukkah* — those who are traveling or otherwise engaged in the performance of a *mitzvah* are exempt from sitting in the *sukkah*. *Cholim umeshamsheihem peturim min ha-sukkah* — sick people and their attendants are likewise exempt. And *ochlin veshotin arai chutz la-sukkah* — one may eat and drink temporarily, meaning a casual snack rather than a fixed meal, outside the *sukkah*. These are the activities that need not be performed inside the *sukkah*.

### Basukot Teshvu

The reason the *mishnah* leads with exemptions rather than with the basic obligation actually reflects a profound conceptual point about the nature of the *mitzvah* itself. The verse states *basukot teshvu shivat yamim* — dwell in *sukkot* seven days. At the most fundamental level, the *mitzvah* is conceived as *living* in the *sukkah*. Everything is to be done inside it. That does not mean the *sukkah* is a prison — one's house is not a prison either — but the starting assumption is total residency. You eat in the *sukkah*, you sleep in the *sukkah*, you do everything in the *sukkah*. Given that, the *mishnah* quite naturally begins by telling us what does *not* have to be done there, because the baseline presumes that everything does. This orientation — *teshvu*, you shall dwell — frames the entire discussion that follows, and it is an important lens through which to understand why the *mishnah* structures itself as it does.

## Osek B'Mitzvah Principle

### Source: Shema & Berakhot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּשִׁבְתְּךָ בְּבֵיתֶךָ״ — פְּרָט לְעוֹסֵק בְּמִצְוָה, ״וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ בַדֶּרֶךְ״ — פְּרָט לְחָתָן. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת הַבְּתוּלָה — פָּטוּר, וְאֶת הָאַלְמָנָה — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived that one who is performing a mitzva is exempt from the mitzva of *sukka*? **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that it is written in the Torah that one recites *Shema* at the following times: "When you sit in your house, and when you walk by the way, and when you lie down, and when you rise up" (Deuteronomy 6:7). The Sages interpret: **"When you sit in your house," to the exclusion of one who is engaged in** the performance of **a mitzva,** who is not sitting at home; **"and when you walk by the way," to the exclusion of a groom,** who is preoccupied with his mitzva of consummating the marriage and is not walking along the way. The *baraita* adds that **from here** the Sages **stated: One who marries a virgin is exempt** from reciting *Shema* on his wedding night, **and** one who marries **a widow is obligated.**

The *gemara* opens with the question *mana hanei milei* — from where do we know that a person engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from the *sukkah*? The answer comes from a *beraita* discussed in *Berakhot* concerning a *chatan* and the recitation of *Shema*. The verse in *Shema* states *beshivtecha b'veitecha uvlechtecha vaderech* — when you sit in your house and when you go on your way. From *beshivtecha b'veitecha* we learn *prat le-osek b'mitzvah*, excluding one who is engaged in a *mitzvah* from the obligation to recite *Shema*. From *uvlechtecha vaderech* we learn *prat le-chatan*, excluding a groom.

The context here is important. The *pesukim* of *veshinnantam levanecha vedibarta bam* are understood by *Chazal* to speak both to *talmud Torah* generally and to the *mitzvah* of *keri'at Shema* specifically — that obligation also appears in the *pasuk* of *v'hayah im shamoa*, where one is commanded to speak of these matters. The obligation of *keri'at Shema* is therefore tied to the conditions of *beshivtecha b'veitecha u'velechtecha vaderech*, and these conditions are what generate the two exemptions.

From this *beraita* the *mishnah* in *Berakhot* derives that a man who marries a *betulah* — a virgin — is exempt from reciting *Shema* on his wedding night, whereas one who marries a widow is obligated. That distinction will require explanation. But first the *gemara* asks: *mai mashma* — what precisely in the verse suggests the exemption for one who is *osek b'mitzvah*?

### Derech/Discretionary Act…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כְּדֶרֶךְ. מָה דֶּרֶךְ רְשׁוּת, אַף כֹּל רְשׁוּת, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הַאי דִּבְמִצְוָה עָסוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** may it **be inferred** in this verse that a groom is exempt from the mitzva of *Shema*? **Rav Huna said:** The circumstances when one is obligated to recite *Shema* are **like** the circumstances when one walks along **the way: Just as** the walking by the **way** described in the verse is **voluntary** and involves no mitzva, **so too, all** those obligated to recite *Shema* are similarly engaged in **voluntary** activities, **to the exclusion of this** groom, **who is engaged in** the performance of **a mitzva.**

Rav Huna explains: *k'derech* — the word *vaderech*, "on the way," indicates something like going on a journey, which is a discretionary, volitional activity. *Mah derech reshut* — just as going on a journey is something one chooses to do, *af kol reshut* — so too the obligation to recite *Shema* applies whenever one is engaged in discretionary activities. *Le-apukei hai d'v'mitzvah asik* — this excludes a person who is engaged in a *mitzvah*, whose activity is obligatory rather than volitional, and who is therefore exempt from *Shema*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָאָזֵיל לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, וְקָא אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא לִיקְרֵי! אִם כֵּן לֵימָא קְרָא ״בְּשֶׁבֶת וּבְלֶכֶת״, מַאי ״בְּשִׁבְתְּךָ״ ״וּבְלֶכְתְּךָ״ — בְּלֶכֶת דִּידָךְ הוּא דְּמִיחַיְּיבַתְּ, הָא בְּלֶכֶת דְּמִצְוָה — פְּטִירַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The verse does not specify the way along which one is walking. **Are we not dealing with one who is walking** along the way **for a matter of a mitzva, and** nevertheless, **the Merciful One says to recite** *Shema*? Apparently, one is obligated to do so even if he set out to perform a mitzva. The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that the intention was to obligate even those who are engaged in performance of a mitzva, **let the verse state: When sitting and when walking. What is** the meaning of: **"When you sit…and when you walk"?** It comes to underscore: **It is in your walking,** undertaken for personal reasons and of one's own volition, **that you are obligated** to recite *Shema*; **in walking** with the objective **of** performing **a mitzva, you are exempt** from reciting *Shema*.

The *gemara* pushes back: who says that *derech* means only a discretionary journey? Perhaps the verse means to obligate *Shema* in all situations, even when traveling for a *mitzvah*, and *v'ka'amar Rachmana likrei* — the Torah is simply telling you to recite *Shema*. The *gemara* responds: if that were so, the verse should have said *b'sheves u'veleches* — in sitting and going — using simple unmodified forms. What does the possessive *beshivtecha uvlechtecha* — *your* sitting and *your* going — add? It tells you that it is specifically *your* going, your discretionary activity, that creates the obligation. *Beleches d'mitzvah patires* — when you are going because of a *mitzvah*, you are exempt.

There is a parallel in the laws of *Shabbat*. The principle of *chafatzecha asurim v'cheftzei shamayim mutarin* captures the same possessive dynamic: it is *your* affairs that are restricted on *Shabbat*, while matters of heaven remain permitted. The possessive language does halachic work in both contexts.

### Two Rishonic Approaches

Before proceeding further, it is important to introduce a fundamental *machlokes rishonim* about the nature of the principle *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah*. The *rishonim* are divided on what this principle actually means.

According to *Rashi* and *Tosafot*, the rule operates as follows: if one is performing *mitzvah* A and *mitzvah* B comes along, one does not stop A to do B — *but only in a scenario where performing B would compromise or take one away from A*. If fulfilling B would not detract from A at all, there is no exemption. For example, if one is reciting *Shema* and would have to stop in order to give *tzedakah* to a poor person, that interruption would compromise the *Shema*, and so one is exempt from doing so. But if one is walking to perform a *mitzvah* and a poor person is walking alongside and it would cost nothing in terms of the first *mitzvah* to reach into one's pocket, the principle would not apply.

The *rishonim* who hold this position are themselves challenged by a pointed question: if that is all the rule means, why does one need a *pasuk* to tell it? If stopping A to do B means abandoning A, one could object to the interruption on independent grounds. One might answer that there are situations where B could appear more pressing — perhaps B is a *mitzvah kiyum* with a passing window, or perhaps B is by some measure more weighty — and in such cases one might think the right move is to abandon A for B. The principle therefore comes to tell us: do not abandon A for B.

Other *rishonim*, however, read the principle in a more expansive way that they argue fits the *pasuk* more naturally. The verse *beshivtecha b'veitecha uvlechtecha vaderech* means: the obligation of any given *mitzvah* applies to you during your discretionary time. When you are already engaged in a *mitzvah*, you are not in your discretionary time, and the Torah therefore does not impose the obligation of any further *mitzvah* upon you — even one whose performance would not compromise the first. The *Torah* addresses its obligations to a person who is free; a person already committed to a *mitzvah* is not free, and so the obligation does not land on him.

The obvious objection to this second approach is that it proves too much: one who is wearing *tzitzit* is fulfilling a *mitzvah*, so by this logic he would be exempt from everything? The response is that the principle is *osek b'mitzvah* — actively engaged — not merely *mekayem mitzvah*, merely receiving the credit of a *mitzvah* one happens to be doing. Wearing *tzitzit* passively does not constitute being *osek*; actively traveling to perform a *mitzvah* does. The distinction matters, but the boundary between them requires attention. This debate will have direct implications for the *sukkah* case, and it also shapes how one understands the case of the *chatan*.

## Chatan Exemption

### Chatan & Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ כּוֹנֵס אֶת הָאַלְמָנָה נָמֵי? כּוֹנֵס אֶת הַבְּתוּלָה טְרִיד, כּוֹנֵס אַלְמָנָה לָא טְרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, even one who marries a widow** should **also** be exempt, as he too is engaged in the performance of a mitzva. That, however, contradicts the *baraita*. The Gemara responds that there is a distinction between one marrying a virgin and one marrying a widow. **One who marries a virgin is preoccupied** by his concern lest he discover that his bride is not a virgin, while **one who marries a widow is not preoccupied.**

Why is a *chatan* exempt from saying *Shema*? The most natural and intuitive answer — the one that appears to animate the presentation in *Berakhot* itself — is that he is distracted. He cannot achieve the requisite *kavanah*. One recalls the famous *mishnah* in which *Rabban Gamliel* recited *Shema* on his wedding night and his students challenged him: did he not himself teach that a *chatan* is exempt? He replied, *ani lo k'she'ar kol adam* — I am not like ordinary people, I cannot bear to go even a moment without accepting the yoke of heaven. The clear implication of that exchange is that the reason other *chatanot* are exempt is precisely because they cannot have *kavanah* — they will be too distracted.

The *gemara* here, however, is framing the *chatan*'s exemption not as a matter of distraction but as an instance of *osek b'mitzvah*. That reframing requires explanation.

### Rashi: Preoccupation Imp…

*Rashi* addresses this directly. He writes that the *chatan* is *tarud* — preoccupied — *libo tarud b'machshavat bi'ah*. He is mentally occupied with the fact that he will be having marital relations that night. And *Rashi* adds — crucially — *u-mistaber ki patrei kra mishum tirda patrei v'eino yachol la'asok bishnayim k'echad*: the Torah exempts him because of this preoccupation, and he cannot be engaged in two things simultaneously. The logic is that stopping to recite *Shema* would take him away from thinking about the *mitzvah* he is about to perform.

A few lines later *Rashi* elaborates: *choson tarud tirda d'mitzvah, v'ashmi'inan kra d'lo mechayev l'hasir da'ato mimachshavat mitzvah rishona* — the *chatan* is preoccupied with a *tirda d'mitzvah*, and the verse teaches us not to require him to remove his mind from that first *mitzvah*. He should continue mentally engaging with the first *mitzvah*, and ensure he performs it well.

This is a demanding construction. According to *Rashi*, who holds that *osek b'mitzvah* exempts only when performing the second *mitzvah* would compromise the first, one must argue that stopping to recite *Shema* would actually compromise the *chatan*'s performance of the *mitzvah* of *bi'at mitzvah* on his wedding night. The exemption is not simply that he is distracted; the distraction must be understood as constituting a form of *osek b'mitzvah* in which interrupting it would harm the *mitzvah* he is engaged with.

The second approach — that of those *rishonim* who hold that *osek b'mitzvah* exempts even absent any compromise — handles the case more smoothly. On that view, one need not argue that stopping for *Shema* would harm the *bi'at mitzvah*. It is sufficient that the *chatan* is mentally engaged with a *mitzvah*. That mental engagement itself constitutes *osek b'mitzvah* and exempts him from other obligations, regardless of whether performing those obligations would have any practical impact on the *mitzvah* he is engaged in. The *chiddush* of the *chatan*'s case is precisely that even *mental* preoccupation — not physical activity — qualifies as being *osek b'mitzvah*.

This also raises the question of why the *bi'at mitzvah* on the wedding night is described as a *mitzvah* at all. If it is because of *peru u'revu*, then every act of marital relations would be a *mitzvah*, not only the first. Some *rishonim* say precisely that — *ein hachi nami*, every such act qualifies — and what is special about this first act is only that even it qualifies, since one might otherwise have argued that conception is unlikely from the very first act and therefore even this initial act is not technically *peru u'revu*. Others propose that the first act of *bi'ah* with a *betulah* is uniquely significant because of the profound physiological and relational bond it creates, as reflected in the principle *ein ishah koreket berit ela lemi she'asah keli* — a woman forms her deepest covenant of loyalty with the man who first made her into, as it were, a completed vessel. Whether or not that constitutes one of the *taryag mitzvot* is itself a question worth considering.

### Virgin vs. Widow

The distinction between marrying a *betulah* and marrying an *almanah* is now addressed. One might assume that since marrying a widow also involves a *bi'at mitzvah* — presumably because of *peru u'revu* — the *chatan* in that case should also be considered *osek b'mitzvah*. The *gemara* therefore asks: *kones almanah nami?* Should the one marrying a widow also be exempt? The answer is: *kones betulah tarid, kones almanah lo tarid* — one who marries a virgin is preoccupied; one who marries a widow is not. In both cases there may be a *mitzvah*, but in one case the preoccupation that defines *osek b'mitzvah* is present and in the other it is not. The difference is not whether a *mitzvah* will be performed, but whether the person is mentally *osek* with it.

### Avel Counterexample

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הֵיכָא דִּטְרִיד, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָטוּר?! אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, טָבְעָה סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם — דִּטְרִיד, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָטוּר?! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: אָבֵל חַיָּיב בְּכׇל הַמִּצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה חוּץ מִן הַתְּפִילִּין, שֶׁהֲרֵי נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״פְּאֵר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And wherever** one **is preoccupied is he indeed exempt? But if** that is **so,** then one **whose ship sank at sea, who is preoccupied,** should **also** be **exempt.** The Gemara reinforces its question: **And if you say** that **indeed, that is so, didn't Rabbi Abba bar Zavda say** that **Rav said: A mourner is obligated in all the mitzvot mentioned in the Torah,** including reciting *Shema*, **except for** the mitzva to don **phylacteries,** from which he is exempt, **as** the term **splendor is stated with regard to** phylacteries? If a mourner, who is clearly pained and preoccupied, is obligated to recite *Shema*, then certainly all others who are preoccupied, even one whose ship sank at sea, whose loss was merely monetary (*Birkat Hashem*), should be obligated. Why, then, is a groom exempted due to his preoccupation and one who lost his property is not?

The word *tarid* — preoccupied — appearing in this answer prompts the *gemara* to consider whether the exemption might have nothing to do with *mitzvah* at all, and everything to do simply with being distracted. *Vechol heicha de-tarid hachi nami de-patur?* — whenever someone is preoccupied, are they exempt from *Shema*? What about someone whose ship sank at sea, who just lost a fortune — is he exempt because he is preoccupied?

The *gemara* might have been inclined to entertain this, but it is immediately refuted by the ruling of Rav Abba bar Zavda in the name of Rav: *avel chayav bechol hamitzvot ha'amoros ba-Torah chutz min ha-tefillin* — a mourner is obligated in all *mitzvot* in the *Torah* except for *tefillin*, which are described in *Yechezkel* as *pe'er*, glory, and whose wearing would therefore be inappropriate during the acute phase of mourning. But for *Shema* — the mourner is obligated. A person in the most profound state of grief, surely the most genuinely preoccupied individual one can imagine, is still required to recite *Shema*. Preoccupation alone cannot be the basis for exemption.

### Tirda Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא טְרִיד טִירְדָּא דְמִצְוָה, הָתָם טְרִיד טִירְדָּא דִרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, there is a distinction between the cases. **Here,** in the case of a groom, **he is preoccupied** with **the preoccupation of a mitzva** that he must perform; **there,** in the case of a ship lost at sea, **he is preoccupied** with **the preoccupation of a voluntary** act that he chooses to perform.

The *gemara* therefore reanchors the exemption: *hacha tarid tirda d'mitzvah, hatam tarid tirda d'reshut* — here, in the case of the *chatan*, the preoccupation is *tirda d'mitzvah*, preoccupation arising from a *mitzvah*; whereas in the case of the person who lost his fortune at sea or the *avel* dwelling on the loss of a loved one, the preoccupation is *tirda d'reshut* — a discretionary or at least non-*mitzvah* preoccupation.

This distinction, *tirda d'mitzvah* versus *tirda d'reshut*, is the *gemara*'s resolution, but it is a striking one. Labeling an *avel*'s grief as *tirda d'reshut* is remarkable. One might have thought that the entire framework of *aveilut* is constructed around the idea that thinking about the *met*, dwelling in one's loss, is not only emotionally natural but religiously appropriate — perhaps even obligatory. *Rashi* engages with this tension directly. He writes that the *avel* is obligated to engage in all the *practices* of *aveilut* — the prohibitions of washing, anointing, and wearing shoes — as expressions of *kavod ha-met*, honor to the deceased. But there is no obligation to *suffer*; the acts of *aveilut* are demonstrations of respect, not necessarily of self-affliction. One could argue that the period of *aveilut*, following as it does upon the period of *aninut*, is already a period of *tanchumim* — of consolation and working through the loss — and that it is unhealthy and inappropriate to remain in a state of acute, unprocessed anguish. The *gemara* is calling upon the *avel* to settle his mind.

Yet the *gemara*'s language — *ihu d'ka mitzta'er nafshei* — goes further than that. It says the *avel* brings the suffering upon himself, implying a degree of volitional choice in remaining in that state. And even on the first day of mourning, the day the *gemara* elsewhere calls *k'yom mar* — a bitter day — and uses as the reason to exempt the *avel* from *tefillin*, the same *gemara* still classifies his preoccupation as *tirda d'reshut* when it comes to *Shema*. The tension between using the *avel*'s mental state to inform his *tefillin* obligation while simultaneously dismissing it as *tirda d'reshut* for *Shema* purposes is, to say the least, a challenging and fascinating corner of these *sugyot*.

What the *gemara* does ultimately affirm is this: the exemption of the *chatan* from *Shema* is an instance of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah*. The *chiddush* is that mental preoccupation with a *mitzvah* is sufficient to constitute being *osek* — one does not need to be physically executing the *mitzvah* at that moment. The distinction between *betulah* and *almanah* is not about whether there is a *mitzvah* to be performed, but about the degree of mental *osek*: when marrying a *betulah* the *chatan* is genuinely preoccupied; when marrying a widow he is not.

## Korban Pesach Source

### Joseph's Coffin Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָעוֹסֵק בְּמִצְוָה פָּטוּר מִן הַמִּצְוָה מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיְהִי אֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר הָיוּ טְמֵאִים לְנֶפֶשׁ אָדָם וְגוֹ׳״ — אוֹתָם אֲנָשִׁים מִי הָיוּ? נוֹשְׂאֵי אֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף הָיוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And** is the halakhic principle that **one who is engaged in a mitzva is exempt from** performing another **mitzva derived from here? It is derived from there, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"There were certain men who were impure by the corpse of a person** and they could not observe the *Pesaḥ* on that day" (Numbers 9:6). Before proceeding with the discussion, the *baraita* seeks to clarify with regard to **those men** who became impure: **Who were they?** The *baraita* answers: **They were the bearers of Joseph's coffin,** which the Jewish people brought with them in the desert. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The *gemara* then raises a challenge: *me-hacha nafka* — are you deriving *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* from the *Shema* case? It was already derived from the case of *Korban Pesach*. The *gemara* cites a *beraita* about the verse *vayehi anashim asher hayu teme'im le-nefesh adam* — there were men who were *tamei* on account of a human corpse and could not bring the *Korban Pesach*, who then came before Moshe. Their coming to Moshe is the event that generated the institution of *Pesach Sheni*. The *beraita* asks: who were those men?

### Identity Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִישָׁאֵל וְאֶלְצָפָן הָיוּ, שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹסְקִין בְּנָדָב וַאֲבִיהוּא. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אִם נוֹשְׂאֵי אֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף הָיוּ, כְּבָר הָיוּ יְכוֹלִין לִיטָּהֵר. אִם מִישָׁאֵל וְאֶלְצָפָן הָיוּ, יְכוֹלִין הָיוּ לִיטָּהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: They were Mishael and Elzaphan, who were engaged in** carrying the bodies of **Nadav and Avihu** after they were burned in the Holy of Holies (see Leviticus 10:4). **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** These identifications are inaccurate, because **if they were the bearers of Joseph's coffin, they could have** already **been purified.** They were camped at Sinai sufficient time to become purified in time to sacrifice the Paschal lamb. And **if they were Mishael and Elzaphan they could have** already **been purified,** as the Tabernacle was erected on the first of Nisan, which was the eighth day of the inauguration, when the sons of Aaron were burned. More than seven days remained until the eve of Passover on the fourteenth of Nisan.

Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: they were the carriers of Yosef's coffin. When it says *vayikach Moshe et atzmot Yosef immo*, Moshe took the bones of Yosef with him — those who had been carrying that coffin became *tamei* and could not bring the *Korban Pesach*. Rabbi Akiva says: they were Mishael and Eltzafan, the nephews of Aharon who removed the bodies of Nadav and Avihu from the Mishkan after the fire consumed them — *she-hayu oskim be-Nadav ve-Avihu* — and thereby became *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, עוֹסְקִין בְּמֵת מִצְוָה הָיוּ שֶׁחָל שְׁבִיעִי שֶׁלָּהֶן לִהְיוֹת בְּעֶרֶב פֶּסַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא יָכְלוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת הַפֶּסַח בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״, בְּיוֹם הַהוּא אֵין יְכוֹלִין לַעֲשׂוֹת, הָא לְמָחָר — יְכוֹלִין לַעֲשׂוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, they were** unnamed people who were **engaged in** tending to **a corpse** whose burial is a **mitzva,** i.e., which has no one else available to bury it, and **their seventh day** of impurity **occurred precisely on the eve of Passover, as it is stated: "And they could not observe the *Pesaḥ* on that day"** (Numbers 9:6). The Gemara infers: **On that day they could not observe it; on the next day they could observe it.** Although they would be purified at nightfall and would then be eligible to partake of the Paschal lamb, at the time of the slaughter and the sprinkling of the blood they were not yet pure. They asked whether the Paschal lamb could be slaughtered on their behalf. Apparently, they were obligated to perform the mitzva of burial of the corpse although it prevented them from fulfilling the mitzva of sacrificing the Paschal lamb, which is a stringent mitzva. This is the source for the principle that one engaged in the performance of a mitzva is exempt from performing another mitzva.

Rabbi Yitzchak then challenges both identifications with the same argument. If they were the carriers of Yosef's coffin: the *Bnei Yisrael* made this *Pesach* while encamped at Har Sinai, approximately ten months after the Exodus. Those who carried the coffin had set it down long before and would have had ample time to become *tahor*. Moreover, the laws of *Parah Adumah*, which enable purification from corpse *tumah*, were taught at *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* — giving anyone who was *tamei* at that point fifteen full days to purify themselves before *Pesach*. The same objection applies to Mishael and Eltzafan: the death of Nadav and Avihu also occurred on *Rosh Chodesh Nisan*, leaving fifteen days for purification. Therefore Rabbi Yitzchak concludes: *ela oskim be-met mitzvah hayu* — they were simply people who happened to be engaged in burying a relative, *she-chal shevi'i shelahem lihyot be-erev ha-pesach* — and their seventh day of *tumah* fell out on *Erev Pesach*, leaving them unable to purify in time.

### Tamei on Erev Pesach

The proof that it was specifically the seventh day is derived from the verse itself: *ve-lo yachlu la'asot ha-pesach ba-yom ha-hu* — they could not do the *Pesach* on that day, implying that *bayom ha-hu* — on that particular day — they could not, but *le-machar yecholim la'asot* — one day later they could have. This indicates they were at the last stage of their *tumah* — had their seventh day fallen one day earlier, they would have been *tahor* in time. And had it fallen even one day later, they would have been able to bring the *Korban Pesach*.

This *sugya* also illustrates *Chazal*'s characteristic impulse to make the anonymous concrete, to tie unidentified figures in a *pasuk* to known people and events. Rabbi Yitzchak ultimately resists this and acknowledges that these were simply unnamed individuals in an unremarkable situation, which is also an important kind of intellectual honesty within the *midrashic* enterprise.

## Korban Pesach Source (II)

### Pesach vs. Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָתָם — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטָא זְמַן חִיּוּבָא דְּפֶסַח, אֲבָל הָכָא, דִּמְטָא זְמַן קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא כָּרֵת, אֲבָל הָתָם, דְּאִיכָּא כָּרֵת — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Both sources **are necessary. As, if it had taught us there,** in the case of impurity imparted by a corpse, the conclusion would have been that the exemption from sacrificing the Paschal lamb is **due to** the fact **that the time of the obligation of the *Pesaḥ* had not yet arrived** when they were obligated to bury the corpse, and therefore they proceeded to fulfill the mitzva that they encountered first. **However, here, where the time** to recite ***Shema* had already arrived** during the wedding, **say no,** that the groom is not exempt; therefore, it is **necessary** to teach that the groom is exempt. **And if it had taught us here,** with regard to *Shema*, the conclusion would have been that the exemption from *Shema* is **due to** the fact **that** it is not a stringent mitzva, as **there is no *karet*** administered to one who fails to fulfill it. **However, there,** with regard to the Paschal lamb, **where there is *karet*** administered to one who fails to observe the *Pesaḥ*, **say that one is not** exempt from performing it. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach both cases.

The *gemara*'s question was whether the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* is derived from the *Korban Pesach* case, rendering the derivation from *Shema* unnecessary. The answer is that both sources are needed, because each one teaches something the other cannot.

The *Korban Pesach* case illustrates a situation where the person engaged in *mitzvah* A — the burial — knew well in advance that doing so would prevent them from performing *mitzvah* B — the *Korban Pesach* — a week later. At the time of the burial, *mitzvah* B was not yet present; it lay in the future. The argument there is: go ahead and do A because B is not yet on the scene. *Mishum d'lo mata zman chiyuva d'Pesach* — the time of the obligation of *Pesach* had not yet arrived. In such a case it is comparatively obvious that one performs the *mitzvah* in front of one right now. *Aval hacha d'mata zman kri'at Shema* — but here by *kri'at Shema*, one is in the middle of performing *mitzvah* A when *mitzvah* B presents itself right now, in real time. Perhaps in that situation one should stop A and do B? *Eima lo* — one might say no, you must now switch. *Tzricha* — you need this case to teach you that even when B comes along and presents itself while you are doing A, you still do not abandon A.

### Both Sources Needed

Conversely, had one only had the *Shema* case, one might have reasoned that the reason to continue with A rather than switch to B is that B is merely *keri'at Shema* — a *mitzvah* that does not carry *karet*. The *Korban Pesach*, however, does carry *karet*. *D'ika karet* — B there is of far greater halachic weight. *Aima lo* — in that case perhaps one should abandon A and ensure B is fulfilled. *Tzricha* — both cases are therefore necessary. From the *Korban Pesach* case one learns not to abandon A even when B is weightier. From the *kri'at Shema* case one learns not to abandon A even when B is present and pressing right now. Neither case alone would have established the full principle.

*Tosafot* notes that this analysis still leaves one open case: what happens when B presents itself *in the moment* — as in the *Shema* case — *and* B is of greater weight, carrying *karet* as in the *Pesach* case? Neither source addresses the combination of the two. *Tosafot* raises this as an unresolved question about the outer limits of the principle.

## Application to Sukkah

### Translation to Sukkah

Now that the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* has been established and its sources examined, the *gemara* turns to how it applies to the case of *shluchei mitzvah* and the *sukkah*. The *mishnah*'s exemption of those traveling to perform a *mitzvah* from the obligation of *sukkah* is derived directly from this principle. As *Rashi*'s example has it, if one is traveling to perform *pidyon shevuyim* — the redemption of captives — one is *osek b'mitzvah* and therefore exempt from *sukkah*.

But a nuance must be addressed. It is one thing to say that the traveler need not stop his journey and search for a *sukkah* in which to eat — stopping would obviously delay him and compromise his mission. But what about nighttime? When he stops to sleep, why is he exempt from sleeping in a *sukkah*? He is no longer in transit. Perhaps he could simply find a friend with a *sukkah* nearby.

### Tosafot Compromise Model

*Tosafot* addresses this tension directly, stating that the *gemara*'s own formulation — *holchei li-dvar mitzvah peturim min ha-sukkah bein ba-yom bein ba-laylah* — explicitly includes both day and night. The reason for this, *Tosafot* explains, is that travelers move during the day but not at night, so one might have thought the nighttime exemption requires independent justification. *Tosafot* then provides that justification: *de-hacha nami ayiri be-hai gavna di-matrid be-kiyum mitzvat sukkah havei mevatlei mi-mitzvot* — the exemption applies when the act of sitting or sleeping in a *sukkah* would itself compromise the performance of the *mitzvah*. One has to construct the scenario such that searching for a *sukkah*, sleeping less comfortably, or losing time will have some actual impact on the execution of their mission. According to *Tosafot*, this is required because they follow the *rishonim* who hold that *osek b'mitzvah* exempts only when performing *mitzvah* B would compromise *mitzvah* A.

### Non-compromise Approach

The alternative approach, as outlined earlier, does not require this construction. On that view, once someone is in the process of performing a *mitzvah* — traveling to perform it, sleeping to maintain their capacity to perform it — they are *osek b'mitzvah* throughout. Everything that is part of this process of being engaged in the *mitzvah* falls under the umbrella of *osek*. There is no requirement to argue that sleeping in a *sukkah* would specifically compromise the mission. Being *osek b'mitzvah* is its own complete exemption from other *mitzvot*, regardless of whether fulfilling them would cause any practical interference.

### Trivial Activity Limits

The broader implications of the non-compromise approach raise a practical concern, as is evident from the *rishonim* who hold it. If one is *osek b'mitzvah* and therefore entirely exempt from all other *mitzvot*, the principle risks becoming extreme. As one might ask: if these *shluchei mitzvah* are on a boat traveling to their destination, and they have nothing to do but sit — does *osek b'mitzvah* exempt them from everything during those idle hours? The *rishonim* who take the broader view acknowledge this implication and pull back from its most extreme application: when the performance of another *mitzvah* is entirely trivial — when it costs nothing in terms of time, energy, or focus — the exemption arguably does not apply, or at minimum one should fulfill the other *mitzvah* in that case. The outer limits remain somewhat open-ended, but even those who hold the broader view resist the conclusion that *osek b'mitzvah* functions as an unlimited blanket exemption from all religious obligation.

## Avel in Sukkah

### Avel & Tefillin Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: אָבֵל חַיָּיב בְּכׇל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה חוּץ מִתְּפִילִּין, שֶׁהֲרֵי נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״פְּאֵר״. מִדַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקְאֵל ״פְּאֵרְךָ חֲבוֹשׁ עָלֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״ — אַתְּ הוּא דְּמִיחַיְּיבַתְּ, אֲבָל כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא פְּטִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **the matter itself, Rabbi Abba bar Zavda said** that **Rav said: A mourner is obligated in all the mitzvot mentioned in the Torah except for** the mitzva to don **phylacteries,** from which a mourner is exempt, **as** the term **splendor is stated** with regard to phylacteries, and it is not proper for a mourner to adorn himself in this manner. This is derived from the fact that **the Merciful One said to Ezekiel:** "Sigh in silence; make no mourning for the dead, **bind your splendor upon you,** and put your shoes upon your feet" (Ezekiel 24:17). Ezekiel was commanded to refrain from mourning for his wife in the manner that others do. God said to Ezekiel: **You are obligated** to don phylacteries even while mourning; **however, everyone** else **is exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּיוֹם רִאשׁוֹן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַחֲרִיתָהּ כְּיוֹם מָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This** exemption **applies** only **on the first day** of mourning, **as it is written:** "And I will make it as the mourning for an only son, **and the end thereof as a bitter day"** (Amos 8:10). From this verse it is derived that the primary bitterness of a mourner lasts only one day.

The *gemara* now turns to a separate ruling of Rav Abba bar Zavda in the name of Rav — the same tradent who earlier established that an *avel* is obligated in *keri'at Shema* even on the first day. The ruling is: *avel chayav bechol mitzvot ha'amoros ba-Torah chutz min ha-tefillin* — a mourner is obligated in all *mitzvot* in the *Torah* with the exception of *tefillin*. The source for the *tefillin* exemption is *Yechezkel*, where God commands the prophet *pe'ercha chavosh alecha* — bind your glory upon you — meaning *tefillin*, in the context of telling him to behave differently than a normal *avel*. From this, *Chazal* derive that for an ordinary *avel*, wearing *tefillin* — described as *pe'er*, glory — is inappropriate and therefore prohibited. It would be disrespectful to the deceased for the *avel* to adorn himself with an object of glory while in mourning.

This exemption is limited to the first day of mourning. The verse in *Yechezkel* also alludes to *acharitah k'yom mar* — its aftermath is like a bitter day — and it is that acute bitterness of the first day that justifies the exemption. Many *rishonim* connect this to the position that the obligation of *aveilut* is *de-oraita* only on the first day, and the suspension of *tefillin* is correspondingly limited.

There is a genuine tension worth noting here. The *gemara* uses the *avel*'s mental state on the first day — the bitterness, the devastation — as the basis for his *tefillin* exemption. Yet the same *gemara* has already classified the *avel*'s grief as *tirda d'reshut* when it comes to *keri'at Shema*. Even on this first bitter day, even when that bitterness is invoked to exempt him from *tefillin*, the *gemara* does not allow that same state to exempt him from *Shema*. The intersection of these two rulings is genuinely challenging.

### Avel Obligated in Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: אָבֵל חַיָּיב בַּסּוּכָּה. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: מִצְטַעֵר פָּטוּר מִן הַסּוּכָּה — הַאי נָמֵי מִצְטַעֵר הוּא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן: הָנֵי מִילֵּי צַעֲרָא דְמִמֵּילָא, אֲבָל הָכָא, אִיהוּ הוּא דְּקָא מְצַעַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְיַתּוֹבֵי דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rabbi Abba bar Zavda said** that **Rav said: A mourner is obligated in** the mitzva of ***sukka*.** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious;** why would he be exempt? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **since Rabbi Abba bar Zavda said** that **Rav said** that **one who is suffering** due to his presence in the *sukka* **is exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*,** one could have said that **this** mourner **too is one who is suffering** and should be exempt as well. Therefore, **he teaches us** that the mourner is obligated in the mitzva of *sukka*. These cases are not similar, since **this** exemption from *sukka* **applies only** with regard to **suffering that is** caused **by** the *sukka* **itself,** e.g., when one is cold or hot or when the roofing has a foul odor. **However, here,** in the case of a mourner, **where he is causing himself to suffer** unrelated to his presence in the *sukka*, **he is required to settle himself** and fulfill the mitzva.

The *gemara* then records a second ruling from the same tradition: *avel chayav ba-sukkah* — a mourner is obligated in the *sukkah*. The *gemara* immediately asks *peshita* — is this not obvious? We have already established that the *avel*'s preoccupation is *tirda d'reshut*, not *tirda d'mitzvah*, and therefore he is not considered *osek b'mitzvah* and has no ground for exemption on that basis. Of course he is obligated. So why is this ruling stated?

### Self-Inflicted Tzaar

The *gemara* explains: *mahu d'teima* — one might have thought otherwise, because of the ruling that *mitzta'er patur min ha-sukkah* — one who is suffering is exempt from the *sukkah*. And *hai nami mitzta'er hu* — this *avel* is also suffering. Perhaps he should be exempt on those grounds?

The *gemara* therefore needs to state: *ka-mashma lan — hanei milei tza'ara de-mimeila* — the exemption of *mitzta'er* applies to suffering that comes on its own accord, from without. *Aval hacha ihu deka mitzta'er nafshei* — here, the *avel* is bringing this suffering upon himself. *Iba'ei lei le-yisuvei da'atei* — he should settle his mind and compose himself. It is a striking statement, and not for the first time in this *sugya*: the *gemara* is distinctly unsympathetic to the *avel*'s *tza'ar*, treating it as something he has the capacity to manage and is choosing not to.

### Rashi: Source of Tzaar

There is an important ambiguity in how to understand the exemption of *mitzta'er* from *sukkah* in the first place, and how the *avel* case bears on it. *Rashi* explains *tza'ara de-mimeila* as follows: *she-ha-sukkah mitzta'arto* — the *sukkah* itself is the source of the suffering. The paradigmatic case is heat, cold, or a foul smell from the *sechach*. The Torah says *teshvu k'ein taduru* — dwell in the *sukkah* as you dwell in your home — and one does not live in a house that causes discomfort. Therefore when the *sukkah* causes the suffering, one is exempt. When the *sukkah* is not the source of the suffering — as in the case of the *avel*, whose grief follows him wherever he goes — the exemption does not apply.

On *Rashi*'s reading, the *gemara* should have simply said: the reason the *avel* is obligated is that his suffering does not come from the *sukkah*. The *avel*'s *tza'ar* is entirely independent of the *sukkah* environment. Therefore he falls outside the parameters of *mitzta'er patur min ha-sukkah* from the outset.

### Denying Credence to Tzaar

The difficulty is that the *gemara* does not say that. It does not say the *avel* is obligated because the *sukkah* is not the source of his *tza'ar*. It says the *avel* is bringing it upon himself and should settle his mind. This formulation opens up a different reading: perhaps *mitzta'er patur min ha-sukkah* applies even when the *sukkah* is not the source of the suffering — whenever a person is genuinely in a state of *tza'ar*, regardless of its origin, they are exempt. The reason the *avel* is obligated, on this reading, is not that the *sukkah* isn't causing his distress, but rather that the *gemara* simply refuses to credit his *tza'ar* as legitimate grounds for exemption. He is capable of setting it aside and he should do so.

### Mitzta'er Ambiguity

This ambiguity has practical consequences. Consider a person who has a severe rash or mosquito bites all over his body — genuinely and highly uncomfortable regardless of where he is. The *sukkah* is not the source of his discomfort; he would be in the same state in his house. Is he exempt from *sukkah* or not? *Rashi*'s reading would say he is not exempt, because the exemption of *mitzta'er* requires that the *sukkah* itself be the cause. The alternative reading — that *mitzta'er patur min ha-sukkah* is a general principle covering any state of genuine suffering regardless of its source — would exempt him, since his suffering is real and not something he is bringing upon himself through volitional choices the way the *avel* is characterized as doing.

The *gemara* thus leaves the scope of *mitzta'er patur min ha-sukkah* in a genuine state of ambiguity: is it a narrow exemption triggered only when the *sukkah* causes the *tza'ar*, or a broader one covering any state of *tza'ar*, with the *avel* case being a special exception because the *gemara* denies credence to his particular form of grief? Both readings are anchored in the text, and both raise their own questions about the relationship between suffering, *mitzvah* obligation, and the *sukkah*'s requirement of genuine, livable dwelling.
