# Yoma 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Echta Ve'ashuv

### Mishna: Echta Ve'ashuv

The daf opens in the midst of a broader discussion about the power of *teshuva* and the complexities of divine accounting — how people deal with past sins, whether *teshuva* should be done publicly or privately, and related themes. The Mishna now introduces a distinct and sobering case: *ha'omer echta ve'ashuv, echta ve'ashuv* — one who says "I will sin and repent, I will sin and repent" — *ein maspikin beyado la'asos teshuva*, he is not given an opportunity to do *teshuva*. This does not mean that *teshuva* would be categorically rejected were he to attempt it; the underlying theme throughout this tractate is that *teshuva* is always accepted. Rather, the opportunities to do *teshuva* are simply not made available to such a person.

### Why Repeated Twice

The Gemara asks: *lama li lemimar echta ve'ashuv, echta ve'ashuv* — why does the Mishna phrase it twice? The answer is drawn from a teaching of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *keivan she'avar adam aveira veshana bah hutra lo* — once a person commits a transgression and repeats it, it becomes as if it were permitted to him. The Gemara immediately questions this: *hutra lo* — literally permitted? *salka da'atach*? Surely not. Rather, *na'asis lo keheter* — it becomes like a *heter* in his own mind. This is the remarkable human capacity for self-rationalization: once we have done something twice, we have effectively constructed a psychological permission for it. As Rav Yisrael Salanter famously observed, do it three times and it has become a *mitzvah*.

### Rationalizing Sin

What is striking about this reading is that it interprets the Mishna's ruling — *ein maspikin beyado la'asos teshuva* — not as some divinely imposed external restriction, but as a psychological observation. If you have said *echta ve'ashuv, echta ve'ashuv*, you have reinforced the behavior in your own mind, rationalized it, and transformed it into something that feels like *heter*. And that is precisely why *teshuva* becomes nearly impossible — how are you supposed to repent for something you no longer genuinely regard as a sin?

There is an irony worth noting here: if you simply sinned twice without reflection, you might still feel guilty and return. But the person who says "I will sin and repent" is already constructing a framework that neutralizes moral accountability. The very invocation of *teshuva* becomes the instrument of sin rather than its remedy. The Gemara is less interested in reading *ein maspikin beyado* as a *midah keneged midah* — a divine measure-for-measure response — and more in reading it as the natural psychological consequence of chronic rationalization.

### Relying on Yom Kippur

The Gemara continues: *echta veYom HaKippurim mechaper, ein Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — one who says "I will sin and Yom Kippur will atone" — Yom Kippur will not atone for him. This case is more straightforwardly a *midah keneged midah*, because whether Yom Kippur atones is in some sense a metaphysical reality — it is about what God does, not merely about the sinner's interior psychology.

### Rabbi's Position

The Gemara now asks: *leima matnisin d'lo ke-Rabbi* — is our Mishna not in accordance with the position of Rabbi? For Rabbi taught that *al kol aveiros shebaTorah bein asa teshuva bein lo asa teshuva Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — for all transgressions in the Torah, whether or not one has done *teshuva*, Yom Kippur atones. If Yom Kippur works independently of *teshuva*, why should the reliance on Yom Kippur undermine its efficacy?

The Gemara responds: *afilu teima Rabbi* — even according to Rabbi, *agav shani* — using Yom Kippur as a psychological support for sinning is categorically different. That very reliance excludes the efficacy of the day, since it was the engine of one's transgression. The Gemara's consistent preference throughout this passage is to read the Mishna's rulings as rooted in the obstruction of genuine *teshuva* rather than as pure *midah keneged midah*.

### Aveiros Bein Adam LaMakom

The Mishna draws a further distinction: for *aveiros bein adam laMakom* — sins between a person and God — Yom Kippur is effective. But for sins between one person and another, Yom Kippur does not atone until one has appeased the person one has wronged.

## Sins Between Man and Fel…

### Verse Challenge

Rav Yosef bar Chavo challenged Rabbi Abbahu by placing two verses in tension. The ruling that Yom Kippur does not atone for *aveiros bein adam lachaveiro* until one has appeased one's fellow — is it really true that God does not forgive interpersonal sins? The verse in Shmuel 1 reads: *im yechete ish l'ish u'pilelo Elokim* — if a person sins against another person, God will *pilelo*, a word of uncertain classical meaning but which in context, given that Eli is rebuking his sons, seems to mean "forgive." The plain reading of the verse would suggest that God does forgive sins between people — seemingly the opposite of the Mishna's ruling.

### Verse Resolution

The Gemara initially offers a reinterpretation: *man Elokim dayana* — here *Elokim* does not mean God but rather judges. If you sin against your neighbor, judges will adjudicate the matter. But the Gemara immediately questions whether this reading works with the second half of the verse: *ve'im l'Hashem yechete ish, mi yitpallel lo* — if he sins against God, who will intercede for him? If *u'pilelo* means "judges will judge," then "who will judge him" when he sins against God makes little sense, since God will obviously judge.

The Gemara therefore offers a more elaborate reading. *Im yechete ish l'ish u'pilelo* — if a person sins against another person and that person forgives him — *Elokim yimchol*, then God will forgive. And *ve'im l'Hashem yechete ish, mi yitpallel ba'ado* — if he sins against God, who will atone for him? *Teshuva u'ma'asim tovim* — *teshuva* will secure that forgiveness. This is admittedly a strained reading of the verse; the *pshat* in context runs in the opposite direction of the Mishna. Nevertheless, the Gemara claims that the verse can be aligned with the Mishna's position: interpersonal forgiveness is granted once the wronged party forgives, while sins against God are resolved through *teshuva*.

### Appeasing Friend

Rabbi Yitzchak states: *kol hamaknit es chaveiro afilu bidvarim tzarich l'fayeso* — anyone who upsets his friend, even with words alone, is required to appease him. The verse in Mishlei is brought: *beni im aravta l're'echa* — my son, if you have entered into some arrangement with your friend — *nokashta b'imrei picha* — and you have wronged him with the words of your mouth — *aseh zos efo beni v'hinatzel* — do this, my son, and save yourself. The Gemara then interprets *lech hitrapes u'rehav re'echa*: if the wrong was financial, *hatter lo pisas yad* — open your palm and return the money, a wordplay on *hitrapes*. But if the wrong was verbal — *nokashta b'imrei picha* — then *harbe alav re'im*: gather many friends and have them come and intercede on your behalf to seek his forgiveness. This is striking: a financial wrong may in some ways be easier to redress, since you simply return what you took. A verbal wrong may actually be more demanding, requiring a sustained social effort at reconciliation.

### Shalosh Shurot

Rav Chisda adds: *u'tzarich le'fayeso be'shalosh shurot* — one must appease one's friend with three rows of people. This is brought down *le'halakha*. The rows consist of *shalosh shurot shel shlosha bnei adam* — three rows of three people each. The verse supports this: *yashur al anashim* — he shall make rows upon people. And you say: *chatati, ve'yashar he'eveti, ve'lo shava li* — I have sinned, I have perverted what is right, and it has not been of benefit to me. Three statements, three rows, three attempts. The total — three rows of three plus the one seeking forgiveness — incidentally does constitute a *minyan*, a point that will become relevant momentarily.

### Three Times Maximum

On the other side of the ledger, Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina teaches: *kol hamevakesh matos me'chaveiro al yivakesh mimenu yoter mi'shalosh pe'amim* — anyone who requests forgiveness from his friend (*matos*, from the idiom *be'matu minach* — with your leave, with your pardon) need not continue to debase himself beyond three times. If three attempts have not succeeded, he has fulfilled his obligation. The verse supports this from the three occurrences of the word "please" addressed to Yosef by his brothers: *ana*, once; *sa na*, twice; *ve'ata sa na*, three times.

### Death Before Forgiveness

What if the wronged party dies before forgiveness is granted — whether because the sinner never completed three attempts, or because despite those attempts reconciliation remained unachieved? The ruling is: *mevi asara bnei adam u'ma'amidan al kivro* — bring ten people and stand them at his grave. Here one does obtain the *minyan*. And one declares: *chatati la'Hashem Elohei Yisrael u'lifloni she'havalti bo* — I have sinned before God and before this person whom I injured. The precise mechanism of this ritual is not entirely transparent — it may be that the public confession itself is sufficient when nothing more can be done, or that the ten people as representatives of the *tzibbur* somehow stand in for the interests of the deceased.

## Stories of Reconciliation

### Rabbi Yirmiya's Humiliat…

The Gemara now records a series of moving and instructive stories about the difficulty of seeking and granting forgiveness. Rabbi Yirmiya had offended Rabbi Abba in some way — the Gemara's language is characteristically understated: *hava leh milta le'Rabbi Abba behadeih*, there was a matter that Rabbi Abba held against Rabbi Yirmiya. Rabbi Yirmiya went and sat by Rabbi Abba's door to seek forgiveness. While sitting on the doorstep, a maidservant threw out water — perhaps cleaning water, perhaps worse — and some of the drops struck his head. He said: *asituni ke'ashpa* — you have made me like a garbage heap. And then he applied to himself the verse *me'ashpot yarim evyon* — God lifts up the poor from the garbage dumps.

The moment is laden with symbolism. In the context of Yom Kippur and *ve'initem et nafshoteichem* — self-affliction — one wonders: does being treated in a degrading way itself constitute a form of *kapara*? The act of seeking forgiveness requires a certain degree of self-abasement, and here that self-abasement was enacted in a startlingly literal way. Notably, rather than reacting with anger, Rabbi Yirmiya found the moment fitting — though there may be a faint undercurrent of complaint in the word *asituni*, "they have made me."

### Accepting Shame

Rabbi Abba heard what had happened and came out to greet him, saying: *hashta tzerichna lemeifak adateich* — now I need to go out and make you leave your agitation, meaning to appease you, since you were treated so badly even inadvertently. The verse *lech hitrapes u'rehav re'echa* is invoked, closing the circle of the halacha above with this lived example. There is something quietly remarkable about this reversal: Rabbi Yirmiya came to seek forgiveness, and the manner in which he was treated inadvertently shifted Rabbi Abba into the posture of the one who must now comfort.

### Rav Zeira's Approach

Rav Zeira's approach to reconciliation reflects a different sensibility, one that fits precisely within the framework established above about not demanding more than three attempts. When Rav Zeira had a complaint against someone — meaning the other person had wronged him — *hava chalif ve'sani lekameih*, he would go out of his way, once and then a second time, to arrange what appeared to be a coincidental encounter. *Ki heichi denaysi venafak leih mada'ateih* — so that the person should come and be moved out of his state of avoidance. The point is not that he would chase after people crying *ani mochel, ani mochel*, but that he recognized how genuinely difficult it is to take the first step. He did not waive the need for the other person to ask for forgiveness — he believed the person's own *teshuva* process required that act — but he made the encounter easier by meeting him halfway.

### Rav and the Butcher

The story of Rav and the butcher is at once the most dramatic and the most cautionary of the collection. A butcher had done something offensive to Rav. The butcher never came to Rav to seek reconciliation. On *erev Yom Kippur*, Rav decided: *eizel ana le'piyusei leih* — I will go and make peace with him, taking upon himself the initiative even though the butcher was the one who had wronged him.

Rav Huna encountered Rav on his way and asked where he was headed. Upon hearing the answer, Rav Huna said: *eizel Abba lemiktal nafsha* — Abba is going to get someone killed. He knew Rav's nature and did not trust that the encounter would end well, even though Rav's intentions were entirely conciliatory.

Rav arrived and found the butcher sitting and chopping the head of an animal. The butcher looked up, saw Rav, and said: *Abba at, zil leis li milsa bahadach* — you, Abba, go away, I have nothing to say to you. And then he continued chopping. A bone flew out of the carcass, struck the butcher in the throat, and killed him.

The tragic irony is hard to miss: Rav Huna had said Abba was going to kill someone, and indeed, in a manner Rav never intended, that is what happened. Yet there is also a broader moral here. It sometimes happens that one resolves to be the bigger person, goes to make peace on *erev Yom Kippur*, and has the door slammed in one's face. That is a painful and humbling experience. And as one might read this story symbolically, the intransigence and anger of the one who refuses forgiveness can ultimately come back to harm that very person.

### Rav and Rabbi Hanina

A different and equally remarkable story: Rav was once teaching a passage of *Tanakh* in front of Rebbi. As various scholars arrived late — Rabbi Hiyya, then Bar Kappara, then Rabbi Shimon the son of Rebbi — Rav would each time go back to the beginning of the section to honor the new arrival. When Rabbi Hanina bar Hama arrived, Rav said: *kulei hai nehadar v'neizeil* — must we really keep starting over? And he did not go back to the beginning. Rabbi Hanina was offended: everyone else merited having the lesson restarted, and he did not.

By any objective measure Rav's behavior was entirely reasonable — Rabbi Hanina had come so late that it would have been absurd to begin yet again. But Rabbi Hanina felt slighted nonetheless. Rav, acutely aware of the offense, went to seek forgiveness on *erev Yom Kippur* — not once, not three times, but thirteen times, *tlasar malei yoma d'kippura*. Each year Rabbi Hanina refused to be appeased. The Gemara notes the apparent problem: Rav himself goes beyond the three-attempt limit, and yet refuses to apply the same limit here, because *Rav shani* — Rav was stringent with himself and wanted to ensure that everything was right.

But the question of how Rabbi Hanina could refuse to forgive — especially given Rava's principle that *kol hamaavir al midosav maavirin lo al kol peshav*, one who overlooks offenses done to him has his own sins overlooked in turn — demands an answer.

### Dream Interpretation

The Gemara explains that Rabbi Hanina had a dream about Rav: *chelma chaza lei l'Rav d'zakfuhu b'dikla* — he dreamed that Rav was suspended from a palm tree. One might assume this to be an ominous sign, but the tradition holds that *kol d'zakfuhu b'dikla, reisha havei* — one who is seen suspended from a palm tree in a dream will become a great leader, for palm trees are tall. Rabbi Hanina concluded that Rav was destined for *rishuta* — a position of leadership.

And here is the telling detail. As Rashi explains, Rabbi Hanina was himself the *rosh yeshiva*. If Rav was going to become the great leader, that could only mean Rabbi Hanina's own time was ending — that he would die. So Rabbi Hanina reasoned: if I refuse to forgive Rav, make life difficult for him here, he will leave, go to Babylonia, fulfill the destiny of his dream there, and become the *rosh yeshiva* in Bavel — and I will live. He was not being petty; he was, in a manner strange to our sensibilities, trying to stay alive. Had it not been for that calculation, he would obviously have forgiven Rav. The entire episode was orchestrated, through his own peculiar logic, so that Rav would leave the Land of Israel and become the towering figure he was destined to be — in Bavel rather than here.

## Viduy - Confession

### Timing of Viduy

The Gemara now turns to a formal halachic discussion of *viduy* — confession — on Yom Kippur. This transition is worth pausing over. The previous passages dealt with deeply human, unritualized encounters: genuinely confronting the person one has wronged, encountering humiliation on a doorstep, a bone flying from a carcass. Now we turn to the ritual framework of confession. *Viduy* raises its own questions about interiority versus form — but that tension is precisely what gives the discussion its depth.

The *tanna* teaches: *mitzvat viduy erev Yom HaKippurim im chasheicha* — the *mitzvah* of *viduy* is performed on *erev Yom Kippur* as it grows dark, meaning after Yom Kippur has already begun. However, the Sages said: *yisvadeh kodem she'yochal v'yishteh* — one should actually recite *viduy* before eating on *erev Yom Kippur*, at *mincha* time. The reason: *shema titoraf da'ato b'seuda* — lest one eat a large meal, become drowsy or disoriented, and miss the opportunity to do *viduy* before Yom Kippur begins.

Yet even though one has done *viduy* before the meal, one does *viduy* again afterward — at *maariv* — since *shema ye'ara d'var kalkala b'seuda*, perhaps something went wrong during the meal: one ate too much, forgot to recite a *bracha*, or committed some other infraction. And even having done *viduy* at night, one does it again at *shacharit*. And *shacharit* does not exempt *mussaf*, nor *mussaf* exempt *mincha*, nor *mincha* exempt *ne'ila*. At every prayer service of Yom Kippur, *viduy* is recited anew.

One might ask what the point of this repetition is. Is the assumption that perhaps one sinned between one recitation and the next? That framing is indeed how the Gemara presents it — each round of *viduy* accounts for the possibility of intervening transgression. One could read this as feeding a kind of hyper-anxiety about sin. But a more measured reading is that Yom Kippur as a day is entirely defined by the theme of forgiveness and *teshuva*. Just as on *Rosh Chodesh* one recites *Ya'aleh V'Yavo* at every *Shmoneh Esrei*, and on Chanukah *Al HaNissim* at every *Shmoneh Esrei*, so on Yom Kippur *viduy* at every opportunity is not anxious redundancy but the appropriate ritual expression of the day's essential character. In the absence of the *Beis HaMikdash* and its *avoda*, the repeated *viduy* becomes the act that defines Yom Kippur as a day of *kappara*. *Kappara* is no longer achieved through ritual sacrifice; it is achieved through *teshuva* — and so the entire character of the day is constituted by the ongoing act of seeking forgiveness.

### Where Viduy is Recited

The Gemara addresses where *viduy* is said. *Yachid l'achar tefilaso* — the individual recites *viduy* after the *Shmoneh Esrei*, not within it. This might seem to slightly complicate the analogy to *Ya'aleh V'Yavo*, which is embedded within the *bracha*. However, the *Shmoneh Esrei* speaks throughout in the communal voice — *Elokeinu*, *Refaeinu*, and so forth. The private *viduy* is thus naturally external to the *Shmoneh Esrei*, done immediately following it so as to remain *lifnei Hashem*, in God's direct presence. It is recited before the final *yihyu l'ratzon imrei fi* so that it remains within the atmosphere of the prayer, with *yihyu l'ratzon* then closing the *Shmoneh Esrei* afterward.

The *shaliach tzibbur*, by contrast, recites *viduy* within the *Shmoneh Esrei* itself — *be'emtza*, in the middle. This is because his *viduy* represents the communal *teshuva* and is therefore appropriately embedded within the public *chazaras hashatz*, the communal repetition of the prayer.

### Opening Formulations

The Gemara then discusses the various formulations used to introduce *viduy*. Rav's formulation is *Ata yodeia razei olam* — You know the secrets of the universe — which is indeed how we open the *viduy* sequence in our *machzor* today, following *Shmoneh Esrei*. Shmuel's formulation was *mima'amakei halev* — from the depths of the heart — a different kind of opening, inward and personal. Levi's formulation was *u'v'sorascha kasuv* — and in Your Torah it is written — which Rashi understands as referring to *ki bayom hazeh yechaper aleichem*, the very verse about Yom Kippur's atoning power that is incorporated into the *Shmoneh Esrei* itself. This is a striking way to open a *viduy*: not with self-abasement but with an invocation of God's own promise of atonement.

Rabbi Yehuda's formulation was *ki avonotenu rabu milimnot vechat'otenu atzmu misaper* — our sins are too many to count, our transgressions too numerous to enumerate — a text familiar to us from our own liturgy. Rav Hamnuna's formulation is perhaps the most affecting: *Elokai, ad shelo notzarti eini kedai* — My God, before I was created I was unworthy; *ve'achshav shenotzarti ke'ilu lo notzarti* — now that I have been created it is as if I had not been created; *afar ani b'chayai kal vachomer b'mitati* — I am like dust in my life, certainly in my death; *harei ani lefanecha kichli malei busha u'kelima* — behold I am before You like a vessel filled with shame and embarrassment; *yehi ratzon milifanecha shelo echta od* — may it be Your will that I not sin again; *umah shechatati mareik berachamecha* — and what I have sinned, cleanse through Your mercy — *aval lo al yedei yissurim* — but not through suffering.

This last petition resonates with what was discussed two days earlier about *yissurim* as *memerkin* — as cleansing and scouring. Here Rav Hamnuna explicitly asks for that cleansing without its painful instrument. Notably, this is the text we recite at the close of *Shmoneh Esrei* on Yom Kippur, before the standard *Elokai netzor leshoni mera*: we say *ad shelo notzarti* and then proceed. The Gemara notes that this *viduy* of Rav Hamnuna was also what Rava would recite throughout the entire year, not only on Yom Kippur.

### Essential Viduy

Mar Zutra states: *lo amran ela de'la amar aval anachnu chatanu* — all these formulations are necessary only if one has not said the three words *aval anachnu chatanu* — "but we have sinned." If one has said those words, *tu lo tzarich* — nothing more is required. This is apparently the irreducible core of *viduy*: the declaration "we have sinned." It is worth noting that the formulations discussed above all frame and surround the act of confession; they do not themselves constitute *chatanu*. The *anachnu chatanu* is the substance; the rest is introduction and context.

Bar Hamdudi relates that he was once standing before Shmuel, and when the *shaliach tzibbur* reached the words *aval anachnu chatanu*, Shmuel rose. *Shma mina ikar viduy hai hu* — one may conclude from this that those three words constitute the essential *viduy*. Some *rishonim* read the *girsa* differently: *shma mina* that *viduy* must be recited standing — that was the point Shmuel was demonstrating by rising.

What is notable throughout this entire passage is what the Gemara does not discuss: the text of *Ashamnu Bagadnu* or *Al Cheit Shechatanu Lefanecha*. Two possibilities suggest themselves. Either that liturgy was so well established that it needed no elaboration — the Gemara is only discussing the framing and the moment of essential declaration, while the detailed confession was understood. Or alternatively, one wonders whether the *ikar viduy* was meant to be something more personal, a fill-in-the-blank recitation of one's actual sins, with the surrounding liturgy as structure but the substance left to the individual.

## Ne'ilah Service

### Three Times Year

A *mishna* elsewhere teaches: *shlosha perakim bashana kohanim nosim et kapeihem arba pe'amim bayom* — there are three times during the year when the *kohanim* perform *birkat kohanim* four times in a single day: at *shacharit*, *mussaf*, *mincha*, and *ne'ilat she'arim*. The explanation is that these are fast days — *ta'aniyot*, *mamadot*, and Yom Kippur — on which a *kohen* would not have been drinking by late afternoon, and therefore *birkat kohanim* at *mincha* is appropriate. And it is on these occasions that *ne'ila* is also recited.

### Defining Ne'ilah

What exactly is *ne'ila*? Rav says: *tzeluta yetirta* — it is an additional *Shmoneh Esrei*. Shmuel says: no, it is a supplication — *mah anu mah chayeinu*, a meditation on human insignificance — but not a full additional *Shmoneh Esrei*.

## Viduy - Confession…

### Closing Viduy

The *baraita* states that on Yom Kippur night one prays seven *brachot* and recites *viduy*, then at *shacharit* seven *brachot* and *viduy*, at *mussaf* seven *brachot* and *viduy*, at *mincha* seven *brachot* and *viduy*, and at *ne'ila* seven *brachot* and *viduy*. This *baraita* itself establishes that *ne'ila* is a *Shmoneh Esrei* with seven *brachot*. A second *baraita* adds that at nightfall of Yom Kippur one prays seven *brachot* and *viduy*, *ve'choteim bevidui* — and concludes with a *chatima* of *viduy*.

Rabbi Meir holds that the closing *bracha* of *ne'ila* is not the standard *mekadesh Yisrael veyom hakippurim* but rather a *chatima* centered on forgiveness — Rashi suggests the formulation *ha-Eil ha-solechan*, the forgiving God. The term *choteim bevidui* — "concludes with *viduy*" — thus encompasses both confession and the divine response of forgiveness: not a confession in the strict sense, but the affirmation of God as the One who forgives Israel. The identification between these two — the human act of confession and the divine act of forgiveness — is precisely what the entire structure of Yom Kippur prayer has been articulating throughout.

The Sages say: one prays seven *brachot* and may, if one wishes, conclude with a *chatima* of forgiveness; otherwise the standard *chatima* suffices. It is worth noting that the middle *bracha* of the Yom Kippur *Shmoneh Esrei* is unusually long by any standard, precisely because it interweaves the normal *kiddusha hayom* theme of *mekadesh Yisrael veyom hakippurim* with the character of the day as a day of forgiveness — *ki atah solechan l'Yisrael*. Such an extended *chatima* in a *bracha* is exceptional, and it reflects exactly this merger.

The Gemara's conclusion on the Shmuel question: *tiyuvta de-Shmuel tiyuvta* — the *baraita* clearly establishes that *ne'ila* is a *Shmoneh Esrei*, as Rav maintained. Ulla bar Rav led the prayer before Rabba, beginning the middle *bracha* with *Atah Vechartanu* and concluding it with *mah anu mah chayeinu* — the full thematic sweep of the *viduy* sensibility within the *bracha* itself. Rabba praised this. Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan adds that an individual says *mah anu mah chayeinu* after his *Shmoneh Esrei* rather than within it, consistent with the earlier rule about individual *viduy* being recited following the *Shmoneh Esrei*.

## Ne'ilah Service…

### Ne'ilah and Maariv

Rav rules: *tfillat Ne'ilah poteret shel Arvit* — the *ne'ila* prayer exempts one from *maariv*, since, as Rav holds, *ne'ila* is an additional *Shmoneh Esrei*, and if one has davened a *Shmoneh Esrei* at night, one is exempt from davening *maariv* again.

The Gemara asks: but did Rav not also rule that *halacha ke'divrei ha'omer tfillat arvit reshut* — *maariv* is optional? If *maariv* is optional, what does it mean to be "exempted" from it? The Gemara resolves: Rav's ruling about *ne'ila* exempting *maariv* was directed *le'divrei ha'omer chova* — toward those who hold that *maariv* is obligatory. Even for them, *ne'ila* at night suffices.

A *baraita* then raises a challenge: it lists the five *Shmoneh Esrei* services of Yom Kippur — night, *shacharit*, *mussaf*, *mincha*, and *ne'ila* — and then continues to describe *maariv* as well. So one does daven *maariv* after *ne'ila*. The Gemara concludes: *tanna'ei hi* — this is a dispute among *tannaim*.

For *maariv* after the fast, one *tanna* says to pray seven *brachot* with *Havineinu* — a summary of the eighteen *brachot* compressed into a single middle *bracha* — to spare the exhausted worshipper. Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, citing his forebears, says one must daven the full eighteen. The reason: *mipnei she'tzarich lomar havdalah be'Chonein HaDa'at* — one must recite *havdalah* within the fourth *bracha* of *Shmoneh Esrei*, and that requires reciting all the *brachot* individually. The hope of a brief *Shmoneh Esrei* after breaking the fast is thus foreclosed by the need to include *havdalah*. The accepted ruling follows the second opinion: a full *Shmoneh Esrei* with *havdalah*.

### Ne'ilah Timing

The question of whether *ne'ila* is recited during the day or after nightfall is addressed obliquely through the following discussion.

### Ba'al Keri Issues

The *tanna* teaches: *kol chayevei tevilot tovlin ke'darkam be'Yom HaKippurim* — all those obligated to immerse in a *mikvah* may do so on Yom Kippur, since immersion is not a form of *ta'anug* prohibited on the fast. A *nidda* or *yoledet* may immerse on *Kol Nidrei* night. A *ba'al keri* — one who has experienced a seminal emission and was therefore subject to the *takkanah* that he may not pray until immersing — may go to the *mikvah* up until *mincha* time. Once he has davened *mincha* and then became a *ba'al keri*, there is no need to go to the *mikvah* because there is no remaining prayer service of the day.

But what about *ne'ila*? The implicit assumption is that *ne'ila* is at night, and therefore the *ba'al keri* need not go during Yom Kippur itself but can wait until nightfall. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, says he may go to the *mikvah* the whole day — including after *mincha* — in order to be able to daven *ne'ila*. This implies that *ne'ila* is during the day. The Gemara thus finds within this dispute an implicit *machlokes* over the timing of *ne'ila*.

The Gemara then notes an apparent contradiction from a second *baraita*, in which Rabbi Yosi seems to say the *ba'al keri* may *not* go after *mincha*. The Gemara resolves: in one case he has already davened *ne'ila*, in the other he has not, and Rabbi Yosi remains consistent that *ne'ila* is during the day.

A further question arises: if according to the *rabbanan* the *ba'al keri* goes to the *mikvah* even after *ne'ila*, why — isn't he already done with *ne'ila*? The Gemara answers: *kasavri Rabbanan tevilah bizmanah mitzvah* — the *rabbanan* hold that immersing on the correct day is itself a *mitzvah*, regardless of whether there is any remaining prayer service. Rabbi Yosi bar Yehuda, by contrast, holds *tevilah bizmanah* is not a *mitzvah*, and this is demonstrated from his ruling about a woman unsure of her *nidda* timing, where he says one final immersion at the end suffices without concern for the precise day. The two positions about *ba'al keri* on Yom Kippur thus map onto the broader dispute about whether *tevilah bizmanah mitzvah*.

## After Yom Kippur

### Maariv After Ne'ilah

As established above, the accepted *halacha* is that one davens a full *Shmoneh Esrei* for *maariv* after *ne'ila*, including *havdalah* in *Chonein HaDa'at*.

### Keri on Yom Kippur

The Gemara closes the tractate with a striking passage. *Haro'eh keri b'Yom HaKippurim* — one who experiences a seminal emission on Yom Kippur. The *baraita* says: *yored v'tovel u'l'erev yeshafshef* — he goes and immerses, and in the evening scrubs himself to remove any *chatzitza*. The Gemara asks: *u'l'erev* — what does scrubbing in the evening accomplish after the immersion has already taken place? *Mai d'hava hava* — what is done is done. The Gemara therefore emends: *me'erev yeshafshef* — he scrubs himself on *erev Yom Kippur*, so as to be able to go properly to the *mikvah* should he become a *ba'al keri* on Yom Kippur itself. Rashi notes that the principle is general — everyone ought to scrub themselves on *erev Yom Kippur* in case they become a *ba'al keri* on Yom Kippur — a characteristically heightened consciousness of potential spiritual contingency.

Finally, the Gemara records a *tanna* teaching before Rav Nachman: *haro'eh keri b'Yom HaKippurim avonotav mechulim lo* — one who experiences a seminal emission on Yom Kippur, his sins are forgiven. This is counterintuitive in the extreme — another *baraita* says *avonotav sedurin*, his sins are laid out; meaning, he will be punished. The Gemara reconciles: *sedurin limachal* — they are laid out to be forgiven. The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a third reading: he should be anxious the whole year, for it is a bad sign to experience sexual gratification on a day when all others are fasting and restraining. Yet if he survives the year, *muvtach lo shehu ben olam habah* — he is assured a place in the World to Come, for God had evidently wanted to give him that satisfaction on Yom Kippur as a gift. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak frames it simply: *shekol haolam kulo ra'ev v'hu saveia* — the whole world is hungry and he is satisfied. And Rav Dimi adds, citing the verse *yireh zera ya'arich yamim*, that such a person will have abundant life and many children.

It is a characteristically Talmudic ending — inverting expectations, refusing easy moral categorization, and leaving the reader with the strange irony that on the very day of *viduy* and *kappara*, the most seemingly embarrassing spiritual mishap may in fact be a concealed sign of divine favor.
