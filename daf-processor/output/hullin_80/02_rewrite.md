# Hullin 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Koy Classification

### Koy & Oto Ve-et Beno

The daf opens on 80b, picking up in the middle of the sugyah of *oto ve-et beno* and the broader question of fatherhood among mammals in *halachah*. The immediate focus is the *koy*, that enigmatic creature whose very nature, as we will see later in the daf, was not entirely clear even to the Amoraim. The working assumption throughout the earlier discussion has been that the *koy* is a hybrid — a crossbreed between a *behemah* (a domesticated animal, typically understood as a goat) and a *chayah* (a wild animal, typically understood as a *tzvi*, some species of deer or antelope). The status of this crossbreed depends on a layered set of questions: who is the mother, who is the father, and does *halachah* take account of the father's contribution at all?

The foundational rule for *oto ve-et beno* is that it follows the mother: the prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its offspring on the same day is triggered only when the mother qualifies as a *behemah*. The child's species-status is irrelevant; what matters is the mother. So the central question becomes: if the mother is a *koy*, does she count as a *behemah* for purposes of this prohibition? And that in turn depends on whether she is fully a *behemah*, partially a *behemah*, or merely an uncertain case.

### Zera Ha-av Question

The previous discussion established that there is a *safek* — a halakhic doubt — as to whether one must cover the blood of a *koy*, since *kisuy ha-dam* (covering the blood after slaughter) is required for a *chayah* but not for a *behemah*. The question is: what generates this *safek*? The answer offered is that it depends on the question of *zera ha-av* — whether the father's genetic contribution is halakhically recognized.

Consider the case where the mother is a *behemah* and the father is a *chayah*. The offspring is certainly at least half *behemah* through its mother. But whether it is also part *chayah* depends on whether we recognize the father's contribution. If we do, the animal is half *behemah* and half *chayah*; if we do not, it is entirely *behemah* by virtue of the mother alone. Since the Rabbis are uncertain whether to count the father's contribution, the animal's status as a *chayah* is itself in doubt, which in turn creates the *safek* about *kisuy ha-dam*.

This framework also explains Rabbi Eliezer's position. Since Rabbi Eliezer holds that the father's lineage is categorically not factored in, the *koy* in this scenario — where the father is a *chayah* — is simply a full *behemah*, with no *chayah* dimension whatsoever. The blood need not be covered, and there is no doubt about it.

### Matanot Debate

The Gemara now introduces a *beraitah* on a different set of laws: the priestly gifts — the *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* — that must be given to a *kohen* upon slaughtering an animal. These gifts apply only to a *behemah*, not to a *chayah*. The *beraitah* records that both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer take positions on whether these gifts are owed from a *koy* and from a *kilayim* (a crossbreed of sheep and goat):

> *Ha-zero'a ve-ha-lechayayim ve-ha-keivah nohegin ba-koy u-va-kilayim.*

The *tanna kamma* holds that the gifts do apply to a *koy*. Rabbi Eliezer concedes that a *kilayim* from a sheep and a goat — an animal entirely within the *behemah* world — is certainly subject to the gifts, but exempts the *koy*.

The Gemara works through how to understand this debate. The instinctive assumption is that we are dealing with the case of a *tayish* (a male goat, a *behemah*) that mated with a female *tzvi* (a *chayah*), producing the *koy*. In that case, the mother is a *chayah*. The Gemara asks: granted that Rabbi Eliezer, who rejects the father's contribution entirely, would say this animal is a full *chayah* — no father, no *behemah* dimension — and therefore exempt from the gifts. But what of the Rabbis? Even granting that they would say "half counts," the animal is at best an uncertain half-*behemah*. The other half is certainly *chayah*, from which no gifts are owed. And for the half that might be *behemah*, one could simply tell the *kohen*: bring proof that the father's contribution is recognized, and you may collect; absent such proof, *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-re'ayah* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to extract property. So why would the Rabbis ever obligate payment of the gifts?

The Gemara therefore reverses the case: *bi-tzvi ha-ba al ha-tayashah* — it is the *tzvi* (the *chayah*) that mated with the female goat (a *behemah*). The mother is the *behemah*; the father is the *chayah*. Now the offspring is certainly at least half *behemah* by virtue of its mother. The question of whether the father's contribution makes it also half *chayah* is what the Rabbis are uncertain about. In this configuration, the Rabbis' position becomes intelligible: since it is at minimum half *behemah*, at least half of the gifts may be owed. But now Rabbi Eliezer's exemption becomes difficult — if the mother is the *behemah*, and Rabbi Eliezer ignores the father, this animal would seem to be a full *behemah*, obligating all the gifts.

The Gemara's resolution is striking: Rabbi Eliezer, too, has a *safek* about whether to count the father's contribution. Both Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis share the same uncertainty regarding *zera ha-av*. This immediately raises the question: if they agree on the uncertainty, what exactly do they disagree about? The Gemara answers that their dispute is not about *zera ha-av* at all — it is about whether partial species-membership is sufficient to trigger a *halachic* consequence. The debate is over *seh afilu miktzas seh*: does "partial *behemah*" count as *behemah* for purposes of a given law?

The Rabbis hold: *seh afilu miktzas seh* — even partial *behemah* status is sufficient to activate laws that apply to a *behemah*. Rabbi Eliezer holds the opposite: if something is not a complete *behemah*, it falls outside the category entirely. Partial membership is no membership.

## Rav Papa's Framework

### Blood & Gifts Case

Rav Papa now systematically applies this two-variable framework — uncertainty about *zera ha-av*, combined with the dispute over *seh afilu miktzas seh* — to each of the three areas of law that have been under discussion: *kisuy ha-dam*, *matanot*, and *oto ve-et beno*.

### Half vs. Full Identity

Rav Papa establishes that for *kisuy ha-dam* and *matanot*, the operative case is specifically *tzvi ha-ba al ha-tayashah* — the *chayah* father with the *behemah* mother. In this case, both Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis share the *safek* about whether the father's contribution is counted, and their dispute turns entirely on whether being half *behemah* is sufficient.

For *matanot*: the animal is certainly at least half *behemah*. Rabbi Eliezer says half is nothing — no gifts are owed, because the animal does not qualify as a complete *behemah*. The Rabbis say half is enough: at least half the gifts must be given, reflecting the animal's undeniable *behemah* dimension. The other half — whose identity depends on the unresolved question of the father's contribution — is at best in doubt, and no gifts are extracted on the basis of doubt alone.

For *kisuy ha-dam*: the animal is certainly at least half *behemah*, and possibly also half *chayah* if the father's contribution is recognized. Rabbi Eliezer says that even if it is half *chayah*, that is insufficient — half does not activate the obligation to cover the blood. The Rabbis say half *chayah* — even as a *safek* — is enough to require covering the blood, since the obligation is triggered by any genuine possibility that the animal is a *chayah*.

### Oto Ve-et Beno Cases

For *oto ve-et beno*, Rav Papa notes that the relevant case can arise in either configuration of parentage, but the consequences differ depending on which parent is the *behemah*.

**First scenario — *tayish ha-ba al ha-tzviyah***: the father is the *behemah*, the mother is the *chayah*. The offspring is therefore certainly at least half *chayah*. Whether it is also half *behemah* depends on the unresolved question of the father. In this case, the maximum *behemah* component is an uncertain half. According to Rabbi Eliezer, even if we were to grant that the father contributes and the animal is half *behemah*, half is nothing — no prohibition applies. According to the Rabbis, if it is half *behemah*, the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno* does apply. However, since it is only *possibly* half *behemah*, no lashes can be administered — the *behemah* dimension itself is in doubt. Therefore, in this scenario the debate is *le-issura* only: the Rabbis would prohibit the slaughter (though without lashes), and Rabbi Eliezer would permit it entirely.

**Second scenario — *tzvi ha-ba al ha-tayashah***: the father is the *chayah*, the mother is the *behemah*. The offspring is therefore certainly at least half *behemah*. Whether it is also half *chayah* depends on the unresolved question of the father. Here, the minimum *behemah* component is definite. According to Rabbi Eliezer, even if we grant the animal is half *behemah*, half is nothing — there is a possible prohibition (since perhaps the father is ignored and it is fully *behemah*), but no lashes, since it might only be half *behemah* and half means nothing to him. According to the Rabbis, since it is certainly at least half *behemah*, lashes are warranted — *seh afilu miktzas seh*. Therefore, in this scenario the debate is *le-malkot*: both sides agree the slaughter is prohibited, but they disagree whether lashes follow.

The result is an elegant and rigorous matrix. All parties agree that uncertainty about *zera ha-av* is the baseline epistemic situation. What differentiates Rabbi Eliezer from the Rabbis is the normative question of whether partial species-membership is legally operative.

### Eliezer vs. Chachamim

This clarifies the underlying conceptual structure of the entire debate. The two variables are independent. The first — *chaishinan le-zera ha-av* or not — is a question that both Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis leave unresolved; neither claims certainty. The second — *seh afilu miktzas seh* — is the real locus of their dispute. When the *Torah* establishes a category (*behemah* or *chayah*) as the trigger for a law, does partial membership in that category suffice, or must the animal fully belong to it?

Rabbi Eliezer holds that legal categories are binary: you are either in or out. Half-membership is no membership at all. The Rabbis hold that partial identity carries partial or full legal weight depending on the context. For prohibitions, half-*behemah* is enough to prohibit. For monetary obligations like *matanot*, the half that is certainly *behemah* generates a proportional obligation; the uncertain half cannot be collected from a position of doubt.

It is worth noting that this question of *chashinan le-zera ha-av* is not always the same question wherever it appears in *Shas*. In the context of *oto ve-et beno* and the earlier discussion of *Chananya*, the question was whether the father is recognized as part of a family unit in the animal world — a question about relational identity. Here the question is more fundamental: does the father's genetics determine what species this animal belongs to? The two framings differ, though the Gemara treats them together.

## Koy Theories

### Rav Yehuda Position

Having established the legal framework, the Gemara now turns to the factual question: what exactly is a *koy*? Several Amoraic opinions are presented, and it becomes clear that by the time of the Amoraim, the precise nature of this animal — whose laws had been debated in Tannaitic literature — was no longer known from direct observation. The Amoraim were speculating about a creature they likely never saw, trying to reconstruct its identity from the Tannaitic discussions.

Rav Yehuda states: *koy biryah bifnei atzmah hi* — the *koy* is a creature unto itself, an independent species. And the Sages, he says, were unable to determine whether it belongs to the category of *behemah* or *chayah*. This position explicitly rejects the assumption that the *koy* is a hybrid. The *safek* in all those laws — *kisuy ha-dam*, *matanot*, *oto ve-et beno* — arises not because the animal is the product of a cross between two known species, but because it is a *safek* species whose identity cannot be resolved on intrinsic grounds. Tosafot observe that one might ask why the *chachamim* didn't simply examine the animal's horns — the Gemara earlier in the tractate established that horn morphology is the primary criterion for distinguishing *behemah* from *chayah*. Tosafot answer that the *koy*'s horns apparently had ambiguous features: perhaps they were ridged but not quite ridged enough, or pointed but not definitively so. The criteria are not always easy to apply in borderline cases.

### Rav Nachman Position

Rav Nachman offers a different identification: *koy zeh ayal ha-bar* — the *koy* is the wild ram of the forests. Rashi clarifies that *ayal* here means a large male animal, not a deer. The force of Rav Nachman's identification is that this creature presents a paradox: it is a ram (suggesting *behemah*), but it lives wild in the forests (suggesting *chayah*). That tension is precisely what generates the halakhic uncertainty.

### Tannaim Debate

The Gemara then cites a *Tosefta* that reveals the same dispute already existed among the *Tannaim* themselves. Rabbi Yossi states, in language identical to Rav Yehuda's: the *koy* is a creature unto itself, and the Sages could not determine whether it is *behemah* or *chayah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, however, disagrees: it is simply a *behemah*. He adds that the family of Dushai used to raise flocks and flocks of them — an empirical claim that the animal was well-known, clearly domesticated, and therefore clearly a *behemah*. According to him, all of the halakhic debates about the *koy* were based on a misidentification or misunderstanding; the animal's status is in fact unambiguous.

## Wild & Domestic Spec (II)

### Wild Goats & Ten Types

The discussion of the *koy* leads naturally into a broader inquiry about how to classify animals that straddle the boundary between *chayah* and *behemah* — specifically, where one strand of a species is domesticated and another is wild. The Gemara reports a ruling in the name of a chain of Amoraim (Rav Hamnuna cited by Rav Safra cited by Rav Zeira): *hanei izei devala keshirot legabei mizbe'ach* — wild forest goats are fit for the altar. The reasoning is that they hold like the position of Rabbi Yitzchak, who states that the *Torah* enumerated exactly ten species of kosher animals, and all kosher animals must be subsumed within one of those ten. The ten are: *shor*, *seh kesavim*, *seh izim* (ox, sheep, and goat — the three *behemot*), and then *ayal*, *tzvi*, *yachmur*, *akko*, *dishon*, *te'o*, and *zamer* — seven *chayot*, most of whose precise identities are uncertain to us. Since wild goats are goats, they fall within the *seh izim* category — they are a *behemah* by definition — and as such they are fit for the *mizbe'ach*.

### Wild Ox Debate

A *mishnah* is cited recording a dispute about the *shor ha-bar* — the wild ox. The *tanna kamma* holds it is a *behemah*; Rabbi Yossi holds it is a *chayah*. Rav Huna bar Chiyya explains that this dispute is specific to the wild ox for a particular reason. The *Targum* of the word *te'o* — one of the seven *chayot* listed in the *Torah* — is *turbala*, which can be broken down as *tor bara*, "wild ox." Since the *Targum* Onkelos was regarded as highly authoritative, the assumption that *te'o* refers to the wild ox was broadly accepted. But that creates a genuine tension: the word *shor* (ox) is used in the *Targum*, suggesting *behemah*, yet *te'o* appears in the *Torah*'s list of *chayot*, suggesting *chayah*. The Rabbis read the *Targum*'s use of *shor*-language as implying *behemah*; Rabbi Yossi reads its placement among the *chayot* in the *pasuk* as definitive.

### Interbreeding & Categori…

The general principle that emerges from Rav Huna's explanation is that the wild ox is debated precisely because the *Torah* appears to have classified it as its own species (*te'o*). For other animals, by contrast, where one branch of the family is domesticated and familiar, the entire species — including any wild branches — would ordinarily be classified as *behemah*, absent a specific Toranic identification of the wild variant as a distinct species. Wild goats, for example, are universally considered to be goats — no one disputes that — and their wild habitat does not change their species classification.

Several objections are raised to this framework. Rav Acha bar Yaakov asks: perhaps the *Torah*'s listing of the ten species should be read not as an exhaustive closed list but as a *perat ve-klal* — exemplification followed by a general rule — in which case any animal with split hooves would be kosher regardless of whether it fits neatly into one of the ten. The Gemara responds: if the *Torah* only meant to give examples, why list them at all? The very act of listing implies limitation. A further objection is raised: perhaps wild goats are not the standard goat (*seh izim*) but one of the unknown *chayot* — perhaps the *akko*, the *te'o*, or the *zamer*. After all, we do not know what those animals are.

## Wild & Domestic Spec (II)

### Ameimar Position

Before this discussion is concluded, Rav Papa reports to Rav Ashi what Ameimar had ruled in practice: *Ameimar shari tarbeihu* — Ameimar permitted the fat of the wild forest goats. If the fat (*chelev*) is permitted, that is a clear indication that the animal is a *chayah* rather than a *behemah* — since *chelev* of a *chayah* is permitted, while *chelev* of a *behemah* is forbidden. So while one view treats wild goats as entirely within the *behemah* category and fit for the *mizbe'ach*, Ameimar's practice demonstrates that at least some authorities held the wild branches of the goat family to be *chayah*, permitting their fat. This leaves the classification genuinely unresolved, with practical implications on both ends: not fit for the altar if it is a *chayah*, but the fat is permitted.

The broader question hovering behind this discussion is whether the *behemah*/*chayah* distinction is determined by fixed physical characteristics or by something more contingent — such as whether a given population has historically been domesticated. The Gemara's discussions about horn morphology earlier in the tractate suggested the former. But the debate about wild branches of domesticated species seems to introduce a relational or ecological dimension as well. These categories, it emerges, are not always cleanly separable in practice.

## Shechita She'eina Re'uya

### Rabbi Oshaya Statement

The Gemara now moves to a major structural observation about the *mishnah*. Rabbi Oshaya declares: *kulla masnisin d'lo ke-Rabbi Shimon* — our entire *mishnah* is not in accordance with Rabbi Shimon. The evidence he adduces concerns the *mishnah*'s treatment of *kodshim ba-chutz*: when sacred animals are slaughtered outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, the first person incurs *karet* and both animals are *pasul* (forbidden for consumption), while both persons receive lashes — the first for the prohibition of slaughtering sacred animals outside the sanctuary, the second for violating *oto ve-et beno*.

### Rabbi Shimon Principle

The principle Rabbi Oshaya invokes is Rabbi Shimon's ruling: *shechita she'eina re'uya lo shema shechita* — an act of slaughter that does not render the meat permissible does not count as *shechita* for other halakhic purposes. This principle has broad applicability across *Shas*: it surfaces in contexts such as the penalties of four- and fivefold restitution for a thief who slaughters a stolen animal, in the laws of *oto ve-et beno*, and wherever the *Torah* assigns consequences to the act of *shechita*. The underlying question is always the same: when the act of slaughter fails to make the meat permissible — because the animal was *treif*, or was a *shor ha-niskal* (an ox condemned to stoning), or was dedicated to *avoda zara*, or was a *korban* slaughtered outside the *Beit Hamikdash* — does it still count as *shechita* for purposes of other laws?

Rabbi Shimon holds that it does not. If the slaughter does not render the meat edible, it is not legally a *shechita* at all. This is a sweeping position with counterintuitive implications.

### Three Case Scenarios

The *mishnah* addressed multiple permutations involving *kodshim* (sacred animals) and *chulin* (non-sacred animals), inside and outside the *Beit Hamikdash*. Rabbi Oshaya works through each of them to show that the *mishnah*'s conclusions are irreconcilable with Rabbi Shimon's principle.

**Kodshim outside the Beit Hamikdash (*kodshim ba-chutz*)**: Both animals are *pasul* since *korbanot* slaughtered outside the sanctuary cannot be eaten. The first person incurs *karet* and lashes for *shechutei chutz* (the prohibition of slaughtering sacred animals outside); the second person incurs lashes for *oto ve-et beno*. According to Rabbi Shimon, however, the first person's act of slaughter — since it did not render the meat permissible — is not a *shechita*. But if the first *shechita* never occurred, the second animal's *korban* was never preceded by a valid *shechita* of its mother or offspring, which means: (a) the second person has no *oto ve-et beno* transgression, since the triggering *shechita* did not count; and (b) more dramatically, from Rabbi Shimon's perspective, the second animal remains one whose "first slaughter" has not happened. It is a *korban* that could still be brought properly inside the *Beit Hamikdash*. The second person, having now slaughtered it outside, incurs *karet* — not merely lashes for *oto ve-et beno*. The *mishnah*'s account of mere lashes for the second person is therefore incompatible with Rabbi Shimon.

**Chulin inside the Beit Hamikdash (*chulin ba-azarah*)**: Both animals are *pasul* since non-sacred meat slaughtered in the Temple courtyard cannot be eaten. The second person gets lashes for *oto ve-et beno*. According to Rabbi Shimon, however, the first person's *shechita* — which produced meat that cannot be eaten — does not count. And the second person's *shechita* produces the same: meat that cannot be eaten. Neither *shechita* is valid in Rabbi Shimon's framework. So the second person should not receive lashes for *oto ve-et beno* at all, since there was no qualifying first *shechita*.

**Kodshim inside the Beit Hamikdash (*kodshim bi-fnim*)**: The first animal is properly slaughtered and its *korban* is valid. The second animal, however, may not be brought that day due to *oto ve-et beno* — having been preceded by the slaughter of its mother or offspring, it is *mechusar zman* for that day. The second person receives lashes for *oto ve-et beno*. According to Rabbi Shimon, though, a *shechita* of *kodshim* — even proper *kodshim* properly slaughtered inside the *Beit Hamikdash* — is itself a *shechita she'eina re'uya*, because the *shechita* alone does not render the meat permissible. Permissibility requires *zerikas ha-dam* (the sprinkling of blood on the altar). Until that is done, the meat remains forbidden. If the very act of *shechita* does not by itself permit the meat, it does not qualify as a proper *shechita* for purposes of other laws. Therefore, the first *shechita* — even of a valid *korban* properly conducted — would not count, the second animal has no *oto ve-et beno* constraint, and the second person should not receive lashes.

### Kodshim Shechita Status

The Gemara anticipates an objection: is it truly obvious that *shechitas kodshim* is a *shechita she'eina re'uya* in Rabbi Shimon's sense? One might think otherwise. After all, if you were simply to kill the animal without performing *shechita* — by stabbing it, for example — you would not be permitted to perform *zerikas ha-dam* at all. The fact that a proper *shechita* is a prerequisite for *zerikas ha-dam*, and that *zerikas ha-dam* ultimately renders the meat permissible, would seem to demonstrate that *shechitas kodshim* is indeed a valid, efficacious *shechita*. It is not like other cases of *shechita she'eina re'uya* where the slaughter is categorically futile; here it is a stage in the process of permitting the meat.

The Gemara responds that this was precisely the point that needed to be established. It would have been reasonable to assume that Rabbi Shimon exempts *shechitas kodshim* from his principle — that he would acknowledge it as a *shechita re'uya* for purposes of other laws, given its integral role in the *korban* process. The Gemara therefore emphasizes that Rabbi Shimon's position, as the Gemara understands it, extends even to *shechitas kodshim*: since the act of *shechita* alone, without the subsequent *zerikah*, does not make the meat permissible, it does not count as *shechita* for external purposes such as *oto ve-et beno*. This is admittedly a striking position, since *shechitas kodshim* is obviously not a futile act — it is the essential gateway to the entire *korban* process. Yet Rabbi Shimon, apparently, measures *shechita* for external purposes by a single criterion: does this act, by itself, permit the meat?

### Mechusar Zman Lashes

One final point is raised at the close of the daf. The Gemara notes that in the case of *kodshim bi-fnim* — where the second person slaughters a *korban* in the *Beit Hamikdash* on the same day as the mother or offspring — the slaughterer not only transgresses *oto ve-et beno* but apparently also brings a *korban* that is *mechusar zman*: not yet eligible to be brought. The *Torah*'s language, *mi-yom ha-shemini va-hal'ah yeiratzeh* — from the eighth day onward it shall be accepted — is read not merely as descriptive but as a prohibition: one may not slaughter as a *korban* an animal that is not yet eligible, whether because it is under eight days old or because, as here, *oto ve-et beno* renders it ineligible that day. Why then does the *mishnah* not mention lashes for *mechusar zman* in addition to lashes for *oto ve-et beno*?

The Gemara draws on a *beraitah*: *minnayin le-chol ha-pesulim she-ba-shor u-va-seh she-hu be-lo yeiratzeh* — how do we know that all disqualifications for ox and sheep fall under the prohibition of *lo yeiratzeh* (it shall not be accepted, which is read as a prohibition)? The *Torah* itself teaches: *ve-shor va-seh saru'a ve-kalut nedavah ta'aseh oto u-le-neder lo yeiratzeh* — a blemished animal may be offered as a donation but not as a vow-fulfillment. From this we derive that anything ineligible is covered by the prohibition of *lo yeiratzeh*. So the slaughterer of the second animal should seemingly receive lashes both for *oto ve-et beno* and for *mechusar zman*.

The Gemara's answer rests on a distinction between *lavay nuchray* — independent, unrelated prohibitions — and prohibitions that are intertwined with the transgression itself. Where the person already receives lashes for *oto ve-et beno*, the *mishnah* does not trouble to enumerate the additional lashes from *mechusar zman*, since both arise from the same act. The *mishnah* mentions *lavay nuchray* — foreign lashes that have an independent source — in specific cases: the first person who slaughters *kodshim ba-chutz*, for example, receives lashes for *shechutei chutz*, which is an independent prohibition unrelated to *oto ve-et beno*. That is noted. But where lashes for *mechusar zman* would be triggered alongside lashes for *oto ve-et beno* in the same act of slaughter, the *mishnah* records only one set of lashes without itemizing the other. The second person is in fact liable for both, but the *mishnah*'s silence about *mechusar zman* lashes reflects a stylistic choice, not an exoneration.
