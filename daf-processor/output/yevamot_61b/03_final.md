# Yevamot 61b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aylonit & Zonah

### Aylonit Zonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט לֹא יִשָּׂא אַיְלוֹנִית, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים לֹא יִשָּׂא אַיְלוֹנִית, שֶׁהִיא זוֹנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּתּוֹרָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין זוֹנָה אֶלָּא גִּיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת וְשֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A common priest may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman [*aylonit*],** who is incapable of bearing children, **unless he** already **has a wife and children. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if **he has a wife and children, he may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman, as she is the *zona*** about whom it is **stated in the Torah** that a priest may not marry her. Intercourse with her is considered a licentious act because she is incapable of bearing children. **And the Rabbis say: The only** women in the category of ***zona*,** who are therefore forbidden to a priest, are **a female convert, a freed** maidservant, **and** any woman **who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse** with a man she is prohibited from marrying.

We open at the top of 61b with the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who rules that an *aylonit* — a woman who is not merely infertile but whose very physical development places her categorically outside the realm of childbearing — is forbidden to a *kohen* on the grounds that she falls into the category of *zonah*. The underlying principle is that sexual relations in a context entirely removed from procreation, even in the broadest theoretical sense, constitutes an act of *zenut*. This is a significant and particular rabbinic attitude; it is certainly not the only such attitude toward marital sex, and it will bear heavily on the discussions that follow.

The *gemara* then cites Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that a *kohen* may not marry a *ketanah*. Notably, the *gemara* does not suggest that a *ketanah* is herself a *zonah*, nor does it characterize relations with her as *bi'as zenut* — presumably because the larger context of procreation remains theoretically within the picture. So the prohibition cannot rest on the same logic Rabbi Yehuda applied to an *aylonit*.

## Ketanah Prohibition

### Rabba: Minority Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לְרַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, אַפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה כֹּהֲנִים הוּא דְּמִפַּקְּדִי וְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא מִפַּקְּדִי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי סֵיפָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים — לֹא יִשָּׂא אַיְלוֹנִית, שֶׁהִיא זוֹנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּתּוֹרָה. דְּאַזּוֹנָה כֹּהֲנִים הוּא דְּמִפַּקְדִי וְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא מִפַּקְּדִי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Exilarch said to Rav Huna: What is the reason** for the *halakha* that a priest may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman? It is **because** he is obligated to fulfill the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply. Is it** only **priests who were commanded to be fruitful and multiply, but Israelites were not commanded?** Why does the mishna specify that a priest may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman? Rav Huna **said to him:** This *halakha* does in fact apply even to Israelites, and the *tanna* mentions priests **because he wants to teach** it in a way that would parallel **the latter clause** of the mishna, which states that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if **he has a wife and he has children, he may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman, as she is the *zona*** about whom it is **stated in the Torah** that a priest may not marry her. **It is priests who were commanded** not to marry **a *zona*, but Israelites were not commanded** this. It is **due to that** reason that **he taught** the first clause of the mishna about **a priest,** even though that *halakha* applies equally to Israelites.

Rabba offers the first explanation: the prohibition is constructed from a combination of two Tannaitic positions — Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that an *aylonit* is forbidden to a *kohen*, and Rabbi Meir's principle of *chayesh l'miyuta*, concern for minority likelihoods. Put together: a *kohen* should not marry a *ketanah* because she might grow up to become an *aylonit*.

## Aylonit & Zonah…

### Rabbi Eliezer & Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כֹּהֵן לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה: פּוֹק עַיֵּין בָּהּ, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא בָּעֵי לַהּ רַב הוּנָא מִינָּךְ. נְפַק עַיֵּין בָּהּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא. וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: אַיְלוֹנִית זוֹנָה הָוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A priest may not marry a minor. Rav Ḥisda said to Rabba: Go** and **investigate** this *halakha*, **as in the evening Rav Huna** will **ask you** the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. **He went** and **investigated it,** and arrived at the following conclusion: **Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and he** also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. Rabba explains: He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who** says that **one must be concerned for the minority.** Rabbi Meir does not allow one to assume that an unknown case is similar to the majority of cases. Consequently, one must take into account the possibility that a minor will turn out to be sexually underdeveloped, although this will not be true of most individuals. **And** he also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that **a sexually underdeveloped woman is a *zona*** and therefore forbidden to a priest.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this construction. Does Rabbi Eliezer actually hold like Rabbi Meir? The challenge comes from a *baraita*: *katan v'ketanah lo choltzin v'lo meyabmin divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir rules that a minor, whether boy or girl, cannot perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*. When challenged on the *yibum* ruling — since *yibum* does not require intent and the *gemara* has established that relations with a girl over three years old and a boy over nine years old constitute a halachic act of intercourse — Rabbi Meir responds: *katan shema yimtza saris*, perhaps the minor will turn out to be a *saris*, and *ketanah shema timtza aylonit*, perhaps the girl will turn out to be an *aylonit*. In such a case, if either party is categorically excluded from the realm of procreation, there is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* — and without a *mitzvah* of *yibum*, the act retroactively becomes a forbidden relation with a sister-in-law, an *isur* of *arayos*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִי סָבַר לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: קָטָן וּקְטַנָּה לֹא חוֹלְצִין וְלֹא מְיַבְּמִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: יָפֶה אָמַרְתָּ שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְצִין. ״אִישׁ״ כְּתִיב בַּפָּרָשָׁה, וּמַקְּשִׁינַן אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ. אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אֵין מְיַבְּמִין? [אָמַר לָהֶם:] קָטָן — שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא סָרִיס, קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּמָּצֵא אַיְלוֹנִית, וְנִמְצְאוּ פּוֹגְעִין בְּעֶרְוָה. וְתַנְיָא: קְטַנָּה מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת וְאֵינָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges Rabba's explanation: **And does** Rabbi Eliezer **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A boy minor and a girl minor may not perform *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: You spoke well** when you said **that they may not perform *ḥalitza*,** as the term **"man" is written in the passage** of *ḥalitza* (Deuteronomy 25:7–10), which limits the *halakha* to an adult male, **and we compare a woman to a man** and therefore limit *ḥalitza* to an adult woman. **However, what is the reason** that **they may not perform levirate marriage? Rabbi Meir said to them: A boy minor** may not perform levirate marriage **lest he be found** to be **a eunuch,** i.e., one who is incapable of fathering children for his late brother. Similarly, **a girl minor** may not perform levirate marriage **lest she be found** to be **sexually underdeveloped** when she grows up. In either case, the mitzva of levirate marriage does not apply, **and they turn out** to have **encountered a forbidden relative. And it was taught** in a different *baraita*: **A girl minor enters into levirate marriage but does not perform *ḥalitza*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.** This proves that Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with Rabbi Meir and is not concerned that a girl may turn out to be sexually underdeveloped.

It is worth pausing here to note what Rabbi Meir does and does not say. He does not invoke the worry *shema timtza akarah* — perhaps she will turn out to be simply infertile. The concern is specifically the categorical status of *aylonit* and *saris*, which implies something more fundamental than merely practical inability to conceive. This connects directly to why, in the earlier discussion, an *aylonit* is considered a *zonah*: the exclusion is categorical and essential, not merely contingent.

The *gemara* now adduces a further *baraita* to show that Rabbi Elazar (the authority whose prohibition of a *kohen* marrying a *ketanah* we are trying to explain) does not hold like Rabbi Meir: *ketanah me'ana bo be-emet ve-eina choletzet divrei Rabbi Elazar* — Rabbi Elazar holds that a *ketanah* can perform *yibum* but not *chalitzah*, the latter being excluded because the Torah specifies *ish*, an adult male, and the woman is *hukshah* to him. Rabbi Elazar's willingness to allow *yibum* by a *ketanah* demonstrates that he is not concerned with the minority possibility of her becoming an *aylonit*. This disposes of Rabba's construction at the first step: Rabbi Elazar clearly does not hold like Rabbi Meir.

### Definitions of Zonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִי סָבַר לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״זוֹנָה״ — זוֹנָה כִּשְׁמָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״זוֹנָה״ זוֹ מוּפְקֶרֶת. רַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לְהַשְׁקוֹתָהּ, וּבָא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ — עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: זוֹנָה — זוֹ אַיְלוֹנִית. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין זוֹנָה אֶלָּא גִּיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת וְשֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: פָּנוּי הַבָּא עַל הַפְּנוּיָה שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת — עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to challenge Rabba's explanation of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. **And does** Rabbi Eliezer **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Wasn't it is taught in** a *baraita*: The ***zona*** forbidden to a priest is as **the name *zona*** implies, i.e., a married woman who committed adultery; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Akiva says: A *zona* is** a woman, even an unmarried woman, **who** is **available** to all, i.e., she has intercourse with whoever is interested. **Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash says: Even** if **her husband went to make her drink** the bitter waters after she disregarded his warning not to seclude herself with a certain man, and **he had intercourse with her on the way, he has** thereby **caused her** to become **a *zona*** because she was forbidden to him at the time, despite the fact that she is his wife. **Rabbi Yehuda says: A *zona* is a sexually underdeveloped woman. And the Rabbis say:** The term ***zona*** applies **only to a female convert, a freed** maidservant, **and one who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse. Rabbi Elazar says:** Even in the case of **an unmarried man who had intercourse with an unmarried woman not for the purpose of marriage, he has** thereby **caused her** to become **a *zona*.** This *baraita* proves that Rabbi Eliezer does not agree with Rabbi Yehuda.

The *baraita* presents a striking range of Tannaitic opinions on the biblical category of *zonah* as it applies to a *kohen*, with almost no *gemara* analysis to sort through them — and yet with enormous practical implications for *kohanim* today.

The first opinion: *zona zona k'shma* — "a *zonah* is as her name implies" — stated by Rabbi Eliezer. The second: *zona zu mufkeret*, said by Rabbi Akiva — a *zonah* is a woman who has sex indiscriminately, not necessarily for pay but promiscuously. Rabbi Matya ben Charash says: even if a husband is on his way to administer the *sotah* waters to his wife and another man has relations with her along the way — even this act of forbidden sex, occurring within the marital context and technically prohibited only on account of *safek* (suspicion), renders her a *zonah*. Rabbi Yehuda says: *zona zu aylonit* — as we saw in the *mishnah*. And the Sages say: *ein zona ela giyoret u'meshuchreret v'shenib'ala beilat zenut* — a *zonah* is only a convert, a freed slave, or a woman who has had a forbidden sexual encounter.

### Rashi on Zonah

Rashi's comment on the phrase *zona zona k'shma* — Rabbi Eliezer's position — is quite unexpected. Rather than reading it as a reference to a prostitute, Rashi explains: *lashon to'eh she-to'eh mi-tachat ba'alah la-acherim de-le-Rabbi Eliezer ein zona ela eshet ish* — a *zonah* is a woman who has strayed from her husband to other men; according to Rabbi Eliezer, *zona* means specifically a married woman who committed adultery. This is a strikingly narrow definition and not the reading one would naturally assume from the phrase *zona k'shma*.

The Sages' category of *beilat zenut* is narrower than Rabbi Matya ben Charash's formulation in one respect: while Rabbi Matya includes violations that are prohibited only rabbinicly or by reason of doubt, the Sages appear to require either an *isur kares* or an *isur kehunah*-level violation. (Though one might note that if she has already violated an *isur kehunah*, the *kohen* question is moot, so that prong may better be understood as pertaining to *chalalah*.) The *giyoret u'meshuchreret* category reflects concerns either about sexual history with non-Jews or a more fundamental perspective on the non-Jewish world.

### Eliezer: Non-Marital Sex

Turning now to Rabbi Elazar's own position in the *baraita*: *panui haba al pnuyah shelo leshem ishut as'ah zona* — any act of extra-marital intercourse, even between two unattached individuals and even in the context of a committed relationship, renders the woman a *zonah* forbidden to a *kohen*. This is in one sense even more expansive than Rabbi Akiva's *mufkeret*, which requires promiscuity; Rabbi Elazar requires only that the sex occur outside of marriage.

This is important for resolving the *gemara*'s investigation. Rabbi Eliezer — whose position we are trying to explain — states *zona zona k'shma*, which, as Rashi reads it, means a woman who committed adultery. That definition does not encompass an *aylonit* at all. We have now established both prongs: Rabbi Elazar does not hold like Rabbi Meir (from the *yibum* case), and Rabbi Eliezer does not hold like Rabbi Yehuda (from the definition of *zonah*). Rabba's combined construction is therefore doubly disproven.

### Tosafot: Cheating Znut

*Tosafot* adds a pointed observation on Rabbi Elazar's formulation of *panui haba al pnuyah*. The *Tosafot* notes: *lav davka nakat panui v'chol shekein nasui* — the word *panui* (single man) is not precise; the ruling applies all the more forcefully if the man is married. The reason: *shemizanneh al ishto d'chashuva zona* — because he is being unfaithful to his wife, which makes the act of *zenut* even more severe.

What is striking here is that *Tosafot* introduces the moral concept of marital fidelity as a category that intensifies the character of the forbidden act — and this in a halachic framework that, in principle, did not prohibit polygamy and in which the prohibitions on sexual relations were asymmetrical between husband and wife. The notion that a man is *mizanneh al ishto* by having sex with another woman almost certainly reflects the reality of *Ashkenaz* after *Cherem de-Rabbeinu Gershom* and the internalization of monogamous norms, such that extramarital sex by a married man is characterized as a betrayal of his wife.

## Ketanah Prohibition…

### Kohen Gadol Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָכָא בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עָסְקִינַן. לְאֵימַת קָנֵי לַה, לְכִי גָדְלָה — בְּעוּלָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that a priest may not marry a minor must be explained differently: **Here we are dealing with a High Priest,** and the problem is as follows: **When can he acquire her** as his wife? Only **when she is grown up.** However, if they had started living together as husband and wife when she was a minor, then when she is grown up and the marriage can legally take effect, **she is** already **a non-virgin,** and a High Priest is commanded to marry a virgin.

Having exhausted Rabba's construction, the *gemara* turns to a second explanation. Rav Adda bar Ahava suggests: *hacha b'kohen gadol askinan* — we are dealing with a *kohen gadol*. A *kohen gadol* may not marry a *bogeret* (a fully mature woman past *na'arut*). The concern is the timing of *kiddushin*: if a *ketanah* were to arrange her own marriage to a *kohen gadol* (without her father's involvement), *kiddushin* would not take legal effect until she reaches adulthood — at which point she is already a *be'ulah* (no longer a virgin), disqualifying her for a *kohen gadol*.

### Fituy & Ones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְכַלֵּי לֵב, אִי דְּקַדְּשַׁהּ אֲבוּהּ — מֵהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הוּא דְּקָנֵי לַהּ. וְאִי דְּקַדִּשָׁה נַפְשַׁהּ — הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט — וְחָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תִּתְפַּתֶּה עָלָיו. אִי הָכִי יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי! פִּתּוּיי קְטַנָּה אוֹנֶס הוּא, וְאוֹנֶס בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִישְׁרָא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This explanation is **without reason. If her father betrothed her** to her husband, her husband **acquired her from that time,** as betrothal that a father carries out on his daughter's behalf when she is a minor is effective by Torah law. **And if** the minor **betrothed herself,** is **this Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion **and not** that of **the Rabbis?** The Rabbis would certainly agree that a High Priest may not marry a minor under these circumstances. **Rather, Rava said: Actually,** Rabbi Eliezer's ruling includes **a common priest,** and the reason he cannot marry a minor is that **we are concerned lest she be seduced** by another man, due to her tender age and naïveté, while married **to him.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the same concern should apply to **an Israelite also.** The Gemara answers: **The seduction of a minor is** considered **rape, and a rape** victim remains **permitted** to her husband **in** a case where she is married to **an Israelite,** but not if she is married to a priest.

Rava rejects this immediately: *michlei lev* — this was said without thinking. If her father arranged the marriage, there is no issue at all; and if she arranged it herself, that would be a universal problem, not specific to Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. Rava then offers his own explanation: *l'olam b'kohen hedyot askinan* — we return to an ordinary *kohen*. The concern is *de-chayshinan shema tispateh alav* — we worry that she will be seduced by another man, thus rendering her an *eshet ish* who committed adultery, making her a *zonah* and therefore forbidden to her *kohen* husband.

The *gemara* objects: if so, why shouldn't even a non-*kohen* avoid marrying a *ketanah*? The answer is: *pituyei ketanah ones hu* — seduction of a minor is legally equivalent to rape (*ones*), non-volitional. And *ones b'Yisrael mishra shari* — if the wife of a non-*kohen* is raped, she remains permitted to her husband. But if she is the wife of a *kohen*, it makes no difference whether the intercourse was voluntary or coerced; she becomes forbidden to him. Hence the prohibition applies specifically to a *kohen*.

### Kohen Gadol Na'arah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְהַאי תַּנָּא הוּא דְּתַנְיָא: ״בְּתוּלָה״, יָכוֹל קְטַנָּה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִשָּׁה״. אִי אִשָּׁה, יָכוֹל בּוֹגֶרֶת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּתוּלָה״. הָא כֵּיצַד? יָצְתָה מִכְּלַל קַטְנוּת, וְלִכְלַל בַּגְרוּת לֹא בָּאתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Rabbi Eliezer's ruling applies specifically **to a High Priest, and it is** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states: **"A virgin of his own people shall he take for a wife [*isha*]" (Leviticus 21:14), one **might** have thought a High Priest may marry **a minor; the verse** therefore **states** that he must marry **a woman [*isha*],** i.e., an adult. **If** he must marry a **woman,** one **might** have thought it means **a grown woman. The verse** therefore **states** that he must marry **a virgin,** which excludes a grown woman, who is considered only a partial virgin because her hymen is not fully intact. **How so?** He must marry a woman who **has left the class of minority but** who **has not** yet **reached the class of grown womanhood,** i.e., he must marry a maiden.

Rav Papa returns to the *kohen gadol* explanation but with a different source. He cites a *baraita* that derives from the verse *ve-hu ishah be-visuleha* that a *kohen gadol* must marry a *be-sulah* who is an *ishah* — an adult. *Yachol ketanah?* Could she be a minor? No — *talmud lomar ishah*. *Yachol bogeret?* Could she be fully mature? No — *talmud lomar be-sulah*, which implies intact *betulim*, characteristic of a *na'arah* rather than a *bogeret*. The result: *ha ketzad? yatza mikhlal katnut ve-lo ba le-khlal bagrus* — she must be in the intermediate stage, neither *ketanah* nor *bogeret*, which is a *na'arah* between twelve and twelve and a half years of age. According to this Tanna, the *kohen gadol* may only marry a woman during that narrow window.

## Aylonit & Zonah……

### Ruling Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: פָּנוּי הַבָּא עַל הַפְּנוּיָה שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת — עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה. אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cited above mentioned that **Rabbi Elazar says:** In the case of **an unmarried man who had intercourse with an unmarried woman not for the purpose of marriage, he has caused her** to become **a *zona*. Rav Amram said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

The *sugiyah* concludes with Rav Amram's ruling: *ein halacha k'Rabbi Elazar* — we do not rule like Rabbi Elazar's position that a woman who has had any non-marital sexual encounter is a *zonah* forbidden to a *kohen*. This is practically significant: questions about whether a *kohen* may marry a woman who has had premarital relations do arise, and Rabbi Elazar's formulation would forbid such a marriage categorically. We do not rule that way. We rule, rather, like the *chachamim* that *zona* means only a *giyoret*, a freed slave, or a woman who has engaged in *beilat zenut* in the more restricted sense.

The question of whether an *aylonit* remains forbidden to a *kohen* as a *zona* is left somewhat open: since we rule like the *chachamim* and not like Rabbi Yehuda, and an *aylonit* does not appear in the *chachamim*'s definition, she would presumably not be prohibited on grounds of *zona*.

This entire discussion — the definition of *zenut*, sex in a non-procreative context, and the *aylonit* — serves as a natural bridge into the *mishnah* before us, which takes up the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* in earnest. It is worth noting that the primary *gemara* on *pru urvu* is found here in Yevamot rather than in Kiddushin or Ketubot, which makes a certain sense: Yevamot deals extensively with personal status and with the legal dimensions of sexual relations, and it is within that frame that the *mitzvah* of having children is most fully analyzed.

## Pru Urvu Basics

### Obligation & Cessation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יִבָּטֵל אָדָם מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בְּרָאָם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A man may not neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply unless he** already **has children. Beit Shammai say:** One fulfills this mitzva with **two males, and Beit Hillel say: A male and a female, as it is stated: "Male and female He created them"** (Genesis 5:2).

The *mishnah* opens: *lo yivatel adam mi-piryah ve-rivyah* — a man must not cease from the *mitzvah* of having children *ela im kein yesh lo banim* — unless he already has children. The phrasing is notable. Rather than stating the threshold at which one has *yatza* the *mitzvah*, the *mishnah* frames it as a prohibition on stopping — which captures something important. *Pru urvu* is not a discrete act like taking a *lulav*; it is an ongoing obligation that some people pursue for years without success. The *mishnah* may therefore be addressing the phase of continued effort: do not abandon the endeavor until you have fulfilled the obligation.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

How many children constitute fulfillment? *Beit Shammai omrim shnei zecharim* — two males. *Beit Hillel omrim zachar u-nekeva* — a male and a female, *she-ne'emar zachar u-nekeva bera'am* — as it is said, "male and female He created them."

### Hillel: Imitatio Dei

Beit Hillel's reasoning, grounded in the verse *zachar u-nekeva bera'am*, points to two distinct rationales simultaneously. On a straightforward level, having one son and one daughter ensures that the world continues to be populated — each child represents a gender necessary for future generations. But the citation of the verse from creation suggests something deeper: the fulfillment of *pru urvu* is not only about achieving a demographic goal but about replicating the original divine act of creation. To bring a male and a female into the world is, in some sense, an act of *imitatio dei*, a participation in the creative act by which God populated the world with *zachar u-nekeva*.

Beit Shammai, by requiring two males, clearly has a different conception of the *mitzvah*'s purpose. One might ask: doesn't requiring only sons skew the population? In practice, the birth rate implied by Beit Hillel's ruling is also above two per couple, since those who have two daughters or two sons must continue. But Beit Shammai appears unconcerned with population balance, suggesting their conception of the *mitzvah* is not fundamentally about the continued settlement of the world in the way Beit Hillel understands it.

## Marriage Intrinsic Value

### Rav Adda: Must Stay Married

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה בָּטֵיל, מֵאִשָּׁה לָא בָּטֵיל. מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְאָדָם כַּמָּה בָּנִים, אָסוּר לַעֲמוֹד בְּלֹא אִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא טוֹב הֱיוֹת הָאָדָם לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the mishna's wording that if **he** already **has children he may neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply,** but **he may not neglect** the mitzva to have **a wife.** This **supports** what **Rav Naḥman said** in the name of **Shmuel, who said: Even** if **a man has several children, it is prohibited to remain without a wife, as it is stated: "It is not good that the man should be alone"** (Genesis 2:18).

The *gemara* infers from the *mishnah* that once a man has children — *batel mi-pru urvu* — his obligation regarding *pru urvu* is fulfilled; however, *mi-ishah lo batel* — he does not cease his obligation to remain married. This inference is said to support the position of Rav Nachman bar Shmuel: *af al pi she-yesh lo le-adam kama banim, assur la'amod belo ishah* — even a man who already has several children is forbidden to remain without a wife if his wife dies or they divorce.

### Lo Tov Levado

The source for this obligation is the verse *lo tov heyot ha-adam levado* — "it is not good for man to be alone." This framing is important. One might argue that a man should remarry simply to manage his own drives and avoid transgression. But Rav Adda's formulation grounds the obligation to be married not in an instrumental concern but in an intrinsic one: the state of marriage is a human good in itself, independent of its procreative function. This is a significant *gemara* for thinking about marriage and companionship as values in their own right.

### Alternative Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — בָּטֵיל מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, וּבָטֵיל נָמֵי מֵאִשָּׁה. נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל! לָא: אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה בַּת בָּנִים, יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה דְּלָאו בַּת בָּנִים. נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לִמְכּוֹר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בִּשְׁבִיל בָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** a different version of the inference from the mishna: If **he** already **has children, he may neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply and** he **may also neglect** the mitzva to have **a wife. Shall we say** this **is a conclusive refutation of** what **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel** said? The Gemara responds: **No,** it means that **if he does not have children he must marry a woman** capable **of** bearing **children,** whereas if **he has children he may marry a woman who is not** capable **of** bearing **children. A practical difference** between a man who has children and one who does not is whether he is permitted **to sell a Torah scroll in order to** marry a woman capable of having **children.** This is permitted only for one who does not yet have children.

The *gemara* then offers an alternative reading of the *mishnah*: *ika de-amri*, some say the *mishnah* implies both that the obligation regarding *pru urvu* ceases *and* that the obligation to remain married ceases — *batel mi-pru urvu u-vatel min ha-ishah*. Under this reading, the *mishnah* would be a challenge to Rav Nachman bar Shmuel's position.

The logic underlying this alternative reading rests on the practical reality of a society without effective contraception. How does a married man stop having children? The only plausible mechanism is by not being married. So the *mishnah*'s statement that one may stop once the *mitzvah* is fulfilled is implicitly a statement about not having to remarry.

### Infertile Partner OK

The *gemara* rejects this challenge: *lo* — it is not a contradiction. The distinction is as follows. A man who has not yet fulfilled *pru urvu* must marry a woman capable of bearing children. Once he has fulfilled *pru urvu*, the *mishnah* is telling him that he need not restrict his choice of wife to a fertile woman — *nosei ishah delav bat banim* — he may marry a woman without fertility potential. He must still marry, but the fertility requirement no longer constrains his choice.

*Tosafot* adds a further *nafka mina*: the *gemara* elsewhere teaches that the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* is so weighty that one would even sell a *sefer Torah* to obtain the funds necessary to marry and fulfill it. That extreme measure applies only before one has fulfilled the *mitzvah*. After *yatza hamitzvah*, the *mishnah*'s declaration that one is *batel mi-piryah ve-rivyah* means he need not take such measures — but it does not mean he is exempt from marriage altogether.

## Pru Urvu Basics…

### Shammai's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? יָלְפִינַן מִמֹּשֶׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּנֵי מֹשֶׁה גֵּרְשׁוֹם וֶאֱלִיעֶזֶר״. וּבֵית הִלֵּל: יָלְפִינַן מִבְּרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם. וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, לֵילְפֵי מִבְּרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם? אֵין דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Beit Shammai say** that one fulfills the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply when he has **two males.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Beit Shammai?** The Gemara answers: **We learn** this **from Moses as it is written: "The sons of Moses, Gershom and Eliezer"** (I Chronicles 23:15). Since Moses did not have any other children, two sons must be sufficient to fulfill the mitzva. **And** the reason of **Beit Hillel** is that **we learn from the creation of the world,** as mankind was created male and female. The Gemara asks: **And Beit Shammai, let them learn from the creation of the world** as well. The Gemara answers that Beit Shammai could say to you: **We do not derive** a case where it is **possible from** one **that is not possible.**

The *gemara* returns to the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. *Mai ta'ama de-Beit Shammai?* — What is Beit Shammai's reasoning? *Yalfina mi-Moshe* — they derive it from Moshe, who had two sons, Gershom and Eliezer. Presumably Moshe would not have stopped had he not been *yatza* the *mitzvah*, and since he stopped with two boys, the fulfillment requires two males.

But why does Beit Shammai not learn from creation — *beri'ato shel olam* — as Beit Hillel does? The *gemara* answers: *ein danin efshar mi-she-iy efshar* — one cannot derive a ruling for the possible from the impossible. God's creation required both male and female for the world to begin; there was no alternative. That extraordinary situation cannot serve as a normative model for individual obligation in an ongoing populated world.

### Hillel & Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל נָמֵי, לֵילְפוּ מִמֹּשֶׁה! אָמְרִי לָךְ: מֹשֶׁה מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ הוּא דַּעֲבַד. דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים עָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה מִדַּעְתּוֹ, וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם: פֵּירַשׁ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, וְשִׁיבֵּר הַלּוּחוֹת, וְהוֹסִיף יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Beit Hillel, let them also learn from Moses.** Beit Hillel could **say to you: Moses acted** based **on his own perception** when he separated from his wife, but this does not mean that a man is permitted to neglect the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply after fathering two males, **as it is taught in** a *baraita*: **Moses did three things** based **on his own perception, and his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent: He separated from** his **wife, he broke the tablets, and he added one day** to the days of separation before the revelation at Sinai.

Conversely, why does Beit Hillel not derive the requirement from Moshe's two sons? The *gemara* answers: *Moshe mi-da'atei hu de-avad* — Moshe acted on his own judgment in ways that were exceptional and unrepeatable. He may not have been *mekayem pru urvu* in the conventional sense at all; *ve-hiskim da'ato le-da'at ha-Makom* — his reasoning aligned with God's will, but it was not a model for others.

### Moshe's 3 Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּירַשׁ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? אֲמַר: וּמָה יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁלֹּא דִּבְּרָה עִמָּהֶם שְׁכִינָה אֶלָּא לְפִי שָׁעָה, וְקָבַע לָהֶם זְמַן, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״אַל תִּגְּשׁוּ אֶל אִשָּׁה״ — אֲנִי, שֶׁמְּיוּחָד לְדִבּוּר בְּכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה, וְלֹא קָבַע לִי זְמַן — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם שׁוּבוּ לָכֶם לְאׇהֳלֵיכֶם. וְאַתָּה פֹּה עֲמוֹד עִמָּדִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: When Moses **separated from** his **wife** after the revelation at Sinai, **what did he interpret** that led him to do so? **He said: If** in the case of **Israel, with whom the Divine Presence spoke only temporarily and for whom** God **set** a specific **time for** revelation, **the Torah stated: "Do not approach a woman"** (Exodus 19:15), **I,** Moses, **who am set aside for** divine **speech all the time and for whom** God **did not set** a specific **time, all the more so** I must separate from my wife. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as it is stated** after the revelation at Sinai: **"Go say to them: Return to your tents; and you, stand here with Me"** (Deuteronomy 5:26–27). This indicates that whereas others could return to their homes and normal married life after the revelation at Sinai, Moses was to stay with God and not return to his wife.

The *gemara* elaborates on three things Moshe did independently — *shlosha devarim asa Moshe mi-da'ato ve-hiskim da'ato le-da'at ha-Makom*:

The first is *pirash min ha-isha* — he separated permanently from his wife after *Matan Torah*. His reasoning: the Jewish people were told to abstain from relations only for the brief period before God spoke to them at Sinai, and the Torah says *al tikrevu el isha* for that duration. Moshe, however, was always potentially available for divine communication — God could speak to him at any moment, without advance notice. Therefore he concluded he must remain in a constant state of readiness, which required permanent separation from his wife. How do we know God ratified this? From the verse *lechu imru lahem shuvu lachem le-oholeichem* — go tell them to return to their tents (i.e., resume marital relations) — *ve-attah poh amod imadi* — but you, stand here with Me, implying an ongoing, not merely temporary, condition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיבֵּר אֶת הַלּוּחוֹת, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? אָמַר: וּמָה פֶּסַח, שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד מִשֵּׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִצְוֹת, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״. הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּדִים — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר שִׁבַּרְתָּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: יִישַׁר כֹּחֲךָ שֶׁשִּׁבַּרְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** Moses **broke the tablets** following the sin of the Golden Calf. **What did he interpret** that led him to do so? Moses **said: If** in the case of the **Paschal lamb, which is** only **one of 613 mitzvot, the Torah states: "No alien shall eat of it"** (Exodus 12:43), excluding not only gentiles but apostate Jews as well, then here, in the case of the Golden Calf, where the tablets represent **the entire Torah and** where **the Jewish people** are **apostates,** as they are worshipping the calf, **all the more so** must they be excluded from receiving them. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as it is written:** "The first tablets **that you broke [*asher shibbarta*]"** (Exodus 34:1), **and Reish Lakish said:** The word *asher* is an allusion to the fact that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: May your strength be true [*yishar koḥakha*] that you broke** the tablets.

The second is *ve-shiber ha-luchot* — he broke the *luchot*. His reasoning: *u-mah Pesach she-hu echad mi-shesh me'ot u-shlosh esreh mitzvos* — the Pesach offering, which is merely one of 613 commandments, is restricted so that *kol ben nechar lo yochal bo* — a foreigner may not eat from it, and this is understood halachically as referring to one who has distanced himself from God. How much more so, he reasoned, should the entire Torah — represented by the *luchot* — not be given to a people who have become *mumarim*? God's ratification is indicated by the verse *asher shibarta* — Reish Lakish reads this as *yishar kochacha she-shibarta*, an expression of divine approval for the breaking.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹסִיף יוֹם אֶחָד מִדַּעְתּוֹ, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתָּם הַיּוֹם וּמָחָר״. הַיּוֹם כְּמָחָר: מָה מָחָר לֵילוֹ עִמּוֹ, אַף הַיּוֹם לֵילוֹ עִמּוֹ. וְלַיְלָה דְּהָאִידָּנָא נְפַק לֵיהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי לְבַר מֵהָאִידָּנָא. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם — דְּלָא שָׁרְיָא שְׁכִינָה עַד שַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** When Moses **added one day** to the days of separation before the revelation at Sinai based **on his** own **perception, what did he interpret** that led him to do so? He reasoned **that** since **it is written: "And sanctify them today and tomorrow"** (Exodus 19:10), the juxtaposition of the words "today" and "tomorrow" teaches that **today** is **like tomorrow: Just as tomorrow** the men and women will separate for that day **and** the **night** preceding **it, so too, today** requires separation for the day **and** the **night** preceding **it.** Since God spoke to him in the morning, **and the night of that day** already **passed,** Moses said: **Conclude from** this that separation must be in effect for **two days aside from now,** i.e., not including the day of the command. Therefore, he extended the mitzva of separation by one day. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as** the **Divine Presence did not rest** upon Mount Sinai **until Shabbat** morning, as Moses had determined.

The third is *ve-hosif yom echad mi-da'ato* — he added one day of separation before *Matan Torah*. The verse says *ve-kiddashtam hayom u-machar* — prepare today and tomorrow. Moshe reasoned: just as tomorrow is a full day beginning from its preceding night, so too must today be a full day from its preceding night — but that night had already passed. Therefore two complete days were required from that point forward. God's approval is evident from the fact that the schedule proceeded without correction.

## Variant Baraitas

### Shammai: 2M 2F

A *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Natan presents a variant reading of Beit Shammai: *Beit Shammai omrim shenei zecharim u-shtei nekevot* — two males and two females. In this version, Beit Shammai agrees that gender parity matters for the purpose of *pru urvu*, but doubles the requirement: *peru* generates one boy and one girl; *revu* doubles it.

### Shammai/Hillel Variants

A second *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Natan presents yet another version: *Beit Shammai omrim zachar u-nekeva* (what we had known as the Beit Hillel position), and *Beit Hillel omrim o zachar o nekeva* — even one child of either sex suffices.

### Hevel & Sister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּשְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נָתָן אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתּוֹסֶף לָלֶדֶת אֶת אָחִיו אֶת הָבֶל״: הֶבֶל וַאֲחוֹתוֹ, קַיִן וַאֲחוֹתוֹ, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי שָׁת לִי אֱלֹהִים זֶרַע אַחֵר תַּחַת הָבֶל כִּי הֲרָגוֹ קָיִן״. וְרַבָּנַן? אוֹדוֹיֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹדְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught in** a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says** that **Beit Shammai say:** The mitzva to be fruitful and multiply is fulfilled with **two males and two females. And Beit Hillel say: A male and a female. Rav Huna said: What is the reason of Rabbi Natan, in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** It is **as it is written: "And again she bore his brother [*et aḥiv*] Abel [*et Hevel*]"** (Genesis 4:2). The use of the superfluous word "et" indicates that she gave birth to **Abel and his sister,** in addition to **Cain and his sister. And it states: "For God has appointed me another seed instead of Abel; for Cain slew him"** (Genesis 4:25). This indicates that one must have at least four children. **And the Rabbis,** how do they understand this verse? In their opinion, Eve **was thanking God** for granting her another child, but one is not obligated to have four children.

Rav Huna explains Rabbi Natan's version of Beit Shammai — two males and two females — from the verse *va-tosef laledet et achiv et Hevel*. The word *et* implies an accompanying sister; so Kayin had a sister and Hevel had a sister. When the *gemara* then reads *ki shat li Elohim zera acher tachat Hevel ki harago Kayin* — that God gave Chava another child to replace Hevel — it understands this as indicating she needed to restore the full complement: if *pru urvu* had been fulfilled with Hevel and his sister and Kayin and his sister, then losing Hevel meant the *mitzvah* was no longer fulfilled, and she needed to replace him. This implies the minimum is two boys and two girls.

The *gemara* objects: perhaps Chava simply wanted to mourn and replace Hevel as a human being she loved, not because she was no longer *mekayem* the *mitzvah*. The *rabbanan* respond: *oduyei hu de-kamodei* — she was simply thanking God for a new child. The verse need not be read as evidence about the parameters of *pru urvu* at all.

### Settlement World

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ זָכָר אוֹ נְקֵבָה. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נָתָן אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹהוּ בְרָאָהּ לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״, וְהָא עֲבַד לַהּ שֶׁבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says** that **Beit Shammai say:** The mitzva to be fruitful and multiply is fulfilled with **a male and a female. And Beit Hillel say: Either a male or a female. Rava said: What is the reason of Rabbi Natan in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel?** It is **as it is stated: "He did not create it a waste; He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18), **and one has made** the earth **inhabited** to a greater degree by adding even one child to the world.

Rava explains Rabbi Natan's version of Beit Hillel — even one child suffices — from the verse *lo tohu vera'ah lashevet yetzarah*: God did not create the world to be desolate but to be settled. The *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* is oriented toward *yishuv ha-olam* — the settling of the world — and even one child constitutes a contribution to that end. *Ve-ha avdei lah shevet* — having even one child is an act of settling the world.

What is striking about this *gemara* is that the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* is being analyzed almost entirely through the lens of its purpose rather than through the technical requirements one might expect. And the verse from *Yeshayahu* — *lo tohu vera'ah lashevet yetzarah* — appears to carry more weight in shaping these parameters than one might have anticipated. This approach of deriving halachic parameters from the purpose of a *mitzvah* is relatively uncommon; here it is the dominant mode of analysis.

## Pre-Conversion Children

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים בְּגַיּוּתוֹ וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over the following issue: If a man **had children when he** was **a gentile and he** subsequently **converted, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He has** already **fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply, and Reish Lakish said:** He has **not fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply.**

The *gemara* now takes up a new case: *hayu lo banim biyoto oveid kochavim ve-nitgayer* — a man who had children while he was a non-Jew and subsequently converted. The question is twofold: first, is he *mekayem pru urvu* through those children? Second — which turns out to require a separate analysis — does his first child born after conversion carry the status of *bechor* for purposes of inheritance?

### Yochanan: Yes

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *kiyam pru urvu* — yes, he has fulfilled the *mitzvah*. His reasoning encompasses two components: the children are biologically his, and even as a non-Jew he was within the framework of *pru urvu*.

### Reish Lakish: No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — דְּהָא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּקָטָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said he has fulfilled** the mitzva **to be fruitful and multiply, as he** already **had** children. **And Reish Lakish said he has not fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply,** as the legal status of **a convert who** just **converted is like** that of **a child** just **born.**

Reish Lakish disagrees: *lo kiyam pru urvu de-keivan she-nitgayer k'katan she-nolad dami* — upon conversion a person is like a newborn child; his prior legal relationships — including parenthood — are severed, and his pre-conversion children do not count.

### Two Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים בְּגַיּוּתוֹ וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין לוֹ בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה, דְּהָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ ״רֵאשִׁית אוֹנוֹ״. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: יֵשׁ לוֹ בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה, גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּקָטָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד דָּמֵי. וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָהִיא קַמַּיְיתָא, בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי בְּנֵי פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה נִינְהוּ, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן נַחֲלָה, דְּלָאו בְּנֵי נַחֲלָה נִינְהוּ — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And they follow their** regular line of reasoning, **as it was stated:** If **one had children** when **he** was **a gentile and he** subsequently **converted, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He does not have a firstborn with regard to inheritance,** i.e., the first son born to him after his conversion does not inherit a double portion, **as** this man already **had "the first of his strength"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), the Torah's description of the firstborn in this context, before he converted. **And Reish Lakish said: He does have a firstborn with regard to inheritance,** as the legal status of **a convert who** just **converted is like** that of **a child** just **born. The Gemara adds: And** it is **necessary** to state their opinions in both cases. **As, had it** only been **taught** to **us with regard to that first** case of the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply, one might have said that **it is** only **in that** case that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** his opinion, **because from the outset,** gentiles **are also subject to** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply. However, with regard to inheritance,** since **they are not subject** to the *halakhot* of **inheritance,** one might **say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan **concedes to Reish Lakish.**

The *gemara* establishes that both cases — *pru urvu* and *bechor* for inheritance — are necessary to state the dispute. If only the *pru urvu* case were stated, one might have thought Rabbi Yochanan rules as he does because non-Jews are themselves within the *parshah* of *pru urvu*, so the act of having children was already a fulfillment of the *mitzvah* even before conversion, and that fulfillment carries over. But in matters of inheritance — where the pre-conversion children have no halachic standing as legal heirs at all — one might have thought Rabbi Yochanan would concede to Reish Lakish. Conversely, if only the inheritance case were stated, one might have thought Reish Lakish concedes on *pru urvu* since it is ultimately about biological children. Stating both cases makes clear that they maintain their respective positions across both contexts.

### Non-Jews Obligated

This discussion carries an important implication: non-Jews are themselves obligated in *pru urvu*. This should not be surprising — God commands *pru urvu* first to Adam and Chava, then to Noach, making it a universal command. And yet there is a genuine *machlokes rishonim* on this question, partly because *pru urvu* does not appear explicitly among the *sheva mitzvos benei Noach*. Rashi here affirms the position clearly: *bnei Noach bnei pru urvu ninhu de-chtiv vayomer lahem pru urvu* — the sons of Noach are in the category of *pru urvu*, as it is written that God commanded them to be fruitful and multiply.

### Parentage Recognition

The *gemara* establishes a further distinction: non-Jews have legally recognized parentage — the example being *Berodach Baladan ben Baladan melech Bavel*, where a non-Jewish king is identified by his father's name, indicating halachic recognition of the parent-child relationship. Slaves, however, have no legally recognized parentage at all — only biological children, with no halachic *chiyus*. The source cited is *shvu lachem po im ha-chamor*, and the famous *drasha*: *am ha-domeh la-chamor* — those who are like donkeys. The point is not about all non-Jews but about the slave Avraham brought with him, indicating that a slave's children carry no legal relationship to him whatsoever.

An additional proof is drawn from the fact that non-Jewish parentage is traced not only one generation back but several: *vayishlach ha-melech Asa ben Hadad ben Tavrimon ben Chezyon melech Aram*, where three generations of ancestry are recorded for a non-Jewish king. This multi-generational tracing indicates a genuine, legally recognized family structure — distinct from the purely biological description that applies to a slave's offspring.

The practical upshot: when it comes to the question of whether a converted man's pre-conversion children count for *pru urvu*, they did at least exist within a halachically recognized legal family relationship as non-Jews. The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is therefore not about whether there was any prior status at all, but about whether conversion severs that prior status for purposes of the Jewish legal framework going forward.

## Children Predecease

### Huna: Fulfilled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים וּמֵתוּ, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over the following issue: If a man **had children and they died, Rav Huna said:** He has **fulfilled** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply** through these children. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He has **not fulfilled** the mitzva.

The *gemara* turns to a final question: *hayu lo banim u-metu* — a man had children who subsequently died. Is he *mekayem pru urvu*? Rav Huna says yes — *kiyem piryah u-rivyah* — because he had the children.

### Assi: Mashiach Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קִיֵּים — מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב אַסִּי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ כׇּל נְשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּגּוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי רוּחַ מִלְּפָנַי יַעֲטוֹף וְגוֹ׳״, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — ״לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the reasons for their opinions: **Rav Huna said** he has **fulfilled** the mitzva **due to** a statement **of Rav Asi, as Rav Asi said** that the reason for this mitzva is that the Messiah, **son of David, will not come until all the souls of the body have been finished,** i.e., until all souls that are destined to inhabit physical bodies will do so, **as it is stated: "For the spirit that enwraps itself is from Me,** and the souls that I have made" (Isaiah 57:16). Consequently, once a child has been born and his soul has entered a body the mitzva has been fulfilled, even if the child subsequently dies. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** he has **not fulfilled** the mitzva, as **we require "He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18), **and** this **is not** fulfilled when the children have passed away and no longer inhabit the earth.

Rav Huna's position rests on the view of Rabbi Assi: *ein Ben David ba ad she-yichlu kol ha-neshamot she-ba-guf* — the Mashiach will not come until all the souls stored in the heavenly *guf* (the divine repository of souls) have been used up, as the verse says *ki ruach mi-lefanai ya'atof*. Under this understanding, the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* is oriented toward bringing about the conditions for *mashiach* — each child born depletes the heavenly reservoir of souls. From this perspective, the child need not survive; the act of birth already accomplishes the purpose of the *mitzvah*.

### Yochanan: Settlement

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *lo kiyem piryah u-rivyah*. He grounds his position in the verse *lashevet yetzarah* — the purpose of *pru urvu* is to settle the world, and settling the world requires that the children actually be present to do so. If they have died, the goal of *yishuv ha-olam* has not been achieved, and the *mitzvah* remains unfulfilled.

### Raising Children

This is a striking position with significant implications. If the purpose of *pru urvu* is *lashevet* — settlement — then not only must children be born, they must live and contribute to the world's settlement. The *mitzvah* is not merely about birth but about the entire arc of bringing children into productive existence.

### Adoption & Pru Urvu

This understanding opens the door to a discussion that has very real relevance for couples challenged by infertility. If part of *pru urvu* is the raising of children — not just their biological production — then adoption can be understood as at least a partial *kiyum* of the *mitzvah*. Indeed, a number of *poskim* take exactly this view: that raising children, even if not one's biological offspring, fulfills a genuine dimension of *piryah u-rivyah*. This remains an active area of halachic discussion, and the *gemara*'s grounding of *pru urvu* in *lashevet yetzarah* provides the conceptual foundation for such a position.
