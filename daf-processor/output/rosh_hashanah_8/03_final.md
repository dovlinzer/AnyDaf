# Rosh Hashanah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Behema Debate

### Two Verses Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים בְּאֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָבְשׁוּ כָרִים הַצֹּאן וַעֲמָקִים יַעַטְפוּ בָר יִתְרוֹעֲעוּ אַף יָשִׁירוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say:** The New Year for animal tithes is **on the first of Tishrei. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And both of them,** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar, **expounded the same verse** in different ways. **As it is stated** in the verse: **"The flocks are clothed in the meadows, and the valleys are wrapped in grain; they shout for joy, they also sing"** (Psalms 65:14).

The daf opens five lines from the top with the position of Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon: *be'echad beTishrei* — the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser behemah* falls on the first of Tishrei. Having spent considerable time unpacking *echad beNisan* — including an extensive treatment of *bal te'acher* and a *baraisa* enumerating the various ways Nisan functions as a *rosh hashanah* — the Gemara now moves on to the remaining *roshei shanah*, beginning with the dispute over *ma'aser behemah*.

Rabbi Yochanan observes that both the *tanna kamma* (who holds Elul) and Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon derive their respective positions from the same verse in Tehillim: *levushu charim hatzon ve'amakim ya'atfu var yitro'u af yashiru* — "The sheep are clothed with coverings, and the valleys are cloaked in grain; they burst forth in trumpeting, even give song." The verse is read as a description of the sheep's mating season, with the "covering" (*levushu*) understood as one sheep mounting another, and the "cloaking of the valleys in grain" and "bursting forth in song" as simultaneous agricultural markers that help identify the precise time of year.

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: אֵימָתַי לָבְשׁוּ כָרִים הַצֹּאן — בִּזְמַן שֶׁעֲמָקִים יַעַטְפוּ בָר. וְאֵימָתַי עֲמָקִים יַעַטְפוּ בָר — בַּאֲדָר, מִתְעַבְּרוֹת בַּאֲדָר וְיוֹלְדוֹת בְּאָב, רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלָּהֶן אֱלוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir holds: When are the flocks clothed in the meadows,** i.e., when do the rams impregnate the ewes and thereby clothe them? It is **at the time when the valleys are wrapped in grain,** i.e., when they are covered in grain. **And when are the valleys wrapped in grain?** It is **in Adar.** Therefore, the sheep **conceive in Adar and give birth** five months later **in Av,** and so it is fitting that **their New Year** is on the first of **Elul,** as most of the year's lambs have been born by then.

Rabbi Meir holds that the sheep mate *bizman she'amakim ya'atfu var* — at the time when the valleys are first cloaked in grain, which is in Adar, before the barley is ripe and ready for harvest. If mating occurs in Adar, then, given a gestation period of five months — Nisan, Iyar, Sivan, Tammuz, Av — the sheep give birth in Av. Since the primary birthing season concludes in Av, the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser behemah* falls in the month immediately following, which is Elul.

### Rabbi Elazar/Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵימָתַי ״לָבְשׁוּ כָרִים הַצֹּאן״ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁ״יִּתְרוֹעֲעוּ אַף יָשִׁירוּ״, אֵימָתַי שִׁבֳּלִים אוֹמְרוֹת שִׁירָה — בְּנִיסָן, מִתְעַבְּרוֹת בְּנִיסָן וְיוֹלְדוֹת בֶּאֱלוּל, רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלָּהֶן תִּשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say: When are the flocks clothed in the meadows? At the time that** the stalks of grain **"shout for joy, and also sing." When do the stalks break out in song,** i.e., when are they full, so that they rustle in the wind and create the whispering sounds of song? It is **in Nisan.** Therefore, the sheep **conceive in Nisan and give birth in Elul,** and so it is fitting that **their New Year** is **on** the first of **Tishrei.**

Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon read the verse differently. They focus not on the phrase *ve'amakim ya'atfu var* but on *yitro'u af yashiru* — when the stalks of grain rustle in the wind and "give song," a phenomenon that occurs only in Nisan once the grain has grown tall enough. According to their reading, the primary mating season occurs in Nisan. The sheep therefore conceive in Nisan, give birth five months later in Elul, and accordingly the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser behemah* is Tishrei, the month after Elul.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב: ״יִתְרוֹעֲעוּ אַף יָשִׁירוּ״! הָהוּא בְּאַפְלָתָא, דְּהָוְיָין בְּנִיסָן. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וַעֲמָקִים יַעַטְפוּ בָר״! הָהוּא בְּחָרַפְיָיתָא, דְּאָתְיָין מֵאֲדָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Meir, **as well, isn't it written: "They shout for joy, they also sing"?** The Gemara answers: **That** is referring to **late** sheep, which were conceived after the usual time, **in Nisan.** The Gemara asks: According to **the other** opinion of Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon **as well, isn't it written: "And the valleys wrapped in grain,"** which is in Adar? The Gemara answers: **That** is referring to **early** sheep, **which were conceived** in **Adar.**

The Gemara objects to both readings. Against Rabbi Meir: does the verse not also mention *yitro'u af yashiru*, implying that mating happens in Nisan as well? The Gemara answers: *hahu be'aflei* — that refers to the late-mating sheep, those who mate after the primary season. Against Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon: does the verse not also say *ve'amakim ya'atfu var*, implying mating in Adar? The Gemara answers: *hahu becharifta* — those are the early ones who begin mating already in Adar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״לָבְשׁוּ כָרִים הַצֹּאן״ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁ״עֲמָקִים יַעַטְפוּ בָר״ בַּאֲדָר, וְהָכָא בְּהַאי קְרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר״ — בִּשְׁנֵי מַעַשְׂרוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר: אֶחָד מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד מַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that the dispute must be explained as follows: **Everyone** is of the opinion **that "the flocks are clothed in the meadows,"** i.e., the sheep conceive, primarily **at the time when "the valleys are wrapped in grain," in** the month of **Adar. But here they disagree about** the meaning of **the** following **verse: "You shall tithe a tithe** from all the increase of your seed that the field brings forth year by year. And you shall eat before the Lord your God, in the place which He shall choose to place His name there, the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborn of your herds, and of your flocks; that you may learn to fear the Lord your God always" (Deuteronomy 14:22–23). **The verse speaks** here **about two tithes, one** being **the animal tithe and the other the grain tithe.**

Rava then reformulates the dispute entirely. In truth, *kulei alma* — everyone agrees that the primary mating period occurs in Adar (as the *pasuk* first states) and that the primary birthing period is therefore in Av. The dispute is not really about sheep biology at all. Rather, it concerns how to read the verse *aser te'aser*, the doubled language of tithing, which the Gemara takes as linking *ma'aser behemah* to *ma'aser dagan* and asks: in what sense are they linked?

It is worth noting a difficulty here: if both sides agree that birthing occurs throughout both Av and Elul — with Av being primary and Elul being secondary — then it is hard to understand why anyone would place the *rosh hashanah* after the primary birth period but before the late births are complete. Logic would seem to demand that the new year begin only after the entire birthing process has concluded, which would mean Tishrei in any case. This is a genuine *he'arah* on the structure of the dispute as initially framed, though Rava's reformulation ultimately moves the argument away from this point.

## Logic vs Form

### Rabbi Meir Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן — סָמוּךְ לִגְמָרוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ, אַף מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה — סָמוּךְ לִגְמָרוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** They disagree about the following: **Rabbi Meir holds** that the verse **juxtaposes the animal tithe to the grain tithe. Just as the grain tithe is set aside close to** the grain's **completion,** after it dries out in the field, **so too, the animal tithe is set aside close to its completion,** after the animals are born.

Rava explains that Rabbi Meir reads the linkage between *ma'aser behemah* and *ma'aser dagan* as a conceptual parallel: *makish ma'aser behemah le-ma'aser dagan* — just as the *rosh hashanah* for grain is determined by the logic of the agricultural cycle, so too the *rosh hashanah* for animals. The principle he identifies is *samuch le-gemaro isuro* — the tithing year begins at the point juxtaposed to the completion of the process. By grain, the new year is Tishrei, because Tishrei is when *Chag Ha-asif* — the Feast of Ingathering — marks the end of the harvest cycle. So too by animals, the new year begins right after the primary birthing period concludes, which is in Elul, immediately following the Av births.

### Elazar/Shimon Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבְרִי: מַקִּישׁ מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלּוֹ תִּשְׁרִי — אַף מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלּוֹ תִּשְׁרִי.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon hold:** The verse **juxtaposes the animal tithe to the grain tithe. Just as** in the case of **the grain tithe, its New Year is in Tishrei, so too,** in the case of **the animal tithe, its New Year is in Tishrei.**

Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon also invoke the linkage between the two *ma'asrot*, but they apply it as a simple formal equation rather than an analogical argument: *mah ma'aser dagan Rosh Hashanah shelo Tishrei, af ma'aser behemah Rosh Hashanah shelo Tishrei* — just as grain has its *rosh hashanah* in Tishrei, so too animals have their *rosh hashanah* in Tishrei. It is a *Tishrei-Tishrei* comparison with no further derivation needed.

The dispute thus comes down to whether the *hekesh* operates by shared logic or by shared form. Rabbi Meir says the Torah links the two tithes to teach that the same underlying rationale — beginning the year *samuch le-gemaro* — governs both. Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say the Torah simply places both tithes under the same calendar date.

### Sukkot Asif

The identification of Tishrei as the *rosh hashanah* for grain is itself rooted in the agricultural cycle. *Sukkot* is *Chag Ha-asif*, the festival of ingathering, when the harvest is being gathered in. It is the moment when one agricultural year concludes and the next begins, which is why the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser dagan* is anchored to Tishrei. Rabbi Meir draws on this logic and extends it by analogy to animals, placing the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser behemah* in the month immediately following the primary birth season.

One might note that even by grain, the new year is proclaimed while the ingathering process is still technically underway — *Sukkot* is when you are still gathering, not when the last stalk has been processed. This raises the same structural question as before: does the new year mark the end of the primary phase of a process, even if secondary elements are still ongoing? It seems that this is indeed the principle, and it applies consistently to both grain and animals.

## Tishrei Applications

### Documents Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לַשָּׁנִים. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לִשְׁטָרוֹת. דִּתְנַן: שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב הַמּוּקְדָּמִין — פְּסוּלִין, וְהַמְאוּחָרִין — כְּשֵׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **On the first of Tishrei is the New Year for** counting **years.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to which *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Pappa said:** It is stated **for** determining the validity of **documents, as we learned** in a mishna: **Antedated promissory notes,** which bear a date that is earlier than the date when the loan actually took place, **are invalid. But postdated promissory notes,** which bear a date that is later than the date when the loan actually took place, **are valid.**

The Gemara now turns to the *mishnah*'s statement that Tishrei is *rosh hashanah le-shanim* — the new year for years. The Gemara asks *le-mai hilchata*, what is the practical application? Rav Pappa answers: *le-shtarot*, for the dating of documents. As the *mishnah* teaches elsewhere, a predated document is invalid while a postdated document is valid, which means one must always know when the year increments in order to write the correct year on a deed or contract. The new year for that purpose begins on the first of Tishrei.

### Kings Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לַמְּלָכִים, וְאָמְרִינַן: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא — וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא לִשְׁטָרוֹת. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לְמַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּאן לְמַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we** already **learn** in the mishna: **On the first of Nisan is the New Year for kings; and we say** about this: **For what *halakha*** is this stated? **And Rav Ḥisda said:** It is **for** determining the validity of **documents.** Therefore, the new year for documents begins in Nisan and not in Tishrei. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; here,** where the document is dated according to the reign of **the Jewish kings,** the year begins in Nisan; and **there,** where the document is dated to the reign of **the** gentile **kings of the nations of the world,** the year begins in Tishrei.

This immediately raises a difficulty: the *mishnah* stated at the outset that the first of Nisan is *rosh hashanah le-melachim*, the new year for kings — and Rav Chisda had explained that this too is *le-shtarot*, for dating documents. Which month is it, then, Nisan or Tishrei?

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The distinction is between *malchei Yisrael* and *malchei umot ha'olam*. Documents dated by reference to Jewish kings increment the regnal year in Nisan, consistent with the special significance of Nisan as the beginning of the months for the Jewish people. Documents dated by reference to non-Jewish kings increment in Tishrei. Depending on which king's reign is being used as the dating reference — and the Gemara notes that certain documents were customarily dated by non-Jewish kings for reasons of *shalom malchut* — the year would increment at different times.

### Two Time Cycles

This distinction points toward something deeper. Tishrei is the natural beginning of the agricultural year; the agricultural cycle begins with planting in the fall, and *Sukkot* — which falls in Tishrei — is *b'tzeit hashanah* or *b'tekufat hashanah*, the going out of the year and the turning of the year's cycle. Just as the school year begins in September because children historically returned from helping on the farm when the planting season commenced, so too the natural agricultural year begins in Tishrei.

The Torah then introduces a second system: *hachodesh hazeh lachem rosh chodashim* — this month shall be for you the first of the months. With the Exodus and the revelation at Sinai, a new dimension of time is introduced: not just the natural agricultural cycle but a historical and redemptive mode of reckoning time, counted from God's intervention in human history. This explains why the months are numbered from Nisan while the years are numbered from Tishrei — they reflect two genuinely different cycles operating simultaneously. The numbering of years from creation, used when we write 5784 or similar, also increments from Tishrei, as *beTishrei nivra ha'olam* is a foundational assumption. Interestingly, the Gemara does not mention *briyat ha'olam* as a practical application for the dating of *shtarot*, listing only *melachim* — a point worth reflection.

It bears noting as well that Babylon, from which many of the month names in our calendar derive, also began its year in Nisan. Spring, when things come alive after winter, is a naturally compelling starting point for a year — both as a redemptive moment and as a natural one. So Nisan, too, has claims on being a natural beginning of the year, which further explains why two valid systems of reckoning exist.

### Astronomical Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַב חִסְדָּא כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ. דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: לַתְּקוּפָה, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר בְּתִשְׁרִי נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that **Rav Ḥisda teaches** the mishna **as** did **Rabbi Zeira,** and he maintains that when it says that the first of Tishrei is the New Year for years, it is not referring to documents, **as Rabbi Zeira said:** The first of Tishrei is the New Year for years **with regard to** calculating the **cycles** of the sun and the moon. **And this is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said: The world was created in** the month of **Tishrei,** and all the calculations with regard to the sun and the moon are based on when they were created.

The Gemara then introduces a second opinion on the meaning of *le-shanim*. According to Rabbi Zeira, Tishrei is the new year *litkufa* — for the astronomical cycle. The idea is that in order to reckon where the sun and stars were positioned at the time of creation, one must know that the creation occurred in Tishrei, in accordance with the view of Rabbi Eliezer that *beTishrei nivra ha'olam*. The astronomical cycle, meaning the calculation of *tekufot* and the position of the celestial bodies relative to where they were *beshat briyat ha'olam*, is therefore anchored to the first of Tishrei.

One might ask: what practical difference does this make? We can calculate where everything is now, and work backwards to any point in history regardless of which month we designate as the starting point. The *Tosafot* engages at length with this question and addresses the computations of *tekufot*, though this is a complex area. The most accessible *nafka mina* is *Birkat HaChama*, the blessing recited when the sun returns to the position it occupied at the time of creation, which is a calculation anchored to the *tekufa* from creation. It is worth noting, however — and this is a striking point raised in the discussion — that our practice for *Birkat HaChama* places the event in Nisan, not Tishrei, despite our general assumption that *beTishrei nivra ha'olam*. The *Tosafot* notes that according to Rabbi Eliezer, the world was actually created on the 25th of Elul, meaning that Adam was created on the first of Tishrei — *Rosh Hashanah* — and according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the equivalent starting point falls in Adar, with Adam's creation on *Rosh Chodesh Nisan*. These competing assumptions underlie the different astronomical reckonings.

## Yom HaDin

### Day Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: לַדִּין, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵרֵאשִׁית הַשָּׁנָה וְעַד אַחֲרִית שָׁנָה״, מֵרֵאשִׁית הַשָּׁנָה נִידּוֹן מָה יְהֵא בְּסוֹפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** When the mishna says that the first of Tishrei is the New Year for years, it is **with regard to judgment,** as on that day God judges the world for the whole year, **as it is written:** "A land that the Lord your God cares for; the eyes of the Lord your God are always upon it, **from the beginning of the year until the end of the year"** (Deuteronomy 11:12); **from the beginning of the year judgment is passed as to what will happen at the end** of the year.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a third application of *rosh hashanah le-shanim*: *ledin*, for judgment. Tishrei marks the beginning of the year in the sense that on *Rosh Hashanah* one is judged for the coming year. The proof text is *mereishit hashanah ve'ad acharit shanah* — God surveys the land, and by extension His people, from the beginning of the year to the end of the year, meaning that at the outset of the year He renders judgment for what will unfold by its end: *mereishit hashanah nidon mah yehei be-sofah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי דְּתִשְׁרִי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״תִּקְעוּ בַחֹדֶשׁ שׁוֹפָר בַּכֵּסֶה לְיוֹם חַגֵּנוּ״, אֵיזֶהוּ חַג שֶׁהַחֹדֶשׁ מִתְכַּסֶּה בּוֹ — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא מִשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: **From where** is it known **that** the day of judgment is in **Tishrei? As it is written: "Blow a *shofar* at the New Moon, at the covered time for our Festival day"** (Psalms 81:4). **Which is the Festival day on which the moon is covered,** i.e., hidden? **You must say** that **this is Rosh HaShana,** which is the only Festival that occurs at the beginning of a month, when the moon cannot be seen. **And it is written** in the next verse: **"For this is a statute for Israel, a judgment of the God of Jacob"** (Psalms 81:5), implying that this is the day of judgment.

The Gemara asks: how do we know this refers to Tishrei? From the verse *tiku bachodesh shofar bakese l'yom chageinu ki chok l'Yisrael hu mishpat lelohei Yaakov* — sound the *shofar* on the new moon, on the *kese*, on our festival day, for it is a statute of Israel, a judgment of the God of Jacob. The Gemara identifies the holiday in question through the phrase *bakese* — the day when the moon is "covered" and barely visible. *Eizu chag shehachodesh mitkaseh bo* — which holiday falls on the day when the month is hidden? Only *Rosh Hashanah*, which occurs on the first of the month when the moon is still a sliver. All other *chagim* fall on the fifteenth, when the moon is full.

The *Tosafot* records a debate among the *baalei Tosafot* connected to this. One opinion held that *chodesh mitkaseh bo* was a reference to the fact that on Tishrei the *korban musaf* of *Rosh Chodesh* was subsumed — not brought separately, since only the *musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah* was offered. A *Tosefta* refutes this by establishing clearly that a separate *korban musaf* of *Rosh Chodesh* is indeed brought on *Rosh Hashanah*. The opinion then retreats to a narrower claim: the *pesukkim* obscure the *Rosh Chodesh* identity of the day, and our *tefilot* similarly do not highlight that aspect — in our *mussaf she-moneh esrei* we do not draw attention to it being *Rosh Chodesh*. To this, Rabbeinu Tam responds that we actually do: the line *olat hachodesh u'minchatah* is explicitly included in the *musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah*. This insertion is attributed to Rabbeinu Tam, and it is in accordance with his view that our practice follows — we recite that line making explicit reference to the *korban* of *Rosh Chodesh* as well.

### Partnership Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא מִשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה נִכְנָסִין לַדִּין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קִידְּשׁוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה אֶת הַחֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this same verse, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"For this is a statute for Israel, a judgment of the God of Jacob"; this teaches that the heavenly court does not assemble for judgment until the earthly court has sanctified the month,** once the Sanhedrin has declared that day as Rosh HaShana.

The Gemara introduces a *baraisa* deriving from *ki chok l'Yisrael hu mishpat lelohei Yaakov* a striking principle: *ein Beit Din shel ma'alah nichnasim ladin ela im ken kidshu Beit Din shel matah et hachodesh* — the heavenly court does not convene for judgment unless and until the earthly *beit din* has sanctified the month.

This is a profound expression of the partnership between God and Israel that runs throughout Jewish law. Just as *Torah she-bichtav* gives the written framework and *Torah she-ba'al peh* fills it with interpretive life, so too the calendar operates through a collaboration: God creates the natural rhythm of the moon, but Israel must observe it, testify to it, and declare it holy. The *moadim* are designated not merely by nature but by human declaration — *asher tikre'u otam*.

What is particularly striking here is the application of this principle to the context of *din*. *Yom HaDin*, the day of judgment, carries an aura of pure submission — of the subject standing before an absolute authority. Yet even *Yom HaDin* only exists because Israel creates it through *kiddush hachodesh*. The day of our judgment is, in a profound sense, a day we bring into being.

This theme has a natural extension to the second day of *Rosh Hashanah*. The second day exists only because of *sfeika d'yoma*, a doubt — it is a *minhag* rooted in uncertainty, not in any direct divine declaration that it is a *yom hadin*. One might therefore have thought that the second day carries no weight as a day of judgment. Yet the same logic operates: by treating it as *Rosh Hashanah*, by declaring it and living it as such, we actually constitute it as a *yom hadin*. And if what makes it a *yom hadin* is the communal act of creating it, then its *din* is naturally a collective one — not the individual judgment of the first day but a judgment for the community as a whole. This connects as well to the idea, found in certain *midrashic* sources, that the first day's *din* is strict while the second day's is sweetened, and to the concept at the heart of *Hoshana Rabbah* where the final seal of the *din* is itself subject to further softening.

### Nations Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, לְאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל נִכְנָסִין תְּחִילָּה לַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states: **"For this is a statute for Israel."** From here **I have** derived **only** that this is the day of judgment **for the Jewish people; from where** do I derive that it is also the day of judgment **for the** gentile **nations of the world?** Therefore, **the verse states: "A judgment for the God of Jacob,"** Who rules over the entire world. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "A statute for Israel"? This teaches that the Jewish people enter for judgment first.**

A second *baraisa* on the same verse asks: *ein li ela le-Yisrael* — perhaps *Rosh Hashanah* is a day of judgment only for the Jewish people? *Umos ha'olam minayin?* — how do I know that the nations are also judged? The answer is drawn from the second half of the verse: *mishpat lelohei Yaakov* — it is the judgment of the God of Jacob, and since He is the universal God, judgment extends to all. The function of *ki chok l'Yisrael* is then to teach something different: *Yisrael nichnasim techilah ladin* — Israel enters judgment first.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מֶלֶךְ וְצִיבּוּר — מֶלֶךְ נִכְנָס תְּחִילָּה לַדִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִשְׁפַּט עַבְדּוֹ וּמִשְׁפַּט עַמּוֹ״. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵיקַם מַלְכָּא אַבָּרַאי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִיפּוֹשׁ חֲרוֹן אַף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda said:** When **a king and a community** are brought before God for judgment, the **king is brought in for judgment first, as it is stated:** "And let these my words, with which I have made supplication before the Lord, be near to the Lord our God day and night, that He make **the judgment of His servant and the judgment of His people** Israel at all times" (I Kings 8:59). This verse is from King Solomon's prayer at the dedication of the Temple, and he is referring to himself a servant of God. **What is the reason** that the king is brought in first? **If you wish, say** that it is **not proper conduct for the king to stand outside** and wait for the trial of his subjects to come to an end. **And if you wish, say** instead that the king is brought in first so that he may be judged **before** God's **anger intensifies** due to the sins of the community, and he may thereby be saved from overly harsh judgment.

The Gemara supports this with a parallel principle stated by Rav Chisda: when a king and his community both stand before the court, *melech nichnas techilah ladin* — the king is judged first. The proof text is *mishpat avdo u-mishpat amo* — first the judgment of His servant (understood here as the leader or king), and afterward the judgment of the nation.

Two explanations are offered for why the king — and by analogy Israel — is judged first. The first is *lav orach ar'a lemeikam malka avraei* — it is a matter of honor; it is undignified to leave the king waiting outside in the corridor while others are being processed. The second: *mikamei de-lifush charon af* — it is better to be judged before God's anger increases. As God proceeds through the judgments of those being tried and witnesses more and more sin, the divine anger intensifies, and one is better positioned at the beginning of the queue.

This second explanation invites reflection. Framing it as an advantage for those judged first seems to run counter to an equally natural reading: the king, or Israel, bears the greatest responsibility. If there is widespread sin or failure, the one who was in the best position to prevent it — the leader, the nation charged with being *or lagoyim* — should perhaps bear the heaviest judgment. From that perspective, being judged first is not a privilege but an expression of heightened accountability. The *pasuk* in Amos — *rak etchem yadati mikol mishpechot ha'adamah al kein efkod aleichem et kol avonotechem* — precisely frames Israel's unique relationship with God as the basis for a more demanding judgment, not a lighter one. The Gemara's framing of this as an advantage is thus somewhat surprising, and the underlying logic remains worth examining.

## Shemitah Year

### Shemitah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַשְּׁמִיטִּין. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבַשָּׁנָה הַשְּׁבִיעִית שַׁבַּת שַׁבָּתוֹן יִהְיֶה לָאָרֶץ״, וְגָמַר ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ מִתִּשְׁרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵרֵאשִׁית הַשָּׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **For** calculating **Sabbatical Years.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written: "But in the seventh year shall be a Shabbat of solemn rest for the land"** (Leviticus 25:4), **and** we **learn** by way of a verbal analogy between one instance of the word **"year"** and another instance of the word **"year"** that the year begins for this purpose **from Tishrei, as it is written: "From the beginning of the year"** (Deuteronomy 11:12). The latter verse is referring to the year that begins at the onset of the rainy season, i.e., Tishrei.

The *mishnah* also lists *shemitah* among the things for which Tishrei is the *rosh hashanah*. The Gemara asks: *menalan*, how do we know the seven-year *shemitah* cycle increments in Tishrei rather than Nisan?

The derivation proceeds by *gezerah shavah*. The verse *u-vashanah ha-shvi'it shabbat shabbaton la-hashem tihyeh la-aretz* uses the word *shanah* — year. The verse *mereishit hashanah ve'ad acharit shanah*, which we established refers to Tishrei, also uses the word *shanah*. We therefore learn *shanah shanah* from Tishrei. Could we not equally learn it from Nisan, using the verse *rishon hu lachem le-chodshei hashanah*, where *shanah* is also mentioned? The Gemara rules this out.

### Shanah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִגְמוֹר ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ מִנִּיסָן, דִּכְתִיב: ״רִאשׁוֹן הוּא לָכֶם לְחׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה״! דָּנִין שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ חֳדָשִׁים מִשָּׁנָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ חֳדָשִׁים, וְאֵין דָּנִין שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ חֳדָשִׁים מִשָּׁנָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהּ חֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But let us learn** by way of a verbal analogy between one instance of the word **"year"** and another instance of the word **"year"** and conclude that for this purpose the year begins **from Nisan, as it is written: "It shall be the first month of the year to you"** (Exodus 12:2), and there the reference is to Nisan. The Gemara answers: The Sages **derive** the meaning of the word **"year"** that appears in the verse about the Sabbatical Year, where **months are not** mentioned **with it, from** the word **"year"** that appears in the verse in Deuteronomy above, where **months** are also **not** mentioned **with it. And** they **do not derive** the meaning of the word **"year"** where **months are not** mentioned **with it from** the word **"year"** that appears in the verse where **months are** mentioned **with it,** i.e., "It shall be the first month of the year for you."

The Gemara's reasoning is that one derives *shanah* (unqualified year) from *shanah* (unqualified year), but one does not derive *shanah* from a verse where *shanah* appears in the context of *chodashim* — months. The verse *rishon hu lachem le-chodshei hashanah* mentions *shanah* in conjunction with *chodesh*, making it a reference to the year in terms of its months. The verses governing *shemitah* and the verse *mereishit hashanah* do not mention *chodashim* — they speak of *shanah* simply. One therefore draws the *gezerah shavah* between the simple *shanah* references, all of which point to Tishrei.

More conceptually: the significance of Nisan as *rosh hashanah* is tied to its identity as the beginning of the *chodashim*, the months. That is its domain. For the larger agricultural and cyclical year — *shemitah*, *yovel*, and related laws — we operate within the natural agricultural calendar that predates the Sinaitic revelation about month-counting, and that calendar begins in Tishrei.

## Yovel Release

### Rosh Hashanah vs Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַיּוֹבְלוֹת. יוֹבְלוֹת בְּאֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי הוּא? יוֹבְלוֹת בַּעֲשָׂרָה בְּתִשְׁרִי הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים תַּעֲבִירוּ שׁוֹפָר״!
>
> **Translation:** **For Jubilee Years.** The Gemara asks: **Is** the New Year for **Jubilee Years on the first of Tishrei?** Isn't the New Year for **Jubilee Years on the tenth of Tishrei,** Yom Kippur? **As it is written:** "Then shall you cause the *shofar* to sound on the tenth day of the seventh month, **on Yom Kippur shall you sound the *shofar*** throughout all your land. And you shall hallow the fiftieth year, and proclaim liberty throughout all the land to all its inhabitants; it shall be a Jubilee for you" (Leviticus 25:9–10).

The Gemara asks: the *mishnah* includes *yovelot* among the things for which Tishrei is the *rosh hashanah* — but is *yovel* really from the first of Tishrei? The Torah explicitly says *be-yom ha-kippurim ta'aviru shofar be-chol artzechem* — the *shofar* of *yovel* is sounded on *Yom Kippur*, the tenth of Tishrei! The Gemara answers: the *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Ishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who gives the first of Tishrei a distinct role even in *yovel*.

### Middle Zone Limbo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתֶּם אֵת שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים שָׁנָה״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים״ — יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת אֶלָּא מִיּוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים וְאֵילָךְ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתֶּם אֵת שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּתְקַדֶּשֶׁת וְהוֹלֶכֶת מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year"? Since it is stated** that the *shofar* is blown **"on Yom Kippur,"** one **might** have thought that the year **is sanctified only from Yom Kippur and onward. Therefore, the verse states: "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year," which teaches that** the year **is sanctified from its beginning onward,** from the first of Tishrei, when the year begins.

The *baraisa* records Rabbi Ishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's view as follows. The verse *ve-kidashtem et shnat ha-chamishim shanah* — "you shall sanctify the fiftieth year" — would seem superfluous, since the Torah already specified *be-yom ha-kippurim ta'aviru shofar*. The word *tishrei* is already established from the *shemitah* analysis as the month when years begin; therefore the verse *sanctify the year* teaches that the year's sanctity as *yovel* begins from the start of the year — from *Rosh Hashanah* — even though the shofar and formal declaration come on *Yom Kippur*. From here: *mitkadeshet ve-holechet* — the sanctity grows progressively from *Rosh Hashanah* onward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא: מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה עַד יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לֹא הָיוּ עֲבָדִים נִפְטָרִין לְבָתֵּיהֶן, וְלֹא מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדִין לַאֲדוֹנֵיהֶם — אֶלָּא אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין וּשְׂמֵחִין, וְעַטְרוֹתֵיהֶן בְּרָאשֵׁיהֶן. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, תָּקְעוּ בֵּית דִּין בְּשׁוֹפָר, נִפְטְרוּ עֲבָדִים לְבָתֵּיהֶן וְשָׂדוֹת חוֹזְרוֹת לְבַעְלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **From here, Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, said: From Rosh HaShana until Yom Kippur** of the Jubilee Year, Hebrew **slaves were not released to their homes** because the *shofar* had not yet been sounded. **And they were** also **not enslaved to their masters,** as the Jubilee Year had already begun. **Rather, they would eat, drink, and rejoice, and** they would wear **their crowns on their heads** like free people. **Once Yom Kippur arrived, the court** would **sound the *shofar*, slaves** would be **released to their houses, and fields** that were sold would be **returned to their** original **owners.**

What then is the practical consequence of this view? *Me-Rosh Hashanah ad Yom Kippur*, during the ten days between the two, the slaves would not go home (*lo hayu avadim niftarin le-vateihem*) and would not be enslaved to their masters (*ve-lo mishtabdin le-adoneihem*). They were in a *limbo* — neither free nor bound. They would eat, drink, and rejoice (*ochlin ve-shotin ve-smechin*), and could even wear crowns on their heads (*ve-atroteihem be-rasheihem*), living as though already elevated. When *Yom Kippur* arrived, *beit din* would sound the *shofar* — corresponding to the verse *u-kratem dror ba-aretz le-chol yoshveha*, "you shall proclaim liberty throughout the land" — and only then would the slaves actually return home and the fields revert to their original owners.

This progression has a beautiful resonance: the ultimate redemption does not arrive instantaneously. There is a preparatory middle period in which the old order is already loosened and the new one anticipated, but the final liberation requires its own distinct moment. The image parallels ideas of *itchalta de-geula*, the beginning of redemption that precedes its full realization.

### Rabbis Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן — שָׁנִים אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ חֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, how do they interpret the verse: "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year"? The Gemara answers: They derive from here that **you sanctify years, but you do not sanctify months.** According to this opinion, the court is commanded to sanctify the Jubilee Year with a proclamation: This year is sanctified; but it is not commanded to sanctify the months with a similar proclamation.

The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Ishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. According to them, everything in *yovel* — both the beginning and end of its sanctified status — is anchored exclusively to *Yom Kippur*. The verse *ve-kidashtem et shnat ha-chamishim shanah* teaches them something different: *shanim atah mekadesh, ve-ein atah mekadesh chadashim* — you sanctify years, not months. This is taken as a ruling that the obligation of *beit din* to perform an act of *kiddush* is limited to the sanctification of the *yovel* year; there is no parallel obligation of *beit din* to formally sanctify the months in the manner associated with *yovel*. This is a striking position, running somewhat against the standard assumption that *beit din*'s act of *mekudash mekudash* is an indispensable requirement for *kiddush hachodesh* in the ordinary sense.

### Cycle Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״יוֹבֵל הִיא״ — מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתֶּם אֵת שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים״, יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּתְקַדֶּשֶׁת וְהוֹלֶכֶת מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ, כָּךְ מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת וְהוֹלֶכֶת בְּסוֹפָהּ. וְאַל תִּתְמַהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי מוֹסִיפִין מֵחוֹל עַל קֹדֶשׁ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹבֵל הִיא שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים״ — שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "It shall be a Jubilee Year"** (Leviticus 25:11)? **Since it is stated: "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year,"** one **might** have thought that **just as** the Jubilee Year **is sanctified from its beginning onward, so too, it is sanctified at its end onward,** i.e., it remains sanctified until Yom Kippur of the fifty-first year. **And do not wonder** why one might think this, **as don't we** regularly **add from the profane to the sacred,** extending a sacred time period by adding to it both before and after from a profane time period? **Therefore, the verse states: "It shall be a Jubilee Year, the fiftieth year,"** to teach that **you sanctify the fiftieth year, but you do not sanctify the fifty-first year,** even partially.

A further *baraisa* addresses the question of what happens at the *end* of the *yovel* year. The verse *yovel hi shnat ha-chamishim shanah* — "it is the *yovel*, the fiftieth year" — is read as defining the year's boundaries precisely. One might have thought that just as the *yovel* year accretes its sanctity from *Rosh Hashanah* onward (per Rabbi Ishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka), so too at the end there might be a tapering-off period extending into the *Yom Kippur* of year fifty-one — *mitkadeshet ve-holechet be-sofah* — a symmetrical de-acclimatization. The verse comes to exclude this: only the fiftieth year is sanctified; the fifty-first is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים אַתָּה מוֹנֶה, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹנֶה שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים וְאַחַת — לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים עוֹלָה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who do not require an additional verse to derive that the Jubilee Year does not extend until Yom Kippur of the fifty-first year, derive this *halakha* from the verse: **You count the fiftieth year** as the Jubilee Year alone, **but you do not count the fiftieth year** as the Jubilee Year **and** also as the **first** year of the next Sabbatical cycle. This *halakha* comes **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that **the fiftieth year is counted for here and for there,** both as the Jubilee Year and also as the first year of the next Sabbatical cycle. Lest someone think that that is the case, **therefore, the verse teaches us that** this is **not** so; rather, the fiftieth year is the Jubilee Year, and the following year is the first year of the next Sabbatical cycle.

The Rabbis, who have no acclimating period to begin with, derive a different lesson from the same verse: *shnat hamishim atah moneh, ve-ein atah moneh shnat hamishim achat* — you count this year as fifty, not as year one of the next cycle. This is directed against the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *shnat hamishim olah le-kan u-le-kan* — the fiftieth year counts both as the last year of the previous cycle and as year one of the next, making the *yovel* cycle effectively forty-nine years rather than fifty. The majority view, established by this verse, is that year fifty has a single identity — it is the fiftieth and nothing more — and year one of the new cycle begins the following year, making the full cycle fifty years.
