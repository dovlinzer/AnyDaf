# Bava Kamma 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mutual collision

### Barrel & beam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זֶה בָּא בְּחָבִיתוֹ וְזֶה בָּא בְּקוֹרָתוֹ, נִשְׁבְּרָה כַּדּוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּקוֹרָתוֹ שֶׁל זֶה – פָּטוּר; שֶׁלָּזֶה רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ, וְלָזֶה רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **this** person **came** in the public domain **with his barrel, and that** person **came** from the opposite direction **with his** cross **beam,** and **this one's jug was broken by that one's** cross **beam,** the one carrying the cross beam is **exempt, because this one had permission to walk** in the public domain, **and that one** also **had permission to walk** there.

The *mishnah* with which we begin, found at the bottom of 31b, presents a classic collision scenario in *reshus harabim*: two people are walking in the public domain, one carrying a barrel of wine or oil, the other transporting a beam to a construction site. The inevitable happens — they crash into one another, and the barrel breaks against the beam. The ruling is *patur*, exempt, and the reason is straightforward: *shelo zeh reshus l'halech v'zeh reshus l'halech* — each person had a right to be there. Neither, in the language of traffic law, ran a red light. Since both were operating with permission, one cannot be held more culpable than the other, even though it was the barrel — and not the beam — that broke. The mere fact that one party sustained more damage is insufficient to impose liability.

### Adam muad principle

At first glance, one might invoke the principle of *adam muad l'olam* — that a person always carries absolute liability for the damage he causes — and argue that the man with the beam should be held responsible for breaking the barrel. *Tosafos* addresses this by explaining that *adam muad l'olam* does not operate as literal absolute liability; there must be some degree of negligence. Under *Tosafos*'s approach, it is sufficient to say that the man with the beam was not fully negligent without reference to the other party. For those who do maintain absolute liability, the principle is offset here by *peshias hanizak* — the contributory negligence of the injured party, who was equally responsible for the collision. Alternatively, one can simply say there is no way to determine who actually caused the accident when both parties were equally active and equally present by right.

### Following collision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה בַּעַל הַקּוֹרָה רִאשׁוֹן וּבַעַל חָבִית אַחֲרוֹן; נִשְׁבְּרָה חָבִית בְּקוֹרָה – פָּטוּר בַּעַל הַקּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If they were walking in the same direction, so that **the owner of the** cross **beam was** walking **first,** in front, **and the owner of a barrel last,** behind him, and **the barrel was broken by the** cross **beam, the owner of the** cross **beam** is **exempt,** since the owner of the barrel saw him in front of him and should have been more careful.

The *mishnah* then addresses a more structured scenario: the man with the beam is walking ahead, and the man with the barrel is following behind him. If the barrel-carrier speeds up and collides with the beam in front of him, the man with the beam is *patur*. The accident was caused by the one behind, who accelerated into the person ahead of him. This maps naturally onto the familiar principle that a rear-end collision is the fault of the vehicle that strikes from behind.

### Standing vs signaling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם עָמַד בַּעַל קוֹרָה – חַיָּיב. וְאִם אָמַר לְבַעַל חָבִית ״עֲמוֹד״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But if the owner of the** cross **beam stopped,** causing the barrel to collide with the beam and break, the former is **liable,** since the latter had no way of anticipating that he would stop. **And if he said to the owner of the barrel: Stop,** he is **exempt** from liability for breaking the barrel.

The analysis shifts, however, when the man with the beam stops short. In that case, *v'im amad ba'al hakora chayav* — he is *chayav*, liable, because his sudden stop is what caused the accident. His action — or rather the sudden cessation of it — is what brought the damage about. The determinative question throughout these cases is: whose act precipitated the collision? However, if the man with the beam stopped and simultaneously signaled, calling out to the one behind him that he was stopping, he is again *patur*. Having communicated his intention, the responsibility shifts back to the man with the barrel, who was duly warned and nonetheless failed to avoid the collision.

### Barrel leads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה בַּעַל חָבִית רִאשׁוֹן וּבַעַל קוֹרָה אַחֲרוֹן; נִשְׁבְּרָה חָבִית בַּקּוֹרָה – חַיָּיב. וְאִם עָמַד בַּעַל חָבִית – פָּטוּר. וְאִם אָמַר לְבַעַל קוֹרָה: ״עֲמוֹד״ – חַיָּיב. וְכֵן זֶה בָּא בְּנֵרוֹ וְזֶה בְּפִשְׁתָּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, if **the owner of the barrel was** walking **first and the owner of the** cross **beam last,** and **the barrel was broken by the** cross **beam,** the owner of the cross beam is **liable. But if owner of the barrel stopped,** the owner of the cross beam is **exempt** from liability for breaking the barrel. **And if he said to the owner of the** cross **beam: Stop,** the owner of the cross beam is **liable. And similarly,** these *halakhot* apply in a case where **this one came with his lamp and that one came with his flax,** and the lamp set fire to the flax.

The *mishnah* then reverses the order: the man with the barrel is leading and the man with the beam is following. Here, if they collide, the man with the beam is *chayav*, because it is he — coming from behind — whose movement caused the impact. If the man with the barrel stops, he is *patur*: the trailing party with the beam simply walked into him. But if the barrel-carrier stopped and then called out to the beam-carrier, signaling the stop, *chayav* — the man with the beam is liable, because the stop was announced and he failed to respond accordingly.

The *mishnah* concludes by noting the same principles apply to one carrying a lit torch and one carrying flax — the logic is identical. Even when one object causes damage to another, the question is never simply which object was the instrument of harm, but rather whose action caused the accident to unfold.

Taken together, the structure of the *mishnah* is clear: when two parties both act with *reshus* and both contribute actively to what happens, no one can be singled out for liability. When the analysis must apportion blame, the question is always whose specific act — movement, sudden stop, or failure to signal — was the proximate cause of the damage.

## Intercourse case

### Intercourse injury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבָּהּ בַּר נָתָן מֵרַב הוּנָא: הַמַּזִּיק אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּבִרְשׁוּת קָעָבֵיד – פָּטוּר, אוֹ דִילְמָא, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְעַיּוֹנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabba bar Natan asked Rav Huna:** With regard to **one who causes injury to his wife during sexual intercourse, what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable to pay damages? Is it reasoned that **since he is acting in a permitted manner** he is **exempt, or perhaps he should pay attention** and be more careful?

With this framework in place, the *gemara* introduces a striking question posed by Rabbah bar Natan to Rav Huna: *ha-mazik et ishto b'tashmish ha-mitah mahu* — what is the law if a husband injures his wife during intercourse? The case is whether she can collect damages from him. The *gemara* frames the question as a tension between two possible principles. On one hand, *keivan d'v'reshus ka-avid patur* — since they are engaged with permission, perhaps the husband is exempt, much as two parties walking with *reshus* in *reshus harabim* are exempt when they collide. On the other hand, *o dilma ibayei lei l'ayuni* — perhaps it does not matter that the act is permitted; the husband is still required to be attentive and careful, and a failure of attention constitutes negligence.

The question also touches on a deeper issue from the *mishnah*: to what extent does each party bear responsibility for paying attention? Is one always obligated to be alert to the potential consequences of one's physical actions, even when acting within a domain of permission?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: שֶׁלָּזֶה רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ, וְלָזֶה רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said to** Rabba bar Natan: **You learned this** *halakha* in the mishna concerning one person walking with a cross beam and another with a barrel, which rules that the owner of the cross beam is exempt **because this one had permission to walk and that one** also **had permission to walk.** Similarly, since the husband has permission to engage in intercourse with his wife, if he injures her in the process he is exempt.

Rav Huna responds by pointing to the *mishnah* itself: *l'ze reshus l'halech u'l'ze reshus l'halech* — each had permission to proceed, and they are both exempt. The implication is that mutual permission and mutual participation together produce exemption.

### Rava's argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר. וּמָה יַעַר – שֶׁזֶּה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס וְזֶה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁנִּכְנַס לִרְשׁוּת חֲבֵירוֹ – וְחַיָּיב; זֶה, שֶׁלִּרְשׁוּת חֲבֵירוֹ נִכְנַס, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** disagreed with Rav Huna's opinion and **said:** The husband is liable due to an ***a fortiori*** inference from the *halakha* with regard to manslaughter, as it is stated in the Torah: "As when a man goes into the forest with his neighbor to chop wood…and the head slips off the helve, and finds his neighbor, and he dies; he shall flee to one of these cities and live" (Deuteronomy 19:5). **And just as** in the **forest, where this** person **entered his domain and that** person **entered his domain,** as it is the domain of the public, and nevertheless the one who kills unintentionally is **considered like one who entered another's domain and** is therefore **liable** to be exiled to a city of refuge, then with regard to **this** husband, **who** actually **enters another's domain, all the more so** is it **not** clear that he should be liable for the injury he causes her?

Rava, however, objects with a *kal vachomer* drawn from the case of *galus*. The *gemara* uses the case of the *yaar* — the forest — where the *Torah* envisions someone chopping wood while another person is also present in the forest, both of them having entered with permission. Despite the mutual *reshus*, the one who causes the death is still liable for *galus*, meaning that even in a domain of shared permission, one is held responsible for harm done to another. From this, Rava reasons: *zeh shebeyedias chavero nichnas lo kol shekein* — here, in the intercourse case, the man is entering the woman's domain, so to speak, and all the more so should he bear responsibility for any injury he causes.

### Active vs passive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם – תַּרְוַיְיהוּ כַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ; הָכָא – אִיהוּ קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The two cases are different. **There,** in the case in the mishna, **both** sides **were similarly** walking, and the one who caused the damage is therefore exempt due to his right to walk there. By contrast, **here,** the husband is the only active participant in the intercourse. Therefore, since **he is** the one **performing an action,** he is liable even though he is acting with permission.

The *gemara* responds by drawing a critical distinction. In the *mishnah*'s case of the barrel and beam, both parties are equally active — *travayhu ka-adaddi ninhu*, they are equivalent to one another. In the intercourse case, *ihu ka-avid ma'aseh vehi lo* — he is the one performing the act, and she is not. This is what removes it from the analogy of the *mishnah*. The *gemara* does not here accept that the woman is fully passive in an ontological sense — it notes the verse *venichresu hanefashos ha'osos mikerev amam*, which refers to both parties in a forbidden act as *osas*, as active, suggesting both derive pleasure. But the *gemara* responds: *hana'ah letravayhu ais lehu, ihu ma'aseh hu dekavid* — yes, they both experience pleasure, which is why the woman has some participatory role, but he is the one performing the physical act. The same framework appears elsewhere in the *gemara* in discussions of coercion, such as the case of *Esther karka olam*, where Esther's passivity is invoked to explain why she was not considered to have actively transgressed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא לָא?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְנִכְרְתוּ הַנְּפָשׁוֹת הָעֹשֹׂת מִקֶּרֶב עַמָּם״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is she not** considered an active participant? **But isn't it written** with regard to forbidden sexual intercourse: **"Even the souls that do them shall be cut off from among their people"** (Leviticus 18:29), indicating that both the man and woman are considered to be performing an action?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲנָאָה – לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ אִית לְהוּ, אִיהוּ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא דְּקָעָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The verse is referring to the fact that **they both have pleasure** from the act. The woman's pleasure is tantamount to active transgression, and she is therefore punished if she participates willfully. But with regard to a wife's injury, **he is** the one who is considered to be **performing an action,** and he is therefore liable.

### Both parameters

This discussion illuminates a precise and important clarification of the *mishnah*. For the exemption to apply — for the crash to be no one's fault — two parameters must simultaneously be present: both parties must be acting, and both must have *reshus*. Remove either parameter, and liability attaches to the one whose situation is deficient.

If both are active but one lacks *reshus* — say, one person had no right to be carrying a beam in *reshus harabim* — that person bears the blame. If both have *reshus* but one is stationary and the other is moving — say, the barrel-carrier is simply standing still and the beam-carrier walks into him — the one who moved is the actor and is liable. The *yaar* case, even granting that *galus* operates by the same logic, involves only one party performing the act — the one chopping wood — while the other is simply present. In the intercourse case, the *gemara* understands the husband as the actor. Hence, in neither of those cases do both parameters obtain simultaneously, and the *mishnah*'s exemption does not apply.

## Cows case

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אַחַת רְבוּצָה וְאַחַת מְהַלֶּכֶת; בָּעֲטָה מְהַלֶּכֶת בָּרְבוּצָה – פְּטוּרָה. בָּעֲטָה רְבוּצָה בַּמְּהַלֶּכֶת – חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** If **two cows** were **in the public domain, one** of them **prone and one walking,** and the cow that was **walking kicked the** cow that was **prone,** its owner is **exempt.** If the **prone** cow **kicked** the cow that was **walking,** its owner is **liable** to pay.

The *gemara* now presents a ruling from Reish Lakish involving two cows in *reshus harabim*: one is crouching, one is walking. Reish Lakish rules that if the walking cow kicks the crouching one, it is *ptura* — exempt. The crouching cow had no right to be crouching in *reshus harabim*, and since it created the problematic situation, the walker bears no liability even for an active kick. The reverse — *revutza bamehalechet*, the crouching cow kicking the walking one — is obviously *chayevet*, liable. The genuine novelty in Reish Lakish's ruling is the first half: that even an active aggressive act like kicking is exempt when the victim had no right to be in that position.

### Support attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הָיָה בַּעַל קוֹרָה רִאשׁוֹן וּבַעַל חָבִית אַחֲרוֹן, נִשְׁבְּרָה הֶחָבִית בַּקוֹרָה – פָּטוּר. וְאִם עָמַד בַּעַל קוֹרָה – חַיָּיב. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּכִרְבוּצָה בַּמְּהַלֶּכֶת דָּמֵי, וְקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say that** the mishna **supports** this statement, as it states: If **the owner of the** cross **beam was first and the owner of the barrel was last,** and **the barrel was broken by the** cross **beam, he is exempt. But if the owner of the** cross **beam stopped,** the owner of the cross beam is **liable.** The Gemara explains the proof: **But here,** it is clear **that** it is **like** a case where the **prone** cow kicked the cow that was **walking,** since the one carrying the barrel was walking and the one carrying the cross beam stopped in the public domain, causing damage to the former. **And** the mishna **teaches** in this case that the owner of the cross beam **is liable.**

The *gemara* attempts to derive support for Reish Lakish from the second part of the *mishnah*: when the barrel-carrier was leading and the beam-carrier was following, and the barrel-carrier stopped, the beam-carrier who walked into him is *patur*. The *gemara* draws a parallel: the barrel-carrier who stopped without permission is like the cow that crouched without permission, and the beam-carrier who continues into him is like the walking cow that kicks the crouching one — and in both cases the result is exemption. This seems to confirm Reish Lakish.

### Horizontal beam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא?! הָא סַיֹּיעֵי בָּעֲיָא; לָא מִסָּתְיָיא דְּלָא מְסַיְּיעִי, אֶלָּא מִקְשָׁה נָמֵי קַשְׁיָא! טַעְמָא דְּבָעֲטָה, הָא הוּזְּקָה מִמֵּילָא – פָּטוּר; וְהָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּמִמֵּילָא, וְקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And** how can **you understand** that reasoning? **You wanted to support** the statement of Reish Lakish from the mishna. **Not only does it not support** Reish Lakish; **it even raises a difficulty** to his opinion. **The reason** Reish Lakish stated that the owner of the prone cow is liable is **that it kicked** the walking cow; **but** if the walking cow **was damaged** because **by itself** it collided with the prone cow, he would be **exempt. But the mishna** discusses a case **where** the one carrying the barrel ran into the cross beam **by himself,** without the one carrying the cross beam actively hitting it, **and it teaches** that the owner of the cross beam is nevertheless **liable.**

The *gemara*, however, rejects the proof — and in fact turns it into a difficulty. The crux is this: if the proposed comparison holds, then by Reish Lakish's logic, only an active kick generates liability for the crouching cow; if the walking cow simply stumbles into the crouching one passively, *huzka mimmeila patur* — the crouching cow would be exempt. But in the *mishnah*, the beam-carrier who passively continues walking into the stopped barrel-carrier is *chayav*. So rather than supporting Reish Lakish, the *mishnah* seems to challenge him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין – דִּפְסַקָה לְאוֹרְחָא כְּשִׁלְדָּא; הָכָא – כְּגוֹן דִּרְבַעָה בְּחַד גִּיסָא, אִיבְּעִי לַהּ לְסַגּוֹיֵי בְּאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The mishna** is referring to a case **where** the cross beam **blocked the** entire width of the **road like a carcass.** Since the one carrying the barrel could not avoid it, the one carrying the cross beam is liable although he did not actively break the barrel. By contrast, **here,** Reish Lakish's statement is referring to a case **where** the cow **was lying down on one side** of the public domain, and the other cow **should have walked on the other,** unobstructed **side.** Therefore, if the damage is caused only as a result of their collision, the owner of the prone cow is exempt.

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the cases on technical grounds. The *mishnah* speaks of a scenario where the beam was being carried horizontally — *depaska le'orcha keshilda* — spanning the width of *reshus harabim*, so that the barrel-carrier had absolutely no way to avoid it. That is why passive continuation of movement into it generates liability. In Reish Lakish's cow case, by contrast, when a cow crouches on one side of the road, *ibayei lei lesiguye be'idach gisa* — the other animal has the ability and natural instinct to detect the obstacle and walk around it. The fact that it kicked rather than navigated around is what makes it an active choice requiring justification.

The same logic applies back to the *mishnah* itself: if the beam were carried vertically rather than horizontally, the barrel-carrier behind would be *patur* even if the beam-carrier stopped, because the one behind should not have been following so closely and at such a pace that he lacked reaction time. It is specifically because the beam was horizontal — blocking the entire width — that the passive continuation of movement gives rise to liability.

## Running in reshus

### Running mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אֶחָד רָץ וְאֶחָד מְהַלֵּךְ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם רָצִין, וְהִזִּיקוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **two** people **who were walking in the public domain,** or **one** who was **running and** another **one** who was **walking, or who were both running, and they damaged one another, both of them are exempt.**

The next *mishnah* introduces another scenario in *reshus harabim*: two people moving together, one running and one walking, or both running, who collide and injure one another. The ruling is that both are *peturin*, exempt. This parallels the earlier *mishnah* in structure, but raises a fresh question: one might have thought that the runner bears more fault because he is moving with greater energy and force. The *mishnah* tells us otherwise. If one person is standing still and a runner crashes into him, the runner is *chayav* — he ran into me. But if we are both in motion, even at different speeds, the question is whether both were moving within their permitted range. The *chiddush* here is the premise that running in *reshus harabim* is itself permissible.

### Isi ben Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּאִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רָץ – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מְשׁוּנֶּה. וּמוֹדֶה אִיסִי בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרָץ בִּרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Isi ben Yehuda. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Isi ben Yehuda says** that one who **runs** in the public domain and causes damage **is liable** to pay for any damage he causes **because** his behavior **is unusual** in the public domain. **And Isi concedes** with regard to one who runs and causes damage at **twilight on the eve of Shabbat that he is exempt, because he is running with permission.**

The *gemara* notes that the *mishnah* is not in accordance with the view of Isi ben Yehuda. As the *baraisa* records: *ratz chayav mipnei shehu meshaneh* — the one running is *chayav* because running constitutes a deviation from normal conduct in *reshus harabim*; one exceeds, as it were, the speed limit. Isi ben Yehuda does grant one exception: *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, when everyone is rushing to prepare for *Shabbos*. In that context, running is permitted — *mipnei sheratz birshus* — because he runs with *reshus* — and so one who collides while running at that time is *patur*.

The *gemara* notes a linguistic precision: Isi ben Yehuda says *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, not simply *erev Shabbos*. The question arises whether *bein hashmashos* is meant literally or serves as a somewhat loose indicator of the broader pre-*Shabbos* rush. There are still preparations one may perform during *bein hashmashos*, and perhaps the term is intended to encompass the entire period of *Shabbos* preparation rather than twilight alone.

### Rav Yochanan rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּאִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה. וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה, וּתְנַן: אֶחָד רָץ וְאֶחָד מְהַלֵּךְ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם רָצִין – פְּטוּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Isi ben Yehuda.** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this,** that if one runs and causes damage he is liable? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say,** as a principle, that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with an unattributed mishna? And we learned** in this mishna that if **one was running and** the other **one was walking, or** if **they were both running, they are exempt.**

Rav Yochanan rules in accordance with Isi ben Yehuda. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: did Rav Yochanan not also rule that the *halacha* follows a *stam mishnah*, an unattributed *mishnah*? And does our *mishnah* not say explicitly that one running and one walking are both *patur*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The mishna,** which exempts one who was running, is referring **to twilight on the eve of Shabbat,** when people are permitted to run in the public domain.

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the *mishnah* as consistent with Isi ben Yehuda. The question is: why does the *mishnah* add the case of two people both running? If one person running and one walking already yields exemption, the case of both running is obviously less of a novelty and need not be stated separately. The *gemara* uses this redundancy as a signal that the cases must be read differently. The implication is as follows: *echad ratz v'echad mehalech patur* — one running and one walking are *patur* — applies specifically during *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, when running has *reshus*. But *aval b'chol*, on a regular weekday, the running party would be *chayav*. The case of both running together being *patur* — that applies even on a weekday, because when both parties are running, neither has more right than the other; both are equally in excess of the permitted pace, and the imbalance that would generate individual liability is absent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי: אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם רָצִין – פְּטוּרִין. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַשְׁתָּא אֶחָד רָץ וְאֶחָד מְהַלֵּךְ – פָּטוּר, שְׁנֵיהֶם רָצִים מִיבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד רָץ וְאֶחָד מְהַלֵּךְ – פָּטוּר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת, אֲבָל בַּחוֹל – אֶחָד רָץ וְאֶחָד מְהַלֵּךְ, חַיָּיב. שְׁנֵיהֶם רָצִין – אֲפִילּוּ בַּחוֹל פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **From where** is it inferred that the mishna is referring to twilight on the eve of Shabbat? It is inferred **from** the fact **that it teaches: Or who were both running, they are exempt. Why do I need this** case **as well? Now** that the mishna teaches that if **one was running and** the other **one was walking,** the one running is **exempt, is** it **necessary** to state that he is exempt when **both of them were running? Rather, this is** what the mishna **is saying:** If **one was running and** the other **one was walking,** he is **exempt. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to twilight on the eve of Shabbat,** when running in the public domain is permitted. **But on a weekday,** if **one was running and** the other **one was walking,** the one who was running is **liable.** If **both were running, even on a weekday, they are exempt.** This emendation explains the need to mention the case where both were running.

This also reveals a broader principle: what matters for liability is not simply whether one is acting improperly in isolation, but whether there is an asymmetry between the parties. If both are below the speed limit, *patur*. If one is above and one is below, the one above is *chayav*. If both are above, *patur* again — the imbalance dissolves.

### Erev Shabbos rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּמוֹדֶה אִיסִי בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרָץ בִּרְשׁוּת. בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת מַאי בִּרְשׁוּת אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above: **And Isi concedes** with regard to one who runs and causes damage at **twilight on the eve of Shabbat that he is exempt, because he is running with permission.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the reason that running at twilight **on the eve of Shabbat** is considered to be **with permission?**

The *gemara* then asks: what exactly is the *reshus* to run on *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*? The question is essentially: why is the urgency of *Shabbos* preparation sufficient to grant permission to run in *reshus harabim*? The answer comes from the practice of Rabbi Chanina.

### Kabalat Shabbos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״בּוֹאוּ וְנֵצֵא לִקְרַאת כַּלָּה מַלְכְּתָא״. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: ״לִקְרַאת שַׁבָּת – כַּלָּה מַלְכְּתָא״. רַבִּי יַנַּאי מִתְעַטֵּף וְקָאֵי, וְאָמַר: ״בּוֹאִי כַלָּה, בּוֹאִי כַלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **like** that **which Rabbi Ḥanina** would say, **as Rabbi Ḥanina would say** at twilight on the eve of Shabbat: **Come and let us go out to greet the bride, the queen. And some say** that this is what he would say: Come and let us go out **to greet Shabbat, the bride, the queen. Rabbi Yannai would wrap himself** in his *tallit* **and stand** at the eve of Shabbat at twilight, **saying: Come, bride; come, bride.** Similarly, it is appropriate for one to run out in honor of Shabbat.

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Chanina, who would go out saying *bo venetzei likras kalla malkesa* — come, let us go out to greet the Shabbos, the bride, the queen. Some versions have him say *likras Shabbos kalla malkesa* explicitly. Rabbi Yannai's practice differed: he would wrap himself in his *tallis* and stand, waiting, and declare *bo'i kalla bo'i kalla* — enter, O bride, enter, O bride. He did not go out to greet; he waited to receive.

These two brief lines of *gemara* are, of course, the foundational source for the practice of *Kabbalas Shabbos*. The image of *Shabbos* as a bride and queen — both titles appear here — is striking in several dimensions. The feminine framing is itself notable: God is typically cast in the masculine in rabbinic literature, and here it is *Shabbos* itself, not God, that is feminized and welcomed as a bride in union with a groom. The Kabbalists developed this imagery extensively, and it was the *Ari* who institutionalized the practice of going out to the fields to welcome *Shabbos*, drawing directly on this passage. The difference between Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Yannai — going out to greet versus standing to receive — even finds expression in certain customs surrounding the *chuppah*. That said, precisely how Rabbi Chanina's practice of greeting the *Shabbos* bride — which *Rabbeinu Chananel* describes as accompanied by dancing — provides the specific *reshus* to run in *reshus harabim* during *bein hashmashos* remains somewhat difficult to fully resolve.

## Chopping wood

### Chopping mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְבַקֵּעַ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד; בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד אַחֵר, חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was chopping** wood **in the public domain and** a chip flew off and **caused damage in** the **private property** of another person, or one who was chopping wood **in** his **private property and caused damage in the public domain,** or one who was chopping wood **in** his **private property** and **caused damage in** the **private property** of **another,** in all these cases he is **liable.**

The next *mishnah* introduces the wood-chopping scenarios, bringing us close to the *gemara*'s broader discussion of *galus*. Three cases are presented: one who chops wood in *reshus harabim* and a chip flies into *reshus hayachid*, causing damage; one who chops in *reshus hayachid* and causes damage in *reshus harabim*; and one who chops in *reshus hayachid* and causes damage in another person's *reshus hayachid*. In all three cases, *bireshus acher chayav* — one is *chayav*. The principle of *adam muad l'olam* applies throughout: the wood-chopper is the *mazik*, the other party is the *nizak*, and since the other party is stationary and the damage flowed from the chopper's act, liability attaches regardless of the domain configuration.

### Three cases needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי תְּנָא: הַמְבַקֵּעַ בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים; אֲבָל מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach that he is liable in all these cases because in each one there is a novel element. **As if it had taught** only the case of **one who was chopping** wood **in** his **private property and caused damage in the public domain,** it might have been reasoned that he is liable, despite the fact that he was working in his private property, **because** it is **common** for the **multitudes** to be there. **But** if a chip flew **from the public domain to** another person's **private property, where** it is **not common** for the **multitudes** to be, one might **say** that he is **not** liable. Therefore, it is necessary to teach this case as well.

The *gemara* explains why all three cases are necessary, as each one might have been thought exempt for a distinct reason. One might have said: if you are chopping in *reshus hayachid* and the chip flies into *reshus harabim*, you are *chayav* only because *mishum deshchichi rabbim* — the public domain is heavily trafficked and you must be especially careful about damage flying in that direction. But perhaps if you are chopping in *reshus harabim* and it flies into *reshus hayachid* — *mishum deshelo birshus avid* — you would be *chayav* only because you had no right to be chopping in the public domain to begin with, not because of the destination. Remove one of these reasons in each case, and one might think there is no liability. The third case — chopping in one's own *reshus hayachid* with damage flying into another's *reshus hayachid* — has neither factor: you had full permission to chop, and the destination has few people. One might think you are exempt. The *mishnah* therefore states all three explicitly: *tzericha*, each case is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא: מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת עָבֵיד; אֲבָל מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, דְּבִרְשׁוּת עָבֵיד – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if it had taught** that he is liable where the chip flew **from the public domain to** another's **private property,** it might have been reasoned that he is liable **because at the outset he was acting without permission by chopping wood in the public domain. But** if it flew **from** his **private property to the public domain, since he was acting with permission** by chopping wood in his private property, one might **say** that he is **not** liable. Therefore, it is necessary to teach both cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי – הָא מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים, וְהָא מִשּׁוּם דְּשֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת; אֲבָל מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד אַחֵר – דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים, וּמֵעִיקָּרָא בִּרְשׁוּת – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna **had taught** only that he is liable in **these two** cases, it might have been reasoned that in **this** case, where the damage was caused in the public domain, he is liable **because** it is **common** for the **multitudes** to be there, **and** in **that** case, where he was chopping in the public domain, he is liable **because** he was acting **without permission. But** in the last case, where the chip flew **from** his **private property** to **another's private property,** where neither of the above reasons applies, **as** it is **not common** for the **multitudes** to be in the place where the damage was caused **and he** was acting **with permission at the outset,** one might **say** he is **not** liable. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach all these cases.

The broader principle is that *adam muad l'olam* governs throughout. As long as you are the *mazik* and the other party the *nizak*, your right to be in your domain does not insulate you from liability for damage that escapes it.

## Carpenter case

### Baraita teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל נַגָּר שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְנִתְּזָה בְּקַעַת וְטָפְחָה עַל פָּנָיו, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִם נִכְנַס בִּרְשׁוּת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 6:25): With regard to **one who entered the workshop of a carpenter without** the latter's **permission, and a chip of wood flew off and hit him in the face and he died,** the carpenter is **exempt. But if he entered** the shop **with permission,** the carpenter is **liable.**

A *baraita* now introduces the case of a carpenter's workshop. Someone enters a carpenter's shop *shelo birshus* — without permission — and a chip flies off the beam the carpenter is working on and strikes him in the face. The ruling is *patur*: the carpenter is exempt. If, however, the person entered *birshus* — with the carpenter's permission — the carpenter is *chayav*. The logic follows directly from the principles established in the *mishnah*: if you are operating freely in your own domain and someone enters without authorization, you are not obligated to anticipate their presence; but if you invited them in, *adam muad l'olam* reasserts itself and you bear responsibility for harm done to them.

### Four payments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חַיָּיב? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים. וּפָטוּר מִגָּלוּת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it rules that the carpenter is **liable? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** If the other person was injured, he is **liable to** pay **four types of indemnity** that one who injures another must pay. These are: Cost of the damage, pain, medical costs, and loss of livelihood. **But** if the one who entered was killed, he is **exempt from exile.**

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina then clarifies the *chayav* side of the ruling: when the person entered with permission and was injured, the carpenter is *chayav* in *arba'ah devarim* — the four payments associated with bodily injury — but *patur migalus*, exempt from *galus*. The question is why. There is clearly liability for *nezek* and the associated payments, but the *galus* exemption requires explanation.

### Not like yaar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דּוֹמֶה לְיַעַר. יַעַר – זֶה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס, וְזֶה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס; זֶה – לִרְשׁוּת חֲבֵירוֹ נִכְנַס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that he is exempt **because** this case **is not similar to** the case of **a forest,** which is the archetypal case stated in the Torah requiring one who kills unintentionally to be exiled, as it is written: "As when a man goes into the forest with his neighbor to chop wood, and his hand fetches a stroke with the axe to cut down the tree, and the head slips off the helve, and finds his neighbor, and he dies" (Deuteronomy 19:5). This is because in the case of the **forest,** both **this one entered his domain and that one entered his domain,** as anyone may use the public domain, whereas in **this** case, the victim **entered another's property.** Therefore, the carpenter is not exiled.

The initial explanation offered is *lefi she'ein domeh leyaar* — because this case is not analogous to the *yaar*, the forest scenario that the *Torah* uses as the paradigm for *galus*. By the forest, *zeh lereshus nichnas vezeh lereshus nichnas* — both parties entered a shared, public space with equal right of access and without awareness of one another. Here, the injured party entered the carpenter's private domain, even if with permission. The argument is formalistic: since the *Torah*'s case is one of shared public access, a case involving entry into private property — even with consent — falls outside that paradigm and does not trigger *galus*.

### Rava's view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה יַעַר – זֶה לְדַעְתּוֹ נִכְנָס וְזֶה לְדַעְתּוֹ נִכְנָס, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁנִּכְנַס לְדַעַת חֲבֵירוֹ – וְגוֹלֶה; זֶה – שֶׁלְּדַעַת חֲבֵירוֹ נִכְנָס, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** On the contrary; it can be inferred ***a fortiori*** that he is exiled. **And if** in the case of the **forest,** where **this one entered of his own accord and that one entered of his own accord,** neither asking for the other's permission, nevertheless the victim **is considered like one who entered with the other's consent and** therefore the one who kills unintentionally **is exiled, all the more so** is it **not** clear that in **this** case, **where** the victim **entered another's** workshop **with his consent,** the carpenter should be exiled?

Rava challenges this formalistic approach with a *kal vachomer*. Rather than focusing on whose domain it is, Rava reframes the discussion in terms of *da'as* — awareness and negligence. In the *yaar* case, *zeh l'da'aso nichnas vezeh l'da'aso nichnas* — each party entered independently, with no knowledge of the other, and yet the one who caused the death is *chayav galus*. Since even low awareness generates *galus* liability in the forest case, how much more so here, where *zeh shebeyedias chavero nichnas lo kol shekein* — the victim entered with the carpenter's knowledge and perhaps his explicit consent? The carpenter here has more awareness than the forest-chopper, not less. By this logic, one might conclude that *galus* should apply here with even greater force.

### Too much negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי פָּטוּר מִגָּלוּת? דְּלָא סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּגָלוּת. וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי לֵיהּ שׁוֹגֵג קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: What** is the reason **he is exempt from exile? As exile is not sufficient for him, and this is the reason of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina,** who exempts him from exile: **Because it is** an **unintentional** killing that is **approaching intentional** manslaughter. The purpose of exile is to atone for one who kills another completely unintentionally; if he was exceedingly negligent, exile is not sufficient to atone for him.

Rava, however, wishes to preserve the ruling of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina that the carpenter is *patur migalus*, and so he reinterprets it. *Patur migalus d'lo sagi lei begalus* — he is exempt from *galus* not because there is too little negligence to warrant it, but because there is too much. *Mishum d'hava lei shogeg karov l'meizid* — his negligence is of the type called *shogeg karov l'meizid*, inadvertence so close to intentionality that it falls outside the redemptive scope of *galus*. This is the counterintuitive dynamic of *galus*: ordinarily, greater culpability brings greater consequence, but *galus* is understood as providing atonement specifically for those in a middle zone of *shogeg*. One who is too close to *meizid* is not granted the atonement that *galus* affords — not because he is being let off lightly, but because *galus* is simply insufficient for his level of guilt.

### Extra lash objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: הוֹסִיף לוֹ רְצוּעָה אַחַת, וָמֵת – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה עַל יָדוֹ. וְהָא הָכָא דְּשׁוֹגֵג קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד הוּא, דְּאִיבְּעִי אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּמַיְיתֵי אִינָשֵׁי בַּחֲדָא רְצוּעָה, וְקָתָנֵי: הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה! אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: דְּטָעֵי בְּמִנְיָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** to his own explanation from a mishna: If one is sentenced to be flogged in court and the doctors assessed that he would be able to endure only a certain number of lashes, but the one administering the lashes **added one lash to his** punishment **and he died,** the agent of the court **is exiled on account of him** (*Makkot* 22b). **But here,** it is clear **that it is** a case of **an unintentional** killing that is **approaching intentional** manslaughter, **as it should have entered his mind that people can die by one** additional **lash. And** the *tanna* **teaches** that the agent of the court **is exiled. Rav Shimi of Neharde'a said** in response: It is a case **where he erred in the counting,** which is not considered approaching intentional manslaughter.

Rabbah raises a challenge from the case of a court official who administered lashes: if the official gave one extra lash beyond the prescribed number and the condemned person died as a result, the official goes into *galus*. Yet here, Rabbah argues, the negligence is very high — *d'ibayei asukei deitei d'mayis b'chadei retzuah* — the official should have been acutely aware that an extra lash could be fatal. Moreover, he had no right whatsoever to administer that extra blow. This seems to place him squarely in the category of *shogeg karov l'meizid*, yet the ruling is that he does go into *galus*.

### Judge miscounted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְפַח לֵיהּ רָבָא בְּסַנְדָּלֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ הוּא מָנֵי?! וְהָתַנְיָא: גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּדַיָּינִין קוֹרֵא, וְהַשֵּׁנִי מוֹנֶה, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי אוֹמֵר: ״הַכֵּהוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava slapped** Rav Shimi **on his sandal,** a gesture of disparagement, and **said to him: Is that to say** that the one administering the lashes is the one who **counts** them? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **the eldest of the judges recites** the verses that are read to a person while he receives lashes, **and the second** judge **counts, and the third says** to the one administering the lashes: **Strike him?** Accordingly, it is not the one administering the lashes who erred in counting.

Rabbah reportedly struck Rav Shimi with his sandal in exasperation at this answer — noting that the official is not the one doing the counting; the *baraisa* specifies that one judge reads the verse, a second counts, and a third calls out to strike. The official is only executing the command, not tracking the number himself. Therefore the error was entirely on the judge's side, and the official had no independent reason to know he was delivering an extra blow. Since the official was acting in good faith on the information he received, he is not in the category of *shogeg karov l'meizid*, and *galus* applies normally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: דִּטְעָה דַּיָּינָא גּוּפֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Shimi of Neharde'a said:** It is a case **where the judge himself erred** in counting, and the one administering the lashes did not notice this error and meted out an extra lash, causing the person to die. It is therefore considered a completely unintentional killing. Consequently, he is exiled.

### Stone-thrower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַזּוֹרֵק אֶת הָאֶבֶן לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהָרַג – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּשׁוֹגֵג קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד הוּא – דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּבִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים שְׁכִיחִי אִינָשֵׁי; וְקָתָנֵי: הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from another mishna: With regard to **one who throws a stone into the public domain and kills** someone, **he is exiled. But here,** it is clear **that it is** a case of **an unintentional** killing that is **approaching intentional** manslaughter, **as it should have entered his mind that people are commonly** found **in the public domain. And** the *tanna* **teaches** that **he is exiled.**

A further challenge is raised from the case of one who throws a stone into *reshus harabim* and kills someone: *hazorek even l'reshus harabim v'harag harei zeh goleh*, he goes into *galus*. Yet this too seems to involve high negligence — *d'ibayei asukei adei d'reshus harabim shchichi inish* — one is obligated to be aware that people are present in the public domain, and throwing a stone there is an action whose dangerous potential is manifest. If such high negligence still yields *galus*, why does the carpenter's case with permission not also yield *galus*?

### Wall demolition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּסוֹתֵר אֶת כּוֹתְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said:** It is not a case where one threw a stone into the public domain for no purpose, but rather **where he demolishes his wall,** which borders on the public domain, and stones fall into the public domain. Therefore, it is not considered to be approaching intentional manslaughter.

Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak responds by situating the stone-thrower case in a particular set of circumstances: he is *soseis kosalo* — knocking down his wall — and doing so at night, when the streets are empty and the risk of encountering a passerby is minimal. The *gemara* presses: even at night one should be paying attention. The answer is that he is knocking it down into a *besusa d'vei hiti beyoma d'ashpa* — into a dump site, a place people do not ordinarily frequent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ עַיּוֹנֵי! בְּסוֹתֵר בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this assertion: Nevertheless, **he should have paid attention** to see if there was anyone there, and therefore it should be considered an unintentional killing that approaches intentional manslaughter. The Gemara answers: It is a case **where he demolishes** the wall **at night.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּלַּיְלָה נָמֵי אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ עַיּוֹנֵי! בְּסוֹתֵר אֶת כּוֹתְלוֹ בַּיּוֹם – לְאַשְׁפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Even **at night, he should also have paid attention** to see if there was anyone there. The Gemara suggests an alternative interpretation: It is a case **where he demolishes his wall during the day into a garbage dump,** where people are not commonly found.

### Dump analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אַשְׁפָּה הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים – מֵזִיד הוּא! וְאִי לָא שְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this garbage dump? If** it is a garbage dump **where the multitudes are commonly** found, **it is** considered **intentional** manslaughter. **And if the multitudes are not commonly** found there, he should be considered not only one who kills unintentionally, but **a victim of circumstances beyond his control,** since he could not have anticipated that someone would be there. Therefore, he should be exempt from exile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: [לָא צְרִיכָא, אֶלָּא] בְּאַשְׁפָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לְהִפָּנוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְהִפָּנוֹת בַּיּוֹם, וְאִיכָּא דְּמִיקְּרֵי וְיָתֵיב; מֵזִיד לָא הָוֵי – דְּהָא אֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְהִפָּנוֹת בַּיּוֹם, אָנוּס נָמֵי לָא הָוֵי – דְּהָא אִיכָּא דְּמִיקְּרֵי וְיָתֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** This *halakha* **is necessary only in** the case of **a garbage dump where** people are **given to relieve themselves at night and are not given to relieve themselves during the day,** as it is near the public domain. **But there are those who chance by and sit** there for this purpose even during the day. On the one hand, **he is not** one who kills **intentionally, as** people **are not given to relieve themselves** there **during the day.** On the other hand, **he is not a victim of circumstances beyond his control either, as there are those who chance by and sit there.** Therefore, he is considered one who kills unintentionally and is liable to be exiled.

Rav Pappa refines the analysis: *lo tzericha ela be'ashpa ha'asuyah lehifanot balaila ve'ein asuyah lehifanot beyom* — the dump is used as a latrine at night but not during the day, except that occasionally someone might use it even during the day out of urgent necessity. The result is a precise calibration: *mezid lo havei* — it is not *meizid*, because the dump is not ordinarily used during the day. *Ones nami lo havei* — but it is not *ones* either, because the occasional urgent visitor does occur. This middle ground of modest but not negligible risk is precisely the zone of *shogeg* in which *galus* applies. The point is that this profile of risk is less foreseeable than the profile in the carpenter case with permission, where — as *Tosafos* emphasizes — the permission was recently granted, meaning the person might walk in at any moment.

### Alternative reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנִי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל נַגָּר שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְנִתְּזָה לוֹ בְּקַעַת וְטָפְחָה לוֹ עַל פָּנָיו, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים, וּפָטוּר מִגָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa taught in the name of Rava** that **this** aforementioned statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina is in reference not to the latter clause of the *baraita* but is **in reference to the first clause:** With regard to **one who enters the workshop of a carpenter without permission, and a chip** of wood **flies off and strikes him in the face and he dies,** the carpenter is **exempt.** In reference to this clause **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** If the one who entered was merely injured, the carpenter is **liable to** pay **four types of indemnity. But** if the one who entered was killed, he is **exempt from exile.**

Rav Pappa, citing Rava, then offers an entirely different reading of the *baraita*. Rather than placing the *baraita*'s entire discussion in the realm of *nezek*, he now teaches the ruling on the *first* part of the *baraita* — the case where the person entered without permission. On this reading, the *baraita* is not primarily about *nezek* at all; it is about *galus*. The case of entering without permission yields exemption from *galus*; entering with permission yields liability for *galus*. This is the *baraita*'s subject throughout.

This reading comes with its own difficulty, however: without permission, the carpenter is *patur* in *galus*, but what is the rule for *nezek*? Rabbi Yose bar Abba Chanina clarifies: *chayav b'arba'ah devarim* — he is still *chayav* in the four payments for bodily harm. This requires explanation, since without permission one might have thought there is no liability at all. Both Rashi and *Tosafos* explain that the carpenter was aware the person had entered even without permission, and that awareness establishes sufficient grounds for *nezek* liability even absent formal *reshus*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַרֵישָׁא; וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא – אֲבָל אַסֵּיפָא, כֵּיוָן דְּבִרְשׁוּת – חַיָּיב גָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: With regard to **the one who teaches this** statement **in reference to the last clause** of the *baraita*, where one enters with permission, **all the more so** would he teach it **in reference to the first clause,** where one enters without permission. **But the one who teaches this** statement **in reference to the first clause** teaches it only in reference to that clause. **But in the** case of the **last clause, since** he entered **with permission** the carpenter is **liable** to go into **exile.**

### Galus focus

The *gemara* closes by drawing out the two readings' implications for one another. The one who teaches the *baraita*'s ruling on the second clause — that with *reshus* one is *patur migalus* because the negligence is too great — will certainly also teach that without *reshus* one is *patur migalus* by simple logic. But the one who teaches the *baraita* as addressing the first clause — that without *reshus* one is *patur migalus* — holds that with *reshus* one is in fact *chayav galus*: *keivan d'v'reshus chayav galus*.

The clean formulation that emerges from the second reading: without *reshus*, *patur* in *galus*; with *reshus*, *chayav* in *galus*. And in both cases, for *nezek* proper, the rule tracks *adam muad l'olam* — without *reshus* one is *patur* in *nezek*, with *reshus* one is *chayav* in *nezek*. The first reading's *chiddush* — that even with permission the carpenter is *patur migalus* because of excessive negligence — is rejected by the second reading, which restores the intuitive symmetry: *reshus* is the operative factor for *galus* liability just as it is for *nezek*.
