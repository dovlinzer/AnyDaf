# Bava Kamma 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mutual collision

### Barrel & beam

The *mishnah* with which we begin, found at the bottom of 31b, presents a classic collision scenario in *reshus harabim*: two people are walking in the public domain, one carrying a barrel of wine or oil, the other transporting a beam to a construction site. The inevitable happens — they crash into one another, and the barrel breaks against the beam. The ruling is *patur*, exempt, and the reason is straightforward: *shelo zeh reshus l'halech v'zeh reshus l'halech* — each person had a right to be there. Neither, in the language of traffic law, ran a red light. Since both were operating with permission, one cannot be held more culpable than the other, even though it was the barrel — and not the beam — that broke. The mere fact that one party sustained more damage is insufficient to impose liability.

### Adam muad principle

At first glance, one might invoke the principle of *adam muad l'olam* — that a person always carries absolute liability for the damage he causes — and argue that the man with the beam should be held responsible for breaking the barrel. *Tosafos* addresses this by explaining that *adam muad l'olam* does not operate as literal absolute liability; there must be some degree of negligence. Under *Tosafos*'s approach, it is sufficient to say that the man with the beam was not fully negligent without reference to the other party. For those who do maintain absolute liability, the principle is offset here by *peshias hanizak* — the contributory negligence of the injured party, who was equally responsible for the collision. Alternatively, one can simply say there is no way to determine who actually caused the accident when both parties were equally active and equally present by right.

### Following collision

The *mishnah* then addresses a more structured scenario: the man with the beam is walking ahead, and the man with the barrel is following behind him. If the barrel-carrier speeds up and collides with the beam in front of him, the man with the beam is *patur*. The accident was caused by the one behind, who accelerated into the person ahead of him. This maps naturally onto the familiar principle that a rear-end collision is the fault of the vehicle that strikes from behind.

### Standing vs signaling

The analysis shifts, however, when the man with the beam stops short. In that case, *v'im amad ba'al hakora chayav* — he is *chayav*, liable, because his sudden stop is what caused the accident. His action — or rather the sudden cessation of it — is what brought the damage about. The determinative question throughout these cases is: whose act precipitated the collision? However, if the man with the beam stopped and simultaneously signaled, calling out to the one behind him that he was stopping, he is again *patur*. Having communicated his intention, the responsibility shifts back to the man with the barrel, who was duly warned and nonetheless failed to avoid the collision.

### Barrel leads

The *mishnah* then reverses the order: the man with the barrel is leading and the man with the beam is following. Here, if they collide, the man with the beam is *chayav*, because it is he — coming from behind — whose movement caused the impact. If the man with the barrel stops, he is *patur*: the trailing party with the beam simply walked into him. But if the barrel-carrier stopped and then called out to the beam-carrier, signaling the stop, *chayav* — the man with the beam is liable, because the stop was announced and he failed to respond accordingly.

The *mishnah* concludes by noting the same principles apply to one carrying a lit torch and one carrying flax — the logic is identical. Even when one object causes damage to another, the question is never simply which object was the instrument of harm, but rather whose action caused the accident to unfold.

Taken together, the structure of the *mishnah* is clear: when two parties both act with *reshus* and both contribute actively to what happens, no one can be singled out for liability. When the analysis must apportion blame, the question is always whose specific act — movement, sudden stop, or failure to signal — was the proximate cause of the damage.

## Intercourse case

### Intercourse injury

With this framework in place, the *gemara* introduces a striking question posed by Rabbah bar Natan to Rav Huna: *ha-mazik et ishto b'tashmish ha-mitah mahu* — what is the law if a husband injures his wife during intercourse? The case is whether she can collect damages from him. The *gemara* frames the question as a tension between two possible principles. On one hand, *keivan d'v'reshus ka-avid patur* — since they are engaged with permission, perhaps the husband is exempt, much as two parties walking with *reshus* in *reshus harabim* are exempt when they collide. On the other hand, *o dilma ibayei lei l'ayuni* — perhaps it does not matter that the act is permitted; the husband is still required to be attentive and careful, and a failure of attention constitutes negligence.

The question also touches on a deeper issue from the *mishnah*: to what extent does each party bear responsibility for paying attention? Is one always obligated to be alert to the potential consequences of one's physical actions, even when acting within a domain of permission?

Rav Huna responds by pointing to the *mishnah* itself: *l'ze reshus l'halech u'l'ze reshus l'halech* — each had permission to proceed, and they are both exempt. The implication is that mutual permission and mutual participation together produce exemption.

### Rava's argument

Rava, however, objects with a *kal vachomer* drawn from the case of *galus*. The *gemara* uses the case of the *yaar* — the forest — where the *Torah* envisions someone chopping wood while another person is also present in the forest, both of them having entered with permission. Despite the mutual *reshus*, the one who causes the death is still liable for *galus*, meaning that even in a domain of shared permission, one is held responsible for harm done to another. From this, Rava reasons: *zeh shebeyedias chavero nichnas lo kol shekein* — here, in the intercourse case, the man is entering the woman's domain, so to speak, and all the more so should he bear responsibility for any injury he causes.

### Active vs passive

The *gemara* responds by drawing a critical distinction. In the *mishnah*'s case of the barrel and beam, both parties are equally active — *travayhu ka-adaddi ninhu*, they are equivalent to one another. In the intercourse case, *ihu ka-avid ma'aseh vehi lo* — he is the one performing the act, and she is not. This is what removes it from the analogy of the *mishnah*. The *gemara* does not here accept that the woman is fully passive in an ontological sense — it notes the verse *venichresu hanefashos ha'osos mikerev amam*, which refers to both parties in a forbidden act as *osas*, as active, suggesting both derive pleasure. But the *gemara* responds: *hana'ah letravayhu ais lehu, ihu ma'aseh hu dekavid* — yes, they both experience pleasure, which is why the woman has some participatory role, but he is the one performing the physical act. The same framework appears elsewhere in the *gemara* in discussions of coercion, such as the case of *Esther karka olam*, where Esther's passivity is invoked to explain why she was not considered to have actively transgressed.

### Both parameters

This discussion illuminates a precise and important clarification of the *mishnah*. For the exemption to apply — for the crash to be no one's fault — two parameters must simultaneously be present: both parties must be acting, and both must have *reshus*. Remove either parameter, and liability attaches to the one whose situation is deficient.

If both are active but one lacks *reshus* — say, one person had no right to be carrying a beam in *reshus harabim* — that person bears the blame. If both have *reshus* but one is stationary and the other is moving — say, the barrel-carrier is simply standing still and the beam-carrier walks into him — the one who moved is the actor and is liable. The *yaar* case, even granting that *galus* operates by the same logic, involves only one party performing the act — the one chopping wood — while the other is simply present. In the intercourse case, the *gemara* understands the husband as the actor. Hence, in neither of those cases do both parameters obtain simultaneously, and the *mishnah*'s exemption does not apply.

## Cows case

### Reish Lakish

The *gemara* now presents a ruling from Reish Lakish involving two cows in *reshus harabim*: one is crouching, one is walking. Reish Lakish rules that if the walking cow kicks the crouching one, it is *ptura* — exempt. The crouching cow had no right to be crouching in *reshus harabim*, and since it created the problematic situation, the walker bears no liability even for an active kick. The reverse — *revutza bamehalechet*, the crouching cow kicking the walking one — is obviously *chayevet*, liable. The genuine novelty in Reish Lakish's ruling is the first half: that even an active aggressive act like kicking is exempt when the victim had no right to be in that position.

### Support attempt

The *gemara* attempts to derive support for Reish Lakish from the second part of the *mishnah*: when the barrel-carrier was leading and the beam-carrier was following, and the barrel-carrier stopped, the beam-carrier who walked into him is *patur*. The *gemara* draws a parallel: the barrel-carrier who stopped without permission is like the cow that crouched without permission, and the beam-carrier who continues into him is like the walking cow that kicks the crouching one — and in both cases the result is exemption. This seems to confirm Reish Lakish.

### Horizontal beam

The *gemara*, however, rejects the proof — and in fact turns it into a difficulty. The crux is this: if the proposed comparison holds, then by Reish Lakish's logic, only an active kick generates liability for the crouching cow; if the walking cow simply stumbles into the crouching one passively, *huzka mimmeila patur* — the crouching cow would be exempt. But in the *mishnah*, the beam-carrier who passively continues walking into the stopped barrel-carrier is *chayav*. So rather than supporting Reish Lakish, the *mishnah* seems to challenge him.

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the cases on technical grounds. The *mishnah* speaks of a scenario where the beam was being carried horizontally — *depaska le'orcha keshilda* — spanning the width of *reshus harabim*, so that the barrel-carrier had absolutely no way to avoid it. That is why passive continuation of movement into it generates liability. In Reish Lakish's cow case, by contrast, when a cow crouches on one side of the road, *ibayei lei lesiguye be'idach gisa* — the other animal has the ability and natural instinct to detect the obstacle and walk around it. The fact that it kicked rather than navigated around is what makes it an active choice requiring justification.

The same logic applies back to the *mishnah* itself: if the beam were carried vertically rather than horizontally, the barrel-carrier behind would be *patur* even if the beam-carrier stopped, because the one behind should not have been following so closely and at such a pace that he lacked reaction time. It is specifically because the beam was horizontal — blocking the entire width — that the passive continuation of movement gives rise to liability.

## Running in reshus

### Running mishnah

The next *mishnah* introduces another scenario in *reshus harabim*: two people moving together, one running and one walking, or both running, who collide and injure one another. The ruling is that both are *peturin*, exempt. This parallels the earlier *mishnah* in structure, but raises a fresh question: one might have thought that the runner bears more fault because he is moving with greater energy and force. The *mishnah* tells us otherwise. If one person is standing still and a runner crashes into him, the runner is *chayav* — he ran into me. But if we are both in motion, even at different speeds, the question is whether both were moving within their permitted range. The *chiddush* here is the premise that running in *reshus harabim* is itself permissible.

### Isi ben Yehuda

The *gemara* notes that the *mishnah* is not in accordance with the view of Isi ben Yehuda. As the *baraisa* records: *ratz chayav mipnei shehu meshaneh* — the one running is *chayav* because running constitutes a deviation from normal conduct in *reshus harabim*; one exceeds, as it were, the speed limit. Isi ben Yehuda does grant one exception: *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, when everyone is rushing to prepare for *Shabbos*. In that context, running is permitted — *mipnei sheratz birshus*, because he runs with *reshus* — and so one who collides while running at that time is *patur*.

The *gemara* notes a linguistic precision: Isi ben Yehuda says *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, not simply *erev Shabbos*. The question arises whether *bein hashmashos* is meant literally or serves as a somewhat loose indicator of the broader pre-*Shabbos* rush. There are still preparations one may perform during *bein hashmashos*, and perhaps the term is intended to encompass the entire period of *Shabbos* preparation rather than twilight alone.

### Rav Yochanan rules

Rav Yochanan rules in accordance with Isi ben Yehuda. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: did Rav Yochanan not also rule that the *halacha* follows a *stam mishnah*, an unattributed *mishnah*? And does our *mishnah* not say explicitly that one running and one walking are both *patur*?

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the *mishnah* as consistent with Isi ben Yehuda. The question is: why does the *mishnah* add the case of two people both running? If one person running and one walking already yields exemption, the case of both running is obviously less of a novelty and need not be stated separately. The *gemara* uses this redundancy as a signal that the cases must be read differently. The implication is as follows: *echad ratz v'echad mehalech patur* — one running and one walking are *patur* — applies specifically during *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*, when running has *reshus*. But *aval b'chol*, on a regular weekday, the running party would be *chayav*. The case of both running together being *patur* — that applies even on a weekday, because when both parties are running, neither has more right than the other; both are equally in excess of the permitted pace, and the imbalance that would generate individual liability is absent.

This also reveals a broader principle: what matters for liability is not simply whether one is acting improperly in isolation, but whether there is an asymmetry between the parties. If both are below the speed limit, *patur*. If one is above and one is below, the one above is *chayav*. If both are above, *patur* again — the imbalance dissolves.

### Erev Shabbos rationale

The *gemara* then asks: what exactly is the *reshus* to run on *erev Shabbos bein hashmashos*? The question is essentially: why is the urgency of *Shabbos* preparation sufficient to grant permission to run in *reshus harabim*? The answer comes from the practice of Rabbi Chanina.

### Kabalat Shabbos

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Chanina, who would go out saying *bo venetzei likras kalla malkesa* — come, let us go out to greet the Shabbos, the bride, the queen. Some versions have him say *likras Shabbos kalla malkesa* explicitly. Rabbi Yannai's practice differed: he would wrap himself in his *tallis* and stand, waiting, and declare *bo'i kalla bo'i kalla* — enter, O bride, enter, O bride. He did not go out to greet; he waited to receive.

These two brief lines of *gemara* are, of course, the foundational source for the practice of *Kabbalas Shabbos*. The image of *Shabbos* as a bride and queen — both titles appear here — is striking in several dimensions. The feminine framing is itself notable: God is typically cast in the masculine in rabbinic literature, and here it is *Shabbos* itself, not God, that is feminized and welcomed as a bride in union with a groom. The Kabbalists developed this imagery extensively, and it was the *Ari* who institutionalized the practice of going out to the fields to welcome *Shabbos*, drawing directly on this passage. The difference between Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Yannai — going out to greet versus standing to receive — even finds expression in certain customs surrounding the *chuppah*. That said, precisely how Rabbi Chanina's practice of greeting the *Shabbos* bride — which *Rabbeinu Chananel* describes as accompanied by dancing — provides the specific *reshus* to run in *reshus harabim* during *bein hashmashos* remains somewhat difficult to fully resolve.

## Chopping wood

### Chopping mishnah

The next *mishnah* introduces the wood-chopping scenarios, bringing us close to the *gemara*'s broader discussion of *galus*. Three cases are presented: one who chops wood in *reshus harabim* and a chip flies into *reshus hayachid*, causing damage; one who chops in *reshus hayachid* and causes damage in *reshus harabim*; and one who chops in *reshus hayachid* and causes damage in another person's *reshus hayachid*. In all three cases, *bireshus acher chayav* — one is *chayav*. The principle of *adam muad l'olam* applies throughout: the wood-chopper is the *mazik*, the other party is the *nizak*, and since the other party is stationary and the damage flowed from the chopper's act, liability attaches regardless of the domain configuration.

### Three cases needed

The *gemara* explains why all three cases are necessary, as each one might have been thought exempt for a distinct reason. One might have said: if you are chopping in *reshus hayachid* and the chip flies into *reshus harabim*, you are *chayav* only because *mishum deshchichi rabbim* — the public domain is heavily trafficked and you must be especially careful about damage flying in that direction. But perhaps if you are chopping in *reshus harabim* and it flies into *reshus hayachid* — *mishum deshelo birshus avid* — you would be *chayav* only because you had no right to be chopping in the public domain to begin with, not because of the destination. Remove one of these reasons in each case, and one might think there is no liability. The third case — chopping in one's own *reshus hayachid* with damage flying into another's *reshus hayachid* — has neither factor: you had full permission to chop, and the destination has few people. One might think you are exempt. The *mishnah* therefore states all three explicitly: *tzericha*, each case is needed.

The broader principle is that *adam muad l'olam* governs throughout. As long as you are the *mazik* and the other party the *nizak*, your right to be in your domain does not insulate you from liability for damage that escapes it.

## Carpenter case

### Baraita teaching

A *baraita* now introduces the case of a carpenter's workshop. Someone enters a carpenter's shop *shelo birshus* — without permission — and a chip flies off the beam the carpenter is working on and strikes him in the face. The ruling is *patur*: the carpenter is exempt. If, however, the person entered *birshus* — with the carpenter's permission — the carpenter is *chayav*. The logic follows directly from the principles established in the *mishnah*: if you are operating freely in your own domain and someone enters without authorization, you are not obligated to anticipate their presence; but if you invited them in, *adam muad l'olam* reasserts itself and you bear responsibility for harm done to them.

### Four payments

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina then clarifies the *chayav* side of the ruling: when the person entered with permission and was injured, the carpenter is *chayav* in *arba'ah devarim* — the four payments associated with bodily injury — but *patur migalus*, exempt from *galus*. The question is why. There is clearly liability for *nezek* and the associated payments, but the *galus* exemption requires explanation.

### Not like yaar

The initial explanation offered is *lefi she'ein domeh leyaar* — because this case is not analogous to the *yaar*, the forest scenario that the *Torah* uses as the paradigm for *galus*. By the forest, *zeh lereshus nichnas vezeh lereshus nichnas* — both parties entered a shared, public space with equal right of access and without awareness of one another. Here, the injured party entered the carpenter's private domain, even if with permission. The argument is formalistic: since the *Torah*'s case is one of shared public access, a case involving entry into private property — even with consent — falls outside that paradigm and does not trigger *galus*.

### Rava's view

Rava (some have the reading *Rabbah*) challenges this formalistic approach with a *kal vachomer*. Rather than focusing on whose domain it is, Rava reframes the discussion in terms of *da'as* — awareness and negligence. In the *yaar* case, *zeh l'da'aso nichnas vezeh l'da'aso nichnas* — each party entered independently, with no knowledge of the other, and yet the one who caused the death is *chayav galus*. Since even low awareness generates *galus* liability in the forest case, how much more so here, where *zeh shebeyedias chavero nichnas lo kol shekein* — the victim entered with the carpenter's knowledge and perhaps his explicit consent? The carpenter here has more awareness than the forest-chopper, not less. By this logic, one might conclude that *galus* should apply here with even greater force.

### Too much negligence

Rava, however, wishes to preserve the ruling of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina that the carpenter is *patur migalus*, and so he reinterprets it. *Patur migalus d'lo sagi lei begalus* — he is exempt from *galus* not because there is too little negligence to warrant it, but because there is too much. *Mishum d'hava lei shogeg karov l'meizid* — his negligence is of the type called *shogeg karov l'meizid*, inadvertence so close to intentionality that it falls outside the redemptive scope of *galus*. This is the counterintuitive dynamic of *galus*: ordinarily, greater culpability brings greater consequence, but *galus* is understood as providing atonement specifically for those in a middle zone of *shogeg*. One who is too close to *meizid* is not granted the atonement that *galus* affords — not because he is being let off lightly, but because *galus* is simply insufficient for his level of guilt.

### Extra lash objection

Rabbah raises a challenge from the case of a court official who administered lashes: if the official gave one extra lash beyond the prescribed number and the condemned person died as a result, the official goes into *galus*. Yet here, Rabbah argues, the negligence is very high — *d'ibayei asukei deitei d'mayis b'chadei retzuah* — the official should have been acutely aware that an extra lash could be fatal. Moreover, he had no right whatsoever to administer that extra blow. This seems to place him squarely in the category of *shogeg karov l'meizid*, yet the ruling is that he does go into *galus*.

### Judge miscounted

Rav Shimi of Nehardea resolves this by explaining that the counter — the judge who tracked the lashes — made an error: *ta'ei b'minyana*, he miscounted and called out a number he had already called, leading the official to deliver the extra lash thinking it was still within the count. Rabbah reportedly struck Rav Shimi with his sandal in exasperation at this answer — noting that the official is not the one doing the counting; the *baraisa* specifies that one judge reads the verse, a second counts, and a third calls out to strike. The official is only executing the command, not tracking the number himself. Therefore the error was entirely on the judge's side, and the official had no independent reason to know he was delivering an extra blow. Since the official was acting in good faith on the information he received, he is not in the category of *shogeg karov l'meizid*, and *galus* applies normally.

### Stone-thrower

A further challenge is raised from the case of one who throws a stone into *reshus harabim* and kills someone: *hazorek even l'reshus harabim v'harag harei zeh goleh*, he goes into *galus*. Yet this too seems to involve high negligence — *d'ibayei asukei adei d'reshus harabim shchichi inish* — one is obligated to be aware that people are present in the public domain, and throwing a stone there is an action whose dangerous potential is manifest. If such high negligence still yields *galus*, why does the carpenter's case with permission not also yield *galus*?

### Wall demolition

Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak responds by situating the stone-thrower case in a particular set of circumstances: he is *soseis kosalo* — knocking down his wall — and doing so at night, when the streets are empty and the risk of encountering a passerby is minimal. The *gemara* presses: even at night one should be paying attention. The answer is that he is knocking it down into a *besusa d'vei hiti beyoma d'ashpa* — into a dump site, a place people do not ordinarily frequent.

### Dump analysis

Rav Pappa refines the analysis: *lo tzericha ela be'ashpa ha'asuyah lehifanot balaila ve'ein asuyah lehifanot beyom* — the dump is used as a latrine at night but not during the day, except that occasionally someone might use it even during the day out of urgent necessity. The result is a precise calibration: *mezid lo havei* — it is not *meizid*, because the dump is not ordinarily used during the day. *Ones nami lo havei* — but it is not *ones* either, because the occasional urgent visitor does occur. This middle ground of modest but not negligible risk is precisely the zone of *shogeg* in which *galus* applies. The point is that this profile of risk is less foreseeable than the profile in the carpenter case with permission, where — as *Tosafos* emphasizes — the permission was recently granted, meaning the person might walk in at any moment.

### Alternative reading

Rav Pappa, citing Rava, then offers an entirely different reading of the *baraita*. Rather than placing the *baraita*'s entire discussion in the realm of *nezek*, he now teaches the ruling on the *first* part of the *baraita* — the case where the person entered without permission. On this reading, the *baraita* is not primarily about *nezek* at all; it is about *galus*. The case of entering without permission yields exemption from *galus*; entering with permission yields liability for *galus*. This is the *baraita*'s subject throughout.

This reading comes with its own difficulty, however: without permission, the carpenter is *patur* in *galus*, but what is the rule for *nezek*? Rabbi Yose bar Abba Chanina clarifies: *chayav b'arba'ah devarim* — he is still *chayav* in the four payments for bodily harm. This requires explanation, since without permission one might have thought there is no liability at all. Both Rashi and *Tosafos* explain that the carpenter was aware the person had entered even without permission, and that awareness establishes sufficient grounds for *nezek* liability even absent formal *reshus*.

### Galus focus

The *gemara* closes by drawing out the two readings' implications for one another. The one who teaches the *baraita*'s ruling on the second clause — that with *reshus* one is *patur migalus* because the negligence is too great — will certainly also teach that without *reshus* one is *patur migalus* by simple logic. But the one who teaches the *baraita* as addressing the first clause — that without *reshus* one is *patur migalus* — holds that with *reshus* one is in fact *chayav galus*: *keivan d'v'reshus chayav galus*.

The clean formulation that emerges from the second reading: without *reshus*, *patur* in *galus*; with *reshus*, *chayav* in *galus*. And in both cases, for *nezek* proper, the rule tracks *adam muad l'olam* — without *reshus* one is *patur* in *nezek*, with *reshus* one is *chayav* in *nezek*. The first reading's *chiddush* — that even with permission the carpenter is *patur migalus* because of excessive negligence — is rejected by the second reading, which restores the intuitive symmetry: *reshus* is the operative factor for *galus* liability just as it is for *nezek*.
