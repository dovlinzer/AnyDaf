# Berakhot 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Framework

### Perek Structure

The ninth and final *perek* of *Masechet Berakhot* follows the pattern of its predecessor in presenting all of its *mishnayot* at the outset as a single extended *mishnah*. Whereas the earlier chapters addressed *keri'at shema*, *tefillah*, and the laws of *birkat ha-mazon*, this concluding chapter turns entirely to *birchas ha-shevach* — blessings of praise and acknowledgment that one recites in response to particular experiences, places, and phenomena.

### Miracles - Bnei Yisrael

The *mishnah* opens: *ha-ro'eh makom shena'asu bo nissim l'Yisrael* — one who sees a place where miracles were performed for the Jewish people recites *baruch she'asa nissim la-avoteinu ba-makom hazeh*, blessed is God who performed miracles for our forefathers in this place.

### Akum Uprooting

The *mishnah* continues: *makom shena'akar mimenu akum* — a place from which idolatry has been completely uprooted, one recites *baruch she'akar akum me'artzeinu*, blessed is God who uprooted idolatry from our land. It should be noted that the *mishnah*'s own text uses the term *avodah zarah*, referring to the physical idols and images themselves, not the practitioners. The background here is significant: the Torah in *Devarim* commands the eradication of all idolatrous shrines upon entering the land, and this *berakhah* celebrates the actual fulfillment of that mandate. This was composed in the era of pagan Rome, when images and icons were ubiquitous — making a genuinely idol-free locale all the more noteworthy.

## Natural Phenomena

### Thunder & Lightning

The *mishnah* then enumerates blessings for a range of frightening natural events: *al hizzikim ve'al ha-zeva'ot ve'al ha-re'amim ve'al ha-ruchot ve'al ha-berakim* — for *hizzikim* and *zeva'ot* (various startling or powerful atmospheric phenomena, to be clarified in the *gemara*), for thunder, for strong winds, and for lightning. For all of these one recites *baruch she-kocho u-gevurato malei olam*, blessed is God whose power and might fills the world. The *halakhah* as we know it does distinguish between the blessing for lightning and that for thunder, though this *mishnah* subsumes them together.

### Shem u-Malchut Debate

This is an appropriate moment to observe something striking about the *mishnah*'s formulation: the text of each *berakhah* is given only as a closing phrase, without the standard opening of *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam*. This omission may simply reflect the unwillingness to write God's name unnecessarily, with the full formula assumed.

However, the question cuts deeper. The first *Tosafot* on the *daf* states explicitly — *perush min ha-shamaya* — that all of these *berakhot* require *hazkarat shem u-malchut*, the full *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam*. This is indeed how we *pasken*. Yet a number of *Rishonim* hold otherwise, arguing that these *berakhot* were never elevated to the status of formal *berakhot* and were always more in the nature of *tefillot* — expressions of praise — than structured *berakhot*. On this view they never received *shem u-malchut* at all.

The tension arises directly from the *mishnah*'s silence on the matter. Since these *berakhot* are not *samuch l'chaverta* — they are not appended to a preceding blessing in a chain — there would be no independent reason to omit *shem u-malchut* if they were genuine *berakhot*. And yet the *mishnah* never mentions it. The accepted ruling is that they are indeed full *berakhot* recited with *shem u-malchut*, but the *Rishonim* who disagree are drawing on exactly this textual silence.

## Yammim & Neharot

### Water & Deserts

For *al ha-yammim ve'al ha-neharot* — exceptional oceans and rivers, presumably ones of remarkable power or scale — and for *al ha-midbarot*, wildernesses and deserts, one recites *baruch oseh ma'aseh bereishit*, blessed is God who performs the work of creation. A practical question arises here as it does throughout: if one encounters these phenomena regularly, does the obligation still apply? The accepted rule is that the *berakhah* applies if thirty days have elapsed since the last sighting.

### Yam HaGadol

*Rabbi Yehuda* rules that one who sees *ha-yam ha-gadol* — the Mediterranean — recites the more specific *baruch she'asa et ha-yam ha-gadol*. This is a dedicated blessing for the Mediterranean in particular. Of course, from our vantage point the Atlantic and Pacific are vastly larger, yet one recites *yam ha-gadol* only for the Mediterranean. The condition *Rabbi Yehuda* attaches is *bizman she-ro'eihu l'frakim* — only when one sees it at intervals, not constantly. If one lives along its shore and sees it daily, the element of wonder and special occasion has dissipated, and the *berakhah* no longer applies.

## Good & Bad News

### Rain Blessing

*Al ha-geshamim ve'al ha-besorot tovot* — for rainfall and for good reports — one recites *baruch ha-tov ve-ha-mativ*, blessed is God who is good and does good. Rain is grouped with good news because from the agricultural perspective life depends on it; it is unambiguously welcome tidings.

### Bad Reports

*Al besorot ra'ot*, or in some versions *shemu'ot ra'ot*, on bad reports, one recites *baruch dayan ha-emet*, blessed is the true Judge. How grave must the news be to require *dayan ha-emet*? This is a question that the *gemara* addresses separately.

### Shehecheyanu

*Bana bayit chadash ve-kana kelim chadashim* — one who builds a new house or acquires new objects recites *shehecheyanu*, the blessing of gratitude for reaching a new and significant moment. The question naturally arises for us: *bayit chadash* is straightforward, but what about purchasing a new suit, a new garment, a new appliance? The answer turns on the degree of joy and significance the acquisition represents. In the era of the *mishnah* and *gemara*, acquiring new clothing or objects was genuinely momentous. For us, it depends on context — when it constitutes a meaningful *simhah*, the *berakhah* applies.

### Herzog Anecdote

A well-known story is told about *Rav Herzog*: at the moment Israel declared independence, he rose and recited *shehecheyanu*. Someone standing beside him challenged him — where is the *halakhic* basis for reciting *shehecheyanu* over such an event? *Rav Herzog* replied simply: he was wearing a new tie. The implicit point was that if a necktie justifies *shehecheyanu*, surely the establishment of Jewish sovereignty in the land of Israel does as well. The story carries a genuine edge, though: it underscores how the formal *halakhic* structure of *shehecheyanu* focuses on the acquisition of *kelim* — physical objects and clothing — while events of far greater existential and spiritual magnitude, which generate incomparably deeper *simhah*, find no explicit home in the same framework.

## Me'ein Principle

### Bad & Good

The *mishnah* states: *mevarech al ha-ra me'ein al ha-tova ve'al ha-tova me'ein al ha-ra* — one blesses over bad [news] in a manner analogous to the blessing over good, and over good in a manner analogous to the blessing over bad. This is admittedly cryptic and the *gemara* will unpack what the analogous relationship means in concrete terms.

### Tefilat Shav

*Tzo'ek l'shavar harei zo tefilat shav* — if one cries out to God over something that has already occurred, that is a *tefilat shav*, a worthless prayer. God does not alter the past, and praying for a different outcome for an event that has already taken place is theologically incoherent.

### Pregnant Wife Example

The *mishnah* gives the first illustration: *hayeta ishto me'uberet* — if one's wife is already pregnant and he prays, *yehi ratzon she-teled ishti zachar*, may it be God's will that my wife gives birth to a son — *harei zo tefilat shav*. The determination of the child's sex has already occurred; the prayer comes too late.

### Misfortune Example

The second example: *haya ba-derech ve-shama kol tzevahah ba-ir* — one is traveling and hears a commotion in the city, the sound of emergency, and says, *yehi ratzon shelo tehei zo b'tokh beiti*, may it be God's will that this calamity not be in my house — *harei zo tefilat shav*. Whatever has happened has already happened. One may hope in general, but to direct a prayer toward changing an already-determined outcome is futile.

## City Prayers

### Entry & Exit Prayers

*Ha-nichnas l'krach mitpallel shtayim* — one who enters a city makes two prayers, *achat b'nichisato ve-achat b'yitziato*, one upon entry and one upon departure. This may seem surprising given the existence of *tefilat ha-derekh*, which addresses the dangers of travel; but entering an unfamiliar city presents its own dangers — unscrupulous people, unknown conditions, the vulnerability of a stranger in an unfamiliar environment. Hence separate prayers are warranted for both arrival and departure.

### Ben Azai View

*Ben Azai* takes a more expansive position: *arba* — four prayers in total, *shtayim b'nichisato u-shtayim b'yitziato*, two at each juncture. His reasoning is that each time one passes through — whether entering or leaving — one should do two things: *noten hoda'ah al she-avar*, give thanks to God for having been protected until this point, and *tzo'ek al he-atid*, call out to God for protection going forward.

### Tzoak Term

It is worth pausing on the *mishnah*'s choice of vocabulary here. The word used is *tzo'ek* — a word that connotes urgent, anguished crying out — rather than the more neutral *mitpallel*. This is somewhat striking. The *mishnah* earlier used *tzo'ek* in the context of a fire engine rushing past, where raw fear and urgency make the term viscerally appropriate. It also appeared in the example of praying for a son while one's wife is pregnant, a situation where the same intensity might seem misplaced. By the time we reach the city prayers, the use of *tzo'ek* may be deliberate — underscoring the real danger that entering an unfamiliar city posed — but it is notable that the term has now become something of a general verb for any earnest supplication, whatever the level of urgency.

### Bad Equals Good

The *mishnah* states: *chayav adam l'varekh al ha-ra k'shem she-mevarekh al ha-tova* — a person is obligated to bless God for bad things just as he blesses God for good things. The theological underpinning is the belief that everything comes from God and ultimately serves a positive purpose. The *mishnah* grounds this in the verse *she-ne'emar ve-ahavta et Hashem Elokekha b'chol levavecha*, and you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart.

### Two Inclinations

The *drasha* on *b'chol levavecha* reads: *bi-shnei yetzarekha*, with both of your inclinations — *yetzer tov* and *yetzer ha-ra*. Even the *yetzer ha-ra*, one's baser drives and desires, must be channeled toward the service of God. The famous *midrash* makes the point clearly: *imlo yetzer ha-ra lo bana adam bayit ve-lo nasa isha* — were it not for those urges, a person would never build a home or marry and raise a family. The obligation is to find constructive uses for every part of one's inner life.

### Soul & Might

*U'v'chol nafshecha*, with all your soul — *afilu hu notel et nafshecha*, even if He takes your life. This speaks to the obligation of martyrdom, the readiness to give up one's life rather than violate the fundamental obligations. *U'v'chol me'odekha*, with all your *me'od* — with all your wealth, *b'chol mamonekha*.

### Middah Drasha

Then *davar acher* — the *drasha* more directly relevant to the present context. *Me'od* is read not as "muchness" or wealth but as *middah*, measure: *b'chol middah u-middah she-hu moded lekha harei modeh lo* — for every measure that God metes out to you, you thank Him. The pun moves from *moded* to *modeh*: whatever God apportions — whether it appears favorable or not — is to be accepted with gratitude, in the recognition that *middah k'neged middah* in its deepest sense means that every divine dispensation is purposeful. One receives what one receives and responds with blessing.

## Temple Mount Conduct

### Eastern Gate

After all this material on *berakhot*, *tefillot*, and *birchas ha-shevach*, the *mishnah* shifts abruptly to rules of conduct at the *beit ha-mikdash* — a transition that may initially seem jarring but proves, as we shall see, to be thematically coherent with the *masekhet* as a whole. *Lo yakel adam et rosho k'neged sha'ar ha-mizrach* — one should not behave frivolously when facing the eastern gate of *har ha-bayit*, because *she-hu mekhuvvan k'neged beit kodshei ha-kodashim* — it is in direct alignment with the Holy of Holies. The entrance gates were oriented so that from the eastern gate one was facing straight through to the innermost sanctum.

### Entry Restrictions

*V'lo yikanes l'har ha-bayit be-maklo* — one may not enter the Temple Mount with one's staff, *be-min'alo* — with one's shoes, *be-fundato* — with a money belt, *uva-avak she'al raglav* — with dust on one's feet. Entry requires a state of deliberate, careful preparation; one does not enter this space casually.

### Shortcut Prohibition

*V'lo ya'asenu kapandarya* — one certainly may not use the Temple Mount as a shortcut to save time getting from one place to another. *Ve-rakika* — and spitting is similarly prohibited. These extensions follow by *kal va-homer* from the other restrictions already enumerated.

## Temple Blessing Formula

### Original Formula

*Kol chotmei berakhot she-ba-mikdash* — all the concluding formulas of blessings in the *mikdash* were sealed with *min ha-olam ve-ad ha-olam*: *baruch atah Hashem Elokei Yisrael min ha-olam ve-ad ha-olam*, blessed forever and ever. The *berakhah* then concluded with *baruch chonen ha-da'at*. This was the standard form.

### Sectarian Denial

The trouble began when the *tzedokim* — or, as the manuscripts may read, *minim* — began to undermine this formula. Their claim was *ein olam ela echad* — there is only one world, denying *olam ha-ba* entirely. It is worth noting that this issue is taken with great seriousness in *Sanhedrin*, where one who denies that *techiyat ha-meitim* is rooted in the Torah forfeits his portion in *olam ha-ba*. Whether the sectarians here are *tzedokim* in the historical sense — who, according to the Christian scriptures, explicitly denied the resurrection — or whether *tzedokim* is a scribal censorship of *minim* is a genuine textual question. What is clear is that some sect was actively propagating the view that there is no world beyond this one, and that this threatened to render the concluding formula of *berakhot* theologically ambiguous.

### Sectarian Response

The institutional response was *hitkinu she-yiyu omrim min ha-olam ve-ad ha-olam* — the formula was changed to *min ha-olam ve-ad ha-olam*, from this world to the next, making the affirmation of *olam ha-ba* explicit and unambiguous. The single seal *ad ha-olam* could be read as encompassing only this one world; the new formulation *min ha-olam ve-ad ha-olam* could not be misread in that way.

## Name in Greetings

### Greeting Enactment

The *mishnah* records a further *takkanah*: *hitkinu she-yehei adam sho'el bi-shalom chaveiro be-shem* — that people should greet one another using God's name. This stands alongside the change to the *berakhah* formula as one of the institutional responses to the challenges of the period.

### Biblical Proofs

Two scriptural sources are adduced. The first is the scene at the harvest in Rus: *ve-hinei Boaz ba mi-beit lechem va-yomer la-kotzrim Hashem imakhem va-yomru lo yevarkhekha Hashem* — Boaz arrived from Beit Lechem and said to the harvesters, "God be with you," and they replied, "God bless you." Boaz used God's name as a greeting, and it is treated as entirely appropriate. The second proof is from the *malakh*'s salutation: *Hashem imkha gibor ha-hayil*, God is with you, O mighty man of valor. Both passages demonstrate that invoking God's name in a greeting is not only permissible but exemplary.

### Elder Respect

The *mishnah* also cites: *al tivuz ki ziknah imekha* — do not despise [the teaching] because your mother has grown old. The reading applied here is that *ziknei amecha*, the elders of your people, have established this practice; follow their example just as one would follow the example set by Boaz.

### Eis La-Asot

The *mishnah* then invokes: *et la-asot la-Hashem heferu toratekha* — a time to act for God, they have violated Your Torah. *Rav Natan* reads this as: *heferu toratekha*, [we are prepared to] violate the Torah *mishum et la-asot la-Hashem*, for the sake of serving God. *Rashi* underscores this point carefully — and indeed more than once in his commentary — noting that what might appear to be a violation of the rule against unnecessary invocation of God's name is in fact justified here precisely because it serves a greater sacred purpose. The concern being addressed is whether using God's name in a casual daily greeting is inappropriate, and the answer the *mishnah* gives is that when the purpose is to bring holiness into daily life, the *takkanah* is warranted even at the apparent cost of what might look like ordinary usage of the divine name.

### Framing & Kedusha

This is a striking and deliberate way to conclude *Masechet Berakhot*. The discussion of God's name in greetings, of conduct on *har ha-bayit*, and of the *berakhah* formula in the *mikdash* all converge around a single question: how does one bring *kedushah* into daily life in a world without the *mikdash*? It is also worth noting how this ending resonates with the *masekhet*'s opening. *Masechet Berakhot* famously begins by fixing the time for *keri'at shema* according to when the *kohanim* descend for their *tevilah* — a reference to the *mikdash* at the very outset. The *masekhet* thus begins and ends with the *mikdash* as its frame of reference. The entire tractate can be read as an extended meditation on how, in the absence of the *mikdash*, one constructs a life of divine service through *berakhot*, *tefillah*, and *keri'at shema* — and perhaps even through a simple greeting to a friend, conducted in God's name.

## Gemara Miracles

### Source Proof

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the first *mishnah* by asking: *mina hani mili* — from where do we know that one makes a *berakhah* at a place where miracles occurred? *Rabbi Yochanan* cites the verse: *va-yomer Yisrael baruch Hashem asher hitzil* — and Israel said, blessed is God who delivered. The model is *Yisrael* himself giving praise; what was sufficient for *Yisrael* establishes the precedent for all generations.

### Public vs Private

This leads immediately to a question: the *mishnah* speaks specifically of *nissim l'Yisrael*, miracles performed for the Jewish people collectively. Does this mean that only *anissa d'rabim* — a public miracle affecting the masses — obligates a *berakhah*, while *anissa d'yachid* — a miracle that happened to an individual — does not? The *gemara* raises this as a genuine issue.

### Lion Miracle

The first story: there was a certain man, *d'hava ka'azil b'Avar Yemina* — who was traveling through *Avar Yemina* — when a lion attacked him and a miracle occurred and he was saved. He came before *Rava*, who told him: *kol eimas d'matyas le-hasam barich* — every time you pass that place, make a *berakhah*: *baruch she'asah li nes ba-makom hazeh*, blessed is God who performed a miracle for me in this place. The *berakhah* is personalized — *li*, for me — and is tied to that specific location.

### Water & Wall

The second story involves *Mar* son of *Ravina*. On one occasion, *hava ka'azil b'Piska d'Aravot*, he was traveling through the valley of *Aravot* and became desperately thirsty. A miracle occurred and a spring of water appeared, from which he drank. On a separate occasion, *zimna chada hava ka'azil b'ristka d'Mechuza*, he was going through the marketplace of *Mechuza* when a wild camel charged at him. A wall collapsed, he entered the opening, and was saved. When he subsequently returned to *Aravot*, he recited *baruch she'asah li nes b'Aravot u'v'gamla* — blessed is God who performed a miracle for me in *Aravot* and with the camel. When he reached the marketplace of *Mechuza*, he recited *baruch she'asah li nes b'gamla u'v'Aravot* — blessed is God who performed a miracle for me with the camel and in *Aravot* — reversing the order.

### Personalization

Several things are notable here. First, *Mar* son of *Ravina* felt at liberty to personalize the formula well beyond the simple *nes ba-makom hazeh* — he named both miracles explicitly and combined them in a single *berakhah*. Second, when he was at each location, he placed that location's miracle first and mentioned the other second — a natural impulse to foreground the miracle relevant to where he was standing. Third, and most importantly for the legal question: these stories demonstrate that even *anissa d'yachid* — a miracle to a single individual — generates an obligation to recite a *berakhah*.

The *gemara* concludes: *anissa d'rabim kuli alma mechayvei l'vrukhei* — for a public miracle affecting *Klal Yisrael*, everyone is obligated to recite the *berakhah*, even if it occurred long ago and one was not personally present. *Anissa d'yachid ihu chayav l'vrukhei* — for a private miracle, the individual who experienced it is obligated, while others are not. This is the distinction: the *mishnah*'s requirement of *nissim l'Yisrael* establishes a category of national miracles that obligate all of Israel in perpetuity, but personal miracles generate a parallel though more limited obligation that falls exclusively on the beneficiary.
