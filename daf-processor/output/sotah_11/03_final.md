# Sotah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Absalom's Hair

### Intro: Absalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַבְשָׁלוֹם בִּשְׂעָרוֹ מָרַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּכְאַבְשָׁלוֹם לֹא הָיָה אִישׁ יָפֶה וְגוֹ׳. וּבְגַלְּחוֹ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְהָיָה מִקֵּץ יָמִים לַיָּמִים אֲשֶׁר יְגַלֵּחַ כִּי כָבֵד עָלָיו וְגִלְּחוֹ וְשָׁקַל אֶת שְׂעַר רֹאשׁוֹ מָאתַיִם שְׁקָלִים בְּאֶבֶן הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: Absalom rebelled due to his hair, as it is stated: "Now in all Israel there was none to be so much praised as Absalom for his beauty; from the sole of his foot even to the crown of his head there was no blemish in him. And when he shaved his head, as it was at every year's end that he shaved it; because the hair was heavy on him, therefore he shaved it, and he weighed the hair of his head at two hundred shekels, by the king's stone" (II Samuel 14:25–26).

Today's daf is 11. We pick up at the bottom of 10b, in the middle of a story about Absalom (*Avshalom*) within the larger context of *midah keneged midah* that began with the *sotah* and her punishments. Now we turn to Absalom: his hair was the most beautiful, almost divine thing about him, and ultimately it proved to be his downfall.

The *Gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *Tanu Rabbanan* — Absalom rebelled through his hair, as it says, "And like Absalom there was no man as beautiful... and when he shaved his head — for it was heavy upon him and he shaved it — he weighed the hair of his head at two hundred *shekalim* by the king's weight." *Rashi* reminds us, in case we have forgotten what we learned in *Masechet Nazir*, that the *Gemara*'s understanding is that Absalom was a *nazir olam* and was therefore permitted to shave his hair once every twelve months. The hair provided him a pretext — gathering people around him and leading an insurrection — and presumably filled him with the sense that he was deserving of the kingship, though the *Gemara* does not say that explicitly.

### Hair & Pride

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אֶבֶן שֶׁאַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא וְאַנְשֵׁי צִיפּוֹרִי שׁוֹקְלִים בָּהּ. לְפִיכָךְ נִתְלָה בִּשְׂעָרוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא אַבְשָׁלוֹם לִפְנֵי עַבְדֵי דָוִד וְאַבְשָׁלוֹם רֹכֵב עַל הַפֶּרֶד וַיָּבֹא הַפֶּרֶד תַּחַת שׂוֹבֶךְ הָאֵלָה הַגְּדוֹלָה וַיֶּחֱזַק רֹאשׁוֹ בָאֵלָה וַיֻּתַּן בֵּין הַשָּׁמַיִם וּבֵין הָאָרֶץ וְהַפֶּרֶד אֲשֶׁר תַּחְתָּיו עָבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught:** A stone with which the people of Tiberias and the people of Tzippori weigh items. **Therefore, he was hung by his hair, as it is stated** in the verse describing the battle between the forces of David and Absalom: **"And Absalom chanced to meet the servants of David. And Absalom was riding upon his mule, and the mule went under the thick boughs of a great terebinth, and his head caught hold of the terebinth, and he was taken up between the heaven and the earth; and the mule that was under him went on"** (II Samuel 18:9).

The *Gemara* then offers a *derasha* on the phrase "the king's weight," identifying it as a standard measure used by the people of Tiberias and Tzipori. From this flows the moral consequence: *lefichach nitlah besearo* — therefore he was caught by his hair. He was, as it were, hoisted by his own petard. As it says, "And Absalom rode before the servants of David, and Absalom rode upon the mule, and the mule went under the thick branches of the great terebinth, and his head caught in the terebinth, and he was suspended between heaven and earth, and the mule that was under him passed on."

### Tree & Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁקַל סַפְסִירָא בְּעָא לְמִיפְסְקֵיהּ. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִבְקַע שְׁאוֹל מִתַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** After he was spotted by the opposing troops, Absalom **took a sword [*safseira*] and wanted to cut his hair** to save himself. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: At that moment,** the gates of **the netherworld opened beneath him** and he was afraid to fall into it, so he did not cut his hair, and he was killed by the opposing troops.

The *Gemara* then addresses a natural question: why did he not simply cut himself free? It says he took a sword and wanted to cut his hair — but at that very moment, *tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* teaches, the depths of *Sheol* opened up beneath him. The insight offered in discussion resolves the difficulty elegantly: Absalom could not cut his hair because he now faced a second peril — if he cut himself free, he would fall into the abyss that had opened below him. That explains his paralysis.

### David's Lament

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּרְגַּז הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיַּעַל עַל עֲלִיַּת הַשַּׁעַר וַיֵּבְךְּ וְכֹה אָמַר בְּלֶכְתּוֹ בְּנִי אַבְשָׁלוֹם בְּנִי בְּנִי אַבְשָׁלוֹם מִי יִתֵּן מוּתִי אֲנִי תַחְתֶּיךָ אַבְשָׁלוֹם בְּנִי בְּנִי. וְהַמֶּלֶךְ לָאַט אֶת פָּנָיו וַיִּזְעַק הַמֶּלֶךְ קוֹל גָּדוֹל בְּנִי אַבְשָׁלוֹם אַבְשָׁלוֹם בְּנִי בְּנִי״. הָנֵי תְּמָנְיָא ״בְּנִי״ לְמָה? שִׁבְעָה דְּאַסְּקֵיהּ מִשִּׁבְעָה מְדוֹרֵי גֵיהִנָּם, וְאִידַּךְ, אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דְּקָרֵיב רֵישֵׁיהּ לְגַבֵּי גוּפֵיהּ, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דְּאַיְיתֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is written with regard to David's reaction after he learns of the death of Absalom: **"And the king was much moved, and went up to the chamber over the gate, and wept; and as he went about he said: O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! Would I had died in your place, O Absalom, my son, my son"** (II Samuel 19:1), and a few verses later it adds: **"And the king covered his face, and the king cried with a loud voice: O my son Absalom, O Absalom, my son, my son"** (II Samuel 19:5). The Gemara asks: **Why** are there **these eight** mentions of **"my son"** by David, i.e., to what do they correspond? The Gemara answers: **Seven** times he said "my son," by **which he raised him up from the seven chambers of Gehenna. And** as for **the other,** eighth, time, **some say that** David **brought** the **head** of Absalom **close to** Absalom's **body, and some say that** with this eighth mention David **brought** Absalom **to the World-to-Come.**

Following Joab's killing of Absalom, the report of his death was brought to King David, who went up to the gate and wept, crying out: *bni Avshalom, bni bni Avshalom, mi yiten muti ani tachtecha Avshalom bni bni* — "My son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom, would that I had died in your place, Absalom my son, my son." And then again: *Avshalom bni bni*. It is difficult to overstate the pathos of these verses — arguably the saddest passage in all of *Tanakh*. No matter what Absalom had done, David's anguish is complete and unconditional. That is precisely what makes it so unbearably poignant.

The *Gemara* asks: *hanei tmanya bni lama* — why are there eight utterances of "my son"? Seven of them, it explains, correspond to the seven chambers of *Gehinnom*: each one of David's cries raised Absalom up from one level of the depths, each one lifting him a little closer to the surface. The eighth utterance — according to one opinion — served to reconnect Absalom's severed head to his body (though this detail does not appear explicitly in the biblical text); according to another opinion, David's prayer actually brought Absalom to *Olam Haba*.

### Gehinnom & Prayer

This last point raises an important question taken up by *Tosafot*: the principle stated in *Chelek* is *bra mezakei aba* — a son's merits can benefit his deceased father — but not *aba mezakei bra*, a father cannot bring benefit to his son in this way. How, then, did David's prayer avail Absalom?

*Tosafot* offers two responses. First, Absalom had not worshipped idols, and therefore the case may be different from those situations in which a sinner is wholly beyond redemption. Second, and more directly: the principle that *aba lo mezakei bra* means only that out of respect for the father, we do not ordinarily count the son's deeds as accruing to the father's merit — but that is a matter of formal accounting. Prayer, however, is different: *aval tefila mo'eles* — prayer is effective. And David prayed for Absalom.

This raises a deeper conceptual issue. *Kaddish* operates on a somewhat different logic: the son's recitation of *kaddish* sanctifies God's name in the world, and since the deceased father brought into the world a child who now performs this act, the merit redounds indirectly to him — *bra mezakei aba* in the classic sense. But praying directly for the welfare of another's soul is a distinct concept from performing good deeds whose merit flows back through a causal chain. One is asking God to act; the other is creating a new meritorious act attributable to the deceased through the child.

It is interesting, in passing, to note the parallels with the legends that grew up around the death of Shabtai Tzvi — the myths about prayer campaigns to elevate him from *Gehinnom* and bring him to *Olam Haba*. He remained so powerful a figure in the popular imagination that people could not accept that his sin was the final word. Something similar seems to be at work in the *Gemara*'s wrestling with whether David's prayer could ultimately bring Absalom to *Olam Haba*. On the question of gradations: once a soul has been removed from punishment, it is one further step to *Olam Haba* — not to the highest level, but to some entry-level portion. The *Gemara* in *Rosh Hashanah* does speak of the *reshaim* dwelling in a kind of temporary state for twelve months before their final judgment, something not entirely unlike the concept of purgatory in other traditions.

### Monument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאַבְשָׁלוֹם לָקַח וַיַּצֶּב לוֹ בְחַיָּיו״. מַאי ״לָקַח״? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שֶׁלָּקַח מִקָּח רַע לְעַצְמוֹ. ״אֶת מַצֶּבֶת אֲשֶׁר בְּעֵמֶק הַמֶּלֶךְ וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: בְּעֵצָה עֲמוּקָּה שֶׁל מַלְכּוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם,
>
> **Translation:** It is written there: **"Now Absalom in his lifetime had taken and reared up for himself** the pillar, which is in the king's valley; for he said: I have no son to keep my name in remembrance" (II Samuel 18:18). The Gemara asks: **What did** Absalom **take? Reish Lakish says: He engaged in a bad transaction for himself** by accepting bad advice for which he was punished. The verse continues: **"The pillar, which is in the king's valley [*be'emek hammelekh*]." Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says:** This alludes to the pillar that is **in the deep [*amukka*] counsel of the King [*melekh*] of the universe,** as God had already decreed in the aftermath of the incident with Bathsheba that this would occur.

The *Gemara* continues: "And Absalom had taken and set up for himself a monument in his lifetime." The *Tanakh* reports that after his death it was recalled that Absalom had already erected this monument, calling it *Yad Avshalom*, because he said, "I have no son to keep my name in remembrance."

The *Gemara* asks: *mai lakach* — what does "he had taken" mean? *Reish Lakish* says: just as *Vayikach Korach* means that Korach made a bad transaction, so too here — *shelakach mekach ra le'atzmo* — he had made a bad investment for himself. Whether this refers to his foreseeing his own death by erecting a monument, or to the entire disastrous enterprise of the rebellion, is left somewhat open.

*Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa* adds: *be'etzah amukah shel malko shel olam* — this was all part of the deep counsel of the King of the universe. *Rashi* explains this with reference to the punishment decreed upon David after the sin of Bathsheba — God had told him that evil would arise from within his own house — *hineni mekim alecha ra'ah mi'beitecha*. Without diminishing Absalom's own *bechirat chofshit*, the episode unfolded in accordance with a divine plan. The *Gemara* draws a parallel to the story of Joseph: *Vayishla'chehu me'emek Chevron* — he was sent from the valley of Hebron. *Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa* reads *emek* here as "deep counsel," the deep strategy of that righteous one buried in Hebron, namely Abraham, and connects this to the divine decree of *yado'a teda ki ger yihyeh zar'acha* — "Know well that your descendants will be strangers." Joseph's being sent to his brothers was all part of the mechanism by which that divine promise would come to be fulfilled. The question of *hashgacha pratit* and *bechirah chofshit* operating simultaneously is, as the *Gemara* itself seems to acknowledge, a formidable one — and the text moves on.

### Children & Wheat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי אָמַר אֵין לִי בֵן״. וְלָא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּוָּלְדוּ לְאַבְשָׁלוֹם שְׁלֹשָׁה בָּנִים וּבַת אַחַת״! אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ בֵּן הָגוּן לַמַּלְכוּת. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר, גְּמִירִי: כׇּל הַשּׂוֹרֵף תְּבוּאָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ בֵּן לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of Absalom. The verse states concerning Absalom: **"For he said: I have no son** to keep my name in remembrance; and he called the pillar after his own name; and it is called Absalom's monument to this day" (II Samuel 18:18). The Gemara asks: **And did** Absalom **not have sons? But isn't it written: "And to Absalom there were born three sons, and one daughter"** (II Samuel 14:27)? **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says:** He meant **that he did not have a son worthy for royalty. Rav Ḥisda said: It is learned** as a tradition: **Anyone who burns the produce of another does not leave a son to inherit** from **him.**

The *Gemara* now addresses the monument's stated rationale: *ki amar ein li ben* — Absalom said he had no son. But this is puzzling, since the *Tanakh* earlier reports that he had three sons and one daughter. *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi* explains: he had no son *hagun lemalkhut* — no son fit to inherit a kingdom. This detail itself implies that even at the time he erected the monument, Absalom was already anticipating his bid for the throne — for why else would fitness for kingship matter?

*Rav Chisda* adds a *gemiri* — a received tradition: whoever burns another's grain (*tevuato shel chavero*) will leave no son to inherit him. This explains why Absalom's sons predeceased him, though *Rav Chisda* acknowledges the tradition is transmitted as a general principle. The underlying logic is a form of *midah keneged midah*: just as one destroys the fruit of another's labor — that which was planted and nurtured — so one's own progeny, the fruit of one's own life, will be cut down.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיהוּ קַלְיַיהּ לִדְיוֹאָב, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל עֲבָדָיו רְאוּ חֶלְקַת יוֹאָב אֶל יָדִי וְלוֹ שָׁם שְׂעֹרִים לְכוּ וְהַצִּיתוּהָ בָּאֵשׁ וַיַּצִּיתוּ עַבְדֵי אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶת הַחֶלְקָה בָּאֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** and he, Absalom, burned the produce of Joab, as it is written: "Therefore he said to his servants: See, Joab's field is near mine, and he has barley there; go and set it on fire. And Absalom's servants set the field on fire" (II Samuel 14:30).

The episode being referenced is Absalom's burning of Joab's wheat field. After Absalom had been permitted to return to Jerusalem but the king still refused to receive him, Absalom needed to compel Joab to intercede on his behalf. Since Joab was not responding to his messages, Absalom instructed his servants: "Go and set fire to Joab's field." And so they burned the field. That act of destruction ultimately led to his restoration in the eyes of the king — and set in motion the chain of events that would culminate in the rebellion.

## Miriam & Measure

### Miriam Hovers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן הַטּוֹבָה מִרְיָם וְכוּ׳. מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם — חֲדָא שַׁעְתָּא, הָכָא — שִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא: וּלְעִנְיַן הַטּוֹבָה אֵינוֹ כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the same** is so **with regard to the** reward for **good** deeds. **Miriam** waited for the baby Moses for one hour at the shore of the Nile; therefore, the Jewish people delayed their travels in the desert for seven days to wait for her when she was smitten with leprosy. The Gemara asks: **Are** these matters **comparable? There,** Miriam waited **one hour,** while **here,** the Jewish people waited for her for **seven days. Abaye said: Say** this with a slight change: **And with regard to** the repaying of **good it is not so,** as a person is not rewarded precisely measure for measure, as the reward may be greater than the good deed.

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah*, which teaches that just as there is *midah keneged midah* for punishment, so there is *midah keneged midah* for good — and the example is Miriam, who waited over Moses for one hour, and in the end the entire nation of Israel waited for her seven days when she was stricken. But *Abaye* raises an objection: can this really be called *midah keneged midah*? She waited one hour — *hatam chada sha'ata* — while Israel waited seven days. That is not measure for measure; it is vastly disproportionate. *Abaye* therefore proposes reading the *Mishnah* as saying that for *midah tovah* it is specifically not so — the reward is greater.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: הָא ״וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן הַטּוֹבָה״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן הַטּוֹבָה, דִּבְאוֹתָהּ מִדָּה, וּלְעוֹלָם מִדָּה טוֹבָה מְרוּבָּה מִמִּדַּת פּוּרְעָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But** the *tanna* **taught** in the mishna: **And the same** is so **with regard to the** reward of **good** deeds. **Rather, Rava said: This is what** the mishna **is teaching: And the same** is so **with regard to the** reward of **good** deeds. It is rewarded **with the same measure,** i.e., a person is rewarded in the same manner as the good deed, **but the measure of good is always greater than the measure of punishment.** Therefore, Miriam was rewarded in the same manner as, but in a greater measure than, her deed.

*Rava* objects: the *Mishnah* says *vechen linyan hatovah* — "and likewise for good." You cannot simply change the *girsa*. The concept is the same; what differs is only the quantity. The resolution is that *midah keneged midah* applies to both directions, but *mida tovah* is always *merubah* — greater — than *midat puranut*.

### Math: Hour & Days

*Tosafot* picks up the mathematical precision of this point. *Tosafot* notes that when the *Mishnah* says Miriam waited "one hour," it cannot be taken literally as a full hour. For the *Tosefta* teaches that *mida tovah merubbah mimiddat puranut al achat chamesh me'ot* — the measure of good exceeds the measure of punishment by five hundred times. Calculating: seven days contain 168 hours. If the ratio is 500:1, then Miriam must have waited only approximately one-third of an hour — roughly twenty minutes — not a full hour. *Tosafot* says "a third or a quarter," though the arithmetic points more precisely to a third.

*Tosafot* then notes a further difficulty: regarding the spies, the punishment was *yom lashanah* — a year for each day they spent scouting, which yields a ratio of 365:1. That is *midat puranut*, not *mida tovah*. But 365:1 is less than 500:1, yet the *Tosefta* claims that *mida tovah* exceeds *midat puranut* fivefold. Is then the *midat puranut* of the spies greater than the *mida tovah* of Miriam? The *Gemara* in the first chapter of *Chagigah* complicates things further: *Rabbi Yochanan* expounds the verse "they seek Me daily" to mean that whoever engages in Torah even one day is regarded as if he engaged all year — again a ratio of 365:1 — and the *Gemara* there explicitly equates this with the punishment of the spies. So are the two measures actually equal after all? The *Gemara* leaves the precise arithmetic to God.

### Divine Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּתַצַּב אֲחוֹתוֹ מֵרָחוֹק״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: פָּסוּק זֶה כּוּלּוֹ עַל שֵׁם שְׁכִינָה נֶאֱמַר: ״וַתֵּתַצַּב״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹא ה׳ וַיִּתְיַצַּב וְגוֹ׳״. ״אֲחוֹתוֹ״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״אֱמֹר לַחׇכְמָה אֲחוֹתִי אָתְּ״. ״מֵרָחוֹק״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵרָחוֹק ה׳ נִרְאָה לִי״. ״לָדַעַת״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אֵל דֵּעוֹת ה׳״. ״מָה״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״מַה ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ שֹׁאֵל מֵעִמָּךְ״. ״יֵּעָשֶׂה״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה ה׳ אֱלֹהִים דָּבָר״. ״לוֹ״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ ה׳ שָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Miriam's deed the verse states: **"And his sister stood afar off,** to know what would be done to him" (Exodus 2:4). **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: This entire verse is stated in reference to the Divine Presence,** i.e., each phrase alludes to the Divine Presence watching over Moses. **"And** his sister **stood"; as it is written: "And the Lord came, and stood"** (I Samuel 3:10). **"His sister"; as it is written: "Say to wisdom: You are my sister"** (Proverbs 7:4). **"Afar off"; as it is written: "From afar the Lord appeared to me"** (Jeremiah 31:2). **"To know"; as it is written: "For the Lord is a God of knowledge"** (I Samuel 2:3). **"What"; as it is written: "What does the Lord God require of you"** (Deuteronomy 10:12). **"Would be done"; as it is written: "For the Lord God will do nothing"** (Amos 3:7). **"To him"; as it is written: "And the Lord said to him: Peace** be with you" (Judges 6:23).

Returning to the verse *vatitatzav achoto merachok* — "and his sister stood from afar" — *Rabbi Yitzchak* teaches: this entire verse was said in the name of the *Shechina*; it was the divine presence that was hovering there to protect Moses, not merely Miriam standing watch. He demonstrates this by showing that each word of the verse is elsewhere associated with God: *vatitatzav* — to stand — appears in *vayavo Hashem vayityatzav*; *achoto* — sister — in *emor lechochma achoti at*, where wisdom, which is close to God, is called "sister"; *merachok* — from afar — in *merachok Hashem nirah li*; *lada'at* — to know — in *ki el de'ot Hashem*; *mah ya'aseh* — what will be done — in *ki lo ya'aseh Hashem Elokim davar*; and *lo* — to him — in *vayikra lo Hashem shalom*. Every word in the verse, on its own, connects back to God.

## Pharaoh's Plan

### New Pharaoh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּקׇם מֶלֶךְ חָדָשׁ וְגוֹ׳״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: חָדָשׁ מַמָּשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁנִּתְחַדְּשׁוּ גְּזֵירוֹתָיו. מַאן דְּאָמַר חָדָשׁ מַמָּשׁ — דִּכְתִיב ״חָדָשׁ״, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁנִּתְחַדְּשׁוּ גְּזֵירוֹתָיו, דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״וַיָּמׇת וַיִּמְלוֹךְ״. ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָדַע אֶת יוֹסֵף״, דַּהֲוָה דָּמֵי כְּמַאן דְּלָא יָדַע לֵיהּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the sojourn of the Jewish people in Egypt. The verse states: **"And there arose a new king** over Egypt, who knew not Joseph" (Exodus 1:8). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree about the interpretation of this verse. **One says** that this means he was **actually a new** king, **and one says** that this means **that his decrees were transformed** as if he were a new king. **The one who says** that he was **actually a new** king holds that it is **because it is written "new." And the one who says that his decrees were transformed** holds that it is **because it is not written: "And** the previous king of Egypt **died and** a new king **reigned."** This indicates that the same king remained. According to this interpretation, the words: **"Who knew not Joseph"** (Exodus 1:8), mean **that he was like someone who did not know him at all.** Although he certainly knew Joseph and his accomplishments, he acted as if he didn't.

With this the *Gemara* opens into the broader narrative of Moses and the Israelites in Egypt — a backdrop that will occupy the remainder of the daf. *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree about *vayakam melech chadash al Mitzrayim* — "and a new king arose over Egypt." One says he was literally a new king; the other says that in fact his decrees were renewed — his entire approach toward Israel changed. The support for the first view is simply the word *chadash* — it says "new." The support for the second view is that the text never says the old king died (*vayamot vayimloch*), implying continuity of the person with a radical change in policy. As for *asher lo yada et Yosef* — "who did not know Joseph" — the one who holds the policy-change view explains: he acted *keman dilo yadalei klal*, as if he had never known Joseph at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל עַמּוֹ הִנֵּה עַם בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. תָּנָא: הוּא הִתְחִיל בְּעֵצָה תְּחִילָּה לְפִיכָךְ לָקָה תְּחִילָּה. הוּא הִתְחִיל בְּעֵצָה תְּחִילָּה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל עַמּוֹ״, לְפִיכָךְ לָקָה תְּחִילָּה — כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וּבְכָה וּבְעַמְּךָ וּבְכׇל עֲבָדֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And he said to his people: Behold, the people of the children of Israel** are too many and too mighty for us" (Exodus 1:9). It was **taught** (*Tosefta* 4:11): **He,** Pharaoh, **initiated the proposal. Therefore,** of his people, **he was stricken first. He initiated the proposal, as it is written: "And he said to his people." Therefore, he was stricken first, as it is written:** "And the frogs shall come up **both upon you, and upon your people, and upon all your servants"** (Exodus 7:29).

The *Gemara* notes: *hu hitchil be'eitzah techila* — Pharaoh was the one who initiated the plot against Israel first, and therefore *lefichach lakah techila* — he was struck first, as it says: "and upon you and upon your nation and upon all your servants the frogs shall come up" — he was the first one hit by the plagues. This is the *midah keneged midah* of the initiator.

### Water Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָבָה נִתְחַכְּמָה לוֹ״. ״לָהֶם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בֹּאוּ וְנֶחְכַּם לְמוֹשִׁיעָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל —
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states that Pharaoh said: **"Come, let us deal wisely with him [*lo*],** lest he multiply, and it come to pass that when there befalls us any war, he will also join our enemies, and fight against us" (Exodus 1:10). The Gemara comments: **He should have** said in plural: **With them [*lahem*],** rather than the singular: "With him." **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** that Pharaoh was saying: **Come, let us deal wisely with regard to the savior of Israel,** referring to God.

The verse *hava nitchachma lo* — "come, let us deal wisely with them" — uses the singular *lo* where the plural *lahem* would be expected. *Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Chanina* understands this as deliberate: *bo'u venitchacham lemoshi'an shel Yisrael* — let us outwit the Savior of Israel. The *lo* refers not to the Israelites but to their divine protector. And the plan was calibrated precisely around the principle of *midah keneged midah*: Pharaoh knew that God avenges measure for measure, and so he sought a method of oppression that God could not repay in kind.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה נְדוּנֵם? נְדוּנֵם בָּאֵשׁ — כְּתִיב: ״כִּי הִנֵּה ה׳ בָּאֵשׁ יָבֹא״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בָאֵשׁ ה׳ נִשְׁפָּט וְגוֹ׳״. בְּחֶרֶב — כְּתִיב: [״וּבְחַרְבּוֹ אֶת כׇּל בָּשָׂר״]. אֶלָּא בּוֹאוּ וּנְדוּנֵם בַּמַּיִם, שֶׁכְּבָר נִשְׁבַּע הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא מַבּוּל לָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי מֵי נֹחַ זֹאת לִי וְגוֹ׳״. וְהֵן אֵינָן יוֹדְעִין שֶׁעַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא, אֲבָל עַל אוּמָּה אַחַת הוּא מֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** His advisors asked: **With what** form of death **shall we judge** and decree upon **them?** If **we shall judge them with fire,** perhaps we will be punished measure for measure by fire, as **it is written: "For behold, the Lord will come in fire"** (Isaiah 66:15), and **it is written** in the verse that follows it: **"For by fire will the Lord contend"** (Isaiah 66:16). Similarly, we cannot judge them **with the sword,** as **it is written** in the continuation of that verse: **"And by His sword with all flesh"** (Isaiah 66:16). **Rather, let us come and judge them with water,** by drowning the Jewish babies. God will not punish us with water, **for the Holy One, Blessed be He, already took an oath that He will not bring a flood upon the world, as it is stated: "For this is as the waters of Noah unto Me;** for as I have sworn that the waters of Noah should no more go over the earth" (Isaiah 54:9). The Gemara comments: **And** Pharaoh's advisors **did not know that He will not bring a flood upon all the world, but He** may **bring** destruction by water **upon one nation.**

Should we destroy them by fire? But Scripture says "Behold, God comes with fire" — God will repay us with fire. By the sword? But "with His sword against all flesh" — repayment in kind. Therefore: *ela bo'u venidonam bamayim* — let us cast them into the water. For God had already sworn never to bring another flood upon the world, as it says, "for this is as the waters of Noah to me, for I have sworn that the waters of Noah shall not again pass over the earth."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: הוּא אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא, אֲבָל הֵן בָּאִים וְנוֹפְלִין בְּתוֹכוֹ. וְכֵן הוא אוֹמֵר: ״וּמִצְרַיִם נָסִים לִקְרָאתוֹ״. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי בַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר זָדוּ עֲלֵיהֶם״ — בִּקְדֵירָה שֶׁבִּישְּׁלוּ, בָּהּ נִתְבַּשְּׁלוּ. מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״זָדוּ״ לִישָּׁנָא דִקְדֵירָה הוּא — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּזֶד יַעֲקֹב נָזִיד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there is an additional way to punish the Egyptians with water: **He does not bring** a flood upon them, **but they** may **come and fall into** water, **and so it says:** "And the sea returned to its strength when the morning appeared; **and the Egyptians fled toward it;** and the Lord overthrew the Egyptians in the midst of the sea" (Exodus 14:27), indicating that the Egyptians fell into the water. **And this is what Rabbi Elazar says: What is the** meaning of that **which is written:** "Now I know that the Lord is greater than all gods, **for in that which they conspired [*zadu*] against them"** (Exodus 18:11)? The phrase means: **In the pot in which they cooked, they** themselves **were cooked,** as they were punished through drowning, measure for measure, for drowning the Jewish babies. The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred that this** word **"*zadu*" is a term** meaning **a pot?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And Jacob simmered a pot [*vayyazed Ya'akov nazid*]"** (Genesis 25:29).

Their miscalculation, the *Gemara* explains, was twofold. First, *she'al kol ha'olam kulo eino meivi* — God swore He would not bring a flood upon the entire world, but He said nothing about destroying one nation by water. Second — and more pointedly — *aval hem ba'im venoflim betocho* — when the Egyptians pursued Israel, they ran into the sea themselves. God did not drown them; they came and fell into it of their own accord, as it says *uMitzrayim nasim likrato* — "and the Egyptians were fleeing toward it." *Rabbi Elazar* crystallizes this: the verse says *ki vadavar asher zadu aleihem* — in the very matter in which they schemed against them. And the word *zadu*, the *Gemara* notes, is the language of cooking — as in *vayazed Yaakov nazid*, "and Jacob cooked a stew." *Bekdeira shebishlu bah nitbashlu* — in the very pot in which they cooked, they themselves were cooked.

One might ask whether this entire passage — attributing to Pharaoh an awareness of *midah keneged midah* and a calculated effort to circumvent it — is consistent with his statement elsewhere that he does not recognize God's authority at all. It is a genuine tension: a *Midrash* that quotes verses from Isaiah anachronistically and posits a Pharaoh who is theologically sophisticated enough to scheme against the God of Israel sits uneasily alongside the Pharaoh who says "I do not know the Lord." This is a place where *midrash* and *peshat* do not align — and where the *Midrash* transforms the entire dynamic of the narrative in a profound way, making Pharaoh from the very outset a figure who knows and fears God, rather than one who simply refuses to recognize His authority.

### Three Advisors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי סִימַאי: שְׁלֹשָׁה הָיוּ בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה: בִּלְעָם, וְאִיּוֹב, וְיִתְרוֹ. בִּלְעָם שֶׁיָּעַץ — נֶהֱרַג, אִיּוֹב שֶׁשָּׁתַק — נִידּוֹן בְּיִסּוּרִין. יִתְרוֹ שֶׁבָּרַח — זָכוּ מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִשְׁפְּחוֹת סוֹפְרִים יוֹשְׁבֵי יַעְבֵּץ תִּרְעָתִים שִׁמְעָתִים שׂוּכָתִים הֵמָּה הַקִּינִים הַבָּאִים מֵחַמַּת אֲבִי בֵית רֵכָב״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי קֵינִי חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Simai says: Three** noteworthy people **were** consulted by Pharaoh **in that counsel** where Pharaoh questioned what should be done with the Jewish people. They were **Balaam, and Job, and Yitro.** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba teaches what occurred to each of them: **Balaam, who advised** Pharaoh to kill all sons born to the Jewish people, **was** punished by being **killed** in the war with Midian (see Numbers 31:8). **Job, who was silent** and neither advised nor protested, **was punished by suffering,** as detailed in the eponymous book in the Bible. **Yitro, who ran away** as a sign of protest, **merited that** some **of his children's children sat** in the Sanhedrin **in the Chamber of Hewn Stone, as it is stated: "And the families of scribes who dwelt at Jabez, Tirathites, Shimeathites, and Sucathites, these were the Kenites who descended from Hammath, the father of the house of Rechab"** (I Chronicles 2:55). **And it is written: "The children of the Kenite, Moses' father-in-law"** (Judges 1:16). This teaches that the Kenites, descendants of Yitro, the father-in-law of Moses, dwelt at Jabez [Yabetz], referring to the place where the Jewish people go for advice [*eitza*], i.e., the Chamber of Hewn Stone.

*Rabbi Simai* teaches: three were in that council who advised Pharaoh on how to deal with Israel — Balaam, Job, and Jethro. Balaam was the one who gave the advice; he is, after all, the figure who throughout the tradition counsels other nations on how to harm Israel — *ba v'iyaatzcha*, "come and I will advise you," and later it is Balaam's counsel that lies behind the incident of the daughters of Midian. For this, *neherag* — he was ultimately killed in the war against Midian.

Job was silent. He neither agreed nor protested — he lacked the courage to oppose Pharaoh. For this, *nidon b'yisurin* — he was afflicted with suffering. This is a striking passage, for it presents us with an answer to the question that the entire book of Job never explicitly resolves: why did Job suffer? Here the answer is that he was a silent enabler. There is a powerful parallel here to the famous *baraita* at the opening of *Masechet Avodah Zarah*, where the three sages before the Romans — one who praised Rome, one who was silent, and one who criticized — are treated not as morally equivalent but in terms of the degree to which their conduct constituted collaboration. Silence in the face of evil is itself a form of complicity.

Jethro fled. Whether he also protested before fleeing is implicit in his departure — his running away was itself a form of refusal, even if incomplete. For this, *zachu mibnei banav sheyeshvu b'lishkat hagazit* — his descendants merited to sit in the Chamber of Hewn Stone, the seat of the Sanhedrin. As the verse says, "the families of scribes who dwell in Yavetz" — *Tirathites, Shimeathites, Sucathites, these are the Kenites who came from Chamath, the father of the house of Rechab* — and the Kenites are identified as the descendants of Jethro, the father-in-law of Moses. They became the scribes and members of the Sanhedrin, serving in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *midah keneged midah* here is of counsel: because Jethro's response was ultimately the right one, his descendants came to be the very advisors of the nation of Israel.

## Treasure Cities

### Brick Ruse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּשִׂימוּ עָלָיו שָׂרֵי מִסִּים״, ״עֲלֵיהֶם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ מַלְבֵּן וְתָלוּ לוֹ לְפַרְעֹה בְּצַוָּארוֹ, וְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶם אִיסְטְנִיס אֲנִי, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כְּלוּם אִיסְטְנִיס אַתָּה יוֹתֵר מִפַּרְעֹה?
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"Therefore they did set over him taskmasters** in order to afflict him with their burdens" (Exodus 1:11). The Gemara comments: **It should have** stated: **Over them,** in the plural. **The school of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, taught:** This **teaches** that at first **they brought a brick mold and they hung it on the neck of Pharaoh** to create the appearance that he was also participating in the labor. **And** with regard to **each and every Jew who said to** the Egyptians: **I am a delicate person [*istenis*]** and I cannot participate in the labor, **they said to him: Are you at all more of a delicate person than Pharaoh,** and he is participating. Therefore, the verse states: "They did set over him," as they first placed the burden on Pharaoh as an artifice to enslave the Jewish people.

The *Gemara* now turns to *vayasimu alav sarei misim* — "and they placed upon him taskmasters." The use of the singular *alav* rather than the plural *aleihem* is noted, and a *baraita* from the school of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Shimon* explains: *she'hevi'u malben vetalu lo l'Paroh betzavaro* — they brought a form of a brick and hung it around Pharaoh's own neck. The ruse was to announce a great communal building project and have Pharaoh himself participate on the first day, so that no Israelite could claim to be too refined for the work. And indeed: if any Israelite said *istinis ani* — "I am too delicate for this" — they were told: "Are you more refined than Pharaoh himself?" In this way, everyone was pressed into labor on that opening day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְמַעַן עַנּוֹתוֹ בְּסִבְלוֹתָם״, [״עַנּוֹתָם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ]! לְמַעַן עַנּוֹתוֹ לְפַרְעֹה בְּסִבְלוֹתָם דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"In order to afflict him with their burdens"** (Exodus1:11). The Gemara comments: **It should have** stated: **"In order to afflict them,"** in the plural. Rather, the intention is, as mentioned previously, **in order to afflict Pharaoh, with** the result of causing **the burdens of the Jewish people.**

The *Gemara* continues with another grammatical tension: *lema'an anoto besivlotam* — the verse mixes singular and plural. The resolution offered is that *lema'an anoto* refers to Pharaoh — they oppressed that one for that single day — while *besivlotam* refers to Israel — all of this was for the sake of impressing the Israelites into their burdens. It was, in other words, a one-day ruse that served as the instrument of a far larger enslavement.

### Pithom/Raamses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּבֶן עָרֵי מִסְכְּנוֹת לְפַרְעֹה״, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: שֶׁמְּסַכְּנוֹת אֶת בַּעְלֵיהֶן. וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁמְּמַסְכְּנוֹת אֶת בַּעְלֵיהֶן, דְּאָמַר מָר: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּבִנְיָן מִתְמַסְכֵּן. ״אֶת פִּיתוֹם וְאֶת רַעַמְסֵס״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: פִּיתוֹם שְׁמָהּ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ רַעַמְסֵס — שֶׁרִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן מִתְרוֹסֵס. וְחַד אָמַר: רַעַמְסֵס שְׁמָהּ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ פִּיתוֹם — שֶׁרִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן פִּי תְהוֹם בּוֹלְעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concludes: **"And they built for Pharaoh storage cities [*miskenot*],** Pithom and Raamses" (Exodus 1:11). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree as to the precise interpretation of the word miskenot. **One says** that they are called this because they were the type of structures **that endanger [*mesakenot*] their owners,** as it is dangerous to work in cities with tall buildings. **And one says** that they are called this because this is the type of property **that impoverishes [*memaskenot*] their owners, as the Master said: All who engage in construction become poor.** The verse states that the names of the cities they built were **"Pithom and Raamses"** (Exodus 1:11). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree as to the precise interpretation of this verse, both assuming that only one city was built, which had primary and secondary names. **One says** that **Pithom** was **its** real **name, and why was it called Raamses?** It is an appellation indicating **that** as the buildings were constructed **they collapsed [*mitroses*] one by one** and needed to be rebuilt. **And one says** that **Raamses** was **its** real **name, and why was it called Pithom?** Because **the opening of the abyss [*pi tehom*] swallowed** each building they constructed **one by one, and it sunk into the ground.**

The *Gemara* addresses the word *miskenot* in *arei miskenot* — "treasure cities." *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree: one says *she'mesaknot et ba'aleihen* — these buildings endanger their builders (*Rashi* understands this as a general observation about construction projects, not limited to this specific case); the other says *she'memaskenot et ba'aleihen* — they impoverish their owners. As the tradition goes: *kol ha'osek bevinyan mitmasken* — anyone involved in building projects will come to poverty.

Regarding Pithom and Raamses themselves, again *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree. One says: Pithom was the city's actual name, and it was called Raamses descriptively — *she'rishon rishon mitroses* — because as soon as they would build it, each new course of construction would crumble and shatter. The other says: Raamses was the actual name, and the name Pithom came from *pi tehom bola'o* — the mouth of the abyss swallowed everything they built, since the ground was swampy and each construction sank immediately.

### Purposeless Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעֲבִדוּ מִצְרַיִם אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּפָרֶךְ״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: בְּפֶה רַךְ. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: בִּפְרִיכָה. ״אֵת כׇּל עֲבֹדָתָם אֲשֶׁר עָבְדוּ בָהֶם בְּפָרֶךְ״, אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: שֶׁהָיוּ מַחְלִיפִין מְלֶאכֶת אֲנָשִׁים לְנָשִׁים וּמְלֶאכֶת נָשִׁים לַאֲנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** *Sotah* 11b states: **"And the Egyptians made the children of Israel work** with rigor [*befarekh*]"** (Exodus 1:13). **Rabbi Elazar says:** The word *befarekh* is a conjugation of the words: **With a soft mouth [*bifeh rakh*],** as the Egyptians enticed the Jewish people into slavery, gradually subjugating them until they had lost their freedom completely. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** The word *befarekh* should be understood as: **With crushing [*bifrikha*],** as the Egyptians subjugated Israel with backbreaking labor. The verse also states: **"In all their service, wherein they made them serve with rigor"** (Exodus 1:14). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The meaning of *befarekh* is that the Egyptians **would exchange** the responsibilities of men and women, giving **men's work to women and women's work to men,** requiring everyone to do work to which they were unaccustomed.

This detail inspired a beautiful observation about the nature of *avodas parech*: beyond the physical brutality of the labor, what was most demoralizing was its utter purposelessness. Everything they built collapsed or sank — like Sisyphus, condemned to roll the stone up the hill only to watch it roll back down. The practice of making men do women's work and women do men's work — which the *Gemara* will shortly address — partakes of the same logic. *Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* citing *Rabbi Yonatan* explains *befarech* as *shehaya machlifim melachet anashim lenashim umelachet nashim la'anashim* — they would interchange the work of men and women and the work of women and men. *Rashi* notes: *vezu kashah shelo hayu regilim bekach* — this was especially harsh because they were completely unaccustomed to it. It was demoralizing on two levels: not only the backbreaking nature of the work but the stripping of dignity that comes with being forced to do that which is alien to your nature and training.

### Multiplying Promise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכַאֲשֶׁר יְעַנּוּ אוֹתוֹ כֵּן יִרְבֶּה וְכֵן יִפְרוֹץ״. ״כֵּן רַבּוּ וְכֵן פָּרְצוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ מְבַשַּׂרְתָּן: ״כֵּן יִרְבֶּה וְכֵן יִפְרוֹץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"But the more they afflicted him, the more he would multiply and the more he would spread about"** (Exodus 1:12). The Gemara comments: **It should have** stated: **The more they multiplied and the more they spread about,** in the past tense. **Reish Lakish says: Divine inspiration proclaimed to** the Egyptians: As long as this nation is afflicted, **the more he will multiply and the more he will spread about.**

The verse *vaka'asher ya'anu oto ken yirbeh veken yifrotz* — "as they afflict him, so shall he increase and so shall he spread" — uses the future tense where past tense (*rabu vefaretzu*, "they increased and they spread") would be expected. *Reish Lakish* explains: *ruach hakodesh mevasertan* — the divine spirit is announcing it. The oppressors say "afflict him," and God's voice comes back: "so shall he increase and so shall he spread." There is also a formulation that is even sharper: *atah amarta pen yirbeh, ani omer ken yirbeh* — you said "lest they multiply," I say "so they shall multiply." The echo of *pen yirbeh* and *ken yirbeh* makes the *midah keneged midah* almost structural: the exact same word, with the negative particle of fear replaced by the affirmative particle of divine promise. Conceptually, punishment operates like contraction, while blessing is expansive — which is precisely why the multiplier for *mida tovah* exceeds the multiplier for *midat puranut*.

## Righteous Women

### Women's Sustenance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב עַוִּירָא: בִּשְׂכַר נָשִׁים צִדְקָנִיּוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר נִגְאֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהוֹלְכוֹת לִשְׁאוֹב מַיִם, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְזַמֵּן לָהֶם דָּגִים קְטַנִּים בְּכַדֵּיהֶן, וְשׁוֹאֲבוֹת מֶחֱצָה מַיִם וּמֶחֱצָה דָּגִים, וּבָאוֹת וְשׁוֹפְתוֹת שְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל חַמִּין וְאַחַת שֶׁל דָּגִים. וּמוֹלִיכוֹת אֵצֶל בַּעְלֵיהֶן לַשָּׂדֶה, וּמַרְחִיצוֹת אוֹתָן, וְסָכוֹת אוֹתָן, וּמַאֲכִילוֹת אוֹתָן, וּמַשְׁקוֹת אוֹתָן, וְנִזְקָקוֹת לָהֶן בֵּין שְׁפַתַּיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם תִּשְׁכְּבוּן בֵּין שְׁפַתָּיִם וְגוֹ׳״, בִּשְׂכַר ״תִּשְׁכְּבוּן בֵּין שְׁפַתָּיִם״ זָכוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְבִיזַּת מִצְרַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה נֶחְפָּה בַכֶּסֶף וְאֶבְרוֹתֶיהָ בִּירַקְרַק חָרוּץ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Avira taught: In the merit of** the **righteous women that were in that generation, the Jewish people were redeemed from Egypt.** He tells of their righteous actions: **At the time** when these women **would go** to the river **to draw water, the Holy One, Blessed be He, would materialize for them small fish** that would enter **into their pitchers, and they would** therefore **draw** pitchers that were **half** filled with **water and half** filled with **fish. And they would** then **come and place two pots on the fire, one** pot **of hot** water for washing their husbands **and one** pot **of fish** with which to feed them. **And they would** then **take** what they prepared **to their husbands, to the field, and would bathe** their husbands **and anoint them** with oil **and feed them** the fish **and give them to drink and bond with them** in sexual intercourse **between the sheepfolds,** i.e., between the borders and fences of the fields, **as it is stated: "When you lie among the sheepfolds,** the wings of the dove are covered with silver, and her pinions with the shimmer of gold" (Psalms 68:14), which is interpreted to mean that **as a reward** for "when you **lie among the sheepfolds," the Jewish people merited to** receive **the plunder of Egypt, as it is stated** in the continuation of the verse, as a reference to the Jewish people: **"The wings of the dove are covered with silver, and her pinions with the shimmer of gold"** (Psalms 68:14).

*Rabbi Avira* expounds: *bisechar nashim tzidkaniyot shehayu beoto hador nigalu Yisrael miMitzrayim* — it was in the merit of the righteous women of that generation that Israel was ultimately redeemed from Egypt. The account is this: when the women would go to draw water, God would arrange that small fish would enter their pails alongside the water — *mesaknot lahem dagim ketanim bekadeihen* — so that their buckets came up half water and half fish. They would then set two pots on the fire, one to heat the water and one to cook the fish, and bring both out to their husbands in the field. There they would wash them, anoint them, feed them, and give them drink — and through this process restore their strength and their spirit until desire returned, and they would lie together *bein shefatayim* — between the folds.

One need not try to resolve every detail of this account with others. The broader theme here is important: it is the woman who is the agent of redemption, through acts of sustenance and intimacy — an echo of the *Yehudah* and *Tamar* story, where again it was the woman who was the initiator, again through an act of intimacy, and again it was ultimately connected to the chain that leads to *David Hamelech*. The drive that can lead to destruction when it crosses the boundaries of what is permitted — as with the *sotah* with whom this tractate began — can equally be the force that brings about the *geulah* when directed toward what is right.

The verse invoked is *im tishkevun bein shefatayim* — "if you lie down between the folds" — and through the merit of that lying down, *zachu Yisrael levizat Mitzrayim* — Israel merited the spoils of Egypt, as the verse continues: "the wings of a dove covered in silver and her feathers in a filigree of gold."

### Apple Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמִּתְעַבְּרוֹת בָּאוֹת לְבָתֵּיהֶם, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ זְמַן מוֹלְדֵיהֶן הוֹלְכוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת בַּשָּׂדֶה תַּחַת הַתַּפּוּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תַּחַת הַתַּפּוּחַ עוֹרַרְתִּיךָ וְגוֹ׳״. וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שׁוֹלֵחַ מִשְּׁמֵי מָרוֹם מִי שֶׁמְּנַקֵּיר וּמְשַׁפֵּיר אוֹתָן, כְּחַיָּה זוֹ שֶׁמְּשַׁפֶּרֶת אֶת הַוָּלָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמוֹלְדוֹתַיִךְ בְּיוֹם הוּלֶּדֶת אוֹתָךְ לֹא כׇרַּת שׇׁרֵּךְ וּבְמַיִם לֹא רֻחַצְתְּ לְמִשְׁעִי וְגוֹ׳״. וּמְלַקֵּט לָהֶן שְׁנֵי עִגּוּלִין, אֶחָד שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן וְאֶחָד שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּנִקֵהוּ דְבַשׁ מִסֶּלַע וְשֶׁמֶן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when** these women would **become pregnant, they would come** back **to their homes, and when the time for them to give birth** would arrive **they would go and give birth in the field under the apple tree, as it is stated: "Under the apple tree I awakened you;** there your mother was in travail with you; there was she in travail and brought you forth" (Song of Songs 8:5). **And the Holy One, Blessed be He, would send from the heavens above** an angel **who would clean and prepare** the newborns, **just as a midwife prepares the newborn, as it is stated: "And as for your birth, on the day you were born, your navel was not cut nor were you washed with water for cleansing;** you were not salted at all, nor swaddled at all" (Ezekiel 16:4). This indicates that there were no midwives to take care of the Jews born in Egypt. **And** then, the angel **would gather for them two round** stones from the field and the babies would nurse from that which would flow out of them. **One of** the stones flowed with **oil and one of** the stones flowed with **honey, as it is stated: "And He would suckle them with honey from a crag and oil** from a flinty rock" (Deuteronomy 32:13).

Once pregnant, the women would return home, and when the time of birth came, they would go out to give birth in the field — *tachat hatapuach*, under the apple tree — away from the eyes of the Egyptians. What exactly a *tapuach* is remains uncertain: apples are not indigenous to the Middle East, and various commentators have suggested the *etrog* or other fruits. Notably, the word "apple" in many languages originally carried the generic meaning of "fruit" — hence *tapuach adamah* for potato. The *Shir Hashirim* verse *tachat hatapuach orarticha* — "under the tapuach I aroused you" — is applied here to this context of birth.

God Himself, the *Gemara* continues, would send something from the heavenly realms to care for the newborns — cleansing them, preparing them, as a skilled midwife prepares a newborn infant, and providing them nourishment. The verse from *Yechezkel* is invoked: "on the day that you were born, your navel cord was not cut, and you were not washed with water" — the text of the *Haggadah*'s *derasha* connects here, where the entire metaphor of *Yechezkel* 16 about the founding of the nation is read literally as describing these miraculous births. God supplied them with *devash misela veshemen mechalmish tzur* — honey from the rock and oil from the flinty stone — as the verse in *Devarim* says: *vayenikeyu devash misela* — He suckled them with honey from the rock.

### Ground & Grass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמַּכִּירִין בָּהֶן מִצְרִים בָּאִים לְהוֹרְגָן, וְנַעֲשָׂה לָהֶם נֵס וְנִבְלָעִין בַּקַּרְקַע, וּמְבִיאִין שְׁוָורִים וְחוֹרְשִׁים עַל גַּבָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל גַּבִּי חָרְשׁוּ חֹרְשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״. לְאַחַר שֶׁהוֹלְכִין הָיוּ מְבַצְבְּצִין וְיוֹצְאִין כְּעֵשֶׂב הַשָּׂדֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רְבָבָה כְּצֶמַח הַשָּׂדֶה נְתַתִּיךְ״. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמִּתְגַּדְּלִין בָּאִין עֲדָרִים עֲדָרִים לְבָתֵּיהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּרְבִּי וַתִּגְדְּלִי וַתָּבֹאִי בַּעֲדִי עֲדָיִים״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בַּעֲדִי עֲדָיִים״, אֶלָּא ״בְּעֶדְרֵי עֲדָרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And once the Egyptians would notice them,** realizing that they were Jewish babies, **they would come to kill them. But a miracle would occur for them and they would be absorbed by the earth. And** the Egyptians **would** then **bring oxen and would plow upon them, as it is stated: "The plowers plowed upon my back;** they made long their furrows" (Psalms 129:3). **After** the Egyptians **would leave,** the babies **would emerge and exit** the ground **like grass of the field, as it is stated: "I caused you to increase even as the growth of the field"** (Ezekiel 16:7). **And once** the babies **would grow, they would come like many flocks** of sheep **to their homes, as it is stated** in the continuation of the verse: **"And you did increase and grow up and you came with excellent beauty [*ba'adi adayim*]"** (Ezekiel 16:7). **Do not read** the verse as: **"*Ba'adi adayim*,"** "with excellent beauty." **Rather,** read it as: ***Be'edrei adarim*,** meaning: As many flocks.

Once the Egyptians became aware that births were occurring, they came to kill the infants. At that moment, a miracle occurred: *venivla'in bakarka* — the babies were swallowed into the ground. The Egyptians then brought oxen and plowed over the places where the babies lay buried, but they were too deep and were protected. After the Egyptians left, *hayu mevatzbetzin veyotzin* — the babies would sprout out from the ground *ke'esev hasadeh*, like the grass of the field. The verse *revava ketzamach hasadeh nesatich* is cited — the very verse from the *Haggadah* that forms part of the *derasha* on that passage in *Yechezkel*: "I caused you to increase like the plant of the field." The *Gemara* is reading that verse with full literalness. The metaphor of the birthing of the nation and the literal birth of these children are collapsed into one.

When these children grew up, *ba'in adarim adarim levateihem* — they would come home in flocks, like flocks of animals from the field. The verse is read *al tikrei ba'adi adayim ela be'edrei adarim* — do not read "in jewels" but "in flocks." The image is striking: born in nature, swallowed by the earth, sprouting like grass, and returning home as flocks — they are almost animals of the field, untamed, raised by the earth itself.

### God Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁנִּגְלָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל הַיָּם, הֵם הִכִּירוּהוּ תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when the Holy One, Blessed be He, revealed Himself at the** Red **Sea,** these children **recognized Him first, as it is stated: "This is my God, and I will glorify Him"** (Exodus 15:2). They recognized Him from the previous time that He revealed Himself to them in their infancy, enabling them to say: "This is my God."

When God revealed Himself at the sea, *hem hikiruhu techila* — they were the first to recognize Him. And why? Because they had already encountered Him in the field when they were infants — it was through God's care that they had been bathed, nourished, and protected. And so at the sea they were able to cry out *zeh Keili ve'anveihu* — "this is my God and I will glorify Him." They recognized Him because they had known Him before.

## Midwives

### Shifra & Puah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם לַמְיַלְּדוֹת הָעִבְרִיּוֹת וְגוֹ׳״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, וְחַד אָמַר: כַּלָּה וַחֲמוֹתָהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ — יוֹכֶבֶד וּמִרְיָם, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר כַּלָּה וַחֲמוֹתָהּ — יוֹכֶבֶד וֶאֱלִישֶׁבַע. תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: שִׁפְרָה זוֹ יוֹכֶבֶד, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ שִׁפְרָה — שֶׁמְּשַׁפֶּרֶת אֶת הַוָּלָד. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״שִׁפְרָה״ — שֶׁפָּרוּ וְרָבוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיָמֶיהָ. ״פּוּעָה״ — זוֹ מִרְיָם. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״פּוּעָה״ שֶׁהָיְתָה פּוֹעָה וּמוֹצִיאָה אֶת הַוָּלָד. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״פּוּעָה״ — שֶׁהָיְתָה פּוֹעָה בְּרוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ וְאוֹמֶרֶת: עֲתִידָה אִמִּי שֶׁתֵּלֵד בֵּן שֶׁמּוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"And the king of Egypt spoke to the Hebrew midwives,** of whom the name of the one was Shiphrah, and the name of the other Puah" (Exodus 1:15). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree as to the proper interpretation of this verse. **One says** that these midwives were **a woman and her daughter, and one says** that they were **a daughter-in-law and her mother-in-law.** According to the **one who says** that they were **a woman and her daughter,** the women were **Jochebed,** the mother of Moses and Aaron, **and** her daughter, **Miriam. And** according to the **one who says** that they were **a daughter-in-law and her mother-in-law,** the verse is referring to **Jochebed and** her daughter-in-law **Elisheba,** the wife of Aaron. It **is taught** in a *baraita* **according to the one who says** that they were **a woman and her daughter, because it** is **taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **Shiphrah,** who is referred to in the verse, **this** is really a reference to **Jochebed. And why was she called Shiphrah? Because she would prepare [*mishapperet*] the newborn. Alternatively,** she is referred to as **Shiphrah because the Jewish people increased and multiplied [*shepparu verabbu*] in her days,** due to her assistance. The *baraita* continues: With regard to **Puah,** who is referred to in the verse, **this** is really a reference to **Miriam. And why was she called Puah? Because she would make a comforting sound [*po'a*] as she would remove the child** from the womb of the mother. **Alternatively,** the word **Puah** is related to one of the verbs that describe speaking, **as she would speak [*po'a*] through divine inspiration and say: In the future, my mother will give birth to a son who will save the Jewish people.**

*Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree about the identity of the two midwives. One says they were a mother and daughter (*isha uvita*), the other says a daughter-in-law and mother-in-law (*kala vechamotah*). The one who holds they were a mother and daughter identifies them as Yocheved and Miriam. The one who holds they were a daughter-in-law and mother-in-law identifies them as Yocheved and Elisheva, Aaron's wife.

A *baraita* sides with the mother-daughter view. *Shifra zu Yocheved* — Shifra was Yocheved. Why was she called Shifra? *Shemeshaperet et havelad* — she would beautify and care for the newborn; and additionally, *sheparu veravu Yisrael beyameha* — Israel was fruitful and multiplied in her days. *Puah zu Miriam* — Puah was Miriam. Why was she called Puah? *She'hayeta po'ah* — she would coo in order to help the child emerge, using soothing sounds to help the infant along. An alternate explanation: *she'hayeta po'ah beruach hakodesh* — she would speak with the divine spirit, saying: "My mother is destined to give birth to a son who will be the savior of Israel." And indeed, *moshi'an shel Yisrael* — the savior of Israel — is the very phrase we encountered earlier in Pharaoh's council, where the plan was to outwit *moshi'an shel Yisrael*. There, one might have taken it as referring to God; here, retrospectively, it becomes clear it refers to Moses.

*Tosafot* cites the explanation of *Rabbeinu Chananel* regarding *po'ah*: it refers to an incantation whispered to ease childbirth — *lo chashot lichisha* — and the practice of whispering such incantations to a woman in labor was common even in Tosafot's own time.

### Birth Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר בְּיַלֶּדְכֶן אֶת הָעִבְרִיּוֹת וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״אׇבְנָיִם״ — אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן: סִימָן גָּדוֹל מָסַר לָהֶן. אָמַר לָהֶן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכּוֹרַעַת לֵילֵד יַרְכוֹתֶיהָ מִצְטַנְּנוֹת כַּאֲבָנִים. וְאִית דְּאָמַר, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וָאֵרֵד בֵּית הַיּוֹצֵר וְהִנֵּה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה עַל הָאׇבְנָיִם״, מָה יוֹצֵר זֶה — יָרֵךְ מִכָּאן וְיָרֵךְ מִכָּאן וְסַדָּן בָּאֶמְצַע, אַף אִשָּׁה — יָרֵךְ מִכָּאן וְיָרֵךְ מִכָּאן וְהַוָּלָד בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse relates the instructions of Pharaoh to the midwives: **"And he said: When you deliver the Hebrew women,** and you look upon the stones [*ovnayim*], if it be a son, then you shall kill him; but if it be a daughter, then she shall live" (Exodus 1:16). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"stones"? Rabbi Ḥanan says:** Pharaoh **transmitted a great sign to them. He said to them: At the time** when a woman **crouches to give birth, her thighs become as cold as stones,** and, therefore, this shall be for you a sign that the woman is about to give birth. **And there are those who say** an alternative explanation for *ovnayim*: **As it is written: "So I went down to the potter's shop, and behold, he was at his work on the wheels [*ovnayim*]"** (Jeremiah 18:3). **Just as this potter** sits so that one **thigh** is **here and** one **thigh** is **there and the block** upon which he works **is in the middle, so too, a woman** giving birth also has one **thigh here and** one **thigh there and the newborn is in the middle.**

*Rabbi Chanana* explains: Pharaoh gave them a sign by which to know that birth was imminent. When a woman crouches to give birth, *yarchoteiha mitztanenot ka'avanim* — her thighs become cold as stone — and that is when the midwives would need to be present to intercept the newborn.

An alternative explanation connects *avnayim* to the image of the potter's wheel: *va'ered beit hayotzer vehinei hu oseh melachah al ha'avnayim* — the potter works the stone, with one side here and one side there and the pedestal in the middle. Similarly, in childbirth: one thigh on this side, one thigh on the other, and the child coming out in the middle. The word *avnayim* thus becomes a metaphor for the birthing position.

### Refusal & Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִם בֵּן הוּא וַהֲמִתֶּן אוֹתוֹ״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, סִימָן גָּדוֹל מָסַר לָהֶן: בֵּן פָּנָיו לְמַטָּה, בַּת פָּנֶיהָ לְמַעְלָה. ״וַתִּירֶאןָ הַמְיַלְּדוֹת אֶת הָאֱלֹהִים וְלֹא עָשׂוּ כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר אֲלֵיהֶן וְגוֹ׳״. ״לָהֶן״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁתְּבָעָן לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה וְלֹא נִתְבְּעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"If it be a son, then you shall kill him;** but if it be a daughter, then she shall live" (Exodus 1:16). **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** Pharaoh **transmitted to them a great sign** to enable them to know the gender of the infant from the beginning of the birth process: **A boy** is born with **his face downward; a girl** is born with **her face upward.** Pharaoh provided them with this sign so that they could kill the boys secretly even before the mother realized what was happening. The next verse states: **"But the midwives feared God, and did not as the king of Egypt spoke about them [*aleihen*],** but they kept the male children alive" (Exodus 1:17). The Gemara comments: **It should have** stated: "Spoke **to them [*lahen*]." Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** This **teaches that** Pharaoh **proposed to them to** engage in **a sinful act,** i.e., sexual intercourse, with him, **but they did not accept** his overtures. The word *aleihen* is often used in reference to sexual intercourse, for example: "And brought her to him; and he consorted with her [*eileha*]" (Genesis 29:23), and that is its connotation here as well.

*Rabbi Chanina* teaches that Pharaoh also gave the midwives a sign by which to distinguish boy from girl even before an examination: *ben panav lematah* — a boy comes out face down; *bat paneha lema'alah* — a girl face up. This connects to the *Gemara*'s broader understanding of conception: since the *Gemara* assumes the male is above and the female below during intercourse, the child is born in the position corresponding to its parent — a boy in the father's position, a girl in the mother's.

The verse says the midwives feared God and did not do as the king of Egypt had commanded them *aleihen*. The use of *aleihen* rather than the expected *lahen* prompts *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina* to suggest that Pharaoh had also tried to seduce them to sin sexually — *sha'tav'an lidvar aveirah* — using *alav* in the sense of *lavo eleiha*, which the *Gemara* reads as a euphemism for sexual approach. They refused on that account as well.

### Women as Beasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתְּחַיֶּיןָ אֶת הַיְּלָדִים״. תָּנָא: לֹא דַּיָּין שֶׁלֹּא הֵמִיתוּ אוֹתָן, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיוּ מַסְפִּיקוֹת לָהֶם מַיִם וּמָזוֹן. ״וַתֹּאמַרְןָ הַמְיַלְּדוֹת אֶל פַּרְעֹה כִּי לֹא כַנָּשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״חָיוֹת״? אִילֵימָא חַיּוֹת מַמָּשׁ, אַטּוּ חַיָּה מִי לָא צְרִיכָה חַיָּה אַחֲרִיתִי לְאוֹלוֹדַהּ? אֶלָּא אָמְרוּ לוֹ, אוּמָּה זוֹ כְּחַיָּה נִמְשְׁלָה: יְהוּדָה — ״גּוּר אַרְיֵה״, דָּן — ״יְהִי דָן נָחָשׁ״, ״נַפְתָּלִי אַיָּלָה שְׁלוּחָה״, ״יִשָּׂשכָר חֲמוֹר גָּרֶם״, יוֹסֵף — ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹר״, ״בִּנְיָמִין זְאֵב יִטְרָף״. דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ — כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ. וּדְלָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ — כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״מַה אִמְּךָ לְבִיָּא בֵּין אֲרָיוֹת רָבָצָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concludes: **"But they kept the male children alive"** (Exodus 1:17). A Sage **teaches:** It is **not only that they did not kill** the children as Pharaoh had commanded them, **but that they would** even **provide for them water and food,** as the phrase "But they kept the male children alive" indicates. After being questioned by Pharaoh concerning their failure to obey his command, the midwives responded, as it is written: **"And the midwives said to Pharaoh: Because** the Hebrew women **are not as the** Egyptian **women,** for they are lively [*ḥayot*], and are delivered before the midwife comes to them" (Exodus 1:19). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"*ḥayot*"? If we say** that the Hebrew women are like *ḥayot*, meaning **actual midwives** for themselves, and therefore they do not need assistance from others, **is that to say** that **a midwife does not need** the assistance of **another midwife** in order **to help her give birth?** **Rather,** the midwives **said to** Pharaoh: **This nation is compared to an animal [*ḥayya*],** and animals give birth without a midwife. For example, with regard to **Judah** it is written: "Judah is **a lion's whelp"** (Genesis 49:9); with regard to **Dan** it is written: **"Dan shall be a serpent** in the way" (Genesis 49:17); with regard to **Naphtali** it is written: **"A hind let loose"** (Genesis 49:21); with regard to **Issachar** it is written: **"A large-boned donkey"** (Genesis 49:14); with regard to **Joseph** it is written: "His **first bullock"** (Deuteronomy 33:17); with regard to **Benjamin** it is written: **"A ravenous wolf"** (Genesis 49:27). **Concerning those where** a comparison to an animal **is written with regard to him,** it **is** already **written with regard to him. And** concerning those **where no** specific metaphor comparing them to an animal is **written with regard to him** explicitly, in any case a general comparison **is written about** the Jewish people: **"How your mother was a lioness; among lions she crouched,** in the midst of the young lions she reared her whelps" (Ezekiel 19:2), indicating that all the Jewish people are compared to animals.

*Vatechayena et hayeladim* — "and they gave life to the children." A *baraita* teaches: not only did they not kill the children, they actively provided them with water and food to sustain them.

The midwives' excuse to Pharaoh — *ki lo ka'nashim hamitzriot ha'ivriyot ki chayot hena* — "for the Hebrew women are not like the Egyptian women, for they are *chayot*" — is read carefully by the *Gemara*. Does *chayot* mean midwives? The *Gemara* objects: *atu chaya mi lo tzaricha chaya achrita l'aolodah* — surely a midwife when she herself gives birth needs another midwife! A barber needs a barber to cut his own hair. So *chayot mamash* cannot be the meaning here.

Rather, *ela amru lo: uma zu kechaya nimshala* — they told him: this nation is compared to an animal. They give birth without help, as animals of the field do. And this is supported by the very tribal blessings of Jacob: Judah is a lion cub, Dan is a serpent, Naphtali a swift deer, Issachar a burdened donkey, Joseph a firstborn ox, Benjamin a wolf. For those tribes where the comparison is explicit in the text, it is written; and for those where it is not, the verse *mah imcha laviya bein arayot* — "your mother was a lioness among lions" — covers the rest. All of Israel, in some sense, is likened to animals.

This is, in fact, a deeply layered *peshat*. The women who gave birth under the apple trees in the field, whose infants were swallowed into the earth and sprouted like grass, who came home in flocks — they are, in the most literal sense of the *Gemara*'s narrative, being born as creatures of the natural world, beyond the reach of Pharaoh's machinery. But there is another dimension as well. Part of the process of enslaving a people is dehumanizing them — reducing them in the eyes of the oppressor to something less than human. Pharaoh could believe the midwives' claim precisely because he had already begun that process of dehumanization. Yet the *Gemara* turns that very trope on its head: yes, they are like animals — and that is their glory, their resilience, their source of redemption.
