# Sotah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Absalom's Hair

### Intro: Absalom

Today's daf is 11. We pick up at the bottom of 10b, in the middle of a story about Absalom (*Avshalom*) within the larger context of *midah keneged midah* that began with the *sotah* and her punishments. Now we turn to Absalom: his hair was the most beautiful, almost divine thing about him, and ultimately it proved to be his downfall.

The *Gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *Tanu Rabbanan* — Absalom rebelled through his hair, as it says, "And like Absalom there was no man as beautiful... and when he shaved his head — for it was heavy upon him and he shaved it — he weighed the hair of his head at two hundred *shekalim* by the king's weight." *Rashi* reminds us, in case we have forgotten what we learned in *Masechet Nazir*, that the *Gemara*'s understanding is that Absalom was a *nazir olam* and was therefore permitted to shave his hair once every twelve months. The hair provided him a pretext — gathering people around him and leading an insurrection — and presumably filled him with the sense that he was deserving of the kingship, though the *Gemara* does not say that explicitly.

### Hair & Pride

The *Gemara* then offers a *derasha* on the phrase "the king's weight," identifying it as a standard measure used by the people of Tiberias and Tzipori. From this flows the moral consequence: *lefichach nitlah besearo* — therefore he was caught by his hair. He was, as it were, hoisted by his own petard. As it says, "And Absalom rode before the servants of David, and Absalom rode upon the mule, and the mule went under the thick branches of the great terebinth, and his head caught in the terebinth, and he was suspended between heaven and earth, and the mule that was under him passed on."

### Tree & Death

The *Gemara* then addresses a natural question: why did he not simply cut himself free? It says he took a sword and wanted to cut his hair — but at that very moment, *tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* teaches, the depths of *Sheol* opened up beneath him. The insight offered in discussion resolves the difficulty elegantly: Absalom could not cut his hair because he now faced a second peril — if he cut himself free, he would fall into the abyss that had opened below him. That explains his paralysis.

Following Joab's killing of Absalom, the report of his death was brought to King David, who went up to the gate and wept, crying out: *bni Avshalom, bni bni Avshalom, mi yiten muti ani tachtecha Avshalom bni bni* — "My son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom, would that I had died in your place, Absalom my son, my son." And then again: *Avshalom bni bni*. It is difficult to overstate the pathos of these verses — arguably the saddest passage in all of *Tanakh*. No matter what Absalom had done, David's anguish is complete and unconditional. That is precisely what makes it so unbearably poignant.

### David's Lament

The *Gemara* asks: *hanei tmanya bni lama* — why are there eight utterances of "my son"? Seven of them, it explains, correspond to the seven chambers of *Gehinnom*: each one of David's cries raised Absalom up from one level of the depths, each one lifting him a little closer to the surface. The eighth utterance — according to one opinion — served to reconnect Absalom's severed head to his body (though this detail does not appear explicitly in the biblical text); according to another opinion, David's prayer actually brought Absalom to *Olam Haba*.

### Gehinnom & Prayer

This last point raises an important question taken up by *Tosafot*: the principle stated in *Chelek* is *bra mezakei aba* — a son's merits can benefit his deceased father — but not *aba mezakei bra*, a father cannot bring benefit to his son in this way. How, then, did David's prayer avail Absalom?

*Tosafot* offers two responses. First, Absalom had not worshipped idols, and therefore the case may be different from those situations in which a sinner is wholly beyond redemption. Second, and more directly: the principle that *aba lo mezakei bra* means only that out of respect for the father, we do not ordinarily count the son's deeds as accruing to the father's merit — but that is a matter of formal accounting. Prayer, however, is different: *aval tefila mo'eles* — prayer is effective. And David prayed for Absalom.

This raises a deeper conceptual issue. *Kaddish* operates on a somewhat different logic: the son's recitation of *kaddish* sanctifies God's name in the world, and since the deceased father brought into the world a child who now performs this act, the merit redounds indirectly to him — *bra mezakei aba* in the classic sense. But praying directly for the welfare of another's soul is a distinct concept from performing good deeds whose merit flows back through a causal chain. One is asking God to act; the other is creating a new meritorious act attributable to the deceased through the child.

It is interesting, in passing, to note the parallels with the legends that grew up around the death of Shabtai Tzvi — the myths about prayer campaigns to elevate him from *Gehinnom* and bring him to *Olam Haba*. He remained so powerful a figure in the popular imagination that people could not accept that his sin was the final word. Something similar seems to be at work in the *Gemara*'s wrestling with whether David's prayer could ultimately bring Absalom to *Olam Haba*. On the question of gradations: once a soul has been removed from punishment, it is one further step to *Olam Haba* — not to the highest level, but to some entry-level portion. The *Gemara* in *Rosh Hashanah* does speak of the *reshaim* dwelling in a kind of temporary state for twelve months before their final judgment, something not entirely unlike the concept of purgatory in other traditions.

### Monument

The *Gemara* continues: "And Absalom had taken and set up for himself a monument in his lifetime." The *Tanakh* reports that after his death it was recalled that Absalom had already erected this monument, calling it *Yad Avshalom*, because he said, "I have no son to keep my name in remembrance."

The *Gemara* asks: *mai lakach* — what does "he had taken" mean? *Reish Lakish* says: just as *Vayikach Korach* means that Korach made a bad transaction, so too here — *shelakach mekach ra le'atzmo* — he had made a bad investment for himself. Whether this refers to his foreseeing his own death by erecting a monument, or to the entire disastrous enterprise of the rebellion, is left somewhat open.

*Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa* adds: *be'etzah amukah shel malko shel olam* — this was all part of the deep counsel of the King of the universe. *Rashi* explains this with reference to the punishment decreed upon David after the sin of Bathsheba — God had told him that evil would arise from within his own house — *hineni mekim alecha ra'ah mi'beitecha*. Without diminishing Absalom's own *bechirat chofshit*, the episode unfolded in accordance with a divine plan. The *Gemara* draws a parallel to the story of Joseph: *Vayishla'chehu me'emek Chevron* — he was sent from the valley of Hebron. *Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa* reads *emek* here as "deep counsel," the deep strategy of that righteous one buried in Hebron, namely Abraham, and connects this to the divine decree of *yado'a teda ki ger yihyeh zar'acha* — "Know well that your descendants will be strangers." Joseph's being sent to his brothers was all part of the mechanism by which that divine promise would come to be fulfilled. The question of *hashgacha pratit* and *bechirah chofshit* operating simultaneously is, as the *Gemara* itself seems to acknowledge, a formidable one — and the text moves on.

### Children & Wheat

The *Gemara* now addresses the monument's stated rationale: *ki amar ein li ben* — Absalom said he had no son. But this is puzzling, since the *Tanakh* earlier reports that he had three sons and one daughter. *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi* explains: he had no son *hagun lemalkhut* — no son fit to inherit a kingdom. This detail itself implies that even at the time he erected the monument, Absalom was already anticipating his bid for the throne — for why else would fitness for kingship matter?

*Rav Chisda* adds a *gemiri* — a received tradition: whoever burns another's grain (*tevuato shel chavero*) will leave no son to inherit him. This explains why Absalom's sons predeceased him, though *Rav Chisda* acknowledges the tradition is transmitted as a general principle. The underlying logic is a form of *midah keneged midah*: just as one destroys the fruit of another's labor — that which was planted and nurtured — so one's own progeny, the fruit of one's own life, will be cut down.

The episode being referenced is Absalom's burning of Joab's wheat field. After Absalom had been permitted to return to Jerusalem but the king still refused to receive him, Absalom needed to compel Joab to intercede on his behalf. Since Joab was not responding to his messages, Absalom instructed his servants: "Go and set fire to Joab's field." And so they burned the field. That act of destruction ultimately led to his restoration in the eyes of the king — and set in motion the chain of events that would culminate in the rebellion.

## Miriam & Measure

### Miriam Hovers

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah*, which teaches that just as there is *midah keneged midah* for punishment, so there is *midah keneged midah* for good — and the example is Miriam, who waited over Moses for one hour, and in the end the entire nation of Israel waited for her seven days when she was stricken. But *Abaye* raises an objection: can this really be called *midah keneged midah*? She waited one hour — *hatam chada sha'ata* — while Israel waited seven days. That is not measure for measure; it is vastly disproportionate. *Abaye* therefore proposes reading the *Mishnah* as saying that for *midah tovah* it is specifically not so — the reward is greater.

*Rava* objects: the *Mishnah* says *vechen linyan hatovah* — "and likewise for good." You cannot simply change the *girsa*. The concept is the same; what differs is only the quantity. The resolution is that *midah keneged midah* applies to both directions, but *mida tovah* is always *merubah* — greater — than *midat puranut*.

### Math: Hour & Days

*Tosafot* picks up the mathematical precision of this point. *Tosafot* notes that when the *Mishnah* says Miriam waited "one hour," it cannot be taken literally as a full hour. For the *Tosefta* teaches that *mida tovah merubbah mimiddat puranut al achat chamesh me'ot* — the measure of good exceeds the measure of punishment by five hundred times. Calculating: seven days contain 168 hours. If the ratio is 500:1, then Miriam must have waited only approximately one-third of an hour — roughly twenty minutes — not a full hour. *Tosafot* says "a third or a quarter," though the arithmetic points more precisely to a third.

*Tosafot* then notes a further difficulty: regarding the spies, the punishment was *yom lashanah* — a year for each day they spent scouting, which yields a ratio of 365:1. That is *midat puranut*, not *mida tovah*. But 365:1 is less than 500:1, yet the *Tosefta* claims that *mida tovah* exceeds *midat puranut* fivefold. Is then the *midat puranut* of the spies greater than the *mida tovah* of Miriam? The *Gemara* in the first chapter of *Chagigah* complicates things further: *Rabbi Yochanan* expounds the verse "they seek Me daily" to mean that whoever engages in Torah even one day is regarded as if he engaged all year — again a ratio of 365:1 — and the *Gemara* there explicitly equates this with the punishment of the spies. So are the two measures actually equal after all? The *Gemara* leaves the precise arithmetic to God.

### Divine Protection

Returning to the verse *vatitatzav achoto merachok* — "and his sister stood from afar" — *Rabbi Yitzchak* teaches: this entire verse was said in the name of the *Shechina*; it was the divine presence that was hovering there to protect Moses, not merely Miriam standing watch. He demonstrates this by showing that each word of the verse is elsewhere associated with God: *vatitatzav* — to stand — appears in *vayavo Hashem vayityatzav*; *achoto* — sister — in *emor lechochma achoti at*, where wisdom, which is close to God, is called "sister"; *merachok* — from afar — in *merachok Hashem nirah li*; *lada'at* — to know — in *ki el de'ot Hashem*; *mah ya'aseh* — what will be done — in *ki lo ya'aseh Hashem Elokim davar*; and *lo* — to him — in *vayikra lo Hashem shalom*. Every word in the verse, on its own, connects back to God.

## Pharaoh's Plan

### New Pharaoh

With this the *Gemara* opens into the broader narrative of Moses and the Israelites in Egypt — a backdrop that will occupy the remainder of the daf. *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree about *vayakam melech chadash al Mitzrayim* — "and a new king arose over Egypt." One says he was literally a new king; the other says that in fact his decrees were renewed — his entire approach toward Israel changed. The support for the first view is simply the word *chadash* — it says "new." The support for the second view is that the text never says the old king died (*vayamot vayimloch*), implying continuity of the person with a radical change in policy. As for *asher lo yada et Yosef* — "who did not know Joseph" — the one who holds the policy-change view explains: he acted *keman dilo yadalei klal*, as if he had never known Joseph at all.

The *Gemara* notes: *hu hitchil be'eitzah techila* — Pharaoh was the one who initiated the plot against Israel first, and therefore *lefichach lakah techila* — he was struck first, as it says: "and upon you and upon your nation and upon all your servants the frogs shall come up" — he was the first one hit by the plagues. This is the *midah keneged midah* of the initiator.

### Water Plan

The verse *hava nitchachma lo* — "come, let us deal wisely with them" — uses the singular *lo* where the plural *lahem* would be expected. *Rabbi Chama b'Rabbi Chanina* understands this as deliberate: *bo'u venitchacham lemoshi'an shel Yisrael* — let us outwit the Savior of Israel. The *lo* refers not to the Israelites but to their divine protector. And the plan was calibrated precisely around the principle of *midah keneged midah*: Pharaoh knew that God avenges measure for measure, and so he sought a method of oppression that God could not repay in kind.

Should we destroy them by fire? But Scripture says "Behold, God comes with fire" — God will repay us with fire. By the sword? But "with His sword against all flesh" — repayment in kind. Therefore: *ela bo'u venidonam bamayim* — let us cast them into the water. For God had already sworn never to bring another flood upon the world, as it says, "for this is as the waters of Noah to me, for I have sworn that the waters of Noah shall not again pass over the earth."

### Pot Metaphor

Their miscalculation, the *Gemara* explains, was twofold. First, *she'al kol ha'olam kulo eino meivi* — God swore He would not bring a flood upon the entire world, but He said nothing about destroying one nation by water. Second — and more pointedly — *aval hem ba'im venoflim betocho* — when the Egyptians pursued Israel, they ran into the sea themselves. God did not drown them; they came and fell into it of their own accord, as it says *uMitzrayim nasim likrato* — "and the Egyptians were fleeing toward it." *Rabbi Elazar* crystallizes this: the verse says *ki vadavar asher zadu aleihem* — in the very matter in which they schemed against them. And the word *zadu*, the *Gemara* notes, is the language of cooking — as in *vayazed Yaakov nazid*, "and Jacob cooked a stew." *Bekdeira shebishlu bah nitbashlu* — in the very pot in which they cooked, they themselves were cooked.

One might ask whether this entire passage — attributing to Pharaoh an awareness of *midah keneged midah* and a calculated effort to circumvent it — is consistent with his statement elsewhere that he does not recognize God's authority at all. It is a genuine tension: a *Midrash* that quotes verses from Isaiah anachronistically and posits a Pharaoh who is theologically sophisticated enough to scheme against the God of Israel sits uneasily alongside the Pharaoh who says "I do not know the Lord." This is a place where *midrash* and *peshat* do not align — and where the *Midrash* transforms the entire dynamic of the narrative in a profound way, making Pharaoh from the very outset a figure who knows and fears God, rather than one who simply refuses to recognize His authority.

### Three Advisors

*Rabbi Simai* teaches: three were in that council who advised Pharaoh on how to deal with Israel — Balaam, Job, and Jethro. Balaam was the one who gave the advice; he is, after all, the figure who throughout the tradition counsels other nations on how to harm Israel — *ba v'iyaatzcha*, "come and I will advise you," and later it is Balaam's counsel that lies behind the incident of the daughters of Midian. For this, *neherag* — he was ultimately killed in the war against Midian.

Job was silent. He neither agreed nor protested — he lacked the courage to oppose Pharaoh. For this, *nidon b'yisurin* — he was afflicted with suffering. This is a striking passage, for it presents us with an answer to the question that the entire book of Job never explicitly resolves: why did Job suffer? Here the answer is that he was a silent enabler. There is a powerful parallel here to the famous *baraita* at the opening of *Masechet Avodah Zarah*, where the three sages before the Romans — one who praised Rome, one who was silent, and one who criticized — are treated not as morally equivalent but in terms of the degree to which their conduct constituted collaboration. Silence in the face of evil is itself a form of complicity.

Jethro fled. Whether he also protested before fleeing is implicit in his departure — his running away was itself a form of refusal, even if incomplete. For this, *zachu mibnei banav sheyeshvu b'lishkat hagazit* — his descendants merited to sit in the Chamber of Hewn Stone, the seat of the Sanhedrin. As the verse says, "the families of scribes who dwell in Yavetz" — *Tirathites, Shimeathites, Sucathites, these are the Kenites who came from Chamath, the father of the house of Rechab* — and the Kenites are identified as the descendants of Jethro, the father-in-law of Moses. They became the scribes and members of the Sanhedrin, serving in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *midah keneged midah* here is of counsel: because Jethro's response was ultimately the right one, his descendants came to be the very advisors of the nation of Israel.

## Treasure Cities

### Brick Ruse

The *Gemara* now turns to *vayasimu alav sarei misim* — "and they placed upon him taskmasters." The use of the singular *alav* rather than the plural *aleihem* is noted, and a *baraita* from the school of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Shimon* explains: *she'hevi'u malben vetalu lo l'Paroh betzavaro* — they brought a form of a brick and hung it around Pharaoh's own neck. The ruse was to announce a great communal building project and have Pharaoh himself participate on the first day, so that no Israelite could claim to be too refined for the work. And indeed: if any Israelite said *istinis ani* — "I am too delicate for this" — they were told: "Are you more refined than Pharaoh himself?" In this way, everyone was pressed into labor on that opening day.

The *Gemara* continues with another grammatical tension: *lema'an anoto besivlotam* — the verse mixes singular and plural. The resolution offered is that *lema'an anoto* refers to Pharaoh — they oppressed that one for that single day — while *besivlotam* refers to Israel — all of this was for the sake of impressing the Israelites into their burdens. It was, in other words, a one-day ruse that served as the instrument of a far larger enslavement.

### Pithom/Raamses

The *Gemara* addresses the word *miskenot* in *arei miskenot* — "treasure cities." *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree: one says *she'mesaknot et ba'aleihen* — these buildings endanger their builders (*Rashi* understands this as a general observation about construction projects, not limited to this specific case); the other says *she'memaskenot et ba'aleihen* — they impoverish their owners. As the tradition goes: *kol ha'osek bevinyan mitmasken* — anyone involved in building projects will come to poverty.

Regarding Pithom and Raamses themselves, again *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree. One says: Pithom was the city's actual name, and it was called Raamses descriptively — *she'rishon rishon mitroses* — because as soon as they would build it, each new course of construction would crumble and shatter. The other says: Raamses was the actual name, and the name Pithom came from *pi tehom bola'o* — the mouth of the abyss swallowed everything they built, since the ground was swampy and each construction sank immediately.

### Purposeless Work

This detail inspired a beautiful observation about the nature of *avodas parech*: beyond the physical brutality of the labor, what was most demoralizing was its utter purposelessness. Everything they built collapsed or sank — like Sisyphus, condemned to roll the stone up the hill only to watch it roll back down. The practice of making men do women's work and women do men's work — which the *Gemara* will shortly address — partakes of the same logic. *Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* citing *Rabbi Yonatan* explains *befarech* as *shehaya machlifim melachet anashim lenashim umelachet nashim la'anashim* — they would interchange the work of men and women and the work of women and men. *Rashi* notes: *vezu kashah shelo hayu regilim bekach* — this was especially harsh because they were completely unaccustomed to it. It was demoralizing on two levels: not only the backbreaking nature of the work but the stripping of dignity that comes with being forced to do that which is alien to your nature and training.

### Multiplying Promise

The verse *veka'asher ya'anu oto ken yirbeh veken yifrotz* — "as they afflict him, so shall he increase and so shall he spread" — uses the future tense where past tense (*rabu vefaretzu*, "they increased and they spread") would be expected. *Reish Lakish* explains: *ruach hakodesh mevasertan* — the divine spirit is announcing it. The oppressors say "afflict him," and God's voice comes back: "so shall he increase and so shall he spread." There is also a formulation that is even sharper: *atah amarta pen yirbeh, ani omer ken yirbeh* — you said "lest they multiply," I say "so they shall multiply." The echo of *pen yirbeh* and *ken yirbeh* makes the *midah keneged midah* almost structural: the exact same word, with the negative particle of fear replaced by the affirmative particle of divine promise. Conceptually, punishment operates like contraction, while blessing is expansive — which is precisely why the multiplier for *mida tovah* exceeds the multiplier for *midat puranut*.

## Righteous Women

### Women's Sustenance

*Rabbi Avira* expounds: *bisechar nashim tzidkaniyot shehayu beoto hador nigalu Yisrael miMitzrayim* — it was in the merit of the righteous women of that generation that Israel was ultimately redeemed from Egypt. The account is this: when the women would go to draw water, God would arrange that small fish would enter their pails alongside the water — *mesaknot lahem dagim ketanim bekadeihen* — so that their buckets came up half water and half fish. They would then set two pots on the fire, one to heat the water and one to cook the fish, and bring both out to their husbands in the field. There they would wash them, anoint them, feed them, and give them drink — and through this process restore their strength and their spirit until desire returned, and they would lie together *bein shefatayim* — between the folds.

One need not try to resolve every detail of this account with others. The broader theme here is important: it is the woman who is the agent of redemption, through acts of sustenance and intimacy — an echo of the *Yehudah* and *Tamar* story, where again it was the woman who was the initiator, again through an act of intimacy, and again it was ultimately connected to the chain that leads to *David Hamelech*. The drive that can lead to destruction when it crosses the boundaries of what is permitted — as with the *sotah* with whom this tractate began — can equally be the force that brings about the *geulah* when directed toward what is right.

The verse invoked is *im tishkevun bein shefatayim* — "if you lie down between the folds" — and through the merit of that lying down, *zachu Yisrael levizat Mitzrayim* — Israel merited the spoils of Egypt, as the verse continues: "the wings of a dove covered in silver and her feathers in a filigree of gold."

### Apple Trees

Once pregnant, the women would return home, and when the time of birth came, they would go out to give birth in the field — *tachat hatapuach*, under the apple tree — away from the eyes of the Egyptians. What exactly a *tapuach* is remains uncertain: apples are not indigenous to the Middle East, and various commentators have suggested the *etrog* or other fruits. Notably, the word "apple" in many languages originally carried the generic meaning of "fruit" — hence *tapuach adamah* for potato. The *Shir Hashirim* verse *tachat hatapuach orarticha* — "under the tapuach I aroused you" — is applied here to this context of birth.

God Himself, the *Gemara* continues, would send something from the heavenly realms to care for the newborns — cleansing them, preparing them, as a skilled midwife prepares a newborn infant, and providing them nourishment. The verse from *Yechezkel* is invoked: "on the day that you were born, your navel cord was not cut, and you were not washed with water" — the text of the *Haggadah*'s *derasha* connects here, where the entire metaphor of *Yechezkel* 16 about the founding of the nation is read literally as describing these miraculous births. God supplied them with *devash misela veshemen mechalmish tzur* — honey from the rock and oil from the flinty stone — as the verse in *Devarim* says: *vayenikehu devash misela* — He suckled them with honey from the rock.

### Ground & Grass

Once the Egyptians became aware that births were occurring, they came to kill the infants. At that moment, a miracle occurred: *venivla'in bakarka* — the babies were swallowed into the ground. The Egyptians then brought oxen and plowed over the places where the babies lay buried, but they were too deep and were protected. After the Egyptians left, *hayu mevatzbetzin veyotzin* — the babies would sprout out from the ground *ke'esev hasadeh*, like the grass of the field. The verse *revava ketzamach hasadeh nesatich* is cited — the very verse from the *Haggadah* that forms part of the *derasha* on that passage in *Yechezkel*: "I caused you to increase like the plant of the field." The *Gemara* is reading that verse with full literalness. The metaphor of the birthing of the nation and the literal birth of these children are collapsed into one.

When these children grew up, *ba'in adarim adarim levateihem* — they would come home in flocks, like flocks of animals from the field. The verse is read *al tikrei ba'adi adayim ela be'edrei adarim* — do not read "in jewels" but "in flocks." The image is striking: born in nature, swallowed by the earth, sprouting like grass, and returning home as flocks — they are almost animals of the field, untamed, raised by the earth itself.

### God Recognition

When God revealed Himself at the sea, *hem hikiruhu techila* — they were the first to recognize Him. And why? Because they had already encountered Him in the field when they were infants — it was through God's care that they had been bathed, nourished, and protected. And so at the sea they were able to cry out *zeh Keili ve'anveihu* — "this is my God and I will glorify Him." They recognized Him because they had known Him before.

## Midwives

### Shifra & Puah

*Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree about the identity of the two midwives. One says they were a mother and daughter (*isha uvita*), the other says a daughter-in-law and mother-in-law (*kala vechamotah*). The one who holds they were a mother and daughter identifies them as Yocheved and Miriam. The one who holds they were a daughter-in-law and mother-in-law identifies them as Yocheved and Elisheva, Aaron's wife.

A *baraita* sides with the mother-daughter view. *Shifra zu Yocheved* — Shifra was Yocheved. Why was she called Shifra? *Shemeshaperet et havelad* — she would beautify and care for the newborn; and additionally, *sheparu veravu Yisrael beyameha* — Israel was fruitful and multiplied in her days. *Puah zu Miriam* — Puah was Miriam. Why was she called Puah? *She'hayeta po'ah* — she would coo in order to help the child emerge, using soothing sounds to help the infant along. An alternate explanation: *she'hayeta po'ah beruach hakodesh* — she would speak with the divine spirit, saying: "My mother is destined to give birth to a son who will be the savior of Israel." And indeed, *moshi'an shel Yisrael* — the savior of Israel — is the very phrase we encountered earlier in Pharaoh's council, where the plan was to outwit *moshi'an shel Yisrael*. There, one might have taken it as referring to God; here, retrospectively, it becomes clear it refers to Moses.

*Tosafot* cites the explanation of *Rabbeinu Chananel* regarding *po'ah*: it refers to an incantation whispered to ease childbirth — *lo chashot lichisha* — and the practice of whispering such incantations to a woman in labor was common even in Tosafot's own time.

### Birth Signs

Pharaoh instructs the midwives: *beyaledchem et ha'ivriyot* — when you deliver the Hebrew women — and he specifies the *avnayim*, the birthing stones. *Rabbi Chanan* explains: Pharaoh gave them a sign by which to know that birth was imminent. When a woman crouches to give birth, *yarchoteiha mitztanenot ka'avanim* — her thighs become cold as stone — and that is when the midwives would need to be present to intercept the newborn.

An alternative explanation connects *avnayim* to the image of the potter's wheel: *va'ered beit hayotzer vehinei hu oseh melachah al ha'avnayim* — the potter works the stone, with one side here and one side there and the pedestal in the middle. Similarly, in childbirth: one thigh on this side, one thigh on the other, and the child coming out in the middle. The word *avnayim* thus becomes a metaphor for the birthing position.

### Refusal & Protection

*Rabbi Chanina* teaches that Pharaoh also gave the midwives a sign by which to distinguish boy from girl even before an examination: *ben panav lematah* — a boy comes out face down; *bat paneha lema'alah* — a girl face up. This connects to the *Gemara*'s broader understanding of conception: since the *Gemara* assumes the male is above and the female below during intercourse, the child is born in the position corresponding to its parent — a boy in the father's position, a girl in the mother's.

The verse says the midwives feared God and did not do as the king of Egypt had commanded them *aleihen*. The use of *aleihen* rather than the expected *lahen* prompts *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina* to suggest that Pharaoh had also tried to seduce them to sin sexually — *she'tav'an lidvar aveirah* — using *alav* in the sense of *lavo eleiha*, which the *Gemara* reads as a euphemism for sexual approach. They refused on that account as well.

### Women as Beasts

*Vatechayena et hayeladim* — "and they gave life to the children." A *baraita* teaches: not only did they not kill the children, they actively provided them with water and food to sustain them.

The midwives' excuse to Pharaoh — *ki lo ka'nashim hamitzriot ha'ivriyot ki chayot hena* — "for the Hebrew women are not like the Egyptian women, for they are *chayot*" — is read carefully by the *Gemara*. Does *chayot* mean midwives? The *Gemara* objects: *atu chaya mi lo tzaricha chaya achrita* — surely a midwife when she herself gives birth needs another midwife! A barber needs a barber to cut his own hair. So *chayot mamash* cannot be the meaning here.

Rather, *ela amru lo: uma zu kechaya nimshala* — they told him: this nation is compared to an animal. They give birth without help, as animals of the field do. And this is supported by the very tribal blessings of Jacob: Judah is a lion cub, Dan is a serpent, Naphtali a swift deer, Issachar a burdened donkey, Joseph a firstborn ox, Benjamin a wolf. For those tribes where the comparison is explicit in the text, it is written; and for those where it is not, the verse *mah imcha laviya bein arayot* — "your mother was a lioness among lions" — covers the rest. All of Israel, in some sense, is likened to animals.

This is, in fact, a deeply layered *peshat*. The women who gave birth under the apple trees in the field, whose infants were swallowed into the earth and sprouted like grass, who came home in flocks — they are, in the most literal sense of the *Gemara*'s narrative, being born as creatures of the natural world, beyond the reach of Pharaoh's machinery. But there is another dimension as well. Part of the process of enslaving a people is dehumanizing them — reducing them in the eyes of the oppressor to something less than human. Pharaoh could believe the midwives' claim precisely because he had already begun that process of dehumanization. Yet the *Gemara* turns that very trope on its head: yes, they are like animals — and that is their glory, their resilience, their source of redemption.
