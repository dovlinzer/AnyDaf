# Pesachim 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pairs & Mixed Drinks

### Pairs & Combining

We pick up at the very bottom of 110b, continuing our discussion of *zugot* — pairs — and how consuming drinks in even numbers exposes one to *mazikin* or to witchcraft if such practices are occurring nearby. The Gemara has been exploring when one may combine different drinks to arrive at an odd number and thereby exit the danger of *zugot*.

The Gemara states: *dechulei alma trei attaka ve-chad levatar attaka lo mitztarfei* — everyone agrees that two cups consumed during a meal and one cup consumed after the meal do not combine. One cannot add the post-meal cup to the during-meal cups in order to reach three and thereby escape the problem of pairs. This is illustrated by the story of Rava bar Nachmani, found in Bava Metzia, who drank during a meal and then, when the table was removed, wished to drink again to exit a pair. He insisted that they bring the table back — demonstrating that a cup consumed after the meal would not combine with those consumed during it.

Moving further, the Gemara cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *kol hamazug mitztaref* — all types of mixed drinks combine with one another. Since wine was habitually diluted with water in antiquity, and various beverages were similarly mixed, any *mazug* — mixed drink — belongs to a sufficiently unified category that a combination of, say, two of one type and one of another can resolve a pair. The exception is *chutz min hamayim* — water is excluded. One might ask: is water not itself a mixed drink? As we will see immediately, the Gemara does envision cases where people mix hot water with cold, presumably to moderate the temperature. Yet since the result remains entirely water, it does not constitute a *mazug* in the relevant sense — perhaps better rendered "a watered drink."

### Water as Mixed Drink

Rabbi Yochanan, who is from Eretz Yisrael — where the Gemara has indicated that these concerns with *zugot* are not accepted — nonetheless enters this discussion, at least partially. He rules: *afilu mayim* — even water qualifies as a mixed drink and can combine to help exit a problematic pair.

### Water Temperature Rules

Rav Pappa qualifies Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: *lo amran ela chamimi lego kriri ve-kriri lego chamimi* — we only said this when hot water is mixed into cold, or cold into hot, creating a meaningful blending of different qualities. *Aval chamimi lego chamimi* — if hot water is added to hot, or cold to cold — *lo*. In such a case, insufficient difference exists between the two waters to constitute a genuine mixture, and therefore they would not combine with other *mazug* drinks toward resolving a pair.

## Four Dangerous Acts

### Reish Lakish's Teaching

Reish Lakish teaches: *arba'a devarim ha'oseh otan damo berosho u-mitchayev benafsho* — four things, if one does them, his blood is on his own head and he bears mortal responsibility for the danger he incurs. The doubled formulation — *damo berosho* and *mitchayev benafsho* — is striking, as both phrases seem to express the same point.

These four are: *ha-nifneh bein dekel le-kotel* — one who relieves himself between a palm tree and a wall; *ve-over bein shnei dekalim* — one who passes between two palm trees; *ve-shoteh mayim she'ulim* — one who drinks borrowed water; and *ve-over al mayim shfuchin* — one who walks over spilled water. Even, the Gemara adds, *v'afilu shafchata ishto befanav* — even if his own wife spilled it in front of him, so that its provenance is entirely known, once water has been spilled it becomes a potential gathering point for *shedim*.

The underlying logic: a work on Jewish magic and superstition quoted in the name of Rashi describes *shedim* as a subset of *mazikin* who possess human forms and eat and drink like men. They therefore need water. Borrowed water is somehow more accessible to their powers, or more vulnerable to their influence, and one who drinks it risks competing with a *shed* for that very water. Spilled water similarly attracts *shedim* to its location.

### Palm Trees & Space

The Gemara now unpacks each of the four dangers in turn.

Regarding relieving oneself between a palm tree and a wall: *lo amran ela d'leit lei arba amot* — this danger applies only when the space does not afford four *amot*, meaning there is insufficient room for the *shed* to pass through without encountering the person. *Aval it lei arba amot* — if four *amot* are available, the *shed* can navigate around the person and there is no forced confrontation. The situation resembles, in a sense, the principle that if one does not provoke, one need not fear being provoked in return.

Furthermore, even where there are not four *amot*, the Gemara adds a further qualification: *lo amran ela d'leika darchah achrinah* — the danger applies only if there is no alternative path available to the *shed*. If another route exists, the *shed* will presumably take it, and there is no problem.

### Public Thoroughfares

Regarding passing between two palm trees: *lo amran ela d'la paskin'hu reshut harabim* — this is only dangerous when a public thoroughfare has not passed through the area. *Shedim* avoid the masses. *Aval paskin'hu reshut harabim* — if a public road passes between the trees, *leit lan bah* — there is no concern. The *shedim* prefer darker, more secluded areas, and where there is steady public traffic they will not linger.

### Borrowed Water Limits

Regarding borrowed water: *lo amran ela d'shailinhu katan* — the danger applies only if a minor did the borrowing, as a minor may not have been able to properly watch over the water, rendering it somehow more susceptible to demonic access. *Aval gadol leit lan bah* — if an adult borrowed the water, there is no concern.

Even if a minor borrowed the water, there is a further qualification: *ela basadeh* — the concern applies only in a field, where water is scarce. *D'la shechichi* — since water is not readily available, one risks actual competition with the *shed* for that water. *Aval ba'ir* — in a city, where wells are plentiful and water is accessible, *leit lan bah* — the *shed* will simply obtain water elsewhere.

And even in a field, *ela maya* — the concern applies only to water. *Aval chamra ve-shichra leit lan bah* — wine and beer are no concern, since the *shed* is evidently health-conscious and drinks only water. It should be noted that consuming multiple cups of wine creates its own danger through *zugot*, but that danger arises from the pairs themselves, not from competition with a *shed*.

### Spilled Water Rules

Regarding walking over spilled water: the Gemara specifies that the danger applies *ela d'la afsikinhu be'afra* — only when no dirt has been placed on the water to interrupt it — *ve-la taf behu ruka* — and no one has spit into it. The spilled water under discussion is primarily soiled water — what one might call household wastewater or sewage, since in antiquity one would pour out dirty water used for washing or other purposes. Something about the nature or perhaps the odor of such water attracts *shedim*. But if dirt is added, or if someone spits in it, the nature of the liquid is sufficiently altered that it is no longer an attractive gathering point.

*Aval hifsiknu o taf bo rok* — if either interruption occurs, *leit lan bah*, no problem. And further: *velo amran ela dela avar aleihu shimsha* — the concern applies only when the sun has not yet passed over the water, and *velo avar aleihu shittin nigrei* — when sixty footsteps have not yet crossed it. Once the sun has shone upon it, or sixty people have walked over it, the danger dissipates.

There is a final amelioration: *ela dela rachiv chamra velo sayim masanei* — the concern applies only if one is neither riding a donkey nor wearing shoes. If one has either protection — either riding or wearing shoes — *leit lan bah*, there is no danger.

### Witchcraft Exception

*Ve-hani milei* — all of these qualifications and leniencies apply *heicha deleika lemichash lechshafim* — in a place where there is no reason to worry about witchcraft being directed against a person. The protections address the *mazikin* acting in their ordinary, autonomous movements through the world. *Aval heicha de-ika lemichash le-chshafim* — but in a place where someone is actively directing witchcraft against a person, *af al gav de-ika kol hanei chaishinan* — even if one has taken all of the precautions described, one must still be concerned.

This is a striking assumption in the Gemara: that the same conditions which expose a person to *shedim* also render him susceptible to the effects of witchcraft. One might wonder whether the underlying conception is that witchcraft operates *through* the *shedim* — drawing on those same forces — such that vulnerability to one implies vulnerability to the other. Once witchcraft is actively being practiced against someone, however, the protections against the *shedim's* ordinary movements provide no defense.

The Gemara illustrates this with a story: a certain man was riding his donkey and wearing shoes — both protective conditions — yet his shoes shriveled and his leg withered. The only explanation the Gemara offers is that someone was directing witchcraft against him, and against that no physical precaution suffices.

One student raised a broader observation: the world of the Talmud is a deeply animated one, full of non-physical forces moving through physical space, such that even the simplest acts — walking between trees, stepping over a puddle — carry consequences because the world is populated by beings with whom one may come into conflict. This contrasts sharply with a modern materialist sensibility in which randomness stands at the center of unexplained events. The *Gemara's* world, by contrast, is profoundly personalized: misfortune has a cause, and that cause is often an encounter with a being that one provoked, however inadvertently.

## Dogs, Trees & Women

### Three Prohibited

A *brayta* is cited: *shlosha ain mimetzin velo mitmetzin* — there are three entities that one must not allow to become an intermediary between two people, and between two of which one must not place oneself. These are *hakelev veha-dekel veha-isha* — a dog, a palm tree, and a woman. Some add *af hachazir* — even a pig — and some add *af hanachash* — even a snake. The concern, as the subsequent discussion makes clear, relates to the demonic or witchcraft-related dangers associated with these entities.

### El/Lo Remedy

*Ve-im mimetzin* — if one of these did become an intermediary, what is the remedy? Rav Pappa teaches: *niftach b'El ve-nisayem b'El* — one should recite a *pasuk* that begins with the divine name *El* and ends with *El*. Alternatively: *niftach belo ve-nisayem belo* — one should begin with *lo* and end with *lo*.

Rashi identifies the *pasuk* for *El*: *El motziam mimitzrayim kito'afot re'eim lo, ki lo nachash beya'akov velo kessem beyisrael, ka'et ye'ameir leya'akov uleyisrael mah pa'al El* — a verse that opens with *El* and closes with *El*. This passage is particularly apt: it invokes *ki lo nachash beya'akov velo kessem beyisrael* — that witchcraft has no power over Israel — making it directly relevant to the danger at hand. Moreover, there is a structural resonance: just as one has been caught in the middle of something — sandwiched between entities — the remedy involves being sandwiched within a verse, with *El* on both sides.

For *lo*, Rashi cites: *lo ish El veyichazev uven adam veyitnecham, hahu amar velo ya'aseh vedibeir velo yekimena* — a verse that opens and closes with *lo*, though the final *lo* is not the very last word. Again, the content is apposite.

### Torah as Incantation

Tosafot raises a significant concern: the Rashbam's interpretation requires reciting *pesukim* as a protective incantation, yet it is generally prohibited to use Torah as a vehicle for healing or protection in this incantatory way — one ought to pray to God, not wield *pesukim* as magical instruments.

Tosafot offers two answers. First: *lehagen shari* — for protection, as opposed to healing an existing ailment, it is permitted, since the danger has not yet materialized and one is acting preventatively. The precise distinction between a preventative recitation and an incantatory one is not entirely transparent, but this is the answer given. Second, Tosafot cites another Gemara in Shabbos that permits reciting a *pasuk* when treating a dangerous fever, and reasons: *mishum sakana shari* — in a life-threatening situation, it is permitted; it is only for non-life-threatening illness that one may not use Torah as incantation.

This discussion touches on a deep tension in the world the Gemara describes. In a world saturated with demonic forces and witchcraft, the demand for protection is immediate and pressing. The mechanisms the Gemara offers — amulets, incantations, formulaic recitations — sit uneasily with a religious sensibility that insists the Torah is not a magic text, and that one's relationship with God is mediated through prayer, *mitzvos*, and *maasim tovim*, not through the deployment of sacred words as protective spells.

### Rabbeinu Chananel

To avoid this difficulty entirely, Rabbeinu Chananel offers a different interpretation: one does not recite *pesukim* at all, but rather composes a *tefillah* — a spontaneous prayer — that opens with *El* and concludes with *El*, or opens with *lo* and concludes with *lo*.

He provides examples. For *El*: *El Elohim Hashem hatzileni mikol ra mikol nezek asher beyadcha koach u-gevurah ki atah El* — God, the Lord, save me from all evil and all harm, for in Your hand is all power and might, for You are *El*. For *lo*: *lo titecheni Elohai ve-lo ta'azveni shamreni k'ishon bat ayin terem ekra ta'aneni ve'al tomar lo* — do not abandon me, my God, do not forsake me; guard me as the apple of the eye; before I call, answer me, and do not say no. In Rabbeinu Chananel's reading, then, the remedy is genuine *tefillah* — turning to God for protection — rather than an incantatory use of scriptural verses.

It should be noted that Tosafot's discussion preserves both the *Rashbam's* approach using *pesukim* and Rabbeinu Chananel's approach using *tefillah*, presenting them as alternative possibilities.

### Women as Niddah

The Gemara continues with a further specification: *hani bei tere de-matzu'ah lehu isha niddah* — if a *niddah* passes between two people. *Im techilas niddasah hi* — if she is at the beginning of her menstrual flow, *horegas achas mehen* — it will cause the death of one of them. *Ve-im sof niddasah hi* — if she is at the end of her period, *meriva osa beineihen* — it will cause a quarrel between them. The remedy is again to recite a verse or prayer beginning and ending with *El*.

### Niddah Discussion

This passage opens onto a broader historical concern. In many ancient societies there existed powerful taboos surrounding menstruating women — they were required to live apart from the rest of the community, forbidden to touch food lest they contaminate it, and treated as dangerous in ways that had nothing to do with the laws of *tumah*. In some communities these practices persist into the present day, with devastating consequences for women's welfare and safety.

Notably, the Torah itself does not reflect this attitude at all. *Niddah* in the Torah is treated as a category of *tumah* and a matter of *issur arayos*, nothing more. The Gemara in Masechet Niddah equally ignores these taboos in its *halakhic* analysis, ruling, for instance, that a husband and wife may sleep in the same bedroom during the *niddah* period — rejecting any notion of casting the woman out, and equally setting aside the concern about *yichud* that would normally apply when a man is secluded with a woman with whom relations are forbidden.

The passage before us, however, is not a *halakhic* discussion. It reflects a popular perception — present in the surrounding culture and apparently not entirely foreign to some strands of rabbinic thought — that a *niddah* carried with her some dangerous power or spiritual contamination beyond the domain of *halakhah*. Tosafot mitigates this somewhat by suggesting: *yesh mefareish im oseh lahen kishuf* — perhaps the concern is specifically about a *niddah* who is also actively practicing witchcraft, such that it is the combination of the two that creates the danger, not the *niddah* status by itself. That is somewhat ameliorating, though if women are in any case routinely assumed to be potential practitioners of witchcraft, the improvement is limited.

It is significant that this perception did find its way into practical life across the medieval period. Works such as the *Baraita de-Masechet Niddah* — a text quoted by *rishonim* including the Ramban, though its authorship is disputed and some regard it as Karaitic — are filled with taboos about menstruating women touching food, reciting *blessings*, or entering the *beit keneset*. In Ashkenaz in particular there developed a concern about whether a *niddah* could attend synagogue even on Yom Kippur, or engage in any form of sacred activity. The Rambam, for his part, was especially concerned about another adjacent phenomenon: the writing of angelic names on *mezuzot*, transforming a *mitzvah* into an amulet. The tendency to treat sacred objects and texts as sources of magical protection rather than as expressions of a rational *halakhic* relationship with God is a recurrent tension — one that the *Gemara's* own material here seems, despite itself, to fuel.

## Women Witchcraft

### Crossroads Women

The Gemara now addresses a related scenario: *hanei trei nashei yatvan b'parashat drachim ve-chada b'hai gisa dishvila ve-chada b'idach gisa umichavnan apaihu hadadi* — two women sitting at a crossroads, one on each side of the road, facing one another. *Vadai b'kshafim askinan* — this positioning is taken as certain evidence that they are engaged in witchcraft.

### Witchcraft Remedies

If one encounters this situation, *ieka darcha achrina leizil bah* — the first and best advice is to take an alternate route. If no other route is available and one has a companion, the two should grasp each other's hands and pass through together. If one is alone, *neima hachi* — one should recite the following: *Igeret azlat asya blusya mitkatla b'cheik tevel*. The first words — *Igeret, azlat, asya, blusya* — appear to be names of demonic entities; the concluding phrase means something like "may murderous arrows be driven into you." The Rashbam understands this as a curse directed at the *shedim* associated with the witchcraft, and indeed the verse concludes with an invocation of destruction against them: *k'tzar harugin heim b'chitzim* — they are already as if slain by arrows.

### Mikvah Encounter

A further scenario: *hai man d'paga b'it-teta b'idna d'salka mitvilas mitzvah* — a person who happens to encounter a woman at the very moment she is emerging from the *mikvah*, having immersed for the *mitzvah* of becoming permissible to her husband. If he then goes home and has relations before she does — *achda lei l'didei ruach znunim* — a spirit of licentiousness takes hold of him. If she has relations before he does — *achda lah l'didah ruach znunim* — it takes hold of her. The remedy in either case is to recite: *shfoch buz al nedivim vayashgem betohu lo darech* — "Pour contempt upon princes and make them wander in pathless wastelands," the apparent intent being that God should drive away the demonic spirit.

### Rav Yochanan Story

Tosafot, printed on the preceding page at 107a, raises an obvious difficulty from a well-known Gemara: Rabbi Yochanan would intentionally sit at the gates of the *mikvah*, so that women emerging from immersion would see him. The reasoning was that Rabbi Yochanan was renowned for his extraordinary beauty, and the Gemara operates on the assumption that what a man or woman contemplates at the moment of conception influences the appearance of the child. Rabbi Yochanan believed that if women looked at him as they left the *mikvah* and then held his image in mind when they later had relations with their husbands, their children would be beautiful and well-formed.

But this creates a tension: the Gemara before us suggests that encountering a woman as she emerges from the *mikvah* leads to a *ruach znunim* seizing one party or the other. Why was Rabbi Yochanan unconcerned?

Tosafot offers several resolutions. The first: *hacha depaga beshata desalka min hamayim* — our concern applies at the precise moment of emerging from the water, while Rabbi Yochanan was waiting at the *gates*, encountering women a few minutes later, already dressed and composed. The second: *hatam yasiv hacha paga* — Rabbi Yochanan was *sitting*, a settled, deliberate posture, while the danger described in the Gemara involves an inadvertent *bumping into* (*paga*), a sudden, unplanned encounter. The third resolution: *Rav Yochanan hava amar shfoch buz al nedivim* — Rabbi Yochanan himself would recite the protective verse, thereby neutralizing the danger. Of course, this third answer raises its own question — what of the woman, who might have relations before Rabbi Yochanan does? — but Tosafot does not address that directly.

## Dangerous Shades

### Shadow Dangers

Rav Yitzchak opens this section with an exegetical observation: what is the meaning of the verse *gam ki elech be-gei tzalmavet lo ira ra ki ata imadi* — "Even as I walk through the valley of the shadow of death I shall fear no evil, for You are with me"? What is this *gei tzalmavet*, this valley of the shadow of death, that represents such acute danger? The Gemara identifies it: *ze hayashen betzel dekel yechidi* — this is one who sleeps in the shade of a solitary palm tree. The linguistic connection is pointed: *betzel*, shade, resonates with *tzalmavet*, shadow of death. And note that the *shedim* are associated not merely with the trees themselves but specifically with their *shade* — making vulnerable any place where shade is cast.

Two specific shade-dangers are enumerated: the shade of a solitary palm tree, and *u-vetzel levana* — the shade of the moon, meaning the shadowed area created when moonlight is blocked by a wall or other obstacle.

Regarding the shade of a solitary palm tree: *lo amran ela dela nafal tula de-chavreih alaveih* — the danger applies only when the shade of another tree does not overlap with it. If the shade of a neighboring tree falls upon it as well, *leit lan bah* — there is no problem.

A *brayta* then specifies that the shade of a solitary palm tree in a *chatzer* — a courtyard — is dangerous, as is sleeping in moon shade. The Gemara works through this *brayta* analytically. If the concern is that no other shade overlaps, then even in an open field it would be dangerous — so why does the *brayta* specify a *chatzer*? The Gemara concludes: *shma mina be-chatzer af al gav de-nafal tula de-chavreih alaveih* — in a courtyard, the danger applies even when the shade of another tree overlaps, because the enclosed nature of the courtyard intensifies the danger. The *brayta* is thus teaching a stringency specific to the *chatzer*, not a limitation to it.

This raises a broader reflection: the Gemara's use of standard *halakhic* analytical language — *lo amran ela*, *shma mina*, the resolution of apparent contradictions between a *sugya* and a *brayta* — to analyze the laws of demonic shade is notable. One might wonder whether this reflects the Gemara's equal weighting of this material alongside *halakhic* discussions, or whether the analytical idiom is borrowed and applied somewhat mechanically. It is genuinely difficult to determine the tone of this material at this distance.

### Moon Shade Danger

Regarding moon shade: *u-v'tzila shel levana lo amran ela b'ma'arava* — the danger of moonlight shade applies only when the shade is cast to the west. The Rashbam explains that toward the end of the night the moon is in the east, and a wall facing east would therefore cast its shadow westward. Shade cast to the east — *aval b'madnacha leit lan bah* — presents no concern. This suggests that only a particular configuration of moonlight and shadow at a specific time of night activates the danger.

### Palm Tree Spirits

Continuing with the palm tree hazards: *hai man d'mafni agidda d'dikla* — one who relieves himself on the stump of a palm tree is seized by *ruach palga*, which may be the name of a specific demon or may refer to a disease — the Rashbam suggests a form of paralysis. One who leans his head against the trunk of a palm tree — *hai man d'matzli reisha agidda d'dikla* — is seized by the demon *tzrada*, which corresponds to a type of migraine headache, perhaps because the head is brought into direct contact with the tree.

### Stepping Over Trees

One who steps over a felled palm tree faces yet further danger: *im mikta katlo* — if the tree was cut down deliberately, he will be killed; *ee yakar u-mayas* — if it simply fell naturally, he will die. However, these dangers apply only if *dilo manach karei ilavei* — if he did not pause to rest his leg upon the trunk as he crossed. *Aval manach karei ilavei leit lan bah* — if he rests his leg on the tree while stepping over, there is no problem. The precise logic underlying this distinction is not explained, but it is presented as a matter of practical guidance.

### Five Shades Rule

The Gemara enumerates five dangerous shades: *tula d'dikla yechida* — the shade of a solitary palm tree; *tula d'kanda* — the shade of a *kanda* tree; *tula d'parcha* — the shade of a *parcha*, identified as a caper bush; *tula d'zarjeta* — the shade of a sorb tree; and, according to some, *af tula d'arva* — the shade of a boat, and *tula d'arveta* — the shade of a willow. (Some versions emend the opening number to four rather than five in light of the count.)

The Gemara then states a general principle: *kol dinapish anfei kashi tulei* — any tree with many branches has dangerous shade; *ve-kol d'kashi silvayei kashi tulei* — and any tree whose wood is very hard, or which has thorns, likewise has dangerous shade. The underlying intuition seems to be that the density of the canopy or the hardness of the wood creates conditions that *shedim* find congenial.

### Karumashe Exception

The *karumashe* tree — identified in some translations as a service tree — is explicitly excluded: *af al gav d'kashi silvayei lo kashi tulei* — even though its wood is hard, its shade is not dangerous.

The reason offered takes the form of a demonic folk narrative: *d'amrah lei sheida livrah* — a *sheida* said to her daughter, *pirchi nafshach mikrumashe* — fly away from the *karumashe* tree, avoid its shade — *d'ihu d'katol la'avuch* — for that tree is what killed your father. *Ve-katol l'didei* — and it went on to kill him too, apparently the son as well, despite his mother's warning. Whatever myth underlies this, the practical result is that *shedim* actively avoid the *karumashe* tree, and therefore its shade poses no danger.

Rav Ashi states: *chazivna l'Rav Kahana d'parish mikulu tulei* — I observed that Rav Kahana avoided all shades, as a matter of general precaution.

### Parcha Spirit Story

The Gemara now identifies the specific *shedim* associated with various locations. The spirit of the *parcha* tree is called *ruchi*. The spirit of the sorb tree is called *sheida*. The spirit that inhabits rooftops is called *rishpi* — an echo of *rishpei esh*, fiery bolts.

*Le-mai nafka mina* — why does it matter to know their names? *Likmiya* — for the writing of amulets. When composing a *kmiya* to protect someone from a specific demonic encounter, one must know the name of the *shed* to whom the amulet is directed.

Regarding the spirit of the *parcha* tree: *birya she-ein la einayim* — it is a creature without eyes. Again, *le-mai nafka mina* — of what practical consequence is this? *Ligzuzei lah* — so that one knows to simply run away from it, since without eyes it cannot follow.

This is illustrated by a story: a Torah student once went to relieve himself near a *parcha* tree and entered its shade. *Shama d'ka ati laveih* — he heard something coming toward him, the rustling of the approaching spirit. *Ve-gazilei* — and he ran, and was safe, since the eyeless *ruchi* could not pursue him. As he ran, he grabbed hold of a palm tree — itself associated with *shedim*, as we have seen — *tzavach dikla upaka hi* — and the palm tree cried out, and something burst. Whether it was the tree, the shade, or the demon is not entirely clear from the text.

### Sixty Demons Story

*Hazira d'tasamicha l'mata lo pachas mi-shishin sheedi* — a sorb tree located near a city harbors no fewer than sixty demons. *Le-mai nafka mina likmiya* — the practical implication is again for amulet-writing: one must know that the amulet must be written against sixty *shedim*, not just one.

The Gemara tells a story that brings this point to life. A certain officer — some leading figure in the city — would rest under a sorb tree near the city. *Alu bei shishin sheedi veistaken* — sixty *shedim* possessed him, and his life was in danger. He went to a certain rabbinic student for help, but this student did not know that a sorb tree near a city harbors sixty demons. *Kesav la kmiya l'chad sheeda* — he wrote a *kmiya* effective against only one *shed*. The student subsequently heard that the demons had hung a harp from the tree and were celebrating, singing mockingly: *sudreih d'mar ki tzurva m'rabanan badikna bei d'mar d'lo yada baruch ata* — that man's head-cloth makes him look like a Torah scholar, but we tested him and he doesn't even know a *bracha*. They were deriding him for not knowing that there were sixty of them — *d'lo yada baruch*, he doesn't know the first thing.

Then a second, properly informed rabbinic student arrived. *Kesav la kmiya d'shishin sheedi* — he wrote a *kmiya* directed against sixty *shedim*. *Panu m'naichu me-hacha* — and the demons were heard to say: clear out your belongings from here. They were expelled.

This story both reflects and reinforces the widespread practice of *kamea*-writing, which remained common through the medieval period and continues in some communities to the present day. The implicit lesson is that the practice requires genuine expertise: an amulet composed without full knowledge of what one is dealing with is not merely ineffective but almost farcical.

## Ketev Pestilences

### Ketev Types

The Gemara now turns to *Ketev Meriri*, a demonic pestilence referenced in the Torah and here associated by the Gemara with a specific class of dangerous spirit. *Trei katvei havei* — there are two forms of *Ketev*: *chad mikamei tihara* — one that operates before noon, and *ve-chad mibatar tihara* — one that operates after noon.

The one that operates before noon is called *Ketev Meriri*. *Umachzi b'kada d'kamcha* — it appears within a jug of *kamcha*, a type of sauce or compound, and looks like a stirring or mixing implement moving within it — perhaps describing a whirling, turbulent appearance.

The one that operates after noon is called *Ketev Yashud Tzahorayim* — echoing the *tzahorayim*, the afternoon or noon hour, mentioned in Tehillim. *Umachzi b'karna d'iza* — it appears among the horns of goats, *ve-hadar bei knafaya* — and wings or feathers surround it. Some translate this as a whirling sieve-like appearance; others emphasize the surrounding wings.

### Abaye Encounter

Abaye was once traveling with Rav Pappa on his right and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua on his left. *Chazya l'hahu Ketev Meriri d'ka ati l'apei* — he saw the *Ketev Meriri* coming toward him, specifically approaching from his left side. He responded by repositioning his companions: he moved Rav Pappa to the left and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua to the right.

Rav Pappa protested: *ana mai shna d'lo chashasht li* — why are you not concerned for my safety? Why am I being moved into the path of the *Ketev*? Abaye responded: *at sha'ata kayima lach* — it is a good time for you, meaning your *mazel* is currently strong. Since Rav Pappa was very wealthy and evidently blessed with considerable *mazel*, Abaye calculated that this protection would extend to the demonic encounter as well.

### Ketev Seasons

*Michad b'Tammuz ad shitser beh* — from the first of Tammuz through the sixteenth, just before the seventeenth of Tammuz, *vadai sh'chichi* — the *Ketev* powers are definitely prevalent. From that point onward — *safek sh'chichi safek lo sh'chichi* — there is uncertainty as to their prevalence. It is worth noting that this temporal proximity to the Three Weeks has generated discussion about whether some of the customs and stringencies surrounding that period were influenced by these beliefs about heightened demonic danger during this stretch of the summer calendar.

### Ketev Habitat

*U'mishtakchei b'tulei d'chatzva d'lo chatzvei garmida* — they are found in the shade of a *chatzva*, a type of low-growing grass that does not reach a *amah* in height. *U'btulei d'tzafra v'panya d'lo havei garmida* — and in the short morning and afternoon shadows that do not extend a *amah* in length. *Ve-hikar b'tulei d'veit hakisei* — but their primary dwelling place, where they are most to be found, is in the shade of the bathroom.

That, the Gemara suggests, is a fitting place to stop.
