# Avodah Zarah 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Breaking Idol Debate

### Self-Broken Idols

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated:** With regard to objects of **idol worship that broke by themselves, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **prohibited** to derive benefit from them. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** It is **permitted.**

The daf opens by returning to a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish that was introduced the previous day: what is the status of an idol that breaks on its own, without any human intervention? Rabbi Yochanan holds that such an idol remains *assur*. His reasoning is that *bittul* of an idol requires an affirmative act — a non-Jew must deliberately break it, thereby expressing that the idol no longer holds significance for him. Spontaneous breakage, in his view, accomplishes nothing of the sort. Reish Lakish disagrees and holds the idol is *mutar*. His reasoning is that a non-Jew who sees his idol collapse and break of its own accord will reason: if the idol cannot even protect itself, it can hardly protect me. In his mind, therefore, he *mevatel*s it — he dismisses it as no longer possessing any power.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, דְּהָא לָא בָּטְלָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת, מִסְּתָמָא בַּטּוֹלֵי מְבַטֵּיל לַהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: אִיהִי נַפְשַׁהּ לָא אַצְּלָה, לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא מַצְּלָה לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the sides of the dispute. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is **prohibited, as** its owner **did not revoke** its status as an object of idol worship. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that it is **permitted,** as the owner **presumably revoked** its status as an object of idol worship, **having said** to himself: If the idol **could not save** even **itself** from harm, **can it save that man,** i.e., myself?

The Gemara proceeds to present a series of challenges — each introduced by the formula *eitivei* — in which Rabbi Yochanan attempts to demonstrate that Reish Lakish's position leads to untenable conclusions. In each case, Reish Lakish (or the Gemara in his name) offers a response, and the responses themselves follow a consistent pattern. That pattern is worth tracking carefully, as it recurs throughout the *amud*.

### Pieces on Pedestals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מָצָא תַּבְנִית יָד, תַּבְנִית רֶגֶל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן נֶעֱבָד. אַמַּאי? הָא שְׁבָרִים נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute with regard to an idol that broke. Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised an objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from the mishna: If one **found** an object in **the figure of a hand** or in **the figure of a foot, these are forbidden, as** objects **similar to those are worshipped.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asks: **Why** are they forbidden? **Aren't they fragments,** which according to Reish Lakish should be permitted?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תַּרְגְּמַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּעוֹמְדִין עַל בְּסִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Didn't Shmuel interpret** the mishna as referring to a case **where** these objects **are standing on their pedestals,** which shows that they were designed this way initially?

The first challenge draws on a ruling we have already encountered: if one finds a *tavnit yad* or *tavnit regel* — a form of just a hand or just a foot — these are *assur*, because they are *keyotzei bahen na'avad*, similar to something that has been worshipped. Rabbi Yochanan presses Reish Lakish: why should these be *assur*? They are, after all, *shevarim* — broken pieces. If a self-broken idol is *mutar* according to Reish Lakish, then broken-off pieces of a statue should similarly be *mutar*.

The Gemara answers by invoking Shmuel's interpretation: *b'omdin al basisam* — these are pieces that are standing on their own pedestals. In other words, the hand or foot is not a fragment broken off from a larger statue; it is, from the outset, a complete and independent idol in the form of a hand or foot. Such objects were evidently worshipped in their own right. The *shevarim* premise of the challenge simply does not apply here, because nothing has broken — the object is whole as it was always meant to be.

### Bending the Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: גּוֹי מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל גּוֹי. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a mishna (52b): **A gentile can revoke** the idolatrous status of **his** own object of **idol worship or that of another** gentile, **but a Jew cannot revoke** the status of **a gentile's** object of **idol worship.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asks: **Why** can't a Jew revoke the status of a gentile's idol according to Reish Lakish? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own,** which Reish Lakish deems permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁפְּחָסָהּ. וְכִי פְּחָסָהּ מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָא תְּנַן: פְּחָסָהּ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא חִסְּרָהּ — בִּטְּלָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** That mishna is referring to a case **where** the Jew **bent** the idol out of shape without actually breaking it. The Gemara asks: **And if he** merely **bent** the idol out of shape, **what of it? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (53a) that if one **bent** an idol, changing its shape, **even if he did not remove** any **part of it,** he thereby **revoked** its status as an object of idol worship?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּפַחֲסַהּ גּוֹי, אֲבָל פַּחֲסַהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא בַּטְּלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This statement** applies only in a case **where a gentile bent** the idol, changing its shape; **but** in a case **where a Jew bent it,** changing its shape, its status as an object of idol worship **is not revoked.**

Rabbi Yochanan tries again. The Gemara rules that a non-Jew can perform *bittul* on his own idol or on another non-Jew's idol, but a Jew cannot perform *bittul* on a non-Jew's idol. Rabbi Yochanan presses: according to Reish Lakish, why should a Jew's act of *bittul* fail? If a self-broken idol is *mutar*, then surely an idol that a Jew actively breaks should be at least as *mutar* — it should be *tehavi k'avodat kochavim shenishbeira me'aleha*.

Abaye clarifies that the case under discussion is not one of breaking the idol but of *pichasa* — a Jew who bends the idol out of shape, or in Rashi's formulation, takes a hammer to it and flattens it. This is a distinct act. The Gemara then asks: but what difference should that make? We even have an explicit *Mishnah* that says *pichasa af al pi shelo chasra batla* — bending the idol, even if nothing is actually removed, constitutes successful *bittul*. So why doesn't it work when a Jew does it?

Reish Lakish's first answer is that the *Mishnah* speaks of when a non-Jew performs the bending: *hani mili depichasa oved kochavim*, but *aval pichasa Yisrael lo batla*. Rashi explains that a non-Jew observing the bending would reason that the idol could not save itself — the same logic Reish Lakish employs for spontaneous breakage. But when a Jew comes and bends it, the non-Jew's reaction is simply: someone came and smashed my idol. There is no theological inference drawn. The distinction is psychologically coherent: spontaneous damage suggests divine disfavor or weakness; deliberate human damage does not.

### Rabba's Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּי פַחֲסַהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי בַּטְּלַהּ, אֶלָּא גְּזֵרָה דִּלְמָא מַגְבַּהּ לַהּ וַהֲדַר מְבַטֵּיל לַהּ, וְהָוֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְכׇל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינָהּ בְּטֵלָה לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said** a different answer: **Actually,** the basic *halakha* is that in a case **where a Jew bent it,** changing its shape, its status as an object of idol worship **is also revoked. But** the Sages issued **a decree** that such an idol retains its idolatrous status, **lest** a Jew first **lift it up and then** attempt to **revoke** its status. In this case the idol's status is not revoked, as when a Jew lifts an idol he acquires it, **and it becomes** an object of **idol worship in a Jew's possession, and any** object of **idol worship in a Jew's possession can never** have its idolatrous status **revoked.** Therefore, only when an idol breaks on its own does Reish Lakish maintain that its status is revoked.

Reish Lakish offers a second, more structurally significant answer through the opinion of Rabba — and this answer will serve as Reish Lakish's primary defense for the remainder of the *amud*. Rabba says: *le'olam ki pichasa Yisrael nami batla* — in truth, when a Jew bends the idol, it theoretically *does* work as *bittul*. However, the Rabbis enacted a *gezeirah*: they were concerned that in order to bend or break the idol, the Jew would first pick it up — *magbah lah vehadar mivattelah* — and by picking it up, he would take ownership of it. And we know that *vechol avodat kochavim b'yad Yisrael einah betelah le'olam* — an idol that comes into Jewish ownership can never be subject to *bittul*. To prevent a Jew from inadvertently acquiring an idol and thereby locking it permanently into *assur* status, the Rabbis decreed that a Jew's act of *bittul* is simply ineffective. The practical consequence is that the Jew should not pick it up in the first place.

It is worth noting that this *gezeirah* is specific to Jewish involvement. There is no concern about a non-Jew picking up an idol and thereby acquiring it in a way that would block *bittul*, because the rule that an idol in Jewish ownership can never be *mevutal* applies only to Jews. This asymmetry is what allows Reish Lakish to maintain that self-broken idols — which involve no Jewish action at all — remain *mutar*.

## Successive Challenges

### Markolis Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: גּוֹי שֶׁהֵבִיא אֲבָנִים מִן הַמַּרְקוּלִיס, וְחִיפָּה בָּהֶן דְּרָכִים וּטְרַטְיָאוֹת — מוּתָּרוֹת, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהֵבִיא אֲבָנִים מִן הַמַּרְקוּלִיס וְחִיפָּה בָּהֶן דְּרָכִים וּטְרַטְיָאוֹת — אֲסוּרוֹת. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: In the case of **a gentile who brought stones from** stone heaps that were used in the worship of the deity **Mercury [*HaMarkulis*], and** who then **paved roads and** built **theaters [*vetarteiot*] with them,** it is **permitted** to derive benefit from them, as the gentile revoked their idolatrous status. **But** in the case of **a Jew who brought stones** that were used in the worship of **Mercury and** who then **paved roads and** built **theaters with them,** it is **prohibited** to derive benefit from them. Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: According to Reish Lakish, **why** does a stone such as this retain its idolatrous status? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own,** which Reish Lakish deems permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's question may be answered **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that the Sages issued a decree that an object of idol worship retains its idolatrous status when a Jew attempts to revoke it, lest the Jew lift and acquire the idol, which would make it impossible to subsequently revoke its status.

Rabbi Yochanan continues with another *eitivei*. The ruling is: a non-Jew who takes stones from a *markolis* — a pile of stones dedicated to the worship of Mercury, into which worshippers would throw additional stones as their act of service — and uses those stones to pave roads or theaters (*v'chippa bahen drachim v'tarti'ot*), those roads are *mutarot*. One may walk on them and derive benefit from them. But if a Jew takes stones from the *markolis* and paves roads with them, those roads are *asurot*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, why should a Jew's paving make these roads *assur*? The Jew has repurposed stones from idol worship into something mundane — should this not be at least as effective as an idol breaking on its own? Reish Lakish answers: *hacha nami k'deRabba* — this too is governed by Rabba's principle. Theoretically, the Jew's act should constitute *bittul*, but the Rabbis enacted the same *gezeirah*: we are concerned the Jew would pick up the stones and thereby take ownership of them, after which *bittul* becomes permanently impossible.

### Shaving the Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: גּוֹי שֶׁשִּׁיפָּה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לְצׇרְכּוֹ — הִיא וְשִׁיפּוּיֶיהָ מוּתָּרִין, לְצׇרְכָּהּ — הִיא אֲסוּרָה וְשִׁיפּוּיֶיהָ מוּתָּרִין, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשִּׁיפָּה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, בֵּין לְצׇרְכּוֹ בֵּין לְצׇרְכָּהּ — הִיא וְשִׁיפּוּיֶיהָ אֲסוּרִין. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: In the case of **a gentile who shaved down** an object of **idol worship for his** own **sake,** as he needed the shavings, the idol itself **and its shavings are** then **permitted.** If he did it **for the sake of** the idol, to improve its appearance, the idol **is forbidden, but its shavings are permitted. But** in the case of **a Jew who shaved down** an object of **idol worship, whether** he did it **for his** own **sake or for the sake of** the idol, the idol itself **and its shavings are forbidden.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: According to Reish Lakish, in a case where a Jew shaved the idol for his own sake, **why** are the shavings forbidden? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's question may be answered **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that the Sages issued a decree that an object of idol worship retains its idolatrous status when a Jew attempts to cause it to be revoked.

Rabbi Yochanan presses again. The ruling is: a non-Jew who shaves down an idol *l'tzarcho* — for his own personal benefit, to obtain the shavings — the idol itself and the shavings are both *mutarin*. The non-Jew's willingness to scrape material off his idol for personal use signals his contempt for it; he is effectively treating it as scrap. By contrast, if he shaves it *l'tzorkha* — for the sake of the idol itself, as a form of upkeep — then the idol remains *assur*, but the shavings themselves are *mutar*, since the shavings have been separated from the main object. However, if a Jew does either of these — whether shaving for his own benefit or for the idol's sake — both the idol and the shavings are *asurin*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, should not the Jew's action be equivalent to spontaneous breakage, and thus *mutar*? And Reish Lakish again invokes Rabba: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. It is Rabba's *gezeirah* that renders it *assur* — the concern that the Jew will pick up the idol before shaving it, acquire it, and thereby block any future *bittul*.

One might ask: why does Rabbi Yochanan keep launching challenges when Reish Lakish has the same answer each time? Tosafot raises exactly this question. The implicit answer is that each challenge is slightly different in its structure, and the question is whether Rabba's *gezeirah* can plausibly be extended to cover each new scenario. Each application of *hacha nami k'deRabba* is its own halakhic move.

### Idol Dust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹחֵק וְזוֹרֶה לָרוּחַ אוֹ מֵטִיל לַיָּם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף הִיא נַעֲשֵׂית זֶבֶל, וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם״. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a mishna (43a): **Rabbi Yosei says:** When one encounters an idol, he should **grind** the idol **and throw** the dust **to the wind or cast** it **into the sea.** The Rabbis **said to him:** What is the good of that? **That also** gives a Jew benefit from the idol, as it **becomes fertilizer** for his crops, **and** deriving any kind of benefit is prohibited, as **it is written:** "And **nothing of the proscribed items shall cleave to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18). Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: According to Reish Lakish, **why** is this prohibited? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's question may be answered **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** cited above, that the Sages issued a decree with regard to this matter.

Rabbi Yochanan tries yet again, now citing the opinion of Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Yossi holds that when one encounters an idol, one should grind it down to dust and scatter it to the wind or cast it into the sea. The Rabbis respond to Rabbi Yossi: but even that dust can serve as fertilizer — *af hi ne'eset zevel* — and thus one would be deriving benefit from it. And we have the verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha me'uma min hacharem* — nothing of what is forbidden should remain in your possession. The implication is that even the ground-up dust of an idol retains the status of *avodah zarah*, because it can yield benefit.

Rabbi Yochanan asks Reish Lakish: if this is so, and even idol dust remains *assur*, how does that cohere with your view that an idol breaking on its own becomes *mutar*? The Gemara answers: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. Rabba's *gezeirah* applies here as well — a Jew cannot pick up the idol to grind it, for doing so would acquire it and permanently foreclose *bittul*. This answer raises an interesting practical question: what, then, is a Jew supposed to do when he finds an idol? He cannot grind it, he cannot pick it up, he cannot break it. One is left wondering whether burying it — without picking it up — might be a workable solution, perhaps by digging a hole nearby and toppling it in.

### Dragon Head Cut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן יָסְיָאן אוֹמֵר: מָצָא צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן וְרֹאשׁוֹ חָתוּךְ, סָפֵק גּוֹי חֲתָכוֹ סָפֵק יִשְׂרָאֵל חֲתָכוֹ — מוּתָּר, וַדַּאי יִשְׂרָאֵל חֲתָכוֹ — אָסוּר. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei ben Yasian says:** If one **found** an object in **the figure of a dragon [*derakon*] with its head severed,** but it is **uncertain** whether **a gentile severed it** and it is **uncertain** whether **a Jew severed it,** the object is **permitted.** But if it is **certain** that **a Jew severed it,** it is **forbidden.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: According to Reish Lakish, **why** is it forbidden? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's question may be answered **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that the Sages issued a decree with regard to this matter.

The final challenge in this series involves an image of a *drakon* — a dragon or serpent-like creature — whose head has been cut off. The ruling is: *safek oved kochavim chatacho, safek Yisrael chatacho* — if there is doubt whether a non-Jew or a Jew cut off the head, the image is *mutar*, since we may be lenient and assume a non-Jew cut it. But if it is known definitively that a Jew cut it, it is *assur*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, this should be equivalent to an idol that broke on its own — *tehavi k'avodat kochavim shenishbeira me'aleha* — and thus *mutar* even when a Jew did the cutting. And once again, Reish Lakish responds: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. The same *gezeirah* applies.

## Asherah Trees

### Winter Planting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, אַף לֹא יְרָקוֹת בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַנְּבִיָּיה נוֹשֶׁרֶת עֲלֵיהֶן. אַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּבְּרָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says** that one may **not even** plant **vegetables in the rainy season** under a tree worshipped as an idol, **as foliage may fall on them,** serving as fertilizer. Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: According to Reish Lakish, **why** should a leaf from such a tree retain its idolatrous status? **Let it be** treated **like** an object of **idol worship that broke on its own.**

Having withstood all of Rabbi Yochanan's challenges, Reish Lakish now turns the tables and launches his own *eitivei* against Rabbi Yochanan. The topic shifts to the *asheira* — a tree dedicated to *avodah zarah*.

Rabbi Yossi rules that one may not plant vegetables beneath an *asheira* even in the winter months: *af lo yerakot biyemot hageshamim*. The background assumption is that in the summer, it is obvious one may not do so, because the shade of the *asheira* benefits the plants, and one thereby derives benefit from the *avodah zarah*. In the winter, one might have thought the shade is actually harmful to plants and thus no benefit is being derived. Rabbi Yossi nonetheless prohibits it, because *mipnei shehaniviana nosheret aleiha* — the foliage and leaves fall off the tree onto the vegetables below and act as fertilizer, which constitutes benefit.

Reish Lakish now poses this as a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan: those leaves fell on their own — *me'aleha*, of their own accord. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that an idol that breaks on its own is *assur*, this is consistent. But since Reish Lakish holds that what breaks or falls of its own accord is *mutar*, he wants to know why Rabbi Yochanan finds this case troubling.

### Falling Leaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּעִיקַּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קַיֶּימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as the main** object of **idol worship,** the tree, still **exists** fully intact.

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is: *shani hatam de'avodat kochavim kayamet* — this case is different, because the main body of the *avodah zarah* is still intact. The tree still stands. The leaves falling off do not diminish the idol in any meaningful way; the *ikar* of the *avodah zarah* is exactly as it was before. This distinguishes it from an idol that breaks on its own, where the idol itself has been damaged or destroyed.

### Natural Growth Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שִׁיפּוּיָין, דְּעִיקַּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קַיֶּימֶת, וְקָתָנֵי: לְצׇרְכָּהּ — הִיא אֲסוּרָה, וְשִׁיפּוּיֶיהָ מוּתָּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** consider the case of **shavings, where the main** object of **idol worship** still **exists, and** nevertheless **it is taught** in the *baraita* cited earlier that if a gentile shaved an idol down **for its** own **sake,** the idol is **forbidden, but its shavings are permitted.** Here too, in the case of the fallen leaves of a tree that is worshipped as an idol, it should be permitted to derive benefit from them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּטֵלָה דֶּרֶךְ גְּדִילָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** The reason the leaves are not permitted is **because** the status of an object of **idol worship cannot be revoked** by **its** natural **manner of growth.** Since the falling of leaves is a natural phenomenon, their detachment from the tree does not effect a revocation of their status as objects of idol worship.

But the Gemara pushes back on Rabbi Yochanan's answer: what about the case of the shavings (*shipuyin*)? There, we said that if the non-Jew shaved the idol for the idol's own sake, the idol remains *assur* but the shavings are *mutar* — and in that case, too, the main body of the idol is still intact. So why are leaves that fall from an *asheira* any different from shavings that fall from an idol?

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua answers: *lefi she'ein avodat kochavim betelah derech gedilata* — *avodah zarah* cannot be *mevutal* through the natural course of its own growth. It is built into the nature of a tree that its leaves fall. Since this is merely the tree's natural developmental process, it cannot constitute *bittul* in any form. This is a distinct principle from Rabba's *gezeirah*: it is not about the concern of Jewish ownership but about the inherent incapacity of natural growth processes to effect *bittul*.

### Nests on Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קֵן שֶׁבְּרֹאשׁ הָאִילָן שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ — לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין, בְּרֹאשָׁהּ שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה — יַתִּיז בְּקָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** from a mishna (*Me'ila* 13b): With regard to a bird's **nest at the top of a tree that belongs to the Temple** treasury, one **may not** derive **benefit** from it *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from it, he is **not** liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple. With regard to a nest that is **at the top of a tree** used as part of idolatrous rites **[*ashera*],** although one may not climb the tree, as that would be benefiting from an object of idol worship, he **may knock** the nest off **with a pole** and benefit from it by using it for firewood and the like.

Reish Lakish continues his challenge to Rabbi Yochanan with a case involving nests. The *mishnah* or *baraita* distinguishes between two trees, each with a bird's nest at its top. The first is a tree that has been made *hekdesh* — dedicated to the Temple. Regarding the nest on this tree, the ruling is *lo nehenin velo mo'alin*: one may not benefit from the nest (*derabbanan*), but if one does benefit, there is no violation of *me'ilah* (*de'oraita*). The absence of *me'ilah* means that *de'oraita*, the nest does not carry the full sanctity of the *hekdesh* tree.

The second case is a nest at the top of an *asheira* tree, where the ruling is *yitiz bekaneh* — one may knock the nest off with a reed and take it. The natural assumption, Reish Lakish argues, is that a bird builds its nest by breaking off twigs from the very tree it sits upon. If that is the case, then the twigs of the nest are part of the *asheira* tree material — yet the ruling permits benefiting from them. This, Reish Lakish argues, supports his position: material that breaks off from the *avodah zarah* on its own becomes *mutar*, just as he holds for self-broken idols.

### Chicks Not Twigs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, כְּגוֹן שֶׁשִּׁבְּרָה מִמֶּנּוּ עֵצִים וְקִינְּתָהּ בָּהֶן, וְקָתָנֵי: יַתִּיז בְּקָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** In analyzing this *baraita*, **it enters your mind** that this is referring to a case **where** the bird **broke off branches from** the worshipped tree **and built a nest with them. And** yet, the *baraita* **teaches** that one may **knock** the nest off **with a pole** and it is then permitted to benefit from it. Apparently, the forbidden branches used in the construction of the nest lost their idolatrous status without human involvement, in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish that an idol that breaks loses its status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּאַיְיתַי עֵצִים מֵעָלְמָא, וְקִינְּתָהּ בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the bird **brought branches from elsewhere and built a nest with them** on top of the worshipped tree. The branches were never part of an object of idol worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי הֶקְדֵּשׁ: לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין; אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאַיְיתַי עֵצִים מֵעָלְמָא, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי הֶקְדֵּשׁ: לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין — לֹא נֶהֱנִין מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּהָא לָא קַדִּישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise** if read with this understanding, **as it teaches with regard to** a nest in **a consecrated** tree: One **may not** derive **benefit** from it *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from it, he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **Granted, if you say that** the bird **brought branches from elsewhere, this** explanation is consistent with that **which** the mishna **teaches with regard to** a nest in **a consecrated** tree, namely, that one **may not** derive **benefit** from the nest, **but** if one derived benefit from it, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property. According to this understanding of the mishna, one **may not** derive **benefit** from the nest **by rabbinic law, but** if one derived benefit from it, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property **by Torah law, as** the branches **are not consecrated,** but were brought from elsewhere.

Rabbi Yochanan defends himself by rejecting Reish Lakish's factual assumption. The source of the *yitiz bekaneh* ruling is *Rabbi Yochanan* himself, who explains: *amai yatiz, yatiz be'afruchin* — what you are knocking out is the baby chicks, not the nest itself. The ruling permits knocking the chicks out of the nest, but it says nothing about benefiting from the nest's twigs. And the chicks themselves were not part of the *asheira* tree material — they belong to the bird, not to the tree. Rabbi Yochanan therefore argues that his permissive ruling does not support Reish Lakish's position at all.

Furthermore, the language of the *hekdesh* case — *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — is itself evidence that the twigs come from elsewhere. If the bird built the nest from twigs of the *hekdesh* tree itself, those twigs would have the full sanctity of *hekdesh*, and benefiting from them would constitute *me'ilah* (*de'oraita*). The fact that the ruling says *velo mo'alin* — implying there is no *de'oraita* violation — demonstrates that the twigs are from some other tree brought in from outside: *de'aytei etzim me'alma vekinita bahen*. By external twigs, the nest carries only a *derabbanan* prohibition, which is why *lo nehenin* applies but *velo mo'alin* (no *de'oraita* violation) follows.

### Eggs & Young Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּמוֹצֵא, אֵימָא מְצִיעֲתָא: כׇּל הַפַּרְצוּפוֹת מוּתָּרִין חוּץ מִפַּרְצוּף אָדָם; אִי בְּמוֹצֵא, פַּרְצוּף אָדָם מִי אָסוּר? וְהָתְנַן: הַמּוֹצֵא כֵּלִים וַעֲלֵיהֶם צוּרַת חַמָּה, צוּרַת לְבָנָה, צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — אִין, פַּרְצוּף אָדָם — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is a case **where** one **finds** vessels with these figures on them, **say the middle clause** of Rav Sheshet's statement: Figures of **all faces are permitted, except for the human face.** Now, **if** it is a case **where** one **finds** vessels with figures on them, **is** a vessel with the figure of **the human face forbidden? But didn't we learn** in the mishna: In the case of **one who finds vessels, and upon them is a figure of the sun, a figure of the moon,** or **a figure of a dragon, he must take them** and cast them **into the Dead Sea?** This indicates that a vessel with **the figure of a dragon is** forbidden, but a vessel with the figure of **a human face is not.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ, בְּאֶפְרוֹחִין — כָּאן וְכָאן מוּתָּרִין, בְּבֵיצִים — כָּאן וְכָאן אֲסוּרִין. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֶפְרוֹחִין שֶׁצְּרִיכִין לְאִמָּן כְּבֵיצִים דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov said to Rabbi Yirmeya bar Taḥlifa: I will explain** the mishna **to you: With regard to** the **chicks,** which can fly away and are not confined to the tree, both **here and there,** i.e., both in the case of a tree consecrated to the Temple treasury and in the case of a tree used for idol worship, deriving benefit from them is **permitted.** But **with regard to** the **eggs,** both **here and there,** i.e., both in the case of a tree consecrated to the Temple treasury and in the case of a tree used for idol worship, deriving benefit from them is **prohibited,** as they are not seen as independent of the tree. **Rav Ashi** added to this and **said: And chicks that** still **need their mother** to survive **are** considered **like eggs;** deriving benefit from them is prohibited.

Reish Lakish attempts to sustain his challenge by pointing to the principle of *ain me'ilah begidulin*: even if the bird took twigs from the *hekdesh* tree itself, those twigs grew *after* the tree was made *hekdesh*, and there is a rule that growth occurring after *hekdesh* is declared does not carry the full force of *me'ilah*. This would explain *velo mo'alin* without requiring Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation that the twigs came from elsewhere.

Rabbi Yaakov clarifies the practical distinctions in this series of cases. Regarding baby birds (*afruchin*): in both the *hekdesh* case and the *asheira* case, the chicks are *mutar*. Regarding eggs (*beitzim*): in both cases, they are *asurin*. The eggs are considered an integral part of the nest in a way that the mobile chicks are not, and therefore the prohibition of the nest extends to them.

Rav Ashi adds a further refinement: *ba'afruchin shetzerichin le'iman k'veitzim damu* — chicks that are still dependent on their mother are treated like eggs and are likewise *assur*. Only chicks capable of independent existence are fully permitted.

## Vessels & Images

### Destroying Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצֵא כֵּלִים וַעֲלֵיהֶם צוּרַת חַמָּה, צוּרַת לְבָנָה, צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שֶׁעַל הַמְכוּבָּדִין — אֲסוּרִין, שֶׁעַל הַמְבוּזִּין — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who finds vessels, and upon them is a figure of the sun, a figure of the moon,** or **a figure of a dragon, he must take them** and cast them **into the Dead Sea** and not derive any benefit from them, as they are assumed to be objects of idol worship. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** Those figures **that** are **upon respectable** vessels **are forbidden.** Those **that are upon disgraceful vessels are permitted.**

The Gemara now begins a new *mishnah*. One who finds vessels (*keilim*) bearing an image of the sun (*tzurat chama*), an image of the moon (*tzurat levana*), or an image of a *drakon*, must carry them to the Dead Sea and cast them in: *yolichim l'yam hamelach*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a qualification: it is only vessels that are *mechubadin* — important or distinguished — that are *assur*. Vessels that are *mevuzin* — humble or contemptible in appearance — are *mutar*. The precise meaning of this distinction will receive further treatment in the Gemara.

### Create vs. Find

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמֵימְרָא דִּלְהָנֵי הוּא דְּפָלְחִי לְהוּ, לְמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא? וּרְמִינְהִי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְשׁוּם יַמִּים, לְשׁוּם נְהָרוֹת, לְשׁוּם מִדְבָּר, לְשׁוּם חַמָּה, לְשׁוּם לְבָנָה, לְשׁוּם כּוֹכָבִים וּמַזָּלוֹת, לְשׁוּם מִיכָאֵל שַׂר הַגָּדוֹל, לְשׁוּם שִׁילְשׁוּל קָטָן — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ זִבְחֵי מֵתִים!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks with regard to the specific figures listed in the mishna: **Is this to say that** people **worship only these** figures, **but not** any **other item? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between this list and that which is taught in another mishna (*Ḥullin* 39b): With regard to **one who slaughters** an animal **for the sake of,** i.e., to worship, **seas, for the sake of rivers, for the sake of the wilderness, for the sake of the sun, for the sake of the moon, for the sake of the stars and constellations, for the sake of Michael the great ministering** angel, or even **for the sake of a small worm,** in all of **these** cases, the animal is forbidden, as these animals have the status of **offerings to the dead,** i.e., idols.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִיפְלָח לְכֹל דְּמַשְׁכְּחִי פָּלְחִי, מֵיצָר וּמִפְלָחי — הָנֵי תְּלָתָא דַּחֲשִׁיבִי צָיְירִי לְהוּ וּפָלְחִי לְהוּ, לְמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לְנוֹי בְּעָלְמָא עָבְדִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in response to the contradiction: With regard to **worship,** people might **worship any** item **that they find.** With regard to **fashioning** figures **and** then **worshipping** them, only with regard to **these three** items listed in the mishna, **which are important,** do people **fashion** figures of **them and worship them. With regard to** figures of any **other entity,** people **make them merely for ornamental** purposes.

The Gemara asks: is the *mishnah* implying that only these three images — sun, moon, and dragon — are worshipped by non-Jews, and that all other images are not? But that cannot be right. A *baraita* from Chullin enumerates a long list of things for which one may slaughter as worship: the sea, rivers, the desert, the sun, the moon, the stars and constellations (*kochavim umazalot*), Michael the great ministering angel, and even the smallest worm (*shilshul katan*) — all of these are classified as *zivchei meitim*, as idol worship.

Abaye resolves this: *miflach l'chol d'meshkach palchi* — non-Jews worship anything they happen to find. Whatever is in front of them, they will potentially worship. But when it comes to objects that they actually craft and create for worship, it is specifically those three things — sun, moon, and dragon — that they manufacture and venerate. Other images, when made, are crafted *l'noi b'alma* — purely for decoration — not as objects of worship. This distinction between what one creates and what one finds will prove to be structurally important going forward.

### Rav Sheshet's Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנַקֵּיט רַב שֵׁשֶׁת חוּמְרֵי מַתְנְיָיתָא וְתָנֵי: כׇּל הַמַּזָּלוֹת מוּתָּרִין — חוּץ מִמַּזַּל חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה, וְכׇל הַפַּרְצוּפִין מוּתָּרִין — חוּץ מִפַּרְצוּף אָדָם, וְכׇל הַצּוּרוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת — חוּץ מִצּוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet would consolidate the principles of the *baraitot*** pertaining to this matter **and teach:** Figures of **all constellations are permitted, except for** the following **celestial objects: The sun and the moon. And** figures of **all faces are permitted, except for the human face. And all figures** of other items **are permitted except for the figure of a dragon.**

Rav Sheshet compiled the relevant *chumrot* from various sources and taught them together in a single *baraita*. He establishes three rules. First: all celestial bodies (*mazalot*) are *mutar*, except for the sun and the moon (*chama u'levana*). Second: all faces (*partzufin*) are *mutar*, except for the face of a human being (*partzuf adam*). Third: all shapes and images are *mutar*, except for the shape of a *drakon*.

The scriptural basis for singling out the sun and moon appears to come from *Shoftim*, where *hashemesh ve'hayareach* — the sun and the moon — are specifically mentioned. The *drakon* and the human face carry their own reasons, which the *sugya* will develop.

### Making Idols Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: כׇּל הַמַּזָּלוֹת מוּתָּרִין, חוּץ מִמַּזַּל חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּעוֹשֶׂה — אִי בְּעוֹשֶׂה, כׇּל הַמַּזָּלוֹת מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי״ — לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּן כִּדְמוּת שַׁמָּשַׁי הַמְשַׁמְּשִׁין לְפָנַי בַּמָּרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said:** Figures of **all constellations are permitted, except for** the following **celestial objects: The sun and the moon.** The Gemara asks: **What are we dealing with here? If we say** that the reference is **to one who forms** these figures, i.e., **if** Rav Sheshet is discussing the issue **of** which figures it is permitted to **form, is** forming figures of **all the** other **constellations permitted? But isn't it written: "You shall not make with Me** gods of silver, or gods of gold, you shall not make for you" (Exodus 20:20)? This verse is interpreted to mean: **You shall not make figures of My attendants who serve before Me on high,** i.e., those celestial bodies, including the constellations, that were created to serve God.

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty with Rav Sheshet's first rule. If all celestial bodies other than the sun and the moon are *mutar*, in what context is this being said? If we are speaking of *making* such images, surely even making a representation of, say, a star or a constellation would be prohibited. The verse in *Shemot* states: *lo ta'asun iti elohei chesef ve'elohei zahav lo ta'asu lachem* — do not make alongside Me gods of silver or gold. And the Rabbinic interpretation of this verse is: *lo ta'asuni k'demut shimshai hamishmishin lefanai bamarom* — do not make images resembling My attendants who serve before Me on high. Those heavenly attendants include all the celestial bodies, not just the sun and the moon. Therefore Rav Sheshet cannot be speaking of a case where one is manufacturing these images.

### Finding vs. Making

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּמוֹצֵא, וּכְדִתְנַן: הַמּוֹצֵא כֵּלִים וַעֲלֵיהֶם צוּרַת חַמָּה, צוּרַת לְבָנָה, צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that this *halakha* is referring to a case **where** one **finds** vessels with these figures on them, **and** this is **as we learned** in the mishna: In the case of **one who finds vessels, and upon them is a figure of the sun, a figure of the moon,** or **a figure of a dragon, he must take them** and cast them **into the Dead Sea.** This indicates that it is permitted to derive benefit from any other vessels that were found and that had figures on them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּמוֹצֵא, אֵימָא מְצִיעֲתָא: כׇּל הַפַּרְצוּפוֹת מוּתָּרִין חוּץ מִפַּרְצוּף אָדָם; אִי בְּמוֹצֵא, פַּרְצוּף אָדָם מִי אָסוּר? וְהָתְנַן: הַמּוֹצֵא כֵּלִים וַעֲלֵיהֶם צוּרַת חַמָּה, צוּרַת לְבָנָה, צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן — אִין, פַּרְצוּף אָדָם — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is a case **where** one **finds** vessels with these figures on them, **say the middle clause** of Rav Sheshet's statement: Figures of **all faces are permitted, except for the human face.** Now, **if** it is a case **where** one **finds** vessels with figures on them, **is** a vessel with the figure of **the human face forbidden? But didn't we learn** in the mishna: In the case of **one who finds vessels, and upon them is a figure of the sun, a figure of the moon,** or **a figure of a dragon, he must take them** and cast them **into the Dead Sea?** This indicates that a vessel with **the figure of a dragon is** forbidden, but a vessel with the figure of **a human face is not.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּעוֹשֶׂה, וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara concludes, it is **obvious** that the statement that the figure of a human face is forbidden is referring to a case **where one forms** a figure, **and** this is prohibited, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** who states (43b) that the interpretation of the verse: "You shall not make with Me gods of silver, or gods of gold, you shall not make for you" (Exodus 20:20), is: Do not make of Me, i.e., do not form the figure of a person, who was created in the image of God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּעוֹשֶׂה, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כׇּל הַצּוּרוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת — חוּץ מִצּוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן; וְאִי בְּעוֹשֶׂה, צוּרַת דְּרָקוֹן מִי אֲסִיר? וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי אֱלֹהֵי כֶסֶף וֵאלֹהֵי זָהָב״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is referring to a case **where one forms** a figure, **say the last clause** of Rav Sheshet's statement: **All figures are permitted except for the figure of a dragon. And if** it is referring to a case **where one forms** a figure, **is** forming **the figure of a dragon prohibited? But isn't it written: "You shall not make with Me gods of silver, or gods of gold"?**

The resolution is that Rav Sheshet's rules apply specifically to the case of *finding* such objects: *ella pshita bimotzei* — it is obvious that he is discussing one who finds an already-made vessel bearing one of these images. And the ruling aligns precisely with the *mishnah* we just read: *hamotzei keilim ve'aleihem tzurat chama tzurat levana tzurat drakon yolichim l'yam hamelach* — one who finds vessels bearing the images of the sun, the moon, or the *drakon* must cast them into the Dead Sea. Implicitly, other images found on vessels do not require this treatment. Rav Sheshet's three rules thus map onto the *finding* scenario: only the sun, the moon, the human face, and the *drakon* raise concern when found, while other celestial bodies, other faces, and other images, when merely found rather than crafted for worship, are *mutar*.

The Gemara is poised to analyze each of Rav Sheshet's three categories in turn — the celestial bodies, the faces, and the *drakon* — examining how each resolves into either the *finding* or the *making* framework. That analysis will continue in the next *amud*.
