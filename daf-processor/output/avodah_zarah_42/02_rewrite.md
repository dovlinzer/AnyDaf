# Avodah Zarah 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Breaking Idol Debate

### Self-Broken Idols

The daf opens by returning to a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish that was introduced the previous day: what is the status of an idol that breaks on its own, without any human intervention? Rabbi Yochanan holds that such an idol remains *assur*. His reasoning is that *bittul* of an idol requires an affirmative act — a non-Jew must deliberately break it, thereby expressing that the idol no longer holds significance for him. Spontaneous breakage, in his view, accomplishes nothing of the sort. Reish Lakish disagrees and holds the idol is *mutar*. His reasoning is that a non-Jew who sees his idol collapse and break of its own accord will reason: if the idol cannot even protect itself, it can hardly protect me. In his mind, therefore, he *mevatel*s it — he dismisses it as no longer possessing any power.

The Gemara proceeds to present a series of challenges — each introduced by the formula *eitivei* — in which Rabbi Yochanan attempts to demonstrate that Reish Lakish's position leads to untenable conclusions. In each case, Reish Lakish (or the Gemara in his name) offers a response, and the responses themselves follow a consistent pattern. That pattern is worth tracking carefully, as it recurs throughout the *amud*.

### Pieces on Pedestals

The first challenge draws on a ruling we have already encountered: if one finds a *tavnit yad* or *tavnit regel* — a form of just a hand or just a foot — these are *assur*, because they are *keyotzei bahen na'avad*, similar to something that has been worshipped. Rabbi Yochanan presses Reish Lakish: why should these be *assur*? They are, after all, *shevarim* — broken pieces. If a self-broken idol is *mutar* according to Reish Lakish, then broken-off pieces of a statue should similarly be *mutar*.

The Gemara answers by invoking Shmuel's interpretation: *b'omdin al basisam* — these are pieces that are standing on their own pedestals. In other words, the hand or foot is not a fragment broken off from a larger statue; it is, from the outset, a complete and independent idol in the form of a hand or foot. Such objects were evidently worshipped in their own right. The *shevarim* premise of the challenge simply does not apply here, because nothing has broken — the object is whole as it was always meant to be.

### Bending the Idol

Rabbi Yochanan tries again. The Gemara rules that a non-Jew can perform *bittul* on his own idol or on another non-Jew's idol, but a Jew cannot perform *bittul* on a non-Jew's idol. Rabbi Yochanan presses: according to Reish Lakish, why should a Jew's act of *bittul* fail? If a self-broken idol is *mutar*, then surely an idol that a Jew actively breaks should be at least as *mutar* — it should be *tehavi k'avodat kochavim shenishbeira me'aleha*.

Abaye clarifies that the case under discussion is not one of breaking the idol but of *pichasa* — a Jew who bends the idol out of shape, or in Rashi's formulation, takes a hammer to it and flattens it. This is a distinct act. The Gemara then asks: but what difference should that make? We even have an explicit *Mishnah* that says *pichasa af al pi shelo chasra batla* — bending the idol, even if nothing is actually removed, constitutes successful *bittul*. So why doesn't it work when a Jew does it?

Reish Lakish's first answer is that the *Mishnah* speaks of when a non-Jew performs the bending: *hani mili depichasa oved kochavim*, but *aval pichasa Yisrael lo batla*. Rashi explains that a non-Jew observing the bending would reason that the idol could not save itself — the same logic Reish Lakish employs for spontaneous breakage. But when a Jew comes and bends it, the non-Jew's reaction is simply: someone came and smashed my idol. There is no theological inference drawn. The distinction is psychologically coherent: spontaneous damage suggests divine disfavor or weakness; deliberate human damage does not.

### Rabba's Gezeirah

Reish Lakish offers a second, more structurally significant answer through the opinion of Rabba — and this answer will serve as Reish Lakish's primary defense for the remainder of the *amud*. Rabba says: *le'olam ki pichasa Yisrael nami batla* — in truth, when a Jew bends the idol, it theoretically *does* work as *bittul*. However, the Rabbis enacted a *gezeirah*: they were concerned that in order to bend or break the idol, the Jew would first pick it up — *magbah lah vehadar mivattelah* — and by picking it up, he would take ownership of it. And we know that *vechol avodat kochavim b'yad Yisrael einah betelah le'olam* — an idol that comes into Jewish ownership can never be subject to *bittul*. To prevent a Jew from inadvertently acquiring an idol and thereby locking it permanently into *assur* status, the Rabbis decreed that a Jew's act of *bittul* is simply ineffective. The practical consequence is that the Jew should not pick it up in the first place.

It is worth noting that this *gezeirah* is specific to Jewish involvement. There is no concern about a non-Jew picking up an idol and thereby acquiring it in a way that would block *bittul*, because the rule that an idol in Jewish ownership can never be *mevutal* applies only to Jews. This asymmetry is what allows Reish Lakish to maintain that self-broken idols — which involve no Jewish action at all — remain *mutar*.

## Successive Challenges

### Markolis Stones

Rabbi Yochanan continues with another *eitivei*. The ruling is: a non-Jew who takes stones from a *markolis* — a pile of stones dedicated to the worship of Mercury, into which worshippers would throw additional stones as their act of service — and uses those stones to pave roads or theaters (*v'chippa bahen drachim v'tarti'ot*), those roads are *mutarot*. One may walk on them and derive benefit from them. But if a Jew takes stones from the *markolis* and paves roads with them, those roads are *asurot*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, why should a Jew's paving make these roads *assur*? The Jew has repurposed stones from idol worship into something mundane — should this not be at least as effective as an idol breaking on its own? Reish Lakish answers: *hacha nami k'deRabba* — this too is governed by Rabba's principle. Theoretically, the Jew's act should constitute *bittul*, but the Rabbis enacted the same *gezeirah*: we are concerned the Jew would pick up the stones and thereby take ownership of them, after which *bittul* becomes permanently impossible.

### Shaving the Idol

Rabbi Yochanan presses again. The ruling is: a non-Jew who shaves down an idol *l'tzarcho* — for his own personal benefit, to obtain the shavings — the idol itself and the shavings are both *mutarin*. The non-Jew's willingness to scrape material off his idol for personal use signals his contempt for it; he is effectively treating it as scrap. By contrast, if he shaves it *l'tzorkha* — for the sake of the idol itself, as a form of upkeep — then the idol remains *assur*, but the shavings themselves are *mutar*, since the shavings have been separated from the main object. However, if a Jew does either of these — whether shaving for his own benefit or for the idol's sake — both the idol and the shavings are *asurin*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, should not the Jew's action be equivalent to spontaneous breakage, and thus *mutar*? And Reish Lakish again invokes Rabba: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. It is Rabba's *gezeirah* that renders it *assur* — the concern that the Jew will pick up the idol before shaving it, acquire it, and thereby block any future *bittul*.

One might ask: why does Rabbi Yochanan keep launching challenges when Reish Lakish has the same answer each time? Tosafot raises exactly this question. The implicit answer is that each challenge is slightly different in its structure, and the question is whether Rabba's *gezeirah* can plausibly be extended to cover each new scenario. Each application of *hacha nami k'deRabba* is its own halakhic move.

### Idol Dust

Rabbi Yochanan tries yet again, now citing the opinion of Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Yossi holds that when one encounters an idol, one should grind it down to dust and scatter it to the wind or cast it into the sea. The Rabbis respond to Rabbi Yossi: but even that dust can serve as fertilizer — *af hi ne'eset zevel* — and thus one would be deriving benefit from it. And we have the verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha me'uma min hacharem* — nothing of what is forbidden should remain in your possession. The implication is that even the ground-up dust of an idol retains the status of *avodah zarah*, because it can yield benefit.

Rabbi Yochanan asks Reish Lakish: if this is so, and even idol dust remains *assur*, how does that cohere with your view that an idol breaking on its own becomes *mutar*? The Gemara answers: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. Rabba's *gezeirah* applies here as well — a Jew cannot pick up the idol to grind it, for doing so would acquire it and permanently foreclose *bittul*. This answer raises an interesting practical question: what, then, is a Jew supposed to do when he finds an idol? He cannot grind it, he cannot pick it up, he cannot break it. One is left wondering whether burying it — without picking it up — might be a workable solution, perhaps by digging a hole nearby and toppling it in.

### Dragon Head Cut

The final challenge in this series involves an image of a *drakon* — a dragon or serpent-like creature — whose head has been cut off. The ruling is: *safek oved kochavim chatacho, safek Yisrael chatacho* — if there is doubt whether a non-Jew or a Jew cut off the head, the image is *mutar*, since we may be lenient and assume a non-Jew cut it. But if it is known definitively that a Jew cut it, it is *assur*.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: according to Reish Lakish, this should be equivalent to an idol that broke on its own — *tehavi k'avodat kochavim shenishbeira me'aleha* — and thus *mutar* even when a Jew did the cutting. And once again, Reish Lakish responds: *hacha nami k'deRabba*. The same *gezeirah* applies.

## Asherah Trees

### Winter Planting

Having withstood all of Rabbi Yochanan's challenges, Reish Lakish now turns the tables and launches his own *eitivei* against Rabbi Yochanan. The topic shifts to the *asheira* — a tree dedicated to *avodah zarah*.

Rabbi Yossi rules that one may not plant vegetables beneath an *asheira* even in the winter months: *af lo yerakot biyemot hageshamim*. The background assumption is that in the summer, it is obvious one may not do so, because the shade of the *asheira* benefits the plants, and one thereby derives benefit from the *avodah zarah*. In the winter, one might have thought the shade is actually harmful to plants and thus no benefit is being derived. Rabbi Yossi nonetheless prohibits it, because *mipnei shehaniviana nosheret aleiha* — the foliage and leaves fall off the tree onto the vegetables below and act as fertilizer, which constitutes benefit.

Reish Lakish now poses this as a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan: those leaves fell on their own — *me'aleha*, of their own accord. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that an idol that breaks on its own is *assur*, this is consistent. But since Reish Lakish holds that what breaks or falls of its own accord is *mutar*, he wants to know why Rabbi Yochanan finds this case troubling.

### Falling Leaves

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is: *shani hatam de'avodat kochavim kayamet* — this case is different, because the main body of the *avodah zarah* is still intact. The tree still stands. The leaves falling off do not diminish the idol in any meaningful way; the *ikar* of the *avodah zarah* is exactly as it was before. This distinguishes it from an idol that breaks on its own, where the idol itself has been damaged or destroyed.

### Natural Growth Rule

But the Gemara pushes back on Rabbi Yochanan's answer: what about the case of the shavings (*shipuyin*)? There, we said that if the non-Jew shaved the idol for the idol's own sake, the idol remains *assur* but the shavings are *mutar* — and in that case, too, the main body of the idol is still intact. So why are leaves that fall from an *asheira* any different from shavings that fall from an idol?

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua answers: *lefi she'ein avodat kochavim betelah derech gedilata* — *avodah zarah* cannot be *mevutal* through the natural course of its own growth. It is built into the nature of a tree that its leaves fall. Since this is merely the tree's natural developmental process, it cannot constitute *bittul* in any form. This is a distinct principle from Rabba's *gezeirah*: it is not about the concern of Jewish ownership but about the inherent incapacity of natural growth processes to effect *bittul*.

### Nests on Trees

Reish Lakish continues his challenge to Rabbi Yochanan with a case involving nests. The *mishnah* or *baraita* distinguishes between two trees, each with a bird's nest at its top. The first is a tree that has been made *hekdesh* — dedicated to the Temple. Regarding the nest on this tree, the ruling is *lo nehenin velo mo'alin*: one may not benefit from the nest (*derabbanan*), but if one does benefit, there is no violation of *me'ilah* (*de'oraita*). The absence of *me'ilah* means that *de'oraita*, the nest does not carry the full sanctity of the *hekdesh* tree.

The second case is a nest at the top of an *asheira* tree, where the ruling is *yitiz bekaneh* — one may knock the nest off with a reed and take it. The natural assumption, Reish Lakish argues, is that a bird builds its nest by breaking off twigs from the very tree it sits upon. If that is the case, then the twigs of the nest are part of the *asheira* tree material — yet the ruling permits benefiting from them. This, Reish Lakish argues, supports his position: material that breaks off from the *avodah zarah* on its own becomes *mutar*, just as he holds for self-broken idols.

### Chicks Not Twigs

Rabbi Yochanan defends himself by rejecting Reish Lakish's factual assumption. The source of the *yitiz bekaneh* ruling is *Rabbi Yochanan* himself, who explains: *amai yatiz, yatiz be'afruchin* — what you are knocking out is the baby chicks, not the nest itself. The ruling permits knocking the chicks out of the nest, but it says nothing about benefiting from the nest's twigs. And the chicks themselves were not part of the *asheira* tree material — they belong to the bird, not to the tree. Rabbi Yochanan therefore argues that his permissive ruling does not support Reish Lakish's position at all.

Furthermore, the language of the *hekdesh* case — *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — is itself evidence that the twigs come from elsewhere. If the bird built the nest from twigs of the *hekdesh* tree itself, those twigs would have the full sanctity of *hekdesh*, and benefiting from them would constitute *me'ilah* (*de'oraita*). The fact that the ruling says *velo mo'alin* — implying there is no *de'oraita* violation — demonstrates that the twigs are from some other tree brought in from outside: *de'aytei etzim me'alma vekinita bahen*. By external twigs, the nest carries only a *derabbanan* prohibition, which is why *lo nehenin* applies but *velo mo'alin* (no *de'oraita* violation) follows.

### Eggs & Young Birds

Reish Lakish attempts to sustain his challenge by pointing to the principle of *ain me'ilah begidulin*: even if the bird took twigs from the *hekdesh* tree itself, those twigs grew *after* the tree was made *hekdesh*, and there is a rule that growth occurring after *hekdesh* is declared does not carry the full force of *me'ilah*. This would explain *velo mo'alin* without requiring Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation that the twigs came from elsewhere.

Rabbi Yaakov clarifies the practical distinctions in this series of cases. Regarding baby birds (*afruchin*): in both the *hekdesh* case and the *asheira* case, the chicks are *mutar*. Regarding eggs (*beitzim*): in both cases, they are *asurin*. The eggs are considered an integral part of the nest in a way that the mobile chicks are not, and therefore the prohibition of the nest extends to them.

Rav Ashi adds a further refinement: *ba'afruchin shetzerichin le'iman k'veitzim damu* — chicks that are still dependent on their mother are treated like eggs and are likewise *assur*. Only chicks capable of independent existence are fully permitted.

## Vessels & Images

### Destroying Vessels

The Gemara now begins a new *mishnah*. One who finds vessels (*keilim*) bearing an image of the sun (*tzurat chama*), an image of the moon (*tzurat levana*), or an image of a *drakon*, must carry them to the Dead Sea and cast them in: *yolichim l'yam hamelach*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a qualification: it is only vessels that are *mechubadin* — important or distinguished — that are *assur*. Vessels that are *mevuzin* — humble or contemptible in appearance — are *mutar*. The precise meaning of this distinction will receive further treatment in the Gemara.

### Create vs. Find

The Gemara asks: is the *mishnah* implying that only these three images — sun, moon, and dragon — are worshipped by non-Jews, and that all other images are not? But that cannot be right. A *baraita* from Chullin enumerates a long list of things for which one may slaughter as worship: the sea, rivers, the desert, the sun, the moon, the stars and constellations (*kochavim umazalot*), Michael the great ministering angel, and even the smallest worm (*shilshul katan*) — all of these are classified as *zivchei meitim*, as idol worship.

Abaye resolves this: *miflach l'chol d'meshkach palchi* — non-Jews worship anything they happen to find. Whatever is in front of them, they will potentially worship. But when it comes to objects that they actually craft and create for worship, it is specifically those three things — sun, moon, and dragon — that they manufacture and venerate. Other images, when made, are crafted *l'noi b'alma* — purely for decoration — not as objects of worship. This distinction between what one creates and what one finds will prove to be structurally important going forward.

### Rav Sheshet's Rules

Rav Sheshet compiled the relevant *chumrot* from various sources and taught them together in a single *baraita*. He establishes three rules. First: all celestial bodies (*mazalot*) are *mutar*, except for the sun and the moon (*chama u'levana*). Second: all faces (*partzufin*) are *mutar*, except for the face of a human being (*partzuf adam*). Third: all shapes and images are *mutar*, except for the shape of a *drakon*.

The scriptural basis for singling out the sun and moon appears to come from *Shoftim*, where *hashemesh ve'hayareach* — the sun and the moon — are specifically mentioned. The *drakon* and the human face carry their own reasons, which the *sugya* will develop.

### Making Idols Problem

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty with Rav Sheshet's first rule. If all celestial bodies other than the sun and the moon are *mutar*, in what context is this being said? If we are speaking of *making* such images, surely even making a representation of, say, a star or a constellation would be prohibited. The verse in *Shemot* states: *lo ta'asun iti elohei chesef ve'elohei zahav lo ta'asu lachem* — do not make alongside Me gods of silver or gold. And the Rabbinic interpretation of this verse is: *lo ta'asuni k'demut shimshai hamishmishin lefanai bamarom* — do not make images resembling My attendants who serve before Me on high. Those heavenly attendants include all the celestial bodies, not just the sun and the moon. Therefore Rav Sheshet cannot be speaking of a case where one is manufacturing these images.

### Finding vs. Making

The resolution is that Rav Sheshet's rules apply specifically to the case of *finding* such objects: *ella pshita bimotzei* — it is obvious that he is discussing one who finds an already-made vessel bearing one of these images. And the ruling aligns precisely with the *mishnah* we just read: *hamotzei keilim ve'aleihem tzurat chama tzurat levana tzurat drakon yolichim l'yam hamelach* — one who finds vessels bearing the images of the sun, the moon, or the *drakon* must cast them into the Dead Sea. Implicitly, other images found on vessels do not require this treatment. Rav Sheshet's three rules thus map onto the *finding* scenario: only the sun, the moon, the human face, and the *drakon* raise concern when found, while other celestial bodies, other faces, and other images, when merely found rather than crafted for worship, are *mutar*.

The Gemara is poised to analyze each of Rav Sheshet's three categories in turn — the celestial bodies, the faces, and the *drakon* — examining how each resolves into either the *finding* or the *making* framework. That analysis will continue in the next *amud*.
