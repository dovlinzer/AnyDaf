# Bava Metzia 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matna al Mah Shekatuv

### Waiving Ona'ah Rights

The daf begins by picking up from the bottom of 51a, where we left off after examining the foundational *pesukim* for *ona'ah* and the curious irony that the Torah's case involves land, yet *ona'ah* applies only to movable goods. One question raised there was whether a seller might be treated more leniently — considered more readily *mochel* — since, as a professional, he presumably knows his business. Building on that discussion, the Gemara now introduces a new scenario: *ha'omer l'chaveiro al m'nat she'ein lach alai ona'ah* — what if someone explicitly conditions a sale on the buyer waiving any *ona'ah* claim?

This condition can be understood in more than one way. On one reading, the buyer is essentially being *mochel* — agreeing in advance to absorb any overcharge, knowing that there may well be one. On another reading, the seller is attempting to declare that the Torah's laws of *ona'ah* simply do not govern this transaction at all. The second reading is the more radical one, since it amounts to *matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah* — stipulating against a Torah-mandated framework. Whether the condition is best understood as a personal waiver or as a structural negation of Torah law turns out to be the crux of the dispute that follows.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

*Rav amar yeish lo ona'ah* — Rav holds that the condition is ineffective and the laws of *ona'ah* apply regardless. *Shmuel amar ein lo alav ona'ah* — Shmuel holds that the condition works and the buyer has no *ona'ah* claim. The Gemara immediately asks whether this dispute simply mirrors the Tannaitic debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah.

### Rabbi Meir vs. Yehudah

The *baraita* cited involves a man who betroths a woman on condition that he bears no obligation for *she'er*, *kesut*, or *onah* — food, clothing, and conjugal rights. Rabbi Meir rules: the *kiddushin* is valid but the condition is void, since one cannot stipulate away Torah obligations. Rabbi Yehudah rules: *b'davar she'b'mamon tna'o kayam* — in monetary matters, the condition holds. The husband remains obligated in *onah*, which is not monetary, but he may legitimately waive *she'er* and *kesut*. The underlying principle of Rabbi Yehudah's position is that Torah law defines the default terms of interpersonal monetary arrangements, but the parties themselves may renegotiate those defaults — the Torah is setting the baseline, not an immutable floor.

### Rav: Knowledge Required

Rav, however, declines to align himself simply with Rabbi Meir. He says: *ana d'amri afilu l'Rabbi Yehudah* — even according to Rabbi Yehudah, the condition would fail here. The reason is *d'yad'ah u'machlah*: in the *kiddushin* case, the woman knows precisely what she is entitled to and is knowingly relinquishing it. That is a genuine, informed *mechilah*. But here, *mi yada d'machil?* — the buyer does not know the extent of any overcharge. He might assume the seller is taking five dollars more than the going rate; in reality, the seller might be gouging him by thousands. Without full knowledge of what right is being waived, there can be no valid waiver. The language of *mochel* is thus critical: Rav frames the issue not as whether Torah law can be contracted away, but whether a genuine *mechilah* has actually occurred — and a *mechilah* requires awareness of what one is giving up.

### Shmuel: Conditional Clai…

Shmuel argues the mirror image. *Ana d'amri afilu l'Rabbi Meir* — even according to Rabbi Meir, the condition would be valid here. In the *kiddushin* case, the man is directly contravening a Torah obligation: the Torah commands him to provide *she'er* and *kesut*, and he is saying he will not. That is a head-on negation of a Torah duty, which is why Rabbi Meir invalidates it. But in the case of *ona'ah*, the seller is not saying "the Torah's prohibition does not apply." He is saying: if it turns out that I am overcharging you, you will not demand your remedy. That is not a direct confrontation with the Torah's mandate — it is a contingent arrangement that only becomes relevant if a particular circumstance materializes. So even Rabbi Meir might agree that such a condition is acceptable.

### Ein Alai vs. Ein Bo

The Gemara then cites Rav Anan, who reports a clarification from Shmuel's school. There are two possible formulations: *al m'nat she'ein lach alai ona'ah* — "on condition that you have no *ona'ah* claim against me" — and *al m'nat she'ein bo ona'ah* — "on condition that there is no *ona'ah* in this transaction." The first formulation works according to Shmuel; the second does not.

Rashi explains the distinction as follows: the second formulation is a factual claim — "there is no overcharge here" — and since there in fact is an overcharge, the claim is simply false. A false factual premise cannot serve as a valid condition. The first formulation, by contrast, is a waiver of a claim, not an assertion of fact. One might note, however, that if a seller makes a false factual claim about price, the result should be *bitul mekach* — a voided transaction — rather than *ona'ah*, which makes the distinction feel less like a genuine *chidush*.

### Tosafos: Waiver Analysis

Tosafos offers a more conceptually compelling distinction. *Ein alai ona'ah* — "you have no *ona'ah* claim against me" — means the buyer is waiving his personal right to demand a remedy. The Torah's laws notionally apply, but the buyer has agreed not to invoke them. *Ein bo ona'ah* — "there is no *ona'ah* in this" — means the seller is declaring that the Torah's framework does not govern this sale at all. According to Tosafos, even Shmuel would agree that this second formulation fails: one cannot simply announce that the Torah's prohibitions are inapplicable. The permissible move is to accept the Torah's framework and then voluntarily forgo the remedy it provides — not to negate the framework itself. This reading aligns the *ein alai/ein bo* distinction directly with the debate over *matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah*.

### Baraita: Explicit Terms

A *baraita* is then cited in challenge. *Ha'nosei v'ha'nosen b'emunah* — someone who does business on trust — and one who stipulates *al m'nat she'ein lach alai ona'ah* has no *ona'ah* claim against him, the *baraita* rules. This presents a difficulty for Rav, who says the condition never works — and worse, Rav had claimed his position holds even according to Rabbi Yehudah. If a *baraita* affirms the condition's validity, and Rav insists it fails under all opinions, how does he account for this source?

Abaye resolves this cleanly: *machvarta* — the simplest reading is correct. Rav follows Rabbi Meir and Shmuel follows Rabbi Yehudah. This is a straightforward Tannaitic dispute over whether one can stipulate against Torah law. The *baraita* reflects the position of Rabbi Yehudah, and it is perfectly consistent for Rav, who holds like Rabbi Meir, to be in tension with it.

Rava, however, proposes a more ambitious reading that would allow Rav to agree with all Tannaitic opinions. The key, Rava argues, is the distinction between *bistam* and *bimfaresh* — an unexplicated condition versus an explicit one. Another *baraita* is introduced to support this: *bamei devarim amurim* — when do the laws of *ona'ah* apply regardless? When no specific condition is stated. *Aval bimfaresh* — but when the seller makes the situation explicit — for example, declaring: "I am selling you this item for 200, but you should know its going rate is 100" — and the buyer accepts, then *ein lo alav ona'ah*, the buyer has no *ona'ah* claim.

This makes sense even on Rav's own terms. Rav's objection was that without explicit disclosure, the buyer cannot truly be *mochel* because he does not know the extent of what he is waiving. But here, where everything is on the table — the overcharge, the fair market value, the terms of the deal — the buyer's agreement is fully informed. One could even argue, as the shiur notes, that this situation does not constitute *ona'ah* at all in the technical sense: *ona'ah* presupposes a buyer who believes he is paying a fair price. A buyer who knows the price is inflated and agrees anyway is not being deceived. The same logic applies symmetrically to the buyer's side: *lokhe'ach she'amar lamokher* — a buyer who tells the seller "I know this is worth 200 but I'm offering 100, and you should know I have no alternative buyer right now" — and the seller accepts, that seller too has no *ona'ah* claim. When everything is explicit and both parties proceed with full knowledge, the entire framework of *ona'ah* as a remedy for deception becomes conceptually inapplicable.

The Tosafos reading reinforces this: one cannot announce that the Torah's laws simply do not apply, but one can, with full disclosure, voluntarily decline to stand on the rights those laws confer.

### Implicit vs. Explicit

This leads to an important contemporary application that the shiur develops. The discussion of explicit pricing — "I know this is the market rate, but given the circumstances I am charging more, and you accept" — maps directly onto questions of price gouging. If a merchant is the last gas station for a hundred miles, or holds the only available batteries during a storm, the market rate may nominally be two dollars, but a willing buyer in a bind may pay ten. The Gemara's analysis suggests that when both parties fully understand the disparity and the buyer proceeds knowingly, the transaction can stand — not because Torah law is displaced, but because the informed consent transforms the nature of the transaction.

The question of how much this fits into the framework of *matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah* turns on precisely this point: explicit knowledge and genuine consent mean one is not contravening Torah law at all, only exercising the autonomy that the Torah's defaults leave room for when fully transparent.

## Nosei v'Noten

### Trust Definition

The Gemara now introduces the case of *ha'nosei v'ha'noten b'emunah* — conducting business on trust. Following Tosafos's reading, this refers to a transaction in which the seller does not fix a market price but instead discloses his actual expenses and charges the buyer a modest premium for profit. The buyer trusts the seller's accounting and pays accordingly.

### Consistent Methods

The *baraita* lays down an important constraint: *harei zeh lo yechashev et hara b'emunah ve'et hayafeh beshaveh* — one may not value the inferior goods by the trust method and the superior goods by their market value. Either both categories must be handled by the trust method, or both must be handled by market value. The two methods may not be mixed.

The practical illustration makes the principle clear. Suppose a merchant purchases ten apples wholesale for ten dollars — an average of one dollar each — but the lot comprises a mix of premium apples worth a dollar fifty and inferior apples worth fifty cents. When reselling, he cannot apply the trust method (cost price, one dollar each) to the inferior apples while using market value (a dollar fifty) for the premium ones. That would systematically favor the seller at every turn. The rule is consistency: either sell all apples at cost plus margin, or sell all apples at their individual market values.

### Profit Margins

A further question concerns which expenses the seller may count when operating under the trust method. The *baraita* permits him to include the cost of porters (*sechar katef*), camel drivers (*sechar gamal*), and innkeeping (*sechar pundak*) — all his out-of-pocket costs. What he may not charge for separately is his own personal labor: *sechar atzmo eino notel*. The reason, as the Gemara explains, is that his own compensation is already embedded in the arrangement. Rav Pappa clarifies this with reference to a particular type of dealer who, when purchasing a hundred units, receives four units gratis from the wholesaler. Those four units constitute the seller's personal profit: he sells the hundred units at cost to recover his expenses, and retains the four bonus units as his own compensation. The system is self-contained — his labor is already paid.

## Defective Coins

### Coin Depreciation

The Mishnah now shifts from *ona'ah* in wares to *ona'ah* in currency. In the ancient economy, coins were worth their weight in precious metal, making a worn or shaved coin genuinely worth less than its face value. The Mishnah asks: *kama tehe sela chasera velo yehe bah ona'ah* — how much may a *sela* be worn away before the recipient has a valid *ona'ah* complaint? The threshold here differs notably from the standard one-sixth rule that governs wares, as the Gemara will explain.

### Tannaim: Coin Standards

Three Tannaitic opinions are presented. Rabbi Meir: *arba issurot*, one *issar* per *dinar* — a ratio of one to twenty-four. To understand the arithmetic: a *sela* equals four *dinarim*, a *dinar* contains twenty-four *issurot* (each *dinar* comprising six *ma'ah*, each *ma'ah* two *pundyon*, each *pundyon* two *issar*). So one *issar* per *dinar* means a total of four *issurot* of depreciation are tolerable for the whole *sela*, representing a one-in-twenty-four deficiency. Rabbi Yehudah: *arba pundiyot*, one *pundyon* per *dinar* — a ratio of one to twelve. Rabbi Shimon: *shemonah pundiyot*, two *pundyon* per *dinar* — a ratio of one to six, which is the same as the standard *ona'ah* threshold for wares. Notably, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah set far more stringent thresholds than the standard one-sixth, while only Rabbi Shimon aligns coin depreciation with the ordinary *ona'ah* standard.

## Return Limits & Keilim

### Coins as Vessels

A Mishnah in tractate *Keilim* asks whether a coin that has been rendered invalid as currency and repurposed as a weight can become susceptible to *tumah* as a *keli*. The answer is yes — once the owner designates it as a vessel for weighing, it acquires the status of a functional implement and can become *tamei*. The discussion of how much depreciation renders this repurposing appropriate naturally connects back to our Mishnah's question of tolerable deficiency.

### City vs. Village

The Mishnah then addresses the time window within which a recipient may return a defective coin and demand a replacement. In cities (*krachim*), the standard is: *ad shekedei sheyireh leshulchani* — however long it takes to show the coin to a money changer (*shulchani*). In villages (*kfarim*), where no money changer is readily available, the window extends until *erev Shabbos*, when people gather in the marketplace.

### Recognition Exception

An additional provision follows: *im hayah makiran* — if the person who gave the coins recognizes them as the same coins he originally passed, then even after twelve months he ought to take them back. The Gemara will clarify that this is an act of *middat chasidus* rather than a legal obligation, and that the phrase *ein lo alav ela taromet* — he has only a complaint, not a legal claim — refers to the moral standing of the aggrieved party if the original passer refuses.

## Defective Coins (II)

### Coins vs. Wares

The Gemara now asks the central comparative question: *mai shna besela depalig u'mai shna betallis dela palig* — why is there a Tannaitic dispute over the tolerable depreciation level for coins, but no comparable debate about wares, where the standard is uniformly one-sixth? One answer, proposed by Rava, is that the earlier Mishnah dealing with wares reflects only the position of Rabbi Shimon — the one Tanna who sets the coin threshold at one-sixth, identical to the standard for wares. But this reading is strained, since a Mishnah presented without attribution (*stam*) is generally not assumed to represent a minority position.

### Abaye: Tolerance Levels

Abaye offers a more nuanced explanation rooted in human behavior. When it comes to garments and other goods, *ad shetus machil inish* — people are willing to tolerate some degree of overpayment. There is even a folk saying: *ashtik legabach* — overpay a little for what goes on your back — but *shavi lekarsach* — pay fair value for what goes in your stomach. The point, more broadly, is that people accept a modest premium when purchasing desirable goods. But coins are fundamentally different. *Keivan delo sagi leih* — once a coin is sufficiently degraded, it simply does not circulate. People refuse it. So the question with coins is not just that someone paid slightly more than fair value — it is that they received currency they cannot use. The inability to pass the coin further makes the loss concrete and non-negotiable in a way that a modest overpayment on a garment is not. That is why people are far less tolerant — *lo machil* — when it comes to defective coins, and why a much lower depreciation threshold triggers an *ona'ah* claim.

A question naturally arises here: at some point, is a badly depreciated coin not simply the wrong item — a case of *mekach ta'ut* rather than *ona'ah*? If a buyer receives a coin so worn that it functions differently from a proper coin, is he not receiving something categorically different from what he bargained for? The shiur acknowledges this as a sharp and important question, noting that the answer likely depends on the degree of depreciation and when, precisely, a coin ceases to be money at all.

### Rava: Currency Viability

Rava's explanation complements Abaye's. With wares — a garment, furniture, food — *kol inish kam lei b'gaveih*: any person has a reasonable sense of what things are worth and can consult a friend or neighbor to assess whether a price was fair. But with coins, *lav kol inish kam lei b'gaveih*: the average person cannot look at a coin and reliably detect that it is missing, say, one twenty-fourth of its proper weight. That level of assessment requires expertise. *Ela shulchani* — only the money changer has that expertise. This explains the city-versus-village distinction in the return window: in cities, a *shulchani* is available, so the window closes when one has had a reasonable opportunity to consult him. In villages, where no *shulchani* exists, the window lasts until *erev Shabbos*, when the marketplace opens and broader commercial contact becomes possible.

### Ona'ah Threshold

Having established the differing thresholds by Tannaitic opinion, the Gemara addresses an apparent contradiction between two sources. Our Mishnah asks *ad kama tehe hasela chasera velo yehe bah ona'ah* — how much deficiency before there is still *no* complaint — while a *baraita* asks *ad kama tehe hasela chasera veyehe bah ona'ah* — how much deficiency before there still *is* a complaint. Both cite the same figures. Rav Pappa resolves this: *tana didan kachashiv mimmata lemala* — our Tanna counts upward from zero, asking how much depreciation is still tolerable, and identifies the point at which it crosses into *ona'ah*. The *baraita* counts downward from the maximum, asking how much depreciation still generates an *ona'ah* claim, reaching the same threshold from above. One is traversing from below the line and one from above, but both agree that the one-twenty-fourth threshold (according to Rabbi Meir) is the point at which *ona'ah* begins.

## Coin vs. Ware Standards

### Different Applications

The Gemara's structural question — why a dispute exists over coin depreciation standards but not over the standard for wares — in fact subsumes two separate problems: why coins have a *lower* tolerable threshold than wares, and why the Mishnah treats the coin case as contested while the ware case appears uniform. Both Abaye and Rava address these in complementary ways.

### Abaye: Context Matters

Abaye's approach is to contextualize each Mishnah independently rather than harmonize them. The earlier Mishnah on wares was stated in the context of urban commerce and implicitly reflects that setting. Had the Mishnah on wares addressed village conditions, a similar distinction in time windows would have appeared. The apparent asymmetry is an artifact of context, not a substantive legal difference.

### Rava: Expertise Factor

Rava argues from principle: the governing factor is whether an ordinary person can evaluate the object in question without professional assistance. For wares, the answer is generally yes; for coins, no. This functional distinction — the need for expertise — explains both why coin standards differ from ware standards and why the time windows for returning defective coins depend on whether expert evaluation (the *shulchani*) is locally accessible.

## Coin Use Limits

### Metal Value Threshold

The Mishnah establishes further rules once a coin's depreciation exceeds the tolerable threshold for *ona'ah*. Once a coin has lost too much value to be used as currency — even at a discount — it may still be sold for its metal content: *yitro al ken mochrah bishviyah* — beyond this point, sell it at its actual weight value. This remains permissible as long as the degree of depreciation has not yet crossed a second, more severe threshold.

The question then becomes: how much may a coin depreciate before even holding it becomes problematic — before we fear that someone might fraudulently pass it off at a value it does not possess? *Ad kama tifchat veyehe rashai lekayemah* — how much can it be worn down and still be kept? For a *sela*: down to a *shekel* — that is, half its face value. For a *dinar*: down to a *rova*. This "quarter" will require clarification.

### Half-Value Rule

The rationale is that once a *sela* has depreciated below a *shekel* in value, Rashi explains, it may visually resemble a *shekel* even though it is worth less than a *shekel*. At that point, an unscrupulous person might pass it as a *shekel* when it does not even meet that standard. So long as it retains at least a *shekel*'s worth of metal, its true status is recognizable — it is clearly neither a proper *sela* nor a counterfeit *shekel*, and it can be sold transparently for its weight. Once it drops below that midpoint, the risk of misrepresentation becomes acute.

The same principle governs the *dinar*: *bedinar ad rova* — a *dinar* may be held until it reaches a *rova* of depreciation. As Abaye clarifies — and as Rava's philological observation confirms — the word *rova* here does not mean one-quarter of the *dinar*'s value but rather one-quarter of a *shekel*, which equals half a *dinar*. This makes the threshold identical for both coins: half-value in each case. Rava's proof is linguistic: the Mishnah says *rova* (a quarter of the *shekel*) rather than *revia* (the coin worth one-quarter of a *dinar*). Had the intent been to set the limit at one-quarter of the *dinar*'s value, it would have used the term *revia*. The choice of *rova shekel* signals that the reference point is the *shekel*, not the *dinar* — yielding a half-*dinar* threshold, consistent with the half-*sela* rule.

An additional Gemara observation notes that a *dinar* can in some cases originate from a *shekel* — a *shekel* that has worn down to the size of a *dinar*. Rabbi Ami uses this to distinguish two types of *dinar*-sized coins: a *dinar haba mishekel* — a worn *shekel* now the size of a *dinar* — may still be kept and used at the *dinar* level, since it has only declined to half its original value. A *dinar haba misela* — a *sela* that has worn all the way down to *dinar* size — is forbidden to keep, since it has lost more than half its original value and is susceptible to fraudulent misrepresentation.

## Disposal of Bad Coin (II)

### Anti-Fraud Rules

Once a coin has crossed the half-value threshold and can no longer be held or used even for its metal weight, a specific prohibition applies: *harei zeh lo yimkerenu lo letagar velo lecherem velo lehereg* — do not sell it to a merchant, do not use it to pay off a creditor seizing property, and do not use it to ransom one's life from a threatening assailant. (In true life-threatening situations one does what one must, but under ordinary circumstances the prohibition holds.) The reason the Gemara gives is stark: *mipnei shemeramin bah et acherim* — because these parties will use it to deceive others. A coin degraded below half its value but still resembling a higher denomination will be passed on as something it is not. Even an honest person cannot hold such a coin, precisely because it will inevitably pass into hands less scrupulous than his own.

### Destruction Methods

What, then, must be done with such a coin? The *baraita* presents several valid options: *yish'chok* — grind it to powder; *yituch* — melt it down; *yakutz* — cut it into pieces; *yolich l'yam hamelach* — cast it into the sea. The Mishnah had also mentioned making a hole in it and wearing it as a necklace around one's child's neck, and indeed archaeological finds suggest this practice was real. However, a separate *baraita* prohibits this, along with using the coin as a weight among one's standard weights and throwing it into one's scrap metal pile — any arrangement where the coin retains its shape creates the risk that it will be removed and misused.

Rabbi Elazar (cited also in the name of Rav Huna citing Rabbi Elazar) reconciles the two sources: *kan b'emtza kan min hatzad* — the permissible necklace-making involves piercing the coin through its center, which renders it clearly non-functional as currency. The prohibited scenario is piercing at the coin's edge, where the hole could be trimmed away and the coin returned to circulation.

## Coin Use Limits (II)

### Abaye vs. Rava

The Gemara re-engages the question of the intermediate range — coins that have depreciated beyond the *ona'ah* threshold but have not yet reached half their value. During this range, the coin cannot circulate as face-value currency but may still be sold for its metal weight. The *baraita* cited earlier had said *yoter al ken mochrah bishviyah* — beyond this point, sell it at its weight value. The question is whether coins in this intermediate range may be freely held or must be destroyed.

Rav Huna holds that both ranges — above and below the half-value threshold — require destruction; the only safe coin is one that has depreciated to exactly half its value (a *sela* now worth exactly a *shekel*, a *dinar* now worth exactly half a *dinar*). Levi disagrees: only below the half-value threshold must a coin be destroyed; in the intermediate range it may be retained and sold at weight. The Gemara challenges Rav Huna from the *baraita*'s language, which appears to permit selling in the intermediate range, and Rav Huna's resolution requires an extremely forced reading of that *baraita* — limiting it to a coin that fell into a fire and depreciated instantaneously, rather than gradually.

### Rova Terminology

The discussion of the *rova* — the threshold below which a *dinar* cannot be retained — required clarification because the same root generates two distinct terms with different meanings. *Rova* in this context means one-quarter of a *shekel*, which equals half a *dinar*. *Revia* refers to the actual coin denomination worth one-quarter of a *dinar*. The Mishnah's use of *rova* rather than *revia* establishes that the threshold is set relative to the *shekel*, not the *dinar*, and therefore represents half the *dinar*'s value — not one-quarter. This ensures the same half-value rule applies uniformly across coin denominations.

### Different Limits

The *baraita*'s phrase *pachat mikan issar assur* — once it goes an *issar* below this, it is forbidden — generates a Talmudic exchange about what exactly is being said. Abaye reads the phrase as a gloss on the *ona'ah* threshold from the earlier Mishnah: what is being said is that the tolerable threshold is four *issurot* per *sela* (one per *dinar*), and once the depreciation exceeds that by even one *issar* — reaching five *issurot* — the coin can no longer pass at face value. Rava objects: if the *ona'ah* threshold is four *issurot*, then anything beyond four *issurot*, however tiny the excess, triggers the prohibition. There is no need or room to speak of one additional *issar*. The phrase *afilu mashehu nami* — even the slightest excess — would logically suffice. Rather, Rava reads the phrase as a restatement of the Mishnah's ruling: *pichas sela issar ledinar assur* — if a *sela* has lost one *issar* per *dinar* of its value (four *issurot* total), it can no longer be used as currency. This is simply the Mishnah's rule restated in the *baraita*, presented as the standard ruling — *ve-stam k'Rabbi Meir* — following Rabbi Meir's position.

## Disposal of Bad Coin (II)

### Piercing Method

As noted above, the resolution of the apparent contradiction between the Mishnah's permission to perforate a coin and make it a necklace and the *baraita*'s prohibition on doing so turns entirely on where the hole is made. A hole through the center of the coin permanently disfigures it as currency, rendering it unambiguously ornamental and non-negotiable. A hole at the edge, however, is superficial enough that an unscrupulous person might clip away the perforated section and return the coin to circulation. The prohibition targets precisely that risk.

## Taromet vs. Obligation

### Middat Chasidus

The Mishnah's final provision states that if the person who passed the defective coins recognizes them as his own, he should accept them back even after twelve months. The Gemara asks how this can be reconciled with the earlier time limits — in the city, until one can show the coin to a money changer; in a village, until *erev Shabbos*. Once those windows have closed, there is no legal obligation to accept a return. Rav Chisda explains: *middat chasidus shanu kan* — the twelve-month rule describes pious conduct, not legal obligation. The right thing to do, when one recognizes one's own coins, is to take them back and make the recipient whole. But this is a standard of *chasidus*, not a legally enforceable duty.

### Moral vs. Legal

This framing leads to a question about the phrase *ein lo alav ela taromet* — "he has only a complaint, not a legal claim." Who has the complaint, and against whom? If the original passer takes the coins back as an act of *chasidus*, there is nothing for anyone to complain about. If he refuses, the aggrieved party has a moral grievance but no legal recourse. The *taromet* thus describes the standing of the person who received the bad coins: he may be legitimately aggrieved, he may protest and complain, but he has no legal mechanism to compel the return.

### Upset But No Claim

The Gemara works through the precise reading. The case described is one in which the original passer refuses to take the coins back after the legal window has closed. The person who holds the bad coins can genuinely be upset — the conduct is not ideal, and the person who originally gave him bad coins and now refuses to remedy the situation is acting poorly. The language of *taromet* captures exactly this: a well-founded moral complaint, carrying social and perhaps reputational weight, but not a legally actionable grievance. The distinction between what the law demands and what decency requires is one the Talmud marks carefully, and the category of *middat chasidus* is precisely the space between them — where the law ends and the higher standard begins.
