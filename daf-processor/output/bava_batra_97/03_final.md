# Bava Batra 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lees & Batches

### Lees Taste Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מֵרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: שְׁמָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן טַעַם יַיִן, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ חַמְרָא הוּא? קִיּוּהָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak asked Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin:** If one steeps **pomace** and produces *tamad* **that has the taste of wine, what is** the blessing that one should recite before drinking it? Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin **said to him: Do you hold** that such a beverage **is wine? It is merely a sharp-flavored** beverage, not wine.

The daf opens with a question posed by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak to Rav Chiya bar Avin: *shemarim she-yesh bahem ta'am yayin* — lees, the grape skins, seeds, and residue left after pressing, that still carry the taste of wine when water is added to them. What is the *halakha*? Presumably the underlying concern is whether one may recite *borei pri ha-gafen* over the resulting liquid, though as the *gemara* will show, related questions about *terumot u-ma'asrot* and the status of *matzah* are also in the background.

Rav Chiya bar Avin's response is almost dismissive: *mi savret chamra?* — Do you really think that is wine? *Kiyu be'alma hu* — it is nothing more than some sharp, acidulated flavor, not real wine in any meaningful sense. The *Rashbam* clarifies that this exchange concerns the case where one added water and extracted the same volume as was put in — not the case of adding three units and taking out four, which was discussed on the previous daf. When the output merely equals the input, everyone would agree the liquid is not wine, which explains Rav Chiya bar Avin's almost exaggerated dismissiveness.

A natural question arises here: why does the flavor of the grape in the water not create a *ta'am ke-ikar* problem? Normally, if a prohibited substance is tasted in a mixture, the mixture itself carries that prohibition. *Tosafot* raises precisely this point. He asks: if the water carries the taste of *terumah* grapes, should a non-*kohen* not be forbidden from drinking it on the basis that the taste of *terumah* is present? His answer is that the taste given off by the lees is *kiyu be'alma* — not a true transfer of the substance itself, but something closer to an ambient flavor without real substance. He draws on the language of the *gemara*: *ein chan nosein ta'am*, *u-k'afra be'alma dami* — there is no real flavor here, it is like dust. The idea is that the grape residue imparts a superficial sensation rather than an actual transfer of the thing itself.

This is admittedly a striking and somewhat puzzling position. Normally the whole framework of *ta'am ke-ikar* — the principle that taste is legally equivalent to the substance itself — would apply precisely to cases like this. The analogy *Tosafot* seems to be drawing is to the principle of *rei'a lav milta hi* — that smell does not constitute legal transfer — but even that analogy is imperfect, since here the flavor did originate from actual grapes. The *gemara* never fully resolves the tension, and even *Tosafot* leaves it somewhat open. What is clear is that the *gemara* treats the question of "is this wine?" as its operative frame, rather than "does this water contain traces of a prohibited substance?"

### Terumah Lees Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁמָרִים שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה – רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי אָסוּר, וּשְׁלִישִׁי מוּתָּר. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף שְׁלִישִׁי, בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* concerning the production of successive batches of *tamad* of decreasing strengths by reusing the pomace after each time a *tamad* is produced: With regard to **pomace of *teruma*** wine, the **first and second** products are considered to be *teruma*, and it is **prohibited** for a non-priest to drink it. **But** with regard to **the third** product, a non-priest **is permitted** to drink it. **Rabbi Meir says: Even** with regard to the **third** product, if the wine that seeps out of the pomace **imparts** the **flavor** of wine to the water, it is forbidden to a non-priest.

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita*: *shemarim shel terumah* — lees belonging to *terumah* grapes. If a *kohen* pressed *terumah* grapes, made wine, and then added water to the remaining lees to produce *temed* (grape-infused water), does that *temed* carry the *kedusha* of *terumah*? The *baraita* rules: *rishon ve-sheini assur, u-v'shlishi mutar* — the first and second batches of water added to those *terumah* lees are still considered *terumah* and may not be consumed by a non-*kohen*; only from the third batch onward is the liquid permitted. Rabbi Meir disagrees: *af shlishi nosein ta'am* — even the third batch, if it still imparts a wine taste, retains the *terumah* status.

The *Rashbam* notes that all of these rulings are understood as *chumrot* — rabbinic stringencies — since in principle, water added in equal quantity to what is extracted is not wine at all. The different thresholds for the first, second, and third batches reflect varying degrees of strictness depending on the type of prohibition or sanctity involved.

### Tumah Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר – רִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר, שֵׁנִי מוּתָּר. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף שֵׁנִי, בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם. וְשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – שְׁלִישִׁי אָסוּר, וּרְבִיעִי מוּתָּר. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף רְבִיעִי, בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** with regard to water added to pomace **of** second**-tithe** wine, the **first** product is also considered to be second tithe, and it is **prohibited** to drink it outside Jerusalem. **But** with regard to **the second** product, it **is permitted** to drink it anywhere. **Rabbi Meir says: Even** with regard to the **second** product, if the wine that seeps out of the pomace **imparts** the **flavor** of wine to the water, it may be consumed only in Jerusalem. **And** with regard to water added to pomace **of** wine that was **consecrated** to the Temple, up to the **third** product it is **prohibited** to derive any benefit from it, as it is considered to be consecrated, **but** from **the fourth** product, it **is permitted. Rabbi Meir says: Even** with regard to the **fourth** product, if the wine that seeps out of the pomace **imparts** the **flavor** of wine to the water, it is prohibited.

The structure of these rulings directly mirrors the graduated hierarchy of *tumah*. People and vessels can become a *rishon le-tumah* but go no further; food can become a *sheini*; *terumah* food can become a *shlishi*; and *kodesh* food can become a *revi'i*. The parallel to the *temed* rulings is immediately apparent: the first and second derivative batches of *terumah* lees water are still treated as *terumah* (analogous to the *shlishi* status that *terumah* food can reach), while *kodesh* — being one degree more stringent — extends its reach one step further. The *gemara* itself does not draw this comparison explicitly, but it lies clearly on the surface of the discussion.

### Rabbi Meir Tastes

Rabbi Meir's position, the *gemara* makes clear, is internally coherent. He does not need to call the water "wine" — he merely observes that if the taste of *terumah* or *hekdesh* is present in the water, that trace is legally significant. In this respect, Rabbi Meir's reasoning aligns naturally with the general principle of *ta'am ke-ikar*. Yet, as we saw above, the *gemara* does not ultimately adopt this reasoning, and the *Rashbam* treats even the earlier batches as mere *chumrot* rather than as legally mandated restrictions.

### Kodesh & Demai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר, וְשֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר – לְעוֹלָם מוּתָּר. קַשְׁיָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ אַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, קַשְׁיָא מַעֲשֵׂר אַמַּעֲשֵׂר! הֶקְדֵּשׁ אַהֶקְדֵּשׁ לָא קַשְׁיָא – כָּאן בִּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, כָּאן בִּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים. מַעֲשֵׂר אַמַּעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא – כָּאן בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וַדַּאי, כָּאן בְּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a different *baraita*: *Tamad* produced from the pomace **of** wine that was **consecrated** to the Temple is **always forbidden,** even after having steeped them many times, **and** the beverage produced from pomace **of** second **tithe is always permitted,** even from the first such product. The Gemara explains: The ruling concerning **consecrated** pomace is **difficult,** as it is contradicted by the ruling of the first *baraita* concerning **consecrated** pomace. And the ruling concerning second-**tithe** pomace is **difficult,** as it is contradicted by the ruling of the first *baraita* concerning second-**tithe** pomace. The Gemara answers: The contradiction between the ruling of one *baraita* concerning **consecrated** pomace and the ruling of the other *baraita* concerning **consecrated** pomace is **not difficult,** as one can explain that **here,** the second *baraita* is referring to pomace **with inherent sanctity,** and **there,** the first *baraita* is referring to pomace **with sanctity** that inheres in its **value.** Also, the contradiction between the ruling of one *baraita* concerning pomace of second **tithe** and the ruling of the other *baraita* concerning pomace of second **tithe** is **not difficult,** as one can explain that **here,** the first *baraita* is referring to pomace whose status as second **tithe** is **certain** and **there,** the second *baraita* is referring to pomace **of** second **tithe** of **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*].**

The *gemara* then introduces a contradicting *baraita*: *shel hekdesh le-olam assur* — water derived from *hekdesh* lees is always forbidden, no matter how many batches; *u-shel ma'aser le-olam mutar* — and water derived from *ma'aser sheni* lees is always permitted, even the first batch. This directly contradicts the earlier *baraita* on both counts.

The *gemara* resolves both contradictions. On *hekdesh*: *kan bi-kdushat ha-guf, kan bi-kdushat damim* — one *baraita* speaks of *kedushat ha-guf*, where the grapes themselves are sanctified for use on the *mizbe'ach* as part of the *korban* wine offerings, a weighty and intrinsic sanctity; the other speaks of *kedushat damim*, where the grapes were donated merely for their monetary value to the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, which carries a lesser degree of sanctity. The more intrinsic sanctity of *kedushat ha-guf* generates an unlimited stringency; *kedushat damim* follows the more limited three-batch rule.

On *ma'aser*: *kan be-ma'aser vadai, kan be-ma'aser demai* — one *baraita* refers to grain that is definitely untithed *ma'aser sheni*, where the first batch remains forbidden; the other refers to *demai*, produce purchased from *amei ha-aretz* who may or may not have already separated *ma'asrot*. Since the obligation to tithe *demai* is only rabbinic in nature — because it is merely doubtful whether tithes were taken — even the first batch is not a problem.

## Machshir Liquids

### Wine/Water Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְעִנְיַן אִיסּוּרָן, כָּךְ אָמְרוּ לְעִנְיַן הֶכְשֵׁירָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak:** In the **same way in which** the Sages **said** concerning differing strengths of *tamad* that **with regard to their prohibition,** after a certain number of times the *tamad* produced is not considered to be wine, **so** too **they said** the same rulings **with regard to their capacity to render** foods **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

The *gemara* cites a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: just as we debated the *issur* status of the various batches of *temed* — whether each derivative batch still carries the status of *terumah*, *ma'aser sheni*, or *hekdesh* — so too the same gradations apply *le-inyan hechsheran*, regarding the capacity of these liquids to render food susceptible to *tumah*.

The background *halakha* is as follows. Once fruit has been picked from a tree, it can become *tamei*, but only after it has been wetted by one of seven designated liquids, wine and water among them. The contact with liquid is called *hechsher*, and it renders the food *mekabel tumah*. The question now is: when this *temed* — grapey water of varying derivative batches — contacts food, does it function as a *machshir*?

### Rain Water Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶכְשֵׁירָן דְּמַאי? אִי דְּמַיָּא – אַכְשׁוֹרֵי מַכְשְׁרִי! אִי דְּחַמְרָא – אַכְשׁוֹרֵי מַכְשְׁרִי! לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁתִּמְּדוֹ בְּמֵי גְשָׁמִים. וְכֵיוָן דְּקָא שָׁקֵיל וְרָמֵי לְהוּ לְמָנָא – אַחְשְׁבִינְהוּ! לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁנִּתַּמֵּד מֵאֵלָיו. וְכֵיוָן דְּקָא נָגֵיד – קַמָּא קַמָּא אַחְשְׁבִינְהוּ! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּפָרָה שֶׁשּׁוֹתָה רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When the *baraita* is referring to **their capacity to render** other foods **susceptible** to ritual impurity, why does it matter **which** kind of beverage the *tamad* is considered to be? **Whether** the *tamad* is regarded **as water** it can **render** food **susceptible** to impurity, or **whether** it is regarded as **wine it** can **render** food **susceptible** to impurity. The Gemara clarifies: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case in **which one produced *tamad* with rainwater** that he had not previously intended to use. Rainwater does not render food susceptible to ritual impurity, so the *tamad* will do so only if it is regarded as wine. The Gemara challenges this: **But since he took** the rainwater **and poured it into a container** holding the grape pomace, **he has** thereby **intended it** for a use. Even if the resulting *tamad* is regarded as water, such rainwater renders food susceptible to ritual impurity. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the pomace **produced *tamad* by itself,** having been steeped in water that happened to fall upon it. The Gemara persists: The *baraita* above states that from the third product, the *tamad* is regarded as water, which the Gemara has explained is referring to rainwater that one did not intend to use and that consequently cannot render food susceptible to impurity. The Gemara asks: **But since he draws off** each subsequent lot of *tamad* that is produced, **one by one,** in order to allow more rainwater to fall onto the pomace and produce more *tamad*, **he** thereby demonstrates his **intent to** use the rainwater. Therefore, even if the *tamad* is regarded as water, it should render food susceptible to impurity. The Gemara answers: **Rav Pappa says** that this is a case **of a cow that drank** the lots of *tamad*, **one by one,** and some inadvertently dripped from the cow's mouth onto food. Since no person intended to use the *tamad*, if it is regarded as water it will not render food susceptible to impurity.

The *gemara* immediately dismisses the apparent significance of the distinction: *ei damya, achshurei machshiri; ei de-chamra, achshurei machshiri* — whether we classify it as water or as wine makes no difference, since both water and wine are among the seven liquids that render food susceptible. So why does the classification matter at all?

The *gemara* explains: the case in question is where rainwater was used — *she-temado be-mei geshamim*. Rainwater has a special *halakha*: it does not function as a *machshir* until the owner demonstrates a desire for it, a concept expressed as *le-ratzon*, willingness. This *halakhic* requirement of *ratzon* does not apply to wine. Wine renders food susceptible to *tumah* regardless of whether anyone wanted it to fall on the food — even if grape juice accidentally dripped onto an apple lying on the ground, that apple is now *mekabel tumah*.

Water, by contrast, requires that the owner or user have shown some intention or desire for that liquid. So if the early batches of *temed* count as wine, they *machshir* automatically; if the later batches are merely water, they only *machshir* when *ratzon* is demonstrated. This is why the wine-or-water classification remains significant even when both are technically on the list of *machshir* liquids.

*Tosafot* is puzzled by the *gemara*'s assumption here, noting that we do not find elsewhere that the *le-ratzon* requirement applies differently to wine versus water. The *gemara* nonetheless works within this framework.

The *gemara* then asks: if the rainwater fell into your bucket and you poured it into the barrel, have you not already demonstrated *ratzon* — *kivan de-ka shakil ve-rami lei le-mana, achshivei* — the act of taking and placing it shows you want the water? So the *gemara* narrows the case further: *lo tzricha she-nitmad me-eila* — the rain fell directly into the open barrel, without any human action whatsoever.

But again the *gemara* challenges: when you tip the barrel and extract the *temed*, does that not demonstrate your desire for it? *De-kivan de-ka nagid kama kama, achshivei* — the very act of extracting it batch by batch shows you want it.

### Rav Papa Intent

Rav Papa resolves all this: the case involves a cow that came and drank directly from the barrel — *be-para she-shota rishon rishon*. The rain fell in on its own, and a cow came and drank the first batch. Then rain fell again and the cow drank again, and so on. In this scenario, no human being took any action at any point. Everything occurred through entirely natural processes: the rain fell without human initiative, and the cow drank without human direction. The human owner never demonstrated any *ratzon* for the water at any stage.

Consequently, the early batches — classified as wine — render food susceptible to *tumah* even without demonstrated desire. The later batches, once they have degraded to the status of mere water, would only *machshir* if the owner showed some affirmative desire for them. Since no such desire was ever expressed, those later batches do not function as a *machshir*.

The *Rashbam* frames the entire discussion as one of *chumrot*: in principle, since the volume extracted equals the volume inserted, none of this liquid is really wine, and all these distinctions among batches are rabbinic stringencies layered on top of what is fundamentally just water.

Before moving on, *Tosafot* draws a practical application from this discussion. *Tosafot* notes that Rabbeinu Tam ruled that if a non-Jew washed out a barrel of wine lees, there is no *stam yeinam* concern, since — *dilav chamra hu, ela ke-maya be'alma hu* — the liquid in question is not wine but merely water-like liquid, and therefore the non-Jew's contact with it raises no issue of *stam yeinam*.

## Kiddush Standards

### Altar Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: אֵין אוֹמְרִים קִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם, אֶלָּא עַל הַיַּיִן הָרָאוּי לִינָּסֵךְ עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zutra bar Toviyya says** that **Rav says: One may recite the sanctification of the** Shabbat **day only over wine** of a quality that is **fit to be poured** as a libation **upon the altar.**

The *gemara* now transitions to one of the most practically significant rulings on the daf. Rav Zutra bar Tuvia said in the name of Rav: *ein omrim kiddush ha-yom ella al ha-yayin ha-rauy le-nasech al gabei ha-mizbe'ach* — one may recite *kiddush* only on wine that is fit to be poured as a libation alongside a *korban* on the *mizbe'ach*.

Before turning to the *halakhic* translation of this principle, it is worth pausing over the analogy itself. The *Rashbam* explains simply that the comparison is meant to convey a quality standard: just as everything brought as a *korban* must be aesthetically fine and top quality — as the *pasuk* in Malachi says, *Hakrivehu na le-fachatecha, ha-yirtzecha o ha-yissa fanecha?* would you offer this to a distinguished person and expect it to find favor? — so too, wine for *kiddush* must meet a genuine standard of quality. But the analogy carries deeper resonance than mere quality control. In Temple times, sacred eating took place in the context of the *Beit ha-Mikdash* — through *terumah*, through *ma'aser sheni* consumed in Jerusalem, through *korbanot* in which the offerer participated or at minimum supplied the food for the *mizbe'ach* and the *kohanim*. Post-*churban*, the framework of sacred eating is sustained through *brachot*, through washing before bread (which parallels washing before *terumah*), and through *kiddush*, which frames the entire *Shabbat* meal — a point reinforced by the rule of *ein kiddush ella bi-mkom seudah*. The comparison to *nesachim* is therefore not merely stylistic; it situates *kiddush* within a broader theology of sanctified consumption.

### Unfermented Wine OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי יַיִן מִגִּתּוֹ, וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: יַיִן מִגִּתּוֹ לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר; וְכֵיוָן דְּאִם הֵבִיא כָּשֵׁר, אֲנַן אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי! דְּאָמַר רָבָא: סוֹחֵט אָדָם אֶשְׁכּוֹל שֶׁל עֲנָבִים, וְאוֹמֵר עָלָיו קִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This statement is said **to exclude what? If we say** it is **to exclude** the use of **wine** fresh **from one's press,** i.e., grape juice, which has not yet fermented, that is difficult. **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach: One may not bring wine** fresh **from his press** as a libation *ab initio*, **but if one brought** it as a libation, **it is valid** after the fact. **And since if one brought** it as a libation **it is valid** after the fact, **we** should **also** be able to use it for the sanctification of the Shabbat day, **even *ab initio*. As Rava says: A person may squeeze** the juice from **a cluster of grapes and** then **recite the sanctification of the** Shabbat **day over it.**

The *gemara* now works through a series of candidates for what the ruling excludes. The first candidate is *yayin mi-gito* — wine taken straight from the pressing floor before fermentation, what we would call unfermented wine or fresh grape juice. The *Rashbam* notes that such wine is problematic for the *mizbe'ach* because the *pasuk* says *hasech nesech shechar* — the libation wine must be *shechar*, intoxicating, and hence must be fermented.

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Chiya: *yayin mi-gito lo yavi* — one should not bring unfermented wine *lechatchila*; *ve-im hevi kasher* — but if one did bring it, it is valid *bedieved*. The *gemara* immediately draws the implication for *kiddush*: *kivan de-im hevi kasher* — since it is *bedieved* acceptable on the *mizbe'ach*, then *anan afilu lechatchila nami* — for our purposes, it is acceptable even *lechatchila* for *kiddush*. The principle the *gemara* establishes here is critical: we do not import *lechatchila* standards of the *mizbe'ach* into our requirements for *kiddush*. What is *pasul bedieved* on the *mizbe'ach* becomes *pasul* for *kiddush*; what is merely not ideal *lechatchila* on the *mizbe'ach* is perfectly acceptable *lechatchila* for us.

Rava goes further: *socheit adam eshkol shel anavim ve-omer alav kiddush ha-yom* — one may squeeze a cluster of grapes on *erev Shabbat* and recite *kiddush* on the fresh juice. (Squeezing on *Shabbat* itself would involve a *melachah* and is not recommended.) This confirms that unfermented wine is acceptable *lechatchila* for *kiddush*.

### Bedieved Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי מִפִּיהָ וּמִשּׁוּלֶיהָ? וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מִפִּיהָ וּמִשּׁוּלֶיהָ לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav's statement is **to exclude** wine that is taken **from** the **mouth of** the barrel, which has scum floating in it, **and** wine taken **from** the **bottom of** the barrel, which contains the pomace. The Gemara challenges this: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach: One may not bring** wine that is taken **from** the **mouth of** the barrel **or from** the **bottom of** the barrel as a libation *ab initio*, **but if one brought** it as a libation **it is valid** after the fact? Accordingly, it should be fit for use for the sanctification of the Shabbat day.

The same logic applies to a second candidate: wine drawn from the top or bottom of the barrel. The top may have accumulated dust and the bottom has lees mixed in, making both inferior in quality. Again, Rabbi Chiya rules: *mipeha u-mishuleha lo yavi ve-im hevi kasher* — not ideal *lechatchila*, but *bedieved* valid on the *mizbe'ach*. And by the same principle: *bedieved* acceptable on the *mizbe'ach* means *lechatchila* acceptable for *kiddush*.

The *gemara* thus establishes its operative rule: the comparison to the *mizbe'ach* functions as a floor, not a ceiling. Only that which is *pasul bedieved* on the *mizbe'ach* is excluded for *kiddush*; that which is merely less than ideal on the *mizbe'ach* is wholly acceptable for *kiddush*.

### Defective Wines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי יַיִן כּוּשִׁי; בּוֹרֵק; הֵילִיסְטוֹן; שֶׁל מַרְתֵּף; שֶׁל צִמּוּקִים? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: בְּכוּלָּן לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav's statement is **to exclude black wine, *borek*** wine, **sweet wine [*heiliston*],** wine **from** the **cellar,** and wine made **from raisins.** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **With regard to all of** these types of wine, **one may not bring** them as a libation *ab initio*, **but if one brought** one of them as a libation **it is valid** after the fact. Accordingly, it should be fit for use for the sanctification of the Shabbat day.

The *gemara* now lists a series of wines that are *bedieved* invalid for the *mizbe'ach*, and therefore presumably excluded from *kiddush* as well. *Yayin Kushi* — wine that is black. *Borak* — wine that is very light or white, presumably not from white grapes but wine that has lost its color and turned pale. *Hiliston* — an unusually sweet wine; the *Rashbam* explains this as wine that has been exposed to sun and concentrated in sugar, and indeed the word appears to derive from the Greek *helios* (sun), as the process involves hanging grapes in the sun to increase their sugar content. *Shemartef* — average wines drawn from the cellar, some of which may already be *yayin koses*, wine that is beginning to sour. And *she-tzimukim* — raisin wine.

All of these, a *baraita* teaches, fall into the category of *bedieved* acceptable: *be-kulam lo yavi ve-im hevi kasher* — one should not bring them *lechatchila*, but if one did they are valid. By the *gemara*'s operative principle, this means they are acceptable *lechatchila* for *kiddush*.

### Mixed Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי יַיִן קוֹסֵס; מָזוּג; מְגוּלֶּה; וְשֶׁל שְׁמָרִים; וְשֶׁרֵיחוֹ רַע – דְּתַנְיָא: בְּכוּלָּן לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל. לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִי לְמַעוֹטֵי קוֹסֵס, פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הִיא! אִי לְמַעוֹטֵי מָזוּג, עַלּוֹיֵי עַלְּיֵיהּ – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּכוֹס שֶׁל בְּרָכָה, שֶׁאֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav's statement serves **to exclude souring wine, diluted** wine, wine that has been left **uncovered,** as there is a concern that a snake may have injected its venom into it, **and** wine made **from** grape **pomace, and** wine that has a **foul odor. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **With regard to all of** these types of wine, **one may not bring** them as a libation, **and if one brought** one of them as a libation, **it is disqualified.** The Gemara asks again: Rav's statement serves **to exclude which** of these types of wine? **If** one suggests Rav intended **to exclude souring** wine, that cannot be, as the status of wine that tastes like wine but has the odor of vinegar **is the** subject of a **dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** (96a). **If** one suggests that Rav's statement serves **to exclude diluted** wine, why would such wine be disqualified for the sanctification of the Shabbat day? Diluting wine **is an improvement of it, as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** Even though the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer and hold that over undiluted wine one recites the blessing: Who created the fruit of the vine, nevertheless the **Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer with regard to a cup** used **for a blessing,** such as the cup of wine over which Grace after Meals is recited, **that one does not recite** the **blessing over it until he adds water to it** to make it palatable.

The *gemara* then turns to *mazug* — wine mixed with water. On the *mizbe'ach*, this is clearly invalid, learned from the *pasuk* in *Sifrei*: *ve-nisko revi'it ha-hin yayin* — the entire *revi'it ha-hin* must be wine, not wine diluted with water. Mixed wine is *pasul bedieved* for the *mizbe'ach*. Yet applying the *gemara*'s operative rule here leads to an unexpected result: if mixed wine is *pasul bedieved* on the *mizbe'ach*, one might conclude it is *pasul* for *kiddush* as well.

But the *gemara* rejects this. *Iluyei ma'ali* — mixing wine with water is actually an improvement for human consumption, not a degradation. Indeed, the *gemara* cites Rav Yossi be-Rabbi Chanina: *modim Chachamim le-Rabbi Eliezer be-kos shel berachah she-ein mevarachin alav ad she-yitnu le-tocho mayim* — even the *Chachamim*, who debate Rabbi Eliezer about whether unmixed (*chai*) wine requires *borei pri ha-gafen*, agree that for a *kos shel berachah*, for *kiddush*, wine must be mixed with water. So far from being excluded, mixed wine appears to be the *lechatchila* standard for *kiddush*. This makes the altar comparison inapplicable here; what degrades wine for the *mizbe'ach* enhances it for human sacred use.

This observation also bears on contemporary practice: we recite *kiddush* today on straight, unmixed wine, which would imply that standards of what counts as wine for human consumption have shifted, and the rules about mixing have become societally dependent.

### Bad Smell Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לְמַעוֹטֵי מְגוּלֶּה, סַכָּנָה הִיא! אִי לְמַעוֹטֵי שֶׁל שְׁמָרִים, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּרְמָא תְּלָתָא וַאֲתָא אַרְבְּעָה, חַמְרָא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא! אִי דִּרְמָא תְּלָתָא וַאֲתָא תְּלָתָא וּפַלְגָא, פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּנַן וַאֲחֵרִים הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **If** one suggests that Rav's statement serves **to exclude** wine that has been left **uncovered,** it would be unnecessary for Rav to teach this, because such wine, which **is dangerous** to drink, as a snake may have injected its venom into it, is already forbidden. **If** one suggests that Rav's statement serves **to exclude** wine made **from pomace, what are the circumstances? If** it is referring to a case **where one poured three** jugs of water over the pomace **and** the volume of the resulting beverage **came to four** jugs, that **is** considered **full-fledged wine,** which may certainly be used for the sanctification of the Shabbat day. **If** it is referring to **where one poured three** jugs of water over the pomace, **and** the resulting beverage **came to three and a half** jugs, this **is the** subject of a **dispute** between **the Rabbis and *Aḥerim*,** and the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, who rule that it is not regarded as wine.

Several other candidates are raised and set aside. *Megulah* — wine that was left exposed, with the concern that a snake may have drunk from it and introduced venom. The *gemara* says this exclusion needs no derivation from the altar: it is simply a life-threatening risk, *sakanah*, and one may not drink it regardless of *kiddush*. However, the *gemara* adds: if one passed the wine through a sieve to remove the poison, it is *halakhically* permitted to drink (*ke-Rav Nechemia*), yet it remains invalid for the *mizbe'ach* — *hei, hakrivehu na le-fachatecha* — one would not bring this to a dignitary — and therefore it remains invalid for *kiddush* as well. *She-shel shemarim* — water from lees — is dismissed because either it has enough grape content to be full wine, in which case there is no issue, or it is merely winey water, in which case one does not even recite *borei pri ha-gafen*, making the *kiddush* question moot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי שֶׁרֵיחוֹ רַע. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לְמַעוֹטֵי מְגוּלֶּה – וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּעַבְּרֵיהּ בִּמְסַנֶּנֶת כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי – ״הַקְרִיבֵהוּ נָא לְפֶחָתֶךָ הֲיִרְצְךָ אוֹ הֲיִשָּׂא פָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav's statement serves **to exclude** wine **that** has **a foul odor. And if you wish, say** that **actually** his statement serves **to exclude** wine that has been left **uncovered, and** it teaches the novelty that **even though one passes it through a sieve, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya** (see *Terumot* 8:7), **nevertheless,** it still may not be used for the sanctification of the Shabbat day. This is because it is disrespectful to use inferior wine for a mitzva, as derived from the verse in which God rebukes the Jewish people for offering lame animals as offerings: **"Present it now unto your governor; will he be pleased with you or will he accept your person?** says the Lord of hosts" (Malachi 1:8).

The *gemara* ultimately arrives at the key exclusion: *she-reicho ra* — wine with a bad smell. This wine is still *halakhically* considered wine — one recites *borei pri ha-gafen* over it — but it is *pasul bedieved* on the *mizbe'ach*, and therefore it is invalid for *kiddush*. This is the primary case the ruling comes to exclude: a wine that has degraded sufficiently to have an off smell, not merely vinegary (*koses*) but genuinely foul-smelling, such that one would not present it to anyone of standing, is disqualified for *kiddush* even though it still carries a *bracha*.

The distinction between *yayin koses* — souring wine — and *yayin she-reicho ra* — wine with a bad smell — is worth noting, even if the *gemara* does not fully elaborate it. *Koses* refers to a wine in the process of turning to vinegar, with a vinegary odor; *reicho ra* seems to refer to an independent category of off-smelling wine that need not be on the path to vinegar. The *gemara*'s earlier discussion of *yayin koses* in relation to the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — whether souring wine is still *halakhically* wine — is somewhat puzzling in this context. The *gemara* implies that if Rabbi Yochanan considers it *yayin*, it would automatically be acceptable for *kiddush* as well, which is not self-evident; *Rashbam* addresses this but acknowledges the difficulty.

## Modern Issues

### Grape Juice Status

The *gemara*'s discussion of *yayin mi-gito* — unfermented wine — and the *Rashbam*'s analysis of *yayin koses* together frame the contemporary question of grape juice for *kiddush*. The *Rashbam*, commenting on *yayin koses*, writes: *hu d'lo havi shechara ve-lo ra'ui li-hiyot shechara* — it is problematic because it is not intoxicating and is incapable of ever becoming intoxicating, as opposed to *yayin mi-gito* — *de-sofo mei-ha li-hiyot shechara* — which, though presently unfermented, is capable of fermenting. The *Rashbam* thus draws a conceptual distinction between unfermented wine that *could* ferment and a liquid that *cannot* ferment.

This distinction maps directly onto the contemporary debate about commercially produced grape juice. The *gemara* permits fresh-pressed unfermented wine for *kiddush lechatchila*; Rava even permits squeezing a cluster of grapes on *erev Shabbat* and making *kiddush* directly on the result. But commercial grape juice is treated — pasteurized, de-aerated, and processed — in ways that render it incapable of fermentation. Two positions emerge from the *poskim*: some hold that since the liquid emerged from grapes, it acquired the *halakhic* status of wine at the moment of pressing, and subsequent treatment does not strip it of that status; others hold that since the liquid can never ferment, it does not qualify as wine under the standard of *shechar*, and perhaps one should not even recite *borei pri ha-gafen* over it.

A distinct category is the product sold in Israel as *mitz anavim tiv'i*, which appears to be genuinely untreated grape juice — pressed under airtight conditions without exposure to air, without pasteurization, transported and sealed without fermentation-inhibiting additives. This product may correspond precisely to the *yayin mi-gito* of the *gemara*, and its status is correspondingly less contested.

In practice, it is worth emphasizing that a *kiddush* table should always include a grape juice option, as for individuals dealing with alcoholism even small quantities of wine present serious health concerns that override the preference for fermented wine.

### Mevushal Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא חֲמוּהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מֵרָבָא: חֲמַר חִוַּורְיָין, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן כִּי יִתְאַדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana, father-in-law of Rav Mesharshiyya, asked Rava:** With regard to using **white wine** for libations and for the sanctification of the Shabbat day, **what is** the *halakha*? **He said to him** in response that the verse states: **"Do not look upon the wine when it is red,** when it gives its color in the cup, when it glides down smoothly" (Proverbs 23:31), which indicates that red wine is considered to be of a superior quality.

*Tosafot* raises a sharp question about *yayin mevushal* — cooked wine, which constitutes the overwhelming majority of commercially produced *kosher* wine today. *Tosafot* notes: *ein le-hochi'ach mikan de-yayin mevushal kasher le-kiddush u-mevarechin alav borei pri ha-gafen* — one cannot prove from the *gemara*'s silence that *mevushal* wine is acceptable for *kiddush* and that *borei pri ha-gafen* is recited over it. The *gemara*'s failure to list *mevushal* among the excluded wines does not mean it is included; it may have been omitted because the *gemara* assumed one does not even recite *borei pri ha-gafen* over cooked wine, so it would never arise as a candidate for *kiddush*. Cooking degrades the quality of wine, and as the previous daf discussed, degrading the character of something can cost it its *bracha*.

However, *Tosafot* then brings a proof from the Yerushalmi in *Shekalim* that *yayin mevushal* is considered *yayin gamur* — full-fledged wine — and that one does recite *borei pri ha-gafen* and may use it for *kiddush*. The question is thus live but resolved in the direction of leniency by *Tosafot*.

### Rambam Strict

The *Rambam* in *Hilchot Kiddush* (chapter 29, *halakha* 14) takes the most stringent position on the standards required for *kiddush* wine. He writes: *ein mekadeshin ella al yayin ha-rauy le-nasech agav ha-mizbe'ach* — one may recite *kiddush* only on wine fit for libation on the *mizbe'ach*. Therefore, if honey or leaven (*se'or*) was mixed into it — even a single drop into a large barrel — *ein mekadeshin alav*, it is invalid for *kiddush*. The force of this ruling becomes clear when one recognizes that honey and leaven are among the substances explicitly forbidden on the *mizbe'ach*; even a trace disqualifies the wine from altar use, and by extension from *kiddush*.

Some *acharonim* read the *Rambam* more broadly, as if any additive whatsoever invalidates the wine. But the plain reading of the *Rambam*, as Dov correctly observed in the course of the shiur, is that *se'or* and *dvash* are specifically forbidden items on the *mizbe'ach*, and the *Rambam* is applying that specific disqualification to *kiddush*, not establishing a general rule against all additives.

The *Rambam* then records a dissenting position: the rule that wine must be fit for the *mizbe'ach* was stated only *le-hotzi yayin she-reicho ra o meguleh o mevushal* — to exclude wine that smells bad, wine that was exposed, or *mevushal* wine. But adding honey or sweeteners does not fall within these exclusions and should be permitted. The *Rambam* states that in the entire West this second ruling (*kach anu morin be-khol ha-ma'arav*) is the accepted practice, attributing it broadly while apparently expressing some personal view alongside it.

### SA Lenient

The *Shulchan Aruch* (Orach Chaim 272) rules in accordance with the more lenient position: *mekadeshin al yayin mevushal ve-al yayin she-yesh bo dvash* — one may make *kiddush* on *mevushal* wine and on wine containing honey. A dissenting opinion (*yesh omrim*) holds one may not. The *Rama* records the custom: *ve-ha-minhag le-kadesh alav afilu yesh lo yayin acher* — the accepted practice is to make *kiddush* on it even when other wine is available. This represents the *halakhic* consensus for Ashkenazic practice.

What emerges from the *Rambam*'s formulation is that even the more lenient position he records still excludes *mevushal* wine. The *Shulchan Aruch*'s explicit ruling to the contrary thus constitutes a departure from both positions in the *Rambam* and aligns with *Tosafot*'s conclusion from the Yerushalmi.

### Ratio Debate

A final contemporary question concerns the wine-to-water ratio in *mazug* — mixed wine. The *gemara* throughout this discussion works with a three-to-one ratio of water to wine: three parts water added, four parts extracted, is the threshold for something to qualify as wine rather than winey water. Everything in the *gemara*'s analysis seems to assume that more than three parts water to one part wine produces a liquid that is no longer wine.

Yet the *Rama* rules in the *halachot* of *borei pri ha-gafen* that a six-to-one ratio — six parts water to one part wine — remains valid. The *Aruch ha-Shulchan* expresses bewilderment at this ruling, observing that the *gemara* clearly implies three-to-one as the outer limit. His explanation is that the *Rama* may have derived the six-to-one standard from the *halakhot* of *stam yeinam*: non-Jewish wine becomes *batel* — nullified — in a liquid mixture at a ratio of one-to-six, rather than the usual one-to-sixty required for *bittul*. This lower threshold in *stam yeinam* law implies that even a one-to-six proportion of wine in a liquid is considered significant and wine-like. The *Rama* appears to have transposed this ratio, concluding that societal standards of what counts as wine may vary, and in communities where a six-to-one mixture is still considered wine, it may be used for *kiddush* and for *borei pri ha-gafen*. The *gemara*'s three-to-one standard thus represents its own cultural context rather than an absolute, context-independent threshold.
