# Nazir 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shaving Ritual: Process

### Metzora/Nazir Shaving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁתִּגְלַחַת הַנֶּגַע וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught **that the shaving of leprosy** overrides the shaving of a nazirite only when he is a confirmed leper.

Daf 61a opens a new *perek* on the heels of a fascinating discussion about one who is a *safek nazir, safek metzora* — a person uncertain whether he is a *nazir* or a *metzora* — who must bring all the various *korbanot* associated with each status. The *mishna* at the top of the daf turns to a case of certainty: where a person is definitively both a *metzora* and a *nazir*, the shaving required for *tzaraat* is performed even though he is a *nazir*. This is the *chidush* learned from the *pesukim* — the phrase *rosho*, appearing in connection with the *nazir*, teaches that the *metzora*'s shaving is performed even if he currently bears the *nazir*'s hair. The elaborate thirty-day waiting gaps encountered in previous discussions arose only because of uncertainty regarding the *metzora* status; once that status is certain, the shaving proceeds without complication.

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָנֵי אַרְבַּע תִּגְלָחִיּוֹת דְּקָאָמַר, מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה, אוֹ מִשּׁוּם אַעְבּוֹרֵי שְׂעַר טוּמְאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma: Those four shavings about which** the *tanna* **spoke,** the four that a nazirite performs for his leprosy and impurity, are they all **due to** the **mitzva** of shaving, **or** are some of them not performed for the sake of a mitzva, but **for the sake of removing hair** that grew in **impurity,** so that other hair can grow?

The *gemara* opens with a question posed by Rami bar Chama: regarding the four shavings — the two belonging to the *metzora* and the two belonging to the *nazir* (*nazir tamei* and the regular *nazir* completing his term) — *tanei arba tiglahat*, are these shavings required *mishum mitzvah*, as a formal ritual act, or *mishum avorei se'ar*, merely for the purpose of removing the hair?

This is a classic *chakira*: is the requirement about the process, or is it about the end result? The question is framed around whether a particular ritual must be carried out in a prescribed manner, or whether all that matters is achieving the outcome — getting the hair removed — regardless of how one gets there.

### Shechita/Milah Analogy

*Tosafot Rid* raises a parallel question that illuminates the distinction. The *gemara* entertains the possibility that a non-Jew could serve as a *mohel*, yet takes it as obvious that if a non-Jew performs *shechitah*, the animal is a *neveilah*. Why the asymmetry between two acts of cutting?

The answer, as *Tosafot Rid* explains, is that *shechitah* is fundamentally about the *process*. There are five distinct laws governing how the cut must be performed — no pressure, no notch in the knife, and so forth — such that if any of these conditions is violated, the *shechitah* is invalid even if the trachea and esophagus are severed. *Milah*, by contrast, is about the *end result*. Nowhere in the *gemara* is there a description of *how* circumcision must be performed — what instrument must be used, what procedures must be followed. What is specified is what must be *achieved*: the *orlah* must be removed, and *priah* — the pulling back of the thin membrane beneath the *orlah* — must be performed. But the process itself carries no formal specifications. If the requirement is about process, then who performs it and how matters greatly. If it is only about achieving the end result, there is considerably more flexibility.

### Depilatory vs. Razor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְעַבּוֹרֵי בְּנָשָׁא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה — לְעַבּוֹרֵי בְּנָשָׁא לָא, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם אַעְבּוֹרֵי שְׂעַר טוּמְאָה — אֲפִילּוּ סַכְיֵהּ נָשָׁא נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the **difference** whether he shaves for one reason or another? The Gemara explains: It is relevant with regard **to removing** hair **with a depilatory. If you say** that the reason is **due to a mitzva, removing with a depilatory** is **not** an option, as the mitzva is specifically to shave. **But if you say** the reason is **due to the removal of hair of impurity, even if he rubs it with a depilatory** that is **also** effective.

The practical *nafka minah* of Rami bar Chama's question is whether the hair could be removed using a *sam* — a depilatory — rather than a razor. If the shaving is a formal ritual, *l'avorei benashta lo*: a depilatory does not suffice. If it is merely about hair removal, *afilu sachi nashta nami*: even a depilatory would fulfill the requirement.

One might note that earlier *gemaras* in the tractate explicitly derived from *pesukim* that a *ta'ar* — a razor — is required for the other shavings. *Tosafot* points out, however, that the derivation was not equally clear regarding the shaving of the *nazir tamei*. The *ta'ar* was not learned out there with the same force. It is therefore the shaving of the *nazir tamei* that is the real locus of the question.

One can understand why the doubt arises specifically there. The *metzora*'s shaving takes place before *tevilah* in the *mikvah* and has the character of a ritual associated with *taharah*. The shaving at the conclusion of a regular *nezirut*, following the *korban*, similarly carries the character of a formal ritual act. But the *nazir tamei* has already become *tahor* the day prior; the shaving that follows is not part of a *taharah* process. One might therefore reason that this shaving serves a different purpose altogether — not a ritual, but simply the removal of hair that became defiled during the *tumah*, so that the *nazir* can begin growing new, *tahor* hair. Perhaps no razor is required; perhaps any means of removal will do.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: וּמְגַלֵּחַ אַרְבַּע תִּגְלָחִיּוֹת. אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ מִשּׁוּם עַבּוֹרֵי שְׂעַר טוּמְאָה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁלֹשׁ נָמֵי סַגְיָא לֵיהּ! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** then is the *halakha*? **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:1): **And he shaves** with **four** acts of **shaving.** Now **if it should enter your mind** that the reason is **for the sake of removing hair of impurity, even three** shavings should **be enough for him,** two for his leprosy, and the last one for his naziriteship of purity. Since the third shaving is performed only to remove his hair of impurity so that he can start his pure naziriteship afresh, why is it included? **Conclude from** the *baraita* that all four shavings are **due to** the **mitzva** of shaving. The Gemara says: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that this is the case.

Rava resolves the question by invoking the *mishna* itself: *ta sha'ma, umigaleach arba tiglahat* — four shavings. If it were merely a matter of removing *tamei* hair, *afilu beshalosh nami sagi lei* — three shavings would suffice. The *mishna* groups all four shavings together in a single category. Since three of those four are unambiguously ritual shavings requiring a razor, the fact that the *mishna* speaks of *four* — categorizing all of them together — demonstrates that the fourth is no different. As *Tosafot* explains, the *mishna*'s decision to count them together as a unified set of four, rather than treating three as ritual shavings and the fourth as mere hair removal, is itself the *limud* that all are equally ritual acts requiring a razor.

The *gemara* thus concludes: all four shavings constitute a *mitzvah*, requiring a razor as a formal ritual instrument. Even the shaving of the *nazir tamei* is not merely *avorei se'ar* — it is not about removing hair by whatever means — but is a ritual act in its own right.

With that conclusion, the *perek* of *Shenei Nezirim* draws to a close.

## Non-Jews/Nezirut

### Hakutim Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגּוֹיִם אֵין לָהֶם נְזִירוּת. נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים יֵשׁ לָהֶם נְזִירוּת. חוֹמֶר בַּנָּשִׁים מִבָּעֲבָדִים, שֶׁהוּא כּוֹפֶה אֶת עַבְדּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ כּוֹפֶה אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Gentiles do not have naziriteship,** i.e., the *halakhot* of naziriteship do not apply to gentiles. They are not subject to the prohibitions of a nazirite, nor does one accept their offerings at the end of naziriteship. However, **women and** Canaanite **slaves do have naziriteship.** The mishna adds: There is a greater **stringency in** the case of **women than in** the case of **slaves, as** a master **may force his slave** to drink wine, shave his hair, or become ritually impure from a corpse, despite the slave's vow of naziriteship, **but** a husband **cannot force his wife** to transgress her naziriteship.

The tractate now moves into its final *perek*, titled in many printed editions *Hakutim* — the Samaritans. The Samaritans (*Kutim*) were a population settled in the land of Israel by the Babylonian king following the exile, replacing the indigenous Jewish population. As the biblical narrative recounts, these settlers initially continued worshipping their own gods, until lions attacked them; they were then instructed that the land belonged to the God of Israel and that they must worship Him. They proceeded to adopt some form of Jewish practice, maintaining a *Sefer Torah* and observing certain *mitzvot*. By the period of the *Bayis Sheni*, when Jews returned from *Bavel*, the Samaritans were an established presence, and the question of their status — whether their conversion was genuine or merely motivated by fear, making them *geirei arayos* rather than *geirei emes* — is debated throughout the Talmud among the *Tannaim*.

However, a student's observation here is apt and important: in many manuscripts and printed editions, the word *Kutim* in this tractate is a product of medieval censorship. The original text uses *ha-goyim* — non-Jews. This is confirmed upon examining the *gemara* itself, where the discussion concerns non-Jews generally, with no analysis of the particular status of Samaritans. The self-censorship of the *Talmud* by Jewish communities in the Middle Ages — undertaken to preempt the Church's censorship and the burning of *Talmud* volumes — operated on several levels. The mildest substitution was *akum* (*ovdei kochavim*, star-worshippers) in place of *goyim*, presenting the text as referring to ancient pagans rather than contemporary Christians. More sensitive passages employed *Kuti*, and the most charged formulations were replaced with *Kena'ani*. An example of the latter is the phrase *gazel Kena'ani* in *Bava Kama*, which in the *Rishonim* reads *gazel goy*. Here in the opening of this *perek*, the heading *Hakutim* is indeed a substitution for *ha-goyim*, and the *gemara*'s internal discussion consistently refers to non-Jews as *ovdei kochavim* — itself another layer of the same self-censorship.

One student offered an intriguing additional observation: the Church may have found the denial of *nezirut* to non-Jews particularly offensive given Christianity's own tradition of asceticism and monastic self-denial, which bears a resemblance to *nezirut*. The suggestion that the Church might have occluded any discussion denying non-Jews a share in this form of sanctity adds an interesting dimension to the censorship question.

### Non-Jews Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָתָנֵי: הַגּוֹיִם אֵין לָהֶם נְזִירוּת. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — וְלֹא לַגּוֹיִם. ״וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵיהֶם״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעֲבָדִים. לְמָה לִי קְרָא? הָאָמְרַתְּ: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה חַיֶּיבֶת בָּהּ — עֶבֶד חַיָּיב בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna **teaches** that **gentiles do not have naziriteship,** whereas women and slaves can be nazirites. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught,** that the beginning of the passage about naziriteship, which states: **"Speak to the children of Israel"** (Numbers 6:2), serves to emphasize that these *halakhot* apply to Jews, **but not to the gentiles.** Furthermore, the continuation of the verse: **"And say to them,** when a man or woman shall clearly utter a vow," serves **to include slaves.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a verse** to include slaves? **Haven't you said** the following principle: **Any mitzva that a woman is obligated in** its performance, **a slave is** also **obligated in** its performance? Since the *halakhot* of naziriteship apply to a woman, they should likewise apply to slaves.

The *mishna* states: *ha-goyim ein lahem nezirut* — non-Jews do not have *nezirut*. *Nashim v'avadim yeish lahem nezirut* — women and slaves, however, do have *nezirut*. This is worth pausing over. Throughout the tractate, the *mishnayot* and *beraitot* have repeatedly assumed that women can be *nezirot*, as the *pasuk* itself states *ish o isha ki yafli lindor neder nazir* — "a man or a woman who takes a *nazir* vow." Women are not in the category typically excluded; they are explicitly included in the *parashah*. It is non-Jews who are singled out here as excluded.

### Women/Slaves Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״לֶאְסֹר אִסָּר עַל נַפְשׁוֹ״, בְּמִי שֶׁנַּפְשׁוֹ קְנוּיָה לוֹ. יָצָא עֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵין נַפְשׁוֹ קְנוּיָה לוֹ. הוֹאִיל וְאֵין נַפְשׁוֹ קְנוּיָה לוֹ, אֵימָא גַּבֵּי נָזִיר נָמֵי לָא. קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Here** it **is different, as** with regard to vows **the verse states: "To bind his soul with a bond"** (Numbers 30:3), and the Sages expounded that this is referring only **to one whose soul is in his possession,** i.e., who is under his own jurisdiction. This **excludes a slave, whose soul is not in his possession,** but who is under his master's control. The verse from Leviticus is necessary, lest you say that **since** a slave's **soul is not in his possession,** one should **say** that **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **a nazirite as well,** he **cannot** undertake this vow notwithstanding the principle that slaves have similar *halakhot* to women with regard to obligations, who can become nazirites. The aforementioned verse **teaches us** that a slave can in fact take a vow of naziriteship.

Having established that both women and slaves have *nezirut*, the *mishna* notes a stringency that applies to women but not to slaves: *she'hu kofeis avdo* — a master can force his slave in the matter of his *nezirut*, meaning he can effectively override his slave's *nezirut* and prevent him from maintaining it. *V'eino kofeis ishto* — a husband, however, cannot force his wife. This is notable because one might expect a husband to have the right to annul his wife's *nedarim*, particularly those touching on their relationship. Whether the *nezirut* restrictions — abstention from wine, prohibition from becoming *tamei* to the dead — constitute *devarim she'beino l'veina* such that the husband could annul them is an open question; the prohibition on cutting hair might theoretically fall within that category since it affects appearance, yet the *mishna* says he cannot override it.

The pseudo-Rashi on this passage clarifies: if a slave took a *nazir* vow, his master may force him to drink wine — i.e., to violate the *nezirut*. But if a wife took a *nazir* vow and the husband did not annul it in time, he can no longer nullify it, and he certainly cannot force her to violate it. The slave's *nezirut* remains revocable at the master's discretion; the wife's, once the window for annulment passes, becomes fully binding.

## Bnei Yisrael Source

### Bnei Yisrael Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְלֹא לַגּוֹיִם. וְכׇל הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב ״יִשְׂרָאֵל״, גּוֹיִם לָא? וְהָא גַּבֵּי עֲרָכִין, דִּכְתִיב ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְתַנְיָא: בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מַעֲרִיכִין, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם מַעֲרִיכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the earlier exposition of the verse. **The Master said** in the *baraita* that the verse specifies: **"Speak to the children of Israel," but not to the gentiles.** The Gemara asks: **And anywhere that "Israel" is written,** are **gentiles not** included in that verse? **But** there is a counterexample in the verse written **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **valuations, as it is written: "Speak to the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 27:2), **and it was taught** in a *baraita*: **The children of Israel can take a valuation vow but gentiles cannot take a valuation vow.** If a gentile declares: I undertake to donate the value of so-and-so, his vow does not take effect.

The *gemara* asks: *minan hani mili* — from where do we know that non-Jews have no *nezirut*? The *baraita* answers: *Daber el Bnei Yisrael* — the Torah says "speak to the *Bnei Yisrael*," excluding non-Jews. Before returning to the exclusion of non-Jews, the *gemara* asks why the *baraita* also needs a verse to *include* slaves, since ordinarily slaves are obligated in all *mitzvot* that women are obligated in. Rava answers: one might have thought that the phrase *le-esor issar al nafsho* — "to bind a restriction upon oneself" — applies only to *b'mi she-nafsho kenuyah lo*, one who fully owns himself and can direct his own conduct. A slave, whose person is legally the property of his master, might not be in a position to impose binding restrictions upon himself. Therefore the *baraita* needs *ve'amarta aleihem* to include slaves — expanding beyond the *Bnei Yisrael* framework to encompass them. Curiously, this framing suggests that while a slave may not be capable of making an ordinary *neder* binding, his *nezirut* can nonetheless take effect — though his master may override it — which is itself a remarkable position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ נֶעֱרָכִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that this means that gentiles **cannot be** the subject **of a valuation** either, i.e., if a Jew says: I am obligated to give the value of so-and-so the gentile, his vow would not take effect. Therefore, **the verse states** the inclusive expression: "When **a man** shall clearly utter a vow of persons to the Lord, according to your valuation" (Leviticus 27:2), to teach that in this regard, every "man," even a gentile, is included in *halakhot* of valuations. Inasmuch as the Torah also states with regard to a nazirite: "When a man or woman shall clearly utter a vow, the vow of a nazirite" (Numbers 6:2), perhaps one should include gentiles in the *halakhot* of naziriteship as well.

Returning to the exclusion of non-Jews, the *gemara* notes that the phrase is *Daber el Bnei Yisrael*, excluding non-Jews. But *Daber el Bnei Yisrael ve'amarta aleihem ish o isha ki yafli lindor neder nazir* — the same passage also says *ish*, "a person," which is a broader inclusive term. By *erchin*, the *gemara* observes a parallel dynamic: *Daber el Bnei Yisrael* excludes non-Jews from being *ma'arich* — the one who declares another's value — yet *talmud lomar ish* comes back to include non-Jews in the category of *ne'erach*, the one whose value is declared. There is thus a "middle position" in *erchin*: non-Jews can be *ne'erach* but not *ma'arich*. One might ask whether the same logic applies to *nezirut*: *Bnei Yisrael* partially excludes non-Jews, but *ish o isha* partially includes them, yielding some middle status.

### Erchin Half-Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לְאָבִיו וּלְאִמּוֹ לֹא יִטַּמָּא״, בְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אָב, יָצָא גּוֹי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אָב. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְעִנְיַן יְרוּשָּׁה, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גּוֹי יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אָבִיו דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יְרֻשָּׁה לְעֵשָׂו נָתַתִּי אֶת הַר שֵׂעִיר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here,** in the case of naziriteship, it **is different, as the verse states: "For his father, or for his mother,** for his brother, or for his sister, **he shall not defile himself"** (Numbers 6:7). From here it is derived that this mitzva applies only **to one who has a father.** This **excludes a gentile, who does not have a father.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what** *halakha* does a gentile lack a father? **If we say** it is **with regard to inheritance, but didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A gentile inherits** the estate of **his father by Torah law, as it is stated: "Because I have given Mount Seir to Esau as an inheritance"** (Deuteronomy 2:5)?

The question is therefore sharpened: by *erchin*, we have a verse that generates a genuine half-status — non-Jews are inside the *parashah* as objects of valuation (*ne'erach*) even though they are outside it as active participants (*ma'arich*). Why can't we say the same about *nezirut*? *Bnei Yisrael* restricts their full participation; *ish o isha* might still grant them some partial entry into the *parashah*. What that middle position would look like is not immediately obvious — perhaps they observe *nezirut* without bringing a *korban*, or perhaps they have some attenuated form of the status — but the structural question is whether any partial inclusion is possible.

The *gemara* responds: *shani hacha d'amar kra* — this case is different, because the verse says *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* — the *nazir* may not become *tamei* to his father and mother. This locates the *nazir*'s laws within a framework of someone who has *tumah* — someone who *can* become *tamei*. From here the *gemara* begins its analysis of whether non-Jews fall within that framework at all.

## Tumah/Tahara Link

### Father Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמּוּזְהָר עַל כִּיבּוּד אָבִיו. מִי כְּתִיב ״כַּבֵּד אָבִיךָ״ גַּבֵּי נָזִיר? אֶלָּא: אָמַר קְרָא ״לְאָבִיו וּלְאִמּוֹ לֹא יִטַּמָּא״ — בְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ טוּמְאָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this mitzva of naziriteship applies **to one who is warned concerning the honor of his father,** and as the mitzva to honor one's father does not apply to a gentile, it is as though he has no father. The Gemara asks: **Is it written: Honor your father, in the context of a nazirite?** What is the connection between these two issues? **Rather, the verse states** with regard to a nazirite: **"For his father, or for his mother,** for his brother, or for his sister, **he shall not defile himself"** (Numbers 6:7), and it should be understood as referring **to one who has** the potential to become **ritually impure,**

The first attempt at reading *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* as an exclusion of non-Jews focuses on the word *aviv* — "his father." Perhaps non-Jews have no legally recognized father, such that the verse simply cannot apply to them. This is rejected immediately: *Rabbi Chiya bar Avin said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan* that a non-Jew does inherit from his father by Torah law, as the verse states *ki yerusha l'Esav natati et Har Se'ir* — "for I gave Mount *Se'ir* as an inheritance to *Esav*." The Torah itself recognizes the paternal line among non-Jews as legally meaningful for purposes of inheritance. The claim that non-Jews have no legal father is therefore untenable.

The *gemara* then asks whether the exclusion might derive from the *mitzvah* of *kibbud av* — perhaps the verse means to speak only about someone who has an obligation of honoring a father. Non-Jews are not obligated in *kibbud av*. The *gemara* rejects this as well: *mi ketiv kibbud avicha, kaveid avicha* by *nazir*? The *nazir* verses say nothing about *kibbud av*; they simply say father and mother. Moreover, the logic of being *mitamei* to relatives is not limited to parents — a regular *kohen* becomes *tamei* to siblings and children as well, where there is no *kibbud* relationship involved. Whatever the conceptual foundation of this category, it is not *kibbud av* per se.

It is worth noting that the idea of being *mitamei* to relatives is conceptually linked to *avelut* — it is from the *kohen*'s obligation to become *tamei* to the seven *krovim* that the obligation of *avelut* itself is derived, at least according to those who hold *avelut* is *d'Oraita*. So while *kibbud av* may be the most concrete expression of a child's responsibility toward a parent, the *gemara*'s category here is the broader one of relational obligation to *krovim*, and that does not map neatly onto *kibbud* alone.

### No Tumah = No Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא אָמְוּן שֶׁאֵין לוֹ טוּמְאָה. מְנָלַן דְּלֵית לְהוּ טוּמְאָה? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִטְמָא וְלֹא יִתְחַטָּא וְנִכְרְתָה הַנֶּפֶשׁ הָהִיא מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״, בְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קָהָל, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קָהָל.
>
> **Translation:** which **excludes a gentile, who has no** potential to become **ritually impure.** A gentile is not rendered impure even if he touches a corpse. An individual of this kind cannot become a nazirite. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** gentiles **do not have** the ability to become **ritually impure?** It is **as the verse states** with regard to one who enters the Temple in a state of impurity: **"But the man who shall be impure and shall not purify himself, that soul shall be cut off from the midst of the assembly"** (Numbers 19:20). This indicates that the *halakhot* of ritual impurity apply only **to one who has** membership in the **assembly** of the Jewish people and **excludes this** gentile, **who has no** membership in the **assembly,** i.e., he is not part of the Jewish people.

The *gemara* then arrives at its key insight: *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* speaks only *b'mi she-yesh lo tumah* — about someone who has the capacity to become *tamei*. *Yatza akum she-ein lo tumah* — a non-Jew is excluded because a non-Jew does not become *tamei*. Since *tumah* is the organizing concept of the *nazir*'s prohibition to his parents — you cannot be prohibited from contracting *tumah* if *tumah* does not apply to you — non-Jews are categorically outside this *parashah*.

This is a striking *gemara*. It is not that the *halacha* lacks practical implications regarding non-Jews and *tumah*; quite the contrary. If a non-Jew touches a dead body and a Jew then touches him, determining the *Jew's* status requires knowing whether the non-Jew contracted *tumah* in the first place. These statuses matter even when they are not part of the non-Jew's own legal obligations.

### Parah Adumah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא כָּרֵת הוּא דְּלָא מִיחַיַּיב, אֲבָל אִיטַּמּוֹיֵי מִיטַמּוּ? אָמַר קְרָא ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּמֵא״, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ טׇהֳרָה — יֵשׁ לוֹ טוּמְאָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ טׇהֳרָה — אֵין לוֹ טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it derived that this verse teaches that ritual impurity does not apply to a gentile at all? **Perhaps** the verse **is** referring merely to **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*],** i.e., it is teaching **that he is not liable** to receive *karet* for entering the Temple when impure, **but** that perhaps a gentile **does become impure.** The Gemara answers that **the** previous **verse states: "And the pure** person **shall sprinkle upon the impure";** this indicates that **anyone who has** the possibility of attaining **ritual purity** by means of the waters of purification also **has** the potential for **ritual impurity, and anyone who does not have** the possibility of attaining **purity** by means of the waters of purification **does not have** the potential for **impurity** either.

The *gemara* asks: *minayan dileis lehu tumah* — from where do we know that non-Jews have no *tumah*? The answer is a verse concerning the *parah adumah*: *veha'ish asher yitma velo yitchata venichreta hanefesh ha'hi mitoch hakahal* — a person who becomes *tamei* and does not undergo the *chitat* (sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes) and then enters the *Mikdash* or eats *kodshim* will be cut off *mitoch hakahal*, from the community. The word *kahal* refers specifically to *Klal Yisrael*. *Yatza zeh she'ein lo kahal* — one who has no share in *kahal*, i.e., a non-Jew, is excluded.

### Tahara-Tumah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: טׇהֳרָה הוּא דְּלָא הָוְיָא לֵיהּ, טוּמְאָה הָוְיָא לֵיהּ! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִטְמָא וְלֹא יִתְחַטָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one can **say** that **it is** only **purity** by means of the waters of purification **that he does not have,** but **he does have** the potential for **impurity.** The Gemara answers: It is for this reason that **the verse states: "But the man who shall be impure and shall not purify himself"** (Numbers 19:20), to teach that these two statuses are interdependent. One who cannot be purified by the waters of purification cannot become impure in the first place.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps all this verse teaches is that a non-Jew is not subject to *karet* for entering the *Mikdash* while *tamei* — *dilma kares hu d'lo mechayev* — but perhaps a non-Jew nonetheless *does* contract *tumah*. The *gemara* responds by citing a second verse: *vehizah hatahor al hatamei* — the *tahor* person sprinkles upon the *tamei* person. From here: *kol she'yesh lo taharah yesh lo tumah, vekol she'ein lo taharah ein lo tumah* — whoever has *taharah* has *tumah*, and whoever lacks *taharah* lacks *tumah*. Since a non-Jew lacks *taharah* — specifically the *taharah* of *parah adumah*, which the verse in *Parshat Chukat* restricts with *vehaysa la'adat Bnei Yisrael lemishmeret* — he therefore lacks *tumah* as well.

*Tosafot* notes a further question here: perhaps the linkage between *taharah* and *tumah* via *vehizah hatahor al hatamei* is drawn for a different *drasha* entirely, or perhaps the exclusion from *parah adumah* covers only that particular form of *taharah*, not all *taharah* generally — perhaps a non-Jew has *taharah* via *mikvah*, for instance. The *gemara* answers by citing *veha'ish asher yitma velo yitchata*, which explicitly connects *tumah* to the *chittui* of *parah adumah* through the phrase *velo yitchata*. That extra phrase, which would be unnecessary if the verse simply said *veha'ish asher yitma*, underscores that *tumah* and the *taharah* of *parah adumah* are inextricably linked. Since non-Jews are excluded from that *taharah* by *Bnei Yisrael* language in *Parshat Chukat*, and since *tumah* follows *taharah*, non-Jews have no *tumah* of any kind.

The upshot of this chain: non-Jews are excluded from *nezirut* because *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* requires the capacity for *tumah*; non-Jews lack *tumah* because they lack *taharah*; they lack *taharah* because the *parah adumah* is restricted to *Bnei Yisrael*; and the link between *taharah* and *tumah* is derived from *vehizah hatahor al hatamei* and *veha'ish asher yitma velo yitchata*.

### Nachala Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִתְנַחַלְתֶּם אֹתָם לִבְנֵיכֶם אַחֲרֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** a different reason why a gentile cannot become a nazirite, despite the fact that the term "man" does include gentiles in the case of valuations. **Here,** with regard to naziriteship, it **is different, as** the verse prohibits a nazirite from becoming impure for his father and mother. This prohibition is not relevant for gentiles because **the verse states: "And you shall make them an inheritance for your children after you"** (Leviticus 25:46). This verse establishes a child as one to whom a person can bequeath his slaves, the subject of this verse.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers an alternative derivation. Rather than proceeding through *tumah* and *taharah*, he returns to the phrase *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* and argues that the word *aviv* is not fully applicable to non-Jews for a different reason. The verse in the context of slaves reads *vehenchaltem otam livneichem achareichem* — "you shall bequeath them to your children after you." From this: *kol she'yesh lo nachala yesh lo tumah* — whoever participates in this category of *nachala* (specifically, the bequeathing of slaves from one generation to the next) is within the *parashah* of *nazir*. *Kol she'ein lo nachala ein lo tumah* — whoever lacks this *nachala* is excluded.

Non-Jews, while they do inherit property from their fathers — as established earlier from *ki yerusha l'Esav* — do not legally bequeath slaves to their children in the manner recognized by *halacha*. The *halacha* recognizes that Jews own *avadim kena'anim* as legal property passed down through generations; it does not recognize the equivalent institution among non-Jews. Therefore, the *avir* of the non-Jew is not the full *aviv* the *pasuk* contemplates, and this excludes them from the *parashah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ נַחֲלָה — יֵשׁ לוֹ טוּמְאָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ נַחֲלָה — אֵין לוֹ טוּמְאָה. אִי הָכִי עֲבָדִים נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov explains: This verse teaches that **anyone who has inheritance,** i.e., he has the ability to bequeath his slaves to his children, also **has** the status of a father with regard to **impurity,** and the verse prohibiting a nazirite from becoming impure to bury his father can be addressing him. **But anyone who does not have inheritance does not have** the status of a father with regard to **impurity.** Since a gentile cannot bequeath slaves to his child (see *Gittin* 38a), the status of a father with regard to impurity also does not apply to him. Consequently, the verse prohibiting a nazirite from becoming impure to bury his father cannot be addressing him. The Gemara asks: **If so, slaves should also not** be included in the *halakhot* of ritual impurity or naziriteship, as they too cannot bequeath slaves to their children.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *i hachi avadim nami lo* — if the criterion is *nachala* of slaves, then slaves themselves would also be excluded from *nezirut*, since slaves obviously do not own and bequeath other slaves. Since we know slaves *do* have *nezirut*, this derivation fails. Accordingly, the *gemara* abandons Rav Acha bar Yaakov's approach and returns to Rava's formulation, which we take up next.

## Korban Exclusion

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא גַּבֵּי עֲרָכִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מַעֲרִיכִין, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם מַעֲרִיכִין. יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ נֶעֱרָכִין, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** a different reason why a gentile cannot become a nazirite, even though he is included in the *halakhot* of valuations, and despite the fact that the term "man" appears in both passages. **Granted, with regard to valuations, as it is stated:** "Speak to **the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 27:2), one can derive that **the children of Israel can take a valuation vow but gentiles cannot take a valuation vow.** One **might** have thought that gentiles **cannot be valuated** either. Therefore, **the verse states: "Man"** (Leviticus 27:2), so as to include a gentile in only one aspect of the *halakhot* of valuations, i.e., that a gentile can be the subject of a valuation. This does not negate the derivation from "the children of Israel" that gentiles are generally excluded.

Having set aside Rav Acha bar Yaakov's alternative, Rava presents a broader logical argument for why non-Jews are *categorically* excluded from *nezirut* — not merely partially excluded — and why the analogy to *erchin* (where a half-status exists) does not apply here.

The question, recall, is why *Bnei Yisrael* and *ish o isha* cannot generate the same middle position for *nezirut* that *Bnei Yisrael* and *ish* generate for *erchin*. Rava argues: *im mishum korban lo mehacha nafka leh* — if you were to say the partial exclusion would concern the *korban* — that non-Jews are excluded from *Bnei Yisrael* when it comes to bringing a *korban nezirut* but included via *ish o isha* in the status of *nezirut* itself — we don't need the *Bnei Yisrael* verse to tell us that. We already know from elsewhere that non-Jews cannot bring a *korban nezirut*.

The source is a verse in *Vayikra* 22:18: *daber el Aharon ve-el banav ve-amarta aleihem ish ish mi-beit Yisrael u-min hager be-Yisrael asher yakriv korbano le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvotam asher yakrivu l-Adonai le-ola* — despite the phrase *mi-beit Yisrael*, the repeated *ish ish* expands the scope to include non-Jews, who may vow and bring *korbanot*. However, the verse concludes *le-ola* — only an *olah*. We accept an *olah* from a non-Jew; we do not accept other types of *korbanot*. Rabbi Yossi HaGlili derives explicitly: *le-ola perat le-nezirot* — the word *le-ola* excludes a *korban nezirut*, which comprises an *olah*, a *chatat*, and a *shelamim*. Since a non-Jew may only bring a *nedavah* *olah* and not the distinctive *korbanot* of *nezirut*, they cannot bring a *korban nezirut*.

Therefore, when we arrive at the *parashah* of *nezirut* and read *Bnei Yisrael*, the partial exclusion — the *korban* — is already accomplished. The only work left for *Bnei Yisrael* to do is *complete* exclusion. There is no room for a middle status, because the middle was already occupied by the earlier derivation.

### Half-Status Hypotheticals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא, בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹזְרִין וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם נוֹזְרִין וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן. יָכוֹל אַף לֹא יְהוּ נְזִירִין כְּלָל — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ״?
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues his explanation: However, **here,** in the case of naziriteship, there is no possibility of interpreting the word "man" to include a gentile in only one aspect of the *halakhot* of naziriteship. Rava explains: If one would suggest the following derivation: From the phrase "the children of Israel" (Numbers 6:2) derive that **the children of Israel can** both **take a vow** of **naziriteship and bring** the nazirite **offering, but gentiles cannot** both **take a vow** of **naziriteship and bring** the nazirite **offering,** one **might** have thought that gentiles **cannot be nazirites at all;** therefore, **the verse states: "Man,"** which includes gentiles in one aspect of the *halakhot* of naziriteship, i.e., that they can become nazirites.

The *gemara* examines whether other potential half-statuses might nonetheless have been entertained, such that we would need *Bnei Yisrael* to exclude them specifically and *ish o isha* to include them in some residual way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִי מִשּׁוּם קׇרְבָּן לָאו מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא מֵהָתָם: ״לְעוֹלָה״ — פְּרָט לִנְזִירוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי!
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues his explanation: However, the Sages would **say** in response to this suggestion: **If** the phrase "the children of Israel" is written **due to** the need to exclude a gentile from bringing a nazirite **offering, one does not** need to **derive** this *halakha* **from here, and** this is because it is already derived **from there,** as a *baraita* teaches that the verse: "Any man from the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel, who sacrifices his offering, whether it be any of their vows, or any of their gift offerings, which are sacrificed to the Lord **as a burnt-offering"** (Leviticus 22:18), **excludes** a gentile from the offering of **naziriteship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** Therefore, the phrase "the children of Israel" must exclude gentiles from the entire passage of naziriteship, and not just from bringing the offerings.

The first suggestion: perhaps *Bnei Yisrael* excludes non-Jews from *nezirut olam* — permanent *nezirut* — but *ish* still includes them in ordinary, time-limited *nezirut*. Rabbi Yochanan dismisses this: *mi ketiv nazir olam*? Nowhere in the *pesukim* is *nezirut olam* even mentioned. If the *pesukim* do not address *nezirut olam* as a category, one cannot construct a partial exclusion based on it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹזְרִים נְזִירוּת עוֹלָם, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם נוֹזְרִים נְזִירוּת עוֹלָם. יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ נְזִירִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ״! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי כְּתִיב ״נְזִיר עוֹלָם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But it can still be argued that "the children of Israel" is excluding a gentile from only some aspects of naziriteship, as one can **say** that this phrase teaches that **the children of Israel can take a vow** of **permanent naziriteship, but gentiles cannot take a vow** of **permanent naziriteship,** and one **might** have thought that gentiles **cannot be nazirites** at all. **The verse** therefore **states: "Man,"** to teach that they can become nazirites. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Is it written: A permanent nazirite?** Since the verse does not specify a particular type of naziriteship, it excludes gentiles from all types.

The second suggestion: perhaps *Bnei Yisrael* excludes non-Jews from the ability to impose *nezirut* on their children, while *ish* still includes them in their own personal *nezirut*. Again, Rabbi Yochanan answers: *halacha hi be-nazir* — the ability to make one's child a *nazir* is not derived from the *pesukim* at all. It is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, a tradition entirely outside the textual framework of the *pesukim*. Since it is not in the *pesukim*, the *pesukim* cannot be read as splitting on it.

### Child Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מַדִּירִין בְּנֵיהֶם בְּנָזִיר, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם מַדִּירִין בְּנֵיהֶם בְּנָזִיר. יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ נְזִירִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ״! הָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another suggestion: But it can still be argued that "the children of Israel" is excluding a gentile from only some aspects of naziriteship, as one can **say** that this phrase teaches that **"the children of Israel" can vow** that **their** minor **sons be nazirites, but gentiles cannot vow** that **their** minor **sons be nazirites;** and one **might** have thought that gentiles **cannot be nazirites** at all. **The verse** therefore **states: "Man,"** to teach that they can become nazirites. The Gemara answers: **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that the fact that a father can take a vow that his minor son will be a nazirite **is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses at Sinai **with regard to a nazirite?** Since this *halakha* is not stated in the Torah, it cannot be excluded by a verse.

A third suggestion parallels the second: perhaps *Bnei Yisrael* excludes non-Jews from the *halacha* of *megalchim al nezirut avihem* — the practice whereby a son may shave, or bring a *korban*, in connection with his father's *nezirut* (a case discussed elsewhere in the tractate where a father and son accepted *nezirut* simultaneously, or where the son carries out the *nezirut* on behalf of the father in some capacity). Again, Rabbi Yochanan responds: *halacha hi be-nazir* — this too is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, not derived from the *pesukim*, and therefore the *pesukim* cannot be generating a partial inclusion or exclusion based on it.

The structure of Rava's argument thus emerges clearly: any partial exclusion of non-Jews from *nezirut* that might have been entertained is either already accomplished through other *pesukim* (the *korban* via *le-ola*), not actually contained in the *pesukim* of *nezirut* (the derivative *halachot* of child *nezirut* being *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*), or simply not referenced in the *pesukim* at all (*nezirut olam*). Since no coherent half-status remains possible, *Bnei Yisrael* in the *parashah* of *nezirut* can only serve as a complete exclusion — and the earlier derivation from *l'aviv ul'imo lo yitama* through *tumah* and *taharah* establishes the categorical ground for that exclusion.
