# Horayot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Individual Following Ret…

### Court Retracts Ruling

The Mishnah at the bottom of 3b opens with a case of *hora'ah* gone wrong: *horu beit din veyadu sheta'u vechazru bahen* — the court issued a ruling, became aware of their error, and retracted. Whether or not they have yet brought the sacrifice to which they are now obligated, an individual who never received the news and continued acting on the original ruling presents a problem. With no newspapers and no radio, it was entirely possible to remain ignorant of the retraction. *Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts such an individual from a personal *korban*. Rabbi Eliezer says it is a matter of doubt: *safek*.

### Shimon vs. Eliezer Dispu…

The disagreement between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Eliezer maps onto a deeper conceptual question about the very basis on which an individual who follows a *beit din* ruling is ever exempt in the first place. There is a position — the *chachamim* — holding that even a blameless individual is obligated in a *korban*, since at the end of the day he is the one who committed the act. Our Mishnah assumes he is not obligated. But why?

### Two Models Debate

Two distinct conceptual models can explain the individual's exemption. The first focuses on blamelessness: the person legitimately believed the act was permitted and is therefore not culpable. The second frames the act differently — it is not so much the individual's personal act as it is an extension of the court's ruling; the individual is, in effect, acting under the court's authority and on the court's account.

These two models yield different results when the court retracts its ruling. If the exemption is grounded in blamelessness alone, then an individual who genuinely never heard of the retraction remains blameless even after the retraction — and should remain exempt. This is Rabbi Shimon's view. If, however, the exemption is grounded in the objective reality of operating under the court's authority, then once the ruling is retracted, the individual is no longer objectively acting under that authority — even if his subjective state of mind remains unchanged — and the exemption may no longer apply.

### Home vs. Away Cases

Rabbi Eliezer says it is a *safek*. The Mishnah then articulates what constitutes that doubt: *yashav lo betoch beito chayav, halach limdinat hayam patur* — if the person remained at home, he is liable; if he was traveling abroad, he is exempt. This requires careful reading. Rashi explains that *chayav* here does not mean obligated in a *chatat*, but in an *asham talui* — the suspended guilt offering brought when one has possibly committed a sin but cannot be certain. The *asham talui* is the appropriate *korban* in a case of objective doubt, as when one ate a piece of fat and cannot determine whether it was permitted *shuman* or forbidden *chelev*.

What emerges from this is that Rabbi Eliezer is not actually arguing with Rabbi Shimon's principle. Both agree: if the individual is truly blameless, he is exempt. Their dispute is over the application. When a person was abroad and had no means of hearing of the retraction, he is entirely blameless and therefore entirely exempt. When a person remained at home, however, it is possible he could have heard — perhaps he simply did not inquire or get out enough — and therefore his blamelessness is not so clear-cut. In that case, Rabbi Eliezer says the situation is a *safek*: perhaps he is blameworthy for failing to stay informed, perhaps not. That unresolved doubt requires an *asham talui*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Reasoning

Ben Azzai questions why the traveler's case warrants more complete exemption than the person who stayed home — the answer seems obvious, but he presses Rabbi Akiva to articulate it. Rabbi Akiva's response: *shehayoshev beiveito efshar hayah lo sheyishma velo hayah lo efshar* — the person at home had a realistic possibility of hearing, whereas the person abroad had none. That difference in practical capacity to hear is what determines the degree of blamelessness. Even that is refined further: if one was at home but in the midst of preparing to depart — packing, waiting for one's ride — Rabbi Akiva says he too is already sufficiently on his way that he counts as departed, while Ben Azzai maintains that as long as one is still physically at home, some possibility of receiving information remains.

## Court Liability Conditio…

### Detail vs. Whole Category

The Mishnah and Gemara now shift to an entirely separate point: not who follows the ruling, but the substance of the ruling itself — what kind of ruling makes the court liable for a communal *korban*. The principle, derived from the verse *vene'elam davar*, is *horu levatel miktzat ulekayem miktzat* — the court must have ruled to nullify a part of a *mitzvah* while upholding the rest. Only a ruling about a detail, erroneously gotten wrong, generates court liability. A ruling that eliminates an entire category of law altogether does not.

### Three Examples

The Mishnah provides three examples of a court ruling on a detail. If they ruled that the concept of *niddah* exists in the *Torah* but that a man who has relations with a *shomeret yom keneged yom* — a woman who bled mid-cycle — is exempt, that is a detail. If they ruled that *Shabbos* exists but that *hotza'ah*, carrying between domains, is permissible, that too is a detail. And if they ruled that *avodas kochavim* is prohibited but that *hishtachavaya*, prostration, is not a forbidden act of worship, that similarly constitutes a detail. In all three cases, *harei eilu chayavin* — the court is liable.

### Textual Proof for Detail

The Gemara derives the requirement that it be a *detail* rather than the whole from the word *davar* in the verse *vene'elam davar* — a matter was hidden — rather than the whole body of law.

## Shimon's State of Mind D…

### Court License Approach

Rav Yehudah cites Rav explaining Rabbi Shimon's position: *ho'il ubireshus beis din hu oseh* — since the individual is acting with the license of the court. Even though, objectively speaking, the court has retracted and the license is technically no longer in effect, in his own mind he is still operating under that license.

### State of Mind Purpose

A second version of Rav Yehudah's explanation frames it more precisely: *kol hora'ah sheyatzah berov tzibur, yachid ha'oseh osah patur, lefi shelo nitenah hora'ah ela lehavchin bein shogeg lemeizid* — any ruling that has been disseminated through the majority of the community exempts the individual who follows it, because the entire purpose of the court's ruling is to determine state of mind. This phrase — normally applied to the formal warning (*hatra'ah*) given before a sin — is here applied to the *hora'ah* itself.

The implication is this: what the court's ruling actually does is not create an objective legal reality that shields the individual, but rather it establishes the individual's subjective state of mind as one of legitimate reliance. Once we recognize that the exemption is grounded in state of mind rather than objective legal reality, it follows that the exemption persists even after the retraction — because the individual's state of mind has not changed. He still believes he is acting on the court's authority. That belief is sufficient.

### Fund Collection Challenge

A challenge is raised from a related law. The *par helam davar shel tzibur* — the communal bull brought when the court's erroneous ruling leads the public to sin — requires funding. Who pays for it? Rabbi Shimon says a special collection is conducted: the community is taxed specifically for this *korban*. Rabbi Yehudah says it comes from the general communal funds, *terumas ha-lishka*, normally used for the daily sacrifices.

The challenge proceeds as follows: Rabbi Shimon, who holds that exemption is entirely a matter of state of mind, rules in the Mishnah that an individual remains exempt even after the *korban* has been brought — that is, even after the court has not merely retracted but has fully closed the matter by bringing their sacrifice. But if the money for that *korban* was raised through a special public collection, as Rabbi Shimon holds, then surely the entire community would have learned of the retraction through that very collection. How then can anyone claim not to have known?

### Collection Resolutions

Three resolutions are offered. First, perhaps the funds were collected *stam* — without explanation, as a general campaign — so the public would not have known the purpose and thus not have learned of the retraction. Second, perhaps the individual in question was absent from his locale precisely during the period of collection. Third, and most striking, perhaps Rav who offered this explanation actually held the reverse of the standard attribution: perhaps it was Rabbi Yehudah who required a special collection and Rabbi Shimon who held the funds came from *terumas ha-lishka* — so no public collection occurred at all. This last resolution underscores just how central the state of mind doctrine is to Rabbi Shimon's position: even after every formal step has been completed — retraction, acknowledgment, and *korban* — if the individual genuinely did not know, he is exempt.

## Four Positions Compared

### Rabbi Meir Position

A *braisa* introduces a fourth position not seen in the Mishnah. Rabbi Meir holds that the individual who acted on the retracted ruling is *chayav* — obligated in a *korban* — straightforwardly and without qualification. This corresponds to the first conceptual model described above: Rabbi Meir does not accept that blamelessness alone exempts a person. His view is anchored in objective reality. Once the court has retracted, the individual is objectively no longer operating under their license, regardless of his subjective state of mind. The sin occurred, the individual committed it, and he must bring a *korban*. The fact that he is blameless is simply irrelevant to the question of obligation.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon exempts the individual in all cases. As established above, his view is entirely anchored in state of mind and blamelessness. Since the individual genuinely believed he was operating under the court's authority, his exemption is complete.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

Rabbi Eliezer says *safek*: it is a doubt, and the appropriate response is an *asham talui*. His position is that we are genuinely uncertain whether the individual's subjective state qualifies as sufficient blamelessness to exempt him.

### Sumchus Position

A fourth view is attributed to Sumchus: *taluy* — suspended. Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Sumchus concerns precisely the *asham talui*: Rabbi Eliezer holds this is a standard case of doubt requiring an *asham talui*, while Sumchus holds there is insufficient basis to bring any *korban* at all. This distinction will be unpacked through two separate analogies.

---

## Rabbi Eliezer Framework

### Forbidden Meat Analogy

Rav Zeira offers the analogy that illuminates Rabbi Eliezer's position. It is like a person who ate a piece of fat and does not know whether it was *shuman* — permitted fat — or *chelev* — forbidden fat, for which *karet* would apply. The reason the analogy uses fat rather than, say, *neveilah*, is that the *asham talui* mechanism applies only to sins that carry *karet* when committed deliberately. When the person later realizes he may have transgressed, he brings an *asham talui*. In Rabbi Eliezer's reading of our case, the individual's situation is structurally identical: there is a genuine factual uncertainty about whether the conditions for obligation are met. Just as we do not know whether that piece of meat was *chelev*, we do not know whether this person's state of mind qualifies as the kind of blameless reliance on the court that would exempt him.

### Two Layers of Doubt

There are in fact two layers of uncertainty at play. The first is whether the person's conscious reliance on the court's ruling — the fact that he subjectively believed he was acting on their authority — is sufficient to define his legal state of mind as one of legitimate reliance. The second is whether, even granting that he thought he was relying on *beit din*, we can treat that as the operative legal reality when objectively the ruling had been withdrawn.

### Court Publicity Analysis

One might argue that the more public the court's *korban* is — particularly if the community was involved in funding it — the harder it becomes to maintain that the individual genuinely could not have known of the retraction. Nevertheless, Rabbi Eliezer holds that this publicity does not resolve the doubt in either direction. Even if the collection was less public, as when *beit din* alone brings the *korban*, one could argue: *dehavei sho'el hava amrinan leh* — had he asked, people would have told him. There is an objective reality in which the information was available. We simply do not know whether his failure to ask rises to the level of culpable negligence. That unresolved question is the *safek*, and it mandates the *asham talui*.

## Sumchus Framework Analys…

### Twilight Sacrifice Analo…

Rabbi Yose bar Avin — some say Rabbi Yose bar Zvida — offers the analogy for Sumchus's position. A person brought his sacrifice right at *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period at day's end. It is uncertain whether the sacrifice was offered while it was still halachically day or once night had already fallen. *Safek mibod yom nitkapeir lo, safek mishehachishah nitkapeir lo* — we simply do not know. In such a case, *she'ein meivi asham talui* — no *asham talui* is brought. The person is not obligated to bring any further *korban*.

This is a striking analogy because the *asham talui* would seem even more justified here than in the forbidden fat case: with the fat, you at least know you ate something that might have been *chelev*. With the twilight sacrifice, you know an action was performed — the question is purely one of timing. And yet no *asham talui* is required. The Gemara pushes the analogy further: this holds even according to the position that the communal *korban* was brought publicly — *demifarsma milsa* — and even according to the position that perhaps he *could* have inquired: *ishal lo askach inish dumeshailei* — had he asked, he might not have found anyone who could have told him definitively, because the uncertainty in *bein hashmashos* is itself objective and irreducible.

### Doubt in Law Not Facts

This is Sumchus's innovation. For Rabbi Eliezer, the doubt is factual: we don't know whether the individual's state of mind meets the threshold for exemption or liability. For Sumchus, the doubt is not in the facts at all — the individual's state of mind is clear, his ignorance is uncontested — the doubt is in the law itself. Two legal frameworks apply simultaneously and point in opposite directions.

### Contradictory Lenses

What Sumchus is really saying is that there are two irreconcilable legal lenses through which to view this individual's act. Through the lens of Rabbi Meir — the objective reality lens — the court has retracted, and the individual is no longer operating under their license; he is therefore *chayav*. Through the lens of Rabbi Shimon — the state of mind lens — the individual genuinely believed he was relying on the court's authority, and he is therefore *patur*. It is worth noting that Sumchus was a student of Rabbi Meir, and what he seems to be saying is: I cannot choose between these two frameworks. Both are valid. Both apply. The individual stands exactly at the intersection of the status of *chayav* and the status of *patur* — not because we don't know the facts, but because the law itself generates a middle state when both frameworks are simultaneously true.

This is analogous to *bein hashmashos*: the doubt there is not about what time the sun set — it is an objective, irreducible twilight whose legal status is genuinely indeterminate. The period is neither day nor night in a legally resolvable sense. Similarly here, the individual is neither clearly *chayav* nor clearly *patur* — the legal frameworks themselves generate an irresolvable middle state, and that middle state is insufficient to generate the certainty required for any *korban* at all.

In summary, the Gemara has laid out two fundamentally different models — objective reality versus subjective state of mind — and three positions within the subjective framework: Rabbi Shimon holds blamelessness is always sufficient to exempt; Rabbi Eliezer holds it is a factual doubt requiring an *asham talui*; and Sumchus holds the contradiction between the two legal frameworks produces a legal suspension in which no *korban* is brought.

## Detail Definition Analys…

### Detail Principle

The Gemara now returns to the second major topic of the daf: what exactly constitutes a *davar* — a detail — as opposed to *kol haguf* — the entire body of law — for the purpose of determining court liability. The *braisa* sets up the problem: the verse says *vene'elam davar*, a matter was hidden. Perhaps *davar* refers to the whole matter? The *tana* anticipates this objection and resolves it: *davar* means a detail of the matter, not the entire category.

Several *amoraim* offer derivations. Ulla reads the verse as *vene'elam mi-davar* — hidden from a matter, meaning from an aspect of it — treating the *mem* at the end of *vene'elam* as a separate prefix. Chizkiyah derives it from the continuation of the verse: *ve'asu achas mi-kol mitzvos* — they did one thing from among all the commandments. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak notes that the word can be read as *mitzvas* rather than *mitzvos* — singular in construct form — meaning they erred in one aspect of a *mitzvah*, not in all the *mitzvos*. Rav Ashi draws a parallel to the *zaken mamre*, the rebellious elder. The verse by the *zaken mamre* uses the same language: *ki yipale mimcha davar*, when a matter is hidden. Just as the *zaken mamre* must be ruling on a detail rather than an entire body of law to be liable for his rebellious ruling, so too the court must be ruling on a detail for their error to constitute a *hora'ah*.

This parallel is more than a textual device — it is conceptually significant. The reason a court that eliminates an entire category of law is exempt is that such a ruling does not meet the threshold of *hora'ah* at all. It is so far off-base that it has no legal traction. If someone goes around saying there is no such thing as *Shabbos* in the *Torah*, he may cause people to sin, but he has not issued a ruling. The same logic applies to the *zaken mamre*: to be considered a competing authority to the court, his ruling must be within the system — a detail, gotten wrong — not a wholesale rejection of a category.

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel adds another criterion alongside the *detail* requirement: *ein beit din chayavin ad sheyoru bedavar she'ein hatzeddukim modim bo* — the court is not liable unless they ruled on something that even the Sadducees would concede is halachically binding. If the ruling goes against something so obvious that even the Sadducees would say it is clearly the law, then the court is so manifestly wrong that it cannot be considered a legitimate *hora'ah*. As the Gemara puts it: *zil krei bei rav hu* — just go ask in *cheder* and the children will tell you the court got it wrong. A ruling that obvious is not a ruling; it is an embarrassment. The court is therefore exempt — paradoxically — precisely because they were too wrong.

### Niddah Example

The Mishnah's first example is a ruling that a man is exempt after relations with a *shomeret yom keneged yom*. One might ask: isn't the concept of *shomeret yom keneged yom* itself explicit in the verse? The Gemara says *vesafra lah* — she shall count for herself — and derives that she counts one day for each day of mid-cycle bleeding. That seems textually grounded, and even the Sadducees would presumably agree. How then could a court be liable for ruling erroneously about it?

The Gemara proposes several ways to refine the case so that it involves something the Sadducees would *not* necessarily concede. Perhaps the ruling concerns *ha'arah* — partial penetration — versus *gmar biah* — full penetration: the court ruled that partial penetration of a *shomeret yom* is exempt. The verse *es mekora he'erah* uses the word *he'erah*, which is read to refer to partial penetration — yet this reading is arguably not so explicit that even the Sadducees would agree. Perhaps the ruling concerns *kedarka* versus *shelo kedarka* — vaginal versus anal intercourse — the court ruled that anal intercourse with a *shomeret yom* is exempt. The Gemara says *mishkvei isha* — the lyings of a woman — in the plural comes to include both, but the court ruled otherwise. And perhaps the ruling holds that a *zavah* is only that status if she bleeds during the day, not at night, based on *kol yemei zovah* — all the days of her *zivah* — which emphasizes daytime. That is a reading that could be made from the verse, and one that the Sadducees would not necessarily reject, so it constitutes a genuine *hora'ah* that can generate liability.

### Shabbat Example

The second case is a ruling that *hotza'ah* — carrying between domains — is exempt. The Gemara asks: isn't *hotza'ah* explicit? It cites *velo totziu masa mibateichem* from the prophets — Yirmiyahu — which says do not carry a burden out of your houses. Even the Sadducees accept the *Tanach*, so this should be something they would agree is forbidden. To keep the case within the framework, the Gemara offers refinements. Perhaps the ruling held that *hotza'ah* — taking out — is forbidden, but *hachnasah* — bringing in — is permissible. Or perhaps both are forbidden, but *moshit* — handing an object through a window — and *zorek* — throwing — are permissible, since in those cases the person is not personally transporting the object across domains. Those latter distinctions are not explicit in the verse, and the Sadducees would not necessarily disagree with them.

### Idolatry Example

The third case is a ruling that *hishtachavaya* — prostration — is an exempt form of idol worship. The verse *lo sishachaveh le'el acher* explicitly prohibits bowing to other gods. But the Gemara proposes that the ruling concerned *shelo kedarka*: if a god is not normally worshipped through prostration, perhaps prostration before it would be exempt. That is not explicit. Alternatively, the ruling may have concerned the precise physical requirements of *hishtachavaya* — perhaps the court held that only a full prostration involving the spreading of both arms and legs (*pishut yadayim veraglayim*) constitutes the forbidden act, and partial prostration does not. That too is not derived from an explicit verse, and the Sadducees would not necessarily disagree. In these ways, each of the Mishnah's examples can be refined into a genuine detail — one that required real legal reasoning, that was not obvious even to the Sadducees, and that therefore constitutes a true *hora'ah* capable of generating court liability.

## One Melachah Question

### Plowing Question

Rav Yosef raises a *ba'ei* — an unresolved question. The court rules that there is no prohibition of plowing on *Shabbos*. Of the thirty-nine *melachos*, they have eliminated precisely one. Is this considered a ruling on a *detail* — and the court is therefore liable — or is it considered eliminating an entire category, making it equivalent to saying *ein Shabbos baTorah* — in which case they would be exempt?

### Two Interpretations

The two sides of the question are drawn cleanly. On one hand: *keivan d'kamodei b'kulhu milsa* — since they affirm all the other thirty-eight *melachos*, eliminating one is merely a partial ruling, a detail, and they should be liable. On the other hand: *keivan d'akrin leh l'charisha k'akira d'gufa damei* — since they completely eliminate the category of plowing, it constitutes the uprooting of an entire thing, and they should be exempt.

### Failed Resolution Attemp…

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from each of the Mishnah's three examples. From the *niddah* case: the court eliminates the entire category of *shomeret yom keneged yom*, and yet this is treated as a detail. Since *shomeret yom* is a distinct personal status within the laws of *niddah*, this would seem to show that even eliminating a full sub-category is still considered a detail — supporting the view that eliminating one of the thirty-nine *melachos* would similarly be a detail. Rav Yosef rejects this: we already explained the *shomeret yom* case in a nuanced way — not as eliminating the whole status, but as a detail within it, such as a particular type of intercourse or timing. So that case cannot serve as a proof.

From the *Shabbos* case: the court eliminates *hotza'ah* entirely — one of the thirty-nine *melachos* — and is treated as liable. This seems directly parallel to the question. The Gemara rejects this as well: we already explained that the ruling was not about all of *hotza'ah* but about a specific aspect of it — taking out but not bringing in, or transporting but not throwing. So the Mishnah's *Shabbos* example is actually a detail within *hotza'ah*, not the elimination of *hotza'ah* altogether.

### Nuancing Problem

From the *avodas kochavim* case: the court eliminates *hishtachavaya* entirely, yet is liable. Again, the Gemara rejects this: we explained that the ruling was about *hishtachavaya shelo kedarka* or about the physical requirements of *hishtachavaya* — again a detail within the category, not its complete elimination. The difficulty is that precisely because the Gemara was forced to nuance all three of the Mishnah's examples into details — in order to satisfy the Sadducees criterion — none of them can now serve as a case where an entire sub-category was eliminated. The question of whether eliminating one complete *melacha* out of thirty-nine constitutes a *detail* or a *whole* therefore remains unresolved.

## Shemitah Year Question

### Plowing Harvest Verses

Rav Zeira poses a parallel *ba'ei*. Let the court rule that *Shabbos* does not apply during a *shemitah* year. The erroneous reasoning might proceed from the verse *b'charish u-v'katzir tishbot* — rest from plowing and from harvesting. One might read this to mean: *Shabbos* applies during years when plowing and harvesting occur, but during *shemitah*, when those activities are forbidden, perhaps *Shabbos* does not apply. That is a reading that could be derived from the verse's phrasing.

### Partial vs. Whole Framew…

Is such a ruling a *detail* or a *whole*? On one reading: *keivan d'mikayim leh b'sha'ar shnei shavua* — since the court affirms that *Shabbos* applies during the six other years of the seven-year cycle, they are only ruling on a partial matter. On the other reading: *keivan d'akrei leh b'shevi'is k'akirat gufa damei* — since during the *shemitah* year itself they are completely eliminating *Shabbos*, it constitutes the total uprooting of a *mitzvah* for that year. The question is left unresolved as stated, but it will be addressed by an analogy from the *navi sheker*.

## False Prophet Analogy

### Prophet Whole Law Liabil…

Ravina offers a resolution from the laws of the false prophet. *Navi she-nitnaba la'akor davar midivrei Torah chayav* — a *navi* who prophesies to uproot a word of *Torah* is liable. Here, strikingly, the framework is the inverse of the court. For a court or a *zaken mamre*, liability requires ruling on a *detail* — a wholesale elimination of an entire category is too far off-base to constitute a *hora'ah*. But for a false prophet who comes with prophetic authority — as the spokesman of God — the more radical the claim, the more clearly liable he is. If he says an entire category of law is abolished, he is *chayav*.

### Authority Distinction

The conceptual contrast is sharp. A sage operates within the legal system. His ruling has traction only when it engages with the law as it is — analyzing, applying, or misapplying a detail. A wholesale denial of a category is simply not within the domain of legal reasoning; it is not a *hora'ah*. A prophet operates outside the system, with claimed divine authority. For him, the most extreme assertion — that an entire category of Torah law is abolished — is precisely the kind of false prophecy that generates liability, because he is positioning himself as a divine voice against the entire *Torah*.

### Idolatry Exception

However, in the case of *avodas kochavim*, even Rabbi Shimon agrees that the false prophet is *chayav* even if he says only: worship today and do not worship tomorrow. *Afilu amar hayom ovdah u'lemachar bitlah chayav* — even telling people to worship the idol today while saying tomorrow it will be abolished is sufficient for liability. A time-limited instruction to worship *avoda zara* is still a partial ruling against *avoda zara*, not a wholesale elimination of the prohibition. Yet the prophet is liable. This establishes that a time-limited ruling — applying to some circumstances and not others — is legally categorized as a ruling on a *detail*, not on the whole.

### Inference to Court Case

This provides the resolution to Rav Zeira's question. If a false prophet who tells people that *Shabbos* does not apply during *shemitah* — limiting *Shabbos* to a subset of years — is analogous to the prophet who says worship today but not tomorrow, then such a time-based limitation is classified as a ruling on a *detail* rather than a complete elimination. The same framework applies to the court: a ruling that *Shabbos* does not apply during *shemitah*, since *Shabbos* remains in force the other six years of the cycle, is a *detail* and not *kol haguf*. The court would therefore be liable.

## Valid Ruling Conditions

### Dissent Prevention

The Mishnah returns to a condition for court liability that was introduced earlier in the tractate. If one member of the court explicitly tells the others *to'in atem* — you are making a mistake — and verbally dissents, the ruling does not generate the communal *korban* obligation. Even though the court normally operates by majority rule and a dissenting vote does not invalidate the ruling for purposes of *dinei Torah*, a verbal dissent is sufficient to prevent the court from being liable in this specific context. The concern, apparently, is not the formal legal validity of the ruling but whether the ruling carries the weight of a unified court acting on full authority.

### Missing Head or Unfit Ju…

Two additional conditions disqualify the ruling from generating liability. First, if the *mufla shebe-beit din* — the head of the court — was absent. Rashi specifies that this refers to a case where the court still had a full quorum, but the head had vacated his seat and someone else filled the number — so the court was technically complete but the leading decisor was not present. Second, if any member of the court was in a disqualified category.

### Disqualified Judge Categ…

The Mishnah lists four categories of disqualified members: a *ger* — a convert; a *mamzer* — a person of illegitimate birth; a *netin* — someone from the Gibeonites who attached themselves to Israel under Joshua in a problematic manner and whose descendants are of questionable lineage; and a *zaken she-lo ra'uy l'vanim* — an elder who has never had children. The disqualification of the *ger* is based on the requirement of *mikerev* — a position of communal authority requires being *mikerev* your brethren, which excludes the convert in this context. The *mamzer* and *netin* disqualifications relate to the genealogical integrity required for the court. Rashi acknowledges uncertainty about the *zaken she-ein lo banim*; the Rambam in his *Perush Hamishnayot* explains it as a concern about insufficient compassion — a person who has never raised children may not have developed the requisite empathetic judgment for capital cases, though this same concern is applied here by extension.

### Edah Textual Basis

The source for requiring that all members be fit for *hora'ah* is derived from the word *edah* appearing in two separate contexts — a *gezeirah shavah*. The concept of *edah* — congregation or court — is linked through textual parallel to a context where all members must be qualified to rule. The Gemara adds the verse *vahisyatzvu sham immach* — when God gathers the seventy elders alongside Moses — and derives *immach bidumim lach*: they must be like you, sharing the same qualities of lineage and fitness for authority.

### Tzaddukim vs. Missing He…

The comparison between the *Sadducees* criterion and the missing *mufla* criterion illuminates an important conceptual distinction. When the court rules against something even the Sadducees would affirm, they are *patur* because the ruling is objectively off-base — anyone paying attention would know it is wrong. The ruling lacks the traction of a genuine *hora'ah*. When the *mufla* is absent, the ruling is not objectively wrong — the substantive legal analysis may be entirely sound — but the court has not operated with full due consideration. They should have consulted him, or waited for him. The absence of the senior decisor means the ruling was issued without the deliberative weight that makes it a true *hora'ah*. As the Gemara formulates it for both the *Sadducees* case and the missing *mufla* case: *shehayah lahem lilmod velo lamdu* — they should have learned and they did not. In the *Sadducees* case this means they should have known the obvious ruling; in the *mufla* case it means they should have sought out the guidance of the court's head. In both cases, the failure of due diligence removes the ruling from the category of genuine *hora'ah*.
