# Bava Kamma 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Husband's Inheritance

### Pasuk Repetition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהֵמִית אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה״ – אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וְכִי מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְחַיֵּיב עַל הָאִשָּׁה כָּאִישׁ – הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִגַּח שׁוֹר אֶת אִישׁ אוֹ אֶת אִשָּׁה״!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states with regard to a forewarned ox: "But if the ox was a goring ox in time past and warning has been given to its owner, and he has not secured it; **and it killed a man or a woman;** the ox shall be stoned" (Exodus 21:29). **Rabbi Akiva said: And what does this come to teach us? If** it is **to deem** the owner **liable for** the killing of **a woman** just **as** for the killing of **a man, this is already stated** with regard to an innocuous ox, where it is stated: **"When an ox gores a man or a woman,** and they die, the ox shall be stoned" (Exodus 21:28).

The daf begins at the very bottom of 42b, in the course of a broader *darshanung* of the *pesukim* governing the goring ox. The question arises: why does the *pasuk* repeat the phrase *ish o isha* — "man or woman"? The Torah first states *ki yigach shor ish o isha vamet*, and then again *vehemit ish o isha*. What is the purpose of this repetition?

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְהַקִּישׁ אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ, מָה אִישׁ – נְזָקָיו לְיוֹרְשָׁיו, אַף אִשָּׁה – נְזָקֶיהָ לְיוֹרְשֶׁיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse is stated **to compare a woman to a man** with regard to another issue: **Just as** with regard to **a man,** payment for **his injuries** is paid **to his heirs, so too,** with regard to **a woman,** payment for **her injuries** is paid **to her heirs,** and not to her husband.

Rabbi Akiva explains that the repetition comes to teach us something about who collects the *kofer*. Since the victims are dead by the time payment is made, the *kofer* is necessarily paid to their heirs. One might have assumed that the *kofer* for the woman should go to her husband, since a husband ordinarily inherits his wife. Rabbi Akiva's *chidush* is that this is not the case: the payment goes to the woman's own heirs, not to her husband.

The underlying rationale is that a husband inherits his wife only with respect to money she had, or was entitled to, during her lifetime — because it is that bond of marriage-during-life that causes the inheritance to flow to him. But money she becomes entitled to only *after* her death carries no such bond, and therefore descends instead to her natural heirs.

### Debt After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לָא יָרֵית לַהּ בַּעַל? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְיָרַשׁ אוֹתָהּ״ – מִכָּאן שֶׁהַבַּעַל יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Akiva hold** that **a husband does not inherit** from his wife? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he shall inherit it [*otah*]"** (Numbers 27:11). **From here** it is derived **that the husband inherits** from **his wife,** as *otah* also means: Her. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *Gemara* probes: according to Rabbi Akiva, where would he *not* apply this rule? That is, where would a woman's heirs fail to collect damages owed to her? The implication is that if money was owed to her during her lifetime — a genuine *nezek* debt incurred while she lived — that money should logically pass to the husband upon her death, since it was a pre-existing obligation. Rabbi Akiva, the *Gemara* assumes, would agree with this. But is it actually so straightforward?

### Miscarriage Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא אָמַר אֶלָּא בְּכוֹפֶר – הוֹאִיל וְאֵין מִשְׁתַּלֵּם אֶלָּא לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ רָאוּי, וְאֵין הַבַּעַל נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** Rabbi Akiva **says** that her heirs and not her husband receive the payment **only with regard to ransom, since it is paid only posthumously and is** therefore **considered** property **due** to her; **and the husband does not take** in inheritance the property **due** to the deceased **as** he does the property she **possessed.** For instance, an inheritance that would have gone to the woman had she been alive is not awarded to the husband, but rather, to her other heirs.

A *braita* complicates the picture. The *braita* presents the Torah's case of a man who strikes a woman and she miscarries: *hika et isha veyatz'u yeladeha*. The ruling is that *nezek* and *tzaar* — the injury and the pain suffered by the woman — are paid to the woman herself, while *demei veladot*, the monetary value of the fetus, goes to the husband: *nosay nezek vetza'ar le'isha udmei veladot leba'al*. So far, this reflects the standard division.

The *braita* then addresses what happens when one of the parties is no longer living at the time of payment. If the husband is deceased, the *demei veladot* are paid to his heirs. And if the woman is deceased — *ein ha'isha, nosay leyorsheha* — the *nezek* and *tzaar* go to *her* heirs. But this creates a problem: the woman suffered this injury during her lifetime, meaning the debt of *nezek* and *tzaar* was incurred while she was alive. Why, then, do her heirs collect rather than her husband? This appears to contradict our assumption that pre-existing debts pass to the husband.

### Divorce Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בִּגְרוּשָׁה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בִּגְרוּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** This *baraita* relates **to a divorcée;** since they got divorced, the husband does not inherit from her. **Likewise, Rav Naḥman said:** The *baraita* relates **to a divorcée.**

Rabbah and Rav Nachman both offer the same resolution: *begerusha* — the case in the *braita* is one where she was divorced. Rashi explains that at least initially, we understand this to mean she was divorced *after* the injury occurred. At the time of the injury, the *demei veladot* were rightly owed to the husband — he was the husband then, and that is what the Torah specifies. But the *nezek* and *tzaar* were owed to the woman herself, and after their divorce she died unmarried, so those payments now pass to her heirs rather than to her former husband. That is why the *braita*'s ruling is perfectly coherent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: גְּרוּשָׁה נָמֵי תִּיפְלוֹג בִּדְמֵי וְלָדוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response: If she is **a divorcée,** she **should also share in** the **compensation for** the miscarried **offspring.** Why should her ex-husband receive the full payment?

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this, however: if the case is one of a *gerusha*, why does the *braita* still award the *demei veladot* to the husband? The *Gemara* assumes that the *demei veladot* are connected to the wife in some way — perhaps through *ma'aseh yadeha* or a similar principle — so if the injury occurred after the divorce, the husband seems entirely out of the picture. Why should he receive anything?

### Father vs. Husband

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַתּוֹרָה זִכְּתָה דְּמֵי וְלָדוֹת לַבַּעַל, אֲפִילּוּ בָּא עָלֶיהָ בִּזְנוּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יָשִׁית עָלָיו בַּעַל הָאִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: The Torah awarded the** payment of **compensation for** miscarried **offspring to the husband, even if he** is not actually her legal husband but rather **engaged in licentious sexual intercourse with her.** Although he has no rights to her property, the damages for the miscarried offspring belong to him alone, as he is the father of the offspring. **What is the reason? The verse states: "As the husband [*ba'al*] of the woman shall impose upon him"** (Exodus 21:22), indicating that damages are not collected by the woman, but by the man who engaged in intercourse [*ba'al*] with her, impregnating her. Therefore, if they got divorced, the ex-husband receives the payment.

Rav Pappa resolves this: *haTorah zachta demei veladot leba'al* — the Torah awarded the *demei veladot* to the husband, but the critical word is *ba'al*. The *pasuk* uses the term *ka'asher yashis alav ba'al ha'isha*, and the word *ba'al* carries the connotation of *boel* — the one who had sexual relations with her. The award, therefore, is not contingent on being the husband at the time of the injury; it goes to the biological father of the lost fetus. Even if the couple was divorced at the time of the *nezek*, and even — as an extreme extension — *afilu ba l'beiznus*, even if the conception occurred entirely outside the context of marriage, the *demei veladot* still go to the biological father.

Why? Because the Torah's use of the term *ba'al*, which linguistically emphasizes the sexual relationship rather than the formal marital bond, signals that it is the father — not the husband — who is awarded these damages. This, indeed, reflects the deeper assumption of *halacha* that children are fundamentally seen as the father's. According to basic *halachic* principle, in a case of divorce where children already exist, they are regarded as belonging to the father unless the children are very young and require the mother's care.

This is in itself a striking *gemara*. The *demei veladot* go not to the husband but to the biological father — and not only in a case of legitimate marriage, but even in a case of conception outside marriage. One might ask whether the concept of *ubar yerech imo hu* — that the fetus is considered part of the mother's body — would complicate this, and indeed it makes the ruling all the more remarkable: even though the fetus is legally considered part of the mother's body, and even in a case of an illicit union, the Torah's award still goes to the biological father.

### Illicit Conception

Tosafos pushes this even further, citing a question from the *Yerushalmi*: what if the child was conceived through an illicit union — *ba al imo, ba al achoso* — producing a *mamzer*? The *Yerushalmi* considers whether even in such a case the *demei veladot* would go to the biological father, and concludes from the word *ba'al* — *oso sherauy likros ba'al*, "the one who is fit to be called *ba'al*" — that the principle applies only where it was at least theoretically possible for the couple to have been married. Cases where marriage was never legally possible — *yatz'u elu she'einam reuyim likros ba'al* — are excluded. Still, this is a major *chidush*: even outside of an actual marriage, the *demei veladot* follow the biological father and not the husband.

### Money vs. Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹקְמַהּ לְרַבָּה כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּבוּ מָעוֹת, וּלְרַב נַחְמָן כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּבוּ קַרְקַע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why do Rabba and Rav Naḥman explain this *baraita* as referring to a divorcée? They could have answered, in accordance with their own opinions elsewhere (*Bava Batra* 124b), that it is referring to payments that are not considered to have been in the woman's possession during her lifetime, but rather are considered property due to the woman, which her husband does not inherit. **Let us establish** this *baraita*, according **to Rabba,** as referring to a case **where they collected money** for the damage and pain, **and** according **to Rav Naḥman, where they collected land.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: גָּבוּ קַרְקַע – יֵשׁ לוֹ, גָּבוּ מָעוֹת – אֵין לוֹ. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: גָּבוּ מָעוֹת – יֵשׁ לוֹ, גָּבוּ קַרְקַע – אֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As** with regard to the right of a firstborn to receive a double portion of the inheritance of his father, he receives a double portion only of the property possessed by his father, not of the property due to him. In a case where money was owed to the father, **Rabba says:** If the heirs **collected** the debt from **land,** the firstborn **has** the right to a double portion, since it is considered property that was already in the father's possession. If **they collected money, he does not have** a double portion, as it is considered property due to the father. **And Rav Naḥman says:** If **they collected money, he has** a double portion, and if **they collected land, he does not have** a double portion.

Having established the *gerusha* as the explanation for the *braita*, the *Gemara* turns to a subtler question. The *braita* says that when the woman is deceased, the *nezek* and *tzaar* go to her heirs. But if those debts were incurred during her lifetime, one might still argue that they should be considered "under her control" at the moment of death — in which case the husband, if she died while married, should inherit. The *Gemara* therefore must clarify the precise conditions under which a debt incurred during life is considered "under one's control."

This brings us to a debate between Rabbah and Rav Nachman found elsewhere in *Bava Batra*, concerning a *bechor* — a firstborn son — and his right to a double portion of the estate. The *halacha* is that a *bechor* receives *pishnayim* only on property already under the father's control at the time of death, not on property that accrues afterwards. The question is: what about a debt owed to the father that was collected only after his death?

Rabbah holds: *gavu karka yesh lo* — if the debt was collected in the form of land, the *bechor* receives a double portion, because land is subject to a lien that was always effectively in the estate's possession; the land was never going anywhere. But *gavu ma'ot ein lo* — if it was collected as cash, the *bechor* does not receive a double portion, because cash collection is uncertain, and the money is viewed as something newly realized rather than already possessed.

Rav Nachman holds the reverse: *gavu ma'ot yesh lo* — money collected after death is viewed as the same money that was lent, representing a continuous chain of possession. But *gavu karka ein lo* — if what was collected was land rather than money, that requires the intervention of a *beis din* and a whole separate process; it is something genuinely new and not considered to have been under the father's control.

The relevance to our *braita* is direct: we can now explain why the *nezek* and *tzaar* go to the woman's heirs rather than to her husband, depending on how payment is made. If paid in cash, then according to Rabbah, the cash is not viewed as having been under her control, and the husband does not inherit. If paid in land, then according to Rabbah, it is viewed as having always been hers, and the husband should inherit — which raises a question about the *braita*'s ruling. The reverse would apply according to Rav Nachman.

One might further ask whether, in the case of *nezek*, there is an automatic lien against the tortfeasor's property — *milvah haksuvah batorah kaksuvah b'shtar damya* — or whether only *metaltelin* are available. By *kofer*, the question would intersect with the entire conceptual framework of *kofer kaparah*, and that would require separate investigation.

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי לִבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That statement** applies **according to the residents of the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who limit the rights of a firstborn to his father's property to the extent that they hold he does not receive a double portion of any appreciation in the property occurring after his father's death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמְרִי הָכָא – כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **when they state** that this *baraita* **here** relates to a divorcée, it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi that the firstborn receives a double portion even in the appreciation of the property occurring after the father's death. According to this opinion, clearly everything due to the father is considered to be in his possession, whether he receives his compensation in money or in land. The same applies to a husband, who inherits the rights to inherit compensation for damage and pain inflicted on his wife. That is why Rabba and Rav Nachman had to interpret this *baraita* as relating to a divorcée.

The *Gemara* clarifies, however, that the debate between Rabbah and Rav Nachman was articulated within the framework of the *Bnei Maarava* according to the *rabbanan* — a particular position in *Bava Batra* that treats loans collected after death as presumptively outside the estate's prior possession. The *braita* we are analyzing operates within the position of Rabbi — *ki ka'amar hacha k'Rabbi* — who rules that any debt owed during a person's lifetime, even if collected only after death, is considered as though it were always under that person's control. On that basis, Rabbi would say the *bechor* always receives a double portion on such debts. And it is precisely because of Rabbi's position that the *Gemara* needed to work harder to explain why, in our *braita*, the woman's heirs collect rather than her husband.

To summarize the framework: a husband inherits his wife only when two conditions are met — they were married at the moment of her death, and the money was under her control at that moment. The *kofer* fails the first condition entirely, since it is owed only after her death. The case of divorce fails the first condition because they are no longer married. Money owed during life but paid after death raises the second condition, and the answer varies depending on the form of payment and the *halachic* position one follows — with Rabbi holding that anything owed during life is always considered under one's control.

Along the way, a significant insight has emerged: the *demei veladot* are not a function of husbandhood at all, but of biological fatherhood — a *chidush* with far-reaching implications.

## Kofer & Stoning

### Unintentional Killing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית אֶת הָעֶבֶד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – פָּטוּר מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֶּסֶף שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים יִתֵּן לַאדֹנָיו, וְהַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל״ – כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר בִּסְקִילָה, הַבְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים; אֵין הַשּׁוֹר בִּסְקִילָה, אֵין הַבְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** With regard to **an ox that killed** a Canaanite **slave unintentionally,** the owner is **exempt from** paying the fine of **thirty shekels, as it is stated: "He shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver, and the ox shall be stoned"** (Exodus 21:32). It is derived from the verse that the liability to pay thirty shekels is dependent on the stoning of the ox; **whenever the ox is** liable **to** be killed by **stoning, the owner pays thirty shekels** as compensation for the damage. If **the ox is not** liable **to** be killed by **stoning,** e.g., if it killed unintentionally, **the owner does not pay thirty shekels.**

Having concluded the digression on inheritance, the *Gemara* now takes up a new and important question: must the payment of *kofer* and the stoning of the ox always go together? This question was raised earlier but not directly resolved. Now Reish Lakish addresses it — beginning, perhaps counterintuitively, not with *kofer* but with the *shloshim shekalim* of the slave.

Reish Lakish states: *shor she-hemit et ha-eved shelo b'kavana, patur mi-shloshim shekalim* — an ox that kills a slave without intent is exempt from the payment of thirty *shekalim*. The case he has in mind is one where the ox had no intention to kill this particular victim — perhaps it was trying to kill an animal and accidentally killed a slave. In such a case, the ox is not stoned, and the question is whether the thirty-*shekel* payment is still owed.

### Pasuk Linkage

The basis for Reish Lakish's ruling is a textual juxtaposition. The *pasuk* states: *kesef shloshim shekalim yitein l'adonav v'ha-shor yisakeil* — "he shall give thirty *shekalim* of silver to his master, and the ox shall be stoned." The payment and the stoning appear in immediate proximity: one word separates the two verbs. From this juxtaposition, Reish Lakish derives: *kol zman she-ha-shor b'sekila ha-be'alim meshalmim shloshim shekalim* — whenever the ox is stoned, the owner pays thirty; *ein ha-shor b'sekila, ein ha-be'alim meshalmim shloshim shekalim* — when the ox is not stoned, the thirty are not paid.

It is worth pausing to consider which of the two payments — *kofer* for a *ben chorin* or *shloshim shekalim* for a slave — one would intuitively expect to be more closely tied to the stoning of the ox. The *kofer*, as a *kaparah*, carries a quasi-criminal dimension: one's ox has effectively committed what resembles a murder, and the *kofer* functions as an atonement in place of the liability that would otherwise be attributed to the owner. It is therefore natural to link *kofer* to the stoning of the ox, which itself reflects the criminal severity of the act. The *shloshim shekalim*, by contrast, is a fixed arbitrary payment for property damage — the slave is treated as the owner's property — and seems more detached from the question of whether the ox is stoned. Yet the *pasuk* juxtaposes the *shloshim* and the stoning with particular closeness, making the textual linkage clearest precisely where the logical linkage might seem weakest.

It is worth acknowledging explicitly how troubling it is that a slave's death is treated here as a monetary loss to his owner — as a matter of damaged property rather than lost life. This framing, and the fixed tariff of thirty *shekalim* entirely unrelated to the slave's actual value or the nature of the ox's act, reflects a legal structure that is deeply disturbing to the modern reader, whatever its internal logical coherence.

### Kofer Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית בֶּן חוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – פָּטוּר מִכּוֹפֶר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל, וְגַם בְּעָלָיו יוּמָת. אִם כֹּפֶר יוּשַׁת עָלָיו״ – כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר בִּסְקִילָה – בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין כּוֹפֶר, אֵין הַשּׁוֹר בִּסְקִילָה – אֵין בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין כּוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabba says:** With regard to **an ox that killed a freeman unintentionally,** its owner **is exempt from** paying **ransom; as it is stated: "The ox shall be stoned, and its owner also shall be put to death. If a ransom is laid on him"** (Exodus 21:29–30). This indicates that **whenever the ox is** liable **to** be killed by **stoning the owner pays ransom;** and if **the ox is not** liable **to** be killed by **stoning the owner does not pay ransom.**

Rava now applies the same principle to *kofer*: *shor she-heimeit ben chorin shelo b'kavana, patur mi-kofer* — an ox that kills a free person without intent is exempt from *kofer*. The *pasuk* here reads: *ha-shor yisakeil v'gam be'alav yumat, im kofer yushat alav v'natan pidyon nafsho k'chol asher yushat alav* — the ox shall be stoned and the owner shall pay the *kofer*. The two are linked, though admittedly less tightly than in the slave case; there are many words intervening between the stoning and the *kofer*. Nevertheless, the same logic applies: *kol zman she-ha-shor b'sekila ha-ba'al meshalem kofer, ein ha-shor b'sekila, ein ha-ba'al meshalem kofer*. When the ox is stoned, you pay *kofer*; when it is not stoned, you do not.

This is a striking result, because until this point the *Gemara* had been proceeding with cases where *kofer* was paid without stoning the ox — for instance, the ox that tried to kill an animal and accidentally killed a person (*nogei'ach shelo b'kavana*), or a *regel* that trod on an infant. These seemed to be accepted cases of *kofer* without *sekila*. Rava's ruling now calls that assumption into question, at least as a general principle.

### Admission Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרִי אֶת פְּלוֹנִי״, אוֹ ״שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. מַאי, לָאו כּוֹפֶר?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba's statement from a mishna: If a person admits: **My ox killed so-and-so, or:** My ox killed **the ox of so-and-so, this** owner **pays based on his own** admission (*Ketubot* 41a). This cannot be referring to the payment of a fine, as a person who admits his responsibility for an act incurring a fine is exempt from paying the fine. Clearly, it must be referring to a payment for which one can render himself liable through admission. **What, is it not** referring to the payment of **ransom?** If so, this proves that payment of ransom is not dependent on whether the ox is liable to be killed by stoning, as the ox cannot be killed based on its owner's admission alone.

Abaye challenges Rava from a *braita*: *heimeit shori et ploni o shoro shel ploni, harei zeh meshalem al pi atzmo* — if someone admits that his ox killed a person or killed another's ox, he pays on the basis of his own admission. The *Gemara* asks: doesn't this constitute a case of paying *kofer* without stoning the ox? After all, there are no witnesses, so the ox will not be stoned — yet the owner admits liability. If the payment is *kofer*, we have exactly such a case.

### Witness Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to payment of the monetary **value** of the victim. Although he is not liable to pay ransom, as the ox is not killed, nevertheless, since by his own admission his ox caused damage, he is liable to pay damages.

The *Gemara* responds: *lo, damim* — no, in that case, what you would pay is not *kofer* but monetary compensation. But before reaching that answer, the *Gemara* addresses Abaye's implicit assumption that admission alone could produce a conflict with Rava's principle. The resolution lies in the rule that stoning an ox is treated like a capital trial for a human being: *k'mitat ha-ba'al kach mitat ha-shor* — just as a person cannot be executed on the basis of his own confession, an ox cannot be stoned on the basis of its owner's admission. You need independent witnesses. Therefore, in any case of admission without witnesses, the ox will never be stoned regardless of the owner's confession, and the question of stoning simply does not arise.

The implication is also that a person's own admission cannot spare his ox from stoning in a case where witnesses already exist — admission neither obligates nor exempts. And conversely, in a case where the owner might prefer to have his ox stoned rather than pay a heavy *kofer*, or vice versa, his admission cannot manipulate the legal outcome. The ox's fate is determined entirely by the testimony of witnesses.

## Compensation Fallback

### Major Chidush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיהוּ, בֶּן חוֹרִין דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? דְּאִי אֲתוֹ סָהֲדֵי וְאַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ דִּקְטַל, וְלָא יָדְעִי אִי תָּם הֲוָה אִי מוּעָד הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר מָרֵיהּ דְּמוּעָד הוּא, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים, מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים;
>
> **Translation:** **but** there is a distinction between the two cases. With regard to an ox killing **a freeman,** there is a scenario **where** the owner **pays ransom based on his own** admission. **And what are the circumstances? If witnesses came and testified that** the ox **killed** a freeman and is therefore liable to be killed by stoning, **but they did not know whether it was innocuous** or **if it was forewarned, and its owner said that it was forewarned.** In this case, the owner **pays ransom based on his own** admission, since the ox is liable to be killed, and the ransom is for atonement and does not constitute a fine. Therefore, even **where there are no witnesses,** and the ox is therefore not liable to be killed by stoning for killing a person, nevertheless, the owner at least **pays** the monetary **value** of the victim based on his admission.

Having established that *kofer* is tied to the stoning of the ox, and that stoning requires witnesses, the *Gemara* now confronts the case of admission without witnesses head-on. The *braita* had stated *harei zeh meshalem al pi atzmo* — he pays on the basis of his own admission. If there are no witnesses, the ox is not stoned, and therefore (by Rava's principle) no *kofer* is owed. Yet the *braita* says he pays. What does he pay?

The *Gemara*'s answer is: *lo, damim* — he pays monetary compensation, not *kofer*. This is a major *chidush*, and the *Gemara* introduces it without any explicit *pasuk* to support it directly. From the very beginning of the *masechet*, the working assumption has been that there is simply no monetary compensation for the taking of a human life — not as a civil matter, not as *nezek*, not as *tashlumin*. The loss of life is handled either criminally (through *retzicha* and *galut*) or through *kofer* as a form of *kaparah*. Here, for the first time, the *Gemara* places on the table the idea that when the primary payment prescribed by the Torah — *kofer* — is unavailable, a fallback of ordinary monetary compensation exists. And this applies specifically when it is not a person who killed but rather a person's ox that killed, since in that context the *Torah* already contemplated a monetary regime.

### Kapara vs. Damim

The conceptual difference between *kofer* and *damim* deserves careful attention. *Kofer* is a *kaparah* — it is atonement for the quasi-criminal liability of the owner whose ox killed. *Damim* is ordinary monetary compensation — a civil debt for the loss of a life. The *Gemara* recognizes these as conceptually distinct even though in many cases their monetary amounts may be similar or identical.

The question of their amounts depends on a separate dispute: is *kofer* calculated as *damei mazik* (the value of the tortfeasor) or *damei nizak* (the value of the victim)? If *kofer* is *damei nizak* — the same measure used for *damim* — one might wonder what difference there is between paying *kofer* and paying compensation. The *Gemara* itself implicitly acknowledges this: when it says *lo, damim*, it is saying that the amount might in some cases be the same, yet the legal category differs.

### Tosfos Differences

Tosafos addresses this directly. He asks: according to the opinion that *kofer* is *damei nizak*, what practical difference exists between *kofer* and *damim*? Both represent the same value — the victim's worth on the slave market — for the same victim. Tosafos identifies three *nafkei minah*.

First: *kegon shemet hamazik, de'ein kaparah le'acher mita* — if the owner of the ox died, *kofer*, as a *kaparah*, cannot be imposed posthumously, just as one does not bring a *korban chatat* or *asham* for a person after death. But *damim*, as a civil debt, remains an obligation of the estate even after the owner's death. This produces the counterintuitive result that *shelo b'kavana* — where the payment is *damim* rather than *kofer* — is in one sense more stringent than *b'kavana*: the obligation is more enduring.

Second: *damim yachol limchol v'kofer ein yachol limchol* — the surviving family of the victim can waive a claim for monetary compensation if they choose. But *kofer*, being a *kaparah*, cannot be waived by the heirs; the owner still requires atonement regardless. One cannot absolve another person's need for *kaparah*.

Third: the case of *shutfin* — partners who jointly own the ox that killed. If *kofer* is a *kaparah*, then as the Rambam rules, each partner pays a full *kofer*, because each individual requires his own full atonement. But if the payment is *damim* — a civil debt — the partners obviously divide the payment between them, since they are jointly compensating a single loss.

These three distinctions reveal the depth of the conceptual gap between *kofer* and *damim*, even when their nominal amounts coincide.

### Slave Compensation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? דְּאִי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים וְאַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ דִּקְטַל, וְלָא יָדְעִי אִי תָּם הֲוָה אִי מוּעָד הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר מָרֵיהּ מוּעָד הוּא – לָא מִשְׁתַּלַּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים – לָא מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **with regard to** an ox killing **a slave,** there is a scenario **where** one **does not pay the fine** of thirty shekels **based on his own** admission. **And what are the circumstances** where one would be liable to pay this fine due to his own admission? **If witnesses came and testified that** the ox **killed** a slave, **but they did not know whether it was innocuous** or **if it was forewarned, and its owner said that it was forewarned.** In this case, even though the ox is liable to be killed, the owner **does not pay the fine based on his own** admission, due to the principle that one does not pay a fine based on his own admission. Therefore, in a case **where there are no witnesses, he does not** even **pay** the **value** of the victim based on his own admission.

The *Gemara* now presses further. Having established that when an ox kills a free person without stoning, *damim* are paid as a fallback, one would expect the same logic to apply when the ox kills a slave. If the ox is not stoned and the *shloshim shekalim* are therefore not paid, shouldn't the owner at least owe compensation for the slave's market value? The *braita* says: *hizik shoro et avdo shel ploni, eino meshalem al pi atzmo* — if someone admits that his ox killed so-and-so's slave, he does not pay on the basis of his own admission. The *Gemara* asks: but even granting that the *shloshim* is a *knas* and therefore cannot be self-imposed, shouldn't there still be *damim* to pay? After all, the owner just admitted his ox killed a slave — why isn't that sufficient to require monetary compensation?

### Admission Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיב רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּבֶן חוֹרִין – חַיָּיב בְּעֶבֶד, בֵּין בְּכוֹפֶר בֵּין בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak raises an objection** from a *baraita* that states a principle: In **any** scenario **where** a person is **liable for** his ox killing **a freeman,** he is **liable for** his ox killing a Canaanite **slave, whether with regard to** liability to pay **ransom or with regard to** the ox being put to **death.**

The *Gemara* first notes: *reisha damim v'seifa knas* — the opening clause of the *braita* concerns *damim* and the closing clause concerns *knas*. But this is acknowledged to be a strained reading: *mihu shinuyei d'chika lo m'shanina lach* — I won't offer you a forced answer. Rather, both clauses concern the same legal framework of *damim* as a fallback. The difference lies elsewhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כּוֹפֶר בְּעֶבֶד מִי אִיכָּא?! אֶלָּא לָאו דָּמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The wording of the *baraita* is unclear: **Is there a ransom** to be paid **in** the case of **a slave?** Ransom is paid only for the killing of a freeman. **Rather, is it not** referring to payment of the **value** of the victim? This poses a difficulty for Rabba's opinion that one is not liable to pay the value of the victim for admitting to his ox's killing of a slave.

The key distinction is: by a *ben chorin*, there exist scenarios where *kofer* can be obligated through the owner's own admission. By a slave, the *shloshim shekalim* — being a *knas* — can never be obligated through the owner's own admission, under any circumstances. This asymmetry in the power of admission carries over to the fallback of *damim* as well.

### Tam/Muad Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִיהוּ, בֶּן חוֹרִין דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? דְּאִי אֲתוֹ סָהֲדֵי וְאַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ דִּקְטַל, וְלָא יָדְעִי אִי תָּם הֲוָה אִי מוּעָד הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר מָרֵיהּ דְּמוּעָד הוּא, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים, מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים;
>
> **Translation:** **but** there is a distinction between the two cases. With regard to an ox killing **a freeman,** there is a scenario **where** the owner **pays ransom based on his own** admission. **And what are the circumstances? If witnesses came and testified that** the ox **killed** a freeman and is therefore liable to be killed by stoning, **but they did not know whether it was innocuous** or **if it was forewarned, and its owner said that it was forewarned.** In this case, the owner **pays ransom based on his own** admission, since the ox is liable to be killed, and the ransom is for atonement and does not constitute a fine. Therefore, even **where there are no witnesses,** and the ox is therefore not liable to be killed by stoning for killing a person, nevertheless, the owner at least **pays** the monetary **value** of the victim based on his admission.

How can *kofer* ever be obligated through the owner's own admission, given that *kofer* requires the ox to be stoned, and stoning requires witnesses? Here is the case: witnesses come and testify that the ox killed someone (*de'asu sahadi v'ashidu bei dekatul*), but they do not know whether the ox is a *tam* or a *muad* (*v'lo yadai ei tam hava ei muad hava*). On the testimony of witnesses, the ox will be stoned regardless — stoning applies to both *tam* and *muad*. But *kofer* is owed only by a *muad*. At this point, stoning is established by witnesses, but *kofer* is not yet established. Now the owner admits: *de'amar mari muad hu* — he declares that the ox was a *muad*. This admission triggers the obligation to pay *kofer*, because the ox is already going to be stoned on the basis of witnesses, and the admission adds only the *muad* status — which is a monetary admission, not a *knas*. Therefore: *meshalem kofer al pi atzmo* — he pays *kofer* on his own admission.

Because such a scenario exists — where admission can obligate a free person in *kofer* — the owner's admission carries sufficient legal force to also obligate *damim* as a fallback when there are no witnesses and the ox will not be stoned. The admission can generate a monetary obligation in the category of life-compensation, even though it cannot generate the *sekila* that would make *kofer* strictly applicable.

By contrast, by a slave, there is no scenario where the owner's admission can ever obligate him in the *shloshim shekalim*, because *shloshim* is a *knas* pure and simple. Even using the parallel structure — witnesses testify the ox killed but don't know *tam* or *muad*, and the owner admits *muad* — the owner will still not pay *knas al pi atzmo*, because *knas* categorically cannot be self-imposed. Since admission never has the power to obligate the base payment in the slave case, it also lacks the power to obligate *damim* as a fallback when there are no witnesses at all.

### Reciprocal Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה – הָכִי קָתָנֵי: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּבֶן חוֹרִין בְּכַוָּונָה עַל פִּי עֵדִים, כּוֹפֶר – חַיָּיב בְּעֶבֶד קְנָס. וְכֹל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּבֶן חוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה עַל פִּי עֵדִים, דָּמִים – חַיָּיב בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה עַל פִּי עֵדִים, דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **he,** Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak, **raised the objection and he resolved it,** and **there are** those **who say** it was **Rabba** who **said to him** in response, that **this is what** the *baraita* **is teaching:** In **any** scenario **where** a person is **liable** to pay **ransom for** his ox killing **a freeman,** e.g., where it gored **intentionally based on** the testimony of **witnesses,** he is **liable** to pay **a fine for** his ox killing **a slave. And** in **any** scenario **where** one is **liable** to pay the **value** of the victim **for** his ox killing **a freeman,** e.g., where it gored **unintentionally based on** the testimony of **witnesses, for a slave** too, one is **liable** to pay the **value,** namely, where it gored **unintentionally based on** the testimony of **witnesses.** Accordingly, one does not pay the value of a slave based on his own admission, although he does pay the value of a freeman if he admits that his ox killed him.

The *Gemara* then considers a *braita* that seems to challenge this distinction: *kol shechayav b'ven chorin chayav b'eved* — whatever obligation applies to a case involving a free person applies equally to a case involving a slave, *bein b'kofer bein b'mita* — whether it is *kofer* or stoning. The *Gemara* asks: *kofer b'eved mi ika?* — is there ever *kofer* by a slave? Clearly not. So the *braita* must mean *damim* — monetary compensation. And this *braita* seems to say that whenever there is *damim* for a free person, there is *damim* for a slave as well, with no distinction.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אִשּׁוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה עַל פִּי עֵדִים – נְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rabba: **If that is so,** that one is liable to pay the value of the victim in cases where he is exempt from paying ransom, then if a person burns another to death **unintentionally** with **his fire,** and the proof is **based on** the testimony of **witnesses, he should,** likewise, at least **pay** the **value** of the victim.

Rava resolves this by reinterpreting the *braita*. The *braita* is not saying that all cases are identical; it is distinguishing between two sub-cases. *Kol shechayav b'ven chorin b'kavana al pi eidim kofer* — whenever the ox kills a free person intentionally with witnesses, you pay *kofer*; in the parallel case of a slave, *chayav b'eved knas* — you pay the fixed *knas* of thirty *shekalim*. Both require witnesses and intentional killing, and both are tied to stoning. *V'chol shechayav b'ven chorin shelo b'kavana al pi eidim damim* — whenever the ox kills a free person unintentionally but with witnesses (so you don't stone the ox, but the act is established), you pay *damim*. And in the parallel case: *chayav b'eved shelo b'kavana al pi eidim damim* — by a slave as well, with witnesses and without intent, you pay monetary compensation rather than the fixed *shloshim*.

This is the critical point: the *braita* is not speaking about admission at all. It is speaking about the case of witnesses establishing the kill without *kavana*, which removes the *sekila* and the primary Torah payment, yet leaves intact an obligation to pay *damim*. In that case, the obligation is symmetric between *ben chorin* and *eved*. The distinction Rava earlier drew — that admission cannot obligate *damim* by a slave because the base payment is a *knas* — remains valid for the admission scenario. It simply does not apply to the witnesses scenario.

The *Gemara* has thus introduced a significant new category: *tashlumin* for human life as a fallback when the primary Torah payment is unavailable. This applies when there are witnesses but no *kavana*, removing the *sekila* and therefore the *kofer* or *shloshim*, yet leaving a residual obligation of monetary compensation. It applies to both *ben chorin* and *eved* in that scenario. The exception is where the owner acts alone through admission: admission can obligate *damim* for a free person (because in some scenarios admission obligates *kofer*), but it cannot obligate *damim* for a slave (because *shloshim* as a *knas* is never self-imposable under any circumstances).

## Fire & Compensation

### Fire Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אִשּׁוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה עַל פִּי עֵדִים – נְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rabba: **If that is so,** that one is liable to pay the value of the victim in cases where he is exempt from paying ransom, then if a person burns another to death **unintentionally** with **his fire,** and the proof is **based on** the testimony of **witnesses, he should,** likewise, at least **pay** the **value** of the victim.

Having established the principle of *damim* as a fallback for *kofer* when the ox is not stoned, Rava raises a further question: *amar Rava, ei hachi, isho shelo b'kavana al pi eidim nishalem damim* — if it is true that an ox pays monetary compensation for human life when *kofer* does not apply, does the same hold for fire? If your fire causes a death, would you pay *damim*?

Tosafos notes that *shelo b'kavana* here is not meant precisely (*lav davka*). The assumption is that *eish* operates as *mishum mamono* — as an extension of the owner's property causing damage — rather than as *mishum chitzav*, a direct projectile. If fire is treated as property-damage rather than direct action, then the same way an ox's unintentional killing generates *damim*, perhaps a fire that kills should as well. And notably, there is an argument that the case is even stronger for fire: the Torah never imposes *kofer* for fire at all, so there is no competing *kaparah* framework to crowd out the *damim*. At least with the ox, the Torah first established *kofer*, and *damim* was introduced as a fallback when *kofer* fails. With fire, one might think the Torah's silence on *kofer* means fire is entirely excluded from any payment for human life — or, alternatively, that the absence of *kofer* is precisely why *damim* should apply from the outset.

### Bound Goat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִילֵּימָא מִדִּתְנַן: הָיָה גְּדִי כָּפוּת לוֹ, וְעֶבֶד סָמוּךְ לוֹ וְנִשְׂרַף עִמּוֹ – חַיָּיב. עֶבֶד כָּפוּת לוֹ, וּגְדִי סָמוּךְ לוֹ וְנִשְׂרַף עִמּוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that it is **from what we learned** in a mishna (61b): If one ignites a heap of grain and **there was a goat bound** to an item adjacent **to it, and** there was also **a slave adjacent to it** but not bound, **and** they were **burned** together **with** the heap of grain, he is **liable** to pay for the heap of grain and for the goat. If the **slave** was **bound to it** in such a way that he was unable to flee from the fire, **and** the **goat** was **adjacent to it, and** they were **burned with it,** he is **exempt** from liability. Apparently, there is no liability even if he unintentionally burned the slave to death.

To probe this, the *Gemara* turns to a *mishna* that presents a case bearing on fire's liability for human life. The *mishna* teaches: if there was a bound goat (*hagdi kafut lo*) and a slave standing nearby (*v'eved samuch lo*) next to a stack of grain, and your fire burned through and consumed them all (*v'nisraf imo*) — the ruling is that you are *chayav* for the goat, which could not escape, but not for the slave. The slave could have run away, so the fire's owner is not held liable for the slave's death. The reasoning that would ordinarily preclude liability for the slave's death — *kim lei* or a straightforward exemption from fire's liability for human life — is left implicit, but the ruling is clear: fire does not generate compensation for a slave's death.

The reverse case is then presented: *eved kafut lo v'gdi samuch lo* — if the slave was bound and the goat was nearby but free. In that case, *patur* — the fire's owner is exempt from liability for the slave. The *Gemara* assumes this exemption reflects the principle that fire, as a category, generates no payment for human life.

### Fire vs. Pit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאֶלָּא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: חוֹמֶר בְּאֵשׁ מִבְּבוֹר – שֶׁהָאֵשׁ מוּעֶדֶת לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּין דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לָהּ בֵּין דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לָהּ, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר. וְאִילּוּ שֶׁהָאֵשׁ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה דָּמִים – מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר, לָא קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And** if Rava's proof is **rather from that which is taught** in a *baraita* (10a): The **stringency that applies to Fire as opposed to Pit** is **that** the one responsible for **Fire is** considered **forewarned** with regard **to** its **consuming both something that is fitting for it and something that is not fitting for it,** i.e., both flammable and non-flammable items. **This is not so with regard to Pit,** as damages are not paid for everything that can be damaged by a pit. **But** the *baraita* **does not teach: That** with regard to **Fire,** one is liable to **pay** the **value** of the victim even if the fire was ignited **unintentionally. This is not so with regard to Pit.** This would seem to support Rava's opinion that one is not liable to pay the value of an unintentional victim of fire.

One might ask: why does the *braita* on fire's greater stringency compare fire to *bor* rather than articulating, if it is true, that fire pays *damim* for human life while *bor* does not? The *braita* states *chomer b'eish mibbor* — fire is more stringent than a pit — in that fire is liable for things fit and unfit for burning (*she'eish muedes le'echol bein davar harauy lo bein davar she'eino rauy lo*), while a pit is exempt from *keilim* and from causing human death (based on the *drasha* of *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo keilim*). If fire does pay *damim* for human life, this would be a far more striking and important stringency distinguishing fire from pit, and the *braita* should have mentioned it.

The *Gemara* answers: *tana v'shayar* — the *braita* enumerated some differences but not all; its list is not exhaustive. This is acknowledged to be an unsatisfactory answer, and the *Gemara* presses further to find a better basis for Rava's question.

### Rava's Tone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא רָבָא גּוּפֵיהּ אִבְּעוֹיֵי מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: אִשּׁוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – מִי מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rava's statement should not be understood as an objection to Rabba's opinion, as **Rava himself had a dilemma** with regard to the matter: If a person burns another to death **unintentionally** with **his fire, does he pay** the **value** of the victim **or not?**

The *Gemara* then reframes Rava's statement entirely: *ella Rava gufei iba'i mi'ba'ei lei* — Rava himself was not asserting that fire does not pay *damim*; he was *asking* whether it does. The tone of voice makes all the difference. When Rava said "so then a fire should pay *damim*," he was posing a genuine question — almost a *tzarich iyun* — not mounting a challenge. *Eish shelo b'kavana mi meshalem dami o lo?* — Does fire pay *damim* for human life, or not?

### Two Possibilities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִי אָמְרִינַן: גַּבֵּי שׁוֹר הוּא דִּבְכַוָּונָה מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים; אֲבָל אִשּׁוֹ, דִּבְכַוָּונָה לָא מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּגַבֵּי שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא כּוֹפֶר, מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים; גַּבֵּי אִשּׁוֹ נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דִּבְכַוָּונָה לָא מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר, שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה מִיהַת מְשַׁלֵּם דָּמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the question: **Do we say** that it **is specifically with regard to** death caused by one's **ox, where** if it was done **intentionally he pays ransom** and **where** it was done **unintentionally he pays** the victim's monetary **value** instead; **but** with regard to death caused by **his fire, where** even if it was done **intentionally he does not pay ransom,** if it was done **unintentionally he does not pay** the **value either? Or perhaps, since with regard to** the case where **his ox** gored **unintentionally, although there is no** liability to pay **ransom,** nevertheless, **he** at least **pays** the **value** of the victim. One should say that **with regard to his fire too, even though** in a case where it was done **intentionally he does not pay ransom,** when it was done **unintentionally, he should pay** the **value in any event.**

Rava's question presents two possibilities. On one hand, one might reason: *gabei shor hu dibe'kavana meshalem kofer shelo bekavana meshalem dami* — by an ox, the Torah established *kofer* as the primary payment for intentional killing. Once the Torah indicated that the ox is subject to a regime of payment for human life, we can extend that to *damim* when *kofer* does not apply. Fire, however — *aval eish dibe'kavana lo meshalem kofer* — is never subject to *kofer* under any circumstances. Since the Torah never placed fire within a payment-for-life framework, perhaps fire is entirely excluded from *damim* as well. The *kofer* served as the anchor that made *damim* possible for the ox; without that anchor, fire has no basis for compensation.

### Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלָא יָדְעִינַן, תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And we do not know** the solution to this dilemma, which **shall stand** unresolved.

On the other hand: *o dilma, keivan de-gabei shor shelo b'kavana af al gav de'leika kofer meshalem dami* — once we understand that an ox pays *damim* precisely in the case *shelo b'kavana* where there is no *kofer*, we see that *damim* is not derived from *kofer* at all. It is an independent obligation, arising whenever an act of damage causes human death and no other payment applies. From that perspective, the absence of *kofer* from fire's legal framework is irrelevant — *damim* should apply to fire as well, since fire causing death *shelo b'kavana* similarly has no primary Torah payment, and the same fallback logic should operate. *Lo yedinan* — we do not know. *Teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

It is worth stepping back to appreciate the full arc of this discussion. There is no explicit *pasuk* that tells us there is *damim* to be paid when an ox kills *shelo b'kavana*. The *Gemara* introduced the concept of *tashlumin* for human life as a way of accounting for the *braita*'s ruling that payment is made on admission without witnesses. The *Gemara* built from there — by ox to slave, by ox to free person, and now potentially by ox to fire — each step an extension of a category that itself lacks direct scriptural basis. This makes the stakes of the *teiku* all the more significant: the entire edifice of *damim* as a fallback for loss of human life caused by property, and whether it extends beyond the ox to the other *avot nezikin*, is left without resolution. Whether a fire that kills can generate civil compensation for that life — a question of fundamental importance in a system that otherwise consistently refuses to assign monetary value to human death — remains open.
