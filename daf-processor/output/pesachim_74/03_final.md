# Pesachim 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Roasting Pesach

### Overview & Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד צוֹלִין אֶת הַפֶּסַח? מְבִיאִין שַׁפּוּד שֶׁל רִמּוֹן, וְתוֹחֲבוֹ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו עַד בֵּית נְקוּבָתוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן אֶת כְּרָעָיו וְאֶת בְּנֵי מֵעָיו לְתוֹכוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּמִין בִּישּׁוּל הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא תּוֹלִין חוּצָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does one roast the Paschal lamb? One brings a spit [*shappud*] of pomegranate** wood **and thrusts it into the mouth** of the lamb **until** it reaches **its anus, and** one then **puts its legs and entrails inside it** and roasts it all together; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** One does not insert its legs and entrails inside it, as **this is a type of cooking.** Anything placed inside the offering does not get roasted directly by the fire and is considered to have been cooked. **Rather, one suspends** the legs and entrails from the spit above the animal's head **outside it.**

With today's daf, 74a, we begin the seventh *perek*, *Keitzad Tzolin* — "How does one roast?" It is worth briefly situating this chapter within the broader arc of the masechta. The fifth *perek* focused heavily on the *shechita* of the *korban pesach* — issues of *shelo lishmah*, its timing relative to the *tamid*, and the intentions required for *korbanot* generally. It concluded with the actual bringing of the *korban pesach*, dividing the people into three groups, the recitation of *Hallel*, and the prohibition against owning *chametz* at the time of *shechita*. The sixth *perek* then addressed bringing the *pesach* on *Shabbat* — which preparatory acts override *Shabbat*, the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, and the question of the *chagiga* that accompanies the *pesach* and whether its preparations similarly override *Shabbat*. The *perek* also treated the case of one who erred and brought the *korban* incorrectly on *Shabbat* — whether that constitutes *to'eh b'davar mitzvah* or requires a *korban chatat*.

Now, with the *korban* already brought — whether on *Shabbat* or a weekday — the seventh *perek* turns to the preparation and eating of the *pesach*: the *mitzvah* to roast it and the mechanics of how that roasting is to be carried out.

## Spit Material Rules

### Pomegranate vs Metal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין צוֹלִין אֶת הַפֶּסַח לֹא עַל הַשַּׁפּוּד וְלֹא עַל הָאַסְכָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **One** may **not roast the Paschal lamb on the** metal **spit nor on** a metal **grill [*askela*].**

The *mishnah* opens: *Keitzad tzolin et hapesach?* — How would one roast the *korban pesach*? The answer: *mevi'in shfud shel rimon* — one would take a spit made of pomegranate wood. *Tochuv letoch piv* — one would insert it through the mouth of the animal, *ad beit nikuvato* — until it emerged from the opening at the other end. Then *v'noten et kra'av v'et b'nei me'av letocho* — one would place the legs and inner organs inside the cavity of the animal. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGelili.

It should be noted that by this point the fats — the *chelev* — have already been separated and burned on the *mizbe'ach*. What remains are the inner organs, which Rabbi Yose HaGelili says are reinserted into the cavity and roasted there.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *kemin bishul hu zeh* — placing them on the inside is like cooking. As the animal roasts, the juices accumulate inside the cavity and effectively cook the organs within — like a pot over a fire. His ruling is therefore *ella talin chutz lo* — one hangs the organs on the outside. As Rashi explains, one would insert them onto the spit itself above the animal's mouth, so that they are directly exposed to the fire and roasted independently, not enclosed within the cavity.

The *mishnah* then rules: *Ein tzolin et hapesach lo al hashfud v'lo al ha'askala* — one does not roast the *pesach* on a [metal] spit nor on a metal grate. The reason, as the *gemara* will explain, is that metal conducts heat: the portion of the spit exposed to the fire heats up, transfers that heat throughout the metal, and the animal ends up being partially roasted by the hot metal rather than directly by the fire. This violates the requirement of *tzli eish* — roasting by fire — and not by the medium of another object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי צָדוֹק: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לְטָבִי עַבְדּוֹ: צֵא וּצְלֵה לָנוּ אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל הָאַסְכָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tzadok said: There was an incident with Rabban Gamliel, who said to his slave Tavi: Go and roast the Paschal lamb for us on the grill.**

Rabbi Tzadok then cites a story: Rabban Gamliel instructed his servant Tevi to roast the *pesach* on a grate (*askala*). This is problematic on two counts — first, we just ruled against using a grate; second, the Rabban Gamliel who had a servant named Tevi lived after the *churban habayit*. That second tension is genuinely interesting and will need to be addressed, though we will leave it for now.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְנַיְתֵי שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת? אַיְּידֵי דְּחַם מִקְצָתוֹ חַם כּוּלּוֹ, וְקָמִטְּוֵי מֵחֲמַת הַשַּׁפּוּד, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״צְלִי אֵשׁ״, וְלֹא צָלִי מֵחֲמַת דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **Let them bring a metal** spit. The Gemara answers: With regard to a metal utensil, **once part of it is hot, it is all hot, and** the meat is **roasted due to the** heat of the **spit. And the Merciful One states** in the Torah that the Paschal lamb must be **roasted in fire and not roasted through something else.**

The *gemara* explains the rejection of the metal spit: *aidi dechami miktzaso cham kulo* — because if any part of it heats up, the entire thing heats up. The result is *v'kamtvei lei mei'achmas hashfud* — the animal gets roasted through the spit — whereas *rachmana amar tzli eish* — the Torah requires *tzli eish*, roasting by fire, *v'lo tzli mei'achmas davar acher* — not roasting mediated through something else.

### Other Woods Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי שֶׁל דֶּקֶל? אַיְּידֵי דְּאִית לֵיהּ שִׁיבֵּי, מַפֵּיק מַיָּא וְהָוֵי כִּמְבוּשָּׁל. וְנַיְתֵי שֶׁל תְּאֵנָה? אַיְּידִי דִּמְחַלְחֵל, מַפֵּיק מַיָּא וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְבוּשָּׁל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks why it is necessary to use specifically a spit of pomegranate wood: **Let them bring** a spit **of palm** wood. The Gemara answers: **Since** the palm branch **has grooves** between the leaves, **it gives off** a small amount of **water** from the grooves during roasting. The meat of the offering that touches the spit **is as though it is cooked.** The Gemara suggests: **Let them bring** a spit **of fig** wood. The Gemara answers: **Since it is hollow** and has sap inside, **it gives off water, and it is as though** the meat **is cooked.**

If the concern with metal is heat transfer, why not simply use a wooden spit of a different variety? The *gemara* works through the alternatives systematically. A palm branch: *aidi d'is lei shivi mafik maya* — because palm wood has grooves, and the moisture absorbed in the wood seeps out through those grooves. The result is *v'hava lei k'mevushal* — the water that collects against the *pesach* as it roasts creates something akin to cooking, which is less than ideal even if not a formal biblical violation of *bishul*.

What about fig wood? *Aidi d'mechalkhal mafik maya* — fig wood has sap that seeps out and similarly produces the effect of having liquid in contact with the roasting *pesach*. And what of oak, carob, or sycamore?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי שֶׁל אַלּוֹן, שֶׁל חָרוּב, וְשֶׁל שִׁקְמָה? אַיְּידֵי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ קִיטְרֵי, מַפֵּיק מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let them bring** a spit made **from an oak** or **from a carob** tree or **from a sycamore,** which are hard and do not have sap. The Gemara answers: With regard to each one of these trees, **since it has knots** and one must cut them off in order to smooth the branch, **it gives off water** from the locations of the cuts during roasting, and the meat is considered cooked.

*Aidi d'is bei kitrei mafik maya* — these species have knots. In order to use a branch as a spit, one must smooth it, which means cutting away the knots and exposing the interior wood beneath the bark. Once that inner wood is exposed, moisture and sap will seep out — again producing the liquid-contact problem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל רִמּוֹן נָמֵי אִית בֵּיהּ קִיטְרֵי? שִׁיעִי קִיטְרֵי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּנַבְגָּא בַּר שַׁתָּא, דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ קִיטְרֵי. וְהָא אִיכָּא בֵּי פִסְקֵיהּ! דְּמַפֵּיק לְבֵי פִסְקֵיהּ לְבַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: A branch **from a pomegranate** tree **also has knots.** The Gemara answers: **Its knots are smooth.** There is no need to straighten the branch with a knife in order to use it, and therefore it does not emit water. **And if you wish, say** that the mishna is referring to **a branch** within its first **year, which does not** yet **have knots.** The Gemara asks: **But there is the place it was cut** from the tree, and water will come from there. The Gemara answers: **One leaves the place it was cut outside** of the animal rather than inserting that side of the branch into the animal.

But does pomegranate wood not also have knots? The *gemara* offers two answers. First, *she'ei kitrei* — pomegranate knots are smoother and do not require cutting away; they can be left as is. Alternatively, *binavga bar shata d'leis bei kitrei* — one uses a one-year-old sapling, which has not yet developed knots. As for the cut ends of the spit — one end protrudes from the animal's mouth and the other from the opposite end, so any moisture that seeps from the cut ends does not come into contact with the *korban*. The case for pomegranate wood is thus established through systematic elimination.

### Metal Spit Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁשַּׁפּוּד שֶׁל עֵץ אֵינוֹ נִשְׂרָף, כָּךְ שַׁפּוּד שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת אֵינוֹ מַרְתִּיחַ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: זֶה חַם מִקְצָתוֹ — חַם כּוּלּוֹ, וְזֶה חַם מִקְצָתוֹ — אֵינוֹ חַם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Just as** the part of **a spit of wood** that is inside the animal **is not burned,** although it is over the fire, **so** the part of **a spit of metal** that is inside the animal **does not become burning hot.** There is no concern that the meat will be roasted from the heat of the spit. The Rabbis **said to him:** This is not the case. With regard to **this,** the metal, when **part of it is hot, it is all hot. And** with regard to **that,** the wood, when **part of it is hot, not all of it is hot,** and therefore the meat is cooked by the heat of the fire and not by the heat of the spit.

The *gemara* notes that our *mishnah* does not follow the view of Rabbi Yehuda. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda argued: just as a wooden spit does not burn when inserted inside the animal — since it is protected from the flame — so too a metal spit inserted into the animal will not get hot and transfer heat, since it too is mostly interior. The Rabbis responded by drawing a fundamental distinction: *zeh cham miktzato cham kulo, zeh cham miktzato eino cham kulo* — with metal, if any portion heats up, the entire thing does; with wood, heat does not transfer along the length of the material. The *mishnah* therefore rules in accordance with the Rabbis: only a wooden spit, and specifically one of pomegranate wood, may be used.

## Organ Placement

### Organ Inside/Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתֵן אֶת כְּרָעָיו וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל קוֹרֵיהוּ ״תּוֹךְ תּוֹךְ״. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן קוֹרֵיהוּ ״גְּדִי מְקוּלָּס״.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, **one places the legs** and entrails inside the lamb's body and roasts them together. **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael** would **call** the Paschal lamb: ***Tokh*, *tokh*,** because when one roasts the legs and entrails inside the lamb they make that sound, like other things that are cooked. **Rabbi Tarfon** would **call it: Helmeted kid.** In his opinion, the entrails must be roasted when they are suspended from the spit above the head of the animal, somewhat resembling a helmet.

Having established the mechanics of the *shfud*, the *mishnah* itself records the dispute about the inner organs. As noted, Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds they are placed inside the cavity, based on the verse *al kra'av v'al kirbo* — it is roasted together with its limbs and intestines. Rabbi Akiva counters that this arrangement creates a cooking effect inside the cavity, and therefore the organs must be placed outside, directly on the spit.

### Gadi Mekulas Term

A *baraita* elaborates on the terminology. Rabbi Yishmael calls the arrangement of organs inside the cavity *tuch tuch* — evoking the bubbling, pot-like sound of the juices as the animal roasts, which simultaneously highlights why Rabbi Akiva objects to it. Rabbi Tarfon refers to the whole-roasted animal, presumably with organs on the outside as per Rabbi Akiva's position, as a *gadi mekulas* — a helmeted kid. Rashi explains the etymology: *mekulas* derives from the word for a bronze helmet (*kova nechoshet*), since the organs, placed on the spit above the head of the animal, resemble a helmet atop a warrior's head. Rabbi Yishmael, then, was describing the inner-cavity arrangement, while *gadi mekulas* as Rabbi Tarfon uses it envisions the organs hanging above the head — the outer arrangement of Rabbi Akiva.

## Gadi Mekulas Rules

### Gadi Mekulas Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ גְּדִי מְקוּלָּס דְּאָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל בְּלֵילֵי פֶסַח בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה — כׇּל שֶׁצְּלָאוֹ כּוּלּוֹ כְּאֶחָד! נֶחְתַּךְ מִמֶּנּוּ אֵבֶר, נִשְׁלַק מִמֶּנּוּ אֵבֶר — אֵין זֶה גְּדִי מְקוּלָּס.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Which is the kid roasted whole that it is prohibited to eat on the nights of Passover in modern times,** so as not appear as though one sacrificed the Paschal lamb outside the Temple? It is **any** kid **that one roasted all at once** in the manner that the Paschal lamb was roasted. However, if **one of its limbs is severed** or **one of its limbs is boiled, it is no** longer considered **a kid roasted whole.**

The *gemara* now connects this term to a practical prohibition in the present day. A *baraita* asks: *eizehu gadi mekulas de'assur le'echol b'leilei pesach bizman hazeh* — what constitutes the *gadi mekulas* that is forbidden to eat on the night of *pesach* nowadays? The answer: *kol shetzla'o kulo ke'echad* — anything roasted entirely as a whole. Roasting a whole goat or lamb in the manner of the *korban pesach* is prohibited because it too closely mimics what was done with the *korban*, and such mimicry is inappropriate in the absence of the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Gadi Exceptions

Two exceptions will remove the prohibition. *Nechtach mimenu ever* — if even one limb was cut off from the animal — or *nishlak mimenu ever* — if one limb was cooked separately — then *ein zeh gadi mekulas* and the whole-roast does not constitute a *gadi mekulas*, and it is permissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר נֶחְתַּךְ מִמֶּנּוּ אֵבֶר, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מִטְּוֵי לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ, אָמְרַתְּ לָא — נִשְׁלַק מִיבַּעְיָא?! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: שֶׁשְּׁלָקוֹ בִּמְחוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at the formulation of this *baraita*. **Now, one can say** that if **one of its limbs is severed, although one roasts it together with** the rest of the animal, **you said** that it is **no** longer considered a kid roasted whole, and it is permitted in modern times. If one of its limbs **is** severed and **boiled,** which is not an approved method of preparation of the Paschal lamb, **is** it **necessary** to say that that it is not considered roasted whole? **Rav Sheshet said:** This is referring to a case **where one boiled** the limb **while it was attached** to the rest of the animal. The *halakha* teaches that even if the animal remains whole, if one of its limbs is cooked it is no longer considered a kid roasted whole.

The *gemara* probes these two cases. Regarding the case where a limb is cut off: even if that separated limb is then roasted together with the rest of the animal on the same spit, it is still not considered a *gadi mekulas* and is permissible. The cutting itself is sufficient to break the resemblance to the *korban*. As for the case where a limb was boiled — *nishlak mibaya?* — is there even a question? Should we need a special ruling for a limb that was cooked rather than merely cut? Rav Sheshet explains: the ruling refers to a limb that was cooked while still attached to the body — *deshalkia bimchubar* — for instance if a leg was somehow immersed in water and boiled while still connected to the rest of the roasting animal. Even in that unusual circumstance, the combination of a cooked limb, though still physically attached, suffices to distinguish the preparation from the *korban pesach*.

### Whole Roasting Query

Before moving on, it is worth pausing at a *Tosafot* near the top of the *amud*. Given that today one is permitted to roast the animal if it is not entirely whole, one might ask: what would the *halacha* be with respect to the *korban pesach* itself if it were roasted in pieces rather than as a whole unit? Tosafot writes that while *lechatchila* there is a *mitzvah* to roast it whole, *bediavad* if it was not roasted whole the *korban* is not rendered *passul*. The proof is drawn from a later passage in the *gemara*. This is a meaningful distinction: the whole-roasting is obligatory as an ideal but does not define the validity of the offering after the fact.

## Blood Removal Rules

### Mulyasa Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הַאי מוּלַיְיתָא — שַׁרְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא קָא בָּלַע דְּמָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבוֹלְעָהּ כָּךְ פּוֹלְטָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a general halakhic discussion related to the mishna. **Rabba said: This stuffing** of raw meat inside another animal that is being roasted **is permitted,** even if the meat that is stuffed inside has not been salted to remove the blood. **Abaye said to him: But** the meat of the animal being roasted **absorbs blood** from the stuffing. **He said to him: As it absorbs it, so it** then **emits it.** The heat of the fire causes blood to be released from the meat used as stuffing into the meat of the animal being stuffed, and the heat then draws the blood out of that meat as well.

The *gemara* now transitions into a lengthy discussion that reads, as the lecturer observes, as though it could have come straight from the pages of *Chullin* — a detailed treatment of blood removal from meat. The occasion is the *mulyasa*: a bird stuffed with ground meat, to be roasted without prior salting. The question is whether the roasting process will adequately draw out the blood both from the bird itself and from the ground meat stuffed inside it.

Before entering the specifics of the *mulyasa*, it is essential to understand the underlying principles of blood removal. Blood is prohibited only when it has left the meat and returned to it. Blood that remains in its natural state within the flesh — not pooled, not released — is not forbidden in that form. One could, in theory, eat raw unsalted meat, since the blood within it has not undergone the forbidden process of leaving and returning.

The primary method of preparing meat is salting: salt draws out the blood, which then runs off the meat, and the meat can subsequently be placed in a pot for cooking. However, roasting over fire is also an accepted method of blood removal. The critical distinction between salting and roasting is this: when one salts meat, the goal is complete blood extraction, after which the meat may be placed in a pot — since there is no blood left to come out and re-enter. With roasting, even if not all the blood is extracted, the blood that remains is not problematic as long as the meat is not subsequently placed in a pot, because whatever blood came out fell away from the fire and whatever remains inside never underwent the forbidden cycle. Roasting well enough will extract all the blood and permit subsequent cooking; roasting less thoroughly is still acceptable as long as no pot is involved.

Now, with the *mulyasa*: the ground meat is stuffed inside the bird, and neither has been salted. As the bird roasts, blood is drawn from the ground meat stuffing. But to exit the bird, that blood must first pass through the walls of the bird's body. There is a genuine concern that the blood from the stuffing, on its way out, gets absorbed by the bird's flesh — in which case the bird's flesh has absorbed blood that came out of another piece of meat, which would render it forbidden.

### Rava vs Abaye

Rava rules: *hai mulyasa sharya* — this stuffed bird is permissible. Abaye challenges him: *v'ha kabla dama?* — but the bird absorbs the blood from the stuffing! Rava replies with the principle *ke-volo kach polto* — as it absorbs, so it releases. The same heat that drives the blood from the stuffing into the walls of the bird continues to drive it outward and out of the bird entirely. The absorption and release are part of a single continuous process driven by the fire.

### Pesach Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: נוֹתֵן אֶת כְּרָעָיו וְאֶת בְּנֵי מֵעָיו לְתוֹכוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן כְּבוֹלְעוֹ כָּךְ פּוֹלְטוֹ? אָמְרִי: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה דִּמְחַלְחֵל, מֵידָב דָּיְיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** this mishna **supports him: He places its legs and its entrails inside** the Paschal lamb and roasts them together. **What is the reason** that it is permitted to do this? Is it **not because we say: As it absorbs it, so it emits it?** Although Rabbi Akiva disputes this statement, his opinion is due to the unique *halakhot* of the Paschal lamb. It seems that everyone agrees that there is no concern about the prohibition against consuming blood. The Gemara refutes this proof: **Say it is different there,** in the case of the Paschal lamb. **Since there is the place of the slaughter, which is hollow** and open, the blood **flows** out.

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof for Rava's position from the *mishnah*'s ruling that one places the inner organs of the *pesach* inside the animal's cavity. Why is there no concern that the blood from the organs gets absorbed by the surrounding flesh? Is it not because we apply *ke-volo kach polto* — the blood drawn out by the heat from the organs likewise exits through the surrounding flesh? The *gemara* rejects this proof, however. *Shani hatam, kivan d'ika bei shechita d'mechalchel* — there it is different, because the place of the *shechita* is open, and that exposed incision — with its severed veins — creates a continuous flow of blood downward, which draws out all blood in the system with greater force. In the case of the *mulyasa*, we cannot assume this same facilitated drainage is present, particularly if the bird is not oriented with the *bei shechita* pointing downward.

### Heart Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַלֵּב, קוֹרְעוֹ וּמוֹצִיא אֶת דָּמוֹ. לֹא קְרָעוֹ — קוֹרְעוֹ לְאַחַר בִּישּׁוּלוֹ, וּמוּתָּר. מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן כְּבוֹלְעוֹ כָּךְ פּוֹלְטוֹ? שָׁאנֵי לֵב, דְּשִׁיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests further: **Let us say** that the following mishna **supports him:** With regard to **the heart** of an animal, one must **tear it and remove its blood** before one roasts or cooks it. And if **he did not tear it** beforehand, **he tears it after it is cooked,** i.e., roasted, **and it is permitted. What is the reason** the heart is permitted although there is presumably still blood inside? **Is it not because we say** that **as it absorbs it, so it emits it,** and therefore as the heart is roasted the blood is absorbed in the meat and then discharged, so that no blood is left in the meat, and whatever is still inside the hollow part of the heart can be removed when it is torn open? This would support the opinion of Rabba. The Gemara refutes the proof: **A heart is different** because **it is smooth** and does not absorb much blood.

The *gemara* next attempts another proof, this time from the *halacha* of the heart. When preparing a heart for consumption, one is required to cut it open and remove the pooled blood from the cavity before cooking or roasting. If one did not cut it open — *lo kra'o* — one may cut it open *after* cooking (*bishulov*) and it remains permissible. Rashi notes that *bishulov* here is not to be taken literally as cooking; it means after roasting. The case then would seem to support *ke-volo kach polto*: the blood in the cavity is drawn into the walls by the heat and then drawn back out again. 

The *gemara* rejects this proof as well, though with a striking claim: *shani lev d'shia* — the heart is different, because its walls are smooth. Rashi goes further — the walls of the heart are so smooth that they do not even absorb the blood at all: *v'lo bala*. According to this answer, *ke-volo kach polto* is not operative here, because there is no absorption in the first place. Indeed, Rashi suggests that on this reading, *bishulov* can be taken literally — even if the heart was actually cooked in a pot, since the walls never absorbed the blood from the cavity, there is nothing forbidden that re-entered the flesh. This is a striking position, difficult to intuit, though the physiological observation that the blood pooled in the heart cavity originates from elsewhere in the circulatory system rather than from the heart muscle itself may be relevant context — though that distinction would not, from a *halachic* standpoint, change the concern about absorption.

### Clear Liquids Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אִינִי) וְהָא רָבִין סָבָא טַפְלֵיהּ הָהִיא בַּר גּוֹזָלָא לְרַב, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מְעַלֵּי טִפְלֵיהּ, הַב לִי וְאֵיכוֹל! הָהִיא בִּסְמִידָא דְּמִפְּרִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Ravin the Elder wrap a particular young dove in dough for Rav** and roast it, **and** Rav **said to him: If its dough** tastes **good, give me** some **and I will eat?** Apparently, according to Rav, although the breading absorbed blood, it also certainly discharged it during the roasting. The Gemara refutes this point: **That** incident involved **fine flour [*semida*], which is crumbly** and allows the blood to flow through it.

The *gemara* records a related incident involving Ravina. Ravina was served a coated pigeon (*bar gozla*) that had been prepared for Rav. Rav said: *ei ma'ali taphlei havu li v'achilna* — if the outer coating has taken on good flavor from the bird, give it to me and I will eat it. Ravina ate it himself. The point is that Rav expressed interest in the juices absorbed by the coating as an indicator of good roasting, and showed no concern that the coating had trapped blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רָבָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא וּטְפַלוּ לֵיהּ בַּר אֲווֹזָא, אָמַר: אִי לָא דַּחֲזִיתֵיהּ דְּזִיג כְּזוּזָא חִיוָּרָא, לָא אֲכַלִי מִינֵּיהּ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״כְּבוֹלְעוֹ כָּךְ פּוֹלְטוֹ״ — מַאי אִירְיָא כִּי זִיג? אֲפִילּוּ כִּי לָא זִיג, נָמֵי! הָתָם בְּחִיוָּרְתָּא דְּשָׁרִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't Rava happen** to come **to the house of the Exilarch, and they breaded a young goose for him,** and **he said: If I had not seen that** the breading **is** as **clear as a white,** i.e., new, **coin, I** would **not eat from it** out of concern that it absorbed some of the blood? **And if it should enter your mind** to accept the principle that **as it absorbs it so it emits it, why** note that he ate it **particularly because it was clear? Even if it was not clear,** it should **also** be permitted. The Gemara responds: **There,** it was talking about **white** flour, **which is firm** and does not allow the blood to pass through; Rava ate it only because its color indicated that no blood remained in the breading.

More instructive is an incident involving Rava at the home of the *Reish Galuta*, where he was served a coated goose. Rava said: *ilo de-hazisei de-zig ke-zuza chivra* — had I not seen the juices flowing off clear, like a white coin, *lo achli minei* — I would not have eaten from it. The implication is that once Rava observed entirely clear liquids flowing from the roasting bird, he was satisfied that all blood had been expelled. Only after that evidence of complete blood removal was he willing to eat.

This raises the question: if we accept *ke-volo kach polto* universally, why should clear liquids be necessary? If whatever is absorbed is always released, why should the color of the flowing liquids matter? The *gemara* answers that Rava's case involved a coating of *chivarta de-shari* — a dense white flour — which actively traps juices and blood. For such a coating, the principle of *ke-volo kach polto* cannot simply be assumed; direct evidence of clear liquids is required.

### Flour Coating Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִילְכְתָא: דִּסְמִידָא, בֵּין אַסְמֵיק בֵּין לָא אַסְמֵיק — שַׁרְיָא. דְּחִיוָּרְתָּא, אִי זִיג כְּזוּזָא חִיוָּרָא — שַׁרְיָא, אִי לָא — אֲסִיר. דִּשְׁאָר קְמָחִים, אַסְמֵיק — אָסוּר, לָא אַסְמֵיק — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that if one makes the breading **of fine flour, whether it turned red** from blood **or did not turn red, it is permitted.** The following rule applies to breading **of white** flour: **If it is clear like a white coin, it is permitted; if not, it is prohibited.** With regard to breading **of other** types of **flour,** which are not especially firm or crumbly, if the breading **turned red, it is prohibited;** if **it did not turn red, it is permitted.**

The discussion of coatings yields a tripartite *halacha*. If the coating is *smida* — a fine, loose flour, like scattered breadcrumbs with gaps between them through which liquids can freely escape — then *bein asmik bein lo asmik sharya*: whether or not the flowing liquids appear red, the coated meat is permissible. Even if blood is still visible, whatever came out of the meat has fallen away, and what remains inside never underwent the forbidden process.

If the coating is the dense white flour that traps juices, then *i zig ke-zuza chivra sharya* — only if one observes clear liquids flowing off is it permissible; *i lo assira* — if clear liquids have not been observed, it is forbidden, since one cannot assume complete blood removal.

If the coating is something intermediate — a medium flour — then *asmik assur, lo asmik sharya*: if the flowing liquids appear red, the coating has absorbed blood and it is forbidden; if they do not appear red, it is permissible, since the absence of red in the flowing liquids is sufficient evidence that no blood is being trapped.

### Mulyasa Final Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מוּלַיְיתָא, מַאן דְּאָסַר — אֲפִילּוּ פּוּמָּא לְתַחַת, וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי — אֲפִילּוּ פּוּמָּא לְעֵיל. וְהִילְכְתָא: מוּלַיְיתָא שְׁרֵי, אֲפִילּוּ פּוּמָּא לְעֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **this** meat **stuffing** in an animal: **The one who prohibits** one to eat it, Abaye, does so **even** if **the opening** is facing **downward,** allowing the blood to escape more easily. **And the one who permits** one to eat it, Rabba, does so **even** if **the opening** is facing **upward.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is that stuffing is permitted even** if **the opening** is facing **upward,** in accordance with the lenient opinion.

Having surveyed all the related cases, the *gemara* arrives at its *halacha* for the *mulyasa* itself. The debate between those who forbid it and those who permit it corresponds to the question of orientation: does it matter whether the *puma* — the opening of the bird, presumably where the neck was cut — is facing downward toward the fire, facilitating drainage, or upward, impeding it? The strict position would forbid the *mulyasa* even when the mouth faces down; the lenient position would permit it even when the mouth faces up. The final *halacha*: *mulyasa sharya afilu puma le'eil* — the stuffed bird is permissible even with the mouth pointing upward. This is a notably lenient conclusion, resting on confidence that the heat of the fire draws out all blood regardless of orientation.

## Red Meat Debate

### Umtsa & Coals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּמְצָא, בֵּיעֵי, וּמִיזְרְקֵי, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא. בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ רַב אַחָא לְחוּמְרָא וְרָבִינָא לְקוּלָּא, וְהִילְכְתָא כְּרָבִינָא לְקוּלָּא. לְבַר מֵהָנֵי תְּלָת דְּרַב אַחָא לְקוּלָּא וְרָבִינָא לְחוּמְרָא, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַב אַחָא לְקוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes a further discussion concerning the topic of blood absorbed in meat and the preparation of meat permitted for eating. The Gemara addresses three cases: **Raw meat [*umtza*]** that is eaten without being salted, **testicles** of an animal, **and** the large **veins** of the neck. **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagreed about this.** The Gemara points out: **In all** their discussions about **the Torah,** whenever there is a dispute between them and there is no explanation as to which of them holds which opinion, the opinion of **Rav Aḥa is stringent and** the opinion of **Ravina is lenient, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ravina to be lenient.** This applies to all their disputes **except for these three,** in which **Rav Aḥa is lenient and Ravina is stringent, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Aḥa to be lenient.**

The *gemara* now turns to three related cases, all involving items with unusually high blood content, and in each case the question is whether placing them directly on coals — rather than salting or mounting on a spit over an open flame — adequately draws the blood out. The three items are *umtsa* (a piece of red meat), *bei* (testicles), and *mizraki* (what Rashi identifies as blood vessels — veins or arteries). In all three, the debate is between Rav Acha and Ravina. This is notable because a general principle applies throughout *Shas*: *Rav Acha l'chumra v'Ravina l'kula* — Rav Acha is characteristically strict and Ravina lenient — and we rule like Ravina leniently. These three cases are among the rare exceptions: *l'var mehani tlata d'Rav Acha l'kula* — in these three, Rav Acha takes the lenient position and Ravina the strict one — and we nonetheless rule like Rav Acha leniently in all three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אוּמְצָא דְּאַסְמֵיק, חַתְכֵיהּ וּמַלְחֵיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ לִקְדֵרָה שְׁרֵי. שַׁפְּדֵיהּ בְּשַׁפּוּדָא — שְׁרֵי, מֵידָב דָּיֵיב. אַחֲתֵיהּ אַגּוּמְרֵי — פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָסַר וְחַד שָׁרֵי. מַאן דְּאָסַר, מִצְמָית צָמֵית. וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי, מִישְׁאָב שָׁאֵיב. וְהִילְכְתָא: מִישְׁאָב שָׁאֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **this** piece of **raw meat that became red** from the blood inside it, if **one cut it and salted it,** it is **permitted even to** cook them in **a pot** because it is clear that salt removes blood from meat. If one **put it on a spit** in order to roast it, **it is permitted** because the blood **flows** out. With regard to a case where one **placed it on coals, Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagreed about** the *halakha* in **this** case; **one prohibited** it **and one permitted** it. **The one who prohibited** it reasoned that the coals cause the meat **to shrivel** and harden, trapping the blood inside. **And the one who permitted** it reasoned that the heat of the coals **draws out** the blood, leaving only the meat. **And the halakha** is that the heat of the coals **draws out** the blood.

For the *umtsa*: if one cuts it and salts it, *afilu l'kdira shari* — it may even be placed in a pot afterward, since the salting has fully extracted all blood. If one places it on a spit (*b'shipuda*), it is permissible — *meidiv diyah*, the blood flows out. If one places it directly on coals (*atchei agumrei*): *pligi bah Rav Acha v'Ravina*. The one who forbids holds *mitzmat tzamit* — the coals cause the meat to contract and trap the blood within. The one who permits holds *mishav shaiv* — even coals draw the blood out. The *halacha* is like the lenient opinion: the coals draw out the blood.

One might ask: even if the coals trap the blood, why should that be forbidden? As we have established, blood that remains inside meat and never comes out is not problematic — only blood that exits and re-enters creates a prohibition. The concern here therefore likely pertains to the question of whether one may subsequently place the meat in a pot after using coals — if the coals did not fully extract the blood, cooking it afterward would cause blood to exit and re-enter.

### Testicles Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בֵּיעֵי, חַתְכִינְהוּ וּמַלְחִינְהוּ — אֲפִילּוּ לִקְדֵרָה שַׁרְיָין. תְּלִינְהוּ בְּשַׁפּוּדָא — שַׁרְיָין, מֵידָב דָּיֵיב. אַחְתִינְהוּ אַגּוּמְרֵי — פְּלִיגִי בֵּיהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָסַר וְחַד שָׁרֵי. מַאן דְּאָסַר, מִצְמָית צָמֵית. וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי, מִישְׁאָב שָׁאֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And, so too,** with regard to **testicles:** If one **cut them and salted them, they are permitted even to** be cooked in **a pot.** If one **hung them on a spit** in order to roast them, **they are permitted** because the blood **flows** out. With regard to a case where one **placed them on coals, Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagreed about this; one prohibited** it **and one permitted** it. **The one who prohibited** it reasoned that **it shrivels, and the one who permitted** it reasoned that the heat **draws out** the blood.

The ruling for *bei* (testicles) is identical in structure. If cut and salted, the meat may be placed even in a pot. If placed on a spit, it is permissible — *meidiv diyah*, the blood flows out. If placed on coals, Rav Acha and Ravina debate: one holds *mitzmat tzamit* (contraction traps the blood), the other *mishav shaiv* (the coals draw it out). The *halacha* follows the lenient view that the coals draw it out.

### Arteries & Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן מִיזְרְקֵי חַתְכֵיהּ וּמַלְחֵיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ לִקְדֵרָה שְׁרֵי. תַּלְיֵיהּ בְּשַׁפּוּדָא, בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה לְתַתַּאי — שְׁרֵי, מֵידָב דָּאֵיב. אַחֲתֵיהּ אַגּוּמְרֵי — פְּלִיגִי רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָסַר וְחַד שָׁרֵי. מַאן דְּאָסַר, מִצְמָית צָמֵית. וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי, מִישְׁאָב שָׁאֵיב. וְהִלְכְתָא: מִישְׁאָב שָׁאֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And, so too,** with regard to large **veins:** If one **cut them and salted them, it is permitted even to** cook them in **a pot.** If one **hung them on a spit** and **the place of the** incision of the **slaughter is** facing **downward, it is permitted** because the blood **flows** out. With regard to a case where one **placed it on coals, Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagreed about this** matter; **one prohibited** it **and one permitted** it. **The one who prohibited** it reasoned that **it shrivels, and the one who permitted** it reasoned that the heat **draws out** the blood. **And the *halakha*** is that the heat of the coals **draws out** the blood, and it is permitted.

The same structure applies to *mizraki*. If cut and salted, permissible even for a pot. On a spit — permissible. Then the *gemara* specifies: *beisa d'shchita l'tata* — the section of the neck where the *shechita* was performed should be oriented downward, so that the exposed veins and arteries at the cut allow blood to drain freely. When this condition is met, *meidiv diyah* — the blood flows out. On coals: the same debate between contracting and drawing, with the same lenient conclusion.

### Liver on Coals

The *gemara* adds a parallel case for the liver (*kavda*) placed on coals. Here the debate is between Rabba and Rav Yosef: one forbids on the grounds that the coals cause contraction and trap the blood; the other permits because the coals draw the blood out. The *halacha* is again: *mishav shaiv* — the coals draw the blood, and the liver is permissible.

## Vinegar Pickling

### Meat & Vinegar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אוּמְצָא דְּאַסְמֵיק חַלְיֵיהּ — אֲסִיר. לָא אַסְמֵיק חַלְיֵיהּ — שְׁרֵי. רָבִינָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לָא אַסְמֵיק נָמֵי חַלְיֵיהּ — אֲסִיר, אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלֵית בֵּהּ שׁוּרְיָיקֵי דְּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another discussion with regard to blood absorbed in meat. People would soak raw meat (*Tosafot*) in vinegar in order to ensure that none of the blood would separate from its original place and prohibit the meat from being eaten, as it is permitted to eat blood that has not separated from its original place. **This** piece of **raw meat, whose vinegar became red** due to the blood absorbed in it, **is forbidden.** If **its vinegar did not become red, it is permitted. Ravina said: Even** if **its vinegar did not become red, it is forbidden; it is impossible that it does not have streaks of blood.**

The final sugya of the daf concerns meat placed in vinegar. The scenario: one takes a piece of meat (*umtsa d'atmik* — that has become very red) and places it in *chala* — vinegar. The meat's redness is the key indicator. When meat becomes red in vinegar, what does that signify? Did the blood get trapped inside, making the meat appear very red? Or did blood come out of the meat into the vinegar and then re-enter, producing the redness? If the latter, then both the meat and the vinegar are forbidden — the blood that exited and returned renders the meat forbidden, and the blood that is now in the vinegar renders it forbidden as well.

Following the *girsa* of Tosafot (based on the Bahag): *hi v'chalyie assur* — when the meat turns red, both the meat and the vinegar are forbidden. The blood came out of the meat, entered the vinegar, and returned to the meat — a complete forbidden cycle. *Lo asmit* — if the meat did not turn red, then *hi v'chalyie shari* — both the meat and the vinegar are permissible, since there is no evidence that blood left the meat and returned.

### Ravina's Vinegar

Ravina disagrees with this latter ruling. *Afilu lo asmit nami chalyie assur* — even when the meat did not turn red, the vinegar is still forbidden. His reasoning: *iy efshar d'leis bei shiriki d'dama* — it is impossible that the vinegar contains no strands of blood at all. Some blood must have exited the meat into the vinegar, even if not enough returned to cause the meat to change color. Therefore, even if the meat itself remains permissible — since it evidently did not re-absorb blood — the vinegar is minimally forbidden because it contains blood that came out.

### Vinegar Reuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר בַּר אַמֵּימָר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַבָּא מְגַמַּע לֵיהּ גַּמּוֹעֵי. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַב אָשֵׁי גּוּפֵיהּ מְגַמַּע לֵיהּ גַּמּוֹעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Ameimar said to Rav Ashi: Father,** i.e., Ameimar, **would swallow** the vinegar and was unconcerned that there may be blood in it. **Some say Rav Ashi himself would swallow it.**

Mar bar Ameimar reported to Rav Ashi: his father (*abba*) would drink (*maglei gimei*) — that is, would freely use — the vinegar after pickling meat, as long as the meat had not turned red. According to an alternate version, Rav Ashi himself would drink such vinegar without concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר בַּר אַמֵּימָר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַבָּא, הַאי חַלָּא דְּחָלֵיט בֵּיהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, תּוּ לָא תָּאנֵי חָלֵיט בֵּיהּ. מַאי שְׁנָא מֵחַלָּא מַתְמְהָא דְּחָלְטִינַן בֵּיהּ? הָתָם — אִיתֵיהּ לְקִיּוּהָא דְפֵירָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ. הָכָא — לֵיתָא לְקִיּוּהָא דְפֵירָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Ameimar said to Rav Ashi:** The practice of my **father,** Ameimar, was that with regard to **vinegar in which he had soaked** meat **one time** to keep in its blood, **he would not soak** meat **in it again.** It could no longer keep the blood in the meat. The Gemara asks: In **what** way is vinegar that has been used once **different from weak vinegar, in which we soak** meat without concern that it will be unable to keep the blood in the meat? The Gemara explains: **There, the sharpness of the fruit** remains present in the vinegar **in its pure,** unadulterated form, despite the fact that the vinegar itself is not sharp. **Here, the sharpness of the fruit does not** remain present in the vinegar **in its pure,** unadulterated form because it has already been used with the meat.

Mar bar Ameimar then raised a further point to Rav Ashi: his father would never reuse vinegar a second time to pickle meat (*hai chala d'chalifei chada zimna, tu lo tanni chalifei chalifei*). This prompts a question from the *gemara*: how is reused vinegar any different from weak vinegar that we are permitted to use for pickling in the first place? If weak vinegar is acceptable, why should strong vinegar that has been used once be rejected?

### Fresh Vinegar Power

The *gemara* resolves the distinction: *hasam issa le-keifa de-peiri be-aneih* — in the case of fresh weak vinegar, even if it is weak, it retains the original power of the fruit (*keifa de-peiri*) in its natural, unspent form. *Hacha leisa le-keifa de-peiri be-aneih* — here, in reused vinegar, the original power of the fruit is no longer intact, since it has already been expended in the first round of pickling. The pickling efficacy of vinegar depends not merely on its current strength but on whether its natural properties remain intact. Once spent, it is qualitatively different from fresh vinegar of equivalent concentration, and therefore unsuitable for reuse.

With this, the *gemara* concludes its extended discussion — one that, as noted, reads very much like *Chullin* — of the principles governing blood removal from meat through salting, roasting, and pickling in vinegar.
