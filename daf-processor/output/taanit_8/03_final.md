# Taanit 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rain's Power & Sins

### Gadol Yom HaGeshamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים כְּיוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַרְעִיפוּ שָׁמַיִם מִמַּעַל וּשְׁחָקִים יִזְּלוּ צֶדֶק תִּפְתַּח אֶרֶץ וְיִפְרוּ יֶשַׁע וּצְדָקָה תַצְמִיחַ יַחַד אֲנִי ה׳ בְּרָאתִיו״. ״בְּרָאתִים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״בְּרָאתִיו״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of rain. **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: The day of the rains is** as **great as the day on which the heavens and earth were created, as it is stated: "Drop down, heavens, from above, let the skies pour down righteousness; let the earth open that they may bring forth salvation, and let it cause righteousness to spring up together; I, the Lord, have created it"** (Isaiah 45:8). The Gemara explains that the verse **does not say: I have created them,** in the plural, **but: I have created it.** In other words, the verse is referring to rain, rather than to the heavens and the earth, which indicates that rainfall is as important as the creation of the world.

Today's *daf* is 8. We pick up at the very bottom of 7b, working through a list of statements about how powerful a day of rain is — *gadol yom hageshamim* — along with related digressions.

### Ruach Avrah VaTetaharem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁרָקִיעַ נַעֲשֶׂה בְּהוֹרִין בְּהוֹרִין לְהוֹרִיד טַל וּמָטָר — ״רוּחַ עָבְרָה וַתְּטַהֲרֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this verse, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Even when the sky is comprised of bright clouds** that serve **to bring down dew and rain,** no rain will fall, as **"the wind passes and cleanses them."**

The *Tanna de-bei Rabbi Ishmael* teaches: even at a time when the heavens appear to be covered in dark splotches, as if they are ready to pour down rain — *afilu besha'ah sherakia na'aseh bahurin bahurin lehorid tal u-matar* — nevertheless, *veruach avrah vatetaharem*, a wind will pass and cleanse the clouds away. This statement is based on the verse "וְעַתָּה לֹא רָאוּ אוֹר בָּהִיר הוּא בַּשְּׁחָקִים וְרוּחַ עָבְרָה וַתְּטַהֲרֵם" — the clouds are ready, yet a wind disperses them. The reason given is the sin of *bitul Torah*.

### Bitul Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִים אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בִּיטּוּל תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּעֲצַלְתַּיִם יִמַּךְ הַמְּקָרֶה״ — בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְלוּת שֶׁהָיָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא עָסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה — נַעֲשֶׂה שׂוֹנְאוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מָךְ. וְאֵין ״מָךְ״ אֶלָּא עָנִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם מָךְ הוּא מֵעֶרְכֶּךָ״. וְאֵין ״מְקָרֶה״ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַמְקָרֶה בַמַּיִם עֲלִיּוֹתָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ketina said: The rains are withheld only due to** the sin of **dereliction in** the study of **Torah, as it is stated: "By slothfulness the rafters [*hamekare*] will sink in [*yimakh*],** and through idleness of the hands the house leaks" (Ecclesiastes 10:18). **Due to slothfulness that was** present **amongst the Jewish people, that they did not occupy** themselves **with Torah, the enemy of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** a euphemism for God Himself, has **sunk. And sunk [*makh*]** means **nothing other** than **poor, as it is stated: "But if he is too poor [*makh*] for your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:8). **And "rafters [*mekare*]"** means **nothing other** than a reference to **the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "Who lays the beams [*hamekare*] of Your upper chambers in the water"** (Psalms 104:3).

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: the opening of this discussion likely should have begun here, approximately ten lines from the bottom of the page. This passage is part of a broader catalog — on one side, a catalog of the ways in which a day of rain is praised and the things it resembles; on the other, a catalog of the sins that cause rain to be withheld.

It is worth exploring whether these correspondences represent causal explanations for the absence of rain, or whether they reflect a deeper logical and religious-symbolic relationship. The sin of *bitul Torah* makes a great deal of sense in this framework: rain is not merely about sustenance; it is seen as a manifestation of God's closeness and connection to the people. *Torah* and *tefillah* are the means through which the people express that relationship from their side. The correspondence between *bitul Torah* and the withholding of rain therefore carries a profound internal logic.

### Gezel & Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִים אֶלָּא בַּעֲוֹן גָּזֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כַּפַּיִם כִּסָּה אוֹר״. בַּעֲוֹן כַּפַּיִם — כִּסָּה אוֹר. וְאֵין ״כַּפַּיִם״ אֶלָּא חָמָס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִן הֶחָמָס אֲשֶׁר בְּכַפֵּיהֶם״, וְאֵין ״אוֹר״ אֶלָּא מָטָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָפִיץ עֲנַן אוֹרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: The rains are withheld only due to the sin of robbery, as it is stated: "He covers His hands with the light,** and He has commanded it due to imploring" (Job 36:32). This means that due **to the sin of** stealing **hands,** God **has covered the light** and no rain will fall. **And** Rabbi Ami adds that the term **"hand"** means **nothing other** than a sin of **violence, as it is stated: "And from the violence that is in their hands"** (Jonah 3:8). **And "light" means nothing other** than **rain, as it is stated: "He spreads abroad the cloud of His light"** (Job 37:11).

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *ein geshamim ne'etzarin ella ba'avon gezel* — rain is withheld only because of the sin of *gezel*, of theft and coveting. The verse states: "עַל כַּפַּיִם כִּסָּה אוֹר" — on the palms, light is hidden. *Ve-ein kapayim ella chamas* — and *kapayim* refers to *chamas*, to theft and coveting, as the verse continues: "וּמִן הַחָמָס אֲשֶׁר בְּכַפֵּיהֶם." *Ve-ein or ella matar* — and *or*, light, refers to rain, as the verse says: "יָפִיץ עָנָן אוֹרוֹ," the cloud spreads forth its light.

### Gezel & Tefillah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ — יַרְבֶּה בִּתְפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְצַו עָלֶיהָ בַּמַּפְגִּיעַ״, וְאֵין פְּגִיעָה אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the remedy** of one who has caused the rain to be withheld? He should **increase** his **prayers, as it is stated** in the same chapter: **"And He has commanded it due to imploring"** (Job 36:32), **and "imploring"** means **nothing other** than **prayer, as it is stated: "Therefore, do not pray you for this nation,** neither lift up cry nor prayer for them, **neither implore Me"** (Jeremiah 7:16).

*Mai takantei?* What is the remedy? *Yarbeh bitfillah* — one should increase in prayer, as the verse states: "וַיְצַו עָלֶיהָ בַּמַּפְגִּיעַ" — and *ein pegi'ah ella tfillah*, and *pegi'ah*, encountering, refers only to *tefillah*, as the verse says: "וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי."

One might ask why the remedy for *gezel* is not *teshuvah* and return of the stolen property, which is the normative correction for theft. One could also note the distinction elsewhere in the *Gemara* between *gezeilah* and *geneivah* — by *gezeilah* one fears neither man nor God — and from that perspective, the remedy of *tefillah* functions to re-establish that very relationship with God that *gezeilah* represents a rejection of. But the major theme of this *masechet* is that rain is God's favor and presence, and the response to its absence is fasting and prayer — the act of reaching out to re-establish that connection.

The connection between *gezel* and the withholding of rain also parallels the earlier discussion of *terumot u-ma'asrot*: God provides; one's acknowledgment of that provision is expressed through giving what is designated. To steal from another is, in its way, to fail to acknowledge that God gives to all — and so what God gives is withheld.

### Rakia Kehah Kabarzel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם קֵהָה הַבַּרְזֶל וְהוּא לֹא פָנִים קִלְקַל״, אִם רָאִיתָ רָקִיעַ שֶׁקֵּיהָה כַּבַּרְזֶל מִלְּהוֹרִיד טַל וּמָטָר — בִּשְׁבִיל מַעֲשֵׂה הַדּוֹר שֶׁהֵן מְקוּלְקָלִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא לֹא פָנִים קִלְקַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ami said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "If the iron is blunt, and does not whet the edge"** (Ecclesiastes 10:10)? **If you see a sky that is blunt as iron, in that** it does not **bring down dew and rain,** this is **due to** the **deeds of the generation, which are corrupt, as it is stated: "And does not whet [*kilkal*] the edge [*panim*]."** *Panim*, which also means face, is often used in reference to the leaders of a generation, while the term *kilkal* is similar to the word for corrupt, *mekulkalin*.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *mai de-chtiv im kehah habarzel vehu lo fanim kilkel?* If you see the heavens as dull as iron — *im raita rakia shekehah kabarzel milehorid tal u-matar* — it is because of the corruption of the entire generation: *bishvil maaseh hador shehein mekulkallin*, as the verse states, *vehu lo fanim kilkel*. The word *lo* can be read as the possessive *lo* — their faces, the way they present themselves, are corrupted — meaning the entire nation has sinned.

This statement is not connected to any particular sin. It echoes the *pesukim* of the *Shema* — you observe the *mitzvot* and rain follows; you transgress and it does not. The absence of rain is a sign of a generation that has gone astray.

### Yitgabru BeRachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה תַּקָּנָתָן — יִתְגַּבְּרוּ בְּרַחֲמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַחֲיָלִים יְגַבֵּר וְיִתְרוֹן הַכְשֵׁיר חׇכְמָה״, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אִם הוּכְשְׁרוּ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is their remedy? They** must **increase** their prayers **for mercy, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"Then must he increase his strength, but wisdom is profitable to direct"** (Ecclesiastes 10:10). This verse hints that rain will fall if one increases his strength, i.e., his prayers for mercy. The last part of the verse means that, **all the more so, if their deeds** had been righteous and **direct from the beginning,** the rains would not have been withheld.

*Mah takanatam?* Again, strikingly, the remedy is not *teshuvah* but prayer: *yitgabru berachamim*, they should strengthen themselves with compassion — that is, call out to God in prayer — as the verse states: "וְחַיָּלִים יִגְבַּר וְיִתְרוֹן הַכְשֵׁר חָכְמָה."

*Veyitron hachsher chochmah kol shekein im hichshiru maaseihem mei'ikara* — it would have been better had they been wise from the outset and corrected their deeds before sinning. But now that they have sinned and rain is not coming, they should call out to God. Not *teshuvah* — call out to God. The emphasis throughout this passage, and indeed throughout this *masechet*, is on prayer as the primary mode of reconnecting with the Divine.

## Talmud Torah & Rain

### Talmid & Kasheh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אִם רָאִיתָ תַּלְמִיד שֶׁתַּלְמוּדוֹ קָשֶׁה עָלָיו כַּבַּרְזֶל — בִּשְׁבִיל מִשְׁנָתוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ סְדוּרָה עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא לֹא פָנִים קִלְקַל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a different interpretation of the same verse. **Reish Lakish said: If you see a student whose studies are hard as iron for him,** i.e., difficult to understand, this is **due to his** lack of familiarity with the **Mishna, which is not organized for him.** If the Mishna is unclear, any further study of Gemara is rendered all the more difficult, **as it is stated: "And does not whet [*kilkal*] the edge [*panim*]"** (Ecclesiastes 10:10). As panim can also mean surface, this indicates that the surface, i.e., the basic statements of the Mishna, is corrupted. As stated previously, *kilkal* can also mean corrupted.

*Reish Lakish* now shifts the discussion — as happens repeatedly in this *daf* — from rain to *Talmud Torah*. *Im raita talmid shelimudo kasheh alav kabarzel* — if you see a student whose learning is as hard as iron, who is hitting his head against the wall and cannot make progress — the reason is *bishvil mishnato she'einah sdura alav*: because his information is not well organized. In an oral culture, one must first internalize and memorize all the material; only then can one analyze and think through issues clearly. If a student struggles to think through halachic problems with clarity, it is because his foundational knowledge is not in order.

The verse says: *vehu lo fanim kilkel* — he has, as it were, corrupted the face of things, disordered the basic organization of his knowledge. *Mah takanto?* The remedy is *yirbeh bishivah* — *Rashi* explains this not merely as sitting, but as spending time in the *yeshiva* environment, as the verse says: *vechayalim yigaber*. *Rashi* reads this as: the students are like troops — *chayalot b'machanot* — and through interaction with fellow *yeshiva* students one's knowledge becomes strengthened. *Veyitron hichshir chachmah kol shekein im mishnato sedurah alav mei'ikara* — it would have been better had his material been organized and clear from the outset.

The same verse that was applied to rain is now applied to *talmud Torah*, and the fit here with the word *chachmah* is even more natural than it was in the rain context.

### Seider Matnitei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ — יַרְבֶּה בִּישִׁיבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַחֲיָלִים יְגַבֵּר וְיִתְרוֹן הַכְשֵׁיר חׇכְמָה״. כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אִם מִשְׁנָתוֹ סְדוּרָה לוֹ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is his remedy?** He must **increase** the time he **sits** and studies, **as it is stated: "Then must he increase his strength"** (Ecclesiastes 10:10). The last part of the verse: **"But wisdom is profitable to direct,"** means that **all the more so, if his** study of the **Mishna is organized for him from the beginning,** he will avoid this trouble.

The *Gemara* goes on to stress the importance of organizing one's material before moving to analysis.

### Forty vs. Twenty-Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה מְסַדַּר מַתְנִיתֵיהּ אַרְבְּעִין זִמְנִין, כְּנֶגֶד אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה, וְעָיֵיל לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה מְסַדַּר מַתְנִיתֵיהּ עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבַּע זִמְנִין, כְּנֶגֶד תּוֹרָה נְבִיאִים וּכְתוּבִים, וְעָיֵיל לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** That is **like this** practice **of Reish Lakish,** who **would review his studies forty times, corresponding to** the **forty days in which the Torah was given** to Moses at Sinai, **and** only afterward would he **go before Rabbi Yoḥanan** to study from his teacher. Similarly, **Rav Adda bar Ahava** would **review his learning twenty-four times, corresponding to** the twenty-four books in the **Torah, Prophets, and Writings,** i.e., the Bible, **and** only afterward **go before Rava** to study with him.

*Ki ha d'Reish Lakish hava mesader matnitei arba'in zimnin* — *Reish Lakish* would organize his material forty times, *k'neged mem yom shenitnah Torah*, corresponding to the forty days on which the *Torah* was given. The point is that one must fully own the *Torah* received — in an oral culture, all previous teachings and *mishnayot* had to be held entirely in the mind. Having invested that effort in mastery, *v'ayeil l'kamei d'Rabbi Yochanan*, he would then go before *Rabbi Yochanan* to apply his intellect and analysis.

This evokes the broader *Gemara*ic discussion of *Sinai v'oker harim* — whether greater importance lies in erudition and breadth of knowledge, or in the power of analysis. The *Gemara* there concludes that analysis cannot precede the facts. The same principle operates here, with the added emphasis that the material must not merely be learned but thoroughly organized and clarified before one begins to think it through. First own the material; then develop the *chiddushim*.

*Rav Adda bar Abba mesader matnitei esrin v'arba zimnin* — Rav Adda bar Abba would organize his material twenty-four times, *k'neged Torah Nevi'im u-Khtuvim*, corresponding to the twenty-four books of *Tanakh*, and *v'ayeil l'kamei d'Rava*, he would then go before *Rava*. Since the twenty-four books of *Tanakh* are written, one works to internalize them to the point that they are as if written before one's eyes — and only then does one turn to analysis.

In both cases the correspondence is elegant: whether it is the forty days of *matan Torah* or the twenty-four books of *Tanakh*, what is received must be fully owned before one's own analytic contribution begins. The relationship between the received material and one's own analysis mirrors, in a certain sense, the relationship between *Torah she-bichtav* and *Torah she-be'al peh*, though here it applies equally to the received dimension of *Torah she-be'al peh* itself.

### Master's Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִם רָאִיתָ תַּלְמִיד שֶׁתַּלְמוּדוֹ קָשֶׁה עָלָיו כַּבַּרְזֶל — בִּשְׁבִיל רַבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַסְבִּיר לוֹ פָּנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא לֹא פָנִים קִלְקַל״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the aforementioned verse from Ecclesiastes, **Rava said: If you see a student whose studies are** as **difficult for him as iron,** this is **due to his teacher, who does not show him a friendly countenance,** but is overly strict with him. This practice inhibits the student's learning, **as it is stated: "And it has not whetted the surface [*panim*]"** (Ecclesiastes 10:10). As explained previously, *panim* can also mean countenance.

*Rava* returns to the verse about iron: *ei chazit talmid shelimudo kasheh alav k'varzel* — if you see a student whose learning is as hard as iron and he cannot grasp what is being taught — *bishvil rabo*, this is not the student's failure to do sufficient *chazarah*, but the master's failure: *she'eino masbir lo panim*, that he does not show the student a friendly, welcoming face. The word *masbir* here carries a double meaning — *sever panim yafot*, a welcoming countenance, but also the plain sense of not explaining things well. Both may be intended.

*Shene'emar vehu lo panim kilkel* — here *lo* may be read as written with a *vav*, meaning the master shows him an unfavorable face. *Vehu keha* — he is not nourishing the student. The *masbirah* is the remedy in both senses.

### Yarbeh Alav Rei'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ — יַרְבֶּה עָלָיו רֵעִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַחֲיָלִים יְגַבֵּר וְיִתְרוֹן הַכְשֵׁיר חׇכְמָה״. כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אִם הוּכְשְׁרוּ מַעֲשָׂיו בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the remedy** for this student? He must **increase** the number of **friends** he sends to the teacher to intercede **for him, as it is stated: "Then must he increase his strength."** The term used for strength, *ḥayalim*, can also mean soldiers or colleagues. Nevertheless: **"But wisdom is profitable to direct,"** meaning that **all the more so** would he be spared this trouble **if his deeds** were properly **directed before his teacher from the beginning.**

*Mah takantei?* Interestingly, the *Gemara* addresses not the master but the student. *Yarbeh alav rei'im* — the student should cultivate friends and social connections around the master, sending people who will speak well of him and cultivate a more positive relationship. *Uvichayalim yigaber* — amass people around him and try to influence the dynamic.

*Veyitron hachshir chochmah kol sheken im huchshru ma'asav lifnei rabbo mei'ikara* — this does not remove the student's own responsibility. The master is presumably not being cold without reason; it would have been better had the student not demonstrated whatever initially caused the rift. But once the dynamic is set, sometimes an initial offense has a disproportionately long impact. Rather than merely complaining about it, the student should think constructively about how to cultivate a more positive relationship.

The movement throughout this section — between rain and *Torah* — is itself significant. Rain is understood as the *shefa*, the divine abundance, that comes from God to the world; what human beings do in return to create and sustain that relationship is *Torah* and *tefillah*. But beyond that: the same *pesukim* that speak about rain keep being read back through the lens of *talmud Torah*, because *Torah* is also spiritual sustenance that comes from God. The parallel runs deep. If the *rebbe* is in some sense a figure for God withholding the rain, then the student's effort to cultivate the relationship through friends echoes the community's turn to prayer to restore the connection with the Divine.

## Lachash & Prayer

### Nachash Belo Lachash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם יִשֹּׁךְ הַנָּחָשׁ בְּלוֹא לָחַשׁ וְאֵין יִתְרוֹן לְבַעַל הַלָּשׁוֹן״, אִם רָאִיתָ דּוֹר שֶׁהַשָּׁמַיִם מִשְׁתַּכִּין כִּנְחֹשֶׁת מִלְּהוֹרִיד טַל וּמָטָר — בִּשְׁבִיל לוֹחֲשֵׁי לְחִישׁוֹת שֶׁאֵין בַּדּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the topic of rain. **And Rabbi Ami said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "If the serpent [*naḥash*] bites [*yishokh*] before it is charmed [*laḥash*], then the charmer has no advantage"** (Ecclesiastes 10:11)? **If you see a generation** for whom **the heavens corrode [*meshatkhin*] like copper [*neḥoshet*],** which prevents them **from bringing down dew and rain,** this is **due to the lack of those who whisper quiet [*loḥashei leḥishot*]** prayers **in the generation.**

*V'amar Rabbi Ami*: *im yishoch hanachash belo lachash v'ein yitron l'va'al halashon* — if the snake bites without hissing, there is no benefit to the person of the tongue. *Im raita dor she-hashamayim mishtachshin k'nechoshet milehorid tal u-matar* — if you see a generation in which the heavens are reddened and rusted over like bronze and no rain is coming — *bishvil lochshei lechishot*, it is because of the absence of those who should be doing the whisperings of prayer. *She'ein bador* — they are not found in that generation.

Whereas in prior passages, sin was the cause and prayer was the remedy, here the very absence of prayer itself is what causes the heavens to be closed. The very absence of *tefillah* — the speech that sustains the relationship with God — is what produces the drought.

### Yeilech Eitzel Mi Sheyod…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקָּנָתָן — יֵלְכוּ אֵצֶל מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לִלְחוֹשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵעוֹ״, ״וְאֵין יתְרוֹן לְבַעַל הַלָּשׁוֹן״. וּמִי שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לִלְחוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ לוֹחֵשׁ, מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **What is their remedy?** They should **go to one who knows** how **to whisper** prayers in the proper manner, **as it is written: "Its noise tells concerning it"** (Job 36:33). As for the phrase: **"Then the charmer has no advantage,"** this is referring to **one who is able to whisper** his prayers correctly **and yet does not whisper** them correctly. In this case, of **what benefit to him** is his ability to pray?

*Mai takantei?* — *yeilech eitzel mi sheyod'e lelachash* — go find someone who knows how to pray. *Dechtiv yagid alav re'o* — let his neighbor speak about him, let his neighbor pray on his behalf.

This evokes the structure of the *Mishnayot* of this *masechet*: when rain fails, the *yechidim* begin to fast; if that does not suffice, the *tzibur* takes on the fasts; ultimately the *tzibur* ceases its efforts while the *yechidim* continue. The special role of the *yachid* in prayer — exemplified most dramatically by the story of *Choni HaMa'agel* — is a sustained theme of this *masechet*. Here it finds expression: if your own prayers are not working, seek out the *yachid* who can pray on your behalf.

*Ve-ein yitron l'va'al halashon* — and the one who has the power of speech and does not use it to pray when he could make a difference — *mah hanayah lo?* What benefit is he?

### Chasid Shebador

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָחַשׁ וְלֹא נַעֲנָה, מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? יֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל חָסִיד שֶׁבַּדּוֹר, וְיַרְבֶּה עָלָיו בִּתְפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְצַו עָלֶיהָ בְּמַפְגִּיעַ״, וְאֵין פְּגִיעָה אֶלָּא תְּפִילָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he whispered** his prayers **and** yet **was not answered, what is his remedy?** He should **go to** the most **pious individual of the generation, and** this pious individual will **increase** his **prayers on his behalf, as it is stated** one verse earlier: **"And He has commanded it due to imploring"** (Job 36:32). **And "imploring"** means **nothing other** than **prayer, as it is stated: "Therefore, do not pray you for this nation, neither lift up cry nor prayer for them, neither implore Me"** (Jeremiah 7:16).

*Ve-im lachash velo ne'enah* — and if you prayed and were not answered — *mai takantei?* — *yeilech eitzel chasid shebador veyeitar alav bitfillah* — go to the most pious person in the generation and have him entreat God on your behalf. *Shenemar vayitzav alav bamafgia*, and *ein pegiah ella tefillah*, as the verse says: "וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי."

There are two stages: first, find someone who simply knows how to *daven* — in an oral culture, perhaps not everyone even knows the text of the *tefillah*. If that is not sufficient, proceed to a genuine *chasid*, someone of elevated spiritual stature who can serve as a *mafgia*, one who can encounter and entreat God. The entire passage functions as a distillation of themes encountered throughout the *daf*: whereas before prayer was the *takanah* for a range of other sins, here the presence or absence of rain is itself a function of whether prayer is being offered at all, and of how effectively.

### Magis Da'ato Alav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָחַשׁ וְעָלְתָה בְּיָדוֹ, וּמֵגִיס דַּעְתּוֹ עָלָיו — מֵבִיא אַף לָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִקְנֶה אַף עַל עוֹלֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he whispered** his prayers for rain, **and** his prayers were **successful,** i.e., rain fell as he requested, **and he becomes prideful** as a result, **he brings anger into the world, as it is stated: "The cattle [*mikne*] also [*af* ] concerning the rising storm [*al oleh*]"** (Job 36:33). This verse can be read homiletically as: Anger [*af* ] is acquired [*mikne*] by one who raises [*al oleh*] his pride.

*Ve-im lachash ve-altah beyado* — but if the prayer was successful and rain fell — *umagis da'ato alav*, if the one who prayed becomes haughty as a result, swelling with pride over the efficacy of his prayers — *meivi af la'olam*, this brings anger to the world. *Shenemar mikneh af* — one who acquires anger because of *oleh da'ato alav*, because his sense of himself has risen up.

One wonders whether this is an allusion to *Choni HaMa'agel*, who famously ordered God around with great specificity regarding the manner of the rain. The power of speech in shaping the relationship with God is immense; but that very power demands profound humility. Haughtiness in the wake of answered prayer is itself a trigger for divine anger.

## Talmidei Chachamim D (II)

### Mitkana'im B'Af

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין בְּעִיר אַחַת וְאֵין נוֹחִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה — מִתְקַנְּאִין בָּאַף וּמַעֲלִין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִקְנֶה אַף עַל עוֹלֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** Following the same interpretation of this verse, **Rava said:** If there are **two Torah scholars who live in one city, and they are not courteous with one another in** their discussions of ***halakha*, they arouse anger** upon the world **and cause it to rise up, as it is stated: "Anger is acquired by one who raises his pride."**

*Rava* says: *shnei talmidei chachamim sheyoshvim b'ir achat v'ein noachin zeh l'zeh b'halacha* — if two Torah scholars live in the same city and are not pleasant to one another in *halacha*, if there is no good collaboration, no genuine shared discourse — *mitkana'im b'af*, it leads to jealousy with anger, *umalin oto*, and it rises up. *Shenemar makne af al olah.*

It is worth noting that the word here is *halacha* rather than *Torah* — perhaps because *halacha* with its practical application carries a particular weight that makes disputes especially charged. As soon as there is this: I rule this way, you rule the other way — things can deteriorate quickly. The reference is to the second half of the verse *yagid alav re'o makne af al olah* — *Rashi* explains that one should be *yagid alav re'o*, speaking and collaborating with one's neighbor; failing that, the result is an escalation of jealousy and anger.

## Talmidei Chachamim D (II)

### Yigid Alav Re'o

The transition here is immediate — from the discussion of rain to *Torah* and back again, without any formal break. *Amar Reish Lakish*: *mai dichtig im yishoch hanachash belo lachash v'ein yitron l'va'al halashon* — returning to the verse about the serpent that bites without hissing. The passage now takes a fresh direction.

## Lashon HaRa & Speech

### Chayot & Nachash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעָתִיד לָבוֹא מִתְקַבְּצוֹת וּבָאוֹת כׇּל הַחַיּוֹת אֵצֶל הַנָּחָשׁ, וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אֲרִי דּוֹרֵס וְאוֹכֵל, זְאֵב טוֹרֵף וְאוֹכֵל, אַתָּה מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לְךָ? אֹמֵר לָהֶם: ״וְאֵין יִתְרוֹן לְבַעַל הַלָּשׁוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another interpretation of the aforementioned verse. **Reish Lakish said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "If the snake bites before it is charmed, then the charmer has no advantage"** (Ecclesiastes 10:11)? **In the future, all the animals will gather together and come to the snake and say to him: A lion mauls** its prey **and eats** it; **a wolf tears apart** its prey **and eats** it; but **you, what pleasure do you have** when you bite a person, as you are incapable of eating him? The snake will **say to them: "The charmer has no advantage."** The Hebrew phrase for snake charmer literally means the master of the tongue, and therefore the snake is saying that he has a more difficult question: What pleasure does a slanderer receive, as he inflicts more harm for which he obtains no physical enjoyment.

*Le-atid lavo*, in the future, all the animals will gather around the serpent and confront it. We understand, they say, why other predators do what they do: *ari dores v'ochel* — the lion tramples and eats; *ze'ev toref v'ochel* — the wolf tears its prey and eats. They commit violence in the service of sustenance. But you — *atah mah hana'ah yesh lach?* — what benefit do you derive? For occasionally a snake kills a creature — a large animal, a human being — that it does not then consume.

### Ein Yitron LeBaal HaLash…

The snake replies: *ve-ein yitron l'va'al halashon* — you can ask the same question of one who speaks *lashon hara*. With words alone, such a person can destroy reputations and ruin lives — and what do they gain? No material benefit, no sustenance. Yet the response is somewhat rhetorical: there *is* a kind of *hana'ah* that people derive from using speech to harm others — not a legitimate *hana'ah*, but a felt one. And so the snake, in turn, derives its own form of *hana'ah* from what it does. The parable is a striking indictment of *lashon hara* as, like the snake's bite, a form of harm that profits the one who inflicts it nothing.

## Sincerity & Prayer

### Nisa Levavenu El Kapayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין תְּפִלָּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם נִשְׁמַעַת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מֵשִׂים נַפְשׁוֹ בְּכַפּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִשָּׂא לְבָבֵנוּ אֶל כַּפָּיִם״, אִינִי?! וְהָא אוֹקֵים שְׁמוּאֵל אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ וּדְרַשׁ: ״וַיְפַתּוּהוּ בְּפִיהֶם וּבִלְשׁוֹנָם יְכַזְּבוּ לוֹ וְלִבָּם לֹא נָכוֹן עִמּוֹ וְלֹא נֶאֶמְנוּ בִּבְרִיתוֹ״, וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן — ״וְהוּא רַחוּם יְכַפֵּר עָוֹן וְגוֹ׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: A person's prayer is heard only if he places his soul in his palm,** i.e., one must submit his entire soul with sincerity in his outstretched hands as he prays, **as it is stated: "Let us lift up our heart with our hands"** (Lamentations 3:41). The Gemara raises an objection: **Is that so? But Shmuel** once **established for himself an interpreter** to teach in public, **and interpreted homiletically** the verse: **"But they beguiled Him with their mouth and lied to Him with their tongue, for their heart was not steadfast with Him, neither were they faithful to His covenant"** (Psalms 78:36–37), **and nevertheless** the psalm continues: **"But He, being full of compassion, forgives iniquity,** and does not destroy" (Psalms 78:38). This indicates that all prayers are accepted, even if they lack sincerity.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *ein tfilato shel adam nishma'at ela im kein masim nafsho b'kapo* — a person's prayer is heard only if he truly places his soul in his hands, exposing his genuine self and bringing it before God. *Shenemar nisa levavenu el kapayim el El bashamayim* — let us lift our hearts on our hands to God in heaven. Only if you come with your heart truly in hand will your prayer be answered.

### Veyefatuhu BeFilhem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּיָחִיד, כָּאן בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult,** as **here** Rabbi Ami is referring **to an individual** who prays without sincerity and consequently his prayer goes unheard, whereas **there** Shmuel is saying that when one prays **with** the **community,** even if his prayers are deficient, they are accepted in the merit of the congregation.

The *Gemara* challenges this: *eini?* Is that really true? *V'hukim Shmuel amora alei v'darash*: Shmuel set up an *amora* to proclaim the following teaching: "וַיְפַתּוּהוּ בְּפִיהֶם וּבִלְשׁוֹנָם יְכַזְּבוּ לוֹ וְלִבָּם לֹא נָכוֹן עִמּוֹ וְלֹא נֶאֶמְנוּ בִּבְרִיתוֹ" — they seduced God with their mouths and lied with their tongues, their hearts were not faithful to Him, they were not true to the covenant. And yet the very next verse says: "וְהוּא רַחוּם יְכַפֵּר עָוֹן" — God, in His mercy, will forgive. Even insincere prayer, it seems, is answered.

### Yachid vs. Tzibur

The resolution is that for an individual, sincerity is required; one must truly bring one's heart. For the community, even prayer that is not fully sincere is answered.

This is a striking and genuinely bold claim. It is one thing to say that communal prayer is *more powerful* than individual prayer — we encounter that in other *Gemarot*, such as *ein tfilat shel tzibur chozeret reikam*. But this is a qualitatively different claim: that prayer which is, as stated explicitly in the verse, insincere — where the heart is not true, where the lips lie — is nonetheless answered when offered by the community.

*Rashi* reads the verse as suggesting that not all the members of the community are insincere — *she'ein lev kulam shalem* — and so the community as a whole can absorb individual insincerity. The *Gemara*, however, uses plural language throughout — *vayefatuhu befihem, uvilshonam yekhazvu lo* — suggesting the entire community is insincere. One might understand the *Gemara* to be making a point about the nature of communication itself: the very act of speech, even when not fully sincere, sustains the relationship. Better to be in communication than not. At the communal level, there is a power in the collective act of reaching out to God even when individual sincerity is lacking — it preserves the structure and reality of the relationship itself, expressed through the medium of *avodah she-balev*.

## Baalei Amana

### Emet Me-Eretz Tizmakh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין גְּשָׁמִים יוֹרְדִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בַּעֲלֵי אֲמָנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֶת מֵאֶרֶץ תִּצְמָח וְצֶדֶק מִשָּׁמַיִם נִשְׁקָף״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami** further **said: Rain falls only due to faithful people, as it is stated: "Truth springs out of the earth, and righteousness has looked down from heaven"** (Psalms 85:12). When "truth springs out of the earth," i.e., if people are faithful, they will find that "righteousness," in the form of rain "has looked down from heaven."

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *ein geshamim yordim ella bishvil baalei amana* — rain falls only because of those who deal faithfully with one another in business. Not those who profess a creed, but those who conduct their commercial affairs with trustworthiness. *Shenemar emet me-eretz tizmakh ve-tzedek mishamayim nishkaf* — truth sprouts from the ground, and justice — here understood as the rain — looks down from heaven. The rain from above is the divine response to the faithful dealing that emerges from below.

### Chulda U-Vor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים בַּעֲלֵי אֲמָנָה, מִנַּיִין — מֵחוּלְדָּה וּבוֹר. וּמָה הַמַּאֲמִין בְּחוּלְדָּה וּבוֹר — כָּךְ, הַמַּאֲמִין בְּהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ami said: Come and see how great** the **faithful people are,** and how God assists them. **From where** is it derived? **From** the story of the **marten [*ḥulda*] and** the **pit.** Once a young man saved a girl who had fallen into a pit. After rescuing her they swore to remain faithful to each other, and they declared the pit and a passing marten their witnesses. As time went by the young man forgot his vow and married another woman. They had two children, both of whom died tragically, one by falling into a pit and the other when he was bitten by a marten. Their unusual deaths led the young man to realize his error and he returned to the first woman. **And if this** is the outcome for one **who believes in** signs from **a pit and a marten, all the more so** for **one who has faith in the Holy One, Blessed be He.**

*De-amar Rabbi Ami bo u-re'eh kama gedolim baalei amana* — come and see how great are those who deal in good faith. *Minayin?* — *me-chulda u-vor*, from the weasel and the pit. *U-ma hamaamin be-chulda u-vor kach hamaamin ba-Kadosh Baruch Hu al achat kama ve-khama* — if one who holds a weasel and a well as witnesses to an oath is held to account by those very witnesses, how much more so one who holds God as witness.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* cite the *midrash*: a young man and woman fell in love and swore not to marry anyone else until they were of age. When asked who would hold him to his oath, the young man named the weasel and the pit as witnesses. Years passed; he broke his promise, married another, and had two children. One child fell into a well and died; the other was killed by a weasel. His wife asked what had happened, he told her the story, and she urged him to keep his oath. He divorced her and married the woman to whom he had originally sworn.

Whether the story teaches the power of dealing faithfully or the consequences of bad faith, the *Gemara*'s point is that faithful dealing implicitly or explicitly invokes God — bringing God into the transaction as a presence and a witness. To deal faithfully with another is therefore to sustain God as a partner in one's daily life. And therefore the natural divine response, the rain, follows. It is not merely an ethical correspondence; it flows from the same logic of relationship that animates the entire *masechet*.

## Matztik Atzmo

### Din Min Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַמַּצְדִּיק אֶת עַצְמוֹ מִלְּמַטָּה — מַצְדִּיקִין עָלָיו הַדִּין מִלְּמַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֶת מֵאֶרֶץ תִּצְמָח וְצֶדֶק מִשָּׁמַיִם נִשְׁקָף״. רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, מֵהָכָא: ״וּכְיִרְאָתְךָ עֶבְרָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Whoever is exacting with himself,** by striving to act righteously in every way on earth **below, he is judged in an exact manner** in Heaven **above,** in order to improve him further still, **as it is stated: "Truth springs out of the earth, and righteousness has looked down from heaven"** (Psalms 85:12). **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin said** that **Rav Huna said** that this idea is derived **from here: "And Your wrath is according to the fear that is due to You"** (Psalms 90:11). The level of God's wrath correlates with the offender's fear of God.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *kol hamatztik et atzmo milmata maztikin alav et hadin milmala* — whoever acts with scrupulous righteousness below, the judgment will be exacting upon him from above. *Tzidduk hadin* is not necessarily a term of comfort: it means that things happen, bad things even, but they are all ultimately just. *Rashi* explains that if someone acts with great scrupulousness, Heaven will be very exacting in judging his own small missteps, and he will be held to a higher standard. *Shenemar emet me-eretz tizmakh ve-tzedek mishamayim nishkaf* — again the same verse: if you are truthful on the ground, a precise accounting comes from above.

### Veki'Yiratekha Evratekha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, מֵהָכָא: ״וּכְיִרְאָתְךָ עֶבְרָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin said** that **Rav Huna said** that this idea is derived **from here: "And Your wrath is according to the fear that is due to You"** (Psalms 90:11). The level of God's wrath correlates with the offender's fear of God.

*Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Rav Huna* cites: *u-kheyiratekha evratekha* — as great as your fear of God is, so too is Your anger. The more one fears God and acts accordingly, the more exacting the Divine judgment becomes.

### Oseh Tzedek BiDrachekha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״פָּגַעְתָּ אֶת שָׂשׂ וְעֹשֵׂה צֶדֶק בִּדְרָכֶיךָ יִזְכְּרוּךָ הֵן אַתָּה קָצַפְתָּ וַנֶּחֱטָא בָּהֶם עוֹלָם וְנִוָּשֵׁעַ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַשָּׂמֵחַ בְּיִסּוּרִין שֶׁבָּאִין עָלָיו — מֵבִיא יְשׁוּעָה לָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּהֶם עוֹלָם וְנִוָּשֵׁעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said** that this principle is derived **from here: "You took him away who joyfully performed righteousness, those who remembered You in Your ways, behold You were wroth, and we sinned, upon them have we stayed of old, that we might be saved"** (Isaiah 64:4). This verse also teaches that God displays wrath specifically due to the transgressions of those who are accustomed to acting righteously. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** concerning the same verse: **Whoever is joyful in** the **suffering** that comes upon him **brings salvation to the world [*olam*], as it is stated: "Upon them have we stayed of old [*olam*], that we might be saved."**

*Reish Lakish* adds a verse: "פָּגַעְתָּ אֶת שָׂשׂ וְעֹשֵׂה צֶדֶק בִּדְרָכֶיךָ יִזְכְּרוּךָ הֵן אָתָּה קָצַפְתָּ וַנֶּחֱטָא בָּהֶם עוֹלָם וְנִוָּשֵׁע" — You have smitten and become angry at the very ones who rejoice in doing righteousness in Your ways; yet through this, the world will be saved.

### Sameach BeYissurin

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: *kol hasameach beyissurin sheba'in alav meivi yeshua la'olam* — whoever rejoices in the afflictions that befall him brings salvation to the world. *Shenemar bahem olam venivashea* — through the very anger and smiting, salvation comes.

This teaching functions on two levels. On one level, it addresses those who might be deterred from righteousness by the prospect of exacting divine judgment — the answer is that there is a larger salvific meaning. On another level, it addresses the problem of *tzadik ve-ra lo* — the righteous who suffer. The suffering of a *tzadik* is not arbitrary; God holds that person to a standard of perfect accounting, and in the end it all works out in the divine reckoning.

It is worth noting here that this *masechet* is not principally about *teshuvah*. While *teshuvah* appears, the dominant concern is prayer and fasting. The question of why it does not rain is, in this *masechet*, less a question of exactly how righteous or sinful people are and more a question of the *relationship* — whether the people are reaching out to God or not. That framing alleviates some of the tension raised by the *tzadik vera lo* problem: if the issue of rain is primarily relational rather than strictly retributive, then the contradictions introduced by righteous suffering are somewhat less severe.

## Holida & Pekida

### Atzrat HaShamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעָצַר אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם״, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַשָּׁמַיִם נֶעֱצָרִים מִלְּהוֹרִיד טַל וּמָטָר — דּוֹמֶה לְאִשָּׁה שֶׁמְּחַבֶּלֶת וְאֵינָהּ יוֹלֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § Returning to the topic of rain, **Reish Lakish said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And He will close up the heavens"** (Deuteronomy 11:17)? This verse teaches that **when the heavens are closed up from bringing down dew and rain,** this is **similar to a woman who has the pangs of labor and** yet **does not give birth,** as the heavens themselves suffer from their inability to bring down rain and dew.

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *mai de-chtiv ve-atzart hashamayim* — what is meant by the verse in which God will close up the heavens? *Besha'ah shehashamayim ne'etzarin milehorid tal u-matar* — when the heavens are closed from bringing rain — *domeh le'isha shemechabelet ve'eina yoledet* — it is like a woman undergoing the pains of labor and unable to give birth.

*Rashi* connects this image to sin — it is difficult for the world because of *cheit*. But that connection is not self-evident from the imagery. If the heavens are straining and unable to deliver, the more natural reading is not divine punishment but divine longing: God wants to bring the rain, is in pain, and is unable to deliver. The image speaks powerfully to the idea of God's desire to connect with Israel, the suffering that results when that connection is blocked, and the relationship itself as the central concern.

### Atzira & Leida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: נֶאֶמְרָה עֲצִירָה בִּגְשָׁמִים וְנֶאֶמְרָה עֲצִירָה בְּאִשָּׁה,

> Reish Lakish elaborates: **Closing up is stated with regard to a woman who cannot give birth, **as it is stated: "For the Lord has fast closed up all the wombs"** (Genesis 20:18), **and closing up is stated with regard to rains, as it is written: "And He will close up the heavens"** (Deuteronomy 11:17).

> נֶאֱמַר לֵידָה בְּאִשָּׁה וְנֶאֱמַר לֵידָה בִּגְשָׁמִים, נֶאֱמַר לֵידָה בְּאִשָּׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתַּהַר וַתֵּלֶד בֵּן״, וְנֶאֱמַר לֵידָה בִּגְשָׁמִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹלִידָהּ וְהִצְמִיחָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, an expression of **giving birth is stated with regard to a woman, and** an expression of **giving birth is** also **stated with regard to rain.** Specifically, **giving birth is stated with regard to a woman, as it is written** in the case of Rachel, when God had mercy on her: **"And she conceived and gave birth to a son"** (Genesis 30:23). **And giving birth is stated with regard to rain, as it is written:** "For as the rain comes down, and the snow from heaven, and does not return there, except it waters the earth **and causes it to give birth and bud"** (Isaiah 55:10).

*Vehainu de-amar Reish Lakish mishum Bar Kappara*: the same word *atzira* — closing, being held back — is used both in the context of rain and in the context of women; and the same word *leida* — birth — is used in both contexts as well. *Ne-emra atzira be-isha*: "כִּי עָצֹר עָצַר ה' בְּעַד כׇּל רָחֶם" — God had closed every womb in the household of Avimelech. *Ve-ne-emra atzira bigshamim*: "וְעָצַר אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם" — the heavens will be stopped up. *Ne-emra leida be-isha*: "וַתַּהַר וַתֵּלֶד בֵּן." *Ve-ne-emra leida bigshamim*: "וְהוֹלִידָה וְהִצְמִיחָה."

It should be noted that *veholidah* is in *hif'il* — the rain causes the earth to give birth. So in the *pesukim* themselves, the rain is masculine and the earth is feminine; it is the earth, the mother, that *holidedt*. The *Gemara* somewhat elides these distinctions as it draws the parallel between woman and rain.

### Pekida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר פְּקִידָה בְּאִשָּׁה, וְנֶאֱמַר פְּקִידָה בִּגְשָׁמִים. נֶאֱמַר פְּקִידָה בְּאִשָּׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ פָּקַד אֶת שָׂרָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר פְּקִידָה בִּגְשָׁמִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״פָּקַדְתָּ הָאָרֶץ וַתְּשֹׁקְקֶהָ רַבַּת תַּעְשְׁרֶנָּה פֶּלֶג אֱלֹהִים מָלֵא מָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** Lastly, an expression of **remembering is stated** in connection **with a woman, and** an expression of **remembering is** also **stated** in connection **to rain. Remembering is stated** in connection **with a woman, as it is written: "And the Lord remembered Sarah"** (Genesis 21:1), **and remembering is stated** in connection **to rain, as it is written: "You have remembered the earth and have watered it; greatly enriching it, with the pool of God that is full of water"** (Psalms 65:10).

*Ne-emra pekida be-isha vene-emra pekida bigshamim* — the word *pekida*, divine remembrance and visitation, is used both for a woman and for rain. *Ne-emra pekida be-isha*: "וַה' פָּקַד אֶת שָׂרָה" — God remembered Sarah. *Ve-ne-emra pekida bigshamim*: "פָּקַדְתָּ הָאָרֶץ וַתְּשֹׁקְקֶהָ רַבַּת תַּעְשְׁרֶנָּה פֶּלֶג אֱלֹהִים מָלֵא מָיִם" — You have remembered the earth and watered it, greatly enriched it; the *peleg Elokim* is full of water.

What is being remembered here is not the clouds or the heavens — it is *ha-aretz*, the earth. The earth is the feminine presence that receives the rain and brings forth life. In two of the three parallel pairs, it is the earth — mother earth — that is the feminine counterpart to the divine: she is *atzura*, she gives birth, she is *nifkedet*. The *Gemara* draws all of this together even though in the underlying *pesukim* the feminine role belongs to the earth rather than to the rain.

### Peleg Elokim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״פֶּלֶג אֱלֹהִים מָלֵא מָיִם״? תָּנָא: כְּמִין קוּבָּה יֵשׁ בָּרָקִיעַ, שֶׁמִּמֶּנָּה גְּשָׁמִים יוֹצְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to this verse. **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"With the pool of God that is full of water"?** The Gemara answers that it was **taught** in a *baraita*: **There is a kind of vault [*kuba*] in the sky, out of which the rain falls.**

*Mai peleg Elokim malei mayim?* — *tana kemin kubba yesh barakia she-mimmenah geshamim yotzim* — there is, as it were, a chamber or hut in the heavens from which the rain issues forth. The imagery throughout this entire passage — of birth, of divine remembrance, of feminine longing and release — speaks to a vision of rain as the expression of an intimate, nurturing, generative relationship between heaven and earth, between God and the world.

## Rain Calibration

### Im Leshevet Letzedaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם לְשֵׁבֶט אִם לְאַרְצוֹ אִם לְחֶסֶד יַמְצִאֵהוּ״. ״אִם לְשֵׁבֶט״ — בְּהָרִים וּבִגְבָעוֹת, ״אִם לְחֶסֶד יַמְצִאֵהוּ לְאַרְצוֹ״ — בְּשָׂדוֹת וּבִכְרָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written:** "Whatever he commands them upon the face of the habitable world, **whether it is for correction, or for His earth, or for mercy that He causes it to come"** (Job 37:12–13)? The phrase **"whether it is for correction"** means that if the people are judged unfavorably, the rain will fall **on the mountains and on the hills.** The phrase "or for His earth" indicates that if they have been judged **"for mercy," He will cause it to come "for His earth," on the fields and on the vineyards.**

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani*: *mai de-chtiv im leshevet im le-artzo im le-chesed yamtzi-ehu?* Rain is not uniform; it is calibrated according to what has been decreed.

*Im leshevet* — if the rain was decreed to be for smiting, harsh and destructive, but then the people did *teshuvah* or God relented, *beharim uvigva'ot* — God will bring it down on the mountains and valleys where it will do no harm. *Im le-artzo* — if it was decreed as beneficial rain for the land, *bisadot uvekramim*, it goes to the fields and vineyards. *Im le-chesed* — if for kindness, *yamtzi-ehu berosh sichin ume'arot*, it goes to fill the wells and cisterns.

*Rashi* refines this: harsh rains (*leshevet*) are directed toward the trees, where they are beneficial; rain for the land (*le-artzo*) goes to the seeds and growing crops; and rain for kindness (*le-chesed*) fills the wells. Each type of rain is precisely targeted according to both the decree and the need. Rain is not merely a natural phenomenon but a calibrated divine communication, directed with specificity to serve the precise purpose intended.

## Famine & Plague

### Kafna U'Motna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימֵי רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי הֲוָה כַּפְנָא וּמוֹתָנָא, אָמְרִי: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי אַתַּרְתֵּי — לָא אֶפְשָׁר. אֶלָּא לִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי אַמּוֹתָנָא, וְכַפְנָא נִיסְבּוֹל. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: נִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי אַכַּפְנָא, דְּכִי יָהֵיב רַחֲמָנָא שׂוּבְעָא — לְחַיֵּי הוּא דְּיָהֵיב, דִּכְתִיב: ״פּוֹתֵחַ אֶת יָדֶךָ וּמַשְׂבִּיעַ לְכׇל חַי רָצוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **In the days of Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani there was a famine and a plague.** The Sages **said: What** should we **do? Should we pray for mercy for two** troubles, both the famine and the plague? This is **not possible,** as it is improper to pray for the alleviation of two afflictions at once. **Rather, let us pray for mercy for the plague, and** as for the **famine, we** must **bear** it. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said to them:** On the contrary, **let us pray for mercy for the famine, as when the Merciful One provides plenty, He gives it for** the sake of **the living,** i.e., if God answers this prayer then he will certainly bring an end to the plague as well, **as it is written: "You open Your hand and satisfy every living thing with favor"** (Psalms 145:16).

*Bimei Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani hava kafna umotna* — in the time of *Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* there was simultaneously a famine and a plague. The people deliberated: *amri heichi na'avid? niba'ei rachmei a'tarti?* Should we pray for relief from both? *Lo efshar* — that is not possible. The impulse was to prioritize the plague: *ella niba'ei rachmei a'motna v'kafna nisbal* — pray for the plague to cease, and we will somehow endure the famine; at least we will be alive.

### Yod Chodesh Mamtir

*Amar lahem Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani*: No — *niba'ei rachmei a'kafna* — we should pray for the famine, for rain. The reasoning: *de-chi yahav rachmana sova l'chayei hu d'yahav* — when God gives plenty, He gives it in order to sustain life. God will not end a season of famine except for the purpose of preserving life, and therefore if our prayer for rain is answered, the plague will cease as well, for God gives sustenance to the living. *Dechtiv potei'ach et yadecha u-masbia l'chol chai ratzon* — when You open Your hand, You satisfy all living things with their desire.

### Nitzuma Vinvakshah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּלָא מְצַלִּינַן אַתַּרְתֵּי — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַנָּצוּמָה וַנְּבַקְשָׁה מֵאֱלֹהֵינוּ עַל זֹאת״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרִיתִי. בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חַגַּי מֵהָכָא: ״וְרַחֲמִין לְמִבְעֵא מִן קֳדָם אֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא עַל רָזָא דְּנָה״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And from where do we** derive **that** one should not pray **for two** troubles simultaneously? **As it is written: "So we fasted and beseeched our God for this"** (Ezra 8:23). **From the fact** that the verse states: "For this," it may be inferred **that there is another** trouble about which the people did not pray. **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say in the name of Rabbi Ḥaggai** that this idea comes **from here: "That they might ask mercy of the God of Heaven concerning this secret"** (Daniel 2:18). **From the fact** that the verse states: "This secret," it may be inferred **that there is another** trouble about which they did not pray.

*Mai takantei?* Where does the principle derive that one cannot pray for two things simultaneously? *Dechtiv v'natzuma v'nivakshah m'elokeinu al zot* — let us fast and ask from our God *on this*, on this one thing. *Michlal d'ika achriti* — the implication is that there is another matter as well, but we direct our petition to only one at a time. The verse from the west (*u-mimarav amri mishemei d'Rabbi Chagai me'ha*) similarly reads: "לְרַחֲמִין לְמִבְעֵי מִן קֳדָם אֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא עַל רָזָא דְנָה" — to seek mercy from the God of heaven *on this matter*, implying there is another matter, but only one is being addressed.

It is worth noting that the verse *v'natzuma v'nivakshah* links fasting and prayer in a single formulation — the pairing is central to the concerns of this *masechet*.

## Gezera & Fasting

### Lo Lemitav B'Ta'anita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא גְּזוּר שְׁמָדָא, וּגְזוּר דְּלָא לְמֵיתַב בְּתַעֲנִיתָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: נְקַבְּלֵיהּ עִילָּוַון, וּלְכִי בָּטֵיל שְׁמָדָא לֵיתְבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar vein, the Gemara relates: **In the days of Rabbi Zeira a decree of** religious **persecution** was decreed against the Jews. **And** as the **decree** was **that they were not** allowed **to fast,** the Jews were certainly unable to fast and pray for the nullification of the decree itself. **Rabbi Zeira said to** the people: **Let us take** a fast **upon ourselves,** despite the fact that in practice we cannot observe it, **and when** the decree of religious **persecution is annulled we will observe the fast.**

*Be-mei Rabbi Zeira gezar gzeira* — in the time of *Rabbi Zeira* the government issued an edict: *d'gazar d'lo l'mitav b'ta'anita* — one may not sit in public religious fast. This is distinct from other persecutory decrees such as bans on *Shabbat* observance or *brit milah*; here the edict specifically targeted communal fasting, at a time when presumably there was a drought and a fasting was urgently needed.

### Nikblu Aleivan

*Amar lahem Rabbi Zeira*: *nikblun aleivan* — let us formally accept upon ourselves the obligation to fast, *u-l'chi batla hagezeira leisvei* — and when the edict passes, we will fulfill it. The community will make a formal commitment to fast now, to be discharged as soon as circumstances permit.

### Natata Libcha L'Hitanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי אַל תִּירָא דָנִיֵּאל כִּי מִן הַיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּ אֶת לִבְּךָ לְהָבִין וּלְהִתְעַנּוֹת לִפְנֵי אֱלֹהֶיךָ נִשְׁמְעוּ דְבָרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: From where do you** know **this,** the fact that one may take a fast upon himself that he cannot observe? Rabbi Zeira **said to them** that the reason is **as it is written: "Then he said to me: Fear not, Daniel, for from the first day that you set your heart to understand, and to fast before your God, your words were heard"** (Daniel 10:12). This verse indicates that from the moment one turns his heart to fast, his prayers are heard.

The *Gemara* asks: *minalan lach ha?* — what is the source for the idea that the very acceptance of an obligation to fast, even before the fast is fulfilled, carries religious efficacy? *Dechtiv*: "אַל תִּירָא דָנִיֵּאל כִּי מִן הַיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּ אֶת לִבְּךָ לְהָבִין וּלְהִתְעַנּוֹת לִפְנֵי אֱלֹהֶיךָ נִשְׁמְעוּ דְבָרֶיךָ" — from the very first day that you placed your heart to understand and to fast before your God, your words were heard. *Natata libcha* — not from the day you actually fasted, but from the day you intended to fast, your prayer was already heard.

### Ta'anit & Anah

The connection between *hit'anot* and *nishma'u devarecha* — between fasting and the hearing of prayer — is deeply embedded in the language. *Ta'anit* and *anah*, fasting and being answered, share a root; and the earliest prayer we will encounter in the *Mishnayot* is the *Anenu* prayer, the prayer to be answered, *k'anenu kmo she-anita*. The equation of *ta'anit* with being heard runs throughout this *masechet*.

The nature of fasting here must be understood correctly. Throughout this *masechet*, fasting has not been presented primarily as a form of *teshuvah*. Rather, fasting is a mode of non-verbal prayer — an intensification of the act of reaching out to God. One throws one's entire self — body and soul — into the outreach. The very desire to fast, that essence of self-sacrifice and dedication to the act of petition, itself carries the power to be heard. The commitment to fast is already a prayer.

## Rain on Fridays

### Siman Klala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁנִים כִּשְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ, וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת — אֵינָן אֶלָּא סִימַן קְלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the topic of rain. **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Even** in **years like the years of Elijah,** when God decreed that no rain would fall, if **rain falls on Shabbat eves it is nothing other** than **a sign of a curse,** as the rain disrupts the preparations for Shabbat.

*Amar Rav Yitzchak*: *afilu shanim k'shnei Eliyahu* — even if the years were like the years of *Eliyahu*, years of drought and famine lasting many years — *yardu geshamim b'arvei shabatot* — if the rain comes on Friday afternoons, *einam ella siman klala* — it is nonetheless a sign of curse, because it disrupts the *Shabbat* preparations and makes it difficult to go to the marketplace.

### Yoma D'Mitra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: קָשֶׁה יוֹמָא דְמִיטְרָא כְּיוֹמָא דְּדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **This is** the same as that **which Rabba bar Sheila said: A rainy day is as difficult as a judgment day.**

*Hainu de-amar Rabba bar Shila*: *kashya yoma d'mitra k'yoma d'dina* — a day of rain can be as burdensome as a day of *din*, a day of judgment. *Rashi* explains this in terms of Mondays and Thursdays — court days and market days — when things are busy and tumultuous, and heavy rain makes the practical demands of the day that much harder.

### Divine Communication

This conclusion can seem jarring after all the foregoing praise of rain as *gadol yom hageshamim*. The crops need the rain; why should it matter that it arrives on a Friday? But the key distinction is between *klala* and *siman klala*. The rain itself may be a *bracha*, necessary and beneficial, yet the fact that God brings it at an inconvenient time — when it disrupts the preparations for *Shabbat* and makes people's lives difficult — is itself a divine message of disfavor.

Rain is throughout this *masechet* a medium of divine communication. It communicates closeness or distance, blessing or withholding, favor or displeasure. Here, even when the substance of what is given is genuinely needed and beneficial, the *timing* communicates something. Not that rain on Friday is objectively evil — but that God is sending a message. Why at this moment of difficulty rather than at a more propitious time? The theological question is not whether rain is good or bad, but what is being communicated through the manner and moment of its arrival.
