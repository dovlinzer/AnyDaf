# Shevuot 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Future vs. Past Oaths

### Intro to Daf 26

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעָתִיד לָבֹא. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה; שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִשָּׁבַע לְבַטֵּא בִשְׂפָתַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says: One is liable only** for an oath on an utterance taken **about the future. The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to an oath on an utterance: From the verse: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips **to do evil, or to do good"** (Leviticus 5:4), **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good apply. From where** do I derive that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good do not apply? The verse states: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips,"** which includes other matters.

Daf 26 opens by examining the parameters governing when one is obligated to bring a *korban* for violating a *shvua*. The Torah's language is explicit: *lehara o leheitiv* — to do bad or to do good — which is inherently future-oriented. There is universal agreement that this phrase extends beyond oaths concerning explicitly harmful or beneficial acts to encompass even neutral activities. A further principle derived from *lehara o leheitiv* is that the oath must be reversible — it must constitute a meaningful *shvua* whether framed positively or negatively.

### Lehara/Leheitiv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְהַבָּא, לְשֶׁעָבַר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יְבַטֵּא הָאָדָם בִּשְׁבֻעָה״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – לְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for oaths referring **to the future. From where** do I derive that one is liable for oaths referring **to the past? The verse** subsequently **states: "Whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly with an oath"** (Leviticus 5:4); this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yishmael says:** The verse states: **"To do evil, or to do good,"** referring exclusively to oaths **about the future.**

The requirement of reversibility means that whatever one swears must be capable of being expressed in both its positive and negative formulations and remain a valid oath in either case. This is part of what the phrase *lehara o leheitiv* communicates structurally: the subject matter must be the kind of thing about which one could meaningfully swear either to do or to refrain from doing.

### Shvuas Sheker

A foundational distinction must be drawn at the outset. When someone swears about something in the past — for example, declaring "I ate" when in fact he did not — the most natural description of that act is a *shvuat sheker*, a false oath. Indeed, that is precisely what it is. If done intentionally, one incurs lashes; the act is classified as *shvuat sheker*. The question that animates the discussion on this daf is narrower: does such an oath also constitute a *shvuat bituy* — that is, does it fall within the Torah's category of an expressed oath whose violation triggers a *korban* when done inadvertently? The term *shvuat bituy* refers to the oath the Torah describes through the phrase *levatei bisfatayim*, to express with one's lips, for which a *korban* is brought when violated *beshogeg*. The specific question is whether a past-oriented *shvuat sheker* belongs to this category — since the structure of *shvuat bituy* normally envisions first making the oath and then potentially violating it through subsequent action, whereas in the past-oriented case the person is lying at the very moment of utterance.

### Rav/Shmuel Debate

This question was explored in the preceding daf through the debate between Rav and Shmuel concerning an oath about something in the past that could not meaningfully be reversed or restated in the future. One category of such cases involves *shvuat ha-edus* — testifying under oath about knowledge of testimony. To negate that oath, one would say "I don't know testimony," which is a valid *shvua*, but it belongs to an entirely different legal category. Similarly, declaring "I will know testimony" or "I will not know testimony" in the future is not within a person's control. Because such oaths cannot be reversed and remain within the same category, Rav and Shmuel debated whether a *korban* would be incurred at all. All of this belongs to the broader inquiry into the boundaries of *korban* liability.

## Rabbi Yishmael vs. Akiva

### Braisa on Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם כֵּן, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֲטָבָה וְהָרָעָה, דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה מִנַּיִן? אָמַר לוֹ: מֵרִבּוּי הַכָּתוּב. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ, רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva said to him: If so,** then **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good apply. From where** do I derive that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good do not apply?** Rabbi Yishmael **said to** Rabbi Akiva in response: It is derived **from an amplification** of the meaning **of the verse.** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: If the verse is amplified for this,** i.e., to extend the *halakha* of an oath on an utterance to matters that do not involve doing evil or good, **the verse is amplified for that,** i.e., oaths about the past.

The Gemara now returns to the central *tannaitic* dispute. *Rabbi Yishmael omer eino chayav ela al ha-asid lavo* — Rabbi Yishmael holds that liability for a *korban* applies only to future-oriented oaths. Rabbi Akiva maintains that even a past-oriented oath can generate *korban* liability.

A *braisa* records the hermeneutical reasoning. The verse begins: *o nefesh ki tishava levatei bisfatayim* — if a soul swears, to express with his lips. This is a general, inclusive statement. It then narrows: *lehara o leheitiv* — to do bad or to do good. The *braisa* derives from the opening *klal* that even oaths about neutral matters — those that involve neither harm nor benefit in any obvious sense — are included. Yet one might have thought the phrase is limited to the future. The verse then closes with a broad statement: *lechol asher yivatei ha-adam bishvua* — anything that a person expresses by oath. Rabbi Akiva reads this final phrase as including past-oriented oaths, citing the pattern of *klal u-prat u-klal*.

Rabbi Yishmael responds: *lehara o leheitiv lehaba* — the oath must be future-oriented. Rabbi Akiva then presses him: if you insist on limiting the verse so strictly, how do you include even neutral acts? Rabbi Yishmael answers that the final inclusive phrase — *me-ribui ha-kasuv* — expands the scope. Rabbi Akiva rejoins: *im riba ha-kasuv lekach riba ha-kasuv lekach* — if you concede that the verse broadens the scope once, that same broadening must also include past-oriented oaths. The Gemara notes: *shapir ka-amar lei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva made a compelling point. Once Rabbi Yishmael concedes that the verse expands beyond the literal *prat*, there is no principled reason to expand toward neutral acts while refusing to expand toward past-oriented ones.

### Akiva's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions: **Rabbi Akiva said well** his critique of the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael.** Why does Rabbi Yishmael disagree?

Rabbi Akiva's critique is pointed precisely because it catches Rabbi Yishmael in an internal inconsistency. Rabbi Yishmael wants to use the *ribui* of the final phrase to include neutral acts, but not to include past-oriented acts. Rabbi Akiva demonstrates that this is hermeneutically incoherent: once expansion is granted, it must proceed along consistent lines.

### Hermeneutics Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ אֶת רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה, שֶׁהָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט – אִיהוּ נָמֵי דּוֹרֵשׁ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ אֶת נַחוּם אִישׁ גַּם זוֹ, שֶׁהָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ בְּרִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט – אִיהוּ נָמֵי דּוֹרֵשׁ רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is because **Rabbi Yishmael** was the one **who served** as a disciple of **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana, who would interpret the entire Torah with** the hermeneutical principle of **a generalization and a detail.** Therefore, Rabbi Yishmael **also interprets** the Torah **with** the method of **a generalization and a detail. Rabbi Akiva** was one **who served** as a disciple of **Naḥum of Gam Zo, who would interpret the entire Torah with** the hermeneutical principle of **amplification and restriction.** Therefore, Rabbi Akiva **also interprets** the Torah by **amplification and restriction.**

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent tension by locating each authority's position within the broader hermeneutical school he inherited. Rabbi Yishmael, who had closely attended upon Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah — *she-shimmesh es Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah* — and who was his foremost disciple, adopted his master's method of interpreting the entire Torah through *klal u-ferat*: general and specific principles. Rabbi Yishmael therefore reads the verse through *klal u-ferat* hermeneutics.

Rabbi Akiva, who had similarly devoted himself to Nachum Ish Gamzu — the sage known for his declaration *gam zu le-tovah* — inherited his master's method of *ribui u-miyut*: inclusion and exclusion. Rabbi Yochanan notes: *ihu nami doresh be-ribui u-miyut* — Rabbi Akiva likewise interprets through inclusion and exclusion, and therefore extracts more from the verses than Rabbi Yishmael does.

This is not the familiar debate about whether *dibrah Torah ki-leshon benei adam* — whether the Torah speaks in ordinary human language, with Rabbi Yishmael being less inclined to derive meaning from every formulation. Here, both authorities agree that the verses present a general statement, a specific one, and another general statement. The disagreement is purely hermeneutical: which system of derivation applies? One approach broadens the specific somewhat; the other yields a far more expansive result.

It is worth noting, as an aside, that the thirteen hermeneutical principles recited each morning — *yud-gimel middos she-ha-Torah nidreshes ba-hen*, attributed to Rabbi Yishmael — appear at the opening of the *Sifra*, the midrashic work on Vayikra associated precisely with the school of Rabbi Akiva. The irony is not lost, though we will not pursue it further here.

## Ribui u-Miyut

### Three Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּדָרֵישׁ רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אוֹ נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִשָּׁבַע״ – רִיבָּה, ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מִיעֵט, ״לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יְבַטֵּא הָאָדָם״ – חָזַר וְרִיבָּה; רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה – רִיבָּה הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the specific instance in this context where one finds **that Rabbi Akiva interprets** with **amplifications and restrictions?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states: **"Or if anyone take an oath** clearly with his lips" (Leviticus 5:4), it **amplifies** the range of possible oaths for which one could be liable to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance. When the verse continues: **"To do evil, or to do good,"** it **restricts** that range. When it further continues: **"Whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly** with an oath," it **then amplifies** again. According to the hermeneutical principle that when a verse **amplified, and** then **restricted, and** then **amplified, it amplified** the relevant category to include **everything** except for the specific matter that was excluded by the restriction.

The Gemara now demonstrates concretely that Rabbi Akiva employs *ribui u-miyut*, citing the relevant *braisa*: *o nefesh ki tishava* — if a soul swears — this is the first term, an inclusive statement, *riba*, which encompasses everything. *Lehara o leheitiv* — to do bad or good — this is the second term, an excluding or narrowing statement, *miyat*. *Lechol asher yivatei ha-adam* — for anything a person enunciates — this third phrase returns and broadens again, *chazar ve-riba*. The pattern is thus *ribui u-miyut ve-ribui* — inclusion, exclusion, inclusion.

What does this final *ribui* include? *Riba kol milli* — it includes everything. The Gemara asks: what does the *miyut* — the narrowing — exclude? The answer is: *mi-et dvar mitzvah* — it excludes an oath involving a matter of religious obligation. This point will be developed, but the basic structure is clear: the pattern of inclusion-exclusion-inclusion under Rabbi Akiva's methodology leads to a near-total inclusion of all oaths, both future and past, with the single exclusion being *dvar mitzvah*.

### Ribui vs. Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רִיבָּה? רִיבָּה כֹּל מִילֵּי, וּמַאי מִיעֵט? מִיעֵט דְּבַר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What** was included when the verse **amplified** the range of liability? **It amplified** it to include **all matters** about which one might take an oath. **And** in **what** way did it **restrict** it when it continued: "To do evil, or to do good"? It **restricted** the range of liability for an oath on an utterance to exclude an oath that is **a matter** involving **a mitzva,** i.e., an oath to refrain from performing a mitzva.

The crucial difference between *ribui u-miyut* and *klal u-prat u-klal* becomes clear when one examines what each system yields at the intermediate stage. Under *ribui u-miyut*, after the first two terms — the inclusive statement and the narrowing statement — one already arrives at the conclusion that the result is something in between: not everything, and not only the specific case. This is because the *ribui* methodology reasons as follows: if the Torah intended only the specific case, why begin with a general statement at all? Therefore, the general and specific terms, operating together, indicate a result somewhere between total inclusion and the specific example.

Under *klal u-prat*, by contrast, the specific term collapses the general entirely at stage two. The *prat* defines the scope completely — it is only at stage three, when a second *klal* appears, that the expansion beyond the specific is restored. The structure is therefore different: *klal u-prat u-klal* says you remain anchored to something *like* the specific, whereas *ribui u-miyut u-ribui* says the two inclusive terms overwhelmingly dominate, and one need only identify the single element the *miyut* removes.

A helpful analogy: if someone says "buy anything for dinner, get some chicken, buy anything," the *klal u-prat u-klal* reading says buy things similar to chicken — perhaps meat, perhaps fish, but remaining in the domain of the specific example. The *ribui u-miyut u-ribui* reading says the instruction is essentially open-ended; the one thing to exclude is whatever the narrowing term singles out.

### Dvar Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דָּרֵישׁ כְּלָל וּפְרָט: ״אוֹ נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִשָּׁבַע לְבַטֵּא בִשְׂפָתַיִם״ – כְּלָל, ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – פְּרָט, ״לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יְבַטֵּא הָאָדָם״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל; כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – לְהַבָּא, אַף כֹּל לְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael interprets** the verse following the hermeneutical principle of **a generalization and a detail: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips"** (Leviticus 5:4), is **a generalization; "to do evil, or to do good,"** is **a detail; "whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly** with an oath," the verse **then** further **generalized.** There is a hermeneutical principle that when a verse contains **a generalization, and a detail, and** another **generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail. Just as the detail** in the verse is **explicitly** an oath referring **to the future, so too, all** the oaths for which one is liable must be referring **to the future.**

Under the *ribui u-miyut u-ribui* framework, one begins from a position of near-total inclusion. Both the first and third terms say "everything." The narrowing term *lehara o leheitiv* identifies a single category to exclude. Given that the framework is so expansive — encompassing future oaths, past oaths, neutral acts, everything — the question becomes: what is the one thing to exclude? The Gemara's answer is *dvar mitzvah*. This is because *lehara o leheitiv* suggests the domain of voluntary, discretionary human activity, things one freely chooses to do or not to do. An oath involving a matter of *mitzvah* falls outside this domain of discretion, and therefore is excluded.

Crucially, under this system one need not confine the scope to future-oriented oaths. Because the overall thrust of the two inclusive terms is to include everything, there is no requirement that the result resemble *lehara o leheitiv* in its temporal orientation. The single exclusion is *dvar mitzvah*; everything else — past or future — is included.

## Klal u-Prat u-Klal

### Structure of Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֲנִי כְּלָלָא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי אֲפִילּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה לְהַבָּא; אַהֲנִי פְּרָטָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי אֲפִילּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה לְשֶׁעָבַר.
>
> **Translation:** **The generalization serves to include even** those **matters that do not concern doing evil or doing good** when they refer **to the future; the detail serves to exclude even matters that concern doing evil or doing good** when they refer **to the past.**

Rabbi Yishmael reads the same three phrases through *klal u-prat u-klal*. The first phrase — *o nefesh ki tishava levatei bisfatayim* — is a *klal*, a general statement encompassing all oaths. The middle phrase — *lehara o leheitiv* — is a *prat*, a specific and concrete instance. The final phrase — *lechol asher yivatei ha-adam* — is a second *klal*, which broadens beyond the *prat*.

The rule of *klal u-prat u-klal* is: *ei ata dan ela ke-ein ha-perat* — you derive only what is *like* the specific instance. The *prat* here, *lehara o leheitiv*, is clearly future-oriented. Therefore: *ma ha-prat meforash le-haba af kol le-haba* — just as the specific case is explicitly future, so too everything included must be future-oriented.

What do the two *klalim* contribute? The first *klal* and the second *klal* expand beyond the precise content of the *prat*. The first broadens the category beyond acts that are literally harmful or beneficial, to include even neutral acts — *hanei klala la-asuyei afilu dvarim she-ein bahem hara-ah ve-hatavah* — as long as they are future-oriented. The *prat* then specifies that future orientation is the essential characteristic; the final *klal* broadens the scope to include any future oath, even on neutral matters. But the *prat* simultaneously limits: *le-miu-tei afilu dvarim she-yesh ban hara-ah ve-hatavah* — even those things that involve actual harm or benefit are excluded if they are past-oriented, because past orientation is unlike *lehara o leheitiv*.

The difference from *ribui u-miyut u-ribui* is now fully apparent. Under *klal u-prat u-klal*, the third term brings one back to "like the *prat*" — which requires future orientation. Under *ribui u-miyut u-ribui*, the third term is an overwhelming inclusion that makes the *miyut* do minimal exclusionary work, so one need only find the single excluded category.

### Yitzchak's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דּוּמְיָא דִּ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מִי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״; יָצָאתָה זוֹ, שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּשַׁקְּרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that Rabbi Yishmael understands that liability is extended to one whose oath **is similar to an oath "to do evil, or to do good"** (Leviticus 5:4). **He whose prohibition is due to** the verse**: "He shall not break his word"** (Numbers 30:3), is liable, as liability for an oath about the future entails breaking one's word. **Excluded is that** oath **whose prohibition is not due to** the verse**: "He shall not break his word"; rather,** it is **due to** the verse**: "You shall not lie"** (Leviticus 19:11), since liability for an oath about the past applies when the oath itself was a lie.

The Gemara raises a challenge: why should "similar to the *prat*" be understood as requiring future orientation rather than requiring that the act involve harm or benefit? One could equally say that the relevant characteristic of *lehara o leheitiv* is its content — harm and benefit — rather than its temporal orientation, in which case past-oriented oaths about harm or benefit would be included.

Rabbi Yitzchak answers: *dumya d'lehara o leheitiv — mi she-isuro mishum bal yachel devaro* — the oath must resemble *lehara o leheitiv* in being the kind of oath whose violation consists in transgressing *bal yachel devarav*, the prohibition against breaking one's word. A future-oriented oath obligates one to act or refrain from acting in a specific way, and if one subsequently violates it, that act of violation is the transgression of *bal yachel devarav*. A past-oriented oath is categorically different: the violation occurs at the moment of utterance, because the person is lying when he speaks. The transgression is *lo tishave-u vishmi lasheker* — a prohibition against swearing falsely — not *bal yachel devarav*. These are entirely different genres of *shvua*, and therefore a past-oriented oath is not *like* the *prat*, no matter how harmful or beneficial its content might be.

### Dimi bar Avin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אָבִין אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִשָּׁבַע לְבַטֵּא בִשְׂפָתַיִם״ – מִי שֶׁהַשְּׁבוּעָה קוֹדֶמֶת לַבִּיטּוּי, וְלֹא שֶׁהַבִּיטּוּי קוֹדֶמֶת לַשְּׁבוּעָה; יָצָא זֶה ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״, שֶׁהַמַּעֲשֶׂה קוֹדֵם לַשְּׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avin says** that there is a different explanation of Rabbi Yishmael's opinion: **The verse states: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips** to do evil, or to do good," referring to **one whose oath precedes** its **clarification,** i.e., the action that breaks it, **and not** to one who takes an oath **where the clarification,** i.e., the action prohibited in the oath, **precedes the oath. Excluded is that** oath where one said, for example: **I ate, or: I did not eat, where the action precedes the oath.**

Rav Dimi bar Avin offers an additional reason why Rabbi Yishmael's reading limits the scope to future-oriented oaths. The verse says *o nefesh ki tishava levatei bisfatayim* — if a person swears to express with his lips. The word *vituy* normally denotes the physical act of verbal expression, but Rav Dimi bar Avin reads it to convey a broader principle: *michdi ha-shvuah kodemet le-vituy* — the oath must precede the action. In a future-oriented oath, the *shvua* is taken first and the action follows; the oath preexists the violation. This is the paradigm case: *she-ma-aseh kodem la-shvua yatza* — it therefore excludes the case of "I ate" or "I did not eat," *achalti velo achalti*, in which the action precedes the oath.

## Oness & Shogeg

### Ha'adam Bishvua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הָאָדָם בִּשְׁבוּעָה״ – פְּרָט לְאָנוּס. ״וְנֶעְלַם״ – פְּרָט לְמֵזִיד. ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ – שֶׁנִּתְעַלְּמָה מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁבוּעָה. יָכוֹל שֶׁנִּתְעַלְּמָה מִמֶּנּוּ חֵפֶץ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בִּשְׁבוּעָה וְנֶעְלַם״ – עַל הֶעְלֵם שְׁבוּעָה הוּא חַיָּיב, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם חֵפֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips to do evil, or to do good, whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly with an oath, and it is hidden from him; and, when he knows of it, be guilty in one of these things" (Leviticus 5:4). The phrase **"a man…with an oath"** serves **to exclude a victim of circumstances beyond his control** from liability to bring an offering. The term **"and it is hidden"** serves **to exclude** from liability one who broke his oath **intentionally,** as he does not deserve to be able to achieve atonement through bringing an offering. The term **"from him"** teaches that **one who was unaware of** his **oath,** i.e., forgot it, and subsequently broke it, is liable to bring an offering. One **might** have thought that an oath taker is also liable when he broke an oath **because he was unaware** that a particular item is forbidden as the **object** of his oath; therefore, **the verse states: "With an oath, and it is hidden from him." He is liable for lack of awareness of the oath but he is not liable for lack of awareness of the object** of the oath.

The Gemara now turns to a separate dimension of *korban* liability: the conditions of consciousness and intent required at the time of the oath. A *braisa* derives from the phrase *ha-adam bishvuah* — the person in his oath — that certain states of mind or circumstance exempt one entirely. *Prat le-oness* — this excludes one who is compelled. If a person was forced into an oath, it is not binding.

A second derivation comes from the continuation of the verse: *ve-ne-elam mimenu* — and it was hidden from him. This teaches that the *korban* is incurred only when one acted *beshogeg* — inadvertently — and not *be-meizid*, intentionally. The Gemara also notes that it says *bishvuah ve-ne-elam* — it is the *shvua* that must be forgotten, not merely the object to which the oath pertains. This distinction between *he-elam shvua* and *he-elam chefetz* will receive extended treatment in the following section.

### Kahana & Assi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי, כִּי הֲווֹ קָיְימִי מִקַּמֵּי דְּרַב, מָר אֲמַר: שְׁבוּעֲתָא דְּהָכִי אֲמַר רַב, וּמָר אָמַר: שְׁבוּעֲתָא דְּהָכִי אֲמַר רַב. כִּי אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר כְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: וַאֲנָא בְּשִׁיקְרָא אִישְׁתְּבַעִי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִבָּךְ אֲנָסָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **as** it was **with Rav Kahana and Rav Asi,** who, **when they were standing up in the presence of Rav,** their teacher, at the conclusion of a lesson, disagreed with regard to exactly what he said. One **Sage said:** On my **oath Rav said like this, and** the other **Sage said:** On my **oath Rav said like that. When they came before Rav** to clarify what he had said, **he stated** his opinion **in accordance with** what **one of them** had said. **The other said to** Rav: **Did I** then **take a false oath?** Rav **said to him: Your heart compelled you.** It is not regarded as a false oath, since at the time that you took the oath you were certain that you were telling the truth.

What constitutes *oness* in the context of a *shvua*? The obvious case is physical compulsion — someone holding one at gunpoint. But the Gemara presents a more nuanced and striking example. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi were both students of Rav. They fell into a dispute about what Rav had actually ruled on some matter. One swore that Rav had said *muttar*, the other swore that Rav had said *assur*. They then came before Rav, who confirmed one of them. The one who was wrong exclaimed: *ve-ana b'shikra ishtavai* — did I swear falsely? He was genuinely convinced that he was telling the truth.

Rav replied: *libcha ansach* — your heart compelled you. Meaning: since you were fully persuaded that you were telling the truth, you are considered *anuss*. This is not a case of physical compulsion, but rather a case where one's entire subjective certainty was misdirected. The Gemara frames this under the category of *oness*, and the broader principle seems to be captured by the requirement of *ha-adam bishvua* — one must be fully present in one's oath, fully aware of all the relevant realities. If one is genuinely mistaken about a material fact underlying the oath, that kind of mistaken subjective certainty is treated as a form of *ones*, not as *shogeg*.

### Full Awareness

This raises a conceptual question: why is being sincerely mistaken about a fact classified as *oness* rather than *shogeg*? The distinction appears to be that *shogeg* by an oath involves being mistaken about the *halacha* — knowing one is lying but not knowing that doing so is prohibited or that a *korban* is required — whereas being mistaken about the underlying facts means one does not experience the act as a transgression at all. Such a person is not "present" in the *shvua* in the way *ha-adam bishvua* demands. The Torah's requirement is that the person fully stand behind the meaning of his words; if his understanding of the relevant facts is fundamentally wrong, something essential to the oath's constitution is absent. This is the framework within which the *oness* exemption operates here.

## He'elam Shvua

### Forgetting Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחֲכוּ עֲלֵיהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁבוּעָה – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּלָא חֵפֶץ, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל פַּת חִטִּין״, וּכְסָבוּר ״שֶׁאוֹכַל״ קָאָמַר – דִּשְׁבוּעֲתֵיהּ אִינְּשִׁי, חֶפְצָא דְּכִיר; אֶלָּא חֵפֶץ בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **They laughed at this in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, and said: **Granted, you find** lack of awareness of one's **oath without** there being lack of awareness of **the object** of the oath, as in a case **where one said:** On my **oath I will not eat wheat bread, and he thought he had said: I will eat** wheat bread, **as** in that case **his oath is forgotten** and **the object** of it **is remembered. But** under **what circumstances** is there a case of lack of awareness of **the object** of the oath **without** lack of awareness of the **oath** itself?

The *braisa* teaches a sharp distinction: one is *chayav* a *korban* for *he-elam shvua* — forgetting the oath — but not for *he-elam chefetz* — forgetting the object of the oath. Forgetting the oath means either forgetting that one had taken the oath entirely, or confusing its terms — for example, thinking one had sworn to eat the bread when in fact one had sworn not to eat it. Forgetting the object means remembering the oath perfectly but being confused about whether the specific item in front of one is covered by it.

### Eretz Yisrael View

In Eretz Yisrael they laughed at this distinction — *mechaichei alei be-Ma-arva*. The challenge: granted, one can readily construct a case of *he-elam shvua* without *he-elam chefetz* — a person swore not to eat wheat bread, confused the terms, and thought he had sworn to eat it; he remembers the bread but forgot which way the oath ran. But what would *he-elam chefetz* without *he-elam shvua* look like? If someone swore not to eat wheat bread and forgot that the oath was about barley — meaning he forgot which grain he had specified — then *keivan de-cheftza inshi lehu*, since the person forgot the object, that is itself a form of forgetting the *shvua*. Whether what one forgot is which action one swore to do, or which object one swore about, either way one has forgotten the particulars of one's oath. The distinction therefore collapses.

Rabbi Elazar in Eretz Yisrael concluded: *da ve-da achas* — this and that are the same; the *braisa*'s distinction is untenable, and one is *chayav* in both cases.

### Yosef's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אַלְמָא חֵפֶץ בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וְהָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ – כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל פַּת חִטִּין״, וְהוֹשִׁיט יָדוֹ לַסַּל לִיטּוֹל פַּת שְׂעוֹרִין; וְעָלְתָה בְּיָדוֹ שֶׁל חִטִּין, וּכְסָבוּר שְׂעוֹרִים הִיא וַאֲכָלָהּ; דִּשְׁבוּעֲתֵיהּ דְּכִיר לֵיהּ, חֶפְצָא הוּא דְּלָא יָדַע לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this.** Is it **really** the case that **you do not find** a case of lack of awareness of **the object** of an oath **without** lack of awareness of the **oath? But you find** it in a case **where he said:** On my **oath I will not eat wheat bread, and he extended his hand to the basket to take barley bread, and wheat** bread **came up in his hand, and he thought it was barley** bread **and ate it.** That is a case **where his oath is remembered by him, and it is the object** of the oath **of which he is unaware.**

Rav Yosef challenged this dismissal. There is a case, he argued, where one clearly forgets the object without forgetting the oath. Suppose one swore not to eat wheat bread. He then reaches into a basket intending to take out barley bread — *ve-alsah be-yado shel chitin* — and what he actually pulls out is wheat bread. He eats it, thinking it is barley. Here, *shvuata dachir lei* — he remembers the oath completely. *Cheftza hu de-lo yada lei* — it is the object he did not correctly identify. This is a precise analog to the classic case of *shogeg* in other areas: eating *treifa* meat that one believed to be *kosher*. One knew exactly what was forbidden; one was simply mistaken about the identity of the item before oneself.

### Ashi's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: כְּלוּם מְחַיְּיבַתְּ לֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן – אֶלָּא אַמַּאי דִּתְפִיס בִּידֵיהּ; הֶעְלֵם שְׁבוּעָה הוּא! לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא – אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: סוֹף סוֹף, קׇרְבָּן דְּקָא מַיְיתֵי עֲלַהּ דְּהַאי פַּת מִיהַת, הֶעְלֵם שְׁבוּעָה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Don't you deem him liable** to bring **an offering** for breaking his oath **only for that which he holds in his hand** and eats? When he eats the bread, that **is lack of awareness of the oath,** since he thinks that the item in his hand is permitted. The Gemara presents **another formulation** of this statement. **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Ultimately, the offering he brings for this bread is in any event** due to **lack of awareness of the oath,** as he thinks that the item in his hand is permitted.

Abaye, however, challenged Rav Yosef. At the end of the day, *korban de-kamyasi alah de-hai pas* — the *korban* one would bring is for eating this particular piece of bread. From the perspective of the violated oath, what happened was that the person did not realize that his *shvua* applied to what he was eating. That, Abaye argued, is itself *he-elam shvua*. The critical question is not whether one remembered the *shvua* in the abstract, but whether one realized at the relevant moment that the *shvua* prevented the action one was about to take.

Rav Yosef responded: *keivan de-ei yada lei de-chitin hu parish minei* — had someone told him "that is wheat bread," he would have immediately stopped. He fully remembers the *shvua*; it is purely the identity of the object that he misapprehended. Rav Yosef therefore maintains that this is properly classified as *he-elam chefetz*, and on that basis one would not bring a *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָזֵינַן; אִי מֵחֲמַת שְׁבוּעָה קָא פָרֵישׁ – הֲרֵי הֶעְלֵם שְׁבוּעָה בְּיָדוֹ, וְחַיָּיב; אִי מֵחֲמַת חֵפֶץ קָא פָרֵישׁ – הֲרֵי הֶעְלֵם חֵפֶץ בְּיָדוֹ, וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We see: If he refrains** from eating **due to the oath,** i.e., when he is reminded that he took an oath, **he had a lack of awareness of the oath and is liable. If he refrains due to the object** of the oath, i.e., when he is reminded what it is he is about to eat, **he had a lack of awareness due to the object, and is exempt.**

Rav Ashi proposed a practical resolution: *chazinan* — we examine the case. If, upon being told "you made a *shvua*," the person would have recognized the prohibition and refrained — that is fundamentally *he-elam shvua*, and he is *chayav*. If, upon being told "that is wheat bread," the person would have recognized the problem and refrained — that is fundamentally *he-elam chefetz*, and he is *patur*. The determination depends on which awareness would have been the primary trigger for his restraint.

### Ravina's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: כְּלוּם פָּרֵישׁ מִשְּׁבוּעָה – אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם חֵפֶץ! כְּלוּם פָּרֵישׁ מֵחֵפֶץ – אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעָה! אֶלָּא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Doesn't he refrain from** breaking the **oath due to** his recognition of the **object? Doesn't he refrain from** the **object due only to** the **oath?** In either case, he needs to remember both the oath and its object, and the manner in which he was reminded does not serve to indicate anything. **Rather, there is no difference** between the two.

Ravina challenged Rav Ashi's resolution: *u-klum parish mi-shvura ela mishum chefetz u-klum parish mi-chefetz ela mishum shvura* — in truth, neither the forgotten oath alone nor the forgotten object alone would have caused the person to stop. He would not have stopped eating unless both memories were restored. The reality is that both were forgotten, and the conceptual question of which would have "triggered" the other is unanswerable. Therefore, *lo shna* — it makes no difference; one cannot distinguish. Rashi, consistent with his reading of the parallel case of *he-elam tumat Mikdash*, appears to hold that in such a case one is *chayav*, since the presence of *he-elam shvua* is sufficient to obligate.

## Shogeg Definition

### Rava's Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: אֵיזוֹ הִיא שִׁגְגַת שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי לְשֶׁעָבַר? אִי דְּיָדַע – מֵזִיד הוּא! אִי דְּלָא יָדַע – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asked of Rav Naḥman: What is** the case of **an unwitting oath on an utterance** referring **to the past,** for which one is liable to bring an offering? **If** it is a case **where he knows** when he takes the oath that it is not true, then **he is an intentional** taker of a false oath and may not bring an offering. **If** it is a case **where he did not know** at the time of taking the oath that what he was saying is not true, then **he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control,** and is exempt from bringing an offering.

The Gemara now turns to the question of defining *shogeg* in the context of past-oriented oaths. According to Rabbi Akiva, a past-oriented false oath can generate *korban* liability — but only if committed *be-shogeg*. Rava asks Rav Nachman: *eizu hi shegagat shvuat bituy le-sha-avar?* — what is the example of *shogeg* for a past-oriented *shvuat bituy*? The difficulty is stark. If you know you are lying, you are *meizid*. If you do not know you are lying — if you believe you are telling the truth — we have said that is classified as *oness*. What space remains for *shogeg*?

### Not Knowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, בְּאוֹמֵר: ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁשְּׁבוּעָה זוֹ אֲסוּרָה, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן אוֹ לָאו״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him** in answer to his question: It is a case **where** the one taking the oath **says: I know that** taking **this oath is prohibited, but I do not know if one is liable** to bring **an offering for it or not.** Since he does not know the full implications of his action, it is regarded as unwitting, and he may still bring an offering to atone for it.

The answer: *de-amar yodeia ani she-shvua zo asurah aval eini yodeia im chayavin aleha korban o lo* — the person knows the oath is forbidden, but did not know whether it carries an obligation of *korban*. This is a peculiar form of *shogeg*: one is fully aware that one is transgressing, yet unaware that the specific *korban* obligation applies. One might object that such a person should receive lashes rather than bring a *korban*, since he knowingly violated a prohibition. Indeed, it may be that such a person incurs both lashes and brings a *korban*; the point is that his ignorance of the *korban* obligation constitutes sufficient *shogeg* to create the *korban* liability.

Tosafot suggests an easier example of *shogeg*: one who did not realize at all that the act was a sin — perhaps thinking one was violating a positive commandment rather than a negative prohibition, or not recognizing the act as forbidden at all. This would more cleanly remove the person from the *meizid* category. But the Gemara proceeds with the narrower case.

### Munbaz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן – כְּמוֹנְבַּז, דְּאָמַר: שִׁגְגַת קׇרְבָּן שְׁמָהּ שְׁגָגָה? אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּמוֹנְבַּז – אֶלָּא בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, דְּלָאו חִידּוּשׁ הוּא; אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא – דִּבְכָל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן לָאו דְּמַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, דְּיָלְפִינַן מֵעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה; וְהָכָא מַיְיתֵי – אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava asked Rav Naḥman further: **In accordance with whose** opinion do you answer in this way? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Munbaz, who says: Lack of intention with regard to the offering,** i.e., ignorance as to whether one's action renders one liable to bring an offering, **is regarded as lack of intention?** There is a dispute between Munbaz and the Rabbis in tractate *Shabbat* (69a–b) with regard to one who knows that a particular action desecrates Shabbat but does not know that it renders one liable to bring a sin-offering. Munbaz holds that even one who is merely ignorant of the liability to bring an offering is deemed an unwitting sinner who brings a sin-offering to atone. The Rabbis disagree and deem him unwitting only if he is unaware that the action is prohibited. Rav Naḥman explains: **You** may **even say** that this explanation accords with the opinion of **the Rabbis. The Rabbis disagree with** the opinion **of Munbaz only with regard to all** typical cases in **the entire Torah** for which one is liable to bring a sin-offering, where that liability **is not a novelty. But here,** with regard to oaths, it could be said **that** bringing a sin-offering **is a novelty, since** there is a principle that **we do not find in the entire Torah** another example of a simple **prohibition for which** one **brings an offering** for its unwitting violation. The reason for this principle is **that we derive** for which prohibitions one brings a sin-offering **from** the prohibition of **idol worship,** where one is liable to receive *karet* for an intentional violation and one is liable to bring a sin-offering for an unwitting violation. **And** yet **here,** in the case of the oath, **one brings** a sin-offering even though intentional violation of the prohibition is not punishable by *karet*. Given the novelty of the offering for an oath on an utterance, **even the Rabbis would agree** that ignorance of the fact that one is performing an action for which the Torah legislates an offering is sufficient for one to be regarded as unwitting and liable to bring a sliding-scale offering.

The Gemara notes that this ruling resembles the position of Munbaz, who holds in Masechet Shabbat that *shegagat korban shmah shegaga* — not knowing that one's act carries *korban* liability is itself sufficient to classify one as *shogeg*. Does the Gemara's answer here follow only Munbaz, against the majority Rabbinical position?

The Gemara answers: *afilu teima rabbanan* — even the Rabbis would agree here. The Rabbis only dispute Munbaz with respect to the rest of the Torah, where the standard rules apply: a *chatat* is brought for sins that carry *karet*, and normal *shogeg* categories are well-defined. But here, the *korban* liability for *shvuat bituy* is itself an expansion of the ordinary rules — *de-lav chiddush hu* elsewhere, but *de-chiddush hu* here, since ordinarily a simple negative prohibition does not generate *korban* liability. We learn that it does here from *avodah zarah* by way of analogy. Since the entire *korban* obligation in this context is already a broadened category, the Rabbis concede that not knowing about the *korban* obligation is sufficient to constitute *shogeg* and trigger the obligation to bring one.

## Shav Mi-Yedi'ah

### Life at Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבִינָא מֵרָבָא: נִשְׁבַּע עַל כִּכָּר וּמִסְתַּכֵּן עָלֶיהָ, מַהוּ? מִסְתַּכֵּן – לִישְׁרֵי לֵיהּ מָר! אֶלָּא מִצְטַעֵר, וַאֲכָלָהּ בְּשִׁגְגַת שְׁבוּעָה – מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina asked Rava:** If one **took an oath concerning** a certain **loaf,** rendering himself prohibited from eating it, **and** later his life **is in danger** due to his not eating it, **what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable to bring an offering in atonement for eating it? Rava responded: If his life is **in danger, let the Master permit him** to eat, as saving his life overrides the prohibition; he is considered a victim of circumstances beyond his control and does not need to atone at all. Ravina said: **Rather,** the question is this: With regard to **one who is suffering** from hunger **and** who **ate it unwittingly,** i.e., he forgot the prohibition generated by the **oath,** although had he known, he would still have eaten this loaf of bread intentionally due to his hunger, **what** is the *halakha*?

Ravina poses a case to Rava: someone took an oath not to eat a particular loaf of bread, and then his life was endangered — *mistakana leh* — and he ate it. Is a *korban* brought? One might have thought that even though *pikuach nefesh* renders the eating permitted and obligatory, there remains some metaphysical dimension to having violated an oath sworn in God's name, perhaps sufficient to generate a *korban*. The Gemara immediately dismisses this: if his life was at risk, he was permitted — indeed required — to eat the bread *le-chat-chila*. There can obviously be no *korban* in such a case.

### Desire Case

The real *seifa* of the question concerns a different scenario. Someone is in anguish — *mitztayer* — intensely desiring this particular loaf of bread, and at the moment of eating he has forgotten his oath. Did he forget because his desire overwhelmed his memory? And regardless of the cause of forgetting, the critical question is: given how desperately he wanted to eat the bread, even had he remembered the oath, would he have violated it anyway? If so, does he bring a *korban* for having eaten it in a state of apparent *shogeg*?

### Rashi View

Rashi reads the case as follows: the forgetting is incidental. The person genuinely forgot his oath — perhaps it had been taken long ago. The fact that he very much wanted to eat the bread is a separate datum: we know that even had he remembered the oath, he would have violated it regardless, because his desire was so strong. The question is therefore whether one who forgets and acts, but who would have acted even had he remembered, is classified as *shogeg* and brings a *korban*.

### Would Violate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: שָׁב מִידִיעָתוֹ – מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ; לֹא שָׁב מִידִיעָתוֹ – אֵין מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said **to him: We learn** in a *baraita*: **One** who, had he known that his action was prohibited, would have **withdrawn** from sinning **due to his knowledge, brings an offering for his unwitting** sin; but **one** who **would not have withdrawn** from sinning **due to his knowledge does not bring an offering for his unwitting** sin. Since he would have eaten anyway, he does not bring an offering.

The Gemara answers by citing a principle: *shav mi-yedi-ah maiti korban al shigegato* — one who would have refrained had he been informed of the oath brings a *korban* for his inadvertence. *Lo shav mi-yedi-ah ein maiti korban* — one who would not have refrained even had he been informed does not bring a *korban*. In the case described, since the person would have violated the oath even had he remembered it, he does not bring a *korban*. He is not genuinely a *shogeg* in the operative sense, because his *shogeg* state did not functionally alter his conduct. The *korban* is *kaparah*; it is appropriate only for someone who, had he known, would have acted differently. For someone who would have willfully violated regardless, the *korban* is not the appropriate response — the violation was effectively *meizid* at the level of intent, even if technically *shogeg* at the level of awareness.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot reads the case differently. The word *mitztayer aleha* indicates something more active: the person was in such anguish over the desired food that he effectively *wished* to forget his oath so that he could eat. Tosafot reads this as a case where the forgetting itself was quasi-volitional — brought about by the person's desire to be in a state of not-knowing. The person did not neutrally forget; he was oriented toward forgetting as a means of enabling himself to eat. In this reading, the case is not one where the person happened to forget and would have violated anyway. Rather, the person willed himself into a state of forgetfulness precisely because he wished to eat. Tosafot therefore identifies this as an instance where the person is not *shav mi-yedi-ah* — not someone who would have pulled back had he known — because his entire orientation was toward eating regardless.

The two readings thus differ on the mechanism: according to Rashi, the forgetting was innocent and the disqualifying factor is the hypothetical counterfactual that he would have violated anyway. According to Tosafot, the forgetting was itself the product of desire, and the disqualifying factor is that the person essentially engineered his own unawareness in order to transgress.

## Verbal Expression

### Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיּוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְבַטֵּא בִּשְׂפָתַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** Even after one **decided** to take an oath, he **needs to express** it **with his lips** for it to take effect, **as it is stated** in the verse: "Or if anyone take an oath **clearly with his lips** to do evil, or to do good" (Leviticus 5:4).

Shmuel rules: *gamar be-libo tzorich she-yotzi be-sfatav* — if someone resolves in his heart to take an oath, the oath is not binding unless he actually gives it verbal expression. The source is *levatei bisfatayim* — to express with the lips. The *shvua* requires articulation.

### Bituy Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״בִּשְׂפָתַיִם״ – וְלֹא בַּלֵּב; גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יְבַטֵּא הָאָדָם בִּשְׁבוּעָה״. הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ ״בִּשְׂפָתַיִם״ וְלֹא בַּלֵּב, וַהֲדַר אָמְרַתְּ ״גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ מִנַּיִן״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: The verse states that one takes an oath **"with his lips," but not with his heart. From where** is it derived that **one who decided in his heart** to take an oath is liable to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance? **The verse states: "Whatsoever it be that a man shall express with an oath"** (Leviticus 5:4). The Gemara points out: **This** *baraita* is **difficult** in **itself. You said: "With his lips," but not with his heart, and then you said: From where** is it derived that **one who decided in his heart** to take an oath is liable? There seems to be a contradiction within the *baraita* concerning the *halakha* where one did not express the oath.

A *braisa* appears to be internally self-contradictory. It first states: *be-sfatayim ve-lo be-lev* — with the lips, not if merely resolved in the heart. It then asks: *gamar be-libo minayin* — how do I know that a heart-commitment is also binding? — and derives it from *lechol asher yivatei ha-adam*, which broadens the scope to include even mental commitment. The Gemara notes: *ha-gufa kashya* — the *braisa* contradicts itself. It begins by excluding heart-commitment and then includes it.

### Sheshet Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״בִּשְׂפָתַיִם״ – וְלֹא שֶׁגָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ לְהוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו וְלֹא הוֹצִיא; גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ סְתָם מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יְבַטֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: This** is **not difficult; this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** One takes an oath **"with his lips" and** does **not** take an oath **when he** merely **decided in his heart to express with his lips but has not** yet actually **expressed** the oath verbally. **From where** is it derived that one who **simply decided in his heart** to take an oath without the intention of stating it with his lips is liable? **The verse states: "Whatsoever it be that** a man **shall express** with an oath" (Leviticus 5:4).

Rav Sheshet resolves the internal contradiction by reading the two clauses of the *braisa* as addressing two different cases. The first clause — *be-sfatayim ve-lo she-gamar le-hotzi be-sfatav ve-lo hotzi* — excludes the case where someone intended to verbalize the oath but did not actually do so. He resolved to speak but never spoke. Since the speech never materialized, even though he had intended to speak, no oath is created. The second clause then asks: what if someone intended the commitment purely internally, never planning to verbalize it at all? The answer, derived from *lechol asher yivatei ha-adam*, is that this is also binding.

Rav Sheshet thus identifies two distinct exclusions within the word *bisfatayim*. The first reading excludes the case where one intended to speak but did not. The second reading, from the broader verse, includes the case where one intended to commit only mentally. This means: without Shmuel's ruling, we would have said that an entirely internal commitment, if that is all that was intended, is binding. Shmuel overrides this and requires actual verbalization in all cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** this *baraita* nevertheless poses **a difficulty,** as it indicates that an oath that was not expressed verbally takes effect.

What, then, does Shmuel do with the *braisa*? The Gemara notes: *ela le-Shmuel kashya* — for Shmuel, the *braisa* remains difficult. The *braisa*'s second clause seems to validate heart-commitment as binding, in tension with Shmuel's ruling that verbalization is always required.

## Exceptions

### Motza Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמוֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁהוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו; גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל נְדִיב לֵב״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Shmuel from a *baraita*: The verse states: **"That which is gone out of your lips you shall observe and do;** according as you have vowed freely to the Lord your God, even that which you have promised with your mouth" (Deuteronomy 23:24). From here **I have** derived **only** a case in **which he expresses with his lips. From where** do I derive a case where **he decided** only **in his heart? The verse states** in the context of the contributions to the building of the Tabernacle: "And they came, both men and women, **as many as were willinghearted,** and brought nose rings, and earrings, and signet rings, and girdles, all jewels of gold" (Exodus 35:22). The fact that the verse describes those who contributed as willinghearted indicates that one becomes liable via a non-verbal decision.

The Gemara challenges Shmuel's position from the verse *motza sefatcha tishmor ve-asita* — what proceeds from your lips, you shall guard and perform — from which one might derive that only verbal commitments bind. The *braisa* then asks how heart-commitment can also obligate, and derives it from *be-chol nediv lev*, concerning donations to the *Beit Hamikdash*. If the heart-commitment of a generous donor is sufficient to create a binding obligation in that context, why should Shmuel's general rule exclude heart-commitment elsewhere?

### Two Verses Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב ״כׇּל נְדִיב לֵב״. וְנִיגְמַר מִינַּהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָווּ תְּרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The case **there,** of contributions to the Tabernacle, **is different, as it is written: "As many as were willinghearted."** This *halakha* is stated only in the context of contributions to the Tabernacle, not in the context of oaths. The Gemara suggests: **And let us learn from it** that in general, oaths can be taken via a non-verbal decision. One cannot extrapolate from the fact that the non-verbal consecrations to the Tabernacle were effective, **because *teruma*,** which can be separated non-verbally, **and** donating **consecrated** items **are two verses that come as one,** i.e., they are both instances where a non-verbal commitment is sufficient, **and any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common element to apply to other cases.

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam, diktiv kol nediv lev* — that case is different, because there the Torah explicitly says *kol nediv lev*. Moreover, this is a case of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba-im ke-echad* — two verses that teach the same exceptional rule simultaneously — and the principle is *shnei ketuvim ha-ba-im ke-echad ein melamdin* — two such verses cannot be used to derive a general rule. One verse is *terumah* — whether referring to the setting aside of grain *terumah*, for which mental designation is sufficient to effect the separation (*ve-nechshav lachem*), or to the *parshat Terumah* context of donations to the *Mikdash*, *kol nediv lev* — and the other is *kodashim*, for which the verse in *Divrei Hayamim* establishes that mental commitment to bring an offering is sufficient to generate the *neder* obligation even without verbalization. Since these are two exceptional cases taught by two separate verses, they cannot be extended to establish a general rule.

### Donation Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָווּ חוּלִּין וְקָדָשִׁים, וְחוּלִּין מִקֳּדָשִׁים לָא גָּמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one **do not teach** their common element, **but according to the one who says** that **they do teach** their common element, **what can be said?** The Gemara answers: The two contexts here are **non-sacred** items, i.e., oaths, **and consecrated** items, donations to the Tabernacle and *teruma*, **and we do not derive** *halakhot* concerning **non-sacred** items **from** *halakhot* concerning **consecrated** items.

The exception of *terumah* and *kodashim* has significant practical implications. The Gemara raises the alternative resolution that *chullin mi-kodshim lo gamrinan* — the laws of non-sacred matters are not derived from sacred ones. Whether the *terumah* category here refers narrowly to the separation of grain *terumah* or broadly to donations of *kol nediv lev* — donations made with a generous heart to religious causes — is a question with direct practical consequence. If someone mentally resolves to donate a sum to the synagogue, does that thought alone create a binding *neder* obligation, even without verbal declaration?

If the *terumah* exception extends to charitable pledges and donations more broadly, then heart-commitment in that domain binds without verbalization. The Gemara leaves open the precise scope of the exception, but the question is a live *halachic* one: whether silent mental commitment to donate to religious causes creates an obligation, based on whether such donations fall under the *terumah* category that the Gemara identifies as an exception to the general requirement of verbal expression.
