# Zevachim 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shchutei Chutz Today

### Contemporary Altars

The daf opens at the bottom of 107b, where the Gemara turns to a thoroughly practical question: what is the law for someone who slaughters or offers up sacrifices outside the *Beis HaMikdash* in the present day? The entire discussion flows from the *issur* of *bamot* — not the mere possession of a private altar, but the act of using one. The Gemara introduces this with the word *itmar*, signaling an Amoraic dispute.

### Kedusha Rishona

*Rabbi Yochanan amar chayav* — Rabbi Yochanan rules that one who burns an offering on an altar today is *chayav*, subject to *kares*, for having offered a sacrifice outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. His reasoning: *kedusha rishona kidsah l'sha'atah v'kidsah l'atid lavo* — the first sanctification of the Temple site was effective for all future times. The *Makom HaMikdash* therefore retains its sanctity even after destruction, and that place alone remains the site at which sacrifices are acceptable. Since the location remains sanctified, any offering made elsewhere still violates the prohibition of *shchutei chutz* with full liability.

### Resh Lakish's View

*Resh Lakish amar patur* — Resh Lakish rules that one is exempt. His reasoning is the inverse: *kedusha rishona kidsah l'sha'atah v'lo kidsah l'atid lavo* — the first sanctification was only for its time and did not extend indefinitely into the future.

### First vs. Second Kedusha

It is worth pausing to clarify the precise contours of this dispute. The framing in terms of *kedusha rishona* should not mislead. The real question is not which *kedusha* — first or second — persists, but whether *any* sanctification of the *Makom HaMikdash* endures until the present day. The Rambam, for instance, holds by *Eretz Yisrael* that the *kedusha rishona* lapsed at the first exile, while the second *kedusha* persists even now. One could similarly argue that while the first *kedusha* of the Temple site may have dissipated, the second is stronger and more enduring. The essential question, regardless of which *kedusha* one invokes, is whether the sanctity of the *Makom HaMikdash* is operative today.

### Bamot & Transition

A further conceptual distinction is worth drawing here. The Gemara elsewhere cites the verse *ki lo batem ad atah el hamenucha v'el hanachala* — *hamenucha* refers to places like *Nov* and *Givon* and *Shiloh*, while *hanachala* refers to *Yerushalayim*. This verse implies that the era of private *bamot* was explicitly a transitional period in history, lasting only until a permanent central site was designated for the *Beis HaMikdash*. Once God designated that location as the permanent home of the *shchina*, the era of *bamot* closed — permanently. This point stands independent of whether the *kedusha* of the *Makom HaMikdash* itself persists.

It is thus possible to distinguish two separate questions: first, whether the *Makom HaMikdash* retains its *kedusha* such that sacrifices could theoretically be brought there today; and second, whether the *issur bamot* has been lifted now that the *Beis HaMikdash* lies in ruins. These need not stand or fall together. Even Resh Lakish, who rules *patur*, may well agree that the *issur bamot* remains in effect — that it is forbidden to bring sacrifices in one's backyard. His point may be more technical: the specific liability of *kares* for *shchutei chutz* requires that the act be *raui lepetach ohel moed*, fit in principle for the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. If the *Makom HaMikdash* no longer retains its *kedusha*, then nothing is *raui lepetach ohel moed*, and the trigger for *kares* is absent, even though a lesser prohibition remains.

### Raui Le'Petach

This is indeed the implication of the Gemara, which cites *v'el petach ohel moed lo heveio* — the verse that grounds the liability for *shchutei chutz* in the concept of *raui lepetach ohel moed*. Rabbi Yochanan holds that even today, in principle, a sacrifice could be brought to the *Makom HaMikdash* — it remains sanctified — and therefore the requirement of *raui lepetach ohel moed* is satisfied, and one is *chayav kares*. Resh Lakish denies this: without operative *kedusha*, there is nowhere that qualifies as *petach ohel moed*, and the *chiyuv kares* accordingly does not apply.

## Curtains in the Temple

### Rabbi Eliezer

The Gemara now asks whether this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish is rooted in an earlier Tannaitic debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. The Gemara cites a *mishnah*: *amar Rabbi Eliezer*, when the builders were constructing the *heichal* during the Second Temple period, they would erect *kelayim* — curtains — both around the *heichal* and around the *azara*. There was a difference in practice between the two areas: for the *heichal*, the builders would work from outside the curtain, so as not to stand within the space where the *heichal* walls would be, whereas in the *azara* they would work from inside the curtain.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: *shamati she'hayu makrivim af al pi she'ein bayis* — I have a tradition that offerings could be brought even without a standing Temple structure. Moreover, *kodshu kodoshim* could be eaten even without *kelayim* to demarcate the *azara*, *kodshim kalim* and *maaser sheini* could be consumed even without a wall encircling *Yerushalayim*. The reasoning is explicit: *mipnei shekedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'atah v'kidsah l'atid lavo* — because the first sanctification endured for all future times.

This is a striking *chiddush* within Rabbi Yehoshua's position. Even if one grants that *kedusha* persists, one might still argue that a physical structure — a *bayit*, walls, curtains — is required before sacrifices may be offered. Rabbi Yehoshua goes further: the *kedusha* is so deeply embedded in the place itself that even the complete absence of structure does not diminish it. The *makom ha'azara* remains defined as such without walls, *Yerushalayim* remains *kedushas Yerushalayim* without a surrounding wall.

### After Destruction

The Gemara then asks: does Rabbi Eliezer's position implicitly reject this? Since Rabbi Eliezer emphasizes that *kelayim* would be erected during construction, and Rabbi Yehoshua responds that *kelayim* are not halachically necessary, it seems as though Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *kedusha* did not persist — and that some form of renewed demarcation was required before sacrifices could resume.

### Ravina's Question

Ravina challenges this inference in a question posed to Rav Ashi: *dilma de'kulei alma kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'atah v'kidsah l'atid lavo* — perhaps everyone agrees with the fundamental principle of Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua may simply be transmitting different traditions, each reporting what he had heard — *mar mai dishmei lei ka'amar u'mar mai dishmei lei ka'amar* — without any underlying disagreement about whether *kedusha* persists.

### Curtains for Modesty

This raises the obvious question: if Rabbi Eliezer also agrees that *kedusha* persists and that one may offer sacrifices without a structure, why does he emphasize the *kelayim*? The Gemara answers: *litzni'usa be'alma* — for modesty alone. Just as construction sites today are screened off from public view as a matter of propriety and respect, the *kelayim* around the emerging *Beis HaMikdash* served an aesthetic and decorous function, not a halachic one. They were not required to establish *kedusha* or to permit the offering of sacrifices.

## Bone & Salt

### Bird's Head Case

The Gemara now introduces another dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, this time concerning what counts toward the *kezayit* required for liability under *shchutei chutz*. The case: one places a bird's limb on the fire, but the meat alone does not constitute a *kezayit* — only together with its bone does the offering reach that measure. *Raui lepetach ohel moed* has been established as a condition; the question is what constitutes the required minimum.

### Chibbur Olin

*Rabbi Yochanan amar chayav* — Rabbi Yochanan rules that one is liable. His principle: *chibbur olin k'olin dami* — something connected to an item that is offered is treated as part of the offering itself. As Rashi notes, this is directly related to the earlier discussion that while a separated bone need not be placed on the altar, a bone still attached to the meat is burned along with it. Since it is connected, it counts as part of what is offered.

### Not Offered Per Se

*Resh Lakish amar patur* — Resh Lakish rules that one is exempt. His principle is the inverse: *chibbur olin lav k'olin dami*. It is true that the bone is burned when connected, but this does not mean the bone is itself *offered*. It is simply dragged along with the meat. It was never independently the object of the sacrificial act, and therefore it cannot contribute to the minimum measure that triggers liability.

### Salt Question

Rava then poses a question that builds on this debate: what if one places a bird's head on the fire where the meat alone does not constitute a *kezayit*, but the salt with which it is coated completes the measure? Salt is required to be brought with every sacrifice — *lo tashbit melach brit Elokecha me'al kol korbancha takriv melach* — so it is routinely offered together with the sacrifice. Rav Pappa bar Zeika suggests to Rav Ashi that this is simply the same dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish. After all, salt is something placed on the *mizbeach* together with the sacrifice, just as a bone is.

### Organic vs. Supplementary

The Gemara rejects this, and the rejection is illuminating: *tiba'i l'Rabbi Yochanan tiba'i l'Resh Lakish* — the question applies independently to both positions, because the case of salt differs from the case of bone in ways that cut in opposite directions for each Amora.

Consider Rabbi Yochanan's position. He held that the bone counts because it is *mina d'bisra* — organically part of the meat itself, in addition to being physically connected. Salt, however, is clearly not an organic part of the bird. Even Rabbi Yochanan might therefore rule that salt does not contribute to the *kezayit*.

Consider Resh Lakish's position. He held that the bone does not count because if it were to separate, there would be no independent obligation to place it on the altar — it is only there because it happens to be attached. Salt, by contrast, if it were to fall off, there *would* be an obligation to reapply it, since salt is independently required on every sacrifice. Salt is thus not a mere passenger but an obligatory component of the offering. Even Resh Lakish might therefore rule that salt counts.

### Teiku

The question thus cannot be resolved by simple extension of the earlier dispute, since each side's reasoning points in an unexpected direction regarding salt. The Gemara concludes: *teiku* — the question stands unresolved.

## Rabbi Yosi HaGlili

### Fit for the Altar

The Gemara turns to a position of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, who holds that liability for *ma'aleh* — burning an offering outside — is limited to cases involving what the Gemara terms *mikudshai p'nim*, meat that was slaughtered inside the *Beis HaMikdash*. The requirement of *raui lepetach ohel moed* means, for Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, that the meat must have been fit for the altar. If one slaughtered outside and then burned the offering outside, the meat was never fit for the *mizbeach* — it was never *raui lepetach ohel moed* — and therefore there is no liability for *ma'aleh bachutz*.

### Rabbis' Challenge

The Rabbis challenge this position. Even if you slaughter inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, the moment you take the meat outside it becomes *pasul*. If liability still exists in that case — as Rabbi Yosi HaGlili presumably concedes — then you must accept that one can be liable even when the meat is no longer *raui*. What then is the meaningful distinction between slaughtering inside and slaughtering outside?

### Sha'as Kosher

*Heshiv Rebbe tachas Rabbi Yosi HaGlili* — Rebbe steps in to defend the position of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili. The distinction is *sha'as kosher*: when one slaughters inside the *Beis HaMikdash* and then takes the meat outside, there was at least one moment — at the time of slaughter — when the meat was fit for the altar, *raui lepetach ohel moed*. That moment of fitness is sufficient. *Tomar b'shochet bachutz u'ma'aleh bachutz shelo haysa lo sha'as kosher* — but when one slaughters outside and then burns it outside, the meat never had such a moment. It was disqualified from the outset, and the liability for *ma'aleh bachutz* is accordingly absent.

### Kodesh Mekablo

Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon also defends Rabbi Yosi HaGlili but frames the distinction differently. *She'chein kodesh mekablo* — when meat is slaughtered inside and then taken outside, even though it became *pasul* as *yotzei*, the *kodesh* still, in a sense, accepted it. One of the principles of the *mizbeach* is that if *pasul* meat that falls into the category of *psula bakodesh* is placed on the altar, it is not removed. Since meat slaughtered inside but taken outside would not be removed from the *mizbeach* if placed there, it remains *raui* in a meaningful sense. *Tomar b'shochet bachutz she'ein kodesh mekablo* — meat slaughtered outside, however, is not *psula bakodesh*, and if placed on the *mizbeach* it would be taken down. It is not *raui* in any sense.

### Night Slaughter

*Amar Zeiri* — what practical case distinguishes Rebbe from Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon? *Shechitas laila ika binaihu* — slaughter performed at night inside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Such slaughter is invalid, and therefore the meat never had a *sha'as kosher* according to Rebbe — the slaughter occurred inside, but it was never fit at any moment, so Rebbe would rule *patur*. Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon, however, would hold that this is *psula bakodesh* — it is the kind of disqualification that occurs in the *kodesh* context — and if placed on the *mizbeach* the meat would be allowed to remain. Therefore Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon would hold one *chayav*.

### Non-Sacred Vessel

*Rabbah amar* — a parallel distinguishing case: if the blood was caught in a non-sacred vessel, *kli chol*, rather than a sacred vessel. Again, according to Rebbe, there is no *sha'as kosher* — the blood reception was invalid from the outset — and Rabbi Yosi HaGlili would therefore render one *patur*. According to Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon, however, this is *psula bakodesh* and the meat remains on the *mizbeach*, generating liability.

## Tamei & Issur

### Double Tumah

The Gemara now moves to a parallel application of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's approach, this time in the context of *tumah*. The case: a person who is *tamei* eats meat of *kodashim* that is itself *tamei*. Two points of law are relevant: eating *kodashim* while personally *tamei* carries *kares*; eating *kodashim* that are *tamei* violates a *lav* but does not carry *kares*. What is the law when both obtain simultaneously?

### No Chiyuv

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili holds that one is *patur* from *kares* in this case. Since the meat was already *tamei* — already defiled — it was not *tahor* meat that the *tamei* person consumed, and the *chiyuv kares* cannot attach.

### Parallel Cases

The Rabbis challenge this position with a pointed observation. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili concedes that in the ordinary case — a *tamei* person eating *tahor kodashim* — there is a *chiyuv kares*. But by the very act of picking up the meat and eating it, a *tamei* person renders the meat *tamei* through contact. So even in the case that Rabbi Yosi HaGlili concedes generates *kares*, the meat ends up *tamei* by the time it is consumed. The Rabbis conclude: *shappir kamreiley rabbanan l'Rabbi Yosi HaGlili* — unlike the earlier debates where Rebbe and Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon came to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's defense, here the challenge is judged a strong one, because at the end of the day the meat is *tamei* in both scenarios.

### Migu Principle

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction within Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position by drawing a crucial distinction. *Kol heicha d'nitma tumas haguf v'achar kach nitma basar d'chuley alma lo pligi d'chayav* — where one first became *tamei* as a person and only thereafter the meat became *tamei*, everyone agrees one is *chayav*. If I became *tamei* first, there was already a full *chiyuv kares* in place the moment that status obtained. The subsequent *tumah* of the meat does not dislodge that liability.

The dispute concerns the reverse sequence: the meat became *tamei* first, and then the person became *tamei*. Here the question is one of *issur chal al issur* — whether the *issur kares* can take effect on top of a pre-existing lesser prohibition. The principle at stake is *migu*: when one becomes *tamei*, one does not merely become forbidden to eat this one piece of already-*tamei* meat. One becomes forbidden to eat all *kodashim*, including the many *tahor* pieces one might otherwise have consumed. This is an *issur kolel* — an inclusive prohibition, one that sweeps in things not previously forbidden. Since the *issur kares* takes hold on all those *tahor* pieces, it also takes hold — by virtue of *migu*, as a side effect of the broader prohibition — on this already-*tamei* piece. The *Rabbanan* accept this reasoning.

### No Migu

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, however, *savar lo amrinan migu* — does not accept the principle that *issur chal al issur* operates by way of *migu* for an *issur kolel*. Even though the second prohibition has a broader scope, it does not take effect atop the pre-existing prohibition on this specific piece of meat.

### Which is Weightier

The Gemara presses Rabbi Yosi HaGlili: even without *migu*, is not *tumas haguf* weightier than *tumas habasar*? *Tumas haguf* carries *kares* and has a broader reach — it forbids the consumption of all *kodashim*. A weightier prohibition taking effect alongside a lesser one might be expected to override it. Rav Ashi responds: *mimai detumas haguf chamura?* — what makes *tumas haguf* unambiguously weightier? There is a sense in which *tumas habasar* is in fact weightier: *she'ein la tahara bemikvah* — the *tumah* of the meat cannot be removed through *immersion*. A person can immerse and become *tahor*; the meat, once *tamei*, cannot. From the perspective of the meat itself, its *tamei* status is more permanent, more deeply embedded. Since the question is whether the *issur kares* takes effect *on the meat*, the permanence of the meat's own *tumah* status may be precisely what blocks it.

## Stringencies

### Shchita Stringent

The *mishnah* introduces a comparative analysis of *shchutei chutz* and *ha'alas chutz*: there are respects in which slaughtering outside is governed by stricter rules, and respects in which burning an offering outside is governed by stricter rules.

*Chomer beshchita* — the stringency of slaughter: *hashocheit lehediot chayav* — one who slaughters a sanctified animal outside the *Beis HaMikdash* for mundane purposes, simply to eat its meat, is nonetheless *chayav*. This is a considerable *chiddush*. Slaughter is not inherently a sacrificial act. If a person has an animal sanctified as a *shelamim* and decides to slaughter it for a barbecue, the act of slaughter carries no intrinsically sacrificial quality — it is simply preparation of meat. Yet because the animal was *kodshim* and was slaughtered outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, liability attaches regardless of the person's intent. By contrast, *ha'ma'aleh lehediot patur* — one who burns the meat outside as an act of homage to another person, or even as an offering to an idol, is *patur* from the specific prohibition of *ha'alas chutz*. Liability for burning outside requires that the act be performed as a sacrificial offering to God; slaughter outside generates liability regardless of purpose.

### Offering Stringent

*Chomer ba'aliyah* — the stringency of offering: *shnayim she'achazu besakin veshachtu peturin* — two people who jointly hold the knife and slaughter together are exempt. *Achzu b'eiver vehe'elu chayavin* — but two people who jointly place a piece of meat on the fire as a sacrificial act are both *chayav*. The rule that partnership in a *melachah* generates liability operates for burning outside but not for slaughtering outside. As the Gemara will explain, this differential treatment is anchored in the verse structure of the relevant *parshiyot*.

## Multiple Offerings

### Multiple Pieces

The *mishnah* then addresses the case where one places multiple limbs or pieces on the fire in succession, pausing between each. *He'ela v'chazar v'he'ela v'chazar v'he'ela chayav* — what is the law regarding repeated acts of placing on the fire? Are these counted as separate transgressions, each generating independent liability, or as one continuous act?

### Rabbi Shimon

*Chayav al kol aliyah divrei Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Shimon holds that if one places multiple limbs — say, the four legs of an animal — on the fire in succession, with an intervening moment of awareness between each act, each constitutes a separate transgression. The *nafka mina* for liability purposes is that if the acts were performed *b'shogeg*, each one would require a separate *korban*.

### Rabbi Yosi

*Rabbi Yose omer eino chayav ela achas* — Rabbi Yosi holds that if all the limbs belong to the same animal, there is only one *chiyuv*, regardless of how many times one placed pieces on the fire. The Gemara will debate the precise contours of this position in detail.

## Stringencies…

### Source: Ish Ish

The Gemara returns to ground the differential treatment of slaughter and offering in the text of the Torah. The prohibition of *shchutei chutz* employs the phrase *ish ish* — *ish ish mibit Yisrael asher yishchat shor o kesev o ez* — and concludes *v'nichrat ha'ish hahu*. The prohibition of *ha'alas chutz* similarly uses *ish ish* — *ish ish asher ya'aleh olah o zavach* — and concludes *el petach ohel mo'ed v'nichrat ha'ish hahu*. Both verses thus contain both *ish ish* and *ha'ish hahu*, but the Gemara derives different things from each phrase in each context.

---

## Stringencies……

### Partnership & Divine

By *ha'alas chutz*, *ish ish* is read to mean that liability extends even to cases of partnership — two people together placing a limb on the fire are both *chayav*. This is in contrast to slaughter, where partnership exempts. The derivation of the rule for offering comes precisely from the *ish ish* of that *parasha*.

---

## Partnership Offering

### Kal VaChomer

The Gemara constructs a *kal vachomer* to challenge this derivation. If by *shchita* — where one who slaughters for a mundane person is nonetheless *chayav* — two people in partnership are *patur*, then by *ha'alah* — where one who offers for a mundane person is *patur* — surely two people in partnership should also be *patur*. The *kal vachomer* argues that *ha'alah* should be *more* lenient than *shchita* in every respect, and since slaughter does not generate liability for partnership, offering certainly should not.

### Ish Ish Split

The response is that *ish ish* in each *parasha* does different work. By *shchita*, *ish ish* teaches that one is *chayav* even if one slaughters the animal for another person — even for a mundane, non-sacrificial purpose. By *ha'alah*, *ish ish* teaches that one is *chayav* even in the context of partnership — two people together can both be liable. The two usages of *ish ish* are divided between the two contexts, each bearing a different *drasha*. The obvious question follows: why not apply *ish ish* by *shchita* to mean partnership as well? Because *ha'ish hahu* — the verse concludes with "that man" — which excludes the case of partnership. Only an individual is *chayav* by *shchita*.

### Hahu Exclusion

The Gemara asks: *ha'ish hahu* also appears by *ha'alah*, so it should likewise exclude partnership there. The response is that by *ha'alah*, *ha'ish hahu* is needed to exclude the case of *ones* — one who is compelled to perform the act — or one who acted under duress or error. The principle *ones rachmana patrei* is operative, and *hahu* here signals that one must have acted with independent volition to be *chayav*. Tosafot notes that this *drasha* of *hahu* might seem superfluous since *ones* is always exempt regardless, and suggests that the *hahu* more precisely excludes the case of one who acted in the mistaken belief that the act was permitted.

By *shchita*, a second *hahu* is available — *dam yeichasheiv la'ish hahu* appears earlier in that *parasha* — and this verse carries the burden of excluding *ones* and related cases. Thus the single *hahu* at the end of the *shchita* verse is freed to exclude partnership, while by *ha'alah* the only *hahu* must exclude *ones*, leaving *ish ish* to include partnership. This is the mechanism by which the Gemara justifies the asymmetric application.

Rabbi Yosi disagrees with the conclusion that *ish ish* by *ha'alah* includes partnership, maintaining that *hahu* there likewise excludes partnership. He derives the liability for slaughtering on behalf of another person not from *ish ish* but from *dam yeichasheiv la'ish hahu dam shafach* — even if the blood was shed for the sake of another person, liability attaches. Rabbi Shimon counters that without deriving partnership-liability from *ish ish* by *ha'alah*, that phrase becomes mere idiomatic expression — *dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam* — with no independent halachic force, a position Rabbi Shimon is unwilling to accept.

The further question *v'ela l'Hashem lamah li* — why does the verse require that the act be directed toward God if one is *chayav* even for mundane slaughter — receives the answer: *l'hotzi sa'ir mishtale'ach* — to exclude the *sa'ir mishtale'ach*, the scapegoat of *Yom Kippur*, which was never designated as an offering *l'Hashem* in the usual sense. If one slaughters the *sa'ir mishtale'ach* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, one is *patur*, since that animal was never in the category of being brought before God.

## Multiple Offerings…

### Resh Lakish

The Gemara returns to the debate of Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yosi regarding multiple offerings, now working through the precise interpretation of their positions. Resh Lakish reads the debate as concerning multiple whole limbs placed on the fire in succession — for instance, the individual legs of an animal offered one by one, with pauses between each.

### Whole Animal

On one reading, the underlying principle is *al hashalem hu chayav v'eino chayav al hachaser* — liability attaches only when one is offering from a whole animal. On this interpretation, one is *chayav* only when the entire animal was intact at the outset; once a portion has been offered, the remaining animal is *chaser* — incomplete — and subsequent acts of placing limbs on the fire generate no further liability. This would mean that liability obtains only for the first act of offering from the whole animal.

### Whole Limb

Tosafot reads the principle differently. *Al hashalem* refers not to the whole animal but to the whole limb — one is *chayav* when offering from a complete limb, and *patur* when the limb is already incomplete. On this reading, each whole limb offered in sequence could in principle generate its own *chiyuv* — since each limb is itself *shalem* at the moment it is placed on the fire. The debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yosi then concerns whether the animal, once some of it has been offered, remains *shalem* enough that subsequent whole limbs from that animal still generate liability, or whether only one *chiyuv* is incurred for the entire animal.

### One Limb Split

Rabbi Yochanan reads the debate more narrowly: it concerns not multiple limbs but a single limb divided in two, where one half was placed on the fire and then the second half was placed subsequently. This is distinct from Resh Lakish's reading, which concerned multiple whole limbs.

### Inside vs. Outside

The context of slaughter matters here. If the animal was slaughtered *inside* the *Beis HaMikdash* and the limbs were then placed on a fire *outside*, even a partial limb would have been fit to go on the *mizbeach* inside. Since *muktarei p'nim* — pieces designated for the internal *mizbeach* — are placed there even when not whole, a partial limb qualifies as *raui*. If the animal was slaughtered *outside*, however, the standard is more demanding, and a whole limb is required.

### Ulla's Reading

Ulla states that on the matter of *muktarei p'nim* — animals slaughtered inside — everyone agrees one is *chayav* even for a partial limb placed outside, precisely because inside the *Beis HaMikdash* a partial limb goes on the *mizbeach*. The debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yosi, according to Ulla, concerns only *muktarei bachutz* — animals slaughtered outside — where one opinion holds *patur* on a partial limb and the other holds *chayav*.

### Father of Shmuel

The father of Shmuel challenges Ulla's first position. He does so with a remarkable turn of phrase: *keman madlinan pekuin legabei mizbeach* — according to whose ruling do we restore to the *mizbeach* items that have fallen off? This question presupposes that there is a current practice of returning fallen pieces to the altar, and it asks which Tannaitic opinion such a practice reflects. The implication is that this practice reflects the view that even a partial limb goes back on the *mizbeach* — and that this view is not universally accepted. If Rabbi Yosi's position is understood as denying liability for a partial limb even in the case of *muktarei p'nim*, then the practice of replacing fallen pieces contradicts Rabbi Yosi and follows only Rabbi Shimon. The father of Shmuel thus understands the debate as concerning *muktarei p'nim* — the inside-slaughter case — rather than Ulla's reading, which placed the debate in the outside-slaughter context.

## Mizbeach Requirement

### Rabbi Yosi

The *mishnah* records a further debate: does the act of *ha'alas chutz* require that the offering be placed on a constructed altar, or does any elevated surface suffice? *V'eino chayav ad she'ya'aleh l'rosh hamizbeach* — Rabbi Yosi holds that one is not *chayav* unless one places the offering atop a *mizbeach*, a constructed altar.

Rav Huna identifies the source: *vayiven Noach mizbeach la'Hashem* — when Noach brought offerings outside any formal sanctuary, the Torah records that he *built* a *mizbeach*. The implication is that a constructed altar is required for an act of offering to be recognized as such. The further implication — and this is the conceptually fascinating move — is that one can infer from what was required when *bamot* were permissible what would generate *chiyuv* when *bamot* are forbidden. Whatever was always required for an acceptable offering to God — even in the era of permitted *bamot* — remains necessary for liability to attach in the era of forbidden *bamot*. If a rock was never acceptable when *bamot* were permitted, placing an offering on a rock is never considered a sacrificial act, and just as *ha'alah* to an idol does not generate liability for *ha'alas chutz*, neither does placing meat on a surface that was never recognized as an altar.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the source for Rabbi Shimon's opposing position: *vayikach Manoach et gdi ha-izim v'et haminchah vaya'al al hatzur la'Hashem* — Manoach placed his offering directly on a rock, and God accepted it. This demonstrates that a rock or stone surface can constitute an acceptable act of offering, without a constructed *mizbeach*.

Rabbi Yosi responds to this *rai'ya*: *hahu govha b'alma* — the word *vayiven* by Noach means he simply built up a height, a raised surface, not a formally constructed altar with halachic specifications. Rashi explains this as follows: Noach built it for convenience and as the common practice of the time, not because construction was ritually mandated. It is therefore no proof that a *mizbeach* is required.

Rabbi Shimon, in turn, responds to the case of Manoach: *hora'as sha'ah haysah* — that was a unique, exceptional dispensation not representative of the general rule.

### When Bamot Permitted

The Gemara then offers an alternative grounding for Rabbi Shimon's position based on a *drasha*: the verse *v'zarak ha-kohen et ha-dam al mizbeach Hashem petach ohel mo'ed* associates the requirement of a *mizbeach* specifically with *petach ohel mo'ed*, the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Only where there is an *ohel mo'ed* is a *mizbeach* mandated. When there is no *Mikdash*, and one is operating under the rules of a *bamah*, no *mizbeach* is required.

### Inference Logic

The logical structure of this argument is elegant: the conditions required for a valid *bamah* when *bamot* were permitted define what constitutes a meaningful sacrificial act. If no *mizbeach* was required for a private *bamah*, then offering on a rock was always a valid sacrificial act during that era.

### Two-Step Logic

The Gemara then addresses an apparent grammatical awkwardness in the *mishnah*'s formulation. The *mishnah* says *he'ela al ha-sela o al ha-even chayav* — "therefore one who places it on a rock or stone is *chayav*." But the preceding reasoning established what was *permitted* during the era of *bamot*, not what generates *chiyuv* when *bamot* are forbidden. The Gemara explains that the argument is two-step: since one fulfills one's obligation with a rock during the era of permitted *bamot*, therefore during the era of forbidden *bamot*, one is *chayav* for doing the same thing. The act on a rock was always a genuine sacrificial act — never a non-act — and therefore always falls within the prohibition of *ha'alas chutz*.

## Altar Components

### What Defines

Having established that Rabbi Shimon requires no *mizbeach* and Rabbi Yosi does require one, the Gemara raises a further question posed by Rabbi Yosef bar Chanina: if a *mizbeach* is indeed required, does it need to possess all the formal components of the Temple *mizbeach*? Must it have *keren* — horns at its corners — *kevesh* — a ramp — *yesod* — a base — and *ribu'a* — a square shape?

### Large vs. Private

The Gemara cites a *braisa* in response: *keren v'kevesh v'ribu'a v'yesod me'akvin b'vamah gedolah* — these components are required for a *bamah gedolah*, a large communal *bamah* such as those that functioned at Nov and Givon during periods when no *Beis HaMikdash* stood. *V'ein me'akvin b'vamah ketanah* — but they are not required for a private *bamah*, the kind maintained by individuals.

### Private Bamot

Since a private *bamah* does not require these components, it follows that one need not construct an altar with horns, ramp, base, and square dimensions in order to generate *chiyuv* under *shchutei chutz* and *ha'alas chutz*. The standard for liability tracks the standard for private *bamot*, and that standard requires only a basic elevated surface — not the full architectural specifications of the great communal altars.
