# Sanhedrin 29b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Atem Edai Requirement

### Awareness of Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ, ״נְשִׂיאִים וְרוּחַ וְגֶשֶׁם אָיִן אִישׁ מִתְהַלֵּל בְּמַתַּת שָׁקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the judges intimidate the witnesses. The Gemara asks: **What do we say to them? Rav Yehuda says** that **this** is what **we say to them:** It is stated: **"As clouds and wind without rain, so is he who boasts himself of a false gift"** (Proverbs 25:14). In other words, there will be no rain and no blessing from your deeds if you lie.

The Gemara opens on 29b with a continuation of the discussion concerning the requirement of *atem edai* — the formal designation of witnesses — as a prerequisite for a binding admission. The background to this discussion involves a case where a borrower was unaware that witnesses were present when he made an admission, and those witnesses were subsequently produced in court. The Gemara had already established that such a person is not considered *huchzak kafran* — not deemed a proven liar for having denied the debt — since, absent any awareness that witnesses were present, a person does not treat casual admissions as serious or memorable. The related point from the Yerushalmi was that in such a case, the debtor would need to actively declare *meshahta ani bach* — "I was only joking" — in order to escape liability; if he fails to say so explicitly, we do not supply that argument on his behalf, though the Yerushalmi was somewhat more willing to do so.

### Canopy Bed Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאַכְמֵין לֵיהּ עֵדִים לְחַבְרֵיהּ בְּכִילְּתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לִי בִּידָךְ״. אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֵן״. אָמַר: ״עֵירֵי וְשָׁכְבֵי לֶיהֱווֹ עֲלָךְ סָהֲדֵי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָא״. אֲמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״לָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who hid witnesses in** the **canopy** above **his** bed **to** hear the statement of **another.** That certain man **said to him: I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession.** The latter **said to him: Yes.** The claimant then **said: Let those awake and those asleep bear witness about you,** hoping to induce him to agree to this testimony, as the respondent might assume that everyone was asleep. The latter **said to him: No.** When the matter came to court **Rav Kahana said:** Since **he said no to him,** his admission did not render him liable.

The Gemara now advances to a new case. A person concealed witnesses against his fellow inside a canopy bed — hidden behind its curtains. The concealing party then said to the other: "What do you owe me?" The respondent confirmed the debt, saying yes. At that point, the questioner asked: "Are you prepared that all those living and asleep should serve as witnesses against you?" — framing the question so as to include the hidden witnesses without revealing their presence. The respondent replied: no, he was not prepared for anyone to serve as witnesses.

Rav Kahana rules that even though the witnesses heard the admission, since the admitting party was unaware of their presence and explicitly declined to have anyone serve as witnesses, this does not constitute a valid admission. Rather than inferring that the denial only demonstrates an attempt to conceal an actual debt, the Gemara returns to the formal principle: unless a person explicitly accepts the witnesses as such — whether by saying *atem edai* or by awareness and acceptance of their presence — the admission carries no legal force.

### Graveyard Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאַכְמֵין עֵדִים בְּקִיבְרָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מָנֶה לִי בִּידָךְ״. אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֵן״. חַיֵּי וּמִיתֵי לֶיהֱווֹ עֲלָךְ סָהֲדִי, אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָא״. אֲמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן [בֶּן לָקִישׁ]: הָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״לָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain** man **who hid witnesses in a grave to** hear the statement of **another.** That certain man **said to him: I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession.** The latter **said to him: Yes.** The claimant then **said: Let the living and the dead bear witness about you.** The respondent **said to him: No. Rabbi Shimon said:** Since **he said no to him,** his admission did not render him liable.

A parallel case is presented: someone concealed witnesses inside a grave — presumably an open grave or a mausoleum — and then asked his fellow to admit a debt in that setting. The fellow admitted, and the questioner then asked: "Are you prepared that the living and the dead should be witnesses against you?" — an absurd formulation designed to trick the admitting party into accepting the concealed witnesses as testimony. The admitting party, thinking this a jest about dead witnesses, responded: no.

Rav Shimon rules that since the person said no, this does not constitute *atem edai*. The more striking implication, however, runs in the opposite direction. Had the admitting party said yes — even without seeing any witnesses, even treating the question as idle talk — that affirmative response would have constituted valid *atem edai*, and the hidden witnesses in the grave would have been duly designated. This is a significant *chiddush*: the formal requirement of *atem edai* operates independently of the subjective seriousness with which the admitting party treats the designation. Once created, the formal act of witness designation is binding even if the designating party assumed it was meaningless. One might then ask whether the admitting party could later claim *meshahta ani bach* — that he was obviously not serious. The answer appears to be no. The Gemara's reasoning seems to be that once the formal requirement of *atem edai* is established as the operative standard, the law deals in formal categories rather than contextual readings of intent. Having erected a demanding formal threshold, the law correspondingly allows that threshold to be satisfied in formal rather than intuitive terms.

### Formal Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מֵהָא, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״אַתֶּם עֵדַיי״, לָא שְׁנָא כִּי אָמַר לֹוֶה, וְלָא שְׁנָא כִּי אָמַר מַלְוֶה וְשָׁתֵיק לֹוֶה. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר לֹוֶה ״לָא״, אֲבָל אִי שָׁתֵיק – הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said, and some say Rav Pappa** said it: **Conclude from it** that with regard to **that which Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** that one **needs to say** to the witnesses of the admission: **You are my witnesses,** there **is no difference whether the debtor said** it, **and** there **is no difference whether the creditor said** it **and the debtor remained silent.** The inference is that **the reason** the Sages deemed the purported debtor exempt in the above cases **is that the debtor** explicitly **said no,** i.e., that he does not accept the witnesses, **but had he remained silent** his admission would have **indeed** rendered him liable.

Rav Yeivi — and some attribute it to Rav Papa — draws the following inference from these cases: the requirement articulated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, that one must say *atem edai*, applies equally whether it is the borrower who initiates — approaching the witnesses and designating them himself — or whether it is the lender who initiates by asking: "Are you willing for Michael and David to serve as witnesses?" and the borrower responds affirmatively. In either formulation, the formal requirement of *atem edai* is satisfied.

### Silence as Consent

The Gemara extends this principle one step further: not only does an affirmative response suffice when the lender initiates the designation, but even silence — a failure to respond either way — constitutes acceptance. The Gemara makes this explicit: the only reason the graveyard case failed was that the admitting party actively said no. Had the party simply remained silent, that silence would have operated as *shtika ke-hoda'ah* — silence as admission — and would have constituted a valid *atem edai*.

This represents a notable internal tension within the Bavli's treatment of witness designation. On one hand, the Bavli imposes a stringent formal demand — *atem edai* must be established, more so than the Yerushalmi requires. On the other hand, having established that demanding standard, the Bavli simultaneously lowers the threshold for what satisfies it: a hidden witness scenario, a fanciful formulation about the living and the dead, an ambiguous designation initiated entirely by the opposing party, and even sheer silence — all suffice. The formalism cuts in both directions.

## Deathbed Admissions

### Kav Rashu Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָרוּ לֵיהּ ״קַב רָשׁוּ״, אֲמַר: מַאן מַסֵּיק בִּי אֶלָּא פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי? אֲתוֹ, תַּבְעוּהּו לְדִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **whom** people **called: A *kav* of debts,** as everyone claimed debts from him, who once **said: Who can claim** debts **from me, other than so-and-so and so-and-so?** I do not owe money to as many as assumed. Those whom he admitted that he owed **came** and **took him to judgment before Rav Naḥman** to claim what he owed them, and he responded in court that his admittance was in jest.

The Gemara introduces the case of a person known by the nickname *kav rashu* — literally a measure of wickedness, but Rashi explains it as someone against whom there exists a bundle of promissory notes, a person so thoroughly in debt that this became his very identity. When challenged about his many debts, this person responded dismissively: "Who has ever lent me money? No one — except so-and-so and so-and-so." Those named creditors then came before Rav Nachman and argued that even his own mouth had confirmed the debt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: A person is prone** to make false statements **so as not to make himself** appear **sated,** i.e., it is possible that he might say falsely in public that he owes money in order that he should not be considered wealthy. Therefore, as long as he has not made a proper admission that he owes a specific sum to a specific individual, he is not liable.

Rav Nachman rejected the claim on the basis of the principle *adam asui she-lo le-hasbiya et atzmo* — a person naturally acts so as not to make himself appear wealthy. This requires some explanation, because on its face the principle seems ill-fitted to a case where the person was trying to appear poor, not wealthy. The deeper logic, however, appears to be that the principle underlies the very requirement of *atem edai*: even a contextually plausible-seeming admission, made without formal witness designation, carries no legal weight, because we can always construct a reason why someone might have said such a thing without intending it as a binding acknowledgment. Here, for instance, the person may have named specific creditors precisely to make himself look burdened with debt rather than flush with assets. The admission, lacking *atem edai*, is thus not binding.

### Miser on Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲווֹ קָרוּ לֵיהּ ״עַכְבְּרָא דְּשָׁכֵיב אַדִּינָרֵי״. כִּי קָא שָׁכֵיב, אֲמַר: ״פְּלָנְיָא וּפְלָנְיָא מַסְּקוּ בִּי זוּזֵי״. בָּתַר דִּשְׁכֵיב, אֲתוֹ תַּבְעִינְהוּ לְיוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **whom** people **called: A mouse that lies on dinars,** as he was a miser who did not benefit from his money, like a mouse that sits and safeguards gold dinars. This man **said on his deathbed: So-and-so and so-and-so claim dinars from me. After he died,** they **came** and **sued the heirs.**

A second case: a person whose nickname was *achbara de-shachiv a-dinarei* — the rat who sleeps on his gold coins, a miser who hoards wealth. When this person was dying, he declared that Reuven and Shimon had lent him money and he owed them repayment. Reuven and Shimon then came to collect — but after his death, from the heirs. The question was whether a deathbed admission should be treated differently from an ordinary admission.

### Deathbed Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּי אָמְרִינַן ״אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ״, הָנֵי מִילֵּי מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל לְאַחַר מִיתָה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They came** to judgment **before Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei. He said to them: When we say a person is prone** to make false statements **so as not to make himself** appear **sated, this statement** applies to an admission **during his lifetime. But after death,** i.e., on his deathbed, this does **not** apply; rather, we presume he told the truth.

The case came before Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yosi. He ruled in favor of the creditors: the principle of *adam asui she-lo le-hasbiya et atzmo* — that a person avoids making himself look wealthy — applies only to the living, *hani mili mechayim*. A dying person, *achar misah*, has no further interest in appearances and will speak truthfully. Therefore, even without *atem edai*, a deathbed admission is accepted, and the creditors may collect from the heirs. Half the debt was paid.

### Living vs. Deathbed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּרַעוּ פַּלְגָא, תַּבְעִינְהוּ לְדִינָא לְאִידַּךְ פַּלְגָא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ, כָּךְ אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת בָּנָיו. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נֵיזִיל וְנַיהְדַּר? אֲמַר לְהוּ: כְּבָר הוֹרָה זָקֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The heirs **paid half** the amount of the claim. The claimants then **sued them for the other half. They came** to judgment **before Rabbi Ḥiyya.** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to them: Just as a person is prone** to make false statements **so as not to make himself** appear **sated, so too, a person is prone** to make false statements **so as not to make his sons** appear **sated.** One might claim falsely on his deathbed that he owes money so that his children will not be considered wealthy. Therefore, there is no room for a claim against the heirs. The heirs then **said to him:** If so, **let us go and overturn** the former verdict, and retrieve the amount we paid the claimants. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to them: The elder,** i.e., Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **has already issued a ruling** in this case, and it is not in my power to overturn his ruling.

When the creditors sought to collect the remaining half, Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yosi was unavailable and they appeared before Rabbi Chiya, who explicitly disagreed with the prior ruling. Just as a person avoids making himself look wealthy, said Rabbi Chiya, so too a person avoids making his children look wealthy — *adam asui she-lo le-hasbiya banav*. A dying man may say that he owes debts precisely to prevent the world from knowing how much his heirs are about to inherit, lest creditors — real or fabricated — descend upon them. Therefore, even a deathbed admission, absent *atem edai*, is not binding on the orphans.

The creditors themselves, acting with integrity, asked whether they should return the half already paid. Rabbi Chiya responded: *kvar hora zaken* — a sage has already ruled. The ruling of Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yosi on the first half stands, since it was a legitimate *psak* and is not to be reversed retroactively. As for the second half, however, Rabbi Chiya's ruling prevails: the deathbed admission without *atem edai* is insufficient, and the heirs need not pay.

## Writing Documents

### Two Witnesses Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹדָה בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם, וְקָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ – כּוֹתְבִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** If one **made an admission in the presence of two** witnesses that he owes money **and they performed** a formal **act of acquisition with him** to verify the admission, the witnesses **can write** a record of the admission in a document and sign it, thereby granting the creditor more rights. **But if** they did **not** perform a formal act of acquisition **they may not write** a record of the admission in a document, as the debtor presumably wants the loan to retain its status as a loan by oral agreement, which grants fewer rights to the creditor than a loan with a promissory note.

The Gemara turns to the question of when witnesses who heard an admission are authorized to memorialize it in a *shtar*. The advantage of a *shtar* over an oral admission is considerable: the creditor need not produce the witnesses in person at trial, and the debtor cannot easily claim subsequent repayment while the creditor holds the document. However, a *shtar* can only be written with the authority of the obligated party.

The Gemara rules: if the debtor admitted in front of two witnesses *and* performed a *ma'aseh kinyan* — a formal symbolic act of acquisition — *kosevin*, the witnesses may write a *shtar*. Without the *kinyan*, they may not.

### Three Without Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְלֹא קָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ. רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר: כּוֹתְבִין, וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וְחַשׁ לַהּ רַב לְהָא דְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** If he made an admission **in the presence of three** witnesses **and they did not perform** a formal **act of acquisition with him, Rav Ami says: They can write** a record of the admission in a document. Since the admission was made in the presence of three individuals, who are considered a court, it is permitted for a court to write its verdicts. **And Rav Asi says: They may not write** it in a document; perhaps the debtor intended for them to serve only as witnesses, not as a court. **There was an incident** in which a person admitted his debt before three witnesses, **and Rav was concerned for this** opinion **of Rav Asi,** and ruled that they may not write a record of the admission.

The question then arises regarding admission before three individuals, without any *kinyan*. Three constitutes a *beis din*, and when a court issues a judgment it writes a *shtar* on its own authority without requiring the losing party's consent. If this admission occurred before something resembling a court, perhaps the three may write a *shtar* even without a *kinyan*.

### Rav Ami vs Rav Assi

On this question Rav Ami and Rav Assi dispute. Rav Ami holds *kosevin* — the three may write a *shtar* even without a *kinyan*. Rav Assi holds *ein kosevin* — they may not. When a case arose in practice, Rav deferred to the position of Rav Assi and did not permit writing the *shtar*.

### Rav Adda bar Ahava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָא אוֹדִיתָא, זִימְנִין כָּתְבִינַן וְזִימְנִין לָא כָּתְבִינַן. כְּנִיפִי וְיָתְבִי – לָא כָּתְבִינַן, כַּנְפִינְהוּ אִיהוּ – כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** With regard to **this** document of **admission, sometimes we write** it **and sometimes we do not write** it. If the three witnesses were **gathered and sitting** when the debtor made his admission before them, **we do not write** it, as perhaps he did not intend for them to serve as a court. But if the debtor **gathered them, we write** the document of admission, as the fact that he took care to bring three people and not just two proves that he intended for them to serve as a court.

Rav Adda bar Ahava, however, introduces a nuance: *zimnin kasvinan ve-zimnin lo kasvinan* — sometimes they write and sometimes they do not, depending on the circumstances. The distinction is as follows. If the three happened to be sitting together and the admission occurred in their presence without any prior arrangement, they lack the status of a *beis din* and may not write a *shtar*. But if the parties specifically convened the three — whether creditor or debtor who gathered them for the purpose of making or receiving the admission — then they function as a *beis din*, and the *shtar* may be written without a *kinyan*.

It is worth noting that a related concept, *odaysa*, bears mention here. In halakhic practice, an *odaysa* — a formal admission — can function as a vehicle for creating new legal realities in monetary matters: a person may admit before witnesses to a transaction that never occurred, and that admission itself effectuates the transfer. This broader concept underlies the Gemara's careful attention to the conditions under which a *shtar* of admission may be written.

### Rava's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ כַּנְפִינְהוּ אִיהוּ – לָא כָּתְבִינַן, עַד דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: ״הֱווֹ עֲלַי דַּיָּינֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Even** if **he gathered them we do not write** it, **unless he says to them: Be my judges.**

Rava adds a further condition: even if the three were deliberately convened, the *shtar* may not be written unless the admitting party explicitly says *havu alai dayanei* — "serve as my judges." Without that verbal designation of the three as *dayanim*, their convening alone is insufficient.

### Mar Bar Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״הֱווֹ עֲלַי דַּיָּינֵי״, לָא כָּתְבִינַן עַד דְּקָבְעִי דּוּכְתָּא, וְשָׁלְחִי, וּמַזְמְנִי לֵיהּ לְבֵי דִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi says: Even** if **he says: Be my judges, we do not write** it **unless they establish a place** for judgment **and send** messengers **and summon him to court.** Unless the entire procedure of an admission in court is implemented, the debtor may not have intended that they serve as a court with regard to this matter.

Mar bar Rav Ashi goes furthest of all: even explicit verbal designation as judges is not enough. A *shtar* may only be written by an actual established court — one that has a fixed location, hears cases on an ongoing basis, and formally summons parties through a written notice. Only such an institution has the standing of a *beis din* capable of writing a *shtar* on the basis of an admission. In his view, the entire framework of treating convened individuals as a quasi-court is a legal fiction that he declines to accept. If a genuine court is hearing the case, let the court write its judgment; if there is no genuine court, then a *kinyan* is required, and without it no *shtar* may be written.

## Chattels vs Land

### Chattels Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹדָה בְּמִטַּלְטְלֵי, וְקָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ – כּוֹתְבִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **he made an admission with regard to movable property** that he owes, **and** the witnesses **performed** a formal **act of acquisition with him, they can write** a record of the admission in a document; **but if** they did **not** perform a formal act of acquisition **they may not write** a record of the admission.

The Gemara now articulates a further distinction between different types of subject matter. Regarding movable property — *metaltlei* — the rule is as stated: admission before two witnesses with a *kinyan* allows writing a *shtar*; without a *kinyan*, it does not.

### Land No Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמְקַרְקְעֵי, וְלֹא קָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ, מַאי? אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין. מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: כּוֹתְבִין. וְהִלְכְתָא: כּוֹתְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** But if he made an admission **with regard to land, and they did not perform** a formal **act of acquisition with him, what** is the *halakha*? Is it considered as though an act of acquisition was performed, as land is always available for collection? **Ameimar says: They may not write** it. **Mar Zutra says: They may write** it. **And the *halakha*** is that **they may write** it.

Land — *karka* — presents a different case. When a debtor admits that a particular piece of land belongs to the creditor, the situation differs from a monetary debt. A monetary debt in movables must still be extracted through legal process; there is a gap between the admission and the actual transfer of value. Land, by contrast, simply sits there. Once the debtor admits it belongs to the creditor, there is nothing further to extract — *lo mechasar guvaina*. Rashi formulates the distinction precisely: with movables and a loan, it is still a debt, it still requires delivery, and there is a meaningful difference between an oral debt and a debt documented by a *shtar* — the *shtar* gives the creditor enhanced enforcement power over encumbered properties, which is something the debtor has reason to resist granting. But with land, writing the *shtar* is merely *gilui milta* — revealing and memorializing what is already an established fact. The question, then, is whether this distinction justifies writing a *shtar* regarding land without any *kinyan*.

### Amemar vs Zutra

Amemar holds *ein kosevin* — even for land without a *kinyan*, no *shtar* may be written. Mar Zutra holds *kosevin* — it may be written. The Gemara rules: *ve-hilchasa kosevin* — the *halacha* follows Mar Zutra, and a *shtar* is written for land even without a *kinyan*, because the admission alone establishes ownership of something that needs no further extraction.

### Concrete Chattels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיקְּלַע לְדִמְהַרְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב דִּימִי בַּר רַב הוּנָא מִדִּמְהַרְיָא לְרָבִינָא: מִטַּלְטְלִי וְאִיתַנְהוּ בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּמְקַרְקְעֵי דָּמוּ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּמְחַסְּרִי גּוּבְיָינָא, לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina happened** to come **to Damharya. Rav Dimi bar Rav Huna of Damharya said to Ravina:** If one makes an admission that he owes **movable property that is extant,** i.e., it is available to be taken immediately, **what** is the *halakha*? Should it be considered like land, as it is similarly available for collection, and therefore the witnesses can write a record of the admission? Ravina **said to him: It is** considered **like land. Rav Ashi says: Since it** still **lacks collection,** i.e., it has not been physically transferred from one party to the other, it is not considered like land, and the witnesses **may not** write a record of the admission unless the debtor asks them to write it.

Rav Dimi bar Rav Huna of Damarya raised a question before Ravina: the foregoing analysis distinguishes land from movables on the basis of the need for extraction. But what of concrete, physical chattels that are present and identifiable — *metaltlei ve-isnahu b'einaihu*, movables that are right there before one's eyes? If Gloria admits that the car in her driveway is mine, that is not an abstract debt; it is a specific, identifiable object, not requiring the legal processing that a monetary debt requires. Perhaps such concrete movables should be treated like land?

### Rav Ashi Disagrees

Rav Ashi disagrees. Even in the case of a specific, present chattel, *keivan de-mechassrei guvaina* — since it still requires extraction, it is not like land. To obtain the car, I must compel Gloria to deliver it; I cannot simply walk up and take possession as I can with land once the admission is made. Therefore, concrete chattels remain categorized with *metaltlei* for this purpose, and a *shtar* may not be written without explicit authorization — that is, without a *kinyan*.
