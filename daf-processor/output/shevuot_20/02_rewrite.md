# Shevuot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Oaths That Are Four

### Opening

We begin Daf 20 by moving from the extended discussion of *tum'ah* and entry into the *Beit HaMikdash* into the subject that stands at the heart of our *masechta*: *shevuot*, oaths. The immediate focus is the question of when one incurs liability to bring a *korban* — specifically the *korban oleh v'yored* — upon violating an oath. In time the tractate will address oaths in a judicial context, the reason this material appears in *Seder Nezikin*, but we begin here with the foundational structure.

### Mishna Structure

The *Mishna* opens with the familiar formula: *shevuot shtayim she-hein arba* — two oaths that are four. The basis is the verse, *nefesh ki tishava levate bisfatayim lehara o le-heitiv* — a person who swears an utterance with his lips to do harm or to do good (Vayikra 5:4). "Doing harm" is understood to refer to swearing one will *not* eat something; "doing good" refers to swearing that one *will* eat. These are the first two. The "four" includes two further cases involving the past: swearing that one did eat something, or that one did not. Violating any of these — whether the underlying act was performed wittingly or unwittingly — generates liability for a *korban*.

### Rabbi Akiva

The *Mishna* then records the view of Rabbi Akiva: *shevuah shelo ochal ve-achal kol shehu chayav* — if one swears not to eat and eats even the smallest amount, he is liable. The Sages challenge him: *heichan matzinu be-ochel be-kol shehu she-hu chayav*? Where do we ever find that eating the smallest amount incurs liability? Ordinarily, whether for a *korban* or for *malkot*, the minimum threshold is a *kezayit*. Rabbi Akiva replies: *u-khe-heichan matzinu be-medaber mevi korban*? Where do we ever find that someone brings a *korban* merely for a speech act? And yet here, in the case of oaths, that is precisely the rule. If one swore to eat something and then did not eat — a purely passive failure — the liability stems entirely from the words spoken. Or in the case of a false oath about the past, again there is no action at all; the *korban* is incurred solely on account of the speech. The lesson, says Rabbi Akiva, is that when it comes to *shevuot*, the operative category is *medaber* — the utterance itself.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi explains the connection between Rabbi Akiva's two points as follows. Since the liability of an oath is rooted entirely in one's words, we interpret the scope of those words by what a person actually meant when speaking. Unlike a Torah prohibition, where the minimum quantity is fixed externally — a *kezayit*, for example, is defined by the Torah — an oath has no externally determined threshold. The person himself sets the terms through his language. Therefore, when someone says "I will not eat," without specifying an amount, we assume he meant even the smallest quantity. The prohibition is not a category of forbidden food with a fixed *shiur*; it is a violation of one's own verbal commitment, and that commitment is interpreted according to its natural scope.

## Context in Oaths

### Linguistic Ambiguity

The *Gemara* opens with an inference from the *Mishna*. If the *Mishna* speaks of a *shevuah she-ochel* — an oath that one will eat — this implies that the word *ochel* straightforwardly means "I will eat." But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this from a *Baraita*: the phrase *shevuah she-ochal lach* — an oath that I will eat of yours — is treated in that source as if it means the opposite, namely that one is binding oneself not to eat. How can the same words bear such different meanings?

### Abaye: Context Matters

Abaye resolves the tension: *le'olam de-achilna lach* — yes, *ochal* ordinarily means "I will eat." But the apparent contradiction is not a contradiction at all, because *lo kashya* — there is a distinction. *Kan de-misarvin bo le-echol* — in the case where others are pressuring the person to eat; *kan de-ein misarvin bo le-echol* — and in the case where no such pressure exists. When someone is being urged to eat and keeps refusing — "come on, just a little" — and finally says *shevuah she-ochal lach*, the context makes clear that what he means is: I am bound by oath against eating anything of yours. He is pushing back against the pressure, not affirming his intention to eat. In that setting, *she-ochal* functions as *she-lo ochal*.

### Pressure Scenarios

The *Mishna*, Abaye continues, is dealing with *she-ein misarvin bo le-echol* — the case where no one is pressuring him, so the straightforward meaning applies: it is an oath that he will eat. The *Baraita*, by contrast, is addressing *misarvin bo le-echol*, where the pressure context inverts the sense of the words. Abaye captures this dynamic succinctly: the one who keeps saying "no, no" and is pressed repeatedly — *de-ka'amar lo achilna ve-lo achilna* — when he finally says *shevuah she-ochal*, everyone understands he means *shelo ochal*. Language does not operate in a vacuum.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reading of the same *Baraita*. *Tani shevuah she-ee ochal lach* — the text of the *Baraita* should actually be read as *she-ee ochal*, meaning "that I will not eat," not *she-ochal*. In his view, *she-ochal* is a phonetic contraction of *she-ee ochal*, the word *she-ee* being an abbreviated form of negation — a linguistic phenomenon found, for example, in Palestinian Hebrew, where a prefixed *lamed* sometimes contracts the word *lo*. The *Gemara* pushes back: if the *Baraita* explicitly says he won't eat, where is the novelty? Rav Ashi answers: one might have thought the person slipped and said *she-ee ochal* when he meant *she-ochal*. The *Baraita* therefore establishes that if one says *she-ee ochal*, we take him at his word.

The question of whether Rav Ashi is substantively disagreeing with Abaye on whether context determines meaning is worth noting. It seems most likely that Rav Ashi is simply offering an alternative textual reading of the *Baraita* — since the *Baraita* does not supply the context of pressure, it is not fair to assume it — rather than disputing the *halakhic* relevance of context as a matter of principle. And indeed, throughout *Nedarim* and *Shevuot*, the principle of *piv v'libo shavin* — that what matters is not merely the formal words but the underlying intent — makes clear that context is genuinely operative.

## Technical Terms

### Mivta Definition

The *Gemara* now moves to a *Baraita* that examines what linguistic formulations constitute a binding *shevuah*. The starting point is the term *mivta*. The verse states: *ki nefesh ki tishava l'vatei bisfatayim* — when a person swears, *l'vatei*, to enunciate with his lips. The root *bet-tet-alef* gives rise to the technical term *mivta* — "enunciation." When someone says *mivta* I will eat such-and-such, or *mivta* I will not eat such-and-such, the word *mivta* functions as a technical term with the force of the word *shevuah* itself. This is confirmed by an additional verse: *u-ndareha aleha o mifta sfateha asher asra al nafshah* — where the word *mifta* appears entirely on its own, without the word *shevuah*, yet clearly refers to an oath. So *mivta* is a standalone technical term for a *shevuah*.

### Issur as Technical Term

The *Baraita* then introduces a second term: *issar*. This too is derived from a verse: *le'esor issar al nafsho* — to make something forbidden upon himself (Bamidbar 30:3), and *kol neder v'chol shevuat issar* (ibid. 30:14). However, the *Baraita*'s treatment of *issar* is more ambiguous than its treatment of *mivta*, and this ambiguity drives the major dispute of the *sugya*.

### Mifas Concept

Central to understanding this section is the concept of *mifas*, or *hatfasa* — "grabbing" or "association." In the context of *nedarim*, *hatfasa* works as follows: if object A is already forbidden to me by a *neder*, and I say "this object B is like that object A," I have transferred the forbidden status of A onto B. This is called *matfis b'neder* and is uncontroversial; it is the classic mechanism of a *neder* throughout *Masechet Nedarim*. The term *davar hanadur* — a vowed object — is operative here: my power to change the status of an object through speech derives from associating it with something already rendered forbidden through speech.

The conceptual distinction between *nedarim* and *shevuot* is critical. A *neder* operates on an *object* — it changes the status of a thing, analogous to the way one can consecrate something to the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *shevuah*, by contrast, operates on a *person* and an *action* — it is a call upon God to witness one's commitment or the truth of one's statement. The two categories are therefore structurally distinct: the association-logic of *hatfasa* fits naturally into the world of *nedarim*, where status is the currency, but it is not self-evident that the same mechanism applies to *shevuot*.

### Baraita Framework

Having laid this groundwork, the *Baraita* presents the case of *issar* and the question of whether *matfis b'shevuah* — doing a *hatfasa* onto a *shevuah* — has the force of actually uttering a *shevuah*. The *Baraita*'s language at this point generates a dispute between Abaye and Rava, both as to how the *Baraita* should be read and as to the underlying *halakhic* question it addresses. Tosafot's reading of this passage differs from Rashi's, and the analysis below follows Tosafot.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Abaye Position

Abaye reads the *Baraita* as follows. The word *mivta* is a *shevuah* — a standalone technical term. The word *issar* in the Torah, by contrast, does not function as a standalone technical term at all. Rather, it describes a scenario of *matfis b'shevuah* — of associating something with a pre-existing *shevuah*. And the pivotal question the *Baraita* then raises is: when someone does a *hatfasa* onto a *shevuah*, is that *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami* — equivalent to having actually uttered a *shevuah* — or not? If it is equivalent, one is liable; if not, one is exempt.

### Tosafot Explanation

Tosafot's reading is that the technical word *issar* itself is not at issue in this section of the *Baraita*. Rather, the *Baraita* is describing a scenario — when the Torah uses the language *le'esor issar al nafsho*, it is characterizing a case of *hatfasa*, of piggybacking onto a pre-existing *shevuah*. The question the *Baraita* then raises concerns what language is required to make that *hatfasa* operative. Does it suffice to say "this is like that," or must one include the action-language of "I will not eat" in order for the statement to carry the weight of a *shevuah*?

### Apple Illustration

To make this concrete, consider the following scenario. A person takes a *shevuah* — using the word *shevuah* or *mivta* — that he will not eat a particular apple (apple number one). He then wants to extend that commitment to a second apple. If he says "I will not eat apple number two, just as I will not eat apple number one," he has used the action-language of a *shevuah* — "I will not eat" — and linked apple two to apple one. This, according to Abaye, is *matfis b'shevuah* in a manner *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami*, and he would be liable if he eats apple two.

If, however, he says only "apple two is like apple one" — focusing on the objects without the action-language of "I will not eat" — that statement is in the idiom of *neder*, not *shevuah*, and would not bind him as a *shevuah*.

A still more elegant reading of Tosafot produces the following refinement. *Issar* in the Torah describes the case where someone links apple two to apple one — that much is the classic case, and one is certainly liable. But then the *Baraita* moves to a further case: *issar issar* — what if the person says "I will not eat apple three just as I will not eat apple two"? This is a *hatfasa* of a *hatfasa*, two degrees removed from the original *shevuah*. Are you liable?

The answer depends on how one understands the liability in the first case. If you are liable in the first case because the *hatfasa* is *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami* — as if you yourself uttered a *shevuah* about apple two — then apple three would equally be as if you uttered a *shevuah* about it, and you would be liable. But if you are liable in the first case not because it is equivalent to uttering a *shevuah*, but merely because the statement is *connected* to a *shevuah*, then apple three is connected only to something connected to a *shevuah* — two degrees removed — and you would be exempt.

This raises a beautiful conceptual question. What exactly is the nature of a *hatfasa*? Does saying "I won't eat apple two like apple one" function as a technical utterance — so that the act of linking is itself *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami*, the equivalent of having said the word *shevuah*? Or does it merely create a derivative association — linked but not equivalent? The same question arises in a different key with the *Rambam*'s ruling that one who answers *Amen* to another's *brachah* is *kemotzi brachah mipiv dami*: is "Amen" itself the equivalent of having said the *brachah*, or is it merely a form of association through *shome'ah k'oneh*?

For Abaye, the word *issar* in the Torah points to *hatfasa* as the operative mechanism, and *hatfasa b'shevuah* — done with the action-language of "I will not eat" — is *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami*. Liability follows.

### Rava Position

Rava disagrees fundamentally. *Matfis b'shevuah lav kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami* — performing a *hatfasa* onto a *shevuah* does not have the force of uttering a *shevuah*. For Rava, *hatfasa* is a mechanism that belongs to the world of *nedarim*, not *shevuot*. A *shevuah* requires the technical language of a *shevuah* — either the word *shevuah* itself, the word *mivta*, or, Rava adds, the word *issar* — which he regards not as a description of a *hatfasa* but as a genuine third technical term.

On Rava's reading, the *Baraita* is not saying that the word *issar* describes a *hatfasa*. Rather, *issar* is a technical term in its own right — like *mivta* — but with an inherent ambiguity: *hetilo hakatuv bein neder l'shevuah* — the Torah has placed it between a *neder* and a *shevuah*, with the word *neder* appearing two words before it and *shevuah* appearing two words after it in the verse. The result is that *issar* can function as either a *neder* or a *shevuah* depending on how it is used. If one says "*issar* this apple is forbidden to me" — focusing on the status of the object — it is a *neder*. If one says "*issar* I will not eat this apple" — focusing on one's action — it is a *shevuah*. The word is context-sensitive, but in both cases it is a proper technical term, not a description of *hatfasa*.

This position is encapsulated in the formula: *hotzio b'lashon neder neder, b'lashon shevuah shevuah* — if expressed in the language of a *neder*, it is a *neder*; if expressed in the language of a *shevuah*, it is a *shevuah*.

## Issur in Nedarim

### Baraita on Issur

The *Gemara* now turns to a *Baraita* that directly addresses the question of *issar*: *eizehu issar ha'amur b'Torah* — what is the *issar* spoken of in the Torah? The *Baraita*'s answer describes a case of *hatfasa*, which, on the surface, would seem to constitute a direct proof for Abaye's position.

### Wine and Meat Case

The *Baraita* presents the following scenario. Someone has already committed, by *neder*, not to eat meat or drink wine on the anniversary of his father's death, or on the anniversary of some other significant event, or on *Tzom Gedaliah* — the fast of Gedaliah ben Achikam. Now, on a different day, he says: meat and wine are forbidden to me today *just as* they are forbidden on that day. This is a paradigmatic *hatfasa b'neder*: the object (meat, wine) is forbidden on the reference day by virtue of a *neder*, and the speaker is associating the current day's prohibition with that pre-existing vowed status. Shmuel adds the qualification: *vehu she-nadar bo me'oto hayom* — this works only if he had indeed taken a *neder* on the reference day from that day onward. The association must connect to a genuine *davar hanadur*, a pre-existing vowed object.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

This *Baraita* poses a difficulty for Rava. If the *Baraita* introduces itself with *eizehu issar ha'amur b'Torah* — asking what the *issar* of the Torah is — and then describes a case of *hatfasa*, it seems to confirm Abaye's reading that *issar* refers to *hatfasa*, not to a standalone technical term.

Rava's response is to reread the *Baraita*. The *Baraita* should be understood as asking not *eizehu issar ha'amur b'Torah* but rather *eizehu issar neder ha'amur b'Torah* — what is the classic case of a forbidden *neder* spoken of in the Torah? On this reading, the word *issar* is not being defined as *hatfasa*; the *Baraita* is simply illustrating what a classic *neder* looks like. And the answer is: a *neder* classically takes the form of *hatfasa* — associating a new object or occasion with something already forbidden as a *davar hanadur*.

For Rava, then, the *Baraita* is not a proof about the technical term *issar* at all. It is a description of classic *neder* formation, and it is entirely consistent with his view that the technical term *issar* is distinct from *hatfasa* and functions as a proper term for either a *neder* or a *shevuah* depending on context.

## Abaye vs. Rava…

### Neder vs. Shevu'a

The deeper conceptual ground of Rava's position comes into focus here. The reason *hatfasa* is the natural and indeed essential mechanism of a *neder* is that a *neder* derives its force from the speaker's power to change the status of objects through speech — a power that is itself derived from the world of *korbanot* and *hekdesh*. When I say *konam this apple upon me*, I am associating this apple with the category of things sanctified to the *Beit HaMikdash*. My speech operates on the object. The verse *ad she-yidor b'davar hanadur* — which requires that a *neder* connect to something already vowed — reflects precisely this structure: the speech-power to change the status of objects flows from the established category of objects that are already vowed.

A *shevuah*, by contrast, operates not on objects but on persons and actions. It is a calling upon God to witness — either the truth of a factual claim or one's commitment to a future course of action. In this domain, saying "A is like B" — associating one object with another — does not engage the logic of a *shevuah* at all. A *shevuah* requires that I place myself and my actions before God. Therefore Rava holds that *hatfasa b'shevuah* is simply not *kemotzi shevuah mipiv dami* — it does not have the force of actually taking an oath.

Abaye and Rava thus represent two fundamentally different views of whether the mechanism of association, which is intrinsic to *nedarim*, can be transposed into the domain of *shevuot*.

## Issur in Nedarim…

### Davar Hanadur

The principle that a *neder* must connect to a *davar hanadur* — something already forbidden through a *neder* — is not merely a rule for *hatfasa* situations; it reflects the constitutive logic of *nedarim* as a whole. Even when one says simply *konam this apple upon me*, one is implicitly associating the apple with the general category of *korban* — of things rendered forbidden through a speech-act of sanctification. The *davar hanadur* requirement means that the power of one's speech to prohibit derives always from association with an already-established vowed category. This is why *hatfasa b'neder* — saying B is like A, where A is already a *davar hanadur* — is a paradigmatic *neder*: it makes explicit what is implicit in every *neder*.

### Tzom Gedaliah

The *Baraita*'s inclusion of *Tzom Gedaliah* as a reference-day introduces a non-obvious dimension. On first consideration, one might think that *Tzom Gedaliah* cannot serve as a *davar hanadur*: the prohibition against eating on *Tzom Gedaliah* is rabbinic in origin, not the product of a personal *neder*. If I say "this meat is forbidden to me today like it is on *Tzom Gedaliah*," am I connecting to something forbidden by a *neder*, or merely to a rabbinic fast — which is not a *davar hanadur* at all?

The *Gemara* addresses this directly. *Salka datach amina*: one might have thought that since the food would be forbidden on *Tzom Gedaliah* even without any personal *neder*, any personal *neder* one adds on top of the rabbinic prohibition never truly takes effect as a distinct layer of *neder*-prohibition. If the *neder* layer never took hold, then when one says "today is like *Tzom Gedaliah*," one is connecting not to a *davar hanadur* but merely to a *taanis* — and *hatfasa* onto a *taanis* would not be a *neder*.

The *Gemara* therefore teaches: *kamashma lan* — the *Baraita* comes to tell us that one *can* add a *neder*-layer on top of a rabbinic fast, and that layer is effective as a *davar hanadur*. The reason this is possible is that the principle of *ein issur chal al issur* — one prohibition cannot take effect on top of another — does not apply here, because the existing rabbinic prohibition and the new Torah-level *neder* are prohibitions of different types. The rabbinic *taanis* does not block the personal *neder* from taking effect as a distinct and additional layer. Once that *neder*-layer is in place, *Tzom Gedaliah* does become a genuine *davar hanadur*, and subsequent *hatfasa* onto it is valid.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Yochanan's View

The *Gemara* closes this section by noting that Rabbi Yochanan takes the position of Rava. When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted: Rabbi Yochanan taught that whether one says *mivta lo ochal lach* or *issar lo ochal lach*, either formulation constitutes a *shevuah*. Both *mivta* and *issar* are technical terms that independently create a binding oath. Rabbi Yochanan thus aligns with Rava's view that *issar* is a standalone technical term — not a description of *hatfasa* — and that both terms function equivalently as formal expressions of a *shevuah*.
