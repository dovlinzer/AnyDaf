# Bava Batra 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Souring Wine Status

### Store Wine Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא רַב יְהוּדָה, הָא רַב חִסְדָּא; מָר כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: This** is the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** and **this** is the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda. In accordance with whose** opinion **does the Master hold?** Rav Yosef **said to him: I know a *baraita*** from which it is possible to derive the *halakha*.

The daf opens with a question that had been left pending: does one recite *borei pri hagafen* over store-bought wine of the quality the *gemara* calls *kosa deshotah* — wine that is in the process of souring? The defining characteristic of *kosa deshotah* is that it has begun to smell like vinegar but still tastes like wine. The analogy to milk is apt: one smells the milk to assess freshness even when the taste has not yet turned, because the olfactory signal precedes the gustatory one. The question, then, is whether wine in this intermediate state retains the legal status of *yayin* — for purposes of *berakhah* and, as we shall see, for *hafrashat terumah* — or whether it has already crossed over into the category of *chometz*, vinegar.

*Abaye* posed this question to *Rav Yosef*, asking how he *paskens*. *Rav Yosef* replied that he has a *baraita* from which the answer can be derived.

### Checking Barrel Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַבּוֹדֵק אֶת הֶחָבִית לִהְיוֹת מַפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת חוֹמֶץ; כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יוֹם – וַדַּאי, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:8): With regard to **one who inspects a barrel** to see if it still contains enough wine **to continually** mentally **separate *teruma* from it** to exempt other untithed wine he has, until all the wine in that barrel would be *teruma* and would be given to a priest, **and afterward** the contents of the barrel **were found** to have turned to **vinegar,** which cannot be set aside as *teruma* for untithed wine, then **all three days** after he had last inspected it, it is **definitely** viewed as having been wine, and any wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is tithed. **From that** point **onward,** more than three days after the previous inspection, it is **uncertain** as to whether it had already turned to vinegar, and any wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is not tithed.

The *baraita* describes the following scenario: a person checks a barrel of wine with the intention of using it as the designated vessel from which he will, over time, be *mafrish* — separating — *terumah* on successive batches of wine. The practical arrangement works as follows: as new wine is pressed week after week, rather than separating *terumah* from each batch individually, the owner continuously designates small portions of this designated barrel as the *terumah* for each new lot. He must verify at the outset that the barrel contains genuine wine, since *terumah* designated from vinegar onto wine is ineffective. Over the course of months, the barrel accumulates these small designations until it is ultimately conveyed in full to a *kohen*.

The *baraita* then presents the problematic scenario: *v'achar kach nimtzeit chometz* — upon opening the barrel, it is discovered to have turned to vinegar. The question is which of the earlier *hafrashos* were valid, which were certainly invalid, and which remain in doubt (*safek*). This matters enormously, because a *safek* designation forces the owner to designate again, leaving two barrels of uncertain *terumah* status in the *kohen*'s hands.

The *baraita* states tersely: *kol gimmel yamim vaday* — three days is certain — and *mikan ve'eilach safek* — from that point forward it is doubtful. But this requires interpretation: which three days, and certain for what?

### Timeline of Spoilage

The ambiguity of the *baraita*'s language opens a dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* that will structure the entire sugya. To understand the stakes, consider the following timeline: on day one the barrel is tasted and found to be good wine. On some later day — call it the final day — it is opened and found to be vinegar. Along the intervening weeks and months, multiple *hafrashos* were made. The question is: with which *hafrashos* could the owner be confident, and at what point does uncertainty set in?

The critical empirical assumption is that wine does not turn to vinegar instantaneously. The process of souring (*reicha chala, ta'ama chamra* — smells like vinegar, tastes like wine) is a transitional phase that takes approximately three days to complete, after which the wine tastes like vinegar as well. This transitional phase is the crux of the dispute.

## Grape Drink Blessing (II)

### Tasting Tevel Issue

Before proceeding to the opinions of *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, an interesting procedural question arises regarding the *baraita* itself. *Rashbam* raises the difficulty: how did one taste the barrel in the first place, given that before *terumah* is separated, the barrel has the status of *tevel* — untithed produce — which may not be eaten? His answer is that the taster would separate a tiny amount, designate its *terumah*, and then taste it. *Tosafot*, by contrast, suggests that no actual tasting occurred at all: the barrel was examined by smell alone. As we noted at the outset, this is precisely the condition of *kosa deshotah* — that one may detect the early stages of souring through smell before it manifests in taste — which makes smell a legally operative mode of assessment.

## Souring Wine Status (II)

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – וַדַּאי יַיִן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** the *baraita* **saying? Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **this** is what it **is saying:** For **all** of **the first three days** following the inspection, it is **definitely** viewed as having been **wine** that had not yet turned to vinegar. **From that** point **onward,** it is **uncertain** as to whether it had already turned to vinegar. Accordingly, any wine for which *teruma* was separated after those three days by means of designating the contents of that barrel as *teruma* has an uncertain status.

*Rabbi Yochanan* reads the *baraita* as follows: *kol gimmel yamim harishonim vaday yayin* — the first three days from the time of tasting are certainly wine. *Mikan ve'eilach safek* — from that point forward, there is doubt. His reasoning is grounded in the known mechanics of the souring process. At the moment of tasting the barrel was sound, which means the souring process had not yet begun — for had it begun, it would have been detectable. The worst-case scenario, then, is that the process commenced a moment after the tasting concluded. The *gemara* explains: *chamra me'ilai akar* — wine begins to sour from the top, and since the tasting was from the top and revealed nothing amiss, the process had certainly not started. Immediately thereafter, in this worst case, the wine begins smelling like vinegar while still tasting like wine: *havei reicha chala ve-ta'ama chamra*. According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, this transitional state is still classified as wine (*chamra*). The souring process takes three days to complete, so even in the worst case the wine remains in a legally valid state for the full three-day period. Therefore, any *hafrasha* made within the first three days was made on definite wine. After three days, one cannot be certain whether the wine had already fully become vinegar, hence *safek*. Only on the final day — when the barrel is opened and found to be vinegar — do we have certainty that it is *vadai chometz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? חַמְרָא – מֵעִילַּאי עָקַר, וְהַאי טַעֲימֵיהּ וְלָא עֲקַר. אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: בָּתַר דְּטַעֲימֵיהּ עֲקַר; הָוֵה רֵיחָא חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא, וְכֹל רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא – חַמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason?** The process in which **wine turns sour** and becomes vinegar starts **with** the wine at the **top** of the barrel; **and** it is **that** wine which **he tasted** when he inspected it, **and** at that time **it had not** yet **soured.** And even **if you say** that immediately **after he tasted it** the wine began to **sour,** during the following three days **it** would **have the odor of vinegar and its taste** would be **of wine, and anything** that **has an odor of vinegar but its taste is of wine** is regarded as **wine.**

The halakhic implication for the original question about *berakhah* is significant: according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, *reicha chala ve-ta'ama chamra* — smells like vinegar but tastes like wine — is still *yayin*, and one would recite *borei pri hagafen*.

### R. Y. ben Levi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָאַחֲרוֹנִים – וַדַּאי חוֹמֶץ. מִכָּאן וּלְהַלָּן – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that there is a different interpretation of the *baraita*: For **all** of **the last three days** preceding the discovery that the wine had turned into vinegar, it is **definitely** viewed as having been **vinegar. From that** point and **earlier,** until the time it had been inspected, it is **uncertain** whether or not it was wine or vinegar.

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* reads the *baraita* in the opposite direction: *kol gimmel yamim acharonim vaday chometz* — the last three days before the vinegar is discovered are certainly vinegar. *Mikan ul'halan safek* — working backwards from there, the earlier periods are doubtful, even from day one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? חַמְרָא מִתַּתַּאי עָקַר, וְאֵימוֹר עֲקַר וְלָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ. וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר מֵעִילַּאי עָקַר, וְהָא טַעֲימֵיהּ וְלָא עֲקַר; דִּלְמָא בָּתַר דְּטַעֲימֵיהּ עֲקַר – הָוֵה רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא, וְרֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא – חַלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason?** The process in which **wine turns sour** and becomes vinegar starts **with** the wine at the **bottom** of the barrel, **and** therefore, since the inspection was limited to the wine at the top of the barrel, it is possible to **say** that wine at the bottom **had** already started to **sour and one was unaware of it.** Consequently, it is possible that on the day he tasted it the wine turned entirely into vinegar. **And** even **if you say** that the process in which **wine turns sour** and becomes vinegar starts **with** the wine at the **top** of the barrel, **and** it is **that** wine that **he tasted** when he inspected it **and** at that time **it had not** yet **soured, perhaps** immediately **after he tasted it** the wine began to **sour,** in which case **its odor** would be **of vinegar and its taste of wine, and** anything that **has an odor of vinegar but its taste is of wine** is regarded as **vinegar.**

His reasoning mirrors *Rabbi Yochanan*'s but inverts the conclusion about the transitional state. The *gemara* notes a supplementary point — *chamra mitatai akar*, wine begins souring from the bottom — meaning that when you tasted from the top on day one, the process may already have begun at the bottom without your awareness. But this is not the main argument. More fundamentally: even granting that wine sours from the top and that the tasting on day one was unimpeachable, *im timtza lomar me'ilai akar* — even if we assume it starts from the top — the moment after the tasting, the souring process could have begun. And in the transitional phase, *havi reicha chala ve-ta'amei chamra — ve-reicha chala ve-ta'amei chamra chala*: when it smells like vinegar and tastes like wine, that is already vinegar. If *reicha chala ve-ta'ama chamra* constitutes vinegar, then the *safek* commences from day one, since the souring process could have begun at any moment after the initial tasting. The last three days are *vadai chometz* because the fully soured vinegar found on the final day must have been preceded by at least three days of the transitional phase — and since that transitional phase is itself vinegar, those three days are certainly *chometz*.

### Resolution & Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרוֹמָאֵי מַתְנוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: רִאשׁוֹנִים – וַדַּאי יַיִן, אַחֲרוֹנִים – וַדַּאי חוֹמֶץ, אֶמְצָעִיִּים – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of the South taught** another interpretation of the *baraita* **in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi:** For the **first** three days it is **definitely** viewed as having been **wine.** For the **last** three days it is **definitely** viewed as having been **vinegar.** The status of the wine during the **intervening** period is **uncertain.**

The *gemara* then cites a tradition attributed to the southerners (*deromay*) in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* that attempts to hold both positions simultaneously: *rishonim vaday yayin, acharonim vaday chometz* — the first three days are definitely wine and the last three days are definitely vinegar. But the *gemara* immediately objects: *hagufa kashya* — this is internally contradictory. If the souring process counts as wine (*reicha chala ve-ta'ama chamra* equals wine), how can the last three days be *vadai chometz*? Perhaps the vinegar only fully formed moments ago. And if the souring process counts as vinegar, how can the first three days be *vadai yayin*? The souring could have begun immediately after the tasting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ: רִאשׁוֹנִים וַדַּאי יַיִן, אַלְמָא רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא – חַמְרָא; וַהֲדַר אָמְרַתְּ: אַחֲרוֹנִים וַדַּאי חוֹמֶץ, אַלְמָא רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא – חַלָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This matter itself** is **difficult,** as, since **you said** that for the **first** three days it is **definitely** viewed as having been **wine; apparently,** if **it has the odor of vinegar but its taste is of wine** it is regarded as **wine. But then you said** that for the **last** three days it is **definitely** viewed as having been **vinegar; apparently,** if **it has the odor of vinegar but its taste is of wine** it is regarded as **vinegar,** as it can be established only that the odor had changed three days ago.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח חַלָּא סִיפְתְּקָא, דְּאִי לָאו דַּעֲקַר תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי – לָא הֲוָה מִשְׁתְּכַח חַלָּא סִיפְתְּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the difficulty: The Sages of the South hold that as long as the wine still tastes like wine, it is regarded as wine. When they said that when a barrel is found to contain vinegar it is certain that the wine had already turned into vinegar three days previously, they were referring to a case **where** the barrel **was found** to contain **strong vinegar, as, had it not** already **soured three days** previously, the barrel **would not have been found** to contain **strong vinegar;** rather, it would contain only mild vinegar.

The *gemara* resolves this: *kegon d'ishtakach chala sipatka* — the case involves exceptionally strong vinegar upon opening. If the vinegar discovered is so potent that, in the assessment of the *gemara*, it could not have reached that strength without at least three days of full fermentation, then we can say with certainty that it had already completed the souring process three days prior. In such a case, one can hold that the transitional stage is classified as wine (first three days certainly good), while also establishing with certainty that the fully formed vinegar had been present for at least the last three days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן פְּשַׁט לֵיהּ? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב מָרִי וְרַב זְבִיד; חַד אָמַר: כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְחַד אָמַר: כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef claimed that this *baraita* can serve as proof with regard to which blessing is recited over wine that has the odor of vinegar but tastes like wine. Having cited three different interpretations of the *baraita*, the Gemara asks: **In accordance with** whose interpretation did Rav Yosef **resolve** the question of which blessing to recite? **Rav Mari and Rav Zevid disagree about it. One said** that Rav Yosef resolved it **in accordance with** the interpretation of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that this liquid is regarded as wine and the blessing for wine should be recited over it. **And one said** that Rav Yosef resolved it **in accordance with** the interpretation of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** that the liquid is regarded as vinegar and the generic blessing: By Whose word all things came to be, should be recited over it. There is no definitive resolution of the dispute.

The conclusion, according to *Rashbam*, is that the dominant position treats the souring process — *reicha chala ve-ta'ama chamra* — as wine. The *baraita* is ultimately amenable to two readings, corresponding to the positions of *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*. *Rav Yosef* cited this *baraita* in response to *Abaye*'s question, but there is itself a dispute — between *Rav Mari* and *Rav Zevid* — as to which way *Rav Yosef* actually read it. One holds he read it like *Rabbi Yochanan*, the other that he read it like *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*. Nonetheless, *Rashbam* concludes that since even the tradition in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* ultimately treats the souring process as wine (the *sipatka* resolution), the *halacha* is that souring wine retains the status of *yayin*.

## Wine Sale Dispute

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר חָבִית יַיִן לַחֲבֵרוֹ, וְהֶחְמִיצָה – אָמַר רַב: כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ בִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § An amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to **one who sells a barrel of wine to another, and** following the sale **it turned to vinegar. Rav said:** If it soured during **any of the first three days** following the sale, it is presumed that it had already began to sour **in the domain of** the **seller,** and he bears financial responsibility for it; **from that** point **onward,** it is presumed that the wine soured **in the domain of** the **buyer,** and it is his loss.

The *gemara* now turns to a related but distinct question that brings us back to the primary theme of this *perek* — commercial disputes and *mekach taut*. The case: a buyer purchases a barrel of wine from a seller. He tasted it in the store and found it to be good wine. Several days later, at home, he discovers it has turned to vinegar — still in the original barrel in which he received it, so there is no claim that a change of vessel caused any damage. He returns to the seller claiming the sale was a *mekach taut*, a mistaken purchase, on the grounds that the wine was already souring at the point of sale, even if imperceptible to the palate at the time.

The background assumption, drawn from an earlier *Mishnah* in the *masechta*, is that if one sells wine and it turns out to be vinegar, that is a *mekach taut*. The question here is how to determine whether the wine was already souring at the point of sale. Both *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* assume the case involves a sale in which the buyer tasted the wine in the store and found it acceptable.

### Rav: 3-Day Rule

*Rav* rules: *kol gimmel yamim harishonim birshat mocher* — if the wine turns vinegar within the first three days, it remains the legal responsibility of the seller. His reasoning follows directly from the three-day rule established earlier: we know empirically that wine does not become vinegar overnight; the souring process takes three days. Therefore, if it is vinegar within three days of sale, the process must have already begun at the moment of sale, making it a *mekach taut*, and the buyer is entitled to a refund. After three days — *mikan va'eilach birshat lokeach* — the wine is in the buyer's domain; the souring process may well have begun only after the transfer, and the seller bears no liability.

One might ask: what about wine that sours on day four? If it takes three days for the process to complete, could not the process have begun a day before sale and only manifested on day four? *Tosafot* addresses this precisely by emphasizing that the case assumes the buyer tasted the wine at the time of purchase. If the buyer tasted it and found it good, and it only went bad four days later, we assume it was sound at the point of sale and that everything happened on the buyer's watch — the sale was valid. If the buyer had not tasted at purchase and the wine went bad after four days, that would be a *safek*, and in that case *Tosafot* invokes the principle of *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah*: if the buyer has already paid, the burden of proof lies with him; if not, the burden shifts to the seller.

### Shmuel: Mazal Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חַמְרָא – אַכַּתְפָּא דְּמָארֵיהּ שָׁוַואר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** Even if the wine sours shortly after the purchase, the seller does not bear responsibility, as **the wine is agitated** as it is carried **upon the shoulders of its** new **owner,** causing it to sour quickly.

*Shmuel* disagrees fundamentally: *chamra akatpa demarei shavar* — wine is, as it were, carried on the shoulders of its owner. This reflects the idea that wine spoilage is connected to the *mazal* of the one who owns it. Even if the wine turns to vinegar within the first three days, *Shmuel* would not allow the buyer to claim *mekach taut*, because one cannot disprove the possibility that something in the buyer's circumstances — his *mazal* — caused the wine to sour. The seller can say: in my hands, this wine was never a problem; in yours, it went bad within days — that is a matter of your fortune, not my fraud.

### Tosafot: Transport Theory

*Tosafot* is uncomfortable with the *mazal* explanation and proposes an alternative reading of the phrase *chamra akatpa demarei shavar*. Rather than invoking metaphysical notions of luck, *Tosafot* suggests a straightforward physical explanation: the buyer carried the wine home on his shoulders, and the jostling and agitation of transport unsettled the wine, causing it to sour prematurely. The wine was sound when sold; the transportation itself triggered the spoilage. *Tosafot* acknowledges a difficulty with this reading — a seller presumably knows that purchased wine will be transported home, so should his liability not account for the foreseeable effects of transport? One possible answer is that the seller might say he has no way of knowing how carefully the buyer handled the merchandise. In any event, at the point of sale the wine was genuinely sound, and its subsequent souring was a consequence of the buyer's handling.

### Rav Yosef's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד רַב יוֹסֵף עוֹבָדָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וּכְווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּחַמְרָא. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef ruled** in an actual **case in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, in** which **beer** spoiled shortly after it was sold, **and in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel in** a similar case involving **wine. And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel.**

*Rav Yosef* issued a practical ruling that split the difference: *uvda kavatei d'Rav b'shichra u'kavatei d'Shmuel b'chamra* — in an actual case involving beer (*shichra*), he ruled like *Rav*, allowing the return within three days; in a case involving wine (*chamra*), he ruled like *Shmuel*, not permitting return even within the three-day period. The precise reasoning for this bifurcation is not explicit in the *gemara*, though it may relate to differing assessments of how long it takes beer to sour, or to the view that the concept of *mazal* applies specifically to wine rather than to beer.

### Final Halacha

The *gemara* concludes: *hilchata kavatei d'Shmuel* — the *halacha* follows *Shmuel*, whether the case involves beer or wine. Even if wine turns to vinegar within the first three days, the buyer cannot claim *mekach taut*. Both *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* note that the *gemara*'s case assumed the buyer tasted the wine at the time of purchase and found it acceptable. The practical application of this ruling in contemporary settings is naturally subject to the underlying factual assumptions — how long spoilage takes, what effect transport has, and whether the original tasting was a reliable indicator of quality.

## Grape Drink Blessing (II)

### Baraita: Beverages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד שֵׁכַר תְּמָרִים, וְאֶחָד שֵׁכַר שְׂעוֹרִים, וְאֶחָד שִׁמְרֵי יַיִן – מְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶם ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיָה בִּדְבָרוֹ״. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: שְׁמָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶם טַעַם יַיִן, מְבָרֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶן ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגֶּפֶן״. רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵין הֲלָכָה כַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Whether** one drinks **date beer, or barley beer, or** a beverage made from soaking **pomace** from the production of **wine** in water, known as *tamad*, **one recites over them the blessing: By Whose word all things came to be. *Aḥerim* say: Over** wine made from **pomace that has the taste of wine** one **recites the blessing: Who creates fruit of the vine. Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: The *halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of ***Aḥerim*.**

The *gemara* returns to the theme of *berakhot* on various beverages. A *baraita* is cited: whether it is *sheichar temarim* — a drink fermented from dates, i.e., a type of mead — or *sheichar se'orim* — beer fermented from barley — or *shimrei yayin* — water that has been infused with the skins (*lees*) left over from wine pressing, giving the water a grapey flavor — in all these cases one recites *shehakol nihiya bidvaro*. Not *borei pri hagafen*, not *borei pri ha'etz*, not *borei pri ha'adama* or *borei minei mezonos* — simply *shehakol*.

### Fruit Juice Principle

This ruling calls for explanation on multiple fronts. The case of *shimrei yayin* is perhaps the easiest to understand: even though the water has acquired some grape flavor, its proportion of actual grape content is so minimal that it cannot be classified as *yayin*, and we will discuss momentarily exactly where the threshold lies. But why does mead not receive *borei pri ha'etz* — it derives, after all, from a fruit?

The governing principle is *nishtaneh le'maliusa* — a product processed from its original fruit form retains the *etz* or *adama* blessing only if the transformation represents an improvement over the original fruit. If the change represents a degradation, or if the product is in a form that is not the fruit's primary or most significant use, the *berakhah* drops to *shehakol*. The general *halachic* assumption, rooted in the *gemara*, is that fruit juices — with the exception of wine and olive oil — are considered of inferior status relative to the whole fruit. They are described in the language of the *gemara* as *zei'ah be'alma* — merely a kind of exudation, not a significant product in their own right.

### Wine & Oil Exception

Wine and olive oil are the two exceptions because they are the primary and most valued products of the vine and the olive respectively — indeed, the *gemara* notes that *sechita* (pressing) on *Shabbos* involves *chiyuv* specifically for grapes and olives, while the halachic significance of pressing other fruits remains debatable. All other fruits, when converted to juice, yield something regarded as a lesser form of the original, and the *berakhah* is accordingly *shehakol*.

*Tosafot*, citing the view in *Keitzad Mevarchin*, notes that *Mar bar Rav Ashi* ruled that even straight date honey — not diluted, not fermented — requires *shehakol*, since *zei'ah be'alma hu*, it is merely the seeping liquid of the fruit. *Tosafot* further notes that *Ba'al Halachot Gedolot* disagreed, holding that undiluted juice flowing directly from a fruit would receive *borei pri ha'etz*; but *Tosafot* rejects this view.

The discussion naturally invites reflection on contemporary beverages. Orange juice, for example, raises the question of whether the *nishtaneh le'maliusa* principle can be applied when the fruit is specifically cultivated for juicing — when the juice is, in a real sense, the fruit's highest form. The argument for *borei pri ha'etz* on commercially produced orange juice is that it comes from a *min* of orange bred specifically for this purpose, making the juice indisputably the primary product. For oranges not of that variety — even where local custom is to squeeze them for juice — the analysis is less clear. And the broader principle remains: the *berakhah* is *borei pri ha'etz* and refers to the *pri* itself; when the product is no longer in its original form, the relevance of that *berakhah* diminishes, and the claim that the transformed product is an improvement requires affirmative justification.

Beer raises an additional consideration: it is predominantly water. Even setting aside the question of whether fermentation constitutes an improvement over the grain, the sheer proportion of water in the final product makes it difficult to treat the drink as genuinely representing the grain. As *Tosafot* notes: *de-mi yeimar de-se'ari ikkar — dilma maya ikkar* — who says barley is the primary component? Perhaps the water is the main substance and the grain is merely flavoring. The *baraita* cited bears this out: *daf* 96a states, in line with this analysis, that beer receives *shehakol*.

One additional note of interest: the question of grain alcohol for *chametz* on *Pesach* connects to this same conceptual framework. Some authorities regard grain alcohol as *chametz gamur*; others hold it is entirely *mutar*, treating it as essentially water with negligible grain significance; and a third position places it in a middle category analogous to *ta'aroves chametz*. The underlying debate tracks precisely the same question of how much identity the grain retains after processing.

The *gemara* then records that *Rava* and *Rav Yosef* both rule: *ein halacha k'acherim* — we do not rule like the minority view (*acherim*) who would give *borei pri hagafen* to water that has acquired a winey taste. Even flavorful grapey water is water, not wine. It is a wine-flavored drink, not *yayin*.

## Grape-Skin Water Ratio

### Extraction Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, רְמָא תְּלָתָא וַאֲתָא אַרְבְּעָה – חַמְרָא הוּא. רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל חַמְרָא דְּלָא דָרֵי עַל חַד תְּלַת מַיָּא – לָאו חַמְרָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** According to the opinions **of everyone** mentioned in the *baraita*, if **one poured three** jugs of water over grape pomace **and** then, after removing the pomace, the volume of the resulting beverage **came to four** jugs, then that beverage **is** regarded as **wine.** Evidently, a quarter of the resulting beverage is from juice that was contained in the pomace, which is pure wine, and that is a sufficient ratio for the beverage as a whole to be regarded as wine. The Gemara interjects Rava's comments: With this statement, **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava said: Any wine that does not contain three** parts **water to one** part pure wine **is not** regarded as **wine,** as it is excessively strong.

*Rava* then frames the precise parameters of the dispute. The process under discussion is *metamed* — soaking grape skins in water and then draining off the liquid, which now carries some grape content. The question is at what ratio of grape juice to water the resulting mixture qualifies as wine (*chamra*) deserving *borei pri hagafen*.

### Full Extra Unit Case

The case of clear consensus: if one puts in three units of water and draws out four units of liquid, then a full extra unit has been extracted from the grape skins, meaning the liquid is one part grape juice to three parts water. *Rava* elsewhere establishes that wine mixed with up to three parts water is still classified as wine — *kol chamra d'lo dari al chad tlata maya lav chamra hu*, any wine that cannot sustain a three-to-one dilution with water is not considered real wine. Since this mixture is exactly at that threshold, it qualifies as *chamra*, and all agree one would recite *borei pri hagafen*.

### Equal Amount Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְמָא תְּלָתָא וַאֲתָא תְּלָתָא, וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי, דִּרְמָא תְּלָתָא וַאֲתָא תְּלָתָא וּפַלְגָא – דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: תְּלָתָא עָיֵיל תְּלָתָא נָפֵיק, פָּשׁ לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא – וּפַלְגָא בְּשִׁתָּא פַּלְגֵי מַיָּא, וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא;
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: If **one poured three** jugs of water over pomace, **and** the volume of the resulting beverage still **came to three** jugs, then **it is nothing,** i.e., it is not regarded as wine. **When** the *tanna'im* in the *baraita* **disagree** is in a case **where one poured three** jugs of water over pomace **and** the volume of the resulting beverage **came to three and a half** jugs, **as the Rabbis,** i.e., the first *tanna*, **hold** that **three** jugs of water **were absorbed into** the pomace and then the same **three** jugs of water **seeped out** of the pomace; therefore, there **remains half** a jug of the resulting beverage that was originally pure wine contained in the pomace. **But half** a jug of pure wine mixed **into six half-**jugs of **water is nothing,** i.e., the mixture is too weak to be regarded as wine.

The other clear case: three units of water go in and exactly three units come out. In this case, all agree that no significant grape juice has been extracted — the liquid is just water that has picked up a slight flavor. Even *acherim*, who would give *borei pri hagafen* to water with a winey taste, would concede here: when no measurable amount of grape juice has been added, it is simply grape-flavored water, and *shehakol* is the appropriate *berakhah*.

### Disputed Middle Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲחֵרִים סָבְרִי: תְּלָתָא עוּל, תְּרֵין וּפַלְגָא נָפֵיק, פָּשׁ לֵיהּ כּוּזָא; וְכוּזָא בִּתְרֵי וּפַלְגָא חַמְרָא, מְעַלְּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And *Aḥerim* hold** that **three** jugs of water **were absorbed into** the pomace but only **two and a half** jugs of water **seeped out** of the pomace, as one jug of water replaced the one jug of pure wine contained in the pomace. Therefore, there **remains** one **jug** of the resulting beverage that is pure wine that was previously contained in the pomace. **And** one **jug** of pure wine mixed **into two and a half** jugs of water **is** regarded as **full-fledged wine.**

The dispute arises in the intermediate case: three units of water go in and three and a half units come out. Clearly some grape juice has been extracted — at least a half unit — but the question is how much.

### Minority vs Majority

The majority view (*tanna kamma*) assumes that all three of the original units of water came back out intact, and the extra half unit is pure grape juice. The mixture is therefore half a unit of grape juice to three units of water, which is a ratio of one to six. At that ratio — *palga be-shisha palge, maya be-alma ve-lo klum* — it remains grape-flavored water, not wine, and the *berakhah* is *shehakol*.

The minority view (*acherim*) calculates differently. Knowing that grape skins absorb some of the water poured into them, *acherim* reason that not all three units of water came back out: two and a half units came out and half a unit was retained by the skins. The full extra half unit came from grape juice alone. But additionally, since half a unit of water stayed behind in the skins, the output is comprised of two and a half units of original water plus one unit of grape juice — a ratio of one to two and a half. At that concentration, which is well within the *borei pri hagafen* threshold of one to three, this qualifies as wine — *chamra ma'alya hu*.

### Mishnah Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְיוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי מִדָּתוֹ, מִי פְּלִיגִי?! וְהָא תְּנַן:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where** the volume of the resulting beverage **is greater than the amount** of water that was poured over the pomace, **do** the Sages ever **disagree? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 5:6):

The *gemara* then raises a potential challenge to the structure of this debate. It seems as though the argument only applies when *more* liquid comes out than went in. But the *Mishnah* in *ma'aser* records a dispute about *metamed* when the same amount came out as went in: the *tanna kamma* exempts such liquid from *trumot u-ma'asrot* since it is not wine, while *Rabbi Yehuda* obligates. One might infer that when more comes out, even the *tanna kamma* would agree it is wine. The *gemara* rejects this: *hu ha-din de-afilu be-yoter mi-kdei midato pligi* — even when more comes out, the *tanna kamma* would still say it is not wine. The *Mishnah* chose to frame the dispute around the case where the same amount emerged only to illustrate *kocho de-Rabbi Yehuda* — how stringent *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is. Even in the case where the output equals the input and there is no obvious evidence of any grape juice, *Rabbi Yehuda* still treats it as potentially wine and requires *ma'aser*. *Rashbam* notes that *Rabbi Yehuda* may be more stringent specifically in the context of *ma'aser* than the same authorities would be when it comes to *berakhot*.

### Tosafot Modern Practice

*Tosafot*, citing *Rabbeinu Tam*, notes that in their time it was common practice to produce wine from grape skins (*chartzanim*). *Tosafot* rules that even if this wine has a genuine wine taste, as long as the extraction process does not yield the requisite ratio of four units out for every three in — i.e., as long as one full extra unit of grape juice per three units of water has not been extracted — the *berakhah* remains *shehakol*. Since practical experience shows that this level of extraction is not being achieved in the typical contemporary process (as *Tosafot* knew it), one does not recite *borei pri hagafen* on such grape-skin wine. The *halacha* follows the rejection of *acherim*'s position, as *Rava* and *Rav Yosef* ruled.

## Wine Ratio & Sanhedrin

### Standard Wine Mixture

The *gemara* cites *Rav*'s foundational principle: *kol chamra d'lo dari al chad tlata maya lav chamra hu* — any wine that cannot sustain a three-to-one ratio of water is not considered proper wine. This means the standard classical mixture (*mazeg*) was one part wine to three parts water, yielding a diluted beverage that still carried the full legal and ritual status of wine.

### Kiddush Cup Measure

This ratio has a direct practical application for the *kos shel berachah*. As cited in the beginning of *Perek Motzi Yayin* (the chapter dealing with *kiddush*), the *shiur* for a *kos* of *kiddush* is a *rova* (quarter) of a *revit*. One must begin with a quarter *revit* of wine so that after mixing it with three parts water the total volume reaches a full *revit* — approximately three and a half ounces — the minimum *shiur* for the *kiddush* cup.

### Sanhedrin Quorum Rule

*Tosafot* raises a textual question. The phrase *al yechsar hamazeg* appears in the context of the *Sanhedrin* in *Sanhedrin* 2b, where it is invoked as a rule about when members may leave during deliberations: if there are 23 members remaining (the *Sanhedrin Ketana* quorum), one may leave; otherwise not. *Tosafot* initially understands *hamazeg* as a reference to *mzigas ha-yayin* — the act of diluting wine — suggesting that the principle is about preserving the core undiluted portion, which would imply a two-to-one ratio (water to wine) rather than three-to-one. This creates tension with *Rav*'s ruling of three-to-one.

### Mazeg Gematria

*Tosafot* then presents an alternative interpretation from *Rabbeinu Menachem* that is both elegant and ingenious. *Mazeg* — *mem, zayin, gimel* — has the numerical value of 40 + 7 + 3 = 50. Therefore *al yechsar hamazeg* means: do not let 50 depart from the 71 members of the *Sanhedrin*. Only up to 49 may leave. If 49 leave, 22 remain — but *ein beis din shakul*, a court may not be evenly divided, so one more is added, bringing the quorum to 23. *Al yechsar hamazeg min ayin-aleph de-Sanhedrin nun; aval mem-tet yachsiru, ve-yisharu kaf-beis, ve-ein beis din shakul mosifin aleihem od echad, harei kaf-gimel* — the 50 are the constraint; 49 may go; 22 remain; an additional one is added; the result is 23. Whether the *pasuk* teaches a wine-dilution ratio or encodes a *gematria* constraining the departure of *Sanhedrin* members, *Tosafot* presents both possibilities and finds the *Rabbeinu Menachem* reading to be a particularly deft interpretive move.
