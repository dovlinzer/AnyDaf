# Ketubot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Praise the Bride Debate

### Shammai vs. Hillel

The daf opens two lines from the bottom of 16b, in the midst of a broader discussion about the practices that served as evidence of a first wedding — relevant decades later when a couple might divorce and the amount of the *ketubah* is disputed. Against that backdrop, the Gemara transitions to one of the more famous debates between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, introduced by the formula *tanu rabbanan*: How does one dance before the *kallah*? What does one say while performing this *rikud*?

### Kallah Kemoshechi

Beit Shammai says: *kallah k'moshechi* — you praise her according to what she actually is. You tell it straight, you are honest about her. Beit Hillel says: *kallah na'ah vechasudah* — you say that she is beautiful and charming, regardless of the particulars.

### Kallah Na'ah Vechasudah

Beit Shammai challenges Beit Hillel directly: What if she was lame or blind — *chigeret o sumah*? Would you still say *kallah na'ah vechasudah*? It is worth pausing over the fact that Beit Shammai hones in specifically on physical disability as its test case. The Torah itself says *midvar sheker tirchak* — distance yourself from falsehood. How can Beit Hillel countenance saying something demonstrably untrue?

### Mikach Ra Debate

Beit Hillel responds with an analogy: If someone made a bad purchase in the marketplace and comes to you and says, "What do you think of what I bought?" — *yishabchenu be'einav o yiganenu be'einav* — do you praise it in his eyes or disparage it? The obvious answer is *yishabchenu be'einav* — of course you say something nice. From here, the *chachamim* derive a broader principle: *le'olam tehei da'ato shel adam me'urevet im habriyot* — a person should always conduct himself in a way that is amenable to other people, sensitive to what they want and need to hear.

It is worth noting what Beit Hillel is *not* saying. They are not claiming that the praise is objectively true. Rather, the rationale is rooted in human sensitivity — in attending to what a person actually wants. Rashi captures this with the phrase *la'asot le-ish ve-ish kirtzono*: to do for each person according to his desire. The bride's husband is the one marrying her; the question is what will serve him and her at this moment. This dynamic, in many ways, captures something essential about the broader Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel divide: does one orient primarily toward some objective truth that human beings must accommodate themselves to, or does one begin with human realities and work outward from there? One way to frame that debate is the question of *mah nivra techilah* — whether *shamayim* or *aretz* was created first. Beit Shammai prioritizes the objective and transcendent; Beit Hillel begins with the human and the earthly.

### Finding Nice Words

Rashi's reading of *kallah k'moshechi* is that you emphasize what is genuinely beautiful about her, what is praiseworthy — *l'fi yofyah vachashivutah m'kalsim otah*. But this immediately raises a difficulty: what if there is simply nothing nice to say?

Tosafot addresses this directly. If she has a blemish — *im yesh bah mum* — then one should be silent, *yishtaku*. As the saying goes, if you have nothing nice to say, don't say anything at all. Alternatively, Tosafot says, *yishabchu b'davar na'eh sheyesh bah* — find something nice to say: perhaps her eyes, perhaps her hands, perhaps some other quality. But Beit Hillel, on Tosafot's reading, says you praise her *l'gamri* — in general terms, *kallah na'ah vechasudah*. And therein lies the difficulty: if for everyone else you say the full formula, but for *this* bride you mention her lovely eyes — people can read between the lines. The selective praise becomes an implicit critique.

Tosafot's reading also illuminates Beit Hillel's position in a different way. *Kallah na'ah vechasudah* is to some extent a formulaic expression, and formulaic language operates differently from a literal claim. Everyone understands that it is a convention; no one hears it as a precise assessment. Furthermore, beauty is subjective — and in the eyes of her husband, she may indeed be all of that.

### Maharsha Reconciles

The Maharsha goes further and tries to tone down the apparent harshness of Beit Shammai's position. When Beit Shammai says *kallah k'moshechi*, he argues, it does not mean that each wedding requires a fresh individual assessment of the bride's qualities. Rather, it means that however this *kallah* is — whoever she is — she is beautiful for *her* husband. She is *k'moshechi* in the sense that she is exactly what he wants and needs. On this reading, Beit Shammai is also making a formulaic statement, just one that more explicitly underscores the subjective dimension: she is beautiful *as she is*, meaning in his eyes. The difference between the two schools, then, is simply that Beit Shammai calls attention to the subjective frame while Beit Hillel does not.

Tosafot makes a related point about the Beit Shammai position elsewhere in the discussion. Even Beit Shammai would not instruct one to disparage a new purchase. Everyone understands that social sensitivity requires saying something kind when someone asks your opinion. Beit Shammai's objection is narrower: one should not establish a rabbinic *takkanah* requiring a formulaic falsehood. *Ain lahem l'chachamim l'saken v'yizakku l'mar sheker* — the sages should not be institutionalizing the practice of saying something that is not true, given that the Torah says *midvar sheker tirchak*. Beit Shammai acknowledges social grace; they simply resist encoding it as law.

## Wedding Practices

### Lo Kchal v'lo Srak

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported what they would sing before the bride in the west: *lo kchal v'lo srak v'lo pirchus v'ya'alat chen* — no eyeshadow, no rouge, no elaborate hairdo, and yet she radiates charm. The song celebrates natural, unadorned beauty — an inner grace that requires no embellishment.

### Smicha Celebration

Strikingly, the Gemara then reports that when the rabbis gave *smicha* to Rabbi Zeira, they sang this same song. They had a full celebration — singing and dancing at the ordination — and the song they chose for the *kallah* was the one they sang for the newly ordained *talmid chacham*. This is at once arresting and profound. The comparison of Torah scholarship to a bride, and of the *talmid chacham* to the beloved, runs deep in rabbinic culture. The relationship between the scholar and the Torah is, in certain registers, an intimate and even erotic one — husbands leaving their wives to go and learn, scholars described as married to the Torah. Here, the honoree of the *smicha* celebration is cast, in the song, in the role of the bride: naturally beautiful, requiring no adornment.

### Ami and Assi Songs

When *smicha* was conferred upon Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi, the song was different. The rabbis sang: *kol mindein v'kol mindein s'muchu lana* — anyone like this one and this one, we should ordain from among us. And: *lo t'samchu lana lo min sarmisin v'lo min sarmitin* — do not ordain for us from among the *sarmisin* and the *sarmitin*.

Rashi explains: the *sarmisin* are those who distort the Torah, from the root *lesares* — to twist. The *sarmitin*, Rashi says, connotes rags, something worthless. There is an alternate version: *lo min chamisin v'lo min turmisim* — not from those who deliver only a fifth of the reasoning, and not from the empty or worthless ones. The Maharsha adds a pointed observation here: that in his own day, many rabbis had fallen into precisely these failings — *ki ba-avonoteinu rabbim me-ha-rabbanim she-nichshalim be-zeh ba-dor ha-zeh* — and that the matter deserved remedy at the hands of the communal and civic leadership. Whoever unpacks the full context of that Maharsha's complaint will find it worth studying.

It is somewhat deflating, of course, to be celebrated at your *smicha* with a song that says, in essence, at least you are not a corrupt distorter of the Torah. The song for Rabbi Zeira — natural beauty, no adornment needed — is rather more flattering.

### Abbahu's Prestige

The Gemara then describes Rabbi Abbahu: when he would come from the *metivta* to the house of the Caesar, the maidservants of Caesar's household would come out to greet him and sing: *rabba de-ameih u-madbarna de-umteih botzina de-nehora baruch matyeich le-shalom* — teacher of his people, leader of his nation, candle of light, blessed be your coming in peace. Presumably he would arrive radiating something from his learning, and even the non-Jewish servants of the imperial household recognized it. The Maharsha notes this context.

What is striking throughout this passage is the persistent way in which discussions of weddings and brides fold into discussions of Torah learning and scholars. The woman as purchase, the *kallah* as beloved, and the *talmid chacham* as one who is himself like a bride — these images keep circling back on one another.

### Dancing with Hadassim

They said about Rabbi Yehuda be-Rabbi Ilai that he would take a branch of myrtle — *bad shel hadas* — and dance before the bride, *merakked lifnei ha-kallah*. And as he danced, he would sing: *kallah na'ah vechasudah*. So he enacted the practice fully — physical dancing with the *hadas*, combined with the Beit Hillel formula. This is the same Rabbi Yehuda be-Rabbi Ilai we will encounter again shortly, and his conduct here is entirely consistent with what we will find there: a person who was *mochel* on his own dignity in order to bring joy to others.

### Three-Fold Dancing

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak would dance *a-tlat* — by threes. Rashi understood this to mean he would juggle three branches of myrtle, though the Maharsha objects on both textual and practical grounds: the Aramaic would be *bi-tlat* rather than *a-tlat* for that meaning, and in any case, myrtle branches do not make for good juggling implements. The Maharsha prefers to read it as meaning he danced with three times the vigor of anyone else — he threw himself into the *rikud* with an abandon that put everyone around him to shame.

Rabbi Zeira, watching, said: *ka-machsif lan sabba* — the elder is embarrassing us. On Rashi's reading, Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak was simply undignified, carrying on in a way unbefitting a scholar. On the Maharsha's reading, he was embarrassing the younger scholars by outpacing them entirely — they were young, and the old man was dancing three times as hard.

### Pillar of Fire

When Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak died, *afsik amuda de-nura bein dideih le-chulei alma* — a pillar of fire separated between him and all the rest of the world. And it is transmitted that a pillar of fire only appears for someone who is singular in the generation, or perhaps one of two people — *le-chad be-dara ei le-trei be-dara* — because it marks them as set apart, in a class unto themselves.

The same Rabbi Zeira who had said the elder was embarrassing them now said: *ahani leih shutita de-sabba* — it was the branch of the elder that helped him. Or, in the alternate versions: his *shtus* — his wildness — or his *shita* — his approach — that was what elevated him. The one who was *mochel* on his own *kavod* to bring *simcha* to the *chatan* was the one who merited this singular distinction. There is an unmistakable echo here of David dancing before the Ark and Michal's critique, and while the Gemara does not make that comparison explicitly, the structural parallel — dignity versus abandon in the service of something sacred — is very much present.

### Bride on Shoulders

Rav Acha would carry the *kallah* on his shoulders and dance. The rabbis asked him: are we permitted to do the same? He replied: *ei damya aleichu k'kashura l'chai* — if she is to you like a beam of wood, if there is no sexual dimension to it whatsoever, then yes, go ahead. *V'ei lo, lo* — but if it will arouse improper thoughts, then no. The ruling is entirely subjective. This opens onto a broader discussion among the *Rishonim* about which categories of male-female interaction are governed by subjective assessment and which by objective prohibition — a question with real practical implications.

## Bride vs. Dead

### Looking All Week

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *mutar le-histakel bifnei kallah kol shiva* — one is permitted to gaze admiringly at the *kallah* all seven days of the wedding celebrations. The purpose is *kedei le-chabevah al ba'alah* — to make her beloved in her husband's eyes: if everyone is looking at her admiringly, the husband will feel that she is genuinely beautiful and desirable. However, the Gemara notes: *leit hilcheta ke-vatei* — the law does not follow this ruling. That is simply asking for trouble.

### Ma'avirin Hamet

The Gemara now turns to other expressions of communal participation in the life-cycle moments of marriage and death. Just as there is *merakdin bifnei ha-kallah* — the community dancing before the bride — there is a broader principle of communal obligation at both weddings and burials. And just as there is a week of *shiva brachot* when the community participates in the joy, there is a week of *shivah* when the community participates in the mourning. These are not private affairs; they are communal experiences.

So the Gemara teaches: *ma'avirin et ha-met milifnei ha-kallah* — you move the funeral procession aside to make way for the wedding party. When the two groups converge in the street, the *kallah* has the right of way. This is a striking ruling. A reader of *Kohelet* might expect the opposite — *tov la-lekhet el beit avel milalechet el beit mishteh* — it is better to go to a house of mourning than a house of feasting. Yet the rabbis make a strikingly life-affirming ruling: attending to joy, to the next generation, to *simchat chatan ve-kallah*, takes precedence over the funeral. Both groups, in turn, yield to the king, who always has priority.

### Agrippas's Humility

The Gemara recounts that Agrippas the king stepped aside before the *kallah*, and the sages praised him for this. *Shibchu chachamim* — they commended him. But the Gemara immediately puzzles over this: if the sages praised him, does that mean it was the right thing to do?

### Royal Honor Doctrine

Rav Ashi weighs in: even according to the view that a *nasi* — a prince or governor — who waives his honor has waived it legitimately (*nasi she-machal al kvodo, kvodo machul*), a *melech* who waives his honor has *not* waived it (*melech she-machal al kvodo, ein kvodo machul*). The verse says *som tasim alecha melech* — the double language of *som tasim* indicates that the king's authority must remain perpetual and inviolable. *She-tehei emato alecha* — his awe must remain upon the people. The Maharsha adds that royal honor is also an expression of *chalak michvodo le-vasar ve-dam* — it reflects a dimension of divine kingship that cannot simply be set aside by the king's personal choice.

So how was Agrippas praised for doing something that seems halachically impermissible? The Gemara answers: *parashat derachim havai* — it was a crossroads. The two parties encountered each other at an intersection, and the king's entourage made a turn that happened to open the way for the wedding party. It was not an explicit act of stepping aside; there was no obvious *mechilah* on the king's *kavod*. The praise was real, but the mechanism was subtle enough not to constitute a formal waiver of royal dignity.

## Suspending Torah Study

### Mevatlin Talmud Torah

The Gemara now turns to the relationship between *talmud Torah* and the communal obligations of burial and wedding celebration. *Mevatlin Talmud Torah le-hotza'at ha-met ule-hachnasat kallah* — one sets aside Torah study for the procession of the dead and for bringing in the bride. This ruling reflects something deeper than a formal principle of *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah*. It captures something about the relationship between the scholar and the community, between the *beit midrash* and the lived realities of the people around it.

### Leaving Beit Midrash

They said about Rabbi Yehuda be-Rabbi Ilai — the same one who danced with the *hadas* — that he would leave the *beit midrash* for both occasions. This is deeply consistent with everything we know about him. He was *mochel* on his *kevod ha-Torah* to be genuinely present with people in their *simchas* and their losses. There is something powerful here. The *Yerushalmi* formulation captures the underlying principle: *kol ha-lomed she-lo al menat la-asot, mutav lo she-lo lamad* — one who studies without the intention to act would have been better off not learning at all. Torah must translate into the real world, into how one actually shows up for people.

The parallel to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai emerging from the cave is worth noting. There too the question is whether the scholar, at his elevated level of Torah, can connect to and participate in ordinary human life and joy. The image of the man running with two *hadassim* to honor *Shabbat* — that mundane, earthy expression of love for a mitzvah — is what opens Rabbi Shimon's eyes. Here, Rabbi Yehuda be-Rabbi Ilai models the same orientation from the outset.

### Enough People Condition

But the Gemara qualifies this: *bameh devarim amurim beshein imo kol tzorko* — when is Torah study suspended? Only when there are not enough people for the occasion. *Aval yesh imo kol tzorko, ein mevatlin* — if there are already enough, there is no obligation to leave the *beit midrash*. The question then becomes: what counts as enough?

### Required Numbers

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Ini mishmeih de-Rav*: twelve thousand men, and six thousand *shofarot* — trumpeters or horn-blowers who would either call people to attend or simply be part of the procession. An alternate version gives thirteen thousand men, with six thousand of those blowing *shofarot*. Ulla offers a different and more visual measure: that there are enough men to form a wall from the gate of the city all the way to the cemetery.

### Natilatah Kentinatah

Rav Sheshet — and some attribute it to Rabbi Yochanan — offers the most demanding standard: *netilatah kentinatah* — the taking away of Torah is like the giving of Torah. Just as the Torah was given with 600,000 people present, so too, for the death of a *talmid chacham*, there are "enough people" only when 600,000 are present. This makes clear what had perhaps not been obvious: these enormous numbers are not about any ordinary deceased person. They are about the death of a *talmid chacham*, and the obligation is rooted in *kevod ha-Torah*, not merely *kevod ha-met*. You leave your *beit midrash* to show honor to the Torah that is being taken from the world. This shifts the calculus significantly: the *beit midrash* is not competing with *chesed* to the dead; it is itself engaged in *kevod ha-Torah*, which is why it requires a demonstration of *kevod ha-Torah* on the other side to dislodge it.

This also raises a question about the *kallah* case: there, there is no threshold tied to the stature of the *chatan* or *kallah* — one simply goes. The asymmetry is worth sitting with.

### Teachers Exception

The Gemara adds a final, remarkable qualification. *Ve-hanie milei le-man de-kari ve-tani* — these thresholds apply for someone who read Scripture and learned Mishnah but never taught. *Aval le-man de-matni, leit lei shiura* — for one who taught Torah, there is no measure; everyone must attend, and no number is sufficient to say there are already enough.

Rashi draws the implication in the other direction as well: *shemeinan de-le-man de-la kari ve-la tani* — for someone who was entirely outside the world of Torah learning, the measure is purely pragmatic. If there are enough people present to accomplish the burial, you are not obligated to leave the *beit midrash* to make the crowd larger. For an ordinary deceased person, ten people may well be enough. The gap between the thresholds is vast. This further confirms that the entire framework is not primarily about dignity for the dead, but about *kevod ha-Torah* — and only *kevod ha-Torah* is weighty enough to pull one out of the *beit midrash*.

## First Marriage Evide (II)

### Hinuma Practice

Returning to the evidential practices that distinguish first weddings from second, the Gemara asks: *ei yesh edim she-yatzah be-hinuma* — if there are witnesses that she went out with a *hinuma*, that establishes that it was a first wedding. But what is a *hinuma*?

### Myrtle Dome

Surhav bar Pappa in the name of Ziri says: *tanura d'asa* — literally, an oven of myrtle, understood by Rashi as a kind of dome constructed from myrtle branches, perhaps something like a canopy or archway for the bride to walk beneath.

### Veil Practice

Rabbi Yochanan says: *kviyasta d'mnamnu bah kallah* — a covering in which the *kallah* would doze off. Rashi understands this as a kind of veil covering her eyes, something that shielded her so thoroughly she could even catch a brief sleep. Both descriptions point toward practices that parallel, in one way or another, what we still recognize as the *chuppah* and the veil — two of the most ancient and enduring features of a Jewish wedding.

### Roasted Grain

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka holds that the distribution of roasted grain is sufficient as evidence. A *Baraita* confirms that in *Yehudah*, the distribution of roasted grain was indeed the recognized sign of a first wedding.

### Oil on Rabbis

But in Babylonia? The Gemara asks: *B'Bavel mai?* Rav answers: *dardugei d'mishcha areisha d'rabbanan* — the dripping of oil on the heads of the rabbis. At a first wedding, they would go to the rabbis' table and pour oil over the heads of the scholars present. Once again, the intersection of the rabbinic world and the wedding celebration is striking.

### Oil Ritual Challenge

Rav Pappa challenged this: *mishcha d'chafifusa ka'amar mar* — are you saying they would take hair-washing oil and pour it over people's heads? Rashi suggests he was offended by the image — as though the rabbis needed a grooming treatment or something unseemly was being implied.

### Abaye's Reminder

Abaye responded pointedly. He called Rav Pappa *yatma* — orphan — which is remarkable, since it is Abaye who was himself orphaned. Whether this is ironic or whether he is speaking loosely, the force of his reply is clear: *v'lo avda lach immach dardugei mishcha areisha d'rabbanan b'sha'as ma'aseh* — did your own mother not do this at your wedding? Are you really going to take offense at a practice that was performed for you? The word *yatma* carries additional poignancy in Abaye's mouth given his own biography.

### Rabbinical Practice

The Gemara then records a concrete historical account: one of the rabbis married his son into the household of Rabbah bar Rav Ulla — or in the alternate version, Rabbah bar Rav Ulla married his son into another rabbinic family — and at the time of the wedding, *dardig mishcha areisha d'rabbanan b'sha'as ma'aseh*, oil was dripped on the heads of the rabbis. The practice is thus attested in actual rabbinic family life.

## First Marriage Evide (II)

### Widow Weddings

The Gemara asks: *almanusa mai* — what about a widow? If the evidential practices mark a first wedding, is there a corresponding practice that marks a second? The Gemara does not provide an affirmative answer. Rav Yosef teaches: *almanusa leisa kisnai* — for a widow, the roasted grain is not distributed. The evidence for a first wedding is thus largely defined by the presence of these practices, and for a second wedding by their absence. Tosafot notes the gap: one might have hoped for a more active marker of the second wedding, but the Gemara does not supply one.

## Ha-Peh She-Asar

### Field Admission Case

The Gemara now returns to the principle of *ha-peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir* — the mouth that bound is the mouth that permits, also called *modeh bi-miktzat* or, in this context, *ha-peh she-asar*. The case is: a man is found on a field and says to the person who challenges him, "This field belonged to your father, and I purchased it from him." By admitting that it once belonged to the other party's father, he opens himself to that claim — but having made that admission, he is believed when he adds that he purchased it, because he could have remained silent.

### Rashi and Tosafot

There is a fundamental debate between Rashi and Tosafot about what "he could have remained silent" means. Rashi holds that the other party — the one challenging him — was entirely oblivious. He had no awareness that this person was on the field, no complaint, no suspicion. In that situation, the occupant had nothing to answer; his voluntary admission and explanation together constitute a *ha-peh she-asar*. Tosafot argues that this is too narrow and that the actual case involves a genuine challenge: the other party says, "That's my field, what are you doing there?" There is, however, no *evidence* that needs answering — it is *stam kala*, an unsubstantiated claim. The occupant could have simply said *lo hayu devarim me-olam* — it is completely false — and the challenger would have had nothing. Instead, he concedes the historical ownership and claims a purchase. That concession, volunteered when it was not required, creates the *ha-peh she-asar*. This is already more of a *chiddush*.

### Father Mentioned

One asks: why does the *Mishnah* frame the case as "I purchased it from your *father*" rather than "I purchased it from you"? On Rashi's reading, asking why the man doesn't say he bought it directly from the challenger is almost incongruous — if the challenger is oblivious, how could he have been the one who sold it? But on Tosafot's reading, the question is sharper: the challenger himself is present, claiming the field is his. Why not have the occupant say, "I bought it from you"? That would be an even stronger case of *bari u-bari* — both parties making certain claims — and yet Rabbi Yehoshua would still believe the occupant.

The Gemara explains: *mishum de-ka-ba'ei le-mitni seifa* — it is because of the need to teach the *seifa*. The end of the *Mishnah* states: if there are witnesses that the field belonged to the challenger, the occupant is not believed. And the Gemara asks — what is the *chiddush* of the *seifa*?

### Three Year Chazakah

If the occupant had three full years of uncontested residence — *achal shenei chazakah* — he should win regardless of witnesses to original ownership. If he did not have three years, of course he is not believed; that is obvious. So where is the novelty?

### Katan Property

The answer: *bishlama gabei aviv* — with the father framing, we can construct a genuine *chiddush*. The case is *mashkachat lah de-achal shtayim be-chayei ha-av ve-achat be-chayei beno* — the occupant was on the field for two years during the father's lifetime and one year during the son's. That gives him three years of residence. Yet he is not believed, because of Rav Huna's principle: *ein machzikin be-nichsei katan* — one cannot establish *chazakah* over the property of a minor.

The reason *chazakah* works at all is that three years of uncontested residence is taken as evidence that the original owner waived his claim — he saw you on his field, knew about it, and said nothing. But a minor cannot be expected to register the presence of someone on his field and formulate a protest. His silence is therefore meaningless; it creates no presumption.

### Afilu Higdil

The larger *chiddush* of Rav Huna is *afilu higdil* — even once the minor becomes an adult, the years that began during his minority do not retroactively become valid years of *chazakah*. If someone was on that field during the child's minority, and the child grows up and says nothing for another nine years, those nine years of adult silence are tainted by the original period. The occupant would need to leave and return — or obtain a *shtar* — to establish a valid *chazakah* from scratch.

### Father's Role

So the *Mishnah* needed the father precisely to give the *seifa* a meaningful *chiddush*: you have three years of residence, witnesses confirm the field once belonged to the other party, and yet you are still not believed — because two of those years were during the son's minority, and *ein machzikin be-nichsei katan afilu higdil*.

### Shelo Befanav

The Gemara then asks: could the *Mishnah* not have achieved the same *chiddush* without introducing the father, by using the scenario of *shelo befanav* — years of residence when the owner was not present? *Chazakah* requires that the owner could have protested; if he was absent, his silence is not meaningful, and the *chazakah* does not take hold. One year's absence could thus undercut an otherwise complete three-year *chazakah*.

### Judea and Galilee

The Gemara invokes the *Mishnah* in *Bava Batra*: *shalosh aratzot le-chazakah* — there are three regions with respect to *chazakah*: *Yehudah*, *Ever HaYarden*, and *ha-Galil*. If the owner was in *Yehudah* and the occupant was on his field in the *Galil* — or the reverse — it is not a valid *chazakah* until they are in the same region. The Gemara probes this: if a protest not in the owner's presence — *mecha'ah shelo befanav* — is legally effective, then news travels across regions and *chazakah* should work regardless of which region each party is in. And if such a protest is *not* effective, then even within the same region, so long as the parties are in different cities, *chazakah* should not work. What is the principled distinction that makes region matter?

### Emergency Situation

Abba bar Memel resolves this: *le'olam kasavar mecha'ah shelo befanav havya mecha'ah* — in principle, a protest not in the owner's presence is valid, and news is presumed to travel. *U-matnitin b'sha'at cheirum shanoy* — but our *Mishnah* is dealing with a time of emergency, when an edict restricts travel. In such circumstances, the normal presumption that news crosses borders does not hold.

Why specifically *Yehudah* and *Galil*? *D'stam Yehudah ve-Galil ke-sha'at cheirum damu* — because as a general rule, the border between *Yehudah* and the *Galil* functioned like a time of emergency: crossing it was difficult and politically fraught. News did not flow freely between them. The ruling thus reflects not an abstract theoretical position, but an observed social reality — that information travels within a country's borders far more reliably than it crosses political and geographic boundaries, especially when those crossings are contested or restricted.

The Gemara concludes that the *shelo befanav* scenario could in principle have served to explain the *Mishnah's seifa* — a case where *chazakah* fails despite three years of residence — but it would have been a less striking *chiddush* than the case of the father and the minor son. Hence the *Mishnah* chose the father formulation, precisely because the *chiddush* of Rav Huna — that *chazakah* begun during a minor's infancy remains invalid even after he reaches majority — is less intuitive and more genuinely novel.
